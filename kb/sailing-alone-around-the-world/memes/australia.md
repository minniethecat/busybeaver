# Australia

Australia was a significant multi-port waypoint in [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s [[voyage|circumnavigation]]. He made his first Australian landfall at [[newcastle|Newcastle]], [[newcastle|New South Wales]], in October 1896.

Over the next seven months, he would visit [[sydney|Sydney]], [[melbourne|Melbourne]], and several [[tasmania|Tasmania]]n ports, experience exceptional hospitality and maritime camaraderie, and deliver his first public lecture. After a brief return to [[sydney|Sydney]] in April 1897, he sailed north along the Australian coast toward [[torres-strait|Torres Strait]] and the [[indian-ocean|Indian Ocean]].

## Geography

Australia, the island continent of the Southern Hemisphere, lies between the Indian and [[pacific-ocean|Pacific Ocean]]s. [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] approached from the east, crossing the [[coral-sea|Coral Sea]] from the [[samoa|Samoan Islands]] over a passage of forty-two days, and made his Australian landfall at [[newcastle|Newcastle]], a major coal-exporting port north of [[sydney|Sydney]] on the [[newcastle|New South Wales]] coast (013-001)(013-008).

The harbor of [[sydney|Sydney]], with its thriving yachting community, lay south of [[newcastle|Newcastle]] (013-012). Farther south, [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] passed through [[bass-strait|Bass Strait]], [[sail|sailing]] past [[eden|Twofold Bay]], Cliff Island, and Wilson's Promontory before reaching [[melbourne|Melbourne Heads]] on December 22, 1896 (013-015)(013-016)(013-017)(013-020). He later explored [[tasmania|Tasmania]]'s northern coast, visiting [[beauty-point|Beauty Point]], [[beacon|Beacon]]sfield, [[devonport|Devonport]] on the Mersey River, and [[launceston|Launceston]] on the Tamar River (014-005)(014-009)(013-029).

## Role in the Voyage

### Arrival at Newcastle

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] had traded to Australia in his earlier seafaring days, long before his solo [[voyage|circumnavigation]] (001-005). After clearing the [[samoa|Samoa]]n islands, "a sudden burst of the [[trade-wind|trades]] brought her down to close [[reefing|reef]]s, and she reeled off one hundred and eighty-four miles the first day," with forty miles of favorable current (013-008). The *[[spray|Spray]]* encountered a severe gale near New Caledonia that foundered the American clipper-ship *Patrician* farther south (013-009).

When he arrived at [[newcastle|Newcastle]] aboard the *[[spray|Spray]]* in October 1896, he "crowded on sail for lovely Australia, which was not a strange land" to him (013-007). He entered in dramatic fashion:

> "I arrived at Newcastle in the teeth of a gale of wind. It was a stormy season. The government pilot, Captain Cumming, met me at the harbor bar, and with the assistance of a steamer carried my vessel to a safe berth." (013-010)

### Sydney and the Yachting Community

Once in Australian waters, [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] navigated toward [[sydney|Sydney]] harbor, [[anchor|anchoring]] near Manly for a night where "the [[sydney|Sydney]] harbor police-boat giving me a pluck into anchorage while they gathered data from an old scrapbook of mine" (013-011). He found [[sydney|Sydney]]'s harbor "blooming with yachts" as the summer [[sail|sailing]] season approached (013-012).

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] observed the Australian maritime character with admiration. He described [[sydney|Sydney]]'s boats:

> "The typical Sydney boat is a handy sloop of great beam and enormous sail-carrying power; but a capsize is not uncommon, for they carry sail like vikings." (013-013)

He was especially struck by the resourcefulness of Australian youth: "If a boy in Australia has not the means to buy him a boat, he builds one" (013-013).

During his [[sydney|Sydney]] stay, the *[[spray|Spray]]* "received a new suit of [[sail|sails]]" through the generosity of Commodore Foy and the yachting community (013-012). [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] remarked on the intensity of Australian weather, noting he "never saw it rain harder even in Australia" (013-012).

### Melbourne and Bass Strait

From [[sydney|Sydney]], [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] sailed south: "Time flew fast those days in Australia, and it was December 6, 1896, when [[spray|the Spray]] sailed from [[sydney|Sydney]]. My intention was now to sail around [[cape-leeuwin|Cape Leeuwin]] direct for [[mauritius|Mauritius]] on my way home, and so I coasted along toward [[bass-strait|Bass Strait]] in that direction" (013-014).

The passage through [[bass-strait|Bass Strait]] was marked by changeable winds: "There was little to report on this part of the voyage, except changeable winds, 'busters,' and rough seas. The 12th of December, however, was an exceptional day, with a fine coast wind, northeast" (013-015).

At Wilson's Promontory, the lightkeeper Mr. J. Clark came aboard and directed [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] to Waterloo Bay for shelter (013-017). There he anchored with the ketch *Secret* and the steam ferryboat *Mary* of [[sydney|Sydney]]:

> "The captain of the Mary was a genius, and an Australian genius at that, and smart. His crew, from a sawmill up the coast, had not one of them ever seen a ship, but they were boatmen and were willing." (013-018)

The *[[spray|Spray]]* reached [[melbourne|Melbourne Heads]] on December 22, and [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] spent Christmas at a berth on the Yarrow River, then shifted to St. Kilda where he stayed nearly a month (013-020)(013-021).

### Tasmania and the Return to Sydney

Delayed by southwest winds, the *[[spray|Spray]]* departed [[melbourne|Melbourne]] on January 24, 1897, towed by the tug *Racer* (013-024). [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] sailed to [[tasmania|Tasmania]], where the *[[spray|Spray]]* was berthed at a small jetty at [[launceston|Launceston]]:

> "In this snug place I left her in charge of three children, while I made journeys among the hills and rested my bones, for the coming voyage, on the moss-covered rocks at the gorge hard by, and among the ferns I found wherever I went." (013-030)

While in [[tasmania|Tasmania]], [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] delivered his first lecture of the voyage at [[devonport|Devonport]]'s new hall — the Mersey Hall (014-009). The *[[spray|Spray]]* was also inspected for seaworthiness at [[devonport|Devonport]] (014-001).

Returning to [[sydney|Sydney]], the *[[spray|Spray]]* arrived on April 22, 1897, [[anchor|anchoring]] in Watson's Bay: "The harbor from the heads to Parramatta, up the river, was more than ever alive with boats and yachts" (014-015).

### Departure Northward

With a full [[current|set]] of Admiralty sheet-charts of the coast and [[great-barrier-reef|Barrier Reef]], [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] felt confident departing northward (014-018). On May 13, off [[port-macquarie|Port Macquarie]], he encountered the distressed cutter-yacht *[[cutter-yacht-akbar|Akbar]]*, a "modern dandy craft" with its mainsail blown to ribbons (014-020)(014-024). After attempting to assist, [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] reported the yacht to the steamship *[[steamship-sherman|Sherman]]* the following day (014-034). The *[[cutter-yacht-akbar|Akbar]]* was later reported lost at Crescent Head on May 31 (014-035).

On May 16, [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] exchanged signals with the [[beacon|lighthouse]] keepers on South Solitary Island, a "dreary stone heap in the ocean just off the coast of [[newcastle|New South Wales]]" (014-037). His last Australian chapter closed as he sailed northward through [[torres-strait|Torres Strait]], watching "pigeons flying across today from Australia toward the islands" (015-033).

## Encounters and Observations

### The "Rain of Blood"

While in [[melbourne|Melbourne]], [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] witnessed an extraordinary meteorological phenomenon: "one of those extraordinary [[gale|storm]]s sometimes called 'rain of blood,' the first of the kind in many years about Australia. The 'blood' came from a fine brick-dust matter afloat in the air from the deserts" (013-026). He noted that such dust-[[gale|storm]]s, common on the coast of Africa, also reach far out over the sea (013-027).

### Australian Character

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s encounters revealed a deep appreciation for Australian character. He noted:

- The *[[spray|Spray]]* "paid no port charges in Australia or anywhere else on the voyage, except at [[pernambuco|Pernambuco]], till she poked her nose into the customhouse at [[melbourne|Melbourne]], where she was charged [[tonnage|tonnage dues]]; in this instance, sixpence a [[tonnage|ton]] on the gross" (013-022).
- He encountered a report circulating in Australia that the island of [[juan-fernandez-island|Juan Fernandez]] had sunk — a rumor that later proved false (010-022).

### A Mysterious Testimonial

While in [[tasmania|Tasmania]], [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] received an anonymous testimonial: "To this day I do not know who wrote it or to whom I am indebted for the generous gift it contained. I could not refuse a thing so kindly meant, but promised myself to pass it on with interest at the first opportunity, and this I did before leaving Australia" (014-004).

## Historical Context

In the 1890s, Australia was a collection of British colonies on the verge of federation (which would occur in 1901). [[newcastle|Newcastle]] was a major coal-exporting and shipbuilding center, while [[sydney|Sydney]] had developed a renowned yachting culture. [[melbourne|Melbourne]], the largest city, was a center of commerce and finance. [[tasmania|Tasmania]], an island colony south of the mainland, maintained thriving ports at [[launceston|Launceston]] and [[devonport|Devonport]].

Australia's maritime economy connected it to trade routes across [[pacific-ocean|the Pacific]] and [[indian-ocean|Indian Ocean]]s — routes [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] himself had sailed as a merchant captain before his solo voyage (001-005). The ports he visited during 1896–1897 represented the complete span of Australian maritime infrastructure: from the industrial harbor of [[newcastle|Newcastle]], through the cosmopolitan yacht clubs of [[sydney|Sydney]], to the agricultural ports of [[tasmania|Tasmania]].
