# Keel

The keel is the principal structural member of a wooden vessel, running the full length of the hull along the centerline of the bottom. It is the "backbone" of the ship — the first timber laid in construction, and the foundation from which the stem, sternpost, and frames extend upward. A stout, well-laid keel gives a vessel strength to withstand grounding, stability to carry sail, and the hydrodynamic profile that determines [[sail|sailing]] performance.

## Definition

In traditional wooden shipbuilding, the keel is a heavy timber (or assembly of timbers) laid along the vessel's centerline. The *false keel* is a sacrificial timber attached below the main keel to protect it in the event of grounding. The *keel shoe* is an iron or hardwood cap added for additional protection. The *keelson* is an internal timber running above the keel inside the hull, sandwiching the frames between keel and keelson for immense structural strength. The phrase "from keelson to truck" — meaning from the very bottom of the hull to the very top of the mast — signifies the entirety of a vessel.

## Nautical Context

A vessel's keel determines much of its [[sail|sailing]] character. A deep, heavy keel provides stability and weatherliness (the ability to sail close to the wind). A long, straight keel with a cutaway forefoot — as the *[[spray|Spray]]* had — allows a vessel to [[course|track]] well, resisting leeway while remaining maneuverable. The keel also bears the brunt of damage: grounding scrapes the bottom, floating debris strikes the hull, and shipworms bore into unprotected timber. Regular careening (hauling the vessel onto a beach at high tide to expose the bottom) allowed sailors to inspect, clean, and repair the keel. The phrase "copper-bottomed" refers to the practice of sheathing the keel and hull with copper to deter marine growth.

## In *Sailing Alone Around the World*

The *[[spray|Spray]]*'s keel appears in the narrative at critical moments — construction, damage, and celebration — each episode illuminating the significance of this hidden but essential timber.

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s rebuilding of the *[[spray|Spray]]* began with the keel itself. He found "a stout oak-tree nearby for a keel" and enlisted "Farmer [[captain-howard|Howard]], for a small sum of money, to haul it to the scene of the shipbuilding" (001-008). The image is almost biblical — a tree becomes a keel, and a derelict sloop becomes a circumnavigator.

Off the coast of [[uruguay|Uruguay]], the *[[spray|Spray]]* struck a rock and "lost her shoe and part of her false keel, and received other damage" (006-007). The false keel — the sacrificial protector — did its job. Carpenters at the dockyard "mended the keel and also the lifeboat" (006-012), [[current|set]]ting the *[[spray|Spray]]* [[rigging|rig]]ht again.

In [[strait-of-magellan|the Strait]] of [[ferdinand-magellan|Magellan]], danger came from beneath: "Kelp waved forebodingly under her keel, and the wreck of a great steamer lay on the beach" (007-007). The mention of the keel here is almost tactile — the kelp brushing the bottom, the wreck a reminder of what the keel might strike.

In a moment of exuberant metaphor, [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] wrote that "a small sail shook her from keelson to truck when it shivered in the gale" (008-007) — the vibration transmitted through every timber from the lowest to the highest, the sail's energy resonating through the entire structure. After careening in the [[pacific-ocean|South Pacific]], he declared "my vessel was tallowed from keelson to truck" (009-023) — the complete maintenance ritual, protecting every inch from the keel upward.

> My ax felled a stout oak-tree nearby for a keel, and Farmer Howard, for a small sum of money, hauled in this and enough timbers for the frame of the new vessel. I rigged a steam-box and a pot for a boiler. The timbers for ribs, being straight saplings, were dressed and steamed till supple, and then bent over a log, where they were secured till set. Something tangible appeared every day to show for my labor, and the neighbors made the work sociable.
>
> (001-008)
