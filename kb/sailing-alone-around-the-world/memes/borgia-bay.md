# Borgia Bay

A bay in [[strait-of-gibraltar|the Strait]] of [[ferdinand-magellan|Magellan]], a few miles west of [[three-island-cove|Three Island Cove]], where the *[[spray|Spray]]* found anchorage during both passages through the strait.

## Geography

Borgia Bay lies in the western reaches of [[strait-of-gibraltar|the Strait]] of [[ferdinand-magellan|Magellan]], on the [[patagonia|Patagonian coast]]. The bay is surrounded by high, wooded mountains typical of the region, with deep water suitable for [[anchor|anchoring]]. It was a recognized stopping point for vessels transiting the strait, evidenced by the "boards on the trees ashore with name and date of harboring carved or painted" left by previous mariners (007-024).

## Role in the Voyage

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] first reached Borgia Bay on his westward passage after departing [[three-island-cove|Three Island Cove]] following a gale. He found the place remarkably isolated — "nothing else could I see to indicate that civilized man had ever been there" aside from the name-boards nailed to trees (007-024). While preparing to go ashore and take notes, the [[chile|Chile]]an gunboat *[[chilean-gunboat-huemel|Huemel]]* arrived. Its officers advised [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] to leave the area, warning of the danger from [[tierra-del-fuego|Fuegia]]n raiders. [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] accepted the captain's offer of a tow to Notch Cove, eight miles farther along (007-024).

On his return eastward passage, the *[[spray|Spray]]* again made Borgia Bay. [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] described "flogging against current and wind" before arriving and casting anchor "for the second time" (009-018). He departed early the next morning, standing out from the bay and mooring off Cape Quod by kelp in twenty fathoms of water against a three-knot current (009-021).

> The *Spray* sailed from Three Island Cove in the morning after the gale went down, but was glad to return for shelter from another sudden gale. Sailing again on the following day, she fetched Borgia Bay, a few miles on her course, where vessels had anchored from time to time and had nailed boards on the trees ashore with name and date of harboring carved or painted. Nothing else could I see to indicate that civilized man had ever been there. I had taken a survey of the gloomy place with my spyglass, and was getting my boat out to land and take notes, when the Chilean gunboat *Huemel* came in, and officers, coming on board, advised me to leave the place at once, a thing that required little eloquence to persuade me to do. I accepted the captain’s kind offer of a tow to the next anchorage, at the place called Notch Cove, eight miles farther along, where I should be clear of the worst of the Fuegians.
>
> (007-024)

## Historical Context

Borgia Bay was part of the network of anchorages used by [[sail|sailing]] vessels in [[strait-of-gibraltar|the Strait]] of [[ferdinand-magellan|Magellan]] during the 19th century. The [[chile|Chile]]an navy maintained a presence in the strait, with gunboats like the *[[chilean-gunboat-huemel|Huemel]]* patrolling to protect shipping and warn vessels of [[tierra-del-fuego|Fuegia]]n raiding parties. The name-boards on trees — a practice of leaving carved or painted records of a ship's visit — were a common tradition among long-distance voyagers of the era.
