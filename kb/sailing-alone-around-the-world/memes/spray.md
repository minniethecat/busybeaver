# Spray

The *Spray* was a 37-foot gaff-[[rigging|rig]]ged oyster sloop — derelict, rotting, and worthless when [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] acquired her, and one of the most famous vessels in maritime history when he returned her to port three years later. Rebuilt by [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s own hands at [[Fairhaven]], modified with a long [[keel]] and exceptional balance, the *Spray* demonstrated qualities that no naval architect had predicted: she could hold her [[course]] without a [[helm|helmsman]] for days at a time, making solo ocean [[sail|sailing]] not merely possible but practical. She was, as [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] later wrote, "the vessel that the sailors of my generation most loved" — the little sloop that changed the possibilities of what one person and one boat could do.

## Specifications

The *Spray* was originally built around 1801 as an oyster dredger, working the waters of Delaware Bay and later Massachusetts. By 1892, she was a wreck — "a derelict," [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] called her, given to him by a retired whaling captain. [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] rebuilt her completely at [[Fairhaven|Fairhaven, Massachusetts]], over the course of 13 months, spending $553.62 on materials. He found "a stout oak-tree nearby for a keel" (001-008), and Farmer [[captain-howard|Howard]] hauled it to the building site.

The *Spray*'s design was unusual: a long keel, a plumb stem, a canoe stern, and a [[rigging|rig]] consisting of a gaff mainsail, a foresail, and a jib. Her beam was 14 feet 2 inches, her draft about 4 feet. She was built of oak and yellow pine, fastened with galvanized iron and copper. The modifications [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] made — particularly the long, straight keel and the balanced sail plan — gave her the self-[[helm|steering]] ability that became legendary.

## History

The *Spray* began life as a working oyster boat, one of hundreds that plied the coastal waters of the American Northeast. Her early history is unrecorded — a working vessel, not a famous one — until the day in 1892 when [[eben-pierce|Captain Eben Pierce]] offered her to [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] as a gift: "a ship, but a ship that wants some repairs." [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s 13-month rebuild transformed her from a derelict into a blue-water cruiser capable of circumnavigating the globe.

After the [[voyage|circumnavigation]] (1895–1898), the *Spray* became a celebrity. She was exhibited, written about, and celebrated. [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] continued to sail her along the American coast. In November 1909, he [[current|set]] out from Martha's Vineyard bound for the Orinoco River. Neither captain nor vessel was ever seen again. The prevailing theory is that the *Spray* was struck by a steamer in the night — a larger vessel that never knew it had run down the most famous small boat in the world.

## Role in the Voyage

The *Spray*'s performance during the [[voyage|circumnavigation]] exceeded all expectations. Her signature quality was her ability to hold a [[course]] with a lashed [[rudder|helm]]. Off the coast of the United States, [[captain-howard|Captain Howard]] "sat near the [[compass|binnacle]] and watched the compass while [[sloop|the sloop]] held her course so steadily that one would have declared that the card was nailed fast. Not a quarter of a point did she deviate from her course" (006-014). This was not a fluke — it was a design feature, the result of [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s intuitive understanding of hull balance.

The *Spray* proved seaworthy in the worst conditions: a [[gale]] off the [[Azores]], the [[tide|tide-race]]s of the [[Bay-of-Fundy|Bay of Fundy]], the williwaws of the [[Strait-of-Magellan]], and the long swells of [[pacific-ocean|the Pacific]]. She was knocked down, swept by seas, and struck rocks — "lost her shoe and part of her [[keel|false keel]]" (006-007) — but she survived everything the oceans could throw at her. At [[Cape Horn]], in "the worst sea that [[cape-horn|Cape Horn]] or its wild regions could afford" (008-004), the Spray faced her ultimate test and emerged undamaged.

Her speed was modest (about 4–5 [[knot|knots]] cruising, 8 [[knot|knots]] maximum) but steady. She made passages — 90 miles in a night through a rough sea (004-011) — that compared favorably with larger vessels. In [[pacific-ocean|the Pacific]], [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] recorded: "For one whole month my vessel held her course true; I had not, the while, so much as a light in the [[compass|binnacle]]" (011-019). The *Spray* was, in effect, her own [[helm|helmsman]].

> "She sat on the water like a swan." — Slocum's description of the Spray's motion, from his log (001-013)

> "The Spray will come back." — the prophetic words of an old whaling captain at the launch (P02-002)

## Significance

The *Spray*'s legacy extends far beyond her own voyage. She proved that a small, simple, well-balanced vessel could sail [[voyage|around the world]] with a single crewman — a proposition that had seemed absurd before [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] demonstrated it. The "Spray replica" movement — dozens of vessels built to her lines in the decades after [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s book appeared — testifies to the enduring fascination of the little sloop. Modern single-handed sailors, from [[fridtjof-nansen|Nansen]]'s era to the Golden Globe racers, sail in the *Spray*'s wake.

She also demonstrated something about the relationship between sailor and vessel. The *Spray* and [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] were partners, not master and servant. He spoke of her with affection — "my little bark," "the good sloop" — and credited her with decisions that saved his life. In an age of iron steamships, the *Spray* was an argument for wood, [[sail|canvas]], and the intelligence of a well-designed hull.
