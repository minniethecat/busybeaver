# Torres Strait

Torres Strait is the shallow, coral-studded passage that separates [[australia|Australia]]'s Cape York Peninsula from [[new-guinea|Papua]] [[new-guinea|New Guinea]]. For mariners, it was legendary — and not for its beauty. Shipwrecks littered its [[reefing|reef]]s, and navigating its narrow channels required favorable winds, good visibility, and a steady nerve. [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]], [[sail|sailing]] the *[[spray|Spray]]* alone, threaded it on his way from [[sydney|Sydney]] to the [[indian-ocean|Indian Ocean]].

## Geography

The strait is about 150 kilometers wide at its narrowest, but the navigable channels are far narrower. Scores of islands, [[reefing|reef]]s, and shoals — the northernmost reach of the [[great-barrier-reef|Great Barrier Reef]] — make it one of the most challenging passages in the world. The [[current|tidal current]]s are fierce, and the winds, when they blow against the tide, raise a dangerous short sea.

## In the Voyage

Torres Strait bookended a significant phase of [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s voyage. Departing [[sydney|Sydney]], he was "northward bound for Torres Strait" — (014-001), [[current|set]]ting his course for this notorious passage. The run up the [[australia|Australia]]n coast brought its own dramas — an amateur shipwreck observed, friendly shores, and the ever-present [[reefing|reef]].

Approaching the strait, he was careful: "made anchor and stood across for [[thursday-island|Thursday Island]], now in plain view, mid-channel in Torres Strait, where I arrived shortly after" — (015-023). He spent a day exploring among the islands — "one day among the islands in Torres Strait. This being a scientific expedition" — (015-024), he noted with characteristic understatement, as though his voyage [[voyage|around the world]] were a routine survey.

But he was candid about the danger. Reflecting on "the dangers of the [[coral-sea|Coral Sea]] and Torres Strait, which, indeed, were not a few" — (015-028), he acknowledged what every sailor in those waters knew: the strait demanded respect. His route, he summarized later, ran "from [[strait-of-gibraltar|the Strait]] of [[ferdinand-magellan|Magellan]] to Torres Strait" — (012-035), connecting two of the world's most challenging passages in a single voyage.

> The Spray had now passed nearly all the dangers of the Coral Sea and Torres Strait, which, indeed, were not a few.
>
> (015-028)

## Historical Context

Torres Strait was named for Luis Váez de Torres, the Spanish navigator who sailed through it in 1606. By the 1890s, it was a regular route for steamers and [[sail|sailing]] ships trading between [[australia|Australia]] and Asia, but it had claimed countless vessels. The establishment of [[thursday-island|Thursday Island]] as a port and pearling center gave mariners a refuge, but the strait remained a test of seamanship.
