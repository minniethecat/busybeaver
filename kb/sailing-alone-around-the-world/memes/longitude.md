# Longitude

Longitude is the east–west coordinate on Earth's surface, measured in degrees (0–180 east or west) from an arbitrary prime meridian (by [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s time, the Greenwich meridian). Unlike latitude — easily determined by the height of the sun or Pole Star — longitude at sea was, for centuries, the great unsolved problem of navigation. Its solution required either a chronometer accurate enough to carry reference time across oceans, or mastery of the intricate lunar-distance method.

## Definition

The Earth rotates 360 degrees in 24 hours; therefore, each hour of time difference corresponds to 15 degrees of longitude. If a navigator knows the local time (from a noon sight of the sun) and simultaneously knows the time at the reference meridian (from a chronometer), subtracting the two yields longitude. Without a chronometer, the *lunar-distance method* can be used: measuring the angular distance between the moon and a known star, then consulting tables to derive Greenwich time — a procedure requiring hours of calculation. By [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s era, affordable chronometers had made lunar distances a backup method, but not all mariners could afford the instrument.

## Nautical Context

An error in longitude was the most common cause of shipwreck before the chronometer era — vessels would make landfall hundreds of miles from their estimated position, striking unmarked [[reefing|reef]]s or running onto coasts they believed still distant. Even with a chronometer, the navigator's work was meticulous: daily noon sights, careful winding of the chronometer at the same hour, recording in the log book, and plotting on the chart. "Running down the longitude" meant [[sail|sailing]] east or west until the known latitude intersected the destination — a method [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] used repeatedly.

## In *Sailing Alone Around the World*

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s relationship with longitude was shaped by his lack of a chronometer. Early in the voyage, a passing captain "gave me the longitude and the time. I admired the businesslike manner in which he took his sights" (003-014) — a moment of professional appreciation, and perhaps envy, from a sailor making do without.

Crossing [[atlantic-ocean|the Atlantic]], [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] noted with characteristic understatement that "no observations for longitude had been made" (005-014) for some time. In the equatorial Atlantic, he recorded his position precisely when another ship confirmed it: "in the latitude of 5° N., longitude 26° 30′ W." (005-022). Shortly after, he marked the milestone: "the *[[spray|Spray]]* crossed the equator in longitude 29° 30′ W." (005-023).

At [[cape-of-good-hope|the Cape]] of Good Hope, a naval officer's kindness filled the gap: he "unsolicited gave the longitude by chronometer as 48° W., 'as near as I can make it'" (006-002). Later in [[pacific-ocean|the Pacific]], [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] described [[sail|sailing]] to 12° S., where he "ran down the longitude for weeks" (011-021) — a phrase that captures the monotony and discipline of ocean navigation.

> The steamship South Wales spoke the Spray and unsolicited gave the longitude by chronometer as 48° W., "as near as I can make it," the captain said. The Spray, with her tin clock, had exactly the same reckoning.
