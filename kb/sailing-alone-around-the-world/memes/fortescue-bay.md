# Fortescue Bay

Fortescue Bay, tucked into the southern shore of the [[strait-of-magellan|Strait of Magellan]], was one of the more unsettling stops of [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s voyage. He was chased by local natives, fired a warning shot at a man called "[[black-pedro|Black Pedro]]," and returned later for an anchorage that was anything but restful. The bay embodied the wild, contested nature of the [[patagonia|Patagonia]]n channels — beautiful, but never entirely safe.

## Geography

Fortescue Bay is a deep inlet on the southern side of the [[strait-of-magellan|Strait of Magellan]], in one of the more remote stretches of the passage. The surrounding country is mountainous and heavily forested, with few European [[current|set]]tlements. In the 1890s, the indigenous [[fuegians|Fuegians]] still moved freely through these waters, and encounters between sailors and natives were common.

## In the Voyage

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s first encounter with Fortescue Bay was alarming. A group of [[fuegians|Fuegians]] pursued him along the shore, and the chapter [[course|heading]] captures the tension: "Chased by Indians from Fortescue Bay — A miss-shot for '[[black-pedro|Black Pedro]]'" — (007-001). The chase was real, and [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] — alone on the *[[spray|Spray]]* — took defensive action.

He later returned to the bay under different circumstances. Driven by weather, "the course which brought her to Fortescue Bay, and at once among the native fires" — (007-017), the *[[spray|Spray]]* again found herself in these contested waters. This time, [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] had more time to observe: "about twenty miles along from Fortescue Bay. I saw on the opposite side" — (007-020) of the strait the many campfires of the [[fuegians|Fuegians]], a sight that was both beautiful and unnerving.

A subsequent visit was more peaceful. "The [[spray|Spray]] crossed over to Fortescue Bay, where she anchored and spent the night" — (009-009). But even then, the sense of watchfulness never left him. On the final departure from the region, he noted the behavior of another vessel "as if bound to Fortescue Bay. This I judged to be a piece of" — (009-017) local knowledge that he himself had now acquired.

## Historical Context

In the 1890s, the [[fuegians|Fuegian people]]s of [[strait-of-gibraltar|the Strait]] of [[ferdinand-magellan|Magellan]] — the Selk'nam, the Yámana, and others — were in rapid decline, decimated by disease and displacement. Their encounters with European sailors ranged from trade to hostility, and [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s experience at Fortescue Bay reflected the mutual suspicion that characterized many of these meetings. His reference to "[[black-pedro|Black Pedro]]" suggests a known figure among the local sealing and shipping community — a reminder that these remote bays had their own legends and their own dangers.

> Canoes manned by savages from Fortescue now came in pursuit. The wind falling light, they gained on me rapidly till coming within hail, when they ceased paddling, and a bowlegged savage stood up and called to me, "Yammerschooner! yammerschooner!" which is their begging term. I said, "No!"
>
> (007-017)
