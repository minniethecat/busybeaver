## Geography

Sable Island is a narrow, crescent-shaped sand bar located approximately 175 kilometers southeast of mainland [[nova-scotia|Nova Scotia]], in the North [[atlantic-ocean|Atlantic Ocean]].

In [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s time it was reported to be about twenty miles long, having reportedly shrunk from an earlier length of forty miles due to erosion (003-004).

The island is surrounded by dangerous shoals and shifting sandbars, and its low elevation makes it nearly invisible to approaching vessels in poor weather. These features have earned it a grim reputation across centuries of Atlantic navigation.

## In Slocum's Voyage

Sable Island was the first major Atlantic landmark [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] encountered after departing from the [[nova-scotia|Nova Scotia]] coast. Having [[current|set]] his course from near [[halifax|Halifax]] harbor on the evening of July 3, he sailed eastward on a course designed to pass north of the island's dangers.

The passage was a somber and isolating experience, marked by thick fog that descended as he approached. At 9:40 p.m. on July 4, 1895, [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] sighted the island's light:

> At 9:40 p.m. I raised the sheen only of the light on the west end of Sable Island, which may also be called the Island of Tragedies. The fog, which till this moment had held off, now lowered over the sea like a pall. I was in a world of fog, shut off from the universe. I did not see any more of the light. (003-004)

Navigating by the lead line alone, [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] sounded his way past the island. "By the lead, which I cast often, I found that a little after midnight I was passing the east point of the island, and should soon be clear of dangers of land and shoals" (003-004).

The fog-enshrouded passage past Sable Island [[current|set]] the [[tonnage|ton]]e for the early days of [[atlantic-ocean|the Atlantic]] crossing — a solitary voyage through an invisible world, where the only connection to reality was the weight of the lead line and the sound of the sea.

## The Island of Tragedies

Sable Island's grim nickname was well earned. Over the centuries, hundreds of vessels had come to grief on its shifting sands. The island lies in the path of the North Atlantic shipping lanes, and its low profile made it a hidden trap in poor visibility.

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s awareness of the island's dangers is evident in his careful navigation. By [[current|set]]ting his course north of the island and taking frequent soundings with the lead, he ensured the *[[spray|Spray]]* would not join the long list of wrecks on Sable's shores.

## Beyond Sable

Clearing Sable Island marked a psychological threshold. The last outlying danger of the North American coast was astern. Ahead lay the open Atlantic — eighteen days to the [[azores|Azores]], as it would prove, with nothing but sea and sky between the *[[spray|Spray]]* and the eastern hemisphere.

Within eight days of passing Sable, the *[[spray|Spray]]* was twelve hundred miles east of [[cape-sable|Cape Sable]] — "one hundred and fifty miles a day for so small a vessel must be considered good [[sail|sailing]]. It was the greatest run the *[[spray|Spray]]* ever made before or since in so few days" (003-009).

The passage past Sable also introduced [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] to the deeper loneliness of the open ocean. Chapter III of his narrative is titled with the phrase "The first fit of loneliness," and the experience of being "shut off from the universe" in the fog off Sable Island was its first sharp expression (003-001).

In this sense, Sable Island was not merely a navigational hazard but a rite of passage — the point where the familiar coast gave way to the vast solitude that would define [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s [[voyage|circumnavigation]].
