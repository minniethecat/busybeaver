## Definition

The doldrums are a belt of low pressure around the equator where the prevailing [[trade-wind|trade winds]] from the northern and southern hemispheres meet and cancel each other out. This region is characterized by light, variable winds, sudden squalls, and heavy rainfall. For [[sail|sailing]] vessels, the doldrums could mean days or weeks of slow progress.

## Slocum's Experience

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] encountered the doldrums while [[sail|sailing]] southward across [[atlantic-ocean|the Atlantic]] in September 1895. The *[[spray|Spray]]* was drawing rapidly toward the region of doldrums, and he observed that the force of the [[trade-wind|trade winds]] was lessening (005-017).

> The sloop was now rapidly drawing toward the region of doldrums, and the force of the trade-winds was lessening. I could see by the ripples that a countercurrent had set in. This I estimated to be about sixteen miles a day. (005-017)

On September 14, a lofty three-masted ship [[course|heading]] north was sighted from the [[mast|masthead]], though not within signal distance (005-018). On September 16, the *[[spray|Spray]]* entered the doldrums proper. Heavy rain-clouds rose in the south, obscuring the sun, which [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] recognized as ominous of doldrums (005-018).

The *[[spray|Spray]]* then endured ten days of punishing conditions, making only three hundred miles on her course in all that time:

> On the 16th the Spray entered this gloomy region, to battle with squalls and to be harassed by fitful calms; for this is the state of the elements between the northeast and the southeast trades, where each wind, struggling in turn for mastery, expends its force whirling about in all directions. Making this still more trying to one's nerve and patience, the sea was tossed into confused cross-lumps and fretted by eddying currents. As if something more were needed to complete a sailor's discomfort in this state, the rain poured down in torrents day and night. (005-018)

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s laconic summation — "I didn't say anything!" — captures his characteristic understatement in the face of hardship (005-018).

## Encounters in the Doldrums

Despite the frustrating conditions, [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] found company in the doldrums. On September 23, the fine schooner *Nantasket* of [[boston|Boston]], lumber-laden and just through the doldrums, came up with the *[[spray|Spray]]*. Her captain exchanged a few words before [[sail|sailing]] on (005-019).

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] noted that the *Nantasket*, being much fouled on the bottom by shellfish, drew along with her a school of fishes that had been following the *[[spray|Spray]]*. Among these was a dolphin that had accompanied the *[[spray|Spray]]* for about a thousand miles (005-019).

The dolphin, identifiable by its scars, had been wounded and could not hunt, so it fed on scraps from [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s table. [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] had grown accustomed to its company and missed it whenever it took excursions away from [[sloop|the sloop]] (005-019).

## The Psychological Challenge

For the solo navigator, the doldrums posed a challenge beyond the merely physical. Without wind to drive the *[[spray|Spray]]* forward, [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] faced hours of idleness in oppressive heat, the heavy moisture-laden air sapping his energy. A crewed vessel could share the burden of trimming [[sail|sails]] to catch every faint breath of wind; [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] bore this alone.

The psychological dimension is implicit in his careful observation of the ripples and counter-currents. By measuring the [[current|drift]] — "about sixteen miles a day" — he gave himself a task, a small statistical victory against the void of the calm (005-017).

His observation of the dolphin's behavior, and the diversion of sharks with a tin pan towed astern, shows a man finding solace and entertainment in the natural world around him when the wind refused to blow (005-019). This self-discipline in the face of stasis was as essential to his success as any feat of seamanship in a gale.

## Navigation Significance

The doldrums posed a particular challenge for a single-handed sailor. Without wind, the *[[spray|Spray]]* could make little progress, and the heavy, moisture-laden air was exhausting. The confused cross-lumps and eddying currents of the region made it impossible to [[current|set]]tle into any predictable rhythm of [[helm|steering]] or sail-trimming (005-018). [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s careful observation of the ripples and counter-currents helped him estimate [[current|drift]] and maintain situational awareness even when becalmed.

The doldrums crossing was also a test of patience. Ten days to cover three hundred miles — an average of only thirty miles per day — would have tried the resolve of any sailor. For [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]], alone at the [[rudder|helm]], the doldrums were as much a psychological barrier as a meteorological one.
