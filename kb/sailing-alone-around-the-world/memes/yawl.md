A two-masted fore-and-aft rigged sailing vessel whose distinguishing feature is that the shorter mizzenmast is stepped aft of the rudder post. By the late 19th century, the yawl rig had become a favored configuration for cruising yachts, prized for its balance of sail area between the two masts and the ability to reduce canvas quickly in heavy weather.

## Definition and Rig

In a yawl, the forward mast (mainmast) carries the principal sails — mainsail, topsail, and headsails — while the shorter mizzenmast, set well aft, carries a small mizzen or jigger sail. Because the mizzen is aft of the rudder, it functions as much as a balancing sail as a driving sail. The mizzen can be used to steady the vessel at anchor (riding sail effect), to balance the helm when sailing, and to heave-to reliably in heavy weather. The total sail area is divided between two masts, making each individual sail smaller and easier for a single-handed sailor to manage than the larger sails of an equivalent sloop or cutter.

## In *Sailing Alone Around the World*

The yawl rig is central to the story of the *Spray* because Slocum deliberately converted the sloop to a yawl before undertaking his circumnavigation. "I had decided to give the Spray a yawl rig for the tempestuous waters of the Strait of Magellan" (005-030). The conversion was undertaken in Montevideo, where a local shipwright "changed the rig to a yawl, though I called the boat a sloop still" (010-004).

Slocum's yawl-rig was an improvisation rather than a textbook design. "The yawl-rig then adopted was an improvement over the old one for all weather conditions" (022-008). He added a mizzenmast and "jigger-sail added to form the yawl-rig with which the rest of the voyage was successfully completed" (022-011). A stout downhaul on the gaff was also necessary "because with the jigger the wind could more easily catch the mainsail aback" (022-008).

The Spray's official registration reflected her modified rig: "the Spray of Boston, a yawl of 12.70 tons gross" (023-008). Despite the technical change, Slocum continued to refer to the *Spray* affectionately as his "sloop," blurring the distinction between her original and modified rigs in everyday speech.

The yawl rig proved its worth throughout the circumnavigation, enabling Slocum to handle the *Spray* alone in conditions ranging from tropical calms to Cape Horn gales — a testament to the suitability of the yawl configuration for single-handed ocean cruising.
