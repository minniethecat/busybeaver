# Henry Morton Stanley

Sir Henry Morton Stanley (1841–1904) was a Welsh-American journalist and explorer whose expeditions into central Africa — most famously his search for Dr. David Livingstone — made him one of the most celebrated travelers of [[victoria|the Victoria]]n era. [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]], navigating his own solitary path across the world's oceans, found in Stanley a kindred spirit.

## Biography

Born John Rowlands in Wales, he took the name of his adoptive father in New Orleans and made his name as a war correspondent before Henry Gordon Bennett of the *New York Herald* sent him to Africa to find Livingstone. His greeting — "Dr. Livingstone, I presume?" — became one of the most famous phrases of the 19th century. He later led expeditions across the Congo, mapping the great river and enduring hardships that killed most of his companions.

## Role in the Voyage

Stanley appears in [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s narrative as a touchstone — another man who had faced the unknown alone (or nearly so) and prevailed. [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]], who had read widely during his long watches at sea, was familiar with Stanley's accounts of African exploration. The comparison was implicit: the African explorer and the ocean circumnavigator, each in his element, each dependent on his own resources.

> At Hotel Royal one day, dining with Colonel Saunderson, M.P., his son, and Lieutenant Tipping, I met Mr. Stanley. The great explorer was just from Pretoria, and had already as good as flayed President Krüger with his trenchant pen.
>
> (017-022)

## Significance

Stanley belongs to the constellation of explorers — [[ferdinand-magellan|Magellan]], [[christopher-columbus|Columbus]], Cook — whom [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] invoked as predecessors and peers. The [[victoria|Victoria]]n era's cult of exploration linked Africa and the oceans in a single narrative of human endeavor against nature, and [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] was conscious of his place in that tradition.
