A **voyage** is a long journey by sea, typically undertaken for exploration, trade, or personal challenge. In nautical tradition, a voyage has a beginning, a prevailing purpose, and a completion—it is more than a passage; it is a narrative. [[joshua-slocum|Joshua Slocum]]'s solo circumnavigation from 1895 to 1898 was the first single-handed voyage around the world, covering approximately 46,000 miles and establishing the template for all ocean voyaging that followed.

## Definition

In maritime usage, a voyage is distinguished from a passage (a single leg between two ports) by its scope and intentionality. A voyage implies a plan—a departure, a route, and a destination—even if the plan is repeatedly revised. The word carries weight in sailor's language: a "maiden voyage" is a vessel's first journey; a "foreign voyage" crosses national waters; a "voyage around the world" implies the circumnavigation of the globe.

The single-handed voyage adds a particular dimension: every decision rests on one person. There is no division of labor, no watch system, no relief. The solo voyager must be navigator, [[helm|helmsman]], cook, carpenter, sailmaker, and doctor—all while managing the profound psychological challenge of isolation.

## Nautical Context

Before [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]], the idea of a solo circumnavigation was widely considered impossible—or at least suicidal. Ocean passages were understood as collective endeavors requiring crew, watches, and specialization. [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s voyage broke that assumption. He demonstrated that a small sloop, properly prepared and handled by a competent sailor, could safely circle the globe alone.

The voyage established principles that remain central to single-handed [[sail|sailing]]: the importance of a self-[[helm|steering]] vessel, the value of simplicity in equipment, the necessity of knowing one's own limits, and the psychological discipline required to endure weeks without sight of land or another human being. Subsequent solo circumnavigators—from Harry Pidgeon to Francis Chichester to Robin Knox-Johns[[tonnage|ton]]—all sailed in [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s wake.

## In *Sailing Alone Around the World*

The voyage is the book's subject. [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] opens his narrative not with the departure but with the idea of departure—the intention formed long before the keel touched water.

His family heritage pointed seaward:

> "On both sides my family were sailors; and if any Slocum should be found not seafaring, he will show at least an inclination to whittle models of boats and contemplate voyages." — (001-002)

The contemplation preceded the act. The voyage began as a thought, a model whittled in the mind, years before the *[[spray|Spray]]* was rebuilt.

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] describes his decision with characteristic understatement:

> "I had resolved on a voyage around the world, and as the wind on the morning of April 24, 1895, was fair, at noon I weighed anchor, set sail, and filled away from Boston." — (002-002)

The resolution was absolute; the execution, matter-of-fact. This quality—treating the extraordinary as ordinary—runs through the entire narrative.

But the voyage was not undertaken lightly. In [[gloucester|Gloucester]], before the final departure, [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] paused:

> "I made for the cove, a lovely branch of Gloucester's fine harbor, again to look the Spray over and again to weigh the voyage, and my feelings, and all that." — (002-006)

"Weigh the voyage"—an honest admission that even the most determined sailor hesitates before committing to the open ocean. The pause, the weighing, the checking, and the final push: this is the rhythm of every long voyage.

Once underway, the voyage developed its own momentum. Reaching the equator for the first time, [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] reflected:

> "Did I tire of the voyage in all that time? Not a bit of it! I was never in better trim in all my life, and was eager for the more perilous experience of rounding the Horn." (005-023)

The voyage was not an ordeal to be endured but a life to be lived—a distinction that separates [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s account from mere survival narratives.

Off the coast of [[australia|Australia]], a notable event occurred: "what is a notable event in every voyage, picked up the trade-winds" (014-040). The trade-winds—steady, reliable, pushing westward—were the voyage's engine, and picking them up was a milestone of progress.

In the first hour of the voyage, the *[[spray|Spray]]* passed the wreck of the steamship *Venetian*, broken on a ledge near [[boston|Boston]]. [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] drew the moral:

> "So in the first hour of my lone voyage I had proof that the Spray could at least do better than this full-handed steamship." — (002-002)

The voyage was validated at its start: the little sloop, handled by one man, was already farther along than a fully crewed steamer that had come to grief.
