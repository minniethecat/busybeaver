The **deck** of a [[sail|sailing]] vessel is both a working platform and a structural element. On the *[[spray|Spray]]*, [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] built the deck himself from inch-and-a-half white pine, caulked and payed—a covering that kept the sea out and gave him a surface from which to handle [[sail|sails]], fish for cod, and watch the stars.

## Definition

A vessel's deck is the permanent covering over the hull, supported by deck beams and sealed at the edges against the hull planking. On small vessels, the deck may be a single flush surface from stem to stern; on larger ships, multiple decks are stacked. The *[[spray|Spray]]* had a single flush deck with a small cabin trunk rising three feet above it. The deck must be absolutely watertight—any leak through the deck means water in the cabin and, potentially, the hold. [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] noted "the deck I made of one-and-a-half-inch white pine" (001-011), thick enough to withstand seas breaking over it.

## Nautical Context

For the solo sailor, the deck is the entire world of action. Every sail change, every [[reefing|reef]], every anchor deployment happens on deck. A well-designed deck keeps the sailor safe: high bulwarks prevent being washed overboard; non-skid surfaces provide grip; jacklines offer attachment points for a safety harness. [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] had none of these modern aids—just the deck, his balance, and decades of experience.

The phrase "on deck" is both literal and metaphorical in [[sail|sailing]] literature—it means being on watch, being ready, being present. "My step was light on deck in the crisp air" (002-002) captures the physical and emotional state of a sailor leaving harbor.

## In *Sailing Alone Around the World*

The deck appears in the narrative as the stage for nearly every significant action. [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] built it himself:

> "The deck I made of one-and-a-half-inch white pine" (001-011)

It served him faithfully [[voyage|around the world]].

In [[gale|heavy weather]], the deck became a battleground. Off the [[azores|Azores]], "the deck, now as white as a shark's tooth" (004-011)—scoured clean by breaking seas. During gales, [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] would go "frequently on deck, but found all well" (003-009), the routine checks of a prudent mariner.

The deck also provided subsistence. Off [[nova-scotia|Nova Scotia]]: "I hauled till dark, landing on deck three cod and two haddocks" (002-011)—the deck serving as both fishing platform and dinner table.

Space was precious on a nine-[[tonnage|ton]] sloop. [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] noted drily: "There was not room on deck for more than the half of a boat" (002-008), explaining why he carried no tender. Everything—anchors, water casks, spare spars, lantern—had to be stowed on or under a deck smaller than a suburban living room.

The deck was also a social space. At the [[azores|Azores]], "the deck of [[spray|the Spray]] was crowded from morning till night" (003-019) with islanders curious about the tiny sloop. At [[juan-fernandez-island|Juan Fernandez]], visitors marveled at the deck that had carried one man across [[atlantic-ocean|the Atlantic]] and through [[strait-of-gibraltar|the Strait]] of [[ferdinand-magellan|Magellan]].

From the deck of stout ships in [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s earlier career, he had "accomplished my desire" (001-005) to become a master mariner. Now, from the deck of his own creation, he was fulfilling a greater ambition.
