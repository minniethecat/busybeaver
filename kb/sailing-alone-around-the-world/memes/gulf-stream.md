# Gulf Stream

The Gulf Stream is the great warm river of [[atlantic-ocean|the Atlantic]] — a current that flows from the Gulf of Mexico up the North American coast and across to Europe. For centuries, sailors have both courted and feared it. It shortens passages eastbound but brews its own weather. [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] encountered it at both ends of his voyage: seeking it as a friend off the American coast, and battling through its fury on the final approach home.

## Geography

The Gulf Stream originates in the warm waters of the Gulf of Mexico, squeezes through the Florida Strait, and flows northeast along the American coast before turning east toward Europe. Its warm core contrasts sharply with the colder surrounding ocean, generating fog, [[gale|storm]]s, and steep seas. In the North Atlantic, it is the defining oceanographic feature for any sailor crossing between America and Europe.

## In the Voyage

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s relationship with the Gulf Stream bookended his [[voyage|circumnavigation]]. Outbound, early in the voyage, he sought it eagerly: "as soon as possible the friendly Gulf Stream. The fog lifting before night" — (003-005). The warm current carried him east and south, shortening his passage toward the [[azores|Azores]].

Nearly three years later, returning from the south, the Gulf Stream was far less friendly. Off the American coast in June, the *[[spray|Spray]]* encountered "the turbulent Gulf Stream itself. She was jumping like a porpoise" — (021-006). The combination of a nor'wester and the warm current produced brutal conditions. Hailstones pelted the deck — "from the nor'west. In the Gulf Stream, thus late in June, hailstones" — (021-008) — an incongruous assault in what should have been summer weather.

> On this day there was soon wind enough and to spare. The same might have been said of the sea. The Spray was in the midst of the turbulent Gulf Stream itself. She was jumping like a porpoise over the uneasy waves.
>
> (021-006)

Later, reflecting on the armchair sailors who questioned his seamanship, [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] retorted: "They never crossed the Gulf Stream in a nor'easter, and they do not know what they are talking about" — (022-007). The Gulf Stream had become, in his mind, the ultimate test — the passage that separated those who had truly sailed from those who merely talked about it.

## Historical Context

By the 1890s, the Gulf Stream was well understood scientifically — Matthew Fontaine Maury had charted it in detail — but for practical sailors, it remained what it had always been: a force of nature to be respected. Steamers could power through it, but for a [[sail|sailing]] vessel, timing a Gulf Stream crossing was crucial. A wind against current produced some of the most dangerous seas in the world, and [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s experience in June — hail in summer, steep breaking waves — was classic Gulf Stream weather.
