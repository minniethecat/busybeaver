The **Visit to Robinson Crusoe Island** occurred on April 26, 1896, when the *[[spray|Spray]]* arrived at [[juan-fernandez-island|Juan Fernandez]] — [[pacific-ocean|the Pacific]] island where the mariner [[alexander-selkirk|Alexander Selkirk]] lived in solitude for four years, inspiring Daniel Defoe's *[[robinson-crusoe|Robinson Crusoe]]*. [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] spent several days trading tallow from his cargo for ancient coins, visiting [[alexander-selkirk|Selkirk]]'s lookout, and enjoying the island's idyllic simplicity (010-018) (011-001).

## Summary

Fifteen days out from [[cape-pillar|Cape Pillar]], at the western entrance to [[strait-of-magellan|the Strait]] of [[ferdinand-magellan|Magellan]], the *[[spray|Spray]]* made landfall at [[juan-fernandez-island|Juan Fernandez]]. "A thousand emotions thrilled me when I saw the island, and I bowed my head to the deck," [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] wrote. The islanders, led by the self-styled "king" Manuel Carroza — a Portuguese from the [[azores|Azores]] who had lived on the island for thirty years — rowed out to meet him, initially alarmed by the sight of [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s rifle. Once assured of his friendly intentions, they towed the *[[spray|Spray]]* to a safe anchorage (010-019) (011-001).

## Context

The *[[spray|Spray]]* had just completed the most harrowing leg of the voyage: [[strait-of-magellan|the Strait]] of [[ferdinand-magellan|Magellan]]. After repeated attempts to clear the strait — driven back six times by williwaws and [[gale|storm]]s — [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] finally reached [[pacific-ocean|the Pacific]]. [[juan-fernandez-island|Juan Fernandez]], approximately 400 miles west of [[chile|Chile]], was a natural stopping point after the ordeal (010-001).

The island was famous as the refuge of [[alexander-selkirk|Alexander Selkirk]], the Scottish mariner marooned there from 1704 to 1709, whose story became the basis for *[[robinson-crusoe|Robinson Crusoe]]*. For [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]], himself engaged in a solitary voyage, the island held special resonance.

## Narrative

### Landfall and First Contact

> "The blue hills of Juan Fernandez, high among the clouds, could be seen about thirty miles off. A thousand emotions thrilled me when I saw the island, and I bowed my head to the deck. We may mock the Oriental salaam, but for my part I could find no other way of expressing myself." — (010-019)

The next morning, a boat with six rowers approached. [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] picked up his rifle — intending only to stow it below — but the islanders, seeing the weapon, "quickly turned and pulled back for shore." After much signaling, they returned and were soon on board, "hungry for news" of the *[[spray|Spray]]*'s voyage (010-020).

### The King of Juan Fernandez

The leader of the islanders, Manuel Carroza, was known as "king" — "King only by courtesy. Having lived longer on the island than any other man in the world — thirty years — he was so dubbed." Carroza piloted the *[[spray|Spray]]* to a safe berth, though his exuberant [[helm|steering]] "so rattled [[spray|the Spray]] that I thought she would never carry herself straight again" (011-001).

> "Blessed island of Juan Fernandez! Why Alexander Selkirk ever left you was more than I could make out." — (011-003)

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] traded tallow — salvaged from a wreck in the strait — for the islanders' "ancient and curious coins, some of them from the wreck of a galleon sunk in the bay no one knows when." He later sold the coins to antiquarians "for more than face value" (011-002).

### Selkirk's Lookout

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] made a pilgrimage to the lookout where [[alexander-selkirk|Selkirk]] had watched for ships. He copied the inscription on a tablet placed there by Commodore Powell and the officers of H.M.S. *Topaze* in 1868, commemorating [[alexander-selkirk|Selkirk]]'s four years and four months of solitude (011-011). He also visited [[robinson-crusoe|Robinson Crusoe]] Bay and the cave [[alexander-selkirk|Selkirk]] inhabited — a site of pilgrimage for passing mariners.

## Aftermath

After his stay, [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] continued westward across [[pacific-ocean|the Pacific]]. The 72-day passage from [[juan-fernandez-island|Juan Fernandez]] to [[samoa|Samoa]] included a near-collision with a great whale — one of the few dramatic incidents of the long crossing (012-003). [[juan-fernandez-island|Juan Fernandez]] remained, in [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s memory, one of the most enchanting stops of the entire [[voyage|circumnavigation]] — an island of goats, ancient coins, and forty-five souls living "without the use of rum or beer of any sort" (011-003).

## Significance

The visit to [[juan-fernandez|Robinson Crusoe Island]] is one of the most richly literary episodes in [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s narrative. The solitary mariner paying homage to history's most famous castaway creates a moment of recognition across two centuries of seafaring. [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]], like [[alexander-selkirk|Selkirk]], was alone on the ocean — but unlike [[alexander-selkirk|Selkirk]], his solitude was chosen, and his voyage would end not in rescue but in triumph.
