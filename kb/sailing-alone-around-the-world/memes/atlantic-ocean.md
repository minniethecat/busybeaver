# Atlantic Ocean

The Atlantic Ocean is the second-largest ocean in the world. For [[joshua-slocum|Joshua Slocum]], it was both a familiar workplace from his merchant-captain years and the stage for the bookend passages of his [[voyage|circumnavigation]]: the outbound crossing from [[cape-ann|Massachusetts]] and the triumphant homeward return.

## In the Voyage

### First Crossing: Boston to Gibraltar (1895)

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s first Atlantic crossing of the voyage began from [[gloucester|Gloucester]], with the boisterous ocean ahead. He "sent the [[mast|topmast]] down, knowing that [[spray|the Spray]] would be the wholesomer with it [[deck|on deck]]," and "gave the lanyards a pull and hitched them afresh." (003-002) Even in summer, he knew, "one may meet with bad weather in the crossing." (003-002)

After reaching [[gibraltar|Gibraltar]], British officers warned him away from [[mediterranean-sea|the Mediterranean]]: longshore pirates made the Suez route too dangerous for a lone sailor. (005-002) [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] changed course: instead of east about through the canal, he would go west — back across the Atlantic to Brazil and around [[cape-horn|Cape Horn]]. (005-002)

He sailed from [[gibraltar|Gibraltar]] on August 25. A British tug towed [[spray|the Spray]] clear of the mount, "where her [[sail|sails]] caught a volant wind, which carried her once more to the Atlantic, where it rose rapidly to a furious gale." (005-002)

> I now stowed all my goods securely, for the boisterous Atlantic was before me, and I sent the topmast down, knowing that the *Spray* would be the wholesomer with it on deck. Then I gave the lanyards a pull and hitched them afresh, and saw that the gammon was secure, also that the boat was lashed, for even in summer one may meet with bad weather in the crossing.
>
> (003-002)

### South Atlantic and the Trade-Winds

Once clear of the coast, [[spray|the Spray]] "[[current|set]]tled down to the trade-winds and to the business of her voyage." (005-012) The [[trade-wind|southeast trades]] gave her a steady push toward Brazil.

On September 30, [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] crossed the equator in longitude 29° 30′ W. (005-023) By October 5 she made the land, "casting anchor in [[pernambuco|Pernambuco harbor]] about noon: forty days from [[gibraltar|Gibraltar]], and all well on board." (005-023)

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] noted the changing character of the sea: "having already crossed the Atlantic twice and being now halfway from [[boston|Boston]] to [[cape-horn|the Horn]], I should find myself still among friends." (005-024)

### Homeward Crossing (1898)

The final Atlantic crossing was the triumphant home stretch. After visiting [[st-helena|St. Helena]] and [[ascension-island|Ascension]], the trade-winds, "comfortably cool and bracing, sent her flying along on her course." (019-020) On May 8, 1898, [[spray|the Spray]] "crossed the [[course|track]], homeward bound, that she had made October 2, 1895, on the voyage out." (019-020)

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] felt the significance of the moment: "I felt a contentment in knowing that [[spray|the Spray]] had encircled the globe... 'Let what will happen, the voyage is now on record.' A period was made." (019-020)

## Trade-Winds and the Mariner's Ocean

Throughout the narrative, [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s relationship with the trade-winds is intimate and practical. Picking up the [[trade-wind|trades]] was "a notable event in every voyage," and "these winds followed her now for many thousands of miles, never ceasing to blow from a moderate gale to a mild summer breeze, except at rare intervals." (014-040)

His musings on ocean meteorology reveal a sailor's scientific curiosity: "The phenomena of ocean meteorology were interesting studies even here in the trade-winds. The sun, we all know, is the creator of the trade-winds and of the wind system over all the earth." (011-021)

Yet he also lamented the passing of an older maritime culture. "There are no poetry-enshrined [[cargo|freight]]ers on the sea now; it is a prosy life when we have no time to bid one another good morning." (005-012)
