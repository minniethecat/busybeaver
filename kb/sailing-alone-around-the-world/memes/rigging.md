**Rigging** is the collective name for the lines, wires, and hardware that support a vessel's mast (standing rigging) and control the [[sail|sails]] (running rigging). On the *[[spray|Spray]]*, [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s rigging was simple and robust—a single-masted sloop rig designed for one man to handle alone.

## Definition

Rigging divides into two categories. **Standing rigging**—stays and shrouds—holds the mast in place, resisting the forward, aft, and lateral forces of wind and sea. These lines are fixed, not adjusted during normal [[sail|sailing]]. **Running rigging**—[[halyard|halyards]], sheets, downhauls, and [[reef|reefing]] lines—moves constantly, used to hoist, trim, and [[reef|shorten sail]]s. The **[[halyard|throat-halyards]]** hoist the forward upper corner of a gaff-rigged sail; the **[[halyard|peak-halyards]]** hoist the after upper corner. When rigging is said to "give out," it means a line has parted under strain—a dangerous event at sea.

Setting up rigging ("[[current|set]]ting up the rigging") means tensioning the shrouds and stays to their proper tension using lanyards or turnbuckles. A vessel's rig must be periodically re-tensioned as lines stretch under load.

## Nautical Context

A [[sloop|sloop's]] rigging is simpler than that of a square-rigger or schooner. The *[[spray|Spray]]* had a single [[current|set]] of shrouds to support the mast laterally, a forestay for the jib, and a backstay to oppose the forestay's forward pull. All [[halyard|halyards]] ran to the deck or to the windlass, allowing [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] to raise and lower [[sail|sails]] without leaving the cockpit.

The rigging must be inspected constantly—chafe where lines pass through blocks, rust on wire splices, and wear at attachment points can lead to sudden failure. In [[gale|heavy weather]], [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] often noted the "slatting of the wind through the rigging" (021-008), the distinctive sound of a gale blowing through taut lines.

## In *Sailing Alone Around the World*

The *[[spray|Spray]]*'s rigging was tested early and often. [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] encountered a vessel with "much broken [[sail|canvas]] hung in his rigging, from the effects of a squall" (002-005) near the Grand Banks—a warning of what lay ahead.

After a rough Atlantic crossing, a friendly hand helped [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] "[[current|set]] up the rigging" at [[fayal|Horta]] in the [[azores|Azores]] (004-002). This was a turning point in the narrative:

> "This incident gave the turn to the voyage" (004-002)

At [[pico|Pico]], "after the rigging was mended, [[spray|the Spray]] stretched away" (004-003)—repair first, then onward. The pattern repeated throughout the voyage.

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s rigging was practical and improvised. In the River Plate, he made do with what he had: "Whatever I found to do about the rigging and the [[sail|sails]] to keep them all right, I did" (005-013). At [[rio-de-janeiro|Rio de Janeiro]], visiting captains "inspected [[spray|the Spray]]'s rigging, and each one contributed something to her outfit" (005-030)—the rigging becoming a shared project of the [[sail|sailing]] fraternity.

The most violent test came in a [[southern-ocean|Southern Ocean]] gale, where "her rigging began to give out. First the main-sheet parted, then the [[halyard|peak-halyard]]" (021-006). Even then, the rigging held enough to keep the mast standing:

> "It was the slatting of the wind through the rigging, and the slopping of the sea against the hull" (021-008)—sounds Slocum knew intimately from decades at sea.
