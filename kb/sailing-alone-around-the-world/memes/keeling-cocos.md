## Geography

The Keeling Cocos Islands are a remote coral atoll in the eastern [[indian-ocean|Indian Ocean]], located approximately 2,750 kilometers northwest of Perth, [[australia|Australia]]. The islands lie between the latitudes of 11° 50′ and 12° 12′ S, and the longitudes of 96° 51′ and 96° 58′ E (016-005). The southern group consists of several small islets surrounding a lagoon.

The atoll was discovered in 1608–9 by Captain William [[keel|Keel]]ing, then in the service of the East India Company (016-005). It later became a dependency of [[strait-of-gibraltar|the Strait]]s Settlements (016-020). North [[keel|Keel]]ing has no harbor, is seldom visited, and is of no importance, while the South [[keel|Keel]]ings are "a strange little world, with a romantic history all their own" (016-005).

## Slocum's Voyage to the Atoll

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] shaped his course for the Keeling Cocos Islands after departing from [[thursday-island|Thursday Island]], a distance of twenty-seven hundred miles across the [[indian-ocean|Indian Ocean]] (022-018). The run required precise navigation; as he noted, even in the final five hundred and fifty miles "it was necessary to be extremely careful in keeping a [[course|true course]] else I would miss the atoll" (016-002).

He arrived after twenty-three days at sea, having steered by hand for only three hours during the entire passage:

> I didn't touch the helm, for with the current and heave of the sea the sloop found herself at the end of the run absolutely in the fairway of the channel. You couldn't have beaten it in the navy! Then I trimmed her sails by the wind, took the helm, and flogged her up the couple of miles or so abreast the harbor landing, where I cast anchor at 3:30 p.m., July 17, 1897, twenty-three days from Thursday Island. (016-003)

The first sign of land was a white tern — called by the islanders the "pilot of [[keel|Keel]]ing Cocos" — that fluttered knowingly about the vessel (016-003). Seeing coconut trees standing out of the water ahead, [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] experienced a powerful emotional reaction:

> I slid down the mast, trembling under the strangest sensations; and not able to resist the impulse, I sat on deck and gave way to my emotions. To folks in a parlor on shore this may seem weak indeed, but I am telling the story of a voyage alone. (016-003)

## The Island Community

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] found a well-ordered society on the Keeling Cocos Islands, administered by the Ross family who had [[current|set]]tled there since Captain John Clunis-Ross took possession in 1814 (016-005). The atoll was home to about a thousand inhabitants who lived under the administration of [[strait-of-gibraltar|the Strait]]s Settlements (016-020, 016-025).

His first impression upon landing was of a healthy, thriving population:

> "The children have all come to welcome you," explained Mr. Ross, as they mustered at the jetty by hundreds, of all ages and sizes. The people of this country were all rather shy, but, young or old, they never passed one or saw one passing their door without a salutation. (016-010)

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] observed that the crime of infanticide appeared unknown there — a striking contrast to other places he had visited (016-010). The men of [[keel|Keel]]ing built "exquisitely modeled canoes," and [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] noted that "by far the best workmanship in boat-building I saw on the voyage was here" (016-020). The hum of the bandsaw and the ring of the anvil were heard from morning till night, a testament to the industry inherited from the first Scotch [[current|set]]tlers (016-020).

During his stay, [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] cleaned and painted the *[[spray|Spray]]* on the beach (016-001). On July 22, H.M.S. *Iphegenia* arrived with court officers on a circuit of inspection. Finding no grievances to try — "at the [[keeling-islands|Keeling Islands]] there had not been a grievance to complain of since the day that Hare migrated" — a dance was organized instead. "All that could get on their feet danced, while the babies lay in heaps in the corners of the room, content to look on" (016-020).

## History: Hare, Ross, and Prison Island

The early history of the atoll was shaped by the conflict between two rival settlers: Alexander Hare, a former governor of Borneo, and Captain John Clunies-Ross. Hare retreated before Ross until he found himself confined to the small island known as [[prison-island|Prison Island]], where he kept his forty wives in a castle "like Bluebeard" (016-008). The feud ended when the women deserted Prison Island and put themselves under Ross's protection; Hare then went to Batavia where he met his death (016-009).

## Departure from the Atoll

After cleaning and provisioning the *[[spray|Spray]]*, [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] loaded thirty tridacna shells as ballast, replacing three [[tonnage|tons]] of cement he threw overboard (016-022). He had timed his voyage carefully, noting he "had no wish to arrive off [[cape-of-good-hope|the Cape]] of Good Hope before midsummer, and it was now early winter" (015-029).

On August 22, the *[[spray|Spray]]* departed:

> Long before dark Keeling Cocos, with its thousand souls, as sinless in their lives as perhaps it is possible for frail mortals to be, was left out of sight, astern. Out of sight, I say, except in my strongest affection. (016-025)

## Significance

The [[keel|Keel]]ing Cocos stopover was one of [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s longer and most warmly remembered island stays during the [[indian-ocean|Indian Ocean]] crossing. The islands provided a safe anchorage and a place to refresh the *[[spray|Spray]]* before continuing westward. The community's well-ordered society, the children's welcome, and the Ross family's hospitality left a lasting impression. [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] later noted that a rat got into his vessel at [[keel|Keel]]ing Cocos, and another at [[rodriguez-island|Rodrigues]], causing him considerable trouble (019-011).
