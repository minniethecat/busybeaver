# Helm

The [[rudder|helm]] is the point from which a vessel is steered — most commonly a tiller (a lever attached directly to the rudder head) or a wheel (which turns the rudder via gears or ropes). To "take the [[rudder|helm]]" is to assume command of the vessel's course; to "lash the [[rudder|helm]]" is to fix it in position, trusting the vessel to steer herself. For [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]], the [[rudder|helm]] was the place where the *[[spray|Spray]]* revealed her most exceptional quality.

## Definition

On a small vessel, the tiller is the simplest and most direct form of [[rudder|helm]]. Moving the tiller to port turns the rudder — and thus the vessel — to starboard, and vice versa (a counterintuitive fact familiar to every novice sailor). The wheel, more common on larger vessels, uses mechanical advantage to turn the rudder with less physical effort. The *helmsman* is the person at the [[rudder|helm]], and *[[rudder|helm]] orders* follow traditional language: "[[rudder|helm]] a-weather" (to windward), "[[rudder|helm]] a-lee" (to leeward). A vessel that carries *weather [[rudder|helm]]* tends to turn into the wind; one with *lee [[rudder|helm]]* tends to fall away — the former is safer, the latter dangerous.

## Nautical Context

A well-balanced vessel requires only the lightest touch on the [[rudder|helm]], and a perfectly balanced vessel — like the *[[spray|Spray]]* — can sail for hours or even days with the [[rudder|helm]] lashed, maintaining her course without human intervention. This quality, rare in any vessel, was essential for a single-handed circumnavigator. It allowed [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] to sleep, cook, read, make repairs, and take sights while the *[[spray|Spray]]* sailed herself. The [[rudder|helm]], in [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s narrative, is almost always described in terms of how little it needed him — a measure of the vessel's excellence.

## In *Sailing Alone Around the World*

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s voyage was made possible by a [[rudder|helm]] that did not need a helmsman. The discovery came early, on [[atlantic-ocean|the Atlantic]] crossing: "I had been steering nearly all the night, when [[sloop|the sloop]], as if to show her perfection, took it into her head to go without the helmsman's aid" (003-005). It was a revelation.

When the fog closed in, he trusted the [[rudder|helm]]: "I lashed the [[rudder|helm]], and my vessel held her course, and while she sailed I slept" (003-006). In the loneliness of mid-ocean, he spoke "to an imaginary man at the [[rudder|helm]], 'How does she head, there?'" (003-008) — the [[rudder|helm]] as companion, the absent helmsman a creation of solitude.

The *[[spray|Spray]]*'s [[rudder|helm]] became almost autonomous: "now without attention to the [[rudder|helm]]. The wind was south; she was [[course|heading]] east" (003-009). When a dangerous sea approached, "a quick [[rudder|helm]] brought her to the wind" (004-002) — the [[rudder|helm]] responsive when needed, unobtrusive when not.

Even after a severe gale knocked [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] down with illness, he found "[[sloop|the sloop]] was still [[course|heading]] as I had left her, and was going like a racehorse" (004-011). The [[rudder|helm]] had held true through the night and the [[gale|storm]].

> The wind still freshening, I settled the throat of the mainsail to ease the sloop's helm, for I could hardly hold her before it with the whole mainsail set. A schooner ahead of me lowered all sail and ran into port under bare poles, the wind being fair. As the *Spray* brushed by the stranger, I saw that some of his sails were gone, and much broken canvas hung in his rigging, from the effects of a squall.
>
> (002-005)
