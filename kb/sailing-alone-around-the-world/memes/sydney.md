# Sydney

Sydney, the capital of [[newcastle|New South Wales]] and [[australia|Australia]]'s premier port, was one of the most significant stops of [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s Pacific passage. After weeks at sea, the harbor — one of the finest natural harbors in the world — offered safe anchorage, supplies, and the welcome of a maritime city that understood the significance of his voyage.

## Geography

Sydney Harbor (Port Jackson) is a drowned river valley, its indented shoreline providing countless sheltered anchorages. The harbor entrance — the Heads — is narrow but deep, and the city in [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s time was already a major Pacific port, connected by steamship and telegraph to the wider world.

## Arrival from Newcastle

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] did not sail directly into Sydney. He first made landfall at [[newcastle|Newcastle]], arriving "in the teeth of a gale of wind" under conditions that prompted the government pilot, Captain Cumming, to meet him at the harbor bar with a steamer to carry the vessel to a safe berth (013-010).

The reception at [[newcastle|Newcastle]] was generous: "All government dues were remitted" (013-010). After resting a few days, a port pilot with a tug carried the *[[spray|Spray]]* to sea again, and she made along the coast toward Sydney, arriving on October 10, 1896 (013-010).

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] anchored in a snug cove near Manly for his first night, where "the Sydney harbor police-boat giving me a pluck into anchorage while they gathered data from an old scrapbook of mine, which seemed to interest them" (013-011). Already, the story of the solo circumnavigator was attracting official curiosity.

## The Sydney Yachting Scene

Summer was approaching, and "the harbor of Sydney was blooming with yachts" (013-012). [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]], a lifelong sailor, took keen interest in the local boats: "The typical Sydney boat is a handy sloop of great beam and enormous sail-carrying power; but a capsize is not uncommon, for they carry sail like vikings" (013-013).

He observed all manner of craft — "from the smart steam launch and [[sail|sailing]] cutter to the smaller sloop and canoe pleasuring on the bay" (013-013). [[australia|Australia]]n boat-building culture impressed him: "If a boy in [[australia|Australia]] has not the means to buy him a boat he builds one, and it is usually one not to be ashamed of" (013-013).

## The Barrel Incident

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s time in Sydney was not without comic mishap. While entertaining visitors aboard the *[[spray|Spray]]*, an unexpected cloudburst sent guests scrambling for cover. "But, as ill luck would have it, a young gentleman of another party on board, in the full uniform of a very great yacht club, with brass but[[tonnage|tons]] enough to sink him, stepping quickly to get out of the wet, tumbled holus-bolus, head and heels, into a barrel of water I had been coopering, and being a short man, was soon out of sight, and nearly drowned before he was rescued" (013-012).

The irony was not lost on [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]: "It had been decided by his club that [[spray|the Spray]] could not be officially recognized, for the reason that she brought no letters from yacht clubs in America" (013-012). That a club which had declined to recognize the *[[spray|Spray]]* should have had a member unceremoniously dunked in her barrel struck [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] as a fine piece of accidental justice.

## Commodore Foy's Gift

Despite the club's bureaucratic reservations, the Sydney yachting community at large embraced [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] warmly. The most significant gesture came from Commodore Foy of the Johnstone's Bay Flying Squadron, who presented the *[[spray|Spray]]* with a new suit of [[sail|sails]].

"The [[spray|Spray]] shed her Joseph's coat, the [[mainsail|Fuego mainsail]], in Sydney," [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] wrote, "and wearing a new suit, the handsome present of Commodore Foy, she was flagship of the Johnstone's Bay Flying Squadron when the circumnavigators of Sydney harbor sailed in their annual regatta" (013-013). The [[australia|Australia]]ns "recognized" [[spray|the Spray]] as belonging to "a club of her own," and "with more [[australia|Australia]]n sentiment than fastidiousness gave her credit for her record" (013-013).

## The Mary of Sydney

Among the vessels [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] encountered in [[australia|Australia]]n waters was the *Mary* of Sydney, "a steam ferryboat fitted for whaling" commanded by a captain [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] described as "a genius, and an [[australia|Australia]]n genius at that, and smart" (013-018). The *Mary*'s crew had been recruited from a sawmill up the coast and "had not one of them seen a live whale when they shipped; but they were boatmen after an [[australia|Australia]]n's own heart, and the captain had told them that to kill a whale was no more than to kill a rabbit" (013-018).

## Departure and Return

Time flew fast those days in [[australia|Australia]]. On December 6, 1896, the *[[spray|Spray]]* sailed from Sydney, bound south toward [[melbourne|Melbourne]] and [[tasmania|Tasmania]] (013-014). [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s intention was to continue southward, but the [[australia|Australia]]n coast would draw him back.

After enduring a severe gale in [[bass-strait|Bass Strait]] that drove him into the shelter of [[devonport|Devonport]], [[tasmania|Tasmania]], [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] returned north. "Again at Sydney," reads the chapter [[course|heading]] for the next leg of the voyage, and on April 17, 1897, the *[[spray|Spray]]* arrived in Port Jackson once more, having completed a circuit through the [[bass-strait|Bass Strait]] and back (014-015).

## Significance

Sydney, alongside [[melbourne|Melbourne]], represents the Anglophone Pacific — British colonial cities that received [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] as a hero. The warmth of the Sydney reception, culminating in Commodore Foy's gift of [[sail|sails]] and the *[[spray|Spray]]*'s role as flagship of the annual regatta, affirmed that [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s voyage had become a matter of public celebration throughout the English-speaking world. His time in Sydney also provided essential rest, repairs, and resupply — the practical foundations without which the second half of his [[voyage|circumnavigation]] could not have proceeded.
