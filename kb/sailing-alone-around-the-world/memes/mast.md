A **mast** is a tall vertical spar, traditionally of wood, stepped (secured) into the keel or deck of a [[sail|sailing]] vessel, serving as the primary support for [[sail|sails]], [[rigging|rig]]ging, and often a lookout position. The *[[spray|Spray]]* carried a single mast—a New Hampshire spruce—making her a sloop, the simplest and most reliable [[rigging|rig]] for single-handed [[sail|sailing]].

## Definition

The mast is the backbone of a [[sail|sailing]] vessel's [[rigging|rig]]. It carries the weight and wind-load of the [[sail|sails]]; its height determines the size and shape of the sail plan; its strength must withstand the compression and bending forces transmitted by the [[rigging|rig]]ging. On a sloop, the single mast is stepped forward of amidships, carrying a mainsail aft and head[[sail|sails]] forward. A **topmast** is an upper section of the mast that can be struck (lowered) in [[gale|heavy weather]] to reduce windage aloft. The **masthead** is the top of the mast, used as a lookout position and a point for mounting navigation lights.

## Nautical Context

Masts must be strong yet flexible—a mast that is too [[rigging|rig]]id will crack; one that is too flexible will not hold the [[sail|sails]] properly. In [[gale|heavy weather]], lowering the topmast reduces the vessel's center of effort and windage, making her more stable. The mast also serves as a climbing point: sailors go aloft to [[reefing|reef]] [[sail|sails]], survey surroundings, or make repairs.

The phrase "before the mast" refers to the forward part of the vessel where ordinary sailors (as opposed to officers) berthed—a term that appears in [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s description of his early seafaring years.

When a vessel loses her mast ("dismasted"), she becomes powerless to sail and must [[rigging|rig]] a jury-mast (a temporary replacement) to reach safety. The *[[spray|Spray]]*'s mast was shortened during the voyage—a deliberate modification, not a casualty.

## In *Sailing Alone Around the World*

The *[[spray|Spray]]*'s mast was a focal point of [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s preparations and modifications. He described it with pride:

> "Then the mast, a smart New Hampshire spruce" (001-015)

This single spar carried the *[[spray|Spray]]* [[voyage|around the world]], through gales that made [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] fear for its survival. In [[atlantic-ocean|the Atlantic]], be[[current|set]] by a violent squall:

> "a squall struck her with such violence that I thought her mast would go" (004-002)

The mast held. [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s decision to send down the topmast before crossing [[atlantic-ocean|the Atlantic]] proved wise:

> "I sent the topmast down, knowing that the Spray would be the wholesomer with it on deck" (003-002)

This foresight—reducing weight and windage aloft before [[gale|heavy weather]]—shows the judgment of a master mariner. In the [[southern-ocean|Southern Ocean]], [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] climbed the mast to survey the wild seascape after passing through [[strait-of-gibraltar|the Strait]] of [[ferdinand-magellan|Magellan]]:

> "Then I climbed the mast to survey the wild scene astern" (007-005)

From the masthead, he could see the seas that Darwin had described from the deck of the *[[hms-beagle|Beagle]]*—"Any landsman seeing the Milky Way would have nightmare for a week" (007-005).

The most significant modification to the mast came at [[buenos-aires|Buenos Aires]], where [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] shortened both it and the bowsprit:

> "I unshipped the sloop's mast at Buenos Aires and shortened it" (006-021)

This was not damage repair but tactical optimization—reducing the [[rigging|rig]] for the [[gale|storm]]y [[southern-ocean|Southern Ocean]] ahead. A four-masted ship passed the *[[spray|Spray]]* near [[pernambuco|Pernambuco]] (005-016), a reminder of the contrast between the tiny [[sloop|sloop's]] single spruce spar and the towering [[rigging|rig]]s of commercial [[sail|sailing]] vessels.

The mast also served as a perch for seabirds: "occasionally one poised on the mast to look [[spray|the Spray]] over, wondering, perhaps, at her odd wings" (008-006). After thousands of miles, the mast was still sound—a testament to New Hampshire timber and [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s care.
