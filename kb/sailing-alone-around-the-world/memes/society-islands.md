The **Society Islands** are an archipelago in the South [[pacific-ocean|Pacific Ocean]], part of French Polynesia. The group includes Tahiti, the largest and most famous island, along with Moorea, Bora Bora, and several others. For [[joshua-slocum|Joshua Slocum]], the Society Islands marked a waypoint on his westward passage across [[pacific-ocean|the Pacific]] during his solo [[voyage|circumnavigation]].

## On Slocum's Route

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s Pacific crossing in the *[[spray|Spray]]* carried him from [[marquesas|the Marquesas]] through the central Pacific toward [[australia|Australia]]. After departing [[juan-fernandez-island|Juan Fernandez]] on May 5, 1896, he sailed before the [[trade-wind|trade winds]] for forty-three days to reach [[nukahiva|Nukahiva]] in the [[marquesas|Marquesas]] group [011-018—023]. He then continued westward through the island groups of the [[pacific-ocean|South Pacific]].

The *[[spray|Spray]]*'s route was later documented by Mr. Andrew J. Leach, a British colonial officer reporting to Joseph Chamberlain in July 1897:

> He arrived at the island on the 17th of July, twenty-three days out from Thursday Island. This extraordinary solitary traveler left Boston some two years ago single-handed, crossed to Gibraltar, sailed down to Cape Horn, passed through the Strait of Magellan to the Society Islands, thence to Australia, and through the Torres Strait to Thursday Island. (023-008)

The timing of [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s passage — arriving at [[thursday-island|Thursday Island]] on June 24, 1897 — suggests he would have been in the vicinity of the Society Islands in approximately May 1897, during the Southern Hemisphere autumn.

## The Pacific Crossing

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s voyage across the vast Pacific was a journey through the steady [[trade-wind|trade winds]]. "Nothing could be easier or more restful than my voyage in the trade-winds," he wrote (011-018). For a full month, his vessel held course without adjustment:

> For one whole month my vessel held her course true; I had not, the while, so much as a light in the binnacle. The Southern Cross I saw every night abeam. The sun every morning came up astern; every evening it went down ahead. I wished for no other compass to guide me, for these were true. (011-019)

The crossing brought him close to the rhythms of the sea. "To cross [[pacific-ocean|the Pacific]] Ocean, even under the most favorable circumstances, brings you for many days close to nature, and you realize the vastness of the sea" (011-020).

## Geographic Context

The Society Islands are divided into two groups: the Windward Islands (including Tahiti and Moorea) to the east, and the Leeward Islands (including Bora Bora and Raiatea) to the west. The islands are volcanic in origin, with high peaks, deep valleys, and barrier [[reefing|reef]]s enclosing lagoons — a topography familiar to [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] from his stops at other Pacific islands.

By the late nineteenth century, Tahiti was a regular port of call for vessels crossing [[pacific-ocean|the Pacific]]. The French colonial administration maintained order throughout the archipelago, and the legendary beauty of these islands had been celebrated by writers and navigators since their European discovery by Samuel Wallis in 1767.
