# Ocean Current

An ocean current is a persistent, directional flow of seawater. For the sailor, the current is an invisible force — unseen but felt in the vessel's drift off course, in the set toward a lee shore, in the unexpected speed (or lag) over ground. A favorable current is a gift, adding free miles; an adverse current is a tax, stealing hard-won progress. The navigator who fails to account for currents will find the vessel far from its expected position.

## Definition

Ocean currents arise from several causes: tidal currents (the horizontal flow of water as tides rise and fall), wind-driven currents (surface water pushed by prevailing winds), and thermohaline circulation (density-driven deep-ocean currents). Nearshore, tidal currents dominate and can reach several [[knot|knots]] in constricted passages. Offshore, the great current systems — the [[gulf-stream|Gulf Stream]], the Brazil Current, the Agulhas Current — set like rivers within the sea. Mariners estimate current set (direction) and drift (speed) by comparing dead-reckoning positions with celestial fixes; the difference is the current's vector. *Countercurrents* — flows running opposite to the main current — are common near continental margins and in equatorial regions.

## Nautical Context

Coastal currents are among the most dangerous phenomena a sailor faces. A strong current setting onto a lee shore can overw[[rudder|helm]] a [[sail|sailing]] vessel's ability to claw offshore. Currents running over uneven bottoms create [[tide|tide-race]]s and overfalls — confused, breaking seas that can stop a small vessel. Conversely, a knowledgeable navigator uses currents strategically: departing on the ebb to gain speed, hugging a countercurrent inshore to make progress against an adverse offshore flow. [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s experience in [[bay-of-fundy|the Bay]] of [[bay-of-fundy|Fundy]] (home to the world's highest tides and fiercest tidal currents) had prepared him for anything the world's oceans could offer.

## In *Sailing Alone Around the World*

From his very first night at sea, [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] contended with currents: "The current being southwest, against the wind, I felt quite sure I would find the *[[spray|Spray]]* still on the bank or near it in the morning" (002-011). His confidence came from knowing exactly how wind and current would interact.

In [[strait-of-magellan|the Strait]] of [[ferdinand-magellan|Magellan]], currents were ferocious: "a fierce current swept around the cape against us" (007-007), testing both vessel and sailor. Near [[tierra-del-fuego|Tierra del Fuego]], he observed "the ripples that a countercurrent had set in" (005-017) — reading the water's surface like a chart. At [[buenos-aires|Buenos Aires]], fortune favored him: "a gale of wind and a current so much in her favor that she outdid herself" (006-014) — wind and current combining to give the *[[spray|Spray]]* a memorable run.

Approaching [[cape-of-good-hope|the Cape]] of Good Hope, [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] noted a "current was experienced setting north" (006-002) — the Agulhas, one of the world's great currents, adding its strength to his passage.
