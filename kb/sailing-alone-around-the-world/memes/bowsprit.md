A **bowsprit** is a spar projecting forward from the bow of a [[sail|sailing]] vessel. It carries the forestay on which the jib is [[current|set]], extending the sail plan forward of the hull and improving the vessel's balance. On the *[[spray|Spray]]*, the bowsprit was a key structural element—and the scene of some of [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s most dangerous moments.

## Definition

The bowsprit is essentially a mast laid nearly horizontal, projecting beyond the stem. The forestay runs from the [[mast|masthead]] to the end of the bowsprit, and the jib is hanked onto this stay. The bowsprit thus determines how far forward the head[[sail|sails]] can be [[current|set]], affecting the vessel's [[rudder|helm]] balance. A longer bowsprit allows a larger jib but creates more leverage on the hull; shortening it reduces sail area and strain.

On a traditional sloop, the bowsprit is a fixed spar, not retractable. It is braced by the bobstay (running from the bowsprit end to the stem at the waterline) and whisker stays (running to the hull sides). Reaching the end of the bowsprit to handle the jib requires climbing out over the water—a routine but hazardous task in rough seas.

## Nautical Context

The bowsprit is not just a structural spar but a working platform. [[sail|Sail]]ors go out onto it to [[current|set]], [[reefing|reef]], or strike the jib. In [[gale|heavy weather]], a wave breaking over the bow can sweep a man off the bowsprit with no warning. [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s decision to shorten the *[[spray|Spray]]*'s bowsprit at [[buenos-aires|Buenos Aires]] was a deliberate safety measure for the [[gale|storm]]y [[southern-ocean|Southern Ocean]] ahead.

The bowsprit also serves as a vantage point. From its end, a sailor can look back at the vessel under sail—a perspective [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] sometimes took to survey the *[[spray|Spray]]*'s trim. The "smiling full moon rising apparently at the very end of the bowsprit" (003-005) is one of the book's most poetic images.

## In *Sailing Alone Around the World*

The bowsprit appears throughout the narrative—in moments of beauty, danger, and practical modification.

The most poetic passage comes early in [[atlantic-ocean|the Atlantic]] crossing:

> "Rising apparently at the very end of the bowsprit, was the smiling full moon" (003-005)

But beauty gave way to violence. Off the coast of [[spain|Spain]], a [[gale|storm]] tore the head[[sail|sails]]:

> "At 2 a.m. the jib was washed off the bowsprit and blown to rags and ribbons" (004-014)

At [[buenos-aires|Buenos Aires]], [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] made a critical alteration. He sawed off a section of the bowsprit to shorten it:

> "I reduced the length of the bowsprit by about five feet, and even then I found it reaching far enough from home" (006-021)

He kept the cutoff piece—and it nearly saved his life. In [[strait-of-gibraltar|the Strait]] of [[ferdinand-magellan|Magellan]], a massive wave broke over the boat:

> "Then the piece of bowsprit which I had sawed off at Buenos Aires, about the bight of the mainsail, stood up two men" (007-019)

The detached spar acted as a life-saving float—[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] clung to it as the wave passed.

The most terrifying bowsprit moment came in the [[indian-ocean|Indian Ocean]]. [[reefing|Reefing]] the jib at the bowsprit's end in rough seas:

> "While I was at the end of the bowsprit reefing the jib, she ducked me under three times" (018-003)

Each time the *[[spray|Spray]]* plunged, [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] went under—holding on by instinct and experience. This was solo [[sail|sailing]] at its most exposed.

In calmer waters, [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] [[rigging|rig]]ged a pole "on the bowsprit, with the sheets hauled flat" (022-008) for a light-weather sail, and reflected at voyage's end: "In [[south-america|South America]]n waters the bowsprit and boom were shortened" (022-011)—acknowledging how the vessel had been modified for survival.
