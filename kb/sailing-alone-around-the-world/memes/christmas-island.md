## Geography

Christmas Island, now known as Kiritimati, is a raised coral atoll in the central [[indian-ocean|Indian Ocean]], lying south of the island of [[java-steamship|Java]]. Its surface appears "evenly rounded from the sea to a considerable height in the center," presenting a remarkably smooth and regular outline. (015-049)

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] described the island's appearance as "smooth as a fish," noting that "a long ocean swell, rolling up, broke against the sides, where it lay like a monster asleep, motionless on the sea." A distinctive blowhole feature caught his attention: "there was a nostril, even, which was a blowhole through a ledge of rock where every wave that dashed threw up a shaft of water, lifelike and real." (015-049)

> On the 11th, with all sail set and with the spinnaker still abroad, Christmas Island, about noon, came into view one point on the starboard bow. Before night it was abeam and distant two and a half miles. The surface of the island appeared evenly rounded from the sea to a considerable height in the center. In outline it was as smooth as a fish, and a long ocean swell, rolling up, broke against the sides, where it lay like a monster asleep, motionless on the sea.
>
> (015-049)

## Role in the Voyage

On November 11, 1897, Christmas Island came into view one point on the [[starboard|starboard bow]] while [[spray|the Spray]] sailed with all [[sail|canvas]] [[current|set]], including the spinnaker. (015-049) [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] passed within two and a half miles of the island, observing its whale-like proportions. (015-049)

This was the first land [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] encountered after leaving [[thursday-island|Thursday Island]] and crossing the [[indian-ocean|Indian Ocean]] — a key waypoint on the final ocean crossing toward [[cape-of-good-hope|the Cape]] of Good Hope. The next day, one hundred miles southwest of Christmas Island, he noted "anti-trade clouds flying up from the southwest" signaling a winter gale near [[cape-of-good-hope|the Cape]] of Good Hope, and adjusted his course accordingly. (016-003)

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] had seen Christmas Island years earlier as a young seaman aboard the *Tawfore*, when the captain called out from the quarterdeck: "Go aloft there, one of ye, with a pair of eyes, and see Christmas Island." The captain's success in spotting the island became a shipboard legend; afterward, the chief mate — "terror of us ordinaries" — walked "very humbly to leeward altogether." (015-050)

## Historical Context

By the late nineteenth century, Christmas Island was a well-known landmark for vessels [[sail|sailing]] between [[pacific-ocean|the Pacific]] and Indian oceans. Its regular, smooth profile made it recognizable at a distance. The chapter [[course|heading]] for this leg of the voyage lists it as the terminal waypoint: "Across the [[indian-ocean|Indian Ocean]] — Christmas Island." (015-001)
