# Hurricane

A **hurricane** is an intense tropical cyclone — a rotating [[gale|storm]] system characterized by a low-pressure center (the eye), a closed low-level atmospheric circulation, and sustained winds of at least 64 [[knot|knots]] (74 mph, 119 km/h). Hurricanes form over warm ocean waters and are among the most destructive forces in nature. The term is used for [[gale|storm]]s in [[atlantic-ocean|the Atlantic]] and eastern Pacific; equivalent [[gale|storm]]s in the western Pacific are called typhoons.

## Definition

Hurricanes are classified by the Saffir-Simpson scale from Category 1 (minimal) to Category 5 (catastrophic), based on sustained wind speed. They form when sea surface temperatures exceed approximately 26.5°C (80°F), drawing energy from the latent heat of condensation. A mature hurricane features spiral rainbands rotating around a calm central eye, with the strongest winds and heaviest rain concentrated in the eyewall. At sea, hurricanes generate enormous waves and confused seas that can overw[[rudder|helm]] any vessel, regardless of size or construction.

## Nautical Context

For the sailor, a hurricane represents the ultimate test of seamanship and vessel seaworthiness. In the era before satellite weather forecasting, mariners relied on barometric pressure, cloud formations, and swell patterns to detect approaching tropical cyclones. The standard heavy-weather tactic — heaving to, running before the [[gale|storm]], or lying to a sea anchor — had to be chosen based on the vessel's position relative to the [[gale|storm]]'s [[course|track]] and the available sea room. [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] sailed an era when hurricane forecasting was in its infancy, and he navigated [[cape-of-good-hope|the Cape]] of Good Hope and [[cape-horn|Cape Horn]] — both regions notorious for [[gale|storm]]-force conditions — relying on seasonal timing and hard-won experience.

## In *Sailing Alone Around the World*

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s narrative uses "hurricane" sparingly but pointedly, always in contexts of extreme danger. Approaching the [[canary-islands|Canary Islands]] early in the voyage, he observed [[gale|storm]]-clouds gathering: "from the east, to all appearances, might come a fierce harmattan, or from the south might come the fierce hurricane. Every point of the compass threatened a wild [[gale|storm]]" (005-008). The anticipation of a hurricane — even one that did not fully materialize — drove him to immediate [[reef|reefing]] and an altered course.

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] observes the psychological effect of surviving a hurricane through a literary aside about "a writer who, after saying beautiful things about the sea, passed through a Pacific hurricane, and he became a changed man" (014-043). The remark underscores the sobering power of tropical cyclones to transform romantic notions of the sea into something far more fearful.

Speaking from experience off [[cape-of-good-hope|the Cape]] of Good Hope, [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] writes: "The stout ship I then commanded encountered only fierce hurricanes, and she bore them ill. I wished for no winter gales now" (015-029). This personal memory of hurricanes off [[cape-of-good-hope|the Cape]] — one of the world's most dangerous [[gale|storm]] corridors — shaped his decision to time the *[[spray|Spray]]*'s passage for the southern summer, when such [[gale|storm]]s were less frequent.

Even far from [[gale|storm]] centers, evidence of hurricanes reaches across the ocean: the [[keeling-cocos|Keeling Cocos Islands]] were "visited occasionally by the floating spar of some hurricane-swept ship, or by a tree that has [[current|drift]]ed all the way from [[australia|Australia]]" (016-005) — [[current|drift]]ing wreckage carrying the story of distant destruction.
