# Tide

The tide is the regular rising and falling of the sea — one of the most powerful and predictable forces a coastal sailor must contend with. A rising (flood) tide can lift a grounded vessel free; a falling (ebb) tide can leave it stranded. Tidal currents in narrow passages can exceed a [[sail|sailing]] vessel's speed, making the difference between a safe passage and disaster. For [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]], navigating without an engine, the tide was at once an ally, an adversary, and a clock by which all coastal movements were measured.

## Definition

Tides are caused primarily by the moon's gravitational pull, with a secondary influence from the sun. Most coasts experience *semidiurnal* tides (two high waters and two low waters per lunar day of 24 hours 50 minutes). The range — the vertical difference between high and low water — varies enormously: a few feet in the open ocean, but 40 feet or more in certain bays and channels (such as [[bay-of-fundy|the Bay]] of [[bay-of-fundy|Fundy]], which [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] navigated). Tidal currents (*tide-races*, *overfalls*) form where the tide flows through constricted passages, creating dangerous confused seas. Mariners consult tide tables giving the times and heights of high and low water at reference ports.

## Nautical Context

For the engineless [[sail|sailing]] vessel, the tide governs everything in coastal waters: when to depart (with the ebb carrying you out of harbor), when to enter (on the flood, to avoid being [[current|set]] onto a lee shore), and where to anchor (accounting for the fall of tide so the vessel does not ground). A tide-race — where the tidal stream runs violently over an uneven bottom — is one of the most dangerous features a sailor can encounter, capable of stopping a [[sail|sailing]] vessel dead or throwing it onto rocks. [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s *[[spray|Spray]]*, with her shoal draft and long keel, was particularly well-suited to working in tidal waters where deeper vessels dared not venture.

## In *Sailing Alone Around the World*

The tide appears early in [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s narrative. Rebuilding the *[[spray|Spray]]* on the beach at [[fairhaven|Fairhaven]], he worked "while she lay a tide or so on the hard beach" (002-007) — the tide [[current|set]]ting the work schedule, the vessel accessible only between high waters.

Departing on his voyage, [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] navigated "through the worst tide-race in [[bay-of-fundy|the Bay]] of [[bay-of-fundy|Fundy]]" (002-013) — [[bay-of-fundy|the Bay]] of [[bay-of-fundy|Fundy]] having the highest tidal range in the world, its tide-races legendary among mariners. The *[[spray|Spray]]* passed through safely.

> I slapped two coats of this paint on the bottom of the Spray while she lay a tide or so on the hard beach.
>
> (002-007)

At [[strait-of-magellan|the Strait]] of [[ferdinand-magellan|Magellan]], the tide became critical. Arriving at a narrow passage, [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] found "the tide was falling and she had already touched bottom" (006-002), forcing him to kedge off. Waiting in a cove, "wait for the coming tide was all I could do" (006-003). When the tide returned, it brought deliverance: the rising water lifted the *[[spray|Spray]]* and carried her through. Yet danger remained: "the tide now at low ebb, with oyster-[[reefing|reef]]s" (006-009) threatening the hull.
