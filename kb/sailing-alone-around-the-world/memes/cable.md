# Cable

A **cable** has two distinct nautical meanings: (1) a heavy rope, hawser, or chain used to anchor or moor a vessel — the *anchor cable* — and (2) a unit of distance at sea, the *cable length*, equal to one-tenth of a [[knot|nautical mile]] or approximately 185 meters (about 100 fathoms).

## Definition

In its primary sense, the **cable** is the connection between a vessel and its anchor — a substantial line that must withstand the strain of wind, current, and sea. Cable is traditionally measured in fathoms (one fathom = six feet), and standard anchor cable lengths ran from 75 to 120 fathoms for vessels of the *[[spray|Spray]]*'s class. The cable is paid out from a windlass or capstan when [[anchor|anchoring]], and hauled in when weighing anchor. In its secondary sense, a **cable** or **cable's length** is a measure of distance at sea: one-tenth of a [[knot|nautical mile]], commonly used to describe a vessel's proximity to land or another object.

## Nautical Context

The anchor cable is one of the most critical pieces of ground tackle aboard any vessel. It must be strong enough to hold the vessel against the forces of wind and sea, yet manageable enough to be handled by the crew — or in [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s case, by one man alone. Cable management involves the windlass (for heaving in), the hawse-pipe (where the cable passes through the bow), and careful coiling below [[deck|decks]]. When [[anchor|anchoring]], enough scope (ratio of cable length to water depth) must be paid out to ensure the anchor holds; too little scope and the anchor drags.

When used as a distance measure, a cable's length (one-tenth of a [[knot|nautical mile]]) is convenient for describing close-quarters navigation — how near a vessel is to shore, a [[reefing|reef]], or another ship.

> The log of my first day on the Atlantic in the *Spray* reads briefly: "9:30 a.m. sailed from Yarmouth. 4:30 p.m. passed Cape Sable; distance, three cables from the land. The sloop making eight knots. Fresh breeze N. W."
>
> (003-003)

## In *Sailing Alone Around the World*

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] records his departure from [[nova-scotia|Nova Scotia]] with a precisely measured distance from land: "4:30 p.m. passed [[cape-sable|Cape Sable]]; distance, three cables from the land. The sloop making [[knot|eight knots]]" (003-003). Here, "three cables" — about 550 meters — underscores the close, confident inshore navigation that characterized his voyage.

The cable as [[anchor|anchoring]] gear features prominently in [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s stranding on the [[uruguay|Uruguayan coast]]. After running hard aground, he attempted to kedge off using his dory, but "the frail dory, when the anchor and cable were in it, was swamped at once in the surf, the load being too great for her" (006-002). Forced to cut the cable and carry it in two loads, he ultimately succeeded: "The cable reached from the anchor in deep water to [[sloop|the sloop]]'s windlass by just enough to secure a turn and no more" (006-003) — a moment of relief after a near-disastrous grounding.

In [[strait-of-gibraltar|the Strait]] of [[ferdinand-magellan|Magellan]], a williwaw (sudden mountain squall) "carried [[sloop|the sloop]] off faster than I could pay out cable," forcing him to "man the windlass" and heave up "fifty fathoms of cable hanging up and down in deep water" (008-018), all through a long, exhausting night.

At [[port-angosto|Port Angosto]], [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] secured the *[[spray|Spray]]* thoroughly: "I moored her with two anchors and secured her besides, by cables to trees" (010-003) — an extra precaution in a region where sudden williwaws could tear a vessel from its anchorage.

The voyage concludes with the nautical image of final mooring: "I now moor ship, weather-bit cables, and leave [[sloop|the sloop]] [[spray|Spray]], for the present, safe in port" (021-021). The phrase evokes the sailor's ritual of making all secure at voyage's end.
