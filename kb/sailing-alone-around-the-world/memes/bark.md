## Definition and Rig

A sailing vessel of three or more masts in which the foremast and mainmast are square-rigged while the mizzenmast (aftermost mast) is fore-and-aft rigged. The bark rig represented an optimal compromise between the speed of a square-rigger running before the wind and the maneuverability of a fore-and-aft vessel working to windward. By the late 19th century, the bark was the dominant rig for long-distance cargo carriers, requiring fewer crew than a full-rigged ship while offering comparable passage times.

## In *Sailing Alone Around the World*

The bark figures in Slocum's story not merely as a rig he encountered at sea, but as the rig of the vessel he commanded before the *Spray* — the *Aquidneck*, the ship that shaped his experience as a deep-water captain.

Slocum's command of the *Aquidneck*, "a little bark which of all man's handiwork seemed to me the nearest to perfection" (001-004), was central to his identity as a mariner. The affection he felt for this vessel is evident in his personification of the *Spray* in similar terms — "I uttered aloud to my bark, passing fairylike silently down the bay" (002-002), addressing the sloop with the language of a bark captain. The "good angel" he sensed aboard the *Spray* at the voyage's outset (002-004) echoed his conviction that the same guardian had watched over the *Aquidneck*.

At sea, Slocum encountered other barks: "Sail ho! The sail was a barkantine, three points on the weather bow" (003-009), and later, "the sea smooth, and a large bark, hull down, came in sight on the weather beam" (003-010). These sightings were occasions for the professional appraisal of a lifelong sailor — reading another vessel's rig, judging her course and speed, and measuring his own progress against hers.
> Two Italian barks, which had been close alongside at daylight, I saw long after I had anchored, passing up the African side of the strait. The Spray had sailed them both hull down before she reached Tarifa. So far as I know, the Spray beat everything going across the Atlantic except the steamers.
>
> (004-017)


The bark rig also appears in passing references that locate Slocum within the 19th-century maritime world. The *Java*, a steamship whose captain he had a heated exchange with, is described in the context of sail-vs-steam tensions that defined the era. And the *Liberdade*, the canoe-like vessel in which Slocum returned from Brazil, echoed in miniature the bark's principle of mixed rigging adapted to purpose.
