## Geography

Cape Leeuwin is the southwesternmost point of the [[australia|Australia]]n continent, marking the junction of the [[indian-ocean|Indian Ocean]] and the [[southern-ocean|Southern Ocean]]. It is a key navigation landmark for vessels [[sail|sailing]] between the [[indian-ocean|Indian Ocean]] and the Great [[australia|Australia]]n Bight. (013-014)

In the summer months — December, January, February, and sometimes March — east winds are normally prevalent through [[bass-strait|Bass Strait]] and round Cape Leeuwin. However, during [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s voyage, a vast amount of ice [[current|drift]]ing up from the Antarctic disrupted the normal wind patterns, bringing "much bad weather" to the region. (013-025)

## Role in the Voyage

When [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] departed [[sydney|Sydney]] on December 6, 1896, his original plan was direct: "My intention was now to sail around Cape Leeuwin direct for [[mauritius|Mauritius]] on my way home." (013-014) He coasted south toward [[bass-strait|Bass Strait]] in that direction, expecting to round the cape and cross the [[indian-ocean|Indian Ocean]].

> Time flew fast those days in Australia, and it was December 6, 1896, when the *Spray* sailed from Sydney. My intention was now to sail around Cape Leeuwin direct for Mauritius on my way home, and so I coasted along toward Bass Strait in that direction.
>
> (013-014)

The plan changed after [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] reached [[tasmania|Tasmania]]. Owing to the unseasonable ice and bad weather, "I considered it impracticable to pursue the course farther." Rather than "thrashing round cold and [[gale|storm]]y Cape Leeuwin," he chose a different route: spending time in [[tasmania|Tasmania]], then [[sail|sailing]] north through [[torres-strait|Torres Strait]] and the [[great-barrier-reef|Great Barrier Reef]], taking advantage of anticyclones "which never fail." (013-025)

Cape Leeuwin was thus a waypoint [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] planned but never reached — a strategic decision that led him instead to the [[great-barrier-reef|Barrier Reef]], the [[bowen|Queensland]] coast, [[thursday-island|Thursday Island]], and eventually across the [[indian-ocean|Indian Ocean]] by a more northerly [[course|track]]. (013-025)

## Historical Context

Cape Leeuwin was first charted by Europeans in 1622 by the Dutch ship *Leeuwin* (Lioness), which gave the cape its name. By the late nineteenth century, it was a familiar landmark for vessels on the [[australia|Australia]]–Europe route, often the last [[australia|Australia]]n land sighted before the long [[indian-ocean|Indian Ocean]] crossing. The Cape Leeuwin [[beacon|lighthouse]] was completed in 1895, the year [[spray|the Spray]] was at sea, making the cape safer for navigation. (013-025)
