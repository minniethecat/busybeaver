**Pretoria** was the capital of the South African Republic (the Transvaal), a Boer republic in the interior of what is now South Africa. [[joshua-slocum|Joshua Slocum]] traveled there by rail during his three-month stay in South Africa in the austral summer of 1897–1898, visiting [[president-kruger|President Paul Krüger]].

## Slocum's Visit

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] reached Pretoria after leaving the *[[spray|Spray]]* in the Alfred Dry Docks at [[cape-town|Cape Town]], "where she remained for about three months in the care of the port authorities, while I traveled the country over from Simons Town to Pretoria, being accorded by the colonial government a free railroad pass over all the land" (018-007).

The journey took him first to Kimberley, then Johannesburg, and finally Pretoria. "The trip to Kimberley, Johannesburg, and Pretoria was a pleasant one. At the last-named place I met Mr. Krüger, the Transvaal president" (018-008).

## The Krüger Interview

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s meeting with [[president-kruger|President Krüger]] is one of the most famous vignettes of the entire voyage. When Judge Beyers, who presented [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]], mentioned that the captain was on a voyage *around* the world, the president took offense:

> "Mr. Krüger corrected the judge rather sharply, reminding him that the world is flat. 'You don't mean *round* the world,' said the president; 'it is impossible! You mean *in* the world. Impossible!' he said, 'impossible!' and not another word did he utter either to the judge or to me." — (018-008)

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] was delighted rather than offended: "The incident pleased me more than anything else that could have happened. It was a nugget of information quarried out of [[president-kruger|Oom Paul]], some of whose sayings are famous" (018-008).

Krüger's other recorded aphorisms included: "Of the English he said, 'They took first my coat and then my trousers.' He also said, 'Dynamite is the cornerstone of the South African Republic'" (018-008). These terse formulations captured the grievances and militant spirit of the Boer republic on the eve of the Second Boer War (1899–1902).

## The Flat-Earth Connection

Earlier, while still at [[durban|Durban]], [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] had been visited by three Boer scholars from Pretoria who were preparing a pamphlet to prove the flat-earth theory. The Krüger encounter confirmed that this belief extended to the highest levels of Transvaal leadership (017-023).

Yet [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] was careful to distinguish between the regime and the people: "Education in the Transvaal is by no means neglected, English as well as Dutch being taught to all that can afford both" (017-025). He predicted that "Real stubborn bigotry with them is only found among old fogies, and will die a natural death" (017-024).
