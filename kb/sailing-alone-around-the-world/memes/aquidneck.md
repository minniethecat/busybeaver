# Aquidneck

The *Aquidneck* was a merchant bark — a three-masted [[sail|sailing]] vessel — commanded by [[joshua-slocum|Joshua Slocum]] during his years in the South American trade. Her loss on the coast of Brazil in 1886, with a cargo of timber, was the greatest professional catastrophe of [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s career — and the event that [[current|set]] in motion the chain of circumstances leading to the *[[spray|Spray]]*'s voyage.

## Design and Construction

The *Aquidneck* was a bark — a vessel with square-[[rigging|rig]]ged fore and main [[mast|masts]] and a fore-and-aft [[rigging|rig]]ged mizzen. Barks were the workhorses of the late 19th-century merchant marine, combining the square-[[rigging|rig]]ger's downwind power with the fore-and-aft [[rigging|rig]]'s maneuverability. [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] commanded her in the Brazil trade, carrying timber and other cargoes between North and South America.

## In Slocum's Words

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s affection for the *Aquidneck* was unmistakable. Looking back on his career in the opening chapter of *[[sail|Sail]]ing Alone Around the World*, he placed her second only to the *[[northern-light|Northern Light]]* among his commands:

> "Afterward I owned and sailed the *Aquidneck*, a little bark which of all man's handiwork seemed to me the nearest to perfection of beauty, and which in speed, when the wind blew, asked no favors of steamers." — (001-004)

The *Aquidneck* was more than a vessel to [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] — she was an aesthetic ideal. In a career spent among ships of all sizes and [[rigging|rig]]s, he judged this "little bark" the most beautiful thing human hands had ever made. That praise, from a man who had commanded the magnificent *[[northern-light|Northern Light]]*, speaks to the *Aquidneck*'s exceptional qualities.

The memory of the *Aquidneck* resurfaced later in the voyage, when [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] anchored at [[maldonado|Maldonado, Uruguay]]. The warm reception he received there — eggs, strawberries, milk, fresh bread — stood in sharp contrast to an earlier visit: "This was a good landfall, and better cheer than I had found at [[maldonado|Maldonado]] once upon a time when I entered the port with a stricken crew in my bark, the *Aquidneck*" (006-008). The passing reference is characteristic of [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s narrative style — a brief, unelaborated allusion to the hardships of his merchant-captain years, which the reader is left to imagine.

## Loss and Aftermath

The *Aquidneck* was wrecked on the Brazilian coast — a total loss. [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]], his wife, and their two sons survived, but the cargo was gone, the vessel destroyed. For a merchant captain, the loss of a ship was a career-ending event. Insurance investigations, owners' recriminations, and the stain on one's professional reputation could make it impossible to find another command.

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s response was extraordinary. Stranded in Brazil with no ship and no prospects, he built a canoe — the *[[liberdade|Liberdade]]* — and sailed it with his family 5,500 miles back to the United States (001-004). The *Aquidneck*'s loss was the end of [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s career as a merchant captain — and the beginning of his career as a small-boat voyager.

## Legacy

The *Aquidneck* is the pivot in [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s life. Before her loss, he was a conventional merchant captain. After it, he became something else: a man who had sailed a home-built canoe across the ocean, a man who knew that a small boat, well handled, could go anywhere. The *[[spray|Spray]]* was the direct inheritor of that knowledge.
