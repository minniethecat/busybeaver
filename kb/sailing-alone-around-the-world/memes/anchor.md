An **anchor** is a heavy metal device, typically with flukes that dig into the seabed, used to hold a vessel in a fixed position. Throughout *[[sail|Sail]]ing Alone Around the World*, [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s anchors—and his decisions about when to weigh them—punctuate the entire voyage.

## Definition

In nautical practice, an anchor works by burying its flukes into the bottom, using the weight of the chain (or "cable") and the scope (ratio of chain length to water depth) to maintain a horizontal pull. A **sea-anchor** is a different device—a [[sea-anchor|drogue]] or [[sail|canvas]] cone streamed from the bow to hold the vessel's head to wind and sea in [[gale|heavy weather]], slowing [[current|drift]] without requiring contact with the seabed. A **kedge-anchor** is a lighter anchor used to warp (haul) a vessel out of a grounding or into a desired position.

## Nautical Context

Anchoring and weighing anchor are rituals that bracket every passage. The phrase "weigh anchor" means to raise the anchor from the bottom, freeing the vessel to get underway. Choosing an anchorage—sheltered water, good holding ground, sufficient depth—is a critical skill for any sailor. The dangers of anchoring include dragging (when the anchor fails to hold and the vessel [[current|drift]]s), fouling (when the anchor snags on rocks or debris), and parting the cable.

A sea-anchor serves a different purpose: streamed from the bow in a [[gale|storm]], it keeps the vessel's head pointing into the waves, reducing the risk of broaching (being turned broadside to the seas). [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] used one in [[atlantic-ocean|the Atlantic]] gale shortly after departure.

## In *Sailing Alone Around the World*

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s voyage begins with the act of weighing anchor at [[boston|Boston]]: "the time arrived to weigh anchor and get to sea in earnest" (002-002). From that moment, the anchor becomes a recurring marker of arrivals, departures, and emergency stops.

The *[[spray|Spray]]* carried a conventional anchor, a kedge-anchor, and a sea-anchor. The sea-anchor saw use early in the voyage, when [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] encountered a gale off the New England coast:

> "I put out a sea-anchor that would hold her head to wind. That was the safe play" (002-011)

This decision—heaving-to with a sea-anchor instead of trying to run before the [[gale|storm]]—reflects the prudent seamanship that characterized the entire [[voyage|circumnavigation]].

The kedge-anchor played a critical role when the *[[spray|Spray]]* grounded on the sands of [[uruguay|Uruguay]]. "I ran out a kedge-anchor and warp; but it was too late" (006-002) describes his attempt to haul her off after she touched bottom—an effort that, despite the anchor work, could not prevent the grounding.

Arrivals were marked by the act of casting anchor. At [[fayal|Fayal]] in the [[azores|Azores]]: "At 4:30 p.m. I cast anchor at [[fayal|Fayal]], exactly eighteen days from [[boston|Boston]]" (003-017). At [[gibraltar|Gibraltar]]: "she cast anchor at 3 p.m. of the same day" (004-016). At [[pernambuco|Pernambuco]]: "casting anchor in [[pernambuco|Pernambuco harbor]] about noon" (005-023). Each anchoring is a small triumph—another leg of the [[voyage|circumnavigation]] completed.

The anchor also appears in [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s reflections. He notes that his grandfather's "calamity made his was an anchor to him" (001-002), using the anchor as a metaphor for stability and rootedness—the same quality it provides a vessel at sea.

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] observed other vessels at anchor too: at [[gibraltar|Gibraltar]] "I had anchored at the old mole, among the native craft" (004-019), and encountered fishermen at anchor who gave him navigational advice—sometimes "a wrong course" (002-013).
