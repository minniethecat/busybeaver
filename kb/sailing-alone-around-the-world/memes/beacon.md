# Beacon

A beacon is a fixed navigational aid marking a coastline, a harbor entrance, or a hazard. Lighthouses are the most prominent form — towers with powerful lights visible for many miles — but beacons also include simple stone cairns, painted posts, and iron frameworks erected by sailors and communities. For the mariner approaching an unfamiliar coast, a beacon is the first sign of land, of human presence, and of safe passage.

## Definition

Maritime beacons are classified by their purpose: *landfall lights* mark the first point of a coastline seen by approaching vessels; *harbor lights* guide vessels into port; *hazard markers* warn of rocks, [[reefing|reef]]s, and shoals. Each beacon has a distinctive *characteristic* — its pattern of flashes (fixed, flashing, occulting, group-flashing) and color — so that mariners can identify it unambiguously at night. By [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s era, most civilized coasts had comprehensive networks of lighthouses, and the great lights — Eddystone, Bell Rock, [[sambro-light|Sambro]] — were famous among seamen.

## Nautical Context

A beacon's significance to the sailor extends beyond its practical function. After weeks at sea, a light on the horizon is a profound psychological event — proof that the navigation has been correct, that land exists where the chart promised, that the voyage has reached its waypoint. But the absence of an expected beacon is equally powerful: a light that should be visible but is not signals danger — the vessel may be off course, or the light may have failed. [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]], navigating without electronic aids, depended on beacons for confirmation of his position, and his narrative records both the relief of lights made and the anxiety of lights missed.

## In *Sailing Alone Around the World*

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s familiarity with the North Atlantic coast is evident in his early references: "[[sable-island|Sable Island]]. There are many beacon lights along the coast. [[sambro-light|Sambro Light]], on the ironbound coast of [[nova-scotia|Nova Scotia]]" (003-004) — he names them as a traveler names familiar inns.

In the remote [[strait-of-magellan|Strait of Magellan]], a different kind of beacon appeared: "a small beacon had been thrown up, showing that some ship had been here" (007-028). Not an official lighthouse — a cairn built by previous sailors, a message across time: *we were here, you are not alone*.

In the [[pacific-ocean|South Pacific]], [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] found beacons erected by missionaries: "each [[current|set]] one up as a beacon to others coming after. They were made of coral stone, whitewashed, and stood out against the dark green of the hills" (009-019) — beacons of faith as much as navigation.

Near [[tasmania|Tasmania]], danger: the *[[spray|Spray]]* "actually hit the beacon rocks to leeward" (010-011) — the beacon marking the hazard, not preventing the near-disaster but at least identifying the place of it. And approaching the [[indian-ocean|Indian Ocean]], a poet's reflection: "Poets have sung of beacon-light and of Pharos, but did any poet ever see such a light as the moon [[tonnage|ton]]ight?" (014-041) — the celestial beacon outshining all human ones.

> At noon on July 3 Ironbound Island was abeam. The *Spray* was again at her best. A large schooner came out of Liverpool, Nova Scotia, this morning, steering eastward. The *Spray* put her hull down astern in five hours. At 6:45 p.m. I was in close under Chebucto Head light, near Halifax harbor. I set my flag and squared away, taking my departure from George’s Island before dark to sail east of Sable Island. There are many beacon lights along the coast. Sambro, the Rock of Lamentations, carries a noble light, which, however, the liner *Atlantic*, on the night of her terrible disaster, did not see. I watched light after light sink astern as I sailed into the unbounded sea, till Sambro, the last of them all, was below the horizon. The *Spray* was then alone, and sailing on, she held her course. July 4, at 6 a.m., I put in double reefs, and at 8:30 a.m. turned out all reefs. At 9:40 p.m. I raised the sheen only of the light on the west end of Sable Island, which may also be called the Island of Tragedies.
>
> (003-004)
