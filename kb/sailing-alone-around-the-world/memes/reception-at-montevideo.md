# Reception at Montevideo

In December 1895, after the *[[spray|Spray]]* narrowly escaped shipwreck on the sands of [[Uruguay]], [[joshua-slocum|Joshua Slocum]] sailed for [[Montevideo]] and received a reception far warmer than he expected.

The [[uruguay|Uruguay]]an port captain dispatched a steam-tug to tow the damaged sloop to harbor; the agents of the Royal Mail Steamship Company docked and repaired her free of charge, adding a gift of twenty pounds sterling; the local newspapers covered her arrival; and on New Year's Day 1896, the editor of the *Standard*, Mr. Mulhall, welcomed [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] into his home.

This reception was [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s first experience of the public recognition that his voyage would increasingly attract, and it provided him with both material support and a morale boost for the long passage ahead.

## Summary

The *[[spray|Spray]]* arrived at [[montevideo|Montevideo]] after a harrowing experience on the [[uruguay|Uruguayan coast]], where she had been driven ashore by a gale and required the assistance of a German [[current|set]]tler and a local boy to be refloated. The port captain of [[montevideo|Montevideo]], learning of the incident, sent both a message of assistance and a steam-tug to bring [[sloop|the sloop]] in. The Royal Mail company's agents — Messrs. Humphreys & Co. — provided free dock repairs and a cash gift, a generosity that [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] later called "to the letter, and more besides" (006-012). The arrival was marked by steam-whistles and public attention, which [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] received with characteristic self-deprecation: "I felt embarrassed and wished that I had arrived unobserved" (006-011).

## Context

[[montevideo|Montevideo]] in the 1890s was a major South American port, the capital of [[uruguay|Uruguay]] and a key stop on the shipping routes between [[atlantic-ocean|the Atlantic]] and [[pacific-ocean|the Pacific]]. It had a substantial British expatriate community and strong commercial ties to [[london|London]]. The Royal Mail Steamship Company, which operated the mail packet service between Britain and South America, was one of the most prominent maritime enterprises in the region.

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s route from [[Rio-de-Janeiro]] to [[strait-of-magellan|the Strait]] of [[ferdinand-magellan|Magellan]] took him along the coast of [[uruguay|Uruguay]] — a stretch of shoreline that was treacherous for small vessels. The prevailing southwest gales could drive a ship onto the sandy beaches, and the tidal waves that accompanied these [[gale|storms]] made refloating difficult. The *[[spray|Spray]]*'s near-disaster on the [[uruguay|Uruguayan coast]] was a reminder that the voyage's dangers were not limited to the remote waters of [[cape-horn|Cape Horn]].

## Narrative

### The Port Captain's Intervention

News of the *[[spray|Spray]]*'s grounding traveled quickly along the coast. The port captain of [[montevideo|Montevideo]] acted with dispatch:

> "Early in the day a despatch came from the port captain of Montevideo, commanding the coastguards to render the Spray every assistance. This, however, was not necessary, for a guard was already on the alert, and making all the ado that would become the wreck of a steamer with a thousand emigrants aboard. The same messenger brought word from the port captain that he would despatch a steam-tug to tow the Spray to Montevideo. The officer was as good as his word; a powerful tug arrived on the following day." — (006-007)

The comparison to "the wreck of a steamer with a thousand emigrants aboard" is classic [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] humor: the fuss being made over his small sloop was, he felt, out of all proportion to the event.

### The Arrival

The *[[spray|Spray]]*'s entrance into [[montevideo|Montevideo]] harbor exceeded even the port captain's preparations:

> "Shortly after, the Spray sailed for Montevideo, where she arrived on the following day and was greeted by steam-whistles till I felt embarrassed and wished that I had arrived unobserved. The voyage so far alone may have seemed to the Uruguayans a feat worthy of some recognition; but there was so much of it yet ahead, and of such an arduous nature, that any demonstration at this point seemed, somehow, like boasting prematurely." — (006-011)

This passage reveals [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s characteristic modesty. At [[montevideo|Montevideo]] he had completed only [[atlantic-ocean|the Atlantic]] crossing — a substantial achievement in itself, but less than a quarter of the total voyage. The steam-whistle salute, while gratifying, felt premature to a man who knew that [[cape-horn|Cape Horn]] and the vast Pacific still lay ahead.

### The Royal Mail's Generosity

The most tangible support came from the British shipping company:

> "The Spray had barely come to anchor at Montevideo when the agents of the Royal Mail Steamship Company, Messrs. Humphreys & Co., sent word that they would dock and repair her free of expense and give me twenty pounds sterling, which, they did to the letter, and more besides. The calkers at Montevideo paid very careful attention to the work of making the sloop tight. Carpenters mended the keel and also the lifeboat (the dory), painting it till I hardly knew it from a butterfly." — (006-012)

The twenty pounds sterling — a significant sum in 1895, equivalent to several months' wages for a working man — was an unsolicited gift. The repairs to the keel, damaged in the grounding, were essential for the [[southern-ocean|Southern Ocean]] passage ahead. And the image of the dory — [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s small rowing boat — painted "till I hardly knew it from a butterfly" conveys both the quality of the work and [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s amused appreciation of the shipyard's thoroughness.

### New Year's Day with Mr. Mulhall

The reception extended beyond maritime circles. The editor of the *Standard*, one of [[montevideo|Montevideo]]'s leading English-language newspapers, had been following the voyage:

> "From Montevideo I had forwarded a letter from Sir Edward Hairby to the owner of the Standard, Mr. Mulhall, and in reply to it was assured of a warm welcome to the warmest heart, I think, outside of Ireland. Mr. Mulhall, with a prancing team, came down to the docks as soon as the Spray was berthed, and would have me go to his house at once, where a room was waiting. And it was New Year's day, 1896. The course of the Spray had been followed in the columns of the Standard." — (006-017)

The "prancing team" — a horse-drawn carriage in fine style — was a marked contrast to the salt-caked sloop and its solitary captain. The detail of the room already waiting suggests that Mulhall had been preparing for [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s arrival, [[course|tracking]] his progress through the newspaper columns. New Year's Day 1896, spent in the warmth of an Irish-[[uruguay|Uruguay]]an household, must have been a welcome contrast to the solitary Christmas [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] had spent refitting the *[[spray|Spray]]* at [[maldonado|Maldonado]].

## Aftermath

The Montevideo reception was the beginning of a pattern that would repeat throughout the [[voyage|circumnavigation]]: [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] would arrive at a port, often in damaged or depleted condition, and be received with hospitality and practical assistance by the local maritime community. The Royal Mail's free repairs meant the *[[spray|Spray]]* was in excellent condition for the passage to [[strait-of-magellan|the Strait]] of [[ferdinand-magellan|Magellan]]. The twenty pounds — supplemented by [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s own funds and later by the [[salvaging-tallow-at-langara|tallow salvage]] at Langara — provided a financial cushion.

The newspaper coverage, too, was significant. [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] was becoming a public figure, his voyage followed in the English-language press of South America and beyond. This attention was a double-edged sword: it brought support and recognition, but it also raised expectations. "There was so much of it yet ahead" — the words [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] wrote at [[montevideo|Montevideo]] would prove prophetic.

## Significance

The reception at [[montevideo|Montevideo]] stands as a testament to the goodwill that [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s voyage inspired among mariners and newspapermen alike. The generosity of the Royal Mail company — a commercial enterprise with no direct stake in the *[[spray|Spray]]*'s success — and the hospitality of Mr. Mulhall are reminders that the voyage, however solitary, was sustained by a network of supporters in ports [[voyage|around the world]].

The episode also marks a transition in the narrative: from [[atlantic-ocean|the Atlantic]] crossing, with its [[pirate-chase-off-gibraltar|pirate chase]] and gales, to the [[southern-ocean|Southern Ocean]], with its far greater dangers. [[montevideo|Montevideo]] was the last major port before [[strait-of-magellan|the Strait]] of [[ferdinand-magellan|Magellan]], and the warmth of the reception there was a welcome that [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] would not experience again until he reached [[pacific-ocean|the Pacific]] islands many months later.
