**Port Macquarie** is a coastal town and port at the mouth of the Hastings River in [[newcastle|New South Wales]], [[australia|Australia]]. Near this port, [[joshua-slocum|Joshua Slocum]] encountered the distressed cutter-yacht *[[cutter-yacht-akbar|Akbar]]* on May 13 during his southward voyage along the [[australia|Australia]]n east coast.

## Geography

Port Macquarie is situated at the entrance of the Hastings River where it meets the Tasman Sea, approximately 390 kilometers north of [[sydney|Sydney]]. The port is protected by a breakwater at the river mouth, with the town spreading along the southern bank of the river. The coastline here is characterized by long sandy beaches backed by coastal dunes and subtropical vegetation.

The region experiences a humid subtropical climate with warm, wet summers and mild, drier winters. In the 1890s, Port Macquarie was a modest coastal [[current|set]]tlement serving the surrounding agricultural district, with regular coastal steamer connections to [[sydney|Sydney]] and [[newcastle|Newcastle]]. The port's bar at the river entrance required local knowledge for safe passage, a hazard well known to coastal mariners of the period.

## The Akbar in Distress

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] had just finished reading "some of the most interesting of the old voyages in woebegone ships" when he approached Port Macquarie and sighted "a modern dandy craft in distress, anchored on the coast" (014-020). Standing in to investigate, he found the cutter-yacht *[[cutter-yacht-akbar|Akbar]]*, which had sailed from Watson's Bay about three days ahead of the *[[spray|Spray]]* and "had run at once into trouble" (014-020).

The crew of the *[[cutter-yacht-akbar|Akbar]]* was, in [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s assessment, dangerously inexperienced. He described the situation with characteristic wit:

> "It was a case of babes in the wood or butterflies at sea." — (014-020)

He described the owner as "all duck trousers" on his maiden voyage; the captain, "distinguished for the enormous yachtsman's cap he wore," had previously been a Murrumbidgee whaler; and the navigating officer "was almost as deaf as a post, and nearly as stiff and immovable as a post in the ground" (014-020). [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] concluded: "None of them knew more about the sea or about a vessel than a newly born babe knows about another world" (014-020). The *[[cutter-yacht-akbar|Akbar]]* was bound for [[new-guinea|New Guinea]], but [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] observed dryly that "perhaps it was as well that three tenderfeet so tender as those never reached that destination" (014-020).

## The Rescue Offer

Seeing their predicament, [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] called out with practical assistance: "Up anchor, and let me tow you into Port Macquarie, twelve miles north of this" (014-025). His offer reflected both seamanship and compassion — towing a disabled vessel twelve miles to the nearest safe harbor was the correct maritime response to vessels in distress.

## Historical Context

Port Macquarie was established in 1821 as a penal [[current|set]]tlement, one of the first outside the [[sydney|Sydney]] region. The location was chosen for its natural harbor at the Hastings River mouth and its distance from [[sydney|Sydney]], which made escape difficult for convicts. The penal [[current|set]]tlement closed in 1830, and the district was opened to free [[current|set]]tlers, developing into an agricultural and timber-producing region.

By the 1890s, Port Macquarie had transitioned from a convict station to a modest coastal town. The Hastings River bar remained a navigational challenge; vessels drawing more than a few feet required local pilots to cross safely. The incident with the *[[cutter-yacht-akbar|Akbar]]* illustrates the hazards that inexperienced yachtsmen faced on this coast — a reminder that even in the relatively [[current|set]]tled waters of [[newcastle|New South Wales]], the sea demanded competence and respect.
