# Mediterranean Sea

The [[mediterranean-sea|Mediterranean Sea]] occupies a peculiar place in [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s narrative — not as a place he visited, but as one he planned to visit and then chose not to. It was the original route home: through [[strait-of-gibraltar|the Strait]] of [[gibraltar|Gibraltar]], up the Mediterranean, and through the [[suez|Suez Canal]] into the [[red-sea|Red Sea]]. That he abandoned this plan in favor of the [[cape-of-good-hope|Cape of Good Hope]] says much about the strategic thinking of a lone sailor weighing risk against distance.

## Geography

The [[mediterranean|Mediterranean Sea]] is the enclosed basin between southern Europe, North Africa, and the Levant, connected to [[atlantic-ocean|the Atlantic]] by the narrow [[strait-of-gibraltar|Strait of Gibraltar]]. In the 1890s, its waters were among the busiest in the world — British, French, Italian, Ottoman, and Greek vessels crowded its shipping lanes, and piracy, though reduced from earlier centuries, had not entirely vanished.

## In the Voyage

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s original intention was explicit: he planned "to proceed up the Mediterranean Sea, through the [[suez|Suez Canal]]" — (005-002) on his passage home from the [[indian-ocean|Indian Ocean]]. This was the natural route — shorter than rounding Africa, and well-served by ports, supplies, and the infrastructure of empire.

> I perceived a felucca making out of the nearest port, and finally following in the wake of the Spray. Now, my course to Gibraltar had been taken with a view to proceed up the Mediterranean Sea, through the Suez Canal, down the Red Sea, and east about, instead of a western route, which I finally adopted. By officers of vast experience in navigating these seas, I was influenced to make the change. Longshore pirates on both coasts being numerous, I could not afford to make light of the advice.
>
> (005-002)

But circumstances changed his mind. In the [[strait-of-gibraltar|Strait of Gibraltar]], the *[[spray|Spray]]* was chased by a Moroccan pirate vessel, an experience that soured [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] on the Mediterranean route. The alternative — rounding the [[cape-of-good-hope|Cape of Good Hope]] — was longer but safer, and [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]], a man who trusted his instincts, chose the longer path.

He had come prepared for the Mediterranean, however. In one of the voyage's more reflective passages, [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] mentions having "[[sail|sailing]] directories for the Mediterranean" — (012-020), and writing notes on their flyleaves as he reconsidered his route. The directories — the Admiralty *[[the-pilot|Pilot]]s* that were the gold standard of [[navigation|maritime navigation]] — would have described every harbor, every current, and every hazard from [[gibraltar|Gibraltar]] to Port Said. That he chose not to use them is one of the voyage's quiet ironies.

## Historical Context

In the 1890s, the Mediterranean was a vital artery of the British Empire. The [[suez|Suez Canal]], opened in 1869, had transformed it from a regional sea into the highway connecting Britain to India, [[australia|Australia]], and the Far East. For a sailor like [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]], the Mediterranean offered a tested, charted, and well-provisioned route home. His decision to avoid it — prompted by the [[pirate-chase-off-gibraltar|pirate chase]] off [[gibraltar|Gibraltar]] — was a significant strategic choice, adding thousands of miles to his voyage but avoiding the shipping lanes, the political complexities of Ottoman waters, and the very real dangers that a lone sailor in a small boat might face among the great powers' navies.
