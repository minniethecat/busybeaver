# Sea Anchor

A sea anchor is a [[sail|canvas]] or fabric cone (or similar drag device) streamed from the bow of a vessel on a long warp (line) to create drag in the water. Its purpose is to hold the vessel's head into the wind and seas during [[gale|storm]] conditions, slowing [[current|drift]] and preventing the dangerous situation of a vessel turning broadside to breaking waves.

## Definition

Unlike a conventional anchor that grips the seabed, a sea anchor works by hydrodynamic drag in open water. It is typically a cone-shaped [[sail|canvas]] bag with a hoop at the mouth to keep it open, attached to a long line led from the bow. As the vessel is pushed backward by wind and waves, the sea anchor fills with water and resists, keeping the bow pointed into the weather. A tripping line allows recovery. The sea anchor is distinct from a *drogue*, which is streamed from the stern to slow the vessel while running before the wind — though in [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s era, the terms were often used loosely.

## Nautical Context

In the age of sail, the sea anchor was a critical piece of [[gale|storm]] equipment. When conditions became too severe to sail — wind above gale force, seas too high to make headway — the prudent mariner hove-to and deployed a sea anchor to ride out the [[gale|storm]]. For a single-handed sailor, the sea anchor had additional value: it held the vessel steady while the exhausted sailor rested below, trusting the arrangement to keep the ship safe through the night. The length and strength of the warp, the size of the sea anchor relative to the vessel, and the depth of water all factored into whether the tactic would succeed.

## In *Sailing Alone Around the World*

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] employed the sea anchor on his very first night at sea alone. After fishing on Cashes Ledge, he prepared for the night: "I put out a [[anchor|sea-anchor]] that would hold her head to windward. The current being southwest, against the wind, I felt quite sure I would find the *[[spray|Spray]]* still on the bank or near it in the morning" (002-011). The confidence in his last sentence — "I felt quite sure" — reflects a lifetime of knowing how a vessel behaves on a sea anchor. Next morning: "I [[reefing|reefed]] the [[sail|sails]], then hauled in my [[anchor|sea-anchor]], and [[current|set]]ting what [[sail|canvas]] [[sloop|the sloop]] could carry" (002-012), the routine complete.

Near the end of his voyage, in the North Atlantic, the sea anchor served again: "rounding to, with a [[anchor|sea-anchor]] ahead, she [[rigging|rig]]hted and faced the [[gale|storm]]" (021-008). The same device, the same maneuver — bookending the [[voyage|circumnavigation]]. The sea anchor was with him from Cashes Ledge to the final approach — a simple tool, but one that made single-handed ocean [[sail|sailing]] possible.
