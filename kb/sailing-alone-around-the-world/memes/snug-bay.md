# Snug Bay

Snug Bay is a small anchorage in the [[strait-of-magellan|Strait of Magellan]] near [[cape-froward|Cape Froward]], where [[joshua-slocum|Joshua Slocum]] anchored the *[[spray|Spray]]* during his passage through the strait.

## In the Book

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] arrived at Snug Bay at gray morning after [[sail|sailing]] all night to elude two canoes of [[fuegians|Fuegians]]. He anchored, only to find at daybreak that the same canoes had entered the bay stealthily under the shadow of the high headland. The savages were well armed with spears and bows. [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] fired a shot from his rifle across their bows, and both canoes turned aside into a small creek out of range (009-003).

Fearing he might be flanked by the savages in the bush close aboard, [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] was obliged to hoist the [[sail|sails]] and make for the opposite side of the strait, six miles away. At this moment he discovered the windlass had broken, and he could not [[anchor|weigh anchor]]. He [[current|set]] all sail and filled away, [[sloop|the sloop]] carrying her anchor and a [[tonnage|ton]] of kelp from a [[reefing|reef]] in the bay (009-003).

After clearing Snug Bay, [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] repaired the windlass and stretched across to a port of refuge under a high mountain, which he named Echo Mountain. At Echo Mountain, his own voice answered back from the cliff face. The anchorage was in nine fathoms, close under a perpendicular cliff (009-005).

## Significance

Snug Bay illustrates the constant tension of [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s passage through [[strait-of-gibraltar|the Strait]] of [[ferdinand-magellan|Magellan]]. Even in a place whose name suggests shelter, the threat of attack by [[fuegians|Fuegians]] was ever-present. [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s experience here — [[anchor|anchoring]] at dawn, only to be forced out by approaching canoes — was typical of his weeks navigating the strait, where every anchorage carried risk and every pause was provisional.

> This feeling was forcibly borne in on me at Snug Bay, where I anchored at gray morning after passing Cape Froward, to find, when broad day appeared, that two canoes which I had eluded by sailing all night were now entering the same bay stealthily under the shadow of the high headland.
>
> (009-003)
