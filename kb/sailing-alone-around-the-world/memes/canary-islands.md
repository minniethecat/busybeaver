# Canary Islands

The Canary Islands are a Spanish archipelago located in [[atlantic-ocean|the Atlantic]] Ocean approximately 100 kilometers off the northwestern coast of Africa. [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] sailed through the islands in September 1895 after departing [[gibraltar|Gibraltar]] and changing his planned route from the [[suez|Suez Canal]] to [[cape-of-good-hope|the Cape]] Horn passage. The islands figure in the chapter [[course|heading]]: "A comparison with [[christopher-columbus|Columbus]] — The Canary Islands — The [[cape-verde-islands|Cape Verde Islands]]" — linking [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s solo voyage to the historic westward route of [[christopher-columbus|Columbus]], who also passed through the Canaries (005-001).

## Geography

The Canary Islands consist of seven main islands of volcanic origin, stretching roughly east to west. [[fuerteventura|Fuerteventura]], the easternmost island, rises to 2,700 feet and is visible from many leagues away in fine weather (005-008). The channel between [[fuerteventura|Fuerteventura]] and the African coast served as [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s passage through the archipelago. The island of Lanzarote lies to the northeast, and Gran Canaria and Tenerife — the largest and most populous islands — sit in the center of the chain. [[st-antonio|St. Antonio]], the northwesternmost of the nearby [[cape-verde-islands|Cape Verde Islands]], was also sighted during this leg of the voyage.

## Role in the Voyage

### Approach and Arrival

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s arrival at the Canary Islands followed a harrowing escape from a Moorish pirate felucca off the coast of [[morocco|Morocco]]. After three days of squalls and shifting winds, "land-clouds rising ahead told of the Canary Islands not far away" (005-008). The approach was marked by rapidly changing and ominous weather:

> "Storm-clouds stretched their arms across the sky; from the east, to all appearances, might come a fierce harmattan, or from the south might come the fierce hurricane. Every point of the compass threatened a wild storm." (005-008)

Fearing the worst, [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] [[reef|reefed sail]]s hurriedly. "My poor boat was getting into a clump of perils, and I was afraid of a vessel from the land that might make an effort to board me," he wrote, still mindful of the recent pirate threat (005-008).

### The Channel Passage

The *[[spray|Spray]]* passed through the channel between Africa and [[fuerteventura|Fuerteventura]], the easternmost island, which stood abeam to starboard at a distance of about seven miles (005-008). The wind freshened overnight:

> "The wind freshened in the night, and the Spray had a fine run through the channel. By daylight, September 3, she was twenty-five miles clear of all the islands, when a calm ensued, which was the precursor of another gale of wind that soon came on, bringing with it dust from the African shore." (005-009)

This reddish-brown dust from the African shore was described by [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] as unusual for the season, though the dust-[[gale|storm]] was of short duration (005-009).

### Strategic Significance

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s route through the Canaries was part of a deliberate decision made at [[gibraltar|Gibraltar]]. Upon learning of cholera in the East and the dangers of the [[red-sea|Red Sea]], he abandoned his original plan to traverse the [[suez|Suez Canal]] and instead "resolved to go around [[cape-horn|Cape Horn]] after all" (005-002).

As he later reflected, "It was not at all strange in a life common to sailors that, having already crossed [[atlantic-ocean|the Atlantic]] twice and being now halfway from [[boston|Boston]] to [[cape-horn|the Horn]], I should find myself still among friends" (005-024). The Canary Islands passage — following the historic [[christopher-columbus|Columbus]] route — placed [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] on the classic Atlantic trade-wind [[course|track]] toward South America.

### The Columbus Comparison

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] explicitly drew comparisons between his voyage and that of [[christopher-columbus|Christopher Columbus]], who departed from the Canary Islands on his first westward crossing in 1492. By framing his own solo navigation within the context of [[christopher-columbus|Columbus]]'s historic expedition, [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] positioned the *[[spray|Spray]]*'s voyage as part of a grand tradition of Atlantic exploration (005-001). The Canary Islands thus serve both as a geographic waypoint and as a symbolic link between eras of seafaring.

## After Passage

After clearing the Canaries, the *[[spray|Spray]]* continued south toward [[cape-of-good-hope|the Cape]] Verde Islands. The weather remained unsettled with "a strong gale of wind" and "high dust" from the African shore (005-009). [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s next notable landfall would be the island of [[st-antonio|St. Antonio]] in [[cape-of-good-hope|the Cape]] Verde archipelago, followed by the long Atlantic crossing to [[pernambuco|Pernambuco]], Brazil.
