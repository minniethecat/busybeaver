# Ballast

Weight carried low in a vessel to lower her center of gravity, increase stability, and counterbalance the force of the wind on her [[sail|sails]].

## Definition

Ballast is any heavy material placed in the bottom of a ship's hull to improve stability and trim. In [[sail|sailing]] vessels, ballast is essential to prevent capsizing under press of sail: without adequate ballast, the heeling force of wind on [[sail|canvas]] would roll the vessel beyond her safe angle. Traditional ballast materials include stone, iron pigs, and lead, but [[spray|the Spray]] carried an unconventional alternative — three [[tonnage|tons]] of concrete cement, poured directly into her bilge and stanchioned securely in place.

## Nautical Context

In the late nineteenth century, ballast technology was evolving. Iron and lead keels were becoming common in racing yachts, but for working craft and small cruising vessels, internal ballast remained standard. Concrete cement offered several advantages: it could be poured to conform exactly to the hull shape, would not shift in heavy seas (a dangerous failing of loose stone ballast), and unlike iron, would not corrode in the bilge water. The [[spray|Spray]] carried no external ballast keel — [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] notes explicitly that "there was no iron or lead or other weight on the keel" (022-014), relying entirely on her internal concrete pour for stability.

The amount of ballast was calculated for the vessel's expected conditions. Too little, and she would be tender and prone to capsize; too much, and she would be sluggish and strain her timbers. [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s choice of three [[tonnage|tons]] of cement for [[spray|the Spray]]'s roughly nine-[[tonnage|ton]] displacement proved well-judged: [[spray|the Spray]] demonstrated extraordinary stability throughout her [[voyage|circumnavigation]], often [[sail|sailing]] herself with the [[rudder|helm]] lashed in conditions that would have required constant attention in a less balanced vessel.

## In *Sailing Alone Around the World*

The [[spray|Spray]]'s concrete ballast appears at two key moments in the narrative, each revealing something different about its role in the voyage.

At the [[keel|Keel]]ing (Cocos) Islands, [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] made a surprising decision. Having acquired a collection of enormous tridacna shells — the famous giant clams of the [[indian-ocean|Indian Ocean]] — he needed space and buoyancy. His solution was radical: "thirty of them taking the place of three [[tonnage|tons]] of cement ballast, which I threw overboard to make room and give buoyancy" (016-024). That [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] was willing to discard his ballast mid-voyage speaks to his confidence in [[spray|the Spray]]'s inherent stability and his practical seamanship — he judged that the shells themselves, properly stowed, would provide sufficient weight low in the hull.

The Appendix to the book provides the definitive technical description: "The ballast, concrete cement, was stanchioned down securely. There was no iron or lead or other weight on the keel" (022-014). This passage clarifies two points often misunderstood: the cement was not merely poured and left to cure — it was mechanically secured with stanchions — and [[spray|the Spray]] was a purely internal-ballast vessel, with no external keel weight of any kind. This configuration contributed to her ability to rise to following seas and surf effortlessly down steep wave faces, qualities [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] repeatedly praises in his narrative.

> "There was no iron or lead or other weight on the keel." — (022-014)
