## Geography

The Keeling Cocos Islands, according to Admiral Fitzroy, R.N., "lie between the latitudes of 11° 50' and 12° 12' S., and the longitudes of 96° 51' and 96° 55' E." (016-005). They are coral atoll islands in the Indian Ocean, distant from the Australian coast (015-029).

## Discovery and History

The islands were named after Captain Keeling, "their first notable visitor" (016-006), who discovered them around 1609. At the time of Slocum's visit, Keeling Cocos was a dependency of the Straits Settlements (016-020).

## Slocum's Visit

Slocum reached the Keeling Islands after a passage of twenty-three days, calling the approach a "delightful sail" (016-003). He described beating into Keeling harbor and lashing the helm (016-004). His stay at the islands was one of the highlights of his voyage; he declared that "if there is a paradise on this earth it is Keeling. There was not a case for a lawyer" (016-021).

During his visit, Slocum observed the local community, noting that "the women at the Keelings do not do all the drudgery" (016-022). He also collected a "monstrous mammoth tridacna shell of Keeling, found in the bayou near by" (016-024). By the fifteenth day out from Keeling, he had sailed one hundred and fifty miles on his way (016-026).

## A Curious Chapter

Slocum's chapter on the Keeling Islands (Chapter 16) is one of the most detailed port-of-call descriptions in the book, capturing the charm of this remote outpost and the hospitality of its residents, including the children who had been waiting for his arrival (016-010).

> The first unmistakable sign of the land was a visit one morning from a white tern that fluttered very knowingly about the vessel, and then took itself off westward with a businesslike air in its wing. The tern is called by the islanders the "pilot of Keeling Cocos."
>
> (016-003)
