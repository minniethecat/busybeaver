# Squall

A squall is a sudden, violent gust of wind — distinct from a sustained gale in its abrupt on[[current|set]] and relatively short duration. Squalls often arrive with visible warning (a dark line of cloud on the horizon, a white froth on the water) and demand immediate action: [[sail|sails]] must be shortened, the [[rudder|helm]] secured, and the vessel prepared for a brief but intense battering. For the solo sailor, a squall is a test of alertness — there is no watch-mate to call the warning.

## Definition

Meteorologically, a squall is defined as a sudden increase in wind speed of at least 16 [[knot|knots]], lasting for at least one minute. Squalls are associated with cumulonimbus clouds, cold fronts, and tropical weather systems. At sea, the approach of a squall is often heralded by a dark arch of cloud on the horizon, a sharp drop in temperature, and a line of disturbed water advancing toward the vessel. Mariners distinguish between *rain squalls* (with heavy precipitation), *white squalls* (with no visible cloud warning — especially dangerous), and *line squalls* (organized along a front). The critical seamanship response is universal: reduce sail immediately, before the squall strikes.

## Nautical Context

The danger of a squall lies not only in the wind itself but in its suddenness. A vessel carrying too much [[sail|canvas]] when a squall hits can be knocked down, [[mast|dismasted]], or driven onto a lee shore. The prudent sailor recognizes the approach of squally weather and [[reefing|reef]]s early. Squalls are particularly common in tropical waters, near capes, and along coastlines where heated landmasses generate convective activity. They are also frequent in the doldrums and intertropical convergence zone. For single-handers, the challenge is compounded: one pair of hands must [[reefing|reef]] [[sail|sails]], secure gear, and steer simultaneously.

## In *Sailing Alone Around the World*

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] encountered squalls in nearly every ocean, and his responses reveal a practiced, almost instinctive seamanship. Off the coast of [[nova-scotia|Nova Scotia]], early damage taught a lasting lesson: "the [[rigging|rig]]ging, from the effects of a squall" (002-005) needed repair — a reminder that even a brief squall could leave its mark.

Crossing [[atlantic-ocean|the Atlantic]], squalls came with the dawn: "squalls came up with the sun, and I was glad enough to get [[reefing|reef]]s in my [[sail|sails]] before I had gone far" (004-002). The timing is telling — not caught by surprise, but [[reef|reefing]] before the squall, reading the morning sky. On the African coast, a violent squall caused real damage: "The wave and squall that carried away my boom disabled [[sloop|the sloop]]" (005-003).

In the doldrums and trade-wind belts, squalls became a daily rhythm: "After three days of squalls and shifting winds I threw myself down" (005-007). Near [[cape-of-good-hope|the Cape]] Verde islands, conditions were "very squally, but I [[reefing|reefed]] her [[sail|sails]] snug" (005-014). And approaching the [[southern-ocean|Southern Ocean]], [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] braced for "a gloomy region, to battle with squalls and to be harassed by fitful [[gale|storm]]s" (005-018).

> I set sail from Horta early on July 24. The southwest wind at the time was light, but squalls came up with the sun, and I was glad enough to get reefs in my sails before I had gone a mile. I had hardly set the mainsail, double-reefed, when a squall of wind down the mountains struck the sloop with such violence that I thought her mast would go. However, a quick helm brought her to the wind.
>
> (005-003)
