# Visit to the S.S. Colombia

In March 1896, while anchored in [[fortescue-bay|Fortescue Bay]] during his return passage through the [[Strait-of-Magellan|Strait of Magellan]], [[joshua-slocum|Joshua Slocum]] made a night visit to the American steamship S.S. *Colombia*, commanded by Captain Henderson of New York.

The visit occurred between two tense encounters with the [[tierra-del-fuego|Fuegian]] outlaw [[encounter-with-black-pedro|Black Pedro]], and provided [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] with fresh provisions, moral support from fellow Americans, and a brief respite from the solitude and danger of [[strait-of-magellan|the Strait]].

The *Colombia*'s crew gave generously, refusing payment, and the encounter became one of the warmest human contacts in the entire [[voyage|circumnavigation]].

## Summary

The *Colombia* appeared in [[fortescue-bay|Fortescue Bay]] on the day after [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s second encounter with [[black-pedro|Black Pedro]] — a moment when the loneliness of the place was particularly acute. [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] recognized the ship's lines instantly as American: "I knew the sheer, the model, and the poise" (009-010). He flew his flag, and the Stars and Stripes were raised in response. That night, carrying all his guns in case he needed to fight his way back through hostile canoes, [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] went aboard the steamer. He discovered that the chief mate, Mr. Hannibal, was an old friend from [[philippines|Manila]] days. Captain Henderson gave a generous order to his steward, and when [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] offered his [[montevideo|Montevideo]] gold in payment, "the captain roared like a lion and told me to put my money up" (009-013). The *Colombia* departed the following morning, saluting the *[[spray|Spray]]* as she passed — a gesture of respect from a great steamship to a small sloop halfway [[voyage|around the world]].

## Context

The [[strait-of-magellan|Strait of Magellan]] in the 1890s was a major shipping artery for vessels [[sail|sailing]] between [[atlantic-ocean|the Atlantic]] and Pacific — a route that avoided the even more dangerous passage around [[Cape Horn]]. American steamships regularly transited [[strait-of-magellan|the Strait]] carrying passengers and cargo between the East and West Coasts. The *Colombia*, built for [[pacific-ocean|the Pacific]] Mail Steamship Company, was one of the premier vessels on this route.

For [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]], the appearance of an American steamer in the wilderness of [[tierra-del-fuego|Tierra del Fuego]] was more than a logistical opportunity. After weeks of solitary navigation among hostile [[tierra-del-fuego|Fuegians]], gales, and williwaws, the sight of a familiar flag and the sound of American voices were profoundly restorative. The visit also came at a critical moment: the *[[spray|Spray]]* was low on provisions after her long passage from [[Montevideo]], and [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] had just weathered a series of encounters with savages that had left him on edge.

## Narrative

### The Steamer Appears

On the morning after his second meeting with [[black-pedro|Black Pedro]], [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] observed a great steamship making for the anchorage. He recognized her immediately:

> "On the following day the loneliness of the place was broken by the appearance of a great steamship, making for the anchorage with a lofty bearing. She was no Diego craft. I knew the sheer, the model, and the poise. I threw out my flag, and directly saw the Stars and Stripes flung to the breeze from the great ship." — (009-010)

The exchange of flags across the gray waters of [[strait-of-magellan|the Strait]] was a moment of mutual recognition — the tiny sloop and the great liner acknowledging each other as countrymen at the far end of the world.

### The Night Visit

That night, [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] went aboard the steamer. He carried his guns — a precaution against the possibility of being attacked by canoes on the return trip to the *[[spray|Spray]]*. Aboard the *Colombia*, he found himself among friends:

> "Under the cover of darkness that night I went to the steamer, which I found to be the Colombia, Captain Henderson, from New York, bound for San Francisco. I carried all my guns along with me, in case it should be necessary to fight my way back. In the chief mate of the Colombia, Mr. Hannibal, I found an old friend, and he referred affectionately to days in Manila when we were there together, he in the Southern Cross and I in the Northern Light, both ships as beautiful as their names." — (009-012)

The reunion with Hannibal bridged [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s past as a merchant captain and his present as a solitary circumnavigator. The *[[northern-light|Northern Light]]* had been [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s finest command — a magnificent clipper ship in which he had [[current|set]] records. Meeting Hannibal in [[strait-of-magellan|the Strait]] of [[ferdinand-magellan|Magellan]], aboard another beautiful American vessel, was a reminder of the world [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] had left behind.

### The Captain's Generosity

Captain Henderson's response to [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s arrival was immediate and generous:

> "The Colombia had an abundance of fresh stores on board. The captain gave his steward some order, and I remember that the guileless young man asked me if I could manage, besides other things, a few cans of milk and a cheese. When I offered my Montevideo gold for the supplies, the captain roared like a lion and told me to put my money up. It was a glorious outfit of provisions of all kinds that I got." — (009-013)

The detail of [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] offering his "[[montevideo|Montevideo]] gold" — the money he had earned from the lecture-show at [[Montevideo]] and the repair work done by the Royal Mail company — and having it refused with a roar is one of the most vivid and affectionate portraits in the book. Henderson's generosity was not grudging charity but a fellow mariner's recognition of a remarkable voyage.

### The Salute

The following morning, the two vessels parted. The *Colombia* had agreed to blow her whistle if she was first under way:

> "It was agreed that the steamer should blow her whistle for me if first on the move. I watched the steamer, off and on, through the night for the pleasure alone of seeing her electric lights, a pleasing sight in contrast to the ordinary Fuegian canoe with a brand of fire in it. The sloop was the first under way, but the Colombia, soon following, passed, and saluted as she went by." — (009-014)

The electric lights of the steamer were a reminder of civilization — of New York, San Francisco, and the world of steam and steel that [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] had temporarily left behind. And the salute as the *Colombia* passed the *[[spray|Spray]]* was a gesture of respect: the great ship and the small one, both American, both under way at the bottom of the world.

> "Had the captain given me his steamer, his company would have been no worse off than they were two or three months later." — (009-014)

This cryptic remark refers to the fact that the *Colombia*'s sister ship, the *Colimbia*, was wrecked on the California coast a few months later — a reminder of the perils faced by even the largest and most modern vessels.

## Aftermath

The provisions from the *Colombia* sustained [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] through the remainder of [[strait-of-magellan|the Strait]] passage and beyond. The cheese, milk, and other stores were luxuries compared to the simple fare of pilot-bread and fish that had been his diet. But the greater gift was psychological: the encounter with countrymen who recognized and honored his endeavor renewed his resolve for the long Pacific passage ahead.

The visit also provided an inadvertent postscript. When the [[chile|Chile]]an gunboat *[[chilean-gunboat-condor|Condor]]* later passed through [[strait-of-magellan|the Strait]], its officers reported seeing [[black-pedro|Black Pedro]] and hearing his boast that he had visited the *[[spray|Spray]]*'s deck — which [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] knew to be false. The *Colombia*'s presence had, in its way, contributed to [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s safety: the savages had gone to the steamer first to beg, and the brief window of distraction had given him time to prepare for departure (009-011).

## Significance

The visit to the *Colombia* is a brief but luminous episode in *[[sail|Sail]]ing Alone Around the World*. Amid the privations of [[strait-of-magellan|the Strait]] — the gales, the williwaws, the hostile canoes, the carpet-tacks scattered [[deck|on deck]] — the steamer appears like a visitation from another world. The generosity of Captain Henderson and the reunion with Mr. Hannibal remind the reader that [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s voyage, however solitary, was not undertaken in a vacuum: it was witnessed, admired, and supported by fellow mariners who understood what it meant.

The episode also underscores the international character of the *[[spray|Spray]]*'s [[voyage|circumnavigation]]. An American sloop, built in Massachusetts, resupplied by an American steamer in [[strait-of-magellan|the Strait]] of [[ferdinand-magellan|Magellan]], carrying [[montevideo|Montevideo]] gold that a British company had given the captain — the voyage wove together a network of goodwill that spanned the maritime world.
