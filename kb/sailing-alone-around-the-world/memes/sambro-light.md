## Geography

Sambro Island is located at the entrance to [[halifax|Halifax]] Harbour, [[nova-scotia|Nova Scotia]]. The Sambro Island Lighthouse, established in 1758, is the oldest operational [[beacon|lighthouse]] in the Americas. The island and its light mark the last prominent navigational feature for vessels departing [[halifax|Halifax]] for the open Atlantic, situated approximately 6 kilometers southeast of the harbor mouth.

## The Departure from Nova Scotia

On the morning of July 3, 1895, [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] was in close under Chebucto Head light, near [[halifax|Halifax]] harbor, when he saw a large schooner come out of Liverpool, [[nova-scotia|Nova Scotia]], [[helm|steering]] eastward. (003-004) He [[current|set]] his flag and squared away, taking his departure from George's Island before dark to sail east of [[sable-island|Sable Island]]. (003-004) This was the beginning of the great Atlantic crossing — the first true ocean leg of the [[voyage|circumnavigation]].

As [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]], a [[nova-scotia|Nova Scotia]]n by birth, sailed past the coast of his native province, there must have been a particular poignancy. He was born on North Mountain, overlooking [[bay-of-fundy|the Bay]] of [[bay-of-fundy|Fundy]], and now he was leaving behind the familiar waters of his youth for the vast, unknown Atlantic. (001-002)

## In Slocum's Voyage

As [[spray|the Spray]] sailed away from the [[nova-scotia|Nova Scotia]] coast on the night of July 3, 1895, [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] watched the coastal lights disappear one by one. Sambro was the last light he saw before entering the open Atlantic:

> I watched light after light sink astern as I sailed into the unbounded sea, till Sambro, the last of them all, was below the horizon. (003-004)

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] gave Sambro a somber epithet, calling it "[[gibraltar|the Rock]] of Lamentations." This name was inspired by the tragedy of the White Star Line steamship *Atlantic*, which struck the rocks near Sambro Island on April 1, 1873:

> Sambro, the Rock of Lamentations, carries a noble light, which, however, the liner Atlantic, on the night of her terrible disaster, did not see. (003-004)

After Sambro faded below the horizon, only one more light was visible — the west end light on [[sable-island|Sable Island]], which [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] called "the [[sable-island|Island of Tragedies]]." (003-004) He noted that within recent years [[sable-island|Sable Island]] had been reduced from forty miles in length to twenty, and that of three [[beacon|lighthouse]]s built on it since 1880, two had already been washed away. (003-004) These grim observations [[current|set]] the [[tonnage|ton]]e for the beginning of the ocean passage.

## Historical Context

The wreck of the *Atlantic* was one of the worst maritime disasters of the 19th century. On April 1, 1873, the White Star Line steamship, approaching [[halifax|Halifax]] at the end of a transatlantic crossing from Liverpool, struck the rocky coast near Sambro Island in [[gale|heavy weather]]. Of the approximately 952 people aboard, over 500 perished, including many women and children. The tragedy was attributed to navigational error — the ship's officers mistook another light for Sambro — and the failure to sight the Sambro light in poor visibility.

For [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]], passing this grave site alone in his small sloop, the memory of the disaster was a stark reminder of the stakes of ocean navigation. The contrast is powerful: the great liner, with all its crew and modern equipment, had been destroyed on these same rocks that [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] now passed safely in a 37-foot sloop rebuilt with his own hands.

## Significance

The fading of Sambro Light below the horizon marks the true beginning of [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s ocean crossing. It was the last connection to the familiar world of the North American coast — the final point of reference before [[spray|the Spray]] was alone on [[atlantic-ocean|the Atlantic]]. The emotional weight of this moment, heightened by the memory of the *Atlantic* disaster, makes it one of the most memorable passages in [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s narrative. (003-004)

The grim catalog of maritime tragedies associated with this coast — the *Atlantic* at Sambro, the shifting sands of [[sable-island|Sable Island]] that swallowed [[beacon|lighthouse]]s and ships alike — underscores the courage required to [[current|set]] out alone. After Sambro, there were no more lights, no more land, until the [[azores|Azores]] rose above the sea eighteen days later.
