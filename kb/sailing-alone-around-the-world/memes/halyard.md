A **halyard** (or halliard) is a line used to hoist a sail, spar, or flag. The name derives from "haul yard"—the original purpose of raising the yard that carried a square sail. On a fore-and-aft [[rigging|rig]]ged sloop like the *[[spray|Spray]]*, halyards raise the gaff (the spar at the head of the mainsail) and the jib, and are among the most heavily loaded lines on the vessel.

## Definition

The **throat-halyards** hoist the forward end of the gaff (the "throat" of the sail, nearest the mast); the **peak-halyards** hoist the after end of the gaff (the "peak"). Together they control the shape and [[current|set]] of the mainsail. The **jib-halyards** hoist the jib up the forestay. On a vessel with a windlass (a horizontal [[windlass|winch]]), halyards can be led to it for mechanical advantage—[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] did this in [[gale|heavy weather]], noting "I took the throat halyards to the windlass" (009-021).

A halyard-block is the pulley through which the halyard runs at the [[mast|masthead]] or gaff end. When a halyard-block "breaks from the gaff" (021-006), the gaff falls uncontrolled—a serious casualty that can disable a vessel.

## Nautical Context

Halyards must be strong and regularly inspected. Because they bear the full weight of the sail and the wind load, a parted halyard means a sail comes down—not necessarily dangerous but disabling if it happens in a gale. [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] used a strop (a loop of rope or webbing) to hook the throat-halyards into the [[rigging|rig]]ging when [[reef|reefing]], taking the load off the halyard while he worked (002-008).

On a small sloop with a single-handed sailor, all halyards must lead to the cockpit or deck so they can be handled without leaving the [[rudder|helm]]. The *[[spray|Spray]]*'s halyards were "rove through ordinary ships' blocks" (022-012)—simple, serviceable, and replaceable at any port.

## In *Sailing Alone Around the World*

Halyards appear in the narrative most often in moments of crisis—when [[sail|sails]] must be raised or lowered quickly, or when gear fails under extreme load.

During the early Atlantic passage, [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] [[rigging|rig]]ged a simple solution for easing the main halyard: "by hooking the throat-halyards into a strop fitted for the purpose" (002-008), he could secure the gaff without holding the halyard under constant strain.

The halyard-block failure in the [[southern-ocean|Southern Ocean]] was one of the most serious equipment casualties of the voyage:

> "The peak halyard-block broke from the gaff. It was a blow to the sloop" (021-006)

Losing the peak halyard meant the gaff could not be properly hoisted—the mainsail would sag and lose efficiency. Repair at sea was essential to continue.

In [[gale|heavy weather]], [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] used the windlass to handle halyard loads beyond the strength of one man. During [[cape-of-good-hope|the Cape]] Horn passage:

> "I took them all in with the throat halyards, which I took to the windlass" (009-021)

He also used halyards creatively. At one anchorage, he hooked the throat-halyards to a dolphin (a mooring post): "accomplished by hooking the throat-halyards to one of his flippers" (004-013)—a whimsical description of securing the boat.

The most common halyard-action was the urgent response to a squall: "I sprang to the jib-halyards and downhaul, and instantly had the jib down" (005-003). Speed with the halyards meant safety.

At the voyage's end, [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] described the *[[spray|Spray]]*'s halyards simply: "The halyards were rove through ordinary ships' blocks" (022-012)—typical of his pragmatic, unpretentious approach to [[rigging|rig]]ging.
