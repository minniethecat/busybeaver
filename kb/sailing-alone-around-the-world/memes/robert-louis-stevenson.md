# Robert Louis Stevenson

Robert Louis Stevenson (1850–1894) was a Scottish novelist, essayist, and poet — author of *Treasure Island*, *Kidnapped*, and *The Strange Case of Dr Jekyll and Mr Hyde*. By the time of [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s voyage, Stevenson had died in [[Samoa]], where he had spent his final years. His grave on Mount Vaea became a pilgrimage site, and [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s visit — recounted with rare emotion — is one of the most reflective passages in the narrative. The meeting, at a grave, between the circumnavigator and the author of *Treasure Island* is a moment where literature and lived experience converge.

## Biography

Born in Edinburgh to a family of [[beacon|lighthouse]] engineers, Stevenson defied expectations by pursuing literature rather than the family profession. His poor health sent him traveling — to France, to California, and finally to the South Seas, where he [[current|set]]tled on the island of [[samoa|Upolu]] in [[Samoa]]. The [[samoa|Samoa]]ns called him *Tusitala* ("Teller of Tales"), and he became a fierce advocate for [[samoa|Samoa]]n [[rigging|rig]]hts against colonial exploitation. He died of a cerebral hemorrhage at age 44 on December 3, 1894, and was buried atop Mount Vaea, overlooking [[pacific-ocean|the Pacific]]. His own "Requiem" — carved on his tomb — became one of the most famous epitaphs in the English language.

## Role in the Voyage

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]], approaching [[Samoa]], thought of Stevenson with the affection of a reader (012-019) who felt he knew the author personally. He had read Stevenson's works at sea — the books stowed in the *[[spray|Spray]]*'s cabin, companions as constant as the [[compass]] (018-016). In Stevenson he found a kindred spirit: another man who had crossed oceans and understood their power, another wanderer who had left home for the far side of the world.

The visit to Stevenson's grave on Mount Vaea was an act of homage. [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] climbed the mountain alone and stood before the tomb (012-025), reading the famous "Requiem" carved into the stone — words that seemed to speak directly to a man who had spent years under that same "wide and starry sky":

> "Under the wide and starry sky, / Dig the grave and let me lie. / Glad did I live and gladly die, / And I laid me down with a will."

For [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]], who had navigated by the [[Southern Cross]] through countless Pacific nights, the words carried a personal weight that few visitors to the grave could match. He knew that sky — had watched it from the deck of the [[Spray]], alone on a vast ocean — and Stevenson's acceptance of death under it resonated with a sailor's fatalism.

## Significance

Stevenson's presence in the narrative elevates the voyage beyond mere adventure. [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]], the practical sea captain, reveals himself as a reader — a man whose imagination had been shaped by books as well as by the sea. The pilgrimage to Mount Vaea is a reminder that [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s [[voyage|circumnavigation]] was as much a journey through the world of stories as through the world of oceans. Stevenson gave [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] a vocabulary for wonder — and the grave on the mountaintop gave him a place to pay his respects.

The connection between [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] and Stevenson also illustrates a broader truth about the *[[spray|Spray]]*'s voyage: that the solitary sailor is never truly alone. He carries books, memories, and the voices of those who wrote about the sea before him.
