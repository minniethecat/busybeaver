The **mainsail** is the principal sail of a [[sail|sailing]] vessel, [[current|set]] on the [[mast|mainmast]] and providing the majority of the vessel's driving power. On the *[[spray|Spray]]*, a gaff-[[rigging|rig]]ged sloop, the mainsail was the largest and most critical sail—handled in virtually every weather condition from flat calm to gale force.

## Definition

In a fore-and-aft [[rigging|rig]]ged sloop like the *[[spray|Spray]]*, the mainsail is a quadrilateral sail [[current|set]] aft of the [[mast|mainmast]], with its head (top) attached to a gaff and its foot to a boom. The **throat** of the mainsail is the upper forward corner where the gaff meets the mast; the **peak** is the upper aft corner. [[reefing|Reefing]] the mainsail—shortening it by tying down part of the [[sail|canvas]]—is the primary method of reducing sail area in increasing wind. A **three-reefed mainsail** has been shortened three times, leaving only a small triangle of [[sail|canvas]]; a **close-reefed mainsail** is shortened as much as possible. A **square-mainsail** is a rectangular sail [[current|set]] square to the mast, which [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] improvised from a tarpaulin when his regular mainsail was destroyed.

## Nautical Context

The mainsail is the engine of a [[sail|sailing]] vessel. Its size, shape, and trim determine speed, balance, and the vessel's ability to point (sail close to the wind). The relationship between mainsail and [[jib]] trim is critical: too much mainsail relative to headsail creates weather [[rudder|helm]] (the vessel rounds up into the wind); too little creates lee [[rudder|helm]] (the vessel bears away). [[reefing|Reefing]] the mainsail is heavy, physical work—sweating up the [[halyard|halyards]] to raise the shortened sail requires hauling on lines with body weight, a demanding task for a single-hander.

The mainsail is also vulnerable: it can be torn by a sudden squall, chafed by rubbing against [[rigging|rig]]ging, or jibed accidentally (when the wind catches it from the wrong side and it crashes across). A torn mainsail in mid-ocean is a serious emergency—the vessel loses its primary means of propulsion and [[helm|steering]].

## In *Sailing Alone Around the World*

The mainsail is one of the most frequently mentioned items in [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s narrative, appearing in nearly every chapter. It was the sail he relied on, cursed, repaired, and reinvented.

The voyage began with a mainsail adjustment. On departure from [[boston|Boston]], [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] "[[current|set]]tled the throat of the mainsail to ease [[sloop|the sloop]]'s [[rudder|helm]]" (002-005)—a trim adjustment to make the vessel steer more comfortably. This attention to mainsail trim was constant throughout the [[voyage|circumnavigation]].

In [[atlantic-ocean|the Atlantic]], a squall struck with such violence that "I thought her mast would go. However, a quick [[rudder|helm]] relieved the danger. I had hardly [[current|set]] the mainsail, [[reefing|double-reefed]], when a squall struck with great violence" (004-002). The sequence is telling: [[reefing|reef]] first, then [[current|set]]—[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s cautious approach to [[gale|heavy weather]].

The most exhausting mainsail work came during a severe [[gale|storm]] when [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] suffered from cramps while [[reef|reefing]]:

> "Between cramps I got the mainsail down, hauled out the earings…" (004-003)

This passage captures the physical reality of single-handed [[sail|sailing]]—performing heavy labor while debilitated by pain, because there was no one else to do it.

In [[strait-of-gibraltar|the Strait]] of [[ferdinand-magellan|Magellan]], [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] adopted a conservative sail plan: "no more than a three-reefed mainsail and fore[[forestaysail|staysail]]; with these we hugged the high, rocky shore" (007-008). The deeply [[reefing|reefed]] mainsail gave him just enough power while maintaining control in the treacherous narrows.

The mainsail was destroyed in a [[gale|storm]] off the [[patagonia|Patagonian coast]]. [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] improvised a replacement:

> "I set a sail on the boom in place of the mainsail, which was torn to rags" (008-006)

He later fashioned a square-mainsail from a tarpaulin: "In the shape of a peak for my square-mainsail, the tarpaulin" (008-015), a remarkable piece of improvisation that kept the *[[spray|Spray]]* moving.

Off [[cape-of-good-hope|the Cape]] of Good Hope, the mainsail's condition was noteworthy enough that a stranger commented: "he added, as he glanced at the mainsail, 'hombre valiente'" (009-007)—"brave man," observing the patched and weathered sail that had carried the *[[spray|Spray]]* across two oceans.

The mainsail also gained a distinctive character. [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] named it the "Fuego mainsail" after [[tierra-del-fuego|Tierra del Fuego]], noting "she now wore her Fuego mainsail, which, like Joseph's coat, was of many colors" (012-005)—a sail so heavily patched and repaired that it resembled a patchwork garment.

One last mainsail drama occurred in the [[indian-ocean|Indian Ocean]] when "a flaw of wind caught the mainsail by the lee, jibed it over, and dear! dear! how nearly she capsized" (010-002). An accidental jibe—the mainsail crashing across from one side to the other—was one of the most dangerous events in [[sail|sailing]], capable of dismasting or capsizing a vessel.
