A **knot** has two essential meanings at sea. As a unit of speed, one knot equals one nautical mile per hour (approximately 1.15 statute miles per hour or 1.85 kilometers per hour). As a practical skill, knot-tying—the art of securing, joining, and manipulating rope—is fundamental to seamanship, and [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s narrative is filled with moments where the [[rigging|rig]]ht knot, properly tied, made the difference between safety and disaster.

## Definition

The speed "knot" derives from the chip log—a wooden board attached to a knotted line, thrown overboard from a [[sail|sailing]] vessel. [[sail|Sail]]ors counted the number of knots that passed through their hands in a measured time (traditionally 28 seconds), giving the vessel's speed in nautical miles per hour. Though [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] likely used a patent log (a mechanical instrument trailed astern), the terminology persisted, and his references to "eight knots" or "making six knots" place the reader in the specific world of [[sail|sailing]] measurement.

The practical knot—the tying of rope—encompasses a vast vocabulary: bowlines, hitches, bends, splices, and stoppers. A sailor's knot must hold under load, release when needed, and not weaken the line. The difference between a well-tied bowline and a slipped hitch can mean the difference between a sail that stays [[current|set]] and one that tears free in a gale.

## Nautical Context

Speed measurement was critical to navigation. Without a reliable speed estimate, [[navigation|dead reckoning]]—calculating position from course and distance traveled—was impossible. A single-hander like [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] could not stream and read the log as frequently as a crewed vessel, so his speed estimates were often approximate, based on experience and the vessel's feel. His repeated mention of specific speeds—"eight knots," "six knots"—reflects a professional sailor's habit of continuous observation.

Knot-tying as a skill permeates every chapter. [[reef|Reef]] points on the mainsail had to be tied securely with [[reefing|reef]] knots; [[halyard|halyards]] were made fast to cleats with proper hitches; the anchor rode was bent to the chain with a tested splice. [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]], having spent a lifetime at sea, performed these acts without comment—they were as natural as breathing—but his survival depended on them.

## In *Sailing Alone Around the World*

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] records his vessel's speed with the precision of a professional navigator, and these numbers convey the *[[spray|Spray]]*'s remarkable performance:

> "The sloop making eight knots. Fresh breeze." (003-003)

Eight knots for a nine-[[tonnage|ton]] sloop was exceptional—a speed that outpaced many larger vessels and earned the *[[spray|Spray]]* her reputation.

After the [[azores|Azores]] run, [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] reports with evident satisfaction:

> "The sloop had made ninety miles in the twenty-four hours" (004-011)

Ninety nautical miles in a day—an average of nearly four knots through all conditions—was solid passage-making for a small cruising vessel.

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s speed observations also served a navigational purpose. By comparing his estimated speed with subsequent position fixes, he could verify the accuracy of his [[navigation|dead reckoning]] and the vessel's leeway (sideways [[current|drift]]). In the doldrums, when [[sloop|the sloop]] "made no more than two knots in the light airs" (005-017), the data told him what any sailor knows: patience is the only response to calm.

The rope-work of knot-tying appears throughout the narrative, though rarely foregrounded. When [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] describes "[[reef|reefing]] the mainsail down" in a gale, he means tying in the [[reef|reef points]]—a series of short lines sewn into the sail—with [[reefing|reef]] knots that must hold through hours of pounding. When he "made fast" a line, he chose the appropriate hitch or bend for the load and conditions. When the *[[spray|Spray]]*'s [[rigging|rig]]ging chafed through, he spliced or knotted a repair.

One of the most dramatic knot-dependent moments came in [[strait-of-gibraltar|the Strait]] of [[ferdinand-magellan|Magellan]], when the *[[spray|Spray]]* dragged her anchor in a williwaw (a violent katabatic wind). [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] had to get another anchor over the side, and fast—the security of every line, every knot, every splice was literally a matter of life and death in that moment.
