A **jib** is a triangular sail [[current|set]] on the stay running from the foremast to the bowsprit, working ahead of the mast to provide drive and balance the vessel's [[rudder|helm]]. On the *[[spray|Spray]]*, the jib was one of the most frequently handled [[sail|sails]]—[[reefing|reefed]], downed, or [[current|set]] anew to match the changing conditions of [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s [[voyage|circumnavigation]].

## Definition

In [[sail|sailing]] [[rigging|rig]] terminology, the jib is the foremost headsail, hanked onto the forestay. Unlike a genoa (which overlaps the mast) or a [[forestaysail|staysail]] (which [[current|set]]s further aft), the jib is a working sail sized for everyday conditions. The **[[halyard|jib-halyards]]** are the lines used to hoist it; the **downhaul** pulls it down. A **flying-jib** is a light-weather sail [[current|set]] further forward, beyond the bowsprit, for additional speed in light airs. A **bobbed jib** is a jib [[reefing|reefed]] or shortened to a small triangle for [[gale|heavy weather]]—[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s standard [[gale|storm]] configuration.

## Nautical Context

The jib serves multiple functions beyond forward propulsion. It improves [[rudder|helm]] balance: a well-trimmed jib reduces weather [[rudder|helm]] (the tendency of the vessel to round up into the wind), making [[helm|steering]] easier. In [[gale|heavy weather]], "downing the jib" (lowering it quickly) is often the first response to a sudden squall, as it rapidly reduces sail area and lets the vessel round up safely. [[reefing|Reefing]] the jib—shortening it—is done by moving the tack attachment point or partially furling it.

In a sloop like the *[[spray|Spray]]*, the jib works in concert with the [[mainsail]] to create a balanced sail plan. Too much jib relative to the mainsail causes lee [[rudder|helm]] (the vessel wants to bear away); too little jib creates excessive weather [[rudder|helm]].

## In *Sailing Alone Around the World*

The jib was central to [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s heavy-weather tactics. The pattern appears clearly in the book: when a squall struck, the first move was always to handle the jib.

During the early Atlantic passage, a squall caught the *[[spray|Spray]]*. [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s reaction was instinctive:

> "I sprang to the jib-halyards and downhaul, and in a moment had the jib down" (004-003)

He then chose a [[gale|storm]] configuration: "[[reefing|double-reefed]] mainsail and whole jib instead, and [[current|set]] her on her course." This combination—shortened mainsail with full jib—was his balanced heavy-weather [[current|set]]up.

The jib also proved its worth in the gale off the Spanish coast. Watching another vessel struggle, [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] noted: "The gale was moderating, and I saw that he had not taken in the jib" (004-011)—observing that the other skipper had kept too much headsail aloft for the conditions.

The most dramatic jib-handling moment came off the [[patagonia|Patagonian coast]], where [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] climbed out onto the bowsprit in rough seas to [[reefing|reef]] the jib:

> "While on the end of it reefing the jib, I regretted that I had not stepped the mast" (006-021)

This was one of the most dangerous moments of the voyage—a solo sailor working at the extreme end of the bowsprit while the vessel plunged into heavy seas.

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] also carried a light-weather flying-jib. In [[pacific-ocean|the Pacific]], he noted: "I got out the flying-jib made at [[juan-fernandez-island|Juan Fernandez]]" (015-032), showing how [[sail|sails]] acquired at various ports along the route contributed to the *[[spray|Spray]]*'s evolving sail inventory.

At [[juan-fernandez-island|Juan Fernandez]], a [[chile|Chile]]an sailor "made a flying-jib for [[spray|the Spray]], taking her pay in wood" (011-002)—a glimpse of the barter economy [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] relied on to keep his vessel equipped.

The jib required constant repair. After the rough passage to [[gibraltar|Gibraltar]], [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] observed: "The [[spray|Spray]] wants a new jib. Construction and repair, they never end" (004-020)—a sailor's observation on the endless maintenance demanded by working [[sail|sails]].
