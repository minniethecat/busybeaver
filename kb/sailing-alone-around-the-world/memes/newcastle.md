**Newcastle** is a port city on the coast of New South Wales, [[australia|Australia]], at the mouth of the Hunter River, roughly 160 kilometers north of [[sydney|Sydney]]. In the 1890s, it was a major coal-exporting harbor and a busy shipping center.

For [[joshua-slocum|Joshua Slocum]], Newcastle was the point of arrival on the [[australia|Australia]]n continent — a welcome landfall after a grueling Pacific crossing.

## The Passage from Samoa

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] departed [[samoa|Samoa]] on August 20, 1896, bound for [[australia|Australia]]. The *[[spray|Spray]]* cleared the islands and immediately met strong [[trade-wind|trades]]: "she reeled off one hundred and eighty-four miles the first day, of which I counted forty miles of current in her favor" (013-007).

The route took him north of [[cape-horn|the Horn]] Islands and north of [[fiji|Fiji]] instead of south as he had planned, then "direct for New South Wales, passing south of New Caledonia" (013-008). The crossing tested the little sloop:

> The *Spray* had barely cleared the islands when a sudden burst of the trades brought her down to close reefs … Thence I sailed direct for New South Wales, passing south of New Caledonia, and arrived at Newcastle after a passage of forty-two days, mostly of storms and gales. [013-007—008]

## Storms at Sea

The passage was marked by severe weather. One gale near New Caledonia "foundered the American clipper-ship *Patrician* farther south" (013-009). A French mail-steamer making the same run from New Caledonia to [[sydney|Sydney]] was blown far off course:

> The *Spray* was all right, lying to like a duck. She was under a goose's wing mainsail, and had had a dry deck while the passengers on the steamer, I heard later, were up to their knees in water in the saloon. (013-009)

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] noted wryly that when the steamer's passengers arrived safely at [[sydney|Sydney]], "they gave the captain a purse of gold for his skill and seamanship in bringing them safe into port. The captain of the *[[spray|Spray]]* got nothing of this sort" (013-009).

## Arrival Under Storm

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s approach to Newcastle was dramatic. He made the land about Seal Rocks — where the steamship *Cather[[tonnage|ton]]* had been lost with many lives shortly before — and "was many hours off the rocks, beating back and forth, but weathered them at last" (013-009).

> I arrived at Newcastle in the teeth of a gale of wind. It was a stormy season. The government pilot, Captain Cumming, met me at the harbor bar, and with the assistance of a steamer carried my vessel to a safe berth. Many visitors came on board, the first being the United States consul, Mr. Brown. Nothing was too good for the *Spray* here. All government dues were remitted, and after I had rested a few days a port pilot with a tug carried her to sea again, and she made along the coast toward the harbor of Sydney, where she arrived on the following day, October 10, 1896. (013-010)

## A Harbor of Refuge

Newcastle's significance extended beyond [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s own experience. During his time in [[australia|Australia]]n waters, he encountered other vessels that had been damaged at sea and were "making for Newcastle under jury-[[sail|sails]]" (014-024). The port served as a haven where [[gale|storm]]-battered ships could refit and repair. One [[mast|dismasted]] vessel's owner declared: "We'll go back to Newcastle. We missed Newcastle on the way out" (014-026) — a testament to the port's importance as a safe harbor on a dangerous coast.
