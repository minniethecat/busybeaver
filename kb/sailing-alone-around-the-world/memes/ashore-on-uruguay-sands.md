After departing [[rio-de-janeiro|Rio de Janeiro]], the *[[spray|Spray]]* ran hard aground on the sandy coast of [[uruguay|Uruguay]]. [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] floated her with local help, but the grounding cost the vessel her shoe, part of her [[keel|false keel]], and other minor damage — all later repaired in [[montevideo|Montevideo]].

## Summary

While [[sail|sailing]] south from [[rio-de-janeiro|Rio de Janeiro]] toward the Río de la Plata, the *[[spray|Spray]]* was driven ashore on the sands of [[uruguay|Uruguay]] in the darkness. [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] found himself on a wild coast with his vessel high and dry. A local boy discovered the beached sloop at dawn, and a German [[current|set]]tler from a nearby ranch provided critical assistance in refloating her. The port captain of [[montevideo|Montevideo]] later dispatched a steam-tug to assist, though [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] had already sailed her off the beach by then. (006-001)

## Context

The *[[spray|Spray]]* had departed [[rio-de-janeiro|Rio de Janeiro]] after a pleasant stay, [[course|heading]] south toward [[montevideo|Montevideo]] and eventually [[strait-of-magellan|the Strait]] of [[ferdinand-magellan|Magellan]]. The coast of [[uruguay|Uruguay]] presented long stretches of sandy beach with few prominent landmarks. [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] was [[sail|sailing]] through unfamiliar waters, and a navigation error or adverse current put him onto the sand at Castillo Chicos, about seven miles south of the dividing line between [[uruguay|Uruguay]] and [[brazil|Brazil]] (006-003). The inhabitants of this region spoke Spanish and lived on scattered ranches.

## Narrative

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] had already been working hard before the grounding and was exhausted when he finally threw himself on the sand above the tide to rest. "I had already done enough work to tire a stouter man, and was only too glad to throw myself on the sand above the tide and rest; for the sun was already up, and pouring a generous warmth over the land" (006-003). But he soon learned he was not alone on this coast.

> "I had not been long on the shore when I heard the patter, patter of a horse's feet approaching along the hard beach, which ceased as it came abreast of the sand-ridge where I lay sheltered from the wind. Looking up cautiously, I saw mounted on a nag probably the most astonished boy on the whole coast. He had found a sloop!" — (006-003)

The boy was convinced the stranded sloop must be his by [[rigging|rig]]ht of first discovery. "It must be mine," he thought, "for am I not the first to see it on the beach?" He hitched his horse to [[sloop|the sloop]]'s bobstay and tried to haul her away, but "of course she was too heavy for one horse to move" (006-003). He did manage to drag [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s skiff some distance and concealed it behind a dune in tall grass. He was about to ride off for reinforcements when [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] revealed himself.

The boy, "bursting into a volley of questions — more than six Yankees could ask," wanted to know everything about the ship and its captain (006-003). [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]], in playful mood, told him "my ship is from the moon, it has taken her a month to come, and she is here for a cargo of boys" — a jest that nearly cost him, as the boy coiled his lariat ready to throw (006-003).

To reconcile the visitor, [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] offered to trade ship's biscuits for butter and milk. "On hearing this a broad grin lighted up his face," and the lad flew home, returning quickly with butter, milk, and eggs (006-004).

> "With the boy came others, old and young, from neighboring ranches, among them a German settler, who was of great assistance to me in many ways." — (006-004)

A despatch soon arrived from the port captain of [[montevideo|Montevideo]], commanding the coastguards to render every assistance. The port captain also promised to send a steam-tug. But [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]], with the help of "the German and one soldier and one Italian, called 'Angel of Milan'," had already floated [[sloop|the sloop]] and was [[sail|sailing]] for port under her own power before the tug arrived (006-004).

## Aftermath

> "The adventure cost the *Spray* no small amount of pounding on the hard sand; she lost her shoe and part of her false keel, and received other damage, which, however, was readily mended afterward in dock." — (006-004)

The *[[spray|Spray]]* reached [[montevideo|Montevideo]], where the agents of the Royal Mail [[steamship|Steamship]] Company, Messrs. Humphreys & Co., "sent word that they would dock and repair her free of expense and give me twenty pounds sterling, which, they did to the letter, and more besides" (006-012). Carpenters mended the keel and also the lifeboat, painting it "till I hardly knew it from a butterfly" (006-012).

## Significance

This grounding was one of the few accidents involving damage to the *[[spray|Spray]]* during the [[voyage|circumnavigation]]. It demonstrated the goodwill [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] encountered from both local inhabitants and officialdom — from the German [[current|set]]tler who helped refloat the vessel to the port captain who dispatched tugs and the Royal Mail company that repaired her free of charge. The event also illustrated [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s resourcefulness: rather than wait passively for the promised tug, he organized a local crew and sailed [[sloop|the sloop]] off the beach himself.
