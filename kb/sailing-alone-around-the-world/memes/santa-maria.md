# Santa Maria

The *Santa Maria* was the flagship of [[christopher-columbus|Christopher Columbus]]'s first voyage across [[atlantic-ocean|the Atlantic]] in 1492. Larger and slower than her companion caravels *[[pinta|Pinta]]* and *Niña*, she carried [[christopher-columbus|Columbus]] himself and served as the expedition's command vessel. She was wrecked on a [[reefing|reef]] off Hispaniola on Christmas Day 1492 — but by then, she had already made history.

## Design and Construction

The *Santa Maria* was a carrack (nao in Spanish) — a three-masted vessel with a rounded hull, high sterncastle, and square-[[rigging|rig]]ged fore and main [[mast|masts]]. She was probably about 60–70 feet [[deck|on deck]] and carried a complement of about 40 men. [[christopher-columbus|Columbus]] disliked her [[sail|sailing]] qualities, finding her slow and unwieldy compared to the caravels, but she was the largest and most imposing vessel of the expedition.

## In Context of the Voyage

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s reference to [[christopher-columbus|Columbus]] and his ships was a benchmark: "[[christopher-columbus|Columbus]] himself could not have held her more exactly on her course" (004-011). The *Santa Maria*, like the *[[pinta|Pinta]]*, belonged to the historical substratum of the narrative — the earlier ships whose voyages gave meaning and context to [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s own.

## Legacy

The *Santa Maria* is one of the most famous ships in history, her name inseparable from the European discovery of the Americas. No image or plan of her survives; all representations are conjectural. But her voyage changed the world, and every Atlantic crossing since — including [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s — has been in her wake.

> Columbus, in the Santa Maria, sailing these seas more than four hundred years before, was not so happy as I, nor so sure of success in what he had undertaken.
>
> (005-006)
