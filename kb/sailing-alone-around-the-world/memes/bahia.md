## Geography

Bahia (present-day Salvador) is a major port city on the northeastern coast of Brazil. Further south lies Bahia Blanca, a coastal city in Argentina near the Gulf of St. Matias. Both locations appear in [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s narrative of the South American coast.

## In the Narrative

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] mentions Bahia in the context of the Brazilian naval war of the 1890s. The Brazilian navy had sunk one of their own ships, the *[[destroyer|Destroyer]]*, at Bahia during their conflict (005-025). This incident was personal for [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]], as the *[[destroyer|Destroyer]]* was a ship he had once commanded.

> The Destroyer was in many ways the better ship of the two, but the Brazilians in their curious war sank her themselves at Bahia. With her sank my hope of recovering some money I had invested in her.
> (005-025)

## The Destroyer Connection

The sinking of the *[[destroyer|Destroyer]]* at Bahia represented a personal financial loss for [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]. He had invested money in the vessel and had once commanded her — a connection that lent a bitter edge to his treatment by Brazilian authorities when he later arrived at [[pernambuco|Pernambuco]]. [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] later learned that "the top of her smokestack being awash in Bahia, it was more than likely that she rested on the bottom there" (005-029), a detail confirming the finality of the ship's fate and his financial hopes alike.

This personal connection to the Brazilian naval conflict explains [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s persistent wariness of Brazilian officialdom throughout his South American passage. The loss of the *[[destroyer|Destroyer]]* was not merely a footnote but a lens through which he viewed subsequent encounters with Brazilian authorities.

## The Gunpowder Offer

While at [[pernambuco|Pernambuco]], a merchant named Mr. Lungrin offered to [[cargo|freight]] the *[[spray|Spray]]* with a cargo of gunpowder for Bahia, which would have put [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] in funds. However, the insurance companies refused to take the risk on cargo shipped on a vessel manned by a crew of only one (005-028). Lungrin then offered to ship it without insurance, taking all the risk himself — a remarkable gesture of confidence in [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s seamanship that the circumnavigator ultimately declined.

> Mr. Lungrin, a merchant, to help me out of the trifling difficulty, offered to freight the Spray with a cargo of gunpowder for Bahia, which would have put me in funds; and when the insurance companies refused to take the risk on cargo shipped on a vessel manned by a crew of only one, he offered to ship it without insurance, taking all the risk himself.
> (005-028)

## Coastal Navigation

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] passed Bahia Blanca safely, along with the Gulf of St. Matias and the Gulf of St. George, while navigating southward along the Argentine coast (007-005). He gave all capes a berth of about fifty miles to avoid the destructive [[tide|tide-race]]s that were the dread of vessels large and small along that coast.

## Significance

Bahia marks a nexus of [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s personal history — his former command, his financial stake, and his brush with Brazilian naval politics — intersecting with his present [[voyage|circumnavigation]]. The gunpowder episode further illustrates the trust that [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] inspired in merchants who knew him personally, even when insurance underwriters balked at the risks of single-handed [[sail|sailing]].
