# Keeling Islands

The Keeling Islands (also known as the Cocos or Cocos-Keeling Islands) are a pair of coral atolls (001-008) in the eastern [[indian-ocean|Indian Ocean]] — 27 islands arranged in a horseshoe around a central lagoon. Remote, low-lying, and utterly isolated, they were one of [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s more unusual stops.

## Geography

The atolls consist of coral islets rising only a few feet above sea level, covered in coconut palms (016-020) — hence the name "Cocos." The surrounding waters are rich in marine life, and the lagoon provides shelter in an otherwise exposed ocean. The islands lie about 1,700 miles west of [[australia|Australia]] and 500 miles southwest of Sumatra — about as far from anywhere as it is possible to be.

## In the Voyage

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s stop at the Keeling Islands was brief but memorable. He found a small [[current|set]]tlement — a Scottish family who had made the atolls their home, living among the coconut palms and depending on passing ships for contact with the outside world. The oddity of finding a Scottish household on a coral atoll in the middle of the [[indian-ocean|Indian Ocean]] appealed to [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s sense of the world's strangeness.

> My reckoning was up, and springing aloft, I saw from halfway up the mast coconut-trees standing out of the water ahead. I expected to see this; still, it thrilled me as an electric shock might have done. I slid down the mast, trembling under the strangest sensations; and not able to resist the impulse, I sat on deck and gave way to my emotions. To folks in a parlor on shore this may seem weak indeed, but I am telling the story of a voyage alone.
>
> (016-003)

The stop provided water, fresh coconuts, and conversation after a long ocean passage. For [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]], the Keeling Islands were a reminder that even the most remote corners of the earth contained surprises — and fellow human beings.

## Significance

The Keeling Islands represent the extreme of remoteness in [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s itinerary. They are not strategic, not famous, not historically significant — simply a place where the ocean traveler could find a palm tree, a fresh coconut, and a fellow Scot.
