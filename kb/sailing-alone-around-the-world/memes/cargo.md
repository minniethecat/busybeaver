# Cargo

Cargo is the commercial freight that a vessel carries — the reason most ships put to sea throughout maritime history. Before the *[[spray|Spray]]*, [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s entire career had been devoted to cargo: commanding merchant vessels that carried the goods of empire across the world's oceans. The *[[spray|Spray]]* herself, though built for a voyage of exploration rather than commerce, carried [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s provisions as her only cargo — but the language and logic of cargo runs through the narrative, a reminder of the sailor's working life.

## Definition

In maritime commerce, cargo is classified by type: *bulk cargo* (grain, coal, ore carried loose), *break-bulk* (goods shipped in crates, barrels, bales), and *general cargo* (mixed consignments). In [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s era (the late 19th century), the great [[sail|sailing]] freighters — clipper ships, barks, and full-[[rigging|rig]]ged ships — still competed with steamships for long-haul routes. A captain's reputation was built on the speed and safety with which he delivered cargo: fast passages meant higher freight rates, and a clean record meant lower insurance premiums. The transition from sail to steam was, for many captains like [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]], the end of a way of life.

## Nautical Context

The merchant captain's relationship with cargo was complex. Cargo paid the bills — but cargo also meant responsibility, paperwork, owners, charter parties, and port agents. [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s early voyages to China, [[australia|Australia]], and Japan were all cargo voyages. His command of the *[[northern-light|Northern Light]]* — "the finest American [[sail|sailing]] vessel afloat" — was a cargo command. The loss of the *[[aquidneck|Aquidneck]]* with a cargo of timber was a career catastrophe. When [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] built the *[[liberdade|Liberdade]]* and sailed her from Brazil to Washing[[tonnage|ton]], D.C., he carried no commercial cargo — but the voyage was still, in a sense, a delivery. The *[[spray|Spray]]* represented liberation from cargo: a voyage undertaken for its own sake.

## In *Sailing Alone Around the World*

> My voyages were all foreign. I sailed as freighter and trader principally to China, Australia, and Japan, and among the Spice Islands. Mine was not the sort of life to make one long to coil up one's ropes on land, the customs and ways of which I had finally almost forgotten.
>
> (001-005)

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s past as a cargo captain informs the *[[spray|Spray]]* narrative at every turn. "My voyages were all foreign. I sailed as freighter and trader principally to China, [[australia|Australia]], and Japan, and among the Spice Islands" (001-005) — the opening chapter establishes his credentials not as a yachtsman but as a working merchant captain.

The *[[spray|Spray]]*'s own provisions were her cargo — calculated, inventoried, and stored with the care of a master stevedore. [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] knew exactly what his vessel carried: the biscuits, the venison, the potatoes, the cans of mut[[tonnage|ton]]. When provisions ran low, he resupplied from the sea (fishing) or from ports along the way.

The commercial world intruded occasionally. Passing vessels, bound on their own cargo voyages, provided news and companionship. And [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s language retained the merchant captain's cadence — distances measured in days' runs, positions recorded like entries in a ship's log, every landfall an arrival at the next port of call.
