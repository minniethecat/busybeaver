# Pernambuco

Pernambuco (now Recife) was [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s first South American landfall — his reward for forty days at sea from the [[cape-verde-islands|Cape Verde Islands]]. It was a port of mixed blessings: a friendly harbor, a restful interlude, and — in [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s characteristic [[tonnage|ton]]e of wry indignation — a customs bill from the Brazilian government that he considered an insult.

## Geography

Pernambuco lies on the northeastern bulge of Brazil, where the South American continent reaches closest to Africa. The harbor is sheltered by a [[reefing|reef]] — the *recife* that gives the modern city its name — and was a regular port of call for [[sail|sailing]] vessels crossing the South Atlantic. The climate is tropical, the vegetation lush, and the shoreline marked by long beaches and mangrove swamps.

## In the Voyage

After [[sail|sailing]] from the [[cape-verde-islands|Cape Verde Islands]], [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] made an efficient crossing of the South Atlantic. Chapter V opens with the march toward Brazil: "Islands — Sea life — [[arrival-at-pernambuco|Arrival at Pernambuco]] — A bill against the Brazilian government" — (005-001). After weeks of solitude, sighting the coast was a moment of relief.

He entered the harbor under sail: "made the land, casting anchor in Pernambuco harbor about noon: forty days from [[cape-verde-islands|the Cape Verdes]]" — (005-023). The landfall was a homecoming of sorts, though not to his own country. "In bringing me to Pernambuco, landed me on familiar shores" — (005-024), he wrote, his earlier [[sail|sailing]] career having taken him to these waters before.

> Along the equatorial limit of the southeast trade winds the air was heavily charged with electricity, and there was much thunder and lightning. It was hereabout I remembered that, a few years before, the American ship *Alert* was destroyed by lightning. Her people, by wonderful good fortune, were rescued on the same day and brought to Pernambuco, where I then met them.
>
> (005-021)

A practical modification came at Pernambuco. "While at Pernambuco I shortened the boom, which had always been too long" — (005-029). It was one of several adjustments — along with the mast shortening at [[buenos-aires|Buenos Aires]] and the bowsprit trimming — that refined the *[[spray|Spray]]*'s [[rigging|rig]] for the demanding southern passages ahead.

But the visit was not without irritation. The Brazilian customs authorities presented him with a bill for [[tonnage|tonnage dues]] and other charges, which he considered excessive — the "bill against the Brazilian government" promised in the chapter [[course|heading]]. It was a small [[gale|storm]] in port, and [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] met it with the same determined independence he showed at sea.

Pernambuco remained a reference point later in the voyage: "nothing else on the voyage, except at Pernambuco, till she poked her nose into the customhouse at [[melbourne|Melbourne]]" — (013-022), he wrote when the *[[spray|Spray]]* arrived in [[australia|Australia]], equating the two ports as the only places where officialdom troubled him.

## Historical Context

At the time of [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s visit, Pernambuco (Recife) was the capital of the Brazilian state of the same name, a major sugar-exporting port with a long colonial history. Its customs house was a familiar institution to visiting mariners — not always a welcome one. Brazil was in its early years as a republic (the empire having fallen in 1889), and the bureaucracy of the new regime was, if anything, more zealous than the old.
