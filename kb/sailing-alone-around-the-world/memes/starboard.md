**Starboard** is the [[rigging|rig]]ht-hand side of a vessel when facing forward (toward the bow). Its counterpart is [[port]] (left). The starboard side has given its name to several critical navigational and [[sail|sailing]] terms, including the starboard tack ([[sail|sailing]] with the wind coming over the starboard side) and the starboard bow (the [[rigging|rig]]ht-forward quadrant of the vessel).

## Definition

The term "starboard" derives from Old English *stēorbord*—"steer-board"—because early vessels were steered by a board or oar mounted on the [[rigging|rig]]ht side of the hull. This also explains why vessels traditionally dock with the port (left) side to the pier: the [[helm|steering]] board on the [[rigging|rig]]ht side would have been damaged against the dock.

In modern navigation, starboard and port are absolute references relative to the vessel's own orientation, independent of the observer's position. A vessel on **starboard tack** has the wind coming over its starboard ([[rigging|rig]]ht) side, meaning the boom is carried to port. Under the International Regulations for Preventing Collisions at Sea (COLREGs), a vessel on starboard tack has [[rigging|rig]]ht of way over a vessel on [[port|port tack]].

The **starboard bow** is the [[rigging|rig]]ht-forward sector (from dead ahead to 45° abaft the starboard beam); the **starboard quarter** is the [[rigging|rig]]ht-aft sector (from the starboard beam to dead astern). These terms allow precise communication about the relative [[course|bearing]] of other vessels, landmarks, or hazards.

## Nautical Context

Starboard tack has a privileged status in [[sail|sailing]] rules. When two [[sail|sailing]] vessels are on opposite tacks, the vessel on starboard tack stands on (maintains course and speed) while the vessel on [[port|port tack]] must give way. This rule is universal and memorized by every sailor: "starboard tack is the stand-on tack."

The starboard side also carries traditional associations. In many [[sail|sailing]] cultures, the starboard side is considered the "honor" side—guests board from starboard, the captain's cabin is traditionally on the starboard side of older vessels. These customs trace back to the same origin: the [[helm|steering]] board on the [[rigging|rig]]ht side made the left side the practical side for docking.

## In *Sailing Alone Around the World*

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] uses starboard references throughout the voyage to describe landfalls, passing vessels, and [[sail|sailing]] tactics, always with the precision of a professional mariner.

The first sighting of the [[azores|Azores]] came on the starboard side:

> "Coming above the clouds on the starboard bow. Lower lands burst forth" (003-016)

This was [[pico|Pico]]—the first land [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] saw after departing from North America, eighteen days out from [[boston|Boston]].

In the South Atlantic, [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] described a comfortable trade-wind passage: "the wind three points on the starboard quarter and a steady pull" (005-004). The wind on the starboard quarter—coming from behind and to the [[rigging|rig]]ht—is the ideal point of sail for a downwind passage: fast, comfortable, and stable.

Approaching the Brazilian coast, land appeared close aboard: "in view, already abeam to starboard, and not more than seven miles" (005-008)—too close for comfort, but a navigator's precise report.

One of the book's most vivid cultural encounters involves the starboard side. In the [[keel|Keel]]ing Cocos atoll, [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s host performed a curious ritual:

> "In his canoe, he rubbed his starboard cat-head for some time" (007-019)

The cat-head is a timber projecting from the bow, used for securing the anchor. The host's rubbing of the starboard cat-head was apparently a gesture of respect or good luck—one of the small cultural exchanges that punctuate the voyage.

Late in the voyage, near the Caribbean, [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] recorded: "came into view one point on the starboard bow" (015-049)—another landfall, delivered with the same spare, professional notation.

In the final Atlantic leg, [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] made a tactical decision: "to secure an offing on the starboard tack, a heavy swell in the night" (020-007). An "offing" is sea-room—distance from shore. Taking the starboard tack to gain an offing was a prudent move to avoid being driven onto a lee shore.

Near the end of the voyage, [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] described the final approach to home waters: "hove [[sloop|the sloop]] round from starboard to [[port|port tack]], to make for a quiet harbor" (021-008). The shift from starboard to [[port|port tack]]—a routine [[sail|sailing]] maneuver—marked the final turn toward [[newport|Newport]], [[newport|Rhode Island]], and the completion of the [[voyage|circumnavigation]].
