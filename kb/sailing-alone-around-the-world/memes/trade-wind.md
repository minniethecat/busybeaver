The **trade winds** are the prevailing easterly surface winds that blow from the subtropical high-pressure belts toward the equatorial low-pressure zone. In the Northern Hemisphere they blow from the northeast; in the Southern Hemisphere, from the southeast. For centuries of sail, the trades were the great ocean highways—reliable, steady winds that carried vessels across [[atlantic-ocean|the Atlantic]] and Pacific with minimal sail changes.

## Definition

Trade winds result from the combination of the Coriolis effect and the Hadley cell circulation: warm air rises at the equator, flows poleward at altitude, cools and descends at roughly 30° latitude, and returns toward the equator as a surface wind deflected westward by the Earth's rotation. The result is a belt of steady easterly winds between roughly 5° and 25° latitude in both hemispheres. The **doldrums** ([[doldrums|Intertropical Convergence Zone]]) between the two trade-wind belts is a region of light, variable winds—feared by sailors for its calms.

In [[sail|sailing]], "running down the trades" means [[sail|sailing]] before a steady following wind for days or weeks with minimal sail handling—the easiest and most pleasant ocean passage.

## Nautical Context

Before steam, the trade winds defined global [[sail|sailing]] routes. Outbound from Europe to the Americas, ships rode the northeast trades to the Caribbean; homeward, they climbed to the latitude of the westerlies. The clipper routes to [[australia|Australia]] and China followed the southeast trades across the [[indian-ocean|Indian Ocean]] and into [[pacific-ocean|the Pacific]]. A sailor who knew the trade-wind belts could plan passages with near-certainty of fair winds; one who misjudged them faced calms or headwinds.

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s [[voyage|circumnavigation]] route deliberately used the trade winds: westward across [[atlantic-ocean|the Atlantic]] in the northeast trades, south to the southeast trades for [[atlantic-ocean|the Atlantic]] crossing to Brazil, and across [[pacific-ocean|the Pacific]] and [[indian-ocean|Indian Ocean]]s in the southern trades.

## In *Sailing Alone Around the World*

When the *[[spray|Spray]]* cleared the coast of Brazil and entered the open South Atlantic, [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] registered the shift with evident relief:

> "The *Spray* had now settled down to the trade-winds and to the business of her voyage." (005-012)

The phrase captures something essential about trade-wind [[sail|sailing]]: it is not merely favorable weather but a transition into the steady rhythm of ocean passage-making — the "business" of covering long distances with minimal drama.

As the *[[spray|Spray]]* approached the equator, the trades began to weaken, giving way to the doldrums:

> "The sloop was now rapidly drawing toward the region of doldrums, and the force of the trade-winds was lessening. I could see by the ripples that a countercurrent had set in." (005-017)

The boundary between the trade-wind belts was marked not just by a change in the wind but by electrical tension in the atmosphere:

> "Near the equatorial limit of the southeast trade winds the air was heavily charged with electricity, and there was much thunder and lightning." (005-021)

This observation — the electrical charge of tropical air — is characteristic of [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s attention to the physical sensations of [[sail|sailing]]. The trade winds brought not just reliable [[sail|sailing]] but a particular quality of air and light.

Though the term itself appears sparingly, the trade winds are implicitly present throughout the tropical passages. The steady following winds that carried the *[[spray|Spray]]* across [[pacific-ocean|the Pacific]] from [[juan-fernandez-island|Juan Fernandez]] to the [[marquesas|Marquesas]], through the South Seas islands, and across the [[indian-ocean|Indian Ocean]] were trade winds — the same winds that had driven [[ferdinand-magellan|Magellan]], Cook, and countless other explorers.

The transition from [[gale|storm]]y temperate latitudes into the steady trades marked a psychological shift in the narrative. After the horrors of [[strait-of-gibraltar|the Strait]] of [[ferdinand-magellan|Magellan]] and the [[patagonia|Patagonian coast]], the trade-wind belt brought warmth, steady progress, and the relative peace of tropical [[sail|sailing]].

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s passage through the doldrums — the zone between trade wind belts — was characteristically brief: the *[[spray|Spray]]* was small enough to catch even light airs, and he never spent long becalmed.
