# Batavia

Batavia, the colonial capital of the Dutch East Indies, was a major port and administrative center in Maritime Southeast Asia during the 19th century. The city, now known as Jakarta, was a hub of the spice trade and a key waypoint on the sea routes between Europe, Asia, and [[australia|Australia]].

## Role in the Voyage

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] did not visit Batavia during his [[voyage|circumnavigation]], but he referenced it twice in his narrative. He recalled the old steamship *Soushay*, which in 1866 sailed "from Batavia for [[sydney|Sydney]]" and put in at [[cooktown|Cooktown]] "for scurvy-grass, as I always thought, and 'incidentally' to land mails" (015-012).

Batavia also appears in [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s retelling of the Alexander Hare episode at the [[keeling-islands|Keeling-Cocos]] Islands, where he notes that after the collapse of Hare's [[current|set]]tlement, "Hare then went to Batavia, where he met his death" (016-009).

> I think it was in 1866 that the old steamship *Soushay*, from Batavia for Sydney, put in at Cooktown for scurvy-grass, as I always thought, and "incidentally" to land mails.
>
> (015-012)

## Historical Context

For centuries Batavia was the linchpin of Dutch colonial power in the East Indies, a cosmopolitan trading city that drew merchants, sailors, and adventurers from across the world. By the time of [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s voyage in the 1890s, the city was still firmly under Dutch control and remained one of the most important ports in the Eastern Hemisphere.

## Batavia as a Maritime Hub

The port of Batavia served as the eastern terminus for the Dutch East India Company's vast trading network. Ships from Amsterdam and Rotterdam called at Batavia before fanning out to the Spice Islands, China, and Japan. The city's roadstead, sheltered by a chain of small islands, could accommodate fleets of deep-draft vessels.

By the 1890s, Batavia had become a regular port of call for the steamship lines connecting Europe to [[australia|Australia]] — hence the *Soushay*'s 1866 voyage "from Batavia for [[sydney|Sydney]]." The city was also a waypoint on the clipper route from [[cape-of-good-hope|the Cape]] of Good Hope to the South China Sea. [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s own course through the [[indian-ocean|Indian Ocean]], though he did not call at [[java-steamship|Java]], passed within a few hundred miles of Batavia's longitude.

## The Alexander Hare Connection

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s interest in Batavia was tied to the strange story of Alexander Hare, the English adventurer who attempted to establish a private [[current|set]]tlement at the [[keeling-cocos-islands|Keeling-Cocos Islands]] in the 1820s. After Hare's island kingdom collapsed — undermined by the rival [[current|set]]tlement of John Clunies-Ross — Hare retreated to Batavia, where he died. The city thus marks the end of a colonial misadventure that fascinated [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] and found its way into his narrative.
