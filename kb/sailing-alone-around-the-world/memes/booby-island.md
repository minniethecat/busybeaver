## Geography

Booby Island is a small island in the western [[torres-strait|Torres Strait]], off the northern tip of [[bowen|Queensland]], [[australia|Australia]]. It marks the western entrance to the dangerous waters of the strait and the [[coral-sea|Coral Sea]]. A [[beacon|lighthouse]] now stands on the island, guiding vessels through these narrow passages. (015-029)

The surrounding waters are known for their striking colors, described by [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] as "milky white and green and purple." At night the sea displayed brilliant phosphorescence, so bright that "by its light I could see the smallest articles [[deck|on deck]], and her wake was a path of fire." (015-031)

## Role in the Voyage

Booby Island appears in the chapter summary for the [[torres-strait|Torres Strait]] passage: "A new ensign for [[spray|the Spray]] — Booby Island — Across the [[indian-ocean|Indian Ocean]] — [[christmas-island|Christmas Island]]." (015-001) [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] passed the island early in the day before shaping his course for [[keel|Keel]]ing Cocos, 2,700 miles across the [[indian-ocean|Indian Ocean]]. (015-029)

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] had seen Booby Island only once before, years earlier, when he was a fever-stricken passenger on the steamship Soushay. He recalled: "Had I died for it, I would have seen that island." At that time, passing ships used a cave on Booby Island as an informal post office, leaving stores for shipwrecked sailors and letters to be carried by the next homeward-bound vessel. Captain Airy of the Soushay sent a boat to the cave to deposit supplies and collected a dozen letters, most left by whalemen, many addressed to [[new-bedford|New Bedford]] and [[fairhaven|Fairhaven]], [[cape-ann|Massachusetts]]. (015-030)

On [[spray|the Spray]]'s passage in 1897, [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] did not call at the island but stood close in, exchanging signals with the [[beacon|lighthouse]] keeper. By then the "beautiful uncertainty" of the cave postal service was a thing of the past, replaced by regular packet communication. (015-031)

> I had no wish to arrive off the Cape of Good Hope before midsummer, and it was now early winter. I had been off that cape once in July, which was, of course, midwinter there. The stout ship I then commanded encountered only fierce hurricanes, and she bore them ill. I wished for no winter gales now. It was not that I feared them more, being in the *Spray* instead of a large ship, but that I preferred fine weather in any case. It is true that one may encounter heavy gales off the Cape of Good Hope at any season of the year, but in the summer they are less frequent and do not continue so long. And so with time enough before me to admit of a run ashore on the islands en route, I shaped the course now for Keeling Cocos, atoll islands, distant twenty-seven hundred miles. Taking a departure from Booby Island, which the sloop passed early in the day, I decided to sight Timor on the way, an island of high mountains.
>
> (015-029)

## Historical Context

Booby Island's cave post office was a tradition of the whaling era, when ships might be months or years from home. Passing vessels left supplies for the shipwrecked and carried letters forward as a charitable maritime custom. The [[beacon|lighthouse]] later established on the island formalized its role as a navigation aid for the busy [[torres-strait|Torres Strait]] shipping route. (015-030) (015-031)
