**Tonnage** is a measure of a vessel's internal capacity, not its weight. For [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]], the *[[spray|Spray]]*'s small tonnage—nine tons net, twelve tons gross—was both a point of pride and a recurring source of bureaucratic friction, as customs officers in various ports struggled to believe that such a tiny vessel had crossed an ocean.

## Definition

In maritime measurement, **gross tonnage** is the total internal volume of a vessel's enclosed spaces (1 ton = 100 cubic feet). **Net tonnage** is the volume of cargo-carrying spaces only, excluding engine rooms, crew quarters, and other non-revenue spaces. For a [[sail|sailing]] sloop like the *[[spray|Spray]]*, the net tonnage primarily reflected hold space and deckhouse. **Tonnage dues** are port fees calculated based on a vessel's registered tonnage—the larger the tonnage, the higher the fee.

The *[[spray|Spray]]* was tiny by commercial standards. [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] recorded her dimensions precisely: "Her tonnage being nine tons net and twelve tons gross" (001-014).

## Nautical Context

Tonnage mattered to [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] because it determined what he paid at each port. Larger vessels paid more in dockage, harbor fees, and tonnage dues. The *[[spray|Spray]]*'s minimal tonnage should have meant minimal fees—but officials were often suspicious of a vessel that had arrived from the other side of the world with a single man aboard.

Tonnage also mattered to the crew [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] could carry—or choose not to. "A sloop of even [[spray|the Spray]]'s small tonnage" (017-018) was too small for passengers, which [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] considered a virtue: no crew meant no disputes, no discipline, and no sharing of the credit.

## In *Sailing Alone Around the World*

The *[[spray|Spray]]*'s tonnage appears in the text primarily in the context of port bureaucracy and [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s running battle with customs officials.

At [[pernambuco|Pernambuco]], Brazil, the customs house charged the *[[spray|Spray]]* tonnage dues despite her being a private yacht on a voyage of pleasure:

> "The customs, who charged the Spray tonnage dues when she cleared, notwithstanding that she was a yacht" (005-027)

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] was indignant—he saw the *[[spray|Spray]]* as a private vessel, not a commercial carrier subject to such fees.

The contrast came at [[buenos-aires|Buenos Aires]], where officialdom was more generous:

> "Her dockage and tonnage dues were all free" (006-016)

The Argentine authorities recognized the *[[spray|Spray]]* as a vessel of special character—a yacht on a [[voyage|circumnavigation]], not a merchant ship transacting business.

By [[melbourne|Melbourne]], the fees returned: "where she was charged tonnage dues; in this instance, sixpence per ton" (013-022). The rate was trivial—a few shillings—but the principle clearly rankled [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] after thousands of miles of free ocean.

At voyage's end, [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] returned to the *[[spray|Spray]]*'s vital statistics with pride:

> "Her tonnage being nine tons net, and twelve tons gross" (022-004)

The repetition of the opening chapter's description created a framing device: the vessel that left [[boston|Boston]] at nine tons came home the same, unchanged in measure if transformed in significance.

The tonnage also invited comparison with larger vessels. [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] noted "a sloop of even [[spray|the Spray]]'s small tonnage" (017-018) when describing a local vessel in the South Seas—even among small boats, the *[[spray|Spray]]* was small.
