# Uruguay

Uruguay was almost the end of the voyage. On its sandy shore, a sudden gale drove the *[[spray|Spray]]* onto the beach, and [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] came closer to shipwreck than at any point since the [[azores|Azores]]. But the Uruguayans who found him — a mix of curiosity and kindness — pulled his little sloop to safety, and the country became, in his telling, a place of unexpected friendship.

> Spray ashore on the sands of Uruguay — A narrow escape from shipwreck.
>
> (006-001)

## Geography

Uruguay is a small nation on the eastern shore of South America, tucked between Brazil to the north and the [[rio-de-janeiro|Rio]] de la Plata to the south. Its coastline is mostly low and sandy, with long beaches broken by rocky points. The shore where [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] beached was, as he noted, south of the dividing line between Uruguay and Brazil — a reminder that borders in this region were fluid and recently drawn.

## In the Voyage

The drama unfolded soon after [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s departure from [[montevideo|Montevideo]]. A southwest gale caught the *[[spray|Spray]]* and drove her toward the beach. "[[spray|Spray]] ashore on the sands of Uruguay — A narrow escape from shipwreck" — (006-001), reads the chapter [[course|heading]], and the content does not soften the alarm.

The local population came to his aid. A man on horseback — "an old cayuse, over the fields of Uruguay" — (006-004) — spotted the stranded sloop and raised help. "The chap was not a native Uruguayan" — (006-006) — but whatever his origin, he knew what needed to be done. The "pampero," as the fierce southwest wind was called, "is sent up the whole shore of Uruguay before a southwest gale" — (006-009), and every sailor on that coast knew its danger.

The kindness he received made a strong impression. Afterward, the British consul at [[maldonado|Maldonado]] extended a warm greeting, and [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] reflected that [[sail|sailing]] "alone may have seemed to the Uruguayans a feat worthy of some recognition" — (006-011). The rescue transformed his view of the country — from a hazard on the chart to a place where strangers became friends.

## Historical Context

Uruguay in the 1890s was a young republic, having achieved stable independence only a few decades earlier. Its economy was based on cattle ranching and agricultural exports, and its coastal communities were small and scattered. Foreign sailors — British, American, and others — were a familiar sight in its ports, but a lone circumnavigator was something new. [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s reception reflected both the practical generosity of coastal people and the fascination that his voyage inspired wherever he went.
