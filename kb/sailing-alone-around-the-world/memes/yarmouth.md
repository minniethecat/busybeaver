## Geography

Yarmouth is a port town on the southwestern tip of [[nova-scotia|Nova Scotia]], Canada, situated on the Gulf of Maine. It lies near [[cape-sable|Cape Sable]] and the entrance to [[bay-of-fundy|the Bay]] of [[bay-of-fundy|Fundy]]. In the late 19th century, Yarmouth was a busy maritime center, with its harbor serving vessels engaged in fishing, coastal trade, and transatlantic shipping.

## The Road to Yarmouth

Yarmouth was not [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s first [[nova-scotia|Nova Scotia]] landfall. Before arriving there, preceded by atrocious weather, the *[[spray|Spray]]* had already visited [[westport|Westport]] on [[briars-island|Briar's Island]], where [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] reunited with old schoolmates. "I was delighted to reach [[westport|Westport]]," he wrote. "Any port at all would have been delightful after the terrible thrashing I got in the fierce sou'west rip, and to find myself among old schoolmates now was charming" (002-015).

At [[briars-island|Briar's Island]], [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] thoroughly inspected the *[[spray|Spray]]* and "tried her seams, but found that even the test of the sou'west rip had started nothing" (002-016). Bad weather and head winds prevailing outside, he made a short excursion to [[st-marys-bay|St. Mary's Bay]] — "an old cruising-ground" — and back to the island before making for Yarmouth (002-016).

The move to Yarmouth was prompted by more fog and head wind. [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] "spent some days pleasantly enough in Yarmouth" (002-016), a stay that proved crucial: it was here that he secured the final provisions, including the one-dollar tin clock, that would see him across [[atlantic-ocean|the Atlantic]] and [[voyage|around the world]].

## Role in Slocum's Voyage

Yarmouth played a pivotal role in [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s voyage as his final provisioning port and the point of departure from North America. After a period of bad weather that kept the *[[spray|Spray]]* near the [[nova-scotia|Nova Scotia]] coast, [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] put into Yarmouth, where he spent several days preparing for [[atlantic-ocean|the Atlantic]] crossing. He loaded essential supplies — butter, a barrel of potatoes, and six barrels of fresh water — all stowed under deck (002-016).

During his stay, [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] acquired the famous tin clock that would become his sole [[chronometer|timepiece]] for the entire [[voyage|circumnavigation]]:

> The price of it was a dollar and a half, but on account of the face being smashed the merchant let me have it for a dollar. (002-016)

On July 1, 1895, after a rude gale, favorable northwest winds arrived, and on the following day [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] sailed from Yarmouth, severing his last connection to the American continent:

> On the following day, the head sea having gone down, I sailed from Yarmouth, and let go my last hold on America. The log of my first day on the Atlantic in the *Spray* reads briefly: "9:30 a.m. sailed from Yarmouth. 4:30 p.m. passed Cape Sable; distance, three cables from the land. The sloop making eight knots. Fresh breeze N.W." (003-003)

Before the sun [[current|set]] that evening, [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] was taking his supper of strawberries and tea in smooth water under the lee of the east-coast land, as the *[[spray|Spray]]* skirted southward along [[nova-scotia|Nova Scotia]].

## The Clock That Circled the Globe

Among the many items [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] acquired at Yarmouth, none became more iconic than the tin clock. With its smashed face — the reason for its discount from $1.50 to $1.00 — the clock became [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s sole chronometer (002-016). He mounted it in the *[[spray|Spray]]*'s cabin, and it ticked faithfully through [[gale|storm]]s and calms alike for the entire three-year voyage.

The clock proved its worth early in the voyage. Off the coast of Brazil, the steamship *[[ss-south-wales|South Wales]]* hailed the *[[spray|Spray]]* and unsolicited gave the [[longitude|longitude by chronometer]] as 48° W. [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] recorded the result with quiet satisfaction: "The *[[spray|Spray]]*, with her tin clock, had exactly the same reckoning. I was feeling at ease in my primitive method of navigation, but it startled me not a little to find my position by account verified by the ship's chronometer" (006-002).

But the tin clock was not infallible. Navigating [[strait-of-gibraltar|the Strait]] of [[ferdinand-magellan|Magellan]], [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] found the tables needing correction, and noted: "The tables being corrected, I sailed on with self-reliance unshaken, and with my tin clock fast asleep" (011-025). Later, in the [[indian-ocean|Indian Ocean]], the clock lost its minute-hand entirely. [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s solution was characteristically resourceful: "My tin clock and only [[chronometer|timepiece]] had by this time lost its minute-hand, but after I boiled her she told the hours, and that was near enough on a long stretch" (015-047).

The clock had one final moment of distinction. At the Royal Observatory at [[cape-of-good-hope|the Cape]] of Good Hope, the astronomer showed [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] the great astronomical clock of the observatory. In return, "I showed him the tin clock on [[spray|the Spray]], and we went over the subject of standard time at sea, and how it was found from the deck of the little sloop without the aid of a clock of any kind" (018-009).

The acquisition of this humble [[chronometer|timepiece]], recorded almost in passing in the narrative, became one of the voyage's most celebrated anecdotes — a testament to [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s frugality and the simplicity with which he equipped his [[voyage|circumnavigation]].

## The Departure

After provisioning at Yarmouth, [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] prepared the *[[spray|Spray]]* for the open Atlantic: "I now stowed all my goods securely, for the boisterous Atlantic was before me, and I sent the [[mast|topmast]] down, knowing that [[spray|the Spray]] would be the wholesomer with it [[deck|on deck]]" (003-002). The decision to lower the [[mast|topmast]] was prudent seamanship — reducing weight and windage aloft for the long ocean passage ahead.

On the day of departure, a large schooner came out of Liverpool, [[nova-scotia|Nova Scotia]], [[helm|steering]] eastward, and [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] "[[current|set]] my flag and squared away, taking my departure from George's Island before dark to sail east of [[sable-island|Sable Island]]" (003-004). Passing [[sambro-light|Sambro]], "[[gibraltar|the Rock]] of Lamentations," he noted its noble light — one which "the liner *Atlantic*, on the night of her terrible disaster, did not see" (003-004). The *[[spray|Spray]]* then raised "the sheen only of the light on the west end of [[sable-island|Sable Island]], which may also be called the [[sable-island|Island of Tragedies]]" (003-004). These somber landmarks — [[sambro-light|Sambro]] and Sable — were the last lights of North America, and [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] passed them with the awareness of a mariner who knew the cost of the sea.

## Significance

Yarmouth marks the true beginning of [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s solo [[voyage|circumnavigation]]. While the *[[spray|Spray]]* had been fitted out in [[fairhaven|Fairhaven]], [[cape-ann|Massachusetts]], and had made several coastal passages, it was from Yarmouth that [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] finally "let go his last hold on America" and committed fully to the open Atlantic. The town thus represents both a practical provisioning stop and a symbolic threshold between the familiar coastal waters of North America and the vast unknown of the solo voyage ahead.
