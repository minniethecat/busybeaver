# Reef

[[reefing|Reefing]] is the seamanship practice of reducing a sail's exposed area to match increasing wind strength. By tying down a portion of the sail along reinforced bands (reef points), the sailor preserves control of the vessel while reducing strain on [[rigging|rig]]ging, mast, and [[sail|canvas]]. [[reefing|Reefing]] is one of the fundamental skills of sail handling — done early, it prevents damage; done late, it invites disaster.

## Definition

A sail is [[reefing|reefed]] by lowering it partially, folding the excess cloth along a horizontal "[[reefing|reef]] band," and securing the bundle with short lines called reef points or [[reefing|reef]] nettles. The depth of reefing is described in fractions: a *single [[reefing|reef]]* reduces sail area modestly, a *double [[reefing|reef]]* substantially, and *close-[[reefing|reefed]]* leaves only a small triangle of [[sail|canvas]] aloft. On a gaff-[[rigging|rig]]ged vessel like the *[[spray|Spray]]*, the mainsail typically carried two or three [[current|set]]s of reef points. The jib could also be [[reefing|reefed]] or changed for a smaller [[gale|storm]] jib. The decision of when to [[reefing|reef]] — and how deep — is a matter of judgment that distinguishes the skilled sailor from the novice.

## Nautical Context

The old sailor's adage holds: "If you're thinking about reefing, it's time to [[reefing|reef]]." Waiting too long makes the work more dangerous — a flogging sail in a rising gale can injure crew, tear [[sail|canvas]], or carry away spars. Single-handing multiplies the difficulty: every [[reefing|reef]] must be tied while the vessel steers herself or lies hove-to. [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s *[[spray|Spray]]*, with her exceptional balance, could be left to hold her course while he worked at the mast — a quality he relied on throughout the voyage. The ability to [[reefing|reef]] quickly and securely was, for the solo circumnavigator, a survival skill.

## In *Sailing Alone Around the World*

[[reefing|Reefing]] punctuates [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s narrative at every major weather event, marking the rhythm of preparation and response.

Crossing [[atlantic-ocean|the Atlantic]] in rough weather: "July 4, at 6 a.m., I put in double [[reefing|reef]]s, and at 8:30 a.m. turned out all [[reefing|reef]]s" (003-004) — the rapid sequence of reefing and shaking out [[reefing|reef]]s reveals the changeable conditions of an Atlantic summer. Later, as a gale approached the [[azores|Azores]], [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] was grateful for his earlier preparation: "I was glad enough to get [[reefing|reef]]s in my [[sail|sails]] before I had gone far" (004-002). The relief in his voice is that of a sailor who read the weather correctly.

When conditions deteriorated further, he [[reefing|reefed]] deeply: "I [[reefing|reefed]] the mainsail point by point, in the double [[reefing|reef]]. There being sea-room, I should have no difficulty" (004-003). The methodical language — "point by point" — shows a practiced hand. And when the gale reached its peak, the *[[spray|Spray]]* was "reaching, under [[mainsail|close-reefed mainsail]] and [[jib|bobbed jib]]" (004-014) — the minimum [[sail|canvas]] needed to maintain steerage.

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s physical condition after the ordeal moved him to a characteristic touch of humor: "I was as thin as a [[reefing|reef]]-point" (004-016) — the sailor's self-deprecation, measuring himself against the very gear he had been handling.
