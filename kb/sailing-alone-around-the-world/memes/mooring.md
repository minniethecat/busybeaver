# Mooring

The act of securing a vessel in a harbor, anchorage, or alongside a dock using anchors, chains, lines, and fixed mooring points — for the single-handed sailor, one of the most demanding maneuvers.

## Definition

In maritime usage, "mooring" encompasses both the action of securing a vessel and the fixed ground tackle (anchors, chains, buoys) to which she is made fast. A vessel may moor to a dedicated mooring buoy, to a dock or wharf, or by [[current|set]]ting her own anchors. The term "mooring-pile" refers to a heavy timber driven into the harbor bottom to which vessels tie up. Mooring requires careful judgment of tide, current, wind, and the vessel's momentum — a misjudged approach can result in collision with the dock or other vessels.

## Nautical Context

For the single-handed sailor, mooring presents unique challenges. In a crewed vessel, one person steers while others handle lines, fenders, and ground tackle. Alone, the sailor must manage every aspect simultaneously: judging the approach, controlling speed, stepping forward to handle lines, and fending off — all without the benefit of a second pair of hands. The [[spray|Spray]]'s small size and [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s deep familiarity with her handling characteristics were essential to his success in this maneuver.

The late nineteenth-century harbors [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] visited ranged from major ports with established mooring facilities — New York, [[sydney|Sydney]], [[cape-town|Cape Town]] — to remote anchorages where he relied solely on his own ground tackle. The [[spray|Spray]] carried three anchors of forty, one hundred, and one hundred and eighty pounds (022-014), allowing [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] to select the appropriate tackle for each situation. In exposed anchorages, he might [[current|set]] multiple anchors to hold against shifting winds; in sheltered harbors, a single anchor or a tie to a mooring-pile sufficed.

## In *Sailing Alone Around the World*

Mooring figures significantly in the narrative's opening pages, where [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] describes his first experience of bringing [[spray|the Spray]] into port alone. Approaching a wharf in [[gloucester|Gloucester]] harbor, the scene is tense: "Old fishermen ran down to the wharf for which [[spray|the Spray]] was [[course|heading]], apparently intent upon braining herself there" (002-006). [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s admission that he "hardly knew how a calamity was averted" is uncharacteristically candid — for all his later confidence, this first solo mooring was a near-disaster, saved by quick action in dousing the jib and letting [[sloop|the sloop]] round up naturally into the wind. The [[spray|Spray]] "laid her cheek against a mooring-pile at the windward corner of the wharf, so quietly, after all, that she would not have broken an egg" (002-006). A veteran skipper's shout of approval — "You couldn't 'a' done it better if you weighed a [[tonnage|ton]]!" — marks the moment as a rite of passage.

The narrative's final pages offer a dark counterpoint. Off Fire Island, approaching New York at the end of his voyage, [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] encounters a violent tornado that had just swept over the city: "ships at docks had parted their moorings and smashed into other ships" (021-008). The image of great vessels torn from their moorings by the [[gale|storm]] underscores the forces at play — and by contrast, the remarkable fact that [[spray|the Spray]], a 36-foot sloop, had weathered three years of such conditions without once parting her own ground tackle.

Between these bookend passages lies a world of anchorages: [[spray|the Spray]] moored in [[strait-of-gibraltar|the Strait]] of [[ferdinand-magellan|Magellan]] among the [[tierra-del-fuego|Fuegia]]n canoes, in the lagoon at [[keel|Keel]]ing beneath the coconut palms, in the bustle of [[sydney|Sydney]] Harbour and the quiet of [[mauritius|Mauritius]]. Each mooring was a small victory — the successful conclusion of a passage, and the beginning of the next chapter.

> "The sloop naturally rounded in the wind, and just ranging ahead, laid her cheek against a mooring-pile at the windward corner of the wharf, so quietly, after all, that she would not have broken an egg." — (002-006)
