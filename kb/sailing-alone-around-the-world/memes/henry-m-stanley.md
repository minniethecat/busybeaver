## Biography

[[henry-morton-stanley|Henry Morton Stanley]] (1841–1904) was the celebrated explorer of Central Africa, best known for his expedition to find Dr. David Livingstone in 1871 and his subsequent journeys mapping the Congo River. By the time [[joshua-slocum|Joshua Slocum]] met him in 1898, [[henry-morton-stanley|Stanley]] had returned from the Emin Pasha Relief Expedition and was a figure of international renown, wielding his pen as trenchantly as any explorer of the age.

## Meeting Slocum

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] encountered [[henry-morton-stanley|Stanley]] at the Hotel Royal in [[durban|Durban]], South Africa, while dining with Colonel Saunderson, M.P., his son, and Lieutenant Tipping (017-022). [[henry-morton-stanley|Stanley]] had just arrived from [[pretoria|Pretoria]], where he had sharply criticized President Paul Krüger in print. [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] noted the American humor that was never at a discount in South Africa — [[henry-morton-stanley|Stanley]], though British-born, had become an American citizen and embodied this transatlantic wit.

> At Hotel Royal one day, dining with Colonel Saunderson, M.P., his son, and Lieutenant Tipping, I met Mr. Stanley. The great explorer was just from Pretoria, and had already as good as flayed President Krüger with his trenchant pen.
> (017-022)

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s descriptive chapter [[course|heading]] for [[durban|Durban]] refers to the meeting as "a friendly cross-examination by Henry M. Stanley" (017-001), suggesting the explorer subjected [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s [[voyage|circumnavigation]] claims to the same [[rigging|rig]]orous scrutiny he had applied to African geography.

## The Cross-Examination

[[henry-morton-stanley|Stanley]], who had himself been a nautical man on Lake Nyanza, approached [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s voyage with professional curiosity. The colonel introduced the two men, and [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]], as he put it, "hauled close to the wind, to go slow," keenly aware that he was speaking before a man of [[henry-morton-stanley|Stanley]]'s experience (017-022).

> The colonel introduced me to the explorer, and I hauled close to the wind, to go slow, for Mr. Stanley was a nautical man once himself — on the Nyanza, I think — and of course my desire was to appear in the best light before a man of his experience.
> (017-022)

The cross-examination focused on the seaworthiness of the *[[spray|Spray]]*. [[henry-morton-stanley|Stanley]] pressed [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] on whether [[sloop|the sloop]] had watertight compartments, to which [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] replied that compartments would not save a vessel if she struck rocks — "she must be kept away from the rocks." When [[henry-morton-stanley|Stanley]] posed the hypothetical of a swordfish piercing the hull, [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] answered with characteristic pragmatism that "the first thing would be to secure the sword" (017-022).

> "What if a swordfish should pierce her hull with its sword?" he asked. "Compartments would not save her if she should hit the rocks lying along her course," said I; adding, "she must be kept away from the rocks."
> (017-022)

Colonel Saunderson was sufficiently intrigued by the exchange to invite [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] to dine with the party again on the following day. [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] had the pleasure of meeting [[henry-morton-stanley|Stanley]] a second time, though he noted with dry humor that he "got no more hints in navigation from the famous explorer" (017-022).

## South African Context

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s arrival in South Africa coincided with a period of intense political tension. The Boer republics and British colonies were vying for influence, and [[henry-morton-stanley|Stanley]]'s journalistic attacks on Krüger reflected broader imperial rivalries. In this charged atmosphere, [[henry-morton-stanley|Stanley]]'s interest in an American sailor circumnavigating alone in a small sloop offered a moment of shared curiosity across political lines.

## Significance

Henry M. Stanley represents the high-profile figures of the late nineteenth century who took an interest in [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s voyage. That an explorer of [[henry-morton-stanley|Stanley]]'s stature would cross-examine [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] speaks to the credibility — and the apparent improbability — of a solo [[voyage|circumnavigation]] in a thirty-six-foot boat. The encounter also reveals [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s self-deprecating humor and his ability to hold his own in conversation with one of the most formidable interrogators of the age.
