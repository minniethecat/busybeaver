# Sherman

The *Sherman* was a steamship that [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] encountered off the [[australia|Australian]] coast on the same passage that brought the distressed *[[cutter-yacht-akbar|Akbar]]* across his path. The *Sherman* was bound down the coast on her regular route — one of the hundreds of coastal steamers that linked [[australia|Australia]]'s ports in the age before rail and road — and her appearance was a reminder that [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]], for all his solitude, was never far from the world's busiest shipping lanes.

## Specifications

The *Sherman* was a coastal steamship of the type that plied the [[australia|Australian]] coast in the 1890s — a workhorse of empire, carrying passengers, mail, and cargo between [[sydney|Sydney]], [[melbourne|Melbourne]], and the smaller ports. She was steam-powered, capable of maintaining a regular schedule regardless of wind, and large enough to be comfortable in coastal waters. For [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]], whose *[[spray|Spray]]* relied entirely on wind and current, a steamship represented both the competition and the convenience of the modern age.

## In Slocum's Narrative

The *Sherman* appears in the narrative alongside the *[[cutter-yacht-akbar|Akbar]]*. "I spoke the steamship *Sherman*, bound down the coast" — (014-034), [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] recorded. Speaking a vessel at sea — coming within hailing distance to exchange news, positions, and courtesies — was a routine event in the age of sail, and for a lone sailor it was a small but meaningful connection to the wider world.

Later, "after speaking the distressed *[[cutter-yacht-akbar|Akbar]]* and the *Sherman*, the voyage" — (014-037) resumed its normal rhythm. The *Sherman* was not the dramatic encounter that the *[[cutter-yacht-akbar|Akbar]]* was — she was not in distress, she was simply doing her job — but her presence on the same stretch of coast underscores the density of maritime traffic that [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] navigated through. He was alone, but he was not isolated.

## Significance

The *Sherman* represents the mundane but essential fabric of maritime life in the 1890s — the coastal steamers that linked the ports of empire, the routine encounters between vessels at sea, and the professional courtesy of sailors who spoke each other in passing. For [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]], a man who had spent his career on such vessels before taking command of the *[[spray|Spray]]*, speaking the *Sherman* must have felt like touching base with a world he had temporarily left behind.

> On the following day I spoke the steamship Sherman, bound down the coast, and reported the yacht in distress and that it would be an act of humanity to tow her somewhere away from her exposed position on an open coast.
>
> (014-034)
