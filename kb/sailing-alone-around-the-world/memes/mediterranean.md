The Mediterranean Sea is the body of water between southern Europe, northern Africa, and western Asia. It features in [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s narrative as the route not taken — his original plan that was abandoned on expert advice — and as a gift of [[sail|sailing]] directories from the widow of [[robert-louis-stevenson|Robert Louis Stevenson]].

## Geography

The Mediterranean connects to [[atlantic-ocean|the Atlantic]] Ocean through [[strait-of-gibraltar|the Strait]] of [[gibraltar|Gibraltar]] and to the [[red-sea|Red Sea]] through the [[suez|Suez Canal]] (opened 1869). At the time of [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s voyage, the canal was a vital shipping artery between Europe and Asia, and [[mediterranean-sea|the Mediterranean]] itself was among the world's busiest maritime regions.

## Role in the Voyage

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s encounter with [[mediterranean-sea|the Mediterranean]] was entirely one of changed plans. After [[sail|sailing]] from [[gibraltar|Gibraltar]], he found himself being pursued by a Moorish felucca — a pirate vessel. He had chosen the [[gibraltar|Gibraltar]] route with a specific intention:

> "Now, my course to Gibraltar had been taken with a view to proceed up the Mediterranean Sea, through the Suez Canal, down the Red Sea, and east about, instead of a western route, which I finally adopted." — (005-002)

Experienced officers advised him to change course. The Mediterranean and [[red-sea|Red Sea]] coasts were both known for longshore pirates, and for a solo sailor the risk was unacceptable. The [[pirate-chase-off-gibraltar|felucca chase]] — which ended when a great wave [[mast|dismasted]] the pursuing vessel — confirmed the wisdom of taking [[cape-of-good-hope|the Cape]] Horn route instead.

Later, during [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s visit to [[samoa|Samoa]], [[mediterranean-sea|the Mediterranean]] reappeared in a gentler context. Mrs. Fanny [[robert-louis-stevenson|Stevenson]], widow of the author [[robert-louis-stevenson|Robert Louis Stevenson]], presented [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] with a significant gift:

> "Following the subject of voyages, she gave me the four beautiful volumes of sailing directories for the Mediterranean, writing on the flyleaf of the first:
>
> 'To Captain Slocum. These volumes have been read and reread many times by my husband, and I am very sure that he would be pleased that they should be passed on to the sort of seafaring man that he liked above all others.'
>
> Fanny V. De G. Stevenson." — (012-020)

[[robert-louis-stevenson|Robert Louis Stevenson]] had spent his final years in [[samoa|Samoa]], far from [[mediterranean-sea|the Mediterranean]], yet he had read and re-read these [[sail|sailing]] directories — perhaps dreaming of voyages he would never make. Passing them to [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] was an act of literary and maritime kinship.

## Historical Context

In the 1890s, [[mediterranean-sea|the Mediterranean]] was under the naval dominance of the British Mediterranean Fleet, based at Malta and [[gibraltar|Gibraltar]]. The [[suez|Suez Canal]] route to India and the Far East was the lifeline of the British Empire. Piracy, though suppressed along the major shipping lanes, persisted along the North African coast.

The Mediterranean–Suez–[[red-sea|Red Sea]] route would have been the conventional eastward passage toward India. [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s decision to abandon it and round [[cape-horn|Cape Horn]] instead was unconventional — but it [[current|set]] the stage for the most dramatic chapters of his voyage: the [[tierra-del-fuego|Fuegia]]n encounters, [[strait-of-gibraltar|the Strait]] of [[ferdinand-magellan|Magellan]], and the rounding of [[cape-horn|Cape Horn]] itself.
