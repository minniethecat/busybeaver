# HMS Beagle

HMS *Beagle* was a Cherokee-class 10-gun b[[rigging|rig]]-sloop of the Royal Navy, launched in 1820 and forever associated with Charles Darwin's voyage of 1831–1836. For [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]], navigating [[strait-of-magellan|the Strait]] of [[ferdinand-magellan|Magellan]], the *Beagle* was a natural reference — Darwin's ship had surveyed these same waters, and the young naturalist's descriptions of [[tierra-del-fuego|Tierra del Fuego]] were among the most vivid ever written.

## Design and Construction

The *Beagle* was a small warship — 90 feet long, 235 [[tonnage|tons]] — built for the Royal Navy's surveying service. She carried a complement of about 65 officers and men, cramped quarters for a voyage that lasted nearly five years. For her second voyage (with Darwin aboard), she was extensively modified: a mizzen mast was added (making her a bark), and her deck was raised to improve sea-keeping.

## In Slocum's Narrative

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] referenced the *Beagle* in the context of [[strait-of-magellan|the Strait]] of [[ferdinand-magellan|Magellan]], whose desolation had been memorably described by Darwin: "Admiral Fitzroy of the *Beagle*, who visited here" (016-023). [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] quotes Darwin's description of the "seascape from the deck of the *Beagle*" (008-009) — a scene of bleak grandeur that matched [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s own impressions. The *Beagle*'s voyage and the *[[spray|Spray]]*'s were separated by 60 years, but the waters were the same, and the observations of one solitary sailor resonated with those of another.

> Then I climbed the mast to survey the wild scene astern. The great naturalist Darwin looked over this seascape from the deck of the Beagle, and wrote in his journal, "Any landsman seeing the Milky Way would have nightmare for a week." He might have added, "or seaman" as well.
>
> (008-009)

## Legacy

The *Beagle* is the most famous survey ship in history, her name inseparable from the theory of evolution. For [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]], she represented the continuity of maritime exploration — the same strait, the same [[gale|storm]]s, the same human response to nature's immensity.
