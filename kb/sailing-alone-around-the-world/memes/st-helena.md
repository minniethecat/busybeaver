# St. Helena

St. Helena is one of the most isolated inhabited islands in the world — a volcanic speck in the South Atlantic, famous for being Napoleon's final prison. For [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]], it was a landmark on the long passage from Africa to [[ascension-island|Ascension]], a place of hospitable Englishmen, a royal mail delivery, and an invitation to luncheon that he never forgot.

## Geography

Rising abruptly from the South Atlantic depths, St. Helena is a volcanic island of about 47 square miles. It lies nearly 2,000 kilometers west of Africa and over 3,000 kilometers east of South America. In the age of sail, it was a vital provisioning stop on [[cape-of-good-hope|the Cape]] of Good Hope route, but by [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s time, steam had made it a backwater — a place preserved in isolation.

## In the Voyage

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] sighted St. Helena from the deck of the *[[spray|Spray]]* on a clear day in the South Atlantic, the island "about twenty miles off" — (018-017). The approach was a moment of reconnection with the wider world, after the solitude of the ocean passage.

The island left an impression. "St. Helena has been an island of tragedy" — (019-004), [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] wrote, reflecting on Napoleon's exile. The former emperor's presence still haunted the place, and [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] — a reader of history — was sensitive to the weight of it.

But his visit was also marked by a memorable kindness. The governor, "the royal mail brought from St. Helena, and inviting me to luncheon" — (019-017), extended hospitality to the lone American sailor. It was the kind of reception that [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] often received from British colonial officials — a mix of curiosity, respect, and the instinctive generosity of empire toward a fellow navigator.

He later joked about the island's famous goats: "I could have nailed the St. Helena goat's pelt to the deck" — (020-009), an image that captured both his irreverence and his constant impulse toward practical improvement of the *[[spray|Spray]]*.

## Historical Context

St. Helena was discovered by the Portuguese in 1502 and became a British possession in 1659. Its most famous resident was Napoleon Bonaparte, exiled there from 1815 until his death in 1821. By the 1890s, the opening of the [[suez|Suez Canal]] had reduced its strategic importance, but it remained a British colony and a waypoint for [[sail|sailing]] vessels on [[cape-of-good-hope|the Cape]] route. [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s visit captured the island at a moment of quiet decline — still British, still hospitable, but fading from the maritime mainstream.

> Very early that morning I was awakened by that rare bird, the booby, with its harsh quack, which I recognized at once as a call to go on deck; it was as much as to say, “Skipper, there’s land in sight.” I tumbled out quickly, and sure enough, away ahead in the dim twilight, about twenty miles off, was St. Helena.
>
> (018-017)
