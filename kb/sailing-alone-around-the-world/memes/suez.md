# Suez Canal

The Suez Canal, opened in 1869, connects [[mediterranean-sea|the Mediterranean]] Sea to the [[red-sea|Red Sea]], dramatically shortening the sea route between Europe and Asia. At the time of [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s voyage in 1895, the canal had been in operation for 26 years and was a vital artery of British imperial shipping.

## Role in the Voyage

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s original plan was to sail eastward: "my course to [[gibraltar|Gibraltar]] had been taken with a view to proceed up [[mediterranean-sea|the Mediterranean]] Sea, through the Suez Canal, down the [[red-sea|Red Sea]], and east about" (005-002). However, he ultimately changed his plan and instead sailed westward across [[atlantic-ocean|the Atlantic]] and around [[cape-horn|Cape Horn]], a decision that shaped the entire voyage and avoided what he described as "the pirates of the [[red-sea|Red Sea]]" (005-024).

> I perceived a felucca making out of the nearest port, and finally following in the wake of the Spray. But here I was, after all, evidently in the midst of pirates and thieves! I changed my course; the felucca did the same, both vessels sailing very fast, but the distance growing less and less between us. The Spray was doing nobly; she was even more than at her best; but, in spite of all I could do, she would broach now and then. She was carrying too much sail for safety. I must reef or be dismasted and lose all, pirate or no pirate.
>
> (005-002)

The course change meant the difference between a routine eastward [[voyage|circumnavigation]] and the far more challenging westward route through [[strait-of-gibraltar|the Strait]] of [[ferdinand-magellan|Magellan]] and across the vast Pacific.

## The Canal in the 1890s

By the mid-1890s, Suez was firmly under British control — a consequence of the 1882 occupation of Egypt. The canal had transformed global shipping, cutting months off the voyage from Europe to India and the Far East. For [[sail|sailing]] vessels, however, the canal presented practical difficulties: the narrow channel and lack of wind in the desert stretches meant most [[sail|sailing]] ships required towage by steam tugs, an expensive proposition for a budget-conscious captain like [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]].

The canal's tolls were calculated by [[tonnage|ton]]nage, and while the *[[spray|Spray]]*'s small size would have kept costs modest, the cumulative expense of pilotage, towage, and canal fees would have strained [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s limited funds. These practical considerations, combined with the pirate threat in the [[red-sea|Red Sea]] beyond, made the westward alternative increasingly attractive.

## A Fork in the Voyage

The decision at [[gibraltar|Gibraltar]] to abandon the Suez route was one of the pivotal moments of the entire [[voyage|circumnavigation]]. Had [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] proceeded eastward, his voyage would have been shorter and more conventional — following the well-established steamship route through the canal and across the [[indian-ocean|Indian Ocean]]. Instead, by turning westward, he committed himself to the [[gale|storm]]y South Atlantic, the treacherous [[strait-of-magellan|Strait of Magellan]], and the vast empty reaches of [[pacific-ocean|the Pacific]]. The book we have — and the legend of the first single-handed [[voyage|circumnavigation]] — was born in that moment of refusal.
