# Course

A vessel's [[course]] is its intended path through the water — the direction the navigator has chosen, the heading the [[helm|helmsman]] steers, the line drawn on the chart. A course is the fundamental unit of a voyage: each leg, each tack, each day's run is defined by its course. To "shape a course" is to commit to a direction; to "hold a course" is to maintain it against wind, wave, and current. For [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]], the [[Spray]]'s ability to hold her course without a [[helm|helmsman]] was the quality that made solo [[circumnavigation]] possible — and the course itself became almost a character in the narrative, an invisible line stretching across the oceans that [[sloop|the sloop]] followed with uncanny precision.

## Definition

A course is expressed in degrees (000–359) or compass points (North, North by East, etc.) relative to a reference [[longitude|meridian]]. *True course* is relative to true (geographic) north; *magnetic course* is relative to magnetic north; *compass course* is what the [[helm|helmsman]] actually steers, including deviation errors. The navigator works in true courses, converting to compass courses by applying variation (the local difference between true and magnetic north) and deviation (error from the vessel's own magnetism).

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| True course | Direction relative to true (geographic) north |
| Magnetic course | Direction relative to magnetic north |
| [[compass|Compass]] course | Direction actually steered (includes deviation) |
| Course made good | Actual track over ground after leeway and current |
| Heading | The direction the vessel's bow actually points at a given moment |
| Bearing | The direction of an object relative to the vessel |

A vessel's *course made good* is its actual track over the ground after accounting for leeway and current [[current|set]] — often different from the course steered. Over long ocean passages, small errors compound: a one-degree course error over 1,000 miles produces a 17-mile off[[current|set]] at the destination.

## Nautical Context

The difference between the course steered and the course made good is the measure of the sea's resistance. A head current subtracts miles; a cross current [[current|set]]s the vessel sideways; leeway (the vessel's sideways [[current|drift]] under sail) pushes it off the wind. The navigator's skill lies in anticipating these forces and adjusting the steered course accordingly.

For the single-handed sailor, the problem of course is transformed. A crewed vessel can maintain a [[helm|helmsman]] at the [[helm|wheel]] 24 hours a day; the single-hander must sleep. The [[Spray]] solved this problem through hull design — a long [[keel]] and balanced sail plan that allowed her to hold her course with a lashed [[rudder|helm]]. But the navigator still had to [[current|set]] the [[rigging|rig]]ht course, check it by celestial observation when possible, and trust the vessel to maintain it during the hours of darkness or exhaustion.

## In *Sailing Alone Around the World*

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] shaped his first offshore course from [[Gloucester]] with deliberate precision: "Thence shaping her course east, by compass, to go north of Cashes Ledge and the Amen Rocks" (002-011). The course was the first decision of the voyage — east, into [[atlantic-ocean|the Atlantic]] — and it established the pattern: every leg began with a course laid down on the chart.

The *[[spray|Spray]]*'s course-holding ability became legendary in the narrative. Off the [[Azores]], ill and exhausted, [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] recorded: "[[spray|the Spray]] was still heading as I had left her, and was going like a racehorse" (004-011). She had "made ninety miles in the night through a rough sea" (004-011) — and the course had held, perfect, without a hand on the [[rudder|helm]].

In the [[Strait-of-Magellan]], where currents ran fierce and the channel twisted between rocks, course-keeping became a matter of life and death. The *[[spray|Spray]]*'s ability to "hold her course so steadily that one would have declared that the card was nailed fast" (006-014) was the margin between safe passage and shipwreck.

> "For one whole month my vessel held her course true; I had not, the while, so much as a light in the binnacle." — (011-019)

In [[pacific-ocean|the Pacific]], the course became something close to autonomous. [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] trusted the *[[spray|Spray]]* to hold her heading while he read, cooked, or slept, and [[sloop|the sloop]] did not disappoint. The [[Southern Cross]] confirmed the course each night — celestial validation of a perfect heading, maintained by wood and [[sail|canvas]] and the intelligence of a well-designed hull. This was the miracle of the *[[spray|Spray]]*: not speed, not size, but the simple, profound ability to stay on course.

## Historical Evolution

Before the [[chronometer|marine chronometer]] (perfected by John Harrison in the 18th century), determining longitude — and therefore one's true course — was the central navigational problem of the Age of [[sail|Sail]]. Mariners could find latitude by the sun's altitude at noon; longitude required knowing the time at a reference [[longitude|meridian]]. Without a chronometer, navigators relied on [[navigation|dead reckoning]]: tracking course steered and speed through the water, then plotting the resulting position on the chart. Errors accumulated with every mile, and the uncertainty made landfall an anxious event.

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] sailed without a chronometer for most of his voyage — his had rusted and become unreliable. He relied on [[navigation|dead reckoning]], celestial observations for latitude, and a lifetime of experience reading the sea. That he navigated the globe with what amounted to a compass, a sextant, and intuition is a testament to seamanship of the old school.
