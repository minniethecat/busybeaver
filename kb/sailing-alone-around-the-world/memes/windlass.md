# Windlass

A **windlass** is a deck-mounted mechanical winch used to raise (weigh) and lower an anchor by winding the [[cable|anchor cable]] around a horizontal drum or barrel. On small vessels like the *[[spray|Spray]]*, the windlass was manually operated — often a "crab" type, a compact hand-powered winch — making anchor handling a demanding solo task.

## Definition

The windlass consists of a horizontal barrel or drum around which the [[cable|anchor cable]] is wound, turned by handles or levers. It typically includes a pawl-and-ratchet mechanism to prevent the cable from running out when tension is released. On a small vessel, the windlass is mounted on the foredeck near the bow, where the cable leads through the hawse-pipe to the anchor. Larger vessels may use a capstan (vertical axis) instead, but the principle is the same: mechanical advantage to overcome the weight of anchor and cable.

## Nautical Context

Weighing anchor by windlass was one of the most physically demanding tasks aboard a [[sail|sailing]] vessel. The sailor inserted bars into sockets on the windlass drum and walked in a circle, heaving the cable in foot by foot while singing a rhythmic chantey to coordinate the effort. On the *[[spray|Spray]]*, with only one man aboard, there was no one to share the labor — a fact [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] confronted repeatedly in adverse conditions. A broken or jammed windlass could leave a vessel unable to raise anchor and at the mercy of wind and current.

## In *Sailing Alone Around the World*

The windlass appears at critical moments throughout [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s narrative, often when he is most tested. During the stranding on the [[uruguay|Uruguayan coast]], after carrying the [[cable|anchor cable]] through the surf in a swamped dory, [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] found, to his relief, that "the cable reached from the anchor in deep water to [[sloop|the sloop]]'s windlass by just enough to secure a turn and no more" (006-003) — his judgment vindicated by a margin of inches.

In Famine Reach in [[strait-of-gibraltar|the Strait]] of [[ferdinand-magellan|Magellan]], a williwaw struck before the anchor could touch bottom. "Therefore, instead of resting," [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] writes, "I had to 'man the windlass' and heave up the anchor with fifty fathoms of cable hanging up and down in deep water... On [[sloop|the sloop]]'s crab windlass I worked the rest of the night" (008-018). He sang old sailor chanties to keep rhythm through the exhausting labor.

At [[snug-bay|Snug Bay]], the windlass failed entirely: "through an accident to the windlass [[rigging|rig]]ht here I could not budge it" (009-003). Unable to [[anchor|weigh anchor]] with [[fuegians|Fuegians]] approaching in canoes, [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] [[current|set]] sail under anchor, dragging it and a [[tonnage|ton]] of kelp across the strait. Only after reaching safety did he "repair the windlass, and hove the anchor to the hawse" (009-005).

The windlass served purposes beyond [[anchor|anchoring]]. When salvaging wreck goods at [[salvaging-tallow-at-langara|Langara Cove]], [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] used it to hoist heavy cargo: "I hoisted them all in with the throat [[halyard|halyards]], which I took to the windlass. The weight of some of the casks was a little over eight hundred pounds" (009-021).

In the technical appendix to his book, [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] describes the *[[spray|Spray]]*'s windlass specifically: "The windlass used was in the shape of a winch, or crab, I think it is called. I had three anchors, weighing forty pounds, one hundred pounds, and one hundred and eighty pounds respectively. The windlass and the forty-pound anchor, and the 'fiddlehead,' or carving, on the end of the cutwater, belonged to the original *[[spray|Spray]]*" (022-014).
