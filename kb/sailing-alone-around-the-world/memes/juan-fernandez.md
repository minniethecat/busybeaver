# Juan Fernandez Islands

The Juan Fernandez Islands are a small archipelago in the southeastern Pacific, about 400 miles west of Valparaíso, [[chile|Chile]]. The largest island, Más a Tierra (now officially [[juan-fernandez-island|Robinson Crusoe Island]]), was the involuntary home of [[alexander-selkirk|Alexander Selkirk]] from 1704 to 1709 — the real-life castaway whose story inspired Daniel Defoe's novel. For [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]], arriving alone after weeks at sea, [[juan-fernandez-island|Juan Fernandez]] was the intersection of maritime history, literary imagination, and his own solitary journey.

## Geography

The three main islands — [[robinson-crusoe|Robinson Crusoe]], Alejandro [[alexander-selkirk|Selkirk]], and Santa Clara — are volcanic in origin, with steep, verdant slopes rising to peaks of 3,000 feet. The islands are remote in the extreme: the nearest continental land is [[chile|Chile]], 400 miles to the east. The climate is [[mediterranean|Mediterranean]], with abundant rainfall supporting lush vegetation. The islands were discovered in 1574 by the Spanish navigator Juan Fernández and became a provisioning stop for vessels rounding [[cape-horn|Cape Horn]].

## In the Voyage

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s visit to [[juan-fernandez-island|Juan Fernandez]] was charged with personal meaning. (010-001) (010-019) He had read *[[robinson-crusoe|Robinson Crusoe]]* and knew [[alexander-selkirk|Selkirk]]'s story well. Walking the island, he saw [[alexander-selkirk|Selkirk]]'s cave and reflected on what four years of solitude would do to a man. His own solitude was of a different order — voluntary, finite, relieved by ports and people — but the kinship with the castaway was real.

> The blue hills of Juan Fernandez, high among the clouds, could be seen about thirty miles off. A thousand emotions thrilled me when I saw the island, and I bowed my head to the deck. We may mock the Oriental salaam, but for my part I could find no other way of expressing myself.
>
> (010-019)

The island provided practical relief as well as literary pilgrimage. Fresh water, fruit, and the simple pleasure of walking on solid ground after weeks at sea restored [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s spirits. The island's beauty — green hills rising from the blue Pacific, goats grazing on the slopes — stood in sharp contrast to the desolation of [[strait-of-magellan|the Strait]] of [[ferdinand-magellan|Magellan]], which he had recently survived.

## Notable Inhabitants

On the island [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] met [[manuel-carroza|Manuel Carroza]], a Portuguese trader known as the "King of Juan Fernandez," who nearly traded [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] out of his ship.

## Significance

[[juan-fernandez-island|Juan Fernandez]] is the most literary of [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s landfalls — a place where the sailor meets the story, where the real island and Defoe's fictional one converge. For a man who sailed with a library of books aboard the *[[spray|Spray]]*, this meeting of literature and geography was profoundly satisfying.
