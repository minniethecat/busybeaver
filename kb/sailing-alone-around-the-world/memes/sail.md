A **sail** is a surface—typically of canvas or similar fabric—hoisted on a vessel's mast or stays to catch the wind and drive the vessel through the water. On the *[[spray|Spray]]*, [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] relied on a simple but carefully balanced sail plan: mainsail, jib, and fore[[forestaysail|staysail]].

## Definition

A sail converts wind energy into forward motion through aerodynamic lift and drag. In the age of sail, the configuration and condition of a vessel's sails determined her speed, stability, and ability to weather [[gale|storm]]s. A sloop like the *[[spray|Spray]]* carried a fore-and-aft [[rigging|rig]]—mainsail and headsails [[current|set]] along the line of the keel—which allowed her to sail close to the wind and be handled by a single person.

## Nautical Context

Sails are categorized by their position and function on a vessel. The **mainsail** is the principal sail, [[current|set]] on the [[mast|mainmast]]; the **jib** is a triangular headsail [[current|set]] forward of the mast; the **fore[[forestaysail|staysail]]** is [[current|set]] on the forestay between the mast and the bowsprit. Sail management—[[reef|reefing]] (reducing sail area in [[gale|heavy weather]]), [[current|set]]ting, and trimming—is the core skill of seamanship. A sailor must constantly adjust sails to match wind conditions, balancing speed against safety.

[[reefing|Reefing]] is the primary means of reducing sail in strong winds: portions of the sail are tied down to the boom or yard with [[reefing|reef-points]], reducing the exposed area. [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] frequently [[reefing|reefed]] the *[[spray|Spray]]*'s sails, often sailing under "[[reefing|double-reefed]] mainsail" or "[[mainsail|close-reefed mainsail]] and [[jib|bobbed jib]]" in the [[gale|heavy weather]] of the [[southern-ocean|Southern Ocean]].

## In *Sailing Alone Around the World*

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s narrative is saturated with references to sail handling—[[reef|reefing]], [[current|set]]ting, and the tactical decisions that come with single-handed sailing. His sail choices reveal the character of the voyage: cautious, self-reliant, and deeply experienced.

> "I reefed the sails, then hauled in my sea-anchor" (002-012)

The *[[spray|Spray]]* began her voyage under sail in [[boston|Boston]], where [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] "weighed anchor, [[current|set]] sail, and filled away" (002-002), and from that moment onward the condition and trim of the canvas was a constant preoccupation. In [[atlantic-ocean|the Atlantic]] crossing, he noted that the *[[spray|Spray]]*'s performance under sail drew admiration: "for some of the ablest sailors in the world were looking" (002-006).

Sailing decisions shaped the leg from [[nova-scotia|Nova Scotia]] to the [[azores|Azores]]. "On May 7 again made sail" (002-010) marked a departure after waiting out weather—a pattern repeated across the entire [[voyage|circumnavigation]]. When encountering a gale near [[cape-horn|Cape Horn]], [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] described the *[[spray|Spray]]* under [[gale|storm]] canvas:

> "I was not long in reefing the mainsail and sweating it up—probably not more than fifteen minutes" (005-003)

The speed of his [[reef|reefing]] under pressure—with a pirate felucca in pursuit—demonstrates the proficiency gained from decades at sea. Later, in the [[southern-ocean|Southern Ocean]], the *[[spray|Spray]]* "with a great mainsail bellying even to light winds" (003-010) showed her easy motion and steady sailing qualities.

The canvas itself wore out over 46,000 miles. In [[pacific-ocean|the Pacific]], [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] noted the *[[spray|Spray]]* "now wore her [[mainsail|Fuego mainsail]], which, like Joseph's coat, was made of many pieces" (008-006), a testament both to the durability of the [[rigging|rig]] and the relentless wear of the voyage.
