# Pacific Ocean

The [[pacific-ocean|Pacific Ocean]] was the great middle passage of [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s [[voyage|circumnavigation]] — the ocean he entered at [[cape-pillar|Cape Pillar]] and crossed alone through the [[juan-fernandez|Juan Fernandez]] Islands, [[samoa|Samoa]], [[australia|Australia]], and beyond. For a single-handed sailor in a small sloop, the Pacific was both the ultimate test and the ultimate reward, its vast emptiness a proving ground for seamanship and self-reliance.

## Geography

The Pacific Ocean covers more of the Earth's surface than all the continents combined. For [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s route, the relevant portion was the South Pacific — from the western entrance of the [[strait-of-magellan|Strait of Magellan]] at about 75° W to the coast of [[australia|Australia]] at about 150° E. This crossing spans roughly 8,000 [[knot|nautical mile]]s of open water, with scattered island groups — [[juan-fernandez|Juan Fernandez]], the [[marquesas|Marquesas]], the [[samoa|Samoan Islands]], the [[fiji|Fiji]] group — providing the only possible anchorages. The southeast [[trade-wind|trade winds]] govern this passage: steady, reliable, and for a westbound vessel, fair.

## In the Voyage

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s encounter with the Pacific began dramatically. From [[cape-pillar|Cape Pillar]], the *[[spray|Spray]]* "plunged into the Pacific Ocean at once, taking her first bath" — (008-002) in the long swells that would become her constant companion for months. The transition from strait to ocean was abrupt and complete.

> I was exultant over the prospect of once more entering the Strait of Magellan and beating through again into the Pacific, for it was more than rough on the outside coast of Tierra del Fuego. It was indeed a mountainous sea.
>
> (008-007)

Later, reflecting on the crossing, [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] wrote: "To cross the Pacific Ocean, even under the most favorable circumstances" — (011-022) was an endeavor that tested every resource of ship and sailor. The Pacific demanded patience: weeks could pass without sight of land or another vessel. The *[[spray|Spray]]* sailed herself much of the time, her [[rudder|helm]] lashed, while [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] read, fished, and maintained the vessel.

The Pacific crossing linked all the major stops of the voyage: from the [[strait-of-magellan|Strait of Magellan]] to [[juan-fernandez|Juan Fernandez]], then to [[samoa|Samoa]] and the South Pacific islands, onward to [[australia|Australia]], and finally through the [[torres-strait|Torres Strait]] into the [[indian-ocean|Indian Ocean]]. It was the longest continuous passage and the one that most fully realized the dream of [[sail|sailing]] alone [[voyage|around the world]].

## Historical Context

By the 1890s, the Pacific was well charted but still remote. Steamers plied the main routes between San Francisco, Honolulu, Auckland, and [[sydney|Sydney]], but a [[sail|sailing]] sloop like the *[[spray|Spray]]* followed the older rhythms of wind and current. The Pacific that [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] crossed was the same ocean that [[ferdinand-magellan|Magellan]] had named four centuries earlier — *Mar Pacifico*, the peaceful sea. For [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]], the name held: his Pacific passage was notably free of the [[gale|storm]]s that had harried him in the [[atlantic-ocean|Atlantic]], and the steady [[trade-wind|trade winds]] bore him west with a consistency that felt almost providential.
