# Cape Verde Islands

The Cape Verde Islands are a group of ten volcanic islands and several islets lying about 350 miles off the coast of West Africa. Uninhabited when discovered by the Portuguese in the 15th century, they became an important waypoint on [[atlantic-ocean|the Atlantic]] [[sail|sailing]] routes — a place to take on water and provisions before the long run across the equator and into the South Atlantic.

> On September 10 the *Spray* passed the island of St. Antonio, the northwesternmost of the Cape Verdes, close aboard. The landfall was wonderfully true, considering that no observations for longitude had been made. The wind, northeast, as the sloop drew by the island, was very squally, but I reefed her sails snug, and steered broad from the highland of blustering St. Antonio. Then leaving the Cape Verde Islands out of sight astern, I found myself once more sailing a lonely sea and in a solitude supreme all around. When I slept I dreamed that I was alone. This feeling never left me; but, sleeping or waking, I seemed always to know the position of the sloop, and I saw my vessel moving across the chart, which became a picture before me.
>
> (005-014)

## Geography

The islands fall into two groups: the windward (Barlavento) islands to the north and the leeward (Sotavento) islands to the south. The climate is tropical and dry, with the Saharan harmattan wind bringing dust and haze. The islands are volcanic, with Fogo — the highest peak — still active.

## Slocum's Passage

[[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] passed near [[cape-of-good-hope|the Cape]] Verde Islands on his southward run from [[gibraltar|Gibraltar]] toward the equator. Coming from the [[canary-islands|Canary Islands]], the Cape Verdes were the last land he would see before crossing into the southern hemisphere.

The chapter [[course|heading]] for this leg of the voyage framed them as a milestone: "The [[canary-islands|Canary Islands]] — The Cape Verde Islands — Sea life — [[arrival-at-pernambuco|Arrival at Pernambuco]]" (005-001).

On September 8, 1895, the *[[spray|Spray]]* raised [[st-antonio|St. Antonio]], the northwesternmost island of the archipelago. [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] recorded the sighting with the characteristic simplicity of a seaman who knew where he was: "[[st-antonio|St. Antonio]], the northwesternmost of the Cape Verdes, close aboard" (005-014).

## Into the Lonely Sea

The Cape Verdes marked the last landfall before the long equatorial passage. After passing the islands, [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] wrote with uncharacteristic solemnity: "Then leaving [[cape-of-good-hope|the Cape]] Verde Islands out of sight astern, I found myself once more [[sail|sailing]] a lonely sea and in a solitude supreme all around" (005-014). These words capture the psychological transition from the familiar shipping routes of the North Atlantic into the vast emptiness of the equatorial ocean.

The passage ahead would test the *[[spray|Spray]]* through the doldrums and the [[trade-wind|southeast trades]]. The air along the equatorial limit was "heavily charged with electricity, and there was much thunder and lightning" (005-021). Yet [[sloop|the sloop]] pressed on steadily: on September 30, at half-past eleven in the morning, "[[spray|the Spray]] crossed the equator in longitude 29° 30′ W. At noon she was two miles south of the line" (005-023).

## Arrival in Brazil

The southeast trade-winds, met rather light in about 4° N., "gave her [[sail|sails]] now a stiff full sending her handsomely over the sea toward the coast of Brazil" (005-023). On October 5, 1895, without further incident, the *[[spray|Spray]]* made land just north of Olinda Point, casting anchor in [[pernambuco|Pernambuco harbor]] about noon — "forty days from [[gibraltar|Gibraltar]], and all well on board" (005-023).

At [[pernambuco|Pernambuco]], [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] found himself on familiar shores. He had made many voyages to Brazilian ports, and in 1893 had been employed as master to take the famous [[destroyer|Ericsson]] ship *[[destroyer|Destroyer]]* from New York to Brazil (005-024). During his stay, he shortened [[spray|the Spray]]'s broken boom and refitted the jaws. On October 24, 1895, "a fine day even as days go in Brazil, [[spray|the Spray]] sailed," bound south toward the [[gale|storm]]s of [[cape-horn|Cape Horn]] (005-029).

## In the Wake of Old Circumnavigators

The route via [[cape-of-good-hope|the Cape]] Verde Islands connected [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] to centuries of Atlantic voyaging. Looking back on this leg, he reflected: "I tried to compare my state with that of old circumnavigators, who sailed exactly over the route which I took from Cape Verde Islands or farther back to this point and beyond, but there was no comparison so far as I had got" (014-019). The Cape Verdes, for [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]] as for his predecessors, represented the gateway between the known Atlantic and the long reach into the southern seas.

## Significance

The Cape Verde Islands represent [[atlantic-ocean|the Atlantic]] [[sail|sailing]] routes — the established paths that mariners had followed for centuries. [[joshua-slocum|Slocum]]'s course past them was a traditional one, connecting the great ocean highways of the North and South Atlantic. His passage demonstrated that the *[[spray|Spray]]*, a small sloop of only 36 feet, could follow in the wake of the greatest navigators, making the same landfalls and crossing the same seas — alone.
