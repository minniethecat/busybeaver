## Overview

Mr. Raymond is a benevolent gentleman in George MacDonald's *At the Back of [[north-wind|the North Wind]]*, characterised as "one of the kindest men in [[london|London]]" (021-192). A poet by avocation, he becomes [[diamond|Diamond]]'s patron and friend, providing the boy with books, education, and moral guidance, while also rescuing the crossing-sweeper [[nanny|Nanny]] from [[suffering|poverty]] and illness. Through Mr. Raymond, MacDonald presents an ideal of Christian [[kindness|charity]] enacted through personal relationships rather than institutional philanthropy.

## Appearance and Character

Mr. Raymond is a man of means — he has a house in [[london|London]] with a servant named John — but his wealth is defined by its use rather than its possession. He is immediately responsive to [[diamond|Diamond]]'s plea on [[nanny|Nanny]]'s behalf: "He sent at once" (021-192) for a doctor. His kindness is not abstract benevolence but practical, immediate action.

His defining trait is his willingness to take children seriously. When [[diamond|Diamond]] brings him a torn book of rhymes and asks for an explanation, Mr. Raymond does not dismiss the child's question. Instead, he reads the poem aloud, explains it in terms [[diamond|Diamond]] can understand, and engages the boy in genuine conversation about right and wrong. "When people want to do right, things about them will try to help them. Only they must kill the snake, you know" (022-023). His moral instruction is concrete, metaphorical, and respectful of the child's intelligence.

Mr. Raymond is also a poet. The narrator notes that "Mr. Raymond was a poet himself, and so, although he had never been at the back of the north wind, he was able to understand the poem pretty well" (022-014). This detail is significant: Mr. Raymond's imaginative capacity allows him to bridge the gap between the material world he inhabits and the spiritual world [[diamond|Diamond]] visits, even though he has never directly experienced it.

## Role in the Story

Mr. Raymond enters the narrative when [[diamond|Diamond]], desperate to help the ill [[nanny|Nanny]], approaches him in the street. [[diamond|Diamond]] has heard that Mr. Raymond is a kind man, and his instinct proves correct. This meeting initiates the relationship that transforms both [[diamond|Diamond]]'s and [[nanny|Nanny]]'s lives.

As [[diamond|Diamond]]'s benefactor, Mr. Raymond provides books, including the little rhyme-book [[north-wind|North Wind]] gave [[diamond|Diamond]], and takes an interest in the boy's education. Their conversations about poems and riddles become a vehicle for MacDonald's moral and theological ideas.

Mr. Raymond also serves as a narrative surrogate for the adult reader. He listens to [[diamond|Diamond]]'s accounts of [[north-wind|the North Wind]] with seriousness rather than condescension, and his own admission that he has "never been at the back of the north wind" (022-014) positions him as one who believes without having seen — the Biblical definition of [[trust|faith]].

## Relationships

- **[[diamond|Diamond]]**: The friendship between the wealthy gentleman and the poor coachman's son is central to the novel's social vision. Mr. Raymond treats [[diamond|Diamond]] as an intellectual and moral equal, and [[diamond|Diamond]], in turn, trusts Mr. Raymond completely.
- **[[nanny|Nanny]]**: When [[diamond|Diamond]] brings [[nanny|Nanny]]'s plight to Mr. Raymond's attention, the gentleman acts immediately, arranging for her care and ultimately overseeing her transformation from a desperate street child into a healthy young woman.
- **[[north-wind|North Wind]]**: Mr. Raymond never meets [[north-wind|the North Wind]] directly, but his poetic imagination allows him to understand the poems she gives [[diamond|Diamond]]. He represents the possibility of spiritual understanding through art rather than direct supernatural experience.

## Themes and Symbolism

Mr. Raymond embodies MacDonald's ideal of Christian patronage — [[kindness|charity]] that is personal, respectful, and aimed at enabling rather than controlling. He does not preach to [[diamond|Diamond]]; he converses with him. He does not pity [[nanny|Nanny]]; he rescues her and then steps back, allowing her own nature to emerge.

The fact that Mr. Raymond is a poet is thematically crucial. In a novel that blurs the boundary between [[truth|reality]] and dream, the poet — one whose work is to create meaning through imagination — is the adult best equipped to understand [[diamond|Diamond]]'s experiences. Mr. Raymond does not need to have visited the back of the north wind to recognise the truth of [[diamond|Diamond]]'s testimony. Art, in MacDonald's vision, is another path to the same [[truth|reality]] that [[diamond|Diamond]] accesses through dream.

## Key Passages

> "Now Mr. Raymond was a poet himself, and so, although he had never been at the back of the north wind, he was able to understand the poem pretty well." (022-014)

This passage establishes the equivalence between poetic imagination and supernatural experience — both are valid paths to spiritual truth.

> "When people want to do right, things about them will try to help them. Only they must kill the snake, you know." (022-023)

Mr. Raymond's moral instruction, concrete and metaphorical, respects the child's capacity for understanding while conveying genuine ethical wisdom.
