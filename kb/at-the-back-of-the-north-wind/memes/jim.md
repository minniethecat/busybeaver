## Overview

Jim, often called "cripple Jim," is a poor lame boy who works as a crossing-sweeper in [[the-street|the London streets]]. He is first mentioned when [[diamond|Diamond]] gives him a penny at [[nanny|Nanny]]'s request (019-104). Despite his physical disability, Jim is described as a good-hearted boy, and his friendship with [[diamond|Diamond]] and [[nanny|Nanny]] becomes one of the novel's most redemptive subplots. Through the intervention of [[mr-raymond|Mr. Raymond]], Jim's circumstances are dramatically improved.

## Appearance and Character

Jim is physically disabled — "lame" and unable to compete with able-bodied crossing-sweepers, which keeps him in [[suffering|poverty]]. His character, however, is defined by [[kindness|goodness]] and gentleness. [[nanny|Nanny]] describes him simply: "he's a good boy, is Jim, and I love Jim dearly" (019-107). [[nanny|Nanny]] would "always keep off a penny for Jim" from her own meager earnings (019-108), demonstrating the bond between the two poor children.

Jim's humility and gratitude are evident throughout. When [[diamond|Diamond]] and [[mr-raymond|Mr. Raymond]] arrange a new life for him, he accepts with quiet wonder rather than grasping ambition. He and [[nanny|Nanny]] "regarded him [[diamond|Diamond]] as a superior being" (035-166), reflecting Jim's modest self-conception.

## Role in the Story

Jim's role unfolds primarily in the later chapters of the novel:

1. **Introduction through [[nanny|Nanny]]** (Chapter XIX): [[nanny|Nanny]] asks [[diamond|Diamond]] to give a penny to "cripple Jim," introducing the character as an object of [[kindness|charity]] and friendship.

2. **[[mr-raymond|Mr. Raymond]]'s intervention** (Chapter XXXIV): [[diamond|Diamond]] tells [[mr-raymond|Mr. Raymond]] about Jim, and [[mr-raymond|Mr. Raymond]], moved by the boy's [[kindness|goodness]], arranges to meet him. "[[diamond|Diamond]] succeeded in bringing Jim to [[mr-raymond|Mr. Raymond]], and the consequence was…" (034-076). [[mr-raymond|Mr. Raymond]] takes Jim into his care and eventually brings him to the seaside, along with [[nanny|Nanny]] and [[diamond|Diamond]]'s family.

3. **Life at the seaside** (Chapters XXXIV–XXXV): Jim becomes part of the household at [[the-cottage|the country cottage]]. He and [[nanny|Nanny]] become constant companions, "who said often to each other that [[diamond|Diamond]] was as good as a father to them both" (035-159).

4. **[[diamond|Diamond]]'s death** (Chapter XXXV): When [[diamond|Diamond]] sings his final [[music|song]], "[[nanny|Nanny]] and Jim sat a little way off" (035-251), and when [[diamond|Diamond]] collapses, they are the first to witness it — "[[nanny|Nanny]] and Jim came running" (035-274). Jim's cry of concern — "It might kill you" (035-285) — shows his deep attachment.

## Themes and Symbolism

Jim embodies the novel's theme of **redemptive [[kindness|charity]]**. Unlike the superficial [[kindness|charity]] of "giving a penny," [[diamond|Diamond]]'s — and through him, [[mr-raymond|Mr. Raymond]]'s — [[kindness|charity]] is comprehensive: it lifts Jim out of his circumstances entirely. Jim's lameness also serves as a physical marker of vulnerability in a harsh urban environment, contrasting with the ethereal freedom [[diamond|Diamond]] experiences in his dream-journeys with [[north-wind|North Wind]].

Jim and [[nanny|Nanny]] together represent the "worthy poor" — children whose innate [[kindness|goodness]] persists despite [[suffering|poverty]] and neglect. Their eventual rescue by [[mr-raymond|Mr. Raymond]] illustrates MacDonald's Christian socialist vision: that society's wealthy have a moral obligation to the poor that goes beyond alms-giving.
