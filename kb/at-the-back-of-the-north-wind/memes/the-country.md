## Overview

The Country serves as the novel's pastoral destination — a peaceful, sunlit alternative to the grimy streets of [[london|London]]. [[mr-raymond|Mr. Raymond]] relocates [[diamond|Diamond]]'s entire family (along with [[nanny|Nanny]], [[jim|Jim]], and [[mrs-crump|Mrs. Crump]]) to a cottage in the country, where the novel's final chapters unfold. The Country is distinct from the mystical "country at the back of the north wind" — the latter is a [[heaven]]ly realm accessible only in [[dream|dreams]] and death, while the former is an earthly place of rest and restoration. MacDonald deliberately blurs the boundary between the two, inviting the reader to see the earthly country as a [[darkness|shadow]] or foretaste of the [[heaven]]ly one.

## Location and Description

The country is described as a place of natural [[beauty]] and peace, contrasting sharply with the urban squalor of [[london|London]]. [[the-cottage|Mr. Raymond's cottage]] is set amid gardens and meadows, with a stream nearby — "the lovely meadow-grass of the country, with the stream flowing" (011-036). The air is clean, the pace of life slower, and the children — [[diamond|Diamond]], [[nanny|Nanny]], and [[jim|Jim]] — thrive in this environment.

The specific location is not geographically precise; MacDonald gives it the generic quality of a pastoral ideal. It is reachable by carriage from [[london|London]], suggesting a [[home|Home]] Counties setting, but the novel deliberately avoids pinning it to a map. This vagueness serves the symbolic function: the Country is as much a state of being as a geographical location.

## Appearance and Character

The country appears in the novel through two interpenetrating registers — the earthly and the visionary. In [[diamond|Diamond]]'s dream-journeys with [[north-wind|North Wind]], the country is a luminous landscape seen from above: "the whole country lay beneath him like a map" (011-038), its meadows and [[the-river|river]]s spread out in miniature perfection. A model of the country — "the map or model of the country still lay at" (011-045) [[diamond|Diamond]]'s bedside — serves as a tangible token of these visions, blurring the line between dream and waking [[truth|reality]].

In its earthly manifestation, the country is a place of honest [[home|domesticity]]. [[the-cottage|Mr. Raymond's cottage]], the surrounding meadows, the garden where children play, and the stream that threads through the property form a coherent image of pastoral sufficiency. MacDonald's prose here is notably restrained — he does not describe the country in rapturous detail but lets its effects speak through the characters' responses: [[nanny|Nanny]]'s bewilderment at her first sight of open fields, [[jim|Jim]]'s physical healing in the clean air, and [[diamond|Diamond]]'s quiet contentment.

The country's character is defined by contrast. Against [[london|London]]'s mud, noise, and crowded mews, the country offers silence, grass, and space. Against the precarious economy of cab-driving, it offers steady rural employment. Against the isolation of urban [[suffering|poverty]], it offers community — the cottage houses not just [[diamond|Diamond]]'s family but a small society of rescued characters, each finding a place in [[mr-raymond|Mr. Raymond]]'s pastoral microcosm.

Crucially, MacDonald depicts the country as neither escape nor reward but as **restoration** — a return to the conditions in which human beings were meant to live. The country is not paradise; it is simply what ordinary life looks like when [[kindness|charity]] and justice have done their work.

## Events

1. **[[north-wind|North Wind]]'s promise** (Chapter IX): [[north-wind|North Wind]] first mentions "[[back-of-the-north-wind|the country at the back of the north wind]]" (009-115), introducing the concept that will haunt the novel. [[diamond|Diamond]]'s desire to reach this country drives much of his dream-journey. [[north-wind|North Wind]] tells him that "that country lies in the very" (009-167) heart of things — a location that is metaphysical rather than geographical.

2. **[[diamond|Diamond]]'s dream-visions** (Chapters X–XI): [[diamond|Diamond]] visits the mystical country in his [[dream|dreams]]: "he visited the country" (010-017), experiencing its meadows, [[the-river|river]]s, and strange cartographic model — "the map or model of the country still lay at" (011-045) his bedside. These visions establish the symbolic resonance of "country" as an image of paradise. The country he sees is perpetually May: "the month of May in that country" (010-041), a season of eternal newness.

3. **The practical relocation** (Chapters XXVIII–XXXIV): [[mr-raymond|Mr. Raymond]] arranges for the family's move to his country cottage. [[diamonds-mother|Diamond's mother]] is initially skeptical — "I don't know so much that way, husband" (029-139) — but the move transforms everyone's circumstances. [[nanny|Nanny]], who has never left [[london|London]], finds the country bewildering: "she had no" (034-008) prior notion of such a place. [[diamonds-father|Diamond's father]] finds steady work; the household gains dignity and security.

4. **[[diamond|Diamond]]'s question** (Chapter XXXIV): When [[diamond|Diamond]] asks [[nanny|Nanny]] about going to the country, she replies pragmatically: "You want your boots shined in the country — don't you, sir?" (034-053). [[diamond|Diamond]]'s more mystical question — "Wouldn't mind going to the country? Is that it?" (034-062) — hovers between the literal relocation and the [[heaven]]ly journey that awaits him.

5. **The final crossing** (Chapters XXXVI–XXXVII): [[diamond|Diamond]]'s last speech refers to "the country at my back" (036-223) and "another peep of the country at your back" (036-230), collapsing the distinction between the earthly countryside and the [[heaven]]ly realm. He dies looking toward the country that has been his destination all along. His final dream — "[[dream|dreaming]] of [[back-of-the-north-wind|the country at the back of the north wind]]" (013-222) — is the novel's closing image of peace.

## Symbolism and Significance

The Country in this novel operates on two registers simultaneously:

**The earthly country** is MacDonald's image of the good life restored — honest work, clean air, natural [[beauty]], and community. It represents the social ideal that the novel advocates: the deserving poor lifted out of urban degradation into pastoral sufficiency. This is not escapism but a concrete social vision.

**The [[heaven]]ly country** — "[[back-of-the-north-wind|the country at the back of the north wind]]" — is MacDonald's image of paradise. Its features mirror the earthly country (meadows, [[the-river|river]]s, light) but perfected. The novel's theological daring lies in its refusal to keep these two registers separate: [[diamond|Diamond]] moves between them, and his death is presented not as departure but as arrival. The earthly country is a foretaste; the [[heaven]]ly country is the fulfillment.

The two registers are linked by the recurring image of the **[[the-river|river]]** that flows through both — the literal stream in [[the-cottage|the country cottage]]'s meadow and the mystical [[the-river|river]] of [[the-river|the River]] that [[diamond|Diamond]] traverses in his [[dream|dreams]].
