## Overview

The Cottage is [[mr-raymond|Mr. Raymond]]'s country house — "a little cottage a short way from the house" (035-015) — provided as a home for [[diamond|Diamond]]'s family after their rescue from [[london|London]] [[suffering|poverty]]. It is the setting for the novel's final chapters (XXXI–XXXVII), a place of healing, community, and quiet joy where [[diamond|Diamond]] spends his last peaceful months, and where the novel's social vision finds its fullest earthly expression.

## Description

The cottage is "a real cottage, with a roof of thick thatch" (035-016), set amid gardens and meadows with a view over the surrounding countryside. It is large enough to accommodate [[diamond|Diamond]]'s family, [[nanny|Nanny]], [[jim|Jim]], and [[mrs-crump|Mrs. Crump]] — a small intentional community gathered under [[mr-raymond|Mr. Raymond]]'s patronage. From the cottage, "you could see over the whole country" (035-035), and MacDonald's description emphasizes openness and light rather than enclosure.

## Events

1. **The relocation** (Chapters XXIX–XXXI): After [[nanny|Nanny]]'s recovery, [[mr-raymond|Mr. Raymond]] arranges the move from [[london|London]] to the country. [[nanny|Nanny]], who has "never seen anything thicker than a row of houses" (034-009), cannot imagine what "country" means. [[diamonds-father|Diamond's father]] [[diamonds-father|Joseph]] finds work at the cottage and "was able to keep a little hope alive in his heart" (031-035).

2. **Domestic peace** (Chapters XXXI–XXXV): The cottage becomes the setting for the novel's quietest scenes — [[diamond|Diamond]] [[music|singing]] to the babies, [[nanny|Nanny]] learning to walk in the garden, [[jim|Jim]] polishing boots on the cottage step. [[diamond|Diamond]] calls it "more like being at the back of the north wind" than anything he has known on earth (034-099).

3. **[[diamond|Diamond]]'s final days** (Chapters XXXVI–XXXVII): [[diamond|Diamond]]'s health declines gently at the cottage. His death is not presented as tragedy but as a quiet passing — he is simply "at home" in both the earthly cottage and [[back-of-the-north-wind|the country at the back of the north wind]].

## Symbolism and Significance

The cottage is the novel's image of redeemed community — a microcosm of what society could be if organized around [[kindness|kindness]] rather than competition. Under [[mr-raymond|Mr. Raymond]]'s patronage, the cottage houses an unlikely family: [[diamond|Diamond]]'s biological family, [[nanny|Nanny]] (the street child), [[jim|Jim]] (the lame boy), and [[mrs-crump|Mrs. Crump]] (the reformed landlady). This is MacDonald's social gospel in narrative form — class barriers dissolved not by revolution but by shared [[home|domesticity]].

That said, [[diamond|Diamond]]'s famous line — "this was more like being at the back of the north wind" (034-099) — places the cottage in theological perspective. It is the closest earthly approximation to [[heaven|heaven]], but it is not [[heaven]]. The cottage is penultimate; the [[back-of-the-north-wind|back of the north wind]] is ultimate. MacDonald's universalism does not collapse the distinction between earth and paradise, but the cottage narrows the gap to the width of a child's dream.
