## Overview

Ruby is a horse acquired by [[diamonds-father|Diamond's father]] to expand the family's cab business. She is a chestnut or bay — "a red one — such a [[beauty]]" (034-003) — and her name, suggested by [[diamond|Diamond]], draws a coincidental parallel with the ruby ring that features in [[nanny|Nanny]]'s story about a kind lady. Ruby appears only briefly in the narrative, but her arrival signals the family's improving for[[music|tune]]s after their move to [[london|London]], and she stands in quiet contrast to [[old-diamond|Old Diamond]], the aging original horse whose long service has left him worn thin.

## Appearance and Character

Ruby is described simply as "a red one — such a [[beauty]]" (034-003), a horse whose glossy coat reminded [[diamond|Diamond]] of the colour of a ruby gem. She joins the original cab-horse — [[old-diamond|Old Diamond]], after whom the boy himself was named — making the family a two-horse operation. Where [[old-diamond|Old Diamond]] is described as growing thin and worn from years of labour, Ruby arrives in her prime: "as plump and sleek as a bishop" (031-004), a phrase that gently contrasts her well-fed vigour with the lean frame of the older horse. The coincidence between Ruby's name and the ruby ring in [[nanny|Nanny]]'s story is a source of [[wonder|delight]] for [[diamond|Diamond]], who exclaims "Oh, that is funny!" (034-003) upon hearing [[nanny|Nanny]]'s tale.

## Role in the Story

Ruby represents a small but meaningful turning point in the family's circumstances. Owning a second horse means the cab business can operate more reliably — [[old-diamond|Old Diamond]] no longer has to bear the full burden alone. The choice of a "[[beauty]]" rather than merely a workhorse suggests a modest prosperity, a family beginning to lift its head above the line of bare survival. [[diamonds-father|Diamond's father]] speaks of the plan to expand: he could take "the other horse out for six hours after tea, or in the morning" (029-160), and [[diamond|Diamond]] "could drive old [[diamond|Diamond]] after dinner" (029-160), sharing the workload between the two horses and the family members who care for them.

The parallel between the horse Ruby and the ruby ring in [[nanny|Nanny]]'s story creates one of MacDonald's characteristically gentle coincidences — the kind of connection that, in the world of the novel, suggests a deeper Providence at work. A horse and a jewel, a stable and a hospital room, a cabman's son and a crossing-sweeper girl: the story [[joy|delight]]s in such unexpected harmonies.

## Ruby Ring Connection

[[nanny|Nanny]], recovering in [[the-hospital|the children's hospital]], tells [[diamond|Diamond]] a story about a lady who lent her a ruby ring. As the lady spoke, [[nanny|Nanny]] "kept seeing deeper and deeper into the stone" (034-011). The ring becomes for [[nanny|Nanny]] an object of absorbed contemplation — a small portal into [[beauty]] during a time of illness and [[suffering|poverty]]. Later, she notices that a window pane catching the light "was just the colour of the ruby" (034-271), the world outside the hospital echoing the jewel's deep red. [[diamond|Diamond]] is struck by the coincidence — their new horse shares not only the name but the deep red [[beauty]] of the jewel in [[nanny|Nanny]]'s story. The ruby — in ring, in window-light, and in horse — becomes a thread of colour running through disparate lives, connecting them in a pattern that only the reader (and perhaps Providence) can fully see.
