CHAPTER XXXV. I MAKE DIAMOND'S ACQUAINTANCE

[035-001]MR. RAYMOND'S house was called The Mound, because it stood upon a little

[035-002]steep knoll, so smooth and symmetrical that it showed itself at once to

[035-003]be artificial. It had, beyond doubt, been built for Queen Elizabeth as a

[035-004]hunting tower--a place, namely, from the top of which you could see the

[035-005]country for miles on all sides, and so be able to follow with your eyes

[035-006]the flying deer and the pursuing hounds and horsemen. The mound had been

[035-007]cast up to give a good basement-advantage over the neighbouring heights

[035-008]and woods. There was a great quarry-hole not far off, brim-full of

[035-009]water, from which, as the current legend stated, the materials forming

[035-010]the heart of the mound--a kind of stone unfit for building--had been

[035-011]dug. The house itself was of brick, and they said the foundations were

[035-012]first laid in the natural level, and then the stones and earth of the

[035-013]mound were heaped about and between them, so that its great height

[035-014]should be well buttressed.

[035-015][[diamonds-father|Joseph]] and his wife lived in a little cottage a short way from the

[035-016]house. It was a real cottage, with a roof of thick thatch, which, in

[035-017]June and July, the wind sprinkled with the red and white petals it shook

[035-018]from the loose topmost sprays of the rose-trees climbing the walls. At

[035-019]first [[diamond|Diamond]] had a nest under this thatch--a pretty little room with

[035-020]white muslin curtains, but afterwards Mr. and [[mrs-raymond|Mrs. Raymond]] wanted to

[035-021]have him for a page in the house, and his father and mother were quite

[035-022]pleased to have him employed without his leaving them. So he was dressed

[035-023]in a suit of blue, from which his pale face and fair hair came out like

[035-024]the loveliest blossom, and took up his abode in the house.

[035-025]“Would you be afraid to [[dream|sleep]] alone, Diamond?” asked his mistress.

[035-026]“I don't know what you mean, ma'am,” said Diamond. “I never was afraid

[035-027]of anything that I can recollect--not much, at least.”

[035-028]“There's a little room at the top of the house--all alone,” she

[035-029]returned; “perhaps you would not mind [[sleep|sleeping]] there?”

[035-030]“I can sleep anywhere, and I like best to be high up. Should I be able

[035-031]to see out?”

[035-032]“I will show you the place,” she answered; and taking him by the hand,

[035-033]she led him up and up the oval-winding stair in one of the two towers.

[035-034]Near the top they entered a tiny little room, with two windows from

[035-035]which you could see over the whole country. Diamond clapped his hands

[035-036]with [[joy|delight]].

[035-037]“You would like this room, then, Diamond?” said his mistress.

[035-038]“It's the grandest room in the house,” he answered. “I shall be near the

[035-039][[stars]], and yet not far from the tops of the trees. That's just what I

[035-040]like.”

[035-041]I daresay he thought, also, that it would be a nice place for North

[035-042]Wind to call at in passing; but he said nothing of that sort. Below him

[035-043]spread a lake of green leaves, with glimpses of grass here and there at

[035-044]the bottom of it. As he looked down, he saw a squirrel appear suddenly,

[035-045]and as suddenly vanish amongst the topmost branches.

[035-046]“Aha! little squirrel,” he cried, “my nest is built higher than yours.”

[035-047]“You can be up here with your books as much as you like,” said his

[035-048]mistress. “I will have a little bell hung at the door, which I can ring

[035-049]when I want you. Half-way down the stair is the drawing-room.”

[035-050]So Diamond was installed as page, and his new room got ready for him.

[035-051]It was very soon after this that I came to know Diamond. I was then a

[035-052]tutor in a family whose estate adjoined the little property belonging

[035-053]to The Mound. I had made the acquaintance of [[mr-raymond|Mr. Raymond]] in [[london|London]] some

[035-054]time before, and was walking up the drive towards the house to call upon

[035-055]him one fine warm evening, when I saw Diamond for the first time. He was

[035-056]sitting at the foot of a great beech-tree, a few yards from the road,

[035-057]with a book on his knees. He did not see me. I walked up behind

[035-058]the tree, and peeping over his shoulder, saw that he was reading a

[035-059]fairy-book.

[035-060]“What are you reading?” I said, and spoke suddenly, with the hope of

[035-061]seeing a startled little face look round at me. Diamond turned his

[035-062]head as quietly as if he were only obeying his mother's voice, and the

[035-063]calmness of his face rebuked my unkind desire and made me ashamed of it.

[035-064]“I am reading the story of the Little Lady and the Goblin Prince,” said

[035-065]Diamond.

[035-066]“I am sorry I don't know the story,” I returned. “Who is it by?”

[035-067]“Mr. Raymond made it.”

[035-068]“Is he your uncle?” I asked at a guess.

[035-069]“No. He's my master.”

[035-070]“What do you do for him?” I asked respectfully.

[035-071]“Anything he wishes me to do,” he answered. “I am busy for him now. He

[035-072]gave me this story to read. He wants my opinion upon it.”

[035-073]“Don't you find it rather hard to make up your mind?”

[035-074]“Oh dear no! Any story always tells me itself what I'm to think about

[035-075]it. Mr. Raymond doesn't want me to say whether it is a clever story or

[035-076]not, but whether I like it, and why I like it. I never can tell what

[035-077]they call clever from what they call silly, but I always know whether I

[035-078]like a story or not.”

[035-079]“And can you always tell why you like it or not?”

[035-080]“No. Very often I can't at all. Sometimes I can. I always know, but I

[035-081]can't always tell why. Mr. Raymond writes the stories, and then tries

[035-082]them on me. Mother does the same when she makes jam. She's made such a

[035-083]lot of jam since we came here! And she always makes me taste it to see

[035-084]if it'll do. Mother knows by the face I make whether it will or not.”

[035-085]At this moment I caught sight of two more children approaching. One was

[035-086]a handsome girl, the other a pale-faced, awkward-looking boy, who limped

[035-087]much on one leg. I withdrew a little, to see what would follow, for they

[035-088]seemed in some consternation. After a few hurried words, they went

[035-089]off together, and I pursued my way to the house, where I was as kindly

[035-090]received by Mr. and Mrs. Raymond as I could have desired. From them I

[035-091]learned something of Diamond, and was in consequence the more glad to

[035-092]find him, when I returned, seated in the same place as before.

[035-093]“What did the boy and girl want with you, Diamond?” I asked.

[035-094]“They had seen a creature that frightened them.”

[035-095]“And they came to tell you about it?”

[035-096]“They couldn't get water out of the well for it. So they wanted me to go

[035-097]with them.”

[035-098]“They're both bigger than you.”

[035-099]“Yes, but they were frightened at it.”

[035-100]“And weren't you frightened at it?”

[035-101]“No.”

[035-102]“Why?”

[035-103]“Because I'm silly. I'm never frightened at things.”

[035-104]I could not help thinking of the old meaning of the word silly.

[035-105]“And what was it?” I asked.

[035-106]“I think it was a kind of an [[angel]]--a very little one. It had a long

[035-107]body and great wings, which it drove about it so fast that they grew a

[035-108]thin cloud all round it. It flew backwards and forwards over the well,

[035-109]or hung right in the middle, making a mist of its wings, as if its

[035-110]business was to take care of the water.”

[035-111]“And what did you do to drive it away?”

[035-112]“I didn't drive it away. I knew, whatever the creature was, the well

[035-113]was to get water out of. So I took the jug, dipped it in, and drew the

[035-114]water.”

[035-115]“And what did the creature do?”

[035-116]“Flew about.”

[035-117]“And it didn't hurt you?”

[035-118]“No. Why should it? I wasn't doing anything wrong.”

[035-119]“What did your companions say then?”

[035-120]“They said--`Thank you, Diamond. What a dear silly you are!'”

[035-121]“And weren't you angry with them?”

[035-122]“No! Why should I? I should like if they would play with me a little;

[035-123]but they always like better to go away together when their work is over.

[035-124]They never heed me. I don't mind it much, though. The other creatures

[035-125]are friendly. They don't run away from me. Only they're all so busy with

[035-126]their own work, they don't mind me much.”

[035-127]“Do you feel lonely, then?”

[035-128]“Oh, no! When nobody minds me, I get into my nest, and look up. And then

[035-129]the sky does mind me, and thinks about me.”

[035-130]“Where is your nest?”

[035-131]He rose, saying, “I will show you,” and led me to the other side of the

[035-132]tree.

[035-133]There hung a little rope-ladder from one of the lower boughs. The boy

[035-134]climbed up the ladder and got upon the bough. Then he climbed farther

[035-135]into the leafy branches, and went out of sight.

[035-136]After a little while, I heard his voice coming down out of the tree.

[035-137]“I am in my nest now,” said the voice.

[035-138]“I can't see you,” I returned.

[035-139]“I can't see you either, but I can see the first star peeping out of the

[035-140]sky. I should like to get up into the sky. Don't you think I shall, some

[035-141]day?”

[035-142]“Yes, I do. Tell me what more you see up there.”

[035-143]“I don't see anything more, except a few leaves, and the big sky over

[035-144]me. It goes swinging about. The earth is all behind my back. There comes

[035-145]another star! The wind is like kisses from a big lady. When I get up

[035-146]here I feel as if I were in [[north-wind|North Wind]]'s arms.”

[035-147]This was the first I heard of North Wind.

[035-148]The whole ways and look of the child, so full of quiet wisdom, yet so

[035-149]ready to accept the judgment of others in his own dispraise, took hold

[035-150]of my heart, and I felt myself wonderfully drawn towards him. It seemed

[035-151]to me, somehow, as if little Diamond possessed the secret of life, and

[035-152]was himself what he was so ready to think the lowest living thing--an

[035-153]angel of God with something special to say or do. A gush of reverence

[035-154]came over me, and with a single goodnight, I turned and left him in his

[035-155]nest.

[035-156]I saw him often after this, and gained so much of his [[trust|confidence]] that he

[035-157]told me all I have told you. I cannot pretend to account for it. I leave

[035-158]that for each philosophical reader to do after his own fashion. The

[035-159]easiest way is that of [[nanny|Nanny]] and [[jim|Jim]], who said often to each other

[035-160]that Diamond had a tile loose. But Mr. Raymond was much of my opinion

[035-161]concerning the boy; while Mrs. Raymond confessed that she often rang her

[035-162]bell just to have once more the pleasure of seeing the lovely stillness

[035-163]of the boy's face, with those blue eyes which seemed rather made for

[035-164]other people to look into than for himself to look out of.

[035-165]It was plainer to others than to himself that he felt the desertion of

[035-166]Nanny and Jim. They appeared to regard him as a mere toy, except when

[035-167]they found he could minister to the scruple of using him--generally with

[035-168]success. They were, however, well-behaved to a wonderful degree; while

[035-169]I have little doubt that much of their good behaviour was owing to the

[035-170]unconscious influence of the boy they called God's baby.
