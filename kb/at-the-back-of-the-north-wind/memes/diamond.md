## Overview

Diamond is the child protagonist of George MacDonald's 1871 fantasy novel *At the Back of [[north-wind|the North Wind]]*. He is the son of a poor [[london|London]] coachman, named after his father's favourite horse. Diamond is characterised by his innate [[kindness|goodness]], simple [[trust|faith]], and childlike wisdom — qualities that allow him to form a unique friendship with [[north-wind|the North Wind]], a beautiful and powerful supernatural being who takes him on journeys through the [[darkness|night]] sky and ultimately to the peaceful land at her back.

## Appearance and Character

Diamond is introduced as a very young boy, small enough to [[dream|sleep]] in a makeshift bed built by his father in the hay-loft above the horses' stable. His name was given because his father "had named him after a favourite horse, and his mother had had no objection" (001-020). He lives with his family in impoverished conditions — the room over the coach-house is cold and draughty, with the north wind blowing through gaps in the old boards.

Despite his [[suffering|poverty]], Diamond's defining trait is his unwavering [[kindness|goodness]]. When his mother remarks on his helpful nature, she tells him, "you're as good to your mother as if you were a girl" (016-049). He cares for his baby sibling, helps around the house, and extends kindness to everyone he meets. The narrator observes that Diamond "never troubled his head about what people thought of him" (038-011), a mark of his guileless character.

Diamond possesses a natural, unlearned wisdom. The narrator describes him as one of those "children [who] are profound in metaphysics" (038-004), capable of discussing the deepest questions of existence with a clarity that eludes adults.

## Role in the Story

Diamond first encounters [[north-wind|the North Wind]] when she blows through a chink in the wall of his hay-loft bedroom. Their initial meeting establishes the key dynamic of their relationship: Diamond is initially [[fear|fright]]ened but curious, while [[north-wind|North Wind]] is both powerful and gentle with him. When she asks him to come out with her, he admits, "I want to go to [[dream|sleep]]... for he did not like to be scolded" (001-195), revealing his childlike vulnerability.

The [[north-wind|North Wind]] takes Diamond on a series of journeys through the [[darkness|night]], carrying him in her hair as she goes about her work of blowing ships to safety or sinking them. Diamond witnesses her dual nature — she is both destructive and loving, beautiful and terrifying. "I did not know you were so tall," he tells her, and she replies that she changes size depending on her work (001-178).

As the story progresses, Diamond's family moves from the country to [[london|London]], and Diamond begins working — first helping his father with the cab, then driving it himself as a young cabman. Despite his youth, he earns the respect and [[love|affection]] of those around him through his [[truth|honesty]] and kindness.

Diamond befriends [[nanny|Nanny]], a poor crossing-sweeper girl, and [[mr-raymond|Mr. Raymond]], a gentleman who becomes his benefactor. Through these relationships, MacDonald explores themes of social class, [[kindness|charity]], and the moral education that comes through helping others.

At the novel's end, Diamond is found lying "on the floor of [[the-hay-loft|the loft]]" (038-051), seemingly dead. But the narrator understands: "I knew that he had gone to the back of the north wind" (038-061). His physical death is presented not as tragedy but as a peaceful passage into the transcendent realm he had visited throughout the story.

## Relationships

- **[[north-wind|North Wind]]**: Diamond's supernatural friend and guide, who carries him through the [[darkness|night]] on her back. Their relationship is one of mutual [[love|affection]] — she loves his innocence, and he trusts her absolutely.
- **[[nanny|Nanny]]**: The poor crossing-sweeper whom Diamond befriends and helps. Their friendship demonstrates Diamond's [[kindness|compassion]] across class boundaries.
- **[[mr-raymond|Mr. Raymond]]**: A kindly gentleman who becomes Diamond's benefactor, providing for his education and well-being.
- **[[old-diamond|Old Diamond]]**: The horse after whom Diamond was named, kept in the stable beneath his hay-loft bedroom.
- **[[diamonds-mother|Diamond's Mother]] and Father**: His parents are loving but poor. His father is a coachman; his mother cares for the household. Diamond's filial devotion is a recurring theme.

## Themes and Symbolism

Diamond embodies MacDonald's theological vision of childlike [[trust|faith]]. He accepts [[north-wind|the North Wind]]'s paradoxical nature — both gentle and destructive — without requiring rational explanation, mirroring the Christian concept of trusting divine mystery. The narrator suggests that Diamond's death is not an ending but a homecoming: "he had gone to the back of the north wind" (038-061), a place beyond suffering where love is perfected.

## Key Passages

> "Dear me!" said Diamond, and thought a minute. "I think, then, you can hardly expect me to keep a window open with my breath." (001-121)

Diamond's literal-minded conversations with [[north-wind|the North Wind]] reveal his blend of childish reasoning and unwitting wisdom.

> "You're as good to your mother as if you were a girl" (016-049)

This maternal observation highlights Diamond's exceptional gentleness, unusual for a Victorian boy of his class.

> I knew that he had gone to the back of the north wind. (038-061)

The novel's closing line reframes death as a journey into peace, the destination Diamond has been approaching all along.
