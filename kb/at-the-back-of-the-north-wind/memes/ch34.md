CHAPTER XXXIV. IN THE COUNTRY

[034-001]BEFORE the end of the month, Ruby had got respectably thin, and [[diamond|Diamond]]

[034-002]respectably stout. They really began to look fit for double harness.

[034-003][[diamonds-father|Joseph]] and his wife got their affairs in order, and everything ready for

[034-004]migrating at the shortest notice; and they felt so peaceful and happy

[034-005]that they judged all the trouble they had gone through well worth

[034-006]enduring. As for [[nanny|Nanny]], she had been so happy ever since she left the

[034-007]hospital, that she expected nothing better, and saw nothing attractive

[034-008]in the notion of the country. At the same time, she had not the least

[034-009]idea of what the word country meant, for she had never seen anything

[034-010]about her but streets and gas-lamps. Besides, she was more attached to

[034-011][[jim|Jim]] than to Diamond: Jim was a reasonable being, Diamond in her eyes at

[034-012]best only an amiable, over-grown baby, whom no amount of expostulation

[034-013]would ever bring to talk sense, not to say think it. Now that she

[034-014]could manage the baby as well as he, she judged herself altogether his

[034-015]superior. Towards his father and mother, she was all they could wish.

[034-016]Diamond had taken a great deal of pains and trouble to find Jim, and had

[034-017]at last succeeded through the help of the tall policeman, who was glad

[034-018]to renew his acquaintance with the strange child. Jim had moved his

[034-019]quarters, and had not heard of Nanny's illness till some time after she

[034-020]was taken to the hospital, where he was too shy to go and inquire about

[034-021]her. But when at length she went to live with Diamond's family, Jim was

[034-022]willing enough to go and see her. It was after one of his visits, during

[034-023]which they had been talking of her new prospects, that Nanny expressed

[034-024]to Diamond her opinion of the country.

[034-025]“There ain't nothing in it but the sun and [[moon]], Diamond.”

[034-026]“There's trees and flowers,” said Diamond.

[034-027]“Well, they ain't no count,” returned Nanny.

[034-028]“Ain't they? They're so beautiful, they make you happy to look at them.”

[034-029]“That's because you're such a silly.”

[034-030]Diamond smiled with a far-away look, as if he were gazing through clouds

[034-031]of green leaves and the vision contented him. But he was thinking with

[034-032]himself what more he could do for Nanny; and that same evening he went

[034-033]to find [[mr-raymond|Mr. Raymond]], for he had heard that he had returned to town.

[034-034]“Ah! how do you do, Diamond?” said Mr. Raymond; “I am glad to see you.”

[034-035]And he was indeed, for he had grown very fond of him. His opinion of him

[034-036]was very different from Nanny's.

[034-037]“What do you want now, my child?” he asked.

[034-038]“I'm always wanting something, sir,” answered Diamond.

[034-039]“Well, that's quite right, so long as what you want is right. Everybody

[034-040]is always wanting something; only we don't mention it in the right place

[034-041]often enough. What is it now?”

[034-042]“There's a friend of Nanny's, a lame boy, called Jim.”

[034-043]“I've heard of him,” said Mr. Raymond. “Well?”

[034-044]“Nanny doesn't care much about going to the country, sir.”

[034-045]“Well, what has that to do with Jim?”

[034-046]“You couldn't find a corner for Jim to work in--could you, sir?”

[034-047]“I don't know that I couldn't. That is, if you can show good reason for

[034-048]it.”

[034-049]“He's a good boy, sir.”

[034-050]“Well, so much the better for him.”

[034-051]“I know he can shine boots, sir.”

[034-052]“So much the better for us.”

[034-053]“You want your boots shined in the country--don't you, sir?”

[034-054]“Yes, to be sure.”

[034-055]“It wouldn't be nice to walk over the flowers with dirty boots--would

[034-056]it, sir?”

[034-057]“No, indeed.”

[034-058]“They wouldn't like it--would they?”

[034-059]“No, they wouldn't.”

[034-060]“Then Nanny would be better pleased to go, sir.”

[034-061]“If the flowers didn't like dirty boots to walk over them, Nanny

[034-062]wouldn't mind going to the country? Is that it? I don't quite see it.”

[034-063]“No, sir; I didn't mean that. I meant, if you would take Jim with you to

[034-064]clean your boots, and do odd jobs, you know, sir, then Nanny would like

[034-065]it better. She's so fond of Jim!”

[034-066]“Now you come to the point, Diamond. I see what you mean, exactly. I

[034-067]will turn it over in my mind. Could you bring Jim to see me?”

[034-068]“I'll try, sir. But they don't mind me much. They think I'm silly,”

[034-069]added Diamond, with one of his sweetest smiles.

[034-070]What Mr. Raymond thought, I dare hardly attempt to put down here. But

[034-071]one part of it was, that the highest wisdom must ever appear folly to

[034-072]those who do not possess it.

[034-073]“I think he would come though--after [[darkness|dark]], you know,” Diamond continued.

[034-074]“He does well at shining boots. People's kind to lame boys, you know,

[034-075]sir. But after dark, there ain't so much doing.”

[034-076]Diamond succeeded in bringing Jim to Mr. Raymond, and the consequence

[034-077]was that he resolved to give the boy a chance. He provided new clothes

[034-078]for both him and Nanny; and upon a certain day, Joseph took his wife and

[034-079]three children, and Nanny and Jim, by train to a certain station in the

[034-080]county of Kent, where they found a cart waiting to carry them and their

[034-081]luggage to The Mound, which was the name of Mr. Raymond's new residence.

[034-082]I will not describe the varied feelings of the party as they went, or

[034-083]when they arrived. All I will say is, that Diamond, who is my only care,

[034-084]was full of quiet [[joy|delight]]--a gladness too deep to talk about.

[034-085]Joseph returned to town the same night, and the next morning drove Ruby

[034-086]and Diamond down, with the carriage behind them, and Mr. Raymond and a

[034-087]lady in the carriage. For Mr. Raymond was an old bachelor no longer: he

[034-088]was bringing his wife with him to live at The Mound. The moment Nanny

[034-089]saw her, she recognised her as the lady who had lent her the ruby-ring.

[034-090]That ring had been given her by Mr. Raymond.

[034-091]The weather was very hot, and the woods very shadowy. There were not a

[034-092]great many wild flowers, for it was getting well towards autumn, and the

[034-093]most of the wild flowers rise early to be before the leaves, because

[034-094]if they did not, they would never get a glimpse of the sun for them. So

[034-095]they have their fun over, and are ready to go to bed again by the time

[034-096]the trees are dressed. But there was plenty of the loveliest grass and

[034-097]daisies about the house, and Diamond's chief pleasure seemed to be to

[034-098]lie amongst them, and breathe the pure air. But all the time, he was

[034-099][[dream|dreaming]] of [[back-of-the-north-wind|the country at the back of the north wind]], and trying to

[034-100]recall the songs the [[the-river|river]] used to sing. For this was more like being at

[034-101]the back of the north wind than anything he had known since he left it.

[034-102]Sometimes he would have his little brother, sometimes his little sister,

[034-103]and sometimes both of them in the grass with him, and then he felt just

[034-104]like a cat with her first kittens, he said, only he couldn't purr--all

[034-105]he could do was to sing.

[034-106]These were very different times from those when he used to drive the

[034-107]cab, but you must not suppose that Diamond was idle. He did not do so

[034-108]much for his mother now, because Nanny occupied his former place; but

[034-109]he helped his father still, both in the stable and the harness-room, and

[034-110]generally went with him on the box that he might learn to drive a pair,

[034-111]and be ready to open the carriage-door. Mr. Raymond advised his father

[034-112]to give him plenty of liberty.

[034-113]“A boy like that,” he said, “ought not to be pushed.”

[034-114]Joseph assented heartily, smiling to himself at the idea of pushing

[034-115]Diamond. After doing everything that fell to his share, the boy had a

[034-116]wealth of time at his disposal. And a happy, sometimes a merry time it

[034-117]was. Only for two months or so, he neither saw nor heard anything of

[034-118][[north-wind|North Wind]].
