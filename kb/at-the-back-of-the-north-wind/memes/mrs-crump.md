## Overview

Mrs. Crump is a housekeeper in the employ of [[mr-raymond|Mr. Raymond]] who serves as a steady domestic presence through the novel's early and closing chapters. She appears during [[diamond|Diamond]]'s earliest childhood — a period he himself cannot remember — tending to him as a very young child left briefly in the Coleman household. Described as "a little cross sometimes" (002-138), she nonetheless performs essential acts of care: leading [[diamond|Diamond]] by the hand, opening doors, conferring with his mother, and participating in household decisions about the boy's welfare.

Her role spans the arc of the novel: she enters during the period of the Coleman family's prosperity and, after [[mr-coleman|Mr. Coleman]]'s financial ruin, relocates with [[mr-raymond|Mr. Raymond]]'s household to a modest house in [[hoxton|Hoxton]] (012-091). She is one of the lasting fixtures of [[diamond|Diamond]]'s world — when [[diamond|Diamond]] later recounts the important people in his life to [[north-wind|North Wind]], he mentions "Mrs. Crump" alongside the Colemans and the clergyman (023-045). Her presence at the story's close signals the continuity of dependable domestic service even through social upheaval.

## Appearance and Character

Mrs. Crump's physical appearance is never described. MacDonald sketches her character entirely through action and narrative commentary. The narrator's remark that "there is a good kind of crossness that is only disagreeable, and there is a bad kind of crossness that is nasty" (002-138) implies that Mrs. Crump possesses the former — a temperament that is brusque but not malicious. This distinction matters in MacDonald's moral economy: a person may be imperfect in manner and yet thoroughly reliable in conduct.

Her characteristic actions throughout the early chapters are escorting, opening, and conferring — she is constantly in motion as an intermediary between spaces and people. She "led him straight into the drawing-room" (002-140); [[diamond|Diamond]] pulls "his hand from Mrs. Crump's" to run toward [[miss-coleman|Miss Coleman]] (002-143); she knocks at [[diamonds-mother|Diamond's mother]]'s door at [[darkness|night]] to return the child (002-168); and she engages in "confabulation" — earnest, extended discussion — with [[diamonds-mother|Diamond's mother]] about the child's needs (002-171).

## Role in the Story

Mrs. Crump's appearances cluster at two structural points:

**1. [[diamond|Diamond]]'s earliest childhood (Chapters II–III)**. During the period when [[diamond|Diamond]] was "a very little child" (002-138), too young to form lasting memories, Mrs. Crump is among the adults who care for him in the Coleman household. This section is narrated retrospectively — [[diamond|Diamond]] himself cannot recall it — which gives Mrs. Crump's actions a ghostly, half-remembered quality. She leads him, carries messages, and participates in the adult conference that determines where the child should [[dream|sleep]]:

> "it was decreed that Mrs. Crump should take him to his mother" (002-166).

She also acts as a potential channel of adult authority — [[diamonds-mother|Diamond's mother]] concludes that "if he had really been brought home to his mother by Mrs. Crump, she would say something to him about it, and that would settle the matter" (002-177). The household's trust in Mrs. Crump is implicit in this assumption: her word carries weight.

**2. The [[hoxton|Hoxton]] relocation (Chapter XII)**. After [[mr-coleman|Mr. Coleman]]'s bankruptcy and the sale of "house, carriage, horses, furniture, and everything" (012-091), [[mr-raymond|Mr. Raymond]] arranges for the household to move:

> "He and his wife and daughter and Mrs. Crump had gone to live in a small house in Hoxton, where he would be unknown" (012-091).

That Mrs. Crump is included in this enumeration — listed alongside family members — indicates her status as an integral part of the household, not a disposable employee. She follows her employers into reduced circumstances, a quiet testament to loyalty.

**3. Mentioned by [[diamond|Diamond]] (Chapter XXIII)**. In a later conversation with [[north-wind|North Wind]], [[diamond|Diamond]] lists the people who matter to him:

> "mentioned Mr. Coleman and Mrs. Coleman, and Miss Coleman, and Mrs. Crump. And then there's the clergyman that spoke to me in the garden that day the tree was blown down" (023-045).

Mrs. Crump's inclusion in [[diamond|Diamond]]'s catalogue of significant persons, alongside the Colemans and the clergyman, confirms that her care left a lasting impression on the boy — even if he could not remember the specific incidents himself.

## Themes and Symbolism

Mrs. Crump embodies the novel's quiet validation of **[[trust|faith]]ful domestic service** as a form of moral participation. In contrast to [[sal|Sal]] — whose rough treatment of [[diamond|Diamond]] represents a failure of care — Mrs. Crump serves willingly and competently. She is not idealized: MacDonald explicitly notes her crossness. But crossness of the "good kind" does not disqualify her from being dependable, and it is dependability, not charm, that the novel values in its servant figures.

Her name may carry a faint comic suggestion ("crump" suggesting crumpled or crusty), but MacDonald does not develop this into satire. She remains a background figure — a structural element of the ordered household. Her presence in the [[hoxton|Hoxton]] cottage at the novel's end suggests that when the social order contracts (from Coleman's wealth to [[mr-raymond|Raymond]]'s modest means), the [[trust|faith]]ful servant contracts with it rather than being discarded.

The **confabulation scene** (002-171) is particularly telling: Mrs. Crump and [[diamonds-mother|Diamond's mother]] talk late into the [[darkness|night]] while [[diamond|Diamond]] falls a[[dream|sleep]]. The word "confabulation" — suggesting unhurried, serious conversation — elevates Mrs. Crump from functionary to confidante. She is entrusted not only with the child's physical care but with the deliberation about his welfare.
