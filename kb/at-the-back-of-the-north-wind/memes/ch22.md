CHAPTER XXII. MR. RAYMOND'S RIDDLE

[022-001]MR. RAYMOND took [[diamond|Diamond]] home with him, stopping at the Mews to tell his

[022-002]mother that he would send him back soon. Diamond ran in with the message

[022-003]himself, and when he reappeared he had in his hand the torn and crumpled

[022-004]book which [[north-wind|North Wind]] had given him.

[022-005]“Ah! I see,” said [[mr-raymond|Mr. Raymond]]: “you are going to claim your sixpence

[022-006]now.”

[022-007]“I wasn't thinking of that so much as of another thing,” said Diamond.

[022-008]“There's a rhyme in this book I can't quite understand. I want you to

[022-009]tell me what it means, if you please.”

[022-010]“I will if I can,” answered Mr. Raymond. “You shall read it to me when

[022-011]we get home, and then I shall see.”

[022-012]Still with a good many blunders, Diamond did read it after a fashion.

[022-013]Mr. Raymond took the little book and read it over again.

[022-014]Now Mr. Raymond was a poet himself, and so, although he had never been

[022-015]at the back of the north wind, he was able to understand the poem pretty

[022-016]well. But before saying anything about it, he read it over aloud, and

[022-017]Diamond thought he understood it much better already.

[022-018]“I'll tell you what I think it means,” he then said. “It means that

[022-019]people may have their way for a while, if they like, but it will get

[022-020]them into such troubles they'll wish they hadn't had it.”

[022-021]“I know, I know!” said Diamond. “Like the poor cabman next door. He

[022-022]drinks too much.”

[022-023]“Just so,” returned Mr. Raymond. “But when people want to do right,

[022-024]things about them will try to help them. Only they must kill the snake,

[022-025]you know.”

[022-026]“I was sure the snake had something to do with it,” cried Diamond

[022-027]triumphantly.

[022-028]A good deal more talk followed, and Mr. Raymond gave Diamond his

[022-029]sixpence.

[022-030]“What will you do with it?” he asked.

[022-031]“Take it home to my mother,” he answered. “She has a teapot--such a

[022-032]black one!--with a broken spout, and she keeps all her money in it. It

[022-033]ain't much; but she saves it up to buy shoes for me. And there's baby

[022-034]coming on famously, and he'll want shoes soon. And every sixpence is

[022-035]something--ain't it, sir?”

[022-036]“To be sure, my man. I hope you'll always make as good a use of your

[022-037]money.”

[022-038]“I hope so, sir,” said Diamond.

[022-039]“And here's a book for you, full of pictures and stories and poems. I

[022-040]wrote it myself, chiefly for the children of the hospital where I hope

[022-041][[nanny|Nanny]] is going. I don't mean I printed it, you know. I made it,” added

[022-042]Mr. Raymond, wishing Diamond to understand that he was the author of the

[022-043]book.

[022-044]“I know what you mean. I make songs myself. They're awfully silly, but

[022-045]they please baby, and that's all they're meant for.”

[022-046]“Couldn't you let me hear one of them now?” said Mr. Raymond.

[022-047]“No, sir, I couldn't. I forget them as soon as I've done with them.

[022-048]Besides, I couldn't make a line without baby on my knee. We make them

[022-049]together, you know. They're just as much baby's as mine. It's he that

[022-050]pulls them out of me.”

[022-051]“I suspect the child's a genius,” said the poet to himself, “and that's

[022-052]what makes people think him silly.”

[022-053]Now if any of my child readers want to know what a genius is--shall

[022-054]I try to tell them, or shall I not? I will give them one very short

[022-055]answer: it means one who understands things without any other body

[022-056]telling him what they mean. God makes a few such now and then to teach

[022-057]the rest of us.

[022-058]“Do you like riddles?” asked Mr. Raymond, turning over the leaves of his

[022-059]own book.

[022-060]“I don't know what a riddle is,” said Diamond.

[022-061]“It's something that means something else, and you've got to find out

[022-062]what the something else is.”

[022-063]Mr. Raymond liked the old-fashioned riddle best, and had written a

[022-064]few--one of which he now read.

[022-065]I have only one foot, but thousands of toes;

[022-066]My one foot stands, but never goes.

[022-067]I have many arms, and they're mighty all;

[022-068]And hundreds of fingers, large and small.

[022-069]From the ends of my fingers my [[beauty]] grows.

[022-070]I breathe with my hair, and I drink with my toes.

[022-071]I grow bigger and bigger about the waist,

[022-072]And yet I am always very tight laced.

[022-073]None e'er saw me eat--I've no mouth to bite;

[022-074]Yet I eat all day in the full sunlight.

[022-075]In the summer with [[music|song]] I shave and quiver,

[022-076]But in winter I fast and groan and shiver.

[022-077]“Do you know what that means, Diamond?” he asked, when he had finished.

[022-078]“No, indeed, I don't,” answered Diamond.

[022-079]“Then you can read it for yourself, and think over it, and see if you

[022-080]can find out,” said Mr. Raymond, giving him the book. “And now you had

[022-081]better go home to your mother. When you've found the riddle, you can

[022-082]come again.”

[022-083]If Diamond had had to find out the riddle in order to see Mr. Raymond

[022-084]again, I doubt if he would ever have seen him.

[022-085]“Oh then,” I think I hear some little reader say, “he could not have

[022-086]been a genius, for a genius finds out things without being told.”

[022-087]I answer, “Genius finds out truths, not tricks.” And if you do not

[022-088]understand that, I am afraid you must be content to wait till you grow

[022-089]older and know more.
