## Definition

In *At the Back of [[north-wind|the North Wind]]*, "dream" is not merely the content of sleep but a narrative and philosophical category that MacDonald uses to dissolve the boundary between the material and spiritual worlds. Throughout the novel, it is never entirely clear whether [[diamond|Diamond]]'s journeys with [[north-wind|the North Wind]] occur in dreams or in waking [[truth|reality]] — and MacDonald deliberately refuses to resolve this ambiguity, because his theological point is that the distinction does not ultimately matter.

## In the Novel

The dream-[[truth|reality]] ambiguity is established from the novel's opening. [[diamond|Diamond]]'s encounters with [[north-wind|the North Wind]] always occur at [[darkness|night]], in or near sleep. "[[diamond|Diamond]] was falling asleep, out blew his cork and hit him on the cheek" (001-067) — the first breach in the wall between waking and dreaming. The [[north-wind|North Wind]] herself, when asked by [[diamonds-mother|Diamond's mother]] whether the boy's stories are true, replies: "I could not say that. But I think the boy is telling what he believes to be true" — a response that frames the entire narrative as a question about the nature of truth rather than a question about facts.

The novel's most explicit treatment of the dream theme comes in its closing pages. Addressing the question of whether [[north-wind|North Wind]] was "only a dream," the narrator writes: "Even if she be a dream, the dream of such a beautiful creature could not be made without a meaning" (038-025). He continues: "there is a still better love than that of the wonderful being you call [[north-wind|North Wind]]. Even if she be a dream, the dream will lead you to the [[truth|reality]]" (038-025).

This is the novel's epistemological key. Whether [[north-wind|North Wind]] exists independently of [[diamond|Diamond]]'s imagination is the wrong question. The right question is whether the experience of her — dream or [[truth|reality]] — has led [[diamond|Diamond]] toward truth, [[kindness|goodness]], and love. By this measure, the dream is more real than many waking experiences.

[[diamond|Diamond]] himself embodies this wisdom. When he is found "lying on the floor of [[the-hay-loft|the loft]]" (038-051), seemingly dead, the narrator does not describe it as tragedy. [[diamond|Diamond]] has been visiting the back of the north wind in dreams throughout his life; now he has simply stayed. The dream has become the permanent [[truth|reality]].

## Key Passages

> "Even if she be a dream, the dream of such a beautiful creature could not be made without a meaning" (038-025)

The novel's central epistemological claim: meaning, not ontological status, is the true measure of [[truth|reality]].

> Diamond was falling asleep, out blew his cork and hit him on the cheek. (001-067)

The first breach between worlds — sleep as the condition for supernatural encounter.

> "I knew that he had gone to the back of the north wind." (038-061)

The dream-journey that has structured [[diamond|Diamond]]'s life becomes, at death, his permanent home.

## Related Characters

- **[[diamond|Diamond]]**: The dreamer through whom the novel's supernatural world is accessed. [[diamond|Diamond]]'s simple [[trust|faith]] means he never agonises over whether his experiences are "real" — he accepts them as given, and in doing so models the novel's ideal response to spiritual experience.
- **[[north-wind|North Wind]]**: She exists in the ambiguous space between dream and [[truth|reality]]. MacDonald never clarifies whether she is an independent being or a projection of [[diamond|Diamond]]'s imagination, because the novel's argument is that the question is misguided.

## Analysis

MacDonald's treatment of dream draws on a long tradition in Romantic and Victorian literature — Coleridge's "Kubla Khan," Keats's "Ode to a Nightingale," Carroll's *Alice* books — but gives it a distinctively theological inflection. For MacDonald, the dream is not an escape from [[truth|reality]] but an access to a deeper [[truth|reality]]. [[diamond|Diamond]] does not retreat into dreams; he advances through them toward the back of the north wind.

This has political implications. Victorian materialism, both in its scientific and its economic forms, insisted that only the measurable and visible were real. MacDonald, a Christian socialist, insisted on the [[truth|reality]] of the invisible: love, justice, the kingdom of [[heaven]]. The dream, in his hands, becomes an argument against the reduction of human experience to what can be weighed and counted.

The novel's dream-logic also serves a literary function. It allows MacDonald to describe the indescribable — [[back-of-the-north-wind|the country at the back of the north wind]] — by appealing to the way dreams present vivid but untranslatable experiences. [[diamond|Diamond]] remembers "a great deal" of his journeys but finds it "very hard to tell" (010-007). Dreams have a coherence that resists narration; so does paradise.
