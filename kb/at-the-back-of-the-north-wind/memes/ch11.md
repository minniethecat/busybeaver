CHAPTER XI. HOW DIAMOND GOT HOME AGAIN

[011-001]WHEN one at the back of the north wind wanted to know how things were

[011-002]going with any one he loved, he had to go to a certain tree, climb the

[011-003]stem, and sit down in the branches. In a few minutes, if he kept very

[011-004]still, he would see something at least of what was going on with the

[011-005]people he loved.

## Section 14

[011-006]One day when [[diamond|Diamond]] was sitting in this tree, he began to long very

[011-007]much to get home again, and no wonder, for he saw his mother crying.

[011-008]Durante says that the people there may always follow their wishes,

[011-009]because they never wish but what is good. Diamond's wish was to get

[011-010]home, and he would fain follow his wish.

[011-011]But how was he to set about it? If he could only see [[north-wind|North Wind]]! But the

[011-012]moment he had got to her back, she was gone altogether from his sight.

[011-013]He had never seen her back. She might be sitting on her doorstep still,

[011-014]looking southwards, and waiting, white and thin and blue-eyed, until she

[011-015]was wanted. Or she might have again become a mighty creature, with

[011-016]power to do that which was demanded of her, and gone far away upon many

[011-017]missions. She must be somewhere, however. He could not go home without

[011-018]her, and therefore he must find her. She could never have intended to

[011-019]leave him always away from his mother. If there had been any danger of

[011-020]that, she would have told him, and given him his choice about going. For

[011-021]North Wind was right honest. How to find North Wind, therefore, occupied

[011-022]all his thoughts.

[011-023]In his anxiety about his mother, he used to climb the tree every day,

[011-024]and sit in its branches. However many of the dwellers there did so, they

[011-025]never incommoded one another; for the moment one got into the tree, he

[011-026]became invisible to every one else; and it was such a wide-spreading

[011-027]tree that there was room for every one of the people of the country

[011-028]in it, without the least interference with each other. Sometimes, on

[011-029]getting down, two of them would meet at the root, and then they would

[011-030]smile to each other more sweetly than at any other time, as much as to

[011-031]say, “Ah, you've been up there too!”

[011-032]One day he was sitting on one of the outer branches of the tree, looking

[011-033]southwards after his home. Far away was a blue shining sea, dotted with

[011-034]gleaming and sparkling specks of white. Those were the icebergs. Nearer

[011-035]he saw a great range of snow-capped mountains, and down below him the

[011-036]lovely meadow-grass of the country, with the stream flowing and flowing

[011-037]through it, away towards the sea. As he looked he began to wonder, for

[011-038]the whole country lay beneath him like a map, and that which was near

[011-039]him looked just as small as that which he knew to be miles away. The

[011-040]ridge of ice which encircled it appeared but a few yards off, and no

[011-041]larger than the row of pebbles with which a child will mark out the

[011-042]boundaries of the kingdom he has appropriated on [[the-sea|the sea-shore]]. He

[011-043]thought he could distinguish the vapoury form of North Wind, seated as

[011-044]he had left her, on the other side. Hastily he descended the tree, and

[011-045]to his [[wonder|amazement]] found that the map or model of the country still lay at

[011-046]his feet. He stood in it. With one stride he had crossed the [[the-river|river]]; with

[011-047]another he had reached the ridge of ice; with the third he stepped over

[011-048]its peaks, and sank wearily down at North Wind's knees. For there she

[011-049]sat on her doorstep. The peaks of the great ridge of ice were as lofty

[011-050]as ever behind her, and the country at her back had vanished from

[011-051]Diamond's view.

[011-052]North Wind was as still as Diamond had left her. Her pale face was white

[011-053]as the snow, and her motionless eyes were as blue as the caverns in the

[011-054]ice. But the instant Diamond touched her, her face began to change like

[011-055]that of one waking from [[dream|sleep]]. [[light|Light]] began to glimmer from the blue of

[011-056]her eyes.

[011-057]A moment more, and she laid her hand on Diamond's head, and began

[011-058]playing with his hair. Diamond took hold of her hand, and laid his face

[011-059]to it. She gave a little start.

[011-060]“How very alive you are, child!” she murmured. “Come nearer to me.”

[011-061]By the help of the stones all around he clambered up beside her, and

[011-062]laid himself against her bosom. She gave a great sigh, slowly lifted her

[011-063]arms, and slowly folded them about him, until she clasped him close. Yet

[011-064]a moment, and she roused herself, and came quite awake; and the cold of

[011-065]her bosom, which had pierced Diamond's bones, vanished.

[011-066]“Have you been sitting here ever since I went through you, dear North

[011-067]Wind?” asked Diamond, stroking her hand.

[011-068]“Yes,” she answered, looking at him with her old kindness.

[011-069]“Ain't you very tired?”

[011-070]“No; I've often had to sit longer. Do you know how long you have been?”

[011-071]“Oh! years and years,” answered Diamond.

[011-072]“You have just been seven days,” returned North Wind.

[011-073]“I thought I had been a hundred years!” exclaimed Diamond.

[011-074]“Yes, I daresay,” replied North Wind. “You've been away from here seven

[011-075]days; but how long you may have been in there is quite another thing.

[011-076]Behind my back and before my face things are so different! They don't go

[011-077]at all by the same rule.”

[011-078]“I'm very glad,” said Diamond, after thinking a while.

[011-079]“Why?” asked North Wind.

[011-080]“Because I've been such a long time there, and such a little while away

[011-081]from mother. Why, she won't be expecting me home from [[sandwich|Sandwich]] yet!”

[011-082]“No. But we mustn't talk any longer. I've got my orders now, and we must

[011-083]be off in a few minutes.”

[011-084]Next moment Diamond found himself sitting alone on the rock. North Wind

[011-085]had vanished. A creature like a great humble-bee or cockchafer flew past

[011-086]his face; but it could be neither, for there were no insects amongst the

[011-087]ice. It passed him again and again, flying in circles around him, and he

[011-088]concluded that it must be North Wind herself, no bigger than Tom Thumb

[011-089]when his mother put him in the nutshell lined with flannel. But she was

[011-090]no longer vapoury and thin. She was solid, although tiny. A moment more,

[011-091]and she perched on his shoulder.

[011-092]“Come along, Diamond,” she said in his ear, in the smallest and highest

[011-093]of treble voices; “it is time we were setting out for Sandwich.”

[011-094]Diamond could just see her, by turning his head towards his shoulder as

[011-095]far as he could, but only with one eye, for his nose came between her

[011-096]and the other.

[011-097]“Won't you take me in your arms and carry me?” he said in a whisper, for

[011-098]he knew she did not like a loud voice when she was small.

[011-099]“Ah! you ungrateful boy,” returned North Wind, smiling “how dare you

[011-100]make game of me? Yes, I will carry you, but you shall walk a bit for

[011-101]your impertinence first. Come along.”

[011-102]She jumped from his shoulder, but when Diamond looked for her upon the

[011-103]ground, he could see nothing but a little spider with long legs that

[011-104]made its way over the ice towards the south. It ran very fast indeed for

[011-105]a spider, but Diamond ran a long way before it, and then waited for

[011-106]it. It was up with him sooner than he had expected, however, and it

[011-107]had grown a good deal. And the spider grew and grew and went faster and

[011-108]faster, till all at once Diamond discovered that it was not a spider,

[011-109]but a weasel; and away glided the weasel, and away went Diamond after

[011-110]it, and it took all the run there was in him to keep up with the weasel.

[011-111]And the weasel grew, and grew, and grew, till all at once Diamond saw

[011-112]that the weasel was not a weasel but a cat. And away went the cat, and

[011-113]Diamond after it. And when he had run half a mile, he found the cat

[011-114]waiting for him, sitting up and washing her face not to lose time. And

[011-115]away went the cat again, and Diamond after it. But the next time he came

[011-116]up with the cat, the cat was not a cat, but a hunting-leopard. And the

[011-117]hunting-leopard grew to a jaguar, all covered with spots like eyes.

[011-118]And the jaguar grew to a Bengal tiger. And at none of them was Diamond

[011-119]afraid, for he had been at North Wind's back, and he could be afraid of

[011-120]her no longer whatever she did or grew. And the tiger flew over the snow

[011-121]in a straight line for the south, growing less and less to Diamond's

[011-122]eyes till it was only a black speck upon the whiteness; and then it

[011-123]vanished altogether. And now Diamond felt that he would rather not run

[011-124]any farther, and that the ice had got very rough. Besides, he was near

[011-125]the precipices that bounded the sea, so he slackened his pace to a walk,

[011-126]saying aloud to himself:

[011-127]“When North Wind has punished me enough for making game of her, she will

[011-128]come back to me; I know she will, for I can't go much farther without

[011-129]her.”

[011-130]“You dear boy! It was only in fun. Here I am!” said North Wind's voice

[011-131]behind him.

[011-132]Diamond turned, and saw her as he liked best to see her, standing beside

[011-133]him, a tall lady.

[011-134]“Where's the tiger?” he asked, for he knew all the creatures from a

[011-135]picture book that [[miss-coleman|Miss Coleman]] had given him. “But, of course,” he

[011-136]added, “you were the tiger. I was puzzled and forgot. I saw it such a

[011-137]long way off before me, and there you were behind me. It's so odd, you

[011-138]know.”

[011-139]“It must look very odd to you, Diamond: I see that. But it is no more

[011-140]odd to me than to break an old pine in two.”

[011-141]“Well, that's odd enough,” remarked Diamond.

[011-142]“So it is! I forgot. Well, none of these things are odder to me than it

[011-143]is to you to eat bread and butter.”

[011-144]“Well, that's odd too, when I think of it,” persisted Diamond. “I should

[011-145]just like a slice of bread and butter! I'm afraid to say how long it

[011-146]is--how long it seems to me, that is--since I had anything to eat.”

[011-147]“Come then,” said North Wind, stooping and holding out her arms. “You

[011-148]shall have some bread and butter very soon. I am glad to find you want

[011-149]some.”

[011-150]Diamond held up his arms to meet hers, and was safe upon her bosom.

[011-151]North Wind bounded into the air. Her tresses began to lift and rise and

[011-152]spread and stream and flow and flutter; and with a roar from her hair

[011-153]and an answering roar from one of the great glaciers beside them, whose

[011-154]slow torrent tumbled two or three icebergs at once into the waves at

[011-155]their feet, North Wind and Diamond went flying southwards.
