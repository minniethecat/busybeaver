CHAPTER XXIII. THE EARLY BIRD

[023-001]WHEN [[diamond|Diamond]] got home he found his father at home already, sitting by

[023-002]the fire and looking rather miserable, for his head ached and he felt

[023-003]sick. He had been doing [[darkness|night]] work of late, and it had not agreed with

[023-004]him, so he had given it up, but not in time, for he had taken some

[023-005]kind of fever. The next day he was forced to keep his bed, and his wife

[023-006]nursed him, and Diamond attended to the baby. If he had not been ill,

[023-007]it would have been delightful to have him at home; and the first day

[023-008]Diamond sang more songs than ever to the baby, and his father listened

[023-009]with some pleasure. But the next he could not bear even Diamond's sweet

[023-010]voice, and was very ill indeed; so Diamond took the baby into his own

[023-011]room, and had no end of quiet games with him there. If he did pull

[023-012]all his bedding on the floor, it did not matter, for he kept baby very

[023-013]quiet, and made the bed himself again, and slept in it with baby all the

[023-014]next night, and many nights after.

[023-015]But long before his father got well, his mother's savings were all but

[023-016]gone. She did not say a word about it in the hearing of her husband,

[023-017]lest she should distress him; and one night, when she could not help

[023-018]crying, she came into Diamond's room that his father might not hear

[023-019]her. She thought Diamond was asleep, but he was not. When he heard her

[023-020]sobbing, he was frightened, and said--

[023-021]“Is father worse, mother?”

[023-022]“No, Diamond,” she answered, as well as she could; “he's a good bit

[023-023]better.”

[023-024]“Then what are you crying for, mother?”

[023-025]“Because my money is almost all gone,” she replied.

[023-026]“O mammy, you make me think of a little poem baby and I learned out of

[023-027][[north-wind|North Wind]]'s book to-day. Don't you remember how I bothered you about

[023-028]some of the words?”

[023-029]“Yes, child,” said his mother heedlessly, thinking only of what she

[023-030]should do after to-morrow.

[023-031]Diamond began and repeated the poem, for he had a wonderful memory.

[023-032]A little bird sat on the edge of her nest;

[023-033]Her yellow-beaks slept as sound as tops;

[023-034]That day she had done her very best,

[023-035]And had filled every one of their little crops.

[023-036]She had filled her own just over-full,

[023-037]And hence she was feeling a little dull.

[023-038]“Oh, dear!” she sighed, as she sat with her head

[023-039]Sunk in her chest, and no neck at all,

[023-040]While her crop stuck out like a feather bed

[023-041]Turned inside out, and rather small;

[023-042]“What shall I do if things don't reform?

[023-043]I don't know where there's a single worm.

[023-044]“I've had twenty to-day, and the children five each,

[023-045]Besides a few flies, and some very fat spiders:

[023-046]No one will say I don't do as I preach--

[023-047]I'm one of the best of bird-providers;

[023-048]But where's the use? We want a storm--

[023-049]I don't know where there's a single worm.”

[023-050]“There's five in my crop,” said a wee, wee bird,

[023-051]Which woke at the voice of his mother's [[suffering|pain]];

[023-052]“I know where there's five.” And with the word

[023-053]He tucked in his head, and went off again.

[023-054]“The folly of childhood,” sighed his mother,

[023-055]“Has always been my especial bother.”

[023-056]The yellow-beaks they slept on and on--

[023-057]They never had heard of the bogy To-morrow;

[023-058]But the mother sat outside, making her moan--

[023-059]She'll soon have to beg, or steal, or borrow.

[023-060]For she never can tell the night before,

[023-061]Where she shall find one red worm more.

[023-062]The fact, as I say, was, she'd had too many;

[023-063]She couldn't [[dream|sleep]], and she called it virtue,

[023-064]Motherly foresight, [[love|affection]], any

[023-065]Name you may call it that will not hurt you,

[023-066]So it was late ere she tucked her head in,

[023-067]And she slept so late it was almost a sin.

[023-068]But the little fellow who knew of five

[023-069]Nor troubled his head about any more,

[023-070]Woke very early, felt quite alive,

[023-071]And wanted a sixth to add to his store:

[023-072]He pushed his mother, the greedy elf,

[023-073]Then thought he had better try for himself.

[023-074]When his mother awoke and had rubbed her eyes,

[023-075]Feeling less like a bird, and more like a mole,

[023-076]She saw him--fancy with what surprise--

[023-077]Dragging a huge worm out of a hole!

[023-078]'Twas of this same hero the proverb took form:

[023-079]'Tis the early bird that catches the worm.

[023-080]“There, mother!” said Diamond, as he finished; “ain't it funny?”

[023-081]“I wish you were like that little bird, Diamond, and could catch worms

[023-082]for yourself,” said his mother, as she rose to go and look after her

[023-083]husband.

[023-084]Diamond lay awake for a few minutes, thinking what he could do to catch

[023-085]worms. It was very little trouble to make up his mind, however, and

[023-086]still less to go to sleep after it.
