CHAPTER X. AT THE BACK OF THE NORTH WIND

[010-001]I HAVE now come to the most difficult part of my story. And why? Because

[010-002]I do not know enough about it. And why should I not know as much about

[010-003]this part as about any other part? For of course I could know nothing

[010-004]about the story except [[diamond|Diamond]] had told it; and why should not Diamond

[010-005]tell about [[back-of-the-north-wind|the country at the back of the north wind]], as well as about

[010-006]his adventures in getting there? Because, when he came back, he had

[010-007]forgotten a great deal, and what he did remember was very hard to tell.

[010-008]Things there are so different from things here! The people there do not

[010-009]speak the same language for one thing. Indeed, Diamond insisted that

[010-010]there they do not speak at all. I do not think he was right, but it may

[010-011]well have appeared so to Diamond. The fact is, we have different reports

[010-012]of the place from the most trustworthy people. Therefore we are bound

[010-013]to believe that it appears somewhat different to different people. All,

[010-014]however, agree in a general way about it.

[010-015]I will tell you something of what two very different people have

[010-016]reported, both of whom knew more about it, I believe, than Herodotus.

[010-017]One of them speaks from his own experience, for he visited the country;

[010-018]the other from the testimony of a young peasant girl who came back from

[010-019]it for a month's visit to her friends. The former was a great Italian

[010-020]of noble family, who died more than five hundred years ago; the latter a

[010-021]Scotch shepherd who died not forty years ago.

[010-022]The Italian, then, informs us that he had to enter that country through

[010-023]a fire so hot that he would have thrown himself into boiling glass to

[010-024]cool himself. This was not Diamond's experience, but then Durante--that

[010-025]was the name of the Italian, and it means Lasting, for his books will

[010-026]last as long as there are enough men in the world worthy of having

[010-027]them--Durante was an elderly man, and Diamond was a little boy, and so

[010-028]their experience must be a little different. The peasant girl, on the

[010-029]other hand, fell fast asleep in a wood, and woke in the same country.

[010-030]In describing it, Durante says that the ground everywhere smelt sweetly,

[010-031]and that a gentle, even-tempered wind, which never blew faster or

[010-032]slower, breathed in his face as he went, making all the leaves point one

[010-033]way, not so as to disturb the birds in the tops of the trees, but, on

[010-034]the contrary, sounding a bass to their [[music|song]]. He describes also a little

[010-035][[the-river|river]] which was so full that its little waves, as it hurried along, bent

[010-036]the grass, full of red and yellow flowers, through which it flowed. He

[010-037]says that the purest stream in the world beside this one would look as

[010-038]if it were mixed with something that did not belong to it, even although

[010-039]it was flowing ever in the brown [[darkness|shadow]] of the trees, and neither sun

[010-040]nor [[moon]] could shine upon it. He seems to imply that it is always the

[010-041]month of May in that country. It would be out of place to describe here

[010-042]the wonderful sights he saw, for the [[song|music]] of them is in another key

[010-043]from that of this story, and I shall therefore only add from the account

[010-044]of this traveller, that the people there are so free and so just and so

[010-045]healthy, that every one of them has a crown like a king and a mitre like

[010-046]a priest.

[010-047]The peasant girl--Kilmeny was her name--could not report such grand

[010-048]things as Durante, for, as the shepherd says, telling her story as I

[010-049]tell Diamond's--

[010-050]“Kilmeny had been she knew not where,

[010-051]And Kilmeny had seen what she could not declare;

[010-052]Kilmeny had been where the cock never crew,

[010-053]Where the rain never fell, and the wind never blew.

[010-054]But it seemed as the harp of the sky had rung,

[010-055]And the airs of [[heaven]] played round her tongue,

[010-056]When she spoke of the lovely forms she had seen,

[010-057]And a land where sin had never been;

[010-058]A land of love and a land of light,

[010-059]Withouten sun, or moon, or night;

[010-060]Where the river swayed a living stream,

[010-061]And the light a pure and cloudless beam:

[010-062]The land of vision it would seem,

[010-063]And still an everlasting dream.”

[010-064]The last two lines are the shepherd's own remark, and a matter of

[010-065]opinion. But it is clear, I think, that Kilmeny must have described

[010-066]the same country as Durante saw, though, not having his experience, she

[010-067]could neither understand nor describe it so well.

[010-068]Now I must give you such fragments of recollection as Diamond was able

[010-069]to bring back with him.

[010-070]When he came to himself after he fell, he found himself at the back of

[010-071]the north wind. [[north-wind|North Wind]] herself was nowhere to be seen. Neither

[010-072]was there a vestige of snow or of ice within sight. The sun too had

[010-073]vanished; but that was no matter, for there was plenty of a certain

[010-074]still rayless light. Where it came from he never found out; but he

[010-075]thought it belonged to the country itself. Sometimes he thought it came

[010-076]out of the flowers, which were very bright, but had no strong colour.

[010-077]He said the river--for all agree that there is a river there--flowed

[010-078]not only through, but over grass: its channel, instead of being rock,

[010-079]stones, pebbles, sand, or anything else, was of pure meadow grass, not

[010-080]over long. He insisted that if it did not sing tunes in people's ears,

[010-081]it sung tunes in their heads, in proof of which I may mention that, in

[010-082]the troubles which followed, Diamond was often heard singing; and when

[010-083]asked what he was singing, would answer, “One of the tunes the river

[010-084]at the back of the north wind sung.” And I may as well say at once that

[010-085]Diamond never told these things to any one but--no, I had better not say

[010-086]who it was; but whoever it was told me, and I thought it would be well

[010-087]to write them for my child-readers.

[010-088]He could not say he was very happy there, for he had neither his father

[010-089]nor mother with him, but he felt so still and quiet and patient and

[010-090]contented, that, as far as the mere feeling went, it was something

[010-091]better than mere [[joy|happiness]]. Nothing went wrong at the back of the north

[010-092]wind. Neither was anything quite right, he thought. Only everything was

[010-093]going to be right some day. His account disagreed with that of Durante,

[010-094]and agreed with that of Kilmeny, in this, that he protested there was no

[010-095]wind there at all. I fancy he missed it. At all events we could not do

[010-096]without wind. It all depends on how big our lungs are whether the wind

[010-097]is too strong for us or not.

[010-098]When the person he told about it asked him whether he saw anybody he

[010-099]knew there, he answered, “Only a little girl belonging to the gardener,

[010-100]who thought he had lost her, but was quite mistaken, for there she was

[010-101]safe enough, and was to come back some day, as I came back, if they

[010-102]would only wait.”

[010-103]“Did you talk to her, Diamond?”

[010-104]“No. Nobody talks there. They only look at each other, and understand

[010-105]everything.”

[010-106]“Is it cold there?”

[010-107]“No.”

[010-108]“Is it hot?”

[010-109]“No.”

[010-110]“What is it then?”

[010-111]“You never think about such things there.”

[010-112]“What a queer place it must be!”

[010-113]“It's a very good place.”

[010-114]“Do you want to go back again?”

[010-115]“No; I don't think I have left it; I feel it here, somewhere.”

[010-116]“Did the people there look pleased?”

[010-117]“Yes--quite pleased, only a little sad.”

[010-118]“Then they didn't look glad?”

[010-119]“They looked as if they were waiting to be gladder some day.”

[010-120]This was how Diamond used to answer questions about that country. And

[010-121]now I will take up the story again, and tell you how he got back to this

[010-122]country.
