## Overview

Sleep in *At the Back of [[north-wind|the North Wind]]* is never merely rest. It is the **portal** through which [[diamond|Diamond]] enters the world of [[north-wind|North Wind]], and by extension, [[back-of-the-north-wind|the country at the back of the north wind]]. MacDonald uses [[dream|sleep]] as a liminal state — a threshold between waking life and the transcendent [[truth|reality]] that lies beyond ordinary perception. Throughout the novel, [[dream|sleep]] blurs into dream, dream into journey, and journey into death, making [[dream|sleep]] the central metaphor for the soul's passage between worlds.

## Sleep as Portal

[[diamond|Diamond]]'s adventures with [[north-wind|North Wind]] always begin with [[dream|sleep]]. The novel's opening chapters establish the pattern: [[diamond|Diamond]] goes to bed, falls a[[dream|sleep]], and wakes — or seems to wake — in [[north-wind|North Wind]]'s presence. She enters through the cracks in the wall, through the window, or simply materializes at his bedside.

The narrative deliberately blurs whether [[diamond|Diamond]] is "really" a[[dream|sleep]]:

> "Diamond had fallen fast asleep is very evident from the strange things that now happened to him" (008-001)

His mother and father interpret his [[darkness|night]] journeys as [[dream|sleep]]walking: "his mother thought he had been walking in his [[dream|sleep]]" (003-048). This ambiguity is central — MacDonald refuses to resolve whether [[diamond|Diamond]]'s experiences are [[dream|dreams]] or genuine supernatural encounters, suggesting that the question itself misses the point. What matters is the *[[truth|reality]]* of the experience to [[diamond|Diamond]] and its transformative effect on his character.

## Sleep and Death

The novel progressively links [[dream|sleep]] to death. In the later chapters, [[diamond|Diamond]]'s [[dream|sleep]] becomes deeper, longer, and harder to wake from. The country at the back of the north wind is explicitly described as a place one can only reach by passing through a kind of death — and [[dream|sleep]] is the rehearsal for this passage.

> "He seemed to fall asleep sometimes, only through the sleep he heard the sounds going on" (009-275, 009-276)

This blurring of [[dream|sleep]] and death reflects MacDonald's Christian Platonism: earthly life is a kind of [[dream|sleep]] from which we wake into true [[truth|reality]]. [[diamond|Diamond]]'s final "[[dream|sleep]]" is his death, but MacDonald presents it not as an ending but as a full awakening into the country he has only visited in [[dream|dreams]].

## Sleepwalking as Double Consciousness

[[diamond|Diamond]]'s "[[dream|sleep]]walking" is a narrative device for **double consciousness** — the ability to inhabit two realities simultaneously. His body remains in bed while his soul travels. This is not presented as pathology but as gift: [[diamond|Diamond]] is one of those rare souls who can cross the threshold while still alive.

The adults around him see only the physical symptoms — a boy walking in his [[dream|sleep]], a child who needs to be held down. They cannot perceive the [[truth|reality]] of his journeys. This gap between adult perception and [[diamond|Diamond]]'s experience is one of the novel's central tensions.
