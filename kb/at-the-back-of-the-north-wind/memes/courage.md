## Definition

In *At the Back of [[north-wind|the North Wind]]*, courage is presented not as innate fearlessness but as a capacity that must be learned through experience and trust. The [[north-wind|North Wind]] explicitly teaches [[diamond|Diamond]] that courage is the opposite of fear — not its absence, but the ability to act despite it. MacDonald frames courage as a moral and spiritual quality rather than a physical one, tied to [[trust|faith]] in others and in the [[kindness|goodness]] of the world.

## In the Novel

The novel's central lesson on courage comes from [[north-wind|the North Wind]] herself when she tells [[diamond|Diamond]]: "You had to be taught what courage was" (007-129). This formulation is crucial — courage is not something [[diamond|Diamond]] possesses naturally but something he acquires through his encounters with the supernatural. The [[north-wind|North Wind]]'s pedagogy involves exposing [[diamond|Diamond]] to terrifying situations precisely so he can learn to overcome his fear.

The first significant test of [[diamond|Diamond]]'s courage occurs early, when he rides [[north-wind|the North Wind]] through the storm. "[[diamond|Diamond]] began to grow warm, [[diamond|Diamond]]'s courage began to grow" (004-220) — the physical sensation of warmth is paired with the moral quality of courage, suggesting that courage is embodied, not abstract. Later, as they fly, he "had not ridden far before he found courage to reach forward and catch hold" of [[north-wind|the North Wind]]'s hair (005-030). Courage here is incremental — a gradual extension of trust.

The [[north-wind|North Wind]]'s sharpest lesson distinguishes courage from mere ignorance of danger. When [[diamond|Diamond]] protests that he does not see why something requires courage, she responds: "I don't see that. It's not courage at all, so long as you don't see the danger" (006-051). True courage, in MacDonald's rendering, requires full awareness of what one faces — it is not bravery born of blindness but trust born of knowledge.

After [[diamond|Diamond]]'s first flight, when he awakes, [[north-wind|the North Wind]] "gave him back his courage" (005-269). The verb "gave" is theologically significant — courage is a gift, not an achievement. This aligns with MacDonald's broader Christian vision in which virtue is received rather than manufactured.

In the embedded "Little [[princess-daylight|Daylight]]" tale in Chapter XXVIII, the prince approaches his task "with what courage he could summon, to meet the" challenge before him (028-111). The phrase "could summon" underscores that courage even for a fairy-tale prince is an effort of will, not an automatic endowment.

## Key Passages

> "You had to be taught what courage was." (007-129)

The [[north-wind|North Wind]]'s definitive statement on courage as an acquired virtue, learned through experience rather than inherited by nature.

> "It's not courage at all, so long as you don't see the danger." (006-051)

The novel's distinction between true courage and mere ignorance — courage requires awareness, or it is not courage at all.

## Courage as Embodied Virtue

One of MacDonald's most distinctive contributions to the novel's moral vision is his insistence that courage is embodied — felt in the body before it is understood by the mind. When [[diamond|Diamond]] flies through the storm, the narrative does not describe a mental decision to be brave but a physical transformation: warmth replaces cold, openness replaces contraction. "Courage was reviving in his little heart" (007-110) — the verb "reviving" suggests that courage is a natural state temporarily lost, not a foreign quality to be imported. The heart, for MacDonald, is the seat of courage, and the body's sensations are its language. This embodied conception distinguishes the novel's moral pedagogy from purely intellectual approaches to virtue: [[diamond|Diamond]] does not think his way into courage but feels his way into it, through the literal warmth of [[north-wind|the North Wind]]'s presence and the physical sensation of flight.

## Courage and Gender

MacDonald's treatment of courage subtly unsettles Victorian gender expectations. The novel's primary teacher of courage is [[north-wind|the North Wind]] — a powerful, physically imposing female figure who is herself "fearless" (007-113). [[diamond|Diamond]], the male protagonist, is the pupil, not the exemplar. This inversion of the expected gender dynamic — in which men teach courage and women require protection — is characteristic of MacDonald's broader resistance to Victorian gender conventions. [[diamond|Diamond]]'s courage is gentle rather than aggressive, receptive rather than assertive: he learns courage by surrendering to [[north-wind|the North Wind]]'s power, not by exercising his own. The novel thus presents a feminised model of courage as trust, in contrast to the masculine model of courage as domination that prevailed in much Victorian children's literature.

## Related Characters

- **[[diamond]]**: The novel's primary pupil of courage, who learns through terrifying adventures that courage is trust in action. His name itself — [[diamond|Diamond]] — suggests something forged under pressure.
- **[[north-wind]]**: Courage's teacher, who exposes [[diamond|Diamond]] to fear precisely so he can learn to overcome it. Her method is experiential, not didactic. Her own fearlessness (007-113) is the model toward which [[diamond|Diamond]] progresses.
