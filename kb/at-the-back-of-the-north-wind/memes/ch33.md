CHAPTER XXXIII. THE PROSPECT BRIGHTENS

[033-001]THE next morning, [[diamonds-mother|Diamond's mother]] said to his father, “I'm not quite

[033-002]comfortable about that child again.”

[033-003]“Which child, Martha?” asked [[diamonds-father|Joseph]]. “You've got a choice now.”

[033-004]“Well, [[diamond|Diamond]] I mean. I'm afraid he's getting into his queer ways

[033-005]again. He's been at his old trick of walking in his [[dream|sleep]]. I saw him run

[033-006]up the stair in the middle of the [[darkness|night]].”

[033-007]“Didn't you go after him, wife?”

[033-008]“Of course I did--and found him fast asleep in his bed. It's because

[033-009]he's had so little meat for the last six weeks, I'm afraid.”

[033-010]“It may be that. I'm very sorry. But if it don't please God to send us

[033-011]enough, what am I to do, wife?”

[033-012]“You can't help it, I know, my dear good man,” returned Martha. “And

[033-013]after all I don't know. I don't see why he shouldn't get on as well as

[033-014]the rest of us. There I'm nursing baby all this time, and I get along

[033-015]pretty well. I'm sure, to hear the little man [[music|singing]], you wouldn't

[033-016]think there was much amiss with him.”

[033-017]For at that moment Diamond was [[song|singing]] like a lark in the clouds. He had

[033-018]the new baby in his arms, while his mother was dressing herself. Joseph

[033-019]was sitting at his breakfast--a little weak tea, dry bread, and very

[033-020]dubious butter--which [[nanny|Nanny]] had set for him, and which he was enjoying

[033-021]because he was hungry. He had groomed both horses, and had got old

[033-022]Diamond harnessed ready to put to.

[033-023]“Think of a fat [[angel]], Dulcimer!” said Diamond.

[033-024]The baby had not been christened yet, but Diamond, in reading his Bible,

[033-025]had come upon the word dulcimer, and thought it so pretty that ever

[033-026]after he called his sister Dulcimer!

[033-027]“Think of a red, fat angel, Dulcimer!” he repeated; “for Ruby's an angel

[033-028]of a horse, Dulcimer. He sprained his ankle and got fat on purpose.”

[033-029]“What purpose, Diamond?” asked his father.

[033-030]“Ah! that I can't tell. I suppose to look handsome when his master

[033-031]comes,” answered Diamond.--“What do you think, Dulcimer? It must be for

[033-032]some good, for Ruby's an angel.”

[033-033]“I wish I were rid of him, anyhow,” said his father; “for he weighs

[033-034]heavy on my mind.”

[033-035]“No wonder, father: he's so fat,” said Diamond. “But you needn't be

[033-036]afraid, for everybody says he's in better condition than when you had

[033-037]him.”

[033-038]“Yes, but he may be as thin as a tin horse before his owner comes. It

[033-039]was too bad to leave him on my hands this way.”

[033-040]“Perhaps he couldn't help it,” suggested Diamond. “I daresay he has some

[033-041]good reason for it.”

[033-042]“So I should have said,” returned his father, “if he had not driven such

[033-043]a hard bargain with me at first.”

[033-044]“But we don't know what may come of it yet, husband,” said his wife.

[033-045]“[[mr-raymond|Mr. Raymond]] may give a little to boot, seeing you've had more of the

[033-046]bargain than you wanted or reckoned upon.”

[033-047]“I'm afraid not: he's a hard man,” said Joseph, as he rose and went to

[033-048]get his cab out.

[033-049]Diamond resumed his singing. For some time he carolled snatches of

[033-050]everything or anything; but at last it settled down into something like

[033-051]what follows. I cannot tell where or how he got it.

[033-052]Where did you come from, baby dear?

[033-053]Out of the everywhere into here.

[033-054]Where did you get your eyes so blue?

[033-055]Out of the sky as I came through.

[033-056]What makes the light in them sparkle and spin?

[033-057]Some of the starry spikes left in.

[033-058]Where did you get that little tear?

[033-059]I found it waiting when I got here.

[033-060]What makes your forehead so smooth and high?

[033-061]A soft hand stroked it as I went by.

[033-062]What makes your cheek like a warm white rose?

[033-063]I saw something better than any one knows.

[033-064]Whence that three-cornered smile of bliss?

[033-065]Three angels gave me at once a kiss.

[033-066]Where did you get this pearly ear?

[033-067]God spoke, and it came out to hear.

[033-068]Where did you get those arms and hands?

[033-069][[love|Love]] made itself into hooks and bands.

[033-070]Feet, whence did you come, you darling things?

[033-071]From the same box as the cherubs' wings.

[033-072]How did they all just come to be you?

[033-073]God thought about me, and so I grew.

[033-074]But how did you come to us, you dear?

[033-075]God thought about you, and so I am here.

[033-076]“You never made that song, Diamond,” said his mother.

[033-077]“No, mother. I wish I had. No, I don't. That would be to take it from

[033-078]somebody else. But it's mine for all that.”

[033-079]“What makes it yours?”

[033-080]“I love it so.”

[033-081]“Does loving a thing make it yours?”

[033-082]“I think so, mother--at least more than anything else can. If I didn't

[033-083]love baby (which couldn't be, you know) she wouldn't be mine a bit. But

[033-084]I do love baby, and baby is my very own Dulcimer.”

[033-085]“The baby's mine, Diamond.”

[033-086]“That makes her the more mine, mother.”

[033-087]“How do you make that out?”

[033-088]“Because you're mine, mother.”

[033-089]“Is that because you love me?”

[033-090]“Yes, just because. Love makes the only myness,” said Diamond.

[033-091]When his father came home to have his dinner, and change Diamond for

[033-092]Ruby, they saw him look very sad, and he told them he had not had a fare

[033-093]worth mentioning the whole morning.

[033-094]“We shall all have to go to the workhouse, wife,” he said.

[033-095]“It would be better to go to the back of the north wind,” said Diamond,

[033-096]dreamily, not intending to say it aloud.

[033-097]“So it would,” answered his father. “But how are we to get there,

[033-098]Diamond?”

[033-099]“We must wait till we're taken,” returned Diamond.

[033-100]Before his father could speak again, a knock came to the door, and in

[033-101]walked Mr. Raymond with a smile on his face. Joseph got up and received

[033-102]him respectfully, but not very cordially. Martha set a chair for him,

[033-103]but he would not sit down.

[033-104]“You are not very glad to see me,” he said to Joseph. “You don't want to

[033-105]part with the old horse.”

[033-106]“Indeed, sir, you are mistaken there. What with anxiety about him, and

[033-107]bad luck, I've wished I were rid of him a thousand times. It was only to

[033-108]be for three months, and here it's eight or nine.”

[033-109]“I'm sorry to hear such a statement,” said Mr. Raymond. “Hasn't he been

[033-110]of service to you?”

[033-111]“Not much, not with his lameness”

[033-112]“Ah!” said Mr. Raymond, hastily--“you've been laming him--have you? That

[033-113]accounts for it. I see, I see.”

[033-114]“It wasn't my fault, and he's all right now. I don't know how it

[033-115]happened, but--”

[033-116]“He did it on purpose,” said Diamond. “He put his foot on a stone just

[033-117]to twist his ankle.”

[033-118]“How do you know that, Diamond?” said his father, turning to him. “I

[033-119]never said so, for I could not think how it came.”

[033-120]“I heard it--in the stable,” answered Diamond.

[033-121]“Let's have a look at him,” said Mr. Raymond.

[033-122]“If you'll step into the yard,” said Joseph, “I'll bring him out.”

[033-123]They went, and Joseph, having first taken off his harness, walked Ruby

[033-124]into the middle of the yard.

[033-125]“Why,” said Mr. Raymond, “you've not been using him well.”

[033-126]“I don't know what you mean by that, sir. I didn't expect to hear that

[033-127]from you. He's sound in wind and limb--as sound as a barrel.”

[033-128]“And as big, you might add. Why, he's as fat as a pig! You don't call

[033-129]that good usage!”

[033-130]Joseph was too angry to make any answer.

[033-131]“You've not worked him enough, I say. That's not making good use of him.

[033-132]That's not doing as you'd be done by.”

[033-133]“I shouldn't be sorry if I was served the same, sir.”

[033-134]“He's too fat, I say.”

[033-135]“There was a whole month I couldn't work him at all, and he did nothing

[033-136]but eat his head off. He's an awful eater. I've taken the best part of

[033-137]six hours a day out of him since, but I'm always afraid of his coming to

[033-138]grief again, and so I couldn't make the most even of that. I declare to

[033-139]you, sir, when he's between the shafts, I sit on the box as miserable as

[033-140]if I'd stolen him. He looks all the time as if he was a bottling up of

[033-141]complaints to make of me the minute he set eyes on you again. There!

[033-142]look at him now, squinting round at me with one eye! I declare to you,

[033-143]on my word, I haven't laid the whip on him more than three times.”

[033-144]“I'm glad to hear it. He never did want the whip.”

[033-145]“I didn't say that, sir. If ever a horse wanted the whip, he do. He's

[033-146]brought me to beggary almost with his snail's pace. I'm very glad you've

[033-147]come to rid me of him.”

[033-148]“I don't know that,” said Mr. Raymond. “Suppose I were to ask you to buy

[033-149]him of me--cheap.”

[033-150]“I wouldn't have him in a present, sir. I don't like him. And I wouldn't

[033-151]drive a horse that I didn't like--no, not for gold. It can't come to

[033-152]good where there's no love between 'em.”

[033-153]“Just bring out your own horse, and let me see what sort of a pair

[033-154]they'd make.”

[033-155]Joseph laughed rather bitterly as he went to fetch Diamond.

[033-156]When the two were placed side by side, Mr. Raymond could hardly keep

[033-157]his countenance, but from a mingling of feelings. Beside the great,

[033-158]red, round barrel, Ruby, all body and no legs, Diamond looked like a

[033-159]clothes-horse with a skin thrown over it. There was hardly a spot of

[033-160]him where you could not descry some sign of a bone underneath. Gaunt and

[033-161]grim and weary he stood, kissing his master, and heeding no one else.

[033-162]“You haven't been using him well,” said Mr. Raymond.

[033-163]“I must say,” returned Joseph, throwing an arm round his horse's neck,

[033-164]“that the remark had better have been spared, sir. The horse is worth

[033-165]three of the other now.”

[033-166]“I don't think so. I think they make a very nice pair. If the one's too

[033-167]fat, the other's too lean--so that's all right. And if you won't buy my

[033-168]Ruby, I must buy your Diamond.”

[033-169]“Thank you, sir,” said Joseph, in a tone implying anything but thanks.

[033-170]“You don't seem to like the proposal,” said Mr. Raymond.

[033-171]“I don't,” returned Joseph. “I wouldn't part with my old Diamond for his

[033-172]skin as full of nuggets as it is of bones.”

[033-173]“Who said anything about parting with him?”

[033-174]“You did now, sir.”

[033-175]“No; I didn't. I only spoke of buying him to make a pair with Ruby. We

[033-176]could pare Ruby and patch Diamond a bit. And for height, they are as

[033-177]near a match as I care about. Of course you would be the coachman--if

[033-178]only you would consent to be reconciled to Ruby.”

[033-179]Joseph stood bewildered, unable to answer.

[033-180]“I've bought a small place in Kent,” continued Mr. Raymond, “and I must

[033-181]have a pair to my carriage, for the roads are hilly thereabouts. I don't

[033-182]want to make a show with a pair of high-steppers. I think these will

[033-183]just do. Suppose, for a week or two, you set yourself to take Ruby down

[033-184]and bring Diamond up. If we could only lay a pipe from Ruby's sides into

[033-185]Diamond's, it would be the work of a moment. But I fear that wouldn't

[033-186]answer.”

[033-187]A strong inclination to laugh intruded upon Joseph's inclination to cry,

[033-188]and made speech still harder than before.

[033-189]“I beg your pardon, sir,” he said at length. “I've been so miserable,

[033-190]and for so long, that I never thought you was only a chaffing of me

[033-191]when you said I hadn't used the horses well. I did grumble at you, sir,

[033-192]many's the time in my trouble; but whenever I said anything, my little

[033-193]Diamond would look at me with a smile, as much as to say: 'I know him

[033-194]better than you, father;' and upon my word, I always thought the boy

[033-195]must be right.”

[033-196]“Will you sell me old Diamond, then?”

[033-197]“I will, sir, on one condition--that if ever you want to part with him

[033-198]or me, you give me the option of buying him. I could not part with him,

[033-199]sir. As to who calls him his, that's nothing; for, as Diamond says, it's

[033-200]only loving a thing that can make it yours--and I do love old Diamond,

[033-201]sir, dearly.”

[033-202]“Well, there's a cheque for twenty pounds, which I wrote to offer you

[033-203]for him, in case I should find you had done the handsome thing by Ruby.

[033-204]Will that be enough?”

[033-205]“It's too much, sir. His body ain't worth it--shoes and all. It's only

[033-206]his heart, sir--that's worth millions--but his heart'll be mine all the

[033-207]same--so it's too much, sir.”

[033-208]“I don't think so. It won't be, at least, by the time we've got him

[033-209]fed up again. You take it and welcome. Just go on with your cabbing for

[033-210]another month, only take it out of Ruby and let Diamond rest; and by

[033-211]that time I shall be ready for you to go down into the country.”

[033-212]“Thank you, sir, thank you. Diamond set you down for a friend, sir, the

[033-213]moment he saw you. I do believe that child of mine knows more than other

[033-214]people.”

[033-215]“I think so, too,” said Mr. Raymond as he walked away.

[033-216]He had meant to test Joseph when he made the bargain about Ruby, but had

[033-217]no intention of so greatly prolonging the trial. He had been taken ill

[033-218]in Switzerland, and had been quite unable to return sooner. He went away

[033-219]now highly gratified at finding that he had stood the test, and was a

[033-220]true man.

[033-221]Joseph rushed in to his wife who had been standing at the window

[033-222]anxiously waiting the result of the long colloquy. When she heard that

[033-223]the horses were to go together in double harness, she burst forth into

[033-224]an immoderate fit of laughter. Diamond came up with the baby in his arms

[033-225]and made big anxious eyes at her, saying--

[033-226]“What is the matter with you, mother dear? Do cry a little. It will do

[033-227]you good. When father takes ever so small a drop of spirits, he puts

[033-228]water to it.”

[033-229]“You silly darling!” said his mother; “how could I but laugh at the

[033-230]notion of that great fat Ruby going side by side with our poor old

[033-231]Diamond?”

[033-232]“But why not, mother? With a month's oats, and nothing to do, Diamond'll

[033-233]be nearer Ruby's size than you will father's. I think it's very good for

[033-234]different sorts to go together. Now Ruby will have a chance of teaching

[033-235]Diamond better manners.”

[033-236]“How dare you say such a thing, Diamond?” said his father, angrily.

[033-237]“To compare the two for manners, there's no comparison possible. Our

[033-238]Diamond's a gentleman.”

[033-239]“I don't mean to say he isn't, father; for I daresay some gentlemen

[033-240]judge their neighbours unjustly. That's all I mean. Diamond shouldn't

[033-241]have thought such bad things of Ruby. He didn't try to make the best of

[033-242]him.”

[033-243]“How do you know that, pray?”

[033-244]“I heard them talking about it one night.”

[033-245]“Who?”

[033-246]“Why Diamond and Ruby. Ruby's an angel.”

[033-247]Joseph stared and said no more. For all his new [[joy|gladness]], he was very

[033-248]gloomy as he re-harnessed the angel, for he thought his darling Diamond

[033-249]was going out of his mind.

[033-250]He could not help thinking rather differently, however, when he found

[033-251]the change that had come over Ruby. Considering his fat, he exerted

[033-252]himself amazingly, and got over the ground with incredible speed. So

[033-253]willing, even anxious, was he to go now, that Joseph had to hold him

[033-254]quite tight.

[033-255]Then as he laughed at his own fancies, a new fear came upon him lest the

[033-256]horse should break his wind, and Mr. Raymond have good cause to think

[033-257]he had not been using him well. He might even suppose that he had taken

[033-258]advantage of his new instructions, to let out upon the horse some of his

[033-259]pent-up dislike; whereas in truth, it had so utterly vanished that he

[033-260]felt as if Ruby, too, had been his friend all the time.
