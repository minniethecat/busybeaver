## Definition

Trust in the novel is the capacity to accept guidance from a power one does not fully understand, to venture into [[darkness|darkness]] confident that the [[darkness|dark]]ness is benevolent. [[diamond|Diamond]]'s trust in [[north-wind|North Wind]] is the novel's central moral and theological virtue — it is faith stripped of doctrine, reduced to the child's willingness to be carried. The narrator notes that "the child was so ready to trust" (036-003), identifying this disposition as [[diamond|Diamond]]'s defining spiritual quality.

Trust in MacDonald's framework is not blind credulity but a reasoned response to demonstrated [[kindness|goodness]]. [[diamond|Diamond]] trusts [[north-wind|North Wind]] because she has proven herself trustworthy — she is powerful, yes, but also gentle with him. His trust grows through experience, not despite it.

## In the Novel

[[diamond|Diamond]]'s trust in [[north-wind|North Wind]] is established in their very first encounter. When she appears through a chink in the wall of his hay-loft, he is [[fear|fright]]ened but does not flee. When she asks him to come out, he agrees — a child stepping into the arms of a being he has only just met. After she carries him through the [[darkness|night]] sky, the narrator observes that "he had trusted her so thoroughly" (009-342) that he no longer questioned her intentions.

His trust is demonstrated repeatedly in his willingness to go wherever [[north-wind|North Wind]] takes him, even when the destination is [[fear|fright]]ening or incomprehensible. When she flies him over the sea, he does not cling or protest; when she disappears for long stretches, he trusts she will return. His trust is not naive — he feels fear — but it overrides fear. "I can't trust," says one character (007-094), but [[diamond|Diamond]] can and does.

The novel contrasts [[diamond|Diamond]]'s trust with adult hesitation. [[diamonds-mother|Diamond's mother]]'s response — "I don't know so much that way, husband" (029-139) — is natural and even wise, but it marks the difference between the child's direct confidence and the adult's calculated prudence. Similarly, [[diamonds-father|Diamond's father]] must learn to trust his son: "he knew his boy, you see, and would trust him" (021-061). MacDonald does not condemn the adults; he simply shows that [[diamond|Diamond]]'s way is the more direct path to the divine.

The most profound expression of trust comes in the novel's final chapters. Before [[diamond|Diamond]] tells [[mr-raymond|Mr. Raymond]] his secret about the [[back-of-the-north-wind|back of the north wind]], the narrator notes that "he trusted me with this" (036-006) only after a period of testing. Trust, for [[diamond|Diamond]], is not given indiscriminately — it is earned through relationship.

## Key Passages

> "Dear me!" said Diamond, and thought a minute. "I think, then, you can hardly expect me to keep a window open with my breath." (001-121)

[[diamond|Diamond]]'s guileless literal-mindedness — the foundation of his trust — is on display in this early exchange with [[north-wind|North Wind]].

> "I don't know so much that way, husband," said Diamond's mother. (029-139)

The contrast between the mother's cautious epistemology and [[diamond|Diamond]]'s trusting epistemology is central to the novel's moral scheme.

> The child was so ready to trust. Upon one subject alone was he reticent. (036-003)

The narrator explicitly identifies trust as [[diamond|Diamond]]'s characteristic disposition, while noting the one area — the [[back-of-the-north-wind|back of the north wind]] — where even [[diamond|Diamond]] exercises reserve.

## Analysis

Trust in *At the Back of [[north-wind|the North Wind]]* functions as MacDonald's child-theology in microcosm. The Victorian religious landscape was marked by doctrinal controversy and the erosion of traditional faith by scientific rationalism. MacDonald's response, embodied in [[diamond|Diamond]], is to bypass intellectual argument entirely and locate religious truth in the child's unguarded capacity to trust what is good.

This is not anti-intellectualism but a different epistemology. [[diamond|Diamond]] does not trust because he is ignorant; he trusts because he is open. Adults in the novel — even the good ones — have lost this capacity, and their moral journey involves recovering something [[diamond|Diamond]] never lost. [[mr-raymond|Mr. Raymond]], the most educated character in the book, becomes [[diamond|Diamond]]'s student in this regard.

Theologically, trust is MacDonald's translation of the Protestant doctrine of *sola fide* (faith alone) into narrative form. [[diamond|Diamond]] is saved — literally carried to the [[back-of-the-north-wind|back of the north wind]] — not by works or knowledge but by trust. Yet MacDonald's trust is not passive; it issues in action. [[diamond|Diamond]] trusts [[north-wind|North Wind]], and therefore he helps [[nanny|Nanny]], drives his cab honestly, and treats every person with [[kindness|kindness]]. Trust is the root; [[kindness|kindness]] and [[love|love]] are the fruit.
