# Hoxton

**Hoxton** is a working-class district in East [[london|London]] that appears in the later chapters of *At the Back of [[north-wind|the North Wind]]* as the place where [[mr-raymond|Mr. Raymond]]'s household relocates after [[mr-coleman|Mr. Coleman]]'s financial ruin. In the symbolic geography of the novel, Hoxton represents the world of reduced circumstances — a place of [[darkness|obscurity]] where dignity can be preserved through honest work rather than social standing.

## Place in the Novel

Hoxton represents a descent in social station — from the Coleman estate with its lawn, shrubbery, and coach-house to a "small house" in an unfashionable district. After "ruin had befallen him" (012-090), [[mr-coleman|Mr. Coleman]] "had sold house, carriage, horses, furniture, and everything" (012-090–012-091) and moved with his family to Hoxton, "where he would be unknown" (012-092). Yet the move is not presented as pure tragedy. Being unknown in Hoxton offers a kind of liberation — he "could walk to his place of business in the City" (012-093), and MacDonald adds a note of hope: "he was not an old man, and hoped yet to retrieve his for[[music|tune]]s" (012-093–012-094). The household contracts — Mr. and [[mrs-coleman|Mrs. Coleman]], [[miss-coleman|Miss Coleman]], and [[mrs-crump|Mrs. Crump]] all live together in the small Hoxton house — but the essential relationships endure, and the family's moral character is tested rather than destroyed by the change.

[[diamonds-father|Diamond's father]] drives [[mr-evans|Mr. Evans]] to "[[mr-coleman|Mr. Coleman]]'s poor little house in Hoxton" in Chapter XXVI (026-109), confirming that the Coleman family remains there through the latter part of the novel. The adjective "poor little" captures the shift in circumstances while MacDonald's tone remains sympathetic rather than mocking — the house may be small and poor, but it is still a home where people care for one another.

## Contrast with the Coleman Estate

The move to Hoxton gains its narrative force from the contrast with the family's former life. The Coleman estate at the novel's opening — with its [[the-hay-loft|hay-loft]] where [[diamond|Diamond]] [[dream|sleep]]s, its lawn where he first meets [[north-wind|the North Wind]], and its coach-house where [[old-diamond|Old Diamond]] is stabled — represents a world of modest rural comfort. Hoxton, by contrast, is urban, anonymous, and defined by proximity to work rather than by land and leisure. MacDonald does not sentimentalize either setting: the estate was never a paradise (the family was always financially precarious), and Hoxton is never a hell (it is where the family rebuilds). The two places together trace the arc of the Coleman family's fall and partial recovery, a secondary plot that mirrors [[diamond|Diamond]]'s own journey through [[suffering|hardship]] toward an ultimate home.

[^1]: The historical Hoxton of MacDonald's time (late 19th century) was a working-class district known for its furniture trade and its proximity to the City of [[london|London]] — consistent with [[mr-raymond|Mr. Raymond]] being able to walk to his place of business. By the 1860s, when MacDonald was writing, Hoxton was undergoing the transition from a semi-rural suburb to a densely populated industrial district, making it an apt setting for a family in reduced but not desperate circumstances.
