## Overview

Sandwich is a real historical town on the Kent coast of England, and it appears in the novel as a destination where [[diamond|Diamond]] and his family briefly stay. [[north-wind|North Wind]] flies [[diamond|Diamond]] toward Sandwich in one of their dream-journeys, and the town serves as a point of connection between the novel's realistic geography and its visionary landscape.

## Location and Description

Sandwich is described as a formerly thriving seaport that has been abandoned by the sea: "Sandwich was a seaport no more; the sea" (009-054) having receded over centuries. This historical detail — the silting of Sandwich Haven, which did indeed end its life as a major medieval port — is one of the novel's few specific geographical references. The town sits inland now, "left high and dry" (009-054), a ghost of its maritime past.

The town's peculiar fate — abandoned by the sea, its harbor now pasture — resonates with the novel's themes of transformation and loss. Just as Sandwich lost its connection to the sea, [[diamond|Diamond]] will lose his connection to the earthly world; but in both cases, what appears to be loss is, in MacDonald's theology, transformation into something better.

## Appearance and Character

Sandwich enters the novel through [[north-wind|North Wind]]'s voice — "We should go to Sandwich" (009-040) — as a destination named but not yet seen. This deferred arrival gives Sandwich a quality of promise, a place held in the imagination before it is experienced. When MacDonald does describe it, he reaches not for sensory detail but for history: the town's character is defined by what it has lost. Sandwich "was a seaport no more" (009-054) — the past tense doing all the work, marking the town as a monument to mutability.

The Sandwich of [[diamond|Diamond]]'s waking life is a quieter place: a seaside town where the family takes modest holidays, where [[diamonds-mother|Diamond's mother]] "collects objects on the shore," and where the rhythm of tides replaces the clatter of cab-wheels. The family's recollection — "When we were down at Sandwich" (017-187) — has the warmth of shared memory, the town serving as a touchstone for the family's brief season of prosperity.

What makes Sandwich distinctive in the novel's geography is its **doubleness**. It is simultaneously a real town on the Kent coast (verifiable on any map) and a station on [[diamond|Diamond]]'s dream-itinerary. [[north-wind|North Wind]] can fly there in a sentence; [[diamond|Diamond]]'s family can travel there by ordinary conveyance. This doubleness is MacDonald's quiet argument that the numinous is not elsewhere — it is woven through the ordinary world, present even in a silted-up seaport on the English Channel.

## Events

1. **[[north-wind|North Wind]]'s plan** (Chapter IX): [[north-wind|North Wind]] announces they "should go to Sandwich" (009-040), establishing it as a waypoint in [[diamond|Diamond]]'s dream-journeys. The announcement is casual, almost offhand — as if Sandwich were just another stop on a familiar route.

2. **The historical meditation** (Chapter IX): MacDonald pauses to describe Sandwich's history — "Sandwich was a seaport no more; the sea" (009-054) — in a passage that reads like a miniature essay on mutability. The sea's slow retreat, measured in centuries, becomes a figure for all processes of loss and change.

3. **[[diamond|Diamond]]'s return** (Chapter XI): [[diamond|Diamond]] tells his mother he's been to Sandwich in his dream, but she dismisses it: "You couldn't be expecting me home from Sandwich yet!" (011-081). His insistence — "the time we were setting out for Sandwich" (011-093) — points to the [[truth|reality]] of his dream-experiences, which his mother can only interpret through the lens of ordinary geography.

4. **The family visit** (Chapters XIII, XV, XVII): [[diamond|Diamond]]'s family does visit Sandwich at some point, and the trip is referenced in later conversations: "When we were down at Sandwich" (017-187). The visit is associated with rest, [[the-sea|the sea-shore]], and [[diamonds-mother|Diamond's mother]]'s collection of treasures. These retrospective references give Sandwich a nostalgic [[light|glow]] in the family's collective memory.

## Symbolism and Significance

Sandwich's history as a "seaport no more" (009-054) makes it an emblem of **mutability** — the inevitable transformation of all earthly things. The sea's retreat is neither good nor bad; it simply is. MacDonald uses this historical fact to suggest that all apparent losses — even death — may, from a larger perspective, be neutral transitions.

The town's name — Sandwich — also carries a faint suggestion of the "sandwich" as something between two things, a middle state. [[diamond|Diamond]]'s journey to Sandwich is a middle passage — not yet the "country at the back of the north wind" but a step away from [[london|London]]. It is an intermediate destination, fitting for a child who is himself between worlds.
