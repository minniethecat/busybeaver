CHAPTER XXVII. THE CHILDREN'S HOSPITAL

[027-001]THE first day his father resumed his work, [[diamond|Diamond]] went with him as

[027-002]usual. In the afternoon, however, his father, having taken a fare to the

[027-003]neighbourhood, went home, and Diamond drove the cab the rest of the

[027-004]day. It was hard for old Diamond to do all the work, but they could

[027-005]not afford to have another horse. They contrived to save him as much as

[027-006]possible, and fed him well, and he did bravely.

[027-007]The next morning his father was so much stronger that Diamond thought he

[027-008]might go and ask [[mr-raymond|Mr. Raymond]] to take him to see [[nanny|Nanny]]. He found him at

[027-009]home. His servant had grown friendly by this time, and showed him in

[027-010]without any cross-questioning. Mr. Raymond received him with his usual

[027-011]kindness, consented at once, and walked with him to the Hospital, which

[027-012]was close at hand. It was a comfortable old-fashioned house, built in

[027-013]the reign of Queen Anne, and in her day, no doubt, inhabited by rich and

[027-014]fashionable people: now it was a home for poor sick children, who were

[027-015]carefully tended for love's sake. There are regions in [[london|London]] where a

[027-016]hospital in every other street might be full of such children, whose

[027-017]fathers and mothers are dead, or unable to take care of them.

[027-018]When Diamond followed Mr. Raymond into the room where those children who

[027-019]had got over the worst of their illness and were growing better lay, he

[027-020]saw a number of little iron bedsteads, with their heads to the walls,

[027-021]and in every one of them a child, whose face was a story in itself.

[027-022]In some, health had begun to appear in a tinge upon the cheeks, and a

[027-023]doubtful brightness in the eyes, just as out of the cold dreary winter

[027-024]the spring comes in blushing buds and bright crocuses. In others there

[027-025]were more of the signs of winter left. Their faces reminded you of

[027-026]snow and keen cutting winds, more than of sunshine and soft breezes

[027-027]and butterflies; but even in them the signs of suffering told that the

[027-028]suffering was less, and that if the spring-time had but arrived, it had

[027-029]yet arrived.

[027-030]Diamond looked all round, but could see no Nanny. He turned to Mr.

[027-031]Raymond with a question in his eyes.

[027-032]“Well?” said Mr. Raymond.

[027-033]“Nanny's not here,” said Diamond.

[027-034]“Oh, yes, she is.”

[027-035]“I don't see her.”

[027-036]“I do, though. There she is.”

[027-037]He pointed to a bed right in front of where Diamond was standing.

[027-038]“That's not Nanny,” he said.

[027-039]“It is Nanny. I have seen her many times since you have. Illness makes a

[027-040]great difference.”

[027-041]“Why, that girl must have been to the back of the north wind!” thought

[027-042]Diamond, but he said nothing, only stared; and as he stared, something

[027-043]of the old Nanny began to dawn through the face of the new Nanny. The

[027-044]old Nanny, though a good girl, and a friendly girl, had been rough,

[027-045]blunt in her speech, and dirty in her person. Her face would always

[027-046]have reminded one who had already been to the back of the north wind

[027-047]of something he had seen in the best of company, but it had been coarse

[027-048]notwithstanding, partly from the weather, partly from her living amongst

[027-049]low people, and partly from having to defend herself: now it was so

[027-050]sweet, and gentle, and refined, that she might have had a lady and

[027-051]gentleman for a father and mother. And Diamond could not help thinking

[027-052]of words which he had heard in [[the-cathedral|the church]] the day before: “Surely it is

[027-053]good to be afflicted;” or something like that. [[north-wind|North Wind]], somehow or

[027-054]other, must have had to do with her! She had grown from a rough girl

[027-055]into a gentle maiden.

[027-056]Mr. Raymond, however, was not surprised, for he was used to see

[027-057]such lovely changes--something like the change which passes upon the

[027-058]crawling, many-footed creature, when it turns sick and ill, and revives

[027-059]a butterfly, with two wings instead of many feet. Instead of her having

[027-060]to take care of herself, kind hands ministered to her, making her

[027-061]comfortable and sweet and clean, soothing her aching head, and giving

[027-062]her cooling drink when she was thirsty; and kind eyes, the [[stars]] of the

[027-063]kingdom of [[heaven]], had shone upon her; so that, what with the fire of

[027-064]the fever and the dew of tenderness, that which was coarse in her had

[027-065]melted away, and her whole face had grown so refined and sweet that

[027-066]Diamond did not know her. But as he gazed, the best of the old face, all

[027-067]the true and good part of it, that which was Nanny herself, dawned upon

[027-068]him, like the [[moon]] coming out of a cloud, until at length, instead of

[027-069]only believing Mr. Raymond that this was she, he saw for himself that it

[027-070]was Nanny indeed--very worn but grown beautiful.

[027-071]He went up to her. She smiled. He had heard her laugh, but had never

[027-072]seen her smile before.

[027-073]“Nanny, do you know me?” said Diamond.

[027-074]She only smiled again, as if the question was amusing.

[027-075]She was not likely to forget him; for although she did not yet know

[027-076]it was he who had got her there, she had dreamed of him often, and had

[027-077]talked much about him when delirious. Nor was it much wonder, for he was

[027-078]the only boy except Joe who had ever shown her kindness.

[027-079]Meantime Mr. Raymond was going from bed to bed, talking to the little

[027-080]people. Every one knew him, and every one was eager to have a look, and

[027-081]a smile, and a kind word from him.

[027-082]Diamond sat down on a stool at the head of Nanny's bed. She laid her

[027-083]hand in his. No one else of her old acquaintance had been near her.

[027-084]Suddenly a little voice called aloud--

[027-085]“Won't Mr. Raymond tell us a story?”

[027-086]“Oh, yes, please do! please do!” cried several little voices which also

[027-087]were stronger than the rest. For Mr. Raymond was in the habit of telling

[027-088]them a story when he went to see them, and they enjoyed it far more than

[027-089]the other nice things which the doctor permitted him to give them.

[027-090]“Very well,” said Mr. Raymond, “I will. What sort of a story shall it

[027-091]be?”

[027-092]“A true story,” said one little girl.

[027-093]“A fairy tale,” said a little boy.

[027-094]“Well,” said Mr. Raymond, “I suppose, as there is a difference, I may

[027-095]choose. I can't think of any true story just at this moment, so I will

[027-096]tell you a sort of a fairy one.”

[027-097]“Oh, jolly!” exclaimed the little boy who had called out for a fairy

[027-098]tale.

[027-099]“It came into my head this morning as I got out of bed,” continued Mr.

[027-100]Raymond; “and if it turns out pretty well, I will write it down, and get

[027-101]somebody to print it for me, and then you shall read it when you like.”

[027-102]“Then nobody ever heard it before?” asked one older child.

[027-103]“No, nobody.”

[027-104]“Oh!” exclaimed several, thinking it very grand to have the first

[027-105]telling; and I daresay there might be a peculiar freshness about it,

[027-106]because everything would be nearly as new to the story-teller himself as

[027-107]to the listeners.

[027-108]Some were only sitting up and some were lying down, so there could not

[027-109]be the same busy gathering, bustling, and shifting to and fro with which

[027-110]children generally prepare themselves to hear a story; but their faces,

[027-111]and the turning of their heads, and many feeble exclamations of expected

[027-112]pleasure, showed that all such preparations were making within them.

[027-113]Mr. Raymond stood in the middle of the room, that he might turn from

[027-114]side to side, and give each a share of seeing him. Diamond kept his

[027-115]place by Nanny's side, with her hand in his. I do not know how much of

[027-116]Mr. Raymond's story the smaller children understood; indeed, I don't

[027-117]quite know how much there was in it to be understood, for in such a

[027-118]story every one has just to take what he can get. But they all listened

[027-119]with apparent satisfaction, and certainly with great attention. Mr.

[027-120]Raymond wrote it down afterwards, and here it is--somewhat altered no

[027-121]doubt, for a good story-teller tries to make his stories better every

[027-122]time he tells them. I cannot myself help thinking that he was somewhat

[027-123]indebted for this one to the old story of The Sleeping Beauty.
