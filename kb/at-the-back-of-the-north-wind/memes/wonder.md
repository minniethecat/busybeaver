## Definition

In *At the Back of [[north-wind|the North Wind]]*, wonder is not merely a passing feeling of surprise but a fundamental orientation toward [[truth|reality]] that George MacDonald presents as native to childhood and essential to spiritual perception. Wonder, in the novel's terms, is the capacity to be astonished — by [[beauty]], by nature, by kindness — and to accept the marvellous without the adult compulsion to explain it away. [[diamond|Diamond]] embodies this capacity, and the narrative repeatedly suggests that wonder is the prerequisite for encountering the divine.

## In the Novel

Wonder enters the novel immediately and sustains it throughout. [[diamond|Diamond]]'s first encounters with [[north-wind|the North Wind]] are suffused with wonder — he does not ask whether she is real but whether he is [[dream|dreaming]] (002-099), a question that preserves the experience of marvel rather than seeking to debunk it.

The narrator's own voice participates in this ethos of wonder. In an early aside, the narrator observes that "the most wonderful thing in the world is" something beyond ordinary telling (002-071), framing the entire narrative within a worldview that takes wonder as the proper response to existence. This metafictional gesture aligns the reader with [[diamond|Diamond]]'s perspective: to read well is to wonder.

Throughout [[diamond|Diamond]]'s adventures, MacDonald presents wonder as the child's inheritance and the adult's loss. [[diamond|Diamond]]'s ability to see marvel in small things — a little window, a horse's coat — exemplifies what the novel values. A simple object is a "wonderful thing for [[beauty]]" to the boy (005-081), and the [[moon]] is perceived as "wonder-working" (007-203), actively transforming the world by its presence. The novel's world is one in which wonder is not a subjective projection but a recognition of the inherent marvel of creation.

The embedded narrative of "Little [[princess-daylight|Daylight]]" in Chapter XXVIII also participates in this theme, as the prince "wondered whether he could with any propriety" act as he wished (028-112) — wonder here bridges the gap between fairy-tale convention and moral deliberation.

The most concentrated expression of wonder appears in [[diamond|Diamond]]'s dream-vision of the star-gardeners. Workers "dig for [[stars]]" (025-148), and one explains that someone "goes round to all the [[stars]]" (025-219), with workers [[music|singing]] "Wash the [[stars]]' faces" (025-268). This vision transforms the cosmos from a mechanical system into a living garden — a vision possible only through the lens of wonder that [[diamond|Diamond]]'s childlike perception provides.

The novel's closing chapter returns to the theme: even the narrator admits that [[diamond|Diamond]]'s experience may be beyond the comprehension of rational adults. The [[north-wind|North Wind]] herself is described as a "wonderful being" (038-026), and whether she is a dream or a [[truth|reality]] is left open — the question matters less than the wonder she evokes.

## Key Passages

> "the most wonderful thing in the world is" (002-071)

The narrator's declaration that wonder is the appropriate lens through which to view the story — and the world.

> "wonderful thing for beauty, the boy thought, for a little window in the" (005-081)

[[diamond|Diamond]]'s capacity to find wonder in the ordinary — a small window becomes an occasion for aesthetic [[joy|delight]].

> "know that the wonder-working moon was behind, and he thought all the" (007-203)

The [[moon]] as an active, wonder-producing agent, not a passive astronomical body. The compound adjective "wonder-working" attributes agency to the natural world.

> "dig for stars" (025-148); "goes round to all the stars" (025-219); "Wash the stars' faces" (025-268)

The star-gardeners' [[music|song]]s, expressing the novel's vision of a cosmos alive with wonder and care.

> "wonderful being you call North Wind." (038-026)

The novel's final assessment of [[north-wind|the North Wind]] — wonder is the only adequate category for her.

## Wonder and Childhood

MacDonald's association of wonder with childhood is not sentimental but theological. In the novel's implicit argument, children perceive the world more truly than adults because they have not yet learned to filter experience through the categories of utility and probability. [[diamond|Diamond]]'s wonder is a form of epistemological clarity — he sees what is actually there: a [[moon]] that works wonders, a wind that is a person, a world that is alive. The adult characters who appear foolish in the novel (such as the sceptical cab-d[[the-river|river]]s or the pragmatic adults who dismiss [[diamond|Diamond]]'s stories) are those who have lost the capacity to wonder. MacDonald thus inverts the expected hierarchy: the child, not the adult, is the more reliable witness to [[truth|reality]].

## Related Characters

- **[[diamond]]**: The novel's primary wonderer, whose openness to the marvellous allows him to perceive [[north-wind|the North Wind]] when others cannot. His wonder is a spiritual faculty.
- **[[north-wind]]**: The primary object and agent of wonder in the novel — she is herself "wonderful" (038-026) and she facilitates [[diamond|Diamond]]'s encounters with the wondrous dimensions of existence.
