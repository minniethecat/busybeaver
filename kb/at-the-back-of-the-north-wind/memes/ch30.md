CHAPTER XXX. NANNY'S DREAM

[030-001]NANNY was not fit to be moved for some time yet, and [[diamond|Diamond]] went to see

[030-002]her as often as he could. But being more regularly engaged now, seeing

[030-003]he went out every day for a few hours with old Diamond, and had his baby

[030-004]to mind, and one of the horses to attend to, he could not go so often as

[030-005]he would have liked.

[030-006]One evening, as he sat by her bedside, she said to him:

[030-007]“I've had such a beautiful dream, Diamond! I should like to tell it

[030-008]you.”

[030-009]“Oh! do,” said Diamond; “I am so fond of [[dream|dreams]]!”

[030-010]“She must have been to the back of the north wind,” he said to himself.

[030-011]“It was a very foolish dream, you know. But somehow it was so pleasant!

[030-012]What a good thing it is that you believe the dream all the time you are

[030-013]in it!”

[030-014]My readers must not suppose that poor [[nanny|Nanny]] was able to say what she

[030-015]meant so well as I put it down here. She had never been to school, and

[030-016]had heard very little else than vulgar speech until she came to the

[030-017]hospital. But I have been to school, and although that could never make

[030-018]me able to dream so well as Nanny, it has made me able to tell her dream

[030-019]better than she could herself. And I am the more desirous of doing this

[030-020]for her that I have already done the best I could for Diamond's dream,

[030-021]and it would be a shame to give the boy all the advantage.

[030-022]“I will tell you all I know about it,” said Nanny. “The day before

[030-023]yesterday, a lady came to see us--a very beautiful lady, and very

[030-024]beautifully dressed. I heard the matron say to her that it was very

[030-025]kind of her to come in blue and gold; and she answered that she knew

[030-026]we didn't like dull colours. She had such a lovely shawl on, just like

[030-027]redness dipped in milk, and all worked over with flowers of the same

[030-028]colour. It didn't shine much, it was silk, but it kept in the shine.

[030-029]When she came to my bedside, she sat down, just where you are sitting,

[030-030]Diamond, and laid her hand on the counterpane. I was sitting up, with my

[030-031]table before me ready for my tea. Her hand looked so pretty in its blue

[030-032]glove, that I was tempted to stroke it. I thought she wouldn't be angry,

[030-033]for everybody that comes to the hospital is kind. It's only in the

[030-034]streets they ain't kind. But she drew her hand away, and I almost cried,

[030-035]for I thought I had been rude. Instead of that, however, it was only

[030-036]that she didn't like giving me her glove to stroke, for she drew it

[030-037]off, and then laid her hand where it was before. I wasn't sure, but I

[030-038]ventured to put out my ugly hand.”

[030-039]“Your hand ain't ugly, Nanny,” said Diamond; but Nanny went on--

[030-040]“And I stroked it again, and then she stroked mine,--think of that! And

[030-041]there was a ring on her finger, and I looked down to see what it was

[030-042]like. And she drew it off, and put it upon one of my fingers. It was a

[030-043]red stone, and she told me they called it a ruby.”
