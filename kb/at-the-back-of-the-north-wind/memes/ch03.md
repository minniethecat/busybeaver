CHAPTER III. OLD DIAMOND

[003-001]DIAMOND woke very early in the morning, and thought what a curious dream

[003-002]he had had. But the memory grew brighter and brighter in his head, until

[003-003]it did not look altogether like a dream, and he began to doubt whether

[003-004]he had not really been abroad in the wind last [[darkness|night]]. He came to the

[003-005]conclusion that, if he had really been brought home to his mother by

[003-006][[mrs-crump|Mrs. Crump]], she would say something to him about it, and that would

[003-007]settle the matter. Then he got up and dressed himself, but, finding that

[003-008]his father and mother were not yet stirring, he went down the ladder to

[003-009]the stable. There he found that even old [[diamond|Diamond]] was not awake yet, for

[003-010]he, as well as young Diamond, always got up the moment he woke, and

[003-011]now he was lying as flat as a horse could lie upon his nice trim bed of

[003-012]straw.

[003-013]“I'll give old Diamond a surprise,” thought the boy; and creeping up

[003-014]very softly, before the horse knew, he was astride of his back. Then

[003-015]it was young Diamond's turn to have more of a surprise than he had

[003-016]expected; for as with an earthquake, with a rumbling and a rocking

[003-017]hither and thither, a sprawling of legs and heaving as of many backs,

[003-018]young Diamond found himself hoisted up in the air, with both hands

[003-019]twisted in the horse's mane. The next instant old Diamond lashed out

[003-020]with both his hind legs, and giving one cry of [[fear|terror]] young Diamond

[003-021]found himself lying on his neck, with his arms as far round it as they

[003-022]would go. But then the horse stood as still as a stone, except that he

[003-023]lifted his head gently up to let the boy slip down to his back. For

[003-024]when he heard young Diamond's cry he knew that there was nothing to

[003-025]kick about; for young Diamond was a good boy, and old Diamond was a good

[003-026]horse, and the one was all right on the back of the other.

[003-027]As soon as Diamond had got himself comfortable on the saddle place, the

[003-028]horse began pulling at the hay, and the boy began thinking. He had never

[003-029]mounted Diamond himself before, and he had never got off him without

[003-030]being lifted down. So he sat, while the horse ate, wondering how he was

[003-031]to reach the ground.

[003-032]But while he meditated, his mother woke, and her first thought was to

[003-033]see her boy. She had visited him twice during the night, and found him

[003-034][[sleep|sleeping]] quietly. Now his bed was empty, and she was frightened.

[003-035]“Diamond! Diamond! Where are you, Diamond?” she called out.

[003-036]Diamond turned his head where he sat like a knight on his steed in

[003-037]enchanted stall, and cried aloud,--

[003-038]“Here, mother!”

[003-039]“Where, Diamond?” she returned.

[003-040]“Here, mother, on Diamond's back.”

[003-041]She came running to the ladder, and peeping down, saw him aloft on the

[003-042]great horse.

[003-043]“Come down, Diamond,” she said.

[003-044]“I can't,” answered Diamond.

[003-045]“How did you get up?” asked his mother.

[003-046]“Quite easily,” answered he; “but when I got up, Diamond would get up

[003-047]too, and so here I am.”

[003-048]His mother thought he had been walking in his [[dream|sleep]] again, and hurried

[003-049]down the ladder. She did not much like going up to the horse, for she

[003-050]had not been used to horses; but she would have gone into a lion's den,

[003-051]not to say a horse's stall, to help her boy. So she went and lifted him

[003-052]off Diamond's back, and felt braver all her life after. She carried him

[003-053]in her arms up to her room; but, afraid of frightening him at his own

[003-054]sleep-walking, as she supposed it, said nothing about last night. Before

[003-055]the next day was over, Diamond had almost concluded the whole adventure

[003-056]a dream.

[003-057]For a week his mother watched him very carefully--going into [[the-hay-loft|the loft]]

[003-058]several times a night--as often, in fact, as she woke. Every time she

[003-059]found him fast asleep.

[003-060]All that week it was hard weather. The grass showed white in the morning

[003-061]with the hoar-frost which clung like tiny comfits to every blade. And

[003-062]as Diamond's shoes were not good, and his mother had not quite saved

[003-063]up enough money to get him the new pair she so much wanted for him,

[003-064]she would not let him run out. He played all his games over and over

[003-065]indoors, especially that of driving two chairs harnessed to the baby's

[003-066]cradle; and if they did not go very fast, they went as fast as could be

[003-067]expected of the best chairs in the world, although one of them had only

[003-068]three legs, and the other only half a back.

[003-069]At length his mother brought home his new shoes, and no sooner did she

[003-070]find they fitted him than she told him he might run out in the yard and

[003-071]amuse himself for an hour.

[003-072]The sun was going down when he flew from the door like a bird from its

[003-073]cage. All the world was new to him. A great fire of sunset burned on the

[003-074]top of the gate that led from [[the-stable|the stables]] to the house; above the fire

[003-075]in the sky lay a large lake of green light, above that a golden cloud,

[003-076]and over that the blue of the wintry heavens. And Diamond thought that,

[003-077]next to his own home, he had never seen any place he would like so much

[003-078]to live in as that sky. For it is not fine things that make home a nice

[003-079]place, but your mother and your father.

[003-080]As he was looking at the lovely colours, the gates were thrown open,

[003-081]and there was old Diamond and his friend in the carriage, dancing with

[003-082]impatience to get at their stalls and their oats. And in they came.

[003-083]Diamond was not in the least afraid of his father driving over him, but,

[003-084]careful not to spoil the grand show he made with his fine horses and his

[003-085]multitudinous cape, with a red edge to every fold, he slipped out of the

[003-086]way and let him dash right on to the stables. To be quite safe he had

[003-087]to step into the recess of the door that led from the yard to the

[003-088]shrubbery.

[003-089]As he stood there he remembered how the wind had driven him to this same

[003-090]spot on the night of his dream. And once more he was almost sure that

[003-091]it was no dream. At all events, he would go in and see whether things

[003-092]looked at all now as they did then. He opened the door, and passed

[003-093]through the little belt of shrubbery. Not a flower was to be seen in the

[003-094]beds on the lawn. Even the brave old chrysanthemums and Christmas roses

[003-095]had passed away before the frost. What? Yes! There was one! He ran and

[003-096]knelt down to look at it.

[003-097]It was a primrose--a dwarfish thing, but perfect in shape--a

[003-098]baby-wonder. As he stooped his face to see it close, a little wind began

[003-099]to blow, and two or three long leaves that stood up behind the flower

[003-100]shook and waved and quivered, but the primrose lay still in the green

[003-101]hollow, looking up at the sky, and not seeming to know that the wind was

[003-102]blowing at all. It was just a one eye that the dull black wintry earth

[003-103]had opened to look at the sky with. All at once Diamond thought it was

[003-104]saying its prayers, and he ought not to be staring at it so. He ran to

[003-105]the stable to see his father make Diamond's bed. Then his father took

[003-106]him in his arms, carried him up the ladder, and set him down at the

[003-107]table where they were going to have their tea.

[003-108]“Miss is very poorly,” said [[diamonds-father|Diamond's father]]. “Mis'ess has been to

[003-109]the doctor with her to-day, and she looked very glum when she came out

[003-110]again. I was a-watching of them to see what doctor had said.”

[003-111]“And didn't Miss look glum too?” asked his mother.

[003-112]“Not half as glum as Mis'ess,” returned the coachman. “You see--”

[003-113]But he lowered his voice, and Diamond could not make out more than a

[003-114]word here and there. For Diamond's father was not only one of the finest

[003-115]of coachmen to look at, and one of the best of drivers, but one of

[003-116]the most discreet of servants as well. Therefore he did not talk about

[003-117]family affairs to any one but his wife, whom he had proved better than

[003-118]himself long ago, and was careful that even Diamond should hear nothing

[003-119]he could repeat again concerning master and his family.

[003-120]It was bed-time soon, and Diamond went to bed and fell fast asleep.

[003-121]He awoke all at once, in the dark.

[003-122]“Open the window, Diamond,” said a voice.

[003-123]Now [[diamonds-mother|Diamond's mother]] had once more pasted up [[north-wind|North Wind]]'s window.

[003-124]“Are you North Wind?” said Diamond: “I don't hear you blowing.”

[003-125]“No; but you hear me talking. Open the window, for I haven't overmuch

[003-126]time.”

[003-127]“Yes,” returned Diamond. “But, please, North Wind, where's the use? You

[003-128]left me all alone last time.”

[003-129]He had got up on his knees, and was busy with his nails once more at the

[003-130]paper over the hole in the wall. For now that North Wind spoke again,

[003-131]he remembered all that had taken place before as distinctly as if it had

[003-132]happened only last night.

[003-133]“Yes, but that was your fault,” returned North Wind. “I had work to do;

[003-134]and, besides, a gentleman should never keep a lady waiting.”

[003-135]“But I'm not a gentleman,” said Diamond, scratching away at the paper.

[003-136]“I hope you won't say so ten years after this.”

[003-137]“I'm going to be a coachman, and a coachman is not a gentleman,”

[003-138]persisted Diamond.

[003-139]“We call your father a gentleman in our house,” said North Wind.

[003-140]“He doesn't call himself one,” said Diamond.

[003-141]“That's of no consequence: every man ought to be a gentleman, and your

[003-142]father is one.”

[003-143]Diamond was so pleased to hear this that he scratched at the paper like

[003-144]ten mice, and getting hold of the edge of it, tore it off. The next

[003-145]instant a young girl glided across the bed, and stood upon the floor.

[003-146]“Oh dear!” said Diamond, quite dismayed; “I didn't know--who are you,

[003-147]please?”

[003-148]“I'm North Wind.”

[003-149]“Are you really?”

[003-150]“Yes. Make haste.”

[003-151]“But you're no bigger than me.”

[003-152]“Do you think I care about how big or how little I am? Didn't you see me

[003-153]this evening? I was less then.”

[003-154]“No. Where was you?”

[003-155]“Behind the leaves of the primrose. Didn't you see them blowing?”

[003-156]“Yes.”

[003-157]“Make haste, then, if you want to go with me.”

[003-158]“But you are not big enough to take care of me. I think you are only

[003-159]Miss North Wind.”

[003-160]“I am big enough to show you the way, anyhow. But if you won't come,

[003-161]why, you must stay.”

[003-162]“I must dress myself. I didn't mind with a grown lady, but I couldn't go

[003-163]with a little girl in my night-gown.”

[003-164]“Very well. I'm not in such a hurry as I was the other night. Dress

[003-165]as fast as you can, and I'll go and shake the primrose leaves till you

[003-166]come.”

[003-167]“Don't hurt it,” said Diamond.

[003-168]North Wind broke out in a little laugh like the breaking of silver

[003-169]bubbles, and was gone in a moment. Diamond saw--for it was a starlit

[003-170]night, and the mass of hay was at a low ebb now--the gleam of something

[003-171]vanishing down the stair, and, springing out of bed, dressed himself as

[003-172]fast as ever he could. Then he crept out into the yard, through the

[003-173]door in the wall, and away to the primrose. Behind it stood North

[003-174]Wind, leaning over it, and looking at the flower as if she had been its

[003-175]mother.

[003-176]“Come along,” she said, jumping up and holding out her hand.

[003-177]Diamond took her hand. It was cold, but so pleasant and full of life, it

[003-178]was better than warm. She led him across the garden. With one bound she

[003-179]was on the top of the wall. Diamond was left at the foot.

[003-180]“Stop, stop!” he cried. “Please, I can't jump like that.”

[003-181]“You don't try” said North Wind, who from the top looked down a foot

[003-182]taller than before.

[003-183]“Give me your hand again, and I will, try” said Diamond.

[003-184]She reached down, Diamond laid hold of her hand, gave a great spring,

[003-185]and stood beside her.

[003-186]“This is nice!” he said.

[003-187]Another bound, and they stood in the road by the [[the-river|river]]. It was full

[003-188]tide, and the [[stars]] were shining clear in its depths, for it lay still,

[003-189]waiting for the turn to run down again to the sea. They walked along its

[003-190]side. But they had not walked far before its surface was covered with

[003-191]ripples, and the stars had vanished from its bosom.

[003-192]And North Wind was now tall as a full-grown girl. Her hair was flying

[003-193]about her head, and the wind was blowing a breeze down the river. But

[003-194]she turned aside and went up a narrow lane, and as she went her hair

[003-195]fell down around her.

[003-196]“I have some rather disagreeable work to do to-night,” she said, “before

[003-197]I get out to sea, and I must set about it at once. The disagreeable work

[003-198]must be looked after first.”

[003-199]So saying, she laid hold of Diamond and began to run, gliding along

[003-200]faster and faster. Diamond kept up with her as well as he could. She

[003-201]made many turnings and windings, apparently because it was not quite

[003-202]easy to get him over walls and houses. Once they ran through a hall

[003-203]where they found back and front doors open. At the foot of the stair

[003-204]North Wind stood still, and Diamond, hearing a great growl, started in

[003-205]terror, and there, instead of North Wind, was a huge wolf by his side.

[003-206]He let go his hold in dismay, and the wolf bounded up the stair. The

[003-207]windows of the house rattled and shook as if guns were firing, and the

[003-208]sound of a great fall came from above. Diamond stood with white face

[003-209]staring up at the landing.

[003-210]“Surely,” he thought, “North Wind can't be eating one of the children!”

[003-211]Coming to himself all at once, he rushed after her with his little fist

[003-212]clenched. There were ladies in long trains going up and down the stairs,

[003-213]and gentlemen in white neckties attending on them, who stared at him,

[003-214]but none of them were of the people of the house, and they said nothing.

[003-215]Before he reached the head of the stair, however, North Wind met him,

[003-216]took him by the hand, and hurried down and out of the house.

[003-217]“I hope you haven't eaten a baby, North Wind!” said Diamond, very

[003-218]solemnly.

[003-219]North Wind laughed merrily, and went tripping on faster. Her grassy robe

[003-220]swept and swirled about her steps, and wherever it passed over withered

[003-221]leaves, they went fleeing and whirling in spirals, and running on their

[003-222]edges like wheels, all about her feet.

[003-223]“No,” she said at last, “I did not eat a baby. You would not have had

[003-224]to ask that foolish question if you had not let go your hold of me. You

[003-225]would have seen how I served a nurse that was calling a child bad names,

[003-226]and telling her she was wicked. She had been drinking. I saw an ugly gin

[003-227]bottle in a cupboard.”

[003-228]“And you frightened her?” said Diamond.

[003-229]“I believe so!” answered North Wind laughing merrily. “I flew at her

[003-230]throat, and she tumbled over on the floor with such a crash that they

[003-231]ran in. She'll be turned away to-morrow--and quite time, if they knew as

[003-232]much as I do.”

[003-233]“But didn't you frighten the little one?”

[003-234]“She never saw me. The woman would not have seen me either if she had

[003-235]not been wicked.”

[003-236]“Oh!” said Diamond, dubiously.

[003-237]“Why should you see things,” returned North Wind, “that you wouldn't

[003-238]understand or know what to do with? Good people see good things; bad

[003-239]people, bad things.”

[003-240]“Then are you a bad thing?”

[003-241]“No. For you see me, Diamond, dear,” said the girl, and she looked down

[003-242]at him, and Diamond saw the loving eyes of the great lady beaming from

[003-243]the depths of her falling hair.

[003-244]“I had to make myself look like a bad thing before she could see me. If

[003-245]I had put on any other shape than a wolf's she would not have seen me,

[003-246]for that is what is growing to be her own shape inside of her.”

[003-247]“I don't know what you mean,” said Diamond, “but I suppose it's all

[003-248]right.”

[003-249]They were now climbing the slope of a grassy ascent. It was Primrose

[003-250]Hill, in fact, although Diamond had never heard of it. The moment they

[003-251]reached the top, North Wind stood and turned her face towards [[london|London]] The

[003-252]stars were still shining clear and cold overhead. There was not a cloud

[003-253]to be seen. The air was sharp, but Diamond did not find it cold.

[003-254]“Now,” said the lady, “whatever you do, do not let my hand go. I might

[003-255]have lost you the last time, only I was not in a hurry then: now I am in

[003-256]a hurry.”

[003-257]Yet she stood still for a moment.
