CHAPTER XXIX. RUBY

[029-001]THE children were delighted with the story, and made many amusing

[029-002]remarks upon it. [[mr-raymond|Mr. Raymond]] promised to search his brain for another,

[029-003]and when he had found one to bring it to them. [[diamond|Diamond]] having taken

[029-004]leave of [[nanny|Nanny]], and promised to go and see her again soon, went away

[029-005]with him.

[029-006]Now Mr. Raymond had been turning over in his mind what he could do both

[029-007]for Diamond and for Nanny. He had therefore made some acquaintance with

[029-008][[diamonds-father|Diamond's father]], and had been greatly pleased with him. But he had come

[029-009]to the resolution, before he did anything so good as he would like to

[029-010]do for them, to put them all to a certain test. So as they walked away

[029-011]together, he began to talk with Diamond as follows:--

[029-012]“Nanny must leave the hospital soon, Diamond.”

[029-013]“I'm glad of that, sir.”

[029-014]“Why? Don't you think it's a nice place?”

[029-015]“Yes, very. But it's better to be well and doing something, you know,

[029-016]even if it's not quite so comfortable.”

[029-017]“But they can't keep Nanny so long as they would like. They can't keep

[029-018]her till she's quite strong. There are always so many sick children they

[029-019]want to take in and make better. And the question is, What will she do

[029-020]when they send her out again?”

[029-021]“That's just what I can't tell, though I've been thinking of it over and

[029-022]over, sir. Her crossing was taken long ago, and I couldn't bear to see

[029-023]Nanny fighting for it, especially with such a poor fellow as has taken

[029-024]it. He's quite lame, sir.”

[029-025]“She doesn't look much like fighting, now, does she, Diamond?”

[029-026]“No, sir. She looks too like an [[angel]]. Angels don't fight--do they,

[029-027]sir?”

[029-028]“Not to get things for themselves, at least,” said Mr. Raymond.

[029-029]“Besides,” added Diamond, “I don't quite see that she would have any

[029-030]better right to the crossing than the boy who has got it. Nobody gave it

[029-031]to her; she only took it. And now he has taken it.”

[029-032]“If she were to sweep a crossing--soon at least--after the illness she

[029-033]has had, she would be laid up again the very first wet day,” said Mr.

[029-034]Raymond.

[029-035]“And there's hardly any money to be got except on the wet days,”

[029-036]remarked Diamond reflectively. “Is there nothing else she could do,

[029-037]sir?”

[029-038]“Not without being taught, I'm afraid.”

[029-039]“Well, couldn't somebody teach her something?”

[029-040]“Couldn't you teach her, Diamond?”

[029-041]“I don't know anything myself, sir. I could teach her to dress the

[029-042]baby; but nobody would give her anything for doing things like that:

[029-043]they are so easy. There wouldn't be much good in teaching her to drive

[029-044]a cab, for where would she get the cab to drive? There ain't fathers and

[029-045]old Diamonds everywhere. At least poor Nanny can't find any of them, I

[029-046]doubt.”

[029-047]“Perhaps if she were taught to be nice and clean, and only speak gentle

[029-048]words.”

[029-049]“Mother could teach her that,” interrupted Diamond.

[029-050]“And to dress babies, and feed them, and take care of them,” Mr. Raymond

[029-051]proceeded, “she might get a place as a nurse somewhere, you know. People

[029-052]do give money for that.”

[029-053]“Then I'll ask mother,” said Diamond.

[029-054]“But you'll have to give her her food then; and your father, not being

[029-055]strong, has enough to do already without that.”

[029-056]“But here's me,” said Diamond: “I help him out with it. When he's tired

[029-057]of driving, up I get. It don't make any difference to old Diamond. I

[029-058]don't mean he likes me as well as my father--of course he can't, you

[029-059]know--nobody could; but he does his duty all the same. It's got to be

[029-060]done, you know, sir; and Diamond's a good horse--isn't he, sir?”

[029-061]“From your description I should say certainly; but I have not the

[029-062]pleasure of his acquaintance myself.”

[029-063]“Don't you think he will go to [[heaven]], sir?”

[029-064]“That I don't know anything about,” said Mr. Raymond. “I confess I

[029-065]should be glad to think so,” he added, smiling thoughtfully.

[029-066]“I'm sure he'll get to the back of the north wind, anyhow,” said Diamond

[029-067]to himself; but he had learned to be very careful of saying such things

[029-068]aloud.

[029-069]“Isn't it rather too much for him to go in the cab all day and every

[029-070]day?” resumed Mr. Raymond.

[029-071]“So father says, when he feels his ribs of a morning. But then he says

[029-072]the old horse do eat well, and the moment he's had his supper, down

[029-073]he goes, and never gets up till he's called; and, for the legs of him,

[029-074]father says that makes no end of a differ. Some horses, sir! they won't

[029-075]lie down all [[darkness|night]] long, but go to [[dream|sleep]] on their four pins, like a

[029-076]haystack, father says. I think it's very stupid of them, and so does old

[029-077]Diamond. But then I suppose they don't know better, and so they can't

[029-078]help it. We mustn't be too hard upon them, father says.”

[029-079]“Your father must be a good man, Diamond.” Diamond looked up in Mr.

[029-080]Raymond's face, wondering what he could mean.

[029-081]“I said your father must be a good man, Diamond.”

[029-082]“Of course,” said Diamond. “How could he drive a cab if he wasn't?”

[029-083]“There are some men who drive cabs who are not very good,” objected Mr.

[029-084]Raymond.

[029-085]Diamond remembered the drunken cabman, and saw that his friend was

[029-086]right.

[029-087]“Ah, but,” he returned, “he must be, you know, with such a horse as old

[029-088]Diamond.”

[029-089]“That does make a difference,” said Mr. Raymond. “But it is quite enough

[029-090]that he is a good man without our trying to account for it. Now, if you

[029-091]like, I will give you a proof that I think him a good man. I am going

[029-092]away on the Continent for a while--for three months, I believe--and I

[029-093]am going to let my house to a gentleman who does not want the use of

[029-094]my brougham. My horse is nearly as old, I fancy, as your Diamond, but

[029-095]I don't want to part with him, and I don't want him to be idle; for

[029-096]nobody, as you say, ought to be idle; but neither do I want him to be

[029-097]worked very hard. Now, it has come into my head that perhaps your father

[029-098]would take charge of him, and work him under certain conditions.”

[029-099]“My father will do what's right,” said Diamond. “I'm sure of that.”

[029-100]“Well, so I think. Will you ask him when he comes home to call and have

[029-101]a little chat with me--to-day, some time?”

[029-102]“He must have his dinner first,” said Diamond. “No, he's got his dinner

[029-103]with him to-day. It must be after he's had his tea.”

[029-104]“Of course, of course. Any time will do. I shall be at home all day.”

[029-105]“Very well, sir. I will tell him. You may be sure he will come. My

[029-106]father thinks you a very kind gentleman, and I know he is right, for I

[029-107]know your very own self, sir.”

[029-108]Mr. Raymond smiled, and as they had now reached his door, they parted,

[029-109]and Diamond went home. As soon as his father entered the house, Diamond

[029-110]gave him Mr. Raymond's message, and recounted the conversation that had

[029-111]preceded it. His father said little, but took thought-sauce to his bread

[029-112]and butter, and as soon as he had finished his meal, rose, saying:

[029-113]“I will go to your friend directly, Diamond. It would be a grand thing

[029-114]to get a little more money. We do want it.” Diamond accompanied his

[029-115]father to Mr. Raymond's door, and there left him.

[029-116]He was shown at once into Mr. Raymond's study, where he gazed with

[029-117]some wonder at the multitude of books on the walls, and thought what a

[029-118]learned man Mr. Raymond must be.

[029-119]Presently Mr. Raymond entered, and after saying much the same about

[029-120]his old horse, made the following distinct proposal--one not

[029-121]over-advantageous to Diamond's father, but for which he had

[029-122]reasons--namely, that Joseph should have the use of Mr. Raymond's horse

[029-123]while he was away, on condition that he never worked him more than six

[029-124]hours a day, and fed him well, and that, besides, he should take Nanny

[029-125]home as soon as she was able to leave the hospital, and provide for her

[029-126]as one of his own children, neither better nor worse--so long, that is,

[029-127]as he had the horse.

[029-128]Diamond's father could not help thinking it a pretty close bargain. He

[029-129]should have both the girl and the horse to feed, and only six hours'

[029-130]work out of the horse.

[029-131]“It will save your own horse,” said Mr. Raymond.

[029-132]“That is true,” answered Joseph; “but all I can get by my own horse is

[029-133]only enough to keep us, and if I save him and feed your horse and the

[029-134]girl--don't you see, sir?”

[029-135]“Well, you can go home and think about it, and let me know by the end of

[029-136]the week. I am in no hurry before then.”

[029-137]So Joseph went home and recounted the proposal to his wife, adding that

[029-138]he did not think there was much advantage to be got out of it.

[029-139]“Not much that way, husband,” said [[diamonds-mother|Diamond's mother]]; “but there would be

[029-140]an advantage, and what matter who gets it!”

[029-141]“I don't see it,” answered her husband. “Mr. Raymond is a gentleman of

[029-142]property, and I don't discover any much good in helping him to save a

[029-143]little more. He won't easily get one to make such a bargain, and I

[029-144]don't mean he shall get me. It would be a loss rather than a gain--I do

[029-145]think--at least if I took less work out of our own horse.”

[029-146]“One hour would make a difference to old Diamond. But that's not the

[029-147]main point. You must think what an advantage it would be to the poor

[029-148]girl that hasn't a home to go to!”

[029-149]“She is one of Diamond's friends,” thought his father.

[029-150]“I could be kind to her, you know,” the mother went on, “and teach her

[029-151]housework, and how to handle a baby; and, besides, she would help

[029-152]me, and I should be the stronger for it, and able to do an odd bit of

[029-153]charing now and then, when I got the chance.”

[029-154]“I won't hear of that,” said her husband. “Have the girl by all means.

[029-155]I'm ashamed I did not think of both sides of the thing at once. I wonder

[029-156]if the horse is a great eater. To be sure, if I gave Diamond two hours'

[029-157]additional rest, it would be all the better for the old bones of him,

[029-158]and there would be four hours extra out of the other horse. That would

[029-159]give Diamond something to do every day. He could drive old Diamond after

[029-160]dinner, and I could take the other horse out for six hours after tea,

[029-161]or in the morning, as I found best. It might pay for the keep of both of

[029-162]them,--that is, if I had good luck. I should like to oblige Mr. Raymond,

[029-163]though he be rather hard, for he has been very kind to our Diamond,

[029-164]wife. Hasn't he now?”

[029-165]“He has indeed, Joseph,” said his wife, and there the conversation

[029-166]ended.

[029-167]Diamond's father went the very next day to Mr. Raymond, and accepted his

[029-168]proposal; so that the week after having got another stall in the same

[029-169]stable, he had two horses instead of one. Oddly enough, the name of the

[029-170]new horse was Ruby, for he was a very red chestnut. Diamond's name came

[029-171]from a white lozenge on his forehead. Young Diamond said they were rich

[029-172]now, with such a big diamond and such a big ruby.
