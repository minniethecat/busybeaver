## Definition

In *At the Back of [[north-wind|the North Wind]]*, "home" is not merely a physical dwelling but a spiritual condition — a state of safety, love, and belonging that anchors identity and makes moral action possible. The novel's narrative arc is structured as a series of departures from and returns to home: [[diamond|Diamond]] leaves his hayloft bed to journey with [[north-wind|North Wind]] but always returns; the family loses their [[london|London]] home but gains a country cottage; and [[diamond|Diamond]]'s death is framed not as departure from home but as arrival at the ultimate home — the "country at the back of the north wind."

## In the Novel

Home appears in the novel in three distinct but related forms:

1. **The hayloft bed** (Chapters I–II): [[diamonds-father|Diamond's father]] builds him a bed in the hayloft of the stable — a home-within-a-home that is both shelter and threshold. It is from this bed that [[diamond|Diamond]] hears the wind in the walls (001-066), and through the hole in the wall that he first encounters [[north-wind|North Wind]]. The hayloft is the novel's primal scene of homely security and visionary opening.

2. **The lost [[london|London]] home** (Chapters XII–XV): When the family loses their livelihood, they lose their home. "[[diamonds-father|Diamond's father]] and mother were, notwithstanding, rather miserable" (015-024). The loss of home is the novel's image of fallenness — the condition of being exposed, unprotected, and adrift.

3. **The country cottage** (Chapters XXIX–XXXVII): [[mr-raymond|Mr. Raymond]] provides a new home in the country, restoring the family to security. [[diamonds-mother|Diamond's mother]] adapts: "[[diamonds-mother|Diamond's mother]] was very considerate" (031-003). But for [[diamond|Diamond]], the earthly home is always penultimate — his final words express a longing for the ultimate home: "I want to get into the country at my back" (036-223).

## Analysis

MacDonald's theology of home is Neoplatonic in structure: earthly homes are [[darkness|shadow]]s or types of the [[heaven]]ly home. The novel's emotional power derives from the fact that the earthly homes — the hayloft, the cottage — are genuinely good and beautiful; the spiritual home does not negate them but fulfills them.

In the Victorian context, the novel participates in the era's powerful cult of domesticity while also critiquing it. The home is sacred, but it is not ultimate. [[diamond|Diamond]]'s willingness to leave the cottage for the "country at the back of the north wind" is not a rejection of home but its transfiguration. MacDonald's universalism allows him to affirm both the earthly home and the [[heaven]]ly one without setting them in opposition.
