## Overview

Old Diamond is the cab-horse belonging to [[diamonds-father|Diamond's father]], and the namesake of the novel's young protagonist [[diamond|Diamond]]. A quiet, dependable horse who "did not go to [[dream|sleep]] standing, but lay down like a reasonable creature" (001-026), Old Diamond is both a working animal and a moral presence in the story — patient, long-suffering, and the object of [[diamond|young Diamond]]'s tender [[love|affection]]. His sale during the family's financial crisis and subsequent repurchase mark one of the novel's key narrative arcs of loss and restoration.

## Name and Identity

The horse's name creates a deliberate doubling with the boy protagonist. Young [[diamond|Diamond]] explains to [[north-wind|North Wind]]: "He is old [[diamond|Diamond]], and I am [[diamond|young Diamond]]; or, if you like it better, for you're very particular, Mr. [[north-wind|North Wind]], he's big [[diamond|Diamond]], and I'm [[diamond|little Diamond]]" (001-032). This doubling — big and little, old and young, horse and boy — runs throughout the novel, with the two [[diamond|Diamond]]s sharing not only a name but a mutual loyalty and a capacity for quiet endurance. The stablemen at [[the-mews|the mews]] [[love|affection]]ately refer to the pair as "the two [[diamond|Diamond]]s" (017-053).

## Character

Old Diamond is defined by his steadiness. He is "a quiet horse" (001-026) who, unlike the more spirited [[ruby|Ruby]], bears his work without complaint. When [[diamond|young Diamond]] surprises him by creeping onto his back, Old Diamond lashes out with his hind legs — but MacDonald is careful to note that "[[diamond|young Diamond]] was a good boy, and old [[diamond|Diamond]] was a good horse, and the one was all right on the back of the other" (003-025). The horse's essential [[kindness|goodness]] is never in doubt; his kick is one of startlement, not malice.

## Role in the Story

### The Coach-House Days

At the novel's opening, Old Diamond is stabled directly beneath the family's living quarters over the coach-house: "[[diamonds-father|Diamond's father]] put old [[diamond|Diamond]] in the stall under the bed" (001-026). This physical proximity — the horse [[sleep|sleeping]] beneath the boy, the boy feeling the horse's morning stirrings through the floorboards — establishes their intimate connection. Young [[diamond|Diamond]] wakes to "old [[diamond|Diamond]] coming awake down below in the stable" (008-055), the horse's movements a familiar rhythm of domestic life.

### Sale and Repurchase

During the family's deepest financial [[suffering|hardship]], Old Diamond is sold. When [[diamonds-father|Diamond's father]] later seeks to buy a horse for a new cab venture, the horse he is led to is "no other than his own old [[diamond|Diamond]], grown very thin and bony and long-legged" (014-065). The recognition is immediate and emotional: "It's my own old [[diamond|Diamond]]. I liked him far the best of the pair" (014-076). [[diamonds-father|Diamond's father]] buys him back along with a four-wheeled cab, and the family moves into rooms over a new stable — a restoration that parallels the novel's broader themes of providence and renewal.

### Reunion with Young Diamond

[[diamonds-father|Diamond's father]] keeps the horse's identity a secret to surprise his son. When [[diamond|young Diamond]] finally recognizes Old Diamond, his joy is overwhelming: "Ain't it jolly, father? Was there ever anybody so lucky as me? Dear old [[diamond|Diamond]]!" He hugs the horse and kisses "both his big hairy cheeks" (016-112–016-113), a scene of unembarrassed tenderness that exemplifies MacDonald's vision of the bond between children and animals.

### Hard Work and Decline

As the family's sole horse during their leanest period, Old Diamond bore the heaviest burden. "It was hard for old [[diamond|Diamond]] to do all the work, but they could not afford to have another horse. They contrived to save him as much as possible, and fed him well" (027-005). Despite these efforts, the toll was visible: by the novel's later chapters, "poor old [[diamond|Diamond]], who worked hardest of all, not even then — so that at the end of it he was as thin as a clothes-horse, while [[ruby|Ruby]] was as plump and sleek as a bishop" (031-004).

## The Silver Crest

Old Diamond's collar retains a silver crest from his former service with a wealthier master — likely [[mr-coleman|Mr. Coleman]]. [[diamonds-father|Diamond's father]] "thought nobody would notice that, and so let it remain for a memorial of the old times" (016-123). The crest is a small but telling detail: a vestige of gentility surviving into the family's reduced circumstances, and a quiet reminder that both horse and master have known better days.

## Young Diamond as Driver

One of the novel's most charming sequences shows [[diamond|young Diamond]] learning to drive Old Diamond. Despite being a small boy, he manages the large horse with remarkable ease — though as MacDonald wryly notes, "the fact that it was old [[diamond|Diamond]], and old [[diamond|Diamond]] on his way to his stable, may have had something to do with [[diamond|young Diamond]]'s success" (016-131). The horse's willing cooperation with the child d[[the-river|river]] underscores their mutual understanding.
