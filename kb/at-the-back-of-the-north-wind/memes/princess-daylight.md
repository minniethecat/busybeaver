## Overview

Princess Daylight is the central figure of an embedded fairy tale told by [[mr-raymond|Mr. Raymond]] to [[diamond|Diamond]] and the children. Cursed from infancy by a wicked fairy, she [[dream|sleep]]s through every hour of daylight and wakes only by [[light|moonlight]], her vitality ebbing and flowing with the [[moon]]'s phases. Her story — of a prince who braves enchantment to love and free her — functions as one of the novel's most sustained allegories of the relationship between light, [[darkness|dark]]ness, and love.

## Appearance and Character

Daylight's [[beauty]] is inseparable from the [[light|moonlight]] that sustains her. She has "the sunniest hair and the loveliest eyes of [[heaven]]ly blue, brilliant and profound as the sky of a June day" (030-148). But her appearance changes dramatically with the lunar cycle. Under the full [[moon]] she is "splendidly well" (030-115), a radiant princess; as the [[moon]] wanes, she withers into "an old woman exhausted with suffering" (030-159), her face "drawn and wrinkled" though her golden hair and blue eyes remain unchanged (030-162). This paradoxical constancy — the [[beauty]] that survives even the worst depletion — is the visual heart of MacDonald's allegory.

She is spirited and willful, frequently escaping her attendants to wander alone in the [[moon]]lit forest. She loves freedom and resents being watched. Her favourite haunt is a "great open glade, covered with the greenest and softest grass" (030-186), where she has a rustic house built.

## The Curse

Daylight was cursed in infancy: the swamp-fairy, a wicked enchantress, decreed that she would [[dream|sleep]] through the day and wake only at [[darkness|night]]. Her life became tethered to the [[moon]] — growing strong as it waxed, fading almost to death as it waned. Her parents, the king and queen, "got so used to the odd state of things that they had ceased to wonder at them" (030-138), organizing the entire court's life around their daughter's nocturnal existence.

The curse represents a spiritual condition — a soul that cannot bear the full light of day, that knows [[beauty]] and vitality only in the mediated, reflected light of the [[moon]]. The swamp-fairy's malice, like all wicked-fairy magic in the tale, proves "[[fear|dread]]fully stupid" (032-087), ultimately serving the good it tried to prevent.

## Role in the Story

A prince discovers Daylight [[sleep|sleeping]] in the forest and falls in love with her. He seeks the aid of a good fairy — an old woman who reveals herself as a fairy — and learns that the only way to break the curse is to kiss the princess during the daylight hours when she appears worst, proving that his love is not contingent on her [[beauty]].

The swamp-fairy tries repeatedly to thwart him, but each of her interventions, paradoxically, brings him closer to his goal. The prince perseveres, and at last kisses the withered princess. The enchantment breaks: Daylight awakens to see the sun for the first time, and the prince's love has restored her to her full [[light|radiance]] — now no longer bound to the [[moon]]'s phases.

## Themes and Symbolism

Daylight's curse is a parable of the human soul. Her name itself is ironic — she is "Daylight" who has never seen the day — until love makes the name true. The [[moon]]-governed existence allegorises a life lived by reflected light (law, nature, reason) rather than direct light (grace, love, divine presence). The prince's willingness to love her at her worst — "like an old woman exhausted with suffering" — is MacDonald's vision of the love that redeems: a love that sees the unchanged gold hair and blue eyes beneath the withered surface.

The embedded tale mirrors [[diamond|Diamond]]'s own relationship with [[north-wind|the North Wind]] — both narratives concern a being divided between two realms (day/[[darkness|night]], earth/sky), loved by one who trusts without fully understanding.

## Key Passages

> She had "the sunniest hair and the loveliest eyes of heavenly blue, brilliant and profound as the sky of a June day." (030-148)

> The more beautiful she was in the full moon, the more withered and worn did she become as the moon waned. (030-154)

> "I've seen a princess who never saw it," said the prince. (032-009)

This single line captures the story's central paradox — the princess named for daylight who has never seen the sun.
