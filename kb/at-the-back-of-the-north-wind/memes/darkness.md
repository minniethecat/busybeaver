## Definition

In *At the Back of [[north-wind|the North Wind]]*, darkness is not the enemy of light but its context and complement. George MacDonald resists the Manichaean dualism that would oppose light and darkness as good against evil, presenting darkness instead as a necessary condition of existence — the medium through which both mystery and revelation become possible. Darkness in the novel is protective, atmospheric, and epistemologically significant: it is the condition of not-knowing that makes knowing meaningful.

## In the Novel

### Darkness as the North Wind's Element

Darkness is introduced early and associated intimately with [[north-wind|the North Wind]]. Her "dark eyes looked a little angry" (001-206) when [[diamond|Diamond]] first sees her, and when her hair streams out, "the darkness in" it spreads around her face (001-209). The [[north-wind|North Wind]] is a creature of darkness — not malevolent but elemental, a force that operates in the spaces where ordinary vision fails. Her hair sweeps "yards deep of darkness like a great curtain from before" (007-055), a gesture that is both powerful and protective. The darkness she carries is not evil but mysterious, the visible sign of realities that exceed human comprehension.

### Darkness and the Journey

Many of [[diamond|Diamond]]'s most significant experiences occur in darkness. He awakes "all at once, in the dark" (003-121) to begin his first adventure with [[north-wind|the North Wind]]. After one journey, he wakes "in the middle of the night and the darkness" (005-260). The pattern is consistent: darkness is the threshold of adventure, the condition under which the supernatural becomes accessible. [[diamond|Diamond]]'s night flights with [[north-wind|the North Wind]] — through "a [[moon]]less night, and very dark, with glimpses of [[stars]]" (009-160) — are not stories of [[fear|terror]] but of wonder experienced in the absence of daylight's clarifying power.

The descent into the underwater realm of the iceberg (Chapter IX) occurs in darkness, and the vision [[diamond|Diamond]] receives there depends on the contrast between dark water and reflected light. The darkness of the deep is not a void but a medium — it carries within it the possibility of [[light|illumination]].

### Darkness as Metaphysical Boundary

The [[north-wind|North Wind]] herself acknowledges darkness as a limit to her own perception. She describes "a darkness" she "can't see through if I knock my eyes into it" (007-052) — even the most powerful being in the novel's cosmology encounters darkness as an absolute boundary. This admission is theologically significant: if even [[north-wind|the North Wind]] cannot see through certain darkness, then the ultimate [[truth|reality]] (God, in MacDonald's Christian framework) remains hidden from all created beings. Darkness thus becomes a figure for divine transcendence — not absence of light but light so complete that it appears as darkness to limited perception.

### Darkness and Comfort

Contrary to the conventions of much Victorian children's literature, darkness in the novel is often protective. When [[diamond|Diamond]] is in the hay-loft, the darkness of the stable is a comfort, a sanctuary. The novel's night scenes are atmospheric rather than threatening. [[diamond|Diamond]] "began to feel a kind of darkness beginning to spread over his" consciousness (015-025) — not a [[fear|terror]] but a transition, the approach of [[dream|sleep]] or another state of being. The darkness that falls upon him is the darkness before waking in another world.

### The Visual Motif

MacDonald is a visual writer, and darkness-as-imagery appears throughout the novel with a [[suffering|pain]]terly quality. The church is "dark. Only a little light came through the windows" (007-164). Things "were appearing to help him, growing out of the night and the darkness" (007-205). Darkness is generative — it produces forms, helpers, visions. It is the canvas upon which light [[suffering|pain]]ts, and without it, the [[suffering|pain]]ting would have no visible form.

## Key Passages

> "the darkness in" her hair (001-209)

The [[north-wind|North Wind]]'s first appearance — darkness as an attribute of the sublime, streaming from her being.

> "swept yards deep of darkness like a great curtain from before" (007-055)

The [[north-wind|North Wind]]'s gesture of revelation — darkness as something that can be lifted, but only by her.

> "a darkness, and I can't see through it if I knock my eyes into it" (007-052)

The [[north-wind|North Wind]]'s confession of limitation — darkness as the boundary even of her knowledge.

> "only a little light came through the windows" (007-164)

The church at night — darkness as the context that makes light precious and partial.

> "He awoke all at once, in the dark" (003-121)

The archetypal [[diamond|Diamond]] experience — darkness as the threshold of adventure.

## Darkness and MacDonald's Theology

MacDonald's treatment of darkness is consistent with his broader theology of the via negativa — the path of knowing God through what God is not. Darkness in the novel is not evil but unknowability, not threat but mystery. When [[diamond|Diamond]] rides through the dark with [[north-wind|the North Wind]], he is not endangered by darkness but sheltered by it; when he sees by partial light, the darkness that surrounds that light is the guarantee that there is always more to see. This positive valuation of darkness distinguishes MacDonald from the mainstream of Victorian moralising children's literature, in which darkness typically symbolised danger, sin, or ignorance.

## Related Characters

- **[[north-wind]]**: The novel's primary dark figure — her hair streams darkness, and she moves through the night as her natural element. Her own limitation before the ultimate darkness (007-052) marks her as a creature, not the Creator.
- **[[diamond]]**: The child who learns to trust darkness because it is the medium of [[north-wind|the North Wind]]'s appearances. His [[courage|fearlessness]] in the dark is a measure of his trust.
- **[[light]]**: Darkness's complement and partner — the two concepts are inseparable in the novel's symbolic economy.
