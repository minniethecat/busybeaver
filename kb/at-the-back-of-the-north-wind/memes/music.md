## Definition

In *At the Back of [[north-wind|the North Wind]]*, [[song|music]] is not simply entertainment or ornament but a mode of access to spiritual [[truth|reality]]. MacDonald presents song as the natural language of joy, the medium through which the deepest truths are communicated when ordinary speech fails. From [[diamond|Diamond]]'s impromptu nursery-rhyme improvisations to the cosmic "far-off song" that only [[north-wind|the North Wind]] can fully hear, [[song|music]] in the novel spans a continuum from the everyday to the transcendent. It is, in MacDonald's rendering, the sound that creation makes when it is being itself.

## In the Novel

### The Far-Off Song

The novel's most philosophically significant treatment of [[song|music]] occurs in Chapter VII, where [[north-wind|the North Wind]] explains to [[diamond|Diamond]] the existence of a song at the edge of audible [[truth|reality]]. She hears "the sound of a far-off song" (007-020) whose meaning she cannot fully articulate — she catches only "the odour of its [[song|music]]" (007-021), a synaesthetic description that suggests [[song|music]] operating at the limit of perception. The [[north-wind|North Wind]] insists that the song must exist, that the world would not cohere without it: "It must. It must. It wouldn't be the song" otherwise (007-035). This mysterious [[song|music]] functions as a metaphor for divine presence — real, sustaining, but only partially accessible to even the most sensitive listener.

The song is described in terms that merge sound and meaning: "the words and the [[song|music]] somehow appeared" as aspects of a single [[truth|reality]] (016-017). Ordinary people cannot hear it, and "if they did, it wouldn't do them any good" (007-027), [[north-wind|the North Wind]] remarks — suggesting that the capacity to hear such [[song|music]] is not a sensory fact but a spiritual disposition.

### Diamond's Songs

[[diamond|Diamond]] himself is a maker of songs. His [[song|music]]al expression ranges from nursery-rhyme fragments — "Hey diddle diddle" and variations on "Hey diddle! hey diddle! hey diddle diddle!" (025-016) — to original compositions. After his return from the country at the back of [[north-wind|the North Wind]], [[diamond|Diamond]] is "often heard singing" (010-082), and when asked what he sings, he answers: "One of the tunes the [[the-river|river]]" taught him (010-083). Later, he sings "oddest, loveliest little songs to the baby — of his own making" (016-025), songs that express a joy too immediate for formal composition. At his happiest, [[diamond|Diamond]] was "too happy either to make a song of his own or to sing sense" (024-215) — [[song|music]] here becomes pure affect, beyond the requirements of verbal meaning.

### The River's Music

An important passage in Chapter X describes [[diamond|Diamond]] insisting that the [[the-river|river]] "did sing tunes in people's ears" (010-080). The [[the-river|river]]'s sound is "sounding a bass to their song" (010-034) — the natural world participates in the novel's [[song|music]]al cosmology. [[diamond|Diamond]]'s perception of the [[the-river|river]]'s [[song|music]] is continuous with his perception of the far-off song: both require a mode of attention that the novel associates with childhood openness.

### Nanny's Songs and the Moonlight

In the "Little [[princess-daylight|Daylight]]" embedded narrative (Chapter XXVIII), [[song|music]] is central to the fairy-tale logic. [[princess-daylight|Princess Daylight]], restored by the prince, "began singing like a [[darkness|night]]ingale, and dancing to her own [[song|music]]" (028-257), her song and dance expressing a vitality that her curse had suppressed. Her laughter is "[[song|music]]al as a brook" (028-266), and she sings to the full [[moon]] "as she would draw her down from" the sky (028-279). Music in this tale is associated with liberation, with the body's recovery of its natural expressiveness after enchantment.

## Key Passages

> "the sound of a far-off song" (007-020); "the odour of its music" (007-021)

The [[north-wind|North Wind]]'s description of the transcendent [[song|music]] at the edge of perception — the novel's primary metaphor for divine presence communicated through sound.

> "It must. It must. It wouldn't be the song" (007-035)

The [[north-wind|North Wind]]'s insistence on the necessity of the song — the world depends upon [[song|music]] that most people cannot hear.

> "a song in which the words and the music somehow appeared" (016-017)

The synaesthetic unity of language and sound in the transcendent song, where meaning and [[beauty]] are one.

> "too happy either to make a song of his own or to sing sense" (024-215)

[[diamond|Diamond]] at the limit of expression — joy so complete it overflows the structures of language but still finds voice in song.

> "One of the tunes the river" taught him (010-083)

The natural world as [[song|music]] teacher — [[diamond|Diamond]] learns songs from a [[the-river|river]], collapsing the distinction between nature and art.

> "singing like a nightingale, and dancing to her own music" (028-257)

[[princess-daylight|Princess Daylight]]'s liberation expressed through spontaneous song and dance — [[song|music]] as the body's recovery of freedom.

## Music as Spiritual Language

MacDonald's use of [[song|music]] in the novel reflects a theological conviction that sound is a more direct carrier of spiritual meaning than discursive language. Words can argue, deceive, and obscure; [[song|music]] can only be itself. When [[north-wind|the North Wind]] speaks of the far-off song, she uses paradox and indirection precisely because the song's [[truth|reality]] exceeds propositional description. [[diamond|Diamond]]'s own songs — fragmentary, improvised, sometimes nonsensical — are truer expressions of his experience than any prose summary could be. The novel thus proposes [[song|music]] as an epistemology: a way of knowing that bypasses the intellect and speaks directly to what MacDonald would call the heart.

## Related Characters

- **[[diamond]]**: The novel's composer-protagonist, whose songs range from nursery rhymes to original compositions. His [[song|music]] is the spontaneous overflow of joy and love.
- **[[north-wind]]**: The only character who can hear the far-off song — or at least its "odour" — making her the novel's [[song|music]]al mystic and intermediary between the audible and inaudible realms.
- **[[princess-daylight|Daylight]]**: The fairy-tale princess whose liberation is marked by singing "like a [[darkness|night]]ingale" and dancing to "her own [[song|music]]" (028-257).
- **[[nanny]]**: [[diamond|Diamond]]'s friend and audience, whose own capacity for joy is awakened in part through the [[song|music]] [[diamond|Diamond]] brings into her life.
