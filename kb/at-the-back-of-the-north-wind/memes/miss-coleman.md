## Overview

Miss Coleman is a gentlewoman of reduced circumstances who employs [[diamonds-father|Diamond's father]] as her coachman. She is one of the novel's most fully drawn adult characters — kind, generous, but physically frail and burdened by a tragic romantic past. Her relationship with [[diamond|Diamond]] and his family, and her eventual reunion with her lost lover [[mr-evans|Mr. Evans]], make her one of MacDonald's most sympathetic creations.

## Appearance and Character

Miss Coleman is introduced as a woman of refinement living in modest circumstances: "the fire in Miss Coleman's [room]" (002-149) suggests warmth and domestic comfort despite her reduced means. She is characterized by spontaneous generosity — when [[diamond|Diamond]]'s family faces [[suffering|hardship]], "she was so pleased that she…" (002-158) gave them assistance without being asked.

MacDonald portrays her as physically delicate: "Miss Coleman was a little…" (005-068) — her health is fragile, and earlier chapters note she "was in a very poor state of health" (012-029). This physical vulnerability makes her kindness more remarkable: she gives from her own scarcity.

## Role in the Story

1. **Early benefactor**: Miss Coleman is present from the early chapters as [[diamonds-father|Diamond's father]]'s employer. She shows immediate kindness to [[diamond|Diamond]] and his family, giving him a picture-book (011-135) and other gifts.

2. **The lost lover**: A significant subplot involves Miss Coleman's romantic history. She was to marry a gentleman named [[mr-evans|Mr. Evans]], but he "had not behaved very well to Miss Coleman" (026-068). He went to sea, and she believed him dead (026-141). [[diamond|Diamond]], through his dream-knowledge, becomes instrumental in reuniting them.

3. **Reunion with [[mr-evans|Mr. Evans]]**: In one of the novel's most emotionally charged scenes, [[mr-evans|Mr. Evans]] returns and seeks Miss Coleman's forgiveness. [[diamond|Diamond]] mediates: "Tell Miss Coleman. It's Miss Coleman he wants to see" (026-133). The reunion is tender: "to his [[joy|delight]] Miss Coleman put her arms round him" (026-150).

4. **Continued presence**: Throughout the novel, Miss Coleman remains a stable, benevolent adult presence in [[diamond|Diamond]]'s world, providing material comfort and moral support.

## Themes and Symbolism

Miss Coleman represents **gentle, unostentatious [[kindness|charity]]**. Unlike the dramatic supernatural interventions of [[north-wind|North Wind]], Miss Coleman's [[kindness|goodness]] operates within the ordinary world — a warm fire, a picture-book, a kind word. Her physical frailty underscores the Christian paradox MacDonald explored throughout his work: that strength is made perfect in weakness.

Her reunion with [[mr-evans|Mr. Evans]] parallels the novel's broader theme of **redemption and restoration**. Just as [[diamond|Diamond]] is restored to [[back-of-the-north-wind|the country at the back of the north wind]] after death, Miss Coleman's lost love is restored to her in life. Both stories affirm MacDonald's belief in a benevolent universe where love ultimately triumphs.
