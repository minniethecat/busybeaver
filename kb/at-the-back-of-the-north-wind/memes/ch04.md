CHAPTER IV. NORTH WIND

[004-001]AND as she stood looking towards [[london|London]], [[diamond|Diamond]] saw that she was

[004-002]trembling.

[004-003]“Are you cold, [[north-wind|North Wind]]?” he asked.

[004-004]“No, Diamond,” she answered, looking down upon him with a smile; “I am

[004-005]only getting ready to sweep one of my rooms. Those careless, greedy,

[004-006]untidy children make it in such a mess.”

[004-007]As she spoke he could have told by her voice, if he had not seen with

[004-008]his eyes, that she was growing larger and larger. Her head went up and

[004-009]up towards the [[stars]]; and as she grew, still trembling through all her

[004-010]body, her hair also grew--longer and longer, and lifted itself from her

[004-011]head, and went out in black waves. The next moment, however, it fell

[004-012]back around her, and she grew less and less till she was only a tall

[004-013]woman. Then she put her hands behind her head, and gathered some of her

[004-014]hair, and began weaving and knotting it together. When she had done, she

[004-015]bent down her beautiful face close to his, and said--

[004-016]“Diamond, I am afraid you would not keep hold of me, and if I were to

[004-017]drop you, I don't know what might happen; so I have been making a place

[004-018]for you in my hair. Come.”

[004-019]Diamond held out his arms, for with that grand face looking at him,

[004-020]he believed like a baby. She took him in her hands, threw him over her

[004-021]shoulder, and said, “Get in, Diamond.”

[004-022]And Diamond parted her hair with his hands, crept between, and feeling

[004-023]about soon found the woven nest. It was just like a pocket, or like

[004-024]the shawl in which gipsy women carry their children. North Wind put her

[004-025]hands to her back, felt all about the nest, and finding it safe, said--

[004-026]“Are you comfortable, Diamond?”

[004-027]“Yes, indeed,” answered Diamond.

[004-028]The next moment he was rising in the air. North Wind grew towering up to

[004-029]the place of the clouds. Her hair went streaming out from her, till it

[004-030]spread like a mist over the stars. She flung herself abroad in space.

[004-031]Diamond held on by two of the twisted ropes which, parted and

[004-032]interwoven, formed his shelter, for he could not help being a little

[004-033]afraid. As soon as he had come to himself, he peeped through the woven

[004-034]meshes, for he did not dare to look over the top of the nest. The earth

[004-035]was rushing past like a [[the-river|river]] or a sea below him. Trees and water and

[004-036]green grass hurried away beneath. A great roar of wild animals rose

[004-037]as they rushed over the Zoological Gardens, mixed with a chattering of

[004-038]monkeys and a screaming of birds; but it died away in a moment behind

[004-039]them. And now there was nothing but the roofs of houses, sweeping along

[004-040]like a great torrent of stones and rocks. Chimney-pots fell, and tiles

[004-041]flew from the roofs; but it looked to him as if they were left behind

[004-042]by the roofs and the chimneys as they scudded away. There was a great

[004-043]roaring, for the wind was dashing against London like a sea; but at

[004-044]North Wind's back Diamond, of course, felt nothing of it all. He was in

[004-045]a perfect calm. He could hear the sound of it, that was all.

[004-046]By and by he raised himself and looked over the edge of his nest. There

[004-047]were the houses rushing up and shooting away below him, like a fierce

[004-048]torrent of rocks instead of water. Then he looked up to the sky, but

[004-049]could see no stars; they were hidden by the blinding masses of the

[004-050]lady's hair which swept between. He began to wonder whether she would

[004-051]hear him if he spoke. He would try.

[004-052]“Please, North Wind,” he said, “what is that noise?”

[004-053]From high over his head came the voice of North Wind, answering him,

[004-054]gently--

[004-055]“The noise of my besom. I am the old woman that sweeps the cobwebs from

[004-056]the sky; only I'm busy with the floor now.”

[004-057]“What makes the houses look as if they were running away?”

[004-058]“I am sweeping so fast over them.”

[004-059]“But, please, North Wind, I knew London was very big, but I didn't know

[004-060]it was so big as this. It seems as if we should never get away from it.”

[004-061]“We are going round and round, else we should have left it long ago.”

[004-062]“Is this the way you sweep, North Wind?”

[004-063]“Yes; I go round and round with my great besom.”

[004-064]“Please, would you mind going a little slower, for I want to see the

[004-065]streets?”

[004-066]“You won't see much now.”

[004-067]“Why?”

[004-068]“Because I have nearly swept all the people home.”

[004-069]“Oh! I forgot,” said Diamond, and was quiet after that, for he did not

[004-070]want to be troublesome.

[004-071]But she dropped a little towards the roofs of the houses, and Diamond

[004-072]could see down into the streets. There were very few people about,

[004-073]though. The lamps flickered and flared again, but nobody seemed to want

[004-074]them.

[004-075]Suddenly Diamond espied a little girl coming along a street. She was

[004-076]dreadfully blown by the wind, and a broom she was trailing behind her

[004-077]was very troublesome. It seemed as if the wind had a spite at her--it

[004-078]kept worrying her like a wild beast, and tearing at her rags. She was so

[004-079]lonely there!

[004-080]“Oh! please, North Wind,” he cried, “won't you help that little girl?”

[004-081]“No, Diamond; I mustn't leave my work.”

[004-082]“But why shouldn't you be kind to her?”

[004-083]“I am kind to her. I am sweeping the wicked smells away.”

[004-084]“But you're kinder to me, dear North Wind. Why shouldn't you be as kind

[004-085]to her as you are to me?”

[004-086]“There are reasons, Diamond. Everybody can't be done to all the same.

[004-087]Everybody is not ready for the same thing.”

[004-088]“But I don't see why I should be kinder used than she.”

[004-089]“Do you think nothing's to be done but what you can see, Diamond, you

[004-090]silly! It's all right. Of course you can help her if you like. You've

[004-091]got nothing particular to do at this moment; I have.”

[004-092]“Oh! do let me help her, then. But you won't be able to wait, perhaps?”

[004-093]“No, I can't wait; you must do it yourself. And, mind, the wind will get

[004-094]a hold of you, too.”

[004-095]“Don't you want me to help her, North Wind?”

[004-096]“Not without having some idea what will happen. If you break down and

[004-097]cry, that won't be much of a help to her, and it will make a goose of

[004-098]little Diamond.”

[004-099]“I want to go,” said Diamond. “Only there's just one thing--how am I to

[004-100]get home?”

[004-101]“If you're anxious about that, perhaps you had better go with me. I am

[004-102]bound to take you home again, if you do.”

[004-103]“There!” cried Diamond, who was still looking after the little girl.

[004-104]“I'm sure the wind will blow her over, and perhaps kill her. Do let me

[004-105]go.”

[004-106]They had been sweeping more slowly along the line of the street. There

[004-107]was a lull in the roaring.

[004-108]“Well, though I cannot promise to take you home,” said North Wind, as

[004-109]she sank nearer and nearer to the tops of the houses, “I can promise

[004-110]you it will be all right in the end. You will get home somehow. Have you

[004-111]made up your mind what to do?”

[004-112]“Yes; to help the little girl,” said Diamond firmly.

[004-113]The same moment North Wind dropt into the street and stood, only a tall

[004-114]lady, but with her hair flying up over the housetops. She put her hands

[004-115]to her back, took Diamond, and set him down in the street. The same

[004-116]moment he was caught in the fierce coils of the blast, and all but blown

[004-117]away. North Wind stepped back a step, and at once towered in stature to

[004-118]the height of the houses. A chimney-pot clashed at Diamond's feet. He

[004-119]turned in [[fear|terror]], but it was to look for the little girl, and when he

[004-120]turned again the lady had vanished, and the wind was roaring along the

[004-121]street as if it had been the bed of an invisible torrent. The little

[004-122]girl was scudding before the blast, her hair flying too, and behind her

[004-123]she dragged her broom. Her little legs were going as fast as ever they

[004-124]could to keep her from falling. Diamond crept into the shelter of a

[004-125]doorway, thinking to stop her; but she passed him like a bird, crying

[004-126]gently and pitifully.

[004-127]“Stop! stop! little girl,” shouted Diamond, starting in pursuit.

[004-128]“I can't,” wailed the girl, “the wind won't leave go of me.”

[004-129]Diamond could run faster than she, and he had no broom. In a few moments

[004-130]he had caught her by the frock, but it tore in his hand, and away went

[004-131]the little girl. So he had to run again, and this time he ran so fast

[004-132]that he got before her, and turning round caught her in his arms, when

[004-133]down they went both together, which made the little girl laugh in the

[004-134]midst of her crying.

[004-135]“Where are you going?” asked Diamond, rubbing the elbow that had stuck

[004-136]farthest out. The arm it belonged to was twined round a lamp-post as he

[004-137]stood between the little girl and the wind.

[004-138]“[[home|Home]],” she said, gasping for breath.

[004-139]“Then I will go with you,” said Diamond.

[004-140]And then they were silent for a while, for the wind blew worse than

[004-141]ever, and they had both to hold on to the lamp-post.

[004-142]“Where is your crossing?” asked the girl at length.

[004-143]“I don't sweep,” answered Diamond.

[004-144]“What do you do, then?” asked she. “You ain't big enough for most

[004-145]things.”

[004-146]“I don't know what I do do,” answered he, feeling rather ashamed.

[004-147]“Nothing, I suppose. My father's [[mr-coleman|Mr. Coleman]]'s coachman.”

[004-148]“Have you a father?” she said, staring at him as if a boy with a father

[004-149]was a natural curiosity.

[004-150]“Yes. Haven't you?” returned Diamond.

[004-151]“No; nor mother neither. [[sal|Old Sal]]'s all I've got.” And she began to cry

[004-152]again.

[004-153]“I wouldn't go to her if she wasn't good to me,” said Diamond.

[004-154]“But you must go somewheres.”

[004-155]“Move on,” said the voice of a policeman behind them.

[004-156]“I told you so,” said the girl. “You must go somewheres. They're always

[004-157]at it.”

[004-158]“But old Sal doesn't beat you, does she?”

[004-159]“I wish she would.”

[004-160]“What do you mean?” asked Diamond, quite bewildered.

[004-161]“She would if she was my mother. But she wouldn't lie abed a-cuddlin' of

[004-162]her ugly old bones, and laugh to hear me crying at the door.”

[004-163]“You don't mean she won't let you in to-[[darkness|night]]?”

[004-164]“It'll be a good chance if she does.”

[004-165]“Why are you out so late, then?” asked Diamond.

[004-166]“My crossing's a long way off at the West End, and I had been indulgin'

[004-167]in door-steps and mewses.”

[004-168]“We'd better have a try anyhow,” said Diamond. “Come along.”

[004-169]As he spoke Diamond thought he caught a glimpse of North Wind turning a

[004-170]corner in front of them; and when they turned the corner too, they found

[004-171]it quiet there, but he saw nothing of the lady.

[004-172]“Now you lead me,” he said, taking her hand, “and I'll take care of

[004-173]you.”

[004-174]The girl withdrew her hand, but only to dry her eyes with her frock, for

[004-175]the other had enough to do with her broom. She put it in his again, and

[004-176]led him, turning after turning, until they stopped at a cellar-door in a

[004-177]very dirty lane. There she knocked.

[004-178]“I shouldn't like to live here,” said Diamond.

[004-179]“Oh, yes, you would, if you had nowhere else to go to,” answered the

[004-180]girl. “I only wish we may get in.”

[004-181]“I don't want to go in,” said Diamond.

[004-182]“Where do you mean to go, then?”

[004-183]“Home to my home.”

[004-184]“Where's that?”

[004-185]“I don't exactly know.”

[004-186]“Then you're worse off than I am.”

[004-187]“Oh no, for North Wind--” began Diamond, and stopped, he hardly knew

[004-188]why.

[004-189]“What?” said the girl, as she held her ear to the door listening.

[004-190]But Diamond did not reply. Neither did old Sal.

[004-191]“I told you so,” said the girl. “She is wide awake hearkening. But we

[004-192]don't get in.”

[004-193]“What will you do, then?” asked Diamond.

[004-194]“Move on,” she answered.

[004-195]“Where?”

[004-196]“Oh, anywheres. Bless you, I'm used to it.”

[004-197]“Hadn't you better come home with me, then?”

[004-198]“That's a good joke, when you don't know where it is. Come on.”

[004-199]“But where?”

[004-200]“Oh, nowheres in particular. Come on.”

[004-201]Diamond obeyed. The wind had now fallen considerably. They wandered on

[004-202]and on, turning in this direction and that, without any reason for one

[004-203]way more than another, until they had got out of the thick of the houses

[004-204]into a waste kind of place. By this time they were both very tired.

[004-205]Diamond felt a good deal inclined to cry, and thought he had been very

[004-206]silly to get down from the back of North Wind; not that he would have

[004-207]minded it if he had done the girl any good; but he thought he had been

[004-208]of no use to her. He was mistaken there, for she was far happier for

[004-209]having Diamond with her than if she had been wandering about alone. She

[004-210]did not seem so tired as he was.

[004-211]“Do let us rest a bit,” said Diamond.

[004-212]“Let's see,” she answered. “There's something like a railway there.

[004-213]Perhaps there's an open arch.”

[004-214]They went towards it and found one, and, better still, there was an

[004-215]empty barrel lying under the arch.

[004-216]“Hallo! here we are!” said the girl. “A barrel's the jolliest bed

[004-217]going--on the tramp, I mean. We'll have forty winks, and then go on

[004-218]again.”

[004-219]She crept in, and Diamond crept in beside her. They put their arms round

[004-220]each other, and when he began to grow warm, Diamond's courage began to

[004-221]come back.

[004-222]“This is jolly!” he said. “I'm so glad!”

[004-223]“I don't think so much of it,” said the girl. “I'm used to it, I

[004-224]suppose. But I can't think how a kid like you comes to be out all alone

[004-225]this time o' night.”

[004-226]She called him a kid, but she was not really a month older than he was;

[004-227]only she had had to work for her bread, and that so soon makes people

[004-228]older.

[004-229]“But I shouldn't have been out so late if I hadn't got down to help

[004-230]you,” said Diamond. “North Wind is gone home long ago.”

[004-231]“I think you must ha' got out o' one o' them Hidget Asylms,” said the

[004-232]girl. “You said something about the north wind afore that I couldn't get

[004-233]the rights of.”

[004-234]So now, for the sake of his character, Diamond had to tell her the whole

[004-235]story.

[004-236]She did not believe a word of it. She said he wasn't such a flat as to

[004-237]believe all that bosh. But as she spoke there came a great blast of wind

[004-238]through the arch, and set the barrel rolling. So they made haste to get

[004-239]out of it, for they had no notion of being rolled over and over as if

[004-240]they had been packed tight and wouldn't hurt, like a barrel of herrings.

[004-241]“I thought we should have had a [[dream|sleep]],” said Diamond; “but I can't say

[004-242]I'm very sleepy after all. Come, let's go on again.”

[004-243]They wandered on and on, sometimes sitting on a door-step, but always

[004-244]turning into lanes or fields when they had a chance.

[004-245]They found themselves at last on a rising ground that sloped rather

[004-246]steeply on the other side. It was a waste kind of spot below, bounded by

[004-247]an irregular wall, with a few doors in it. Outside lay broken things in

[004-248]general, from garden rollers to flower-pots and wine-bottles. But the

[004-249]moment they reached the brow of the rising ground, a gust of wind

[004-250]seized them and blew them down hill as fast as they could run. Nor could

[004-251]Diamond stop before he went bang against one of the doors in the wall.

[004-252]To his dismay it burst open. When they came to themselves they peeped

[004-253]in. It was the back door of a garden.

[004-254]“Ah, ah!” cried Diamond, after staring for a few moments, “I thought

[004-255]so! North Wind takes nobody in! Here I am in master's garden! I tell you

[004-256]what, little girl, you just bore a hole in old Sal's wall, and put your

[004-257]mouth to it, and say, 'Please, North Wind, mayn't I go out with you?'

[004-258]and then you'll see what'll come.”

[004-259]“I daresay I shall. But I'm out in the wind too often already to want

[004-260]more of it.”

[004-261]“I said with the North Wind, not in it.”

[004-262]“It's all one.”

[004-263]“It's not all one.”

[004-264]“It is all one.”

[004-265]“But I know best.”

[004-266]“And I know better. I'll box your ears,” said the girl.

[004-267]Diamond got very angry. But he remembered that even if she did box his

[004-268]ears, he musn't box hers again, for she was a girl, and all that boys

[004-269]must do, if girls are rude, is to go away and leave them. So he went in

[004-270]at the door.

[004-271]“Good-bye, mister” said the girl.

[004-272]This brought Diamond to his senses.

[004-273]“I'm sorry I was cross,” he said. “Come in, and my mother will give you

[004-274]some breakfast.”

[004-275]“No, thank you. I must be off to my crossing. It's morning now.”

[004-276]“I'm very sorry for you,” said Diamond.

[004-277]“Well, it is a life to be tired of--what with old Sal, and so many holes

[004-278]in my shoes.”

[004-279]“I wonder you're so good. I should kill myself.”

[004-280]“Oh, no, you wouldn't! When I think of it, I always want to see what's

[004-281]coming next, and so I always wait till next is over. Well! I suppose

[004-282]there's somebody happy somewheres. But it ain't in them carriages. Oh

[004-283]my! how they do look sometimes--fit to bite your head off! Good-bye!”

[004-284]She ran up the hill and disappeared behind it. Then Diamond shut the

[004-285]door as he best could, and ran through the kitchen-garden to the stable.

[004-286]And wasn't he glad to get into his own blessed bed again!
