## Overview

The Crossing is the specific urban location where the novel's poorest children work — a stretch of muddy [[london|London]] street that [[nanny|Nanny]] and [[jim|Jim]] sweep clean for passing pedestrians, earning a few pennies a day. It is the place where [[diamond|Diamond]] first meets [[nanny|Nanny]] and where the novel's theme of child labor and urban [[suffering|poverty]] is most concretely realized.

Crossings were a feature of Victorian [[london|London]]: before paved roads became universal, wealthy pedestrians paid children to sweep a clear path through the mud and horse-droppings. The crossing is thus both a physical place and an economic one — a spot of contested territory where the poorest children compete for the chance to earn a living.

## Appearance and Character

The crossing is never described in picturesque terms. It is muddy, cold, and exposed — a place where children stand for hours in all weathers, hoping for the kindness of strangers. [[nanny|Nanny]]'s crossing is at the West End, the wealthier part of [[london|London]] where pedestrians are more likely to pay. [[jim|Jim]]'s crossing is less favorably located, and his lameness puts him at a disadvantage: "Nobody gave it to him; he took it" (029-030) is said of the boy who displaces him.

MacDonald uses the crossing as a moral testing-ground. It is where [[diamond|Diamond]] proves his character — not by giving money (though he does), but by actively helping [[nanny|Nanny]] sweep. And it is where the "tall gentleman" who will become [[mr-raymond|Mr. Raymond]]'s agent first notices [[nanny|Nanny]] (019-006–019-010), setting in motion the chain of events that leads to her rescue.

## Events

1. **[[diamond|Diamond]] meets [[nanny|Nanny]]** (Chapter IV): [[diamond|Diamond]] spots [[nanny|Nanny]] at a crossing and insists on helping her sweep. When his mother protests, he replies: "Yes; to help the little girl" (004-112). This is the moment [[diamond|Diamond]]'s moral life becomes active rather than passive.

2. **The lady at the crossing** (Chapter V): [[diamond|Diamond]] sees a girl sweeping "a crossing scuddingly before a lady" (005-042) — the lady is his father's mistress, [[mrs-coleman|Mrs. Coleman]]. He watches the encounter with fascination, the crossing becoming a place where the novel's social classes momentarily intersect.

3. **[[jim|Jim]]'s crossing lost** (Chapter XXIX): "Her crossing was taken long ago, and I couldn't bear to see her crying about it" (029-022). [[nanny|Nanny]] tells [[drunken-cabman|the cabman]] that her crossing was taken by another child while she was ill. The crossing is a precarious possession — held only by continuous physical presence.

4. **A contested space** (Chapter XXIX): "Nobody gave it to him; he took it," says [[nanny|Nanny]] of the boy who now sweeps her crossing. "If she were to sweep a crossing--soon at least--after the illness she had, she'd be laid up again the next week" (029-030–032). MacDonald makes clear that the crossing is not a gift or a right but something seized and defended.
