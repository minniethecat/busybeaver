# Angel

The figure of the **angel** recurs throughout *At the Back of [[north-wind|the North Wind]]* as a symbol of purity, otherworldly [[kindness|goodness]], and the boundary between the mortal and the divine. Characters are compared to angels, angelic beings populate [[diamond|Diamond]]'s dream-visions, and the concept serves as a bridge between the earthly narrative and the story's spiritual dimensions.

## Diamond as Angel

[[diamond|Diamond]] is repeatedly associated with angels by those who encounter him. His innocence and [[kindness|goodness]] lead other characters to perceive him as something more than an ordinary child. A cab passenger remarks that [[diamond|Diamond]] drove "as if he had been an angel with a flaming sword" (018-027). After [[diamond|Diamond]] departs, a woman exclaims, "that wur a angel just gone" (018-126), comparing him to the "baby-angels you sees on the gravestones" (018-128). These comparisons establish [[diamond|Diamond]]'s exceptional nature within the story's moral framework.

## Angelic Laborers in Dream

The most elaborate angel episode occurs in a dream-vision where [[diamond|Diamond]] encounters angelic beings engaged in celestial work. The little angels work diligently, digging and tending, and "the moment the angel saw what it was" they react with [[joy|delight]] (025-185). When children fall a[[dream|sleep]], "a troop of angels" comes to tend them (025-218). The angels sing, work, and celebrate — and [[diamond|Diamond]] is told "now the girl-angels are coming" (025-240). The dream weaves together childlike wonder with a vision of cosmic order maintained by angelic labor.

## Angel-Horses and Heaven

In a later conversation, [[diamond|Diamond]] declares himself an angel: "Because I'm an angel" (032-139). The discussion turns to whether "there are horses in [[heaven]] for angels to ride upon" (032-145), and [[diamond|Diamond]] explains that the horses in [[heaven]] must be "angel-horses" because ordinary horses would not be suitable for angels to ride (032-147). This whimsical theological speculation is characteristic of MacDonald's blend of childlike reasoning and spiritual imagination.

## Symbol of Moral Purity

Throughout the story, the angel functions as a measure of moral excellence. When [[diamond|Diamond]] is compared to an angel, it signals his exceptional kindness and innocence. The angelic realm represents the ultimate destination of [[kindness|goodness]] — a place where the values [[diamond|Diamond]] embodies in his earthly life find their fullest expression.
