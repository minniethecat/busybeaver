## Overview

The river appears throughout *At the Back of [[north-wind|the North Wind]]* as a boundary space between [[london|London]] and the dream-world. It is the site of some of [[diamond|Diamond]]'s most significant encounters with [[north-wind|North Wind]], and in the later chapters, its "[[music|song]]" becomes a metaphor for the eternal [[song|music]] of [[back-of-the-north-wind|the country at the back of the north wind]]. The river functions as both a literal geographical feature and a symbolic threshold.

## Location and Description

The river flows near [[diamond|Diamond]]'s home in [[london|London]]. It is described with a wall or embankment: "[[north-wind|North Wind]] perched again upon the river wall" (005-228). The road runs alongside it: "they stood in the road by the river" (003-187). MacDonald uses the river to delineate the urban from the natural — on one side, the grimy [[london|London streets]]; on the other, the open sky and water.

The river's appearance changes with the narrative mood. In early chapters, it is "full" (003-187) and wind-swept: "the wind was blowing a breeze down the river" (003-193). In the dream-saturated later passages, it becomes a living, [[music|singing]] entity: "the river sings and they drink it" (013-169).

## Role in the Story

### The Early Journeys

In the opening chapters, the river serves as the launch-point for [[diamond|Diamond]]'s flights with [[north-wind|North Wind]]. [[north-wind|North Wind]] leads him across the garden and over the river, and their [[darkness|night]] journeys often begin or end at its banks. "Away across the river went a long ripple" (005-245) marks [[north-wind|North Wind]]'s departure — the river carrying the trace of her passage.

### The River's Song

In the later chapters (XIII–XVI), the river acquires a mystical quality. [[diamond|Diamond]] hears the river sing: "One of the [[music|tune]]s the river sings" (010-083). The [[music|song]] is connected to [[back-of-the-north-wind|the country at the back of the north wind]]:

> "the river sings and they drink it and the wind blows and they dream" (013-169)

The river's [[song|music]] becomes a bridge between worlds — it flows from the dream-country into waking [[london|London]], and those who hear it are transported.

### The Final Passage

At the novel's close, [[diamond|Diamond]]'s last journeys are associated with the river's fading [[music|song]]: "the last far-off sounds of the river dying away" (016-008). The river that once carried him to adventure now carries him toward his final homecoming.

## Symbolism and Themes

The river functions as MacDonald's central symbol for the **boundary between mortality and eternity**. It is a threshold space — neither fully of [[london|London]] nor fully of the dream-country. This liminal quality makes it the natural setting for [[diamond|Diamond]]'s transformations: he is too awake for [[dream|dreams]] and too dream-touched for full wakefulness, and the river is where these states meet.

The river's [[music|song]] represents **divine communication through nature**. MacDonald, a Christian minister, infused his fantasy with theological meaning. The river that "sings" is nature bearing witness to its Creator — a Romantic and sacramental vision in which the physical world is transparent to the spiritual.
