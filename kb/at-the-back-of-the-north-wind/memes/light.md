## Definition

In *At the Back of [[north-wind|the North Wind]]*, light functions as more than descriptive detail — it is a theological motif that marks the presence of the supernatural within the natural world. MacDonald uses light, particularly moonlight, starlight, and [[north-wind|the North Wind]]'s own radiance, to signal moments when the boundary between the material and spiritual worlds grows thin. Light in the novel is consistently associated with [[north-wind|the North Wind]], with [[beauty]], and with the mystery of a world that is more than it appears.

## In the Novel

Light is the first sensory detail associated with [[north-wind|the North Wind]]. [[diamond|Diamond]] first sees her face illuminated: "all the light by which [[diamond|Diamond]] saw her face" (001-215) is the light of her own being — she is self-luminous, not dependent on external illumination. This establishes light as a property of the supernatural, not the natural world.

As [[diamond|Diamond]] flies with [[north-wind|the North Wind]], light becomes his guide through [[darkness|dark]]ness. When they travel at [[darkness|night]], "only a little light came" through the [[darkness|dark]]ness (007-069), enough to navigate by but never enough to dispel mystery entirely. In [[the-cathedral|the church]] where they pause, "only a little light came through the windows" (007-164), and the [[stars]] themselves provide insufficient illumination: "there was not enough of light in the [[stars]] to show the colours" (007-167). Light in MacDonald's world is given sparingly — enough to see by, never enough to fully comprehend.

The novel's most sustained meditation on light occurs during [[diamond|Diamond]]'s journey through the sky in Chapter IX. He observes "a strange light" (009-300) and [[north-wind|the North Wind]] explains it in terms of [[dream|sleep]] and [[dream|dreaming]]: "I am very [[dream|sleep]]y, and that is why the light he sends" is soft and gentle (009-302). The [[north-wind|North Wind]] herself then "with a single bound lighted on" an iceberg (009-307), making light a verb of action as well as a noun of perception.

[[diamond|Diamond]]'s underwater vision of the iceberg reveals another dimension of light: while the water above reflects light normally, "below water as above," a "light south wind" (009-350) illuminates the depths. The iceberg itself "reflected so much light below the water" (009-350) that [[diamond|Diamond]] could see into realms normally hidden. This passage is one of the novel's clearest metaphors for spiritual sight — light reveals what [[darkness|dark]]ness conceals, and the supernatural world is always present, merely unilluminated.

In the novel's embedded hymn, light is placed at the centre of MacDonald's eschatological vision: "A land of love and a land of light, / And the light a pure and cloudless beam" (010-058, 010-061). The pairing of love and light frames the afterlife not as an escape from the body but as an entry into perfect visibility — a world where nothing is hidden.

In the daylight world, light serves a more ordinary but no less significant function. Old [[diamond|Diamond]] the horse is seen "in the light of the morning" (008-054), a gentle domestic image that grounds the supernatural in the everyday. Even the natural light of dawn carries the novel's conviction that the world is fundamentally good.

## Key Passages

> "A land of love and a land of light, / And the light a pure and cloudless beam" (010-058, 010-061)

The novel's clearest statement of light as a theological symbol — the afterlife as perfect illumination, where all is seen and known.

> all the light by which Diamond saw her face (001-215)

The [[north-wind|North Wind]] is introduced as a source of light, not merely an object illuminated — she is supernatural presence made visible.

## Light and the Platonic Tradition

MacDonald's use of light draws on a long philosophical tradition stretching back to Plato's allegory of the cave, in which light represents truth that must be gradually learned to see. In the novel, [[diamond|Diamond]] never sees the full radiance of the supernatural all at once — he receives "only a little light" (007-069), always partial, always mediated. This is MacDonald's acknowledgment that finite creatures cannot bear direct vision of the divine; light must be filtered through [[north-wind|the North Wind]]'s presence, through dream, through the natural world. The novel thus participates in what scholars of MacDonald's work have called his "sacramental realism" — the conviction that material light is not merely a metaphor for spiritual light but a genuine participation in it.

## Light and Darkness as Complementary

Unlike a Manichaean framework that would oppose light and [[darkness|dark]]ness as good and evil, MacDonald's novel presents them as complementary conditions. [[darkness|Darkness]] is not the enemy of light but its context — without [[darkness|dark]]ness, light would have nothing to illuminate, and the partiality of light would have no meaning. The novel's [[darkness|night]] scenes are not threatening in themselves: [[diamond|Diamond]]'s flights occur mostly at [[darkness|night]], and the [[darkness|dark]]ness is protective as often as it is [[fear|fright]]ening. When [[diamond|Diamond]] rides through "a [[moon]]less [[darkness|night]], and very [[darkness|dark]], with glimpses of [[stars]]" (009-160), the [[darkness|dark]]ness is atmospheric rather than menacing. This theology of light-and-[[darkness|dark]]ness as collaborative rather than antagonistic reflects MacDonald's broader conviction that suffering and joy, fear and courage, death and life are not opposites in battle but partners in a single divine purpose.

## Related Characters

- **[[north-wind]]**: The novel's primary light-bearer, self-luminous and associated with moonlight and starlight throughout [[diamond|Diamond]]'s journeys. Her radiance signals her supernatural nature.
- **[[diamond]]**: The witness to light, whose name itself evokes the refraction and brilliance of light through a prism. His journey is in part a journey toward light — toward understanding what the supernatural illumination reveals.
