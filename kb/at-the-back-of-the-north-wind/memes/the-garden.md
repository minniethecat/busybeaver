## Overview

The Garden at [[mr-raymond|Mr. Raymond]]'s country cottage is the setting of the novel's final, peaceful chapters — a place of flowers, sunlight, and quiet companionship where [[diamond|Diamond]], [[nanny|Nanny]], and [[jim|Jim]] spend their days. It represents the earthly paradise that MacDonald's social vision offers as the reward of [[trust|faith]]ful endurance: a place where damaged children can heal and where [[diamond|Diamond]] prepares for his final journey to "[[back-of-the-north-wind|the country at the back of the north wind]]."

## Location and Description

The garden belongs to [[the-cottage|the country cottage]] that [[mr-raymond|Mr. Raymond]] provides for [[diamond|Diamond]]'s family. It is described as a cultivated but natural space — flowers grow, a gardener tends the grounds, and children play freely. A "little girl belonging to the gardener" (010-099) appears in one of [[diamond|Diamond]]'s dream-visions, linking the earthly garden to the [[heaven]]ly landscapes [[diamond|Diamond]] visits with [[north-wind|North Wind]].

The garden's [[beauty]] is understated. MacDonald does not lavish botanical detail on it; instead, he emphasizes the quality of life it enables — peace, rest, and community. The garden is less a spectacle than a condition: the condition of being safe, fed, and loved.

## Appearance and Character

The garden makes its first appearance in the novel not as a physical place but as a forbidden territory: [[mr-dyves|Mr. Dyves]]'s garden, into which [[diamond|Diamond]] ventures on his first flight with [[north-wind|North Wind]]. "You are out in [[mr-dyves|Mr. Dyves]]'s garden, and I can't" (001-198) — the broken sentence captures the garden's initial character as a space of trespass, [[beauty]] mixed with danger. When [[diamond|Diamond]] later recalls "I run through his garden" (020-156) to the clergyman, the garden has become a memory of childhood transgression transfigured by time.

The garden at [[mr-raymond|Mr. Raymond]]'s country cottage is altogether different in character. It is a "good long garden" (025-052) — the adjective "good" carrying MacDonald's full theological weight. This garden is not walled off like [[mr-dyves|Mr. Dyves]]'s; it opens onto meadows and streams. Its defining quality is **permission**: children may run through it freely, without fear of being caught. Where the [[london|London]] garden was a place of exclusion ([[diamond|Diamond]] could only visit it secretly, carried by [[north-wind|North Wind]]), the country garden is a place of belonging.

[[dream|Dream]] and [[truth|reality]] converge in the garden. [[diamond|Diamond]]'s vision of "a little girl belonging to the gardener" (010-099) prefigures the actual children — [[nanny|Nanny]], [[jim|Jim]], and [[diamond|Diamond]] himself — who will later play among its flowers. The gardener's daughter of the dream becomes, in the novel's symbolic logic, every child restored to the garden of childhood. In its final chapters, the garden hosts [[diamond|Diamond]]'s last [[music|song]], with "[[nanny|Nanny]] and [[jim|Jim]] sat a little way off" (035-251) — an image of quiet community that is the novel's highest vision of earthly [[joy|happiness]].

The garden also appears in a minor key earlier in the novel: the Colemans' garden, where [[diamond|Diamond]] "sat a good deal in the garden" (005-069) during his convalescence at their house. These scattered garden scenes — [[mr-dyves|Mr. Dyves]]'s, the Colemans', [[the-cottage|the country cottage]] — form a progressive sequence: from forbidden garden to borrowed garden to the garden that is truly home.

## Events

1. **[[diamond|Diamond]]'s trespass** (Chapter I): On his first flight with [[north-wind|North Wind]], [[diamond|Diamond]] finds himself in "[[mr-dyves|Mr. Dyves]]'s garden" (001-113). Mrs. Eve Dropper, "the gardener's wife" (001-278), spies him through the window. The episode establishes the garden as a liminal space between the familiar and the wondrous.

2. **The Colemans' garden** (Chapter V): During his recovery at the Colemans' house, [[diamond|Diamond]] "sat a good deal in the garden" (005-069). This garden is a place of peaceful convalescence, and [[diamond|Diamond]] is given "leave to run in the garden as he pleased" (005-072) — a foretaste of the freedom he will later enjoy at [[the-cottage|the country cottage]].

3. **[[diamond|Diamond]]'s vision of the gardener's daughter** (Chapter X): In a dream, [[diamond|Diamond]] sees "a little girl belonging to the gardener" (010-099) — a fore[[darkness|shadow]]ing of the garden's role as a meeting-place between earthly and [[heaven]]ly [[truth|reality]]. The gardener's child is both a real person (the gardener at [[the-cottage|Mr. Raymond's cottage]] has a daughter) and a type of Paradise.

4. **The country interlude** (Chapters XXX–XXXV): After the family's relocation, the garden becomes the daily setting for [[diamond|Diamond]]'s last months. He, [[nanny|Nanny]], and [[jim|Jim]] play among the flowers while the adults manage the household. These scenes are notable for their quiet [[home|domesticity]] — the novel's emotional climax occurs not in dramatic action but in peaceful ordinariness. In the twilight, [[diamond|Diamond]] runs in "the old garden" (025-049), and the garden of his dream becomes indistinguishable from the garden of his waking life.

5. **[[diamond|Diamond]]'s final days** (Chapter XXXV): [[diamond|Diamond]]'s last [[music|song]] is sung in or near the garden, with [[nanny|Nanny]] and [[jim|Jim]] "sat a little way off" (035-251). The garden frames his departure — not as tragedy but as a natural transition from one garden to another.

## Symbolism and Significance

The Garden is MacDonald's image of **earthly paradise** — not Eden before the Fall but the restored garden that honest work and Christian [[kindness|charity]] can create. It is the novel's answer to urban [[suffering|poverty]]: not revolution but relocation, not systemic change but personal generosity.

Theologically, the garden participates in the novel's central theme of **the interpenetration of worlds**. The earthly garden is real — flowers, earth, sunlight — but it is also a type of the [[heaven]]ly garden. [[diamond|Diamond]] moves between them without anxiety; his death is figured as walking from one garden into another. MacDonald's universalism erases the boundary between the earthly and [[heaven]]ly gardens, suggesting that both are aspects of a single divine [[truth|reality]].
