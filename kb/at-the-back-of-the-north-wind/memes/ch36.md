CHAPTER XXXVI. DIAMOND QUESTIONS NORTH WIND

[036-001]MY READERS will not wonder that, after this, I did my very best to gain

[036-002]the friendship of [[diamond|Diamond]]. Nor did I find this at all difficult, the

[036-003]child was so ready to trust. Upon one subject alone was he reticent--the

[036-004]story of his relations with [[north-wind|North Wind]]. I fancy he could not quite make

[036-005]up his mind what to think of them. At all events it was some little time

[036-006]before he trusted me with this, only then he told me everything. If

[036-007]I could not regard it all in exactly the same light as he did, I was,

[036-008]while guiltless of the least pretence, fully sympathetic, and he

[036-009]was satisfied without demanding of me any theory of difficult points

[036-010]involved. I let him see plainly enough, that whatever might be the

[036-011]explanation of the marvellous experience, I would have given much for a

[036-012]similar one myself.

[036-013]On an evening soon after the thunderstorm, in a late twilight, with

[036-014]a half-[[moon]] high in the heavens, I came upon Diamond in the act of

[036-015]climbing by his little ladder into the beech-tree.

[036-016]“What are you always going up there for, Diamond?” I heard [[nanny|Nanny]] ask,

[036-017]rather rudely, I thought.

[036-018]“Sometimes for one thing, sometimes for another, Nanny,” answered

[036-019]Diamond, looking skywards as he climbed.

[036-020]“You'll break your neck some day,” she said.

[036-021]“I'm going up to look at the moon to-[[darkness|night]],” he added, without heeding

[036-022]her remark.

[036-023]“You'll see the moon just as well down here,” she returned.

[036-024]“I don't think so.”

[036-025]“You'll be no nearer to her up there.”

[036-026]“Oh, yes! I shall. I must be nearer her, you know. I wish I could dream

[036-027]as pretty [[dream|dreams]] about her as you can, Nanny.”

[036-028]“You silly! you never have done about that dream. I never dreamed but

[036-029]that one, and it was nonsense enough, I'm sure.”

[036-030]“It wasn't nonsense. It was a beautiful dream--and a funny one too, both

[036-031]in one.”

[036-032]“But what's the good of talking about it that way, when you know it was

[036-033]only a dream? Dreams ain't true.”

[036-034]“That one was true, Nanny. You know it was. Didn't you come to grief for

[036-035]doing what you were told not to do? And isn't that true?”

[036-036]“I can't get any sense into him,” exclaimed Nanny, with an expression of

[036-037]mild despair. “Do you really believe, Diamond, that there's a house in

[036-038]the moon, with a beautiful lady and a crooked old man and dusters in

[036-039]it?”

[036-040]“If there isn't, there's something better,” he answered, and vanished in

[036-041]the leaves over our heads.

[036-042]I went into the house, where I visited often in the evenings. When I

[036-043]came out, there was a little wind blowing, very pleasant after the heat

[036-044]of the day, for although it was late summer now, it was still hot. The

[036-045]tree-tops were swinging about in it. I took my way past the beech, and

[036-046]called up to see if Diamond were still in his nest in its rocking head.

[036-047]“Are you there, Diamond?” I said.

[036-048]“Yes, sir,” came his clear voice in reply.

[036-049]“Isn't it growing too dark for you to get down safely?”

[036-050]“Oh, no, sir--if I take time to it. I know my way so well, and never let

[036-051]go with one hand till I've a good hold with the other.”

[036-052]“Do be careful,” I insisted--foolishly, seeing the boy was as careful as

[036-053]he could be already.

[036-054]“I'm coming,” he returned. “I've got all the moon I want to-night.”

[036-055]I heard a rustling and a rustling drawing nearer and nearer. Three or

[036-056]four minutes elapsed, and he appeared at length creeping down his little

[036-057]ladder. I took him in my arms, and set him on the ground.

[036-058]“Thank you, sir,” he said. “That's the north wind blowing, isn't it,

[036-059]sir?”

[036-060]“I can't tell,” I answered. “It feels cool and kind, and I think it may

[036-061]be. But I couldn't be sure except it were stronger, for a gentle wind

[036-062]might turn any way amongst the trunks of the trees.”

[036-063]“I shall know when I get up to my own room,” said Diamond. “I think I

[036-064]hear my mistress's bell. Good-night, sir.”

[036-065]He ran to the house, and I went home.

[036-066]His mistress had rung for him only to send him to bed, for she was very

[036-067]careful over him and I daresay thought he was not looking well. When he

[036-068]reached his own room, he opened both his windows, one of which looked to

[036-069]the north and the other to the east, to find how the wind blew. It blew

[036-070]right in at the northern window. Diamond was very glad, for he thought

[036-071]perhaps North Wind herself would come now: a real north wind had never

[036-072]blown all the time since he left [[london|London]]. But, as she always came of

[036-073]herself, and never when he was looking for her, and indeed almost never

[036-074]when he was thinking of her, he shut the east window, and went to bed.

[036-075]Perhaps some of my readers may wonder that he could go to sleep with

[036-076]such an expectation; and, indeed, if I had not known him, I should have

[036-077]wondered at it myself; but it was one of his peculiarities, and seemed

[036-078]nothing strange in him. He was so full of quietness that he could go

[036-079]to sleep almost any time, if he only composed himself and let the sleep

[036-080]come. This time he went fast asleep as usual.

[036-081]But he woke in the dim blue night. The moon had vanished. He thought he

[036-082]heard a knocking at his door. “Somebody wants me,” he said to himself,

[036-083]and jumping out of bed, ran to open it.

[036-084]But there was no one there. He closed it again, and, the noise still

[036-085]continuing, found that another door in the room was rattling. It

[036-086]belonged to a closet, he thought, but he had never been able to open it.

[036-087]The wind blowing in at the window must be shaking it. He would go and

[036-088]see if it was so.

[036-089]The door now opened quite easily, but to his surprise, instead of a

[036-090]closet he found a long narrow room. The moon, which was sinking in the

[036-091]west, shone in at an open window at the further end. The room was

[036-092]low with a coved ceiling, and occupied the whole top of the house,

[036-093]immediately under the roof. It was quite empty. The yellow light of

[036-094]the half-moon streamed over the dark floor. He was so delighted at the

[036-095]discovery of the strange, desolate, moonlit place close to his own snug

[036-096]little room, that he began to dance and skip about the floor. The wind

[036-097]came in through the door he had left open, and blew about him as he

[036-098]danced, and he kept turning towards it that it might blow in his face.

[036-099]He kept picturing to himself the many places, lovely and desolate, the

[036-100]hill-sides and farm-yards and tree-tops and meadows, over which it had

[036-101]blown on its way to The Mound. And as he danced, he grew more and more

[036-102]delighted with the motion and the wind; his feet grew stronger, and his

[036-103]body lighter, until at length it seemed as if he were borne up on the

[036-104]air, and could almost fly. So strong did his feeling become, that at

[036-105]last he began to doubt whether he was not in one of those precious

[036-106]dreams he had so often had, in which he floated about on the air at

[036-107]will. But something made him look up, and to his unspeakable [[joy|delight]], he

[036-108]found his uplifted hands lying in those of North Wind, who was dancing

[036-109]with him, round and round the long bare room, her hair now falling to

[036-110]the floor, now filling the arched ceiling, her eyes shining on him like

[036-111]thinking [[stars]], and the sweetest of grand smiles playing breezily about

[036-112]her beautiful mouth. She was, as so often before, of the height of a

[036-113]rather tall lady. She did not stoop in order to dance with him, but held

[036-114]his hands high in hers. When he saw her, he gave one spring, and his

[036-115]arms were about her neck, and her arms holding him to her bosom. The

[036-116]same moment she swept with him through the open window in at which

[036-117]the moon was shining, made a circuit like a bird about to alight, and

[036-118]settled with him in his nest on the top of the great beech-tree. There

[036-119]she placed him on her lap and began to hush him as if he were her own

[036-120]baby, and Diamond was so entirely happy that he did not care to speak a

[036-121]word. At length, however, he found that he was going to sleep, and

[036-122]that would be to lose so much, that, pleasant as it was, he could not

[036-123]consent.

[036-124]“Please, dear North Wind,” he said, “I am so happy that I'm afraid it's

[036-125]a dream. How am I to know that it's not a dream?”

[036-126]“What does it matter?” returned North Wind.

[036-127]“I should, cry” said Diamond.

[036-128]“But why should you cry? The dream, if it is a dream, is a pleasant

[036-129]one--is it not?”

[036-130]“That's just why I want it to be true.”

[036-131]“Have you forgotten what you said to Nanny about her dream?”

[036-132]“It's not for the dream itself--I mean, it's not for the pleasure of

[036-133]it,” answered Diamond, “for I have that, whether it be a dream or not;

[036-134]it's for you, North Wind; I can't bear to find it a dream, because then

[036-135]I should lose you. You would be nobody then, and I could not bear that.

[036-136]You ain't a dream, are you, dear North Wind? Do say No, else I shall

[036-137]cry, and come awake, and you'll be gone for ever. I daren't dream about

[036-138]you once again if you ain't anybody.”

[036-139]“I'm either not a dream, or there's something better that's not a dream,

[036-140]Diamond,” said North Wind, in a rather sorrowful tone, he thought.

[036-141]“But it's not something better--it's you I want, North Wind,” he

[036-142]persisted, already beginning to cry a little.

[036-143]She made no answer, but rose with him in her arms and sailed away over

[036-144]the tree-tops till they came to a meadow, where a flock of sheep was

[036-145]feeding.

[036-146]“Do you remember what the [[music|song]] you were [[song|singing]] a week ago says about

[036-147]Bo-Peep--how she lost her sheep, but got twice as many lambs?” asked

[036-148]North Wind, sitting down on the grass, and placing him in her lap as

[036-149]before.

[036-150]“Oh yes, I do, well enough,” answered Diamond; “but I never just quite

[036-151]liked that rhyme.”

[036-152]“Why not, child?”

[036-153]“Because it seems to say one's as good as another, or two new ones are

[036-154]better than one that's lost. I've been thinking about it a great deal,

[036-155]and it seems to me that although any one sixpence is as good as any

[036-156]other sixpence, not twenty lambs would do instead of one sheep whose

[036-157]face you knew. Somehow, when once you've looked into anybody's eyes,

[036-158]right deep down into them, I mean, nobody will do for that one any more.

[036-159]Nobody, ever so beautiful or so good, will make up for that one going

[036-160]out of sight. So you see, North Wind, I can't help being frightened to

[036-161]think that perhaps I am only dreaming, and you are nowhere at all. Do

[036-162]tell me that you are my own, real, beautiful North Wind.”

[036-163]Again she rose, and shot herself into the air, as if uneasy because she

[036-164]could not answer him; and Diamond lay quiet in her arms, waiting

[036-165]for what she would say. He tried to see up into her face, for he was

[036-166]dreadfully afraid she was not answering him because she could not say

[036-167]that she was not a dream; but she had let her hair fall all over her

[036-168]face so that he could not see it. This frightened him still more.

[036-169]“Do speak, North Wind,” he said at last.

[036-170]“I never speak when I have nothing to say,” she replied.

[036-171]“Then I do think you must be a real North Wind, and no dream,” said

[036-172]Diamond.

[036-173]“But I'm looking for something to say all the time.”

[036-174]“But I don't want you to say what's hard to find. If you were to say one

[036-175]word to comfort me that wasn't true, then I should know you must be a

[036-176]dream, for a great beautiful lady like you could never tell a lie.”

[036-177]“But she mightn't know how to say what she had to say, so that a little

[036-178]boy like you would understand it,” said North Wind. “Here, let us get

[036-179]down again, and I will try to tell you what I think. You musn't suppose

[036-180]I am able to answer all your questions, though. There are a great many

[036-181]things I don't understand more than you do.”

[036-182]She descended on a grassy hillock, in the midst of a wild furzy common.

[036-183]There was a rabbit-warren underneath, and some of the rabbits came out

[036-184]of their holes, in the [[light|moonlight]], looking very sober and wise, just like

[036-185]patriarchs standing in their tent-doors, and looking about them before

[036-186]going to bed. When they saw North Wind, instead of turning round and

[036-187]vanishing again with a thump of their heels, they cantered slowly up to

[036-188]her and snuffled all about her with their long upper lips, which moved

[036-189]every way at once. That was their way of kissing her; and, as she talked

[036-190]to Diamond, she would every now and then stroke down their furry backs,

[036-191]or lift and play with their long ears. They would, Diamond thought, have

[036-192]leaped upon her lap, but that he was there already.

[036-193]“I think,” said she, after they had been sitting silent for a while,

[036-194]“that if I were only a dream, you would not have been able to love me

[036-195]so. You love me when you are not with me, don't you?”

[036-196]“Indeed I do,” answered Diamond, stroking her hand. “I see! I see! How

[036-197]could I be able to love you as I do if you weren't there at all, you

[036-198]know? Besides, I couldn't be able to dream anything half so beautiful

[036-199]all out of my own head; or if I did, I couldn't love a fancy of my own

[036-200]like that, could I?”

[036-201]“I think not. You might have loved me in a dream, dreamily, and

[036-202]forgotten me when you woke, I daresay, but not loved me like a real

[036-203]being as you love me. Even then, I don't think you could dream anything

[036-204]that hadn't something real like it somewhere. But you've seen me in many

[036-205]shapes, Diamond: you remember I was a wolf once--don't you?”

[036-206]“Oh yes--a good wolf that frightened a naughty drunken nurse.”

[036-207]“Well, suppose I were to turn ugly, would you rather I weren't a dream

[036-208]then?”

[036-209]“Yes; for I should know that you were beautiful inside all the same. You

[036-210]would love me, and I should love you all the same. I shouldn't like you

[036-211]to look ugly, you know. But I shouldn't believe it a bit.”

[036-212]“Not if you saw it?”

[036-213]“No, not if I saw it ever so plain.”

[036-214]“There's my Diamond! I will tell you all I know about it then. I don't

[036-215]think I am just what you fancy me to be. I have to shape myself various

[036-216]ways to various people. But the heart of me is true. People call me

[036-217]by dreadful names, and think they know all about me. But they don't.

[036-218]Sometimes they call me Bad Fortune, sometimes Evil Chance, sometimes

[036-219]Ruin; and they have another name for me which they think the most

[036-220]dreadful of all.”

[036-221]“What is that?” asked Diamond, smiling up in her face.

[036-222]“I won't tell you that name. Do you remember having to go through me to

[036-223]get into the country at my back?”

[036-224]“Oh yes, I do. How cold you were, North Wind! and so white, all but your

[036-225]lovely eyes! My heart grew like a lump of ice, and then I forgot for a

[036-226]while.”

[036-227]“You were very near knowing what they call me then. Would you be afraid

[036-228]of me if you had to go through me again?”

[036-229]“No. Why should I? Indeed I should be glad enough, if it was only to get

[036-230]another peep of the country at your back.”

[036-231]“You've never seen it yet.”

[036-232]“Haven't I, North Wind? Oh! I'm so sorry! I thought I had. What did I

[036-233]see then?”

[036-234]“Only a picture of it. The real country at my real back is ever so much

[036-235]more beautiful than that. You shall see it one day--perhaps before very

[036-236]long.”

[036-237]“Do they sing songs there?”

[036-238]“Don't you remember the dream you had about the little boys that dug for

[036-239]the stars?”

[036-240]“Yes, that I do. I thought you must have had something to do with that

[036-241]dream, it was so beautiful.”

[036-242]“Yes; I gave you that dream.”

[036-243]“Oh! thank you. Did you give Nanny her dream too--about the moon and the

[036-244]bees?”

[036-245]“Yes. I was the lady that sat at the window of the moon.”

[036-246]“Oh, thank you. I was almost sure you had something to do with that too.

[036-247]And did you tell [[mr-raymond|Mr. Raymond]] the story about the Princess Daylight?”

[036-248]“I believe I had something to do with it. At all events he thought about

[036-249]it one night when he couldn't sleep. But I want to ask you whether you

[036-250]remember the song the boy-angels sang in that dream of yours.”

[036-251]“No. I couldn't keep it, do what I would, and I did try.”

[036-252]“That was my fault.”

[036-253]“How could that be, North Wind?”

[036-254]“Because I didn't know it properly myself, and so I couldn't teach it to

[036-255]you. I could only make a rough guess at something like what it would be,

[036-256]and so I wasn't able to make you dream it hard enough to remember it.

[036-257]Nor would I have done so if I could, for it was not correct. I made you

[036-258]dream pictures of it, though. But you will hear the very song itself

[036-259]when you do get to the back of----”

[036-260]“My own dear North Wind,” said Diamond, finishing the sentence for her,

[036-261]and kissing the arm that held him leaning against her.

[036-262]“And now we've settled all this--for the time, at least,” said North

[036-263]Wind.

[036-264]“But I can't feel quite sure yet,” said Diamond.

[036-265]“You must wait a while for that. Meantime you may be hopeful, and

[036-266]content not to be quite sure. Come now, I will take you home again, for

[036-267]it won't do to tire you too much.”

[036-268]“Oh, no, no. I'm not the least tired,” pleaded Diamond.

[036-269]“It is better, though.”

[036-270]“Very well; if you wish it,” yielded Diamond with a sigh.

[036-271]“You are a dear good, boy” said North Wind. “I will come for you again

[036-272]to-morrow night and take you out for a longer time. We shall make a

[036-273]little journey together, in fact, we shall start earlier, and as the

[036-274]moon will be later, we shall have a little moonlight all the way.”

[036-275]She rose, and swept over the meadow and the trees. In a few moments

[036-276]the Mound appeared below them. She sank a little, and floated in at the

[036-277]window of Diamond's room. There she laid him on his bed, covered him

[036-278]over, and in a moment he was lapt in a dreamless sleep.
