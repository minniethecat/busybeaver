CHAPTER VII. THE CATHEDRAL

[007-001]I MUST not go on describing what cannot be described, for nothing is

[007-002]more wearisome.

[007-003]Before they reached the sea, [[diamond|Diamond]] felt [[north-wind|North Wind]]'s hair just

[007-004]beginning to fall about him.

[007-005]“Is the storm over, North Wind?” he called out.

[007-006]“No, Diamond. I am only waiting a moment to set you down. You would not

[007-007]like to see the ship sunk, and I am going to give you a place to stop in

[007-008]till I come back for you.”

[007-009]“Oh! thank you,” said Diamond. “I shall be sorry to leave you, North

[007-010]Wind, but I would rather not see the ship go down. And I'm afraid the

[007-011]poor people will cry, and I should hear them. Oh, dear!”

[007-012]“There are a good many passengers on board; and to tell the truth,

[007-013]Diamond, I don't care about your hearing the cry you speak of. I am

[007-014]afraid you would not get it out of your little head again for a long

[007-015]time.”

[007-016]“But how can you bear it then, North Wind? For I am sure you are kind. I

[007-017]shall never doubt that again.”

[007-018]“I will tell you how I am able to bear it, Diamond: I am always hearing,

[007-019]through every noise, through all the noise I am making myself even, the

[007-020]sound of a far-off [[music|song]]. I do not exactly know where it is, or what it

[007-021]means; and I don't hear much of it, only the odour of its [[song|music]], as it

[007-022]were, flitting across the great billows of [[the-sea|the ocean]] outside this air in

[007-023]which I make such a storm; but what I do hear is quite enough to make

[007-024]me able to bear the cry from the drowning ship. So it would you if you

[007-025]could hear it.”

[007-026]“No, it wouldn't,” returned Diamond, stoutly. “For they wouldn't hear

[007-027]the music of the far-away song; and if they did, it wouldn't do them

[007-028]any good. You see you and I are not going to be drowned, and so we might

[007-029]enjoy it.”

[007-030]“But you have never heard the psalm, and you don't know what it is like.

[007-031]Somehow, I can't say how, it tells me that all is right; that it is

[007-032]coming to swallow up all cries.”

[007-033]“But that won't do them any good--the people, I mean,” persisted

[007-034]Diamond.

[007-035]“It must. It must,” said North Wind, hurriedly. “It wouldn't be the song

[007-036]it seems to be if it did not swallow up all their fear and [[suffering|pain]] too, and

[007-037]set them singing it themselves with the rest. I am sure it will. And do

[007-038]you know, ever since I knew I had hair, that is, ever since it began

[007-039]to go out and away, that song has been coming nearer and nearer. Only I

[007-040]must say it was some thousand years before I heard it.”

[007-041]“But how can you say it was coming nearer when you did not hear it?”

[007-042]asked doubting little Diamond.

[007-043]“Since I began to hear it, I know it is growing louder, therefore I

[007-044]judge it was coming nearer and nearer until I did hear it first. I'm not

[007-045]so very old, you know--a few thousand years only--and I was quite a baby

[007-046]when I heard the noise first, but I knew it must come from the voices

[007-047]of people ever so much older and wiser than I was. I can't sing at all,

[007-048]except now and then, and I can never tell what my song is going to be; I

[007-049]only know what it is after I have sung it.--But this will never do. Will

[007-050]you stop here?”

[007-051]“I can't see anywhere to stop,” said Diamond. “Your hair is all down

[007-052]like a darkness, and I can't see through it if I knock my eyes into it

[007-053]ever so much.”

[007-054]“Look, then,” said North Wind; and, with one sweep of her great white

[007-055]arm, she swept yards deep of darkness like a great curtain from before

[007-056]the face of the boy.

[007-057]And lo! it was a blue [[darkness|night]], lit up with [[stars]]. Where it did not shine

[007-058]with stars it shimmered with the milk of the stars, except where, just

[007-059]opposite to Diamond's face, the grey towers of a cathedral blotted out

[007-060]each its own shape of sky and stars.

[007-061]“Oh! what's that?” cried Diamond, struck with a kind of [[fear|terror]], for he

[007-062]had never seen a cathedral, and it rose before him with an awful [[truth|reality]]

[007-063]in the midst of the wide spaces, conquering emptiness with grandeur.

[007-064]“A very good place for you to wait in,” said North Wind. “But we shall

[007-065]go in, and you shall judge for yourself.”

[007-066]There was an open door in the middle of one of the towers, leading out

[007-067]upon the roof, and through it they passed. Then North Wind set Diamond

[007-068]on his feet, and he found himself at the top of a stone stair, which

[007-069]went twisting away down into the darkness for only a little light came

[007-070]in at the door. It was enough, however, to allow Diamond to see that

[007-071]North Wind stood beside him. He looked up to find her face, and saw that

[007-072]she was no longer a beautiful giantess, but the tall gracious lady he

[007-073]liked best to see. She took his hand, and, giving him the broad part

[007-074]of the spiral stair to walk on, led him down a good way; then, opening

[007-075]another little door, led him out upon a narrow gallery that ran all

[007-076]round the central part of [[the-cathedral|the church]], on the ledges of the windows

[007-077]of the clerestory, and through openings in the parts of the wall that

[007-078]divided the windows from each other. It was very narrow, and except when

[007-079]they were passing through the wall, Diamond saw nothing to keep him

[007-080]from falling into the church. It lay below him like a great silent gulf

[007-081]hollowed in stone, and he held his breath for fear as he looked down.

[007-082]“What are you trembling for, little Diamond?” said the lady, as she

[007-083]walked gently along, with her hand held out behind her leading him, for

[007-084]there was not breadth enough for them to walk side by side.

[007-085]“I am afraid of falling down there,” answered Diamond. “It is so deep

[007-086]down.”

[007-087]“Yes, rather,” answered North Wind; “but you were a hundred times higher

[007-088]a few minutes ago.”

[007-089]“Ah, yes, but somebody's arm was about me then,” said Diamond, putting

[007-090]his little mouth to the beautiful cold hand that had a hold of his.

[007-091]“What a dear little warm mouth you've got!” said North Wind. “It is a

[007-092]pity you should talk nonsense with it. Don't you know I have a hold of

[007-093]you?”

[007-094]“Yes; but I'm walking on my own legs, and they might slip. I can't trust

[007-095]myself so well as your arms.”

[007-096]“But I have a hold of you, I tell you, foolish child.”

[007-097]“Yes, but somehow I can't feel comfortable.”

[007-098]“If you were to fall, and my hold of you were to give way, I should be

[007-099]down after you in a less moment than a lady's watch can tick, and catch

[007-100]you long before you had reached the ground.”

[007-101]“I don't like it though,” said Diamond.

[007-102]“Oh! oh! oh!” he screamed the next moment, bent double with terror, for

[007-103]North Wind had let go her hold of his hand, and had vanished, leaving

[007-104]him standing as if rooted to the gallery.

[007-105]She left the words, “Come after me,” sounding in his ears.

[007-106]But move he dared not. In a moment more he would from very terror have

[007-107]fallen into the church, but suddenly there came a gentle breath of cool

[007-108]wind upon his face, and it kept blowing upon him in little puffs, and at

[007-109]every puff Diamond felt his faintness going away, and his fear with it.

[007-110][[courage|Courage]] was reviving in his little heart, and still the cool wafts of

[007-111]the soft wind breathed upon him, and the soft wind was so mighty and

[007-112]strong within its gentleness, that in a minute more Diamond was marching

[007-113]along the narrow ledge as fearless for the time as North Wind herself.

[007-114]He walked on and on, with the windows all in a row on one side of him,

[007-115]and the great empty nave of the church echoing to every one of his brave

[007-116]strides on the other, until at last he came to a little open door, from

[007-117]which a broader stair led him down and down and down, till at last all

[007-118]at once he found himself in the arms of North Wind, who held him close

[007-119]to her, and kissed him on the forehead. Diamond nestled to her, and

[007-120]murmured into her bosom,--“Why did you leave me, dear North Wind?”

[007-121]“Because I wanted you to walk alone,” she answered.

[007-122]“But it is so much nicer here!” said Diamond.

[007-123]“I daresay; but I couldn't hold a little coward to my heart. It would

[007-124]make me so cold!”

[007-125]“But I wasn't brave of myself,” said Diamond, whom my older readers will

[007-126]have already discovered to be a true child in this, that he was given to

[007-127]metaphysics. “It was the wind that blew in my face that made me brave.

[007-128]Wasn't it now, North Wind?”

[007-129]“Yes: I know that. You had to be taught what courage was. And you

[007-130]couldn't know what it was without feeling it: therefore it was given

[007-131]you. But don't you feel as if you would try to be brave yourself next

[007-132]time?”

[007-133]“Yes, I do. But trying is not much.”

[007-134]“Yes, it is--a very great deal, for it is a beginning. And a beginning

[007-135]is the greatest thing of all. To try to be brave is to be brave. The

[007-136]coward who tries to be brave is before the man who is brave because he

[007-137]is made so, and never had to try.”

[007-138]“How kind you are, North Wind!”

[007-139]“I am only just. All kindness is but justice. We owe it.”

[007-140]“I don't quite understand that.”

[007-141]“Never mind; you will some day. There is no hurry about understanding it

[007-142]now.”

[007-143]“Who blew the wind on me that made me brave?”

[007-144]“I did.”

[007-145]“I didn't see you.”

[007-146]“Therefore you can believe me.”

[007-147]“Yes, yes; of course. But how was it that such a little breath could be

[007-148]so strong?”

[007-149]“That I don't know.”

[007-150]“But you made it strong?”

[007-151]“No: I only blew it. I knew it would make you strong, just as it did the

[007-152]man in the boat, you remember. But how my breath has that power I cannot

[007-153]tell. It was put into it when I was made. That is all I know. But really

[007-154]I must be going about my work.”

[007-155]“Ah! the poor ship! I wish you would stop here, and let the poor ship

[007-156]go.”

[007-157]“That I dare not do. Will you stop here till I come back?”

[007-158]“Yes. You won't be long?”

[007-159]“Not longer than I can help. [[trust|Trust]] me, you shall get home before the

[007-160]morning.”

[007-161]In a moment North Wind was gone, and the next Diamond heard a moaning

[007-162]about the church, which grew and grew to a roaring. The storm was up

[007-163]again, and he knew that North Wind's hair was flying.

[007-164]The church was dark. Only a little light came through the windows, which

[007-165]were almost all of that precious old stained glass which is so much

[007-166]lovelier than the new. But Diamond could not see how beautiful they

[007-167]were, for there was not enough of light in the stars to show the colours

[007-168]of them. He could only just distinguish them from the walls, He looked

[007-169]up, but could not see the gallery along which he had passed. He could

[007-170]only tell where it was far up by the faint glimmer of the windows of

[007-171]the clerestory, whose sills made part of it. The church grew very lonely

[007-172]about him, and he began to feel like a child whose mother has forsaken

[007-173]it. Only he knew that to be left alone is not always to be forsaken.

[007-174]He began to feel his way about the place, and for a while went wandering

[007-175]up and down. His little footsteps waked little answering echoes in the

[007-176]great house. It wasn't too big to mind him. It was as if the church knew

[007-177]he was there, and meant to make itself his house. So it went on giving

[007-178]back an answer to every step, until at length Diamond thought he should

[007-179]like to say something out loud, and see what the church would answer.

[007-180]But he found he was afraid to speak. He could not utter a word for fear

[007-181]of the loneliness. Perhaps it was as well that he did not, for the sound

[007-182]of a spoken word would have made him feel the place yet more deserted

[007-183]and empty. But he thought he could sing. He was fond of singing, and

[007-184]at home he used to sing, to tunes of his own, all the nursery rhymes he

[007-185]knew. So he began to try `Hey diddle diddle', but it wouldn't do. Then

[007-186]he tried `Little Boy Blue', but it was no better. Neither would `Sing a

[007-187]Song of Sixpence' sing itself at all. Then he tried `Poor old Cockytoo',

[007-188]but he wouldn't do. They all sounded so silly! and he had never thought

[007-189]them silly before. So he was quiet, and listened to the echoes that came

[007-190]out of the dark corners in answer to his footsteps.

[007-191]At last he gave a great sigh, and said, “I'm so tired.” But he did not

[007-192]hear the gentle echo that answered from far away over his head, for at

[007-193]the same moment he came against the lowest of a few steps that stretched

[007-194]across the church, and fell down and hurt his arm. He cried a little

[007-195]first, and then crawled up the steps on his hands and knees. At the top

[007-196]he came to a little bit of carpet, on which he lay down; and there he

[007-197]lay staring at the dull window that rose nearly a hundred feet above his

[007-198]head.

[007-199]Now this was the eastern window of the church, and the [[moon]] was at that

[007-200]moment just on the edge of the horizon. The next, she was peeping over

[007-201]it. And lo! with the moon, St. John and St. Paul, and the rest of them,

[007-202]began to dawn in the window in their lovely garments. Diamond did not

[007-203]know that the wonder-working moon was behind, and he thought all the

[007-204]light was coming out of the window itself, and that the good old men

[007-205]were appearing to help him, growing out of the night and the darkness,

[007-206]because he had hurt his arm, and was very tired and lonely, and North

[007-207]Wind was so long in coming. So he lay and looked at them backwards over

[007-208]his head, wondering when they would come down or what they would do

[007-209]next. They were very dim, for the [[light|moonlight]] was not strong enough for

[007-210]the colours, and he had enough to do with his eyes trying to make out

[007-211]their shapes. So his eyes grew tired, and more and more tired, and his

[007-212]eyelids grew so heavy that they would keep tumbling down over his eyes.

[007-213]He kept lifting them and lifting them, but every time they were heavier

[007-214]than the last. It was no use: they were too much for him. Sometimes

[007-215]before he had got them half up, down they were again; and at length he

[007-216]gave it up quite, and the moment he gave it up, he was fast asleep.
