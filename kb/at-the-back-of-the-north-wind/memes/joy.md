## Definition

In *At the Back of [[north-wind|the North Wind]]*, joy is more than a passing emotion — it is a fundamental orientation toward the world that MacDonald presents as both natural to childhood and indicative of spiritual health. Joy arises spontaneously in [[diamond|Diamond]], the protagonist, and serves as a bridge between the ordinary world of Victorian [[london|London]] and the extraordinary realm accessed through [[north-wind|the North Wind]]. MacDonald distinguishes between surface pleasures and a deeper joy rooted in love, trust, and wonder, making joy one of the novel's primary thematic instruments for conveying its Christian vision without explicit sermonising.

## In the Novel

Joy appears in the novel through three primary channels: [[diamond|Diamond]]'s innate disposition, the joy of reunion and love, and the natural world's capacity to awaken delight.

[[diamond|Diamond]]'s character is defined from the outset by a joyfulness that persists despite [[suffering|hardship]]. He does not require favourable circumstances to be glad; rather, his gladness is a quality of perception, a way of seeing the world that transforms even [[suffering|poverty]] and illness into occasions for gratitude. When [[diamond|Diamond]] returns home with shillings earned driving the cab, the narrative notes his mother's "pride in her boy was even greater than her joy in the shillings" (025-007) — joy at [[diamond|Diamond]] himself surpasses material relief, underscoring the novel's hierarchy of values.

The natural world functions as an engine of joy throughout the novel. The [[north-wind|North Wind]]'s presence is itself a source of overwhelming gladness: [[diamond|Diamond]]'s heart is described as "beating with joy" at the sight of her face (002-026). This connection between natural grandeur — the wind, the sky, the [[stars]] — and joy runs through the entire narrative, suggesting that the created world is designed to evoke joy in those who remain open to it.

The novel also explores joy as a social and communal experience. [[diamond|Diamond]] becomes "the centre of their enjoyment" among the children at the hospital, "rejoicing over him as if they had found a" treasure (025-143). Joy here is contagious — [[diamond|Diamond]]'s presence generates joy in others, and their joy in turn amplifies his own. This reciprocal structure positions joy as a moral force that builds community.

The most intense expression of joy in the novel occurs at the boundary between worlds. When [[diamond|Diamond]] enters the country at the back of [[north-wind|the North Wind]] in Chapter XXXIII, and again in the closing chapters, joy is described in language that approaches the ineffable — a joy so complete that it "did not know what to break for" (025-179). These moments link joy to the novel's eschatological dimension, where the ultimate joy awaits beyond the threshold of ordinary experience.

## Key Passages

> "beating with joy: the face of the North Wind was so grand!" (002-026)

[[diamond|Diamond]]'s first encounter with [[north-wind|the North Wind]], establishing joy as the natural response to encountering the sublime.

> "whose pride in her boy was even greater than her joy in the shillings." (025-007)

Joy and love intertwined — the mother's joy in [[diamond|Diamond]] transcends material gain, defining the novel's value system.

> "the centre of their enjoyment, rejoicing over him as if they had found a" (025-143)

Joy as a social force — [[diamond|Diamond]]'s presence creates communal gladness, illustrating the contagious nature of authentic joy.

> "break for, joy; and he said that if he had not cried, he did not know" (025-179)

Joy at the limits of language — the novel's representation of joy so complete it overwhelms expression, pointing toward the ineffable.

> "It blew into Diamond's heart, and made him so happy" (025-146)

The wind as a direct conduit of joy — a literal in-spiration that transforms [[diamond|Diamond]]'s emotional state, linking nature to spiritual experience.

## Joy and Sorrow

MacDonald's treatment of joy in the novel is not naïvely optimistic. Joy coexists with suffering — [[diamond|Diamond]]'s family is poor, his health is fragile, and his adventures with [[north-wind|the North Wind]] often [[fear|fright]]en him. The novel does not present joy as the absence of sorrow but as a deeper current that runs beneath it. [[diamond|Diamond]]'s tears and his laughter spring from the same source: an openness to experience that refuses to harden into cynicism. This integration of joy and sorrow reflects MacDonald's theological understanding that true joy is eschatological — it belongs ultimately to the world to come, and its appearances in this world are anticipatory glimpses rather than permanent possessions.

## Related Characters

- **[[diamond]]**: The novel's embodiment of joy, whose natural gladness transforms everyone he encounters. His joy is a quality of being rather than a reaction to circumstances.
- **[[north-wind]]**: As the source of [[diamond|Diamond]]'s initial joy and the guide who leads him toward deeper understanding, [[north-wind|the North Wind]] functions as joy's teacher and gatekeeper.
- **[[nanny]]**: [[diamond|Diamond]]'s friend whose own capacity for joy is awakened through his kindness, demonstrating joy's contagious quality.
