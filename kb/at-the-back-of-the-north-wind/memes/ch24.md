CHAPTER XXIV. ANOTHER EARLY BIRD

[024-001]HE GOT up in the morning as soon as he heard the men moving in the yard.

[024-002]He tucked in his little brother so that he could not tumble out of bed,

[024-003]and then went out, leaving the door open, so that if he should cry his

[024-004]mother might hear him at once. When he got into the yard he found the

[024-005]stable-door just opened.

[024-006]“I'm the early bird, I think,” he said to himself. “I hope I shall catch

[024-007]the worm.”

[024-008]He would not ask any one to help him, fearing his project might meet

[024-009]with disapproval and opposition. With great difficulty, but with the

[024-010]help of a broken chair he brought down from his bedroom, he managed to

[024-011]put the harness on [[diamond|Diamond]]. If the old horse had had the least objection

[024-012]to the proceeding, of course he could not have done it; but even when it

[024-013]came to the bridle, he opened his mouth for the bit, just as if he had

[024-014]been taking the apple which Diamond sometimes gave him. He fastened the

[024-015]cheek-strap very carefully, just in the usual hole, for fear of choking

[024-016]his friend, or else letting the bit get amongst his teeth. It was a job

[024-017]to get the saddle on; but with the chair he managed it. If old Diamond

[024-018]had had an education in physics to equal that of the camel, he would

[024-019]have knelt down to let him put it on his back, but that was more than

[024-020]could be expected of him, and then Diamond had to creep quite under him

[024-021]to get hold of the girth. The collar was almost the worst part of the

[024-022]business; but there Diamond could help Diamond. He held his head very

[024-023]low till his little master had got it over and turned it round, and

[024-024]then he lifted his head, and shook it on to his shoulders. The yoke was

[024-025]rather difficult; but when he had laid the traces over the horse's neck,

[024-026]the weight was not too much for him. He got him right at last, and led

[024-027]him out of the stable.

[024-028]By this time there were several of the men watching him, but they would

[024-029]not interfere, they were so anxious to see how he would get over the

[024-030]various difficulties. They followed him as far as the stable-door, and

[024-031]there stood watching him again as he put the horse between the shafts,

[024-032]got them up one after the other into the loops, fastened the traces, the

[024-033]belly-band, the breeching, and the reins.

[024-034]Then he got his whip. The moment he mounted the box, the men broke into

[024-035]a hearty cheer of [[joy|delight]] at his success. But they would not let him go

[024-036]without a general inspection of the harness; and although they found it

[024-037]right, for not a buckle had to be shifted, they never allowed him to do

[024-038]it for himself again all the time his father was ill.

[024-039]The cheer brought his mother to the window, and there she saw her little

[024-040]boy setting out alone with the cab in the gray of morning. She tugged at

[024-041]the window, but it was stiff; and before she could open it, Diamond, who

[024-042]was in a great hurry, was out of the mews, and almost out of the street.

[024-043]She called “Diamond! Diamond!” but there was no answer except from Jack.

[024-044]“Never fear for him, ma'am,” said Jack. “It 'ud be only a devil as would

[024-045]hurt him, and there ain't so many o' them as some folk 'ud have you

[024-046]believe. A boy o' Diamond's size as can 'arness a 'oss t'other Diamond's

[024-047]size, and put him to, right as a trivet--if he do upset the keb--'ll

[024-048]fall on his feet, ma'am.”

[024-049]“But he won't upset the cab, will he, Jack?”

[024-050]“Not he, ma'am. Leastways he won't go for to do it.”

[024-051]“I know as much as that myself. What do you mean?”

[024-052]“I mean he's a little likely to do it as the oldest man in the stable.

[024-053]How's the gov'nor to-day, ma'am?”

[024-054]“A good deal better, thank you,” she answered, closing the window in

[024-055]some fear lest her husband should have been made anxious by the news

[024-056]of Diamond's expedition. He knew pretty well, however, what his boy

[024-057]was capable of, and although not quite easy was less anxious than

[024-058]his mother. But as the evening drew on, the anxiety of both of them

[024-059]increased, and every sound of wheels made his father raise himself in

[024-060]his bed, and his mother peep out of the window.

[024-061]Diamond had resolved to go straight to the cab-stand where he was best

[024-062]known, and never to crawl for fear of getting annoyed by idlers. Before

[024-063]he got across Oxford Street, however, he was hailed by a man who wanted

[024-064]to catch a train, and was in too great a hurry to think about the

[024-065]driver. Having carried him to King's Cross in good time, and got a good

[024-066]fare in return, he set off again in great spirits, and reached the stand

[024-067]in safety. He was the first there after all.

[024-068]As the men arrived they all greeted him kindly, and inquired after his

[024-069]father.

[024-070]“Ain't you afraid of the old 'oss running away with you?” asked one.

[024-071]“No, he wouldn't run away with me,” answered Diamond. “He knows I'm

[024-072]getting the shillings for father. Or if he did he would only run home.”

[024-073]“Well, you're a plucky one, for all your girl's looks!” said the man;

[024-074]“and I wish ye luck.”

[024-075]“Thank you, sir,” said Diamond. “I'll do what I can. I came to the old

[024-076]place, you see, because I knew you would let me have my turn here.”

[024-077]In the course of the day one man did try to cut him out, but he was a

[024-078]stranger; and the shout the rest of them raised let him see it would not

[024-079]do, and made him so far ashamed besides, that he went away crawling.

[024-080]Once, in a block, a policeman came up to him, and asked him for his

[024-081]number. Diamond showed him his father's badge, saying with a smile:

[024-082]“Father's ill at home, and so I came out with the cab. There's no fear

[024-083]of me. I can drive. Besides, the old horse could go alone.”

[024-084]“Just as well, I daresay. You're a pair of 'em. But you are a rum 'un

[024-085]for a cabby--ain't you now?” said the policeman. “I don't know as I

[024-086]ought to let you go.”

[024-087]“I ain't done nothing,” said Diamond. “It's not my fault I'm no bigger.

[024-088]I'm big enough for my age.”

[024-089]“That's where it is,” said the man. “You ain't fit.”

[024-090]“How do you know that?” asked Diamond, with his usual smile, and turning

[024-091]his head like a little bird.

[024-092]“Why, how are you to get out of this ruck now, when it begins to move?”

[024-093]“Just you get up on the box,” said Diamond, “and I'll show you. There,

[024-094]that van's a-moving now. Jump up.”

[024-095]The policeman did as Diamond told him, and was soon satisfied that the

[024-096]little fellow could drive.

[024-097]“Well,” he said, as he got down again, “I don't know as I should be

[024-098]right to interfere. Good luck to you, my little man!”

[024-099]“Thank you, sir,” said Diamond, and drove away.

[024-100]In a few minutes a gentleman hailed him.

[024-101]“Are you the driver of this cab?” he asked.

[024-102]“Yes, sir” said Diamond, showing his badge, of which, he was proud.

[024-103]“You're the youngest cabman I ever saw. How am I to know you won't break

[024-104]all my bones?”

[024-105]“I would rather break all my own,” said Diamond. “But if you're afraid,

[024-106]never mind me; I shall soon get another fare.”

[024-107]“I'll risk it,” said [[mr-raymond|the gentleman]]; and, opening the door himself, he

[024-108]jumped in.

[024-109]He was going a good distance, and soon found that Diamond got him over

[024-110]the ground well. Now when Diamond had only to go straight ahead, and had

[024-111]not to mind so much what he was about, his thoughts always turned to the

[024-112]riddle Mr. Raymond had set him; and this gentleman looked so clever that

[024-113]he fancied he must be able to read it for him. He had given up all hope

[024-114]of finding it out for himself, and he could not plague his father about

[024-115]it when he was ill. He had thought of the answer himself, but fancied it

[024-116]could not be the right one, for to see how it all fitted required some

[024-117]knowledge of physiology. So, when he reached the end of his journey, he

[024-118]got down very quickly, and with his head just looking in at the window,

[024-119]said, as the gentleman gathered his gloves and newspapers:

[024-120]“Please, sir, can you tell me the meaning of a riddle?”

[024-121]“You must tell me the riddle first,” answered the gentleman, amused.

[024-122]Diamond repeated the riddle.

[024-123]“Oh! that's easy enough,” he returned. “It's a tree.”

[024-124]“Well, it ain't got no mouth, sure enough; but how then does it eat all

[024-125]day long?”

[024-126]“It sucks in its food through the tiniest holes in its leaves,” he

[024-127]answered. “Its breath is its food. And it can't do it except in the

[024-128]daylight.”

[024-129]“Thank you, sir, thank you,” returned Diamond. “I'm sorry I couldn't

[024-130]find it out myself; Mr. Raymond would have been better pleased with me.”

[024-131]“But you needn't tell him any one told you.”

[024-132]Diamond gave him a stare which came from the very back of the north

[024-133]wind, where that kind of thing is unknown.

[024-134]“That would be cheating,” he said at last.

[024-135]“Ain't you a cabby, then?”

[024-136]“Cabbies don't cheat.”

[024-137]“Don't they? I am of a different opinion.”

[024-138]“I'm sure my father don't.”

[024-139]“What's your fare, young innocent?”

[024-140]“Well, I think the distance is a good deal over three miles--that's two

[024-141]shillings. Only father says sixpence a mile is too little, though we

[024-142]can't ask for more.”

[024-143]“You're a deep one. But I think you're wrong. It's over four miles--not

[024-144]much, but it is.”

[024-145]“Then that's half-a-crown,” said Diamond.

[024-146]“Well, here's three shillings. Will that do?”

[024-147]“Thank you kindly, sir. I'll tell my father how good you were to

[024-148]me--first to tell me my riddle, then to put me right about the distance,

[024-149]and then to give me sixpence over. It'll help father to get well again,

[024-150]it will.”

[024-151]“I hope it may, my man. I shouldn't wonder if you're as good as you

[024-152]look, after all.”

[024-153]As Diamond returned, he drew up at a stand he had never been on before:

[024-154]it was time to give Diamond his bag of chopped beans and oats. The men

[024-155]got about him, and began to chaff him. He took it all good-humouredly,

[024-156]until one of them, who was an ill-conditioned fellow, began to tease old

[024-157]Diamond by poking him roughly in the ribs, and making general game of

[024-158]him. That he could not bear, and the tears came in his eyes. He undid

[024-159]the nose-bag, put it in the boot, and was just going to mount and drive

[024-160]away, when the fellow interfered, and would not let him get up. Diamond

[024-161]endeavoured to persuade him, and was very civil, but he would have his

[024-162]fun out of him, as he said. In a few minutes a group of idle boys had

[024-163]assembled, and Diamond found himself in a very uncomfortable position.

[024-164]Another cab drew up at the stand, and the driver got off and approached

[024-165]the assemblage.

[024-166]“What's up here?” he asked, and Diamond knew the voice. It was that of

[024-167]the drunken cabman.

[024-168]“Do you see this young oyster? He pretends to drive a cab,” said his

[024-169]enemy.

[024-170]“Yes, I do see him. And I sees you too. You'd better leave him alone. He

[024-171]ain't no oyster. He's a [[angel]] come down on his own business. You be off,

[024-172]or I'll be nearer you than quite agreeable.”

[024-173]The drunken cabman was a tall, stout man, who did not look one to take

[024-174]liberties with.

[024-175]“Oh! if he's a friend of yours,” said the other, drawing back.

[024-176]Diamond got out the nose-bag again. Old Diamond should have his feed out

[024-177]now.

[024-178]“Yes, he is a friend o' mine. One o' the best I ever had. It's a pity

[024-179]he ain't a friend o' yourn. You'd be the better for it, but it ain't no

[024-180]fault of hisn.”

[024-181]When Diamond went home at [[darkness|night]], he carried with him one pound one

[024-182]shilling and sixpence, besides a few coppers extra, which had followed

[024-183]some of the fares.

[024-184]His mother had got very anxious indeed--so much so that she was almost

[024-185]afraid, when she did hear the sound of his cab, to go and look, lest

[024-186]she should be yet again disappointed, and should break down before her

[024-187]husband. But there was the old horse, and there was the cab all right,

[024-188]and there was Diamond in the box, his pale face looking triumphant as a

[024-189]full [[moon]] in the twilight.

[024-190]When he drew up at the stable-door, Jack came out, and after a good many

[024-191]friendly questions and congratulations, said:

[024-192]“You go in to your mother, Diamond. I'll put up the old 'oss. I'll take

[024-193]care on him. He do deserve some small attention, he do.”

[024-194]“Thank you, Jack,” said Diamond, and bounded into the house, and into

[024-195]the arms of his mother, who was waiting him at the top of the stair.

[024-196]The poor, anxious woman led him into his own room, sat down on his bed,

[024-197]took him on her lap as if he had been a baby, and cried.

[024-198]“How's father?” asked Diamond, almost afraid to ask.

[024-199]“Better, my child,” she answered, “but uneasy about you, my dear.”

[024-200]“Didn't you tell him I was the early bird gone out to catch the worm?”

[024-201]“That was what put it in your head, was it, you monkey?” said his

[024-202]mother, beginning to get better.

[024-203]“That or something else,” answered Diamond, so very quietly that his

[024-204]mother held his head back and stared in his face.

[024-205]“Well! of all the children!” she said, and said no more.

[024-206]“And here's my worm,” resumed Diamond.
