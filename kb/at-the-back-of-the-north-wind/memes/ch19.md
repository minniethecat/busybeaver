CHAPTER XIX. DIAMOND'S FRIENDS

[019-001]ONE day when old [[diamond|Diamond]] was standing with his nose in his bag between

[019-002]Pall Mall and Cockspur Street, and his master was reading the newspaper

[019-003]on the box of his cab, which was the last of a good many in the row,

[019-004]little Diamond got down for a run, for his legs were getting cramped

[019-005]with sitting. And first of all he strolled with his hands in his pockets

[019-006]up to the crossing, where the girl and her broom were to be found in all

[019-007]weathers. Just as he was going to speak to her, a tall gentleman stepped

[019-008]upon the crossing. He was pleased to find it so clean, for the streets

[019-009]were muddy, and he had nice boots on; so he put his hand in his pocket,

[019-010]and gave the girl a penny. But when she gave him a sweet smile in

[019-011]return, and made him a pretty courtesy, he looked at her again, and

[019-012]said:

[019-013]“Where do you live, my child?”

[019-014]“Paradise Row,” she answered; “next door to the Adam and Eve--down the

[019-015]area.”

[019-016]“Whom do you live with?” he asked.

[019-017]“My wicked old grannie,” she replied.

[019-018]“You shouldn't call your grannie wicked,” said [[mr-raymond|the gentleman]].

[019-019]“But she is,” said the girl, looking up confidently in his face. “If you

[019-020]don't believe me, you can come and take a look at her.”

[019-021]The words sounded rude, but the girl's face looked so simple that

[019-022]the gentleman saw she did not mean to be rude, and became still more

[019-023]interested in her.

[019-024]“Still you shouldn't say so,” he insisted.

[019-025]“Shouldn't I? Everybody calls her wicked old grannie--even them that's

[019-026]as wicked as her. You should hear her swear. There's nothing like it in

[019-027]the Row. Indeed, I assure you, sir, there's ne'er a one of them can shut

[019-028]my grannie up once she begins and gets right a-going. You must put her

[019-029]in a passion first, you know. It's no good till you do that--she's so

[019-030]old now. How she do make them laugh, to be sure!”

[019-031]Although she called her wicked, the child spoke so as plainly to

[019-032]indicate pride in her grannie's pre-eminence in swearing.

[019-033]The gentleman looked very grave to hear her, for he was sorry that such

[019-034]a nice little girl should be in such bad keeping. But he did not know

[019-035]what to say next, and stood for a moment with his eyes on the ground.

[019-036]When he lifted them, he saw the face of Diamond looking up in his.

[019-037]“Please, sir,” said Diamond, “her grannie's very cruel to her sometimes,

[019-038]and shuts her out in the streets at [[darkness|night]], if she happens to be late.”

[019-039]“Is this your brother?” asked the gentleman of the girl.

[019-040]“No, sir.”

[019-041]“How does he know your grandmother, then? He does not look like one of

[019-042]her sort.”

[019-043]“Oh no, sir! He's a good boy--quite.”

[019-044]Here she tapped her forehead with her finger in a significant manner.

[019-045]“What do you mean by that?” asked the gentleman, while Diamond looked on

[019-046]smiling.

[019-047]“The cabbies call him God's baby,” she whispered. “He's not right in the

[019-048]head, you know. A tile loose.”

[019-049]Still Diamond, though he heard every word, and understood it too, kept

[019-050]on smiling. What could it matter what people called him, so long as he

[019-051]did nothing he ought not to do? And, besides, God's baby was surely the

[019-052]best of names!

[019-053]“Well, my little man, and what can you do?” asked the gentleman, turning

[019-054]towards him--just for the sake of saying something.

[019-055]“Drive a cab,” said Diamond.

[019-056]“Good; and what else?” he continued; for, accepting what the girl had

[019-057]said, he regarded the still sweetness of Diamond's face as a sign of

[019-058]silliness, and wished to be kind to the poor little fellow.

[019-059]“Nurse a baby,” said Diamond.

[019-060]“Well--and what else?”

[019-061]“Clean father's boots, and make him a bit of toast for his tea.”

[019-062]“You're a useful little man,” said the gentleman. “What else can you

[019-063]do?”

[019-064]“Not much that I know of,” said Diamond. “I can't curry a horse, except

[019-065]somebody puts me on his back. So I don't count that.”

[019-066]“Can you read?”

[019-067]“No. But mother can and father can, and they're going to teach me some

[019-068]day soon.”

[019-069]“Well, here's a penny for you.”

[019-070]“Thank you, sir.”

[019-071]“And when you have learned to read, come to me, and I'll give you

[019-072]sixpence and a book with fine pictures in it.”

[019-073]“Please, sir, where am I to come?” asked Diamond, who was too much a

[019-074]man of the world not to know that he must have the gentleman's address

[019-075]before he could go and see him.

[019-076]“You're no such silly!” thought he, as he put his hand in his pocket,

[019-077]and brought out a card. “There,” he said, “your father will be able to

[019-078]read that, and tell you where to go.”

[019-079]“Yes, sir. Thank you, sir,” said Diamond, and put the card in his

[019-080]pocket.

[019-081]The gentleman walked away, but turning round a few paces off, saw

[019-082]Diamond give his penny to the girl, and, walking slower heard him say:

[019-083]“I've got a father, and mother, and little brother, and you've got

[019-084]nothing but a wicked old grannie. You may have my penny.”

[019-085]The girl put it beside the other in her pocket, the only trustworthy

[019-086]article of dress she wore. Her grandmother always took care that she had

[019-087]a stout pocket.

[019-088]“Is she as cruel as ever?” asked Diamond.

[019-089]“Much the same. But I gets more coppers now than I used to, and I can

[019-090]get summats to eat, and take browns enough home besides to keep her from

[019-091]grumbling. It's a good thing she's so blind, though.”

[019-092]“Why?” asked Diamond.

[019-093]“'Cause if she was as sharp in the eyes as she used to be, she would

[019-094]find out I never eats her broken wittles, and then she'd know as I must

[019-095]get something somewheres.”

[019-096]“Doesn't she watch you, then?”

[019-097]“O' course she do. Don't she just! But I make believe and drop it in my

[019-098]lap, and then hitch it into my pocket.”

[019-099]“What would she do if she found you out?”

[019-100]“She never give me no more.”

[019-101]“But you don't want it!”

[019-102]“Yes, I do want it.”

[019-103]“What do you do with it, then?”

[019-104]“Give it to [[jim|cripple Jim]].”

[019-105]“Who's cripple Jim?”

[019-106]“A boy in the Row. His mother broke his leg when he wur a kid, so he's

[019-107]never come to much; but he's a good boy, is Jim, and I love Jim dearly.

[019-108]I always keeps off a penny for Jim--leastways as often as I can.--But

[019-109]there I must sweep again, for them busses makes no end o' dirt.”

[019-110]“Diamond! Diamond!” cried his father, who was afraid he might get no

[019-111]good by talking to the girl; and Diamond obeyed, and got up again

[019-112]upon the box. He told his father about the gentleman, and what he had

[019-113]promised him if he would learn to read, and showed him the gentleman's

[019-114]card.

[019-115]“Why, it's not many doors from the Mews!” said his father, giving him

[019-116]back the card. “Take care of it, my boy, for it may lead to something.

[019-117]God knows, in these hard times a man wants as many friends as he's ever

[019-118]likely to get.”

[019-119]“Haven't you got friends enough, father?” asked Diamond.

[019-120]“Well, I have no right to complain; but the more the better, you know.”

[019-121]“Just let me count,” said Diamond.

[019-122]And he took his hands from his pockets, and spreading out the fingers of

[019-123]his left hand, began to count, beginning at the thumb.

[019-124]“There's mother, first, and then baby, and then me. Next there's old

[019-125]Diamond--and the cab--no, I won't count the cab, for it never looks at

[019-126]you, and when Diamond's out of the shafts, it's nobody. Then there's the

[019-127]man that drinks next door, and his wife, and his baby.”

[019-128]“They're no friends of mine,” said his father.

[019-129]“Well, they're friends of mine,” said Diamond.

[019-130]His father laughed.

[019-131]“Much good they'll do you!” he said.

[019-132]“How do you know they won't?” returned Diamond.

[019-133]“Well, go on,” said his father.

[019-134]“Then there's Jack and [[mr-stonecrop|Mr. Stonecrop]], and, deary me! not to have

[019-135]mentioned [[mr-coleman|Mr. Coleman]] and [[mrs-coleman|Mrs. Coleman]], and [[miss-coleman|Miss Coleman]], and Mrs.

[019-136]Crump. And then there's the clergyman that spoke to me in the garden

[019-137]that day the tree was blown down.”

[019-138]“What's his name!”

[019-139]“I don't know his name.”

[019-140]“Where does he live?”

[019-141]“I don't know.”

[019-142]“How can you count him, then?”

[019-143]“He did talk to me, and very kindlike too.”

[019-144]His father laughed again.

[019-145]“Why, child, you're just counting everybody you know. That don't make

[019-146]'em friends.”

[019-147]“Don't it? I thought it did. Well, but they shall be my friends. I shall

[019-148]make 'em.”

[019-149]“How will you do that?”

[019-150]“They can't help themselves then, if they would. If I choose to be their

[019-151]friend, you know, they can't prevent me. Then there's that girl at the

[019-152]crossing.”

[019-153]“A fine set of friends you do have, to be sure, Diamond!”

[019-154]“Surely she's a friend anyhow, father. If it hadn't been for her, you

[019-155]would never have got Mrs. Coleman and Miss Coleman to carry home.”

[019-156]His father was silent, for he saw that Diamond was right, and was

[019-157]ashamed to find himself more ungrateful than he had thought.

[019-158]“Then there's the new gentleman,” Diamond went on.

[019-159]“If he do as he say,” interposed his father.

[019-160]“And why shouldn't he? I daresay sixpence ain't too much for him to

[019-161]spare. But I don't quite understand, father: is nobody your friend but

[019-162]the one that does something for you?”

[019-163]“No, I won't say that, my boy. You would have to leave out baby then.”

[019-164]“Oh no, I shouldn't. Baby can laugh in your face, and crow in your ears,

[019-165]and make you feel so happy. Call you that nothing, father?”

[019-166]The father's heart was fairly touched now. He made no answer to this

[019-167]last appeal, and Diamond ended off with saying:

[019-168]“And there's the best of mine to come yet--and that's you, daddy--except

[019-169]it be mother, you know. You're my friend, daddy, ain't you? And I'm your

[019-170]friend, ain't I?”

[019-171]“And God for us all,” said his father, and then they were both silent

[019-172]for that was very solemn.
