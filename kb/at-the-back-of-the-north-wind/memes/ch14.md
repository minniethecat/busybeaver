CHAPTER XIV. OLD DIAMOND

[014-001]AFTER this [[diamond|Diamond]] recovered so fast, that in a few days he was quite

[014-002]able to go home as soon as his father had a place for them to go. Now

[014-003]his father having saved a little money, and finding that no situation

[014-004]offered itself, had been thinking over a new plan. A strange occurrence

[014-005]it was which turned his thoughts in that direction. He had a friend in

[014-006]the Bloomsbury region, who lived by letting out cabs and horses to the

[014-007]cabmen. This man, happening to meet him one day as he was returning from

[014-008]an unsuccessful application, said to him:

[014-009]“Why don't you set up for yourself now--in the cab line, I mean?”

[014-010]“I haven't enough for that,” answered [[diamonds-father|Diamond's father]].

[014-011]“You must have saved a goodish bit, I should think. Just come home with

[014-012]me now and look at a horse I can let you have cheap. I bought him only a

[014-013]few weeks ago, thinking he'd do for a Hansom, but I was wrong. He's got

[014-014]bone enough for a waggon, but a waggon ain't a Hansom. He ain't got go

[014-015]enough for a Hansom. You see parties as takes Hansoms wants to go like

[014-016]the wind, and he ain't got wind enough, for he ain't so young as he once

[014-017]was. But for a four-wheeler as takes families and their luggages, he's

[014-018]the very horse. He'd carry a small house any day. I bought him cheap,

[014-019]and I'll sell him cheap.”

[014-020]“Oh, I don't want him,” said Diamond's father. “A body must have time

[014-021]to think over an affair of so much importance. And there's the cab too.

[014-022]That would come to a deal of money.”

[014-023]“I could fit you there, I daresay,” said his friend. “But come and look

[014-024]at the animal, anyhow.”

[014-025]“Since I lost my own old pair, as was [[mr-coleman|Mr. Coleman]]'s,” said Diamond's

[014-026]father, turning to accompany the cab-master, “I ain't almost got the

[014-027]heart to look a horse in the face. It's a thousand pities to part man

[014-028]and horse.”

[014-029]“So it is,” returned his friend sympathetically.

[014-030]But what was the ex-coachman's [[joy|delight]], when, on going into the stable

[014-031]where his friend led him, he found the horse he wanted him to buy was

[014-032]no other than his own old Diamond, grown very thin and bony and

[014-033]long-legged, as if they, had been doing what they could to fit him for

[014-034]Hansom work!

[014-035]“He ain't a Hansom horse,” said Diamond's father indignantly.

[014-036]“Well, you're right. He ain't handsome, but he's a good un” said his

[014-037]owner.

[014-038]“Who says he ain't handsome? He's one of the handsomest horses a

[014-039]gentleman's coachman ever druv,” said Diamond's father; remarking to

[014-040]himself under his breath--“though I says it as shouldn't”--for he did

[014-041]not feel inclined all at once to confess that his own old horse could

[014-042]have sunk so low.

[014-043]“Well,” said his friend, “all I say is--There's a animal for you, as

[014-044]strong as a church; an'll go like a train, leastways a parly,” he added,

[014-045]correcting himself.

[014-046]But the coachman had a lump in his throat and tears in his eyes. For the

[014-047]old horse, hearing his voice, had turned his long neck, and when his

[014-048]old friend went up to him and laid his hand on his side, he whinnied

[014-049]for joy, and laid his big head on his master's breast. This settled the

[014-050]matter. The coachman's arms were round the horse's neck in a moment, and

[014-051]he fairly broke down and cried. The cab-master had never been so fond of

[014-052]a horse himself as to hug him like that, but he saw in a moment how it

[014-053]was. And he must have been a good-hearted fellow, for I never heard of

[014-054]such an idea coming into the head of any other man with a horse to sell:

[014-055]instead of putting something on to the price because he was now pretty

[014-056]sure of selling him, he actually took a pound off what he had meant to

[014-057]ask for him, saying to himself it was a shame to part old friends.

[014-058]Diamond's father, as soon as he came to himself, turned and asked how

[014-059]much he wanted for the horse.

[014-060]“I see you're old friends,” said the owner.

[014-061]“It's my own old Diamond. I liked him far the best of the pair, though

[014-062]the other was good. You ain't got him too, have you?”

[014-063]“No; nothing in the stable to match him there.”

[014-064]“I believe you,” said the coachman. “But you'll be wanting a long price

[014-065]for him, I know.”

[014-066]“No, not so much. I bought him cheap, and as I say, he ain't for my

[014-067]work.”

[014-068]The end of it was that Diamond's father bought old Diamond again, along

[014-069]with a four-wheeled cab. And as there were some rooms to be had over the

[014-070]stable, he took them, wrote to his wife to come home, and set up as a

[014-071]cabman.
