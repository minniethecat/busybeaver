## Definition

Song in *At the Back of [[north-wind|the North Wind]]* is not merely aesthetic expression but a form of spiritual agency. [[diamond|Diamond]] sings to the baby, to the [[drunken-cabman|drunken cabman]], and ultimately to [[nanny|Nanny]] and [[jim|Jim]] in the garden — and in each case, the [[music|song]] effects real change. Song is the novel's image of how the divine enters the world: not through argument or force but through [[beauty]] that penetrates where logic cannot reach.

## In the Novel

1. **[[diamond|Diamond]]'s [[music|song]]s to the baby** (Chapter XVI): [[diamond|Diamond]] sings "the sweetest, loveliest little [[music|song]]s to the baby — of his own making" (016-025). The baby "crowed like a little bantam" (016-073) in response. Song here is pure gift — made up on the spot, offered without self-consciousness.

2. **The drunken cabman episode** (Chapter XVIII): This is the novel's most explicit demonstration of [[music|song]]'s redemptive power. [[diamond|Diamond]] sits with the unconscious cabman and sings: "But they did [[drunken-cabman|the cabman]] good" (018-072). The narrative is unambiguous — the [[music|song]]s have quasi-sacramental efficacy. "As soon as he stopped [[music|singing]], [[drunken-cabman|the cabman]] began to wake up" (018-076), leading to [[drunken-cabman|the cabman]]'s confession and reconciliation.

3. **[[diamond|Diamond]]'s last [[music|song]]** (Chapter XXXV): [[diamond|Diamond]]'s final [[music|song]] is sung in the country garden with [[nanny|Nanny]] and [[jim|Jim]] "sat a little way off" (035-251). It is his last act before his death — a valediction that transforms departure into gift.

## Analysis

Song in *At the Back of [[north-wind|the North Wind]]* functions as MacDonald's alternative to theological argument. [[diamond|Diamond]] does not debate [[drunken-cabman|the cabman]] into sobriety or persuade [[nanny|Nanny]] with doctrine — he sings. The efficacy of [[music|song]] is presented as a given: "they did [[drunken-cabman|the cabman]] good" (018-072) is stated as simple fact, not miracle. MacDonald's implicit claim is that [[beauty]] carries moral and spiritual power that rational discourse cannot match.

This aligns with MacDonald's broader aesthetic theology, in which the artist — especially the poet-musician — is a type of the divine creator. [[diamond|Diamond]]'s spontaneous compositions mirror the creative act; he makes [[music|song]]s "of his own making" (016-025) just as God makes the world. The child artist is the image of God in miniature.

In Victorian literary context, the trope of redemptive music appears across the period — from George Eliot's *Daniel Deronda* to the hymn-[[music|singing]] of Dickens's poor. MacDonald's distinctive contribution is to make [[music|song]] not merely consoling but causally effective. [[diamond|Diamond]]'s [[music|song]]s do not merely comfort [[drunken-cabman|the cabman]]; they wake him. The aesthetic is not decorative but operative — [[beauty]] that acts.
