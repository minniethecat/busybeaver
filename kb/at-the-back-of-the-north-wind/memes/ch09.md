CHAPTER IX. HOW DIAMOND GOT TO THE BACK OF THE NORTH WIND

[009-001]WHEN [[diamond|Diamond]] went home to breakfast, he found his father and mother

[009-002]already seated at the table. They were both busy with their bread and

[009-003]butter, and Diamond sat himself down in his usual place. His mother

[009-004]looked up at him, and, after watching him for a moment, said:

[009-005]“I don't think the boy is looking well, husband.”

[009-006]“Don't you? Well, I don't know. I think he looks pretty bobbish. How do

[009-007]you feel yourself, Diamond, my boy?”

[009-008]“Quite well, thank you, father; at least, I think I've got a little

[009-009]headache.”

[009-010]“There! I told you,” said his father and mother both at once.

[009-011]“The child's very poorly” added his mother.

[009-012]“The child's quite well,” added his father.

[009-013]And then they both laughed.

[009-014]“You see,” said his mother, “I've had a letter from my sister at

[009-015][[sandwich|Sandwich]].”

[009-016]“Sleepy old hole!” said his father.

[009-017]“Don't abuse the place; there's good people in it,” said his mother.

[009-018]“Right, old lady,” returned his father; “only I don't believe there are

[009-019]more than two pair of carriage-horses in the whole blessed place.”

[009-020]“Well, people can get to [[heaven]] without carriages--or coachmen either,

[009-021]husband. Not that I should like to go without my coachman, you know. But

[009-022]about the boy?”

[009-023]“What boy?”

[009-024]“That boy, there, staring at you with his goggle-eyes.”

[009-025]“Have I got goggle-eyes, mother?” asked Diamond, a little dismayed.

[009-026]“Not too goggle,” said his mother, who was quite proud of her boy's

[009-027]eyes, only did not want to make him vain.

[009-028]“Not too goggle; only you need not stare so.”

[009-029]“Well, what about him?” said his father.

[009-030]“I told you I had got a letter.”

[009-031]“Yes, from your sister; not from Diamond.”

[009-032]“La, husband! you've got out of bed the wrong leg first this morning, I

[009-033]do believe.”

[009-034]“I always get out with both at once,” said his father, laughing.

[009-035]“Well, listen then. His aunt wants the boy to go down and see her.”

[009-036]“And that's why you want to make out that he ain't looking well.”

[009-037]“No more he is. I think he had better go.”

[009-038]“Well, I don't care, if you can find the money,” said his father.

[009-039]“I'll manage that,” said his mother; and so it was agreed that Diamond

[009-040]should go to Sandwich.

[009-041]I will not describe the preparations Diamond made. You would have

[009-042]thought he had been going on a three months' voyage. Nor will I describe

[009-043]the journey, for our business is now at the place. He was met at the

[009-044]station by his aunt, a cheerful middle-aged woman, and conveyed in

[009-045]safety to the sleepy old town, as his father called it. And no wonder

[009-046]that it was sleepy, for it was nearly dead of old age.

[009-047]Diamond went about staring with his beautiful goggle-eyes, at the quaint

[009-048]old streets, and the shops, and the houses. Everything looked very

[009-049]strange, indeed; for here was a town abandoned by its nurse, the sea,

[009-050]like an old oyster left on the shore till it gaped for weariness. It

[009-051]used to be one of the five chief seaports in England, but it began to

[009-052]hold itself too high, and the consequence was the sea grew less and less

[009-053]intimate with it, gradually drew back, and kept more to itself, till at

[009-054]length it left it high and dry: Sandwich was a seaport no more; the sea

[009-055]went on with its own tide-business a long way off, and forgot it. Of

[009-056]course it went to [[dream|sleep]], and had no more to do with ships. That's what

[009-057]comes to cities and nations, and boys and girls, who say, “I can do

[009-058]without your help. I'm enough for myself.”

[009-059]Diamond soon made great friends with an old woman who kept a toyshop,

[009-060]for his mother had given him twopence for pocket-money before he left,

[009-061]and he had gone into her shop to spend it, and she got talking to him.

[009-062]She looked very funny, because she had not got any teeth, but Diamond

[009-063]liked her, and went often to her shop, although he had nothing to spend

[009-064]there after the twopence was gone.

[009-065]One afternoon he had been wandering rather wearily about the streets

[009-066]for some time. It was a hot day, and he felt tired. As he passed the

[009-067]toyshop, he stepped in.

[009-068]“Please may I sit down for a minute on this box?” he said, thinking the

[009-069]old woman was somewhere in the shop. But he got no answer, and sat down

[009-070]without one. Around him were a great many toys of all prices, from a

[009-071]penny up to shillings. All at once he heard a gentle whirring somewhere

[009-072]amongst them. It made him start and look behind him. There were the

[009-073]sails of a windmill going round and round almost close to his ear. He

[009-074]thought at first it must be one of those toys which are wound up and go

[009-075]with clockwork; but no, it was a common penny toy, with the windmill at

[009-076]the end of a whistle, and when the whistle blows the windmill goes. But

[009-077]the wonder was that there was no one at the whistle end blowing, and

[009-078]yet the sails were turning round and round--now faster, now slower, now

[009-079]faster again.

[009-080]“What can it mean?” said Diamond, aloud.

[009-081]“It means me,” said the tiniest voice he had ever heard.

[009-082]“Who are you, please?” asked Diamond.

[009-083]“Well, really, I begin to be ashamed of you,” said the voice. “I wonder

[009-084]how long it will be before you know me; or how often I might take you in

[009-085]before you got sharp enough to suspect me. You are as bad as a baby that

[009-086]doesn't know his mother in a new bonnet.”

[009-087]“Not quite so bad as that, dear [[north-wind|North Wind]],” said Diamond, “for I didn't

[009-088]see you at all, and indeed I don't see you yet, although I recognise

[009-089]your voice. Do grow a little, please.”

[009-090]“Not a hair's-breadth,” said the voice, and it was the smallest voice

[009-091]that ever spoke. “What are you doing here?”

[009-092]“I am come to see my aunt. But, please, North Wind, why didn't you come

[009-093]back for me in [[the-cathedral|the church]] that [[darkness|night]]?”

[009-094]“I did. I carried you safe home. All the time you were dreaming about

[009-095]the glass Apostles, you were lying in my arms.”

[009-096]“I'm so glad,” said Diamond. “I thought that must be it, only I wanted

[009-097]to hear you say so. Did you sink the ship, then?”

[009-098]“Yes.”

[009-099]“And drown everybody?”

[009-100]“Not quite. One boat got away with six or seven men in it.”

[009-101]“How could the boat swim when the ship couldn't?”

[009-102]“Of course I had some trouble with it. I had to contrive a bit, and

[009-103]manage the waves a little. When they're once thoroughly waked up, I have

[009-104]a good deal of trouble with them sometimes. They're apt to get stupid

[009-105]with tumbling over each other's heads. That's when they're fairly at it.

[009-106]However, the boat got to a desert island before noon next day.”

[009-107]“And what good will come of that?”

[009-108]“I don't know. I obeyed orders. Good bye.”

[009-109]“Oh! stay, North Wind, do stay!” cried Diamond, dismayed to see the

[009-110]windmill get slower and slower.

[009-111]“What is it, my dear child?” said North Wind, and the windmill began

[009-112]turning again so swiftly that Diamond could scarcely see it. “What a big

[009-113]voice you've got! and what a noise you do make with it? What is it you

[009-114]want? I have little to do, but that little must be done.”

[009-115]“I want you to take me to [[back-of-the-north-wind|the country at the back of the north wind]].”

[009-116]“That's not so easy,” said North Wind, and was silent for so long that

[009-117]Diamond thought she was gone indeed. But after he had quite given her

[009-118]up, the voice began again.

[009-119]“I almost wish old Herodotus had held his tongue about it. Much he knew

[009-120]of it!”

[009-121]“Why do you wish that, North Wind?”

[009-122]“Because then that clergyman would never have heard of it, and set you

[009-123]wanting to go. But we shall see. We shall see. You must go home now, my

[009-124]dear, for you don't seem very well, and I'll see what can be done for

[009-125]you. Don't wait for me. I've got to break a few of old Goody's toys;

[009-126]she's thinking too much of her new stock. Two or three will do. There!

[009-127]go now.”

[009-128]Diamond rose, quite sorry, and without a word left the shop, and went

[009-129]home.

[009-130]It soon appeared that his mother had been right about him, for that same

[009-131]afternoon his head began to ache very much, and he had to go to bed.

[009-132]He awoke in the middle of the night. The lattice window of his room had

[009-133]blown open, and the curtains of his little bed were swinging about in

[009-134]the wind.

[009-135]“If that should be North Wind now!” thought Diamond.

[009-136]But the next moment he heard some one closing the window, and his aunt

[009-137]came to his bedside. She put her hand on his face, and said--

[009-138]“How's your head, dear?”

[009-139]“Better, auntie, I think.”

[009-140]“Would you like something to drink?”

[009-141]“Oh, yes! I should, please.”

[009-142]So his aunt gave him some lemonade, for she had been used to nursing

[009-143]sick people, and Diamond felt very much refreshed, and laid his head

[009-144]down again to go very fast asleep, as he thought. And so he did, but

[009-145]only to come awake again, as a fresh burst of wind blew the lattice

[009-146]open a second time. The same moment he found himself in a cloud of North

[009-147]Wind's hair, with her beautiful face, set in it like a [[moon]], bending

[009-148]over him.

[009-149]“Quick, Diamond!” she said. “I have found such a chance!”

[009-150]“But I'm not well,” said Diamond.

[009-151]“I know that, but you will be better for a little fresh air. You shall

[009-152]have plenty of that.”

[009-153]“You want me to go, then?”

[009-154]“Yes, I do. It won't hurt you.”

[009-155]“Very well,” said Diamond; and getting out of the bed-clothes, he jumped

[009-156]into North Wind's arms.

[009-157]“We must make haste before your aunt comes,” said she, as she glided out

[009-158]of the open lattice and left it swinging.

[009-159]The moment Diamond felt her arms fold around him he began to feel

[009-160]better. It was a moonless night, and very dark, with glimpses of [[stars]]

[009-161]when the clouds parted.

[009-162]“I used to dash the waves about here,” said North Wind, “where cows and

[009-163]sheep are feeding now; but we shall soon get to them. There they are.”

[009-164]And Diamond, looking down, saw the white glimmer of breaking water far

[009-165]below him.

[009-166]“You see, Diamond,” said North Wind, “it is very difficult for me to

[009-167]get you to the back of the north wind, for that country lies in the very

[009-168]north itself, and of course I can't blow northwards.”

[009-169]“Why not?” asked Diamond.

[009-170]“You little silly!” said North Wind. “Don't you see that if I were to

[009-171]blow northwards I should be South Wind, and that is as much as to say

[009-172]that one person could be two persons?”

[009-173]“But how can you ever get home at all, then?”

[009-174]“You are quite right--that is my home, though I never get farther than

[009-175]the outer door. I sit on the doorstep, and hear the voices inside. I am

[009-176]nobody there, Diamond.”

[009-177]“I'm very sorry.”

[009-178]“Why?”

[009-179]“That you should be nobody.”

[009-180]“Oh, I don't mind it. Dear little man! you will be very glad some day

[009-181]to be nobody yourself. But you can't understand that now, and you had

[009-182]better not try; for if you do, you will be certain to go fancying some

[009-183]egregious nonsense, and making yourself miserable about it.”

[009-184]“Then I won't,” said Diamond.

[009-185]“There's a good boy. It will all come in good time.”

[009-186]“But you haven't told me how you get to the doorstep, you know.”

[009-187]“It is easy enough for me. I have only to consent to be nobody, and

[009-188]there I am. I draw into myself and there I am on the doorstep. But you

[009-189]can easily see, or you have less sense than I think, that to drag you,

[009-190]you heavy thing, along with me, would take centuries, and I could not

[009-191]give the time to it.”

[009-192]“Oh, I'm so sorry!” said Diamond.

[009-193]“What for now, pet?”

[009-194]“That I'm so heavy for you. I would be lighter if I could, but I don't

[009-195]know how.”

[009-196]“You silly darling! Why, I could toss you a hundred miles from me if I

[009-197]liked. It is only when I am going home that I shall find you heavy.”

[009-198]“Then you are going home with me?”

[009-199]“Of course. Did I not come to fetch you just for that?”

[009-200]“But all this time you must be going southwards.”

[009-201]“Yes. Of course I am.”

[009-202]“How can you be taking me northwards, then?”

[009-203]“A very sensible question. But you shall see. I will get rid of a few of

[009-204]these clouds--only they do come up so fast! It's like trying to blow a

[009-205]brook dry. There! What do you see now?”

[009-206]“I think I see a little boat, away there, down below.”

[009-207]“A little boat, indeed! Well! She's a yacht of two hundred tons; and the

[009-208]captain of it is a friend of mine; for he is a man of good sense, and

[009-209]can sail his craft well. I've helped him many a time when he little

[009-210]thought it. I've heard him grumbling at me, when I was doing the very

[009-211]best I could for him. Why, I've carried him eighty miles a day, again

[009-212]and again, right north.”

[009-213]“He must have dodged for that,” said Diamond, who had been watching the

[009-214]vessels, and had seen that they went other ways than the wind blew.

[009-215]“Of course he must. But don't you see, it was the best I could do? I

[009-216]couldn't be South Wind. And besides it gave him a share in the business.

[009-217]It is not good at all--mind that, Diamond--to do everything for those

[009-218]you love, and not give them a share in the doing. It's not kind. It's

[009-219]making too much of yourself, my child. If I had been South Wind, he

[009-220]would only have smoked his pipe all day, and made himself stupid.”

[009-221]“But how could he be a man of sense and grumble at you when you were

[009-222]doing your best for him?”

[009-223]“Oh! you must make allowances,” said North Wind, “or you will never do

[009-224]justice to anybody.--You do understand, then, that a captain may sail

[009-225]north----”

[009-226]“In spite of a north wind--yes,” supplemented Diamond.

[009-227]“Now, I do think you must be stupid, my dear” said North Wind. “Suppose

[009-228]the north wind did not blow where would he be then?”

[009-229]“Why then the south wind would carry him.”

[009-230]“So you think that when the north wind stops the south wind blows.

[009-231]Nonsense. If I didn't blow, the captain couldn't sail his eighty miles

[009-232]a day. No doubt South Wind would carry him faster, but South Wind is

[009-233]sitting on her doorstep then, and if I stopped there would be a dead

[009-234]calm. So you are all wrong to say he can sail north in spite of me; he

[009-235]sails north by my help, and my help alone. You see that, Diamond?”

[009-236]“Yes, I do, North Wind. I am stupid, but I don't want to be stupid.”

[009-237]“Good boy! I am going to blow you north in that little craft, one of the

[009-238]finest that ever sailed the sea. Here we are, right over it. I shall

[009-239]be blowing against you; you will be sailing against me; and all will be

[009-240]just as we want it. The captain won't get on so fast as he would like,

[009-241]but he will get on, and so shall we. I'm just going to put you on board.

[009-242]Do you see in front of the tiller--that thing the man is working, now to

[009-243]one side, now to the other--a round thing like the top of a drum?”

[009-244]“Yes,” said Diamond.

[009-245]“Below that is where they keep their spare sails, and some stores of

[009-246]that sort. I am going to blow that cover off. The same moment I will

[009-247]drop you on deck, and you must tumble in. Don't be afraid, it is of no

[009-248]depth, and you will fall on sail-cloth. You will find it nice and warm

[009-249]and dry-only dark; and you will know I am near you by every roll and

[009-250]pitch of the vessel. Coil yourself up and go to sleep. The yacht shall

[009-251]be my cradle and you shall be my baby.”

[009-252]“Thank you, dear North Wind. I am not a bit afraid,” said Diamond.

[009-253]In a moment they were on a level with the bulwarks, and North Wind sent

[009-254]the hatch of the after-store rattling away over the deck to leeward. The

[009-255]next, Diamond found himself in the dark, for he had tumbled through the

[009-256]hole as North Wind had told him, and the cover was replaced over his

[009-257]head. Away he went rolling to leeward, for the wind began all at once to

[009-258]blow hard. He heard the call of the captain, and the loud trampling of

[009-259]the men over his head, as they hauled at the main sheet to get the boom

[009-260]on board that they might take in a reef in the mainsail. Diamond felt

[009-261]about until he had found what seemed the most comfortable place, and

[009-262]there he snuggled down and lay.

[009-263]Hours after hours, a great many of them, went by; and still Diamond

[009-264]lay there. He never felt in the least tired or impatient, for a strange

[009-265]pleasure filled his heart. The straining of the masts, the creaking of

[009-266]the boom, the [[music|singing]] of the ropes, the banging of the blocks as they

[009-267]put the vessel about, all fell in with the roaring of the wind above,

[009-268]the surge of the waves past her sides, and the thud with which every now

[009-269]and then one would strike her; while through it all Diamond could hear

[009-270]the gurgling, rippling, talking flow of the water against her planks,

[009-271]as she slipped through it, lying now on this side, now on that--like a

[009-272]subdued air running through the grand [[song|music]] his North Wind was making

[009-274]the back of her doorstep.

[009-275]How long this lasted Diamond had no idea. He seemed to fall asleep

[009-276]sometimes, only through the sleep he heard the sounds going on. At

[009-277]length the weather seemed to get worse. The confusion and trampling of

[009-278]feet grew more frequent over his head; the vessel lay over more and

[009-279]more on her side, and went roaring through the waves, which banged and

[009-280]thumped at her as if in anger. All at once arose a terrible uproar. The

[009-281]hatch was blown off; a cold fierce wind swept in upon him; and a long

[009-282]arm came with it which laid hold of him and lifted him out. The same

[009-286]vessel, with two or three sails set, and towards it North Wind was

[009-289]after that I must do the best I can.”

[009-291]all snugly stowed away, and on and on they sped towards the north. At

[009-296]sea.

[009-298]“Neither or both, which you please. I can hardly tell which myself. If

[009-299]he is setting now, he will be rising the next moment.”

[009-307]the ship. North Wind seized Diamond, and with a single bound lighted on

[009-308]one of them--a huge thing, with sharp pinnacles and great clefts. The

[009-333]“It's very uncomfortable,” she answered; “but I don't mind it, for I

[009-334]shall come all right again before long. I thought I should be able to go

[009-336]Just go straight on, and you will come all right. You'll find me on the

[009-339]could still see her eyes shining through the blue. When he went closer,

[009-342]had not trusted her so thoroughly. So he sat still in the blue air of

[009-343]the cavern listening to the wash and ripple of the water all about the

[009-345]It was an excellent craft to go with the current, for there was twice as

[009-346]much of it below water as above. But a light south wind was blowing too,

[009-348]After a little while Diamond went out and sat on the edge of his
