## Overview

The Mews is the [[london|London]] lodging-house where [[diamond|Diamond]] lives with his parents during their period of urban [[suffering|poverty]]. Originally a stable building, it has been converted to cheap rented rooms occupied by working-class families. It is here that [[diamonds-father|Diamond's father]] builds him the hayloft bed, where the [[drunken-cabman|drunken cabman]] lives downstairs, and where the sound of the wind in the roof becomes indistinguishable from the voice of [[north-wind|North Wind]].

## Description

The mews is a converted stable block of the kind common in Victorian [[london|London]] — originally built to house horses and carriages for wealthier households, then repurposed as cheap housing as the neighborhood declined. [[diamond|Diamond]]'s family occupies rooms that once housed horses; the hayloft where [[diamond|Diamond]] [[dream|sleep]]s is a remnant of the building's original function. The building is crowded, noisy, and cold — the drunken cabman's room opens directly onto the stair, and sounds carry easily between the thin walls.

## Appearance and Character

The Mews is the novel's most intimate domestic setting, and MacDonald renders it through sensory impressions rather than architectural description. The dominant sound is wind — whistling through "the hole in the roof" (006-003), it is the medium through which [[diamond|Diamond]] first hears [[north-wind|North Wind]]'s voice. The hayloft bed that [[diamonds-father|Diamond's father]] constructs is both makeshift and magical: a child's refuge built from the materials of [[suffering|poverty]], transforming a drafty stable-loft into a threshold of visionary experience.

The mews is characterized by **proximity** — of bodies, of sounds, of lives. The thin walls mean that the drunken cabman's rages, [[diamond|Diamond]]'s parents' anxious conversations, and the neighing of horses in the stable below all interpenetrate. This lack of privacy is oppressive but also, in MacDonald's treatment, spiritually significant: [[diamond|Diamond]] cannot insulate himself from the suffering of others because it is literally audible through the floor. His ministry to the drunken cabman begins not with a decision but with unavoidable nearness.

The building's hybrid identity — part stable, part dwelling — mirrors the family's social position. They live where horses were once kept; [[diamonds-father|Diamond's father]], a cabman, works with horses below and [[dream|sleep]]s above them. The boundary between animal and human habitation is permeable, marking the family's [[suffering|poverty]] but also creating the conditions for [[diamond|Diamond]]'s unique spiritual ecology. His hayloft bed, "built for him" (001-023) by his father, is a gift of love that makes a sacred space from the most unpromising materials.

When the family eventually moves to [[the-country|the Country]], the mews is left behind without sentiment. MacDonald does not linger over the departure; the mews was never home in the emotional sense — it was a place of endurance. Its gift to the novel is that it was the place where [[diamond|Diamond]] learned to listen for the wind.

## Events

1. **[[diamond|Diamond]]'s bed** (Chapter I): [[diamonds-father|Diamond's father]] builds him a bed in the hayloft — a whimsical but practical arrangement that becomes the threshold for [[diamond|Diamond]]'s encounters with [[north-wind|North Wind]]. The hayloft bed, constructed with care from available materials, is the novel's first image of love made tangible.

2. **The wind in the roof** (Chapter I): [[diamond|Diamond]] hears the wind whistling through the roof-hole of the mews and converses with it. The mews' physical permeability — its inability to keep the elements out — becomes the condition for [[diamond|Diamond]]'s first numinous encounter.

3. **The drunken cabman** (Chapter XVIII): [[diamond|Diamond]] descends from his hayloft to find the drunken cabman in a room below, leading to the novel's most concentrated episode of redemptive ministry. The mews' thin walls and shared stairway make this encounter inevitable — [[diamond|Diamond]] cannot avoid his neighbor's suffering.

4. **Departure** (Chapter XXVIII): When [[mr-raymond|Mr. Raymond]] arranges the family's relocation, the mews is left behind. [[diamonds-mother|Diamond's mother]]'s relief — escaping the cold, the noise, the cramped quarters — is palpable, but the narrative does not linger. The mews has served its purpose.
