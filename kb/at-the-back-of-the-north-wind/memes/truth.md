## Definition

In *At the Back of [[north-wind|the North Wind]]*, truth operates on multiple levels simultaneously. At its simplest, it is the moral obligation to speak honestly; at its most profound, it is the correspondence between a person's being and the fundamental nature of reality. George MacDonald, a Christian minister as well as a novelist, embeds in the novel a theology of truth in which [[kindness|goodness]] and truth are inseparable — what is truly good cannot be "too good to be true" (030-145). [[diamond|Diamond]], the child protagonist, becomes the novel's primary vehicle for expressing this vision, not through intellectual argument but through his way of being in the world.

## In the Novel

### Truth as Childlike Quality

The narrator identifies [[diamond|Diamond]] early as "a true child" — one "given to" asking questions and believing answers (007-126). The adjective "true" here carries double weight: [[diamond|Diamond]] is genuinely a child (not prematurely adult) and he is aligned with truth (not self-deceived or deceptive). This linkage of childhood with truthfulness is central to MacDonald's moral epistemology: children, in his view, are closer to truth because they have not yet learned the adult arts of evasion and rationalisation.

### Diamond's Philosophy of Truth and Goodness

The novel's most concentrated philosophical exchange about truth occurs between [[diamond|Diamond]] and [[nanny|Nanny]] in Chapter XXX. When [[nanny|Nanny]] protests that something is "too good to be true" (030-144), [[diamond|Diamond]] replies with remarkable theological sophistication: "There are very few things good enough to be true" (030-145). He continues: "Too good to be true it can't be. Isn't true good? and isn't good true?" (030-146). This brief exchange crystallises MacDonald's Platonic-Christian conviction that [[kindness|goodness]] and truth are convertible — that the highest good is also the truest reality, and that scepticism about [[kindness|goodness]] is a failure of perception rather than a triumph of realism.

The passage is striking because it is spoken by a child to a girl only slightly older, in the context of ordinary conversation. MacDonald embeds his deepest theology not in sermons but in dialogue, and he places it in the mouth of a child-cab-d[[the-river|river]], not a learned theologian. This democratisation of truth is itself a theological statement: truth is accessible to the humble and the childlike.

### Truth and Dreams

The novel consistently interrogates the boundary between dream and reality, and truth is the criterion by which that boundary is negotiated. When [[nanny|Nanny]] says "[[dream|Dream]]s ain't true" (036-033), [[diamond|Diamond]] responds: "That one was true, [[nanny|Nanny]]. You were doing what you were told not to do? And isn't that true?" (036-034–035). [[dream|Dream]]-content, for [[diamond|Diamond]], can be true if it corresponds to moral or experiential reality, even if it does not correspond to physical fact. This is not relativism but a redefinition of "true" to include the moral and the imaginative — a move consistent with MacDonald's broader resistance to Victorian materialism.

### Genius and Truth

In one of the narrator's rare direct interventions, we are told that "Genius finds out truths, not tricks" (022-087). This aphorism, presented as a general truth, applies to [[diamond|Diamond]] himself: his genius — if the word can be used of a child — consists in his capacity to perceive what is genuinely the case about people and situations, without the obfuscations of social convention.

### Speaking Truth

The moral dimension of truth-telling appears throughout the novel. The princess in the "Little [[princess-daylight|Daylight]]" tale is praised because she and another character "both do what you are told and speak the truth" (028-399). Truthfulness is presented as a foundational virtue, linked to obedience and integrity — not as mere rule-following but as alignment of word with reality.

## Key Passages

> "a true child in this, that he was given to" (007-126)

The narrator's characterisation of [[diamond|Diamond]] — truth and childhood linked as essential qualities.

> "Genius finds out truths, not tricks." (022-087)

The novel's aphoristic statement on the nature of genius — truth is discovered, not manufactured.

> "There are very few things good enough to be true." (030-145)

[[diamond|Diamond]]'s theological-philosophical declaration — the starting point for his integration of truth and [[kindness|goodness]].

> "Too good to be true it can't be. Isn't true good? and isn't good true?" (030-146)

The novel's most concentrated philosophical argument, placed in the mouth of a child. Goodness and truth are one.

> "That one was true, Nanny." (036-034)

[[diamond|Diamond]]'s redefinition of dream-truth — moral and experiential correspondence, not mere physical factuality.

> "both do what you are told and speak the truth" (028-399)

Truth-telling as foundational virtue, paired with obedience.

## Truth and the Child Philosopher

MacDonald's decision to make a child the novel's primary vehicle for truth-claims is a deliberate narrative strategy. [[diamond|Diamond]] does not argue for his positions — he simply states them, with a [[trust|confidence]] that the narrative validates. This rhetorical strategy disarms the adult reader's instinct to counter-argue: one does not debate a child; one either dismisses or receives. By placing the novel's deepest theological claims in [[diamond|Diamond]]'s mouth, MacDonald asks the reader to receive rather than analyse — to adopt the child's posture of wonder rather than the adult's posture of scepticism. The novel's theory of truth is thus inseparable from its narrative technique.

## Related Characters

- **[[diamond]]**: The novel's philosopher of truth, whose simple statements carry the weight of theological conviction. His truthfulness is not intellectual but ontological — he is true, and therefore what he says is true.
- **[[nanny]]**: [[diamond|Diamond]]'s interlocutor in the novel's key truth dialogue — her scepticism ("too good to be true") prompts [[diamond|Diamond]]'s most significant philosophical statement.
