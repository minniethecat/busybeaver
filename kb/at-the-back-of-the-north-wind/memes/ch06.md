CHAPTER VI. OUT IN THE STORM

[006-001]THE hand felt its way up his arm, and, grasping it gently and strongly

[006-002]above the elbow, lifted [[diamond|Diamond]] from the bed. The moment he was through

[006-003]the hole in the roof, all the winds of [[heaven]] seemed to lay hold upon

[006-004]him, and buffet him hither and thither. His hair blew one way, his

[006-005][[darkness|night]]-gown another, his legs threatened to float from under him, and

[006-006]his head to grow dizzy with the swiftness of the invisible assailant.

[006-007]Cowering, he clung with the other hand to the huge hand which held his

[006-008]arm, and fear invaded his heart.

[006-009]“Oh, [[north-wind|North Wind]]!” he murmured, but the words vanished from his lips as

[006-010]he had seen the soap-bubbles that burst too soon vanish from the mouth

[006-011]of his pipe. The wind caught them, and they were nowhere. They couldn't

[006-012]get out at all, but were torn away and strangled. And yet North Wind

[006-013]heard them, and in her answer it seemed to Diamond that just because she

[006-014]was so big and could not help it, and just because her ear and her mouth

[006-015]must seem to him so dreadfully far away, she spoke to him more tenderly

[006-016]and graciously than ever before. Her voice was like the bass of a deep

[006-017]organ, without the groan in it; like the most delicate of violin tones

[006-018]without the wail in it; like the most glorious of trumpet-ejaculations

[006-019]without the defiance in it; like the sound of falling water without

[006-020]the clatter and clash in it: it was like all of them and neither

[006-021]of them--all of them without their faults, each of them without its

[006-022]peculiarity: after all, it was more like his mother's voice than

[006-023]anything else in the world.

[006-024]“Diamond, dear,” she said, “be a man. What is fearful to you is not the

[006-025]least fearful to me.”

[006-026]“But it can't hurt you,” murmured Diamond, “for you're it.”

[006-027]“Then if I'm it, and have you in my arms, how can it hurt you?”

[006-028]“Oh yes! I see,” whispered Diamond. “But it looks so dreadful, and it

[006-029]pushes me about so.”

[006-030]“Yes, it does, my dear. That is what it was sent for.”

[006-031]At the same moment, a peal of thunder which shook Diamond's heart

[006-032]against the sides of his bosom hurtled out of the heavens: I cannot

[006-033]say out of the sky, for there was no sky. Diamond had not seen the

[006-034]lightning, for he had been intent on finding the face of North Wind.

[006-035]Every moment the folds of her garment would sweep across his eyes and

[006-036]blind him, but between, he could just persuade himself that he saw great

[006-037]glories of woman's eyes looking down through rifts in the mountainous

[006-038]clouds over his head.

[006-039]He trembled so at the thunder, that his knees failed him, and he sunk

[006-040]down at North Wind's feet, and clasped her round the column of her

[006-041]ankle. She instantly stooped, lifted him from the roof--up--up into her

[006-042]bosom, and held him there, saying, as if to an inconsolable child--

[006-043]“Diamond, dear, this will never do.”

[006-044]“Oh yes, it will,” answered Diamond. “I am all right now--quite

[006-045]comfortable, I assure you, dear North Wind. If you will only let me stay

[006-046]here, I shall be all right indeed.”

[006-047]“But you will feel the wind here, Diamond.”

[006-048]“I don't mind that a bit, so long as I feel your arms through it,”

[006-049]answered Diamond, nestling closer to her grand bosom.

[006-050]“Brave boy!” returned North Wind, pressing him closer.

[006-051]“No,” said Diamond, “I don't see that. It's not courage at all, so long

[006-052]as I feel you there.”

[006-053]“But hadn't you better get into my hair? Then you would not feel the

[006-054]wind; you will here.”

[006-055]“Ah, but, dear North Wind, you don't know how nice it is to feel your

[006-056]arms about me. It is a thousand times better to have them and the wind

[006-057]together, than to have only your hair and the back of your neck and no

[006-058]wind at all.”

[006-059]“But it is surely more comfortable there?”

[006-060]“Well, perhaps; but I begin to think there are better things than being

[006-061]comfortable.”

[006-062]“Yes, indeed there are. Well, I will keep you in front of me. You will

[006-063]feel the wind, but not too much. I shall only want one arm to take care

[006-064]of you; the other will be quite enough to sink the ship.”

[006-065]“Oh, dear North Wind! how can you talk so?”

[006-066]“My dear boy, I never talk; I always mean what I say.”

[006-067]“Then you do mean to sink the ship with the other hand?”

[006-068]“Yes.”

[006-069]“It's not like you.”

[006-070]“How do you know that?”

[006-071]“Quite easily. Here you are taking care of a poor little boy with one

[006-072]arm, and there you are sinking a ship with the other. It can't be like

[006-073]you.”

[006-074]“Ah! but which is me? I can't be two mes, you know.”

[006-075]“No. Nobody can be two mes.”

[006-076]“Well, which me is me?”

[006-077]“Now I must think. There looks to be two.”

[006-078]“Yes. That's the very point.--You can't be knowing the thing you don't

[006-079]know, can you?”

[006-080]“No.”

[006-081]“Which me do you know?”

[006-082]“The kindest, goodest, best me in the world,” answered Diamond, clinging

[006-083]to North Wind.

[006-084]“Why am I good to you?”

[006-085]“I don't know.”

[006-086]“Have you ever done anything for me?”

[006-087]“No.”

[006-088]“Then I must be good to you because I choose to be good to you.”

[006-089]“Yes.”

[006-090]“Why should I choose?”

[006-091]“Because--because--because you like.”

[006-092]“Why should I like to be good to you?”

[006-093]“I don't know, except it be because it's good to be good to me.”

[006-094]“That's just it; I am good to you because I like to be good.”

[006-095]“Then why shouldn't you be good to other people as well as to me?”

[006-096]“That's just what I don't know. Why shouldn't I?”

[006-097]“I don't know either. Then why shouldn't you?”

[006-098]“Because I am.”

[006-099]“There it is again,” said Diamond. “I don't see that you are. It looks

[006-100]quite the other thing.”

[006-101]“Well, but listen to me, Diamond. You know the one me, you say, and that

[006-102]is good.”

[006-103]“Yes.”

[006-104]“Do you know the other me as well?”

[006-105]“No. I can't. I shouldn't like to.”

[006-106]“There it is. You don't know the other me. You are sure of one of them?”

[006-107]“Yes.”

[006-108]“And you are sure there can't be two mes?”

[006-109]“Yes.”

[006-110]“Then the me you don't know must be the same as the me you do

[006-111]know,--else there would be two mes?”

[006-112]“Yes.”

[006-113]“Then the other me you don't know must be as kind as the me you do

[006-114]know?”

[006-115]“Yes.”

[006-116]“Besides, I tell you that it is so, only it doesn't look like it. That I

[006-117]confess freely. Have you anything more to object?”

[006-118]“No, no, dear North Wind; I am quite satisfied.”

[006-119]“Then I will tell you something you might object. You might say that the

[006-120]me you know is like the other me, and that I am cruel all through.”

[006-121]“I know that can't be, because you are so kind.”

[006-122]“But that kindness might be only a pretence for the sake of being more

[006-123]cruel afterwards.”

[006-124]Diamond clung to her tighter than ever, crying--

[006-125]“No, no, dear North Wind; I can't believe that. I don't believe it. I

[006-126]won't believe it. That would kill me. I love you, and you must love me,

[006-127]else how did I come to love you? How could you know how to put on such a

[006-128]beautiful face if you did not love me and the rest? No. You may sink

[006-129]as many ships as you like, and I won't say another word. I can't say I

[006-130]shall like to see it, you know.”

[006-131]“That's quite another thing,” said North Wind; and as she spoke she gave

[006-132]one spring from the roof of the hay-loft, and rushed up into the clouds,

[006-133]with Diamond on her left arm close to her heart. And as if the clouds

[006-134]knew she had come, they burst into a fresh jubilation of thunderous

[006-135]light. For a few moments, Diamond seemed to be borne up through the

[006-136]depths of an ocean of dazzling flame; the next, the winds were writhing

[006-137]around him like a storm of serpents. For they were in the midst of

[006-138]the clouds and mists, and they of course took the shapes of the wind,

[006-139]eddying and wreathing and whirling and shooting and dashing about like

[006-140]grey and black water, so that it was as if the wind itself had taken

[006-141]shape, and he saw the grey and black wind tossing and raving most madly

[006-142]all about him. Now it blinded him by smiting him upon the eyes; now it

[006-143]deafened him by bellowing in his ears; for even when the thunder came he

[006-144]knew now that it was the billows of the great ocean of the air dashing

[006-145]against each other in their haste to fill the hollow scooped out by the

[006-146]lightning; now it took his breath quite away by sucking it from his body

[006-147]with the speed of its rush. But he did not mind it. He only gasped first

[006-148]and then laughed, for the arm of North Wind was about him, and he was

[006-149]leaning against her bosom. It is quite impossible for me to describe

[006-150]what he saw. Did you ever watch a great wave shoot into a winding

[006-151]passage amongst rocks? If you ever did, you would see that the water

[006-152]rushed every way at once, some of it even turning back and opposing

[006-153]the rest; greater confusion you might see nowhere except in a crowd of

[006-154]frightened people. Well, the wind was like that, except that it went

[006-155]much faster, and therefore was much wilder, and twisted and shot and

[006-156]curled and dodged and clashed and raved ten times more madly than

[006-157]anything else in creation except human passions. Diamond saw the threads

[006-158]of the lady's hair streaking it all. In parts indeed he could not tell

[006-159]which was hair and which was black storm and vapour. It seemed sometimes

[006-160]that all the great billows of mist-muddy wind were woven out of the

[006-161]crossing lines of North Wind's infinite hair, sweeping in endless

[006-162]intertwistings. And Diamond felt as the wind seized on his hair, which

[006-163]his mother kept rather long, as if he too was a part of the storm, and

[006-164]some of its life went out from him. But so sheltered was he by North

[006-165]Wind's arm and bosom that only at times, in the fiercer onslaught of

[006-166]some curl-billowed eddy, did he recognise for a moment how wild was the

[006-167]storm in which he was carried, nestling in its very core and formative

[006-168]centre.

## Section 9

[006-169]It seemed to Diamond likewise that they were motionless in this centre,

[006-170]and that all the confusion and fighting went on around them. Flash after

[006-171]flash illuminated the fierce chaos, revealing in varied yellow and blue

[006-172]and grey and dusky red the vapourous contention; peal after peal of

[006-173]thunder tore the infinite waste; but it seemed to Diamond that North

[006-174]Wind and he were motionless, all but the hair. It was not so. They were

[006-175]sweeping with the speed of the wind itself towards the sea.
