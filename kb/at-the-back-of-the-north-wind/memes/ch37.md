CHAPTER XXXVII. ONCE MORE

[037-001]THE next [[darkness|night]] [[diamond|Diamond]] was seated by his open window, with his head on

[037-002]his hand, rather tired, but so eagerly waiting for the promised visit

[037-003]that he was afraid he could not [[dream|sleep]]. But he started suddenly, and

[037-004]found that he had been already asleep. He rose, and looking out of the

[037-005]window saw something white against his beech-tree. It was [[north-wind|North Wind]].

[037-006]She was holding by one hand to a top branch. Her hair and her garments

[037-007]went floating away behind her over the tree, whose top was swaying about

[037-008]while the others were still.

[037-009]“Are you ready, Diamond?” she asked.

[037-010]“Yes,” answered Diamond, “quite ready.”

[037-011]In a moment she was at the window, and her arms came in and took him.

[037-012]She sailed away so swiftly that he could at first mark nothing but the

[037-013]speed with which the clouds above and the dim earth below went rushing

[037-014]past. But soon he began to see that the sky was very lovely, with

[037-015]mottled clouds all about the [[moon]], on which she threw faint colours like

[037-016]those of mother-of-pearl, or an opal. The night was warm, and in the

[037-017]lady's arms he did not feel the wind which down below was making waves

[037-018]in the ripe corn, and ripples on the rivers and lakes. At length they

[037-019]descended on the side of an open earthy hill, just where, from beneath a

[037-020]stone, a spring came bubbling out.

[037-021]“I am going to take you along this little brook,” said North Wind. “I am

[037-022]not wanted for anything else to-night, so I can give you a treat.”

[037-023]She stooped over the stream and holding Diamond down close to the

[037-024]surface of it, glided along level with its flow as it ran down the hill.

[037-025]And the [[music|song]] of the brook came up into Diamond's ears, and grew and

[037-026]grew and changed with every turn. It seemed to Diamond to be [[song|singing]] the

[037-027]story of its life to him. And so it was. It began with a musical tinkle

[037-028]which changed to a babble and then to a gentle rushing. Sometimes its

[037-029]song would almost cease, and then break out again, tinkle, babble, and

[037-030]rush, all at once. At the bottom of the hill they came to a small [[the-river|river]],

[037-031]into which the brook flowed with a muffled but merry sound. Along the

[037-032]surface of the river, darkly clear below them in the [[light|moonlight]], they

[037-033]floated; now, where it widened out into a little lake, they would hover

[037-034]for a moment over a bed of water-lilies, and watch them swing about,

[037-035]folded in sleep, as the water on which they leaned swayed in the

[037-036]presence of North Wind; and now they would watch the fishes asleep among

[037-037]their roots below. Sometimes she would hold Diamond over a deep hollow

[037-038]curving into the bank, that he might look far into the cool stillness.

[037-039]Sometimes she would leave the river and sweep across a clover-field. The

[037-040]bees were all at home, and the clover was asleep. Then she would return

[037-041]and follow the river. It grew wider and wider as it went. Now the armies

[037-042]of wheat and of oats would hang over its rush from the opposite banks;

[037-043]now the willows would dip low branches in its still waters; and now it

[037-044]would lead them through stately trees and grassy banks into a lovely

[037-045]garden, where the roses and lilies were asleep, the tender flowers

[037-046]quite folded up, and only a few wide-awake and sending out their life in

[037-047]sweet, strong odours. Wider and wider grew the stream, until they came

[037-048]upon boats lying along its banks, which rocked a little in the flutter

[037-049]of North Wind's garments. Then came houses on the banks, each standing

[037-050]in a lovely lawn, with grand trees; and in parts the river was so high

[037-051]that some of the grass and the roots of some of the trees were under

[037-052]water, and Diamond, as they glided through between the stems, could see

[037-053]the grass at the bottom of the water. Then they would leave the river

[037-054]and float about and over the houses, one after another--beautiful rich

[037-055]houses, which, like fine trees, had taken centuries to grow. There was

[037-056]scarcely a light to be seen, and not a movement to be heard: all the

[037-057]people in them lay fast asleep.

[037-058]“What a lot of dreams they must be dreaming!” said Diamond.

[037-059]“Yes,” returned North Wind. “They can't surely be all lies--can they?”

[037-060]“I should think it depends a little on who dreams them,” suggested

[037-061]Diamond.

[037-062]“Yes,” said North Wind. “The people who think lies, and do lies, are

[037-063]very likely to dream lies. But the people who love what is true will

[037-064]surely now and then dream true things. But then something depends on

[037-065]whether the dreams are home-grown, or whether the seed of them is blown

[037-066]over somebody else's garden-wall. Ah! there's some one awake in this

[037-067]house!”

[037-068]They were floating past a window in which a light was burning. Diamond

[037-069]heard a moan, and looked up anxiously in North Wind's face.

[037-070]“It's a lady,” said North Wind. “She can't sleep for [[suffering|pain]].”

[037-071]“Couldn't you do something for her?” said Diamond.

[037-072]“No, I can't. But you could.”

[037-073]“What could I do?”

[037-074]“Sing a little song to her.”

[037-075]“She wouldn't hear me.”

[037-076]“I will take you in, and then she will hear you.”

[037-077]“But that would be rude, wouldn't it? You can go where you please, of

[037-078]course, but I should have no business in her room.”

[037-079]“You may trust me, Diamond. I shall take as good care of the lady as of

[037-080]you. The window is open. Come.”

[037-081]By a shaded lamp, a lady was seated in a white wrapper, trying to read,

[037-082]but moaning every minute. North Wind floated behind her chair,

[037-083]set Diamond down, and told him to sing something. He was a little

[037-084]frightened, but he thought a while, and then sang:--

[037-085]The sun is gone down,

[037-086]And the moon's in the sky;

[037-087]But the sun will come up,

[037-088]And the moon be laid by.

[037-089]The flower is asleep

[037-090]But it is not dead;

[037-091]When the morning shines,

[037-092]It will lift its head.

[037-093]When winter comes,

[037-094]It will die--no, no;

[037-095]It will only hide

[037-096]From the frost and the snow.

[037-097]Sure is the summer,

[037-098]Sure is the sun;

[037-099]The night and the winter

[037-100]Are shadows that run.

[037-101]The lady never lifted her eyes from her book, or her head from her hand.

[037-102]As soon as Diamond had finished, North Wind lifted him and carried him

[037-103]away.

[037-104]“Didn't the lady hear me?” asked Diamond when they were once more

[037-105]floating down the river.

[037-106]“Oh, yes, she heard you,” answered North Wind.

[037-107]“Was she frightened then?”

[037-108]“Oh, no.”

[037-109]“Why didn't she look to see who it was?”

[037-110]“She didn't know you were there.”

[037-111]“How could she hear me then?”

[037-112]“She didn't hear you with her ears.”

[037-113]“What did she hear me with?”

[037-114]“With her heart.”

[037-115]“Where did she think the words came from?”

[037-116]“She thought they came out of the book she was reading. She will search

[037-117]all through it to-morrow to find them, and won't be able to understand

[037-118]it at all.”

[037-119]“Oh, what fun!” said Diamond. “What will she do?”

[037-120]“I can tell you what she won't do: she'll never forget the meaning of

[037-121]them; and she'll never be able to remember the words of them.”

[037-122]“If she sees them in [[mr-raymond|Mr. Raymond]]'s book, it will puzzle her, won't it?”

[037-123]“Yes, that it will. She will never be able to understand it.”

[037-124]“Until she gets to the back of the north wind,” suggested Diamond.

[037-125]“Until she gets to the back of the north wind,” assented the lady.

[037-126]“Oh!” cried Diamond, “I know now where we are. Oh! do let me go into the

[037-127]old garden, and into mother's room, and Diamond's stall. I wonder if the

[037-128]hole is at the back of my bed still. I should like to stay there all the

[037-129]rest of the night. It won't take you long to get home from here, will

[037-130]it, North Wind?”

[037-131]“No,” she answered; “you shall stay as long as you like.”

[037-132]“Oh, how jolly,” cried Diamond, as North Wind sailed over the house with

[037-133]him, and set him down on the lawn at the back.

[037-134]Diamond ran about the lawn for a little while in the moonlight. He found

[037-135]part of it cut up into flower-beds, and the little summer-house with the

[037-136]coloured glass and the great elm-tree gone. He did not like this, and

[037-137]ran into the stable. There were no horses there at all. He ran upstairs.

[037-138]The rooms were empty. The only thing left that he cared about was the

[037-139]hole in the wall where his little bed had stood; and that was not enough

[037-140]to make him wish to stop. He ran down the stair again, and out upon the

[037-141]lawn. There he threw himself down and began to cry. It was all so dreary

[037-142]and lost!

[037-143]“I thought I liked the place so much,” said Diamond to himself, “but I

[037-144]find I don't care about it. I suppose it's only the people in it that

[037-145]make you like a place, and when they're gone, it's dead, and you don't

[037-146]care a bit about it. North Wind told me I might stop as long as I liked,

[037-147]and I've stopped longer already. North Wind!” he cried aloud, turning

[037-148]his face towards the sky.

[037-149]The moon was under a cloud, and all was looking dull and dismal. A

[037-150]star shot from the sky, and fell in the grass beside him. The moment it

[037-151]lighted, there stood North Wind.

[037-152]“Oh!” cried Diamond, joyfully, “were you the shooting star?”

[037-153]“Yes, my child.”

[037-154]“Did you hear me call you then?”

[037-155]“Yes.”

[037-156]“So high up as that?”

[037-157]“Yes; I heard you quite well.”

[037-158]“Do take me home.”

[037-159]“Have you had enough of your old home already?”

[037-160]“Yes, more than enough. It isn't a home at all now.”

[037-161]“I thought that would be it,” said North Wind. “Everything, dreaming and

[037-162]all, has got a soul in it, or else it's worth nothing, and we don't care

[037-163]a bit about it. Some of our thoughts are worth nothing, because they've

[037-164]got no soul in them. The brain puts them into the mind, not the mind

[037-165]into the brain.”

[037-166]“But how can you know about that, North Wind? You haven't got a body.”

[037-167]“If I hadn't you wouldn't know anything about me. No creature can know

[037-168]another without the help of a body. But I don't care to talk about that.

[037-169]It is time for you to go home.”

[037-170]So saying, North Wind lifted Diamond and bore him away.
