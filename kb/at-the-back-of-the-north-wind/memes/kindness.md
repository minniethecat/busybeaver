## Definition

Kindness in the novel is not passive benevolence but active intervention. [[diamond|Diamond]] does not merely feel sorry for [[nanny|Nanny]] — he gives her money, arranges her rescue, and risks [[sal|Sal]]'s anger. [[mr-raymond|Mr. Raymond]] does not merely pity the poor — he provides homes, employment, and hospital care. Kindness is the novel's translation of theology into ethics: if God is [[love|love]], then the human response is active kindness to those in need. The [[north-wind|North Wind]] herself articulates this principle: "I am only just. All kindness is but justice. We owe it" (007-139).

## In the Novel

Kindness structures the novel's most important plot movements and appears as a chain reaction — one act of kindness triggers another, creating a network of moral interdependence:

1. **[[diamond|Diamond]] helps [[nanny|Nanny]]** (Chapters IV, XXI): [[diamond|Diamond]] insists on helping the "little girl" at the crossing: "Yes; to help the little girl" (004-112). When [[nanny|Nanny]] falls ill, he enters [[sal|Sal]]'s cellar despite the danger. His kindness is physical, costly, and effective.

2. **[[diamonds-father|Diamond's father]] returns the purse** (Chapter XVII): The honest return of a lost purse is an act of kindness to strangers: "[[diamonds-father|Diamond's father]] gave the ladies a ticket with his name on it" (017-149). This act triggers the plot's resolution, suggesting that kindness — even in small, everyday forms — has providential consequences.

3. **[[mr-raymond|Mr. Raymond]]'s patronage** (Chapters XXIX–XXXIV): [[mr-raymond|Mr. Raymond]]'s kindness is institutional — he provides housing, employment, and medical care. It extends the reach of individual kindness into systemic change. He does not merely feel sympathy; he acts.

4. **[[jim|Jim]] and the cab-horse**: [[diamond|Diamond]] shows kindness to [[jim|Jim]], a poor boy, by sharing what little he has. The narrator notes that "he pushed him aside with rough kindness" (026-006) — kindness that is physically rough but morally tender, a recurring MacDonald pattern where sincere love may not always look gentle.

5. **[[nanny|Nanny]]'s transformative gratitude**: Having received kindness, [[nanny|Nanny]] is herself transformed. The narrator observes that "she had never known kindness" (027-078) before [[diamond|Diamond]] entered her life. Her subsequent devotion to him is the natural fruit of kindness received.

MacDonald's novel insists that kindness is not a luxury of the wealthy — [[diamond|Diamond]], the poorest character materially, is the richest in kindness. Conversely, the novel warns that the loss of kindness is spiritual death: "when we lose it, we lose even the kindness which alone can" (032-008) sustain human community.

## Key Passages

> "Yes; to help the little girl," said Diamond firmly. (004-112)

[[diamond|Diamond]]'s simple declaration encapsulates the novel's ethics — kindness requires no justification beyond the presence of need.

> "I am only just. All kindness is but justice. We owe it." (007-139)

The [[north-wind|North Wind]]'s startling equation of kindness with justice reframes charity not as supererogatory virtue but as moral obligation.

> "Diamond's father gave the ladies a ticket with his name on it." (017-149)

A small honest act — returning what was lost — becomes the hinge on which the entire plot turns.

> She had never known kindness until Diamond came. (027-078)

The novel's most radical claim: kindness is so rare in [[nanny|Nanny]]'s world that its appearance is transformative.

## Analysis

Kindness in *At the Back of [[north-wind|the North Wind]]* is MacDonald's answer to the Victorian "condition of England" question — the problem of urban [[suffering|poverty]] that dominated the social novel. Unlike Dickens, who portrays systemic injustice as requiring systemic reform, MacDonald locates the solution in individual moral action. This is not naivety — [[mr-raymond|Mr. Raymond]]'s institutional kindness is explicitly systemic — but it is grounded in the conviction that no system can replace the individual's choice to notice and respond to [[suffering|suffering]].

Theologically, kindness is MacDonald's incarnation principle. If God is [[love|love]], and love is an active force, then the human task is to embody that love in concrete acts. [[diamond|Diamond]] is not Christlike because he preaches — he rarely does — but because he acts. His kindness to [[nanny|Nanny]] is the novel's version of the Good Samaritan: crossing class boundaries, risking personal cost, expecting nothing in return.

MacDonald's emphasis on kindness as justice (007-139) is particularly radical. In a Victorian context where charity was often framed as the voluntary benevolence of the rich toward the deserving poor, MacDonald insists that kindness is owed — it is a debt of justice, not a gift of generosity. This anticipates later Christian socialist thought and gives the novel's ethics a structural dimension that belies its fairy-tale surface.
