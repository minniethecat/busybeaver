# Heaven

**Heaven** in *At the Back of [[north-wind|the North Wind]]* serves as both a physical sky and a spiritual destination, blending MacDonald's interest in the natural world with his theological imagination. The "heavens" appear as the realm through which [[diamond|Diamond]] travels with [[north-wind|the North Wind]], but the word also carries the weight of its religious meaning as the ultimate home of [[kindness|goodness]], and — in [[diamond|Diamond]]'s childlike theology — a place where even beloved animals find their rest.

## The Physical Heavens

The heavens are frequently described in cosmic terms as the expanse through which [[diamond|Diamond]] flies. The "blue of the wintry heavens" (003-076) provides the backdrop for his aerial journeys. The winds themselves are described as "all the winds of heaven" (006-003), and [[north-wind|the North Wind]]'s power is felt as something that "hurtled out of the heavens" (006-032). These descriptions give the sky a sublime, almost overwhelming quality — the heavens are vast, powerful, and not entirely tame. The narrator too observes the sky in contemplative moments: "a half-[[moon]] high in the heavens" (036-014) sets the scene for [[diamond|Diamond]]'s climbing of the beech-tree, linking the celestial to the everyday world of the child.

## Heaven as Spiritual Destination

The concept of heaven as an afterlife destination appears in character conversations. [[diamond|Diamond]] asks simply, "people can get to heaven without carriages — or coachmen either" (009-020), revealing his childlike trust that the journey to heaven does not require earthly means. Another character asks, "Don't you think he will go to heaven, sir?" (029-063), expressing concern for a loved one's fate. These questions ground the story's spiritual dimension in the everyday concerns of its characters. Even casual speech reflects this cultural assumption: characters say "No, thank Heaven!" (013-053, 017-196) as a natural utterance, as if heaven were a presence close at hand rather than a distant theological abstraction.

## Diamond's Theology of Heaven

[[diamond|Diamond]] holds a distinctively childlike and generous vision of heaven. When he declares that [[north-wind|North Wind]] "knows well enough there are horses in heaven for [[angel]]s to ride upon, as well as other animals, lions and eagles and bulls" (032-145–032-146), he extends the promise of paradise beyond the human to the animal kingdom. This is not mere childish fancy — it reflects MacDonald's own theological conviction that creation in its fullness is destined for redemption. For [[diamond|Diamond]], the horses he loves — [[old-diamond|Old Diamond]] and [[ruby|Ruby]] — are not excluded from heaven's promise. His heaven is a place of reunion, where the bonds of [[love|affection]] formed on earth are not broken but fulfilled.

## The Kingdom of Heaven

The "kingdom of heaven" is invoked as a source of transformative light: it "had shone upon her" (027-063), suggesting that heavenly grace can touch and change a person. The "airs of heaven played round her tongue" (010-055), associating speech with celestial influence. In these passages, heaven is not merely a distant destination but an active, illuminating presence in mortal life. For MacDonald, the kingdom of heaven is not postponed to an afterlife — it breaks into the present world through acts of [[kindness]], through the speech of the innocent, and through the [[beauty]] of the natural order that [[diamond|Diamond]] experiences in his flights with [[north-wind|the North Wind]].
