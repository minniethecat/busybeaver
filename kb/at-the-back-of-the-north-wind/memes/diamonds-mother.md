## Overview

Diamond's mother is a working-class wife and mother whose life is shaped by the economic precarity of Victorian [[london|London]]. She is introduced early in the novel as a domestic presence: "his mother had the name of [[diamond|Diamond]] at the back of the north wind, as his mother very well knew" (001-011). This early mention — the mother's knowledge of [[north-wind|North Wind]] — hints at a connection to the supernatural that is never developed into explicit vision, but which informs her patient acceptance of her son's peculiar nature.

She appears throughout the novel as a figure of domestic stability — anxious, sometimes overwhelmed, but fundamentally loving and practical. She was named "after a favourite horse" (001-012), a detail that links her, through naming, to the stable world her husband inhabits. She endures [[suffering|poverty]] alongside her husband, worries over [[diamond|Diamond]]'s strange [[dream|sleep]] and dream-talk, and eventually finds security through the family's connection with [[mr-raymond|Mr. Raymond]].

## Appearance and Character

Diamond's mother is less vividly drawn than her husband, reflecting her more conventional domestic role. MacDonald sketches her through domestic details: "Sometimes, when his mother had undressed him" (001-017), she would find [[diamond|Diamond]] staring at the loose board in the wall — the hole that [[north-wind|North Wind]] used to enter. The mother who undresses her child and notices his fixations is a mother who pays attention.

She is practical — it is she who "had once more pasted up [[north-wind|North Wind]]'s picture" (003-123), the picture of a tombstone that serves as a *memento mori* in the family's home. The verb "pasted" and the adverbial "once more" suggest a repetitive, housekeeping duty performed without fanfare. She manages the household's small resources carefully: "[[diamond|Diamond]]'s shoes were not good, and his mother had not quite saved enough money to get him a new pair" (003-097).

When [[suffering|hardship]] comes, her response is immediate and emotional: "'Oh dear!' said Diamond's mother, with a deep sigh, 'it's a sad story'" (013-015). Her anxiety is not weakness but the realistic response of a woman who knows exactly how thin the margin of survival is.

She is cautious by nature — a trait that complements her husband's bolder [[trust|faith]]. When [[mr-raymond|Mr. Raymond]] offers the family advantageous terms, she expresses hesitation: "'I don't know so much that way, husband,' said Diamond's mother; 'but there would be'" (029-139) grounds for concern. This is not ingratitude but prudence, born of experience. The novel validates both her caution and her husband's trust.

Her relationship with [[diamond|Diamond]] is characterized by love mingled with incomprehension. She "watched him very carefully — going into [[the-hay-loft|the loft]]" (003-094) after his strange [[darkness|night]] with [[north-wind|North Wind]]. She does not understand her son's visionary experiences, but she does not dismiss them either. When [[diamond|Diamond]] says something unusual, "his mother" (002-131) listens with the same attentiveness she gives to his physical needs.

## Role in the Story

Diamond's mother anchors the novel's domestic realism through several phases:

**1. The keeper of the household (Chapters I–V).** In the early chapters, she is the center of the family's domestic life. She pastes up pictures, manages what little money there is, and watches over [[diamond|Diamond]] with anxious care. When [[diamond|Diamond]] returns from his first [[darkness|night]] journey with [[north-wind|North Wind]], "for a week his mother watched him very carefully" (003-094) — the vigilance of a mother who suspects something has happened but cannot name it.

**2. The bearer of bad news (Chapter XIII).** When the family's economic collapse becomes clear, it is Diamond's mother who carries the emotional weight: "it's a sad story" (013-015). Her sigh captures the accumulated anxiety of a woman who has managed [[suffering|poverty]]'s daily arithmetic and now faces its catastrophic form.

**3. The voice of caution (Chapter XXIX).** When [[mr-raymond|Mr. Raymond]]'s patronage offers an escape from [[suffering|poverty]], Diamond's mother is the one who asks practical questions. Her "I don't know so much that way" (029-139) represents the prudent hesitation of someone who has learned not to trust sudden good for[[music|tune]]. The narrative does not rebuke this caution; instead, the subsequent resolution of the plot proves both her caution and her husband's [[trust|faith]] to have been appropriate.

**4. Peace in the country (Chapters XXX–XXXIII).** In [[the-cottage|the country cottage]] provided by [[mr-raymond|Mr. Raymond]], Diamond's mother finds her proper sphere. "Diamond's mother was very considerate of her" (031-003) — the pronoun "her" refers to [[nanny|Nanny]], now a member of the household — and the mother's kindness to the orphan girl reveals a generosity that [[suffering|poverty]] had constrained but not extinguished. The country chapters show Diamond's mother in a state of quiet contentment, her domestic competence finally matched by adequate resources.

## Themes and Symbolism

Diamond's mother represents the **ordinary [[kindness|goodness]]** that MacDonald values as highly as visionary spirituality. She does not travel with [[north-wind|North Wind]]; she does not see [[angel]]s. But she pastes up the picture, feeds her family, watches over her son, and endures. In MacDonald's theology, such quotidian [[trust|faith]]fulness is no less sacred than [[diamond|Diamond]]'s ecstasies.

The picture she repeatedly pastes up — [[north-wind|North Wind]]'s picture, which is actually a tombstone — is a subtle symbol of her role. She does not understand the picture's significance; [[diamond|Diamond]] later reveals that the hole behind it was [[north-wind|North Wind]]'s entrance. But the mother's instinct to cover the hole — to paste over the aperture through which the supernatural intrudes — is the instinct of ordinary [[home|domesticity]], which MacDonald treats not as a failure of vision but as a different and equally valid mode of being in the world.

Her caution about [[mr-raymond|Mr. Raymond]]'s offer reflects the hard-won wisdom of the poor. She has learned that good for[[music|tune]] is not to be trusted too quickly. The novel's happy resolution — [[the-cottage|the country cottage]], the secure employment — vindicates her caution while rewarding her husband's [[trust|faith]], suggesting that MacDonald sees these domestic virtues as complementary rather than opposed.
