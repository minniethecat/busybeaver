CHAPTER XVII. DIAMOND GOES ON

[017-001]DIAMOND became a great favourite with all the men about the mews. Some

[017-002]may think it was not the best place in the world for him to be brought

[017-003]up in; but it must have been, for there he was. At first, he heard a

[017-004]good many rough and bad words; but he did not like them, and so they did

[017-005]him little harm. He did not know in the least what they meant, but there

[017-006]was something in the very sound of them, and in the tone of voice in

[017-007]which they were said, which [[diamond|Diamond]] felt to be ugly. So they did not

[017-008]even stick to him, not to say get inside him. He never took any notice

[017-009]of them, and his face shone pure and good in the middle of them, like

[017-010]a primrose in a hailstorm. At first, because his face was so quiet

[017-011]and sweet, with a smile always either awake or asleep in his eyes, and

[017-012]because he never heeded their ugly words and rough jokes, they said he

[017-013]wasn't all there, meaning that he was half an idiot, whereas he was a

[017-014]great deal more there than they had the sense to see. And before long

[017-015]the bad words found themselves ashamed to come out of the men's mouths

[017-016]when Diamond was near. The one would nudge the other to remind him that

[017-017]the boy was within hearing, and the words choked themselves before they

[017-018]got any farther. When they talked to him nicely he had always a good

[017-019]answer, sometimes a smart one, ready, and that helped much to make them

[017-020]change their minds about him.

[017-021]One day Jack gave him a curry-comb and a brush to try his hand upon

[017-022]old Diamond's coat. He used them so deftly, so gently, and yet so

[017-023]thoroughly, as far as he could reach, that the man could not help

[017-024]admiring him.

[017-025]“You must make haste and, grow” he said. “It won't do to have a horse's

[017-026]belly clean and his back dirty, you know.”

[017-027]“Give me a leg,” said Diamond, and in a moment he was on the old horse's

[017-028]back with the comb and brush. He sat on his withers, and reaching

[017-029]forward as he ate his hay, he curried and he brushed, first at one side

[017-030]of his neck, and then at the other. When that was done he asked for a

[017-031]dressing-comb, and combed his mane thoroughly. Then he pushed himself on

[017-032]to his back, and did his shoulders as far down as he could reach. Then

[017-033]he sat on his croup, and did his back and sides; then he turned around

[017-034]like a monkey, and attacked his hind-quarters, and combed his tail. This

[017-035]last was not so easy to manage, for he had to lift it up, and every now

[017-036]and then old Diamond would whisk it out of his hands, and once he sent

[017-037]the comb flying out of the stable door, to the great amusement of the

[017-038]men. But Jack fetched it again, and Diamond began once more, and did not

[017-039]leave off until he had done the whole business fairly well, if not in

[017-040]a first-rate, experienced fashion. All the time the old horse went

[017-041]on eating his hay, and, but with an occasional whisk of his tail when

[017-042]Diamond tickled or scratched him, took no notice of the proceeding.

[017-043]But that was all a pretence, for he knew very well who it was that

[017-044]was perched on his back, and rubbing away at him with the comb and the

[017-045]brush. So he was quite pleased and proud, and perhaps said to himself

[017-046]something like this--

[017-047]“I'm a stupid old horse, who can't brush his own coat; but there's my

[017-048]young godson on my back, cleaning me like an [[angel]].”

[017-049]I won't vouch for what the old horse was thinking, for it is very

[017-050]difficult to find out what any old horse is thinking.

[017-051]“Oh dear!” said Diamond when he had done, “I'm so tired!”

[017-052]And he laid himself down at full length on old Diamond's back.

[017-053]By this time all the men in the stable were gathered about the two

[017-054]Diamonds, and all much amused. One of them lifted him down, and from

[017-055]that time he was a greater favourite than before. And if ever there was

[017-056]a boy who had a chance of being a prodigy at cab-driving, Diamond was

[017-057]that boy, for the strife came to be who should have him out with him on

[017-058]the box.

[017-059]His mother, however, was a little shy of the company for him, and

[017-060]besides she could not always spare him. Also his father liked to have

[017-061]him himself when he could; so that he was more desired than enjoyed

[017-062]among the cabmen.

[017-063]But one way and another he did learn to drive all sorts of horses, and

[017-064]to drive them well, and that through the most crowded streets in [[london|London]]

[017-065]City. Of course there was the man always on the box-seat beside him, but

[017-066]before long there was seldom the least occasion to take the reins

[017-067]from out of his hands. For one thing he never got frightened, and

[017-068]consequently was never in too great a hurry. Yet when the moment came

[017-069]for doing something sharp, he was always ready for it. I must once more

[017-070]remind my readers that he had been to the back of the north wind.

[017-071]One day, which was neither washing-day, nor cleaning-day nor

[017-072]marketing-day, nor Saturday, nor Monday--upon which consequently Diamond

[017-073]could be spared from the baby--his father took him on his own cab. After

[017-074]a stray job or two by the way, they drew up in the row upon the stand

[017-075]between Cockspur Street and Pall Mall. They waited a long time, but

[017-076]nobody seemed to want to be carried anywhere. By and by ladies would be

[017-077]going home from the Academy exhibition, and then there would be a chance

[017-078]of a job.

[017-079]“Though, to be sure,” said [[diamonds-father|Diamond's father]]--with what truth I cannot

[017-080]say, but he believed what he said--“some ladies is very hard, and keeps

[017-081]you to the bare sixpence a mile, when every one knows that ain't enough

[017-082]to keep a family and a cab upon. To be sure it's the law; but mayhap

[017-083]they may get more law than they like some day themselves.”

[017-084]As it was very hot, Diamond's father got down to have a glass of beer

[017-085]himself, and give another to the old waterman. He left Diamond on the

[017-086]box.

[017-087]A sudden noise got up, and Diamond looked round to see what was the

[017-088]matter.

[017-089]There was a crossing near the cab-stand, where a girl was sweeping. Some

[017-090]rough young imps had picked a quarrel with her, and were now hauling

[017-091]at her broom to get it away from her. But as they did not pull all

[017-092]together, she was holding it against them, scolding and entreating

[017-093]alternately.

[017-094]Diamond was off his box in a moment, and running to the help of the

[017-095]girl. He got hold of the broom at her end and pulled along with her. But

[017-096]the boys proceeded to rougher measures, and one of them hit Diamond on

[017-097]the nose, and made it bleed; and as he could not let go the broom to

[017-098]mind his nose, he was soon a dreadful figure. But presently his father

[017-099]came back, and missing Diamond, looked about. He had to look twice,

[017-100]however, before he could be sure that that was his boy in the middle

[017-101]of the tumult. He rushed in, and sent the assailants flying in all

[017-102]directions. The girl thanked Diamond, and began sweeping as if nothing

[017-103]had happened, while his father led him away. With the help of old Tom,

[017-104]the waterman, he was soon washed into decency, and his father set him on

[017-105]the box again, perfectly satisfied with the account he gave of the cause

[017-106]of his being in a fray.

[017-107]“I couldn't let them behave so to a poor girl--could I, father?” he

[017-108]said.

[017-109]“Certainly not, Diamond,” said his father, quite pleased, for Diamond's

[017-110]father was a gentleman.

[017-111]A moment after, up came the girl, running, with her broom over her

[017-112]shoulder, and calling, “Cab, there! cab!”

[017-113]Diamond's father turned instantly, for he was the foremost in the rank,

[017-114]and followed the girl. One or two other passing cabs heard the cry, and

[017-115]made for the place, but the girl had taken care not to call till she was

[017-116]near enough to give her friends the first chance. When they reached

[017-117]the curbstone--who should it be waiting for the cab but Mrs. and Miss

[017-118]Coleman! They did not look at [[drunken-cabman|the cabman]], however. The girl opened the

[017-119]door for them; they gave her the address, and a penny; she told the

[017-120]cabman, and away they drove.

[017-121]When they reached the house, Diamond's father got down and rang the

[017-122]bell. As he opened the door of the cab, he touched his hat as he had

[017-123]been wont to do. The ladies both stared for a moment, and then exclaimed

[017-124]together:

[017-125]“Why, Joseph! can it be you?”

[017-126]“Yes, ma'am; yes, miss,” answered he, again touching his hat, with all

[017-127]the respect he could possibly put into the action. “It's a lucky day

[017-128]which I see you once more upon it.”

[017-129]“Who would have thought it?” said [[mrs-coleman|Mrs. Coleman]]. “It's changed times for

[017-130]both of us, Joseph, and it's not very often we can have a cab even; but

[017-131]you see my daughter is still very poorly, and she can't bear the motion

[017-132]of the omnibuses. Indeed we meant to walk a bit first before we took a

[017-133]cab, but just at the corner, for as hot as the sun was, a cold wind came

[017-134]down the street, and I saw that [[miss-coleman|Miss Coleman]] must not face it. But to

[017-135]think we should have fallen upon you, of all the cabmen in London! I

[017-136]didn't know you had got a cab.”

[017-137]“Well, you see, ma'am, I had a chance of buying the old horse, and I

[017-138]couldn't resist him. There he is, looking at you, ma'am. Nobody knows

[017-139]the sense in that head of his.”

[017-140]The two ladies went near to pat the horse, and then they noticed Diamond

[017-141]on the box.

[017-142]“Why, you've got both Diamonds with you,” said Miss Coleman. “How do you

[017-143]do, Diamond?”

[017-144]Diamond lifted his cap, and answered politely.

[017-145]“He'll be fit to drive himself before long,” said his father, proudly.

[017-146]“The old horse is a-teaching of him.”

[017-147]“Well, he must come and see us, now you've found us out. Where do you

[017-148]live?”

[017-149]Diamond's father gave the ladies a ticket with his name and address

[017-150]printed on it; and then Mrs. Coleman took out her purse, saying:

[017-151]“And what's your fare, Joseph?”

[017-152]“No, thank you, ma'am,” said Joseph. “It was your own old horse as took

[017-153]you; and me you paid long ago.”

[017-154]He jumped on his box before she could say another word, and with a

[017-155]parting salute drove off, leaving them on the pavement, with the maid

[017-156]holding the door for them.

[017-157]It was a long time now since Diamond had seen [[north-wind|North Wind]], or even

[017-158]thought much about her. And as his father drove along, he was thinking

[017-159]not about her, but about the crossing-sweeper, and was wondering what

[017-160]made him feel as if he knew her quite well, when he could not remember

[017-161]anything of her. But a picture arose in his mind of a little girl

[017-162]running before the wind and dragging her broom after her; and from that,

[017-163]by degrees, he recalled the whole adventure of the [[darkness|night]] when he got

[017-164]down from North Wind's back in a London street. But he could not quite

[017-165]satisfy himself whether the whole affair was not a dream which he had

[017-166]dreamed when he was a very little boy. Only he had been to the back of

[017-167]the north wind since--there could be no doubt of that; for when he woke

[017-168]every morning, he always knew that he had been there again. And as he

[017-169]thought and thought, he recalled another thing that had happened that

[017-170]morning, which, although it seemed a mere accident, might have something

[017-171]to do with what had happened since. His father had intended going on the

[017-172]stand at King's Cross that morning, and had turned into Gray's Inn Lane

[017-173]to drive there, when they found the way blocked up, and upon inquiry

[017-174]were informed that a stack of chimneys had been blown down in the night,

[017-175]and had fallen across the road. They were just clearing the rubbish

[017-176]away. Diamond's father turned, and made for Charing Cross.

[017-177]That night the father and mother had a great deal to talk about.

[017-178]“Poor things!” said the mother. “it's worse for them than it is for us.

[017-179]You see they've been used to such grand things, and for them to come

[017-180]down to a little poky house like that--it breaks my heart to think of

[017-181]it.”

[017-182]“I don't know” said Diamond thoughtfully, “whether Mrs. Coleman had

[017-183]bells on her toes.”

[017-184]“What do you mean, child?” said his mother.

[017-185]“She had rings on her fingers, anyhow,” returned Diamond.

[017-186]“Of course she had, as any lady would. What has that to do with it?”

[017-187]“When we were down at [[sandwich|Sandwich]],” said Diamond, “you said you would have

[017-188]to part with your mother's ring, now we were poor.”

[017-189]“Bless the child; he forgets nothing,” said his mother. “Really,

[017-190]Diamond, a body would need to mind what they say to you.”

[017-191]“Why?” said Diamond. “I only think about it.”

[017-192]“That's just why,” said the mother.

[017-193]“Why is that why?” persisted Diamond, for he had not yet learned that

[017-194]grown-up people are not often so much grown up that they never talk like

[017-195]children--and spoilt ones too.

[017-196]“Mrs. Coleman is none so poor as all that yet. No, thank Heaven! she's

[017-197]not come to that.”

[017-198]“Is it a great disgrace to be poor?” asked Diamond, because of the tone

[017-199]in which his mother had spoken.

[017-200]But his mother, whether conscience-stricken I do not know hurried him

[017-201]away to bed, where after various attempts to understand her, resumed and

[017-202]resumed again in spite of invading [[dream|sleep]], he was conquered at last, and

[017-203]gave in, murmuring over and over to himself, “Why is why?” but getting

[017-204]no answer to the question.
