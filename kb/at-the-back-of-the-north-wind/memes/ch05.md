CHAPTER V. THE SUMMER-HOUSE

[005-001]DIAMOND said nothing to his mother about his adventures. He had half a

[005-002]notion that [[north-wind|North Wind]] was a friend of his mother, and that, if she did

[005-003]not know all about it, at least she did not mind his going anywhere with

[005-004]the lady of the wind. At the same time he doubted whether he might not

[005-005]appear to be telling stories if he told all, especially as he could

[005-006]hardly believe it himself when he thought about it in the middle of the

[005-007]day, although when the twilight was once half-way on to [[darkness|night]] he had no

[005-008]doubt about it, at least for the first few days after he had been with

[005-009]her. The girl that swept the crossing had certainly refused to believe

[005-010]him. Besides, he felt sure that North Wind would tell him if he ought to

[005-011]speak.

[005-012]It was some time before he saw the lady of the wind again. Indeed

[005-013]nothing remarkable took place in [[diamond|Diamond]]'s history until the following

[005-014]week. This was what happened then. Diamond the horse wanted new shoes,

[005-015]and [[diamonds-father|Diamond's father]] took him out of the stable, and was just getting on

[005-016]his back to ride him to the forge, when he saw his little boy standing

[005-017]by the pump, and looking at him wistfully. Then the coachman took his

[005-018]foot out of the stirrup, left his hold of the mane and bridle, came

[005-019]across to his boy, lifted him up, and setting him on the horse's back,

[005-020]told him to sit up like a man. He then led away both Diamonds together.

[005-021]The boy atop felt not a little tremulous as the great muscles that

[005-022]lifted the legs of the horse knotted and relaxed against his legs, and

[005-023]he cowered towards the withers, grasping with his hands the bit of mane

[005-024]worn short by the collar; but when his father looked back at him,

[005-025]saying once more, “Sit up, Diamond,” he let the mane go and sat up,

[005-026]notwithstanding that the horse, thinking, I suppose, that his master

[005-027]had said to him, “Come up, Diamond,” stepped out faster. For both the

[005-028]Diamonds were just grandly obedient. And Diamond soon found that, as he

[005-029]was obedient to his father, so the horse was obedient to him. For he had

[005-030]not ridden far before he found courage to reach forward and catch hold

[005-031]of the bridle, and when his father, whose hand was upon it, felt the boy

[005-032]pull it towards him, he looked up and smiled, and, well pleased, let go

[005-033]his hold, and left Diamond to guide Diamond; and the boy soon found that

[005-034]he could do so perfectly. It was a grand thing to be able to guide a

[005-035]great beast like that. And another discovery he made was that, in order

[005-036]to guide the horse, he had in a measure to obey the horse first. If he

[005-037]did not yield his body to the motions of the horse's body, he could not

[005-038]guide him; he must fall off.

[005-039]The blacksmith lived at some distance, deeper into [[london|London]]. As they

[005-040]crossed the angle of a square, Diamond, who was now quite comfortable

[005-041]on his living throne, was glancing this way and that in a gentle pride,

[005-042]when he saw a girl sweeping a crossing scuddingly before a lady. The

[005-043]lady was his father's mistress, [[mrs-coleman|Mrs. Coleman]], and the little girl was

[005-044]she for whose sake he had got off North Wind's back. He drew Diamond's

[005-045]bridle in eager anxiety to see whether her outstretched hand would

[005-046]gather a penny from Mrs. Coleman. But she had given one at the last

[005-047]crossing, and the hand returned only to grasp its broom. Diamond could

[005-048]not bear it. He had a penny in his pocket, a gift of the same lady the

[005-049]day before, and he tumbled off his horse to give it to the girl. He

[005-050]tumbled off, I say, for he did tumble when he reached the ground. But he

[005-051]got up in an instant, and ran, searching his pocket as he ran. She

[005-052]made him a pretty courtesy when he offered his treasure, but with a

[005-053]bewildered stare. She thought first: “Then he was on the back of the

[005-054]North Wind after all!” but, looking up at the sound of the horse's feet

[005-055]on the paved crossing, she changed her idea, saying to herself, “North

[005-056]Wind is his father's horse! That's the secret of it! Why couldn't he say

[005-057]so?” And she had a mind to refuse the penny. But his smile put it all

[005-058]right, and she not only took his penny but put it in her mouth with a

[005-059]“Thank you, mister. Did they wollop you then?”

[005-060]“Oh no!” answered Diamond. “They never wollops me.”

[005-061]“Lor!” said the little girl, and was speechless.

[005-062]Meantime his father, looking up, and seeing the horse's back bare,

[005-063]suffered a pang of awful [[fear|dread]], but the next moment catching sight of

[005-064]him, took him up and put him on, saying--

[005-065]“Don't get off again, Diamond. The horse might have put his foot on

[005-066]you.”

[005-067]“No, father,” answered the boy, and rode on in majestic safety.

[005-068]The summer drew near, warm and splendid. [[miss-coleman|Miss Coleman]] was a little

[005-069]better in health, and sat a good deal in the garden. One day she saw

[005-070]Diamond peeping through the shrubbery, and called him. He talked to her

[005-071]so frankly that she often sent for him after that, and by degrees it

[005-072]came about that he had leave to run in the garden as he pleased. He

[005-073]never touched any of the flowers or blossoms, for he was not like some

[005-074]boys who cannot enjoy a thing without pulling it to pieces, and so

[005-075]preventing every one from enjoying it after them.

[005-076]A week even makes such a long time in a child's life, that Diamond had

[005-077]begun once more to feel as if North Wind were a dream of some far-off

[005-078]year.

[005-079]One hot evening, he had been sitting with the young mistress, as they

[005-080]called her, in a little summer-house at the bottom of the lawn--a

[005-081]wonderful thing for [[beauty]], the boy thought, for a little window in the

[005-082]side of it was made of coloured glass. It grew dusky, and the lady began

[005-083]to feel chill, and went in, leaving the boy in the summer-house. He sat

[005-084]there gazing out at a bed of tulips, which, although they had closed for

[005-085]the night, could not go quite asleep for the wind that kept waving

[005-086]them about. All at once he saw a great bumble-bee fly out of one of the

[005-087]tulips.

[005-088]“There! that is something done,” said a voice--a gentle, merry, childish

[005-089]voice, but so tiny. “At last it was. I thought he would have had to stay

[005-090]there all night, poor fellow! I did.”

[005-091]Diamond could not tell whether the voice was near or far away, it was so

[005-092]small and yet so clear. He had never seen a fairy, but he had heard of

[005-093]such, and he began to look all about for one. And there was the tiniest

[005-094]creature sliding down the stem of the tulip!

[005-095]“Are you the fairy that herds the bees?” he asked, going out of the

[005-096]summer-house, and down on his knees on the green shore of the tulip-bed.

[005-097]“I'm not a fairy,” answered the little creature.

[005-098]“How do you know that?”

[005-099]“It would become you better to ask how you are to know it.”

[005-100]“You've just told me.”

[005-101]“Yes. But what's the use of knowing a thing only because you're told

[005-102]it?”

[005-103]“Well, how am I to know you are not a fairy? You do look very like one.”

[005-104]“In the first place, fairies are much bigger than you see me.”

[005-105]“Oh!” said Diamond reflectively; “I thought they were very little.”

[005-106]“But they might be tremendously bigger than I am, and yet not very big.

[005-107]Why, I could be six times the size I am, and not be very huge. Besides,

[005-108]a fairy can't grow big and little at will, though the nursery-tales do

[005-109]say so: they don't know better. You stupid Diamond! have you never seen

[005-110]me before?”

[005-111]And, as she spoke, a moan of wind bent the tulips almost to the ground,

[005-112]and the creature laid her hand on Diamond's shoulder. In a moment he

[005-113]knew that it was North Wind.

[005-114]“I am very stupid,” he said; “but I never saw you so small before, not

[005-115]even when you were nursing the primrose.”

[005-116]“Must you see me every size that can be measured before you know me,

[005-117]Diamond?”

[005-118]“But how could I think it was you taking care of a great stupid

[005-119]bumble-bee?”

[005-120]“The more stupid he was the more need he had to be taken care of. What

[005-121]with sucking honey and trying to open the door, he was nearly dated; and

[005-122]when it opened in the morning to let the sun see the tulip's heart, what

[005-123]would the sun have thought to find such a stupid thing lying there--with

[005-124]wings too?”

[005-125]“But how do you have time to look after bees?”

[005-126]“I don't look after bees. I had this one to look after. It was hard

[005-127]work, though.”

[005-128]“Hard work! Why, you could blow a chimney down, or--or a boy's cap off,”

[005-129]said Diamond.

[005-130]“Both are easier than to blow a tulip open. But I scarcely know the

[005-131]difference between hard and easy. I am always able for what I have to

[005-132]do. When I see my work, I just rush at it--and it is done. But I mustn't

[005-133]chatter. I have got to sink a ship to-night.”

[005-134]“Sink a ship! What! with men in it?”

[005-135]“Yes, and women too.”

[005-136]“How dreadful! I wish you wouldn't talk so.”

[005-137]“It is rather dreadful. But it is my work. I must do it.”

[005-138]“I hope you won't ask me to go with you.”

[005-139]“No, I won't ask you. But you must come for all that.”

[005-140]“I won't then.”

[005-141]“Won't you?” And North Wind grew a tall lady, and looked him in the

[005-142]eyes, and Diamond said--

[005-143]“Please take me. You cannot be cruel.”

[005-144]“No; I could not be cruel if I would. I can do nothing cruel, although I

[005-145]often do what looks like cruel to those who do not know what I really am

[005-146]doing. The people they say I drown, I only carry away to--to--to--well,

[005-147]the back of the North Wind--that is what they used to call it long ago,

[005-148]only I never saw the place.”

[005-149]“How can you carry them there if you never saw it?”

[005-150]“I know the way.”

[005-151]“But how is it you never saw it?”

[005-152]“Because it is behind me.”

[005-153]“But you can look round.”

[005-154]“Not far enough to see my own back. No; I always look before me. In

[005-155]fact, I grow quite blind and deaf when I try to see my back. I only mind

[005-156]my work.”

[005-157]“But how does it be your work?”

[005-158]“Ah, that I can't tell you. I only know it is, because when I do it I

[005-159]feel all right, and when I don't I feel all wrong. East Wind says--only

[005-160]one does not exactly know how much to believe of what she says, for she

[005-161]is very naughty sometimes--she says it is all managed by a baby; but

[005-162]whether she is good or naughty when she says that, I don't know. I just

[005-163]stick to my work. It is all one to me to let a bee out of a tulip, or to

[005-164]sweep the cobwebs from the sky. You would like to go with me to-night?”

[005-165]“I don't want to see a ship sunk.”

[005-166]“But suppose I had to take you?”

[005-167]“Why, then, of course I must go.”

[005-168]“There's a good Diamond.--I think I had better be growing a bit. Only

[005-169]you must go to bed first. I can't take you till you're in bed. That's

[005-170]the law about the children. So I had better go and do something else

[005-171]first.”

[005-172]“Very well, North Wind,” said Diamond. “What are you going to do first,

[005-173]if you please?”

[005-174]“I think I may tell you. Jump up on the top of the wall, there.”

[005-175]“I can't.”

[005-176]“Ah! and I can't help you--you haven't been to bed yet, you see. Come

[005-177]out to the road with me, just in front of the coach-house, and I will

[005-178]show you.”

[005-179]North Wind grew very small indeed, so small that she could not have

[005-180]blown the dust off a dusty miller, as the Scotch children call a yellow

[005-181]auricula. Diamond could not even see the blades of grass move as she

[005-182]flitted along by his foot. They left the lawn, went out by the wicket

[005-183]in the-coach-house gates, and then crossed the road to the low wall that

[005-184]separated it from the [[the-river|river]].

[005-185]“You can get up on this wall, Diamond,” said North Wind.

[005-186]“Yes; but my mother has forbidden me.”

[005-187]“Then don't,” said North Wind.

[005-188]“But I can see over,” said Diamond.

[005-189]“Ah! to be sure. I can't.”

[005-190]So saying, North Wind gave a little bound, and stood on the top of the

[005-191]wall. She was just about the height a dragon-fly would be, if it stood

[005-192]on end.

[005-193]“You darling!” said Diamond, seeing what a lovely little toy-woman she

[005-194]was.

[005-195]“Don't be impertinent, Master Diamond,” said North Wind. “If there's one

[005-196]thing makes me more angry than another, it is the way you humans judge

[005-197]things by their size. I am quite as respectable now as I shall be six

[005-198]hours after this, when I take an East Indiaman by the royals, twist her

[005-199]round, and push her under. You have no right to address me in such a

[005-200]fashion.”

[005-201]But as she spoke, the tiny face wore the smile of a great, grand woman.

[005-202]She was only having her own beautiful fun out of Diamond, and true

[005-203]woman's fun never hurts.

[005-204]“But look there!” she resumed. “Do you see a boat with one man in it--a

[005-205]green and white boat?”

[005-206]“Yes; quite well.”

[005-207]“That's a poet.”

[005-208]“I thought you said it was a bo-at.”

[005-209]“Stupid pet! Don't you know what a poet is?”

[005-210]“Why, a thing to sail on the water in.”

[005-211]“Well, perhaps you're not so far wrong. Some poets do carry people over

[005-212]the sea. But I have no business to talk so much. The man is a poet.”

[005-213]“The boat is a boat,” said Diamond.

[005-214]“Can't you spell?” asked North Wind.

[005-215]“Not very well.”

[005-216]“So I see. A poet is not a bo-at, as you call it. A poet is a man who is

[005-217]glad of something, and tries to make other people glad of it too.”

[005-218]“Ah! now I know. Like the man in the sweety-shop.”

[005-219]“Not very. But I see it is no use. I wasn't sent to tell you, and so I

[005-220]can't tell you. I must be off. Only first just look at the man.”

[005-221]“He's not much of a rower” said Diamond--“paddling first with one fin

[005-222]and then with the other.”

[005-223]“Now look here!” said North Wind.

[005-224]And she flashed like a dragon-fly across the water, whose surface

[005-225]rippled and puckered as she passed. The next moment the man in the boat

[005-226]glanced about him, and bent to his oars. The boat flew over the rippling

[005-227]water. Man and boat and river were awake. The same instant almost, North

[005-228]Wind perched again upon the river wall.

[005-229]“How did you do that?” asked Diamond.

[005-230]“I blew in his face,” answered North Wind. “I don't see how that could

[005-231]do it,” said Diamond. “I daresay not. And therefore you will say you

[005-232]don't believe it could.”

[005-233]“No, no, dear North Wind. I know you too well not to believe you.”

[005-234]“Well, I blew in his face, and that woke him up.”

[005-235]“But what was the good of it?”

[005-236]“Why! don't you see? Look at him--how he is pulling. I blew the mist out

[005-237]of him.”

[005-238]“How was that?”

[005-239]“That is just what I cannot tell you.”

[005-240]“But you did it.”

[005-241]“Yes. I have to do ten thousand things without being able to tell how.”

[005-242]“I don't like that,” said Diamond.

[005-243]He was staring after the boat. Hearing no answer, he looked down to the

[005-244]wall.

[005-245]North Wind was gone. Away across the river went a long ripple--what

[005-246]sailors call a cat's paw. The man in the boat was putting up a sail. The

[005-247][[moon]] was coming to herself on the edge of a great cloud, and the sail

[005-248]began to shine white. Diamond rubbed his eyes, and wondered what it was

[005-249]all about. Things seemed going on around him, and all to understand

[005-250]each other, but he could make nothing of it. So he put his hands in his

[005-251]pockets, and went in to have his tea. The night was very hot, for the

[005-252]wind had fallen again.

[005-253]“You don't seem very well to-night, Diamond,” said his mother.

[005-254]“I am quite well, mother,” returned Diamond, who was only puzzled.

[005-255]“I think you had better go to bed,” she added.

[005-256]“Very well, mother,” he answered.

[005-257]He stopped for one moment to look out of the window. Above the moon the

[005-258]clouds were going different ways. Somehow or other this troubled him,

[005-259]but, notwithstanding, he was soon fast asleep.

[005-260]He woke in the middle of the night and the darkness. A terrible noise

[005-261]was rumbling overhead, like the rolling beat of great drums echoing

[005-262]through a brazen vault. The roof of [[the-hay-loft|the loft]] in which he lay had no

[005-263]ceiling; only the tiles were between him and the sky. For a while he

[005-264]could not come quite awake, for the noise kept beating him down, so that

[005-265]his heart was troubled and fluttered painfully. A second peal of thunder

[005-266]burst over his head, and almost choked him with fear. Nor did he recover

[005-267]until the great blast that followed, having torn some tiles off the

[005-268]roof, sent a spout of wind down into his bed and over his face, which

[005-269]brought him wide awake, and gave him back his courage. The same moment

[005-270]he heard a mighty yet musical voice calling him.

[005-271]“Come up, Diamond,” it said. “It's all ready. I'm waiting for you.”

[005-272]He looked out of the bed, and saw a gigantic, powerful, but most lovely

[005-273]arm--with a hand whose fingers were nothing the less ladylike that they

[005-274]could have strangled a boa-constrictor, or choked a tigress off its

[005-275]prey--stretched down through a big hole in the roof. Without a moment's

[005-276]hesitation he reached out his tiny one, and laid it in the grand palm

[005-277]before him.
