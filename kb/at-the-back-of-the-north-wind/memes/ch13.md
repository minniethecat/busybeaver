CHAPTER XIII. THE SEASIDE

[013-001]DIAMOND and his mother sat down upon the edge of the rough grass that

[013-002]bordered the sand. The sun was just far enough past its highest not to

[013-003]shine in their eyes when they looked eastward. A sweet little wind blew

[013-004]on their left side, and comforted the mother without letting her know

[013-005]what it was that comforted her. Away before them stretched the sparkling

[013-006]waters of [[the-sea|the ocean]], every wave of which flashed out its own [[joy|delight]]

[013-007]back in the face of the great sun, which looked down from the stillness

[013-008]of its blue house with glorious silent face upon its flashing children.

[013-009]On each hand the shore rounded outwards, forming a little bay. There

[013-010]were no white cliffs here, as further north and south, and the place was

[013-011]rather dreary, but the sky got at them so much the better. Not a house,

[013-012]not a creature was within sight. Dry sand was about their feet, and

[013-013]under them thin wiry grass, that just managed to grow out of the

[013-014][[suffering|poverty]]-stricken shore.

[013-015]“Oh dear!” said [[diamonds-mother|Diamond's mother]], with a deep sigh, “it's a sad world!”

[013-016]“Is it?” said [[diamond|Diamond]]. “I didn't know.”

[013-017]“How should you know, child? You've been too well taken care of, I

[013-018]trust.”

[013-019]“Oh yes, I have,” returned Diamond. “I'm sorry! I thought you were taken

[013-020]care of too. I thought my father took care of you. I will ask him about

[013-021]it. I think he must have forgotten.”

[013-022]“Dear boy!” said his mother, “your father's the best man in the world.”

[013-023]“So I thought!” returned Diamond with triumph. “I was sure of it!--Well,

[013-024]doesn't he take very good care of you?”

[013-025]“Yes, yes, he does,” answered his mother, bursting into tears. “But

[013-026]who's to take care of him? And how is he to take care of us if he's got

[013-027]nothing to eat himself?”

[013-028]“Oh dear!” said Diamond with a gasp; “hasn't he got anything to eat? Oh!

[013-029]I must go home to him.”

[013-030]“No, no, child. He's not come to that yet. But what's to become of us, I

[013-031]don't know.”

[013-032]“Are you very hungry, mother? There's the basket. I thought you put

[013-033]something to eat in it.”

[013-034]“O you darling stupid! I didn't say I was hungry,” returned his mother,

[013-035]smiling through her tears.

[013-036]“Then I don't understand you at all,” said Diamond. “Do tell me what's

[013-037]the matter.”

[013-038]“There are people in the world who have nothing to eat, Diamond.”

[013-039]“Then I suppose they don't stop in it any longer. They--they--what you

[013-040]call--die--don't they?”

[013-041]“Yes, they do. How would you like that?”

[013-042]“I don't know. I never tried. But I suppose they go where they get

[013-043]something to eat.”

[013-044]“Like enough they don't want it,” said his mother, petulantly.

[013-045]“That's all right then,” said Diamond, thinking I daresay more than he

[013-046]chose to put in words.

[013-047]“Is it though? Poor boy! how little you know about things! [[mr-coleman|Mr. Coleman]]'s

[013-048]lost all his money, and your father has nothing to do, and we shall have

[013-049]nothing to eat by and by.”

[013-050]“Are you sure, mother?”

[013-051]“Sure of what?”

[013-052]“Sure that we shall have nothing to eat.”

[013-053]“No, thank Heaven! I'm not sure of it. I hope not.”

[013-054]“Then I can't understand it, mother. There's a piece of gingerbread in

[013-055]the basket, I know.”

[013-056]“O you little bird! You have no more sense than a sparrow that picks

[013-057]what it wants, and never thinks of the winter and the frost and, the

[013-058]snow.”

[013-059]“Ah--yes--I see. But the birds get through the winter, don't they?”

[013-060]“Some of them fall dead on the ground.”

[013-061]“They must die some time. They wouldn't like to be birds always. Would

[013-062]you, mother?”

[013-063]“What a child it is!” thought his mother, but she said nothing.

[013-064]“Oh! now I remember,” Diamond went on. “Father told me that day I went

[013-065]to Epping Forest with him, that the rose-bushes, and the may-bushes, and

[013-066]the holly-bushes were the bird's barns, for there were the hips, and the

[013-067]haws, and the holly-berries, all ready for the winter.”

[013-068]“Yes; that's all very true. So you see the birds are provided for. But

[013-069]there are no such barns for you and me, Diamond.”

[013-070]“Ain't there?”

[013-071]“No. We've got to work for our bread.”

[013-072]“Then let's go and work,” said Diamond, getting up.

[013-073]“It's no use. We've not got anything to do.”

[013-074]“Then let's wait.”

[013-075]“Then we shall starve.”

[013-076]“No. There's the basket. Do you know, mother, I think I shall call that

[013-077]basket the barn.”

[013-078]“It's not a very big one. And when it's empty--where are we then?”

[013-079]“At auntie's cupboard,” returned Diamond promptly.

[013-080]“But we can't eat auntie's things all up and leave her to starve.”

[013-081]“No, no. We'll go back to father before that. He'll have found a

[013-082]cupboard somewhere by that time.”

[013-083]“How do you know that?”

[013-084]“I don't know it. But I haven't got even a cupboard, and I've always had

[013-085]plenty to eat. I've heard you say I had too much, sometimes.”

[013-086]“But I tell you that's because I've had a cupboard for you, child.”

[013-087]“And when yours was empty, auntie opened hers.”

[013-088]“But that can't go on.”

[013-089]“How do you know? I think there must be a big cupboard somewhere, out of

[013-090]which the little cupboards are filled, you know, mother.”

[013-091]“Well, I wish I could find the door of that cupboard,” said his mother.

[013-092]But the same moment she stopped, and was silent for a good while. I

[013-093]cannot tell whether Diamond knew what she was thinking, but I think I

[013-094]know. She had heard something at church the day before, which came back

[013-095]upon her--something like this, that she hadn't to eat for tomorrow as

[013-096]well as for to-day; and that what was not wanted couldn't be missed.

[013-097]So, instead of saying anything more, she stretched out her hand for the

[013-098]basket, and she and Diamond had their dinner.

[013-099]And Diamond did enjoy it. For the drive and the fresh air had made him

[013-100]quite hungry; and he did not, like his mother, trouble himself about

[013-101]what they should dine off that day week. The fact was he had lived so

[013-102]long without any food at all at the back of the north wind, that he knew

[013-103]quite well that food was not essential to existence; that in fact, under

[013-104]certain circumstances, people could live without it well enough.

[013-105]His mother did not speak much during their dinner. After it was over she

[013-106]helped him to walk about a little, but he was not able for much and soon

[013-107]got tired. He did not get fretful, though. He was too glad of having the

[013-108]sun and the wind again, to fret because he could not run about. He lay

[013-109]down on the dry sand, and his mother covered him with a shawl. She then

[013-110]sat by his side, and took a bit of work from her pocket. But Diamond

[013-111]felt rather sleepy, and turned on his side and gazed sleepily over the

[013-112]sand. A few yards off he saw something fluttering.

[013-113]“What is that, mother?” he said.

[013-114]“Only a bit of paper,” she answered.

[013-115]“It flutters more than a bit of paper would, I think,” said Diamond.

[013-116]“I'll go and see if you like,” said his mother. “My eyes are none of the

[013-117]best.”

[013-118]So she rose and went and found that they were both right, for it was a

[013-119]little book, partly buried in the sand. But several of its leaves were

[013-120]clear of the sand, and these the wind kept blowing about in a very

[013-121]flutterful manner. She took it up and brought it to Diamond.

[013-122]“What is it, mother?” he asked.

[013-123]“Some nursery rhymes, I think,” she answered.

[013-124]“I'm too sleepy,” said Diamond. “Do read some of them to me.”

[013-125]“Yes, I will,” she said, and began one.--“But this is such nonsense!”

[013-126]she said again. “I will try to find a better one.”

[013-127]She turned the leaves searching, but three times, with sudden puffs, the

[013-128]wind blew the leaves rustling back to the same verses.

[013-129]“Do read that one,” said Diamond, who seemed to be of the same mind as

[013-130]the wind. “It sounded very nice. I am sure it is a good one.”

[013-131]So his mother thought it might amuse him, though she couldn't find any

[013-132]sense in it. She never thought he might understand it, although she

[013-133]could not.

[013-134]Now I do not exactly know what the mother read, but this is what Diamond

[013-135]heard, or thought afterwards that he had heard. He was, however, as I

[013-136]have said, very sleepy. And when he thought he understood the verses he

[013-137]may have been only [[dream|dreaming]] better ones. This is how they went--

[013-138]I know a [[the-river|river]] whose waters run asleep run run ever [[music|singing]] in the

[013-139]shallows dumb in the hollows [[sleep|sleeping]] so deep and all the swallows that

[013-140]dip their feathers in the hollows or in the shallows are the merriest

[013-141]swallows of all for the nests they bake with the clay they cake with

[013-142]the water they shake from their wings that rake the water out of the

[013-143]shallows or the hollows will hold together in any weather and so the

[013-144]swallows are the merriest fellows and have the merriest children and

[013-145]are built so narrow like the head of an arrow to cut the air and go just

[013-146]where the nicest water is flowing and the nicest dust is blowing for

[013-147]each so narrow like head of an arrow is only a barrow to carry the

[013-148]mud he makes from the nicest water flowing and the nicest dust that is

[013-149]blowing to build his nest for her he loves best with the nicest cakes

[013-150]which the sunshine bakes all for their merry children all so callow with

[013-151]beaks that follow gaping and hollow wider and wider after their father

[013-152]or after their mother the food-provider who brings them a spider or a

[013-154]beaks as yellow as the buttercups growing beside the flowing of the

[013-202]Here Diamond became aware that his mother had stopped reading.

[013-203]“Why don't you go on, mother dear?” he asked.

[013-204]“It's such nonsense!” said his mother. “I believe it would go on for

[013-205]ever.”

[013-206]“That's just what it did,” said Diamond.

[013-207]“What did?” she asked.

[013-208]“Why, the river. That's almost the very tune it used to sing.”

[013-209]His mother was frightened, for she thought the fever was coming on

[013-210]again. So she did not contradict him.

[013-211]“Who made that poem?” asked Diamond.

[013-212]“I don't know,” she answered. “Some silly woman for her children, I

[013-213]suppose--and then thought it good enough to print.”

[013-214]“She must have been at the back of the north wind some time or other,

[013-215]anyhow,” said Diamond. “She couldn't have got a hold of it anywhere

[013-216]else. That's just how it went.” And he began to chant bits of it here

[013-217]and there; but his mother said nothing for fear of making him, worse;

[013-218]and she was very glad indeed when she saw her brother-in-law jogging

[013-219]along in his little cart. They lifted Diamond in, and got up themselves,

[013-220]and away they went, “home again, home again, home again,” as Diamond

[013-221]sang. But he soon grew quiet, and before they reached [[sandwich|Sandwich]] he was

[013-222]fast asleep and dreaming of [[back-of-the-north-wind|the country at the back of the north wind]].
