## Overview

Mr. Coleman is Diamond's father's master and the owner of the estate where the [[diamond|Diamond]] family lives in the coach-house. "Mr. Coleman was his father's master" (002-087). A merchant whose speculative ventures lead to financial ruin, his downfall has cascading consequences for everyone connected to his household. The narrator notes that the estate's geography was dominated by "Mr. Coleman's house" flanked by lawn and trees, with the coach-house situated at the back (002-106).

## Appearance and Character

Mr. Coleman is never described physically in the narrative; he appears only through the consequences of his actions. He is characterised as a man who began as an honest merchant but was gradually corrupted by speculative ambition. [[north-wind|North Wind]] explains that she "had to look after Mr. Coleman, and try to make an honest man of him" (012-068), implying that moral reclamation was the purpose behind sinking his ships. The narrator's tone suggests a man whose reach exceeded his grasp — "the trouble did not end with Mr. Coleman and his family" (012-076) — for his ruin pulled down everyone dependent on his estate.

## Role in the Story

Mr. Coleman employs [[diamonds-father|Diamond's father]] as his coachman; [[diamond|Diamond]] describes this plainly: "My father's Mr. Coleman's coachman" (004-147). Over time, Coleman's speculative business dealings grow dishonest, and [[north-wind|North Wind]] intervenes by sinking his last ship: "that very [[darkness|night]] belonged to Mr. Coleman" (012-056). The narrator explains: "[[north-wind|North Wind]] had to look after Mr. Coleman, and try to make an honest man of him" (012-068).

His ruin is complete: "the fall of Mr. Coleman crushed the little family that lived over his coach-house and stable" (012-086). [[diamond|Diamond]]'s family loses their home as Coleman sells "house, carriage, horses, furniture, and everything" and moves to a small house in [[hoxton|Hoxton]], where he hopes "yet to retrieve his for[[music|tune]]s" (012-089). Even in reduced circumstances, his former servants maintain a respectful distance — [[diamonds-father|Diamond's father]] "did not think it polite to Mr. Coleman" to speak ill of him after the collapse (016-120).

Coleman's property, known as "The Wilderness" (026-062), features in the story's geography long after his departure. The narrator's later reflection — "his love is but a poor affair. Mr. Coleman took him" (026-072) — comments on the quality of Coleman's stewardship over those in his care. [[diamond|Diamond]], reflecting on the changed for[[music|tune]]s of the household, observes simply that the fall of one man can shake many lives (013-047).

## Relationships

Mr. Coleman is married to [[mrs-coleman]] and is the father of [[miss-coleman]]. He employs [[diamonds-father]] as coachman, and [[mrs-crump]] serves in the Coleman household as a domestic. His dishonest speculation attracts the moral judgment of [[north-wind]], who orchestrates his financial downfall. After the ruin, [[diamonds-father|Diamond's father]] recalls losing his livery "as was Mr. Coleman's" (014-025), a small detail that underscores how completely the family's identity had been bound to Coleman's service.
