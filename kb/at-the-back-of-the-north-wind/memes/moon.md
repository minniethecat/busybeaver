# Moon

The **moon** is a recurring celestial image in *At the Back of [[north-wind|the North Wind]]*, serving as a symbol of cyclical change, feminine presence, and the boundary between the earthly and the wondrous. It appears throughout the narrative, from [[diamond|Diamond]]'s early encounters with [[north-wind|the North Wind]] to the fairy-tale episode of the moon-princess in a late chapter.

## As Symbol of Light and Guidance

The moon provides light in [[darkness|dark]]ness throughout the story. During [[diamond|Diamond]]'s early adventures, the moon appears as a companion to the [[darkness|night]] sky. When [[diamond|Diamond]] looks out from [[the-cathedral|the church]] window, "the moon was at that moment" visible through the eastern window, and the [[light|moonlight]] illuminated the figures of saints (007-199). On a moonless [[darkness|night]], the [[darkness|dark]]ness is described as nearly impenetrable: "It was a moonless [[darkness|night]], and very [[darkness|dark]], with glimpses of [[stars]]" (009-160).

## The Moon and the North Wind

The moon is closely associated with [[north-wind|the North Wind]]'s [[beauty]]. Her face is described as "set in it like a moon" amid her flowing hair (009-147). This simile connects [[north-wind|the North Wind]]'s majestic presence with the moon's luminous quality. In an earlier description, her emergence is likened to "a moon out of a cloud" (001-214), reinforcing the celestial imagery that surrounds her appearances.

## The Waning Moon and the Fairy Princess

A significant moon-themed narrative appears in the later chapters with the story of a princess whose health waxes and wanes with the moon. This embedded tale presents the moon as a cosmic force directly affecting mortal life: "she shall wax and wane with its mistress, the moon" (028-094). The princess grows strong at the full moon and weakens as the moon wanes: "as the moon waned, she faded" (028-122). The connection between the lunar cycle and [[princess-daylight|the princess]]'s vitality creates a rhythmic pattern of hope and decline.

## The Man in the Moon

The nursery-rhyme figure of the "man in the moon" appears in the story, blending folk tradition with the narrative's dreamlike quality. In one passage, a character is said to have "humbled the man in the moon" (024-238), and the man in the moon himself speaks (024-262). These playful references connect the story to the tradition of children's verse and folklore that runs through MacDonald's work.
