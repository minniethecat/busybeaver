# Beauty

**Beauty** in *At the Back of [[north-wind|the North Wind]]* is associated with transformative power, moral [[kindness|goodness]], and the natural world. It is embodied most strikingly in the figure of [[north-wind|the North Wind]] herself, whose appearance shifts between terrible majesty and luminous loveliness, and in [[princess-daylight|Princess Daylight]], whose beauty waxes and wanes with the [[moon]]. Beauty in the story is never merely decorative — it always points toward deeper truths, and its corruption or loss signals a moral or spiritual disorder.

## The North Wind's Mighty Beauty

The [[north-wind|North Wind]]'s beauty is described with [[wonder|awe]]. [[diamond|Diamond]] is "entranced with her mighty beauty" when he first beholds her fully (001-212). Her hair transforms and gathers itself as she reveals her true form. The phrase "mighty beauty" captures the dual quality MacDonald gives her — she is beautiful not in spite of her power but because of it, and her beauty is inseparable from her formidable nature. She is at once terrifying and alluring, and [[diamond|Diamond]]'s response — entrancement rather than fear — reveals his unique capacity to perceive beauty where others might see only threat.

## Beauty and Moral Goodness

The story suggests a connection between beauty and moral quality. The [[north-wind|North Wind]] remarks that some beings allow "their badness to spoil their beauty" (001-266), implying that beauty can be corrupted by moral failing. This idea — that beauty is not merely physical but reflects inner [[kindness|goodness]] — runs through MacDonald's moral vision in the story. Beauty and [[kindness|goodness]] are not identical, but they are deeply related: true beauty arises from and is sustained by [[kindness|goodness]], while moral corruption eventually mars even the loveliest outward form.

## The Beauty of Princess Daylight

The embedded fairy tale of [[princess-daylight|Princess Daylight]] (Chapter XXVIII) offers the novel's most extended meditation on beauty. [[princess-daylight|Daylight]] is cursed by a wicked fairy so that her vitality and beauty are bound to the [[moon]]'s phases: "The more beautiful she was in the full [[moon]], the more she was sickly and miserable as the [[moon]] waned" (028-152). Her beauty is not a static possession but a living rhythm — "As she grew older she had grown more and more beautiful" (028-149) — and it reaches its peak only in [[light|moonlight]], when she wakes from her daylight slumber. The "fame of her beauty and sweetness had gone abroad" (028-185), drawing suitors from distant lands, yet only the prince who loves her in her faded state can break the curse. MacDonald's point is clear: beauty is most precious when it is vulnerable, cyclical, and tied to love rather than mere admiration.

## Beauty in Simple Things

[[diamond|Diamond]] perceives beauty in humble objects and creatures. A simple thing is a "wonderful thing for beauty" (005-081), and a red horse is admired for its appearance: "such a beauty!" (030-045). [[ruby|Ruby]] the horse is named for the beauty of her red coat, and even [[old-diamond|Old Diamond]], the aging cab-horse, is seen with an [[love|affection]] that transforms the ordinary into the beloved. This appreciation for beauty in everyday things reflects [[diamond|Diamond]]'s characteristic way of seeing the world — with wonder and gratitude that finds the extraordinary in the ordinary.

## Beauty as Living Growth

Beauty is presented as something that grows from within rather than being applied externally. A character says, "from the ends of my fingers my beauty grows" (022-069), suggesting that true beauty is an organic expression of one's nature rather than an artificial adornment. This principle applies equally to the [[north-wind|North Wind]] (whose beauty is inseparable from her being), to [[princess-daylight|Princess Daylight]] (whose beauty waxes naturally with the [[moon]]), and to the moral life itself — beauty cannot be [[suffering|pain]]ted on; it must be grown.
