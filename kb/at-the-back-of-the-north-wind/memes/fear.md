## Definition

In *At the Back of [[north-wind|the North Wind]]*, fear appears as a recurring emotional state that [[diamond|Diamond]] must confront and overcome. MacDonald presents fear not simply as a natural reaction to danger but as a spiritual obstacle — something that must be surrendered through trust. The [[north-wind|North Wind]] explicitly frames fear as the opposite of the courage she wishes to teach [[diamond|Diamond]], treating it as a condition to be unlearned rather than a permanent state.

## In the Novel

Fear is introduced early in [[diamond|Diamond]]'s encounters with [[north-wind|the North Wind]]. When he first finds himself flying through the [[darkness|night]] sky, "fear invaded his heart" (006-008), and he is nearly overwhelmed. The [[north-wind|North Wind]]'s response is instructive: "be a man. What is fearful to you is not the least fearful to me" (006-024). She does not comfort [[diamond|Diamond]] by removing the danger but by challenging his perception of it — fear is a matter of perspective, not objective threat.

The novel's most concentrated treatment of fear appears during [[diamond|Diamond]]'s first flight to the back of the north wind, when he is "almost choked with fear" (005-266). Yet immediately after the crisis passes, [[north-wind|the North Wind]] "gave him back his courage" (005-269). This pattern — fear followed by restoration — recurs throughout [[diamond|Diamond]]'s adventures, suggesting that fear is a necessary passage toward courage rather than its simple absence.

In Chapter VII, [[diamond|Diamond]] rides [[north-wind|the North Wind]]'s hair along a narrow ledge above an abyss. Initially he "held his breath for fear as he looked down" (007-081), but as the ride continues, his fear transforms: he feels "his faintness going away, and his fear with it" (007-109). By the passage's end he is described as "fearless for the time as [[north-wind|North Wind]] herself" (007-113). This progression — terror, endurance, [[courage|fearlessness]] — is the novel's template for spiritual maturation.

Fear also appears in the domestic sphere. [[diamonds-mother|Diamond's mother]] "said nothing for fear of making him worse" (013-217) during his illness, a silence born of love rather than cowardice. [[diamonds-father|Diamond's father]] [[diamonds-father|Joseph]], lost in the coal mine, experiences "some fear lest her husband should have" met with harm (024-055) — a natural human anxiety that the novel treats with [[kindness|compassion]] rather than judgment.

The novel expands fear beyond [[diamond|Diamond]]'s experience through the embedded tale of "Little [[princess-daylight|Daylight]]" in Chapter XXVIII, where "the prince feared for some time that she was" lost forever (028-336), and later "feared that [[princess-daylight|the princess]] would" never awaken (028-363). Fear in this tale-within-a-tale mirrors [[diamond|Diamond]]'s own journey — a prince who must persist through fear to reach his beloved.

The [[north-wind|North Wind]] herself acknowledges fear's limits when she tells [[diamond|Diamond]], "Indeed, as it is, I fear I must vanish" (009-325), revealing that even she is subject to conditions beyond her control. This admission humanises her and makes [[diamond|Diamond]]'s own fear less isolating — fear is universal, not a mark of failure.

## Key Passages

> "be a man. What is fearful to you is not the least fearful to me." (006-024)

The [[north-wind|North Wind]]'s challenge reframes fear as subjective — what terrifies [[diamond|Diamond]] does not trouble her, and by implication, [[diamond|Diamond]] can learn her perspective.

> Diamond began to grow warm, Diamond's courage began to grow. (004-220)

The transformation from cold fear to warm courage, described in the novel's characteristic pairing of physical sensation with moral state.

## Fear and Faith

MacDonald's treatment of fear cannot be separated from his theological commitments. Throughout the novel, fear is consistently counterposed to trust — particularly trust in [[north-wind|the North Wind]], who serves as a divine surrogate. When [[diamond|Diamond]] is afraid during the storm, the narrative does not resolve with the danger passing but with [[diamond|Diamond]] learning to trust: the fear remains but its power over him diminishes. This pattern mirrors MacDonald's Christian understanding of [[trust|faith]] as a relationship that transforms fear rather than eliminating its cause.

The novel's theology of fear is most explicit in the drowning scene. [[diamond|Diamond]], caught in the rising waters, experiences what should be the ultimate terror — yet the narrative describes not panic but surrender. The fear of death is overcome not by rescue but by acceptance of [[north-wind|the North Wind]]'s presence even in annihilation. This is MacDonald's version of the Christian consolation: that fear of death is conquered not by immortality but by trust in divine [[kindness|goodness]] through and beyond death.

## Fear and Class

The novel also embeds fear within its social landscape. [[diamond|Diamond]]'s family, living in [[suffering|poverty]] above the coach-house, inhabits a world of economic precarity in which fear is structural rather than episodic. His mother's fear for [[diamond|Diamond]]'s health is inseparable from the family's inability to afford proper care — a fear that the novel treats with dignity rather than sentimentality. [[diamonds-father|Joseph]]'s fear in the coal mine (024-055) is likewise tied to his working-class identity: his labour is dangerous, and his family's survival depends on his safe return. MacDonald does not resolve these fears through social critique but through the assurance of divine care — a consolation that, for the novel's original Victorian readers, would have carried both comfort and political quietism.

## Fear as Narrative Engine

Structurally, fear functions as the novel's primary engine of suspense. Each of [[diamond|Diamond]]'s adventures begins with a moment of terror — the first flight, the ledge ride, the drowning — and the reader's investment depends on sharing [[diamond|Diamond]]'s fear before experiencing its resolution. MacDonald understood that fear, properly managed, creates readerly sympathy: we care about [[diamond|Diamond]] because we have felt his terror. The pattern of fear-and-release that structures individual episodes also structures the novel as a whole, which moves from [[diamond|Diamond]]'s initial terror at [[north-wind|the North Wind]]'s strangeness to his final peaceful acceptance of death as the ultimate passage to the back of the north wind.

## Related Characters

- **[[diamond]]**: The primary subject of fear in the novel, who learns to overcome it through his experiences with [[north-wind|the North Wind]]. His progression from terror to [[courage|fearlessness]] models the novel's vision of moral growth.
- **[[north-wind]]**: The agent who teaches [[diamond|Diamond]] to overcome fear — not by eliminating danger but by demonstrating that what seems fearful need not be. Her own admission of fear makes her a fellow traveller rather than a remote ideal.
