## Overview

Mrs. Coleman is the mistress of the Coleman household, wife of [[mr-coleman]] and mother of [[miss-coleman]]. She appears as a lady of means who, like the rest of her family, is affected by her husband's financial ruin. [[diamond|Diamond]] first encounters her on the street: "the lady was his father's mistress, Mrs. Coleman" (005-043).

## Appearance and Character

Mrs. Coleman is portrayed as a woman of refinement and quiet dignity. Her manner is that of a lady accustomed to a comfortable station, yet she shows genuine kindness to those beneath her — [[diamond|Diamond]] recalls receiving a penny from her as a gift (005-046). Even after her husband's financial collapse, she retains her composure. [[diamond|Diamond]] observes that "Mrs. Coleman is none so poor as all that yet" (017-196), suggesting she carries herself with a bearing that transcends her reduced material circumstances. The narrator's passing mentions convey a woman who adapts to [[suffering|hardship]] without losing her essential gentility.

## Role in the Story

Mrs. Coleman is a background presence in the Coleman estate, where [[diamond]]'s family lives in the coach-house. She shows kindness to [[diamond|Diamond]] on at least one occasion — he recalls receiving a penny from her as a gift (005-046). After her husband's speculative ventures fail and [[north-wind|North Wind]] sinks his last ship, Mrs. Coleman is among those who must adjust to drastically reduced circumstances, moving with the family to a small house in [[hoxton|Hoxton]] (012-089).

In the story's later chapters, Mrs. Coleman retains her dignity despite the family's losses. [[diamond|Diamond]] observes that "Mrs. Coleman is none so poor as all that yet" (017-196), noting that she still carries herself with the bearing of her former station. When [[diamond|Diamond]] visits the Colemans in their reduced circumstances, she reflects philosophically: "It's changed times for us" (017-129), and still manages small generosities — the narrator notes how "Mrs. Coleman took out her purse" (017-150) to offer what little she could.

## Relationships

Mrs. Coleman is married to [[mr-coleman]] and is the mother of [[miss-coleman]]. [[mrs-crump]] serves in her household. She is the mistress of [[diamonds-father]], who works as the Coleman family coachman. Together with her husband and daughter, she is listed among those who felt the effects of the family's ruin, as noted when "[[mr-coleman|Mr. Coleman]] and Mrs. Coleman, and [[miss-coleman|Miss Coleman]]" (019-135) are mentioned in the context of the family's diminished state.
