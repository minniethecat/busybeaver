## Overview

The Sea appears in the novel in multiple registers: as a literal geographical destination [[diamond|Diamond]] visits with his mother, as the vast element over which [[north-wind|North Wind]] flies with [[diamond|Diamond]] on her back, and as a metaphor for the unknown — death, eternity, the boundary between worlds. The sea-shore provides some of the novel's loveliest domestic scenes, while the open sea furnishes its most sublime visionary passages.

## Location and Description

The sea is geographically located on the English coast, in the vicinity of [[sandwich|Sandwich]] — a town described as having once been a seaport before the sea receded: "a town abandoned by its nurse, the sea" (009-049). MacDonald describes the historical silting of [[sandwich|Sandwich]] harbor: "the sea grew less and less" (009-052) until "[[sandwich|Sandwich]] was a seaport no more" (009-054).

In [[diamond|Diamond]]'s dream-flights with [[north-wind|North Wind]], the sea is vast and luminous: "so fast they went that the sea slid away from under them" (012-001). From above, ships appear as toys — "the finest that ever sailed the sea" (009-238). The sea's scale dwarfs human concerns and provides the novel's most powerful images of sublimity.

The sea-shore, by contrast, is domestic and intimate. [[diamond|Diamond]] visits it with his mother, who "had picked up on the sea-shore" (020-005) treasures. [[mr-raymond|Mr. Raymond]] recommends "a whiff of the sea-air" (012-109) for its restorative properties — a Victorian commonplace that MacDonald endorses without irony.

## Events

1. **[[diamond|Diamond]] and his mother at the shore** (Chapters III, XVI, XX): [[diamonds-mother|Diamond's mother]] takes him to the sea-shore, where he collects objects and listens to her read. "Sometimes he thought" (016-010) of the stories while on the shore. The sea-shore is a place of maternal love and quiet pleasure.

2. **[[north-wind|North Wind]]'s flight over the sea** (Chapters IX, XII): Flying on [[north-wind|North Wind]]'s back, [[diamond|Diamond]] sees the sea from above — a perspective that transforms the familiar into the sublime: "they flew so fast" (012-001) that the sea itself seemed to move beneath them. [[north-wind|North Wind]] shows him ships and the "precipices that bounded the sea" (011-125).

3. **The sound of the sea** (Chapter XXXII): In [[the-cottage|the country cottage]], [[diamond|Diamond]] hears the wind in the roof "like the sea moaning" (032-013), a sound that connects his present domestic peace with his earlier visionary experiences.

## Symbolism and Significance

The Sea functions as a multivalent symbol in the novel:

**As death and the unknown**: The sea is the traditional symbol of death — vast, uncontrollable, the boundary beyond which one cannot see. [[diamond|Diamond]]'s flights over the sea with [[north-wind|North Wind]] are rehearsals for his final journey into the unknown. The sea's depth and [[darkness|dark]]ness correspond to the mystery that [[diamond|Diamond]] approaches without fear.

**As sublimity**: MacDonald uses the sea to evoke the Romantic sublime — nature at a scale that overwhelms human comprehension. The "precipices that bounded the sea" (011-125) and the ships reduced to toys from [[north-wind|North Wind]]'s altitude create the characteristic double movement of sublime experience: [[fear|terror]] and exaltation.

**As maternal nurture**: Paradoxically, the sea is also nurturing. The sea-air restores health; the sea-shore yields treasures; and [[diamonds-mother|Diamond's mother]] — the novel's most nurturing figure — is associated with the shore. MacDonald's sea is both the threatening deep and the life-giving margin, a duality that reflects his theology of a God who is both terrible and tender.
