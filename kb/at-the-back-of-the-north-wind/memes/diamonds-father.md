## Overview

Diamond's father is a [[london|London]] cabman — a working man who drives a horse-drawn cab for a living — and later, through the patronage of [[mr-raymond|Mr. Raymond]], a coachman in a more secure position. He is the novel's central representative of the honest working poor, and his economic arc — from modest comfort through devastating [[suffering|poverty]] to restored security — provides the material counterpoint to [[diamond|Diamond]]'s spiritual journey.

He is introduced at the very opening of the novel: "his father, who was a coachman" built [[diamond|Diamond]] a bed in the hayloft above the stable, "and Diamond's father put old [[diamond|Diamond]] in the stable" (001-023). This domestic detail — a father who builds his son a bed in an unusual but cherished place — immediately establishes him as a figure of [[love|affection]]ate competence.

MacDonald's summary judgment of him is unequivocal: "Diamond's father was not only one of the finest of his class, but was a gentleman of the true sort" (003-114). The qualifier "of the true sort" distinguishes him from the merely well-born and aligns him with the novel's persistent critique of class-based moral assessments.

## Appearance and Character

Diamond's father is physically capable and skilled at his trade. When offered an inferior horse, his protest carries professional dignity: "'he ain't a Hansom horse,' said Diamond's father indignantly" (014-035). He knows his business and takes pride in it.

His character is marked by several consistent traits: **[[truth|honesty]]** — he returns a lost purse to its owners, an act that transforms the family's circumstances (017-149); **loyalty** — once his for[[music|tune]]s recover, he "bought old [[diamond|Diamond]] again, along with another horse and cab" (014-068), repurchasing the horse that had been his companion in better days; and **practical [[love|affection]]** — he builds [[diamond|Diamond]]'s bed, worries over his strange [[dream|dreams]], and provides for his family even when work is scarce.

The narrative offers a moment of intimacy between father and son at the stable:

> "Diamond was not in the least afraid of his father driving over him, but, watching his chance, darted in under the hoofs of the horse and caught his father's hand." (003-115)

The child's [[courage|fearlessness]] — "not in the least afraid of his father driving over him" — testifies to the trust that [[diamond|Diamond]] places in his father's competence and care.

He is not idealized. Under financial strain, he can be short-tempered: "Diamond's father and mother were, notwithstanding, rather miserable" (015-024). His anxiety about money sometimes expresses itself as gruffness toward his wife and child. But the novel consistently portrays this as the pressure of circumstance rather than a flaw of character.

## Role in the Story

Diamond's father traces one of the novel's most carefully plotted economic arcs:

**1. Modest comfort (Chapters I–V).** He owns a horse and cab, lives securely if not lavishly, and has the leisure to build [[diamond|Diamond]] a bed in the hayloft. He works for [[mr-coleman|Mr. Coleman]] — "[[mr-coleman|Mr. Coleman]] was his father's master" (002-077) — and the family's place in the social order, though low, is stable.

**2. Economic collapse (Chapters XII–XIV).** [[mr-coleman|Mr. Coleman]]'s bankruptcy (Chapter XII) destroys this stability. "Diamond's father had nothing to do for a time" (012-097). He must sell his horse and cab — and, more [[suffering|pain]]fully, old [[diamond|Diamond]] the horse. The family moves to a lodging-house in a poorer quarter. Diamond's father "having saved a little money, and finding that no situation" (014-033) is forthcoming, faces the prospect of permanent downward mobility.

**3. The honest act (Chapter XVII).** The turning point is a small but morally weighty action: "Diamond's father gave the ladies a ticket with his name on it" (017-149). He has found and returned a lost purse. The act is not performed for reward — he is simply incapable of dis[[truth|honesty]] — but it sets in motion the chain of events that leads to [[mr-raymond|Mr. Raymond]]'s intervention.

**4. Recovery and security (Chapters XXVII–XXX).** [[mr-raymond|Mr. Raymond]], learning of the honest cabman, offers him employment as a coachman at his country residence. The offer is described as "over-advantageous to Diamond's father" (029-121) — more generous than his work strictly merits — and represents the novel's vision of benevolent patronage as the proper response to deserving [[suffering|poverty]]. Diamond's father "went the very next day to [[mr-raymond|Mr. Raymond]]'s" (029-167), and the family is relocated to [[the-country|the countryside]], where [[diamond|Diamond]] will spend his final, luminous days.

## Themes and Symbolism

Diamond's father embodies the Victorian ideal of the **deserving poor** — those whose [[suffering|poverty]] stems not from moral failing but from economic circumstance, and whose [[truth|honesty]] and industry create a moral claim on the benevolence of the wealthy. The novel's resolution — the father's restoration through patronage — is an economic fairy tale, but it rests on a character whose [[truth|honesty]] has been carefully established across the preceding chapters.

The symbolic doubling of names — [[diamond|Diamond]] the boy and Old [[diamond|Diamond]] the horse — creates a resonance around the father's relationships. He cares for both [[diamond|Diamond]]s: the horse who pulls his cab and the son who [[dream|sleep]]s in the stable. His loyalty to the horse (repurchasing him when for[[music|tune]]s improve) mirrors his loyalty to his son (whom he never abandons, even when [[diamond|Diamond]]'s strange [[dream|dreams]] and mysterious absences trouble him). The father is, in this sense, the stable center around which both [[diamond|Diamond]]s orbit.

His worldly arc also mirrors [[diamond|Diamond]]'s spiritual one: where the son is drawn toward the back of [[north-wind|the North Wind]] — toward death and transcendence — the father is drawn toward [[the-cottage|the country cottage]] — toward earthly security and domestic peace. Both arcs are presented as good, and neither is privileged over the other. MacDonald's theology holds that the material and the spiritual are not opposed but different registers of the same divine [[kindness|goodness]].
