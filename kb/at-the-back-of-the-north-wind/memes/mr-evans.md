## Overview

Mr. Evans is a gentleman who appears in a single but significant episode (Chapter XXVI). He is connected to [[miss-coleman|Miss Coleman]] as a business associate — "The gentleman was Mr. Evans, to whom [[miss-coleman|Miss Coleman]] was to..." (026-066). Having lost his for[[music|tune]] in a speculative venture ("their last venture, and Mr. Evans had gone out with it," 026-076), he is found wandering the streets on a stormy [[darkness|night]], exhausted and starving. His physical and spiritual rescue — by [[north-wind|North Wind]]'s providential steering of [[diamonds-father|Diamond's father]]'s cab, and then by [[miss-coleman|Miss Coleman]]'s care — forms one of the novel's most tightly plotted examples of divine providence working through apparent coincidence.

## Appearance and Character

Mr. Evans is portrayed as a gentleman brought low by misfor[[music|tune]] rather than vice. When [[diamonds-father|Diamond's father]]'s cab d[[the-river|river]] finds him, he is described as "too tired and too much occupied" (026-109) to function properly. His physical state is dire: "from fatigue and want of food, Mr. Evans yielded to the" (026-128) elements. [[miss-coleman|Miss Coleman]] initially thinks "Mr. Evans was dead" (026-141), underscoring the gravity of his condition.

Unlike the drunken cabman, whose suffering is self-inflicted, Mr. Evans's misfor[[music|tune]] comes from external circumstances — a failed business venture. This distinction is important to MacDonald's moral typology: Evans is among the "deserving" sufferers who merit restoration.

## Role in the Story

Mr. Evans appears in a tightly constructed episode that demonstrates the novel's providential logic:

1. **The venture fails** (Chapter XXVI): Evans and [[miss-coleman|Miss Coleman]] have undertaken "their last venture" (026-076), which proves unsuccessful. Evans is ruined financially.

2. **The storm** (Chapter XXVI): On a [[darkness|night]] of terrible weather, Evans is out in the storm, weakened and directionless. [[diamonds-father|Diamond's father]]'s cab is steered — implicitly by [[north-wind|North Wind]] — toward him: "if he should" (026-101) encounter [[mr-raymond|the gentleman]], he should help him.

3. **Rescue and recovery** (Chapter XXVI): [[diamonds-father|Diamond's father]] finds Evans and takes him to [[miss-coleman|Miss Coleman]]'s house. Initially thought dead, Evans revives: "Almost as soon as Mr. Evans went in, the wind began to cease" (026-144), a detail that explicitly links his rescue to the calming of the storm and, symbolically, to [[north-wind|North Wind]]'s agency. "In a little while Mr. Evans came out, and asked him to come" (026-149) — restored, he resumes his place in the social order.

## Themes and Symbolism

Mr. Evans's episode is a case study in the novel's theology of **providence through apparent chance**. No miracle is explicitly narrated — the cab simply happens to be in the right place. But MacDonald's narrative structure strongly implies that [[north-wind|North Wind]] has arranged the coincidence. As [[north-wind|North Wind]] herself tells [[diamond|Diamond]] earlier, she works through natural means as often as through supernatural ones.

The episode also reinforces the novel's **social gospel**: the wealthy ([[miss-coleman|Miss Coleman]]) have an obligation to rescue the fallen, and the honest poor ([[diamonds-father|Diamond's father]] as cabman) are the instruments through which such rescue is effected. Evans's restoration is not merely personal but social — he "came out" (026-149) of [[miss-coleman|Miss Coleman]]'s house reintegrated into the community of mutual obligation.
