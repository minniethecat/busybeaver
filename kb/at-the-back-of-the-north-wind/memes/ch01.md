CHAPTER I. THE HAY-LOFT

[001-001]I HAVE been asked to tell you about the back of the north wind. An old

[001-002]Greek writer mentions a people who lived there, and were so comfortable

[001-003]that they could not bear it any longer, and drowned themselves. My

[001-004]story is not the same as his. I do not think Herodotus had got the right

[001-005]account of the place. I am going to tell you how it fared with a boy who

[001-006]went there.

[001-007]He lived in a low room over a coach-house; and that was not by any means

[001-008]at the back of the north wind, as his mother very well knew. For one

[001-009]side of the room was built only of boards, and the boards were so old

[001-010]that you might run a penknife through into the north wind. And then let

[001-011]them settle between them which was the sharper! I know that when you

[001-012]pulled it out again the wind would be after it like a cat after a mouse,

[001-013]and you would know soon enough you were not at the back of the north

[001-014]wind. Still, this room was not very cold, except when the north wind

[001-015]blew stronger than usual: the room I have to do with now was always

[001-016]cold, except in summer, when the sun took the matter into his own hands.

[001-017]Indeed, I am not sure whether I ought to call it a room at all; for it

[001-018]was just a loft where they kept hay and straw and oats for the horses.

[001-019]And when [[diamond|little Diamond]]--but stop: I must tell you that his father, who

[001-020]was a coachman, had named him after a favourite horse, and his mother

[001-021]had had no objection:--when little Diamond, then, lay there in bed, he

[001-022]could hear the horses under him munching away in the [[darkness|dark]], or moving

[001-023]sleepily in their [[dream|dreams]]. For [[diamonds-father|Diamond's father]] had built him a bed in

[001-024][[the-hay-loft|the loft]] with boards all round it, because they had so little room in

[001-025]their own end over the coach-house; and Diamond's father put old Diamond

[001-026]in the stall under the bed, because he was a quiet horse, and did not

[001-027]go to sleep standing, but lay down like a reasonable creature. But,

[001-028]although he was a surprisingly reasonable creature, yet, when young

[001-029]Diamond woke in the middle of the night, and felt the bed shaking in the

[001-030]blasts of the north wind, he could not help wondering whether, if the

[001-031]wind should blow the house down, and he were to fall through into

[001-032]the manger, old Diamond mightn't eat him up before he knew him in his

[001-033]night-gown. And although old Diamond was very quiet all night long, yet

[001-034]when he woke he got up like an earthquake, and then young Diamond knew

[001-035]what o'clock it was, or at least what was to be done next, which was--to

[001-036]go to sleep again as fast as he could.

[001-037]There was hay at his feet and hay at his head, piled up in great trusses

[001-038]to the very roof. Indeed it was sometimes only through a little lane

[001-039]with several turnings, which looked as if it had been sawn out for him,

[001-040]that he could reach his bed at all. For the stock of hay was, of course,

[001-041]always in a state either of slow ebb or of sudden flow. Sometimes the

[001-042]whole space of the loft, with the little panes in the roof for the

[001-043][[stars]] to look in, would lie open before his open eyes as he lay in bed;

[001-044]sometimes a yellow wall of sweet-smelling fibres closed up his view at

[001-045]the distance of half a yard. Sometimes, when his mother had undressed

[001-046]him in her room, and told him to trot to bed by himself, he would

[001-047]creep into the heart of the hay, and lie there thinking how cold it was

[001-048]outside in the wind, and how warm it was inside there in his bed, and

[001-049]how he could go to it when he pleased, only he wouldn't just yet; he

[001-050]would get a little colder first. And ever as he grew colder, his bed

[001-051]would grow warmer, till at last he would scramble out of the hay, shoot

[001-052]like an arrow into his bed, cover himself up, and snuggle down, thinking

[001-053]what a happy boy he was. He had not the least idea that the wind got in

[001-054]at a chink in the wall, and blew about him all night. For the back of

[001-055]his bed was only of boards an inch thick, and on the other side of them

[001-056]was the north wind.

[001-057]Now, as I have already said, these boards were soft and crumbly. To be

[001-058]sure, they were tarred on the outside, yet in many places they were more

[001-059]like tinder than timber. Hence it happened that the soft part having

[001-060]worn away from about it, little Diamond found one night, after he lay

[001-061]down, that a knot had come out of one of them, and that the wind was

[001-062]blowing in upon him in a cold and rather imperious fashion. Now he had

[001-063]no fancy for leaving things wrong that might be set right; so he jumped

[001-064]out of bed again, got a little strike of hay, twisted it up, folded it

[001-065]in the middle, and, having thus made it into a cork, stuck it into the

[001-066]hole in the wall. But the wind began to blow loud and angrily, and, as

[001-067]Diamond was falling asleep, out blew his cork and hit him on the

[001-068]nose, just hard enough to wake him up quite, and let him hear the wind

[001-069]whistling shrill in the hole. He searched for his hay-cork, found it,

[001-070]stuck it in harder, and was just dropping off once more, when, pop! with

[001-071]an angry whistle behind it, the cork struck him again, this time on the

[001-072]cheek. Up he rose once more, made a fresh stopple of hay, and corked the

[001-073]hole severely. But he was hardly down again before--pop! it came on his

[001-074]forehead. He gave it up, drew the clothes above his head, and was soon

[001-075]fast asleep.

[001-076]Although the next day was very stormy, Diamond forgot all about the

[001-077]hole, for he was busy making a cave by the side of his mother's fire

[001-078]with a broken chair, a three-legged stool, and a blanket, and then

[001-079]sitting in it. His mother, however, discovered it, and pasted a bit of

[001-080]brown paper over it, so that, when Diamond had snuggled down the next

[001-081]night, he had no occasion to think of it.

[001-082]Presently, however, he lifted his head and listened. Who could that be

[001-083]talking to him? The wind was rising again, and getting very loud, and

[001-084]full of rushes and whistles. He was sure some one was talking--and very

[001-085]near him, too, it was. But he was not frightened, for he had not yet

[001-086]learned how to be; so he sat up and hearkened. At last the voice, which,

[001-087]though quite gentle, sounded a little angry, appeared to come from the

[001-088]back of the bed. He crept nearer to it, and laid his ear against the

[001-089]wall. Then he heard nothing but the wind, which sounded very loud

[001-090]indeed. The moment, however, that he moved his head from the wall, he

[001-091]heard the voice again, close to his ear. He felt about with his hand,

[001-092]and came upon the piece of paper his mother had pasted over the

[001-093]hole. Against this he laid his ear, and then he heard the voice quite

[001-094]distinctly. There was, in fact, a little corner of the paper loose, and

[001-095]through that, as from a mouth in the wall, the voice came.

[001-096]“What do you mean, little boy--closing up my window?”

[001-097]“What window?” asked Diamond.

[001-098]“You stuffed hay into it three times last night. I had to blow it out

[001-099]again three times.”

[001-100]“You can't mean this little hole! It isn't a window; it's a hole in my

[001-101]bed.”

[001-102]“I did not say it was a window: I said it was my window.”

[001-103]“But it can't be a window, because windows are holes to see out of.”

[001-104]“Well, that's just what I made this window for.”

[001-105]“But you are outside: you can't want a window.”

[001-106]“You are quite mistaken. Windows are to see out of, you say. Well, I'm

[001-107]in my house, and I want windows to see out of it.”

[001-108]“But you've made a window into my bed.”

[001-109]“Well, your mother has got three windows into my dancing room, and you

[001-110]have three into my garret.”

[001-111]“But I heard father say, when my mother wanted him to make a window

[001-112]through the wall, that it was against the law, for it would look into

[001-113][[mr-dyves|Mr. Dyves]]'s garden.”

[001-114]The voice laughed.

[001-115]“The law would have some trouble to catch me!” it said.

[001-116]“But if it's not right, you know,” said Diamond, “that's no matter. You

[001-117]shouldn't do it.”

[001-118]“I am so tall I am above that law,” said the voice.

[001-119]“You must have a tall house, then,” said Diamond.

[001-120]“Yes; a tall house: the clouds are inside it.”

[001-121]“Dear me!” said Diamond, and thought a minute. “I think, then, you can

[001-122]hardly expect me to keep a window in my bed for you. Why don't you make

[001-123]a window into Mr. Dyves's bed?”

[001-124]“Nobody makes a window into an ash-pit,” said the voice, rather sadly.

[001-125]“I like to see nice things out of my windows.”

[001-126]“But he must have a nicer bed than I have, though mine is very nice--so

[001-127]nice that I couldn't wish a better.”

[001-128]“It's not the bed I care about: it's what is in it.--But you just open

[001-129]that window.”

[001-130]“Well, mother says I shouldn't be disobliging; but it's rather hard. You

[001-131]see the north wind will blow right in my face if I do.”

[001-132]“I am [[north-wind|the North Wind]].”

[001-133]“O-o-oh!” said Diamond, thoughtfully. “Then will you promise not to blow

[001-134]on my face if I open your window?”

[001-135]“I can't promise that.”

[001-136]“But you'll give me the toothache. Mother's got it already.”

[001-137]“But what's to become of me without a window?”

[001-138]“I'm sure I don't know. All I say is, it will be worse for me than for

[001-139]you.”

[001-140]“No; it will not. You shall not be the worse for it--I promise you that.

[001-141]You will be much the better for it. Just you believe what I say, and do

[001-142]as I tell you.”

[001-143]“Well, I can pull the clothes over my head,” said Diamond, and feeling

[001-144]with his little sharp nails, he got hold of the open edge of the paper

[001-145]and tore it off at once.

[001-146]In came a long whistling spear of cold, and struck his little naked

[001-147]chest. He scrambled and tumbled in under the bedclothes, and covered

[001-148]himself up: there was no paper now between him and the voice, and he

[001-149]felt a little--not frightened exactly--I told you he had not learned

[001-150]that yet--but rather queer; for what a strange person this North Wind

[001-151]must be that lived in the great house--“called Out-of-Doors, I suppose,”

[001-152]thought Diamond--and made windows into people's beds! But the voice

[001-153]began again; and he could hear it quite plainly, even with his head

[001-154]under the bed-clothes. It was a still more gentle voice now, although

[001-155]six times as large and loud as it had been, and he thought it sounded a

[001-156]little like his mother's.

[001-157]“What is your name, little boy?” it asked.

[001-158]“Diamond,” answered Diamond, under the bed-clothes.

[001-159]“What a funny name!”

[001-160]“It's a very nice name,” returned its owner.

[001-161]“I don't know that,” said the voice.

[001-162]“Well, I do,” retorted Diamond, a little rudely.

[001-163]“Do you know to whom you are speaking!”

[001-164]“No,” said Diamond.

[001-165]And indeed he did not. For to know a person's name is not always to know

[001-166]the person's self.

[001-167]“Then I must not be angry with you.--You had better look and see,

[001-168]though.”

[001-169]“Diamond is a very pretty name,” persisted the boy, vexed that it should

[001-170]not give satisfaction.

[001-171]“Diamond is a useless thing rather,” said the voice.

[001-172]“That's not true. Diamond is very nice--as big as two--and so quiet all

[001-173]night! And doesn't he make a jolly row in the morning, getting upon his

[001-174]four great legs! It's like thunder.”

[001-175]“You don't seem to know what a diamond is.”

[001-176]“Oh, don't I just! Diamond is a great and good horse; and he sleeps

[001-177]right under me. He is old Diamond, and I am young Diamond; or, if you

[001-178]like it better, for you're very particular, Mr. North Wind, he's big

[001-179]Diamond, and I'm little Diamond; and I don't know which of us my father

[001-180]likes best.”

[001-181]A beautiful laugh, large but very soft and musical, sounded somewhere

[001-182]beside him, but Diamond kept his head under the clothes.

[001-183]“I'm not Mr. North Wind,” said the voice.

[001-184]“You told me that you were the North Wind,” insisted Diamond.

[001-185]“I did not say Mister North Wind,” said the voice.

[001-186]“Well, then, I do; for mother tells me I ought to be polite.”

[001-187]“Then let me tell you I don't think it at all polite of you to say

[001-188]Mister to me.”

[001-189]“Well, I didn't know better. I'm very sorry.”

[001-190]“But you ought to know better.”

[001-191]“I don't know that.”

[001-192]“I do. You can't say it's polite to lie there talking--with your head

[001-193]under the bed-clothes, and never look up to see what kind of person you

[001-194]are talking to.--I want you to come out with me.”

[001-195]“I want to go to sleep,” said Diamond, very nearly crying, for he did

[001-196]not like to be scolded, even when he deserved it.

[001-197]“You shall sleep all the better to-morrow night.”

[001-198]“Besides,” said Diamond, “you are out in Mr. Dyves's garden, and I can't

[001-199]get there. I can only get into our own yard.”

[001-200]“Will you take your head out of the bed-clothes?” said the voice, just a

[001-201]little angrily.

[001-202]“No!” answered Diamond, half peevish, half frightened.

[001-203]The instant he said the word, a tremendous blast of wind crashed in a

[001-204]board of the wall, and swept the clothes off Diamond. He started up in

[001-205][[fear|terror]]. Leaning over him was the large, beautiful, pale face of a woman.

[001-206]Her dark eyes looked a little angry, for they had just begun to flash;

[001-207]but a quivering in her sweet upper lip made her look as if she were

[001-208]going to cry. What was the most strange was that away from her head

[001-209]streamed out her black hair in every direction, so that the darkness in

[001-210]the hay-loft looked as if it were made of her hair but as Diamond gazed

[001-211]at her in speechless [[wonder|amazement]], mingled with [[trust|confidence]]--for the boy was

[001-212]entranced with her mighty [[beauty]]--her hair began to gather itself out

[001-213]of the darkness, and fell down all about her again, till her face looked

[001-214]out of the midst of it like a [[moon]] out of a cloud. From her eyes came

[001-215]all the light by which Diamond saw her face and her hair; and that was

[001-216]all he did see of her yet. The wind was over and gone.

[001-217]“Will you go with me now, you little Diamond? I am sorry I was forced to

[001-218]be so rough with you,” said the lady.

[001-219]“I will; yes, I will,” answered Diamond, holding out both his arms.

[001-220]“But,” he added, dropping them, “how shall I get my clothes? They are in

[001-221]mother's room, and the door is locked.”

[001-222]“Oh, never mind your clothes. You will not be cold. I shall take care of

[001-223]that. Nobody is cold with the north wind.”

[001-224]“I thought everybody was,” said Diamond.

[001-225]“That is a great mistake. Most people make it, however. They are cold

[001-226]because they are not with the north wind, but without it.”

[001-227]If Diamond had been a little older, and had supposed himself a good deal

[001-228]wiser, he would have thought the lady was joking. But he was not older,

[001-229]and did not fancy himself wiser, and therefore understood her well

[001-230]enough. Again he stretched out his arms. The lady's face drew back a

[001-231]little.

[001-232]“Follow me, Diamond,” she said.

[001-233]“Yes,” said Diamond, only a little ruefully.

[001-234]“You're not afraid?” said the North Wind.

[001-235]“No, ma'am; but mother never would let me go without shoes: she never

[001-236]said anything about clothes, so I dare say she wouldn't mind that.”

[001-237]“I know your mother very well,” said the lady. “She is a good woman.

[001-238]I have visited her often. I was with her when you were born. I saw her

[001-239]laugh and cry both at once. I love your mother, Diamond.”

[001-240]“How was it you did not know my name, then, ma'am? Please am I to say

[001-241]ma'am to you, ma'am?”

[001-242]“One question at a time, dear boy. I knew your name quite well, but I

[001-243]wanted to hear what you would say for it. Don't you remember that day

[001-244]when the man was finding fault with your name--how I blew the window

[001-245]in?”

[001-246]“Yes, yes,” answered Diamond, eagerly. “Our window opens like a door,

[001-247]right over the coach-house door. And the wind--you, ma'am--came in, and

[001-248]blew the Bible out of the man's hands, and the leaves went all flutter,

[001-249]flutter on the floor, and my mother picked it up and gave it back to him

[001-250]open, and there----”

[001-251]“Was your name in the Bible--the sixth stone in the high priest's

[001-252]breastplate.”

[001-253]“Oh!--a stone, was it?” said Diamond. “I thought it had been a horse--I

[001-254]did.”

[001-255]“Never mind. A horse is better than a stone any day. Well, you see, I

[001-256]know all about you and your mother.”

[001-257]“Yes. I will go with you.”

[001-258]“Now for the next question: you're not to call me ma'am. You must call

[001-259]me just my own name--respectfully, you know--just North Wind.”

[001-260]“Well, please, North Wind, you are so beautiful, I am quite ready to go

[001-261]with you.”

[001-262]“You must not be ready to go with everything beautiful all at once,

[001-263]Diamond.”

[001-264]“But what's beautiful can't be bad. You're not bad, North Wind?”

[001-265]“No; I'm not bad. But sometimes beautiful things grow bad by doing

[001-266]bad, and it takes some time for their badness to spoil their beauty.

[001-267]So little boys may be mistaken if they go after things because they are

[001-268]beautiful.”

[001-269]“Well, I will go with you because you are beautiful and good, too.”

[001-270]“Ah, but there's another thing, Diamond:--What if I should look ugly

[001-271]without being bad--look ugly myself because I am making ugly things

[001-272]beautiful?--What then?”

[001-273]“I don't quite understand you, North Wind. You tell me what then.”

[001-274]“Well, I will tell you. If you see me with my face all black, don't be

[001-275]frightened. If you see me flapping wings like a bat's, as big as the

[001-276]whole sky, don't be frightened. If you hear me raging ten times worse

[001-277]than Mrs. Bill, the blacksmith's wife--even if you see me looking in at

[001-278]people's windows like Mrs. Eve Dropper, the gardener's wife--you must

[001-279]believe that I am doing my work. Nay, Diamond, if I change into a

[001-280]serpent or a tiger, you must not let go your hold of me, for my hand

[001-281]will never change in yours if you keep a good hold. If you keep a hold,

[001-282]you will know who I am all the time, even when you look at me and can't

[001-283]see me the least like the North Wind. I may look something very awful.

[001-284]Do you understand?”

[001-285]“Quite well,” said little Diamond.

[001-286]“Come along, then,” said North Wind, and disappeared behind the mountain

[001-287]of hay.

[001-288]Diamond crept out of bed and followed her.
