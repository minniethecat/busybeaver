## Overview

North Wind is the central supernatural figure in George MacDonald's 1871 fantasy novel *At the Back of the North Wind*. She is a paradoxical being: a personified force of nature who appears to the child [[diamond|Diamond]] as a beautiful woman of immense and variable stature, with long [[darkness|dark]] hair in which she can carry him. She is at once destructive and loving — she sinks ships and brings storms, yet she is tender, beautiful, and guided by a distant, redemptive "[[music|song]]" that assures her that all suffering is ultimately swallowed up in [[kindness|goodness]].

## Appearance and Character

North Wind's physical form is mutable. When [[diamond|Diamond]] first encounters her, she is small enough to enter through a chink in his hay-loft wall. But when she stands to her full height, "as she grew, still trembling through all her body, her hair also grew — longer and longer, and lifted itself from her head, and went out in black waves" (004-009). She can shrink to the size of a tall woman on a [[london|London]] street, or tower to the [[heaven]]s when she goes about her work.

Her voice, like her form, changes with the scale of her being. When she is immense, her voice "was like the bass of a deep organ" (006-016). When she is in her gentler aspect, she speaks with a "loving" voice that [[diamond|Diamond]] finds comforting.

The narrator does not treat her as simply a personification of wind. She is a being with agency, purpose, and something approaching a moral framework. She tells [[diamond|Diamond]] that she is "only waiting" to set him down before sinking a ship, because she does not want him to hear the cries of the drowning (007-006).

## Role in the Story

North Wind is [[diamond|Diamond]]'s guide into the transcendent. Their relationship begins when she blows through the wall of his hay-loft bedroom, and [[diamond|Diamond]] — after initially trying to block the hole — engages her in conversation. Her first words to him, "What do you mean, little boy — closing up my window?" (001-096), establish her as a being who claims ownership of the natural world.

She carries [[diamond|Diamond]] on her back through the [[darkness|night]] sky, her long hair serving as both cloak and harness. "[[diamond|Diamond]] held on by two of the twisted ropes which, parted and hung down, were strong and thick as cables" (004-031). From this vantage point, [[diamond|Diamond]] witnesses her work — both its [[beauty]] and its [[fear|terror]].

The novel's central theological drama unfolds when North Wind sinks a ship. [[diamond|Diamond]] protests that the drowning people will not hear the distant [[music|song]] that comforts her. She insists: "It must. It must. It wouldn't be the [[music|song]] it seems to be if it did not swallow up all their fear and [[suffering|pain]] too, and set them [[music|singing]] it themselves with the rest" (007-035). This passage encapsulates MacDonald's theodicy — the idea that divine providence works through even the most destructive natural forces toward an ultimate [[music|harmony]].

North Wind also appears in humbler forms. On a [[london|London]] street, she shrinks to human size and walks behind a poor little girl, protecting her from the wind's own blast. [[diamond|Diamond]] recognises her presence: "The same moment North Wind dropt into the street and stood, only a tall lady" (004-113). This duality — the cosmic being who also attends to a single child on a cold street — is central to her character.

## Relationships

- **[[diamond|Diamond]]**: North Wind's relationship with [[diamond|Diamond]] is the heart of the novel. She loves him with a fierce tenderness, calling him "my [[diamond|little Diamond]]" (004-098), and protects him even as she exposes him to the harsh truths of her work. Their bond bridges the gap between the natural and supernatural worlds.
- **The Far-Off [[song|Song]]**: North Wind herself is guided by a distant, redemptive [[music|song]] that she describes as "the sound of a far-off [[music|song]]" (007-019). This [[music|song]] represents a higher order of divine [[music|harmony]] that North Wind serves without fully understanding.

## Themes and Symbolism

North Wind is MacDonald's most ambitious literary creation — a character who embodies the Victorian theological problem of suffering. She is simultaneously the cause of death (shipwreck) and the agent of comfort (warming winds, guiding the poor). MacDonald uses her to argue that suffering and destruction are not contradictions of divine love but instruments within it.

Her femininity is significant. Unlike the stern male God of Victorian ev[[angel]]icalism, North Wind is maternal and beautiful. She presses [[diamond|Diamond]] to her bosom, carries him in her hair, and speaks to him with "loving eyes" (003-242). MacDonald reimagines divine power through a maternal rather than patriarchal lens.

The "back of the north wind" — the peaceful land [[diamond|Diamond]] ultimately reaches at death — represents a state beyond suffering, a paradise that North Wind's work ultimately serves.

## Key Passages

> "Are you cold, North Wind?" he asked. "No, Diamond," she answered, looking down upon him with a smile; "I am never cold." (004-003)

This exchange reveals North Wind's otherness — she is not subject to the same physical laws as mortals.

> "It must. It must," said North Wind, hurriedly. "It wouldn't be the song it seems to be if it did not swallow up all their fear and pain too, and set them singing it themselves with the rest." (007-035)

The novel's central theological statement: suffering is redeemed through a cosmic [[music|harmony]] that mortal ears cannot yet hear.

> She stepped back a step, and at once towered in stature to the height of the houses, and then went striding away after the little girl, with her hair flying out behind. (004-117)

North Wind's tenderness toward a single poor child on a [[london|London]] street illustrates the novel's vision of divine attention to the small and forgotten.
