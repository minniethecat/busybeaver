## Overview

Nanny is a poor crossing-sweeper girl in Victorian [[london|London]] who becomes one of [[diamond|Diamond]]'s closest friends in George MacDonald's *At the Back of [[north-wind|the North Wind]]*. Initially a ragged, dirt-covered street child, she is transformed through [[diamond|Diamond]]'s kindness and [[mr-raymond|Mr. Raymond]]'s patronage into a healthy, happy young woman. Her story illustrates the novel's central theme that love and [[kindness|charity]] can literally remake a person.

## Appearance and Character

When [[diamond|Diamond]] first meets Nanny, she is a pitiable figure — a poor street child who sweeps crossings for coins. She lives with her grandmother, [[sal|Sal]], who treats her cruelly. [[diamond|Diamond]] explains to a gentleman: "her grannie's very cruel to her sometimes" (019-037). Nanny is initially described as desperate and ill, so sick that [[diamond|Diamond]] fears for her life.

But when [[diamond|Diamond]] later visits Nanny after she has been cared for, the transformation is so complete he cannot recognise her. [[mr-raymond|Mr. Raymond]] shows him a healthy, clean young woman, and [[diamond|Diamond]] protests: "That's not Nanny" (027-038). The gentleman replies: "It is Nanny. I have seen her many times since she came here" (027-039). Slowly, "the face of the old Nanny began to dawn through the face of the new" (027-043), and [[diamond|Diamond]] recognises that "that which was Nanny herself dawned upon him more and more" (027-067).

This scene dramatises MacDonald's belief that kindness and proper care do not change a person's essential self but allow it to emerge from beneath the grime of [[suffering|poverty]] and neglect.

## Role in the Story

Nanny serves as the primary object of [[diamond|Diamond]]'s [[kindness|charity]]. When [[diamond|Diamond]] discovers how ill she is, he immediately seeks help from [[mr-raymond|Mr. Raymond]], his gentleman benefactor. [[diamond|Diamond]]'s concern for Nanny reveals his [[kindness|compassion]]ate nature — he is distressed by her suffering and determined to help, even though he has little to give himself.

Nanny's story also serves as a counterpoint to [[diamond|Diamond]]'s. Both are poor children in Victorian [[london|London]], but [[diamond|Diamond]] has loving parents while Nanny has the cruel [[sal|Sal]]. [[diamond|Diamond]]'s family eventually prospers through his work as a cabman and [[mr-raymond|Mr. Raymond]]'s kindness, while Nanny needs direct rescue. The parallel suggests that [[suffering|poverty]] alone is not the problem — it is [[suffering|poverty]] without love.

After her recovery, Nanny becomes a devoted friend to [[diamond|Diamond]]. In later chapters, she is seen interacting with him naturally, asking him about his rooftop excursions: "What are you always going up there for, [[diamond|Diamond]]?" (036-016). Her transformation from desperate street child to healthy, curious young woman is one of the novel's most hopeful arcs.

## Relationships

- **[[diamond|Diamond]]**: The friendship between [[diamond|Diamond]] and Nanny is built on his [[kindness|compassion]] and her gratitude. [[diamond|Diamond]], though himself poor, never hesitates to help her, and she becomes one of his closest companions.
- **[[mr-raymond|Mr. Raymond]]**: The gentleman who takes in Nanny, provides her with medical care and a home, and facilitates her transformation.
- **[[sal|Sal]]**: Nanny's cruel grandmother, who represents the [[darkness|dark]]er side of Victorian [[suffering|poverty]].

## Themes and Symbolism

Nanny's transformation embodies MacDonald's Christian socialism — the belief that material deprivation is not merely an economic problem but a spiritual one, and that acts of [[kindness|charity]] can restore human dignity. Her story echoes the novel's larger theological argument: just as [[north-wind|North Wind]]'s seemingly destructive work serves a higher [[music|harmony]], the apparent hopelessness of Nanny's situation is overcome through love.

MacDonald also uses Nanny to critique the social conditions of Victorian [[london|London]]. The crossing-sweeper — a child who sweeps mud from street crossings for tossed coins — was a real and common figure of urban [[suffering|poverty]]. By making Nanny a full character with an arc of redemption rather than a mere object of pity, MacDonald insists on the full humanity of the urban poor.

## Key Passages

> "her grannie's very cruel to her sometimes, and shuts her out in the streets at night" (019-037)

[[diamond|Diamond]]'s simple, direct description of Nanny's suffering, reported to [[mr-raymond|the gentleman]] whose help he seeks.

> "It is Nanny. I have seen her many times since she came here." (027-039)

[[mr-raymond|Mr. Raymond]]'s gentle insistence that the transformed young woman before [[diamond|Diamond]] is the same person he rescued. This is the novel's clearest statement that [[kindness|charity]] does not change identity — it reveals it.

> "that which was Nanny herself dawned upon him more and more" (027-067)

The narrator's description of [[diamond|Diamond]]'s gradual recognition captures MacDonald's Platonic theology: the true self is eternal and good; suffering merely obscures it.
