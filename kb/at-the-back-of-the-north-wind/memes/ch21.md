CHAPTER XXI. SAL'S NANNY

[021-001]DIAMOND managed with many blunders to read this rhyme to his mother.

[021-002]“Isn't it nice, mother?” he said.

[021-003]“Yes, it's pretty,” she answered.

[021-004]“I think it means something,” returned [[diamond|Diamond]].

[021-005]“I'm sure I don't know what,” she said.

[021-006]“I wonder if it's the same boy--yes, it must be the same--Little Boy

[021-007]Blue, you know. Let me see--how does that rhyme go?

[021-008]Little Boy Blue, come blow me your horn--

[021-009]Yes, of course it is--for this one went `blowing his horn and beating

[021-010]his drum.' He had a drum too.

[021-011]Little Boy Blue, come blow me your horn;

[021-012]The sheep's in the meadow, the cow's in the corn,

[021-013]He had to keep them out, you know. But he wasn't minding his work. It

[021-014]goes--

[021-015]Where's the little boy that looks after the sheep?

[021-016]He's under the haystack, fast asleep.

[021-017]There, you see, mother! And then, let me see--

[021-018]Who'll go and wake him? No, not I;

[021-019]For if I do, he'll be sure to cry.

[021-020]So I suppose nobody did wake him. He was a rather cross little boy,

[021-021]I daresay, when woke up. And when he did wake of himself, and saw the

[021-022]mischief the cow had done to the corn, instead of running home to his

[021-023]mother, he ran away into the wood and lost himself. Don't you think

[021-024]that's very likely, mother?”

[021-025]“I shouldn't wonder,” she answered.

[021-026]“So you see he was naughty; for even when he lost himself he did not

[021-027]want to go home. Any of the creatures would have shown him the way if he

[021-028]had asked it--all but the snake. He followed the snake, you know, and he

[021-029]took him farther away. I suppose it was a young one of the same serpent

[021-030]that tempted Adam and Eve. Father was telling us about it last Sunday,

[021-031]you remember.”

[021-032]“Bless the child!” said his mother to herself; and then added aloud,

[021-033]finding that Diamond did not go on, “Well, what next?”

[021-034]“I don't know, mother. I'm sure there's a great deal more, but what it

[021-035]is I can't say. I only know that he killed the snake. I suppose that's

[021-036]what he had a drumstick for. He couldn't do it with his horn.”

[021-037]“But surely you're not such a silly as to take it all for true,

[021-038]Diamond?”

[021-039]“I think it must be. It looks true. That killing of the snake looks

[021-040]true. It's what I've got to do so often.”

[021-041]His mother looked uneasy. Diamond smiled full in her face, and added--

[021-042]“When baby cries and won't be happy, and when father and you talk about

[021-043]your troubles, I mean.”

[021-044]This did little to reassure his mother; and lest my reader should have

[021-045]his qualms about it too, I venture to remind him once more that Diamond

[021-046]had been to the back of the north wind.

[021-047]Finding she made no reply, Diamond went on--

[021-048]“In a week or so, I shall be able to go to the tall gentleman and tell

[021-049]him I can read. And I'll ask him if he can help me to understand the

[021-050]rhyme.”

[021-051]But before the week was out, he had another reason for going to Mr.

[021-052][[mr-raymond|Raymond]].

[021-053]For three days, on each of which, at one time or other, [[diamonds-father|Diamond's father]]

[021-054]was on the same stand near the National Gallery, the girl was not at her

[021-055]crossing, and Diamond got quite anxious about her, fearing she must be

[021-056]ill. On the fourth day, not seeing her yet, he said to his father, who

[021-057]had that moment shut the door of his cab upon a fare--

[021-058]“Father, I want to go and look after the girl, She can't be well.”

[021-059]“All right,” said his father. “Only take care of yourself, Diamond.”

[021-060]So saying he climbed on his box and drove off.

[021-061]He had great [[trust|confidence]] in his boy, you see, and would trust him

[021-062]anywhere. But if he had known the kind of place in which the girl lived,

[021-063]he would perhaps have thought twice before he allowed him to go alone.

[021-064]Diamond, who did know something of it, had not, however, any fear. From

[021-065]talking to the girl he had a good notion of where about it was, and he

[021-066]remembered the address well enough; so by asking his way some twenty

[021-067]times, mostly of policemen, he came at length pretty near the place. The

[021-068]last policeman he questioned looked down upon him from the summit of six

[021-069]feet two inches, and replied with another question, but kindly:

[021-070]“What do you want there, my small kid? It ain't where you was bred, I

[021-071]guess.”

[021-072]“No sir” answered Diamond. “I live in Bloomsbury.”

[021-073]“That's a long way off,” said the policeman.

[021-074]“Yes, it's a good distance,” answered Diamond; “but I find my way about

[021-075]pretty well. Policemen are always kind to me.”

[021-076]“But what on earth do you want here?”

[021-077]Diamond told him plainly what he was about, and of course the man

[021-078]believed him, for nobody ever disbelieved Diamond. People might think he

[021-079]was mistaken, but they never thought he was telling a story.

[021-080]“It's an ugly place,” said the policeman.

[021-081]“Is it far off?” asked Diamond.

[021-082]“No. It's next door almost. But it's not safe.”

[021-083]“Nobody hurts me,” said Diamond.

[021-084]“I must go with you, I suppose.”

[021-085]“Oh, no! please not,” said Diamond. “They might think I was going to

[021-086]meddle with them, and I ain't, you know.”

[021-087]“Well, do as you please,” said the man, and gave him full directions.

[021-088]Diamond set off, never suspecting that the policeman, who was a

[021-089]kind-hearted man, with children of his own, was following him close, and

[021-090]watching him round every corner. As he went on, all at once he thought

[021-091]he remembered the place, and whether it really was so, or only that

[021-092]he had laid up the policeman's instructions well in his mind, he went

[021-093]straight for the cellar of old [[sal|Sal]].

[021-094]“He's a sharp little kid, anyhow, for as simple as he looks,” said the

[021-095]man to himself. “Not a wrong turn does he take! But old Sal's a rum un

[021-096]for such a child to pay a morning visit to. She's worse when she's sober

[021-097]than when she's half drunk. I've seen her when she'd have torn him in

[021-098]pieces.”

[021-099]Happily then for Diamond, old Sal had gone out to get some gin. When

[021-100]he came to her door at the bottom of the area-stair and knocked, he

[021-101]received no answer. He laid his ear to the door, and thought he heard

[021-102]a moaning within. So he tried the door, and found it was not locked! It

[021-103]was a dreary place indeed,--and very [[darkness|dark]], for the window was below the

[021-104]level of the street, and covered with mud, while over the grating which

[021-105]kept people from falling into the area, stood a chest of drawers, placed

[021-106]there by a dealer in second-hand furniture, which shut out almost all

[021-107]the light. And the smell in the place was dreadful. Diamond stood still

[021-108]for a while, for he could see next to nothing, but he heard the moaning

[021-109]plainly enough now, When he got used to the darkness, he discovered his

[021-110]friend lying with closed eyes and a white suffering face on a heap of

[021-111]little better than rags in a corner of the den. He went up to her and

[021-112]spoke; but she made him no answer. Indeed, she was not in the least

[021-113]aware of his presence, and Diamond saw that he could do nothing for her

[021-114]without help. So taking a lump of barley-sugar from his pocket, which he

[021-115]had bought for her as he came along, and laying it beside her, he

[021-116]left the place, having already made up his mind to go and see the tall

[021-117]gentleman, Mr. Raymond, and ask him to do something for [[nanny|Sal's Nanny]], as

[021-118]the girl was called.

[021-119]By the time he got up the area-steps, three or four women who had seen

[021-120]him go down were standing together at the top waiting for him. They

[021-121]wanted his clothes for their children; but they did not follow him down

[021-122]lest Sal should find them there. The moment he appeared, they laid their

[021-123]hands on him, and all began talking at once, for each wanted to get some

[021-124]advantage over her neighbours. He told them quite quietly, for he was

[021-125]not frightened, that he had come to see what was the matter with Nanny.

[021-126]“What do you know about Nanny?” said one of them fiercely. “Wait till

[021-127]old Sal comes home, and you'll catch it, for going prying into her house

[021-128]when she's out. If you don't give me your jacket directly, I'll go and

[021-129]fetch her.”

[021-130]“I can't give you my jacket,” said Diamond. “It belongs to my father and

[021-131]mother, you know. It's not mine to give. Is it now? You would not think

[021-132]it right to give away what wasn't yours--would you now?”

[021-133]“Give it away! No, that I wouldn't; I'd keep it,” she said, with a rough

[021-134]laugh. “But if the jacket ain't yours, what right have you to keep it?

[021-135]Here, Cherry, make haste. It'll be one go apiece.”

[021-136]They all began to tug at the jacket, while Diamond stooped and kept his

[021-137]arms bent to resist them. Before they had done him or the jacket any

[021-138]harm, however, suddenly they all scampered away; and Diamond, looking in

[021-139]the opposite direction, saw the tall policeman coming towards him.

[021-140]“You had better have let me come with you, little man,” he said, looking

[021-141]down in Diamond's face, which was flushed with his resistance.

[021-142]“You came just in the right time, thank you,” returned Diamond. “They've

[021-143]done me no harm.”

[021-144]“They would have if I hadn't been at hand, though.”

[021-145]“Yes; but you were at hand, you know, so they couldn't.”

[021-146]Perhaps the answer was deeper in purport than either Diamond or the

[021-147]policeman knew. They walked away together, Diamond telling his new

[021-148]friend how ill poor Nanny was, and that he was going to let the tall

[021-149]gentleman know. The policeman put him in the nearest way for Bloomsbury,

[021-150]and stepping out in good earnest, Diamond reached Mr. Raymond's door

[021-151]in less than an hour. When he asked if he was at home, the servant, in

[021-152]return, asked what he wanted.

[021-153]“I want to tell him something.”

[021-154]“But I can't go and trouble him with such a message as that.”

[021-155]“He told me to come to him--that is, when I could read--and I can.”

[021-156]“How am I to know that?”

[021-157]Diamond stared with astonishment for one moment, then answered:

[021-158]“Why, I've just told you. That's how you know it.”

[021-159]But this man was made of coarser grain than the policeman, and, instead

[021-160]of seeing that Diamond could not tell a lie, he put his answer down as

[021-161]impudence, and saying, “Do you think I'm going to take your word for

[021-162]it?” shut the door in his face.

[021-163]Diamond turned and sat down on the doorstep, thinking with himself that

[021-164]the tall gentleman must either come in or come out, and he was therefore

[021-165]in the best possible position for finding him. He had not waited long

[021-166]before the door opened again; but when he looked round, it was only the

[021-167]servant once more.

[021-168]“Get, away” he said. “What are you doing on the doorstep?”

[021-169]“Waiting for Mr. Raymond,” answered Diamond, getting up.

[021-170]“He's not at home.”

[021-171]“Then I'll wait till he comes,” returned Diamond, sitting down again

[021-172]with a smile.

[021-173]What the man would have done next I do not know, but a step sounded from

[021-174]the hall, and when Diamond looked round yet again, there was the tall

[021-175]gentleman.

[021-176]“Who's this, John?” he asked.

[021-177]“I don't know, sir. An imperent little boy as will sit on the doorstep.”

[021-178]“Please sir” said Diamond, “he told me you weren't at home, and I sat

[021-179]down to wait for you.”

[021-180]“Eh, what!” said Mr. Raymond. “John! John! This won't do. Is it a habit

[021-181]of yours to turn away my visitors? There'll be some one else to turn

[021-182]away, I'm afraid, if I find any more of this kind of thing. Come in, my

[021-183]little man. I suppose you've come to claim your sixpence?”

[021-184]“No, sir, not that.”

[021-185]“What! can't you read yet?”

[021-186]“Yes, I can now, a little. But I'll come for that next time. I came to

[021-187]tell you about Sal's Nanny.”

[021-188]“Who's Sal's Nanny?”

[021-189]“The girl at the crossing you talked to the same day.”

[021-190]“Oh, yes; I remember. What's the matter? Has she got run over?”

[021-191]Then Diamond told him all.

[021-192]Now Mr. Raymond was one of the kindest men in [[london|London]]. He sent at once to

[021-193]have the horse put to the brougham, took Diamond with him, and drove to

[021-194]the Children's Hospital. There he was well known to everybody, for he

[021-195]was not only a large subscriber, but he used to go and tell the children

[021-196]stories of an afternoon. One of the doctors promised to go and find

[021-197]Nanny, and do what could be done--have her brought to the hospital, if

[021-198]possible.

[021-199]That same night they sent a litter for her, and as she could be of no

[021-200]use to old Sal until she was better, she did not object to having her

[021-201]removed. So she was soon lying in the fever ward--for the first time in

[021-202]her life in a nice clean bed. But she knew nothing of the whole affair.

[021-203]She was too ill to know anything.
