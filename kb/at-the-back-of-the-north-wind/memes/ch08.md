CHAPTER VIII. THE EAST WINDOW

[008-001]THAT [[diamond|Diamond]] had fallen fast asleep is very evident from the strange

[008-002]things he now fancied as taking place. For he thought he heard a sound

[008-003]as of whispering up in the great window. He tried to open his eyes, but

[008-004]he could not. And the whispering went on and grew louder and louder,

[008-005]until he could hear every word that was said. He thought it was the

[008-006]Apostles talking about him. But he could not open his eyes.

[008-007]“And how comes he to be lying there, St. Peter?” said one.

[008-008]“I think I saw him a while ago up in the gallery, under the Nicodemus

[008-009]window. Perhaps he has fallen down.

[008-010]“What do you think, St. Matthew?”

[008-011]“I don't think he could have crept here after falling from such a

[008-012]height. He must have been killed.”

[008-013]“What are we to do with him? We can't leave him lying there. And we

[008-014]could not make him comfortable up here in the window: it's rather

[008-015]crowded already. What do you say, St. Thomas?”

[008-016]“Let's go down and look at him.”

[008-017]There came a rustling, and a chinking, for some time, and then there was

[008-018]a silence, and Diamond felt somehow that all the Apostles were standing

[008-019]round him and looking down on him. And still he could not open his eyes.

[008-020]“What is the matter with him, St. Luke?” asked one.

[008-021]“There's nothing the matter with him,” answered St. Luke, who must

[008-022]have joined the company of the Apostles from the next window, one would

[008-023]think. “He's in a sound [[dream|sleep]].”

[008-024]“I have it,” cried another. “This is one of [[north-wind|North Wind]]'s tricks. She

[008-025]has caught him up and dropped him at our door, like a withered leaf or a

[008-026]foundling baby. I don't understand that woman's conduct, I must say. As

[008-027]if we hadn't enough to do with our money, without going taking care

[008-028]of other people's children! That's not what our forefathers built

[008-029]cathedrals for.”

[008-030]Now Diamond could not bear to hear such things against North Wind, who,

[008-031]he knew, never played anybody a trick. She was far too busy with her own

[008-032]work for that. He struggled hard to open his eyes, but without success.

[008-033]“She should consider that a church is not a place for pranks, not to

[008-034]mention that we live in it,” said another.

[008-035]“It certainly is disrespectful of her. But she always is disrespectful.

[008-036]What right has she to bang at our windows as she has been doing the

[008-037]whole of this [[darkness|night]]? I daresay there is glass broken somewhere. I know

[008-038]my blue robe is in a dreadful mess with the rain first and the dust

[008-039]after. It will cost me shillings to clean it.”

[008-040]Then Diamond knew that they could not be Apostles, talking like this.

[008-041]They could only be the sextons and vergers and such-like, who got up at

[008-042]night, and put on the robes of deans and bishops, and called each other

[008-043]grand names, as the foolish servants he had heard his father tell of

[008-044]call themselves lords and ladies, after their masters and mistresses.

[008-045]And he was so angry at their daring to abuse North Wind, that he jumped

[008-046]up, crying--“North Wind knows best what she is about. She has a good

[008-047]right to blow the cobwebs from your windows, for she was sent to do it.

[008-048]She sweeps them away from grander places, I can tell you, for I've been

[008-049]with her at it.”

[008-050]This was what he began to say, but as he spoke his eyes came wide open,

[008-051]and behold, there were neither Apostles nor vergers there--not even a

[008-052]window with the effigies of holy men in it, but a dark heap of hay all

[008-053]about him, and the little panes in the roof of his loft glimmering blue

[008-054]in the light of the morning. Old Diamond was coming awake down below in

[008-055]the stable. In a moment more he was on his feet, and shaking himself so

[008-056]that young Diamond's bed trembled under him.

[008-057]“He's grand at shaking himself,” said Diamond. “I wish I could shake

[008-058]myself like that. But then I can wash myself, and he can't. What fun

[008-059]it would be to see Old Diamond washing his face with his hoofs and iron

[008-060]shoes! Wouldn't it be a picture?”

[008-061]So saying, he got up and dressed himself. Then he went out into the

[008-062]garden. There must have been a tremendous wind in the night, for

[008-063]although all was quiet now, there lay the little summer-house crushed

[008-064]to the ground, and over it the great elm-tree, which the wind had broken

[008-065]across, being much decayed in the middle. Diamond almost cried to see

[008-066]the wilderness of green leaves, which used to be so far up in the blue

[008-067]air, tossing about in the breeze, and liking it best when the wind blew

[008-068]it most, now lying so near the ground, and without any hope of ever

[008-069]getting up into the deep air again.

[008-070]“I wonder how old the tree is!” thought Diamond. “It must take a long

[008-071]time to get so near the sky as that poor tree was.”

[008-072]“Yes, indeed,” said a voice beside him, for Diamond had spoken the last

[008-073]words aloud.

[008-074]Diamond started, and looking around saw a clergyman, a brother of Mrs.

[008-075]Coleman, who happened to be visiting her. He was a great scholar, and

[008-076]was in the habit of rising early.

[008-077]“Who are you, my man?” he added.

[008-078]“Little Diamond,” answered the boy.

[008-079]“Oh! I have heard of you. How do you come to be up so early?”

[008-080]“Because the sham Apostles talked such nonsense, they waked me up.”

[008-081]The clergyman stared. Diamond saw that he had better have held his

[008-082]tongue, for he could not explain things.

[008-083]“You must have been dreaming, my little man,” said he. “Dear! dear!” he

[008-084]went on, looking at the tree, “there has been terrible work here. This

[008-085]is the north wind's doing. What a pity! I wish we lived at the back of

[008-086]it, I'm sure.”

[008-087]“Where is that sir?” asked Diamond.

[008-088]“Away in the Hyperborean regions,” answered the clergyman, smiling.

[008-089]“I never heard of the place,” returned Diamond.

[008-090]“I daresay not,” answered the clergyman; “but if this tree had been

[008-091]there now, it would not have been blown down, for there is no wind

[008-092]there.”

[008-093]“But, please, sir, if it had been there,” said Diamond, “we should not

[008-094]have had to be sorry for it.”

[008-095]“Certainly not.”

[008-096]“Then we shouldn't have had to be glad for it, either.”

[008-097]“You're quite right, my boy,” said the clergyman, looking at him very

[008-098]kindly, as he turned away to the house, with his eyes bent towards the

[008-099]earth. But Diamond thought within himself, “I will ask North Wind next

[008-100]time I see her to take me to that country. I think she did speak about

[008-101]it once before.”
