CHAPTER XXXII. DIAMOND AND RUBY

[032-001]IT WAS Friday [[darkness|night]], and [[diamond|Diamond]], like the rest of the household, had

[032-002]had very little to eat that day. The mother would always pay the week's

[032-003]rent before she laid out anything even on food. His father had been very

[032-004]gloomy--so gloomy that he had actually been cross to his wife. It is

[032-005]a strange thing how [[suffering|pain]] of seeing the suffering of those we love will

[032-006]sometimes make us add to their suffering by being cross with them. This

[032-007]comes of not having [[trust|faith]] enough in God, and shows how necessary this

[032-008]faith is, for when we lose it, we lose even the kindness which alone can

[032-009]soothe the suffering. Diamond in consequence had gone to bed very quiet

[032-010]and thoughtful--a little troubled indeed.

[032-011]It had been a very stormy winter, and even now that the spring had come,

[032-012]the north wind often blew. When Diamond went to his bed, which was in

[032-013]a tiny room in the roof, he heard it like the sea moaning; and when he

[032-014]fell asleep he still heard the moaning. All at once he said to himself,

[032-015]“Am I awake, or am I asleep?” But he had no time to answer the question,

[032-016]for there was [[north-wind|North Wind]] calling him. His heart beat very fast, it was

[032-017]such a long time since he had heard that voice. He jumped out of bed,

[032-018]and looked everywhere, but could not see her. “Diamond, come here,” she

[032-019]said again and again; but where the here was he could not tell. To be

[032-020]sure the room was all but quite dark, and she might be close beside him.

[032-021]“Dear North Wind,” said Diamond, “I want so much to go to you, but I

[032-022]can't tell where.”

[032-023]“Come here, Diamond,” was all her answer.

[032-024]Diamond opened the door, and went out of the room, and down the stair

[032-025]and into the yard. His little heart was in a flutter, for he had long

[032-026]given up all thought of seeing her again. Neither now was he to see her.

[032-027]When he got out, a great puff of wind came against him, and in obedience

[032-028]to it he turned his back, and went as it blew. It blew him right up to

[032-029]the stable-door, and went on blowing.

[032-030]“She wants me to go into the stable,” said Diamond to himself, “but the

[032-031]door is locked.”

[032-032]He knew where the key was, in a certain hole in the wall--far too high

[032-033]for him to get at. He ran to the place, however: just as he reached it

[032-034]there came a wild blast, and down fell the key clanging on the stones at

[032-035]his feet. He picked it up, and ran back and opened the stable-door, and

[032-036]went in. And what do you think he saw?

[032-037]A little light came through the dusty window from a gas-lamp, sufficient

[032-038]to show him Diamond and Ruby with their two heads up, looking at each

[032-039]other across the partition of their stalls. The light showed the white

[032-040]mark on Diamond's forehead, but Ruby's eye shone so bright, that he

[032-041]thought more light came out of it than went in. This is what he saw.

[032-042]But what do you think he heard?

[032-043]He heard the two horses talking to each other--in a strange language,

[032-044]which yet, somehow or other, he could understand, and turn over in

[032-045]his mind in English. The first words he heard were from Diamond, who

[032-046]apparently had been already quarrelling with Ruby.

[032-047]“Look how fat you are Ruby!” said old Diamond. “You are so plump and

[032-048]your skin shines so, you ought to be ashamed of yourself.”

[032-049]“There's no harm in being fat,” said Ruby in a deprecating tone. “No,

[032-050]nor in being sleek. I may as well shine as not.”

[032-051]“No harm?” retorted Diamond. “Is it no harm to go eating up all poor

[032-052]master's oats, and taking up so much of his time grooming you, when you

[032-053]only work six hours--no, not six hours a day, and, as I hear, get along

[032-054]no faster than a big dray-horse with two tons behind him?--So they tell

[032-055]me.”

[032-056]“Your master's not mine,” said Ruby. “I must attend to my own master's

[032-057]interests, and eat all that is given me, and be sleek and fat as I can,

[032-058]and go no faster than I need.”

[032-059]“Now really if the rest of the horses weren't all asleep, poor

[032-060]things--they work till they're tired--I do believe they would get up and

[032-061]kick you out of the stable. You make me ashamed of being a horse. You

[032-062]dare to say my master ain't your master! That's your gratitude for the

[032-063]way he feeds you and spares you! Pray where would your carcass be if it

[032-064]weren't for him?”

[032-065]“He doesn't do it for my sake. If I were his own horse, he would work me

[032-066]as hard as he does you.”

[032-067]“And I'm proud to be so worked. I wouldn't be as fat as you--not for all

[032-068]you're worth. You're a disgrace to the stable. Look at the horse next

[032-069]you. He's something like a horse--all skin and bone. And his master

[032-070]ain't over kind to him either. He put a stinging lash on his whip

[032-071]last week. But that old horse knows he's got the wife and children

[032-072]to keep--as well as his drunken master--and he works like a horse. I

[032-073]daresay he grudges his master the beer he drinks, but I don't believe he

[032-074]grudges anything else.”

[032-075]“Well, I don't grudge yours what he gets by me,” said Ruby.

[032-076]“Gets!” retorted Diamond. “What he gets isn't worth grudging. It comes

[032-077]to next to nothing--what with your fat and shine.

[032-078]“Well, at least you ought to be thankful you're the better for it. You

[032-079]get a two hours' rest a day out of it.”

[032-080]“I thank my master for that--not you, you lazy fellow! You go along like

[032-081]a buttock of beef upon castors--you do.”

[032-082]“Ain't you afraid I'll kick, if you go on like that, Diamond?”

[032-083]“Kick! You couldn't kick if you tried. You might heave your rump up

[032-084]half a foot, but for lashing out--oho! If you did, you'd be down on your

[032-085]belly before you could get your legs under you again. It's my belief,

[032-086]once out, they'd stick out for ever. Talk of kicking! Why don't you put

[032-087]one foot before the other now and then when you're in the cab? The abuse

[032-088]master gets for your sake is quite shameful. No decent horse would bring

[032-089]it on him. Depend upon it, Ruby, no cabman likes to be abused any more

[032-090]than his fare. But his fares, at least when you are between the shafts,

[032-091]are very much to be excused. Indeed they are.”

[032-092]“Well, you see, Diamond, I don't want to go lame again.”

[032-093]“I don't believe you were so very lame after all--there!”

[032-094]“Oh, but I was.”

[032-095]“Then I believe it was all your own fault. I'm not lame. I never was

[032-096]lame in all my life. You don't take care of your legs. You never lay

[032-097]them down at night. There you are with your huge carcass crushing down

[032-098]your poor legs all night long. You don't even care for your own legs--so

[032-099]long as you can eat, eat, and [[dream|sleep]], sleep. You a horse indeed!”

[032-100]“But I tell you I was lame.”

[032-101]“I'm not denying there was a puffy look about your off-pastern. But my

[032-102]belief is, it wasn't even grease--it was fat.”

[032-103]“I tell you I put my foot on one of those horrid stones they make the

[032-104]roads with, and it gave my ankle such a twist.”

[032-105]“Ankle indeed! Why should you ape your betters? Horses ain't got any

[032-106]ankles: they're only pasterns. And so long as you don't lift your feet

[032-107]better, but fall asleep between every step, you'll run a good chance

[032-108]of laming all your ankles as you call them, one after another. It's not

[032-109]your lively horse that comes to grief in that way. I tell you I believe

[032-110]it wasn't much, and if it was, it was your own fault. There! I've done.

[032-111]I'm going to sleep. I'll try to think as well of you as I can. If you

[032-112]would but step out a bit and run off a little of your fat!” Here Diamond

[032-113]began to double up his knees; but Ruby spoke again, and, as young

[032-114]Diamond thought, in a rather different tone.

[032-115]“I say, Diamond, I can't bear to have an honest old horse like you think

[032-116]of me like that. I will tell you the truth: it was my own fault that I

[032-117]fell lame.”

[032-118]“I told you so,” returned the other, tumbling against the partition as

[032-119]he rolled over on his side to give his legs every possible privilege in

[032-120]their narrow circumstances.

[032-121]“I meant to do it, Diamond.”

[032-122]At the words, the old horse arose with a scramble like thunder, shot his

[032-123]angry head and glaring eye over into Ruby's stall, and said--

[032-124]“Keep out of my way, you unworthy wretch, or I'll bite you. You a horse!

[032-125]Why did you do that?”

[032-126]“Because I wanted to grow fat.”

[032-127]“You grease-tub! Oh! my teeth and tail! I thought you were a humbug! Why

[032-128]did you want to get fat? There's no truth to be got out of you but by

[032-129]cross-questioning. You ain't fit to be a horse.”

[032-130]“Because once I am fat, my nature is to keep fat for a long time; and I

[032-131]didn't know when master might come home and want to see me.”

[032-132]“You conceited, good-for-nothing brute! You're only fit for the

[032-133]knacker's yard. You wanted to look handsome, did you? Hold your tongue,

[032-134]or I'll break my halter and be at you--with your handsome fat!”

[032-135]“Never mind, Diamond. You're a good horse. You can't hurt me.”

[032-136]“Can't hurt you! Just let me once try.”

[032-137]“No, you can't.”

[032-138]“Why then?”

[032-139]“Because I'm an [[angel]].”

[032-140]“What's that?”

[032-141]“Of course you don't know.”

[032-142]“Indeed I don't.”

[032-143]“I know you don't. An ignorant, rude old human horse, like you, couldn't

[032-144]know it. But there's young Diamond listening to all we're saying; and he

[032-145]knows well enough there are horses in [[heaven]] for angels to ride upon,

[032-146]as well as other animals, lions and eagles and bulls, in more important

[032-147]situations. The horses the angels ride, must be angel-horses, else the

[032-148]angels couldn't ride upon them. Well, I'm one of them.”

[032-149]“You ain't.”

[032-150]“Did you ever know a horse tell a lie?”

[032-151]“Never before. But you've confessed to shamming lame.”

[032-152]“Nothing of the sort. It was necessary I should grow fat, and necessary

[032-153]that good [[diamonds-father|Joseph]], your master, should grow lean. I could have pretended

[032-154]to be lame, but that no horse, least of all an angel-horse would do. So

[032-155]I must be lame, and so I sprained my ankle--for the angel-horses have

[032-156]ankles--they don't talk horse-slang up there--and it hurt me very much,

[032-157]I assure you, Diamond, though you mayn't be good enough to be able to

[032-158]believe it.”

[032-159]Old Diamond made no reply. He had lain down again, and a sleepy snort,

[032-160]very like a snore, revealed that, if he was not already asleep, he was

[032-161]past understanding a word that Ruby was saying. When young Diamond found

[032-162]this, he thought he might venture to take up the dropt shuttlecock of

[032-163]the conversation.

[032-164]“I'm good enough to believe it, Ruby,” he said.

[032-165]But Ruby never turned his head, or took any notice of him. I suppose

[032-166]he did not understand more of English than just what the coachman and

[032-167]stableman were in the habit of addressing him with. Finding, however,

[032-168]that his companion made no reply, he shot his head over the partition

[032-169]and looking down at him said--

[032-170]“You just wait till to-morrow, and you'll see whether I'm speaking the

[032-171]truth or not.--I declare the old horse is fast asleep!--Diamond!--No I

[032-172]won't.”

[032-173]Ruby turned away, and began pulling at his hayrack in silence.

[032-174]Diamond gave a shiver, and looking round saw that the door of the stable

[032-175]was open. He began to feel as if he had been dreaming, and after a

[032-176]glance about the stable to see if North Wind was anywhere visible, he

[032-177]thought he had better go back to bed.
