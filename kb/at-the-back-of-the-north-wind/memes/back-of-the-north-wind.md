## Definition

The Back of the North Wind is the transcendent realm that lies beyond the reach of [[north-wind|the North Wind]]'s power — a country of perfect peace, still light, and a [[music|singing]] [[the-river|river]], accessible to [[diamond|Diamond]] through his supernatural journeys and ultimately his death. It is George MacDonald's vision of paradise, rendered not as a doctrinal [[heaven]] but as a liminal country at the edge of the natural world, accessible to children, dreamers, and those who, like [[diamond|Diamond]], possess "childlike [[trust|faith]]."

## In the Novel

The novel's title names this place as its destination, and the entire narrative is structured as a journey toward it. [[diamond|Diamond]] first hears of it when he meets [[north-wind|the North Wind]], and he finally reaches it in Chapter X after falling from her back.

MacDonald deliberately complicates the description of the place by offering multiple testimonies. The narrator admits: "I have now come to the most difficult part of my story... I do not know enough about it" (010-001). He then presents three accounts, each from a different source, each describing the same [[truth|reality]] in different terms.

The Italian poet Dante (Durante) entered "through a fire so hot that he would have thrown himself into boiling glass to cool himself" (010-023), describing a land where "a gentle, even-tempered wind, which never blew faster or slower, breathed in his face" (010-031) and "every one of them has a crown like a king and a mitre like a priest" (010-045).

The Scottish peasant girl Kilmeny "fell fast a[[dream|sleep]] in a wood, and woke in the same country" (010-029), a land "where the cock never crew, where the rain never fell, and the wind never blew" (010-052), "a land of love and a land of light, withouten sun, or [[moon]], or [[darkness|night]]" (010-058).

[[diamond|Diamond]]'s own account is simpler, more childlike. He found himself there after falling: "[[north-wind|North Wind]] herself was nowhere to be seen. Neither was there a vestige of snow or of ice within sight" (010-070). The light was "a certain still rayless light" (010-074) that "belonged to the country itself" (010-075). A [[the-river|river]] flowed "not only through, but over grass" (010-078), and it "sung [[music|tune]]s in people's heads" (010-081). Crucially, [[diamond|Diamond]] reports: "he was not very happy there" in the conventional sense — he missed his parents — but "he felt so still and quiet and patient and contented, that, as far as the mere feeling went, it was something better than mere [[joy|happiness]]" (010-089).

The novel's final sentence returns to this image: "I knew that he had gone to the back of the north wind" (038-061). [[diamond|Diamond]]'s physical death is revealed not as tragedy but as arrival.

## Key Passages

> "Nothing went wrong at the back of the north wind. Neither was anything quite right, he thought. Only everything was going to be right some day." (010-091)

[[diamond|Diamond]]'s childlike report captures the eschatological tension of MacDonald's paradise — it is not yet the final perfection, but it is the place where everything tends toward rightness.

> "he felt so still and quiet and patient and contented, that, as far as the mere feeling went, it was something better than mere happiness" (010-089)

MacDonald distinguishes between [[joy|happiness]] (an emotion) and a deeper state of peace that transcends emotion. This is the novel's most important distinction between earthly and [[heaven]]ly experience.

> "I knew that he had gone to the back of the north wind." (038-061)

The narrator's claim of knowledge, not belief, reframes death as a movement from one real place to another. [[diamond|Diamond]] has been visiting this country all along; death is simply the journey made permanent.

## Related Characters

- **[[diamond|Diamond]]**: The child who visits the back of the north wind in his journeys and finally arrives there at death. [[diamond|Diamond]] is the novel's witness that the country is real.
- **[[north-wind|North Wind]]**: She cannot enter the back of the north wind herself — there is no wind there. She is the guide who brings [[diamond|Diamond]] to its threshold but cannot cross it.

## Analysis

The multiple testimonies that MacDonald uses to describe the back of the north wind — Dante, Kilmeny, [[diamond|Diamond]] — constitute a deliberate theological method. By presenting three incompatible descriptions of the same [[truth|reality]] and insisting that all are true from their different perspectives, MacDonald argues that transcendent truth cannot be captured by any single doctrinal formulation. The Catholic poet, the Scottish peasant, and the little English boy all see the same thing, each according to their capacity.

This is MacDonald's version of the *via negativa*: paradise cannot be described, only approached through the diverse witness of those who have glimpsed it. The "still rayless light" (010-074), the [[the-river|river]] that "sung [[music|tune]]s in people's heads" (010-081), the complete absence of wind — these are not literal descriptions but imaginative gestures toward the indescribable.

MacDonald's paradise is notably non-hierarchical. In Dante's account, every person wears "a crown... and a mitre" (010-045) — the symbols of king and priest are universal, not restricted to an elect. This democratic vision of [[heaven]] reflects MacDonald's Christian universalism, his belief that all souls will ultimately be saved.
