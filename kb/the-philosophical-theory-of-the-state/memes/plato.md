# Plato

Plato (c. 428–348 BCE) is the originating figure of the idealist tradition in political philosophy. His vision of the state as the embodiment of reason—expressed most famously in the _[[republic|Republic]]_—provides the classical foundation for the tradition that runs through [[jean-jacques-rousseau|Rousseau]] and [[g-w-f-hegel|Hegel]] to [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]].

## Plato in Bosanquet's Theory

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] treats Plato not as a historical curiosity but as a living philosophical presence. The [[greece|Greek]] conception of the polis as an ethical community (001-011), articulated most fully by Plato and [[aristotle|Aristotle]], is the starting point for [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]]'s own account of the state as the realization of the common good (001-024).

### The Rational State

Plato's central thesis—that the state should be governed by reason, not by appetite or force—is the guiding idea of [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]]'s political philosophy. [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] shares Plato's conviction that political life has an objective rational structure that philosophy can discern. The [[general-will|general will]], in [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]]'s reading of [[jean-jacques-rousseau|Rousseau]], is a modern reformulation of Plato's insight that the true interest of the [[individuality|individual]] coincides with the common good.

### The Limits of the Greek Polis

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] acknowledges the limitations of the [[greece|Greek city-state]] as a political form. The [[greece|Greek]]s "never worked through to the idea that true sovereignty lay in the dominion of a common law or a common [[real-will|rational will]]" in the fully developed modern sense (001-024). The [[greece|Greek]] polis was particularistic, exclusive, and ultimately unable to sustain itself against larger political forces. Yet its philosophical achievement—the idea that the state is an ethical community—remains foundational.
