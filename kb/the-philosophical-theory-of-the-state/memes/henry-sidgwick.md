# Henry Sidgwick

Henry Sidgwick (1838–1900) was Knightbridge Professor of Moral Philosophy at Cambridge and a central figure in late Victorian ethical thought. His _Methods of Ethics_ (1874) is widely regarded as the most sophisticated statement of classical utilitarianism. In _The Philosophical Theory of [[state|the State]]_, [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] cites Sidgwick alongside Ritchie as a notable exception to the general tendency of [[england|English]] ethical thought to treat [[individuality|individual]] and social good as externally related (004-022).

## Core Ideas in Bosanquet's Reading

### The Simplification of Ethical Theory

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] notes that Sidgwick "has simplified an ethical problem" by reducing it to a clash between two principles (002-053). This simplification, while analytically valuable, is treated by [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] with philosophical reserve. The tendency to reduce ethical dilemmas to binary oppositions — egoism versus altruism, self versus other — is, for [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]], symptomatic of a deeper failure to grasp the organic unity of [[individuality|individual]] and social good. Sidgwick's analytical clarity thus paradoxically reveals the limits of the analytical method itself when applied to ethical life.

### Individual and Social Good

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] groups Sidgwick with Ritchie as philosophers who recognise, more than most [[england|English]] thinkers, the internal relation between [[individuality|individual]] welfare and the common good (004-022). Sidgwick's utilitarianism, while not sharing the metaphysical apparatus of [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]]'s idealism, arrives at a structurally similar conclusion: the good of the [[individuality|individual]] cannot be coherently specified in abstraction from the good of the social whole. This convergence between utilitarian and idealist [[morality|ethics]] is significant for [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]]'s broader argument that philosophical traditions normally considered antithetical may, at their best, converge on common truths.

## Bosanquet's Assessment

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]]'s treatment of Sidgwick is respectful but qualified. Sidgwick is acknowledged as a rigorous thinker whose ethical analyses command attention, but his utilitarianism — like all empiricist ethical theories — lacks the metaphysical foundation that idealism provides. For [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]], Sidgwick's [[morality|ethics]], however refined, remains an [[morality|ethics]] of consequences rather than an [[morality|ethics]] of self-realisation. It tells us what actions tend to produce good outcomes but cannot tell us what the good _is_ in terms of the rational structure of human nature.
