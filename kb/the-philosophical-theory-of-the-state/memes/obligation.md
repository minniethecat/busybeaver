# Obligation

In [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]]'s _Philosophical Theory of [[state|the State]]_, **Obligation** — particularly political obligation — is not a burden externally imposed upon the [[individuality|individual]] but a rational necessity that arises from the nature of [[will|the will]] itself. The [[individuality|individual]] is obligated to [[state|the State]] because [[state|the State]] embodies the conditions of his own rational [[liberty|freedom]].

## The Paradox of Obligation

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] identifies the "paradox of obligation in its purest form" (003-004) as the central puzzle of political philosophy: how can a free being be bound? The answer, he argues, lies in understanding that the obligation is not to an alien power but to the rational structure of one's own will:

> We have the paradox of obligation in its purest form. As applied to the State, it means that the individual is bound by a law which is yet the expression of his own will. (003-004)

This paradox is resolved when we distinguish the [[real-will|actual will]] (momentary desire) from the real will (rational self-direction). Obligation is the demand of the real will upon the [[real-will|actual will]].

## Obligation and the Common Good

For [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]], obligation is inseparable from the idea of the common good. An obligation is not merely a command backed by force but a requirement rooted in the rational structure of communal life:

> It is less bound up with social obligations — as for the denying spirits, they deny the very idea of a common good, and to break an obligation... with less than this there is no political society. (002-019, 002-045)

The "facts of law and recognised obligation" (002-039) are more fundamental than explicit consent or contract. They constitute the [[morality|moral]] fabric of society, prior to and more durable than any [[individuality|individual]] act of agreement.

## The Ground of Political Obligation

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] locates "the root and ground of political obligation" (003-003) in the [[individuality|individual]]'s membership in a rational community:

> The phenomena of political obligation reveal that man is not first an individual and then a member of society, but finds himself always already bound by ties that constitute his very selfhood. (003-002, 003-003)

This is not a denial of [[liberty|freedom]] but its true foundation. The [[individuality|individual]] who recognises his obligations to [[state|the State]] is not enslaved but liberated — because those obligations are the expression of his own rational nature.

## Moral and Legal Obligation

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] distinguishes between [[morality|moral]] and legal obligation while insisting on their interconnection:

| Type of Obligation | Source | Enforcement |
|-------------------|--------|------------|
| Moral | Rational will, conscience | Internal ([[autonomy|self-legislation]]) |
| Legal | [[state|State]], [[general-will|common will]] | External (coercion by community) |

Yet he argues that "[[morality|moral obligation]]s ought to be enforced by [[state|the State]]" (003-031) in certain cases — for instance, the obligation to support one's family. The boundary between [[morality|moral]] and legal obligation is not fixed but depends on what the common good requires at a given stage of civilisation.

## Obligation and the Real Will

The concept of obligation ultimately leads back to [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]]'s theory of [[will|the will]]. The [[individuality|individual]] is obligated because his real will — what he would will if he were fully rational — coincides with the [[general-will|General Will]] expressed in the laws of [[state|the State]]:

> The varied play of relations and obligations in society... lies in a something deeper than any explicit contract — in the logic of the will itself. (003-016)
