# Social Organism

In the final chapter of _The Philosophical Theory of [[state|the State]]_, [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] addresses the **organic theory of the state**—the idea that the [[community|political community]] is not a mere aggregate of [[individuality|individual]]s but a unified whole analogous to a living organism (011-003). He defends a qualified version of this theory while distinguishing it from the biological determinism of [[herbert-spencer|Herbert Spencer]].

## The Organism Analogy

The analogy between the state and a living organism has ancient roots—[[plato|Plato]] compared the polis to the human soul, and [[aristotle|Aristotle]] described the state as prior to the [[individuality|individual]] as the whole is prior to the part (011-002). In the modern period, the organic analogy was revived as a counter to the mechanistic [[individuality|individual]]ism of [[thomas-hobbes|Hobbes]] and [[john-locke|Locke]], which treated society as an artificial construction of pre-existing [[individuality|individual]]s.

## Bosanquet's Qualified Defense

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] defends the organic theory against the charge that it subordinates the [[individuality|individual]] to the collective. For him, the organic character of the state means not that [[individuality|individual]]s are mere cells in a larger body but that their [[individuality|individual]]ity is constituted through their participation in the social whole. The state is an organism of minds, not of bodies—its unity is a unity of consciousness, not of biological function.

## Critique of Spencer

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] distinguishes his position from [[herbert-spencer|Herbert Spencer]]'s evolutionary organicism. [[herbert-spencer|Spencer]] treated society as a literal organism subject to biological laws of development. [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] rejects this reduction: the state operates at the level of mind and will, not biological instinct. The organic analogy illuminates the interdependence of [[individuality|individual]]s within the state but does not justify treating social life as a branch of biology.
