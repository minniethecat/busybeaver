# Reason

In [[bernard-bosanquet|Bernard Bosanquet]]'s _The Philosophical Theory of [[state|the State]]_ (1899), **reason** is not merely a faculty of [[individuality|individual]] cognition but the constitutive principle of political life itself. [[politics-aristotle|Politics]], [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] holds, "is the expression of reason in the relations that bind man to man" (001-008). The [[state|State]] is reason made concrete in [[institution|institutions]], law, and shared will.

## Reason as the Foundation of Politics

For [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]], political philosophy must begin from the recognition that human association is not driven solely by appetite or force but is structured by reason. The social contract theorists, in his view, sensed this truth but expressed it inadequately by imagining reason as a calculation of [[individuality|individual]] advantage:

> It would be as true to say that reason is independent of civilisation as that civilisation is independent of reason. (002-037)

Reason and social life develop together. The [[institution|institutions]] of law, property, and government are not external impositions on a pre-rational [[individuality|individual]] but the very forms in which reason achieves concrete existence.

## Reason and the Rule of Law

The rule of law is, for [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]], one aspect of the rule of reason in human affairs. [[law|Law]] is not arbitrary command but "the expression of reason" — a systematic ordering of social relations that enables [[individuality|individual]]s to pursue rational ends:

| Domain | Expression of Reason |
|--------|---------------------|
| [[law|Law]] | Systematic ordering of rights and obligations |
| [[morality|Morality]] | Inner sense of [[obligation|duty]] and rational self-direction |
| [[politics-aristotle|Politics]] | Organisation of [[community|common life]] toward shared goods |
| The [[state|State]] | The complete institutional embodiment of reason |

> The sense of duty, and the exercise of rational self-control, is one aspect of the rule of reason. (004-050)

## Reason vs. Inclination

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] follows [[jean-jacques-rousseau|Rousseau]] and [[immanuel-kant|Kant]] in distinguishing reason from mere inclination or appetite. The [[individuality|individual]], "before listening to his inclinations," ought to "consult his reason" (004-044). This is not a rejection of human desire but an insistence that desire must be ordered by rational principle if it is to contribute to genuine [[liberty|freedom]].

The paradox of self-government — that one may be "forced to be free" — is resolved by recognising that the rational self is the true self. When [[state|the State]] compels an [[individuality|individual]] to obey a law that reason would endorse, it is not violating [[liberty|freedom]] but actualising it:

> To restrain a man, for reasons affecting himself alone, from doing what he wills — this, Mill held, could never be right. But Bosanquet's analysis shows that the very distinction between "self" and "others" breaks down when we consider the rational will. (003-036, paraphrasing Bosanquet's engagement with Mill)

## The Reasoning of Political Philosophy

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] is attentive to the method of political reasoning itself. He observes that even the most influential political thinkers often fail to follow their premises to their logical conclusions:

> We cannot find a clue to it in the reasoning of our most recent and popular political philosophers. (003-062)

His own method is to press the logic of idealist premises — that mind is the ultimate reality, that [[individuality|individual]]s are constituted by social relations, that [[liberty|freedom]] is rational [[autonomy|self-determination]] — to their systematic conclusion in the theory of [[state|the State]]. Each step in the argument is tested by asking whether it adequately explains the concrete phenomena of political life.

## Bosanquet's Use of the Concept

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] deploys the concept of reason in three interconnected roles:

1. **Explanatory**: Reason explains why political association is not merely a matter of force or convenience — it is the expression of mind in social relations

2. **Normative**: Reason provides the standard by which political arrangements are judged — a [[state|State]] is good insofar as it embodies rational order, not merely efficient administration

3. **Transformative**: Reason transforms the [[individuality|individual]] from a creature of appetite into a self-governing [[morality|moral]] agent through participation in the rational life of the community
