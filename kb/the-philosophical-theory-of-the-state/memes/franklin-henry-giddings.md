# Franklin Henry Giddings

Franklin Henry Giddings (1855–1931) was an [[america|American]] sociologist at Columbia University, best known for his *[[principles-of-sociology|Principles of Sociology]]* (1896). [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] engages with Giddings' work as a representative example of late nineteenth-century sociological psychology, citing him to support the claim that a science of society must ultimately be a science of mind, while also registering a critical reservation about the teleological drift of Giddings' method [002-089].

## Core Ideas in Bosanquet's Reading

### Sociology as a Science of Mind

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] invokes Giddings in support of the idealist thesis that social phenomena cannot be understood in purely external or behavioural terms. After arguing that "a science of man must be a science of mind," [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] cites Giddings as an authority who, from within the sociological tradition itself, confirms that the study of society requires attending to consciousness and mental life [002-055]. This alignment between Giddings' positivist sociology and [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]]'s philosophical idealism illustrates the convergence, in [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]]'s view, of empirical social science and the philosophical theory of the state.

### The Teleological Tendency of Sociological Method

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]]'s most sustained engagement with Giddings is critical. He observes that sociological psychology, when pursued as a "critical and philosophical science," tends to abandon the purely positive and impartial stance it claims for itself. "It will assume, as in the hands of Professor Giddings, for example, it seems inclined to, at least a teleological attitude, testing social phenomena by the quantity and quality of life which they display" [002-089]. For [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]], this drift toward teleology — toward evaluating social arrangements by how much and what kind of life they make possible — is no defect. It is an implicit acknowledgment that the study of society necessarily involves value judgments about human well-being, judgments that point toward the philosophical theory of the state as the realisation of the best life for rational beings.

## Bosanquet's Assessment

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]]'s treatment of Giddings is doubly significant. Positively, he enlists Giddings as an ally in the argument that sociology, properly understood, converges with philosophical idealism — it cannot avoid questions of mind, consciousness, and value. Negatively, he uses Giddings' example to illustrate that even self-consciously "positive" social science is driven by an ineliminable teleological impulse, one that [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]]'s own theory of the state makes explicit and systematic. Giddings thus serves as a bridge figure between empirical sociology and the philosophical theory of the state that [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] is developing.
