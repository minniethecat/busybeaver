# Philosophy of Right

[[g-w-f-hegel|Hegel]]'s _Grundlinien der Philosophie des Rechts_ (1821) is the most systematic statement of [[germany|German]] idealist political philosophy. [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] engages with it as both a profound influence and a text requiring careful interpretation, devoting sustained attention to it in the later chapters of _The Philosophical Theory of [[state|the State]]_.

## Core Arguments

[[g-w-f-hegel|Hegel]]'s central thesis is that the state is the "actuality of the ethical Idea"—the concrete institutional embodiment of [[liberty|freedom]]. Freedom is not the absence of restraint but the rational organization of social life through which [[individuality|individual]]s participate in the universal (009-035). The dialectical structure moves from Abstract Right through [[morality|Morality]] to Ethical Life ([[morality|Sittlichkeit]]), in which [[family|the family]], [[society|civil society]], and the state form an ascending series of ethical [[institution|institutions]].

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] defends [[g-w-f-hegel|Hegel]] against the charge that this subordinates the [[individuality|individual]] to the state. He argues that [[g-w-f-hegel|Hegel]]ian [[liberty|freedom]] means "being at home with oneself in one's other"—the [[individuality|individual]] finds genuine [[liberty|freedom]] not in isolation but in rational participation in [[institution|social institutions]] (009-050).

## Bosanquet's Interpretation

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] reads the _Philosophy of Right_ as an attempt to reconcile ancient and modern liberty—the participatory [[citizenship|citizen]]ship of the [[greece|Greek]] polis with the subjective [[liberty|freedom]] of modern [[individuality|individual]]ism (009-010). [[g-w-f-hegel|Hegel]]'s theory of the state is not a justification of [[prussia|Prussian]] absolutism but an account of the institutional conditions for human [[liberty|freedom]].

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] emphasizes [[g-w-f-hegel|Hegel]]'s distinction between [[society|civil society]] and the state. Civil society is the sphere of particular interests and economic competition; the state is the sphere of universal interest where [[individuality|individual]]s recognize their mutual interdependence (009-035). This distinction is critical for [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]]'s own argument that the state represents the "real will" of [[individuality|individual]]s, not merely an aggregate of private preferences.

The _Philosophy of Right_ first appeared in its mature form in [[g-w-f-hegel|Hegel]]'s _Encyclopedia of the Philosophical Sciences_, and [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] traces its development through [[g-w-f-hegel|Hegel]]'s earlier writings, emphasizing the continuity of [[g-w-f-hegel|Hegel]]'s concern with the relation between [[individuality|individual]] [[liberty|freedom]] and [[institution|social institutions]] (009-059).
