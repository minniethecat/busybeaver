# Common Good

The **common good** is the good that is shared by all members of a [[community|political community]], as distinct from the aggregate of [[individuality|individual]] private goods. In [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]]'s idealist political philosophy, the common good is the object of the [[general-will|general will]] and the rational foundation of [[authority|state authority]].

## The Common Good and the General Will

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] follows [[jean-jacques-rousseau|Rousseau]] in identifying the common good as the distinctive object of the [[general-will|general will]]. The [[general-will|general will]] is always directed toward the common good—this is what distinguishes it from [[will|the will]] of all, which is merely the sum of private interests (005-015). "[[law|Law]]s and [[institution|institutions]] are only possible because man is already, what they gradually make more and more explicit; because he has a [[general-will|general will]], that is, because the good which he presents to himself as his own is necessarily in some degree a good which extends beyond himself, or a common good" (005-055).

The relationship is constitutive: the [[general-will|general will]] is defined by its orientation to the common good, and the common good is made effective through the [[general-will|general will]]. [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] writes that "the substantial unity and final purpose of the self in which the real common good resides" (005-031) is the philosophical ground of political life.

## Against Individualism

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]]'s emphasis on the common good is directed against the [[individuality|individual]]ist tradition, which treats the good as essentially private. He argues that the private interest, pursued in abstraction from the common good, is self-defeating: "his particular interest may speak to him quite differently from the common interest" (004-041). The common good is not a constraint on [[individuality|individual]] fulfillment but its condition—the [[individuality|individual]] can only realize their rational nature through participation in a shared good.

> The identity of myself with others in a common good is the same in principle as the identity of my present self with my future self. (005-023)

## The Common Good as Rational Standard

For [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]], the common good is not a matter of majority preference but of rational judgment. The common good serves as a critical standard: it is what the community _would_ will if it reasoned correctly about its own interests. This is why "the [[general-will|general will]] is always right, but the judgment which guides it is not always enlightened" (005-051)—the common good is objective, even when empirical [[individuality|individual]]s fail to recognize it.
