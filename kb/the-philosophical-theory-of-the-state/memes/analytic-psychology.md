# Analytic Psychology

_Analytic Psychology_ (1896) by [[george-frederick-stout|G.F. Stout]] is a systematic account of mental structure and process from the standpoint of the late nineteenth-century [[england|British]] psychological tradition. [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] draws on Stout's psychological analysis at key points in _The Philosophical Theory of [[state|the State]]_, particularly in his account of the [[individuality|individual]] mind as a structured system of apperceptive masses.

## Core Arguments

Stout's _Analytic Psychology_ develops a detailed theory of mental activity organized around the concept of apperception—the process by which new experiences are assimilated to existing mental structures. Mind is not a collection of discrete ideas but an organized system in which each element derives its character from its relation to the whole. This systematic character of mind is, for Stout, the psychological basis for the unity of consciousness.

Stout's analysis of attention, interest, and the selective character of mental activity provides a psychological framework for understanding how [[individuality|individual]]s apprehend the social world. The mind does not passively receive social influences but actively organizes experience through its own structures of apperception.

## Bosanquet's Use

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] uses Stout's psychological analysis to support his account of the relation between [[individuality|individual]] mind and [[institution|social institutions]]. The theory of apperceptive systems—organized structures of mental content that determine how new experience is interpreted—provides a psychological analogue to [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]]'s account of how [[individuality|individual]]s internalize social norms and [[institution|institutions]]. The [[individuality|individual]] mind, like the state, is a "structure of systems" rather than an aggregate of independent elements.
