# David George Ritchie

David George Ritchie (1853–1903) was a Scottish philosopher of the [[england|British]] idealist school, best known for his work _[[natural-rights|Natural Rights]]_ (1895), which offered a critical examination of the concept of [[rights|natural rights]] from an idealist perspective. [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] draws on Ritchie at several key junctures in _The Philosophical Theory of [[state|the State]]_.

## Core Ideas in Bosanquet's Reading

### The Historical Conception of Society

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] cites Ritchie's insight that the early Christian conception of society—"a kingdom not of this world"—produced a dangerous dualism. As Ritchie pointed out, this idea, "while it gave the [[individuality|individual]] a new value, a worth independent of his place in any worldly organisation," also made the secular order seem "alien to religion and [[morality|moral]]ity." [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] extends this analysis, noting how this dualism reappeared in Protestant fanaticism and continues to shape modern political thought (001-016).

### Natural Rights and Their Critique

Ritchie's _[[natural-rights|Natural Rights]]_ is referenced by [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] as a landmark treatment of the subject. In an appendix to that work, Ritchie engages with the historical and philosophical dimensions of [[rights|rights]] discourse (001-022). [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] places Ritchie alongside [[henry-sidgwick|Henry Sidgwick]] as notable exceptions among [[england|British]] thinkers who recognized the metaphysical dimensions of political theory—"[[henry-sidgwick|Henry Sidgwick]] and Professor Ritchie are notable exceptions" to the general neglect of philosophical foundations (004-022).

### The Idealist Recovery of Rights

Ritchie's contribution, as [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] presents it, lies in showing that [[rights|rights]] are not pre-social attributes of abstract [[individuality|individual]]s but are constituted within and through the ethical life of the [[community|political community]]. This idealist recovery of [[rights|rights]] theory aligns with [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]]'s own argument that rights are "claims recognized by the state" rather than pre-political possessions.

## Bosanquet's Assessment

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] treats Ritchie as a philosophical ally within the [[england|British]] idealist tradition. Ritchie's work on [[rights|natural rights]] provides historical and analytical support for [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]]'s own argument that rights are socially constituted. While [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] develops his theory of [[rights|rights]] more systematically through the lens of [[jean-jacques-rousseau|Rousseau]] and [[g-w-f-hegel|Hegel]], Ritchie's contribution to the Anglo-Scottish reception of idealist political philosophy is acknowledged as significant.
