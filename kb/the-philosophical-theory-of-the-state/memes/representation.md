# Representation

In [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]]'s _Philosophical Theory of [[state|the State]]_, **Representation** is a far richer concept than the mere electoral mechanism by which [[individuality|individual]]s are deputed to a legislature. Drawing on [[jean-jacques-rousseau|Rousseau]] and [[g-w-f-hegel|Hegel]], [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] understands representation as the symbolic embodiment of the [[community|common life]] in [[institution|institutions]], laws, and persons that "stand for" the [[real-will|rational will]] of the community.

## Representation as Standing For

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] begins from [[jean-jacques-rousseau|Rousseau]]'s radical claim that the [[general-will|general will]] cannot be represented — that sovereignty is inalienable. Yet he recognises that all [[institution|political institutions]] involve representation in a deeper sense:

> The remarkable representation of this state of things under the figure of a single legal "person" — this is the device by which modern political theory has expressed the unity of the State. (003-010, 001-016)

The [[state|State]] itself is a representation — it "stands for" the [[community|common life]] in a concentrated, institutional form. Every law, every official, every institution represents not particular interests but the rational order of the whole.

## Rousseau and the Denial of Representation

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] takes seriously [[jean-jacques-rousseau|Rousseau]]'s argument that "the [[general-will|general will]] denies all possibility of other representation of the people where there is a true commonwealth" (005-009). This does not mean that government is impossible but that representation can never be the mere transmission of [[individuality|individual]] votes:

> Rousseau's denial of representation is not a denial of government but an insistence that government must express the general will, not the will of all. (005-009)

The representative does not "represent" his constituents' particular opinions; he represents the rational interest they share as members of the community.

## The Real Will and Representation

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] acknowledges the imperfection of all actual representation: "It is needless to observe that such a representation of the [[real-will|Real Will]] is imperfect" (005-055). No electoral mechanism can perfectly capture the [[real-will|rational will]] of a community:

| Aspect | Mechanical View | [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]]'s View |
|--------|----------------|-----------------|
| What is represented | Individual preferences | The common good |
| How representation works | Aggregation of votes | Symbolic embodiment |
| Criterion of success | Numerical accuracy | Rational adequacy |

Yet this imperfection does not invalidate the idea of representation; it only means that representation is always an approximation, an effort that "will always give meaning to the representation of the effort after any kind of complete life" (006-062).

## Representation and the Modern State

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] sees modern representative [[institution|institutions]] as the distinctive contribution of the modern world to political theory. The "last idea which governs his representations of the modern [[state|State]]" (010-085) is that representation must penetrate every sphere of social life — not only parliament but local government, corporations, and [[association|associations]] all participate in the work of representing the [[community|common life]]:

> Our loyalty can only stand by virtue of that representation being recognised. (008-061)

## Representation, Loyalty, and Legitimacy

For [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]], representation is the ground of political legitimacy. We obey [[state|the State]] not because we have personally consented to every law but because [[state|the State]]'s [[institution|institutions]] "represent" — that is, make present and effective — the [[real-will|rational will]] in which we participate as members of the community.
