# Giambattista Vico

Giambattista Vico (1668–1744) was an [[italy|Italian]] philosopher of history whose *[[new-science|New Science]]* (1725) [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] identifies as one of the earliest attempts to inaugurate a systematic science of society. For [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]], Vico stands alongside [[montesquieu|Montesquieu]] as a thinker who revived political philosophy by revealing the living social substance within what had become the "dry formulae of law" [001-019].

## Core Ideas in Bosanquet's Reading

### The New Science and the Science of Society

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] situates Vico's *[[new-science|New Science]]* at the head of a tradition of attempts "to inaugurate the Science of [[society|Society]] as a new departure" in [[europe|modern Europe]]. He notes that while the distinctive spirit of sociology first found unmistakable expression in [[auguste-comte|Auguste Comte]] half a century later, Vico's work represents an earlier, more philosophical recognition that society is not merely an aggregate of [[individuality|individual]]s but a coherent whole with its own laws of development [002-003]. This insight — that the forms of law and political life are not arbitrary conventions but expressions of a deeper social [[reason|rationality]] — is central to [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]]'s own philosophical theory of the state.

### Vico and the Revival of Political Philosophy

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] traces a lineage from Vico through [[montesquieu|Montesquieu]] to [[jean-jacques-rousseau|Rousseau]] that, in his view, rescued political thought from juristic formalism. "Between [[john-locke|Locke]] and [[jean-jacques-rousseau|Rousseau]] the genius of Vico and of [[montesquieu|Montesquieu]] had given a new meaning to the dry formulae of law by showing the sap of society circulating within them" [001-019]. The inauguration of modern political philosophy "was inaugurated by Vico and [[montesquieu|Montesquieu]], and decisively declared itself in Jean Jacques [[jean-jacques-rousseau|Rousseau]]" [001-021].

### Recognition of Fundamental Social Unity

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] credits Vico with a recognition — however inchoate — of "the fundamental unity" of social life. He groups Vico's endeavour with later attempts to grasp society as more than a contractual arrangement, seeing in Vico's conceptions "the forerunners of the many-sided" attempts to understand the state as the embodiment of a [[general-will|common will]] and a common good [002-047]. This reading aligns Vico with [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]]'s own idealist conviction that the state is not an external mechanism but the living organ of a self-conscious community.

## Bosanquet's Assessment

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]]'s treatment of Vico is appreciative but brief. He positions Vico as an important forerunner — the thinker who first broke with the atomistic social philosophy of early modern [[law|natural law]] — while recognising that Vico's insights required the fuller development they received in [[montesquieu|Montesquieu]], [[jean-jacques-rousseau|Rousseau]], and ultimately [[g-w-f-hegel|Hegel]]. Vico's limitation, implicitly, is that his conception remained more intuition than system; it fell to later thinkers to elaborate the philosophical logic of the state that Vico had glimpsed in the "sap of society" circulating through its legal and political forms.
