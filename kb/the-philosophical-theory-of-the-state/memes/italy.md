# Italy

Italy appears in [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]]'s _Philosophical Theory of [[state|the State]]_ in two main contexts: as a distinctive contributor to the tradition of [[europe|European]] political thought, and as an example of national unification—the process by which a people becomes a state.

## Italy in Political Thought

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] groups Italy alongside [[greece|Greece]] and [[england|England]] as distinct national voices in the history of political thought: "the voice of [[greece|Greece]], another that of Italy, and the third that of [[england|England]]" (002-028). The Italian contribution includes Machiavelli's realist analysis of political power, the civic humanist tradition of the Renaissance city-states, and the [[rome|Roman]] inheritance that continued to shape Italian political thinking.

## National Unification

The "unity of Italy"—achieved through the Risorgimento in the nineteenth century—is cited by [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] as an example of the formation of a nation-state (010-128). Italian unification represents a case in which cultural and linguistic identity provided the basis for political unification, illustrating the relation between nation and state that is a central theme of modern political thought.

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]]'s reference to Italian unification is brief but significant: it acknowledges that the nation-state is not a static form but a historical achievement, the result of complex processes of political development. The Italian case, like the [[germany|German]], shows how national identity and state [[institution|institutions]] can develop in dynamic relation to each other.
