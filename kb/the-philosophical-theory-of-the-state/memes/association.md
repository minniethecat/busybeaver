# Association

In Chapter VII of _The Philosophical Theory of [[state|the State]]_, [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] addresses the relation between the **state and other forms of human association**—a critical question for any theory that claims a unique status for the state. He argues that while families, churches, trade unions, and other associations are genuine expressions of human sociality, the state has a distinctive character that sets it apart.

## The State as an Association

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] acknowledges that the state shares many features with other associations: it is a form of organized collective life, it involves common purposes, and it shapes the character of its members. However, he rejects the pluralist claim that the state is merely one association among others. The state differs from all other associations in its comprehensiveness—it is the "association of associations (007-002)," providing the framework within which all other forms of collective life operate.

## The Limits of Voluntary Association

A key theme in [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]]'s analysis is that voluntary associations are necessarily partial. A church serves religious purposes, a trade union serves economic interests, a family serves domestic needs. None of these can claim to represent the community as a whole or to harmonize the competing claims of different spheres of life (007-008). The state alone has this comprehensive function: it "maintains the external conditions necessary (007-015) for the realization of the best life" across all spheres.

## Against Pluralism

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]]'s argument is directed against pluralist theories that would treat the state as merely one competitor among many in the marketplace of associations. For him, this view misunderstands the nature of [[authority|political authority]]. The state is not the enemy of other associations—it is the condition of their ordered coexistence. Without the state's coordinating function, associations would fall into conflict, and [[individuality|individual]] personality would be torn between incompatible demands.
