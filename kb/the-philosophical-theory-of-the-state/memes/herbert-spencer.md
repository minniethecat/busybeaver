# Herbert Spencer

Herbert Spencer (1820–1903) was an [[england|English]] philosopher who applied evolutionary theory to social and political life. [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] engages Spencer's work primarily to distinguish his own organic theory of the state from Spencer's biological reductionism.

## Spencer's Evolutionary Organicism

Spencer treated society as a literal organism, subject to the same laws of development, differentiation, and adaptation as biological entities. He argued that social evolution proceeds toward greater [[individuality|individual]] [[liberty|freedom]], and that state interference with this natural process is both futile and harmful. [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] rejects this position: the state operates at the level of mind and will, not biological instinct.

## Bosanquet's Critique

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]]'s critique of Spencer in Chapter XI is a key part of his argument for the distinctiveness of political philosophy. The organic character of the state is a matter of consciousness and [[real-will|rational will]], not of biological function (011-001). Spencer's version of organicism, by reducing society to biology, misses precisely what is distinctive about political life: the determination of conduct by ideas of the common good rather than by instinct or natural selection.
