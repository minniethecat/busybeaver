# Socrates

Socrates (c. 470–399 BCE) is the founding figure of Western philosophy. His decision to accept the death [[punishment|penalty]] rather than flee Athens—dramatized in [[plato|Plato]]'s _Crito_—establishes the classical paradigm of [[obligation|political obligation]]: the [[citizenship|citizen]] owes obedience to the laws (004-032) even when they are unjustly applied.

## The Socratic Paradigm of Obligation

In the _Crito_, Socrates argues that his lifelong residence in Athens constitutes an implicit agreement to obey its laws, and that to flee would be to destroy the laws that made his life as a philosopher possible. [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] reinterprets this argument through the concept of the [[general-will|general will]]: Socrates obeys the laws not because of a historical contract but because the laws express his own rational nature as a [[citizenship|citizen]] of Athens.

## Bosanquet's Reading

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] treats Socrates as an illustration of the principle that [[obligation|political obligation]] is grounded in the self's recognition of its own [[real-will|rational will]] in the law. The [[individuality|individual]] "may have a particular will contrary to ... the [[general-will|general will]] which he has as [[citizenship|citizen]]" (004-041), but the [[general-will|general will]]—expressed in the laws of Athens—represents Socrates' real will, which he chooses to honor even at the cost of his life (004-032).
