# Real Will

The distinction between **actual will** and **real will** is fundamental to [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]]'s idealist political philosophy (004-033). The actual will is what an [[individuality|individual]] happens to desire at any given moment; the real will is what that [[individuality|individual]] would will if they were fully rational and adequately informed. The [[general-will|general will]], in [[jean-jacques-rousseau|Rousseau]]'s sense, corresponds to the real will of each [[citizenship|citizen]].

## The Paradox of Self-Government

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] deploys the real will concept to resolve the paradox of self-government: how can an [[individuality|individual]] be both the author of the law and subject to it? The answer is that the law expresses the [[individuality|individual]]'s real will—their rational self—even when it conflicts with their actual, empirical desires.

He draws on [[jean-jacques-rousseau|Rousseau]]'s analysis: "Each [[individuality|individual]] may, as a man, have a particular will contrary to or unlike the [[general-will|general will]] which he has as [[citizenship|citizen]]" (004-041). The particular will reflects private interest; the [[general-will|general will]] reflects the real interest that each person shares with all others as a member of the community.

## Philosophical Foundations

The real will concept has deep roots in the idealist tradition. [[g-w-f-hegel|Hegel]] argued that [[liberty|freedom]] consists not in doing whatever one happens to want but in willing the rational. [[t-h-green|T.H. Green]] developed a similar distinction in his lectures on [[obligation|political obligation]]. [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] synthesizes these sources to argue that the state, in enforcing law, is not imposing an alien will but enabling [[individuality|individual]]s to realize their own rational nature.

## The General Will as Real Will

For [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]], the [[general-will|general will]] _is_ the real will of the community's members. It is not a mysterious entity distinct from [[individuality|individual]] wills but the rational core of each person's willing insofar as they will the common good. This is why [[jean-jacques-rousseau|Rousseau]] can say that "the [[general-will|general will]] is always right"—it is "always right" because it is directed toward the good, even when empirical [[individuality|individual]]s fail to recognize it (005-051).

## Criticisms

Critics have argued that the real will concept is inherently paternalistic—a philosophical device for justifying coercion in the name of a "true self" that the coerced [[individuality|individual]] does not actually experience. [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]]'s response, implicit in his argument, is that rational [[autonomy|self-determination]] is not a matter of arbitrary preference but of acting in accordance with the conditions of one's own rational nature. The person who acts against the [[general-will|general will]] is not free but enslaved to appetite.
