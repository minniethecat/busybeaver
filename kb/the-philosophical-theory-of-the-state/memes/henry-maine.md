# Henry Maine

Sir Henry James Sumner Maine (1822–1888) was a [[england|British]] jurist and legal historian, best known for *Ancient [[law|Law]]* (1861), in which he argued that legal systems evolve from arrangements based on status toward those based on contract. [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] references Maine in a footnote to the chapter on the analysis of rights, engaging with his theory that criminal law originates in private feud [008-139].

## Core Ideas in Bosanquet's Reading

### The Origin of Criminal Law

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]]'s sole substantive engagement with Maine concerns the origin of criminal law. In a footnote to a passage on the social consciousness that maintains the [[rights|system of rights]], [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] notes: "It may be noted that Durkheim, relying chiefly on early religious sentiment, denies Maine's view that criminal law arises out of private feud" [008-139]. By juxtaposing Maine with Durkheim, [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] situates Maine's theory within a broader debate about whether law emerges from private dispute resolution or from collective religious and [[morality|moral]] sentiment — a question that bears directly on [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]]'s own account of the state as the embodiment of a [[general-will|common will]] rather than a mere arbiter of private conflicts.

## Bosanquet's Assessment

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]]'s treatment of Maine is indirect: he neither endorses nor elaborately refutes Maine's position, but places it in tension with Durkheim's contrary view. The implication is that Maine's feud-origin theory, while historically suggestive, may understate the role of collective sentiment in the formation of legal [[institution|institutions]] — a point consistent with [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]]'s broader argument that law is not reducible to the management of private antagonisms but expresses the positive ethical life of a community.
