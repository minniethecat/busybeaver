# Bernard Bosanquet

Bernard Bosanquet (1848–1923) was an [[england|English]] philosopher of the [[england|British]] idealist school and the author of _The Philosophical Theory of [[state|the State]]_ (1899), the source text for this wiki. A student of [[g-w-f-hegel|Hegel]]'s philosophy and a central figure in the Anglo-[[america|American]] idealist movement, Bosanquet developed a comprehensive idealist theory of political life.

## Core Ideas

### The State as a Psychical Fact

Bosanquet's central thesis is that the [[state|state]] is fundamentally a "psychical fact"—a work of mind and will rather than a mechanical apparatus. The state embodies the [[general-will|general will]] of the [[community|political community]], which is not the aggregate of [[individuality|individual]] preferences but the rational content of collective [[autonomy|self-determination]].

### The Philosophical Theory of the State

The work itself was published in 1899, with editions appearing through the early twentieth century. Bosanquet's dedication page reads: "In the ablest and most industrious of democracies, there are probably few [[individuality|individual]]s who would care to reread the whole of their own statute book. And yet, for a systematic knowledge of themselves, there is your [[america|America]]" (P01-010).

Bosanquet's approach draws deeply on [[jean-jacques-rousseau|Rousseau]]'s theory of the [[general-will|general will]] and the [[social-contract|social contract]], which he reinterprets through the lens of post-[[immanuel-kant|Kant]]ian [[germany|German idealism]], particularly the philosophy of [[g-w-f-hegel|Hegel]].

### Related Works

Bosanquet's broader philosophical output included works on logic, aesthetics, and social philosophy. His essay on "Klassenkampf" in _The Standard of Life_, co-authored with H. Bosanquet (010-066), addresses the relation between class struggle and ethical life. This work is cited in the main text as part of the discussion of economic and social dimensions of political theory (011-059).

## Bosanquet's Place in British Idealism

Bosanquet was part of a remarkable generation of [[england|British]] philosophers—alongside [[thomas-hill-green|T.H. Green]], [[francis-herbert-bradley|F.H. Bradley]], and [[david-george-ritchie|D.G. Ritchie]]—who brought [[germany|German]] idealist philosophy into [[england|English]]-language political thought. His distinctive contribution was to apply idealist logic to the concrete [[institution|institutions]] of modern political life: [[law|law]], [[rights|rights]], [[sovereignty|sovereignty]], and the [[state|state]] itself.
