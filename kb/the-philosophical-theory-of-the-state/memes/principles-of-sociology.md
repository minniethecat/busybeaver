# Principles of Sociology

*The Principles of Sociology* (1896) by [[franklin-henry-giddings|Franklin Henry Giddings]] is an early [[america|American]] treatise in academic sociology, synthesising evolutionary and statistical approaches to the study of society. [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] references Giddings's work in a discussion of the physical and mechanical conditions of modern social life [003-018].

## Mechanical Conditions of Social Life

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] cites Giddings in the context of a broader discussion of the relationship between material conditions and social organisation. He draws on Giddings's observations — alongside those of sanitary reformer George Vivian Poore — regarding "the mechanical conditions of modern city life," noting their relevance to understanding how urbanisation and industrialisation transform the texture of social relations [003-018].

## Bosanquet's Use of This Work

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]]'s reference to Giddings is brief and largely illustrative. He invokes the *Principles of Sociology* not as a theoretical authority to be engaged with at length, but as a source of empirical observation supporting his broader argument that the physical organisation of modern life — the conditions of city dwelling, the mechanisms of public health, the infrastructure of collective existence — provides the material basis for the development of the state as an expression of the [[general-will|general will]] [003-018].
