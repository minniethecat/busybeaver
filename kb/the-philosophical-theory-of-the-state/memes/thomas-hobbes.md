# Thomas Hobbes

Thomas Hobbes (1588–1679) is a foundational figure in modern political philosophy. His _[[leviathan|Leviathan]]_ (1651) formulated the problem of sovereignty and [[obligation|political obligation]] in terms that shaped all subsequent discussion, including [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]]'s idealist theory.

## Hobbes's Theory of Sovereignty

Hobbes argued that sovereignty must be absolute and undivided (004-044)—a single will capable of making and enforcing law without appeal. The sovereign's authority derives from a social contract in which [[individuality|individual]]s surrender their natural right to all things in exchange for security. [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] engages this argument critically: he agrees with Hobbes that sovereignty must lie in a will, but rejects the Hobbesian conclusion that this will must be that of a single ruler.

## Bosanquet's Critique

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] argues that Hobbes's [[individuality|individual]]ist premises undermine his conclusion. If [[individuality|individual]]s are motivated only by fear and self-preservation, the sovereign's authority rests on force rather than right. [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] seeks to ground sovereignty in the [[real-will|rational will]] of the community (004-041) rather than in the feared power of the ruler. True sovereignty "must lie in a will, and that [[will|the will]] in question must be real, not merely ostensible" (005-009)—a standard that Hobbes's absolute monarch cannot meet.
