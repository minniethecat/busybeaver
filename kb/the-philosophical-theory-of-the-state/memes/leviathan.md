# Leviathan

[[thomas-hobbes|Thomas Hobbes]]'s _Leviathan_ (1651) is the foundational text of modern sovereignty theory. Its argument that sovereignty must be absolute and undivided, grounded in a social contract that [[individuality|individual]]s make to escape the state of nature, established the framework for all subsequent discussion of [[authority|political authority]].

## Hobbes's Argument

[[thomas-hobbes|Hobbes]] argues that in the state of nature—a condition of universal war—life is "solitary, poor, nasty, brutish, and short." To escape this condition, [[individuality|individual]]s agree to surrender their natural right to all things to a sovereign who will enforce peace. The sovereign's authority is absolute because any limitation would reintroduce the conflicts that the contract was designed to prevent.

## Bosanquet's Engagement

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] engages the _Leviathan_ as both a landmark achievement and a philosophical dead end. He agrees with [[thomas-hobbes|Hobbes]] that sovereignty must lie in a will, but rejects the conclusion that this will must be that of a single ruler. "[[sovereignty|Sovereignty]] must lie in a will, and that [[will|the will]] in question must be real, not merely ostensible" (005-009)—a standard that [[thomas-hobbes|Hobbes]]'s absolute monarch cannot meet. [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]]'s alternative—that sovereignty resides in the [[general-will|general will]] of the community—transforms [[thomas-hobbes|Hobbes]]'s framework by relocating sovereignty from the ruler to the people.
