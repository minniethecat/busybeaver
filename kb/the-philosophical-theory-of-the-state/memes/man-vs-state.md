# The Man versus the State

*The Man versus the State* (1884) by [[herbert-spencer|Herbert Spencer]] is a collection of four political essays — "The New Toryism," "The Coming Slavery," "The Sins of Legislators," and "The Great Political Superstition" — arguing that the rapid expansion of legislative state intervention in Victorian [[england|Britain]] represents a return to coercive paternalism under the guise of progressive reform. [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] engages with [[herbert-spencer|Spencer]]'s work at several critical junctures in *The Philosophical Theory of [[state|the State]]*, treating it as the most articulate expression of the [[individuality|individual]]istic theory of the state that he seeks to overcome through his own theory of the [[general-will|general will]] [003-037].

## Core Arguments

### Liberty as Absence of Government

[[herbert-spencer|Spencer]] advances the proposition that "perfect liberty is equivalent to total absence of government" — a formulation [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] cites as the logical endpoint of the [[individuality|individual]]ist tradition [003-047]. On this view, every act of state intervention diminishes liberty by restricting the sphere of [[individuality|individual]] self-assertion. [[herbert-spencer|Spencer]] argues that the proliferation of [[law|legislation]] in areas such as public health, education, and labour regulation constitutes a "New Toryism" that reverses the historical progress from status to contract and threatens to re-impose a regime of compulsory cooperation upon [[individuality|individual]]s who ought to be free to pursue their own ends.

### Rights as Pre-Governmental

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] draws particular attention to [[herbert-spencer|Spencer]]'s doctrine of [[natural-right|natural rights]], which [[herbert-spencer|Spencer]] grounds in a biological foundation. [[rights|Rights]], on [[herbert-spencer|Spencer]]'s account, are not created by the state; rather, they pre-exist government, which merely recognises and enforces them [003-040]. [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] contrasts this view with [[jeremy-bentham|Bentham]]'s position that rights are creations of the state, noting that on their "common premises" both thinkers have "equal reason" — yet both remain trapped within an abstract opposition between [[individuality|individual]] and government that the theory of the [[general-will|general will]] dissolves [003-040].

### The Convergence of Extremes

A central theme of [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]]'s analysis is the observation that [[herbert-spencer|Spencer]]'s uncritical [[individuality|individual]]ism converges with the very collectivism it opposes. [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] argues that an abstract [[individuality|individual]]ism, which treats the self as a pre-social atom, "is always in danger of transformation into an uncritical collectivism. The basis of the two is in fact the same" [003-037]. Both positions share the assumption that the [[individuality|individual]] and the state stand in external relation to one another; they differ only in which pole they privilege. [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]]'s own theory seeks to transcend this dichotomy by reconceiving the state as the embodiment of the [[real-will|rational will]] of [[individuality|individual]]s.

## Bosanquet's Use of This Work

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] treats *The Man versus the State* not merely as an opponent to be refuted but as an instructive case study in the logic of [[individuality|individual]]ism. He urges the sociological student to study [[herbert-spencer|Spencer]]'s writings carefully, noting two reasons: first, [[herbert-spencer|Spencer]] powerfully illustrates "the fatal possibilities of a collective governmental stupidity" — a warning that [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] does not dismiss but incorporates into a more adequate theory of state action; and second, the "instructive meeting of extremes" between [[herbert-spencer|Spencer]]'s [[individuality|individual]]ism and popular socialist theories reveals the conceptual limitations shared by both [003-037].

In [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]]'s broader argument, [[herbert-spencer|Spencer]] represents the "paradox of self-government" in its most acute form. The [[individuality|individual]]ist cannot explain how a government that limits [[individuality|individual]] self-assertion can be an expression of [[individuality|individual]] [[liberty|freedom]]. [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]]'s answer — that true liberty is not the absence of restraint but the realisation of the rational self through participation in the [[general-will|general will]] — is developed in sustained dialogue with, and as a direct response to, the [[herbert-spencer|Spencer]]ian challenge [003-047].

## Key Passages

> "Perfect liberty is equivalent to total absence of government" — Seeley, quoted by Bosanquet as the quintessential individualist thesis he contests [003-047]

> Bosanquet observes that in Spencer's framework "the self, which has ranked throughout these views as the end, to whose liberty all is to be sacrificed, turns out to be the non-ethical element of life" [003-047]

## Historical Context

*The Man versus the State* appeared at a pivotal moment in [[england|British]] political thought, as the laissez-faire consensus of mid-Victorian liberalism gave way before demands for state intervention in social and economic life. [[herbert-spencer|Spencer]]'s essays were a rear-guard defence of the older [[individuality|individual]]ism, and their influence extended well beyond [[england|Britain]] — particularly in the [[america|United States]], where they became foundational texts for libertarian political thought. [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]]'s engagement with [[herbert-spencer|Spencer]] forms part of a broader idealist response to this [[individuality|individual]]ist tradition, shared by [[t-h-green|T.H. Green]], [[f-h-bradley|F.H. Bradley]], and [[david-george-ritchie|D.G. Ritchie]].
