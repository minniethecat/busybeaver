# J.R. Seeley

Sir John Robert Seeley (1834–1895) was Regius Professor of Modern History at Cambridge and a pioneer of political science as an academic discipline in [[england|Britain]]. His _[[introduction-to-political-science|Introduction to Political Science]]_ (1896) provided [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] with a working definition of government that he subjects to sustained critical scrutiny. Seeley's influence on [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] is primarily dialectical: [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] uses Seeley's formulations as a foil to clarify his own, more philosophically ambitious conception of the state (006-032).

## Core Ideas in Bosanquet's Reading

### The Definition of Government

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] quotes Seeley's definition of government and subjects it to philosophical analysis. Seeley characterises government in broadly functional terms — as the institution that coordinates collective action. [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] finds this definition useful but insufficient: it captures what government _does_ but not what government _is_ in relation to the [[real-will|rational will]] of the community (006-034). The limitation of Seeley's approach, in [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]]'s view, is that it remains at the level of empirical description without ascending to the philosophical ground of [[obligation|political obligation]].

### Comparative Method in Politics

Seeley's application of comparative method to [[institution|political institutions]] — paralleling Freeman's _[[comparative-politics|Comparative Politics]]_ — is noted by [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] as a significant methodological development (002-050). However, [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]]'s own method is philosophical rather than comparative-historical. Where Seeley seeks to understand [[institution|political institutions]] by comparing them across time and place, [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] seeks to understand them by analysing their rational necessity within the structure of human self-consciousness.

## Bosanquet's Assessment

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]]'s treatment of Seeley is respectful but ultimately supersessionary. Seeley represents the best of the empirical political science of his day, but his method cannot reach the philosophical questions that [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] considers fundamental: What is the state in its essence? Why ought [[individuality|individual]]s to obey it? By demonstrating both the value and the limits of Seeley's approach, [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] clarifies the distinctive contribution of philosophical idealism to political theory — a contribution that empirical science alone cannot make.
