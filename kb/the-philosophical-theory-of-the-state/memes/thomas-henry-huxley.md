# T.H. Huxley

Thomas Henry Huxley (1825–1895) was a [[england|British]] biologist known as "Darwin's Bulldog" for his advocacy of evolutionary theory. In _The Philosophical Theory of [[state|the State]]_, [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] engages critically with Huxley's _[[evolution-and-ethics|Evolution and Ethics]]_ (1893), focusing on the supposed opposition between natural self-assertion and social [[morality|ethics]] — a dualism [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] rejects as philosophically untenable (002-069).

## Core Ideas in Bosanquet's Reading

### Nature versus Society: A False Dualism

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] identifies in Huxley a fundamental dichotomy between "nature" and "society." In Huxley's view, "nature" is essentially self-assertion, while "society" demands restraint — a "cosmic process" versus an "ethical process" (002-019). [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] groups Huxley with [[herbert-spencer|Spencer]] in this regard, arguing that both thinkers treat the "self" and the "other" as irreconcilable opposites (002-069). For [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]], this opposition betrays a failure to grasp the organic unity of [[individuality|individual]] and [[will|social will]] — the very foundation of his own political philosophy.

### Self-Restraint and the Ethical Process

Huxley's characterisation of "self-restraint as the essence of the ethical process" (003-049) receives direct critical attention. [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] acknowledges the descriptive accuracy of Huxley's observation — social life does require restraint — but rejects the implication that this restraint is external to the self. In [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]]'s idealist framework, genuine self-restraint is not a curtailment of [[liberty|freedom]] but its realisation: the restraint in question is the restraint of the merely impulsive or partial self by the rational, social self.

## Bosanquet's Assessment

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]]'s engagement with Huxley is ultimately negative in character. Huxley serves, alongside [[herbert-spencer|Spencer]], as a representative of what [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] considers the "negative" view of liberty — liberty as mere absence of external constraint rather than as positive participation in a rational social order. By placing Huxley's naturalistic [[morality|ethics]] in opposition to his own idealist theory, [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] sharpens his argument that the state is not an artificial check on [[liberty|natural liberty]] but the very medium through which human [[liberty|freedom]] achieves rational form.
