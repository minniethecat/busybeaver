# General Will

The **general will** (_volonté générale_) is [[will|the will]] of the [[community|political community]] as a whole, directed toward the [[common-good|common interest]], as distinct from the mere aggregate or sum of [[individuality|individual]] private wills. In _The Philosophical Theory of [[state|the State]]_ (1899), [[bernard-bosanquet|Bernard Bosanquet]] builds extensively on [[jean-jacques-rousseau|Rousseau]]'s concept, treating the general will as the rational foundation of [[authority|state authority]], [[obligation|political obligation]], and [[individuality|individual]] liberty properly understood.

## Definition and Origin

The concept originates in [[jean-jacques-rousseau|Jean-Jacques Rousseau]]'s _[[contrat-social|Du Contrat Social]]_ (1762), where the general will is distinguished from the "will of all" (_volonté de tous_). The will of all is merely the sum of particular interests, whereas the general will attends only to the [[common-good|common interest]]. [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] summarizes [[jean-jacques-rousseau|Rousseau]]'s [[social-contract|social pact]] formula:

> Each of us puts into the common stock his person and his entire powers under the supreme direction of the general will: and we further receive each individual as an indivisible member of the whole. (004-032)

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] notes that, putting aside [[jean-jacques-rousseau|Rousseau]]'s "defective terminology," this passage contains far-reaching ideas: "the essence of [[society|human society]] consists in a common self, a life and a will, which belong to and are exercised by the society as such, or by the [[individuality|individual]]s in society as such" (004-033).

## Bosanquet's Analysis

### The General Will as the Basis of the State

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] argues that the general will is not an external constraint on [[individuality|individual]]s but the expression of their own rational nature as members of a [[community|political community]]. The state, understood as the institutional embodiment of the general will, is therefore not opposed to [[individuality|individual]] liberty but is its necessary condition (005-002).

He draws a key distinction between the [[individuality|individual]]'s "particular will" as a private person and the "general will" they possess as a [[citizenship|citizen]]:

> Each individual may, as a man, have a particular will contrary to or unlike the general will which he has as citizen; his particular interest may speak to him quite differently from the common interest. (004-041)

### General Will and the Common Good

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] follows [[jean-jacques-rousseau|Rousseau]] in maintaining that the general will is always directed toward the good, even when [[individuality|individual]]s fail to recognize it. As [[jean-jacques-rousseau|Rousseau]] puts it in a passage [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] quotes approvingly:

> Of itself, the people always wills the good, but it does not always see it. The general will is always right, but the judgment which guides it is not always enlightened. (005-051)

For [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]], this paradox "precisely expresses the fact. [[law|Law]]s and [[institution|institutions]] are only possible because man is already, what they gradually make more and more explicit; because he has a general will, that is, because the good which he presents to himself as his own is necessarily in some degree a good which extends beyond himself, or a common good" (005-055).

### Relation to Self-Government

The general will resolves the paradox of self-government: how can an [[individuality|individual]] be both the ruler and the ruled? [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] argues that self-government is only meaningful if the "self" in question is the rational self expressed through the general will, not the arbitrary private self. He criticizes theories that fail to "give the fullest force to both terms of the paradox, at the same time that it exhibits their reconciliation" (003-063).

## Philosophical Heritage

### Rousseau's Innovation

[[jean-jacques-rousseau|Rousseau]]'s concept of the general will represented a departure from the [[individuality|individual]]istic social contract theories of [[thomas-hobbes|Hobbes]] and [[john-locke|Locke]]. Where [[thomas-hobbes|Hobbes]] saw the sovereign as external to the people and [[john-locke|Locke]] emphasized [[individuality|individual]] [[natural-right|natural rights]], [[jean-jacques-rousseau|Rousseau]] located sovereignty in the people themselves, understood as a collective [[morality|moral]] person. [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] acknowledges this lineage while arguing that [[jean-jacques-rousseau|Rousseau]]'s theory, properly interpreted, "is further from popular modes of thought" than its critics suppose (003-063).

### The General Will in Bosanquet's System

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]]'s own contribution is to connect the general will with the idealist tradition of political philosophy, particularly [[g-w-f-hegel|Hegel]]'s concept of objective spirit. He argues that the general will is "as much implied in the life of a society as some sort of will for good in the life of an [[individuality|individual]]. The two, in fact, are not merely analogous but to a great extent identical" (005-020).

## Key Distinctions

### General Will vs. Will of All

The most important distinction in [[jean-jacques-rousseau|Rousseau]]'s theory, which [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] defends at length, is between the general will (_volonté générale_) and [[will|the will]] of all (_volonté de tous_). The will of all is the sum of private interests—what you get by adding up what each [[individuality|individual]] happens to want. The general will, by contrast, is what remains when private interests cancel each other out, leaving only the [[common-good|common interest]] (005-033).

### General Will vs. Real Will

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] develops a parallel distinction between the "[[real-will|actual will]]" (what a person happens to want at any moment) and the "real will" (what a person would want if they were fully rational and informed). The general will corresponds to the real will of each [[citizenship|citizen]]—it is what each person, insofar as they reason correctly about the common good, would will.

## Critical Reception

Critics of the general will concept, from liberal [[individuality|individual]]ists to pluralists, have argued that it is inherently authoritarian—a metaphysical fiction that justifies suppressing [[individuality|individual]] dissent in the name of a spurious "common good." [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] addresses this concern indirectly by emphasizing that the general will is not imposed from without but recognized from within: the [[individuality|individual]], in obeying laws that express the general will, is obeying their own rational nature. Nevertheless, the tension between the general will and [[individuality|individual]] liberty remains a central problem in political philosophy.
