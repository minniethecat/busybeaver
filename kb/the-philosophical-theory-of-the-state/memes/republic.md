# Republic

[[plato|Plato]]'s _Republic_ (c. 375 BCE) is the founding text of Western political philosophy. Its central argument—that the just state is one governed by reason and directed toward the common good—establishes the idealist tradition in political thought.

This tradition culminates in [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]]'s _Philosophical Theory of [[state|the State]]_.

## Core Doctrines

### Justice as Harmony

[[plato|Plato]]'s central thesis is that **justice** (_dikaiosyne_) consists in each part performing its proper function. In the soul, this means reason ruling appetite and spirit. In the polis, it means each class—rulers, guardians, producers—fulfilling its distinctive role.

Justice is not a compromise among competing interests but a harmonious ordering in which each element contributes to the good of the whole.

| Element | In the Soul | In the Polis | Virtue |
|---------|-----------|-------------|--------|
| Rational | [[reason|Reason]] (λογιστικόν) | Philosopher-kings | Wisdom (σοφία) |
| Spirited | Spirit (θυμοειδές) | Guardians | Courage (ἀνδρεία) |
| Appetitive | Appetite (ἐπιθυμητικόν) | Producers | Temperance (σωφροσύνη) |

### The Ideal Polis

[[plato|Plato]]'s ideal state—the _kallipolis_—is governed by philosopher-kings who have grasped the Form of the Good. Its defining feature is that it is organized around knowledge rather than opinion, reason rather than appetite. The state exists not merely to secure life but to enable the good life.

### The Critique of Democracy

[[plato|Plato]]'s critique of democracy anticipates themes that [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] develops in his analysis of the limitations of merely procedural or aggregative conceptions of political legitimacy. For [[plato|Plato]], democracy fails because it treats all opinions as equally valid and all desires as equally worthy of satisfaction—it lacks a principle for distinguishing the rational from the merely popular.

## Bosanquet's Platonism

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] treats [[plato|Plato]] as a living philosophical presence rather than a historical figure. The [[greece|Greek]] conception of the polis as an ethical community, articulated most fully in the _Republic_, provides the classical foundation for [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]]'s own account of the state as the realization of reason in social life (001-024).

### The Greek Inheritance

For [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]], the [[greece|Greek]] achievement was to grasp that political life has an intrinsic rational structure. "In considering the [[greece|Greek city-state]]s in connection with the birth of political philosophy," he notes, "we find the idea that the state exists for the sake of the good life already fully formed" (001-005). The _Republic_ is the most complete expression of this insight.

### Limits of the Greek Polis

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] acknowledges the limitations of the [[greece|Greek city-state]]—its particularism, its dependence on slavery, its inability to transcend the distinction between [[greece|Greek]] and barbarian. These limitations do not invalidate the [[plato|Plato]]nic insight but indicate the need for its development. The modern state, in [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]]'s account, universalizes what the [[greece|Greek]] polis achieved in particular form.

### Plato and the Idealist Tradition

The lineage from [[plato|Plato]] through [[jean-jacques-rousseau|Rousseau]] and [[g-w-f-hegel|Hegel]] to [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] is a continuous tradition in which the state is understood as an ethical reality:

> We find law and political unity treated as the essential conditions of a rational human life from the earliest Greek reflection on the polis. (001-010)

[[plato|Plato]]'s _Republic_ inaugurates this tradition by demonstrating that political philosophy is not a subsidiary branch of [[morality|ethics]] but the inquiry into the conditions under which human beings can lead fully rational lives.
