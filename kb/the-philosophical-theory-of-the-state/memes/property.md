# Property

In [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]]'s _Philosophical Theory of [[state|the State]]_, **Property** is not a natural right antecedent to [[society|civil society]] but a juridical institution created and sustained by [[state|the State]]. It represents the external sphere within which the [[individuality|individual]] will can exercise its [[liberty|freedom]], recognised and guaranteed by the rational community.

## Property as a Creation of the State

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] explicitly rejects the [[john-locke|Locke]]an theory that property arises from the [[individuality|individual]]'s labour prior to government. For him, property is a social institution that can only exist within an organised [[community|political community]]:

> Property can only be founded on a positive title, and cannot be derived from the right of the first occupant. (004-046)

The right of property is thus not natural but civil. It presupposes the existence of [[state|the State]] as the guarantor of rights and the arbiter of competing claims. Without [[state|the State]]'s recognition and [[force|enforcement]], property would be mere possession backed by force — not a genuine right.

## Property and Liberty

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] follows the tradition that links property to liberty, but with a distinctive idealist inflection. Property is "[[liberty|civil liberty]] and the property of what [one] possesses" (004-045) — it is the external condition for the exercise of rational [[liberty|freedom]]. The connection between property and personality is essential: the [[individuality|individual]] requires a recognised sphere of external control in order to develop as a rational agent.

## The Sphere of Property

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] characterises [[society|civil society]] — the sphere of economic exchange and contract — as "the sphere of property" (010-014). In this sphere [[individuality|individual]]s interact through voluntary agreements concerning their external goods:

| Sphere | Principle | Mode of Relation |
|--------|-----------|-----------------|
| The [[state|State]] | Common good / [[general-will|General Will]] | Membership, participation |
| Civil [[society|Society]] (Property) | Contract, exchange | Agreement between persons |

The law of property is the archetypal form of law in [[society|civil society]]: "most especially in the law of property — Shylock's law — the sheer fact" of agreement takes on legal force (010-010). Yet [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] insists that this sphere, while necessary, is subordinate to [[state|the State]]'s higher ethical purpose.

## Property, Person, and the State

The [[state|State]]'s role extends to protecting "at once the person and the property of every member with the whole force of the community" (009-012). This dual protection — person and property — reflects the idealist view that personality requires both an internal and an external dimension. The [[state|State]] secures the external conditions so that the internal conditions can flourish:

> The protection for person and property may seem an easy thing to define, yet it involves the whole theory of the State. (006-042)

## Critiques of Natural Property Theories

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] is critical of theories that "account for property simply by the fact of occupation" (010-017). Such theories confuse the historical origin of possession with the rational justification of property as a right. A right requires recognition by a [[general-will|common will]] — and this can only be provided by [[state|the State]]. The _de facto_ must become _de jure_ through the rational organisation of the community:

> The theories which account for property simply by the fact of occupation... mistake the beginning for the end, the de facto for the de jure. (010-017)
