# Social Contract

The **social contract** is the hypothetical agreement by which [[individuality|individual]]s leave the state of nature and constitute themselves as a political society. In _The Philosophical Theory of [[state|the State]]_, [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] examines the social contract tradition—particularly [[jean-jacques-rousseau|Rousseau]]'s version—as a philosophical analysis of the rational basis of [[obligation|political obligation]].

## The Contract Tradition

The social contract idea runs through modern political philosophy from [[thomas-hobbes|Hobbes]] to [[immanuel-kant|Kant]]. In its classical form, it posits that [[individuality|individual]]s in a state of nature agree to surrender certain natural liberties in exchange for the security and benefits of [[society|civil society]]. [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] notes that [[jean-jacques-rousseau|Rousseau]] "substitutes, in answer to the historical fiction of a social contract," a deeper analysis of what makes a people a people (004-028).

## Rousseau's Social Pact

[[jean-jacques-rousseau|Rousseau]]'s formulation is distinct: the social pact is not a contract between [[individuality|individual]]s and a ruler but an agreement among [[individuality|individual]]s to form a collective [[morality|moral]] person. [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] summarizes [[jean-jacques-rousseau|Rousseau]]'s formula: "Each of us puts into the common stock his person and his entire powers under the supreme direction of the [[general-will|general will]]" (004-032).

The key innovation is that [[individuality|individual]]s do not alienate their [[liberty|freedom]] to a sovereign but transform it: "What man loses by the social contract is his [[liberty|natural liberty]] and an unlimited right to everything that tempts him and that he can get; what he gains is [[liberty|civil liberty]]" (004-045).

## Bosanquet's Assessment

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] treats the social contract not as a historical event but as a philosophical analysis of the rational conditions of political life. He defends [[jean-jacques-rousseau|Rousseau]] against the charge that the contract is a mere fiction by arguing that it captures the logical structure of [[obligation|political obligation]]: "that form of the supposed social contract in which it was regarded as a historical event" misunderstands its philosophical purpose (004-024).

However, [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] also acknowledges the limits of contract theory. The idea that [[obligation|political obligation]] rests on a promise is "a confusion" because it presupposes the very social framework it seeks to explain (010-016). The state is not a [[association|voluntary association]] that [[individuality|individual]]s may join or leave at will; it is the necessary condition of their [[morality|moral]] personality.
