# Sparta

Sparta appears in [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]]'s _Philosophical Theory of [[state|the State]]_ as one of the two exemplary [[greece|Greek city-state]]s, contrasted with Athens. The "[[individuality|individual]]ity of Athens and Sparta" represents the diversity of political forms within the common framework of the ancient polis (001-015).

## Sparta in Political Thought

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] groups Sparta with "republican [[rome|Rome]]" and other ancient republics as models that have influenced subsequent political thought (004-017). Sparta represented an ideal of civic virtue, discipline, and political unity that appealed to later thinkers from [[plato|Plato]] through [[jean-jacques-rousseau|Rousseau]] to the revolutionaries of [[europe|modern Europe]].

## Contrast with Athens

The contrast between Sparta and Athens—military discipline versus cultural achievement, oligarchic stability versus democratic innovation—is one of the fundamental oppositions of classical political thought. [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] uses this contrast to illustrate the range of political possibilities within the ancient city-state form. The fact that one person cannot be "both Spartan and Athenian" points to the way in which political identities were tied to specific communities in the ancient world (007-054).
