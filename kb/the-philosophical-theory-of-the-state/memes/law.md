# Law

In _The Philosophical Theory of [[state|the State]]_, [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] develops an idealist theory of law that rejects both the [[john-austin|Austin]]ian command theory and the [[individuality|individual]]ist view of law as inherently opposed to liberty. For [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]], **law is the systematic expression of the [[general-will|general will]]**—the rational structure through which a [[community|political community]] secures the conditions of its members' self-realization.

## Law as Expression of the General Will

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]]'s central claim is that law, properly understood, is not an external imposition on [[individuality|individual]]s but the articulation of their own [[real-will|rational will]] as members of a [[community|political community]]. Drawing on [[jean-jacques-rousseau|Rousseau]], he argues that "law and political unity" are treated as inseparable from the earliest [[greece|Greek]] reflection on the state (001-010). The [[greece|Greek]] distinction between government by law (_eunomia_) and arbitrary rule was already a recognition that [[authority|political authority]] must take a rational, universal form.

> The philosophical theory of the state does not treat law as a command with a penalty attached, but as a system of rights which expresses the rational will of a self-governing community. (002-047)

## The Critique of the Command Theory

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] directly challenges the [[john-austin|Austin]]ian conception of law as the command of a sovereign backed by sanctions. He traces this reductive view to [[jeremy-bentham|Bentham]], for whom "every law is an evil" because "every law, for him, is contrary to liberty" (003-010). The command theory, [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] argues, cannot explain why law commands obedience beyond mere fear of punishment—why [[citizenship|citizen]]s recognize law as _binding_ rather than merely _compulsory_.

The facts of "recognised obligation, [[morality|moral]]ity, and the usages of civilized communities" set themselves against any theory that reduces law to force (002-039). A legal system is not a collection of threats but a framework of mutual recognition that makes cooperative social life possible.

| Theory of Law | Core Claim | [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]]'s Criticism |
|--------------|-----------|----------------------|
| [[john-austin|Austin]]ian command theory | Law = sovereign command + sanction | Cannot explain obligation; confuses force with authority |
| Natural law (abstract) | Law derives from pre-political rights | Inverts the real relation; rights presuppose social recognition |
| [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]]'s idealist theory | Law = expression of the [[general-will|general will]] | — (the position he defends) |

## Law, Liberty, and Self-Government

For [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]], the apparent opposition between law and liberty dissolves once we understand that true [[liberty|freedom]] is not the absence of restraint but the actualization of one's rational nature through participation in a law-governed community. "Government by one's own law"—the [[greece|Greek]] idea of _eunomia_—is the original form in which the connection between law and [[liberty|freedom]] was grasped (001-007).

The [[citizenship|citizen]] who obeys the law is not submitting to an alien power but to the conditions of their own rational [[autonomy|self-determination]]. This is the philosophical truth behind [[jean-jacques-rousseau|Rousseau]]'s paradox that [[citizenship|citizen]]s can be "forced to be free": law, when it genuinely expresses the [[general-will|general will]], removes the obstacles to rational [[liberty|freedom]] rather than imposing external constraints.

## The System of Rights

Law, in [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]]'s account, constitutes and maintains a **[[rights|system of rights]]**. [[rights|Rights]] are not pre-political possessions that [[individuality|individual]]s bring to the state but "positions" recognized and maintained by the legal order (008-065). The "whole of 'rights' attaching to the selves who are determined by [[state|the State]]" forms a coherent structure in which each right implies a corresponding obligation (008-072).

> The system of rights which the State maintains may be described as the organization of the conditions of rational self-realization for all members of the community. (008-067)

This is a decisive break with [[natural-right|natural rights]] theory. [[rights|Rights]] are not prior to the state but constituted by it; they are "the imperative position which [[state|the State]] maintains" (008-076) in the service of the common good.

## Law and the Modern State

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] traces the development of legal order from the [[greece|Greek city-state]] through [[rome|Roman]] jurisprudence to the modern nation-state. The [[greece|Greek]] achievement was to grasp that "law and political unity" are inseparable; the [[rome|Roman]] contribution was the universalization of legal reason; the modern task is to reconcile the rational universality of law with the particularity of national life.

In this synthesis, law is neither the arbitrary will of a sovereign nor a set of abstract natural principles but the concrete expression of a people's rational self-consciousness—what [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]], following [[g-w-f-hegel|Hegel]], calls _objective spirit_.
