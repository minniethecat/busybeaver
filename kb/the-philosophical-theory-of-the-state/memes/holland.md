# Holland

Holland—the Dutch Republic or the Netherlands—receives brief reference in [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]]'s _Philosophical Theory of [[state|the State]]_, primarily as a historical example in the development of modern international law and republican [[institution|political institutions]].

## Holland in Political History

The Dutch Republic of the seventeenth and eighteenth centuries represented an important alternative to monarchical government in early [[europe|modern Europe]]. Dutch political experience contributed to the development of republican theory and practice, as well as to the emergence of international law through the work of Hugo Grotius. [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]]'s theory of the state, while primarily focused on the philosophical tradition of [[germany|German]] and [[england|British]] idealism, acknowledges the broader [[europe|European]] history within which that tradition developed.

Holland also serves as an example of the smaller [[europe|European]] state that, despite limited territorial extent, played a significant role in the development of modern political and legal [[institution|institutions]]. The Dutch case illustrates the distinction between the size of a state and its contribution to political civilization—a distinction relevant to [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]]'s argument that the significance of the state lies not in its power but in its embodiment of rational principles.
