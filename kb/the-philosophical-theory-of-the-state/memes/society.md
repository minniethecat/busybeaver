# Society

For [[bernard-bosanquet|Bernard Bosanquet]], **Society** is not a mere aggregate of [[individuality|individual]]s but a living, organic whole — "an actual living society is an infinitely higher creature than any [[individuality|individual]] can be" (P01-010). This conviction, drawn from [[plato|Plato]], [[aristotle|Aristotle]], [[jean-jacques-rousseau|Rousseau]], and [[g-w-f-hegel|Hegel]], is the foundation of his entire political philosophy.

## Society as an Organism

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] insists that society must be understood as a genuine unity, not a fiction or a shorthand for a collection of separate [[individuality|individual]]s. The outward organisation of society "is really as it were a body which is constituted by the [[community|common life]]" (001-009):

> They hardly believe in actual society as a botanist believes in plants. (P01-009)

This organic metaphor is not merely decorative. It means that [[individuality|individual]]s are not self-sufficient atoms who subsequently enter into social relations; rather, they are constituted as persons through their participation in the social whole. The "sap of society" circulates within all its [[institution|institutions]] (001-019).

## Society and the Individual

The relationship between society and the [[individuality|individual]] is not one of opposition but of mutual constitution. [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] repeatedly criticises theories that treat the [[individuality|individual]] as a pre-social datum whose rights and interests are then limited by society:

> The philosophical conception of society was confronted by an antagonistic view — the view that the individual is the sole reality, and society a mere abstraction or aggregate. (001-010)

For [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]], this atomistic view is philosophically untenable. The [[individuality|individual]] achieves selfhood only within a community of minds. The question is not how the [[individuality|individual]] limits society or vice versa, but how each finds its completion in the other.

## Civil Society vs. the State

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] adopts and develops the [[g-w-f-hegel|Hegel]]ian distinction between civil society (_bürgerliche Gesellschaft_) and [[state|the State]]:

| Dimension | Civil Society | The [[state|State]] |
|-----------|--------------|-----------|
| Principle | Particular interest | Universal / Common good |
| Mode of relation | Contract, exchange | Membership, [[citizenship|citizen]]ship |
| Sphere | Economics, property | [[law|Law]], political participation |
| Freedom type | Formal, negative | Substantive, positive |

Civil society is the sphere of "the self-governing [[individuality|individual]] community" (001-016) — the realm of economic activity, contract, and private interest. The [[state|State]], by contrast, is the sphere in which particular interests are transcended and the common good is pursued for its own sake.

## Modern Society and the Scientific Spirit

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] acknowledges the contribution of modern sociology and evolutionary thought to the understanding of society. The inclusion of "human society among the objects of natural science" (002-003) has taught us to see society as a continuous development:

> It has made us see that human society is not a mere aggregate but a structure with a life and history of its own. (002-010)

Yet he warns against reducing society to a merely biological or mechanical phenomenon. Human society corresponds to "an [[individuality|individual]] mind" more than to an [[individuality|individual]] body (002-013), because the bond of society is consciousness and will, not mere physical causation.

## Society and the General Will

Ultimately, for [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]], society points beyond itself toward [[state|the State]]. The "will of the whole society 'as such'" (005-015) is the [[general-will|General Will]] — and this will cannot be adequately expressed in the diffuse interactions of civil society alone. It requires the institutional structure of [[state|the State]]:

> It is, we might say, the will of the whole society "as such" — not the will of individuals in their private capacity, but the will that emerges when they think and act as members of a rational whole. (005-015)
