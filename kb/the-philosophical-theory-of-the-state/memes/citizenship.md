# Citizenship

The philosophical status of membership in a [[community|political community]], which in [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]]'s idealist framework rests not on a contractual agreement between [[individuality|individual]]s but on the recognition of law as an expression of one's own [[real-will|rational will]] within an organic social whole (009-040).

## Greek Origins and the Cosmopolitan Critique

The problem of citizenship first emerged in ancient [[greece|Greek]] political philosophy, where [[aristotle|Aristotle]] defined man as a creature formed for the life of the City-state. However, even during the genesis of this conception, an antagonistic view was powerfully represented. Some [[individuality|individual]]s rejected identification with a single [[community|political community]], "disdaining to identify himself with the citizenship of a single state, but claiming to be a stranger in the city and a citizen of the world" (001-010). This tension between particular civic membership and universal cosmopolitan identity set the terms for the entire subsequent philosophical debate about citizenship.

## Citizenship and the Recognition of Law

For [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]], the essence of citizenship is not a formal legal status but a substantive recognition of the state's law as one's own will. The criminal who fails altogether to recognise "in any form the assertion of something which he normally respects in the law which punishes him" exemplifies the negation of citizenship: "he is outlawed by himself and the essentials of citizenship are not in him" (008-133). Citizenship thus requires an internal acknowledgment that the law which constrains the [[individuality|individual]] is in fact the expression of a rational system to which the [[individuality|individual]] himself belongs.

## The Organic Foundation of Citizenship

In the development of [[germany|German idealism]], [[johann-gottlieb-fichte|Fichte]] transformed the contractarian basis of citizenship into an organic theory. The "contract" on which citizenship rests, "by the fact that it is general, forges an indiscernible unity of the social whole" (009-040). Unlike earlier contract theories that conceived citizenship as a voluntary agreement between independent [[individuality|individual]]s, [[johann-gottlieb-fichte|Fichte]]'s organic conception treats [[state|the State]] as "a necessary implication of the human self" — for a self involves a society of selves, and law is simply the relation between selves in a bodily world.
