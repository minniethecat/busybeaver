# Political Obligation

**Political obligation** is the [[morality|moral]] [[obligation|duty]] to obey the law and support the [[institution|institutions]] of the state. In _The Philosophical Theory of [[state|the State]]_, [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] seeks to ground [[obligation|political obligation]] not in consent, fear, or calculation but in the rational nature of the [[individuality|individual]] as a member of a [[community|political community]].

## The Problem

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] opens his discussion by noting that the phenomena of [[obligation|political obligation]] appear paradoxical: [[individuality|individual]]s feel bound by laws they did not personally enact and by decisions they may actively oppose. He writes of the "true root and ground of [[obligation|political obligation]]" as a fundamental problem of political philosophy (003-062). If popular instinct insists that government must rest on consent, how can law bind the dissenting [[individuality|individual]]?

## Bosanquet's Solution

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]]'s answer draws on [[jean-jacques-rousseau|Rousseau]]'s theory of the [[general-will|general will]]. Political obligation is grounded in the fact that the law expresses the [[individuality|individual]]'s own [[real-will|rational will]]. The obligation to obey is not an external imposition but a recognition that the law articulates the conditions of one's own [[liberty|freedom]]. "This is the ultimate root of [[obligation|political obligation]]" (006-069): the [[individuality|individual]] finds in the state the expression of their own rational nature.

> We find the only true account of political obligation in the idea that the self, in obeying the law which expresses the general will, is obeying nothing but its own rational nature. (006-076)

## Obligation and the Real Will

The distinction between [[real-will|actual will]] and real will is essential to [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]]'s theory of [[obligation|political obligation]]. The [[individuality|individual]] whose [[real-will|actual will]] conflicts with the law may still be obligated because the law expresses their real will—what they would will if they were fully rational. This is not a device for justifying tyranny but an analysis of what it means to be a member of a rational community.

## The Limits of Consent Theory

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] critiques consent-based theories of obligation as philosophically superficial. [[consent|Consent]] presupposes the very [[morality|moral]] personality that political society makes possible—it cannot serve as the foundation of that society. The true ground of obligation is not a historical act of agreement but the ongoing rational relationship between the [[individuality|individual]] and the [[community|political community]].
