# Natural Right

The doctrine of **natural right** holds that [[individuality|individual]]s possess certain rights by nature, independently of any political constitution. In _The Philosophical Theory of [[state|the State]]_, [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] traces the historical role of natural right theory in the development of modern political thought while arguing that it must ultimately be transcended by a more adequate idealist account of rights and [[obligation|political obligation]].

## Historical Significance

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] acknowledges the historical importance of natural right theory. The conception that [[individuality|individual]]s have rights prior to the state served as a critical tool against arbitrary government and helped establish the modern idea of limited [[authority|political authority]]. He notes that "the conception of natural right and a law of nature makes a standard to which the [[law|positive law]] of the state is responsible" (001-016). [[jean-jacques-rousseau|Rousseau]] himself, despite his transformative theory of the [[general-will|general will]], drew on the language of natural right: "the vulgar notion of [[sovereignty|popular sovereignty]] and of natural right may have been necessary to do justice to the idea that government exists for the people" (001-025).

## Bosanquet's Critique

Despite its historical utility, [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] argues that natural right theory is philosophically inadequate. The idea that rights exist prior to society is, in his view, a fiction. [[rights|Rights]] are not pre-political possessions of isolated [[individuality|individual]]s but are constituted by the recognition of the community. He observes that the theory "culminates against the idea of natural right as representing those claims" that [[individuality|individual]]s make against society (003-040).

The concept of natural right, [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] contends, resulted from the breakdown of the [[greece|Greek city-state]] and the rise of [[individuality|individual]]ism—it "resulted in the conception of natural right" as a way of making sense of human claims outside the framework of the polis (002-037). But this historical origin also reveals its limitation: it treats as natural what is actually the product of a specific historical situation.

## The Idealist Alternative

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]]'s alternative is that rights are aspects of the [[individuality|individual]]'s position within the rational state. "The idea of [[rights|individual rights]] comes down to us from the doctrine of natural right, and has generally been discussed in abstraction from the idea of the state" (008-063). His task is to reconnect rights with their true foundation: the [[morality|moral]] personality that is only realized through participation in the rational community. The state does not _grant_ rights but _recognizes_ them as necessary conditions for the development of personality.
