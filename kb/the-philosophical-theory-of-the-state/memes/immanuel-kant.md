# Immanuel Kant

Immanuel Kant (1724–1804) is a foundational figure for the idealist tradition in which [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] works. While [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] follows [[g-w-f-hegel|Hegel]] in criticizing certain aspects of Kant's [[morality|moral]] philosophy—particularly its [[individuality|individual]]ism—he draws on Kant's concept of autonomy and rational [[autonomy|self-determination]].

## Kant's Contribution

### Autonomy and Self-Legislation

Kant's concept of autonomy—the capacity of a rational being to act according to laws they give to themselves—is one of the most influential ideas in modern [[morality|moral]] philosophy (003-049). [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] adopts this concept but transforms it: for [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]], autonomy is achieved not in isolation but through participation in the rational community (003-044). The [[individuality|individual]] who obeys the [[general-will|general will]] is self-governing in Kant's sense—acting according to a law that reason gives.

### The Kingdom of Ends

Kant's idea of the kingdom of ends—a systematic union of rational beings under common laws—anticipates [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]]'s vision of the state as a rational community. [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] socializes Kant's [[morality|moral]] kingdom, arguing that the state is the institutional form of the union of [[real-will|rational will]]s.

## Beyond Kant

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] follows [[g-w-f-hegel|Hegel]] in arguing that Kant's [[morality|moral]] philosophy is too abstract. Kant treats the [[morality|moral]] law as a formal principle that the [[individuality|individual]] applies in isolation, without adequate attention to the social and institutional conditions of [[morality|moral]] life. [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]]'s theory seeks to overcome this limitation by embedding rational [[autonomy|self-determination]] in the concrete life of the [[community|political community]].
