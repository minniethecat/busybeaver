# Lectures and Essays

_Lectures and Essays on Natural Theology and Ethics_ (1898) is a posthumous collection of lectures by [[will|Will]]iam Wallace, Whyte's Professor of Moral Philosophy at Oxford and translator of [[g-w-f-hegel|Hegel]]. [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] cites Wallace at several points in _The Philosophical Theory of [[state|the State]]_, drawing on his account of the ethical self and his interpretation of [[g-w-f-hegel|Hegel]]'s social philosophy.

## Core Arguments

Wallace argued that [[morality|ethics]] cannot be founded on natural theology alone; the [[morality|moral]] life requires an account of the self as actively constituting its world through [[reason|practical reason]]. His lectures develop a broadly [[g-w-f-hegel|Hegel]]ian account of the relation between [[individuality|individual]] selfhood and [[institution|social institutions]], emphasizing that the self is not a given datum but an achievement—something constituted through its relations to others and to the institutional structures of ethical life.

## Bosanquet's Use

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] cites Wallace's _Lectures and Essays_ in support of the argument that the [[individuality|individual]] self is not a fixed atom but a developing structure whose content is social through and through (012-428). Wallace's account of the ethical self as constituted by its practical engagements provides philosophical support for [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]]'s central claim that the state is not external to [[individuality|individual]] [[liberty|freedom]] but is the institutional form of the [[individuality|individual]]'s own [[real-will|rational will]].

Wallace's interpretation of [[g-w-f-hegel|Hegel]] is also significant for [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]]. As one of the first major [[england|English]] translators and interpreters of [[g-w-f-hegel|Hegel]], Wallace helped shape the reception of [[germany|German idealism]] in [[england|Britain]]. [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] follows Wallace in reading [[g-w-f-hegel|Hegel]] not as an apologist for [[prussia|Prussian]] authoritarianism but as a philosopher of [[liberty|freedom]] who saw the state as the institutional condition for human self-realization.
