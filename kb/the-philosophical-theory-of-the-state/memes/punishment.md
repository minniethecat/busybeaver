# Punishment

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] treats the **right of the state to punish** as a test case for his theory of the [[general-will|general will]] (008-062). In Chapter VIII, he argues that punishment is not retribution imposed by an external authority but the reassertion of the criminal's own [[real-will|rational will]] against their particular transgression.

## The Paradox of Punishment

The justification of punishment presents a paradox parallel to that of [[obligation|political obligation]]: how can the state justly inflict harm on [[individuality|individual]]s in the name of their own good? [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]]'s answer builds on the distinction between the particular will and the [[general-will|general will]]. The criminal, in violating the law, acts on their particular will against the [[general-will|general will]] which, as [[citizenship|citizen]], they also possess (008-062).

## Punishment as Self-Enforcement

For [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]], punishment is not an external imposition but the [[force|enforcement]] of the criminal's own rational nature. The law that punishes is the same law that the criminal, as a rational being, wills. Punishment thus "annuls the crime (008-063)" by reasserting the [[general-will|general will]] against the particular act that denied it.

## The Limits of Deterrence Theory

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] criticizes purely utilitarian theories of punishment that treat it merely as a means of deterrence. While deterrence may be a consequence of punishment, it cannot be its justification—any more than the state itself can be justified merely by its usefulness. The justification must lie in the rational relationship between the [[individuality|individual]] and the law.
