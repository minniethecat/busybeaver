# Nation

In [[bernard-bosanquet|Bernard Bosanquet]]'s _The Philosophical Theory of [[state|the State]]_ (1899), the **nation** is understood not as a mere aggregate of [[individuality|individual]]s but as a living community of shared culture, spirit, and historical consciousness — a natural formation that provides the ethical substance out of which [[state|the State]] emerges as a rational unity (002-047).

## National Consciousness and the Individual

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] traces the emergence of national consciousness to the seventeenth century, when "the national consciousness of the [[england|English]] people may fairly be said to date" (001-015). This consciousness is not an artificial construct but a growth — a shared understanding that binds [[individuality|individual]]s into a community before any formal political structure:

> The revival of national individuality which was reflected in the literature of the Elizabethan age is the type of such a consciousness. (002-047)

The [[individuality|individual]], for [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]], is shaped and nourished by "his race and his nation" (002-028). Far from being an external constraint, the national context provides the very materials out of which [[individuality|individual]] personality is formed. The national spirit is a participant in the [[individuality|individual]]'s rational life:

> From the side of the national spirit and culture — this should seem as real an influence in determining the mind's growth as any that we can point to. (002-039)

## The Nation and the Family

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] sees the nation as an extension of the same natural principle that produces [[family|the family]] and the clan:

| Level | Form of [[association|Association]] | Principle |
|-------|-------------------|-----------|
| [[family|Family]] | Natural kinship | Immediate bonds of affection |
| Clan / Tribe | Extended kinship | Shared ancestry and custom |
| Nation | Cultural community | Common language, history, spirit |
| [[state|State]] | Rational-political unity | General will and law |

The nation occupies a middle position between the intimate bonds of family life and the abstract [[reason|rationality]] of [[state|the State]]. It is, for [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]], "a growth, an expression of the same principle that makes [[family|the family]] and the clan and the nation" (001-008).

## National Mind and the Conception of Character

Drawing on idealist philosophy, [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] develops the idea of a "national mind" — not a mystical entity but a real pattern of shared thought, valuation, and purpose:

> The conception of a national mind and character takes its place as a factor in the determination of the individual's growth. (002-048)

This conception is crucial for [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]]'s larger argument: if a national mind can be acknowledged as real, then [[state|the State]] — as the rational organisation of that national life — is no more mysterious. Both are manifestations of mind working through [[institution|social institutions]].

## The Nation-State and the General Will

The nation provides the cultural substance of the [[general-will|general will]]. Without a shared national life, the [[general-will|general will]] would be an empty abstraction. But [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] is careful to distinguish the nation from [[state|the State]]: the nation is a community of culture and sentiment; [[state|the State]] is the nation organised for rational self-direction through law and [[institution|institutions]].

The nation's customs, traditions, and shared understandings form the "ethical substance" (a term [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] borrows from [[g-w-f-hegel|Hegel]]) that [[state|the State]] rationalises and protects. The transition from nation to [[state|State]] is the transition from implicitly shared life to explicitly recognised rational order.

## Bosanquet's Use of the Concept

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] employs the concept of nation primarily in three contexts:

1. **Historical explanation**: The rise of national consciousness provides the historical backdrop for modern political philosophy, supplanting the non-national creeds of antiquity — "Epicureanism, the earliest non-national creeds of the western world" (001-011)

2. **Sociological grounding**: The nation illustrates how social unities are real and formative, not merely aggregates — a key premise for the organic theory of [[state|the State]]

3. **Philosophical idealism**: The national mind serves as an analogical bridge to the concept of [[state|the State]] as a [[morality|moral]] person — if national consciousness is real, [[state|the State]]'s [[real-will|rational will]] is no less real
