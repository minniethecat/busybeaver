# Prussia

Prussia occupies a distinctive place in [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]]'s _Philosophical Theory of [[state|the State]]_ as the political entity most closely associated with [[g-w-f-hegel|Hegel]]'s political philosophy. The charge that [[g-w-f-hegel|Hegel]]'s theory of the state is merely a rationalization of the Prussian status quo is one that [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] directly addresses and seeks to refute.

## Hegel and the Prussian State

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] confronts the criticism that "[[g-w-f-hegel|Hegel]] found his ideal in the Prussian bureaucracy" (009-051). He argues that this reading misunderstands both [[g-w-f-hegel|Hegel]]'s philosophical method and the actual character of the Prussian state in [[g-w-f-hegel|Hegel]]'s time. The Prussian state of the early nineteenth century was not the militaristic autocracy of later reputation but a state undergoing significant reform.

The reformist character of the Prussian state is suggested by [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]]'s reference to "Hardenberg, the Prussian minister," who exclaimed upon hearing of political developments elsewhere—indicating the open and progressive spirit of at least some elements within the Prussian administration (009-055). [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] argues that [[g-w-f-hegel|Hegel]]'s philosophy of the state "is pretty much the Prussian [[state|State]] of his time, tempered by the constitutional ideas" that were then current in reformist circles (009-061).

## Bosanquet's Defense

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]]'s treatment of Prussia is ultimately a defense of [[g-w-f-hegel|Hegel]] against the charge of political conservatism. The Prussian state that [[g-w-f-hegel|Hegel]] described was, in [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]]'s reading, not a finished product but a work in progress—a state in the process of developing constitutional forms adequate to modern [[liberty|freedom]]. [[g-w-f-hegel|Hegel]]'s philosophy of the state, far from being an apology for absolutism, is an account of the rational structure that any modern state must embody to be worthy of the name.
