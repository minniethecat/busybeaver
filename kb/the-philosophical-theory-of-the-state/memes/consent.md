# Consent

In [[bernard-bosanquet|Bernard Bosanquet]]'s _The Philosophical Theory of [[state|the State]]_ (1899), the **consent** theory of [[obligation|political obligation]] — traceable to [[john-locke|Locke]] and influential in liberal thought — is subjected to sustained critique. For [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]], the authority of [[state|the State]] cannot be grounded in the express or tacit consent of [[individuality|individual]]s, because the [[individuality|individual]] will that would do the consenting is itself constituted by the social whole.

## The Inadequacy of Tacit Consent

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] directly challenges the idea that [[obligation|political obligation]] can be explained by "those hypotheses of tacit consent which would reduce a community to a partnership" (003-059). The metaphor of contract, however refined, misrepresents the relation between [[individuality|individual]] and [[state|State]]:

> This is more than consent or concord; it is a real unity of them all in one and the same person. (005-009)

The unity of [[state|the State]], following [[jean-jacques-rousseau|Rousseau]], is not that of a contractual agreement among separate parties but that of a single [[morality|moral]] person constituted by the rational cooperation of its members. Consent theories, by treating [[individuality|individual]]s as pre-political atoms who subsequently agree to form society, reverse the actual order of things: [[individuality|individual]]s are formed by society before they can consent to anything.

## Locke's Idea of Consent

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] engages critically with [[john-locke|Locke]]'s formulation that there is "no power but that established by consent in the commonwealth" (004-056, quoting [[john-locke|Locke]]). He acknowledges the [[morality|moral]] impulse behind this view — the insistence that government must respect the rational agency of the governed — but argues that it fails to account for the depth of the [[individuality|individual]]'s membership in the community:

| Theorist | Basis of [[obligation|Obligation]] | [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]]'s Criticism |
|----------|-------------------|----------------------|
| [[john-locke|Locke]] | Express or tacit consent | Assumes a pre-social [[individuality|individual]] who then chooses society |
| [[jean-jacques-rousseau|Rousseau]] | The [[general-will|general will]] (not mere consent) | Rightly sees unity as deeper than agreement |
| [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] | The real will realised through [[state|the State]] | [[obligation|Obligation]] arises from rational nature, not contingent agreement |

> The comparison with Locke's idea of consent is instructive. For Locke, consent creates the obligation; for Bosanquet, the obligation is already implicit in the rational nature of the self, and the State is the form in which that nature achieves actuality. (004-055)

## Consent and the Real Will

The deepest problem with consent theory, in [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]]'s view, is that it assumes the [[individuality|individual]] knows his own [[real-will|true will]] prior to and independently of social life. But the [[individuality|individual]]'s immediate desires — what he would consent to if asked — may be at odds with his real will:

> One who takes the rights of the citizen without consenting to fulfil the duties of the citizen is in contradiction with himself. (004-041)

[[rights|Rights]] and duties are correlative, and both are constituted by [[state|the State]]. One cannot consent to the benefits of political order while withholding consent from its obligations without falling into practical contradiction. The real will — as distinct from the arbitrary will of the moment — always consents to the rational order of [[state|the State]], because that order is the condition of its own realisation.

## Bosanquet's Use of the Concept

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] deploys the critique of consent in three argumentative contexts:

1. **Against [[individuality|individual]]ism**: Consent theory presupposes the very atomistic [[individuality|individual]]ism that [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]]'s organic theory rejects. If [[individuality|individual]]s are constituted by social relations, consent cannot be the foundation of those relations.

2. **For the [[general-will|general will]]**: By showing that consent is inadequate, [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] clears the ground for [[jean-jacques-rousseau|Rousseau]]'s concept of the [[general-will|general will]] — a unity that is "more than consent or concord."

3. **For [[obligation|political obligation]]**: True [[obligation|political obligation]] arises not from an act of agreement but from the rational recognition that one's own fulfilment is inseparable from the common good.
