# G.W.F. Hegel

Georg Wilhelm Friedrich Hegel (1770–1831) is, after [[jean-jacques-rousseau|Rousseau]], the most significant philosophical influence on [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]]'s _Philosophical Theory of [[state|the State]]_. [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] draws on Hegel's concept of objective spirit, his account of the rational state as the actualization of [[liberty|freedom]], and his dialectical method for reconciling the apparent opposition between [[individuality|individual]] and community.

Hegel spent his most productive years at the University of Berlin (1818–1831), where his lectures attracted students from across [[europe|Europe]]. His political philosophy, developed most fully in the _[[philosophy-of-right|Philosophy of Right]]_ (1821), represents the culmination of [[germany|German idealism]]'s engagement with the legacy of the [[french-revolution|French Revolution]] and the Napoleonic reorganization of [[europe|Europe]].

## Hegel's Influence on Bosanquet

### Objective Spirit

Hegel's concept of **objective spirit** (_objektiver Geist_)—the embodiment of reason in [[institution|social institutions]]—provides the philosophical framework for [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]]'s entire theory. The state, for Hegel, is not merely a mechanism for protecting [[rights|individual rights]] but the concrete realization of ethical life (_[[morality|Sittlichkeit]]_). [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] draws on this concept throughout his argument:

> The mind as a second nature—this is the character which social institutions take on when they genuinely express the rational will of a community. (008-070, quoting Hegel's _Philosophie des Rechts_)

### The Rational State

Hegel's insistence that the state is rational—that it embodies reason rather than mere power—is central to [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]]'s argument against [[individuality|individual]]ist and utilitarian theories. "The philosophical theory of the state springs from the same Evangelical tradition as the ethical theory of [[immanuel-kant|Kant]], [[johann-gottlieb-fichte|Fichte]], and Hegel" (009-002).

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] follows Hegel in treating the state as the condition of [[individuality|individual]] [[liberty|freedom]] rather than its limitation.

> It is true on the whole to say with Hegel that, in the matter of a civilized community, the state is the realization of freedom. (008-148)

### Dialectic of Individual and Community

Hegel's dialectical method informs [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]]'s treatment of the most fundamental problem in political philosophy: the relation between [[individuality|individual]] liberty and [[authority|state authority]]. The opposition between these terms is, for both thinkers, a false one—the [[individuality|individual]] achieves genuine [[liberty|freedom]] only through rational participation in the life of the state.

This is the philosophical meaning of Hegel's controversial claim that the state is "the actuality of the ethical Idea"—not that empirical governments are perfect, but that the rational structure of [[community|political community]] is the condition under which [[individuality|individual]]s can lead fully human lives.

## Bosanquet's Hegelianism

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] was a leading figure in the [[england|British]] idealist movement, which sought to integrate Hegelian philosophy with [[england|British]] political thought. His _Philosophical Theory of [[state|the State]]_ is an extended defense of Hegel's core insights—the [[reason|rationality]] of the state, the reality of the [[general-will|general will]], and the reconciliation of liberty and authority.

These arguments are presented in terms accessible to Anglo-[[america|American]] readers shaped by the liberal tradition.

### Defending Hegel Against Misunderstanding

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] directly addresses the common criticism that Hegel's political philosophy is authoritarian:

> If Hegel had wished to have a partisan purpose, he could hardly have selected a worse means than his book. (009-052)

He argues that Hegel's account of the rational state has been conflated with apologetics for the [[prussia|Prussian]] monarchy, when in fact the _[[philosophy-of-right|Philosophy of Right]]_ presents a normative ideal against which actual states are to be measured. The publication of the book "with a preface attacking the superficiality of prevailing political thought" (009-055) confirms its critical, not conformist, intent.

### Beyond Hegel

While deeply indebted to Hegel, [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] is not an uncritical disciple. He draws equally on [[jean-jacques-rousseau|Rousseau]]'s democratic theory and the [[england|British]] liberal tradition to temper Hegel's tendency toward metaphysical abstraction. The resulting synthesis is distinctively [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]]'s own: an idealist theory of the state grounded in the concrete experience of [[community|political community]].
