# Coercion

For [[bernard-bosanquet|Bernard Bosanquet]], **Coercion** is the central puzzle of political theory. How can a community of free beings legitimately coerce its own members? The answer, he argues in _The Philosophical Theory of [[state|the State]]_, lies in understanding coercion not as alien force but as the self-assertion of the [[real-will|rational will]] against the merely particular will.

## The Paradox of Self-Coercion

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] frames the problem starkly: how can a person "exercise authority or coercion over himself" (003-005)? This is the paradox of self-government in its sharpest form. If the [[individuality|individual]] is free, how can [[state|the State]] coerce him?

> The question is how a human being can exercise authority or coercion over himself; how, in short, a person can be both the ruler and the ruled. (003-005)

The solution lies in the distinction between the [[real-will|actual will]] and the real will. When [[state|the State]] coerces an [[individuality|individual]], it is not an alien power acting upon him but his own [[real-will|rational will]] — [[will|the will]] he would have if he were fully rational — overcoming his momentary and particular desires.

## Coercion as Social, Not Merely Physical

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] insists that [[state|State]] coercion is fundamentally different from mere [[force|physical force]]. It is "accepted as authority or social coercion" (003-006), not simply endured as violence:

> The term coercion, when we speak of the State, must be understood not as brute force but as the organised pressure of a rational system upon the recalcitrant particular will. (003-006)

This is why [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] can argue that coercion is compatible with [[liberty|freedom]]: the [[individuality|individual]] who is coerced by [[state|the State]] is being "forced to be free," in [[jean-jacques-rousseau|Rousseau]]'s famous phrase. The coercion enforces the conditions under which genuine [[liberty|freedom]] is possible for all.

## The Justification of State Coercion

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] finds "a justification of social coercion latent in the term 'self-government'" (003-062). If the government genuinely represents the [[real-will|rational will]] of the governed, then its coercive acts are acts of the community upon itself:

| Type of [[force|Force]] | Source | Legitimacy |
|--------------|--------|-----------|
| Private violence | Particular will | Illegitimate |
| [[state|State]] coercion | General will / common good | Legitimate, if proportional |
| Social pressure | Custom, opinion | Variable |

> The justification of social coercion latent in the term "self-government" is that it is the community acting upon itself to maintain the conditions of its own rational life. (003-062)

## Coercion and the Common Good

Coercion is legitimate only when it serves the common good. [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] acknowledges that "it would seem, with the coercion of some by others as a necessary element in all government" (003-058), but this appearance dissolves when we recognise that the coercing agency is not "some" but the community as a whole:

> What appears as coercion exercised by "the others" over "the one" reveals itself, on examination, as coercion exercised by the community over its own members in the interest of the common life. (003-059)

## Coercion, Intention, and Liberty

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] addresses the objection that "coercion as a means to liberty" (008-041) is a contradiction. His response is that liberty, properly understood, is not the absence of all restraint but the presence of the conditions for rational self-direction. Coercion that removes obstacles to this rational self-direction — for instance, laws against fraud or violence — is not the enemy of liberty but its servant.
