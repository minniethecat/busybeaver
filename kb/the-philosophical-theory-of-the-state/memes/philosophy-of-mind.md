# Philosophy of Mind

[[g-w-f-hegel|Hegel]]'s _Philosophy of Mind_ (_Philosophie des Geistes_) is the third and culminating part of his _Encyclopedia of the Philosophical Sciences_ (1817, revised 1827 and 1830). [[will|Will]]iam Wallace's [[england|English]] translation (1894) made this text available to [[england|British]] readers, and [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] draws on it extensively, particularly for [[g-w-f-hegel|Hegel]]'s theory of the relation between subjective and objective mind.

## Core Arguments

The _Philosophy of Mind_ traces the development of Geist (mind/spirit) through three stages: Subjective Mind (anthropology, phenomenology, psychology), Objective Mind (right, [[morality|moral]]ity, ethical life), and Absolute Mind (art, religion, philosophy). The section on Objective Mind contains [[g-w-f-hegel|Hegel]]'s political philosophy in its most condensed form. [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] notes that [[g-w-f-hegel|Hegel]]'s treatment of the modern state as a chapter in the _Philosophy of Mind_ is itself philosophically significant—it embeds political philosophy within the larger science of spirit (009-068).

For [[g-w-f-hegel|Hegel]], mind is not a substance but an activity—the process by which spirit comes to know itself through its objectifications in law, [[institution|institutions]], and the state. The analysis of the state in the _Philosophy of Mind_ proceeds through the "earlier division" comprising Anthropology and Phenomenology, and culminates in the treatment of Objective Mind (009-076).

## Bosanquet's Use

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] relies on the _Philosophy of Mind_ for two central claims. First, that the [[individuality|individual]] mind is not a self-contained unit but a structure whose content is derived from social participation. He cites Wallace's translation and commentary directly, drawing on Wallace's editorial analysis of [[g-w-f-hegel|Hegel]]'s concept of mind (009-057). Second, that the state is not external to [[individuality|individual]] [[liberty|freedom]] but is the institutional form through which [[liberty|freedom]] becomes actual.

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]]'s theory of the state as the "real will" of [[individuality|individual]]s draws on [[g-w-f-hegel|Hegel]]'s account in the _Philosophy of Mind_ of how subjective will is transformed through its participation in objective [[institution|institutions]]. He traces this through the second division of the _Philosophy of Mind_, under the name of Mind Objective, where the transition from subjective to objective spirit is most fully articulated (009-079). Wallace's commentary is cited by [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] as an authoritative guide to [[g-w-f-hegel|Hegel]]'s conception of the relation between subjective mind and the objective [[institution|institutions]] of ethical life (010-056).
