# Principles of Political Obligation

_Lectures on the Principles of Political Obligation_ (1886) is [[t-h-green|T.H. Green]]'s major work of political philosophy, published posthumously from his lectures as Whyte's Professor of Moral Philosophy at Oxford. [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] engages with it throughout _The Philosophical Theory of [[state|the State]]_ as the most important [[england|English]]-language treatment of [[obligation|political obligation]] in the idealist tradition.

## Core Arguments

[[t-h-green|Green]] argues that [[obligation|political obligation]] arises not from consent or contract but from the [[individuality|individual]]'s recognition that the state is a necessary condition for the exercise of [[morality|moral]] personality. The state's claim to obedience rests on its function as the "hindrance of hindrances" to the good life (008-070). [[obligation|Obligation]] is rooted in the common good, which [[individuality|individual]]s rationally will insofar as they will their own true good.

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] draws on [[t-h-green|Green]]'s analysis of rights as powers necessary for the fulfillment of [[morality|moral]] capacity. As [[t-h-green|Green]] argues, "rights are the conditions of realizing the capacity for a common good" (004-035). The state recognizes and maintains rights, but does not create them—they arise from the [[morality|moral]] relations between persons within a community (008-022).

## Bosanquet's Use of Green

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] treats [[t-h-green|Green]] as a major ally in the argument that the state is essential to [[individuality|individual]] [[liberty|freedom]] rather than opposed to it. He cites [[t-h-green|Green]]'s account of state interference as "hindrance of hindrances" to defend the view that [[coercion|state coercion]] can enhance liberty (008-040). [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] also draws on [[t-h-green|Green]]'s discussion of the relation between legal and [[morality|moral obligation]], where mere external conformity to law is distinguished from action from recognition of the common good (006-053).

[[t-h-green|Green]]'s critique of laissez-faire [[individuality|individual]]ism is central to [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]]'s argument that the state is not merely a necessary evil but a positive instrument of [[morality|moral]] development (008-102). The state, by removing obstacles to the good life, enables [[individuality|individual]]s to realize capacities that would otherwise remain dormant (010-049).
