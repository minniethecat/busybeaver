# John Locke

John Locke (1632–1704) is the principal philosophical architect of the liberal tradition in political thought. His _[[second-treatise|Second Treatise of Government]]_ (1689) argued for [[natural-right|natural rights]], government by consent, and the right of revolution—ideas that [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] engages as both historically significant and philosophically limited (001-016).

## Natural Rights and Limited Government

Locke's theory begins with [[individuality|individual]]s possessing [[natural-right|natural rights]] to life, liberty, and property in a state of nature. Government is established by consent to protect these pre-existing rights, and its authority is limited by the purposes for which it was created. [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] acknowledges the historical importance of this argument—"the conception of natural right and a law of nature makes a standard to which the [[law|positive law]] of the state is responsible" (001-016)—but rejects its philosophical foundations. [[rights|Rights]], for [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]], are not pre-political possessions but are constituted by social recognition.

## Bosanquet's Idealist Response

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] argues that Locke's theory cannot explain [[obligation|political obligation]]. If rights exist prior to society and government is merely instrumental, why should the [[individuality|individual]] obey when obedience conflicts with self-interest? [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]]'s answer—that the [[individuality|individual]]'s real will is expressed in the [[general-will|general will]], not in pre-social [[natural-right|natural rights]]—is a direct challenge to the Lockean framework (004-041).
