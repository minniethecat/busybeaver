# Force

In [[bernard-bosanquet|Bernard Bosanquet]]'s _The Philosophical Theory of [[state|the State]]_ (1899), **force** is a multi-layered concept that ranges from brute physical compulsion to the [[morality|moral]] force of [[institution|social institutions]]. [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]]'s distinctive contribution is to show that force — far from being a regrettable externality to political life — is integral to [[state|the State]]'s role in sustaining the conditions of rational [[liberty|freedom]].

## Force as a Social Fact

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] begins from the recognition that force is an ineliminable element of social life. The sociologist, he observes, treats social phenomena as "a mere fact, a mere force" (002-046), analysing the interplay of "elementary social forces" (002-039) without asking normative questions about their legitimacy. This descriptive approach, while useful, cannot by itself ground a theory of [[obligation|political obligation]]:

> Obviously such a view has the merit of not confusing fact with value. But it leaves unanswered the question that political philosophy must address: when is force legitimate? (002-039)

## Force and the Transformation of Claims into Rights

The central transformation in [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]]'s theory is the conversion of mere force into [[authority|legitimate authority]] through the medium of [[state|the State]]. In the pre-political condition, rights are "mere claims of force" — an [[individuality|individual]] has a "right" only insofar as he can enforce it. The [[state|State]] changes this:

| Stage | Character of Force | Status of Claims |
|-------|-------------------|-----------------|
| Pre-political | Brute power, private enforcement | Mere claims, no recognition |
| Political ([[state|State]]) | [[institution|Institution]]alised, rule-governed | [[rights|Rights]] — recognised and guaranteed |
| Rational ideal | Force recedes as rational consensus grows | [[rights|Rights]] become self-evident to reason |

> All solid right is actually in the State, though conceived by him as withheld from it. The State transforms de facto power into de jure authority. (004-005)

## "Forced to Be Free"

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] engages directly with [[jean-jacques-rousseau|Rousseau]]'s notorious paradox — that a man may be "forced to be free" (003-036, quoting [[jean-jacques-rousseau|Rousseau]]). This is not, for [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]], a celebration of tyranny but a recognition that the conditions of rational [[liberty|freedom]] sometimes require compulsion:

> He speaks of a man "being forced to be free" — meaning not that any arbitrary coercion is justified, but that the individual's real will is served even when his immediate desires are overridden by the rational requirements of the common good. (003-036)

The restraint "enforced by the impact of others" is, in [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]]'s view, "a condition of the realisation of the true self" (003-013). Social pressure, legal compulsion, and even the threat of punishment can serve the [[individuality|individual]]'s deeper interest in becoming a rational, self-governing agent.

## The Enforcement of Morality

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] carefully distinguishes between the enforcement of [[morality|moral obligation]]s that are essential to the maintenance of the [[community|common life]] and those that belong to the sphere of [[individuality|individual]] conscience. He rejects the view that [[morality|moral]]ity as such should be enforced by law:

> The proposal of universal moral enforcement mistakes the nature both of morality and of law. Some moral obligations ought to be enforced by law — those on which the common life depends. Others lose their moral character if compelled. (003-031, 003-035)

The criterion is not the [[morality|moral]] importance of the obligation but its connection to the conditions of [[community|common life]]. [[obligation|Obligation]]s that sustain the framework within which all [[individuality|individual]]s pursue their rational ends may be enforced; obligations that belong to [[individuality|individual]] perfection may not.

## Force and the State

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]]'s conclusion is characteristically nuanced. Force is "inherent in [[state|the State]], and no true ideal points in the direction of eliminating it" (006-074). But force in [[state|the State]] is not the same as force outside it. Within [[state|the State]], force is:

1. **[[institution|Institution]]alised**: Subject to law and procedure, not arbitrary will
2. **Generalised**: Applied impartially to all, not selectively to enemies
3. **Rationalised**: Directed toward the maintenance of the conditions of [[liberty|freedom]], not private advantage

> The cohesive forces of any community whatever are ultimately moral, not physical. Force without right is fragile; right without force is, in the actual world, incomplete. (003-056)

## Bosanquet's Use of the Concept

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]]'s treatment of force operates on three levels:

1. **Sociological**: Force as an observable fact of social life, studied by the empirical sciences

2. **Philosophical**: Force as a problem for political theory — how can coercion be reconciled with [[liberty|freedom]]?

3. **Normative**: Force as transformed by [[state|the State]] from brute power into the legitimate sanction of a rational order

The progression from the first to the third level mirrors the progression of [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]]'s larger argument: from the empirical facts of social life, through philosophical analysis, to the normative ideal of [[state|the State]] as the embodiment of reason.
