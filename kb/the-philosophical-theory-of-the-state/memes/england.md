# England

England is the implicit national context of [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]]'s political philosophy and the home of the British idealist tradition. While [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]]'s theory of the state aims at philosophical universality, it is informed throughout by English political experience and the distinctive tradition of English political thought.

## England in Political Thought

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] invokes England most directly through literary and cultural references. "John of Gaunt's praises of England in Shakespeare's Richard II" represent the patriotic attachment to the nation that forms one element of modern political consciousness (001-015). This attachment, while not itself philosophical, points to the concrete relationship between [[individuality|individual]] identity and [[community|political community]] that [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]]'s theory aims to articulate.

The reference to "the third that of England" in the context of distinct national contributions to political thought suggests England's place alongside [[greece|Greece]] and [[italy|Italy]] as a source of political ideas (002-028). The world of political thought is shaped by distinct national traditions, and the English tradition—with its emphasis on common law, parliamentary government, and [[individuality|individual]] liberty—provides one of the essential elements of modern political philosophy.

## Legal and Social Context

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]]'s references to English legal [[institution|institutions]] reflect his engagement with the concrete working of the English political system. The distinction "between civil and criminal law in England" is cited as an example of how legal systems develop in ways that serve practical purposes without necessarily following abstract logical principles (008-148). English law, like English [[institution|political institutions]] more generally, embodies a form of [[reason|practical reason]] that resists reduction to simple theoretical formulas.

The social context of later nineteenth-century England—industrial, urban, and increasingly democratic—forms the background against which [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] develops his theory. The references to "later generations in England" and the revival of philosophical inquiry point to the intellectual context of British idealism as a response to the challenges of modern English society (003-016).
