# F.H. Bradley

Francis Herbert Bradley (1846–1924) was a leading [[england|British]] idealist philosopher and a contemporary of [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]]. His _[[ethical-studies|Ethical Studies]]_ (1876) developed an idealist [[morality|ethics]] that profoundly influenced [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]]'s theory of the self and its realization in social life.

## My Station and Its Duties

Bradley's most famous argument is that the [[individuality|individual]]'s [[morality|moral]] identity is constituted by their "station and its duties (002-045)"—the social roles and obligations that define a person's place in the community. [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] extends this insight: the [[individuality|individual]] is not an atom existing prior to society but a person whose very selfhood is constituted through participation in the social whole.

## The Self as Social

Bradley argued that the self is not a given entity but an achievement (005-020)—something realized through the progressive integration of [[individuality|individual]] desire with rational social purposes (005-023). [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] adopts this developmental view of the self and applies it to political theory: the [[general-will|general will]] is what the self wills insofar as it has achieved rational integration with the community. This connection between Bradley's ethical idealism and [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]]'s political theory is a defining feature of [[england|British]] idealist thought.
