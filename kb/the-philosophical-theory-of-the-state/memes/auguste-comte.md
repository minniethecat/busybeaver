# Auguste Comte

Auguste Comte (1798–1857) was a [[france|French]] philosopher who founded the discipline of sociology—which he originally named _social physics_—and whose positivist methodology profoundly shaped nineteenth-century social thought. In [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]]'s _Philosophical Theory of [[state|the State]]_, Comte represents the principal sociological counterpoint to philosophical idealism.

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] opens his analysis of sociological method by placing Comte at its origin: "the distinctive and modern spirit of what is known as Sociology... first found unmistakable expression in Auguste Comte" (002-003).

## Core Ideas in Bosanquet's Reading

### Sociology as Natural Science

Comte's defining innovation, as [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] presents it, was to include [[society|human society]] among the objects of natural science. His watchwords were "[[law]] and cause" in the strictly positivist sense—together with scientific prediction (002-003). [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] recognizes the affinity between Comte's vision of unified knowledge and the [[greece|Greek]] philosophical tradition:

> The close interdependence of all social phenomena among each other, the unity of man with nature, and the consequent correlation of [[morality|moral]] and political theory with the organised hierarchy of mathematical and physical sciences, are ideas which Comte might have borrowed directly from [[plato|Plato]] and [[aristotle|Aristotle]]. (002-003)

### The Positivist Method

According to Comte's program, the sociologist must ask, "in the language of physical science, what are the laws and causes operative among aggregations of human beings, and what are their predictable effects?" (002-004). [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] contrasts this with the ancient philosopher's question—"what is the completest and most real life of the human soul?"—a contrast that frames the entire methodological tension of the book (002-004).

Comte's alternative name for sociology—Social Physics—captures the ambition to make the study of society as rigorous as the mathematical sciences (002-007).

### The Limits of Positivism

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]]'s assessment of Comte is measured but ultimately critical. He argues that "the doctrines of Comte were no more than a very one-sided development from the principle that social facts must be explained by reference to other social facts" (002-048). By reducing social explanation to external causation, Comte's method misses the inner, psychical dimension of [[community|political community]] that is central to [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]]'s own theory.

## Bosanquet's Assessment

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] treats Comte as the defining representative of sociological method—an approach he respects for its empirical discipline but ultimately finds inadequate to grasp the [[state|state]] as a psychical and ethical reality. The positivist framework, in [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]]'s view, cannot account for the rational [[general-will|will]] that constitutes the state's inner life.

The divide between Comtean sociology and post-[[jean-jacques-rousseau|Rousseau]]-[[g-w-f-hegel|Hegel]]ian political philosophy is a central theme of [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]]'s argument. He sees these "two streams of thought" running in "independent courses" through the nineteenth century, with his own work attempting to show why they must ultimately converge—though on idealist rather than positivist terms (002-004).
