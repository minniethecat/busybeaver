# The Principles of Logic

*The Principles of Logic* (1883) is a major philosophical treatise by **[[f-h-bradley|F.H. Bradley]]**, the leading figure of [[england|British]] Idealism. [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] cites the work in Chapter 2 of *The Philosophical Theory of [[state|the State]]* in the context of a methodological critique.

## Bosanquet's Use

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] references [[f-h-bradley|Bradley]]'s logical work while criticising a certain approach to explanation in social philosophy:

> "From a logical point of view this idea of explanation seems seriously defective. See Bradley's *Principles of Logic*, p. 491." [002-085]

The citation supports [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]]'s broader argument that the methods of physical science, when applied to social and political phenomena, yield an inadequate conception of explanation. [[f-h-bradley|Bradley]]'s logical analysis of the nature of judgment and inference provides the philosophical foundation for [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]]'s critique.

## Place in British Idealism

[[f-h-bradley|Bradley]] is identified by [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] in his Preface as one of the principal philosophical influences on the work, alongside [[g-w-f-hegel|Hegel]], [[t-h-green|T.H. Green]], and [[william-wallace|William Wallace]] [P01-003]. [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] singles out [[f-h-bradley|Bradley]] (with [[g-w-f-hegel|Hegel]]) as being free from a certain "touch" of dualism that he finds in other writers — a tendency to separate the ideal from the actual in [[community|common life]] [P01-009].
