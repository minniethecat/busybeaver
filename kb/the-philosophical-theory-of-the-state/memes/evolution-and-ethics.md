# Evolution and Ethics

_Evolution and Ethics_ (1893) was [[thomas-henry-huxley|T.H. Huxley]]'s [[rome|Roman]]es Lecture at Oxford, in which he famously argued that ethical progress consists in combating rather than imitating the cosmic process of natural selection. [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] discusses it critically at several points in _The Philosophical Theory of [[state|the State]]_, treating [[thomas-henry-huxley|Huxley]]'s position as emblematic of the [[individuality|individual]]istic approach to [[morality|ethics]] and society that his own theory opposes.

## Core Arguments

[[thomas-henry-huxley|Huxley]] draws a sharp contrast between the "cosmic process" of evolution by natural selection—characterized by struggle, competition, and survival of the fittest—and the "ethical process" by which humans cultivate cooperation, sympathy, and [[morality|moral]] restraint. The ethical progress of society depends, on [[thomas-henry-huxley|Huxley]]'s view, not on imitating the cosmic process but on combating it (002-024).

For [[thomas-henry-huxley|Huxley]], nature is fundamentally non-[[morality|moral]], and [[morality|moral]]ity consists in the human capacity to resist and redirect natural impulses. The garden, cultivated by human effort, is his central metaphor for ethical life: it must be constantly defended against the encroaching wilderness.

## Bosanquet's Critique

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] treats [[thomas-henry-huxley|Huxley]]'s position as emblematic of the [[individuality|individual]]istic view of society that his own theory aims to overcome. He argues that the view "decidedly adopted by Mr. [[thomas-henry-huxley|Huxley]]" treats the self as a unit whose relation to society is external rather than constitutive (002-019). The separation between the [[individuality|individual]] and the social environment is, for [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]], a fundamental error that vitiates the entire [[individuality|individual]]istic approach.

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] groups [[thomas-henry-huxley|Huxley]] together with [[herbert-spencer|Spencer]] as thinkers who regard the ethical aspect of human life as essentially negative—as a limitation upon natural impulse rather than a positive fulfillment of human nature (002-069). He notes that [[thomas-henry-huxley|Huxley]] speaks of "self-restraint as the essence of the ethical process," which [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] acknowledges as partially true but argues captures only the negative side of ethical life (003-049). For [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]], the ethical process is not merely the negation of natural impulse but the organization of human capacities into a coherent system of social life.
