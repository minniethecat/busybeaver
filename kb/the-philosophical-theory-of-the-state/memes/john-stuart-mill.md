# John Stuart Mill

John Stuart Mill (1806–1873) is the most important nineteenth-century representative of the liberal tradition that [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]]'s idealist theory of the state challenges. Mill's _On [[liberty|Liberty]]_ (1859) articulates the principle that [[individuality|individual]] [[liberty|freedom]] should be limited only to prevent harm to others.

## Mill's Liberty Principle

Mill's harm principle—that "the only purpose for which power can be rightfully exercised over any member of a civilized community, against his will, is to prevent harm to others"—represents the liberal position that [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] seeks to transcend. [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] argues that this negative conception of liberty as absence of interference misunderstands the nature of [[liberty|freedom]]. "[[law|Law]], for him, is contrary to liberty" (003-010)—but this opposition between law and liberty is, for [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]], a philosophical error (003-010).

## Bosanquet's Critique

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]]'s response to Mill is not to reject liberty but to reconceive it. [[liberty|Liberty]] is not [[liberty|freedom]] _from_ law but [[liberty|freedom]] _through_ law—the self-realization made possible by rational participation in the [[general-will|general will]]. Mill's [[individuality|individual]], standing apart from society and defending a sphere of private action against state interference, is a philosophical abstraction (003-044). The real [[individuality|individual]] is constituted through social relations and achieves genuine [[liberty|freedom]] only within the rational state (003-044).
