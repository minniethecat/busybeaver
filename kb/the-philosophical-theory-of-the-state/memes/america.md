# America

America—the United States of America—appears in [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]]'s _Philosophical Theory of [[state|the State]]_ primarily as an example of modern federal republican government and as a distinctive development of the nation-state form. [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] discusses America in several contexts: as a site of political innovation, as an example of federal organization, and as a contrast to [[europe|European]] patterns of political development.

## America as Political Innovation

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] acknowledges America as a distinctive political formation. The "foundation of the United States of America" represents a deliberate act of political constitution—the creation of a new [[community|political community]] through conscious design rather than gradual historical accretion (010-096). This makes America an important case for the philosophical theory of the state: it exemplifies the possibility of founding [[institution|political institutions]] on explicit principles.

The American experiment had a significant impact on [[europe|European]] political thought. [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] notes the influence of American political ideas "especially upon [[france|French]] and American soil," indicating the transatlantic character of modern democratic thought (002-004).

## Federal Structure

The territorial extent of the United States—"see, e.g., the map of North America"—raises questions about the scale of [[community|political community]] that [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] explores in his discussion of the state's relation to other [[association|associations]] (011-074). American federalism represents one solution to the problem of organizing political life across a large territory while maintaining republican [[institution|institutions]].

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]]'s discussion of "rewards" in the United States suggests an interest in the social and institutional differences between American and [[europe|European]] political cultures (008-112). These differences illuminate the variety of forms that modern political life can take while still embodying the essential character of the state as a rational institution.
