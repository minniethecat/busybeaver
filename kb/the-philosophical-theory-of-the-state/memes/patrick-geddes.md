# Patrick Geddes

Sir Patrick Geddes (1854–1932) was a Scottish biologist, sociologist, and pioneer of town planning. [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] references Geddes twice in the body of his argument: once in connection with biological concepts of degeneration and parasitism, and once for his educational idea of the "Regional Survey" [002-078].

## Core Ideas in Bosanquet's Reading

### Degeneration and Social Parasitism

In an early chapter discussing the scope of sociology, [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] cites Geddes' entry in the *Encyclopaedia Britannica* on "the process of retrograde metamorphosis and of the enormously important phenomena of degeneration" [002-022]. This biological register — degeneration, parasitism, retrograde metamorphosis — enters [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]]'s argument as part of his survey of the sociological resources available for understanding social pathology. Geddes' biological approach represents one strand of late nineteenth-century social thought that [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] engages with, even as he ultimately subordinates biological analogies to the philosophical analysis of will and consciousness.

### The Regional Survey

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] invokes Geddes' educational innovation — the "Regional Survey" conducted at his Summer School — as an illustration of how [[individuality|individual]]s come to understand their social environment as a "theatre of action" shaped by intelligence and purpose. "Cf. Professor Geddes' idea of a 'Regional Survey,' with which visitors to his delightful 'Summer School' become acquainted" [002-078]. This reference appears in the context of [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]]'s argument that the social world is not a blind mechanism but an environment that the conscious [[individuality|individual]] presents to himself as his field of action — an argument that supports [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]]'s broader thesis about the role of self-consciousness in social life.

## Bosanquet's Assessment

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]]'s engagement with Geddes is limited but appreciative. He draws on Geddes' biological expertise for conceptual vocabulary (degeneration, parasitism) and on his educational practice for a concrete illustration of how social consciousness develops through active engagement with one's environment. Geddes appears as a figure whose work, while not philosophical in the strict sense, supplies empirical material that can be integrated into the philosophical theory of the state.
