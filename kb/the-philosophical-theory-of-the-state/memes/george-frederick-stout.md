# G.F. Stout

George Frederick Stout (1860–1944) was a leading [[england|British]] psychologist and philosopher, editor of _Mind_ from 1891 to 1920, and author of the influential _[[analytic-psychology|Analytic Psychology]]_ (1896). In _The Philosophical Theory of [[state|the State]]_, [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] draws substantively on Stout's psychological analyses — particularly the theory of mental control and the concept of "proportional systems" — to articulate the relationship between [[individuality|individual]] psychology and social organisation (007-018).

## Core Ideas in Bosanquet's Reading

### Mental Control and Social Organisation

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] was directed to a crucial passage in Stout's _[[analytic-psychology|Analytic Psychology]]_ concerning the theory of mental control (007-018). Stout's analysis of how the mind exercises governance over its own contents — selecting, inhibiting, and organising impulses and ideas — provides [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] with a psychological analogue for the function of the state. Just as mental control does not suppress the self but constitutes it as a coherent unity, so political governance does not suppress [[individuality|individual]] [[liberty|freedom]] but enables its rational expression. This parallel between psychological and political organisation is a recurring motif in [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]]'s argument.

### Proportional Systems

Stout's discussion of "proportional systems" — mechanisms by which different elements within a whole are assigned weight relative to their contribution — is cited by [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] in the context of [[representation|political representation]] (007-030). [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] uses Stout's psychological analysis to illuminate the normative structure of [[institution|political institutions]]: a well-ordered state, like a well-ordered mind, does not give equal weight to every impulse but proportionally represents each element according to its rational significance within the whole.

## Bosanquet's Assessment

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]]'s use of Stout is illustrative and constructive. Unlike [[thomas-henry-huxley|Huxley]] or [[herbert-spencer|Spencer]], whom [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] treats as representatives of an erroneous position, Stout is enlisted as a supporting witness — his psychological findings are presented as converging with the idealist theory of the state. The _[[analytic-psychology|Analytic Psychology]]_ serves [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] not as a target of criticism but as a scientific corroboration of the philosophical thesis that unity-in-diversity is the structural principle of both mind and polity.
