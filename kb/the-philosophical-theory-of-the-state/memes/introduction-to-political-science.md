# Introduction to Political Science

*Introduction to Political Science* (1896) by [[john-robert-seeley|J.R. Seeley]] is an early attempt to establish political science as an academic discipline distinct from history and philosophy. [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] references [[john-robert-seeley|Seeley]]'s work twice in *The Philosophical Theory of [[state|the State]]*, each time drawing attention to [[john-robert-seeley|Seeley]]'s definition of liberty, which [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] treats as the quintessential expression of the [[individuality|individual]]istic conception of the state [006-032].

## Liberty as Absence of Government

[[john-robert-seeley|Seeley]]'s central claim, as [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] presents it, is that "perfect liberty is equivalent to total absence of government" [003-047]. [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] cites this formulation as a candid statement of the [[individuality|individual]]ism he seeks to overcome. In [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]]'s account, this view treats government as a necessary evil — an external constraint upon a pre-existing [[individuality|individual]] [[liberty|freedom]] — rather than as the institutional expression of the [[real-will|rational will]] of a self-governing community [006-032].

## Bosanquet's Use of This Work

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] groups [[john-robert-seeley|Seeley]] with [[herbert-spencer|Herbert Spencer]] and [[thomas-henry-huxley|T.H. Huxley]] as representatives of the [[england|English]] [[individuality|individual]]ist tradition, which, he argues, fails to grasp the paradox of self-government. For [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]], the notion that [[liberty|freedom]] and government stand in an inverse relation is the fundamental error of the [[individuality|individual]]ist position, and [[john-robert-seeley|Seeley]]'s candid formulation of this error makes his work a useful point of departure for the idealist alternative [006-032].
