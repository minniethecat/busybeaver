# Family

In [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]]'s _Philosophical Theory of [[state|the State]]_, the **Family** occupies a distinctive place as the earliest and most fundamental ethical community. It is, [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] argues following [[aristotle|Aristotle]] and [[g-w-f-hegel|Hegel]], the institution in which nature first becomes ethical — where the [[individuality|individual]] will learns to find itself in something larger than itself.

## The Family as Pre-Political Community

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] treats the family as the primary form of human association, prior to and partly independent of [[state|the State]]. It is a "corporation, larger than the family" (001-003) that [[state|the State]] transcends, yet the family remains the seedbed of ethical life:

> In the family at its best the will has an object which is not alien to it — an object which is yet another self. (006-068)

This is the ethical core of the family: it is the first institution in which the [[individuality|individual]] experiences the identity of his own good with the good of another. The family teaches — in a natural, unreflective form — the lesson that [[state|the State]] later raises to rational self-consciousness.

## Family, Property, and Civilisation

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] traces the development of the family alongside the development of property and civilisation. "After [[morality|moral]]ity and the family have begun to form themselves, and language and property have made some advance" (004-019) — only then do the conditions for political society emerge:

> The type of the family, for example, and the order relative to it, are among the earliest forms in which the rational asserts itself against the merely natural. (002-025, 002-028)

The family is thus both a natural institution (rooted in biology and affection) and an ethical one (transformed by custom, law, and [[morality|moral]] ideals). Different economic conditions "determine the type of the family; an agricultural and an industrial community produce different family forms" (002-028).

## The Family and the State

The [[state|State]]'s relation to the family is one of both support and transcendence. The [[state|State]] must "maintain the monogamous family" (003-028) because the family is essential to the [[morality|moral]] development of [[individuality|individual]]s:

| [[institution|Institution]] | Principle | Relation to Individual |
|------------|-----------|----------------------|
| Family | Natural affection, ethical custom | Immediate, unreflective |
| Civil [[society|Society]] | Contract, exchange | Formal, self-interested |
| [[state|State]] | Rational will, common good | Reflective, participatory |

> In maintaining the general interest, the State supports the monogamous family as a condition of moral life. (003-028)

The obligation to support one's family is one of [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]]'s key examples of a "[[morality|moral obligation]] that ought to be enforced by [[state|the State]]" (003-031). The [[state|State]]'s interest in the family is not intrusive but constitutive — the family is a condition of the [[morality|moral]] personality that [[state|the State]] exists to foster.

## The Family as Ethical Model

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] uses the family as a model for understanding higher forms of community. In a "happy and devoted family, we see the difference between a merely external relation and one in which each member finds his own good in the good of the whole" (006-067):

> In the actions of a happy and devoted family, we see the difference between acting for oneself alone and acting as a member of a whole. The family is the first school of citizenship. (006-067)

This makes the family not merely a private arrangement but a public institution of the highest importance — the nursery of the ethical disposition that [[state|the State]] presupposes and cultivates.
