# Jeremy Bentham

Jeremy Bentham (1748–1832) founded the utilitarian tradition in [[morality|moral]] and political philosophy. His principle of utility—that actions should be judged by their tendency to promote the greatest happiness of the greatest number (003-046)—is a primary target of [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]]'s idealist critique of [[individuality|individual]]ism.

## The Principle of Utility

Bentham argued that all human action is motivated by the pursuit of pleasure and the avoidance of pain, and that the rightness of actions and [[institution|institutions]] is determined solely by their consequences for aggregate happiness. This reduction of [[morality|moral]]ity to calculation is, for [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]], philosophically inadequate. It treats [[individuality|individual]]s as isolated units of pleasure and pain (003-047) rather than as members of a rational community whose good is essentially shared.

## Bosanquet's Idealist Response

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] argues that Bentham's utilitarianism cannot account for the distinctive character of [[obligation|political obligation]]. If the state is justified merely by its usefulness in maximizing pleasure, then [[obligation|political obligation]] is contingent on calculation—it collapses whenever an [[individuality|individual]] judges that disobedience would produce more pleasure. [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]]'s alternative grounds obligation in the [[real-will|rational will]] itself: the [[individuality|individual]]'s real will is expressed in the [[general-will|general will]], and this relationship is constitutive, not calculative (004-041).
