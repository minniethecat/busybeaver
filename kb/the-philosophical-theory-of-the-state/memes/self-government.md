# Self-Government

**Self-government** is the principle that those who are subject to law should also be its authors. In _The Philosophical Theory of [[state|the State]]_, [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] grapples with the apparent paradox of self-government: how can the same person be both ruler and ruled?

## The Paradox

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] identifies self-government as a central problem of modern political philosophy. If the [[individuality|individual]] is both sovereign and subject, there must be a distinction within the self between the governing and the governed aspect. He writes:

> If there is anything satisfactory in the conception of self-government, every interpretation of it is at once condemned which does not give the fullest force to both terms of the paradox, at the same time that it exhibits their reconciliation. (003-063)

The paradox cannot be resolved by treating "self" as the empirical [[individuality|individual]] with all their contingent desires. That self cannot govern itself—it needs an external authority to resolve conflicts. The resolution requires a distinction between two aspects of the self: the rational self (expressed in the [[general-will|general will]]) and the empirical self (expressed in particular will).

## Rousseau's Solution

[[jean-jacques-rousseau|Rousseau]]'s theory of the [[general-will|general will]] provides the framework for [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]]'s resolution. When [[citizenship|citizen]]s participate in the formation of the [[general-will|general will]], they are not merely expressing private preferences but contributing to the determination of the common good. Self-government, in this sense, means governance by one's rational nature as expressed through the [[general-will|general will]].

## Self-Government and Political Obligation

For [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]], the obligation to obey law is grounded in self-government properly understood. The [[individuality|individual]] who disobeys a law that expresses the [[general-will|general will]] is not asserting [[liberty|freedom]] but contradicting their own rational nature:

> His particular interest may speak to him quite differently from the common interest; his absolute and naturally independent existence may make him regard what he owes to the common cause as a gratuitous contribution. (004-041)

In such cases, the [[individuality|individual]] is not "governing themselves" in the philosophically relevant sense. Self-government requires self-discipline—the subordination of particular appetite to rational judgment about the common good.

## The State as Self-Government

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]]'s conclusion is that the state, far from being an external constraint, is the institutional form of self-government. Through the state, [[individuality|individual]]s collectively determine the conditions of their own [[liberty|freedom]]. "Self-government" and "the [[general-will|general will]]" are not "meaningless phantoms" but the essential character of political life (003-063).
