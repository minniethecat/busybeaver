# Comparative Politics

_Comparative Politics_ (1873) by Edward A. Freeman was an influential early attempt to apply the comparative method—modeled on comparative philology and comparative anatomy—to the study of [[institution|political institutions]]. [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] cites Freeman's work in _The Philosophical Theory of [[state|the State]]_ as part of the nineteenth-century development of political science.

## Core Arguments

Freeman argued that [[institution|political institutions]], like languages and biological species, could be studied comparatively to reveal their historical development and essential structures. The comparative method promised to elevate political study from mere description to genuine science, identifying the common patterns underlying diverse constitutional forms.

Freeman's approach drew on the historical method associated with the Oxford school, emphasizing continuity and gradual development rather than revolutionary breaks in political history. His work contributed to the "Comparative Politics" movement that [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] references in the context of the expanding scope of political science.

## Bosanquet's Reference

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] mentions Freeman's _Comparative Politics_ in the broader discussion of the scientific study of [[institution|political institutions]]. The comparative method represents, for [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]], both the promise and the limitation of a purely empirical approach to politics: it can identify patterns and regularities in political life, but it cannot by itself establish the philosophical basis for [[obligation|political obligation]] or the nature of the state as an ethical institution.

Freeman's work exemplifies the nineteenth-century ambition to make politics a positive science, an ambition that [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] both acknowledges and seeks to transcend through philosophical analysis of the state's ethical character.
