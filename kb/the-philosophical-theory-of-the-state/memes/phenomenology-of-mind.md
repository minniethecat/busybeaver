# Phenomenology of Mind

**Phenomenology of Mind** (_Phänomenologie des Geistes_, 1807) is a major philosophical work by [[g-w-f-hegel]], described by [[bernard-bosanquet]] in _[[philosophy-of-right|The Philosophical Theory of the State]]_ as "perhaps the greatest of his works" [009-075]. The work traces the developmental history of consciousness — what [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] calls "the history of the emergence of the free or modern spirit from the undeveloped consciousness of the ancient world" [009-075].

## Bosanquet's Account

In his discussion of [[g-w-f-hegel|Hegel]]'s philosophical system, [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] positions the _Phenomenology_ within [[g-w-f-hegel|Hegel]]'s tripartite division of the [[philosophy-of-mind]]. According to [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]], [[philosophy-of-mind|Hegel's Philosophy of Mind]] comprises three parts [009-076]:

| Division | Subject |
|----------|---------|
| Anthropology | The fixation of a soul in the disciplined powers and habits of a human body |
| **Phenomenology** | The history of consciousness rising from primitive perception to [[morality|moral]] and scientific certainty |
| Psychology | The free mind as object of modern psychology |

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] emphasises that [[g-w-f-hegel|Hegel]]'s treatment is distinctive in that his "romance of the intelligence not only begins long before the phase of free mind is reached, but continues long after" [009-075]. The [[individuality|individual]] mind cannot be understood as a separable entity; rather, consciousness emerges through a historical process.

## Role in Post-Kantian Political Theory

The _Phenomenology_ is foundational to the post-[[immanuel-kant|Kant]]ian theory of [[liberty|freedom]] that [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] traces from [[immanuel-kant|Kant]] through [[johann-gottlieb-fichte|Fichte]] to [[g-w-f-hegel|Hegel]]. [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] situates [[g-w-f-hegel|Hegel]]'s _[[philosophy-of-right]]_ as the culminating political expression of the developmental account of mind first laid out in the _Phenomenology_. The tracing of subjectivity's emergence — "the emergence of the man of full mental stature, aware of himself, of his ideas and purposes" [009-076] — provides the philosophical anthropology underlying the theory of the modern state as objective mind.
