# Jean-Jacques Rousseau

Jean-Jacques Rousseau (1712–1778) was a Genevan philosopher whose _[[contrat-social|Du Contrat Social]]_ (1762) is the single most important influence on [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]]'s _Philosophical Theory of [[state|the State]]_.

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] treats Rousseau as a living philosophical presence whose insights into the [[general-will|general will]], sovereignty, and [[community|political community]] remain definitive. "It is Rousseau who stands out among the crowd of eighteenth-century writers as the one man who saw below the surface of political life" (001-019).

## Life and Works

| Period | Work | Significance |
|--------|------|-------------|
| 1755 | _Discourse on Inequality_ | Critique of civilization; natural man vs. civil man |
| 1762 | _[[contrat-social|Du Contrat Social]]_ | Foundation of modern democratic theory; theory of the [[general-will|general will]] |
| 1762 | _Émile_ | Philosophy of education; natural development |

Rousseau's life was marked by exile and controversy. Born in Geneva, he spent much of his career in [[france|France]], where his ideas were both celebrated and condemned. His influence extended far beyond political theory into education, literature, and the early [[rome|Roman]]tic movement.

## Core Ideas in Bosanquet's Reading

### The General Will

Rousseau's central innovation, as [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] reads him, is the concept of the **[[general-will|general will]]** (_[[general-will|volonté générale]]_)—[[will|the will]] of the community directed toward the common good, as distinct from the **will of all** (_volonté de tous_), which is merely the aggregate of private interests. [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] summarizes Rousseau's [[social-contract|social pact]] formula:

> Each of us puts into the common stock his person and his entire powers under the supreme direction of the general will; and we receive back as a body each member as an indivisible part of the whole. (004-032)

The [[general-will|general will]] is not the sum of [[individuality|individual]] preferences but the rational content of collective [[autonomy|self-determination]]. It is "always right" not because majorities are infallible but because it expresses what the community truly wills when it wills rationally.

### The Social Contract

Rousseau transformed contract theory by making the [[social-contract|social pact]] an agreement among [[individuality|individual]]s to constitute themselves as a people, rather than a contract between people and ruler. [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] notes that Rousseau "substitutes, in answer to the historical fiction of a social contract," a philosophical analysis of "the act by which a people is a people" (004-033).

> What man loses by the social contract is his natural liberty and an unlimited right to everything that tempts him and that he can get; what he gains is civil liberty and the property of all that he possesses. (004-045)

### Sovereignty of the General Will

For Rousseau, "sovereignty consists in the exercise of the [[general-will|General Will]]" (005-002). This was a radical departure from earlier theories that located sovereignty in the monarch or the state apparatus. [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] credits Rousseau with insisting "that sovereignty must lie in a will, and that [[will|the will]] in question must be real, not merely ostensible" (005-009).

## Bosanquet's Assessment

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] treats Rousseau as a philosophical ally whose insights need to be clarified and defended against misunderstanding. He acknowledges that Rousseau's paradoxes—"forced to be free," the [[general-will|general will]] as always right—invite misinterpretation, but argues that they "precisely express the fact" about the relation between [[individuality|individual]] liberty and [[community|political community]] (005-055).

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]]'s idealist theory of the state can be read as a systematic development of Rousseau's core intuition: [[authority|political authority]] is legitimate only insofar as it expresses the [[real-will|rational will]] of the governed.

Where Rousseau left tensions unresolved—between [[individuality|individual]] and community, liberty and authority—[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] deploys post-[[immanuel-kant|Kant]]ian idealism to show that these oppositions are dialectical rather than contradictory.

### Rousseau's Influence on Modern Political Thought

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] traces the lineage from Rousseau through [[immanuel-kant|Kant]] and [[g-w-f-hegel|Hegel]] to the [[england|British]] idealists. "The idealist philosophy dating from Rousseau and [[g-w-f-hegel|Hegel]]" (002-004) constitutes a coherent tradition in which the state is understood as the embodiment of reason in social life, not a mere mechanism for coordinating private interests.

Rousseau's declaration that the problem of politics is "to find a form of association which defends and protects with the whole common force the person and goods of each associate" remains, for [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]], the definitive statement of the task of political philosophy.
