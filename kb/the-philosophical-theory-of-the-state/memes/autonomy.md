# Autonomy

**Autonomy**—literally, self-law or self-legislation—is the capacity of a rational being to act according to principles they give to themselves. [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] engages with the [[immanuel-kant|Kant]]ian concept of autonomy while arguing that genuine self-determination is only possible through participation in the [[real-will|rational will]] of the [[community|political community]].

## Kantian Autonomy

For [[immanuel-kant|Kant]], autonomy is the foundation of [[morality|moral obligation]]: a rational being is bound only by laws they could will as universal. This conception of autonomy as rational self-legislation is one of the most influential ideas in modern [[morality|moral]] and political philosophy. [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] acknowledges this heritage while arguing that [[immanuel-kant|Kant]]'s conception remains too [[individuality|individual]]istic.

## Autonomy and the General Will

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]]'s key move is to socialize autonomy. The [[individuality|individual]] does not achieve self-determination in isolation but through membership in a rational community. When the [[individuality|individual]] obeys the [[general-will|general will]], they are not submitting to an alien power but acting according to their own rational nature. Autonomy, in this sense, coincides with [[citizenship|citizen]]ship: "the liberty which a [[citizenship|citizen]] enjoys is" precisely the liberty of rational self-determination (003-049) (003-044).

## Autonomy vs. Individualism

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]]'s theory of autonomy challenges the liberal [[individuality|individual]]ist equation of autonomy with the absence of external constraint. For [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]], the unconstrained [[individuality|individual]] is not autonomous but enslaved to arbitrary desire (003-046). True autonomy requires the discipline of reason, and reason is inherently social—it takes the form of law, institution, and the [[general-will|general will]].
