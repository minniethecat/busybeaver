# Aristotle

Aristotle (384–322 BCE) provides the classical formulation of the organic theory of the state—the idea that the [[community|political community]] is not an artificial construction but a natural growth, and that the [[individuality|individual]] is constituted as a [[morality|moral]] being through participation in the polis.

## Political Naturalism

Aristotle's famous claim that "man is by nature a political animal" is fundamental to [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]]'s rejection of contractarian [[individuality|individual]]ism. The state is not a [[association|voluntary association]] that [[individuality|individual]]s choose to join; it is the natural condition of human flourishing (001-024). [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] draws on this Aristotelian insight throughout his argument against theories that treat the [[individuality|individual]] as prior to society.

### The Whole and the Part

Aristotle's principle that the whole is prior to the part—the polis is prior to the [[individuality|individual]] as the body is prior to the hand (011-002)—informs [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]]'s organic theory of the state. [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] defends a qualified version of this thesis: the [[individuality|individual]]'s [[morality|moral]] personality is not given prior to society but is constituted through social participation.

## Bosanquet's Aristotelianism

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]]'s debt to Aristotle is most evident in his treatment of the state as an ethical community whose purpose is the good life of its members. While [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]]'s theory is mediated through [[jean-jacques-rousseau|Rousseau]] and [[g-w-f-hegel|Hegel]], its deepest roots lie in Aristotle's vision of the polis as the sphere in which human beings realize their nature (011-003) as rational and social beings.
