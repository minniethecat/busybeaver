# Du Contrat Social

_Du Contrat Social_ (1762) is [[jean-jacques-rousseau|Rousseau]]'s masterpiece of political philosophy and the single most important source for [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]]'s theory of the state. [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]]'s _Philosophical Theory of [[state|the State]]_ can be read as an extended philosophical commentary on and defense of [[jean-jacques-rousseau|Rousseau]]'s central insights—above all, the concept of the [[general-will|general will]].

## Core Arguments

[[jean-jacques-rousseau|Rousseau]]'s central thesis is that legitimate [[authority|political authority]] can only be based on the **[[general-will|general will]]** (_[[general-will|volonté générale]]_)—[[will|the will]] of the people directed toward the common good. This requires a [[social-contract|social pact]] in which each person unites with all while remaining as free as before:

> Each of us puts into the common stock his person and his entire powers under the supreme direction of the general will; and we receive back as a body each member as an indivisible part of the whole. (004-032)

### Key Distinctions

| Concept | Definition | Role in [[jean-jacques-rousseau|Rousseau]]'s Theory |
|---------|-----------|--------------------------|
| General will (_[[general-will|volonté générale]]_) | The will directed toward the common good | Source of sovereignty; always right |
| [[will|Will]] of all (_volonté de tous_) | Sum of private interests | Not a source of [[authority|legitimate authority]] |
| Natural liberty | Unlimited right to everything attainable | Surrendered in the social contract |
| Civil liberty | Freedom limited by the [[general-will|general will]] | Gained through the social contract |

[[jean-jacques-rousseau|Rousseau]] distinguishes the [[general-will|general will]] from [[will|the will]] of all: the latter is the sum of private interests, while the former attends only to the [[common-good|common interest]]. [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] defends this distinction as capturing the essential character of political life—the difference between what people happen to want and what they rationally will as members of a [[community|political community]].

### Sovereignty and Law

For [[jean-jacques-rousseau|Rousseau]], sovereignty is the exercise of the [[general-will|general will]] and is inalienable and indivisible. [[law|Law]], in turn, is the [[general-will|general will]]'s expression in universal form. The [[citizenship|citizen]] who obeys the law is not submitting to an external power but to their own [[real-will|rational will]] as a member of the sovereign people.

## Bosanquet's Reading

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] treats the _Contrat Social_ not as a historical curiosity but as a work of living philosophical significance. He defends [[jean-jacques-rousseau|Rousseau]] against the charge that the [[general-will|general will]] is a mystical or authoritarian concept, arguing that it "precisely expresses the fact" about the relation between [[individuality|individual]] [[liberty|freedom]] and [[community|political community]] (005-055).

### Defending Rousseau's Paradoxes

The paradoxes for which [[jean-jacques-rousseau|Rousseau]] is famous—"forced to be free," the [[general-will|general will]] as "always right"—are, in [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]]'s reading, rigorous formulations of truths that liberal [[individuality|individual]]ism cannot accommodate:

> The problem which was enunciated in Rousseau's paradox, that 'law itself makes men free,' is the central problem of all political philosophy. (002-047)

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] follows [[jean-jacques-rousseau|Rousseau]] in arguing that the opposition between [[individuality|individual]] liberty and [[authority|political authority]] is a false one. The [[citizenship|citizen]] who obeys a law that genuinely expresses the [[general-will|general will]] is not constrained by an alien power but realizes their own rational nature.

### The Philosophical vs. the Historical Contract

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] emphasizes that [[jean-jacques-rousseau|Rousseau]]'s social contract is a philosophical analysis of what makes [[authority|political authority]] legitimate, not a historical claim about how states actually originated. [[jean-jacques-rousseau|Rousseau]] "substitutes, in answer to the historical fiction of a social contract," a logical analysis of "the act by which a people is a people" (004-033).

## Place in the Idealist Tradition

[[jean-jacques-rousseau|Rousseau]]'s _Contrat Social_ is the founding text of a tradition that runs through [[immanuel-kant|Kant]] and [[g-w-f-hegel|Hegel]] to the [[england|British]] idealists. [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] situates himself within this lineage, arguing that "the idealist philosophy dating from [[jean-jacques-rousseau|Rousseau]] and [[g-w-f-hegel|Hegel]]" (002-004) provides the most adequate framework for understanding the state.

The _Contrat Social_'s enduring contribution is its demonstration that political philosophy must address not merely the mechanics of government but the nature of [[community|political community]] itself—the question of what it means for a people to govern itself.
