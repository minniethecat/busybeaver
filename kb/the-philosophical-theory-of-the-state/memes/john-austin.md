# John Austin

John Austin (1790–1859) was an [[england|English]] legal philosopher whose command theory of [[law|law]]—defining law as the command of the sovereign, backed by the threat of sanction—established the framework of classical legal positivism. In [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]]'s _Philosophical Theory of [[state|the State]]_, Austin appears as the paradigmatic representative of the external, sociological-juridical view of law that philosophical idealism must overcome.

## Core Ideas in Bosanquet's Reading

### The Command Theory of Law

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] references Austin's theory at a crucial methodological turning point in Chapter X. He identifies the "general type of theory which underlies such a theory as Austin's" as one that treats [[law|law]] as an external imposition—a command issued by a determinate superior to a determinate inferior (010-006). For [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]], this is the theory that philosophical analysis must transcend.

### Law as Command: The Positivist Reduction

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] extends his analysis by noting that Austin's approach "might also be illustrated by Austin's theory of law as a command"—a theory that identifies law with the empirical fact of sovereign will, stripping it of its rational and ethical content (010-017). The index entry further confirms [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]]'s engagement with Austin's theory throughout the work (012-023).

### The Philosophical Alternative

Against Austin's command theory, [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] develops the idealist conception of [[law|law]] as the articulation of the rational [[general-will|will]] of the [[community|political community]]. [[law|Law]] is not merely a command backed by force but "the [[rights|system of rights]] which the state maintains," and the [[state|state]] itself is not merely the commander but the embodiment of the community's rational [[autonomy|self-determination]].

## Bosanquet's Assessment

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] treats Austin's command theory with respect as a clear formulation of the view that must be overcome. Austin's positivism has the merit of analytical clarity—it identifies exactly what a non-philosophical theory of [[law|law]] looks like. But for [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]], this clarity reveals its limitation: by reducing law to command, Austin renders unintelligible the normative dimension of law, the sense in which law claims rational [[authority|authority]] rather than mere coercive power.

The positivist position that Austin represents is treated not as false but as incomplete—it describes the external form of law but misses its inner, psychical reality as an expression of the [[general-will|general will]].
