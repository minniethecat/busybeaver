## Definition

**Sovereignty** (*souveraineté*), in Proudhon's analysis in *What is [[property|Property]]?*, is the claimed power to make law by will — whether [[will|the will]] of one man (monarchy), a few (aristocracy), or the national majority (democracy). In all cases, Proudhon argues, it is "the sovereignty of man instead of the sovereignty of the law, the sovereignty of [[will|the will]] instead of the sovereignty of the reason" (001-030).

This critique of sovereignty is structurally parallel to his critique of property: just as property replaces justice with the arbitrary will of the proprietor, sovereignty replaces law with the arbitrary will of the ruler — even when that ruler is called "the people."

## The Critique of Popular Sovereignty

Proudhon wrote in the aftermath of the [[french-revolution|French Revolution]], which had replaced monarchy with popular sovereignty. His critique is directed not at monarchy (which he considers discredited) but at the democratic successor:

> "But what was monarchy? The sovereignty of one man. What is democracy? The sovereignty of the nation, or, rather, of the national majority. But it is, in both cases, the sovereignty of man instead of the sovereignty of the law, the sovereignty of the will instead of the sovereignty of the reason; in one word, the passions instead of justice." (001-031)

### Progress Without Revolution

Proudhon acknowledges that the transition from monarchy to democracy represents progress — "in multiplying the sovereigns we increase the opportunities of the reason to substitute itself for [[will|the will]]" — but insists that "in reality there is no revolution in the government, since the principle remains the same" (001-031).

The principle that remains unchanged is: **someone commands, others obey**. The identity of the commander changes; the structure of command does not.

### Delegation and Abdication

The nation, being a collective, cannot exercise sovereignty directly. It must delegate to representatives — five, ten, a hundred, or a thousand:

> "Be these agents five, ten, one hundred, or a thousand, of what consequence is the number; and what matters the name? It is always the government of man, the rule of will and caprice. I ask what this pretended revolution has revolutionized?" (001-032)

Proudhon traces the historical sequence: sovereignty exercised by the Convention → then by the Directory → then confiscated by the Consul → then by the Emperor, who "dared to demand its suffrage: that is, its abdication, the abdication of this inalienable sovereignty; and he obtained it" (001-033).

## Sovereignty as the Power to Make Law

The standard definition of sovereignty — "the power to make law" — Proudhon dismisses as "another absurdity, a relic of despotism" (001-034). For fifty years, the nation had seen kings issue commands with "FOR SUCH IS OUR PLEASURE"; now the nation wished to taste the pleasure of making laws in its turn.

But law, properly understood, is not an act of will. It is the expression of relations that already exist — relations of justice, of equality, of necessity. To say "I make the law" is to claim that will determines justice, which is precisely the error of despotism.

## The Connection to Property

Sovereignty and property share the same logical structure:

| Aspect | Sovereignty | [[property|Property]] |
|--------|------------|----------|
| Absolute dominion | Ruler over subjects | Proprietor over things |
| Basis | [[will|Will]], not reason | [[will|Will]], not justice |
| Effect | Subjects obey | Laborers pay tribute |
| Revolutionary slogan | "Sovereignty of the people" | "Right to the product of labor" |
| Proudhon's verdict | Rule of will, still despotism | Rule of exclusion, still robbery |

Proudhon suggests that political absolutism and economic absolutism are two faces of the same error — the claim that one person's will can rightfully dominate another. The revolutionaries abolished royal sovereignty but preserved property, not realizing that property is itself a form of private sovereignty.

## Sovereignty of Reason vs. Sovereignty of Will

Proudhon's positive alternative is not anarchy in the sense of no rules, but the replacement of will-sovereignty with reason-sovereignty — or, more precisely, with **the rule of law grounded in justice**:

> "Undoubtedly, when a nation passes from the monarchical to the democratic state, there is progress, because in multiplying the sovereigns we increase the opportunities of the reason to substitute itself for the will; but in reality there is no revolution in the government, since the principle remains the same." (001-031)

The goal is not more sovereigns but **no sovereigns** — a society governed by the recognition of relations of justice rather than by the commands of any person or assembly.

## Historical Context

Proudhon's analysis of sovereignty reflects the political experience of [[france|France]] between 1789 and 1840:

- **1789–1792**: Constitutional monarchy; sovereignty shifted from king to nation
- **1793–1794**: The Convention; popular sovereignty exercised through terror
- **1795–1799**: The Directory; sovereignty delegated to five, then undermined
- **1799–1815**: Napoleon; sovereignty confiscated by one man, then offered back to the people as plebiscite (abdication disguised as consent)
- **1815–1830**: Bourbon Restoration; sovereignty returned to the king
- **1830–1840**: July Monarchy; sovereignty shared between king and chamber

Fifty years of oscillation between monarchy and democracy, with no stable settlement, convinced Proudhon that the principle of sovereignty itself was the problem.
