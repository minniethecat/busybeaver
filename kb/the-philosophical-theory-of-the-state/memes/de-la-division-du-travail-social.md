# De la Division du Travail Social

*De la Division du Travail Social* (*The Division of Labour in Society*, 1893) by Émile Durkheim is a foundational work of modern sociology, arguing that the division of labour is not merely an economic phenomenon but a [[morality|moral]] and social fact that shapes the forms of social solidarity. [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] references Durkheim's work in his discussion of the sociological method and its contribution to the understanding of social life [002-035].

## Durkheim's Sociological Method

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] cites Durkheim as an exemplar of the scientific approach to social phenomena. He draws attention to Durkheim's "investigations on the effect of density of population upon the division of labour" as an instance of sociological research that seeks to identify "fairly trustworthy uniformities" in social life [002-035]. For [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]], Durkheim's work represents the promise of a genuinely scientific sociology — one that moves beyond biological analogies and economic reductionism toward the systematic study of the "wealth of experience and of ideas" furnished by [[institution|social institutions]] [002-035].

## Bosanquet's Use of This Work

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] acknowledges an intellectual debt to Durkheim, noting in a footnote that he is "indebted for it to M. Durkheim, whose writings appear to me to have the very highest degree of methodological importance for the study of social life" [002-040]. While [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]]'s own philosophical idealism differs markedly from Durkheim's sociological positivism, he values Durkheim's empirical approach to the study of social facts — an approach that, in [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]]'s view, provides the necessary material foundation for philosophical reflection on the nature of the state.
