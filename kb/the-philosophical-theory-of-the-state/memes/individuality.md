# Individuality

In [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]]'s _Philosophical Theory of [[state|the State]]_, **Individuality** is not the brute fact of separateness but a positive achievement — the fullest development of the self through participation in the rational life of the community. Individuality and society are not antithetical; the richest individuality is the fruit of the deepest social engagement.

## Individuality and the Common Life

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] opens his work by noting that "all things have their distinct individuality" (001-005), but for human beings, individuality is not given — it is achieved through culture, [[institution|institutions]], and the [[community|common life]]. An artist "expresses his individuality in the creations of his genius" (001-006), but this expression presupposes the entire tradition and technique he inherits:

> The brilliant individuality of the ancient city-state — this was not the product of isolation but of an intense common life. (002-048)

## The Defence of Individuality

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] is keenly aware of the objection that his organic theory of [[state|the State]] threatens individuality. He addresses this directly in his discussion of [[john-stuart-mill|Mill]] and the liberal tradition:

> The defence of individuality has already well-nigh turned into a paradox. The question is not whether individuality is valuable, but what individuality truly consists in. (003-023)

The error of atomistic individualism, [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] argues, is to identify individuality with mere difference or eccentricity. True individuality is the development of one's distinctive capacities within a rational order — not the absence of external determination but [[autonomy|self-determination]] through reason.

## How Much to Individuality, How Much to Society?

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] frames the central question of political philosophy as one of proportion: "How much of human life should be assigned to individuality, and how much to society?" (003-021). But he immediately undermines the premise of the question:

| View | Individuality | [[society|Society]] | Relation |
|------|--------------|---------|----------|
| Atomistic | Pre-social datum | External limitation | Antagonistic |
| Idealist | Achieved through society | Medium of development | Constitutive |

> The term "self" is antagonistic to the self or true individuality of man; while the term "society" is in truth the medium in which alone the self can come into its own. (003-007)

## Individuality and the State

The [[state|State]], far from suppressing individuality, is its ultimate guarantor. By providing the rational framework of law and rights, [[state|the State]] enables individuals to develop their capacities without mutual interference:

> The relation of individuality to the Calvinistic theory of the State shows how a strong doctrine of individual responsibility can be compatible with — indeed, can require — a strong State. (003-016)

For [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]], the highest individuality is achieved not in opposition to [[state|the State]] but through it. The [[citizenship|citizen]] who participates in the [[general-will|General Will]] is not less individual but more so — because his will has been raised from particular caprice to rational self-direction.
