# State

In [[bernard-bosanquet|Bernard Bosanquet]]'s _The Philosophical Theory of the State_ (1899), **the State** is not merely a contractual arrangement or a coercive apparatus, but a "real unity in one person" — a [[morality|moral]] organism through which [[individuality|individual]] wills find their fullest rational expression (004-035).

## The State as a Natural Growth

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] rejects the social contract tradition's view of the State as an artificial construct imposed upon naturally free [[individuality|individual]]s. Drawing on the classical [[greece|Greek]] conception, he holds that "the State is natural," meaning it is a growth and expression of human nature itself:

> The State is natural, i.e. is a growth, an expression of the same principle that makes the family and the clan and the nation. (001-008)

This organic conception places the State in continuity with other forms of human association, from [[family|the family]] upward, rather than in opposition to [[individuality|individual]] [[liberty|freedom]].

## The State, Rights, and Recognition

For [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]], rights do not precede the State but are created and sustained by it. The State is the mechanism through which _de facto_ claims become _de jure_ rights:

| Aspect | Pre-State Condition | Under the State |
|--------|-------------------|-----------------|
| [[rights|Rights]] | Mere claims of force | Recognised and guaranteed |
| Individual | Isolated, powerless | Member with recognised standing |
| Freedom | Natural licence | Rational self-direction |

> All solid right is actually in the State, though conceived by him as withheld from it. (004-005)

The State "sees to it that certain conditions are maintained" (003-031), transforming what would otherwise be mere force into a system of reciprocal recognition. A right, in [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]]'s view, "is for him a creation of the State, and there can be no right where there is no State to recognise and enforce it" (003-040).

## The Moral Person of the State

Drawing on [[jean-jacques-rousseau|Rousseau]] and [[g-w-f-hegel|Hegel]], [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] characterises the State as a "[[morality|moral]] person" — not a fiction but a real unity constituted by the rational cooperation of its members:

> The moral person which constitutes the State as an abstraction... when active, this "moral" or "public" person represents the real will of the community. (004-041, 005-002)

This [[morality|moral]] personality is not distinct from the [[individuality|individual]]s who compose it; rather, it is their rational life organised into a coherent whole. The State is thus "above all" the movement and circulation of spiritual life:

> It follows that the State, in this sense, is, above all, the movement and circulation of the common life, and that its members are not passive recipients but living spiritual agencies. (006-072, 006-073)

## Force and the State

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] acknowledges that force is inherent in the State, but insists that no true ideal points toward its abolition. The State's coercive power is not an unfortunate externality but an essential aspect of its role in maintaining the conditions of rational [[liberty|freedom]]:

> Force is inherent in the State, and no true ideal points in the direction of eliminating it. (006-074)

The State's use of force is justified not as an end in itself but as the necessary precondition for the realisation of rights and the common good. Without the State's ultimate sanction, rights would revert to mere claims backed by private power.

## The State and the General Will

The State, in [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]]'s idealist framework, is the institutional embodiment of the [[general-will]]. It is not the sum of particular interests (the "will of all") but the rational unity that "generalises [[will|the will]]" (005-026). Through participation in the life of the State, the [[individuality|individual]] transcends mere appetite and achieves genuine self-government.

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] follows [[Jean-Jacques Rousseau|Rousseau]] in identifying the State with the [[general-will|general will]], but goes further in [[g-w-f-hegel|Hegel]]ian fashion, treating the State as the concrete universal in which [[individuality|individual]] and collective [[reason|rationality]] coincide:

> Rousseau identified with the State the general will. In this theory they are followed by Hegel, who treats the State as the actuality of the ethical Idea. (006-070)
