# T.H. Green

Thomas Hill Green (1836–1882) was the leading figure of the [[england|British]] idealist movement and a direct philosophical influence on [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]]. His _[[principles-of-political-obligation|Lectures on the Principles of Political Obligation]]_ developed a theory of positive [[liberty|freedom]] and the common good that [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] both draws upon and extends.

## Positive Freedom

Green's most important contribution is his theory of positive [[liberty|freedom]]: liberty is not merely the absence of restraint but the positive capacity to realize one's rational nature. This conception directly challenges the negative liberty of the liberal tradition (003-010). [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] adopts Green's framework and develops it further, arguing that the state is the institutional condition of positive [[liberty|freedom]] (003-010).

## The Common Good

Green argued that the [[individuality|individual]]'s true good is inseparable from the common good of the community. This thesis is fundamental to [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]]'s theory: the [[general-will|general will]] is directed toward the common good, and the [[individuality|individual]]'s real will coincides with it (005-020). Green's idealist liberalism provides the bridge between [[immanuel-kant|Kant]]ian [[morality|moral]] philosophy and [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]]'s full-fledged theory of the state.
