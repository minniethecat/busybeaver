# Physics and Politics

_Physics and Politics_ (1872) by Walter Bagehot applies evolutionary and anthropological ideas to the study of political development, attempting to identify the "natural" laws governing the formation and progress of nations. [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] cites Bagehot's work in _The Philosophical Theory of [[state|the State]]_, primarily in connection with the distinction between "predominant modes and types" of social organization.

## Core Arguments

Bagehot argued that political development could be understood through the lens of natural selection and inherited custom. [[nation|Nation]]s progress through a process of variation and selection, in which successful political practices and [[institution|institutions]] are preserved and transmitted across generations. The "cake of custom"—the accumulated weight of inherited practice—is both the condition for social stability and an obstacle to innovation.

Bagehot's analysis of the role of conflict and competition in nation-formation, particularly his account of how "the strongest and most vigorous nations" prevail in historical struggle, represents an early attempt to integrate evolutionary thinking with political analysis.

## Bosanquet's Reference

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] engages with Bagehot primarily in the context of the comparative and historical study of [[institution|political institutions]]. He cites Bagehot's "predominant modes and types" as an example of the empirical approach to political science that identifies patterns in political life (002-007). For [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]], Bagehot's work illustrates both the value and the limitation of a purely naturalistic approach to politics: it can describe how nations have developed, but it cannot by itself establish the ethical basis for [[obligation|political obligation]] or the character of the state as an embodiment of reason.
