# Majority Rule

In [[bernard-bosanquet|Bernard Bosanquet]]'s _The Philosophical Theory of [[state|the State]]_ (1899), **majority rule** is critically examined not as a practical decision-procedure but as a purported foundation of political legitimacy. [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] argues that mere numerical superiority cannot generate the rational authority that [[state|the State]] requires — and that identifying [[will|the will]] of the majority with the [[general-will|general will]] is a category error.

## The Majoritarian Illusion

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] traces the majoritarian logic to its source in social contract thinking. If [[state|the State]] is founded on [[individuality|individual]] consent, and unanimous consent is impossible, then majority rule appears as the only workable approximation. But this reasoning, [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] contends, confuses a practical expedient with a [[morality|moral]] principle:

> The notion that political obligation can be discharged by acquiescing "in the will of the majority as if it had been their own" rests on a fundamental confusion between the will of all and the general will. (001-007)

The "will of all" — the sum of [[individuality|individual]] preferences — is not the same as the "[[general-will|general will]]" — the rational content that serves the common good. A majority vote may or may not coincide with the [[general-will|general will]]; the mere fact of numerical superiority adds nothing to the rational quality of the decision.

## Rousseau's Paradox and the Minority

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] follows [[jean-jacques-rousseau|Rousseau]] in confronting the hardest case for any theory of [[obligation|political obligation]]: the minority that finds itself on the losing side. If the [[general-will|general will]] is the [[real-will|rational will]], and the minority disagrees with the majority's decision, who is right?

> When "the minority has to obey the majority, there is no freedom." Rather, freedom consists in obedience to the general will, which may or may not coincide with the majority's decision. (009-014)

The resolution lies in distinguishing between the empirical majority and the rational [[general-will|general will]]. The minority is obligated to obey not because it was outvoted but because the decision, if it truly expresses the [[general-will|general will]], is what the minority's own real will would endorse upon rational reflection:

| Concept | Definition | [[authority|Authority]] |
|---------|-----------|-----------|
| [[will|Will]] of all | Sum of [[individuality|individual]] preferences | None — merely factual |
| Majority will | [[will|Will]] of the numerical majority | Provisional — may or may not align with reason |
| General will | Rational content serving the common good | Absolute — commands obedience from the real will |

## The Active Part of the People

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] is acutely aware that in practice, majorities are not neutral aggregations. Drawing on [[jean-jacques-rousseau|Rousseau]]'s observations, he notes that "the active part of the people; the majority, or those who succeed in making themselves accepted as the majority" (003-058) often determines what passes for the public will:

> The majority, or those who succeed in making themselves accepted as the majority, may in fact represent a particular interest rather than the common good. (003-058)

This recognition places [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] in an ambiguous position: he is critical of majority rule as a legitimating principle, yet he offers no simple alternative procedure. His solution is not anti-democratic institutional design but the cultivation of rational public life — a [[citizenship|citizen]]ship formed by participation in the [[institution|institutions]] of [[state|the State]].

## The State Beyond Numbers

For [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]], [[state|the State]]'s authority cannot be reduced to "that chance combination of [[individuality|individual]] voices in a majority" (001-024). The [[state|State]] represents something deeper: the rational organisation of a [[community|common life]] that cannot be disaggregated into [[individuality|individual]] votes without losing its essential character.

> To understand this to mean that a majority, or any persons in power, may do whatever they please would be a complete misunderstanding. The State, like every rational institution, is bound by its own idea. (008-145)

The [[state|State]] is not authorised by majority vote to do whatever it pleases; it is bound by the rational idea it embodies. The majority principle is a useful procedural device, but it cannot bear the [[morality|moral]] weight that consent theorists place upon it.

## Bosanquet's Use of the Concept

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]]'s treatment of majority rule serves three purposes in his larger argument:

1. **Diagnostic**: It exposes the inadequacy of aggregative theories of political legitimacy

2. **Constructive**: By clearing away the majoritarian justification, it forces the reader to confront the deeper question — what makes a political decision genuinely authoritative?

3. **Pedagogical**: The contrast between majority rule and the [[general-will|general will]] illustrates the difference between empirical and rational accounts of political life
