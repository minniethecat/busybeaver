# William Wallace

William Wallace (1844–1897) was a Scottish philosopher and a central figure in the [[england|British]] Idealist movement, alongside [[t-h-green|T.H. Green]] and [[f-h-bradley|F.H. Bradley]]. [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] acknowledges Wallace in the Preface as one of the formative influences on the idealist school, and draws substantively on Wallace's essays — particularly "Our [[natural-rights|Natural Rights]]" — in developing his own theory of rights as recognitions within a rational social order (P01-003).

## Core Ideas in Bosanquet's Reading

### Natural Rights as Social Recognition

Wallace's essay "Our [[natural-rights|Natural Rights]]" provides [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] with a crucial argumentative resource. Wallace contends that what are called "[[natural-right|natural rights]]" are not pre-social entitlements possessed by [[individuality|individual]]s in isolation, but rather are recognitions that a rational community extends to its members (P01-004). [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] adopts and extends this line of thought, arguing that rights "are, strictly speaking, claims" whose reality depends entirely on the organised recognition of a [[community|political community]]. Wallace's formulation allows [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] to reject the atomistic [[individuality|individual]]ism of the social contract tradition without dismissing the normative force of rights-language altogether.

### Hegel's Philosophy of Mind

Wallace's translation of and commentary on [[g-w-f-hegel|Hegel]]'s _[[philosophy-of-mind|Philosophy of Mind]]_ (1894) earned [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]]'s explicit citation (009-057). Wallace's rendering of [[g-w-f-hegel|Hegel]]ian concepts into [[england|English]] philosophical idiom provided [[england|British]] Idealists with a shared vocabulary for discussing the relationship between subjective mind, objective spirit, and the rational state. [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]]'s own treatment of the state as a "[[morality|moral]] person" draws on this [[g-w-f-hegel|Hegel]]ian inheritance as mediated by Wallace.

## Bosanquet's Assessment

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]]'s references to Wallace are uniformly respectful and appropriative rather than critical. Wallace is cited as an authority whose arguments [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] builds upon rather than contests. The late Professor Wallace's collected _[[lectures-and-essays|Lectures and Essays]]_ (1898) are recommended to the reader as an authoritative source on the idealist theory of rights (004-018). Wallace represents for [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] the mature expression of [[england|British]] Idealist political philosophy — a tradition in which [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] himself stands as both heir and contributor.
