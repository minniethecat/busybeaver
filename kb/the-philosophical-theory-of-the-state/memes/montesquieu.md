# Montesquieu

Charles-Louis de Secondat, Baron de Montesquieu (1689–1755), was a [[france|French]] political philosopher whose _De l'Esprit des Lois_ (1748) analyzed the relationship between laws, [[institution|institutions]], and social conditions. [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] references Montesquieu in his discussion of the institutional structure of the state.

## The Spirit of Laws

Montesquieu argued that laws are not arbitrary commands (004-032) but expressions of the "spirit" of a people—its climate, customs, history, and form of government. This sociological approach to [[institution|political institutions]] influenced the historicist dimension of [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]]'s thought: the state is not an abstract construction but a living reality shaped by the character of the community it governs.

## Forms of Government

Montesquieu's classification of governments—republic, monarchy, despotism—and his analysis of their characteristic principles informed later political philosophy, including the idealist tradition. [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] draws on this analysis in his own discussion of sovereignty and the [[general-will|general will]], arguing that the form of government must express the rational character of the community (006-069).
