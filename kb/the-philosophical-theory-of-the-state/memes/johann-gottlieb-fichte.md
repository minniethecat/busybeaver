# Johann Gottlieb Fichte

Johann Gottlieb Fichte (1762–1814) was a central figure in [[germany|German idealism]] whose political philosophy contributed to the tradition of ethical state theory that culminates in [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]].

## Between Kant and Hegel

Fichte occupies a transitional position between [[immanuel-kant|Kant]]'s [[morality|moral]] philosophy and [[g-w-f-hegel|Hegel]]'s systematic idealism. His attempt to derive the structure of political life from the nature of self-consciousness (010-010) anticipated [[g-w-f-hegel|Hegel]]'s project of demonstrating the [[reason|rationality]] of the state (010-013). [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]], working in the [[g-w-f-hegel|Hegel]]ian tradition, inherits Fichte's conviction that political philosophy must be grounded in a theory of the self and its realization.

## Ethical Nationalism

Fichte's _Addresses to the [[germany|German]] [[nation|Nation]]_ developed a conception of national identity grounded in language, culture, and ethical purpose rather than race or blood. This ethical nationalism, while distinct from [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]]'s more cosmopolitan [[g-w-f-hegel|Hegel]]ianism, shares the idealist conviction that the state is not merely a mechanism for protecting [[individuality|individual]] interests but an ethical community that shapes the character of its members (010-012).
