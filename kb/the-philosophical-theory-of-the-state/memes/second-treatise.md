# Second Treatise of Government

[[john-locke|John Locke]]'s _Second Treatise of Government_ (1689) is the classic statement of liberal political philosophy. Its argument that [[individuality|individual]]s possess [[natural-right|natural rights]] prior to government, and that [[authority|political authority]] rests on consent, established the framework that [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]]'s idealist theory of the state challenges.

## Natural Rights and Consent

[[john-locke|Locke]] argues that [[individuality|individual]]s in the state of nature possess [[natural-right|natural rights]] to life, liberty, and property. Government is established by consent to protect these pre-existing rights, and its authority is limited to the purposes for which it was created. [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] acknowledges the historical importance of this argument: "the conception of natural right and a law of nature makes a standard to which the [[law|positive law]] of the state is responsible" (001-016).

## Bosanquet's Critique

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] argues that [[john-locke|Locke]]'s framework cannot explain [[obligation|political obligation]]. If rights exist prior to society, why should the [[individuality|individual]] obey when obedience conflicts with self-interest? The idealist alternative grounds rights not in pre-political nature but in the recognition of the community. [[rights|Rights]] are aspects of the [[individuality|individual]]'s position within the rational state, not possessions they bring to it from outside (004-041).
