# The Reformation

The Protestant Reformation appears in [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]]'s _Philosophical Theory of [[state|the State]]_ as a pivotal moment in the emergence of modern consciousness. [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] identifies the Reformation as one of the forces that shaped "the modern consciousness" (001-012) and contributed to the development of the theory of the state.

## The Rise of Modern Consciousness

For [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]], the Reformation represents more than a religious movement. It is a phase in the development of the modern mind—the assertion of [[individuality|individual]] judgment against institutional authority. This shift in consciousness created the conditions for modern political philosophy, in which the legitimacy of the state must be grounded in reason rather than tradition.

## Individualism and Authority

The Reformation's emphasis on [[individuality|individual]] conscience and direct access to truth parallels the political philosophy [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] critiques: the [[individuality|individual]]ist tradition that treats the state as an external constraint on pre-existing [[rights|individual rights]]. [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]]'s interest in the Reformation is not theological but philosophical—it exemplifies the broader modern tendency to locate authority in the [[individuality|individual]] rather than the community.

## In Bosanquet's Argument

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] mentions the Reformation in connection with the celebration of its anniversary alongside "the Battle of Leipzig" (009-053), suggesting its symbolic importance in the development of [[europe|modern Europe]]an political identity. However, his treatment is brief—the Reformation serves as a historical marker rather than a sustained object of analysis.

## Significance for State Theory

The Reformation's legacy for state theory is ambiguous. On one hand, it contributed to the development of [[rights|individual rights]] discourse. On the other, it reinforced the identification of [[authority|political authority]] with secular sovereignty, preparing the way for the modern theory of the state that [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] develops. The tension between [[individuality|individual]] conscience and collective authority that the Reformation dramatized remains central to [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]]'s philosophical project.
