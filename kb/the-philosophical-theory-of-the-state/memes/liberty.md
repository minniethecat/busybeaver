# Liberty

In _The Philosophical Theory of [[state|the State]]_, [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] challenges the purely negative conception of liberty as absence of restraint. Drawing on [[jean-jacques-rousseau|Rousseau]] and the idealist tradition, he argues that **true liberty** consists not in freedom _from_ law but in freedom _through_ law—the self-realization made possible by membership in a rational [[community|political community]].

## Natural Liberty vs. Civil Liberty

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] follows [[jean-jacques-rousseau|Rousseau]] in distinguishing **natural liberty** from **civil liberty**. Natural liberty is the unlimited right to everything one can obtain—a condition of "total absence of government" that is, in practice, no liberty at all (003-047). Civil liberty, by contrast, is "limited by the [[general-will|general will]]" (004-046) and consists in the security and [[morality|moral]] development that only law-governed society can provide.

> What man loses by the social contract is his natural liberty and an unlimited right to everything that tempts him and that he can get; what he gains is civil liberty and the property of all that he possesses. (004-045)

## Liberty and the General Will

For [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]], the key insight of [[jean-jacques-rousseau|Rousseau]]'s theory is that liberty is not opposed to the [[general-will|general will]] but realized through it. The [[individuality|individual]] who obeys the [[general-will|general will]] is not submitting to an external power but to their own rational nature as a member of the [[community|political community]] (005-002). This is the meaning of [[jean-jacques-rousseau|Rousseau]]'s famous claim that [[citizenship|citizen]]s can be "forced to be free"—not a justification of tyranny, but the recognition that law expresses the conditions of rational freedom.

## The Critique of Negative Liberty

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] directly engages the liberal [[individuality|individual]]ist tradition, which treats law and liberty as inherently antagonistic:

> Law, for him, is contrary to liberty; and every infraction of liberty... (003-010)

He argues that this view misunderstands both law and liberty. The "liberty which a [[citizenship|citizen]] enjoys is" not the mere absence of interference but "the liberty which consists in having a rational end and in being able to pursue it under the protection of law" (003-044). The coercion of the state, properly understood, removes obstacles to rational [[autonomy|self-determination]] rather than imposing external constraints.

## Bosanquet's Contribution

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] extends [[jean-jacques-rousseau|Rousseau]]'s analysis by connecting it to the post-[[immanuel-kant|Kant]]ian idealist tradition. He argues that liberty is a form of **self-realization**—the actualization of one's rational nature through participation in the [[institution|institutions]] of the state. This conception has affinities with [[t-h-green|T.H. Green]]'s theory of positive freedom and [[g-w-f-hegel|Hegel]]'s analysis of objective spirit.

He is particularly concerned to show that the opposition between "the liberty of each and the welfare of all" is a false one (004-007). In a well-ordered state, [[individuality|individual]] liberty and the common good are not competing values but complementary aspects of the same rational order.
