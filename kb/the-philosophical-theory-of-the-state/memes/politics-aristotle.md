# Politics

[[aristotle|Aristotle]]'s _Politics_ (c. 350 BCE) is, alongside [[plato|Plato]]'s _[[republic|Republic]]_, the foundational text of classical political philosophy. Its argument that the polis exists by nature and that man is a political animal provides the classical basis for [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]]'s organic theory of the state.

## Core Arguments

[[aristotle|Aristotle]]'s central thesis is that the state is not an artificial construction but a natural growth. "Man is by nature a political animal"—the [[individuality|individual]] who exists outside the polis is either a beast or a god. The polis is prior to the [[individuality|individual]] as the whole is prior to the part. [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]] draws on this Aristotelian framework in his critique of contractarian [[individuality|individual]]ism: the state is not a [[association|voluntary association]] but the natural condition of human flourishing.

## Bosanquet's Aristotelianism

[[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]]'s debt to the _Politics_ is most evident in his treatment of the state as an ethical community. While [[bernard-bosanquet|Bosanquet]]'s theory is mediated through [[jean-jacques-rousseau|Rousseau]] and [[g-w-f-hegel|Hegel]], its deepest roots lie in [[aristotle|Aristotle]]'s vision of the polis as the sphere in which human beings realize their nature as rational and social beings (001-024).
