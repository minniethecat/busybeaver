# Ulysses

Ulysses (Greek: Odysseus), king of Ithaca, is the exemplar of cunning and eloquence in the *Metamorphoses*. When the arms of the fallen [[Achilles]] are contested after his death, [[Ajax]] — the embodiment of martial strength — and Ulysses — the voice of strategic intelligence — each lay claim to the prize. Ulysses's victory speech in Book XIII is a bravura defense of mind over muscle, setting the stage for Troy's fall and the subsequent suffering of the Trojan women. (114-001)

## Role in the Metamorphoses

Ulysses appears in the aftermath of the Trojan War, when the Greek chiefs gather to decide who deserves the armor of the dead [[Achilles]]. Ajax speaks first — a blunt, indignant claim based on his deeds in battle and his kinship to Achilles. Ulysses follows with a masterful display of rhetoric: he argues that his cunning did more to win the war than Ajax's brute force, that it was he who persuaded Achilles to fight, he who stole the Palladium, he who devised the stratagem of the wooden horse. The Greek chiefs award him the armor, Ajax kills himself in shame, and from his blood springs the hyacinth-like flower. (115-001)

### Key Episodes

- **The Contest for Achilles's Arms**: After Achilles is slain by the arrows of [[Paris]] and [[Apollo]], both Ajax and Ulysses advance their claims to the hero's divine armor (114-001)
- **Ulysses's Speech**: Speaking second, Ulysses dismantles Ajax's claim point by point — he argues that wisdom, not strength, conquers cities; that he carried the weight of command while Ajax merely swung a spear (115-001)
- **The Victory**: The Grecian chiefs award the armor to Ulysses; his triumph is a victory of words over deeds, of intelligence over blind force (115-010)
- **Aftermath: Ajax's Death**: Unable to bear the disgrace, Ajax falls on his sword; from his blood springs the purple flower marked with "AI" — the letters of grief (115-077)

## Transformation

Ulysses himself undergoes no physical metamorphosis, but his rhetorical victory **catalyzes** one of the poem's most poignant transformations: the suicide of Ajax and the flower that springs from his blood. Ulysses is the agent who, through words rather than weapons, sets in motion the chain of events that ends in a metamorphosis of shame into eternal botanical inscription.
