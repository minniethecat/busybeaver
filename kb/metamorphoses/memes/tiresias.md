# Tiresias

Tiresias is the blind prophet of Thebes whose wisdom and clairvoyance cut across the *Metamorphoses*, appearing at crucial narrative junctures to deliver prophecies that are invariably correct and invariably ignored. Famous for having lived as both a man and a woman — a transformation resulting from striking two copulating snakes — Tiresias is uniquely positioned to pronounce on questions of pleasure and fate, a role that earns him both the gift of prophecy from Jupiter and the wrath of Juno (who blinds him for his verdict against her).

## Role in the Metamorphoses

Tiresias first appears in Book III as the sage consulted by Liriope, the mother of the beautiful youth Narcissus, who asks whether her son will live to old age. Tiresias replies with the famous paradox: "If he never knows himself, he surely shall" (024-001). The truth of this prophecy is proven when Narcissus falls in love with his own reflection and dies. Tiresias's fame spreads through Greece as a consequence. Later, when Pentheus, king of Thebes, refuses to acknowledge the divinity of Bacchus, Tiresias warns him of the coming consequences — but Pentheus mocks the blind prophet, deriding his useless skill. Tiresias foretells Pentheus's doom at the hands of his own mother and aunts, and this too comes to pass when Pentheus is torn apart on Mount Cithaeron (025-001).

### Key Episodes

- **The Prophecy for Narcissus**: Tiresias tells Liriope that her son Narcissus will live to old age only "if he never knows himself" — a prophecy fulfilled when Narcissus wastes away before his own reflection (024-001)
- **Warning to Pentheus**: Tiresias foretells the violent death of Pentheus for his impiety toward Bacchus, but Pentheus scorns and derides him (025-001)

## Transformation

- **Form**: Man → Woman → Man (the result of striking copulating snakes, first with his staff, then again seven years later)
- **Agent**: The snakes themselves, acting as a natural portent
- **Cause**: Tiresias's intervention in the act of serpentine coupling — a disturbance of nature that nature answered with transformation and eventual restoration
- **Source**: (024-001)
