[108-001] Cycnus, a son of Neptune, and invulnerable in every part of his body, is at length strangled by Achilles, who strips him of his armour, when he is changed into a swan.

[108-002] From these first onsets, the Sigaean shore

[108-003] Was strew’d with carcasses, and stain’d with gore.

[108-004] Neptunian Cycnus troops of Greeks had slain;

[108-005] Achilles in his car had scoured the plain,

[108-006] And clear’d the Trojan ranks; where’er he fought,

[108-007] Cycnus, or Hector, through the fields he sought.

[108-008] Cycnus he found; on him his force essay’d;

[108-009] For Hector was to the tenth year delay’d.

[108-010] His white maned steeds, that bow’d beneath the yoke,

[108-011] He cheer’d to courage, with a gentle stroke,

[108-012] Then urged his fiery chariot on the foe,

[108-013] And rising shook his lance, in act to throw.

[108-014] But first, he cried, “Oh youth, be proud to bear

[108-015] Thy death, ennobled by Pelides’ spear.”

[108-016] The lance pursued the voice without delay,

[108-017] Nor did the whizzing weapon miss the way,

[108-018] But pierced his cuirass, with such fury sent,

[108-019] And sign’d his bosom with a purple dint.

[108-020] At this the seed of Neptune: “Goddess born,

[108-021] For ornament, not use, these arms are worn;

[108-022] This helm, and heavy buckler, I can spare,

[108-023] As only decorations of the war;

[108-024] So Mars is arm’d for glory, not for need.

[108-025] ’Tis somewhat more from Neptune to proceed,

[108-026] Than from a daughter of the sea to spring;

[108-027] Thy sire is mortal, mine is ocean’s king.

[108-028] Secure of death, I should contemn thy dart,

[108-029] Though naked, and impassable depart.”

[108-030] He said, and threw; the trembling weapon pass’d

[108-031] Through nine bull hides, each under other placed,

[108-032] On his broad shield, and stuck within the last.

[108-033] Achilles wrench’d it out, and sent again

[108-034] The hostile gift: the hostile gift was vain.

[108-035] He tried a third, a tough, well-chosen spear,

[108-036] The inviolable body stood sincere,

[108-037] Though Cycnus then did no defence provide,

[108-038] But scornful offer’d his unshielded side.

[108-039] Not otherwise the impatient hero fared,

[108-040] Than as a bull encompass’d with a guard,

[108-041] Amid the circus roars, provoked from far

[108-042] By sight of scarlet and a sanguine war:

[108-043] They quit their ground; his bended horns elude;

[108-044] In vain pursuing, and in vain pursued.

[108-045] Before to further fight he would advance,

[108-046] He stood considering, and survey’d his lance;

[108-047] Doubts if he wielded not a wooden spear

[108-048] Without a point: he look’d; the point was there.

[108-049] “This is my hand, and this my lance,” he said,

[108-050] “By which so many thousand foes are dead;

[108-051] Oh whither is their usual virtue fled!

[108-052] I had it once; and the Lyrnessian wall,

[108-053] And Tenedos, confess’d it in their fall.

[108-054] Thy streams, Caicus, rolled a crimson flood;

[108-055] And [[thebes|Thebes]] ran red with her own natives’ blood

[108-056] Twice Telephus employ’d their piercing steel,

[108-057] To wound him first, and afterward to heal.

[108-058] The vigour of this arm was never vain:

[108-059] And that my wonted prowess I retain,

[108-060] Witness these heaps of slaughter on the plain.”

[108-061] He said; and, doubtful of his former deeds,

[108-062] So some new trial of his force proceeds.

[108-063] He chose Menaetes from among the rest;

[108-064] At him he launch’d his spear, and pierced his breast

[108-065] On the hard earth the Lycian knock’d his head,

[108-066] And lay supine, and forth the spirit fled.

[108-067] Then thus the hero: “Neither can I blame

[108-068] The hand or javelin; both are still the same:

[108-069] The same I will employ against this foe,

[108-070] And wish but with the same success to throw.”

[108-071] So spoke the chief; and while he spoke he threw;

[108-072] The weapon with unerring fury flew,

[108-073] At his left shoulder aim’d: nor entrance found;

[108-074] But back, as from a rock, with swift rebound,

[108-075] Harmless return’d: a bloody mark appear’d,

[108-076] Which, with false joy, the flatter’d hero cheer’d:

[108-077] Wound there was none; the blood that was in view,

[108-078] The lance before from slain Menaetes drew.

[108-079] Headlong he leaps from off his lofty car,

[108-080] And in close fight on foot renews the war:

[108-081] Raging with high disdain, repeats his blows:

[108-082] Nor shield nor armour can their force oppose:

[108-083] Huge cantlets of his buckler strew the ground,

[108-084] And no defence in his bored arms is found:

[108-085] But on his flesh no wound or blood is seen;

[108-086] The sword itself is blunted on the skin.

[108-087] This vain attempt the chief no longer bears,

[108-088] But round his hollow temples and his ears

[108-089] His buckler beats: the son of Neptune, stunn’d

[108-090] With these repeated buffets, quits his ground;

[108-091] A sickly sweat succeeds, and shades of night;

[108-092] Inverted nature swims before his sight:

[108-093] The insulting victor presses on the more,

[108-094] And treads the steps the vanquished trod before,

[108-095] Nor rest nor respite gives. A stone there lay

[108-096] Behind his trembling foe, and stopp’d his way:

[108-097] Achilles took the advantage which he found,

[108-098] O’erturn’d, and push’d him backward on the ground.

[108-099] His buckler held him under, while he press’d

[108-100] With both his knees above his panting breast:

[108-101] Unlaced his helm: about his chin the twist

[108-102] He tied; and soon the strangled soul dismiss’d.

[108-103] With eager haste he went to strip the dead:

[108-104] The vanish’d body from his arms was fled:

[108-105] His sea-god sire, to immortalize his frame,

[108-106] Had turn’d it to the bird that bears his name.

[108-107] A truce succeeds the labours of this day,

[108-108] And arms suspended with a long delay.

[108-109] While Trojan walls are kept with watch and ward,

[108-110] The Greeks before their trenches mount the guard:

[108-111] The feast approach’d, when to the blue-eyed maid

[108-112] His vows for Cycnus slain the victor paid,

[108-113] And a white heifer on her altar laid;

[108-114] The reeking entrails on the fire they threw,

[108-115] And to the gods the grateful odour flew:

[108-116] Heaven had its part in sacrifice; the rest

[108-117] Was broil’d and roasted for the future feast.

[108-118] The chief invited guests were set around;

[108-119] And, hunger first assuaged, the bowls were crown’d,

[108-120] Which in deep draughts their cares and labours drown’d.

[108-121] The mellow harp did not their ears employ;

[108-122] And mute was all the warlike symphony:

[108-123] Discourse, the food of souls, was their delight,

[108-124] And pleasing chat prolong’d the summer’s night;

[108-125] The subject, deeds of arms, and valour shown,

[108-126] Or on the Trojan side, or on their own:

[108-127] Of dangers undertaken, fame achieved,

[108-128] They talk’d by turns—the talk by turns relieved.

[108-129] What things but these could fierce Achilles tell;

[108-130] Or what could fierce Achilles hear so well?

[108-131] The last great act perform’d, of Cycnus slain,

[108-132] Did most the martial audience entertain;

[108-133] Wondering to find a body free by fate

[108-134] From steel, and which could ev’n that steel rebate

[108-135] Amazed, their admiration they renew;

[108-136] And scarce Pelides could believe it true.
