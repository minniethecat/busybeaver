# Atlas

Atlas is one of the Titans, a primordial being of enormous stature who rules the farthest western edge of the known world, where the sun sets and the heavens rest upon the earth. In the *Metamorphoses*, Atlas is encountered by Perseus after the hero's victory over the Gorgon Medusa. When Atlas — warned by an ancient oracle that a son of Jupiter would one day rob him of his golden orchard — refuses Perseus hospitality, the hero retaliates by displaying Medusa's severed head. The Titan's immense body is instantly petrified, and he becomes the mountain range that still bears his name: Mount Atlas, spanning the western horizon and holding up the sky.

## Role in the Metamorphoses

Atlas appears in Book IV, where his realm is described as lying at the world's western extreme, marked by the garden of the Hesperides with its golden apples — the very prize an oracle warned would be stolen by a son of Jupiter (037-001). Perseus, returning from slaying Medusa, lands in Atlas's kingdom and claims descent from Jupiter, asking only for rest. Atlas, mindful of the prophecy, refuses, and Perseus reveals the Gorgon's head. "Atlas ghastly stared" (037-031), and his body transforms: his beard and hair become forests, his shoulders ridges, his bones stone — "Soon the high Atlas a high mountain stood" (037-051). The passage also notes his part in the cosmic upheaval of Phaeton's ride: "Atlas becomes unequal to his freight" when the earth scorches (010-348), showing his double role as both agent and victim of cosmic-scale metamorphosis.

### Key Episodes

- **Refusal of Perseus**: Perseus arrives at Atlas's kingdom seeking rest; Atlas, remembering the oracle, denies him (037-001)
- **The Petrification**: Perseus reveals Medusa's head; Atlas turns to stone and becomes a mountain (037-031)
- **Cosmic Burden**: When Phaeton loses control of the sun-chariot, even Atlas struggles under the weight of the scorched heavens (010-348)

## Transformation

Atlas's metamorphosis is punishment for inhospitality, but it also represents a literalization of his mythological function: the Titan who held up the sky becomes the mountain that seems to do the same.

- **Form**: Titan of immense size → Mount Atlas (a mountain range)
- **Agent**: Perseus, using the head of Medusa
- **Cause**: Punishment for refusing hospitality and defying prophecy
- **Source**: (037-031, 037-051)
