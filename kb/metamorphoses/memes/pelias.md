# Pelias

Pelias, king of Iolcus, is one of the *Metamorphoses*' most vivid villains — a usurper whose death by Medea's cunning serves as the dark mirror-image of Aeson's rejuvenation. Where Aeson receives the gift of restored youth through Medea's genuine magic, Pelias is promised the same gift and receives instead a butcher's death: his own daughters, deceived by the sorceress, cut him to pieces in the belief that they are performing the same rejuvenation ritual. The episode is Ovid at his most gruesome and psychologically acute — a study in deceit, filial piety corrupted, and magic's capacity for cruelty.

## Role in the Metamorphoses

Pelias appears in Book 7, in the extended narrative of Medea's magic. Having successfully rejuvenated Aeson, Medea turns her attention to Pelias — Aeson's usurping half-brother — with a darker purpose. She approaches Pelias's daughters with a false promise: that she can restore their father's youth as she did Aeson's. To prove her power, she rejuvenates an old ram into a lamb before their eyes. Convinced, the daughters butcher their father and cast his pieces into Medea's cauldron — but Medea withholds the crucial herbs, and Pelias dies, not reborn, in the boiling pot.

### Key Episodes

- **The Usurper**: Pelias deposed his half-brother Aeson and seized the throne of Iolcus; to secure his rule, he sent his nephew Jason on the seemingly impossible quest for the Golden Fleece
- **Medea's Deception**: After rejuvenating Aeson, Medea offers the same service to Pelias's daughters — a calculated lie that exploits their filial devotion to destroy their father (064-001)
- **The Ram and the Lamb**: Medea's demonstration — an old ram boiled in her cauldron and emerging as a lamb — convinces Pelias's daughters that the ritual works. It does — but only for the ram (064-001)
- **The Butchery of Pelias**: The daughters, believing they are saving their father, stab and dismember him. Medea withholds the magic herbs, and Pelias dies — his daughters transformed from loving children into unwitting patricides (064-001)

## Transformation

Pelias's death is a grotesque parody of Aeson's rejuvenation. The same ritual structure — draining old blood, replacing it with a magical elixir — is performed, but without the crucial element of Medea's sincere intent. Where Aeson is reborn, Pelias is dismembered. The episode transforms filial love into murder, magic into deception, and the promise of renewal into the reality of butchery.

- **Form**: Aged mortal king → Dismembered corpse (failed rejuvenation as destruction)
- **Agent**: His own daughters (at Medea's instigation)
- **Cause**: Medea's vengeance against Pelias for usurping Aeson's throne; the corruption of filial piety
