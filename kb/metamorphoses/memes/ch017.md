[013-001] Calisto, a nymph in the train of Diana, is seduced by [[jupiter|Jupiter]], under the form of that goddess, and is delivered of a son, named Arcas Juno transforms Calisto into the shape of a bear; but Jupiter removes both mother and child to the celestial mansions, where they are converted into a constellation called the Bear.

[013-002] The day was settled in its course, and Jove

[013-003] Walk’d the wide circuit of the heavens above.

[013-004] To search if any cracks or flaws were made;

[013-005] But all was safe: the earth he then survey’d,

[013-006] And cast an eye on ev’ry different coast,

[013-007] And ev’ry land, but on Arcadia most.

[013-008] Her fields he clothed, and cheer’d her blasted face

[013-009] With running fountains and with springing grass.

[013-010] No tracks of heaven’s destructive fire remain,

[013-011] The fields and woods revive, and nature smiles again.

[013-012] But as the god walk’d to and fro the earth,

[013-013] And raised the plants, and gave the spring its birth,

[013-014] By chance a fair Arcadian nymph he view’d,

[013-015] And felt the lovely charmer in his blood.

[013-016] The nymph nor spun nor dress’d with artful pride.

[013-017] Her vest was gather’d up, her hair was tied:

[013-018] Now in her hand a slender spear she bore,

[013-019] Now a light quiver on her shoulders wore;

[013-020] To chaste Diana from her youth inclined,

[013-021] The sprightly warriors of the wood she join’d.

[013-022] Diana too the gentle huntress loved,

[013-023] Nor was there one of all the nymphs that roved

[013-024] O’er Maenalus, amid the maiden throng,

[013-025] More favour’d once; but favour lasts not long.

[013-026] The sun now shone in all its strength, and drove

[013-027] The heated virgin panting to the grove:

[013-028] The grove around a grateful shadow cast:

[013-029] She dropp’d her arrows, and her bow unbraced;

[013-030] She flung herself on the cool grassy bed,

[013-031] And on the painted quiver raised her head.

[013-032] Jove saw the charming huntress unprepared,

[013-033] Stretch’d on the verdant turf, without a guard.

[013-034] “Here I am safe,” he cries, “from Juno’s eye:

[013-035] Or should my jealous queen the theft descry,

[013-036] Yet I would venture on a theft like this,

[013-037] And stand her rage, for such, for such a bliss!”

[013-038] Diana’s shape and habit straight he took,

[013-039] Soften’d his brows, and smooth’d his awful look,

[013-040] And mildly in a female accent spoke:

[013-041] “How fares my girl? how went the morning chase?”

[013-042] To whom the virgin, starting from the grass,

[013-043] “All hail! bright deity, whom I prefer

[013-044] To Jove himself, though Jove himself were here.”

[013-045] The god was nearer than she thought, and heard,

[013-046] Well pleased, himself before himself preferr’d.

[013-047] He then salutes her with a warm embrace;

[013-048] And, ere she half had told the morning chase,

[013-049] With love inflamed, and eager on his bliss:

[013-050] Smother’d her words, and stopp’d her with a kiss:

[013-051] His kisses with unwonted ardour glow’d,

[013-052] Nor could Diana’s shape conceal the god.

[013-053] Possess’d at length of what his heart desired,

[013-054] Back to his heavens the exulting god retired.

[013-055] But now Diana, with a sprightly train

[013-056] Of quiver’d virgins, bounding o’er the plain,

[013-057] Call’d to the nymph; the nymph began to fear

[013-058] A second fraud, a Jove disguised in her;

[013-059] But when she saw the sister nymphs, suppress’d

[013-060] Her rising fears, and mingled with the rest.

[013-061] How in the look does conscious guilt appear!

[013-062] Slowly she moved, and loiter’d in the rear;

[013-063] Nor lightly tripp’d, nor by the goddess ran,

[013-064] As once she used, the foremost of the train;

[013-065] Her looks were fush’d, and sullen was her mien,

[013-066] That sure the virgin goddess (had she been

[013-067] Aught but a virgin) must the guilt have seen.

[013-068] ’Tis said the nymphs saw all, and guess’d aright.

[013-069] And now the moon had nine times lost her light,

[013-070] When Dian, fainting in the midday beams,

[013-071] Found a cool covert and refreshing streams,

[013-072] That in soft murmurs through the forest flow’d,

[013-073] And a smooth bed of shining gravel show’d.

[013-074] A covert so obscure and streams so clear

[013-075] The goddess praised: “And now no spies are near;

[013-076] Let’s strip, my gentle maids, and wash,” she cries.

[013-077] Pleased with the motion, every maid complies;

[013-078] Only the blushing huntress stood confused,

[013-079] And form’d delays, and her delays excused:

[013-080] In vain excused; her fellows round her press’d,

[013-081] And the reluctant nymph by force undress’d.

[013-082] The naked huntress all her shame reveal’d,

[013-083] In vain her hands her pregnancy conceal’d;

[013-084] “Begone!” the goddess cries, with stern disdain,

[013-085] “Begone! nor dare the hallow’d stream to stain.”

[013-086] She fled, for ever banish’d from the train.

[013-087] This Juno heard, who long had watch’d her time

[013-088] To punish the detested rival’s crime;

[013-089] The time was come; for, to enrage her more,

[013-090] A lovely boy the teeming rival bore.

[013-091] The goddess cast a furious look, and cried,

[013-092] “It is enough! I’m fully satisfied!

[013-093] This boy shall stand a living mark, to prove

[013-094] My husband’s baseness and the harlot’s love:

[013-095] But vengeance shall awake: those guilty charms,

[013-096] That drew the Thunderer from Juno’s arms,

[013-097] No longer shall their wonted force retain,

[013-098] Nor please the god, nor make the mortal vain.”

[013-099] This said, her hand within her hair she wound,

[013-100] Swung her to earth, and dragg’d her on the ground.

[013-101] The prostrate wretch lifts up her arms in prayer;

[013-102] Her arms grow shaggy and deform’d with hair,

[013-103] Her nails are sharpen’d into pointed claws,

[013-104] Her hands bear balf her weight and turn to paws,

[013-105] Her lips, that once could tempt a god, begin

[013-106] To grow distorted in an ugly grin;

[013-107] And, lest the supplicating brute might reach

[013-108] The cars of Jove, she was deprived of speech;

[013-109] Her surly voice through a hoarse passage came

[013-110] In savage sounds, her mind was still the same.

[013-111] The furry monster fix’d her eyes above,

[013-112] And heaved her new unwieldy paws to Jove,

[013-113] And begg’d his aid with inward groans; and though

[013-114] She could not call him false she thought him so.

[013-115] How did she fear to lodge in woods alone,

[013-116] And haunt the fields and meadows once her own!

[013-117] How often would the deep-mouth’d dogs pursue,

[013-118] While from her hounds the frighted huntress flew!

[013-119] How did she fear her fellow brutes, and shun

[013-120] The shaggy bear, though now herself was one!

[013-121] How from the sight of rugged wolves retire,

[013-122] Although the grim Lycaon was her sire!

[013-123] But now her son had fifteen summers told,

[013-124] Fierce at the chase, and in the forest bold;

[013-125] When, as he beat the woods in quest of prey,

[013-126] He chanced to rouse his mother where she lay.

[013-127] She knew her son, and kept him in her sight,

[013-128] And fondly gazed. The boy was in a fright,

[013-129] And aim’d a pointed arrow at her breast,

[013-130] And would have slain his mother in the beast;

[013-131] But Jove forbade, and snatch’d them through the air

[013-132] In whirlwinds up to heaven, and fix’d them there;

[013-133] Where the new constellations nightly rise,

[013-134] And add a lustre to the northern skies.

[013-135] When Juno saw the rival in her height,

[013-136] Spangled with stars and circled round with light,

[013-137] She sought old Ocean in his deep abodes,

[013-138] And Tethys, both revered among the gods.

[013-139] They ask what brings her there. “Ne’er ask,” says she,

[013-140] “What brings me here, heaven is no place for me.

[013-141] You’ll see, when all things are obscured by night,

[013-142] Jove’s starry mistress with resplendent light

[013-143] Usurp the heavens; you’ll see her proudly roll

[013-144] In her new orb, and brighten all the pole.

[013-145] And who shall now on Juno’s altars wait,

[013-146] When those she hates grow greater by her hate?

[013-147] I on the nymph a brutal form impress’d,

[013-148] Jove to a goddess has transform’d the beast.

[013-149] This, this was all my weak revenge could do;

[013-150] But let the god his chaste amours pursue,

[013-151] And, as he acted after Io’s rape,

[013-152] Restore the adultress to her former shape;

[013-153] Then may he cast his Juno off, and lead

[013-154] The great Lycaon’s offspring to his bed.

[013-155] But you, ye venerable powers, be kind,

[013-156] And, if my wrongs a due resentment find,

[013-157] Receive not in your waves their setting beams,

[013-158] Nor let the glaring harlot taint your streams.”

[013-159] The goddess ended, and her wish was given

[013-160] Back she return’d in triumph up to heaven;

[013-161] Her gaudy peacocks drew her through the skies;

[013-162] Their tails were spotted with a thousand eyes;

[013-163] The eyes of Argus on their tails were ranged,

[013-164] At the same time the raven’s colour changed.
