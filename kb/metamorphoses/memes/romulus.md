# Romulus

Romulus, son of the god Mars and the Vestal virgin Rhea Silvia (Ilia), is the legendary founder and first king of Rome. In the *Metamorphoses*, his apotheosis — translation to heaven and transformation into the god Quirinus — is one of the crucial deification episodes in the poem's later books, linking Ovid's metamorphic cosmos to the sacred origins of Roman power (138-001).

## Role in the Metamorphoses

Romulus appears in Book XIV as one of the poem's climactic examples of apotheosis. After his life of founding and ruling Rome — a life of martial achievement and civic order, narrated briefly by Ovid — Mars approaches Jupiter and requests his son's deification. Jupiter grants the request: Mars descends in his chariot and carries Romulus up to the heavens. During the ascent, Romulus's mortal body falls away, and he is received among the gods as Quirinus (138-001). His wife Hersilia, grieving his loss, is later also translated to the heavens by Juno and becomes the goddess Hora — an apotheosis that mirrors and completes Romulus's own.

### Key Episodes

- **The Petition of Mars**: Mars asks Jupiter to grant Romulus's deification, arguing that his son has earned a place among the gods (138-001)
- **The Ascent**: Mars carries Romulus to heaven in his chariot; his mortal form dissolves, and he becomes the god Quirinus (138-001)
- **The Deification of Hersilia**: Juno sends Iris to conduct Romulus's grieving wife Hersilia to the heavens, where she becomes the goddess Hora (146-023)

## Transformation

- **Agent**: Mars, with Jupiter's consent — Romulus is translated to the heavens
