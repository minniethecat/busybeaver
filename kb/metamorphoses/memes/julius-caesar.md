# Julius Caesar

Julius Caesar (100–44 BCE), Roman general, statesman, and dictator, appears in the final book of Ovid's *Metamorphoses* as the figure whose death and apotheosis authenticate the entire cosmic order the poem has described. His transformation — from murdered mortal to divine star (the *sidus Iulium*) — is the last metamorphosis narrated in the poem before Ovid's epilogue, and it serves to ground the entire work in contemporary Roman politics (006-070).

## Role in the Metamorphoses

Caesar appears in Book XV, the poem's final book, in a passage that explicitly links the metamorphic cosmos to Roman history. Ovid narrates Caesar's assassination — the senators' daggers, Venus's anguish, Jupiter's consolation — and then his apotheosis. Venus, Caesar's divine ancestress, attempts to prevent his murder but is stopped by Jupiter, who reveals that Caesar's death is fated (006-073). After the assassination, Venus catches Caesar's soul as it rises from his body and carries it to the heavens, where it becomes a comet — the *sidus Iulium* — that outshines all other stars. This deification is explicitly framed as preparing the way for Augustus's even greater destiny: "Caesar must be made a god, so that Augustus may be born a god's son" (134-019).

### Key Episodes

- **The Assassination**: Caesar is murdered in the Senate; Venus, his divine ancestress, is horrified and attempts to intervene (006-070)
- **Jupiter's Consolation**: Jupiter reveals to Venus that Caesar's death is fated and that greater glory awaits his heir Augustus (006-073)
- **Apotheosis**: Venus bears Caesar's soul to the heavens, where it ignites as a comet — the *sidus Iulium* — visible at the games Augustus holds in his honor

## Transformation

- **Form**: Mortal man → Divine star (the *sidus Iulium*, a comet)
- **Agent**: Venus, acting on Jupiter's decree
- **Cause**: Reward for his achievements and to prepare for Augustus's rule
- **Source**: (134-019, 134-035)
