[024-001] Pentheus, King of [[thebes|Thebes]], refuses to acknowledge the divinity of [[bacchus|Bacchus]], and forbids his subjects to pay adoration to the new god, who, under the disguise of Acetes, is led in captivity to the presence of the monarch.

[024-002] This sad event gave blind Tiresias fame,

[024-003] Through Greece establish’d in a prophet’s name.

[024-004] The unhallow’d Pentheus only durst deride

[024-005] The cheated people and their eyeless guide.

[024-006] To whom the prophet in his fury said,

[024-007] Shaking the hoary honours of his head,

[024-008] “ ’Twere well, presumptuous man, ’twere well for thee,

[024-009] If thou wert eyeless too, and blind like me:

[024-010] For the time comes, nay, ’tis already here,

[024-011] When the young god’s solemnities appear,

[024-012] Which, if thou dost not with just rites adorn,

[024-013] Thy impious carcass, into pieces torn,

[024-014] Shall strew the woods, and hang on every thorn.

[024-015] Then, then remember what I now foretell,

[024-016] And own the blind Tiresias saw too well.”

[024-017] Still Pentheus scorns him, and derides his skill;

[024-018] But time did all the prophet’s threats fulfil.

[024-019] For now through prostrate Greece young Bacchus rode,

[024-020] While howling matrons celebrate the god.

[024-021] All ranks and sexes to his orgies ran,

[024-022] To mingle in the pomps and fill the train,

[024-023] When Pentheus thus his wicked rage express’d:

[024-024] “What madness, Thebans, has your souls possess’d?

[024-025] Can hollow timbrels, can a drunken shout,

[024-026] And the lewd clamours of a beastly rout,

[024-027] Thus quell your courage? Can the weak alarm

[024-028] Of women’s yells those stubborn souls disarm,

[024-029] Whom nor the sword nor trumpet e’er could fright,

[024-030] Nor the loud din and horror of a fight?

[024-031] And you, our sires, who left your old abodes,

[024-032] And fix’d in foreign earth your country gods,

[024-033] Will you without a stroke your city yield,

[024-034] And poorly quit an undisputed field?

[024-035] But you, whose youth and vigour should inspire

[024-036] Heroic warmth, and kindle martial fire,

[024-037] Whom burnish’d arms and crested helmets grace,

[024-038] Not flowery garlands and a painted face;

[024-039] Remember him to whom you stand allied;

[024-040] The serpent for his well of waters died.

[024-041] He fought the strong, do you his courage show,

[024-042] And gain a conquest o’er a feeble foe.

[024-043] If Thebes must fall, O might [[fate|the Fates]] afford

[024-044] A nobler doom from famine, fire, or sword;

[024-045] Then might the Thebans perish with renown:

[024-046] But now a beardless victor sacks the town,

[024-047] Whom nor the prancing steed, nor ponderous shield,

[024-048] Nor the hack’d helmet, nor the dusty field,

[024-049] But the soft joys of luxury and ease,

[024-050] The purple vests, and flowery garlands, please.

[024-051] Stand then aside, I’ll make the counterfeit

[024-052] Renounce his godhead, and confess the cheat.

[024-053] Acrisius from the Grecian walls repell’d

[024-054] This boasted power: why then should Pentheus yield?

[024-055] Go quickly, drag the impostor boy to me,

[024-056] I’ll try the force of his divinity.”

[024-057] Thus did the audacious wretch those rites profane;

[024-058] His friends dissuade the andacious wretch in vain,

[024-059] In vain his grandsire urged him to give o’er

[024-060] His impious threats, the wretch but raves the more.

[024-061] So have I seen a river gently glide

[024-062] In a smooth course and inoffensive tide,

[024-063] But if with dams its current we restrain,

[024-064] It bears down all, and foams along the plain.

[024-065] But now his servants came, besmear’d with blood,

[024-066] Sent by their haughty prince to seize the god;

[024-067] The god they found not in the frantic throng,

[024-068] But dragg’d a zealous votary along.
