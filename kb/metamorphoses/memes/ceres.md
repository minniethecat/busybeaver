# Ceres

Ceres (Greek: Demeter), goddess of agriculture and the harvest, appears in the *Metamorphoses* as a figure of immense power and profound grief. Her desperate search for her abducted daughter Proserpine moves through earth and underworld, reshaping landscapes and mortals with her sorrow and wrath. She is one of the oldest and most revered deities in Ovid's poem — the goddess who first taught mankind to cultivate the earth.

## Role in the Metamorphoses

Ceres appears primarily in Book 5 of the poem, where her search for Proserpine frames a series of interconnected transformations. She is both the initiating cause of the narrative — her daughter is taken, the earth withers — and the agent of justice who punishes those who mock her grief or violate her sacred rites.

### Key Episodes

- **First Teacher of Agriculture**: Ceres is credited as the goddess who "first taught the labouring hind" to till the soil and cultivate grain, earning her the devotion of all mortals (045-002)
- **The Rape of Proserpine**: When Pluto abducts Proserpine, Ceres searches the earth for her daughter, her grief causing crops to fail and the world to wither (046-046)
- **The Old Woman and the Drink**: Disguised as a mortal, Ceres requests drink from an old woman; a boy mocks her greed, and she transforms him into an eft, a small lizard-like creature (048-001)
- **Transformation of Ascalaphus**: When Ascalaphus reveals that Proserpine has eaten pomegranate seeds in the underworld — dooming her to remain — Ceres transforms him into an owl (049-001)
- **The Sirens**: Ceres decrees that the daughters of Achelous be transformed into Sirens, half-bird creatures, to search for her lost daughter forever (050-001)

## Transformation

Ceres is exclusively an **agent** of transformation rather than its subject. Her power to reshape others stems from her divine authority over the earth's fertility and her role as the wronged mother seeking justice. Unlike Jupiter's transformations (often driven by desire), Ceres' metamorphoses are acts of punishment or, more rarely, reward — each one a judgment on mortal conduct.

- **Form**: Divine (Olympian) → Does not transform herself
- **Agent**: Transforms mortals who offend her or violate her sacred rites
- **Cause**: Grief over Proserpine, punishment of impiety, enforcement of divine law
- **Source**: (045-002, 046-046, 048-001, 049-001, 050-001)
