[122-001] The goddess Circe, becoming enamoured of Glaucus, and finding his preference for Scylla, revenges herself on her unhappy rival by a hideous transformation—This sudden metamorphosis so terrifies her, that she throws herself into that part of the sea which separates the coasts of Italy and Sicily, where she is changed into dangerous rocks, which still bear her name.

[122-002] Now Glaucus, with a lover’s haste, bounds o’er

[122-003] The swelling waves, and seeks the Latian shore:

[122-004] Messena, Rhegium, and the barren coast

[122-005] Of flaming Aetna, to his sight are lost:

[122-006] At length he gains the Tyrrhene seas, and views

[122-007] The hills where baneful filters Circe brews;

[122-008] Monsters in various forms around her press;

[122-009] And thus the god salutes the sorceress:

[122-010] “Oh Circe, be indulgent to my grief,

[122-011] And give a lovesick deity relief.

[122-012] Too well the mighty power of plants I know,

[122-013] To those my figure and new fate I owe.

[122-014] Against Messena, on the Ausonian coast,

[122-015] I Scylla view’d, and from that hour was lost.

[122-016] In tenderest sounds I sued; but still the fair

[122-017] Was deaf to vows, and pitiless to prayer.

[122-018] If numbers can avail, exert their power;

[122-019] Or energy of plants, if plants have more.

[122-020] I ask no cure; let but the virgin pine

[122-021] With dying pangs, or agonies, like mine.”

[122-022] No longer Circe could her flame disguise,

[122-023] But to the suppliant god marine replies:

[122-024] “When maids are coy, have manlier aims in view;

[122-025] Leave those that fly, but those that like pursue.

[122-026] If love can be by kind compliance won,

[122-027] See, at your feet, the daughter of the sun.”

[122-028] “Sooner,” said Glaucus, “shall the ash remove

[122-029] From mountains, and the swelling surges love,

[122-030] Or humble seaweed to the hills repair,

[122-031] Ere I think any but my Scylla fair.”

[122-032] Straight Circe reddens with a guilty shame,

[122-033] And vows revenge for her rejected flame.

[122-034] Fierce liking oft a spite as fierce creates;

[122-035] For love refused, without aversion, hates.

[122-036] To hurt her hapless rival she proceeds,

[122-037] And, by the fall of Scylla, Glaucus bleeds.

[122-038] Some fascinating beverage now she brews,

[122-039] Composed of deadly drugs, and baneful juice.

[122-040] At Rhegium she arrives; the ocean braves,

[122-041] And treads with unwet feet the boiling waves.

[122-042] Upon the beach a winding bay there lies,

[122-043] Shelter’d from seas, and shaded from the skies:

[122-044] This station Scylla chose; a soft retreat

[122-045] From chilling winds, and raging Cancer’s heat.

[122-046] The vengeful sorceress visits this recess;

[122-047] Her charm infuses, and infects the place.

[122-048] Soon as the nymph wades in, her nether parts

[122-049] Turn into dogs, then at herself she starts;

[122-050] A ghastly horror in her eyes appears,

[122-051] But yet she knows not who it is she fears:

[122-052] In vain she offers from herself to run,

[122-053] And drags about her what she strives to shun.

[122-054] Oppress’d with grief the pitying god appears,

[122-055] And swells the rising surges with his tears;

[122-056] From the detested sorceress he flies;

[122-057] Her art reviles, and her address denies;

[122-058] While hapless Scylla, changed to rocks, decrees

[122-059] Destruction to those barks that beat the seas.
