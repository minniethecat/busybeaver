[120-001] Galatea, a sea nymph, is passionately beloved by the cyclop Polyphemus, whom she treats with disdain, while Acis, a shepherd of Sicily, is the object of her affections—Stung with jealousy, the cyclop crushes his rival with a piece of broken rock—His mistress is inconsolable for his loss; and since she is unable to restore him to life, changes him into a fountain.

[120-002] “Acis, the lovely youth, whose loss I mourn,

[120-003] From Faunus, and the nymph Symethis, born,

[120-004] Was both his parents’ pleasure, but to me,

[120-005] Was all that love could make a lover be.

[120-006] The gods our minds in mutual bands did join,

[120-007] I was his only joy, and he was mine.

[120-008] Now sixteen summers the sweet youth had seen,

[120-009] And doubtful down began to shade his chin,

[120-010] When Polyphemus first disturbed our joy,

[120-011] And loved me fiercely, as I loved the boy.

[120-012] Ask not which passion in my soul was higher

[120-013] My last aversion, or my first desire,

[120-014] Nor this the greater was, nor that the less,

[120-015] Both were alike, for both were in excess.

[120-016] Thee, [[venus|Venus]], thee, both heaven and earth obey,

[120-017] Immense thy power, and boundless is thy sway.

[120-018] The cyclop, who defied the ethereal throne,

[120-019] And thought no thunder louder than his own,

[120-020] The terror of the woods, and wilder far

[120-021] Than wolves in plains, or bears in forests, are,

[120-022] The inhuman host, who made his bloody feasts

[120-023] On mangled members of his butcher’d guests,

[120-024] Yet felt the force of love, and fierce desire,

[120-025] And burn’d for me with unrelenting fire

[120-026] Forgot his caverns, and his woolly care,

[120-027] Assumed the softness of a lover’s air,

[120-028] And comb’d, with teeth of rakes, his rugged hair:

[120-029] Now with a crooked scythe his beard he sleeks,

[120-030] And mows the stubborn stubble of his cheeks;

[120-031] Now in the crystal stream he looks, to try

[120-032] His courteous bows, and rolls his glaring eye.

[120-033] His cruelty and thirst for blood are lost;

[120-034] And ships securely sail along the coast.

[120-035] “The prophet Telemus (arrived by chance

[120-036] Where Aetna’s summits to the seas advance,

[120-037] Who mark’d the tracks of every bird that flew,

[120-038] And sure presages from their flying drew)

[120-039] Foretold the cyclop that Ulysses hand

[120-040] In his broad eye should thrust a flaming brand.

[120-041] The giant, with a scornful grin, replied,

[120-042] ‘Vain augur, thou hast falsely prophesied;

[120-043] Already love his flaming brand has toss’d,

[120-044] Looking on two fair eyes my sight I lost.’

[120-045] Thus, warn’d in vain, with stalking pace he strode,

[120-046] And stamp’d the margin of the briny flood

[120-047] With heavy steps, and weary, sought again

[120-048] The cool retirement of his gloomy den.

[120-049] “A promontory, sharpening by degrees,

[120-050] Ends in a wedge, and overlooks the seas,

[120-051] On either side below, the water flows;

[120-052] This airy walk the giant lover chose.

[120-053] Here on the midst he sat, his flocks unled,

[120-054] Their shepherd follow’d, and securely fed;

[120-055] A pine, so burly, and of length so vast,

[120-056] That sailing ships required it for a mast,

[120-057] He wielded for a staff, his steps to guide,

[120-058] But laid it by, his whistle while he tried;

[120-059] A hundred reeds, of a prodigious growth,

[120-060] Scarce made a pipe proportion’d to his mouth,

[120-061] Which, when he gave it wind, the rocks around,

[120-062] And watery plains, the dreadful hiss resound.

[120-063] I heard the ruffian shepherd rudely blow,

[120-064] Where in a hollow cave I sat below;

[120-065] On Acis’ bosom I my head reclined,

[120-066] And still preserve the poem in my mind.

[120-067] “ ‘Oh, lovely Galatea! whiter far

[120-068] Than falling snows and rising lilies are;

[120-069] More flowery than the meads, as crystal bright;

[120-070] Erect as alders, and of equal height:

[120-071] More wanton than a kid, more sleek thy skin

[120-072] Than orient shells, that on the shores are seen:

[120-073] Than apples fairer, when the boughs they lade;

[120-074] Pleasing as winter suns, or summer shade:

[120-075] More grateful to the sight than goodly plains,

[120-076] And softer to the touch than down of swans;

[120-077] Or curds new turn’d; and sweeter to the taste

[120-078] Than swelling grapes, that to the vintage haste:

[120-079] More clear than ice, or running streams, that stray

[120-080] Through garden plots, but, ah! more swift than they.

[120-081] “ ‘Yet, Galatea, harder to be broke

[120-082] Than bullocks, unreclaim’d to bear the yoke,

[120-083] And far more stubborn than the knotted oak:

[120-084] Like sliding streams, impossible to hold;

[120-085] Like them fallacious, like their fountains cold;

[120-086] More warping than the willow, to decline

[120-087] My warm embrace, more brittle than the vine;

[120-088] Immoveable and fix’d in thy disdain:

[120-089] Rough as these rocks, and of a harder grain.

[120-090] More violent than is the rising flood;

[120-091] And the praised peacock is not half so proud.

[120-092] Fierce as the fire, and sharp as thistles are,

[120-093] And more outrageous than a mother bear:

[120-094] Deaf as the billows to the vows I make;

[120-095] And more revengeful than a trodden snake

[120-096] In swiftness fleeter than the flying hind,

[120-097] Or driven tempests, or the driving wind.

[120-098] All other faults with patience I can bear,

[120-099] But swiftness is the vice I only fear.

[120-100] “ ‘Yet, if you knew me well, you would not shun

[120-101] My love, but to my wish’d embraces run:

[120-102] Would languish in your turn, and court my stay,

[120-103] And much repent of your unwise delay.

[120-104] “ ‘My palace in the living rock is made

[120-105] By nature’s hand: a spacious pleasing shade;

[120-106] Which neither heat can pierce, nor cold invade.

[120-107] My garden fill’d with fruits you may behold,

[120-108] And grapes in clusters, imitating gold;

[120-109] Some blushing bunches of a purple hue;

[120-110] And these, and those, are all reserved for you.

[120-111] Red strawberries, in shades, expecting stand,

[120-112] Proud to be gather’d by so white a hand.

[120-113] Autumnal cornels later fruit provide,

[120-114] And plums, to tempt you, turn their glossy side:

[120-115] Not those of common kinds, but such alone

[120-116] As in Phaeacian orchards might have grown:

[120-117] Nor chestnuts shall be wanting to your food,

[120-118] Nor garden fruits, nor wildings of the wood;

[120-119] The laden boughs for you alone shall bear;

[120-120] And yours shall be the product of the year.

[120-121] “ ‘The flocks you see are all my own; beside

[120-122] The rest that woods and winding valleys hide,

[120-123] And those that folded in the caves abide.

[120-124] Ask not the numbers of my growing store;

[120-125] Who knows how many, knows he has no more:

[120-126] Nor will I praise my cattle, trust not me,

[120-127] But judge yourself, and pass your own decree:

[120-128] Behold their swelling dugs, the sweepy weight

[120-129] Of ewes, that sink beneath the milky freight;

[120-130] In the warm folds their tender lambkins lie,

[120-131] Apart from kids, that call with human cry.

[120-132] New milk in nut-brown bowls is duly served

[120-133] For daily drink; the rest for cheese reserved.

[120-134] Nor are these household dainties all my store:

[120-135] The fields and forests will afford us more;

[120-136] The deer, the hare, the goat, the savage boar.

[120-137] All sorts of venison; and of birds the best;

[120-138] A pair of turtles taken from the nest.

[120-139] I walk’d the mountains, and two cubs I found,

[120-140] (Whose dam had left them on the naked ground,)

[120-141] So like, that no distinction could be seen:

[120-142] So pretty, they were presents for a queen;

[120-143] And so they shall: I took them both away,

[120-144] And keep to be companions of your play.

[120-145] “ ‘Oh raise, fair nymph, your beauteous face above

[120-146] The waves, nor scorn my presents and my love.

[120-147] Come, Galatea, come, and view my face;

[120-148] I late beheld it in the watery glass,

[120-149] And found it lovelier than I fear’d it was.

[120-150] Survey my towering stature, and my size:

[120-151] Not [[jupiter|Jove]], the Jove you dream that rules the skies,

[120-152] Bears such a bulk, or is so largely spread:

[120-153] My locks (the plenteous harvest of my head)

[120-154] Hang o’er my manly face, and dangling down,

[120-155] As with a shady grove, my shoulders crown:

[120-156] Nor think, because my limbs and body bear

[120-157] A thickset underwood of bristling hair,

[120-158] My shape deform’d; what fouler sight can be

[120-159] Than the bald branches of a leafless tree?

[120-160] Foul is the steed without a flowing mane,

[120-161] And birds without their feathers and their train.

[120-162] Wool decks the sheep, and man receives a grace

[120-163] From bushy limbs, and from a bearded face:

[120-164] My forehead with a single eye is fill’d,

[120-165] Round as a ball, and ample as a shield;

[120-166] The glorious lamp of heaven, the radiant sun,

[120-167] Is nature’s eye, and she’s content with one:

[120-168] Add, that my father sways your seas, and I,

[120-169] Like you, am of the watery family;

[120-170] I make you his, in making you my own;

[120-171] You I adore, and kneel to you alone.

[120-172] Jove, with his fabled thunder, I despise,

[120-173] And only fear the lightning of your eyes.

[120-174] Frown not, fair nymph; yet I could bear to be

[120-175] Disdain’d, if others were disdain’d with me

[120-176] But to repulse the cyclop, and prefer

[120-177] The love of Acis, heavens! I cannot bear.

[120-178] But let the stripling please himself; nay, more,

[120-179] Please you, though that’s the thing I most abhor;

[120-180] The boy shall find, if e’er we cope in fight,’

[120-181] These giant limbs endued with giant might.

[120-182] His living bowels, from his belly torn,

[120-183] And scatter’d limbs, shall on the flood be borne;

[120-184] Thy flood, ungrateful nymph, and fate shall find

[120-185] That way for thee and Acis to be join’d:

[120-186] For, oh! I burn with love, and thy disdain

[120-187] Augments at once my passion and my pain.

[120-188] Translated Aetna flames within my heart,

[120-189] And thou, inhuman, wilt not ease my smart.’

[120-190] “Lamenting thus in vain, he rose, and strode

[120-191] With furious paces to the neighbouring wood:

[120-192] Restless his feet, distracted was his walk,

[120-193] Mad were his motions, and confused his talk:

[120-194] Mad as the vanquish’d bull when forced to yield

[120-195] His lovely mistress, and forsake the field.

[120-196] “Thus far unseen I saw; when fatal chance

[120-197] His looks directing, with a sudden glance,

[120-198] Acis and I were to his sight betray’d,

[120-199] Where, naught suspecting, we securely play’d,

[120-200] From his wide mouth a bellowing cry he cast:

[120-201] ‘I see, I see; but this shall be your last.’

[120-202] A roar so loud made Aetna to rebound;

[120-203] And all the cyclop labour’d in the sound.

[120-204] Affrighted with his monstrous voice, I fled,

[120-205] And in the neighbouring ocean plunged my head:

[120-206] Poor Acis turn’d his back, and, ‘Help,’ he cried,

[120-207] ‘Help, Galatea; help, my parent gods,

[120-208] And take me, dying, to your deep abodes.’

[120-209] The cyclop follow’d, but he sent before

[120-210] A rib, which from the living rock he tore:

[120-211] Though but an angle reach’d him of the stone,

[120-212] The mighty fragment was enough alone

[120-213] To crush all Acis. ’Twas too late to save;

[120-214] But what the fntes allow’d to give, I gave;

[120-215] That Acis to his lineage should return,

[120-216] And roll among the river gods his urn.

[120-217] Straight issued from the stone a stream of blood,

[120-218] Which lost the purple, mingling with the flood:

[120-219] Then like a double torrent it appear’d,

[120-220] The torrent too, in little space was clear’d

[120-221] The stone was cleft and through the yawning chink

[120-222] New reeds arose on the new river’s brink.

[120-223] The rock, from out its hollow womb, disclosed

[120-224] A sound like water in its course opposed,

[120-225] When, wondrous to behold! full in the flood,

[120-226] Up starts a youth, and navel-high he stood;

[120-227] Horns from his temples rise, and either horn

[120-228] Thick wreaths of reeds (his native growth) adorn.

[120-229] Were not his stature taller than before,

[120-230] His bulk augmented, and his beauty more,

[120-231] His colour blue, for Acis he might pass,

[120-232] And Acis changed into a stream he was:

[120-233] But mine no more; he rolls along the plains

[120-234] With rapid motion, and his name retains.”
