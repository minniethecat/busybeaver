# Cycnus

Cycnus, the son of Sthenelus and a close friend (or, in some traditions, a lover) of Phaeton, embodies grief so profound that it triggers metamorphosis. When Phaeton is struck from the sky by Jupiter's thunderbolt, Cycnus repeatedly visits the tomb by the river Eridanus, mourning inconsolably. His unending lamentation moves the gods — or perhaps exhausts his own humanity — and he is transformed into a swan, the bird whose name (cycnus in Latin) he already bore. The swan's mournful song, its white plumage, and its association with water are all mythologically traced to Cycnus's metamorphosis.

## Role in the Metamorphoses

Cycnus appears in Book II, immediately following the death of Phaeton. As Phaeton's sisters, the Heliades, are transformed into poplars weeping amber tears, Cycnus witnesses their metamorphosis: "Cycnus beheld the nymphs transform'd, allied" (012-002). His own transformation follows: "Cycnus, the son of Sthenelus, is deeply affected at the death of his friend and relation, Phaeton; and, in the midst of his grief, he is changed into a swan" (012-001). The story is brief but significant: it transforms a natural creature (the swan) into a living monument of grief, and it links the swan's legendary song — the "swan song" of classical tradition — to the mourning for Phaeton.

### Key Episodes

- **Witnessing the Heliades**: Cycnus sees Phaeton's sisters transformed into poplars and their tears into amber (012-002)
- **The Transformation**: Overcome with grief for Phaeton, Cycnus is changed into a swan beside the river Eridanus (012-001)
- **The Swan's Legacy**: The swan's mournful song and white plumage are explained as the lasting form of Cycnus's sorrow

## Transformation

Cycnus's metamorphosis is one of the purest examples of grief-driven transformation in the poem. Unlike many metamorphoses that serve as punishment or reward, this one is the expression of an emotional state so intense that the body can no longer contain it.

- **Form**: Mortal man (Cycnus) → Swan (cycnus)
- **Agent**: Divine will, responding to inconsolable grief
- **Cause**: Mourning for Phaeton's death
- **Source**: (012-001)
