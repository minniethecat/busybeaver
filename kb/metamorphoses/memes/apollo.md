# Apollo

Apollo (also called Phoebus), the god of the sun, prophecy, music, and poetry, is second only to Jupiter in his prominence within Ovid's *Metamorphoses*. His pursuit of Daphne in Book I establishes the poem's fundamental pattern — god desires mortal, mortal flees, transformation resolves — and his appearances across all fifteen books link the poem's Greek mythological material to its Roman imperial conclusion.

## Role in the Metamorphoses

Apollo appears throughout the poem in multiple roles: as lover, as father, as punisher, and as the divine patron of poetry itself. His most famous episode — the pursuit of Daphne (007-001) — occurs in Book I and sets the template for dozens of subsequent transformation stories. As the poem progresses, Apollo's role shifts from active participant to ancestral figure: his son Phaeton's disastrous chariot ride (010-001) and his grandson Aesculapius's arrival in Rome (Book XV) connect the mythological past to Ovid's Augustan present.

### Key Episodes

- **Daphne and the Laurel**: Struck by Cupid's arrow, Apollo pursues the nymph Daphne, who prays for escape and is transformed into the laurel tree — Apollo's sacred plant thereafter (007-001)
- **Phaeton's Doom**: Apollo reluctantly grants his mortal son Phaeton a wish, which the youth uses to drive the sun-chariot; the resulting conflagration threatens the cosmos until Jupiter strikes Phaeton down (010-001)
- **Coronis and the Raven**: Apollo's beloved Coronis is unfaithful; a raven reports the infidelity, and Apollo kills her — the raven's white feathers are turned black as punishment (014-001)
- **Marsyas Flayed**: The satyr Marsyas challenges Apollo to a musical contest; defeated, he is flayed alive — his tears become the river Marsyas (043-001)
- **Hyacinthus**: Apollo's beloved Spartan youth is killed by a discus; from his blood the god creates the hyacinth flower inscribed with the lament "AI AI" (061-001)
- **Aesculapius in Rome**: Apollo's son Aesculapius, the god of healing, is summoned to Rome as a serpent to end a plague — linking Apollo's lineage to the foundation of Roman civic religion (156-001)

## Transformation

Apollo is more often the **agent** of transformation than its subject. His most famous metamorphic act is the creation of the laurel from Daphne's body (007-001) and the hyacinth from Hyacinthus's blood (061-001). When he transforms others, the change is typically compensatory — honoring the beloved's memory — rather than punitive. This distinguishes Apollo from Jupiter: where Jupiter's transformations serve his own desires, Apollo's serve commemoration.

- **Form**: Agent of transformation (not typically himself transformed)
- **Agent**: Apollo himself
- **Cause**: Commemoration of the beloved, punishment of betrayal
- **Source**: (007-001, 061-001, 014-001)
