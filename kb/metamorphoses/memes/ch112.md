[099-001] The hospitality of Midas towards Silenus, the tutor of [[bacchus|Bacchus]], is rewarded by the grateful deity with a permission to choose whatever recompense he pleases—Midas imprudently demands that whatever he touches may be turned into gold—His prayers are granted; and he is in danger of perishing by hunger, when the indulgent god supplies a remedy—Some time after this adventure Midas has the folly to maintain the superiority of Pan to [[apollo|Apollo]] in musical skill; for which rash opinion his ears are changed into those of an ass, to denote his ignorance and stupidity.

[099-002] Nor this sufficed; the god’s disgust remains,

[099-003] And he resolves to quit their hated plains:

[099-004] The vineyards of Tymole engross his care,

[099-005] And with a better choir he fixes there;

[099-006] Where the smooth streams of clear Pactolus roll’d,

[099-007] Then undistinguish’d for its sands of gold.

[099-008] The satyrs with the nymphs, his usual throng,

[099-009] Come to salute their god, and jovial dance along:

[099-010] Silenus only miss’d; for while he reel’d,

[099-011] Feeble with age and wine, about the field,

[099-012] The hoary drunkard had forgot his way,

[099-013] And to the Phrygian clowns became a prey;

[099-014] Who to King Midas drag the captive god,

[099-015] While on his totty pate the wreaths of ivy nod.

[099-016] Midas from [[orpheus|Orpheus]] had been taught his lore,

[099-017] And knew the rites of Bacchus long before:

[099-018] He, when he saw his venerable guest,

[099-019] In honour of the god ordain’d a feast.

[099-020] Ten days in course, with each continued night,

[099-021] Were spent in genial mirth and brisk delight;

[099-022] Then on the eleventh, when, with brighter ray,

[099-023] Phosphor had chased the fading stars away,

[099-024] The king through Lydia’s fields young Bacchus sought,

[099-025] And to the god his foster-father brought.

[099-026] Pleased with the welcome sight, he bids him soon

[099-027] But name his wish, and swears to grant the boon.

[099-028] A glorious offer! yet but ill bestow’d

[099-029] On him whose choice so little judgment show’d.

[099-030] “Give me,” says he, (nor thought he ask’d too much,)

[099-031] “That with my body whatsoe’er I touch,

[099-032] Changed from the nature which it held of old,

[099-033] May be converted into yellow gold.”

[099-034] He had his wish: but yet the god repined,

[099-035] To think the fool no better wish could find.

[099-036] But the brave king departed from the place

[099-037] With smiles of gladness sparkling in his face;

[099-038] Nor could contain, but, as he took his way,

[099-039] Impatient longs to make the first essay.

[099-040] Down from a lowly branch a twig he drew,

[099-041] The twig straight glitter’d with a golden hue.

[099-042] He takes a stone, the stone was turn’d to gold:

[099-043] A clod he touches, and the crumbling mould

[099-044] Acknowledged soon the great transforming power,

[099-045] In weight and substance like a mass of ore:

[099-046] He pluck’d the corn, and straight his grasp appears

[099-047] Fill’d with a bending tuft of golden ears.

[099-048] An apple next he takes, and seems to hold

[099-049] The bright Hesperian vegetable gold:

[099-050] His hand he careless on a pillar lays,

[099-051] With shining gold the fluted pillars blaze;

[099-052] And, while he washes, as the servants pour,

[099-053] His touch converts the stream to Dane’s shower.

[099-054] To see these miracles so finely wrought

[099-055] Fires with transporting joy his giddy thought.

[099-056] The ready slaves prepare a sumptuous board,

[099-057] Spread with rich dainties for their happy lord;

[099-058] Whose powerful hands the bread no sooner hold,

[099-059] But its whole substance is transform’d to gold:

[099-060] Up to his mouth he lifts the savoury meat,

[099-061] Which turns to gold as he attempts to eat:

[099-062] His patron’s noble juice of purple hue,

[099-063] Touch’d by his lips, a gilded cordial grew,

[099-064] Unfit for drink; and, wondrous to behold,

[099-065] It trickles from his jaws a fluid gold.

[099-066] The rich poor fool, confounded with surprise,

[099-067] Starving in all his various plenty lies;

[099-068] Sick of his wish, he now detests the power,

[099-069] For which he ask’d so earnestly before;

[099-070] Amid his gold with pinching famine cursed,

[099-071] And justly tortured with an equal thirst:

[099-072] At last, his shining arms to heaven he rears,

[099-073] And, in distress, for refuge flies to prayers.

[099-074] “Oh, Father Bacchus, I have sinn’d,” he cried,

[099-075] “And foolishly thy gracious gift applied;

[099-076] Thy pity now, repenting, I implore,

[099-077] Oh may I feel the golden plague no more!”

[099-078] The hungry wretch, his folly thus confess’d,

[099-079] Touch’d the kind deity’s good-natured breast;

[099-080] The gentle god annull’d his first decree,

[099-081] And from the cruel compact set him free.

[099-082] But then, to cleanse him quite from further harm,

[099-083] And to dilute the relics of the charm,

[099-084] He bids him seek the stream, that cuts the land

[099-085] Nigh where the towers of Lydias Sardis stand;

[099-086] Then trace the river to the fountain head

[099-087] And meet it rising from its rocky bed;

[099-088] There, as the bubbling tide pours forth amain,

[099-089] To plunge his body in, and wash away the stain.

[099-090] The king, instructed, to the fount retires,

[099-091] But with the golden charm the stream inspires;

[099-092] For, while this quality the man forsakes,

[099-093] An equal power the limpid water takes;

[099-094] Informs with veins of gold the neighbouring land,

[099-095] And glides along a bed of golden sand.

[099-096] Now loathing wealth, the occasion of his woes,

[099-097] Far in the woods, he sought a calm repose

[099-098] In caves and grottoes, where the nymphs resort,

[099-099] And keep with mountain Pan their sylvan court.

[099-100] Ah! had he left his stupid soul behind;

[099-101] But his condition alter’d not his mind.

[099-102] For where high Tmolus rears his shady brow,

[099-103] And from his cliffs surveys the seas below

[099-104] In his descent, by Sardis bounded here,

[099-105] By the small confines of Hypaepa there,

[099-106] Pan to the nymphs his frolic ditties play’d,

[099-107] Tuning his reeds beneath the checker’d shade.

[099-108] The nymphs are pleased, the boasting sylvan plays,

[099-109] And speaks with slight of great Apollo’s lays.

[099-110] Tmolus was arbiter; the boaster still

[099-111] Accepts the trial with unequal skill.

[099-112] The venerable judge was seated high

[099-113] On his own hill, that seem’d to touch the sky.

[099-114] Above the whispering trees his head he rears,

[099-115] From their encumbering boughs to free his ears;

[099-116] A wreath of oak alone his temples bound,

[099-117] The pendant acorns loosely dangled round.

[099-118] “In me, your judge,” says he, “there’s no delay;”

[099-119] Then bids the goatherd god begin and play.

[099-120] Pan tuned his pipe, and with his rural song

[099-121] Pleased the low taste of all the vulgar throng.

[099-122] Such songs a vulgar judgment mostly please,

[099-123] Midas was there, and Midas judged with these.

[099-124] The mountain sire, with grave deportment, now

[099-125] To Phoebus turns his venerable brow;

[099-126] And, as he turns, with him the listening wood

[099-127] In the same posture of attention stood.

[099-128] The god his own Parnassian laurel crown’d,

[099-129] And in a wreath his golden tresses bound;

[099-130] Graceful his purple mantle swept the ground.

[099-131] High on the left his ivory lute he raised;

[099-132] The lute, emboss’d with glittering jewels, blazed;

[099-133] In his right hand he nicely held the quill,

[099-134] His easy posture spoke a master’s skill;

[099-135] The strings he touch’d with more than human art,

[099-136] Which pleased the judge’s ear, and soothed his heart;

[099-137] Who soon judiciously the palm decreed,

[099-138] And to the lute postponed the squeaking reed.

[099-139] All, with applause, the rightful sentence heard,

[099-140] Midas alone dissatisfied appear’d;

[099-141] To him unjustly given the judgment seems,

[099-142] For Pan’s barbaric notes he most esteems.

[099-143] The lyric god, who thought his untuned ear

[099-144] Deserved but ill a human form to wear,

[099-145] Of that deprives him, and supplies the place

[099-146] With some more fit, and of an ampler space,

[099-147] Fix’d on his noddle an unseemly pair,

[099-148] Flagging, and large, and full of whitish hair;

[099-149] Without a total change from what he was,

[099-150] Still in the man preserves the simple ass.

[099-151] He, to conceal the scandal of the deed,

[099-152] A purple turban folds about his head,

[099-153] Veils the reproach from public view, and fears

[099-154] The laughing world would spy his monstrous ears.

[099-155] One trusty barber slave, that used to dress

[099-156] His master’s hair, when lengthen’d to excess,

[099-157] The mighty secret knew, but knew alone,

[099-158] And, though impatient, durst not make it known.

[099-159] Restless, at last a private place he found,

[099-160] Then dug a hole, and told it to the ground;

[099-161] In a low whisper he reveal’d the case,

[099-162] And cover’d in the earth, and silent left the place.

[099-163] In time, of trembling reeds a plenteous crop

[099-164] From the confided furrow sprouted up,

[099-165] Which, high advancing with the ripening year,

[099-166] Made known the tiller, and his fruitless care;

[099-167] For then the rustling blades and whispering wind

[099-168] To tell the important secret both combined.
