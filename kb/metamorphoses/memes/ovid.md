# Ovid

Publius Ovidius Naso — Ovid — is both the author and, in a real sense, a character of the *Metamorphoses*. His narrative voice is not a transparent window onto myth but a self-conscious, witty, and often irreverent presence that shapes, comments on, and occasionally intervenes in the stories it tells. The poem's final book contains his most explicit self-insertion: the prophecy of his own immortality through poetry, a metamorphosis of the poet himself into a body of text that will outlast empires.

## Role in the Metamorphoses

Ovid's presence in the poem is multifaceted. As **narrator**, he selects, orders, and links the stories, creating the continuous "perpetual song" he announces in the opening lines. As **commentator**, he offers ironic asides, moral judgments, and self-aware reflections on the act of storytelling. As **prophet of his own fame**, he concludes the poem with a stunning assertion: that his work — and through it, his name — will survive the destruction of the world, a metamorphosis of mortal poet into immortal text.

### Key Episodes

- **The Invocation** (Book 1): Ovid opens with an address to the gods, asking them to inspire his "perpetual song" of forms changed into new bodies — a statement of poetic ambition that frames the entire work (001-001)
- **The Poet's Self-Defense**: Throughout the poem, Ovid's narrative persona reflects on the nature of poetic truth, the relationship between art and power, and the poet's role as both servant and critic of imperial authority
- **The Final Prophecy** (Book 15): In the poem's closing lines, Ovid declares that his work will outlive "the wrath of Jove, fire, sword, and the gnawing tooth of time" — the poet himself undergoes the ultimate metamorphosis, transformed from mortal author into immortal text (147-001)

## Transformation

Ovid's own metamorphosis — the one he orchestrates for himself — is literary rather than physical. In the poem's final passage, he asserts that "the better part of me shall be borne above the lofty stars, and my name shall never die." The transformation is from mortal to immortal, body to text, silence to eternal song. It is the poem's final and most audacious metamorphosis: the poet writes himself into perpetuity.

- **Form**: Mortal poet → Immortal text (the *Metamorphoses* itself)
- **Agent**: His own art — the poem as self-transformation
- **Cause**: The desire for immortality through art rather than through physical metamorphosis
- **Source**: (147-001)
