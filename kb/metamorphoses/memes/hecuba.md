# Hecuba

Hecuba (Greek: Hecabe), queen of Troy and wife of King Priam, endures perhaps the most brutal arc of suffering in the *Metamorphoses*. Having already witnessed the destruction of her city and the deaths of her husband and most of her children, she discovers the body of her youngest son Polydorus — murdered for gold by the Thracian king to whom he had been entrusted. Her subsequent vengeance and transformation into a dog make her the poem's most powerful emblem of grief made visible, a human being whose sorrow is so absolute that it changes her species.

## Role in the Metamorphoses

Hecuba appears in Book 13, in the aftermath of Troy's fall. Already a captive of the Greeks, she discovers the body of her son Polydorus washed ashore — murdered by Polymestor, the Thracian king to whom Priam had sent the boy with gold for safekeeping. What follows is a sequence of mounting horror: the discovery, the grief, the calculated vengeance, and the final, terrible metamorphosis.

### Key Episodes

- **Discovery of Polydorus**: Hecuba finds the body of her son Polydorus on the shore; "Last Hecuba on board, sad sight, appears" — she is the last of the Trojan captives to be led away, and this discovery is the final blow to a woman who has already lost everything (117-001)
- **Vengeance on Polymestor**: "So Hecuba with mingled grief and rage" — she contrives to lure Polymestor to her, and with the other Trojan women, tears out his eyes, exacting the only justice available to a captive queen (117-197)
- **Transformation into a Dog**: Her grief and fury are so absolute that they reshape her body; she is transformed into a dog, her howls of mourning becoming literal — a metamorphosis that externalizes a grief too vast for human form to contain (118-079)

## Transformation

Hecuba's metamorphosis is one of the most psychologically acute in the poem. She does not become a dog through the agency of any god or external force — her transformation is driven by the sheer magnitude of her grief and rage. It is emotion itself that remakes her body, converting a queen's lament into an animal's howl. The metamorphosis is both punishment and release: a form that matches the inhuman scope of her suffering.

- **Form**: Mortal queen → Dog
- **Agent**: Grief itself — her transformation is driven by emotional extremity rather than divine intervention
- **Cause**: The murder of Polydorus, the destruction of Troy, the loss of her entire family
- **Source**: (117-001, 117-197, 118-004, 118-079)
