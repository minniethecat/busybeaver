## Themes

- **Art and Loss**: [[Orpheus]]'s song — the entire book except the frame — is art born from grief. Having lost [[Eurydice]] twice, he turns away from women and sings of boys loved by gods. His song is both a renunciation and a sublimation: desire channeled into narrative.

- **The Artist as Creator**: [[Pygmalion]] sculpts his ideal woman and she comes to life — a metamorphosis wrought not by gods but by art and prayer combined. Ovid makes Pygmalion the poet's double: both create worlds through love and craft.

- **Forbidden Love and Its Price**: [[Myrrha]]'s incestuous passion for her father [[Cinyras]] is the book's darkest story, answered by [[Adonis]]'s birth from the myrrh tree and his death in [[Venus]]'s arms. The cycle of forbidden birth and tragic death encloses Book X in a ring of sorrow.

## Narrative Arc

Book X is framed entirely within Orpheus's song, making it the most unified book in the poem — a concert performance. The stories move from divine love (Ganymede, Hyacinthus) through artistic creation (Pygmalion) to transgressive passion (Myrrha) and tragic loss (Adonis). Each tale mirrors Orpheus's own wound: the impossibility of holding onto what you love.

## Key Transformations

- [[Cyparissus]] → Cypress tree: grieving the accidental killing of his pet stag (090-095)
- [[Hyacinthus]] → Hyacinth flower: killed by a discus thrown by Apollo; the flower bears his name (090-095)
- [[Cerastae]] → Bulls, [[Propoetides]] → Stone: for impiety and prostitution (090-095)
- [[Pygmalion]]'s statue → Living woman ([[Galatea]]): Venus answers the sculptor's prayer (090-095)
- [[Myrrha]] → Myrrh tree: fleeing her father Cinyras after incest; Adonis born from the tree (090-095)
- [[Adonis]] → Anemone (short-lived flower): gored by a boar; Venus's tears mingle with his blood (090-095)
