Juno (Greek: [[Hera]]) is the queen of the [[Olympus|Olympian]] gods, daughter of [[Saturn]], sister and wife of [[Jupiter]], and the goddess of marriage, childbirth, and women. In Ovid's *Metamorphoses*, Juno appears primarily as a figure of jealous wrath — she is far less the majestic queen than the vindictive wife, relentlessly pursuing [[Jupiter]]'s mortal lovers and their illegitimate children across the mythological world. Her actions drive several of the poem's most memorable transformation narratives, including those of [[Io]], [[Callisto]], [[Semele]], and [[Tiresias]].

## Role in the Metamorphoses

Juno appears across multiple books of the *Metamorphoses*, most prominently in the early books where her jealousy drives the persecution of Jupiter's mortal lovers. Her narrative function is consistently antagonistic: she is the divine obstacle that sets transformation plots in motion. When she is not actively punishing a rival, she is dispatching messengers (like [[Iris]]) or avenging slights against her dignity.

### Key Episodes

- **The Jealousy of Juno and Io's Transformation** (I.568–747): Juno suspects Jupiter's infidelity and descends from Olympus to investigate a mysterious cloud. Jupiter transforms [[Io]] into a heifer to conceal her, but Juno demands the beautiful animal as a gift and sets hundred-eyed [[Argus]] to guard her. After [[Mercury]] slays Argus, Juno transfers his eyes to the peacock's tail (006-177; 008-001).
- **Callisto's Punishment** (II.401–530): Juno discovers that [[Callisto]], a nymph of [[Diana]]'s train, has borne Jupiter a son ([[Arcas]]). She transforms Callisto into a bear; years later, Jupiter places both mother and son among the stars as Ursa Major and Ursa Minor. Juno, enraged at this celestial honor, persuades [[Oceanus]] and [[Tethys]] never to let the Great Bear touch their waters (013-001; 013-034).
- **Juno's Argument with Jove over Tiresias** (III.316–338): When [[Jupiter]] and Juno debate whether men or women experience greater pleasure in love, they consult [[Tiresias]], who has lived as both. He agrees with Jupiter; Juno, furious at losing the argument, blinds Tiresias. Jupiter compensates with the gift of prophecy (013-087; 013-096).
- **Semele's Destruction** (III.259–315): Juno, learning of [[Semele]]'s pregnancy by Jupiter, disguises herself as the girl's aged nurse and plants doubt in her mind. She tricks Semele into demanding Jupiter reveal his true divine form — the resulting epiphany incinerates Semele instantly. The unborn [[Bacchus]] is rescued from her womb (009-053).
- **Juno and the House of Cadmus** (IV.416–562): Juno's wrath extends across generations: after the death of [[Semele]], she continues to torment the Theban royal house, eventually driving [[Athamas]] and [[Ino]] mad as punishment for their role in nurturing the infant Bacchus.

## Transformation

While Juno herself never undergoes metamorphosis, she is one of the poem's most prolific **agents of transformation**:

- **Io**: Woman → Heifer (by Jupiter to conceal; Juno's jealousy precipitates the chain of events)
- **Callisto**: Nymph → Bear (directly transformed by Juno as punishment, 013-001)
- **Tiresias**: Man → Blind prophet (blinded by Juno as punishment, 013-087)
