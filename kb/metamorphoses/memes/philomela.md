# Philomela

Philomela, daughter of Pandion, king of Athens, is at the center of one of the most harrowing and artistically significant episodes in Ovid's *Metamorphoses*. Abducted, violated, and mutilated by her brother-in-law Tereus, king of Thrace, she nevertheless achieves justice through her weaving — a story that has been read as Ovid's most profound meditation on art, violence, and female agency. Her transformation into a nightingale completes one of the poem's most complex metamorphic sequences (060-001).

## Role in the Metamorphoses

Philomela's story occupies Book VI (chapters 60–65) and is one of the longest continuous narratives in the poem. Her sister Procne, married to Tereus, king of Thrace, asks Tereus to bring Philomela from Athens for a visit. Tereus, inflamed with lust at the sight of Philomela, abducts her, imprisons her in a forest tower, and rapes her (060-025). When Philomela threatens to tell the world of his crime, Tereus cuts out her tongue to silence her. But Philomela weaves the story of her violation into a tapestry and sends it to Procne — an act of communication through art that transcends physical silencing. The sisters then take a terrible revenge: Procne kills her own son Itys and serves him to Tereus at a banquet. As Tereus pursues the sisters with a sword, all three are transformed into birds: Philomela becomes a nightingale, Procne a swallow, Tereus a hoopoe (060-030).

### Key Episodes

- **Abduction and Violation**: Tereus, charged with escorting Philomela to Thrace, instead abducts and imprisons her (060-001)
- **The Mutilation**: When Philomela threatens exposure, Tereus cuts out her tongue — but she weaves her story into a tapestry (060-025)
- **The Revenge of the Sisters**: Procne, discovering the truth through the tapestry, kills Itys and serves him to Tereus (060-030)
- **The Triple Transformation**: Philomela becomes a nightingale, Procne a swallow, Tereus a hoopoe
