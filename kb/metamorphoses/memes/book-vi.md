## Themes

- **Art and Hubris**: Book VI opens with [[Arachne]], the mortal weaver who challenges [[Minerva]]. Her tapestry — depicting the gods' rapes and deceits — is flawless, but Minerva destroys it anyway. The contest raises an unsettling question: is the punishment for bad art, or for art that tells the wrong truth?

- **The Suffering of Mothers**: [[Niobe]] boasts of her fourteen children and is punished by the loss of every one. [[Procne]] kills her own son Itys to avenge her sister [[Philomela]]. Motherhood in Book VI is a site of unbearable pain — children are weapons, losses, and sometimes, meals.

- **The Body as Canvas**: From Arachne's woven bodies to Philomela's severed tongue to the tapestry she weaves to communicate her rape, Book VI obsessively links bodies, textiles, and communication. When speech is taken, cloth speaks; when cloth is destroyed, bodies are transformed.

## Narrative Arc

Book VI is the poem's darkest book. It moves from divine contests (Arachne, Niobe) into the realm of human atrocity (Tereus, Procne, Philomela), and closes with [[Boreas]]'s abduction of [[Orithyia]] — a narrative that mirrors the opening concern with violated bodies but in a mythic, less graphic register. It is a study in escalation: each story is more violent than the last.

## Key Transformations

- [[Arachne]] → Spider: for daring to weave better than Minerva and depicting divine crimes (053-060)
- [[Niobe]] → Weeping rock: her fourteen children slain by Apollo and Diana; she weeps eternally (053-060)
- Lycian peasants → Frogs: for denying Latona water from their marsh (053-060)
- [[Marsyas]] → River (or flayed): flayed alive by Apollo for challenging him to a music contest (053-060)
- [[Philomela]] → Nightingale, [[Procne]] → Swallow: after the infanticide of Itys and the revelation of Tereus's crimes (053-060)
- [[Tereus]] → Hoopoe: pursuing the sisters with a sword, transformed mid-chase (053-060)
