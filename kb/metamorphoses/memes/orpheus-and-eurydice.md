# Orpheus and Eurydice

The descent of Orpheus to the Underworld — and his fatal backward glance — is the *Metamorphoses*' most poignant exploration of the relationship between art, love, and death. Orpheus's song moves the rulers of the dead, suspends the torments of the damned, and nearly triumphs over mortality itself. But at the very threshold of the upper world, he turns to look at Eurydice — and loses her forever.

## Setting

The Underworld — the realm of Pluto and Proserpina — where Orpheus descends through the Gate of Taenarus. The path leads past the shades of the dead, Tantalus's pool, Ixion's wheel, and the Furies themselves, all of whom pause at the sound of his lyre.

## Narrative

On her wedding day, Eurydice is bitten by a serpent and dies. Orpheus, inconsolable, descends to the Underworld. His song — a plea for his wife's return — moves Pluto and Proserpina, who grant Eurydice's release on one condition: Orpheus must not look back at her until they reach the upper world. They traverse the dark path upward; Orpheus, at the very threshold, "anxious to see her, and afraid to lose her," turns. Eurydice, dying a second time, can only whisper "Farewell" before being drawn back into darkness. Orpheus attempts to follow but is barred by Charon. He returns to the upper world alone.

## Themes

- **The limits of art**: Orpheus's song can move the gods but cannot overcome human anxiety
- **The backward glance**: The fatal compulsion to verify, to confirm, to see — the epistemological tragedy
- **Love as loss**: Eurydice is lost twice — first by accident, then by love itself
- **The poet's grief**: Orpheus's subsequent songs (Book X) are all born from this loss
