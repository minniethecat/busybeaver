[118-001] Memnon, the son of Aurora, is killed by Achilles at the siege of [[troy|Troy]]—In honour of his memory, and in compliance with the prayers of his mother, [[jupiter|Jupiter]] causes birds, called Memnonides, to spring from his ashes, who divide into two parties, and contend with mutual acrimony.

[118-002] Yet bright Aurora, partial as she was

[118-003] To Troy, and those that loved the Trojan cause,

[118-004] Nor Troy nor Hecuba can now bemoan,

[118-005] But weeps a sad misfortune, more her own.

[118-006] Her offspring Memnon, by Achilles slain,

[118-007] She saw extended on the Phrygian plain:

[118-008] She saw, and straight the purple beams, that grace

[118-009] The rosy morning, vanish’d from her face;

[118-010] A deadly pale her wonted bloom invades,

[118-011] And veils the lowering skies with mournful shades.

[118-012] But when his limbs upon the pile were laid,

[118-013] The last kind duty that by friends is paid,

[118-014] His mother to the skies directs her flight,

[118-015] Nor could sustain to view the doleful sight:

[118-016] But frantic, with her loose neglected hair,

[118-017] Hastens to Jove, and falls a suppliant there.

[118-018] “Oh king of heaven, oh father of the skies,”

[118-019] The weeping goddess passionately cries;

[118-020] “Though I the meanest of immortals am,

[118-021] And fewest temples celebrate my fame,

[118-022] Yet still a goddess, I presume to come

[118-023] Within the verge of your ethereal dome;

[118-024] Yet still may plead some merit, if my light

[118-025] “With purple dawn controls the powers of night;

[118-026] If from a female hand that virtue springs,

[118-027] Which to the gods and men such pleasure brings.

[118-028] Yet I nor honours seek, nor rites divine,

[118-029] Nor for more altars or more fanes repine;

[118-030] Oh that such trifles were the only cause

[118-031] From whence Aurora’s mind its anguish draws!

[118-032] For Memnon lost, my dearest only child,

[118-033] With weightier grief my heavy heart is fill’d;

[118-034] My warrior son! that lived but half his time,

[118-035] Nipp’d in the bud, and blasted in his prime;

[118-036] Who for his uncle early took the field,

[118-037] And by Achilles’ fatal spear was kill’d.

[118-038] To whom but Jove should I for succour come?

[118-039] For Jove alone could fix his cruel doom.

[118-040] Oh sovereign of the gods, accept my prayer,

[118-041] Grant my request, and soothe a mother’s care;

[118-042] On the deceased some solemn boon bestow,

[118-043] To expiate the loss, and ease my wo.”

[118-044] Jove, with a nod, complied with her desire;

[118-045] Around the body flamed the funeral fire;

[118-046] The pile decreased, that lately seem’d so high,

[118-047] And sheets of smoke roll’d upward to the sky:

[118-048] As humid vapours from a marshy bog

[118-049] Rise by degrees, condensing into fog,

[118-050] That intercept the sun’s enlivening ray,

[118-051] And with a cloud infect the cheerful day;

[118-052] The sooty ashes wafted by the air,

[118-053] Whirl round, and thicken in a body there;

[118-054] Then take a form, which their own heat and fire,

[118-055] With active life and energy inspire.

[118-056] Its lightness makes it seem to fly, and soon

[118-057] It skims on real wings, that are its own;

[118-058] A real bird, it beats the breezy wind,

[118-059] Mix’d with a thousand sisters of the kind,

[118-060] That, from the same formation newly sprung,

[118-061] Upborne aloft on plumy pinions hung.

[118-062] Thrice round the pile advanced the circling throng;

[118-063] Thrice, with their wings, a whizzing consort rung.

[118-064] In the fourth flight their squadron they divide,

[118-065] Rank’d in two different troops, on either side:

[118-066] Then two and two, inspired with martial rage,

[118-067] From either troop in equal pairs engage.

[118-068] Each combatant with beak and pounces press’d,

[118-069] In wrathful ire, his adversary’s breast;

[118-070] Each falls a victim, to preserve the fame

[118-071] Of that great hero whence their being came.

[118-072] From him their courage and their name they take;

[118-073] And, as they lived, they die for Memnon’s sake.

[118-074] Punctual to time, with each revolving year,

[118-075] In fresh array the champion birds appear;

[118-076] Again, prepared with vengeful minds, they come

[118-077] To bleed, in honour of the soldier’s tomb.

[118-078] Therefore in others it appear’d not strange

[118-079] To grieve for Hecuba’s unhappy change:

[118-080] But poor Aurora had enough to do

[118-081] With her own loss, to mind another’s wo;

[118-082] Who still in tears her tender nature shows,

[118-083] Besprinkling all the world with pearly dews.
