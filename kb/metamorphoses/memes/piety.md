# Piety

Piety (*pietas*) is the cardinal virtue of Ovid's mythological world: the proper reverence that mortals owe to the gods. Throughout the *Metamorphoses*, piety determines survival — the pious are rewarded with salvation or apotheosis, while the impious are punished with transformation or destruction. Piety is thus the moral axis around which the poem's system of divine justice revolves.

## Definition

In the *Metamorphoses*, piety encompasses religious observance (sacrifice, prayer, recognition of divine power), filial duty, and hospitality toward strangers — the latter being a sacred obligation in the ancient Mediterranean world. Its opposite, impiety (*impietas*), is the denial or mockery of divine authority, exemplified by figures like Lycaon (who serves human flesh to Jupiter), Pentheus (who denies Bacchus's divinity), and the daughters of Minyas (who refuse to worship). Ovid presents piety not as an abstract ideal but as a practical necessity: the gods walk the earth in disguise, and mistreating a stranger may mean mistreating a deity.

## Significance

Piety structures the moral universe of the *Metamorphoses*. Unlike Fate (which is inexorable) or Love (which is compulsive), piety involves **choice** — and the consequences of that choice are immediate and visible in the body. The pious are transformed as a reward (Baucis and Philemon become sacred trees, Deucalion and Pyrrha survive to renew humanity), while the impious are transformed as punishment. This moral clarity is complicated by the poem's ending: Ovid's claim to poetic immortality (147-002) represents a new form of piety — devotion not to the gods but to art itself.
