# Pluto

Pluto (also called Dis or Hades) is the god of the underworld and ruler of the dead in the *Metamorphoses*. His realm lies beneath the earth, a shadowy kingdom separated from the world of the living. Pluto's most significant role in the poem is his abduction of Proserpine (Persephone), the daughter of Ceres — an act that sets in motion the cycle of the seasons and one of the most elaborate mythological narratives in Ovid's work. Though a formidable and fearsome deity, Pluto is also vulnerable to love: struck by Cupid's arrow at Venus's command, he becomes inflamed with passion for Proserpine and seizes her while she gathers flowers in the plains of Enna.

## Role in the Metamorphoses

Pluto appears primarily in Book V, where Venus, seeking to extend her dominion to the underworld, instructs Cupid to strike Pluto with an arrow of love. The god is surveying the foundations of his realm when "Pluto with the flash of day" is startled (010-304) — his first appearance. The Muses' song then describes how Venus and Cupid conspire to "inflame the god Pluto with a passion for Proserpine" (045-001). Pluto surprises Proserpine as she gathers flowers in Enna and transports her to the underworld (046-001). The palace is depicted with grim detail: "Know not the palace where grim Pluto reigns" (033-036), and "The courtiers smoothly glide to Pluto's court" (033-048), describing the shadowy attendants of his domain.

### Key Episodes

- **The Conspiracy of Venus**: Venus commands Cupid to strike Pluto with love for Proserpine, extending her power to the underworld (045-001)
- **The Abduction of Proserpine**: Pluto seizes Proserpine in the fields of Enna and carries her to his subterranean kingdom (046-001)
- **The Court of Shadows**: Pluto's realm is described with its ghostly courtiers and grim palace (033-036, 033-048)

## Transformation

Pluto himself does not undergo metamorphosis — as a god, he is a fixed presence. However, he is the *agent* of a transformative event: his abduction of Proserpine changes the natural world, causing Ceres to withhold the harvest and bringing about the cycle of the seasons. His realm is, by definition, the destination for many of the poem's transformations — the place where mortals go after their final metamorphosis (death).

- **Agent of Change**: Pluto's abduction of Proserpine → seasonal cycle
- **Source**: (045-001, 046-001)
