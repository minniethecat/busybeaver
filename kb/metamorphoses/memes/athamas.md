# Athamas

Athamas, king of Boeotia, is the victim of one of the *Metamorphoses*' most terrifying demonstrations of divine wrath. Because his wife Ino had helped raise the infant Bacchus — the child of Jupiter's affair with Semele — Juno's vengeance falls on Athamas and his household. The goddess sends the Fury Tisiphone to drive Athamas mad; in his frenzy, he seizes his infant son Learchus and dashes his brains out against a rock. The episode is brutal, swift, and entirely undeserved — a study in the absolute vulnerability of mortals to the unaccountable rage of the gods.

## Role in the Metamorphoses

Athamas appears in Book 4, as part of the cycle of Theban myths. Juno, enraged that Ino (Athamas's wife and Semele's sister) helped raise the infant Bacchus, descends to the underworld and summons the Fury Tisiphone. The Fury, armed with her torches and serpent crown, enters the palace of Athamas and Ino, afflicting them both with madness. What follows is one of the poem's most concentrated sequences of horror.

### Key Episodes

- **Juno's Wrath**: Juno, surveying the triumph of Bacchus and the honor given to Ino, determines to destroy them both; she descends to the underworld and enlists Tisiphone as her instrument of vengeance (034-001)
- **The Fury's Attack**: Tisiphone, with "her torches dripping blood," enters the palace, flings two serpents at Athamas and Ino, and breathes madness into their minds (034-001)
- **The Death of Learchus**: Athamas, in his frenzy, seizes his infant son Learchus and whirls him around before dashing his skull against a rock — "the child's brains were scattered on the stone" — one of the poem's most shocking acts of violence (034-001)
- **Ino's Flight**: Ino, also maddened, seizes their other son Melicertes and flees, eventually leaping from a cliff into the sea — where, by Venus's intercession, she and her son are transformed into sea-deities (034-001)

## Transformation

Athamas's transformation is psychological rather than physical: a king turned into a wild animal by the Fury's breath. His madness is itself a metamorphosis — his reason, his fatherhood, his humanity stripped away in an instant by divine malice. Unlike most of the poem's transformations, Athamas's is not permanent in form but in consequence: the father who killed his son can never again be the man he was.

- **Form**: Sane king → Maddened killer (psychological transformation inflicted by divine agency)
- **Agent**: The Fury Tisiphone, acting at Juno's command
- **Cause**: Juno's vengeance against Ino and Bacchus; Athamas is collateral damage
- **Source**: (034-001)
