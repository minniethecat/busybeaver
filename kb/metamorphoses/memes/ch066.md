[058-001] Marsyas, a celebrated player on the flute, is hanged and flayed alive by [[apollo|Apollo]], as a punishment for his imprudence in challenging the god to a trial of skill—The death of the musician is universally lamented by the Fauns, Satyrs, and Dryads; and from their abundant tears arises a river of [[phrygia|Phrygia]], well known by the name of Marsyas.

[058-002] Scarce had the mar; this famous story told,

[058-003] Of vengeance on the Lycians shown of old,

[058-004] When, straight, another pictures to their view

[058-005] The satyr’s fate, whom angry Phoebus slew;

[058-006] Who, raised with high conceit, and puff’d with pride,

[058-007] At his own pipe the skilful god defied.

[058-008] “Why do you tear me from myself?” he cries;

[058-009] “Ah! cruel; must my skin be made the prize?

[058-010] This for a silly pipe?” he roaring said;

[058-011] Meanwhile the skin from off his limbs was flay’d.

[058-012] All bare, and raw, one large continued wound,

[058-013] With streams of blood his body bathed the ground.

[058-014] The bluish veins their trembling pulse disclosed,

[058-015] The stringy nerves lay naked and exposed,

[058-016] His entrails too distinctly each express’d,

[058-017] With every shining fibre of his breast.

[058-018] The fauns and sylvans, with the nymphs that rove

[058-019] Among the satyrs in the shady grove,

[058-020] [[olympus|Olympus]], known of old, and every swain

[058-021] That fed, or flock, or herd, upon the plain,

[058-022] Bewail’d the loss, and with their tears, that flow’d,

[058-023] A kindly moisture on the earth bestow’d,

[058-024] That soon, conjoin’d and in a body ranged,

[058-025] Sprung from the ground, to limpid water changed;

[058-026] Which, down through Phrygia’s rocks, a mighty stream,

[058-027] Comes tumbling to the sea, and Marsya is its name.
