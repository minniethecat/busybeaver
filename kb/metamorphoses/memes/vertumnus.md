# Vertumnus

Vertumnus (or Vortumnus) is a distinctly Roman deity, the god of the changing seasons, orchards, and metamorphosis itself. His power — the ability to assume any shape — makes him a fitting figure in Ovid's poem of transformations. Unlike the predatory pursuits common in the *Metamorphoses*, Vertumnus's courtship of the orchard-nymph Pomona is notable for its gentleness: he approaches her through a series of disguises, eventually taking the form of an old woman to deliver an eloquent argument for love and partnership. When words fail to move her, he reveals his true divine form, and Pomona, moved by his beauty and sincerity, accepts him. Their union represents transformation in service of persuasion rather than conquest — a love story built on change rather than against it.

## Role in the Metamorphoses

Vertumnus appears in Book XIV, in a tale that serves as an interlude among the book's Italian myths. Pomona, a nymph devoted entirely to her orchards, has shut herself off from all suitors, walling her garden against the outside world. Vertumnus, burning with love, approaches her not as a god but in a succession of mortal guises — a reaper, a ploughman, a vine-dresser, a soldier, a fisherman — each time admiring her from a distance. Finally, disguised as a crone, he enters her garden and delivers a passionate speech, using the parable of the elm and the vine (the tree supports the vine, and the vine adorns the tree) to argue for marriage. He also warns her of the fate of Anaxarete, who scorned love and was turned to stone. When he casts off the disguise and stands revealed in his divine beauty, Pomona yields — won by words, not force.

### Key Episodes

- **The Disguises**: Vertumnus visits Pomona in multiple forms — reaper, ploughman, soldier, fisherman — admiring her from afar
- **The Old Woman's Speech**: Disguised as an old woman, Vertumnus uses the parable of the elm and vine and the cautionary tale of Anaxarete to persuade Pomona
- **The Revelation**: Vertumnus sheds his disguise and stands revealed as a god; Pomona accepts him

## Transformation

Vertumnus does not undergo transformation — he is the god *of* transformation, the embodiment of change. His shape-shifting is voluntary and strategic, a means of courtship rather than escape or punishment. His union with Pomona represents the harmonious marriage of change (Vertumnus) and cultivation (Pomona).

- **Agent of Change**: Vertumnus himself, through voluntary shape-shifting
- **Purpose**: Courtship — transformation as persuasion, not conquest
- **Source**: Book XIV
