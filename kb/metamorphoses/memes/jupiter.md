# Jupiter

Jupiter (Greek: Zeus), king of the gods and ruler of Olympus, is the most frequently appearing divine character in Ovid's *Metamorphoses*. He is simultaneously the poem's ultimate authority — the god who destroys the impious with thunder and flood — and its most prolific agent of transformation, whose serial amorous pursuits of mortal women generate a cascade of metamorphoses. This duality makes Jupiter the poem's most complex figure: both cosmic judge and cosmic predator.

## Role in the Metamorphoses

Jupiter appears across all fifteen books of the poem, his power and desires threading through the narrative from creation to apotheosis. He presides over the cosmic order established from Chaos (001-001), destroys the impious race of the Iron Age with the Flood (006-069), and receives the final petitions for the deification of Roman heroes (145-001). But his most frequent role is that of the amorous pursuer: the god who sees a mortal woman, desires her, and transforms either her or himself to achieve his ends.

### Key Episodes

- **Destruction of the Impious Race**: Disguised in human form, Jupiter tests Lycaon's hospitality; finding cannibalism, he destroys the race with a Flood, saving only Deucalion and Pyrrha (006-069)
- **Io and the Heifer**: Jupiter transforms Io into a heifer to conceal her from Juno; Argus, the hundred-eyed guard, is slain by Mercury at Jupiter's command (008-001)
- **Calisto and the Bear**: Disguised as Diana, Jupiter seduces the nymph Calisto; Juno transforms her into a bear, and Jupiter catasterizes mother and son as Ursa Major and Minor (013-001)
- **Europa and the Bull**: Jupiter assumes the form of a beautiful white bull to abduct Europa, carrying her across the sea to Crete (041-001)
- **Baucis and Philemon**: Jupiter, with Mercury, tests mortal hospitality in disguise; the pious couple is rewarded with salvation and eternal union (074-001)
- **Apotheosis of Caesar**: Venus petitions Jupiter to deify Julius Caesar; though Fate prevents him from averting the assassination, Jupiter ensures Caesar's soul becomes a star (145-001)

## Transformation

Jupiter is both agent and subject of transformation. As **agent**, he transforms others to punish (Lycaon into a wolf), protect (Io into a heifer), reward (Baucis and Philemon into trees), and immortalize (Caesar's catasterism). As **subject**, he transforms himself — into Diana to approach Calisto (013-001), into a bull to abduct Europa (041-001), into a shower of gold, an eagle, a swan, and a satyr — making his own body as mutable as those he transforms. This self-transformation is the ultimate expression of divine power: the god who can assume any form is the god who rules a cosmos defined by metamorphosis.

- **Form**: Divine (Olympian) → Animal, mortal, or other divine forms
- **Agent**: Self-transformation (voluntary)
- **Cause**: Amorous pursuit, testing mortals, divine judgment
- **Source**: (006-087, 008-001, 013-001, 074-001)
