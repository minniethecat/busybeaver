# Erisichthon

Erisichthon (also spelled Erysichthon) is the central figure of one of the *Metamorphoses*' most harrowing tales of divine punishment. A king of Thessaly, he defied the goddess Ceres by cutting down a sacred oak in her grove — a tree inhabited by a dryad — despite the protests of his own servants and the tree's bleeding wounds. Ceres, outraged, dispatched Famine herself to visit Erisichthon in his sleep and breathe insatiable hunger into his body. What follows is a relentless descent: Erisichthon devours everything he owns, sells his daughter Mestra for food (only for her to escape, transformed by Neptune, to be sold again), and ultimately, in Ovid's chilling conclusion, consumes his own limbs, dying as both predator and prey.

## Role in the Metamorphoses

Erisichthon's story is told in Book VIII. He enters the narrative through a single, fateful act of sacrilege: felling Ceres's sacred oak. When his axe strikes the tree, blood flows from the wound, and a voice from the dryad within warns of Ceres's vengeance. Undeterred, Erisichthon completes the destruction. Ceres's punishment is exquisitely calibrated: she sends an oread to the frozen wastes of Scythia to summon Famine, who breathes upon the sleeping king and fills him with a hunger that no amount of food can satisfy. The hunger consumes his kingdom — his wealth, his palace, his livestock — and then his family. His daughter Mestra, granted shape-shifting powers by Neptune (who had once loved her), transforms herself into various forms to escape the buyers to whom her father sells her for more food. In the end, Erisichthon turns his hunger upon himself, gnawing at his own flesh in an act of auto-cannibalism.

### Key Episodes

- **The Sacred Oak**: Erisichthon fells Ceres's sacred tree, ignoring the dryad's warning and the tree's bleeding wounds
- **The Visit of Famine**: Ceres sends Famine to breathe insatiable hunger into Erisichthon
- **The Sale of Mestra**: Erisichthon sells his daughter for food; she transforms by Neptune's gift to escape, only to be sold again
- **Auto-Cannibalism**: In the final stage of his punishment, Erisichthon devours his own flesh

## Transformation

Erisichthon's punishment is transformation by consumption: his body remains human, but his relationship to consumption is monstrously transformed. Mestra, his daughter, undergoes actual metamorphosis — her shape-shifting ability, granted by Neptune, allows her to escape slavery repeatedly. The tale is a dark counterpoint to other transformation stories: here, transformation is a desperate survival mechanism against a hunger that can never be satisfied.

- **Agent**: Ceres (punishment) and Neptune (Mestra's gift)
- **Cause**: Sacrilege — the destruction of a sacred tree
- **Source**: Book VIII
