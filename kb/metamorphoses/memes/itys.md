# Itys

Son of King [[Tereus]] of [[Thrace]] and [[Procne]], sacrificed by his own mother in one of the most brutal revenge narratives in Ovid's *Metamorphoses*.

## Role in the Metamorphoses

Itys appears in Book VI of the *Metamorphoses* as the innocent victim at the centre of the Tereus–Procne–Philomela tragedy. Too young to understand the violence around him, he runs to his mother with affection, only to be slain by her as the ultimate act of vengeance against his father. His death is the catalyst for the final transformations of the tale: Procne becomes a nightingale, [[Philomela]] a swallow, and Tereus a hoopoe. (060-001)

### Key Episodes

- **Birth and Early Promise**: After Tereus marries Procne, Itys is born to great celebration; the "newborn prince" is the apparent seal of a happy union, though the marriage conceals Tereus's brutal nature. (060-042)
- **The Fateful Embrace**: Procne, learning of her husband's crimes against Philomela, is on the verge of killing him when Itys runs to her, "fondling to his mother." In a moment of terrible resolve, she strikes her own son instead. (060-346)
- **The Banquet of Vengeance**: Procne and Philomela cook Itys's body and serve him to Tereus at a feast. Tereus calls for his son repeatedly, only to be told: "Within yourself your Itys you may find." (060-411, 060-415)
- **The Gory Revelation**: Philomela bursts in and throws Itys's severed head at Tereus, exposing the horror he has unknowingly consumed. This precipitates the chase and the final triple transformation. (060-423)

## Transformation

Itys himself does not undergo a physical metamorphosis, but his violent death is the narrative pivot that triggers the transformations of three others into birds:

- **Procne** → Nightingale: eternally mourning her son with a mournful song
- **Philomela** → Swallow: forever silenced, with a stained throat
- **Tereus** → Hoopoe: a crested bird, perpetually pursued

- **Agent**: [[Procne]] and [[Philomela]] (the murder); the gods (the avian metamorphoses)
- **Cause**: Revenge for Tereus's rape and mutilation of Philomela
