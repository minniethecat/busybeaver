# Perseus

Perseus, son of Jupiter and the mortal Danaë, is one of the few heroic figures in the *Metamorphoses* whose story arcs toward triumph rather than tragedy. His exploits — slaying Medusa, rescuing Andromeda, and the feast that ends in mass petrification — demonstrate that transformation can serve heroic agency rather than divine punishment.

## Role in the Metamorphoses

Perseus appears in Books IV and V, occupying a pivotal position between the Greek mythological material and the poem's broader concerns with power and transformation. His use of Medusa's severed head — which petrifies enemies even in death — makes him an agent of transformation in reverse: where the gods change living bodies into new forms, Perseus changes living bodies into permanent stone.

### Key Episodes

- **Slaying Medusa**: Perseus, aided by Minerva's polished shield and Mercury's winged sandals, beheads the Gorgon Medusa, from whose blood springs Pegasus (034-001)
- **Rescuing Andromeda**: Finding Andromeda chained to a rock as sacrifice to a sea-monster, Perseus slays the beast and claims her as his bride (034-001)
- **The Feast of Phineus**: At the wedding feast, Andromeda's former betrothed Phineus attacks; Perseus uses Medusa's head to transform him and his followers into stone statues (034-001)
- **Origin of Coral**: Perseus lays the Gorgon's head on seaweed, which absorbs its power and hardens into coral — transformation as material rather than bodily change (034-001)

## Transformation

Perseus is a unique transformer: he changes others to stone through the **agency of an object** (Medusa's head) rather than through divine power or magic. This mediated transformation positions him between mortal heroism and divine metamorphic power.

- **Form**: Mortal → Agent of petrification (via Medusa's head)
- **Agent**: Perseus, wielding the severed head of Medusa
- **Cause**: Self-defense; heroic rescue
