## Themes

- **Flight and Fall**: [[Daedalus and Icarus]] is the book's emblematic story: human ingenuity creates wings, but human ambition flies too high. The theme of flight — from Scylla's attempted escape by sea to Daedalus's aerial route to the Calydonian boar's charge — unifies the book's disparate episodes.

- **Piety and Its Opposite**: [[Philemon and Baucis]] are rewarded for their humble hospitality to disguised gods; [[Erysichthon]] is punished for cutting down a sacred tree. These two tales, placed at the book's center, form a diptych of piety and impiety — the moral poles around which the surrounding violence orbits.

- **Hunger**: Erysichthon's insatiable hunger — he sells his daughter repeatedly to buy food, and finally devours himself — is metamorphosis as addiction. Unlike other transformations in the poem, this one is not a single event but an escalating condition, a body consuming itself.

## Narrative Arc

Book VIII moves from Crete (Scylla, Minotaur, Daedalus) to mainland Greece (Calydonian boar, Achelous) to Phrygia (Philemon and Baucis, Erysichthon). It is the book of thresholds — between land and sea, sky and earth, human and divine, satiety and starvation. Daedalus's middle way (*medium tenuere beati*) becomes the book's implicit moral: extremes destroy.

- [[Scylla]] (daughter of Nisus) → Sheared lock → Betrayal: cuts her father's purple lock of hair to give Minos victory (067-077)
- [[Daedalus and Icarus]]: Icarus → Fallen boy: wax wings melt; the sea bears his name (067-077)
- [[Meleager]]'s life → Log consumed by fire: his mother Althaea burns the brand that marks his lifespan (067-077)
- [[Philemon and Baucis]] → Oak and Linden trees: rewarded for piety, they become intertwined trees guarding the temple (067-077)
- [[Erysichthon]] → Self-consuming hunger → Self-devoured: punished by Ceres for felling her sacred oak; he eats himself (067-077)
