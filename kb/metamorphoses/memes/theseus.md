# Theseus

Theseus, the legendary king of Athens, is one of the great heroes of classical myth. In the *Metamorphoses*, Ovid does not narrate his slaying of the Minotaur directly but instead embeds Theseus in a network of stories: he is the intended victim of Medea's poison, the audience for Achelous's tale of transformation, and a figure whose heroic reputation is invoked as a benchmark — "Great Theseus! thee the Marathonian plain / Admires" (065-064). He serves as both an actor in and a witness to the poem's cascade of metamorphic tales, a hero whose own fame is so great that it needs no elaboration.

## Role in the Metamorphoses

Theseus first appears in Book VII, where Medea, having fled Corinth, arrives in Athens and becomes the wife of Aegeus. She immediately "attempts to poison his son Theseus" (065-001), recognizing him as a threat to her own children's inheritance. Aegeus, however, recognizes the sword Theseus carries and dashes the poisoned cup from his hands — a recognition scene that Ovid treats briefly, alluding to the fuller story his audience would know. The court rejoices: "And thus resound their matchless Theseus' praise" (065-063). In Book VIII, Theseus appears as the slayer of the Minotaur: the chapter heading notes "Theseus destroys the Minotaur by the aid of Ariadne" (069-001). In Book IX, Theseus is the audience for Achelous's tale of his wrestling match with Hercules (079-001), positioning him as the archetypal listener-hero who receives and transmits stories of metamorphosis.

### Key Episodes

- **Medea's Poison Plot**: Medea, now queen of Athens, attempts to poison Theseus; Aegeus recognizes his son and saves him (065-001)
- **The Minotaur**: Theseus slays the Minotaur in the Labyrinth with Ariadne's guidance — treated briefly as a known tale (069-001)
- **Achelous's Tale**: In Book IX, Theseus hears Achelous narrate his contest with Hercules (079-001)

## Transformation

Theseus himself does not undergo metamorphosis in the poem, but his actions precipitate transformations in others — most notably, Ariadne's abandonment leads to her discovery by Bacchus and her bridal crown becoming the constellation Corona Borealis.

- **Agent of Change**: Theseus's abandonment of Ariadne → her transformation (bridal crown → constellation)
