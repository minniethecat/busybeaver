# Ino

Ino, daughter of Cadmus and Harmonia, is one of the most complex figures in the Theban cycle of Ovid's *Metamorphoses*. As the sister of Semele, she becomes the nurse of the infant Bacchus after Semele's death — an act of familial piety that enrages Juno, whose hatred of the house of Cadmus (rooted in Jupiter's seduction of Europa) now finds a new target. Juno's vengeance drives Ino's husband Athamas into homicidal madness, and Ino herself, clutching her son Melicerta, flees to a cliff's edge and leaps into the sea. But Venus, pitying her granddaughter's innocent suffering, intercedes with Neptune, and Ino is transformed into the sea-goddess Leucothea ("the white goddess"), while Melicerta becomes the sea-deity Palaemon. Her story in Book IV is both a culmination of Juno's persecution of the Theban royal house and a rare instance in the poem where metamorphosis is explicitly salvific rather than punitive.

## Role in the Metamorphoses

Ino's story in Book IV marks the culmination of Juno's persecution of the house of Cadmus. After Semele's death, Ino takes the infant Bacchus into her care, nursing and rearing the future god — an act that places her directly in Juno's line of sight. Juno, surveying her triumphs over Semele and contemplating the ruin of the Theban line, descends to the underworld to enlist the Fury Tisiphone. Tisiphone drives Athamas, Ino's husband, into a sudden and violent madness: mistaking his wife and children for a lioness and her cubs, he seizes their son Learchus and dashes his brains out against a rock. Ino, driven equally mad, grabs her other son Melicerta and flees, crying out the name of Bacchus — the god she had nursed. Pursued by Athamas and the Furies, she reaches a cliff overlooking the sea and, with no other escape, leaps. Venus, pitying the suffering of her granddaughter (Ino is descended from Harmonia, daughter of Venus and Mars), pleads with Neptune to grant Ino and her son divinity. Neptune consents: he strips away their mortality and clothes them in divine form. Ino becomes Leucothea, goddess of the sea and protector of sailors; Melicerta becomes Palaemon. Her Theban attendants, mourning on the shore, are transformed by Juno into stones and birds (033-001, 034-001).

### Key Episodes

- **The Nurse of Bacchus**: After Semele's death, Ino takes the infant Bacchus and raises him — an act that draws Juno's wrath
- **Juno's Descent to the Underworld**: Juno visits Tisiphone and unleashes the Fury upon the house of Athamas
- **The Madness of Athamas**: Athamas, driven mad, kills their son Learchus; Ino flees with Melicerta (033-001)
- **The Leap into the Sea**: Cornered at a cliff, Ino leaps with Melicerta into the waves — death or transformation the only remaining choices
- **Apotheosis**: Venus intercedes with Neptune; Ino becomes Leucothea, Melicerta becomes Palaemon — a rare salvific metamorphosis (034-001)

## Transformation

- **Form**: Mortal woman (Theban princess) → Sea-goddess (Leucothea)
- **Agent**: Neptune, at the intercession of Venus; mortality is "stripped away" and replaced with divinity
- **Cause**: A rare instance of compassion in the poem — Venus's pity for her innocent descendant, contrasting with Juno's vindictive persecution
- **Source**: (034-001)
