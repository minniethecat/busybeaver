# Phaeton

Phaeton, the mortal son of Apollo (Phoebus) and the ocean-nymph Clymene, is the central figure of one of the *Metamorphoses*' most dramatic cautionary tales about pride and divine power. Doubted by his peers about his divine parentage, Phaeton seeks confirmation from Apollo himself, who swears by the river Styx to grant his son any request. Phaeton's demand — to drive the chariot of the sun across the sky for a single day — proves catastrophic: unable to control the immortal horses, he veers too close to the earth, scorching it, and too high, threatening the heavens, until Jupiter strikes him from the sky with a thunderbolt. The story occupies a central position in Ovid's cosmic narrative, bridging the creation of the world with its near-destruction.

## Role in the Metamorphoses

Phaeton appears primarily in Book II, where his story serves as a mythological explanation for the origins of deserts, the dark skin of Ethiopians, and the poplar tree. His episode begins with a quarrel: Epaphus taunts Phaeton for claiming Apollo as his father (009-098). Seeking proof, Phaeton travels to the palace of the Sun and extracts an oath from Apollo that any request will be granted (010-001). The demand to drive the sun-chariot proves the folly of mortal ambition — Apollo warns him at length, detailing the dangers of the steep ascent, the terrifying celestial beasts (the Crab, the Scorpion), and the uncontrollable horses, but Phaeton insists (010-025). The ride goes disastrously wrong, and Phaeton is blasted from the sky (010-376).

### Key Episodes

- **The Quarrel with Epaphus**: Epaphus questions Phaeton's claim of divine parentage, setting the story in motion (009-098)
- **The Oath and the Request**: Apollo swears by the Styx to grant Phaeton any wish — Phaeton demands to drive the sun-chariot (010-001)
- **Apollo's Warning**: In a lengthy speech, Apollo describes the perils of the sky-journey; Phaeton, undeterred, insists on his right (010-025, 010-120)
- **The Catastrophe**: Phaeton loses control; the earth catches fire; Jupiter strikes him down with a thunderbolt (010-376)
- **The Mourning Sisters**: Phaeton's sisters (the Heliades) weep at his tomb until they are transformed into poplars, their tears becoming amber (011-001)

## Transformation

Phaeton himself does not undergo metamorphosis — he dies from Jupiter's thunderbolt. However, his death precipitates the transformation of his grieving sisters (the Heliades) into poplar trees, their tears hardening into amber. His story is thus an indirect catalyst of metamorphosis.

- **Form**: Phaeton's sisters: mortal women → poplar trees
- **Agent**: Grief / divine will
- **Cause**: Mourning for their brother's death
