## Themes

- **War Without and Within**: Book XII is Ovid's *Iliad* in miniature, but with characteristic indirection: the Trojan War is reported, not narrated; the battle of the Lapiths and Centaurs — a wedding feast turned massacre — becomes the book's set-piece. Ovid treats epic warfare as both spectacle and absurdity.

- **Invulnerability and Its Limits**: [[Cycnus]] cannot be pierced by [[Achilles]]'s weapons and must be strangled; [[Caeneus]] (born [[Caenis]], transformed from woman to man) is invulnerable to weapons and is buried alive under trees. Invulnerability in Book XII is a paradox: it saves no one.

- **The Death of Achilles**: The book closes with the greatest Greek hero's death — not in battle but in a debate over his armor. Ovid reduces Achilles's end to a rhetorical contest between [[Ajax]] and [[Ulysses]], denying epic closure its expected grandeur.

## Narrative Arc

Book XII is the metaliterary book: Ovid self-consciously engages with Homeric epic only to subvert it. The Trojan War is told through a series of framed narratives, each digressing further from the main event. The book's true subject is how stories are made — and unmade.

## Key Transformations

- [[Cycnus]] → Swan: invulnerable warrior killed by Achilles; transformed by his father Neptune (105-113)
- [[Caenis]] → [[Caeneus]] (woman → invulnerable man): granted by Neptune after rape; later buried under trees in battle (105-113)
- [[Caeneus]] → Eagle (unique bird): emerges from the tree-pile as a golden eagle, according to some witnesses (105-113)
- [[Periclymenos]] → Eagle: granted shape-shifting by Neptune; killed by Hercules in eagle form (105-113)
- [[Achilles]] → Dead hero → Ashes: killed by Paris and Apollo; his armor becomes the object of contest (105-113)
