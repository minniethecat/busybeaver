# Reflection

The motif of reflection — the self seen in water, shield, or another's eyes — recurs throughout the *Metamorphoses* as an image of transformation's psychological dimension. Most powerfully realized in the story of Narcissus (Book III), the reflection motif captures the moment when the self becomes other to itself: Narcissus falls in love with his own image, not knowing it is himself, and wastes away into a flower. Reflection thus becomes a form of metamorphosis enacted not on the body but on identity.

## Definition

In the *Metamorphoses*, reflection functions as a **doubling of the self** that disrupts the boundary between subject and object. Narcissus, bending to drink, sees "another" in the pool — "He sees, and weeps at his approaching fate" (026-022) — and becomes simultaneously lover and beloved, pursuer and pursued. This doubling is itself a kind of metamorphosis: the self is split, and the split self is unsustainable. Narcissus's transformation into a flower is the resolution of this unsustainable doubling — the two become one, but at the cost of the human form. Ovid extends the motif beyond Narcissus: Perseus uses Medusa's reflection in his shield to avoid her petrifying gaze, and Pygmalion's statue reflects back his own desire until the reflection becomes real.

## Significance

Reflection in the *Metamorphoses* is transformation's **psychological double**: where bodily metamorphosis changes form, reflection changes identity without changing form. Narcissus's body remains human as he wastes away; what transforms is his self-understanding — or rather, his failure to achieve it. The motif connects Ovid's poem to broader philosophical questions about knowledge, desire, and identity that Pythagoras will address in Book XV: if all things change, what is the self that perceives the change? The reflection offers no answer, only an image — beautiful, unreachable, and ultimately fatal.
