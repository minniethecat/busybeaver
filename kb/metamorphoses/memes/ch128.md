[114-001] Achilles, having fallen a sacrifice to the hostility of [[apollo|Apollo]] and the shafts of Paris, Ajax and Ulysses advance their claims to the armour of the deceased hero.

[114-002] The sire of Cycnus, monarch of the main,

[114-003] Meantime laments his son in battle slain,

[114-004] And vows the victor’s death; nor vows in vain.

[114-005] For nine long years the smother’d pain he bore:

[114-006] (Achilles was not ripe for fate before:)

[114-007] Then when he saw the promised hour was near,

[114-008] He thus bespoke the god that guides the year:

[114-009] “Immortal offspring of my brother [[jupiter|Jove]],

[114-010] My brightest nephew, and whom best I love,

[114-011] Whose hands were join’d with mine, to raise the wall

[114-012] Of tottering [[troy|Troy]], now nodding to her fall,

[114-013] Dost thou not mourn our power employ’d in vain,

[114-014] And the defenders of our city slain?

[114-015] To pass the rest, could noble Hector lie

[114-016] Unpitied, dragg’d around his native Troy?

[114-017] And yet the murderer lives: himself by far

[114-018] A greater plague than all the wasteful war:

[114-019] He lives, the proud Pelides lives, to boast

[114-020] Our town destroy’d, our common labour lost.

[114-021] Oh, could I meet him! but I wish too late:

[114-022] To prove my trident is not in his fate!

[114-023] But let him try (for that’s allow’d) thy dart,

[114-024] And pierce his only penetrable part.”

[114-025] Apollo bows to the superior throne,

[114-026] And to his uncle’s anger adds his own;

[114-027] Then, in a cloud involved, he takes his flight,

[114-028] Where Greeks and Trojans mix’d in mortal fight,

[114-029] And found out Paris, lurking where he stood,

[114-030] And stain’d his arrows with plebeian blood:

[114-031] Phoebus to him alone the god confess’d,

[114-032] Then to the recreant knight he thus address’d:

[114-033] “Dost thou not blush, to spend thy shafts in vain

[114-034] On a degenerate and ignoble train?

[114-035] If fame or better vengeance be thy care,

[114-036] There aim; and with one arrow end the war.”

[114-037] He said; and show’d from far the blazing shield

[114-038] And sword, which, but Achilles, none could wield,

[114-039] And how he moved a god, and mow’d the standing field.

[114-040] The deity himself directs aright

[114-041] The envenom’d shaft, and wings the fatal flight.

[114-042] Thus fell the foremost of the Grecian name,

[114-043] And he, the base adulterer, boasts the fame;

[114-044] A spectacle to glad the Trojan train,

[114-045] And please old Priam, after Hector slain.

[114-046] If by a female hand he had foreseen

[114-047] He was to die, his wish had rather been

[114-048] The lance and double axe of the fair warrior queen.

[114-049] And now the terror of the Trojan field,

[114-050] The Grecian honour, ornament, and shield,

[114-051] High on a pile the unconquer’d chief is placed;

[114-052] The god that arm’d him first, consumed at last.

[114-053] Of all the mighty man, the small remains

[114-054] A little urn, and scarcely fill’d, contains.

[114-055] Yet great in Homer, still Achilles lives,

[114-056] And equal to himself, himself survives.

[114-057] His buckler owns its former lord, and brings

[114-058] New cause of strife between contending kings;

[114-059] Who worthiest after him his sword to wield,

[114-060] Or wear his armour, or sustain his shield.

[114-061] Ev’n Diomede sat mute, with downcast eyes,

[114-062] Conscious of wonted worth to win the prize;

[114-063] Nor Menelaus presumed these arms to claim;

[114-064] Nor he, the king of men, a greater name:

[114-065] Two rivals only rose: Laertes’ son,

[114-066] And the vast bulk of Ajax Telamon.

[114-067] The king, who cherish’d each with equal love,

[114-068] And from himself all envy would remove,

[114-069] Left both to be determined by the laws,

[114-070] And to the Grecian chiefs transferr’d the cause.
