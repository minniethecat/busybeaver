[110-001] The marriage of Pirithous, king of the Lapithae, with Hippodamia, is rendered memorable by a furious contest with their centaur guests, who endeavour to seize the bride, but are defeated.

[110-002] “Now brave Pirithous, bold Ixion’s son,

[110-003] The love of fair Hippodame had won.

[110-004] The cloud begotten race, half men, half beast,

[110-005] Invited, came to grace the nuptial feast:

[110-006] In a cool cave’s recess the treat was made,

[110-007] Whose entrance trees with spreading boughs o’ershade.

[110-008] They sat; and, summon’d by the bridegroom, came,

[110-009] To mix with those the Lapithaean name:

[110-010] Nor wanted I. The roofs with joy resound;

[110-011] And, ‘*Hymen, Io Hymen*,’ rung around.

[110-012] Raised altars shone with holy fires: the bride,

[110-013] Lovely herself, (and lovely, by her side,

[110-014] A bevy of bright nymphs, with sober grace,)

[110-015] Came glittering like a star, and took her place:

[110-016] Her heavenly form beheld, all wish’d her joy;

[110-017] And little wanted; but in vain their wishes all employ:

[110-018] “For one, most brutal of the brutal brood,

[110-019] Or whether wine or beauty fired his blood,

[110-020] Or both at once, beheld with joyful eyes

[110-021] The bride, at once resolved to make his prize.

[110-022] Down went the board; and, fastening on her hair,

[110-023] He seized with sudden force the frighted fair.

[110-024] ’Twas Eurytus began: his bestial kind

[110-025] His crime pursued; and each, as pleased his mind,

[110-026] Or her whom chance presented, took: the feast

[110-027] An image of a taken town express’d.

[110-028] “The cave resounds with female shrieks; we rise,

[110-029] Mad with revenge, to make a swift reprise:

[110-030] And Theseus first: ‘What frenzy has possess’d,

[110-031] Oh Eurytus,’ he cried, ‘thy brutal breast,

[110-032] To wrong Pirithous, and not him alone,

[110-033] But while I live, two friends conjoin’d in one?’

[110-034] “To justify his threat, he thrusts aside

[110-035] The crowd of centaurs, and redeems the bride.

[110-036] The monster naught replied; for words were vain,

[110-037] And deeds could only deeds unjust maintain;

[110-038] But answers with his hand, and forward press’d,

[110-039] With blows redoubled, on his face and breast.

[110-040] An ample goblet stood, of antique mould,

[110-041] And rough with figures of the rising gold;

[110-042] The hero snatch’d it up, and toss’d in air

[110-043] Full at the front of the foul ravisher:

[110-044] He falls, and falling, vomits forth a flood

[110-045] Of wine, and foam, and brains, and mingled blood.

[110-046] Half roaring, and half neighing, through the hall,

[110-047] ‘Arms! arms!’ the double-form’d with fury call;

[110-048] To wreak their brother’s death: a medley flight

[110-049] Of bowls and jars at first supply the fight;

[110-050] Once instruments of feasts, but now of fate:

[110-051] Wine animates their rage, and arms their hate.

[110-052] “Bold Amycus from the robb’d vestry brings

[110-053] The chalices of heaven, and holy things

[110-054] Of precious weight: a sconce that hung on high,

[110-055] With tapers fill’d, to light the sacristy,

[110-056] Torn from the cord, with his unhallow’d hand,

[110-057] He threw amid the Lapithaean band.

[110-058] On Celadon the ruin fell, and left

[110-059] His face of feature, and of form bereft:

[110-060] So, when some brawny sacrificer knocks,

[110-061] Before an altar led, an offer’d ox,

[110-062] His eyeballs, rooted out, are thrown to ground;

[110-063] His nose, dismantled, in his mouth is found;

[110-064] His jaws, cheeks, front, one undistinguished wound.

[110-065] “This Belates, the avenger, could not brook,

[110-066] But, by the foot, a maple board he took,

[110-067] And hurl’d at Amycus: his chin it bent

[110-068] Against his chest, and down the centaur sent;

[110-069] Whom, sputtering bloody teeth, the second blow

[110-070] Of his drawn sword despatch’d to shades below.

[110-071] “Grineus was near, and cast a furious look

[110-072] On the side altar, censed with sacred smoke,

[110-073] And bright with flaming fires: ‘The gods,’ he cried,

[110-074] ‘Have with their holy trade our hands supplied:

[110-075] Why use we not their gifts?’ Then from the floor

[110-076] An altar stone he heaved, with all the load it bore:

[110-077] Altar, and altar’s freight, together flew,

[110-078] Where thickest throng’d the Lapithaean crew,

[110-079] And at once Broteas and Oryus slew.

[110-080] Oryus’ mother, Mycale, was known

[110-081] Down from her sphere to draw the labouring moon.

[110-082] “Exadius cried: ‘Unpunish’d shall not go,

[110-083] This fact, if arms are found against the foe.

[110-084] He look’d about, where on a pine were spread

[110-085] The votive horns of a stag’s branching head:

[110-086] At Grineus these he throws; so just they fly,

[110-087] That the sharp antlers stuck in either eye:

[110-088] Breathless and blind he fell, with blood besmear’d;

[110-089] His eyeballs, beaten out, hung dangling on his beard.

[110-090] Fierce Rhaetus from the hearth a burning brand

[110-091] Selects, and whirling waves, till from his hand

[110-092] The fire took flame, then dash’d it on the right,

[110-093] On fair Charaxus’ temples, near the sight:

[110-094] The whistling pest came on, and pierced the bone,

[110-095] And caught the yellow hair, that shrivell’d while it shone:

[110-096] Caught, like dry stubble fired, or like seerwood;

[110-097] Yet from the wound ensued no purple flood,

[110-098] But look’d a bubbling mass of frying blood.

[110-099] His blazing locks sent forth a crackling sound,

[110-100] And hiss’d, like red-hot iron within the smithy drown’d.

[110-101] The wounded warrior shook his flaming hair;

[110-102] Then (what a team of horse could hardly rear)

[110-103] He heaves the threshold stone, but could not throw;

[110-104] The weight itself forbad the threaten’d blow;

[110-105] Which, dropping from his lifted arms, came down

[110-106] Full on Cometes’ head, and crush’d his crown.

[110-107] Nor Rhaetus then restrained his joy, but said:

[110-108] ‘So by their fellows may our foes be sped:’

[110-109] Then with redoubled strokes he plies his head:

[110-110] The burning lever not deludes his pains;

[110-111] But drives the batter’d scull within the brains.

[110-112] “Thus flush’d, the conqueror, with force renew’d,

[110-113] Evagrus, Dryas, Corythus, pursued.

[110-114] First Corythus, with downy cheeks, he slew,

[110-115] Whose fall when fierce Evagrus had in view,

[110-116] He cried: ‘What palm is from a beardless prey?’

[110-117] Rhaetus prevents what more he had to say,

[110-118] And drove within his mouth the fiery death,

[110-119] Which enter’d hissing in, and choked his breath.

[110-120] At Dryas next he flew: but weary chance

[110-121] No longer would the same success advance;

[110-122] For while he whirl’d in fiery circles round

[110-123] The brand, a sharpen’d stake strong Dryas found,

[110-124] And in the shoulder’s joint inflicts the wound.

[110-125] The weapon stuck, which, roaring out with pain,

[110-126] He drew; nor longer durst the fight maintain,

[110-127] But turn’d his back, for fear, and fled amain.

[110-128] With him fled Orneus, with like dread possess’d;

[110-129] Thaumas, and Medon, wounded in the breast;

[110-130] And Mermeros, in the late race renown’d,

[110-131] Now limping ran, and tardy, with his wound.

[110-132] Pholus and Melaneous from fight withdrew,

[110-133] And Abas maim’d, who boars encountering slew:

[110-134] And Augur Astylos, whose art in vain

[110-135] From fight dissuaded the four-footed train,

[110-136] Now beat the hoof with Nessus on the plain,

[110-137] But to his fellow cried: ‘Be safely slow;

[110-138] Thy death deferr’d is due to great Alcides’ bow.’

[110-139] “Meantime strong Dryas urged his chance so well,

[110-140] That Lycidas, Areos, Imbreus, fell,

[110-141] All one by one, and fighting face to face:

[110-142] Crenaeus fled, to fall with more disgrace;

[110-143] For, fearful, while he look’d behind, he bore,

[110-144] Between his nose and front, the blow before.

[110-145] Amid the noise and tumult of the fray,

[110-146] Snoring, and drunk with wine, Aphidas lay;

[110-147] Ev’n then the bowl within his hand he kept,

[110-148] And on a bear’s rough hide securely slept:

[110-149] Him Phorbas with his flying dart transfix’d:

[110-150] ‘Take thy next draught with Stygian waters mix’d,

[110-151] And sleep thy fill,’ the insulting victor cried:

[110-152] Surprised with death unfelt, the centaur died:

[110-153] The ruddy vomit, as he breathed his soul,

[110-154] Repass’d his throat, and fill’d his empty bowl.

[110-155] “I saw Petraeus’ arms employ’d around

[110-156] A well-grown oak, to root it from the ground:

[110-157] This way and that he wrench’d the fibrous bands;

[110-158] The trunk was like a sapling in his hands,

[110-159] And still obey’d the bent: while thus he stood,

[110-160] Pirithous’ dart drove on, and nail’d him to the wood:

[110-161] Lycus and Chromis fell, by him oppress’d:

[110-162] Helops and Dictys added to the rest

[110-163] A nobler palm: Helops through either ear,

[110-164] Transfix’d, received the penetrating spear:

[110-165] This Dictys saw, and, seized with sudden fright,

[110-166] Leap’d headlong from the hill of steepy height,

[110-167] And crush’d an ash beneath, that could not bear his weight:

[110-168] The shatter’d tree receives his fall, and strikes

[110-169] Within his full-blown paunch the sharpen’d spikes.

[110-170] Strong Aphareus had heaved a mighty stone,

[110-171] The fragment of a rock, and would have thrown;

[110-172] But Theseus, with a club of harden’d oak,

[110-173] The cubit-bone of the bold centaur broke,

[110-174] And left him maim’d; nor seconded the stroke:

[110-175] Then leap’d on tall Bianor’s back; (who bore

[110-176] No mortal burden but his own before;)

[110-177] Press’d with his knees his sides: the double man,

[110-178] His speed with spurs increased, unwilling ran.

[110-179] One hand the hero fasten’d on his locks;

[110-180] His other plied him with repeated strokes;

[110-181] The club rang round his ears and batter’d brows:

[110-182] He falls, and, lashing up his heels, his rider throws.

[110-183] “The same Herculean arms Nedymnus wound,

[110-184] And lay by him Lycotas on the ground;

[110-185] And Hippasus, whose beard his breast invades;

[110-186] And Ripheus, hunter of the woodland shades;

[110-187] And Tereus, used with mountain bears to strive,

[110-188] And from their dens to draw the indignant beasts alive.

[110-189] “Demoleon could not bear this hateful sight,

[110-190] Or the long fortune of the Athenian knight,

[110-191] But pull’d with all his force, to disengage

[110-192] From earth a pine, the product of an age:

[110-193] The root stuck fast: the broken trunk he sent

[110-194] At Theseus: Theseus frustrates his intent,

[110-195] And leaps aside; by [[minerva|Pallas]] warn’d the blow

[110-196] To shun: (for so he said, and we believed it so.)

[110-197] Yet not in vain the enormous weight was cast,

[110-198] Which Crantor’s body sunder’d at the waist;

[110-199] Thy father’s ’squire, Achilles, and his care,

[110-200] Whom, conquer’d in the Pelopeian war,

[110-201] Their king, his present ruin to prevent,

[110-202] A pledge of peace implored, to Peleus sent.

[110-203] “Thy sire, with grieving eyes, beheld his fate,

[110-204] And cried, ‘Not long, loved Crantor, shalt thou wait

[110-205] Thy vow’d revenge.’ At once he said, and threw

[110-206] His ashen spear, which quiver’d as it flew;

[110-207] With all his force and all his soul applied,

[110-208] The sharp point enter’d in the centaur’s side:

[110-209] Both hands to wrench it out the monster join’d,

[110-210] And wrench’d it output left the steel behind;

[110-211] Stuck in his lungs it stood: enraged he rears

[110-212] His hoofs, and down to ground thy father bears.

[110-213] Thus trampled under foot, his shield defends

[110-214] His head; his other hand the lance portends:

[110-215] Ev’n while he lay extended on the dust,

[110-216] He sped the centaur with one single thrust:

[110-217] Two more his lance before transfix’d from far;

[110-218] And two his sword had slain in closer war.

[110-219] To these was added Dorylas, who spread

[110-220] A bull’s two goring horns round his head:

[110-221] With these he push’d: in blood already died,

[110-222] Him fearless I approach’d, and thus defied:

[110-223] ‘Now, monster, now by proof it shall appear

[110-224] Whether thy horns are sharper, or my spear.’

[110-225] At this, I threw: for want of other ward,

[110-226] He lifted up his hand, his front to guard:

[110-227] His hand it pass’d, and fix’d it to his brow:

[110-228] Loud shouts of ours attend the lucky blow.

[110-229] Him Peleus finish’d, with a second wound,

[110-230] Which through the navel pierced: he reel’d around,

[110-231] And dragg’d his dangling bowels on the ground;

[110-232] Trod what he dragg’d, and what he trod, he crush’d,

[110-233] And to his mother earth with empty belly rush’d.”
