# Hospitality

Hospitality (*xenia* or *hospitium*) is a sacred bond in Ovid's mythological world: the obligation to welcome, feed, and shelter strangers without question, for the stranger may be a god in disguise. The theme appears throughout the *Metamorphoses* as a test of mortal character, producing some of the poem's most poignant transformations — those who pass the test are rewarded with salvation, while those who fail are destroyed.

## Definition

Hospitality in the *Metamorphoses* is more than social convention — it is a **religious obligation** enforced directly by the gods. The institution of *theoxenia* (divine visitation in disguise) means that every stranger at the door might be Jupiter, Mercury, or another deity testing mortal worth. The proper response is immediate, unconditional welcome: offering one's best food, wine, and shelter without inquiring into the guest's identity. The consequences of this test are absolute — the hospitable are saved from catastrophe, while the inhospitable are annihilated. Ovid uses hospitality as a narrative device that reveals character through action and provides the moral justification for otherwise arbitrary divine intervention.

## Significance

Hospitality is the **moral test** that distinguishes the saved from the damned in the *Metamorphoses*. Unlike the abstract concepts of Fate or Prophecy, hospitality is enacted in the most concrete domestic details: the offered cup, the shared meal, the humble bed. This concreteness gives the Baucis and Philemon episode (074-001) its extraordinary emotional power: the entire cosmic machinery of divine reward and punishment turns on whether a poor old couple will share their modest provisions with strangers. The theme also connects Ovid's poem to the broader epic tradition — the *Odyssey*'s suitors are punished for violating hospitality, while Odysseus himself is both guest and host across his wanderings.
