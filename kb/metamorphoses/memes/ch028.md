[023-001] A handsome youth, named [[narcissus|Narcissus]], beholding his face reflected in a fountain, becomes enamoured with it, and wastes away with grief—After his death the Naiads raise a funeral pile to burn his body, when they find nothing but a beautiful flower, which still bears his name.

[023-002] Thus did the nymphs in vain caress the boy,

[023-003] He still was lovely, but he still was coy,

[023-004] When one fair virgin of the slighted train

[023-005] Thus pray’d the gods, provoked by his disdain:

[023-006] “O! may he love like me, and love like me in vain!”

[023-007] Rhamnusia pitied the neglected fair,

[023-008] And with just vengeance answer’d to her prayer.

[023-009] There stands a fountain in a darksome wood,

[023-010] Nor stain’d with falling leaves, nor rising mud,

[023-011] Untroubled by the breath of winds it rests,

[023-012] Unsullied by the touch of men or beasts;

[023-013] High bowers of shady trees above it grow,

[023-014] And rising grass and cheerful greens below.

[023-015] Pleased with the form and coolness of the place,

[023-016] And overheated by the morning chase,

[023-017] Narcissus on the grassy verdure lies;

[023-018] But while within the crystal fount he tries

[023-019] To quench his heat, he feels new heat arise:

[023-020] For, as his own bright image he survey’d,

[023-021] He fell in love with the fantastic shade,

[023-022] And o’er the fair resemblance hung unmoved;

[023-023] Nor knew, fond youth! it was himself he loved.

[023-024] The well-turn’d neck and shoulders he descries,

[023-025] The spacious forehead, and the sparkling eyes,

[023-026] The hands that [[bacchus|Bacchus]] might not scorn to show,

[023-027] And hair that round [[apollo|Apollo]]’s head might flow,

[023-028] With all the purple youthfulness of face,

[023-029] That gently blushes in the watery glass.

[023-030] By his own flames consumed the lover lies,

[023-031] And gives himself the wound by which he dies.

[023-032] To the cold water oft he joins his lips,

[023-033] Oft catching at the beauteous shade he dips

[023-034] His arms, as often from himself he slips.

[023-035] Nor knows he who it is his arms pursue

[023-036] With eager clasps, but loves he knows not who.

[023-037] What could, fond youth, this helpless passion move?

[023-038] What kindled in thee this unpitied love?

[023-039] Thy own warm blush within the water glows,

[023-040] With thee the colour’d shadow comes and goes,

[023-041] Its empty being on thyself relies;

[023-042] Step thou aside and the frail charmer dies.

[023-043] Still o’er the fountain’s watery gleam he stood,

[023-044] Mindless of sleep, and negligent of food,

[023-045] Still view’d his face, and languish’d as he view’d.

[023-046] At length he raised his head, and thus began

[023-047] To vent his griefs, and tell the woods his pain:

[023-048] “You trees,” says he, “and thou surrounding grove,

[023-049] Who oft have been the kindly scenes of love,

[023-050] Tell me, if e’er within your shades did lie

[023-051] A youth so tortured, so perplex’d as I?

[023-052] I, who before me see the charming fair,

[023-053] While there he stands, and yet he stands not there:

[023-054] In such a maze of love my thoughts are lost;

[023-055] And yet no bulwark’d town nor distant coast

[023-056] Preserves the beauteous youth from being seen,

[023-057] No mountains rise nor oceans flow between;

[023-058] A shallow water hinders my embrace,

[023-059] And yet the lovely mimic wears a face

[023-060] That kindly smiles, and when I bend to join

[023-061] My lips to his, he fondly bends to mine.

[023-062] Hear, gentle youth, and pity my complaint;

[023-063] Come from thy well, thou fair inhabitant.

[023-064] My charms an easy conquest have obtain’d

[023-065] O’er other hearts, by thee alone disdain’d.

[023-066] But why should I despair? I’m sure he burns

[023-067] With equal flames, and languishes by turns.

[023-068] Whene’er I stoop, he offers at a kiss,

[023-069] And when my arms I stretch, he stretches his;

[023-070] His eyes with pleasure on my face he keeps,

[023-071] He smiles my smiles, and when I weep he weeps:

[023-072] Whene’er I speak his moving lips appear

[023-073] To utter something, which I cannot hear.

[023-074] “Ah, wretched me! I now begin too late

[023-075] To find out all the long perplex’d deceit;

[023-076] It is myself I love, myself I see,

[023-077] The gay delusion is a part of me;

[023-078] I kindle up the fires by which I burn,

[023-079] And my own beauties from the well return.

[023-080] Whom should I court? how utter my complaint?

[023-081] Enjoyment but produces my restraint,

[023-082] And too much plenty makes me die for want.

[023-083] How gladly would I from myself remove!

[023-084] And at a distance set the thing I love;

[023-085] My breast is warm’d with such unusual fire,

[023-086] I wish him absent whom I most desire;

[023-087] And now I faint with grief, my fate draws nigh,

[023-088] In all the pride of blooming youth I die.

[023-089] Death will the sorrows of my heart relieve.

[023-090] O! might the visionary youth survive,

[023-091] I should with joy my latest breath resign!

[023-092] But, oh! I see his fate involved in mine.”

[023-093] This said, the weeping youth again return’d

[023-094] To the clear fountain, where again he burn’d.

[023-095] His tears defaced the surface of the well,

[023-096] With circle after circle as they fell;

[023-097] And now the lovely face but half appears,

[023-098] O’errun with wrinkles and deform’d with tears.

[023-099] “Ah! whither,” cries Narcissus, “dost thou fly?

[023-100] Let me still feed the flame by which I die;

[023-101] Let me still see, though I’m no further bless’d.”

[023-102] Then rends his garment off and beats his breast;

[023-103] His naked bosom redden’d with the blow,

[023-104] In such a blush as purple clusters show,

[023-105] Ere yet the sun’s autumnal heats refine

[023-106] Their sprightly juice, and mellow it to wine;

[023-107] The glowing beauties of his breast he spies,

[023-108] And with a new redoubled passion dies.

[023-109] As wax dissolves, as ice begins to run

[023-110] And trickle into drops before the sun,

[023-111] So melts the youth, and languishes away,

[023-112] His beauty withers, and his limbs decay,

[023-113] And none of those attractive charms remain,

[023-114] To which the slighted [[echo|Echo]] sued in vain.

[023-115] She saw him in his present misery,

[023-116] Whom, spite of all her wrongs, she grieved to see.

[023-117] She answer’d sadly to the lover’s moan,

[023-118] Sigh’d back his sighs, and groan’d to every groan.

[023-119] “Ah youth! beloved in vain,” Narcissus cries;

[023-120] “Ah youth! beloved in vain,” the nymph replies.

[023-121] “Farewell,” says he; the parting sound scarce fell

[023-122] From his faint lips, but she replied, “Farewell.”

[023-123] Then on the unwholesome earth he gasping lies,

[023-124] Till death shuts up those self-admiring eyes.

[023-125] To the cold shades his fitting ghost retires,

[023-126] And in the Stygian waves itself admires.

[023-127] For him the Naiads and the Dryads mourn,

[023-128] Whom the sad Echo answers in her turn;

[023-129] And now the sister-nymphs prepare his urn:

[023-130] When, looking for his corpse, they only found

[023-131] A rising stalk with yellow blossoms crown’d.
