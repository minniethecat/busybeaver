[012-001] Cycnus, the son of Sthenelus, is deeply affected at the death of his friend and relation, Phaeton; and, in the midst of his lamentations, is metamorphosed into a swan.

[012-002] Cycnus beheld the nymphs transform’d, allied

[012-003] To their dead brother on the mortal side,

[012-004] In friendship and affection nearer bound,

[012-005] He left the cities and the realms he own’d,

[012-006] Through pathless fields and lonely shores to range,

[012-007] And woods made thicker by the sisters’ change.

[012-008] While here within the dismal gloom alone,

[012-009] The melancholy monarch made his moan,

[012-010] His voice was lessen’d as he tried to speak,

[012-011] And issued through a long extended neck;

[012-012] His hair transforms to down, his fingers meet

[012-013] In skinny films and shape his oary feet;

[012-014] From both his sides the wings and feathers break,

[012-015] And from his mouth proceeds a blunted beak:

[012-016] All Cycnus now into a swan was turn’d,

[012-017] Who, still remembering how his kinsman burn’d,

[012-018] To solitary pools and lakes retires,

[012-019] And loves the waters as opposed to fires.

[012-020] Meanwhile [[apollo|Apollo]], in the gloomy shade

[012-021] (The native lustre of his brows decay’d),

[012-022] Indulging sorrow, sickens at the sight

[012-023] Of his own sunshine, and abhors the light.

[012-024] The hidden griefs that in his bosom rise,

[012-025] Sadden his looks and overcast his eyes,

[012-026] As when some dusky orb obstructs his ray,

[012-027] And sullies, in a dim eclipse, the day.

[012-028] Now secretly with inward griefs he pined,

[012-029] Now warm resentments to his grief he join’d,

[012-030] And now renounced his office to mankind.

[012-031] “E’er since the birth of time,” said he, “I’ve borne

[012-032] A long ungrateful toil without return;

[012-033] Let now some other manage, if he dare,

[012-034] The fiery steeds, and mount the burning car;

[012-035] Or, if none else, let [[jupiter|Jove]] his fortune try,

[012-036] And learn to lay his murd’ring thunder by;

[012-037] Then will he own, perhaps, but own too late,

[012-038] My son deserved not so severe a fate.”

[012-039] The gods stand round him, as he mourns, and pray

[012-040] He would resume the conduct of the day,

[012-041] Nor let the world be lost in endless night;

[012-042] Jove too himself, descending from his height,

[012-043] Excuses what had happen’d, and entreats,

[012-044] Majestically mixing prayers and threats.

[012-045] Prevail’d upon at length, again he took

[012-046] The harness’d steeds, that still with horror shook,

[012-047] And plies them with the lash, and whips them on,

[012-048] And, as he whips, upbraids them with his son.
