Rome (*Roma*) is the city upon which the entire teleology of Ovid's *Metamorphoses* converges. From the primordial [[Chaos]] of Book I to the apotheosis of [[Julius Caesar]] and the promised deification of [[Augustus]] in Book XV, the poem traces a mythological arc that culminates in Rome as the divinely ordained center of the world. More than a geographical setting, Rome functions as the endpoint of metamorphic history: where chaos once reigned, eternal city now stands.

## Appearances in the Metamorphoses

Rome appears at critical junctures throughout the poem, but its most concentrated treatment comes in the final books (XIV–XV), where Ovid transitions from Greek mythology to Roman legend and history. The city is not merely a backdrop but an active participant in transformation narratives — its very foundation is a metamorphosis from wilderness to civilization.

### Key Passages

- **The Foundation of Rome** (XIV.772–851): [[Romulus]], son of [[Mars]], founds the city after the death of his brother [[Remus]]. His subsequent deification as Quirinus establishes a pattern that will repeat with [[Julius Caesar]] and eventually [[Augustus]] — Roman rulers become gods.
- **The Apotheosis of Julius Caesar** (XV.745–870): [[Venus]], ancestor of the [[Julian clan]], intercedes to prevent Caesar's assassination, but the [[Fates]] decree otherwise. After his death, she transforms his soul into a comet (the *sidus Iulium*), and he is received among the gods.
- **The Praise of Augustan Rome** (XV.871–879): The poem's final lines look forward to the deification of [[Augustus]], whom Ovid praises as the peace-bringer — *"Jupiter who rules in heaven controls the celestial realms, and Augustus shall rule the tripartite earth"*.
- **Pythagoras in Rome** (XV.60–478): The Greek philosopher [[Pythagoras]] delivers his great discourse on metamorphosis and the transmigration of souls — fittingly, in or near Rome, the city where all transformations find their historical resolution.

## Mythological Significance

Rome represents the end-point of the metamorphic process that Ovid traces from the poem's opening lines. The city's mythological significance operates on three levels:

1. **Political teleology**: All prior transformations lead toward Rome's founding and imperial destiny
2. **Divine genealogy**: Rome's rulers descend from [[Venus]] through [[Aeneas]], making Rome literally a city of divine lineage
3. **Eternal permanence**: In a poem about endless change, Rome (and its deified rulers) represents the one thing that endures

Rome also marks a shift in the poem's geography — the movement from Greek landscapes ([[Thebes]], [[Thrace]], [[Crete]]) to Italian and Roman settings tracks the westward transmission of mythological authority from Greece to Rome.

## Transformation Connection

Rome is not transformed itself, but it is the **destination of transformations**: [[Aeneas]], fleeing the ruins of [[Troy]], carries the household gods to [[Latium]]; [[Romulus]] ascends from Rome's walls to godhood; [[Julius Caesar]]'s soul becomes a star over Rome; and the poem itself is transformed from Greek myth to Roman history within the city's orbit. In Ovid's cosmic architecture, Rome is where metamorphosis achieves permanence — the eternal city beyond change.
