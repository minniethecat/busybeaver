[140-001] The Pythagorean system of philosophy, which is here minutely described, is adopted and taught by Numa, who is chosen by the Romans to be the successor of Romulus.

[140-002] A king is sought to guide the growing state,

[140-003] One able to support the public weight,

[140-004] And fill the throne where Romulus had sate.

[140-005] Renown, which oft bespeaks the public voice,

[140-006] Had recommended Numa to their choice:

[140-007] A peaceful pious prince; who not content

[140-008] To know the Sabine rites, his study bent

[140-009] To cultivate his mind; to learn the laws

[140-010] Of nature, and explore their hidden cause.

[140-011] Urged by this care, his country he forsook,

[140-012] And to Crotona thence his journey took.

[140-013] Arrived, he first inquired the founder’s name

[140-014] Of this new colony, and whence he came.

[140-015] Then thus a senior of the place replies:

[140-016] (Well read, and curious of antiquities:)

[140-017] “ ’Tis said, Alcides hither took his way

[140-018] From Spain, and drove along his conquer’d prey;

[140-019] Then, leaving in the fields his grazing cows,

[140-020] He sought himself some hospitable house;

[140-021] Good Croton entertain’d his godlike guest;

[140-022] While he repair’d his weary limbs with rest.

[140-023] The hero, thence departing, bless’d the place;

[140-024] ‘And here,’ he said, ‘in lime’s revolving race,

[140-025] A rising town shall take his name from thee.’

[140-026] Revolving time fulfill’d the prophecy.

[140-027] For Myscelos, the justest man on earth,

[140-028] Alemon’s son, at Argos had his birth.

[140-029] Him Hercules, arm’d with his club of oak,

[140-030] O’ershadow’d in a dream, and thus bespoke:

[140-031] ‘Go, leave thy native soil, and make abode

[140-032] Where Aesaris rolls down his rapid flood.’

[140-033] He said; and sleep forsook him, and the god.

[140-034] Trembling he waked, and rose with anxious heart;

[140-035] His country laws forbade him to depart.

[140-036] What should he do? ’twas death to go away,

[140-037] And the god menaced if he dared to stay.

[140-038] All day he doubted, and when night came on,

[140-039] Sleep, and the same forewarning dream, begun:

[140-040] Once more the god stood threatening o’er his head:

[140-041] With added curses if he disobey’d.

[140-042] Twice warn’d, he studied flight; but would convey

[140-043] At once his person and his wealth away:

[140-044] Thus while he linger’d his design was heard;

[140-045] A speedy process form’d, and death declared.

[140-046] Witness there needed none of his offence;

[140-047] Against himself the wretch was evidence:

[140-048] Condemn’d, and destitute of human aid,

[140-049] To him for whom he suffer’d thus he pray’d:

[140-050] “ ‘Oh power, who hast deserved in heaven a throne,

[140-051] Not given, but by thy labours made thy own,

[140-052] Pity thy suppliant, and protect his cause,

[140-053] Whom thou hast made obnoxious to the laws.’

[140-054] “A custom was of old, and still remains,

[140-055] Which life or death by suffrages ordains:

[140-056] White stones and black within an urn are cast;

[140-057] The first absolve, but fate is in the last.

[140-058] The judges to the common urn bequeath

[140-059] Their votes, and drop the sable signs of death;

[140-060] The box receives all black, but, pour’d from thence,

[140-061] The stones came candid forth the hue of innocence.

[140-062] Thus Alemonides his safety won,

[140-063] Preserved from death by Alcumena’s son:

[140-064] Then to his kinsman god his vows he pays,

[140-065] And cuts with prosperous gales the Ionian seas:

[140-066] He leaves Tarentum favour’d by the wind,

[140-067] And Thurine bays, and Temises, behind;

[140-068] Soft Sybaris, and all the capes that stand

[140-069] Along the shore, he makes in sight of land;

[140-070] Still doubling, and still coasting, till he found

[140-071] The mouth of Aesaris, and promised ground;

[140-072] Then saw, where, on the margin of the flood,

[140-073] The tomb that held the bones of Croton stood:

[140-074] Here, by the gods’ command, he built, and wall’d

[140-075] The place predicted; and Crotona call’d.

[140-076] Thus fame, from time to time, delivers down

[140-077] The sure tradition of the Italian town.

[140-078] “Here dwelt the man divine, whom [[samos|Samos]] bore,

[140-079] But now self-banish’d from his native shore,

[140-080] Because he hated tyrants, nor could bear

[140-081] The chains, which none but servile souls will wear.

[140-082] He, though from heaven remote, to heaven could move,

[140-083] With strength of mind, and tread the abyss above;

[140-084] And penetrate, with his interior light,

[140-085] Those upper depths which nature hid from sight:

[140-086] And what he had observed and learn’d from thence,

[140-087] Loved in familiar language to dispense.

[140-088] “The crowd with silent admiration stand,

[140-089] And heard him as they heard their god’s command;

[140-090] While he discoursed of Heaven’s mysterious laws,

[140-091] The world’s original, and nature’s cause;

[140-092] And what was God; and why the fleecy snows

[140-093] In silence fell, and rattling winds arose;

[140-094] What shook the steadfast earth, and whence begun

[140-095] The dance of planets round the radiant sun;

[140-096] If thunder was the voice of angry [[jupiter|Jove]],

[140-097] Or clouds with nitre pregnant, burst above;

[140-098] Of these, and things beyond the common reach,

[140-099] He spoke, and charm’d his audience with his speech.

[140-100] “He first the taste of flesh from tables drove,

[140-101] And argued well, if arguments could move:

[140-102] ‘Oh mortals, from your fellows’ blood abstain,

[140-103] Nor taint your bodies with a food profane.

[140-104] While corn and pulse by nature are bestow’d,

[140-105] And planted orchards bend their willing load;

[140-106] While labour’d gardens wholesome herbs produce,

[140-107] And teeming vines afford their generous juice;

[140-108] Nor tardier fruits of cruder kind are lost,

[140-109] But tamed with fire, or mellow’d by the frost;

[140-110] While kine to pails distended udders bring,

[140-111] And bees their honey redolent of spring;

[140-112] While earth not only can your needs supply,

[140-113] But lavish of her store, provides for luxury;

[140-114] A guiltless feast administers with ease,

[140-115] And without blood is prodigal to please.

[140-116] Wild beasts their maws with their slain brethren fill;

[140-117] And yet not all, for some refuse to kill;

[140-118] Sheep, goats, and oxen, and the nobler steed,

[140-119] On browse, and corn, and flowery meadows feed.

[140-120] Bears, tigers, wolves, the lion’s angry brood,

[140-121] Whom Heaven indued with principles of blood,

[140-122] He wisely sunder’d from the rest, to yell

[140-123] In forests, and in lonely caves to dwell;

[140-124] Where stronger beasts oppress the weak by might,

[140-125] And all in prey and purple feasts delight.

[140-126] “ ‘Oh impious use! to nature’s laws opposed,

[140-127] Where bowels are in other bowels closed:

[140-128] Where fatten’d by their fellows’ fat, they thrive;

[140-129] Maintain’d by murder, and by death they live.

[140-130] ’Tis then for naught, that mother earth provides

[140-131] The stores of all she shows, and all she hides,

[140-132] If men with fleshy morsels must be fed,

[140-133] And chew with bloody teeth the breathing bread

[140-134] What else is this, but to devour our guests,

[140-135] And barb’rously renew cyclopean feasts!

[140-136] We, by destroying life, our life sustain;

[140-137] And gorge the ungodly maw with meats obscene.

[140-138] “ ‘Not so the golden age, who fed on fruit,

[140-139] Nor durst with bloody meals their mouths pollute.

[140-140] Then birds in airy space might safely move,

[140-141] And timorous hares on heaths securely rove:

[140-142] Nor needed fish the guileful hooks to fear,

[140-143] For all was peaceful; and that peace sincere.

[140-144] Whoever was the wretch (and cursed be he)

[140-145] That envied first our food’s simplicity,

[140-146] The essay of bloody feasts on brutes began,

[140-147] And after forged the sword to murder man.

[140-148] Had he the sharpen’d steel alone employ’d

[140-149] On beasts of prey, that other beasts destroy’d;

[140-150] Or man invaded with their fangs and paws,

[140-151] This had been justified by nature’s laws

[140-152] And self-defence: but who did feasts begin

[140-153] Of flesh, he stretch’d necessity to sin.

[140-154] To kill man-killers man has lawful power,

[140-155] But not the extended license to devour.

[140-156] “ ‘Ill habits gather by unseen degrees,

[140-157] As brooks make rivers, rivers run to seas.

[140-158] The sow, with her broad snout, for rooting up

[140-159] The entrusted seed, was judged to spoil the crop,

[140-160] And intercept the sweating farmer’s hope:

[140-161] The covetous churl, of unforgiving kind,

[140-162] The offender to the bloody priest resign’d:

[140-163] Her hunger was no plea: for that she died.

[140-164] The goat came next in order to be tried:

[140-165] The goat had cropp’d the tendrils of the vine:

[140-166] In vengeance laity and clergy join,

[140-167] Where one had lost his profit, one his wine.

[140-168] Here was at least some shadow of offence;

[140-169] The sheep was sacrificed on no pretence,

[140-170] But meek and unresisting innocence.

[140-171] A patient, useful creature, born to bear

[140-172] The warm and woolly fleece, that clothed her murderer;

[140-173] And daily to give down the milk she bred,

[140-174] A tribute for the grass on which she fed.

[140-175] Living, both food and raiment she supplies,

[140-176] And is of least advantage when she dies.

[140-177] “ ‘How did the toiling ox his death deserve,

[140-178] A downright simple drudge, and horn to serve?

[140-179] Oh tyrant! with what justice canst thou hope

[140-180] The promise of the year, a plenteous crop;

[140-181] When thou destroy’st thy labouring steer, who till’d

[140-182] And plough’d with pains, thy else ungrateful field?

[140-183] From his yet reeking neck, to draw the yoke,

[140-184] That neck with which the surly clods he broke;

[140-185] And to the hatchet yield thy husbandman,

[140-186] Who finish’d autumn, and the spring began.

[140-187] “ ‘Nor this alone! but Heaven itself to bribe,

[140-188] We to the gods our impious acts ascribe;

[140-189] First recompense with death their creatures’ toil;

[140-190] Then call the bless’d above to share the spoil:

[140-191] The fairest victim must the powers appease,

[140-192] (So fatal ’tis sometimes too much to please!)

[140-193] A purple fillet his broad brow adorns,

[140-194] With flowery garlands crown’d, and gilded horns:

[140-195] He hears the murderous prayer the priest prefers,

[140-196] But understands not ’tis his doom he hears:

[140-197] Beholds the meal between his temples cast;

[140-198] (The fruit and product of his labours past;)

[140-199] And in the water views perhaps the knife,

[140-200] Uplifted to deprive him of his life;

[140-201] Then broken up alive, his entrails sees

[140-202] Torn out, for priests to inspect the gods’ decrees.

[140-203] “ ‘From whence, oh mortal men, this gust of blood

[140-204] Have you derived, and interdicted food?

[140-205] Be taught by me this dire delight to shun,

[140-206] Warn’d by my precepts, by my practice won:

[140-207] And when you eat the well-deserving beast,

[140-208] Think on the labourer of your field you feast!

[140-209] “ ‘Now since the god inspires me to proceed,

[140-210] Be that, whate’er inspiring power, obey’d:

[140-211] For I will sing of mighty mysteries,

[140-212] Of truths conceal’d before, from human eyes,

[140-213] Dark oracles unveil, and open all the skies.

[140-214] Pleased as I am to walk along the sphere

[140-215] Of shining stars, and travel with the year,

[140-216] To leave the heavy earth, and scale the height

[140-217] Of Atlas, who supports the heavenly weight;

[140-218] To look from upper light, and thence survey

[140-219] Mistaken mortals wandering from the way,

[140-220] And wanting wisdom, fearful for the state

[140-221] Of future things, and trembling at their fate!

[140-222] “ ‘Those I would teach; and by right reason bring

[140-223] To think of death as but an idle thing.

[140-224] Why thus affrighted at an empty name,

[140-225] A dream of darkness, and fictitious flame?

[140-226] Vain themes of wit, which but in poems pass,

[140-227] And fables of a world that never was?

[140-228] What feels the body when the soul expires,

[140-229] By time corrupted, or consumed by fires?

[140-230] Nor dies the spirit, but new life repeats

[140-231] In other forms, and only changes seats.

[140-232] “ ‘Ev’n I, who these mysterious truths declare,

[140-233] Was once Euphorbus in the Trojan war;

[140-234] My name and lineage I remember well,

[140-235] And how in fight by Sparta’s king I fell.

[140-236] In Argive Juno’s fame I late beheld

[140-237] My buckler hung on high, and own’d my former shield.

[140-238] “ ‘Then death, so call’d, is but old matter dress’d

[140-239] In some new figure, and a varied vest:

[140-240] Thus all things are but alter’d, nothing dies;

[140-241] And here and there the unbodied spirit flies,

[140-242] By time, or force, or sickness dispossess’d,

[140-243] And lodges, where it lights, in man or beast;

[140-244] Or hunts without, till ready limbs it find,

[140-245] And actuates those according to their kind;

[140-246] From tenement to tenement is toss’d,

[140-247] The soul is still the same, the figure only lost:

[140-248] And, as the soften’d wax new seals receives,

[140-249] This face assumes, and that impression leaves;

[140-250] Now call’d by one, now by another name;

[140-251] The form is only changed, the wax is still the same.

[140-252] So death, so call’d, can but the form deface;

[140-253] The immortal soul flies out in empty space,

[140-254] To seek her fortune in some other place.

[140-255] “ ‘Then let not piety be put to flight,

[140-256] To please the taste of glutton appetite;

[140-257] But suffer inmate souls secure to dwell,

[140-258] Lest from their seats your parents you expel;

[140-259] With rabid hunger feed upon your kind,

[140-260] Or from a beast dislodge a brother’s mind.

[140-261] “ ‘And since, like Typhis parting from the shore,

[140-262] In ample seas I sail, and depths untried before,

[140-263] This let me farther add, that nature knows

[140-264] No steadfast station, but or ebbs or flows:

[140-265] Ever in motion; she destroys her old,

[140-266] And casts new figures in another mould.

[140-267] Ev’n times are in perpetual flux, and run,

[140-268] Like rivers from their fountain, rolling on;

[140-269] For time, no more than streams, is at a stay;

[140-270] The flying hour is ever on her way:

[140-271] And as the fountain still supplies her store,

[140-272] The wave behind impels the wave before:

[140-273] Thus in successive course the minutes run,

[140-274] And urge their predecessor minutes on,

[140-275] Still moving, ever new: for former things

[140-276] Are set aside, like abdicated kings;

[140-277] And every moment alters what is done,

[140-278] And innovates some act, till then unknown.

[140-279] “ ‘Darkness we see emerges into light,

[140-280] And shining suns descend to sable night;

[140-281] Ev’n heaven itself receives another dye,

[140-282] When wearied animals in slumbers lie

[140-283] Of midnight ease: another, when the gray

[140-284] Of morn preludes the splendour of the day.

[140-285] The disk of [[apollo|Phoebus]], when he climbs on high,

[140-286] Appears at first but as a bloodshot eye;

[140-287] And when his chariot downward drives to bed,

[140-288] His ball is with the same suffusion red;

[140-289] But mounted high in his meridian race,

[140-290] All bright he shines, and with a better face:

[140-291] For there, pure particles of ether flow,

[140-292] Far from the infection of the world below.

[140-293] “ ‘Nor equal light the unequal moon adorns,

[140-294] Or in her waxing, or her waning horns;

[140-295] For every day she wanes, her face is less;

[140-296] But gathering into globe, she fattens at increase.

[140-297] “ ‘Perceiv’st thou not the process of the year,

[140-298] How the four seasons in four forms appear,

[140-299] Resembling human life in every shape they wear?

[140-300] Spring, first, like infancy, shoots out her head,

[140-301] With milky juice requiring to be fed:

[140-302] Helpless, though fresh, and wanting to be led.

[140-303] The green stem grows in stature, and in size,

[140-304] But only feeds with hope the farmer’s eyes;

[140-305] Then laughs the childish year with flow’rets crown’d,

[140-306] And lavishly perfumes the fields around.

[140-307] But no substantial nourishment receives;

[140-308] Infirm the stalks, unsolid are the leaves.

[140-309] “ ‘Proceeding onward whence the year began,

[140-310] The summer grows adult, and ripens into man.

[140-311] This season, as in men, is most replete

[140-312] With kindly moisture, and prolific heat.

[140-313] “ ‘Autumn succeeds, a sober tepid age,

[140-314] Not froze with fear, nor boiling into rage;

[140-315] More than mature, and tending to decay,

[140-316] When our brown locks repine to mix with odious gray.

[140-317] “ ‘Last, winter creeps along with tardy pace;

[140-318] Sour is his front, and furrow’d is his face:

[140-319] His scalp, if not dishonour’d quite of hair,

[140-320] The ragged fleece is thin; and thin is worse than bare.

[140-321] “ ‘Ev’n our own bodies daily change receive,

[140-322] Some part of what was theirs before, they leave;

[140-323] Nor are to-day what yesterday they were;

[140-324] Nor the whole same to-morrow will appear.

[140-325] “ ‘Time was when we were sow’d, and just began

[140-326] To show the promise of a future man:

[140-327] Then nature’s hand (fermented as it was)

[140-328] Moulded to shape the soft coagulated mass;

[140-329] And when the little man was fully form’d,

[140-330] The breathless embryo with a spirit warm’d;

[140-331] But when the mother’s throes begin to come,

[140-332] The creature, pent within the narrow room,

[140-333] Breaks his blind prison, pushing to repair

[140-334] His stifled breath, and draw the living air;

[140-335] Cast on the margin of the world he lies,

[140-336] A helpless babe, but by instinct he cries.

[140-337] He next essays to walk, but downward press’d

[140-338] On four feet imitates his brother beast:

[140-339] By slow degrees he gathers from the ground

[140-340] His legs, and to the rolling chair is bound;

[140-341] Then walks alone; a horseman now become,

[140-342] He rides a stick, and travels round the room.

[140-343] In time he vaunts among his youthful peers;

[140-344] Strong boned, and strung with nerves, in pride of years,

[140-345] He runs with mettle his first merry stage,

[140-346] Maintains the next, abated of his rage,

[140-347] But manages his strength, and spares his age.

[140-348] Heavy the third, and stiff, he sinks apace,

[140-349] And though ’tis downhill all, he creeps along the race.

[140-350] Now sapless on the verge of death he stands,

[140-351] Contemplating his former feet and hands;

[140-352] And, Milo like, his slacken’d sinews sees,

[140-353] And wither’d arms, once fit to cope with Hercules,

[140-354] Unable now to shake, much less to tear, the trees.

[140-355] “ ‘So Helen wept, when her too faithful glass

[140-356] Reflected on her eyes the ruins of her face:

[140-357] “ ‘Thy teeth, devouring time, thine, envious age,

[140-358] On things below still exercise your rage:

[140-359] With venom’d grinders you corrupt your meat,

[140-360] And then, at lingering meals, the morsels eat.

[140-361] “ ‘Nor those, which elements we call, abide,

[140-362] Nor to this figure, nor to that are tied;

[140-363] For this eternal world is said, of old,

[140-364] But four prolific principles to hold,

[140-365] Four different bodies; two to heaven ascend,

[140-366] And other two down to the centre tend:

[140-367] Fire first with wings expanded mounts on high,

[140-368] Pure, void of weight, and dwells in upper sky;

[140-369] Then air, because unclogg’d in empty space,

[140-370] Flies after fire, and claims the second place:

[140-371] But weighty water, as her nature guides,

[140-372] Lies on the lap of earth; and mother earth subsides.

[140-373] “ ‘All things are mix’d of these, which all contain,

[140-374] And into these are all resolved again:

[140-375] Earth rarifies to dew; expanded more,

[140-376] The subtle dew in air begins to soar;

[140-377] Spreads, as she flies, and weary of her name

[140-378] Extenuates still, and changes into flame;

[140-379] Thus having by degrees perfection won,

[140-380] Restless they soon untwist the web they spun,

[140-381] And fire begins to lose her radiant hue,

[140-382] Mix’d with gross air, and air descends to dew;

[140-383] And dew condensing, does her form forego,

[140-384] And sinks a heavy lump of earth below.

[140-385] “ ‘Thus are their figures never at a stand,

[140-386] But changed by nature’s innovating hand;

[140-387] All things are alter’d, nothing is destroy’d,

[140-388] The shifted scene for some new show employ’d.

[140-389] “ ‘Then, to be born is to begin to be

[140-390] Some other thing we were not formerly:

[140-391] And what we call to die, is not to appear,

[140-392] Or be the thing that formerly we were.

[140-393] Those very elements, which we partake

[140-394] Alive, when dead some other bodies make:

[140-395] Translated grow, have sense, or can discourse;

[140-396] But death on deathless substance have no force.

[140-397] “ ‘That forms are changed, I grant; that nothing can

[140-398] Continue in the figure it began:

[140-399] The golden age to silver was debased,

[140-400] To copper that; our metal came at last.

[140-401] “ ‘The face of places, and their forms decay;

[140-402] And that is solid earth that once was sea:

[140-403] Seas in their turn retreating from the shore,

[140-404] Make solid land, what ocean was before

[140-405] And far from strands are shells of fishes found,

[140-406] And rusty anchors fix’d on mountain ground:

[140-407] And what were fields before, now wash’d and worn

[140-408] By falling floods from high, to valleys turn,

[140-409] And crumbling still descend to level lands;

[140-410] And lakes, and trembling bogs, are barren sands:

[140-411] And the parch’d desert floats in streams unknown,

[140-412] Wondering to drink of waters not her own.

[140-413] “ ‘Here nature living fountains opes; and there

[140-414] Seals up the wombs, where living fountains were:

[140-415] Or earthquakes stop their ancient course, and bring

[140-416] Diverted streams to feed a distant spring.

[140-417] So Lycus, swallow’d up, is seen no more,

[140-418] But far from thence knocks out another door.

[140-419] Thus Erasmus dives; and blind in earth

[140-420] Runs on, and gropes his way to second birth,

[140-421] Starts up in Argos’ meads, and shakes his locks

[140-422] Around the fields, and fattens all the flocks.

[140-423] So Mysus by another way is led,

[140-424] And, grown a river, now disdains his head;

[140-425] Forgets his humble birth, his name forsakes,

[140-426] And the proud title of Caicus takes.

[140-427] Large Amenane, impure with yellow sands,

[140-428] Runs rapid often, and as often stands;

[140-429] And here he threats the drunken fields to drown;

[140-430] And there his dugs deny to give their liquor down.

[140-431] “ ‘Anigros once did wholesome draughts afford,

[140-432] But now his deadly waters are abhorr’d:

[140-433] Since, hurt by Hercules, as fame resounds,

[140-434] The centaurs in his current wash’d their wounds.

[140-435] The streams of Hypanis are sweet no more,

[140-436] But brackish lose the taste they had before.

[140-437] Antissa, Pharos, Tyre, in seas were pent,

[140-438] Once isles, but now increase the continent;

[140-439] While the Leucadian coast, main land before,

[140-440] By rushing seas is sever’d from the shore.

[140-441] So Zancle to the Italian earth was tied,

[140-442] And men once walk’d where ships at anchor ride;

[140-443] Till Neptune overlook’d the narrow way,

[140-444] And in disdain pour’d in the conquering sea.
