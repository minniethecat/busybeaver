# Caunus

A mortal prince of Miletus, Caunus is the twin brother of [[Byblis]] and the grandson of Apollo. When Byblis develops an incestuous passion for him and declares her love in a letter, Caunus recoils with revulsion and abandons his homeland. His flight from unnatural desire leads him to found a new city in Caria, while Byblis, consumed by grief, is transformed into an ever-weeping fountain (089-001).

## Role in the Metamorphoses

Caunus appears in Book IX as the object of his sister Byblis's forbidden desire. His role in the narrative is largely passive — he is the unwilling beloved whose horrified rejection triggers Byblis's descent into madness and her eventual metamorphosis. The episode serves as one of the poem's most psychologically nuanced treatments of illicit passion, contrasting Byblis's agonized self-awareness with Caunus's swift, unambiguous flight (089-001).

### Key Episodes

- **Byblis's Declaration**: Byblis, after a long internal struggle, writes Caunus a letter confessing her incestuous love. She argues that the gods themselves practise sibling marriage and that their shared blood should bring them closer, not divide them. Caunus, reading the letter, is filled with disgust and casts it aside in fury (089-036).
- **Flight from Miletus**: Rejecting Byblis utterly, Caunus flees his homeland to escape her advances. His departure is swift and final; he offers no comfort, no explanation — only refusal. The text emphasizes his revulsion: "Caunus, with disdain, her impious love returns" (089-188).
- **Foundation of Caunus**: Caunus settles in Caria, where he founds the city that bears his name. His flight marks the dissolution of the family — Byblis, abandoned and raving, wanders through Caria and Lycia until the gods transform her into a fountain (089-180).

## Transformation

Caunus himself undergoes no physical metamorphosis. His narrative function is as the catalyst for [[Byblis]]'s transformation: his rejection drives her to madness and her eventual change into a fountain. Caunus's own "transformation" is geographical — from prince of Miletus to founder of a new city — a human-scale parallel to the physical metamorphoses that surround him.
