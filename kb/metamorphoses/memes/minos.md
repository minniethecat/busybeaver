# Minos

King Minos of Crete, son of [[Jupiter]] and [[Europa]], is a formidable mortal ruler whose military campaign against Athens and conquest of Megara drive the political narrative of Book VIII. After his death, he assumes the role of judge in the underworld — a rare instance in the *Metamorphoses* of a mortal ascending to an eternal office, his authority extending beyond the grave. ## Role in the Metamorphoses

Minos enters the poem in Book VIII as a threatening military power. After the death of his son Androgeus in Athens, Minos prepares to invade — his fleet and reputation for justice precede him. Ovid uses Minos's campaign to explore themes of loyalty, betrayal, and the cost of desire: the maiden [[Scylla (daughter of Nisus)|Scylla]], daughter of his enemy King Nisus of Megara, falls in love with Minos at first sight and betrays her city for him — cutting the purple lock of hair on which the city's safety depends. Minos, disgusted by her treachery, rejects her and sails away. (068-001)

### Key Episodes

- **The War with Athens**: Following the death of his son Androgeus, Minos threatens Athens with invasion, seeking to avenge his loss by force of arms (065-001)
- **The Siege of Megara**: Minos besieges the city of King Nisus, whose safety depends on a single purple lock of hair; Scylla, Nisus's daughter, sees Minos from the walls and falls desperately in love (068-001)
- **Scylla's Betrayal**: Scylla steals the fateful lock from her sleeping father and delivers it to Minos — expecting love as her reward. Minos receives the city but recoils from the daughter who betrayed her own father: "the gods should banish such a monster from the earth" (068-010)
- **Scylla Transformed**: As Minos sails away, Scylla swims after his ship, clinging to its stern; her father Nisus, transformed into an osprey, attacks her; she is transformed into the bird Ciris, forever pursued (068-233)

## Transformation

Minos himself does not undergo physical transformation in the poem. Rather, he serves as the **catalyst** for Scylla's metamorphosis: his rejection transforms a princess into a desperate swimmer, then into the bird Ciris. His role as judge in the underworld — a mortal elevated to divine office — represents a transformation of status rather than form.

- **Form**: Mortal king → Judge of the dead (status transformation after death)
- **Agent**: His own reputation for justice
- **Cause**: His earthly authority and divine parentage
- **Source**: (065-001)
