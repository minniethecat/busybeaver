[139-001] A seat in the celestial mansions is assigned to Hersilia, the wile of Romulus, who assumes the name of Ora.

[139-002] Ere long the goddess of the nuptial bed,

[139-003] With pity moved, sends Iris in her stead

[139-004] To sad Hersilia. Thus the meteor maid:

[139-005] “Chaste relict! in bright truth to heaven allied,

[139-006] The Sabines’ glory, and the sex’s pride;

[139-007] Honour’d on earth, and worthy of the love

[139-008] Of such a spouse, as now resides above,

[139-009] Some respite to thy killing griefs afford;

[139-010] And if thou wouldst once more behold thy lord,

[139-011] Retire to yon steep mount, with groves o’erspread,

[139-012] Which with an awful gloom his temples shade.”

[139-013] With fear the modest matron lifts her eyes,

[139-014] And to the bright ambassadress replies:

[139-015] “Oh goddess, yet to mortal eyes unknown,

[139-016] But sure thy various charms confess thee one:

[139-017] Oh quick to Romulus thy votress bear,

[139-018] With looks of love he’ll smile away my care:

[139-019] In whate’er orb he shines, my heaven is there.”

[139-020] Then hastes with Iris to the holy grove;

[139-021] And up the Mount Quirinal as they move

[139-022] A lambent flame glides downward through the air,

[139-023] And brightens with a blaze Hersilia’s hair.

[139-024] Together on the bounding ray they rise,

[139-025] And shoot a gleam of light along the skies.

[139-026] With opening arms Quirinus met his bride,

[139-027] Now Ora named, and press’d her to his side.
