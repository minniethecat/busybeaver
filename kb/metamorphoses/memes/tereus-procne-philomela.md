The story of [[Tereus]], [[Procne]], and [[Philomela]] (Book VI.412–674) is among the darkest and most structurally complex transformation narratives in Ovid's *Metamorphoses*. It unfolds as a chain of escalating violations: Tereus's lust-driven rape and mutilation of his sister-in-law Philomela, Procne's cannibalistic revenge upon their son [[Itys]], and the final triple metamorphosis into birds. The episode stands as Ovid's most sustained meditation on the loss of voice, the failure of communication, and the transformation of grief into violence.

## Narrative

[[Tereus]], king of [[Thrace]], marries the Athenian princess [[Procne]] at the request of her father, King [[Pandion]]. After five years in Thrace, Procne longs to see her sister [[Philomela]]. Tereus sails to [[Athens]] to fetch her — but the moment he sees Philomela's beauty, he is consumed with predatory lust.

On the voyage back to Thrace, Tereus drags Philomela to a remote hut deep in the woods and rapes her. When Philomela threatens to tell the world of his crime, he severs her tongue with a sword, imprisoning her in the forest hut. He returns to Procne with a false story of Philomela's death.

Philomela, mute and captive, weaves her story into a tapestry and has it delivered to Procne. Upon understanding what has happened, Procne — driven by a grief so intense it becomes fury — rescues her sister during the Bacchanalian rites. Together they plot revenge: Procne kills her own son [[Itys]], cooks his flesh, and serves him to Tereus at a banquet. When Tereus discovers the truth and pursues the sisters with a sword, all three are transformed into birds: Procne becomes a swallow, Philomela a nightingale, and Tereus a hoopoe — his crest resembling a warrior's helmet.

## Themes

- **The Loss of Voice**: Philomela's severed tongue and her woven tapestry form the poem's most powerful meditation on silenced speech and alternative modes of communication. The tapestry is a *text* that speaks when the body cannot.
- **Revenge and Its Escalation**: Each act of violation — rape, mutilation, infanticide, cannibalism — exceeds the last, creating a spiral from which only metamorphosis offers escape.
- **The Corruption of Sacred Bonds**: Tereus violates *hospitium* (the sacred guest-host relationship) by raping his wife's sister; Procne violates motherhood by killing her son. The story systematically destroys every familial and social bond.
- **Art as Witness**: Philomela's woven tapestry prefigures Ovid's own poem — art as testimony that outlasts the silencing of individual voices.
