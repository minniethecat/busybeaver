# Iole

Iole, the daughter of Eurytus of Oechalia, is a mortal woman whose beauty proves fatal to [[hercules|Hercules]]. After Hercules captures Oechalia, he takes Iole as a captive and sends her ahead to his household. News of his love for "the fair Iole" reaches his wife [[dejanira|Dejanira]], who, believing she is losing her husband's affection, sends him the shirt dipped in the poisoned blood of the centaur Nessus — believing it to be a love charm. The shirt burns Hercules alive, and in the aftermath, Iole becomes a witness to the tragedy she inadvertently caused. She is consoled by [[alcmena|Alcmena]], Hercules's mother, and is eventually given in marriage to Hercules's son Hyllus (081-011, 084-008, 086-002).

## Role in the Metamorphoses

Iole appears briefly but crucially in the narrative of Hercules's death (Book IX). She is not an active agent but rather the catalyst — her mere presence and beauty set off the chain of events that leads to the hero's demise. Ovid uses Iole to dramatize the destructive power of jealousy and miscommunication. After Hercules's death, Iole serves as a witness to the transformation of Iolaus (who is restored to youth) and acts as a narrator of these events.

### Key Episodes

- **Hercules's Love**: News spreads "that Hercules the fair Iole loved," reaching Dejanira and triggering her fear and jealousy (081-011)
- **Captive in Trachis**: Iole is brought to Hercules's household "by Hercules' commands" as a captive and future bride for Hyllus (084-008)
- **Comforted by Alcmena**: After Hercules's death, Alcmena goes "to Iole, to vent maternal woes" — the two women who loved Hercules share their grief (084-005)
- **Witness to Renewal**: "While Iole the fatal change declares," she witnesses the miraculous rejuvenation of Iolaus by [[hebe|Hebe]], at Hercules's posthumous request (086-002)

## Transformation

Iole herself is not transformed, but she is present at a crucial metamorphosis — the restoration of Iolaus to youth — and her story frames transformation as a narrative device: from the fatal "transformation" of Nessus's poisoned blood into a weapon, to the miraculous reversal of aging.
