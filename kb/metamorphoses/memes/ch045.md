[039-001] [[medusa|Medusa]], one of the three Gorgons, and celebrated for her personal beauty, is violated by Neptune in the temple of [[minerva|Minerva]] who changes the flowing ringlets, which had attracted the admiration of the god, into hissing snakes, which are finally transferred to the aegis of Minerva.

[039-002] The hero with his just request complies,

[039-003] Shows how a vale beneath cold Atlas lies,

[039-004] Where, with aspiring mountains fenced around,

[039-005] He the two daughters of old Phorcus found.

[039-006] [[fate|Fate]] had one common eye to both assign’d,

[039-007] Each saw by turns, and each by turns was blind.

[039-008] But while one strove to lend her sister sight,

[039-009] He stretch’d his hand, and stole their mutual light,

[039-010] And left both eyeless, both involved in night.

[039-011] Through devious wilds, and trackless woods, he pass’d,

[039-012] And at the Gorgon seats arrived at last:

[039-013] But as he journey’d, pensive, he survey’d

[039-014] What wasteful havoc dire Medusa made.

[039-015] Here, stood still breathing statues, men before;

[039-016] There, rampant lions seem’d in stone to roar.

[039-017] Nor did he, yet affrighted, quit the field;

[039-018] But in the mirror of his polish’d shield,

[039-019] Reflected, saw Medusa slumbers take,

[039-020] And not one serpent, by good chance, awake.

[039-021] Then backward an unerring blow he sped,

[039-022] And from her body lopp’d at once her head.

[039-023] The gore prolific proved; with sudden force

[039-024] Sprung Pegasus, and wing’d his airy course.

[039-025] The heaven-born warrior faithfully went on,

[039-026] And told the numerous dangers which he run;

[039-027] What subject seas, what lands he had in view,

[039-028] And nigh what stars the advent’rous hero flew.

[039-029] At last he silent sat; the list’ning throng

[039-030] Sigh’d at the pause of his delightful tongue.

[039-031] Some begg’d to know why this alone should wear,

[039-032] Of all the sisters, such destructive hair.

[039-033] Great [[perseus|Perseus]] then: “With me you shall prevail,

[039-034] Worth the relation, to relate a tale.

[039-035] Medusa once had charms; to gain her love

[039-036] A rival crowd of envious lovers strove.

[039-037] They who have seen her own, they ne’er did trace

[039-038] More moving features in a sweeter face:

[039-039] Yet, above all, her length of hair, they own,

[039-040] In golden ringlets waved, and graceful shone.

[039-041] Her Neptune saw, and with such beauties fired,

[039-042] Resolved to compass what his soul desired.

[039-043] The bashful goddess turn’d her eyes away,

[039-044] Nor durst such bold impurity survey;

[039-045] But on the lovely virgin vengeance takes,

[039-046] Her shining hair is changed to hissing snakes.

[039-047] These, in her aegis, Pallas joys to bear:

[039-048] The hissing snakes her foes more sure insnare,

[039-049] Than they did lovers once, when shining hair.”
