[115-001] Ajax and Ulysses lay claim to the armour of Achilles, which is assigned to the latter by the Grecian chiefs.

[115-002] The chiefs were set; the soldiers crown’d the field;

[115-003] To these the master of the sevenfold shield

[115-004] Upstarted fierce, and kindled with disdain.

[115-005] Eager to speak, unable to contain

[115-006] His boiling rage, he roll’d his eyes around

[115-007] The shore and Grecian galleys haul’d aground;

[115-008] Then, stretching out his hands, “Oh [[jupiter|Jove]],” he cried,

[115-009] “Must then our cause before the fleet be tried?

[115-010] And dares Ulysses for the prize contend,

[115-011] In sight of what he durst not once defend?

[115-012] But basely fled that memorable day,

[115-013] When I from Hector’s hands redeem’d the flaming prey;

[115-014] So much ’tis safer at the noisy bar

[115-015] With words to flourish, than engage in war.

[115-016] By different methods we maintain our right;

[115-017] Nor am I made to talk, nor he to fight:

[115-018] In bloody fields I labour to be great;

[115-019] His arms are a smooth tongue and soft deceit:

[115-020] Nor need I speak my deeds, for those you see;

[115-021] The sun and day are witnesses for me:

[115-022] Let him who fights unseen relate his own,

[115-023] And vouch the silent stars and conscious moon.

[115-024] Great is the prize demanded, I confess;

[115-025] But such an abject rival makes it less:

[115-026] That gift, those honours, he but hoped to gain,

[115-027] Can leave no room for Ajax to be vain:

[115-028] Losing, he wins, because his name will be

[115-029] Ennobled by defeat, who durst contend with me.

[115-030] Were my known valour question’d, yet my blood

[115-031] Without that plea, would make my title good:

[115-032] My sire was Telamon, whose arms, employ’d

[115-033] With Hercules, these Trojan walls destroy’d;

[115-034] And who before, with Jason sent from Greece,

[115-035] In the first ship brought home the golden fleece.

[115-036] Great Telamon from Aeacus derives

[115-037] His birth: (the inquisitor of guilty lives

[115-038] In shades below; where Sisyphus, whose son

[115-039] This thief is thought, rolls up the restless heavy stone.)

[115-040] Just Aeacus, the king of gods above

[115-041] Begot: thus Ajax is the third from Jove:

[115-042] Nor should I seek advantage from my line,

[115-043] Unless, Achilles, it was mix’d with thine.

[115-044] As next of kin, Achilles’ arms I claim:

[115-045] This fellow would ingraft a foreign name

[115-046] Upon our stock; and the Sisyphian seed

[115-047] By fraud and theft asserts his father’s breed.

[115-048] Then must I lose these arms, because I came

[115-049] To fight uncall’d, a voluntary name;

[115-050] Nor shunn’d the cause, but offer’d you my aid?

[115-051] While he long lurking was to war betray’d:

[115-052] Forced to the field he came, but in the rear,

[115-053] And feign’d distraction to conceal his fear,

[115-054] Till one more cunning caught him in the snare,

[115-055] (Ill for himself,) and dragg’d him into war.

[115-056] Now let a hero’s arms a coward vest,

[115-057] And he who shunn’d all honours gain the best;

[115-058] And let me stand excluded from my right,

[115-059] Robb’d of my kinsman’s arms, who first appear’d in fight.

[115-060] Better for us, at home had he remain’d,

[115-061] Had it been true the madness which he feign’d,

[115-062] Or so believed; the less had been our shame,

[115-063] The less his counsell’d crime, which brands the Grecian name

[115-064] Nor Philoctetes had been left enclosed

[115-065] In a bare isle, to wants and pains exposed,

[115-066] Where to the rocks, with solitary groans,

[115-067] His sufferings and our baseness he bemoans:

[115-068] And wishes (so may Heaven his wish fulfil!)

[115-069] The due reward to him who caused his ill:

[115-070] Now he, with us to [[troy|Troy]]’s destruction sworn,

[115-071] Our brother of the war, by whom are borne

[115-072] Alcides’ arrows, pent in narrow bounds,

[115-073] With cold and hunger pinch’d, and pain’d with wounds,

[115-074] To find him food and clothing, must employ

[115-075] Against the birds the shafts due to the fate of Troy.

[115-076] Yet still he lives, and lives from treason free,

[115-077] Because he left Ulysses’ company:

[115-078] Poor Palamede might wish, so void of aid

[115-079] Rather to have been left, than so to death betray’d.

[115-080] The coward bore the man immortal spite,

[115-081] Who shamed him out of madness into fight;

[115-082] Nor daring otherwise to vent his hate,

[115-083] Accused him first of treason to the state,

[115-084] And then, for proof, produced the golden store

[115-085] Himself had hidden in his tent before:

[115-086] Thus of two champions he deprived our host,

[115-087] By exile one, and one by treason lost.

[115-088] Thus fights Ulysses, thus his fame extends,

[115-089] A formidable man but to his friends:

[115-090] Great, for what greatness is in words and sound;

[115-091] Ev’n faithful Nestor less in both is found.

[115-092] But that he might without a rival reign,

[115-093] He left his faithful Nestor on the plain:

[115-094] Forsook his friend ev’n at his utmost need,

[115-095] Who tired, and tardy with his wounded steed,

[115-096] Cried out for aid, and call’d him by his name;

[115-097] But cowardice has neither ears nor shame.

[115-098] Thus fled the good old man, bereft of aid,

[115-099] And, for as much as lay in him, betray’d.

[115-100] That this is not a fable forged by me,

[115-101] Like one of his, an Ulyssean lie,

[115-102] I vouch ev’n Diomed, who, though his friend,

[115-103] Cannot that act excuse, much less defend:

[115-104] He call’d him back aloud, and tax’d his fear;

[115-105] And sure enough he heard, but durst not hear.

[115-106] “The gods with equal eyes on mortals look,

[115-107] He justly was forsaken who forsook:

[115-108] Wanted that succour he refused to lend,

[115-109] Found every fellow such another friend:

[115-110] No wonder if he roar’d that all might hear;

[115-111] His elocution was increased by fear:

[115-112] I heard, I ran; I found him out of breath,

[115-113] Pale, trembling, and half dead with fear of death.

[115-114] Though he had judged himself by his own laws,

[115-115] And stood condemn’d, I help’d the common cause;

[115-116] With my broad buckler hid him from the foe:

[115-117] (Ev’n the shield trembled as he lay below:)

[115-118] And from impending fate the coward freed:

[115-119] Good Heaven forgive me for so bad a deed!

[115-120] If still he will persist, and urge the strife,

[115-121] First let him give me back his forfeit life:

[115-122] Let him return to that opprobrious field;

[115-123] Again creep under my protecting shield:

[115-124] Let him lie wounded; let the foe be near;

[115-125] And let his quivering heart confess his fear:

[115-126] There put him in the very jaws of fate;

[115-127] And let him plead his cause in that estate.

[115-128] And yet when snatch’d from death, when from below

[115-129] My lifted shield I loosed, and let him go,

[115-130] Good heavens, how light he rose, with what a bound

[115-131] He sprung from earth, forgetful of his wound;

[115-132] How fresh, how eager then his feet to ply:

[115-133] Who had not strength to stand, had speed to fly!

[115-134] “Hector came on, and brought the gods along:

[115-135] Fear seized alike the feeble and the strong:

[115-136] Each Greek was an Ulysses; such a dread

[115-137] The approach, and ev’n the sound, of Hector bred:

[115-138] Him, flesh’d with slaughter, and with conquest crown’d,

[115-139] I met, and overturn’d him to the ground;

[115-140] When after, matchless as he deemed in might,

[115-141] He challenged all our host to single fight:

[115-142] All eyes were fixed on me: the lots were thrown;

[115-143] But for your champion I was wish’d alone.

[115-144] Your vows were heard: we fought, and neither yield;

[115-145] Yet I return’d unvanquish’d from the field.

[115-146] With Jove to friend, the insulting Trojan came,

[115-147] And menaced us with force, our fleet with flame.

[115-148] Was it the strength of this tongue-valiant lord,

[115-149] In that black hour, that saved you from the sword?

[115-150] Or was my breast exposed alone, to brave

[115-151] A thousand swords, a thousand ships to save?

[115-152] The hopes of your return! And can you yield,

[115-153] For a saved fleet, less than a single shield?

[115-154] Think it no boast, oh Grecians, if I deem

[115-155] These arms want Ajax, more than Ajax them:

[115-156] Or, I with them an equal honour share;

[115-157] They honour’d to be worn, and I to wear.

[115-158] Will he compare my courage with his sleight?

[115-159] As well he may compare the day with night.

[115-160] Night is indeed the province of his reign:

[115-161] Yet all his dark exploits no more contain

[115-162] Than a spy taken, and a sleeper slain;

[115-163] A priest made prisoner; [[minerva|Pallas]] made a prey:

[115-164] But none of all these actions done by day;

[115-165] Nor aught of these was done, and Diomed away.

[115-166] If on such petty merits you confer

[115-167] So vast a prize, let each his portion share;

[115-168] Make a just dividend; and if not all,

[115-169] The greater part to Diomed will fall.

[115-170] But why for Ithacus such arms as those,

[115-171] Who naked, and by night, invades his foes?

[115-172] The glittering helm by moonlight will proclaim

[115-173] The latent robber, and prevent his game:

[115-174] Nor could he hold his tottering head upright

[115-175] Beneath that morion, or sustain the weight;

[115-176] Nor that right arm could toss the beamy lance;

[115-177] Much less the left that ampler shield advance,

[115-178] Ponderous with precious weight, and rough with cost,

[115-179] Of the round world in rising gold emboss’d.

[115-180] That orb would ill become his hand to wield,

[115-181] And look as for the gold he stole the shield;

[115-182] Which, should your error on the wretch bestow,

[115-183] It would not frighten, but allure the foe.

[115-184] Why asks he what avails him not in fight,

[115-185] And would but cumber and retard his flight,

[115-186] In which his only excellence is placed?

[115-187] You give him death, that intercept his haste.

[115-188] Add, that his own is yet a maiden shield,

[115-189] Nor the least dint has suffer’d in the field,

[115-190] Guiltless of fight: mine, batter’d, hew’d, and bored,

[115-191] Worn out of service, must forsake its lord.

[115-192] What further need of words, our right to scan?

[115-193] My arguments are deeds; let action speak the man.

[115-194] Since from a champion’s arms the strife arose,

[115-195] Go cast the glorious prize amid the foes;

[115-196] Then send us to redeem both arms and shield,

[115-197] And let him wear who wins them in the field.”

[115-198] He said: a murmur from a multitude,

[115-199] Or somewhat like a stifled shout ensued;

[115-200] Till from his seat arose Laertes’ son,

[115-201] Look’d down a while, and paused ere he begun;

[115-202] Then to the expecting audience raised his look,

[115-203] And not without prepared attention spoke:

[115-204] Soft was his tone, and sober was his face;

[115-205] Action his words, and words his action grace.

[115-206] “If Heaven, my lords, had heard our common prayer,

[115-207] These arms had caused no quarrel for an heir;

[115-208] Still great Achilles had his own possess’d,

[115-209] And we with great Achilles had been bless’d:

[115-210] But since hard fate, and Heaven’s severe decree,

[115-211] Have ravish’d him away from you and me,”

[115-212] (At this he sigh’d, and wiped his eyes, and drew,

[115-213] Or seem’d to draw, some drops of kindly dew,)

[115-214] “Who better can succeed Achilles lost,

[115-215] Than he who gave Achilles to your host?

[115-216] This only I request, that neither he

[115-217] May gain, by being what he seems to be,

[115-218] A stupid thing, nor I may lose the prize,

[115-219] By having sense, which Heaven to him denies;

[115-220] Since great or small, the talent I enjoy’d

[115-221] Was ever in the common cause employ’d:

[115-222] Nor let my wit, and wonted eloquence,

[115-223] Which often has been used in your defence,

[115-224] And in my own, this only time be brought

[115-225] To bear against myself, and deem’d a fault:

[115-226] Make not a crime where nature made it none;

[115-227] For every man may freely use his own.

[115-228] The deeds of long-descended ancestors

[115-229] Are but by grace of imputation ours,

[115-230] Theirs in effect; but since he draws his line

[115-231] From Jove, and seems to plead a right divine;

[115-232] From Jove, like him, I claim my pedigree,

[115-233] And am descended in the same degree.

[115-234] My sire Laertes was Arcesius’ heir;

[115-235] Arcesius was the son of Jupiter:

[115-236] No parricide, no banish’d man is known,

[115-237] In all my line: let him excuse his own.

[115-238] Hermes ennobles too my mother’s side,

[115-239] By both my parents to the gods allied.

[115-240] But not because that on the female part

[115-241] My blood is better, dare I claim desert,

[115-242] Or that my sire from parricide is free;

[115-243] But judge by merit between him and me:

[115-244] The prize be to the best; provided yet

[115-245] That Ajax for a while his kin forget,

[115-246] And his great sire, and greater uncle’s name,

[115-247] To fortify by them his feeble claim;

[115-248] Be kindred and relation laid aside,

[115-249] And honour’s cause by laws of honour tried:

[115-250] For if he plead proximity of blood;

[115-251] That empty title is with ease withstood.

[115-252] Peleus, the hero’s sire, more nigh than he,

[115-253] And Pyrrhus, his undoubted progeny,

[115-254] Inherit first these trophies of the field;

[115-255] To Scyros, or to Phthia, send the shield:

[115-256] And Teucer has an uncle’s right; yet he

[115-257] Waves his pretensions, nor contends with me.

[115-258] Then since the cause on pure desert is placed,

[115-259] Whence shall I take my rise, what reckon last?

[115-260] I not presume on every act to dwell,

[115-261] But take these few, in order as they fell.

[115-262] Thetis, who knew the fates, applied her care

[115-263] To keep Achilles in disguise from war;

[115-264] And till the threatening influence was past,

[115-265] A woman’s habit on the hero cast:

[115-266] All eyes were cozen’d by the borrow’d vest,

[115-267] And Ajax (never wiser than the rest)

[115-268] Found no Pelides there: at length I came

[115-269] With proffer’d wares to this pretended dame;

[115-270] She, not discover’d by her mien or voice,

[115-271] Betray’d her manhood by her manly choice;

[115-272] And while on female toys her fellows look,

[115-273] Grasp’d in her warlike hand, a javelin shook:

[115-274] Whom, by this act reveal’d, I thus bespoke:

[115-275] ‘Oh goddess born! resist not Heaven’s decree,

[115-276] The fall of Ilium is reserved for thee.’

[115-277] Then seized him, and produced in open light,

[115-278] Sent blushing to the field the fatal knight.

[115-279] Mine then are all his actions of the war;

[115-280] Great Telephus was conquer’d by my spear,

[115-281] And after cured: to me the Theban’s owe,

[115-282] Lesbos, and Tenedos, their overthrow;

[115-283] Scyros and Cylla: not on all to dwell,

[115-284] By me Lyrnessus and strong Chrysa fell:

[115-285] And since I sent the man who Hector slew,

[115-286] To me the noble Hector’s death is due:

[115-287] Those arms I put into his living hand,

[115-288] Those arms, Pelides dead, I now demand.

[115-289] “When Greece was injured in the Spartan prince,

[115-290] And met at Aulis to avenge the offence,

[115-291] ’Twas a dead calm, or adverse blasts, that reign’d,

[115-292] And in the port the wind-bound fleet detain’d:

[115-293] Bad signs were seen, and oracles severe

[115-294] Were daily thunder’d in our general’s ear;

[115-295] That by his daughter’s blood we must appease

[115-296] Diana’s kindled wrath, and free the seas.

[115-297] Affection, interest, fame, his heart assail’d;

[115-298] But soon the father o’er the king prevail’d:

[115-299] Bold, on himself he took the pious crime,

[115-300] As angry with the gods as they with him.

[115-301] No subject could sustain their sovereign’s look,

[115-302] Till this hard enterprise I undertook:

[115-303] I only durst the imperial power control,

[115-304] And undermined the parent in his soul:

[115-305] Forced him to exert the king for common good,

[115-306] And pay our ransom with his daughter’s blood.

[115-307] Never was cause more difficult to plead,

[115-308] Than where the judge against himself decreed:

[115-309] Yet this I won by dint of argument;

[115-310] The wrongs his injured brother underwent,

[115-311] And his own office, shamed him to consent.

[115-312] “ ’Twas harder yet to move the mother’s mind,

[115-313] And to this heavy task was I design’d:

[115-314] Reasons against her love I knew were vain;

[115-315] I circumvented whom I could not gain:

[115-316] Had Ajax been employ’d, our slacken’d sails

[115-317] Had still at Aulis waited happy gales.

[115-318] “Arrived at Troy, your choice was fix’d on me

[115-319] A fearless envoy, fit for a bold embassy:

[115-320] Secure, I enter’d through the hostile court,

[115-321] Glittering with steel, and crowded with resort:

[115-322] There, in the midst of arms, I plead our cause,

[115-323] Urge the foul rape, and violated laws;

[115-324] Accuse the foes, as authors of the strife,

[115-325] Reproach the ravisher, demand the wife.

[115-326] Priam, Antenor, and the wiser few,

[115-327] I moved; but Paris and his lawless crew

[115-328] Scarce held their hands and lifted swords; but stood

[115-329] In act to quench their impious thirst of blood:

[115-330] This Menelaus knows; exposed to share

[115-331] With me the rough preludium of war.

[115-332] “Endless it were to tell what I have done

[115-333] In arms, or council, since the siege begun:

[115-334] The first encounter’s pass’d, the foe repell’d,

[115-335] They skulk’d within the town, we kept the field.

[115-336] War seem’d asleep for nine long years; at length

[115-337] Both sides resolved to push, we tried our strength.

[115-338] Now what did Ajax, while our arms took breath.

[115-339] Versed only in the gross mechanic trade of death?

[115-340] If you require my deeds, with ambush’d arms

[115-341] I trapp’d the foe, or tired with false alarms;

[115-342] Secured the ships, drew lines along the plain,

[115-343] The fainting cheer’d, chastised the rebel train.

[115-344] Provided forage, our spent arms renew’d;

[115-345] Employ’d at home, or sent abroad, the common cause pursued.

[115-346] “The king, deluded in a dream by Jove,

[115-347] Despair’d to take the town, and order’d to remove.

[115-348] What subject durst arraign the power supreme?

[115-349] Producing Jove to justify his dream.

[115-350] Ajax might wish the soldiers to retain

[115-351] From shameful flight, but wishes were in vain:

[115-352] As wanting of effect had been his words,

[115-353] Such as of course his thundering tongue affords.

[115-354] But did this boaster threaten, did he pray,

[115-355] Or by his own example urge their stay?

[115-356] None, none of these; but ran himself away.

[115-357] I saw him run, and was ashamed to see;

[115-358] Who plied his feet so fast to get aboard as he?

[115-359] Then speeding through the place, I made a stand,

[115-360] And loudly cried, ‘Oh, base degenerate band,

[115-361] To leave a town already in your hand!

[115-362] After so long expense of blood for fame,

[115-363] To bring home nothing but perpetual shame!’

[115-364] These words, or what I have forgotten since,

[115-365] (For grief inspired me then with eloquence,)

[115-366] Reduced their minds; they leave the crowded port,

[115-367] And to their late forsaken camp resort:

[115-368] Dismay’d the council met: this man was there,

[115-369] But mute, and not recover’d of his fear:

[115-370] Thersites tax’d the king, and loudly rail’d,

[115-371] But his wide-opening mouth with blows I seal’d.

[115-372] Then, rising, I excite their souls to fame,

[115-373] And kindle sleeping virtue into flame.

[115-374] From thence, whatever he perform’d in fight

[115-375] Is justly mine, who drew him back from flight.

[115-376] “Which of the Grecian chiefs consorts with thee?

[115-377] But Diomed desires my company,

[115-378] And still communicates his praise with me.

[115-379] As guided by a god, secure he goes,

[115-380] Arm’d with my fellowship, amid the foes:

[115-381] And sure no little merit I may boast,

[115-382] Whom such a man selects from such a host;

[115-383] Unforced by lots I went without affright,

[115-384] To dare with him the dangers of the night:

[115-385] On the same errand sent, we met the spy

[115-386] Of Hector, double-tongued, and used to lie;

[115-387] Him I despatch’d, but not till undermined,

[115-388] I drew him first to tell what treacherous Troy design’d:

[115-389] My task perform’d, with praise I had retired,

[115-390] But not content with this, to greater praise aspired;

[115-391] Invaded Rhesus, and his Thracian crew,

[115-392] And him and his in their own strength I slew;

[115-393] Return’d a victor, all my vows complete,

[115-394] With the king’s chariot, in his royal seat:

[115-395] Refuse me now his arms, whose fiery steeds

[115-396] Were promised to the spy for his nocturnal deeds;

[115-397] And let dull Ajax bear away my right,

[115-398] When all his days outbalance this one night.

[115-399] “Nor fought I darkling still: the sun beheld

[115-400] With slaughter’d Lycians when I strew’d the field:

[115-401] You saw, and counted as I pass’d along,

[115-402] Alastor, Chromius, Ceranos the strong,

[115-403] Alcander, Prytanis, and Halius,

[115-404] Noemon, Charopes, and Ennomus;

[115-405] Coon, Chersidamas; and five beside,

[115-406] Men of obscure descent, but courage tried:

[115-407] All these this hand laid breathless on the ground;

[115-408] Nor want I proofs of many a manly wound:

[115-409] All honest, all before: believe not me,

[115-410] Words may deceive, but credit what you see.”

[115-411] At this he bared his breast, and show’d his scars,

[115-412] As of a furrow’d field, well plough’d with wars;

[115-413] “Nor is this part unexercised,” said he;

[115-414] “That giant bulk of his from wounds is free:

[115-415] Safe in his shield, he fears no foe to try,

[115-416] And better manages his blood than I:

[115-417] But this avails me not; our boaster strove

[115-418] Not with our foes alone, but partial Jove,

[115-419] To save the fleet: this I confess is true:

[115-420] (Nor will I take from any man his due:)

[115-421] But thus assuming all, he robs from you.

[115-422] Some part of honour to your share will fall,

[115-423] He did the best indeed, but did not all.

[115-424] Patroclus in Achilles’ arms, and thought

[115-425] The chief he seem’d, with equal ardour fought;

[115-426] Preserved the fleet, repell’d the raging fire,

[115-427] And forced the fearful Trojans to retire.

[115-428] “But Ajax boasts, that he was only thought

[115-429] A match for Hector, who the combat sought:

[115-430] Sure he forgets the king, the chiefs, and me:

[115-431] All were as eager for the fight as he:

[115-432] He but the ninth, and not by public voice,

[115-433] Or ours preferr’d, was only fortune’s choice:

[115-434] They fought; nor can our hero boast the event,

[115-435] For Hector from the field unwounded went.

[115-436] “Why am I forced to name that fatal day,

[115-437] That snatch’d the prop and pride of Greece away?

[115-438] I saw Pelides sink, with pious grief,

[115-439] And ran in vain, alas! to his relief;

[115-440] For the brave soul was fled: full of my friend

[115-441] I rush’d amid the war, his relics to defend:

[115-442] Nor ceased my toil till I redeem’d my prey,

[115-443] And, loaded with Achilles, march’d away:

[115-444] Those arms which on these shoulders then I bore
