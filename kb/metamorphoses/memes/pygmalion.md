# Pygmalion

Pygmalion, a sculptor of Cyprus, is a unique figure in the *Metamorphoses*: a mortal whose act of creation rivals the divine. His love for the ivory statue he has carved — and Venus's answering transformation of the statue into a living woman — makes his story the poem's most optimistic meditation on art, desire, and the relationship between creator and creation.

## Role in the Metamorphoses

Pygmalion's story appears in Book X, sung by Orpheus as part of his extended narrative of love after the loss of Eurydice. Disgusted by the Propoetides — women transformed to stone by Venus for their wantonness — Pygmalion carves an ivory statue of ideal female beauty and falls in love with it. At the festival of Venus, he prays for a wife "like my ivory maiden." Venus, interpreting his true desire, grants the statue life. When Pygmalion returns home and kisses the statue, it warms beneath his lips, softens, and opens its eyes. Their daughter Paphos gives her name to the Cyprian city.

### Key Episodes

- **The Carving**: Disgusted by real women, Pygmalion carves an ivory statue of ideal beauty — and falls in love with his own creation (105-001)
- **The Prayer**: At Venus's festival, Pygmalion prays for a wife "like my ivory maiden" — a prayer that asks for similitude rather than identity (105-001)
- **The Animation**: Returning home, Pygmalion kisses the statue; the ivory warms, softens, "yields beneath his fingers" — art becomes life through divine grace (105-001)
- **Birth of Paphos**: The living woman bears Pygmalion a daughter, Paphos, who gives her name to the city sacred to Venus (105-001)

## Transformation

Pygmalion is the poem's only mortal **creator** whose art is transformed into life through divine agency. Unlike Arachne (whose art is destroyed) or Orpheus (whose art cannot save Eurydice), Pygmalion's creation becomes real — the statue's transformation from ivory to flesh is the *Metamorphoses*' most hopeful metamorphosis.

- **Form**: Ivory statue → Living woman
- **Agent**: Venus (answering Pygmalion's prayer)
- **Cause**: Pygmalion's devotion and Venus's grace
- **Source**: (105-001)
