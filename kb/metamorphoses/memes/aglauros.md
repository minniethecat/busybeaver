# Aglauros

Aglauros, daughter of Cecrops, the serpent-born first king of Athens, is a central figure in one of Ovid's most psychologically acute transformation narratives. When Mercury falls in love with her sister Herse, Aglauros is approached by Minerva — who, still nursing her grudge against Mercury, commands Envy to poison Aglauros's heart. Consumed by jealousy of her sister's happiness, Aglauros physically blocks Mercury from entering Herse's chamber, and the god, in disgust, transforms her into a black statue — a metamorphosis that externalizes the darkening of her soul. Her story in Book II of the *Metamorphoses* is a remarkable depiction of envy as a self-destroying force, in which the transformation is less a punishment than a revelation of what Aglauros has already become.

## Role in the Metamorphoses

Aglauros's story unfolds in Book II, following Mercury's slaying of Argus and his liberation of Io. Flying over Athens, the god sees the beautiful Herse returning from a festival of Minerva and is instantly smitten. He presents himself at the palace, where Aglauros — Herse's sister — agrees to help him, but demands a heavy price in gold. Minerva, who despises Mercury for his role in her defeat by Arachne, visits the cave of Envy (Invidia), a hideous hag feeding on snakes and venom, and commands her to poison Aglauros. Envy breathes her corrosive influence into Aglauros's breast, filling her with such tormented jealousy of Herse's fortune that she sits before her sister's door and refuses to move, blocking Mercury's access. When the god demands entry, Aglauros taunts him. Mercury, recognizing that her heart has been turned to stone by envy, speaks the fatal words: "You shall be what you have become" — and transforms her into a black statue, a motionless figure whose darkened form perfectly embodies the soul within (017-001).

- **Minerva's Intervention**: Still angry at Mercury, Minerva summons Envy to poison Aglauros's heart

- **The Transformation**: Aglauros blocks Mercury's path and is transformed into a black stone statue — envy made visible and permanent (017-001)
