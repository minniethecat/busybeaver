# Themis

Themis, the Titaness of divine law, order, and prophecy, occupies a unique position in the *Metamorphoses*' divine hierarchy. Older than the Olympians yet consulted by them, she embodies the immutable cosmic order that even Jupiter must respect. Her voice — always prophetic, always just — punctuates the poem at critical moments: after the great Flood, before the Theban war, and in the councils of the gods.

## Role in the Metamorphoses

Themis appears at three distinct junctures in the poem, each time as a voice of prophetic authority. After the destruction of the impious race by flood, she is worshipped alongside the mountain nymphs as the one who can restore humanity — Deucalion and Pyrrha consult her oracle (006-243). Much later, as the Theban war approaches, she foretells the conflict with words that the gods themselves must heed (087-001). Her authority transcends the Olympian order because it derives not from power but from law — the law that governs even the gods.

### Key Episodes

- **Worship After the Flood**: Following the destruction of the impious race, the "mountain nymphs and Themis they adore" — she is the divine authority consulted for the restoration of humankind (006-243)
- **Oracle to Deucalion and Pyrrha**: Themis delivers the famous oracle instructing the survivors to "throw behind you the bones of your great mother" — the stones that become the new human race (006-317)
- **Prophecy of the Theban War**: Themis foretells the Theban war, a conflict whose inevitability is rooted in divine law and fate; "had not just Themis thus maturely said" — her warning is both prophecy and confirmation of what must come (087-001)
- **Council of the Gods**: When Themis speaks "with prescient voice" before the assembled gods, her words carry the weight of cosmic law, shaping the divine response to mortal affairs (088-002)

## Transformation

Themis herself does not transform — she is, by her very nature, immutable. As the embodiment of divine law and cosmic order, she represents the unchanging framework within which all metamorphoses occur. Every transformation in the poem, whether punishment or reward, is ultimately an expression of the cosmic justice that Themis personifies.

- **Form**: Divine Titaness — unchanging
- **Agent**: Not an agent of transformation; rather, the prophetic voice that declares the lawful outcome
- **Cause**: Her role is to speak what must happen, not to cause it
- **Source**: (006-243, 006-317, 087-001, 088-002)
