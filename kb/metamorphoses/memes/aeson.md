# Aeson

Aeson, the aged and enfeebled king of Iolcus, is the father of the hero Jason and the recipient of one of the *Metamorphoses*' most spectacular transformations: a magical rejuvenation performed by Medea. Where most of the poem's metamorphoses are punishments or escapes, Aeson's is a gift — the gift of youth restored by a sorceress's art, a demonstration that magic can reverse even the most inexorable of natural processes.

## Role in the Metamorphoses

Aeson appears in Book 7, in the extended episode of Medea's magic. Jason, returning from Colchis with Medea, finds his father Aeson near death from old age. Medea, moved by filial piety — or by her own desire to demonstrate her power — undertakes an elaborate magical ritual to rejuvenate the old king. The description of her spell is one of the poem's most detailed and atmospheric accounts of supernatural power.

### Key Episodes

- **The Aged Father**: Aeson is introduced as an old man "worn out with years," too frail to participate in the celebrations of Jason's return — a father whose life is ebbing as his son's triumphs (063-001)
- **Medea's Rejuvenation Spell**: Medea, under the light of the full moon, performs an intricate ritual lasting nine days and nine nights — gathering herbs from distant mountains, brewing potions, and invoking the powers of earth and sky. She drains Aeson's aged blood and replaces it with her elixir (063-001)
- **Youth Restored**: Aeson emerges from the spell transformed — his white hair darkens, his withered flesh fills out, his limbs regain their youthful strength. He is himself, but forty years younger, a living demonstration of magic's power over nature

## Transformation

Aeson's rejuvenation is unique among the *Metamorphoses*' transformations: it reverses time rather than changing form. Unlike Daphne becoming a laurel or Actaeon becoming a stag, Aeson becomes what he already was — a younger version of himself. This backward metamorphosis, achieved through Medea's art, serves as a counterpoint to the poem's usual forward-racing tide of change. It asks: if magic can reverse age, what limits can there be on transformation itself?

- **Form**: Aged mortal → Youthful mortal (time reversed, not form changed)
- **Agent**: Medea (witchcraft and magical art)
- **Cause**: Filial devotion (Jason's plea), Medea's desire to demonstrate and exercise her power
