[040-001] Phineus, the brother of Cepheus, had been betrothed to his niece Andromeda before she had been exposed to the rage of a sea-monster; and, in order to interrupt the marriage ceremony, he collects a considerable number of his adherents, who assault [[perseus|Perseus]]—The hero, after defending himself with courage, is in danger of being overpowered, when the assailants are suddenly turned into stone by the head of [[medusa|Medusa]].

[040-002] While Perseus entertain’d with this report

[040-003] His father Cepheus, and the list’ning court,

[040-004] Within the palace walls was heard aloud

[040-005] The roaring noise of some unruly crowd;

[040-006] Not like the songs which cheerful friends prepare

[040-007] For nuptial days, but sounds that threaten’d war;

[040-008] And all the pleasures of this happy feast,

[040-009] To tumult turn’d, in wild disorder ceased:

[040-010] So, when the sea is calm, we often find

[040-011] A storm raised sudden by some furious wind.

[040-012] Chief in the riot Phineus first appear’d,

[040-013] The rash ringleader of this boist’rous herd,

[040-014] And brandishing his brazen-pointed lance,

[040-015] “Behold,” he said, “an injured man advance,

[040-016] Stung with resentment for his ravish’d wife;

[040-017] Nor shall thy wings, O Perseus, save thy life;

[040-018] Nor [[jupiter|Jove]] himself, though we’ve been often told,

[040-019] Who got thee in the form of tempting gold.”

[040-020] His lance was aim’d, when Cepheus ran and said,

[040-021] “Hold! brother, hold! what brutal rage has made

[040-022] Your frantic mind so black a crime conceive?

[040-023] Are these the thanks that you to Perseus give?

[040-024] This the reward that to his worth you pay,

[040-025] Whose timely valour saved Andromeda?

[040-026] Nor was it he, if you would reason right,

[040-027] That forced her from you, but the jealous spite

[040-028] Of envious Nereids, and Jove’s high decree,

[040-029] And that devouring monster of the sea,

[040-030] That ready, with his jaws wide gaping, stood

[040-031] To eat my child, the fairest of my blood.

[040-032] You lost her then, when she seem’d past relief,

[040-033] And wish’d, perhaps, her death to ease your grief

[040-034] With my afflictions: not content to view

[040-035] Andromeda in chains, unhelp’d by you,

[040-036] Her spouse, and uncle; will you grieve that he

[040-037] Exposed his life the dying maid to free?

[040-038] And shall you claim his merit? Had you thought

[040-039] Her charms so great, you should have bravely sought

[040-040] That blessing on the rocks where fix’d she lay:

[040-041] But now let Perseus bear his prize away,

[040-042] By service gain’d, by promised faith possess’d;

[040-043] To him I owe it, that my age is bless’d

[040-044] Still with a child: nor think that I prefer

[040-045] Perseus to thee, but to the loss of her.”

[040-046] Phineus on him and Perseus roll’d about

[040-047] His eyes in silent rage, and seem’d to doubt

[040-048] Which to destroy, till, resolute at length,

[040-049] He threw his spear with the redoubled strength

[040-050] His fury gave him, and at Perseus struck;

[040-051] But missing Perseus, in his seat it stuck;

[040-052] Who, springing nimbly up, return’d the dart,

[040-053] And almost plunged it in his rival’s heart;

[040-054] But he for safety to the altar ran;

[040-055] Unfit protection for so vile a man:

[040-056] Yet was the stroke not vain, as Rhoetus found,

[040-057] Who in his brow received a mortal wound;

[040-058] Headlong he tumbled, when his scull was broke,

[040-059] From which his friends the fatal weapon took,

[040-060] While he lay trembling, and his gushing blood

[040-061] In crimson streams around the table flow’d.

[040-062] But this provoked the unruly rabble worse:

[040-063] They flung their darts; and some in loud discourse

[040-064] To death young Perseus and the monarch doom;

[040-065] But Cepheus left before the guilty room,

[040-066] With grief appealing to the gods above,

[040-067] Who laws of hospitality approve,

[040-068] Who faith protect, and succour injured right,

[040-069] That he was guiltless of this barb’rous fight.

[040-070] [[minerva|Pallas]] her brother Perseus close attends,

[040-071] And with her ample shield from harm defends,

[040-072] Raising a sprightly courage in his heart:

[040-073] But Indian Athis took the weaker part:

[040-074] Born in the crystal grottoes of the sea,

[040-075] Limnate’s son, a fenny nymph, and she

[040-076] Daughter of Ganges: graceful was his mien,

[040-077] His person lovely, and his age sixteen:

[040-078] His habit made his native beauty more:

[040-079] A purple mantle fringed with gold he wore;

[040-080] His neck, well turn’d, with golden chains was graced;

[040-081] His hair, with myrrh perfumed, was nicely dress’d.

[040-082] Though with just aim he could the javelin throw,

[040-083] Yet with more skill he drew the bending bow;

[040-084] And now was drawing it with artful hand,

[040-085] When Perseus, snatching up a flaming brand,

[040-086] Whirl’d sudden at his face the burning wood,

[040-087] Crush’d his eyes in, and quench’d the fire with blood;

[040-088] Through the soft skin the splinter’d bones appear,

[040-089] And spoil’d the face that lately was so fair.

[040-090] When Lycabas his Athis thus beheld,

[040-091] How was his heart with friendly horror fill’d!

[040-092] A youth so noble, to his soul so dear,

[040-093] To see his shapeless look, his dying groans to hear!

[040-094] He snatch’d the bow the boy was used to bend,

[040-095] And cried, “With me, false traitor, dare contend;

[040-096] Boast not a conquest o’er a child, but try

[040-097] Thy strength with me, who all thy powers defy,

[040-098] Nor think so mean an act a victory.”

[040-099] While yet he spoke he flung the whizzing dart,

[040-100] Which pierced the plaited robe, but miss’d his heart.

[040-101] Perseus defied, upon him fiercely press’d

[040-102] With sword unsheathed, and plunged it in his breast:

[040-103] His eyes o’erwhelm’d with night, he stumbling falls,

[040-104] And with his latest breath on Athis calls;

[040-105] Pleased that so near the lovely youth he lies,

[040-106] He sinks his head upon his friend, and dies.

[040-107] Next eager Phorbas, old Methion’s son,

[040-108] Came rushing forward with Amphimedon,

[040-109] When the smooth pavement, slippery made with gore,

[040-110] Tripp’d up their feet, and flung them on the floor:

[040-111] The sword of Perseus, who by chance was nigh,

[040-112] Prevents their rise; and where they fall, they lie:

[040-113] Full in his ribs Amphimedon he smote,

[040-114] And then stuck fiery Phorbas in the throat.

[040-115] Eurythus lifting up his axe, the blow

[040-116] Was thus prevented by his nimble foe:

[040-117] A golden cup he seizes, high emboss’d,

[040-118] And at his head the massy goblet toss’d:

[040-119] It hits, and from his forehead bruised rebounds,

[040-120] And blood and brains he vomits from his wounds;

[040-121] With his slain fellows on the floor he lies,

[040-122] And death for ever shuts his swimming eyes.

[040-123] Then Polydaemon fell, a goddess born:

[040-124] Phlegias and Elycen, with locks unshorn,

[040-125] Next follow’d: next the stroke of death he gave

[040-126] To Clytus, Abanis, and Lycetus brave;

[040-127] While o’er unnumber’d heaps of ghastly dead

[040-128] The Argive hero’s feet triumphant tread.

[040-129] But Phineus stands aloof, and dreads to feel

[040-130] His rival’s force, and flies his pointed steel;

[040-131] Yet threw a dart from far; by chance it lights

[040-132] On Idas, who for neither party fights:

[040-133] But wounded, sternly thus to Phineus said:

[040-134] “Since of a neuter thou a foe hast made,

[040-135] This I return thee,” drawing from his side

[040-136] The dart, which, as he strove to fling, he died.

[040-137] Odites fell by Clymenus’s sword;

[040-138] The Cephen court had not a greater lord.

[040-139] Hypseus his blade does in Protenor sheath;

[040-140] But brave Lyncides soon revenged his death.

[040-141] Here too was old Emathion, one that fear’d

[040-142] The gods, and in the cause of Heaven appear’d,

[040-143] Who, only wishing the success of right,

[040-144] And by his age exempted from the fight,

[040-145] Both sides alike condemns: “This impious war

[040-146] Cease, cease,” he cries; “these bloody broils forbear.”

[040-147] This scarce the sage, with high concern, had said,

[040-148] When Chromis, at a blow, struck off his head,

[040-149] Which, dropping, on the royal altar roll’d,

[040-150] Still staring on the crowd with aspect bold;

[040-151] And still it seem’d their horrid strife to blame;

[040-152] In life and death his pious zeal the same:

[040-153] While clinging to the horns the trunk expires,

[040-154] The sever’d head consumes amid the fires.

[040-155] Then Phineus, who from far his javelin threw,

[040-156] Broteas and Ammon, twins and brothers, slew;

[040-157] For knotted gauntlets matchless in the field;

[040-158] But gauntlets must to swords and javelins yield.

[040-159] Ampycus next, with hallow’d fillets bound,

[040-160] As Ceres’ priest, and with a mitre crown’d,

[040-161] His spear transfix’d, and struck him to the ground.

[040-162] O Iapetides, with pain I tell

[040-163] How you, sweet lyrist, in the riot fell:

[040-164] What worse than brutal rage his breast could fill

[040-165] Who did thy blood, O bard celestial! spill?

[040-166] Kindly you press’d amid the princely throng,

[040-167] To crown the feast, and give the nuptial song:

[040-168] Discord abhorr’d the music of thy lyre,

[040-169] Whose notes did gentle peace so well inspire:

[040-170] Thee when fierce Pettalus far off espied,

[040-171] Defenceless with thy harp, he scoffing cried,

[040-172] “Go, to the ghosts thy soothing lessons play;

[040-173] We loathe thy lyre, and scorn thy peaceful lay;”

[040-174] And, as again he fiercely bid him go,

[040-175] He pierced his temples with a mortal blow.

[040-176] His harp he held, though sinking on the ground,

[040-177] Whose strings in death his trembling fingers found,

[040-178] By chance, and tuned by chance a dying sound.

[040-179] With grief Lycormas saw him fall, from far,

[040-180] And wresting from the door a massy bar,

[040-181] Full in his poll lays on a load of knocks,

[040-182] Which stun him, and he falls like a devoted ox.

[040-183] Another bar Pelates would have snatch’d,

[040-184] But Corythus his motions slyly watch’d;

[040-185] He darts his weapon from a private stand,

[040-186] And rivets to the post his veiny hand;

[040-187] When straight a missive spear transfix’d his side,

[040-188] By Abas thrown, and, as he hung, he died.

[040-189] Melaneus on the prince’s side was slain,

[040-190] And Dorylas, who own’d a fertile plain,

[040-191] Of Nasamonia’s fields the wealthy lord,

[040-192] Whose crowded barns could scarce contain their hoard:

[040-193] A whizzing spear obliquely gave a blow,

[040-194] Stuck in his groin, and pierced the nerves below:

[040-195] His foe beheld his eyes convulsive roll,

[040-196] His ebbing veins, and his departing soul,

[040-197] Then taunting said: “Of all thy spacious plains,

[040-198] This spot thy only property remains.”

[040-199] He left him thus; but had no sooner left,

[040-200] Than Perseus in revenge his nostrils cleft;

[040-201] From his friend’s breast the murdering dart he drew,

[040-202] And the same weapon at the murderer threw;

[040-203] His head in halves the darted javelin cut,

[040-204] And on each side the brain came issuing out.

[040-205] Fortune his friend, his deaths around he deals,

[040-206] And this his lance, and that his falchion feels:

[040-207] Now Clytius dies; and, by a different wound,

[040-208] The twin, his brother Clanis, bites the ground:

[040-209] In his rent jaw the bearded weapon sticks,

[040-210] And the steel’d dart does Clytius’ thigh transfix.

[040-211] With these Mendesian Celadon he slew;

[040-212] And Astreus next, whose mother was a Jew;

[040-213] His sire uncertain: then by Perseus fell

[040-214] Aethion, who could things to come foretell;

[040-215] But now he knows not whence the javelin flies

[040-216] That wounds his breast, nor by whose arm he dies.

[040-217] The squire to Phineus next his valour tried,

[040-218] And fierce Agyrtes stain’d with parricide.

[040-219] As these are slain, fresh numbers still appear,

[040-220] And wage with Perseus an unequal war;

[040-221] To rob him of his right—the maid he won,

[040-222] By honour, promise, and desert his own.

[040-223] With him the father of the beauteous bride,

[040-224] The mother, and the frighted virgin, side:

[040-225] With shrieks and doleful cries they rend the air:

[040-226] Their shrieks confounded with the din of war,

[040-227] With clashing arms, and groanings of the slain,

[040-228] They grieve unpitied, and unheard complain.

[040-229] The floor with ruddy streams Bellona stains;

[040-230] And Phineus a new war with double rage maintains.

[040-231] Perseus begirt, from all around they pour

[040-232] Their lances on him, a tempestuous shower,

[040-233] Aim’d all at him; a cloud of darts and spears,

[040-234] Or blind his eyes, or whistle round his ears.

[040-235] Their numbers to resist, against the wall

[040-236] He guards his back secure, and dares them all.

[040-237] Here from the left Molpeus renews the fight,

[040-238] And bold Ethemon presses on the right:

[040-239] As when a hungry tiger near him hears

[040-240] Two lowing herds, a while he both forbears,

[040-241] Nor can his hopes of this or that renounce,

[040-242] So strong he lusts to prey on both at once:

[040-243] Thus Perseus now with that or this is loath

[040-244] To war distinct, but rain would fall on both:

[040-245] And first Chaonian Molpeus felt his blow,

[040-246] And fled, and never after faced his foe:

[040-247] Then fierce Ethemon, as he turn’d his back,

[040-248] Hurried with fury, aiming at his neck,

[040-249] His brandish’d sword against the marble struck

[040-250] With all his might; the brittle weapon broke,

[040-251] And in his throat the point rebounding stuck.

[040-252] Too slight the wound for life to issue thence,

[040-253] And yet too great for battle or defence:

[040-254] His arms extended, in this piteous state,

[040-255] For mercy he would sue, but sues too late;

[040-256] Perseus has in his bosom plunged the sword,

[040-257] And ere he speaks, the wound prevents the word.

[040-258] The crowds increasing, and his friends distress’d

[040-259] Himself by warring multitudes oppress’d;

[040-260] “Since thus unequally you fight, ’tis time,”

[040-261] He cried, “to punish your presumptuous crime:

[040-262] Beware, my friends:” his friends were soon prepar’d;

[040-263] Their sight averting, high the head he rear’d,

[040-264] And Gorgon on his foes severely stared.

[040-265] “Vain shift!” says Thescelus, with aspect bold,

[040-266] “Thee and thy bugbear monster I behold

[040-267] With scorn:” he lifts his arm, but ere he threw

[040-268] The dart, the hero to a statue grew.

[040-269] In the same posture still the marble stands,

[040-270] And holds the warrior’s weapons in its hands.

[040-271] Amphyx, whom yet this wonder can’t alarm,

[040-272] Heaves at Lyncides’ breast his impious arm;

[040-273] But, while thus daringly he presses on,

[040-274] His weapon and his arrn are turn’d to stone.

[040-275] Next Nileus, he who vainly said he owed

[040-276] His origin to Nile’s prolific flood;

[040-277] Who on his shield seven silver rivers bore,

[040-278] His birth to witness by the arms he wore;

[040-279] Full of his sevenfold father, thus express’d

[040-280] His boast to Perseus, and his pride confess’d:

[040-281] “See whence we sprung: let this thy comfort be,

[040-282] In thy sure death, that thou did’st die by me.”

[040-283] While yet he spoke, the dying accents hung

[040-284] In sounds imperfect on his marble tongue:

[040-285] Though changed to stone, his lips he seem’d to stretch,

[040-286] And through the insensate rock would force a speech.

[040-287] This Eryx saw, but seeing would not own:

[040-288] “The mischief by yourselves,” he cries, “is done;

[040-289] ’Tis your cold courage turns your hearts to stone:

[040-290] Come, follow me; fall on the stripling boy,

[040-291] Kill him, and you his magic arms destroy.”

[040-292] Then rushing on, his arm to strike he rear’d,

[040-293] And marbled o’er his varied frame appear’d.

[040-294] These for affronting Pallas were chastised,

[040-295] And justly met the death they had despised;

[040-296] But brave Aconteus, Perseus’ friend, by chance

[040-297] Look’d back, and met the Gorgon’s fatal glance;

[040-298] A statue now become, he ghastly stares,

[040-299] And still the foe to mortal combat dares.

[040-300] Astyages the living likeness knew,

[040-301] On the dead stone with vengeful fury flew;

[040-302] But impotent his rage; the jarring blade

[040-303] No print upon the solid marble made:

[040-304] Again, as with redoubled might he struck,

[040-305] Himself astonish’d in the quarry stuck.

[040-306] The vulgar deaths ’twere tedious to rehearse,

[040-307] And fates below the dignity of verse:

[040-308] Their safety in their flight two hundred found;

[040-309] Two hundred by Medusa’s head were stoned.

[040-310] Fierce Phineus now repents the wrongful fight,

[040-311] And views his varied friends; a dreadful sight;

[040-312] He knows their faces, for their help he sues,

[040-313] And thinks, not hearing him, that they refuse;

[040-314] By name he begs their succour, one by one,

[040-315] Then doubts their life, and feels the friendly stone.

[040-316] Struck with remorse, and conscious of his pride,

[040-317] Convict of sin, he turn’d his eyes aside;

[040-318] With suppliant mien, to Perseus thus he prays:

[040-319] “Hence with the head, as far as winds and seas

[040-320] Can bear thee; hence; O quit the Cephen shore,

[040-321] And never curse us with Medusa more;

[040-322] That horrid head, which stiffens into stone

[040-323] Those impious men, who, daring death, look on.

[040-324] I warr’d not with thee out of hate or strife;

[040-325] My honest cause was to defend my wife,

[040-326] First pledged to me: what crime could I suppose,

[040-327] To arm my friends, and vindicate my spouse?

[040-328] But vain, too late, I see, was our design;

[040-329] Mine was the title, but the merit thine.

[040-330] Contending made me guilty, I confess;

[040-331] But penitence should make that guilt the less:

[040-332] ’Twas thine to conquer by Minerva’s power;

[040-333] Favour’d by heaven, thy mercy I implore;

[040-334] For life I sue, the rest to thee I yield:

[040-335] In pity from my sight remove the shield.”

[040-336] He suing said, nor durst revert his eyes

[040-337] On the grim head; and Perseus thus replies:

[040-338] “Coward, what is in me to grant I will,

[040-339] Nor blood, unworthy of my valour, spill;

[040-340] Fear not to perish by my vengeful sword;

[040-341] From that secure, ’tis all [[fate|the Fates]] afford.

[040-342] Where now I see thee, thou shalt still be seen,

[040-343] A lasting monument, to please our queen;

[040-344] There still shall thy betroth’d behold her spouse,

[040-345] And find his image in her father’s house.”

[040-346] This said, where Phineus turn’d to shun the shield,

[040-347] Full in his face the staring head he held;

[040-348] As here and there he strove to turn aside,

[040-349] The wonder wrought; the man was petrified:

[040-350] All marble was his frame, his humid eyes

[040-351] Dropp’d tears, which hung upon the stone like ice;

[040-352] In suppliant posture, with uplifted hands,

[040-353] And fearful look, the guilty statue stands

[040-354] Hence Perseus to his native city hies,

[040-355] Victorious, and rewarded with his prize:

[040-356] Conquest, o’er Praetus the usurper, won,

[040-357] He reinstates his grandsire in the throne.

[040-358] Praetus his brother dispossess’d by might,

[040-359] His realm enjoy’d, and still detain’d his right:

[040-360] But Perseus pull’d the haughty tyrant down,

[040-361] And to the rightful king restored the throne;

[040-362] Weak was the usurper, as his cause was wrong:

[040-363] Where Gorgon’s head appears, what arms are strong?

[040-364] When Perseus to his host the monster held,

[040-365] They soon were statues, and their king expell’d.

[040-366] Thence to Seriphus with the head he sails,

[040-367] Whose prince his story treats as idle tales:

[040-368] Lord of a little isle, he scorns to seem

[040-369] Too credulous, but laughs at that and him;

[040-370] Yet did he not so much suspect the truth,

[040-371] As, out of pride or envy, hate the youth.

[040-372] The Argive prince, at his contempt enraged,

[040-373] To force his faith by fatal proof engaged:

[040-374] “Friends, shut your eyes,” he cries: his shield he takes,

[040-375] And to the king exposed Medusa’s snakes:

[040-376] The monarch felt the power he would not own,

[040-377] And stood convict of folly in the stone.
