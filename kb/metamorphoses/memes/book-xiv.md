## Themes

- **Transformation and Empire**: Book XIV traces the westward movement of myth from Greece to Italy. [[Aeneas]]'s voyage continues through a series of Italian metamorphoses, each marking a stage in the founding of what will become [[Rome]]. Ovid reads the *Aeneid* through a metamorphic lens: every stop is a transformation.
- **Love and Monstrosity**: [[Glaucus and Circe]], [[Scylla]] transformed to a sea-monster, [[Picus and Canens]], [[Vertumnus and Pomona]] — Book XIV intertwines love stories with political foundation, suggesting that desire and empire are woven from the same cloth.
- **From Myth to History**: The book closes with the deification of [[Aeneas]], the Alban kings, and the assumption of [[Romulus]] — the boundary between myth and Roman history dissolves. Metamorphosis becomes apotheosis, and apotheosis becomes state religion.

## Narrative Arc

Book XIV is the most Virgilian book of the poem, shadowing the *Aeneid* from Sicily to Latium. But where Virgil's epic is linear and teleological, Ovid's is digressive and metamorphic — the founding of Rome happens in the margins of transformation stories.

## Key Transformations

- [[Scylla]] → Sea-monster (rock/reef with dogs): poisoned by [[Circe]] in jealous rage at [[Glaucus]]'s love for Scylla (121-138)
- Cercopians → Apes: punished by Jupiter for perjury; their name preserved in Pithecusae (121-138)
- [[Picus]] → Woodpecker: refused Circe's love; she transformed him (121-138)
- [[Canens]] → Disembodied voice → Air: grief at Picus's disappearance; she dissolved into the wind (121-138)
- Trojan ships → Sea-nymphs: Cybele transforms the fleet to save them from Turnus's fire (121-138)
- [[Aeneas]] → God (Indiges): Venus obtains his apotheosis from Jupiter (121-138)
- [[Romulus]] → God (Quirinus): assumed into heaven during a storm; [[Hersilia]] follows as Hora (121-138)
