[021-001] Semele, becoming pregnant by [[jupiter|Jupiter]], is persuaded by Juno, in the disguise of an old nurse, to prove the divinity of her lover, by exacting an exhibition of the same majesty as when he approached Juno—The god, having sworn by Styx to grant whatever she required, is unable to withstand her solicitations, and the unfortunate Semele is reduced to ashes—The infant [[bacchus|Bacchus]] is rescued from destruction, and lodged in the thigh of Jupiter till the full period of his birth.

[021-002] Actaeon’s sufferings, and Diana’s rage,

[021-003] Did all the thoughts of men and gods engage;

[021-004] Some call’d the evils which Diana wrought

[021-005] Too great, and disproportion’d to the fault:

[021-006] Others, again, esteem’d Actaeon’s woes

[021-007] Fit for a virgin goddess to impose.

[021-008] The hearers into different parts divide,

[021-009] And reasons are produced on either side.

[021-010] Juno alone, of all that heard the news,

[021-011] Nor would condemn the goddess, nor excuse;

[021-012] She heeded not the justice of the deed,

[021-013] But joy’d to see the race of Cadmus bleed;

[021-014] For still she kept Europa in her mind,

[021-015] And, for her sake, detested all her kind.

[021-016] Besides, to aggravate her hate, she heard

[021-017] How Semele, to Jove’s embrace preferr’d,

[021-018] Was now grown big with an immortal load,

[021-019] And carried in her womb a future god.

[021-020] Thus, terribly incensed, the goddess broke

[021-021] To sudden fury, and abruptly spoke:

[021-022] “Are my reproaches of so small a force?

[021-023] ’Tis time I then pursue another course.

[021-024] It is decreed the guilty wretch shall die,

[021-025] If I’m indeed the mistress of the sky;

[021-026] If rightly styled, among the powers above,

[021-027] The wife and sister of the thundering Jove

[021-028] (And none can sure a sister’s right deny),

[021-029] It is decreed the guilty wretch shall die.

[021-030] She boasts an honour I can hardly claim,

[021-031] Pregnant she rises to a mother’s name;

[021-032] While proud and vain she triumphs in her Jove,

[021-033] And shows the glorious tokens of his love:

[021-034] But if I’m still the mistress of the skies,

[021-035] By her own lover the fond beauty dies.”

[021-036] This said, descending in a yellow cloud,

[021-037] Before the gates of Semele she stood.

[021-038] Old Beroe’s decrepit shape she wears,

[021-039] Her wrinkled visage, and her hoary hairs,

[021-040] While in her trembling gait she totters on,

[021-041] And learns to tattle in the nurse’s tone.

[021-042] The goddess thus disguised in age, beguiled

[021-043] With pleasing stories her false foster-child.

[021-044] Much did she talk of love, and when she came

[021-045] To mention to the nymph her lover’s name,

[021-046] Fetching a sigh, and holding down her head,

[021-047] “ ’Tis well,” says she, “if all be true that’s said.

[021-048] But trust me, child, I’m much inclined to fear

[021-049] Some counterfeit in this your Jupiter.

[021-050] Many an honest, well-designing maid,

[021-051] Has been by these pretended gods betray’d.

[021-052] But if he be indeed the thund’ring Jove,

[021-053] Bid him, when next he courts the rites of love,

[021-054] Descend triumphant, from the ethereal sky,

[021-055] In all the pomp of his divinity,

[021-056] Encompass’d round by those celestial charms

[021-057] With which he fills the immortal Juno’s arms.”

[021-058] The unwary nymph, insnared with what she said,

[021-059] Desired of Jove, when next he sought her bed,

[021-060] To grant a certain gift which she would choose.

[021-061] “Fear not,” replied the god, “that I’ll refuse

[021-062] Whate’er you ask: may Styx confirm my voice,

[021-063] Choose what you will, and you shall have your choice.”

[021-064] “Then,” says the nymph, “when next you seek my arms,

[021-065] May you descend in those celestial charms

[021-066] With which your Juno’s bosom you inflame,

[021-067] And fill with transport heaven’s immortal dame.”

[021-068] The god, surprised, would fain have stopp’d her voice,

[021-069] But he had sworn, and she had made her choice.

[021-070] To keep his promise he ascends, and shrouds

[021-071] His awful brow in whirlwinds and in clouds;

[021-072] While all around, in terrible array,

[021-073] His thunders rattle and his lightnings play;

[021-074] And yet the dazzling lustre to abate,

[021-075] He set not out in all his pomp and state,

[021-076] Clad in the mildest lightning of the skies,

[021-077] And arm’d with thunder of the smallest size:

[021-078] Not those huge bolts by which the giants slain

[021-079] Lay overthrown on the Phlegrean plain;

[021-080] ’Twas of a lesser mould and lighter weight,

[021-081] They call it thunder of a second rate;

[021-082] For the rough Cyclops, who by Jove’s command

[021-083] Temper’d the bolt, and turn’d it to his hand,

[021-084] Work’d up less flame and fury in its make,

[021-085] And quench’d it sooner in the standing lake.

[021-086] Thus dreadfully adorn’d with horror bright,

[021-087] The illustrious god, descending from his height,

[021-088] Came rushing on her in a storm of light.

[021-089] The mortal dame, too feeble to engage

[021-090] The lightning’s flashes and the thunder’s rage,

[021-091] Consumed amid the glories she desired,

[021-092] And in the terrible embrace expired.

[021-093] But to preserve his offspring from the tomb,

[021-094] Jove took him smoking from his mother’s womb,

[021-095] And, if on ancient tales we may rely,

[021-096] Enclosed the abortive infant in his thigh.

[021-097] Here when the babe had all his time fulfill’d,

[021-098] Ino first took him for her foster-child;

[021-099] Then the Niseans, in their dark abode,

[021-100] Nursed secretly with milk the thriving god.
