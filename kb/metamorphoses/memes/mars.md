# Mars

Mars (Greek: Ares), god of war, appears in the *Metamorphoses* not primarily as a battlefield deity but as a figure entangled in the personal dramas of Olympus. His affair with Venus, exposed by Vulcan's cunning net, is one of the poem's most famous scenes — a moment where the god of war is rendered helpless and ridiculous by love and craft. Later, his role as the divine father of Rome's founder elevates him from comic cuckold to the progenitor of an empire.

## Role in the Metamorphoses

Mars' most prominent appearance is in the tale of his affair with Venus — a story of divine infidelity exposed by Vulcan's ingenuity. The scene is one of Ovid's most irreverent treatments of the Olympians: the god of war, caught in a net of invisible chains, is stripped of his martial dignity and made a spectacle for the other gods. Yet the poem also honors Mars as the divine ancestor of Rome, whose intervention secures the apotheosis of Romulus.

### Key Episodes

- **The Affair with Venus**: Mars and Venus conduct their affair in secret until Vulcan, the cuckolded husband, forges an invisible net of bronze chains, trapping the lovers in the act and exposing them to the laughter of the assembled gods — a scene of divine humiliation and cosmic comedy (094-001)
- **Father of Rome**: Mars, as the father of Romulus by the Vestal Rhea Silvia, is the divine progenitor of the Roman people; his plea to Jupiter secures Romulus's deification (138-001)
- **The Wolf and Woodpecker**: Mars' sacred animals — the wolf and the woodpecker — serve as his earthly agents, nurturing the abandoned twins Romulus and Remus

## Transformation

Mars does not undergo metamorphosis himself, but his exposure in Vulcan's net is a kind of symbolic transformation — the god of war rendered passive, his martial body immobilized, his honor converted into public spectacle. In this, Ovid demonstrates that even the most violent of the gods is subject to the transformative power of shame and laughter.

- **Form**: Divine (Olympian) — physically unchanged but symbolically transformed by exposure
- **Agent**: Vulcan's craft transforms Mars from secret lover to public spectacle
- **Cause**: Adultery, divine rivalry, the triumph of art (Vulcan's chains) over strength (Mars' martial power)
- **Source**: (094-001, 138-001)
