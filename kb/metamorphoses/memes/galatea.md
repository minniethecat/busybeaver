# Galatea

A Nereid sea nymph in Ovid's *Metamorphoses*, Galatea is the unwilling object of the cyclops [[Polyphemus]]'s desire and the devoted lover of the shepherd [[Acis]]. When Polyphemus kills Acis in a jealous rage, Galatea transforms her beloved into a river-god, ensuring his immortality through metamorphosis.

## Role in the Metamorphoses

Galatea appears in Book XIII, where she narrates her own story to the sea goddess [[Scylla]]. Her tale forms one of the poem's most fully realized love triangles: the nymph, the monstrous cyclops who adores her, and the beautiful shepherd she loves in return (119-001).

### Key Episodes

- **Polyphemus's Courtship**: The cyclops Polyphemus, inflamed with passion for Galatea, sings an elaborate love song cataloguing his wealth, his pastoral domain, and — with grim irony — his single eye, which he compares to the sun. Galatea listens from her hiding place, unmoved and contemptuous (120-067).
- **The Death of Acis**: Discovering Galatea with Acis, Polyphemus tears a fragment of a mountainside and hurls it at the shepherd, crushing him beneath its weight. As Acis cries out for help, his blood begins to pale and thin into water (120-207).
- **Transformation into a River-God**: Galatea, unable to save Acis's life, exercises her divine power to change him into a fountain. The crushed rock cracks open, and a reed rises through the cleft, from which a stream of clear water bursts forth — Acis reborn as a river-god (120-207).

## Transformation

- **Form**: Mortal shepherd [[Acis]] → River-god / fountain
- **Agent**: Galatea (a sea nymph, acting through her divine heritage)
- **Cause**: Grief and love — unable to restore Acis to life, she grants him immortal form as a living stream
