# Glaucus

Glaucus, once a mortal fisherman of Boeotia, undergoes one of the poem's most marvelous transformations: after eating a mysterious herb that restores his catch to life, he finds himself drawn into the sea, where Oceanus and Tethys purge him of his mortality and make him a sea-god, his body adorned with a fish's tail. But his divine form brings no happiness — his love for the nymph [[Scylla]] is rejected, and when he seeks [[Circe]]'s aid, the enchantress falls in love with him instead, poisoning Scylla's bathing pool and transforming the innocent nymph into a monstrous rock-peril. (121-001)

## Role in the Metamorphoses

Glaucus's story, spanning Books XIII and XIV, is a tragic chain of desire and unintended consequence. Having become a sea deity, he glimpses the nymph [[Scylla (nymph)|Scylla]] bathing and is instantly enamored. He pleads his divine status — but Scylla flees, repelled by his hybrid form. Desperate, Glaucus travels to the island of [[Circe]] and begs her for a love charm. Circe, however, desires Glaucus for herself; when he rebuffs her, she turns her magic not on him but on her rival, transforming Scylla into a monster encircled by baying dogs. The innocent Scylla, who rejected no one but was caught between two divine passions, suffers the cruelest fate. (121-010)

### Key Episodes

- **The Fisherman's Transformation**: Glaucus, a mortal fisherman, notices his dying catch revive when laid upon a certain herb; tasting it himself, he is seized by an irresistible urge toward the sea and transformed into a sea deity (121-001)
- **The Love for Scylla**: Now a sea-god, Glaucus sees Scylla bathing and falls in love; he approaches her, describing his divine transformation, but she rejects his hybrid form and flees (121-027)
- **Circe's Jealousy**: Glaucus appeals to [[Circe]] for a love potion; Circe, smitten with Glaucus, offers herself instead — he refuses, declaring his love for Scylla will endure "till Scylla's self forget to be a maid" (122-001)
- **Scylla's Transformation**: In jealous rage, Circe poisons Scylla's favorite pool; the nymph wades in and finds her lower body transformed into snarling dogs — she becomes the monster of the strait, a perpetual danger to sailors (122-001)

## Transformation

Glaucus undergoes a **voluntary but irreversible** metamorphosis from mortal fisherman to sea god. The herb that first revives his dying fish also remakes him: his human form is purged by Oceanus and Tethys, replaced with a blue-green body, a fish's tail, and streaming sea-hair. His transformation is the poem's archetype of the sea-change — a metamorphosis wrought not by punishment or desire but by the inherent magic of the natural world.

- **Form**: Mortal fisherman → Sea deity (blue-green body, fish-tailed)
- **Agent**: A magical herb (initial), Oceanus and Tethys (purification)
- **Cause**: Accidental ingestion of the transformative herb; drawn by the sea's pull
- **Source**: (121-001)
