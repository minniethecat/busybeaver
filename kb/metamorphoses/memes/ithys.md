# Itys

Itys is the innocent child at the center of one of the *Metamorphoses*' most harrowing episodes. The son of the Thracian king Tereus and the Athenian princess Procne, Itys is murdered by his own mother and her sister Philomela, cooked, and served to his father as a feast of vengeance. His death — the killing of a child to punish a husband and father — transforms the entire family: Procne and Philomela become birds, Tereus becomes a hoopoe, and Itys himself, the only innocent in the tragedy, is memorialized in the eternal cry of the nightingale.

## Role in the Metamorphoses

Itys appears in Book 6, in the story of Tereus, Procne, and Philomela — one of Ovid's longest and darkest single narratives. Tereus rapes Philomela (his wife's sister) and cuts out her tongue to silence her. Philomela weaves her story into a tapestry and sends it to Procne. The sisters, reunited, exact their revenge: Procne kills her own son Itys — "the boy who called her mother" — and together they cook his body and feed it to the unsuspecting Tereus. When Tereus, realizing what he has eaten, pursues the sisters with his sword, all three are transformed into birds.

### Key Episodes

- **The Innocent Victim**: Itys is introduced as Procne's beloved son — a child whose resemblance to his father Tereus fills Procne with conflicted fury; "how like his father he is!" she thinks, and the resemblance seals his fate
- **The Murder**: Procne, "dragging Itys as a tigress drags a fawn," kills her son in a secluded part of the palace — the child reaching for his mother, calling her name, not understanding why she holds the knife
- **The Cannibal Feast**: The sisters cook Itys's body and serve it to Tereus; "Send for Itys!" Tereus calls, and Procne replies, "He is within" — the moment of horrifying revelation
- **The Transformation**: As Tereus pursues the sisters, all three are transformed: Procne into a swallow, Philomela into a nightingale, and Tereus into a hoopoe — Itys's death is rendered permanent in the birds' eternal forms

## Transformation

Itys's transformation is indirect — he is the victim whose death drives the metamorphosis of others. But his presence persists in the nightingale's song: Philomela's eternal lament for the child she helped to kill. Itys is thus transformed from a mortal boy into an eternal sound — grief made audible, innocence mourned forever in the birdsong of spring.

- **Form**: Mortal child → Cannibal meal → Eternal lament (in the nightingale's song)
- **Agent**: Procne (his mother) and Philomela (his aunt)
- **Cause**: Vengeance for Tereus's rape of Philomela; Itys is killed for resembling his father
