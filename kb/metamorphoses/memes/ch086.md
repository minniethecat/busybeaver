[076-001] Erisichthon impiously derides the worship of Ceres, whose groves he destroys.

[076-002] In various shapes thus to deceive the eyes,

[076-003] Without a settled stint of her disguise,

[076-004] Rash Erisichthon’s daughter had the power,

[076-005] And brought it to Autolycus in dower.

[076-006] Her atheist sire the slighted gods defied,

[076-007] And ritual honours to their shrines denied.

[076-008] As fame reports, his hand an axe sustain’d,

[076-009] Which Ceres’ consecrated grove profaned;

[076-010] Which durst the venerable gloom invade,

[076-011] And violate with light the awful shade.

[076-012] An ancient oak in the dark centre stood,

[076-013] The covert’s glory, and itself a wood:

[076-014] Garlands embraced its shaft, and from the boughs

[076-015] Hung tablets, monuments of prosp’rous vows.

[076-016] In the cool dusk its unpierced verdure spread,

[076-017] The dryads oft their hallow’d dances led;

[076-018] And oft, when round their gauging arms they cast,

[076-019] Full fifteen ells it measured in the waist:

[076-020] Its height all under-standards did surpass,

[076-021] As they aspired above the humbler grass.

[076-022] These motives, which would gentler minds restrain,

[076-023] Could not make Triope’s bold son abstain;

[076-024] He sternly charged his slaves with strict decree

[076-025] To fell with gashing steel the sacred tree.

[076-026] But while they, lingering, his commands delay’d,

[076-027] He snatch’d an axe, and thus blaspheming said:

[076-028] “Was this no oak, nor Ceres’ favourite care,

[076-029] But Ceres’ self, this arm, unawed, should dare

[076-030] Its leafy honours in the dust to spread,

[076-031] And level with the earth its airy head.”

[076-032] He spoke, and as he poised a slanting stroke,

[076-033] Sighs heaved, and tremblings shook the frighted oak:

[076-034] Its leaves look’d sickly, pale its acorns grew,

[076-035] And its long branches sweat a chilly dew.

[076-036] But when his impious hand a wound bestow’d,

[076-037] Blood from the mangled bark in currents flow’d.

[076-038] When a devoted bull of mighty size,

[076-039] A sinning nation’s grand atonement, dies,

[076-040] With such a plenty from the spouting veins,

[076-041] A crimson stream the turfy altars stains.

[076-042] The wonder all amazed; yet one more bold,

[076-043] The fact dissuading, strove his axe to hold.

[076-044] But the Thessalian, obstinately bent,

[076-045] Too proud to change, too harden’d to repent,

[076-046] On his kind monitor his eyes, which burn’d

[076-047] With rage, and with his eyes his weapon turn’d:

[076-048] “Take the reward,” says he, “of pious dread:”

[076-049] Then with a blow lopp’d off his parted head.

[076-050] No longer check’d, the wretch his crime pursu’d,

[076-051] Doubled his strokes, and sacrilege renew’d;

[076-052] When from the groaning trunk a voice was heard:

[076-053] “A dryad I, by Ceres’ love preferr’d,

[076-054] Within the circle of this clasping rind

[076-055] Coeval grew, and now in ruin join’d:

[076-056] But instant vengeance shall thy sin pursue,

[076-057] And death is cheer’d with this prophetic view.”

[076-058] At last the oak with cords enforced to bow,

[076-059] Strain’d from the top, and sapp’d with wounds be low,

[076-060] The humbler wood, partaker of its fate,

[076-061] Crush’d with its fall, and shiver’d with its weight.

[076-062] The grove destroy’d, the sister dryads moan,

[076-063] Grieved at its loss, and frighted at their own.

[076-064] Straight suppliants for revenge to Ceres go,

[076-065] In sable weeds, expressive of their wo.

[076-066] The beauteous goddess with a graceful air

[076-067] Bow’d in consent, and nodded to their prayer.

[076-068] The awful motion shook the fruitful ground,

[076-069] And waved the fields with golden harvests crown’d.

[076-070] Soon she contrived in her projecting mind

[076-071] A plague severe, and piteous in its kind

[076-072] (If plagues for crimes of such presumptuous height

[076-073] Could pity in the softest breast create);

[076-074] With pinching want, and hunger’s keenest smart,

[076-075] To tear his vitals, and corrode his heart.

[076-076] But since her near approach by [[fate|Fate]]’s denied

[076-077] To Famine, and broad climes their powers divide,

[076-078] A nymph, the mountain’s ranger, she address’d,

[076-079] And, thus resolved, her high commands express’d.
