# Actaeon

Actaeon, grandson of Cadmus and a devoted hunter, suffers one of the most famous and unsettling transformations in Ovid's *Metamorphoses*: having accidentally wandered into a grotto where Diana and her nymphs are bathing, he is punished not for any crime or intention, but for the sheer misfortune of seeing what no mortal should see. Turned into a stag, he is hunted down and torn apart by his own pack of hounds — a death that Ovid renders in excruciating detail, forcing the reader to confront the question of whether such a punishment can ever be called just.

## Role in the Metamorphoses

Actaeon's story appears in Book III, following the founding of Thebes by his grandfather Cadmus. After a successful morning's hunt, Actaeon wanders away from his companions and stumbles upon the grotto of Gargaphia, a secluded valley sacred to Diana. The goddess, naked and bathing with her attendant nymphs, is enraged at being seen by a mortal male. Unable to reach her arrows in time, she splashes water onto Actaeon's face with the words: "Now you may tell that you saw me naked — if you can tell at all." Antlers sprout from his forehead, his hands become hooves, and his skin turns to a dappled hide. Most cruelly, his human consciousness remains within the animal form, aware of what he has become yet unable to speak — when he tries to call out to his hunting companions, no human voice emerges (020-001). His own hounds — Ovid names over thirty of them in a virtuosic catalogue — catch his scent and pursue him. His companions, not recognizing their master, urge the pack on. Actaeon is pulled down and torn to pieces, and only then is Diana's anger finally appeased.

### Key Episodes

- **The Accidental Glimpse**: After a morning's hunt, Actaeon wanders into the grotto of Gargaphia where Diana bathes, seeing the goddess naked purely by chance (020-001)
- **The Transformation**: Diana splashes water onto Actaeon, turning him into a stag while preserving his human consciousness
- **The Hunt**: Actaeon's own hounds — whom Ovid names in an extended catalogue of over thirty dogs — pursue and catch him
- **The Death**: Unable to speak or gesture to identify himself, Actaeon is torn apart by his hounds while his companions cheer the kill

## Transformation

- **Form**: Man (Theban hunter) → Stag
- **Agent**: Diana, goddess of the hunt
- **Cause**: Punishment for the accidental glimpse of the goddess bathing — an act of fortune rather than intent, making this one of the poem's most troubling examples of divine justice
- **Source**: (020-001)
