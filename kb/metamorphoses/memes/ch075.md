[066-001] King Aeacus relates to Cephalus that a pestilence having formerly depopulated his territories, he entreated [[jupiter|Jupiter]] to repeople his kingdom—His request was granted, and, according to his desire, all the ants in an old oak were changed into men, and called by Aeacus Myrmidons, from a Greek word signifying an ant.

[066-002] “A dreadful plague from angry Juno came,

[066-003] To scourge the land that bore her rival’s name.

[066-004] Before her fatal anger was reveal’d,

[066-005] And teeming malice lay as yet conceal’d,

[066-006] All remedies we try, all med’cines use,

[066-007] Which nature could supply, or art produce;

[066-008] The unconquer’d foe derides the vain design,

[066-009] And art and nature foil’d, declare the cause divine.

[066-010] “At first we only felt the oppressive weight

[066-011] Of gloomy clouds, then teeming with our fate,

[066-012] And lab’ring to discharge unactive heat:

[066-013] But ere four moons alternate changes knew,

[066-014] With deadly blasts the fatal south wind blew,

[066-015] Infected all the air, and poison’d as it flew

[066-016] Our fountains too a dire infection yield,

[066-017] For crowds of vipers creep along the field,

[066-018] And, with polluted gore, and baneful steams,

[066-019] Taint all the lakes, and venom all the streams.

[066-020] “The young disease with milder force began,

[066-021] And raged on birds and beasts, excusing man.

[066-022] The lab’ring oxen fall before the plough;

[066-023] The unhappy ploughmen stare, and wonder how:

[066-024] The tabid sheep, with sickly bleatings, pines,

[066-025] Its wool decreasing as its strength declines:

[066-026] The warlike steed, by inward foes compell’d,

[066-027] Neglects his honours, and deserts the field,

[066-028] Unnerved and languid, seeks a base retreat,

[066-029] And at the manger groans, but wish’d a nobler fate:

[066-030] The stags forget their speed, the boars their rage,

[066-031] Nor can the bears the stronger herds engage;

[066-032] A general faintness does invade them all,

[066-033] And in the woods and fields promiscuously they fall

[066-034] The air receives the stench, and, strange to say,

[066-035] The rav’nous birds and beasts avoid the prey;

[066-036] The offensive bodies rot upon the ground,

[066-037] And spread the dire contagion all around.

[066-038] “But now the plague, grown to a larger size,

[066-039] Riots on man, and scorns a meaner prize.

[066-040] Intestine heats begin the civil war,

[066-041] And flushings first the latent flame declare,

[066-042] And breath inspired, which seem’d like fiery air.

[066-043] Their black dry tongues are swell’d, and scarce can move,

[066-044] And short thick sighs from panting lungs are drove;

[066-045] They gape for air, with flattering hopes to abate

[066-046] Their raging flames, but that augments their heat.

[066-047] No bed, no covering, can the wretches bear,

[066-048] But on the ground, exposed to open air,

[066-049] They lie, and hope to find a pleasing coolness there.

[066-050] The suffering earth, with that oppression cursed,

[066-051] Returns the heat which they imparted first.

[066-052] “In vain physicians would bestow their aid,

[066-053] Vain all their art, and useless all their trade;

[066-054] And they, even they, who fleeting life recall,

[066-055] Feel the same powers, and undistinguish’d fall.

[066-056] If any proves so daring to attend

[066-057] His sick companion, or his darling friend,

[066-058] The officious wretch sucks in contagious breath,

[066-059] And with his friend does sympathize in death.

[066-060] “And now the care and hopes of life are pass’d,

[066-061] They please their fancies and indulge their taste:

[066-062] At brooks and streams, regardless of their shame,

[066-063] Each sex, promiscuous, strives to quench their flame;

[066-064] Nor do they strive in vain to quench it there,

[066-065] For thirst and life at once extinguish’d are.

[066-066] Thus in the brooks the dying bodies sink,

[066-067] But heedless still the rash survivers drink.

[066-068] “So much uneasy down the wretches hate,

[066-069] They fly their beds, to struggle with their fate,

[066-070] But if decaying strength forbids to rise,

[066-071] The victim crawls and rolls, till on the ground he lies:

[066-072] Each shuns his bed as each would shun his tomb,

[066-073] And thinks the infection only lodged at home.

[066-074] “Here one, with fainting steps, does slowly creep

[066-075] O’er heaps of dead, and straight augments the heap:

[066-076] Another, while his strength and tongue prevail’d,

[066-077] Bewails his friend, and falls himself, bewail’d:

[066-078] This, with imploring looks, surveys the skies,

[066-079] The last dear office of his closing eyes,

[066-080] But finds the heavens implacable, and dies.

[066-081] “What now, ah, what! empioy’d my troubled mind,

[066-082] But only hopes my subjects’ fate to find?

[066-083] What place soe’er my weeping eyes survey,

[066-084] There in lamented heaps the vulgar lay;

[066-085] As acorns scatter when the winds prevail,

[066-086] Or mellow fruit from shaken branches fall.

[066-087] “You see that dome which rears its front so high.

[066-088] ’Tis sacred to the monarch of the sky:

[066-089] How many there, with unregarded tears,

[066-090] And fruitless vows, sent up successless prayers!

[066-091] There fathers for expiring sons implored,

[066-092] And there the wife bewail’d her gasping lord:

[066-093] With pious offerings they appease the skies,

[066-094] But they, ere yet the atoning vapours rise,

[066-095] Before the altars fall, themselves a sacrifice;

[066-096] They fall while yet their hands the gums contain,

[066-097] Their gums surviving, but their offerer’s slain.

[066-098] “The destined ox, with holy garlands crown’d,

[066-099] Prevents the blow, and feels an unexpected wound.

[066-100] When I myself invoked the powers divine,

[066-101] To drive the fatal pest from me and mine:

[066-102] When now the priest with hands uplifted stood,

[066-103] Prepared to strike, and shed the sacred blood,

[066-104] The gods themselves the mortal stroke bestow,

[066-105] The victim falls, but they impart the blow:

[066-106] Scarce was the knife with the pale purple stain’d,

[066-107] And no presages could be then obtain’d,

[066-108] From putrid entrails, where the infection reign’d.

[066-109] “Death stalk’d around with such resistless sway,

[066-110] The temples of the gods his force obey,

[066-111] And suppliants feel his stroke while yet they pray.

[066-112] ‘Go now,’ said he, ‘your deities implore

[066-113] For fruitless aid, for I defy their power;’

[066-114] Then with a cursed, malicious joy survey’d

[066-115] The very altars, stain’d with trophies of the dead.

[066-116] “The rest grown mad, and frantic with despair,

[066-117] Urge their own fate, and so prevent the fear.

[066-118] Strange madness that, when death pursued so fast,

[066-119] To anticipate the blow with impious haste.

[066-120] “No decent honours to their urns are paid,

[066-121] Nor could the graves receive the numerous dead;

[066-122] For, or they lay unburied on the ground,

[066-123] Or, unadorn’d, a needy funeral found:

[066-124] All reverence past, the fainting wretches fight

[066-125] For funeral piles which were another’s right.

[066-126] Unmourn’d they fall, for who survived to mourn?

[066-127] And sires and mothers unlamented burn;

[066-128] Parents and sons sustain an equal fate,

[066-129] And wandering ghosts their kindred shadows meet:

[066-130] The dead a larger space of ground require,

[066-131] Nor are the trees sufficient for the fire.

[066-132] “Despairing under grief’s oppressive weight,

[066-133] And sunk by these tempestuous blasts of fate,

[066-134] ‘O Jove,’ said I, ‘if common fame says true,

[066-135] If e’er Aegina gave those joys to you,

[066-136] If e’er you lay enclosed in her embrace,

[066-137] Fond of her charms, and eager to possess;

[066-138] O father, if you do not yet disclaim

[066-139] Paternal care, nor yet disown the name,

[066-140] Grant my petitions, and with speed restore

[066-141] My subjects numerous as they were before,

[066-142] Or make me partner of the fate they bore.’

[066-143] I spoke, and glorious lightning shone around,

[066-144] And rattling thunder gave a prosperous sound:

[066-145] ‘So let it be, and may these omens prove

[066-146] A pledge,’ said I, ‘of your returning love.’

[066-147] “By chance a reverend oak was near the place,

[066-148] Sacred to Jove, and of Dodona’s race,

[066-149] Where frugal ants laid up their winter meat,

[066-150] Whose little bodies bear a mighty weight:

[066-151] We saw them march along, and hide their store,

[066-152] And much admired their number and their power;

[066-153] Admired at first, but after envied more.

[066-154] Full of amazement, thus to Jove I pray’d:

[066-155] ‘O grant, since thus my subjects are decay’d,

[066-156] As many subjects to supply the dead.’

[066-157] I pray’d, and strange convulsions moved the oak,

[066-158] Which murmur’d, though by ambient winds unshook:

[066-159] My trembling hands and stiff-erected hair

[066-160] Express’d all tokens of uncommon fear;

[066-161] Yet both the earth and sacred oak I kiss’d,

[066-162] And scarce could hope, yet still I hoped the best;

[066-163] For wretches, whatsoe’er [[fate|the Fates]] divine,

[066-164] Expound all omens to their own design.

[066-165] “But now ’twas night, when even distraction wears

[066-166] A pleasing look, and dreams beguile our cares:

[066-167] Lo! the same oak appears before my eyes,

[066-168] Nor alter’d in his shape nor former size;

[066-169] As many ants the numerous branches bear,

[066-170] The same their labour and their frugal care;

[066-171] The branches too a like commotion found,

[066-172] And shook the industrious creatures on the ground,

[066-173] Who by degrees (what’s scarce to be believed)

[066-174] A nobler form and larger bulk received,

[066-175] And on the earth walk’d an unusual pace,

[066-176] With manly strides and an erected face:

[066-177] Their numerous legs and former colour lost,

[066-178] The insects could a human figure boast.

[066-179] “I wake, and, waking, find my cares again,

[066-180] And to the unperforming gods complain,

[066-181] And call their promise and pretences vain.

[066-182] Yet in my court I heard the murm’ring voice

[066-183] Of strangers, and a mix’d, uncommon noise:

[066-184] But I suspected all was still a dream,

[066-185] Till Telamon to my apartment came,

[066-186] Opening the door with an impetuous haste—

[066-187] ‘O come,’ said he, ‘and see your faith and hopes surpass’d.’

[066-188] I follow, and, confused with wonder, view

[066-189] Those shapes which my presaging slumbers drew:

[066-190] I saw, and own’d, and call’d them subjects; they

[066-191] Confess’d my power, submissive to my sway.

[066-192] To Jove, restorer of my race decay’d,

[066-193] My vows were first with due oblations paid;

[066-194] I then divide, with an impartial hand,

[066-195] My empty city, and my ruin’d land,

[066-196] To give the newborn youth an equal share,

[066-197] And call them Myrmidons, from what they were.

[066-198] You saw their persons, and they still retain

[066-199] The thrift of ants, though now transform’d to men;

[066-200] A frugal people, and inured to sweat,

[066-201] Lab’ring to gain, and keeping what they get.

[066-202] These, equal both in strength and years, shall join

[066-203] Their willing aid, and follow your design,

[066-204] With the first southern gale that shall present

[066-205] To fill your sails, and favour your intent.”

[066-206] With such discourse they entertain the day;

[066-207] The evening pass’d in banquets, sport, and play;

[066-208] Then, having crown’d the night with sweet repose,

[066-209] Aurora (with the wind at east) arose.

[066-210] Now [[minerva|Pallas]]’ sons to Cephalus resort,

[066-211] And Cephalus with Pallas’ sons to court,

[066-212] To the king’s levee; him sleep’s silken chain

[066-213] And pleasing dreams beyond his hour detain;

[066-214] But then the princes of the blood, in state,

[066-215] Expect and meet them at the palace gate.
