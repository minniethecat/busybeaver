# Arachne

Arachne, a mortal weaver of humble Lydian origins, is the protagonist of the *Metamorphoses*' most ethically complex transformation. Her challenge to Minerva — woven into a tapestry depicting twenty-one scenes of divine seduction — raises questions about artistic merit, divine justice, and the relationship between craft and power that Ovid deliberately leaves unresolved.

## Role in the Metamorphoses

Arachne appears in Book VI in a single extended episode. When her weaving skill attracts the attention of the nymphs — and the jealous notice of Minerva — the goddess appears disguised as an old woman and warns Arachne to yield. Arachne refuses. The contest that follows produces two tapestries: Minerva's depicts the gods in majesty punishing mortal presumption; Arachne's depicts twenty-one instances of divine seduction and rape — a catalogue of the very transformations that fill the *Metamorphoses*. Minerva, finding no flaw in Arachne's work but enraged by its content, tears the tapestry and strikes Arachne with her shuttle. When Arachne attempts to hang herself in despair, Minerva transforms her into a spider, condemned to weave forever.

### Key Episodes

- **The Challenge**: Minerva, disguised as an old woman, warns Arachne not to challenge a goddess; Arachne refuses, and the weaving contest begins (052-001)
- **Two Tapestries**: Minerva weaves the official divine narrative — gods triumphant over mortals. Arachne weaves the suppressed narrative — gods seducing, deceiving, and assaulting mortal women (052-001)
- **The Transformation**: Minerva, finding Arachne's work technically flawless, tears the tapestry and strikes the mortal with her shuttle; Arachne hangs herself; Minerva transforms her into a spider — the weaver condemned to weave forever (052-001)

## Transformation

Arachne's transformation — into a spider (*arachne* in Greek) — is the poem's most transparent instance of **poetic justice**: the weaver becomes the creature defined by weaving. But the justice is undercut by the circumstances: Arachne's tapestry is superior, her depiction of the gods is accurate, and her punishment is for content rather than quality.

- **Form**: Human weaver → Spider
- **Agent**: Minerva (goddess of weaving)
- **Cause**: Artistic hubris; depiction of divine misdeeds
