# Charybdis

Charybdis is the personified whirlpool of the Strait of Messina, an insatiable maritime hazard who, according to Ovid, was once a mortal woman transformed by the gods as punishment for her ravenous greed. Paired eternally with Scylla — the six-headed monster lurking in a cave across the strait — Charybdis swallows the sea three times a day and vomits it forth again, making the narrow passage between her vortex and Scylla's lair one of the most dangerous navigational challenges in ancient geography. In the *Metamorphoses*, her brief but vivid appearances link the poem's geography of transformation to the actual maritime world of the Mediterranean, blurring the line between myth and navigational reality.

## Role in the Metamorphoses

Charybdis appears as a geographical and mythological constant in the poem's later books, referenced wherever sea voyages pass through the Strait of Messina. According to Ovid's account, she was once a mortal woman of insatiable appetite — in some versions she stole the cattle of Hercules — and her transformation into a whirlpool by Jupiter (or Neptune) literalized her greed: condemned to eternally swallow and disgorge the sea, her bodily hunger became a geographical feature. She is most prominently paired with Scylla, the nymph-turned-monster transformed by Circe's jealous magic, creating the famous navigational dilemma described in the poem: to escape Charybdis, a ship must steer closer to Scylla, and vice versa. In Book XIV, when Aeneas's fleet sails past the twin hazards, they survive only through the timely intervention of the transformed Trojan ships (now sea-nymphs) who push them clear of the vortex. Charybdis thus embodies the metamorphic principle at its most elemental: a person reduced entirely to their dominant trait, their body replaced by the geography of their sin.

### Key Episodes

- **The Original Theft and Transformation**: Charybdis, a mortal woman of insatiable greed, is transformed by Jupiter's lightning into a whirlpool — her hunger made permanent and geographical
- **The Paired Hazard with Scylla**: Together with the monster Scylla, Charybdis forms the deadly navigational choke-point of the Sicilian Strait
- **Aeneas's Passage**: In Book XIV, Aeneas's fleet nearly falls victim to Charybdis before being rescued by sea-nymphs
