## Themes

- **Love as Magic, Magic as Transformation**: [[Medea]]'s love for [[Jason]] is described as a disease, and her magic is both its symptom and its cure. From rejuvenating [[Aeson]] to murdering [[Pelias]] to escaping in a dragon-drawn chariot, Medea's transformations are willed, not suffered — a mortal who has mastered metamorphosis itself.

- **Internal versus External Change**: Jason asks Medea only to make his father young; she offers to give him years of her own life. The distinction between borrowed time and renewed time, between magic that heals and magic that kills, runs through every episode.

- **Aeacus and the Plague**: The Myrmidon story — ants transformed into men to repopulate Aegina after a plague — is one of Ovid's most vivid set-pieces, a miniature epic of cosmic destruction and miraculous rebirth.

## Narrative Arc

Book VII is divided into Medea's arc (Jason, Aeson, Pelias, escape) and the Athenian sequence (Theseus, Aeacus, Cephalus). Medea is the poem's first fully realized tragic figure — her soliloquy in Book VII is Ovid's most extended psychological portrait. After she flies away, the book shifts to more conventional heroic and erotic material, but the afterimage of her dragon-chariot lingers.

## Key Transformations

- [[Aeson]] → Young man: Medea's potion drains years from his veins and replaces them with youth (061-066)
- [[Pelias]] → Dead (deceived daughters fail to rejuvenate): Medea's trick: she shows the daughters a false ritual, they stab their father (061-066)
- Ants → Men (the Myrmidons): Jupiter answers Aeacus's prayer after the plague (061-066)
- [[Cephalus]]'s javelin → Always hits its mark: a gift from Procris, inadvertently fatal when Cephalus mistakes her for prey (061-066)
