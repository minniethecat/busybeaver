[137-001] Romulus, having restored his grandfather Numitor to the throne of which he had been unjustly dispossessed by his brother Amulius, at length succeeds to the crown.

[137-002] Now Procas yielding to the fates, his son

[137-003] Mild Numitor succeeded to the crown.

[137-004] But false Amulius, with a lawless power,

[137-005] At length deposed his brother Numitor.

[137-006] Then Ilia’s valiant issue, with the sword,

[137-007] Her parent re-enthroned, the rightful lord.

[137-008] Next Romulus to people Rome contrives;

[137-009] The joyous time of Pales’ feast arrives;

[137-010] He gives the word to seize the Sabine wives.

[137-011] The sires enraged take arms, by Tatius led,

[137-012] Bold to revenge their violated bed.

[137-013] A fort there was, not yet unknown to fame,

[137-014] Call’d the Tarpeian, its commander’s name.

[137-015] This by the false Tarpeia was betray’d,

[137-016] But death well recompensed the treacherous maid.

[137-017] The foe on this new-bought success relies,

[137-018] And, silent, march, the city to surprise.

[137-019] Saturnia’s arts with Sabine arms combine;

[137-020] But [[venus|Venus]] countermines the vain design;

[137-021] Entreats the nymphs that o’er the springs preside,

[137-022] Which near the fane of hoary Janus glide,

[137-023] To send their succours; every urn they drain,

[137-024] To stop the Sabines’ progress, but in vain.

[137-025] The Naiads now more stratagems essay,

[137-026] And kindling sulphur to each source convey.

[137-027] The floods ferment, hot exhalations rise,

[137-028] Till from the scalding ford the army flies.

[137-029] Soon Romulus appears in shining arms,

[137-030] And to the war the Roman legions warms:

[137-031] The battle rages, and the field is spread

[137-032] With nothing but the dying and the dead.

[137-033] Both sides consent to treat without delay,

[137-034] And their two chiefs at once the sceptre sway.

[137-035] But Tatius by Lavinian fury slain,

[137-036] Great Romulus continued long to reign.
