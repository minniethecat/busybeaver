# Dryope

A woman of exceptional beauty, transformed into a lotus-tree by the [[Lotis|sylvan deities]] as punishment for unwittingly plucking a branch from a sacred tree, while her infant son and sister look on in helpless witness.

## Role in the Metamorphoses

Dryope's story appears in Book IX of the *Metamorphoses*, narrated by [[Iole]] to [[Alcmena]]. It is one of the poem's gentlest yet most unsettling transformations: a young mother, walking by a pool with her infant son, plucks a blossom from a lotus-tree to amuse the child, unaware that the tree is the transformed body of the nymph [[Lotis]]. The tree bleeds where she breaks the branch, and the angry sylvan deities turn Dryope herself into a tree. Her final words are a plea that her son be brought to play beneath her branches. (085-001)

### Key Episodes

- **A Beauty Surpassed Only by One**: Before her transformation, Dryope was renowned for her beauty; only her sister [[Iole]] was more beautiful. She married Andraemon and bore a son, Amphissus. (085-009)
- **The Fatal Blossom**: Walking by a lotus-pool with her infant son in her arms, Dryope plucks a purple flower from a lotus-tree for the child to play with. The broken stem drips blood — the tree is the transformed nymph Lotis, who had fled Priapus's advance by becoming the tree. (085-001)
- **The Transformation**: As Dryope tries to flee, her feet take root. Bark climbs her legs, then her torso, then her arms. Only her face remains human long enough for her to speak a farewell to her father, husband, and sister, and to beg that her son remember this tree as his mother. (085-059, 085-060)

## Transformation

- **Form**: Mortal woman → Lotus-tree
- **Agent**: The sylvan deities (nymphs and woodland gods)
- **Cause**: Unknowing desecration of a sacred tree (the transformed nymph Lotis); Dryope plucked a branch without knowing the tree was a transformed being
- **Source**: (085-001)
