[126-001] [[apollo|Apollo]] becomes enamoured of the sibyl, and offers to grant whatever she asks—The request is made of a continuance of life for as many years as there are grains in a heap of sand; but the enjoyment of health and beauty are unfortunately forgotten by the applicant.

[126-002] “I am no deity,” replied the dame,

[126-003] “But mortal, and religious rites disclaim,

[126-004] Yet had avoided death’s tyrannic sway,

[126-005] Had I consented to the god of day.

[126-006] With promises he sought my love, and said:

[126-007] ‘Have all you wish, my fair Cumaean maid.’

[126-008] I paused: then pointing to a heap of sand,

[126-009] ‘For every grain, to live a year demand.’

[126-010] But, ah! unmindful of the effect of time,

[126-011] Forgot to covenant for youth and prime.

[126-012] The smiling bloom I boasted once is gone

[126-013] And feeble age with lagging limbs creeps on.

[126-014] Seven centuries have I lived; three more fulfil

[126-015] The period of the years to finish still.

[126-016] Who’ll think that Phoebus, dress’d in youth divine,

[126-017] Had once believed his lustre less than mine?

[126-018] This wither’d frame (so fates have will’d) shall waste

[126-019] To nothing but prophetic words at last.”

[126-020] The sibyl mounting now from nether skies,

[126-021] And the famed Ilian prince at Cuma rise.

[126-022] He sail’d, and near the place to anchor came,

[126-023] Since call’d Cajeta from his nurse’s name;

[126-024] Here did the luckless Macareus, a friend

[126-025] To wise Ulysses, his long labours end;

[126-026] Here, wandering, Achaemenides he meets,

[126-027] And, sudden, thus his late associate greets:

[126-028] “Whence came you here, oh friend, and whither bound?

[126-029] All gave you lost on fair cyclopean ground;

[126-030] A Greek’s at last aboard a Trojan found.”
