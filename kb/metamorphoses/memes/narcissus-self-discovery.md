# Narcissus and His Reflection

The encounter of Narcissus with his own reflected image is the *Metamorphoses*' definitive exploration of self-knowledge as fatal transformation. Tiresias's prophecy — that the boy will live long "if he never knows himself" (022-003) — is fulfilled with tragic irony when Narcissus bends to drink from a pool and falls in love with the beautiful face he sees, not knowing it is his own.

## Characters

| Character | Role |
|-----------|------|
| Narcissus | The beautiful youth, son of Cephisus and Liriope, who falls in love with his own image |
| Echo | The nymph cursed to repeat others' words, who loves Narcissus and is rejected |
| Tiresias | The blind seer whose prophecy frames the tragedy |

## Setting

A clear, silver pool in a woodland grove — untouched by shepherds, animals, or fallen leaves. The stillness of the water is essential: only perfect stillness enables the fatal reflection.

## Narrative

Returning from the hunt, thirsty, Narcissus bends to drink from a pool and sees a face — the most beautiful he has ever seen. He reaches for it; the water ripples; the image vanishes. He weeps; the image weeps back. He pleads with the image to come to him, not understanding that he is separated from himself by the thinnest possible barrier — the surface of the water. As realization dawns — "This is myself!" — it comes too late. He beats his breast, tears at his clothes, and wastes away. The nymphs prepare a funeral pyre but find only a flower: white petals, yellow center, the narcissus.

## The Transformation

| Aspect | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| **Original Form** | Beautiful youth |
| **Transformed Form** | Narcissus flower (*Narcissus poeticus*) |
| **Agent** | Self-inflicted — the inability to leave his own reflection |
| **Cause** | Self-love; the fatal fulfillment of Tiresias's prophecy |
| **Source** | (023-001, 026-022) |

## Themes

- **Self-knowledge as death**: The prophecy that Narcissus will live if he never knows himself is fulfilled through its violation
- **The impossibility of self-love**: Narcissus's desire is eternally frustrated by the surface that both shows and separates
- **Echo as double**: Echo's fate — reduced to pure repetition — mirrors Narcissus's reduction to pure image
- **Art as reflection**: The pool is nature's mirror, the flower nature's monument
