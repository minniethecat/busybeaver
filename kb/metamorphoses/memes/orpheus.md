# Orpheus

Orpheus, son of Apollo (or Oeagrus) and the Muse Calliope, is the poet-hero of the *Metamorphoses* — the figure whose art most closely rivals Ovid's own. His descent to the Underworld to recover his wife Eurydice, his songs that move the natural world, and his violent death at the hands of the Bacchic Maenads make him the poem's most extended meditation on the power and limits of art.

## Role in the Metamorphoses

Orpheus dominates the central portion of Book X and the opening of Book XI in an extended narrative that is itself a series of embedded songs. After losing Eurydice on the threshold of the upper world — the backward glance that undoes his triumph over death — Orpheus retreats to the wilds of Thrace and sings the tales that fill Book X: Ganymede, Hyacinthus, Pygmalion, Myrrha, Venus and Adonis. His death (Book XI) at the hands of the Maenads — who tear him apart because his songs of love for boys offend their Bacchic heterosexuality — returns him to the Underworld where he is reunited with Eurydice.

### Key Episodes

- **Descent to the Underworld**: Orpheus's music moves Pluto and Proserpina to release Eurydice on the condition that he not look back — the backward glance loses her forever (100-001)
- **The Songs of Book X**: Grieving, Orpheus sings the tales that comprise Book X — including Pygmalion, Myrrha, Venus and Adonis, and Atalanta — creating art from loss (100-001)
- **Death of Orpheus**: The Maenads, enraged by Orpheus's turn to love of boys after Eurydice's death, tear him apart; his head, still singing, floats down the Hebrus River (111-001)
- **Reunion with Eurydice**: Orpheus's shade descends to the Underworld and finds Eurydice — this time, no backward glance is needed (111-001)

## Transformation

Orpheus undergoes no bodily transformation — his metamorphosis is through **art and death**. His songs transform the natural world (trees gather to hear him, stones soften) and his dismemberment transforms him from living artist to disembodied voice — the head that floats down the Hebrus "still calling on Eurydice." This final image makes Orpheus a figure of art's persistence beyond bodily dissolution.
