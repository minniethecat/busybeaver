[136-001] The disguised Vertumnus cautions his mistress from the indulgence of an unfeeling disregard to the sufferings of her lover by the example of Anaxarete, who is converted into a statue as a punishment for her pride—The god then resumes his natural shape, and Pomona renounces her prepossessions in favour of a single life.

[136-002] “Iphis, of vulgar birth, by chance had view’d

[136-003] Fair Anaxarete of Teucer’s blood.

[136-004] Not long had he beheld the royal dame

[136-005] Ere the bright sparkle kindled into flame.

[136-006] Oft did he struggle with a just despair,

[136-007] Unfix’d to ask, unable to forbear.

[136-008] But love, who flatters still his own disease,

[136-009] Hopes all things will succeed he knows will please

[136-010] Where’er the fair one haunts, he hovers there,

[136-011] And seeks her confidant with sighs, and prayer;

[136-012] Or letters he conveys, that seldom prove

[136-013] Successless messengers in suits of love.

[136-014] “Now shivering at her gates the wretch appears,

[136-015] And myrtle garlands on the columns rears,

[136-016] Wet with a deluge of unbidden tears.

[136-017] The nymph, more hard than rocks, more deaf than seas,

[136-018] Derides his prayers, insults his agonies;

[136-019] Arraigns of insolence the aspiring swain,

[136-020] And takes a cruel pleasure in his pain.

[136-021] Resolved at last to finish his despair,

[136-022] He thus upbraids the inexorable fair:

[136-023] “ ‘Oh, Anaxarete, at last forget

[136-024] The license of a passion indiscreet.

[136-025] Now triumph, since a welcome sacrifice

[136-026] Your slave prepares to offer to your eyes.

[136-027] My life, without reluctance, I resign;

[136-028] That present best can please a pride like thine.

[136-029] But, oh! forbear to blast a flame so bright,

[136-030] [[fate|Doom]]’d never to expire but with the light.

[136-031] And you, great powers, do justice to my name;

[136-032] The hours, you take from life, restore to fame.’

[136-033] “Then o’er the posts, once hung with wreaths, he throws

[136-034] The ready cord, and fits the fatal noose;

[136-035] For death prepares; and bounding from above,

[136-036] At once the wretch concludes his life and love.

[136-037] “Ere long the people gather, and the dead

[136-038] Is to his mourning mother’s arms convey’d.

[136-039] First, like some ghastly statue she appears;

[136-040] Then bathes the breathless corse in seas of tears,

[136-041] And gives it to the pile; now as the throng

[136-042] Proceed in sad solemnity along,

[136-043] To view the passing pomp the cruel fair

[136-044] Hastes, and beholds her breathless lover there.

[136-045] Struck with the sight, inanimate she seems;

[136-046] Set are her eyes, and motionless her limbs;

[136-047] Her features without fire, her colour gone,

[136-048] And, like her heart, she hardens into stone.

[136-049] In Salamis the statue still is seen,

[136-050] In the famed temple of the Cyprian queen.

[136-051] Warn’d by this tale, no longer then disdain,

[136-052] Oh, nymph beloved, to ease a lover’s pain.

[136-053] So may the frosts in spring your blossoms spare,

[136-054] And winds their rude autumnal rage forbear.”

[136-055] The story oft Vertumnus urged in vain,

[136-056] But then assumed his heavenly form again.

[136-057] Such looks, and lustre the bright youth adorn,

[136-058] As when with rays glad [[apollo|Phoebus]] paints the morn.

[136-059] The sight so warms the fair admiring maid,

[136-060] Like snow she melts: so soon can youth persuade.

[136-061] Consent, on eager wings, succeeds desire;

[136-062] And both the lovers glow with mutual fire.
