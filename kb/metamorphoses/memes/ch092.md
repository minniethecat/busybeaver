[081-001] Dejanira sends the poisoned tunic of Nessus, by the hands of Lychas to recall the hero from the attractions of a rival.

[081-002] Now a long interval of time succeeds,

[081-003] When the great son of [[jupiter|Jove]]’s immortal deeds,

[081-004] And stepdame’s hate, had fill’d earth’s utmost round,

[081-005] He from Oechalia, with new laurels crown’d,

[081-006] In triumph was return’d: he rites prepares,

[081-007] And to the king of gods directs his prayers:

[081-008] “When Fame (whom Falsehood clothes in Truth’s disguise,

[081-009] And swells her little bulk with growing lies)

[081-010] Thy tender ear, O Dejanira, moved,

[081-011] That Hercules the fair Iole loved.”

[081-012] Her love believes the tale; the truth she fears

[081-013] Of his new passion, and gives way to tears.

[081-014] The flowing tears diffused her wretched grief,

[081-015] “Why seek I thus, from streaming eyes, relief?”

[081-016] She cries; “indulge not thus these fruitless cares,

[081-017] The harlot will but triumph in thy tears:

[081-018] Let something be resolved, while yet there’s time,

[081-019] My bed not conscious of a rival’s crime.

[081-020] In silence shall I mourn, or loud complain?

[081-021] Shall I seek Calydon, or here remain?

[081-022] What though allied to Meleager’s fame,

[081-023] I boast the honours of a sister’s name?

[081-024] My wrongs, perhaps, now urge me to pursue

[081-025] Some desp’rate deed, by which the world shall view

[081-026] How far revenge and woman’s rage can rise,

[081-027] When welt’ring in her blood the harlot dies.”

[081-028] Thus various passions ruled by turns her breast,

[081-029] She now resolves to send the fatal vest,

[081-030] Died with Lernaean gore, whose power might move

[081-031] His soul anew, and rouse declining love.

[081-032] Nor knew she what her sudden rage bestows,

[081-033] When she to Lychas trusts her future woes;

[081-034] With soft endearments she the boy commands

[081-035] To bear the garment to her husband’s hands.

[081-036] The unwitting hero takes the gift in haste,

[081-037] And o’er his shoulders Lerna’s poison cast:

[081-038] At first the fire with frankincense he strows,

[081-039] And utters to the gods his holy vows,

[081-040] And on the marble altar’s polish’d frame

[081-041] Pours forth the grapy stream; the rising flame

[081-042] Sudden dissolves the subtle pois’nous juice,

[081-043] Which taints his blood, and all his nerves bedews.

[081-044] With wonted fortitude he bore the smart,

[081-045] And not a groan confess’d his burning heart.

[081-046] At length his patience was subdued by pain;

[081-047] He rends the sacred altar from the plain;

[081-048] Oete’s wide forests echo with his cries:

[081-049] Now to rip off the deathful robe he tries.

[081-050] Where’er he plucks the vest, the skin he tears,

[081-051] The mangled muscles and huge bones he bares,

[081-052] (A ghastful sight!) or raging with his pain,

[081-053] To rend the sticking plague he tugs in vain.

[081-054] As the red iron hisses in the flood,

[081-055] So boils the venom in his curdling blood.

[081-056] Now with the greedy flame his entrails glow,

[081-057] And livid sweats down all his body flow;

[081-058] The cracking nerves burnt up are burst in twain,

[081-059] The lurking venom melts his swimming brain.

[081-060] Then, lifting both his hands aloft, he cries,

[081-061] “Glut thy revenge, dread emp’ress of the skies;

[081-062] Sate with my death the rancour of thy heart,

[081-063] Look down with pleasure, and enjoy my smart.

[081-064] Or, if e’er pity moved a hostile breast

[081-065] (For here I stand thy enemy profess’d),

[081-066] Take hence this hateful life, with tortures torn,

[081-067] Inured to trouble, and to labours born.

[081-068] Death is the gift most welcome to my wo,

[081-069] And such a gift a stepdame may bestow.

[081-070] Was it for this Busiris was subdued,

[081-071] Whose barbarous temples reek’d with strangers’ blood?

[081-072] Press’d in these arms his fate Antaeus found,

[081-073] Nor gain’d recruited vigour from the ground.

[081-074] Did I not triple-form’d Geryon fell?

[081-075] Or did I fear the triple dog of hell?

[081-076] Did not these hands the bull’s arm’d forehead hold?

[081-077] Are not our mighty toils in Elis told?

[081-078] Do not Stymphalian lakes proclaim thy fame?

[081-079] And fair Parthenian woods resound thy name?

[081-080] Who seized the golden belt of Thermodon?

[081-081] And who the dragon-guarded apples won?

[081-082] Could the fierce centaur’s strength my force with stand,

[081-083] Or the fell boar that spoil’d the Arcadian land?

[081-084] Did not these arms the hydra’s rage subdue,

[081-085] Who from his wounds to double fury grew?

[081-086] What if the Thracian horses, fat with gore,

[081-087] Who human bodies in their mangers tore,

[081-088] I saw, and with their barb’rous lord o’erthrew?

[081-089] What if these hands Nemaea’s lion slew?

[081-090] Did not this neck the heavenly globe sustain?

[081-091] The female partner of the Thunderer’s reign

[081-092] Fatigued at length suspends her harsh commands,

[081-093] Yet no fatigue hath slack’d these valiant hands.

[081-094] But now new plagues pursue me, neither force,

[081-095] Nor arms, nor darts, can stop their raging course.

[081-096] Devouring flame through my rack’d entrails strays,

[081-097] And on my lungs and shrivell’d muscles preys.

[081-098] Yet still Eurystheus breathes the vital air.

[081-099] What mortal now shall seek the gods with prayer?”
