# Pentheus

Pentheus, king of Thebes and grandson of Cadmus, is the central figure of the final movement of Book III of the *Metamorphoses* — a ruler whose rational rejection of the new god Bacchus sets him on a collision course with divine power that ends in his spectacular dismemberment. Ovid's portrait of Pentheus is unusually complex: he is both a tyrant who scorns the prophet Tiresias and brutally imprisons the disguised Bacchus, and a figure whose resistance to irrational divine possession carries a disturbing intellectual dignity — a man destroyed not for vice but for his refusal to surrender reason to ecstasy.

## Role in the Metamorphoses

When Bacchus arrives in Thebes with his ecstatic rites, the city's women — including Pentheus's own mother Agave and his aunts — flock to Mount Cithaeron to worship the new god. Pentheus alone resists. He scorns the blind prophet Tiresias when warned of the consequences of his impiety, and he orders his soldiers to seize the mysterious stranger who has brought the new cult to Thebes. This stranger is Bacchus himself in disguise (as Acetes, a Lydian sailor-priest). Even when Bacchus relates the story of the Tyrrhenian sailors transformed into dolphins for their impiety, Pentheus remains unmoved and orders him imprisoned. The chains fall away, the prison doors open of their own accord, and Bacchus walks free. Pentheus then climbs Mount Cithaeron to observe the Bacchic rites, driven by a mixture of outrage and morbid curiosity. Spotted by the frenzied women — who see him as a wild boar — he is torn apart by his mother Agave and his aunts Ino and Autonoe. His severed head is paraded through Thebes as a hunting trophy, and only when the Bacchic frenzy subsides does Agave realize whose blood stains her hands (025-001).

### Key Episodes

- **The Rejection of Prophecy**: Pentheus scorns Tiresias's warning about Bacchus, mocking the blind prophet (025-001)
- **The Imprisonment of Bacchus**: Pentheus orders the disguised Bacchus (as Acetes) arrested; the god's chains fall away and the prison doors open
- **The Voyage to Cithaeron**: Pentheus climbs the mountain to spy on the rites, driven by anger and fascinated revulsion
- **The Sparagmos**: The maenads, mistaking Pentheus for a boar, tear him limb from limb — his mother Agave leading the violence

## Transformation

Pentheus himself is not transformed into a non-human form; rather, his death is a form of ritual dismemberment (sparagmos) that destroys his bodily integrity:
- **Form**: Man (king) → Dismembered corpse
- **Agent**: His mother Agave and his aunts, possessed by Bacchic frenzy
- **Cause**: Punishment for impiety — refusal to acknowledge the divinity of Bacchus
- **Source**: (025-001)
