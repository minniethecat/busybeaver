# Caeneus

Caeneus is one of the most striking figures of transformation in the *Metamorphoses*. Born as the maiden Caenis, she was desired by Neptune (Poseidon), who granted her a wish after taking her to the seashore. She asked to be changed into a man so that she might never again suffer violation — and Neptune granted her invulnerability on top of the sex-change: no weapon could wound her. As the male warrior Caeneus, she became a celebrated Lapith hero, participating in the great battle between the Lapiths and the Centaurs at the wedding of Pirithous. There, the Centaurs, enraged by his invulnerability, buried him under a mountain of trees — but Caeneus emerged from the pile as a golden bird, a second transformation that echoed the first.

## Role in the Metamorphoses

Caeneus's story is recounted in Book XII by Nestor, who tells the tale of the battle between the Lapiths and Centaurs at Pirithous's wedding. Nestor declares: "These eyes have seen in Caeneus long before" (109-004), and confirms that "Caeneus in courage and in strength excell'd" (109-006). The Centaurs, unable to harm him with weapons, mock him for his origin and pile tree trunks upon him until he is crushed into the earth. This double story — gender transformation followed by bodily dissolution and release — marks Caeneus as a figure who defies fixed categories, his invulnerable male body ultimately giving way to an even stranger form.

### Key Episodes

- **The Wish**: Neptune, enamored of Caenis, grants her wish to become a man; she becomes Caeneus and is made invulnerable
- **The Centaur Battle**: At Pirithous's wedding, Caeneus fights the Centaurs, who cannot wound him (109-004)
- **Burial Under Trees**: The Centaurs bury Caeneus under a mass of trees; he either sinks into the earth or emerges as a golden bird (109-006)

## Transformation

Caeneus undergoes transformation twice: first, a voluntary sex-change from maiden (Caenis) to invulnerable warrior (Caeneus), granted by Neptune; second, an involuntary metamorphosis when crushed by the Centaurs' trees, emerging as a golden bird. This sequence — from female to male to avian — is one of the most radical transformation arcs in the poem.

- **Form 1**: Caenis (maiden) → Caeneus (invulnerable male warrior)
- **Agent 1**: Neptune, answering her wish
- **Form 2**: Caeneus (male warrior) → Golden bird
- **Agent 2**: Unknown divine force; implicit in the tree-burial
- **Source**: (109-004, 109-006)
