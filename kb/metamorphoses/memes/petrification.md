# Petrification

Petrification — transformation into stone — is one of the most frequent and thematically significant metamorphosis types in Ovid's poem. From Battus the treacherous shepherd to Niobe the grieving mother, the change from flesh to stone serves as both punishment and paradoxical preservation: the victim is immobilized forever, yet fixed eternally in the gesture of their transgression or grief (016-001, 040-285).

## Definition

In the *Metamorphoses*, petrification is the process by which a living being is transformed into stone, marble, flint, or rock. The transformation is typically irreversible and is often imposed as a divine punishment for betrayal, loquacity, pride, or excessive grief (016-035, 022-056). Unlike other transformation types that may allow continued consciousness or speech (as with some arboreal metamorphoses), petrification represents the most total form of physical stasis: the victim becomes utterly immobile and voiceless, frozen in a single eternal moment. Yet petrification also functions as a form of memorial — the stone body preserves the victim's form and story for all time (011-027).

## Occurrences in the Metamorphoses

| Book | Context |
|-------|---------|
| Book II | [[Battus]] — A shepherd who betrays Mercury's secret is turned into a touchstone (pumice-stone) as punishment for his faithlessness (016-001) |
| Book II | [[Aglauros]] — The daughter of Cecrops, consumed by envy of her sister Herse, is turned to stone by Mercury. Minerva's agent Envy touches her heart, and she freezes into a statue (017-153, 017-159) |
| Book III | [[Echo]] and Narcissus — Echo, pining from unrequited love for Narcissus, wastes away until only her bones — turned to stone — and her voice remain (022-056) |
| Book V | [[Phineus]] and his followers — Perseus uses Medusa's head to turn his enemies to stone in the battle at Cepheus's court. Phineus himself is petrified mid-supplication (040-285) |
| Book VI | [[Niobe]] — Transformed into a weeping rock on Mount Sipylus, her tears eternal streams flowing from the marble (011-027) |
| Book X | [[Pygmalion]] — Inverted: a stone statue is transformed *from* stone into living flesh through divine favor |

## Significance

Petrification in the *Metamorphoses* carries a distinctive philosophical weight. To be turned to stone is to lose animation — the very quality that, in Ovid's Pythagorean cosmology, distinguishes the living from the inert. Yet stone also grants a terrible immortality: the petrified body endures, a monument to divine justice or human grief. Ovid exploits this duality throughout the poem. Niobe's petrification is at once punishment for her hubris and the only possible response to her immeasurable loss — only stone can bear such sorrow without being destroyed (011-027). Echo's petrification preserves her voice even as her body vanishes (022-056). In the *Metamorphoses*, to become stone is to lose life but gain permanence.
