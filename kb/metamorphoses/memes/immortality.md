# Immortality

Immortality is the ultimate transformation in Ovid's *Metamorphoses* — the change not merely of form but of ontological status, from mortal to divine. The poem traces a progressive arc toward this supreme metamorphosis: from the creation of the world, through countless bodily transformations, to the apotheosis of Roman heroes and finally the poet's own claim to eternal life through his verse. Immortality is thus both a mythological theme and Ovid's personal argument for the value of his art.

## Definition

In the *Metamorphoses*, immortality is achieved through three distinct mechanisms. **Apotheosis** is the elevation of a mortal to divine status by the gods — Hercules, Aeneas, Romulus, and Julius Caesar all undergo this transformation. **Catasterism** is transformation into a constellation — Callisto becomes Ursa Major, Arcas becomes Ursa Minor, and several other figures are placed among the stars. **Poetic immortality** is Ovid's innovation: the poet claims that his "nobler part" — his fame — will survive the death of his body and endure as long as the Roman Empire (147-002). This third form elevates art to the level of divine power.

## Significance

The sequence of apotheoses near the poem's end is not random but carefully structured to culminate in Ovid himself. The progression — Hercules (Greek hero), Aeneas (Trojan founder), Romulus (Roman founder), Caesar (Roman ruler) — leads directly to Augustus, whom Ovid flatteringly praises (146-001) before claiming his own immortality. The implication is radical: the poet's transformation through art equals or surpasses the emperor's through politics. Where Caesar becomes a star, Ovid becomes eternal through the *Metamorphoses* itself — a claim the poem's survival for two millennia has vindicated. Immortality is thus both the poem's subject and its proof.
