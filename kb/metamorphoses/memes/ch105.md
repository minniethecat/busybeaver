[093-001] A beautiful youth, named Hyacinthus, is accidentally killed while playing at quoits with [[apollo|Apollo]], who changes his blood into a flower hearing the name of his deceased friend.

[093-002] Phoebus for thee too, Hyacinth, design’d

[093-003] A place among the gods, had fate been kind:

[093-004] Yet this he gave: as oft as wintry rains

[093-005] Are pass’d, and vernal breezes soothe the plains,

[093-006] From the green turf a purple flower you rise,

[093-007] And with your fragrant breath perfume the skies.

[093-008] You, when alive, were Phoebus’ darling boy;

[093-009] In you he placed his hopes and fix’d his joy:

[093-010] Their god the Delphic priests consult in vain.

[093-011] Eurotas now he loves, and Sparta’s plain:

[093-012] His hands the use of bow and harp forget,

[093-013] And hold the dogs, or bear the corded net;

[093-014] O’er hanging cliffs swift he pursues the game;

[093-015] Each hour his pleasure, each augments his flame.

[093-016] The midday sun now shone with equal light

[093-017] Between the past and the succeeding light;

[093-018] They strip, then, smooth’d with suppling oil, essay

[093-019] To pitch the rounded quoit, their wonted play.

[093-020] A well-poised disk first hasty Phoebus threw;

[093-021] It cleft the air, and whistled as it flew;

[093-022] It reach’d the mark, a most surprising length,

[093-023] Which spoke an equal share of art and strength.

[093-024] Scarce was it fallen, when, with too eager hand,

[093-025] Young Hyacinth ran to snatch it from the sand;

[093-026] But the curs’d orb, which met a stony soil,

[093-027] Flew in his face with violent recoil.

[093-028] Both faint, both pale and breathless, now appear,

[093-029] The boy with pain, the anxious god with fear.

[093-030] He ran, and raised him bleeding from the ground,

[093-031] Chafes his cold limbs, and wipes the fatal wound;

[093-032] Then herbs of noblest juice in vain applies;

[093-033] The wound is mortal, and his skill defies.

[093-034] As in a water’d garden’s blooming walk,

[093-035] When some rude hand has bruised its tender stalk,

[093-036] A fading lily droops its languid head,

[093-037] And bends to earth, its life and beauty fled;

[093-038] So Hyacinth, with head reclined, decays,

[093-039] And, sickening, now no more his charms displays.

[093-040] “Oh, thou art gone, my boy,” Apollo cried,

[093-041] “Defrauded of thy youth in all its pride!

[093-042] Thou, once my joy, art all my sorrow now;

[093-043] And to my guilty hand my grief I owe.

[093-044] Yet from myself I might the fault remove,

[093-045] Unless to sport and play a fault should prove,

[093-046] Oh could I for thee, or but with thee, die!

[093-047] But cruel fates to me that power deny:

[093-048] Yet on my tongue thou shalt for ever dwell;

[093-049] Thy name my lyre shall sound, my verse shall tell;

[093-050] And to a flower transform’d, unheard of yet,

[093-051] Stamp’d on thy leaves, my cries thou shalt repeat:

[093-052] The time shall come, prophetic I foreknow,

[093-053] When, join’d to thee, a mighty chief8 shall grow,

[093-054] And with my plaints his name thy leaf shall show.”

[093-055] While Phoebus thus the laws of fate reveal’d,

[093-056] Behold, the blood which stain’d the verdant field

[093-057] Is blood no longer; but a flower full blown,

[093-058] Far brighter than the Tyrian scarlet, shone:

[093-059] A lily’s form it took; its purple hue

[093-060] Was all that made a difference to the view:

[093-061] Nor stopp’d he here: the god upon its leaves

[093-062] The sad expression of his sorrow weaves;

[093-063] And to this hour the mournful purple wears

[093-064] *Ai, Ai*, inscribed in funeral characters.

[093-065] Nor are the Spartans, who so much are famed

[093-066] For virtue, of their Hyacinth ashamed,

[093-067] But still, with pompous wo and solemn state,

[093-068] The Hyacinthian feasts they yearly celebrate.
