[074-001] [[jupiter|Jupiter]] and Mercury, while travelling in disguise, arrive at the cottage of an aged pair, who entertain their guests with unaffected hospitality, which is amply requited by the transformation of their humble dwelling into a magnificent temple, of which they are appointed the priests—After living to an extreme age, they expire at the same time, and their bodies are changed into trees.

[074-002] Thus Achelous ends; his audience hear

[074-003] With admiration, and, admiring, fear

[074-004] The powers of heaven, except Ixion’s son,

[074-005] Who laugh’d at all the gods, believed in none:

[074-006] He shook his impious head, and thus replies:

[074-007] “These legends are no more than pious lies.

[074-008] You attribute too much to heavenly sway,

[074-009] To think they gave us forms, and take away.”

[074-010] The rest, of better minds, their sense declared

[074-011] Against this doctrine, and with horror heard.

[074-012] Then Lelex rose, an old experienced man,

[074-013] And thus, with sober gravity, began:

[074-014] “Heaven’s power is infinite: earth, air, and sea,

[074-015] The manufacture mass, the making power obey:

[074-016] By proof to clear your doubt; in Phrygian ground

[074-017] Two neighbouring trees, with walls encompass’d round,

[074-018] Stand on a moderate rise, with wonder shown,

[074-019] One a hard oak, a softer linden one:

[074-020] I saw the place, and them, by Pittheus sent

[074-021] To Phrygian realms; my grandsire’s government.

[074-022] Not far from thence is seen a lake, the haunt

[074-023] Of coots, and of the fishing cormorant:

[074-024] Here Jove with Hermes came; but in disguise

[074-025] Of mortal men conceal’d their deities;

[074-026] One laid aside his thunder, one his rod,

[074-027] And many toilsome steps together trod:

[074-028] For harbour at a thousand doors they knock’d;

[074-029] Not one of all the thousand but was lock’d.

[074-030] At last a hospitable house they found,

[074-031] A homely shed; the roof, not far from ground,

[074-032] Was thatch’d, with reeds and straw together bound.

[074-033] There Baucis and Philemon lived, and there

[074-034] Had lived long married, and a happy pair:

[074-035] Now old in love, though little was their store,

[074-036] Inured to want, their poverty they bore,

[074-037] Nor aim’d at wealth, professing to be poor.

[074-038] For master or for servant here to call

[074-039] Were all alike, where only two were all.

[074-040] Command was none, where equal love was paid,

[074-041] Or rather both commanded, both obey’d.

[074-042] “From lofty roofs the gods repulsed before,

[074-043] Now stooping, enter’d through the little door:

[074-044] The man (their hearty welcome first express’d)

[074-045] A common settle drew for either guest,

[074-046] Inviting each his weary limbs to rest.

[074-047] But ere they sat, officious Baucis lays

[074-048] Two cushions stuff’d with straw, the seat to raise;

[074-049] Coarse, but the best she had; then rakes the load

[074-050] Of ashes from the hearth, and spreads abroad

[074-051] The living coals; and, lest they should expire,

[074-052] With leaves and bark she feeds her infant fire:

[074-053] It smokes; and then with trembling breath she blows,

[074-054] Till in a cheerful blaze the flames arose.

[074-055] With brushwood and with chips she strengthens these,

[074-056] And adds at last the boughs of rotten trees.

[074-057] The fire thus form’d, she sets the kettle on

[074-058] (Like burnish’d gold the little seether shone);

[074-059] Next took the coleworts which her husband got

[074-060] From his own ground (a small, well-water’d spot);

[074-061] She stripp’d the stalks of all their leaves; the best

[074-062] She cull’d, and them with handy care she dress’d.

[074-063] High o’er the hearth a chine of bacon hung;

[074-064] Good old Philemon seized it with a prong,

[074-065] And from the sooty rafter drew it down,

[074-066] Then cut a slice, but scarce enough for one;

[074-067] Yet a large portion of a little store,

[074-068] Which for their sakes alone he wish’d were more.

[074-069] This in the pot he plunged without delay,

[074-070] To tame the flesh, and drain the salt away.

[074-071] The time between, before the fire they sat,

[074-072] And shorten’d the delay by pleasing chat.

[074-073] “A beam there was, on which a beechen pail

[074-074] Hung by the handle, on a driven nail:

[074-075] This fill’d with water, gently warm’d, they set

[074-076] Before their guests; in this they bathed their feet,

[074-077] And after with clean towels dried their sweat.

[074-078] This done, the host produced the genial bed,

[074-079] Sallow the feet, the borders, and the sted,

[074-080] Which with no costly coverlet they spread,

[074-081] But coarse old garments; yet such robes as these

[074-082] They laid alone at feasts on holydays.

[074-083] The good old housewife, tucking up her gown

[074-084] The table sets; the invited gods lie down.

[074-085] The trivet-table of a foot was lame,

[074-086] A blot which prudent Baucis overcame,

[074-087] Who thrust beneath the limping leg a sherd;

[074-088] So was the mended board exactly rear’d:

[074-089] Then rubb’d it o’er with newly-gather’d mint,

[074-090] A wholesome herb, that breathed a grateful scent.

[074-091] [[minerva|Pallas]] began the feast, where first was seen

[074-092] The parti-colour’d olive, black and green:

[074-093] Autumnal cornels next in order served,

[074-094] In lees of wine well pickled and preserved.

[074-095] A garden salad was the third supply,

[074-096] Of endive, radishes, and succory:

[074-097] Then curds and cream, the flower of country fare,

[074-098] And new-laid eggs, which Baucis’ busy care

[074-099] Turn’d by a gentle fire, and roasted rare.

[074-100] All these in earthenware were served to board;

[074-101] And, next in place, an earthen pitcher stored

[074-102] With liquor of the best the cottage could afford.

[074-103] This was the table’s ornament and pride,

[074-104] With figures wrought: like pages at his side

[074-105] Stood beechen bowls; and these were shining clean,

[074-106] Varnish’d with wax without, and lined within.

[074-107] By this the boiling kettle had prepared,

[074-108] And to the table sent the smoking lard;

[074-109] On which with eager appetite they dine,

[074-110] A sav’ry bit, that served to relish wine;

[074-111] The wine itself was suiting to the rest,

[074-112] Still working in the must, and lately press’d.

[074-113] The second course succeeds like that before,

[074-114] Plums, apples, nuts; and of their wintry store

[074-115] Dry figs, and grapes, and wrinkled dates were set

[074-116] In canisters, to enlarge the little treat:

[074-117] All these a milk-white honeycomb surround,

[074-118] Which in the midst the country banquet crown’d:

[074-119] But the kind hosts their entertainment grace

[074-120] With hearty welcome, and an open face:

[074-121] In all they did, you might discern with ease

[074-122] A willing mind, and a desire to please.

[074-123] “Meantime the beechen bowls went round, and still,

[074-124] Though often emptied, were observed to fill:

[074-125] Fill’d without hands, and of their own accord

[074-126] Ran without feet, and danced about the board.

[074-127] Devotion seized the pair, to see the feast

[074-128] With wine, and of no common grape, increased;

[074-129] And up they held their hands, and fell to pray’r,

[074-130] Excusing, as they could, their country fare.

[074-131] “One goose they had (’twas all they could allow),

[074-132] A wakeful sentry, and on duty now,

[074-133] Whom to the gods for sacrifice they vow:

[074-134] Her with malicious zeal the couple view’d;

[074-135] She ran for life, and limping they pursued:

[074-136] Full well the fowl perceived their bad intent,

[074-137] And would not make her master’s compliment;

[074-138] But persecuted, to the powers she flies,

[074-139] And close between the legs of Jove she lies:

[074-140] He with a gracious ear the suppliant heard,

[074-141] And saved her life; then what he has declared,

[074-142] And own’d the god. ‘The neighbourhood,’ said he,

[074-143] ‘Shall justly perish for impiety:

[074-144] You stand alone exempted; but obey

[074-145] With speed, and follow where we lead the way:

[074-146] Leave these accursed, and to the mountain’s height

[074-147] Ascend, nor once look backward in your flight.’

[074-148] “They haste, and what their tardy feet denied,

[074-149] The trusty staff (their better leg) supplied.

[074-150] An arrow’s flight they wanted to the top,

[074-151] And there secure, but spent with travel, stop;

[074-152] Then turn their now no more forbidden eyes;

[074-153] Lost in a lake the floated level lies:

[074-154] A watery desert covers all the plains,

[074-155] Their cot alone, as in an isle, remains.

[074-156] Wondering, with weeping eyes, while they deplore

[074-157] Their neighbours’ fate, and country now no more;

[074-158] Their little shed, scarce large enough for two,

[074-159] Seems, from the ground increased, in height and bulk to grow.

[074-160] A stately temple shoots within the skies,

[074-161] The crotches of their cot in columns rise;

[074-162] The pavement polish’d marble they behold,

[074-163] The gates with sculpture graced, the spires and tiles of gold.

[074-164] “Then thus the sire of gods, with looks serene:

[074-165] ‘Speak thy desire, thou only just of men;

[074-166] And thou, O woman, only worthy found

[074-167] To be with such a man in marriage bound.’

[074-168] “A while they whisper; then to Jove address’d,

[074-169] Philemon thus prefers their joint request:

[074-170] ‘We crave to serve before your sacred shrine,

[074-171] And offer at your altar rites divine:

[074-172] And since not any action of our life

[074-173] Has been polluted with domestic strife,

[074-174] We beg one hour of death, that neither she

[074-175] With widow’s tears may live to bury me,

[074-176] Nor weeping I, with wither’d arms, may bear

[074-177] My breathless Baucis to the sepulchre.’

[074-178] The godheads sign their suit. They run the race

[074-179] In the same tenor all the appointed space:

[074-180] Then, when their hour was come, while they relate

[074-181] These past adventures at the temple gate,

[074-182] Old Baucis is by old Philemon seen

[074-183] Sprouting with sudden leaves of sprightly green:

[074-184] Old Baucis look’d where old Philemon stood,

[074-185] And saw his lengthen’d arms a sprouting wood:

[074-186] New roots their fasten’d feet begin to bind,

[074-187] Their bodies stiffen in a rising rind:

[074-188] Then, ere the bark above their shoulders grew,

[074-189] They give and take at once their last adieu.

[074-190] ‘At once farewell, O faithful spouse,’ they said;

[074-191] At once the encroaching rinds their closing lips invade.

[074-192] Ev’n yet, an ancient Tyanaean shows

[074-193] A spreading oak, that near a linden grows;

[074-194] The neighbourhood confirm the prodigy,

[074-195] Grave men, not vain of tongue, or like to lie.

[074-196] I saw myself the garlands on their boughs,

[074-197] And tablets hung for gifts of granted vows;

[074-198] And offering fresher up, with pious prayer,

[074-199] ‘The good,’ said I, ‘are God’s peculiar care,

[074-200] And such as honour Heaven shall heavenly honour share.’ ”
