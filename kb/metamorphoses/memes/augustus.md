# Augustus

Augustus (born Gaius Octavius, later Gaius Julius Caesar Octavianus) is the first Roman emperor and the ruler under whom Ovid composed the *Metamorphoses*. While not a mythological character in the conventional sense, Augustus occupies a structurally crucial position in the poem: the final book (Book XV) traces the chain of transformations from the founding of Rome through the deification of Julius Caesar to the reign of Augustus himself, presenting the emperor as the culmination of a cosmic history that began with the ordering of Chaos. Ovid's treatment of Augustus is famously ambivalent — at once panegyric in its surface praise and subtly subversive in its insistence that imperial power is just another instance of metamorphic change.

## Role in the Metamorphoses

Augustus appears explicitly in Book XV, the poem's final book, which traces Roman history from the legendary kings through the Republic to the imperial present. After narrating the miraculous deification of Julius Caesar — whose soul Venus transforms into a comet, the Julian Star — Ovid turns directly to Augustus in a passage that frames the emperor's rise as the providential endpoint of the entire narrative. Though Julius Caesar's achievements are great, Ovid declares, his adopted son has surpassed them all: the gods have granted Augustus to rule the world, and his eventual apotheosis — the final transformation of the poem — is foretold. This passage (145-001) has been read both as earnest flattery and as carefully coded irony, particularly given that Ovid would later be exiled by Augustus to Tomis on the Black Sea — a biographical fact that casts a long shadow over any reading of the poem's closing encomium. The *Metamorphoses* thus ends not with a myth but with the living emperor, suggesting that the poem's logic of transformation continues into the reader's own world and time.

### Key Episodes

- **The Deification of Julius Caesar**: Venus attempts to save Caesar from assassination but is thwarted by Fate; she transforms his soul into a comet (145-001)
- **The Encomium to Augustus**: Ovid declares Augustus's greatness exceeds even Julius Caesar's, and foretells the emperor's eventual apotheosis — the telos of the entire poem

## Transformation

Augustus himself has not yet undergone metamorphosis at the time of the poem's composition, but his future apotheosis is prophesied:
- **Form**: Mortal emperor → Future god (prophesied)
- **Agent**: Venus and Jupiter, following the precedent of Julius Caesar's deification
- **Cause**: Imperial power and the providential logic of Roman history
- **Source**: (145-001)
