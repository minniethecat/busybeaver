Book II of Ovid's *Metamorphoses* opens with the palace of the Sun and closes with the rape of [[Europa]]. Its centerpiece is the tragedy of [[Phaeton]], whose demand to drive his father's chariot nearly destroys the world — a story that inverts Book I's creation narrative, bringing the cosmos to the brink of chaos through mortal ambition. The book then shifts to Jupiter's earthly seductions, linking cosmic cataclysm with the intimate violence of divine desire.

## Themes

- **Mortal Ambition and Cosmic Consequence**: Phaeton's desire to prove his divine parentage drives him to seize control of the Sun's chariot — a mortal grasping at divine prerogative that literally scorches the earth. The story is Ovid's most vivid illustration of the boundary between mortal and divine, and the catastrophic cost of crossing it.

- **Grief as Transformation**: The [[Heliades]] (Phaeton's sisters) weep until they become poplar trees, their tears amber. [[Cycnus]], Phaeton's grieving lover, becomes a swan. Grief, like desire, is a metamorphic force in Ovid's world — it reshapes the body into a permanent monument of loss.

- **The Geography of Seduction**: Jupiter's pursuit of [[Callisto]] (disguised as [[Diana]]) and abduction of [[Europa]] (disguised as a bull) map divine desire onto the landscape. Callisto becomes Ursa Major in the sky; Europa gives her name to a continent.

## Narrative Arc

Book II bridges the cosmogonic scope of Book I and the Theban cycle that dominates Books III–IV. Phaeton's story, set largely in the heavens and at the edges of the known world, maintains the cosmic register of Book I's opening. The shift to Callisto, Coronis, and Europa brings the action to earth — specifically toward [[Thebes]], whose founding in Book III is foreshadowed by the book's concluding reference to [[Cadmus]].
