# Neptune

Neptune (Greek: Poseidon), god of the sea and brother of Jupiter, is one of the three great rulers of the cosmos in the *Metamorphoses*. His domain encompasses all waters — oceans, rivers, and springs — and his power over them is absolute. Though less central to the poem's narrative than Jupiter, Neptune's interventions are decisive: he floods the earth at Jupiter's command, transforms mortals who offend him, and presides over the metamorphic boundary between land and sea.

## Role in the Metamorphoses

Neptune appears across the poem as a figure of immense destructive and creative power. He participates in the great Flood that destroys the impious race, rescues the shipwrecked, punishes the impious with transformation, and presides over the boundary where land becomes sea — the ultimate metamorphic threshold.

### Key Episodes

- **The Great Flood**: Neptune joins Jupiter in destroying the impious human race, striking the earth with his trident to release the waters and calling upon the rivers to overflow their banks (006-069)
- **Transformation of the Phaeacian Ship**: Neptune transforms the ship that carried Odysseus into stone, fixing it forever in the harbor as a monument to divine power over the sea (133-001)
- **Sons and Offspring**: Neptune fathers numerous beings in the poem, including the Cyclops Polyphemus and the hero Theseus, establishing him as a generative as well as destructive force
- **The Flooded Landscape**: Many of the poem's landscapes — coastlines, islands, river-mouths — are shaped by Neptune's power, marking him as the god who sculpts the boundary between land and water

## Transformation

Neptune's transformations are acts of power exercised over the physical world. He does not transform himself but reshapes others — drowning landscapes, petrifying ships, and flooding continents — using the sea as his instrument. His metamorphoses blur the boundary between earth and water, asserting that the solid world is always at the mercy of the god who commands the waves.

- **Form**: Divine (Olympian) — does not transform himself
- **Agent**: Transforms the physical world through water and earthquakes
- **Cause**: Divine judgment, punishment, protection of the sea's honor
- **Source**: (006-069, 133-001)
