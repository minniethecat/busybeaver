[017-001] Herse, a daughter of Cecrops, is beloved by Mercury, who discloses his passion to Aglauros, her sister, who exacts large sums of money as the price of her connivance at the intrigue—[[minerva|Minerva]], offended at the rapacity of the maiden, commands Envy to torment her continually; and Mercury at length changes her into a stone.

[017-002] This done, the god flew up on high, and pass’d

[017-003] O’er lofty Athens, by Minerva graced,

[017-004] And wide Munichia, while his eyes survey

[017-005] All the vast region that beneath him lay.

[017-006] ’Twas now the feast, when each Athenian maid

[017-007] Her yearly homage to Minerva paid,

[017-008] In canisters with garlands cover’d o’er,

[017-009] High on their heads their mystic gifts they bore;

[017-010] And now, returning in a solemn train,

[017-011] The troop of shining virgins fill’d the plain.

[017-012] The god, well pleased, beheld the pompous show,

[017-013] And saw the bright procession pass below,

[017-014] Then veer’d about and took a wheeling flight,

[017-015] And hover’d o’er them; as the spreading kite,

[017-016] That smells the slaughter’d victims from on high,

[017-017] Flies at a distance, if the priests are nigh,

[017-018] And sails around and keeps it in her eye,

[017-019] So kept the god the virgin choir in view,

[017-020] And in slow winding circles round them flew.

[017-021] As Lucifer excels the meanest star,

[017-022] Or as the full-orb’d Phoebe Lucifer,

[017-023] So much did Herse all the rest outvie,

[017-024] And gave a grace to the solemnity.

[017-025] Hermes was fired as in the clouds he hung;

[017-026] So the cold bullet, that with fury slung

[017-027] From Balearic engines, mounts on high,

[017-028] Glows in the whirl, and burns along the sky.

[017-029] At length he pitch’d upon the ground, and show’d

[017-030] The form divine, the features of a god.

[017-031] He knew their virtue o’er a female heart,

[017-032] And yet he strives to better them by art.

[017-033] He hangs his mantle loose, and sets to show

[017-034] The golden edging on the seam below;

[017-035] Adjusts his flowing curls, and in his hand,

[017-036] Waves with an air the sleep-procuring wand;

[017-037] The glittering sandals to his feet applies,

[017-038] And to each heel the well-trimm’d pinion ties.

[017-039] His ornaments with nicest art display’d,

[017-040] He seeks the apartment of the royal maid.

[017-041] The roof was all with polish’d ivory lined,

[017-042] That richly mix’d, in clouds of tortoise shined;

[017-043] Three rooms, contiguous, in a range were placed,

[017-044] The midmost by the beauteous Herse graced,

[017-045] Her virgin sisters lodged on either side.

[017-046] Aglauros first the approaching god descried,

[017-047] And as he cross’d her chamber asked his name,

[017-048] And what his bus’ness was, and whence he came.

[017-049] “I come,” replied the god, “from heaven, to woo

[017-050] Your sister, and to make an aunt of you.

[017-051] I am the son and messenger of [[jupiter|Jove]],

[017-052] My name is Mercury, my bus’ness love;

[017-053] Do you, kind damsel, take a lover’s part,

[017-054] And gain admittance to your sister’s heart.”

[017-055] She stared him in the face with looks amaz’d,

[017-056] As when she on Minerva’s secret gaz’d,

[017-057] And asks a mighty treasure for her hire,

[017-058] And till he brings it makes the god retire.

[017-059] Minerva griev’d to see the nymph succeed,

[017-060] And now remembering the late impious deed,

[017-061] When, disobedient to her strict command,

[017-062] She touch’d the chest with an unhallow’d hand,

[017-063] In big-swoln sighs her inward rage express’d,

[017-064] That heav’d the rising aegis on her breast;

[017-065] Then sought out Envy in her dark abode,

[017-066] Defil’d with ropy gore and clots of blood:

[017-067] Shut from the winds and from the wholesome skies,

[017-068] In a deep vale the gloomy dungeon lies,

[017-069] Dismal and cold, where not a beam of light

[017-070] Invades the winter or disturbs the night.

[017-071] Directly to the cave her course she steer’d,

[017-072] Against the gates her martial lance she rear’d,

[017-073] The gates flew open, and the fiend appear’d.

[017-074] A pois’nous morsel in her teeth she chew’d,

[017-075] And gorged the flesh of vipers for her food.

[017-076] Minerva, loathing, turn’d away her eye;

[017-077] The hideous monster, rising heavily,

[017-078] Came stalking forward with a sullen pace,

[017-079] And left her mangled offals on the place.

[017-080] Soon as she saw the goddess gay and bright,

[017-081] She fetch’d a groan at such a cheerful sight;

[017-082] Livid and meager were her looks, her eye

[017-083] In foul distorted glances turn’d awry;

[017-084] A hoard of gall her inward parts possess’d,

[017-085] And spread a greenness o’er her canker’d breast;

[017-086] Her teeth were brown with rust, and from her tongue,

[017-087] In dangling drops, the stringy poison hung;

[017-088] She never smiles but when the wretched weep,

[017-089] Nor lulls her malice with a moment’s sleep;

[017-090] Restless in spite, while watchful to destroy,

[017-091] She pines and sickens at another’s joy;

[017-092] Foe to herself, distressing and distress’d,

[017-093] She bears her own tormentor in her breast.

[017-094] The goddess gave (for she abhorr’d her sight)

[017-095] A short command: “To Athens speed thy flight;

[017-096] On cursed Aglauros try thy utmost art,

[017-097] And fix thy rankest venoms in her heart.”

[017-098] This said, her spear she push’d against the ground,

[017-099] And mounting from it with an active bound,

[017-100] Flew off to heaven. The hag with eyes askew

[017-101] Look’d up, and mutter’d curses as she flew;

[017-102] For sore she fretted, and began to grieve

[017-103] At the success which she herself must give;

[017-104] Then takes her staff, hung round with wreaths of thorn,

[017-105] And sails along, in a black whirlwind borne,

[017-106] O’er fields and flowery meadows. Where she steers

[017-107] Her baneful course a mighty blast appears,

[017-108] Mildews and blights; the meadows are defaced,

[017-109] The fields, the flowers, and the whole year, laid waste.

[017-110] On mortals next and peopled towns she falls,

[017-111] And breathes a burning plague among their walls.

[017-112] When Athens she beheld, for arts renown’d,

[017-113] With peace made happy, and with plenty crown’d,

[017-114] Scarce could the hideous fiend from tears forbear

[017-115] To find out nothing that deserved a tear.

[017-116] The apartment now she enter’d where at rest

[017-117] Aglauros lay, with gentle sleep oppress’d,

[017-118] To execute Minerva’s dire command;

[017-119] She stroked the virgin with her canker’d hand,

[017-120] Then prickly thorns into her breast convey’d,

[017-121] That stung to madness the devoted maid;

[017-122] Her subtle venom still improves the smart,

[017-123] Frets in the blood, and festers in the heart.

[017-124] To make the work more sure, a scene she drew,

[017-125] And placed before the dreaming virgin’s view

[017-126] Her sister’s marriage, and her glorious fate;

[017-127] The imaginary bride appears in state,

[017-128] The bridegroom with unwonted beauty glows;

[017-129] For envy magnifies whate’er she shows.

[017-130] Full of the dream, Aglauros pin’d away

[017-131] In tears all night, in darkness all the day;

[017-132] Consumed like ice, that just begins to run,

[017-133] When feebly smitten by the distant sun;

[017-134] Or like unwholesome weeds, that, set on fire,

[017-135] Are slowly wasted, and in smoke expire.

[017-136] Given up to envy (for in every thought

[017-137] The thorns, the venom, and the vision wrought),

[017-138] Oft did she call on death, as oft decreed,

[017-139] Rather than see her sister’s wish succeed,

[017-140] To tell her awful father what had pass’d;

[017-141] At length before the door herself she cast,

[017-142] And, sitting on the ground with suilen pride,

[017-143] A passage to the lovesick god denied.

[017-144] The god caress’d and for admission pray’d,

[017-145] And soothed in softest words the envenom’d maid.

[017-146] In vain he soothed. “Begone!” the maid replies,

[017-147] “Or here I keep my seat and never rise.”

[017-148] “Then keep thy seat for ever,” cries the god,

[017-149] And touch’d the door, wide opening to his rod.

[017-150] Fain would she rise and stop him, but she found

[017-151] Her trunk too heavy to forsake the ground;

[017-152] Her joints are all benumb’d, her hands are pale,

[017-153] And marble now appears in every nail.

[017-154] As when a cancer in the body feeds,

[017-155] And gradual death from limb to limb proceeds,

[017-156] So does the chillness to each vital part

[017-157] Spread by degrees, and creeps into her heart,

[017-158] Till hardening everywhere, and speechless grown,

[017-159] She sits unmoved, and freezes to a stone.

[017-160] But still her envious hue and sullen mien

[017-161] Are in ’he sedentary figure seen.
