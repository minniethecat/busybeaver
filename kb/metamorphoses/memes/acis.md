# Acis

Acis is the mortal shepherd whose love for the sea-nymph Galatea and death at the hands of the Cyclops Polyphemus form one of the *Metamorphoses*' most poignant love triangles. Though he appears late in the poem (Book 13), his story encapsulates Ovid's vision of metamorphosis as both tragedy and transcendence: killed by the violence of a monstrous rival, Acis is transformed into a river-god whose waters flow forever — love immortalized in the landscape itself.

## Role in the Metamorphoses

Acis appears in Book 13, within a larger narrative recounted by Galatea. She tells of Polyphemus's monstrous, unrequited love for her — and of her own love for the beautiful young shepherd Acis. The Cyclops, discovering the lovers together, hurls a massive piece of mountain at Acis, crushing him. Galatea, powerless to save him, transforms him into a river-god whose waters flow from beneath the very rock that killed him.

### Key Episodes

- **Love of Galatea**: Acis is introduced as "the lovely youth" whose loss Galatea mourns, the beloved shepherd who won the nymph's heart while the monstrous Cyclops pursued her in vain (120-001)
- **The Lovers Discovered**: Polyphemus, enraged by jealousy, discovers Acis and Galatea together — "Acis and I were to his sight betrayed" — and his fury erupts (120-198)
- **Death by the Boulder**: Polyphemus tears a massive fragment from the mountain and hurls it at Acis; the shepherd turns to flee, crying "Help!" — but it is too late (120-206)
- **Transformation into a River-God**: Though crushed beneath the rock, Acis is transformed by Galatea into a river deity — his blood becomes water, and from beneath the boulder a new stream flows eternally (120-213)

## Transformation

Acis' metamorphosis is a rescue from death — a transformation that preserves the beloved youth as an immortal feature of the Sicilian landscape. Where his mortal body is destroyed by Polyphemus's violence, his essence survives as the river Acis, flowing beneath the very rock that crushed him. The transformation asserts that love, even when physically annihilated by brute force, can be made eternal through metamorphosis.

- **Form**: Mortal shepherd → River-god (river Acis at the foot of Mount Etna)
- **Agent**: Galatea (divine intervention born of love and grief)
- **Cause**: Death by Polyphemus's boulder; the nymph's desire to preserve her beloved
- **Source**: (120-001, 120-177, 120-198, 120-206, 120-213)
