[144-001] The city of Rome is delivered from a plague by the presence of Esculapius, who willingly accompanies the Roman ambassadors from Epidaurus in the form of a serpent.

[144-002] Melodious maids of Pindus, who inspire

[144-003] The flowing strains, and tune the vocal lyre,

[144-004] Tradition’s secrets are unlock’d to you,

[144-005] Old tales revive, and ages past renew;

[144-006] You who can hidden causes best expound,

[144-007] Say, whence the isle which Tiber flows around,

[144-008] Its altars with a heavenly stranger graced,

[144-009] And in our shrines the god of physic placed.

[144-010] A wasting plague infected [[latium|Latium]]’s skies;

[144-011] Pale, bloodless looks were seen, with ghastly eyes;

[144-012] The dire disease’s marks each visage wore,

[144-013] And the pure blood was changed to putrid gore:

[144-014] In vain were human remedies applied;

[144-015] In vain the power of healing herbs was tried:

[144-016] Wearied with death, they seek celestial aid,

[144-017] And visit [[apollo|Phoebus]] in his Delphic shade;

[144-018] In the world’s centre sacred Delphos stands,

[144-019] And gives its oracles to distant lands:

[144-020] Here they implore the god, with fervent vows,

[144-021] His salutary power to interpose,

[144-022] And end a great afflicted city’s woes.

[144-023] The holy temple sudden tremours proved;

[144-024] The laurel grove and all its quivers moved;

[144-025] In hollow sounds the priestess thus began,

[144-026] And through each bosom thrilling horrors ran:

[144-027] “The assistance, Roman, which you here implore,

[144-028] Seek from another, and a nearer shore;

[144-029] Relief must be implored, and succour won,

[144-030] Not from Apollo, but Apollo’s son;

[144-031] My son, to Latium borne, shall bring redress;

[144-032] Go with good omens, and expect success.”

[144-033] When these clear oracles the senate knew,

[144-034] The sacred tripod’s counsels they pursue,

[144-035] Depute a pious and a chosen band,

[144-036] Who sail to Epidaurus’ neighbouring land.

[144-037] Before the Grecian elders when they stood,

[144-038] They pray them to bestow the healing god:

[144-039] “Ordain’d was he to save Ausonia’s state;

[144-040] So promised Delphos, and unerring fate.”

[144-041] Opinions various their debates enlarge:

[144-042] Some plead to yield to Rome the sacred charge;

[144-043] Others, tenacious of their country’s wealth,

[144-044] Refuse to grant the power who guards its health.

[144-045] While dubious they remain’d, the wasting light

[144-046] Withdrew before the growing shades of night;

[144-047] Thick darkness now obscured the dusky skies:

[144-048] Now, Roman, closed in sleep were mortal eyes,

[144-049] When health’s auspicious god appears to thee,

[144-050] And thy glad dreams his form celestial see:

[144-051] In his left hand, a rural staff preferr’d,

[144-052] His right is seen to stroke his decent beard.

[144-053] “Dismiss,” said he, with mildness all divine,

[144-054] “Dismiss your fears; I come, and leave my shrine.

[144-055] This serpent view, that with ambitious play

[144-056] My staff encircles, mark him every way;

[144-057] His form, though larger, nobler, I’ll assume,

[144-058] And changed, as gods should be, bring aid to Rome.”

[144-059] Here fled the vision, and the vision’s flight

[144-060] Was follow’d by the cheerful dawn of light.

[144-061] Nor was the morn with blushing streaks o’erspread,

[144-062] And all the starry fires of heaven were fled;

[144-063] The chiefs perplex’d, and fill’d with doubtful care,

[144-064] To their protector’s sumptuous roofs repair,

[144-065] By genuine signs implore him to express,

[144-066] What seats he deigns to choose, what land to bless:

[144-067] Scarce their ascending prayers had reached the sky;

[144-068] Lo, the serpentine god, erected high!

[144-069] Forerunning hissings his approach confess’d;

[144-070] Bright shone his golden scales, and waved his lofty crest;

[144-071] The trembling altar his appearance spoke;

[144-072] The marble floor, and glittering ceiling shook;

[144-073] The doors were rock’d; the statue seemed to nod;

[144-074] And all the fabric own’d the present god;

[144-075] His radiant chest he taught aloft to rise,

[144-076] And round the temple cast his flaming eyes:

[144-077] Struck was the astonish’d crowd; the holy priest,

[144-078] His temples with white bands of ribboned dress’d,

[144-079] With reverent awe the power divine confess’d!

[144-080] “The god! the god!” he cries; “all tongues be still!

[144-081] Each conscious breast devoutest ardour fill!

[144-082] Oh beauteous! oh divine! assist our cares,

[144-083] And be propitious to thy vot’ries prayers!”

[144-084] All with consenting hearts, and pious fear,

[144-085] The words repeat, the deity revere:

[144-086] The Romans in their holy worship join’d,

[144-087] With silent awe, and purity of mind:

[144-088] Gracious to them, his crest is seen to nod,

[144-089] And, as an earnest of his care, the god,

[144-090] Thrice hissing, vibrates thrice his forked tongue;

[144-091] And now the smooth descent he glides along:

[144-092] Still on the ancient seats he bends his eyes,

[144-093] In which his statue breathes, his altars rise;

[144-094] His long-loved shrine with kind concern he leases,

[144-095] And to forsake the accustom’d mansion grieves:

[144-096] At length his weeping bulk in state is borne

[144-097] Through the throng’d streets, which scatter’d flowers adorn;

[144-098] Through many a fold he winds his mazy course,

[144-099] And gains the port and moles, which break the ocean’s force.

[144-100] ’Twas here he made a stand, and having view’d

[144-101] The pious train, who his last steps pursued,

[144-102] Seem’d to dismiss their zeal with gracious eyes,

[144-103] While gleams of pleasure in his aspect rise.

[144-104] And now the Latian vessel he ascends;

[144-105] Beneath the weighty god the vessel bends:

[144-106] The Latins on the strand great [[jupiter|Jove]] appease,

[144-107] Their cables loose, and plough the yielding seas:

[144-108] The high-rear’d serpent from the stern displays

[144-109] His gorgeous form, and the blue deep surveys;

[144-110] The ship is wafted on with gentle gales,

[144-111] And o’er the calm Ionian smoothly sails;

[144-112] On the sixth morn the Italian coast they gain,

[144-113] And touch Lacinia, graced with Juno’s fane;

[144-114] Now fair Calabria to the sight is lost,

[144-115] And all the cities on her fruitful coast;

[144-116] They pass at length the rough Sicilian shore,

[144-117] The Brutian soil, rich with metallic ore,

[144-118] The famous isles, where Aeolus was king,

[144-119] And Paestus blooming with eternal spring:

[144-120] [[minerva|Minerva]]’s cape they leave, and Capreae’s isle,

[144-121] Campania, on whose hills the vineyards smile,

[144-122] The city, which Alcides’ spoils adorn,

[144-123] Naples, for soft delight and pleasure born;

[144-124] Fair Stabiae, with Cumean sibyls’ seats,

[144-125] And Baiae’s tepid baths, and green retreats;

[144-126] Linternum next they reach, where balmy gums

[144-127] Distil from mastic trees, and spread perfumes;

[144-128] Cajeta, from the nurse so named for whom

[144-129] With pious care Aeneas raised a tomb.

[144-130] Vulturne, whose whirlpools suck the numerous sands,

[144-131] And Trachas, and Minturnae’s marshy lands,

[144-132] And Formia’s coast is left, and Circe’s plain,

[144-133] Which yet remembers her enchanting reign;

[144-134] To Antium, last, his course the pilot guides.

[144-135] Here, while the anchor’d vessel safely rides,

[144-136] (For now the ruffled deep portends a storm,)

[144-137] The spiry god unfolds his spheric form,

[144-138] Through large indentings draws his lubric train,

[144-139] And seeks the refuge of Apollo’s fane;

[144-140] The fane is situate on the yellow shore:

[144-141] When the sea smiled, and the winds raged no more,

[144-142] He leaves his father’s hospitable lands,

[144-143] And furrows, with his rattling scales, the sands

[144-144] Along the coast; at length the ship regains,

[144-145] And sails to Tibur, and Lavinium’s plains.

[144-146] Here mingling crowds to meet their patron came,

[144-147] Ev’n the chaste guardians of the Vestal flame,

[144-148] From every part tumultuous they repair,

[144-149] And joyful acclamations rend the air:

[144-150] Along the flow’ry banks, on either side,

[144-151] Where the tall ship floats on the swelling tide,

[144-152] Disposed in decent order altars rise,

[144-153] And crackling incense, as it mounts the skies,

[144-154] The air with sweets refreshes; while the knife,

[144-155] Warm with the victim’s blood, lets out the streaming life.

[144-156] The world’s great mistress, Rome, receives him now;

[144-157] On the mast’s top reclined he waves his brow,

[144-158] And from that height surveys the great abodes,

[144-159] And mansions, worthy of residing gods.

[144-160] The land, a narrow neck, itself extends,

[144-161] Round which his course the stream divided bends;

[144-162] The stream’s two arms, on either side, are seen,

[144-163] Stretch’d out in equal length; the land between.

[144-164] The isle, so call’d, from hence derives its name:

[144-165] ’Twas here the salutary serpent came;

[144-166] Nor sooner has he left the Latian pine,

[144-167] But he assumes again his form divine,

[144-168] And now no more the drooping city mourns,

[144-169] Joy is again restored, and health returns.
