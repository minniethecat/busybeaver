[145-001] [[venus|Venus]], unable to arrest the impending death of Julius Cesar, procures his admission into the celestial mansions.

[145-002] But Esculapius was a foreign power:

[145-003] In his own city Caesar we adore:

[145-004] Him arms and arts alike renown’d beheld,

[145-005] In peace conspicuous, dreadful in the field;

[145-006] His rapid conquests, and swift-finish’d wars,

[145-007] The hero justly fix’d among the stars;

[145-008] Yet is his progeny his greatest fame:

[145-009] The son immortal makes the father’s name.

[145-010] The sea-girt Britons, by his courage tamed,

[145-011] For their high rocky cliffs, and fierceness famed;

[145-012] His dreadful navies, which victorious rode

[145-013] O’er Nile’s affrighted waves and seven-sourced flood;

[145-014] Numidia, and the spacious realms regain’d,

[145-015] Where Cinyphis or flows, or Juba reign’d;

[145-016] The powers of titled Mithridates broke,

[145-017] And Pontus added to the Roman yoke;

[145-018] Triumphal shows decreed, for conquests won,

[145-019] For conquests, which the triumphs still outshone;

[145-020] These are great deeds; yet less, than to have given

[145-021] The world a lord, in whom, propitious Heaven,

[145-022] When you decreed the sovereign rule to place,

[145-023] You bless’d with lavish bounty human race.

[145-024] Now lest so great a prince might seem to rise

[145-025] Of mortal stem, his sire must reach the skies;

[145-026] The beauteous goddess, that Aeneas bore,

[145-027] Foresaw it, and foreseeing did deplore;

[145-028] For well she knew her hero’s fate was nigh,

[145-029] Devoted by conspiring arms to die.

[145-030] Trembling, and pale, Jo every god she cried:

[145-031] “Behold, what deep and subtle arts are tried,

[145-032] To end the last, the only branch that springs

[145-033] From my lulus, and the Dardan kings!

[145-034] How bent they are! how desperate to destroy

[145-035] All that is left me of unhappy [[troy|Troy]]!

[145-036] Am I alone by fate ordain’d to know

[145-037] Uninterrupted care and endless wo?

[145-038] Now from Tydides’ spear I feel the wound:

[145-039] Now Ilium’s towers the hostile flames surround:

[145-040] Troy laid in dust, my exiled son I mourn,

[145-041] Through angry seas, and raging billows borne;

[145-042] O’er the wide deep his wandering course he bends;

[145-043] Now to the sullen shades of Styx descends:

[145-044] With Turnus driven at last fierce wars to wage,

[145-045] Whether with unpitying Juno’s rage.

[145-046] But why record I now my ancient woes?

[145-047] Sense of past ills in present fears I lose;

[145-048] On me their points the impious daggers throw;

[145-049] Forbid it, gods, repel the direful blow:

[145-050] If by cursed weapons Numa’s priest expires,

[145-051] No longer shall ye burn, ye Vestal fires.”

[145-052] While such complainings Cypria’s grief disclose,

[145-053] In each celestial breast compassion rose:

[145-054] Not gods can alter fate’s resistless will;

[145-055] Yet they foretold by signs the approaching ill.

[145-056] Dreadful were heard, among the clouds, alarms

[145-057] Of echoing trumpets, and of clashing arms;

[145-058] The sun’s pale image gave so faint a light,

[145-059] That the sad earth was almost veil’d in night;

[145-060] The ether’s face with fiery meteors glow’d;

[145-061] With storms of hail were mingled drops of blood;

[145-062] A dusky hue the morning star o’erspread,

[145-063] And the moon’s orb was stain’d with spots of red;

[145-064] In every place portentous shrieks were heard,

[145-065] The fatal warnings of the infernal bird;

[145-066] In every place the marble melts to tears;

[145-067] While in the groves, revered through length of years,

[145-068] Boding and awful sounds the ear invade,

[145-069] And solemn music warbles through the shade;

[145-070] No victim can atone the impious age,

[145-071] No sacrifice the wrathful gods assuage;

[145-072] Dire wars and civil fury threat the state;

[145-073] And every omen points out Caesar’s fate:

[145-074] Around each hallow’d shrine, and sacred dome,

[145-075] Night-howling dogs disturb the peaceful gloom;

[145-076] Their silent seats the wandering shades forsake,

[145-077] And fearful tremblings the rock’d city shake.

[145-078] Yet could not, by these prodigies, be broke

[145-079] The plotted charm, or stay’d the fatal stroke;

[145-080] Their swords the assassins in the temple draw:

[145-081] Their murdering hands nor gods nor temples awe;

[145-082] This sacred place their bloody weapons stain,

[145-083] And virtue falls, before the altar slain.

[145-084] ’Twas now fair Cypria, with her woes oppress’d,

[145-085] In raging anguish smote her heavenly breast;

[145-086] Wild with distracting fears, the goddess tried

[145-087] Her hero in the ethereal cloud to hide;

[145-088] The cloud, which youthful Paris did conceal,

[145-089] When Menelaus urged the threat’ning steel;

[145-090] The cloud, which once deceived Tydides’ sight,

[145-091] And saved Aeneas in the unequal fight.

[145-092] When [[jupiter|Jove]]: “In vain, fair daughter, you essay

[145-093] To o’errule destiny’s unconquer’d sway:

[145-094] Your doubts to banish, enter fate’s abode;

[145-095] A privilege to heavenly powers allow’d;

[145-096] There you shall see the records graved, in length,

[145-097] On iron and solid brass, with mighty strength;

[145-098] Which heaven and earth’s concussion shall endure,

[145-099] Maugre all shocks, eternal, and secure:

[145-100] There, on perennial adamant design’d,

[145-101] The various fortunes of your race you’ll find:

[145-102] Well I have mark’d them, and will now relate

[145-103] To thee the settled laws of future fate.

[145-104] He, goddess, for whose death the fates you blame,

[145-105] Has finish’d his determined course with fame:

[145-106] To thee ’tis given at length, that he shall shine

[145-107] Among the gods, and grace the worshipp’d shrine:

[145-108] His son to all his greatness shall be heir,

[145-109] And worthily succeed to empire’s care:

[145-110] Ourself will lead his wars, resolved to aid

[145-111] The brave avenger of his father’s shade:

[145-112] To him its freedom Mutina shall owe,

[145-113] And Decius his auspicious conduct know;

[145-114] His dreadful powers shall shake Pharsalia’s plain,

[145-115] And drench in gore Phillippi’s fields again:

[145-116] A mighty leader, in Sicilia’s flood,

[145-117] Great Pompey’s warlike son, shall be subdued;

[145-118] Egypt’s soft queen, adorn’d with fatal charms,

[145-119] Shall mourn her soldiers’ unsuccessful arms:

[145-120] Too late shall find her swelling hopes were vain,

[145-121] And know that Rome o’er Memphis still must reign:

[145-122] What name I Afric, or Nile’s hidden head

[145-123] For as both oceans roll, his power shall spread:

[145-124] All the known earth to him shall homage pay,

[145-125] And the seas own his universal sway:

[145-126] When cruel war no more disturbs mankind,

[145-127] To civil studies shall he bend his mind;

[145-128] With equal justice guardian laws ordain,

[145-129] And by his great example vice restrain:

[145-130] Where will his bounty or his goodness end?

[145-131] To times unborn his generous views extend;

[145-132] The virtues of his heir our praise engage,

[145-133] And promise blessings to the coming age:

[145-134] Late shall he in his kindred orbs be placed,

[145-135] With Pylian years, and crowded honours graced.

[145-136] Meantime, your hero’s fleeting spirit bear,

[145-137] Fresh from his wounds, and change it to a star:

[145-138] So shall great Julius rites divine assume,

[145-139] And from the skies eternal smile on Rome.”

[145-140] This spoke, the goddess to the senate flew;

[145-141] Where, her fair form conceal’d from mortal view,

[145-142] Her Caesar’s heavenly part she made her care,

[145-143] Nor left the recent soul to waste to air;

[145-144] But bore it upward to its native skies:

[145-145] Glowing with newborn fires she saw it rise;

[145-146] Forth springing from her bosom up it flew,

[145-147] And, kindling as it soar’d, a comet grew:

[145-148] Above the lunar sphere it took its flight,

[145-149] And shot behind it a long trail of light.
