# Pomona

Pomona is a uniquely Roman nymph, the goddess of orchards, fruit-trees, and cultivation. Unlike the huntress-nymphs of the Greek tradition (Diana's followers), Pomona has no interest in the wild: her entire devotion is to the cultivated garden, the art of grafting, pruning, and nurturing fruit. In the *Metamorphoses*, she is defined by her self-sufficiency — she walls herself into her orchard, refusing all suitors, content with her trees. The god Vertumnus, who loves her, does not abduct or assault her (as so many gods do in the poem) but instead wins her through patience, disguise, and eloquent persuasion. Pomona's story is Ovid's model of courtship through words: love achieved by transformation of argument rather than transformation of the beloved.

## Role in the Metamorphoses

Pomona appears in Book XIV as the object of Vertumnus's courtship. Her defining characteristic is her devotion to the garden: she carries a pruning-knife rather than a hunting-spear, and she spends her days grafting branches and watering roots. The wall around her orchard is both literal and metaphorical — she has shut out the world of desire. Vertumnus, disguised as an old woman, enters her garden and delivers Ovid's most eloquent argument for love: the parable of the elm and vine (the vine needs the elm's support, and the elm is barren without the vine's adornment) and the cautionary tale of Anaxarete, who rejected love and was petrified for her cruelty. When Vertumnus reveals his true form — a beautiful young god — Pomona's resistance dissolves, and the two become the divine patrons of orchard and season united.

### Key Episodes

- **The Walled Garden**: Pomona, devoted to her orchard, shuts out all suitors
- **The Parable of Elm and Vine**: Vertumnus (as the old woman) uses the elm and vine as a metaphor for marriage
- **The Warning of Anaxarete**: Vertumnus tells Pomona of Anaxarete, who scorned a lover and was turned to stone
- **The Revelation**: Vertumnus sheds his disguise; Pomona, moved by his words and beauty, accepts him

## Transformation

Pomona herself does not undergo metamorphosis — she is a fixed, nurturing presence. Her story is about the transformation of the heart rather than the body: she moves from self-imposed isolation to loving union, persuaded by words rather than conquered by force. Together with Vertumnus, she represents the harmonious union of cultivation (Pomona) and change (Vertumnus).

- **Agent of Change**: Vertumnus, through persuasion and self-revelation
