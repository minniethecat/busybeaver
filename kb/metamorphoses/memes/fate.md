# Fate

In Ovid's *Metamorphoses*, Fate (*fatum*) is a cosmic principle that transcends even the power of the gods. While Jupiter and the Olympians can transform bodies, they cannot alter what the Fates have decreed. This tension between divine will and inexorable destiny runs throughout the poem, shaping both narrative outcomes and the philosophical framework of Ovid's universe.

## Definition

Fate in the *Metamorphoses* operates on two levels. At the **cosmic level**, it is the Fates (*Parcae* or *Moirae*) — three sisters who spin the thread of life at birth, measure its length, and sever it at death. At the **narrative level**, fate is the principle of inevitability that drives each transformation story toward its predetermined conclusion. Even Jupiter, the king of the gods, cannot save his mortal son Sarpedon from his fated death — a limitation acknowledged across classical mythology. Ovid inherits this conception but adapts it to his poem's theme: fate in the *Metamorphoses* often manifests *through* transformation, making metamorphosis itself an instrument of cosmic order.

## Significance

Fate provides the metaphysical framework within which transformation operates. While individual metamorphoses appear arbitrary — the result of a god's whim, a lover's desperation, or a punishment — they are ultimately contained within a cosmos governed by the Fates. Ovid plays with this tension throughout the poem: characters resist their fate (Phaeton, Niobe), accept it (Baucis and Philemon), or are unaware of it (Actaeon). The Pythagorean discourse in Book XV resolves this tension by equating fate with nature's immutable laws: transformation is not capricious but inevitable, the mechanism by which "all things are but alter'd, nothing dies" (140-240).
