# Medusa

Medusa, the only mortal among the three Gorgon sisters, is one of the *Metamorphoses*' most complex figures: a beautiful woman transformed into a monster as punishment for a violation committed against her, who then becomes the instrument of transformation for others. Her severed head — which petrifies all who see it — makes her an agent of metamorphosis even in death.

## Role in the Metamorphoses

Medusa appears in Books IV and V, primarily through Perseus's narrative of her slaying. Ovid's account emphasizes the **injustice** of her transformation: Medusa was originally a beautiful maiden, violated by Neptune in Minerva's temple. Minerva, unable or unwilling to punish Neptune, transformed Medusa's beautiful hair into serpents and made her face a weapon. This detail — the victim punished for her own violation — makes Medusa one of the poem's most ethically charged transformations.

### Key Episodes

- **The Transformation**: Medusa, a beautiful maiden with lovely hair, is violated by Neptune in Minerva's temple; Minerva transforms her hair into serpents as punishment (034-001)
- **The Slaying**: Perseus, using Minerva's polished shield as a mirror, approaches the sleeping Medusa and beheads her — from her blood spring Pegasus and Chrysaor (034-001)
- **The Petrifying Head**: Even in death, Medusa's head retains its power — Perseus uses it to transform Phineus and his followers to stone, and the seaweed it touches becomes coral (034-001)

## Transformation

Medusa undergoes a **double transformation**: first from beautiful maiden to snake-haired monster (Minerva's punishment), then from living being to severed instrument (Perseus's slaying). Each transformation paradoxically increases her power: as monster she petrifies by gaze; as severed head she petrifies even without volition.

- **Form**: Beautiful maiden → Gorgon monster → Severed head (instrument of petrification)
- **Agent**: Minerva (hair to serpents); Perseus (beheading)
- **Cause**: Punishment for Neptune's violation; heroic quest
- **Source**: (034-001)
