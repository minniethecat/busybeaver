# Ajax

Ajax Telamon (Greek: Aias), the colossal Greek warrior of the Trojan War, appears in the *Metamorphoses* at the moment of his greatest humiliation and tragedy. When the arms of the fallen Achilles are to be awarded to the worthiest Greek, Ajax — the army's greatest warrior after Achilles — expects to claim them by right of strength and lineage. Instead, the prize goes to Ulysses, whose eloquence defeats Ajax's silent power. The hero's subsequent madness and suicide mark one of the poem's darkest meditations on the inadequacy of pure martial virtue in a world governed by words.

## Role in the Metamorphoses

Ajax appears primarily in Books 12–13, at the climax of the Trojan War narrative. After Achilles falls to the arrows of Paris and Apollo, the question of who should inherit his divine armor becomes a contest that pits Ajax's claim of martial supremacy against Ulysses's cunning and rhetorical skill. The debate, rendered as competing speeches, is one of the poem's most extended dramatic set-pieces — and Ajax's defeat in it is the prelude to his self-destruction.

### Key Episodes

- **Death of Achilles**: Ajax joins Ulysses in advancing claims to the arms of the fallen Achilles, whose death by "the shafts of Paris" and Apollo leaves the Greek army without its greatest champion (114-001)
- **The Contest of Arms**: Ajax, "the vast bulk of Ajax Telamon," presents his case based on lineage (he is the third from Jove), strength, and battlefield deeds — Ulysses counters with wit, strategy, and the argument that the mind is mightier than the body (115-001)
- **Defeat and Rage**: Ajax's humiliation at losing the arms to Ulysses drives him to madness; the poem's account of "the vast bulk of Ajax Telamon" emphasizes the physical magnitude of the man whom words alone have defeated (114-066)
- **Death of Ajax**: Unable to bear the dishonor, Ajax takes his own life — his blood, falling to the earth, gives birth to the hyacinth flower, inscribed with the letters of grief (116-001)

## Transformation

Ajax's metamorphosis is posthumous: his blood, spilled in suicide, produces the hyacinth flower. The flower's markings — "AI AI" (alas, alas) — become the eternal inscription of his grief, a transformation that converts the hero's physical vastness into delicate botanical permanence. The irony is complete: the warrior who could not be defeated by any mortal enemy is undone by words, and his monument is not a tomb of stone but a flower.

- **Form**: Mortal hero → Suicide → Hyacinth flower (from his blood)
- **Agent**: The earth (spontaneous metamorphosis from his blood)
- **Cause**: Defeat in the contest of arms, dishonor, the inadequacy of strength against eloquence
- **Source**: (114-001, 115-001, 116-001)
