[116-001] Ajax, in despair, puts a period to his existence, and the blood of the hero is changed into a hyacinth.

[116-002] He who could often, and alone, withstand

[116-003] The foe, the fire, and [[jupiter|Jove]]’s own partial hand,

[116-004] Now cannot his unmaster’d grief sustain,

[116-005] But yields to rage, to madness, and disdain;

[116-006] Then snatching out his falchion, “Thou,” said he,

[116-007] “Art mine; Ulysses lays no claim to thee.

[116-008] Oh often tried, and ever-trusty sword,

[116-009] Now do thy last kind office to thy lord:

[116-010] ’Tis Ajax who requests thy aid, to show

[116-011] None but himself himself could overthrow:”

[116-012] He said, and with so good a will to die,

[116-013] Did to his breast the fatal point apply.

[116-014] It found his heart, a way till then unknown,

[116-015] Where never weapon enter’d but his own.

[116-016] No hands could force it thence, so fix’d it stood,

[116-017] Till out it rush’d, expell’d by streams of spouting blood.

[116-018] The fruitful blood produced a flower, which grew

[116-019] On a green stem, and of a purple hue:

[116-020] Like his, whom unaware [[apollo|Apollo]] slew:

[116-021] Inscribed in both, the letters are the same,

[116-022] But those express the grief, and these the name.
