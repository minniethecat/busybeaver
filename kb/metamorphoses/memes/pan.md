# Pan

Pan (also identified with the Roman Faunus), the goat-footed god of the wild, embodies the fertile, untamed energies of the natural world. In the *Metamorphoses*, his most significant appearance is in the tale of Syrinx — the nymph who fled his pursuit and was transformed into marsh reeds, from which Pan crafted the first pan-pipes. The story makes Pan simultaneously a figure of desire, loss, and artistic creation: the god who cannot possess the nymph he loves instead transforms her into the instrument through which he will be remembered.

## Role in the Metamorphoses

Pan appears most notably in the story of Syrinx, told by Mercury to lull Argus to sleep before killing him. The tale within a tale — Mercury's story of Pan and Syrinx — serves as a narrative sedative while also introducing one of the poem's most elegant metamorphoses: the nymph who becomes reeds, which become music.

### Key Episodes

- **Pursuit of Syrinx**: Pan, enamored of the Arcadian nymph Syrinx, pursues her through the wilderness. She flees to the river Ladon and, trapped at the water's edge, prays to her sister nymphs for deliverance (009-001)
- **The First Pan-Pipes**: Syrinx is transformed into marsh reeds at the moment of Pan's embrace. The wind through the reeds produces a mournful sound; Pan, recognizing the voice of his beloved, cuts the reeds to different lengths, binds them with wax, and creates the instrument that bears her name forever (009-001)
- **Pan's Musical Challenge**: In later tradition (referenced obliquely in the poem), Pan challenges Apollo to a musical contest — his rustic pipes against the god's lyre — and loses, a defeat that nevertheless establishes his place in the musical order of the cosmos

## Transformation

Pan is the agent of a unique kind of metamorphosis: the conversion of loss into art. When Syrinx becomes reeds, she disappears as a body but persists as sound. When Pan crafts those reeds into pipes, he completes a double transformation — nymph to reed, reed to music — that makes metamorphosis itself the origin of artistic creation. Pan's story is Ovid's myth of the origin of poetry: art is born from the grief of not possessing what one desires.

- **Form**: Syrinx: Nymph → Marsh reeds → Pan-pipes (musical instrument)
- **Agent**: The sister nymphs (first transformation), Pan's craft (second transformation)
- **Cause**: Pan's pursuit, Syrinx's flight, and the conversion of frustrated desire into artistic creation
- **Source**: (009-001)
