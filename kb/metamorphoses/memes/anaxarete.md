# Anaxarete

A noblewoman of [[Cyprus]], descended from [[Teucer]], whose cold-hearted rejection of her suitor [[Iphis (Book XIV)|Iphis]] earns her the wrath of [[Venus]] and transformation into a stone statue — an exemplum of pride punished, recounted by [[Vertumnus]] to [[Pomona]] in Book XIV. (136-001)

## Role in the Metamorphoses

Anaxarete appears in Book XIV as a cautionary tale embedded within Vertumnus's courtship of Pomona. The disguised [[Vertumnus]] warns [[Pomona]] of the dangers of emotional coldness by telling the story of Anaxarete, whose rejection drove her devoted lover [[Iphis (Book XIV)|Iphis]] to suicide — and whose unfeeling response to his death provoked Venus to transform her into a stone statue, a monument to her own hardness of heart. (136-001)

### Key Episodes

- **Iphis's Devotion**: Iphis, a young man of humble birth, falls desperately in love with the noble Anaxarete. He pleads at her door, decorates her threshold with garlands, and pours out his heart in tears and lamentations — all met with scorn and derision. (136-003)
- **The Suicide**: Unable to bear her continued rejection, Iphis hangs himself at Anaxarete's doorstep, addressing his final words to her: "Oh, Anaxarete, at last forget / Thy pride, and triumph in my death." (136-023)
- **The Stone Transformation**: As Iphis's funeral procession passes, Anaxarete watches from a window. Not a single tear escapes her. Venus, offended by such hardness, turns her to stone — a statue that preserves her cold expression forever, worshipped in Cyprus as "Venus the Looker-on." (136-001)

## Transformation

- **Form**: Mortal woman → Stone statue
- **Agent**: [[Venus]]
- **Cause**: Cruel rejection of Iphis's love, leading to his suicide; Anaxarete's complete lack of pity even at his funeral
- **Source**: (136-001)
