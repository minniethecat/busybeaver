# Ekphrasis

Ekphrasis — the verbal description of a visual artwork — is one of Ovid's most sophisticated narrative techniques in the *Metamorphoses*. At key moments, the poem pauses to describe a woven tapestry, a carved door, or an engraved cup, and within that description unfolds a miniature narrative that mirrors, comments on, or contrasts with the surrounding story. These ekphrastic passages make the *Metamorphoses* a hall of mirrors: stories within stories, transformations represented within transformations.

## Definition

In classical rhetoric, ekphrasis is a vivid description that brings a scene before the reader's eyes. Ovid deploys this technique in the *Metamorphoses* to create **embedded narratives** — stories told not by a character but by an object. The most famous example is Arachne's tapestry (Book VI), which depicts the amorous misdeeds of the gods in twenty-one scenes — a woven catalogue of transformations that mirrors the poem's own content and critiques the divine power it ostensibly celebrates. By embedding these visual narratives, Ovid creates structural parallels between the visual and verbal arts, between the static image and the changing story, and between divine creation (the world from Chaos) and human creation (the tapestry from thread).

## Significance

Ekphrasis is the **self-reflexive dimension** of the *Metamorphoses*. By describing artworks that themselves depict transformations, Ovid creates a mise en abyme — the poem looks at itself looking at transformation. Arachne's tapestry is the most radical instance: its content (divine transformations used for seduction and rape) is indistinguishable from the content of the *Metamorphoses* itself, yet it is offered as evidence *against* the gods in a contest with Minerva. The ekphrastic passages thus raise a question that haunts the entire poem: is the *Metamorphoses* a celebration of divine power or an indictment of it? The woven image refuses to answer, leaving the reader suspended between wonder and critique.
