[009-001] Syrinx, a nymph of Arcadia, escapes from the solicitations of the god Pan, and is changed into a reed, called Syrinx, with which the god makes himself a pipe.

[009-002] Then Hermes thus: “A nymph of late there was,

[009-003] Whose heavenly form her fellows did surpass;

[009-004] The pride and joy of fair Arcadia’s plains;

[009-005] Beloved by deities, adored by swains;

[009-006] Syrinx her name; by sylvans oft pursued,

[009-007] As oft she did the lustful gods delude;

[009-008] The rural and the woodland powers disdain’d;

[009-009] With Cynthia hunted, and her rites maintain’d;

[009-010] Like Phoebe clad, even Phoebe’s self she seems,

[009-011] So tall, so straight, such well-proportion’d limbs:

[009-012] The nicest eye did no distinction know,

[009-013] But that the goddess bore a golden bow:

[009-014] Distinguish’d thus, the sight she cheated too.

[009-015] Descending from Lycaeus, Pan admires

[009-016] The matchless nymph, and burns with new desires.

[009-017] A crown of pine upon his head he wore;

[009-018] And thus began her pity to implore:—

[009-019] But ere he thus began, she took her flight,

[009-020] So swift, she was already out of sight;

[009-021] Nor stay’d to hear the courtship of the god;

[009-022] But bent her course to Ladon’s gentle flood;

[009-023] There by the river stopp’d, and, tired before,

[009-024] Relief from water-nymphs her prayers implore.

[009-025] “Now while the amorous god, with speedy pace,

[009-026] Just thought to strain her in a strict embrace,

[009-027] He fills his arms with reeds, new rising on the place:

[009-028] And while he sighs, his ill success to find,

[009-029] The tender canes were shaken by the wind,

[009-030] And breathed a mournful air, unheard before,

[009-031] That, much surprising Pan, yet pleased him more.

[009-032] Admiring this new music—‘Thou,’ he said,

[009-033] ‘Who canst not be the partner of my bed,

[009-034] At least shall be the consort of my mind,

[009-035] And often, often to my lips be join’d.’

[009-036] He form’d the reeds, proportion’d as they are,

[009-037] Unequal in their length, and wax’d with care:

[009-038] They still retain the name of his ungrateful fair.”

[009-039] While Hermes piped, and sung, and told his tale,

[009-040] The keeper’s winking eyes began to fail,

[009-041] And drowsy slumber on the lids to creep,

[009-042] Till all the watchman was at length asleep.

[009-043] Then soon the god his voice and song suppress’d,

[009-044] And with his powerful rod confirm’d his rest;

[009-045] Without delay his crooked falchion drew,

[009-046] And at one fatal stroke the keeper slew.

[009-047] Down from the rock fell the dissever’d head,

[009-048] Opening its eyes in death, and falling, bled,

[009-049] And mark’d the passage with a crimson trail:

[009-050] Thus Argus lies in pieces, cold and pale,

[009-051] And all his hundred eyes, with all their light,

[009-052] Are closed at once in one perpetual night.

[009-053] These Juno takes, that they no more way fail,

[009-054] And spreads them in her peacock’s gaudy tail.

[009-055] Impatient to revenge her injured bed,

[009-056] She wreaks her anger on her rival’s head;

[009-057] With furies frights her from her native home,

[009-058] And drives her, gadding, round the world to roam;

[009-059] Nor ceased her madness, and her flight, before

[009-060] She touch’d the limits of the Pharian shore.

[009-061] At length, arriving on the banks of Nile,

[009-062] Wearied with length of ways, and worn with toil,

[009-063] She laid her down, and, leaning on her knees,

[009-064] Invoked the cause of all her miseries,

[009-065] And cast her languishing regards above,

[009-066] For help from Heaven and her ungrateful [[jupiter|Jove]].

[009-067] She sigh’d, she wept, she low’d; ’twas all she could;

[009-068] And with unkindness seem’d to tax the god:

[009-069] Last, with an humble prayer, she begg’d repose,

[009-070] Or death, at least, to finish all her woes.

[009-071] Jove heard her vows, and, with a flattering look,

[009-072] In her behalf to jealous Juno spoke.

[009-073] He cast his arms about her neck, and said,

[009-074] “Dame, rest secure; no more thy nuptial bed

[009-075] This nymph shall violate; by Styx I swear,

[009-076] And every oath that binds the Thunderer.”

[009-077] The goddess was appeased; and at the word

[009-078] Was Io to her former shape restored:

[009-079] The rugged hair began to fall away;

[009-080] The sweetness of her eyes did only stay;

[009-081] Though not so large: her crooked horns decrease;

[009-082] The wideness of her jaws and nostrils cease;

[009-083] Her hoofs to hands return, in little space;

[009-084] The five long taper fingers take their place;

[009-085] And nothing of the heifer now is seen,

[009-086] Beside the native whiteness of the skin.

[009-087] Erected on her feet she walks again;

[009-088] And two the duty of the four sustain.

[009-089] She tries her tongue; her silence softly breaks,

[009-090] And fears her former lowings when she speaks:

[009-091] A goddess now, through all the Egyptian state,

[009-092] And served by priests, who in white linen wait.

[009-093] Her son was Epaphus, at length believed

[009-094] The son of Jove, and as a god received;

[009-095] With sacrifice adored, and public prayers,

[009-096] He common temples with his mother shares.

[009-097] Equal in years, and rival in renown,

[009-098] With Epaphus, the youthful Phaeton

[009-099] Like honour claims, and boasts his sire the sun.

[009-100] His haughty looks, and his assuming air,

[009-101] The son of Isis could no longer bear.

[009-102] “Thou takest thy mother’s word too far,” said he,

[009-103] “And hast usurp’d thy boasted pedigree:

[009-104] Go, base pretender to a borrow’d name.”

[009-105] Thus tax’d, he blush’d with anger and with shame:

[009-106] But shame repressed his rage: the daunted youth

[009-107] Soon seeks his mother, and inquires the truth.

[009-108] “Mother,” said he, “this infamy was thrown

[009-109] By Epaphus on you, and me your son.

[009-110] He spoke in public, told it to my face,

[009-111] Nor durst I vindicate the dire disgrace:

[009-112] Even I, the bold, the sensible of wrong,

[009-113] Restrain’d by shame, was forced to hold my tongue.

[009-114] To hear an open slander is a curse;

[009-115] But not to find an answer is a worse.

[009-116] If I am heaven-begot, assert your son,

[009-117] By some sure sign, and make my father known,

[009-118] To right my honour, and redeem your own.”

[009-119] He said, and saying, cast his arms about

[009-120] Her neck, and begg’d her to resolve the doubt.

[009-121] ’Tis hard to judge if Clymene were moved

[009-122] More by his prayer, whom she so dearly loved,

[009-123] Or more with fury fired, to find her name

[009-124] Traduced, and made the sport of common fame.

[009-125] She stretch’d her arms to heaven, and fix’d her eyes

[009-126] On that fair planet that adorns the skies.

[009-127] “Now by those beams,” said she, “whose holy fires

[009-128] Consume my breast, and kindle my desires;

[009-129] By him who sees us both, and cheers our sight,

[009-130] By him, the public minister of light,

[009-131] I swear that sun begot thee; if I lie,

[009-132] Let him his cheerful influence deny;

[009-133] Let him no more this perjured creature see,

[009-134] And shine on all the world but only me.

[009-135] If still you doubt your mother’s innocence,

[009-136] His eastern mansion is not far from hence;

[009-137] With little pains you to his levee go,

[009-138] And from himself your parentage may know.”

[009-139] With joy the ambitious youth his mother heard,

[009-140] And, eager for his journey, soon prepared.

[009-141] He longs the world beneath him to survey,

[009-142] To guide the chariot, and to give the day.

[009-143] From Meroe’s burning sands he bends his course,

[009-144] Nor less in India feels his father’s force;

[009-145] His travel urging, till he came in sight,

[009-146] And saw the palace by the purple light.
