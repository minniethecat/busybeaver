[048-001] Overcome with fatigue, while in pursuit of her daughter, Ceres requests an old woman to supply her with a draught of water—A more generous liquor is hospitably afforded by the matron; and the goddess, while eagerly allaying her thirst, is derided by a boy, who is immediately transformer into an eft.

[048-002] Thus while through all the earth and all the main,

[048-003] Her daughter mournful Ceres sought in vain,

[048-004] Aurora, when with dewy looks she rose,

[048-005] Nor burnish’d Vesper found her in repose.

[048-006] At Aetna’s flaming mouth two pitchy pines,

[048-007] To light her in her search, at length she tines;

[048-008] Restless, with these, through frosty night she goes,

[048-009] Nor fears the cutting winds, nor heeds the snows;

[048-010] And when the morning star the day renews,

[048-011] From east to west her absent child pursues.

[048-012] Thirsty at last by long fatigue she grows,

[048-013] But meets no spring, no riv’let near her flows:

[048-014] Then looking round, a lowly cottage spies,

[048-015] Smoking among the trees, and thither hies.

[048-016] The goddess knocking at the little door,

[048-017] ’Twas open’d by a woman old and poor,

[048-018] Who, when she begg’d for water, gave her ale

[048-019] Brew’d long, but well preserved from being stale.

[048-020] The goddess drank: a chuffy lad was by,

[048-021] Who saw the liquor with a grudging eye,

[048-022] And grinning cries, “She’s greedy more than dry.”

[048-023] Ceres, offended at his foul grimace,

[048-024] Flung what she had not drunk into his face.

[048-025] The sprinklings speckle where they hit the skin,

[048-026] And a long tail does from his body spin;

[048-027] His arms are turn’d to legs, and, lest his size

[048-028] Should make him mischievous, and he might rise

[048-029] Against mankind, diminutives his frame

[048-030] Less than a lizard, but in shape the same.

[048-031] Amazed the dame the wondrous sight beheld,

[048-032] And weeps, and fain would touch her quondam child;

[048-033] Yet her approach the affrighted vermin shuns,

[048-034] And fast into the greatest crevice runs:

[048-035] A name they gave him, which the spots express’d,

[048-036] That rose like stars, and varied all his1 breast.

[048-037] What lands, what seas, the goddess wander’d o’er,

[048-038] Were long to tell; for there remain’d no more;

[048-039] Searching all round, her fruitless toil she mourns,

[048-040] And with regret to Sicily returns.

[048-041] At length, where Cyane now flows she came,

[048-042] Who could have told her, were she still the same

[048-043] As when she saw her daughter sink to hell;

[048-044] But what she knows she wants a tongue to tell;

[048-045] Yet this plain signal manifestly gave;

[048-046] The virgin’s girdle floating on a wave,

[048-047] As late she dropp’d it from her slender waist,

[048-048] When with her uncle through the deep she pass’d.

[048-049] Ceres the token by her grief confess’d,

[048-050] And tore her golden hair, and beat her breast:

[048-051] She knows not on what land her curse should fall,

[048-052] But, as ingrate, alike upbraids them all,

[048-053] Unworthy of her gifts; Trinacria most,

[048-054] Where the last steps she found of what she lost.

[048-055] The plough for this the vengeful goddess broke,

[048-056] And with one death the ox and owner struck.

[048-057] In vain the fallow fields the peasant tills,

[048-058] The seed, corrupted ere ’tis sown, she kills;

[048-059] The fruitful soil, that once such harvests bore,

[048-060] Now mocks the farmer’s care, and teems no more,

[048-061] And the rich grain, which fills the furrow’d glade,

[048-062] Rots in the seed, or shrivels in the blade;

[048-063] Or too much sun burns up, or too much rain

[048-064] Drowns, or black blights destroy the blasted plain;

[048-065] Or greedy birds the new-sown seed devour;

[048-066] Or darnel, thistles, and a crop impure

[048-067] Of knotted grass, along the acres stand,

[048-068] And spread their thriving roots through all the land.

[048-069] Then from the waves soft Arethusa rears

[048-070] Her head, and back she flings her dropping hairs.

[048-071] “O mother of the maid, whom thou so far

[048-072] Hast sought, of whom thou canst no tidings hear;

[048-073] O thou,” she cried, “who art to life a friend,

[048-074] Cease here thy search, and let thy labour end.

[048-075] Thy faithful Sicily’s a guiltless clime,

[048-076] And should not suffer for another’s crime;

[048-077] She neither knew nor could prevent the deed:

[048-078] Nor think that for my country thus I plead:

[048-079] My country’s Pisa; I’m an alien here;

[048-080] Yet these abodes to Elis I prefer;

[048-081] No clime to me so sweet, no place so dear.

[048-082] These springs I, Arethusa, now possess,

[048-083] And this my seat, O gracious goddess, bless.

[048-084] This island why I love, and why I cross’d

[048-085] Such spacious seas to reach Ortygia’s coast,

[048-086] To you I shall impart, when, void of care,

[048-087] Your heart’s at ease, and you’re more fit to hear;

[048-088] When on your brow no pressing sorrow sits;

[048-089] For gay content alone such tales admits.

[048-090] When through earth’s caverns I a while have roll’d

[048-091] My waves, I rise, and here again behold

[048-092] The long-lost stars; and, as I late did glide

[048-093] Near Styx, Proserpina there I espied:

[048-094] Fear still with grief might in her face be seen;

[048-095] She still her loss laments: yet, made a queen,

[048-096] Beneath those gloomy shades her sceptre sways;

[048-097] And ev’n the infernal king her will obeys.”

[048-098] This heard, the goddess like a statue stood,

[048-099] Stupid with grief, and in that musing mood

[048-100] Continued long; new cares a while suppress’d

[048-101] The reigning powers of her immortal breast.

[048-102] At last to [[jupiter|Jove]], her daughter’s sire, she flies,

[048-103] And with her chariot cuts the crystal skies:

[048-104] She comes in clouds, and with dishevell’d hair,

[048-105] Standing before his throne, prefers her prayer:

[048-106] “King of the gods, defend my blood and thine,

[048-107] And use it not the worse for being mine.

[048-108] If I no more am gracious in thy sight,

[048-109] Be just, O Jove, and do thy daughter right.

[048-110] In vain I sought her the wide world around,

[048-111] And when I most despair’d to find her, found.

[048-112] But how can I the fatal finding boast,

[048-113] By which I know she is for ever lost?

[048-114] Without her father’s aid, what other power

[048-115] Can to my arms the lovely maid restore?

[048-116] Let him restore her, I’ll the crime forgive;

[048-117] My child, dishonour’d, I’d with joy receive.

[048-118] Pity your daughter with a thief should wed,

[048-119] Though mine, you think, deserves no better bed.”

[048-120] Jove thus replies: “It equally belongs

[048-121] To both to guard our common pledge from wrongs:

[048-122] But if to things we proper names apply,

[048-123] This hardly can be call’d an injury:

[048-124] The theft is love; nor need we blush to own

[048-125] The thief, if I can judge, to be our son;

[048-126] Had you of his desert no other proof,

[048-127] To be Jove’s brother is, methinks, enough:

[048-128] Nor was my throne by worth superior got;

[048-129] Heaven fell to me, as hell to him, by lot:

[048-130] If you are still resolved her loss to mourn,

[048-131] And nothing less will serve than her return,

[048-132] Upon these terms she may again be yours

[048-133] (The irrevocable terms of fate, not ours);

[048-134] Of Stygian food if she did never taste,

[048-135] Hell’s bounds may then, and only then, be pass’d.”
