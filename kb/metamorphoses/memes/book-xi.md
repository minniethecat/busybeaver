## Themes

- **The Silencing of Song**: Book XI opens with the death of [[Orpheus]], torn apart by Maenads. His head floats down the Hebrus, still singing. [[Bacchus]] punishes the Thracian women by turning them to trees. The death of the poet is the death of the poem's most resonant voice.

- **Gold and Grief**: [[Midas]]'s golden touch — a gift that becomes a curse — and his ass's ears (punishment for preferring Pan's music to Apollo's) make him Ovid's great comic-tragic figure. Paired with [[Ceyx and Alcyone]], whose love survives shipwreck and transformation, the book oscillates between satire and pathos.

- **Metamorphosis as Consolation**: [[Ceyx and Alcyone]], transformed into halcyon birds (kingfishers), are granted seven days of calm seas each winter for nesting. It is the poem's gentlest transformation — not punishment but compensation, a permanent truce between love and the elements.

## Narrative Arc

Book XI is a book of endings and beginnings: the end of Orpheus, the end of Midas's reign, the end of Ceyx's mortal life, but also the first stones of Troy and the beginning of the Homeric world. It is a transitional book, preparing the ground for the epic material of Books XII–XIII.

## Key Transformations

- [[Orpheus]] → Disembodied head (still singing): torn apart by Maenads; his shade rejoins Eurydice in the underworld (096-104)
- Thracian women → Trees: punished by Bacchus for killing Orpheus (096-104)
- [[Midas]]'s touch → Gold (and back): Bacchus grants then revokes the gift; Midas washes in the Pactolus (096-104)
- [[Midas]]'s ears → Ass's ears: for preferring Pan's pipes to Apollo's lyre (096-104)
- [[Ceyx and Alcyone]] → Halcyon birds (kingfishers): grief and divine mercy after Ceyx's shipwreck (096-104)
- [[Aesacus]] → Cormorant (diving bird): leaps from a cliff in grief for Hesperia; transformed by Tethys (096-104)
