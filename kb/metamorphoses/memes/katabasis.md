# Katabasis

Katabasis (Greek κατάβασις, "going down") is the mythological motif of a living hero's descent into the underworld. In Ovid's *Metamorphoses*, this journey to the realm of Pluto and Proserpine recurs as a crucial narrative pattern that tests the traveler's courage, love, or ambition — and often reveals the boundary between mortal and immortal (045-001, 046-001).

## Definition

In classical mythology, katabasis describes the journey of a living mortal or god into the underworld — the realm of the dead — and their subsequent return to the world above. The underworld in the *Metamorphoses* encompasses the Stygian shades, the palace of Pluto and Proserpine, the Elysian fields, and the infernal regions of punishment (125-013). A successful katabasis requires the traveler to confront death itself and return transformed. Unlike the epic katabasis of Virgil's Aeneas or Homer's Odysseus, Ovid's underworld journeys are typically motivated by love, grief, or divine mission rather than martial destiny (049-001, 060-452).

## Occurrences in the Metamorphoses

| Book | Context |
|-------|---------|
| Book V | [[orpheus-and-eurydice]] — Orpheus descends to the underworld to retrieve his wife Eurydice, charming Pluto and Proserpine with his song. His return is the most famous katabasis in the poem (110-070) |
| Book V | Ceres descends to the infernal regions to reclaim her daughter Proserpine, who has eaten the pomegranate seeds that bind her to Pluto's kingdom (049-001) |
| Book IV | Juno visits the underworld to enlist the Furies in her persecution of Ino and Athamas (060-452) |
| Book XIV | Aeneas descends to the shades, guided by the Sibyl, to consult his father Anchises — a katabasis that links Ovid's poem to Virgil's *Aeneid* (115-038) |

## Significance

The katabasis motif in the *Metamorphoses* functions as a narrative threshold: to enter the underworld is to cross from the ordinary world into a space where the normal rules of life and death are suspended. Each katabasis in the poem tests the traveler's defining quality — Orpheus's art, Ceres's maternal devotion, Aeneas's pietas — against the implacable reality of death. Ovid treats these descents with varying tonalities: Orpheus's journey is lyrical and tragic, Juno's is vengeful and cruel, Aeneas's is heroic and foundational. The underworld in the *Metamorphoses* is thus not a single landscape but a mirror that reflects the character of each traveler (145-043).
