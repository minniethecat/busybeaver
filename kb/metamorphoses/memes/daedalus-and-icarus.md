# The Flight of Daedalus and Icarus

The flight of Daedalus and Icarus from Crete is the *Metamorphoses*' definitive statement on the perils of human ambition. Imprisoned in the very Labyrinth he designed for King Minos, the master craftsman Daedalus fashions wings of feathers and wax — an act of invention that allows him and his son to escape their island prison by air. But Icarus, intoxicated by the wonder of flight, soars too near the sun; the wax melts; and the boy plunges into the sea that now bears his name. The myth is a cautionary tale, a technological triumph, and a father's tragedy — all at once.

## Setting

The island of Crete, where Daedalus and Icarus are held captive by King Minos. The flight path crosses the Aegean Sea — from Crete northward — passing over Samos, Delos, and Lebynthos before Icarus's fatal ascent. The waters where Icarus falls become the Icarian Sea.

## Narrative

Imprisoned by Minos, who controls both land and sea, Daedalus turns to the one remaining domain: the sky. He constructs two pairs of wings from feathers, thread, and wax — a triumph of craft and desperation. Before their flight, he warns Icarus to follow a middle course: neither too low (where sea spray would weigh down the wings) nor too high (where the sun would melt the wax). But the boy, exhilarated by flight, forgets his father's warning and soars upward. The sun's heat dissolves the wax; the feathers scatter; and Icarus falls, calling his father's name as the sea closes over him. Daedalus, now alone, buries his son on an island and curses his own art.
