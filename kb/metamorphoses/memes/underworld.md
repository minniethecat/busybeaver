# The Underworld

The Underworld in the *Metamorphoses* is both a physical destination — reached through gates, rivers, and downward paths — and a metaphysical limit: the place where transformation stops. The dead, once they cross the Styx, are fixed in their final form, beyond the reach of further metamorphosis. Orpheus's attempt to reverse this fixity — to retrieve Eurydice from death — is the poem's most powerful exploration of transformation's limits.

## Mythological Significance

The Underworld represents the **limit of metamorphosis**. The dead, fixed in their shades, are beyond the transformations that define the living world. Orpheus's near-success — his song almost reverses this fixity — makes the backward glance all the more devastating: transformation can reach the threshold of death but cannot cross it.
