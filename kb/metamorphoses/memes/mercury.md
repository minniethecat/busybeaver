# Mercury

Mercury (Greek [[Hermes]]), the swift-footed messenger of the gods, is one of the most versatile divine agents in the *Metamorphoses*. Son of [[Jupiter]] and the Pleiad Maia, he serves his father as enforcer, trickster, and executioner — slaying the hundred-eyed [[Argus]], punishing oath-breakers, courting mortal women, and accompanying Jupiter on earthly visits that test human hospitality (008-001).

## Role in the Metamorphoses

Mercury appears across multiple books as Jupiter's primary agent of intervention in mortal affairs. Unlike many Olympians who act from personal passion, Mercury's actions in the poem are predominantly instrumental — he carries out divine commands with efficiency and occasional cunning. His episodes span Books I through XI, reflecting his role as a connective figure who moves between the divine and mortal realms (044-019).

### Key Episodes

- **The Slaying of Argus** (Book I): Jupiter, having transformed [[Io]] into a heifer to hide her from [[Juno]], sends Mercury to kill the hundred-eyed Argus who guards her. Mercury lulls Argus to sleep with his pan-pipes, then beheads him. Juno places Argus's eyes in the peacock's tail (008-001).
- **Battus and the Stolen Flocks** (Book II): When Mercury steals the cattle of Apollo, the shepherd [[Battus]] promises silence — then betrays him. Mercury transforms Battus into a touchstone (pumice-stone), a lasting monument to the punishment of broken oaths (016-001).
- **Herse and Aglauros** (Book II): Mercury falls in love with the Athenian princess Herse. Her sister [[Aglauros]] demands gold in exchange for access, but Minerva, offended by her greed, sends Envy to torment her. Mercury ultimately transforms the petrified Aglauros into stone (017-001).
- **Baucis and Philemon** (Book VIII): Mercury accompanies Jupiter in disguise to test human hospitality. After being rejected by a thousand homes, they are welcomed by the aged couple [[Baucis and Philemon]], whose humble cottage is transformed into a temple — one of the poem's few episodes of rewarded virtue (074-001).

## Transformation

Mercury himself undergoes no metamorphosis in the poem. He is consistently an **agent of transformation** for others:
- [[Argus]] → killed; his hundred eyes transplanted to the peacock's tail
- [[Battus]] → pumice-stone (punishment for perjury)
- [[Aglauros]] → stone (punishment for greed, via Minerva's Envy)
- [[Baucis and Philemon]] → priests of a temple, then intertwined trees (reward for hospitality)
