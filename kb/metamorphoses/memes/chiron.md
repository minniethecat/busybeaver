# Chiron

Chiron, the son of Saturn and the nymph Philyra, is the one centaur in classical mythology who transcends the brutish reputation of his kind: he is the wise tutor of heroes (Achilles, Jason, Aesculapius), a master of medicine, music, and prophecy, and a figure of profound gentleness. In the *Metamorphoses*, Chiron appears primarily in Book II through the story of his daughter Ocyrrhoe, whose gift of prophecy reveals his destined transformation: wounded accidentally by one of Hercules's poisoned arrows, Chiron will suffer incurable pain and, though immortal, will long for death — a wish finally granted when Jupiter permits him to surrender his immortality and he is placed among the stars as the constellation Centaurus.

## Role in the Metamorphoses

Chiron first appears in Book II, when Ocyrrhoe, his daughter, visits him as he tends the infant Aesculapius (the future god of medicine, entrusted to Chiron by Apollo). Possessed by the spirit of prophecy, Ocyrrhoe reveals the child's future greatness, then turns to her father and foretells his own fate: that his immortality, his divine birthright, will become a curse when he is struck by a serpent's venom (actually the Hydra's poison on Hercules's arrow), and that the gods, taking pity, will permit him to die and be placed among the constellations (015-001). For revealing these secrets, Ocyrrhoe is herself transformed into a mare. In Book VII, Chiron is referenced again in connection with the rejuvenation of Iolaus — Hebe's restoration of youth is compared to the days when Chiron tutored the young. Throughout, Chiron appears as a liminal figure: a centaur who transcends centaur-nature, an immortal who longs for mortality, a creature of wisdom whose daughter's wisdom costs her her human form.

### Key Episodes

- **The Nursing of Aesculapius**: Chiron tends the infant Aesculapius, entrusted to him by Apollo — the future god of healing learning from the wisest of healers
- **Ocyrrhoe's Prophecy**: Chiron's daughter, seized by prophetic frenzy, foretells both the child's greatness and her father's eventual fate (015-001)
- **The Surrender of Immortality**: Wounded by Hercules's poisoned arrow, Chiron's immortal suffering is ended when the gods permit him to die, placing him in the stars

## Transformation

- **Form**: Immortal centaur → Mortal → Constellation (Centaurus)
- **Agent**: Hercules's arrow (wound); Jupiter (permission to die); the gods (catasterism)
- **Cause**: The paradox of immortal suffering — Chiron's gift of eternal life becomes a curse when his wound cannot heal
- **Source**: (015-001)
