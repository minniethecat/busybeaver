# Hercules

Hercules (Heracles in Greek), son of Jupiter and the mortal Alcmena, is the preeminent hero of classical myth. His legendary strength, his twelve labors, and his eventual death and deification are referenced throughout the *Metamorphoses*, often in connection with other characters whose fates intersect with his. Ovid does not narrate the labors in full but weaves Hercules into multiple episodes: his contest with Achelous for the hand of Deianira, his accidental death by the poisoned robe of Nessus, and his ultimate apotheosis — received into the heavens as a god. Hercules represents the hero who transcends mortality through suffering and becomes divine.

## Role in the Metamorphoses

Hercules appears across multiple books, often as a reference point for other characters' stories. In Book IX, Achelous recounts his own transformation while wrestling Hercules for Deianira (079-001). The river-god tells Theseus how Hercules prevailed despite Achelous's shape-shifting, tearing off one of his horns. Elsewhere, his name is invoked as a measure of supreme strength and divine parentage: "By Hercules and matrons more disgraced" (064-095) and "her whom Heaven for Hercules decreed" (071-399) — the latter referring to Hebe, his divine bride after apotheosis. His death by the centaur Nessus's poisoned robe (a gift from the deceived Deianira) and his subsequent fiery apotheosis on Mount Oeta are alluded to rather than narrated in full, consistent with Ovid's episodic treatment of even the greatest figures.

### Key Episodes

- **Contest with Achelous**: Hercules wrestles the river-god Achelous for Deianira's hand; Achelous changes shape but is defeated (079-001)
- **Death by Nessus's Robe**: The centaur Nessus, dying from Hercules's arrow, gives Deianira his poisoned blood, telling her it is a love charm; she sends the poisoned robe to Hercules, causing his death
- **Apotheosis**: After his mortal death in flames, Hercules is received among the gods on Olympus and wedded to Hebe, goddess of youth

## Transformation

Hercules undergoes the ultimate transformation: apotheosis. His mortal body is consumed by fire (the poisoned robe of Nessus eating into his flesh), but his divine essence — inherited from Jupiter — ascends to Olympus, where he becomes a god. This is transformation as reward and culmination, the hero's journey completed in immortal form.

- **Form**: Mortal hero → Divine being (apotheosis)
- **Agent**: Jupiter, acknowledging his son's worthiness
- **Cause**: Reward for heroic suffering and completion of the labors
- **Source**: (079-001)
