Book III of Ovid's *Metamorphoses* is the first book of the Theban cycle — a dark sequence of intertwined tragedies centered on the house of [[Cadmus]]. From the founding of [[Thebes]] to the dismemberment of [[Pentheus]], the book traces a pattern of humans punished for seeing what gods would keep hidden: [[Actaeon]] sees [[Diana]] bathing, [[Semele]] demands to see [[Jupiter]]'s true form, [[Tiresias]] knows both sexes' secrets, [[Narcissus]] falls in love with his own reflection, and Pentheus refuses to see [[Bacchus]]'s divinity. Each fails to respect the boundary between mortal and divine — and each is destroyed by that failure.

## Themes

- **Forbidden Sight**: The unifying motif of Book III is the catastrophic consequence of seeing the forbidden. Actaeon stumbles upon Diana bathing; Semele demands to see Jupiter's divine radiance; Narcissus is destroyed by seeing his own image; Pentheus spies on the Bacchic rites. In each case, sight transgresses a sacred boundary, and the punishment is metamorphosis or death — often both. Ovid presents vision itself as a form of violation.

- **The Theban Curse**: The house of [[Cadmus]], founder of Thebes, is cursed from its origin — Cadmus slays a dragon sacred to Mars and sows its teeth to produce the Spartoi, a race born from violence. His descendants (Semele, Actaeon, Pentheus) inherit this burden: Thebes is both the greatest city of Greek myth and the stage for its most relentless tragedies.

- **Doubles and Reflections**: Narcissus's story — the boy who loves his own reflection — literalizes the book's structural preoccupation with doubling. Echo cannot speak her own words; Narcissus cannot embrace his own image; Tiresias has lived as both man and woman. Identity in Book III is unstable, refracted through mirrors and repetitions.

## Narrative Arc

Book III begins the Theban cycle (Books III–IV), which shifts the poem's focus from the cosmos to the city. Where Book I was cosmogonic and Book II was celestial-terrestrial, Book III is intensely local — its entire action radiates from Thebes. Yet the local is universal: the questions Book III raises about sight, knowledge, and divine justice echo throughout the remaining twelve books.

## Key Transformations

- Dragon's teeth → [[Spartoi]] (armed men): Cadmus sows the serpent's teeth; the crop is warriors (019)
- [[Actaeon]] → Stag: Seeing Diana naked, he is transformed and torn apart by his own hounds (020)
- [[Semele]] → Incinerated: Demanding to see Jupiter's true form, she is consumed by divine fire; the unborn [[Bacchus]] is rescued (021)
- [[Echo]] → Disembodied voice: Punished by Juno for distracting her with talk while Jupiter pursued nymphs (022)
- [[Narcissus]] → Flower: Unable to leave his own reflection, he wastes away; a narcissus blooms where he died (023)
- Mariners → Dolphins: Tyrrhenian sailors who mock Bacchus are transformed and leap into the sea (025)
- [[Pentheus]] → Dismembered corpse: Torn apart by Bacchants led by his own mother [[Agave]] (026)
