# Eurydice

Eurydice, a nymph and the beloved wife of the legendary musician Orpheus, is the figure around whom one of the most enduring myths of the *Metamorphoses* turns. Her sudden death from a serpent's bite on her wedding day sends Orpheus on a katabasis — a descent to the underworld — where his music so moves the rulers of the dead that they grant him permission to lead her back to the living world, on one condition: that he not look back at her until they have reached the upper air. His fateful glance backward, born of love and doubt, loses her forever, and her second death, unlike her first, is absolute. Eurydice's story in Book X is Ovid's meditation on love, loss, art, and the tragic gap between what song can achieve and what it cannot sustain.

## Role in the Metamorphoses

Eurydice's story opens Book X. On her wedding day, while walking through the grass with her attendants, she is bitten on the ankle by a serpent and dies — a moment of astonishing narrative brevity, as if Ovid wants the reader to feel the suddenness that Orpheus himself experiences. Overcome with grief, Orpheus descends to the underworld and sings before Pluto and Proserpine, arguing that all things ultimately belong to Death and asking only that Eurydice be lent to him for a time. His song is so powerful that the Furies weep, the wheel of Ixion stops, and the rulers of the dead grant his request — with the sole condition that he must not look back until both have reached the world above. Orpheus leads Eurydice through the darkness, but at the very threshold of the upper world, fearful that she has stumbled or that he is being deceived, he turns to look at her. She fades away with a single word — "Farewell" — and is lost to him a second time, this time irrevocably. The gates of Death are closed against him, and Orpheus is left to mourn on the bare hills of Thrace (091-001).

### Key Episodes

- **The Wedding Day Death**: Eurydice, bitten by a snake while walking in the meadow, dies on the day of her marriage to Orpheus
- **The Katabasis**: Orpheus descends to the underworld and wins her release through the power of his song (091-001)
- **The Backward Glance**: At the threshold of the living world, Orpheus looks back at Eurydice, and she is lost forever
- **The Second Death**: Eurydice's final words — a quiet farewell — as she is drawn back into the shadows

## Transformation

Eurydice undergoes no physical metamorphosis; her story is one of death and the soul's passage rather than bodily change. Her "transformation" is existential — from living bride to shade, from retrieved soul to the twice-dead:
- **Form**: Nymph → Shade (in the underworld)
- **Agent**: Death (serpent's venom); Orpheus's backward glance
- **Cause**: A tragic accident followed by a tragic failure of trust
- **Source**: (091-001)
