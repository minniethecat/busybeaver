# Apotheosis

Apotheosis, or deification, is the transformation of a mortal being into a god. In Ovid's *Metamorphoses*, this represents the highest and most consequential form of metamorphosis: the elevation of human status to divine rank (134-019). Unlike other transformations that typically punish or preserve, apotheosis rewards exceptional merit and serves the political and cosmological order of the poem.

## Definition

In the context of classical mythology, apotheosis (from Greek ἀποθέωσις, "making a god") is the process by which a mortal — typically a hero, ruler, or figure of exceptional virtue — is granted immortality and admitted to the company of the gods (134-019). Within the *Metamorphoses*, deification serves as the culminating transformation type, appearing prominently in the poem's final books as Ovid moves from Greek myth toward Roman history and the imperial cult. Unlike the punitive or preservative metamorphoses that dominate the earlier books, apotheosis represents transformation as elevation and reward (134-035).

## Occurrences in the Metamorphoses

| Book | Context |
|-------|---------|
| Book IX | [[apotheosis-of-caesar]] — The deification of Julius Caesar, whose soul is received among the stars, prefiguring Augustus's eventual divinity (134-019) |
| Book XIV | The deification of Aeneas, whose mother Venus obtains his immortality after the Trojan hero's mortal part is washed away (014-120) |
| Book XIV | The apotheosis of Romulus, who is translated to heaven by Mars and becomes the god Quirinus |
| Book XV | Ovid's philosophical coda ties deification to Pythagorean doctrine — great souls ascend while the body returns to earth |

## Significance

Apotheosis in the *Metamorphoses* serves a crucial structural and ideological function. By placing deification as the final transformation type, Ovid creates a teleological arc that moves from chaos to cosmos, from punishment to reward, and — most importantly for his Roman audience — from Greek myth to Roman history. Caesar's apotheosis is not merely one more metamorphosis; it is the transformation that legitimizes Augustus's rule and anchors Rome's imperial destiny in the divine order (134-035). The poem thus culminates not in random change but in purposeful elevation, mirroring Ovid's own claim to poetic immortality in the epilogue.
