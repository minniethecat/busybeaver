## Themes

- **Words as Weapons**: The book opens with the great rhetorical duel: [[Ajax]] versus [[Ulysses]] for the arms of [[Achilles]]. Ovid transforms an epic contest of strength into a contest of eloquence — and eloquence wins. The death of Ajax that follows is a critique of the heroic code itself.

- **After Troy**: The fall of Troy is narrated through its victims: [[Hecuba]] loses everything and is transformed into a dog; the Trojan women are scattered. Ovid follows [[Aeneas]]'s voyage through the Mediterranean, using Virgil's epic as a backdrop for entirely different stories.

- **Love Against Epic**: The book's second half shifts register entirely: [[Acis and Galatea]] (a pastoral love destroyed by the Cyclops [[Polyphemus]]) and [[Scylla and Glaucus]] (unrequited love leading to transformation). Ovid refuses to stay in epic mode, insisting that love stories deserve equal space.

## Narrative Arc

Book XIII is the longest and most tonally varied book in the poem. It contains courtroom drama (Ajax vs. Ulysses), war narrative (fall of Troy), epic voyage (Aeneas), pastoral romance (Acis and Galatea), and maritime fantasy (Glaucus). The glue is metamorphosis — every story, however different in register, ends in bodily change.

## Key Transformations

- [[Ajax]] → Hyacinth flower (his blood → flower): suicide after losing the arms of Achilles to Ulysses (114-120)
- [[Hecuba]] → Dog: mad with grief after the deaths of Polyxena and Polydorus (114-120)
- [[Memnon]]'s ashes → Birds (Memnonides): Jupiter grants them annual combat at Memnon's tomb (114-120)
- [[Acis]] → River-god: killed by [[Polyphemus]] with a boulder; his blood becomes a stream (114-120)
- [[Glaucus]] → Sea-god (half-fish): eats magical grass and transforms; falls in love with [[Scylla]] (114-120)
