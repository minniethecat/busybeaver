The story of [[Byblis]] and [[Caunus]] (Book IX.454–665) is Ovid's most psychologically penetrating treatment of forbidden desire. Unlike the violent transformations of [[Tereus]] or the divine punishments of [[Actaeon]], Byblis's metamorphosis is entirely internal in origin: she falls in love with her own twin brother, and her inability to suppress or consummate this love gradually dissolves her identity. Her final transformation — dissolving into a fountain of never-ending tears — is the physical manifestation of a grief that was always already liquid.

## Setting

- **Location**: [[Miletus]] (Caria, Asia Minor), later the wilds of [[Caria]] and [[Lycia]]
- **Book**: Book IX.454–665

## Narrative

[[Byblis]], daughter of [[Miletus]] and the nymph [[Cyanee]], grows up in Caria with her twin brother [[Caunus]]. As she matures, her affection for Caunus deepens into erotic love — a passion she initially fails to recognize. When she dreams of union with her brother and wakes disturbed, she begins a prolonged internal struggle, cataloguing the arguments for and against incestuous love. She notes that the gods themselves marry their siblings ([[Juno]] and [[Jupiter]], [[Isis]] and [[Osiris]]), but acknowledges that human custom forbids it.

Resolving to confess, Byblis writes Caunus a letter on wax tablets, declaring her love. Caunus, horrified, rejects her and flees Caria entirely. Byblis, devastated by his flight and the finality of his rejection, pursues him through [[Caria]], [[Lycia]], and beyond, weeping uncontrollably. Her grief becomes uncontainable, and she collapses in the wilderness. As she lies weeping, the nymphs of the forest take pity: her tears become a spring, and she herself dissolves into a fountain — still flowing, still grieving, eternally.

## Themes

- **Forbidden Desire and Self-Knowledge**: Byblis's long internal monologue — debating whether her love is natural or monstrous — is the poem's most detailed anatomy of desire coming to consciousness. She knows what she wants before she can name it, and naming it is the beginning of her destruction.
- **Writing and the Failure of Persuasion**: Byblis's wax tablet letter is a crucial counterpart to [[Philomela]]'s woven tapestry — both are texts produced by women who cannot speak directly. But where Philomela's text succeeds in communicating and enabling revenge, Byblis's text fails catastrophically, driving Caunus further away.
- **Metamorphosis as Dissolution**: Unlike transformations that produce a new, stable form (bird, tree, stone), Byblis's metamorphosis is a *dissolution* — the self does not become something new; it simply ceases to be solid, becoming liquid grief.
- **Isolation**: Unlike almost every other story in the *Metamorphoses*, Byblis has no confidante, no chorus, no divine intervener. Her tragedy is utterly solitary.
