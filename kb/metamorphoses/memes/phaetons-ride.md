# Phaeton's Ride

Phaeton's disastrous attempt to drive the chariot of the Sun is one of the *Metamorphoses*' most spectacular episodes. Seeking confirmation that Apollo is truly his father, the youth extracts an oath from the god and demands to drive the solar chariot for a day — a task beyond mortal capacity that nearly destroys the world.

## Characters

| Character | Role |
|-----------|------|
| Phaeton | Mortal youth, son of Apollo and Clymene; the doomed charioteer |
| Apollo | Phaeton's divine father; bound by oath to grant the fatal wish |
| Jupiter | The supreme god who stops the catastrophe with his thunderbolt |
| Clymene | Phaeton's mother, whose claim of Apollo's paternity sets the story in motion |

## Setting

The palace of the Sun, the zodiac highway across the sky, and the earth below — scorched, cracked, and burning as the out-of-control chariot veers too low.

## Narrative

Phaeton, taunted by Epaphus for claiming divine parentage, travels to the palace of the Sun to demand proof. Apollo acknowledges him as his son and swears by the Styx — the unbreakable oath — to grant him any wish. Phaeton demands to drive the sun-chariot. Apollo warns him of the dangers — the steep ascent, the terrifying height, the monstrous constellations (the Crab, the Scorpion) — but Phaeton insists. Once aloft, the horses sense the weakness of the driver, bolt from their course, and plunge the chariot close to the earth, scorching it. The Earth herself cries out in protest. Jupiter, to prevent cosmic destruction, strikes Phaeton from the chariot with a thunderbolt. The youth falls like a shooting star into the river Eridanus.

## The Transformation

| **Transformed Form** | Falling star → Corpse in the Eridanus → Monument and mourning sisters (the Heliades) → Poplar trees |
| **Agent** | Jupiter (thunderbolt); Helios (grief) transmutes the sisters |
| **Cause** | Hubris — mortal overreach into divine territory |

## Themes

- **Hubris and its price**: Phaeton's ambition is sympathetic but fatal
- **The weak charioteer**: A political allegory — the ruler who cannot control the reins
- **Cosmic fragility**: The ordered cosmos is vulnerable to a single mortal's mistake
- **Grief as transformation**: Phaeton's sisters, weeping, become poplar trees; their tears become amber
