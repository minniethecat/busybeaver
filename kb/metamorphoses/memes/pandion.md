# Pandion

Pandion, king of Athens, is a figure whose brief but consequential appearance in Book VI of the *Metamorphoses* places him at the origin of the Tereus-Procne-Philomela tragedy. When Tereus, king of Thrace, comes to the aid of Athens in war, Pandion rewards him by giving his daughter Procne in marriage — a dynastic alliance that seems prudent but which initiates a chain of rape, mutilation, infanticide, and transformation. Later, Pandion's reluctant consent to let Philomela visit her sister in Thrace seals both daughters' fates. He is the father whose authority, exercised in good faith, produces catastrophe.

## Role in the Metamorphoses

Pandion appears at the beginning of the Tereus episode in Book VI. When Athens is threatened by foreign enemies, Tereus of Thrace — a warlike king descended from Mars — comes to Athens's aid and secures victory. In gratitude, Pandion gives his daughter Procne in marriage to Tereus. The wedding is attended by the Furies, who carry torches stolen from a funeral, and an ill-omened screech-owl perches above the bridal chamber — signs that Pandion, a mortal king, cannot read but that Ovid's audience cannot miss. Years later, Procne asks Tereus to fetch her sister Philomela from Athens for a visit. Pandion, his health failing and sensing something ominous, begs Tereus to protect Philomela as a father would, entrusting her to his care with tears and trembling hands. Tereus swears by the gods to do so — and the reader already knows the horror that awaits. Pandion's final appearance in the narrative is as the worried father who has unknowingly sent his daughter into the hands of her rapist (060-001). He is never shown learning of his daughters' fates: Procne and Philomela transformed into birds, his grandson Itys murdered and served as a feast.

### Key Episodes

- **The Alliance with Thrace**: Pandion gives Procne to Tereus in marriage as a reward for military aid — a decision made under ominous signs
- **The Farewell to Philomela**: The aged king, trembling with foreboding, entrusts Philomela to Tereus's protection and begs him to bring her home soon (060-001)

## Transformation

Pandion himself undergoes no metamorphosis. His tragedy is to be the unwitting origin of the chain of violence:
- **Form**: N/A — he remains unchanged while his daughters are transformed
- **Cause**: The gap between mortal decision-making and divine or fated outcomes — Pandion does everything a king and father should, and it is not enough
