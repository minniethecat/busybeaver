# Cephalus

Cephalus, an Athenian prince renowned for his beauty and skill in hunting, is the central figure of one of the *Metamorphoses*' most tragic love stories. Married to Procris, the daughter of Erechtheus, he was abducted by Aurora (Dawn), who fell in love with him — but Cephalus's heart remained with Procris. Aurora, angered, sowed suspicion between the couple, planting the seed of jealousy. Cephalus, returning to Procris in disguise, tested her fidelity; she wavered, and the marriage was nearly destroyed before being repaired. The reconciliation, however, proved fatal: Procris, suspecting Cephalus of calling upon a lover when he invoked the breeze ("Aura") to cool him after the hunt, spied on him in the forest. Mistaking the rustling for an animal, Cephalus hurled his unerring javelin — a gift from Procris herself — and killed his wife.

## Role in the Metamorphoses

Cephalus's story is told in Book VII, narrated by Cephalus himself to Phocus as an explanation of his marvelous javelin — a weapon that never misses its mark. The tale traces the arc of his relationship with Procris: their marriage, his abduction by Aurora ("Procris to Cephalus in wedlock tied" — 061-010), the goddess's jealous scheming, the near-destruction of their marriage, the reconciliation, and finally the catastrophic hunting accident. Cephalus's invocation to the breeze — "Come, Aura, come" — heard by a gossiping servant and reported to Procris as evidence of a rival, triggers the fatal misunderstanding. It is a story about the impossibility of trust once doubt has been planted, and about the deadly irony of a gift (the javelin) that becomes an instrument of tragedy.

### Key Episodes

- **Abduction by Aurora**: The dawn-goddess seizes the beautiful Cephalus, but he remains faithful in his heart to Procris (061-010)
- **The Test of Fidelity**: Aurora sows suspicion; Cephalus returns disguised and tests Procris — she hesitates, and they separate
- **The Fatal Hunt**: Procris, suspecting infidelity, hides in the woods; Cephalus, mistaking her rustling for game, kills her with his javelin
- **The Narrative Frame**: Cephalus tells his own story to Phocus, the son of Aeacus, explaining the origin of his magical javelin (Book VII)

## Transformation

Cephalus himself does not undergo metamorphosis. His story is one of psychological transformation — trust destroyed, love corrupted, and innocence lost — rather than physical change. The tragedy lies in the gap between intention and outcome: a faithful husband kills the wife he loves through the very token of their reconciliation.
