Diana (Greek: [[Artemis]]) is the twin sister of [[Apollo]], daughter of [[Jupiter]] and [[Latona]], and the goddess of the hunt, wild animals, the moon, and childbirth. She presides over forests and mountains with a train of virgin nymphs. In Ovid's *Metamorphoses*, Diana is defined above all by her uncompromising defense of her chastity — her wrath at those who violate her privacy or the virginity of her followers triggers some of the poem's most brutal transformations, most famously that of [[Actaeon]].

## Role in the Metamorphoses

Diana appears in several books of the *Metamorphoses*, always in narratives that test the boundary between the sacred and the profane. Her virginity is inviolable; to see her naked, to violate her nymphs, or to boast against her mother is to invite swift and terrible retribution. Unlike [[Juno]], whose jealousy is directed at romantic rivals, Diana's wrath is the cold fury of violated sanctity.

### Key Episodes

- **Actaeon's Transformation** (III.138–252): The hunter [[Actaeon]], wandering after a day's sport, stumbles upon Diana bathing in a woodland pool. The goddess, enraged at being seen naked by a mortal man, flings water into his face and transforms him into a stag. His own hounds, failing to recognize their master, tear him to pieces — the most famous punishment-by-metamorphosis in the poem.
- **Callisto's Expulsion** (II.401–530): [[Callisto]], a devoted nymph of Diana's train, is seduced by [[Jupiter]] disguised as Diana herself (013-020, 013-038). When Callisto's pregnancy is discovered at the bathing pool, Diana banishes her from the nymphs' company. The goddess's punishment is for lost virginity, not the deception that caused it — Diana's code is absolute (013-001).
- **The Vengeance on Niobe** (VI.146–312): When [[Niobe]] boasts that she surpasses [[Latona]] in children (seven sons and seven daughters to Latona's two), Diana and [[Apollo]] descend to avenge their mother's honor. Apollo slays the sons; Diana slays the daughters. The bereaved Niobe hardens into a weeping rock on [[Mount Sipylus]].
- **The Death of Chione** (XI.300–345): When [[Chione]], beloved of both [[Mercury]] and Apollo, boasts that her beauty surpasses Diana's, the goddess shoots her through the tongue with an arrow — a punishment precisely fitted to the organ of the offense.

## Transformation

Diana is a powerful **agent of transformation** in the poem:

- **Actaeon**: Man → Stag (transformed by Diana as punishment for seeing her naked; then killed by his own hounds)
- **Niobe's Daughters**: Living women → Corpses (slain by Diana's arrows)
- **Chione**: Living woman → Corpse (shot through the tongue by Diana)

The goddess herself never undergoes metamorphosis; she embodies an eternal, unchanging principle of virgin wildness.
