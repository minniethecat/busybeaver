[065-001] From Corinth [[medea|Medea]] proceeds to Athens, where she becomes the wife of Aegeus, and attempts to poison his son Theseus: the hero, however, is fortunately recognised by his father, who compels his cruel queen to quit the Athenian territories—In the meantime, Minos, king of [[crete|Crete]], threatens to invade Athens, in order to revenge the murder of his son Androgeus—For this purpose he forms treaties of alliance with the neighbouring states, and endeavours to effect a league with Aeacus, the king of Aegina, who continues faithful to his compact with the Athenians, and entertains their ambassador Cephalus with hospitality and kindness.

[065-002] From hence to Athens she directs her flight,

[065-003] Where Phineus, so renown’d for doing right,

[065-004] Where Periphas, and Polyphemon’s niece,

[065-005] Soaring with sudden plumes, amazed the towns of Greece.

[065-006] Here Aegeus so engaging she address’d,

[065-007] That first he treats her like a royal guest,

[065-008] Then takes the sorc’ress for his wedded wife;

[065-009] The only blemish of his prudent life.

[065-010] Meanwhile his son, from actions of renown,

[065-011] Arrives at court, but to his sire unknown.

[065-012] Medea, to despatch a dangerous heir,

[065-013] (She knew him) did a pois’nous draught prepare:

[065-014] Drawn from a drug, was long reserved in store

[065-015] For desperate uses, from the Scythian shore;

[065-016] That from the Echydnaean monster’s jaws

[065-017] Derived its origin, and this the cause:—

[065-018] Through a dark cave a craggy passage lies,

[065-019] To ours ascending from the nether skies,

[065-020] Through which, by strength of hand, Alcides drew

[065-021] Chain’d Cerberus, who lagg’d, and restiff grew,

[065-022] With his blear’d eyes our brighter day to view.

[065-023] Thrice he repeated his enormous yell,

[065-024] With which he scares the ghosts and startles hell;

[065-025] At last outrageous (though compell’d to yield),

[065-026] He sheds his foam in fury on the field,

[065-027] Which, with its own, and rankness of the ground,

[065-028] Produced a weed, by sorcerers renown’d,

[065-029] The strongest constitution to confound,

[065-030] Call’d aconite, because it can unlock

[065-031] All bars, and force its passage through a rock.

[065-032] The pious father, by her wheedles won,

[065-033] Presents this deadly potion to his son,

[065-034] Who with the same assurance takes the cup,

[065-035] And to the monarch’s health had drunk it up:

[065-036] But in the very instant he applied

[065-037] The goblet to his lips, old Aegeus spied

[065-038] The iv’ry-hilted sword that graced his side.

[065-039] That certain signal of his son he knew,

[065-040] And snatch’d the bowl away; the sword he drew;

[065-041] Resolved, for such a son’s endanger’d life,

[065-042] To sacrifice the most perfidious wife.

[065-043] [[revenge|Revenge]] is swift; but her more active charms

[065-044] A whirlwind raised, that snatch’d her from his arms;

[065-045] While conjured clouds their baffled sense surprise,

[065-046] She vanishes from their deluded eyes,

[065-047] And through the hurricane triumphant flies.

[065-048] The gen’rous king, although o’erjoy’d to find

[065-049] His son was safe, yet, bearing still in mind

[065-050] The mischief by his treach’rous queen design’d,

[065-051] The horror of the deed, and then how near

[065-052] The danger drew, lie stands congeal’d with fear.

[065-053] But soon that fear into devotion turns;

[065-054] With grateful incense ev’ry altar burns;

[065-055] Proud victims, and unconscious of their fate,

[065-056] Stalk to the temple, there to die in state.

[065-057] In Athens never had a day been found,

[065-058] For mirth, like that grand festival renown’d.

[065-059] Promiscuously the peers and people dine,

[065-060] Promiscuously their thankful voices join

[065-061] In songs of wit, sublimed by sprightly wine:

[065-062] To list’ning spheres their joint applause they raise,

[065-063] And thus resound their matchless Theseus’ praise:

[065-064] Great Theseus! thee the Marathonian plain

[065-065] Admires, and wears with pride the noble stain

[065-066] Of the dire monster’s blood by valiant Theseus slain:

[065-067] That now Cromyon’s swains in safety sow

[065-068] And reap their fertile field, to thee they owe:

[065-069] By thee the infested Epidaurian coast

[065-070] Was clear’d, and now can a free commerce boast:

[065-071] The traveller his journey can pursue,

[065-072] With pleasure the late dreadful valley view,

[065-073] And cry, “Here Theseus the grand robber slew:

[065-074] Cephisus’ flood cries to his rescued shore;

[065-075] The merciless Procrustes is no more:

[065-076] In peace, Eleusis, Ceres’ rites renew,

[065-077] Since Theseus’ sword the fierce Cercyon slew;

[065-078] By him the torturer Sinis was destroy’d,

[065-079] Of strength (but strength to barb’rous use employ’d)

[065-080] That tops of tallest pines to earth could bend,

[065-081] And thus in pieces wretched captives rend:

[065-082] Inhuman Scyron now has breathed his last,

[065-083] And now Alcatho’s roads securely pass’d;

[065-084] By Theseus slain, and thrown into the deep;

[065-085] But earth nor sea his scatter’d bones would keep,

[065-086] Which, after floating long, a rock became,

[065-087] Still infamous with Scyron’s hated name.

[065-088] When Fame to count thy acts and years proceeds,

[065-089] Thy years appear but ciphers to thy deeds.

[065-090] For thee, brave youth, as for our commonwealth,

[065-091] We pray, and drink, in yours, the public health:

[065-092] Your praise the senate and plebeians sing;

[065-093] With your loved name the court and cottage ring:

[065-094] You make our shepherds and our sailors glad;

[065-095] And not a house in this vast city’s sad.”

[065-096] But mortal bliss will never come sincere:

[065-097] Pleasure may lead, but grief brings up the rear:

[065-098] While, for his son’s arrival, rev’lling joy

[065-099] Aegeus and all his subjects does employ;

[065-100] While they for only costly feasts prepare,

[065-101] His neighb’ring monarch, Minos, threatens war:

[065-102] Weak in land forces, nor by sea more strong,

[065-103] But powerful in a deep-resented wrong;

[065-104] For a son’s murder, arm’d with pious rage:

[065-105] Yet prudently, before he would engage,

[065-106] To raise auxiliaries resolved to sail,

[065-107] And with the powerful princes to prevail.

[065-108] First Anaphe, then proud Astypalaea gains,

[065-109] By presents that, and this by threats, obtains:

[065-110] Low Mycone; Cymolus, chalky soil;

[065-111] Tall Cythnos; Scyros; flat Seriphos’ isle;

[065-112] Paros, with marble cliffs afar display’d;

[065-113] Impregnable Sithonia, yet betray’d

[065-114] To a weak foe, by a gold-admiring maid,

[065-115] Who, changed into a daw of sable hue,

[065-116] Still hoards up gold, and hides it from the view.

[065-117] But as these islands cheerfully combine,

[065-118] Others refuse to embark in his design.

[065-119] Now leftward, with an easy, sail, he bore,

[065-120] And prosperous passage, to Oenopia’s shore;

[065-121] Oenopia once, but now Aegina call’d,

[065-122] And with his royal mother’s name install’d

[065-123] By Aeacus, under whose reign did spring

[065-124] The Myrmidons, and now their reigning king.

[065-125] Down to the port, amid the rabble, run

[065-126] The princes of the blood; with Telamon,

[065-127] Peleus, the next, and Phocus, the third son.

[065-128] Then Aeacus, although oppress’d with years,

[065-129] To ask the cause of their approach appears.

[065-130] That question does the Gnossian’s grief renew,

[065-131] And sighs from his afflicted bosom drew;

[065-132] Yet, after a short solemn respite made,

[065-133] The river of the hundred cities said:

[065-134] “Assist our arms, raised for a murder’d son;

[065-135] In this religious war no risk you’ll run

[065-136] Revenge the dead; for who refuse to give

[065-137] Rest to their urns, unworthy are to live.”

[065-138] “What you request,” thus Aeacus replies,

[065-139] “Not I, but truth and common faith denies:

[065-140] Athens and we have long been sworn allies:

[065-141] Our leagues are fix’d, confed’rate are our powers,

[065-142] And who declare themselves their foes, are ours.”

[065-143] Minos rejoins: “Your league shall dearly cost;”

[065-144] Yet (mindful how much safer ’twas to boast,

[065-145] Than there to waste his forces and his fame,

[065-146] Before in field with his grand foe he came)

[065-147] Parts without blows; nor long had left the shore,

[065-148] Ere into port another navy bore,

[065-149] With Cephalus, and all his jolly crew:

[065-150] The Aeacides their old acquaintance knew.

[065-151] The princes bid him welcome, and in state

[065-152] Conduct the hero to their palace gate,

[065-153] Who, ent’ring, seem’d the charming mien to wear

[065-154] As when in youth he paid his visit there.

[065-155] In his right hand an olive-branch he holds,

[065-156] And, salutation pass’d, the chief unfolds

[065-157] His embassy from the Athenian state,

[065-158] Their mutual friendship, leagues of ancient date,

[065-159] Their common danger; ev’ry thing could wake

[065-160] Concern, and his address successful make;

[065-161] Strength’ning his plea with all the charms of sense,

[065-162] And those, with all the charms of eloquence.

[065-163] Then thus the king: “Like suiters do you stand

[065-164] For that assistance which you may command?

[065-165] Athenians, all our listed forces use

[065-166] (They’re such as no bold service will refuse);

[065-167] And when ye’ve drawn them off, the gods be praised,

[065-168] Fresh legions can within our isle be raised;

[065-169] So stock’d with people, that we can prepare

[065-170] Both for domestic and for distant war,

[065-171] Ours or our friends’ insulters to chastise.”

[065-172] “Long may he flourish thus,” the prince replies.

[065-173] “Strange transport seized me as I pass’d along,

[065-174] To meet so many troops and all so young,

[065-175] As if your army did of twins consist;

[065-176] Yet among them my late acquaintance miss’d;

[065-177] Ev’n all that to your palace did resort,

[065-178] When first you entertain’d me at your court;

[065-179] And cannot guess the cause from whence could spring

[065-180] So vast a change.” Then thus the sighing king:

[065-181] “Illustrious guest, to my strange tale attend,

[065-182] Of sad beginning, but a joyful end:

[065-183] The whole to a vast history would swell;

[065-184] I shall but half, and that confusedly, tell.

[065-185] That race whom so deservedly you admired

[065-186] Are all into their silent tombs retired:

[065-187] They fell, and falling, how they shook my state,

[065-188] Thought may conceive, but words can ne’er relate.”
