# Aeneas

Aeneas, son of the goddess Venus and the mortal Anchises, is the Trojan hero whose journey from fallen Troy to Italy provides the mythological foundation for Rome. In Ovid's *Metamorphoses*, Aeneas appears primarily in the later books (XIII–XIV) as a figure linking Greek myth to Roman destiny — the man whose wanderings, katabasis, and eventual deification bridge the worlds of Homeric epic and Augustan ideology.

## Role in the Metamorphoses

Aeneas enters the *Metamorphoses* in Book XIII, after the fall of Troy, and his story continues through Book XIV. Ovid treats the Aeneas material selectively, focusing not on the martial epic of Virgil but on the metamorphic episodes that occur during and after his wanderings. His journey takes him from Troy to Delos (where Anius, priest of Apollo, hosts him and his father), to Sicily, to Carthage, and ultimately to Italy (119-001). His descent to the underworld to consult his father Anchises — narrated in Book XIV — is one of the poem's key katabasis episodes, linking the *Metamorphoses* to Virgil's *Aeneid* while maintaining Ovid's distinctive focus on transformation rather than empire.

### Key Episodes

- **Hospitality of Anius**: Aeneas and Anchises are received by Anius, priest of Apollo at Delos, whose daughters have been transformed into doves (119-001)
- **Descent to the Underworld**: Aeneas visits the infernal regions to consult his father Anchises, one of the poem's most significant katabasis narratives
- **The Trojan Ships Transformed**: When Turnus attacks, the Trojan ships are transformed into sea-nymphs by Cybele to prevent their destruction
- **Apotheosis of Aeneas**: After his death, Venus obtains his deification — his mortal part is washed away and he is numbered among the gods, becoming the deity Indiges

## Transformation

- **Form**: Mortal hero → God (Indiges)
- **Agent**: Venus, with the consent of Jupiter — his mortal elements are purified in the waters of the Numicius river
- **Cause**: Reward for his pietas and his role in founding the Roman people
