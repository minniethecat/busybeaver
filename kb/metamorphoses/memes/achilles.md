# Achilles

Achilles, son of Peleus and the sea-nymph Thetis, is the supreme Greek warrior of the Trojan War — a hero whose martial excellence is shadowed, in Ovid's *Metamorphoses*, by the knowledge of his mortality. Rather than narrating Achilles's exploits directly (which belonged more properly to epic), Ovid focuses on the liminal moments around his life: the wedding of his parents Peleus and Thetis in Book XI, his death at Troy and the contest for his arms between Ajax and Ulysses in Books XII and XIII, and the posthumous appearance of his ghost demanding the sacrifice of Polyxena. Achilles thus functions in the poem as a boundary figure — the mortal hero whose death marks the transition from epic warfare to the metamorphic logic of the poem's later books.

## Role in the Metamorphoses

Achilles appears across the later books of the poem. His parents' wedding — Peleus and Thetis — is a pivotal event in Book XI, where the uninvited goddess Discordia throws the golden apple that will lead to the Judgment of Paris and the Trojan War itself. In Book XII, Ovid briefly treats Achilles's exploits at Troy — including his duel with Cycnus, whom he kills by strangulation, and whose body is transformed by Neptune. But Achilles's most significant role in the *Metamorphoses* is posthumous: after his death (struck by Paris's arrow in the heel, his one vulnerable spot), the contest for his divine armor — forged by Vulcan — becomes the subject of a rhetorical agon between Ajax and Ulysses in Book XIII. Ulysses's speech, a masterpiece of Ovidian persuasion, wins him the arms; Ajax, humiliated, kills himself and is transformed into the hyacinth flower. Achilles's ghost later appears to demand the sacrifice of Polyxena on his tomb, a grim epilogue to his mortal glory (106-001, 114-001).

### Key Episodes

- **The Wedding of Peleus and Thetis**: The marriage of Achilles's parents — the event that sets the Trojan War in motion through Discordia's apple
- **The Death of Cycnus**: Achilles strangles the invulnerable Trojan warrior Cycnus, who is then transformed into a swan by Neptune (108-001)
- **The Contest for the Arms**: After Achilles's death, Ajax and Ulysses compete for his divine armor in a rhetorical contest — Ulysses wins, Ajax dies and is transformed
- **The Ghost of Achilles**: Achilles's shade demands the sacrifice of Polyxena on his tomb, the final act of his posthumous story

## Transformation

Achilles himself does not undergo physical metamorphosis in the poem. His significance lies in his mortality — the one vulnerable point (his heel) that makes him, uniquely among the sons of goddesses, subject to death:
- **Form**: Mortal hero (despite divine parentage) → Shade (in the underworld)
- **Agent**: Paris (with Apollo's guidance) for his death
- **Cause**: The fatal vulnerability that even a goddess mother could not fully prevent
- **Source**: (114-001)
