# Pegasus

Pegasus, the winged horse of Greek myth, enters the *Metamorphoses* not through his own birth narrative but through his association with the Muses and the sacred spring Hippocrene on Mount Helicon. Sprung from the blood of the Gorgon Medusa when Perseus beheaded her, Pegasus is a creature of liminal power — born from death, he becomes a source of creative inspiration. In Ovid's telling, he is invoked as the discoverer of the Muses' spring and a wonder of the natural world.

## Role in the Metamorphoses

Pegasus appears in Book V, where Minerva visits Mount Helicon to see the Hippocrene spring, which Pegasus struck open with his hoof. The narrative describes Pegasus as having "wing'd his airy course" (039-024) and credits him with discovering the spring: "'Twas Pegasus discover'd first your well" (041-013). The Muses themselves give thanks: "Thanks for our spring to Pegasus are due" (041-025). His presence marks Helicon as a site of poetic inspiration, linking the violent birth from Medusa's blood to the gentler realm of art and knowledge.

### Key Episodes

- **Birth from Medusa**: Pegasus sprang from the blood of the slain Gorgon along with his brother Chrysaor (039-024)
- **The Hippocrene Spring**: Pegasus struck the ground of Mount Helicon with his hoof, bringing forth the sacred spring of the Muses (041-013)
- **Thanks of the Muses**: The Muses acknowledge Pegasus as the source of their inspiration (041-025)

## Transformation

Pegasus himself does not undergo transformation in the *Metamorphoses* — he is the *product* of a transformation (Medusa's death giving rise to new life) and an agent of creation (the spring). His nature as a winged horse is itself miraculous.

- **Form**: Medusa's blood → Winged horse (Pegasus)
- **Agent**: The slaying of Medusa by Perseus
- **Cause**: Generative — new life from monstrous death
