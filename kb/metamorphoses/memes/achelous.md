# Achelous

Achelous is the great river-god of Aetolia, a shape-shifting deity whose combat with [[Hercules]] over the nymph [[Deianira]] is one of the poem's most vivid demonstrations of divine mutability. When physical force fails him, Achelous transforms — first into a serpent, then into a bull — but Hercules overcomes every form, tearing off one of the god's horns. The broken horn, filled with fruits and flowers by the Naiads, becomes the Cornucopia: a metamorphosis of loss into abundance. (072-001)

## Role in the Metamorphoses

Achelous appears in Books VIII and IX as both narrator and participant. When [[Theseus]] and his companions seek shelter at Achelous's grotto on their journey, the river-god tells the story of the Echinades — islands he created by transforming neglectful Naiads — and of Perimele, the nymph he loved and whom [[Neptune]] turned into an island at his plea (072-001). But Achelous's central episode is his own: his battle with Hercules for the hand of the beautiful Deianira. The combat is a spectacle of metamorphic power — the river-god shifts from man to serpent to bull, yet Hercules conquers each form, wrenching off one horn and claiming Deianira as his bride. (073-001)

### Key Episodes

- **The Echinades**: Achelous, offended by Naiads who invited every god but him to a banquet, transforms them into the islands called the Echinades — a demonstration of both his power and his pride (072-001)
- **The Tale of Perimele**: The nymph Perimele, cast into the sea by her enraged father after Achelous violated her, is transformed by Neptune at Achelous's tearful plea into a rocky island — a metamorphosis of pity (073-001)
- **The Combat with Hercules**: Achelous and Hercules both seek Deianira; the river-god declares his divine lineage before the fight, transforming into a serpent, then a bull — Hercules breaks his horn, a wound that becomes the symbol of plenty (072-026)
- **The Horn of Plenty**: The Naiads fill Achelous's broken horn with fruits and fragrant flowers, creating the Cornucopia — a transformation of defeat into eternal abundance (073-001)

## Transformation

Achelous is one of the poem's most prolific self-transformers. His element — water — is itself the medium of change, and his body reflects this: he shifts from human form to serpent to bull at will during combat. Unlike the involuntary metamorphoses of mortals, Achelous's transformations are expressions of divine power — though they ultimately fail against Hercules's strength.

- **Form**: River-god (human) → Serpent → Bull → partially broken (one-horned)
- **Agent**: Self-transformation (voluntary); [[Hercules]] (involuntary — horn removal)
- **Cause**: Combat for the hand of Deianira; the horn removed by force becomes the Cornucopia
- **Source**: (072-026, 073-001)
