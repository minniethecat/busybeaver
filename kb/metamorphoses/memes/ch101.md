[090-001] A poor man named Lygdus directs his wife to destroy her newborn child should it prove a female—The tenderness of a mother induces her to conceal the sex of her daughter, and Lygdus, at a fit age, provides a suitable partner for his supposed son, whose sex is changed by the interposition of the goddess Isis.

[090-002] The fame of this, perhaps, through [[crete|Crete]] had flown,

[090-003] But Crete had newer wonders of her own,

[090-004] In Iphis changed; for near the Gnossian bounds

[090-005] (As loud report the miracle resounds),

[090-006] At Phaestus dwelt a man of honest blood,

[090-007] But meanly born, and not so rich as good,

[090-008] Esteem’d and loved by all the neighbourhood,

[090-009] Who, to his wife, before the time assign’d

[090-010] For childbirth came, thus bluntly spoke his mind:

[090-011] “If heaven,” said Lygdus, “will vouchsafe to hear,

[090-012] I have but two petitions to prefer,

[090-013] Short pains for thee, for me a son and heir.

[090-014] Girls cost as many throes in bringing forth;

[090-015] Besides, when born, they prove of little worth,

[090-016] Weak, puling things, unable to sustain

[090-017] Their share of labour, and their bread to gain.

[090-018] If, therefore, thou a creature shalt produce,

[090-019] Of so great charges, and so little use

[090-020] (Bear witness, heaven, with what reluctancy),

[090-021] Her helpless innocence I doom to die.”

[090-022] He said; and tears the common grief display,

[090-023] Of him who bade, and her who must obey.

[090-024] Yet Telethusa still persists, to find

[090-025] Fit arguments to move a father’s mind,

[090-026] To extend his wishes to a larger scope,

[090-027] And in one vessel not confine his hope.

[090-028] Lygdus continues hard: her time drew near,

[090-029] And she her heavy load could scarcely bear,

[090-030] When slumbering, in the latter shades of night,

[090-031] Before the approaches of returning light,

[090-032] She saw, or thought she saw, before her bed,

[090-033] A glorious train, and Isis at their head:

[090-034] Her moony horns were on her forehead placed,

[090-035] And yellow sheaves her shining temples graced;

[090-036] A mitre, for a crown, she wore on high;

[090-037] The dog and dappled bull were waiting by;

[090-038] Osiris, sought along the banks of Nile:

[090-039] The silent god; the sacred crocodile;

[090-040] And, last, a long procession moving on

[090-041] With timbrels, that assist the labouring moon.

[090-042] Her slumbers seem’d dispell’d, and, broad awake,

[090-043] She heard a voice that thus distinctly spake:

[090-044] “My votary, thy babe from death defend,

[090-045] Nor fear to save whate’er the gods will send.

[090-046] Delude with art thy husband’s dire decree;

[090-047] When danger calls, repose thy trust on me,

[090-048] And know thou hast not served a thankless deity.”

[090-049] This promise made, with night the goddess fled;

[090-050] With joy the woman wakes and leaves her bed,

[090-051] Devoutly lifts her spotless hands on high,

[090-052] And prays the powers their gifts to ratify.

[090-053] Now grinding pains proceed to bearing throes,

[090-054] Till its own weight the burden did disclose.

[090-055] ’Twas of the beauteous kind, and brought to light

[090-056] With secrecy, to shun the father’s sight;

[090-057] The indulgent mother did her care employ,

[090-058] And pass’d it on her husband for a boy.

[090-059] The nurse was conscious of the fact alone.

[090-060] The father paid his vows as for a son,

[090-061] And call’d him Iphis, by a common name,

[090-062] Which either sex with equal right may claim.

[090-063] Iphis his grandsire was; the wife was pleased,

[090-064] Of half the fraud by Fortune’s favour eased.

[090-065] The doubtful name was used without deceit,

[090-066] And truth was cover’d with a pious cheat;

[090-067] The habit show’d a boy, the beauteous face

[090-068] With manly fierceness mingled female grace.

[090-069] Now thirteen years of age were swiftly run,

[090-070] When the fond father thought the time drew on

[090-071] Of settling in the world his only son.

[090-072] Ianthe was his choice, so wondrous fair,

[090-073] Her form alone with Iphis could compare,

[090-074] A neighbour’s daughter of his own degree,

[090-075] And not more bless’d with fortune’s goods than he.

[090-076] They soon espoused; for they with ease were join’d,

[090-077] Who were before contracted in the mind;

[090-078] Their age the same, their inclinations too,

[090-079] And bred together, in one school they grew.

[090-080] Thus, fatally disposed to mutual fires,

[090-081] They felt, before they knew, the same desires;

[090-082] Equal their flame, unequal was their care,

[090-083] One loved with hope, one languish’d in despair;

[090-084] And, scarce refraining tears, “Alas,” said she,

[090-085] “What issue of my love remains for me!

[090-086] How wild a passion works within my breast!

[090-087] With what prodigious flames am I possess’d!

[090-088] Could I the care of Providence deserve,

[090-089] Heaven must destroy me, if it would preserve;

[090-090] And that’s my fate, or sure it would have sent

[090-091] Some usual evil for my punishment:

[090-092] Not this unkindly curse, to rage and burn,

[090-093] Where nature shows no prospect of return.

[090-094] “And yet no guards against our joys conspire,

[090-095] No jealous husband hinders our desire,

[090-096] My parents are propitious to my wish,

[090-097] And she herself consenting to the bliss;

[090-098] All things concur to prosper our design,

[090-099] All things to prosper any love but mine.

[090-100] Heaven has been kind, as far as Heaven can be,

[090-101] Our parents with our own desires agree;

[090-102] But Nature, stronger than the gods above,

[090-103] Refuses her assistance to my love;

[090-104] She sets the bar that causes all my pain:

[090-105] One gift refused makes all their bounty vain

[090-106] And now the happy day is just at hand

[090-107] To bind our hearts in Hymen’s holy band.”

[090-108] Thus lovesick Iphis her vain passion mourns,

[090-109] With equal ardour fair Ianthe burns,

[090-110] Invoking Hymen’s name, and Juno’s power,

[090-111] To speed the work, and haste the happy hour.

[090-112] She hopes, while Telethusa fears the day,

[090-113] And strives to interpose some new delay,

[090-114] Now feigns a sickness, now is in a fright

[090-115] For this bad omen, or that boding sight.

[090-116] But having done whate’er she could devise,

[090-117] And emptied all her magazine of lies,

[090-118] The time approach’d, the next ensuing day

[090-119] The fatal secret must to light betray.

[090-120] Then Telethusa had recourse to prayer,

[090-121] She, and her daughter, with dishevell’d hair;

[090-122] Trembling with fear, great Isis they adored,

[090-123] Embraced her altar, and her aid implored.

[090-124] “Fair queen, who dost on fruitful Egypt smile,

[090-125] Who sway’st the sceptre of the Pharian isle,

[090-126] And sevenfold falls of disemboguing Nile,

[090-127] Relieve, in this our last distress,” she said,

[090-128] “A suppliant mother, and a mournful maid.

[090-129] Thou, goddess, thou wert present to my sight;

[090-130] Reveal’d I saw thee by thy own fair light;

[090-131] I saw thee, in my dream, as now I see,

[090-132] With all thy marks of awful majesty,

[090-133] The glorious train that compass’d thee around,

[090-134] And heard the hollow timbrels’ holy sound.

[090-135] Thy words I noted, which I still retain,

[090-136] Let not thy sacred oracles be vain.

[090-137] That Iphis lives, that I myself am free

[090-138] From shame and punishment, I owe to thee.

[090-139] On thy protection all our hopes depend;

[090-140] Thy counsel saved us, let thy power defend.”

[090-141] Her tears pursued her words, and, while she spoke,

[090-142] The goddess nodded, and her altar shook;

[090-143] The temple doors, as with a blast of wind,

[090-144] Were heard to clap; the lunar horns, that bind

[090-145] The brows of Isis, cast a blaze around,

[090-146] The trembling timbrel made a murm’ring sound.

[090-147] Some hopes these happy omens did impart,

[090-148] Forth went the mother with a beating heart,

[090-149] Not much in fear, nor fully satisfied;

[090-150] But Iphis follow’d with a larger stride:

[090-151] The whiteness of her skin forsook her face,

[090-152] Her looks imbolden’d with an awful grace;

[090-153] Her features and her strength together grew,

[090-154] And her long hair to curling locks withdrew;

[090-155] Her sparkling eyes with manly vigour shone;

[090-156] Big with her voice, audacious was her tone.

[090-157] The maid becomes a youth. No more delay

[090-158] Your vows, but look, and confidently pay.

[090-159] Their gifts the parents to the temple bear,

[090-160] The votive tables this inscription wear:

[090-161] “Iphis, the man, has to the goddess paid

[090-162] The vows that Iphis offer’d when a maid.”

[090-163] Now, when the star of day had shown his face,

[090-164] [[venus|Venus]] and Juno with their presence grace

[090-165] The nuptial rites, and Hymen, from above,

[090-166] Descending to complete their happy love;

[090-167] The gods of marriage lend their mutual aid,

[090-168] And the fond youth obtains the lovely maid.
