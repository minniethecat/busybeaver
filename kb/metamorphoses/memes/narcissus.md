# Narcissus

Narcissus, the impossibly beautiful son of the river-god Cephisus and the nymph Liriope, is the central figure of one of the *Metamorphoses*' most psychologically profound episodes. His refusal of all lovers — including the nymph Echo — and his subsequent fatal infatuation with his own reflected image make him the poem's definitive exploration of identity, desire, and the self transformed by its own gaze.

## Role in the Metamorphoses

Narcissus's story (Book III) is framed by the prophecy of Tiresias: the boy will live long "if he never knows himself" (022-003). The prophecy is fulfilled with terrible irony when Narcissus, bending to drink from a pool, sees his own image and falls in love — not knowing it is himself. His story is intertwined with Echo's: the nymph who can only repeat others' words falls in love with Narcissus, is rejected, and wastes away to a disembodied voice. Narcissus, in turn, falls in love with an image that cannot respond, and wastes away to a flower.

### Key Episodes

- **Rejection of Echo**: Echo, cursed by Juno to repeat only the last words she hears, attempts to embrace Narcissus; he flees, and she wastes away until only her voice remains (022-001)
- **The Pool**: Thirsty from hunting, Narcissus bends to drink and sees his own face reflected in the water — "He sees, and weeps at his approaching fate" (026-022)
- **Self-Recognition**: Realizing too late that the beloved image is himself, Narcissus beats his breast in grief — the recognition that should save him, per the prophecy, arrives only when salvation is impossible (023-001)
- **Transformation into a Flower**: After his death, the nymphs prepare a funeral pyre but find only a flower — white petals surrounding a yellow center, the narcissus (023-001)

## Transformation

Narcissus's transformation is both physical (body to flower) and psychological (self to image of self). The flower that replaces his body — growing by the pool that mirrored his image — makes the transformation permanent: Narcissus becomes what he loved, a beautiful object of contemplation that returns the viewer's gaze.

- **Form**: Beautiful youth → Narcissus flower
- **Agent**: Self-inflicted (through the inability to leave his own reflection)
- **Cause**: Self-love; the fatal realization of Tiresias's prophecy
- **Source**: (023-001)
