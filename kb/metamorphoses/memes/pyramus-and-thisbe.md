# Pyramus and Thisbe

Pyramus and Thisbe, two young lovers in Babylon separated by a wall and parental prohibition, arrange a secret meeting — only for a series of misapprehensions to lead to their mutual deaths. Their blood stains the white mulberries dark red, and the fruit remains so forever.

## Characters

| Character | Role |
|-----------|------|
| Pyramus | Babylonian youth, the most beautiful of young men |
| Thisbe | Babylonian maiden, equally beautiful |

## Setting

Babylon, where neighboring houses share a wall with a crack through which the lovers whisper. Their meeting place: the tomb of Ninus beneath a white-fruited mulberry tree.

## Narrative

Pyramus and Thisbe, forbidden to marry by their parents, communicate through a crack in the shared wall. They arrange to meet at night by the tomb of Ninus. Thisbe arrives first; a lioness, jaws bloody from a recent kill, comes to drink at the nearby spring. Thisbe flees, dropping her veil. The lioness tears the veil, leaving bloodstains. Pyramus arrives, finds the bloodied veil, and assumes Thisbe has been devoured. He stabs himself beneath the mulberry tree. Thisbe returns, finds Pyramus dying, and stabs herself with the same sword. Their blood soaks the tree's roots, staining the white fruit dark red — as it remains today.

## Themes

- **Miscommunication**: The crack in the wall is both the lovers' connection and the symbol of their fatal distance
- **Double suicide**: The symmetrical deaths (Pyramus assumes → acts → Thisbe sees → acts) create a perfect tragic structure
- **The permanent stain**: The mulberry's dark color preserves the memory of the tragedy in nature itself
