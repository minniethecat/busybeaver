[096-001] [[venus|Venus]] becomes enamoured of young Adonis, whom she cautions against the pursuit of wild beasts, lest he should meet a premature death—The youth disregards this advice, and receives a mortal bite from a wild boar which he has wounded; and Venus, after lamenting his fate, changes him into a flower called anemone.

[096-002] For Cytherea’s lips while Cupid press’d,

[096-003] He with a heedless arrow razed her breast:

[096-004] The goddess felt it, and, with fury stung,

[096-005] The wanton mischief from her bosom flung:

[096-006] Yet thought at first the danger slight; but found

[096-007] The dart too faithful, and too deep the wound.

[096-008] Fired with a mortal beauty, she disdains

[096-009] To haunt the Idalian mount or Phrygian plains:

[096-010] She seeks not Cnidos, nor her Paphian shrines

[096-011] Nor Amathus, that teems with brazen mines:

[096-012] Ev’n heaven itself, with all its sweets unsought,

[096-013] Adonis far a sweeter heaven is thought:

[096-014] On him she hangs, and fonds with ev’ry art,

[096-015] And never, never knows from him to part.

[096-016] She whose soft limbs had only been display’d

[096-017] On rosy beds, beneath the myrtle shade,

[096-018] Whose pleasing care was to improve each grace,

[096-019] And add more charms to an unrivall’d face,

[096-020] Now buskin’d, like the virgin huntress, goes

[096-021] Through woods, and pathless wilds, and mountain snows:

[096-022] With her own tuneful voice she joys to cheer

[096-023] The panting hounds, that chase the flying deer:

[096-024] She runs the labyrinth of fearful hares;

[096-025] But fearless beasts and dangerous prey forbears;

[096-026] Hunts not the grinning wolf or foamy boar,

[096-027] And trembles at the lion’s hungry roar.

[096-028] Thee too, Adonis, with a lover’s care,

[096-029] She warns, if warn’d, thou wouldst avoid the snare:

[096-030] “To furious animals advance not nigh;

[096-031] Ply those that follow, follow those that fly;

[096-032] ’Tis chance alone must the survivors save,

[096-033] Whene’er brave spirits will attempt the brave.

[096-034] Oh, lovely youth! in harmless sports delight;

[096-035] Provoke not beasts, which, arm’d by nature, fight:

[096-036] For me, if not thyself, vouchsafe to fear;

[096-037] Let not thy thirst of glory cost me dear.

[096-038] Boars know not how to spare a blooming age,

[096-039] No sparkling eyes can soothe the lion’s rage:

[096-040] Nor all thy charms a savage breast can move,

[096-041] Which have so deeply touch’d the queen of love.

[096-042] When bristled boars from beaten thickets spring,

[096-043] In grinded tusks a thunderbolt they bring:

[096-044] The daring hunters lions roused devour;

[096-045] Vast is their fury, and as vast their power:

[096-046] Cursed be their tawny race: if thou wouldst hear

[096-047] What kindled thus my hate, then lend an ear;

[096-048] The wondrous tale I will to thee unfold,

[096-049] How the fell monsters rose from crimes of old:

[096-050] But by long toils I faint. See! wide display’d,

[096-051] A grateful poplar courts us with a shade;

[096-052] The grassy turf, beneath, so verdant shows,

[096-053] We may secure delightfully repose:

[096-054] “Perhaps thou mayst have heard a virgin’s name,

[096-055] Who still in swiftness swiftest youths o’ercame.

[096-056] Wondrous, that female weakness should outdo

[096-057] A manly strength; the wonder yet is true

[096-058] ’Twas doubtful if her triumphs in the field

[096-059] Did to her form’s triumphant glories yield;

[096-060] Whether her face could with more ease decoy

[096-061] A crowd of lovers, or her feet destroy:

[096-062] For once [[apollo|Apollo]] she implored to show

[096-063] If courteous fates a consort would allow.

[096-064] ‘A consort brings thy ruin,’ he replied:

[096-065] ‘Oh learn to want the pleasures of a bride!

[096-066] Nor shalt thou want them to thy wretched cost,

[096-067] And Atalanta living shall be lost.’

[096-068] With such a rueful fate the affrighted maid

[096-069] Sought green recesses in the woodland glade;

[096-070] Nor signing suitors her resolves could move;

[096-071] She bade them show their speed, to show their love.

[096-072] He only who could conquer in the race

[096-073] Might hope the conquer’d virgin to embrace;

[096-074] While he whose tardy feet had lagg’d behind,

[096-075] Was doom’d the sad reward of death to find.

[096-076] Though great the prize, yet rigid the decree;

[096-077] But blind with beauty, who can rigour see?

[096-078] Ev’n on these laws the fair they rashly sought,

[096-079] And danger in excess of love forgot.

[096-080] “There sat Hippomenes, prepared to blame

[096-081] In lovers such extravagance of flame.

[096-082] ‘And must,’ he said, ‘the blessings of a wife

[096-083] Be dearly purchased by a risk of life?’

[096-084] But when he saw the wonders of her face,

[096-085] And her limbs naked, springing to the race,

[096-086] Her limbs, as exquisitely turned as mine,

[096-087] Or, if a woman thou, might vie with thine,

[096-088] With lifted hands, he cried, ‘Forgive the tongue

[096-089] Which durst, ye youths, your well-timed courage wrong:

[096-090] I knew not that the nymph for whom you strove

[096-091] Deserved the unbounded transports of your love.’

[096-092] He saw, admired, and thus her spotless frame

[096-093] He praised, and praising, kindled his own flame.

[096-094] A rival now to all the youths who run,

[096-095] Envious, he fears they should not be undone.

[096-096] ‘But why,’ reflects he, ‘idly thus is shown

[096-097] The fate of others, yet untried my own?

[096-098] The coward must not on love’s aid depend;

[096-099] The god was ever to the bold a friend.’

[096-100] Meantime the virgin flies, or seems to fly,

[096-101] Swift as a Scythian arrow cleaves the sky:

[096-102] Still more and more the youth her charms admires:

[096-103] The race itself to exalt her charms conspires.

[096-104] The golden pinions, which her feet adorn,

[096-105] In wanton flutterings by the winds are borne:

[096-106] Down from her head the long fair tresses flow,

[096-107] And sport with lovely negligence below:

[096-108] The waving ribbons, which her buskins tie,

[096-109] Her snowy skin with waving purple die;

[096-110] As crimson veils in palaces display’d,

[096-111] To the white marble lend a blushing shade.

[096-112] Nor long he gazed, yet while he gazed, she gain’d

[096-113] The goal, and the victorious wreath obtain’d.

[096-114] The vanquish’d sigh, and, as the law decreed,

[096-115] Pay the dire forfeit, and prepare to bleed.

[096-116] “Then rose Hippomenes, not yet afraid,

[096-117] And fix’d his eyes full on the beauteous maid.

[096-118] ‘Where is,’ he cried, ‘the mighty conquest won,

[096-119] To distance those who want the nerves to run?

[096-120] Here prove superior strength; nor shall it be

[096-121] Thy loss of glory, if excell’d by me.

[096-122] High my descent; near Neptune I aspire,

[096-123] For Neptune was grand parent to my sire:

[096-124] From that great god the fourth myself I trace,

[096-125] Nor sink my virtues yet beneath my race.

[096-126] Thou from Hippomenes, o’ercome, mayst claim

[096-127] An envied triumph, and a deathless fame.’

[096-128] “While thus the youth the virgin power defies,

[096-129] Silent she views him still with softer eyes:

[096-130] Thoughts in her breast a doubtful strife begin:

[096-131] If ’tis not happier now to lose than win.

[096-132] ‘What god, a foe to beauty, would destroy

[096-133] The promised ripeness of this blooming boy?

[096-134] With his life’s danger does he seek my bed?

[096-135] Scarce am I half so greatly worth,’ she said.

[096-136] ‘Nor has his beauty moved my breast to love;

[096-137] And yet, I own, such beauty well might move;

[096-138] ’Tis not his charms, ’tis pity would engage

[096-139] My soul to spare the greenness of his age.

[096-140] What, that heroic courage fires his breast,

[096-141] And shines through brave disdain of fate confess’d?

[096-142] What, that his patronage by close degrees

[096-143] Springs from the imperial ruler of the seas?

[096-144] Then add the love, which bids him undertake

[096-145] The race, and dare to perish for my sake.

[096-146] Of bloody nuptials, heedless youth, beware!

[096-147] Fly, timely fly, from a too barb’rous fair.

[096-148] At pleasure choose: thy love will be repaid

[096-149] By a less foolish and more beauteous maid.

[096-150] But why this tenderness, before unknown?

[096-151] Why beats and pants my breast for him alone?

[096-152] His eyes have seen his numerous rivals yield;

[096-153] Let him too share the rigour of the field,

[096-154] Since, by their fates untaught, his own he courts,

[096-155] And thus with ruin insolently sports.

[096-156] Yet for what crime shall he his death receive?

[096-157] Is it a crime with me to wish to live?

[096-158] Shall his kind passion his destruction prove?

[096-159] Is this the fatal recompense of love?

[096-160] So fair a youth destroy’d, would conquest shame,

[096-161] And nymphs eternally detest my fame.

[096-162] Still why should nymphs my guiltless fame upbraid?

[096-163] Did I the fond adventurer persuade?

[096-164] Alas! I wish thou wouldst the course decline,

[096-165] Or that my swiftness was excell’d by thine.

[096-166] See what a virgin’s bloom adorns the boy!

[096-167] Why wilt thou run, and why thyself destroy?

[096-168] Hippomenes! oh that I ne’er had been

[096-169] By those bright eyes unfortunately seen!

[096-170] Ah! tempt not thus a swift untimely fate;

[096-171] Thy life is worthy of the longest date.’

[096-172] “Thus she disclosed the woman’s secret heart,

[096-173] Young, innocent, and new to Cupid’s dart.

[096-174] Her thoughts, her words, her actions, wildy rove,

[096-175] With love she burns, yet knows not that ’tis love.

[096-176] “Her royal sire now with the murm’ring crowd.

[096-177] Demands the race impatiently aloud.

[096-178] Hippomenes then with true fervour pray’d:

[096-179] ‘My bold attempt let Venus kindly aid:

[096-180] By her sweet power I felt this amorous fire;

[096-181] Still may she succour whom she did inspire.’

[096-182] A soft, unenvious wind, with speedy care,

[096-183] Wafted to heaven the lover’s tender prayer.

[096-184] Pity, I own, soon gain’d the wish’d consent,

[096-185] And all the assistance he implored I lent.

[096-186] The Cyprian lands, though rich, in richness yield

[096-187] To that surnamed the Tamasenian field:

[096-188] That field of old was added to my shrine,

[096-189] And its choice products consecrated mine:

[096-190] A tree there stands, full glorious to behold,

[096-191] Gold are the leaves, the crackling branches gold,

[096-192] It chanced, three apples in my hands I bore,

[096-193] Which newly from the tree I sportive tore;

[096-194] Seen by the youth alone, to him I brought

[096-195] The fruit, and when and how to use it taught.

[096-196] The signal sounding by the king’s command,

[096-197] Both start at once, and sweep the imprinted sand:

[096-198] So swiftly moved their feet, they might with ease,

[096-199] Scarce moisten’d, skim along the glassy seas;

[096-200] Or, with a wondrous levity be borne

[096-201] O’er yellow harvests of unbending corn.

[096-202] Now favouring peals resound from every part,

[096-203] Spirit the youth, and fire his fainting heart.

[096-204] ‘Hippomenes!’ they cried, ‘thy life preserve,

[096-205] Intensely labour, and stretch every nerve:

[096-206] Base fear alone can baffle thy design;

[096-207] Shoot boldly onward, and the gaol is thine.’

[096-208] ’Tis doubtful whether shouts like these convey’d

[096-209] More pleasures to the youth or to the maid.

[096-210] When a long distance oft she could have gain’d,

[096-211] She check’d her swiftness, and her feet restrain’d:

[096-212] She sigh’d, and dwelt, and languish’d, on his face,

[096-213] Then with unwilling speed pursued the race.

[096-214] O’erspent with heat, his breath he faintly drew,

[096-215] Parch’d was his mouth, nor yet the gaol in view,

[096-216] And the first apple on the plain he threw.

[096-217] The nymph stopp’d sudden at the unusual sight,

[096-218] Struck with the fruit so beautifully bright.

[096-219] Aside she starts, the wonder to behold,

[096-220] And eager stoops to catch the rolling gold.

[096-221] The observant youth pass’d by, and scour’d along,

[096-222] While peals of joy rung from the applauding throng.

[096-223] Unkindly she corrects the short delay,

[096-224] And to redeem the time fleets swift away,

[096-225] Swift as the lightning, or the northern wind,

[096-226] And far she leaves the panting youth behind.

[096-227] Again he strives the flying nymph to hold

[096-228] With the temptation of the second gold:

[096-229] The bright temptation fruitlessly was toss’d

[096-230] So soon, alas! she won the distance lost.

[096-231] Now but a little interval of space

[096-232] Remain’d for the decision of the race.

[096-233] ‘Fair author of the precious gift,’ he said,

[096-234] ‘Be thou, oh goddess, author of my aid!’

[096-235] Then of the shining fruit the last he drew,

[096-236] And with his full-collected vigour threw;

[096-237] The virgin still the longer to detain,

[096-238] Threw not directly, but across the plain.

[096-239] She seem’d a while perplex’d in dubious thought,

[096-240] If the far distant apple should be sought:

[096-241] I lured her backward mind to seize the bait,

[096-242] And to the massy gold gave double weight:

[096-243] My favour to my votary was show’d;

[096-244] Her speed I lessen’d, and increased her load.

[096-245] But lest, though long, the rapid race he run,

[096-246] Before my longer, tedious tale is done,

[096-247] The youth the gaol, and so the virgin, won.

[096-248] “Might I, Adonis, now not hope to see

[096-249] His grateful thanks pour’d out for victory?

[096-250] His pious incense on my altars laid?

[096-251] But he nor grateful thanks, nor incense paid.

[096-252] Enraged, I vow’d, that with the youth the fair,

[096-253] For his contempt, should my keen vengeance share:

[096-254] That future lovers might my power revere,

[096-255] And, from their sad examples, learn to fear.

[096-256] The silent fanes, the sanctified abodes,

[096-257] Of Cybele, great mother of the gods,

[096-258] Raised by Echion in a lonely wood,

[096-259] And full of brown, religious horror stood:

[096-260] By a long painful Journey faint, they chose

[096-261] Their weary limbs here secret to repose.

[096-262] But soon my power inflamed the lustful boy;

[096-263] Careless of rest, he sought untimely joy.

[096-264] A hallow’d gloomy cave, with moss o’ergrown,

[096-265] The temple join’d, of native pumice stone,

[096-266] Where antique images by priests were kept,

[096-267] And wooden deities securely slept;

[096-268] Thither the rash Hippomenes retires,

[096-269] And gives a loose to all his wild desires,

[096-270] And the chaste cell pollutes with wanton fires.

[096-271] The sacred statues trembled with surprise;

[096-272] The towery goddess, blushing, veil’d her eyes,

[096-273] And the vile pair to Stygian sounds had sent;

[096-274] But unrevengeful seem’d that punishment:

[096-275] A heavier doom such black profaneness draws—

[096-276] Their taper fingers turn to crooked paws:

[096-277] No more their necks the smoothness can retain,

[096-278] Now cover’d sudden with a yellow mane:

[096-279] Arms change to legs: each finds the hard’ning breast

[096-280] Of rage unknown, and wond’rous strength possess’d:

[096-281] Their alter’d looks with fury grim appear;

[096-282] And on the ground their brushing tails they bear:

[096-283] They haunt the woods: their voices, which before

[096-284] Were musically sweet, now hoarsely roar.

[096-285] Hence lions, dreadful to the lab’ring swains,

[096-286] Are tamed by Cybele, and curb’d with reins,

[096-287] And humbly draw her car along the plains.

[096-288] ‘But thou, Adonis, my delightful care,

[096-289] Of these, and beasts as fierce as these, beware!

[096-290] The savage, which not shuns thee, timely shun;

[096-291] For by rash prowess shouldst thou be undone,

[096-292] A double ruin is contain’d in one.’ ”

[096-293] Thus cautious Venus school’d her favourite boy;

[096-294] But youthful heat all cautions will destroy.

[096-295] His sprightly soul beyond grave counsel flies,

[096-296] While with yoked swans the goddess cuts the skies

[096-297] His faithful hounds, led by the tainted wind,

[096-298] Lodged in thick coverts chanced a boar to find.

[096-299] The callow hero show’d a manly heart,

[096-300] And pierced the savage with a sidelong dart:

[096-301] The flying savage, wounded, turn’d again,

[096-302] Wrench’d out the gory dart, and foam’d with pain.

[096-303] The trembling boy by flight his safety sought,

[096-304] And now recall’d the lore which Venus taught:

[096-305] But now, too late, to fly the boar he strove,

[096-306] Who in the groin his tusks impetuous drove:

[096-307] On the discolour’d grass Adonis lay—

[096-308] The monster trampling o’er his beauteous prey.

[096-309] Fair Cytherea, [[cyprus|Cyprus]] scarce in view,

[096-310] Heard from afar his groans, and own’d them true,

[096-311] And turn’d her snowy swans, and backward flew.

[096-312] But as she saw him gasp his latest breath,

[096-313] And quivering agonize in pangs of death,

[096-314] Down with swift flight she plunged, nor rage forbore,

[096-315] At once her garments and her hair she tore:

[096-316] With cruel blows she beat her guiltless breast,

[096-317] The fates upbraided, and her love confess’d.

[096-318] “Nor shall they yet,” she cried, “the whole devour,

[096-319] With uncontroll’d inexorable power.

[096-320] For thee, lost youth, my tears and restless pain

[096-321] Shall in immortal monuments remain:

[096-322] With solemn pomp, in annual rites return’d,

[096-323] Be thou for ever, my Adonis, mourn’d.

[096-324] Could Pluto’s queen with jealous fury storm,

[096-325] And Menthe to a fragrant herb transform?

[096-326] Yet dares not Venus with a change surprise,

[096-327] And in a flower bid her fallen hero rise?”

[096-328] Then on the blood sweet nectar she bestows—

[096-329] The scented blood in little bubbles rose;

[096-330] Little as rainy drops, which fluttering fly,

[096-331] Borne by the winds, along a lowering sky.

[096-332] Short time ensued, till where the blood was shed,

[096-333] A flower began to rear its purple head;

[096-334] Such as on Punic apples is reveal’d,

[096-335] Or in the filmy rind but half conceal’d.

[096-336] Still here the fate of lovely forms we see,

[096-337] So sudden fades the sweet anemone:

[096-338] The feeble stems, to stormy blasts a prey,

[096-339] Their sickly beauties droop, and pine away:

[096-340] The winds forbid the flowers to flourish long,

[096-341] Which, owe to winds their names in Grecian song.
