# Daphne

Daphne, daughter of the river-god Peneus, is the subject of the *Metamorphoses*' first major transformation: pursued by Apollo, she prays for escape and is changed into a laurel tree. Her metamorphosis establishes the poem's fundamental pattern — god desires, mortal flees, transformation resolves — and her laurel becomes Apollo's sacred plant, worn by poets and triumphators throughout antiquity.

## Role in the Metamorphoses

Daphne's episode (Book I) occurs immediately after Apollo's slaying of Python and his mockery of Cupid's archery. Cupid's revenge — striking Apollo with a golden arrow of desire and Daphne with a leaden arrow of aversion — sets the story in motion. Apollo pursues; Daphne flees; her prayer to her river-god father transforms her into a laurel tree. Apollo, unable to possess the woman, claims the tree: "Since thou canst not be my bride, thou shalt be my tree" — and the laurel becomes the symbol of poetic and military victory.

### Key Episodes

- **Cupid's Arrows**: Apollo, proud from slaying Python, mocks Cupid; the love-god strikes Apollo with desire and Daphne with aversion — the asymmetrical passion that drives the pursuit (007-001)
- **The Pursuit**: Apollo chases Daphne through the woods, calling out his divine credentials — but divine status is no advantage when the beloved flees (007-001)
- **The Transformation**: Exhausted, Daphne prays to her father Peneus; her feet become roots, her arms branches, her hair leaves — she is absorbed into the landscape (007-001)
- **Apollo's Claim**: Apollo embraces the laurel, declares it his sacred tree, and wreathes his brow with its leaves — the transformation of bodily desire into symbolic possession (007-001)

## Transformation

Daphne's transformation is the **founding metamorphosis** of the poem: the first human-to-plant change, establishing the pattern of desire → flight → prayer → transformation that recurs in dozens of subsequent episodes. Her laurel becomes a living symbol — worn by poets, emperors, and victors — of the intersection between desire, art, and power.

- **Form**: Nymph → Laurel tree (*Laurus nobilis*)
- **Agent**: Peneus (her river-god father), answering her prayer
- **Cause**: Escape from Apollo's pursuit; preservation of virginity
