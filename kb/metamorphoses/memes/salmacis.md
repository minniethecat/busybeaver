# Salmacis

Salmacis is a nymph associated with a spring in Caria whose waters have the peculiar reputation of weakening and softening those who bathe in them. Unlike other predatory nymphs in the *Metamorphoses*, Salmacis does not pursue transformation but instead achieves complete fusion with the object of her desire: the beautiful youth Hermaphroditus. When he enters her pool, she wraps herself around him and prays that they may never be separated — the gods grant her wish, and the two become a single being, both male and female. The Salmacis episode is one of Ovid's most striking explorations of erotic union, bodily dissolution, and the paradox of desire fulfilled through loss of self.

## Role in the Metamorphoses

Salmacis appears in Book IV, introduced in a phrase that defines her by her spring's effect: "How Salmacis, with weak, enfeebling stream" (031-002). The narrator reproaches her directly — "'Fy, Salmacis: what! always idle; fy!'" (031-030) — for her indolence, contrasting her with the hunting nymphs like Diana's followers. Her refusal to hunt, her obsession with her own beauty, and her singular fixation on Hermaphroditus set her apart typologically: she is the anti-huntress, the nymph who consumes rather than flees. By the poem's end, she is remembered simply as "Salmacis obscene" (140-480), a figure whose name became a byword for sexual dissolution.

### Key Episodes

- **The Enfeebling Spring**: Salmacis's pool is introduced as a place whose waters weaken those who enter (031-002)
- **The Reproach of Idleness**: The narrator rebukes Salmacis for refusing to join the hunt, preferring her mirror and her pool (031-030)
- **Fusion with Hermaphroditus**: Salmacis embraces Hermaphroditus in the water and prays for eternal union; their bodies merge (Book IV)

## Transformation

Salmacis does not transform *into* something else — she transforms *with* another. Her merging with Hermaphroditus produces a single being who is both male and female, an act of metamorphosis driven by erotic desire rather than punishment or escape. This is one of the few voluntary fusions in the poem.

- **Form**: Salmacis (nymph) + Hermaphroditus (male youth) → Hermaphroditus (dual-sex being)
- **Agent**: The gods, answering Salmacis's prayer
- **Cause**: Salmacis's desire for eternal union with Hermaphroditus
- **Source**: (031-002)
