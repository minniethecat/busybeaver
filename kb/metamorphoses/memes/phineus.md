# Phineus

Phineus, the blind king and prophet of Thrace, appears in the *Metamorphoses* as a figure of suffering and divine punishment. Tormented by the Harpies — monstrous bird-women who snatch or defile his food — he is rescued by the Argonauts Zetes and Calais, the winged sons of Boreas. His story bridges the poem's episodic narrative with the larger epic tradition of the Argonautica.

## Role in the Metamorphoses

Phineus represents the peril of prophetic knowledge. His punishment — blindness and the endless torment of the Harpies — is the price exacted by the gods for a seer who revealed divine secrets too freely. His rescue by the Boreads connects Ovid's mythological tapestry to the Argonautic cycle, reminding readers that the *Metamorphoses*' transformations unfold within a shared heroic universe.

### Key Episodes

- **The Torment**: Phineus, cursed by the gods, is beset by the Harpies whenever he attempts to eat — they seize or befoul his food, leaving him perpetually starving in the midst of abundance
- **The Rescue**: The Boreads, sons of the North Wind, pursue the Harpies across the sky and drive them away from Phineus, freeing the seer from his torment — an act of heroic intervention
- **Prophecy in Return**: In gratitude, Phineus reveals to the Argonauts the path through the Symplegades — the Clashing Rocks — enabling their continued voyage
