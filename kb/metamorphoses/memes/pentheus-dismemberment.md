# The Dismemberment of Pentheus

Pentheus's denial of Bacchus is answered with the most violent transformation in the *Metamorphoses* — not metamorphosis of form but dismemberment of body. Torn apart by the Maenads, with his own mother Agave leading the assault, Pentheus's death is the definitive consequence of refusing to recognize divine power.

## Characters

| Character | Role |
|-----------|------|
| Pentheus | King of Thebes, denies Bacchus's divinity |
| Bacchus | God of wine and ecstasy, the denied deity |
| Agave | Pentheus's mother, driven to Bacchic frenzy, leads his dismemberment |

## Setting

Mount Cithaeron, where the Bacchic rites are celebrated, and the woods where Pentheus spies upon the Maenads — his voyeurism becoming the justification for their assault.

## Narrative

Pentheus, refusing to acknowledge his cousin Bacchus as a god, has the god imprisoned. Bacchus escapes; the chains fall away; the prison doors open. Pentheus ascends Mount Cithaeron to spy on the Bacchic rites. The Maenads, seeing him, mistake him for a wild boar — Agave, his mother, at their head. She tears off his arm; the others dismember him limb by limb. Only after the frenzy subsides does Agave realize what she has done, holding her son's severed head.
