# Catasterism

Catasterism (from Greek *katasterismos*, "placement among the stars") is a distinctive form of transformation in the *Metamorphoses*: a figure is changed not into an animal, plant, or stone but into a constellation, their body dissolved and reassembled as a pattern of stars in the night sky. Catasterism represents transformation at its most transcendent — the change from mortal to eternal, from terrestrial to celestial.

## Definition

In Ovid's treatment, catasterism differs from ordinary metamorphosis in two ways. First, it is always a **reward** or compensation — unlike punitive transformations (wolf, spider, stone), placement among the stars honors the transformed figure. Second, it is **incomplete as bodily change**: the figure's form is recognized in the constellation's pattern (Callisto as Ursa Major, the bear), but the body itself is dematerialized into starlight. Catasterism thus occupies a middle ground between bodily metamorphosis and full apotheosis — the figure becomes immortal but not divine, visible but not tangible. This liminal status makes catasterism particularly suited to figures whose stories involve injustice: transformation into stars compensates for suffering on earth.

## Significance

Catasterism is the **cosmic dimension** of Ovidian metamorphosis. While most transformations in the poem are downward movements — human to animal, animal to stone, stone to water — catasterism moves upward, from earth to sky. This upward trajectory becomes increasingly important as the poem progresses toward its Roman conclusion: Caesar's comet (145-001) and Ovid's claim to stellar fame (147-006) both invoke the language of catasterism to assert that transformation can elevate rather than degrade. The night sky, in Ovid's vision, is a museum of metamorphoses — every constellation tells a story of change.
