[059-001] Pelops, the son of Tantalus, is murdered by his father, and served up in an entertainment to the gods, to make trial of their divinity—[[jupiter|Jupiter]] discovers the perfidious cruelty, and restores Pelops to life—Part of the shoulder, which Ceres incautiously devoured, is supplied by a substitute made of ivory.

[059-002] From these relations straight the people turn

[059-003] To present truths, and lost Amphion mourn;

[059-004] The mother most was blamed, yet some relate

[059-005] That Pelops pitied and bewail’d her fate,

[059-006] And stripp’d his clothes, and laid his shoulder bare

[059-007] And made the ivory miracle appear.

[059-008] This shoulder, from the first, was form’d of flesh,

[059-009] As lively as the other, and as fresh;

[059-010] But, when the youth was by his father slain,

[059-011] The gods restored his mangled limbs again;

[059-012] Only that place which joins the neck and arm,

[059-013] The rest untouch’d, was found to suffer harm;

[059-014] The loss of which an ivory piece sustain’d;

[059-015] And thus the youth his limbs and life regain’d.
