# Cupid

Cupid (Greek: Eros, also called Amor in the poem), the son of Venus, is the god of erotic desire whose arrows compel gods and mortals to fall in love — often with catastrophic consequences. In the *Metamorphoses*, Cupid appears most dramatically in Book I, where his wounding of Apollo with a golden arrow (inflaming love) and Daphne with a leaden arrow (repelling love) directly precipitates the poem's first great transformation narrative: Apollo's pursuit of Daphne and her metamorphosis into a laurel tree. Cupid's role is thus foundational: he is the agent who sets the poem's central dynamic of desire, pursuit, flight, and transformation into motion, a figure whose capricious power makes even the greatest of the Olympians helpless.

## Role in the Metamorphoses

Cupid's most significant appearance comes in Book I, immediately after Apollo's victory over the Python. Apollo, proud of his conquest, mocks the boy Cupid for carrying a bow — a weapon, the god of archery suggests, better suited to a real warrior. Cupid's reply is swift and precisely targeted: "Your arrows may pierce everything else, Apollo — but mine shall pierce you." He draws two arrows from his quiver: a golden one that kindles love, and a leaden one that repels it. With the golden arrow he strikes Apollo; with the leaden, the nymph Daphne. Apollo burns with passion; Daphne recoils from all suitors and flees. The pursuit that follows — Apollo's desperate chase, Daphne's prayer for deliverance, and her transformation into the laurel — is the first fully developed metamorphosis narrative in the poem and establishes the paradigm that will recur throughout: divine desire, mortal flight, and transformation as both escape and memorial (007-001). Cupid thus functions as the prime mover of the poem's erotic logic. His arrows explain why the gods desire mortals, why those mortals resist, and why the resulting transformations are at once punishment, preservation, and art.

### Key Episodes

- **The Quarrel with Apollo**: Fresh from killing the Python, Apollo taunts Cupid for his bow — and Cupid's revenge reshapes the poem's entire narrative logic
- **The Two Arrows**: Cupid strikes Apollo with the golden arrow and Daphne with the leaden, initiating the pursuit-and-flight pattern (007-001)

## Transformation

Cupid himself does not transform, but he is one of the poem's most potent agents of transformation — his arrows create the conditions of desire that lead, over and again, to metamorphosis:
