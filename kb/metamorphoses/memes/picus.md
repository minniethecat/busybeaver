# Picus

Picus, a legendary king of Latium and son of Saturn, was famed for his extraordinary beauty and his skill in horsemanship. Married to the nymph Canens, whose singing could move rocks and trees, Picus was hunting in the forest when the enchantress Circe saw him and was instantly smitten. He rejected her advances, declaring his fidelity to Canens — and Circe, enraged by the rejection, used her magic to transform him into a woodpecker, the bird that in Latin still bears his name (*picus*). His wife Canens, searching for him in vain, dissolved into the air from grief, leaving only her voice behind.

## Role in the Metamorphoses

Picus's story is told in Book XIV, narrated as a tale-within-a-tale. Circe, the dangerous sorceress already encountered in earlier books, spies Picus while hunting. Struck by his beauty, she conjures a phantom boar to lure him away from his companions, then confronts him directly. Picus's firm refusal — "I am another's, and will be hers till death" — enrages Circe, who twice turns him toward Canens with her wand and twice he resists. On the third attempt, she transforms him, his royal purple cloak becoming wings, his golden belt becoming feathers. Canens, his wife, searches for him for six days and nights without food or rest, and finally, on the banks of the Tiber, she sings her last song and dissolves into the air — only her voice, the *canor* (song), remaining.

### Key Episodes

- **Circe's Infatuation**: The sorceress sees Picus hunting and is overcome with desire
- **The Phantom Boar**: Circe creates a magical boar to separate Picus from his retinue
- **The Rejection**: Picus refuses Circe, declaring his fidelity to Canens
- **Transformation**: Circe changes Picus into a woodpecker; his former royal attributes become the bird's plumage
- **Canens's Dissolution**: Canens searches the forest for six days, then dissolves into the air, leaving only her song

## Transformation

Picus's metamorphosis is a punishment for rejection — Circe transforms him from a handsome king into the bird that bears his name. It is also an aetiological myth: the woodpecker (Latin *picus*) was sacred to Mars and played a role in Roman augury, linking the mythical king to Italian religious practice.

- **Form**: Picus (mortal king) → Woodpecker (*picus*)
- **Agent**: Circe, using her wand and spells
- **Cause**: Rejection of Circe's love; fidelity to Canens
- **Source**: Book XIV
