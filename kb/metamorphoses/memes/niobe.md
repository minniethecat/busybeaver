# Niobe

Niobe, queen of Thebes and wife of Amphion, is the central figure of one of the *Metamorphoses*' most harrowing episodes. Her boast that she surpasses Latona in children — fourteen to two — provokes Apollo and Diana to slaughter all her offspring in a single day. Transformed by grief into a weeping stone on Mount Sipylus, Niobe becomes the poem's most powerful image of maternal loss made permanent.

## Role in the Metamorphoses

Niobe's episode (Book VI) functions as a companion piece to Arachne's: where Arachne challenges Minerva in art, Niobe challenges Latona in fertility. The mechanism of punishment is different — her children are killed rather than her body transformed — but the logic is the same: mortal pride that challenges divine prerogative must be answered. What distinguishes Niobe's story is its relentless accumulation of grief: each death is described in sequence, the children falling one by one, until Niobe sits among their corpses, petrifying.

### Key Episodes

- **The Boast**: Niobe, proud of her fourteen children, demands worship from the Thebans instead of Latona, who has only Apollo and Diana — "Why is Latona worshipped, and not I?" (053-001)
- **The Slaughter**: Apollo kills the seven sons with his arrows; the daughters, still defiant, are killed by Diana — each death described in vivid anatomical detail (053-001)
- **The Transformation**: Niobe, sitting among the corpses of her children, hardens into stone — but even as stone, she weeps: a spring flows from the rock on Mount Sipylus (053-001)

## Transformation

Niobe's transformation is the poem's most powerful instance of **petrification as grief**: she becomes stone not through divine decree but through the sheer weight of loss. The detail that the stone continues to weep — "tears still trickle from the marble" — ensures that the transformation is not an end to suffering but its permanent embodiment.

- **Form**: Living queen → Weeping stone
- **Agent**: Latona (through Apollo and Diana); transformation is the cumulative effect of grief
- **Cause**: Hubris — boasting superiority over a goddess; maternal pride
