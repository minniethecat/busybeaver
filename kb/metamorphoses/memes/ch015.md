[011-001] The nymphs of [[latium|Latium]] erect a monument to the memory of Phaeton, whose sisters are changed into poplars while bewailing their brother’s untimely fate.

[011-002] The Latian nymphs came round him, and amazed,

[011-003] On the dead youth, transfix’d with thunder, gazed,

[011-004] And, while yet smoking from the bolt he lay,

[011-005] His shatter’d body to a tomb convey;

[011-006] And o’er the tomb an epitaph devise:

[011-007] “Here he who drove the sun’s bright chariot lies;

[011-008] His father’s fiery steeds he could not guide,

[011-009] But in the glorious enterprise he died.”

[011-010] [[apollo|Apollo]] hid his face, and pined for grief,

[011-011] And, if the story may deserve belief,

[011-012] The space of one whole day is said to run,

[011-013] From morn to wonted ev’n, without a sun;

[011-014] The burning ruins, with a fainter ray,

[011-015] Supply the sun, and counterfeit a day,

[011-016] A day that still did Nature’s face disclose,

[011-017] This comfort from the mighty mischief rose.

[011-018] But Clymene, enraged with grief, laments,

[011-019] And as her grief inspires her passion vents;

[011-020] Wild for her son, and frantic in her woes,

[011-021] With hair dishevell’d, round the world she goes

[011-022] To seek where’er his body might be cast,

[011-023] Till, on the borders of the Po, at last

[011-024] The name inscribed on the new tomb appears

[011-025] The dear, dear name she bathes in flowing tears,

[011-026] Hangs o’er the tomb, unable to depart,

[011-027] And hugs the marble to her throbbing heart.

[011-028] Her daughters too lament, and sigh, and mourn

[011-029] (A fruitless tribute to their brother’s urn),

[011-030] And beat their naked bosoms, and complain,

[011-031] And call aloud for Phaeton in vain;

[011-032] All the long night their mournful watch they keep,

[011-033] And all the day stand round the tomb and weep.

[011-034] Four times, revolving, the full moon return’d,

[011-035] So long the mother and the daughters mourn’d,

[011-036] When now the eldest, Phaethusa, strove

[011-037] To rest her weary limbs, but could not move;

[011-038] Lampetia would have help’d her, but she found

[011-039] Herself withheld and rooted to the ground;

[011-040] A third, in wild affliction as she grieves,

[011-041] Would rend her hair, but fills her hands with leaves:

[011-042] One sees her thighs transform’d, another views

[011-043] Her arms slot out and branching into boughs,

[011-044] And now their legs, and breasts, and bodies, stood

[011-045] Crusted with bark, and harden’d into wood;

[011-046] But still above were female heads display’d,

[011-047] And mouths, that call’d the mother to their aid.

[011-048] What could, alas! the weeping mother do?

[011-049] From this to that with eager haste she flew,

[011-050] And kiss’d her sprouting daughters as they grew;

[011-051] She tears the bark that to each body cleaves;

[011-052] And from their verdant fingers strips the leaves;

[011-053] The blood came trickling where she tore away

[011-054] The leaves and bark. The maids were heard to say

[011-055] “Forbear, mistaken parent, O forbear!

[011-056] A wounded daughter in each tree you tear;

[011-057] Farewell for ever.” Here the bark increased,

[011-058] Closed on their faces, and their words suppress’d.

[011-059] The new-made trees in tears of amber run,

[011-060] Which, harden’d into value by the sun,

[011-061] Distil for ever on the stream below;

[011-062] The limpid streams their radiant treasure show

[011-063] Mix’d in the sand, whence the rich drops convey’d

[011-064] Shine in the dress of the bright Latian maid.
