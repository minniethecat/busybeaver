# The Death of Actaeon

Actaeon's transformation and death is one of the *Metamorphoses*' most haunting episodes: a young hunter, guilty of nothing but an accidental glimpse of the goddess Diana bathing, is transformed into a stag and torn apart by his own hounds. The injustice of the punishment — accident treated as crime — makes Actaeon's fate one of the poem's most ethically troubling transformations.

## Characters

| Character | Role |

| Actaeon | Grandson of Cadmus, skilled hunter, transformed into a stag |
| Diana | Goddess of the hunt, bathing in a sacred grotto, punishes the accidental witness |

## Setting

The valley of Gargaphie in Boeotia, where Diana's sacred grotto and pool are hidden in a wooded dell. Actaeon, separated from his hunting companions, wanders into this forbidden space.

## Narrative

Actaeon, having completed a morning's successful hunt, dismisses his companions and wanders into an unfamiliar valley. He enters Diana's sacred grotto at the moment the goddess is bathing, attended by her nymphs. The nymphs scream and cluster around Diana, who, unable to reach her arrows, splashes water in Actaeon's face with a curse: "Now tell you saw me here, naked, if you can." Antlers sprout from Actaeon's head; his hands become hooves; his own hounds — Melampus, Ichnobates, Pamphagus, and the rest — see a stag and give chase. He tries to call their names but cannot speak. The hounds tear him apart.

## Themes

- **Guilt without crime**: Actaeon's punishment is for an accident — Ovid explicitly says "no crime was his, only a mistake"
- **The hunter hunted**: The transformation inverts Actaeon's identity; the predator becomes prey
- **Silenced voice**: Actaeon cannot call his hounds by name — his human identity is erased before his body is destroyed
