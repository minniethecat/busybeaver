# Polyphemus

Polyphemus, the Cyclops of Sicily, appears in Book 13 of the *Metamorphoses* in a role quite different from his Homeric portrayal. Here he is not the man-eating ogre of the Odyssey but a lovesick monster — a giant in love with the beautiful sea-nymph Galatea. His passion, expressed in an elaborate pastoral song, is both comic and terrifying: the ugliest creature in the world speaking the language of the lovelorn shepherd, only to revert to his true violent nature when he discovers Galatea with her mortal lover Acis.

## Key Episodes

- **The Cyclops in Love**: Polyphemus, inflamed with love for Galatea, abandons his flocks and his ferocity to compose love songs addressed to her beauty — a monstrous parody of pastoral poetry (120-001)
- **The Song**: In a lengthy aria, Polyphemus catalogs his wealth (flocks, milk, fruits), describes his single eye ("like the sun"), and offers himself to Galatea — the monster as would-be lover, both ridiculous and strangely sympathetic
- **The Murder of Acis**: Discovering Galatea with the shepherd Acis, Polyphemus tears a fragment from the mountain and hurls it at them, crushing Acis — a return to his true nature of violence and jealousy (120-206)
- **Acis Transformed**: From beneath the boulder, Acis's blood becomes water; he is transformed into a river-god, his love for Galatea preserved eternally in the landscape
