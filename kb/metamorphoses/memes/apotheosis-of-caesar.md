# The Apotheosis of Julius Caesar

The deification of Julius Caesar is the penultimate transformation of the *Metamorphoses*. Venus, unable to alter Fate's decree of assassination, ensures that her descendant's soul becomes a star — the *sidus Iulium* — visible in the Roman sky. This catasterism bridges mythological transformation and Roman imperial ideology.

## Characters

| Character | Role |
|-----------|------|
| Venus | Divine ancestor of the Julian line, petitions for Caesar's immortality |
| Julius Caesar | Dictator of Rome, assassinated in the Senate, transformed into a comet |
| Jupiter | Grants the apotheosis, acknowledging Caesar's greatness and Augustus's destiny |

## Setting

Heaven — Venus's desperate appeal to Jupiter — and Rome, where the assassination occurs in the Senate house and the comet blazes during Caesar's funeral games.

## Narrative

Venus, foreseeing the conspiracy against Caesar, appeals to Jupiter. The king of the gods acknowledges that Fate's decree cannot be altered — even for a descendant of Venus. But he assures Venus that Caesar's soul will be received among the gods and his adopted son, Augustus, will surpass him in glory. When Caesar is assassinated, Venus descends to claim his soul, which rises as a comet — brighter than any star — during the seven-day festival of games in his honor. Ovid concludes by praising Augustus's greater destiny, then seamlessly transitions to his own claim to immortality.
