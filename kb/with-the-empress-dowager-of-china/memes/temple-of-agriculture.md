# Temple of Agriculture

The Temple of Agriculture (Xiannong Tan) was one of the great ritual temples of imperial Peking, part of the complex of Nature-worship sites where the Emperor of China performed solemn rites as the empire's High Priest. It was the site of the annual agricultural ceremonies—the Emperor plowing the first furrow and sowing the first seeds—that connected the imperial house to the land and its cultivation.

## Location and Context

The Temple of Agriculture stood within a magnificent park in the Chinese City of Peking, part of a sacred complex that included the more famous Temple of Heaven and the temples to the Earth, the Sun, and the Moon (033-006). The park was surrounded by groves of centuries-old arborvitae, the Chinese tree of life and emblem of immortality, which Carl noted were planted around all the great sacrificial sites (033-006).

The temple was situated "near the great triple altar" where the most solemn rites of the Nature cult were performed (033-011). This grouping of temples—Heaven, Earth, Sun, Moon, and Agriculture—reflected the Chinese cosmological order that linked the ruler's ritual performance to the prosperity of the realm.

## Description

Carl's description of the Nature-worship temples emphasizes the Temple of Heaven as the most imposing: "spherical and triple-domed, rears its proud height here and is visible from afar. Its triple dome tiled, without, in the sacred green of Nature and vaulted within in Heaven's own blue" (033-006). The Temple of Agriculture was one of the "scarcely less splendid Temples" within the same park (033-006).

The central altar for the worship of the Invisible Deity was of pure white marble, round as the Earth, built in three superposed circular platforms surrounded by an exquisitely carved balustrade and approached by flights of nine steps (033-008). The Temple of Agriculture shared this architectural language of circular platforms, marble carving, and cosmological symbolism, adapted to its agricultural function.

## Role in Court Life

The Emperor's agricultural ceremonies were among the most symbolically significant rites of the Qing calendar. Carl described the annual ritual: "The annual plowing of a furrow and sowing of the first seeds of the year by the Emperor, the planting of a mulberry tree (to nourish the silkworms) by the Empress, are other touches of Nature which show how near the Chinese are to the heart of things" (029-021).


> I was to see the Emperor plow the first furrow of the year! For it was only on the morrow that the official public ceremony was to take place at the Temple of Agriculture, near the great triple altar of Heaven. I was to see the private plowing, done in the Palace grounds and viewed only by the Princes of the Imperial Family and the highest Manchu nobles.
>
> (033-011)
The ceremony at the Temple of Agriculture was described as taking place "near the great triple altar" (033-011). The emperor performed this rite in his triple capacity as Emperor, High Priest, and Expiator for the nation (033-007). By publicly performing agricultural labor, the emperor elevated farming from mere toil to a sacred act: "Labor, which the Emperor publicly performs, loses all taint and grows into an Inspiration" (029-021).

The Empress's parallel ceremony—planting a mulberry tree to nourish silkworms—connected the imperial women to the silk industry, one of the great sources of national prosperity. Ladies of high rank were appointed "Guardians of the Cocoons" and made "annual pilgrimage to the mulberry groves, where the cocoons flourish, to make sacrifices and prayers for the health and growth of the cocoon" (029-021).

## In the Narrative

The Temple of Agriculture appears in Carl's extended discussion of Chinese religious rites (Chapter XXIX), where she distinguishes the three great religions of China—Buddhism, Taoism, and the worship of Nature—and identifies the Nature cult as "the purest form of religion in China" (033-006).

Carl, a Western observer, was clearly impressed by the theology of the Nature temples: the worship without priestly hierarchy, the emperor serving as sole High Priest, and the grandest temple being one with "foundations the Earth, walls limitless Ether, dome Heaven's own vault" (033-006). Her account of the agricultural ceremonies reflects her broader argument that the Chinese, "being so near to Nature, are naturally a pastoral people, a race of agriculturists" (029-021).

## Historical Significance

The Temple of Agriculture embodied the deep connection between ritual governance and agricultural prosperity in imperial China. The emperor's public performance of farm labor was not merely symbolic; it ritually enacted the relationship between the "Son of Heaven" and the land that sustained his people. When famine or drought struck, the emperor publicly held himself responsible and performed expiatory rites in these temples (033-012).

The ceremonies at the Temple of Agriculture, along with the Festival of the Harvest Moon and the Awakening of Spring rites, formed part of a ritual calendar that aligned the imperial house with the agricultural cycle. By Carl's time in 1903–1904, with the Qing state under pressure to modernize, these ancient rites continued to be performed, representing the dynasty's connection to the agrarian foundations of Chinese civilization even as railways, automobiles, and constitutional reform movements threatened to render them anachronistic.
