# Great Audience Hall

The Great Audience Hall was the primary ceremonial building in the
[[summer-palace|Summer Palace]] compound, where [[empress-dowager|Empress Dowager]] Cixi and the
[[emperor-kwang-hsu|Emperor Kwang-Hsu]] held their daily official audiences with ministers
and members of the [[grand-council|Grand Council]].
Katherine Carl observed its customs and architecture during her stay
at the Chinese Court, dedicating an entire chapter to its formal procedures.

## Location and Context

The Audience Hall occupied a detached position in the Palace enclosure,
deliberately set apart from the imperial living quarters.
As Carl described:

> Their Majesties' Audiences are held in the Great Audience Hall,
> a detached building apart and quite distinct from all the other
> buildings of the Palace enclosure. The inscription over its great
> doors points out that it is the "Hall where Industry is to be applied
> to State Affairs." (016-003)

This placement was strategic — officials granted audiences
could enter the outer courts without approaching the private apartments.
At the [[summer-palace|Summer Palace]], unlike the [[winter-palace|Winter Palace]],
the Audience Hall was not surrounded by interior walls,
making the compound feel more open. (016-003)

## Description

The interior of the Audience Hall was richly furnished
in a style matching the Throne-rooms, with
"splendid ornaments, curios, tea-tables and chairs." (016-004)
A notable feature was the presence of three pianos —
what Carl called a "curious anachronism" in an imperial Chinese setting.

The walls were adorned with ornamental scrolls bearing
calligraphy by emperors and sages. Carl noted one scroll
that admonished the Emperor to remember that
"he is responsible to Heaven for the happiness and prosperity
of his people." (016-004)

At the center of the hall stood a great dais supporting
the Throne, behind which rose a multi-leaved screen.
Carl observed that the antique Throne, with its capacious size,
was "more like a lounge than the modern Throne,"
suggesting that ancient imperial justice was
"less formal and more patriarchal than today." (016-005)

## Role in Court Life

Daily audiences were the central activity of the Audience Hall.
Heads of departments attended on designated days each month,
while the [[prince-ching|Prime Minister]] and Grand Secretary had daily audiences.
The Prime Minister, [[prince-ching|Prince Ching]], held the last audience each day,
during which business from earlier sessions was reviewed and discussed. (016-006)

A strict protocol governed who could kneel upon cushions
and who must kneel on the bare marble floor.
The five cushions before the dais were reserved for
members of the Grand Council — the Prime Minister's cushion
placed nearest the Throne. Other officials
"must kneel upon the bare marble floor, and must kneel beyond
the space occupied by these five cushions." (016-008)

Carl recorded an amusing detail about officials
who attended frequent audiences:

> The officials who are obliged to go often to Audiences
> resort to an amusing subterfuge to protect their knees
> from the marble floor. They strap heavily wadded cushions
> around their knees before they go in. (016-011)

The long Chinese gowns worn by the men concealed these knee-protectors,
allowing them to kneel in comfort during lengthy sessions.

## In the Narrative

Carl devoted Chapter 16 entirely to the Audience Hall,
titled "The Great Audience Hall — Some Official Customs." (016-001)
The chapter appears after her detailed descriptions of
the Summer Palace grounds and the eunuchs,
offering readers a window into the machinery of Qing governance.

The hall also features in Carl's account of the
[[first-audience|First Audience]] with the foreign ladies of the Legations, (032-012)
and during the Emperor's Birthday ceremonies,
when Carl observed the formal processions
to the Audience Hall. (009-008)

A memorable anecdote from the hall involves the young
Emperor Kwang-Hsu, who, grown impatient with long-winded ministers,
would occasionally slip off the Throne and retreat behind the screen
to smoke a cigarette, leaving the kneeling official
to discover an empty dais. (016-014)

## Historical Significance

The Great Audience Hall was the theater of Qing imperial governance
at a pivotal moment. During Carl's residence in 1903–1904,
telegrams about the rebellion in Kwang-Si,
the Russian evacuation of Manchuria, and the outbreak of hostilities
in Manchuria arrived constantly at the Palace,
often delivered to the Empress Dowager outside regular Audience hours. (016-007)

The formal protocols Carl described — the kowtow, the kneeling cushions,
the eunuch's ritual introduction — represented centuries of
imperial tradition at the very moment these customs
were being challenged by foreign diplomatic norms.
After the 1911 Revolution, the Audience Hall and its ceremonies
vanished into history, but Carl's detailed chapter preserves
a rare eyewitness record of late-Qing court procedure.
