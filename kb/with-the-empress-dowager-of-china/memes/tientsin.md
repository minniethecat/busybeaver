# Tientsin

Tientsin (天津, *Tiānjīn*) was one of the most important cities of late Qing China — a major treaty port, the seat of the Viceroy of Zhili province, and the gateway through which foreign influences entered the imperial capital of [[peking|Peking]].

## In Carl's Account

### The European Circus

The most colorful appearance of Tientsin in Carl's memoir involves a European circus. Carl, who had been in China two years without European entertainment, was delighted to learn that a circus was to be brought to the Palace: "This circus was then in Tientsin, and someone had suggested to Their Majesties it would be an interesting thing to see" (021-002). A young Manchu was dispatched to investigate, and upon receiving a favorable report, "it was decided that the circus should be brought up from Tientsin" (021-002).

The circus performance became one of the most unusual events of Carl's residency, with the Imperial party watching European performers within the Palace enclosure.

### The Viceroy's Band

At the circus, Carl heard two foreign-style bands — one belonging to Yuan Shih-Kai, Viceroy of Tientsin, and another belonging to [[sir-robert-hart|Sir Robert Hart]], Inspector-General of Imperial Customs (021-009). Yuan's band was military, with "fifty musicians, who play only on brass instruments" (021-009).

### The St. Louis Portrait

When the portrait of the Empress Dowager was sent to the St. Louis Exposition, it traveled by special train from Peking to Tientsin, where it "was met at Tientsin by the Viceroy of the Province, surrounded by all his official staff" (033-012). It was then placed "with great ceremony upon the steamer" bound for Shanghai (033-012).

## Historical Significance

Tientsin appears in Carl's account as a seat of military and administrative power. The Viceroy of Tientsin (Yuan Shih-Kai) commanded one of China's most modern military forces, and the city served as a crucial junction between the foreign legations in Peking and the sea route to the wider world.
