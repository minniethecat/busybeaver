# Imperial Throne

The Imperial Throne was the symbolic and physical center of Qing Court power -- a magnificent piece of furniture before which all ceremonies, audiences, and official functions took place (001-007).

## Description

Carl encountered several thrones during her residency. The "Double Dragon Throne" was a portable throne used in the throne room for portrait sittings (001-014). In the great hall stood "an immense, couch-like throne, with a large footstool" (004-003). Another was "a great antique throne and footstool of red lacquer" (024-013).

The Empress Dowager also used a "yellow, throne-like chair" for less formal occasions (002-013). The color yellow was reserved for the Imperial family, reinforcing the throne's exclusive association with Imperial authority.

Carl described the main Throne-room and its furniture:

> In the rear of the hall is a magnificent five-leaved screen of teakwood, inlaid with lapis lazuli, chalcedony, and many other semiprecious stones. In front of this screen, on a dais, stood an immense, couch-like throne, with a large footstool. These couch-like thrones, where Their Celestial Majesties may recline when holding Audiences, are not at all favored by the Empress Dowager, who always sits extremely erect, without leaning upon a cushion or the back of the throne.
>
> (004-003)

## Cultural Significance

The throne (御座, *yùzuò*) was more than furniture -- it was the embodiment of Imperial authority. Approaching the throne required the kowtow (016-016), and no one could sit upon it except the Emperor or Empress Dowager. Carl noted that when "the Empress Dowager sits upon the throne with him," even the Emperor's authority was shared (008-002).

## In Carl's Account

For her portrait work, Carl needed to position her canvas "very near the throne where she was to sit" (001-010). She described how the Empress Dowager, after each sitting, "descended from the throne and came over to look at the progress of the painting" (001-018) -- a gesture that broke Court protocol and revealed Her Majesty's personal interest in the portrait.
