The Portrait for St. Louis was a large official portrait of the [[empress-dowager|Empress Dowager Cixi]] painted by the American artist [[katherine-carl|Katharine Carl]] for the 1904 Louisiana Purchase Exposition in St. Louis. It was among the first — if not the first — oil portrait ever painted of a Chinese Empress, and its creation, transport, and exhibition formed one of the central narrative threads of Carl's memoir *With the Empress Dowager of China*. (P04-001)

## Origins of the Commission

The commission originated when the idea was proposed of sending a portrait of the Empress Dowager to the Exposition at St. Louis. Carl recounts that "She thought such a portrait" would be appropriate for display. (P04-001) Breaking with centuries of tradition — "there was no precedent for an Imperial portrait to be painted" — the Empress Dowager agreed to sit for the American artist, commissioning not only the portrait for the St. Louis Exposition but three additional portraits as well. (P04-003)

## The Painting Process

The portrait for St. Louis was the largest and most formal of the works Carl produced at the Chinese Court. She began it in [[peking|Peking]] at the [[winter-palace|Winter Palace]], where "the big, official portrait for St. Louis was advancing." (028-002) Carl worked on it throughout the winter months, and the Empress Dowager took a keen interest in its progress, visiting the studio to observe and occasionally offering suggestions.

As the date of the Exposition's opening approached, the pressure to complete the portrait intensified. Carl wrote that "the date of the opening of the St. Louis Exposition was approaching!" (032-009) The work continued through the spring at the [[sea-palace|Sea Palace]], where Chapter 33 opens with a continuation of the portrait amid the spring days and changing scenery. (033-001)

## Diplomatic Significance

The portrait was more than a work of art — it was a diplomatic instrument. The Empress Dowager, conscious that China's image abroad was shaped by incomplete and often hostile accounts, saw the portrait as a means of presenting a dignified and favorable representation of herself and the Chinese Court to the Western world. The commission to an American woman artist was itself a statement of China's engagement with Western culture.

Upon completion, the portrait was carefully packed and sent "off on its long journey to St. Louis." (034-010) A special train carried it through China to Shanghai, from where it was transported by sea under the escort of a high Official and his suite. (034-011)

## Arrival and Presentation

When the portrait arrived in Washington before proceeding to St. Louis, His Excellency Sir [[sir-chentung-liang-cheng|Chentung Liang Cheng]], the Chinese Minister to Washington, attended by his Secretaries, made a formal presentation of the portrait to the President, which [[theodore-roosevelt|Mr. Roosevelt]] received on behalf of the United States Government. (034-015)

At the St. Louis Exposition itself, the portrait awaited the arrival of Their Majesties' representation. It became the centerpiece of the Chinese exhibit, offering American and international visitors a unprecedented glimpse of the woman who had ruled China for decades. (034-012)

> The idea of sitting for her portrait met with Her Majesty's approval, although it meant breaking through all precedent; for never before had any Emperor or Empress of China allowed a foreign hand to paint their sacred person.
>
> (P04-003)
