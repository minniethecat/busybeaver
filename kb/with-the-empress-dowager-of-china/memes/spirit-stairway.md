# Spirit-Stairway

The "spirit-stairway" was one of the most striking architectural features Katherine Carl encountered at the Chinese Court — a carved marble ramp centered in ceremonial staircases, bearing the [[double-dragon|Double Dragon]] in high relief, "not to be touched by mortal feet" (016-007).

## Description

Carl first saw a spirit-stairway at the [[sea-palace|Sea Palace]], leading to the second of three great halls: "This 'spirit-stairway' consists of a block of marble placed in the center and reaching from the top to the bottom of the stairway. This block, instead of being cut into steps, is elaborately carved with the double dragon. It lies in the middle of the stairway like a beautiful heavy carpet thrown over it, too stiff to take the form of steps" (016-007).

## Sacred Function

The spirit-stairway was not for practical use — it served a symbolic purpose, providing a path for spirits rather than humans. Mortals used the ordinary steps flanking either side. Carl noted that it was "used in the approaches to all the fine temples" (016-007).

## In Temple and Palace Architecture

The spirit-stairway was one of the defining features of important ceremonial structures. In secular architecture, as at the Sea Palace, its presence signaled the elevated status of the building. At the [[temple-of-heaven|Temple of Heaven]], where the stairs were higher, "the effect is as beautiful as it is original and unique" (016-007).
