# Kite-Flying

Kite-flying was one of the most popular pastimes in China, and, like all customs dear to the people, it was "always honored in the Palace" (033-009). Katherine Carl observed the [[empress-dowager|Empress Dowager]] and her Ladies flying kites in the gardens of the [[sea-palace|Sea Palace]] with remarkable skill.

## At the Palace

Carl described the spring kite-flying tradition: "In April, kite-flying time begins in China. High Officials and dignified literati indulge in the pastime as well as children and young people" (033-009).

The kites themselves were works of art: "The kites were of paper, wonderfully fashioned, representing birds, fish, bats, and even personages. The strings were wound on curiously shaped reels" (033-009).

Carl recorded the moment the Dowager invited her to participate:

> The first day the kites were to be flown Her Majesty sent for me to come into the garden, where the kite-flying was to take place. After she had let one go, she graciously handed me her own reel and told me she would teach me to fly a kite.
>
> (033-009)

## The Empress Dowager's Skill

Carl was struck by the Empress Dowager's proficiency: "The cleverness with which Her Majesty let out the string and manipulated the kites was wonderful" (033-009). After launching one herself, she "graciously handed me her own reel and told me she would teach me to fly a kite" — an offer Carl politely declined, preferring to watch.

The [[emperor-kwang-hsu|young Empress]] and Princesses were also "very proficient in flying them, and Her Majesty flew hers as she did everything else, with unusual grace" (033-009).
