# Corps Diplomatique

The Corps Diplomatique was the community of foreign diplomatic representatives stationed in Peking during the late Qing dynasty. It served as the primary channel of communication between the foreign powers and the Imperial Chinese Court, and played a significant role during the period of Katherine Carl's residence at the Palace in 1903–1904.

## Overview

The Corps Diplomatique comprised the Ministers and attachés of foreign legations accredited to the Court of China. By the time of Carl's memoir, the diplomatic quarter in Peking had been firmly established, and regular audiences between the diplomatic corps and the Imperial Court had become a recognized feature of foreign relations. The corps operated under the leadership of a Doyen—during Carl's time, the Austrian Minister, Baron Czikan.

## Role in the Late Qing

The Corps Diplomatique represented the interests of Western powers and Japan in China, following the Boxer Rebellion of 1900 and the subsequent reorganization of foreign relations. The diplomats maintained legations in the [[peking|Peking]] Legation Quarter, a fortified enclave established after the Boxer crisis. Their interactions with the Chinese Court were governed by elaborate protocol, managed through the [[wai-wu-pu|Wai-Wu-Pu]] (Chinese Foreign Office).

## In Carl's Account

The Corps Diplomatique appears at several key moments in Carl's narrative. The first diplomatic garden party at the [[summer-palace|Summer Palace]] occupied two days:

> Not long after this, Her Majesty gave a garden party for the ladies and gentlemen of the Legations. These garden parties occupy two days, for ladies and gentlemen are not received at the same time by Their Majesties of China. The Corps Diplomatique and attachés were entertained the first day, and the ladies of the Legations the following day.
>
> (019-002)

At the Chinese New Year celebrations, members of the Corps presented their congratulations: "A few days after the Court moved to the [[sea-palace|Sea Palace]], the members of the Corps Diplomatique were received in Audience to present their congratulations to the Emperor and Empress Dowager" (032-012). Carl notes the Empress Dowager's different demeanor with the diplomatic corps compared to ladies' audiences: "At the Audiences of the Diplomatic Corps, where only gentlemen were present, the Empress Dowager had a sort of shyness and did not show the same ease of manner" (031-013).

The formal presentation of ladies was conducted by the Doyen: "The ladies of the Legation were presented by the Baron Czikan, the Austrian Minister, Doyen of the Corps. He made a graceful address in French, wishing Their Majesties a Happy New Year" (032-012).

## Key Figures

- **Baron Czikan**: Austrian Minister, Doyen of the Corps Diplomatique during Carl's residence
- **Sir Claude MacDonald**: British Minister, who reported on the first foreign audience with the [[empress-dowager|Empress Dowager]]
- **Sir Chentung Liang Cheng**: Chinese Minister in Washington, who formally presented the St. Louis portrait
- **Members of the Wai-Wu-Pu**: Chinese foreign office officials who acted as interpreters at diplomatic functions

## Historical Trajectory

The Corps Diplomatique's presence in Peking represented the unequal treaty system that had defined China's foreign relations since the Opium Wars. After the 1911 Revolution and the fall of the Qing dynasty, the diplomatic corps transitioned to dealing with the Republic of China. The Legation Quarter remained a foreign enclave until the mid-20th century.
