# Emperor's Birthday

The Emperor's Birthday was a major ceremonial event at the Qing Court,
celebrated in August 1903 at the [[summer-palace|summer palace]] with four days of theatrical performances,
elaborate decorations, and formal congratulations from the Princes and Nobles of the Empire.
[[katherine-carl|katherine carl]] was the first foreigner ever invited to witness this celebration,
which she described as displaying "the Oriental pomp and pageantry that accompanied these ceremonial celebrations in China."

## Date and Tradition

The Emperor's Birthday was not celebrated on the actual anniversary of his birth, but two days earlier.
This unusual arrangement was dictated by the [[ancestor-worship|ancestral sacrifice]] calendar:

> His Majesty must make the Autumnal Sacrifices to his Ancestors three days
> after the real date of his Birthday, and he must prepare himself for these
> sacrifices by a rigorous fast of three days. As it would be impossible
> to accomplish the ceremonial prescribed for the Imperial Birthday while
> fasting, the celebration of the Birthday was advanced. (009-002)

A special edict had been issued by the two Empresses when they served as Co-Regents for the young
[[emperor-kwang-hsu|emperor kwang-hsu]], ordering the Birthday celebrations to be advanced by two days, since the date
of the sacrifices could not be changed — the "sacrifice to one's Ancestors being the most sacred
of obligations to the Chinese."

Additionally, the Emperor's Birthday was not celebrated for two years after the death of his predecessor,
reflecting the rigorous rules of filial respect and ancestral rites in China.

## Katherine Carl's Invitation

Carl, then residing at the Palace to paint the [[empress-dowager|empress dowager]]'s portrait, expected to return
to the [[american-legation|american legation]] during the festivities, believing no painting could be done.
Instead, she received an unprecedented invitation:

> A week before the Birthday itself, when out for one of our walks with Her Majesty,
> she called me up to her side and said the Emperor's Birthday was to be celebrated
> the next week, and invited me to remain in the Palace for these festivities. (009-002)

Carl described herself as "overjoyed at this gracious mark of her favor, and delighted to be able
to see the Oriental pomp and pageantry."

## Preparations

The preparations for the imperial celebration involved the entire Palace establishment.
The [[empress-dowager|empress dowager]] personally directed every detail:

- **Theatrical performances**: The Imperial company of actors rehearsed special poems and plays written to celebrate the occasion.
- **Decorations**: Eunuchs constantly brought specimens of the decorators' and painters' work for Her Majesty's approval.
- **Literary compositions**: The literati prepared original poems and submitted manuscripts for her judgment.
- **Musical instruments**: Magnificent antique bronzes and instruments — used only on great state occasions — were brought out as decorations.

> In the principal courts, magnificent bronzes, all sorts of antique instruments
> of music, used only on these great occasions, were brought out as decorations;
> for music forms part of every ceremonial, official or religious, in China. (009-006)

Among these instruments were bronze frames with triangular musical-stones, carved supports for
different-toned bells, huge tam-tams, large drums on bronze stands, and many other instruments
used only for official and state processions.

## Palace Decorations

The Palace buildings themselves were elaborately transformed:

> The slanting and projecting, upturned roofs of the different buildings forming
> the Palaces were decorated with scarfs of varicolored silk, knotted into a
> curious sort of fringe of rosettes, about two feet long; yellow, the Imperial
> color, and red, the festive color, predominating. (009-008)

The large Square in front of the Imperial gateway was filled with huge tent-like yellow satin
umbrellas with deep curtains — typically red for other festivities, but imperial yellow for the
Emperor's Birthday. These were richly embroidered with emblematic designs.

Presents for the Emperor arrived daily from all parts of the Empire, creating great commotion
despite the splendid system that directed everything.

## Mat-Sheds and Outdoor Spaces

For the summer celebration, elaborate mat-roofs were erected over the Palace courts to provide shade:

> Most of the large courts of the Summer Palace have roofs of matting erected
> over them, to keep out the sun. These mat-roofs make, of the flower-filled
> courts, delightfully cool, outdoor parlors. (009-011)

These mat-sheds were almost works of art: tall painted poles reaching twenty to thirty feet high,
transversal beams, and strips of fine matting. Side-curtains and roof sections were movable by cords
and pulleys. The entire structure was tied together with ropes — not a single nail was used.
These sheds were erected in June and taken down in September each year.

## The Ceremonial Days

### Arrival of Guests

New ladies arrived at the Palace every day for a week before the Birthday — members of the Imperial
Family from a distance, and the wives and daughters of Manchu nobles of sufficient rank to present
their congratulations in person. The [[princess-imperial|young Empress]] introduced Carl to each of
these ladies. Carl noted that "a foreigner in the Chinese Court is a much more extraordinary
circumstance than a Chinese at a European Court would be," yet the Princesses were "uniformly
courteous and even cordial."

### Gala Performances

The festivities began four days before the Birthday with gala performances at the Palace Theater.
The [[empress-dowager|empress dowager]] occupied her loge nearly all day, sending eunuchs to the stage to transmit
her commands about the speaking of certain lines or the use of certain postures.

She invited all the Ladies of the Palace to lunch in the court of the Theater — the first such
invitation since Carl's arrival — with tables laid and served "with all the pomp and ceremony
that characterized the meals at the Palace."

### Exploring the Theater

After the performance, Carl approached the stage to examine its construction. The Palace Theater
was raised about twelve feet from the ground, with three stories and a cellar for scenery.
Like a Greek theater, the stage was open on three sides.

> Her Majesty, herself, led the way across the stage and we went behind the scenes.
> Here, I examined closely a number of "Floats" that were to be used, in the
> procession in honor of the Emperor, on the day of the Birthday. (009-013)

The Empress Dowager herself climbed the steep steps to show Carl the upper stages,
managing the difficult ascent in her six-inch-high Manchu soles with "as much ease and
lightness as I did" in comfortable European shoes.

### The Birthday Ceremony

On the day of the Birthday, Carl went to the Palace early. The formal congratulations
took place in Her Majesty's Throne-room, where the Emperor had come to receive the
private congratulations of the Princesses and Ladies of the Court — Chinese etiquette
forbade these Ladies from entering the Emperor's own Palace.

> His Majesty was dressed a little more elaborately than usual, in a yellow gown,
> tightly belted in around the waist, with a small Mandarin cap on his head
> with the p'o-ling, or narrow band of crimson silk fluttering from it,
> turned up from his shoulders in a jaunty manner. (009-004)

Each Prince and Noble knelt and performed the prostration prescribed by
the [[book-of-rites|Book of Rituals]], after which the Emperor invited them to a
ceremonial repast.

## In the Context of the Memoir

Carl's invitation to the Emperor's Birthday was significant for several reasons.
It demonstrated the [[empress-dowager|empress dowager]]'s unprecedented favor toward a foreign guest,
granting her access to ceremonies "hitherto unseen by any foreigner." The event also
provided Carl with material for her memoir, offering Western readers a rare glimpse of
Qing court ceremonial life during the dynasty's final decade.
