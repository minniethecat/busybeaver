## 道教在清代朝廷

Taoism is one of the three great religions of China, alongside [[buddhism|Buddhism]] and the worship of Nature. (029-002) The [[emperor-kwang-hsu|Emperor of China]] serves as the nominal Head and High Priest of Taoism, and he assists at Taoist ceremonies and encourages the cult alongside Buddhism with impartiality. (013-004)(029-010)

All the festivals and fasts of Taoism are celebrated within the Palace, with the same "rigid impartiality and equal pomp" as Buddhist and Confucian rites. (013-004) The Emperor's official participation in these diverse religious traditions underscores the syncretic nature of Chinese imperial spirituality.

## 道教与老子

Taoism traces its origins to [[laotze|Laotze]] (Laozi), the ancient Chinese philosopher whose teachings form the foundation of Taoist thought. However, by the late Qing period, the narrator observes that the doctrines of Laotze, like those of Buddha, had "become so encrusted with error in China as to afford no moral or ideal help to their followers." (029-011)

## 晚清道教的仪式化

By the time of the [[empress-dowager|Empress Dowager]]'s court, Taoism (together with Buddhism) had become largely a matter of outward ceremonial rather than inward spiritual conviction. The narrator describes these religions as having transformed into:

> mere conventional representations of ancient rites. They are empty shells which may once have contained the Spirit, but have now become mere conventional representations of ancient rites.
>
> (029-011)

Taoist ceremonies at Court involved "a great deal of display and much form, but very little real worship." (029-011) The Chinese people, the narrator argues, derived their true moral support from the writings of [[confucius|Confucius]] and their ideals from communion with Nature, rather than from Taoist or Buddhist doctrines. (029-011)

## 与其他信仰的关系

Despite the formal separation of the three religions, in practice Chinese religious life was highly syncretic. The Chinese "participate indiscriminately in either of these religious rites." (029-011) All religious rites in China have their origin in, or are in celebration of, some natural phenomenon or periodical event in Nature — the solstices, equinoxes, the New Year, and the awakening of spring. (029-012) Taoism, like Buddhism, thus formed part of a complex tapestry of belief and ritual that permeated every level of Chinese society, from the Imperial Palace to the humblest village.
