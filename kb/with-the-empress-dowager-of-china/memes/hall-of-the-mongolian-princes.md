# Hall of the Mongolian Princes

The Hall of the Mongolian Princes was a grand ceremonial hall in the northeastern part of the [[sea-palace|Sea Palace]], used exclusively for the annual reception of the Mongolian Princes when they came to Peking to pay homage and tribute to the Qing Emperor. Katherine Carl visited it on her second day at the Sea Palace and described its impressive scale, its archaic throne, and its Manchu–Chinese inscriptions.

## Location and Context

The hall stood in the Sea Palace park, "some distance away from Her Majesty's and the Emperor's Palaces" (011-005). It was situated in the northeastern part of the grounds, accessible by chair along the banks of the lake and beyond the Marble Bridge (011-005). Its rear opened onto a court surrounded by smaller buildings used as waiting-rooms for the retainers and followers of the visiting Mongol princes (011-005).

The hall's location, isolated from the main residences, reflected its specialized diplomatic function: it was a space for managing the relationship between the Qing court and the Mongol nobility, part of the elaborate system of tribute and alliance that had underpinned Manchu rule since the dynasty's founding.

## Description

Carl described the Hall of the Mongolian Princes as "a magnificent hall" of a single story but "nearly forty feet high" (011-005). The interior was spacious and deliberately austere, furnished with "only a few dragon tables and chairs and no ornaments or other furniture" (011-005). At the back, a raised dais with several steps supported a splendid Throne of archaic design (011-005).

Above the throne hung two huge tablets of black marble, bearing inscriptions in both Chinese and Manchu characters (011-005). During a later visit, the Empress Dowager pointed out these inscriptions to Carl, explaining the differences between the two scripts and noting that Manchu, with its alphabet, would be easier for a foreigner to learn than Chinese (011-014).

## Role in Court Life

The hall had a single, specific purpose: "This great hall is used only for receiving the Mongolian Princes on their annual visit to Peking, when they come in state, with hundreds of followers and retainers, to pay homage and tribute to the Emperor of China" (011-005). Unlike the multi-purpose audience halls and throne-rooms of the Winter Palace, this hall's function was diplomatically focused on the Mongol–Manchu relationship.

During the Mongols' annual visit, they were domiciled in palaces within the Imperial City, and Carl observed them in the streets of Peking, "with their fur-dressed and leather-booted followers, their quaintly caparisoned horses, and splendidly bedecked camels" (025-005).

The hall also served informally. During Carl's promenade around the Sea Palace grounds, a sudden rainstorm drenched the party, and the chair-bearers turned toward the Hall of the Mongolian Princes for shelter. There, the Empress Dowager had her chair placed before the dais, and she occupied herself with scraping gourds—a traditional craft—while conversing with Carl about the Manchu language (011-014).

## In the Narrative

The Hall of the Mongolian Princes appears in Carl's account of her second visit to the Sea Palace (Chapter XI). It was the first stop on a promenade that continued to the Dragon Wall, the archery grounds, and the lake. Her description of the hall serves as a companion piece to her detailed account of the Winter Palace's throne-rooms, contrasting their different functions and decorative programs.

The hall's bilingual Manchu–Chinese tablets prompted the Empress Dowager's only recorded comment on the Manchu language in Carl's memoir, a rare instance of Cixi discussing linguistic heritage with a foreign visitor (011-014).

## Historical Significance

The Hall of the Mongolian Princes embodied the Qing dynasty's dual identity as both Chinese emperors and Manchu khans. The Mongol nobility had been key allies of the Manchus since before the conquest of China, and the annual tribute ceremony performed in this hall reaffirmed those ties. The black marble tablets inscribed in both Manchu and Chinese—the two official languages of the Qing state—visually represented this dual sovereignty.

By the time of Carl's visit in 1903–1904, this system of tribute-based diplomacy was under severe strain. Japan's growing influence in Mongolia, Russia's encroachment in Manchuria, and the Qing state's own post-Boxer weakness threatened the traditional relationships the hall was built to celebrate.
