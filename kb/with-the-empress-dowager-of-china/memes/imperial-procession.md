# Imperial Processions at the Qing Court

The ceremonial processions of the Qing Court, witnessed by Katherine Carl during her residence at the Imperial Palaces in 1903‚Äì1904, ranging from daily Imperial promenades to the spectacular torch-lit and lantern-lit nighttime processions of the festival season.

## Overview

Processions were integral to Qing Court life, marking every significant occasion ‚Äî religious ceremonies, festival celebrations, diplomatic receptions, and even the Empress Dowager's daily walks. Carl's memoir documents processions in vivid detail, capturing their visual splendor and the elaborate protocol that governed them.

The processions ranged from intimate daily affairs, where the Empress Dowager led her Ladies through Palace gardens, to grand ceremonial marches with hundreds of lantern-bearing eunuchs and the full Court in gala dress. Each type of procession served distinct ceremonial and social functions within Palace life.

## Daily Processions

The Empress Dowager's daily constitutionals through the Palace grounds were themselves miniature processions. Carl described how Her Majesty always walked at the head of the party "at a brisk pace that made it difficult for the Ladies to keep up with her" (011-011). The Empress Dowager's dog, Shadza, invariably ran ahead, and the party "sped along for about fifteen minutes" before the Empress Dowager would pause to rest (011-011).

These walks were attended by the Princesses, Ladies of the Court, and a retinue of eunuchs carrying the Empress Dowager's necessities ‚Äî her yellow chair, cushions, and refreshments. Carl noted the contrast between these informal promenades, where the Empress Dowager displayed her most charming and unaffected character, and the rigid formality of official processions.

## Religious and Ceremonial Processions

For the semiannual sacrifices to Confucius, the Court proceeded in full ceremonial dress. Carl described how "Their Majesties, with the Empress and Ladies, preceded and surrounded by eunuchs and officials, in full Court dress, went in ceremonious procession through the verandahed corridors, from Her Majesty's Throne-room to the temple," their approach accompanied by "the slow beating of drums" (013-006).

The Festival of the Harvest Moon featured a particularly striking procession. Carl wrote: "The procession, with the Emperor and Empress Dowager and Ladies in full dress, as usual for a ceremony, was accompanied by hundreds of lantern-bearing eunuchs. It wound, in and out, through the verandahed corridors and the paths of the garden like some great glowworm, until it came to the marble terrace beneath the Temple of the Ten Thousand Buddhas" (018-005).

At the close of the Moon ceremony, the procession continued to the lake, where eunuchs holding gleaming lanterns lined the terrace and knelt on the steps while Their Majesties descended to the Imperial barge (018-008).

## Festival Processions

The Chinese New Year and Lantern Festival featured the most elaborate processions Carl witnessed. Tall eunuchs in gala red held great lanterns aloft "like huge caryatides with luminous burdens," while others carrying vermilion lanterns "wound in and out through corridors and courts" (032-014).

When the lantern-bearers reached the court of the illuminated *pai-lou*, they "maneuvered and made intricate designs and luminous tableaux, holding aloft their red-globed lanterns to form characters and phrases of 'felicitous omen.'" Carl marveled that these "huge, luminous characters were wonderfully accurate" (032-014).

Following the lantern processions, illuminated dragons performed the struggle for the "flaming pearl" ‚Äî a symbolic representation of the Double Dragon (the Powers of Earth) striving to absorb the Flaming Pearl (Emblem of the Dynasty, symbol of Heaven) (032-015).

## The Emperor's Birthday Procession

Carl witnessed a grand procession in honor of the Emperor's birthday. Her Majesty had arranged for her to see it from a vantage point overlooking the route, where "palace attendants, richly dressed, bore wonderful symbolic banners of golden dragons beneath fluttering pennants of Imperial yellow" (007-014).

The procession included hundreds of eunuchs carrying painted regalia and processional paraphernalia. Carl particularly noted the "two immense, processional fans of peafowl feathers" that stood at the sides of the Imperial party (004-004).

## Arrival and Departure Processions

When the Imperial party arrived at the Sea Palace after moving from the Winter Palace, the approach was accompanied by ceremonial spectacle. For one audience, the door was "thrown open and the Imperial procession came in sight. Splendidly gowned" (024-007).

A picturesque moment occurred when Carl's party crossed the lake: "It was a picturesque procession that started from the landing" (021-008). The barge landing and embarking was itself a choreographed sequence, with eunuchs positioned precisely on the steps and along the terrace.

## Carl's Observations

Carl, as a painter, was particularly attuned to the visual qualities of these processions. She described the "quaint procession, bright with color, that started from the pavilion" (011-010) and noted how red umbrellas and yellow cushions created striking chromatic effects against Palace architecture.

She also observed the hierarchical precision: for the birthday procession, the Empress Dowager had arranged for Carl to view it from the "best possible vantage," demonstrating the Empress Dowager's consideration for her guest's artistic interests (007-014).

The Empress Dowager invariably led processions herself, Carl noting that "Her Majesty gave the word and the procession turned toward the Hall" (011-014). Even in the most formal ceremonial contexts, the Empress Dowager's personal authority shaped the proceedings.

## Significance

The imperial processions served multiple functions in Qing Court life: they displayed Imperial majesty and hierarchy to both domestic and foreign observers; they enacted the ceremonial sequences that structured Palace time around the ritual calendar; and they provided Carl with some of her most vivid visual memories of the Court.

The lantern and torchlight processions in particular demonstrated the sophisticated integration of illumination, choreography, and symbolism in Qing ceremonial arts. Carl's detailed observations make her memoir a valuable source for understanding the visual and performative dimensions of late Qing Court culture.
