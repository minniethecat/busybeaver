The Hundred Days' Reform was a period of ambitious modernization efforts initiated by the [[emperor-kwang-hsu|Emperor Kwang-Hsu]] in 1898. Inspired by Japan's Meiji Restoration and humiliated by China's defeat in the Sino-Japanese War of 1894, the young Emperor sought to transform China's educational, governmental, and military systems through sweeping decrees. The reform was cut short by a conservative backlash — known to foreigners as the coup d'état of 1898 — after which the [[empress-dowager|Empress Dowager Cixi]] resumed direct control of the government.

## Background

The first part of Kwang-Hsu's reign was uneventful. He followed the moderate policy laid down by the Empress Dowager and seemed to have no special designs for China. The Japanese victory in the 1894 war changed everything. The victory gave China one of her most humiliating lessons, for the Chinese looked down upon the Japanese as a race of imitators. (030-012)

The young Emperor, smarting under this defeat, felt that China had only been conquered by Japan's use of modern methods of warfare and determined on sweeping reforms in the government. (030-012)

## The Reform Movement

Full of youthful enthusiasm, Kwang-Hsu abandoned the moderate policy of the Regency and surrounded himself with a number of hotheaded, self-seeking reformers, each pushing some new method of reform. The reformers wished to change the system of education, the system of government — to make such sweeping changes that the conservative nation would have risen in a mass had they been carried out. (030-012)

> The young Emperor, smarting under this galling defeat, felt that China had only been conquered by Japan's use of modern methods of warfare and determined on sweeping reforms in the government. Full of youthful enthusiasm, he felt he could put the Great Empire on the road to progress and wished to institute sweeping reforms in all departments.
>
> (030-012)

## Coalition of Extremes

Besides the Radicals who formed the reform party, ultraconservatives also began to push forward their own schemes. The coalition of these two diametrically opposed parties — Radicals who thought the moderate policy too conservative, and Reactionaries who thought it too progressive — was for the purpose of persuading the Emperor to depart from the moderate opportunist policy of Cixi's regime. (030-012)

The ringleader of the Reformers was Kang-Yu-Wei, who later escaped and was outlawed. His subsequent career proved how self-seeking he was. (030-013)

## The Coup d'État of 1898

China's wisest statesmen saw the peril of the Emperor being crushed between these two self-seeking factions. They beseeched the Empress Dowager to return from her retirement. When she realized the danger, she returned. The Emperor issued an edict recalling her successful prior regencies and imploring her to advise him in government. This was what foreigners call the coup d'état of 1898. (030-012)

> Her Majesty's keenness of insight and fine judgment served her well again in this crisis. She dismissed not only the self-seeking Radicals, but the self-seeking ultraconservatives. Such of the Reformers as were caught were tried, convicted of treason, summarily and cruelly punished.
>
> (030-013)

## Aftermath

The coup d'état and the consequent check upon the Emperor's dreams of progress was a great blow to him. He saw that he had been over-sanguine in hoping to realize at once his enthusiastic dreams for the immediate rehabilitation of China's prestige. (030-014)

The Empress Dowager, having crushed the Reformers and reseated herself upon the Throne, was, from the time of the coup d'état, considered to be anti-foreign by outsiders. (030-016) The Emperor, however, showed no animosity toward Cixi. He seemed to understand that it was not she who put a stop to his dreams of Progress but Chinese conservatism and a coalition of powerful ministers. (008-007)

## The Emperor's Perspective

Despite the setback, the Emperor appeared to accept the lesson. He realized that to hope to reform in a few years the century-old traditions of his most conservative people was the wild, irrealizable dream of youth. Though he knew he had been led away by his wishes, the disappointment was severe and most depressing to his sensitive nature. (030-014)
