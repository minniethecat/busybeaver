# Temple of Heaven

The most imposing of the temple group in the Park of the Temple of Heaven, located in the Chinese City of Peking. The Temple of Heaven is a spherical, triple-domed structure topped with sacred green tiles, and served as the site of the Emperor's annual sacrifices to the Invisible Deity of Nature worship (029-002).

## Location and Context

The Temple of Heaven is situated in a magnificent park in the Chinese City of Peking, the southernmost of the walled cities. The temple complex is surrounded by groves of century-old arborvitae trees. Within the same park stand other major temples dedicated to the Earth, the Sun and Moon, and to Agriculture (029-002). The park grounds also include the altar where the most sacred ceremonies of Nature worship take place (029-018).

## Description

The Temple of Heaven is a spherical building with a triple dome. The exterior is tiled in the sacred green of Nature, while the interior vault is painted in the blue of Heaven. It is visible from afar, rearing its proud height above the park's ancient trees (029-002).

The temple approach features a "spirit-stairway" — a central block of marble carved with the double dragon motif, reaching from the top to the bottom of the stairway. Not to be touched by mortal feet, this feature is used in the approaches to all fine temples in China. At the Temple of Heaven, where the stairs are high, the effect is described as being as beautiful as it is original and unique (024-024).

Within the park, the great marble altar is built in the center of a marble-paved space, surrounded by long concentric avenues of arborvitae. The altar is circular, of pure white marble, round as the Earth. Its three superposed circles symbolize the Trinity in Nature and its Infinity. Each platform is surrounded by an exquisitely carved balustrade and approached by flights of nine steps from the north, south, east, and west, so that the central point of the upper circle represents the center of the Universe (029-002).

## Role in Imperial Ritual

The Emperor of China, in his capacity as "Son of Heaven" and Great High Priest, alone was worthy to sacrifice on the great altars to the Invisible Deity. He offered sacrifices at the Temple of Heaven at times prescribed by the Book of Rites and on special occasions. When famine, drought, or any national calamity visited the Empire, the Emperor prayed for its cessation, publicly holding himself responsible for the misfortunes of the people. According to the Book of Rites, he would declare: "I will purify myself by sacrifice that these calamities may be lifted from the Empire and the people. I alone am responsible." (029-018)


> The Emperor is not only the one High Priest worthy to sacrifice on the great altars to the Invisible Deity, the Priestly Hierarchy of the whole of this Cult of Nature is vested in his Sacred Person. He alone offers sacrifices in the Temples of Heaven and the other Great Temples, at times set aside in the Book of Rites, and on special occasions.

The Emperor's official, public exercise of religion was limited to the worship of Heaven and Earth, for which he made annual public sacrifices in the Great Temple of Heaven at Peking. Though he also served as High Priest of Buddhism and Taoism, and observed Confucian rites, the Heaven worship ceremonies were the most significant public religious acts (013-004).

## Historical Significance

The Temple of Heaven complex, built in the early 15th century during the Ming dynasty, represents the highest expression of the state cult of Nature worship in imperial China. The Emperor's role as sole officiant at the Heaven altar embodied the Confucian concept of the ruler as the intermediary between Heaven and Earth. The temple's design — round like Heaven, with its triple dome and circular altar — expresses Chinese cosmological principles in architectural form. Today, the Temple of Heaven is a UNESCO World Heritage Site and one of the most recognized symbols of traditional Chinese architecture.
