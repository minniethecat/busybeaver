# Dagoba

The Dagoba was a distinctive white Buddhist monument crowning the summit of Coal Hill in Peking, instantly recognizable and "so well known in all views of the Imperial City" (010-017).

## Location and Context

The Dagoba stands at the summit of Coal Hill (Jingshan), the artificial hill rising at the northern edge of the Imperial City. From the marble terrace of a nearby lama temple, Carl enjoyed "a beautiful view of the Coal Hill, surmounted by the curious Dagoba" (010-017). The monument served as a visual anchor for the northern skyline of the imperial quarter.

## Description

Carl describes the structure simply as "the curious Dagoba" — a phrase that reflects the fascination it held for Western observers (010-017). A dagoba (from the Sanskrit *dāgaba*, meaning a stupa or reliquary monument) is a type of Buddhist shrine, typically a solid white structure of domed or bell-shaped form. The dagoba on Coal Hill is a prominent example of this architectural form in an imperial Chinese setting, visible against the skyline from many parts of the palace district.

From the terrace of a lama temple, Carl surveyed the panorama of Peking:

> From this terrace we had a beautiful view of the Coal Hill, surmounted by the curious Dagoba, so well known in all views of the Imperial City, as well as of the belvedere that marks the spot where the last Emperor of the Mings committed suicide when he was conquered.
>
> (010-017)

## In the Narrative

The Dagoba appears in a single but memorable passage in the narrative, during a boat excursion to a Buddhist temple. From the temple's high marble terrace, Carl surveys the Imperial City panorama, noting both the Dagoba at the summit of Coal Hill and the belvedere lower down that marked the suicide spot of the last Ming emperor (010-017).
