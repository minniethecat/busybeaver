# Imperial Audience Ceremony

The Imperial Audience Ceremony was the formal protocol governing the reception of foreign representatives by Their Majesties the [[empress-dowager|Empress Dowager]] and the [[emperor-kwang-hsu|Emperor Kwang-Hsu]]. Katherine Carl's memoir provides detailed descriptions of these ceremonies, which represented a significant modernization of the Qing Court's engagement with foreign powers.

## Protocol and Procedure

Audiences were conducted according to elaborate ritual. Foreign visitors approached the throne in order of diplomatic precedence, each performing the prescribed bows. Chinese officials knelt on silk cushions during the ceremony: "the official who is being received at the audience kneels on a silk cushion" (016-012). The [[wai-wu-pu|Wai-Wu-Pu]] officials served as interpreters.

The Empress Dowager presided from behind a screen or, in the reformed protocol of the post-Boxer period, in full view. Carl notes that the audience setting was designed to impress: the throne room, the courtiers in their ceremonial [[silk-court-dress|silk robes]], and the measured pace of the proceedings all contributed to an atmosphere of Imperial majesty.

## In Carl's Account

Carl's own first audience—her presentation to the Empress Dowager—sets the tone for her entire memoir. She was received not in the formal audience hall but in the more intimate setting of the Empress Dowager's private quarters, a mark of the special favor extended to the portrait artist.

The public audiences Carl witnessed included the [[chinese-new-year-court|Chinese New Year]] reception for the [[corps-diplomatique|Corps Diplomatique]] and the presentations of individual foreign visitors. She describes the [[first-audience|first audience]] given to foreign ladies as a historic occasion, noting the Empress Dowager's skill as a hostess.

## Significance

The Imperial Audience Ceremony was a crucial institution in late Qing foreign relations. By receiving foreign representatives in audience, the Empress Dowager signaled China's engagement with the international community, while the elaborate protocol preserved traditional Chinese conceptions of Imperial dignity and hierarchy.

> As it was a great innovation in Chinese customs and a breaking away from long-established tradition for an Imperial portrait to be painted, there was no precedent to follow and all arrangements were of the vaguest kind; and when I went into the Palace for my first Audience, I did not know whether I would have one sitting or ten, and no one else seemed to have any more definite information. All was uncertainty. Everything depended upon Her Majesty's inclination, and future developments must be awaited.
>
> (P04-003)
