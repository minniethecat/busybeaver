# The Sacred Picture

"The Sacred Picture" was the term used at the Qing court
to refer to the life-size portrait of the [[empress-dowager|Empress Dowager]] Cixi
painted by [[katherine-carl|Katherine Carl]] in 1903–1904.
The phrase — which Carl found both charming and revealing —
encapsulated the quasi-religious reverence with which
the imperial likeness was treated throughout its creation and display.

## Description

The Sacred Picture was a full-length oil portrait of the Empress Dowager
seated on her throne, executed on a large canvas
measuring approximately six by ten feet.
It was painted over nine months at the [[summer-palace|Summer Palace]]
and the [[sea-palace|Sea Palace]], with the Empress Dowager sitting for regular sessions.

The portrait was always kept covered with yellow silk
when not being worked on or viewed:

> When we went in, the Chief Eunuch ceremoniously removed the yellow
> covering over the "Sacred Picture," which hung flat against the wall
> in a very bad light, with annoying reflections. (018-013)

The yellow silk covering was not merely protective — it was a mark
of the imperial status of the image. Yellow was the color
reserved exclusively for the imperial family,
and its use here signaled that the portrait was not a mere painting
but a proxy for the imperial presence itself.

## Cultural Significance

The term "Sacred Picture" reflected a conception of portraiture
fundamentally different from the Western artistic tradition.
In Carl's world, a portrait was a work of art — a representation
created by the artist's skill and imagination.
At the Chinese Court, the imperial likeness carried an aura
that transcended its material form.

Carl observed this with a mixture of amusement and respect.
The portrait was treated with protocols usually reserved for the Empress herself:
it was covered and uncovered with ceremony,
transported with an entourage of officials,
and displayed in a manner that commanded reverence.

The ritual surrounding the Sacred Picture culminated
at the moment of its departure for the [[st-louis-exposition|St. Louis Exposition]]:

> His Imperial Highness Prince Pu L'un, Imperial Commissioner and
> personal representative of Their Majesties at the Exposition of
> St. Louis, awaited the arrival of the portrait there, delaying his
> departure for several days in order to be able himself to assist at
> the reception and placing of the portrait. (034-012)

## In Carl's Account

The Sacred Picture dominates the final chapters of Carl's memoir.
The completion of the portrait, its transportation from the Palace
to the Legation Quarter, and its dispatch to America
are narrated with the gravity of a diplomatic event.

Carl describes the farewell moment:

> The Officials of the Wai-Wu-Pu, as well as many other of the high
> Officials in Peking, dressed in full dress, accompanied it to the
> station, and stood to watch the Sacred Picture start off on its long
> journey to St. Louis. (034-010)

The portrait traveled by train to the coast, then by steamer to San Francisco:

> The Sacred Picture was accompanied on its journey from Shanghai to
> St. Louis by a high Official and his suite. (034-011)

Upon reaching the Exposition grounds, the cases containing the portrait were opened:

> The cases containing the portrait, one within the other, were opened,
> and finally within the last, lined with yellow silk, lay the "Sacred
> Picture," covered with a screen of brocaded satin of Imperial hue.
> This satin cover was ceremoniously removed, and the picture was unveiled.
> (034-013)

Throughout the painting process, Carl was conscious that she was creating
not just a likeness but a diplomatic instrument.
She noted that her painting was, at times, overshadowed
by the elaborate ceremonies of the Court. (012-002)

## Historical Context

The Sacred Picture was part of a tradition of imperial portraiture
that stretched back through Chinese history.
Qing emperors had long commissioned formal portraits
for use in ancestor veneration rituals,
and the expectation that an imperial likeness commanded reverence
was deeply embedded in court culture.

What made Carl's Sacred Picture unique was its destination.
Never before had a Chinese imperial portrait been created
specifically for exhibition to a foreign public.
The decision to send the Empress Dowager's likeness to St. Louis
represented a significant departure from tradition —
a willingness to subject the imperial image to the gaze of strangers
for the sake of diplomatic advantage.

After the Exposition, the portrait was presented to the United States government
and eventually entered the collections of the Smithsonian Institution,
where it remains as a tangible artifact of the cultural encounter
between late-Qing China and the American Gilded Age.
