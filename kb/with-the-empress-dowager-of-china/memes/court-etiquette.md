# Court Etiquette

Court Etiquette at the Qing Imperial Court was an elaborate system of rules
and conventions governing every aspect of behavior in the presence of
Their Majesties the [[emperor-kwang-hsu|Emperor]] and [[empress-dowager|Empress Dowager]].
[[katherine-carl|Katherine Carl]], as a foreign guest unbound by these rules, was able to
observe and document the system with a detachment impossible for its Chinese
participants, recording the distinctive combination of extreme formality and
surprising informality that characterized court life.

## Sacredness and Its Limits

Carl observed that the sacredness of the imperial persons was situational rather
than absolute — it attached to them as rulers, not as individuals:

> In the Audience Hall when administering justice, they are not approached
> nor addressed, except upon bended knee. In the Palace, in their own privacy,
> when they give an order or any command touching upon official affairs, this
> order is received by the attendant, be he courtier, high official or great
> prince, on his knees. (034-006)

However, when Their Majesties were in their private capacity and speaking of
ordinary affairs, "they are addressed almost familiarly, and the courtier or
simple attendant stands while speaking to them." If, in the midst of a familiar
conversation, an order was given, the attendant would "immediately drop upon his
knees to receive it."

This situational quality of imperial sacredness was one of Carl's most perceptive
observations about the Qing Court. It revealed a system that was rigid without
being inflexible — capable of maintaining ceremonial distance while allowing
genuine human interaction.

## Prostration and the Kowtow

The [[kowtow|Kowtow]] — the full prostration of three kneelings and nine knockings of the
head — was the most formal act of submission at Court. At the
[[emperor-birthday|Emperor-Birthday]], Carl witnessed the ceremony:

> Each splendidly garbed Prince and Noble knelt and made the prostration
> prescribed by the Book of Rituals. (009-011)

The order of prostration was strictly governed by rank, with the highest
princes performing first and lower-ranking officials following in sequence.
The ceremony began at precisely calculated auspicious hours — for the
[[empress-dowager-birthday|Empress-Dowager-Birthday]], the prostrations began at 2 a.m., the hour
of her birth.

## Audience Protocol

The formal audience followed an exacting procedure. When an official was granted
an Audience:

> When the official granted an Audience hears the last echo of the steps
> of the departing eunuch, he falls upon his knees and begins the relation
> of his business. Their Majesties question him, if necessary, to elicit
> further explanations. When the Audience is finished, the official rises
> and walks out. (034-005)

Carl noted with interest that "the Chinese never back out of the Presence, and it is
not considered a breach of etiquette to turn their backs upon Royalty" — a contrast
with European court protocol, where backing away from the sovereign was mandatory.

## Rules Governing Imperial Criticism

One of the most significant rules of Chinese propriety was the prohibition against
any commentary on the Sacred Persons of Their Majesties:

> No act of theirs is ever criticized, no report in reference to them is ever
> explained, no slander about them is ever refuted by loyal Chinese, and the
> generality of Chinese are loyal. Thus the falsest statements, not being refuted
> by those in a position to know, gain in credence until they are reported as facts. (001-003)

Carl herself knowingly transgressed this rule by writing her memoir, acknowledging
that it risked "offending the sensibilities of my Chinese friends" and that "this
account will be looked upon by them as an 'indiscretion'."

## Flowers and Fruits Rule

Even the simplest actions in the Palace were governed by protocol.
The rules regarding flowers and fruits exemplified how etiquette permeated
everyday life:

> Though the Princesses and Ladies have the freedom of the gardens and may
> pull as many flowers and cull as many fruits as they wish, it is not etiquette
> for them to gather the smallest flower or to touch a fruit when in the presence
> of the Empress Dowager, unless they are especially told to do so. (010-010)

The first fruits of every tree and vegetable, the first flowers of every plant
in the Palace grounds, were considered sacred to Their Majesties. No Princess,
attendant, or eunuch would touch a flower or fruit until the Empress Dowager
had been presented with the first of them. These "apparently trivial, marks
of respect" were "religiously observed."

## Gender and Spatial Etiquette

Chinese etiquette imposed strict limitations on the movement of women within
ceremonial spaces. At the Emperor's Birthday, the Princesses and Ladies of the
Court could not enter the Emperor's Palace to congratulate him — he had to come
to the Empress Dowager's Throne-room to receive their private congratulations.

Similarly, the separation of male and female spaces within the Palace precincts
was governed by elaborate rules. Carl, as a foreigner, occupied an ambiguous
position that granted her access to spaces and occasions normally closed to women.

## Punctuality

Despite Western stereotypes about Oriental attitudes toward time, Carl found
punctuality to be an essential part of court etiquette:

> Punctuality is the etiquette of Oriental as well as of Occidental potentates.
> (004-001)

Her first audience was scheduled for precisely 10:30 a.m., after much deliberation
and many consultations of the almanac to select the most auspicious day, month, and hour.
The three-hour carriage journey from Peking to the Summer Palace required an early start
to ensure timely arrival.

## Carl's Position Outside the Rules

Carl's status as a foreign guest placed her outside the normal etiquette system,
giving her unique freedoms and perspectives. She made the European reverence
rather than the [[kowtow|Kowtow]]. She stood when others knelt. She asked questions
about the [[boxer-rebellion|Boxer Rebellion]] that a Chinese courtier could never have raised.

Yet Carl was also bound by her own sense of propriety. She understood that
publishing her observations would be seen as a breach of the trust the
[[empress-dowager|Empress Dowager]] had placed in her, and her memoir reflects this tension
between the obligations of a guest and the duties of a witness.
