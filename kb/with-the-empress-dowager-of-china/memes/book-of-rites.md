## Definition and Background

The Book of Rites (*Liji* or *Li Chi*, 礼记) is one of the Five Classics of the Confucian canon. Compiled during the Han dynasty from earlier Zhou-dynasty texts, it codifies the ritual protocols, social hierarchies, and ceremonial observances that formed the ethical and political foundation of imperial Chinese governance. The text prescribes correct behavior (*li*, 礼) for every station of life—from the Emperor's sacrifices at the great altars to the filial duties of the common people.

In Qing China, the Book of Rites was not an antiquarian document but a living code. Its prescriptions governed the rhythm of court life: the calendar of imperial sacrifices, the forms of official prostrations, and the celebration of birthdays as filial obligations.

## Role in Qing Court Life

Three distinct domains of court life in the Empress Dowager's era were structured by the Book of Rites:

### Ceremonial Protocol

During the Emperor's Birthday celebrations, each Prince and Noble "knelt and made the prostration prescribed by the Book of Rites" before presenting a jade *ruyie* emblem to the throne (009-011). The rigid postures, the graded access to imperial spaces, and the order of presentations were all codified in the text. The Master of Ceremonies who attended the Emperor ensured every gesture conformed to its ancient prescriptions.

### Filial Duty and Birthday Observance

The Book of Rites elevated the celebration of parental birthdays to a near-religious duty. As the text records: "The poorest in the land, if they are not able to keep any other festival, always celebrate with as much pomp as possible the birthdays of their parents. This is one of the duties enjoined by the Book of Rites" (023-003). This provision compelled the Guangxu Emperor to insist on a fitting celebration of the Empress Dowager's birthday despite her expressed wish for economy during a time of national distress.

### The Emperor as High Priest

The Book of Rites assigned to the Emperor the singular role of High Priest in the state cult. He alone was worthy to sacrifice at the Temple of Heaven and the other great altars, "at times set aside in the Book of Rites, and on special occasions" (029-008). In times of famine, drought, or national calamity, the Emperor—as "Son of Heaven"—publicly declared his responsibility, following the formula prescribed by the text: "I will purify myself by sacrifice that these calamities may be lifted from the Empire and the people. I alone am responsible" (029-008).

> The Emperor is not only the one High Priest worthy to sacrifice on the great altars to the Invisible Deity, the Priestly Hierarchy of the whole of this Cult of Nature is vested in his Sacred Person.
>
> (029-008)
