The young Empress ([[empress-dowager|Longyu]]), the first wife of the [[emperor-kwang-hsu|Emperor Kwang-Hsu]], was the first Lady of the Court after Her Majesty the [[empress-dowager|Empress Dowager]]. Katharine Carl, who observed her closely throughout her year at the Chinese Court, described her as "a charming character" — a woman of sweet dignity, kindly nature, and sometimes poignant resignation. (005-002)

## Family and Marriage

The young Empress was the daughter of Duke Chow, General of one of the [[manchu-customs|Manchu]] Banner Corps and a brother of the reigning Empress Dowager. She was thus a first cousin of the Emperor, and his senior by three years. Her mother, a lady of high birth, ancient lineage, and great distinction, brought her up with much care, and she also "had the advantage of being a great deal at the Court with her august Aunt," becoming highly accomplished by Chinese standards. (005-002)

She was affianced at an early age to the Emperor, but as custom dictated, their marriage did not take place for several years. It was "celebrated with great pomp at the [[winter-palace|Winter Palace]] in February, 1889, the week before the young Emperor himself took in hand the reins of Government" — until then held by the Empress Dowager — and became Emperor in reality. (005-002)

## Physical Appearance

Carl provides a detailed portrait of the young Empress's appearance. She was small, "not quite five feet tall, with exquisitely dainty hands and feet, of most patrician type." (005-003) She had a narrow, high-bred face with a thin, high nose, and eyes "more of the Chinese type, as we conceive it, than either the Emperor's or Empress Dowager's." Her chin was long and of the type "generally called strong," while her mouth was "large and extremely sensitive." (005-003)

Most striking, however, was the quality of her expression: "Her eyes have so kindly a look, her face shines with so sweet an expression, criticism is disarmed and she seems beautiful." Carl found her to possess "a sweet dignity, charming manners, and a lovable nature," though she noted "there is sometimes a look in her eyes of patient resignation that is almost pathetic." (005-003)

## Position at Court

The young Empress was constantly at the Empress Dowager's side. On the [[imperial-barge|Imperial barge]], she sat on Her Majesty's left while Carl occupied the place of honor on the right. (002-017) At meals, she dined with the Empress Dowager and accompanied the Imperial party on lake excursions, festival processions, and daily promenades through the Palace grounds.

Carl observed that while she did not possess "any great executive ability, though full of tact," when the Empress Dowager was in retirement and she was the first Lady at Court, "she is said to have shown great capability in her conduct of affairs." (005-003) Her "dignity, perfect breeding, and natural kindness of heart would insure this." (005-003)

## The Imperial Marriage

Carl took care to correct Western misconceptions about the Imperial household. She noted pointedly: "I have often seen allusions made to the 'Imperial Harem'; there is no such thing as an Imperial Harem at the Court of His Majesty the Emperor Kwang-Hsu. He has only these two wives." (005-004) The secondary wife, daughter of a Viceroy, was treated with "the most kindly consideration by the young Empress" and held precedence over all other Ladies, her position at Court "second only to that of the young Empress." (005-004)

## Court Relationships

The complex web of relationships at the Court all connected to the young Empress. One evening at dinner, the Princess Sih-Gerga explained to Carl "the relationships of the different Princesses to each other and to the young Empress," which made them all related to the Emperor and Empress Dowager as well — though "neither of Their Majesties' names was mentioned in this connection, for such would have been a great piece of presumption, amounting almost to sacrilege." (005-014)

The young Empress had her own maids, tiring-women, and attendants within the Palace. Along with the secondary wife, each Princess also maintained her own private household staff. (005-015)
