# Sir Claude MacDonald

Sir Claude MacDonald was the British Minister to China during Katherine Carl's residence at the Chinese Court in 1903–1904. A senior figure in the [[corps-diplomatique|Corps Diplomatique]], MacDonald played an important role in the normalization of foreign relations with the Qing Court following the [[boxer-rebellion|Boxer Rebellion]].

## Role in Late Qing Diplomacy

As British Minister, MacDonald was one of the most influential foreign diplomats in [[peking|Peking]]. Britain was the dominant foreign power in China at this period, and the British Minister's views carried significant weight within the diplomatic community. MacDonald had served through the Boxer crisis of 1900 and was well positioned to assess the Court's evolving attitude toward foreigners.

## In Carl's Account

MacDonald appears in Carl's memoir in connection with the historic first audience given to foreign ladies. Carl notes that MacDonald's report on the Empress Dowager was notably positive: "At her first Audience to foreigners, Sir Claude MacDonald, in reporting it, spoke of the Empress Dowager as 'a kind and courteous hostess, who displayed both the tact and softness of a woman'" (031-013).

This favorable characterization was politically significant. Following decades of hostile Western perception of the Empress Dowager—particularly after the Boxer Rebellion—MacDonald's diplomatic endorsement helped reshape the foreign view of the Chinese ruler. Carl's own portrait project was, in many respects, an extension of this public-relations effort.

## Significance

MacDonald exemplifies the diplomatic corps' gradual reassessment of the Empress Dowager in the post-Boxer period. His characterization of her as a gracious hostess—rather than the malevolent figure of wartime propaganda—reflected the Court's successful campaign to improve its international image, a campaign in which Carl's portrait played a central role.
