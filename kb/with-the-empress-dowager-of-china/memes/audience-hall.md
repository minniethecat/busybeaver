# Audience Hall

The Audience Hall was the principal ceremonial chamber of the Imperial Palace in [[peking|Peking]], where the [[empress-dowager|Empress Dowager]] and the [[emperor-kwang-hsu|Emperor]] received foreign diplomats, [[chinese-official|Chinese officials]], and distinguished visitors. Katherine Carl's memoir contains numerous references to this great hall, the setting for the most formal occasions of Court life.

## Architecture and Setting

The Audience Hall was designed to impress. The throne stood at the northern end of the hall, elevated on a dais and approached through ranks of courtiers. Silk cushions were arranged for kneeling officials, and the elaborate decorations—[[silk-court-dress|silk hangings]], [[incense-burning|incense burners]], and imperial insignia—created an atmosphere of majesty. Carl notes that the hall's grandeur served a political purpose: to overawe foreign visitors with the power and antiquity of the Chinese throne.

## In Carl's Account

The Audience Hall appears throughout Carl's memoir as the setting for key ceremonial events: the [[first-audience|first audience]] of foreign ladies, the [[imperial-audience-ceremony|imperial audience ceremonies]], the [[chinese-new-year-court|Chinese New Year]] receptions, and individual presentations of distinguished visitors such as [[prince-adalbert|Prince Adalbert of Prussia]].

Carl's own presentation to the Empress Dowager, however, did not take place in the great Audience Hall but in the more intimate setting of the Empress Dowager's private quarters—a distinction that emphasized the special favor extended to the portrait artist.

The ritual of the hall is vividly described: officials kneeling on silk cushions (016-012), the measured approach to the throne, the interpreters of the [[wai-wu-pu|Wai-Wu-Pu]] mediating between languages, and the Empress Dowager's commanding presence presiding over all.

> When we arrived within the Precincts we met the Empress Dowager and the Emperor coming out of the Great Audience Hall from their joint Audience. When Her Majesty saw us she stopped, as did the whole train of her attendant Ladies and eunuchs. She called me up to her side, took my hand, and asked me how I had rested and if I were ready to work again.
>
> (002-002)
