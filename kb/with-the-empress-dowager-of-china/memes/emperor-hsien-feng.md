# Emperor Hsien-Feng

Emperor Hsien-Feng (the Xianfeng Emperor) was the seventh ruler of the Qing dynasty and the husband of the Empress Dowager Cixi. His death in 1861, far from Peking at the summer palace of Jehol, set in motion the political arrangements that would shape the Court Katherine Carl encountered four decades later (030-006).

## Role at the Chinese Court

Hsien-Feng was the reigning emperor during the Second Opium War (1856–1860) and the Taiping Rebellion — twin crises that threatened the dynasty's survival. He was the brother of Prince Kung, the reformist statesman whose name later became "synonymous with Progress" (003-018). His decision to make the Sea Palace a "sacred enclosure" established it as an imperial retreat (011-006), a status it retained when Carl painted the Empress Dowager's portrait there in 1903.

## Biographical Sketch


> His father, the Emperor Hsien-Feng died at Jehol, far from Peking, where the Court had gone at the approach of the foreigners, who were aiding in quelling the Taiping rebellion. The times were critical. The integrity of China, the future, even, of the Empire, depended upon the action of its ministers and its rulers at this crisis.
>
> (030-006)
Hsien-Feng ascended the throne in 1850 at the age of nineteen. His first wife died "two months before he came to the Throne," an early personal tragedy that preceded the dynasty's mounting political disasters (030-004). He took Cixi (the future Empress Dowager) as a consort, and she bore him his only son — the future Emperor Tung-Chih (030-005). In 1861, with the dynasty besieged by foreign invasion and domestic rebellion, Hsien-Feng died at Jehol, the imperial summer retreat north of Peking (030-006). His death left the throne to his five-year-old son and the regency to two Empresses Dowager — Cixi and Ci'an — who together "brought the Emperor Tung-Chih through his minority" (030-005) (030-008).

## Interaction with the Author

Katherine Carl never met Hsien-Feng — he had been dead for over forty years by the time of her residence at the Court. His presence in the memoir is entirely through memory and consequence: the widows who survived him (015-003), the son who succeeded him (003-018), the political structures his death created (030-005), and the physical spaces — the Sea Palace (011-006) — that he shaped and that Carl later inhabited.

## Character and Appearance

Carl offers no description of Hsien-Feng's appearance or personality. The memoir's account of him is purely historical, referring to the structural facts of his reign and death rather than to the man himself. The one emotional detail Carl records is the devotion of his widows: two of the "remarkable women, widows of the Emperor Hsien-Feng" remained at Court decades after his death, one living "the life of a student" and the other absorbed in the domestic life of the palace (015-003).

## Historical Significance

Hsien-Feng's reign was a turning point in Qing history. The disasters of his final years — military defeat by Britain and France, the burning of the Summer Palace, the humiliating treaties of 1860 — shattered the dynasty's confidence and opened the door to the reform movements and conservative reactions that would define the next half-century. His death at Jehol created the regency that brought Cixi to power, an arrangement from which she never relinquished control (030-005) (030-006). When Carl arrived at the Court in 1903, the political architecture Hsien-Feng's death had created was still in place — his widow still ruled, and the Court still revolved around the woman who had entered it as his consort half a century earlier.

## In the Narrative

Hsien-Feng appears in Carl's memoir only as a point of origin — the husband, the father, the dead emperor whose absence structured everything that followed. His name is invoked to explain the Sea Palace (011-006), the childless succession (003-018), the two Empresses' shared regency (030-005), and the anniversary rituals that marked Court life (028-012). He is less a character than a historical force: the sovereign whose premature death set in motion the political narrative in which Carl, four decades later, would play her small part.
