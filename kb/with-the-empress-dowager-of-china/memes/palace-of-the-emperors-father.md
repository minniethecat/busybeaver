# Palace of the Emperor's Father

The Palace of the Emperor's Father was the former residence of
Prince Ch'un, the Seventh Prince — father of the reigning
[[emperor-kwang-hsu|Emperor Kwang-Hsu]] and brother of the late Emperor Hsien-Feng.
During Katherine Carl's portrait commission, the
[[empress-dowager|Empress Dowager]] set aside this palace as Carl's private residence,
restoring it after its occupation by foreign troops in 1900.

## Location and Context

The palace occupied an extensive park within Peking,
its high walls enclosing pavilions, gardens, a lake, and a small theater.
Carl described the approach:

> The Palace of the Emperor's Father, which the Empress Dowager
> had set aside for me to live in while I was at work on her portrait,
> was a splendid demesne, with a noble park and spacious buildings. (003-002)

The palace had been damaged during the [[boxer-rebellion|Boxer Rebellion]]
and stood vacant until the Empress Dowager ordered it
"hastily restored and refurnished" for Carl's occupation. (003-002)
Many of the outlying pavilions and summerhouses remained in ruins,
while the stables were "but partly rebuilt." (003-002)

## Description

The palace followed the classic Chinese pattern of
"verandahed pavilions built around spacious courts." (003-002)
Carl chose as her living quarters a group of buildings
in a walled garden facing a lotus-covered lake,
with a winding stream spanned by a picturesque bridge at the rear. (003-003)

The main pavilion featured a lofty central hall
with great doors opening onto a wide verandah.
Marble steps descended into a court filled with flowering shrubs.
A stone wall with latticework faced the central hall,
its massive wooden doors opening onto a terrace
"shaded by fine old elms, over the lake." (003-003)

The Chinese nicknamed this residence the "Ker-Gunia Fu" —
"Ker-Gunia" being "Miss Carl" rendered into Chinese,
and "Fu" meaning "Palace." (003-003)
Carl learned that these very pavilions had been the boyhood home
of the future Emperor Kwang-Hsu after his selection as Heir. (003-003)

The park beyond contained eminences crowned by summerhouses.
In one of these, Carl discovered a stone inscribed with a poem
by Prince Ch'un himself — a meditation on the transience of glory,
inspired by the lotus flowers below. (003-005)

## Role in Court Life

The palace served Carl as both home and studio,
its peaceful gardens offering respite from the formality of the Court.
Her mornings were free to explore after the Empress Dowager's
sittings concluded at ten or eleven o'clock,
and "I discovered new beauties each day." (003-004)

The palace's restoration for a foreign artist was itself
a diplomatic gesture. By housing Carl in the former residence
of the Emperor's own father, the Empress Dowager signaled
the importance she attached to the portrait commission
and her regard for its painter.

## In the Narrative

Chapter 3 of Carl's memoir is devoted entirely to this palace,
titled "The Palace of the Emperor's Father
(Prince Ch'un, the Seventh Prince)." (003-001)
The chapter opens the domestic portion of Carl's narrative,
establishing the setting where she would live and work
for the duration of her commission.

The palace also appears at the close of the memoir,
when Carl, having returned to the [[summer-palace|Summer Palace]] for her departure,
fondly recalled:

> My garden in the Park of the Palace of the Emperor's Father
> was full of them, and their sweet perfume loaded the air. (035-002)

The memory of its gardens lingered as her final impression of China.

## Historical Significance

Prince Ch'un's palace embodied the intimate connection
between the Empress Dowager's family and the throne.
The Seventh Prince was brother to the Emperor Hsien-Feng
(husband of the Empress Dowager), and his wife —
the Empress Dowager's own sister — was the mother of
Emperor Kwang-Hsu. (003-018)

Carl recognized the Prince's historical importance:

> This Prince was a valued friend of the two Empresses
> during the Regency, and he remained, up to the time of his death,
> one of the most trusted advisers. He was recognized by foreigners,
> as well as by the Chinese, to be an enlightened Prince
> as well as a man of fine character. (003-018)

The palace's occupation by foreign troops in 1900 and subsequent
restoration for a foreign artist in 1903 traces, in microcosm,
the trajectory of China's forced opening and the Qing court's
efforts at diplomatic rehabilitation.
