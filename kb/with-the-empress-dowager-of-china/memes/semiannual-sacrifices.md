# Semiannual Sacrifices at the Qing Court

The semiannual ritual sacrifices of the Qing Court, encompassing both the Confucian memorial ceremonies performed at the Palace temple and the Emperor's solemn solstice sacrifices at the great Altar of Heaven, witnessed by Katherine Carl in 1903.

## Overview

The Qing Court observed two principal forms of semiannual sacrifice: the memorial ceremony to Confucius, held at the Palace temple beneath the Temple of the Ten Thousand Buddhas, and the Emperor's great solstice sacrifices to the Invisible Deity at the Temple of Heaven. Both were elaborate rituals combining music, offerings, prostrations, and the ceremonial burning of gifts and prayers (013-005)(029-004).

Katherine Carl attended the Confucian ceremony at the Summer Palace, drawn into the temple by the young Empress herself. Her account provides one of the most detailed foreign descriptions of Qing ritual practice from inside the ceremonial space (013-007).

## The Confucian Memorial Ceremony

### Background

Although Confucianism is a philosophy rather than a religion, it has "certain rites and ceremonies observed by the Confucians" (013-004). The semiannual sacrifices commemorated Confucius as "an ethical teacher, a wise philosopher, a Sage" (013-005).

Confucian temples contained no images. They were "classic halls, bare of all church-like ornamentation," with quotations from the Analects adorning the walls on scrolls, in carved wood, and cut into stone. At the place where an altar would stand in a religious temple was a plain niche painted in red, bearing a tablet inscribed in gold: "The Seat of the Perfect One." On either side stood similar niches containing tablets of four other great Sages, including Mencius (013-005).

### Carl's Account

The ceremony took place on the afternoon of Carl's first steam-launch excursion on the lake. Their Majesties, the Empress, and the Ladies proceeded in full Court dress through the verandahed corridors from the Throne-room to the temple, accompanied by "the slow beating of drums" (013-006).

Three yellow cushions were placed on the paved floor for Their Majesties and the Empress; red cushions for the Ladies. The ceremony commenced with rhythmic music while Their Majesties knelt and prostrated themselves three times, with the Empress and Ladies doing likewise. Officials and other participants knelt outside in the court (013-006).

The ritual involved multiple genuflections and prostrations by the celebrants, punctuated by the ceremonial moving of offerings on the dragon table. The chief officiant read an address from a long scroll, then placed it on a casket on the altar. After the interior portion, celebrants proceeded to the outer court to intone six hymns — the "Odes to Peace" — and make renewed prostrations (013-006).

### The Ceremonial Burning

At the conclusion, the scroll with the address, along with portions of each offering, was placed in the huge iron incense-burner in the center of the outer court and set alight by the chief celebrant. A flagon of wine was poured over the blaze (013-006).

The Chinese explained to Carl that the address had "filled its mission when it was read" and that "the ashes of a literary essay were a most fitting offering to the memory of Confucius, the great philosopher" (013-008). After the ceremony, the Empress Dowager invited Carl to examine the rare old bronze ornaments, candelabra, and other ritual vessels on the altar (013-008).

Carl, though not participating in the ritual, "listened reverently to the intoning of the hymns and the reading of the address" and "conducted myself as I would at any religious ceremony." The Chinese Ladies, she noted, "seemed to appreciate it" (013-007).

## The Emperor's Solstice Sacrifices

The second form of semiannual sacrifice — the Emperor's great ceremonies at the Temple of Heaven — represented the highest expression of Chinese state religion. Carl described these with evident admiration, drawing on what she had learned at Court.

### The Nature Worship

The worship of Nature, embodying "their highest idea of an Invisible Deity," was the purest form of religion in China (029-002). Its temples were located in a magnificent park in the Chinese City of Peking, dominated by the Temple of Heaven — "spherical and triple-domed," tiled in sacred green and vaulted in Heaven's blue (029-002).

The grandest temple was that to the Invisible Deity itself: "Its foundations are the Earth, its walls are limitless Ether, its dome Heaven's own vault!" (029-002). At its great open altar, the Nature worship "has its culmination and reaches its highest fulfilment" — a holy of holies built of pure white marble, round as the Earth, with three superposed circles symbolizing "the Trinity in Nature and its Infinity" (029-003).

### The Emperor as High Priest

The Emperor of China, as "Son of Heaven," was the one High Priest of this cult. He alone was worthy "to perform its unique ceremonies, on its one great Altar, in its single great Temple" (029-004). Before the semiannual celebration, the Emperor prepared himself with a rigorous three-day fast, spending the final night in vigil at the Purifying Palace in the Great Park (029-005).

Carl described the dual nature of the ceremony: it was simultaneously "the most solemn of the religious rites the Emperor performs" and "the most formal of his official acts as ruler of the Great Empire" (029-009). For the religious aspect, he fasted and subdued the body; for the official ceremony, he was "accompanied by all his ministers and the highest nobles of the land and surrounded by splendid pomp and Imperial pageantry" (029-009).

### The Imperial Office

The Emperor was not only High Priest but also Expiator. In times of national calamity — famine, drought, or other misfortune — the Emperor publicly held himself responsible, saying according to the Book of Rites: "I will purify myself by sacrifice that these calamities may be lifted from the Empire and the people. I alone am responsible" (029-008).

## Cultural and Religious Context

Carl observed that despite the elaborate ceremonial forms, the Chinese were "not a religious people, though so moral a race." They were "rather followers of a philosophy than members of religious bodies" (029-011). The doctrines of Buddha and Laozi had become "so encrusted with error in China as to afford no moral or ideal help to their followers" (029-011).

Instead, the Chinese derived "all their moral support from the writings of Confucius and all their ideals from communion with Nature." Carl concluded that they were "really philosophers and worshipers of Nature, and the Emperor's semiannual sacrifice on the altar of Heaven and those at the Temples of Nature typify the real worship of the people" (029-011).

## Significance

The semiannual sacrifices reveal fundamental aspects of Qing imperial ideology. The Emperor's triple role — ruler, high priest, and expiator — fused political authority with cosmic responsibility. The sacrifices publicly enacted the relationship between the Son of Heaven, the natural order, and the welfare of the people.

For Carl, these ceremonies demonstrated that beneath the elaborate conventions of Qing Court life lay a philosophy intimately connected to Nature. She believed this connection to Nature was "the secret of their wonderful vitality" as a civilization (029-012).
