# Double Dragon

The double dragon was the most important imperial emblem of the Chinese court. It depicted two five-clawed dragons entwined in pursuit of a flaming pearl, symbolising the sovereign's eternal power.

## Description

The double dragon motif showed two dragons rampant, facing each other across a central flaming pearl. The dragons bore five claws on each foot — a privilege reserved exclusively for the imperial family.

Their bodies intertwined in dynamic curves, with finely detailed scales, whiskers, and horns. The motif appeared in multiple media: embroidered in gold on silk robes (023-007), carved in relief on wooden doors (024-014), and cast in gilded ornaments throughout palace interiors.

On ceremonial costume, the double dragon was the dominant decorative element. The Empress Dowager's robes featured golden dragons against dark red or imperial yellow silk (009-006). High-ranking courtiers wore dragon garments too, though with fewer claws than the imperial five.

## Cultural Significance

In Chinese cosmology, the dragon was a beneficent symbol of cosmic energy, water, and Heaven's mandate. It was never the malevolent creature of Western lore.

The double-dragon form represented duality in harmony — yin and yang — in an eternal quest for balance (027-010). The flaming pearl between them symbolised wisdom, the cosmic essence that only the sovereign could channel.

Carl noted how the motif "sprawled" across every surface of imperial life. It covered footstools (018-015) and grand ceremonial halls alike (024-024). This omnipresence declared that the sovereign's authority reached into every corner of the physical world.

## In Carl's Account

Carl encountered the double dragon constantly during her nine months at court. She saw it on the "dragon footstool" beneath the Empress Dowager's feet, the carved motif visible under Her Majesty's robes (018-014) (018-015).

The motif dominated ceremonial dress. Carl noted the Empress Dowager's summer headdress was "elaborately embroidered in the golden double dragon" (023-007). The imperial robe itself bore "the double dragon, reaching below the knee" (026-013).

During Audience ceremonies, officials lined up in dragon-embroidered robes, their rank readable in the dragon patterns they wore. Each man was marked as a servant of the throne by the emblem on his costume.

The architecture equally impressed her. The Audience Hall doors had "huge gilded dragons in high relief" (024-014). The imperial throne was "elaborately carved with the double dragon" (024-024). She saw in it "the eternal quest" at the heart of imperial ideology (027-010).

## Historical Context

The five-clawed dragon as imperial prerogative was codified under the Ming and strictly enforced under the Qing. Unauthorised use was a capital offence. Lesser princes used four-clawed dragons; commoners were forbidden dragon imagery on their dress.

The double-dragon configuration gained special prominence under the Qing. It appeared on the 1889 national flag — a blue dragon on yellow — and on coinage, seals, and diplomatic gifts. After 1912, the emblem was retired from official use, though it lives on in the decorative arts.

## Related Customs

The double dragon was closely tied to [[imperial-yellow]], the colour of the emperor. Dragon embroidery on [[silk-court-dress]] and [[brocade]] was done by specialised Palace workshops using gold-wrapped silk. The motif also appeared on [[imperial-throne]] furniture and the [[pearl-ornaments]] of imperial headdresses.
