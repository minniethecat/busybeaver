# Ruyie

The *ruyie* (如意, *rúyì*) was a ceremonial jade emblem central to Qing Court ritual. Katherine Carl, observing its presentation at the [[emperor-birthday|Emperor's Birthday]], noted that Westerners "erroneously supposed to be a scepter by most foreigners," but she clarified that it was in fact "simply an emblem of Good Luck, and may be presented on festive occasions to anyone whom the givers wish to honor" (009-011).

## Ceremonial Use

At the Emperor's Birthday ceremony, each Prince and Noble presented a *ruyie* to His Majesty: "The Emperor held each of these *ruyie* in his hands for a few seconds after their presentation, bowed profoundly to the kneeling Prince, and then handed the emblem to an attendant eunuch, who placed it on a Dragon table" (009-011).

The young Empress and the Ladies of the Court each also presented a *ruyie*. The Empress's *ruyie* was "of a much richer style than those presented by the other Ladies" (009-011), reflecting her higher rank.

## Not a Scepter

Carl was careful to distinguish the *ruyie* from Western symbols of power: it was not "an emblem of Imperial authority" but rather an emblem of good fortune (009-011). Its presentation was a gesture of honor and well-wishing, not of submission or fealty — a nuance that foreign observers often missed.

## In Gift-Giving

The *ruyie* also appeared among the reciprocal gifts from the Emperor. After the ceremonial congratulations, each invited guest received a tray containing "a jade *ruyie*... such as had been handed the Emperor at the morning ceremony, and an Archer's ring" (009-013). This practice reflected the elaborate [[present-giving|gift-giving]] customs of the Qing Court.

## Original Account

> Each splendidly garbed Prince and Noble knelt and made the prostration prescribed by the Book of Rites, and each presented His Majesty with a jade emblem, called by the Chinese *ruyie*, erroneously supposed to be a scepter by most foreigners; but the *ruyie* is simply an emblem of Good Luck, and may be presented on festive occasions to anyone whom the givers wish to honor, and is not an emblem of Imperial authority.
>
> (009-011)
