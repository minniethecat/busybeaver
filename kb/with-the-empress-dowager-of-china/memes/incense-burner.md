# Incense-Burner

Incense-burners, in bronze and gold, were among the most visible ritual objects at the Qing Court. Katherine Carl encountered them constantly — swung by eunuch acolytes before Imperial processions, standing on great tripods in moonlit ceremonies, and smoking with offerings in Palace temples.

## Ceremonial Use

The incense-burner accompanied the [[empress-dowager|Empress Dowager]] wherever she moved in state. At the conclusion of the [[emperor-birthday|Emperor's Birthday]] ceremonies, she descended the steps "preceded by acolytes, swinging golden incense-burners which gave forth azure clouds of perfumed smoke" (009-029).

At the [[sea-palace|Sea Palace]], Carl saw the Empress Dowager "coming out, preceded by acolytes swinging incense-burners, the musicians playing the '[[imperial-hymn|Imperial Hymn]]'" (010-014). The combination of music, incense, and stately movement created the distinctive atmosphere of Qing ritual.

## Moon Festival Ceremony

The incense-burner featured prominently in the [[moon-festival|Moon Festival]] ceremony: "Over all was poured some of the inflammable wine from the flagons on the altar, and the flames leaped high above the huge incense-burner that stood on a great bronze tripod in the center of the moonlit terrace" (018-007). Carl described "the brilliant circle of splendidly gowned Ladies... around the flaming censer, whose leaping flames glinted and glowed upon the jewels and gold embroidery of their costumes" (018-007).

## In Buddhist Worship

Incense-burning was essential to [[buddhist-worship|Buddhist worship]] at the Palace. Carl observed the Empress Dowager making offerings at her private chapels, preceded by eunuchs carrying incense-burners. The practice was part of the daily ritual of the Court.

Carl noted the ever-present fragrance in the Throne-room chapel:

> In front of the figure of Buddha stood the incense-burner, with perfumes constantly burning.
>
> (004-003)
