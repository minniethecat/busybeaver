# Edwin H. Conger

Edwin H. Conger served as the United States Minister to China during the pivotal years following the Boxer Rebellion. His wife, Mrs. Sarah Pike Conger, was the principal intermediary who arranged Katherine Carl's commission to paint the Empress Dowager Cixi's portrait [P04-001].

## Role at the Chinese Court

As American Minister, Conger represented the United States at the Qing Court during a period of intense diplomatic activity. The Boxer Rebellion of 1900 had severely damaged relations between China and the Western powers, and the post-Rebellion years required careful diplomatic reconstruction. Conger's wife, Mrs. Conger, developed a personal relationship with the Empress Dowager -- an unusual channel of communication that led directly to Carl's commission to paint the imperial portrait [P04-001].

## Biographical Sketch

Edwin Hurd Conger was born in Illinois in 1843 and served as a Union soldier in the American Civil War before entering politics. He served in the U.S. House of Representatives and was appointed Minister to Brazil before being transferred to China in 1898. His tenure in Peking spanned the Boxer Rebellion, during which the Legation Quarter was besieged for fifty-five days, and the subsequent period of reconstruction. He and his wife were "naturally, much interested" in the St. Louis Exposition, where the Empress Dowager's portrait -- the product of Carl's commission -- would be displayed [P04-001].

## Interaction with the Author

Katherine Carl's memoir opens with the letter she received from Mrs. Conger, "wife of the Minister of the United States to Peking," proposing that she paint the Empress Dowager's portrait [P04-001]. The memoir notes that both Mrs. Conger and Mr. Conger were "naturally, much interested" in the Exposition project [P04-001]. Carl recounts that she was transported in a cart "large enough to carry the party, Mrs. Conger and her interpreter and myself" (001-003), and that "the servants that followed Mrs. Conger's carriage were joined by a Chinese Guard of Honor" (001-004). While Mr. Conger does not appear directly in Carl's narrative of Court life, his position as Minister was the diplomatic framework within which the entire commission -- and thus Carl's entire memoir -- became possible.

> In April, 1903, while I was visiting in Shanghai, I received a letter from Mrs. Conger, wife of the Minister of the United States to Peking, in which she said there was a question of Her Majesty the Empress Dowager's having her portrait painted, and asking me if such a thing should be arranged would I be willing to come to Peking and undertake it.
>
> (P04-001)
