## 定义与名称

The Manchu Dynasty is the ruling imperial house of China throughout the narrative of *With the Empress Dowager of China*. Officially designated the "Dynasty of the Great Purity" (Da-Qing), it is referred to in the book as "the Manchu Dynasty." (008-003) The dynasty's formal name reflects Confucian ideals, as the author notes that the Manchu rulers "call theirs the 'Dynasty of the Great Purity' with a knowledge of Confucian teaching." (036-059)

His Majesty the [[emperor-kwang-hsu|Emperor Kwang-Hsu]] was the twelfth Emperor of this dynasty. (008-003) By the time of the author's visit to the court (1903–1904), the dynasty had already endured for more than two and a half centuries since [[manchu-customs|the Manchu conquest]] of China. (007-015)

## 统治与宫廷

At the time described in the book, the real power behind the throne was the [[empress-dowager|Empress Dowager Cixi]], who served as Regent during the Emperor's minority and continued to co-rule with him after his majority. The author describes the dynasty's rule as persisting "hundreds of years before the Manchu Dynasty came to the Throne" (031-010), emphasizing its longevity and institutional stability.

The dynasty's military was organized into Banner Corps — hereditary Manchu military units. The author mentions "General of one of the Manchu Banner Corps" (005-002) in connection with the imperial family, indicating the close integration of the military aristocracy with the court.

## 与汉族的关系

The Manchus were an ethnically distinct people from the Han Chinese majority they ruled. The author observes that "the Manchus have adopted many of the manners and customs of the Chinese" (007-015) over their centuries of rule, but also notes key differences — particularly regarding women's status. [[manchu-customs|Manchu women]] retained their distinctive dress, hairstyle, and most notably, "have never bound their feet" (007-015), a custom the author describes the Manchu women viewing with "as great a horror of it as Europeans have."

## 帝国晚期的改革

By the early 1900s, the dynasty faced mounting pressure for reform. The [[hundred-days-reform|1898 coup d'état]] represented a pivotal moment — the young Emperor Kwang-Hsu's reform program was halted by conservative forces, and the Empress Dowager returned to active rule. The author observes that by 1903–1904, "Her late edicts show" (008-010) a renewed commitment to gradual reform and progress, suggesting the dynasty was adapting — however slowly — to modern pressures.
