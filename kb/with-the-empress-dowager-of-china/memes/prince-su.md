# Prince Su

Prince Su was a Manchu prince of the Imperial clan who served at the Court during the period of Katherine Carl's memoir. He is noted in Carl's account as a witness to the Court's flight during the [[boxer-rebellion|Boxer Rebellion]] of 1900.

## Role in the Boxer Period

Prince Su was among the Imperial entourage that fled [[peking|Peking]] for Singan Fu (Xi'an) as the foreign relief force approached the capital. His account of this journey provides one of the few Chinese perspectives on the experience.

## In Carl's Account

Carl relates Prince Su's testimony concerning the hardships of the flight: "Prince Su, in his account of the journey to Singan Fu, relates that neither Her Majesty nor the Emperor had enough to eat; that the soldiers stole the food from them; that they had to sleep on ''k'angs'' without bedding, and that at last even the coarse brick ''k'angs'' were not to be had" (030-006).

The prince's account paints a picture of genuine privation: the Imperial party, accustomed to the elaborate provisioning of the Court, reduced to the most basic conditions. Carl notes that the local populations did not provide the expected support: "They felt the Court had been against them and for the foreigners" (030-006).

## Significance

Prince Su's testimony serves Carl's broader argument that the Empress Dowager's post-Boxer openness to foreigners was sincere and motivated by genuine experience of the costs of conflict. The prince's account of shared hardship humanizes the Imperial family and lends credibility to the narrative of reform and reconciliation that Carl's memoir advances.
