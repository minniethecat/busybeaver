# Flaming Pearl

The Flaming Pearl was the paramount symbol of the Qing Imperial Dynasty, appearing throughout the Palace on everything intended for Imperial use. Katherine Carl observed that it was "emblematic of the Unattainable" — the object of the [[double-dragon|Double Dragon's]] eternal pursuit (009-027).

## Symbolism

Carl was unable to discover the exact origin of the legend, but offered this interpretation: "The Double Dragon represents the Powers of Earth or Evil which try ever to absorb the Flaming Pearl, Emblem of the Dynasty, symbol of Heaven or Perfection. The Flaming Pearl, the Unattainable, keeps ever beyond and above their grasp, seeming to serve always as an incentive for further effort" (032-013).

## Appearances at Court

The Flaming Pearl was ubiquitous in Qing Imperial art and ceremony:

The Double Dragon's "contortions, as it struggled for the Flaming Pearl, emblematic of the unattainable, were most curious" during theatrical performances at the Emperor's Birthday (009-027).

At the portrait frame design, "the Double Dragon at the top struggled for the 'flaming pearl' with the character 'Sho' on it" — ''Sho'' meaning Longevity (033-003).

During the New Year celebrations, "a pair of illuminated dragons writhed into the court and struggled for the 'flaming pearl,' which flitted around with elusive fantastic movements, ever beyond their grasp" (032-013).

## The Princess Button

The Flaming Pearl also appeared as a personal ornament. The "Princess Button," worn only by Ladies of the Court, "represents the Flaming Pearl of the Dynasty" (029-013). It consisted of "a large pearl, surrounded by three alternating rows of seed-pearls and corals, which are supposed to represent flames" (029-013). This jewel was "established by the founders of the Dynasty and is the distinguishing jewel of the members of the Imperial Family" (029-013).

## Imperial Iconography

Carl catalogued the Flaming Pearl's appearances: "It is on all the Thrones of the Dynasty; it adorns the Imperial pennant; it is cut into stone, carved into wood, and painted in pictures. It decorates the gowns of the higher officials, and is embroidered upon the Court dresses of the Ladies of the Palace" (032-013).
