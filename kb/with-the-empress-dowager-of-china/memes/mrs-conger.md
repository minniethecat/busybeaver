# Mrs. Conger

Wife of the United States Minister to China, [[mrs-conger|Mrs. Conger]] was the pivotal intermediary
who brought [[katherine-carl|Katherine Carl]] to the attention of the [[empress-dowager|Empress Dowager]] Cixi and
facilitated the portrait commission that is the subject of Carl's memoir.
Residing at the [[american-legation|American Legation]] in [[peking|Peking]],
she was one of the few Western women granted regular access to the inner court
during the late Qing dynasty.

## Role at the Chinese Court

Mrs. Conger occupied an unusual position in the diplomatic world of late-Qing Peking.
As the wife of Edwin H. Conger, the United States Minister to China (1898–1905),
she enjoyed diplomatic privileges without the formal constraints that bound her husband's official role.
This allowed her to cultivate personal relationships with the women of the imperial household.

The Empress Dowager received her with marked cordiality:

> …I looked around and saw a charming little lady, with a brilliant smile,
> greeting Mrs. Conger very cordially. One of the Ladies Yu-Keng whispered,
> "Her Majesty"… (001-007)

Unlike most foreign visitors, who encountered the Empress Dowager only in formal audience settings,
Mrs. Conger was invited to private dinners, theatrical performances, and informal viewings of the portrait in progress.
She was seated in "the place of honor" beside the Empress Dowager during entertainments —
a mark of favor that astonished other foreign observers. (001-025)

## Biographical Sketch

Sarah Pike Conger (circa 1843–1932) was married to Edwin Hurd Conger,
a former Iowa congressman who served as United States Minister to China from 1898 to 1905.
The Congers arrived in Peking shortly before the Boxer Rebellion of 1900
and were among the foreign community besieged in the Legation Quarter during that crisis.

Mrs. Conger's letters from China, later published, reveal a woman of keen observation
and genuine curiosity about Chinese culture — qualities that distinguished her
from many of her diplomatic contemporaries.
After the Boxer crisis, she became one of the first Western women received
in private audience by the Empress Dowager, in December 1902. [P04-001]

It was Mrs. Conger who, "by her own individual initiative," (010-009)
conceived the idea of having the Empress Dowager's portrait painted
by a Western artist for exhibition at the St. Louis Exposition.
She wrote to Katherine Carl, then staying in Shanghai, proposing the commission:

> …I received a letter from Mrs. Conger, wife of the Minister of the
> United States to Peking, in which she said there was a question of
> Her Majesty the Empress Dowager's having her portrait painted, and
> asking me if such a thing should be arranged would I be willing to
> come to Peking and undertake it. [P04-001]

## Interaction with the Author

Mrs. Conger's relationship with Katherine Carl was both professional and personal.
The two women shared quarters at the American Legation in Peking, where Carl was
"hospitably entertained by my kind friends, Mr. and Mrs. Conger." (010-008)

Mrs. Conger accompanied Carl on the first journey to the Summer Palace,
riding in a procession of sedan chairs and Chinese carts
with servants and luggage following. (001-003)
She was called to inspect the portrait at every stage:

> When she looked at the portrait, she called Mrs. Conger and the Princesses
> to see what I had been doing. (001-019)

Beyond formal moments, Carl depicts Mrs. Conger as a steadying presence —
someone who understood the intricate protocols of the Court
and could smooth Carl's path through unfamiliar rituals.
When Carl grew fatigued by palace life, Mrs. Conger arranged visits to Peking
where the two could rest in the familiarity of the Legation compound. (010-006)

## Character and Appearance

Carl's memoir does not provide an extended physical description of Mrs. Conger,
but her character emerges clearly through the narrative.
She is portrayed as tactful, resourceful, and possessed of a diplomatic instinct
that allowed her to navigate late-Qing court protocol with grace.

The Empress Dowager's evident trust in her speaks to Mrs. Conger's discretion and goodwill:

> She then inquired if I had had a good trip into Peking, and how Mrs. Conger was. (010-011)

This solicitude was remarkable in a ruler who rarely expressed personal interest in foreigners,
testifying to the genuine affection Mrs. Conger had cultivated at the highest levels of the Qing court.

## Historical Significance

Mrs. Conger's role in the portrait commission has significance beyond the personal.
The project represented one of the first major acts of cultural diplomacy
between the Qing court and the United States after the Boxer Rebellion.
By arranging for a Western artist to paint the Empress Dowager's portrait
for display at the 1904 St. Louis World's Fair,
she helped shape American perceptions of China at a pivotal moment.

The portrait and Carl's subsequent memoir offered Western audiences
an intimate view of the Chinese court that ran counter to prevailing stereotypes.
Mrs. Conger's conception — that a sympathetic artistic representation
could do more for international understanding than diplomatic correspondence —
was visionary for its time.

## In the Narrative

Mrs. Conger appears at every crucial juncture of the memoir.
She is the instigator (writing the initial letter), the facilitator
(arranging transport, interpreting protocol, providing lodging),
and the witness (present at audiences, inspections, and the final viewing).

The narrative arc follows Mrs. Conger from initiator to observer:
she sets events in motion, then steps back as Carl develops her own relationship
with the Empress Dowager, reappearing at critical moments —
a theatrical performance, a garden-party, the final viewing —
as both participant in and audience to the unfolding drama of the portrait.
