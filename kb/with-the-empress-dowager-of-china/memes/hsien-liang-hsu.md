# Hsien-Liang-Hsu

The Hsien-Liang-Hsu, or "Temple of the Loyal and Virtuous," is a temple compound in [[peking|Peking]] that formerly served as the Peking residence of [[li-hung-chang|Li-Hung-Chang]], the renowned Chinese statesman and Viceroy. After Li's death, a portion of the temple became the home of the Yu-Keng family (010-010).

## Location and Context

The Hsien-Liang-Hsu is situated within Peking, on the route between the Legation Quarter and the [[sea-palace|Sea Palace]]. Katherine Carl passed through it on her way to visit the Sea Palace during the Court's sojourn in Peking (010-010).

## Description

The temple compound was substantial enough to accommodate both the former residence of a Viceroy and, in part, the home of the Yu-Keng family. The Yu-Kengs, a prominent Manchu family who had served as diplomats in Paris, arranged their residence there after the destruction of their own semi-foreign house by the Boxers in 1900 (010-010).

## Role in Court Life

The Hsien-Liang-Hsu served as a waypoint on Carl's journey from the Legation Quarter to the Sea Palace:

> I was first carried to the Hsien-Liang-Hsu, the "Temple of the loyal and virtuous," where Li-Hung-Chang formerly had his home in Peking, and a part of which the Yu-Kengs had arranged for their home after their return from their mission at Paris, their own semi-foreign house having been destroyed by the Boxers. (010-010)

At the Hsien-Liang-Hsu, Carl was joined by the Ladies Yu-Keng—the daughters of Lord Yu-Keng who served as her interpreters and companions at Court—and they continued together to the Sea Palace (010-010).

## In the Narrative

The Hsien-Liang-Hsu appears in Chapter 10 as part of Carl's description of her day-trip to the Sea Palace. It provides a glimpse into the living arrangements of the Manchu elite who straddled the Chinese and foreign worlds in late Qing Peking.

## Historical Significance

The temple's association with Li-Hung-Chang (1823–1901), one of the most important Chinese officials of the late 19th century, gives it particular historical significance. Li served as Viceroy of Zhili, negotiated the Boxer Protocol with the foreign powers, and was a key figure in China's Self-Strengthening Movement. The Yu-Keng family's residence there after Li's death illustrates the interconnected world of late Qing officialdom.
