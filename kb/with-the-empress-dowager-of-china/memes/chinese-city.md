# Chinese City

The Chinese City is the southernmost of the three walled districts of [[peking|Peking]], lying outside the [[tartar-city|Tartar City]] to the south. It was observed by Katherine Carl during her residence at the Chinese Court in 1903–1904 and is noted primarily for its magnificent temple parks.

## Location and Context

The City of Peking is composed of three walled towns—the Chinese City, the [[tartar-city|Tartar City]], and the [[imperial-city|Imperial City]] (024-005). The Chinese City forms the outer southern section, separated from the Tartar City by the great southern wall. Within its boundaries lie some of the most important religious sites of the Empire.

## Description

The Chinese City contains a magnificent Park housing the great Nature-worship temples of China:

> There are three great religions in China—Buddhism, Taoism, and the worship of Nature. The worship of Nature, in which is embodied their highest idea of an Invisible Deity, is the purest form of religion in China. Its Temples are situated in a magnificent Park in the Chinese City of Peking. (029-002)

The most imposing of these temples is the [[temple-of-heaven|Temple of Heaven]], described by Carl as "spherical and triple-domed," rearing its proud height and visible from afar. Its triple dome, tiled externally in the sacred green of Nature and vaulted internally in blue, is surrounded by groves of century-old arborvitae trees (029-002).

The park also contains the scarcely less splendid Temples to the Earth, the Sun and Moon, and the [[temple-of-agriculture|Temple of Agriculture]]. Most unique among them is the Temple to the Invisible Deity, whose foundations are the Earth, whose walls are limitless Ether, and whose dome is Heaven's own vault (029-002).

## Role in Court Life

The Chinese City's great temples were the setting for the Emperor's most solemn religious ceremonies. As the "Son of Heaven," the [[emperor-guangxu|Emperor Guangxu]] served as the one High Priest of the Nature cult, and it was in the Chinese City's Temple of Heaven that he performed the semiannual sacrifices for the prosperity of the Empire (029-004).

The Emperor prepared for these ceremonies with a rigorous three-day fast, spending the final night in vigil at the Purifying Palace within the great Park of the Nature Temples (029-005).

## In the Narrative

The Chinese City is described in Chapter 29, where Carl provides her account of Chinese religious rites. She visited the Temple of Heaven park and described the Altar to the Invisible Deity, a great open marble altar built in the center of a marble-paved space with secular arborvitae radiating in concentric avenues (029-003).

## Historical Significance

The Chinese City was the original Han Chinese district of Peking during the Ming and Qing dynasties, while the Tartar City to the north was reserved for the Manchu conquerors. The presence of the great Nature-worship temples in the Chinese City reflects its status as the spiritual heart of the capital. Today, the Temple of Heaven park remains one of Beijing's most iconic landmarks and a UNESCO World Heritage site.
