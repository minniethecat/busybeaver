# Prince Pu Lun

Prince Pu Lun was a Manchu prince of the Imperial clan who served as Imperial Commissioner to the [[st-louis-exposition|St. Louis Exposition]] of 1904. As the personal representative of Their Majesties, he was the highest-ranking Chinese official at the exposition and played a central role in the ceremonial presentation of the [[sacred-picture|Empress Dowager's portrait]].

## Role at the St. Louis Exposition

The Qing Court's decision to participate in the St. Louis Exposition was a significant diplomatic initiative, intended to improve China's international image and foster commercial ties. Prince Pu Lun's appointment as Imperial Commissioner signaled the importance the Court attached to the event.

## In Carl's Account

Carl describes the Prince's role in receiving the portrait: "His Imperial Highness Prince Pu L'un, Imperial Commissioner and personal representative of Their Majesties at the Exposition of St. Louis, awaited the arrival of the portrait" (034-012). A special railway car conveyed the painting from San Francisco, and the Prince's presence at its reception underscored its status as a state gift.

Following the exposition, the portrait continued to Washington, where [[sir-chentung-liang-cheng|Sir Chentung Liang Cheng]] formally presented it to President [[theodore-roosevelt|Theodore Roosevelt]]. Prince Pu Lun's role in the portrait's journey linked the exposition's cultural diplomacy with the formal channels of Sino-American relations.

## Significance

Prince Pu Lun represents the Imperial family's direct engagement with international cultural diplomacy. His presence at St. Louis—a Manchu prince on American soil—was itself a statement of China's willingness to participate in the international community on modern terms.
