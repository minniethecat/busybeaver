# Moon Festival

The Moon Festival (中秋节, *Zhōngqiū Jié*), also known as the Mid-Autumn Festival or the Festival of the Harvest Moon, was one of the major seasonal celebrations observed at the [[chinese-court|Imperial Court]]. Katherine Carl's memoir describes this festival in the context of the Court's elaborate calendar of seasonal observances.

## Traditional Observance

The Moon Festival falls on the fifteenth day of the eighth lunar month, when the moon is at its fullest and brightest. Traditional observances include family gatherings, moon-viewing parties, offerings to the moon goddess, and the consumption of moon cakes—round pastries symbolizing completeness and reunion.

## In Carl's Account

Carl describes the Mid-Autumn Festival as "popularly known as the Festival of the Harvest Moon, which is at its full at the time of the celebration" (018-003). It was "observed with due ceremony at the Palace," and featured theatrical performances including "the dramatization of the Legend of the Festival" (018-003). The legend told of a young handmaiden who ate a fairy's herb of immortality and "flew to the skies and took refuge in the Moon" (018-003).

Carl was particularly struck by the evening ceremony held "in an open space bathed in the rays of the softly glowing moon" (018-006), where Their Majesties "made the bows and prostrations to the Moon, and placed floral offerings on the altar" (018-007). The theatrical finale that day was "one of the most beautiful spectacular tableaux" Carl ever witnessed, representing "a lake covered with luminous lotus, with the full moon floating above" (018-004).

The festival coincided with the period when the Court was in residence at the [[summer-palace|Summer Palace]] or transitioning to the [[sea-palace|Sea Palace]], and the seasonal setting—the autumn gardens, the full moon over the palace lakes—provided a picturesque backdrop for the festivities.
