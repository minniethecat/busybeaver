# Sea Palace

The Sea Palace (Zhongnanhai) was an imperial palace complex in central [[peking|Peking]],
used by the [[empress-dowager|Empress Dowager]] Cixi as a secondary residence alongside the
[[summer-palace|Summer Palace]] and the [[winter-palace|Winter Palace]].
During [[katherine-carl|Katherine Carl]]'s portrait commission,
the Court moved to the Sea Palace in the spring of 1904,
providing Carl with a new setting for her work and fresh impressions of imperial life.

## Location and Context

The Sea Palace occupied a strategic position in central Peking,
its name deriving from the lakes ("seas") that formed its landscape.
Unlike the Summer Palace, which lay hours outside the city walls,
the Sea Palace was within easy reach of the Legation Quarter
and the Forbidden City — a convenience that made it a favored residence
for Court functions requiring diplomatic participation. (010-001)

Carl described it succinctly:

> The Sea Palace is a comparatively new Palace. (010-011)

This "newness" distinguished it from the ancient halls of the Forbidden City
and gave it a character more suited to the Empress Dowager's modernizing instincts.

## Description

The Sea Palace grounds featured a blend of traditional Chinese architecture
and cosmopolitan embellishments.
Carl noted the presence of two theaters — "one for winter use and one for summer" (010-021) —
reflecting the Empress Dowager's well-known passion for dramatic performance.

The garden landscape was dominated by ancient cedars:

> One of the most picturesque spots in the Sea Palace,
> shaded by magnificent cedars, was a favorite retreat. (010-020)

The Dragon Wall was another remarkable feature.
Carl observed this "splendid work of art" with its glazed tiles
and noted that "the Dragon Wall, in the Sea Palace, must have formed a part
of some former magnificent pleasure-ground." (011-006)
The wall's presence hinted at layers of imperial history
beneath the Palace's relatively recent construction.

## Role in Court Life

The Sea Palace hosted a range of Court activities.
Its theaters were the setting for the [[empress-dowager|Empress Dowager]]'s private performances,
where she entertained foreign visitors and court ladies alike.
The lakes and gardens provided the backdrop for the garden-parties
and informal gatherings that punctuated Carl's narrative.

The Palace was also a working seat of government.
When the Court moved to the Sea Palace in 1904,
"the members of the Corps Diplomatique" were received there, (032-012)
and the Grand Council continued its deliberations within its halls.

The move itself was a moment of transition in Carl's narrative:

> Her Majesty decided to move the Court to the Sea Palace.
> This Palace, though not so magnificent as the Summer Palace,
> was more convenient for receiving foreign visitors. (032-008)

## In the Narrative

The Sea Palace features prominently in the later chapters of Carl's memoir.
Chapter 10 opens with "Peking — The Sea Palace," (010-001)
marking the Court's relocation and Carl's first extended stay
in a palace within the capital itself.

The move to the Sea Palace in spring 1904 marked a new phase
in the portrait's creation. Carl's sedan chair now carried her
"to the Sea Palace instead of to the Winter Palace," (032-010)
and she found herself working in different surroundings —
the lakes and willows of Zhongnanhai replacing the courtyards
of the earlier palaces.

The Sea Palace also represented a modernization of imperial infrastructure.
Carl marveled at "a small railway" installed in the grounds, (033-007)
and noted that "splendid buildings were being erected" (033-005) —
signs of the Empress Dowager's willingness to adopt Western technologies
within her traditional domain.

## Historical Significance

The Sea Palace was more than a residence — it was a stage
for the Qing court's diplomatic reinvention after the Boxer Rebellion.
By receiving foreign visitors in a "comparatively new Palace"
equipped with modern amenities, the Empress Dowager presented an image
of a dynasty looking forward rather than back.

After the 1911 Revolution, the Sea Palace complex became
the headquarters of the Republican government and later
the central leadership compound of the People's Republic —
a trajectory that began, in Carl's account,
with the Court's spring 1904 decision to relocate there.
