# European Circus at the Palace

The European Circus at the Palace was a notable entertainment event held in the grounds of the [[summer-palace|Summer Palace]] during Katherine Carl's residence in 1903. The performance, described in Carl's memoir as a rare occasion when European entertainment was imported into the Imperial precincts, provided the artist with her first opportunity to observe the full complement of Manchu courtiers in one place.

## Context

Chinese Court entertainments typically centered on traditional forms—[[chinese-theater|Chinese theater]], musical performances, and ceremonial observances. The importation of a European circus represented a significant departure, reflecting the [[empress-dowager|Empress Dowager's]] curiosity about Western culture and her willingness to incorporate foreign elements into court life.

## Carl's Account

Carl's description of the circus appears in Chapter 21. The event was notable not merely for the performance itself but for the access it provided to the broader court: "Chinese ceremonies and entertainments are peculiar, and it was interesting to see the enjoyment of these Orientals who watched, for the first time in their lives, European entertainers" (021-002).

The preparations for the circus produced an unexpectedly rustic scene:

> One day we went out into the turnip field, and the Empress Dowager herself pulled the first turnip; then the Empress and all the Princesses pulled some, and when they found a curiously shaped one, it was given to Her Majesty. It was a strange sight to see the Great Empress Dowager, sitting there at the side of the field, on her yellow campstool, smiling and interested, with the turnips piled around her.
>
> (021-004)

The circus offered a rare social configuration: "At the performance of the European circus in the Palace grounds I saw, for the first time, nearly all the Princes and Manchu officials" (026-011). Normally, court protocol strictly segregated the male officials from the ladies of the court, but the novelty of the spectacle created an exceptional gathering.

Carl used this occasion to observe Manchu physical characteristics, noting that "The Manchus are a taller race than the Chinese" (026-011), a distinction frequently remarked upon by foreign observers of the period.

## Significance

The circus exemplifies the cultural diffusion occurring at the late Qing Court. While the Empress Dowager maintained traditional Chinese protocol in most matters, she selectively incorporated Western entertainments, technologies, and customs. The event also demonstrates Carl's anthropological eye—she used every gathering as an opportunity to observe and document Manchu society.
