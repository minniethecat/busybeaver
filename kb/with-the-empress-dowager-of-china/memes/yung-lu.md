# Yung Lu

Yung Lu was a Grand Secretary of the Qing Court whose death preceded Katherine Carl's arrival in Peking, but whose family — his widow and daughter — remained at the center of Court life during her residence in 1903–1904.

## Role at the Chinese Court

As Grand Secretary, Yung Lu held one of the highest civil offices in the Qing government. His position placed him among the small circle of senior officials who advised the throne and conducted the business of the empire (026-002).

## Biographical Sketch

Yung Lu died before Carl's arrival at the Court. His widow survived him and was described by Carl's memoir as having lost, "in one year, her husband and her only son" — a double bereavement that marked her as a figure of particular pathos at the Court (026-002). His daughter married into the family of Prince Ching, the Prime Minister, and was described as "more attractive in every way than Yung Lu's daughter, the wife of Prince Ching's son" (026-004). Through this marriage, Yung Lu's lineage became entwined with the highest echelons of the late Qing aristocracy.

## Interaction with the Author

Katherine Carl never met Yung Lu. She encountered him through the women who survived him — his widow, the "clever Princess Schun" (026-002), and his daughter, who had married into Prince Ching's family (026-004). The prominence of these women at the Court, and the detailed attention Carl pays to their personal histories, reflects the importance of the Yung Lu connection in the social fabric of the imperial household.

## Character and Appearance

Carl provides no direct description of Yung Lu. His character must be inferred from the position he held — Grand Secretary was an office reserved for men of proven administrative competence and political reliability — and from the status his family retained after his death. That his widow was described as "clever" and his daughter's marriage connected her to the Prime Minister's household suggests a family of substance and political consequence (026-002) (026-004).

## Historical Significance

Yung Lu represents the senior civil officials who formed the administrative backbone of the late Qing state. While figures like Prince Ching and the Empress Dowager dominate the memoir's foreground, men like Yung Lu — Grand Secretaries, Viceroys, and senior ministers — conducted the actual business of governance. His family's continued prominence after his death illustrates how official rank translated into enduring social status within the Court's hierarchy.

## In the Narrative

Yung Lu appears in Carl's memoir only through his survivors. His widow, Princess Schun, and his daughter are mentioned with the kind of specific personal detail Carl reserves for figures of genuine interest (026-002) (026-004). In the memoir's social geography, belonging to Yung Lu's family conferred a status that Carl — attentive as always to rank and connection — took care to record.
