## 职位与职责

The Grand Secretary was one of the most senior officials in the Qing imperial government. Every day, Their Majesties the [[empress-dowager|Empress Dowager]] and the [[emperor-kwang-hsu|Emperor]] held Audience and saw the Prime Minister and Grand Secretary, and there were frequent meetings of the [[grand-council|Grand Council]]. (016-006) The Grand Secretary, together with the Prime Minister and other members of the Grand Council, was responsible for reporting on affairs of State and advising Their Majesties on matters of governance.

The position carried immense prestige and was among the highest offices a Chinese official could attain. The Grand Secretary worked in close coordination with the [[wai-wu-pu|Wai-Wu-Pu]] (Foreign Office) and other Boards in managing the vast machinery of the Chinese Empire.

## 朝会中的特权

In the [[great-audience-hall|Great Audience Hall]], the Grand Secretary enjoyed a distinctive privilege: a permanently reserved kneeling cushion placed on the floor in front of the Throne dais. (016-008) During the hours of Audience, five cushions were arranged for members of the Grand Council to kneel upon when memorializing Their Majesties. The Prime Minister's cushion was nearest the Throne, and the Grand Secretary's cushion occupied the next position of honor. (016-008)

Crucially, the Prime Minister's and Grand Secretary's cushions "may on no condition be removed" — unlike the cushions of the three lower-ranking Cabinet members, which could be removed by the introducing eunuch for a fixed price, allowing lesser officials to kneel nearer the dais. (016-008) This immovability symbolized the unassailable rank of the Grand Secretary within the Court hierarchy.

## 著名任职者：荣禄

One of the most notable holders of the office was Yung Lu (荣禄), who served as Grand Secretary during the late Qing period. The narrative mentions "the widow of the Grand Secretary Yung Lu, who lost, in one year, her husband, her son, and her daughter-in-law, the Princess Schun." (026-002) Yung Lu was a powerful official and close ally of the Empress Dowager, playing a significant role in the politics of the late Qing court.

## 礼制与馈赠

The Grand Secretary's elevated status extended to the elaborate system of [[present-giving|gift exchange]] at Court. The presents the Grand Secretary and the Prime Minister received were determined by rank, reflecting their position at the apex of the official hierarchy. (027-003) The system of Court presents, regulated by the [[book-of-rites|Book of Rites]], was a significant part of official life, and the Grand Secretary's place in this system underscored his preeminence among China's civil servants.

> The Prime Minister's and Grand Secretary's cushions may on no condition be removed, but the other three are subject to the will of the introducing eunuch. If this latter be sufficiently paid, and there is a fixed price for each cushion, he will remove the three of the lower members of the Cabinet.
>
> (016-008)
