## 定义与意义

"Son of Heaven" (天子) is the sacred and formal title of the Emperor of China. The narrator, upon first being presented at Court, was shown a young man standing beside the Empress Dowager's throne and told: "this was the Son of Heaven, the Emperor of China!" (001-007) The title carries profound religious and political significance — the Emperor is regarded as the representative of Heaven on earth, a unique fusion of temporal ruler and spiritual intercessor.

There is a "Sphinx-like quality" about the title "Son of Heaven," which adds to the mystery and remoteness surrounding the Imperial Person. (008-005) The author notes that she was "domiciled in any residence of a Son of Heaven since the time of Marco Polo" — the first Western woman ever to reside within the Imperial Palace precincts. (001-026)

## 天子与自然崇拜

The most solemn act performed by the Emperor in his quality of Son of Heaven is the glorification of the Invisible Deity at the summer and winter solstices. (029-005) As the Son of Heaven, the Emperor alone is worthy to perform the unique ceremonies of Nature worship:

> Here in this symbolic center of the world, in this great Temple, whose walls are Space, whose towers are Infinity, on this great triple altar, canopied with Heaven itself, the Emperor of China, "Son of Heaven," glorifies the Invisible Deity and sacrifices for the prosperity of "the Great, Pure Kingdom" and his people.
>
> (029-004)

This worship of the Invisible Deity has no Priestly Hierarchy — the Emperor of China is its one High Priest. (029-004) He alone offers sacrifices in the [[temple-of-heaven|Temple of Heaven]] and the other great Temples at times prescribed by the [[book-of-rites|Book of Rites]], and on special occasions. (029-008)

## 三重身份：天子、大祭司、赎罪者

The Son of Heaven holds a triple role, unique among the world's sovereigns. As Emperor, he is the temporal ruler of the Great Empire, accompanied at the great semiannual celebration by all his ministers and the highest nobles, surrounded by splendid pomp and Imperial pageantry. (029-009) As High Priest, he alone is worthy to sacrifice on the great altars to the Invisible Deity. (029-008)

Most remarkably, as Expiator, the Son of Heaven publicly holds himself responsible for the misfortunes of the Empire. When famine devastates the land, when drought or any other National Calamity visits the Empire, the Emperor prays in these Temples for its cessation. According to the Book of Rites, he says in time of trouble: "I will purify myself by sacrifice that these calamities may be lifted from the Empire and the people. I alone am responsible." (029-008)

The Emperor prepares himself for the great semiannual ceremony by a rigorous fast of three days, spending the final night in a vigil in the Great Park of the Nature Temples, where a Purifying Palace is set aside for his use. (029-005)

## 天子之神圣性与宫廷礼仪

The sacredness attaching to the Son of Heaven belongs to him as ruler, not as an individual. In the [[great-audience-hall|Audience Hall]], when administering justice, he is approached and addressed only upon bended knee. Any official communication made to him, in private or elsewhere, is made kneeling. However, when addressed on ordinary affairs in private, the Son of Heaven may be spoken to almost familiarly, with the courtier standing while speaking. If in the midst of a familiar conversation an order is given, the attendant immediately drops upon his knees to receive it. (016-015)

Even the physical person of the Son of Heaven is subject to strict conventions. It is against the laws of [[propriety]] for any man under forty, even if he be the Son of Heaven, to wear a mustache or whiskers. (008-004) On the Emperor's birthday, officials offered "homage and congratulations to the Son of Heaven on the happy occasion of his completing another year of his life," extolling him as "the Son of Heaven and representative on earth of the great Invisible Deity." (009-008)(009-027)
