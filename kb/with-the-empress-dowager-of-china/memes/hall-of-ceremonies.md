# Great Hall of Ceremonies

The Great Hall of Ceremonies was the primary ceremonial space of the Qing imperial court in Peking, used for the most formal state occasions. Katherine Carl observed it during the Emperor's Birthday celebrations, where she witnessed the elaborate protocol of prostrations, jade *ruyie* presentations, and the rigid spatial hierarchy that governed access to the imperial presence.

## Location and Context

The Hall of Ceremonies was located within the imperial palace enclosure in Peking. It was a space distinct from the Empress Dowager's private throne-room, used only for grand state ceremonies. The hall's position, with outer courts for officials of lower rank, reflected the graded access system of Qing court protocol.

The Great Audience Hall was described as "a detached building apart and quite distinct from all the other buildings of the Palace enclosure," with an inscription over its great doors reading "Hall where Industry is to be applied to State Affairs" (016-003). It was situated nearest the outside walls and entrances so that privileged officials could reach it while passing only through the outer courts, keeping them well away from the private imperial apartments (016-003).

## Description

The hall was designed to accommodate the strict hierarchy of Qing court ceremony. Its interior centered on the Dynastic Throne, where the Emperor sat "erect and stiff as an archaic figure; no longer the shy boy, but the Monarch clothed in all his power" (009-008). The throne room was attended by a gorgeously attired Master of Ceremonies who "stood in the rigid attitude prescribed for this ceremony" (009-008).

The spatial arrangement separated participants by rank. High-ranking Princes and Nobles were permitted to enter the great hall itself, while officials whose rank was insufficient "kneel and make the prostrations in the outer courts" (009-008). This graded access—from the outer courts to the hall interior to the space before the throne—visually and spatially enacted the hierarchy of the Qing state.

## Role in Court Life

Carl's most detailed observation of the Hall of Ceremonies in use occurred during the Emperor's Birthday celebrations (Chapter IX). The ceremonial procession advanced with the clash of cymbals and the playing of the "Imperial Hymn," the only music she ever heard in China that approached a national air (009-008).

The ceremony followed the prescriptions of the Book of Rites. Each Prince and Noble knelt and performed the prescribed prostrations, then presented the Emperor with a jade *ruyie* (an emblem of good luck, often mistaken by foreigners for a scepter) (009-008). The Emperor held each *ruyie* briefly, bowed profoundly, and handed it to an attendant eunuch.

Significantly, the Empress Dowager did not attend this ceremony in her own person. Instead, she observed from a distance—"the Empress Dowager, who was always the most gorgeously attired person at Court, was, on His Majesty's Birthday, dressed with an extreme simplicity that amounted almost to plainness, and she wore no jewels" (009-012). She deliberately chose understated attire so that the young Emperor and Empress would be the central figures of the day's events (009-012).

After the formal congratulations, the imperial party processed with equal ceremony to the Theater, passing again through the Hall of Ceremonies (009-012).

Carl described the Empress Dowager's preference for a lighter throne over the traditional one:

> Except in the Great Audience Hall, where she uses the traditional throne of state of the Dynasty, she prefers a much lighter and quite modern one, which she has introduced into the Palaces. The thrones favored by Her Majesty are of open carved teakwood, circular in form, with cushions of Imperial yellow.
>
> (004-003)

## In the Narrative

The Hall of Ceremonies appears in Carl's account of the Emperor's Birthday (Chapter IX), one of the most detailed descriptions of Qing court ritual in her memoir. Her observation captures the tension between the Emperor's dual nature—the "shy boy" who conversed awkwardly with her and the "Monarch clothed in all his power" seated on the Dynastic Throne.

Carl also noted the Empress Dowager's careful self-effacement on the Emperor's day, a calculated piece of political theater that emphasized both Cixi's continuing authority (she controlled the narrative) and her ostensible deference to her nephew, the reigning Emperor.

## Historical Significance

The rituals performed in the Great Hall of Ceremonies represented the formal structure of the Qing state at its most traditional. Every gesture—the prostrations, the *ruyie* presentations, the graded access—was codified in the Book of Rites, connecting the dynasty to two millennia of Confucian statecraft.

By 1903–1904, however, these ceremonies took on an increasingly anachronistic quality. The Qing state was struggling to reform itself, creating new ministries (like the Wai-Wu-Pu) and experimenting with constitutional government, while the court continued to perform rites that would have been recognizable to the Kangxi Emperor two centuries earlier. The Hall of Ceremonies thus stood as both the symbolic heart of imperial authority and a monument to the tension between tradition and modernization that defined the dynasty's final years.
