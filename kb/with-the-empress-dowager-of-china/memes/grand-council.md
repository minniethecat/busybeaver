# Grand Council

The Grand Council (Junjichu) was the highest policy-making body
of the Qing Empire, serving as the emperor's privy council
and the effective executive of the imperial government.
During [[katherine-carl|Katherine Carl]]'s residence at the Chinese Court
in 1903–1904, the Council was headed by [[prince-ching|Prince Ching]],
and its deliberations shaped the diplomatic environment
within which the portrait commission proceeded.

## Overview

The Grand Council originated in 1733 as an informal advisory body
to the Yongzheng Emperor but quickly evolved into the empire's supreme decision-making organ.
Its members — typically five to seven high-ranking Manchu and Chinese officials —
advised the throne on all matters of state, from foreign policy to military affairs.

Carl observed that the Council met frequently:

> Every day Their Majesties hold Audience and see the Prime Minister
> and Grand Secretary, and there are frequent meetings of the Grand
> Council. The Prime Minister, Prince Ching, has the last Audience of
> the day, and business reported on during the other Audiences is then
> discussed. (016-006)

The [[empress-dowager|Empress Dowager]] Cixi, though formally outside the Council,
was its de facto master. Carl notes that "at the meeting of the Grand Council,
Her Majesty is always present" (008-009) — a detail that reveals
how thoroughly the Empress Dowager controlled the levers of imperial power.

## Role in the Late Qing

By the early 1900s, the Grand Council had become the nervous system
of the Qing state. It coordinated the work of the six traditional
ministries, managed relations with foreign powers,
and oversaw the empire's finances.

After the [[boxer-rebellion|Boxer Rebellion]], the Council became the primary vehicle
for the reform program known as the "New Policies" (Xinzheng),
which sought to modernize China's military,
education system, and government.

The Council's deliberations were conducted in Manchu and Chinese,
with members kneeling on cushions before the throne:

> In front of the Throne dais, during the hours of Audience, there are
> five cushions placed on the floor for the members of the Grand Council
> to kneel upon when they are memorializing Their Majesties. (016-008)

This physical arrangement — officials on their knees before the sovereign —
symbolized the Council's constitutional position
as an instrument of imperial will rather than an independent body.

## In Carl's Account

Carl's encounters with the Grand Council were indirect but revealing.
She observed its members arriving for audiences at the Palace,
their appearances marking the daily rhythm of imperial governance.

She also recorded that "the Grand Council and the Princes of the Imperial family
took an intelligent interest in her work" (030-007) —
a reference to the portrait commission that suggests the Council
was aware of and approved the diplomatic project
that had brought a Western artist into the imperial precincts.

The Council's involvement in the portrait project was not merely ceremonial.
As the body responsible for foreign relations, it would have reviewed
the proposal for the Empress Dowager's portrait to be exhibited
at the [[st-louis-exposition|St. Louis Exposition]], weighing its diplomatic risks and benefits.

## Historical Trajectory

The Grand Council was abolished in 1911 as part of the Qing court's
last-ditch constitutional reforms, replaced by a modern cabinet system.
The abolition came too late to save the dynasty, which fell later that year.

The Council's legacy is complex. To its critics, it embodied
the concentration of unaccountable power in the hands of a few Manchu aristocrats.
To its defenders, it provided the institutional continuity
that enabled the Qing to govern a vast multi-ethnic empire for nearly two centuries.
