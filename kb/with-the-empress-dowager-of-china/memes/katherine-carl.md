# Katharine A. Carl

Katharine Augusta Carl (1865–1938) was an American portrait painter who spent
nine months at the Qing Imperial Court from August 1903 to May 1904, painting
the [[empress-dowager|empress dowager]] Cixi's portrait for the [[st-louis-exposition|st. louis exposition]].
She was the first Western artist — and one of the few Westerners of any profession —
ever permitted to reside within the Imperial Palace precincts and observe the
daily life of the Qing Court at close quarters.

Her memoir, *With the Empress Dowager of China* (1905), is the sole first-hand
Western account of life inside the [[summer-palace|summer palace]] and the [[sea-palace|sea palace]]
during the dynasty's final decade. Through Carl's eyes, Western readers gained
an unprecedented glimpse of Cixi's personality, the Manchu court ladies, and the
elaborate ceremonial life of imperial China.

## The Commission

### An Unexpected Invitation

In April 1903, Carl was visiting Shanghai when she received a letter from
[[mrs-conger|mrs. conger]], wife of the United States Minister to Peking:

> In April, 1903, while I was visiting in Shanghai, I received a letter from
> Mrs. Conger, wife of the Minister of the United States to Peking, in which
> she said there was a question of Her Majesty the Empress Dowager's having
> her portrait painted, and asking me if such a thing should be arranged would
> I be willing to come to Peking and undertake it. [P04-001]

Mrs. Conger hoped the portrait might later be sent to the [[st-louis-exposition|st. louis exposition]],
believing that "if the world could see a true likeness of her, it might modify
the generally accepted idea which prevailed as to Her Majesty's character."

Carl accepted immediately. But Chinese bureaucracy moved slowly:

> The "mills of" Chinese Officialdom "grind slowly," and not until July did
> Mrs. Conger receive an official notification from the Wai-Wu-Pu (Chinese
> Foreign Office) requesting "Her Excellency Mrs. Conger to present the
> American artist, Miss Carl, to Her Imperial Majesty on the fifth day of
> August, for the purpose of painting a portrait of Her Majesty." [P04-001]

### Arrival in Peking

Carl left Shanghai on July 29, 1903, and was "cordially received" by
[[conger|Conger]] and Mrs. Conger at the [[american-legation|american legation]].
On August 5 — a date chosen after "much deliberation and many consultations
of the almanac" as the most auspicious — she was presented to the [[empress-dowager|empress dowager]]
at the [[summer-palace|summer palace]] in private audience with Mrs. Conger.

## Life at the Imperial Court

### The "Ker-Gunia Fu"

The [[empress-dowager|empress dowager]] assigned Carl the [[palace-of-the-emperors-father|Palace of the Emperor's Father]]
as her residence — a splendid demesne that had belonged to Prince Ch'un,
the seventh Prince, and the childhood home of [[emperor-kwang-hsu|emperor kwang-hsu]] after
his selection as heir to the throne:

> The Palace of the Emperor's Father, which the Empress Dowager had set aside
> for me to live in while I was at work on her portrait, was a splendid demesne,
> with a noble park and spacious buildings. It had been much injured by the
> foreign troops in 1900 and had been unoccupied since, until Her Majesty decided
> it would be a suitable dwelling-place for her "Portrait Painter." (010-002)

Carl selected a charming group of pavilions in a walled-in garden fronting on
a lotus-covered lake. This residence soon came to be known as the "Ker-Gunia Fu" —
"Ker-Gunia" being "Miss Carl" rendered into Chinese, and "Fu" meaning "Palace" —
for, as Carl noted, "the Chinese are very fond of nicknames."

> I learned later that these pavilions had been the dwelling-place of the
> Seventh Prince's son, the present Emperor Kwang-Hsu, after he had been
> chosen as Heir to the Throne and until he went to live regularly at the
> Imperial Palace. (010-002)

### Daily Routine

Carl's position was historically unprecedented. A "great innovation in Chinese
customs and a breaking away from long-established tradition for an Imperial
portrait to be painted" [P04-001], her presence at Court required the creation
of new protocols. She was neither a courtier (bound by Chinese etiquette) nor
a mere visitor (she resided for nine months). This ambiguous status gave her
unique freedoms and perspectives.

She accompanied the [[empress-dowager|empress dowager]] on daily walks through the Palace gardens,
attended theatrical performances, participated in ceremonial occasions including
the [[emperor-birthday|Emperor-Birthday]], and dined with the Court. Her memoir
records conversations with Cixi on subjects ranging from art and flowers to
politics and the [[boxer-rebellion|boxer rebellion]].

### The Sittings

Carl painted four portraits of the Empress Dowager during her residency.
Of the portrait sessions, she wrote:

> Every portrait painter knows the sort of intimacy that establishes itself
> between him and his sitter, however unsympathetic the latter may be at first
> sight, which was certainly not the case in this instance. The effort of the
> painter to get under the exterior and discover the real person of his sitter;
> the desire to see the best side and make the most of it, meets generally with
> a sympathetic response. (020-001)

Through these sittings, Carl developed a relationship with Cixi that she believed
was unique — "Though I saw Her Majesty so intimately at other times, I felt I was
not seeing her 'face to face' (figuratively speaking), except at the sittings."

### The Palace Painters

Carl discovered she was not the only painter at Court:

> Her Majesty has a corps of painters always there. These painters decorate
> the thousands of lanterns used in the Palace ceremonies and processions.
> They paint the scenery for the spectacular plays at the Theater, and the
> flowers used for the decorations. Some are very clever flower painters, and
> one even paints portraits, but they have never seen the Empress Dowager
> except from afar! (020-003)

Though Mandarins of the Third Rank, these Chinese court painters were obliged
to withdraw from any court the Empress Dowager entered. Carl found it "amusing
to see these dignified, handsomely gowned officials being hurried out of the
court on Her Majesty's approach by the eunuchs who precede her." Their paintings
were submitted to Her Majesty through eunuch intermediaries — they never received
direct instruction.

### "Fearless" — The Peacock Feather

One day in the gardens, the [[princess-imperial|young Empress]] playfully
decorated Carl with an arborvitae branch shaped like a peacock feather —
the decoration awarded to China's highest officials:

> When she had placed it, she told me to rise, and called me "Your Excellency
> Carl," which is the title of those who possess the decoration of the peacock
> feather. (020-005)

When the [[empress-dowager|empress dowager]] noticed the improvised decoration, she smiled and said
it was her prerogative to award the peacock feather, but added: "If you were a man
you would win it, and probably a yellow jacket also, for you are fearless."

Carl wondered: "Why did she think me fearless? Could she have heard that the
foreigners in Peking seemed to think it was almost as much as taking my life
in my own hands to go and live entirely alone among the Chinese at Court,
after the Boxer trouble?"

## Why She Wrote the Memoir

Carl's decision to publish her account was driven by the proliferation of false
statements attributed to her after her return to America:

> After I returned to America, I was constantly seeing in newspapers (and
> hearing of) statements ascribed to me which I never made. Her Majesty was
> represented as having stood over me in threatening attitudes, forcing me
> to represent her as a young and beautiful woman! (001-003)

The London *Times* reported that Carl had said the Empress Dowager had "the soul
of a tiger in the body of a woman" — a statement Carl never made. She found that
"if a correction be made, it never seems to get the same circulation or publicity
as the first statement."

Carl decided that "in justice to my August Patroness as well as to my humbler
self, it is incumbent upon me to correct them, and it seems to me the only proper
way to do so is to write a full and true relation of my life at the Palace."

## Literary Legacy

*With the Empress Dowager of China* (1905) is dedicated to [[sir-robert-hart|Sir Robert Hart]],
Inspector General of Chinese Customs, "to whose helpful encouragement I owe so much."
The memoir covers Carl's experiences from her arrival in August 1903 through her
departure in May 1904. It includes:

- Detailed descriptions of the [[summer-palace|summer palace]], [[sea-palace|sea palace]], and [[winter-palace|winter palace]]
- Accounts of major ceremonies: the [[emperor-birthday|Emperor-Birthday]],
  [[empress-dowager-birthday|Empress-Dowager-Birthday]], [[chinese-new-year-court|Chinese-New-Year-Court]], and [[first-audience|First Audience]]
- Observations of [[manchu-customs|Manchu Customs]], [[court-ceremony|Court Ceremony]], and daily life
- A portrait of the [[empress-dowager|empress dowager]] that emphasizes her charm, intelligence,
  and artistic sensibility
- Descriptions of the [[boxer-rebellion|boxer rebellion]]'s aftermath and foreign relations

Carl's account is remarkable for its sympathy toward its subject — at a time when
most Western writing about Cixi was hostile — while remaining grounded in specific,
observable detail. Her artist's eye for visual description makes the memoir an
invaluable record of the material culture of the late Qing Court.

## Later Life

Carl returned to America in 1904 and published her memoir the following year.
She exhibited the portraits of the Empress Dowager and continued to paint,
though none of her later work achieved the historical significance of her
Chinese commission. She died in 1938.
