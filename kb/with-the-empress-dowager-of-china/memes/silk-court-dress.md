# Silk Court Dress

The Qing Imperial Court was renowned for its magnificent silk costumes, which Katherine Carl described in detail during her portrait-painting residency at the Summer Palace.

## Description

Court dress varied by season and occasion. The summer costume, worn at the Emperor's Birthday, was made of "stiff transparent silk" (009-006). For the married Court Ladies, this was elaborately embroidered. The Empress Dowager's portrait gown was of "Imperial yellow, brocaded in the wistaria vine in realistic colors and richly embroidered in pearls" (001-011).

Carl described the Empress Dowager's portrait gown in lavish detail:

> She had been clothed in a gown of Imperial yellow, brocaded in the wistaria vine in realistic colors and richly embroidered in pearls. It was made, in the graceful Manchu fashion, in one piece, reaching from the neck to the floor; fastened from the right shoulder to the hem with jade buttons. The stuff of the gown was of a stiff, transparent silk, and was worn over a softer under-gown of the same color and length.
>
> (001-011)

The Court Ladies' formal attire included distinctive Manchu elements — "the long red silk tassels flowing down the back from the silver butterfly pins on the side of the head" (009-006). Their everyday dress was equally prescribed, with rank indicated through fabric quality, color, and ornamentation (009-004).

## Cultural Significance

Silk (丝, *sī*) had been a Chinese monopoly for millennia, and its use at Court reinforced Imperial prestige. The color yellow was reserved for the Emperor and Empress Dowager (015-020), while other ranks had designated colors. The quality and pattern of silk brocade signaled the wearer's exact position in the Court hierarchy (009-004).

## In Carl's Account

Carl, as a portrait painter, paid close attention to fabric and costume. She was struck by the daily changes of the Empress Dowager's attire, noting that Her Majesty never appeared in the same gown twice during Carl's entire residency. She described the "magnificent" effect of the Court Ladies assembled in their silk costumes for the Emperor's Birthday ceremony (009-006).
