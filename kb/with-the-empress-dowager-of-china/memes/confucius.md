## 在中国朝廷与哲学中的地位

Confucius is revered in China not as a religious divinity but as an ethical teacher and philosopher. Though many temples have been erected to his memory, they function as Halls of Science rather than temples to a divinity — there are no images of Confucius or the Sages in these temples. (013-005) His philosophy, rather than being a religion, is a system of ethics that has directed the lives and laws of the Chinese people for nearly twenty-five hundred years. (013-005)(029-011)

The Chinese people are described in the narrative as "really Confucians" — whether they be Buddhists or Taoists, they are all followers of Confucius and live by the rules the Great Sage laid down for them. (029-011) They "get all their moral support from the writings of Confucius and all their ideals from communion with Nature." (029-011)

## 宫廷中的祭孔仪式

Semiannual sacrifices to the memory of Confucius were celebrated with great solemnity at the Summer Palace. The ceremony took place in a chapel at the foot of the hill crowned with the [[temple-of-the-ten-thousand-buddhas|Temple of the Ten Thousand Buddhas]]. (013-005) These sacrifices commemorated Confucius as "an ethical teacher, a wise philosopher, a Sage." (013-005)

The service included an address to the memory of the great Sage, accompanied by music and hymns — rhythmic verses containing truths inculcated by the Sage, called the "Odes to Peace," all of uniform length and in praise of Confucius. (013-006) An altar with a dragon table stood in front for [[present-giving|offerings]], rich with splendid vases, rare old bronze bowls, incense-burners, and sweet with flowers and fruit. Offerings of millet, meat, and wine were placed on the dragon table. (013-005)

Their Majesties the [[empress-dowager|Empress Dowager]] and the [[emperor-kwang-hsu|Emperor Kwang-Hsu]], together with the young Empress and Court Ladies in full Court dress, proceeded in ceremonious procession to the temple, their approach accompanied by the slow beating of drums. (013-006) At the conclusion of the ceremony, the scroll bearing the address was burned — the ashes of a literary essay being considered a most fitting offering to the memory of Confucius, the great philosopher. (013-008)

## 儒学教义与宫廷生活

Confucian precepts permeated every aspect of Court life. The [[empress-dowager|Empress Dowager]] herself was known to quote Confucius directly when enforcing Court discipline. On one occasion, when a child of a high-ranking family refused to observe Court etiquette, Her Majesty declared:

> "It is by the rules of propriety that the character is established" (Confucius).
>
> (022-016)

The [[emperor-kwang-hsu|Emperor of China]], as the Son of Heaven, is the High Priest of Heaven, the High Priest of Buddhism and Taoism, and is also a Confucian — though Confucianism is regarded as a philosophy rather than a religion. (013-004) The [[manchu-customs|Manchu]] rulers, though originally a "wild and warlike race," came to live "by the laws of Confucius" and are now "as quiet and peaceful a race as the Chinese themselves." (029-015)

Confucius' rules extend beyond ritual into practical governance. The Empress Dowager herself, in reviewing a proposed new scheme of taxation, applied the Confucian principle of protecting the common people, repeating several times: "I fear it may harass the people; we cannot harass the people; they have enough burdens to bear." (031-016)

## 经典与著作

The teachings of Confucius are preserved in the "[[analects|Analects]]," from which quotations are painted on scrolls, cut into wood, and carved out of stone to adorn the walls of his temples — not only the interiors but also the courts and verandahs. (013-005) The Empress Dowager, a great reader of the classics, could repeat pages from Confucius and other favorite authors from memory. (015-004)

In the Confucian temples, at the place where an altar would stand in a religious temple, there is a plain niche painted in red with a tablet bearing the inscription in gold: "The Seat of the Perfect One." On either side are similar niches containing the tablets of four other great Sages, among whom was [[mencius|Mencius]]. (013-005)
