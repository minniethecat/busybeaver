# Invisible Deity

The Invisible Deity represented the highest form of religious conception in late Imperial China — the worship of Nature in its purest, most abstract form. Katherine Carl observed this worship during her time at the Court, describing it as "the purest form of religion in China" (029-002).

## Nature Worship

Carl identified "three great religions in China — Buddhism, Taoism, and the worship of Nature." Of these, she considered the worship of Nature, "in which is embodied their highest idea of an Invisible Deity," to be the purest (029-002). Its temples were situated in a magnificent park in the [[chinese-city|Chinese City]] of [[peking|Peking]], with the [[temple-of-heaven|Temple of Heaven]] as the most imposing structure.

Carl described the Temple of the Invisible Deity in almost mystical terms: "Its foundations are the Earth, its walls are limitless Ether, its dome Heaven's own vault! On its great open altar this Nature worship has its culmination and reaches its highest fulfilment" (029-002).

## The Altar of Heaven

The great altar was "built in the center of a great marble-paved space... of pure white marble, round as is the Earth" (029-003). Each of its three superimposed circular platforms — symbolizing "the Trinity in Nature and its Infinity" — was surrounded by exquisitely carved balustrades and approached by flights of nine steps. The central point of the uppermost circle "represents the center of the Universe, accessible from every point of the compass" (029-003).

## The Emperor as High Priest

The worship of the Invisible Deity had no priestly hierarchy apart from the Emperor himself: "The Emperor of China is its one High Priest. He alone is worthy, as the Son of Heaven, to perform its unique ceremonies, on its one great Altar, in its single great Temple of China" (029-004).

Carl described the semiannual rituals: "Here in this symbolic center of the world, in this great Temple, whose walls are Space, whose towers are Infinity... the Emperor of China, 'Son of Heaven,' glorifies the Invisible Deity and sacrifices for the prosperity of 'the Great, Pure Kingdom' and his people" (029-004).

## The Semiannual Sacrifice

The Emperor prepared for the great ceremony "by a rigorous fast of three days, spending the final night before the celebration in a vigil in the Great Park of the Nature Temples" (029-005). This glorification of the Invisible Deity at the summer and winter solstices was "the most solemn act performed by the Emperor in his quality of Son of Heaven" (029-005).

Beyond the ritual aspect, the ceremony was also "the most formal of his official acts as ruler of the Great Empire," combining "the religious rite" with "splendid pomp and Imperial pageantry" (029-009).
