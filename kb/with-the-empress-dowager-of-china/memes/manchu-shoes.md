# Manchu Shoes

The Manchu platform shoes were a distinctive element of Manchu women's dress at the Imperial Court, featuring high central soles that elevated the wearer by several inches. Katherine Carl's memoir provides detailed observations of this unique footwear, which she encountered from her very first day at the [[chinese-court|Chinese Court]].

## Design and Construction

Manchu shoes were constructed with a high wooden platform sole positioned at the center of the foot, creating a stilt-like effect. Unlike the bound feet of Han Chinese women, which rendered walking difficult, the Manchu platform shoe allowed for relative mobility while achieving a similar effect of stature and a characteristic swaying gait. The shoes were typically decorated with embroidery, reflecting the wearer's rank and the occasion.

## In Carl's Account

Carl first encounters the shoes on her initial meeting with the [[empress-dowager|Empress Dowager]]: "Although not more than five feet tall, as she wears the Manchu shoes with six-inch-high, stilt-like soles, to avoid throwing the knees up higher than the lap she must sit upon cushions" (001-007). This observation captures both the physical effect of the shoes and the adjustments they required in posture and seating.

The shoes were part of the complete Manchu court costume that distinguished the ruling ethnic group from the Han Chinese majority. Carl notes that the [[lady-of-the-court|Ladies of the Court]] all wore these distinctive shoes, along with the elaborate [[manchu-coiffure|Manchu headdress]] and the formal [[silk-court-dress|silk court robes]]. Together, these elements created the imposing appearance that Carl describes throughout her memoir.

The Empress Dowager's short stature—compensated for by the platform shoes—is a recurring theme in Carl's physical descriptions. The shoes contributed to what Carl calls her "queenly bearing," transforming a woman of modest height into an imposing Imperial presence.

## Cultural Significance

The Manchu platform shoe was a marker of ethnic identity. Unlike Han Chinese women, Manchu women did not bind their feet, and the platform shoe provided an alternative form of elevated footwear that preserved the natural foot while achieving the desired effect of altered stature and gait. The retention of this distinctive footwear was part of the broader Manchu effort to maintain cultural distinctiveness even as the Court adopted many Han Chinese customs.

> Although not more than five feet tall, as she wears the Manchu shoes with six-inch-high, stilt-like soles, to avoid throwing the knees up higher than the lap she must sit upon cushions, and when she is seated she looks a much larger woman than when standing.
>
> (001-014)
