# British Legation

The British Legation was the diplomatic compound of the United Kingdom in [[peking|Peking]], located within the Legation Quarter of the [[tartar-city|Tartar City]]. It played a critical role during the Boxer Rebellion of 1900 as the central refuge where all foreigners gathered for protection during the siege.

## Location and Context

The British Legation was situated in the Legation Quarter, near the canal and within proximity to the [[american-legation|United States Legation]] and the [[water-gate|Water Gate]]. Katherine Carl passed the "splendid enclosure of the English Legation" on her daily journey from the American Legation to the Palace (025-003).

## Description

The British Legation compound was described by Carl as having a "splendid enclosure," suggesting substantial grounds and impressive architecture befitting the premier foreign power in Peking at the time (025-003).

## Role in Court Life

The British Legation served as the diplomatic home of the British Minister to China. During the Boxer Rebellion, it became the central stronghold of the foreign community:

> …especially that of the British Legation, where all the foreigners
> finally congregated—open to attack on every side, lying under the
> very walls of the Palace and the Imperial City… (030-021)

The siege of the Legations in 1900 was a defining moment in Sino-foreign relations. The survival of the foreign community within the British Legation compound, despite weeks of bombardment and attack, became a symbol of Western resilience and Chinese vulnerability.

## In the Narrative

The British Legation appears in Carl's account primarily as a geographical landmark along her route to the Palace and in the context of the Boxer Rebellion's aftermath. By 1903–1904, when Carl resided in Peking, the compound had been restored and diplomatic life had returned to normal (010-008).

## Historical Significance

The British Legation was the epicenter of the 55-day siege of the International Legations during the Boxer Rebellion (June–August 1900). The compound housed approximately 900 foreigners and 3,000 Chinese Christians who sought refuge from the Boxers and Imperial troops. Its survival until the arrival of the Allied relief force was a pivotal event that shaped the subsequent peace settlement and the imposition of the Boxer Protocol.
