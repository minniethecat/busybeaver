# Princess Imperial

The adopted daughter of the Empress Dowager Cixi, the Princess Imperial held the highest rank among the women of the Court and presided over its ceremonial life during the period of Katherine Carl's memoir (1903–1904).

## Role at the Chinese Court

The Princess Imperial was the First Lady of the Court, a formal designation that placed her at the head of the Court's female hierarchy (004-017) (005-008). When the Empress Dowager received guests, the Princess Imperial attended as "the first of the Ladies of the Court" (001-021). She was "the first of the Princesses at Court," leading the other princesses and ladies-in-waiting in all ceremonial observances (005-013).

## Biographical Sketch

The Princess Imperial was the Empress Dowager Cixi's adopted daughter, a relationship that gave her precedence over even the princesses of the imperial blood (005-013). Unlike the other princesses who attended Court only on prescribed festive occasions, the Princess Imperial maintained a constant presence in the Empress Dowager's entourage (005-013). Her status as an adopted daughter of the sovereign, rather than as a daughter by birth, illustrates the Empress Dowager's practice of binding key Court figures through constructed kinship ties.

## Interaction with the Author

Katherine Carl encountered the Princess Imperial during her earliest days at the Chinese Court. At Carl's first formal audience, the Princess Imperial was among the princesses in attendance as the Empress Dowager received her foreign guest (001-021). Later, when tea was served after the ceremony, the Princess Imperial was seated with the young Empress (019-009). Throughout Carl's nine-month residence, the Princess Imperial was a fixture of Court routine — present at audiences, joining garden processions, and sitting among the princesses during the Empress Dowager's informal afternoon gatherings (001-024) (004-009).

## Character and Appearance

In the memoir's account, the Princess Imperial appears as a figure of formal authority. She led the line of princesses as they descended the steps of the Audience Hall, a visual emblem of her precedence (019-006). Like the other Manchu princesses, she wore the elaborate Court coiffure and brightly colored silk robes, but the deference shown to her — the way other princesses yielded precedence and the Empress Dowager treated her as first among them — marked her higher rank with unmistakable clarity.

## Historical Significance

The role of Princess Imperial offers a window into the internal structure of the Qing Court's female world. This parallel hierarchy — with its own ranks, precedence, and ceremonial functions — operated alongside the male bureaucracy but was almost entirely invisible to foreign observers before the Boxer Rebellion [P04-006] [P04-009]. The Princess Imperial's position as First Lady made her the public face of this inner Court at all formal occasions, and her status as the Empress Dowager's adopted daughter demonstrates how kinship — whether biological or constructed — served as the organizing principle of Court life.

## In the Narrative

The Princess Imperial appears in Carl's memoir primarily in scenes of collective Court activity. She is most prominent in the early chapters that describe the audience ceremony and the daily routines of the palace women (001-021) (019-006). Her presence in the narrative is less that of an individual personality than of an institutional figure — she embodies the rank and ceremony of the Court hierarchy. Carl's account treats her not as a character with a personal story arc but as the living symbol of the order that governed every woman's place in the Empress Dowager's household.
