# Brocade

The rich figured fabric woven with raised patterns, used in Imperial Qing costume as described in Carl's detailed observations of court dress.

## Description

Brocade was a prominent textile at the Qing Imperial Court. The Empress Dowager herself appeared "in a gown of Imperial yellow, brocaded in the wistaria vine in realistic colors" (001-011). Court ladies wore festive red gowns "with brocaded figures, representing the character for longevity and happiness" (009-002), demonstrating the fabric's integration of decorative artistry with symbolic meaning.

## Cultural Significance

Brocade served both ornamental and symbolic functions in Qing Court life. The Empress Dowager's throne was framed by "carved wooden screens, with rich brocaded silk of brilliant green" (010-016), while her portrait for the St. Louis Exposition was "covered with a screen of brocaded satin of Imperial hue" (034-013). These brocaded textiles were markers of rank and occasion, their colors and patterns governed by sumptuary conventions of the Qing Court.

## In Carl's Account

Carl, as a foreign artist granted unprecedented access to the Inner Court, documented these textiles with an outsider's eye for detail. Her observations provide one of the few first-hand Western accounts of the material culture of the Forbidden City and Summer Palace, where brocaded silks adorned everything from Imperial gowns (001-011) to ceremonial screens (010-016) (034-013).

## Original Account

> Luckily, I had but a few moments to consider all these adverse circumstances, for Her Majesty soon returned! She had been clothed in a gown of Imperial yellow, brocaded in the wistaria vine in realistic colors and richly embroidered in pearls. It was made, in the graceful Manchu fashion, in one piece, reaching from the neck to the floor; fastened from the right shoulder to the hem with jade buttons.
>
> (001-011)
