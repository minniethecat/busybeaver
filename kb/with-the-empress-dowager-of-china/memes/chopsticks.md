# Chopsticks

Chopsticks (*kuaizi*) were the universal eating utensil of the Chinese court, a pair of tapered sticks wielded with dexterity to convey food from dish to mouth. At the Empress Dowager's table, they were objects of both daily necessity and refined craftsmanship.

## Description

Chinese chopsticks were typically made of wood, bamboo, or ivory, and at court were often lacquered or inlaid. At the Empress Dowager's dining table, each place was set with "a small saucer, and a pair of chopsticks" alongside a bowl and a folded napkin (002-009) (022-011). The arrangement was uniform, reflecting a standardised protocol for imperial dining.

Carl described her own attempt at mastering the implement: she "was learning to use the chopsticks" and "thought them such graceful" tools (011-009). Like many Western visitors, she found the technique challenging — she described the Empress Dowager watching with amusement as her foreign guest tried "trying to use the chopsticks, though knives and forks were" also provided (001-022). The provision of Western cutlery alongside chopsticks was a mark of the court's cosmopolitan hospitality.


> Each person has a bowl, a small saucer, and a pair of chopsticks. A small square of very soft and very fine napkin is folded and placed beside these, but is rarely used, the Manchus not being troubled with the gout. At the Palace the food is served in tall dishes of painted Chinese porcelain, and everything is placed upon the table at once — soups, roast, sweets, all except the rice and macaroni.
>
> (002-009)
The Empress Dowager's personal chopsticks were of the highest quality. Carl noted that "Her Majesty dines alone, her chopsticks and spoons, as well as the co"ntainers, were of exceptional materials (008-016). Special embroidered cases were made "for fans, chopsticks and knives, and many other or"naments, indicating that even utilitarian objects received decorative treatment at court (026-013).

## Cultural Significance

Chopsticks carried cultural meaning beyond their practical function. Their use demanded a specific etiquette — food was to be lifted delicately, never speared, and the sticks were never to be left standing upright in rice, an act associated with funeral rites. Mastery of chopsticks marked one as civilised; clumsy use marked the foreigner.

The implements also embodied Confucian values. Unlike knives, which suggested violence and the cutting of flesh, chopsticks reflected the Chinese preference for food prepared in the kitchen — cut into bite-sized pieces before reaching the table. The diner's role was to select and appreciate, not to dissect.

## In Carl's Account

Carl's encounters with chopsticks capture the cross-cultural comedy of her position at court. As a Western artist attempting to navigate Chinese dining customs, she found the implements both beautiful and baffling. The Empress Dowager, far from being offended by her guest's clumsiness, seems to have found it entertaining.

Carl's observation that knives and forks were provided alongside chopsticks (001-022) reveals the court's pragmatic approach to foreign guests. Protocol was not so rigid as to deny visitors the tools they needed. Yet Carl clearly felt the cultural pressure to adapt, practising with chopsticks and admiring their grace (011-009).

The material culture of chopsticks at court — lacquered, embroidered cases, precious materials — reflected the broader aesthetic of the Qing palace, where even the humblest object was elevated by craftsmanship.

## Historical Context

Chopsticks originated in ancient China, with archaeological evidence dating their use to the Shang dynasty (c. 1600–1046 BCE). They spread throughout East Asia, with each culture developing distinctive regional forms — longer and thicker in China, shorter and pointed in Japan, metal in Korea. By the Qing period, they were a well-established feature of Chinese material culture, unchanged in essential design for millennia.

## Related Customs

Chopsticks were integral to [[chinese-cuisine]], the culinary tradition they were designed to serve. Their use was governed by the same code of [[propriety]] that shaped all court behaviour. The contrast between chopsticks and Western cutlery was one of many cultural differences that foreign visitors like Carl had to navigate in the [[manchu-customs]] of the Qing court.
