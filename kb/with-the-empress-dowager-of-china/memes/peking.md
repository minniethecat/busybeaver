# Peking

Peking (now Beijing) was the imperial capital of the Qing dynasty
and the city where [[katherine-carl|katherine carl]] resided during the painting of the
[[empress-dowager|empress dowager]]'s portrait in 1903–1904.
The walled city with its towers and gates formed the backdrop to Carl's memoir,
encompassing the [[american-legation|american legation]] where she stayed,
the [[forbidden-city|Forbidden City]] where the Emperor held court, and the roads leading westward
to the [[summer-palace|summer palace]] and [[sea-palace|sea palace]].

## Location and Context

Peking in the early 1900s was a city of sharp contrasts —
ancient imperial grandeur rubbing against the raw wounds of the Boxer Rebellion.
The foreign Legation Quarter, where Carl resided,
occupied a fortified enclave in the heart of the city,
ringed by walls that had withstood the siege of 1900. (010-008)

From the elevated vantage of the Palace grounds, Carl could see Peking spread below:

> In the distance, beyond a soft gray undulating landscape, with
> fields of brilliant green here and there, lay Peking, with its walls
> and towers, enveloped in a golden haze. (010-003)

The city's dry climate was noted by Carl, who remarked that
"Peking is a dry place, and rain is a rarity,
which probably accounts for this predilection" —
a practical observation from an artist concerned with paint drying times. (011-011)

## Description

The Peking Carl knew was accessed by "two-wheeled vehicles" peculiar to the city —
Chinese carts that bounced along unpaved roads, carrying passengers
between the Legation Quarter and the imperial palaces beyond the city walls. (003-017)

The Legation Quarter, where Carl lived with the Congers,
was a self-contained diplomatic village.
The [[american-legation|american legation]] occupied a prominent position within it,
and the Congers were the first among the Legations
"to issue an invitation to the Chinese Court" after the Boxer crisis. (010-009)

Beyond the diplomatic enclave lay the great monuments of imperial Peking.
Carl visited the "Great Temple of Heaven" ([[temple-of-heaven|Temple of Heaven]]),
whose blue-tiled roofs and marble terraces ranked among the city's
most magnificent sights. (013-004)

## Role in Court Life

Peking was the winter residence of the Court. Each autumn,
"the moving of the Court to Peking" was a major logistical operation,
as the [[empress-dowager|empress dowager]] and her vast household relocated
from the [[summer-palace|summer palace]] to the [[winter-palace|winter palace]] within the city. (010-002)

The annual cycle of court life was anchored to Peking's seasons:
winter audiences in the Forbidden City, spring excursions to the Summer Palace,
and ceremonial visits to the city's temples and altars.
When Carl accompanied the Court on its move from the Sea Palace to Peking,
the journey was brief — a reminder of how compact the imperial geography was,
with palaces and gardens clustered around the capital's periphery. (010-001)

## In the Narrative

Peking appears throughout Carl's memoir as both setting and subject.
The first chapter opens with the journey "out to the Summer Palace from Peking" (001-002) —
a trip of several hours by sedan chair and cart that marked the boundary
between the familiar world of the Legation Quarter and the unknown realm of the imperial court.

The city's layout shaped the rhythms of Carl's life.
When she needed respite from palace demands,
she returned to Peking and the Legation for rest.
When the Empress Dowager inquired after her wellbeing,
she asked about Carl's "trip into Peking" as if the capital
were a distant province rather than a few miles away. (010-011)

## Historical Significance

Peking in 1903–1904 was a city in transition.
The Boxer Protocol of 1901 had imposed foreign garrisons and indemnities,
and the presence of armed foreign soldiers in the capital
was a daily humiliation for the Qing court.
Yet it was also a moment of cautious reopening:
the Empress Dowager's willingness to receive Western visitors
and commission a foreign artist reflected a court
tentatively engaging with the outside world.

The Peking Carl described would soon be swept away by history.
The dynasty fell in 1911, the Legation Quarter was dismantled,
and the city's walls were demolished in the mid-20th century.
Carl's memoir preserves a glimpse of Peking at its last moment
as an imperial capital.
