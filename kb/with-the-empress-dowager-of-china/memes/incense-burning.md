# Incense-Burning

Incense-burning was a pervasive ritual practice at the Qing court, marking sacred space with perfumed smoke during Buddhist ceremonies, imperial processions, and acts of ancestor veneration.

## Description

The primary vessel for court incense was the censer, or incense-burner, of which Carl observed multiple types. In palace temples, a "huge iron incense-burner" stood in the centre of the courtyard, into which offerings were cast during ceremonies (013-006). For processions, smaller "golden incense-burners" were carried by acolytes, "swinging" them to diffuse "azure" smoke through the air (009-029) (010-011).

The Empress Dowager's private apartments also contained an incense-burner. Before "the figure of Buddha stood the incense-burner, with perfumes constantly" replenished, filling her chambers with fragrance (004-003). This domestic use of incense blurred the line between religious devotion and daily comfort — the sacred and the sensory were one.

The Empress Dowager maintained a personal altar within her private chambers:

> In front of the figure of Buddha stood the incense-burner, with perfumes constantly burning.
>
> (004-003)

The ritual use of incense followed prescribed forms. During temple ceremonies, "sacrifices, incense, and music" were combined in a multi-sensory liturgy (013-005). The acolytes' swinging of censers was choreographed, their movements synchronised with the musicians and the approach of the imperial procession.

## Cultural Significance

Incense in Chinese culture served as a medium between the earthly and divine realms. Its rising smoke carried prayers upward; its fragrance purified the ritual space. At the Qing court, incense-burning marked transitions — the approach of the sovereign, the beginning of ceremony, the boundary between profane and sacred time.

The practice also had a social dimension. The quality and quantity of incense denoted the importance of an occasion. The "huge iron" censer in the temple courtyard (013-006) spoke to the scale of state religious observance, while the smaller golden censers of the private apartments (004-003) reflected the Empress Dowager's personal devotion. Incense was a language of magnitude, readable by all who entered its fragrant radius.

## In Carl's Account

Carl was struck by the sensuality of incense at court. She described the "perfumes constantly rising" from the Empress Dowager's private altar (004-003), and the visual drama of acolytes "swinging golden incense-burners" during a procession across the lake (010-011). The "azure" smoke that "gave forth" from these censers (009-029) added a dreamlike quality to ceremonies that already seemed, to Carl's Western eyes, almost theatrical.

She also noted the material richness of the practice. The incense-burners were themselves works of art — "golden," "huge," finely wrought. The investment in these objects, and in the perfumes they consumed, reflected the court's understanding that spiritual devotion was properly expressed through material magnificence.

## Historical Context

Incense has been used in Chinese ritual since the Zhou dynasty, with the practice reaching its greatest elaboration under the Tang and Song. By the Qing period, it was deeply embedded in Buddhist, Taoist, and Confucian observance. The court's incense consumption was prodigious — sandalwood, aloeswood, and complex compound perfumes were imported at great expense. After 1912, the grand scale of court incense-burning disappeared, though the practice persists in Chinese temples today.
