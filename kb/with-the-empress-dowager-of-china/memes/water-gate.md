# Water Gate

The Water Gate is a gate in the walls of [[peking|Peking]], situated between the [[tartar-city|Tartar City]] and the canal adjacent to the Legation Quarter. Its opening was exacted by the Allied forces in 1900 during the Boxer Rebellion, and it subsequently served as the entry point for foreign troops into the city (025-002).

## Location and Context

The Water Gate is located at the left side of the canal in the Tartar City, near the Legation Quarter. Before 1900, there was no gate at this position between the Chien-Mên and the Hata-Mên in the southern wall of the Tartar City (025-002).

The [[american-legation|United States Legation]], in 1904, occupied a Chinese temple on the canal, positioned at the left of the Water Gate (010-008). This placed the American diplomatic compound in close proximity to the gate that had been forcibly opened just a few years earlier.

## Description

The Water Gate was constructed as a breach in the existing city wall, created under military exigency rather than as a planned architectural feature. Its position along the canal gave it its name, reflecting the waterway that ran through this section of Peking's fortifications (025-002).

## Role in Court Life

During Katherine Carl's residence in Peking in 1903–1904, the Water Gate had become a normal part of the city's geography. Carl passed near it when traveling between the Legation Quarter and the [[imperial-city|Imperial City]]. The gate served as a reminder of the recent conflict, even as diplomatic life and Court routine had returned to their established patterns (010-008).

## In the Narrative

### The Boxer Rebellion

The Water Gate is most significant in the narrative for its role during the Boxer Rebellion of 1900. When the Allied forces finally reached Peking, they passed through the Water Gate and entered the city. The [[empress-dowager|Empress Dowager Cixi]] remained in the Palace until several hours after the foreign troops had passed through the Water Gate and were already within the walls of the [[british-legation|British Legation]] (030-023).

> She did not leave the Palace until several hours after the foreign troops had passed the Water Gate and were already within the walls of the English Legation. She had held out as long as possible. (030-023)

This passage marks the critical moment when the Empress Dowager, "discouraged and finding herself powerless," finally consented to flee the capital. Her long fingernails—the visible marker of her exalted rank—were cut off, and she escaped disguised as a common woman in a cart (030-023).

### Aftermath

The opening of the Water Gate symbolized the breaching of Peking's defenses and the collapse of the Qing Court's resistance to foreign intervention. By the time of Carl's residence three years later, the gate had become an established feature of the Legation Quarter's geography, serving the daily traffic of diplomats and officials moving between the Quarter and the Imperial City (025-002).

## Historical Significance

The Water Gate represents the physical legacy of the Boxer Rebellion on Peking's urban landscape. Its forced opening by the Allies was one of the conditions imposed after the relief of the Legations, ensuring that foreign forces would retain direct access to the diplomatic quarter. The gate remained in use throughout the late Qing period and symbolized the unequal power dynamics between China and the foreign powers in the aftermath of 1900.
