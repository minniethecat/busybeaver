# Cixi's Portrait: From Commission to St. Louis

The portrait of the [[empress-dowager|empress dowager]] Cixi painted by [[katherine-carl|katherine carl]] in
1903–1904 was more than a work of art — it was a diplomatic project, a cultural
experiment, and a historical watershed. The first Western-style portrait of a
Chinese Empress ever painted from life, it marked a deliberate gesture of
openness by the Qing Court after the trauma of the [[boxer-rebellion|boxer rebellion]], and its
journey from the [[summer-palace|summer palace]] to the [[st-louis-exposition|St. Louis Exposition]] traced a
narrative arc that illuminates the intersection of art, diplomacy, and
cross-cultural encounter at the twilight of the Qing Dynasty.

## Conception (April–July 1903)

The idea originated not in Peking but in the diplomatic circle of the
[[american-legation|american legation]]. Mrs. [[mrs-conger|Sarah Pike Conger]], wife of
the United States Minister to China, had cultivated an unusual relationship
with the Empress Dowager — one of the few Westerners to have met her personally
after the Boxer Rebellion. Mrs. Conger saw an opportunity to reshape Western
perceptions of Cixi through art.

In April 1903, she wrote to Katherine Carl, then visiting Shanghai, asking
if Carl would be willing to paint the Empress Dowager's portrait:

> Mrs. Conger hoped, if the project should materialize, that Her Majesty
> might later consent to send the portrait to the Exposition at St. Louis.
> She thought such a portrait would be of great interest to the American
> people. [P04-001]

Mrs. Conger also "felt, as she had had an opportunity of seeing a good deal of
the Empress Dowager, that if the world could see a true likeness of her, it
might modify the generally accepted idea which prevailed as to Her Majesty's
character."

The Empress Dowager approved the idea, agreeing to "arrange an Audience and set
a day for beginning." But Chinese officialdom moved at its own pace:

> The "mills of" Chinese Officialdom "grind slowly," and not until July did
> Mrs. Conger receive an official notification from the Wai-Wu-Pu (Chinese
> Foreign Office) requesting "Her Excellency Mrs. Conger to present the
> American artist, Miss Carl, to Her Imperial Majesty on the fifth day of
> August." [P04-001]

The date — August 5, 1903 — had been chosen after "much deliberation and many
consultations of the almanac" as the most auspicious for beginning work on
"the first likeness ever made of Her Majesty."

## The First Audience (August 5, 1903)

Carl arrived in Peking on July 29 and was received at the [[american-legation|american legation]]
by Mr. and Mrs. Conger. On the appointed day, they made the three-hour carriage
journey to the [[summer-palace|summer palace]].

The [[first-audience|First Audience]] was an elaborate ceremonial occasion. Carl was presented
by Mrs. Conger to the Empress Dowager in private audience. After the formal
greetings, the practical matter of the portrait was discussed:

- The Empress Dowager inquired about Carl's methods and materials
- A schedule for sittings was established
- Carl was assigned the [[palace-of-the-emperors-father|Palace of the Emperor's Father]] as her residence
- The [[lady-yu-keng|Yu-Keng ladies]] were appointed to attend and interpret for her

Carl wrote that she was struck by the contrast between the fearsome reputation
of the "Dragon Empress" and the charming, attentive woman who spoke with her.
This impression — of a person far more complex than Western caricature allowed —
would become the central theme of both her portrait and her memoir.

## The Residency (August 1903 – May 1904)

### Living at Court

Carl took up residence in the "Ker-Gunia Fu" — the Palace of the Emperor's Father,
a splendid demesne that had been Prince Ch'un's residence and the childhood home
of [[emperor-kwang-hsu|emperor kwang-hsu]]. The buildings had been "much injured by the foreign
troops in 1900" and hastily restored for her occupation (010-002).

Her daily routine combined the disciplines of a portrait painter with the
ceremonies of court life. She attended the [[empress-dowager|empress dowager]] on walks through
the Palace gardens, dined with the Court, witnessed theatrical performances,
and was invited to the [[emperor-birthday|Emperor-Birthday]] — the first
foreigner ever granted such access.

### The Sittings

The portrait sessions were the core of Carl's mission. The Empress Dowager sat
for her with what Carl described as patience and interest in the artistic process.
The intimacy of the sittings — the painter studying her subject, the Empress
observing the painter — created a relationship Carl believed was unique:

> Every portrait painter knows the sort of intimacy that establishes itself
> between him and his sitter... If the rapport is properly established, they
> get to know each other better by the time the portrait is finished than
> they could otherwise have done, perhaps in years. (020-001)

Carl painted four portraits during her residency — the principal one for the
St. Louis Exposition and three additional versions.

### The Palace Painters

Carl discovered she was not the only painter at Court. The Empress Dowager
maintained a corps of Chinese court painters who decorated lanterns, painted
scenery, and executed flower paintings — but "they have never seen the Empress
Dowager except from afar" (020-003). Though Mandarins of the Third Rank, they
were obliged to withdraw from any court Her Majesty entered and communicated with
her only through eunuch intermediaries.

The contrast between their invisible labor and Carl's privileged access could
not have been greater. She was the one painter who saw the Empress face to face.

## The Completed Portrait

The [[sacred-picture|Sacred Picture]], as it came to be called, depicted the Empress Dowager
seated on her throne in full court regalia. Carl's artistic choices reflected
both Western portrait conventions and Chinese symbolic requirements:

- The Empress Dowager was shown frontally, a pose associated with imperial authority
- The [[imperial-yellow|Imperial Yellow]] of her robes dominated the composition
- Symbols of sovereignty — the [[double-dragon|Double Dragon]], the [[imperial-throne|Imperial Throne]],
  [[jade-ornaments|Jade Ornaments]] — were prominently featured
- The face, however, was rendered with the psychological depth of Western portraiture,
  showing the intelligence and complexity Carl had observed over months of sittings

The portrait thus embodied the cross-cultural nature of the entire project:
Chinese in its symbolism and ceremonial purpose, Western in its technique
and psychological approach.

## Journey to St. Louis

The portrait was completed in early 1904 and prepared for transport to the
[[st-louis-exposition|St. Louis Exposition]] (the Louisiana Purchase Exposition), which opened
in April 1904.

The journey was itself a diplomatic operation. The portrait — an object of
imperial significance — had to be packed, crated, and shipped across the
Pacific with appropriate ceremony. It traveled under the protection of the
[[wai-wu-pu|Wai-Wu-Pu]] (Chinese Foreign Office) and the [[american-legation|american legation]].

At the Exposition, the portrait was displayed in the Chinese pavilion, where
it attracted considerable attention. For most American visitors, it was their
first glimpse of the Chinese Empress in anything approaching a realistic
likeness — a marked departure from the caricatures and hostile illustrations
that had circulated during the Boxer Rebellion.

## Aftermath and Legacy

### Carl's Return

Carl left China in May 1904 and returned to America, where she found herself
the subject of journalistic invention:

> After I returned to America, I was constantly seeing in newspapers (and
> hearing of) statements ascribed to me which I never made. Her Majesty was
> represented as having stood over me in threatening attitudes, forcing me
> to represent her as a young and beautiful woman! (001-003)

These falsehoods prompted her to write *With the Empress Dowager of China*
(1905), which remains the principal Western source for the portrait project
and for life at the late Qing Court.

### The Portrait's Fate

The portrait displayed at St. Louis was later presented to the United States
government as a diplomatic gift. It was initially exhibited at the Smithsonian
Institution and now resides in the collection of the Freer Gallery of Art
(though not always on public display).

### Historical Significance

The portrait project represented a strategic gesture by the Empress Dowager
at a moment when China's relationship with the West was at its most fraught.
After the Boxer Rebellion and the foreign occupation of Peking, Cixi needed to
rehabilitate her image — literally and figuratively. The portrait was an
instrument of soft diplomacy: a carefully crafted representation of Chinese
imperial authority designed to command Western respect rather than fear.

That a woman — a female American artist — was chosen for this task was itself
significant. Cixi, who ruled in a patriarchal system, may have recognized in
Carl a counterpart who could represent her to the world with sympathy rather
than the contempt that male Western observers typically directed at a female
sovereign.

The project succeeded in its immediate aims. The portrait was well received
at St. Louis; Carl's memoir provided a counter-narrative to anti-Cixi propaganda;
and the artistic collaboration demonstrated that cultural exchange between China
and the West was possible even in the aftermath of violent conflict.
