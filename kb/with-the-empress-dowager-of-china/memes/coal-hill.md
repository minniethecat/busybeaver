# Coal Hill

Coal Hill (Jingshan) was a prominent landmark in Peking, overlooking the Imperial City and the Winter Palace. It was crowned by the distinctive Dagoba visible in all views of the Imperial City, and bore the dark historical association of being the spot where the last emperor of the Ming dynasty committed suicide in 1644.

## Location and Context

Coal Hill stood at the northern edge of the Imperial City, just beyond the Winter Palace enclosure. Carl noted that the marble bridge leading into the Winter Palace gate lay "at the foot of the Coal Hill," near the Manchu Banner quarters (024-005). The hill formed a visual anchor for the palace district, its profile dominating the northern skyline of the Imperial City.

The hill was an artificial construction, built from the earth excavated to create the moats surrounding the Imperial City and the Forbidden City during the Ming dynasty. By the Qing period, it had become a landscaped imperial garden with temples, pavilions, and the iconic white Dagoba at its summit.

## Description

From the marble terrace of a lama temple south of the hill, Carl enjoyed "a beautiful view of the Coal Hill, surmounted by the curious Dagoba, so well known in all views of the Imperial City" (010-017). The terrace, thirty feet high with a balustrade of the conventionalized lotus design, also offered a view of "the belvedere that marks the spot where the last Emperor of the Mings committed suicide when he was conquered" (010-017).

At the two extremities of the temple terrace stood charming octagonal summerhouses where priests could "go for rest and contemplation, and, while murmuring their prayers, could feast their eyes upon a charming view" (010-017). The temple beneath the hill was surrounded by a grove of centuries-old arborvitae trees, planted in the form of a cross—the arborvitae being the Chinese tree of life and emblem of immortality (010-017).


> From this terrace we had a beautiful view of the Coal Hill, surmounted by the curious Dagoba, so well known in all views of the Imperial City, as well as of the belvedere that marks the spot where the last Emperor of the Mings committed suicide when he was conquered.
>
> (010-017)
The Dagoba itself was the hill's most recognizable feature: a white, bottle-shaped stupa of Tibetan Buddhist design, visible from across the city and featured prominently in Western illustrations and photographs of Peking.

## Role in Court Life

Unlike the palaces and audience halls that structured official court life, Coal Hill served as a visual landmark and contemplative backdrop. The lama temple at its foot, with its marble terrace and octagonal summerhouses, was a place of retreat where priests could meditate while overlooking the city. The hill's temples were among those "so ruthlessly destroyed and unnecessarily desecrated by the Allies during their occupation of Peking" after the Boxer Rising (010-017).

For the Empress Dowager's court, Coal Hill was a constant presence. Its proximity to the Winter Palace—literally at the palace gates—meant that every arrival and departure from the imperial residence passed beneath its shadow.

## In the Narrative

Coal Hill appears in Carl's memoir during her visit to a lama temple (Chapter X), where the view from the temple terrace provides one of the few panoramic descriptions of Peking's topography. The hill also marks the approach to the Winter Palace during the Empress Dowager's ceremonial entry into the city for the winter of 1903 (024-005).

Carl's mention of the belvedere marking the Ming emperor's suicide site connects the landscape to the founding narrative of the Qing dynasty. The last Ming ruler, the Chongzhen Emperor, hanged himself on Coal Hill as rebel forces overran the capital, clearing the path for the Manchu conquest.

## Historical Significance

Coal Hill occupies a unique position in Chinese history as both a symbol of Ming–Qing dynastic transition and a feature of the Qing imperial landscape. The suicide of the Chongzhen Emperor at this site in 1644 ended 276 years of Ming rule and became a touchstone of loyalty and tragedy in Chinese historical memory.

By Carl's time, the hill had been incorporated into the Qing imperial garden system, its Ming-era associations overlaid with Buddhist temple architecture and Manchu imperial landscaping. The damage inflicted during the Allied occupation of 1900–1901 represented yet another layer of historical violence upon the site.
