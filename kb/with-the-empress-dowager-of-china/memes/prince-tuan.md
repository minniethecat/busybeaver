# Prince Tuan

Prince Tuan was a Manchu prince of the imperial clan whose support for the Boxer movement — and whose son's designation as heir to the throne — gave the anti-foreign uprising its political legitimacy at the highest levels of the Qing Court (030-017).

## Role at the Chinese Court

Prince Tuan occupied a uniquely powerful position at the turn of the century: his son had been designated as the next heir to the Dragon Throne, making Tuan the father of the future emperor (030-017). This dynastic connection gave his political positions — including his fervent support for the Boxers — an authority that other officials could not easily challenge. Carl's memoir records that the Boxer movement gained its political force when "the next heir to the Throne, the Prince Tuan" and his associates "gave the movement an authority which eventually carried everything before it" (030-017).

## Character and Appearance

Carl provides no physical description of Prince Tuan. The memoir's single mention of him is political rather than personal: he appears as a force, not a portrait — a prince whose dynastic position amplified the Boxer cause into a national crisis (030-017).

## Historical Significance

Prince Tuan's support for the Boxers was a pivotal factor in the events of 1900. As the father of the designated heir, his endorsement of the anti-foreign movement signaled to provincial officials and military commanders that the Boxers had friends at the very summit of Qing power. The consequences — the siege of the Legations, the foreign expedition that crushed the uprising, and the punitive settlement that followed — reshaped China's relationship with the outside world and created the conditions in which Katherine Carl's conciliatory portrait commission, four years later, would take place.

## In the Narrative

Prince Tuan appears in a single sentence of Carl's memoir — in the chapter recounting the history of the Boxer Rebellion (030-017). But that sentence is freighted with consequence: it connects the imperial succession, the anti-foreign movement, and the near-collapse of the dynasty into a single chain of causation. For Carl and her readers, Prince Tuan's name served as shorthand for the Court faction that had brought China and the Western powers to the brink of war.
