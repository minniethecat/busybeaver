Manchuria, the northeastern region of China, held profound significance during the late Qing Dynasty as the ancestral homeland of the ruling [[manchu-dynasty|Manchu]] people. Known as the "cradle of the Dynasty," it was both a source of cultural identity and, during the [[empress-dowager|Empress Dowager Cixi]]'s later reign, a theater of mounting geopolitical crisis that weighed heavily on the Imperial Court.

## Cradle of the Dynasty

Manchuria was the heartland from which the Manchu people originated before their conquest of China and establishment of the Qing Dynasty in 1644. Even centuries later, during the Empress Dowager's reign, ladies of the Court came "from the heart of distant Manchuria, the cradle of the Dynasty" to attend the Imperial Birthday celebrations in Peking. (023-007)

The region retained its cultural distinctiveness within the Empire. Traditional Manchu sports such as polo were still played there during a time when they had largely disappeared from the capital. (026-011)

## Russian Occupation and the Evacuation Crisis

During the Boxer Rebellion of 1900, Russian forces occupied Manchuria. In the aftermath, Russia promised to evacuate, but repeatedly delayed doing so. This became a source of constant anxiety for the Empress Dowager. The date on which the Russians had promised to evacuate passed, and "they were making no move toward doing so." Rumors of war began to circulate. (023-002)

The Empress Dowager anxiously watched for Russian compliance. The failure of Russia to honor its evacuation pledges was one of the gravest concerns discussed at Imperial Audiences, with despatches arriving at all hours of the day. (016-007)

> The date on which the Russians had promised to evacuate Manchuria had passed and they were making no move toward doing so; and that there were rumors of war between Japan and Russia. This, then, must be what was weighing upon the mind of the Empress Dowager.
>
> (023-002)

## The Russo-Japanese War

The tensions over Manchuria eventually erupted into the Russo-Japanese War of 1904–1905. The Empress Dowager, already burdened with internal rebellions, now faced the prospect of foreign powers fighting a war on Chinese territory. "The Japanese were already in Manchuria, and no one knew how it might end." (032-004)

The Emperor [[emperor-kwang-hsu|Kwang-Hsu]] closely followed the course of the conflict. He was "not indifferent to affairs in Manchuria" and watched the war operations with intense interest, even having plans of the region drawn up so he could follow the military movements. (035-005)(035-006)

> The coming struggle was to take place in Manchuria, the cradle of the Dynasty. The Court was watching the course of events with the keenest solicitude.
>
> (028-014)

## Impact on the Court

The Manchuria crisis cast a shadow over the Empress Dowager's later reign. During her sixty-ninth birthday celebrations, she "seemed to take these State troubles to heart" and made but little effort to conceal her anxiety. She issued edicts calling for economy, saying "it would be improper and unworthy at this time of National distress" to celebrate extravagantly. (023-003)(023-004)

The Empress Dowager had hoped that "China would soon obtain her full rights in Manchuria again," but the worsening situation made everything seem darker for the nation's outlook. (023-004)
