# St. Louis Exposition

The Louisiana Purchase Exposition, held in St. Louis, Missouri in 1904,
was the event for which [[katherine-carl|Katherine Carl]] was commissioned
to paint the [[empress-dowager|Empress Dowager]] Cixi's portrait.
The Exposition provided the diplomatic rationale for Carl's presence
at the Chinese Court and shaped every aspect of the portrait project
that her memoir recounts.

## Overview

The St. Louis World's Fair of 1904 was one of the largest international expositions
of the early 20th century, attracting exhibits and visitors from across the globe.
The Qing government's decision to participate — and specifically to send
a life-size portrait of the Empress Dowager — represented a calculated act
of cultural diplomacy aimed at improving China's international image
in the aftermath of the [[boxer-rebellion|Boxer Rebellion]].

It was [[mrs-conger|Mrs. Conger]] who first conceived the idea:

> She thought such a portrait would be of great interest to
> the American people and might prove an attractive feature to
> the Exposition, in which she and Mr. Conger were, naturally,
> much interested. [P04-001]

Carl was commissioned not only for the main portrait
but for "three other pictures" destined for the Exposition as well. [P04-003]

## Background

The Louisiana Purchase Exposition was conceived as a celebration
of the centennial of the 1803 Louisiana Purchase, but its ambitions were far broader.
Exhibits from over 60 nations and territories filled a 1,200-acre fairground,
showcasing the latest in technology, agriculture, art, and culture.

For China, participation was freighted with diplomatic significance.
The [[boxer-rebellion|Boxer Rebellion]] had left the country internationally isolated,
and the Qing court saw the Exposition as an opportunity to present a different face —
one of an ancient civilization open to modernization and international friendship.
The portrait of the Empress Dowager was to be the centerpiece of the Chinese pavilion.

## Carl's Account

The approaching Exposition deadline gave urgency to the portrait project.
Carl notes with anxiety that "the opening of the St. Louis Exposition was approaching" (032-009)
as the Court prepared to move to the [[sea-palace|Sea Palace]],
threatening further delays in the painting schedule.

When the portrait was finally completed and dispatched,
it became a diplomatic event in its own right:

> His Imperial Highness Prince Pu L'un, Imperial Commissioner and
> personal representative of Their Majesties at the Exposition of
> St. Louis, awaited the arrival of the portrait there, delaying
> his departure for several days in order to be able himself to
> assist at the reception and placing of the portrait. (034-012)

The portrait's reception at the Fair confirmed Mrs. Conger's intuition.
Thousands of American visitors saw the Empress Dowager not as the
"Yellow Peril" villain of Boxer-era propaganda,
but as a dignified and even sympathetic figure —
precisely the perceptual shift that the Qing court had hoped to achieve.

## Outcome and Significance

The St. Louis Exposition portrait project produced three lasting outcomes.
First, it established a personal relationship between the Empress Dowager
and a Western artist — an unprecedented intimacy that yielded
Carl's richly detailed memoir.

Second, the portrait itself became a diplomatic artifact.
After the Exposition closed, it was presented as a gift
to the United States government and later displayed
at the Smithsonian Institution.

Third, and most significantly for cultural history,
Carl's memoir — published as *With the Empress Dowager of China* in 1905 —
offered Western readers their first extended, sympathetic account
of daily life in the innermost circles of the Qing Court.
The book's very existence was a product of the Exposition project,
and it shaped American perceptions of China for a generation.

> At the close of the Exposition, a delegate was sent from
> the Chinese Legation in Washington to arrange for the
> transportation of the picture to the latter place. (034-014)
