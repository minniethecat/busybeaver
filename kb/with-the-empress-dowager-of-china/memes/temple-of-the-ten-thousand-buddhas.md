# Temple of the Ten Thousand Buddhas

The Temple of the Ten Thousand Buddhas crowned the highest hill
in the [[summer-palace|Summer Palace]] park, its glazed yellow-tile facade
glittering above the lake. Katherine Carl witnessed both
the semiannual Confucian sacrifices and the Harvest Moon festival
at this temple, while also recording the damage inflicted
by foreign troops during the [[boxer-rebellion|Boxer Rebellion]] of 1900.

## Location and Context

The temple occupied a commanding position:

> The highest of the hills in the park of the Summer Palace
> is crowned by the Great Temple of the Ten Thousand Buddhas.
> This is approached by hundreds of steps, which lead up
> from the broadest part of the marble terrace over the lake. (017-005)

Beautiful palaces and picturesque memorial arches (*pai-lou*)
lined the terraced approach, positioned at points where
"Nature herself seems to have designed these positions for them." (017-005)
At the foot of the hill stood a chapel used for
the semiannual Confucian ceremonies. (013-005)

## Description

The temple derived its name from its distinctive construction:

> The Temple of the Ten Thousand Buddhas is so-called from its
> being built in glazed yellow tiles, each representing a niche,
> in which is seated a Buddha, of which there are many more
> than ten thousand. (017-013)

The interior comprised three chapels, the central one housing
the Great Buddha. A second Buddha image, "invested with peculiarly
sacred qualities," had been destroyed in 1900 when foreign troops
"hurled it into the lake below and broken it into a thousand pieces." (017-013)

The Empress Dowager felt the temple depredations deeply.
Carl observed:

> Her Majesty seemed to feel the depredations to the temples,
> by the foreigners, more keenly than anything else. (017-013)

She attributed this sensitivity to the Chinese attitude of
religious tolerance — unable to comprehend "our contempt
for any other kind of worship except that in which we
ourselves indulge." (017-013)

## Role in Court Life

The temple served dual ceremonial functions.
Its hilltop chapel hosted the semiannual sacrifices to Confucius,
described by Carl as "a splendid ceremony." (013-005)
The service featured an address to the Sage's memory,
music and hymns, and offerings of millet, meat, and wine
on a dragon table before the altar. (013-005)

The marble terrace beneath the temple also served as the setting
for the [[festival-of-the-harvest-moon|Festival of the Harvest Moon]].
Carl described the Court procession winding through the gardens,
"accompanied by hundreds of lantern-bearing eunuchs,"
climbing to the terrace beneath the temple, where
"the great terrace over the lake" provided a natural stage. (018-005)

## In the Narrative

The temple appears in Chapters 13, 17, and 18 of Carl's memoir.
Chapter 13 describes the Confucian ceremony in detail,
while Chapter 17 provides the architectural description
within the broader tour of the Summer Palace grounds.

In Chapter 18, the temple's illuminated terrace forms
the climax of the Harvest Moon festival, with the Court
assembled beneath its height as lanterns transformed
the lakeside into a scene of enchantment. (018-005)

## Historical Significance

The Temple of the Ten Thousand Buddhas represented
the layered religious culture of the Qing court —
Buddhist in structure, Confucian in ritual practice.
The destruction of its sacred Buddha in 1900
and the Empress Dowager's grief over the act
encapsulated the cultural wounds of the foreign occupation.

Carl's account preserves the temple as it stood in 1903–1904:
still bearing the scars of 1900, still hosting the ancient rites,
and still dominating the Summer Palace landscape
as a beacon of imperial piety.
