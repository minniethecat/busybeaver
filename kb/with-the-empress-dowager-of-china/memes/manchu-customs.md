# Manchu Customs

The distinctive cultural practices and fashions of the Manchu ruling class at the Qing Imperial Court, including characteristic dress, hairstyles, footwear, and social conventions that set them apart from the Han Chinese population.

## Description

The Manchu people, founders of the Qing Dynasty (the Dynasty of the "Great Purity"), maintained a distinct cultural identity at the Imperial Court. Manchu ladies wore their hair in an elaborate style, carried "out from this coil over a golden, jade, or tortoiseshell sword-like pin, into a large-winged bow" (001-012). By the time of Carl's visit, the Empress Dowager and Court Ladies had "substituted satin instead of the real hair for this bow" (001-012).

Manchu women did not practice foot-binding — "that custom prevailed in China before the Manchu conquest" (007-015). Instead, they wore distinctive "Manchu shoes with six-inch-high, stiff white soles in the centre of the foot" (001-014), which gave them added height. The Manchu coiffure was worn by all Court ladies in official settings, with "the long red silk tassels flowing down the back from the silver butterfly pins on the side of the head" (009-006).

Carl described the Manchu coiffure in detail:

> Formerly all Manchu ladies who have marvelous hair carried the hair itself out from this coil over a golden, jade, or tortoiseshell sword-like pin, into a large-winged bow. The Empress Dowager and the Ladies of the Court have substituted satin instead of the hair, for this winglike construction, as being more practicable and less liable to get out of order. So satin-like and glossy is their hair that it is difficult to tell where it ends and the satin begins.
>
> (001-012)

## Cultural Significance

The Manchu Dynasty ruled China for over two and a half centuries prior to Carl's memoir. The Manchus "have adopted many of the manners and customs of the Chinese, but the Manchu women have retained their own coiffure and their own distinctive dress" (007-015). This selective cultural retention served as a marker of ruling-class identity.

Court rank and Manchu lineage were closely intertwined. The young Empress was "the daughter of the Duke Chow, General of one of the Manchu Banner Corps and a brother of the reigning Empress Dowager" (005-002). The Manchu Princes alone held hereditary rights to certain ornaments and insignia (009-004).

The Manchu nobility observed strict sumptuary rules. Manchu ladies "use a great deal of paint and powder on their faces; but a widow can never add one artificial iota to the rose of her cheek, to the cherry of her lips, or the lily of her brow" (005-012). This prohibition on cosmetics for widows was strictly enforced at Court.

## In Carl's Account

Katherine Carl observed Manchu customs firsthand during her months at the Summer Palace. She noted that Her Majesty the Empress Dowager, though "not more than five feet tall," appeared more imposing because she wore "the Manchu shoes with six-inch-high, stiff white soles" (001-014).

Carl also recorded the presence of younger Manchu women at Court, including "a young Manchu girl at Court whose father had been an attaché at Berlin, who spoke German and English" (002-007), reflecting the cosmopolitan education some Manchu aristocratic families provided their daughters.

She observed that the wives and daughters of Manchu nobles "who were of sufficient rank to present their congratulations in person" would arrive at the Palace for important occasions such as the Emperor's Birthday (007-010). Carl also noted the physical distinctiveness of the Manchu ruling family, remarking that "the Manchus have whiter skins than the Chinese" (008-004).

## Historical Context

The Manchu conquest of China in 1644 established the Qing Dynasty, which Carl refers to as the Dynasty of the "Great Purity" (008-003). The Manchus originated from Manchuria, northeast of the Great Wall, and maintained their ethnic identity throughout their rule through institutions such as the Manchu Banner Corps (005-002), a hereditary military and social organization. The Manchu women notably retained their distinctive customs of dress and coiffure even as the ruling class adopted many Chinese manners (007-015).

By the time of Carl's visit in 1903--1904, the Qing Dynasty was in its final years (it would fall in 1912). The Empress Dowager's court still observed traditional Manchu customs, but the presence of Western-educated Manchu women at Court signaled the beginning of cultural transformation.

## Related Customs

- **Imperial Yellow** — A color reserved for the Emperor and Empress Dowager; Carl describes Her Majesty's gown of "Imperial yellow, brocaded in the wistaria vine" (001-011)
- **Court Dress** — The "magnificent" costume of Court Ladies, made of "stiff transparent silk" (009-006), distinct from Manchu daily attire
- **Foot-binding** — Practiced by Han Chinese women but explicitly rejected by Manchu women (007-015)
- **Morning Salutation** — Daily ceremonial greetings to the Emperor by the Ladies of his family (009-004)
