# Boxer Rebellion

The Boxer Rebellion (Yìhétuán Yùndòng, 1900) was a violent anti-foreign uprising
in northern China that resulted in the siege of the foreign Legation Quarter in [[peking|Peking]]
and the military intervention of an eight-nation alliance.
The rebellion formed the essential political backdrop to [[katherine-carl|Katherine Carl]]'s memoir:
it was the crisis that made the [[empress-dowager|Empress Dowager]]'s subsequent gesture of commissioning
a Western portrait so diplomatically significant.

## Overview

In the summer of 1900, the "Society of Righteous and Harmonious Fists"
— known to Westerners as "Boxers" — launched attacks on Chinese Christians
and foreign nationals across northern China.

> The secret society of the Boxers began their sanguinary attacks
> upon the Native Christians and upon all the foreigners in China. (030-016)

The violence culminated in the two-month siege of the Peking Legation Quarter,
where the foreign diplomatic community — including the Congers and other figures
who would later feature in Carl's story — held out against Boxer attacks
until relieved by an international expeditionary force in August 1900.

The Boxers' rallying cry reflected deep popular resentment
against foreign encroachment:

> The secret society of the Boxers took "China for the Chinese"
> as their motto. (030-017)

## Background

The rebellion's roots lay in decades of foreign pressure on Qing China:
unequal treaties, territorial concessions, missionary activity,
and the humiliating defeat in the Sino-Japanese War of 1894–1895.
Anti-foreign sentiment found fertile ground among peasants
suffering from economic dislocation and natural disasters.

The Boxer movement initially targeted Christian converts and missionaries
before escalating to include all foreigners and foreign-connected Chinese.
By June 1900, the violence had reached Peking,
where the Empress Dowager Cixi made the fateful decision
to support the Boxers rather than suppress them.

The subsequent invasion by the Eight-Nation Alliance and the occupation of Peking
left deep scars. Carl records that many at court spoke of the rebellion
as "the Boxer troubles" (031-004) — a euphemism that reflected
the trauma the events had inflicted on the imperial household.

## Carl's Account

Carl arrived in China three years after the rebellion,
but its shadow falls across every chapter of her memoir:

> The Boxer rebellion was a frequent topic of conversation. [P04-009]

The physical evidence of the crisis was still visible.
The Legation Quarter where Carl stayed had been "occupied by the United States troops
after the Boxer rebellion" (010-008) — a reminder that the diplomatic compound
was, in effect, a post-siege military garrison.

Carl also reports the Empress Dowager's own reflections on the rebellion.
During one conversation, Her Majesty described how objects and documents
had been "lost during the Boxer troubles" (028-003) —
a phrase that hints at the material and symbolic damage the uprising inflicted
on the imperial establishment.

## Outcome and Significance

The Boxer Rebellion ended with the decisive defeat of the Boxer forces
and the signing of the Boxer Protocol in September 1901.
The terms imposed on China were draconian: a massive indemnity of 450 million taels,
the stationing of foreign troops in Peking, and the destruction of defensive fortifications.

For the Empress Dowager, the aftermath was personally and politically devastating.
She had fled the capital disguised as a peasant, and upon her return in 1902,
faced the challenge of rebuilding the dynasty's legitimacy.
It was in this context — of a court seeking to project reform and openness —
that Mrs. Conger's proposal for a Western portrait commission
was received with unexpected enthusiasm.

Carl's memoir itself became part of the post-Boxer diplomatic project:
by presenting a sympathetic portrait of the Empress Dowager to an American audience,
it aimed to counteract the "Yellow Peril" imagery
that the rebellion had entrenched in Western popular culture.
