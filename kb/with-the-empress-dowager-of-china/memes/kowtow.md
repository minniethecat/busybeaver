# Kowtow

The kowtow (磕头, *kētóu*) — a ceremonial prostration involving three kneelings
and nine knockings of the forehead to the ground (三跪九叩, *sān guì jiǔ kòu*) —
was the most formal gesture of respect and submission at the Qing Imperial Court.
It was both the supreme ritual of internal court hierarchy and the focal point of
the most intractable diplomatic conflict between China and the Western powers,
who refused to perform what they regarded as a degrading act before the Chinese Emperor.

## Description

The full kowtow ceremony comprised three sequences: the supplicant knelt, performed
three prostrations touching the forehead to the ground, rose, knelt again for three
more prostrations, and repeated the sequence once more — nine prostrations in total.

The ceremony was prescribed by the [[book-of-rites|Book of Rites]], the ancient Confucian text that
codified Chinese ritual practice. At the [[emperor-birthday|Emperor-Birthday]],
Carl witnessed the ceremony in its full formality:

> Each splendidly garbed Prince and Noble knelt and made the prostration
> prescribed by the Book of Rituals. (009-011)

The order of prostration was strictly governed by rank: the highest princes
performed first, followed by lower-ranking officials in descending order.
For the [[empress-dowager-birthday|Empress-Dowager-Birthday]], the prostrations began at 2 a.m. — the
exact hour of her birth — and continued through the pre-dawn hours.

## Occasions

The kowtow was performed on all major ceremonial occasions at the Qing Court:

- **Imperial birthdays**: Both the [[emperor-birthday|Emperor-Birthday]] and
  [[empress-dowager-birthday|Empress-Dowager-Birthday]] required formal prostrations from the entire Court
- **New Year ceremonies**: The [[chinese-new-year-court|Chinese-New-Year-Court]] observances included
  kowtow rituals before the Imperial throne
- **Receiving Imperial decrees**: Any official communication from the Throne was
  received on bended knee
- **Audiences**: Officials granted formal audience performed the kowtow before
  addressing Their Majesties
- **Theatrical performances**: Even actors in ceremonial plays performed the
  kowtow as part of their roles — Carl watched costumed performers "come onto
  the stage and perform the kowtow" (022-013)

## At Court: Carl's Observations

Carl, as a Western observer unbound by Chinese ritual obligations, was able to
watch the kowtow with a detachment impossible for its participants. She noted
how naturally the [[lady-of-the-court|Court Ladies]] performed the ceremony,
and how the [[empress-dowager|Empress Dowager]] received it as her unquestioned due.

The situational nature of the ritual was one of her most perceptive observations.
When Their Majesties were acting in their official capacity, all communication
was conducted on bended knee — even a great prince would drop to his knees to
receive a simple command. But in private, familiar conversation, courtiers stood
while speaking. If an official order was given mid-conversation, the attendant
would "immediately drop upon his knees to receive it" (034-006).

Carl herself was exempted from the kowtow. As a foreign guest, she made the
European reverence (a curtsy or bow) instead — one of many accommodations the
[[empress-dowager|Empress Dowager]] made for her Western portrait painter.

## The Kowtow Controversy

No Chinese ritual caused greater friction with the West than the kowtow.
The conflict crystallized around the question of diplomatic protocol:
should foreign ambassadors — representatives of sovereign nations — prostrate
themselves before the Chinese Emperor?

### Macartney (1793)

The first major kowtow crisis came with Lord Macartney's embassy to the
Qianlong Emperor. Macartney refused to perform the full kowtow, offering
instead to kneel on one knee as he would before his own sovereign, George III.
The Chinese court regarded this as an insult; the embassy's diplomatic goals
went largely unfulfilled.

### Amherst (1816)

Lord Amherst's embassy fared even worse. His refusal to kowtow resulted in
his being denied an audience with the Jiaqing Emperor altogether and expelled
from Peking — a diplomatic humiliation that hardened British attitudes toward
China for decades.

### The Treaty System

After the Opium Wars, the unequal treaties explicitly exempted Western diplomats
from the kowtow. The ritual that had symbolized Chinese supremacy over "tributary"
nations became the emblem of China's diplomatic subordination to the West.
The foreign refusal to kowtow was not merely a matter of comfort or dignity —
it was a declaration that Western powers did not accept China's claim to
universal sovereignty.

## Cultural Significance

The kowtow embodied the Confucian hierarchy that structured Chinese society.
The Emperor, as the Son of Heaven, stood at the apex of a cosmic order; all
mortals — from the highest prince to the humblest peasant — owed him absolute
submission. The physical act of prostration made this metaphysical relationship
visible and tangible.

The ritual also reflected the Chinese conception of propriety (*li*, 礼) as
the organizing principle of social life. The [[propriety|Book of Rites]] prescribed
not just the form of the kowtow but its timing, its sequence, and the precise
gradations of ceremony appropriate to each rank and occasion. In performing the
kowtow, the courtier was not merely submitting to the Emperor — he was affirming
the entire cosmic and social order that the Emperor represented.

## In Carl's Account

Carl's account of the kowtow is valuable precisely because she was not required
to perform it. She could observe the ritual as a spectacle — "beautiful and alien
to Western sensibilities" — while recognizing its profound cultural significance.
Her descriptions of the Birthday ceremonies, with their "splendidly garbed Prince[s]
and Noble[s]" making their prostrations in strict order of rank, capture both
the visual magnificence and the hierarchical rigor of Qing court ritual.

The kowtow appears throughout her memoir not as a single event but as a recurring
motif of court life: in the Audience Hall, at the Theater, during the ceremonial
receptions, and in the daily protocols governing interaction with Their Majesties.
