# Lady Yu

Lady Yu Keng was one of the most important intermediaries between the [[empress-dowager|Empress Dowager]] and the foreign community in [[peking|Peking]]. As the daughter of a former Chinese Minister who had served abroad, she spoke Western languages fluently—a rare skill among the [[lady-of-the-court|court ladies]]—and served as interpreter for the Empress Dowager during audiences with foreign visitors.

## Background

Lady Yu's father, Yu Keng, had served as Chinese Minister to Japan and to France. His daughters received a Western education, learning French and English, and acquired the cosmopolitan polish that made them invaluable at the post-Boxer Court. Lady Yu and her sister, known in Western accounts as "the Misses Yu," were among the few Chinese women who could move comfortably between the worlds of the Imperial Court and the foreign legations.

## In Carl's Account

Carl first encountered the Ladies Yu-Keng upon her first arrival at the Summer Palace: "A group of Princesses and Ladies-in-waiting stood to receive us. The Ladies Yu-Keng, wife and daughter of a former Chinese Minister to France, stood near the Princesses; and their perfect knowledge of both Chinese and English rendered them delightful mediums of communication" (001-007). Carl found them to be "a link between the real, everyday world and this Arabian Nights Palace into which we had been wafted" (001-007).

Throughout her nine-month residency, Lady Yu and her sister served as indispensable interpreters. When Carl first began painting the Empress Dowager's portrait, it was the Ladies Yu-Keng who translated Her Majesty's instructions: "For a few moments I heard the faintest ticking of the eighty-five clocks... Then, happily, I became interested, and absolutely unconscious of anything but my sitter and my work" (001-021). Her Majesty later ordered the Ladies Yu-Keng "to keep me company for the meals in my own quarters" so that Carl might "have pleasant company and be able to converse in my own language" (002-007).

Carl particularly admired the elder Miss Yu's literary accomplishments, noting that she was "a remarkably clever girl and well posted in Chinese literature" (010-014). When the Empress Dowager wrote large calligraphy characters for her guests, "Lady Yu-Keng" served as interpreter, conveying Her Majesty's witty observations about brushwork (020-011).

Lady Yu represents the small but significant cohort of bicultural, bilingual Chinese women who facilitated the Court's engagement with the foreign world in the Empress Dowager's final years.
