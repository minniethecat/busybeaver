# Festival of the Harvest Moon

The Mid-Autumn Festival at the Qing Court, celebrated with the dramatization of the Legend of the Harvest Moon, an elaborate illuminated ceremony on the marble terrace beneath the Temple of the Ten Thousand Buddhas, and a spectacular lantern-lit lake procession.

## Overview

The Mid-Autumn Festival, popularly known as the Festival of the Harvest Moon, was observed with due ceremony at the Palace in the autumn of 1903. As with all Chinese festivals, there were representations at the Palace Theater, including a dramatization of the festival's founding legend (018-003). The celebration culminated in an elaborate illuminated ceremony on the great marble terrace, followed by a lantern-lit procession across the lake in the Palace fleet (018-005).

Katherine Carl witnessed the festival while resident at the Summer Palace painting the Empress Dowager's portrait. Her account provides one of the most detailed foreign descriptions of Qing Court Mid-Autumn observances.

## Background

The Harvest Moon Festival takes place at the full moon of the eighth Chinese month. Carl observed that the Chinese, despite their reputation for being unemotional, have more popular festivals "indulged in by all classes of people, than in any country in the world, except perhaps Japan" (018-002). All popular festivals and religious ceremonies were celebrated in the Palace with apparent equal zest to that shown among the common people (018-002).

The festival's creation legend tells of an Emperor who received an herb of immortality from a visiting fairy. A young handmaiden, entering the Throne-room during the Emperor's absence, tasted and ate the entire herb. When the Emperor discovered this, he ordered her killed to recover the herb, but before the eunuchs could act, the charm began to work — the maiden grew the wings of immortality and flew to the Moon, where she still lives with her pet white rabbit, compounding the Elixir of Immortality (018-003).

## Carl's Account

Carl attended the theatrical performances in Her Majesty's loge and described the day's climax as "one of the most beautiful spectacular tableaux I have ever seen" (018-004). The stage depicted a lake covered with luminous lotus, beneath a floating full moon. An immense golden Buddha, throned on a gigantic lotus flower in the center, sat impassive and serene, while illuminated lotus flowers and luminous birds — emblems of Immortality — hovered over the lake. The tableau represented Nirvana, "when the soul is absorbed into Nature and forms a part of it" (018-004).

After the performance, the Court hurried to the gardens where the ceremony was to take place. The procession, with the Emperor, Empress Dowager, and Ladies in full dress, accompanied by "hundreds of lantern-bearing eunuchs," wound through the verandahed corridors and garden paths "like some great glowworm" until reaching the marble terrace beneath the Temple of the Ten Thousand Buddhas (018-005).

The Mid-Autumn celebration was observed with due ceremony at the Palace:

> The Mid-Autumn Festival, popularly known as the Festival of the Harvest Moon, which is at its full at the time of the celebration, was, of course, observed with due ceremony at the Palace. For these festivals there are always representations at the Palace Theater, and one of the plays on such days is the dramatization of the Legend of the Festival.
>
> (018-003)

## The Moon Ceremony

The ceremony took place in an open space bathed in moonlight, with the setting sun still glowing in the west. Before the great Stone Pai-lou stood an illuminated floral *pai-lou* dedicated to the Moon, constructed entirely of chrysanthemums, bearing the inscription "To the Glory of the Chaste and Pure Celestial Orb" in white blooms "like gleaming stars" (018-006).

Their Majesties made bows and prostrations to the Moon and placed floral offerings on the altar, followed by the young Empress and Ladies. Throughout the ceremony, eunuchs recited a poem to the Moon in melodious, rhythmic cadence, with two voices alternating to "wonderful effect" (018-007).

After the offerings, an auto-da-fé was made — the offerings, sticks of sweet incense, and paper cut in curious designs were set alight, with wine poured over the flames. The flames "leaped high above the huge incense-burner" on its great bronze tripod (018-007).

Carl described the scene as "wonderfully picturesque" — the brilliant circle of splendidly gowned Ladies with the Emperor and Empress Dowager at their center, surrounded by the glowing ring of lantern-bearing eunuchs, all beneath the brilliant Harvest Moon that "shone with unwonted splendor" (018-007).

## The Lake Procession

When the flames subsided and only white incense smoke remained, the lantern-lit procession followed Their Majesties to the banks of the lake, where the entire Palace fleet lay moored beneath the marble terrace, brilliantly illuminated (018-008).

Eunuchs holding gleaming lanterns lined the terrace and knelt on the steps leading into the water while Their Majesties descended. On two boats flanking the Imperial barge, eunuchs held their lanterns to form the characters "Peace" and "Prosperity" (018-008). The lake glowed with the reflections of myriad lanterns, while the Harvest Moon's reflection "gleamed like liquid diamonds across the lake" (018-008).

## Outcome and Significance

The Harvest Moon Festival exemplified the Qing Court's preservation of traditional seasonal rituals that connected imperial ceremony to natural cycles. Carl noted that all Chinese religious rites had their origin in "some natural phenomenon or some periodical event in Nature" (029-012), and the Harvest Moon typified "the season when the life-giving element in Nature goes to rest for the Winter" (029-019).

The festival also demonstrated the Court's sophisticated theatrical and illumination arts, which Carl, a professional painter, found artistically remarkable. The Chinese, she observed, obtained "most artistic effects in their illuminations, and by the most simple means" (018-004).
