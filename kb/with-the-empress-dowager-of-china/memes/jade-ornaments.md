# Jade Ornaments

Jade, the most prized of precious stones in Chinese culture, appeared throughout the Qing Imperial Court in ornaments, accessories, ritual objects, and decorative items (009-011).

## Description

Jade was used extensively in court dress and accessories. The Empress Dowager's portrait gown featured "jade buttons" fastening from shoulder to hem (001-011). Court ladies wore their hair over "a golden, jade, or tortoiseshell sword-like pin" (001-012). The Emperor's attire included "a handsome belt buckle of jade" at his waist (009-004).

Beyond personal adornment, jade appeared in ritual and decorative objects. At the Emperor's Birthday ceremony, officials presented "a jade ruyie in each tray" alongside other precious items (009-026). The Empress Dowager kept "small jade curios" among her favorite ornaments in her private apartments (022-003).

Carl described the ceremonial presentation of jade *ruyie* emblems during the Emperor's Birthday:

> Each splendidly garbed Prince and Noble knelt and made the prostration prescribed by the Book of Rites, and each presented His Majesty with a jade emblem, called by the Chinese *ruyie*, erroneously supposed to be a scepter by most foreigners; but the *ruyie* is simply an emblem of Good Luck, and may be presented on festive occasions to anyone whom the givers wish to honor, and is not an emblem of Imperial authority.
>
> (009-011)

## Cultural Significance

Jade (玉, *yù*) traditionally symbolized virtue, purity, and status in Chinese culture. At the Qing Court, its use was tied to rank — the quality and type of jade ornament indicated the wearer's position (009-026). Carl noted that the Empress Dowager wore jewelry where pieces "on the left being of brilliant green jade, while those on the right hand were of pure white jade" (001-013), suggesting deliberate aesthetic and symbolic choices.

## In Carl's Account

Carl observed jade in various contexts during her months at Court. She noted that dried flowers were presented "in a jade bowl, with two long cherry sticks" during entertainments (012-009). In the Empress Dowager's temple, there was "a great Buddha of white jade, with jeweled stole and cuffs" (010-019). During a game she witnessed, objects were thrown "into a jade bowl" and the "numbers uppermost were counted" (022-006).
