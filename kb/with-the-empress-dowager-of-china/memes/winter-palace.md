# Winter Palace

The Winter Palace was the primary winter residence of the Empress Dowager Cixi and the Qing imperial court, located within the Imperial City of Peking. Katherine Carl stayed and painted there during the winter of 1903–1904, and her memoir provides one of the most detailed Western accounts of its interiors, architecture, and court routines.

## Location and Context

The Winter Palace lay within the Imperial City, the innermost of Peking's three walled cities—the Chinese, the Tartar, and the Imperial City (024-005). Its battlemented and turreted walls were surrounded by a moat, and the palace was approached by crossing a stone bridge over the Grain-bearing Canal, then passing through massive gates and winding passages between high walls (024-005). It stood at the foot of Coal Hill, near the Manchu Banner quarters (024-005).

Unlike the Summer Palace—the Empress Dowager's favorite—which lay some distance from the capital, the Winter Palace was embedded within the dense political geography of Peking. The Audience Hall, located in the Emperor's enclosure, was near one of the Great Gates (016-003). The Empress Dowager's quarters were separated by high walls and guarded gates from the Emperor's (024-029).

## Description

Carl approached the Winter Palace by chair, entering through a series of progressively more restricted gates. The red outer walls, "faded by Time and weather to a charming gray-pink," were crowned with "airy-looking turrets reflected in the still waters of the moat" (024-005). She found the approach labyrinthine: "even after entering the exterior gates, one winds in and out between high walls, through massive gates and heavy wooden doors studded with huge iron nails and ornamental copper balls" (024-005).

Inside, Carl was struck by the "somber splendor" of the great central hall (the domed Throne-room), which she considered "the most satisfying, the most picturesque of all the restful, harmonious Chinese interiors I had seen" (024-008). The hall had dull red walls, a "splendid coffered ceiling glowing in color and glinting in gold," and a central dome with elaborately carved pendentives painted in brilliant primary colors, "subdued into a rich harmony by the demi-obscurity" (024-008). A notable feature of the domes in the palaces of the Violet City was that they were invisible from outside, preserving "the beautiful straight line of the roof, with its upturned corners" (024-025).

The floor was paved with great blocks of highly polished black marble (024-009). On a low dais stood an antique throne of red lacquer framed in ebony and inlaid with cloisonné, backed by a three-leaved bronze screen with landscapes in low relief (024-009). Heavy padded satin portières hung from lintels, and the lower halves of the windows were of plate-glass, the upper of transparent Korean paper (024-009).

In the second hall—the most formal of the three in the Empress Dowager's enclosure—Carl noted a "spirit-stairway" of carved marble with the double dragon, a feature normally reserved for temple architecture (024-015). The interior, fifty feet high, had a "splendid coffered ceiling" and walls of "wonderfully carved wood, with cloisonné medallions" (024-015). A cistern of carved jade stood opposite the throne dais, intended for cooling the temperature in summer (024-015).

The Empress Dowager's private apartments opened off the domed Throne-room. Her sitting-room, brilliantly lit by two sides of windows, was "in dazzling contrast to the somber splendor" of the adjoining hall, filled with "gay flowers and growing plants, the fruits piled high in great painted bowls" (024-010). A small shrine to Buddha stood in the room, and a hidden stairway in an alcove behind embroidered satin curtains led to a private chapel above, where services were held by lama priests (024-012). The chapel contained a great golden Buddha, tall golden candlesticks "shining with pearls and rubies," and a dado of "red-and-gold clothed saints" around the walls (024-013).

The third hall, one of the Emperor's Throne-rooms, was given to Carl for use as her studio while painting the Empress Dowager's portraits. Though magnificent in scale, she found it dark and plagued by reflection from the yellow-tiled roof of the adjoining palace, calling it "a dreadful disappointment" (024-028).

## Role in Court Life

The Winter Palace was the seat of official court business during the cold months. The Grand Council and the members of the Cabinet conducted state affairs from the capital, and the Empress Dowager held audiences in the Great Audience Hall (016-003). The palace's many walls within walls reflected the rigid spatial hierarchy of Qing court protocol: the Empress Dowager's private quarters, the Emperor's enclosure, the Audience Hall, and the Theater each occupied distinct, walled-off zones (024-029).

Tradition was "more rigidly observed here than at the Summer Palace" (024-029). At the Winter Palace, "everything seemed to be referred to the Emperor, whereas Her Majesty seemed to be the first figure at the Summer Palace" (024-029). The palace's confined layout—only "walled-in courts and the shut-in walks, between high walls"—offered little scope for the outdoor promenades that the Empress Dowager enjoyed at the Summer Palace (028-005).

Carl witnessed the ceremony of the Empress Dowager's arrival at the Winter Palace in December 1903, describing how the young Empress, Princesses, and Ladies of the Court preceded Her Majesty and stood on the threshold in full Court dress to receive her (024-003). The cymbals and flutes of the Imperial Hymn announced the procession, and the Empress Dowager entered seated in an "open chair," having abandoned her closed palanquin as soon as she reached the palace grounds (024-007).

## In the Narrative

The Winter Palace dominates Chapter XXIV of Carl's memoir, which bears its name as the chapter title (024-001). Carl's account of the palace's interiors—from the domed Throne-room to the Empress Dowager's private chapel—forms the most detailed architectural description in the book.


> The Summer Palace was always the Empress Dowager's favorite Palace, but after the Boxer rising and the subsequent occupation of Peking by the Allies, when foreign troops were stationed in both the Peking Palaces, and so much damage done them, she would have preferred to have lived the whole year round at the Summer Palace. As it is, she occupies it from eight to nine months of the year, going out to it at the first opportunity in the spring, and leaving it only when it is so cold as to make it impracticable.
>
> (024-002)
Earlier chapters mention the palace as the site of the Emperor and Empress's wedding, "celebrated with great pomp at the Winter Palace in February, 1889" (005-002). The palace also figures in the narrative of the Boxer Rising: the Empress Dowager returned from the Summer Palace to the Winter Palace just a week before the Allies reached Peking in 1900, "hoping as a *dernier ressort* that the presence of the Sacred Persons, Their Majesties, in the city might serve as a check upon the soldiers and people" (030-022).

Carl's departure from the Winter Palace to the Sea Palace marked a significant transition in her portrait-painting timeline, as the move forced her to relocate her studio just when she had "begun to progress with my work" and the St. Louis Exposition deadline approached (032-009).

## Historical Significance

The Winter Palace (Tzu-Chin Cheng, the Purple Forbidden City) was the ceremonial and administrative heart of the Qing Empire. By the time of Carl's residence in 1903–1904, it had already survived the trauma of the Boxer Rising and the Allied occupation of Peking, during which foreign troops were stationed in the Peking palaces and "so much damage done them" (024-002). The Empress Dowager, who had fled the capital during the occupation, returned to find the palace a site of diminished imperial prestige, yet the elaborate court ceremonies Carl witnessed—the formal receptions, the rigid traditions, the splendid interiors—testify to the dynasty's determination to maintain its symbolic authority.

Carl's detailed description of the Empress Dowager's private chapel, with its jeweled altar ornaments and "dim, religious light," captures a rarely witnessed dimension of Cixi's personal devotion (024-012). The presence of steel engravings of Queen Victoria in the Empress Dowager's sitting-room reflects Cixi's awareness of and identification with other female monarchs, a cross-cultural gesture that Carl found surprising and revealing (024-012).
