# Chinese New Year at the Qing Court

The greatest of the popular Chinese festivals, celebrated at the Imperial Palace with elaborate decorations, diplomatic receptions, and two weeks of festivities in early 1904, coinciding with the outbreak of the Russo-Japanese War.

## Overview

The Chinese New Year of 1904 was observed at the Qing Court with extensive pomp and ceremony. The Palace was decorated with hundreds of horn lanterns bearing the red "Sho" (longevity) character, painted figures of red-clothed gods, and vermilion-faced monsters on the outer doors intended to frighten away evil spirits (032-002). The celebrations included theatrical gala representations, formal diplomatic audiences, and filled the Palace with visitors (032-002).

The festival season spanned approximately two weeks, beginning with the New Year observances and continuing through the Lantern Festival. During this period, the Court moved from the Winter Palace to the Sea Palace, and Katherine Carl continued work on the Empress Dowager's portrait for the St. Louis Exposition (032-009).

## Background

The Chinese New Year is the most important festival in the Chinese calendar, marked by the custom of settling all debts before the new year begins. As Carl observed, the Chinese consider it "tempting Heaven" to begin the New Year in debt, and people would dispose of valuables to clear their obligations (032-003). Silver imitation money was exchanged as an old custom believed to bring abundance (032-003).

Present-giving reached its peak at this season, with everyone from the highest official to the beggar at the gate receiving gifts. Their Majesties gave presents to all the Ladies and Princesses and to every inmate of the Palace (032-004). The presents exchanged at the New Year, however, were never as elaborate as those given for birthdays (032-004).

The 1904 New Year carried particular tension: the long-anticipated war between Russia and Japan had just begun, with Japanese forces already in Manchuria. The Empress Dowager was "mortally anxious" about how the conflict might affect China (032-004).

## Carl's Account

Carl witnessed the New Year observances at close quarters while resident at the Palace painting the Empress Dowager's portrait. She received gifts from the Empress Dowager, who sent her "two curiously fashoned" items (027-007).

Though Carl did not work on the portrait during the New Year festivities, the period brought significant developments. The Empress Dowager, after seeing the hands painted with fur undersleeves, ultimately agreed with Carl's suggestion to change their position, remarking that Carl's "idea about the position of the hands was not bad" and suggesting the left hand "would look well on a cushion" (032-007).

Immediately after the New Year festivities, the Court moved to the Sea Palace. This required Carl to relocate her studio once again. Despite her concerns about lost time with the St. Louis Exposition approaching, the move proved fortuitous — her new quarters fronting the lake, with upper glass windows installed, became "the best working-room I had ever had at any of the Palaces" (032-011).

## The Official Audience

A few days after the move to the Sea Palace, the Corps Diplomatique were received in formal audience to present New Year congratulations. The gentlemen were received first in the Great Audience Hall, while the ladies of the Legation were received the following day (032-012).

The ladies' reception took place in the Empress Dowager's Throne-room, which opened onto the Court of the large Theater at the Sea Palace. Because of the cold, the Theater court was entirely enclosed in glass, with each pane painted in red with the "Sho" character surrounded by five bats. The marble pavement and steps were carpeted in red (032-012).

Baron Czikan, the Austrian Minister and Doyen of the Corps Diplomatique, presented the ladies and delivered an address in French, wishing Their Majesties a Happy New Year and China prosperity. The address was translated into Chinese by a Secretary of the Wai-Wu-Pu. The Empress Dowager replied in Chinese, interpreted by His Excellency Liang Fang (032-013).

## The Lantern Festival

Following the diplomatic receptions came the Lantern Festival, featuring spectacular illuminated displays. A beautiful *pai-lou* of transparent gauze, painted in charming designs and illuminated from within, was hung with luminous flowers and lanterns (032-014).

Tall eunuchs in gala red held great lanterns aloft, while others maneuvered through corridors and courts with vermilion lanterns, forming intricate designs and luminous tableaux. They shaped their red-globed lanterns into Chinese characters and phrases of "felicitous omen" with remarkable accuracy (032-014).

After the lantern processions, illuminated dragons performed the struggle for the "flaming pearl." Carl investigated the origin of this Imperial legend and concluded: "The Double Dragon represents the Powers of Earth or Evil which try ever to absorb the Flaming Pearl, Emblem of the Dynasty, symbol of Heaven or Perfection" (032-015).

## The Fireworks

For a fortnight after the Lantern Festival, fireworks were set off every night on the banks of the lake. Their Majesties and the Ladies would dine in the Throne-room, then proceed through the garden paths to view the displays. Large sleds, normally used for skating on the frozen lake, served as viewing platforms (032-016).

The fireworks included elaborate set pieces — pagodas with ladies on balconies, pavilions with grapevines, wisteria arbors, and flower beds rendered so lifelike they "seemed to grow at the side of the luminous cascades" (032-017). Carl noted effects she had never before seen in fireworks. There were even daytime fireworks, during which paper devices — fish, dragons, animals, flags, and baskets — fell to the ground for the Empress Dowager's inspection (032-017).

## Outcome and Significance

The 1904 New Year celebrations demonstrated the Qing Court's continued adherence to traditional ceremonial life even amid mounting external pressures. The elaborate diplomatic receptions, conducted with careful protocol and bilingual interpretation, reflected the Court's efforts to maintain proper relations with the foreign Legations in the aftermath of the Boxer Rebellion (032-013).

The festival period also coincided with the Court's relocation to the Sea Palace, which Carl described as a welcome change from the Winter Palace's "small, shut-in courts, walled-in walks, and rigid traditions" (032-008). The move signaled the seasonal rhythm of Court life that governed even the Empress Dowager's portrait-painting schedule.
