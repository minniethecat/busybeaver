# Li Lien-ying

Li Lien-ying was the Empress Dowager Cixi's Chief Eunuch, a man of "exceptional ability" who wielded substantial influence within the Qing Court during the period of Katherine Carl's residence (1903–1904) (014-004).

## Role at the Chinese Court

The Chief Eunuch occupied "the first place" in the Court's administrative hierarchy beneath Their Majesties (014-002). Under his jurisdiction fell the vast corps of eunuchs who staffed the Imperial Household — a parallel bureaucracy that managed every aspect of the sovereign's daily life, from the removal of the portrait each day after painting (012-008) to the ceremonial presentation of entertainments (021-002). The Chief Eunuch served as the indispensable intermediary between the Empress Dowager and the world beyond her private apartments (014-003).

## Biographical Sketch

Little of Li Lien-ying's personal history appears in Carl's account. He emerges in the memoir as a fully formed figure of authority — experienced, watchful, and deeply embedded in the Court's power structure. Carl notes that the Empress Dowager's two Chief Eunuchs (one for her household and one for the Emperor's) "seem to have been chosen in a spirit of contrast" (014-006). Li Lien-ying's counterpart serving the Emperor was described as unlike him "as two people could well be" — implying that Li Lien-ying's particular combination of qualities had been deliberately selected by the Empress Dowager for her service (014-006).

## Interaction with the Author

Katherine Carl's interactions with the Chief Eunuch were frequent and varied. On her very first day at the Palace, she was met by the Chief Eunuch, who conducted her into the Empress Dowager's presence (001-006). Throughout her nine months of work on the portrait, the Chief Eunuch personally removed the painting from its easel at the end of each day's session — a ritual that underscored the portrait's status as an object of imperial concern (012-008). When Carl was cold, the Empress Dowager called to the Chief Eunuch to bring her a wrap, and he "soon returned with a quantity of magnificent pelisses" (012-011). On another occasion, when the launch carrying the Court moved too slowly, the Chief Eunuch intervened, "not wishing to run the risk of a ducking" (013-003).

## Character and Appearance

Carl's most sustained description of the Chief Eunuch appears in her chapter on the Court's internal hierarchy. Li Lien-ying, she writes, "is really of exceptional ability" — a judgment that carries weight given Carl's generally restrained tone (014-004). She describes him as a man who conducted much of his business "in a private way," suggesting a style of influence that relied on personal access rather than public display (014-004). Carl also acknowledges the darker reputation of the Chief Eunuchs: their "power and unscrupulousness" were widely discussed, and she notes that it "would be necessary to be on good terms with them" — a pragmatic observation about the realities of Court politics (014-012).


> The peculiar position of a Chinese Emperor, which shuts him in his Palace like a Buddha in a temple, makes some sort of confidential private messenger an absolute necessity. There is much business of an unofficial kind, which must be transacted in a private way. The Chief Eunuchs are naturally called upon in such cases.
>
> Her Majesty's Chief Eunuch, Li Lien Ying, is really of exceptional ability!
>
> (014-004)
## Historical Significance

Li Lien-ying represents a distinctive type in late Qing history: the powerful palace eunuch whose influence derived entirely from proximity to the sovereign. Unlike the civil officials who rose through the examination system or the Manchu princes who held office by birth, the Chief Eunuch's authority rested on the Empress Dowager's personal trust — a trust that Li Lien-ying evidently earned and maintained through decades of service (014-004). His presence in Carl's memoir offers Western readers a rare first-person account of the eunuch hierarchy at the apex of its influence, just before the Qing dynasty's collapse swept away the institutional world he inhabited.

## In the Narrative

The Chief Eunuch appears throughout Carl's memoir as a figure of quiet omnipresence. He is there at the first audience (001-006), at the painting sessions (012-008), on the imperial launch (013-003), at theatrical performances (015-009), and in the small moments of daily life — ordering a wrap (012-011), explaining the presence of visitors (015-025), ceremoniously removing the yellow silk cover from the finished portrait (018-013). He seldom speaks in Carl's account, but his actions — competent, discreet, and unfailingly attentive to the Empress Dowager's wishes — communicate his role more clearly than any direct description could.
