# Dragon Wall

The Dragon Wall is a famous landmark in [[peking|Peking]], situated near the thoroughfare that Katherine Carl traveled on her daily journey from the Legation Quarter to the Palace. Known for its decorative glazed-tile dragon motif, it is a characteristic example of Chinese imperial architectural ornament (011-006).

## Location and Context

The Dragon Wall stands along the route between the Legation Quarter and the [[imperial-city|Imperial City]]. Carl passed it during her travels through Peking, noting its position among the grounds where "most of the Chinese houses" were located (011-006).

The wall is situated in proximity to the [[tartar-city|Tartar City]] and the canal that runs through the Legation Quarter, forming part of the architectural landscape that Carl observed during her residence in Peking from 1903 to 1904.

## Description

The Dragon Wall is an example of the elaborately decorated screen walls (*yingbi*) that stand before the gates of important Chinese buildings. These walls traditionally feature a central design of dragons rendered in glazed ceramic tiles, their sinuous forms executed in brilliant colors against a deep ground. The dragon motif—the supreme symbol of imperial authority—marks the wall as belonging to a building or compound of the highest status.


> From this hall we were carried in our chairs along the banks of the lake, beyond the Marble Bridge to a distant part of the grounds, where stands the famous Dragon Wall. Most of the Chinese houses have a sort of stone screen opposite the principal gate of entrance. This screen, called "A Wall of Respect," often has some sort of painted or carved representation of a dragon, which is supposed to chase away the evil spirits.
>
> (011-006)
Carl referred to it simply as "the famous Dragon Wall," suggesting that it was already a recognized landmark in Peking by the early 1900s (011-006).

## Role in Court Life

The Dragon Wall did not have a direct role in Court ceremonies, but it served as a notable landmark along the route between the diplomatic quarter and the Palace. Carl likely passed it during her daily commutes from the [[american-legation|United States Legation]] to her studio within the Imperial City.

## In the Narrative

The Dragon Wall is mentioned in Chapter 11 during Carl's description of her observations of Peking. Its single reference in the narrative places it among the "grounds where stands the famous Dragon Wall," suggesting it was a point of local orientation and interest (011-006).

## Historical Significance

Dragon Walls (also known as Nine-Dragon Walls or *Jiulongbi*) are found at several imperial sites across China, including the Forbidden City and the [[summer-palace|Summer Palace]]. These glazed-tile screen walls served both a decorative and a spiritual function, believed to protect the buildings behind them from evil spirits—which, according to Chinese tradition, could only travel in straight lines. The Dragon Wall that Carl observed was likely one of several such structures that adorned the approaches to important buildings in late imperial Peking.
