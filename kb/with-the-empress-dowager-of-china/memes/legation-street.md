# Legation Street

Legation Street is a thoroughfare in the Legation Quarter of [[peking|Peking]], where new diplomatic compounds were constructed in the aftermath of the Boxer Rebellion. The [[american-legation|United States Legation]] was building its new premises on this street during Katherine Carl's residence in 1903–1904 (010-008).

## Location and Context

Legation Street lies within the Legation Quarter of the [[tartar-city|Tartar City]], near the canal and the [[water-gate|Water Gate]]. It became the focus of diplomatic construction in the years following the Boxer Rebellion, as foreign powers rebuilt and expanded their compounds after the destruction of 1900.

## Description

The street was the site of active construction during Carl's time in Peking. The United States was building a new Legation on Legation Street, while temporarily occupying a Chinese temple near the Water Gate that had been given to the U.S. Government after the Boxer Rebellion (010-008).

## Role in Court Life

Legation Street represented the expanding foreign presence in Peking. The construction of new Legations along this street symbolized the renewed and strengthened diplomatic foothold of the Western powers after the Boxer Protocol of 1901 granted them expanded privileges and a permanent guarded Legation Quarter.

## In the Narrative

Carl mentions Legation Street in Chapter 10, noting that the U.S. Legation's temporary home in a Chinese temple would serve until the "construction of the new Legation on Legation Street" was complete (010-008). This passing reference captures the transitional nature of foreign diplomatic life in Peking during the early 1900s.

## Historical Significance

Legation Street was part of the broader transformation of Peking's diplomatic infrastructure after 1901. The Boxer Protocol allowed foreign powers to station troops in the Legation Quarter and to fortify their compounds, effectively creating an extraterritorial enclave within the Imperial capital. Legation Street became the address of several major legations in the early 20th century.
