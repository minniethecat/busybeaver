# Propriety

The Confucian concept of ritual propriety (li) governing behavior at the Qing Court, including correct forms of address, gesture, and ceremony.

## Description

Propriety was a fundamental organizing principle of Qing Court life. Carl observed specific instances where ritual norms dictated behavior: an official could not raise his eyes to look directly at Her Majesty because it was not "according to the laws of propriety" (016-014). The Confucian classical tradition held "that 'it is by the rules of propriety that the character is established'" (022-016), making proper conduct not merely etiquette but a foundation of moral cultivation.

## Cultural Significance

For the Chinese, Carl observed, "propriety is a religion, and a thing that enters into every phase of their life" (025-008). This pervasive ritual consciousness shaped every interaction at Court, from the kowtow performed before the Imperial box (009-014) to the precise deportment required of officials in the Imperial presence (016-014). The codes of propriety governed rank, gesture, dress, and speech, creating a highly structured social environment that Carl, as a Western observer, found both fascinating and constraining.

## In Carl's Account

Carl, as a foreign artist granted unprecedented access to the Inner Court, documented these customs with an outsider's eye for detail. Her observations provide one of the few first-hand Western accounts of how Confucian ritual norms (022-016) structured daily life within the Forbidden City and Summer Palace.
