# Prince Ch'un

Prince Ch'un (醇親王, *Chún Qīnwáng*), known as the "Seventh Prince," was a prominent member of the Qing Imperial Family and the father of the [[emperor-kwang-hsu|Emperor Kwang-Hsu]]. His palace and its park at the [[summer-palace|Summer Palace]] served as Katherine Carl's residence during the nine months she painted the portrait of the [[empress-dowager|Empress Dowager]].

## Family and Lineage

Prince Ch'un was the seventh brother of the [[emperor-hsien-feng|Emperor Hsien-Feng]], the husband of the Empress Dowager Cixi, "hence his Chinese name of 'Seventh Prince'" (003-018). His wife — the mother of Emperor Kwang-Hsu — was the Empress Dowager's own sister, creating a dense web of kinship at the apex of Qing power.

The Prince's second son was chosen as the successor to the [[emperor-tung-chih|Emperor Tung-Chih]], who died childless. Carl explains that "the Chinese Emperors and their Council may choose the Successor to the Throne," and Prince Ch'un's second son was selected as "most suited for the exalted position" (003-018).

## Political Role

Carl describes him as "a most interesting personality" who was "a valued friend of the two Empresses, the present Empress Dowager and She of the [[eastern-palace|Eastern Palace]], while they were Co-Regents" (003-018). He was "recognized by foreigners, as well as by the Chinese, to be an enlightened Prince as well as a man of fine character" (003-018).

Until his death, he remained "one of the most trusted advisers of the Regency" (003-018).

## The Palace of the Emperor's Father

Carl's residence during her time at the Summer Palace was in Prince Ch'un's former pavilions — a group of buildings known as the Palace of the Emperor's Father. These pavilions had been the dwelling-place of the young Emperor Kwang-Hsu "after he had been chosen as Heir to the Throne and until he went to live regularly at the Imperial Palace" (003-003).

Carl discovered that the inscription on a stone at the entrance of a summerhouse in the park was a poem "written by no less a personage than the Seventh Prince himself" (003-005). He was a scholar-poet, and his verses were carved into the stones of the garden where he had rested and contemplated.

## Personal Character

The Prince was "a great lover of animals, and is said to have had the best kennels and stables of any of the Imperial Princes" (003-014). Carl found small tombstones in the park marking the resting-places of his favorite dogs and horses, each with an inscription extolling the virtues of the beloved animal.

## Original Text

> The Seventh Prince (Prince Ch’un) must have been a most interesting personality. He was brother to the Emperor Hsien-Feng, the husband of the present Empress Dowager; and his wife, the mother of the present Emperor, was Her Majesty’s sister. This Prince was a valued friend of the two Empresses, the present Empress Dowager and She of the Eastern Palace, while they were Co-Regents during the minority of the late and a part of that of the present Emperor, and he remained, up to the time of his death, one of the most trusted advisers of the Regency. He was recognized by foreigners, as well as by the Chinese, to be an enlightened Prince as well as a man of fine character.
>
> (003-018)
