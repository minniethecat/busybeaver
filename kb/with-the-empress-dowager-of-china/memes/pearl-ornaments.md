# Pearl Ornaments

Pearls were among the most prized ornaments at the Qing Court, used in Imperial costume, jewelry, and decorative arts. The Empress Dowager possessed "an immense number of pearls—for the pearl is her favorite jewel" (031-013).

## Description

The most spectacular use of pearls Carl witnessed was on the Empress Dowager's portrait gown, which was "richly embroidered in pearls" (001-011). Pearls were also a key element of Court Ladies' headdresses. The distinctive Manchu coiffure incorporated pearl ornaments (009-006), and Carl described the "veil of pearls" in the court coiffure as "most becoming" (028-011).

Freshwater pearls from Manchuria were particularly valued. Their use was restricted by sumptuary laws — only the Imperial family and highest-ranking nobles could wear certain sizes and quantities of pearls.

## In Carl's Account

Carl, as an artist, was attentive to the visual effect of pearls. She noted how they caught the light during the portrait sittings and described the overall impression of magnificence created by the accumulation of pearls, jade, and silk in Court costume. She recorded the Empress Dowager's famous pearl mantle (025-011), which was later "made over" after a change in fashion (028-002). The combination of pearl embroidery on Imperial yellow silk created what she considered the most striking visual effect at Court.

## Original Text

> She had been clothed in a gown of Imperial yellow, brocaded in the wistaria vine in realistic colors and richly embroidered in pearls. It was made, in the graceful Manchu fashion, in one piece, reaching from the neck to the floor; fastened from the right shoulder to the hem with jade buttons. At the top button, from the right shoulder, hung a string of eighteen enormous pearls separated by flat pieces of brilliant, transparent green jade. From the same button was suspended a large, carved pale ruby, which had yellow silk tassels terminating in two immense pear-shaped pearls of rare beauty!
>
> (001-011)
