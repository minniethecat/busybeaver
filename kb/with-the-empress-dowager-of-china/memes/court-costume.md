# Court Costume

The Court costume of the Qing dynasty was a rigidly codified system of ceremonial dress that visually marked every person's rank, status, and role. Katherine Carl, with her artist's eye for detail, documented this system extensively during her residency at the Palace.

## The Ladies' Court Dress

Carl described the summer Court costume worn at the [[emperor-birthday|Emperor's Birthday]]: "The Court costume of the married ladies is of dark red, embroidered in golden dragons. The widows wear blue; the unmarried girls, bright red — all with the [[double-dragon|Double Dragon]] embroidered thereon" (009-006).


> The Court costume of the Ladies is magnificent. That worn at the Emperor's Birthday (the summer costume) was of the stiff transparent silk I have described in the gown worn by Her Majesty for the portrait. The Court costume of the married ladies is of dark red, embroidered in golden dragons. The widows wear blue; the unmarried girls, bright red — all with the Double Dragon embroidered thereon.
>
> (009-006)
Color was the primary marker of status: "The married ladies and widows, when in Court attire, wear a magnificent court headdress with jeweled crown. The young girls, even in Court dress, wear the ordinary [[manchu-coiffure|Manchu coiffure]], with the long red silk tassels falling to their shoulders" (009-006).

## The Empress's Regalia

The young Empress's Court costume was the most elaborate: "Her headdress was of golden filigree, thickly set with jewels. Across the front, nine beautifully chased golden phoenix, with jeweled tails outspread, held in their bills strings of pearls that fell to her shoulders and veiled her forehead" (009-006).

"Her gown was of the [[imperial-yellow|Imperial yellow]], embroidered with the golden Double Dragon" (009-006). She wore "a solid piece of chased gold" around her neck, "the official beads that are always worn in Court dress by Princes and Officials and their wives," and a magnificently embroidered stole that "only worn by the wives of Emperors, during their husband's lifetime" (009-006).

## Rank and Distinction

The Empress Dowager, "always the most gorgeously attired person at Court," demonstrated her mastery of symbolic dress by wearing extreme simplicity at the Emperor's Birthday — "she wished the Emperor and Empress to be the central figures of this day's festivities, and did not wish to vie with the Empress even in her attire" (009-012).

## Enforcement

The Empress Dowager was "extremely rigid as to all the details of Court dress," inspecting each Lady's attire and "calling their attention to the way their official beads hung and signaling any little deviation from traditional forms that she noticed" (009-005).
