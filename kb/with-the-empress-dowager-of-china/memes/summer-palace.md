# Summer Palace

The Summer Palace (颐和园, *Yíhéyuán*), located about sixteen miles northwest of [[peking|Peking]] among the Western Hills, was the [[empress-dowager|Empress Dowager]] Cixi's "favorite residence" (017-002).

During 1903–1904, [[katherine-carl|Katherine Carl]] lived and worked at the Summer Palace for nearly nine months, painting the Empress Dowager's portrait and documenting the rhythms of imperial life. The Court resided "sixteen miles from the Capital" (001-005), and the Empress Dowager "occupies it from eight to nine months of the year" (024-002).

## Location and Context

The Summer Palace occupied a naturally dramatic setting. Carl described it as a "superb domain" whose "naturally picturesque situation among the beautiful Western Hills" (017-002) had been enhanced by centuries of landscape art. The approach from Peking took about three hours, passing through "fertile fields and market gardens" (001-003) and past "hills crowned with teahouses and temples" (001-004).

Unlike the [[sea-palace|Sea Palace]] and [[winter-palace|Winter Palace]], which lay within Peking's walls, the Summer Palace offered the Court isolation and natural beauty — and served as Carl's primary home during the portrait commission. "The Empress Dowager loved the Summer Palace and it always seemed a pleasure to her to return to it" (012-009).

## Grounds and Architecture

The Palace grounds formed a vast park-like domain. "The many buildings that constitute an Oriental Palace have been most picturesquely grouped on the banks of its great lake" (017-002), and "the eminences and natural undulations have all been made the most of as sites for Palaces and temples" (017-002).

The principal buildings were "massed in one great town-like group at the southeastern end of the lake" (017-003), including the Theater, the [[great-audience-hall|Great Audience Hall]], and the private apartments. "Palaces, temples, summerhouses, tea-booths, dot the whole surface of the great park, and all the vantage-points have been utilized for constructions" (017-003).

### The Lake and Marble Boat

The lake was the centerpiece of the grounds. Carl described "boating on the lake" (008-012) as a regular Court pastime, and recounted excursions "across the lake to one of the islands" (002-019). The Marble Boat, an elaborate white marble structure, served as a floating pavilion:

> This Marble Boat was built ostensibly for the Empress Dowager to take tea in while boating on the lake. (019-016)

### Gardens and Flora

Carl was struck by the horticultural artistry of the Chinese gardeners:

> Wherever available, in the grounds of the Summer Palace, flowers are planted, and they succeed each other almost the whole year round, for the Chinese are wonderful gardeners. (017-009)

The grounds featured a terraced hillside called the "[[flowery-mountain|Flowery Mountain]]," which in peony season became "one mass of blooms of exquisitely blended colors and faint evanescent perfume" (017-010). Chrysanthemums, asters, and even fields of wheat and millet were cultivated within the park — "it was curious to me to see how picturesque so prosaic a thing as a field of turnips might become" (017-009).

### Marble Bridge and Island

A graceful bridge connected the mainland to an island pavilion:

> A fair, verdure-clad island lies peacefully on the bosom of the lake, and the Palace and temple built thereon seem a part of the natural formation. A graceful seventeen-arched bridge of white marble connects this island with the northern bank of the lake. (017-006)

## Carl's Studio and Living Quarters

Carl was given her own pavilion as living quarters. She found that her "apartments occupied an entire pavilion" (001-027) set aside for her use, and a Throne-room was adapted as her studio: "the Throne-room that was now my studio" (035-004). This arrangement allowed her to work in comfort while remaining within the Palace compound.

The pavilion was near the Empress Dowager's own quarters, and Carl noted that "the pavilion at the Summer Palace where the Empress Dowager's dogs are kept" (006-011) was nearby — a detail that humanizes the otherwise formal setting.

## Daily Life at the Summer Palace

Carl's nine months at the Summer Palace gave her an intimate view of Court routine. She became "acquainted with the charms of the Summer Palace" (006-004) through daily walks, boat rides, and garden strolls. The Palace was not merely a workplace — it was her home.

The Court's social calendar followed the Palace's seasonal rhythms:

> New ladies were arriving at the Palace every day for a week before the Birthday (007-010).

During summer, "the large courts of the Summer Palace have roofs of matting erected" (007-009) to provide shade. Carl found that "the drives around the sixteen miles between the Summer Palace and Peking" (008-015) were a regular feature of Court life.

The Empress Dowager herself set the tone:

> Her Majesty loved the Summer Palace and it always seemed a pleasure to her to return to it. (012-009)

## Ruins and History

The grounds held layers of imperial history. On a slope above the lake lay the ruins of the old Summer Palace, destroyed by European troops in 1860:

> The ruins of the old Summer Palace, destroyed by the European troops fifty years ago… Her Majesty has nearly hidden all trace of the 1900 devastations to the Summer Palace, but these old ruins of the former Palace still remain, and they are not a blot upon the landscape. On the contrary, they have become picturesque with time. (017-015)

Carl observed that this area held painful memories for the Empress Dowager, who "had passed the early years of her married life in these now crumbling ruins" (017-015). The presence of these ruins within the otherwise idyllic grounds gave the Summer Palace a historical gravity that Carl clearly felt.

## View of the Outside World

From the Palace's highest elevations, the Court could glimpse the road from Peking — one of the few connections to the outside world available to the imperial women:

> From the highest elevations in the grounds of the Summer Palace, we could see the road from Peking! Sometimes Her Majesty and the Ladies would watch from some of the summerhouses, the carts and chairs and vehicles as they passed along. (017-016)

Carl noted that "these views of the high road from the eminences of the Summer Palace were all Her Majesty and the young Empress ever saw of the outside world and common humanity" (017-016) — a poignant observation about the cloistered nature of imperial life.

## Historical Significance

The Summer Palace was more than a residence — it was the primary stage for the Qing court's final years of splendor. Carl's memoir captures the Palace at a specific historical moment: after the Boxer Rebellion (1900), when the Court had returned from its flight to Xi'an, and before the 1911 Revolution that would end the dynasty.

The Palace represented the Empress Dowager's aesthetic and political domain — a world of ritual, beauty, and carefully managed encounters with the West, embodied in Carl herself. Through her detailed descriptions of "the beautiful grounds of the Summer Palace with its hills, valleys, canals, lakes, and gardens" (001-004), Carl preserved a record of this imperial landscape that would be largely inaccessible to outsiders within a decade.
