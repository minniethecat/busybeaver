# Tartar City

The Tartar City is one of the three walled sections of [[peking|Peking]], forming the inner district that houses the [[imperial-city|Imperial City]] at its center and the Legation Quarter along its southern edge. It was observed by Katherine Carl during her residence at the Chinese Court in 1903–1904.

## Location and Context

The Tartar City lies between the outer [[chinese-city|Chinese City]] to the south and the walls of the [[imperial-city|Imperial City]] to the north. The City of Peking is composed of three walled towns—the Chinese City, the Tartar City, and the Imperial City (024-005). The Legation Quarter of Peking lies within the Tartar City, situated directly beneath the walls of the Imperial City (025-002).

The [[water-gate|Water Gate]], opened by the Allies in 1900, connects the Legation Quarter to the canal at the left side of the district. Before this time, there was no gate between the Chien-Mên and the Hata-Mên, the two major gates in the southern wall of the Tartar City (025-002).

## Description

> The main thoroughfares of the Tartar City are very wide, with a raised causeway, about two feet high, in the center. When Their Majesties go abroad, this is covered with yellow sand and is used as an Imperial roadway. (025-004)

The raised causeway was always maintained in excellent condition. Carl observed that it appeared to be a survival of the elevated roads described by Marco Polo in his account of the Palace of Kublai Khan (025-004).

The lower roads flanking the causeway presented a stark contrast, generally existing in what Carl described as a "lamentable state." These streets hosted a vibrant street life:

| Street Activity | Description |
|----------------|-------------|
| Itinerant cooks | Frying goods and selling food in the open |
| Barbers | Shaving, manicure, and chiropody services in public view |
| Vendors | Selling old iron, clothes, and vegetables from the roadway |
| Animals | Wandering fowls, dogs, and pigs roaming freely |

The streets, despite pools of stagnant water and piles of refuse, retained a certain charm. Elaborately carved shop fronts, graceful signs with red pennants, gaily colored lanterns, and great umbrellas unfurled over vendors created a picturesque scene (025-004).

## Role in Court Life

The Tartar City served as the setting for diplomatic activity surrounding the Court. The [[american-legation|United States Legation]] occupied a Chinese temple on the canal within the Tartar City, at the left of the Water Gate. Katherine Carl stayed there during Court visits to Peking (025-002).

When the Court moved from the [[summer-palace|Summer Palace]] into Peking, Carl passed through the Tartar City on her way to and from the Legation. Her daily journey to the Palace took her past the [[british-legation|British Legation]], across the [[marble-bridge|Marble Bridge]], and along the narrow street beneath the great red walls of the Imperial City (025-003).

## In the Narrative

The Tartar City features most prominently in Chapter 25, when Carl begins the portrait of the [[empress-dowager|Empress Dowager Cixi]] destined for the St. Louis Exposition. Her studio was located in a hall within the Imperial City, and her daily commute across the Tartar City offered glimpses of Peking street life, official processions, and occasional encounters with figures such as [[prince-ching]] and the ministers of the Wai-Wu-Pu (025-005).

> All this we could see as we went on our way to the Great Gate of the Palace, itself. (025-006)

## Historical Significance

The Tartar City was the Manchu district of Peking, established after the Manchu conquest in the 17th century. The division of Peking into separate walled cities reflected the ethnic segregation imposed by the Qing Dynasty. After the fall of the Qing in 1912, the walls and gates that defined the Tartar City were gradually dismantled. Today, the area corresponds roughly to the central and eastern portions of modern Beijing's Dongcheng and Xicheng districts.
