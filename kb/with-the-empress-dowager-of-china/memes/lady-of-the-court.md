# Lady of the Court

The Ladies of the Court were the Manchu princesses, wives and daughters of nobles,
and attendants who resided within the Imperial Palace precincts during the Qing Dynasty.
[[katherine-carl|katherine carl]], during her nine months at the [[summer-palace|summer palace]] painting the
[[empress-dowager|empress dowager]]'s portrait, lived among these women and recorded detailed observations
of their appearance, customs, and daily life — providing one of the few Western accounts
of Qing court women from an insider's perspective.

## Composition of the Court

The female court comprised several distinct ranks:

- **The Young Empress**: The Emperor's consort, who acted as the senior lady of the court after the Empress Dowager
- **Princesses of the Blood**: Manchu imperial princesses, including the [[princess-imperial|princess imperial]]
- **Noble Consorts and Ladies-in-Waiting**: Wives and daughters of high Manchu officials
- **Court Attendants**: Lower-ranking women who assisted with daily routines

New ladies arrived at the Palace for major ceremonial occasions, particularly
the [[emperor-birthday|Emperor-Birthday]] and the [[empress-dowager-birthday|Empress-Dowager-Birthday]],
swelling the court to its fullest numbers.

## Manchu Women's Distinctiveness

Carl was struck by how Manchu women differed from their Han Chinese counterparts.
Unlike Chinese women of the period, Manchu women did not bind their feet and retained
their own distinctive costume and customs:

> The Manchu women have retained their own individuality; and today, after more
> than two hundred and fifty years in China, they still wear their native costume,
> entirely different from the Chinese women. They still dress their hair in the
> picturesque Manchu fashion. They not only have never bound their feet, but they
> have as great a horror of it as Europeans have. (009-029)

Carl considered Manchu women to have "more individual freedom than any other Oriental women,"
comparing them favorably to their counterparts in other Asian societies.

## Appearance and Dress

### The Court Coiffure

The elaborate headdress was the most distinctive element of Manchu court appearance.
The hair was drawn up over a satin wing-like construction, with the transition between
real hair and satin so seamless that "it is difficult to tell where it ends and the satin begins":

> A band of pearls, with an immense "flaming pearl" in the center, encircled the coil.
> On either side of the winged bow were bunches of natural flowers and a profusion
> of jewels. From the right side of the headdress hung a tassel of eight strings
> of beautiful pearls reaching to the shoulder. (005-002)

### Court Costume

The [[silk-court-dress|court costume]] worn at ceremonial occasions was magnificent,
particularly the summer costume worn at the Emperor's Birthday. Rich embroidered silks
in prescribed colors indicated the wearer's rank and status. The [[imperial-yellow|Imperial Yellow]]
was reserved exclusively for the Empress Dowager; other members of the court wore
designated colors according to their position.

### Manchu Shoes

The distinctive [[manchu-shoes|Manchu platform shoes]], with their six-inch-high
central sole, gave the ladies their characteristic gait. Carl marveled at how the
Empress Dowager could climb steep theater stairs in these shoes "with as much ease
and lightness as I did" in European footwear.

## Daily Life

The Ladies of the Court followed a structured daily routine centered on attendance
to the [[empress-dowager|empress dowager]]:

- **Morning**: The ladies assembled to greet Her Majesty and accompany her to audiences or on walks through the Palace gardens
- **Afternoon**: Attendance at theatrical performances, meals with the Empress Dowager, or leisure time in the flower-filled courts
- **Evening**: Further attendance or private time in their apartments

Flowers and gardening were a central pleasure. The ladies had freedom to gather flowers
and fruits from the Palace gardens, but court etiquette forbade them from touching any
plant or fruit in the presence of the Empress Dowager unless specifically invited to do so.

## Etiquette and Deportment

The Ladies of the Court observed elaborate rules of propriety in their interactions
with Their Majesties. When receiving an official command from the Empress Dowager,
even a Princess of the Blood would drop to her knees. Yet in private conversation,
the same Princess might speak to the Empress Dowager almost familiarly while standing.

The distinction Carl observed was revealing:

> In the Audience Hall when administering justice, they are not approached nor
> addressed, except upon bended knee. In the Palace, in their own privacy...
> when Their Majesties are in their private capacity and spoken to on ordinary
> affairs, they are addressed almost familiarly. (034-006)

## Interactions with Carl

Carl's presence at Court was a novel experience for both parties. For most of the
Manchu ladies, Carl was the first foreigner they had ever met:

> This was, in most instances, the first meeting of these Princesses with any
> foreigner; but they were uniformly courteous and even cordial, never evincing
> the slightest curiosity as to my dress or my habits. (009-011)

Carl was impressed by their breeding and discretion. Even the children at Court
"were as well-bred as their elders in their treatment of the 'foreign lady'."
She noted with some irony that "a Chinese at a European Court, or at our White House"
might not have been treated with the same consideration.

## The Boxer Rebellion Conversations

The [[boxer-rebellion|boxer rebellion]] was a frequent topic of conversation among the Court Ladies.
Unlike many sensitive subjects, the uprising was "not considered at all indiscreet to ask
questions on," and Carl learned much about the Court's perspective on the conflict
from these conversations. The ladies spoke candidly about the siege of the Legations
and the foreign occupation of Peking, providing Carl with material that she later
incorporated into her memoir.
