# Embroidery

The decorative needlework art used extensively in Qing Court costume, from gold embroidery on Court Ladies' dresses to silk thread designs on shoes and robes.

## Description

Embroidery was a ubiquitous craft at the Qing Imperial Court. Carl observed palace attendants holding "a number of strips of embroidery in his hand," pieces worked in elaborate designs (010-011). During theatrical performances the lantern light "showed upon the jewels and gold embroidery of their costumes" (018-007), illuminating the richness of court attire.

The Princesses' handiwork was a constant presence at the Palace:

> The Princesses did a great deal of beautiful embroidery, making their own shoes, which are of exquisitely embroidered satin, but they could not do this at night, for only candles are used in the two Peking Palaces.
>
> (028-010)

## Cultural Significance

Embroidery was a skill practiced by women across the Court hierarchy. Carl noted that Court Ladies "did a great deal of beautiful embroidery, making their own shoes, which were very artistic" (028-010). The craft was not merely decorative but integral to personal adornment: "These pieces of ribbon and embroidery come in lengths for one dress" (031-014), suggesting that embroidered elements were designed and produced as coordinated costume components.

## In Carl's Account

Carl, as a foreign artist granted unprecedented access to the Inner Court, documented these crafts with an outsider's eye for detail. Her observations provide one of the few first-hand Western accounts of the textile arts practiced daily within the Forbidden City and Summer Palace, from the gold embroidery of theatrical costumes (018-007) to the hand-stitched shoes made by Court Ladies (028-010).
