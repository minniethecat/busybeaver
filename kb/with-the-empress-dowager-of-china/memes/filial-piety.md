# Filial Piety

Filial piety (孝, *xiào*) is the Confucian virtue of reverence, obedience, and devotion toward one's parents and ancestors. In Katherine Carl's memoir, the concept appears as a defining characteristic of the [[emperor-kwang-hsu|Emperor Kwang-Hsu]], cited in both Chinese official praise and Carl's own observations of Court life.

## Confucian Foundation

Filial piety is the foundational virtue of Confucian ethics. It extends beyond the parent-child relationship to structure the entire hierarchy of social relations: the devotion of subject to ruler, wife to husband, younger to elder. In the Imperial context, the Emperor's filial devotion to his mother—the [[empress-dowager|Empress Dowager]]—was both a personal virtue and a political principle, legitimizing her continued exercise of power.

## In Carl's Account

Carl presents the Emperor's filial piety as one of his most admirable qualities. She cites [[chang-chih-tung|Chang Chih-tung's]] famous ode: "Who does not admire the filial reverence and piety with which he waits upon his august mother?" (036-052). The Viceroy's poem goes on to praise the Emperor's devotion in explicitly Confucian terms, grounding his legitimacy in his virtue as a son.

Carl herself observes the Emperor's filial behavior during Court ceremonies: at the [[emperor-birthday|Emperor's Birthday]] celebration, the [[chinese-new-year-court|Chinese New Year]], and other occasions where the ritualized deference of son to mother was publicly enacted. She notes that the custom of adoption carries the same filial obligations as biological parenthood in Chinese law and custom (036-052).

The concept also appears in Carl's broader characterization of Chinese society, where "the worship of ancestors" and "the duty of children to their parents" form the ethical backbone of family and state alike.

## Significance

Filial piety was not merely a private virtue but a political principle that structured the late Qing Court. The Empress Dowager's authority over the Emperor was justified through filial obligation: she ruled as his mother (technically, his aunt and adoptive mother), and his deference to her was framed as the highest expression of Confucian virtue rather than political subordination.

## 原文引述

The Viceroy [[chang-chih-tung|Chang Chih-Tung]], in his famous ode to the Emperor, extolled filial piety as the sovereign's defining virtue:

> Who does not admire the filial reverence and piety with which he waits upon his august mother? Setting a brilliant example to all, he inquires early and late after her well-being and watches over her meals in person. Let us now add a new ode, extolling to the skies our Emperor's fidelity to his Imperial mother.
>
> (036-063)

Carl explains that the custom of kneeling before one's parents on their birthdays is universal in China, and that "should the Emperor fail to do so, the whole of China would be horrified and cry out against his unfilial conduct." (036-063)
