# The Boxer Rebellion's Aftermath at the Qing Court

When [[katherine-carl|katherine carl]] arrived at the [[summer-palace|summer palace]] in August 1903, the
[[boxer-rebellion|boxer rebellion]] and its traumatic aftermath were barely three years past.
The ruins of foreign-destroyed pavilions still dotted the Palace grounds.
The diplomatic wounds were fresh. And the [[empress-dowager|empress dowager]], who had fled
Peking during the Allied advance, was navigating a precarious path between
maintaining imperial dignity and accommodating the Western powers who had
humiliated her empire.

Carl's memoir captures this transitional moment with unique intimacy. She was
close enough to see the physical scars of 1900, sufficiently trusted to hear
the Court's perspective on the conflict, and foreign enough to represent the
very powers that had occupied Peking. Her account illuminates the Qing Court's
recovery strategy: a combination of architectural restoration, diplomatic
outreach, and symbolic gestures — including the portrait commission itself —
designed to rehabilitate China's standing and Cixi's reputation.

## Visible Scars

### The Damaged Palaces

Physical evidence of the foreign occupation was everywhere in the Imperial
precincts. The [[palace-of-the-emperors-father|Palace of the Emperor's Father]], assigned to Carl as her
residence, had been "much injured by the foreign troops in 1900 and had been
unoccupied since" (010-002). It was "hastily restored and refurnished for our
occupation, but many of the pavilions and summerhouses in the grounds were
in ruins, and the stables but partly rebuilt."

The [[temple-of-the-ten-thousand-buddhas|Temple of the Ten Thousand Buddhas]] in the [[sea-palace|sea palace]] grounds was
another casualty:

> This was one of the temples so ruthlessly destroyed and unnecessarily
> desecrated by the Allies during their occupation of Peking. (010-020)

When Carl visited, workmen were still "repairing and regilding the Great Buddha,"
and "most of the effigies of the saints and images of the personified attributes
were standing in dejected rows in the corridors awaiting the completion of their
niches and chapels."

The [[winter-palace|winter palace]] had also suffered: "foreign troops were stationed in
the Peking palaces and so much damage done them" (024-002). Carl noted that
after the occupation, "the whole Palace had been renovated and restored" by 1903,
but the memory of desecration lingered.

### The Emotional Toll

Beyond the physical damage, Carl sensed the emotional weight the occupation
had left on the Court. The [[empress-dowager|empress dowager]], who had returned from her flight
to Xi'an to find her palaces occupied by foreign soldiers, bore the humiliation
with what Carl described as characteristic resilience — but the tension was
palpable.

The Boxer Rebellion was "a frequent topic of conversation at the Palace," and
Carl "heard a great deal about it from the Ladies of the Court" (001-005).
Unlike many sensitive subjects, the uprising was "not considered at all
indiscreet to ask questions on" — the Court Ladies seemed eager to tell their
side of the story to a sympathetic foreign listener.

## The Diplomatic Recovery

### The Portrait as Diplomacy

Carl's portrait commission was itself a diplomatic instrument. [[mrs-conger|Mrs. Conger]]
conceived it explicitly as a tool for reshaping Western opinion:

> She also felt, as she had had an opportunity of seeing a good deal of
> the Empress Dowager, that if the world could see a true likeness of her,
> it might modify the generally accepted idea which prevailed as to Her
> Majesty's character. [P04-001]

The [[empress-dowager|empress dowager]], for her part, used the portrait sittings as occasions
for informal diplomacy. Through Carl — and through the [[lady-yu-keng|Yu-Keng
ladies]] who interpreted — Cixi communicated a carefully calibrated image of
herself: cultivated, intelligent, curious about the West, and unjustly maligned
by foreign opinion.

The decision to send the portrait to the [[st-louis-exposition|St. Louis Exposition]] extended this
diplomatic logic to an international stage. The 1904 World's Fair was an
opportunity for China to present a modern, civilized face to the world — and
Cixi's portrait, painted by an American artist in Western style, was the
centerpiece of that effort.

### Foreign Guests at Court

Carl was not the only foreigner at the Qing Court during this period. The
[[empress-dowager|empress dowager]] had begun a cautious program of diplomatic hospitality:

- Mrs. Conger was received in private audience — an unprecedented honor for
  a foreign woman
- The wives of other foreign diplomats were occasionally invited to Palace events
- The diplomatic corps in Peking maintained relations with the
  [[wai-wu-pu|Wai-Wu-Pu]] (Foreign Office), though formal audiences with the Emperor
  remained rare

Carl's nine-month residency was the most extended example of this new openness.
That the "Dragon Empress" — who had been demonized in the Western press as the
architect of the Boxer atrocities — would invite an American woman to live in
her Palace and paint her portrait was a diplomatic statement in itself.

### The Congers' Role

The American Minister [[conger|Edwin H. Conger]] and his wife Sarah were key
intermediaries in the post-Boxer diplomatic landscape. During the siege of the
Legations in 1900, the Congers had been among the besieged foreigners — and had
survived. Their return to Peking and the establishment of cordial relations with
the Empress Dowager was one of the more remarkable diplomatic reversals of the
period.

The [[american-legation|american legation]], housed in a former Chinese temple near the Water Gate,
became a bridge between the Imperial Court and the foreign community. The Congers
dispensed "kindly favors" to visitors, and the Legation's shaded court — filled
with "beautiful, growing flowers (many of them gifts from the Empress Dowager to
Mrs. Conger)" [024-XXX] — was a symbolic space where Chinese and American
diplomacy intermingled.

## The Empress Dowager's Strategy

Cixi's post-Boxer strategy can be reconstructed from Carl's account. It had
several components:

1. **Restoration of the Palaces**: Physical repair as symbolic renewal —
   showing that the dynasty had survived the catastrophe
2. **Diplomatic hospitality**: Selective engagement with foreign representatives,
   particularly women, who could serve as informal ambassadors
3. **Image management**: The portrait commission, the memoir (tacitly approved),
   and carefully staged public appearances designed to counter the "Dragon Empress"
   caricature
4. **Continuity of ceremony**: The elaborate rituals Carl witnessed — the
   [[emperor-birthday|Emperor-Birthday]], the [[empress-dowager-birthday|Empress-Dowager-Birthday]],
   the [[chinese-new-year-court|Chinese-New-Year-Court]] observances — were assertions that the
   cosmic and social order remained intact despite the trauma of 1900

This was not a strategy of defiance. It was a strategy of resilience — an
attempt to preserve imperial authority by adapting to new realities rather
than resisting them.

## What Carl's Account Leaves Out

Carl's memoir, for all its detail, has significant silences about the Boxer
aftermath:

- **The Court's flight to Xi'an** (1900): Carl arrived in 1903 and could only
  report what she was told. The experience of the Imperial family's desperate
  flight across China — a profound humiliation — is absent from her account.
- **The indemnity negotiations**: The Boxer Protocol (1901) imposed crushing
  reparations on China. Carl does not discuss the financial dimensions of
  the aftermath.
- **The execution of Boxer supporters**: The protocol required the punishment
  of officials who had supported the Boxers. These internal purges are not
  mentioned in the memoir.
- **The role of [[prince-ching|Prince Ching]] and [[prince-kung|Prince Kung]]**: These key figures in
  the post-Boxer diplomatic settlement are described by Carl but their
  political roles are understated.

These silences are partly a function of Carl's position — she was a guest, not
an investigator — and partly a function of what the Court chose to show her.
The memoir captures what Cixi wanted the West to see: a restored, dignified,
and welcoming Court. The darker realities of the aftermath remained behind
the Palace walls.

## Legacy

The post-Boxer recovery strategy Carl witnessed was partially successful.
The portrait at St. Louis was well received; the memoir provided a sympathetic
counter-narrative; and diplomatic relations between China and the Western
powers stabilized. But the underlying tensions — foreign encroachment,
dynastic weakness, internal reform pressures — continued to build, erupting
in the 1911 Revolution that ended the Qing Dynasty just six years after
Carl's residency.

Carl's account preserves the moment before that collapse — a moment when
the Qing Court, battered but unbowed, was making its last sustained effort
to engage with the modern world on its own terms.
