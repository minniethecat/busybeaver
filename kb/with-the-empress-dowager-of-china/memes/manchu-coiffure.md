# Manchu Coiffure

The Manchu coiffure was the distinctive hairstyle worn by Manchu women
of the Qing dynasty, particularly at the imperial court in [[peking|Peking]].
[[katherine-carl|Katherine Carl]] devoted detailed attention to this elaborate arrangement
of hair, flowers, jewels, and silk tassels,
recognizing it as a key marker of ethnic identity, marital status, and court rank.

## Description

The Manchu coiffure was fundamentally different from the Han Chinese hairstyle.
Where Han women gathered their hair into a chignon at the nape of the neck,
Manchu women arranged theirs in a wide, winged structure
that spread horizontally from the temples —
a style known in Chinese as *liangbatou* ("two-handfuls hairstyle").

Carl observed that the ladies of the court
"wear the ordinary Manchu coiffure, with the long red silk tassels
hanging down from it" (009-006) — the tassels being a distinctive feature
that marked the wearer's connection to the Manchu ruling elite.

Seasonal variation was built into the style:

> The picturesque summer coiffure had also been replaced by winter hats
> of fur with jewels across the front and an elaborate crown, studded
> with precious stones. (023-007)

Flowers were an essential element. The [[empress-dowager|Empress Dowager]]
"wears natural flowers in her coiffure always, winter and summer,"
Carl noted, adding that this was "an unusual thing in North China
at that season of the year." (004-014) The use of fresh flowers
in winter was a mark of imperial privilege,
made possible by the heated greenhouses of the Palace gardens.

## Cultural Significance

The Manchu coiffure was more than fashion — it was a visual declaration
of ethnic and political identity. After the Manchu conquest of China in 1644,
the Qing rulers imposed their hairstyles on the subject population as a sign of submission.
For Han men, this meant the infamous queue (pigtail);
for Manchu women, the winged coiffure marked their status
as members of the conquering elite.

At the late-Qing court Carl visited, these distinctions had softened
but not disappeared. The coiffure remained "an important part of the dress
and ceremony of the Palace," and "no Manchu lady's coiffure is considered complete
without the long tassels and ornaments" that tradition prescribed. (015-018)

## In Carl's Account

Carl was a keen observer of the coiffure in its most formal incarnation.
She described the "Court coiffure" worn by married ladies
on ceremonial occasions:

> These brides are always magnificently dressed in embroidered gowns
> of rich colors, and wear, for the first time, the Court coiffure of
> the married ladies, the magnificent golden filigree, jeweled
> construction, which I have already described, and for this occasion
> they wear a profusion of jewels. (026-003)

The pearl veil was a particularly striking feature.
For the highest-ranking ladies, strings of pearls were suspended
from the coiffure to form a shimmering curtain that framed the face:

> I did so, and there, on a throne, sat the young Empress in full
> Court dress, wearing the Court coiffure, with its veil of pearls,
> which was most becoming to her narrow patrician face. (028-011)

The [[empress-dowager|Empress Dowager]] herself, however, was often seen
"in her ordinary dress and without the Manchu coiffure,
which she only wears at her audiences" (020-003) —
a detail that humanizes the ruler and shows the coiffure as formal regalia
rather than everyday wear.

## Historical Context

The Manchu coiffure originated in the pre-conquest traditions
of the Manchu tribes of Manchuria, where practicality dictated
a hairstyle that kept hair clear of the face during riding and hunting.
Over the course of the Qing dynasty (1644–1911), it evolved
from a relatively simple arrangement into the elaborate winged form
Carl witnessed at court.

After the 1911 Revolution, the Manchu coiffure — like the queue —
vanished rapidly from Chinese life. It was too closely associated
with the discredited Qing regime to survive as a fashion.
Carl's detailed description is therefore one of the last
Western eyewitness accounts of this distinctive tradition
in its imperial context.
