# Kublai Khan

Kublai Khan (1215–1294) was the Mongol Emperor who founded the Yuan Dynasty in China. Grandson of Genghis Khan, he completed the Mongol conquest of China and established his capital at Khanbaliq (modern [[peking|Peking]]). His reign is referenced in the book through Imperial traditions and the historical landscape of the capital.

## In the Imperial Poetry Tradition

During the [[emperor-kwang-hsu|Emperor Kwang-Hsu]]'s birthday celebrations, a poem composed by the Imperial Herald of the time of Kublai Khan was recited. This poem was most impressive, chanted in the measured, sonorous, and strongly rhythmic manner of the classics. The connection to Kublai Khan's era demonstrates the continuity of Chinese Imperial ceremony across dynasties. (009-014)

Carl describes the theatrical performance at the Emperor's Birthday:

> When Their Majesties and the Empress were seated in their loge, the principal actors came to the front of the stage, knelt, and kowtowed to the Imperial box. Then the play began. There was first a noisy burst of weird music, then the chief actor recited a laudatory, congratulatory poem in honor of the Birthday of the Emperor, wishing His Majesty "ten thousand years" of happiness and all the blessings of the gods.
>
> (009-014)

## The Palace of Kublai Khan

The grounds of the [[summer-palace|Summer Palace]] occupied land that once formed part of the grounds of the Palace of Kublai Khan. The lower roads on either side of the Palace lake ran through these historic grounds, linking the Qing Imperial residence to the deeper Mongol Imperial past of the capital region. (025-004)

## Legacy

Though only briefly mentioned in the narrative, Kublai Khan represents the deep historical strata underlying Qing Imperial culture. The Yuan Dynasty that he founded established Peking as the Imperial capital, a status it retained through the Ming and Qing dynasties and into the modern era.
