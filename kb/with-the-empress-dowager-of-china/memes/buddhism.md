## 中国佛教与朝廷

Buddhism is one of the three great religions of China, alongside [[taoism|Taoism]] and the worship of Nature. (029-002) The [[emperor-kwang-hsu|Emperor of China]], as the Son of Heaven, is not only the head of one church but of all the churches in China — he is the Great High Priest of Heaven, the High Priest of Buddhism and Taoism, and is also a Confucian. (013-004)

All the great ceremonies of the different cults, including Buddhist rites, are celebrated in the Palace temples with "rigid impartiality and equal pomp." Whatever may be the individual leanings of the Emperor, he participates in each of these celebrations. (013-004)

## 佛教的中国化

When Buddhism was first brought into China from India, it underwent a distinctly Chinese transformation. As the narrative observes, the adaptation was "typically Chinese" — the religion was reshaped to fit the Chinese temperament and aesthetic sensibilities. (010-019) This sinicization of Buddhism is emblematic of China's broader pattern of absorbing and transforming foreign influences while maintaining its essential cultural identity.

## 佛教在晚清的面貌

By the time of the [[empress-dowager|Empress Dowager]]'s reign, Buddhism (like Taoism) had become, in the narrator's assessment, "more or less outward forms" — "empty shells which may once have contained the Spirit, but have now become mere conventional representations of ancient rites." (029-011) The doctrines of Buddha had "become so encrusted with error in China as to afford no moral or ideal help to their followers." (029-011)

Despite this decline in spiritual vitality, Buddhist ceremonies continued to be performed with elaborate ritual at Court, with the Emperor assisting at Buddhist ceremonies and encouraging the cult alongside Taoism with impartiality. (029-010) The great festivals and fasts of Buddhism were all celebrated within the Palace walls.

## 佛教寺庙与景观

The Buddhist presence at the Summer Palace was most visible in landmarks such as the [[temple-of-the-ten-thousand-buddhas|Temple of the Ten Thousand Buddhas]], situated on a hill overlooking the lake. Buddhist temples, with their distinctive architecture, formed part of the rich religious landscape of Imperial Peking, alongside the [[temple-of-heaven|Temple of Heaven]] (Nature worship) and numerous other sacred sites.

> The Emperor has all the religions of his Empire under his protection and is their nominal Head. He assists indiscriminately at Buddhist or Taoist ceremonies, and encourages with impartiality both cults. All the festivals and fasts of both are celebrated in the Palace.
>
> (029-010)
