# Imperial Palaces of the Qing Court

The Qing Imperial Court maintained three principal palaces in and around
[[peking|Peking]], each serving a distinct role in the seasonal and ceremonial
rhythm of court life. [[katherine-carl|katherine carl]], who resided at all three palaces
during her nine months painting the [[empress-dowager|empress dowager]]'s portrait, provided
the most detailed Western account of their architecture, interiors, and
the different character of court life at each.

## The Three Palaces

### Summer Palace (Yiheyuan)

The [[summer-palace|summer palace]], located outside Peking's walls, was the
[[empress-dowager|empress dowager]]'s favorite residence and the primary setting of Carl's memoir.
It was here that Carl had her [[first-audience|First Audience]], painted the Empress Dowager's
portrait, and witnessed the [[emperor-birthday|Emperor-Birthday]] celebrations.

The Summer Palace was a landscape palace — a vast park centered on Kunming Lake,
with pavilions, temples, bridges, and halls scattered across wooded hills.
Carl described it as a place where "Nature and art are everywhere so blended,
so harmonized, it is difficult to tell which is which" [017-XXX].

Key features:
- **The Lake and Marble Boat**: A two-story European-style marble pavilion on the lake
- **The Long Corridor**: A covered walkway running along the lake shore
- **The Theater**: A three-story palace theater where the Empress Dowager watched performances
- **The Peony Mountain (Flowery Mountain)**: Terraced hillsides planted with peonies
- **The Mat-Sheds**: Elaborate temporary roofs erected over courtyards in summer, taken down in September

The Summer Palace operated in a seasonal rhythm. The court moved there in late spring,
and the mat-sheds were "put up in June and taken down in September." Carl noted that
"Her Majesty seemed to be the first figure at the Summer Palace" (024-029) — it was
her personal domain, where the formal constraints of court protocol relaxed slightly.

The palace had been extensively damaged during the [[boxer-rebellion|boxer rebellion]] and the foreign
occupation of Peking. Carl observed ruins throughout the grounds — "many of the pavilions
and summerhouses in the grounds were in ruins, and the stables but partly rebuilt" (010-002).
The Empress Dowager had begun restoration, but evidence of the destruction remained visible.

### Sea Palace (Zhongnanhai)

The [[sea-palace|sea palace]], located within Peking's walls adjacent to the [[imperial-city|Imperial City]],
served as an intermediate residence between the Summer and Winter Palaces.
The court moved there in autumn as the weather cooled.

The Sea Palace was built around a series of lakes, with halls and pavilions set
among willow-lined shores. It contained the three great halls of state — including
the Throne-room where Carl first saw the "spirit-stairway" — and the
[[temple-of-the-ten-thousand-buddhas|Temple of the Ten Thousand Buddhas]], which Carl visited and described in detail.

The Sea Palace combined elements of both the Summer Palace's landscape aesthetic
and the Winter Palace's formal state functions. It was here that the
[[empress-dowager-birthday|Empress-Dowager-Birthday]] was celebrated in November 1903.

### Winter Palace (Forbidden City / Zijincheng)

The [[winter-palace|winter palace]] — the Purple Forbidden City — was the ceremonial and
administrative heart of the Qing Empire. Located within the [[imperial-city|Imperial City]],
surrounded by moats and massive red walls, it was the most formally constrained
of the three palaces.

Carl found the approach labyrinthine: "even after entering the exterior gates,
one winds in and out between high walls, through massive gates and heavy wooden
doors" (024-005). Inside, she was struck by the contrast between the "somber
splendor" of the great domed Throne-room and the bright, flower-filled
sitting-room of the Empress Dowager.

Tradition was "more rigidly observed here than at the Summer Palace" (024-029).
At the Winter Palace, "everything seemed to be referred to the Emperor, whereas
Her Majesty seemed to be the first figure at the Summer Palace." The confined
layout — "walled-in courts and the shut-in walks, between high walls" — offered
little scope for the outdoor promenades the Empress Dowager enjoyed elsewhere.

Carl used one of the Emperor's Throne-rooms as her studio during the winter months,
and it was here that she completed work on the portrait destined for the
[[st-louis-exposition|st. louis exposition]].

## Seasonal Rhythm

The Qing Court's movement between palaces followed a seasonal calendar:

| Season | Palace | Character |
|--------|--------|-----------|
| **Late Spring–Early Autumn** | Summer Palace | Landscape, leisure, informal |
| **Autumn** | Sea Palace | Transitional, lakeside, ceremonial |
| **Winter–Early Spring** | Winter Palace | Formal, administrative, constrained |

This seasonal migration was not merely a matter of comfort — it was a ritual
cycle that structured the year's ceremonies. Imperial birthdays, New Year
observances, and diplomatic audiences were distributed across the three palaces
according to tradition. The [[emperor-birthday|Emperor-Birthday]] (August) was
celebrated at the Summer Palace; the [[empress-dowager-birthday|Empress-Dowager-Birthday]] (November) at
the Sea Palace; the [[chinese-new-year-court|Chinese-New-Year-Court]] observances at the Winter Palace.

## Architectural Comparison

The three palaces represented three distinct modes of Chinese palatial architecture:

| Feature | Summer Palace | Sea Palace | Winter Palace |
|---------|---------------|------------|---------------|
| **Setting** | Landscape park | Lakeside enclosure | Walled city compound |
| **Scale** | Expansive, horizontal | Medium, waterside | Vertical, enclosed |
| **Character** | Garden-pavilion | Lake-pavilion | Courtyard-hall |
| **Roof style** | Varied (yellow, green, blue tiles) | Varied | Yellow imperial tiles |
| **Relationship to nature** | Integrated | Framed | Excluded (walled courts) |
| **Primary axis** | The lake and Long Hill | The lakes | The central north-south axis |

What united them was the underlying architectural system Carl analyzed — the
tent-derived pavilion form, the courtyard grouping, the elaborate roof
ornamentation, and the harmonious color palette that made each building
seem "a part of Nature itself" [017-XXX].

## Carl's Experience Across the Three

Carl's movement between the three palaces traced the seasonal arc of her
nine-month residency. She arrived at the Summer Palace in August 1903 for
her first audience; witnessed the Emperor's Birthday there; moved to the
Sea Palace for the Empress Dowager's Birthday in November; and spent the
winter months at the Winter Palace before moving back to the Sea Palace
in spring 1904.

Each move required relocating her studio — a logistical challenge that
disrupted her work on the portrait. "I had just begun to progress with my work"
at the Winter Palace when the move to the Sea Palace was ordered, and the
St. Louis Exposition deadline loomed (032-009).

## Historical Significance

The triple-palace system of the Qing Court was not merely a matter of
architectural grandeur — it was a spatial expression of imperial authority.
Each palace projected a different face of monarchy: the Summer Palace showed
the cultivated, personal Cixi; the Sea Palace bridged leisure and state; the
Winter Palace asserted dynastic power and cosmic order.

Carl's account preserves this system at a moment of transition. The palaces
she described — damaged by the Boxer occupation, under restoration, hosting
unprecedented foreign guests — were adapting to new political realities even
as they maintained the "immutable" traditions of the Qing Court.
