# Princess Schun

Princess Schun was a Manchu princess at the Imperial Court during the early 20th century. She was the widow of [[yung-lu|Yung Lu]], the Grand Secretary, and the sister of the young Empress, consort of the [[emperor-kwang-hsu|Emperor Kwang-Hsu]]. Katherine Carl describes her as "the clever Princess Schun" (026-005).

## Family and Position

Princess Schun occupied a prominent position in the Court's social hierarchy through her dual connections: her late husband, Yung Lu, had been one of the most powerful officials in the Qing government, and her sister was the Empress consort. Carl describes her personal tragedy: she "lost, in one year, her husband and a promising son, and who was nearly crazed with grief" (026-005).

Despite her personal losses, Princess Schun maintained her place in the circle of [[lady-of-the-court|court ladies]] who surrounded the Empress Dowager. Carl includes her among "the secondary wives and their daughters, and the sisters of the young Empress" (026-005) who composed the ladies' precincts.

## In Carl's Account

Carl's characterization of Princess Schun as "clever" suggests a woman of notable intelligence and wit, a figure who had navigated both personal tragedy and the complex politics of the late Qing Court. The Princess represents the inner circle of Manchu aristocratic women whose lives were otherwise invisible to Western observers.

Carl met the wives and families of the Manchu elite at the Court:

> I met the wives and families of all the Princes, nobles, and high Manchu officials in Peking, for they came to the Court at stated intervals, besides on many special occasions, when they were invited by Her Majesty. The most frequent of these visitors to the Palace were Prince Ching's wives and daughters, the wives of the Emperor's brothers, his father's secondary wives and their daughters, and the sisters of the young Empress.
>
> (026-002)
