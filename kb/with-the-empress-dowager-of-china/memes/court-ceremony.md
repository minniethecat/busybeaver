# Court Ceremony

The Qing Imperial Court operated through an elaborate system of ceremonies and rituals governing every aspect of daily life, from morning greetings to annual celebrations.

## Description

Court ceremony ranged from daily routines to grand annual events. The "morning salutation" required the Ladies of the Imperial Family to greet the Emperor in a prescribed manner (009-004), with "the attitude prescribed for this ceremony" carefully observed (009-010). For major events, the Court assembled with "all the pomp and ceremony that characterized the meals of state" (007-008).

The kotow (磕头, *kētóu*) — the ceremonial prostration before the throne — was the most formal gesture of submission and respect (016-016). Carl witnessed it performed by officials and family members during the Emperor's Birthday celebrations (009-014). After the formal ceremony, the Emperor and Empress Dowager received congratulations and gifts from assembled nobles (009-012).

## In Carl's Account

Carl observed that ceremony permeated every aspect of Court life. Even meals were served with elaborate ritual: "Her Majesty had all the pomp and ceremony of the boat-rides" (006-005). During the Emperor's Birthday, she witnessed the full sequence of "formal congratulations" followed by feasting and theatrical performances (009-012). At the ceremony's conclusion, "the music ceased. The ceremony was finished" (009-029).

During the Emperor's Birthday celebrations, Carl witnessed the full solemnity of the ceremony:

> The Emperor, for these official congratulations, was seated upon the Dynastic Throne, erect and stiff as an archaic figure; no longer the shy boy, but the Monarch clothed in all his power, and, for today, alone upon his great ancestral Throne. He was attended by his Master of Ceremonies, gorgeously attired, who stood in the rigid attitude prescribed for this ceremony.
>
> (009-010)
