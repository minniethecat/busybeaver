# Garden Party at the Summer Palace

A formal diplomatic garden party hosted by the Empress Dowager Cixi at the Summer Palace near Peking, where the gentlemen and ladies of the foreign Legations were received in separate audiences followed by a tour of the lakes and gardens.

## Overview

The garden party at the Summer Palace was a two-day diplomatic event: the Corps Diplomatique and attachés were entertained on the first day, and the ladies of the Legations on the second (019-002). Both days followed identical protocols — formal reception by Their Majesties in the Great Audience Hall, a banquet in a nearby pavilion, and a tour of the gardens and lakes, departing around two o'clock in the afternoon (019-002).

Katherine Carl, who had been resident at the Palace for some months painting the Empress Dowager's portrait, participated as a special intermediary. Her account provides a unique perspective on Qing diplomatic hospitality in the post-Boxer era.

## Background

Following the Boxer Rebellion of 1900, the Qing Court made concerted efforts to improve relations with the foreign Legations. Hosting garden parties at the Summer Palace was one expression of this diplomatic outreach. The Empress Dowager, who had fled to Xi'an during the Boxer crisis, returned to Peking in 1902 and resumed hosting foreign guests.

By the time of this garden party, Carl had been working on the Empress Dowager's portrait for several months. Her presence at the Court provided the Empress Dowager with informal insight into foreign customs, and Carl's observations of the diplomatic receptions record the cultural dynamics at play.

## Carl's Account

Carl was initially uncertain about her role at the formal reception. As a foreigner, she felt it incongruous to receive with the Chinese Ladies. The Empress Dowager, displaying her characteristic tact, resolved this by suggesting that Carl go to the Foreign Office to meet Mrs. Conger on her arrival and enter the Throne-room with her (019-003).

The Foreign Office, located only a few hundred yards from the Imperial entrance, served as the assembly point. When the ladies arrived, they walked to the Palace gate, entered, and arranged themselves in presentation order in a pavilion at the right of the Audience Hall (019-004).

## The Presentation Ceremony

The approach to the Audience Hall was prepared for the occasion: the verandah and marble platform were shaded with silken awnings and covered with red carpets — a concession to foreign taste, since the Empress Dowager never used carpets in the interior of the Palace (019-005).

A double line of Princesses, led by the Princess Imperial, descended the steps of the Audience Hall to meet the ladies, then preceded them inside and arranged themselves on either side of the Throne dais (019-006). In the dim interior, the Empress Dowager sat on the Dynastic Throne with the Emperor at her left, an official table of Imperial yellow before her (019-007).

Each lady made three reverences upon entering, then advanced to the dais for individual presentation. The elder Miss Yu, serving as interpreter, repeated each lady's Chinese name and title. The Empress Dowager, possessing "a royal memory for faces," recognized each lady who had been presented on previous occasions (019-008).

Carl noted that the Empress Dowager's uniform cordiality was sometimes misinterpreted by first-time guests as special personal interest. She observed: "Like all well-bred hostesses, she was most particular to show no difference even to those ladies she liked best" (019-008).

## The Luncheon and Lake Tour

After the presentations, the Empress Dowager descended from the dais and received the ladies informally while tea was served (019-009). The luncheon, served in Her Majesty's Throne-room, alternated Chinese and foreign courses, accompanied by foreign wines and "quantities of sweet champagne, without which, the Chinese imagine, no foreigner can eat" (019-010). The Imperial Princess and Princesses acted as hostesses.

Following the meal, the party proceeded to the marble terrace overlooking the lake, where the young Empress and the Emperor's secondary wife joined them (019-011). Three large houseboats carried the guests across the lake, each with a yellow-covered seat reserved for Her Majesty that was never used by anyone else — "Anything covered with yellow is sacred to Their Majesties" (019-012).

The lake tour included a visit to the island temple and to the Marble Boat, a summerhouse built over the lake by a previous Emperor. Carl described it as "a curiosity, though not a thing of beauty," noting that it was "never made the objective point of any of Her Majesty's promenades, nor visited, except when foreigners were invited to the Palace" (019-016). Light refreshments were served on the lower deck.

## Cultural Observations

Carl recorded the Chinese Ladies' comments on foreign dress and appearance with interest. The Empress Dowager thought foreign costume becoming to well-proportioned figures but "unfortunate for anyone who was not so blessed," while the Chinese costume, falling in straight lines, was more forgiving (019-018).

One universal observation among the Chinese Ladies was that foreigners "look old for their years." Carl attributed this to the Chinese practice of repressing outward emotion from early childhood, combined with simple, wholesome lives, resulting in unlined faces until advanced age (019-018).

She also noted the Chinese aversion to blond hair — on the stage, all demons were represented with blond hair, and the more blond, the more wicked the demon (019-019).

## Diplomatic Tensions

Despite the Empress Dowager's cordiality and the efforts of the Princesses, Carl observed that the garden parties were not always as pleasant as they might have been. There was "an absolute lack of harmony among the ladies of the Legation," with some displaying private animosities "on the steps of the Throne" (019-017).

Carl was troubled that the Chinese, the "most punctilious of people," should have this display of foreign discord as their primary impression of European society. She was astonished at "how observant the latter were and how accurately they gauged our standing" (019-017).

## Outcome and Significance

The garden parties represented the Qing Court's careful cultivation of post-Boxer diplomatic relations through traditional hospitality. The elaborate protocol — separate days for gentlemen and ladies, formal presentations with bilingual interpretation, the blending of Chinese and foreign cuisine — demonstrated sophisticated cultural diplomacy.

For Carl, the event also highlighted the cultural gap between the disciplined formalism of the Chinese Court and the undisciplined behavior of some foreign guests. Her account suggests that the Empress Dowager and her Court managed these occasions with greater diplomatic skill than many of their foreign visitors.
