# Imperial Hymn

The "Imperial Hymn" was the distinctive ceremonial music of the Qing Imperial Court, performed on traditional Chinese instruments whenever [[empress-dowager|the Empress Dowager]] and [[emperor-kwang-hsu|the Emperor]] proceeded to official functions. Katherine Carl, who heard it countless times during her nine-month residency, gave it this name and described it as "the only approach to a National air that I ever heard in China" (009-007).

## Musical Character

Carl describes the Imperial Hymn in vivid detail: "The cymbals clashed. The flutes sounded and all the instruments of the Imperial Band played the curious minor air, with its tragic undertone of sound, its rhythm like a Gregorian chant" (009-007). It was played exclusively at "the passing of Their Majesties for some great ceremony or official function" (009-007).

The music had a solemn, almost religious quality — "a weird accompaniment of the flutes and cymbals" — that accompanied the Imperial processions through the palace courts (009-029).

## Ceremonial Uses

The Imperial Hymn was played at many of the Court's most important occasions:

- The Emperor's Birthday: After the morning salutations, "the cymbals clashed" and the Imperial Band played as Their Majesties processed to the [[great-audience-hall|Great Audience Hall]] (009-007).
- The Sea Palace arrival: When Carl first arrived at the [[sea-palace|Sea Palace]], she saw the Empress Dowager "coming out, preceded by acolytes swinging incense-burners, the musicians playing the 'Imperial Hymn'" (010-014).
- Theater days: On the first and fifteenth of each month, "the Imperial Hymn was played on Their Majesties' entrance into the court of the Theater" (024-009).
- Ancestral ceremonies: At the conclusion of the Birthday celebrations, the Emperor and Empress knelt before the Empress Dowager to "the weird accompaniment of the flutes and cymbals playing the 'Imperial Hymn'" (009-029).

During the Confucian ceremony at the Palace temple, the liturgy combined multiple elements:

> There was an address to the memory of the great Sage, with music and hymns; the latter were rhythmic verses, containing some truth inculcated by the Sage.
>
> (013-005)

## The Imperial Band

The musicians who played the Imperial Hymn were palace eunuchs trained in traditional Chinese instruments — cymbals, flutes, and other ceremonial instruments. Carl noted that the music preceded Their Majesties wherever they went in state, creating an atmosphere of solemn ritual that marked every official movement through the palace precincts.
