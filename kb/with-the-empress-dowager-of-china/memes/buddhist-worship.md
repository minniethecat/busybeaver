# Buddhist Worship

Buddhist worship was an integral part of Qing court life, observed in palace temples with elaborate rituals of incense, offerings, and ceremonial procession. Katherine Carl encountered its material culture — temples, altars, incense-burners — throughout the imperial precincts.

## Description

The palace compound contained multiple Buddhist temples, ranging from intimate shrines to grand ceremonial halls. Carl described "the temple-crowned hills" visible from the lake (010-003) and the "ruins of a great lama temple" on the approach to Peking (001-004). Within the palace walls, temples were integrated into the landscape: one stood on an island in the lake with "no landing-place" (010-015), another was approached through an avenue of arborvitae trees that "seem to be sacred" (010-019).

The temples were richly appointed. Altars held figures of "the contemplative Buddha seated on the lotus" (004-003), surrounded by fresh offerings of fruit and flowers. "Tall, lighted candles" and "fresh offerings of fruit and flowers" were constantly replenished (010-019). Carl witnessed the Empress Dowager herself tending a temple altar: "She rearranged the offerings, placed a picture of the Mother of Buddha" with her own hands (024-016).


> The Chinese are not a religious people, though so moral a race. They are rather followers of a philosophy than members of religious bodies. The two most popular religions of China, Buddhism and Taoism, have become more or less outward forms. They are empty shells which may once have contained the Spirit, but have now become mere conventional representations of a religious belief.
>
> (029-011)
Ritual implements included "huge iron incense-burners" (013-006) and "golden incense-burners which gave forth azure" smoke, swung by acolytes during processions (009-029) (010-011). The sensory experience — perfumed air, candlelight, the rhythm of ceremony — impressed Carl as fundamentally different from Western religious practice.

## Cultural Significance

Buddhism at the Qing court was both personal devotion and state spectacle. The Empress Dowager's private faith was well known — she made offerings, visited temples, and assumed "Buddha-like poses" during ceremonies (009-028). Carl described Her Majesty's "Buddha-like pose" as a cultivated expression of serene authority (006-003).

At the same time, Buddhist ritual served political purposes. Religious ceremonies "are celebrated in the Palace temples with rigid impartiality" (013-004), encompassing Buddhist, Taoist, and Confucian observances. This ritual inclusiveness legitimised Qing rule over a multi-ethnic empire by demonstrating the throne's protection of all faiths. The "great ceremonies in the Palace temples" that Carl witnessed were as much acts of state as acts of worship (010-011).

## In Carl's Account

Carl's access to palace temples gave her a privileged view of Qing religious life. She accompanied the Empress Dowager to temple ceremonies, observing the procession from the "Throne-room to the temple" with "Their Majesties and the" court (013-006) (013-007).

She was particularly struck by the material richness of Buddhist practice. The "golden incense-burners," the offerings of "fresh flowers and fruit" (004-003), the "effigies of" Buddha being regilded (010-016) — all spoke to a tradition in which spiritual devotion was expressed through material splendour. The destruction of temples during the Boxer Rising, which she noted had left some "ruthlessly destroyed," added a layer of historical poignancy to her account (010-016).

Carl also noted the syncretic character of Chinese religious life. The same court that worshipped Buddha also honoured Confucius, whose "memorial arches" and "temple-crowned hills" dotted the landscape (002-019) (013-005). Religion at the Qing court was not exclusive but cumulative — a layering of traditions rather than a choice between them.

## Historical Context

Buddhism entered China from India during the Han dynasty and was fully naturalised by the Tang. Tibetan Buddhism (Lamaism) held particular favour at the Qing court, reflecting the dynasty's strategic relationship with Mongol and Tibetan allies. The great lama temple Carl saw from her approach to Peking was a physical manifestation of this political-religious alliance.

The Empress Dowager Cixi was a noted patron of Buddhist institutions, commissioning temple restorations and sponsoring ceremonies. Her personal faith was eclectic — she observed Buddhist, Taoist, and popular religious customs with equal seriousness.
