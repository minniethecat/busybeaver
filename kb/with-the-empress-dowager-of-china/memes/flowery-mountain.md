# Flowery Mountain

The Flowery Mountain is a terraced hillside in the grounds of the [[summer-palace|Summer Palace]], celebrated for its magnificent displays of peonies—which the Chinese call "The King of Flowers"—during the blooming season (017-010).

## Location and Context

The Flowery Mountain is situated within the extensive pleasure grounds of the Summer Palace, the [[empress-dowager|Empress Dowager Cixi]]'s favorite residence, located among the Western Hills sixteen miles from [[peking|Peking]] (017-002).

## Description

During the peony season, the terraced slope transforms into what Carl described as "a flowery mountain—one mass of blooms of exquisitely blended colors and faint evanescent perfume" (017-010).

The [[summer-palace|Summer Palace]] gardens also featured other seasonal displays. In autumn, the grounds "fairly blaze" with chrysanthemums, which the Chinese gardeners cultivated for unusual color combinations and curious shapes rather than for extraordinary size. During Carl's year at the Palace, the Empress Dowager was particularly delighted by a new green variety of chrysanthemum and another with petals "like threads, they were so thin and hairlike" (017-010).

Carl recorded the seasonal spectacle of the terraced hillside:

> In the season of the peonies, which the Chinese call "The King of Flowers," this is really a flowery mountain—one mass of blooms of exquisitely blended colors and faint evanescent perfume.
>
> (017-010)

## Role in Court Life

The Flowery Mountain was part of the designed landscape that made the Summer Palace a place of aesthetic retreat for the Court. The [[empress-dowager|Empress Dowager Cixi]], who spent most of the year at the Summer Palace, took pride in its gardens. The seasonal rhythms of flowering plants marked the passage of time at the Court, and the gardens were maintained by skilled Chinese gardeners whom Carl praised as "wonderful" (017-009).

## In the Narrative

The Flowery Mountain is described in Chapter 17, Carl's detailed account of the Summer Palace grounds. Her description emphasizes the integration of natural beauty with landscape design that characterized the Summer Palace, where "Nature and art are everywhere so blended, so harmonized, it is difficult to tell which is which" (017-007).

## Historical Significance

The Flowery Mountain exemplifies the Chinese imperial garden tradition, where natural landscapes were enhanced through careful planting and terracing to create composed views. The cultivation of peonies at the Summer Palace continued a tradition dating back to the Tang Dynasty, when the flower became associated with imperial power and prosperity. Today, the Summer Palace remains a UNESCO World Heritage site, and its gardens continue to display seasonal flowers in the imperial tradition.
