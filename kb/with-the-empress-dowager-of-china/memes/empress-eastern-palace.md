The Empress of the Eastern Palace was one of the two Co-Regent Empresses who governed China during the minority of the Tongzhi Emperor and the early years of the Guangxu Emperor. Together with the Empress of the Western Palace (the [[empress-dowager|Empress Dowager Cixi]]), she formed the dual Regency that steered China through the Taiping Rebellion's aftermath and fraught foreign relations.

## Historical Role

The Empress of the Eastern Palace became Co-Regent after the death of the [[emperor-hsien-feng|Emperor Hsien-Feng]], when her adopted son [[emperor-tung-chih|Tung-Chih]] ascended the throne as a child. She and the Empress of the Western Palace were appointed Co-Regents with equal rank and power. (030-005)

Together, they governed through the young Tung-Chih's minority and later resumed the Regency for [[emperor-kwang-hsu|Kwang-Hsu]], the nephew they adopted as successor. This dual Regency spanned nearly three decades, during which China navigated internal rebellion and increasing foreign pressure. (030-009)

## The "Literary Empress"

Unlike her politically astute Co-Regent Cixi, the Empress of the Eastern Palace devoted herself to literary pursuits and led the life of a student. She was a woman of such fine literary ability that she herself sometimes examined the essays of aspirants for the highest literary honors at the University of [[peking|Peking]]. She was also a writer of distinction. (015-002)

> When Her Majesty the Empress Dowager was Empress of the Western Palace, Co-Regent with the Empress of the Eastern Palace, who died in 1881, the Empress of the Eastern Palace was known as the "Literary Empress." All State affairs were left to the stronger executive ability of the Empress of the Western Palace; while she of the Eastern Palace gave herself up to literary pursuits and led the life of a student.
>
> (015-002)

## Relationship with Cixi

Though the two Empresses were strikingly different in temperament — one a scholar, the other a ruler — they lived amicably together and are said to have had a sincere affection for each other. This mutual regard never weakened during their long association as wives of Emperor Hsien-Feng, as Regents for Tung-Chih, and afterward as Regents for Kwang-Hsu. (030-005)

Had the Empress of the Eastern Palace not been such an exceptional literary woman and had Cixi not possessed so many other remarkable qualities, Cixi's name might also have gone down to history as a "literary Empress." (015-003)

## The Seventh Prince Connection

Prince Ch'un, the Seventh Prince, was a valued friend of both Empresses during the Co-Regency. He was brother to Emperor Hsien-Feng and his wife was Cixi's sister, making him the father of the future Emperor Kwang-Hsu. (003-018)

## Death and Legacy

The Empress of the Eastern Palace died in 1881. After her death, Cixi ruled alone as Regent, and posthumous honors were lavished upon her departed Co-Regent. (030-005) Her death marked the end of the dual Regency system that had characterized Chinese governance since Tung-Chih's accession.
