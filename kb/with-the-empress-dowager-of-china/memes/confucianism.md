## 定义与背景

Confucianism is the dominant ethical and philosophical system in China, described in the book as "a system of ethics, a philosophy rather than a religion." (029-011) The Chinese people, including the Manchu rulers of the Qing Dynasty, are identified as Confucians — the author notes that "this is a philosophy rather than a religion." (013-004)

The Empress Dowager herself "is, of course, a Confucian" (013-004), and the entire framework of Qing court ritual and ceremony is structured around Confucian principles as codified in the [[book-of-rites|Book of Rites]].

## 在清宫中的实践

### 半年度祭孔

One of the most important Confucian observances at the Qing court was the [[semiannual-sacrifices|semiannual sacrifices]] to Confucius. These sacrifices commemorated Confucius himself and "four other great Sages, among whom was Mencius." (013-005) The ceremonies were conducted with strict adherence to ritual forms, reflecting the deep respect accorded to Confucian tradition by the imperial household.

### 祭祖与孝道

Confucian ethics placed supreme importance on [[ancestor-worship|ancestor worship]] and [[filial-piety|filial piety]]. The book notes that "the sacrifice to one's Ancestors" is "the most sacred of obligations to the Chinese, and most rigidly and religiously observed." (007-002) Even the Emperor's birthday celebrations were scheduled around sacrificial obligations prescribed by Confucian ritual.

### 朝廷礼仪

Court ceremonies were governed by Confucian ritual texts. When Princes and Nobles presented themselves before the Emperor, "each splendidly garbed Prince and Noble knelt and made the prostration prescribed by the Book of Rites." (009-011) The Empress Dowager herself was "mindful of the duties enjoined by the [[book-of-rites|Book of Rites]]." (023-003)

## 与其他信仰的关系

Confucianism in China coexisted alongside [[taoism|Taoism]] and [[buddhism|Buddhism]] — the "Three Teachings" (*Sanjiao*) that together shaped Chinese spiritual life. While the Empress Dowager was a Confucian in matters of state ritual and ethics, she also patronized Buddhist temples and observed Taoist practices. The author notes that Confucianism is "a philosophy rather than a religion" (013-004), allowing it to coexist with the religious observances practiced at court.

## 儒家典籍

The most important Confucian text referenced in the book is the [[book-of-rites|Book of Rites]] (*Liji*), one of the Five Classics of the Confucian canon. It prescribes the forms of ceremony and propriety that governed all official life at the Qing court. At times, however, its strictures could be "set aside in the Book of Rites, and on special occasions" (029-008), showing that even Confucian orthodoxy allowed flexibility when circumstances required.

## 孔庙

The [[temple-of-heaven|Confucian Temple]] in Peking featured a distinctive "spirit-stairway" — an architectural feature unique to Confucian sacred spaces. (P05-015) At the Winter Palace, a Confucian Temple with its Spirit-Stairway stood within the imperial grounds (024-026), serving as a site for the official observances that linked the ruling dynasty to the ancient Confucian tradition.

> The Chinese people are really Confucians, and Confucianism is a system of ethics, a philosophy rather than a religion.
>
> (029-011)
