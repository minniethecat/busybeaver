# Prince Ching

Prince Ching (Yikuang, 1838–1917), also known as Prince Qing,
was the [[Prime Minister]] of the Qing Empire and head of the [[grand-council|Grand Council]]
during the period of [[katherine-carl|Katherine Carl]]'s portrait commission.
As the senior official responsible for foreign affairs through the [[wai-wu-pu|Wai-Wu-Pu]],
he was a key figure in the diplomatic interactions
that framed Carl's experience at the Chinese Court.

## Role at the Chinese Court

Prince Ching held the highest civil office in the Qing government.
As Prime Minister, he presided over the [[grand-council|Grand Council]] (Junjichu),
the empire's supreme policymaking body,
and simultaneously directed the [[wai-wu-pu|Wai-Wu-Pu]] (Foreign Office),
which managed all relations with foreign powers. (016-006)

His position placed him at the intersection of the imperial household and the diplomatic corps.
Carl observed that "the Prime Minister, Prince Ching, has the last Audience of the day"
in the sequence of officials received by the [[empress-dowager|Empress Dowager]],
indicating his preeminence in the court hierarchy. (016-006)

## Biographical Sketch

Yikuang was a descendant of the Qianlong Emperor and a member
of the imperial Aisin Gioro clan. His rise through the Qing bureaucracy
was accelerated by his skill in managing foreign relations —
a domain that became critically important after the Boxer Rebellion of 1900.
He was appointed to lead the newly created Wai-Wu-Pu in 1901
and became head of the Grand Council in 1903, the year of Carl's arrival in Peking.

Unlike many conservative Manchu nobles, Prince Ching was pragmatic in his dealings
with Western powers. He recognized that the dynasty's survival depended on
managing foreign relations adeptly, and he cultivated working relationships
with the diplomatic community in Peking — including the [[american-legation|American Legation]].

## Interaction with the Author

Carl's encounters with Prince Ching were largely indirect, mediated through court ceremonies
and diplomatic functions. She provides a vivid physical description
of him among the officials at court:

> The thin white-bearded face of Prince Ching, or the heavy
> Jewish-looking physiognomy of Na-Tung, the new minister, who seems
> likely to grow in favor. (025-005)

Though Carl did not interact with Prince Ching as closely as she did
with the women of the court, his presence shaped the diplomatic framework
within which the portrait project operated.
It was through the Foreign Office under Prince Ching's direction
that arrangements were made for Carl's access to the Palace. (019-003)

## Character and Appearance

Carl notes Prince Ching's "thin white-bearded face" (025-005) —
a detail that distinguishes him from the younger or clean-shaven officials
she observed at court. His age and bearing marked him as a senior statesman
in a government where longevity often determined rank.

His family was visible at court: Carl records visits from
"Prince Ching's wives and daughters" to the Palace, (026-002)
and notes that a daughter of Prince Ching — the Fourth Princess —
was a widow serving among the Empress Dowager's Ladies-in-Waiting. (005-005)

## Historical Significance

Prince Ching presided over the Qing foreign policy apparatus
during one of its most challenging periods.
In the aftermath of the Boxer Rebellion, the dynasty faced indemnity payments,
foreign garrisons in Peking, and deep international distrust.
His leadership of the Wai-Wu-Pu represented the Qing court's attempt
to engage with the Western powers on a footing of diplomatic reciprocity.

| Role | Period | Significance |
|------|--------|-------------|
| Head, Wai-Wu-Pu | 1901–1911 | Managed post-Boxer foreign relations |
| Head, Grand Council | 1903–1911 | Highest civil official in the empire |
| Negotiator, Boxer Protocol | 1901 | Signed the peace settlement with foreign powers |

His legacy is contested: modern historians view him as a pragmatic reformer
who bought time for the dynasty, while his contemporaries criticized him
for corruption and excessive accommodation of foreign demands.
He retired from public life after the 1911 Revolution.

## In the Narrative

Prince Ching appears in Carl's narrative primarily as a structural presence —
the embodiment of the Qing state machinery that enabled and constrained her work.
His "last Audience of the day" marks the rhythm of imperial governance,
while his family members appear among the court ladies who formed Carl's daily company.

A drawing of Prince Ching is included among the illustrations of the memoir,
testifying to his importance as a recognizable figure
whom Carl's readers were expected to identify. [P05-016]
