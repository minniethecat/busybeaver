# Nikíta the Tanner

Nikíta the Tanner (Nikíta Kozhemyáka) is a folk hero of Russian legend — a humble leather worker in Kíev whose extraordinary strength enables him to defeat a dragon that had been devouring maidens from the land. Having slain the monster, he asks for no reward and returns quietly to his trade of tanning hides (057-001, 057-012).

## Identity

- **Role**: hero
- **Epithets**: "the Tanner" (Kozhemyáka)
- **Relations**: A citizen of Kíev, summoned by the Tsar to deliver the princess and the land from a dragon; moved to action not by the Tsar's authority but by the tears of five thousand children (057-002, 057-003)

The tale of Nikíta the Tanner appears as a standalone legend in Afanasyev's collection, listed in the table of contents (P04-058). It combines the dragon-slayer motif with an etiological explanation of a geographical feature — the deep furrow that runs from Kíev to the Caspian Sea.

## Traits and Attributes

Nikíta's primary attribute is his **prodigious physical strength**, demonstrated in the story's opening: when the Tsar arrives at his workshop, Nikíta is holding twelve ox-hides in his hands, and his hands tremble so much at the royal visit that he tears all twelve at once (057-003).

His character is marked by **humility and reluctance**. Unlike the bogatyri of the byliny, who ride eagerly to battle, Nikíta must be persuaded to fight. The Tsar's personal plea fails to move him; only when five thousand children come and weep before him does he relent (057-003). He prepares not with knightly arms but with the materials of his trade — thirty puds of hemp, tarred and wrapped around his body so the dragon will find him "a hard morsel" (057-003).

His **cunning** matches his strength. When the defeated dragon proposes they divide the earth, Nikíta agrees, harnesses the creature to a three-hundred-pud plough, and makes it carve a furrow from Kíev to the Caspian Sea. Then he points out that the sea remains undivided — and when the dragon follows him into the water, Nikíta kills and drowns it (057-008, 057-010).

Most tellingly, after his great deed, he **demands no reward** and returns home to continue tanning skins (057-012).

## Narrative Function

Nikíta the Tanner fulfills the role of the **reluctant hero** — the common man whose strength and virtue are discovered only in crisis, and who returns to obscurity when the crisis is resolved. His tale is an inversion of the aristocratic heroism of the byliny: here the hero is a craftsman, not a knight; he fights in tarred hemp, not armor; and his reward is the resumption of his humble trade.

The tale also serves an **etiological function**: the furrow ploughed by the dragon-drawn plough is presented as the origin of a real geographical feature, a trench "two fathoms deep" that can still be seen, and which local people call a battlement (057-011).

## Folkloric Context

Nikíta the Tanner shares the dragon-slayer motif with the byliny of [[dobrynya-nikitich|Dobrýnya Nikítich]] and [[alyosha-popovich|Alyósha Popóvich]], but his tale belongs to a different register — that of the **local legend** rather than the heroic epic. The dragon here is less a cosmic adversary than a predatory beast, and the conflict is resolved not through divine aid or knightly prowess but through the strength and cunning of an ordinary artisan.

The tale of the hero who ploughs a furrow with a dragon is also found in other Slavic traditions, and Nikíta's name — "the Tanner" — links him to the mythology of the craftsman-hero, a figure whose power derives from his mastery of a material trade rather than from noble birth or supernatural election.
