# Eléna the Fair

Princess Eléna the Fair is the royal heroine of the tale [[ivan-tsarevich|Iván Tsarévich]], the [[the-firebird-motif|Firebird]], and the Grey Wolf — the beautiful princess from a distant kingdom whom Iván Tsarévich must win as a bride. Residing in a realm across the thrice-tenth kingdom, she is abducted by the [[grey-wolf]], falls in love with the hero on the journey, endures a death-threat from his murderous brothers, and ultimately exposes the truth to reclaim her true bridegroom.

## Identity

- **Role**: royal — princess of a distant kingdom
- **Epithets**: the Fair, the Beautiful
- **Relations**: Wife of [[ivan-tsarevich|Iván Tsarévich]]; desired by Tsar Afrón of the golden-maned horse; cast by lot to the brother Vasíli Tsarévich during the usurpation [020-061]

## Kingdom and Abode

Eléna the Fair inhabits a distant realm beyond the ordinary world. Tsar Afrón describes her kingdom as lying "across thrice-nine lands into the thrice-tenth kingdom" [020-025]. Her dwelling is described as a palace enclosed by "the golden palisade which surrounded the wonderful garden" [020-030], where she customarily walks in the evening hours:

> "In the evening, when the little sun was setting fast to the West, Princess Eléna the Fair went into the garden to take a walk with all of her maids of honour and servants and attendants and all the boyáryni around." [020-031]

## Abduction by the Grey Wolf

Eléna enters the tale when Tsar Afrón, whom Iván Tsarévich has offended by attempted theft of the golden-maned horse, demands that Iván obtain the princess as the price of forgiveness:

> "You must ride across thrice-nine lands into the thrice-tenth kingdom and gain me Princess Eléna the Fair, whom I have for long loved with all my heart and soul, but cannot gain." [020-025]

The grey Wolf fulfills the task by ambush. Lurking behind the golden palisade of her garden, the Wolf "leapt across the grating to the garden, seized Princess Eléna the Fair, leapt back and ran away with all his might and strength" [020-031]. Despite the huntsmen sent in pursuit, "however many the hunters that hunted, they could not hunt down the grey Wolf" [020-032].

## Love and the Wolf's Deception

While riding on the grey Wolf's back, the captive princess and her abductor fall in love: "Iván Tsarévich, seated on the grey Wolf's back with Princess Eléna the Fair, fell in love with her and she with him" [020-033]. To spare the lovers from separation at the hands of Tsar Afrón, the grey Wolf performs a transformative deception:

- The Wolf strikes the grey earth and transforms into the exact likeness of Princess Eléna [020-037]
- Iván delivers the Wolf-Eléna to Tsar Afrón in exchange for the golden-maned horse [020-038]
- The true Eléna rides away with Iván while the Wolf-Eléna remains three days in Tsar Afrón's palace before escaping [020-039]

## Trial and Vindication

Eléna's most dramatic test comes when Iván's envious brothers Dmítri and Vasíli Tsarévich discover the sleeping couple on the journey home. They murder Iván in his sleep and threaten Eléna:

> "Listen, Eléna the Fair, you are now in our hands: we will take you to our father, Tsar Výslav Andrónovich, and you are to tell him that we found you and the Bird of Light and the golden-maned steed. If you do not say this, we will slay you at once." [020-050]

Under duress, she "swore by all the holy relics that she would do as she was bidden" [020-051]. Yet even in captivity she defends the slain hero's honor, rebuking his brothers: "if ye were good champions, ye would have gone with him into the open field and have slain him in fair fight. But ye have slain him in his sleep, and how shall ye receive praise? Is not a man asleep as one dead?" [020-049]

After Iván is resurrected by the grey Wolf with the Water of Life and Death [020-056], Eléna's true loyalty is revealed at the wedding feast:

> "When Eléna the Fair saw Iván Tsarévich, she jumped up at once from her chair and began to kiss his sweet lips and to cry out, 'O my beloved bridegroom, Iván Tsarévich: this is he, and not that other who sits at table.'" [020-059]

She then "told him all the real truth, how it had been" [020-060], leading Tsar Výslav to imprison his treacherous sons and bless the true marriage of Iván and Eléna, who "lived with her friendlywise and lovingly, so that one might never be seen anywhere without the other" [020-061].

Eléna functions in the tale structure primarily as the prize-object of the hero's quest — the beautiful princess whom the hero must "gain" [020-025]. However, her role deepens beyond the passive trophy: she speaks truth in the face of mortal threat, rebukes treachery with moral clarity, and acts decisively to unmask usurpers at the climactic moment.

## Folkloric Context

The motif of the beautiful princess in a guarded garden, stolen by a supernatural helper for the hero, belongs to the pan-Indo-European folklore stock. The golden palisade evokes the enchanted boundaries found across Slavic wonder-tales, while the princess's evening garden walk with her retinue of "boyáryni" — noblewomen [020-031] — situates her within the Russian courtly imagination. Her name "Eléna" (Helen) connects her to the broader Indo-European tradition of the abducted beauty, comparable to figures like [[vasilisa-the-fair|Vasilísa]] in other Russian tales.
