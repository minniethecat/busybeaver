# Donotknow

Donotknow is the assumed identity of **Iván the merchant's son**, a hero in Russian folklore who conceals his true nature beneath the hide of a bull and a bladder worn on his head, answering every question with a single phrase — "Idonotknow" — while secretly defending a kingdom from foreign invasion with the aid of a magical horse.

## Identity

- **Role**: hero
- **Epithets**: "the Merchant's Son"
- **Relations**: son of a merchant; persecuted stepson of a malevolent stepmother; owner of a magical foal; husband of the youngest princess of an unnamed kingdom [053-002]–[053-066]

## Traits and Attributes

Iván possesses a **magical horse**, purchased as a "sorry, scabby foal" for a single silver rouble, which turns out to be a prophetic and powerful steed capable of speech and transformation [053-008], [053-034]. The horse warns him of three assassination attempts by his stepmother — a poisoned threshold, a poisoned draught, and an enchanted shirt — saving his life each time [053-014]–[053-022].

His disguise is deliberately grotesque: dressed in a bull's hide with a bladder on his head, he appears as "an animal which was not an animal, a man that was not a man" [053-035]. Despite his monstrous appearance, he proves to be "a splendid and handsome knight" whose true form "no tale can tell and no pen can write" [053-065].

As a warrior, Donotknow is exceptionally capable: in the first battle he slays the Arab host single-handedly — "wherever he turned, heads flew: it was like mowing hay" [053-052]. In the second battle he defeats three mighty knights in single combat, sustaining a wound to his hand that ultimately leads to his recognition by the youngest princess [053-056].

## Narrative Function

Donotknow embodies the folklore motif of the **disguised hero** — the capable protagonist who hides his prowess beneath a repulsive exterior, only to be recognized by a perceptive princess. The youngest princess notices her kerchief on his wounded hand while he stands as a scarecrow in the garden, and from that moment she "became thoughtful, never giving heed to the festivals and to the merriment" [053-059].

His story follows a classic heroic arc: (1) persecution by the stepmother, (2) departure into the world in disguise, (3) threefold combat against overwhelming forces, (4) recognition by the princess, and (5) elevation from scarecrow to royal heir [053-066].

The magical horse functions as both **donor** and **helper**, providing the hero with the means of transformation and appearing whenever a hair from its tail is burned [053-034].

## Folkloric Context

The name "Donotknow" (*Neznayko* in Russian) connects to a broader tradition of "fool" and "nobody" heroes in Slavic folklore who triumph through concealed virtue rather than apparent strength. The tale appears in Afanasyev's collection and exhibits affinities with the international tale type ATU 314 ("Goldener"), in which a youth in disguise, aided by a magical horse, rises from servitude to royal status [053-001]–[053-066].

The stepmother's three assassination attempts — poison at the threshold, poison in a drink, and an enchanted garment — form a triadic pattern characteristic of Russian wonder-tale structure, with each attempt escalating in cunning and each escape confirming the hero's destined invulnerability [053-014]–[053-022].
