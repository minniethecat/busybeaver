# The Church in Russian Folklore

The Russian folktales preserved in Afanasyev's collection exist in a fascinating tension between pre-Christian pagan content and the Orthodox Christian framework that overlaid it after 988 CE. The Church appears both as narrative element and as the very institution that drove the old beliefs underground.

## Christianization and Folk Survival

Ralston's Introduction traces the precise mechanism of religious transformation. When the Eastern Slavs accepted Christianity, "the great national divinities were proscribed and submerged; some of their features reappearing in the legendary feats of the saints. The local cults continued, with this difference, that they were now condemned by the Church and became clandestine magic" (P02-004). The folktales as we have them are thus double-coded: overtly Christian in vocabulary and framing, pagan in deep structure and cosmology.

## Priests as Characters

Priests (*pops*) appear frequently in the tales, often as figures of satire or moral ambiguity. They are sometimes greedy, sometimes wise, and occasionally function as the butt of folk humor — a tradition that reflects the complex relationship between the peasantry and the institutional Church. The tales were collected in the nineteenth century, "industriously and conscientiously compiled" just as "the Slav peoples conserved primitive Aryan customs almost up to the middle of the nineteenth century" (P02-026).

## Saints and Legendary Figures

The boundary between folktale hero and Christian saint is porous. Saint Nicholas appears in multiple tales, while Christ and the Apostles walk the earth in stories such as *The Poor Widow* and *Christ and the Geese*. The Introduction notes that features of the old gods "reappearing in the legendary feats of the saints" (P02-004), making the saint legends a crucial bridge genre between pagan folk narrative and Christian hagiography.

## The Three Categories

The Russian folk record preserved three categories: "First of all, the Chronicles... Secondly, the ballads or bylíny... Thirdly, the traditional ceremonial songs, magical incantations and popular melodies" (P02-005). The Church chronicles and the folk ballads represent parallel but distinct traditions — one ecclesiastical, one vernacular — that only partially merged in the folktale corpus.
