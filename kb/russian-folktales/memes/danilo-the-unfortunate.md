# Danílo the Unfortunate

Danílo the Unfortunate is a noble henchman at the court of [[prince-vladimir|Prince Vladímir]] in Kíev, who transforms from an abused underling into a triumphant hero through the intervention of a magical Swan-bird maiden. His tale, recorded in Afanásyev's collection, is a prose adaptation of a traditional ballad and features an encounter with [[baba-yaga|the Wise Woman of Kíev]], lending it strong folkloric depth (076-003).

## Identity

- **Role**: hero
- **Epithets**: the Unfortunate
- **Relations**: servant of Prince Vladímir; husband of the Swan-bird, the fair maiden; rival of Alyósha Popóvich, the Mocker of Women (007-001, 007-016, 007-025)

## The Impossible Task and Magical Aid

Danílo's story begins with his abject status at court. While other servants received goblets of wine on Sundays, Danílo received only blows, and on feast days everyone else was sated while Danílo had nothing (007-001). On the eve of Easter, Prince Vladímir assigned Danílo an impossible quest:

> "He gave him eighty score of sable skins, and he bade him sew a shúba for the feast: the sable skins were not prepared, and the buttons had not been moulded, and the buttonholes had not been made. In the buttons he was bidden mould the wild beasts of the wood and to sew into the buttonholes all the seabirds." (007-002)

Distraught, Danílo went outside and wept. An old woman — elsewhere identified as the Wise Woman of Kíev (076-003) — directed him to the shore of the blue sea. At midnight he was to seize Chúdo-Yúda, the Old Man of the Sea, by the grey beard and beat him until the [[animal-helpers-in-folklore|Swan-bird, the fair maiden]], appeared. Danílo followed her instructions:

> "Danílo seized him by his beard and began to beat him on to the grey earth. Then at last Chúdo-Yúda asked him: 'Why do you beat me, Danílo the Unfortunate?' 'For this reason: let me see the Swan, the fair maiden; let her body glint through her wings, and through her body let her bones appear, and from bone to bone let the marrow run like a flowing string of pearls.'" (007-006)

The Swan-bird swam ashore and agreed to help. With a flap of her wings, twelve craftsmen appeared who built Danílo a princely house and, in a single night, sewed the miraculous shúba — complete with lions moulded on the buttons and singing shepherds embroidered on the buttonholes (007-015--007-019).

## Traits and Attributes

- **Initial condition**: Abused, impoverished, tearful, quick to anger (calls the old woman "old fatty" before apologizing) (007-003--007-004)
- **Fatal flaw**: Susceptible to drink and pride — the Swan-maiden explicitly warns him: "Do not drink the glass to the bottom: if you drink it to the bottom no good will befall you; and do not boast of me" (007-021)
- **Arc**: From the lowest-ranking henchman to master of his own house, seated at the head of the table (007-049)
- **Magical gifts bestowed**: A silver staff that causes the shúba's birds to sing and lions to roar; three eggs — two silver for the Prince and Princess, one gold to keep (007-021--007-022)

## Narrative Function

Danílo exemplifies the folkloric motif of the unpromising hero who triumphs through magical help — a variant of the [[the-fool-in-russian-folklore|fool-hero]] type found throughout Russian folktales. He follows a classic hero's [[the-heros-quest|journey]] structure:

1. **Initial lack**: Low status, hunger, mistreatment (007-001)
2. **Impossible task**: The shúba commission (007-002)
3. **Donor encounter**: The old Wise Woman provides the means (007-005)
4. **Magical helper**: The Swan-bird maiden and her craftsmen (007-006--007-019)
5. **Transgression**: Drunken boasting violates the helper's warning (007-025)
6. **Crisis**: Wager of heads with Alyósha Popóvich (007-025, 007-035)
7. **Resolution**: The Swan-maiden humiliates the Prince's court and elevates Danílo (007-044--007-049)

## Folkloric Context

The tale is a prose *bylína* — a prose version of a ballad — and contains a "very full account" of the Swan-maiden legend (076-003). The Swan-bird herself belongs to the pan-Slavic tradition of [[magic-in-russian-folklore|transformation magic]], where supernatural brides shift between bird and human form. The rivers of beer, mead, wine, and vodka that incapacitate the Prince's retinue en route to the feast (007-041) echo the magical waters common in Slavic folklore, akin to the [[the-water-of-life-and-death|Water of Life and Death]] motif.

The tale also illustrates the [[reward-and-punishment|reward and punishment]] dynamic: the long-suffering Danílo is ultimately elevated, while the boorish court — particularly [[alyosha-popovich|Alyósha Popóvich]], the Mocker of Women — is left covered in mud "from head to foot" (007-050).
