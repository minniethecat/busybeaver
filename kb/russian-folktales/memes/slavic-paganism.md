# Slavic Paganism

The supernatural apparatus of Russian folktales — witches, magicians, shape-shifters, and the thrice-nine realm — is not pure literary invention but the transformed residue of pre-Christian Slavic religion. When the Eastern Slavs were Christianized in 988 CE under Prince Vladímir of Kíev, the old gods were not erased but absorbed, transformed, and driven underground into folk narrative.

## From Gods to Folktales

Ralston's Introduction traces the process precisely: "When Christianity transformed the face of the world, it necessarily left much below the surface unaffected. The great national divinities were proscribed and submerged; some of their features reappearing in the legendary feats of the saints" (P02-004). The "local cults continued, with this difference, that they were now condemned by the Church and became clandestine magic; or else they were adopted by the Church, and the rites and sanctuaries transferred" (P02-004). What we encounter in the folktales is thus not living paganism but its fossilized afterlife — genuine ritual and belief refracted through centuries of Christian reinterpretation.

## Survivals in the Afanasyev Collection

The tales preserve multiple strata of pagan belief. The witch Bába Yagá, who "lives in the forest, rides the winds in a mortar, devours human flesh, lives in a hut on cocks' legs" (P02-016), echoes the pre-Christian *ved'ma* (wise woman / witch) who mediated between the living community and the forest spirits. Koshchéy the Deathless, with his external soul hidden in an egg, embodies the archaic concept of separable soul-stuff that predates Christian notions of the immortal soul.

The pantheon of minor spirits — the Wood Sprite (*leshiy*), the Water Sprite (*vodanoy*), and the household spirit (*domovoy*) — are direct inheritances from Slavic animism, each guarding a domain that the Christian God was never fully understood to govern.

## The Three Categories of Folk Record

The Russian people preserved pre-Christian material through three channels: "First of all, the Chronicles, which are very full, very accurate... Secondly, the ballads or *bylíny*; epic songs in an ancient metre... Thirdly, the traditional ceremonial songs, magical incantations and popular melodies" (P02-005). The third category is the most direct conduit of pagan survival into the folktale corpus — incantations and ritual songs preserved verbatim for centuries after their original religious context had vanished.

## Scholarly Significance

Ralston emphasizes that "the store of primitive folklore of the Slavs has scarcely been touched. The Slav peoples conserved primitive Aryan customs almost up to the middle of the nineteenth century" (P02-026). This makes the Afanasyev collection one of the richest repositories of Indo-European folk belief in existence — a living museum of religious concepts that elsewhere survived only in fragmentary archaeological evidence.
