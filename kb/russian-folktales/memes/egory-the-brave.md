# Egóri the Brave

Egóri the Brave (Egóri Khrábry) is a divine messenger figure in Russian folklore who travels between God and humanity, delivering the Lord's decrees on how each man should live and what trade each should pursue. He is widely understood as the Russian folkloric counterpart of Saint George the Dragon-slayer (077-006).

## Identity

- **Role**: saint
- **Epithets**: "the Brave" (Khrábry)
- **Relations**: Serves as an intermediary between God and mortals; in the tale "Egóri the Brave and the Gipsy," he is tasked with conveying divine instructions on human occupations (006-006, 006-018)

Egóri appears chiefly in the tale "Egóri the Brave and the Gipsy," listed in the table of contents among the religious legends (P04-007). The story belongs to a group of folk narratives concerned with the origins of social roles and trades.

## Traits and Attributes

Egóri rides a horse and carries a **golden stirrup** — a detail that proves central to his one recorded tale. He is portrayed as **forgetful**: in the story of the gipsy, he three times neglects to deliver the peasant's petition to God, and only remembers when the gipsy takes his golden stirrup as collateral (006-010, 006-012, 006-018). When Egóri mounts his horse and looks down at the single stirrup, his memory is jogged — suggesting the stirrup functions as a mnemonic device rooted in folk ritual or oral-formulaic practice (006-018, 006-020).

His forgetfulness is not depicted as malice but as a narrative device that allows the clever gipsy to secure his divine mandate through trickery rather than piety.

## Narrative Function

In "Egóri the Brave and the Gipsy," Egóri serves as the **divine intermediary** — a messenger who shuttles between the heavenly court and the mortal road. His narrative function is to authenticate the gipsy's trade: the Lord's answer, carried back by Egóri, declares that the gipsy shall live by theft, cheating, and perjury (006-018, 006-021). The tale thus provides a folk etiology for the stereotype of the thieving gipsy, sanctioned — albeit ironically — by divine decree.

The character of Egóri bridges the formal theology of saints' lives and the irreverent humor of Russian folk narrative. He is a holy figure, yet he is forgetful; he carries God's word, yet he is outwitted by a peasant with a piece of collateral.

## Folkloric Context

Egóri the Brave is the Russian folk adaptation of **Saint George** (Georgios → Egóri / Yegóri), the dragon-slaying military saint venerated throughout Eastern Orthodox Christendom (077-006). Like his canonical counterpart, Egóri is associated with bravery and horsemanship, but the folk tradition strips him of the hagiographic solemnity and places him in a comic narrative of divine bureaucracy and peasant cunning.

The tale reflects the broader Russian folk practice of domesticating sacred figures — bringing saints down from icons and into the village road, where they can be bargained with, tricked, and made to serve the needs of folk narrative logic.
