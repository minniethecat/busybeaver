# Bába Yagá

Bába Yagá is the most iconic supernatural being in Russian folklore — a cannibal witch who lives in the liminal forest, tests those who enter her domain, and functions as both antagonist and reluctant helper. She serves as the fearsome threshold guardian whom [[vasilisa-the-fair]] must survive to obtain magical fire, and whom [[ivan-tsarevich]] must pass to reach [[the-thrice-tenth-realm]] in [[the-heros-quest]]. The Introduction describes her: "The Witch who lives in the forest, rides the winds in a mortar, devours human flesh, lives in a hut on cocks' legs, is one of the commonest" supernatural beings (P02-016).

## The Hut on Cocks' Legs

Bába Yagá's dwelling is her most distinctive attribute — a hut that stands on chicken legs and can rotate to face or reject visitors. Ralston's Notes reference studies "on revolving houses" and note that "the legend is cognate with magic wheels that revolve at great speed, or turn on wheels emitting flame and poison" (075-003). The hut is thus not mere whimsy but a genuine folk memory of archaic ritual structures at the boundary between village and forest.

## Mortal Vulnerability

Despite her fearsome power, Bába Yagá is mortal. In one tale, having pursued fleeing heroines, she "fell into the fire and was burned to death" (017-055). Her death by fire — the purifying element — demonstrates that even the most terrifying supernatural beings are subject to folk justice.

## Threshold Guardian

Bába Yagá's primary narrative function is as threshold guardian. Vasilísa the Fair must survive her tests to obtain the magical fire; Iván Tsarévich must pass through her domain to reach the thrice-nine realm. She is dangerous but not purely evil — she rewards the worthy and devours the unworthy, serving a cosmic sorting function.
