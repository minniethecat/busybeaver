# Dragon in Russian Folklore

The dragon (*zmey* or *zmei*, also *Chudo-Yudo*) appears throughout the Afanasyev collection as the archetypal monster — a multi-headed flying serpent that demands human tribute, abducts princesses, and tests the bogatyr's strength. It is the adversary that defines the hero.

## The Multi-Headed Serpent

Russian folk dragons typically possess multiple heads — three, six, nine, or twelve — each of which must be severed to ensure death. The tale of *Nikíta the Tanner* opens: "One day, somewhere near Kíev, a dragon appeared, who demanded heavy tribute from the people." The dragon occupies the liminal space between the civilized court at Kíev and the wild lands beyond — its emergence into human territory signaling a cosmic imbalance that only the bogatýr can correct.

## Folkloric Context

The Introduction identifies "the common Aryan stock" of narrative elements "all tinged with the characteristic Slav temperament" (P02-024). The multi-headed dragon belongs to this shared Indo-European inheritance, but the Russian variant is distinguished by its connection to the specific geography of Kíev and its historical undertones — the dragon as Tatar invader, the tribute as Mongol yoke.

## The Dragon's Mortality

Unlike Koshchéy the Deathless with his hidden soul, the dragon is straightforwardly mortal — albeit immensely powerful. The bogatyr's task is not to find a secret weakness but to muster sufficient strength and courage to face the beast in direct combat. This simplicity makes the dragon the purest test of the hero's martial virtue.
