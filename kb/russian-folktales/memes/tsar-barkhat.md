# Tsar Bárkhat

Tsar Bárkhat is a king in Russian folklore who appears as the foil in a comic tale of gender disguise. Encountering Vasilísa Vasílyevna — a maiden who dresses, rides, shoots, and drinks like a man — he devises three tests to unmask her, and is outwitted in every one (032-001, 032-016).

## Identity

- **Role**: royal
- **Epithets**: "the Crow" (bestowed mockingly by Vasilísa) (032-015)
- **Relations**: Served by an "old evil-minded housekeeper" who advises him on his schemes; adversary-turned-dupe of Vasilísa Vasílyevna, the cross-dressing daughter of Pope Vasíli (032-001, 032-005)

Tsar Bárkhat appears in a single tale in Afanasyev's collection, where he is the ruler who encounters the disguised Vasilísa while hunting and becomes obsessed with determining her true gender (032-002).

## Traits and Attributes

Tsar Bárkhat is portrayed as **credulous and easily manipulated** — not by Vasilísa, but by his own "old evil-minded housekeeper," who devises each of the three tests intended to expose the disguised maiden (032-005, 032-009, 032-011). He follows her counsel without question, suggesting a ruler who relies on the cunning of others rather than his own judgment.

The tests themselves — the embroidery-frame versus the gun, the pearls hidden in the pie, and the invitation to the bathhouse — rely on stereotyped gender expectations of the Russian folk tradition. Vasilísa evades each one by performing masculinity more convincingly than any man, scolding the Tsar for his "girlish trash" and beating him to the bathhouse (032-008, 032-010, 032-013).

In the end, Vasilísa leaves him a letter addressing him as "O you crow, you Tsar Bárkhat! The crow has not caught the falcon in the garden" (032-015) — a folk metaphor that marks him as the dull-witted predator outflown by a sharper prey.

## Narrative Function

Tsar Bárkhat serves as the **comic authority figure** — a king whose power is undercut by his own credulity and the cleverness of a woman who defies the gender categories he relies upon. The tale is not a heroic narrative but a domestic farce set at the highest level of society, where the tsar's three attempts to resolve an ambiguity become the engine of the plot and the source of the humor.

His role illuminates a recurring pattern in Russian folk narrative: the powerful man who cannot see what is in front of him because he relies on convention rather than observation.

## Folkloric Context

The tale of Tsar Bárkhat and Vasilísa Vasílyevna belongs to the international tale type of the **woman disguised as a man**, widespread in both European and Asian folklore. In the Russian context, the tale is distinctive for its comic treatment of the tsar — a figure who in the heroic byliny is treated with reverence but here is reduced to a puzzled dupe.

The name "Bárkhat" itself is glossed simply in the notes to Afanasyev's collection, and the tale is one of several in the collection that feature clever women outwitting powerful men through wit and performance.
