# Vasíli Tsarévich

Vasíli Tsarévich is the middle of three brothers in the tale cycle of [[ivan-tsarevich|Iván Tsarévich]], the [[the-firebird-motif|Bird of Light]], and the [[grey-wolf|Grey Wolf]] — alongside the eldest brother [[dmítri-tsarevich|Dmítri Tsarévich]] and the youngest, heroic [[ivan-tsarevich|Iván Tsarévich]]. His character embodies the folktale archetype of the envious elder brother who, having failed at his own task, seeks to usurp the rewards of the true hero.

## Identity

- **Role**: royal (second son of Tsar Výslav Andrónovich)
- **Position**: Middle brother, between Dmítri Tsarévich (eldest) and Iván Tsarévich (youngest)
- **Epithets**: "the middle brother," "envious brother"
- **Father**: Tsar Výslav Andrónovich (020-001)

The three sons are introduced at the outset of the tale:

> "There was once, in a certain kingdom, a certain state, where there lived Tsar Výslav Andrónovich, who had three sons: the first was called Dmítri Tsarévich, the second Vasíli Tsarévich, and the third Iván Tsarévich."
> — (020-001)

## The Failure at the Garden

When Tsar Výslav's beloved golden-apple tree is raided nightly by the Bird of Light, he sends each of his sons in turn to stand guard. After Dmítri fails on the first night, Vasíli is given the second watch — and proves no more capable:

> "So the next night Vasíli Tsarévich went to keep watch in the garden. He sat under the same apple-tree, and sat there one hour and went to sleep so soundly that he never heard the coming of the Bird of Light, which flew on to the tree, perched on it, and plucked many apples."
> — (020-005)

Like his elder brother, Vasíli lies to the Tsar in the morning, claiming the Bird of Light never came (020-006). It is only the youngest, Iván, who stays awake and manages to seize a golden feather from the bird's tail (020-007–020-008).

## Envy and Betrayal

Vasíli's defining trait is envy. When Iván succeeds in obtaining a feather, neither Vasíli nor Dmítri can bear it:

> "Dmítri and Vasíli were envious of their younger brother Iván that he had succeeded in pulling the feather out of the Bird of Light's tail."
> — (020-010)

After failing separately to find the Bird of Light on their own quests, the two elder brothers encounter Iván asleep in the open field, together with Princess Eléna the Fair, the golden-maned horse, and the Bird of Light in its golden cage. Rather than rejoice at their brother's success, they choose murder:

| Act | Detail | PN Ref |
|-----|--------|--------|
| Failure | Falls asleep on watch; lies to the Tsar | (020-005, 020-006) |
| Envy | Resents Iván for pulling the Firebird's feather | (020-010) |
| Murder | Participates in the killing of Iván as he sleeps | (020-048) |
| Plunder | Casts lots for the spoils; wins Princess Eléna | (020-051) |
| Usurpation | Attempts to marry Iván's bride | (020-057) |

After the murder, the brothers divide the spoils by lot:

> "Dmítri Tsarévich and Vasíli Tsarévich began to cast lots who should have the fair Princess Eléna and who should have the golden-maned horse, and the lot fell that the fair Princess Eléna should belong to Vasíli and the horse to Dmítri."
> — (020-051)

## Downfall

Vasíli's triumph is short-lived. The grey Wolf revives Iván Tsarévich with the [[the-water-of-life-and-death|waters of Life and Death]], and warns him:

> "Hasten there as fast as possible. Sit on me, on the grey Wolf, and I will take you there. ... your brother Vasíli is today going to marry your bride, Princess Eléna the Fair."
> — (020-057)

Iván arrives at the palace just as Vasíli returns from the wedding ceremony. Princess Eléna immediately identifies her true bridegroom, and the Tsar learns the full truth. The verdict is swift:

> "Tsar Výslav Andrónovich was then very wroth with Dmítri and Vasíli and sent them into the darkness of the dungeon."
> — (020-061)

## Narrative Function

Within the folktale structure, Vasíli functions as a **false hero** — the Proppian "usurper" who falsely claims the hero's rewards. His failure sequence (cannot stay awake, cannot find the quest object, resorts to treachery) serves to contrast with Iván's virtue. The tale punishes him not with death but with imprisonment, a fate that underscores the folktale moral: the fruit of unjust deeds will not endure.

## Disambiguation

Not to be confused with **Vasíli the Merchant's Son** (Chapter 13, "The Prophetic Nightingale"), a wise boy who understands the speech of birds, resolves the dispute of the three Crows at the court of the King of Khvalýnsk, and is eventually reunited with his parents (013-001–013-020). That Vasíli is a hero figure unrelated to the Tsarévich bloodline.
