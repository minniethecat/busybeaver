# Katomá

Katomá, known by the epithet "Oaken-cap" (Dyádka), is the faithful servant and supernatural helper to [[ivan-tsarevich|Iván Tsarévich]] in the tale of Ánna the Fair. A figure of immense physical strength, shrewd cunning, and unshakeable loyalty, Katomá embodies the [[magic-in-russian-folklore|magical helper]] archetype who guides the hero through impossible trials, devises riddles, tames magical beasts, and ultimately triumphs over treachery, mutilation, and death itself.

## Identity

- **Role**: Helper — the supernatural servant and protector
- **Epithets**: Katomá Dyádka, Oaken-cap
- **Master**: Iván Tsarévich, to whom he was assigned as attendant from youth (072-001)
- **Relations**: Marries the merchant's daughter after rescuing her from Bába Yagá (072-067); allies with a blind knight whom Ánna the Fair had also wronged (072-044–045)

## The Riddle-Maker and Task-Doer

When Iván Tsarévich wishes to marry Ánna the Fair — a princess who demands a riddle she cannot solve or else the suitor dies — it is Katomá who devises the winning riddle. Spotting a purse on the road, he instructs Iván:

> "Iván Tsarévich, there is your riddle for the Tsarévna; give her this riddle to solve: 'Good lies on the road: we took the good with good, and set it down to our good.' That she will never solve all her life long, for every riddle she has solved at once, for she had only to look in her magical book; and she would then have your head cut off." (072-011)

Ánna the Fair, unable to solve the riddle even with her conjuring book, is forced to accept Iván as her husband. But she then sets him impossible tasks. Katomá performs each one: he carries a great iron column into the kitchen, splits it into fuel, and secretly keeps four iron shafts "for later use" (072-017). When Ánna provides a magical horse meant to kill Iván, Katomá tames it by beating it with those very iron shafts until it pleads:

> "Father Katomá, leave me some life, and I will come down to earth and whatever you will I will do." (072-022)

## Mutilation and Resilience

Ánna the Fair, realizing that "Your man, Katomá Oaken-cap, has deceived me finely" (072-028), schemes to destroy him. Taking advantage of a drowsy Iván Tsarévich, she has Katomá's legs cut off and his body set upon a lofty stump, while Iván himself is tied behind the carriage and reduced to herding cows (072-035–039).

Left alone to die, Katomá survives through an alliance with a blind knight — another of Ánna's victims, whose eyes she had put out (072-043). The two form a symbiotic partnership: the blind man carries the legless Katomá on his back, and Katomá directs him with his sight (072-046).

## Bába Yagá and the Water of Life

The two knights capture a merchant's daughter to serve as their housekeeper, but their household is soon attacked by Bába Yagá, who comes to suck the maiden's blood (072-050–051). Katomá and the blind knight capture the witch, who in exchange for her life reveals the location of the Water of Life and Death.

| Trial | Outcome | PN Ref |
|-------|---------|--------|
| First well (deceptive) | Twig bursts into flame — fiery death-water | (072-061) |
| Second well (true) | Dry twig sprouts green — the healing water | (072-065) |
| Bába Yagá's fate | Thrown into the fiery brook and burned to death | (072-066) |

With the true water, the blind knight washes his eyes and regains his sight, and Katomá's legs are restored (072-065). Katomá then marries the merchant's daughter (072-067).

## Liberation and Resolution

The three companions return to Ánna the Fair's kingdom, where Katomá finds Iván Tsarévich reduced to a cowherd. Swapping clothes with his former master, Katomá drives the cows into the royal courtyard and — when the last cow hesitates — seizes its tail so mightily that "the entire skin remained in his hand" (072-075). Summoned before the Tsarévna, he reveals himself:

> "I am Katomá, whose legs you once cut off and then set on a tree trunk." (072-079)

Ánna, realizing she cannot prevail against a man who has overcome mutilation and death, repents, swears to love and obey Iván, and is forgiven (072-080–081). Katomá returns to live with his wife at the merchant's house (072-081).

## Narrative Function

Katomá fulfills the classic donor-helper function in the folktale structure, as outlined in [[the-heros-quest]]: he provides the hero with the means to overcome the bride-test (the riddle), performs the impossible tasks that the hero himself cannot accomplish, suffers vicariously for the hero's failures, and ultimately engineers the hero's liberation. Unlike passive magical gifts, Katomá's aid is active, loyal, and persists even after he is betrayed — marking him as the tale's true agent of resolution.
