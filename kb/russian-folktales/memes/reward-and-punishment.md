# Reward and Punishment

Russian folktales operate on a clear moral calculus: virtue is rewarded, wickedness is punished, and cleverness can sometimes substitute for virtue. This moral architecture drives narrative resolution across the entire Afanasyev collection.

## The Reward Structure

The standard reward in Russian tales is substantial — not merely treasure but cosmic elevation. The Tsar "had promised anyone who should cure her half of his reign and rule and realm; but anyone who failed was to have his head cut off" (021-010). This formula — half a kingdom or death — encodes the folktale's high-stakes reward economy. The generous reward justifies the extraordinary risk.

The reward is not always material. In many tales, the hero receives a magical helper or object that enables further quests. The Introduction identifies this as part of "the common Aryan stock, all tinged with the characteristic Slav temperament" (P02-024) — the reward structure being a shared Indo-European inheritance.

## Punishment and Retribution

The punishment of villains in Russian tales is implacable and often symmetrical to the crime. The wicked stepmother and treacherous apprentice face destruction: "the father bade his wife be chopped up into bits. He tied the apprentice to the tail of horses, and the horses broke in every direction and scattered his bones over the fields" (015-063). The judgment is absolute: "Let the dog die a dog's death!" (015-063).

Even supernatural beings face mortal punishment. Bába Yagá, having pursued the fleeing heroines, "fell into the fire and was burned to death" (017-055). The death of a witch by fire is itself a form of folk justice — the purifying element destroys the impure being.

## Death as Cosmic Punishment

In *The Soldier and Death*, the disruption of natural death becomes a form of cosmic punishment. The soldier who imprisoned Death creates a world where "there is no single soul left on earth who is dying" (022-064) — a state framed not as liberation but as unnatural stasis requiring correction.
