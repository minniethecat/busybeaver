# The Soldier Hero

Alongside princes and bogatyrí, Russian folktales feature a distinct everyman hero: the discharged soldier. After serving "God and the Great Sovereign for twenty-five whole years," he returns home with nothing but "three biscuits" — yet his military resourcefulness enables him to defeat supernatural adversaries that would destroy ordinary men.

## Twenty-Five Years' Service

The soldier's backstory is consistently evoked. He "had served through three campaigns, but had never earned as much as an addled egg, and was then put on the retired list." This framing — the loyal service poorly rewarded, the veteran cast adrift — establishes the soldier as a sympathetic figure whose subsequent triumphs read as cosmic justice. The soldier represents the Russian peasant's experience of military conscription under the Tsarist system, transformed into folk narrative.

## The Soldier and Death

The soldier's most famous encounter is with Death herself in *The Soldier and Death*. When he finds that "Death was standing at the Tsar's head" (022-051), he negotiates with Death, tricks her into a nosebag, and hangs her "on the bitter aspen, on the very top twig" (022-062) in the Bryánski Woods. His imprisonment of Death creates cosmic disruption: "there is no single soul left on earth who is dying" (022-064). Eventually he releases her, choosing to die by "my own death rather than to suffer such a cruel punishment" (022-054).

## Class and Power

The soldier hero occupies a unique class position: neither peasant nor noble, neither civilian nor fully military. This liminal status gives him license to move between worlds, to address the Tsar directly, and to bargain with supernatural powers as equals. The Introduction's observation about "the common Aryan stock, all tinged with the characteristic Slav temperament" (P02-024) is embodied in the soldier: the clever trickster who is distinctively Russian.
