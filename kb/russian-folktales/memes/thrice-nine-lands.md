# Thrice-Nine Lands

"Beyond thrice-nine lands in the thrice-tenth realm" (*za tridevyat' zemel', v tridesyatom tsarstve*) is the canonical opening formula of the Russian folktale, establishing a mythic geography in which all wondrous events occur by definition outside ordinary space. The phrase signals immediately that the listener has entered the world of marvels.

## Formula and Function

The number twenty-seven (thrice-nine) encodes symbolic completeness in the Slavic folk mind — three multiplied by itself, squared. The "thrice-tenth" realm is the thirtieth kingdom, lying just beyond the furthest boundary of the known world. Together, the formula performs several narrative functions: it banishes questions of plausibility before they arise, locates the tale in a space governed by magic rather than physics, and signals the genre contract between teller and audience.

## Appearances

The formula appears at the opening of many of the most significant tales in the Afanasyev collection. In the tale of Iván Tsarévich and the grey Wolf, the quest requires the helper Vóron Vóronovich to "fly across thrice-nine lands into the thrice-tenth realm and bring me the waters of Life and Death" (020-053), treating the faraway realm as a journey of cosmic distance that only magical creatures can traverse. The thrice-nine lands are thus not merely a spatial designation but a marker of the supernatural — only beings of power can cross them.

## The Mythic Landscape

The thrice-nine lands contain the characteristic features of the Russian folk otherworld: the witch's hut on cocks' legs, the fiery river, the impenetrable Bryánski forest, the island of Buyán, and the underworld itself. Ralston notes that "the underworld is the home of magic" (078-001), and the thrice-nine lands serve as the surface-level gateway to this chthonic realm. The Bryánsk woods "which were in ancient times impenetrable, became the legendary home of magic, and of weird happenings" (075-005), functioning as the liminal zone between the ordinary world and the thrice-nine realm.

## Indo-European Parallels

The formulaic otherworld journey has parallels across Indo-European narrative traditions — the Irish *Tír na nÓg*, the Greek realm beyond the Pillars of Hercules, the Norse worlds reached via Yggdrasil. The Introduction to Afanasyev's collection notes that "the store of primitive folklore of the Slavs has scarcely been touched" (P02-026), and the thrice-nine formula represents one of the most durable survivals of the archaic Slavic worldview.
