# Zamorýshek

Zamorýshek is the **weakling-turned-hero** of Russian folklore, the forty-first of forty-one sons born miraculously from eggs, whose name means "the stunted one" — but whose cunning and strength far surpass those of his forty robust brothers.

## Identity

- **Role**: hero
- **Epithets**: "the Weakling" (Zamorýshek)
- **Relations**: youngest of forty-one brothers born from eggs; son of a childless old couple who received the eggs from a mysterious old gaffer in the forest [014-001]–[014-004]

## Traits and Attributes

Zamorýshek is born the last and weakest of forty-one brothers who "grew up not by days, but by hours" [014-004]. While his forty brothers work the fields, he stays home — yet when a hayrick goes missing, it is Zamorýshek who takes action, commissioning an iron chain from the smith and breaking two before one holds [014-008]–[014-012].

His **strength belies his name**: he wrestles a magical mare that rises from the sea, binds her with chains, and rides her until she submits, winning forty-one superlative foals — "every single horse was better than every other horse" — for himself and his brothers [014-013].

His **cleverness** saves all forty-one brothers from death: warned by his talking horse that [[baba-yaga|Bába Yagá]] plans to behead them in the night, he instructs the brothers to swap clothes with Bába Yagá's forty-one daughters. When the Yagá's servants come at midnight, they cut off the daughters' heads instead, and the brothers escape with the heads mounted on stakes as trophies [014-025]–[014-028].

## Narrative Function

Zamorýshek exemplifies the **unpromising youngest child** motif with a distinctive birth narrative: the children hatch from eggs collected from every house in the village, a magical origin that echoes creation myths and the widespread folklore motif of miraculous birth [014-001]–[014-004].

His role as the **clever weakling** who outsmarts a powerful adversary mirrors the broader pattern of the trickster-hero in Slavic folklore, where intelligence and cunning prevail over brute force. The magical mare he captures functions as both adversary and donor, providing the supernatural horses essential to the brothers' subsequent adventures [014-013].

## Folkloric Context

The tale "Bába Yagá and Zamorýshek" appears in Afanasyev's collection and draws on the motif of the childless couple blessed with miraculous offspring. The hatching from eggs recalls Indo-European myths of cosmic eggs and the birth of heroes from non-human origins [014-001]–[014-004].

The number forty-one — forty strong brothers plus one weakling — is significant in Russian folk tradition, where forty (*sorok*) is a traditional round number for multitudes. The forty-one hayricks, forty-one foals, and forty-one daughters of Bába Yagá create a numerological symmetry throughout the tale [014-003], [014-013], [014-023].
