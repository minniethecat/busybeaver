# Mark the Rich

Mark the Rich is a wealthy merchant in Russian folklore who embodies the sin of **pride and avarice**: he refuses charity to the poor, attempts to murder a prophesied child three times, and ultimately perishes in the very trap he set for his enemy — a classic narrative of poetic justice in the Russian wonder-tale tradition.

## Identity

- **Role**: villain
- **Epithets**: "the Rich"
- **Relations**: husband of an unnamed wife; father-in-law (unwillingly) to the foundling boy who inherits his wealth [016-001]–[016-008]

## Traits and Attributes

Mark's defining trait is **inhospitality elevated to the point of sacrilege**: "he never suffered the poor man to come to his door, so ungracious was he" [016-001]. When he dreams that God Himself will visit, he prepares a lavish feast but stations servants at every entrance to drive away the poor — and in doing so he drives away the very Guest he awaited, who comes disguised as a bent old beggar in rags [016-002].

His **cunning** manifests in three escalating murder attempts against the prophesied child:
1. He throws the infant into a snowdrift to freeze, but hunters find and raise the boy [016-005]
2. He sends a letter ordering his wife to poison the youth, but Christ intercepts it and rewrites it to command the boy's marriage to Mark's own daughter [016-007]
3. He orders his distillery workers to throw the youth into a burning cauldron, but when Mark himself goes to check, the workers mistake him for the victim and cast him into the flames instead [016-008]

## Narrative Function

Mark the Rich serves as the **counter-example to Christian charity** in the folktale moral universe. His refusal to welcome the poor is a refusal to welcome God, and each of his murder plots is foiled by divine intervention — the hunters, Christ on the road, and a providential illness that sends the youth home instead of to the distillery [016-002]–[016-008].

The tale follows the pattern of **the rich man's downfall** (related to the biblical parable of the Rich Fool): a prophecy declares that a poor peasant's son will inherit Mark's wealth, and despite all Mark's efforts, the prophecy is fulfilled through supernatural means [016-004], [016-008].

## Folkloric Context

This tale belongs to the type ATU 930 ("The Prophecy") and draws on widespread Indo-European motifs of the rich man who tries to cheat fate. The three murder attempts form a triadic structure typical of Russian wonder-tales, with each attempt escalating in sophistication and each divine counter-intervention reinforcing the moral lesson [016-005]–[016-008].

The figure of Christ walking the earth as a beggar reflects the Russian folk Orthodox tradition of the *strannik* (wandering pilgrim), in which hospitality to strangers is a sacred obligation and its refusal a grave sin [016-002], [016-007].
