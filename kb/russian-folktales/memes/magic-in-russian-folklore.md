# Magic in Russian Folklore

Magic is a pervasive element in Russian folktales, serving as both narrative engine and cultural memory of pre-Christian Slavic belief systems. From the incantations of [[baba-yaga]] to the deathless sorcery of [[koshchey-the-deathless]], and from [[magical-horse|enchanted steeds]] to the [[the-nosebag-of-death|nosebag of death]], magic permeates every layer of the Afanasyev collection. The Introduction traces its roots to the survival of "local cults" that "became clandestine magic" after Christianization (P02-004).

## Definition

In the context of Russian folklore, magic encompasses both the structural enchantment driving fairy-tale plots and the historical residue of Slavic pagan rituals. Ralston's introductory essay identifies three categories of Russian folk records — chronicles, ballads (*bylíny*), and traditional songs — the last of which includes "magic formulas" and "ceremonial songs of Pagan origin" (P02-005). This dual nature distinguishes Russian folk magic: it is simultaneously a literary device and a genuine cultural artifact.

## Manifestations

### Objects of Power

Magical objects recur throughout the Afanasyev collection with remarkable consistency. The **magic mirror** appears in multiple tales as a scrying device — in *Vasilísa the Fair*, the Tsarévna "saw the evil thing that had been done, through her magic mirror" (030-062). The **magical horse** appears across many tales as a supernatural steed, often restrained by twelve chains and twelve locks, capable of flight "higher than the treetops in the forest, lower than the clouds in heaven" (072-021). When mounted by the rightful hero, the magical horse "stood there as though he were made of stone, and never pricked an ear" (072-025), signifying submission to the worthy protagonist.

### Beings of Magic

The most prominent magical being is **Koshchéy the Deathless**, described as "the great baleful magician" whose soul "is contained in an egg far away, fearsomely guarded" (P02-016). The **Witch** (Bába Yagá) "lives in the forest, rides the winds in a mortar, devours human flesh, lives in a hut on cocks' legs" (P02-016), representing one of the commonest supernatural figures. Magical servants such as Katomá represent "the marvellous servants whom fortunate princes possess in folklore" and "have magical attributes" (078-009).

### Transformations

Shape-shifting and transformation incidents are identified as part of "the common Aryan stock" of narrative elements, alongside "magic, witches, the valorous youngest son, the beautiful princess wronged by the evil stepmother" (P02-024).

## Folkloric Context

The Bryánsk forest region, with its "wonderful woods which were in ancient times impenetrable, became the legendary home of magic, and of weird happenings" (075-005). The aspen tree holds particular significance: "always associated in Russian folklore with magic and wizardry; it is also said that Judas hanged himself on this tree" (075-005). The underworld itself is characterized as "the home of magic" (078-001), situating magical power outside normal human space.

Ralston notes that the Slav peoples "conserved primitive Aryan customs almost up to the middle of the nineteenth century," and that "the store of primitive folklore of the Slavs has scarcely been touched" (P02-026), suggesting the magic documented in Afanasyev's collection represents only a fraction of a much larger oral tradition.
