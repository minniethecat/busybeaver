# Antiochus

**Antiochus** is the chief advisor to [[hadrian|Emperor Hadrian]] in Hrotsvit's play *[[sapientia|Sapientia]]* and the primary instigator of the persecution against [[sapientia|Sapientia]] and her daughters. While [[hadrian|Hadrian]] initially inclines toward persuasion, Antiochus consistently pushes for coercion and violence, serving as the play's embodiment of political opportunism disguised as loyalty.

## The Denunciation

Antiochus opens the play by reporting to [[hadrian|Hadrian]] that a foreign woman ([[sapientia|Sapientia]]) and her three daughters have arrived in [[rome|Rome]] and are converting citizens to Christianity. He frames this as a threat to the state, not out of religious conviction, but from calculated political instinct.

| Antiochus | My Lord Emperor, what desire has your servant but to see you powerful and prosperous? What ambition apart from the welfare and peace and greatness of the state you rule? (006-010) |

When [[hadrian|Hadrian]] questions whether "a handful of women threaten danger to the state," Antiochus sharpens his argument: Christian wives now refuse to share their husbands' beds — a disruption of the domestic order that [[hadrian|Hadrian]] concedes is "a real danger."

## Strategy of Violence

Antiochus consistently advocates for the harshest measures. When [[hadrian|Hadrian]], struck by the women's beauty and dignity, suggests persuasion first, Antiochus reluctantly agrees but only because "this frail sex is easily moved by flattery." When flattery fails, he urges [[hadrian|Hadrian]] to abandon diplomacy:

| Antiochus | It is beneath your dignity to bandy words with this obstinate woman. Have you not had enough of her insolence and presumption? |

His tactical advice is chillingly pragmatic — target the daughters to break the mother:

| Antiochus | Address yourself to the little girls. If they defy you, do not spare them because of their tender years, but have them put to death. That will teach their obstinate mother a lesson. |

> Antiochus: It is beneath your dignity to bandy words with this obstinate woman. Have you not had enough of her insolence and presumption?
>
> Hadrian: What then?
>
> Antiochus: Address yourself to the little girls. If they defy you, do not spare them because of their tender years, but have them put to death. That will teach their obstinate mother a lesson.
>
> (006-014)

## The Enforcer

Throughout the torture sequences, Antiochus serves as [[hadrian|Hadrian]]'s enforcer and echo. When [[faith|Faith]] calls [[hadrian|Hadrian]] a fool, Antiochus erupts: "You dare to speak to the Emperor so!" He orders the centurions to scourge her, demands that her breasts be mutilated, and calls for increasingly brutal measures. Each escalation of violence passes through Antiochus before execution, making him the instrumental agent of the Emperor's will (006-014).

## Dramatic Function

Antiochus is the necessary dramatic foil to [[hadrian|Hadrian]]'s initial restraint. Where [[hadrian|Hadrian]] is curious and even momentarily admiring, Antiochus is single-minded in his determination to crush the Christian threat. He personifies the machinery of persecution — the courtier who transforms imperial anxiety into lethal policy (006-015). Without Antiochus, [[hadrian|Hadrian]] might have dismissed the women as harmless; with him, the play's [[martyrdom|martyr]]dom narrative becomes inevitable.
