# Mercy

**Mercy** (*misericordia*) is a central divine attribute in Hrotsvit's plays, operating as the theological complement to [[conversion]]. Where [[penance]] is the human response to sin, mercy is the divine initiative that makes [[penance]] possible (001-040).

## In the Hermit Dramas

The hermit dramas display mercy most dramatically. In [[play-Abraham|Abraham]], [[mary|Mary]]'s twenty-year fall into prostitution is answered not by divine retribution but by [[abraham|Abraham]]'s patient pursuit — an embodied allegory of God's mercy seeking the lost soul. In [[play-Paphnutius|Paphnutius]], [[thais|Thais]] the courtesan represents the most extreme case: a life entirely given to sin, transformed by mercy into a model of [[penance]].

> What mighty succour God in His mercy sends to those who humbly trust in Him!
> (001-002)

## In the Martyr Plays

Mercy appears differently in the martyr plays — as the imperial quality the persecutor lacks. Emperor [[constantine|Constantine]] shows mercy to defeated enemies, while [[diocletian|Diocletian]] and [[julian-the-apostate|Julian]] refuse it to Christians. The contrast serves Hrotsvit's political theology: Christian empire extends mercy; pagan empire withholds it.

> And you showed them mercy?
> (001-032)

## Theological Significance

Mercy in Hrotsvit is never cheap — it requires the sinner's full [[repentance]] and sustained [[penance]]. Thais's three-year enclosure and Mary's twenty-year discipline demonstrate that mercy initiates restoration but does not bypass it. This distinguishes Hrotsvit's theology from both legalism (mercy must be earned) and antinomianism (mercy requires nothing).
