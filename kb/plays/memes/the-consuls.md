**The Consuls** (*consules*) are the imperial advisors who appear in court scenes (001-029), functioning as a collective character representing the political establishment that supports — or at least acquiesces in — the persecution of Christians.

## Dramatic Function

The consuls serve a choral function in the imperial court scenes. They echo the emperor's judgments, express appropriate outrage at Christian defiance, and provide the political cover of collective decision-making (001-045). When [[constantine|Constantine]] consults his consuls about [[gallicanus-character|Gallicanus]]'s demand for [[constance|Constance]]'s hand, their responses reveal the constraints of imperial politics: the emperor cannot simply refuse his general.

## The Consuls as Political Morality

The consuls represent the moral danger of political conformism. They do not initiate persecution — that is the emperor's role — but they enable it through their silence and assent (001-046). They are not villains but functionaries, and their moral mediocrity is in some ways more damning than the emperor's active malice.

## The Consuls and Audience

For Hrotsvit's original audience — the canonesses of [[gandersheim|Gandersheim]], many from noble families with connections to the [[ottonian-dynasty|Ottonian]] court — the consuls would have been recognizable as a type: the courtier who follows power rather than conscience. The plays thus function as implicit critiques of court politics, even as they celebrate Ottonian patronage.
