# Callimachus (Play)

**[[callimachus|Callimachus]]** is the third of [[hrotsvit|Hrotsvit]]'s six dramatic works and the most psychologically complex — a play that "timidly sounds a note of passion, rare, if it exists at all, in medieval literature" (Translator's Introduction). It traces the arc of [[callimachus|its protagonist]] from feverish adulterous desire through sacrilegious violation of the dead, death by divine serpent, and finally [[resurrection]] and [[conversion]].

> Callimachus cherishes a guilty passion for Drusiana, not only while she is alive but after she has died in the Lord. He dies from the bite of a serpent, but, thanks to the prayers of Saint John the Apostle, he is restored to life, together with Drusiana, and is born again in Christ.
> (003-001)

## Plot Summary

[[callimachus|Callimachus]], a young pagan, burns with passion for [[drusiana|Drusiana]], the chaste Christian wife of [[andronicus|Andronicus]]. Warned by friends that she "will not even go to the bed of [[andronicus|Andronicus]] although he is a devout Christian," [[callimachus|Callimachus]] persists in his pursuit (003-002). [[drusiana|Drusiana]], learning of his intent, prays for death rather than become the occasion of sin — and dies (003-004).

Even death does not extinguish [[callimachus|Callimachus]]'s desire. He bribes [[fortunatus|Fortunatus]], the custodian of her tomb, to help him violate the corpse. In the sepulchre, a divine serpent appears and kills both men (003-007). The Apostle [[john-and-paul|John]] arrives, and through [[prayer]] restores both [[drusiana|Drusiana]] and [[callimachus|Callimachus]] to life. [[callimachus|Callimachus]] rises transformed, begging to be shown "how a Pagan may change into a Christian, a fornicator into a chaste man" (003-009). [[fortunatus|Fortunatus]], offered the same gift, refuses — and dies permanently.

## Themes

*[[callimachus|Callimachus]]* is Hrotsvit's most daring work. The language of passion — "that feverish desire absorbing the senses and the soul, which leads to sin or madness or self-slaughter" — has no parallel in 10th-century literature. Commentators have noted resemblances to *[[rome|Rome]]o and Juliet*: in both, a sepulchre, a woman's open grave, the shroud lifted by a desperate lover's hand, and two men arriving at the tragic scene bowed by grief.

Yet Hrotsvit transforms the material into Christian [[conversion|conversion narrative]]. [[callimachus|Callimachus]]'s passion, however destructive, contains the seed of genuine desire for beauty — a desire that, redirected, becomes the foundation of his faith. [[fortunatus|Fortunatus]]'s greed, by contrast, offers no such foundation. The play's double [[resurrection]] — one accepted, one refused — makes visible the [[divine-grace|theology of grace]]: redemption is offered to all but received only by those whose souls, however sin-stained, remain capable of turning toward the light.
