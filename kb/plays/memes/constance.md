**Constance** is the [[chastity|consecrated virgin]] daughter of Emperor [[constantine|Constantine]] and the catalyst of the central dramatic conflict in [[play-Gallicanus|Gallicanus]]. (001-001) Having secretly dedicated her [[chastity|virginity]] to God, she faces a political crisis when her father betroths her to [[gallicanus-character|Gallicanus]], the empire's most powerful general. Her solution — sending [[john-and-paul|John and Paul]] to convert [[gallicanus-character|Gallicanus]] on the battlefield — sets the play's entire action in motion, though she herself never leaves the palace. (001-002)

## Biography

Constance (Constantia in Latin) is the daughter of [[constantine|Emperor Constantine]] and a consecrated virgin who has secretly dedicated herself to Christ before the play opens. (001-001) She lives in the imperial palace in [[rome|Rome]], where her father's political obligations — specifically, rewarding [[gallicanus-character|Gallicanus]] for military service — threaten to override her spiritual vow. Unlike the [[martyrdom|martyrs]] who confront pagan authorities directly, Constance works through intermediaries: she converts [[gallicanus-character|Gallicanus]] not by argument but by proxy, dispatching [[john-and-paul|John and Paul]] as her spiritual delegates. (001-002)

## Role in the Plays

Constance never appears on the [[scythia|Scythian]] battlefield where the play's central action occurs, yet she is the invisible axis around which the plot revolves. Her secret vow of [[chastity|virginity]] creates the initial dramatic problem: how can [[gallicanus-character|Gallicanus]] be refused without offending the empire's most powerful military commander? Her solution is theological as well as strategic — she sends [[john-and-paul|John and Paul]], Grand Almoners, not merely to negotiate but to [[conversion|convert]]. (001-002)

## Key Scenes

1. **The Secret Vow** — Before the play opens, Constance has dedicated herself to Christ. Her secrecy creates the play's central conflict: her father doesn't know she cannot marry. (001-001)
2. **The Betrothal Crisis** — [[constantine|Constantine]] promises Constance to [[gallicanus-character|Gallicanus]] as a reward for military service, unaware of her vow. (001-001)
3. **The Strategic Solution** — Constance proposes sending [[john-and-paul|John and Paul]] with Gallicanus to the [[scythia|Scythian]] front — nominally as companions, actually as missionaries. (001-002)
4. **Prayer at Saint Agnes** — Constance prays at the church of Saint Agnes in Rome for the preservation of her [[chastity|virginity]], offering a theological defense of her vow. (001-036)
5. **The Resolution** — [[gallicanus-character|Gallicanus]] returns converted, renounces his claim to Constance, and himself takes a vow of celibacy — the symmetry of mutual renunciation.

## Themes

Constance embodies a distinctive model of female agency in Hrotsvit's dramaturgy. Unlike the [[martyrdom|martyrs]] who confront pagan authorities directly, Constance works through intermediaries — she converts [[gallicanus-character|Gallicanus]] not by argument but by proxy. (001-002) Her [[chastity|virginity]] is preserved not through confrontation but through the [[conversion|transformation]] of her would-be husband into a fellow celibate.

This pattern — a woman's vow of virginity redirecting the course of imperial history — is quintessentially Hrotsvitian. It reflects the Gandersheim canoness's conviction that female spiritual agency, exercised within the constraints of late antique and medieval social structures, could nonetheless shape the destiny of empires. Constance is the most politically consequential of Hrotsvit's virgins: her private vow triggers a chain of events that converts a general, baptizes a barbarian king, and creates a Christian community that survives imperial persecution. (001-001)

The symmetry between Constance and Gallicanus is theologically precise: his vow of celibacy mirrors her vow of [[chastity|virginity]], and they are united not in marriage but in shared renunciation. This is Hrotsvit's answer to the Terentian marriage plot — the comic resolution is not a wedding but a double consecration. (001-002)

## Historical Resonance

The figure of Constantia (renamed Constance in English translation) carries layered historical echoes. Her name invokes the Constantinian dynasty — her father in the play is Constantine the Great — and through it the pivotal moment when Christianity transitioned from persecuted faith to imperial religion. The historical Constantia was Constantine's daughter, known for her theological influence and correspondence with Eusebius of Caesarea. Hrotsvit's Constance is a dramatic reimagining of this historical figure, adapted to serve the play's argument about the priority of spiritual over political allegiance.
