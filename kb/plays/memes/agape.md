**Agape** is the eldest of the three [[chastity|virgin]] sisters — [[agape]], [[chionia]], and [[irena]] — who are the protagonists of [[Dulcitius|Dulcitius (Play II)]]. When [[diocletian|Emperor Diocletian]] commands the sisters to marry his courtiers and sacrifice to the Roman gods, Agape is the first to refuse — and her defiance sets the rhetorical pattern that her younger sisters follow. Her name (Greek: *agapē*, love/charity) marks her as the embodiment of divine love in Hrotsvit's theological scheme. (002-001)

> The martyrdom of the holy virgins Agape, Chionia, and Irena. The Governor Dulcitius seeks them out in the gloom of night, but his desires are frustrated.
>
> (002-001)

## Biography

Agape is a young Christian woman of noble Roman birth, living in [[thessalonica|Thessalonica]] during the [[diocletian|Diocletian]]ic persecution (c. 303 CE). (002-001) With her sisters [[chionia]] and [[irena]], she has dedicated her life to Christ and taken a vow of [[chastity|virginity]]. The Emperor himself — impressed by their beauty and lineage — offers them advantageous marriages to high courtiers, on the sole condition that they sacrifice to the Roman gods. (002-013) For Agape, the choice is not a dilemma: nothing, not even the Emperor's favor, can compel her to deny Christ or compromise her [[chastity|purity]].

## Role in the Plays

Agape is the anchor of the sisterly trio in [[play-Dulcitius|Dulcitius]]. Her dramatic function is to:

1. **Set the rhetorical tone** — In the opening confrontation with [[diocletian|Diocletian]], Agape is the first to speak and the sharpest in debate (002-013)
2. **Establish the theological position** — Her reply "We beg you not to concern yourself about us" dismisses imperial authority with Christian confidence (002-013)
3. **Support her sisters** — When [[diocletian|Diocletian]] dismisses her as "raving," [[chionia]] defends her; the solidarity of the three is unbroken throughout (002-013)
4. **Witness through suffering** — After the [[dulcitius-character|Dulcitius]] interlude (where divine intervention drives the governor mad), Agape and [[chionia|Chionia]] are burned at the stake together (002-023)

## Key Scenes

1. **Confrontation with [[diocletian|Diocletian]]** — Before the Emperor, Agape delivers the first refusal and engages in the sharpest theological debate, directly accusing [[diocletian|Diocletian]] of blasphemy against God. (002-013)
2. **The [[dulcitius-character|Dulcitius]] Humiliation** — While imprisoned, Agape and her sisters [[martyrdom|witness]] [[dulcitius-character|Governor Dulcitius]]'s divinely-inflicted madness — he attempts to assault them in the dark but embraces pots and pans instead, emerging covered in soot. (002-016)
3. **Before [[sisinnius|Sisinnius]]** — When the new governor [[sisinnius|Sisinnius]] demands sacrifice, Agape again refuses with unshaken resolve. (002-023)
4. **[[martyrdom|Martyrdom]]** — Agape and [[chionia]] are burned together; their deaths are the first of the three. (002-023)

> Sisinnius | Agape, and you, Chionia, take my advice. | Agape | And if we do, what then? | Sisinnius | You will sacrifice to the gods.
>
> (002-023)

## Themes

Agape's name is programmatic — she incarnates divine love (*caritas*) in its most radical form: love that refuses the world's highest offers (imperial marriage) and accepts its worst threats (death by fire). (002-013) Her rhetorical fearlessness before [[diocletian|Diocletian]] is a hallmark of Hrotsvit's virgin [[martyrdom|martyr]]s: speech is their primary battlefield, and Agape's tongue is sharper than the Emperor's sword. (002-013) The solidarity of the three sisters — each supporting the other's defiance — makes *[[dulcitius-character|Dulcitius]]* the most collective of Hrotsvit's [[martyrdom|martyr]] plays. (002-023)
