# Obedience

**Obedience** (*oboedientia*) is one of the three evangelical counsels — alongside poverty and [[chastity]] — that structure the religious life in Hrotsvit's monastic universe. In the plays, obedience appears both as a vowed religious virtue and as the dramatic pivot between earthly and divine authority.

## Obedience to God vs. Obedience to Empire

The martyr plays are structured around a conflict of obediences. [[constance|Constance]] obeys her vow of [[chastity]] rather than her father's betrothal plan. [[irena|Irena]] and her sisters refuse the imperial command to sacrifice to pagan gods. In each case, obedience to divine law overrides obedience to earthly power — and the dramatic tension arises from the cost of that choice.

> We offer a perpetual sacrifice of praise to the true God, the eternal Father, to His Son, co-eternal, and to the Holy Ghost.
> (002-026)

## Monastic Obedience

In the hermit dramas, obedience takes a different form — it is the voluntary submission of the penitent to spiritual direction. [[mary|Mary]] submits to [[abraham|Abraham]]'s penitential regime (004-001); [[thais|Thais]] accepts [[paphnutius|Paphnutius]]'s command to enter the sealed cell. In both cases, obedience is the mechanism through which [[penance]] becomes effective. The sinner's will, which led to the fall, is retrained through submission.

## Hrotsvit's Authorial Obedience

In her Prefaces, Hrotsvit frames her own writing as an act of obedience — she writes at the command of her Abbess and for the glory of God, not from personal ambition. This claim of obedient authorship protects her from the charge of pride while simultaneously authorizing her work as divinely sanctioned. The translator notes that Hrotsvit "was in no doubt that the life of chastity, poverty, and obedience is the highest life" (P06-005), confirming obedience as a foundational value of her monastic world.
