# Andronicus

**Andronicus** is a character in Hrotsvit's play *[[callimachus|Callimachus]]*, the husband of [[drusiana|Drusiana]]. A Christian living in Ephesus, he is defined by his relationship to his wife's sanctity and his role as [[martyrdom|witness]] to the miraculous events that follow her death.

## The Bereaved Husband

When [[drusiana|Drusiana]] dies — having prayed for death rather than face [[callimachus|Callimachus]]'s adulterous pursuit — Andronicus is left in grief. His sorrow, however, is cushioned by his faith (003-012). He entrusts [[drusiana|Drusiana]]'s body to the care of the Apostle [[john-and-paul|John]], whom he reveres as a spiritual father (003-013).

## Witness to the Miraculous

Andronicus accompanies [[john-and-paul|John]] to [[drusiana|Drusiana]]'s tomb, where they discover the bodies of [[callimachus|Callimachus]] and [[fortunatus|Fortunatus]] lying dead, slain by a divine serpent. His reaction to these wonders is one of awe rather than horror — the posture of a believer who sees God's hand even in terrible events (003-016).

> | John | Come, Andronicus, let us go to Drusiana's tomb, and commend her soul to Christ in prayer. | | Andronicus | It is like your holiness not to forget one who trusted in you. | | John | Behold! The invisible God appears to us, made visible in the form of a beautiful youth.
>
> (003-016)

When [[john-and-paul|John]] resurrects [[callimachus|Callimachus]], Andronicus [[martyrdom|witness]]es the young man's conversion and accepts him (003-017) — a striking act of Christian forgiveness toward the man who had pursued his wife with adulterous intent.

## Dramatic Role

Andronicus serves a structural rather than psychologically complex function in the play. He is the steady, faithful presence against which the drama of passion, sin, and redemption plays out. His grief dignifies [[drusiana|Drusiana]]'s death; his presence legitimizes [[john-and-paul|John]]'s miraculous authority; his acceptance of the repentant [[callimachus|Callimachus]] models Christian mercy. He is less a character in his own right than the necessary moral baseline from which the play's more dramatic figures — [[callimachus|Callimachus]], [[fortunatus|Fortunatus]], [[drusiana|Drusiana]], and [[john-and-paul|John]] — depart and to which they return.
