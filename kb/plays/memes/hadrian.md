# Hadrian

**Hadrian** (Latin: Hadrianus) is the Roman Emperor and principal antagonist in Hrotsvit's play *[[sapientia|Sapientia]]*. He presides over the interrogation, torture, and execution of the three virgin [[martyrdom|martyr]]s [[faith|Faith]], [[hope|Hope]], and [[charity|Charity]], and their mother [[sapientia|Sapientia]]. Hrotsvit portrays him not as a crude tyrant but as a ruler initially disposed to persuasion and flattery, who escalates to violence only when his rhetorical overtures fail.

## Dramatic Entrance

Hadrian first appears seated on his throne, receiving the report of [[antiochus|Antiochus]], who has discovered that a foreign woman ([[sapientia|Sapientia]]) and her three daughters are converting Romans to Christianity (006-001). Initially skeptical that "a handful of women threaten danger to the state," Hadrian concedes the political reality when [[antiochus|Antiochus]] reports that Christian wives now refuse to share their husbands' beds (006-001).

| Hadrian | This is a real danger, I admit. (006-001) |

## The Rhetorical Strategy

Hrotsvit's Hadrian is distinguished by his initial preference for persuasion over force. When [[sapientia|Sapientia]] and her daughters are brought before him, he is struck by their beauty and dignity (006-003). He rebukes [[antiochus|Antiochus]]'s calls for immediate coercion:

| Hadrian | It would be wiser to ask it as a favour to me at first. Then they may yield. (006-003) |

He approaches [[sapientia|Sapientia]] "as lovingly as if I were their own father," appealing to her noble ancestry and the wisdom suggested by her name (006-003). His flattery proves futile against [[sapientia|Sapientia]]'s theological wit.

> I am amazed at their beauty; I cannot help admiring their noble and dignified manner.
>
> (006-012)

## The Arithmetic Interlude

A distinctive feature of Hadrian's characterisation is his genuine intellectual curiosity. When [[sapientia|Sapientia]] answers his question about her daughters' ages with a discourse on number theory — diminished and augmented numbers, evenly even and evenly uneven — Hadrian listens and asks follow-up questions, genuinely trying to understand (006-003). His eventual confession of bewilderment is almost sympathetic:

| Hadrian | Little did I think that a simple question as to the age of these children could give rise to such an intricate and unprofitable dissertation. (006-003) |

## The Turn to Violence

When persuasion fails, Hadrian grants the prisoners three days to reflect, then — at [[antiochus|Antiochus]]'s urging — directs his threats at the daughters to break their mother's will (006-005). He orders [[faith|Faith]] scourged, her breasts mutilated, and finally beheaded. Each torture elicits not submission but defiance and miraculous immunity to pain, culminating in [[faith|Faith]]'s claim that boiling pitch feels "as cool to me as the morning dew" (006-005).

Hadrian repeats this pattern with [[hope|Hope]] and [[charity|Charity]], each time alternating between incredulity at their resistance and increasingly gruesome tortures. The miracles that attend each [[martyrdom|martyr]]dom — milk flowing from wounds, angelic interventions, spontaneous conversions among the onlookers — render Hadrian's imperial power theatrically impotent.

## Historical Background

The historical Hadrian (Publius Aelius Hadrianus, 76–138 CE) reigned as Roman Emperor from 117 to 138. While the historical Hadrian did preside over the execution of Christians (notably Saint Symphorosa and her seven sons, a legend closely related to the [[sapientia|Sapientia]] narrative), Hrotsvit's portrait is dramatically stylised. Her Hadrian serves as the archetypal pagan emperor: cultured, initially reasonable, but ultimately revealed as the enemy of divine truth.

## Significance in the Play

Hadrian functions as the necessary foil to [[sapientia|Sapientia]]'s wisdom. His imperial authority — the greatest power the Roman world can muster — proves helpless against the spiritual fortitude of a mother and her adolescent daughters. The play's theological point is made visible through this asymmetry: earthly power, however magnificent, cannot compel worship or extinguish faith.
