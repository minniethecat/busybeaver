# Dulcitius (Play)

**[[dulcitius-character|Dulcitius]]** is the second of [[hrotsvit|Hrotsvit]]'s six dramatic works and the most tonally distinctive — a [[martyrdom|martyr play]] infused with comic farce. Where her other [[martyrdom|martyr]] plays treat persecution with solemnity, *[[dulcitius-character|Dulcitius]]* deploys divine delusion to make its Roman persecutors ridiculous: the governor who would rape virgins embraces kitchen pots instead.

> The martyrdom of the holy virgins Agape, Chionia, and Irena. The Governor Dulcitius seeks them out in the silence of the night with criminal intent, but hardly has he entered their dwelling than he becomes the victim of a delusion, under which he mistakes for the objects of his passion the saucepans and frying-pans in the kitchen.
> (002-001)

## Plot Summary

The play opens with Emperor [[diocletian|Diocletian]] interrogating three Christian sisters — [[agape|Agape]], [[chionia|Chionia]], and [[irena|Irena]] — and demanding they worship the pagan gods. When they refuse, he hands them to Governor [[dulcitius-character|Dulcitius]] for examination. [[dulcitius-character|Dulcitius]], overcome by their beauty, attempts to assault them at night — but divine intervention causes him to mistake the kitchen's pots and pans for the women. He passionately embraces sooty cookware while the virgins watch through a crack in the door, providing delighted commentary (002-004).

Emerging blackened and unrecognizable, [[dulcitius-character|Dulcitius]] is beaten by palace ushers and recoiled from by his own wife. [[diocletian|Diocletian]], exasperated, transfers authority to Count [[sisinnius|Sisinnius]], who proves more competent but no less vulnerable to the miraculous. [[agape|Agape]] and [[chionia|Chionia]] are burned — but the flames refuse to touch their bodies (002-011). [[irena|Irena]], the youngest, faces the threat of the brothel, but divine emissaries redirect her to a mountaintop. [[sisinnius|Sisinnius]], attempting pursuit, wanders blindly. He orders [[irena|Irena]] shot with arrows, and she dies declaring she will receive "the [[martyrdom|martyr]]'s palm, and, adorned with the crown of [[virginity]], I shall enter the azure palace of the Eternal King" (002-014).

## Comic and Theological Dimensions

The play's tonal mixture is unprecedented in hagiographic drama. The kitchen scene — in which a would-be rapist passionately kisses frying pans while his intended victims narrate the spectacle — belongs to the tradition of Roman comedy even as it serves Christian theology. The delusion demonstrates that [[divine-grace|God]] protects the faithful not only from death but from dishonour. [[idolatry|Idolatry]] is unmasked as absurd: the persecutor who would compel worship of false gods ends by worshipping cookware. The play's comedy is thus never mere entertainment — it is theological argument by other means.
