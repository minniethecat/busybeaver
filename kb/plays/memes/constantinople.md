# Constantinople

**Constantinople** (modern Istanbul) was the eastern capital of the Roman Empire, founded by Emperor [[constantine|Constantine the Great]] in 330 CE on the site of ancient Byzantium. As the "New Rome," it symbolized the Christianization of imperial power — a transformation Hrotsvit celebrates through the figure of Constantine in [[play-Gallicanus|Gallicanus]].

## Imperial Context

Though not named directly in the plays' dialogue, Constantinople is the implicit seat of the Christian empire Hrotsvit contrasts with pagan [[rome|Rome]]. Under [[constantine|Constantine]], the city represented the union of political authority and Christian faith — the ideal that Hrotsvit's Ottonian patrons sought to emulate in 10th-century Saxony.

## Constantinople and the Plays

[[play-Gallicanus|Gallicanus]] is set in the Constantinian moment when the imperial court is Christian but the army remains largely pagan. The drama of [[gallicanus-character|Gallicanus]]'s [[conversion]] enacts the larger historical transformation: the empire's military arm submitting to the cross. Constantinople is the stage on which this transformation occurs.

## Hrotsvit's Political Vision

For Hrotsvit, writing at [[gandersheim|Gandersheim]] under Ottonian patronage, the Constantinian model of Christian empire was aspirational. The Ottonian dynasty claimed continuity with the Roman imperial tradition through the translation of empire (*translatio imperii*). Hrotsvit's Constantine is thus both a historical figure and a model for her own patrons.
