**Terence** (Publius Terentius Afer, c. 195–159 BCE) is the Roman comic playwright whom [[hrotsvit|Hrotsvit of Gandersheim]] names as her primary literary model. In her Prefaces, Hrotsvit explicitly frames her project as a Christian response to Terence — adopting his form and technique while replacing his tales of seduction with dramas of [[chastity|virginal integrity]] and [[martyrdom|faithful endurance]]. Though Terence does not appear as a character in any play, his presence as the implicit intertextual antagonist — the "master" she follows and subverts — pervades the entire corpus.

> Although Roswitha claims Terence as her master in the art of playwrighting, something higher and holier than his models was always before her.
>
>
> (P05-011)

## Biography

Terence was born in Carthage, North Africa, and brought to [[rome|Rome]] as a slave (P05-011). His master, the senator Terentius Lucanus, recognized his literary talent, educated him, and eventually freed him — hence his name "Terentius." In [[rome|Rome]], Terence wrote six comedies between 166 and 160 BCE, all of which survive (P05-011).: *Andria*, *Hecyra*, *Heauton Timorumenos*, *Eunuchus*, *Phormio*, and *Adelphoe*. His works are distinguished from those of his predecessor Plautus by their refined Latin, psychological subtlety, and humane ethos. Terence died young, possibly on a voyage to Greece to gather more material for adaptation.

Hrotsvit knew Terence through manuscripts preserved in the [[gandersheim-abbey|Gandersheim Abbey]] library (P07-001). Her six plays mirror Terence's surviving corpus in number, and her prefaces engage directly with the Terentian dramatic tradition.

## Role in the Works

Terence does not appear as a character but functions as Hrotsvit's literary foil. Throughout her Prefaces — especially the Epistle to Learned Patrons and the Preface to the Plays — she names him repeatedly as:

1. **The model to emulate** — She admires his polished Latin and dramatic construction
2. **The tradition to subvert** — Where Terence depicted women seduced and abandoned, Hrotsvit dramatizes women who preserve their [[chastity|virtue]] against all threats
3. **The authority to invoke** — By aligning herself with Terence, she claims a classical lineage for her Christian drama

> Fascinated by the charm of the style, I was drawn to make the small talent vouchsafed me by Heaven give, under the form of the same composition, a Christian instead of a pagan note to the sound of this praise.
>
>
> (P07-001)

## Literary Significance

The Terence-Hrotsvit relationship is a foundational dynamic in medieval literature — one of the earliest and most explicit cases of a Christian writer adapting classical forms to sacred content. Hrotsvit's project is more sophisticated than simple imitation: she preserves Terence's five-act structure, his dialogue-driven scenes, and his character types, but systematically inverts his moral universe.

> I was eager to combat, in the manner and speaking of my ability, the shameless acts of lascivious women by the pure and self-restrained actions of holy virgins.
>
>
> (P05-008)

## Themes

Terence represents, for Hrotsvit, the powerful but morally neutral art of dramatic form. By choosing him rather than Seneca (tragedy) or Plautus (farce), Hrotsvit aligns herself with the most humane and psychologically acute of Roman dramatists. Her recurring gesture — "what Terence did for seduction, I will do for [[chastity]]" — is both an act of literary humility and a radical claim of creative authority.
