# Sapientia (Play)

**[[sapientia|Sapientia]]** is the sixth and final play of [[hrotsvit|Hrotsvit]]'s dramatic corpus and, by critical consensus, the best constructed of her [[martyrdom|martyr]]dom plays. It depicts the torture and execution of the noblewoman [[sapientia|Sapientia]] ([[sapientia|Wisdom]]) and her three daughters — [[faith|Faith]], [[hope|Hope]], and [[charity|Charity]] — under Emperor [[hadrian|Hadrian]]. The final scene has been described as "a ray of Sophocles shining through a Christian mind."

> The martyrdom of the holy virgins Faith, Hope, and Charity, who are put to the torture by the Emperor Hadrian and slain in the presence of their mother Sapientia, she encouraging them by her admonitions to bear their sufferings.
> (006-001)

## Plot Summary

The play opens with [[antiochus|Antiochus]], advisor to [[hadrian|Hadrian]], denouncing a foreign woman and her three daughters who are converting Romans to Christianity. [[hadrian|Hadrian]] summons [[sapientia|Sapientia]] and, struck by her beauty and dignity, attempts persuasion before coercion. In one of medieval drama's most distinctive scenes, [[sapientia|Sapientia]] answers a simple question about her daughters' ages with an elaborate discourse on number theory — diminished and augmented numbers, evenly even and evenly uneven — baffling the Emperor while establishing her intellectual superiority (006-003).

Persuasion fails. At [[antiochus|Antiochus]]'s urging, [[hadrian|Hadrian]] targets the daughters. [[faith|Faith]], scourged and mutilated, feels no pain; boiling pitch is "as cool to me as the morning dew" (006-005). [[hope|Hope]] endures similar tortures with equal defiance. [[charity|Charity]], the youngest, is thrown into a furnace that instead consumes her executioners. All three are beheaded. [[sapientia|Sapientia]], having [[martyrdom|witness]]ed each daughter's death with unshakeable encouragement, buries their bodies outside [[rome|Rome]] and prays at their graves. Forty days later, her soul takes flight to heaven while she is still praying (006-014).

## Structure

The play follows a pattern of escalating torture, with each daughter's ordeal more spectacular than the last. [[faith|Faith]] endures scourging, breast-mutilation, gridiron, and boiling pitch before beheading. [[hope|Hope]] matches her sister's endurance. [[charity|Charity]]'s furnace consumes her persecutors instead. [[sapientia|Sapientia]]'s presence throughout — exhorting, encouraging, never wavering — binds the episodes into a unified dramatic arc. The arithmetic interlude in Scene III provides intellectual relief while reinforcing the play's central claim: divine wisdom transcends imperial power.

## Themes

*[[sapientia|Sapientia]]* is Hrotsvit's most intellectually ambitious play. Its heroine does not merely suffer — she teaches, deploying the full resources of classical learning against pagan authority. The play's theology is incarnational: wisdom is not an abstraction but a mother who watches her children die. The final image — [[sapientia|Sapientia]] praying at her daughters' graves, her soul ascending — unites classical dignity with Christian hope. The play that begins with an Emperor on his throne ends with a mother on her knees, and the throne proves the more precarious seat.
