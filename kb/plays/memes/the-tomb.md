# The Tomb

**The Tomb** (*sepulcrum*) is the dramatic setting of the most theologically dense scene in Hrotsvit's corpus — the attempted violation of [[drusiana|Drusiana]]'s corpse in [[play-Callimachus|Callimachus]] and the divine intervention that transforms the scene from horror to [[resurrection]].

## A Liminal Space

The tomb in Hrotsvit is a liminal space — poised between death and life, sin and grace, the old creation and the new. [[callimachus|Callimachus]] enters it driven by passion that pursues its object beyond death (003-013); he leaves it converted, resurrected from a death inflicted by a divine serpent (003-014). The tomb is the most radical conversion-site in the corpus because the conversion requires actual death and resurrection to accomplish.

> In the depths of the sepulchre their lifeless bones work astounding miracles, testifying to their sanctity and to the grace of our Lord Jesus Christ.
> (001-003)

## The Tomb as Miracle-Site

The tomb is also the site of posthumous [[miracle|miracles]]. The bones of [[john-and-paul|John and Paul]] work healings from the sepulchre, authenticating their sanctity and converting those who witness. This reflects the early Christian and medieval cult of martyrs' tombs, where the boundary between heaven and earth was understood to be thin.

## Dramatic Function

The tomb-scene in [[play-Callimachus|Callimachus]] is Hrotsvit's most extreme exploration of the limits of dramatic decorum. A man pursuing a corpse into a tomb; a serpent that kills with a touch; a resurrection effected by apostolic prayer — the scene pushes beyond the plausible into the theological, insisting that divine power operates precisely where human possibility ends.
