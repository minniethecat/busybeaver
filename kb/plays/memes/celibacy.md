# Celibacy

**Celibacy** (*celibatus*) is the vowed renunciation of marriage and sexual activity for religious reasons. In Hrotsvit's plays, celibacy is the male counterpart to female [[virginity]] — both are expressions of [[chastity]], adapted to different life-circumstances.

## Gallicanus's Vow

The most dramatic instance of celibacy in the corpus is [[gallicanus-character|Gallicanus]]'s battlefield vow. Immediately after his [[conversion]] and [[baptism]] by [[john-and-paul|John and Paul]], Gallicanus renounces his claim to [[constance|Constance]] and takes a vow of celibacy. The symmetry is theologically precise: her [[virginity]] is met by his celibacy, and they are united not in marriage but in shared renunciation.

> Later he is converted by John and Paul, Grand Almoners to Constance, and immediately baptized — taking a vow of celibacy.
> (001-002)

## Celibacy and Conversion

The connection between celibacy and [[conversion]] is structural in Hrotsvit's theology. Conversion redirects desire from earthly objects to divine ones; celibacy is the institutional form of that redirection. Gallicanus does not merely cease to desire [[constance|Constance]] — he formalizes his renunciation through a vow that mirrors and completes hers. Even Hrotsvit acknowledges the human cost: the renunciation, however theologically necessary, is acknowledged to be bitter (P06-005).

> O Christ, lover of virginity and fount of chastity! Thou Who through the intercession of Thy holy martyr Agnes hast preserved my body from stain and my mind from pagan errors — I implore Thee to grant my prayer! May Gallicanus, who seeks to gain the love which I can give only to Thee, be turned from his unlawful purpose.
>
> (001-036)


## Monastic Celibacy

The hermit-saints — [[abraham|Abraham]], [[paphnutius|Paphnutius]] — practice celibacy as part of their [[monasticism|monastic]] vocation, though this is assumed rather than dramatized. Their authority to guide fallen women like [[mary|Mary]] and [[thais|Thais]] depends partly on their own sexual discipline: only those who have mastered desire can guide others through its consequences.
