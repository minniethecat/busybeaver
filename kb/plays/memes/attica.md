# Attica

Attica is the daughter of the Roman general Gallicanus and sister of Artemia in Hrotsvit's play *Gallicanus* (001-010).

## Role in the Play

Attica appears together with her sister Artemia as daughters of the general Gallicanus. She is listed in the dramatis personae as "Attica, daughter of Gallicanus" (001-010). When Gallicanus asks for Constance's hand in marriage, Constance devises a plan: she asks Gallicanus to leave his two daughters Attica and Artemia (referred to as "Anemia" in one passage) with her as "pledges of the bond of love" (001-035) while he goes to war, while sending her Almoners John and Paul to accompany him (001-033).

## Conversion and Faith

Attica is brought before Constance along with her sister. The soldiers describe them as "beautiful, wise and virtuous, and in every way worthy of your friendship" (001-036). Constance greets both sisters warmly — "Greeting, my sisters, Artemia and Attica" (001-036) — and immediately begins to instruct them in the Christian faith.

Although Artemia does most of the speaking, Attica is present throughout the conversion scene. The text implies that both sisters are equally receptive to Constance's teaching and become consecrated virgins (001-036).

> Soldiers: Most noble Constance, the illustrious daughters of Gallicanus are here. They are beautiful, wise and virtuous, and in every way worthy of your friendship.
>
> Constance: Greeting, my sisters, Artemia and Attica. Stand up, stand up! No, do not kneel. Salute me rather with a loving kiss.
>
> (001-036)

## Significance

Attica, along with her sister Artemia, represents the second generation of Roman nobility that Hrotsvit portrays as open to Christian conversion. As daughters of a pagan general, their willing acceptance of Christianity through Constance's influence illustrates the theme of spiritual transformation spreading through personal relationship and witness (001-036).
