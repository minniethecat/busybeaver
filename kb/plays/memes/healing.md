# Healing

**Healing** (*sanatio*) is the restoration of physical or spiritual health through divine power. In Hrotsvit's plays, healing appears primarily as posthumous [[miracle]] at martyrs' tombs — the visible sign that sanctity survives death.

## Posthumous Healing

The most notable healings in the corpus occur at the tomb of [[john-and-paul|John and Paul]] after their [[martyrdom]] under [[julian-the-apostate|Julian]]. Their bones work "astounding miracles" from the sepulchre, healing the sick and converting onlookers. This reflects the early Christian and medieval cult of martyrs' relics — the belief that the martyr's body, having been offered in witness, retained divine power. The living hermit-saints were also sought as healers — Antony asks Paphnutius whether he has come "to old Antony for healing" (005-018), acknowledging the therapeutic dimension of the hermit's spiritual authority.

> In the depths of the sepulchre their lifeless bones work astounding miracles, testifying to their sanctity and to the grace of our Lord Jesus Christ.
> (001-003)

## Healing and Resurrection

[[callimachus|Callimachus]]'s [[resurrection]] by the Apostle [[john-and-paul|John]] is healing in its most extreme form — the restoration not of health but of life itself. The man killed by the divine [[the-serpent|serpent]] is raised converted — his first speech a [[confession]] of faith. Resurrection is the healing that addresses not the body's illness but its death.

## Healing and Penance

[[penance|Penance]] is spiritual healing — the long discipline through which the soul wounded by sin is restored. [[mary|Mary]]'s twenty-year enclosure and [[thais|Thais]]'s three-year cell are therapeutic regimes: the body's confinement enables the soul's recovery. Healing in Hrotsvit is never instant — it is a process that requires time, discipline, and the cooperation of the healed.
