# Patience

**Patience** (*patientia*) is the theological virtue of enduring suffering, delay, or provocation without anger or despair. In Hrotsvit's plays, patience operates on two levels: the divine patience of Christ, who withholds judgment even from deliberate sinners, and the human patience of the martyrs and hermits, who endure persecution and the slow work of conversion.

## Divine Patience

The most theologically significant expression of patience in the plays comes from [[paphnutius|Paphnutius]] in his encounter with [[thais|Thais]]. When the courtesan, who is secretly a Christian, reveals that she knows the faith yet continues to destroy souls, Paphnutius does not condemn her — he marvels at the divine restraint that permits her to live:

> O Christ! How wondrous is Thy patience! How wondrous is Thy love! Even when those who believe in Thee sin deliberately, Thou dost delay their destruction!
>
> (005-011)

This passage crystallizes a recurring Hrotsvitian theme: God's patience is not passive tolerance but an active, merciful suspension of judgment that creates space for [[conversion]]. Paphnutius's own mission to Thais is an extension of this divine patience — he travels from the desert to [[alexandria|Alexandria]] to become the instrument of the mercy that patience makes possible.

## Patience in the Hermit Dramas

In *[[paphnutius|Paphnutius]]*, patience is not only divine but also pastoral. Paphnutius's disciples express gratitude for his patience in teaching them: "We are grateful for your patience with us" (005-009). The hermit's patience mirrors God's — he endures the slow understanding of his disciples just as God endures the slow repentance of sinners.

The hermit dramas repeatedly stage patience as the precondition for effective spiritual direction. [[abraham|Abraham]] waits twenty years before going to rescue [[mary|Mary]]; Paphnutius endures a long journey and a dangerous masquerade to reach Thais. In both cases, patience is the virtue that bridges the gap between the desire to save and the actual work of salvation.

## Patience in the Martyr Plays

In *[[sapientia|Sapientia]]*, patience takes the form of endurance under interrogation. [[sapientia|Sapientia]] bears [[hadrian|Hadrian]]'s threats and lectures "with patience" (006-012), hoping to persuade him to abandon paganism. Her serenity under pressure becomes itself a form of witness — patient endurance testifies to the truth of the faith more eloquently than argument.

The contrast is sharply drawn: Hadrian, who boasts of his power, has his patience "exhausted" by the women's resistance (006-014). Where divine patience is inexhaustible, human impatience — especially the impatience of the powerful — reveals the fragility of earthly authority.

In *[[play-Gallicanus|Gallicanus]]*, patience also appears in the political sphere. [[constantine|Constantine]] chides Gallicanus: "this tries my patience" (001-032). Here patience is the imperial virtue of forbearance — the ruler's capacity to endure delay and frustration without rash action.
