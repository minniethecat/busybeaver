# Paul of Thebes

Paul (also known as Paul the Hermit) is a disciple of Saint Antony in Hrotsvit's play *Paphnutius* (005-018). He plays a pivotal role as the recipient of a divine vision that confirms the salvation of the penitent Thais (005-019).

## Role in the Play

Paul is introduced as Antony's disciple. When Paphnutius visits Antony seeking reassurance about Thais's penance, Antony calls upon Paul to recount a heavenly vision he has received (005-018). The vision provides the climactic confirmation that Thais's three years of penance have been accepted (005-019).

## The Vision

Paul describes his vision in detail: "I saw in my vision a splendid bed. It was adorned with white hangings and coverings, and a crown was laid on it, and round it were four radiant virgins. They stood there as if they were guarding the crown" (005-019). Seeing this glory, Paul initially assumes it is prepared for his master Antony. But a divine voice corrects him: "This glory is prepared, not, as you think, for Antony, but for the harlot, Thais!" (005-019).

## Significance

Paul's vision serves as the divine seal on Thais's penance. Through a humble disciple rather than the renowned hermits Paphnutius or Antony, God delivers the message of forgiveness — illustrating the theme that spiritual insight is not reserved for the most celebrated figures. Paul's function in the play is instrumental: his vision triggers Paphnutius's return to Thais to release her from her cell and announce her pardon (005-019).

## Critical Assessment

> With true insight Roswitha makes Paphnutius treat his penitent with far more severity than the hermit Abraham treats Mary. Yet the angelic love of Paphnutius for Thais, thanks to the dramatist's power of suggestion, penetrates through his austerity, although he never manifests it until the moment when he is assured through the vision of Paul, St. Anthony's disciple, that the repentance of the sinner has caused that joy in heaven which exceeds all the joy that can be given by the righteous.
>
> (P06-010)
