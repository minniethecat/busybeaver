# Idolatry

**Idolatry** — the worship of false gods and images — is the central theological conflict in Hrotsvit's [[martyrdom|martyr]]dom plays. The Roman authorities' demand that Christians sacrifice to the pagan gods provides the dramatic pretext for every torture and execution. Hrotsvit's treatment of idolatry is both theological and satirical: she condemns the practice while mocking its objects.

## The Imperial Demand

The pattern is consistent across the [[martyrdom|martyr]]dom plays. A Roman authority — [[diocletian|Diocletian]] in *[[dulcitius-character|Dulcitius]]*, [[hadrian|Hadrian]] in *[[sapientia|Sapientia]]*, [[julian-the-apostate|Julian]] in *[[gallicanus-character|Gallicanus]]* — demands that Christians worship the pagan gods. The demand is framed as civic duty: refusal is not merely impiety but treason.

| [[diocletian|Diocletian]] | The pure and famous race to which you belong and your own rare beauty make it fitting that you should be wedded to the highest in our court. Thus we decree, making the condition that you first promise to deny your Christ and sacrifice to the gods. (002-001)(001-013) |

The Christians' refusal is absolute. [[agape|Agape]] responds: "Nothing can make us deny that Name which all should confess, or let our [[chastity|purity]] be stained" (002-001)(001-013).

## The Satirical Critique

Hrotsvit's critique of idolatry is most pointed in [[irena|Irena]]'s mockery. Asked to worship the gods, she dismantles the very concept of pagan divinity:

| [[irena|Irena]] | A god who can be bought cheap in the marketplace, what is he but a slave? (002-001)(001-013) |

This argument — that a manufactured image cannot be divine — echoes the Hebrew prophets and the early Christian apologists. But Hrotsvit gives it a distinctive edge: the absurdity of idolatry is not merely argued but demonstrated. [[dulcitius-character|Dulcitius]]'s delusion, in which he passionately embraces pots and pans, literalizes the equation of idols with household objects. The governor who would force virgins to worship false gods ends by worshipping — with kisses and embraces — his own kitchenware (002-004)(001-016).

> A god who can be bought cheap in the marketplace, what is he but a slave?
>
> (002-013)

## Idolatry as Adultery

For Hrotsvit's Christian virgins, idolatry and unchastity are structurally parallel. Both involve giving to a false object what belongs rightly to Christ alone. [[constance|Constance]] frames her refusal to marry [[gallicanus-character|Gallicanus]] in the language of exclusive divine possession: "the love which I can give only to Thee" (001-004)(002-011). Sacrificing to idols would be spiritual adultery — a violation of the virgin's vow as surely as physical defilement.

## The Political Dimension

Hrotsvit's plays recognize idolatry's political function. [[antiochus|Antiochus]] articulates the Roman perspective with clarity: "What disturbs the peace and harmony of states more than religious differences?" (006-001). Idolatry is the state religion; Christianity, by refusing it, is sedition. The [[martyrdom|martyr]]s die not merely as religious dissidents but as threats to the Roman order — an order whose gods are, in [[irena|Irena]]'s devastating phrase, merely "a bit of metal" (006-005).
