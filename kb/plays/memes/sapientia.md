**Sapientia** (Latin for "Wisdom") is the protagonist and titular martyr-mother of Hrotsvit's [[play-Sapientia|Play VI]] — a Greek noblewoman who has come to [[rome|Rome]] with her three daughters [[faith|Faith]], [[hope|Hope]], and [[charity|Charity]]. She embodies divine wisdom in her intellectual brilliance, her unshakeable faith, and her heroic encouragement of her children through [[martyrdom]]. She represents the unity of classical learning and Christian faith that [[hrotsvit|Hrotsvit]] herself, as a Benedictine canoness of [[gandersheim-abbey|Gandersheim Abbey]], would have embodied.

> The martyrdom of the holy virgins Faith, Hope, and Charity, who, encouraged by their most holy mother Sapientia, endured death under the Emperor Hadrian.
>
>
> (006-001)

## Biography

Sapientia introduces herself as the daughter of Greek princes who left her homeland "to know the truth" (006-005). and to learn more of the Christian faith. Her noble lineage is evident in her bearing, which even the emperor [[hadrian|Hadrian]] cannot help but admire. Hadrian himself acknowledges the correspondence between her name and her character (006-005):

> | Hadrian | The splendour of your ancestry is blazoned in your face, and the wisdom of your name sparkles on your lips.
>
>
> (006-005)

Sapientia has come to [[rome|Rome]] with her three daughters and, when summoned before [[hadrian|Hadrian]], refuses to sacrifice to Roman gods. Her trial becomes an extended intellectual contest in which she deploys arithmetic, theology, and maternal authority against imperial power.

## Role in the Plays

Sapientia is the sole protagonist of [[play-Sapientia|Sapientia (Play VI)]], the last of Hrotsvit's martyr plays. Her dramatic function is unique: she is not martyred alongside her daughters but must endure the spiritual martyrdom of witnessing their torture and death. Her role shifts across the play's three movements:

| Phase | Role | Action |
|-------|------|--------|
| Tribunal (006-001–006-005) | Intellectual antagonist to [[hadrian|Hadrian]] | Arithmetic display; refuses sacrifice |
| Prison (006-008–006-013) | Mother-exhorter | Encourages daughters to persevere |
| Burial & Death (006-014) | Mourner transfigured | Buries daughters; dies in prayer 40 days later |

## Key Scenes

### The Arithmetic Display

One of Hrotsvit's most distinctive dramatic inventions is the scene in which Sapientia, asked her daughters' ages, delivers an elaborate disquisition on number theory. [[charity|Charity]] has lived "a diminished evenly even number of years" (8, or two olympiads); [[hope|Hope]] "a number also diminished, but evenly uneven" (10, or two lustres); and [[faith|Faith]] "an augmented number, unevenly even" (12, or three olympiads) (006-005).

This display baffles [[hadrian|Hadrian]] but serves multiple dramatic purposes: it establishes Sapientia's intellectual superiority over the pagan emperor; it aligns her name (Wisdom) with the classical tradition of learned discourse; and it introduces the play's central theme — that divine wisdom transcends earthly power.

> | Sapientia | It would be unprofitable if it did not lead us to appreciate the wisdom of our Creator, and the wonderous knowledge of the Author of the world, Who in the beginning created the world out of nothing, and set everything in number, measure, and weight.
>
>
> (006-005)

### Mother of Martyrs

Sapientia's defining role is as mother-encourager. From the prison cell to the tribunal, she exhorts her daughters to persevere, inverting natural maternal instinct — she rejoices in their [[martyrdom]] because it guarantees their heavenly reward (006-013):

> | Sapientia | Oh, my dearest ones! My beloved children! Do not let this narrow prison sadden you. Do not be frightened because you see this place of torment.
>
>
> (006-008)

> | Sapientia | We shall be purified by suffering, and after suffering we shall be united to the spotless virgins in the kingdom of heaven — adorned with the crown of virginity and glorified by your martyrdom.
>
>
> (006-013)

### Transfiguration and Death

After burying her daughters' bodies with honour outside [[rome|Rome]], Sapientia prays at their graves. Forty days later, her spirit takes flight to heaven while she is still praying (006-014). The French commentator Magnin described this final scene as "a ray of Sophocles shining through a Christian mind" — Sapientia's death as the culmination of wisdom's journey, the mother reunited with her children in eternal glory.

## Themes

Sapientia operates on multiple symbolic levels. On the surface, she is a Roman noblewoman and mother. Allegorically, her name (Latin for "Wisdom") aligns her with the Old Testament tradition of Lady Wisdom (*Sophia* in the Septuagint, *Sapientia* in the Vulgate), particularly as depicted in Proverbs 8–9 and the Wisdom of Solomon.

The three daughters — Fides ([[faith|Faith]]), Spes ([[hope|Hope]]), and Caritas ([[charity|Charity]]) — literalize the Pauline triad of theological virtues (1 Corinthians 13:13). Their martyrdom before their mother's eyes inverts the natural order: Sapientia must witness the death of Faith, Hope, and Charity before achieving her own transfiguration. This narrative structure suggests that wisdom is not prior to the virtues but is perfected through their costly exercise.

The emperor [[hadrian|Hadrian]]'s demand that the daughters worship idols represents the conflict between pagan philosophy (which sought wisdom through reason alone) and Christian revelation (which locates wisdom in sacrificial love). Hrotsvit thus stages a theological argument in dramatic form: true *sapientia* is inseparable from *caritas*.

## Literary Analysis

Sapientia is Hrotsvit's most intellectually ambitious character — a woman who defeats the Roman Empire not with arms but with arithmetic, who transforms maternal love into the instrument of [[martyrdom]], and whose name is both identity and programme. The arithmetic passage (006-005) is the longest non-dramatic digression in the corpus, and its placement at the play's climax — rather than as prologue or epilogue — signals Hrotsvit's confidence in her audience's capacity for theological abstraction within dramatic form.

Sapientia also represents Hrotsvit's most direct authorial self-portrait. Like Hrotsvit, she is a woman of learning operating within a male-dominated intellectual tradition; like Hrotsvit, she deploys classical knowledge in service of Christian truth; like Hrotsvit, she is defined by the union of *sapientia* (wisdom) and *virginitas* (virginity). The play can be read as Hrotsvit's meditation on her own vocation — the canoness as mother of virtues rather than children.
