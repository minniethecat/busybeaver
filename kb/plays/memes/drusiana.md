**Drusiana** is the saintly wife of [[andronicus|Andronicus]] in [[Callimachus|Callimachus (Play III)]]. (003-001) When the pagan youth [[callimachus|Callimachus]] conceives a guilty passion for her, she prays for death rather than be the occasion of sin — and her prayer is answered. (003-012) Even after her death, [[callimachus|Callimachus]]'s lust pursues her to the tomb, where divine intervention strikes him dead, only for both to be [[conversion|resurrected]] through the prayers of the Apostle [[john-and-paul|John]]. Drusiana's story is Hrotsvit's most theologically ambitious treatment of the body — desired, dead, and restored. (003-001)

>
> (003-001)

## Biography

Drusiana is a Christian woman of Ephesus, married to [[andronicus|Andronicus]] and known for her exceptional [[chastity|virtue]]. (003-001) She has dedicated herself to Christ and lives a life of such evident sanctity that she is a "disciple" to the Apostle [[john-and-paul|John]] in the play's framework. When the wealthy pagan [[callimachus|Callimachus]] confesses his obsessive love for her, Drusiana's response is radical: she prays for death, preferring to leave the world than become an occasion of sin — even unwillingly. (003-012) Her death is the first turn in a drama of transgression, punishment, and miraculous restoration.

## Role in the Plays

Drusiana is the passive center around which [[Callimachus]] revolves — not an agent in her own right but the object of desire whose very passivity (in death) becomes the instrument of divine action:

1. **The Object of Lust** — [[callimachus|Callimachus]]'s obsession with Drusiana drives the entire plot (003-002)
2. **The Voluntary Death** — Her prayer to die rather than cause sin is an extreme act of spiritual self-defense (003-012)
3. **The Corpse Pursued** — Even dead, [[callimachus|Callimachus]] pursues her body to the tomb (003-011)
4. **The Miraculous [[resurrection|Resurrection]]** — Through the Apostle [[john-and-paul|John]]'s prayer, Drusiana is restored to life, bringing her would-be violator [[callimachus|Callimachus]] to [[conversion|repentance]] (003-017)

## Key Scenes

1. **[[callimachus|Callimachus]]'s Confession** — The pagan youth confesses his passion for Drusiana to his friends, who warn him against violating a Christian marriage. (003-002)
2. **Drusiana's [[prayer|Prayer]]** — Learning of [[callimachus|Callimachus]]'s designs, Drusiana prays to die; her death is immediate, preserving her [[chastity|purity]]. (003-012)
3. **The Tomb Scene** — [[callimachus|Callimachus]] and his servant [[fortunatus|Fortunatus]] break into Drusiana's tomb to violate her corpse; a serpent strikes [[fortunatus|Fortunatus]] dead, and [[callimachus|Callimachus]] dies of terror. (003-015)
4. **The [[resurrection|Resurrection]]** — The Apostle [[john-and-paul|John]] arrives, prays, and raises both Drusiana and [[callimachus|Callimachus]] from death — she to renewed life, he to [[conversion|repentance]]. (003-017)

## Themes

Drusiana embodies radical spiritual self-preservation — choosing death rather than being, even involuntarily, the occasion of another's sin. (003-012) This is Hrotsvit's most extreme statement on [[chastity|purity]]: the body must be protected not only from violation but from being *looked at* with lust. The resurrection reverses the play's logic — Drusiana restored to life after [[callimachus|Callimachus]] is converted means the threat has been neutralized, and the saint can safely re-enter the world. (003-017) Her story contrasts with [[mary|Mary of Abraham]] and [[thais|Thais]]: she is the virgin who never fell, even when pursued beyond the grave. (003-001)
