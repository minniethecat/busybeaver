**Rome** is the political and spiritual center of gravity in [[hrotsvit|Hrotsvit]]'s dramatic universe — the seat of imperial power from which [[constantine|Constantine]] rules and [[julian-the-apostate|Julian the Apostate]] persecutes, the city whose senatorial and military elite provide the antagonists for her Christian protagonists. It functions as both a literal place and a theological concept: the City of Man against which the City of God is measured.

> You set out from Rome with great pomp. The moment he arrived in Rome he went to the Church of Saint...
> (001-042)

## Geographic Context

Rome in Hrotsvit's plays is the 4th-century imperial capital — the political, military, and religious center of the Mediterranean world. The plays move between Rome and its dependencies: the [[scythia|Scythian]] frontier in [[play-Gallicanus|Gallicanus]], the Egyptian desert in [[play-Paphnutius|Paphnutius]], and the martyrdom site outside the city walls in [[play-Sapientia|Sapientia]]. Rome is the origin point of imperial authority and the destination of military triumph:

## Historical Significance

Rome in the 4th century CE — the dramatic setting of Hrotsvit's plays — was a city in transition. Constantine had founded Constantinople as a "New Rome" in 330, shifting the empire's center of gravity eastward. The Rome of the plays is thus already a city conscious of its own decline as a political capital, even as it retained immense symbolic prestige. The tension between Rome's historical grandeur and its diminished present mirrors the plays' central theological argument: that temporal power, however magnificent, is transient.

For Hrotsvit's 10th-century audience at [[gandersheim-abbey|Gandersheim Abbey]], Rome carried additional layers of meaning. It was the seat of the papacy, the destination of pilgrimage, and the source of monastic reform movements that had shaped Benedictine life in Saxony. The Ottonian emperors — Hrotsvit's patrons — had been crowned in Rome, making the city a living political symbol as well as a historical setting.

## In the Plays

### Gallicanus

Rome is the stage for [[gallicanus-character|Gallicanus]]'s departure and triumphant return. [[constantine|Constantine]]'s court in Rome is where the betrothal to [[constance|Constance]] is negotiated, where [[gallicanus-character|Gallicanus]] departs "with great pomp" for the [[scythia|Scythian]] campaign (001-042), and where he returns — converted — to renounce his military ambitions (001-001). The church of Saint Agnes in Rome is the site of [[constance|Constance]]'s prayer and the preservation of her [[chastity|virginity]].

### Callimachus

In *[[play-Callimachus|Callimachus]]*, Rome functions as the background of pagan Roman society — the world of senatorial ambition and erotic pursuit that [[callimachus|Callimachus]] must renounce upon encountering the risen [[drusiana|Drusiana]] and the Apostle John.

### Sapientia

In *[[play-Sapientia|Sapientia]]*, the imperial court in Rome is the seat of [[hadrian|Hadrian]]'s persecution. [[sapientia|Sapientia]] and her daughters [[faith|Faith]], [[hope|Hope]], and [[charity|Charity]] are brought before the emperor in Rome and martyred five miles outside the city walls (006-001):

> Sapientia, having buried her martyred children outside Rome, lifts up her soul in an ecstasy of prayer.
> (P06-007)

## Symbolic Significance

Rome in Hrotsvit's conception represents the City of Man — the temporal order of power, wealth, and coercion that stands in opposition to the City of God. Every Christian [[martyrdom|martyr]] in the plays is a Roman subject who refuses Rome's ultimate claim: the right to command religious allegiance. The martyrs' deaths outside the city walls are spatial theology — the true city is not Rome but the heavenly Jerusalem. This Augustinian framework (from *De Civitate Dei*) is implicit throughout the plays: Rome is simultaneously the greatest earthly city and the most dangerous spiritual temptation.

The contrast between Rome and [[gandersheim|Gandersheim]] — imperial capital vs. Saxon abbey, 4th century vs. 10th — structures Hrotsvit's entire historical imagination. She writes from the margin of empire about its center, translating Roman imperial drama into the liturgical rhythms of Benedictine life.
