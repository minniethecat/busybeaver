# Bradan

Bradan is the King of the Scythians in Hrotsvit's play *Gallicanus*. He appears as the antagonist in the military conflict that forms the play's central action.

## Role in the Play

Bradan leads the Scythian army against the Roman forces commanded by Gallicanus. He is listed in the dramatis personae as "Bradan, King of the Scythians" (001-017). His forces initially overwhelm the Roman legions, and the tribunes urge Gallicanus to surrender (001-040).

## The Battle and Surrender

At the height of the battle, Bradan's forces appear to be winning decisively. One of his soldiers exclaims: "Woe to us, King Bradan! Fortune, who but now promised us victory, was mocking us. Our men are weakening, their strength is exhausted" (001-040). The tide turns when Gallicanus vows to become a Christian, and divine intervention secures the Roman victory.

Bradan surrenders personally, pleading: "Gallicanus, do not destroy us! Be merciful! Spare our lives and do with us what you will" (001-040). Gallicanus accepts his surrender and demands hostages and tribute, declaring that the Scythians "shall live happy under a Roman peace" (001-040).

> One of the Enemy: Woe to us, King Bradan! Fortune, who but now promised us victory, was mocking us. Our men are weakening, their strength is exhausted — they have lost heart and are giving up the struggle.
>
> Bradan: I am uncertain what to do. A strange faintheartedness has seized me also. There is but one course — we must surrender.
>
> Bradan: Gallicanus, do not destroy us! Be merciful! Spare our lives and do with us what you will.
>

Later, Gallicanus recounts to Emperor Constantine how he "came face to face with their King, by name Bradan. Suddenly overcome by the strangest terror he threw himself at my feet, surrendered with his whole army, and promised to pay tribute in perpetuity to the ruler of the Roman world" (001-043).

## Significance

Bradan functions as a foil to Gallicanus's conversion narrative. His defeat is attributed not to Roman military prowess but to divine intervention following Gallicanus's promise to become a Christian. Bradan's sudden terror and surrender represent the spiritual triumph of faith over worldly power — a recurring theme in Hrotsvit's dramas.
