**Conrad Celtes** (1459–1508) was a German Renaissance humanist, poet, and scholar — the man who discovered [[hrotsvit|Hrotsvit of Gandersheim]]'s manuscript and brought her works to the attention of European scholarship.

## The Discovery

In the late 1490s, Celtes discovered a manuscript of Hrotsvit's works in the monastery of St. Emmeram in Regensburg (P05-009). Recognizing its significance, he prepared the first printed edition, which appeared in Nuremberg in 1501 (P03-004) with woodcut illustrations — among them the famous image of Hrotsvit presenting her works to the Emperor. Celtes's edition included the six plays, the eight poetic legends, and the historical poems.

## Humanist Appropriation

Celtes presented Hrotsvit as a German national treasure — proof that Germany had a classical literary tradition to rival Italy's. He dedicated the edition to the Elector of Saxony and emphasized Hrotsvit's Saxon origins (P05-007). His framing of Hrotsvit as "the German Sappho" shaped her reception for centuries.

## Impact

Celtes's edition made Hrotsvit available to Renaissance Europe. Without his discovery, her works might have remained unknown — a manuscript in a monastic library, unread for centuries. The survival of Hrotsvit's corpus is, in a direct sense, owed to this German humanist.

## Scholarly Witness

> Conrad Celtes, the well-known humanist, discovered the manuscript, the writing of which cannot be earlier than the 9th, or later than the 10th century, in the library of the Benedictine monastery of St. Emmeran, Ratisbon, in the last days of the 15th century. In the year 1501 it was printed.
>
> (P05-009)
