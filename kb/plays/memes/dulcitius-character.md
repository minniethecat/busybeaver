# Dulcitius

**Dulcitius** is the governor of [[thessalonica|Thessalonica]] and the titular comic antagonist of Hrotsvit's play *Dulcitius*. Charged by [[diocletian|Emperor Diocletian]] with examining three Christian virgins — [[agape|Agape]], [[chionia|Chionia]], and [[irena|Irena]] — he becomes the victim of a divinely sent delusion that transforms his attempted seduction into farce. He is one of Hrotsvit's most memorable creations: a persecutor rendered ridiculous by divine power.

## The Lustful Governor

Assigned to interrogate the three sisters, Dulcitius is immediately overcome by their beauty. His first words upon seeing them set the tone for his character: "Ye Gods, but these girls are beautiful! What grace, what charm!" (002-002). When soldiers warn him that the women's faith is too strong for flattery, he responds with blunt pragmatism: "Then I shall woo in another fashion — with torture!" (002-002).

His real plan, however, is seduction. He orders the virgins locked in a room adjoining the kitchen, explaining — with transparent dishonesty — that he can "visit them oftener" there (002-002).

## The Divine Delusion

The play's central comic scene occurs when Dulcitius enters the virgins' dwelling in darkness, intending to assault them. A divine intervention causes him to mistake the kitchen's pots, pans, and kettles for the women. He embraces and kisses them passionately until his face and clothes are black with soot (002-004).

The virgins watch through a crack in the door, providing the audience with a running commentary of delighted mockery:

| [[irena|Irena]] | Now he presses the saucepans tenderly to his breast, now the kettles and frying-pans! He is kissing them hard! (002-004) |

| [[agape|Agape]] | I am glad. His body should turn black — to match his soul, which is possessed of a devil. (002-004) |

## Public Humiliation

Emerging from the kitchen, Dulcitius is unrecognizable. His own soldiers flee in terror, crying "This devil means us no good!" (002-005). The palace ushers beat him and throw him down the steps. Even his wife fails to recognize him:

> | Wife of Dulcitius | My lord, my lord, what evil has come on you? Have you lost your reason, Dulcitius? Have the Christ-worshippers put a spell on you? (002-007) |

Only when his wife speaks does Dulcitius realize he has been supernaturally deceived: "Now at last I know! Those artful women have made an ass of me!" (002-007).

## Vengeance and Replacement

Humiliated, Dulcitius orders the virgins stripped naked in public — but another miracle prevents the soldiers from removing their garments. The soldiers find Dulcitius mysteriously asleep and unable to be woken (002-008). [[diocletian|Diocletian]], exasperated, transfers the case to [[sisinnius|Count Sisinnius]].

## Comic and Theological Dimensions

Dulcitius is a rare figure in hagiographic drama: a genuinely comic villain. His lust, vanity, and blundering make him absurd rather than terrifying. Yet Hrotsvit's comedy serves theology — Dulcitius's delusion demonstrates that God protects the faithful not only from death but from dishonour, and that the power of persecutors is not merely evil but, against divine will, impotent and ridiculous.
