# Resurrection

**Resurrection** — the miraculous restoration of the dead to life — appears in Hrotsvit's *[[callimachus|Callimachus]]* as the climactic divine intervention that transforms the play's spiritual landscape. Through the prayer of the Apostle [[john-and-paul|John]], both [[drusiana|Drusiana]] and her would-be violator [[callimachus|Callimachus]] are raised from death, making resurrection the literal mechanism of the play's redemptive conclusion.

## The Double Death

The resurrection in *[[callimachus|Callimachus]]* is preceded by a double death. [[drusiana|Drusiana]] has died after praying for release rather than become the occasion of [[callimachus|Callimachus]]'s sin (003-004). [[callimachus|Callimachus]] and his accomplice [[fortunatus|Fortunatus]], attempting to violate [[drusiana|Drusiana]]'s corpse, are slain by a divine serpent in the tomb (003-007). Three bodies lie in the sepulchre when the Apostle [[john-and-paul|John]] arrives.

## John's Prayer

The Apostle's prayer at the tomb is both supplication and command. Drawing on Christ's promise that his followers would perform greater works, [[john-and-paul|John]] calls [[drusiana|Drusiana]] back to life. Her resurrection is a restoration — she returns as the chaste Christian woman she was, vindicated by divine protection of her body even in death (003-008).

## Callimachus's Resurrection as Conversion

[[callimachus|Callimachus]]'s resurrection is more theologically freighted. He dies in the act of sin; his restoration to life is simultaneously a restoration to spiritual possibility. He rises not as the lust-driven pagan he was but as a penitent:

| [[callimachus|Callimachus]] | Oh, hasten then to help a man in dire need! Give me some comfort! Help me to throw off the grief which crushes me! Show me how a Pagan may change into a Christian, a fornicator into a chaste man! (003-009) |

Physical resurrection enables spiritual rebirth. The pattern — death as consequence of sin, resurrection as opportunity for conversion — mirrors the theological structure of baptism itself.

## The Refused Resurrection

[[fortunatus|Fortunatus]] provides the dark counterpoint. [[john-and-paul|John]] offers him the same gift, but [[fortunatus|Fortunatus]] refuses. His "unfortunate" choice — to remain dead rather than accept resurrection on Christ's terms — dramatizes the limits of even miraculous grace. Resurrection can restore life; it cannot compel belief (003-009).

## Theological Significance

Resurrection in Hrotsvit is never merely spectacular. It always serves conversion — [[drusiana|Drusiana]]'s restoration vindicates chastity; [[callimachus|Callimachus]]'s enables his transformation; [[fortunatus|Fortunatus]]'s refusal underscores human freedom. The miracle points beyond itself to the greater resurrection promised to all believers, of which these individual raisings are both sign and foretaste.


Andronicus marvels at the divine judgment revealed in the resurrection:

> What astonishes me most is that the Divine Voice should have promised the resurrection of him who planned the crime, and not of him who was only an accomplice.
>
> (003-017)
