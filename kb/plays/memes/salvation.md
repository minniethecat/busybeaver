# Salvation

**Salvation** (*salus*) is the ultimate horizon of every dramatic action in Hrotsvit's plays. Whether through [[martyrdom]], [[penance]], or [[conversion]], the movement of each plot is toward the soul's deliverance from sin and its restoration to God. Callimachus confesses his sins and John assures him that "it will be your salvation" (003-017) — the trajectory of every Hrotsvit play in miniature.

## Paths to Salvation

Hrotsvit dramatizes multiple paths to salvation, corresponding to different spiritual conditions. The virgin martyrs — [[agape|Agape]], [[chionia|Chionia]], [[irena|Irena]], [[faith|Faith]], [[hope|Hope]], [[charity|Charity]] — achieve salvation through the defence of [[chastity]] unto death. The converted pagans — [[gallicanus-character|Gallicanus]], [[callimachus|Callimachus]] — achieve it through dramatic reversal of life-direction followed by [[martyrdom]]. The fallen penitents — [[mary|Mary]], [[thais|Thais]] — achieve it through sustained [[penance]] after [[conversion]].

## Salvation and the Sacraments

[[Baptism]] is the ordinary instrument of salvation in the plays. Gallicanus receives baptism on the battlefield from [[john-and-paul|John and Paul]] immediately after his conversion. The connection is causal: conversion leads to baptism, which seals salvation. For those already baptized who fall into sin — Mary and Thais — salvation is regained through [[penance]], understood as a "second baptism" of tears and discipline. Ephrem articulates the paradox: "she who was their ruin may be their salvation" (004-015).

## Universal Hope

Hrotsvit's soteriology is notably optimistic. No character is beyond salvation: the courtesan Thais, the necrophiliac Callimachus, the apostate-in-potential Gallicanus — all are saved. Thais, dying in her cell, hears the words: "I have yearned for your salvation" (005-020). The only characters excluded from salvation are those who refuse it, principally the persecuting emperors. The plays thus make a theological argument through dramatic form: salvation is universally offered but must be freely received.


Abraham reports on Mary's penitential intercession:

> She prays continually for the men who through her were tempted to sin, and begs that she who was their ruin may be their salvation.
>
> (004-015)
