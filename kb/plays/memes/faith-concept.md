# Faith (Fides)

Faith (*fides*) is the theological virtue of belief in and fidelity to God, foundational to Christian identity and celebrated throughout Hrotsvit's plays as the wellspring of the martyrs' courage. In [[play-Sapientia|Sapientia]], it is personified in the character of [[faith|Faith]] (Fides), one of Sapientia's three daughters alongside Hope (Spes) and Charity (Caritas).

## Faith as Confession

Throughout the plays, faith is expressed as public confession — a declaration of Christian allegiance before hostile authorities. When Gallicanus converts, he declares: "purified in the waters of baptism, I have surrendered myself completely to the keeping of Christ and will make a humble confession of faith in the Church of the Apostles" (001-041). The virgin martyrs of Dulcitius similarly ground their resistance on faith: "you exhort us to confess our faith and keep our vow of purity" (001-036). Their persecutors are baffled precisely because faith renders earthly threats powerless — "Their faith is too strong," the soldiers report of Agape, Chionia, and Irena (002-014).

Gallicanus's conversion speech to Constantine exemplifies faith as total surrender:

> For my own part, purified in the waters of baptism, I have surrendered myself completely to the will of God. I am ready to renounce even your daughter, whom I love more than anything in the world. I wish to abstain from marriage that I may devote myself wholly to the service of the Virgin's Son.
>
> (001-043)

## Faith and Martyrdom

The link between faith and martyrdom recurs throughout the corpus. John and Paul instruct Callimachus: "We are taught by our faith that man must forgive his fellow" (003-017). The martyrdom narratives consistently present faith not merely as intellectual assent but as lived fidelity — the willingness to die rather than deny Christ. Constantia's father Constantine permits her to "be faithful to your vow, as a sovereign prince should permit his daughter to keep her promise to God" (001-033), framing faith as the bond that even imperial authority must respect.

## The Sapientia Personification

In [[play-Sapientia|Sapientia]], faith becomes a dramatic character. Fides, the eldest daughter of Sapientia (Wisdom), is martyred before her mother's eyes yet encourages her sisters to follow. This personification draws on the Christian tradition of the three theological virtues (1 Corinthians 13:13) and dramatizes faith as both gift and choice.
