# Peace

**Peace** (*pax*) in Hrotsvit's plays operates on multiple levels: the political peace of the Roman Empire, the spiritual peace of the Christian soul, and the eternal peace of the blessed in heaven. The word recurs throughout the text as a formula of valediction, a political demand, and a theological promise.

## Political Peace

In *[[play-Gallicanus|Gallicanus]]*, peace is framed as a Roman imperial condition. The emperor's concern is the refusal of a foreign power to "make peace with Rome" (001-032), and the promise to defeated enemies is that they "shall live happy under a Roman peace" (001-040). Here peace is the *Pax Romana* — the stability enforced by imperial power.

However, Hrotsvit subtly transforms the Roman ideal. The peace offered by Constantine is personal and humane, not merely military. His valediction to Gallicanus — "Go in peace, my lord" (001-033) — echoes the Christian liturgical dismissal, blending imperial and spiritual registers.

## Spiritual Peace

The recurring valediction "Go in peace" (001-033, 005-011) carries theological weight beyond its formulaic surface. In the hermit drama *[[play-Paphnutius|Paphnutius]]*, this phrase sends disciples into the world on missions of conversion and mercy.

The spiritual peace of the faithful dead is also invoked: "those souls we know rejoice in peace" (003-013). In *[[play-Abraham|Abraham]]*, Ephrem speaks of "the harbour of her innocence and peace" (004-009) — a metaphor that transforms peace from a political condition into a spiritual destination.

The ultimate peace is eschatological. Paphnutius's final words celebrate "the beatitude of everlasting peace" (005-015), linking earthly virtue to heavenly reward.

> Be at peace, most holy virgins. Persevere in the fear of God.
> (001-044)

## Peace Lost and Restored

Peace in Hrotsvit is not static — it can be lost through sin and restored through penance. Antony's lament in *[[play-Paphnutius|Paphnutius]]* gives the most poignant expression to this fragility: "it is three years since my peace was broken and disturbed" (005-018). The saint's peace is vulnerable, dependent on continued fidelity, and its loss is felt as a wound.

The restoration of peace comes through martyrdom. In *[[play-Sapientia|Sapientia]]*, the dying Wisdom asks her children: "give me the kiss of peace" (006-014) — a liturgical gesture that transforms impending death into a rite of reconciliation. The matrons' final prayer petitions God to "give rest and peace to their bones" (006-018), closing the play with peace as the ultimate gift the faithful bestow upon the martyred dead. Peace is thus both the soul's starting point and its destination — lost through sin, restored through sacrifice, perfected in eternity.
