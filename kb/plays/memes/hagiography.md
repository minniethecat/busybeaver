# Hagiography

Hagiography is the literary tradition of saints' lives — biographies and narratives that recount the virtues, miracles, and martyrdoms of Christian holy figures. Hrotsvit of Gandersheim's six plays are a dramatic expression of this tradition, adapting hagiographic conventions into theatrical form.

## Hrotsvit and the Hagiographic Tradition

Hrotsvit explicitly positioned her work within the hagiographic tradition. The Introduction to her plays notes that she was "an author familiar not only with the Scriptures, the works of the Fathers of the Church, of the hagiographers, and of the Christian philosophers, but with Plautus, Terence, Horace, Virgil, and Ovid" (P05-005). Her original manuscript is divided into three parts: the first contains "eight poems or metrical legends of the Saints in which reliable authorities are carefully followed" (P05-010), demonstrating her mastery of prose hagiography before she turned to drama.

> She was an author familiar not only with the Scriptures, the works of the Fathers of the Church, of the hagiographers, and of the Christian philosophers, but with Plautus, Terence, Horace, Virgil, and Ovid — an author who, on her own confession, took the theatre of Terence as her model.
>
> (P05-005)

## The Dramatic Hagiography of the Plays

Each of Hrotsvit's six plays can be read as dramatized hagiography. [[play-Gallicanus|Gallicanus]] presents the conversion and martyrdom of a Roman general. [[play-Dulcitius|Dulcitius]] and [[play-Sapientia|Sapientia]] dramatize the passion of virgin martyrs. [[play-Callimachus|Callimachus]] relates the miraculous resurrection and conversion of Drusiana. [[play-Abraham|Abraham]] and [[play-Paphnutius|Paphnutius]] dramatize the repentance and salvation of fallen women — a hagiographic subgenre of the converted courtesan.

## Literary Significance

The plays' hagiographic character sets them apart from their Terentian model. Where Terence's comedies turn on sexual frailty, Hrotsvit's turn on "heroic adherence to chastity" (P05-011). Her innovation was to fuse the hagiographic tradition — previously expressed in prose vitae and metrical legends — with the dramatic form borrowed from classical Roman comedy. The "Passio Sancti Pelagii," one of her metrical legends, "once enjoyed a very high reputation, and is often quoted by Spanish and Portuguese hagiographers" (P05-010).
