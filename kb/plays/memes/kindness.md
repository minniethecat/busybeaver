# Kindness

**Kindness** (*benignitas*) in Hrotsvit's plays appears as a constellation of related qualities: maternal care, personal generosity, and divine benevolence. Though less prominent than mercy or grace, kindness functions as the practical expression of love in everyday relationships — between author and patron, between the faithful and the dead, and between God and the soul.

## In the Plays

The most theologically significant instance of kindness occurs in *[[play-Sapientia|Sapientia]]*, where the martyr Wisdom addresses God from the furnace:

> Incomparable is Thy generosity and wonderful the kindness you show to me and my dead.
> (006-017)

Here kindness is linked with divine generosity (*largitas*) — God's kindness is not a small, personal nicety but the cosmic benevolence that extends even to the dead. The pairing frames kindness as an attribute of the God who remembers the departed and fulfills His promises to the patriarchs.

## Human Kindness

In the preface, the translator-narrator invokes the "maternal kindness" (*materna benignitas*) of the patron to whom the work is dedicated (P04-003). This phrase blends courtly convention with theological resonance — the patron's kindness mirrors, however imperfectly, the maternal care attributed to God in Scripture.

The epilogue reflects similarly on the inadequacy of human language to repay kindness: "I cannot pay an adequate tribute to your kindness and affection" (P07-006). This rhetorical humility is conventional but also theologically pointed: human kindness points toward divine kindness, which alone is adequate.

> Incomparable is Thy generosity and wonderful the kindness you show to me and my dead.
> (006-017)

## Kindness as Social Glue

Beyond the theological register, kindness in Hrotsvit appears as the oil of daily human relations. In *[[play-Abraham|Abraham]]*, the innkeeper offers [[abraham|Abraham]] "a kind welcome" (004-011) — a small gesture that enables the hermit's disguised entry into the [[the-brothel|brothel]] and thus the entire plot of Mary's recovery. Kindness here is instrumental to salvation history: without the innkeeper's ordinary hospitality, the extraordinary work of repentance could not proceed.

In *[[play-Paphnutius|Paphnutius]]*, the citizens describe [[thais|Thais]] as one who "is kind to all men; she'll not deny them anything they ask" (005-010). This pagan testimony to Thais's natural kindness — her generous disposition before conversion — complicates Hrotsvit's moral universe. Thais was not wicked; she was kind, even in her sin. Her conversion is not a reversal of character but a redirection of an already-generous nature toward its proper end.

The translator's preface adds a final note: "impelled by your kindly interest and your express wish" (P07-007), the work was undertaken. Kindness — the patron's interest, the reader's attention, the saint's generosity — proves to be the motive force behind the entire literary enterprise.

## Theological Significance

Kindness in Hrotsvit shares semantic space with mercy but is distinct: mercy responds to sin and guilt, kindness expresses positive benevolence regardless of fault. In the furnace scene of Sapientia, Wisdom's praise of divine kindness is not a plea for forgiveness but a joyful acknowledgment of God's character — a response to what God is, not merely to what God does.
