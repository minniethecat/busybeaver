# Joy

**Joy** (*gaudium*) in Hrotsvit's plays is more than an emotion — it is a theological sign. When characters weep, leap, or shout for joy, they register the presence of divine grace. The joy of conversion, the joy of faithfulness, and the joy of heaven itself form a recurring motif across the corpus.

## In the Plays

In *[[play-Gallicanus|Gallicanus]]*, joy punctuates the conversion narrative. When Gallicanus resolves to embrace Christianity, he exclaims, "Oh, joy, joy! All things are falling into place" (001-035). His daughters and their companions "all weep for joy" (001-043), and the holy virgins profess they "shall keep our vows with more joy now we know that you are on our side" (001-044). The clustering of joy language around Gallicanus's baptism marks conversion as the supreme occasion of Christian gladness.

The calm joy of the faithful contrasts with worldly pleasure. Constantine observes of Gallicanus: "If this be true, the calm joy in his face reflects a kindly mood" (001-034). This tranquil joy — distinguished from mere excitement — signals interior peace and right relationship with God.

> Oh, joy, joy! All things are falling into place.
> (001-035)

## False Joy and True Joy

Hrotsvit distinguishes sharply between worldly "joy" and Christian joy. The persecutor Sisinnius's cry — "I will go in and enjoy myself in those lovely arms!" (002-015) — epitomises the false joy of sensuality, a pleasure that blinds him to the spiritual reality before him. His "enjoyment" is predatory; it seeks the destruction of the holy virgins he pursues.

The virgin [[irena|Irena]] provides the counter-witness. Facing death, she declares: "To me my death means joy, but to you calamity. For you will be damned to hell for your cruelty" (002-026). Her joy in martyrdom inverts Sisinnius's worldly calculus — what he sees as defeat is for her eternal victory. Earlier she had expressed the same conviction: she endures suffering "that I may share their eternal joy" (002-024). The juxtaposition of Sisinnius's predatory "enjoyment" with Irena's martyr joy is Hrotsvit's most direct dramatisation of the gulf between false and true gladness.

## Eschatological Joy

Joy culminates in heaven. The narrator declares that the repentance of a single sinner "has caused that joy in heaven which exceeds all the joy that the just can give" (P06-010). This echoes the Gospel parable of the lost sheep and anchors Hrotsvit's conversion theology in New Testament precedent.

The promise of joy extends to the faithful reader. The preface prays for the audience's "health in this life and your joy in eternity" (P07-005), framing joy as the eschatological reward of those who attend to the saints' examples.
