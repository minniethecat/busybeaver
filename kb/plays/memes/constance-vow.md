# Secret Vow

A **Secret Vow** (*votum secretum*) is a religious commitment made privately, without public knowledge — the hidden consecration that generates dramatic crisis when it conflicts with public obligations. [[constance|Constance]]'s secret vow of [[virginity]] is the paradigmatic instance and the engine of [[play-Gallicanus|Gallicanus]].

## Constance's Vow

Before the play opens, Constance has secretly dedicated herself to Christ as a consecrated virgin (001-001). Her father, Emperor [[constantine|Constantine]], does not know — which is why he can promise her in marriage to [[gallicanus-character|Gallicanus]]. The secret vow creates the play's central conflict: Constance cannot marry, Constantine cannot retract his promise, and Gallicanus cannot be refused (001-033). The entire plot is an elaborate solution to the problem created by secrecy.

> I consecrated myself to God. I will keep my vow inviolate. Nothing can ever force me to break it.
>
> (001-033)


## Dramatic Function

The secret vow is a brilliantly efficient dramatic device. It establishes the crisis before the play begins, creates a situation that requires all subsequent action, and generates a resolution (Gallicanus's [[conversion]] and [[celibacy]] vow) that is theologically symmetrical with the original problem. The secret vow is the seed from which the entire play grows.

## Secrecy and Sanctity

Hrotsvit does not condemn Constance's secrecy — she treats it as a legitimate, if complicating, expression of devotion. The secret vow is not deception but discretion: Constance has dedicated herself to God, and the world does not need to know. But the play demonstrates that secrecy has consequences — the private vow inevitably becomes public through the crisis it generates.
