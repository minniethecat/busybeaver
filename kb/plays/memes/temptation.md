# Temptation

**Temptation** (*tentatio*) is the solicitation to sin — the dramatic mechanism through which virtue is tested, crisis is initiated, and the conditions for either fall or triumph are established. Every Hrotsvit play begins with a temptation scene.

## Temptation as Dramatic Engine

Temptation serves a precise dramaturgical function in Hrotsvit: it creates the crisis that the play must resolve. [[gallicanus-character|Gallicanus]] is tempted by ambition and desire for [[constance|Constance]]. He himself asks, "What temptation is to be feared more than the loss of one's own son?" (001-044). The persecutors tempt the martyrs with offers of life and honour in exchange for sacrifice. [[callimachus|Callimachus]] is tempted by passion for [[drusiana|Drusiana]] that pursues its object beyond death. He confesses: "You have heard the dreadful tale of my temptation" (003-017). [[mary|Mary]] has already succumbed to temptation before [[play-Abraham|Abraham]] opens.

> | John | In Christ’s name, what miracle is this? The sepulchre is open, and Drusiana’s body has been cast forth. And near it lie two other corpses enlaced in a serpent’s coils. | | Andronicus | I begin to understand.
>
> (003-017)

## Resisting Temptation

The saints resist temptation through different means. The martyrs resist through explicit [[confession]] of faith. The hermits resist through [[asceticism]] that has already mastered desire before temptation arrives. [[constance|Constance]] resists through the prior commitment of her secret [[constance-vow|vow]] — her will is already bound, leaving no room for temptation to operate.

## Temptation as Testing

In the theology Hrotsvit inherits, temptation is not only danger but opportunity. Every temptation resisted strengthens the virtue it threatens. The martyr who refuses sacrifice is not merely preserved but perfected — her [[chastity]] and faith are more fully hers because they have been tested. Temptation is the forge in which sanctity is made.
