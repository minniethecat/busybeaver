# Helena

Helena (also known as Saint Helena or Helena Augusta) is the mother of Emperor Constantine, appearing in Hrotsvit's play *Gallicanus*. She is a historical figure — the Roman Empress who, according to Christian tradition, discovered the True Cross in Jerusalem.

## Role in the Play

Helena appears at the conclusion of *Gallicanus*, joining the triumphal reunion scene. When Constantine goes to the apartments of the consecrated virgins, "my glorious mother, noble Helena, is with them" (001-043). The stage direction notes that "They all weep for joy" (001-043), suggesting Helena's deep emotional investment in the spiritual victories achieved through her son's court. The character is listed among the dramatis personae of the play (001-022).

> | Constantine | Come, let us go to their apartments. | | Gallicanus | Lead on. I will follow. | | Constantine | They are coming here. Look, they hasten to greet us, and my glorious mother, noble Helena, is with them. They all weep for joy.
>
> (001-043)

## Historical Background

Historically, Helena was the consort of Constantius Chlorus and mother of Constantine the Great. After Constantine's conversion to Christianity, Helena became a devout Christian and undertook a pilgrimage to Jerusalem, where tradition holds she discovered the True Cross. She is venerated as a saint in both Eastern and Western Christianity.

## Significance in the Play

Helena's presence in the play, though brief, is meaningful. As the mother of the first Christian emperor and a saint in her own right, her appearance alongside the consecrated virgins reinforces the theme of imperial Rome's transformation from paganism to Christianity. She embodies the continuity of faith across generations within the imperial household — from mother to son to granddaughter (Constance).
