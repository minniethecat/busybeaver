# Sacrifice

**Sacrifice** (*sacrificium*) is a contested term in Hrotsvit's plays, marking the boundary between pagan and Christian worship. The plays repeatedly stage confrontations over what constitutes true sacrifice — and the answer is always the same: the self, not the animal.

## Pagan Sacrifice

Pagan sacrifice in the plays is represented as a demand for external compliance. Emperors [[diocletian|Diocletian]], [[hadrian|Hadrian]], and [[julian-the-apostate|Julian]] command Christians to offer incense to Roman gods. The demand is understood by both sides as an act of religious allegiance, not merely civic duty. [[gallicanus-character|Gallicanus]] himself, before his conversion, offers "hideous sacrifices" (001-032) to obtain military victory — and receives only defeat.

> We must first go to the Capitol, and visit the temples to propitiate the gods with the customary sacrifices.
> (001-032)

## Christian Self-Sacrifice

The Christian answer transforms the meaning of sacrifice entirely. [[agape|Agape]] declares: "We offer a perpetual sacrifice of praise to the true God" (002-026). This sacrifice requires no altar, no animal, no incense — only the witness of a faithful life, even unto [[martyrdom]]. The virgin martyrs' deaths are not tragic endings but sacrificial consummations — the fullest possible act of worship.

## Sacrifice and Conversion

In [[play-Gallicanus|Gallicanus]], the trajectory from pagan to Christian sacrifice maps the protagonist's [[conversion]]. Gallicanus moves from offering material sacrifices at the Capitol to offering himself in [[martyrdom]] under [[julian-the-apostate|Julian]]. The external object of sacrifice is replaced by the internal subject — the worshipper becomes the offering.
