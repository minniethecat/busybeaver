# Miracle

**Miracle** (*miraculum*) is the visible intrusion of divine power into the natural order, serving as both dramatic climax and theological proof in Hrotsvit's plays. Miracles authenticate the saint, confound the persecutor, and demonstrate that the God of the Christians is stronger than the gods of Rome.

## Types of Miracle

Hrotsvit deploys a range of miraculous modes. In [[play-Gallicanus|Gallicanus]], the battlefield theophany — a heavenly host appearing to save the Roman army — is a miracle of military deliverance that precipitates [[gallicanus-character|Gallicanus]]'s [[conversion]]. In [[play-Dulcitius|Dulcitius]], the governor's delusion — mistaking kitchen pots for the virgins he intends to violate — is a miracle of protective farce. In [[play-Callimachus|Callimachus]], the serpent that kills the would-be necrophiliac and the subsequent resurrection are miracles of lethal grace and restorative power (003-017).

## Posthumous Miracles

A distinctive category in Hrotsvit is the posthumous miracle worked through martyrs' relics. After their execution, [[john-and-paul|John and Paul]]'s bones work "astounding miracles" from the sepulchre — healings and exorcisms that testify to their sanctity and convert onlookers (001-053). This reflects the 10th-century cult of relics familiar to Hrotsvit's audience at [[gandersheim|Gandersheim]].

## Miracle and Dramatic Form

Miracle in Hrotsvit is never merely decorative — it is always structurally necessary. The conversion of Gallicanus, the protection of the virgins, the resurrection of Callimachus — none could occur without supernatural intervention. Miracle is the dramaturgical mechanism by which the plot moves from crisis to resolution, replacing tragic inevitability with divine possibility (005-011).


The apostle John’s discovery of the opened sepulchre:

> In Christ’s name, what miracle is this? The sepulchre is open, and Drusiana’s body has been cast forth. And near it lie two other corpses enlaced in a serpent’s coils.
>
> (003-017)
