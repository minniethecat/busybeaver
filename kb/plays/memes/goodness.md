# Goodness

**Goodness** (*bonitas*) in Hrotsvit's theological vocabulary is primarily a divine attribute — it names the character of God as benevolent Creator and merciful Redeemer. Human goodness, where it appears, is always derivative: a participation in the divine goodness through grace.

## In the Plays

The concept is anchored in the preface, where the author reflects on sin as an offence "against the infinite beauty and goodness of God" (P06-008). This formulation — pairing beauty (*pulchritudo*) with goodness (*bonitas*) — echoes the Augustinian tradition in which God's goodness is inseparable from His being and is the ultimate standard against which all creaturely acts are measured.

In *[[play-Gallicanus|Gallicanus]]*, goodness becomes a practical ground for trust. Constantine exhorts: "a heart which trusts in God's goodness is armed against sorrow" (001-033). Here goodness is not an abstract perfection but a pastoral resource — the conviction that God is good enables the believer to endure suffering.

In *[[play-Callimachus|Callimachus]]*, the apostle Andronicus cries out: "Glory to His divine goodness!" (003-017) at the resurrection miracle wrought through John. The exclamation frames the miraculous restoration of Drusiana's life as a manifestation of God's goodness — not merely a display of power.

In *[[play-Sapientia|Sapientia]]*, the martyr Wisdom prays: "Oh, in Thy goodness do not delay the fulfillment of the promises made of old to the patriarchs" (006-018). Here goodness is invoked as the divine reliability that guarantees the fulfilment of prophecy and the vindication of the martyrs.

> (001-033)

## Theological Significance

Goodness in Hrotsvit is never mere benevolence. It is the ontological perfection of God from which creation, providence, and redemption all flow. When characters appeal to God's goodness, they invoke the entire economy of salvation — the conviction that the Creator who made all things good will bring His purposes to completion.

## Goodness Offended

Sin in Hrotsvit's moral universe is framed explicitly as an offence against divine goodness. Paphnutius, pleading with the penitent Thais, names her sin as a wound inflicted "at the cost of an injury to the goodness and beauty of the Creator" (005-011). This formulation — which pairs goodness with beauty, echoing the preface's language (P06-008) — makes clear that sin is not merely a violation of law but a personal affront to the character of God.

The repetition of the goodness-beauty pairing across the corpus signals a consistent theological aesthetic. God is not only powerful and just but beautiful and good — and sin, accordingly, is not only transgression but ugliness, a marring of the divine image in the creature. The saint's work of [[penance]] and [[conversion]] is thus a work of restoration — returning the sinner to participation in the goodness that was always there.
