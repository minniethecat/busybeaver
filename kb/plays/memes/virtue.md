# Virtue

**Virtue** (*virtus*) in Hrotsvit's Christian Latin carries the combined weight of classical moral philosophy and New Testament theology. It signifies both the acquired moral excellence praised by Roman tradition and the supernatural power (*virtus Dei*) that enables the martyrs to endure what human strength cannot.

## In the Plays

The preface explicitly claims that Hrotsvit's project is to celebrate "the virtue of Christ working in His saints" (P07-003). This framing defines virtue not as autonomous human achievement but as Christ's power manifest through human agents — a theological claim that distinguishes Christian hagiography from classical heroic literature.

> Proclaiming wherever possible the virtue of Christ working in His saints.
> (P07-003)

In *[[play-Gallicanus|Gallicanus]]*, virtue is associated with religious commitment. Julian taunts the converted Gallicanus with the question: "In your religion and your virtue?" (001-049). The pairing of *religio* and *virtus* reflects the patristic synthesis in which true virtue depends on right worship.

In *[[play-Callimachus|Callimachus]]*, the apostle John exclaims at the sight of the resurrected Drusiana and Callimachus: "Oh, incredible energy of divine love, making them swell with grace and virtue!" (003-017). Here virtue is explicitly linked with divine grace — it is not earned but received.

In *[[play-Paphnutius|Paphnutius]]*, the citizens who lay gifts at Thais's feet are described as persons "of high standing and virtue" (005-009). This civic virtue is the natural excellence of the well-ordered life, which Thais — despite her sin — is recognized as possessing once she turns to [[penance]].

## Theological Framework

The epilogue underscores the didactic purpose of Hrotsvit's portrayal of virtue: stories "at the expense of old-fashioned virtues never fail to appeal" (007-002). The adjective "old-fashioned" (*priscae*) signals Hrotsvit's self-conscious medievalism — she champions the ancient Christian virtues against what she perceives as the moral laxity of her own tenth century.

## Virtue as Witness

Virtue in Hrotsvit is never a private possession — it is public testimony. The preface's programmatic declaration (P07-003) makes clear that the plays exist to make Christ's virtue visible. Every saint's endurance, every martyr's composure, every hermit's patient reclaiming of a fallen soul is a public demonstration that virtue originates beyond the human agent.

This public dimension distinguishes Hrotsvit's conception from classical virtue ethics. Roman *virtus* was demonstrated in the forum and on the battlefield for the glory of the agent and the state; Christian virtue is demonstrated before the persecutor, the sceptic, and the reader for the glory of Christ. The epilogue's reference to "old-fashioned virtues" (007-002) acknowledges this transformation — what the tenth century found archaic was precisely the classical ideal that Hrotsvit had Christianised and redirected toward the edification of the faithful.
