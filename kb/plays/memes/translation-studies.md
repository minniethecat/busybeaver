# Translation and Mediation

**Translation and Mediation** refers to the processes by which [[hrotsvit|Hrotsvit]]'s 10th-century Latin reaches modern readers — a chain of transmission that includes manuscript copying, Renaissance editing, and modern translation.

## The Chain of Transmission

Hrotsvit wrote in Latin around 960–970 CE at [[gandersheim|Gandersheim]] (P01-001). Her manuscript survived in the monastery of [[st-emmeran-monastery|St. Emmeran]] in Regensburg until its discovery by [[conrad-celtes|Conrad Celtes]] around 1493 (P05-009). Celtes prepared the first printed edition (Nuremberg, 1501). Subsequent editions refined the Latin text. [[christopher-st-john|Christopher St. John]]'s 1923 English translation brought the plays to a modern Anglophone audience (P01-002).

## Translation Choices

Every translation involves choices that shape interpretation. St. John renders Hrotsvit's Latin in fluent, slightly archaizing English prose — readable but formal. Dialogue that in Latin is concise and stylized becomes, in English, more expansive. These choices are not errors but the inevitable condition of translation: Hrotsvit in English is not identical to Hrotsvit in Latin.

## Mediation and Access

The modern reader accesses Hrotsvit through layers of mediation: Celtes's editorial decisions, St. John's translation choices, the Foreword and Introduction that frame the plays. This mediation is not a defect but a fact — and acknowledging it is part of responsible reading.
