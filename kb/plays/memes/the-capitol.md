# The Capitol

**The Capitol** (*Capitolium*) in [[rome|Rome]] is the symbolic center of pagan worship in Hrotsvit's plays — the place where emperors demand sacrifices to Roman gods and where Christians' refusal constitutes their [[martyrdom|martyr-witness]].

## Symbolic Function

The Capitol represents the entirety of the pagan religious system in spatial form. When [[gallicanus-character|Gallicanus]], before his [[conversion]], declares "We must first go to the Capitol, and visit the temples to propitiate the gods with the customary sacrifices" (001-032), he is expressing the default piety of the Roman ruling class. The Capitol is where loyalty to Rome and loyalty to the gods are ritually fused — which is why Christian refusal to sacrifice there is understood as political treason (001-038).

## The Capitol and Martyrdom

Every martyr play includes a scene of summons to the Capitol. The pattern is consistent: the emperor commands sacrifice; the Christian refuses; the refusal is treated as both religious and political crime. The Capitol is thus the stage on which the Christian's confession of faith becomes a capital offense. What happens at the Capitol determines what happens everywhere else — the prison, the torture chamber, the execution ground.

## Dramatic Geography

Hrotsvit establishes a spatial opposition between the Capitol and the Christian sites of worship. The Capitol's temples are filled with idols; the Christians' worship is offered in spirit, requiring no building. This contrast serves her theological argument: the true God is not localized in any temple, while the false gods are tied to their cultic sites. The Capitol's grandeur is the measure of its emptiness.
