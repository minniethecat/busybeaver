**The Innkeeper** (*caupo*) is a minor character in [[play-Abraham|Abraham]] (004-006) — the keeper of the establishment where [[mary|Mary]] works as a prostitute. His role is small but structurally significant: he is the gatekeeper whom [[abraham|Abraham]] must pass to reach Mary.

## Dramatic Function

The innkeeper serves a practical dramatic function: he is the point of contact between the disguised Abraham and the brothel's world. Abraham, presenting himself as a prospective client, negotiates with the innkeeper for access to Mary (004-011). The innkeeper, seeing only a customer, facilitates the encounter without recognizing that he is enabling a rescue mission. His commercial indifference contrasts sharply with the spiritual stakes of the scene.

## The Innkeeper as Worldly Blindness

In Hrotsvit's theological economy, the innkeeper represents worldly blindness — the inability to recognize sanctity when it appears in disguise. Abraham is a holy man, but the innkeeper sees only a paying customer (004-012). This misrecognition is both comic and tragic: comic because the audience knows what the innkeeper does not; tragic because it reveals how easily the world overlooks the sacred.

## The Economics of Sin

The innkeeper's presence also highlights the economic dimension of Mary's fall. The brothel is a business; the innkeeper is a merchant of sin. Abraham's payment for access implicates him, temporarily, in this economy — an uncomfortable participation that is part of the cost of rescue. The saint must enter the market of sin to extract its victim.
