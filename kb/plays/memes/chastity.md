**Chastity** is the dramatic engine of [[hrotsvit|Hrotsvit]]'s entire corpus — the virtue that [[conversion|pagan]] antagonists seek to violate and that [[divine-grace|divine grace]] miraculously preserves. Hrotsvit explicitly frames her project in the Prefaces as glorifying "the laudable chastity of Christian virgins" using the same dramatic form that pagan writers reserved for "the shameless acts of licentious women" (P07-001). This is the organizing principle of all six plays: chastity threatened, chastity defended, chastity restored.

> ...that selfsame form of composition in which the shameless acts of licentious women were portrayed — the laudable chastity of Christian virgins.
> (P07-001)

## Definition

In Hrotsvit's theological framework, chastity is not mere abstinence but a positive consecration — the virgin is a "bride of Christ" whose bodily integrity signifies spiritual wholeness. The defense of chastity creates the dramatic crisis in every play: a pagan authority demands that a Christian virgin renounce her vow, and her refusal sets the conflict in motion. Hrotsvit carefully distinguishes between three states: **virginity** (never violated, as with [[constance|Constance]] and the [[agape|Dulcitius]] sisters), **chastity within marriage** (as with [[drusiana|Drusiana]]), and **restored chastity through penance** (as with [[mary|Mary]] and [[thais|Thais]]). This tripartite scheme covers the full range of female experience within a single virtue framework.

> O Christ, lover of virginity and fount of chastity! Thou Who through the intercession of Thy holy martyr Agnes hast preserved my body from the taint of earthly desire...
> (001-036)

The Dulcitius sisters articulate the classic formula of virgin martyrdom in their defiance of Governor Dulcitius:

> But I shall receive the martyr's palm, and, adorned with the crown of virginity, I shall enter the azure palace of the Eternal King.
> (002-026)

## Historical / Theological Context

Medieval Christianity ranked virginity above marriage, drawing on Pauline teaching (1 Corinthians 7) and patristic elaboration by Augustine and Jerome. The consecrated virgin was understood as engaged in spiritual warfare — her body a battlefield where divine and demonic forces contended. Hrotsvit's innovation at [[gandersheim-abbey|Gandersheim Abbey]] was to translate this theological abstraction into dramatic action, making the preservation of chastity a matter of plot, suspense, and theatrical triumph.

The cult of virgin martyrs — Agnes, Cecilia, Agatha — provided the hagiographic models Hrotsvit adapted. Her originality lies in making the threat to chastity dramatically real: Dulcitius's lust, Callimachus's necrophilic passion, the false hermit's seduction of Mary. These are not abstract temptations but embodied threats that generate genuine suspense, even though the audience knows the theological outcome.

## Literary Analysis

Hrotsvit's treatment of chastity rewrites the sexual economy of classical comedy. In Terence, the virgo is an object of exchange between men — her virginity a problem to be resolved through marriage. Hrotsvit inverts this: the virgo's chastity is non-negotiable, and the dramatic question is how the world will reorganize itself around her vow. This is most explicit in *Gallicanus*, where [[constance|Constance]]'s secret consecration drives the entire plot without her ever leaving the palace.

The plays also construct a **hierarchy of chastity** that maps onto dramatic genre: virgin martyrs ([[agape|Agape]], [[chionia|Chionia]], [[irena|Irena]], [[faith|Faith]], [[hope|Hope]], [[charity|Charity]]) occupy martyr plays; fallen-but-restored women ([[mary|Mary]], [[thais|Thais]]) occupy hermit dramas. This generic division suggests Hrotsvit saw chastity-threatened-and-defended as the martyr play's core, and chastity-lost-and-restored as the hermit drama's core — two faces of the same virtue.
