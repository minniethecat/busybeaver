# The Body

**The Body** (*corpus*, *caro*) is the central site of dramatic conflict in Hrotsvit's plays — the contested territory where sin and sanctity, desire and discipline, mortality and [[resurrection]] meet.

## The Body as Battleground

Every dramatic crisis in the corpus is, at root, a crisis of the body. [[constance|Constance]]'s body is claimed by [[gallicanus-character|Gallicanus]] in marriage (001-036); the virgin martyrs' bodies are threatened with violation and death (002-024); [[drusiana|Drusiana]]'s body is desired even after death (003-013); [[mary|Mary]] and [[thais|Thais]] have used their bodies in prostitution. The body is where sin operates and where sanctity must be defended.

## The Body and Asceticism

Hrotsvit's answer to the body's vulnerability is [[asceticism]] — the systematic discipline of the body through [[fasting]], [[vigil]], and [[prayer]]. The hermit-saints have so mastered their bodies that they are no longer subject to temptation. But this mastery is not hatred of the body — it is its redirection. The body that was the instrument of sin becomes the instrument of sanctification.

## The Body Transfigured

The ultimate destiny of the body in Hrotsvit's theology is not destruction but transfiguration. [[Martyrdom]] does not annihilate the body but transforms it — the martyr's corpse works [[miracle|miracles]], the virgin's body is preserved incorrupt, the [[resurrection|resurrected]] body is more glorious than the mortal one. The body is not to be escaped but to be offered — and in the offering, transformed.


Constance explains to Gallicanus why she has renounced earthly marriage:

> The Lover of virginal purity and the Author of all good resolutions made you renounce me because He had already claimed me for His own. May He Who has separated us in the body on earth unite us in the joys of eternity.
>
> (001-044)
