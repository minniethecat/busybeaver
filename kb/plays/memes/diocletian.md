# Diocletian

**Diocletian** (c. 244–312 CE) is the Roman Emperor who appears in Hrotsvit's play *[[dulcitius-character|Dulcitius]]*, presiding over the initial interrogation of the three virgin [[martyrdom|martyr]]s [[agape|Agape]], [[chionia|Chionia]], and [[irena|Irena]]. He functions as the ultimate source of persecution: the authority who sets the machinery of [[martyrdom|martyr]]dom in motion before delegating its execution to subordinates (002-001).

## The Imperial Examiner

Diocletian opens the play by interrogating the three sisters himself. His approach combines imperial condescension with the threat of violence. He begins by offering the sisters marriage into the highest ranks of the court — provided they deny Christ and sacrifice to the gods.

| Diocletian | The pure and famous race to which you belong and your own rare beauty make it fitting that you should be wedded to the highest in our court. (002-013) |

When [[agape|Agape]] refuses, Diocletian's tone shifts from patronage to exasperation. Each sister proves more defiant than the last. [[irena|Irena]], the youngest, proves the most unyielding — mocking the very concept of pagan gods: "A god who can be bought cheap in the marketplace, what is he but a slave?"

Diocletian responds with predictable imperial logic: the rack will end their "presumptuous chatter."

## Delegation and Distance

A key structural feature of Diocletian's role is his withdrawal after the opening scene. Having handed the prisoners to [[dulcitius-character|Governor Dulcitius]], he retreats from the action, reappearing only to reassign the case when [[dulcitius-character|Dulcitius]] proves incompetent:

| Diocletian | I grieve to hear of the outrageous way in which the [[dulcitius-character|Governor Dulcitius]] has been insulted and hoaxed! But these girls shall not boast of having blasphemed our gods with impunity. I will entrust the execution of my vengeance to [[sisinnius|Count Sisinnius]]. (002-021) |

> Diocletian: I grieve to hear of the outrageous way in which the Governor Dulcitius has been insulted and hoaxed! But these girls shall not boast of having blasphemed our gods with impunity, or of having made a mock of those who worship them. I will entrust the execution of my vengeance to Count Sisinnius.
>
> (002-021)

This pattern — order, delegate, reappoint when delegation fails — makes Diocletian structurally similar to [[hadrian|Hadrian]] in *[[sapientia|Sapientia]]*. Both are distant figures of ultimate authority whose commands generate the dramatic action but who remain largely above its immediate horrors.

## Historical Background

The historical Diocletian (Gaius Aurelius Valerius Diocletianus) reigned as Roman Emperor from 284 to 305 CE and initiated the last and most severe of the Roman Empire's persecutions of Christians — the so-called "Great Persecution" of 303–311. Hrotsvit's choice to make him the presiding emperor in *[[dulcitius-character|Dulcitius]]* is historically appropriate, as the play's [[martyrdom|martyr]]dom narrative is set against the backdrop of this persecution.

However, Hrotsvit shows little interest in historical accuracy. Her Diocletian is the archetypal persecuting emperor — a type repeated with variations in [[hadrian|Hadrian]] (*[[sapientia|Sapientia]]*) and [[julian-the-apostate|Julian the Apostate]] (*[[gallicanus-character|Gallicanus]]*). Each shares the same dramatic function: to represent the worldly power that Christianity must resist and, through resistance, transcend.

## Dramatic Significance

Diocletian embodies the paradox of imperial power in Hrotsvit's [[martyrdom|martyr]]ology: absolute authority that proves absolutely impotent. He commands armies and orders executions, yet he cannot compel three adolescent girls to worship his gods. His delegation to [[dulcitius-character|Dulcitius]] and [[sisinnius|Sisinnius]] only multiplies the humiliations — each subordinate fails more spectacularly than the last, until the play's conclusion demonstrates that no chain of command, however imposing, can reach the soul.
