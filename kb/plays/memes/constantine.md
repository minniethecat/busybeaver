**Constantine** (c. 272–337) appears in [[play-Gallicanus|Gallicanus]] as the Roman Emperor — father of [[constance|Constance]], patron of [[gallicanus-character|Gallicanus]], and the figure of legitimate imperial authority against which the play's [[conversion|spiritual]] drama unfolds. He is the first Christian emperor in historical fact and in Hrotsvit's dramatic world, though his Christianity in the play is more political than devotional.

> The Emperor Constantine.
> (001-004)

## Role in Gallicanus

Constantine's role in [[play-Gallicanus|Gallicanus]] is structurally that of the benign but limited secular ruler. He loves his daughter [[constance|Constance]] and his general [[gallicanus-character|Gallicanus]], but his solutions are always political: betroth [[constance|Constance]] to [[gallicanus-character|Gallicanus]] to reward a victorious general, deploy the army to protect the empire's frontiers. He does not understand [[constance|Constance]]'s spiritual vocation — her consecrated [[chastity|virginity]] — and his betrothal plan inadvertently creates the crisis that only [[conversion|divine intervention]], working through [[john-and-paul|John and Paul]], can resolve.

> Constantine | Gallicanus, this tries my patience. You have kept us long in suspense — explain this sudden silence.
> (001-032)

## Historical Context

The historical Constantine I (Constantine the Great) was the first Roman emperor to legalize and patronize Christianity, issuing the Edict of Milan in 313 and convening the Council of Nicaea in 325. Hrotsvit's portrayal is not historically precise — she compresses events and freely invents the character of [[constance|Constance]] — but it captures a theological truth: even the Christian emperor is a worldly figure whose authority is subordinate to [[martyrdom|divine]] will. Constantine's death between Parts I and II of the play clears the stage for the persecuting emperor [[julian-the-apostate|Julian the Apostate]] (001-003), creating the structural transition from tolerance to [[martyrdom]].
