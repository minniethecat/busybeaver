**Irena** is the youngest of the three [[chastity|virgin]] [[martyrdom|martyr]]s in [[Dulcitius|Dulcitius (Play II)]]. Though the youngest, she proves the most rhetorically devastating — her retort that a god "who can be bought cheap in the marketplace" is no god at all is one of the sharpest theological one-liners in all of Hrotsvit's plays. While [[agape]] and [[chionia]] are burned together, Irena is [[martyrdom|martyr]]ed separately as the last survivor, making her endurance the most prolonged.

> Irena | What could be more shameful baseness, what baser shame, than to venerate slaves as if they were lords?
>
>
> (002-013)

## Biography

Irena is a young Christian virgin of noble Roman family in [[thessalonica|Thessalonica]] (002-001). Along with her elder sisters [[agape]] and [[chionia]], she has consecrated her life to Christ during the [[diocletian|Diocletian]]ic persecution. When brought before the Emperor, [[diocletian|Diocletian]] singles her out as the youngest — hoping she might be the weakest link — only to find her defiance as unshakeable as her sisters' (002-013).

## Role in the Plays

Irena's role in [[play-Dulcitius|Dulcitius]] is the most individually textured of the three sisters:

1. **The Marketplace God** — When [[diocletian|Diocletian]] urges her to worship the gods, Irena asks whether a god "bought cheap in the marketplace" can be anything but a slave — a devastating critique of idol-craft that leaves [[diocletian|Diocletian]] ordering the rack
2. **The Humiliation Scene** — With her sisters, she watches [[dulcitius-character|Dulcitius]]'s lunacy through the cell door, providing the play's comic narration: "Now he presses the saucepans tenderly to his breast, now the kettles and frying-pans!"
3. **Separated from her Sisters** — [[sisinnius|Sisinnius]] singles out Irena as the youngest, hoping she will be easier to break when isolated — a tactic that fails
4. **Last [[martyrdom|Martyrdom]]** — After [[agape]] and [[chionia]] are burned, Irena faces her own execution, the last of the three

> Irena | Oh, look! He must be out of his senses! I believe he thinks that he is kissing us.
>
>
> (002-016)

## Key Scenes

1. **The Rhetorical Triumph** — Before [[diocletian|Diocletian]], Irena delivers the "slaves as lords" speech (002-013) that so enrages the Emperor he orders the rack — the first physical threat in the play.
2. **The Comedic Witness** — Peeking through the cell door, Irena narrates [[dulcitius-character|Dulcitius]]'s kitchen-ware romance with vivid comic detail, providing the play's most memorable comic relief.
3. **Separation** — [[sisinnius|Sisinnius]] keeps Irena back while executing her sisters, hoping isolation will break her — a tactic that backfires.
4. **Final Defiance** — Summoned last, Irena sees her sisters' fate and refuses all the more resolutely.

> Sisinnius | Irena, take warning from the fate of your sisters.
>
>
> (002-024)

## Themes

Irena's name (Greek *eirēnē*, peace) is ironic — she is the least "peaceful" of the three, the most combative in argument. Her intelligence is her weapon: where [[agape]] confronts with theological authority and [[chionia]] with loyal [[martyrdom|witness]], Irena deploys logic and satire. The marketplace-god argument is philosophically sophisticated — it anticipates medieval critiques of idolatry by locating the problem not in worship but in manufacture. Hrotsvit gives her youngest heroine the sharpest mind, subverting the expectation that youth means pliability.
