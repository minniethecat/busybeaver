# Vigil

**Vigil** (*vigilia*) is the practice of keeping watch through the night in [[prayer]] — one of the primary disciplines of the ascetic life, alongside [[fasting]] and manual labour. In Hrotsvit's plays, vigil is the hidden infrastructure of sanctity.

## The Night Watch

The hermit-saints [[abraham|Abraham]] and [[paphnutius|Paphnutius]] are practitioners of vigil, though Hrotsvit does not dramatize their night watches directly. The practice is documented in the corpus: Abraham's life is marked by "many tears, fastings, vigils, and prayers" (004-001), and Paphnutius is described with "his hair grown white through vigils and fasts" (P06-009). The practice is assumed as part of the [[hermit-life|hermit]]'s daily round: prayer by day, vigil by night, the body denied sleep as it is denied food. This sustained wakefulness is understood as both discipline and preparation — the soul kept alert for divine encounter.

## Vigil Before Crisis

The most significant vigil in the corpus is implicit: [[gallicanus-character|Gallicanus]]'s night before the decisive battle in [[scythia|Scythia]] is a vigil of crisis, though he does not yet know it as prayer. His vulnerability in that night — facing defeat, stripped of confidence in Roman arms and pagan gods — prepares him for the [[conversion]] that [[john-and-paul|John and Paul]] will effect. Vigil creates the conditions for transformation.

## Vigil and Prayer

In the ascetic tradition Hrotsvit inherits, vigil and [[prayer]] are inseparable. Mary's fall is lamented as the loss of "all her vigils, prayers, and fasts" (004-009), and Abraham is taunted: "your fasting, and prayers, and vigils — what can they avail you now?" (004-013). Mary's restoration is marked by "continual vigils and fasts" (004-015) — the discipline resumed as the sign of conversion. The night is the time of spiritual combat — when demons are most active and the soul most vulnerable. To keep vigil is to stand guard against these enemies, turning the hours of darkness into hours of encounter. The hermit who has mastered vigil has mastered fear.


The Prologue describes Mary's decades of penance:

> For twenty years she does penance for her sins with many tears, fastings, vigils, and prayers.
>
> (004-001)
