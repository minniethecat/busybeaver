# Artemia

Artemia is the daughter of the Roman general Gallicanus and sister of Attica in Hrotsvit's play *Gallicanus* (001-008).

## Role in the Play

Artemia and her sister Attica are sent by their father to live with Constance, the daughter of Emperor Constantine, as "pledges of the bond of love" (001-035) that is to unite Gallicanus and Constance in marriage. Constance, however, has taken a vow of virginity and secretly plans to use the sisters' presence as an opportunity to convert them to Christianity (001-036).

## Conversion

When Artemia first meets Constance, she declares: "We come joyfully to offer you our homage, lady. We are ready to serve you with our whole hearts, and we seek no reward but your love" (001-036). Constance redirects this devotion toward God, and Artemia responds eagerly, saying: "You will find us eager to obey you in all things, but never so eager as when you exhort us to confess our faith and keep our vow of purity" (001-036).

Artemia reveals a surprising openness to Christian teaching, asking rhetorically: "How could we poor idolators have any good thought if light had not been given us from above?" (001-036). This suggests that divine grace is already at work in her before any formal instruction has taken place.

> Constance: That is a good answer, and one worthy of a noble mind. I see that through divine grace you already have the faith.
>
> Artemia: How could we poor idolators have any good thought if light had not been given us from above?
>
> (001-036)

## Significance

Artemia, along with her sister Attica, represents the theme of conversion through contact with holy persons — a recurring motif in Hrotsvit's dramas (001-036). The pagan daughters of a powerful Roman general become consecrated virgins through the influence of Constance, demonstrating the transformative power of Christian witness in the imperial court (001-036).
