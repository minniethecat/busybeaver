**Julian the Apostate** (331–363 CE) is the Roman Emperor who appears as the principal antagonist in Part II of [[play-Gallicanus|Gallicanus]]. Raised Christian but later reverting to paganism — hence "the Apostate" — Julian plays the role of persecutor in Hrotsvit's drama: he exiles [[gallicanus-character|Gallicanus]] to [[alexandria|Alexandria]], orders the secret execution of [[john-and-paul|John and Paul]], and thereby sets the stage for the [[martyrdom|martyr]]dom that closes the play.

> Later he is exiled by order of Julian the Apostate, and receives the crown of martyrdom. John and Paul are put to death by the same prince.
>
>
> (001-003)

## Historical Background

Julian (Flavius Claudius Julianus) was a nephew of [[constantine|Constantine the Great]] and the last non-Christian Roman Emperor (001-003). After surviving the massacre that killed most of his family following [[constantine|Constantine]]'s death, Julian was educated in Christian doctrine but secretly embraced Neoplatonic philosophy and the old Roman gods. Upon becoming Augustus in 361, he launched a program of pagan restoration — reopening temples, restoring sacrificial cults, and excluding Christians from public office. His reign lasted only 19 months; he died in Mesopotamia during a campaign against Persia in 363.

Hrotsvit's portrait of Julian draws on the medieval Christian historiographical tradition — Eusebius, Orosius, and the hagiographers — which cast him as the archetypal apostate-tyrant. In *[[gallicanus-character|Gallicanus]]*, Julian is less a complex historical figure than the dramatic embodiment of imperial persecution, structurally balancing the Christian emperor [[constantine|Constantine]] of Part I.

## Role in the Plays

Julian is the antagonist of [[play-Gallicanus|Gallicanus]] Part II. His dramatic function is twofold:

1. **Persecutor of [[gallicanus-character|Gallicanus]]** — Julian exiles the now-Christian [[gallicanus-character|Gallicanus]] to [[alexandria|Alexandria]], where the governor Rhatianus executes him
2. **Persecutor of [[john-and-paul|John and Paul]]** — Julian summons the [[john-and-paul|Grand Almoners]] and demands they sacrifice to Jupiter (001-049); on their refusal, he orders their secret execution (001-050)

His court scenes follow the familiar hagiographic pattern: the pagan emperor commands sacrifice, the Christian refuses, the emperor threatens torture, the [[martyrdom|martyr]] remains steadfast. Julian's rhetorical strategy — appealing to [[john-and-paul|John and Paul]]'s palace upbringing and their long service — makes his persecution psychologically pointed: it is betrayal of loyalty, not merely religious conflict.

## Key Scenes

1. **Summoning [[john-and-paul|John and Paul]]** — Julian calls them before him and demands they serve him as they served [[constantine|Constantine]] — a loyalty test that becomes a religious test.
2. **The Demand for Sacrifice** — Julian orders them to sacrifice to Jupiter; they refuse.
3. **The Secret Execution Order** — Julian instructs Terentianus to kill [[john-and-paul|John and Paul]] in private, not publicly, because "they once held office in this palace" — a detail that reveals Julian's political sensitivity to the optics of [[martyrdom|martyr]]ing palace officials.

> Julian | Go, Terentianus. Take with you a few trusted soldiers and compel John and Paul to sacrifice to Jupiter. If they persist in their refusal, let them be put to death, not publicly, but with the greatest possible secrecy, since they once held office in this palace.
>
>
> (001-050)

## Themes

Julian embodies the classic Antagonist type in Hrotsvit's moral universe — the earthly power that demands what belongs to God. His role is structurally symmetrical: where [[constantine|Constantine]] in Part I enables Christian flourishing, Julian in Part II provokes Christian [[martyrdom]]. Hrotsvit's Julian is not a tragic figure but a providential instrument — his persecution creates the conditions for the [[martyrdom|martyr]]s' glory and the posthumous conversion of their executioner. The secret nature of the execution underscores the theme: Christian [[martyrdom|witness]] cannot be silenced even by covert violence.
