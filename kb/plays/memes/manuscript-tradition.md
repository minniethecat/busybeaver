# Manuscript Tradition

The **Manuscript Tradition** of [[hrotsvit|Hrotsvit]]'s works is the chain of physical copies by which her texts survived from the 10th century to the age of print. The principal witness is the Emmeram Manuscript, discovered by [[conrad-celtes|Conrad Celtes]] in the late 15th century (P05-009).

## The Emmeram Manuscript

The most important manuscript of Hrotsvit's works was preserved at the Benedictine monastery of [[st-emmeran-monastery|St. Emmeran]] in Regensburg. Written in the late 10th or early 11th century — possibly at [[gandersheim|Gandersheim]] itself — it contains the six plays, the eight poetic legends, and the historical poems (P05-010). Celtes discovered it around 1493 and used it as the basis for his 1501 printed edition.

## Survival

The survival of a single manuscript across five centuries is remarkable. Wars, fires, neglect, and the dissolution of monasteries destroyed countless medieval texts. That Hrotsvit's plays survived at all — let alone in a complete, well-preserved copy — is a matter of historical contingency as much as literary merit (P06-002).

## Loss and Recovery

The Emmeram Manuscript was subsequently lost after Celtes's edition — a common fate for manuscripts used as printer's copy. Modern editions of the Latin text depend on Celtes's printed version collated with later manuscript discoveries. The textual history is thus a story of survival, loss, and scholarly reconstruction.

## From the Translator's Foreword

> The lively interest provoked by the stage performance of one of the translations (that of the play *Paphnutius*) by the Pioneer Players in January 1914 led me to think that the publication of the whole theatre of Roswitha in English would be welcomed by all students of the drama. Unfortunately, the war delayed publication, and the manuscript was entirely destroyed by a fire at the publisher's premises in Dublin during the Irish insurrection of Easter 1916.
>
