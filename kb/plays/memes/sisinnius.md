# Sisinnius

**Sisinnius** is a Roman count and secondary antagonist in Hrotsvit's play *[[dulcitius-character|Dulcitius]]*. After [[dulcitius-character|Governor Dulcitius]] is humiliated by a divinely sent delusion — mistaking kitchen pots for the virgin prisoners — [[diocletian|Emperor Diocletian]] entrusts the execution of vengeance to Sisinnius, who proves more competent but no less vulnerable to divine intervention.

## Appointment by Diocletian

Sisinnius enters the play when [[diocletian|Diocletian]], outraged by [[dulcitius-character|Dulcitius]]'s humiliation at the hands of the Christian virgins [[agape|Agape]], [[chionia|Chionia]], and [[irena|Irena]], transfers authority to a more capable executor.

| [[diocletian|Diocletian]] | I grieve to hear of the outrageous way in which the [[dulcitius-character|Governor Dulcitius]] has been insulted and hoaxed! But these girls shall not boast of having blasphemed our gods with impunity. I will entrust the execution of my vengeance to Count Sisinnius. (002-021) |

## Strategic Approach

Unlike [[dulcitius-character|Dulcitius]], who was undone by lust, Sisinnius approaches his task with cold rationality. He separates [[irena|Irena]] — the youngest — from her sisters, calculating that she "may be more easily influenced when not intimidated by her sisters" (002-022). He attempts persuasion before coercion, offering the sisters the chance to sacrifice to the gods and live.

When [[agape|Agape]] and [[chionia|Chionia]] refuse, Sisinnius orders them burned alive. The fire, however, does not touch them: "Neither their hair, nor their garments, much less their bodies, have been touched by the flames!" Their souls depart peacefully, leaving their bodies miraculously intact.

## The Threat of Sexual Violence

With [[irena|Irena]], Sisinnius deploys a more psychologically acute weapon: the threat of sexual dishonour. When torture fails to intimidate her, he threatens to send her to a brothel, reasoning that if her body is defiled, "you can no longer be numbered among the virgins" (002-024).

[[irena|Irena]]'s response — "Better far that my body should suffer outrage than my soul" — and her theological distinction that "if the soul does not consent, there is no guilt" — renders Sisinnius's threat powerless.

## Divine Frustration

When soldiers attempt to carry out the brothel sentence, two mysterious strangers — divine emissaries — redirect them to take [[irena|Irena]] to a mountaintop instead. Sisinnius, attempting to pursue, finds himself wandering blindly around the hill, unable to find a path forward or back.

| Sisinnius | What has happened to me? These Christians have bewitched me. I wander blindly round this hill, and when I stumble on a path I can neither follow it nor return upon my steps. (002-026) |

In frustrated rage, he orders [[irena|Irena]] shot with arrows. Even in death, [[irena|Irena]] taunts him: "For your cruelty you will be damned in Tartarus. But I shall receive the [[martyrdom|martyr]]'s palm."

## Comparison with Dulcitius

Where [[dulcitius-character|Dulcitius]] is comic — a lustful governor made ridiculous by divine delusion — Sisinnius is a figure of cold, systematic persecution. He is more dangerous because he is competent. Yet his competence proves equally powerless against divine protection. Together, the two antagonists illustrate Hrotsvit's theme: neither passion nor reason, neither lust nor strategy, can overcome Christian faith.
