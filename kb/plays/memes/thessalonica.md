## Overview

Thessalonica (modern Thessaloniki, Greece) was a major city of the Roman Empire and the capital of the province of Macedonia. In Hrotsvit's *[[dulcitius-character|Dulcitius]]*, it serves as the seat of [[dulcitius-character|Governor Dulcitius]], who presides over the persecution and [[martyrdom|martyr]]dom of the holy virgins [[agape|Agape]], [[chionia|Chionia]], and [[irena|Irena]] (002-007). The city's role in the play is that of the imperial administrative center — the place where Roman authority confronts Christian resistance.

## In the Plays

Thessalonica appears in *[[dulcitius-character|Dulcitius]]* as the location of [[dulcitius-character|Dulcitius]]'s governance. The three virgin sisters are brought before the imperial court there, and it is from Thessalonica that the orders for their punishment originate (002-013). The city functions as a symbol of Roman imperial power — ordered, hierarchical, and hostile to the Christian refusal to sacrifice to the state gods (002-001).

## Historical Significance

Historically, Thessalonica was founded c. 315 BCE and became an important center of early Christianity. The Apostle [[john-and-paul|Paul]] wrote two epistles to the Thessalonian church (1 and 2 Thessalonians), and the city was an early center of Christian worship. By the 4th century CE — the approximate setting of *[[dulcitius-character|Dulcitius]]* during the [[diocletian|Diocletian]]ic Persecution — Thessalonica was a major administrative hub where imperial decrees against Christians would have been enforced. Hrotsvit's choice of this setting grounds her fictionalized [[martyrdom|martyr]]dom narrative in a well-known historical-geographical context.
