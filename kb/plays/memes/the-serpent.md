# The Serpent

**The Serpent** (*serpens*) in [[play-Callimachus|Callimachus]] is among Hrotsvit's most startling dramatic inventions — a supernatural creature that kills two men at [[drusiana|Drusiana]]'s tomb, yet functions as an instrument of divine [[divine-grace|grace]]. Callimachus dies "from the bite of a serpent" (003-002) — and in dying finds life.

## Lethal Grace

When [[callimachus|Callimachus]] and [[fortunatus|Fortunatus]] enter [[the-tomb|the tomb]] to violate [[drusiana|Drusiana]]'s corpse, a serpent appears and kills them both — Fortunatus fatally, Callimachus temporarily. The serpent is not a demonic presence but a divine agent: it protects the sanctity of Drusiana's body and prepares Callimachus for [[resurrection]] and [[conversion]]. The serpent's venom is the medicine that cures Callimachus's passion — lethal grace applied to a soul that could not be reached by gentler means.

## Theological Paradox

The serpent presents a theological paradox central to Hrotsvit's thought: divine love sometimes wears a terrifying face. The same God who sends [[john-and-paul|John and Paul]] to convert [[gallicanus-character|Gallicanus]] through gentle ministry sends a serpent to kill Callimachus — whose own words, "I will be as cunning as the serpent" (003-011), become a self-fulfilling prophecy of judgment. The "serpent's coils" (003-017) that bind the corpses are simultaneously punishment and the instrument of resurrection. — because Callimachus's sin (necrophiliac passion pursuing its object beyond death) is so extreme that only an equally extreme intervention can break it.

## Dramatic Function

The serpent is Hrotsvit's most audacious departure from classical dramatic decorum. A supernatural serpent killing characters on stage (or reported immediately after) pushes beyond Terentian realism into theological theater. The scene insists that divine power operates where human possibility ends — and that even a serpent can be an instrument of salvation.
