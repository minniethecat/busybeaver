# Almsgiving

**Almsgiving** (*eleemosyna*) is the practice of giving material aid to the poor as a religious duty. In Hrotsvit's plays, almsgiving appears as a companion virtue to [[religious-poverty]] — the active distribution of wealth that complements the passive renunciation of it.

## Almsgiving as Penitential Practice

In the early Christian tradition Hrotsvit inherits, almsgiving is one of the three cardinal penitential practices alongside [[fasting]] and [[prayer]]. It is the social dimension of [[penance]] — the sinner's wealth, which may have been acquired unjustly, is redistributed to the poor. [[thais|Thais]] does not merely burn her wealth (005-012) — though the bonfire is the most dramatic image — she could have given it away. The destruction of ill-gotten goods is itself a form of almsgiving to the community, returning what was taken.

## Almsgiving and the Saints

[[john-and-paul|John and Paul]], as Grand Almoners to [[constance|Constance]] (001-002), embody the institutional form of almsgiving — the Church's organized distribution of resources to the needy. Their position at court gives them access to wealth that they redirect to the poor, practicing the economic inversion that is central to Christian ethics.

## Almsgiving and Conversion

Almsgiving is one of the visible fruits of [[conversion]]. The converted [[gallicanus-character|Gallicanus]] does not merely renounce his claim to [[constance|Constance]] — he commends his daughters to her care (001-044), an act that redistributes his family wealth into the Christian community. Conversion changes how one relates to possessions.

Gallicanus's conversion manifests through the redistribution of his wealth:

> I am giving to my daughters the portion of my property which is theirs by right. Another I am devoting to the support of pilgrims. With the remainder I propose to enrich my slaves—whom I have freed—and to relieve the poor.
>
> (001-044)
