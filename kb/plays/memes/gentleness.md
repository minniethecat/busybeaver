# Gentleness

**Gentleness** (*mansuetudo*) in Hrotsvit's plays is a distinctly Christ-like mode of action — the opposite of coercion, the method of persuasion. Where the world uses force and threats, the saints use gentleness, and in this they imitate Christ, who "wins the sinner with gentleness" (003-017).

## In the Plays

The primary theological statement on gentleness occurs in *[[play-Callimachus|Callimachus]]*, where the apostle John reflects on the divine pedagogy:

> Thou dost win the sinner with gentleness, now Thou dost chastise him with severity.
> (003-017)

This couplet articulates a pattern central to Hrotsvit's soteriology: God's primary mode is gentleness — persuasion, patience, love — but gentleness is not weakness. When the sinner resists, gentleness gives way to severity. The two modes are complementary, not contradictory.

In *[[play-Abraham|Abraham]]*, the hermit Abraham applies this principle pastorally. Ephrem counsels: "Let us approach him with gentleness and love, for he will repent not by threats but by gentleness" (004-013). The hermit's method mirrors God's — he seeks to win, not to compel. Gentleness becomes the instrument of conversion.

In *[[play-Sapientia|Sapientia]]*, the virtue appears in dialogue. Sapientia addresses Hadrian: "In all friendship, I ask you in all gentleness to join me in an act of worship" (006-012). Even toward a persecutor, the Christian martyr extends gentleness — not from fear but from the conviction that souls are won, not conquered.

## Pastoral Application

Gentleness in Hrotsvit's theology is inseparable from patience. The sinner's conversion is a slow work; force and threats may produce outward compliance but not interior transformation. The hermit dramas — Abraham, Paphnutius — dramatise this principle: the pastor approaches the fallen soul not with condemnation but with the gentleness that creates space for repentance.

## Gentleness and the Contrite Soul

Hrotsvit extends gentleness beyond pastoral strategy into eschatology. [[thais|Thais]], awaiting death, prays that her soul "be mercifully cleansed in a gentle fire until my spirit is fit for the company of saints" (005-020). Here gentleness becomes an attribute of divine justice itself — the fire that purifies is *gentle*, not consuming. The adjective transforms purgatorial suffering from punishment into remedy.

This gentle fire mirrors the divine method articulated by John in *Callimachus*: God wins first with gentleness, chastises only second. Thais's prayer completes the theological arc — even when God must chastise, He does so with a gentleness that heals rather than destroys. The contrite soul does not fear the fire because she trusts the gentleness of the hand that kindles it.
