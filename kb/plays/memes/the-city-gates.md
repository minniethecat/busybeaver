# The City Gates

**The City Gates** (*porta*) mark the boundary between the city and the [[the-desert|desert]] in Hrotsvit's hermit dramas — the liminal threshold where the saint passes from purification to mission and returns with the rescued soul.

## Threshold of Mission

When [[abraham|Abraham]] and [[paphnutius|Paphnutius]] leave the desert for the city (003-012), they cross a threshold that is at once geographical and spiritual. The desert is the place of achieved sanctity; the city is the place of active sin. Crossing the gates is an act of descent — the saint who has ascended to contemplation now descends to rescue those who cannot ascend on their own.

## Threshold of Penance

The return journey is equally significant. [[mary|Mary]] and [[thais|Thais]] are led out of the city, through the gates, into the desert for [[penance]]. Crossing the gates outward is the beginning of their restoration — the spatial enactment of leaving the world behind (001-048). The gates are the architectural form of [[conversion]]: the sinner enters the city one person and leaves it another.

## Liminal Space

The gates are a liminal space — neither city nor desert, neither world nor wilderness. In Hrotsvit's spatial theology, liminal spaces are places of decision. At the gates, one chooses which direction to face: toward the city and its temptations, or toward the desert and its disciplines (P06-006).


The translator notes how Dulcitius is humiliated at the palace gates:

> All the fun of Dulcitius lies in the action. No dramatist who had not in mind the effect on spectators could have conceived the scene in which the foolish governor, black as a sweep from his amorous encounter with the kitchen pots and pans which he mistakes for young women, is chased away from the palace gates, asking the while if there is anything amiss with his fine and handsome appearance.
>
> (P06-006)
