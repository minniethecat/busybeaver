**Virginity** — the state of consecrated sexual purity undertaken as a solemn religious vow — is the central moral ideal of Hrotsvit's dramatic universe. Every one of her six plays turns on the defence, violation, or triumphant preservation of [[chastity|virginity]]. As she declares in her Preface, her object is "to glorify, within the limits of my poor talent, the laudable chastity of Christian virgins in that selfsame form of composition which has been used to describe the shameless acts of licentious women" (P07-001).

## Definition

In Hrotsvit's usage, [[chastity|virginity]] is not merely physical intactness but a total dedication of body and soul to Christ. [[constance|Constance]] articulates this comprehensive understanding when she refuses [[gallicanus-character|Gallicanus]]:

> | Constance | We have one Lord Who is in heaven. He alone should be served like that. We owe Him a love and fidelity which must be shown not only with whole hearts but with whole bodies. That is if we would enter His kingdom with the virgin's palm.
> (001-004)

The "virgin's palm" — the heavenly reward reserved for those who preserve their chastity — is invoked across all six plays as the ultimate aspiration of Hrotsvit's heroines.

## In the Plays

### Gallicanus: The Virgin Princess

[[constance|Constance]], daughter of [[constantine|Emperor Constantine]], has taken a secret vow of [[chastity|virginity]] before the play opens. When [[gallicanus-character|Gallicanus]] claims her hand as a reward for military service, she navigates between her father's political obligations and her spiritual commitment. Her prayer to Christ as "lover of [[chastity|virginity]] and fount of chastity" (001-036) results in [[gallicanus-character|Gallicanus]]'s battlefield conversion, after which he renounces her and himself embraces celibacy:

> | Gallicanus | I am ready to renounce even your daughter, whom I love more than anything in the world. I wish to abstain from marriage that I may devote myself wholly to the service of the Virgin's Son.
> (001-008)

### Dulcitius: Virginity Under Assault

[[agape|Agape]], [[chionia|Chionia]], and [[irena|Irena]] face a dual threat: the Emperor's demand that they worship pagan gods, and Governor [[dulcitius-character|Dulcitius]]'s lust. Their [[chastity|virginity]] is threatened both spiritually (idolatry as adultery against Christ) and physically ([[dulcitius-character|Dulcitius]]'s attempted rape). Divine intervention protects them — [[dulcitius-character|Dulcitius]] is deluded into embracing kitchen pots (002-001), and the flames refuse to touch [[agape|Agape]] and [[chionia|Chionia]]'s bodies. [[irena|Irena]], facing the threat of the brothel, makes the crucial theological distinction:

> | Irena | Better far that my body should suffer outrage than my soul. The wage of sin is death; the wage of suffering a crown. If the soul does not consent, there is no guilt.
> (002-012)

She dies declaring she will receive "the martyr's palm, and, adorned with the crown of [[chastity|virginity]], I shall enter the azure palace of the Eternal King" (002-026).

### Callimachus: The Chaste Dead

[[drusiana|Drusiana]]'s story inverts the pattern. Already married to [[andronicus|Andronicus]] (though living in continence as a Christian), she prays for death rather than risk being the occasion of [[callimachus|Callimachus]]'s sin. The Apostle [[john-and-paul|John]] praises her motive: "Such was her love of chastity." [[callimachus|Callimachus]]'s attempt to violate her corpse is thwarted by divine serpent, and his eventual conversion is framed as a movement from "fornicator into a chaste man" (003-009).

### Abraham: Virginity Lost and Mourned

[[mary|Mary]]'s fall represents [[chastity|virginity]]'s tragic dimension. Consecrated as a child and enclosed in a hermit's cell, she is seduced by a man disguised as a monk and flees to a brothel. [[abraham|Abraham]]'s grief is the grief of a spiritual father who has lost a virgin to the world. Her recovery and penitential return restores not her physical [[chastity|virginity]] but her spiritual standing — a theological nuance that distinguishes Hrotsvit's treatment from simpler hagiographic patterns (004-001).

### Sapientia: The Virgin Martyrs

[[faith|Faith]], [[hope|Hope]], and [[charity|Charity]] die as virgins before they die as [[martyrdom|martyrs]]. Their mother [[sapientia|Sapientia]] frames their consecration in nuptial terms: "I gave you milk. I nourished and cherished you, that I might wed you to a heavenly bridegroom, not to an earthly one" (006-004). Their tortures — scourging, mutilation, fire, beheading — are simultaneously acts of [[martyrdom]] and defences of [[chastity|virginity]]; the two categories merge in Hrotsvit's theology.

## Historical / Theological Context

The "Spouse of Christ" motif runs throughout the plays. [[chastity|Virginity]] is expressed through the metaphor of spiritual marriage: the consecrated virgin is the "spouse of Christ," and her vow is a marriage vow. [[abraham|Abraham]] desires [[mary|Mary]] to be "the spouse of Christ and devoted entirely to His service" (004-001). [[ephrem|Ephrem]] paints the reward in vividly eroticized celestial imagery: Christ the Bridegroom receiving the virgin "in His mother's dazzling bridal room" (004-002).

This nuptial theology, rooted in the early Church's interpretation of the Song of Songs and developed by Ambrose and Jerome, receives dramatic embodiment in Hrotsvit's plays. The virgin's body is a contested site — desired by earthly lovers, claimed by Christ, protected by divine power, and ultimately rewarded with heavenly marriage. Hrotsvit's innovation is to make this theological abstraction the engine of dramatic plot: the preservation of virginity generates suspense, conflict, and resolution across all six plays.

## Literary Analysis

Virginity in Hrotsvit's plays is more than a moral category; it is the lens through which all other values are refracted. [[martyrdom|Martyrdom]] is [[chastity|virginity]] defended to the death. [[conversion|Conversion]] is the renunciation of carnal desire for divine love. [[penance|Penance]] is the restoration of spiritual [[chastity|virginity]] through suffering.

Hrotsvit's distinctive contribution is to make virginity *dramatic* rather than merely devotional. In classical comedy, the virgo is an object to be exchanged; in Hrotsvit's Christian comedy, the virgo is an agent whose vow drives the entire plot. This inversion of Terentian sexual economy — "fragile woman victorious and strong man routed with confusion" (P07-001) — is the organizing principle of her dramatic project. The virgin does not flee the stage; she commands it.
