# Scythia

**Scythia** is the region north of the Roman Empire that serves as the battlefield setting for the first part of Hrotsvit's play *[[play-Gallicanus|Gallicanus]]*. It is the destination of [[gallicanus-character|Gallicanus]]'s military campaign and, more importantly, the site of his [[conversion]] — the place where a Roman general, facing defeat, encounters a heavenly vision and becomes a Christian.

## Military Campaign

The play opens with [[gallicanus-character|Gallicanus]] preparing to lead an expedition against the Scythians, a campaign that serves as the pretext for his betrothal to [[constance|Constance]] (001-001)(002-008). The campaign is presented as a significant military undertaking — the Emperor cannot refuse [[gallicanus-character|Gallicanus]]'s request for [[constance|Constance]]'s hand "on account of the value of his military services" (001-001)(002-008).

| [[constantine|Constantine]] | We dare not offend him by a refusal. We are in his hands. On the one side is our daughter's vow; on the other a powerful suitor whose military services are indispensable to the state. (001-001)(002-008) |

## Battlefield Conversion

Scythia is the setting for the play's central miracle. Surrounded and facing defeat in battle, [[gallicanus-character|Gallicanus]] is rescued by a heavenly host — an intervention attributed to [[constance|Constance]]'s prayers and the presence of [[john-and-paul|John and Paul]], who accompany the expedition as [[constance|Constance]]'s almoners (001-007)(002-014). This battlefield conversion transforms the campaign from military conquest into spiritual pilgrimage.

## Symbolic Geography

Scythia functions symbolically in Hrotsvit's dramatic geography. As a barbarian territory beyond the Roman frontier, it represents the world outside Christian order — a place of danger and darkness where divine intervention is most dramatically needed. [[gallicanus-character|Gallicanus]]'s journey to Scythia and return is a spiritual arc: he departs as a pagan general, encounters God on the battlefield, and returns as a Christian ready to renounce marriage and embrace celibacy.

The contrast with [[rome|Rome]] is deliberate. [[rome|Rome]] is the seat of imperial power and [[constance|Constance]]'s consecrated [[chastity|virginity]]; Scythia is the proving ground where worldly power encounters divine reality. The play's geographic movement — [[rome|Rome]] → Scythia → [[rome|Rome]] — mirrors its spiritual movement from pagan ambition through conversion to Christian renunciation.

## Scholarly Context

> Gallicanus, one of the Emperor Constantine's generals, claims the hand of the Emperor's daughter as a reward for undertaking a dangerous campaign against the Scythians. The Emperor knows that Constance has taken a solemn vow of chastity, but he dares not offend Gallicanus by a refusal, on account of the value of his military services.
