# Fortunatus

**Fortunatus** is a minor but theologically significant character in Hrotsvit's play *[[callimachus|Callimachus]]*. He is the custodian of [[drusiana|Drusiana]]'s tomb whom [[callimachus|Callimachus]] bribes to gain access to the corpse. His death and refusal of resurrection provide the play's cautionary contrast to [[callimachus|Callimachus]]'s sincere conversion.

## The Bribed Custodian

When [[callimachus|Callimachus]]'s adulterous passion persists even after [[drusiana|Drusiana]]'s death, he turns to Fortunatus, who guards her sepulchre. [[callimachus|Callimachus]] offers him money to help violate the tomb and strip the shroud from [[drusiana|Drusiana]]'s body. Fortunatus, motivated by greed, agrees (003-014).

## Death by Divine Serpent

In the tomb, as the two men prepare to commit their sacrilege, a serpent sent by divine agency appears and kills them both. Fortunatus dies alongside [[callimachus|Callimachus]], but the similarity ends there — what follows reveals the gulf between the two men's souls (003-015).

## The Refusal of Grace

When the Apostle [[john-and-paul|John]] arrives and resurrects [[callimachus|Callimachus]], he offers the same gift to Fortunatus. But Fortunatus, unlike [[callimachus|Callimachus]], rejects the offer. His reasons are not elaborated — Hrotsvit presents the refusal as a fact, leaving the audience to infer that Fortunatus's spiritual state is fundamentally different from [[callimachus|Callimachus]]'s. Where [[callimachus|Callimachus]]'s passion, however sinful, contained the seed of genuine desire for beauty, Fortunatus's greed offers no foundation for conversion (003-017).

> Fortunatus: If it is as you say, if Drusiana has restored me to life and Callimachus believes in Christ, I reject life and choose death. I would rather not exist than see them swelling with grace and virtue!
>
> John: Oh, incredible envy of the devil! Oh, malice of the old serpent, who since he made our first parents taste death has never ceased to writhe at the glory of the righteous! Oh, Fortunatus, brimful of Satan's bitter gall, how much do you resemble the rotten tree that, bearing only bad fruit, must be cut down and cast into the fire!
>
> (003-017)

Fortunatus dies permanently, unredeemed.

## Dramatic Function

Fortunatus serves as [[callimachus|Callimachus]]'s dark mirror. Both men sin grievously; both die in the act of sin. But [[callimachus|Callimachus]] repents and receives grace, while Fortunatus rejects it. The contrast makes visible the play's theology of conversion: redemption is available to all, but it requires a disposition of the soul that Fortunatus lacks. His very name — "fortunate" — becomes bitterly ironic, for he is offered the greatest fortune of all and refuses it.
