**Conversion** is the primary dramatic engine of four of [[hrotsvit|Hrotsvit]]'s six plays — the moment at which a character renounces a previous life of sin, paganism, or worldliness and embraces Christian faith. Unlike the gradual spiritual development depicted in later devotional literature, Hrotsvitian conversion is instantaneous: a definitive turning that reorganizes the protagonist's entire identity. It is the counterpoint to [[martyrdom]] — where martyrdom is the ultimate witness through death, conversion is the initial witness through transformed life.

## Definition

Conversion in Hrotsvit's plays is never portrayed as a gradual process but as a definitive moment of transformation, often catalyzed by a miraculous event, the intervention of a holy person, or a direct encounter with divine power. The convert does not negotiate terms — [[gallicanus-character|Gallicanus]] on the battlefield, [[thais|Thais]] in her [[alexandria|Alexandrian]] chamber, [[mary|Mary]] in her inn — each responds immediately and totally. This suddenness reflects the theological understanding of [[divine-grace|grace]] as an unmerited gift that can transform a soul instantaneously, requiring only the will to accept it.

> The conversion of Gallicanus, Commander-in-Chief. On the eve of his campaign against the Scythians, threatened with defeat in battle, Gallicanus is converted by John and Paul, Grand Almoners to Constance, and immediately baptized — taking a vow of celibacy.
> (001-001)

## In the Plays

### Gallicanus: Battlefield Conversion

*[[play-Gallicanus|Gallicanus]]* presents the conversion of a Roman military commander on the [[scythia|Scythian]] battlefield. Facing defeat against the Scythians, [[gallicanus-character|Gallicanus]] is converted by [[john-and-paul|John and Paul]], Grand Almoners to [[constance|Constance]], and immediately baptized with a [[chastity|vow of celibacy]] (001-001). His conversion is not merely personal — it is political: the empire's most powerful general renounces marriage, military ambition, and eventually life itself.

> When threatened with defeat in battle, Gallicanus is converted by John and Paul, Grand Almoners to Constance, and immediately baptized — taking a vow of celibacy.
> (001-002)

The Gallicanus model is conversion-through-intercession: [[constance|Constance]]'s secret [[chastity|vow]] creates the need, and [[john-and-paul|John and Paul]] execute the mission. Conversion here is mediated — the holy persons act as instruments, not the direct agents.

### Paphnutius: Intellectual Conversion

*[[play-Paphnutius|Paphnutius]]* shows the conversion of [[thais|Thais]] the courtesan, who renounces her wealth and sinful life to do penance in a sealed cell. [[paphnutius|Paphnutius]] approaches her not with threats but with rational argument, drawing on the philosophical tradition of *protreptic* discourse (005-001). Thais's response is immediate and total — she burns her wealth publicly and consents to three years of solitary penance.

> The conversion of Thais by the hermit Paphnutius. Obedient to a vision, he leaves the desert, and, disguised as a lover of Thais, seeks her out in Alexandria, and leads her to repentance.
> (005-001)

This is conversion-through-reason: [[paphnutius|Paphnutius]] uses Socratic questioning to lead Thais to self-knowledge. Her conversion is intellectual as well as moral — she understands *why* her life must change, not merely *that* it must.

### Abraham: Conversion Through Disguise

*[[play-Abraham|Abraham]]* depicts [[mary|Mary]]'s conversion from harlotry back to religious life. [[abraham|Abraham]], her uncle and former hermit mentor, disguises himself as a lover to gain access to her, then reveals his identity and persuades her to return (004-001). The dramatic tension comes from the disguise itself — a holy hermit feigning desire to save a soul from desire.

> The fall and repentance of Mary, the niece of the hermit Abraham, who, after she has spent twenty years as a hermit, is corrupted by a false hermit, flees to a house of ill-fame, and is rescued by Abraham disguised as a lover.
> (004-001)

### Callimachus: Conversion Through Resurrection

*[[play-Callimachus|Callimachus]]* features the most supernatural conversion in the corpus: the pagan [[callimachus|Callimachus]], struck dead while attempting to violate [[drusiana|Drusiana]]'s corpse, is restored to life by [[john-and-paul|Saint John]] and immediately converted. His first words upon resurrection are a confession of faith. The play's double restoration — both [[drusiana|Drusiana]] and [[callimachus|Callimachus]] return from death — makes conversion here literally a death-and-rebirth experience.

## Historical / Theological Context

Conversion narratives were a staple of early medieval hagiography, modeled on biblical accounts such as the conversion of Saint Paul on the road to Damascus. The dramatic suddenness of conversion in Hrotsvit's plays reflects both the literary conventions of saints' lives and the theological understanding of [[divine-grace|grace]] as an unmerited divine gift. The hermit or holy person who facilitates conversion — [[abraham|Abraham]], [[paphnutius|Paphnutius]], [[john-and-paul|John and Paul]] — functions as an instrument of divine grace, not merely as a persuasive human agent.

Hrotsvit adapts the desert ascetic conversion model for her [[gandersheim-abbey|Benedictine context]] at Gandersheim, emphasizing not isolation but reintegration into the Christian community after conversion. Gallicanus returns from Scythia to Rome and joins the Christian community there; Thais emerges from her cell into the communion of saints; Mary returns to her hermitage under Abraham's guidance.

### Literary Antecedents

Hrotsvit's treatment of conversion draws on both classical and Christian literary traditions. The *conversio* motif in late antique romance — particularly the Greek novels of Achilles Tatius and Heliodorus — provided a narrative template of transformation through ordeal that Hrotsvit Christianizes. Where the Greek romances depict erotic conversion (the chastening of desire into marital fidelity), Hrotsvit redirects the arc toward spiritual conversion: the redirection of eros toward agape, of worldly attachment toward divine devotion.

The influence of the *vitae patrum* (desert father narratives) is equally significant. The conversion of Thais mirrors the repentance of Pelagia and Mary of Egypt in hagiographic tradition — courtesans whose dramatic encounters with holy men lead to radical life transformation.

## Literary Analysis

Hrotsvit's conversion scenes deploy a consistent dramaturgical pattern that distinguishes her from both classical and hagiographic sources:

**The disguise motif.** In both *Abraham* and *Paphnutius*, the holy converter disguises himself — Abraham as a lover, Paphnutius as a suitor — to gain access to the fallen woman. This is a Christian inversion of the classical *recognition scene* (anagnorisis): instead of a long-lost family member being revealed, a spiritual identity is uncovered beneath a carnal disguise. The disguise is also a test of the converter's own virtue — the hermit must navigate genuine temptation while feigning desire.

**The public renunciation.** Thais burns her wealth in a public bonfire; Mary confesses before the community; Gallicanus renounces his military command before the emperor. Conversion in Hrotsvit is never private — it requires public witness, transforming the convert's social identity as thoroughly as their spiritual state.

**Conversion as dramatic climax, not prologue.** In classical drama, conversion (when it appears at all) is typically backstory. Hrotsvit makes it the central dramatic event — the moment the audience has been waiting for. This is her most radical dramaturgical innovation: converting the *peripeteia* (reversal) of Terentian comedy into a theological crisis that resolves in grace rather than marriage.
