# Antony

**Antony** (also Anthony or Antony the Great, c. 251–356 CE) is a legendary Christian hermit and desert father who appears in Hrotsvit's play *[[paphnutius|Paphnutius]]* as a revered spiritual counselor. When [[paphnutius|Paphnutius]] seeks divine confirmation that the penitent harlot [[thais|Thais]] has found favour with God, he turns to his "brother Antony" for intercessory prayer.

## Role in Paphnutius

Antony serves as the play's spiritual anchor. [[paphnutius|Paphnutius]], unable to discern whether [[thais|Thais]]'s three-year penance has been accepted by God, journeys to Antony's desert retreat to seek his prayers (005-009)(006-010).

| [[paphnutius|Paphnutius]] | Three years of her penance are over, and I cannot tell whether her sorrow has found favour with God. For some reason He will not enlighten me. I know what I will do. I will go to my brother Antony and beg him to intercede for me. (005-009)(006-010) |

## Spiritual Authority

Antony's authority in Hrotsvit's rendering is absolute and unquestioned. When [[paphnutius|Paphnutius]] — himself a holy hermit — meets Antony, the dynamic is one of a younger brother deferring to an elder. Antony receives [[paphnutius|Paphnutius]] with warmth and joy, yet immediately assumes the role of examiner and guide (005-010)(006-011).

| [[paphnutius|Paphnutius]] | I know that God will listen to his good servant Antony. (005-010)(006-011) |

## The Vision of Paul

The pivotal miracle of the play occurs through Antony's spiritual mediation. He gathers his disciples, including his disciple [[john-and-paul|Paul]], to join [[paphnutius|Paphnutius]] in prayer. [[john-and-paul|Paul]] receives a vision of a splendid bed prepared in heaven, guarded by four radiant virgins and surrounded by angels. A divine voice declares this glory is prepared not for Antony but for [[thais|Thais]] (005-011)(006-012).

| [[john-and-paul|Paul]] | Father, I saw in my vision a splendid bed. It was adorned with white hangings and coverings, and a crown was laid on it, and round it were four radiant virgins. (005-011)(006-012) |

Antony interprets this vision as divine confirmation that [[thais|Thais]]'s penance is accepted. He urges [[paphnutius|Paphnutius]] to return to the penitent and assure her of pardon (005-011)(006-012).

| Antony | You must indeed. It is time her valiant penance ended. You should assure her that her pardon is complete; you should fill her with hope, and speak to her only of the beatitude in store for her. (005-011)(006-012) |

## Character and Significance

Antony embodies the ideal of desert sanctity in Hrotsvit's hagiographic universe. His humility is evident when he deflects the heavenly vision — his disciple [[john-and-paul|Paul]] assumes the glory is for Antony, but Antony knows himself "unworthy of such honour" (005-011)(006-012). This renunciation of spiritual pride marks him as a figure of perfected holiness rather than a dramatic protagonist; he exists in the play to validate and complete the penitential arc of others.

> Son, did you not know Antony was unworthy of such honour?
>
> (005-019)

## Historical Background

The historical Antony (Anthony the Great) was an Egyptian Christian monk and one of the founders of desert monasticism. By Hrotsvit's time, his legend — popularised through Athanasius's *Life of Antony* — had made him the archetype of the desert hermit throughout Christendom. Hrotsvit's depiction draws on this well-established tradition.
