**Gallicanus** is the titular protagonist of [[play-Gallicanus|Play I]] and Hrotsvit's most complex military character — a Roman general whose battlefield [[conversion]] transforms him from empire-builder to [[martyrdom|martyr]]. His arc spans the full [[play-Gallicanus|play]] in two parts: military triumph and [[conversion]] in Part I, exile and [[martyrdom]] in Part II. He is unique among Hrotsvit's protagonists in occupying both the conqueror and the conquered roles — first the agent of Roman power, then its victim.

> The conversion of Gallicanus, Commander-in-Chief. On the eve of his campaign against the Scythians, threatened with defeat in battle, Gallicanus is converted by John and Paul, Grand Almoners to Constance.
>
>
> (001-001)

## Biography

Gallicanus serves as Commander-in-Chief (*magister militum*) under Emperor [[constantine|Constantine]] (001-001). At the play's opening, he is betrothed to [[constantine|Constantine]]'s daughter [[constance|Constance]] (001-001) — unaware that she is a consecrated [[chastity|virgin]] who has secretly dedicated herself to God.

His military campaign against the [[scythia|Scythians]] brings him to the brink of defeat, where [[john-and-paul|John and Paul]], Grand Almoners to [[constance|Constance]], minister to him and precipitate his dramatic [[conversion]] (001-002). Upon returning to [[rome|Rome]], he renounces his claim to [[constance|Constance]], releases her from the betrothal, and withdraws from public life (001-002).

Under [[julian-the-apostate|Julian the Apostate]], he is exiled and receives the [[martyrdom|martyr's crown]] (001-003).

> Gallicanus, my General, whose victories have so often added to the glory of Rome — your silence fills me with alarm!
>
>
> (001-032)

## Role in the Plays

Gallicanus is the sole protagonist of [[play-Gallicanus|Play I]]. His dramatic trajectory follows a pattern that recurs throughout Hrotsvit's work — worldly power confronted by divine authority, followed by renunciation and [[martyrdom]]. He is unique among the protagonists as a high-ranking military figure whose conversion causes him to surrender not only his marital claim to [[constance|Constance]] but his entire military identity. The soldier who conquered nations now conquers himself.

## Key Scenes

1. **Betrothal to [[constance|Constance]]** — Gallicanus requests [[constance|Constance]]'s hand before the [[scythia|Scythian]] campaign, unaware of her secret [[chastity|vow]]. [[constantine|Constantine]] grants the request, setting the dramatic crisis in motion.
2. **Battlefield Crisis** — Facing defeat in [[scythia|Scythia]], Gallicanus is at his most vulnerable — the moment that prepares him for [[conversion]] (001-032).
3. **Conversion and Baptism** — [[john-and-paul|John and Paul]] convert Gallicanus on the battlefield; he receives baptism and immediately takes a vow of [[chastity|celibacy]] (001-002). The Scythian king Bradan also submits and is baptized.
4. **Renunciation** — Returning to [[rome|Rome]], Gallicanus renounces his claim to [[constance|Constance]] and withdraws from military life. He commends his daughters [[artemia|Artemia]] and [[attica|Attica]] to [[constance|Constance]]'s care.
5. **Martyrdom under [[julian-the-apostate|Julian]]** — Exiled by [[julian-the-apostate|Julian the Apostate]], Gallicanus receives the [[martyrdom|martyr's crown]] (001-003).

> Later he is exiled by order of Julian the Apostate, and receives the crown of martyrdom.
>
>

## Themes

Gallicanus embodies the intersection of military valor and Christian virtue. His [[conversion]] transforms a potential tragedy into spiritual triumph — the commander who conquers himself is greater than one who conquers nations. His [[chastity|celibacy]] vow after conversion parallels [[constance|Constance]]'s [[chastity|virginity]], creating a spiritual symmetry: both renounce marriage for a higher calling.

The two-part structure of the [[play-Gallicanus|play]] mirrors the shape of a saint's life — worldly success followed by suffering that leads to heavenly reward. Gallicanus's arc from general to hermit-like renunciant to martyr is the fullest realization of Hrotsvit's thesis that secular power must ultimately yield to spiritual authority.

His character also illustrates the **instrumentality of conversion**: Gallicanus does not seek conversion — it comes to him through [[constance|Constance]]'s agency and [[john-and-paul|John and Paul]]'s ministry. He is simultaneously the active protagonist of the military plot and the passive recipient of the spiritual plot, a duality that makes him dramatically richer than the self-directed hermits of other plays.

## Literary Analysis

Gallicanus is Hrotsvit's most complex protagonist because he is the only one who must be *converted* before he can be *martyred*. Unlike [[paphnutius|Paphnutius]] or [[abraham|Abraham]], who begin as holy men and remain so, Gallicanus undergoes a genuine transformation of character. His conversion is not a reinforcement of existing virtue but a radical reorientation — from pagan general to Christian celibate to martyr. This makes him the most "dramatic" of Hrotsvit's protagonists in the Aristotelian sense: his character changes through action.

Gallicanus also represents Hrotsvit's most direct engagement with the military culture of her Ottonian patrons. By making a successful general into a saint, she Christianizes the martial ideal without rejecting it — Gallicanus's military virtue is not erased but redirected. This would have resonated at the Ottonian court, where the fusion of military and Christian identity was a living ideological project.
