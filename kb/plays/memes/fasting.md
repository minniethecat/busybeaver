# Fasting

**Fasting** (*ieiunium*) is the voluntary abstention from food for spiritual purposes, one of the primary disciplines of the ascetic life. In Hrotsvit's plays, fasting is assumed rather than dramatized — it is the invisible infrastructure of sanctity.

## Fasting in the Hermit Life

The hermit-saints [[abraham|Abraham]] and [[paphnutius|Paphnutius]] have practiced fasting for decades before their plays open. Combined with vigil and [[prayer]], fasting is the means by which the body is subdued and the soul made receptive to divine guidance (001-049). The desert fathers' authority — their ability to command demons and guide souls — is built on this foundation of bodily discipline.

## Penitential Fasting

For the fallen penitents [[mary|Mary]] and [[thais|Thais]], fasting is part of the penitential regime. Mary's twenty-year enclosure includes severe dietary restriction; Thais's three-year sealed cell reduces her to bread and water (004-001). Here fasting functions as both punishment and medicine — the body that indulged in sin is retrained through deprivation.

Abraham's lament over Mary reveals the spiritual weight that ascetic tradition places on the discipline of fasting:

> Oh, Mary, think what you have thrown away! Think what a reward you had earned by your fasting, and prayers, and vigils. What can they avail you now! You have hurled yourself from heavenly heights into the depths of hell!
>
> (004-013)

## Fasting and Prayer

In the ascetic tradition Hrotsvit inherits, fasting and [[prayer]] are inseparable. The fasted body prays differently — more attentively, more humbly. This connection is implicit in the hermit dramas: Abraham's and Paphnutius's effectiveness in prayer is inseparable from their life of fasting (004-013). The disciplined body enables the attentive soul.
