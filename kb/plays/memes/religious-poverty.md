# Religious Poverty

**Religious Poverty** (*paupertas voluntaria*) is the voluntary renunciation of material possessions for the sake of spiritual perfection. As one of the three [[monastic-vows]], it is the least dramatized in Hrotsvit's plays — yet it underlies the entire ascetic economy and receives one of the most vivid theatrical images in the corpus.

## The Hermits' Poverty

The poverty of [[abraham|Abraham]] and [[paphnutius|Paphnutius]] is assumed rather than displayed. They live in the [[the-desert|desert]], owning nothing, sustained by what the wilderness provides. Their poverty is the precondition of their spiritual authority — having renounced everything, they have nothing left to lose and therefore nothing that can be used against them (P06-005).

## Thais's Bonfire

The most dramatic enactment of religious poverty is [[thais|Thais]]'s public burning of her wealth in [[play-Paphnutius|Paphnutius]]. The courtesan's riches are the visible sign of her sin; their destruction is the visible sign of her [[conversion]]. The bonfire transforms ill-gotten wealth into a sacrificial offering (005-012) — an act that inverts the economic logic of the brothel and demonstrates that the woman who was bought now burns what she was bought with.

## Poverty and Freedom

For Hrotsvit, poverty is not deprivation but liberation. The hermit in the desert is freer than the emperor in the [[the-imperial-court|court]]. Wealth binds the soul to the world; poverty releases it for God. This paradox — that having nothing is having everything — is central to the plays' value system.

## The Scholarly Perspective

> Roswitha often shows such touches of sympathy with natural human desires — and we are made to feel that, although the dramatist was in no doubt that the life of chastity, poverty, and obedience is the highest life, she understood how hard it is for those who embrace it to believe that the yoke will be easy and the burden light.
>
> (P06-005)
