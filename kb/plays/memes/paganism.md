# Paganism

**Paganism** (*paganismus*, *gentilitas*) is the pre-Christian religious system of the Roman Empire — the ideological antagonist against which every martyr in Hrotsvit's plays defines herself. It is represented institutionally by the [[the-capitol|Capitol]] temples, ritually by animal [[sacrifice]], and personally by the persecuting emperor.

## Paganism as Dramatic Antagonist

In Hrotsvit's dramatic economy, paganism is not merely a backdrop but an active antagonist. It demands sacrifice; it punishes refusal; it conflates religious and political loyalty (001-036). The martyr plays are structured around the confrontation between Christian confession and pagan demand — a confrontation that ends in [[martyrdom]] when the Christian will not yield.

## The Gods of Rome

Hrotsvit represents the Roman gods — Jupiter, the Capitoline triad — as demons or empty names. Her Christians do not argue that the gods exist but are weak; they argue that the gods are nothing at all. [[irena|Irena]]'s mockery of "a god bought cheap in the marketplace" is the most devastating articulation of this position: the Roman gods are not supernatural beings but human artifacts.

## Paganism and Empire

The identification of paganism with imperial power is central to Hrotsvit's political theology. The Emperor who demands sacrifice does so not from private devotion but from public policy — the gods protect Rome, and refusal to honour them is treason. Christian [[conversion]] is thus simultaneously a religious and political act: to accept Christ is to reject the divine basis of Roman authority.

## Paganism and the Literary Tradition

Hrotsvit situated her own work in conscious opposition to the pagan literary tradition. In her Preface, she acknowledges the "pernicious voluptuousness of pagan writers" (P07-004) even as she borrows from their forms. Her project — to replace Terentian comedy with Christian drama — was a literary act of conversion: taking the vessels of pagan culture and filling them with Christian content (P07-001).
