# Monasticism

Monasticism is the institutional framework of religious community life that shaped Hrotsvit of Gandersheim's world. As a Benedictine nun at [[gandersheim-abbey|Gandersheim Abbey]] in 10th-century Saxony, Hrotsvit wrote her plays within a monastic context that defined her intellectual and spiritual formation.

## The Benedictine Framework

Hrotsvit was "a Benedictine nun of Gandersheim, in Saxony" (P05-003) and "a nun of the Order of St. Benedict, who spent her religious life in the Convent of Gandersheim" (P05-004). The Benedictine Rule governed every aspect of daily life in the community. A contemporary chronicler records that the nuns "were forbidden to eat away from the common table at the appointed times, except in case of sickness. They slept together, and came together to celebrate the canonical hours. And they set to work together whenever work had to be done" (P05-007).

> Leuckfeld, in his voluminous history of Gandersheim, quotes a contemporary chronicler who praises the royal nuns for keeping all luxury and state out of the life of the community, and for observing the Rule of St. Benedict strictly. ## Gandersheim Abbey

The [[gandersheim-abbey|Abbey of Gandersheim]] was founded about the year 850 by Ludolph, Duke of Saxony, at the request of his wife Oda, a Frankish princess (P05-006). It was one of the "free abbeys" — its Abbess held authority directly from the King, with rights of overlordship extending for many miles, her own law courts, and men-at-arms. As such, the Abbess had the right to a seat in the Imperial Diet (P05-006). During the 10th and 11th centuries, the Abbesses were drawn chiefly from the royal house of Saxony.

## Monasticism and the Plays

The monastic context is essential to understanding Hrotsvit's work. Cardinal Gasquet notes that the plays are "a remarkable phenomenon, interesting to students of monasticism and of the drama alike" (P05-002). The plays' consistent glorification of vowed chastity, their rhetorical engagement with the tension between pagan classical literature and Christian scripture, and their sophisticated Latinity all reflect the educational environment of a 10th-century Saxon abbey, where monasteries like Corbei were "centres of learning and civilization" (P05-006).
