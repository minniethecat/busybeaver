**Christopher St. John** (1871–1960), born Christabel Gertrude Marshall, was an English author, suffragist, and translator. Her 1923 translation of [[hrotsvit|Hrotsvit of Gandersheim]]'s six plays — *The Plays of Roswitha* — is the source text for this wiki (P01-002).

## The Translation

St. John's translation was a landmark in Hrotsvit's English reception. Working from the Latin text, she produced readable, stageable English versions that brought Hrotsvit to a modern audience (P06-001). Her translation includes an extensive Foreword discussing Hrotsvit's life, sources, and dramatic technique, as well as a historical Introduction situating the plays in the context of [[gandersheim-abbey|Gandersheim]] and the [[ottonian-dynasty|Ottonian]] renaissance.

## Literary Judgment

St. John's Foreword offers one of the earliest sustained critical assessments of Hrotsvit's dramatic art (P03-012). She judges [[play-Abraham|Abraham]] and [[play-Paphnutius|Paphnutius]] as Hrotsvit's best work, notes structural weaknesses in [[play-Dulcitius|Dulcitius]], and defends the plays' dramatic viability against critics who considered them closet drama. Her judgment that Hrotsvit is "the first dramatist of the Christian West" has become a standard claim.

## Mediation

All quotations in this wiki attributed to Hrotsvit are mediated through St. John's translation. Her choices of diction, emphasis, and tone shape how English readers encounter the 10th-century canoness. This mediation is unavoidable but should be acknowledged: we read Hrotsvit through St. John.

## The Translator's Credo

> Those readers who are unable or unwilling to compare the translations with the original should be warned that Roswitha's dialogue is characterized by a simplicity and conciseness hardly attainable in any tongue but Latin. The difficulty of finding equivalents for the terse phrases employed tempts the translator to "write them up." Although I have aimed at producing a readable translation for lovers of the drama in all its forms rather than an exact paraphrase for scholars, I have tried to resist this temptation at the risk of making the dialogue seem at times almost ludicrously bald.
>
> (P06-004)
