# Pilgrimage

**Pilgrimage** (*peregrinatio*) is the practice of traveling to sacred sites for spiritual purposes. (001-044) While Hrotsvit's plays do not depict pilgrimage directly, the structural metaphor of pilgrimage — spiritual transformation enacted through physical movement — underlies the hermit dramas.

## Pilgrimage as Metaphor

The hermit dramas are structured as journeys: the saint descends from the [[the-desert|desert]] to the city, rescues the fallen soul, and returns with her to the desert for [[penance]]. This movement is an allegorical pilgrimage — a sacred journey whose destination is not a place but a state of soul. [[abraham|Abraham]] and [[paphnutius|Paphnutius]] are pilgrims in this extended sense: their lives are perpetual movement toward God.

## Pilgrimage and Penance

In medieval practice, pilgrimage was often imposed as [[penance]] — the sinner was required to travel to a distant shrine as an act of restitution. [[mary|Mary]]'s journey from the [[the-brothel|brothel]] to the desert cell and [[thais|Thais]]'s journey from [[alexandria|Alexandria]] to the [[the-sealed-cell|sealed cell]] are penitential pilgrimages — journeys that are simultaneously physical displacement and spiritual transformation.

## Pilgrimage and Conversion

[[gallicanus-character|Gallicanus]]'s military campaign to [[scythia|Scythia]] is inverted into a pilgrimage: what begins as imperial conquest ends as spiritual [[conversion]]. The battlefield becomes a baptismal font; the general returns a pilgrim who has found not territory but God. Movement through space enables transformation of soul.
