# Ephrem

**Ephrem** is a hermit and the trusted spiritual confidant of [[abraham|Abraham]] in Hrotsvit's play *[[abraham|Abraham]]*. He functions as the play's chorus-like [[martyrdom|witness]]: the steady, discerning companion who hears [[abraham|Abraham]]'s anxieties, validates his decisions, and ultimately rejoices in the redemption of [[mary|Mary]].

## Role in Abraham

Ephrem appears at every crucial turn of the play's plot. [[abraham|Abraham]] opens the drama by approaching Ephrem for counsel about his young niece [[mary|Mary]], whom he has taken in after her parents' death (004-001).

| [[abraham|Abraham]] | Brother Ephrem, my dear comrade in the hermit life, may I speak to you now, or shall I wait until you have finished your divine praises? (004-001) |

Ephrem's reply immediately establishes the spiritual unity between the two hermits:

| Ephrem | We have one heart and one soul. We ought to agree. (004-001) |

## Counselor on Mary's Vocation

When [[abraham|Abraham]] confides his plan to raise [[mary|Mary]] as a consecrated virgin, Ephrem joins him in persuading the child. His counsel is marked by theological eloquence: he explains that [[mary|Mary]]'s name means "star of the sea," urging her to live up to its celestial significance (004-002).

| Ephrem | [[mary|Mary]], my child, means "star of the sea" — that star which rules the world and all the peoples in the world. (004-002) |

He paints a vivid picture of the heavenly reward awaiting a virgin: passing through the air with angels, walking the sky, sweeping round the zodiac, until the Virgin's Son receives her in His mother's bridal room (004-002). [[mary|Mary]]'s swift acceptance prompts Ephrem's delighted exclamation:

| Ephrem | Out of the mouths of babes and sucklings! A childish heart, but a mature mind! (004-002) |

## Witness to Tragedy

When [[mary|Mary]] falls into sin and flees the hermitage, [[abraham|Abraham]] returns to Ephrem in anguish. Ephrem's reactions guide the audience's response — from shuddering horror at the seducer's wiles (004-003), to marvel at the devil's triumph over the hermits (004-003), to interpreting [[abraham|Abraham]]'s prophetic dream as a sign that "some day [[mary|Mary]] will return to you" (004-003).

When [[abraham|Abraham]] resolves to seek [[mary|Mary]] in disguise, Ephrem provides crucial theological justification for bending the strict hermit rule:

| Ephrem | He Who knows the secret places of the heart can tell with what motive every action is done. That scrupulous and fair Judge will not condemn a man for relaxing our strict rule for a time and descending to the level of weaker mortals if by so doing he can make more sure of rescuing an errant soul. (004-003) |

## Rejoicing in Redemption

In the play's final scene, Ephrem receives the news of [[mary|Mary]]'s recovery with profound joy. His questions about her penitential life — her hair shirt, vigils, fasts, and rigorous discipline — draw out the fullness of her transformation (004-007). He closes his role by joining [[abraham|Abraham]] in a song of thanksgiving:

| Ephrem | Let us sing a song of thanksgiving — let us glorify the only begotten Son of God, Who of His love and mercy will not let them perish whom He redeemed with His holy blood. (004-007) |

## Dramatic Function

Ephrem is the play's steady moral compass. Unlike [[abraham|Abraham]], who is wracked by anxiety and grief, Ephrem remains composed and discerning throughout. His presence allows [[abraham|Abraham]]'s interior struggles to be voiced externally, and his theological judgments — on [[mary|Mary]]'s name, on the permissibility of disguise, on the joy of redemption — provide the play's interpretive framework. He is less a character than the embodiment of wise spiritual friendship.

> A mind taught so early to despise temporal things should be fixed on heaven.
>
> (004-007)
