**Callimachus** is the protagonist of Hrotsvit's [[play-Callimachus|Play III]] — a young pagan whose obsessive passion for the married Christian woman [[drusiana|Drusiana]] drives the drama from sin through [[conversion|repentance]] to miraculous resurrection. (003-001) He is one of Hrotsvit's most psychologically complex characters: a figure who moves from feverish desire to genuine [[conversion]] through death and divine intervention, making his story the most supernatural redemption arc in the corpus. (003-002)

## Biography

Callimachus is a young pagan of the Roman senatorial class, living in the same city as [[drusiana|Drusiana]] and her husband [[andronicus|Andronicus]]. (003-002) The play opens with Callimachus already in the grip of an overwhelming passion for [[drusiana|Drusiana]], which Hrotsvit presents not as tender affection but as a destructive force consuming senses and soul alike. His love is described in the vocabulary of illness and fire — a pathological desire that persists even when its object is beyond reach. (003-002)

## Role in the Plays

Callimachus is the protagonist of [[play-Callimachus|Callimachus (Play III)]], the only play in the corpus centered on a male sinner's redemption. Unlike [[gallicanus-character|Gallicanus]], whose conversion is mediated by holy intercessors, or [[abraham|Abraham]], who is already perfected, Callimachus must be *killed and resurrected* to be saved — making his conversion the most violent and supernatural in the corpus. (003-017) His dramatic arc inverts the classical pattern: in Terence, the passionate young lover succeeds; in Hrotsvit, the passionate young lover must die and be reborn to escape his passion. (P06-008)

## Key Scenes

1. **The Fever of Desire** — The play opens with Callimachus in the grip of overwhelming passion for [[drusiana|Drusiana]]. His desire is presented as a destructive fire consuming senses and soul. (003-002)

2. **Sin Against the Dead** — After [[drusiana|Drusiana]] dies (having prayed for death rather than be the occasion of sin), Callimachus bribes [[fortunatus|Fortunatus]], the custodian of her tomb, to help him violate the corpse. (003-014) His attempt to strip the shroud from [[drusiana|Drusiana]]'s body in the sepulchre represents passion untamed by Christian virtue — desire so fierce it pursues its object beyond death.

3. **Divine Intervention and Death** — A serpent — sent by divine agency — appears in the tomb and kills both Callimachus and his accomplice [[fortunatus|Fortunatus]]. (003-015) This miraculous intervention protects [[drusiana|Drusiana]]'s body while delivering divine judgment on the transgressors. Callimachus dies in the very act of his sin.

4. **Resurrection and Conversion** — The Apostle [[john-and-paul|John]] arrives at the tomb and, through prayer, restores Callimachus to life. (003-017) This resurrection is both physical and spiritual: Callimachus rises a changed man, his passion transformed into faith. He confesses his sin openly and receives the Apostle's absolution. [[john-and-paul|John]] also offers to restore [[fortunatus|Fortunatus]], but [[fortunatus|Fortunatus]] — whose spiritual state differs — rejects the offer and dies permanently. (003-017) The contrast between the two men underscores the sincerity of Callimachus's conversion.

## Themes

Callimachus represents a distinct type in Hrotsvit's character repertoire: the passionate sinner who becomes a penitent. (P06-008) Unlike the [[martyrdom|martyrs]] of [[play-Sapientia|Sapientia]] or [[play-Dulcitius|Dulcitius]] who resist external persecution, Callimachus fights an internal battle against his own desires. His arc from lust to redemption mirrors the penitential themes of [[play-Abraham|Abraham]] and [[play-Paphnutius|Paphnutius]], but with the unique addition of literal resurrection — a physical death and rebirth that sacramentalizes his spiritual transformation. (003-017)

The play also explores the **limits of desire**: Callimachus's passion is so extreme that it transgresses the boundary between life and death, pursuing [[drusiana|Drusiana]] into the tomb. Only a death-and-resurrection can break this bond — mere persuasion would be insufficient. (003-014) This makes *Callimachus* Hrotsvit's darkest play before its redemptive ending, the work where sin is most tenacious and grace most violent. (P06-008)

> I am filled with such remorse that I have no desire to live unless I can be born again in Christ and changed.
>
> (003-017)

## Literary Analysis

Callimachus is Hrotsvit's most radical experiment in the psychology of sin. Where her other protagonists are either already holy ([[paphnutius|Paphnutius]], [[abraham|Abraham]]) or converted through rational persuasion ([[gallicanus-character|Gallicanus]], [[thais|Thais]]), Callimachus requires supernatural violence to be saved. (P06-008) This makes him dramatically unique: he is the only protagonist who must *die* before he can convert.

The play's necrophilic episode — Callimachus attempting to strip a corpse in its tomb — is the most transgressive scene Hrotsvit ever wrote. (003-014) It tests the limits of her program of Christianizing Terentian form: Terence never went this dark. The serpent that kills Callimachus is a complex symbol — simultaneously the serpent of Eden (punishing sin), the serpent of Asclepius (healing through death), and a theatrical *deus ex machina* that resolves an otherwise unresolvable dramatic crisis. (003-015)
