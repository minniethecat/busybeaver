# St. Emmeran Monastery

St. Emmeran Monastery is the Benedictine monastery in Ratisbon (modern Regensburg, Germany) where the original manuscript of Hrotsvit of Gandersheim's works was discovered in the late 15th century. This discovery, by the humanist Conrad Celtes, brought Hrotsvit's long-forgotten plays to the attention of Renaissance Europe.

## The Discovery

Conrad Celtes, "the well-known humanist, discovered the manuscript, the writing of which cannot be earlier than the 9th, or later than the 10th century, in the library of the Benedictine monastery of St. Emmeran, Ratisbon, in the last days of the 15th century" (P05-009). The manuscript's survival through five centuries of European history is remarkable, and its discovery ensured that Hrotsvit's unique voice — "Rara avis in Saxonia visa est" (P05-003) — would not be lost.

## Publication History

"In the year 1501 it was printed" (P05-009), the first edition featuring an interesting frontispiece depicting Hrotsvit presenting her works to the Emperor Otho II in the presence of her Abbess Gerberg. The plates illustrating incidents in the plays "have been attributed to both Dürer and Cranach, but they are not signed" (P05-009). A second edition, that of Schurzfleisch, was issued in 1707 with biographical and philological notes.

## The Manuscript's Journey

"In 1803 [the manuscript] had been moved from St. Emmeran to the Munich library" (P05-009), where it remains. The French commentator Magnin collated the Celtes and Schurzfleisch texts with the original manuscript at Munich and "found one or two readings preferable to those of Celtes" (P05-009). Magnin also restored stage directions omitted by Celtes, including one in the eighth scene of Callimachus that "affords valuable evidence that the play was acted, or at least intended for representation" (P05-009).

## Significance

The monastery's preservation of Hrotsvit's manuscript is a testament to the role of Benedictine monasticism in transmitting medieval literature. Without the library of St. Emmeran, the plays of the 10th-century nun of Gandersheim would likely have been lost to history.
