# Religion and Morality

Middlemarch is, among other things, a novel about the difficulty of being good. Its religious characters — [[edward-casaubon|Edward Casaubon]] the desiccated scholar, [[camden-farebrother|Camden Farebrother]] the humane naturalist-clergyman, [[nicholas-bulstrode|Nicholas Bulstrode]] the evangelical banker with a hidden past, and [[dorothea-brooke|Dorothea Brooke]] whose ardent religious feeling finds no adequate institutional form — represent different answers to the question of what a moral life looks like in a world without easy certainties.

## Theological and Moral Concepts

Eliot herself had lost her orthodox faith by the time she wrote Middlemarch, but the novel is anything but secular. It is saturated with the language of religious aspiration redirected toward earthly objects — Dorothea's longing for "an object, a life, which shall be a binding theory of duty," Lydgate's "spots of commonness," the narrator's insistence that "the growing good of the world is partly dependent on unhistoric acts." The novel seeks a morality that can survive the loss of doctrinal certainty, grounded not in revelation but in the disciplined exercise of sympathy.
