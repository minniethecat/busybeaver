# Evangelical Party

The network of evangelical influence — centered on Bulstrode but extending through [[medical-board|the Infirmary board]], charitable committees, and the town's religious institutions — that represents the intersection of piety and power in [[middlemarch-town|Middlemarch]].

## Organization Overview

The Evangelical Party in [[middlemarch-town|Middlemarch]] is not a formal organization but a constellation of aligned interests: Bulstrode's banking wealth, Mr. Tyke's candidacy for the Infirmary chaplaincy, the prayer meetings and charitable committees that enact evangelical piety in institutional form. Its power is real but informal — exercised through appointments, patronage, and the quiet pressure of moral disapproval.

## Theological and Social Character

The evangelicalism Eliot depicts is not the enthusiastic revivalism of Whitefield or the Wesleys but a later, more institutional phase: a religion of sobriety, self-examination, and moral surveillance. Its adherents in Middlemarch are distinguished less by doctrinal commitments than by a shared idiom — the language of "providence," "the Lord's work," and "the state of one's soul." Bulstrode's version of evangelicalism is particularly revealing: he speaks the language of divine instrumentality while exercising what the narrator calls "a power in the town disproportionate to his years or his origin." The irony is that his piety — however genuine in its own terms — functions as a social technology, enabling a banker of uncertain background to command deference from men who would otherwise consider him a social inferior.

## Role in the Narrative

The party is the vehicle through which Bulstrode exercises institutional power, and its workings are the novel's study of how religious conviction becomes social control. The chaplaincy dispute — Tyke vs. Farebrother — is the party's most visible intervention, and Lydgate's vote for Tyke (against his own preference for Farebrother) is one of his first moral compromises.

## The Bulstrode Paradox

The novel's treatment of Bulstrode's evangelicalism is more complex than simple exposure of hypocrisy. Bulstrode is not a fraud — he genuinely believes in the doctrines he professes, and his charitable giving is real. What Eliot exposes is not insincerity but the psychological mechanism by which sincere religious conviction can coexist with moral blindness. Bulstrode justifies his acquisition of wealth — including the fortune that rightfully belonged to Will Ladislaw's mother — as "the application of a providential order." His piety provides the narrative through which he reconciles his actions with his self-image. When [[john-raffles|Raffles]] reappears with knowledge of the past, it is this narrative that collapses, leaving Bulstrode exposed to a guilt his theology cannot accommodate without breaking.

## The Farebrother Alternative

The novel uses [[camden-farebrother|Mr. Farebrother]] as a counter-type to evangelical rigor. Farebrother is a naturalist, a whist-player, a man who lives comfortably with his own imperfections — and yet he is the novel's most genuinely good clergyman. His pastoral care is real, his kindness to his parishioners unforced. The contrast is structural: Bulstrode's evangelicalism produces institutional power but spiritual isolation; Farebrother's latitudinarian tolerance produces no power but genuine human connection. When Farebrother is eventually appointed to the living at Lowick — after Bulstrode's disgrace — the novel signals its preference for a religion of humane accommodation over one of rigorous judgment.
