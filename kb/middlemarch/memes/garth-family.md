# Garth family

The Garth family is the household of Caleb and Susan Garth, representing one of the novel's most positive portrayals of domestic life and provincial integrity. Described as "rather a large one," the Garth family embodies Eliot's ideal of useful labor, mutual affection, and unpretentious virtue (PN=024-005).

## Overview

The Garth family is introduced through **[[caleb-garth]]**, the land agent and surveyor whose honesty and dedication to work are absolute. He and his wife **[[susan-garth]]** have raised a large family on modest means. The family lives simply — as one character notes, "the Garths are so poor, and live in such small way" (PN=011-074) — yet their household is characterized by warmth, industry, and moral clarity.

Eliot presents the Garths as a counterpoint to the materialism and social climbing that pervade Middlemarch society. While families like the Vincys pursue status and wealth, the Garths find contentment in honest work and family solidarity. The family includes several children, most notably **[[mary-garth]]**, whose plain-speaking integrity and practical wisdom make her one of the novel's most admirable figures.

## In the Novel

The Garth family serves as a moral touchstone throughout the narrative. Caleb Garth's philosophy of work — that "business" is a form of service to the community — infuses the family's values (PN=024-005). His decision to take on the management of Stone Court and later the large estates demonstrates his competence, while his refusal to profit from questionable dealings shows his incorruptibility.

Mary Garth embodies the family's best qualities. Her refusal to destroy Featherstone's will, even when offered money, and her steady affection for Fred Vincy despite his shortcomings, reflect the Garth ethos of principled action. The family's poverty is never presented as shameful but as an honest condition that sharpens their moral discernment.

The Garths also serve as a narrative contrast to characters whose ambition leads to moral compromise — particularly Bulstrode and, in a different register, Lydgate. Where those characters are undone by their desire for wealth or influence, the Garths' contentment with their station preserves their integrity.
