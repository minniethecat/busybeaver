# Songs of Innocence (William Blake)

[[will-ladislaw|Will]]iam Blake's illuminated poetry collection, first printed in 1789 and later combined with *Songs of Experience* as the composite *Songs of Innocence and of Experience*, supplies the epigraph to Chapter LXXIV of *[[middlemarch-town|Middlemarch]]* — the chapter in which Dorothea Casaubon, now widowed, resolves to help Lydgate in his professional crisis and discovers a new sense of purpose beyond her own grief.

## Work Overview

*Songs of Innocence* is a sequence of deceptively simple lyrics, each illustrated by Blake's own engraved plates, that evoke a world of childhood vision, pastoral harmony, and divine immanence. Poems such as "The Lamb," "The Chimney Sweeper," and "The Divine Image" present a universe in which mercy, pity, peace, and love are the human form divine. Blake's radical Christianity — he was a visionary poet who saw the divine in the human, and whose political sympathies were with revolution and liberation — made him an outsider in his own time. By the mid-Victorian period, however, Blake had been recovered by the Pre-Raphaelites and by Alexander Gilchrist's biography (1863), and Eliot, who was deeply interested in the visionary tradition and in dissenting religious thought, would have found in Blake a kindred spirit.

## Author & Context

[[will-ladislaw|Will]]iam Blake (1757–1827) was a poet, engraver, and visionary whose work was almost entirely ignored in his lifetime. His recovery in the 1860s placed him at the centre of Victorian debates about the relationship between poetry and prophecy, and about the possibility of a religious vision compatible with Romantic individualism. Eliot's quotation from *Songs of Innocence* aligns her novel with Blake's conviction that the divine is not located in institutional religion but in the exercise of human love — a conviction Dorothea embodies in her post-Casaubon awakening.

## Appearance in the Novel

The epigraph to Chapter LXXIV reads:

> "And Love, the human form divine; / And Peace, the human dress." —WILLIAM BLAKE: *Songs of Innocence.* [074-002]

The lines come from "The Divine Image," Blake's great hymn to the four virtues — Mercy, Pity, Peace, and Love — which constitute both God and human being. Eliot places them at the head of the chapter in which Dorothea, widowed and at liberty, turns her attention outward to Lydgate's plight: "The idea of some active good within her reach haunted her like a passion." The epigraph's theology of incarnate love — love taking human form — is precisely what Dorothea enacts. Her "human fellowship" with Lydgate, her determination to act rather than merely to feel, embodies Blake's vision of a divine that dwells not in heaven but in the mutual care of human beings.

## Significance

This epigraph marks a turning point in Dorothea's moral trajectory. Freed from Casaubon's oppressive scholarship, she discovers that her "ardent nature" has an object: the relief of another's suffering. Blake's "human form divine" provides a theological framework for what might otherwise seem mere philanthropy — Dorothea's impulse is not condescending benevolence but the recognition that love itself is sacred. The epigraph's conjunction of "Love" and "Peace" as human attributes ("dress") rather than abstractions captures the chapter's movement from Dorothea's inner turmoil to the "confident hope" with which she awaits Lydgate. In Blake's vision, as in Eliot's, salvation is not individual but relational: it happens between people.
