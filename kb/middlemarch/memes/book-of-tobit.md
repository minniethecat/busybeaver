# Book of Tobit (Biblical Apocrypha)

The Book of Tobit, a deuterocanonical narrative composed in the Second Temple period (c. 225–175 BCE), supplies the epigraph to Chapter LXXIV of *[[middlemarch-town|Middlemarch]]* — the chapter in which [[middlemarch-town|the town of Middlemarch]] discovers the scandal enveloping Bulstrode, and Rosamond Lydgate's marriage faces its first public test.

## Work Overview

The Book of Tobit recounts the story of the pious Tobit, blinded by bird droppings and impoverished in exile, and his son Tobias, who journeys to Media to recover a family debt and finds a wife in Sarah, a young woman tormented by a demon that has killed her seven previous bridegrooms. The "[[marriage|Marriage]] Prayer" — spoken by Tobias and Sarah on their wedding night — is one of the Bible's most beautiful expressions of marital devotion: "Mercifully grant that we may grow aged together." The book exists in the Septuagint and the Vulgate, was retained in the King James Apocrypha, and was familiar to Victorian readers of all denominations. For Eliot, whose novels return obsessively to the theme of marriage as a moral crucible, the Prayer offered a vision of conjugal love as shared pilgrimage — a vision against which the novel's actual marriages are painfully measured.

## Author & Context

The Book of Tobit belongs to the wisdom and diaspora literature of the Hellenistic period, combining folkloric elements (the demon Asmodeus, the magical fish) with practical piety and moral instruction. Its inclusion in the Apocrypha meant it occupied a liminal space in Protestant biblical culture — canonical for Catholics, edifying but not authoritative for Anglicans. Eliot, whose early evangelical piety had been transformed by German biblical criticism, would have read Tobit as a human document, valuable not for its supernatural machinery but for its moral insight into marriage, fidelity, and the providence that works through ordinary human faithfulness.

## Appearance in the Novel

The epigraph to Chapter LXXIV reads:

> "Mercifully grant that we may grow aged together." —BOOK OF TOBIT: *Marriage Prayer.* [074-002]

The prayer is the plea of Tobias and Sarah, united against the demonic force that has destroyed Sarah's previous husbands — a plea that their marriage should be blessed with longevity. Eliot places it at the head of a chapter in which marriage is everywhere under threat. The town's "ardent charity" — the busy benevolence that delights in making a neighbour unhappy for her own good — descends upon Rosamond and Mrs. Bulstrode, whose husbands' disgrace has made them objects of collective moral scrutiny. The epigraph's vision of marriage as shared endurance stands in painful contrast to Rosamond's self-absorption and Lydgate's growing despair.

## Significance

Eliot's deployment of this particular biblical text is devastatingly precise. The [[marriage|Marriage]] Prayer asks for what Rosamond's marriage conspicuously lacks: the willingness to grow old together, to face hardship as a shared condition rather than an individual grievance. The chapter traces the first public consequences of Bulstrode's disgrace, and Rosamond — who has always regarded her marriage as a vehicle for social ascent — discovers that it has become a vehicle for social descent instead. The epigraph's quiet plea for mercy frames the chapter's action as a test: can a marriage survive the discovery that it was built on something other than the desire to "grow aged together"? The answer, for the Lydgates, will be as equivocal as [[middlemarch-town|Middlemarch]] itself.
