# Pseudodoxia Epidemica

*Pseudodoxia Epidemica* (commonly known as *Vulgar Errors*) is an encyclopaedic work by the English physician and essayist Sir Thomas Browne (1605–1682), first published in 1646 and expanded through six editions in the author's lifetime. It systematically examines and refutes widely held but erroneous beliefs — about natural history, medicine, geography, and scripture — in a prose of extraordinary baroque splendour. In *[[middlemarch-town|Middlemarch]]*, George Eliot draws on Browne twice: once as a chapter epigraph that frames the novel's treatment of prejudice and opposition, and once in a narrative aside that reveals [[will-ladislaw|Will Ladislaw]]'s temperament through Browne's own idiom.

## Work Overview

*Pseudodoxia Epidemica* belongs to the great tradition of seventeenth-century prose that includes Robert Burton's *Anatomy of Melancholy* and Jeremy Taylor's sermons — a tradition that Eliot knew intimately and that shaped her own prose rhythms. Browne's project is at once sceptical and credulous: he debunks the belief that badgers have legs shorter on one side, but affirms the existence of griffins; he dismantles vulgar errors with scientific rigour while writing in a style of sumptuous Latinate complexity. The book's central insight — that error is not individual but "epidemical," a contagion passed from age to age — is one that *[[middlemarch-town|Middlemarch]]* dramatises across its social canvas, from the gossip of [[middlemarch-town|Middlemarch town]] to the intellectual vanities of its scholars.

## Author & Context

Sir Thomas Browne (1605–1682) practised medicine in Norwich through the turbulent decades of the Civil War and Restoration, producing a body of prose — *[[religio-medici|Religio Medici]]*, *Hydriotaphia*, *The Garden of Cyrus*, *Pseudodoxia Epidemica* — that is unmatched in English for its fusion of scientific curiosity, religious meditation, and stylistic magnificence. Eliot's admiration for Browne was profound and lifelong: she read him alongside Burton and Jeremy Taylor in her early years, and his influence on her prose — its syntactic architecture, its learned allusiveness, its movement between scepticism and sympathy — is among the most consequential in her development. The epigraph to Chapter XLV shows Eliot returning to Browne at the height of her powers, drawing on his authority to frame one of the novel's most socially expansive sequences.

## Appearance in the Novel

Browne enters *[[middlemarch-town|Middlemarch]]* first through an indirect allusion. Describing [[will-ladislaw|Will Ladislaw]]'s habit of vehement self-expression, the narrator notes that "[[will-ladislaw|Will]] had — to use Sir Thomas Browne's phrase — a 'passionate prodigality' of statement both to himself and others" [037-033]. The phrase is characteristic: Browne's prose furnishes a vocabulary for psychological states that the novel's realist idiom cannot quite capture, lending [[will-ladislaw|Will]]'s emotional extravagance a quality both literary and self-regarding.

The more substantial Browne presence is the epigraph to Chapter XLV, drawn from *Pseudodoxia Epidemica*:

> "It is the humor of many heads to extol the days of their forefathers, and declaim against the wickedness of times present. Which notwithstanding they cannot handsomely do, without the borrowed help and satire of times past; condemning the vices of their own times, by the expressions of vices in times which they commend, which cannot but argue the community of vice in both. Horace, therefore, Juvenal, and Persius, were no prophets, although their lines did seem to indigitate and point at our times." [045-001]

The attribution — "—SIR THOMAS BROWNE: *Pseudodoxia Epidemica*." [045-002] — explicitly names the work. The chapter that follows concerns the opposition to Lydgate's New Fever Hospital, a social drama in which "the illimitable range of objections" [045-003] draws on prejudice, professional jealousy, and sheer ignorance — Browne's "vulgar errors" translated into the key of provincial medical politics.

## Significance

Eliot's invocation of *Pseudodoxia Epidemica* at Chapter XLV is as precise as it is resonant. Browne's thesis — that error is not a personal failing but a social phenomenon, "epidemical" in its spread — provides the philosophical frame for the novel's anatomy of [[middlemarch-town|Middlemarch]] opinion. The opposition to the Fever Hospital is not merely a plot point; it is a case study in how communities manufacture and sustain false beliefs, how "the borrowed help and satire of times past" is recruited to condemn the present, and how even Horace and Juvenal can be conscripted into the service of prejudice. In a novel that consistently resists the romance of the heroic reformer, Browne's epigraph reminds the reader that Lydgate's defeat is not an individual tragedy but a systemic one — a "community of vice" that links the [[middlemarch-town|Middlemarch]] of the 1830s to the Norwich of Browne's day and, by implication, to every human society that has ever preferred comfortable error to uncomfortable truth.
