# Homeric Epics

The *Iliad* and *Odyssey* are epic poems traditionally attributed to Homer, composed in ancient Greek and dating from approximately the eighth century BCE. The *Iliad* narrates a pivotal episode in the Trojan War; the *Odyssey* recounts the hero Odysseus's ten-year journey home. In *[[middlemarch-town|Middlemarch]]*, George Eliot deploys the adjective "Homeric" as a sophisticated critical term — a way of measuring the distance between the heroic scale of classical epic and the constrained moral drama of English provincial life in the 1830s.

## Work Overview

The Homeric epics stand at the fountainhead of the Western literary tradition. For nineteenth-century readers, Homer represented not merely antiquity but a certain quality of literary experience: amplitude, particularity, the ability to render ordinary details — a warrior's shield, a household's daily routines — with what Matthew Arnold called "nobility." This quality of "Homeric particularity" is exactly what Eliot's narrator invokes in *[[middlemarch-town|Middlemarch]]*, but with a characteristic realist twist: the modern novelist, unlike the epic poet, must find nobility in lives that lack the scale and stakes of the Trojan plain.

## Author & Context

The Homeric Question — whether the *Iliad* and *Odyssey* were the work of a single poet, multiple authors, or an oral tradition — was a live scholarly debate in the nineteenth century. F. A. Wolf's *Prolegomena ad Homerum* (1795) had challenged the unity of the poems, and the question engaged scholars across Europe. For Eliot's intellectual circle, Homer was simultaneously the foundational figure of European literature and a site of critical controversy. This dual status — Homer as both monument and problem — made him an apt reference point for a novel that is itself preoccupied with the relationship between tradition and critique.

## Appearance in the Novel

The Homeric adjective appears at four significant moments, each marking a different facet of the novel's engagement with epic conventions. The earliest and most deflating comes from [[fred-vincy|Fred Vincy]] at a dinner party:

> "'Aha, Miss Rosy, you don't know Homer from slang. I shall invent a new game; I shall write bits of slang and poetry on slips, and give them to you to separate.'" [011-041]

Fred's remark — playful, snobbish, characteristic — reduces Homer to a marker of class distinction: knowing Homer separates the educated from the uneducated, and [[rosamond-vincy|Rosamond Vincy]], for all her accomplishments, may not pass the test.

The narrator deploys "Homeric" more seriously in describing the quasi-criminal intrigue around Bulstrode's past:

> "a sheep-stealing epic written with Homeric particularity" [039-016]

The formulation is a compressed essay in literary criticism. An epic about sheep-stealing is a contradiction in terms — the epic mode demands heroic scale, and sheep-stealing is the quintessence of the petty and sordid. But "Homeric particularity" — the attention to concrete detail that is one of the epics' enduring strengths — is precisely what a realist novel about provincial crime requires. The sentence distils Eliot's realist programme: apply epic seriousness to unheroic material.

A similar usage appears in the narrator's characterisation of the auction scenes:

> "the care which he did not know to be Homeric" [056-035]

Here, "Homeric" denotes a quality of attention that the character himself does not recognise — a dignity of observation bestowed by the narrator's framing rather than the character's consciousness. The adjective bridges the gap between the character's limited self-awareness and the narrator's sympathetic omniscience.

[[will-ladislaw|Will Ladislaw]], the novel's most aesthetically articulate character, invokes Homer in defence of his own sensibility, describing certain passages of poetry as "exquisitely Homeric" [022-052]. For [[will-ladislaw|Will]], Homer represents a standard of artistic integrity against which he measures his own ambitions — a touchstone of the artistic life he imagines but cannot quite sustain.

## Significance

"Homeric" in *[[middlemarch-town|Middlemarch]]* is never a neutral descriptive term. It carries the weight of a literary tradition that the novel simultaneously invokes and subverts. Epic expects heroes, battles, divine intervention, and cosmic stakes. *[[middlemarch-town|Middlemarch]]* offers instead [[dorothea-brooke|Dorothea Brooke]] making "ardent" but limited choices, Lydgate compromised by debt and marriage, Bulstrode undone by a past that is sordid rather than tragic. Yet the narrator's insistence on "Homeric particularity" as a quality worth noticing — even in sheep-stealing, even in auction catalogues — asserts that the realist novel, no less than epic, can confer dignity on its subjects through the quality of attention it brings to them. This is the aesthetic argument embedded in the adjective.
