# Henry V (Shakespeare)

[[will-ladislaw|Will]]iam Shakespeare's historical drama, first performed around 1599, furnishes the epigraph to Chapter LXXVII of *[[middlemarch-town|Middlemarch]]* — a chapter concerned with the gap between outward appearance and inward character, and with the moral danger of suspicion misapplied.

## Work Overview

*Henry V* is the final play in Shakespeare's second historical tetralogy, depicting the young king's transformation from the dissolute Prince Hal of *Henry IV* into the heroic victor of Agincourt. The play is famously ambivalent about its hero: Henry is at once the ideal Christian king and a calculating political operator whose rhetoric can mask ruthlessness. The Chorus's appeals to the audience's imagination — asking us to "piece out our imperfections with your thoughts" — reflect on the very nature of representation and judgment, themes that Eliot extends into the novelistic domain. Victorian audiences knew *Henry V* well; it was a staple of the nineteenth-century stage and a frequent source of patriotic quotation.

## Author & Context

Shakespeare's history plays were undergoing a revival of critical interest in the Victorian period, and George Eliot's intellectual circle — particularly G. H. Lewes, who wrote extensively on drama — read them as psychological studies in leadership, legitimacy, and the performance of virtue. Eliot herself, in her essays and reviews, had reflected on the moral complexity of theatrical representation. The choice of *Henry V* for Chapter LXXVII is pointed: this is the chapter in which Dorothea's "simplicity" — her refusal to entertain "subtle constructions and suspicions of hidden wrong" — stands in implicit contrast to the more suspicious, and more worldly, judgments of those around her.

## Appearance in the Novel

The epigraph to Chapter LXXVII reads:

> "To mark the full-fraught man and best indued / With some suspicion." —*Henry V*. [077-004]

The chapter opens on Lydgate and Rosamond, then moves to Dorothea's reflections on [[will-ladislaw|Will Ladislaw]]. The epigraph's irony is structural: the "full-fraught man" marked with suspicion is, in the chapter's moral economy, the suspicious judge himself rather than the person judged. Rosamond suspects Lydgate of failing her; the [[middlemarch-town|Middlemarch]] gentry suspect Ladislaw of being tainted by his grandfather's disreputable past. But Dorothea — whose "nature was of that kind" that holds up "an ideal for others in her believing conception of them" — refuses the suspicious gaze. Her faith in [[will-ladislaw|Will]] is not naive; it is, Eliot suggests, a form of moral power: "There are natures in which, if they love us, we are conscious of having a sort of baptism and consecration."

The chapter thus inverts the epigraph's apparent meaning. To be "best indued with some suspicion" is not, in Eliot's treatment, to be shrewdly distrustful but to be suspicious of suspicion itself — to question the easy moral judgments that circulate in drawing-rooms and over dinner tables. Dorothea's "simplicity" is not simple-mindedness but the hard-won refusal to participate in the social machinery of character assassination.

## Significance

Eliot's use of *Henry V* participates in the novel's sustained critique of how communities judge individuals. The Shakespearean king is a master of public performance, a man who knows he is always being watched and calibrates his actions accordingly. The characters of *[[middlemarch-town|Middlemarch]]* — Bulstrode, Lydgate, even Dorothea — are subject to the same social scrutiny, but without the king's power to control the narrative. The "suspicion" of the epigraph becomes, in the novel's dense web of gossip and reputation, less a tool of discernment than a collective moral failure. Dorothea's alternative — belief in others not because evidence demands it but because love consecrates it — is offered not as epistemological certainty but as ethical practice.
