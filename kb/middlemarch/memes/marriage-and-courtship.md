# Marriage and Courtship

Marriage is the central social transaction of Middlemarch, and its courtship plots — Dorothea's disastrous first marriage, Rosamond's calculated pursuit of Lydgate, Fred Vincy's long probation before winning Mary Garth — are the novel's primary engines of narrative and moral inquiry. This page indexes every person and event related to the marriage plots, and every concept page that illuminates Eliot's complex vision of love, duty, and self-deception.

In Middlemarch, no marriage is purely personal. Each union — or failed union — reverberates through families, alters property arrangements, and reshapes the social landscape. Eliot's genius lies in showing how the most intimate choices are also the most consequential, and how the line between love and self-interest is never as clear as her characters believe.
