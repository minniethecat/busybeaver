# Susan Garth

**Susan Garth** (née unknown), known throughout the novel as **Mrs. Garth**, is the wife of [[caleb-garth|Caleb Garth]] and mother of [[mary-garth|Mary]], [[letty-garth|Letty]], Ben, and other children. A former schoolmistress, she brings grammatical rigour and domestic discipline to the Garth household.

## Character and Domestic Life

Mrs. Garth is first seen in her kitchen, "with her sleeves turned above her elbows" making pies while simultaneously drilling her son Ben in English grammar [024-007]. This image — simultaneously domestic and pedagogical — defines her character. She educated her children using "Lindley Murray's Grammar," and the narrator notes with gentle irony that "in a general wreck of society [she] would have tried to hold her 'Lindley Murray' above the waves" [024-009].

Her approach to child-rearing combines practicality with precision:

> "To teach you to speak and write correctly, so that you can be understood," said Mrs. Garth, with severe precision. "Should you like to speak as old Job does?" [024-011]

## Relation to Fred Vincy

Mrs. Garth occupies an important place in [[fred-vincy|Fred Vincy]]'s story. He had always been "rather more in awe of her" than of Caleb, and came to the Garth kitchen to make his confession about the debt [024-006]. Mrs. Garth received him with characteristic composure, "surprised" but not given to expressing it [024-030].

Her quiet moral authority is evident in her response to Fred's troubles. She does not moralise but allows the situation to unfold, trusting in her husband's judgment while maintaining her own steady presence.

## Financial Realism

When Fred asks whether Mary and Ben are her only pupils now, Mrs. Garth replies with unpretentious candour:

> "I am at a low ebb in the matter of pupils," said Mrs. Garth, smiling. "I don't know how it is — parents object to grammar so much." [024-044]

Her remark reveals both her awareness of the family's precarious finances and her wry acceptance of reality — qualities she shares with her daughter Mary.
