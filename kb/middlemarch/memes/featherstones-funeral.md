# Featherstone's Funeral

The death of old [[peter-featherstone|Peter Featherstone]] and the subsequent reading of his will — an episode that stages the novel's darkest comedy of greed, revealing the avarice that property inspires in those who expect to inherit it.

## Overview

[[peter-featherstone|Peter Featherstone]]'s deathbed is the novel's great theatrical set-piece. Surrounded by relatives who have gathered not to comfort him but to secure their claims on his estate, the old man — lucid, malicious, and still in control — plays them against each other with grim satisfaction. His funeral is an extension of the same drama: the relatives attend not to mourn but to hear the will, and their disappointment when the estate goes to the unknown [[joshua-rigg|Joshua Rigg]] is one of the novel's most satisfying reversals.

## Background

Featherstone has spent his life accumulating property and exercising power through the promise and threat of inheritance. His two marriages — both to women with their own money — have enriched him; his childlessness has made him the object of every relative's calculation. He has "the programme of his funeral" (032-032) arranged in detail, treating his own death as one more opportunity to control the living.

## Key Developments

The deathbed scene is a masterpiece of black comedy. Featherstone, drifting in and out of consciousness, demands that [[mary-garth|Mary Garth]] destroy one of his wills — a request she refuses on moral grounds, one of the novel's key moments of integrity. After his death, the will is read: the bulk of the estate goes to [[joshua-rigg|Joshua Rigg]], an illegitimate son whose existence the family never suspected, while the expectant relatives receive trinkets and insults. The funeral itself is a parade of disappointed greed.

## Consequences and Significance

Featherstone's fortune — and its unexpected destination — shapes the novel's subsequent events. [[fred-vincy|Fred Vincy]]'s hopes are dashed; [[mary-garth|Mary Garth]]'s integrity is confirmed; [[joshua-rigg|Joshua Rigg]] becomes a person of consequence, however briefly. The episode is the novel's most concentrated treatment of property as a moral force — how the expectation of inheritance distorts character and poisons relationships.
