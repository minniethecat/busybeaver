# Casaubon Wedding

The marriage ceremony uniting [[dorothea-brooke|Dorothea Brooke]] and the Reverend [[edward-casaubon|Edward Casaubon]], followed by their departure for a wedding journey to [[rome|Rome]] — an event that marks the end of Dorothea's girlhood and the beginning of her painful education in marital reality.

## Event Summary

[[dorothea-brooke|Dorothea Brooke]] and [[edward-casaubon|Edward Casaubon]] are married in a quiet ceremony in Chapter 10 of the novel. The "proper preliminaries to the wedding" [010-014] are managed with provincial decorum, and Dorothea is "glad of a reason to be out of doors" as the emotional weight of her decision settles upon her. The couple then depart for an extended wedding journey to [[rome|Rome]] — "in those days, the wedding journey was still the mark of a superior union" [019-004] — a choice that reflects Casaubon's scholarly pretensions but that proves emotionally disastrous for Dorothea.

In [[rome|Rome]], surrounded by "severe classical nudities and smirking Renaissance heads" [reference to 009-009], Dorothea experiences the first acute pangs of disillusionment. Her new husband withdraws into his research in the [[vatican|Vatican]] library, leaving her to confront the emptiness of her marriage amid the alien splendor of the Eternal City. The wedding journey, intended as the romantic beginning of a shared intellectual life, becomes the setting for Dorothea's first recognition that she has made a terrible mistake.

## Context & Background

The wedding follows quickly upon the engagement, with Dorothea's characteristic intensity driving the preparations forward. The "proper preliminaries" include the negotiation of the marriage settlement, which establishes the financial terms of the union and which will later become relevant when Casaubon adds his punitive codicil [the codicil event is covered in [[casaubons-will]]].

The choice of [[rome|Rome]] as the wedding destination is significant. For Casaubon, [[rome|Rome]] is a research site — the location of manuscripts relevant to his "Key to All Mythologies." For Dorothea, it represents the classical and Renaissance heritage she has studied but never experienced. The gap between Dorothea's romantic expectations and the reality of being left alone in rented apartments while her husband pursues his dusty scholarship is the novel's first sustained depiction of marital disappointment. "She is married; I saw her wedding-ring on that wonderful left hand" [019-007] — [[will-ladislaw|Will Ladislaw]]'s observation of Dorothea in [[rome|Rome]] introduces the figure who will become her true romantic partner.

## Key Participants

- **[[dorothea-brooke|Dorothea Brooke]] Casaubon** — The bride, who enters marriage with high spiritual hopes and begins to confront its emotional poverty almost immediately.
- **[[edward-casaubon|Edward Casaubon]]** — The groom, whose scholarly obsessions and emotional limitations are thrown into relief by the intimacy of the wedding journey.
- **[[celia-brooke|Celia Brooke]]** — Dorothea's sister, who remains at [[tipton-grange|Tipton Grange]] and represents the life Dorothea has left behind.
- **[[will-ladislaw|Will Ladislaw]]** — Casaubon's young cousin, whom Dorothea encounters in [[rome|Rome]] and who becomes an implicit contrast to her husband: young, warm, artistic, and emotionally alive.
- **[[mr-brooke|Mr. Brooke]]** — The uncle who gives Dorothea away and who later becomes involved in the political dimensions of the novel.

## Aftermath & Consequences

The wedding journey to [[rome|Rome]] serves as the novel's crucible for Dorothea's transformation. Her tears in the [[vatican|Vatican]] — "the weeping six weeks after her wedding" [020-010] — mark the death of her romantic illusions and the beginning of her moral maturity. The wedding also sets in motion the plot machinery involving [[will-ladislaw|Will Ladislaw]], whose presence in [[rome|Rome]] creates the first threads of the romantic alternative that will develop through the remainder of the novel.

Upon their return to [[lowick|Lowick]], the Casaubons settle into the cold routines that define their marriage, and Casaubon's suspicions about [[will-ladislaw|Will]] — first kindled during the [[rome|Rome]] sojourn — will eventually crystallize into the punitive codicil. The wedding journey is thus the pivotal event that transforms Dorothea from an idealistic girl into a woman learning the difficult truths of adult life.
