# Passion

The powerful current of intense feeling — romantic, intellectual, and moral — that courses through [[middlemarch-town|Middlemarch]] and that Eliot portrays as both an animating and a destabilizing force.

## Overview

"All Dorothea's passion was transfused through a mind struggling towards an ideal life" [005-010] — this sentence captures Eliot's characteristic approach to passion. Unlike the romantic novelists who treat passion as a force that overwhelms reason, Eliot presents it as intimately connected with intellect and moral aspiration. Dorothea's passion is not merely romantic but intellectual and spiritual; it is her "passionate desire to know and to think" [004-035] as much as her capacity for love.

The novel's Prelude explicitly frames passion through the figure of Saint Theresa, whose "passionate, ideal nature demanded an epic life" [P01-001]. This framing establishes passion as heroic rather than merely emotional — a force that, properly directed, could change the world, but that in the constrained circumstances of provincial life finds no adequate outlet.

## In the Novel

Dorothea is the novel's primary vehicle for exploring passion. Her marriage to Casaubon is a passionate mistake — she pours "all her youthful passion" [003-020] into the union with a man she imagines as a great intellect but who proves emotionally and spiritually arid. The tragedy is not passion itself but its misdirection: Dorothea's passionate nature needed an object worthy of its intensity, and Casaubon was not that object.

The novel also explores passion through its secondary characters. [[will-ladislaw|Will Ladislaw]] represents a more conventional romantic passion, but his relationship with Dorothea is deepened by shared intellectual and moral commitments. [[rosamond-vincy|Rosamond Vincy]]'s passion is for social elevation and aesthetic refinement — a cooler, more calculating version that contrasts sharply with Dorothea's ardor. Lydgate's passion for medical science is the intellectual equivalent of romantic passion, and his tragedy is that this nobler passion is compromised by his susceptibility to Rosamond's more superficial charms.

Eliot's narrator is notably skeptical of passion divorced from judgment. "There was no passion behind those sonnets to Delia" [005-055] — this observation about Casaubon's sterile engagement with love poetry epitomizes the novel's insistence that genuine passion must be rooted in genuine feeling, not merely in performance or convention.
