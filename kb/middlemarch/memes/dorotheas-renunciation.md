# Dorothea's Renunciation

The climactic moral choice of the novel — when Dorothea learns of the codicil in Casaubon's will and must decide between the fortune that would secure her independence and the love that would fulfill her heart.

## Overview

Casaubon's will contains a provision that disinherits Dorothea if she marries [[will-ladislaw|Will Ladislaw]]. The choice it forces is brutally simple: Casaubon's money or [[will-ladislaw|Will Ladislaw]]. Dorothea chooses [[will-ladislaw|Will]], renouncing the fortune, and the novel presents this choice as both a moral victory — she refuses to let the dead man's jealousy govern her life — and a quiet tragedy: the woman who dreamed of "more complete renunciation" (020-022) and great works settles for domestic happiness, however genuine. The renunciation is, in its way, an inverted echo of the novel's opening meditation on Saint Theresa: Dorothea, too, lives "a life beyond the ordinary woman's," but its field is narrow and its monuments are invisible.

## Background

Dorothea's first marriage to Casaubon was itself a form of renunciation — she gave up comfort, status, and the prospect of a conventional marriage for the promise of intellectual companionship that never materialized. Her relationship with [[will-ladislaw|Will Ladislaw]], which develops in the long aftermath of Casaubon's death, represents the life she might have had: passionate, equal, alive. The codicil makes that life cost something real — not just money but the independence that money represents.

## Key Developments

The scene of Dorothea's decision is one of the novel's emotional peaks. She has seen [[will-ladislaw|Will]] with Rosamond, believed — wrongly — that he loves another woman, and spent a night of anguish that strips away every illusion. In the morning, she opens her curtains and sees the world with new clarity: "She opened her curtains and looked out towards the bit of road that lay in view." What she sees is not a vision of glory but the ordinary world — a man with a bundle, a woman with a child — and the ordinariness is the point. Her renunciation is not of the world but of her own fantasies about it.

## Consequences and Significance

The renunciation is the novel's final argument about the relationship between love and property. By choosing [[will-ladislaw|Will]], Dorothea affirms that she is not Casaubon's property — that her life belongs to her, not to his posthumous jealousy. But the novel refuses to make this choice costless: she loses the fortune, the social position, the capacity for large-scale philanthropy. What she gains is real but quiet — a love that is particular, embodied, and sufficient.
