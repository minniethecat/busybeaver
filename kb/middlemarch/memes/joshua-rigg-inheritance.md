# Joshua Rigg's Inheritance

The revelation that [[peter-featherstone|Peter Featherstone]]'s fortune goes not to any of the relatives who courted him but to an illegitimate son no one knew existed — the novel's most dramatic demonstration that property follows its own logic, indifferent to merit or expectation.

## Overview

When [[peter-featherstone|Peter Featherstone]]'s will is read, the assembled relatives — Vincys, Waules, and Featherstones who have spent years positioning themselves for this moment — learn that the bulk of the estate goes to [[joshua-rigg|Joshua Rigg]], an illegitimate son whose existence Featherstone had never acknowledged. The relatives receive trinkets and insults; the unknown son receives everything. It is the novel's most satisfying narrative reversal, and its most pointed commentary on the relationship between inheritance and justice.

## Background

Featherstone has spent his life using the prospect of inheritance to manipulate his relatives. His two marriages, his accumulation of property, his careful cultivation of expectation — all were exercises in power. The will, when it comes, is his final act of manipulation: it rewards no one who expected reward, punishes no one who deserved punishment, and obeys no logic but the dead man's caprice.

## Key Developments

[[joshua-rigg|Joshua Rigg]] himself is an anti-climax. He arrives at [[stone-court|Stone Court]], takes possession, and sells the property almost immediately — to Bulstrode, who transforms it into the site of his own moral catastrophe. Rigg disappears from the narrative as abruptly as he entered it, having served his function: to demonstrate that inheritance is not a moral system but a mechanism for the arbitrary transfer of property.

## Consequences and Significance

The inheritance is the novel's most direct challenge to the idea that property embodies moral desert. The relatives who expected Featherstone's money were greedy, but their greed was shaped by a system that made inheritance the primary path to security. The will exposes that system as arbitrary — and the novel, characteristically, declines to offer a comforting alternative.
