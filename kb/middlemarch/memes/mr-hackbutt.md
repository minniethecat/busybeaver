# Mr. Hackbutt

Mr. Hackbutt is a wealthy tanner and Middlemarch townsman in George Eliot's *Middlemarch* (1871–1872). Known for his "fluent speech" and "glittering spectacles," he is an active participant in the town's political and social life — a Director of the Infirmary, a vocal presence at public meetings, and a representative of the comfortable provincial middle class whose opinions tend to shift with self-interest.

## Character Overview

Eliot introduces Mr. Hackbutt during the Infirmary chaplaincy debate: "a rich tanner of fluent speech, whose glittering spectacles and erect hair were turned with some severity towards innocent Mr. Powderell."[^1] His fluency is both his defining trait and his limitation — his speech floats "more widely than usual, and leaving much uncertainty as to its ultimate channel."[^2] He is a man whose opinions are elaborate but whose convictions are shallow, a type of the provincial improver who enjoys the sound of his own voice at public meetings.

His social position is established by his property: he owns a house on Peter's Place, and his wife Fanny Hackbutt is among the women who form Middlemarch's informal social jury. The Hackbutts are not quite at the top of Middlemarch society — "the Middlemarch tribes of Toller, Hackbutt, and the rest" look down on outsiders like Lydgate[^3] — but they are securely entrenched within it.

## Role in the Narrative

Mr. Hackbutt appears at several key moments of Middlemarch public life. During the Infirmary chaplaincy election (chapter 18), he speaks against Mr. Tyke's appointment, suggesting that the Directors should not simply "carry out propositions emanating from a single quarter"[^1] — a veiled reference to Bulstrode's influence. However, his intervention backfires when Mr. Plymdale retorts that Hackbutt has implied anyone voting for Tyke is "a Servile Crawler,"[^4] and the room turns away from Hackbutt, "leaving him to feel the uselessness of superior gifts in Middlemarch."[^5]

When rumors circulate about Mr. Brooke buying the *Pioneer*, Hackbutt is heard spreading the story in Mr. Hawley's office.[^2] During Lydgate's medical reform efforts, Hackbutt weighs in on the dispensing debate, siding with the established practitioners against the newcomer's innovations.[^6]

His most significant appearance, however, comes through his wife in chapter 74, during the Bulstrode scandal. Mrs. Hackbutt provides a running commentary on the public disgrace: "I think she ought to separate from him," "Fancy living with such a man!"[^7] When Mrs. Bulstrode calls at the Hackbutt home seeking information, Mrs. Hackbutt receives her with "more tightness of lip and rubbing of her hands than was usually observable,"[^8] delivering veiled cruelties disguised as sympathy. The Hackbutt household thus becomes a site where Middlemarch's moral judgment is enacted through social exclusion.

## Relationships

- **Fanny Hackbutt** — his wife Fanny, hostess of Middlemarch tea-tables where reputations are made and unmade
- **[[nicholas-bulstrode]]** — the banker whose influence Hackbutt resists, but whose downfall the Hackbutts help enforce through social ostracism
- **[[mr-hawley]]** — the lawyer with whom Hackbutt has occasional political disagreements
- **[[mr-toller]]** — fellow Middlemarch notable; they banter over wine about Lydgate's reforms
- **[[tertius-lydgate]]** — the reformist physician whom "the Middlemarch tribes of Toller, Hackbutt, and the rest" view as an outsider

## Key Appearances

- **Chapter 18** — Speaks at the Infirmary Board meeting on the chaplaincy question; his intervention is dismissed
- **Chapter 37** — Spreads the rumor that Mr. Brooke has secretly purchased the *Pioneer*
- **Chapter 45** — Discusses Lydgate's medical reforms with Mr. Toller at a dinner party
- **Chapter 74** — The Hackbutt household becomes the stage for Mrs. Bulstrode's social humiliation (primarily through Mrs. Hackbutt's dialogue)
