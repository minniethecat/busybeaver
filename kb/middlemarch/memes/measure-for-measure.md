# Measure for Measure (Shakespeare)

[[will-ladislaw|Will]]iam Shakespeare's dark comedy or "problem play," first performed around 1604, furnishes epigraphs for two chapters of *[[middlemarch-town|Middlemarch]]* — both concerned with guilt, temptation, and the distance between public reputation and private conduct.

## Work Overview

*Measure for Measure* centres on the Duke of Vienna, who feigns absence and leaves the puritanical Angelo as his deputy. Angelo proceeds to enforce long-dormant laws against sexual immorality, condemning Claudio to death for impregnating his fiancée. When Claudio's sister Isabella, a novice nun, pleads for his life, Angelo offers to spare Claudio in exchange for Isabella's virginity — revealing the hypocrisy beneath his righteous exterior. The play's title comes from the Sermon on the Mount ("with what measure ye mete, it shall be measured to you again"), and its central preoccupation — that those who judge most severely are themselves most vulnerable to the temptations they condemn — resonates through *[[middlemarch-town|Middlemarch]]*'s treatment of Bulstrode, Lydgate, and the [[middlemarch-town|Middlemarch]] community's appetite for moral scandal.

## Author & Context

[[will-ladislaw|Will]]iam Shakespeare (1564–1616) was widely performed and read in the Victorian period, and George Eliot's novels are saturated with Shakespearean allusion. The "problem plays" — *Measure for Measure*, *Troilus and Cressida*, *All's Well That Ends Well* — held particular appeal for Victorian intellectuals because of their moral ambiguity and resistance to easy comic resolution. Eliot, who reviewed performances and argued for Shakespeare's philosophical seriousness, found in *Measure for Measure* a dramatic model for her own method: the refusal to divide characters into the simply virtuous and the simply vicious.

## Appearance in the Novel

**Chapter LXVI** epigraph:

> "'Tis one thing to be tempted, Escalus, / Another thing to fall." —*Measure for Measure*. [066-003]

This chapter follows Lydgate as he succumbs to the temptation of gambling at the Green Dragon billiard-room. The epigraph's distinction between temptation and fall is brutally apt: Lydgate, who prided himself on scientific detachment and moral superiority, finds that debt and despair have narrowed his world to the point where "one powerful snatch at the devil's bait" seems rational. [[fred-vincy|Fred Vincy]], watching, is startled by the "strange reversal" — the high-minded doctor behaving exactly as the careless young man might have done. Yet Eliot refuses to equate Lydgate's fall with Angelo's calculated hypocrisy: his gambling is desperate, not predatory, and the chapter's sympathy remains with the man whose "conscious process of high, difficult combination" has been reduced to betting on billiard strokes.

**Chapter LXXI** epigraph:

> "*Froth*. I have so: because it is an open room, and good for winter. / *Clo*. Why, very well then: I hope here be truths." —*Measure for Measure*. [071-005]

This epigraph introduces the chapter in which the story of Bulstrode's past — and Raffles's death — begins to circulate through [[middlemarch-town|Middlemarch]]. The Clown's ironical "I hope here be truths" foreshadows the mixture of fact and malicious embellishment that will destroy Bulstrode's social standing. Bambridge, Hawley, and the gossips at the Green Dragon assemble a story that is, in its broad outlines, true — Bulstrode did profit from a shady past, Raffles did die at [[stone-court|Stone Court]] — but the moral texture of the truth, the "inward argument and motive," is precisely what gossip cannot capture.

## Significance

Eliot's double use of *Measure for Measure* epigraphs creates a diptych of moral judgment. In Chapter LXVI, the epigraph serves to complicate judgment: Lydgate is tempted and falls, but the reader is made to understand the narrowing of circumstance that precedes and produces the fall. In Chapter LXXI, the epigraph ironises the very act of judging: the town's appetite for "truths" about Bulstrode is indistinguishable from its appetite for scandal, and the "open room" of public discourse becomes the site of a moral trial in which the accused has no real chance of being understood. Together, the two epigraphs enact the novel's deepest procedural commitment: to judge not, lest ye be judged.
