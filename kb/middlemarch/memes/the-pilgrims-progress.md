# The Pilgrim's Progress

*The Pilgrim's Progress from This World, to That Which Is to Come* is a Christian allegory by John Bunyan (1628–1688), published in two parts (1678 and 1684). One of the most widely read works in the English language, it narrates the journey of a protagonist named Christian from the "City of Destruction" to the "Celestial City," encountering emblematic figures and trials along the way. In the Finale of *[[middlemarch-town|Middlemarch]]*, George Eliot quotes Bunyan's trial scene at length and uses it to frame the novel's culminating moral judgment on [[nicholas-bulstrode|Nicholas Bulstrode]].

## Work Overview

*The Pilgrim's Progress* employs a deceptively simple narrative — a dream-vision of a journey — to construct a comprehensive allegory of the Protestant experience of conversion, sanctification, and death. Bunyan's plain style, his vivid personifications of vices and virtues, and his rigorous Calvinist theology made the book a staple of English-speaking households well into the nineteenth century. Eliot, despite her own movement away from orthodox Christianity, knew Bunyan's text intimately and respected its moral psychology. Her epigraph to Chapter 85 draws on the scene from the trial of [[faith|Faith]]ful at Vanity Fair — a passage in which a jury composed of personified passions unanimously condemns an innocent man.

## Author & Context

John Bunyan (1628–1688) was a Nonconformist preacher and tinker by trade, imprisoned for twelve years in Bedford jail for preaching without a licence. He wrote *The Pilgrim's Progress* during a later imprisonment (1675–1676). Bunyan's biography — a working man who became one of the most influential writers in English — embodied the Dissenting tradition's belief in the spiritual authority of ordinary experience, a theme that resonates deeply with Eliot's own commitment to finding moral seriousness in "unhistoric" lives. The novel's narrator explicitly invokes Bunyan as "immortal" [085-002], acknowledging the enduring power of his allegorical imagination even from a post-Christian narrative standpoint.

## Appearance in the Novel

The novel's most sustained engagement with Bunyan comes in its closing movement. Chapter 85 opens with an epigraph drawn from the trial of [[faith|Faith]]ful before the jury at Vanity Fair:

> "Then went the jury out whose names were Mr. Blindman, Mr. No-good, Mr. Malice, Mr. Love-lust, Mr. Live-loose, Mr. Heady, Mr. High-mind, Mr. Enmity, Mr. Liar, Mr. Cruelty, Mr. Hate-light, Mr. Implacable, who every one gave in his private verdict against him among themselves, and afterwards unanimously concluded to bring him in guilty before the judge." [085-001]

The narrator's gloss on this passage is immediate and pointed. Unlike Bunyan's [[faith|Faith]]ful — who knows himself guiltless and is martyred for it — Bulstrode suffers a more complex fate:

> "When immortal Bunyan makes his picture of the persecuting passions bringing in their verdict of guilty, who pities Faithful? That is a rare and blessed lot which some greatest men have not attained, to know ourselves guiltless before a condemning crowd — to be sure that what we are denounced for is solely the good in us. The pitiable lot is that of the man who could not call himself a martyr even though he were to persuade himself that the men who stoned him were but ugly passions incarnate — who knows that he is stoned, not for professing the Right, but for not being the man he professed to be." [085-002]

This is the prelude to Bulstrode's internal reckoning. The town's condemnation of him is not unjust — his hands are stained by the death of Raffles — yet Eliot refuses the clean allegorical binary of Bunyan's world. Bulstrode is neither [[faith|Faith]]ful (innocent and martyred) nor a simple villain. He is "the man who could not call himself a martyr" because the charges against him are, in some measure, true. His tragedy is that of the divided conscience: a man who has "washed and diluted" his acts with "inward argument and motive" [085-003] and cannot bring himself to confess them to his wife.

## Significance

Eliot's use of *The Pilgrim's Progress* is a masterstroke of moral framing. In Bunyan's allegory, the jury of personified passions — Blindman, No-good, Malice, and the rest — are clearly in the wrong: they convict an innocent man. By invoking this scene at the moment of Bulstrode's disgrace, Eliot sets up a deliberately uncomfortable analogy. [[middlemarch-town|Middlemarch]]'s collective judgment of Bulstrode is not wholly wrong — he *is* guilty — but the passions that drive the town's condemnation (self-righteousness, gossip, schadenfreude) are precisely Bunyan's "ugly passions incarnate." The novel thus complicates the very moral clarity that Bunyan's allegory provides: what happens when the jury's verdict is factually correct but spiritually corrupt? This tension between outward judgment and inward truth is one of the novel's deepest preoccupations, and Bunyan's trial scene gives it a precise theological genealogy.
