# Jeremy Taylor

Jeremy Taylor (1613–1667) was an English cleric and theologian in the Church of England,
whose devotional writings are among the works [[dorothea-brooke|Dorothea Brooke]] has
committed to memory in George Eliot's *Middlemarch*. His presence in the novel's Prelude
establishes the intellectual and spiritual traditions that shape Dorothea's character
before the narrative proper begins.

## Character Overview

Although Taylor never appears as a character in the novel, his theological writings form
part of the intellectual fabric of Dorothea Brooke's religious sensibility. In the Prelude,
Eliot notes that Dorothea "knew many passages of Pascal's Pensees and of Jeremy Taylor
by heart" (001-004), placing Taylor alongside [[pascals-pensees|Pascal]] as one of the
formative influences on her ardent, ascetic spirituality. Taylor's works, particularly
*Holy Living* and *Holy Dying*, were widely read in nineteenth-century England and
represent the Anglican tradition of practical divinity that shaped provincial religious culture.

Taylor's prominence in Dorothea's reading is telling. As a seventeenth-century Anglican divine,
Taylor represents a moderate, humane form of Protestant piety—one that emphasizes the beauty of
holiness and the discipline of daily devotion rather than doctrinal rigidity. This aligns with
Dorothea's own religious temperament, which is fervent but not fanatical, ascetic but not
joyless. The fact that she knows Taylor "by heart" suggests not merely intellectual engagement
but deep spiritual internalization—she has absorbed Taylor's vision of the holy life into her
own moral imagination.

## *Holy Living* and *Holy Dying*

Taylor's two most famous works, *The Rule and Exercises of Holy Living* (1650) and *The Rule
and Exercises of Holy Dying* (1651), are manuals of practical devotion that guide the reader
through the disciplines of Christian life and the preparation for a good death. In the context
of *Middlemarch*, these works resonate with Dorothea's own aspirations toward a life of
significant action. Her desire to improve cottages, to assist in scholarship, to be of use
in the world—all can be read as secular translations of Taylor's ideal of holy living.

The theme of holy dying also finds echoes in the novel, particularly in the death of
[[edward-casaubon|Edward Casaubon]] and in Dorothea's own repeated encounters with mortality
and renunciation. Taylor's meditations on dying well as the culmination of living well provide
a theological framework for understanding the novel's persistent concern with how lives end
and what they mean.

## Quotations

Taylor's presence in the novel is concentrated in a single passage of the Prelude, but its
placement is strategic: it establishes Dorothea's intellectual seriousness before the
narrative begins and defines the religious tradition that will be tested by experience.

> Dorothea knew many passages of Pascal's Pensees and of Jeremy Taylor by heart; and to her the destinies of mankind, seen by the light of Christianity, made the solicitudes of feminine fashion appear an occupation for Bedlam. (001-004)

This is the novel's first substantive description of Dorothea's inner life, and the pairing
of Pascal and Taylor is carefully chosen. Pascal represents Catholic intensity and
philosophical rigor; Taylor represents Anglican moderation and practical devotion. That
Dorothea has absorbed both traditions "by heart" — not merely read but internalized —
marks her as a figure of genuine intellectual ambition, however constrained by her
provincial circumstances. The sentence also establishes the central tension of her
character: a mind formed for large purposes, trapped in a world that expects only
"the solicitudes of feminine fashion."

The narrator's reference to Taylor also carries a structural function. By invoking a
seventeenth-century divine in the opening paragraphs, Eliot signals that *Middlemarch*
will operate on a scale of historical and theological seriousness unusual for a
provincial novel. Taylor, like Saint Theresa in the Prelude's famous opening, belongs
to a world of epic spiritual endeavor — and Dorothea's kinship with that world is
what makes her story, in Eliot's phrase, one of "the many Theresas" who found for
themselves "no epic life."

## Role in the Narrative

Taylor functions in the novel as a cultural reference point rather than a character. His
inclusion in the Prelude establishes Dorothea's intellectual seriousness and her taste
for rigorous religious thought—a quality that distinguishes her from the more conventional
piety of her uncle [[arthur-brooke|Mr. Brooke]] and sister Celia. The juxtaposition of
Taylor with Pascal underscores the tension in Dorothea's character between Anglican
moderation and Catholic intensity, a tension that Eliot develops throughout the novel.

Taylor's influence also illuminates the intellectual gulf between Dorothea and Casaubon.
While Casaubon is engaged in arid scholarly labor on his *Key to All Mythologies*, Dorothea
has absorbed a tradition of practical divinity that seeks to transform life rather than merely
catalogue it. This asymmetry—Dorothea's vital spirituality versus Casaubon's desiccated
scholarship—is one of the novel's most important structural oppositions.

## Historical Context

Jeremy Taylor was a chaplain to Charles I and later a bishop in the Church of Ireland under
the Restoration. His prose style, characterized by elaborate metaphor and emotional warmth,
made him one of the most widely read Anglican writers of the seventeenth century. By the
nineteenth century, his works had become standard reading for devout Anglican households,
particularly among those with evangelical or High Church sympathies. Eliot's choice to name
Taylor specifically—rather than a more generic devotional writer—grounds Dorothea's religious
formation in a recognizable historical tradition and suggests a seriousness that goes beyond
conventional piety.

## Relationships

- **[[dorothea-brooke|Dorothea Brooke]]** — Taylor's writings are among the works that
  shape Dorothea's religious and intellectual formation. She knows his works "by heart,"
  indicating a depth of engagement that goes beyond mere reading to internalization of
  his vision of holy living and holy dying (001-004).

- **[[pascals-pensees|Pascal's Pensées]]** — paired with Taylor in the Prelude as the
  twin sources of Dorothea's intellectual-spiritual formation; the juxtaposition of
  Anglican moderation (Taylor) and Catholic intensity (Pascal) defines the creative
  tension in Dorothea's religious sensibility.

- **[[edward-casaubon|Edward Casaubon]]** — the implicit contrast between Taylor's vital
  practical divinity and Casaubon's dead scholarship structures one of the novel's central
  oppositions between living faith and sterile learning.
