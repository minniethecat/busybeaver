# Egoism

The great obstacle to sympathy, to justice, to love — the tendency of every consciousness to mistake its own view for the view, which *[[middlemarch-town|Middlemarch]]* anatomizes with unmatched psychological precision.

## Overview

Eliot's narrator famously observes that "we are all of us born in moral stupidity, taking the world as an udder to feed our supreme selves." Egoism, in this view, is not a sin of the especially selfish but the default condition of consciousness. Every character in *[[middlemarch-town|Middlemarch]]* struggles against it; most fail. The novel's moral achievement is to make this struggle visible and sympathetic, even when it fails.

## In the Novel

[[rosamond-vincy|Rosamond Vincy]] is the novel's purest study in egoism — a character so completely enclosed in her own perspective that she is genuinely incapable of understanding why Lydgate is distressed by debt, why the [[middlemarch-town|Middlemarch]] community judges her, or why anyone would prioritize anything above her comfort and amusement. She is not malicious; she is simply unable to imagine that other people's needs are as real as her own.

But egoism is not confined to the obviously selfish. Casaubon's scholarly isolation is a form of egoism — he cannot receive criticism or collaboration. Dorothea's early idealism is a form of egoism — she mistakes her own desires for universal moral truths. Even Lydgate's scientific ambition has an egoistic core — he wants to be great, not merely useful.

The novel's moral vision is not that egoism can be eliminated but that it can be recognized and, through effort and sympathy, partially overcome. Dorothea's growth is the novel's demonstration that this is possible — and its difficulty is the measure of the achievement.
