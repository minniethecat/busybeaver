# Le Légataire Universel (Jean-François Regnard)

Jean-François Regnard's five-act verse comedy, first performed at the Comédie-Française in 1708, supplies the epigraph to Chapter XXXV of *[[middlemarch-town|Middlemarch]]* — the chapter in which [[peter-featherstone|Peter Featherstone]]'s funeral procession becomes a parade of disappointed expectations, and the reading of the will shatters the hopes of every relation who had counted on a legacy.

## Work Overview

*Le Légataire Universel* is Regnard's most celebrated comedy, a farcical treatment of inheritance in which a cunning valet, Crispin, conspires to secure his master's legacy by impersonating a dying relative and dictating a fraudulent will. The play belongs to the tradition of Molière, whose company Regnard wrote for after Molière's death, but its cynicism about human greed is distinctly its own. Every character in the play is motivated by the desire for inheritance, and the comedy consists in watching their schemes collide. For Eliot, whose novel is structured around two major inheritance plots — Featherstone's will and Casaubon's codicil — Regnard's farce offered a vocabulary for thinking about the moral deformations that the anticipation of legacy produces.

## Author & Context

Jean-François Regnard (1655–1709) was the most successful French comic dramatist of the generation after Molière. His plays, characterised by nimble plotting, sharp social observation, and a worldly cynicism about human motives, held the [[paris-middlemarch|Paris]] stage for decades and were known to cultivated English readers through translation and theatrical adaptation. Eliot's French was excellent, and her acquaintance with French classical drama — Corneille, Racine, and Molière — would have extended to Regnard, whose treatment of avarice and inheritance offered a comic counterpoint to the more sombre English treatments of the same themes.

## Appearance in the Novel

The epigraph to Chapter XXXV reads:

> "Pour voir au naturel leur tristesse profonde / Je reviendrais, je crois, exprès de l'autre monde." —REGNARD: *Le Légataire Universel.* [035-003]

Translated: "To see in its natural state their profound grief, / I would return, I believe, expressly from the other world." The lines express the pleasure a dying man might take in returning from the dead purely to witness the disappointed faces of his would-be heirs. Eliot places them at the head of the chapter in which [[peter-featherstone|Peter Featherstone]]'s funeral procession winds towards [[lowick|Lowick]] church, and the relatives — "the Christian Carnivora who formed [[peter-featherstone|Peter Featherstone]]'s funeral procession" — assemble for the reading of the will with "their minds bent on a limited store which each would have liked to get the most of." The epigraph's sardonic delight in disappointed expectations frames the chapter's comedy of collective chagrin.

## Significance

Eliot's choice of Regnard is a masterstroke of tonal control. The Featherstone inheritance plot is at once genuinely comic — the parade of disappointed relatives is one of the novel's great set pieces — and morally serious, since it determines [[fred-vincy|Fred Vincy]]'s financial future and, indirectly, his fitness for Mary's love. The epigraph from *Le Légataire Universel* licenses the comedy: it tells the reader that the spectacle of greed disappointed is a legitimate source of laughter, and that Eliot's narrator is entitled to the same satiric pleasure Regnard's dying man would take in witnessing it. But the laughter is not cruel; it is the laughter of moral clarity, the recognition that the scramble for unearned wealth is beneath human dignity. The epigraph thus does double work: it authorises the comedy while quietly pointing towards its moral implications.
