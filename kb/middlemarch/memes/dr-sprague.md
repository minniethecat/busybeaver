# Dr. Sprague

Dr. Sprague is the senior physician of Middlemarch in George Eliot's *Middlemarch* (1871–1872). "Rugged and weighty," he represents the established medical order against which the reformist Tertius Lydgate must contend. He is a man of blunt opinions and professional solidity — a pillar of the old guard whose authority derives from position and presence rather than innovation.

## Character Overview

Eliot introduces Dr. Sprague through a series of contrasting physical and professional descriptions. He is "superfluously tall; his trousers got creased at the knees, and showed an excess of boot at a time when straps seemed necessary to any dignity of bearing."[^1] This sartorial detail — the unfashionable excess of boot — signals both his indifference to surface polish and his secure, unshakeable status. He is "considered the physician of most 'weight,' though Dr. Minchin was usually said to have more 'penetration.'"[^2] The word "weight" operates on multiple levels: physical (he has a "large heavy face"), professional (he is the senior physician), and intellectual (his opinions carry authority).

The narrator extends a surprising sympathy toward him: "For my part I have some fellow-feeling with Dr. Sprague: one's self-satisfaction is an untaxed kind of property which it is very unpleasant to find deprecated."[^3] This authorial aside complicates what might otherwise be a simple portrait of reactionary resistance.

## Role in the Narrative

Dr. Sprague appears at critical junctures of Lydgate's professional life in Middlemarch. At the Vincy dinner party (chapter 16), when Fred's fever requires medical consultation, Mr. Vincy glances "first at Dr. Sprague, the senior physician of the town, and then at Lydgate who sat opposite"[^4] — a gesture that marks the pecking order Lydgate must navigate. Sprague listens to Lydgate's opinion while "divest[ing] his large heavy face of all expression, and look[ing] at his wine-glass."[^2]

In the Infirmary chaplaincy controversy (chapter 18), Sprague declares his position without equivocation: "I go for Farebrother. A sharp man inside his pulpit, I recommend him."[^5] His blunt partisanship contrasts with Lydgate's attempt to remain above the fray.

Sprague's opposition to Lydgate is not, the narrator insists, about the younger man's medical knowledge or his desire to improve treatment: "what they disliked was his arrogance, which nobody felt to be altogether deniable."[^6] This careful distinction reveals the social dynamics that doom Lydgate's reforms: it is not his science but his manner — his failure to defer to established figures like Sprague — that turns the town against him.

His professional philosophy is summed up in his denunciation of Thomas Wakley, the medical reformer: "I disapprove of Wakley, no man more: he is an ill-intentioned fellow, who would sacrifice the respectability of the profession, which everybody knows depends on the London Colleges, for the sake of getting some notoriety for himself."[^7] The speech inadvertently reveals everything Lydgate is fighting against: a medical culture that prizes institutional respectability over reform.
