# Musophilus (Samuel Daniel)

Samuel Daniel's verse dialogue between a poet-lover (Musophilus) and a worldly skeptic (Philocosmus), published in 1599 and revised in 1601, supplies the epigraph to Chapter LXVIII of *[[middlemarch-town|Middlemarch]]* — the chapter in which [[nicholas-bulstrode|Nicholas Bulstrode]], trapped in his own house with the returned Raffles, discovers the limits of his capacity to control the narrative of his life.

## Work Overview

*Musophilus: Containing a General Defence of Learning* is Daniel's most ambitious philosophical poem, a debate in which Philocosmus — the lover of the world — argues that learning is futile vanity, while Musophilus — the lover of the Muse — defends the life of the mind and the enduring value of art. The poem belongs to the tradition of Renaissance Neoplatonism that runs from Castiglione through Spenser, but its melancholy, self-questioning tone sets it apart. Musophilus's defence of learning is never triumphalist; it is shadowed by the awareness that "this busy world" cares nothing for poetry, and that the poet's consolation — the "intelligence" he holds with all ages — may be indistinguishable from self-deception. For Eliot, whose own intellectual ambition was shadowed by a similarly chastened sense of its limits, Daniel's poem offered a mirror.

## Author & Context

Samuel Daniel (1562–1619), poet and historian, was a court servant under both Elizabeth and James I, admired by his contemporaries but largely forgotten by the nineteenth century. Eliot's recovery of Daniel — she quotes both *Musophilus* and *The Tragedy of Philotas* in *[[middlemarch-town|Middlemarch]]*'s epigraphs — is a mark of her extraordinary range as a reader. Daniel's stoicism, his willingness to acknowledge the fragility of the intellectual enterprise without abandoning it, places him in a line of thought that Eliot would have recognised from her own engagement with Spinoza and the German Higher Criticism: the conviction that truth is worth pursuing even — especially — when the pursuit yields no worldly reward.

## Appearance in the Novel

The epigraph to Chapter LXVIII reads:

> "And with all ages holds intelligence, / Go safer than Deceit without a guide!" —DANIEL: *Musophilus.* [068-013]

The couplet asserts that the person who communes with the wisdom of the past — who "holds intelligence" with all ages — walks more safely than the deceiver who relies on his own cunning. Eliot places these lines at the head of the chapter in which Bulstrode, the novel's supreme deceiver, discovers that his cunning has "overcast its mark." Raffles, drunk and unmanageable, threatens to expose the past that Bulstrode has spent decades burying. The epigraph's opposition between wisdom and deceit maps precisely onto the chapter's action: Bulstrode, the man without a guide beyond his own calculation, finds himself trapped by the very instrument — Raffles — that he once used to build his fortune.

## Significance

Eliot's choice of Daniel is brilliant in its thematic precision. Bulstrode is Philocosmus made flesh: a man so immersed in the world's calculations that he has lost all "intelligence" with any wisdom beyond his own interests. The chapter traces the crumbling of his elaborate self-justification — his half-belief that his wealth and piety redeem his past, his desperate prayers "pleading his motives for averting the worst evil." The epigraph's claim that the wise walk "safer than Deceit without a guide" is both true and ironic: true because Bulstrode's deceit has indeed led him into danger, and ironic because the "safety" of wisdom is precisely what Bulstrode has traded away. He is the man without a guide, and the chapter is the beginning of his long walk into exposure.
