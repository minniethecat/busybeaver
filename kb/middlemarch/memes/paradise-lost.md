# Paradise Lost

*Paradise Lost* is an epic poem in blank verse by the English poet John Milton (1608–1674), first published in 1667 in ten books and revised in 1674 into twelve books. The poem recounts the Biblical story of the Fall of Man — the temptation of Adam and Eve by Satan and their expulsion from the Garden of Eden. In *[[middlemarch-town|Middlemarch]]*, George Eliot deploys *Paradise Lost* as a recurring intellectual and moral touchstone, woven into chapter epigraphs, character psychology, and the novel's broader meditation on vocation and idealism.

## Work Overview

*Paradise Lost* is one of the foundational texts of English literature, treating cosmic themes — free will, obedience, knowledge, and the nature of evil — through a poetic idiom of extraordinary range and density. Its ambition to "justify the ways of God to men" (Book I, line 26) made it a touchstone for nineteenth-century intellectual life. For Eliot's generation of serious readers, Milton represented the highest synthesis of classical learning and Protestant devotion, and his poem was read not merely as literature but as moral philosophy.

## Author & Context

John Milton (1608–1674) was a poet, polemicist, and civil servant under Oliver Cromwell. Blind from around 1652, he composed *Paradise Lost* by dictation to amanuenses, famously including his daughters. This biographical detail enters *[[middlemarch-town|Middlemarch]]* directly: [[dorothea-brooke|Dorothea Brooke]], eager to be useful to her future husband, imagines herself reading aloud to [[edward-casaubon|Edward Casaubon]] "as Milton's daughters did to their father, without understanding what they read" [007-005]. Milton embodied for the Victorians — and for Eliot in particular — the figure of the scholar-hero, an association that Dorothea projects onto Casaubon and that the novel ultimately tests and complicates.

## Appearance in the Novel

*Paradise Lost* surfaces in *[[middlemarch-town|Middlemarch]]* at multiple levels. Chapter 3 opens with an epigraph drawn from Book VII, the passage in which the "affable archangel" Raphael instructs Adam and Eve in cosmic matters:

> "The affable archangel . . . / The story heard attentive, and was filled / With admiration, and deep muse, to hear / Of things so high and strange." [003-002–006]

The attribution — "—*Paradise Lost*, B. vii." [003-007] — immediately frames the chapter's action. In the narrative that follows, Mr. Casaubon is described as being "as instructive as Milton's 'affable archangel'" [003-009], linking the pedantic clergyman to Raphael's benevolent teaching while also exposing the gap between Casaubon's arid scholarship and the archangel's living wisdom.

Milton appears as a biographical model earlier in the courtship: Dorothea, contemplating marriage to Casaubon, imagines the union as akin to a life of intellectual service — "she felt sure that she would have accepted the judicious Hooker... or John Milton when his blindness had come on" [001-010]. The narrator later invokes Milton in a more ironic register, observing that even the greatest figures cannot escape unflattering reflection — "even Milton, looking for his portrait in a spoon, must submit to have the facial angle of a bumpkin" [010-003].

The novel's most psychologically acute Miltonic moment comes after Casaubon's cold response to Dorothea in [[rome|Rome]]: "Dorothea had thought that she could have been patient with John Milton, but she had never imagined him behaving in this way" [029-012]. The line crystallises the collapse of Dorothea's idealised vision of marriage-as-discipleship. Finally, in an offhand remark by [[mr-brooke|Mr. Brooke]], Milton is grouped with Hobbes and Swift as the type of man who would make "a good secretary" [034-049] — a deflating gesture that brings the epic poet down to the level of a county functionary.

## Significance

Eliot's deployment of *Paradise Lost* operates as a quiet structural irony. Dorothea enters marriage aspiring to be an Eve to Casaubon's Adam — eager to learn, to serve, to participate in a grand intellectual project. But where Adam and Eve's story in Milton is one of tragic disobedience followed by redemptive hope, the Casaubon marriage offers no such arc: Dorothea finds herself immured not in a garden but in a "virtual tomb" [048-008], her husband's scholarship a sterile labyrinth rather than a living epic. The Miltonic allusion thus measures the distance between Dorothea's spiritual ambition and the diminished realities of provincial intellectual life in the 1830s. At the same time, Eliot's own narrative method — sympathetic, philosophic, attentive to the "mixed conditions" of ordinary life — quietly repudiates the epic mode in favour of what the novel elsewhere calls the "unhistoric" acts of those who "lived faithfully a hidden life" (Finale).
