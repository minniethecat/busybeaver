# Mr. Hawley

The [[middlemarch-town|Middlemarch]] attorney whose legal acumen and moral severity make him a formidable antagonist in the exposure of [[nicholas-bulstrode|Nicholas Bulstrode]]'s past.

## Character Overview

Mr. Hawley is a lawyer of the old school: sharp, suspicious, and possessed of a conviction that gentlemen should be held to account for their actions. Unlike the more genial professional men of [[middlemarch-town|Middlemarch]] — Farebrother, Lydgate before his fall — Hawley is characterized by a prosecutorial temperament that makes him both respected and feared in the community. He is, in Eliot's rendering, the embodiment of provincial justice in its most unforgiving form.

Hawley's role in the narrative is that of an informal prosecutor. When the Raffles scandal breaks and Bulstrode's past comes to light, it is Hawley who most effectively pursues the implications, connecting the dots between Bulstrode's financial history, his marriage to the wealthy widow, and the suspicious circumstances of Raffles's death. His investigation is not official — Bulstrode is never formally charged — but it is socially decisive.

## Role in the Narrative

Hawley's investigation produces what he calls "a malignant effect on Lydgate's reputation" [071-022]. His questioning of Lydgate's role in Raffles's treatment, and his implication that Lydgate was complicit in Bulstrode's wrongdoing, demonstrates how guilt by association operates in a provincial community. Hawley's moral certainty, while directed at a genuinely guilty man, produces collateral damage to the innocent Lydgate that the novel treats as one of its central injustices.

Hawley represents a particular type of provincial authority: the self-appointed guardian of public morality whose judgments, while often factually correct, lack the mercy and discrimination that Eliot's narrator consistently advocates. His treatment of the Bulstrode affair illustrates the novel's ambivalence about the relationship between justice and compassion.
