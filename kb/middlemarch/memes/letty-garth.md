# Letty Garth

Letty Garth is the younger daughter of Caleb and Susan Garth in George Eliot's *Middlemarch* (1871–1872). She appears throughout the novel as a bright, spirited child whose scenes with her family provide a counterpoint to the novel's more troubled domestic narratives. Along with her brother Ben, she represents the next generation of the Garth household — energetic, opinionated, and shaped by their parents' exacting moral education.

## Character Overview

Letty is first introduced in the Garth kitchen, where she and Ben compete to tell the story of Cincinnatus under their mother's supervision. Her competitive spirit is evident: when Ben hesitates, Letty uses "her elbow contentiously"[^1] to claim the narrative. But Mrs. Garth corrects her firmly: "Letty, I am ashamed of you. When your brother began, you ought to have waited to see if he could not tell the story. How rude you look, pushing and frowning, as if you wanted to conquer with your elbows!"[^2] The scene establishes both Letty's lively personality and the Garth household's moral pedagogy — lessons delivered with Roman examples and maternal firmness.

She is the keeper of family lore. Mary Garth knows Rumpelstiltskin "well by heart, because Letty was never tired of communicating it to her ignorant elders from a favorite red volume."[^3] Letty's passion for retelling stories — from fairy tales to Roman history — marks her as a natural transmitter of narrative, the child who ensures that what matters is repeated until it sticks.

Her relationship with Fred Vincy is affectionate: when Fred arrives at the Garths' door, "he put his arm round Letty's neck silently, and led her into the kitchen without his usual jokes and caresses"[^4] — his unusual quietness signaling to the reader that something is wrong. She is the barometer of the household's emotional weather.

## Role in the Narrative

Letty functions primarily as part of the Garth family portrait, one of Eliot's most fully realized depictions of domestic virtue. Her scenes with her mother, father, and siblings ground the novel's moral universe: the Garth kitchen, with its washing-tub pedagogy and its red-bound storybooks, is where character is formed.

In chapter 40, Letty appears as an eager participant in the family's postal ritual, snatching up a red seal "like an eager terrier"[^5] and pressing her head close to Mary to understand why a particular envelope is "one of a dozen."[^6] Her curiosity is insatiable and her relationship with Mary is one of the novel's few depictions of healthy, uncomplicated sibling affection.

In the novel's closing chapters, Mary swings Letty "gloriously" in the garden,[^7] and Letty's laughter punctuates the scene in which Caleb Garth receives the news of his appointment as manager of the Stone Court estate — a moment of domestic joy that the novel has earned through its many darker passages.

## Relationships

- **[[caleb-garth]]** — her father, whose "tender gravity"[^8] she recognizes from earliest childhood
- **[[susan-garth]]** — her mother, whose moral instruction combines Roman history with practical domestic labor
- **[[mary-garth]]** — her elder sister, who tells her stories and swings her in the garden
- **Ben Garth** — her brother and rival in storytelling; they "were uncle and aunt before they were well in their teens"[^9]
- **[[fred-vincy]]** — an affectionate family friend who puts his arm around her neck in moments of trouble

## Key Appearances

- **Chapter 24** — The Cincinnatus storytelling competition; Fred's silent arrival at the Garth door
- **Chapter 40** — The evening mail ritual; Letty's eager questions about seals and letters
- **Chapter 63** — Referenced as the child for whom Mary memorizes Rumpelstiltskin
- **Chapter 86** — Swinging in the garden as her father receives life-changing news
