# Stone Court

The Featherstone family estate — a modest but symbolically charged property that becomes the gravitational center of one of *[[middlemarch-town|Middlemarch]]*'s most intricate plotlines. As the object of [[peter-featherstone|Peter Featherstone]]'s two contradictory wills, the site of [[joshua-rigg|Joshua Rigg]]'s brief and contentious ownership, and the place where [[john-raffles|John Raffles]] dies in [[nicholas-bulstrode|Bulstrode's]] compromised presence, Stone Court functions as the novel's most concentrated symbol of the way property relations entangle morality, mortality, and human desire.

## The Featherstone Era

[[peter-featherstone|Peter Featherstone]], the estate's most memorable owner, is a man whose power derives entirely from property. "Old Featherstone" has no children of his own but a vast network of expectant relatives, all of whom perform solicitude in hopes of inheritance. The estate under his tenure becomes a theater of avarice: "the brothers and sisters expected a division of the property, and each of them had a plan" [032-005].

Featherstone's manipulation of these expectations — dangling and withdrawing favor, hinting at bequests, summoning relatives for bedside audiences — is a study in the corrupting power of property. The estate is not merely land and buildings; it is the material form of a power relation that shapes the behavior of everyone within its orbit.

> "Old Featherstone had often been called a miser, but he was willing to spend money when it would give him importance." [032-014]

## The Inheritance Contest

The contest over Featherstone's property generates one of the novel's most elaborate plotlines. Two wills exist: an earlier one favoring the Featherstone relatives, and a later one — produced under the influence of [[mary-garth|Mary Garth's]] refusal to assist in its destruction — that leaves everything to [[joshua-rigg|Joshua Rigg]], Featherstone's illegitimate son.

The [[joshua-rigg-inheritance]] introduces into [[middlemarch-town|Middlemarch]] a disruptive figure: the stranger-heir who arrives from nowhere, sells the property to Bulstrode, and vanishes — leaving behind a chain of consequences that culminates in the [[raffles-scandal]]. [[mary-garth|Mary Garth]]'s moral choice — refusing to help old Featherstone destroy the later will, even when offered money — stands as one of the novel's crucial acts of ethical clarity in a plot otherwise driven by self-interest.

## Raffles's Death and Bulstrode's Downfall

Stone Court's darkest association is with the death of [[john-raffles|John Raffles]], the dissolute stepfather of [[joshua-rigg|Joshua Rigg]], who returns to [[middlemarch-town|Middlemarch]] with knowledge of Bulstrode's criminal past. Bulstrode, who has acquired the mortgage on Stone Court, takes charge of the dangerously ill Raffles in the house — and his ambiguous actions during Raffles's final illness constitute the novel's most explicit engagement with the question of moral responsibility in the gray zone between care and negligence.

The house becomes the scene of the novel's moral crisis: "The transaction was that of a man who was enabled to keep out of sight" [070-024], the narrator judges. When the town learns of Raffles's death and Bulstrode's past, the scandal destroys Bulstrode's position and nearly destroys Lydgate by association. Stone Court, the property that was meant to secure Bulstrode's respectability, becomes the instrument of his exposure.

## Symbolic Function

Stone Court's name is not accidental. It invokes the hardness of property, the weight of inheritance, the stone tablets of law. Against the organic, living community that the novel values — represented by [[caleb-garth|Caleb Garth's]] ethic of work and [[dorothea-brooke|Dorothea's]] ethic of sympathy — Stone Court stands for the dead hand of wealth, the way property outlasts and corrupts the lives that pass through it.

|-----------------|---------|
| The two wills | The arbitrariness of inheritance and the fragility of expectation |
| The stranger-heir | Property's power to disrupt social order |
| Raffles's death chamber | The fatal intersection of money and morality |
| Bulstrode's mortgage | Hidden claims, the impossibility of clean dealing |
| [[caleb-garth|Caleb Garth]] as steward | The alternative: work, not ownership, as ethical relation to land |
