# Conscience

The inner tribunal before which Eliot's characters stand — a faculty that can guide, torment, or be quietly reshaped into a more convenient form.

## Overview

Conscience in *[[middlemarch-town|Middlemarch]]* is never a simple, reliable guide. It is shaped by social position, by self-interest, and by the stories characters tell themselves about their own motives. Dorothea possesses "an active conscience and a great mental need" [003-020], yet her conscience leads her into a disastrous marriage — a devastating demonstration that moral sincerity is no guarantee of moral wisdom.

## Conscience and Self-Deception

Eliot's treatment of conscience is fundamentally psychological rather than theological. She is less interested in whether characters obey conscience than in how they negotiate with it. The conscience, in her hands, is not a fixed moral compass but a faculty that can be educated, corrupted, or systematically deceived.

Bulstrode is the novel's supreme case study in conscience management. His public piety and private compromises represent the process by which "conscience" is gradually brought into alignment with desire — a moral accounting trick that Eliot exposes with surgical precision. His past — a fortune built on ill-gotten gains — is not hidden from his conscience so much as reclassified by it, until the past returns in the person of Raffles to dismantle his self-exoneration.

What makes Bulstrode's case so powerful is its ordinariness. Eliot does not present him as a monster but as a man who has done what many people do — rationalised his own behaviour until the rationalisation becomes invisible to himself. The arrival of Raffles forces Bulstrode to confront not just his past actions but the entire edifice of self-justification he has constructed around them.

## Professional and Personal Conscience

Lydgate's conscience is professional: he knows what medicine requires of him, yet he consistently compromises — charging patients he meant to treat for free, voting with Bulstrode when he knows better. His is not a failure of moral knowledge but of moral courage, and the novel treats it with the severity of disappointed hope. Lydgate's tragedy is not that he doesn't know what is right but that he cannot summon the will to act on what he knows.

The novel's treatment of Lydgate's professional conscience connects to its larger critique of the gentlemanly ideal. Lydgate wants to be both a scientific reformer and a gentleman — to do good and to live well. His conscience is caught between these two ambitions, and the gentleman usually wins. The debts accumulate, the compromises multiply, and the conscience adapts — not by changing its judgments but by learning to live with them.

## Dorothea and the Education of Conscience

Dorothea's growth is the novel's most hopeful account of conscience: a movement from abstract moral ambition to particular, embodied acts of goodness — the visit to Rosamond, the decision to give up Casaubon's fortune. Conscience, in its mature form, is less a voice than a practice.

The crucial moment in Dorothea's moral education comes in Chapter 80, when she confronts her own jealousy of [[rosamond-vincy|Rosamond]] and chooses to act against it. "She felt the need to be disinterested, to override all personal feeling" [080-024] — and this time, unlike her earlier moral ambitions, the feeling leads to action. She visits Rosamond, speaks honestly, and in doing so performs an act of genuine moral courage. The conscience that had led her into the Casaubon marriage — sincere but blind — has been educated by experience into something wiser.
