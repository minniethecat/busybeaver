# Rome

The setting of Dorothea and Casaubon's wedding journey in Book II, where the ancient city's "stupendous fragmentariness" mirrors and intensifies the young bride's emotional dislocation.

## Overview

Rome in [[middlemarch-town|Middlemarch]] is not the city of the Grand Tour but a place of disorientation and spiritual crisis. George Eliot uses the setting to dramatize the collapse of Dorothea's romantic expectations: surrounded by "the ruins of a world more gigantic than her own," she experiences the first acute pangs of recognition that her marriage is a mistake. The city's temporal depth — its layering of pagan, Christian, and Renaissance histories — mirrors the layering of illusion and disillusionment that characterizes Dorothea's psychological journey.

## Significance in the Novel

Rome functions as the novel's primary symbolic landscape of disillusionment. The contrast between Dorothea's provincial expectations and the overwhelming scale of Rome — "the city of visible history, where the past of a whole hemisphere seems moving in funeral procession" [019-004] — produces what the narrator describes as a crisis of feeling. Casaubon's absorption in his [[vatican|Vatican]] manuscripts while his young wife wanders alone through the galleries transforms Rome from a honeymoon destination into a prison of the spirit.

The Rome chapters (Book II) are structurally crucial to the novel. They introduce [[will-ladislaw|Will Ladislaw]] into Dorothea's married life in a setting that makes the contrast between the two men unavoidable. Casaubon belongs to the dead world of dusty manuscripts; [[will-ladislaw|Will]], the artist's friend Naumann, and the living city itself represent vitality and creative energy. Dorothea's tears in the [[vatican|Vatican]] galleries mark the novel's first major turning point in her emotional education.

## Key Passages

> In those days the world in general was more ignorant of good and evil by forty years than it is at present. [019-004]

> To have in general but little feeling, seems to be the only security against feeling too much on any particular occasion. [019-005]
