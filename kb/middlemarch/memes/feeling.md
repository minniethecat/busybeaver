# Feeling

The great subject of the novel according to its own metaphoric logic — not thought or action but the life of feeling that underlies both, "quite apart from religious feeling" yet inseparable from moral judgment.

## Overview

Eliot is the great novelist of inner life, and *[[middlemarch-town|Middlemarch]]* is her supreme achievement in rendering the texture of consciousness. The novel's famous opening meditation — "quite apart from religious feeling; but in [[dorothea-brooke|Miss Brooke]]'s case, religion alone would have determined it" [001-004] — establishes feeling as a domain distinct from, though related to, both thought and belief.

## In the Novel

The novel's psychological acuity lies in its ability to render the gap between what characters feel and what they think they feel. Dorothea believes she marries Casaubon for intellectual reasons; the narrator shows us the feeling — the ardor, the idealization, the need for a great object — that actually drives her choice. Rosamond believes she loves Lydgate; the novel shows us that what she feels is ambition, aesthetic attraction, and the thrill of transgressing her mother's expectations.

Casaubon's tragedy is in part a tragedy of feeling: he experiences jealousy, insecurity, and desire, but his emotional vocabulary is so impoverished that he can only express these feelings as scholarly pedantry or passive-aggressive control. His proposal to Dorothea is a document of emotional illiteracy.

The novel's moral psychology insists that feeling and judgment cannot be separated. [[sympathy|Sympathy]] *is* a feeling; conscience operates through emotional channels; even the coldest calculation is driven by desires that are ultimately emotional. To understand a character in *[[middlemarch-town|Middlemarch]]* is to understand their feeling — and the novel's narrator is the most attentive feeler of all.
