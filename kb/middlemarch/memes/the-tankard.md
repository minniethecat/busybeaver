# The Tankard

The public house in Slaughter Lane whose landlady, Mrs. Dollop, presides over the town's alternative public opinion — rougher, less forgiving, and ultimately more consequential than the drawing-room version.

## Location and Character

The Tankard stands in Slaughter Lane, a working-class district of [[middlemarch-town|Middlemarch]]. Its landlady, "Mrs. Dollop, the landlady of the Tankard in Slaughter Lane" (045-002), is a formidable character in her own right — the keeper of a different kind of court from the one at [[tipton-grange|Tipton Grange]] or [[lowick-manor|Lowick Manor]]. The opinion formed at the Tankard "was unimportant only to those who had never been the subject of it" (045-004) — a reminder that reputation operates at every social level.

## Events and Associations

It is at the Tankard that the story of Lydgate's supposed complicity in Raffles's death takes hold. Mrs. Dollop's version of events — colored by class resentment and the love of a good story — spreads through the town with a speed no formal institution could match. The landlady had predicted "that he would recklessly expose himself" (045-055) — the lower orders, the novel suggests, have their own acute forms of moral judgment.

## Significance

The Tankard represents the novel's democratic insistence that opinion matters at every social level. What is said in a Slaughter Lane public house can destroy a gentleman's practice as effectively as anything said in a Tipton drawing-room.
