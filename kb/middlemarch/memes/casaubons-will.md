# Casaubon's Will

The testamentary codicil that reveals Casaubon's posthumous jealousy — and becomes the instrument by which he attempts, from beyond the grave, to control the woman he could never understand in life.

## Overview

[[edward-casaubon|Edward Casaubon]]'s will contains a single devastating provision: if Dorothea marries his young cousin [[will-ladislaw|Will Ladislaw]], she forfeits his entire estate. The codicil is discovered after Casaubon's death and functions as a narrative bomb — it transforms what seemed like the end of Dorothea's suffering into a new and crueler form of imprisonment. The will exposes Casaubon's jealousy — which his living self could never acknowledge — and makes it legally binding, a permanent claim on the woman he treated as property.

## Background

Casaubon's suspicion of [[will-ladislaw|Will Ladislaw]] builds throughout the early books of the novel. [[will-ladislaw|Will]]'s easy manner, his artistic interests, and his instinctive sympathy for Dorothea all threaten the elderly scholar, who hoards his wife's attention as jealously as he hoards his uncompleted research. The narrator notes Casaubon's "sense of injury" at any sign of Dorothea's independence — and his will is that sense of injury given legal force.

## Key Developments

The codicil is revealed after Casaubon's death, when the family gathers to hear the will read. Its effect is immediate: Dorothea's relationship with [[will-ladislaw|Will]], which had been developing naturally, is now shadowed by the threat of poverty and scandal. The will makes Dorothea's choice explicit: Casaubon's fortune, or [[will-ladislaw|Will Ladislaw]] — she cannot have both. The codicil provision reflects the novel's understanding that wills can be altered at whim — "I can make five codicils if I like" (012-058) — and that inheritance is an exercise of power, not justice.

## Consequences and Significance

Dorothea ultimately renounces the fortune and marries [[will-ladislaw|Will]], choosing love over property in what the novel presents as both a moral victory and a quiet tragedy. The will's real victim is not Dorothea but Casaubon himself: the document immortalizes his pettiness, his inability to trust, his reduction of marriage to a property relation. It is the final, damning evidence of his spiritual failure.
