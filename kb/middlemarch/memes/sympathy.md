# Sympathy

The imaginative capacity to enter into another's experience — for George Eliot, the foundation of all genuine morality, and a faculty that *[[middlemarch-town|Middlemarch]]* dramatizes in its triumphs, failures, and self-deceptions.

## Overview

Sympathy, for Eliot, is not mere pity or sentimental identification. It is an active, imaginative effort to understand another person's inner life — their motives, their suffering, their point of view. The novel's famous metaphor describes it as a transformative force: consciousness that "quivers thread-like in small currents of self-consciousness, and is spent, before it can be transformed into sympathy" [029-004].

This vision of sympathy as something that must be cultivated, that requires effort and imagination, is central to the novel's ethical project. Characters who fail at sympathy — Casaubon, Rosamond, Bulstrode — are the novel's moral failures. Characters who achieve it, even imperfectly — Dorothea in her growth, Farebrother, [[mary-garth|Mary Garth]] — represent the novel's highest moral ideal.

## In the Novel

Dorothea's arc is fundamentally an education in sympathy. She begins with an abstract, theoretical benevolence — the desire to reform cottages, to assist Casaubon's scholarship — that is really about her own self-image. Her growth consists in learning to feel with actual, particular others: Rosamond, Lydgate, even the memory of Casaubon after his death.

[[will-ladislaw|Will Ladislaw]], with his impetuous defense of what he calls "the fanaticism of sympathy" [022-058], articulates the novel's moral standard in its most ardent form. His challenge to Dorothea — that she should not sacrifice her own life to Casaubon's dead hand — is a claim made in the name of sympathetic understanding, not selfish desire.

But the novel is not naive about sympathy's limits. The narrator observes that even Dorothea's sympathy can be a kind of self-projection, a "fanatical sympathy" [030-034] that mistakes her own feelings for the other person's good. The finest moral moment in the novel is perhaps Dorothea's decision to help Lydgate and Rosamond, an act of sympathy that requires her to overcome her own jealousy and disappointment — and that is performed without the expectation of recognition or reward.

## Key Passages

> If we had a keen vision and feeling of all ordinary human life, it would be like hearing the grass grow and the squirrel's heart beat, and we should die of that roar which lies on the other side of silence. [020-019]

> She was given to the fanaticism of sympathy. [022-058]
