# The Canterbury Tales

*The Canterbury Tales* is a collection of stories in verse and prose by the English poet Geoffrey Chaucer (c. 1343–1400), composed in the last decades of the fourteenth century. Framed as a pilgrimage narrative in which a company of travellers recount tales to pass the journey from [[london-middlemarch|London]] to Canterbury, the work is celebrated for its panoramic portrait of medieval society — from knight to miller, prioress to pardoner — and for Chaucer's ironical, tender, and psychologically acute narrative voice. In *[[middlemarch-town|Middlemarch]]*, George Eliot invokes Chaucer three times in chapter epigraphs, drawing on his authority as a wise and humane observer of the relations between the sexes.

## Work Overview

*The Canterbury Tales* occupies a foundational place in English literature. Written in Middle English at a time when French and Latin dominated literary production, it established the vernacular as a vehicle for art of the highest ambition. The General Prologue's gallery of pilgrims — each described with a blend of idealisation and gentle satire — created a template for character-based narrative that would resonate through English fiction. For Eliot, whose own novel aspires to a Chaucerian breadth of social observation, the *Tales* offered a precedent for the serious treatment of ordinary life and a model of the narrator as sympathetic ironist.

## Author & Context

Geoffrey Chaucer (c. 1343–1400) was a courtier, diplomat, and civil servant as well as a poet. His career — spanning the reigns of Edward III, Richard II, and Henry IV — placed him at the centre of English political life, but his literary production drew on French, Italian, and Latin sources to create something distinctively English. By Eliot's time, Chaucer had been securely canonised as the "father of English poetry," though Victorian readers often encountered him in modernised or expurgated editions. Eliot's own deep reading in English literary history — she was, as Gordon Haight notes, "steeped in Chaucer from her girlhood" — made the *Tales* a natural source for the epigraphic apparatus of *[[middlemarch-town|Middlemarch]]*.

## Appearance in the Novel

Chaucer appears three times across the novel's chapter epigraphs. The first, prefacing Chapter XII, offers a terse attribution only — "—CHAUCER." [012-001] — appended to lines that meditate on the deceptions practised in courtship. The second, at Chapter XXI, again gives only the poet's name [021-001], introducing a chapter concerned with the shifting balance of power in the Casaubon marriage. The third and most specific invocation comes at Chapter LXV, where Eliot cites the *Tales* by name:

> "One of us two must bowen douteless, / And, sith a man is more reasonable / Than woman is, ye [men] moste be suffrable." [065-003]
>
> —CHAUCER: *Canterbury Tales*. [065-004]

The quoted lines appear in a chapter that traces the deteriorating relations between Lydgate and Rosamond, and Chaucer's gently ironic recommendation — that men, being allegedly more reasonable, should therefore be more patient with women — acquires a darker inflection in the context of a marriage sliding into mutual incomprehension.

## Significance

Eliot's use of Chaucer is strategic and cumulative. The early epigraphs, marked only "—CHAUCER.," establish the poet as a standing authority on human folly and courtship; the late attribution to the *Canterbury Tales* by name makes explicit what the earlier citations had implied — that the novel's domestic dramas belong to a literary tradition stretching back five centuries. The Chapter LXV epigraph is especially pointed: Chaucer's *Wife of Bath's Tale* and *Merchant's Tale* offer some of the most caustic treatments of marriage in English literature, and by invoking that tradition at a moment of marital crisis, Eliot reminds the reader that the Lydgates' unhappiness is not a private pathology but a variation on enduring human themes.
