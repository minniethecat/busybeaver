# Shakespeare's Works

The works of William Shakespeare are a significant source of allusion and epigraph in George Eliot's *Middlemarch* (1871–1872). Eliot draws on Shakespearean characters, themes, and language to enrich the novel's moral and psychological texture, connecting the provincial world of Middlemarch to the broadest currents of English literature.

## Presence in Middlemarch

Eliot uses Shakespearean allusions to elevate the provincial drama of Middlemarch to universal significance. The novel's characters are frequently measured against Shakespearean archetypes — [[edward-casaubon|Casaubon]] against Hamlet's scholarly paralysis, [[rosamond-vincy|Rosamond Vincy]] against the charming but destructive heroines of the comedies. These comparisons are rarely explicit but operate through structural parallels and verbal echoes.

The most sustained Shakespearean presence in the novel is the epigraph system. Eliot, who composed many chapter epigraphs herself, often adopts a Shakespearean or pseudo-Shakespearean register to frame the action. This practice reflects her deep familiarity with Shakespeare's works and her understanding of how literary allusion can create layered meaning—the epigraph proposes a universal theme, and the chapter that follows tests it against the particularities of provincial life.

## Epigraphs

Several of the novel's chapter epigraphs draw on or echo Shakespearean passages. Eliot, who wrote many of the epigraphs herself in imitation of various styles, occasionally adopts a Shakespearean register to frame the chapter's action within a wider literary tradition. The epigraph to Chapter 1, though written by Eliot herself, echoes the sonnet tradition that culminates in Shakespeare, establishing from the outset the novel's ambition to treat provincial life with the seriousness usually reserved for epic or tragic subjects.

## Specific Allusions

Key Shakespearean allusions in *Middlemarch* include the implicit comparison between Casaubon and Hamlet—both scholars paralyzed by the gap between intention and execution—and the structural parallel between Dorothea's story and the trajectory of Shakespearean heroines who must navigate between duty and desire. The novel also draws on the Shakespearean fool tradition in its treatment of characters like [[arthur-brooke|Mr. Brooke]], whose rambling speeches contain unwitting wisdom in the manner of a Polonius or a Touchstone.

## Function

The Shakespearean dimension of *Middlemarch* serves several purposes. It connects the provincial English world of the 1830s to the broadest currents of European literature. It also provides a measuring stick for the novel's moral vision — where Shakespeare's tragedies turn on heroic flaws, Eliot's realism finds tragedy in the slow accumulation of small choices and missed possibilities. Most importantly, Shakespeare serves as a bridge between the classical epic tradition and the modern novel. By invoking Shakespeare, Eliot claims for her "study of provincial life" the same cultural authority and moral seriousness that Shakespeare claimed for his histories and tragedies.

## Eliot's Shakespeare Criticism

Eliot's engagement with Shakespeare was not merely literary but critical. Her essays, particularly "The Natural History of German Life" and various reviews, articulate a theory of realism that owes much to Shakespeare's practice of mixing high and low, tragic and comic. She admired Shakespeare's ability to render ordinary life with the same intensity as extraordinary events—precisely the ambition she pursues in *Middlemarch*, where a provincial election, a doctor's career, and a marriage choice are treated with the gravity of tragic drama.
