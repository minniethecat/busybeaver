# Vocation

The life-defining work through which characters in *[[middlemarch-town|Middlemarch]]* seek to realize their capabilities — and the social, financial, and personal obstacles that stand between aspiration and achievement.

## Overview

Vocation in *[[middlemarch-town|Middlemarch]]* is never merely a job. It is a calling — to heal, to know, to improve, to create. Lydgate's medical ambition, Casaubon's scholarship, Dorothea's desire for meaningful contribution, [[caleb-garth|Caleb Garth]]'s devotion to land management, even [[fred-vincy|Fred Vincy]]'s reluctant turn toward honest work — each represents a version of the question that organizes the novel: what should a person *do* with their life?

## In the Novel

Lydgate is the novel's central case study in vocational ambition. He arrives in [[middlemarch-town|Middlemarch]] determined to reform medicine, to pursue research at the cellular level, to be a different kind of doctor. His failure is overdetermined: financial pressure, marital incompatibility, the resistance of provincial society, and his own blind spots all contribute. But the novel does not mock his ambition — it mourns its defeat.

Dorothea's vocational crisis is the novel's most intimate. She has no profession available to her — as a woman of her class, the only sanctioned vocation is marriage. Her entire story can be read as the search for an adequate object for her considerable energies: first Casaubon's scholarship, then estate improvement, then — after Casaubon's death and the threat of his will — the choice of a life with [[will-ladislaw|Will Ladislaw]] that requires her to give up fortune for love.

[[caleb-garth|Caleb Garth]] represents vocation in its most admirable, least grandiose form. He does good work because good work is worth doing. He is not ambitious in Lydgate's sense or idealistic in Dorothea's — he is simply competent and honest, and the novel treats this as a profound moral achievement.
