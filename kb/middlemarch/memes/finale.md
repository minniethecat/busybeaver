# Finale

The Finale to George Eliot's *Middlemarch* (1871–1872) is the novel's closing passage, a brief coda that traces the subsequent lives of its surviving characters and delivers the work's most famous moral statement. It is one of the most celebrated conclusions in English fiction.

## Overview

The Finale departs from the novel's usual third-person narrative mode to adopt the stance of a historian summarizing the known facts of several lives. Dorothea's fate is described in deliberately muted terms: "Her full nature, like that river of which Cyrus broke the strength, spent itself in channels which had no great name on the earth" (Finale). She marries Will Ladislaw, bears children, and lives a life of quiet domestic usefulness — a fate that the narrator presents not as tragedy but as a different, less visible form of significance.

The other characters receive summary treatment: Lydgate dies at fifty, having achieved only moderate professional success; Rosamond remarries a wealthy elderly physician; Mary and Fred Garth prosper modestly through work; the Farebrothers continue their quiet ministry; Bulstrode's disgrace leaves him a diminished figure, though his wife's loyalty never wavers.

## The Closing Passage

The Finale's most famous paragraph — and arguably the most famous passage in all of Eliot's work — redirects the reader's attention from the "heroic" achievements that the novel's characters failed to accomplish to the unremarked goodness that sustains the world:

> "But the effect of her being on those around her was incalculably diffusive: for the growing good of the world is partly dependent on unhistoric acts; and that things are not so ill with you and me as they might have been, is half owing to the number who lived faithfully a hidden life, and rest in unvisited tombs."

This is the novel's final word on the Saint Theresa problem established in the Prelude. The "many Theresas" who "found for themselves no epic life" are redeemed not by achievement but by influence — by the "incalculably diffusive" effect of their goodness on those around them. The moral vision is radically democratic: significance is not the monopoly of the famous, the powerful, or the visibly successful.

## Structure and Significance

The Finale mirrors the Prelude structurally, creating a frame around the novel's 800-plus pages. Where the Prelude poses the problem — how can an ardent nature find adequate expression in ordinary life? — the Finale offers an answer: through "unhistoric acts" of fidelity, kindness, and useful work that ripple outward in ways no one can measure.

The passage's style is notable for its compression. In a few hundred words, Eliot dispenses with characters she has spent 800 pages developing — not because they do not matter but because the novel's ultimate claim is that the most important effects of a life are precisely those that cannot be narrated, only gestured toward.
