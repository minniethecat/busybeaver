# Reform

The political and moral concept that provides *[[middlemarch-town|Middlemarch]]* with its historical horizon and its deepest metaphor. Set during the crisis of [[reform-bill-debates|the First Reform Bill]] (1831–1832), the novel uses the national struggle over parliamentary representation to frame its local dramas of ambition, resistance, and transformation. Every character's story is, in one register or another, a story about reform: of institutions, of marriages, of the self.

## The Reform Bill as Historical Context

The novel is precisely set in the months leading to the passage of the Reform Act of 1832, which expanded the electorate, abolished rotten boroughs, and redistributed parliamentary seats to reflect the growing industrial cities. "Things are changing," Mr. Brooke observes [051-015], and his uncharacteristic understatement captures the magnitude of the historical shift.

Eliot chose this moment deliberately. Writing in 1871–1872, she was herself living through the debates surrounding the Second Reform Bill (1867). The historical novel allowed her to examine reform's dynamics at one remove — to see the pattern of resistance, fear, opportunism, and genuine idealism that accompanies any attempt to remake institutions.

> "A man is seldom more innocently employed than when he is making money." [035-007] — the epigraph that ironizes the reform era's conflation of moral and economic progress.

## Reform as Moral Concept

The novel extends "reform" beyond its political application to encompass the moral transformation of individual lives. [[dorothea-brooke|Dorothea Brooke]]'s entire arc is a story of reform: the reform of her idealism into practical sympathy, the reform of her marriage through the choice of love over wealth. [[tertius-lydgate|Lydgate's]] ambition to reform medical practice in [[middlemarch-town|Middlemarch]] parallels Dorothea's moral ambition — and his failure parallels her partial success.

Eliot's treatment of reform is characteristically double: she values the impulse to improve institutions and lives, but she is skeptical of the heroic self-conception that often accompanies it. The novel's most admired characters — [[caleb-garth|Caleb Garth]], [[mary-garth|Mary Garth]], the late Dorothea — are reformers whose work is practical, local, and unheroic. "The growing good of the world is partly dependent on unhistoric acts" [Finale], the narrator concludes, and reform, in Eliot's vision, is less a matter of legislation than of the quiet, cumulative work of honest lives.

## Medical Reform

[[tertius-lydgate|Lydgate's]] medical reform ambitions represent the novel's most detailed case study of institutional change. He arrives in [[middlemarch-town|Middlemarch]] with advanced training ([[paris-middlemarch|Paris]], Edinburgh), a commitment to scientific medicine over traditional practice, and a vision of the physician as "a discoverer of new paths" [015-018].

His defeat is overdetermined: the town's [[medical-board|medical establishment]] resists innovation, his marriage to [[rosamond-vincy|Rosamond]] drains his resources and focus, and his association with [[nicholas-bulstrode|Bulstrode]] — the one man with the money and vision to fund reform — ultimately destroys his reputation. Lydgate's failure is the novel's argument that reform cannot succeed without both institutional support and personal integrity, and that the reformer who isolates himself from his community will be crushed by it.

## The Brooke Candidacy as Reform Farce

Mr. Brooke's [[brooke-candidacy|campaign for Parliament]] on a reform platform is the novel's comic treatment of the theme. His inability to complete a sentence, his retreat from every statement that provokes opposition, and his final humiliation in front of the crowd at the Green Dragon — "A man's pocket is his Bible" [051-028] — make reform's political face look ridiculous. But the farce has a serious underside: Brooke's candidacy shows how easily reform rhetoric can be detached from reform conviction, and how the machinery of politics can absorb and neutralize the impulse for change.

## Reform and the Marriage Plot

The novel's most audacious conceptual move is to treat marriage itself as an institution in need of reform. Dorothea's two marriages — first to [[edward-casaubon|Casaubon]] (a union of mistaken idealism) and then to [[will-ladislaw|Will Ladislaw]] (a union of mutual feeling) — trace an arc from institutional misery to personal fulfillment. The novel does not overthrow marriage as an institution, but it insists that marriage must be reformed from within: grounded in genuine knowledge of the other rather than projection, and sustained by sympathy rather than legal obligation.
