# Mr. Toller

Mr. Toller is one of the senior physicians of [[middlemarch-town|Middlemarch]], described as sharing "the highest practice in the town" and belonging "to an old Middlemarch family: there were Tollers in the law and everything else above the line of retail trade" [045-029]. Together with [[mr-wrench|Mr. Wrench]], he forms the core of the established medical faction that views [[tertius-lydgate|Tertius Lydgate]] with suspicion and hostility.

## Character

Toller is distinguished from the irascible Wrench by a more genial, pacific temperament. When disputes arise among the medical men, it is often Toller who intercedes: "'My dear fellow,' said Mr. Toller, striking in pacifically" [045-029]. This amiability, however, does not extend to Lydgate, whom he regards as an upstart threatening the professional and social order that has sustained the Toller family for generations.

The narrator's description of Toller carries a characteristic edge: "It may seem odd that with such pleasant habits he should have been given to the heroic treatment, bleeding and blistering and starving his patients" [045-029]. The irony is deliberate — Toller's personal charm coexists with a medical practice that Lydgate would consider dangerous quackery, and the town sees no contradiction between the two.

## Professional Standing

Toller's professional authority derives not from scientific knowledge but from social embeddedness. He is a Toller of Middlemarch — the name itself confers legitimacy. His practice, like Wrench's, is "laborious" in the sense of being busy rather than intellectually demanding. When [[mr-hackbutt|Mr. Hackbutt]] mentions at a dinner-party that a rival apothecary is undercutting prices, "Mr. Toller said, laughingly, 'Dibbitts will get rid of his stale drugs, then'" [045-029] — a remark that reveals both professional arrogance and a casual indifference to the quality of medical care.

## Medical Methods and the Heroic Treatment

Toller's adherence to the "heroic treatment" — bleeding, blistering, and purging — places him squarely in the pre-scientific medical tradition that Lydgate's training in Paris and Edinburgh has taught him to reject. The heroic approach, dominant in English medicine well into the early nineteenth century, was based on the humoral theory that disease resulted from imbalances best corrected by evacuation. "It was a principle with Mr. Toller that the remedies did the patient as much good as the disease did harm" [045-029] — a principle that Lydgate, with his commitment to evidence-based practice, would have found not merely wrong but morally culpable.

The irony of Toller's position is that his patients trust him precisely because he shares their assumptions about how medicine should work. A physician who *didn't* bleed and blister would have seemed negligent, even fraudulent, to the Middlemarch public. Lydgate's tragedy, in part, is that his scientific correctness counts for nothing against the social authority of men like Toller, whose methods are validated by tradition rather than by results.

## Role in Medical Politics

Toller and Wrench together represent the "old-guard" physicians whose opposition to Lydgate is at once professional and social. They agree "that Lydgate was a jackanapes, just made to serve Bulstrode's purposes" [018-041], their animus joining medical conservatism with anti-Evangelical sentiment (since [[nicholas-bulstrode|Bulstrode]] is Lydgate's patron). In their resistance, Eliot dramatizes the structural obstacles to reform: it is not enough to be right; one must also navigate the entrenched interests of those who benefit from things as they are.

Toller's resistance to Lydgate takes subtler forms than Wrench's open hostility. Where Wrench fumes and scoffs, Toller deploys genial condescension — a more effective weapon in a society where direct conflict is distasteful. His ability to smile while undermining a rival is the art of provincial power at its most refined.

## Social Position

The Toller family's reach extends beyond medicine into "the law and everything else above the line of retail trade" [045-029], making them exemplars of the interconnected professional elite that governs Middlemarch. This multi-generational embeddedness is precisely what Lydgate lacks and cannot acquire through talent alone. Toller's patients are loyal not because he is a better physician but because he is a *known* physician — his name, his family, and his long residence in the town constitute a form of social capital that no newcomer, however brilliant, can quickly match.

## Significance

Mr. Toller, like the other Middlemarch physicians, serves a dual function in the novel. As a character, he is a sharply observed provincial type — the genial, well-connected professional man whose competence is taken on trust by a community that cannot evaluate it. As a narrative element, he is part of the system of resistance that Lydgate must contend with, a reminder that every reformer faces not malign opposition so much as the dead weight of custom, comfort, and inherited position. His pleasant manners and lethal treatments are not a contradiction but a unity: the very qualities that make him socially acceptable also make him professionally dangerous, and neither the town nor Toller himself can see the difference.
