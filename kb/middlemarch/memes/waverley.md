# Waverley

*Waverley; or, 'Tis Sixty Years Since* is a historical novel by Sir Walter Scott (1771–1832), published anonymously in 1814. It is widely regarded as the first major work of historical fiction in English and launched the celebrated "Waverley Novels" series that dominated nineteenth-century literary culture. In *[[middlemarch-town|Middlemarch]]*, Scott's works — and *Waverley* specifically — function as a cultural touchstone, distinguishing characters by their literary tastes and marking the decline of Scott's reputation among the scientifically minded younger generation.

## Work Overview

*Waverley* tells the story of Edward Waverley, an English gentleman drawn into the Jacobite rising of 1745. The novel established the template for the historical romance: a fictional protagonist moving through real historical events, with detailed attention to manners, dialect, and social conflict. Scott's method — embedding private lives within public history — influenced the development of the nineteenth-century novel profoundly, and George Eliot's own fusion of provincial realism with historical consciousness owes a complex debt to the Waverley model, even as *[[middlemarch-town|Middlemarch]]* quietly critiques the romance conventions Scott perfected.

## Author & Context

Sir Walter Scott (1771–1832) was the most commercially successful novelist of the Romantic period and a foundational figure in the development of the European novel. By the time of *[[middlemarch-town|Middlemarch]]*'s setting (1829–1832), Scott was recently dead but his literary dominance was being reassessed. The novel registers this historical moment with precision: the older generation ([[trumbull|Mr. Trumbull]], the auctioneer who "sonorously" reads aloud from *Anne of Geierstein* [032-046]) still treats Scott with reverence, while the younger, scientifically oriented characters ([[tertius-lydgate|Tertius Lydgate]]) have moved on to other intellectual pursuits. Scott's centrality to provincial reading culture — his books sold at auctions, discussed at dinner parties, memorised by the young — makes him an ideal index of *[[middlemarch-town|Middlemarch]]*'s cultural sociology.

## Appearance in the Novel

Scott surfaces repeatedly as a marker of generational and temperamental difference. At a dinner party, the young Plymdale name-drops Scott to impress Lydgate:

> "'But Sir Walter Scott — I suppose Mr. Lydgate knows him,' said young Plymdale, a little cheered by this advantage." [027-029]

Lydgate's reply is dismissive: "Oh, I read no literature now... I used to know Scott's poems by heart" [027-030]. The exchange captures the tension between the literary culture of the early nineteenth century and the emerging scientific professionalism that Lydgate represents. For him, Scott belongs to boyhood; adulthood means medical journals and reform politics.

The novel's most extended Scott moment occurs during the sale of Mr. Featherstone's effects. The auctioneer, [[trumbull|Mr. Trumbull]], reads aloud from Scott's *Anne of Geierstein* — "pronounced *Jeersteen*" — as a performance of cultivated taste [032-046]. When [[mary-garth|Mary Garth]] is observed reading a book "by the author of 'Waverley': that is Sir Walter Scott," Mr. Featherstone remarks that he has "bought one of his works myself — a very nice thing, a very superior publication, entitled '[[ivanhoe|Ivanhoe]]'" [032-052]. The narrator's rendering of Featherstone's commercial vocabulary ("a very nice thing, a very superior publication") gently ironises the reduction of literary value to material acquisition.

Beyond these explicit mentions, Scott's influence shapes the novel's deeper structures. The narrator's comparison of Dorothea's experience to "the author of Waverley" — "Scott might have worked her into a fine romantic heroine" but something more complex is intended [039-056] — explicitly distinguishes Eliot's realist method from Scott's romance conventions. And the chapter 57 epigraph, "Toward Walter Scott who living far away / Sent them to cheer our solitary play," [057-010] acknowledges Scott as a shaping presence in the imaginative lives of Eliot's generation.

## Significance

Eliot's treatment of Scott in *[[middlemarch-town|Middlemarch]]* is characteristically double-edged. On one hand, Scott represents a literary culture that the novel treats with affectionate respect — the shared imaginative world of provincial readers for whom the Waverley Novels were a common currency. On the other hand, Scott's romance mode is precisely what *[[middlemarch-town|Middlemarch]]* defines itself against. The novel's commitment to unglamorous moral realism — its insistence that "the growing good of the world is partly dependent on unhistoric acts" — is a quiet repudiation of the heroic individualism that Scott's fiction celebrates. Yet Eliot's debt to Scott's project — the attempt to render historical change through the texture of ordinary life — is everywhere visible in the novel's weave.
