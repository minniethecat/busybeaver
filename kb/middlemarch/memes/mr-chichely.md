# Mr. Chichely

Mr. Chichely is the coroner of Middlemarch and a "coursing celebrity" in George Eliot's *Middlemarch* (1871–1872). A middle-aged bachelor with carefully tended vanity, he represents the type of the provincial sportsman-gentleman whose opinions on women, medicine, and professional qualifications are delivered with an assurance wholly unburdened by reflection.

## Character Overview

Eliot introduces Mr. Chichely at Mr. Brooke's dinner party at Tipton Grange (chapter 10) with one of her most economically satirical portraits: "a middle-aged bachelor and coursing celebrity, who had a complexion something like an Easter egg, a few hairs carefully arranged, and a carriage implying the consciousness of a distinguished appearance."[^1] The Easter-egg complexion — smooth, pinkish, faintly ridiculous — is typical of Eliot's method: a single visual detail that exposes a character's self-regard.

His views on women are delivered with the confidence of a connoisseur: "Ay, to be sure, there should be a little devil in a woman... And I like them blond, with a certain gait, and a swan neck."[^2] The narrator drily notes that "his study of the fair sex seemed to have been detrimental to his theology"[^2] — a gentle understatement that leaves the reader to infer how much actual study has occurred. Chichely is, pointedly, not going to marry: "he was not going to incur the certainty of being accepted by the woman he would choose."[^3] His ideal — revealed to be Rosamond Vincy — remains safely at a distance, preserved from the risk of actual courtship.

## Role in the Narrative

Chichely serves as a comic counterpoint to Lydgate's seriousness. At the Vincy dinner party (chapter 16), where he appears as "a great coursing comrade of Mr. Vincy's,"[^4] he takes exception to Lydgate's argument that coroners should have medical training. His defense of the status quo — "I should like to know how a coroner is to judge of evidence if he has not had a legal training?"[^5] — reveals both his professional self-interest and his intellectual limitations. Dr. Sprague gallantly defends him: "I hope it will be long before this part of the country loses the services of my friend Chichely."[^6]

Lydgate, who "had really lost sight of the fact that Mr. Chichely was his Majesty's coroner,"[^7] compounds the social offense by appearing to monopolize Rosamond Vincy in the drawing-room afterward — an injury Chichely feels acutely: "Fred Vincy had called Lydgate a prig, and now Mr. Chichely was inclined to call him prick-eared."[^8] The professional slight and the romantic rivalry merge in Chichely's resentment.

His final appearance, at the tumultuous public meeting in chapter 71, is fleeting but characteristic: when the word "chicanery" is spoken, Chichely is among the four men who start up in outrage — but "Mr. Hawley's outburst was instantaneous, and left the others behind in silence."[^9] Even in moral indignation, Chichely is a follower, not a leader.
