# Mr. Wrench

Mr. Wrench is one of the long-established medical practitioners of [[middlemarch-town|Middlemarch]], whose settled practice and traditional methods are challenged by the arrival of [[tertius-lydgate|Tertius Lydgate]]. As the medical attendant to the [[walter-vincy|Vincy family]], Wrench occupies a position of professional comfort that Lydgate's ambition directly threatens.

## Character

The narrator introduces Wrench with characteristic precision: "Mr. Wrench was a small, neat, bilious man, with a well-dressed wig: he had a laborious practice, an irascible temper, a lymphatic wife and seven children" [026-001]. This description — compact, clinical, faintly satirical — captures the man entirely: competent and industrious but uninspired, his professional identity inseparable from the social structures that sustain it.

Wrench's medical philosophy reflects the old school: empirical, traditional, and suspicious of innovation. He represents what Lydgate calls the "retailers" of medicine — practitioners who dispense treatments by rote rather than by scientific reasoning. His antagonism toward Lydgate is both professional rivalry and genuine bewilderment at a younger man who questions methods Wrench has spent a lifetime perfecting.

## The Fred Vincy Case

The most dramatic professional confrontation between Wrench and Lydgate occurs over the treatment of [[fred-vincy|Fred Vincy]]'s illness. Wrench, attending the Vincy family as their regular physician, diagnoses Fred's condition with the confidence of long familiarity. Lydgate, called in for a second opinion, contradicts Wrench's diagnosis and prescribes a different course of treatment — one that proves correct. This professional humiliation, occurring in front of the socially prominent Vincy family, crystallizes Wrench's antipathy toward the newcomer.

The episode is significant beyond its immediate medical stakes. It dramatizes the social dynamics of professional authority in a provincial town: Wrench's claim to competence rests on his established relationship with the family, while Lydgate's rests on his scientific training. The fact that Lydgate is correct does not, in the social logic of Middlemarch, necessarily make him the better physician — at least not in terms the town can readily assess. Wrench's wounded pride is both personal and systemic, a defense of a professional order that cannot survive empirical scrutiny.

## Professional Rivalry

From the outset, "Mr. Wrench, medical attendant to the Vincy family, very early had grounds for thinking lightly of Lydgate's professional discretion, and there was no report about Lydgate which was not retailed" [011-001]. This early antipathy deepens into open rivalry when Lydgate's treatment of [[fred-vincy|Fred Vincy]]'s illness contradicts Wrench's diagnosis, creating a professional schism that divides Middlemarch medical opinion.

At a gathering of medical men, Wrench stands "apart and having a friendly colloquy" with [[mr-toller|Mr. Toller]], the two established practitioners united in their agreement "that Lydgate was a jackanapes, just made to serve Bulstrode's purposes" [018-041]. The alliance of Wrench, Toller, and the other old-guard physicians forms a formidable obstacle to Lydgate's professional aspirations.

## Role in the Medical Community

Wrench belongs to the network of Middlemarch physicians — alongside [[mr-toller|Mr. Toller]], [[dr-sprague|Dr. Sprague]], [[mr-powderell|Mr. Powderell]], and [[mr-minchin|Mr. Minchin]] — who share the town's medical practice. This group functions as both a professional guild and a social clique, enforcing conformity through gossip and ostracism. Wrench's hostility to Lydgate is less personal than systemic: Lydgate's scientific ambitions threaten the economic and social basis of the established practitioners' livelihoods.

The economics of this rivalry are worth noting. Each physician's income depends on the loyalty of families like the Vincys — families who pay annually for medical attendance. A new physician of superior training represents not merely an intellectual challenge but a direct economic threat. Wrench's seven children and lymphatic wife are not incidental details; they are the domestic realities that make professional competition a matter of survival, not merely of pride.

## Wrench and the Reform Era

Wrench's conservatism is not limited to medicine. He belongs to the generation and class that regards all reform — political, social, professional — with suspicion. The [[reform|Reform Bill]] that animates Mr. Brooke and troubles the gentry likely strikes Wrench as another instance of unwelcome innovation, though Eliot does not give him explicit political opinions. His entire professional identity is a rejection of the new: new methods, new ideas, new men. In this, he is the medical counterpart to the political conservatives who resist the "growing spirit of reform" that the novel documents.

## Significance

Mr. Wrench embodies the inertia of professional conservatism that Lydgate must contend with — and ultimately fails to overcome. His very ordinariness makes him a more effective antagonist than any single villain could be. Through Wrench and his colleagues, Eliot dramatizes the social mechanisms by which innovation is resisted not through argument but through the accumulated weight of custom, convenience, and collective self-interest. In the novel's larger meditation on reform and resistance, Wrench is the medical face of the "retarding" forces that frustrate every would-be reformer in Middlemarch. His tragedy — if a character so minor can be said to have one — is that he cannot even perceive the inadequacy of his own methods, and so cannot begin to correct them.
