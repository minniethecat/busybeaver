# The Green Dragon

The inn that serves as [[middlemarch-town|Middlemarch]]'s unofficial male club — where elections are discussed, reputations are made and unmade, and the billiard-room becomes the setting for the novel's darkest moral trap.

## Location and Character

The Green Dragon is the town's principal inn, distinguished by "a billiard-room at the Green Dragon, which some anxious mothers and wives regarded as the chief temptation in [[middlemarch-town|Middlemarch]]" (018-008). Its billiard-room is "the constant resort of a certain set" (066-007) — the town's male population, from idle gentlemen to political operatives, gathers there to drink, play, and talk. It is, in the novel's geography, the masculine counterpart to the drawing-rooms where women conduct their own forms of social negotiation.

## Events and Associations

The Green Dragon is where Raffles — the man who knows Bulstrode's criminal past — stays when he comes to [[middlemarch-town|Middlemarch]], turning the inn from a place of leisure into a theater of exposure and moral crisis. It also serves as the campaign headquarters for [[mr-brooke|Mr. Brooke]]'s Parliamentary run, with "[[mr-brooke|Mr. Brooke]], conscious of having made a disreputable appearance" (051-028) after his disastrous speech from the White Hart balcony. The innkeeper "knew some anecdotes about the horses and riders" (023-019), making the Green Dragon a repository of the town's informal knowledge — the kind of knowledge that can undo a man's reputation.

## Significance

The Green Dragon represents the town's unofficial institutions — the places where power is exercised not through formal authority but through talk, influence, and the slow accumulation of damaging information. It is where [[middlemarch-town|Middlemarch]]'s public opinion is distilled and dispensed.
