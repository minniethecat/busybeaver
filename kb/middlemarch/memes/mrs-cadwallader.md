# Mrs. Cadwallader

The rector's wife whose acid tongue and pragmatic realism make her the novel's unofficial chorus — a woman who sees through every pretense and refuses, on principle, to be impressed.

## Character Overview

Mrs. Cadwallader is introduced as "the Rector's wife, and the small group of gentry with whom the Vincys were on visiting terms" [001-011] — a woman whose aristocratic connections (she is related to Lord someone) give her the freedom to say exactly what she thinks. Her speech is the novel's most reliable instrument of deflation: no character's pretensions survive her commentary. She is not malicious — her sharpness is the expression of a genuinely clearsighted morality — but she is absolutely without sentimentality.

She belongs to a venerable tradition in English fiction: the sharp-tongued woman who speaks truth to power precisely because her gender and social position exempt her from the ordinary rules of deference. Like Jane Austen's Mrs. Bennet (whom she resembles only in her determination to see her neighbors married off advantageously), Mrs. Cadwallader compensates for her lack of formal authority with an unstoppable fluency of opinion. Unlike Mrs. Bennet, however, her opinions are almost always correct.

## Role in the Narrative

Mrs. Cadwallader functions as a one-woman Greek chorus. She sees immediately that Dorothea's marriage to Casaubon is a disaster in the making; she understands that "Sir James's man knew from Mrs. Cadwallader's maid" [004-014] more about the town's affairs than its leading citizens admit to themselves. Her perspective is the novel's own: unsentimental, socially acute, and fundamentally generous beneath its surface harshness. She is, in her way, [[middlemarch-town|Middlemarch]]'s most reliable moral witness.

Her narrative function is to articulate what the reader is already suspecting but what decorum prevents the other characters from saying aloud. When she describes [[edward-casaubon|Mr. Casaubon]] as "a great bladder for dried peas to rattle in" [006-001], she is not being cruel; she is being accurate, and the accuracy is the reader's relief. In a novel where so much goes unsaid — where characters choke on their own reticence — Mrs. Cadwallader's willingness to speak is a kind of moral courage.

## Social Position and Class

Mrs. Cadwallader's sharp tongue is underwritten by her social position. As the rector's wife and a woman with aristocratic connections, she occupies a protected niche in Middlemarch society. She can say what others cannot because no one can afford to take offense — her rank insulates her from the consequences that would befall a less well-connected critic. This is a characteristically Eliot observation: even the freedom to speak truth is a privilege of class.

Yet Mrs. Cadwallader does not abuse this privilege. Her interventions, however brusquely delivered, are motivated by genuine concern for the people she advises. She steers [[james-chettam|Sir James Chettam]] toward [[celia-brooke|Celia Brooke]] not from a love of matchmaking (though she enjoys that) but from a clear-eyed assessment of who will make him happy. Her pragmatism is the expression of a moral realism that the novel endorses: knowing what people are actually like is more useful than wishing them to be better.
