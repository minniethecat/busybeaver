This page indexes every political event, concept, and person connected to the reform movement that Eliot uses to frame her novel's deeper concern with the possibility and limits of human change.

Eliot's treatment of reform is characteristically ambivalent. Mr. Brooke's muddled reformism is satirized; the town's resistance to Lydgate's medical innovations is anatomized with clinical precision; and the novel's deepest interest is not in legislation but in the quieter, more difficult work of moral reform — the slow, unglamorous process by which individuals learn to see beyond their own desires and act for the common good.
