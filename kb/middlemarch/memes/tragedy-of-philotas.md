# The Tragedy of Philotas (Samuel Daniel)

Samuel Daniel's verse tragedy, first published in 1605 and performed (it is thought) before King James, supplies the epigraph to Chapter XXXVI of *[[middlemarch-town|Middlemarch]]* — the chapter in which [[fred-vincy|Fred Vincy]]'s expectations of an inheritance from [[peter-featherstone|Peter Featherstone]] are abruptly shattered, and the gap between what was silently hoped for and what is actually delivered becomes the chapter's bitter subject.

## Work Overview

*The Tragedy of Philotas* is a Senecan closet drama in five acts, recounting the fall of Philotas, son of Parmenion, a general in Alexander the Great's army accused of conspiring against the king. Daniel's play belongs to the tradition of neo-Stoic political tragedy that flourished in the late Elizabethan and early Jacobean period, alongside works by Fulke Greville and the Countess of Pembroke. Its central preoccupation is the fatal gap between inner thought and outer expression — Philotas's tragedy turns on his failure to communicate his true intentions, and the court's readiness to construe silence as treason. By Eliot's time, Daniel was a relatively obscure figure, known more as the author of the sonnet sequence *Delia* and the philosophical poem *Musophilus* than as a dramatist; her quotation from *Philotas* signals the depth and eclecticism of her reading.

## Author & Context

Samuel Daniel (1562–1619) was a poet, historian, and court servant whose career spanned the transition from Elizabeth to James. He was admired by his contemporaries — including Shakespeare, who drew on Daniel's *Civil Wars* for the history plays — but fell out of fashion in the centuries that followed. Eliot's recovery of Daniel as an epigraph source is characteristic of her intellectual method: she read widely in the byways of English literature, finding in neglected works a moral precision that more canonical texts sometimes lacked. Daniel's stoicism, his chastened sense of the limits of human knowledge, and his preoccupation with the gap between public reputation and private truth all resonated with Eliot's own philosophical commitments.

## Appearance in the Novel

The epigraph to Chapter XXXVI reads:

> "Which they suppose they cannot, 'less they give / Notice of their extreme and highest thoughts." —DANIEL: *Tragedy of Philotas.* [036-013]

The lines describe the predicament of those who expect great things — wealth, status, love — without having declared those expectations openly. The tragedy of Philotas is a tragedy of unarticulated hope, and Eliot applies this structure to [[fred-vincy|Fred Vincy]]'s comic but genuinely painful disappointment. Fred had counted on inheriting from Featherstone, building an entire future on the unspoken assumption that the old man's favour meant a legacy. The reading of the will disabuses him, and the chapter opens on Mr. Vincy's redirected anger: "He was an open-minded man, but given to indirect modes of expressing himself — when he was disappointed in a market for his silk braids, he swore at the groom."

## Significance

Eliot's epigraph choice diagnoses the particular moral failing at the heart of Fred's disappointment. The problem is not merely that Fred expected something he did not receive; it is that he never gave "notice" of those expectations — to himself, to Mary, or to the world. He lived in a haze of assumed entitlement, and the chapter's action forces him, for the first time, to confront the gap between what he silently hoped for and what life will actually deliver. The epigraph from Daniel thus frames Fred's comedy of disappointment within a genuinely tragic structure: like Philotas, Fred has been undone by the things he left unspoken. The difference — and it is the difference that separates *[[middlemarch-town|Middlemarch]]*'s providential comedy from Senecan tragedy — is that Fred will survive his fall and learn from it.
