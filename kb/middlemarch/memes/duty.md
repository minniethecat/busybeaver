# Duty

The language of obligation that pervades *[[middlemarch-town|Middlemarch]]* — a vocabulary characters use to ennoble their choices, excuse their failures, and navigate the demands of conscience against the pull of desire.

## Overview

"Duty" is the most frequently invoked moral category in *[[middlemarch-town|Middlemarch]]*. It appears in courtship, in professional life, in religious observance, and in the management of property. For Eliot, duty is never a simple matter of fixed rules; it is always mediated by character, circumstance, and self-deception. The novel's ethical intelligence lies in its ability to show how the same word can express genuine moral seriousness, unthinking conformity, or self-serving rationalization.

Dorothea, contemplating marriage to Casaubon, resolves that "it would be my duty to study that I might help him" [003-021] — a formulation that transforms romantic infatuation into a program of self-abnegation. The irony is that her "duty" to Casaubon becomes a prison from which only his death releases her.

## In the Novel

Casaubon himself is a master of duty's rhetoric. In proposing to Dorothea, he presents the matter as obligation: "It was his duty to do so" [004-062], treating marriage as a solemn administrative act rather than an expression of love. His scholarly work — the perpetually unfinished *Key to All Mythologies* — is likewise framed as duty, though the novel quietly suggests it is more a monument to vanity than to scholarship.

Professional duty organizes the medical and clerical storylines. Lydgate's ambition to reform medicine is driven by "unusual views of social duty" [015-021], a progressive vision that the provincial community finds suspicious rather than admirable. The clergyman Farebrother, by contrast, practices a modest, humane version of duty — doing the parish work, playing whist for money without hypocrisy — that the novel treats as far more admirable than Bulstrode's ostentatious piety.

[[caleb-garth|Caleb Garth]] represents duty in its most admirable form: "perseverance in a landlord's duty, to which he had at first been urged by a letter from his predecessor" [008-041]. His is a duty grounded in workmanship and trust, not in abstract principle. The novel's moral vision consistently favors this kind of embedded, practical obligation over the grand gestures of self-denial that Dorothea initially embraces.

## Key Passages

> "It would be my duty to study that I might help him," said Dorothea, who was given to the most poetical of all forms of self-deception — the belief that her own desire was the expression of a universal duty. [003-021]

> He had put the risks of marriage before her in a striking manner. It was his duty to do so. [004-062]
