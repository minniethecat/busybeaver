# Adolf Naumann

A German painter living in Rome, Adolf Naumann is a friend and artistic companion of [[will-ladislaw|Will Ladislaw]]
and one of the first to recognize [[dorothea-brooke|Dorothea Brooke]]'s distinctive beauty as a subject for painting.
He belongs to the Nazarene school of painting and provides an important aesthetic perspective within the novel's
exploration of art, beauty, and perception.

## Character Overview

Adolf Naumann is a painter of the Nazarene school working in Rome, where he shares a studio with Will Ladislaw.
He is described as a "painter of grand historical and religious subjects" and is characterized by his keen
artistic eye, particularly for feminine beauty. Eliot presents him as a contrast to Will—a professional artist
wholly dedicated to his craft, who views the world through the lens of pictorial composition (019-019).

Naumann embodies the Continental artistic sensibility that the novel's English characters encounter in Rome.
His studio, filled with "studies for saints and prophets," represents a world of aesthetic commitment that
stands apart from the provincial concerns of Middlemarch. Unlike the English characters who are largely defined
by their social and economic roles, Naumann is defined entirely by his artistic vocation. This gives him a
freedom of perspective that allows him to see Dorothea in a way the English characters cannot—as pure form
and aesthetic possibility, unencumbered by social convention (022-016).

## Role in the Narrative

Naumann appears primarily in the Rome chapters (19–22), where he encounters Dorothea Brooke at the Vatican.
He is immediately struck by her "fine curves" as a subject for painting and urges Will to help persuade her
to sit for a portrait, declaring that "English ladies are not at everybody's beck" (019-024). Naumann
functions as a catalyst who sharpens Will's own awareness of Dorothea, both as an aesthetic object and a
person. When Will brings Dorothea and [[casaubon|Edward Casaubon]] to Naumann's studio, Naumann's professional
detachment and his artist's commentary on the scene provide ironic counterpoint to the emotional currents
among the English visitors (022-017).

Naumann's most significant narrative contribution is his role as an external observer who articulates what
Will himself cannot yet admit. By treating Dorothea as a subject for artistic representation, Naumann
authorizes a way of looking at her that transcends the narrow social categories available in Middlemarch.
His painterly vocabulary—"antique form," "perfect young genius," "fine curves"—introduces a language of
aesthetic appreciation that Will will later translate into emotional attachment. Naumann thus serves as a
necessary intermediary between Will's artistic sensibility and his romantic feelings (022-020).

## Quotations

Naumann is a creature of direct speech, and his painter's eye produces some of the novel's most vivid
dialogue. His studio scenes, in particular, stage a collision between Continental aestheticism and
English provincial sensibility.

> "Confound you, Naumann! I don't know what I mean myself. That is a way you have: you ask for a definition and then you call the thing by a worse name than I do." (019-016)

This early exchange between Will and Naumann establishes the playful, combative dynamic of their
friendship. Will's exasperation — "that is a way you have" — suggests a long familiarity with Naumann's
habit of pressing definitions where none can be given. The passage also hints at the German painter's
intellectual rigor: he wants precise language for things, a trait that marks him as a genuine artist
rather than a mere dilettante.

> "—so far as it is straining through me, Adolf Naumann: that stands firm," said the good-natured painter, putting a hand on Ladislaw's shoulder, and not in the least disturbed by the unaccountable touch of ill-humor in his tone. "See now! my existence presupposes a universe, you know. And you had better not contradict that." (019-019)

Naumann's cheerful metaphysical confidence — "my existence presupposes a universe" — serves as a foil
to Will's romantic restlessness. Where Will is uncertain, Naumann is secure; where Will drifts between
vocations, Naumann has one. His "good-natured" response to Will's ill-humor marks him as emotionally
generous, a quality that allows him to serve as both friend and artistic mentor.

> They found Naumann painting industriously, but no model was present; his pictures were advantageously arranged, and his own plain vivacious person set off by a dove-colored blouse and a maroon velvet cap, so that everything was as fortunate as if he had expected the beautiful young English lady exactly at that time. (022-017)

This is Naumann's theatrical entrance — the studio perfectly composed, the artist himself a picture
within the picture. The phrase "his own plain vivacious person" captures the paradox of the man:
physically unremarkable but endlessly alive. Eliot's suggestion that "everything was as fortunate as
if he had expected" Dorothea hints at the aesthetic providence that seems to follow Naumann: he is
the kind of artist for whom life arranges itself into compositions.

> "Now if I could lay this by for half an hour and take it up again — come and look, Ladislaw — I think it is perfect so far." Presently Naumann said, after turning the sketch this way and that, "Decidedly I am destined to be the glory of my century." (022-030)

Naumann's self-mocking grandiosity — "destined to be the glory of my century" — is characteristic.
He is confident enough to be playful about his own ambition. This lightness distinguishes him from
the novel's English characters, whose ambitions are almost always leaden with self-importance
(Lydgate, Casaubon) or masked by false modesty.

## The Nazarene Context

Naumann's affiliation with the Nazarene school is significant. The Nazarenes were a group of early
nineteenth-century German painters who sought to revive the spiritual sincerity of medieval and early
Renaissance art. Based in Rome, they emphasized religious subjects and a deliberately archaic style. Eliot's
choice to make Naumann a Nazarene painter aligns him with the novel's broader interest in the relationship
between artistic form and spiritual content—a concern that also animates Dorothea's own religious and
aesthetic aspirations (019-019).

## Relationships

- **[[will-ladislaw|Will Ladislaw]]** — friend and studio companion in Rome; Naumann teases Will about his
  feelings for Dorothea and provides a foil for Will's amateur artistic sensibilities. Their friendship
  represents a formative period in Will's aesthetic education, though Naumann's professional dedication
  to art contrasts with Will's more diffuse and ultimately political ambitions (022-020).

- **[[dorothea-brooke|Dorothea Brooke]]** — Naumann sees her as a perfect painting subject and a figure of
  "antique beauty," though he quickly recognizes the social impossibility of an English lady sitting for
  a German painter. His aesthetic appreciation of Dorothea anticipates the way Will will come to see her—as
  something rare and valuable that the provincial world cannot properly value (019-025).

- **[[edward-casaubon|Edward Casaubon]]** — Naumann's encounter with Casaubon in the studio produces one of
  the novel's most barbed moments of ironic observation. Naumann, with an artist's eye for physical
  decrepitude, sees Casaubon as "the perfect model of a dying scholar" and notes the tragic mismatch
  between the young Dorothea and her aging husband. His detached, painterly assessment cuts through the
  polite fictions that surround the Casaubon marriage (022-017).

## Thematic Significance

Naumann represents the aesthetic dimension of the novel's inquiry into vision and perception. Throughout
*Middlemarch*, characters are judged by how they see others—with sympathy, with egoism, with imagination,
or with narrowness. Naumann's painterly vision is both a gift and a limitation: he sees form beautifully
but may miss the moral and emotional substance within. In this, he prefigures the novel's broader argument
that no single way of seeing—aesthetic, scientific, religious, or social—is sufficient to grasp the
complexity of human life.
