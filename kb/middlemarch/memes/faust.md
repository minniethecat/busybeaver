# Faust (Goethe)

Johann Wolfgang von Goethe's two-part dramatic poem, completed in 1832, quoted in the epigraph to Chapter LXXXI of *[[middlemarch-town|Middlemarch]]* — the chapter in which [[dorothea-brooke|Dorothea Brooke]] makes her second attempt to visit Rosamond Lydgate and the novel's central moral crisis begins to resolve.

## Work Overview

*Faust* is Goethe's magnum opus, a monumental dramatic poem in two parts (1808, 1832) that reimagines the medieval legend of the scholar who sells his soul to the devil. Part I follows Faust's pact with Mephistopheles and his seduction of the innocent Gretchen; Part II, from which Eliot draws her epigraph, moves into a vast symbolic panorama of myth, politics, and the nature of human striving. By the time George Eliot was writing *[[middlemarch-town|Middlemarch]]* in the early 1870s, Goethe was a towering figure in European letters, and his works were central to the intellectual milieu Eliot and George Henry Lewes inhabited. Lewes had published his landmark *Life of Goethe* in 1855, which Eliot had assisted with extensively during her years as an editor and translator.

## Author & Context

Johann Wolfgang von Goethe (1749–1832) was the dominant figure of German Romanticism and Weimar Classicism — poet, dramatist, novelist, scientist, and statesman. His influence on George Eliot was profound and personal. She translated Das Leben Jesu by David Friedrich Strauss (a work deeply indebted to German idealist thought), and her partnership with Lewes — whose Goethe biography was the standard English-language study for decades — placed Goethe at the centre of her intellectual life. The concept of *Entsagung* (renunciation), so central to *[[middlemarch-town|Middlemarch]]*'s moral vision, has deep roots in Goethe's late work.

## Appearance in the Novel

The epigraph to Chapter LXXXI reads:

> "Du Erde warst auch diese Nacht beständig, / Und athmest neu erquickt zu meinen Füssen, / Beginnest schon mit Lust mich zu umgeben, / Du regst und rührst ein kräftiges Beschliessen / *Zum höchsten Dasein immerfort zu streben*." —*Faust:* 2r Theil. [081-006]

Translated: "Thou Earth, wert also constant through this night, / And breathest newly quickened at my feet, / Beginnest now with joy to compass me, / Thou rousest and stirrest a strong resolve / To strive ever onward toward the highest being."

The chapter opens with Dorothea walking towards [[middlemarch-town|Middlemarch]] to make her second attempt at seeing Rosamond. The epigraph's theme — constancy through darkness, and the renewal of purposeful striving — mirrors Dorothea's moral state. She has passed through a night of anguished reflection after witnessing what she believed to be [[will-ladislaw|Will Ladislaw]]'s intimate conversation with Rosamond. Yet she resolves to act not from jealousy but from compassion, setting aside her own pain to help another woman. The Faustian striving here is transfigured from ambition into moral action, from seeking knowledge and power into seeking human connection.

## Significance

Eliot's deployment of the *Faust* epigraph is characteristically layered. In Goethe's poem, Faust speaks these lines at dawn after a night of restless wandering; the Earth's constancy renews his will to strive. Dorothea, likewise, emerges from her night of "beseeching" to act with purpose. But Eliot's irony, as always, is gentle rather than caustic: where Faust's striving is cosmic and mythological, Dorothea's is domestic and human — a visit to a troubled marriage. The epigraph thus enacts the novel's great theme: that the "highest being" is not reached through heroic transcendence but through the moral courage to act rightly in the small, unhistoric sphere of daily life. This is the same Goethe who appears in the Finale's famous meditation on "unhistoric acts" — the striving that "lies for the most part in the consciousness" of the actor alone.
