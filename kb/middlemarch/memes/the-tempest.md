# The Tempest (Shakespeare)

[[will-ladislaw|Will]]iam Shakespeare's late romance, first performed around 1611 and published in the First Folio of 1623, supplies the epigraph to Chapter XXXII of *[[middlemarch-town|Middlemarch]]* — the chapter in which the Featherstone relatives descend upon [[stone-court|Stone Court]], circling [[peter-featherstone|Peter Featherstone]]'s deathbed with hungry expectation.

## Work Overview

*The Tempest* is one of Shakespeare's final solo-authored plays, a work of extraordinary compression that unites themes of justice, forgiveness, power, and illusion on a remote island ruled by the exiled Duke Prospero. Shipwrecked by Prospero's magic, a party of Italian nobles — including the brother who usurped his dukedom — wanders the island, each character subjected to a form of moral testing. The play's metatheatrical dimension, in which Prospero's magic figures the playwright's own art, was fully appreciated by the Romantic era and became central to nineteenth-century criticism. Eliot's own intellectual circle, especially through Coleridge's influential lectures on Shakespeare, would have known *The Tempest* as a meditation on the nature of authority and the limits of human knowledge — themes deeply resonant with *[[middlemarch-town|Middlemarch]]*'s own preoccupations.

## Author & Context

[[will-ladislaw|Will]]iam Shakespeare (1564–1616) appears more frequently in *[[middlemarch-town|Middlemarch]]*'s epigraphs than any other author. Eliot's deployment of his works is never merely ornamental: each quotation is chosen for its diagnostic precision, isolating the moral kernel of the chapter it introduces. *The Tempest*, in particular, offered Eliot a language for thinking about how human beings are manipulated by their own desires — a theme that runs through the novel's treatment of inheritance, ambition, and self-deception.

## Appearance in the Novel

The epigraph to Chapter XXXII reads:

> "They'll take suggestion as a cat laps milk." —*Tempest.* [032-002]

The line, spoken in the play by Antonio and Sebastian as they mock the credulity of the shipwrecked courtiers who swallow Prospero's illusion, deploys the image of a cat lapping milk as a figure for the greed with which people absorb any flattering hint. Eliot transposes this image from the enchanted island to [[stone-court|Stone Court]], where the dying [[peter-featherstone|Peter Featherstone]]'s relatives gather in expectation of inheritance. Brother Solomon, Sister Jane, Brother Jonah — each "takes suggestion" from the old man's presence as eagerly as a cat at a saucer, reading providential design into the mere fact of being on the premises. The epigraph's sardonic edge frames the ensuing comedy of self-interested hope: "Probabilities are as various as the faces to be seen at will in fretwork or paper-hangings — every form is there, from Jupiter to Judy, if you only look with creative inclination."

## Significance

Eliot's choice of this particular line from *The Tempest* is pointed. In Shakespeare's play, the gullibility of the courtiers is a function of Prospero's magic — they are not merely foolish but systematically deceived. In *[[middlemarch-town|Middlemarch]]*, the Featherstone relatives are deceived not by magic but by their own desires, which arrange probability into whatever shape flatters them. The epigraph thus introduces the chapter's central insight: that self-interest is its own enchanter, conjuring prospects of inheritance from the mere fact of proximity to a dying man. This is the same ironic intelligence that Eliot brings to all the novel's inheritance plots, from [[fred-vincy|Fred Vincy]]'s expectations to Bulstrode's clandestine past — the recognition that "suggestion" is the most intoxicating of all intoxicants.
