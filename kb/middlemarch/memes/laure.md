# Laure

**Laure** is a French actress living in Paris who becomes the object of [[tertius-lydgate|Tertius Lydgate]]'s intense romantic infatuation during his student years. Her story — culminating in her murder of her husband on stage — serves as one of the novel's most striking episodes and leaves a lasting mark on Lydgate's psyche.

## Lydgate's Infatuation

During his medical studies in Paris, the young Lydgate became "completely fascinated" by Laure, attending the theatre nightly to see her perform [015-022]. She played the part of a woman who murders her lover, and Lydgate was captivated by the "melodious fragments" of her voice and her "long southern eyes" [015-023].

Lydgate's attraction was intense but unrequited:

> Laure looked at him in silence with a strange smile on her lips, and then said, quietly, "You are a most dangerous young man." [015-026]

Despite her warning, Lydgate persisted. He believed himself deeply in love and imagined a future with her, viewing her as a "great actress" worthy of his devotion.

## The Murder

The turning point came when Lydgate discovered that Laure had killed her husband — who was also her acting partner — by deliberately stabbing him with a real dagger during a performance, making it appear as an accident [015-029]. The investigation ended in Laure's release, but the revelation shattered Lydgate's romantic illusions.

This episode marked Lydgate deeply. It taught him that "scientific" observation could be clouded by emotion, and that beautiful surfaces could conceal dark realities.

## Lasting Impact

The memory of Laure stayed with Lydgate long after he returned to England:

> Since he had had the memory of Laure, Lydgate had lost all taste for large-scale romance. [016-031]

He believed the experience had immunised him against romantic folly [016-060]. Yet the narrator suggests otherwise — during his courtship of [[rosamond-vincy|Rosamond Vincy]], Lydgate's "old drama" with Laure resurfaces in his mind, hinting at patterns of idealisation he has not fully escaped [036-036]. When he later looks at Rosamond during their marital difficulties, "his mind glanc[es] back to Laure," drawing an implicit parallel between the two women [058-061].
