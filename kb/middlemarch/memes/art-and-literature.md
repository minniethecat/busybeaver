# Art and Literature

George Eliot's Middlemarch is one of the most densely allusive novels in the English language. Its epigraphs, its characters' reading, its narrator's frame of reference — all draw on a vast cultural inheritance from classical antiquity through the Renaissance to the nineteenth century. This page indexes every literary work, author, artist, and aesthetic concept documented in the wiki, mapping the intellectual world that Eliot's characters inhabit and the narrator deploys.

The allusions are never merely decorative. When Dorothea is compared to Saint Theresa, when Casaubon labors on his [[key-to-all-mythologies|Key to All Mythologies]], when Lydgate dreams of being a second Vesalius — each reference is a lens that clarifies character while also placing it within a tradition. Eliot's narrator moves easily among Homer, Dante, Shakespeare, Pascal, and Wordsworth, creating a polyphonic texture in which the provincial dramas of Middlemarch resonate with the deepest themes of Western literature.
