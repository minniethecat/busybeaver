# Mrs. Farebrother

Mrs. Farebrother is the white-haired mother of the Reverend Camden Farebrother, Vicar of St. Botolph's in Middlemarch. A widow past seventy, she lives with her son, her sister Miss Noble, and her elder daughter Miss Winifred Farebrother in the old stone parsonage, where she exerts a commanding domestic presence (PN=017-004).

## Character Overview

Mrs. Farebrother is described as "befrilled and kerchiefed with dainty cleanliness, upright, quick-eyed, and still under seventy" when Lydgate first meets her (PN=017-004). Eliot presents her as a woman of sharp intelligence and stronger opinions, accustomed to directing both conversation and conduct within her household. She prides herself on having raised her children to wear flannel and not over-eat, which she considers the chief reason people need doctors (PN=017-005).

## Role in the Narrative

Mrs. Farebrother appears primarily in scenes set at the Farebrother parsonage, where she serves as the dominant conversational voice — the narrator notes that the Vicar "seemed a trifle milder and more silent, the chief talker being his mother, while he only put in a good-humored moderating remark here and there" (PN=017-004). She takes an active interest in her son's affairs, discussing the chaplaincy controversy and expressing her views on nearly every subject that arises.

She is particularly vocal about her son's merits, telling Lydgate that Camden undervalues himself: "I tell him he is undervaluing the God who made him, and made him a most excellent preacher" (PN=017-022). She speaks of others in the parish with characteristic frankness, and Mary Garth observes that "Mrs. Farebrother talked of every one else in the parish" (PN=054-044).

## Relationships

Mrs. Farebrother is the central figure of the Farebrother household. Her son **[[camden-farebrother]]**, the Vicar, treats her with filial devotion and gentle tolerance. Her sister **[[miss-noble]]** lives with them as a dependent — a tiny, meek old lady who contrasts sharply with Mrs. Farebrother's commanding presence. Her daughter Winifred Farebrother attends to her needs with quiet efficiency.

She also interacts with **[[tertius-lydgate]]**, who visits the parsonage and becomes a friend of her son. Mrs. Farebrother welcomes him with "lively formality and precision" and promptly informs him that her household rarely needs medical aid (PN=017-005).
