# Class

Social class is a central thematic concern in George Eliot's *Middlemarch* (1871–1872), structuring the novel's social world and shaping the destinies of its characters. The novel anatomizes the gradations of early-1830s English provincial society with ethnographic precision.

## Class Structure in Middlemarch

The town of Middlemarch encompasses a spectrum of social positions: the landed gentry represented by [[james-chettam|Sir James Chettam]] and the Brooke family; the manufacturing and commercial class embodied by the [[walter-vincy|Vincy family]]; the professional class of clergy, doctors, and lawyers such as [[tertius-lydgate|Lydgate]] and [[camden-farebrother|Farebrother]]; and the laboring and servant class.

Eliot's rendering of class is notable for its granularity. She does not present "the gentry," "the middle class," and "the poor" as homogeneous blocs but as internally stratified groups whose members are acutely conscious of fine distinctions. The Vincys, for example, are manufacturing wealth — "old manufacturers could not any more than dukes be connected with none but equals" (023-007) — but they are nonetheless beneath the landed gentry. Within the professional class, a clergyman like Farebrother occupies a different social tier than a physician like Lydgate, and Lydgate's expectation that his professional expertise will confer social standing is one of his most painful miscalculations.

## Class and Marriage

The novel explores the tensions created by cross-class marriages. [[edward-casaubon|Casaubon]]'s marriage to [[dorothea-brooke|Dorothea Brooke]] crosses lines of age and temperament rather than class, but [[tertius-lydgate|Lydgate]]'s marriage to [[rosamond-vincy|Rosamond Vincy]] is explicitly a cross-class union — the professional gentleman marrying the manufacturer's daughter — and the novel traces the financial and emotional consequences of their different class expectations.

Rosamond's class aspirations are perhaps the novel's most sustained study of social ambition and its costs. Raised at Mrs. Lemon's finishing school to speak and dress like a lady, she has absorbed the outward forms of gentility without the inner substance — a distinction that Eliot, with her characteristic moral precision, treats as damning. Rosamond expects a gentleman's income without understanding the work that produces it; she mistakes refinement for virtue and luxury for happiness. Her tragedy is not that she aims too high but that she aims at the wrong things.

The marriage market in Middlemarch is governed by class logic that characters ignore at their peril. Fred Vincy's expectations of inheriting from [[peter-featherstone|Peter Featherstone]] represent an attempt to short-circuit the class system — to vault from commercial precarity to landed security without working for it. The novel's punishment of this ambition is swift and decisive, forcing Fred into the Garth family's ethos of productive labor as the only legitimate route to social standing.

## Class and Profession

The rise of the professional class is one of the novel's most significant social themes. Lydgate represents the new ideal of the medical professional — a gentleman by education rather than birth, committed to scientific reform. His ambition to "do good small work for Middlemarch" (PN=015-016) is thwarted not by lack of skill but by his failure to understand the class dynamics of the town: he alienates established practitioners like [[dr-sprague|Dr. Sprague]] and [[mr-minchin|Mr. Minchin]], and his refusal to dispense drugs — a point of professional principle — is interpreted by his neighbors as class arrogance.

The clergy, too, map onto class positions. [[camden-farebrother|Mr. Farebrother]]'s modest living and his devotion to natural history mark him as professionally under-rewarded — a man whose talents exceed his station. By contrast, [[edward-casaubon|Mr. Casaubon]]'s independent means and grand scholarly project align him with the gentry even as his intellectual isolation renders his work sterile.

The legal profession appears through figures like [[mr-standish|Mr. Standish]], the family lawyer whose "old professional tone" (PN=035-007) reflects the conservative function of law in preserving the existing class order. The Garth family business offers an alternative model: [[caleb-garth|Caleb Garth]] is not a professional gentleman but a man of practical skill whose integrity transcends class categories entirely. His devotion to "business" — a word he uses, the narrator notes, in a sense "peculiarly his own" — represents an alternative hierarchy in which honest work, not social rank, determines worth.

## Class Mobility

*Middlemarch* is set on the eve of the 1832 Reform Bill, a period of social flux. Characters like [[nicholas-bulstrode|Bulstrode]] represent the self-made man whose ascent provokes class anxiety in the established order. [[fred-vincy|Fred Vincy]]'s eventual adoption into the [[garth-family-business|Garth family]]'s ethos of work represents a movement away from commercial-class pretension toward the dignity of productive labor. And [[mary-garth|Mary Garth]], whose plainness and integrity contrast with Rosamond Vincy's polished beauty, embodies a different kind of class virtue — one rooted in character rather than appearance.

The novel is notably pessimistic about upward mobility through individual effort, and notably optimistic about the moral benefits of accepting one's station. The characters who rise — Bulstrode, Lydgate in his professional ambition, Rosamond in her social climbing — are punished for their presumption, while those who remain within their sphere — Caleb Garth, Mary Garth, Farebrother — are rewarded with the novel's deepest respect. This is not conservatism in a political sense so much as a moral conviction that the desire to transcend one's circumstances is, in most cases, a form of egoism.

## Eliot's Perspective

Eliot's treatment of class is characteristically nuanced. She is neither a revolutionary nor an apologist for the existing order. Her narrator observes class prejudice with ironic detachment while acknowledging the real constraints it imposes on individual lives. The novel's famous final paragraph, with its tribute to "the growing good of the world" wrought by "unhistoric acts," is itself a class statement — a redirection of moral attention from the grand stage of history to the ordinary lives that most novels of the period ignored.

The narrator's famous remark that "we are all of us born in moral stupidity" (PN=021-001) functions as a class critique as well as a moral one: our inability to imagine the inner lives of those outside our immediate circle is, Eliot implies, a failure of class imagination as much as individual sympathy. The novel's remedy for this stupidity is not political revolution but imaginative effort — the sustained, difficult work of seeing others as they really are, regardless of their station.
