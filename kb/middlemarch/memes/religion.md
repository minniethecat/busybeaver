# Religion

The systems of belief, worship, and moral commitment that structure the inner lives and social identities of [[middlemarch-town|Middlemarch]]'s characters, ranging from genuine devotion to calculated performance.

## Overview

Religion in [[middlemarch-town|Middlemarch]] is not a single force but a spectrum of commitments and postures. At one end stands [[dorothea-brooke|Dorothea Brooke]], whose "spiritual religion" is a "submergence of self in communion with Divine perfection" [003-011]; at the other stands [[nicholas-bulstrode|Nicholas Bulstrode]], whose evangelicalism serves as an instrument of social power. Between these poles, the novel maps a rich territory of Anglican clergymen, Dissenting ministers, skeptical professionals, and ordinary believers whose faith is woven into the fabric of daily life without fanaticism or hypocrisy.

George Eliot, herself a religious skeptic who translated Strauss and Feuerbach, brings both sympathy and critical intelligence to her treatment of religion. She values the moral seriousness that genuine religious commitment can foster, but she is unsparing in her exposure of religion used as a mask for ambition, greed, or self-righteousness.

## In the Novel

Dorothea's religious sensibility is the novel's most sympathetic portrait of faith. Her "religion alone would have determined it" [001-004] — the "it" being her choice of plain dress and ascetic habits that puzzle her more worldly sister Celia. Dorothea's piety is genuine but also naive; her attraction to Casaubon is partly a religious attraction to what she imagines as a life of shared spiritual and intellectual devotion. Her disillusionment is, in part, the discovery that Casaubon's scholarship lacks the religious and moral dimension she had projected onto it.

The variety of clerical characters — Casaubon (the arid scholar-clergyman), Farebrother (the humane, worldly vicar), Tyke (the evangelical preacher), and Cadwallader (the comfortably established rector) — creates a taxonomy of religious types. Each represents a different way in which religion can be lived: as intellectual project, as pastoral care, as doctrinal fervor, or as social position.

Bulstrode represents the dangerous extreme: "all men needed the bridle of religion, which, properly speaking, was the dread of a Hereafter" [002-020]. His instrumental view of religion as a mechanism of social control is the novel's sharpest critique of faith divorced from genuine sympathy. When his past is exposed and he attempts to "want God Almighty to come in and stop" the consequences [012-056], the hollowness of his religion is revealed.

## Key Passages

> In Miss Brooke's case, religion alone would have determined it. [001-004]

> That spiritual religion, that submergence of self in communion with Divine perfection. [003-011]

> All men needed the bridle of religion, which, properly speaking, was the dread of a Hereafter. [002-020]

> He wants God Almighty to come in and stop him. And that's what his religion means. [012-056]
