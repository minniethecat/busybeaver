# Twelfth Night (Shakespeare)

[[will-ladislaw|Will]]iam Shakespeare's comedy of mistaken identity and festive misrule, written around 1601, provides the epigraph to Chapter XLI of *[[middlemarch-town|Middlemarch]]* — the chapter in which [[john-raffles|John Raffles]], the swaggering embodiment of past secrets, arrives at [[stone-court|Stone Court]].

## Work Overview

*Twelfth Night* opens with a shipwreck that separates twins Viola and Sebastian on the coast of Illyria. Viola, disguised as the page Cesario, enters the service of Duke Orsino and becomes an unwilling go-between in his suit to the mourning Countess Olivia — who promptly falls in love with Cesario/Viola. The comedy proceeds through a whirl of mistaken identity, drunken revelry (presided over by Sir Toby Belch), and the gulling of the puritanical steward Malvolio. The song from which Eliot draws her epigraph is sung by Feste, the fool — the one character licensed to speak truth to power without punishment. His refrain, "For the rain it raineth every day," is a memento mori disguised as nonsense, a reminder that fortune and weather are indifferent to human pretension.

## Author & Context

Shakespeare's comedies were widely read and performed in the Victorian period. George Eliot's engagement with Shakespeare was both critical and creative: her novels quote, allude to, and rework Shakespearean themes with a density unmatched by any other Victorian novelist. *Twelfth Night* held particular appeal for its exploration of gender performance and the instability of identity — themes that resonate with Eliot's own complex relationship to female authorship and the conventions of the marriage plot.

## Appearance in the Novel

The epigraph to Chapter XLI reads:

> "By swaggering could I never thrive, / For the rain it raineth every day." —*Twelfth Night*. [041-003]

The chapter introduces [[john-raffles|John Raffles]], the man whose knowledge of Bulstrode's past will eventually destroy the banker's reputation and peace of mind. Raffles is introduced in terms that echo the epigraph precisely: he is a "swaggerer" with "the air of a swaggerer, who would aim at being noticeable even at a show of fireworks." He stands in vivid contrast to the "sleek and cool" [[joshua-rigg|Joshua Rigg]] Featherstone, and his blustering self-confidence — his conviction that his own remarks are "more interesting than the performance itself" — marks him as a descendant of Shakespeare's braggarts.

The epigraph's second line — "For the rain it raineth every day" — sounds a darker note. Feste's song is, at bottom, about the indifference of the world to human designs. Raffles swaggers, but the "rain" of circumstance — his own alcoholism, his loose tongue, his fatal return to [[stone-court|Stone Court]] — will undo him. The chapter is set at the moment when the past begins its inexorable return, and the epigraph's comic fatalism is perfectly judged: what enters as Shakespearean foolery will exit as tragedy.

## Significance

Eliot's deployment of *Twelfth Night* in Chapter XLI is a small masterpiece of tonal control. The epigraph announces comedy — the world of swaggerers and jesters — but the chapter, and the narrative sequence it inaugurates, will unfold into some of the novel's darkest territory. Raffles will die; Bulstrode will be exposed; the web of provincial respectability will tear. By placing a fool's song at the head of this sequence, Eliot reminds us that the distinction between comedy and tragedy is not a property of events but of the distance from which we view them. The "rain that raineth every day" is, in *[[middlemarch-town|Middlemarch]]*, the steady pressure of consequences — moral law operating not through the heavens but through the ordinary mechanisms of memory, gossip, and the return of the repressed.
