# Tamburlaine the Great

*Tamburlaine the Great* is a two-part play by the English dramatist Christopher Marlowe (1564–1593), first performed around 1587. It dramatises the rise of the Central Asian conqueror Timur (Tamburlaine) from Scythian shepherd to emperor, celebrated in Marlowe's "mighty line" of blank verse for its audacious ambition and rhetorical splendour. In *[[middlemarch-town|Middlemarch]]*, the play appears not as epigraph but as the subject of a painting by [[will-ladislaw|Will Ladislaw]], becoming the occasion for an exchange about the meaning of symbolic art that illuminates the intellectual temperaments of three major characters.

## Work Overview

*Tamburlaine* revolutionised English drama. Its protagonist — an overreacher who defies gods and emperors, who harnesses conquered kings to his chariot — inaugurated a tradition of Marlovian heroes whose limitless ambition is at once magnificent and monstrous. The play's blank verse, muscular and declamatory, broke decisively with the rhyming couplets of earlier English drama and established the verse-form that Shakespeare, Jonson, and their successors would bring to its highest refinement. For Victorian readers, Marlowe represented the raw energy of the Elizabethan stage before Shakespearean polish — a figure of volcanic creativity and moral unruliness.

## Author & Context

Christopher Marlowe (1564–1593) was a playwright, poet, and translator whose brief, meteoric career — he was killed in a Deptford tavern at twenty-nine — produced some of the most dynamic verse in English. His intellectual interests ranged from classical history to Machiavellian statecraft, and his plays (*Tamburlaine*, *Doctor Faustus*, *The Jew of Malta*, *Edward II*) explore the extremes of human will with an intensity unmatched in English drama. Eliot, whose own intellectual formation included deep study of Elizabethan literature, would have recognised in Marlowe's overreaching heroes a type that *[[middlemarch-town|Middlemarch]]* subjects to realist scrutiny: the ambition that exceeds its object, the will that cannot accommodate the ordinariness of the world.

## Appearance in the Novel

*Tamburlaine* enters *[[middlemarch-town|Middlemarch]]* during Dorothea and Casaubon's honeymoon in [[rome|Rome]], when [[will-ladislaw|Will Ladislaw]] escorts them to the studio of his friend [[naumann|Adolf Naumann]]. [[will-ladislaw|Will]] describes a sketch he has been working on:

> "I have been making a sketch of Marlowe's Tamburlaine Driving the Conquered Kings in his Chariot. I am not so ecclesiastical as Naumann, and I sometimes twit him with his excess of meaning. But this time I mean to outdo him in breadth of intention. I take Tamburlaine in his chariot for the tremendous course of the world's physical history lashing on the harnessed dynasties." [022-012]

Dorothea responds with characteristic directness: "The sketch must be very grand, if it conveys so much. I should need some explanation even of the meaning you give. Do you intend Tamburlaine to represent earthquakes and volcanoes?" [022-013] [[will-ladislaw|Will]], laughing, affirms that his Tamburlaine stands for "migrations of races and clearings of forests — and America and the steam-engine. Everything you can imagine!" [022-013] The exchange draws a dry response from Casaubon, who receives "this offhand treatment of symbolism very uneasily" [022-012], and Dorothea's comment — "What a difficult kind of shorthand! It would require all your knowledge to be able to read it" [022-013] — is directed smilingly towards her husband, marking the tacit alliance between [[will-ladislaw|Will]]'s mode of thinking and Dorothea's.

## Significance

The Tamburlaine episode is one of *[[middlemarch-town|Middlemarch]]*'s most economically revealing scenes. [[will-ladislaw|Will]]'s choice of subject — the conqueror who harnesses kings to his chariot — is an act of self-dramatisation: the young man without a settled profession, dependent on the Casaubons' patronage, pictures himself as the master of historical forces. His "mythical interpretation" of Tamburlaine as a figure for geological and social change is at once intellectually playful and evasive — a refusal to commit to any single meaning that Casaubon, the scholar of dogged literalness, can only find irresponsible. Dorothea, characteristically, seeks to understand rather than to judge, and her "What a difficult kind of shorthand!" is both a genuine effort at comprehension and an unconscious revelation of her own hunger for a mode of knowledge more alive than her husband's desiccated researches. In a novel structured around the collision between ambition and circumstance, Marlowe's overreaching hero offers a compressed image of the question at the novel's heart: what happens when the will to conquer meets a world too stubborn to be subdued?
