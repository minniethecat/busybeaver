Lydgate's story is the novel's most sustained examination of professional ambition. His commitment to evidence-based medicine — treating patients with careful observation rather than received formulas — brings him into conflict with the established practitioners of Middlemarch. The New Hospital becomes the arena where science, politics, and personal weakness collide, and Lydgate's fate reflects Eliot's larger concern with the difficulty of living an uncompromised life.
