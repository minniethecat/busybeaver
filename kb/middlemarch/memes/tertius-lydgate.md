# Tertius Lydgate

The novel's second protagonist — a talented, ambitious physician whose determination to reform medicine founders on the rocks of provincial society, financial imprudence, and a disastrous marriage.

## Character Overview

Lydgate arrives in [[middlemarch-town|Middlemarch]] as "this new young surgeon, Mr. Lydgate" [010-042], bringing advanced medical training from [[paris-middlemarch|Paris]] and Edinburgh, an interest in cellular pathology, and a determination to practice medicine as a science rather than a trade. He is "one of the Lydgates of Northumberland" [010-044] — good family, good prospects, and the temperament of a reformer.

## Role in the Narrative

Lydgate's story is the novel's great tragedy of wasted potential. He chooses [[middlemarch-town|Middlemarch]] as the site for his medical career, believing a provincial town offers the right conditions for research and reform. Instead, he is ensnared by the very provincialism he hoped to transcend. His marriage to [[rosamond-vincy|Rosamond Vincy]] — pursued as a pleasing interlude, consummated in haste — becomes a slow catastrophe of mutual incomprehension. His financial difficulties force him into dependence on Bulstrode, the town's sanctimonious banker, and when Bulstrode's past is exposed and Raffles dies under questionable circumstances, Lydgate is implicated by association.

By the novel's end, Lydgate has abandoned research for a lucrative [[london-middlemarch|London]] practice, dying at fifty of diphtheria — a man who "had gained an excellent practice, alternating, according to the season, between [[london-middlemarch|London]] and a Continental watering-place," but who considered himself a failure.
