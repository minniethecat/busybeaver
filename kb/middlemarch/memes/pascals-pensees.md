# Pascal's Pensées

The *Pensées* ("Thoughts") is a collection of philosophical and theological fragments by the French mathematician and Jansenist thinker Blaise Pascal (1623–1662), published posthumously in 1670. The work was intended as an apologetic for Christianity aimed at the sceptical reader, arguing that faith is not contrary to reason but encompasses truths that reason alone cannot reach. In *[[middlemarch-town|Middlemarch]]*, Pascal's *Pensées* is [[dorothea-brooke|Dorothea Brooke]]'s intellectual companion — a text she turns to in youth, in courtship, and in the disappointments of marriage.

## Work Overview

The *Pensées* is among the most distinctive works in European thought: a collection of aphorisms, arguments, and psychological observations that Pascal assembled in preparation for a never-completed defence of Christianity. Its most famous passage — the "wager" argument — proposes that belief in God is rationally justified because the potential gain (eternal bliss) infinitely outweighs the finite cost of belief. But the *Pensées* is equally celebrated for its penetrating analyses of human nature: *ennui*, vanity, diversion, and the paradox of a creature "midway between nothing and everything." For Eliot's generation of intellectually serious readers, Pascal represented not dogmatic orthodoxy but the union of scientific rigour (he was a pioneering mathematician) with spiritual intensity — precisely the combination [[dorothea-brooke|Dorothea Brooke]] longs for.

## Author & Context

Blaise Pascal (1623–1662) was a mathematician, physicist, and religious thinker. Before the age of twenty he had made fundamental contributions to projective geometry; by thirty he had helped establish probability theory and conducted landmark experiments on atmospheric pressure. A profound religious experience in 1654 — recorded in his *Mémorial*, sewn into the lining of his coat — turned his energies towards theology. He associated with the Jansenist movement at Port-Royal, a Catholic reform community that emphasised original sin, divine grace, and the insufficiency of unaided reason — doctrines that placed Jansenists in tension with both the Jesuits and the French crown. Pascal's combination of mathematical intellect and passionate faith made him an emblematic figure for the nineteenth-century crisis of belief: a man who had looked unflinchingly at the case for unbelief and found it wanting.

## Appearance in the Novel

Pascal is introduced in the novel's opening chapter as one of the coordinates of Dorothea's inner life. She is described as a young woman of intense religious sensibility whose reading has shaped her character:

> "Dorothea knew many passages of Pascal's Pensées and of Jeremy Taylor by heart; and to her the destinies of mankind, seen by the light of Christianity, made the solicitudes of feminine fashion appear an occupation for Bedlam." [001-004]

This is more than a bibliographical aside. The *Pensées*, with its austere vision of human grandeur and misery, its insistence that the most urgent question is the state of one's soul, provides the intellectual idiom for Dorothea's youthful ardour. The passage establishes her as a reader for whom books are not decoration but existential equipment.

Pascal returns during the courtship with Casaubon, in a passage of devastating dramatic irony. Dorothea, walking through the woods and imagining her future as Casaubon's wife, thinks:

> "It would be like marrying Pascal. I should learn to see the truth by the same light as great men have seen it by. And then I should know what to do, when I got older: I should see how it was possible to lead a grand life here — now — in England." [003-021]

The comparison is doubly revealing. It shows the scale of Dorothea's aspiration — to be the partner of a great intellect, not merely a domestic companion. But it also shows the scale of her misapprehension. Casaubon is no Pascal: his scholarship is desiccated, his vision narrow, his life's work a "Key to All Mythologies" that will prove as lifeless as the myths it purports to explain. Dorothea's invocation of Pascal measures the height from which she will fall.

In the novel's later movement, Pascal reappears as a remembered consolation. During a Sunday afternoon of marital loneliness, Dorothea sits with "a little heap" of books — "her old companion Pascal, and Keble's 'Christian Year'" [048-008] — but finds she can read none of them. The books that once nourished her now offer no sustenance; the texts have not changed, but the reader has. This quiet moment encapsulates one of the novel's deepest themes: the loss of a certain kind of intellectual innocence that comes with adult experience.

## Significance

Pascal functions in *[[middlemarch-town|Middlemarch]]* as a barometer of Dorothea's spiritual trajectory. In Book I, the *Pensées* is a living text — its fragments memorised, its vision internalised, its author imagined as the ideal intellectual companion. By Book V, Pascal has become an "old companion" — still present but drained of comfort, a reminder of a former self rather than a guide for the present. This arc is not simply a narrative of disillusionment. Eliot does not mock Dorothea's Pascal-reading — the novel takes her intellectual seriousness seriously. Rather, the Pascalian thread traces the maturation of a soul that must learn to find moral purpose not in the heroic models of sainthood and martyrdom but in the "unhistoric" acts of ordinary kindness and duty that the Finale celebrates. Pascal's vision of human existence — poised between grandeur and misery — remains true; what changes is the arena in which that vision is lived out.
