# Key to all Mythologies

The "Key to all Mythologies" is the great scholarly work that the Reverend Edward Casaubon has spent his adult life attempting to write — a comprehensive treatise that would unlock the underlying unity of all world mythologies. The work remains unfinished at Casaubon's death, and its failure symbolises the gap between intellectual ambition and achievement that runs through George Eliot's *Middlemarch* (007-004).

## Overview

Casaubon's project represents a characteristically Victorian ambition: to synthesise all human mythological traditions into a single explanatory framework. Eliot presents it as a work of genuine scholarship, yet also hints at its fatal flaw — Casaubon's inability to read German scholarship means he is ignorant of the continental advances in comparative mythology that have already rendered his approach obsolete. The "Key to all Mythologies" is thus both a monument to Casaubon's learning and a testament to his intellectual isolation, his "languid" soul that "went on fluttering in the swampy ground where it was hatched" (029-004).

The narrator intervenes directly to suggest that Casaubon's character is not fairly represented by those who see only pedantry and failure. To Dorothea, he had been the occasion that "set alight the fine inflammable material of her youthful illusions," and the narrator cautions against a "too hasty judgment" (010-003). Yet the work's incompleteness is inseparable from Casaubon's nature: as his health fails, the question of his authorship "haunted his silence with a more harassing importunity" (042-006).

The Key's intellectual obsolescence is measured by what Casaubon does not know. [[will-ladislaw|Will Ladislaw]] observes that Casaubon has not kept up with the German philological scholarship that transformed comparative mythology in the early nineteenth century — the work of scholars like Creuzer, Müller, and the brothers Grimm. Casaubon's project belongs intellectually to the eighteenth century; by the time of the novel's setting, it is already an anachronism. Eliot, who translated Strauss's *Life of Jesus* and Feuerbach's *Essence of Christianity* from the German, was intimately familiar with the German critical tradition that Casaubon ignores. Her portrait of his scholarship is thus drawn from inside knowledge: she knows exactly what Casaubon is missing because she herself has done the intellectual work he refused.

## In the Novel

The "Key to all Mythologies" first appears as the grand project that makes Casaubon look forward eagerly to the end of courtship, so that he might return to his labours unimpeded (007-004). Dorothea initially venerates the work as proof of her husband's greatness, imagining herself as his helpmate and amanuensis. As her disillusionment grows, however, she comes to perceive the project's sterility — a recognition sharpened by Will Ladislaw's observation that Casaubon's scholarship is fundamentally out of date.

The work also functions as a measure of Casaubon's relationship to language itself. His prose is described as "anfractuous" — winding, labyrinthine — and his notes accumulate without ever cohering into argument. The Key never achieves the one thing scholarship requires: it never becomes a book. It remains a collection of "pamphlets" and "synoptical tabulations" — fragments that gesture toward a whole they can never constitute. In this, it mirrors Casaubon himself, whose emotional life is equally fragmentary, equally incapable of synthesis.

After Casaubon's death, Dorothea discovers among his papers only the "Synoptical Tabulation," which was "probably only the beginning of many intended directions for her guidance" (050-046). The codicil to his will — which would disinherit Dorothea if she marries Ladislaw — extends the work's symbolic reach: Casaubon's mistrust, like his scholarship, projects itself beyond the grave in the form of unfinished business. Throughout the novel, the "Key to all Mythologies" operates as a metaphor for Casaubon's mind itself: elaborate, erudite, painstaking, and ultimately sterile (037-127).

The Key also functions as a critique of a certain kind of scholarly ambition — the ambition to say everything, to comprehend everything, to leave nothing outside the system. Eliot's narrator is consistently skeptical of totalizing projects, whether in scholarship (Casaubon), medicine (Lydgate's search for the "primitive tissue"), or religion (Bulstrode's evangelical certainty). The Key's failure is not merely personal but epistemological: it embodies the nineteenth century's discovery that knowledge, far from being a single edifice, is a field of competing methods and incomplete translations.

## Key Passages

> Mr. Casaubon, as might be expected, spent a great deal of his time at the Grange in these weeks, and the hindrance which courtship occasioned to the progress of his great work — the Key to all Mythologies — naturally made him look forward the more eagerly to the happy termination of courtship. (007-004)

> It is true that this last might be called his central ambition; but there are some kinds of authorship in which by far the largest result is the uneasy susceptibility accumulated in the consciousness of the author — one knows of the river by a few streaks amid a long-gathered deposit of uncomfortable mud. (042-006)

> In carrying out this bequest of labor to Dorothea, as in all else, Mr. Casaubon had been slow and hesitating, oppressed in the plan of transmitting his work, as he had been in executing it. (050-046)
