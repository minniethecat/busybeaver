# Tantripp

Tantripp is Dorothea Brooke's lady's maid, a domestic servant whose presence throughout the novel provides a quiet witness to Dorothea's emotional life — from the girlish days at Tipton Grange to the sombre married years at Lowick. Though a minor character, Tantripp's appearances mark critical turns in Dorothea's story (004-014).

## Character Overview

Tantripp belongs to the servant class that Eliot depicts with attentive realism throughout *Middlemarch*. As Dorothea's personal maid, she occupies a position of physical intimacy — brushing her mistress's hair, bringing cards to her door, relaying household information — that also makes her a conduit for the gossip and social intelligence circulating below stairs. Celia Brooke defends Tantripp's talkativeness as harmless, arguing that "it is better to hear what people say" (004-016), while Dorothea finds such servant gossip degrading. This small disagreement between the sisters captures something of the class dynamics that shape domestic life in the novel.

## Role in the Narrative

Tantripp's narrative function is largely structural: she appears at moments of transition and emotional intensity in Dorothea's story. Early in the novel, her report of the rumour that Sir James Chettam means to marry Dorothea — relayed from Sir James's man via Mrs. Cadwallader's maid — precipitates the first significant tension between the Brooke sisters (004-014, 004-015). During the Rome honeymoon, Tantripp accompanies Dorothea as her sole English companion, a sign of the narrowing of Dorothea's world after marriage (020-008). In one of the novel's most charged moments, it is Tantripp who brings Will Ladislaw's card to the sobbing Dorothea in Rome (021-005).

At Lowick, Tantripp continues her quiet service. The butler Pratt, "a red-cheeked man given to lively converse with Tantripp," often agrees with her that "it must be dull for Madam" (037-035) — a servant's-eye commentary on the emotional poverty of the Casaubon marriage. On the night of Casaubon's death, Tantripp delivers the news that he had already risen and read prayers before Dorothea awoke (048-042).

## Key Appearances

- **Chapter 4**: Tantripp's gossip about Sir James's intentions triggers the first sisterly conflict between Dorothea and Celia (004-014–016)
- **Chapter 10**: Dorothea assures Will Ladislaw that Tantripp will be sufficient company in Rome (010-013)
- **Chapter 20–21**: Tantripp accompanies Dorothea through the lonely Rome honeymoon; brings Will Ladislaw's card to the weeping Dorothea (020-008, 021-005)
- **Chapter 37**: Pratt and Tantripp's kitchen conversations provide a servant's perspective on the Casaubon household (037-035)
- **Chapter 48**: Tantripp informs Dorothea of Casaubon's morning routine on the day of his final illness (048-042)

## Relationships

- **Dorothea Brooke** — mistress; Tantripp is Dorothea's personal maid and loyal companion through her marriage and widowhood
- **Celia Brooke** — Celia defends Tantripp's right to share servant gossip, seeing no harm in it (004-016)
- **Pratt** — the Lowick butler, with whom Tantripp shares lively conversation and mutual agreement about the dullness of Madam's life (037-035)
