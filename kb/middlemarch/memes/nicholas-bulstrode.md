# Nicholas Bulstrode

The town's most powerful man and its most compromised — a banker whose Evangelical piety is genuine in its way, but whose past conceals a fortune built on stolen goods and a lost daughter never sought.

## Character Overview

"Mr. Bulstrode, the banker, seemed to be addressed" as a moral authority [010-020] — he is a man of weight, of substance, of evident piety. He holds prayer meetings, supports charitable causes, and exercises considerable influence over the town's affairs. His purchase of [[stone-court|Stone Court]] and his management of the new hospital give him power over both land and medicine.

## Role in the Narrative

Bulstrode's story is the novel's most compelling study of the relationship between past and present. His fortune originated in a [[london-middlemarch|London]] pawnbroking business that dealt in stolen goods; when the business's criminal nature was exposed, Bulstrode took the money and fled, marrying the widow of his former employer and leaving behind a daughter he never sought. This past returns in the person of [[john-raffles|John Raffles]], a disreputable figure who knows Bulstrode's history and appears in [[middlemarch-town|Middlemarch]] to blackmail him.

When Raffles falls ill while staying at Bulstrode's house, Bulstrode's care of him — or more precisely, his decision to follow Lydgate's medical instructions too literally, administering alcohol to a man for whom it is dangerous — results in Raffles's death. The question of whether Bulstrode murdered Raffles, or merely allowed him to die, is left deliberately ambiguous. What is not ambiguous is the town's judgment: the scandal destroys Bulstrode's reputation, his marriage, and his position.

The novel's treatment of Bulstrode is theologically sophisticated. He is not a simple hypocrite — his faith is real, his charitable impulses genuine. But his faith has been entirely bent into the service of self-justification, a machine for manufacturing moral approval of his own actions.
