# Purgatorio (Dante)

Dante Alighieri's *Purgatorio*, the middle cantica of the *Divine Comedy* (completed c. 1320), supplies the epigraph to Chapter XIX of *[[middlemarch-town|Middlemarch]]* — a chapter concerned with the relationship between outward beauty and inward moral state, and with the nature of sympathetic observation.

## Work Overview

The *Divine Comedy* traces Dante-pilgrim's journey through Hell, Purgatory, and Paradise, guided first by Virgil and then by Beatrice. *Purgatorio* is unique among the three cantiche in its temporal setting: it is the only realm in which souls change, where repentance is active and growth is possible. The penitents on Mount Purgatory are not fixed in eternal states, as the damned and the blessed are, but are in motion — purging their sins through effort, moving upward toward redemption. This emphasis on moral growth, on the possibility of self-correction through suffering and reflection, made *Purgatorio* especially congenial to George Eliot's moral imagination. She read Dante in the original Italian and her notebooks show deep engagement with the *Commedia*. George Henry Lewes's work on Renaissance Italian literature further embedded Dante in the intellectual atmosphere of the Eliot-Lewes household.

## Author & Context

Dante Alighieri (c. 1265–1321) was the supreme poet of medieval Italy, and his *Divine Comedy* established Italian as a literary language. The Victorian period saw a major revival of Dante studies in England — translations by Cary and Longfellow, critical studies by Symonds and others — and George Eliot was among the most Dante-literate of nineteenth-century English novelists. Her earlier work *Romola* (1863) was set in Savonarola's Florence, and Dantean allusion runs throughout her fiction. The *Purgatorio* epigraph in *[[middlemarch-town|Middlemarch]]* is one of several Dante citations that structure the novel's moral architecture (see also the *Vita Nuova* epigraph in Chapter LIV).

## Appearance in the Novel

The epigraph to Chapter XIX reads:

> "L'altra vedete ch'ha fatto alla guancia / Della sua palma, sospirando, letto." —*Purgatorio*, vii. [019-003]

Translated: "The other you see, who has made a bed for her cheek of her palm, sighing."

In Canto VII of the *Purgatorio*, these lines describe one of the Negligent Rulers — souls who, absorbed in worldly concerns, postponed their spiritual duties. The figure sits apart, head on hand, in a posture of melancholy self-absorption that Dante's pilgrim observes with fellow-feeling.

Chapter XIX marks a turning point in the relationship between [[will-ladislaw|Will Ladislaw]] and Dorothea. [[will-ladislaw|Will]] visits the museum in [[rome|Rome]] with his German artist friend [[naumann|Adolf Naumann]], who observes [[will-ladislaw|Will]]'s distractedness and shrewdly asks about Dorothea. The epigraph's image of melancholy self-absorption — the figure who rests her cheek on her palm, sighing — mirrors both [[will-ladislaw|Will]]'s lovesick distraction and, by anticipation, Dorothea's own state of inward suffering during her Roman honeymoon. The chapter is about seeing and being seen: Naumann wants to paint Dorothea, [[will-ladislaw|Will]] cannot stop thinking about her, and the epigraph's posture of melancholy observation frames the entire episode in a Dantean mode of sympathetic looking.

## Significance

Eliot's Dantean allusions in *[[middlemarch-town|Middlemarch]]* do something distinctive: they elevate the domestic and psychological into a mode of moral seriousness without inflating it into the heroic. The *Purgatorio* epigraph, like the *Vita Nuova* epigraph later in the novel, borrows Dante's language of spiritualised love and applies it to the very human, very fallible affections of a provincial English community. The effect is not parody but transposition: Eliot finds, in the sighing penitent of the *Purgatorio*, an image of the melancholy that attends all genuine moral awareness — the recognition that one has been "negligent," that one's attention has been misplaced, that growth requires suffering and time. This is the Purgatorial condition that *[[middlemarch-town|Middlemarch]]*, alone among nineteenth-century English novels, makes its central subject.
