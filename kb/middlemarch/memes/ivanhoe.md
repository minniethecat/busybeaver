# Ivanhoe

*Ivanhoe: A Romance* is a historical novel by Sir Walter Scott, published in 1819. Set in twelfth-century England during the reign of Richard the Lionheart, it weaves the conflicts between Saxon and Norman, Christian and Jew, into a tale of chivalric adventure. In *[[middlemarch-town|Middlemarch]]*, *Ivanhoe* appears not as a literary allusion but as a material object — a book bought, read aloud, and discussed — making it one of the novel's most concrete representations of how literature actually circulates in provincial life.

## Work Overview

*Ivanhoe* was one of Scott's most commercially successful novels and remained a fixture of nineteenth-century domestic reading well into the Victorian period. Its themes — national reconciliation, racial and religious tolerance, the romance of the medieval past — spoke to the historical imagination of Scott's readership, and its characters (Rebecca, Rowena, the Disinherited Knight) entered the common stock of cultural reference. For Eliot's generation, *Ivanhoe* represented the apotheosis of a certain kind of reading experience: immersive, emotionally direct, shared across class and age within the household.

## Author & Context

Published when Scott was at the height of his fame, *Ivanhoe* marked a departure from his earlier Scottish-set novels into English medieval history. Its sympathetic treatment of the Jewish heroine Rebecca was, for its time, notably progressive, though modern readers have found its representations limited. In the context of *[[middlemarch-town|Middlemarch]]*, Scott's significance lies less in any particular novel than in his status as the last writer whom educated and uneducated readers could share — a common imaginative currency that the fragmentation of nineteenth-century print culture would soon dissolve.

## Appearance in the Novel

*Ivanhoe* appears twice, in markedly different social settings. At the auction of [[peter-featherstone|Peter Featherstone]]'s effects, the auctioneer [[trumbull|Mr. Trumbull]] performs his cultural literacy by naming Scott's works:

> "'You have an interesting work there, I see, Miss Garth,' he observed, when Mary re-entered. 'It is by the author of "Waverley": that is Sir Walter Scott. I have bought one of his works myself — a very nice thing, a very superior publication, entitled "Ivanhoe."'" [032-052]

Mr. Featherstone's vocabulary — "a very nice thing, a very superior publication" — reduces literary value to commercial acquisition, and the narrator's quiet irony at his expense is one of the novel's characteristic tonal effects.

The second appearance is altogether warmer. In the Garth household orchard, on an afternoon of family leisure, Jim Garth reads *Ivanhoe* aloud:

> "The volume was 'Ivanhoe,' and Jim was in the great archery scene at the tournament, but suffered much interruption from Ben, who had fetched his own old bow and arrows, and was making himself dreadfully disagreeable." [057-016]

This is Scott at his most domesticated: not the monument of national literature but the book that is read aloud on summer afternoons, interrupted by younger siblings and family dogs. The scene is one of the novel's most affectionate renderings of reading as a shared activity — not the solitary, high-minded engagement of Dorothea with Pascal, but the communal, half-distracted experience that was, for most nineteenth-century readers, what literature actually meant. The Garth children's absorption in the archery scene, and Ben's immediate impulse to act it out with his own bow, captures something essential about Scott's hold on the popular imagination that the novel's more sophisticated characters — Lydgate, Ladislaw — can only condescend to.

## Significance

The *Ivanhoe* references participate in the novel's broader sociology of reading. Different characters read differently: Dorothea reads for spiritual guidance (Pascal, Jeremy Taylor), Casaubon reads for scholarly accumulation, Lydgate has abandoned literature for science, and the Garths read for the shared pleasure of story. Scott occupies a privileged position in this taxonomy because his works bridge the gap between high culture and popular consumption that the novel elsewhere insists upon. When Jim reads *Ivanhoe* aloud under the apple tree, and Ben rushes off to shoot his toy bow, we are witnessing the last generation for whom Scott was not yet "literature" — not yet cordoned off from life by the institutions of criticism and education that were, in 1871, already beginning to form.
