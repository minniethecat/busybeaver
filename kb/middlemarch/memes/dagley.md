# Dagley

The tenant farmer at Freeman's End on [[mr-brooke|Mr. Brooke]]'s [[tipton-grange|Tipton Grange]] estate, whose confrontation with his landlord over his son's poaching, the dilapidation of his farm, and the coming of Reform makes him the novel's most vivid representative of the rural working class. Dorothea herself had earlier cited "those poor Dagleys, in their tumble-down farmhouse, where they live in the back kitchen and leave the other rooms to the rats" as evidence of the neglect that her uncle's reformist rhetoric failed to address (039-023).

## The Homestead

Dagley's farm, Freeman's End, is given one of the novel's most elaborate set-piece descriptions — a passage that begins in the picturesque mode only to pivot into political indictment:

> It is true that an observer, under that softening influence of the fine arts which makes other people's hardships picturesque, might have been delighted with this homestead called Freeman's End: the old house had dormer-windows in the dark red roof, two of the chimneys were choked with ivy, the large porch was blocked up with bundles of sticks, and half the windows were closed with gray worm-eaten shutters about which the jasmine-boughs grew in wild luxuriance; the mouldering garden wall with hollyhocks peeping over it was a perfect study of highly mingled subdued color, and there was an aged goat (kept doubtless on interesting superstitious grounds) lying against the open back-kitchen door. (039-058)

The narrator immediately names the comfortable aesthetic as a form of blindness: "these troublesome associations were just now strongly present to Mr. Brooke, and spoiled the scene for him" (039-058). The pauper laborers, the broken barn-doors, the scanty dairy — these are not scenery but evidence, and Brooke's discomfort is the discomfort of a man forced to see what his position has always allowed him to overlook. Dagley himself appears in the tableau "carrying a pitchfork and wearing his milking-hat — a very old beaver flattened in front," having treated himself to a rare dinner at the Blue Bull after market, his discontent "less tongue-tied than usual" from drink and "muddy political talk" (039-058).

## The Confrontation

The episode occurs during Brooke's parliamentary campaign, when he visits Freeman's End to speak about Dagley's son Jacob, caught killing a leveret. Brooke opens with the condescension of a landlord who assumes goodwill — "Dagley, my good fellow" (039-059) — and is met with snarling irony:

> "Oh, ay, I'm a good feller, am I? Thank ye, sir, thank ye," said Dagley, with a loud snarling irony which made Fag the sheep-dog stir from his seat and prick his ears. (039-060)

The confrontation escalates from the specific grievance to the structural. When Brooke tries to retreat toward the house to speak with Mrs. Dagley instead, Dagley follows, "only the more inclined to 'have his say' with a gentleman who walked away from him" (039-064). His wife — "a thin, worn woman, from whose life pleasure had so entirely vanished that she had not even any Sunday clothes which could give her satisfaction in preparing for church" (039-066) — tries to silence him, but Dagley will not be stopped.

His longest speech is a torrent of class resentment, fueled by rumors of Reform as a force that will sweep away landlords like Brooke:

> "Niver do you mind what he's done," said Dagley, more fiercely, "it's my business to speak, an' not yourn. An' I wull speak, too. I'll hev my say — supper or no. An' what I say is, as I've lived upo' your ground from my father and grandfather afore me, an' hev dropped our money into't, an' me an' my children might lie an' rot on the ground for top-dressin' as we can't find the money to buy, if the King wasn't to put a stop." (039-069)

The speech culminates in a direct threat, garbled but unmistakable: Reform will send Brooke and his kind "a-scuttlin'" (039-072). The fork he strikes into the ground for emphasis proves "inconvenient as he tried to draw it up again" (039-072) — a comic detail that Eliot uses, characteristically, to acknowledge the limits of Dagley's agency without diminishing the force of his grievance.

## Narrative Significance

Dagley's narrative function is to expose the gap between Brooke's liberal principles and his management of his own estate. Brooke's Reformism is tested not in the abstract but in the specific: a tenant whose grievances are real, whose farm is crumbling through neglect, and whose anger will not be mollified by condescension. The scene is the novel's most direct collision between the world of ideas and the world of material suffering.

The narrator's coda is devastating in its layered irony:

> Some who follow the narrative of his experience may wonder at the midnight darkness of Mr. Dagley; but nothing was easier in those times than for an hereditary farmer of his grade to be ignorant, in spite somehow of having a rector in the twin parish who was a gentleman to the backbone, a curate nearer at hand who preached more learnedly than the rector, a landlord who had gone into everything, especially fine art and social improvement, and all the lights of Middlemarch only three miles off. (039-074)

The institutions that ought to elevate — church, landlord, the intellectual "lights" of the town — fail by their very structure to reach the Dagleys. The passage is a miniature of the novel's deepest social critique: that the web of mutual obligation which ought to bind a community together is, in practice, a web that the powerful can slip through and the powerless cannot escape.

## Relationships

- **[[mr-brooke|Mr. Brooke]]** — His landlord, whose liberal principles and neglectful management create the conditions for their confrontation. Brooke's attempt at friendly condescension collapses under the weight of Dagley's real grievances.
- **[[caleb-garth|Caleb Garth]]** — The estate manager whose competence and integrity contrast sharply with Brooke's fecklessness. It is Garth, not Brooke, who actually understands what the land and its tenants require.
- **Mrs. Dagley** — His overworked wife, who tries to silence her husband's drunken protest only to be shouted down: "it's my business to speak, an' not yourn" (039-069).
