# The Anatomy of Melancholy

*The Anatomy of Melancholy* is a sprawling philosophical and medical treatise by the English scholar Robert Burton (1577–1640), first published in 1621 under the pseudonym Democritus Junior. Part medical textbook, part satire, part anthology of all prior learning on the subject of melancholy, it was expanded through five editions during Burton's lifetime and remained widely read into the nineteenth century. In *[[middlemarch-town|Middlemarch]]*, George Eliot deploys a passage from the Anatomy as the epigraph to Chapter 5, using Burton's catalogue of the physical afflictions of scholars to frame her portrait of [[edward-casaubon|Edward Casaubon]].

## Work Overview

The *Anatomy* is one of the most eccentric monuments of English prose: a labyrinthine compendium that classifies the causes, symptoms, and cures of melancholy while digressing into virtually every subject known to early modern learning. Burton's method is accumulative rather than argumentative — he piles quotation upon quotation, authority upon authority, creating a textual architecture that mimics the very condition it describes. For the nineteenth-century reader, the *Anatomy* was both a curiosity of antiquarian scholarship and a source of genuine psychological insight. Its portrait of the scholar — solitary, hypochondriac, consumed by study to the detriment of body and soul — provided a template that Eliot would transform into the tragicomic figure of Casaubon.

## Author and Context

Robert Burton (1577–1640) spent his entire adult life at Christ Church, Oxford, as a student, tutor, and librarian. He never married, rarely travelled, and devoted decades to the accumulation of the learning compressed into the *Anatomy*. The irony — that a book about melancholy was written by a man whose own life exemplified the scholarly seclusion it diagnoses — was not lost on Burton's contemporaries or on later readers. Anthony Wood reported that Burton "paid his natural debt" by hanging himself, though the story may be apocryphal. For Eliot, who knew the *Anatomy* well, Burton represented both the grandeur and the pathos of the solitary scholarly life: a mind capacious enough to contain all knowledge, but imprisoned by the very study that should have liberated it.

The *Anatomy*'s enduring influence on English literature extends well beyond Eliot. Samuel Johnson praised it as the only book that ever got him out of bed two hours early. Charles Lamb called it "that fantastic old great man." Laurence Sterne plundered it for *Tristram Shandy*. For the Victorian reader, the *Anatomy* occupied a peculiar position: at once a monument of Renaissance learning and a cautionary tale about the costs of solitary scholarship — precisely the double vision Eliot brings to her portrait of Casaubon.

## Appearance in the Novel

The *Anatomy* enters *[[middlemarch-town|Middlemarch]]* as the epigraph to Chapter 5, which introduces Mr. Casaubon in his element — the library at [[lowick|Lowick]]:

> "Hard students are commonly troubled with gowts, catarrhs, rheums, cachexia, bradypepsia, bad eyes, stone, and collick, crudities, oppilations, vertigo, winds, consumptions, and all such diseases as come by over-much sitting: they are most part lean, dry, ill-colored... and all through immoderate pains and extraordinary studies. If you will not believe the truth of this, look upon great Tostatus and Thomas Aquinas' works; and tell me whether those men took pains." —BURTON'S *Anatomy of Melancholy*, P. I, s. 2. [005-001]

The passage is both epigraph and diagnosis. Burton's catalogue of scholarly ailments — each term a Latinate mouthful that itself enacts the pedantry it describes — hovers over the chapter that follows, in which Dorothea and Celia visit [[lowick|Lowick]] and Dorothea begins to form her impression of Casaubon as a man of immense learning. The Burton passage is perfectly poised between sympathy and irony: scholars *do* suffer for their work, and Casaubon is genuinely unwell; but Burton's own prose, with its manic enumeration, hints at the futility of a scholarship that consists entirely of accumulation without synthesis.

The passage's mention of "great Tostatus and Thomas Aquinas' works" resonates throughout the novel. Casaubon's portrait is painted as Saint Thomas Aquinas by [[adolf-naumann|Naumann]] in [[rome|Rome]] — an association that Casaubon himself encourages and that the novel quietly undermines. To be compared to Aquinas is to claim a place among the scholastic giants; but Burton's epigraph reminds us that Aquinas, like Tostatus, is invoked as evidence not of theological insight but of sheer scholarly exertion — "whether those men took pains." The deflation is exquisite.

## Casaubon as Burton's Scholar

The epigraph's portrait of the scholar — "lean, dry, ill-colored" — maps almost perfectly onto Casaubon's physical description. The narrator notes Casaubon's "pale brown hair" and "deep eye-sockets" [002-017], presenting a man whose body has been consumed by his intellectual labors. Burton's catalogue of ailments — gout, catarrh, vertigo — anticipates Casaubon's own declining health, which the novel treats with characteristic ambivalence. Casaubon is genuinely ill, and his physical suffering deserves sympathy; but his illness is also, in part, self-inflicted — the product of a mode of scholarship that has sealed him off from human connection, from fresh air, from the life of the body.

The cruelest irony is temporal. Burton's *Anatomy*, however eccentric, was completed and published — five expanding editions that made their author famous. Casaubon's *Key to All Mythologies* remains unfinished at his death, "a heap of fish-bones" as [[will-ladislaw|Will Ladislaw]] will later describe it. The epigraph thus establishes a standard — the *Anatomy* as a monumental work of solitary scholarship — against which Casaubon's project is measured and found not merely incomplete, but impossible.

## Significance

The Burton epigraph operates as a compressed critical essay on Casaubon's intellectual project. The *Key to All Mythologies* is itself a kind of *Anatomy* — an attempt to gather "all the mythical systems or erratic mythical fragments in the world" into a single comprehensive framework [003-009]. But where Burton's accumulative method is energised by wit and self-awareness, Casaubon's is paralysed by a lack of both. Burton wrote a book about melancholy that is one of the funniest works in English; Casaubon writes a book about mythology that drains all the life from its subject. The epigraph thus establishes the standard against which Casaubon will be measured — and found wanting — before the reader has even met him.

Eliot's use of Burton also reflects her own intellectual formation. As a young woman she had read widely in seventeenth-century prose — Browne, Taylor, Burton — and the *Anatomy*'s influence can be detected in the accumulative, essayistic texture of her own narrative voice. The narrator of *Middlemarch* is, in a sense, a Burton for the nineteenth century: a mind that surveys the full range of human folly and affliction, diagnosing with precision and sympathising with generosity.
