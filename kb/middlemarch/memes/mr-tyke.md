# Mr. Tyke

Mr. Tyke is an Evangelical clergyman whose candidacy for the salaried chaplaincy of the [[middlemarch-hospital|Middlemarch Hospital]] becomes the catalyst for one of the novel's central political conflicts. He is the preferred candidate of [[nicholas-bulstrode|Nicholas Bulstrode]], whose influence over the hospital board and whose Evangelical convictions make Tyke's appointment a test of Bulstrode's power in the town.

## The Chaplaincy Dispute

"The question whether Mr. Tyke should be appointed as salaried chaplain to the hospital was an exciting topic to the Middlemarchers" [016-006]. The dispute pits Bulstrode's Evangelical faction against the more moderate Anglican establishment represented by [[camden-farebrother|Camden Farebrother]], the current unpaid chaplain whose sermons and character are widely admired. Bulstrode argues that the hospital requires a chaplain of "sound doctrine" — meaning Evangelical doctrine — while Farebrother's supporters see the move as a sectarian power grab.

[[tertius-lydgate|Lydgate]], newly arrived and eager to secure Bulstrode's support for his medical reforms, is drawn into the controversy. When asked his opinion, Lydgate responds with characteristic neutrality: "As a medical man I could have no opinion on such a point unless I knew Mr. Tyke, and even then I should require to know the cases in which he was applied" [013-027]. This careful non-commitment reflects Lydgate's determination to remain above factional politics — a determination that will prove impossible to sustain.

## Evangelical Piety

Tyke represents the Evangelical wing of the Church of England, characterized by doctrinal rigor, moral seriousness, and a suspicion of worldly pleasures. His sermons are described as "all doctrine" [016-006], emphasizing theological correctness over the humane, broad-church sensibility that Farebrother embodies. In this contrast, Eliot dramatizes one of the period's key religious tensions: between the warm, pragmatic, somewhat worldly Anglicanism of the eighteenth-century tradition and the earnest, reforming zeal of the Evangelical revival.

## Significance

Mr. Tyke himself remains a somewhat shadowy figure — more a symbol of faction than a fully realized character. His importance lies in what his candidacy reveals about the others: Bulstrode's determination to extend his influence, Lydgate's naive belief that he can float above party politics, and Farebrother's vulnerable position as a clergyman whose human weaknesses (a taste for cards, financial imprudence) make him an easy target for the doctrinally pure. The chaplaincy dispute is, in miniature, the novel's recurring drama: the collision between private virtue and public power, between conscience and conformity.
