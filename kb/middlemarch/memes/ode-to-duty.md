# Ode to Duty (William Wordsworth)

[[will-ladislaw|Will]]iam Wordsworth's Horatian ode, first published in the 1807 *Poems, in Two Volumes*, supplies the epigraph to Chapter LXXIX of *[[middlemarch-town|Middlemarch]]* — the chapter in which Dorothea, visiting the Farebrother parsonage after her return from Freshitt, begins to anchor her diffuse moral energy in the steady practice of daily neighbourliness.

## Work Overview

*Ode to [[duty|Duty]]* is Wordsworth's apostrophe to the "Stern Daughter of the Voice of God" — a poem that marks his turn from the revolutionary ardour of youth to the steadier, more conservative convictions of middle age. The ode invokes [[duty|Duty]] not as a repressive external command but as an internal principle of order, a "light" that guides the will and preserves the cosmos from chaos: "Thou dost preserve the Stars from wrong; / And the most ancient Heavens, through thee, are fresh and strong." The poem belongs to the central current of Wordsworth's thought — the belief that moral life requires discipline, that freedom is not licence but the willing submission to law. For Eliot, who had translated Strauss and Feuerbach, Wordsworth's naturalised religion of duty offered a vocabulary for ethical seriousness that did not depend on supernatural sanction.

## Author & Context

[[will-ladislaw|Will]]iam Wordsworth (1770–1850) was the defining figure of English Romanticism and, by the time Eliot was writing *[[middlemarch-town|Middlemarch]]*, a canonical presence whose influence on Victorian moral thought was immense. Eliot's own intellectual formation was deeply Wordsworthian: *The Prelude* and the *Lyrical Ballads* informed her conviction that the ordinary life — the life of rural communities, of unhistoric acts — was the proper subject of the highest art. The *Ode to [[duty|Duty]]* speaks particularly to the tension that animates Dorothea's story: the tension between the ardent, spontaneous impulse to do good and the recognition that lasting good requires steady, institutional, unglamorous effort.

## Appearance in the Novel

The epigraph to Chapter LXXIX reads:

> "Thou dost preserve the Stars from wrong; / And the most ancient Heavens, through thee, are fresh and strong." —WORDSWORTH: *Ode to Duty.* [079-002]

The lines invoke [[duty|Duty]] as a cosmic principle — the force that keeps the stars in their courses and renews the ancient heavens. Eliot places them at the head of a chapter remarkable for its quiet domesticity: Dorothea visits the schoolhouse, discusses garden seeds with old Master Bunney, and spends an evening at the parsonage with Mr. Farebrother and his family. Nothing of dramatic consequence occurs — and that is precisely the point. The epigraph dignifies the ordinary: Dorothea's small acts of neighbourly attention are the "stars" that duty preserves from wrong, the "ancient Heavens" that, through her steady goodness, remain fresh and strong.

## Significance

This epigraph enacts Eliot's most cherished conviction: that the moral life is not made of heroic gestures but of daily faithfulness. Wordsworth's cosmic rhetoric — stars, ancient heavens — is applied to a chapter whose most dramatic event is the loss of a tortoiseshell lozenge-box. The disproportion is deliberate and deeply serious. Dorothea's "duty" after Casaubon's death could have been a second spectacular marriage; instead, Eliot shows her learning the harder lesson of living well in a small sphere. The epigraph from Wordsworth provides the philosophical framework: duty is not the enemy of ardour but its completion, the form that love takes when it settles into a life.
