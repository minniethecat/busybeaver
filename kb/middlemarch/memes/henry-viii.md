# Henry VIII (Shakespeare)

[[will-ladislaw|Will]]iam Shakespeare and John Fletcher's history play, first performed in 1613 as *All Is True*, supplies the epigraph to Chapter XLII of *[[middlemarch-town|Middlemarch]]* — the chapter in which Mr. Casaubon's brooding jealousy over [[will-ladislaw|Will Ladislaw]] crystallises into silent persecution, and the reader is granted the most intimate view of his inner desolation.

## Work Overview

*Henry VIII* dramatises the fall of Cardinal Wolsey, the King's divorce from Katherine of Aragon, his marriage to Anne Boleyn, and the birth of the future Queen Elizabeth. Long recognised as a collaboration between Shakespeare and John Fletcher, the play belongs to the final phase of Shakespeare's career, when the King's Men were exploiting the fashion for elaborate stage spectacle. The play is notable for its ambivalent treatment of authority: Wolsey's fall is at once a moral fable about ambition and a study in the arbitrary cruelty of power. By Eliot's time, *Henry VIII* was read less for its dramatic unity than for individual speeches — including Wolsey's famous "Farewell, a long farewell to all my greatness" — which had entered the common stock of English moral reflection.

## Author & Context

Shakespeare and Fletcher's collaboration placed the play at the intersection of two dramatic sensibilities: Shakespeare's deepening irony about power and Fletcher's more overtly spectacular, emotionally direct style. For Eliot, the play offered a vocabulary for thinking about the psychology of the powerful man who senses his own diminishing — Wolsey's fall from royal favour providing a historical analogue for the quieter, more domestic erosion of Casaubon's authority. The choice of a line spoken by Queen Katherine — not Wolsey — is characteristic of Eliot's preference for the moral perspectives of those who suffer power rather than those who wield it.

## Appearance in the Novel

The epigraph to Chapter XLII reads:

> "How much, methinks, I could despise this man / Were I not bound in charity against it!" —SHAKESPEARE: *Henry VIII.* [042-003]

The lines belong to Queen Katherine, speaking of Cardinal Wolsey, the man she knows to be her enemy yet whom Christian charity forbids her to hate. Eliot places them at the head of the chapter in which Casaubon's jealousy of [[will-ladislaw|Will Ladislaw]] reaches its most painful articulation. Lydgate has just visited [[lowick|Lowick]] and confirmed that Casaubon's heart condition is serious but unpredictable — a diagnosis that makes the old scholar's mortality suddenly visible, and with it the threat [[will-ladislaw|Will]] represents. The epigraph captures the exact moral position Eliot extends to the reader: Casaubon is pitiable as well as despicable, and charity demands we hold both judgements in tension.

## Significance

Eliot's ironic intelligence works through this epigraph on multiple levels. The most obvious target of "how much I could despise this man" is Casaubon himself, whose jealousy has curdled into a "morbid consciousness that others did not give him the place which he had not demonstrably merited." Yet the epigraph also invites the reader to interrogate their own response: are we, like Katherine, "bound in charity" against despising him? The chapter's power lies in its refusal to reduce Casaubon to a villain. His suffering is real, his intellectual failure pathetic rather than wicked, and the epigraph's appeal to charity — a virtue Eliot placed at the centre of her moral vision — asks the reader to extend to him the same difficult sympathy the novel extends to all its characters. The drowning man, Eliot seems to say, is not to be despised for clutching at straws.
