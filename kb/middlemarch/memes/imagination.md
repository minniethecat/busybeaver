# Imagination

The double-edged faculty through which characters in *[[middlemarch-town|Middlemarch]]* escape the prison of egoism — or construct more elaborate cells within it.

## Overview

Eliot treats imagination as the indispensable moral faculty. Without it, sympathy is impossible: you cannot feel with another person if you cannot imagine their experience. But imagination is also the engine of self-deception: Dorothea imagines Casaubon as a great scholar, Rosamond imagines Lydgate as a route to metropolitan glamour, and both imaginations prove catastrophic. The novel's ambivalence about imagination mirrors its ambivalence about everything: the same faculty that enables our best acts enables our worst errors.

## In the Novel

Dorothea's imagination is her defining trait. She sees possibilities — in people, in projects, in cottages — that others miss. Her tragedy is that imagination without experience is prone to error: she imagines Casaubon as a Milton when he is a pedant, imagines poverty as picturesque, imagines sacrifice as the highest good. Her growth is not the abandonment of imagination but its education — learning to imagine what is actually there rather than what she wishes to see.

[[will-ladislaw|Will Ladislaw]] represents imagination in its artistic form — he is a poet, a painter, a man of sensibility. His imagination makes him attractive to Dorothea but also suspect to the [[middlemarch-town|Middlemarch]] community, for whom artistic sensibility is indistinguishable from unreliability. The novel's tentative resolution — Dorothea choosing [[will-ladislaw|Will]] over Casaubon's fortune — is an endorsement of imagination over convention, feeling over duty, life over the "dead hand" of the past.
