# Inheritance

The legal and moral mechanism by which property, status, and obligation pass between generations in provincial England, functioning as both a plot engine and a thematic lens in [[middlemarch-town|Middlemarch]].

## Overview

Inheritance is one of the central organizing forces of [[middlemarch-town|Middlemarch]]'s social world. It determines who holds power, who must wait, and whose ambitions are thwarted or enabled. George Eliot examines inheritance not merely as a legal transaction but as a moral and psychological reality: the expectation of inheritance shapes character, the disappointment of inheritance breeds resentment, and the administration of inheritance reveals the deepest values of both individuals and the society they inhabit.

The novel presents a spectrum of inheritance scenarios: the conventional expectation ([[fred-vincy|Fred Vincy]]'s anticipation of Featherstone's fortune), the contested will (Casaubon's codicil disinheriting Dorothea if she marries [[will-ladislaw|Will Ladislaw]]), the unexpected bequest (Mr. Featherstone's two wills), and the moral inheritance of character and obligation that passes through families. Each case serves to illustrate Eliot's conviction that inheritance is never purely financial — it carries with it a freight of moral consequence.

## In the Novel

The most dramatic treatment of inheritance occupies the early and middle volumes of the novel: [[fred-vincy|Fred Vincy]]'s precarious position as the expected heir of his wealthy but capricious uncle, Peter Featherstone. Fred has shaped his entire life around the expectation of this inheritance, and when Featherstone's death reveals a last-minute will that leaves almost everything to the stranger Joshua Rigg, Fred's world collapses. This plotline exemplifies Eliot's critique of the moral dangers of living on expectation rather than earning one's own way.

Casaubon's will introduces inheritance as an instrument of posthumous control. His codicil, which disinherits Dorothea if she marries [[will-ladislaw|Will Ladislaw]], represents what the narrator describes as "the inheritance of a fortune" turned into a curse [047-010]. The codicil is Casaubon's attempt to govern Dorothea from beyond the grave, using inheritance law as a weapon of jealousy. It reveals the dark convergence of property and patriarchal power.

The theme also surfaces in more subtle forms. [[will-ladislaw|Will Ladislaw]]'s ambiguous relation to the Casaubon inheritance — he has "a claim of inheritance on the property which was held" by the family [054-075] — creates one of the novel's central tensions. The question of whether [[will-ladislaw|Will]] has a legitimate claim, and whether he will pursue it, becomes a test of his character. His refusal to accept Casaubon's tainted money marks his moral independence.

Eliot also uses inheritance to critique the use of wealth to "cheat offspring of their due inheritance in order to support religion" [071-066], a pointed reference to Bulstrode's financial manipulations. Throughout the novel, the disposition of property serves as a moral barometer, revealing whether characters act from generosity, jealousy, convention, or genuine care for the next generation.

## Key Passages

> The codicil — towards Dorothea, was like the inheritance of a fortune. What others might regard as provision for her security, she felt as a fetter. [047-010]

> He had already for some time been considering himself as having a claim of inheritance on the property which was held by the Casaubon family. [054-075]

> To cheat offspring of their due inheritance in order to support religion — that was Bulstrode's method. [071-066]
