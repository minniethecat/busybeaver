# John Raffles

The figure from Bulstrode's past whose reappearance sets in motion the chain of events that destroys Bulstrode's reputation and fatally compromises Lydgate — a villain who is also, in the novel's characteristic way, an agent of truth.

## Character Overview

"His name was John Raffles, and he sometimes wrote jocosely" [041-009] — Raffles is introduced as a man of raffish, threatening charm, the kind of person who knows things and is not above using what he knows. He is a former associate of Bulstrode's from the [[london-middlemarch|London]] days when Bulstrode — then known by another name — ran a pawnbroking business that was also a front for receiving stolen goods. Raffles knows about the heiress Bulstrode married under false pretenses, about the daughter whose inheritance was suppressed, about the entire structure of lies on which Bulstrode's [[middlemarch-town|Middlemarch]] respectability rests.

Eliot's naming is characteristically precise: "Raffles" suggests both the raffish (disreputable, flashy) and the raffle (a game of chance). John Raffles is the random element that Bulstrode's elaborate system of self-justification cannot account for — the wild card, the loose thread, the past that refuses to stay buried. His very name announces his function: he is chance itself, breaking into a world that has tried to eliminate contingency.

## Role in the Narrative

Raffles is the novel's most purely instrumental character — he exists to expose Bulstrode's past — but Eliot gives him a vivid, unpleasant life of his own. His drinking, his alternating charm and menace, his physical deterioration as he falls ill at [[stone-court|Stone Court]], all are rendered with the novelist's unsparing particularity. He is the agent of Bulstrode's destruction, but he is also the agent of truth: the past Bulstrode has spent decades burying returns in Raffles's person, and no amount of piety or philanthropy can keep it away.

The Raffles plot is Eliot's most sustained engagement with the genre of melodrama — the blackmailer, the hidden past, the deathbed confession — but she transfigures these conventions through her characteristic moral realism. Raffles is not a cardboard villain; he is a man with real grievances (however disreputably he pursues them) and a real history. And Bulstrode is not simply his victim; he is the author of his own exposure, a man whose attempt to bury the past has made its eventual resurrection inevitable and devastating.

## The Moral Economy of Exposure

Raffles's function in the novel's moral economy is complex. He is undoubtedly a villain — a blackmailer, a drunkard, a man who takes pleasure in the power his knowledge gives him — yet he is also the instrument of a rough justice. Bulstrode's crimes — defrauding an heiress, suppressing a daughter's inheritance, building a life of respectability on the proceeds of theft — are real, and Raffles is the only character in a position to expose them.

Eliot does not allow us the comfort of simple judgment. Raffles is despicable, but he is also right; Bulstrode is sympathetic in his suffering, but he is also guilty. The novel's moral vision is capacious enough to hold both truths simultaneously: that a wicked man can be the bearer of justice, and that just exposure can destroy as well as cleanse. This is the tragic dimension of the Bulstrode-Raffles plot — the recognition that truth, in a fallen world, rarely arrives in a form we can welcome.

## The Death at Stone Court

Raffles's final illness and death at Stone Court is the novel's crisis point. [[tertius-lydgate|Tertius Lydgate]], called to attend the dying man, becomes entangled in Bulstrode's moral catastrophe — his medical instructions are followed only selectively, and when Raffles dies, the town concludes (wrongly but understandably) that Lydgate was complicit in a murder. The scene is structured like a moral experiment: two guilty men, a dying witness, and a chain of events that neither can control.
