# Songs of Experience (William Blake)

[[will-ladislaw|Will]]iam Blake's illuminated collection, engraved in 1794 as the dark companion to *Songs of Innocence*, supplies the epigraph to Chapter XXIV of *[[middlemarch-town|Middlemarch]]* — the chapter in which [[fred-vincy|Fred Vincy]] confesses his financial failure to [[mary-garth|Mary Garth]], and the consequences of self-indulgence begin their patient work of moral education.

## Work Overview

*Songs of Experience* answers the pastoral visions of *Innocence* with a world of social cruelty, institutional corruption, and the loss of childhood's clear sight. Poems such as "The Tyger," "[[london-middlemarch|London]]," and "The Sick Rose" explore the deformations visited upon the human spirit by injustice, hypocrisy, and the repressive structures of church and state. Published together as *Songs of Innocence and of Experience: Shewing the Two Contrary States of the Human Soul*, the two collections represent Blake's dialectical vision: innocence is not superseded by experience but answered by it, and true wisdom lies in the tension between the two. For Victorian readers, Blake's radical social criticism — his unsparing depiction of poverty, prostitution, and child labour — gave *Experience* a political edge that resonated with the reformist conscience Eliot shared.

## Author & Context

Blake (1757–1827) composed *Songs of Experience* during the ferment of the French Revolution, and the collection reflects both the revolutionary hope of the 1790s and the darker recognition of oppression's persistence. Eliot, writing during the [[reform|Reform]] era half a century later, would have found in Blake a precedent for her own insistence that moral seriousness must attend to the material conditions of human life. The epigraph she selects — a couplet about the perversity of joy at another's pain — speaks to her novel's persistent concern with how people treat one another in the intimate economy of daily life.

## Appearance in the Novel

The epigraph to Chapter XXIV reads:

> "Joys in another's loss of ease, / And builds a hell in heaven's despite." —W. BLAKE: *Songs of Experience.* [024-003]

The couplet comes from one of Blake's "Proverbs of Hell" or a related passage — a gnomic statement about the perversity of human nature, the capacity to derive satisfaction from another's suffering. Eliot places it at the head of the chapter in which [[fred-vincy|Fred Vincy]] arrives at [[stone-court|Stone Court]], ashen-faced, to confess to Mary that he cannot pay her father's debt. Fred's misery is genuine, but his confession is complicated by his awareness that Mary has always been right about his irresponsibility. The epigraph's diagnosis of Schadenfreude — "joys in another's loss of ease" — sets a dark background against which Mary's generosity stands out in relief. She does not joy in Fred's humiliation; she listens, and her severity is tempered by love.

## Significance

Eliot's use of Blake here is characteristically dialectical. The epigraph describes a moral failure — the capacity to feed on another's pain — that the chapter then refuses. Mary does not gloat over Fred's confession; she receives it with the "firm little frown" of someone who has long expected this moment and has already forgiven it in advance. The Blake quotation works by negation: it names the hell that human beings are capable of building, and the chapter's action demonstrates the heaven that is also possible — the heaven of honest confession, of love that sees clearly and remains loyal. This is the deepest rhythm of *[[middlemarch-town|Middlemarch]]*: the naming of moral danger followed by the dramatisation of moral possibility.
