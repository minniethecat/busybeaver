# Lowick

The rural parish and village that is the setting for [[lowick-manor|Lowick Manor]], the home of [[edward-casaubon|Edward Casaubon]] and, after their marriage, of [[dorothea-brooke|Dorothea Brooke]]. Lowick is first introduced in the novel through Dorothea's idealized imagination and later confronted as a place of sequestration and disappointment.

## First Impressions

Lowick first enters the narrative as a destination — "only five miles from Tipton" (003-018) — when Mr. Casaubon drives home to his rectory and Dorothea, walking through the bordering wood, begins to imagine her future there. Her fantasy is characteristically ardent:

> "I should learn everything then. It would be my duty to study that I might help him the better in his great works. There would be nothing trivial about our lives. Everyday things with us would mean the greatest things. It would be like marrying Pascal." (003-021)

The same walk produces one of the novel's most poignantly ironic lines — Dorothea's declaration that "I hope I should be able to get the people well housed in Lowick! I will draw plenty of plans while I have time" (003-021). The cottages she dreams of improving will become, in fact, one of the first disappointments of her marriage: Casaubon does not care about building cottages and diverts the conversation to "the extremely narrow accommodation which was to be had in the dwellings of the ancient Egyptians" (003-050).

## The Manor

Lowick Manor is given its definitive description when Dorothea visits for the first time on a gray November morning:

> The building, of greenish stone, was in the old English style, not ugly, but small-windowed and melancholy-looking: the sort of house that must have children, many flowers, open windows, and little vistas of bright things, to make it seem a joyous home. In this latter end of autumn, with a sparse remnant of yellow leaves falling slowly athwart the dark evergreens in a stillness without sunshine, the house too had an air of autumnal decline, and Mr. Casaubon, when he presented himself, had no bloom that could be thrown into relief by that background. (009-007)

The description works by negation — "not ugly, but small-windowed and melancholy-looking" — and by absence: the house *must* have children, flowers, open windows, bright vistas, precisely because it has none of these things. The passage fuses place and person; the house's "autumnal decline" is Casaubon's own, and the marriage that will be lived within these walls is already shadowed by it.

Dorothea, characteristically, sees what she wants to see: "the dark book-shelves in the long library, the carpets and curtains with colors subdued by time, the curious old maps and bird's-eye views on the walls" — these "had no oppression for her, and seemed more cheerful than the casts and pictures at the Grange" (009-009). Her capacity for finding grandeur in the gloomy will soon be tested to its limit.

## The Parish

The parish itself, as described by the curate Mr. Tucker, is a model of rural contentment — which is precisely the problem for Dorothea:

> Everybody, he assured her, was well off in Lowick: not a cottager in those double cottages at a low rent but kept a pig, and the strips of garden at the back were well tended. The small boys wore excellent corduroy, the girls went out as tidy servants, or did a little straw-plaiting at home: no looms here, no Dissent; and though the public disposition was rather towards laying by money than towards spirituality, there was not much vice. (009-036)

The curate's report is a masterpiece of complacent enumeration. Dorothea's response is telling: "She felt some disappointment, of which she was yet ashamed, that there was nothing for her to do in Lowick" (009-040). She had imagined a parish "which had a larger share of the world's misery, so that she might have had more active duties in it." Lowick, in its tidy sufficiency, offers no scope for the ardor she brought to it. Casaubon's response — "Yours, I trust, as the mistress of Lowick, will not leave any yearning unfulfilled" (009-044) — is as blind to her actual yearning as it is well-intentioned.

## Significance in the Novel

Lowick's primary function is as the container for the Casaubon marriage. The parish's quiet routines, its distance from the social stimulation of [[middlemarch-town|Middlemarch town]], its subordination to Casaubon's scholarly rhythms — all contribute to the atmosphere of suspended life that characterizes Dorothea's married years. It is a place where nothing is wrong and nothing flourishes.

The parish also serves as a point of contrast with other locations in the novel. Compared to the commercial energy of [[middlemarch-town|Middlemarch town]], the agricultural rhythms of [[stone-court|Stone Court]], the political bustle of [[mr-brooke|Mr. Brooke]]'s [[tipton-grange|Tipton Grange]], or the bustling inadequacy of the Dagleys' [[tipton-grange|Freeman's End]], Lowick is conspicuously inert — a place where, as Dorothea discovers, nothing happens and nothing is needed. When she finally leaves Lowick after Casaubon's death, the departure represents liberation from both a place and a phase of life.
