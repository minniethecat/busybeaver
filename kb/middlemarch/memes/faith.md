# Faith

The religious feeling that animates some of Eliot's most sympathetic characters and the institutional religion that provides cover for some of her worst — *[[middlemarch-town|Middlemarch]]* distinguishes carefully between faith as lived conviction and faith as social performance.

## Overview

The novel opens with a world "helped by no coherent social faith and order which could perform the function of knowledge" [P01-002]. This is not merely a description of the 1830s but a diagnosis of modernity itself. The old certainties of religious and social order have crumbled; individuals must construct their own moral frameworks. Dorothea's story is, in one sense, the quest for a faith adequate to her ardent nature.

## In the Novel

Dorothea's religion is intense, personal, and aesthetic — she loves religious art, religious architecture, the idea of religious vocation. Her attraction to Casaubon is partly religious: she imagines him as a kind of scholarly saint, and herself as his acolyte. This is faith as self-deception, but the novel does not mock it. Eliot, who had herself lost orthodox faith and translated Strauss and Feuerbach, treats Dorothea's religious feeling with respect even as she exposes its naivety.

Bulstrode is the counter-example. His Evangelical piety is genuine in its way, but it has been bent entirely into the service of self-justification. His prayer meetings and charitable donations are not hypocritical in any simple sense — he really does believe — but his faith has become a machine for manufacturing moral approval of his own actions. The Raffles crisis is, for Bulstrode, fundamentally a religious crisis: the moment when the scaffolding of self-justification collapses.

Farebrother offers a third model: a clergyman who plays whist for money, collects specimens, and does his parish work without grandiosity. His faith is modest, practical, and humane — and the novel quietly prefers it to both Dorothea's ardor and Bulstrode's intensity.

## Key Passages

> Helped by no coherent social faith and order which could perform the function of knowledge for the ardently willing soul. [P01-002]
