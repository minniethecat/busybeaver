# Justice

The principle of moral and social fairness that serves as both an ideal and a reckoning in [[middlemarch-town|Middlemarch]], operating through character, plot, and the narrator's judgment.

## Overview

Justice in [[middlemarch-town|Middlemarch]] operates on multiple levels: as a legal concept (the disposition of wills, the absence of criminal prosecution for Bulstrode), as a social mechanism (the community's judgment of character and reputation), and as a moral principle (the narrator's distribution of sympathy and consequence). George Eliot's treatment of justice is characteristically nuanced — she is interested less in whether justice is achieved than in how characters relate to the idea of justice and what their relation reveals about them.

The novel's famous closing passage, with its vision of "the growing good of the world," suggests a kind of cosmic justice that operates through the accumulation of "unhistoric acts" rather than through dramatic interventions. This vision — justice as the slow, distributed work of ordinary life — is Eliot's alternative to the simpler models of poetic justice offered by more conventional novelists.

## In the Novel

The question of justice is raised most explicitly in relation to wills and inheritance. Featherstone's two wills — the earlier one favoring [[fred-vincy|Fred Vincy]], the final one leaving everything to Joshua Rigg — raise the question of what constitutes a just disposition of property. Casaubon's codicil, designed to prevent Dorothea from marrying [[will-ladislaw|Will]], is an instrument of posthumous injustice that uses the law to enforce jealousy from beyond the grave.

Bulstrode's fate raises the most complex questions about justice. He is never prosecuted for his role in Raffles's death, and his crimes are largely financial rather than violent. Yet the novel subjects him to a social death that is, in its way, more complete than any legal punishment. Is this justice? The narrator withholds a simple answer, but the community's treatment of Bulstrode — and its effect on the innocent Lydgate — suggests that social justice can be as indiscriminate and unfair as its absence.

Dorothea represents a different model of justice: "her ideal was not to claim justice, but to give tenderness" [020-037]. This renunciation of the demand for justice in favor of generosity is presented not as weakness but as moral maturity. The novel ultimately suggests that the truest justice is not retributive but restorative — not the punishment of wrong but "the growing good of the world."

## Key Passages

> Her ideal was not to claim justice, but to give tenderness. [020-037]

> Any man who wants to do justice does not wait till the last moment to do it. [018-037]

> There's justice to be thought of. [011-072]
