# The Not-Browne Mayde (Medieval Ballad)

The anonymous medieval ballad preserved in the Stationers' Register (1521) and popularised in Thomas Percy's *Reliques of Ancient English Poetry* (1765) supplies the epigraph to Chapter LXXXIV of *[[middlemarch-town|Middlemarch]]* — the chapter in which the [[reform|Reform Bill]]'s defeat provides the backdrop for domestic reflection at [[freshitt-hall|Freshitt Hall]], and the novel turns its attention to the politics of reputation.

## Work Overview

*The Not-Browne Mayde* is a dialogue-ballad in which a knight tests his beloved's fidelity by claiming he has been outlawed and must flee to the forest. The "not-brown" (i.e., fair-complexioned) maid declares she will follow him into exile, and her constancy is rewarded when the knight reveals the test and declares his love. The ballad's dramatisation of faithfulness under the pressure of disgrace made it a favourite of the Romantic revival of medieval poetry — Percy, Warton, and later Wordsworth all praised its artless nobility. For Eliot, whose interest in the ballad tradition was both scholarly and creative, *The Not-Browne Mayde* offered a vernacular vision of loyalty tested and proven, a vision the novel both honours and complicates.

## Author & Context

Anonymous and composed in the late fifteenth century, *The Not-Browne Mayde* belongs to the great tradition of English popular balladry that Eliot knew through Percy's *Reliques* and through her own deep reading in the literature of "the people." The ballad's concern with name, reputation, and the cost of loyalty spoke directly to *[[middlemarch-town|Middlemarch]]*'s preoccupation with how individuals sustain or fail one another in the face of social judgement. Eliot's quotation is characteristically layered: she invokes the ballad's ideal of faithfulness while the novel itself anatomises how difficult — and how rare — such faithfulness actually is.

## Appearance in the Novel

The epigraph to Chapter LXXXIV reads:

> "Theyrs be the charge, that spoke so large / In hurtynge of my name." —*The Not-Browne Mayde.* [084-003]

The lines are spoken by the knight, who lays the blame for his damaged reputation at the feet of those who have spoken "so large" — so freely, so carelessly — against him. Eliot places them at the head of a chapter set in the aftermath of the [[reform|Reform Bill]]'s defeat, in which the conversation at [[freshitt-hall|Freshitt Hall]] turns on political reputation and the casual destruction of names. The epigraph's archaic language ("hurrynge," "theyrs") evokes a world of courtly honour, but its diagnosis of careless speech as a form of violence is entirely contemporary — and entirely applicable to the novel's own world, where gossip is a weapon and reputation a fragile possession.

## Significance

This epigraph brings the novel's longest-running theme — the power of speech to create and destroy — into explicit focus. The "charge" of damaging a name is laid not against the accused but against the accusers, those who "spoke so large" without regard for consequence. In *[[middlemarch-town|Middlemarch]]*, such speech is everywhere: the town's "ardent charity" towards Mrs. Bulstrode and Rosamond, the gossip that swirls around Lydgate, the careless words that wound Dorothea. The epigraph from an anonymous medieval ballad dignifies this concern by revealing its antiquity: the problem of reputation and the damage done by loose speech is as old as English poetry itself. By placing these archaic lines among quotations from Shakespeare, Dante, and Blake, Eliot makes a quiet argument for the ballad tradition as a repository of moral wisdom equal to the highest achievements of the literary canon.
