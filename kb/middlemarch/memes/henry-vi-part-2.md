# Henry VI, Part 2 (Shakespeare)

[[will-ladislaw|Will]]iam Shakespeare's early history play, composed around 1591 and first published in quarto as *The First Part of the Contention*, supplies the epigraph to Chapter XXXIII of *[[middlemarch-town|Middlemarch]]* — the chapter in which [[peter-featherstone|Peter Featherstone]] dies, and [[mary-garth|Mary Garth]] keeps solitary vigil through the small hours of the night.

## Work Overview

*Henry VI, Part 2* is the second instalment of Shakespeare's first tetralogy, dramatising the events leading to the Wars of the Roses: the collapse of English rule in France, the rise of the Duke of York, and the Jack Cade rebellion. The play is notable for its panoramic political scope, its mixture of high tragedy and low comedy, and its unsparing depiction of ambition and self-interest dismantling a kingdom from within. The lines Eliot selects come from Act III, Scene 3, in which the deposed King Henry, wandering the battlefield at Towton, delivers a soliloquy on the peace of a shepherd's life — a peace forever denied to the wearer of a crown. For Eliot's Victorian readers, the Henry VI plays, though less frequently staged than the later histories, were a familiar part of the Shakespearean canon through widely circulated editions and schoolroom study.

## Author & Context

Shakespeare (1564–1616) is the most frequently quoted author in *[[middlemarch-town|Middlemarch]]*'s epigraphs, and Eliot's selection from the Henry VI plays demonstrates her willingness to reach beyond the obvious tragedies and comedies into the less celebrated corners of the canon. The second tetralogy (*Richard II*, the two parts of *Henry IV*, *Henry V*) was far more prominent in the Victorian imagination, but Eliot's choice of *2 Henry VI* reveals the depth of her Shakespeare reading and her ear for lines that, detached from their dramatic context, acquire a new and penetrating relevance to her own narrative purposes.

## Appearance in the Novel

The epigraph to Chapter XXXIII reads:

> "Close up his eyes and draw the curtain close; / And let us all to meditation." —2 *Henry VI.* [033-003]

The lines are spoken by King Henry as he contemplates the peace of a simple life, free from the burdens of sovereignty. Eliot places them at the head of the chapter in which [[peter-featherstone|Peter Featherstone]] dies in the night, attended only by [[mary-garth|Mary Garth]]. The epigraph's injunction — to close the eyes of the dead and turn inward — frames the chapter's movement between physical death and moral reflection. Mary, sitting alone with the dying man through the small hours, enacts precisely what the epigraph calls for: she draws the curtain on Featherstone's life and uses the stillness for her own "meditation" on human folly and self-deception.

## Significance

Eliot's irony is at its most compressed in this epigraph. The dying King Henry's meditation on mortality is transferred from a royal battlefield to a provincial bedroom, from the fall of a kingdom to the death of a miserly old man whose "life is not visibly anything but a remnant of vices." The chapter's treatment of Featherstone's death is notably unsentimental — Mary feels no pathos, only the detached observation that "people were so ridiculous with their illusions." The Shakespearean framework dignifies the scene not by elevating Featherstone but by granting Mary's clear-eyed intelligence a tragic depth: her vigil is not a sentimental deathbed watch but a moral education in the vanity of human wishes. The epigraph thus performs the novel's characteristic movement from the grandiose to the domestic, finding in a provincial death the same materials for meditation that Shakespeare found in a king's.
