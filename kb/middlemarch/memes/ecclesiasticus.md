# Ecclesiasticus (The Wisdom of Ben Sira)

The Book of Ecclesiasticus, also known as the Wisdom of Ben Sira or simply Sirach, was composed in Hebrew around 200–175 BCE by Yeshua ben Sira and translated into Greek by his grandson. George Eliot draws upon this deuterocanonical text — long familiar to English readers through the King James Apocrypha — for the epigraph to Chapter LXIX of *[[middlemarch-town|Middlemarch]]*, the chapter in which [[nicholas-bulstrode|Nicholas Bulstrode]]'s carefully buried past begins its inexorable return.

## Work Overview

Ecclesiasticus is one of the longest books of the biblical wisdom tradition, a compendium of moral maxims, practical counsel, and theological reflection that stands between the earlier Proverbs and the later Wisdom of Solomon. Its author, a Jerusalem scribe, addresses every department of life — friendship, wealth, speech, death, the fear of God — in a voice at once worldly-wise and devout. The book's fascination with the power and danger of speech, and its insistence that the wise person governs the tongue, made it an especially apt source for Eliot, whose novel is a sustained meditation on the moral consequences of what is said and unsaid. By including Ecclesiasticus among her epigraph sources, Eliot gestures towards a wisdom tradition older than Shakespeare, grounding her modern novel of provincial life in the ancient literature of ethical reflection.

## Author & Context

Yeshua ben Sira wrote in a period of profound cultural transition, as Hellenistic thought pressed upon Jewish tradition. His book attempts to synthesise the inherited wisdom of Israel with the intellectual challenge of Greek philosophy — a project not unlike Eliot's own effort to reconcile the moral seriousness of her evangelical upbringing with the philosophical radicalism of her maturity. The King James translators retained Ecclesiasticus in the Apocrypha, and it was read alongside canonical scripture in Victorian households of all denominations, including those that shaped Eliot's early life in Warwickshire.

## Appearance in the Novel

The epigraph to Chapter LXIX reads:

> "If thou hast heard a word, let it die with thee." —*Ecclesiasticus.* [069-002]

The verse is drawn from Sirach 19:10, a maxim counselling discretion in the face of dangerous knowledge. Eliot places it at the head of the chapter in which [[caleb-garth|Caleb Garth]] confronts Bulstrode at the Bank, and in which the banker learns that Raffles — the one living witness to his dishonourable past — has reappeared. The epigraph's irony is devastating: the "word" Bulstrode has heard (and done) cannot die, because Raffles embodies it. What Bulstrode most wishes to bury is precisely what will not stay buried, and the chapter traces the first cracks in the edifice of respectability he has constructed.

## Significance

The choice of Ecclesiasticus is thematically precise. The verse Eliot selects belongs to a passage in Sirach about the danger of repeating secrets: "He that can rule his tongue shall live without strife; and he that hateth babbling shall have less evil" (19:6). Bulstrode's entire social position depends on ruling his tongue — on ensuring that the knowledge of his past dies with those who know it. But *[[middlemarch-town|Middlemarch]]* is a novel in which secrets have a way of speaking themselves, in which the web of community ensures that no word truly dies. The epigraph's imperative — "let it die with thee" — is exactly what Bulstrode cannot obey, and the chapter is the beginning of his undoing. In the economy of the novel, silence is never stable; the buried word rises, and the buried past demands its reckoning.
