# Of the Laws of Ecclesiastical Polity

*Of the Laws of Ecclesiastical Polity* is a major work of Anglican theology by Richard Hooker (1554–1600), published in eight books between 1594 and 1662. A foundational text of the Elizabethan Settlement, it argues for a middle way between Roman Catholicism and Puritanism, grounding ecclesiastical authority in reason, scripture, and tradition. In *[[middlemarch-town|Middlemarch]]*, Hooker's name — invariably prefixed with the epithet "judicious" — serves as a shorthand for the union of learning and piety that [[dorothea-brooke|Dorothea Brooke]] idealises, and that her marriage to [[edward-casaubon|Edward Casaubon]] signally fails to realise.

## Work Overview

Hooker's *Laws* is one of the monuments of English prose: a work of sustained argument that marries scholastic precision with a grave, musical style. Its central contention — that the [[church-of-england|Church of England]]'s governance is lawful because it is reasonable, not merely expedient — shaped the intellectual framework of Anglicanism for centuries. For the nineteenth-century reader, Hooker represented not just a theological position but an ideal of the scholar-cleric: learned, measured, devoted to truth rather than party. It is this ideal — not Hooker's specific arguments about church polity — that George Eliot invokes in *[[middlemarch-town|Middlemarch]]*.

## Author & Context

Richard Hooker (1554–1600) was an Anglican priest and theologian whose career coincided with the consolidation of the Elizabethan church. As Master of the Temple in [[london-middlemarch|London]], he engaged in public disputation with the Puritan Walter Travers, and his experience of these controversies shaped the *Laws*. Hooker's method — patient, ironic, resistant to dogmatic extremes — made him an emblematic figure for later generations seeking a via media in English religious life. Izaak Walton's affectionate biography (1665) cemented the image of "the judicious Hooker" that Eliot inherits: the saintly scholar whose intellectual calm masks the personal costs of a life devoted to study.

## Appearance in the Novel

Hooker appears early in the novel, in the narratorial exposition of Dorothea's "childlike ideas about marriage":

> "She felt sure that she would have accepted the judicious Hooker, if she had been born in time to save him from that wretched mistake he made in matrimony; or John Milton when his blindness had come on; or any of the other great men whose odd habits it would have been glorious piety to endure." [001-010]

The passage is poised between sympathy and irony. Dorothea's desire to serve genius is genuine and noble, but the formulation — "save him from that wretched mistake he made in [[marriage|matrimony]]" — is poised on the edge of the comic. Hooker's "wretched mistake" was his marriage to Joan Churchman, a match that Walton's biography portrayed as ill-suited to a scholar's temperament. Dorothea's fantasy of being a better wife to Hooker than the historical Joan was reveals both her generosity and the self-deception that will lead her into the Casaubon disaster: she imagines herself as the helpmeet who makes scholarship possible, not recognising that Casaubon's scholarship is itself the problem.

The second Hooker reference deepens this theme retrospectively. After months of marriage, Dorothea reflects with "a certain terror" on her growing alienation from Casaubon:

> "How far the judicious Hooker or any other hero of erudition would have been the same at Mr. Casaubon's time of life, she had no means of knowing, so that he could not have the advantage of comparison." [020-015]

The irony has now turned inward. Dorothea can no longer sustain the comparison between Casaubon and the heroic scholars of her youthful imagination. The phrase "the judicious Hooker" — repeated verbatim from the earlier passage — now carries the weight of disillusionment. Hooker remains "judicious"; Casaubon, it is implied, is not. The gap between the ideal and the reality measures the distance Dorothea has travelled.

## Significance

Hooker functions in *[[middlemarch-town|Middlemarch]]* as part of a constellation of intellectual heroes — alongside Milton, Pascal, and Jeremy Taylor — against whom Casaubon is implicitly measured and found wanting. But the Hooker references do more than this. They locate Dorothea's intellectual formation within a specific tradition of Anglican scholarship, one that values moderation, learning, and moral seriousness over dogmatic certainty. This is, in many respects, George Eliot's own intellectual tradition — the tradition of Hooker, of the Cambridge Platonists, of a rational and humane Christianity that could survive the loss of doctrinal orthodoxy. By having Dorothea invoke Hooker, Eliot signals both the young woman's genuine intellectual seriousness and the naivety with which she applies inherited categories to the untidy realities of lived experience.
