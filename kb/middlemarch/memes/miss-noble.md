# Miss Noble

Miss Noble is the elderly aunt of the Reverend Camden Farebrother and the sister of Mrs. Farebrother. A "tiny old lady of meeker aspect, with frills and kerchief decidedly more worn and mended" than her sister's (PN=017-004), she is one of the most quietly charitable figures in Middlemarch.

## Character Overview

When Lydgate first visits the Farebrother parsonage, he encounters Miss Noble as part of a "quaint group" of old-fashioned ladies. Unlike her domineering sister Mrs. Farebrother, Miss Noble is gentle and unassuming. Her defining trait is her secret charity: she carries a small basket into which she diverts bits of sugar and portable food, destined for the children of her poor friends (PN=017-004). The narrator describes her as "fostering and petting all needy creatures being so spontaneous a delight to her, that she regarded it much as if it had been a pleasant vice that she was addicted to" (PN=017-004).

The simile of a "pleasant vice" is characteristically Eliotic — it renders goodness not as duty but as appetite, as instinctive and uncalculating as a taste for sweets. Miss Noble does not give because she ought to; she gives because she cannot help herself. This formulation quietly undermines the more strenuous, self-conscious virtue of characters like [[dorothea-brooke|Dorothea Brooke]], whose acts of charity are always shadowed by the question of whether they are sufficient to the grandeur of her aspirations. Miss Noble's kindness, being unexamined, is also unanxious.

Her position within the Farebrother household is telling. She is a dependent — a poor relation living on her nephew's charity — and yet she is herself a source of charity to those still poorer. This recursive structure of giving, in which the recipient becomes the giver, is one of the novel's subtlest moral observations: generosity is not the privilege of the wealthy but a disposition that can flourish at any economic level.

## Thematic Significance

Miss Noble embodies a form of goodness that the novel particularly values: unobtrusive, habitual, and directed at the immediate needs of others. Unlike the grand schemes of [[edward-casaubon|Mr. Casaubon]] or the reforming ambitions of [[tertius-lydgate|Lydgate]], her charity requires no system, no theory, and no recognition. She simply sees need and responds to it, with a consistency that makes goodness seem as natural as breathing.

This quality places her in the novel's constellation of "unhistoric" figures — those whose quiet virtue forms what the narrator, in the famous final paragraph, calls "the growing good of the world." Miss Noble will never be celebrated, never be remembered beyond her small circle, but her daily acts of kindness — a lump of sugar slipped into a basket, a visit paid to plead a friend's cause — represent the novel's deepest moral argument: that significance is measured not by fame or achievement but by the steady accumulation of small mercies.

Her friendship with [[will-ladislaw|Will Ladislaw]] is particularly telling. Will, whose own social position is ambiguous and whose sympathies are genuine but unfocused, is drawn to Miss Noble's simplicity. His gift of a German box — a small, beautiful object with no practical use — is an act of kindness that matches her own scale: personal, attentive, uncalculating. That Miss Noble treasures this gift, keeping it "under a chiffonier," suggests a reciprocity of affection that transcends the condescension implicit in charity. Will gives Miss Noble a beautiful object; Miss Noble gives Will the gift of being seen and valued in a town that regards him with suspicion.

In the novel's moral taxonomy, Miss Noble stands with [[caleb-garth|Caleb Garth]] and [[mary-garth|Mary Garth]] as figures of untheorized goodness — people who do right not because they have reasoned their way to it but because they are constitutionally incapable of doing otherwise. This is not anti-intellectualism on Eliot's part but a recognition that the most reliable virtue is often the least articulate.

## Role in the Narrative

Miss Noble appears in domestic scenes at the Farebrother household, where her presence provides a gentle counterpoint to her sister's forceful personality. She forms an affectionate bond with Will Ladislaw, whom she greets with delight when he visits Lowick. She is particularly touched by his gift of a German box, which she kept "under a chiffonier" and "grasped with delight" (PN=080-015–016).

In one significant scene, Miss Noble visits Dorothea Casaubon at Lowick to speak on behalf of Will Ladislaw, showing both her courage and her loyalty to those she cares about. She tells Dorothea, "I will not stay... else I must go and call on Mrs. Ladislaw" (PN=083-010–013), revealing her gentle determination. This visit is a quiet turning point: it is Miss Noble — the least powerful person in the novel's social hierarchy — who provides Dorothea with information that will shape her final decisions about Will. The obscurity of the messenger only underscores the importance of the message.

## Relationships

Miss Noble lives in the Farebrother parsonage as a dependent of her nephew **[[camden-farebrother|Camden Farebrother]]**, the Vicar. She is the younger sister of **[[mrs-farebrother|Mrs. Farebrother]]**, whose commanding personality overshadows her. She shares the household with her niece Winifred Farebrother.

She develops a warm friendship with **[[will-ladislaw|Will Ladislaw]]**, who is described as making "many small compassionate" gestures toward her (PN=059-007). She is also known to **[[dorothea-brooke|Dorothea Brooke]]**, whom she visits at Lowick, and to **[[mary-garth|Mary Garth]]**.
