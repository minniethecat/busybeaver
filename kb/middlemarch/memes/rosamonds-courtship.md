# Rosamond's Courtship

The romance that both parties misunderstand — Rosamond seeing a gentleman of prospects, Lydgate seeing a perfect feminine creature — and whose disastrous outcome is the novel's most concentrated study of the gap between fantasy and reality in marriage.

## Overview

[[rosamond-vincy|Rosamond Vincy]] is the mayor's beautiful daughter; Lydgate is the brilliant newcomer whose education and manner mark him as superior to the provincial young men she has hitherto encountered. Their courtship is a masterpiece of mutual misreading. Rosamond sees Lydgate as an escape from [[middlemarch-town|Middlemarch]]'s limitations — a gentleman with a future. Lydgate sees Rosamond as the perfect feminine complement to his scientific life — beautiful, graceful, undemanding. Both are wrong, and the wrongness is the engine of their shared tragedy.

## Background

Lydgate arrives in [[middlemarch-town|Middlemarch]] determined to avoid marriage until his career is established. He has a clear, almost clinical theory of the kind of wife he needs: a woman who will not interfere with his work, who will provide domestic comfort without making demands on his attention. Rosamond, with her perfect manners and ornamental beauty, exactly fits this theory — which is itself the problem. A theory of marriage is not the same thing as a marriage.

## Key Developments

The courtship proceeds with a kind of silent inevitability. Rosamond's maneuvers are so subtle that Lydgate never recognizes them as maneuvers; Lydgate's interest is so flattering that Rosamond never doubts it means what she wants it to mean. The engagement, once made, is irreversible — not because either party is trapped but because neither can imagine acknowledging a mistake. Their first "misunderstanding" (039-066) after marriage is the beginning of a long unraveling.

## Consequences and Significance

The marriage that begins as a fairy tale becomes a prison. Rosamond's will — subtle, obstinate, implacable — defeats Lydgate's every attempt at economy or reform. The novel's treatment of their courtship is a devastating analysis of how romantic love, when it is really self-love in disguise, produces not happiness but mutual entrapment.
