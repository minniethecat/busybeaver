# Mrs. Waule

Jane Waule (née Featherstone) is Peter Featherstone's widowed sister in George Eliot's *Middlemarch* (1871–1872). Along with her brother Solomon, she is among the most persistent of the relatives who hover around the old man's deathbed hoping for an inheritance. Her cold, calculating demeanor, muffled voice, and "chill-looking purplish tint" make her one of Eliot's most memorably unappealing minor characters.

## Character Overview

Mrs. Waule appears primarily in the scenes at Stone Court during Peter Featherstone's final illness (chapters 12 and 32). She had been Jane Featherstone for twenty-five years before marrying a man named Waule — described as "a warm man" (meaning wealthy) — and becoming a widow. Eliot draws her with acid precision: "It was a bright fire, but it made no difference to the chill-looking purplish tint of Mrs. Waule's face, which was as neutral as her voice; having mere chinks for eyes, and lips that hardly moved in speaking."[^1] Her speech is characterized by a "low, muffled, neutral tone, as of a voice heard through cotton wool,"[^2] a detail that makes her both comic and faintly sinister.

She drives about in "the last yellow gig left" in Middlemarch and always wears black crape — a visible marker of both her widowhood and her identification with respectable mourning conventions. The gig itself becomes a symbol of the Featherstone-Waule clan's peculiar combination of wealth and outdatedness: "When I see Mrs. Waule in it, I understand how yellow can have been worn for mourning."[^3]

## Role in the Narrative

Mrs. Waule's narrative function is twofold: she embodies the greedy, grasping relatives who circle the dying Featherstone, and she serves as a vector for the rumor about Fred Vincy's debts that threatens his standing with Mary Garth.

During Featherstone's illness, Mrs. Waule and her brother Solomon present themselves at Stone Court as devoted siblings, but their true interest is transparently financial. They resent Mary Garth's presence as Featherstone's nurse and attendant, viewing her as a rival for the old man's favor. Mrs. Waule declares herself "seated on her own brother's hearth"[^2] and entitled to speak freely — a claim that rings hollow given her transparently mercenary motives.

Her most consequential action is spreading gossip about Fred Vincy. Mary Garth reports that "Mrs. Waule has been telling uncle that Fred is very unsteady."[^4] This insinuation — that Fred has been borrowing money against his expectations from Featherstone — threatens to poison the old man against Mary's childhood friend and potential suitor. Mary, with characteristic integrity, refuses to repeat the more damaging details: "I dislike hearing scandal too much to wish to repeat it."[^5]

In the deathbed scene (chapter 32), Mrs. Waule and Solomon make a final attempt to secure the inheritance, only to be dismissed by the old man: "Back, back, Mrs. Waule!"[^6] Featherstone's rejection of his own blood relatives in favor of the honest Mary Garth (who ultimately receives nothing either, since Featherstone dies before completing his new will) is one of the novel's bitter ironies.
