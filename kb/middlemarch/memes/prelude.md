# Prelude

The Prelude to George Eliot's *Middlemarch* (1871–1872) is a short, philosophically dense opening passage that frames the entire novel. It invokes the figure of Saint Theresa of Ávila (1515–1582), the Spanish mystic and reformer, as an example of a woman whose "passionate, ideal nature demanded an epic life" (001-001) — and contrasts her with the "many Theresas" who, born into less propitious circumstances, "found for themselves no epic life" and lived out their ardour in obscurity.

## Overview

The Prelude performs several functions simultaneously. It establishes the novel's central theme — the gap between spiritual aspiration and the constraints of ordinary life — in a single extended metaphor. It signals that *Middlemarch* will be, despite its provincial setting, a work of epic seriousness. And it announces the narrator's distinctive voice: philosophically ambitious, sympathetically ironic, and committed to finding significance in lives that the world overlooks.

The passage's key rhetorical move is the generalization from Saint Theresa to the "many Theresas" — a shift from the singular, celebrated saint to the plural, unknown women whose ardour "alternated between a vague ideal and the common yearning of womanhood" (001-001). This generalization is the novel's founding gesture: it declares that *Middlemarch* will be the epic of those who had no epic, the biography of those who had no biographer.

Eliot's choice of Saint Theresa is carefully calculated. Theresa was a Counter-Reformation saint — a figure of Catholic intensity in a novel largely concerned with English Protestantism. Dorothea Brooke's own religious ardour, shaped by Pascal and Jeremy Taylor, shares something of Theresa's intensity, but the nineteenth-century English context provides no convent to channel it into. The Prelude thus anticipates the novel's central drama: Dorothea's search for an adequate object for her spiritual energy.

## In the Novel

The Prelude opens directly into Chapter 1's famous first sentence: "Miss Brooke had that kind of beauty which seems to be thrown into relief by poor dress" (001-001). The transition from the grand, historical register of the Prelude to the sharply observed domestic comedy of the opening chapter is characteristic of the novel's method: it insists that the ordinary is worthy of the same serious attention as the exceptional.

Throughout the novel, the Prelude's framework reverberates. Dorothea's attempts to find meaningful work — through marriage to Casaubon, through cottage-building, through philanthropy — are all versions of the Theresa-problem: how does a woman of "passionate, ideal nature" live a significant life when the available forms of significance are closed to her? The novel's answer — that significance is found not in epic achievement but in "unhistoric acts" of fidelity and kindness — is implicit in the Prelude's structure. The "many Theresas" are not failures; they are the subject of a different kind of story, one that Eliot is inventing as she writes.

## Key Passage

> Who that cares much to know the history of man, and how the mysterious mixture behaves under the varying experiments of Time, has not dwelt, at least briefly, on the life of Saint Theresa, has not smiled with some gentleness at the thought of the little girl walking forth one morning hand-in-hand with her still smaller brother, to go and seek martyrdom in the country of the Moors? (001-001)
