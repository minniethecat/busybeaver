# Peter Featherstone

The aged, wealthy owner of [[stone-court|Stone Court]] whose manipulation of his expectant heirs and the dramatic revelation of his two wills constitute one of [[middlemarch-town|Middlemarch]]'s most darkly comic plotlines.

## Character Overview

Peter Featherstone is the proprietor of [[stone-court|Stone Court]], a substantial estate near [[middlemarch-town|Middlemarch]], and the brother of [[fred-vincy|Fred Vincy]]'s mother. Now elderly and in declining health, Featherstone wields his fortune as an instrument of power over his numerous relatives, who cluster around his deathbed in hopes of inheriting his property. George Eliot presents him as a figure of almost Dickensian grotesquerie: cunning, tyrannical, and possessed of a malicious enjoyment in the spectacle of his heirs' avarice.

Featherstone's social position is important to the novel's anatomy of provincial class relations. He is not a gentleman by birth but a self-made man whose wealth gives him the power to elevate or disappoint his social-climbing relatives. His manipulation of expectations — particularly those of [[fred-vincy|Fred Vincy]], who has been led to believe himself the principal heir — exemplifies the corruption that attends the culture of inheritance in the novel's world.

## Role in the Narrative

Featherstone's most significant role is posthumous: the revelation of his two wills — the first favoring [[fred-vincy|Fred Vincy]], the second leaving everything to the stranger [[joshua-rigg|Joshua Rigg]] — is a major plot event that destroys Fred's financial expectations and drives him toward a more honest and self-reliant life. The episode of Featherstone's deathbed, where [[mary-garth|Mary Garth]] refuses his request to burn one of the wills, is one of the novel's most morally charged scenes.

In life, Featherstone serves as a foil to other elderly male characters. Unlike [[mr-brooke|Mr. Brooke]]'s vague good nature or Casaubon's arid scholarship, Featherstone represents the life force in its most selfish and domineering form. His marriage to a much younger second wife and his delight in the rivalries among his relatives reveal a character who has refined manipulation into an art form.

## Relationships

- **[[fred-vincy|Fred Vincy]]** — His nephew by marriage and presumed heir, whose expectations Featherstone cultivates and ultimately disappoints.
- **[[mary-garth|Mary Garth]]** — His attendant during his final illness, whose moral integrity in refusing to destroy the second will is one of the novel's key ethical moments.
- **[[joshua-rigg|Joshua Rigg]]** — His illegitimate son and ultimate heir, whose inheritance of [[stone-court|Stone Court]] reveals the secret side of Featherstone's life.
- **Mrs. Vincy** — His sister, whose son Fred is the chief victim of Featherstone's testamentary gamesmanship.
- **The Garth Family** — [[caleb-garth|Caleb Garth]] had managed [[stone-court|Stone Court]]'s affairs, connecting the Featherstone plot to the novel's exploration of honest work versus speculation.
