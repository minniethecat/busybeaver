# Medical Board

The governing body of the [[middlemarch-hospital|Middlemarch New Hospital]] — a seemingly minor administrative entity that becomes, in George Eliot's hands, a precise instrument for examining how institutions resist reform, how professional authority is negotiated, and how the best intentions are neutralized by the "petty medium" of local politics.

## Structure and Composition

The board is composed of the town's leading citizens — property owners, clergymen, and medical practitioners — who govern the infirmary's finances, appointments, and policies. Its composition reflects the pre-reform power structure of [[middlemarch-town|Middlemarch]]: a closed circle of property and influence in which medical expertise is only one voice among many, and rarely the decisive one.

| Member Type | Representative | Interest |
|------------|---------------|----------|
| Banker/Patron | [[nicholas-bulstrode|Nicholas Bulstrode]] | Financial control, moral authority |
| Clergyman | Rev. [[camden-farebrother|Camden Farebrother]] | Religious interests, chaplaincy |
| Physician (reform) | [[tertius-lydgate|Tertius Lydgate]] | Scientific medicine, professional autonomy |
| Physician (traditional) | Dr. Sprague, Dr. Minchin | Established practice, resistance to change |
| Lay Governors | Various property owners | Local influence, conservative interest |

> "The board was a kind of representative body, and the question of the chaplaincy brought out all the dormant hostility between the medical and the clerical interests." [048-005]

## The Chaplaincy Controversy

### Bulstrode's Challenge

The board's defining conflict centers on the hospital chaplaincy. [[nicholas-bulstrode|Bulstrode]], who largely funds the hospital, wants to appoint a clergyman of his own evangelical persuasion — Mr. Tyke — rather than the more popular [[camden-farebrother|Rev. Farebrother]]. The chaplaincy is, on its surface, a religious appointment; in practice, it is a test of Bulstrode's power to impose his will on the institution he funds.

### Lydgate's Dilemma

[[tertius-lydgate|Lydgate]], as the hospital's superintendent, is drawn into the conflict against his will. His professional judgment is that the chaplaincy dispute is irrelevant to medical practice — he wants nothing to do with it. But neutrality is not an option in [[middlemarch-town|Middlemarch]]'s interconnected social world. His vote for Tyke, cast to preserve his working relationship with Bulstrode, earns him the permanent enmity of Farebrother's supporters and marks him, in the town's eyes, as Bulstrode's creature.

This choice — professionally defensible, politically disastrous — illustrates the novel's thesis that no institutional decision is purely technical. Every choice in a web of interdependent interests has moral and social consequences that the chooser cannot control. "He did not care for the chaplaincy question" [048-012], the narrator observes, but his unconcern does not protect him from its consequences.

## The Board as Institutional Theater

The medical board functions in the novel as a case study in institutional dynamics:

- **The Limits of Expertise**: Lydgate, the most qualified physician on the board, cannot translate his expertise into institutional authority. The board's structure — lay governance over professional practice — ensures that medical decisions are made by non-medical men for non-medical reasons.

- **[[reform|Reform]] vs. Resistance**: The board embodies the conservative inertia of institutions. Even Bulstrode, the reform patron, cannot push through changes that threaten the established balance of interests. His defeat on the chaplaincy question prefigures his later public humiliation.

- **The [[religion|Religion]]-Medicine Nexus**: The chaplaincy controversy exposes the entanglement of medical and religious authority in provincial life. The question is not whether the hospital should have a chaplain but whose religious interest — evangelical or establishment — should prevail.

## Legacy in the Novel

The medical board's significance extends beyond the chaplaincy vote. When the [[raffles-scandal|Raffles scandal]] breaks, Lydgate's association with Bulstrode — forged in that earlier vote — becomes the evidence of his complicity. The board's internal politics have external consequences: a professional decision made in good faith becomes the instrument of a public destruction.

The institution that was meant to advance medical reform becomes, through the logic of provincial politics, its graveyard. Lydgate's defeat is complete not when he is publicly accused but when he internalizes the town's judgment, ceases to resist, and settles into a diminished practice — the "petty medium" having extinguished his "ardent nature" [076-015].
