# The Green Dragon

The Green Dragon is the principal inn of Middlemarch in George Eliot's *Middlemarch* (1871–1872). It serves as a hub of the town's public life — a place where business is transacted, politics debated, and the informal social networks that sustain provincial society are maintained over drink and conversation.

## Overview

The Green Dragon is mentioned throughout the novel as a gathering place for Middlemarch's male citizenry. It is the kind of establishment where commercial travellers stay, where electioneering happens, and where the town's professional men congregate to discuss business and politics. In the context of a novel deeply concerned with the difference between public and private life, the Green Dragon represents the public sphere in its most local and tangible form.

The inn also functions as a site of class mixing that would be impossible in the drawing rooms of Middlemarch's grander houses. At the Green Dragon, the manufacturer, the lawyer, the physician, and the commercial traveller occupy the same space, and the hierarchies that govern the town's social life are temporarily relaxed — though never entirely suspended.

## In the Novel

The Green Dragon appears most prominently in connection with Mr. Brooke's parliamentary candidacy, when it serves as a headquarters for the political maneuvering of the reform election. It is also mentioned as a place where Lydgate's professional reputation is discussed — and damaged — by the town's medical establishment. The inn's function as a site of gossip and reputation-making makes it a key node in the novel's social geography.

The Green Dragon's counterpart among the town's inns is the Tankard, which serves a similar function for a slightly different clientele. Between them, these establishments map the social divisions of Middlemarch with something close to cartographic precision.
