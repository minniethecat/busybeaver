# Solomon Featherstone

Solomon Featherstone is the brother of the wealthy but childless Peter Featherstone, and one of the expectant blood-relations hoping to inherit the old man's property. A landowner with "oddities," he has already made his own will, dividing his estate equally among the kin he favours — a fact that his sister Mrs. Waule deploys strategically in family disputes (012-029).

## Character Overview

Solomon Featherstone belongs to the network of Featherstone relatives who circle around the dying Peter Featherstone in hopes of an inheritance. Unlike some of the poorer relations such as Brother Jonah, Solomon is independently wealthy alongside his sister Jane, which places him in a position of relative power within the family hierarchy. He serves as a conduit of local gossip, relaying talk about Fred Vincy's unsteady habits to his sister Mrs. Waule (012-016). His character illuminates Eliot's satirical treatment of provincial family politics and the mercenary calculations that attend the approach of death.

## Role in the Narrative

Solomon Featherstone never appears directly as a speaking character in the novel. He functions instead as an offstage presence whose opinions and actions shape the behaviour of other Featherstone relations. His reports about Middlemarch gossip, relayed through Mrs. Waule, contribute to the atmosphere of surveillance and moral judgment that surrounds Fred Vincy (012-025, 012-027). More significantly, Solomon's own will-making — the fact that he has already divided his property equally among friendly kin — becomes a rhetorical weapon in the family jostling for Peter Featherstone's favour, since it establishes a standard of fair dealing against which Peter's capriciousness can be measured (012-029).

His wealth and independence also mark a contrast with the needier relations. In the scene of Peter Featherstone's final illness, the poorer kin such as Brother Jonah resent Solomon and his sister Jane for their prosperity, which they feel has been achieved at the expense of closer family solidarity (032-003, 032-004). Through Solomon, Eliot sketches the intricate economics of family feeling, where love and money are perpetually entangled.

## Relationships

- **Peter Featherstone** — brother; Solomon is one of the blood-relations expecting a share of the old man's property (012-029)
- **Mrs. Waule** — sister; she frequently cites Solomon's opinions as authoritative in family disputes (012-016, 012-027)
- **Jane Featherstone** — sister; alongside Solomon she represents the wealthy branch of the Featherstone family (032-004)
- **Fred Vincy** — no direct relationship, but Solomon circulates damaging gossip about Fred's gambling and unsteady character (012-016)
