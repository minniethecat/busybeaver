# Hypocrisy

The disjunction between professed moral standards and actual conduct, explored most devastatingly through the banker [[nicholas-bulstrode|Nicholas Bulstrode]] but threaded throughout the social fabric of [[middlemarch-town|Middlemarch]].

## Overview

George Eliot treats hypocrisy not as simple deception but as a psychologically complex phenomenon — a "process which shows itself occasionally in us all" (061-032). The novel's most sustained examination of hypocrisy comes through Nicholas Bulstrode, the evangelical banker whose public piety masks a history of moral compromise and whose eventual exposure serves as the novel's climactic moral reckoning. But Eliot extends the theme beyond a single villain: Middlemarch society as a whole operates on convenient self-deceptions, and even sympathetic characters are shown to be capable of the moral compartmentalization that makes hypocrisy possible.

Eliot's treatment is characteristically sympathetic even as it is unsparing. Bulstrode is not a cardboard villain but a man who has convinced himself of his own righteousness while accumulating moral debts that ultimately come due. His hypocrisy is the hypocrisy of self-deception as much as of deliberate fraud — a distinction the narrator underscores by observing that "there may be coarse hypocrites, who consciously affect beliefs for the sake of gain, but Bulstrode was not one of them" (061-032).

## Bulstrode's Double Life

Bulstrode presents himself as a pillar of evangelical piety while using his wealth and position to control others. The novel establishes him early as a man who "seemed to be addressed in a tone of respect as one whose will was law" (010-020), yet whose moral authority is subtly contested by those who "were not fond of Mr. Bulstrode" (010-056). His choice of a "red-blooded" evangelicalism — noted among "the signs of Bulstrode's hypocrisy" (074-004) — serves his social ambitions, allowing him to occupy a position of moral leadership while pursuing worldly power.

The foundational hypocrisy of Bulstrode's life lies in the origin of his fortune. He married a wealthy widow, inherited her property from her first husband, and disinherited her daughter — all while maintaining his identity as a Christian businessman guided by Providence. When the past returns in the form of Raffles, Bulstrode's elaborate structure of self-justification collapses. The Raffles scandal exposes not merely a single misdeed but an entire career built on the gap between public sanctimony and private ruthlessness.

## Beyond Bulstrode

Eliot extends the hypocrisy theme beyond a single villain. [[rosamond-vincy|Rosamond Vincy]]'s outward refinement masks a steely selfishness; [[mr-brooke|Mr. Brooke]] professes liberal principles while living as a negligent landlord; the Middlemarch community that condemns Bulstrode at his scandal had long accepted his influence while it served their interests. Even [[tertius-lydgate|Lydgate]], in his marriage, discovers capacities for self-deception he had not recognized in himself. Eliot's point is structural rather than personal: hypocrisy is not an individual moral failing so much as a feature of how social respectability operates.
