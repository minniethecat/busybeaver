# Mr. Standish

Mr. Standish is the old lawyer of [[middlemarch-town|Middlemarch]], "who had been so long concerned with the landed gentry that he had become landed himself" [010-022]. His role in the novel centers on the legal proceedings surrounding [[peter-featherstone|Peter Featherstone]]'s estate — proceedings that become a test of character for several of the novel's principals.

## Character

Standish embodies the comfortable conservatism of the provincial legal establishment. He speaks of the [[reform|Reform Bill]] with the dismissive skepticism of a man whose interests are thoroughly bound up with the existing order: "That is all very fine," he replies to [[mr-brooke|Mr. Brooke]]'s reformist talk, with the instinctive resistance of the propertied [010-056]. His genial, jocose manner — "Well, make up, make up... you see the middle-aged fellows carry the day" [010-025] — masks a shrewd professional intelligence and an unshakeable commitment to the status quo.

The narrator treats Standish with characteristic irony: his very voice carries the authority of the system he serves, his oaths and pleasantries the natural idiom of a man at ease with his own importance.

## The Lawyer as Social Institution

Standish represents the legal profession as it operated in provincial England: not an abstract system of justice but a network of personal relationships, family connections, and inherited practice. "He had been so long concerned with the landed gentry that he had become landed himself" [010-022] — the sentence is almost a miniature sociology of the provincial bar. Lawyering among the gentry was not merely a profession but a mechanism of class assimilation; the attorney who managed estates and wills eventually acquired an estate of his own, becoming what he served.

This assimilation had consequences for the administration of justice. A lawyer whose fortune was tied to the landed interest could hardly be expected to challenge that interest in court. Standish's professional competence is never in question, but his impartiality — like that of any human institution — is shaped by the social world he inhabits. Eliot, with characteristic nuance, neither condemns nor excuses this fact; she simply records it, allowing readers to draw their own conclusions about the relationship between law and class.

## The Featherstone Will

When [[peter-featherstone|Peter Featherstone]] dies, Standish presides over the reading of the will [035-001]. The scene is one of the novel's great set-pieces: the assembled relatives, each nursing private expectations; the dramatic revelation that Featherstone's fortune goes not to any of them but largely to the surprised [[joshua-rigg|Joshua Rigg]]; and the subsequent legal wrangling over the second will that [[mary-garth|Mary Garth]] witnessed.

The Featherstone will episode is more than a plot device; it is a study in the moral psychology of inheritance. The assembled relatives — the Waules, the Featherstones, the various collateral connections — have each constructed elaborate fantasies of enrichment that the actual will destroys in an instant. Standish's role is to be the instrument of this destruction, reading the dispositive language without inflection or sympathy, because the law's function, in this context, is precisely to be indifferent to the hopes it disappoints.

Standish's role in these proceedings is that of the dispassionate instrument of the law. He reads the will without favor, processes the claims without sentiment, and represents a legal order whose impersonality is both its strength and its limitation. In a novel deeply concerned with the relationship between formal justice and moral desert, Standish stands for the law as it is — precise, procedural, and indifferent to the hopes it disappoints.

## Political Views

Standish's resistance to reform extends from the legal to the political. When [[mr-brooke|Mr. Brooke]] discourses on the Reform Bill with his usual muddled enthusiasm, Standish replies with the skepticism of a man who has prospered under the existing order and sees no reason to change it. "That is all very fine," is his characteristic response — the dismissive formula of someone who has heard reformist rhetoric before and has learned that it rarely disturbs the deeper structures of power [010-056].

His conservatism is not ideological in the modern sense; it is the practical conservatism of the propertied, grounded in the reasonable fear that any alteration of the system might diminish one's own position within it. In this respect, Standish speaks for an entire class of provincial professionals whose fortunes depended on the stability of the landed interest.

## Significance

Mr. Standish belongs to that company of minor characters — lawyers, clergymen, physicians, auctioneers — through whom Eliot constructs the institutional fabric of provincial life. He is not a figure of psychological depth but of social function: the law as it operates in a country town, shaping the fates of families while itself remaining unchanging. His presence reminds us that the great events of the novel — deaths, wills, marriages — are all mediated by institutions and the men who run them. Standish is the law made flesh in Middlemarch, and the flesh, as always in Eliot, proves to be thoroughly, recognizably human.
