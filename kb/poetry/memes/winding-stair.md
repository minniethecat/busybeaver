# The Winding Stair

The winding stair is the spiral staircase of [[thoor-ballylee]], the 15th-century Norman tower house in County Galway that W. B. Yeats purchased in 1917 and made his summer home. Narrow, stone-built, turning clockwise as it ascends through the tower's four storeys, the stair gave its name to Yeats's 1933 collection *The Winding Stair and Other Poems* and became one of the most richly layered symbols of his mature work — the [[gyres|gyre]] made architecture, the physical form of the soul's spiral ascent.

## The Physical Stair

In the poem "Blood and the Moon" — part of the prefatory sequence that opens *The Winding Stair* — Yeats describes the tower interior with concrete precision:

> "A winding stair, a chamber arched with stone, / A grey stone fireplace with an open hearth, / A candle and written page" [047-280]–[047-282]

These lines place the stair in its domestic context — it is not merely a symbol but a lived space, connecting the rooms where Yeats wrote, slept, and raised his family. The "candle and written page" make explicit that the stair's significance arises from the creative work done in the rooms it connects.

The stair also appears in the earlier collection *The Tower* (1928), where Yeats invites the reader to "Climb up the narrow winding stair to bed" [045-039], blending the domestic and the symbolic — the act of climbing is both a nightly routine and a metaphor for the soul's journey.

## Symbolic Meanings

The winding stair operates at multiple symbolic levels in Yeats's poetry:

- **The gyre made stone**: The spiral stair is the three-dimensional expression of Yeats's central symbol — the interpenetrating gyres that govern history and the soul. To climb the stair is to move through the gyre, to "perne in a gyre" [047-019] in physical space.
- **The soul's ascent**: The upward movement of the stair represents the soul's progress toward wisdom, purification, and the "holy fire" of Byzantium. Each turn of the stair is a stage of the journey.
- **The union of opposites**: The stair's spiral reconciles ascent and descent, inner and outer, temporal and eternal. As the climber rises, she also turns inward — the upward journey is simultaneously a journey into the self.
- **Ancestral continuity**: The stair connects Yeats to the tower's earlier inhabitants — "A man-at-arms / Gathered a score of horse" [047-290]–[047-291] — embedding his personal symbol in a chain of Irish historical identity.

## In the Poetry

The winding stair appears across multiple poems:

| Poem | Collection | Reference |
|------|-----------|-----------|
| "Blood and the Moon" | *The Winding Stair* | "A winding stair, a chamber arched with stone" [047-280] |
| "A Dialogue of Self and Soul" | *The Winding Stair* | The stair as the setting for the soul's debate |
| Prefatory verses | *The Tower* | "Climb up the narrow winding stair to bed" [045-039] |
| "Sailing to Byzantium" | *The Tower* | "perne in a gyre" — the stair's motion described |

The collection *The Winding Stair and Other Poems* (1933) is named after the stair, signalling that the symbol has become the organising principle of Yeats's poetic world at this stage of his career. The collection opens with the death of Lady Gregory and the sale of Coole Park, and the stair — rooted in Yeats's own property — is a declaration of survival and continuity amid loss.
