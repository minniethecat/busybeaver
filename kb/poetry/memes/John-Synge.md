# John Millington Synge

John Millington Synge (1871–1909) was the pre-eminent playwright of the Irish Literary Revival and a close associate of W. B. Yeats. His six plays — particularly *Riders to the Sea* (1904), *The Well of the Saints* (1905), and *The Playboy of the Western World* (1907) — brought a new poetic realism to the Irish stage, drawing on the speech patterns of the Aran Islands and the west of Ireland.

## Yeats and Synge

Yeats met Synge in Paris in 1896 and famously advised him to abandon the study of French literature and go to the Aran Islands instead: "Give up Paris. You will never create anything by reading Racine... Go to the Aran Islands. Live there as if you were one of the people themselves; express a life that has never found expression."

Synge followed the advice, spending five summers on the Aran Islands, and the resulting fusion of peasant speech with a sophisticated dramatic sensibility produced the greatest Irish drama of the century. Yeats regarded Synge as his most important discovery, writing in his *Autobiographies*: "Synge was the only man I have ever known who could create a character as living as those of Shakespeare."

## The Playboy Riots

When *The Playboy of the Western World* premiered at the Abbey Theatre in January 1907, it provoked a week of riots. Audiences were outraged by what they saw as Synge's insult to Irish womanhood and his suggestion that Irish peasants might shelter a patricide. Yeats defended the play passionately, standing on the Abbey stage and addressing the rioters: "You have disgraced yourselves again. Is this to be an ever-recurring celebration of the arrival of Irish genius?"
