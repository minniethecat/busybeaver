## Overview

The Tree of Life is a recurring and significant symbol in W. B. Yeats's poetry and prose, appearing in multiple forms: the hazel tree of Irish mythology, the cosmic axletree around which the heavens revolve, and the sacred tree bearing the Rose as the Flower of Life. Yeats drew on Irish folklore, Neoplatonic mysticism, and Near Eastern mythology to weave the symbol through his work.

## The Tree of Life in Yeats's Prose

In his endnotes to the collected poems, Yeats explains his use of the Tree of Life symbol with characteristic erudition. He identifies the constellation Draco as "the guardian of the Rose" and the Bear constellation (the Seven Lights) as lamenting its theft, "because these constellations move about the pole of the heavens, the ancient Tree of Life in many countries, and are often associated with the Tree of Life in mythology."[048-044]

Yeats consciously adapted the symbol for Irish contexts. The cosmic Tree of Life, he notes, "I have put into the 'Song of Mongan' under its common Irish form of a hazel."[048-044] The hazel tree held particular significance in Irish mythology — according to one legend, an enchanted hazel grew on the lake-island of Innisfree, its berries capable of endowing mortals with superhuman powers or proving poisonous, and serving as the food of the Tuatha Dé Danann.[048-002]

## The Axletree Image

In "Aedh hears the Cry of the Sedge," Yeats represents the Tree of Life as an axletree — "another ancient way of representing it."[048-044] This cosmic axletree imagery connects the terrestrial and celestial realms, with the pole star hung upon it as "the Pilot Star." The axletree functions as the axis mundi, the central pillar around which the cosmos turns, a concept found across Indo-European mythologies.

## The Rose upon the Tree

Yeats describes a vision in which the Tree of Life bears the Rose — the western equivalent of the eastern lotus, the Flower of Life. He recounts standing beside a man in Ireland who "saw it growing there in a vision, that seemed to have rapt him out of his body."[048-043] In this vision, the man passed the Tree of Knowledge (bearing fruit "full of troubled faces" through whose branches flowed sap that was "human souls") before reaching "a tall, dark tree, with little bitter fruits" containing a stair or ladder ascending through it. Near the top, "a beautiful woman, like the Goddess of Life" gave him a rose.

## The Hazel in the Poems

The hazel tree appears throughout Yeats's verse. In "The Song of Wandering Aengus," the speaker goes "out to the hazel wood" and cuts "a hazel wand,"[038-120][038-122] which becomes the instrument of visionary encounter — "a man with a hazel wand came without sound."[038-178] In a later transformation, the speaker declares "I have been a hazel tree and they hung / The pilot star and the crooked plough among my leaves,"[038-499] directly invoking the Tree of Life's celestial associations.

The hazel appears in earlier poems as well, from "The Wanderings of Usheen" with its "shadowy hazel grove"[031-276] to the more atmospheric images of hazel and oak in the natural landscapes of the early lyrics.[015-658][015-668]

## Mythological Sources

Yeats drew on multiple traditions. The Tree of Life bearing the Rose connects to the Assyrian sacred tree and the Goddess of Life associated with it. The Irish hazel tradition — the tree of wisdom whose nuts fell into the Well of Segais and were eaten by the Salmon of Knowledge — provided an indigenous Celtic parallel to Near Eastern cosmic tree mythology. In Yeats's syncretic imagination, these diverse traditions converge into a single, potent symbol of spiritual ascent and cosmic order.
