# Stone Imagery

Stone in Yeats is the antithesis of water. Where water signifies the fruitful body, generation, and flux, stone signifies permanence, artifice, and the monumental — the "monuments of unageing intellect" that outlast the "dying generations." Stone is the element of Thoor Ballylee (Yeats's Norman tower), of the "cold stone" that calls the poet, of the marble floor of Byzantium, of the sculpted form that defeats decay. Stone is not dead — it is the living form that art gives to the transitory.

## Origins and Sources

- **Thoor Ballylee**: Yeats purchased the 15th-century Norman tower in Co. Galway in 1917. It became his summer home and the presiding symbol of his late poetry — a stone monument linking him to the Anglo-Irish tradition
- **Classical statuary**: Greek and Roman marble — the "monuments" of Horace, the sculpted perfection that defies time
- **Irish megalithic monuments**: Newgrange, dolmens, stone circles — Ireland's prehistoric stone architecture, witness to civilizations older than writing
- **Blake**: The grain of sand that contains the world — stone as the particular that holds the universal

## Symbolic Meaning

1. **The tower**: Thoor Ballylee is Yeats's most personal stone symbol — "I declare this tower is my symbol" (047). It is rooted in the earth but aspiring upward, inhabited by the poet and his family, connected to history by its stone walls.

2. **Stone and silence**: Stone is silent, like wisdom — "their wisdom being half silence" (042-026). The poet who "casts a cold eye" achieves the stone's impassivity, its refusal to be moved.

3. **Monumental art**: The marble of Byzantium, the "monuments of unageing intellect" — stone is the medium of the most permanent art, the art that outlasts civilizations.

4. **Stone and mortality**: "Old kettles, old bottles, and a broken can, / Old iron, old bones, old rags" — the detritus of mortal life contrasts with "the stone" that is the true, permanent self.

## Development Across Yeats's Career

**Early period**: Stone appears infrequently, mostly as landscape feature — the stones of Sligo, the rocky Irish coast. It has not yet become a charged symbol.

**Middle period (1917–1925)**: The purchase of Thoor Ballylee transforms stone from background to foreground. The tower becomes the central symbol of the mature poet — not the dreaming youth of the Celtic twilight but the hard, monumental, unyielding artificer.

**Late period**: Stone achieves its apotheosis in Byzantium — the "golden" bird that is both golden (the most precious metal, akin to stone in its permanence) and singing (alive, animate). This is Yeats's final resolution: not stone alone, but stone animated by art, the cold eye warmed by vision.
