# Irish Folklore

Irish folklore is the living oral tradition from which W. B. Yeats drew the greater part of his poetic subjects. "When writing I went for nearly all my subjects to Irish folklore and legends," Yeats acknowledged in his endnotes, listing "The Meditation of the Old Fisherman," "The Lamentation of the Old Pensioner," and "The Wanderings of Oisin" among the poems whose matter came directly from folk sources [048-005]. For Yeats, folklore was not decorative local colour but the surviving residue of an ancient spiritual knowledge — "true symbols" that in moments of vision "took upon themselves what seemed an independent life and became a part of a mystic language" [048-011].

## Definition

Irish folklore, in the Yeatsian sense, encompasses three overlapping domains: (1) **narrative tradition** — the legends of the Sidhe, the fairy doctors, changelings, and heroic tales preserved in oral recitation; (2) **folk belief** — the living practices and convictions of the Irish country people regarding the Otherworld, the "good people," and the permeable boundary between the natural and supernatural; and (3) **folk poetry** — songs, ballads, and verse fragments that Yeats collected, adapted, and wove into his own work. Unlike the literary mythology of the Ulster and Fenian Cycles, folklore was, for Yeats, a living tradition — he heard it spoken by fishermen at Rosses Point, by an old man on the Two Rock Mountain, by a priest at Collooney [048-005].

## Origins and Sources

Yeats's engagement with Irish folklore came through direct fieldwork and literary intermediaries:

- **Field collecting**: In the 1890s and early 1900s, Yeats travelled through Galway, Sligo, and the Aran Islands collecting stories. He recorded the words of fishermen, farmers, and "faery doctors" — oral informants whose testimony he treated with anthropological seriousness and poetic reverence.
- **Lady Gregory**: Yeats's closest collaborator in folklore collecting, Gregory published *Gods and Fighting Men* (1904) and *Cuchulain of Muirthemne* (1902), which Yeats praised as having "retold all that is greatest in the ancient literature of Ireland in a style that has to my ears an immortal beauty" [048-005].
- **Precursors**: Standish O'Grady's *History of Ireland* (1878–1880) and Douglas Hyde's *Beside the Fire* (1890) provided Yeats with scholarly models for treating oral tradition as serious literary material.
- **Occult framework**: Yeats interpreted folk belief through the lens of Hermeticism and Theosophy, seeing in the country people's tales of the Sidhe a folk memory of pre-Christian initiates — "the gods of ancient Ireland, the *Tuatha De Danaan*" now reduced to fairy status in the popular imagination.

## Development Across Yeats's Career

Yeats's relationship with Irish folklore moved through three distinct phases:

**Early phase (1885–1895)**: Direct adaptation. Yeats retold folk stories in the manner of the Young Ireland poets, "writing 'Down by the Salley Garden' by adding a few lines to a couple of lines I heard sung at Ballisodare" [048-005]. The poems of *Crossways* (1889) and the early *Rose* poems draw directly on specific folk narratives — the "enchanted tree" of Innisfree whose berries could "endow them with more than mortal powers" [048-005], the fairy doctor on the borders of Galway and Clare who explained the battle over the dying as a contest between "the friends and enemies of the dying" [048-013].

**Middle phase (1895–1914)**: Symbolic transformation. The folklore material ceased to be merely narrated and became a symbolic language. "I had so meditated over the images that came to me in writing … and other images from Irish folklore, that they had become true symbols" [048-011]. The Sidhe, the fairy thorn, the hazel tree, the salley gardens — these ceased to be local references and became elements in a personal symbolic system that Yeats described as "a mystic language" [048-011].

**Late phase (1914–1939)**: Absorption into the system. In the poems of *The Tower* (1928) and *The Winding Stair* (1933), Irish folklore is no longer a distinct register — it has been fully absorbed into the Yeatsian synthesis of mythology, occultism, and personal vision. The country people's belief that the dead ride "with flowers upon the heads of the horses" merges, in "Nineteen Hundred and Nineteen," with Platonic philosophy and the political violence of the Black and Tan War [048-032].

## Scholarly Interpretation

Scholars have emphasised Yeats's dual approach to folklore — at once anthropological and mystical. R. F. Foster notes that Yeats's folklore collecting was inseparable from his occult interests: the same notebooks that record fairy stories from Galway informants also contain séance transcripts and Golden Dawn ritual instructions. This fusion is not a confusion but a deliberate Yeatsian strategy — treating the Irish country people as unconscious initiates whose "superstitions" preserve, in fragmentary form, the wisdom of an ancient mystery tradition.
