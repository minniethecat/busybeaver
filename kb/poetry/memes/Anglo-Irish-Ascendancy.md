The Anglo-Irish Ascendancy was the Protestant (principally Church of Ireland) landowning class that dominated Ireland's political, economic, and cultural life from the Williamite settlement (1691) through the Act of Union (1800) and, in diminishing form, into the early twentieth century. For W. B. Yeats, the Ascendancy was something more personal and more symbolic than a mere historical phenomenon. It was the world into which he was born, the civilisation he mourned as it passed, and the source of his most potent political-aesthetic ideal: the "great house" as a centre of cultivated life, classical learning, and spiritual freedom — an aristocracy of the imagination standing against the levelling forces of democracy, commerce, and modernity.

---
## Definition

In Yeats's usage, "the Ascendancy" does not refer neutrally to a historical ruling class. It is an idealised and mythologised construction — what the critic Seamus Deane called "an aristocracy of the imagination" — in which a particular group of 18th-century Anglo-Irish figures (Swift, Berkeley, Burke, Goldsmith, Grattan) represents a lost golden age of intellectual courage, classical taste, and political independence. This idealised Ascendancy is sharply contrasted with what Yeats saw as the degraded modern Catholic middle class that was replacing it — "the filthy modern tide," as he wrote in "The Statues."

## Origins and Sources

- **Primary source**: Yeats's own family background. His father, John Butler Yeats, belonged to the declining Protestant professional class, and his mother's family, the Pollexfens, were Sligo mill-owners — local Ascendancy figures in the west of Ireland
- **Eighteenth-century Ireland**: Yeats read deeply in the Anglo-Irish writers of the 18th century — Jonathan Swift, George Berkeley, Edmund Burke, Oliver Goldsmith — and constructed from them an ideal of intellectual independence and classical poise that he called "the eighteenth-century tradition"
- **Lady Gregory and Coole Park**: [[coole-park]] was the living model of Yeats's Ascendancy ideal — a great house with a library, a park, and a tradition of literary patronage. Lady Gregory herself embodied the Ascendancy virtues: aristocratic bearing, literary culture, and commitment to Irish cultural nationalism
- **Key texts**: The essays in _Explorations_ (especially "The Irish Dramatic Movement," "The Bounty of Sweden"), the late poems "The Seven Sages," "Blood and the Moon," "Coole Park and Ballylee, 1931," and the political prose of _On the Boiler_ (1939)

## Development Across Yeats's Career

**Early period (1880s–1900)**: The young Yeats largely ignored his Ascendancy heritage except as a source of embarrassment. His early nationalism was a Catholic nationalism, identifying with the peasantry and the mythological tradition. The Protestant Ascendancy figured in his imagination mainly as the oppressor of Catholic Ireland — the "alien" ruling class.

**Middle period (1900–1920)**: The turn toward the Ascendancy tradition began with Yeats's deepening friendship with Lady Gregory (from 1896) and his involvement in the Abbey Theatre. Coole Park became his summer home and his model of civilised life. He began to read the Anglo-Irish 18th-century writers seriously and to see them as a native Irish intellectual tradition — continuous with the Gaelic literary tradition — rather than as a colonial imposition. The essay "Poetry and Tradition" (1907) and the poem "Upon a House Shaken by the Land Agitation" (1910) mark the beginning of his Ascendancy mythology.

**Late period (1920–1939)**: The Ascendancy ideal reaches its fullest expression. Poems like "Ancestral Houses" (from "Meditations in Time of Civil War"), "The Seven Sages," "Blood and the Moon," and "Coole Park and Ballylee, 1931" celebrate the great house as a centre of civilisation. But the celebration is always elegiac. The houses are burning or empty; the tradition is passing. Yeats's late Ascendancy poetry is a poetry of loss — and also, paradoxically, a poetry of creative fury, as the poet builds out of that loss a "monument of magnificence" that outlasts the houses themselves.

## Symbolic Manifestations

- **The great house**: The central symbol. Coole Park, Lissadell, Thoor Ballylee — these are not merely buildings but containers of civilisation, "befitting descendants of men of intellect and power." The house is always threatened: by fire, by "levelling" democracy, by time
- **The tree**: The great chestnut tree or copper beech on the estate lawn, rooted in the land and reaching toward the sky — a symbol of organic civilisation, unlike the mechanical modern world
- **The library**: The Greek and Latin classics, the leather-bound editions — symbols of learning that is not utilitarian but spiritual, connecting the Ascendancy to the Renaissance and the classical world
- **The eighteenth century**: In Yeats's mythology, the 18th century is the Ascendancy's golden age — the age of Swift, Burke, Goldsmith, Grattan, and the Parliament on College Green — before the corruption brought by the Act of Union

## Scholarly Interpretation

The Ascendancy in Yeats has been one of the most contested topics in Yeats criticism. Elizabeth Cullingford reads Yeats's aristocratic politics as a mask adopted to distinguish the artist from the "shopkeeping" middle class — a form of aesthetic self-fashioning rather than genuine political commitment. Seamus Deane argues more critically that Yeats's Ascendancy mythology was a reactionary fantasy that ignored the actual history of Protestant landownership in Ireland — the Penal Laws, the evictions, the exploitation. Roy Foster's biography takes a more nuanced position: Yeats's Ascendancy ideal was, Foster argues, a deliberate piece of myth-making that the poet himself knew to be a myth, and the poetry is strongest precisely where it acknowledges the gap between the ideal and the reality — the burning house, the empty room, the "vanished" hands.
