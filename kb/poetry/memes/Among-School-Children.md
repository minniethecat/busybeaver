# Among School Children

"Among School Children" is the culminating poem of W. B. Yeats's 1928 collection *The Tower*. Written after Yeats, then a sixty-year-old senator, visited a Montessori school in Waterford, the poem moves from a specific, observed scene — children in a classroom — to a profound philosophical meditation on the relationship between body and soul, youth and age, image and reality. Its final line — "How can we know the dancer from the dance?" [047-731] — is among the most celebrated in twentieth-century poetry.

## Publication Details

- **Type**: lyric poem / philosophical meditation
- **First published**: 1928, in *The Tower*
- **Form**: eight ottava rima stanzas (eight lines each)
- **Occasion**: Yeats's visit to St. Otteran's School, Waterford, as a senator in 1926

## The Poem

### I–III — The Schoolroom and the Vision of Maud Gonne

The poem opens with the poet walking "through the long schoolroom questioning" [047-668], a "kind old nun" [047-669] replying as children stare at "A sixty year old smiling public man" [047-675]. The scene is comic and tender — the elderly senator, the wondering children, the nun's patient explanations.

But the schoolroom rapidly becomes a theatre of memory. In stanza II, the poet "dream[s] of a Ledaean body" [047-676] — Maud Gonne, imagined as a daughter of Leda, bending over a fire to tell "a tale that she / Told of a harsh reproof, or trivial event / That changed some childish day to tragedy" [047-677–679]. The present schoolchildren dissolve into an image of the young Maud, and Yeats wonders if she herself once "stood so at that age" [047-686]. The memory is so vivid that "she stands before me as a living child" [047-691].

### IV–V — The Body's Decline

Stanza IV confronts the passage of time: Maud Gonne's "present image floats into the mind" [047-692] — a face "Hollow of cheek as though it drank the wind" [047-694]. Yeats, too, has aged: "I though never of Ledaean kind / Had pretty plumage once" [047-696–697]. The consolation he offers — "Better to smile on all that smile, and show / There is a comfortable kind of old scarecrow" [047-698–699] — is deliberately hollow.

Stanza V deepens the meditation by considering what any mother would think, seeing "that shape / With sixty or more winters on its head" [047-704–705] — would she think her infant son "A compensation for the pang of his birth?" [047-706]

### VI–VII — The Philosophers

Stanza VI turns to the great philosophers: "Plato thought nature but a spume that plays / Upon a ghostly paradigm of things" [047-708–709]; "Solider Aristotle played the taws / Upon the bottom of a king of kings" [047-710–711]; Pythagoras "Fingered upon a fiddle stick or strings / What a star sang" [047-713–714]. But the stanza's devastating conclusion reduces them all to the same condition as the aged poet: "Old clothes upon old sticks to scare a bird" [047-715]. The intellect, however magnificent, cannot escape the body's decay.

Stanza VII turns to the theme of images and worship: "Both nuns and mothers worship images" [047-716] — the nun adores marble or bronze statues of saints; the mother adores the living image of her child. Both kinds of image "break hearts" [047-720], and both are ultimately "self-born mockers of man's enterprise" [047-723].

### VIII — The Chestnut Tree

The final stanza is one of the great moments in English-language poetry. Against the dualism that divides body from soul, image from reality, labour from fruit, Yeats sets an image of organic unity:

> Labour is blossoming or dancing where<br/>
> The body is not bruised to pleasure soul,<br/>
> Nor beauty born out of its own despair,<br/>
> Nor blear-eyed wisdom out of midnight oil.<br/>
> O chestnut tree, great rooted blossomer,<br/>
> Are you the leaf, the blossom or the bole?<br/>
> O body swayed to music, O brightening glance,<br/>
> How can we know the dancer from the dance? [047-724–731]

The chestnut tree and the dancer are Yeats's final images of a unity that transcends the dualisms the poem has explored — the tree is simultaneously root, trunk, leaf, and blossom; the dance exists only as the dancer's moving body. The question "How can we know the dancer from the dance?" is not a puzzle to be solved but a vision to be inhabited.

## Themes

- **Body and soul**: The poem is Yeats's most sustained meditation on the relationship between physical being and spiritual aspiration — and his strongest statement against the Platonic denigration of the body.
- **Aging and memory**: The schoolroom triggers a layered exploration of time: the poet sees the children, remembers the young Maud Gonne, confronts her aged face and his own.
- **Unity of being**: The chestnut tree and the dancer embody a state in which "Labour is blossoming or dancing" — where maker and made, doer and deed, are one.
