# Georgie Yeats

Georgie Yeats (born Bertha Georgie Hyde-Lees, 1892–1968) was W. B. Yeats's wife and his most important collaborator in occult investigation. Her automatic writing — begun on their honeymoon in October 1917 — provided the raw material from which Yeats constructed the elaborate symbolic system of *A Vision* (1925, revised 1937). For the remaining twenty-two years of Yeats's life, Georgie was his amanuensis, medium, editor, and intellectual partner.

## Marriage and Automatic Writing

Yeats married Georgie Hyde-Lees on 20 October 1917, at the age of fifty-two, after Maud Gonne's final rejection and his unsuccessful proposal to Gonne's daughter Iseult. The marriage was initially unsettled, and Yeats was tormented by doubt. Four days into the honeymoon, in a hotel in Ashdown Forest, Georgie attempted automatic writing to distract her husband from his despair — a decision that transformed both their lives.

The automatic writing sessions continued for years, producing thousands of pages of script in Georgie's hand. The communicators — whom Yeats called "Instructors" — delivered a complex system of historical cycles, personality types based on lunar phases, and a cosmology of gyres and cones. Yeats spent the remainder of his life systematising this material into *A Vision*.

## Role in Yeats's Work

Georgie's contribution extended far beyond mere transcription:

- She acted as medium, receiving communications from the "Instructors" through automatic writing and, occasionally, sleep-speaking
- She participated actively in the interpretation of the symbols, questioning the communicators and helping Yeats refine the system
- After *A Vision* was published, she served as his editor, typist, and literary executor
- Her patience, intelligence, and emotional steadiness provided the domestic foundation that made Yeats's extraordinary late productivity possible

## Later Life

After Yeats's death in 1939, Georgie served as his literary executor, overseeing the publication of his collected works and managing his estate. She lived quietly at their Dublin home on Palmerston Road until her death in 1968. Her role in Yeats's creative life was only fully appreciated by scholars in the decades after her death, particularly following the publication of the automatic writing scripts and the Yeatses' letters.
