# The Tower in Yeats

The tower is the dominant architectural symbol of W. B. Yeats's later poetry, giving its name to his 1928 collection *The Tower* and his 1933 collection *The Winding Stair and Other Poems*. It serves as both a literal dwelling — Thoor Ballylee, the Norman keep in County Galway that Yeats purchased and restored in 1917 — and a complex symbolic figure for solitude, ancestral continuity, the poet's stance toward history, and the soul's arduous vertical ascent. The tower's "narrow winding stair" [045-039] becomes the central mandala of Yeats's mature imagination: a spiralling path that images both the gyres of history and the soul's climb toward wisdom.

## Origins and Sources

Yeats's tower symbolism draws on three converging sources:

1. **Thoor Ballylee**: In 1917, Yeats purchased a derelict Norman tower house at Ballylee, near Gort in County Galway, for £35 [045-003]. He restored it as a summer dwelling and renamed it Thoor Ballylee — deliberately using the Irish "Thoor" (from *túr*, tower) to claim the Norman structure for Gaelic tradition. The tower became both home and symbol, a place of "a fire of turf in th' ancient tower" [045-037] where the poet could survey the landscape and the centuries.

2. **Irish Round Towers**: The slender stone bell-towers of early medieval Irish monastic sites provided a native architectural precedent — solitary, vertical, defensive, and sacred all at once.

3. **Mythological Towers**: In Irish legend, towers mark Otherworld thresholds: Manannán mac Lir's "dark tower" [015-638], the "towery gates of Gorias" [040-162] from the tale of the Tuatha Dé Danann, and Aengus's "tower of glass" [042-047].

- **Primary source**: Thoor Ballylee (purchased 1917) + Irish mythological towers
- **Key text**: *The Tower* (1928), *The Winding Stair and Other Poems* (1933), *Meditations in Time of Civil War*

## Development Across Yeats's Career

### Early Period: Mythological Towers

In the early narrative poems, towers appear as features of the Irish mythological landscape. In "The Wanderings of Usheen" (1889), the hero beholds "dark towers" [015-431] and "Mananan's dark tower, stone by stone" [015-638], linking the tower to the sea-god Manannán mac Lir. In "Baile and Aillinn" (1903), Aengus dwells in his "tower of glass" [042-047] — a magical construction suggesting both visionary transparency and inhuman isolation. These mythological towers establish a symbolic lineage that runs from Celtic myth through Norman architecture to the modern poet's dwelling.

### Middle Period: The Tower Acquired

The pivotal moment came in 1917 with the purchase of Ballylee Castle. The tower appears immediately in the poems of the ensuing years, carrying the weight of Yeats's new domestic identity as husband and father. In a dedicatory poem, he records restoring "this tower for my wife George" and calls "God grant a blessing on this tower and cottage" [045-965]. The tower set "on the stream's edge" [045-086] connected the vertical aspiration of stone to the flowing temporality of water — a spatial dialectic that would shape his later symbolism.

### Late Period: The Tower and the Tower

The 1928 collection *The Tower* represents the symbol's fullest elaboration. In the title poem, the aging poet climbs the tower and surveys his life and nation: "I pace upon the battlements and stare / On the foundations of a house, or where / Tree, like a sooty finger, starts from the earth" — an act of vertical survey that is simultaneously physical, historical, and metaphysical. The "winding, gyring, spiring treadmill of a stair" images the soul's ascent and the gyres of history as a single motion.

In "Meditations in Time of Civil War" (1923), the tower becomes the vantage from which the poet contemplates ancestral portraits and national violence amidst the Irish Civil War: "the sea-wind scream upon the tower" [046-423] evokes both isolation and exposure. The tower here is neither refuge nor fortress — it is the precarious perch of the contemplative mind in a world of violence.

The 1933 collection *The Winding Stair* continued the tower meditation. "A Dialogue of Self and Soul" takes place on the tower's "winding stair" [047-280]; the stair's spiral becomes the stage for the soul's debate between renunciation and worldly affirmation. In the late poem "Under Ben Bulben," Yeats summons the "indomitable Irishry" to remember the "poet's tower" — the symbol has become a national bequest.

## Symbolic Meaning

The tower operates on four interconnected symbolic levels:

1. **Vertical Ascent**: The tower's height figures the soul's climb toward wisdom — the winding stair is the difficult, spiralling path of artistic and spiritual effort.
2. **Solitude and Contemplation**: The tower separates the poet from the crowd, providing the isolation necessary for vision — but also risking the coldness of detachment.
3. **Ancestral Continuity**: The Norman stone connects the modern poet to a lineage of builders, warriors, and landowners across centuries — the tower is history made visible.
4. **Violence and Defence**: The tower was built for war; its stone walls carry the memory of conflict, making it an ambiguous symbol during the Irish Civil War.
