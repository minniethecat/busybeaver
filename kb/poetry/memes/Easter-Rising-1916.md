# Easter Rising 1916

The Easter Rising was an armed insurrection in Dublin during Easter Week, April 1916. Organised by the Irish Republican Brotherhood, the Irish Volunteers, and the Irish Citizen Army, the Rising seized key buildings in central Dublin and proclaimed an Irish Republic from the steps of the General Post Office. After six days of fighting, the rebels surrendered. Fifteen leaders were executed by firing squad in May 1916.

## Yeats's Response

The Rising profoundly affected Yeats, who had been in London when it occurred. His initial reaction was ambivalent — he had been critical of the militarisation of Irish nationalism and had personal connections to several of the executed leaders, including [[John-MacBride|John MacBride]], Maud Gonne's estranged husband.

His poem "Easter, 1916" — written between May and September 1916 but not published until 1920 — is his response. Its refrain "A terrible beauty is born" captures the paradox that haunted Yeats: the Rising was tragic and wasteful, yet it had transformed its participants from ordinary people ("a drunken, vainglorious lout," "that woman") into figures of tragic grandeur. The poem names several of the leaders: [[Patrick-Pearse|Patrick Pearse]], [[Thomas-MacDonagh|Thomas MacDonagh]], [[John-MacBride|John MacBride]], and [[James-Connolly|James Connolly]].
