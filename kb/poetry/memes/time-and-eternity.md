# Time and Eternity

The opposition between **time and eternity** is one of the fundamental structural tensions in W. B. Yeats's mature poetry, pitting the organic decay of the temporal world against the unchanging perfection of eternal art.

## The Core Tension

In Yeats's late work, time is identified with the natural body — ageing, decay, and death. Eternity is identified with art, intellect, and the soul's transcendence. This opposition is crystallised in "Sailing to Byzantium" (1928), where the ageing speaker rejects the "sensual music" of the young and seeks to be gathered "into the artifice of eternity" (047-031).

The body is described as "a dying animal" tied to time's passage, while the soul longs to become an eternal golden bird — a mechanical artifice that sings "of what is past, or passing, or to come" to the lords and ladies of Byzantium (047-031).

## "Sailing to Byzantium": The Journey from Time to Artifice

The poem moves through three stages of the soul's escape from time. The first stanza depicts a natural world of "sensual music" — birds, fish, flesh — from which the aged speaker feels excluded. The second stanza identifies the body itself as the prison of time: "An aged man is but a paltry thing, / A tattered coat upon a stick" (047-031). The third stanza turns toward the solution: the soul must be gathered "into the artifice of eternity" through the purifying agency of "God's holy fire" (047-017). The final stanza imagines the speaker reborn as a golden mechanical bird, singing eternally to an audience of Byzantine nobility — the consummation of the journey from mortal decay to timeless art.

## The Gyre as Temporal Model

In *[[a-vision\|A Vision]]*, Yeats models time as interpenetrating gyres — cones of opposing forces that expand and contract across two-thousand-year cycles (048-056). Each historical era (classical, Christian, and the coming age) is a single turn of the gyre. Eternity is what lies outside the gyres: the "Thirteenth Cone" or sphere that transcends cyclical time entirely.

## "Byzantium" (1933)

The companion poem "Byzantium" deepens the time-eternity tension. The opening line presents "the unpurged images of day" — the temporal world's unresolved forms — receding before the eternal city. The golden bird performs "upon the star-lit golden bough" of the Emperor's palace, an image of art that has achieved timelessness through the purifying "holy fire" (047-019).

## In the Early Work

Even in Yeats's early poetry, the tension appears in mythic form. Oisín in *The Wanderings of Usheen* (1889) experiences three hundred years in the timeless Otherworld of [[tir-na-nOg\|Tír na nÓg]], only to age instantly upon touching mortal soil — a folk-expression of the irreconcilability of time and eternity.

## Bitter Eternity: The Tension in the Later Poems

The promise of eternity in Yeats becomes increasingly ambivalent. In "The Tower" (1928), the speaker confronts old age not with the yearning for Byzantium's golden bird but with a defiant assertion of creative will: despite his "decrepit age," he will make his "soul" — through poetry — outlast the body. The eternity his verse confers is not the calm artifice of Byzantium but a restless, passionate one.

This darker vision reaches its extreme in "The Man and the Echo" (1938), where the poet imagines eternity not as liberation but as a condition of perpetual recurrence — the soul forced to "live it all again" (047-031). Here time and eternity are no longer opposites but a single, punishing continuum. The progression from "Sailing to Byzantium" through "Byzantium" to the late confessional poems traces an arc from hopeful transcendence to the recognition that, for Yeats, even eternity could not escape the passions and regrets of time.
