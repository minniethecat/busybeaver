# The Hosting of the Sidhe

"The Hosting of the Sidhe" is one of the most haunting of Yeats's early poems, published in *The Wind Among the Reeds* (1899). It draws on Irish folk tradition — the belief that the fairy host (the Sidhe) rides out on certain nights, sweeping mortals away into the Otherworld. The poem is spoken in the voice of a mortal who has heard the call and knows he will be taken.

## Mythological Sources

The poem draws directly on Irish mythological tradition:

- **The Sidhe** (pronounced "shee") are the fairy folk of Irish tradition — the Tuatha Dé Danann who retreated into the hollow hills after their defeat. Yeats's version owes much to the folk beliefs he collected in County Sligo and recorded in *The Celtic Twilight*.
- **Niamh** — who calls "Away, come away" — is the fairy woman who carried Oisin away to Tír na nÓg in the Fenian cycle. She appears throughout Yeats's early poetry as the embodiment of otherworldly beauty and the call away from mortal life.
- **Knocknarea** — the mountain in Co. Sligo visible from Yeats's childhood home, topped with Queen Maeve's cairn, is named as the site from which the host rides.

## Poetic Structure

The poem employs short, insistent lines and the refrain-like repetition of "Away, come away" — a technique that mimics the hypnotic pull of fairy enchantment. The rhythm suggests galloping horses. The imagery of fire and burning — "unquiet" mortal hearts versus the "flame" of the Sidhe — establishes the tension between mortal passion and immortal otherness that runs through Yeats's work.
