The Irish Civil War (28 June 1922 – 24 May 1923) was the conflict that followed the [[Irish-War-of-Independence]] and divided the Irish nationalist movement between those who accepted the Anglo-Irish Treaty (establishing the [[Irish-Free-State]] as a dominion within the British Commonwealth) and those who rejected it as a betrayal of republican ideals. For W. B. Yeats, the Civil War was not merely a political catastrophe — it was a spiritual test. Living at Thoor Ballylee in County Galway as the war raged around him, Yeats wrote some of his most anguished and philosophically profound poems in direct response to the conflict, transforming political tragedy into meditations on civilisation, violence, and the role of art.

## Historical Background

**The Treaty split**: The Anglo-Irish Treaty of December 1921 granted Ireland dominion status but required an oath of allegiance to the British Crown and accepted the partition of the six counties of Northern Ireland. The Dáil (Irish parliament) ratified the Treaty by a narrow vote of 64–57 in January 1922. Pro-Treaty forces, led by Michael Collins and Arthur Griffith, formed the Provisional Government; anti-Treaty forces, led by Éamon de Valera, repudiated the Dáil's authority and occupied the Four Courts in Dublin.

**The outbreak of war**: The conflict began when the Provisional Government, under British pressure, bombarded the Four Courts with artillery on 28 June 1922. Fighting spread across the country, with the anti-Treaty IRA employing guerrilla tactics against the newly-formed Free State Army. The war was shorter but more bitter than the War of Independence, marked by atrocities on both sides and the assassination of Michael Collins in an ambush at Béal na Bláth on 22 August 1922.

**Outcome**: The Free State forces prevailed by mid-1923; approximately 1,500–2,000 combatants and civilians were killed. The war left a legacy of bitterness that shaped Irish politics for the rest of the twentieth century, with the two main political parties — Fianna Fáil and Fine Gael — tracing their origins to the opposing sides.

## Yeats and the Civil War

Yeats's relationship to the Civil War was complex and contradictory. He had been a senator of the Irish Free State since December 1922, aligning himself with the pro-Treaty government, and his public statements condemned the anti-Treaty republicans as "destructionists." But his private feelings were more ambivalent: he admired the courage and idealism of the republican side even as he deplored their methods, and he understood — perhaps better than anyone — that the violence of the Civil War was not a foreign intrusion but the dark underside of the very nationalist passion he had helped cultivate through the [[Celtic-Revival]].

**Thoor Ballylee**: During the summer of 1922, Yeats and his family remained at Thoor Ballylee, the Norman tower in County Galway he had purchased and renovated. The tower was near territory controlled by anti-Treaty forces; bridges were blown up, and the Yeats family heard gunfire and explosions. Yeats recorded the experience in "Meditations in Time of Civil War" (written 1922–1923, published in _The Tower_, 1928), a sequence of seven poems that moves from the physical setting of the tower through ancestral portraits, the poet's own creative vocation, and the violence outside, to a concluding vision of "the half-read wisdom of daemonic images."

## Yeats's Political Philosophy

The Civil War crystallised a central tension in Yeats's thought: the conflict between order and passion, civilisation and anarchy. In his Senate speeches, Yeats defended the Free State's authority and the need for lawful government. In his poetry, however, he gave voice to the appalling beauty of destructive energy — the "terrible beauty" he had already identified in the Easter Rising. The Civil War confirmed his belief, articulated throughout _A Vision_, that history moves in violent gyres and that civilisation is perpetually threatened by the arrival of "the rough beast" — the new dispensation that must replace the old.

This tension is never resolved. It is, rather, the generating paradox of Yeats's late political poetry, which simultaneously mourns the destruction of a great house and aristocratic civilisation ("Ancestral Houses") and acknowledges that the violence destroying it may itself be the instrument of historical renewal.

## Scholarly Interpretation

Critics have long debated Yeats's political stance during the Civil War. Seamus Deane's _Celtic Revivals_ (1985) argues that Yeats's aristocratic pose was a mask for reactionary politics; Elizabeth Cullingford's _Yeats, Ireland and Fascism_ (1981) traces the poet's brief and ambiguous flirtation with authoritarianism. More recent work by Roy Foster and R. F. Foster situates Yeats's Civil War writings within the material conditions of life at Thoor Ballylee and the poet's genuine fear for his family's safety — reading the "Meditations" not as abstract political allegory but as the lived experience of a man who chose to remain in a war zone.
