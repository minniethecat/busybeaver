# Ben Bulben in Yeats

Ben Bulben is the flat-topped limestone mountain that dominates the skyline of north County Sligo. For W. B. Yeats, who spent his childhood summers in Sligo and could see Ben Bulben from his grandfather's house, the mountain was more than a geographical feature: it was the tutelary presence of his imaginative world. He chose to be buried in its shadow at Drumcliff churchyard, and his final poem, "Under Ben Bulben" — the last poem in his *Last Poems* (1939) — is his self-written epitaph, addressed to the mountain itself.

## In Yeats's Poems

Ben Bulben is named directly in the poetry only in Yeats's late work. In "Under Ben Bulben," the mountain is a commanding, almost supernatural presence: "Under Ben Bulben to the meet" (046-294) and "Upon Ben Bulben side" (046-322) place the mountain as the scene of a ghostly horseman's ride and the poet's final resting place. The poem's famous closing tercet — "Cast a cold eye / On life, on death. / Horseman, pass by!" — is carved on Yeats's headstone beneath the mountain.

In the earlier poem "The Wanderings of Usheen," the mountain appears in a more mythological register: "for the humbler worm, I climbed Ben Bulben's back" (047-041). Here the mountain is part of the legendary landscape that Usheen traverses — already, in Yeats's earliest long poem, a feature of the heroic geography of Irish myth.

## Symbolic Significance

- **The mountain as tutelary presence**: Ben Bulben is the presiding genius of Yeats's Sligo — the visible marker of the place where his imagination was formed
- **Death and return**: By choosing burial beneath Ben Bulben, Yeats made the mountain the endpoint of his life's arc — the return to the landscape of childhood
- **The horseman**: The ghostly rider in "Under Ben Bulben" links the mountain to the Irish mythological tradition of the *sluagh sidhe*, the fairy host that rides the wind

## Ben Bulben and Yeats's Sligo Childhood

Yeats's connection to Ben Bulben was rooted in the summers he spent in Sligo as a child. From his grandfather William Pollexfen's house at Merville, the young Yeats could see the distinctive flat-topped silhouette of the mountain on the northern horizon. In an endnote to his collected poems, Yeats recalled: "When I was a child at Sligo I could see above my grandfather's trees a little column of smoke from 'the pern mill,' and was told that 'pern' was another name for the spool" (048-052). The smoke from the mill looked as if it came from the mountain itself, and Yeats records that a foreign sea-captain once asked him if Ben Bulben was a burning mountain — a childhood anecdote that captures how the mountain loomed in his early imagination as something mysterious and alive.

This childhood intimacy with the Sligo landscape made Ben Bulben a natural choice for his burial place. The mountain that had watched over his formative years would also watch over his grave. The decision to be buried at Drumcliff churchyard, in the mountain's shadow, was an act of imaginative return — the poet who had spent a lifetime building symbolic geographies in verse chose to end his physical journey in the real landscape that had inspired them all. The mountain functioned as what Yeats might have called a "permanent symbol" — a physical place that had been saturated with so much personal and poetic meaning that it had become, in his imaginative system, an image of eternal recurrence.
