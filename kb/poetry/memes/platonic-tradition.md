# Platonic Tradition

The Platonic tradition is the philosophical spine of Yeats's symbolic universe. From the Rose poems of the 1890s to the death-haunted late verse, Plato and Plotinus are the thinkers Yeats wrestles with most persistently — sometimes as allies, sometimes as adversaries. "Choose Plato and Plotinus for a friend," he writes in "The Tower" [047-249], only to reverse himself decades later in "The Delphic Oracle upon Plotinus": "I mock Plotinus' thought / And cry in Plato's teeth, / Death and life were not / Till man made up the whole" [047-560]. This movement — from reverence to rebellion, from Platonic transcendence to Yeatsian immanence — is one of the great arcs of his poetic development.

The Platonic tradition, as Yeats understood and used it, encompasses: (1) **Plato's theory of Forms** — the doctrine that the visible world is a shadow of eternal, unchanging archetypes, most famously rendered in Yeats's line "Plato thought nature but a spume that plays / Upon a ghostly paradigm of things" [047-400]; (2) **Plotinian emanationism** — the Neoplatonic vision of all existence flowing outward from the One through successive spheres of being, which Yeats adapted into his own system of gyres and lunar phases; (3) **Renaissance Neoplatonism** — the synthesis of Platonic philosophy with Hermeticism, magic, and Christian mysticism that reached Yeats through his Golden Dawn studies; and (4) **the Platonic Year** — the doctrine of cosmic cycles, the Great Year in which all things return, which Yeats invoked as a figure for historical recurrence: "So the platonic year / Whirls out new right and wrong, / Whirls in the old instead" [047-380].

## Origins and Sources

Yeats encountered the Platonic tradition through multiple channels:

- **Direct reading**: Yeats read Plato and Plotinus in translation throughout his life. Stephen MacKenna's celebrated translation of Plotinus's *Enneads* (1917–1930) was a particularly important source for the late philosophical poems.
- **Golden Dawn teachings**: The Hermetic Order of the Golden Dawn synthesised Platonic and Neoplatonic elements into its initiatory curriculum — the doctrine of correspondences, the ascent through the spheres, the soul's return to the One.
- **Shelley and Blake**: The Romantic Platonism of Shelley's "Hymn to Intellectual Beauty" and Blake's visionary idealism provided Yeats with an imaginative rather than scholastic access to Platonic ideas.
- **Porphyry**: Yeats drew from Porphyry's essay "On the Cave of the Nymphs" — "I have taken 'the honey of generation' from Porphyry's essay" [048-033] — and used the Neoplatonic allegory of the cave to structure his own thinking about the soul's descent into embodiment.

## Development Across Yeats's Career

Yeats's relationship with the Platonic tradition evolved dramatically:

**Early phase (1890–1914)**: Uncritical Platonism. The young Yeats embraced Platonic idealism as the philosophical justification for his own Symbolist poetics. The Rose is an eternal Form; the visible world is a veil that the symbolist poet pierces. "Eternal beauty wandering on her way" [031-016] is a Platonic Idea in poetic dress. There is no tension between Yeats and Plato at this stage — they are natural allies.

**Middle phase (1917–1925)**: Systematic adaptation. The composition of *A Vision* (1925) represents Yeats's most ambitious engagement with the Platonic tradition. He adapts Plotinus's scheme of emanation and return, Plato's myth of Er, and the Neoplatonic doctrine of the soul's descent through the spheres into a personal system of twenty-eight lunar phases, interpenetrating gyres, and the *Daimon*. But the adaptation is creative, not scholarly — Yeats borrows the architecture of Neoplatonism while filling it with his own content.

**Late phase (1925–1939)**: Rebellion and reconciliation. The late poems stage an explicit quarrel with Plato and Plotinus. In "The Tower," Yeats chooses them "for a friend" [047-249] as a deliberate renunciation of the sensual world. But in "The Delphic Oracle upon Plotinus" and "Among School Children," he rebels: the abstract Forms cannot account for the "blooming" of a chestnut tree, the dance of a living body. "Labour is blossoming or dancing where / The body is not bruised to pleasure soul" [047-400]. Yeats's late position is not a rejection of Platonism but a correction of it — an insistence that the eternal must be found *in* the temporal, not beyond it.

## The Platonic Year and the Gyres

One of Yeats's most distinctive adaptations of the Platonic tradition is his use of the **Platonic Year** — the doctrine, preserved in Plato's *Timaeus* and elaborated by later Neoplatonists, that the cosmos moves through a Great Year of approximately 26,000 years, after which all celestial bodies return to their original positions and history begins again. Yeats transforms this astronomical doctrine into a figure for historical recurrence:

> "So the platonic year / Whirls out new right and wrong, / Whirls in the old instead" [047-380]

The Platonic Year becomes, in Yeats's hands, the cosmic pattern that the gyres — his symbol of interpenetrating cones of opposing force — enact in historical time. Each civilisation is a gyre; each gyre runs its course in approximately two thousand years; the Platonic Year is the largest gyre of all.

## Scholarly Interpretation

Kathleen Raine, in *Yeats the Initiate* (1986), argues that Yeats's Platonism is not a literary metaphor but a genuine metaphysical commitment — that Yeats believed, as Plato and Plotinus believed, in the reality of eternal Forms accessible through visionary experience. Harold Bloom's *Yeats* (1970) takes a more sceptical view, reading Yeats's Platonism as a "mythology" — a system adopted not because it is true but because it is useful for poetry. The truth is probably somewhere between: Yeats was both a genuine believer in the Platonic vision and a pragmatist who recognised that "metaphors for poetry" are what a poet needs, whether the metaphysics is true or not.
