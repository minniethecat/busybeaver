## Introduction

The theme of apocalypse — the violent end of one historical era and the birth of another — is one of the most powerful and recurrent motifs in W. B. Yeats's mature poetry. Its most concentrated expression is the 1919 poem "[[the-second-coming|The Second Coming]]", but apocalyptic imagery permeates his work from the 1890s onward, drawing on Christian eschatology, Irish mythological cycles, and the systematic philosophy of history he developed in [[a-vision|A Vision]] (1925).

Yeats's apocalypse is distinctive in fusing the Christian expectation of a final revelation with a cyclical, pagan conception of history. The end he envisions is not a terminal event but a turning — what he calls "the widening gyre" [046-392] — in which one two-thousand-year era gives way violently to its opposite.

## "The Second Coming" — The Central Text

"The Second Coming" (first published in *The Dial*, 1920, later collected in *Michael Robartes and the Dancer*, 1921) is the definitive Yeatsian apocalypse. The poem opens with the famous image of centrifugal collapse:

> Turning and turning in the widening gyre<br/>
> The falcon cannot hear the falconer;<br/>
> Things fall apart; the centre cannot hold;<br/>
> Mere anarchy is loosed upon the world,<br/>
> The blood-dimmed tide is loosed, and everywhere<br/>
> The ceremony of innocence is drowned; [046-392–397]

The gyre — a spiralling cone central to the system of [[a-vision|A Vision]] — represents the trajectory of a historical era. As it widens, the centripetal force that held civilisation together dissolves. The "blood-dimmed tide" evokes both the First World War (still raw in 1919) and the Irish War of Independence, which had begun the same year.

The poem's middle section turns inward, to a visionary moment drawn from [[spiritus-mundi|Spiritus Mundi]] — the collective unconscious or world-soul that Yeats believed all human minds shared:

> The Second Coming! Hardly are those words out<br/>
> When a vast image out of Spiritus Mundi<br/>
> Troubles my sight: somewhere in sands of the desert<br/>
> A shape with lion body and the head of a man,<br/>
> A gaze blank and pitiless as the sun, [046-401–405]

This sphinx-like figure — part beast, part human — is the antithesis of the Christian Christ-child. Where the first coming brought mercy and innocence, this "rough beast" brings blank indifference. The poem closes with one of the most famous couplets in twentieth-century poetry:

> And what rough beast, its hour come round at last,<br/>
> Slouches towards Bethlehem to be born? [046-412–413]

## Sources and Symbolism

### The Gyre and Cyclical History

The "widening gyre" is not merely a poetic metaphor but derives from the elaborate system of historical cycles Yeats outlined in *A Vision*. Each era lasts approximately two thousand years; the Christian era, inaugurated by the birth of Christ, is now reaching its end:

> That twenty centuries of stony sleep<br/>
> Were vexed to nightmare by a rocking cradle, [046-410–411]

The "rocking cradle" is the infant Jesus at Bethlehem, whose birth began a cycle that is now dissolving. The gyre of Christian civilisation has widened to its breaking point; a contrary gyre — bestial, anti-human, born in the desert rather than the manger — is beginning its ascent.

### The Sphinx and the Beast

Yeats's "rough beast" draws on multiple sources: the sphinx of Egyptian and Greek mythology; the beast of the Book of Revelation; and the "indignant desert birds" [046-408] that evoke both the waste land of T. S. Eliot's near-contemporary poem and the desert of the Levant where the original Christian revelation occurred. The creature's "gaze blank and pitiless as the sun" suggests a cosmos indifferent to human moral categories — an apocalypse that brings not judgment but simple replacement.

## Apocalyptic Themes in Other Yeats Poems

Beyond "The Second Coming", apocalypse appears in several other major poems. The 1896 "[[The-Rose|The Rose]]" volume already contains eschatological imagery drawn from the [[magic-and-occult|Rosicrucian]] tradition and Irish myth: the end of the world is figured in "The Valley of the Black Pig" as a climactic battle. The later "[[Lapis-Lazuli|Lapis Lazuli]]" (1938) offers a more serene vision of civilisational collapse, with "Old civilisations put to the sword" accepted as inevitable and even aesthetically necessary.

### Apocalypse in the Late Poems (1930s)

In the final decade of his life, Yeats's apocalyptic imagination deepened and darkened. The poems of *Last Poems* (1939) return repeatedly to the theme of civilisational death, but with a new note of personal implication: the end of an era is now also the end of the poet's own life. "The Gyres" (1938) opens with a stark acceptance — "The gyres! the gyres! Old Rocky Face, look forth" — and proceeds to catalogue the dissolution of all things with an almost ecstatic detachment. The refrain "What matter?" recurs through the poem, suggesting that the poet who once feared the rough beast has now become its herald.

"Lapis Lazuli" (1938), written in the shadow of the approaching Second World War, stages apocalypse as aesthetic spectacle. The poem moves from the hysteria of "hysterical women" who fear bombing raids, through the destruction of civilisations — "Hammered gold and gold enamelling" of Byzantium, "Old civilisations put to the sword" — to a closing image of three Chinamen carved in lapis lazuli, climbing a mountain toward "a little half-way house," gazing on "the tragic scene" below. This is the Yeatsian apocalypse fully realised: not an event to be feared or lamented, but one to be contemplated with the detachment of the artist. The destruction of worlds becomes material for tragic joy.

### Revelation as Personal Experience

Yeats also uses "revelation" in a personal, mystical sense. In the autobiographical passages of *The Trembling of the Veil* (1922), he describes moments of sudden insight:

> I was in my twenty-third year when some disturbing event brought me a strange revelation. [048-011]

And in his speculations on the afterlife, he suggests that the soul "has a moment of revelation immediately after death" [048-058]. These private apocalypses mirror the historical one: in Yeats's thought, the macrocosm of civilisation and the microcosm of the individual soul move through the same gyres.
