# Soul in Yeats

The soul in Yeats's work is the immortal, experiencing self — distinct from the mortal body and the merely personal self. It is the vehicle through which the individual participates in the Anima Mundi (World Soul), and it drives the antithetical dynamic at the heart of Yeats's philosophical system. The soul seeks its opposite, its Mask, through successive lives — a doctrine formalized in _A Vision_ (1925) but present in Yeats's poetry from the earliest work.

## Origins and Sources

- **Neoplatonism**: The soul's emanation from and return to the One, mediated through Plotinus, whom Yeats read deeply
- **Irish fairy-lore**: The fairy as "soulless" ((001-002)) — a being of pure nature, lacking the Christian soul but possessing a different kind of animating spirit
- **The Anima Mundi**: Yeats's concept of a World Soul or collective unconscious, developed in _Per Amica Silentia Lunae_ (1918), where all individual souls participate in a shared spiritual substance
- **Golden Dawn / occultism**: Ritual practices for contacting the Higher Self, the "Holy Guardian Angel" of the Hermetic tradition

## Symbolic Meaning

1. **Soul vs. Self**: Yeats distinguishes the soul from the everyday self. "My soul" ((003-085)) speaks with a different voice — darker, older, more truthful than the social personality.

2. **The antithetical soul**: In Yeats's system, each soul seeks its opposite — its Mask or Daimon. This pursuit generates the creative tension that drives both art and personal evolution.

3. **The fairy's lack of soul**: Fairies are "soulless" ((001-002), (001-014)) — they live forever but lack the Christian soul that can be saved or damned. This makes them figures of pure, amoral vitality.

4. **The soul's decay**: "I hear my soul drop down into decay" ((015-637)) — the soul can degenerate, fall, or be lost. Spiritual decline is a recurring Yeatsian anxiety.

## Development Across Yeats's Career

**Early period**: The soul is embedded in Irish folklore — fairies without souls, souls in purgatory, the soul "in love with" the supernatural. The vocabulary is Catholic but the sensibility is pagan.

**Middle period**: The soul becomes philosophical. _Per Amica Silentia Lunae_ (1918) develops the Anima Mundi doctrine — all souls share a common reservoir of images and memories. Poetry written in this period increasingly treats the soul as a participant in cosmic cycles.

**Late period**: The soul is systematized in _A Vision_ (1925), where it moves through the phases of the moon, seeking its antithetical Mask. Late poems treat the soul with hard-won equanimity — neither the rapturous soul of the early work nor the anxious soul of the middle period, but a soul that has "cast a cold eye."
