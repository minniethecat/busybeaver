## Introduction

The idealisation of aristocracy — particularly the Anglo-Irish Protestant Ascendancy — is a defining element of W. B. Yeats's mature thought. For Yeats, the aristocratic great house represented not merely a social class but a spiritual and aesthetic ideal: a way of life governed by ceremony, courtesy, custom, and noblesse oblige, standing against what he saw as the vulgarising forces of modern democracy and commercial society.

This aristocratic vision is most fully articulated in poems such as "[[a-prayer-for-my-daughter|A Prayer for My Daughter]]" (1919), "[[Coole-Park-1929|Coole Park, 1929]]", and "[[ancestral-houses|Ancestral Houses]]" (the opening poem of *Meditations in Time of Civil War*, 1923), and is embodied in the figure of [[lady-gregory|Lady Augusta Gregory]] and her estate at [[coole-park|Coole Park]], County Galway.

## Ceremony, Custom, and Courtesy

The core of Yeats's aristocratic ideal is expressed in three interrelated virtues: ceremony, custom, and courtesy. In "A Prayer for My Daughter", written for his infant daughter Anne in 1919, Yeats sets out the qualities he wishes for her — qualities he explicitly associates with the aristocratic tradition:

> In courtesy I'd have her chiefly learned; [046-446]

and later, imagining her future:

> How but in custom and in ceremony<br/>
> Are innocence and beauty born? [046-490–491]

The answer to the question is the aristocratic great house itself, where life is ordered by inherited ritual:

> Where all's accustomed, ceremonious; [046-487]

The poem links the destruction of ceremony — "The ceremony of innocence is drowned" [046-397] — to the apocalyptic chaos of the present age, as described in "[[the-second-coming|The Second Coming]]". For Yeats, the loss of aristocratic order is inseparable from the loss of civilisational coherence.

### The Laurel and the Wind

The poem's closing stanza offers a sustained pastoral image of the aristocratic ideal:

> And may her bridegroom bring her to a house<br/>
> Where all's accustomed, ceremonious;<br/>
> For arrogance and hatred are the wares<br/>
> Peddled in the thoroughfares.<br/>
> How but in custom and in ceremony<br/>
> Are innocence and beauty born?<br/>
> Ceremony's a name for the rich horn,<br/>
> And custom for the spreading laurel tree. [046-486–493]

The "spreading laurel tree" evokes permanence and rootedness, drawing on classical associations (the laurel as Apollo's tree, symbol of poetic and heroic achievement). It also echoes the famous "great-rooted blossomer" of the chestnut tree in "[[Among-School-Children|Among School Children]]" — another image of organic, traditional life contrasted with modern fragmentation.

## Nobility and the Great House

Yeats repeatedly invokes nobility — "nobleness" and "that great nobleness" — as a quality he associates with aristocratic lineage. In "[[The-Friends-That-Have-It-I-Do-Wrong|The Friends That Have It I Do Wrong]]" he describes a figure of "high courtesy" [041-112], while elsewhere he celebrates "That nobleness made simple as a fire" [043-064].

The great house itself — Slievenamon in "[[Baile-and-Aillinn|Baile and Aillinn]]", Coole Park, Lissadell — functions as the architectural symbol of this tradition. In "Baile and Aillinn" (1903), the lovers meet at a great house:

> In their great house at Slievenamon. [044-045]

But the great house is fragile. In "[[meditations-in-time-of-civil-war|Meditations in Time of Civil War]]", written during the Irish Civil War (1922–23), Yeats confronts the possibility that the Ascendancy world he idealises is already being swept away. The tension between the ideal of permanence and the reality of historical change runs through all his aristocratic poetry.

## The Anglo-Irish Ascendancy as Model

For Yeats, the Anglo-Irish landed gentry provided a living model of the aristocratic virtues he valued. Unlike the English aristocracy, which he found decadent, the Irish Ascendancy had, in his view, produced exceptional individuals: [[Jonathan-Swift|Swift]], [[George-Berkeley|Berkeley]], [[Edmund-Burke|Burke]], [[Oliver-Goldsmith|Goldsmith]] — all figures he celebrated in "[[The-Seven-Sages|The Seven Sages]]" and "[[Blood-and-the-Moon|Blood and the Moon]]".

Lady Gregory (1852–1932) was the living embodiment of this ideal for Yeats. In the endnotes to his early poems, he acknowledges his debt to her scholarship and her collection of Irish folklore [048-005][048-015][048-035]. Her house at Coole Park became his spiritual home and the setting for some of his greatest poems.
