# Water Imagery

Water is one of the four elements in Yeats's symbolic system, identified in his endnotes as "everywhere the signature of the fruitfulness of the body" (048-015). It appears in his poetry as lake (Innisfree, Lough Gill), sea (the "warm sea" of the Rose, the "bitter sea" of mortality), well (the sacred well of Irish folklore), and fountain (the artificial water of Byzantium). Water is the element of generation, desire, the unconscious, and the maternal — opposed to the dry intellect of old age.

## Origins and Sources

- **Sligo landscape**: Lough Gill, the lakes of Co. Sligo, the Atlantic coast — water was the dominant element of Yeats's childhood landscape
- **Irish folklore**: Sacred wells (Tobar na nGealt, the Well of the Moon), the sea as threshold to the Otherworld (Tir na nÓg across the western ocean)
- **Esoteric tradition**: The four elements of Hermeticism, where Water corresponds to the emotional/astral plane
- **Symbolist poetry**: Mallarmé, Verlaine — water as mirror, as dissolution, as the fluidity of consciousness

## Symbolic Meaning

1. **The fruitful body**: Water is "the signature of the fruitfulness of the body" (048-015) — desire, sexuality, generation. It is the element of youth, opposed to the "dry" wisdom of age.

2. **The lake of escape**: Innisfree's "lake water lapping" is the call of authentic life away from "the roadway" and "the pavement grey" — water as refuge from urban modernity.

3. **The bitter sea**: The sea also represents mortality, loss, the passage of time. The "warm sea" from which the Rose emerges is also the sea that separates the living from Tir na nÓg.

4. **The well of wisdom**: Sacred wells in Irish folklore are sources of wisdom and healing. The well in Yeats's system is the access point to the unconscious, the Anima Mundi, the feminine source of vision.

## Development Across Yeats's Career

**Early period**: Water is the element of the Celtic twilight — dreamy, atmospheric, the lake isle of escape and reverie. Innisfree (1888) sets the template.

**Middle period**: Water becomes philosophically significant. The endnotes to _The Tower_ systematize water as a cosmic element signifying the body and generation.

**Late period**: Water is what the aging poet must transcend. "Sailing to Byzantium" and "Byzantium" posit water (the sea of generation, "Those dying generations") against the dry, golden artifice of eternity. Yet the transcendence is never complete — the golden bird still sings beside the sea.
