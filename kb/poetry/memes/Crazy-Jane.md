# Crazy Jane

Crazy Jane is the most vivid of the dramatic personae — or "masks" — that W. B. Yeats created in the final phase of his career. An old woman, poor, sexually frank, and theologically belligerent, Jane appears in a sequence of seven poems in the 1932 collection *Words for Music Perhaps* (later incorporated into *The Winding Stair and Other Poems*, 1933). She represents, in Yeats's own symbolic vocabulary, the "anti-self" of the Nobel laureate senator: where the public Yeats was decorous, Jane is bawdy; where he brooded on the decay of the body, she celebrates desire; where he pondered theology as a system, she argues with the bishop directly.

## The Anti-Self Made Flesh

In the doctrine of the [[mask-theory|Mask]] that Yeats developed in *[[a-vision|A Vision]]* and elaborated throughout his late work, every self seeks its opposite — the "anti-self" that completes it. The doctrine was not only philosophical but practical: throughout his career, Yeats created dramatic personae — [[michael-robartes]], [[owen-aherne]], Crazy Jane, the [[Wild-Old-Wicked-Man]] — who gave voice to perspectives antithetical to his own.

Crazy Jane is the Mask for the aging poet in its most concentrated form. Where Yeats himself wrote poems of anguished meditation on bodily decay — "An aged man is but a paltry thing, / A tattered coat upon a stick" [047-009–010] — Jane speaks with defiant, unashamed vitality. She is everything the senator and Nobel laureate was not: female, poor, uneducated, sexually uninhibited, and utterly indifferent to the opinion of bishops or polite society.

## The Crazy Jane Poems

The Crazy Jane sequence — seven poems first published together in *Words for Music Perhaps* (1932) — presents Jane in a series of confrontations and colloquies that explore her distinctive theology of the body:

- **"Crazy Jane and the Bishop"**: Jane confronts the bishop who tells her to "live in a heavenly mansion" rather than in her "foul sty." Her reply is the sequence's theological keynote: "Fair and foul are near of kin, / And fair needs foul," she insists — a doctrine of necessary opposites that mirrors Yeats's own philosophy. The bishop's disembodied heaven is, for Jane, no heaven at all.

- **"Crazy Jane Reproved"**: Jane is reproved for her raucous behavior, and her response — "Fol de rol, fol de rol" — is a deliberate refusal of solemnity, an insistence on the value of what the decorous world dismisses.

- **"Crazy Jane on the Day of Judgment"**: Jane imagines the Last Judgment not as a reckoning of sin but as a reunion of lovers — "Love is all / Unsatisfied / That cannot take the whole / Body and soul" — a characteristically Yeatsian refusal to separate physical love from spiritual meaning.

- **"Crazy Jane Grown Old Looks at the Dancers"**: This poem directly parallels Yeats's own late meditations on the dance as a symbol of unity of being. Jane, old and watching young dancers, finds in their movement an image of the wholeness that age has taken from her.

- **"Crazy Jane Talks with the Bishop"**: The bishop returns, and Jane's rebuttal is again a defence of the body's sacredness against the abstractions of clerical morality.

The sequence's cumulative effect is a theology of embodied life — a gospel according to Crazy Jane — in which the body is not a prison for the soul but its necessary partner, and in which "nothing can be sole or whole / That has not been rent."

## Jane's Theology

Jane's most important contribution to the Yeatsian universe is her theology of necessary opposites. In her argument with the bishop, she insists that love and the body cannot be separated from the sacred — a position that echoes Yeats's lifelong resistance to the Platonic and Christian denigration of physical existence. But Jane goes further than Yeats could in his own voice: her poverty, her female body, and her complete freedom from the constraints of reputation allow her to speak truths that the decorous poet could only hint at.

## The Body and the Sacred

The theological core of the Crazy Jane sequence is Jane's insistence on the inseparability of body and soul. This was not merely a poetic conceit but a serious philosophical position that Yeats had been developing for decades. In his system, as articulated in *A Vision*, the antithetical self — the Mask — is not a disembodied ideal but an incarnate reality, accessible only through the full engagement of the senses. Jane is the most radical embodiment of this principle in Yeats's poetry: an old woman whose insistence on the sacredness of her own desiring body challenges both the bishop's Christian asceticism and the decorum of respectable society.

Jane's argument with the bishop replays, in dramatic form, a conflict that runs through all of Yeats's work: the conflict between the abstract and the incarnate, between the "heavenly mansion" and the "foul sty." When Jane declares that "fair needs foul," she is restating, in the idiom of the ballad and the street song, the same doctrine that Yeats had expressed philosophically in *A Vision* and poetically in "Sailing to Byzantium": that the condition of human wholeness is the acceptance of contrariety, not its transcendence. The bishop's heaven — a place where the body has been left behind — is, for Jane, no heaven at all. "Love is all / Unsatisfied," she declares, "That cannot take the whole / Body and soul." This is Yeats's own conviction, given a voice that neither the senator nor the Nobel laureate could provide.
