# Maud Gonne

Maud Gonne (1866–1953) was an Irish nationalist, actress, and political activist — and the single most important person in Yeats's imaginative life. He met her in 1889 and was immediately overwhelmed: "I had never thought to see in a living woman so great beauty." He proposed marriage to her repeatedly over the next 20 years; she refused each time, marrying Major John MacBride in 1903. She is the Rose, the Phoenix, Helen of Troy, and the "she" of countless poems written across more than half a century.

## Introduction

Maud Gonne was an English-born woman who embraced Irish nationalism and became one of its most charismatic advocates. She founded Inghinidhe na hÉireann (Daughters of Ireland), performed in Irish nationalist plays, and campaigned tirelessly for Irish independence. For Yeats, she embodied the fusion of beauty and political conviction that defined his ideal — but also the frustration of that ideal, since her political commitments (and eventual marriage to MacBride) kept her unattainable. The unrequited love was, paradoxically, Yeats's greatest creative asset: the poetry it generated far exceeds what consummation could have produced.

## Key Facts

- **Born**: 1866, Aldershot, England
- **Died**: 1953, Dublin
- **Role**: Irish nationalist, actress, muse
- **Connection to Yeats**: Lifelong love, muse, and unattainable inspiration

## Significance in Yeats's Work

Gonne is the biographical reality behind Yeats's most charged symbols: the Rose, the Phoenix, Helen of Troy, the Countess Cathleen. The Rose poems (1893) transform her into a mystical figure — "Red Rose, proud Rose, sad Rose of all my days" (031-005) — while later poems acknowledge the real, flawed, political woman beneath the symbol. "No Second Troy" (1910) asks whether she could have been anything other than what she was: "Why should I blame her that she filled my days / With misery?"

After her marriage to MacBride (1903) and the Easter Rising (1916, in which MacBride was executed), Gonne's presence in the poetry shifts from romantic longing to elegiac distance. "A Prayer for My Daughter" (1921) imagines a different kind of beauty — gentle, domestic, "rooted in one dear perpetual place" — as a counter-image to Gonne's fierce, unattainable glamour. In the final poems, Gonne appears as an aged presence, still powerful, still distant, still generative of poetry.

## Further Context

Gonne's political activism — her involvement with French Boulangist circles, her campaigns against evictions, her work during the Irish War of Independence — is essential context for understanding her in Yeats's poetry. She was never merely a beautiful woman; she was a formidable political actor whose convictions Yeats both admired and feared. The tension in the love poems comes from this doubleness: she was at once the most beautiful woman he had ever seen and a political radical whose methods he sometimes deplored.

After 1916, Gonne lived largely in France and Dublin, remaining active in republican politics. She and Yeats maintained a complex friendship into old age. She survived him by 14 years, dying in 1953 at the age of 86.
