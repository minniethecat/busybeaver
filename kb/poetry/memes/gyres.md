# Gyres

The **gyre** (or **gyres**, also called "cones" or "spheres" in Yeats's prose) is the central structural symbol of Yeats's philosophical system in *A Vision*, representing cyclical historical movement as two interpenetrating cones rotating in opposite directions [048-056]. The image unites Yeats's lifelong preoccupations — the conflict of opposites, the recurrence of historical patterns, the soul's journey through incarnations — into a single geometric figure of extraordinary explanatory power.

## Definition

In *A Vision* (1925, revised 1937), Yeats described history as governed by "cones made up of revolving gyres intersecting each other at various angles" [048-056]. Each gyre expands from a point to a wide base while the opposite gyre contracts — one civilisation or era reaches its maximum expansion just as its opposite reaches its point of greatest contraction [048-058]. The intersection produces the turbulence of historical change: wars, revolutions, the birth of new religions, the death of old orders.

The gyre represents the conflict of opposites: subjective and objective, aristocratic and democratic, Christian and pagan. Each two-thousand-year historical era is marked out by these opposing gyres [048-058]. Yeats traced the pattern through Western history: the classical era (2000 BCE–1 CE) gave way to the Christian era (1 CE–2000 CE), and the Christian era, in its turn, is giving way — in the violence of the twentieth century — to a new, antithetical age whose character Yeats could only dimly glimpse.

## Origins and Development

Yeats developed the gyre theory through the automatic writing sessions he and his wife George conducted between 1917 and 1920, in the immediate aftermath of their marriage. George Hyde-Lees, whom Yeats had married shortly after Maud Gonne's final rejection, discovered during their honeymoon that she could produce automatic writing — pages of script dictated, she claimed, by spiritual "instructors" who wished to communicate a systematic philosophy through her. Yeats, initially sceptical, was soon convinced that the communications were genuine, and over the next three years the couple filled thousands of pages with the raw material that Yeats would spend the rest of his life organising into *A Vision*.

The gyre image was not, however, purely a product of the automatic writing. Yeats had long been drawn to cyclical theories of history — Vico's *corsi e ricorsi*, Nietzsche's eternal recurrence, Blake's dialectical contraries — and the gyre synthesised these influences into a visual symbol that could be grasped intuitively even by readers who rejected the occult apparatus that produced it. The gyre is, in this sense, Yeats's answer to the problem that had preoccupied him since his youth: how to give imaginative form to philosophical conviction.

## In the Poetry

The gyre appears directly in several of Yeats's most famous poems:

- **"The Second Coming"** (1920) — The poem opens with the image of a "widening gyre" in which "things fall apart; the centre cannot hold" [046-392]. This is the gyre of the Christian era expanding to its breaking point, preparing the birth of a new, terrible age. The falcon, turning in the widening gyre, loses contact with the falconer — an image of lost control that condenses the entire poem's vision of civilisational collapse into a single figure.

- **"Sailing to Byzantium"** (1928) — The speaker asks the sages to "perne in a gyre" and consume his heart away [047-019], using the gyre as a mechanism of spiritual transformation rather than historical epoch. Here the gyre is not destructive but purgative — it burns away the "dying animal" of the body to release the soul into the artifice of eternity.

- **"The Statues"** (1939) — Mentions "those gyres and cubes and midnight things" [047-1170], coupling the gyre with other geometric symbols of Yeats's system. The poem moves from Greek sculpture to Irish revolution, tracing the gyre of Western civilisation from Pythagoras to Pearse.

- **"A Dialogue of Self and Soul"** (1929) — The Self, having "pernned in the gyre" of incarnation [046-346], chooses to return to the world of becoming rather than escape into the Soul's pure contemplation. The gyre here is the mechanism of rebirth — the wheel of karma, given Yeats's characteristic geometric form.

## The Philosophical System

Yeats developed the gyre theory through automatic writing sessions with his wife George (1917–1920). The system posits:

| Element | Meaning |
|---------|---------|
| Primary gyre | Objectivity, order, social unity, democracy, the external world |
| Antithetical gyre | Subjectivity, individuality, aristocracy, art, the internal world |
| Point of intersection | A moment of crisis where one era gives way to the next |
| Thirteenth Cone | A sphere transcending the gyres entirely — the realm of ultimate spiritual freedom |

## Critical Assessment

The gyre system poses a perennial question for Yeats criticism: is it a genuine philosophy, or is it a poetic metaphor elevated to the status of cosmology? Yeats himself seemed uncertain. In the 1937 revision of *A Vision*, he famously confessed that he did not know whether the system was "a true revelation" or "a hard symbolic study" — and that the uncertainty was itself productive. The gyres, on this reading, are less a doctrine to be believed than a lens through which experience becomes intelligible: they give shape to the chaos of history, make pattern visible in the flux of event.

What is certain is the system's extraordinary fertility for Yeats's poetry. The gyre gave him a vocabulary — a set of images and relations — through which he could address the great subjects of his late work: the collapse of civilisation, the violence of political transformation, the relationship between the individual soul and the vast movements of history. Without the gyre, "The Second Coming" would be a conventional lament for a troubled time; with it, the poem becomes a vision of epochal crisis, a prophecy that has proved more accurate than its author could have imagined.
