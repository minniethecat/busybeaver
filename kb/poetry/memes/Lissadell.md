# Lissadell

Lissadell House is a neoclassical country house on the shores of Sligo Bay in Co. Sligo, Ireland. Built in the 1830s for the Gore-Booth family, Lissadell became, through W. B. Yeats's poetry, one of the emblematic great houses of the Anglo-Irish literary imagination — a place of beauty, youth, and tragic transformation.

## Yeats's Visit

Yeats visited Lissadell in 1894 at the invitation of the Gore-Booth family. He was twenty-nine years old. The two Gore-Booth daughters — Constance, then twenty-six, and Eva, then twenty-four — made a profound impression on him. Yeats later recalled "two girls in silk kimonos, both beautiful, one a gazelle" — the gazelle being the slender, ethereal Eva.

## "In Memory of Eva Gore-Booth and Con Markievicz"

In 1927, over thirty years after his visit, Yeats wrote his great elegy for the sisters. The poem opens with a luminous memory of Lissadell:

> The light of evening, Lissadell,
> Great windows open to the south,
> Two girls in silk kimonos, both
> Beautiful, one a gazelle.

This remembered beauty is set against the later fates of the sisters — Constance as a revolutionary condemned to death, Eva as a pacifist and social reformer in industrial Manchester — and against the poet's own ageing. The poem's lament that "the innocent and the beautiful / Have no enemy but time" links Lissadell to Yeats's larger meditation on the passage of time and the erosion of the aristocratic world.

## The House

Lissadell was designed by the architect Francis Goodwin and completed in 1833. Its Greek Revival portico, with four massive Doric columns, looks out across Sligo Bay towards Knocknarea and Ben Bulben — the same landscape that shapes Yeats's poetry of the west of Ireland. The house remained in the Gore-Booth family until 2003.
