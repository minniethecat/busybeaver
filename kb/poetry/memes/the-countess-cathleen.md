# The Countess Cathleen

*The Countess Cathleen and Various Legends and Lyrics* (1892) is the volume that established W. B. Yeats as a major voice in fin-de-siècle poetry. Published when Yeats was twenty-seven, it marks the transition from the Pre-Raphaelite dreaminess of *The Wanderings of Usheen* (1889) toward a more dramatic, more Irish, and more morally complex mode. The title work — a blank-verse drama of a Countess who sells her soul to demon-merchants to save her people from famine — drew on Irish folklore, the Faust tradition, and Yeats's deepening engagement with the occult, while the shorter "Legends and Lyrics" that accompany it include some of his most enduring early poems.

## Overview

The title drama, *The Countess Cathleen*, is the most substantial work in the volume and the first of Yeats's plays written specifically for the Irish stage — though it would not receive its first performance until 1899, at the Irish Literary Theatre in Dublin. The plot is drawn from a story Yeats found in *Fairy and Folk Tales of the Irish Peasantry* (1888): during a famine, demon-merchants offer gold for souls, and the starving peasantry sell. The Countess Cathleen, an aristocratic woman of saintly compassion, sells her own soul to redeem theirs — and is saved, at the moment of death, by divine intervention because her intention was pure.

Yeats later renamed one of the poems from this collection "The Countess Cathleen in Paradise" [048-008], a lyric that envisions the Countess in the afterlife among Mary and the saints. The title change — from "A Dream of a Blessed Spirit," its name in the 1892 edition — reflects Yeats's long engagement with the Countess figure, whom he associated with Maud Gonne's beauty and her activist compassion for the Irish poor.

## Themes and Motifs

The volume's central thematic cluster is **sacrifice and redemption**, embodied in the Countess's act of self-immolation and her miraculous salvation. This Christianised version of the Faust story — where the soul-seller is saved rather than damned — is characteristically Yeatsian in its insistence that intention transforms the moral quality of an act. The Countess sells her soul, but because she does so for others, "the very demons" themselves are thwarted.

The shorter lyrics explore the **Rose symbolism** that would dominate Yeats's next collection ([[the-rose-collection|The Rose]], 1893). "The Rose of the World" — with its famous opening, "Who dreamed that beauty passes like a dream?" — establishes the Rose as the symbol of eternal, unchanging beauty that survives the flux of time. This is also the volume in which Yeats perfected the **lyric of unrequited love**, with "When You Are Old" — a poem based on Ronsard that becomes, in Yeats's handling, a devastatingly tender address to [[maud-gonne]].

Finally, the volume is saturated with **Irishness** — not yet the hard-edged political Irishness of *Responsibilities* (1914), but the atmospheric Irishness of the Celtic Twilight. The demons in *The Countess Cathleen* are Irish demons; the famine is the Irish famine; and the landscape throughout is the landscape of [[sligo-county]] and the west.

## The Countess and Maud Gonne

Yeats met Maud Gonne in 1889, and the encounter transformed his life and poetry. Gonne — a strikingly beautiful English-born Irish nationalist, six feet tall, a political activist and a public speaker of mesmeric power — became, for Yeats, the living embodiment of the Countess Cathleen. The dedication of the volume to Gonne was a declaration of love, and the play itself is, among other things, a fantasy of Gonne's own dedication to the Irish cause translated into the language of sanctity.

The relationship between Yeats and Gonne — decades of unrequited love, multiple proposals, her marriage to [[john-macbride]], her widowhood, her refusal to the end — is one of the great biographical dramas of modern literature. *The Countess Cathleen* is its first major monument.
