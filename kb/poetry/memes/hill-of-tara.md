# Hill of Tara

The Hill of Tara (Irish: _Teamhair na Rí_, "Hill of the Kings") in Co. Meath was the ancient seat of the High Kings of Ireland — a complex of Neolithic and Iron Age earthworks that served as the ceremonial and political center of Gaelic Ireland for over a millennium. In Yeats's poetry, Tara symbolizes a lost unity: the Ireland that existed before the Norman invasion, before colonization, before the sectarian divisions of the modern era.

## Location and Geography

- **Type**: real Irish location (archaeological complex)
- **Region**: Co. Meath, Leinster
- **Key monuments**: the Mound of the Hostages (passage tomb), the Royal Seat (Lia Fáil, the Stone of Destiny), the Banqueting Hall

## Significance to Yeats

Tara appears in Yeats's work as a metonym for Ireland itself — specifically, for the Ireland of myth and legend, before history became a chronicle of defeat and dispossession. In _The Wanderings of Usheen_ and related poems, the heroes and kings associated with Tara (Cormac mac Airt, the Fianna) represent a golden age of Gaelic civilization.

For Yeats and other figures of the Irish Literary Revival, Tara was charged with nationalist significance. The 1798 United Irishmen's rebellion had culminated in a battle at Tara; Daniel O'Connell held monster meetings there in the 1840s. Yeats inherited this tradition of Tara as a site of Irish political aspiration, but he translated it into mythological rather than directly political terms.

The Lia Fáil (Stone of Destiny) at Tara was said to cry out when touched by the rightful king — a symbol of the connection between landscape and sovereignty that permeates Yeats's understanding of place.

## Literary and Historical Context

Tara's symbolic power in Irish nationalism coexists with its archaeological reality: a complex of earthworks dating from the Neolithic through the Iron Age. Yeats, like other Revival writers, was more interested in the symbolic than the archaeological Tara. The hill represented what Ireland had been and might become again — a unified, self-governing nation rooted in its own traditions.
