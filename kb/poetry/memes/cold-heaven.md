# Cold Heaven

"The Cold Heaven" is both the title of a 1914 poem from W. B. Yeats's *Responsibilities* collection and a recurring image in his poetry — a winter sky that is not merely a weather phenomenon but a visionary state in which the soul confronts its own guilt, memories, and the possibility of divine judgement. The poem crystallises one of Yeats's central preoccupations: the moment of supernatural vision that strips away ordinary consciousness and leaves the self exposed to a reality it cannot control.

## The Poem

"The Cold Heaven" is a short, intense poem of twelve lines, written in a single sentence that accumulates through enjambment. It begins with a sudden vision — "Suddenly I saw the cold and rook-delighting Heaven" [044-913] — and moves through a series of paradoxical images towards a question about the afterlife:

> "Suddenly I saw the cold and rook-delighting Heaven / That seemed as though ice burned and was but the more ice" [044-913]–[044-914]

The opening lines establish the poem's central paradox: a heaven that is both burning and freezing, both illuminated and obscuring. The vision drives "imagination and heart" to such wildness that "every casual thought of that and this / Vanished" [044-915]–[044-917], leaving only memories "that should be out of season / With the hot blood of youth, of love crossed long ago" [044-917]–[044-918].

The speaker takes "all the blame out of all sense and reason" [044-919], trembling and weeping under a sky "Riddled with light" [044-921]. The poem then turns to its culminating question:

> "Ah! when the ghost begins to quicken, / Confusion of the death-bed over, is it sent / Out naked on the roads, as the books say, and stricken / By the injustice of the skies for punishment?" [044-921]–[044-924]

This question — whether the soul after death is exposed, naked and without protection, to a punishing sky — is left unanswered, its very open-endedness being the poem's theological force.

## Key Imagery

- **The rook-delighting Heaven**: Rooks (corvids) are birds of omen and battlefield scavengers; a heaven that delights them is a heaven of harshness, not comfort.
- **Ice that burns**: The paradoxical fusion of cold and heat — a characteristic Yeatsian image of supernatural intensity, where opposites meet.
- **Riddled with light**: The sky is pierced through with illumination, like a body riddled with arrows or bullets — light as wound.
- **The ghost on the roads**: The soul after death, stripped of bodily protection, exposed to the "injustice of the skies."

## Place in Yeats's System

The Cold Heaven belongs to the realm of what Yeats, in *A Vision*, called the "Beatific Vision" — the moment of revelation that follows death, described in the Endnotes as a state in which the soul is "carried into the presence of all his dead kindred" [048-058]. But in this poem, the revelation is not beatific but terrifying: the sky is not welcoming but accusing, and the vision is not of unity but of guilt.

The cold sky recurs throughout Yeats's poetry as a site of existential exposure. In "The Wild Swans at Coole," the swans "paddle in the cold" [045-024]; in later poems, the "pure cold light in the sky" [045-1135] and "cold spirits that are born" [045-1220] extend the image of a heaven that offers not warmth but a clarifying, often painful, truth.
