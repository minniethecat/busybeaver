# Magic and the Occult

Magic for Yeats was not a decorative interest but a central intellectual commitment. His membership in the Hermetic Order of the Golden Dawn (from 1890), his study of Rosicrucianism, his experiments with spiritualism and automatic writing (with his wife Georgie), and his reading of Neoplatonic and Hermetic texts all fed directly into his poetry. The "magic" in Yeats is not escapist fantasy but a systematic effort to access what he called "the other world" — a reality more real than the material world perceived by the senses.

## Origins and Sources

- **The Hermetic Order of the Golden Dawn**: Yeats joined in 1890 (taking the motto _Demon est Deus Inversus_) and remained involved for decades. The Order's ritual magic, kabbalistic symbolism, and hierarchical initiations provided the structural model for his later philosophical system
- **Rosicrucianism**: The Rose Cross — symbol of spiritual perfection through esoteric knowledge — pervades the Rose poems and the 1890s mysticism
- **Spiritualism and automatic writing**: Yeats's wife Georgie Hyde-Lees began automatic writing in 1917, producing the material that became _A Vision_ (1925). Yeats treated these communications as genuine transmissions from spiritual intelligences
- **Neoplatonism**: Plotinus, Porphyry, and the tradition of emanation and return — the soul's journey from the One, through the spheres, and back

## Symbolic Meaning

1. **The Golden Dawn as mental discipline**: Ritual magic trained the imagination to visualize symbols with intense clarity — a skill directly transferable to poetic composition. Yeats's famous "symbols" (gyre, rose, tower, mask) were first encountered in occult contexts.

2. **Automatic writing as muse**: Georgie's automatic scripts gave Yeats the raw material of _A Vision_ — the phases of the moon, the gyres of history, the antithetical Mask. The occult was literally generative of his philosophical system.

3. **Magic vs. science**: Yeats consistently opposed the occult imagination to scientific rationalism. Magic preserved what modernity destroyed: the sense of a living, meaningful cosmos in which human consciousness participates.

4. **The other world**: All Yeats's occult practices aimed at contacting "the other world" — whether conceived as the Anima Mundi, the realm of the dead, or the Platonic world of Forms. Poetry itself was a form of magic, an evocation.

## Development Across Yeats's Career

**Early period (1885–1899)**: Occult symbolism dominates — the Rose, the Cross, the elemental correspondences. Poems like "To the Secret Rose" are explicitly Rosicrucian invocations.

**Middle period (1900–1917)**: The occult systematizes. Yeats moves from ritual practice to philosophical construction — _Per Amica Silentia Lunae_ (1918) and the automatic writing sessions with Georgie produce a coherent occult system.

**Late period (1918–1939)**: _A Vision_ (1925, revised 1937) is the culmination — a complete cosmology based on the phases of the moon, the antithetical gyres, and the Mask/Daimon dynamic. Late poems like "Byzantium" and "Sailing to Byzantium" are dense with this system's symbols, though they work as poetry even without the occult key.
