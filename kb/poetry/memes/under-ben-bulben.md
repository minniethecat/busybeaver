# Under Ben Bulben

"Under Ben Bulben" is one of the last poems W. B. Yeats ever wrote. Composed in 1938, the year before his death, it was first published in *Last Poems and Two Plays* (1939). The poem is a testament, a last will and testament in verse — part manifesto ("Irish poets, learn your trade"), part meditation on mortality, part self-written epitaph. Its closing lines — "Cast a cold eye / On life, on death. / Horseman, pass by!" — are carved on Yeats's gravestone in Drumcliff churchyard, at the foot of Ben Bulben mountain in [[sligo-county|County Sligo]], where the poet is buried. The poem thus stands not only as a work of art but as Yeats's chosen final word, the text by which he wished to be remembered.

## Structure and Argument

The poem falls into five distinct movements. The opening section summons the "Sages" of pre-Christian Ireland — "Swear by what the sages spoke / Round the Mareotic Lake" [044-303] — calling on the wisdom of ancient philosophers to witness the poet's testament. The invocation of the Mareotic Lake, associated with the desert fathers of early Christianity and with Plotinus's Neoplatonic academy, establishes the poem's ambition to speak from the intersection of pagan, Christian, and esoteric traditions.

The second section states the poem's aesthetic creed: "Irish poets, learn your trade, / Sing whatever is well made" [044-303]. Yeats commands the next generation to sing the substance of Irish life — not abstractions but "the peasantry, and then / Hard-riding country gentlemen" [044-303] — and to scorn the "base-born products of base beds" [044-303] of modern mediocrity. This is Yeats at his most aristocratic and hierarchical; the passage has been controversial since its first publication.

The third section shifts to a visionary register, drawing on the moon-phase symbolism of [[a-vision|A Vision]]. The poet sees "that pale, long-visaged company" [044-303] of the dead — the company of souls that have passed through the phases of the moon and achieved "the completed form" [044-303]. This is the Yeatsian afterlife, a realm of perfected spiritual beauty that the living can only gesture toward.

The fourth section — the personal turn — describes the poet's own death and burial: "Under Ben Bulben to the meet" [046-294], at the foot of the Sligo mountain where his great-grandfather had been rector. The mountain itself — the flat-topped limestone massif that dominates the Sligo skyline — becomes a symbol of permanence, of geological time indifferent to human vanity, and of Ireland itself. "No marble, no conventional phrase" [044-303] on the gravestone but the simple words of the epitaph.

The closing section is the epitaph itself, three lines of almost unbearable compression that have become among the most quoted in English poetry: "Cast a cold eye / On life, on death. / Horseman, pass by!" [044-303]

## Themes

**Art and mortality**: The poem is a meditation on what survives. The "poet's tower" [044-508] — Thoor Ballylee — is Yeats's monument in stone; the poem "Under Ben Bulben" is his monument in verse. Both are offered as bequests to Ireland, but the poem's closing lines undercut any pretension to permanence: the "cold eye" cast on "life, on death" is an eye that sees through both, that refuses to be consoled by either.

**Irish identity**: The poem's nationalist strain — "Sing the peasantry, and then / Hard-riding country gentlemen" — is complicated by its aristocratic disdain for the modern ("base-born products of base beds"). Yeats's Ireland is a fusion of the pagan (the "sages" at the Mareotic Lake), the esoteric (the moon-phases of *A Vision*), the aristocratic (the "hard-riding country gentlemen"), and the geographical (Ben Bulben itself). It is an Ireland of the imagination as much as an Ireland of fact.

**The epitaph as genre**: "Under Ben Bulben" belongs to a long tradition of poets writing their own epitaphs — Keats's "Here lies one whose name was writ in water," Rilke's "Rose, oh reiner Widerspruch" — but Yeats's version is characteristically self-assured. "Horseman, pass by!" is a dismissal as much as an invitation: the poet has said what he came to say; the living have their own business to attend to.
