# Irish Nationalism

Irish nationalism is the political ground-bass beneath the music of Yeats's poetry — a force that gave him his most intense personal relationships, his public platform, and his deepest ambivalences. He loved Maud Gonne, the nationalist agitator; he admired [[John-OLleary|John O'Leary]], the Fenian elder who taught him that "there are things a man must not do to save a nation"; he mourned the executed leaders of the Easter Rising in "Easter 1916," the poem in which nationalism and poetry achieve their most tense, most magnificent equilibrium. Throughout his career, Yeats wrestled with a question that Irish nationalism posed with unique intensity: what does the poet owe his nation — and what does the nation owe its poets?

## Definition

Irish nationalism, as Yeats encountered and engaged with it, was not a single doctrine but a spectrum of movements united by the aim of ending British rule in Ireland. It encompassed: (1) **constitutional nationalism** — the parliamentary tradition of Daniel O'Connell and Charles Stewart Parnell, seeking Home Rule through Westminster; (2) **physical-force republicanism** — the Fenian tradition of armed insurrection, culminating in the Easter Rising of 1916; (3) **cultural nationalism** — the Irish Literary Revival, the Gaelic League, and the effort to create a distinctively Irish national culture as the foundation for political independence; and (4) **revolutionary feminism** — represented by Maud Gonne and Constance Markievicz, who fused the nationalist and women's movements. Yeats's own position shifted across this spectrum throughout his life, but his deepest allegiance was always to cultural nationalism — the idea that "Ireland is a great imagination," not merely a political grievance.

## Origins and Sources

Yeats's Irish nationalism drew from personal, literary, and political sources:

- **John O'Leary**: The Fenian veteran who returned from exile in 1885 and became Yeats's mentor. O'Leary embodied a romantic, disinterested patriotism — nationalism as moral nobility rather than sectarian hatred. Yeats's famous line "Romantic Ireland's dead and gone, / It's with O'Leary in the grave" ("September 1913") is an elegy for this older, more generous nationalism.
- **Young Ireland tradition**: The poets of the 1840s — Thomas Davis, James Clarence Mangan — who had tried to create a national literature as the basis for national identity. Yeats both inherited and criticised this tradition: he shared its ambition but rejected its propagandistic simplifications.
- **Maud Gonne**: The living embodiment of nationalist intensity. Through Gonne, Yeats experienced nationalism not as an abstract idea but as a personal, painful, and beautiful force. She was, he wrote, "the troubling of my life."
- **The Easter Rising (1916)**: The event that transformed Yeats's relationship to Irish nationalism. Before 1916, he had grown sceptical of political nationalism; after the executions, he wrote "Easter 1916," acknowledging that the rebels had been "changed, changed utterly" — and that a "terrible beauty" had been born.

## Development Across Yeats's Career

Yeats's engagement with Irish nationalism passed through several distinct phases:

**Early phase (1885–1900)**: Cultural nationalism predominates. The young Yeats believed that "the only way to create a national literature is to create a nation." His work with the Irish Literary Society, the National Literary Society, and the early Abbey Theatre was all aimed at providing Ireland with an imaginative foundation — a cultural infrastructure — that would make political independence meaningful when it came.

**Middle phase (1900–1916)**: Disillusionment with politics. The Dublin lock-out of 1913, the controversy over *The Playboy of the Western World*, and the growing bitterness of the Home Rule crisis soured Yeats on popular nationalism. "September 1913" is the keynote poem of this phase: a bitter lament for the death of "Romantic Ireland" in the squabbles of the Catholic middle class. "Responsibilities" (1914) closes with the challenge: "In dreams begins responsibility."

**The Easter Rising (1916)**: The Rising shattered Yeats's disillusionment. "Easter 1916" registers the shock of recognition: the men and women he had dismissed as "motley" had been transformed by their sacrifice into figures of tragic dignity. The poem's refrain — "A terrible beauty is born" — acknowledges the Rising's power while refusing to reduce it to simple celebration or condemnation.

**Late phase (1919–1939)**: Nationalism absorbed into the system. In the poems of *The Tower* and *The Winding Stair*, nationalism is no longer a distinct theme but one thread in the larger Yeatsian tapestry — woven together with the gyres of history, the phases of the moon, and the meditations of an ageing man facing death. "Meditations in Time of Civil War" (1923) and "Nineteen Hundred and Nineteen" (1921) reflect the violence of the Anglo-Irish War and the Civil War with a cold, disillusioned clarity that is nonetheless deeply engaged.

## Scholarly Interpretation

Seamus Deane argues in *Celtic Revivals* (1985) that Yeats's nationalism is inseparable from his occultism: both are attempts to access a hidden order beneath the surface of modern life — one political, one spiritual. R. F. Foster's biography emphasises the extent to which Yeats's political involvements were shaped by personal relationships, particularly with Gonne and O'Leary, rather than by ideological commitment. Elizabeth Cullingford's *Yeats, Ireland and Fascism* (1981) examines the uncomfortable proximity between Yeats's late "aristocratic" politics and the European fascist movements of the 1930s — a proximity that Yeats himself acknowledged and partially repudiated.
