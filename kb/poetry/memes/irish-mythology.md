# Irish Mythology

Irish mythology is the imaginative ground upon which W. B. Yeats built his poetic world. From his earliest work to his last, the figures of the Ulster Cycle — [[cuchulain-hero|Cuchulain]], [[queen-maeve]], [[fergus-hero|Fergus mac Róich]] — and the Fenian Cycle — [[oisin]], [[niamh-goddess|Niamh]] — walk through his stanzas not as antiquarian curiosities but as living presences, "the gods of ancient Ireland, the *Tuatha De Danaan*" [048-013]. For Yeats, these stories were not merely literary material: they were evidence of a distinctively Irish mode of imagination — visionary, aristocratic, anti-materialist — that could counter the "filthy modern tide" of commercial modernity.

## Definition

Irish mythology, as Yeats understood and deployed it, comprises three great story-cycles preserved in medieval Irish manuscripts: the **Mythological Cycle** (the *Tuatha Dé Danann*, the invasions of Ireland, the Battle of Moytura), the **Ulster Cycle** (Cuchulain, Queen Maeve, Fergus, the *Táin Bó Cúailnge*), and the **Fenian Cycle** (Oisin, Finn mac Cumhaill, the *Agallamh na Seanórach*). To these Yeats added the folk and fairy lore that he collected with [[lady-gregory]] in the west of Ireland — stories of the *Sidhe*, the fairy people who are, in the Yeatsian reading, the diminished remnant of the ancient gods.

What distinguishes Yeats's use of Irish mythology from that of his contemporaries in the Irish Literary Revival — Standish O'Grady, Douglas Hyde, J. M. Synge — is his insistence on treating the mythological figures not as characters in a national epic but as symbols in a personal system. Cuchulain is both the hero of Ulster and a type of the Yeatsian tragic hero, fighting the waves to the death; Maeve is both the warrior-queen of Connacht and an image of the terrible, impersonal beauty that Yeats celebrated in his later poems.

## Origins and Sources

Yeats encountered Irish mythology through multiple channels:

- **Literary sources**: Standish James O'Grady's *History of Ireland* (1878–1880) supplied the core narratives from the Ulster and Fenian Cycles. Yeats called O'Grady's work "the first great book to come out of Ireland in my time."
- **Folkloric sources**: Yeats's own collecting expeditions in Galway and Sligo, often with Lady Gregory, yielded stories of the *Sidhe*, changelings, and the fairy world that he published in *The [[celtic-twilight]]* (1893, enlarged 1902).
- **Occult synthesis**: Yeats read Irish myth through the lens of the Hermetic tradition, seeing in the *Tuatha Dé Danann* a parallel to the initiates of the ancient mysteries. "The gods of ancient Ireland" [048-013] were, in his interpretation, not pagan deities but members of a pre-Christian initiatic order — wisdom-teachers whose knowledge was preserved, in degraded form, in folk memory.

## Development Across Yeats's Career

Yeats's relationship with Irish mythology evolved across three phases:

**Early phase (1885–1900)**: The mythological poems of *The Wanderings of Usheen* (1889) and *The Rose* (1893) are lush, decorative, Pre-Raphaelite in their handling of the ancient stories. Oisin describes the "Druid dream" [015-210] in which he witnesses the end of the world; Fergus converses with a Druid who carries dreams in a "little bag" [031-059]. The mythology is a mood, an atmosphere of enchantment.

**Middle phase (1900–1919)**: The mythological material becomes more austere, more dramatic. The Cuchulain cycle of plays — *On Baile's Strand* (1904), *The Green Helmet* (1910), *At the Hawk's Well* (1916), *The Only Jealousy of Emer* (1919), *The Death of Cuchulain* (1939) — strips away the decorative manner and confronts the hero with the starkest existential choices. Mythology becomes a vehicle for tragic vision.

**Late phase (1919–1939)**: The mythological figures are absorbed into Yeats's personal symbolic system. Cuchulain is compared to the Easter 1916 rebels; the Sidhe become emblems of the "cold and rook-delighting heaven" of "The Cold Heaven" (1912). The ancient stories are no longer retold but alluded to, compressed into single lines that carry the weight of an entire tradition.

## Mythological Figures in Yeats's Personal System

The figures of Irish myth became, in Yeats's hands, types of permanent human possibility. He identified each with a phase of the moon in the schema of *[[a-vision]]*:

| Figure | Yeatsian Type | Key Poems |
|--------|--------------|-----------|
| [[Cuchulain-Hero\|Cuchulain]] | The tragic hero — fights the sea, knows his fate | "Cuchulain's Fight with the Sea," *The Death of Cuchulain* |
| [[queen-maeve]] | Terrible beauty — the feminine as power and destruction | *The Old Age of Queen Maeve* |
| [[Fergus-Hero\|Fergus]] | The king who abdicates — the choice of wisdom over power | "Fergus and the Druid" |
| [[oisin]] | The poet who returns — exile and belatedness | *The Wanderings of Usheen* |
| [[Niamh-Goddess\|Niamh]] | The immortal beloved — beauty that draws the mortal out of time | *The Wanderings of Usheen* |
| [[Aengus-God\|Aengus]] | The god of love — dreamer of the impossible beloved | "The Song of Wandering Aengus" |

## Irish Mythology and the Literary Revival

Yeats's mythological project was inseparable from the cultural politics of the Irish Literary Revival. By recovering and reanimating the pre-Christian stories, he aimed to provide Ireland with an imaginative foundation that was neither English nor Catholic — a "third Ireland" of the spirit. The Abbey Theatre, which he co-founded with Lady Gregory in 1904, was conceived as the stage upon which these mythological figures would walk again.

The political implications were not lost on Yeats's contemporaries. The Easter Rising of 1916, which Yeats both mourned and celebrated, was itself saturated with mythological reference — Patrick Pearse had written of Cuchulain as a model for the Irish rebel, and the statue of Cuchulain that stands in the General Post Office in Dublin commemorates the Rising in mythological terms. Yeats was ambivalent about this appropriation — "Did that play of mine send out / Certain men the English shot?" he wondered of *Cathleen ni Houlihan* — but the ambivalence was itself a sign of how deeply mythology and politics had become entangled in his imagination.
