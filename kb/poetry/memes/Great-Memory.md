# The Great Memory

The Great Memory is W. B. Yeats's name for what the Neoplatonic tradition calls the _Anima Mundi_ — the soul of the world, a universal consciousness in which all individual memories, images, and symbols are stored and from which the poet draws his deepest material. Yeats first encountered the concept through his reading of the Neoplatonists ([[plotinus]], Porphyry, Proclus) and later developed it, through his occult studies and his wife's automatic writing, into a central pillar of the philosophical system expounded in _[[a-vision]]_ (1925).

## Names and Sources

Yeats uses several terms for the same underlying concept, each with distinct connotations:

- **Anima Mundi**: The Latin "soul of the world," drawn from Plotinus via the Renaissance Neoplatonists. Yeats uses this term most frequently in his prose essays, particularly _Per Amica Silentia Lunae_ (1918).
- **Spiritus Mundi**: The Latin "spirit of the world," immortalised in the opening of "The Second Coming": "a vast image out of _Spiritus Mundi_ / Troubles my sight" [046-403]. Here the Great Memory releases archetypal images — the rough beast, the falcon, the desert birds — into the poet's vision.
- **The Great Memory**: Yeats's preferred term in _Per Amica Silentia Lunae_, emphasising the continuity of the storehouse across time — it is a memory because it contains all that has been, not merely all that is.

The concept draws on multiple traditions:
- [[platonic-tradition|Neoplatonic]] emanationism: the world-soul as intermediary between the One and the material cosmos
- [[Golden-Dawn|Golden Dawn]] kabbalism: the astral light as the medium of magical working
- Celtic folk belief: the "other world" of the Sidhe as a realm contiguous with human consciousness

## In Yeats's System

In _A Vision_, the Great Memory is the storehouse of the _Daimon_ — the spiritual opposite or anti-self that each individual soul must encounter and assimilate. The poetic imagination, for Yeats, is precisely the faculty that can access this storehouse. When the poet writes, he does not invent but _discovers_ — he pulls images from the Great Memory that already exist there, deposited by the collective experience of humanity.

This is why Yeats insists that the symbols that matter most are not personal but traditional: the rose, the tower, the gyre, the tree. These are not Yeats's inventions; they are discoveries from the Great Memory, images "that have been thought over and over" until they have acquired what he calls "the authority of the soul."

## Automatic Writing and the Great Memory

The most dramatic evidence for the Great Memory, in Yeats's view, came not from books but from the automatic writing of his wife, George Yeats. Beginning on 24 October 1917, four days after their marriage, George began producing pages of script in trance, purportedly dictated by spiritual "instructors." Over the next several years, thousands of pages of automatic writing accumulated — material that Yeats spent the rest of his life interpreting and systematising into *A Vision*.

For Yeats, the automatic writing was direct proof of the Great Memory's reality. The scripts contained elaborate geometrical diagrams, historical schemata, and philosophical doctrines that, he believed, neither he nor George could have invented from their own reading. The "instructors" — whom Yeats came to call the _Communicators_ — described themselves as discarnate spirits drawing on the universal storehouse of images, presenting material from the Great Memory in symbolic form. The gyre, the phases of the moon, the twenty-eight incarnations — all of the central images of *A Vision* emerged from these sessions, which Yeats understood as a controlled access to the world-soul.

The relationship between the automatic writing and the Great Memory also shaped Yeats's understanding of the poetic process. If the _Communicators_ could draw symbols from the Great Memory through trance, then the poet in a state of creative concentration was doing something analogous: not inventing images but _receiving_ them from the same universal source. The difference was one of degree, not kind.

## The Daimon

Each individual soul, in Yeats's system, has a counterpart in the Great Memory — the _Daimon_, Mask, or anti-self — which is the perfected image of what the living person might become. The Daimon is not a guardian angel but an adversary: it challenges, contradicts, and completes the self. The drama of the soul in Yeats's system is the struggle between the self and the anti-self, out of which the Unity of Being — the ideal state in which all faculties are harmonised — may emerge.

## Spiritus Mundi in the Poetry

The Great Memory enters Yeats's poetry most dramatically in the opening of "The Second Coming" (1919), where it appears under its Latin name *Spiritus Mundi*:

> "Surely some revelation is at hand;
> Surely the Second Coming is at hand.
> The Second Coming! Hardly are those words out
> When a vast image out of *Spiritus Mundi*
> Troubles my sight: somewhere in sands of the desert
> A shape with lion body and the head of a man,
> A gaze blank and pitiless as the sun,
> Is moving its slow thighs, while all about it
> Reel shadows of the indignant desert birds." [046-396–404]

This passage is the most famous poetic deployment of the Great Memory concept. The "vast image" that troubles the poet's sight does not originate in his personal imagination but rises up from the collective storehouse — the *Spiritus Mundi* — as an archetypal form. The rough beast, the desert birds, the "gaze blank and pitiless as the sun": these are not private symbols but images "that have been thought over and over" until they have acquired, as Yeats wrote in his essays, "the authority of the soul."

The context is significant. "The Second Coming" was written in the immediate aftermath of the First World War and the Russian Revolution, with the Irish War of Independence unfolding as Yeats composed. The Spiritus Mundi, under the pressure of historical catastrophe, releases not comforting images but terrifying ones — the "rough beast" that "slouches towards Bethlehem to be born." The Great Memory, in this poem, is not a refuge from history but the medium through which history's deepest patterns become visible. The images that rise from it are not chosen by the poet; they choose him, troubling his sight whether he wills it or not. This is the darker dimension of the Great Memory doctrine — the recognition that the storehouse of images contains nightmares as well as visions of beauty.
