## Introduction

County Sligo, in the province of Connacht on Ireland's north-western coast, is the psychic and poetic heartland of W. B. Yeats. Though born in Dublin and schooled partly in London, Yeats spent much of his childhood with his mother's family, the Pollexfens, in Sligo, and the county's landscape — its mountain, lake, sea, and stone — became the permanent imaginative ground of his poetry.

In the endnotes to his collected works, Yeats acknowledges this formative influence directly: "When I was a child at Sligo I could see above my grandfather's house..." [048-052]. The mountains, lakes, and folklore of Sligo shaped his earliest memories and, through them, his most enduring poetic images.

## Key Sligo Settings in Yeats's Poetry

### Innisfree

The most famous Sligo location in Yeats's work is the uninhabited island of Innisfree in Lough Gill:

> I will arise and go now, and go to Innisfree [031-247]

"The Lake Isle of [[innisfree-isle|Innisfree]]" (1890), first published in *The Rose* (1893), is a poem of escape and simplicity — the poet dreaming of building a small cabin and living alone on the island, away from "the pavements grey" of London. Yeats later wrote that the image came to him while walking through Fleet Street, triggered by a shop-window fountain that reminded him of the lake water of Sligo.

The island also appears in an earlier poem, where a figure "plucked small fruits on Innisfree" [016-005], linking it to the fairy-lore of the Danaan Quicken Tree.

### Ben Bulben

Ben Bulben, the distinctive flat-topped mountain that dominates the Sligo skyline, provides the title and setting for one of Yeats's last poems, "[[under-ben-bulben|Under Ben Bulben]]" (1938), which contains his self-written epitaph:

> Under Ben Bulben to the meet [046-294]

and closes with the famous lines:

> Cast a cold eye<br/>
> On life, on death.<br/>
> Horseman, pass by! [046-322–324]

Yeats is buried in the churchyard at [[Drumcliff|Drumcliff]], at the foot of Ben Bulben, as he had directed. The mountain also appears in the earlier poem "[[ben-bulben-in-yeats|Alternative Song for the Severed Head in 'The King of the Great Clock Tower']]", where the speaker declares "I climbed Ben Bulben's back" [047-041].

### Knocknarea

The mountain of Knocknarea, crowned with the megalithic cairn of Queen Maeve, opens one of Yeats's most haunting early lyrics:

> The host is riding from Knocknarea [038-001]

"The [[the-host-of-the-air|Host of the Air]]" (1899) draws on Sligo folklore — Yeats notes in his endnotes that "Knocknarea is in Sligo, and the country people say..." [048-015], recording the local belief that the fairy host rides out from the cairn at certain times of year. The name also appears in "[[The-Rose|The Rose]]" volume: "The birds from Knocknarea" [032-413].

### Sligo Town and Fair

Sligo town itself appears in more quotidian contexts — the ballad-like "[[A-Legend|A Legend]]" mentions "the take to Sligo town to be sold" [032-378], and a poem records a purchase "at the Sligo fair" [038-516]. These details ground the mythological landscape in the ordinary life of the western Irish countryside.

## The Sligo Landscape in Yeats's Imagination

For Yeats, Sligo was not merely scenery but a complete imaginative world — a place where the mythological past (Queen Maeve's cairn on Knocknarea, the fairy host, the Danaan Quicken Tree) coexisted with the present (the Sligo fair, the fishing boats, the country people). This fusion of the mythological and the ordinary became the signature of his mature style, and its origin lies in the Sligo of his childhood.

The Sligo landscape also provided the concrete imagery for Yeats's philosophical and symbolic system. The mountain, the lake-island, the wandering waters, and the stone became, in poems written decades later, symbols in the private mythology of *A Vision*.
