# In the Seven Woods

## In the Seven Woods

[041-001]I have heard the pigeons of the Seven Woods   

[041-002]Make their faint thunder, and the garden bees   

[041-003]Hum in the lime tree flowers; and put away   

[041-004]The unavailing outcries and the old bitterness   

[041-005]That empty the heart. I have forgot awhile   

[041-006][[hill-of-tara|Tara]] uprooted, and new commonness   

[041-007]Upon the throne and crying about the streets   

[041-008]And hanging its paper flowers from post to post,   

[041-009]Because it is alone of all things happy.   

[041-010]I am contented for I know that Quiet   

[041-011]Wanders laughing and eating her wild heart   

[041-012]Among pigeons and bees, while that Great Archer,   

[041-013]Who but awaits His hour to shoot, still hangs   

[041-014]A cloudy quiver over Parc-na-Lee.

[041-015]August 1902.

## The Arrow

[041-016]I thought of your beauty, and this arrow,   

[041-017]Made out of a wild thought, is in my marrow.   

[041-018]There’s no man may look upon her, no man;   

[041-019]As when newly grown to be a woman,   

[041-020]Tall and noble but with face and bosom   

[041-021]Delicate in colour as apple blossom.   

[041-022]This beauty’s kinder, yet for a reason   

[041-023]I could weep that the old is out of season.

## The Folly of Being Comforted

[041-024]One that is ever kind said yesterday:   

[041-025]“Your [[water-imagery|well]]-beloved’s hair has threads of grey,   

[041-026]And little shadows come about her eyes;   

[041-027]Time can but make it easier to be wise   

[041-028]Though now it seem impossible, and so   

[041-029]Patience is all that you have need of.” No,   

[041-030]I have not a crumb of comfort, not a grain,   

[041-031]Time can but make her beauty over again:   

[041-032]Because of that great nobleness of hers   

[041-033]The fire that stirs about her, when she stirs   

[041-034]Burns but more clearly. O she had not these ways,   

[041-035]When all the wild summer was in her gaze.   

[041-036]O heart! O heart! if she’d but turn her head,   

[041-037]You’d know the folly of being comforted.

## Old Memory

[041-038]O thought, fly to her when the end of day   

[041-039]Awakens an old memory, and say,   

[041-040]“Your strength, that is so lofty and fierce and kind,   

[041-041]It might call up a new age, calling to mind   

[041-042]The queens that were imagined long ago,   

[041-043]Is but half yours: he kneaded in the dough   

[041-044]Through the long years of youth, and who would have thought   

[041-045]It all, and more than it all, would come to naught,   

[041-046]And that dear words meant nothing?” But enough,   

[041-047]For when we have blamed the wind we can blame love;   

[041-048]Or, if there needs be more, be nothing said   

[041-049]That would be harsh for children that have strayed.

## Never Give All the Heart

[041-050]Never give all the heart, for love   

[041-051]Will hardly seem worth thinking of   

[041-052]To passionate women if it seem   

[041-053]Certain, and they never dream   

[041-054]That it fades out from kiss to kiss;   

[041-055]For everything that’s lovely is   

[041-056]But a brief dreamy kind delight.   

[041-057]O never give the heart outright,   

[041-058]For they, for all smooth lips can say,   

[041-059]Have given their hearts up to the play.   

[041-060]And who could play it well enough   

[041-061]If deaf and dumb and blind with love?   

[041-062]He that made this knows all the cost,   

[041-063]For he gave all his heart and lost.

## The Withering of the Boughs

[041-064]I cried when the moon was murmuring to the birds:   

[041-065]“Let peewit call and curlew cry where they will,   

[041-066]I long for your merry and tender and pitiful words,   

[041-067]For the roads are unending, and there is no place to my mind.”   

[041-068]The honey-pale moon lay low on the sleepy hill,   

[041-069]And I fell asleep upon lonely Echtge of streams.   

[041-070]No boughs have withered because of the wintry wind;   

[041-071]The boughs have withered because I have told them my dreams.

[041-072]I know of the leafy paths that the witches take,   

[041-073]Who come with their crowns of pearl and their spindles of wool,   

[041-074]And their secret smile, out of the depths of the lake;   

[041-075]I know where a dim moon drifts, where the Danaan kind   

[041-076]Wind and unwind their dances when the [[shadow-and-light|light]] grows cool   

[041-077]On the island lawns, their feet where the pale foam gleams.   

[041-078]No boughs have withered because of the wintry wind;   

[041-079]The boughs have withered because I have told them my dreams.

[041-080]I know of the sleepy country, where swans fly round   

[041-081]Coupled with golden chains, and sing as they fly.   

[041-082]A king and a queen are wandering there, and the sound   

[041-083]Has made them so happy and hopeless, so deaf and so blind   

[041-084]With wisdom, they wander till all the years have gone by;   

[041-085]I know, and the curlew and peewit on Echtge of streams.   

[041-086]No boughs have withered because of the wintry wind;   

[041-087]The boughs have withered because I have told them my dreams.

## Adam’s Curse

[041-088]We sat together at one summer’s end,   

[041-089]That beautiful mild woman, your close friend,   

[041-090]And you and I, and talked of [[song-and-poetry|poetry]].

[041-091]I said: “A line will take us hours maybe;   

[041-092]Yet if it does not seem a moment’s thought,   

[041-093]Our stitching and unstitching has been naught.   

[041-094]Better go down upon your marrow bones   

[041-095]And scrub a kitchen pavement, or break stones   

[041-096]Like an old pauper, in all kinds of weather;   

[041-097]For to articulate sweet sounds together   

[041-098]Is to work harder than all these, and yet   

[041-099]Be thought an idler by the noisy set   

[041-100]Of bankers, schoolmasters, and clergymen   

[041-101]The martyrs call the world.”

[041-102]And thereupon   

[041-103]That beautiful mild woman for whose sake   

[041-104]There’s many a one shall find out all heartache   

[041-105]On finding that her voice is sweet and low   

[041-106]Replied: “To be born woman is to know,   

[041-107]Although they do not talk of it at school﻿—   

[041-108]That we must labour to be beautiful.”

[041-109]I said: “It’s certain there is no fine thing   

[041-110]Since Adam’s fall but needs much labouring.   

[041-111]There have been lovers who thought love should be   

[041-112]So much compounded of high courtesy   

[041-113]That they would sigh and quote with learned looks   

[041-114]Precedents out of beautiful old books;   

[041-115]Yet now it seems an idle trade enough.”

[041-116]We sat grown quiet at the name of love;   

[041-117]We saw the last embers of daylight die,   

[041-118]And in the trembling blue-green of the sky   

[041-119]A moon, worn as if it had been a shell   

[041-120]Washed by time’s waters as they rose and fell   

[041-121][[About]] the stars and broke in days and years.

[041-122]I had a thought for no one’s but your ears;   

[041-123]That you were beautiful, and that I strove   

[041-124]To love you in the old high way of love;   

[041-125]That it had all seemed happy, and yet we’d grown   

[041-126]As weary hearted as that hollow moon.

## The Old Men Admirings Themselves in the Water

[041-127]I heard the old, old men say,   

[041-128]“Everything alters,   

[041-129]And one by one we drop away.”   

[041-130]They had hands like claws, and their knees   

[041-131]Were twisted like the old thorn trees   

[041-132]By the waters.   

[041-133]I heard the old, old men say,   

[041-134]“All that’s beautiful drifts away   

[041-135]Like the waters.”

## Under the Moon

[041-136]I have no happiness in [[dream-and-vision|dreaming]] of Brycelinde,   

[041-137]Nor Avalon the grass-green hollow, nor Joyous Isle,   

[041-138]Where one found Lancelot crazed and hid him for a while;   

[041-139]Nor Ulad, when Naoise had thrown a sail upon the wind;   

[041-140]Nor lands that seem too dim to be burdens on the heart:   

[041-141]Land-under-Wave, where out of the moon’s light and the sun’s   

[041-142]Seven old sisters wind the threads of the long-lived ones,   

[041-143]Land-of-the-Tower, where [[aengus-god|Aengus]] has thrown the gates apart,   

[041-144]And Wood-of-Wonders, where one kills an ox at dawn,   

[041-145]To find it when night falls laid on a golden bier.   

[041-146]Therein are many queens like Branwen and Guinivere;   

[041-147]And [[niamh-goddess|Niamh]] and Laban and Fand, who could change to an otter or fawn,   

[041-148]And the wood-woman, whose lover was changed to a blue-eyed hawk;   

[041-149]And whether I go in my dreams by woodland, or dun, or shore,   

[041-150]Or on the unpeopled waves with kings to pull at the oar,   

[041-151]I hear the harp-string praise them, or hear their mournful talk.

[041-152]Because of something told under the famished horn   

[041-153]Of the hunter’s moon, that hung between the night and the day,   

[041-154]To dream of women whose beauty was folded in dismay,   

[041-155]Even in an old story, is a burden not to be borne.

## The Ragged Wood

[041-156]O hurry where by water among the trees,   

[041-157]The delicate steeping stag and his lady sigh   

[041-158]When they have but looked upon their images,   

[041-159]Would none had ever loved but you and I!

[041-160]Or have you heard that sliding silver-shoed,   

[041-161]Pale silver-proud queen-woman of the sky,   

[041-162]When the sun looked out of his golden hood:   

[041-163]O that none ever loved but you and I!

[041-164]O hurry to the ragged wood, for there   

[041-165]I will drive all those lovers out and cry﻿—   

[041-166]O my share of the world, O yellow hair,   

[041-167]No one has ever loved but you and I!

## O Do Not Love Too Long

[041-168]Sweetheart, do not love too long:   

[041-169]I loved long and long,   

[041-170]And grew to be out of fashion   

[041-171]Like an old song.

[041-172]All through the years of our youth   

[041-173]Neither could have known   

[041-174]Their own thought from the other’s,   

[041-175]We were so much at one.

[041-176]But, O in a minute she changed﻿—   

[041-177]O do not love too long,   

[041-178]Or you will grow out of fashion   

[041-179]Like an old song.

## The Players Ask for a Blessing on the Psalteries and Themselves

[041-180]Three voices together |  Hurry to bless the hands that play,   

[041-181]The mouths that speak, the notes and strings,   

[041-182]O masters of the glittering town!   

[041-183]O! lay the shrilly trumpet down,   

[041-184]Though drunken with the flags that sway   

[041-185]Over the ramparts and the towers,   

[041-186]And with the waving of your wings.  

[041-187]---|---  

[041-188]First voice |  Maybe they linger by the way.   

[041-189]One gathers up his purple gown;   

[041-190]One leans and mutters by the wall﻿—   

[041-191]He dreads the weight of mortal hours.  

[041-192]Second voice |  O no, O no! they hurry down   

[041-193]Like plovers that have heard the call.  

[041-194]Third voice |  O kinsmen of the Three in One,   

[041-195]O kinsmen bless the hands that play.   

[041-196]The notes they waken shall live on   

[041-197]When all this heavy history’s done;   

[041-198]Our hands, our hands must ebb away.  

[041-199]Three voices together |  The proud and careless notes live on,   

[041-200]But bless our hands that ebb away.
