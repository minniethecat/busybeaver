## Introduction

Innisfree (Inis Fraoigh, "Island of Heather") is a small island in Lough Gill, County Sligo, that became one of the most enduring pastoral symbols in W. B. Yeats' poetry. The island represents an ideal of peaceful solitude, a retreat from urban life into a self-sufficient harmony with nature. Yeats first encountered the island in his Sligo childhood, and it remained a touchstone of imaginative escape throughout his career. The endnotes describe Innisfree as "the little lake-island" where Yeats dreamed of living alone in a cabin of clay and wattles [048-002].

## The Lake Isle of Innisfree

The poem "The Lake Isle of Innisfree," first published in 1890 and collected in *The Rose* (1893), is Yeats' definitive treatment of the island motif. The speaker declares his intention to "arise and go now, and go to Innisfree" [031-247], establishing the island as a destination of spiritual pilgrimage. The poem's famous imagery — the small cabin, the bean-rows, the hive for the honey-bee — constructs a vision of deliberate, self-contained life in harmony with natural rhythms. The poem's tripartite structure moves from intention ("I will arise and go now") through sensory immersion ("I hear lake water lapping") to interiorized memory ("I hear it in the deep heart's core"), tracing a withdrawal from the outward world into an inner landscape of sound and longing.

## Innisfree in the Early Poems

Innisfree also appears in the earlier poem "The Danaan Quicken Tree," where the speaker recalls a mythic past when the Danaän immortals "plucked small fruits on Innisfree" [016-005]. This reference connects the island to the Irish mythological tradition of the Tuatha Dé Danann, layering the personal pastoral ideal with a deeper resonance of Celtic otherworld mythology. The island thus operates on two levels: as a real geographical place from Yeats' Sligo boyhood, and as a threshold to the fairy-haunted landscape of Irish legend.

## Biographical Origins

The poem's genesis is well documented. Yeats wrote it in London in 1888, prompted by a jet of water in a shop window on the Strand that reminded him of lake water. He later recalled in *The Trembling of the Veil* (1922) that the sight brought back the sound of Lough Gill lapping against the shore, a sensory memory from his Sligo boyhood spent with his Pollexfen grandparents. At the time of composition, Yeats was living in Bedford Park, London, immersed in the city's "pavements grey" — the poem is thus as much a product of urban exile as of rural nostalgia. He said of it that it was the first poem of his "with anything of his own music in it," marking a decisive turn away from the derivative Pre-Raphaelite manner of his earliest verse.

## Innisfree and the Irish Literary Revival

Though seemingly apolitical, "The Lake Isle of Innisfree" played a significant role in the Irish Literary Revival. Its publication in *The Rose* (1893) and its inclusion in anthologies of Irish verse established Yeats as the leading voice of a new, distinctly Irish poetry that located mythic and symbolic power in the actual Irish landscape. The poem's insistence on a specifically Irish place-name — Innisfree, not Arcadia — was itself a cultural claim: it asserted that the Irish countryside could carry the same symbolic weight as the classical landscapes that dominated English Romantic verse. Yeats later grew to dislike the poem's popularity, feeling it typecast him as a purveyor of Celtic twilight whimsy. In "The Circus Animals' Desertion" he would look back on his career and count "the Lake Isle of Innisfree" among the "old themes" he had outgrown.

## Rhythmic and Prosodic Innovation

The poem's hypnotic quality owes much to its hexameter line and internal assonance. The long, slow vowels — "I will arise and go now, and go to Innisfree" — mimic the lapping of lake water, while the repeated "I will" constructions enact the speaker's determined will. Yeats noted that he read the poem to himself "with a strong beat" and that the rhythm was modeled on a passage from Thoreau's *Walden*, which he had heard his father read aloud. The poem thus represents an early experiment in what would become Yeats's distinctive mature style: a fusion of natural speech-rhythm with ceremonial, almost incantatory cadence.

## Symbolic Significance

As a symbol, Innisfree embodies the tension between the real and the ideal that runs through Yeats' early work. The island is at once a specific, nameable place in the Sligo landscape and an imaginative construction — a "deep heart's core" that the speaker carries within him even when standing "on the roadway, or on the pavements grey." This doubleness — the yearning for a concrete simplicity that is also a spiritual condition — anticipates Yeats' later symbolic method, where actual places (Thoor Ballylee, Coole Park) take on complex symbolic weight.

## Critical Reception and Legacy

The poem's enduring popularity has made Innisfree one of the most recognized place-names in Anglophone poetry. It has been set to music, quoted in political speeches, and inscribed on Irish passports. T. S. Eliot praised its "hypnotic" quality while noting that it belonged to a Yeats the poet himself had outgrown. Contemporary Irish criticism has read the poem as an early instance of decolonizing the landscape — reclaiming a Gaelic place-name and investing it with symbolic autonomy independent of British literary tradition. The island itself, uninhabited and privately owned, remains a site of literary pilgrimage.
