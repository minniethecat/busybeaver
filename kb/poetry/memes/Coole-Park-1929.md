# Coole Park, 1929

"Coole Park, 1929" is a poem in which W. B. Yeats meditates on the great house of Lady Augusta Gregory — Coole Park in County Galway — as a symbol of aristocratic civility, artistic patronage, and a vanishing Irish world. The poem commemorates the estate where Yeats spent many summers writing, and where the Irish Literary Revival found one of its principal gathering places.

## Coole Park in Yeats's Life

Coole Park, the Gregory family demesne in County Galway, was central to Yeats's creative life for over three decades. He first visited in 1897 and returned almost every summer until Lady Gregory's death in 1932. The "seven woods of Coole" [042-001] became the setting for some of his most celebrated poems: "The Wild Swans at Coole," "In the Seven Woods," and the present meditation written as Lady Gregory aged and the estate's future grew uncertain. The house and its grounds represented, in Yeats's imagination, the ideal conditions for art: the convergence of natural beauty, cultivated landscape, and a patron who understood the value of poetry.

## The Great House as Symbol

In "Coole Park, 1929," Yeats transforms the Gregory estate into a symbol of the great Anglo-Irish house — a centre of civilisation that nurtured art, conversation, and "the intellect of man." The poem catalogues the distinguished visitors who gathered at Coole: Douglas Hyde, John Millington Synge, and others who formed the nucleus of the Irish Literary Revival. The house becomes a vessel of cultural memory, its rooms and grounds saturated with the conversations that shaped modern Irish literature. The poem's meditation is shadowed by the knowledge that this world — the Ascendancy great house, its traditions of patronage and cultivated leisure — was passing into history.

## Landscape and Elegy

The landscape of Coole appears throughout Yeats's body of work. "The Wild Swans at Coole" [050-199] established the estate as a site of beauty and mutability — the swans whose number the poet counts "upon the brimming water" are emblems of permanence in a world of change. "Coole Park, 1929" extends this elegiac meditation, linking the natural landscape of woods, lake, and swans to the cultural landscape of the house and its company. The poem belongs to the genre of the country-house poem, but with a specifically Irish and elegiac inflection: it celebrates not an enduring order but one already fading.
