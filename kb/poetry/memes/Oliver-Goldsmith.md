# Oliver Goldsmith

Oliver Goldsmith (1728–1774) was an Irish-born novelist, poet, and dramatist, best known for his novel *The Vicar of Wakefield* (1766), his poem "The Deserted Village" (1770), and his comedy *She Stoops to Conquer* (1773). For W. B. Yeats, Goldsmith represented a specifically Irish contribution to English literature — a writer whose geniality, humour, and social sympathy the poet celebrated as a counterpoint to the more severe aspects of the Ascendancy tradition.

## Goldsmith in Yeats's Gallery

Yeats places Goldsmith among the "Seven Sages" in the 1933 poem of that name — a gathering of the great Anglo-Irish intellects of the eighteenth century. Alongside Jonathan Swift, Edmund Burke, and George Berkeley, Goldsmith stands for the cosmopolitan achievement of Protestant Ireland at a moment when Dublin rivalled Edinburgh and equalled most European capitals as a centre of intellectual and literary life. Yeats admired Goldsmith's combination of Irish origins and European reputation: a writer who belonged fully to English literature while remaining distinctively Irish in sensibility.

## The "Deserted Village" and Irish Landscape

Goldsmith's poem "The Deserted Village" (1770), with its lament for a rural community displaced by enclosure and economic change, resonated with Yeats's own elegies for a vanishing Ireland. The poem's depiction of "Sweet Auburn, loveliest village of the plain" finds echoes in Yeats's elegies for Coole Park and the disappearing world of the great house — both poets mourning a way of life destroyed by forces beyond the individual's control. Goldsmith's combination of social criticism with pastoral nostalgia offered a model for Yeats's own late poetry of cultural lament.

## The Eighteenth-Century Ideal

In Yeats's mature imagination, the eighteenth century represented a golden age of Irish intellect — a moment before the sectarian divisions of the nineteenth century narrowed the possibilities of Irish cultural life. Goldsmith, Burke, Swift, and Berkeley were all members of the established Protestant Church, yet Yeats claimed them as a national tradition that transcended religious affiliation. This idealisation of the Ascendancy eighteenth century was both a genuine cultural recovery and a polemical counter-narrative to the version of Irish history that privileged exclusively Catholic and Gaelic sources.
