# Irish War of Independence

The Irish War of Independence (1919–1921) was the guerrilla conflict between the Irish Republican Army (IRA) and British forces that followed the Easter Rising of 1916 and led to the Anglo-Irish Treaty of 1921, which established the Irish Free State. For W. B. Yeats, the war was not only the political background to his most creative period but the event that forced him to reckon directly with the meaning of revolutionary violence — a reckoning that produced some of the greatest political poetry of the twentieth century.

## Historical Background

The conflict's roots lay in the aftermath of the Easter Rising. The British execution of the Rising's leaders in May 1916 had transformed Irish public opinion, and in the 1918 general election, the republican party Sinn Féin won a landslide victory, securing 73 of Ireland's 105 seats in the House of Commons. Rather than take their seats at Westminster, the Sinn Féin members constituted themselves as Dáil Éireann (the Assembly of Ireland) and declared Irish independence on 21 January 1919 — the same day that an IRA unit ambushed and killed two Royal Irish Constabulary officers at Soloheadbeg, County Tipperary, in what is generally regarded as the opening action of the war.

The conflict that followed was not a conventional war but a guerrilla campaign. The IRA, under the leadership of Michael Collins, employed ambushes, assassinations, and sabotage against the Royal Irish Constabulary (RIC) and the British Army. The British government responded by deploying auxiliary forces — the "Black and Tans" and the Auxiliary Division — whose brutal reprisals against civilians (including the burning of Cork city in December 1920) became notorious.

## Yeats and the War

Yeats spent most of the war period either in England, at his tower at Ballylee in County Galway, or in Dublin. He was not a combatant — at fifty-four in 1919, he was too old for active involvement — but the war's events pressed upon his imagination with extraordinary intensity.

The war years were, paradoxically, among Yeats's most creatively productive. Between 1919 and 1921 he wrote many of the poems that would define his late mastery: "[[the-second-coming|The Second Coming]]" (1919), "[[easter-1916|Easter, 1916]]" (1916, published 1921), "[[a-prayer-for-my-daughter|A Prayer for My Daughter]]" (1919), and "[[meditations-in-time-of-civil-war|Meditations in Time of Civil War]]" (1922–23). The war is the unspoken presence in "The Second Coming" — written in the immediate aftermath of the First World War and the Russian Revolution, but with the Irish conflict's spiral of violence at the poem's heart. The famous lines "Things fall apart; the centre cannot hold; / Mere anarchy is loosed upon the world" [046-394–395] describe a condition as true of Ireland in 1919–21 as of Europe in 1914–18.

The war also shaped Yeats's personal circumstances. The tower at Ballylee — Thoor Ballylee — which he purchased in 1917 and made the symbolic centre of his late poetry, was in the Galway countryside, directly affected by the conflict. The IRA blew up bridges in the area, and the "roadside trees half stripped and bare" that appear in "Meditations in Time of Civil War" bear witness to a landscape scarred by violence.

## The Treaty and Its Aftermath

The war ended with a truce in July 1921, followed by the negotiation of the Anglo-Irish Treaty, signed on 6 December 1921. The treaty established the Irish Free State as a self-governing dominion within the British Commonwealth but required an oath of allegiance to the Crown and permitted Northern Ireland (the six counties) to opt out of the Free State — which it immediately did.

The treaty split the Irish republican movement. The pro-Treaty faction, led by Michael Collins and Arthur Griffith, argued that the Free State offered "the freedom to achieve freedom." The anti-Treaty faction, led by Éamon de Valera, rejected the oath of allegiance and the partition of the island. In June 1922, the split descended into the [[Irish-Civil-War|Irish Civil War]] — a conflict even more bitter than the war against the British, and one that Yeats would grapple with directly in poems like "Meditations in Time of Civil War" and "The Stare's Nest by My Window."

Yeats's attitude toward the treaty was complex. As a senator of the Free State from 1922 to 1928, he was associated with the pro-Treaty side, but his poetry shows sympathy for the anti-Treaty republicans — figures like [[Countess-Markievicz]] and the executed republican leaders became, in his elegies, emblems of a heroic intransigence that the poet both admired and feared.

## In Yeats's Poetry

The war and its legacy permeate Yeats's work from "The Second Coming" onward:

| Poem | Year | Collection | Connection |
|------|------|-----------|------------|
| "The Second Coming" | 1919 | *Michael Robartes and the Dancer* | Written during the war; the "rough beast" slouching toward Bethlehem is informed by the period's violence |
| "Easter, 1916" | 1916/1921 | *Michael Robartes and the Dancer* | Elegy for the Rising's executed leaders, privately circulated during the war, published in 1921 |
| "Nineteen Hundred and Nineteen" | 1921 | *The Tower* | Direct meditation on the war; "We too had many pretty toys when young" |
| "Meditations in Time of Civil War" | 1922–23 | *The Tower* | Set at Ballylee during the Civil War; "We are closed in, and the key is turned / On our uncertainty" |

The war's deepest legacy in Yeats's poetry is its contribution to his mature understanding of history as cyclical and violent — the vision encoded in the gyres of *A Vision*. The war of 1919–21 was, in Yeats's symbolic scheme, one of the "violent annunciations" by which one era gives way to another. The "terrible beauty" of "Easter, 1916" is this process's poetic name.

## Yeats in the Senate

Yeats served as a senator of the Irish Free State from 1922 to 1928, a period that put him at the center of the political life shaped by the War of Independence and its aftermath. His appointment was a recognition of his cultural standing rather than any political affiliation: he was not a party man but a Protestant nationalist who had helped to build the literary wing of the Irish Revival. In the Senate, Yeats spoke most memorably on cultural matters — the protection of Irish manuscripts, the censorship of films, the design of the new coinage — but his speeches also addressed the deeper questions raised by the war: the nature of national identity, the place of the Anglo-Irish minority in the new state, and the relationship between political freedom and cultural renewal.

The Senate was, for Yeats, a stage on which the drama of the new Ireland was performed. His famous speech on divorce in 1925 — in which he defended the rights of the Protestant minority against the Catholic majority's legislation — was one of the defining moments of his public career, and it drew directly on the cultural politics that the war had brought into focus. The Ireland that emerged from the War of Independence was, Yeats argued, in danger of exchanging one form of oppression for another: "We against whom you have done this thing are no petty people. We are one of the great stocks of Europe. We are the people of Burke; we are the people of Grattan; we are the people of Swift, the people of Emmet, the people of Parnell." This appeal to a shared Irish heritage — Protestant and Catholic alike — was Yeats's answer to the sectarian logic that the war had intensified.
