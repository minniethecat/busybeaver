# Last Poems

*Last Poems* was published in 1939, shortly after Yeats's death in January of that year. It was his final gathering of verse, assembled in the last months of his life and containing some of his most powerful late work — poems written in the shadow of age, illness, and approaching death that nevertheless burn with a fierce creative energy.

## Major Poems

The collection includes several of Yeats's most celebrated late poems:

- **"Under Ben Bulben"** — Yeats's self-written epitaph, ending with the famous lines "Cast a cold eye / On life, on death. / Horseman, pass by!"
- **"Lapis Lazuli"** — a meditation on art and tragedy inspired by a carved Chinese stone
- **"The Circus Animals' Desertion"** — Yeats's reckoning with his own poetic career, beginning "I sought a theme and sought for it in vain" and concluding with the anti-climactic image of the "foul rag-and-bone shop of the heart"
- **"Cuchulain Comforted"** — a Dantesque vision of the hero Cuchulain in the afterlife
- **"The Statues"** — a meditation on Greek sculpture, Irish nationalism, and the relationship between form and violence
- **"News for the Delphic Oracle"** — a sensuous vision of the afterlife, featuring Plotinus and other figures from Yeats's personal mythology

## Late Style

The poems of *Last Poems* are characterised by an extraordinary directness and compression. Yeats's late style — stripped of the decorative Pre-Raphaelite manner of his youth — achieves a hard, spare music. The poems are bone-clean, urgent, unafraid of violent juxtaposition. The poet who wrote "The Lake Isle of Innisfree" in his twenties became, in his seventies, a radically different artist — tougher, stranger, more naked.
