## The Grey Rock

[044-031]Poets with whom I learned my trade,   

[044-032]Companions of the Cheshire Cheese,   

[044-033]Here’s an old story I’ve re-made,   

[044-034]Imagining ’twould better please   

[044-035]Your ears than stories now in fashion,   

[044-036]Though you may think I waste my breath   

[044-037]Pretending that there can be [[love-in-yeats|passion]]   

[044-038]That has more life in it than death,   

[044-039]And though at bottling of your wine   

[044-040]The bow-legged Goban had no say;   

[044-041]The moral’s yours because it’s mine.

[044-042]When cups went round at close of day﻿—   

[044-043]Is not that how good stories run?﻿—   

[044-044]The gods were sitting at the board   

[044-045]In their great house at Slievenamon.   

[044-046]They sang a drowsy song, or snored,   

[044-047]For all were full of wine and meat.   

[044-048]The smoky torches made a glare   

[044-049]On metal Goban ’d hammered at,   

[044-050]On old deep silver rolling there   

[044-051]Or on some still unemptied cup   

[044-052]That he, when frenzy stirred his thews,   

[044-053]Had hammered out on mountain top   

[044-054]To hold the sacred stuff he brews   

[044-055]That only gods may buy of him.

[044-056]Now from the juice that made them wise   

[044-057]All those had lifted up the dim   

[044-058]Imaginations of their eyes,   

[044-059]For one that was like woman made   

[044-060]Before their sleepy eyelids ran   

[044-061]And trembling with her passion said,   

[044-062]“Come out and dig for a dead man,   

[044-063]Who’s burrowing somewhere in the ground,   

[044-064]And mock him to his face and then   

[044-065]Hollo him on with horse and hound,   

[044-066]For he is the worst of all dead men.”

[044-067]We should be dazed and terror-struck,   

[044-068]If we but saw in dreams that room,   

[044-069]Those wine-drenched eyes, and curse our luck   

[044-070]That emptied all our days to come.   

[044-071]I knew a woman none could please,   

[044-072]Because she dreamed when but a child   

[044-073]Of men and women made like these;   

[044-074]And after, when her blood ran wild,   

[044-075]Had ravelled her own story out,   

[044-076]And said, “In two or in three years   

[044-077]I need must marry some poor lout,”   

[044-078]And having said it burst in tears.

[044-079]Since, tavern comrades, you have died,   

[044-080]Maybe your images have stood,   

[044-081]Mere bone and muscle thrown aside,   

[044-082]Before that roomful or as good.   

[044-083]You had to face your ends when young﻿—   

[044-084]’Twas wine or women, or some curse﻿—   

[044-085]But never made a poorer song   

[044-086]That you might have a heavier purse,   

[044-087]Nor gave loud service to a cause   

[044-088]That you might have a troop of friends.   

[044-089]You kept the Muses’ sterner laws,   

[044-090]And unrepenting faced your ends,   

[044-091]And therefore earned the right﻿—and yet   

[044-092]Dowson and Johnson most I praise﻿—   

[044-093]To troop with those the world’s forgot,   

[044-094]And copy their proud steady gaze.

[044-095]“The Danish troop was driven out   

[044-096]Between the dawn and dusk,” she said;   

[044-097]“Although the event was long in doubt,   

[044-098]Although the King of [[ireland|Ireland]]’s dead   

[044-099]And half the kings, before sundown   

[044-100]All was accomplished. When this day   

[044-101]Murrough, the King of Ireland’s son,   

[044-102]Foot after foot was giving way,   

[044-103]He and his best troops back to back   

[044-104]Had perished there, but the Danes ran,   

[044-105]Stricken with panic from the attack,   

[044-106]The shouting of an unseen man;   

[044-107]And being thankful Murrough found,   

[044-108]Led by a footsole dipped in blood   

[044-109]That had made prints upon the ground,   

[044-110]Where by old thorn trees that man stood;   

[044-111]And though when he gazed here and there,   

[044-112]He had but gazed on thorn trees, spoke,   

[044-113]‘Who is the friend that seems but air   

[044-114]And yet could give so fine a stroke?’   

[044-115]Thereon a young man met his eye,   

[044-116]Who said, ‘Because she held me in   

[044-117]Her love, and would not have me die,   

[044-118]Rock-nurtured Aoife took a pin,   

[044-119]And pushing it into my shirt,   

[044-120]Promised that for a pin’s sake,   

[044-121]No man should see to do me hurt;   

[044-122]But there it’s gone; I will not take   

[044-123]The fortune that had been my shame   

[044-124]Seeing, King’s son, what wounds you have.’   

[044-125]’Twas roundly spoke, but when night came   

[044-126]He had betrayed me to his grave,   

[044-127]For he and the King’s son were dead.   

[044-128]I’d promised him two hundred years,   

[044-129]And when for all I’d done or said﻿—   

[044-130]And these immortal eyes shed tears﻿—   

[044-131]He claimed his country’s need was most,   

[044-132]I’d save his life, yet for the sake   

[044-133]Of a new friend he has turned a ghost.   

[044-134]What does he care if my heart break?   

[044-135]I call for spade and horse and hound   

[044-136]That we may harry him.” Thereon   

[044-137]She cast herself upon the ground   

[044-138]And rent her clothes and made her moan:   

[044-139]“Why are they faithless when their might   

[044-140]Is from the holy shades that rove   

[044-141]The grey [[stone-imagery|rock]] and the windy [[shadow-and-light|light]]?   

[044-142]Why should the faithfullest heart most love   

[044-143]The bitter sweetness of false faces?   

[044-144]Why must the lasting love what passes,   

[044-145]Why are the gods by men betrayed!”

[044-146]But thereon every god stood up   

[044-147]With a slow smile and without sound,   

[044-148]And stretching forth his arm and cup   

[044-149]To where she moaned upon the ground,   

[044-150]Suddenly drenched her to the skin;   

[044-151]And she with Goban’s wine adrip,   

[044-152]No more remembering what had been,   

[044-153]Stared at the gods with laughing lip.

[044-154]I have kept my faith, though faith was tried,   

[044-155]To that rock-born, rock-wandering foot,   

[044-156]And the world’s altered since you died,   

[044-157]And I am in no good repute   

[044-158]With the loud host before the [[water-imagery|sea]],   

[044-159]That think sword strokes were better meant   

[044-160]Than lover’s [[song-and-poetry|music]]﻿—let that be,   

[044-161]So that the wandering foot’s content.

## The Two Kings

[044-162]King Eochaid came at sundown to a wood   

[044-163]Westward of [[hill-of-tara|Tara]]. Hurrying to his queen   

[044-164]He had out-ridden his war-wasted men   

[044-165]That with empounded cattle trod the mire;   

[044-166]And where beech trees had mixed a pale green light   

[044-167]With the ground-ivy’s blue, he saw a stag   

[044-168]Whiter than curds, its eyes the tint of the sea.   

[044-169]Because it stood upon his path and seemed   

[044-170]More hands in height than any stag in the world   

[044-171]He sat with tightened rein and loosened mouth   

[044-172]Upon his trembling horse, then drove the spur;   

[044-173]But the stag stooped and ran at him, and passed,   

[044-174]Rending the horse’s flank. King Eochaid reeled   

[044-175]Then drew his sword to hold its levelled point   

[044-176]Against the stag. When horn and steel were met   

[044-177]The horn resounded as though it had been silver,   

[044-178]A sweet, miraculous, terrifying sound.   

[044-179]Horn locked in sword, they tugged and struggled there   

[044-180]As though a stag and unicorn were met   

[044-181]In Africa on Mountain of the [[moon-symbolism|Moon]],   

[044-182]Until at last the double horns, drawn backward,   

[044-183]Butted below the single and so pierced   

[044-184]The entrails of the horse. Dropping his sword   

[044-185]King Eochaid seized the horns in his strong hands   

[044-186]And stared into the sea-green eye, and so   

[044-187]Hither and thither to and fro they trod   

[044-188]Till all the place was beaten into mire.   

[044-189]The strong thigh and the agile thigh were met,   

[044-190]The hands that gathered up the might of the world,   

[044-191]And hoof and horn that had sucked in their speed   

[044-192]Amid the elaborate wilderness of the air.   

[044-193]Through bush they plunged and over ivied root,   

[044-194]And where the stone struck fire, while in the leaves   

[044-195]A squirrel whinnied and a bird screamed out;   

[044-196]But when at last he forced those sinewy flanks   

[044-197]Against a beech bole, he threw down the beast   

[044-198]And knelt above it with drawn knife. On the instant   

[044-199]It vanished like a shadow, and a cry   

[044-200]So mournful that it seemed the cry of one   

[044-201]Who had lost some unimaginable treasure   

[044-202]Wandered between the blue and the green leaf   

[044-203]And climbed into the air, crumbling away,   

[044-204]Till all had seemed a shadow or a vision   

[044-205]But for the trodden mire, the pool of blood,   

[044-206]The disembowelled horse. King Eochaid ran,   

[044-207]Toward peopled Tara, nor stood to draw his breath   

[044-208]Until he came before the painted wall,   

[044-209]The posts of polished yew, circled with bronze,   

[044-210]Of the great door; but though the hanging lamps   

[044-211]Showed their faint light through the unshuttered windows,   

[044-212]Nor door, nor mouth, nor slipper made a noise,   

[044-213]Nor on the ancient beaten paths, that wound   

[044-214]From well-side or from plough-land, was there noise;   

[044-215]And there had been no sound of living thing   

[044-216]Before him or behind, but that far-off   

[044-217]On the horizon edge bellowed the herds.   

[044-218]Knowing that silence brings no good to kings,   

[044-219]And mocks returning victory, he passed   

[044-220]Between the pillars with a beating heart   

[044-221]And saw where in the midst of the great hall   

[044-222]Pale-faced, alone upon a bench, Edain   

[044-223]Sat upright with a sword before her feet.   

[044-224]Her hands on either side had gripped the bench,   

[044-225]Her eyes were cold and steady, her lips tight.   

[044-226]Some passion had made her stone. Hearing a foot   

[044-227]She started and then knew whose foot it was;   

[044-228]But when he thought to take her in his arms   

[044-229]She motioned him afar, and rose and spoke:   

[044-230]“I have sent among the fields or to the woods   

[044-231]The fighting men and servants of this house,   

[044-232]For I would have your judgment upon one   

[044-233]Who is self-accused. If she be innocent   

[044-234]She would not look in any known man’s face   

[044-235]Till judgment has been given, and if guilty,   

[044-236]Will never look again on known man’s face.”   

[044-237]And at these words he paled, as she had paled,   

[044-238]Knowing that he should find upon her lips   

[044-239]The meaning of that monstrous day. Then she:   

[044-240]“You brought me where your brother Ardan sat   

[044-241]Always in his one seat, and bid me care him   

[044-242]Through that strange illness that had fixed him there,   

[044-243]And should he die to heap his burial mound   

[044-244]And carve his name in Ogham.” Eochaid said,   

[044-245]“He lives?” “He lives and is a healthy man.”   

[044-246]“While I have him and you it matters little   

[044-247]What man you have lost, what evil you have found.”   

[044-248]“I bid them make his bed under this roof   

[044-249]And carried him his food with my own hands,   

[044-250]And so the weeks passed by. But when I said   

[044-251]‘What is this trouble?’ he would answer nothing,   

[044-252]Though always at my words his trouble grew;   

[044-253]And I but asked the more, till he cried out,   

[044-254]Weary of many questions: ‘There are things   

[044-255]That make the heart akin to the dumb stone.’   

[044-256]Then I replied: ‘Although you hide a secret,   

[044-257]Hopeless and dear, or terrible to think on,   

[044-258]Speak it, that I may send through the wide world   

[044-259]For medicine.’ Thereon he cried aloud:   

[044-260]‘Day after day you question me, and I,   

[044-261]Because there is such a storm amid my thoughts   

[044-262]I shall be carried in the gust, command,   

[044-263]Forbid, beseech and waste my breath.’ Then I,   

[044-264]‘Although the thing that you have hid were evil,   

[044-265]The speaking of it could be no great wrong,   

[044-266]And evil must it be, if done ’twere worse   

[044-267]Than mound and stone that keep all virtue in,   

[044-268]And loosen on us dreams that waste our life,   

[044-269]Shadows and shows that can but turn the brain.’   

[044-270]But finding him still silent I stooped down   

[044-271]And whispering that none but he should hear,   

[044-272]Said: ‘If a woman has put this on you,   

[044-273]My men, whether it please her or displease,   

[044-274]And though they have to cross the Loughlan waters   

[044-275]And take her in the middle of armed men,   

[044-276]Shall make her look upon her handiwork,   

[044-277]That she may quench the rick she has fired; and though   

[044-278]She may have worn silk clothes, or worn a crown,   

[044-279]She’ll not be proud, knowing within her heart   

[044-280]That our sufficient portion of the world   

[044-281]Is that we give, although it be brief giving,   

[044-282]Happiness to children and to men.’   

[044-283]Then he, driven by his thought beyond his thought,   

[044-284]And speaking what he would not though he would,   

[044-285]Sighed: ‘You, even you yourself, could work the cure!’   

[044-286]And at those words I rose and I went out   

[044-287]And for nine days he had food from other hands,   

[044-288]And for nine days my mind went whirling round   

[044-289]The one disastrous zodiac, muttering   

[044-290]That the immedicable mound’s beyond   

[044-291]Our questioning, beyond our pity even.   

[044-292]But when nine days had gone I stood again   

[044-293]Before his chair and bending down my head   

[044-294]Told him, that when Orion rose, and all   

[044-295]The women of his household were asleep,   

[044-296]To go﻿—for hope would give his limbs the power﻿—   

[044-297]To an old empty woodman’s house that’s hidden   

[044-298]Close to a clump of beech trees in the wood   

[044-299]Westward of Tara, there to await a friend   

[044-300]That could, as he had told her, work his cure   

[044-301]And would be no harsh friend. When night had deepened,   

[044-302]I groped my way through boughs, and over roots,   

[044-303]Till oak and hazel ceased and beech began,   

[044-304]And found the house, a sputtering torch within,   

[044-305]And stretched out sleeping on a pile of skins   

[044-306]Ardan, and though I called to him and tried   

[044-307]To shake him out of sleep, I could not rouse him.   

[044-308]I waited till the night was on the turn,   

[044-309]Then fearing that some labourer, on his way   

[044-310]To plough or pasture-land, might see me there,   

[044-311]Went out. Among the ivy-covered rocks,   

[044-312]As on the blue light of a sword, a man   

[044-313]Who had unnatural majesty, and eyes   

[044-314]Like the eyes of some great kite scouring the woods,   

[044-315]Stood on my path. Trembling from head to foot   

[044-316]I gazed at him like grouse upon a kite;   

[044-317]But with a voice that had unnatural music,   

[044-318]‘A weary wooing and a long,’ he said,   

[044-319]‘Speaking of love through other lips and looking   

[044-320]Under the eyelids of another, for it was my craft   

[044-321]That put a passion in the sleeper there,   

[044-322]And when I had got my will and drawn you here,   

[044-323]Where I may speak to you alone, my craft   

[044-324]Sucked up the passion out of him again   

[044-325]And left mere sleep. He’ll wake when the sun wakes,   

[044-326]Push out his vigorous limbs and rub his eyes,   

[044-327]And wonder what has ailed him these twelve months.’   

[044-328]I cowered back upon the wall in terror,   

[044-329]But that sweet-sounding voice ran on: ‘Woman,   

[044-330]I was your husband when you rode the air,   

[044-331]Danced in the whirling foam and in the dust,   

[044-332]In days you have not kept in memory,   

[044-333]Being betrayed into a cradle, and I come   

[044-334]That I may claim you as my wife again.’   

[044-335]I was no longer terrified, his voice   

[044-336]Had half awakened some old memory,   

[044-337]Yet answered him: ‘I am King Eochaid’s wife   

[044-338]And with him have found every happiness   

[044-339]Women can find.’ With a most masterful voice,   

[044-340]That made the body seem as it were a string   

[044-341]Under a bow, he cried: ‘What happiness   

[044-342]Can lovers have that know their happiness   

[044-343]Must end at the dumb stone? But where we build   

[044-344]Our sudden palaces in the still air   

[044-345]Pleasure itself can bring no weariness,   

[044-346]Nor can time waste the cheek, nor is there foot   

[044-347]That has grown weary of the whirling dance,   

[044-348]Nor an unlaughing mouth, but mine that mourns,   

[044-349]Among those mouths that sing their sweethearts’ praise,   

[044-350]Your empty bed.’ ‘How should I love,’ I answered,   

[044-351]‘Were it not that when the dawn has lit my bed   

[044-352]And shown my husband sleeping there, I have sighed,   

[044-353]“Your strength and nobleness will pass away.”   

[044-354]Or how should love be worth its pains were it not   

[044-355]That when he has fallen asleep within my arms,   

[044-356]Being wearied out, I love in man the child?   

[044-357]What can they know of love that do not know   

[044-358]She builds her nest upon a narrow ledge   

[044-359]Above a windy precipice?’ Then he:   

[044-360]‘Seeing that when you come to the [[death-in-yeats|death-bed]]   

[044-361]You must return, whether you would or no,   

[044-362]This human life blotted from memory,   

[044-363]Why must I live some thirty, forty years,   

[044-364]Alone with all this useless happiness?’   

[044-365]Thereon he seized me in his arms, but I   

[044-366]Thrust him away with both my hands and cried,   

[044-367]‘Never will I believe there is any change   

[044-368]Can blot out of my memory this life   

[044-369]Sweetened by death, but if I could believe   

[044-370]That were a double hunger in my lips   

[044-371]For what is doubly brief.’ And now the shape,   

[044-372]My hands were pressed to, vanished suddenly.   

[044-373]I staggered, but a beech tree stayed my fall,   

[044-374]And clinging to it I could hear the cocks   

[044-375]Crow upon Tara.” King Eochaid bowed his head   

[044-376]And thanked her for her kindness to his brother,   

[044-377]For that she promised, and for that refused.

[044-378]Thereon the bellowing of the empounded herds   

[044-379][[rose-symbol|Rose]] round the walls, and through the bronze-ringed door   

[044-380]Jostled and shouted those war-wasted men,   

[044-381]And in the midst King Eochaid’s brother stood.   

[044-382]And bade all welcome, being ignorant.

[044-421]But fumble in a greasy till   

[044-422]And add the halfpence to the pence   

[044-423]And prayer to shivering prayer, until

[044-432]For whom the hangman’s rope was spun,

[044-434]Romantic Ireland’s dead and gone,

[044-445]And call those exiles as they were

## A Coat

[044-979]I made my song a coat   

[044-980]Covered with embroideries   

[044-981]Out of old mythologies   

[044-982]From heel to throat;

[044-984]Wore it in the world’s eyes

[044-987]For there’s more enterprise
