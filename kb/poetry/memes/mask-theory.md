# Mask Theory

The doctrine of the Mask (or "anti-self") is one of the central concepts of W. B. Yeats's mature philosophy, articulated most fully in the essay *Per Amica Silentia Lunae* (1918) and systematized in *A Vision* (1925, 1937). The Mask is not a disguise in the ordinary sense — a concealment of the true self — but rather the deliberate cultivation of an opposite or antithetical self, a chosen image of what one wishes to become. It is the "anti-self" that completes the incomplete self through the creative tension of opposites: "The most unlike, being my anti-self" [045-959]. For Yeats, the Mask was simultaneously a personal discipline, a theory of artistic creation, and a principle of historical change — the poet does not express a pre-existing self but forges a self through the dramatic act of making, just as each historical age is the Mask of its predecessor.

## Origins and Sources

Yeats derived the Mask concept from multiple converging sources:

- **Neoplatonism**: Plotinus's conception of the soul's ascent through successive forms of being — each higher form being the "opposite" of the lower — informs the idea of self-completion through opposition.
- **Romantic Self-Fashioning**: Byron and Shelley provided the aesthetic model of the artist who creates himself as a dramatic character. Yeats, shy and introspective by nature, consciously cultivated a public persona of magisterial authority — the Mask in practice.
- **Oscar Wilde**: Wilde's insistence that "Man is least himself when he talks in his own person. Give him a mask, and he will tell you the truth" resonated deeply with Yeats's conviction that artifice reveals deeper truth than sincerity.
- **The Daimon**: In Yeats's occult thought, each person has a Daimon or opposite spiritual self — the Mask is the psychological translation of this esoteric doctrine.

- **Primary source**: Neoplatonism + Wildean paradox + occult Daimon doctrine
- **Key text**: *Per Amica Silentia Lunae* (1918), *A Vision* (1925/1937)

## Development Across Yeats's Career

### Early Premonitions (1890s–1900s)

Before the Mask was named as a doctrine, it appeared as a poetic intuition. The early poems are filled with figures of doubleness, concealment, and the gulf between appearance and reality. The "damask roses" that appear in *The Wanderings of Usheen* — "The damask roses, bloom on bloom" [015-290] and "Shall fall the leaves of damask roses" [015-300] — carry the etymological trace of "mask" in their name (damask < Damascus), an associative thread that continues in the late poetry: "In damask clothed and on a seat" [047-626]. These are not doctrinal uses but premonitions — the word "mask" haunting the poems before it became a system.

### Formulation (1910–1918)

The Mask crystallized as an explicit concept in two phases. First, the poem "The Mask" (1910), from *The Green Helmet and Other Poems*, stages a dramatic dialogue in which an unnamed speaker urges: "Put off that mask of burning gold / With emerald eyes" [043-160], and the reply comes: "It was the mask engaged your mind" [043-167]. The poem distills the Yeatsian conviction that the surface is the depth — what we call a "mask" is actually the shaped, intensified reality of the self, and removing it would reveal not the "true person" but an emptiness. The poem "The Mask" occupies position 050-159 in the collected works table of contents, identifying it as one of Yeats's explicit thematic statements.

Second, the prose essay *Per Amica Silentia Lunae* (1918) gave the doctrine its philosophical articulation: the self finds completion not by expressing its nature but by seeking its opposite.

### Systematization (1925–1937)

In *A Vision*, the Mask became one of the four Faculties governing the soul's journey through the twenty-eight phases of the moon. Each phase has a corresponding Mask — the opposite type that the Will must strive toward. The creative tension between Will and Mask drives individual development: the self is a dramatic conflict between what it is and what it seeks to become. The Mask, in this systematic form, is the hinge between psychology and cosmology.

### The Late Personae (1920s–1930s)

The doctrine's fullest poetic realization came through the dramatic personae of the late work. Michael Robartes, Owen Aherne, Crazy Jane, the Wild Old Wicked Man, the Woman of the Sidhe — these are all Masks in the Yeatsian sense, antithetical selves through which the poet explored possibilities of being that his natural self could not reach. Crazy Jane, in particular — earthy, sexually frank, theologically defiant — is the perfect Mask for the aging, decorous, spiritually anxious poet. Each persona is "the most unlike" Yeats, and therefore, by the doctrine's own logic, the most complete expression of him.

## Symbolic Meaning

The Mask carries three interrelated meanings in Yeats's thought:

1. **Psychological**: The Mask is the chosen opposite of the natural self — the discipline by which a shy man becomes a public poet, or a romantic dreamer becomes a man of action.
2. **Aesthetic**: The Mask theory means that poetry is not self-expression but self-creation. The poet does not pour out feeling but constructs a dramatic self through which feeling can be shaped.
3. **Historical**: Each civilization is the Mask of its predecessor — history advances through antithesis. The coming "rough beast" of "The Second Coming" is, in this sense, the Mask of Christian civilization.
