# Constance Markievicz

Constance Georgine Markievicz (1868–1927), born Constance Gore-Booth at Lissadell House in Co. Sligo, was an Irish revolutionary, suffragist, and politician. She fought in the 1916 Easter Rising as second-in-command at St Stephen's Green, was sentenced to death (commuted to life imprisonment because of her sex), and became the first woman elected to the British House of Commons — though like all Sinn Féin MPs, she refused to take her seat.

## Yeats's Elegy

Constance and her younger sister Eva are the subjects of Yeats's great elegy "In Memory of Eva Gore-Booth and Con Markievicz" (1927). The poem contrasts the remembered beauty of the sisters at [[Lissadell]] — "two girls in silk kimonos, both / Beautiful, one a gazelle" — with their later fates:

> The older is condemned to death,
> Pardoned, drags out lonely years
> Conspiring among the ignorant.

Yeats's response to Constance's revolutionary career was complex. He admired her courage but was troubled by what he saw as the coarsening effect of political violence on the spirit. The poem's lament is not for Constance alone but for the world that transformed a beautiful aristocrat into a revolutionary and condemned her to a life of "conspiring among the ignorant."

## Life and Legacy

- Born at [[Lissadell|Lissadell House]], Co. Sligo, in 1868
- Married Count Casimir Markievicz, a Polish artist, in 1900
- Fought in the Easter Rising (1916), sentenced to death but reprieved
- Elected as Sinn Féin MP for Dublin St Patrick's in 1918
- Served as Minister for Labour in the First Dáil (1919–1922)
- Died in 1927, aged fifty-nine
