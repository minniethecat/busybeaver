# Vision in Yeats

"A Vision" is W. B. Yeats's most ambitious and strangest prose work — a systematic cosmology produced through the automatic writing of his wife Georgie, which Yeats spent years interpreting, refining, and elaborating into a philosophy of history, personality, and the soul.

## A Vision (1925, revised 1937)

*A Vision* was first published in 1925 in a limited edition, then substantially revised for a public edition in 1937. The book presents itself as the communications of "Instructors" — spirits who contacted Yeats through [[Georgie-Yeats|Georgie's]] automatic writing, beginning on their honeymoon in October 1917.

The system includes:

- **The Great Wheel**: Twenty-eight lunar phases, each representing a personality type, arranged around a circle
- **The Gyres**: History moves in two-thousand-year cycles, each driven by the opposition between "primary" (objective, democratic, rational) and "antithetical" (subjective, aristocratic, imaginative) principles
- **The Afterlife**: A detailed account of the soul's journey after death, through successive states of purification and rebirth
- **The Daimon**: Each person has an opposite spirit — the [[daimon|daimon]] or anti-self — through which they achieve completion

## Significance

Whether *A Vision* should be read as philosophy, mythology, or metaphor has been debated since its publication. Yeats himself was ambivalent, describing the system as "a mythology" in one passage and as "a machine for the production of metaphors" in another. What is not in doubt is the fertility of the system for Yeats's poetry: the gyre, the phases of the moon, the mask, and the daimon are the symbolic vocabulary of all his greatest late poems.
