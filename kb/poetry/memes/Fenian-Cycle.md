# Fenian Cycle in Yeats

The Fenian Cycle (also called the Ossianic Cycle, after Oisin or Usheen, the poet-warrior who narrates many of its tales) is one of the four great cycles of Irish mythology. It is set in the third century and centres on Fionn mac Cumhaill and his band of warriors, the Fianna — a heroic age of hunt, battle, and otherworld adventure.

For W. B. Yeats, the Fenian Cycle was the most fruitful of the Irish mythological cycles. His earliest long poem, *The Wanderings of Usheen* (1889), is a direct retelling of Fenian material: the aged Oisin returns from three hundred years in Tír na nÓg to tell St Patrick of his adventures in the Land of Youth. Throughout his career, Yeats returned to Fenian figures — Oisin, Niamh, Aengus — as symbols of the otherworldly beauty and heroic passion that he saw as Ireland's distinctive imaginative inheritance.

## Key Figures

- **Fionn mac Cumhaill** — leader of the Fianna, the central hero of the cycle
- **Oisin (Usheen)** — Fionn's son, the poet-warrior who narrates the cycle's tales
- **Niamh** — the fairy woman from Tír na nÓg who carries Oisin away
- **Aengus** — the god of love, associated with the Fenian tales as a figure of supernatural beauty and desire

## Yeats's Use of Fenian Material

Yeats's engagement with the Fenian Cycle was personal and programmatic. He identified with Oisin, the last of the Fianna — a survivor from a heroic age, telling stories to an uncomprehending Christian audience (St Patrick). This identification with the last of a dying tradition runs through Yeats's early work and into his mature persona as the "last Romantic."

The Fenian tales also provided Yeats with a language for the tension between mortal and immortal worlds — the call "away" from the mortal realm that recurs in poems like "The Hosting of the Sidhe" and "The Song of Wandering Aengus." This motif, drawn from Fenian material, became one of Yeats's most persistent symbolic patterns.
