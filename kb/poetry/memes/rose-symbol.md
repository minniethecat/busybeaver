# Rose (Symbol)

The Rose is Yeats's most enduring and multi-layered symbol, operating simultaneously as a signifier of eternal beauty, mystical love (associated with Maud Gonne), Ireland itself (through the _Róisín Dubh_ tradition), and spiritual transcendence. It appears across all phases of his career, from _The Rose_ (1893) to late poems.

## Origins and Sources

Yeats drew the Rose symbol from multiple overlapping traditions:

- **Rosicrucian tradition**: The Rose as mystical symbol of spiritual perfection, encountered through Yeats's membership in the Hermetic Order of the Golden Dawn ((048-003))
- **Irish nationalist poetry**: The _Róisín Dubh_ ("Dark Rosaleen") tradition, where the rose personifies Ireland as a woman awaiting deliverance
- **Catholic Mariology**: The rose as symbol of the Virgin Mary, mystical and incorruptible
- **Dante and courtly love**: The rose as symbol of the beloved and the divine, inherited from the _Divine Comedy_'s Celestial Rose

As Yeats reflected in his endnotes: "The Rose was part of my second book, the _Countess Kathleen and Various Legends and Lyrics_. I notice upon reading these poems that the quality symbolized as The Rose differs from the Intellectual Beauty of Shelley and of Spenser" ((048-007)).

## Symbolic Meaning

In Yeats's system, the Rose carries several overlapping layers of meaning:

1. **Eternal Beauty**: The Rose represents a beauty that transcends time and decay — "the Rose of all Roses, Rose of all the World" ((031-194)). It is the Platonic form of beauty itself, immune to mutability.

2. **Mystical Love and Maud Gonne**: The Rose is inseparable from Yeats's lifelong love for Maud Gonne. The poem "To the Rose upon the Rood of Time" invokes the Rose as both beloved and cosmic principle: "Red Rose, proud Rose, sad Rose of all my days" ((031-005)).

3. **Ireland**: Through the _Róisín Dubh_ tradition, the Rose is Ireland — "A Rose Tree" uses the image to lament Ireland's political disillusionment after 1916: "Has withered our Rose Tree" ((046-266)).

4. **Spiritual Perfection**: In the Rosicrucian poems, the Rose marks the goal of mystical quest — "The Incorruptible Rose" ((038-389)), "the most secret, and inviolate Rose" ((038-394)).

## Development Across Yeats's Career

**Early period (1889–1899)**: The Rose dominates Yeats's second collection, functioning as a symbol of ideal beauty and the beloved. Poems like "To the Rose upon the Rood of Time" fuse Catholic crucifixion imagery ("Rood") with Rosicrucian mysticism, creating a syncretic symbol that is simultaneously religious, national, and personal.

**Middle period (1900–1919)**: The Rose recedes from prominence as Yeats's style hardens, but the symbol evolves. "The Secret Rose" (_The Wind Among the Reeds_, 1899) transforms it into a symbol of occult initiation: "most secret, and inviolate Rose" ((038-394)).

**Late period (1920–1939)**: The Rose returns in a political key. "The Rose Tree" (046-266) to (046-280) from _Michael Robartes and the Dancer_ (1921) reworks the _Róisín Dubh_ convention into a dialogue about Ireland after the Easter Rising — Pearse and Connolly's blood must water the withered Rose Tree for it to bloom again.

## Scholarly Interpretation

Scholarship on Yeats's Rose emphasizes its syncretism: the symbol's power derives from its ability to hold Catholic, nationalist, and occult meanings simultaneously. Ellmann (1948) reads the Rose poems as the site where Yeats first achieves the fusion of personal emotion with mythological system that characterizes his mature work. Vendler (2007) traces the Rose's evolution from a decorative Pre-Raphaelite emblem to a hardened political symbol in the later poems.
