# John O'Leary

John O'Leary (1830–1907) was a Fenian — a member of the Irish Republican Brotherhood, the secret revolutionary organisation dedicated to ending British rule in Ireland by force. He spent five years in an English prison and fifteen years in exile in Paris for his beliefs. But for W. B. Yeats, who met him in 1885 upon O'Leary's return to Dublin, he was something rarer: a nationalist whose patriotism was moral and magnanimous rather than sectarian or hateful. Yeats's most famous political line — "Romantic Ireland's dead and gone, / It's with O'Leary in the grave" [044-426] — is an elegy for an ideal of disinterested patriotism that O'Leary embodied and that Yeats believed had died with him.

## Introduction

John O'Leary was born in Tipperary in 1830 to a Catholic family. He studied at Trinity College Dublin but abandoned medicine for politics. In the 1860s he became editor of the *Irish People*, the newspaper of the Irish Republican Brotherhood, for which he was arrested in 1865, tried for treason, and sentenced to twenty years' penal servitude. He served five years before being released on condition of exile; he spent the next fifteen years in Paris, where he read widely in French literature and developed the cosmopolitan culture that distinguished him from the narrower nationalists of his generation. He returned to Dublin in 1885, and it was then that the twenty-year-old Yeats sought him out.

Yeats's tribute to O'Leary was lifelong. In his *Autobiographies*, he wrote: "From O'Leary I learnt that a man must not do certain things to save his nation. He would not write a lie to save it, and he thought nobody else had a right to hold any other opinion." This maxim — that the ends do not justify the means, that integrity matters more than political victory — became a touchstone of Yeats's political thinking.

## Key Facts

- **Born**: 1830, Tipperary, Ireland
- **Died**: 1907, Dublin, Ireland
- **Role**: Fenian leader, editor, mentor to Yeats
- **Connection to Yeats**: Yeats's most influential early mentor; the emblem of a heroic, magnanimous Irish nationalism

## Significance in Yeats's Work

O'Leary appears in Yeats's poetry less as a living presence than as an absent ideal. The great poem of O'Leary is "September 1913," written six years after O'Leary's death, in which Yeats surveys the squalid Dublin of the Lock-out and the Home Rule crisis and contrasts it with the Ireland of O'Leary's generation. The refrain — "Romantic Ireland's dead and gone, / It's with O'Leary in the grave" [044-426] — is a triple hammer-blow, each iteration more devastating than the last.

The poem lists the heroes of an earlier nationalism — John O'Leary, Edward Fitzgerald, Robert Emmet, Wolfe Tone — and asks: "Was it for this the wild geese spread / The grey wing upon every tide; / For this that all that blood was shed?" The answer is implicit in the question. The Ireland of 1913, "fumbling in a greasy till," has betrayed the heroic nationalism of O'Leary's generation. Yeats added a note to the poem in 1916, after the Easter Rising: "'Romantic Ireland's dead and gone' sounds old-fashioned now. It seemed true in 1913, but I did not foresee 1916" [048-019].

O'Leary also appears in "The Phases of the Moon," where Yeats places him — along with other figures from his personal pantheon — in the symbolic system of *A Vision*. The gesture is characteristic: O'Leary the historical person is absorbed into the Yeatsian symbolic order, becoming a type of a certain phase of being.

## Further Context

O'Leary's influence on Yeats was not only political but literary. O'Leary had a fine library and introduced Yeats to the Young Ireland poets — Thomas Davis, James Clarence Mangan — whose ambition to create a national literature for Ireland shaped Yeats's own sense of vocation. O'Leary urged Yeats to write on Irish subjects and to develop a distinctively Irish voice. In this sense, O'Leary stands at the origin of Yeats's cultural nationalism — the conviction that Ireland needed a literature before it could become a nation.

O'Leary was also a link to an older, more generous tradition of Irish republicanism that predated the Catholic sectarianism Yeats deplored. O'Leary was a Catholic but not a sectarian; a revolutionary but not a hater; a nationalist who read French literature and had lived in Paris. He represented, for Yeats, what Irish nationalism could be at its best — and what, by 1913, it had ceased to be.
