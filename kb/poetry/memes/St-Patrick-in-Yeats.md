# Saint Patrick

Saint Patrick (c. 385–461), the Romano-British missionary traditionally credited with bringing Christianity to Ireland in the fifth century, appears in W. B. Yeats's poetry primarily through his early epic "The Wanderings of Oisin" (1889), where he serves as the central antagonist — the representative of Christian orthodoxy confronting the dying pagan world of the Fenian hero [[oisin]].

## In "The Wanderings of Oisin"

Yeats's "The Wanderings of Oisin" is structured as a dialogue between the aged Oisin, returned to Ireland after three hundred years in the immortal islands of the Sidhe, and Saint Patrick, who has converted Ireland to Christianity during Oisin's absence. The poem is framed by Patrick's questions and Oisin's replies: Patrick interrogates, Oisin remembers — and defends the pagan world that Patrick's faith has supplanted.

The opposition between Patrick and Oisin embodies the central conflict of Yeats's early imagination: the struggle between the enchanted, heroic, pre-Christian Ireland and the doctrinal, ascetic, repentant Ireland of Christian orthodoxy. Patrick speaks for the new order — "You are still wrecked among heathen dreams" (015-035) — while Oisin speaks for the world of the Fenian warriors, the Sidhe, and the immortal beauty of the islands he has visited. Patrick wins the argument (Oisin is baptised before he dies, and Ireland is Christian) but the poem's emotional weight is entirely with Oisin — with the lost world, the enchanted islands, the heroic companionship of the Fianna.

## The Voice of the New Order

Patrick's role in the poem is not merely that of a debating opponent; he is the dramatic frame that shapes Oisin's narrative into elegy. Every stanza of Oisin's recollection is an answer to Patrick's interrogation: "What dream came with you that you came?" (015-051). Patrick's questioning voice represents the pressure of the new Christian order upon the old — a pressure that transforms Oisin's heroic autobiography into a confession, and his celebration of pagan joy into a lament.

This framing device is Yeats's most sophisticated formal innovation in the poem. By making Patrick the auditor rather than the speaker, Yeats reverses the expected hierarchy: the Christian saint is silenced, reduced to asking questions, while the pagan hero — who should be the defeated party — fills the poem with the rich imagery of the immortal islands, the dancing immortals, and the heroic deeds of the Fianna. Patrick's presence is felt throughout the poem as the force that compels Oisin to remember, but Oisin's voice overwhelms Patrick's theology with the sheer sensual abundance of the lost world.

## Yeats's Source

Yeats drew the Saint Patrick of his poem not from hagiography but from Gaelic poetic tradition. The Endnotes record that "The Wanderings of Oisin" was derived "from a Gaelic poem of the Eighteenth Century" (048-005), and the figure of Patrick in that tradition is already established as Oisin's interrogator in a series of _Agallamh Oisín agus Phádraig_ (Colloquy of Oisin and Patrick) poems. In these Gaelic sources, Patrick typically questions Oisin about the Fianna and the pagan past, while Oisin replies with tales of heroic glory, lamenting the present age of fasting and bells. Yeats adopted this inherited structure but intensified the emotional stakes: in his version, Patrick represents not merely a rival religion but a metaphysical impoverishment — the world that has exchanged the "immortal desire of immortals" for the thin diet of Christian penance.

## Symbolic Role

Saint Patrick functions in Yeats's poetic mythology as a figure for:

- **The triumph of Christianity over paganism**: The historical shift that extinguished the old Ireland Yeats sought to recover through poetry. Patrick represents the doctrinal, ascetic force that displaced the heroic and magical.
- **The conflict between faith and imagination**: Patrick's Christianity demands belief and repentance; Oisin's pagan memory offers vision and celebration. Yeats consistently sides with the poet over the priest.
- **The elegiac frame**: Patrick's presence makes Oisin's story an elegy, not an epic — a lament for what has been lost, told to the very force that destroyed it.
- **The poet as pagan**: By identifying with Oisin against Patrick, the young Yeats claimed for poetry the authority of the pre-Christian imagination — a claim he would maintain throughout his career, even as his occult and philosophical interests deepened.

## Beyond Oisin

Saint Patrick appears elsewhere in Yeats's work only obliquely. In "The Tower" (1928), Yeats invokes the "ancient, glittering" figures of the Irish saints in passing, and his late interest in the Byzantine Empire suggests a search for a Christianity not shaped by the ascetic, world-denying impulse that Patrick, in Yeats's reading, represented. The Endnotes also record that Yeats associated the quality of "the magical sleep" — a visionary trance state — with figures from Irish legend including the faery who carried Oisin into Faeryland (048-018). But it is in the youthful epic of 1889 that Patrick achieves his fullest Yeatsian embodiment — as the voice of the world that turned Ireland from a land of heroes into a land of penitents.
