## Introduction

Trees form one of the most ancient and densely symbolic image clusters in the poetry of W. B. Yeats. From the "hazel wood" [038-120] of the early Celtic Twilight lyrics to the "great boughs" [032-171] of the middle-period Rose poems, arboreal imagery connects Yeats' verse to the deep roots of Irish mythology — where the sacred hazel tree drops its nuts of wisdom into the waters of the Otherworld — and to the Romantic tradition of nature as a site of visionary experience. The principal trees of the Yeatsian forest — hazel, apple, oak, and poplar — each carry distinct symbolic weight rooted in Celtic lore, while the woodland itself recurs as a liminal space where the boundaries between the natural and supernatural dissolve.

## The Hazel

No tree is more charged with symbolic meaning in Yeats' work than the hazel. In Irish mythology, the nine hazel trees of wisdom grow at the well of Segais in the Otherworld, their nuts dropping into the water to be eaten by the Salmon of Knowledge. Yeats draws on this mythological substrate in "The Song of Wandering Aengus," where the speaker goes "out to the hazel wood" [038-120], cuts "a hazel wand" [038-122], and experiences a visionary encounter with a "glimmering girl" [038-127] — the hazel becoming the instrument of supernatural revelation. The poet later declares "I have been a hazel tree and they hung / The pilot star and the crooked plough among my leaves" [038-499], identifying himself with the world-tree that links earth to the constellations. In "The Two Trees," "the shadowy hazel grove" [031-276] stands in contrast to the "bitter glass" [031-279] of worldly perception — the hazel grove is the site of authentic, inward vision. The hazel reappears in the late poem "The Tower" as part of the landscape of Thoor Ballylee, rooting the mythological tree in the actual Irish ground.

## The Apple

The apple tree belongs to the faery realm and to erotic love. In "The Song of Wandering Aengus," the "silver apples of the moon" [038-141] and "golden apples of the sun" [038-142] are the object of the visionary quest — fruits that combine celestial light with earthly sweetness. The apple tree appears throughout the Rose poems as a site of romantic encounter: "I heard where among the apple trees" [005-019] the voice of the beloved in "The Rose of the World." In "A Prayer for My Daughter," the "shadow of cherry, and shadow of apple trees" [022-029] provide the sheltering domestic landscape in which the child may grow — the apple shifting from faery symbol to emblem of rooted, familial life. The apple tree thus traces Yeats' movement from the Celtic Twilight's supernatural orchard to the middle period's celebration of earthly rootedness.

## The Oak and the Ancient Wood

The oak represents endurance, antiquity, and rooted wisdom. In the early poem "The Sullen Wood," the speaker encounters a "woodland valley" [003-002] and "the sullen wood" [003-032] — the forest as a place of mysterious, brooding presence. "Green oak and poplar tree" [014-010] appear in "The Road at My Door" as landmarks of the Irish landscape that endure beyond human sorrow. The woodland itself — "the living woods" [011-001], "the hollow woodlands" [011-017], "the fragrant wood" [009-010] — is the primary setting of Yeats' early supernatural verse, a liminal zone where faeries dance and the "bird in the branches will cry for us clear" [009-007]. The ancient wood is the Irish equivalent of the Romantic forest: a place outside ordinary time where vision becomes possible. "The reason of the trembling trees" [015-170] in "The Wanderings of Usheen" is the life that animates all nature, a force at once beautiful and uncanny.

## The Sacred Tree

Beyond individual species, the tree in Yeats functions as a figure for the axis mundi — the world-axis that connects earth, humanity, and heaven. In "The Two Trees," the sacred tree "shakes its blossoms free" [031-011] and its "great boughs drop tranquillity" [032-171], making it a source of mystical peace. The hazel that bears "the pilot star and the crooked plough" [038-499] among its leaves is the cosmic tree of Irish and universal mythology. In "A Prayer for My Daughter," the poet wishes for his child to be "like some green laurel / Rooted in one dear perpetual place" [022-097] — the tree becoming the ideal image of the rooted, flourishing life. This sacred-tree symbolism reaches back to the "quick tree" [031-365] of Irish legend and forward to the "great-rooted blossomer" [045-083] of the late poems, the chestnut tree that images the unity of being in Yeats' final philosophical vision.
