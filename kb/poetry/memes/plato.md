# Plato

Plato occupies a singular place in W. B. Yeats's intellectual universe — not merely as a philosopher to be studied, but as a living presence whose thought permeates the mature poetry. Yeats read Plato through multiple lenses: the Neoplatonic tradition of [[plotinus]] and Proclus, the Romantic Platonism of Shelley and Blake, and his own occult studies in the Hermetic Order of the Golden Dawn. The result is a Plato who is at once the philosopher of eternal Forms and a fellow-traveller in the Western esoteric tradition — "Choose Plato and Plotinus for a friend" [047-044], Yeats writes, as though the ancient thinkers were companions at the fireside.

## Introduction

Yeats's engagement with Plato spans his entire career, intensifying dramatically in the period of *[[a-vision]]* (1925) and the poems of *[[the-tower-collection]]* (1928). What drew Yeats to Plato was not the dialectical method or the political philosophy but the visionary core — the doctrine that the visible world is a shadow cast by eternal realities, that the soul pre-exists and survives the body, and that history moves in great cycles. Each of these convictions found its way into Yeats's own symbolic system.

The Platonic Year — the 36,000-year cycle in which the celestial spheres return to their original positions — became, in Yeats's hands, the model for the interpenetrating [[gyres|gyres]] that govern historical change. When Yeats wrote of "the Platonic Year" [048-085] and "whirling dust," he was collapsing cosmology and history into a single image of eternal recurrence.

## Plato in Yeats's Poetry

Plato's presence in Yeats's verse is most explicit in "The Tower" (1928), where he appears in a list of the great minds the aging poet summons for contemplation:

> "And cry in Plato's teeth, / Death and life were not / Till man made up the whole, / Made lock, stock and barrel / Out of his bitter soul" [047-179]

This passage is characteristically Yeatsian in its ambivalence: he invokes Plato only to argue against him. The Forms, in Yeats's reading, are not pre-existent realities but human creations — "mockers" and "mocker's" fabrications. Yet the very act of arguing with Plato affirms the philosopher's centrality: Yeats cannot think through the problem of Being without Plato as his interlocutor.

In "Among School Children," Yeats alludes to the Platonic doctrine of pre-natal knowledge — the idea that learning is recollection:

> "'Il Penseroso''s Platonist toiled on / In some like chamber, finding with a sigh / That even the greatest of the dead are gone" [047-283]

The "Platonist" of Milton's companion poem becomes a type of Yeats himself — the solitary thinker who discovers that even the Forms cannot arrest time's passage. The famous close of "Among School Children" — "How can we know the dancer from the dance?" — is, among other things, a Yeatsian answer to Plato's separation of form from matter, of the eternal from the temporal.

## Plato, Plotinus, and the Neoplatonic Tradition

Yeats rarely read Plato alone. His Plato was always already mediated by the Neoplatonic tradition — the synthesis of Platonic, Aristotelian, and mystical thought that flowed from [[plotinus]] (c. 204–270 CE) through Proclus, the Florentine Academy, and the Cambridge Platonists to the occult revival of the nineteenth century.

> "Plato thought nature but a spume that plays / Upon a ghostly paradigm of things" [047-708]

This couplet from "The Tower" distills the Neoplatonic reading of Plato into pure poetry. "Ghostly paradigm" is Yeats's rendering of the Platonic Form — not a rational abstraction but something haunting, almost Gothic. The word "ghostly" does the work of yoking the Platonic *eidos* to the Celtic and occult traditions of spirit-presence that Yeats had cultivated since his youth.

When Yeats wrote "Choose Plato and Plotinus for a friend" [047-044], the conjunction is significant. Plotinus, the third-century Alexandrian who taught that the soul emanates from the One and yearns to return, is for Yeats the necessary complement to Plato — the mystic who completed what the dialectician began.
