# Ancestral Houses

"Ancestral Houses" is the opening poem of Yeats's seven-part sequence "Meditations in Time of Civil War," written at [[thoor-ballylee|Thoor Ballylee]] in 1922 during the Irish Civil War. The poem examines the great houses of the Anglo-Irish Ascendancy — their architectural grandeur, their blood-stained foundations, and their ambiguous legacy — as a meditation on inheritance, violence, and civilisation itself.

## Position in the Sequence

"Meditations in Time of Civil War" consists of seven sections: "Ancestral Houses," "My House," "My Table," "My Descendants," "The Road at My Door," "The Stare's Nest by My Window," and "I See Phantoms of Hatred." "Ancestral Houses" sets the philosophical frame: the great houses of the gentry, built with wealth extracted from the land and its people, are nevertheless objects of beauty that seem to transcend their origins.

## Themes

The poem's central paradox is that beauty emerges from violence. The great house — "slippered contemplation," "a painted floor" — is built on foundations laid by "some violent bitter man, some powerful man." Yeats does not resolve this paradox; he holds it in suspension, acknowledging both the beauty of the houses and the violence that made them possible.

This ambivalence reflects Yeats's own position: a Protestant Irishman who admired the Ascendancy while recognising its historical injustices, a poet who lived in a Norman tower while a civil war raged around him.
