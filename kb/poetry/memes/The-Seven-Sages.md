# The Seven Sages

"The Seven Sages" is a poem from Yeats's 1933 collection *The Winding Stair and Other Poems*, in which the poet champions the Anglo-Irish intellectual tradition of the eighteenth century as an alternative model of Irish identity to the one offered by contemporary nationalist politics. The poem takes the form of a dialogue in which the speaker names the great figures of Protestant Ascendancy Ireland — Jonathan Swift, Edmund Burke, George Berkeley, and Oliver Goldsmith — as the true founders of Irish intellectual life.

## The Anglo-Irish Tradition

The poem is a central statement of Yeats's late attachment to the Anglo-Irish Ascendancy as a cultural ideal. Writing in the years following the establishment of the Irish Free State (1922), in which he served as a senator, Yeats grew increasingly disillusioned with the direction of Irish politics. "The Seven Sages" articulates an alternative genealogy: not the revolutionary nationalism of the nineteenth century, but the eighteenth-century "Protestant intellect" represented by writers and thinkers who, in Yeats's view, combined Irish roots with European breadth.

The poem's central figures — Swift, Burke, Berkeley, and Goldsmith — were all born in Ireland, all members of the established Protestant Church, and all figures of international stature. Yeats names "the people of Burke and of Grattan" [047-164] as bearers of a tradition of eloquence, intellect, and public service that the poet saw as endangered by the cultural narrowing of the new state.

## The Dialogue Form

Like several of Yeats's late poems, "The Seven Sages" employs a dialogue structure in which speakers debate the value of the Anglo-Irish inheritance. This conversational form allows Yeats to dramatize conflicting attitudes toward the Ascendancy tradition — acknowledging its limitations while fiercely defending its intellectual and cultural achievements. The poem stages a quarrel within the poet's own sensibility: between the nationalist who wanted an authentically Irish culture and the aristocrat who admired the cosmopolitan achievements of the Protestant eighteenth century.

## Significance in Yeats's Late Work

"The Seven Sages" belongs to a cluster of late Yeats poems — including "Blood and the Moon," "Coole Park, 1929," and "Ancestral Houses" — that meditate on the great house, the Ascendancy tradition, and the relationship between architecture, lineage, and intellectual life. Together these poems constitute Yeats's most sustained engagement with the question of what kind of civilisation Ireland could claim as its own. The "Sages" provide an answer: a tradition older than the revolutionary nationalism of Yeats's youth, and one that connected Ireland to the mainstream of European thought.
