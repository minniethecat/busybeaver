# Song and Poetry

Song in Yeats is poetry in its most authentic form — oral, rhythmic, communally shared, inseparable from music and ritual. The "thousand songs" ((015-072)) that Usheen promises to make connect the poet to the Irish bardic tradition, where poetry was sung, not read, and the poet's voice carried ancestral memory and prophetic authority. This self-understanding shapes Yeats's entire career: he wrote to be heard, not merely read.

## Origins and Sources

- **Irish bardic tradition**: The filidh and bards of Gaelic Ireland, who composed and performed orally, serving as historians, genealogists, and satirists
- **Celtic otherworld**: The "Danaan songs" ((015-129)) — the music of the Sidhe, which mortal poets can overhear in moments of vision
- **Romanticism**: Shelley's "unacknowledged legislators of the world" — the poet as prophetic voice
- **Folk ballad**: The popular song tradition of rural Ireland, which Yeats collected and imitated in his early work

## Symbolic Meaning

1. **Song as immortality**: "I will make a thousand songs" ((015-072)) — through song/poetry, the poet and his subjects achieve a kind of immortality. The songs outlast the singer.

2. **Song as otherworldly communication**: The "Danaan songs" ((015-129)) come from the fairy realm — true poetry is a transmission from the supernatural, not a personal invention.

3. **Song vs. silence**: "the lying clerics murder song" ((015-598)) — in Usheen's lament, Christianity silences the pagan music of Ireland. The suppression of song is the suppression of a civilization.

4. **Song as love-offering**: Songs of love ((006-036)) are the poet's gift to the beloved — the traditional courtly love gesture, elevated to mythic scale.

## Development Across Yeats's Career

**Early period**: Song is explicitly bardic — Yeats positions himself as inheritor of the Irish oral tradition. The early poems are designed to sound like folk ballads, with refrains and musical repetition.

**Middle period**: Song becomes more complex, less literally musical. The "thousand songs" promise is paradoxically fulfilled through increasingly sophisticated written poetry that nevertheless retains oral rhythms and refrain structures.

**Late period**: The concept matures into self-awareness. The late poems acknowledge the artifice of song even as they achieve its most perfect form — poems like "Sailing to Byzantium" are "songs" of a golden bird, artificial yet immortal, singing "of what is past, or passing, or to come."
