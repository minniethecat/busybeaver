Anne Butler Yeats (26 February 1919 – 4 July 2001) was the daughter of the poet W. B. Yeats and [[Georgie-Yeats|George Hyde-Lees]] — born during the period of the automatic writing sessions, raised at [[thoor-ballylee]] in County Galway, and later the keeper of her father's literary estate. She appears directly and indirectly in several of Yeats's most intimate poems, where she serves as a figure of the next generation — the fragile human future that justifies the poet's labour, even as he knows he will not live to see it. In her own right, Anne Yeats became a significant Irish painter, set and costume designer for the [[abbey-theatre]], and founding member of the Yeats Society.

## Biographical Summary

Anne was born in Dublin, the second child and only daughter of W. B. and George Yeats (her brother Michael was born in 1921). She spent her early childhood at Thoor Ballylee in County Galway, where the family lived during the summers from 1919 to 1929, and at the family home at 82 Merrion Square in Dublin. Her father wrote "A Prayer for My Daughter" in the weeks following her birth, and the poem's central image — the child sleeping through a storm while her father meditates on the future — established Anne as a symbolic presence in Yeats's poetry: the innocent inheritor of a violent and uncertain world.

After Yeats's death in 1939, Anne and her brother Michael were raised by their mother George in Dublin. Anne studied stage design at the Dublin Metropolitan School of Art and became a painter and theatrical designer, working extensively at the Abbey Theatre and at the Peacock. Her paintings — large-scale abstract and semi-abstract works, often in rich earth tones — were exhibited regularly in Dublin and London. She never married and lived her entire adult life in the family home on Merrion Square, where she maintained her father's papers and manuscripts until they were transferred to the National Library of Ireland under the terms of the Yeats family trust.

Anne Yeats died in Dublin on 4 July 2001, aged 82. Her death marked the end of the direct Yeats family line and the release of the remaining Yeats manuscripts — including the automatic writing notebooks — to public access.

## Anne in Yeats's Poetry

**"A Prayer for My Daughter"** (1919): The most sustained poetic address to Anne. Written at Thoor Ballylee during a storm, the poem moves from the immediate image of the infant sleeping in her cradle to a wide-ranging meditation on beauty, virtue, hatred, and the kind of life the poet wishes for his child. The famous closing stanza imagines her future marriage in "a house / Where all's accustomed, ceremonious" — a vision of rooted, traditional life opposed to the "arrogance and hatred" of the public world outside.

**"A Prayer for My Son"** (1921): Written for Anne's brother Michael, but conceptually paired with the earlier poem for Anne. Together, the two "prayer" poems establish the Yeats children as the poet's stake in the future — the fragile bearers of hope in a world of gyring violence.

**"In Memory of Eva Gore-Booth and Con Markievicz"** (1927/1929): Though not directly about Anne, the poem's meditation on the lost world of Lissadell — "the light of evening, Lissadell, / Great windows open to the south" — is informed by Yeats's experience of fatherhood and his sense of the fragility of the beauty he wishes to pass on to his children.

## Role in Yeats Studies

Anne Yeats's most lasting contribution to Yeats scholarship was her stewardship of the Yeats papers. For over fifty years, she controlled access to her father's manuscripts — a role that made her, according to one biographer, "the most powerful gatekeeper in modern Irish literary studies." Her decisions about what to release and when shaped the scholarly understanding of Yeats, particularly of the automatic writing sessions and the drafting process behind _A Vision_. The gradual release of the papers under the Yeats family trust, completed after her death, has enabled the "Yeats's Vision" project and the ongoing re-evaluation of George Yeats's role in the composition of _A Vision_.
