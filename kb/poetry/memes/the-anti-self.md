# The Anti-Self

The anti-self is the central term in Yeats's doctrine of the Mask — the idea that the artist, and indeed every person, achieves their fullest being not by expressing their natural self but by deliberately cultivating its opposite. "The most unlike, being my anti-self" (045-959) — the line, from the dialogue poem "Ego Dominus Tuus," condenses the entire doctrine into a single hexameter.

## Origins

The concept emerges most fully in *Per Amica Silentia Lunae* (1918) and is systematised in *A Vision* (1925). Yeats's source is partly Platonic (the *daimon* as a spiritual double), partly from his occult studies in the Hermetic Order of the Golden Dawn, and partly from his own experience as a poet who found that his most powerful verse was written not in his own voice but in what he called the "other self" — a voice antithetical to the shy, dreamy character of his youth.

In *Per Amica Silentia Lunae*, Yeats describes the moment he first grasped the doctrine clearly. Reflecting on the creative process, he writes that the poet "never speaks directly as to someone at the breakfast table" — the authentic poetic voice is always the voice of another self, constructed through discipline and artifice. The essay "Anima Hominis," which opens the volume, develops the idea that each person is accompanied through life by a *Daimon* — a spiritual opposite — and that the highest human achievement is to "seek out" and unite with this antithetical being.

## The Doctrine

Yeats's argument, developed across the dialogues of "Ego Dominus Tuus" and the prose of *A Vision*, is that human personality is constituted by a tension between the self and the anti-self. The self is what one is by nature; the anti-self is what one wills oneself to become. The creative life — and the heroic life — consists in overcoming the self to achieve union with the anti-self, a process Yeats called "the Mask."

The anti-self is not a disguise but a revelation. By striving toward what one is not, one discovers what one most truly is. The doctrine reverses the Romantic cult of sincerity: for Yeats, the sincere self is merely the unformed self, and it is only through artifice — through the deliberate assumption of an antithetical mask — that the deepest authenticity is achieved.

## The Anti-Self in Yeats's Poetic Practice

The doctrine of the anti-self was not only a philosophical proposition but a working method for Yeats. Throughout his career, he created dramatic personae — Michael Robartes, Owen Aherne, Crazy Jane, the Wild Old Wicked Man — who gave voice to perspectives antithetical to his own. These masks allowed him to explore extremes of thought and feeling that would have been unavailable to the decorous, middle-aged public figure he had become. Crazy Jane, in particular, represents the anti-self of the aging poet: where Yeats himself brooded on decay and lost passion, Jane speaks with defiant, bawdy vitality.

The dialogue form of "Ego Dominus Tuus" — in which the poem's two voices, *Hic* and *Ille*, debate the nature of artistic creation — is itself a dramatisation of the self / anti-self tension. *Ille*, who speaks for the doctrine of the Mask, argues that the poet "never speaks directly as to someone at the breakfast table" and that all true poetry comes from the antithetical self. The poem's title, Latin for "I, your master," suggests the commanding authority that the anti-self exercises over the merely natural self.

### The Anti-Self in the Later Poetry

The doctrine becomes increasingly autobiographical in Yeats's poetry of the 1920s and 1930s. In "The Tower" (1928), the aging poet confronts his own physical decline by summoning up an antithetical image of himself — proud, defiant, commanding — that his body can no longer sustain. The famous lines "What shall I do with this absurdity — / O heart, O troubled heart — this caricature, / Decrepit age that has been tied to me / As to a dog's tail?" draw their power from the gap between the bodily self and the anti-self that the imagination projects. Similarly, "Sailing to Byzantium" (1928) stages the anti-self not as a mask assumed in this life but as the "artifice of eternity" — a golden bird that will sing "to lords and ladies of Byzantium" after the natural body has been consumed. The poem's entire movement from "that country" of the young to the "holy city" of art is a dramatisation of the self's journey toward its antithesis.

## Relation to the Gyres

The anti-self is the psychological expression of Yeats's larger historical cosmology. In *A Vision*, history moves through alternating gyres — one primary (objective, democratic, natural), the other antithetical (subjective, aristocratic, artificial). Just as civilisations are driven toward their opposites, so individual souls are driven toward their anti-selves. The personal and the historical are governed by the same law. The artist who cultivates the anti-self is therefore not merely pursuing a private discipline but aligning with the deepest rhythm of existence.
