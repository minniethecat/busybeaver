# George Berkeley

George Berkeley (1685–1753) was an Irish philosopher of the early modern period, an Anglican bishop, and — in W. B. Yeats's imagination — one of the great intellectual heroes of Protestant Ireland. Berkeley's idealist philosophy (*esse est percipi* — "to be is to be perceived") resonated deeply with Yeats's own metaphysical preoccupations, particularly his conviction that mind and spirit are primary and that the material world is a construct of perception.

## Berkeley in Yeats's Poetry

Berkeley appears in two of Yeats's most significant late poems about the Anglo-Irish intellectual tradition:

**"The Seven Sages" (1933)** — Yeats assembles a personal pantheon of eighteenth-century Irish intellects: Swift, Burke, Berkeley, and Goldsmith. These figures are praised not merely as writers or thinkers but as exemplars of a mode of being — passionate, independent, intellectually courageous — that Yeats saw as endangered by the cultural narrowing of the new Irish state.

**"Blood and the Moon" (1928)** — Berkeley is cited alongside Swift, Burke, and Grattan as part of the "blood" of Irish intellectual lineage, connected to the tower and the moon — Yeats's symbols of ancestral authority and the life of the mind. The poem's famous half-line, "And haughtier-headed Burke that proved the State a tree," is followed by the invocation of Berkeley's immaterialism as a counter-force to Locke's empiricism.

## Philosophical Significance for Yeats

Berkeley's immaterialist philosophy — the argument that material objects exist only as perceptions in minds, and that their continued existence depends upon their being perceived by God — offered Yeats a philosophical framework compatible with his own anti-materialist convictions. Yeats was drawn to any philosophy that denied the ultimacy of matter:

- **Anti-empiricism**: Yeats saw Berkeley as the great opposite of John Locke, whose empiricism Yeats blamed for the soullessness of modernity. Where Locke grounded knowledge in sensory experience of a material world, Berkeley dissolved matter into perception and affirmed the primacy of spirit.
- **Unity of Being**: Berkeley's vision of a world held in existence by divine perception offered an analog to Yeats's "Unity of Being" — the ideal state in which thought and action, soul and body, are not in opposition.
- **Irish intellectual tradition**: Berkeley was central to Yeats's construction of an eighteenth-century Protestant golden age — an Ireland of Swift, Berkeley, Burke, and Goldsmith that represented intellectual cosmopolitanism before the sectarian divisions of the nineteenth and twentieth centuries.

## Sources and References

- Berkeley appears primarily in Yeats's prose (*A Vision*, essays on Berkeley and idealism) and in the late poems "The Seven Sages" and "Blood and the Moon"
- Yeats read Berkeley through the lens of his own occult and idealist commitments, treating him less as a systematic philosopher than as a symbol of metaphysical revolt against materialism
- Berkeley's Irishness — he was born in Kilkenny and educated at Trinity College Dublin — was essential to Yeats's claim that the Anglo-Irish had produced a distinctive intellectual tradition independent of England
