# Knocknarea

Knocknarea (Irish: _Cnoc na Rí_, "Hill of the King") is a mountain in Co. Sligo, visible from much of the surrounding countryside, crowned by a massive Neolithic stone cairn traditionally identified as the tomb of Queen Maeve (Medb) of Connacht. Yeats incorporates Knocknarea into his mythological landscape, using it as a symbol of ancient Irish sovereignty, feminine power, and the persistence of the pre-Christian past beneath the surface of modern Ireland.

## Location and Geography

- **Type**: real Irish location (mountain with megalithic monument)
- **Region**: Co. Sligo, Connacht
- **Summit**: 327m, topped by Maeve's Cairn (Miosgán Meadhbha) — one of the largest unexcavated passage tombs in Ireland

## Significance to Yeats

Knocknarea appears in Yeats's poetry and prose as the grave of Queen Maeve — the warrior queen of Connacht, protagonist of the _Táin Bó Cúailnge_, and symbol of pre-Christian Irish sovereignty. In his endnotes to _The Tower_, Yeats records the local belief "that Maeve, still a great queen of the western Sidhe, is buried in the cairn," (048-015) blending archaeological fact with living folk tradition.

Queen Maeve fascinated Yeats throughout his career: she appears in "The Old Age of Queen Maeve" (1903) and in the late poem "The Three Bushes." She represents a specifically female, pre-Christian, and Irish form of power — sexual, martial, sovereign — that Yeats contrasted with the patriarchal authority of both British rule and Catholic Ireland.

The cairn itself, visible for miles, is a monument to memory — a Neolithic structure that has outlasted every civilization that has occupied Ireland. For Yeats, Knocknarea embodied the "monuments of unageing intellect" that the aging poet aspired to join.

## Literary and Historical Context

Knocknarea is part of a rich Neolithic landscape in Co. Sligo that includes Carrowmore (one of the largest megalithic cemeteries in Ireland) and Carrowkeel. Yeats's use of the site taps into a deep vein of Irish cultural nationalism that saw the megalithic monuments as evidence of a sophisticated pre-Christian civilization.
