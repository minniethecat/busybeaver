## Introduction

The Sidhe (pronounced "shee") are the faery folk of Irish mythology, identified by W. B. Yeats as the surviving gods of ancient Ireland. Known in Gaelic as _Aes Sidhe_ ("people of the faery hills") or _Sluagh Sidhe_ ("host of the Sidhe"), they are the [[tuatha-de-danaan|Tuatha De Danaan]] of old — the Tribes of the goddess Danu — who, after being defeated by the Milesians, retreated into the hills and forts of Ireland, where they continue to inhabit an invisible world parallel to the mortal one [048-013]. For Yeats, the Sidhe were not merely folkloric decorations but living presences, intimately connected to the Irish landscape and the imaginative life of its people.

## The Sidhe and the Wind

Yeats notes the linguistic and symbolic connection between the Sidhe and the wind: "_Sidhe_ is also Gaelic for wind, and certainly the Sidhe have much to do with the wind. They journey in whirling wind, the winds that were called the dance of the daughters of Herodias in the Middle Ages" [048-013]. When country people see leaves whirling on the road, they bless themselves because they believe the Sidhe to be passing by. The Sidhe are almost always said to wear no covering upon their heads, letting their hair stream out, and the great among them go much upon horseback [048-013]. This association with the wind is woven into Yeats's poetry — in "[[the-host-of-the-air|The Host of the Air]]" and "[[The-Hosting-of-the-Sidhe|The Hosting of the Sidhe]]," the faery host rides the wind, their presence signalled by atmospheric disturbance.

## Physical Description and Habits

In the folk traditions Yeats collected, the Sidhe are described as tall, beautiful, and bare-headed, with streaming hair. A man cutting a hedge near Gort, in Galway, described seeing "a girl picking nuts, with her hair hanging down over her shoulders; brown hair; and she had a good, clean face, and she was tall, and nothing on her head, and her dress no way gaudy, but simple" [048-024]. When she became aware of him, she "gathered herself up, and was gone, as if the earth had swallowed her up" [048-024]. This ability to vanish into the landscape is characteristic of Sidhe encounters.

The Sidhe are shape-shifters. Those that dwell in the waters "take often the shape of fish" and "sometimes try to come over the side of the boat in the form of fishes, for they can take their choice shape" [048-023]. At other times they appear as beautiful women with shining eyes, capable of raising storms at sea. One Galway fisherman reported seeing a woman in the sea with eyes "shining like candles" who nearly sank his boat [048-023].

## The "Touch" and Those Who Are "Away"

Perhaps the most distinctive aspect of Sidhe lore in Yeats's work is the phenomenon of being "taken" or "away." Those who become too interested in the Sidhe "lose all interest in ordinary things" [048-013]. The person who is "away" falls into a half-dream, their true life having gone out of the world to dwell among the hills and forts of the Sidhe [048-018]. A faery doctor explained the condition of his wife, who "got the touch" at her marriage: she had entered "the magical sleep, to which people have given a new name lately, that makes the imagination so passive that it can be moulded by any voice in any world into any shape" [048-018].

A mere likeness — "some one or some thing the Sidhe have no longer a use for" — is believed to be left in place of the person who is "away" [048-018]. This substitution motif, reminiscent of the changeling tradition, appears throughout Yeats's folkloric writings. The belief holds that the Sidhe value most those whom they cast into a half-dream lasting years, for they "need the help of a living person in most of the things they do" [048-018].

## The Battles of the Sidhe

Yeats identifies a complex of mythological battles involving the Sidhe. He compares four battles: a battle the Sidhe fight when a person is being taken away; a battle they fight in November for the harvest; the great battle the Tuatha De Danaan fought against the Fomor at Moy Tura; and the prophesied Battle of the Black Pig [048-031]. Yeats reads these as manifestations of a single cosmic struggle: "the battle among the Sidhe for the harvest is the annual battle of summer and winter; the battle among the Sidhe at a man's death is the battle of life and death; and the battle of the Black Pig is the battle between the manifest world and the ancestral darkness at the end of all things" [048-032]. He concludes: "all these battles are one, the battle of all things with shadowy decay" [048-032].

Yeats heard accounts of the death-battle firsthand. An old Aran fisherman told him it was fought over two of his children, and that he "found blood in a box he had for keeping fish, when it was over" [048-032]. The faery doctor on the borders of Galway and Clare explained it as "a battle between the friends and enemies of the dying, the one party trying to take them, the other trying to save them from being taken" [048-032].

## The Sluagh Gaoith and Sluagh Sidhe

Some writers distinguish between the _Sluagh Gaoith_, the host of the air, and _Sluagh Sidhe_, the host of the Sidhe, describing the host of the air as possessing "a peculiar malignancy" [048-016]. Dr. Joyce notes that "of all the different kinds of goblins … air demons were most dreaded by the people. They lived among clouds, and mists, and rocks, and hated the human race with the utmost malignity" [048-016]. Yeats, however, is "inclined to think that the distinction came in with Christianity and its belief about the prince of the air, for the host of the Sidhe … are closely associated with the wind" [048-016]. A very old Aran charm captures the dual threat: "Send God, by his strength, between us and the host of the Sidhe, between us and the host of the air" [048-016].

## The Sidhe in Yeats's Poetry

In "The Hosting of the Sidhe," the faery host rides out from Knocknarea: "The host is riding from [[knocknarea-mountain|Knocknarea]] / And over the grave of Clooth-na-Bare" [039-033]. The poem invokes the many-changing nature of the Sidhe and their seductive, dangerous power. In "The Old Age of Queen [[Maeve|Queen-Maeve]]," matters are "afoot among the Sidhe" [039-053] as the ancient queen stirs. The Sidhe appear throughout Yeats's early and middle-period poetry, often at the threshold between the mortal and immortal worlds — a liminal space that was central to his imaginative project.

For Yeats, once a symbolism "has possessed the imagination of large numbers of men, it becomes … an embodiment of disembodied powers, and repeats itself in dreams and visions, age after age" [048-032]. The Sidhe were, in his view, not merely surviving pagan deities but living symbols — and perhaps living presences — inhabiting the Irish landscape and the collective imagination.
