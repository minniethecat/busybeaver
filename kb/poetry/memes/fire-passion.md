# Fire and Passion

Fire in Yeats is the element of transformation. It burns away the inessential — whether in the "holy fire" of Byzantium's golden smithies, the passion of love that consumes the lover, or the purgatorial flame that refines the soul. Drawing on Hermetic elemental symbolism, Christian purgatory, and Irish mythological fire (the hearth-fire of the filidh, the fire in the head of poetic inspiration), Yeats's fire is always dangerous and always sacred.

## Origins and Sources

- **Hermetic element**: Fire as the highest of the four elements in esoteric tradition, corresponding to spirit/will
- **Christian purgatory**: Fire as purification of the soul before its entry into paradise — a concept Yeats adapts to his own system of spiritual evolution
- **Irish tradition**: The "fire in the head" — the poetic frenzy of the filidh; the hearth-fire as center of the home and site of storytelling
- **Alchemical fire**: The athanor/furnace of transformation — base matter transmuted to gold, the lover transmuted by passion

## Symbolic Meaning

1. **The holy fire**: "perne in a gyre" from the "holy fire" (047-019) — the fire of Byzantium's smithies is the fire of artistic transformation, burning away the biological to reveal the aesthetic.

2. **Passion as fire**: Love burns — "wisdom that caught fire like the driest tree" (039-020). Passion is destructive as well as creative; it consumes what it touches.

3. **The firelight**: "In the Firelight" (012) — the domestic fire as site of intimacy, memory, and reflection. Fire in the hearth is fire tamed, made gentle, but always with the potential to escape.

4. **Fire and age**: The aging poet envies the young their "fire" — passion diminishes with age, and the late poems are attempts to replace the fire of the body with the colder fire of the intellect.

## Development Across Yeats's Career

**Early period**: Fire is domestic and atmospheric — firelight as setting for reverie and storytelling. Passion is romantic, not yet philosophical.

**Middle period**: Fire becomes alchemical. "Wisdom that caught fire" (039-020) — passion and wisdom are no longer opposed but fused. The fire of love transmutes the lover.

**Late period**: Fire achieves its most exalted form in Byzantium — the "holy fire" that consumes and transforms, the "flames that no faggot feeds" (the fire of art that burns without fuel). The aging poet no longer has the fire of the body, but he has gained the fire of the imagination, which burns brighter and longer.
