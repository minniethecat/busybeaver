# Nature in Yeats

Nature in Yeats is a symbolic language. The landscapes of Co. Sligo — Ben Bulben, Lough Gill, Innisfree — are not backdrops but active presences, saturated with mythological and spiritual meaning. Yeats's nature poetry draws on the Romantic tradition but transforms it through Irish folklore: the natural world is the threshold between mortal and fairy realms, the visible face of invisible powers.

## Origins and Sources

- **Sligo childhood**: Yeats spent formative years in Co. Sligo with his maternal grandparents, the Pollexfens. The landscapes of the west of Ireland — lake, mountain, sea — provided the imagery that recurs throughout his career
- **Celtic nature mysticism**: The pre-Christian Irish relationship to land as sacred, animate, populated by the Sidhe
- **Pastoral tradition**: The classical and Renaissance pastoral, adapted to Irish landscape — Innisfree as Yeats's Arcadia
- **Romantic nature**: Wordsworth's nature as spiritual teacher, Blake's nature as symbolic language

## Symbolic Meaning

1. **Nature as escape**: "I will arise and go now, and go to Innisfree" — nature is the refuge from urban modernity, the site of authentic life. The lake isle of Innisfree is Yeats's most famous nature image.

2. **Nature as signature**: The endnotes identify water as the "signature of the fruitfulness of the body" ((048-015)) in Yeats's symbolic system — nature is a code to be read, each element signifying spiritual qualities.

3. **Nature and the body**: "Our two natures blent" ((047-680)) — "nature" in Yeats often means human nature, the physical dimension of being that resists the purely spiritual. This creates productive tension with the transcendent impulse.

4. **Autumn beauty**: "The trees are in their autumn beauty" ((045-005)) — nature's beauty in decline parallels the poet's own aging, transforming elegy into acceptance.

## Development Across Yeats's Career

**Early period**: Nature is mythological — the "central nature" ((005-002)) that the sage delves into is the nature of Celtic legend, populated by fairies and Druids. Landscape is inseparable from story.

**Middle period**: Nature becomes more austere. The "autumn beauty" of Coole Park ((045-005)) is beautiful precisely because it is passing — nature now carries the weight of time and mortality.

**Late period**: Nature is transcended but never abandoned. "Once out of nature I shall never take / My bodily form from any natural thing" ((047-025)) — the aging poet desires to escape the decay of the natural body, but the desire itself is expressed in nature imagery: the golden bird, the Grecian goldsmiths. Nature remains the only language available.
