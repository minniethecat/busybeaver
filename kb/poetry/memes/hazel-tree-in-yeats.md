# Hazel Tree in Yeats

The **hazel tree** is a persistent natural symbol in W. B. Yeats's poetry, drawing on Irish folk tradition where the hazel is the tree of wisdom — its nuts falling into the Well of Wisdom (Connla's Well) to be eaten by the Salmon of Knowledge. In Yeats's poetic cosmos, the hazel bridges the natural Irish landscape with the Otherworld, serving as both a concrete feature of the Fenian countryside and a threshold symbol marking encounters with supernatural beings. The hazel wand — cut and peeled from the living tree — becomes the poet's instrument of vision, linking craftsmanship with inspiration.

## Origins and Sources

Yeats's use of the hazel draws primarily on Irish mythological tradition. In the *Dindshenchas* (place-name lore) and related narratives, nine hazel trees grow over Connla's Well in the Otherworld, dropping their crimson nuts into the water where the Salmon of Knowledge consumes them — gaining all the world's wisdom. This tradition gives the hazel an association with esoteric knowledge that Yeats wove into his occult symbolism.

Yeats also encountered the hazel through the Irish landscape poetry he absorbed from the Fenian Cycle, where "hazel and oak" form a recurring formula for the wild Irish woodland. The hazel's prominence in Sligo — the landscape of Yeats's childhood — gave it a personal resonance distinct from its mythological weight.

- **Primary source**: Irish mythology (Well of Wisdom / Connla's Well)
- **Key texts**: Fenian Cycle poems, *The Wanderings of Usheen*, *The Wind Among the Reeds*

## Development Across Yeats's Career

### Early Poetry

The hazel first appears in the early narrative poem *The Wanderings of Usheen* (1889) as part of the Fenian landscape. The "dripping hazel and oak" frames the mortal world that Oisín returns to after his sojourn in the Otherworld [015-658]. Across the poem, the paired formula "hazel and oak" recurs as a metonym for the natural Irish landscape the Fenians inhabited [015-668], [015-669], [015-760]. In these early appearances, the hazel carries no explicit symbolic weight — it is simply part of the heroic terrain.

### The Wind Among the Reeds (1899)

The hazel reaches its fullest poetic expression in "The Song of Wandering Aengus," where the speaker "went out to the hazel wood" and "cut and peeled a hazel wand" [038-120], [038-122]. This act of crafting a wand from the hazel initiates a visionary encounter with the "glimmering girl" — an Otherworld being who appears from nowhere and then vanishes, setting the speaker on an eternal, unfulfillable quest. Later in the poem, "a man with a hazel wand came without sound" [038-178], reinforcing the hazel wand as the instrument of supernatural contact. Yeats's endnote to the poem confirms the mythological genealogy: "The man in my poem who has a hazel wand may have been Aengus, Master of Love" [048-027].

In the same collection, "He and She" reimagines the hazel through the lens of metempsychosis: the speaker recalls having been "a hazel tree" in a past life [038-499], tracing the soul's passage through natural forms. In *The Shadowy Waters*, "the shadowy hazel grove" serves as a liminal space between worlds [031-276].

### Middle and Late Poetry

In the middle period, the hazel appears more incidentally — "Flickering with ash and hazel, but half showed" (from *The Player Queen*, [039-004]) and "Where hazel and ash and privet blind the" (from *Upon a Dying Lady*, [042-008]) — as a marker of the Irish landscape rather than a charged symbol.

In the late poetry, the hazel reappears with renewed symbolic force. In "Under Ben Bulben," the landscape transforms: "Till oak and hazel ceased and beech began" [044-303], using the hazel-oak threshold to mark a boundary between wild Celtic terrain and cultivated English landscape. In *The Death of Cuchulain*, the natural imagery includes "a bat rose from the hazels" [045-1126], a fleeting but characteristic detail that roots the mythological action in the Irish landscape. Earlier, "The Three Beggars" opens with burial "in a hazel dell" [024-044], placing the hazel at the threshold of death and the underworld.

## Symbolic Meaning

The hazel operates on three symbolic levels in Yeats's work:

1. **Wisdom and Inspiration**: Drawing on the Irish myth of Connla's Well, the hazel represents hidden knowledge that must be actively sought — the nuts do not offer themselves; they must fall into the water and be consumed. The poet cutting his hazel wand enacts this search for inspiration.

2. **Liminal Threshold**: The "hazel wood" and "hazel dell" mark boundaries between the mortal world and the Otherworld. Entering the hazel wood is the precondition for supernatural encounter — the glimmering girl appears only after the speaker goes into the hazel wood.

3. **Irish Identity**: The "hazel and oak" formula functions as a nationalist synecdoche — naming the native Irish woodland against the absence of these trees in English landscape poetry.
