# John Aherne

John Aherne is one of the strangest figures in Yeats's poetry — a man who may have been real, may have been invented, or may have been some fusion of the two. Yeats claimed, in his endnotes, that he had "used the actual names of two friends" — Michael Robartes and Owen Aherne — and that "one of these friends, Michael Robartes, has but lately returned from Mesopotamia where he has partly found and partly thought out much philosophy" [048-022]. John Aherne, he added, was "either the original of Owen Aherne or some near relation of the man that was." The uncertainty is deliberate: Aherne and Robartes exist in the borderland between biography and fiction, and it is precisely this ontological ambiguity that makes them such effective vehicles for Yeats's philosophical poetry.

## Introduction

Aherne first appears — as "Owen Aherne" — in Yeats's early stories, alongside Michael Robartes, as part of a fictional brotherhood of mystical seekers. The two figures re-emerge, decades later, in the philosophical poems of Yeats's maturity: "The Phases of the Moon," "The Double Vision of Michael Robartes," and "Michael Robartes and the Dancer." In these poems, Robartes and Aherne serve as mouthpieces for Yeats's system — the lunar phases, the gyres, the *Daimon* — but they are mouthpieces with personalities, capable of quarrelling with each other and with their creator.

Yeats described the relationship with characteristic deadpan in his endnotes: "I consider that John Aherne is either the original of Owen Aherne or some near relation of the man that was, and that both he and Robartes, to whose namesake I had attributed a turbulent life and death, have quarrelled with me. They take their place in a phantasmagoria in which I endeavour to explain my philosophy of life and death" [048-022]. The "phantasmagoria" is Yeats's term for the visionary apparatus of *A Vision* — and Aherne is one of its principal inhabitants.

## Key Facts

- **Born**: Unknown — possibly a real friend of Yeats, possibly an invented persona
- **Died**: Unknown
- **Role**: Yeatsian persona, philosophical interlocutor, member of the Robartes-Aherne "phantasmagoria"
- **Connection to Yeats**: Vehicle for Yeats's philosophical system; appears in the Robartes-Aherne poems

## Significance in Yeats's Work

Aherne appears most prominently in "The Phases of the Moon," where he and Robartes discuss the twenty-eight phases of the lunar cycle that structure Yeats's system. The poem is a compressed philosophical dialogue, and Aherne's role is partly that of the interlocutor — the one who asks the questions that allow Robartes (and through him, Yeats) to expound the system.

But Aherne is more than a device. The "phantasmagoria" in which he moves — the half-real, half-invented world of Robartes, Aherne, Kusta ben Luka (the "philosopher of Bagdad"), and his Bedouin followers — is Yeats's way of distancing himself from his own system while simultaneously committing to it. By attributing the philosophy to these semi-fictional figures, Yeats can expound it without claiming it as literal truth — it is what these characters believe, what they have "partly found and partly thought out" [048-022].
