# The Song of Wandering Aengus

"The Song of Wandering Aengus" is one of the most celebrated lyrics of Yeats's early period, first published in *The Wind Among the Reeds* (1899). The poem takes its name from Aengus Óg, the Irish god of love and youth, but transforms the mythological figure into a visionary persona: the speaker who goes out to the hazel wood, catches a silver trout, and watches it transform into a "glimmering girl" who calls his name and vanishes, sending him on an eternal quest.

## The Poem

The poem's three stanzas follow a dreamlike narrative arc:

1. The speaker goes to the hazel wood, cuts a hazel wand, baits a hook with a berry, and catches "a little silver trout"
2. Laying the fish on the floor, he goes to blow the fire — and hears a rustle: the fish has become "a glimmering girl / With apple blossom in her hair"
3. She calls his name and vanishes "through the brightening air." The speaker, now aged, declares he will find her "though I am old with wandering" — and will kiss her and walk "among long dappled grass" until "time and times are done"

## Symbolic Reading

The poem works on multiple levels. It is a love poem of supernatural longing; an allegory of the artistic process (inspiration that appears, transforms, and flees, sending the artist on an endless pursuit); and a meditation on the nature of desire itself — the thing that vanishes as soon as it is grasped, leaving only the hunger to pursue it.

The silver trout turning into a girl recalls the *aisling* tradition of Irish poetry, in which a vision-woman appears to the poet as an emblem of Ireland or of poetic inspiration. The poem's setting — the hazel wood, the moon, the "hollow lands and hilly lands" — draws on the Sligo landscape of Yeats's childhood.
