# Gyre

The gyre is the master-symbol of W. B. Yeats's mature thought — a cone-shaped spiral through which all things pass: civilisations rise and fall in 2,000-year gyres, the soul incarnates and reincarnates through lunar gyres, and the poet himself "pernes in a gyre" [047-019] between opposite states of being. Derived from Yeats's reading of [[plato|Plato's]] Great Year, the Neoplatonic tradition of [[plotinus]], and his own occult experiments, the gyre became the organising image of *[[a-vision]]* and the key to the prophetic voice of his greatest poems.

## Definition

A gyre, in Yeats's system, is a conical spiral — narrower at one end, wider at the other — that represents a cycle of expansion and contraction. Two gyres, interpenetrating, form the fundamental unit of historical and spiritual change: as one civilisation or soul-phase reaches its maximum expansion, the opposing gyre begins its ascent. The entire system is geometric: "cones made up of revolving gyres intersecting each other at various angles" [048-056]. Every era, every soul, every relationship is the intersection of two contrary motions.

The word "gyre" itself is Yeats's coinage — an archaic or dialect form of "gyrate" that he encountered in his reading and elevated into a philosophical term. Its companion verb "perne" (meaning to spin or whirl, from the Irish *peirn*, a bobbin or spool) appears throughout the later poetry: "I had long perned in the gyre" [046-346].

## Origins and Sources

The gyre has multiple intellectual origins, synthesised by Yeats into a single image:

- **Primary source**: Plato's doctrine of the Great Year (*Timaeus* 39d) — the 36,000-year cycle in which the celestial bodies return to their original positions — which Yeats transformed into his own 2,000-year historical cycles. "The Platonic Year" [048-085] and "whirling dust" condense cosmology into poetic image.
- **Occult source**: The Hermetic Order of the Golden Dawn's teachings on cyclical vibration and the interpenetration of opposites, which Yeats encountered through his initiatory training in the 1890s.
- **Poetic source**: William Blake's "contraries" — the dialectic of innocence and experience, heaven and hell — which Yeats had studied since his youth.
- **Personal source**: Yeats's own automatic writing sessions with his wife George (1917–1920), from which the detailed geometry of the gyres was, he claimed, dictated by spiritual intelligences.

## Development Across Yeats's Career

The gyre appears in fully developed form in the poems of *Michael Robartes and the Dancer* (1921) and *The Tower* (1928), but its antecedents are visible earlier. The spiral stair of Yeats's tower-house at [[thoor-ballylee]] — "the winding stair" of the later poems — is a gyre made architectural. The rose of the early symbolist poems, with its concentric petals, is already a spiral in embryo.

The great breakthrough comes with "The Second Coming" (1919, published 1921):

> "Turning and turning in the widening gyre / The falcon cannot hear the falconer" [046-392]

These lines — among the most famous Yeats ever wrote — compress the entire philosophy into a single image. The falcon spiralling outward, losing contact with its master, is the present gyre of history reaching its point of maximum expansion, the moment before revelation or catastrophe. The poem's second stanza — "And what rough beast, its hour come round at last, / Slouches towards Bethlehem to be born?" — prophesies the birth of the antithetical gyre, the rough beast that will rule the coming dispensation.

In the later poetry, the gyre becomes personal as well as historical. In "Sailing to Byzantium," the poet declares: "perne in a gyre" [047-019] — the soul turning on itself, gathering wisdom from the "holy fire" as it prepares for its translation out of the "dying animal" of the body.

## Symbolic Manifestations

The gyre is not only an abstract concept but a shape that recurs across Yeats's imagery:

- **The Falcon**: spiralling outward in "The Second Coming" — the gyre made animal, the loss of control at the cycle's extreme point
- **The Winding Stair**: the tower's spiral staircase (*The Winding Stair and Other Poems*, 1933) — the gyre made architectural, the soul's ascent
- **The Perne**: the bobbin or spool that spins thread — the gyre made domestic, the daily turning of consciousness
- **The Sphere**: the gyre's completion — Unity of Being, the still point where all contraries resolve — contrasted with "gyres and cubes and midnight things" [047-1170]

## Scholarly Interpretation

T. R. Henn (*The Lonely Tower*, 1950) reads the gyre as Yeats's "geometry of history" — an attempt to give poetic form to the cyclical theories of history advanced by Vico, Spengler, and Toynbee. Richard Ellmann (*The Identity of Yeats*, 1954) traces the gyre to Yeats's occult diagrams and reads it as a mandala — a symbol of psychological integration as much as historical prediction. More recently, critics have emphasised the gyre's relationship to Yeats's reading of Nietzsche's doctrine of eternal recurrence and to the Hermetic principle of "as above, so below" — the macrocosm of history mirrored in the microcosm of the individual soul.
