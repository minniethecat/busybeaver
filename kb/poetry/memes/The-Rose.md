# The Rose[3](endnotes.xhtml#note-3)[4](endnotes.xhtml#note-4)

[031-001]
> “Sero te amavi, pulchritudo tam antiqua et tam nova! Sero te amavi.”

[031-002]
> 

[031-003]
> S. Augustine

[031-004]To Lionel Johnson

## To the Rose Upon the Rood of Time

[031-005]Red [[rose-symbol|Rose]], proud Rose, sad Rose of all my days!   

[031-006]Come near me, while I sing the ancient ways:   

[031-007][[cuchulain-hero|Cuchulain]] battling with the bitter tide;   

[031-008]The [[druidism-and-celtic-mysticism|Druid]], gray, wood-nurtured, quiet-eyed,   

[031-009]Who cast round Fergus dreams, and ruin untold;   

[031-010]And thine own sadness, whereof stars, grown old   

[031-011]In [[dance-in-yeats|dancing]] silver sandalled on the [[water-imagery|sea]],   

[031-012]Sing in their high and lonely melody.   

[031-013]Come near, that no more blinded by man’s fate,   

[031-014]I find under the boughs of love and hate,   

[031-015]In all poor foolish things that live a day,   

[031-016]Eternal beauty wandering on her way.

[031-017]Come near, come near, come near﻿—Ah, leave me still   

[031-018]A little space for the rose-breath to fill!   

[031-019]Lest I no more hear common things that crave;   

[031-020]The weak worm hiding down in its small cave,   

[031-021]The field mouse running by me in the grass,   

[031-022]And heavy mortal hopes that toil and pass;   

[031-023]But seek alone to hear the strange things said   

[031-024]By God to the bright hearts of those long dead,   

[031-025]And learn to chaunt a tongue men do not know.   

[031-026]Come near; I would, before my time to go,   

[031-027]Sing of old Eire and the ancient ways:   

[031-028]Red Rose, proud Rose, sad Rose of all my days.

## Fergus and the Druid

[031-029]Fergus |  The whole day have I followed in the rocks,   

[031-030]And you have changed and flowed from shape to shape,   

[031-031]First as a raven on whose ancient wings   

[031-032]Scarcely a feather lingered, then you seemed   

[031-033]A weasel moving on from stone to stone,   

[031-034]And now at last you wear a human shape,   

[031-035]A thin gray man half lost in gathering night.  

[031-036]---|---  

[031-037]Druid |  What would you, king of the proud Red Branch kings?  

[031-038]Fergus |  This would I say, most wise of living souls:   

[031-039]Young subtle Conchubar sat close by me   

[031-040]When I gave judgment, and his words were wise,   

[031-041]And what to me was burden without end,   

[031-042]To him seemed easy, so I laid the crown   

[031-043]Upon his head to cast away my sorrow.  

[031-044]Druid |  What would you, king of the proud Red Branch kings?  

[031-045]Fergus |  A king and proud! and that is my despair.   

[031-046]I feast amid my people on the hill,   

[031-047]And pace the woods, and drive my chariot wheels   

[031-048]In the white border of the murmuring sea;   

[031-049]And still I feel the crown upon my head.  

[031-050]Druid |  What would you, Fergus?  

[031-051]Fergus |  Be no more a king   

[031-052]But learn the [[wisdom-in-yeats|dreaming wisdom]] that is yours.  

[031-053]Druid |  Look on my thin gray hair and hollow cheeks   

[031-054]And on these hands that may not lift the sword,   

[031-055]This body trembling like a wind-blown reed.   

[031-056]No woman’s loved me, no man sought my help.  

[031-057]Fergus |  A king is but a foolish labourer   

[031-058]Who wastes his blood to be another’s dream.  

[031-059]Druid |  Take, if you must, this little bag of dreams;   

[031-060]Unloose the cord, and they will wrap you round.  

[031-061]Fergus |  I see my life go dripping like a river   

[031-062]From change to change; I have been many things,   

[031-063]A green drop in the surge, a gleam of [[shadow-and-light|light]]   

[031-064]Upon a sword, a fir-tree on a hill,   

[031-065]An old slave grinding at a heavy quern,   

[031-066]A king sitting upon a chair of gold,   

[031-067]And all these things were wonderful and great;   

[031-068]But now I have grown nothing, knowing all,   

[031-069]And the whole world weighs down upon my heart:   

[031-070]Ah! Druid, Druid, how great webs of sorrow   

[031-071]Lay hidden in the small slate-coloured thing?  
  
## Cuchulain’s Fight with the Sea

[031-072]A man came slowly from the setting sun,   

[031-073]To Emer, raddling raiment in her dun,   

[031-074]And said, “I am that swineherd, whom you bid   

[031-075]Go watch the road between the wood and tide,   

[031-076]But now I have no need to watch it more.”

[031-077]Then Emer cast the web upon the floor,   

[031-078]And raising arms all raddled with the dye;   

[031-079]Parted her lips with a loud sudden cry.

[031-080]That swineherd stared upon her face and said:   

[031-081]“No man alive, no man among the dead,   

[031-082]Has won the gold his cars of battle bring.”

[031-083]“But if your master comes home triumphing   

[031-084]Why are you pale and shake from foot to crown?”

[031-085]Thereon he shook the more and cast him down   

[031-086]Upon the web-heaped floor, and cried his word:   

[031-087]“With him is one sweet-throated like a bird.”

[031-088]“You dare me to my face,” and thereupon   

[031-089]She smote with raddled fist, and where her son   

[031-090]Herded the cattle came with stumbling feet,   

[031-091]And cried with angry voice, “It is not meet   

[031-092]To idle life away, a common herd.”

[031-093]“I have long waited, mother, for that word:   

[031-094]But wherefore now?”

[031-095]“There is a man to die;   

[031-096]You have the heaviest arm under the sky.”

[031-097]“Whether under its daylight or the stars   

[031-098]My father stands amid his battle cars.”

[031-099]“But you have grown to be the taller man.”

[031-100]“Yet somewhere under starlight or the sun   

[031-101]My father stands amid his battle cars.”

[031-102]“But he is old and sad with many wars.”

[031-103]“I only ask what way my journey lies.   

[031-104]For He who made you bitter, made you wise.”

[031-105]“The Red Branch camp in a great company   

[031-106]Between wood’s rim and the horses of the sea.   

[031-107]Go there, and light a camp fire at wood’s rim;   

[031-108]But tell your name and lineage to him   

[031-109]Whose blade compels, and wait till they have found   

[031-110]Some feating man that the same oath has bound.”

[031-111]Among those feasting kings Cuchulain dwelt,   

[031-112]And his young sweetheart close beside him knelt,   

[031-113]Stared on the mournful wonder of his eyes,   

[031-114]Even as Spring upon the ancient skies,   

[031-115]And pondered on the glory of his days;   

[031-116]And all around the harp-string told his praise,   

[031-117]And Conchubar, the Red Branch king of kings,   

[031-118]With his own fingers touched the brazen strings.

[031-119]At last Cuchulain spake, “Some man has made   

[031-120]His evening fire amid the leafy shade.   

[031-121]I have often heard him [[song-and-poetry|singing]] to and fro,   

[031-122]I have often heard the sweet sound of his bow,   

[031-123]Seek out what man he is.”

[031-124]One went and came.   

[031-125]“He bade me let all know he gives his name   

[031-126]At the sword point, and waits till we have found   

[031-127]Some feating man that the same oath has bound.”

[031-128]Cuchulain cried, “I am the only man   

[031-129]Of all this host so bound from childhood on.”

[031-130]After short fighting in the leafy shade,   

[031-131]He spake to the young man, “Is there no maid   

[031-132]Who loves you, no white arms to wrap you round,   

[031-133]Or do you long for the dim sleepy ground,   

[031-134]That you have come and dared me to my face?”

[031-135]“The dooms of men are in God’s hidden place.”

[031-136]“Your head a while seemed like a woman’s head   

[031-137]That I loved once.”

[031-138]Again the fighting sped,   

[031-139]But now the war rage in Cuchulain woke,   

[031-140]And through that new blade’s guard the old blade broke,   

[031-141]And pierced him.

[031-142]“Speak before your breath is done.”

[031-143]“Cuchulain I, mighty Cuchulain’s son.”

[031-144]“I put you from your pain. I can no more.”

[031-145]While day its burden on to evening bore,   

[031-146]With head bowed on his knees Cuchulain stayed;   

[031-147]Then Conchubar sent that sweet-throated maid,   

[031-148]And she, to win him, his grey hair caressed;   

[031-149]In vain her arms, in vain her soft white breast.   

[031-150]Then Conchubar, the subtlest of all men,   

[031-151]Ranking his Druids round him ten by ten,   

[031-152]Spake thus, “Cuchulain will dwell there and brood,   

[031-153]For three days more in dreadful quietude,   

[031-154]And then arise, and raving slay us all.   

[031-155]Chaunt in his ear delusions magical,   

[031-156]That he may fight the horses of the sea.”   

[031-157]The Druids took them to their mystery,   

[031-158]And chanted for three days.

[031-159]Cuchulain stirred,   

[031-160]Stared on the horses of the sea, and heard   

[031-161]The cars of battle and his own name cried;   

[031-162]And fought with the invulnerable tide.

## The Rose of the World

[031-163]Who dreamed that beauty passes like a dream?   

[031-164]For these red lips, with all their mournful pride,   

[031-165]Mournful that no new wonder may betide,   

[031-166]Troy passed away in one high funeral gleam,   

[031-167]And Usna’s children died.

[031-168]We and the labouring world are passing by:   

[031-169]Amid men’s souls, that waver and give place,   

[031-170]Like the pale waters in their wintry race,   

[031-171]Under the passing stars, foam of the sky,   

[031-172]Lives on this lonely face.

[031-173]Bow down, archangels, in your dim abode:   

[031-174]Before you were, or any hearts to beat,   

[031-175]Weary and kind one lingered by His seat;   

[031-176]He made the world to be a grassy road   

[031-177]Before her wandering feet.

## The Rose of Peace

[031-178]If Michael, leader of God’s host   

[031-179]When Heaven and Hell are met,   

[031-180]Looked down on you from Heaven’s door-post   

[031-181]He would his deeds forget.

[031-182]Brooding no more upon God’s wars   

[031-183]In his Divine homestead,   

[031-184]He would go weave out of the stars   

[031-185]A chaplet for your head.

[031-186]And all folk seeing him bow down,   

[031-187]And white stars tell your praise,   

[031-188]Would come at last to God’s great town,   

[031-189]Led on by gentle ways;

[031-190]And God would bid His warfare cease,   

[031-191]Saying all things were well;   

[031-192]And softly make a rosy peace,   

[031-193]A peace of Heaven with Hell.

## The Rose of Battle

[031-194]Rose of all Roses, Rose of all the World!   

[031-195]The tall thought-woven sails, that flap unfurled   

[031-196]Above the tide of hours, trouble the air,   

[031-197]And God’s bell buoyed to be the water’s care;   

[031-198]While hushed from fear, or loud with hope, a band   

[031-199]With blown, spray-dabbled hair gather at hand.   

[031-200]" Turn if you may from battles never done,   

[031-201]" I call, as they go by me one by one,   

[031-202]" Danger no refuge holds, and war no peace,   

[031-203]For him who hears love sing and never cease,   

[031-204]Beside her clean-swept hearth, her quiet shade:   

[031-205]But gather all for whom no love hath made   

[031-206]A woven silence, or but came to cast   

[031-207]A song into the air, and singing past   

[031-208]To smile on the pale dawn; and gather you   

[031-209]Who have sought more than is in rain or dew   

[031-210]Or in the sun and moon, or on the earth,   

[031-211]Or sighs amid the wandering, starry mirth,   

[031-212]Or comes in laughter from the sea’s sad lips   

[031-213]And wage God’s battles in the long grey ships.   

[031-214]The sad, the lonely, the insatiable,   

[031-215]To these Old Night shall all her mystery tell;   

[031-216]God’s bell has claimed them by the little cry   

[031-217]Of their sad hearts, that may not live nor die. "

[031-218]Rose of all Roses, Rose of all the World!   

[031-219]You, too, have come where the dim tides are hurled   

[031-220]Upon the wharves of sorrow, and heard ring   

[031-221]The bell that calls us on; the sweet far thing.   

[031-222][[eternal-beauty|Beauty]] grown sad with its [[time-and-eternity|eternity]]   

[031-223]Made you of us, and of the dim grey sea.   

[031-224]Our long ships loose thought-woven sails and wait,   

[031-225]For God has bid them share an equal fate;   

[031-226]And when at last defeated in His wars,   

[031-227]They have gone down under the same white stars,   

[031-228]We shall no longer hear the little cry   

[031-229]Of our sad hearts, that may not live nor die.

## A Faery Song

[031-230]Sung by the people of faery over Diarmuid and Grania, who lay in their bridal sleep under a Cromlech.

[031-231]We who are old, old and gay,   

[031-232]O so old!   

[031-233]Thousands of years, thousands of years,   

[031-234]If all were told:

[031-235]Give to these children, new from the world,   

[031-236]Silence and love;   

[031-237]And the long dew-dropping hours of the night,   

[031-238]And the stars above:

[031-239]Give to these children, new from the world,   

[031-240]Rest far from men,   

[031-241]Is anything better, anything better?   

[031-242]Tell us it then:

[031-243]Us who are old, old and gay,   

[031-244]O so old!   

[031-245]Thousands of years, thousands of years,   

[031-246]If all were told.

## The Lake Isle of Innisfree

[031-247]I will arise and go now, and go to [[innisfree-isle|Innisfree]],   

[031-248]And a small cabin build there, of clay and wattles made:   

[031-249]Nine bean rows will I have there, a hive for the honey bee,   

[031-250]And live alone in the bee-loud glade.

[031-251]And I shall have some peace there, for peace comes dropping slow,   

[031-252]Dropping from the veils of the morning to where the cricket sings;   

[031-253]There midnight’s all a glimmer, and noon a purple glow,   

[031-254]And evening full of the linnet’s wings.

[031-255]I will arise and go now, for always night and day   

[031-256]I hear lake water lapping with low sounds by the shore;   

[031-257]While I stand on the roadway, or on the pavements grey   

[031-258]I hear it in the deep heart’s core.

## A Cradle Song

[031-259]The angels are stooping   

[031-260]Above your bed;   

[031-261]They weary of trooping   

[031-262]With the whimpering dead.

[031-263]God’s laughing in heaven   

[031-264]To see you so good;   

[031-265]The Sailing Seven   

[031-266]Are gay with His mood.

[031-267]I sigh that kiss you,   

[031-268]For I must own   

[031-269]That I shall miss you   

[031-270]When you have grown.

## The Pity of Love

[031-271]A pity beyond all telling   

[031-272]Is hid in the heart of love:   

[031-273]The folk who are buying and selling,   

[031-274]The clouds on their journey above,   

[031-275]The cold wet winds ever blowing,   

[031-276]And the shadowy hazel grove   

[031-277]Where mouse-grey waters are flowing,   

[031-278]Threaten the head that I love.

## The Sorrow of Love

[031-279]The brawling of a sparrow in the eaves,   

[031-280]The brilliant moon and all the milky sky,   

[031-281]And all that famous harmony of leaves,   

[031-282]Had blotted out man’s image and his cry.

[031-283]A girl arose that had red mournful lips,   

[031-284]And seemed the greatness of the world in tears.   

[031-285]Doomed like Odysseus and the labouring ships   

[031-286]And proud as Priam murdered with his peers;

[031-287]Arose, and on the instant clamorous eaves,   

[031-288]A climbing moon upon an empty sky,   

[031-289]And all that lamentation of the leaves,   

[031-290]Could but compose man’s image and his cry.

## When You Are Old

[031-291]When you are old and grey and full of sleep,   

[031-292]And nodding by the fire, take down this book,   

[031-293]And slowly read; and dream of the soft look   

[031-294]Your eyes had once, and of their shadows deep;

[031-295]How many loved your moments of glad grace,   

[031-296]And loved your beauty with love false or true,   

[031-297]But one man loved the pilgrim soul in you,   

[031-298]And loved the sorrows of your changing face;

[031-299]And bending down beside the glowing bars   

[031-300]Murmur, a little sadly, how love fled   

[031-301]And paced upon the mountains overhead   

[031-302]And hid his face amid a crowd of stars.

## The White Birds

[031-303]I would that we were, my beloved, white birds on the foam of the sea!   

[031-304]We tire of the [[fire-passion|flame]] of the meteor, before it can fade and flee;   

[031-305]And the flame of the blue star of twilight, hung low on the rim of the sky,   

[031-306]Has awaked in our hearts, my beloved, a sadness that may not die.

[031-307]A weariness comes from those dreamers, dew dabbled, the lily and rose;   

[031-308]Ah, dream not of them, my beloved, the flame of the meteor that goes,   

[031-309]Or the flame of the blue star that lingers hung low in the fall of the dew:   

[031-310]For I would we were changed to white birds on the wandering foam: I and you!

[031-311]I am haunted by numberless islands, and many a Danaan shore,   

[031-312]Where Time would surely forget us, and Sorrow come near us no more;   

[031-313]Soon far from the rose and the lily, and fret of the flames would we be,   

[031-314]Were we only white birds, my beloved, buoyed out on the foam of the sea!

## A Dream of Death

[031-315]I dreamed that one had died in a strange place   

[031-316]Near no accustomed hand;   

[031-317]And they had nailed the boards above her face,   

[031-318]The peasants of that land,   

[031-319]Wondering to lay her in that solitude,   

[031-320]And raised above her mound   

[031-321]A cross they had made out of two bits of wood,   

[031-322]And planted cypress round;   

[031-323]And left her to the indifferent stars above   

[031-324]Until I carved these words:   

[031-325]" She was more beautiful than thy first love,   

[031-326]But now lies under boards. "

## The Countess Cathleen in Paradise

[031-327]All the heavy days are over;   

[031-328]Leave the body’s coloured pride   

[031-329]Underneath the grass and clover,   

[031-330]With the feet laid side by side.

[031-331]Bathed in flaming founts of duty   

[031-332]She’ll not ask a haughty dress;   

[031-333]Carry all that mournful beauty,   

[031-334]To the scented oaken press.

[031-335]Did the kiss of Mother Mary,   

[031-336]Put that music in her face?   

[031-337]Yet she goes with footsteps wary,   

[031-338]Full of earth’s old timid grace.

[031-339]’Mong the feet of angels seven   

[031-340]What a dancer glimmering!   

[031-341]All the heavens bow down to heaven,   

[031-342]Flame to flame and wing to wing.

## Who Goes with Fergus

[031-343]Who will go drive with Fergus now,   

[031-344]And pierce the deep wood’s woven shade,   

[031-345]And dance upon the level shore?   

[031-346]Young man, lift up your russet brow,   

[031-347]And lift your tender eyelids, maid,   

[031-348]And brood on hopes and fears no more.

[031-349]And no more turn aside and brood   

[031-350]Upon [[love-in-yeats|Love]]’s bitter mystery;   

[031-351]For Fergus rules the brazen cars,   

[031-352]And rules the shadows of the wood,   

[031-353]And the white breast of the dim sea   

[031-354]And all dishevelled wandering stars.

## The Man Who Dreamed of Faeryland

[031-355]He stood among a crowd at Drumahair;   

[031-356]His heart hung all upon a silken dress,   

[031-357]And he had known at last some tenderness,   

[031-358]Before earth made of him her sleepy care;   

[031-359]But when a man poured fish into a pile,   

[031-360]It seemed they raised their little silver heads,   

[031-361]And sang how day a Druid twilight sheds   

[031-362]Upon a dim, green, well-beloved isle,   

[031-363]Where people love beside star-laden seas;   

[031-364]How Time may never mar their faery vows   

[031-365]Under the woven roofs of quicken boughs:   

[031-366]The singing shook him out of his new ease.

[031-367]He wandered by the sands of Lisadell;   

[031-368]His mind ran all on money cares and fears,   

[031-369]And he had known at last some prudent years   

[031-370]Before they heaped his grave under the hill;   

[031-371]But while he passed before a plashy place,   

[031-372]A lug-worm with its gray and muddy mouth   

[031-373]Sang how somewhere to north or west or south   

[031-374]There dwelt a gay, exulting, gentle race;   

[031-375]And how beneath those three times blessed skies   

[031-376]A Danaan fruitage makes a shower of moons,   

[031-377]And as it falls awakens leafy tunes:   

[031-378]And at that singing he was no more wise.

[031-379]He mused beside the well of Scanavin,   

[031-380]He mused upon his mockers: without fail   

[031-381]His sudden vengeance were a country tale,   

[031-382]Now that deep earth has drunk his body in;   

[031-383]But one small knot-grass growing by the pool   

[031-384]Told where, ah, little, all-unneeded voice!   

[031-385]Old Silence bids a lonely folk rejoice,   

[031-386]And chaplet their calm brows with leafage cool,   

[031-387]And how, when fades the sea-strewn rose of day,   

[031-388]A gentle feeling wraps them like a fleece,   

[031-389]And all their trouble dies into its peace:   

[031-390]The tale drove his fine angry mood away.

[031-391]He slept under the hill of Lugnagall;   

[031-392]And might have known at last unhaunted sleep   

[031-393]Under that cold and vapour-turbaned steep,   

[031-394]Now that old earth had taken man and all:   

[031-395]Were not the worms that spired about his bones   

[031-396]Proclaiming with a low and reedy cry,   

[031-397]That God had leant His hands out of the sky,   

[031-398]To bless that isle with honey in His tones;   

[031-399]That none may feel the power of squall and wave   

[031-400]And no one any leaf-crowned dancer miss   

[031-401]Until He burn up [[nature-in-yeats|Nature]] with a kiss:   

[031-402]The man has found no comfort in the grave.

## The Dedication to a Book of Stories Selected from the Irish Novelists

[031-403]There was a green branch hung with many a bell   

[031-404]When her own people ruled this tragic Eire;   

[031-405]And from its murmuring greenness, calm of faery,   

[031-406]A Druid kindness, on all hearers fell.

[031-407]It charmed away the merchant from his guile,   

[031-408]And turned the farmer’s memory from his cattle,   

[031-409]And hushed in sleep the roaring ranks of battle:   

[031-410]And all grew friendly for a little while.

[031-411]Ah, Exiles, wandering over lands and seas,   

[031-412]And planning, plotting always that some morrow   

[031-413]May set a stone upon ancestral Sorrow?   

[031-414]I also bear a bell branch full of ease.

[031-415]I tore it from green boughs winds tore and tossed   

[031-416]Until the sap of summer had grown weary!   

[031-417]I tore it from the barren boughs of Eire,   

[031-418]The country where a man can be so crossed;

[031-419]Can be so battered, badgered and destroyed   

[031-420]That he’s a loveless man: gay bells bring laughter,   

[031-421]That shakes a mouldering cobweb from the rafter;   

[031-422]And yet the saddest chimes are best enjoyed

[031-423]Gay bells or sad, they bring you memories   

[031-424]Of half-forgotten innocent old places:   

[031-425]We and our bitterness have left no traces   

[031-426]On Munster grass and Connemara skies.

## The Lamentation of the Old Pensioner

[031-427]Although I shelter from the rain   

[031-428]Under a broken tree   

[031-429]My chair was nearest to the fire   

[031-430]In every company.   

[031-431]That talked of love or politics

## The Ballad of Father Gilligan

[031-445]The old priest Peter Gilligan   

[031-446]Was weary night and day;   

[031-447]For half his flock were in their beds,   

[031-448]Or under green sods lay.

[031-449]Once, while he nodded on a chair,   

[031-450]At the moth-hour of eve,   

[031-451]Another poor man sent for him,   

[031-452]And he began to grieve.

[031-453]“I have no rest, nor joy, nor peace,   

[031-454]For people die and die”;   

[031-455]And after cried he, “God forgive!   

[031-456]My body spake, not I!”

[031-457]He knelt, and leaning on the chair   

[031-458]He prayed and fell asleep;   

[031-459]And the moth-hour went from the fields,   

[031-460]And stars began to peep.

[031-461]They slowly into millions grew,   

[031-462]And leaves shook in the wind;   

[031-463]And God covered the world with shade,   

[031-464]And whispered to mankind.

[031-465]Upon the time of sparrow chirp   

[031-466]When the moths came once more,   

[031-467]The old priest Peter Gilligan   

[031-468]Stood upright on the floor.

[031-469]“Mavrone, mavrone! the man has died,   

[031-470]While I slept on the chair”;   

[031-471]He roused his horse out of its sleep,   

[031-472]And rode with little care.

[031-473]He rode now as he never rode,   

[031-474]By rocky lane and fen;   

[031-475]The sick man’s wife opened the door:   

[031-476]“Father! you come again!”

[031-477]“And is the poor man dead?” he cried.   

[031-478]“He died an hour ago,”   

[031-479]The old priest Peter Gilligan   

[031-480]In grief swayed to and fro.

[031-481]“When you were gone, he turned and died   

[031-482]As merry as a bird.”   

[031-483]The old priest Peter Gilligan   

[031-484]He knelt him at that word.

[031-485]“He who hath made the night of stars   

[031-486]For souls, who tire and bleed,   

[031-487]Sent one of His great angels down   

[031-488]To help me in my need.

[031-489]“He who is wrapped in purple robes,   

[031-490]With planets in His care,   

[031-491]Had pity on the least of things   

[031-492]Asleep upon a chair.”

## The Two Trees

[031-493]Beloved, gaze in thine own heart,   

[031-494]The holy tree is growing there;   

[031-495]From joy the holy branches start,   

[031-496]And all the trembling flowers they bear,   

[031-497]The changing colours of its fruit   

[031-498]Have dowered the stars with merry light;   

[031-499]The surety of its hidden root   

[031-500]Has planted quiet in the night;   

[031-501]The shaking of its leafy head   

[031-502]Has given the waves their melody,   

[031-503]And made my lips and music wed,   

[031-504]Murmuring a wizard song for thee.   

[031-505]There, through bewildered branches, go   

[031-506]Winged Loves borne on in gentle strife,   

[031-507]Tossing and tossing to and fro   

[031-508]The flaming circle of our life.   

[031-509]When looking on their shaken hair,   

[031-510]And [[dream-and-vision|dreaming]] how they dance and dart,   

[031-511]Thine eyes grow full of tender care:   

[031-512]Beloved, gaze in thine own heart.

[031-513]Gaze no more in the bitter glass   

[031-514]The demons, with their subtle guile,   

[031-515]Lift up before us when they pass,   

[031-516]Or only gaze a little while;   

[031-517]For there a fatal image grows,   

[031-518]That the stormy night receives,   

[031-519]Roots half hidden under snows,   

[031-520]Broken boughs and blackened leaves.   

[031-521]All things turn to barrenness   

[031-522]In the dim glass the demons hold,   

[031-523]The glass of outer weariness,   

[031-524]Made when God slept in times of old.   

[031-525]There, through the broken branches, go   

[031-526]The ravens of unresting thought;   

[031-527]Peering and flying to and fro,   

[031-528]To see men’s souls bartered and bought.   

[031-529]When they are heard upon the wind,   

[031-530]And when they shake their wings; alas!   

[031-531]Thy tender eyes grow all unkind:   

[031-532]Gaze no more in the bitter glass.

## To Ireland in the Coming Times

[031-533]Know, that I would accounted be   

[031-534]True brother of that company,   

[031-535]That sang to sweeten [[ireland|Ireland]]’s wrong,   

[031-536]Ballad and story, rann and song;   

[031-537]Nor be I any less of them,   

[031-538]Because the red-rose-bordered hem   

[031-539]Of her, whose history began   

[031-540]Before God made the angelic clan,   

[031-541]Trails all about the written page.   

[031-542]When Time began to rant and rage   

[031-543]The measure of her flying feet   

[031-544]Made Ireland’s heart begin to beat;   

[031-545]And Time bade all his candles flare   

[031-546]To light a measure here and there;   

[031-547]And may the thoughts of Ireland brood   

[031-548]Upon a measured quietude.

[031-549]Nor may I less be counted one   

[031-550]With Davis, Mangan, Ferguson,   

[031-551]Because to him, who ponders well,   

[031-552]My rhymes more than their rhyming tell   

[031-553]Of things discovered in the deep,   

[031-554]Where only body’s laid asleep.   

[031-555]For the elemental creatures go   

[031-556][[About]] my table to and fro,   

[031-557]That hurry from unmeasured mind   

[031-558]To rant and rage in flood and wind;   

[031-559]Yet he who treads in measured ways   

[031-560]May surely barter gaze for gaze.   

[031-561]Man ever journeys on with them   

[031-562]After the red-rose-bordered hem.   

[031-563]Ah, faeries, dancing under the moon,   

[031-564]A Druid land, a Druid tune!

[031-565]While still I may, I write for you   

[031-566]The love I lived, the dream I knew.   

[031-567]From our birthday, until we die,   

[031-568]Is but the winking of an eye;   

[031-569]And we, our singing and our love,   

[031-570]What measurer Time has lit above,   

[031-571]And all benighted things that go   

[031-572]About my table to and fro,   

[031-573]Are passing on to where may be,   

[031-574]In truth’s consuming ecstasy   

[031-575]No place for love and dream at all;   

[031-576]For God goes by with white foot-fall.   

[031-577]I cast my heart into my rhymes,   

[031-578]That you, in the dim coming times,   

[031-579]May know how my heart went with them   

[031-580]After the red-rose-bordered hem.
