# Coole Park

Coole Park, near Gort in Co. Galway, was the home of Lady Augusta Gregory — folklorist, playwright, and Yeats's most steadfast patron and collaborator. For over thirty years (1897–1932), Yeats spent summers at Coole, finding there the conditions for sustained creative work: a library, a lake, seven woods, and the company of one of the most remarkable women in Irish cultural history. "The Wild Swans at Coole" (1919) is the most famous product of these visits — but Coole's presence in Yeats's poetry extends far beyond that single poem.

## Location and Geography

- **Type**: real Irish location (country estate)
- **Region**: Co. Galway, Connacht
- **Key features**: Coole Lake (home to the swans), the Seven Woods, the walled garden, the house (demolished 1941)

## Significance to Yeats

Coole Park provided Yeats with something his peripatetic early life lacked: a home. Lady Gregory's hospitality gave him a study, a library, and the freedom to write without financial anxiety during the most productive period of his career. Many of the poems in _The Wild Swans at Coole_ (1919), _Michael Robartes and the Dancer_ (1921), and _The Tower_ (1928) were written or conceived at Coole.

The "seven woods" of the estate — named in Yeats's poetry — became a private symbolic landscape: not the wild, mythological Sligo of his childhood, but a cultivated, walled, ordered nature that was nonetheless touched by the supernatural. The swans on Coole Lake are the most famous of these presences: fifty-nine of them in the 1916 autumn, counted by the poet, returning year after year while he aged.

Coole also represents a phase in Yeats's relationship to the Anglo-Irish Ascendancy. The Big House tradition — the Protestant landowning class that produced Swift, Burke, and Goldsmith — was embodied for Yeats in Coole Park. When the house was sold and demolished after Lady Gregory's death, Yeats wrote "Coole Park, 1929" and "Coole Park and Ballylee, 1931" as elegies for a vanishing civilization.

## Literary and Historical Context

Coole Park was a salon as well as a refuge. Lady Gregory hosted the key figures of the Irish Literary Revival — Synge, Hyde, O'Casey, George Moore, and many others. The conversations at Coole shaped the direction of Irish literature in the early 20th century. For Yeats, Coole was the institutional counterpart to the imaginative landscape of Sligo: a place where literature was made, not just dreamed.
