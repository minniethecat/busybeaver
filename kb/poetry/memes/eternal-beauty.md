# Eternal Beauty

Eternal Beauty in Yeats's poetry is a Platonic ideal — an unchanging, transcendent form of beauty that exists outside time and decay. It is the beauty that "wanders on her way" ((031-016)), immune to the mutability that claims mortal things. This concept draws on Shelley's "Intellectual Beauty," Neoplatonic philosophy, and Yeats's own mystical system, where eternal beauty serves as a gateway to the spiritual realm.

## Origins and Sources

- **Platonic / Neoplatonic philosophy**: The Idea of Beauty as an eternal Form, accessible through contemplation but not through the senses
- **Shelley's "Hymn to Intellectual Beauty"**: A direct influence — Yeats acknowledged the debt in his endnotes, noting that his Rose "differs from the Intellectual Beauty of Shelley" ((048-007))
- **Blakean vision**: Eternal beauty accessed through imaginative perception, not rational thought
- **Irish legend**: The beauty of the Sidhe (fairy folk), who live outside mortal time

## Symbolic Meaning

Eternal Beauty operates in Yeats's system as:

1. **The antinomy of mortal beauty**: Mortal beauty fades, withers, and dies — "your beauty perish and be lost" ((038-448)). In contrast, Eternal Beauty "wandering on her way" ((031-016)) is immune to time.

2. **The artist's quest**: The poet labors "to build a perfect beauty in rhyme" ((038-332)), attempting to capture the eternal in the temporal medium of language.

3. **Platonic transcendence**: Beauty is the form through which the soul ascends to the eternal — "Men have named beauty" ((038-400)) as that which enfolds the ancient, sacred wisdom.

## Development Across Yeats's Career

**Early period**: Eternal Beauty is closely tied to the Rose symbol — a mystical, almost religious vision of beauty as salvific force. The 1893 Rose poems posit beauty as a Platonic absolute.

**Middle period**: The concept complicates. "The Old Age of Queen Maeve" (1903) acknowledges beauty's hollow quality: "all their beauty's like a hollow dream" ((039-080)). The tension between eternal and mortal beauty becomes a productive paradox.

**Late period**: Beauty becomes more concrete and elegiac. "The Wild Swans at Coole" ((045-005)) finds eternal beauty not in abstract forms but in the swans themselves — creatures that seem exempt from time even as the poet ages. "A Prayer for My Daughter" ((046-266)) reclaims beauty from abstraction into lived ceremonial tradition.
