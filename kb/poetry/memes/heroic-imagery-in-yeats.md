# Heroic Imagery in Yeats

Heroic imagery in Yeats's poetry draws upon Irish mythological figures — Cuchulain,
Fergus, Oisin — as embodiments of aristocratic virtue, tragic courage, and the
anti-modern ideal of the noble warrior.

## Definition

Yeats deployed heroic figures not as nostalgic antiquarianism but as moral exemplars
set against what he saw as the vulgarity of modern democratic culture. The hero in
Yeats's system functions as a symbol of the unified self — one who acts with
complete identity between will and deed, unlike the fragmented modern subject
[048-015].

## Origins and Sources

- **Primary source**: Irish mythology (Ulster Cycle, Fenian Cycle)
- **Key texts**: "Cuchulain's Fight with the Sea," "Fergus and the Druid," "The Wanderings of Oisin"

Yeats drew these figures from Standish O'Grady's retellings of Irish legend and
from Lady Gregory's *Cuchulain of Muirthemne*. The heroic material offered Yeats
an alternative to both Christian humility and modern materialism. The endnotes
describe the landscape as resonant "because of the heroic tales and beautiful old
myths" embedded in it [048-015].

## Development Across Yeats's Career

Heroic imagery evolved from the dreamy romanticism of early Yeats into the harder,
more tragic vision of middle and late period. In early work such as "The Wanderings
of Oisin" (1889), the hero's journey into the Otherworld is lush and mythological.
By "Cuchulain's Fight with the Sea" (from *The Rose*, 1893), the tone darkens:
Cuchulain battles both his son and the sea in a tragedy of unwitting kinship
[031-007] [031-119].

In the middle period, Fergus appears in "Fergus and the Druid" as a king who
renounces his crown for druidic wisdom — a specifically Yeatsian twist on the
heroic ideal, exchanging martial glory for visionary knowledge [031-038] [031-051].
The late poetry recasts the hero in explicitly anti-modern terms: the speaker of
"The Tower" invokes heroic precedent against the degradations of old age, and
"the heroes' crescent is the twelfth" in Yeats's lunar symbolism [045-1030].

## The Heroic Mask and Anti-Self

The hero in Yeats's mature thought is inseparable from his doctrine of the mask.
In *Per Amica Silentia Lunae* (1918), Yeats argues that the poet, like the hero,
achieves unity of being by pursuing his opposite — his anti-self. The heroic
figure thus becomes a psychological model as much as a mythological one: Cuchulain
represents not just a legendary warrior but the possibility of achieving what Yeats
called "the complete man" through disciplined self-remaking. The hero fights
not only outward enemies but the fragmentation of the modern self. This idea
reaches its fullest statement in the late plays, particularly *The Death of
Cuchulain* (1939), where the dying hero ties himself to a pillar so he may meet
death standing — an image of defiant self-possession that Yeats intended as his
own epitaph in art.

## Heroism and the Aristocratic Ideal

Yeats's heroic imagery is inseparable from his anti-democratic politics. The hero
functions as the supreme instance of what Yeats called "the aristocratic
principle" — the belief that excellence is rare, hereditary, and fragile in the
face of mass culture. In poems like "Upon a House Shaken by the Land Agitation"
and "Ancestral Houses," the hero's lineage — rather than his individual deeds —
is what matters. The Coole Park poems celebrate Lady Gregory's house not for any
single heroic act but for embodying a tradition of heroic hospitality and
patronage. This is a backward-looking, elegiac mode of heroism: the hero's
greatness is always already passing, threatened by "the levelling, rancorous,
rational sort of mind." The tension between this aristocratic heroism and the
actual heroism of the Irish revolutionaries Yeats commemorates in "Easter 1916"
creates some of the most productive ambiguities in his work.

## The Dying Hero and Tragic Joy

The hero's death is the climax of Yeats's heroic imagination. Cuchulain fighting
the sea, Fergus dying as a "wild god," the "fool" in *The Hero, the Girl, and
the Fool* meeting his end with ecstatic indifference — in each case, death
transfigures the hero rather than extinguishing him. This is the core of what
Yeats called "tragic joy": the recognition that destruction and fulfillment
are, in the heroic life, the same thing. The late poem "Cuchulain Comforted"
imagines the dead hero received by "convicted cowards" who teach him to sew a
shroud — a deliberate anti-climax that nevertheless affirms the hero's
incorporation into the community of the dead. The heroic trajectory in Yeats
thus moves from mythological triumph through aristocratic nostalgia to a final,
austere acceptance of death itself as the completing act of the heroic life.
