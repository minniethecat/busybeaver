# The Wild Swans at Coole

## Preface

[045-001]This book is, in part, a reprint of _The Wild Swans at [[coole-park|Coole]]_ , printed a year ago on my sister’s hand-press at Dundrum, Co. [[dublin-city|Dublin]]. I have not, however, reprinted a play which may be a part of a book of new plays suggested by the dance plays of Japan, and I have added a number of new poems. [[michael-robartes|Michael Robartes]] and [[john-aherne|John Aherne]], whose names occur in one or other of these, are characters in some stories I wrote years ago, who have once again become a part of the phantasmagoria through which I can alone express my convictions about the world. I have the fancy that I read the name [[owen-aherne|John Aherne]] among those of men prosecuted for making a disturbance at the first production of “The Play Boy,” which may account for his animosity to myself.

[045-002]W. B. Y.

[045-003][[thoor-ballylee|Ballylee]], [[galway-county|County Galway]],

[045-004]September 1918.

## The Wild Swans at Coole

[045-005]The trees are in their autumn beauty,   

[045-006]The woodland paths are dry,   

[045-007]Under the October twilight the water   

[045-008]Mirrors a still sky;   

[045-009]Upon the brimming water among the stones   

[045-010]Are nine and fifty swans.

[045-011]The nineteenth Autumn has come upon me   

[045-012]Since I first made my count;   

[045-013]I saw, before I had [[water-imagery|well]] finished,   

[045-014]All suddenly mount   

[045-015]And scatter wheeling in great broken rings   

[045-016]Upon their clamorous wings.

[045-017]I have looked upon those brilliant creatures,   

[045-018]And now my heart is sore.   

[045-019]All’s changed since I, hearing at twilight,   

[045-020]The first time on this shore,   

[045-021]The bell-beat of their wings above my head,   

[045-022]Trod with a lighter tread.

[045-023]Unwearied still, lover by lover,   

[045-024]They paddle in the cold,   

[045-025]Companionable streams or climb the air;   

[045-026]Their hearts have not grown old;   

[045-027]Passion or conquest, wander where they will,   

[045-028]Attend upon them still.

[045-029]But now they drift on the still water   

[045-030]Mysterious, beautiful;   

[045-031]Among what rushes will they build,   

[045-032]By what lake’s edge or pool   

[045-033]Delight men’s eyes, when I awake some day   

[045-034]To find they have flown away?

## In Memory of Major Robert Gregory

### 1

[045-035]Now that we’re almost settled in our house   

[045-036]I’ll name the friends that cannot sup with us   

[045-037]Beside a fire of turf in th’ ancient tower,   

[045-038]And having talked to some late hour   

[045-039]Climb up the narrow [[winding-stair|winding stair]] to bed:   

[045-040]Discoverers of forgotten truth   

[045-041]Or mere companions of my youth,   

[045-042]All, all are in my thoughts to-night, being dead.

### 2

[045-043]Always we’d have the new friend meet the old   

[045-044]And we are hurt if either friend seem cold,   

[045-045]And there is salt to lengthen out the smart   

[045-046]In the affections of our heart,   

[045-047]And quarrels are blown up upon that head;   

[045-048]But not a friend that I would bring   

[045-049]This night can set us quarrelling,   

[045-050]For all that come into my mind are dead.

### 3

[045-051]Lionel Johnson comes the first to mind,   

[045-052]That loved his learning better than mankind,   

[045-053]Though courteous to the worst; much falling he   

[045-054]Brooded upon sanctity   

[045-055]Till all his Greek and Latin learning seemed   

[045-056]A long blast upon the horn that brought   

[045-057]A little nearer to his thought   

[045-058]A measureless consummation that he dreamed.

### 4

[045-059]And that enquiring man John Synge comes next   

[045-060]That [[death-in-yeats|dying]] chose the living world for text   

[045-061]And never could have rested in the tomb   

[045-062]But that, long travelling, he had come   

[045-063]Towards nightfall upon certain set apart   

[045-064]In a most desolate stony place,   

[045-065]Towards nightfall upon a race   

[045-066]Passionate and simple like his heart.

### 5

[045-067]And then I think of old George Pollexfen,   

[045-068]In muscular youth well known to Mayo men   

[045-069]For horsemanship at meets or at race-courses,   

[045-070]That could have shown how purebred horses   

[045-071]And solid men, for all their [[fire-passion|passion]], live   

[045-072]But as the outrageous stars incline   

[045-073]By opposition, square and trine;   

[045-074]Having grown sluggish and contemplative.

### 6

[045-075]They were my close companions many a year,   

[045-076]A portion of my mind and life, as it were,   

[045-077]And now their breathless faces seem to look   

[045-078]Out of some old picture-book;   

[045-079]I am accustomed to their lack of breath,   

[045-080]But not that my dear friend’s dear son,   

[045-081]Our Sidney and our perfect man,   

[045-082]Could share in that discourtesy of death.

### 7

[045-083]For all things the delighted eye now sees   

[045-084]Were loved by him; the old storm-broken trees   

[045-085]That cast their shadows upon road and bridge;   

[045-086]The tower set on the stream’s edge;   

[045-087]The ford where drinking cattle make a stir   

[045-088]Nightly, and startled by that sound   

[045-089]The water-hen must change her ground;   

[045-090]He might have been your heartiest welcomer.

### 8

[045-091]When with the Galway foxhounds he would ride   

[045-092]From Castle Taylor to the Roxborough side   

[045-093]Or Esserkelly plain, few kept his pace;   

[045-094]At Mooneen he had leaped a place   

[045-095]So perilous that half the astonished meet   

[045-096]Had shut their eyes, and where was it   

[045-097]He rode a race without a bit?   

[045-098]And yet his mind outran the horses’ feet.

### 9

[045-099]We dreamed that a great painter had been born   

[045-100]To cold Clare [[stone-imagery|rock]] and Galway rock and thorn,   

[045-101]To that stern colour and that delicate line   

[045-102]That are our secret discipline   

[045-103]Wherein the gazing heart doubles her might.   

[045-104]Soldier, scholar, horseman, he,   

[045-105]And yet he had the intensity   

[045-106]To have published all to be a world’s delight.

### 10

[045-107]What other could so well have counselled us   

[045-108]In all lovely intricacies of a house   

[045-109]As he that practised or that understood   

[045-110]All work in metal or in wood,   

[045-111]In moulded plaster or in carven stone?   

[045-112]Soldier, scholar, horseman, he,   

[045-113]And all he did done perfectly   

[045-114]As though he had but that one trade alone.

### 11

[045-115]Some burn damp fagots, others may consume   

[045-116]The entire combustible world in one small room   

[045-117]As though dried straw, and if we turn about   

[045-118]The bare chimney is gone black out   

[045-119]Because the work had finished in that flare.   

[045-120]Soldier, scholar, horseman, he,   

[045-121]As ’twere all life’s epitome.   

[045-122]What made us dream that he could comb grey hair?

### 12

[045-123]I had thought, seeing how bitter is that wind   

[045-124]That shakes the shutter, to have brought to mind   

[045-125]All those that manhood tried, or childhood loved   

[045-126]Or boyish intellect approved,   

[045-127]With some appropriate commentary on each;   

[045-128]Until imagination brought   

[045-129]A fitter welcome; but a thought   

[045-130]Of that late death took all my heart for speech.

## An Irish Airman Foresees His Death

[045-131]I know that I shall meet my fate   

[045-132]Somewhere among the clouds above;   

[045-133]Those that I fight I do not hate,   

[045-134]Those that I guard I do not love;   

[045-135]My country is Kiltartan Cross,   

[045-136]My countrymen Kiltartan’s poor,   

[045-137]No likely end could bring them loss   

[045-138]Or leave them happier than before.   

[045-139]Nor law, nor duty bade me fight,   

[045-140]Nor public man, nor angry crowds,   

[045-141]A lonely impulse of delight   

[045-142]Drove to this tumult in the clouds;   

[045-143]I balanced all, brought all to mind,   

[045-144]The years to come seemed waste of breath,   

[045-145]A waste of breath the years behind   

[045-146]In balance with this life, this death.

## Men Improve with the Years

[045-147]I am worn out with dreams;   

[045-148]A weather-worn, marble triton   

[045-149]Among the streams;   

[045-150]And all day long I look   

[045-151]Upon this lady’s beauty   

[045-152]As though I had found in book   

[045-153]A pictured beauty,   

[045-154]Pleased to have filled the eyes   

[045-155]Or the discerning ears,   

[045-156]Delighted to be but wise,   

[045-157]For men improve with the years;   

[045-158]And yet and yet   

[045-159]Is this my dream, or the truth?   

[045-160]O would that we had met   

[045-161]When I had my burning youth;   

[045-162]But I grow old among dreams,   

[045-163]A weather-worn, marble triton   

[045-164]Among the streams.

## The Collar-Bone of a Hare

[045-165]Would I could cast a sail on the water   

[045-166]Where many a king has gone   

[045-167]And many a king’s daughter,   

[045-168]And alight at the comely trees and the lawn,   

[045-169]The playing upon pipes and the [[dance-in-yeats|dancing]],   

[045-170]And learn that the best thing is   

[045-171]To change my loves while dancing   

[045-172]And pay but a kiss for a kiss.

[045-173]I would find by the edge of that water   

[045-174]The collar-bone of a hare   

[045-175]Worn thin by the lapping of water,   

[045-176]And pierce it through with a gimlet and stare   

[045-177]At the old bitter world where they marry in churches,   

[045-178]And laugh over the untroubled water   

[045-179]At all who marry in churches,   

[045-180]Through the white thin bone of a hare.

## Under the Round Tower

[045-181]“Although I’d lie lapped up in linen   

[045-182]A deal I’d sweat and little earn   

[045-183]If I should live as live the neighbours,”   

[045-184]Cried the beggar, Billy Byrne;   

[045-185]“Stretch bones till the daylight come   

[045-186]On great-grandfather’s battered tomb.”

[045-187]Upon a grey old battered tombstone   

[045-188]In Glendalough beside the stream,   

[045-189]Where the O’Byrnes and Byrnes are buried,   

[045-190]He stretched his bones and fell in a dream   

[045-191]Of sun and moon that a good hour   

[045-192]Bellowed and pranced in the round tower;

[045-193]Of golden king and silver lady,   

[045-194]Bellowing up and bellowing round,   

[045-195]Till toes mastered a sweet measure,   

[045-196]Mouth mastered a sweet sound,   

[045-197]Prancing round and prancing up   

[045-198]Until they pranced upon the top.

[045-199]That golden king and that wild lady   

[045-200]Sang till stars began to fade,   

[045-201]Hands gripped in hands, toes close together,   

[045-202]Hair spread on the wind they made;   

[045-203]That lady and that golden king   

[045-204]Could like a brace of blackbirds sing.

[045-205]“It’s certain that my luck is broken,”   

[045-206]That rambling jailbird Billy said;   

[045-207]“Before nightfall I’ll pick a pocket   

[045-208]And snug it in a feather-bed,   

[045-209]I cannot find the peace of home   

[045-210]On great-grandfather’s battered tomb.”

## Solomon to Sheba

[045-211]Sang Solomon to Sheba,   

[045-212]And kissed her dusky face,   

[045-213]“All day long from mid-day   

[045-214]We have talked in the one place,   

[045-215]All day long from shadowless noon   

[045-216]We have gone round and round   

[045-217]In the narrow theme of love   

[045-218]Like an old horse in a pound.”

[045-219]To Solomon sang Sheba,   

[045-220]Planted on his knees,   

[045-221]“If you had broached a matter   

[045-222]That might the learned please,   

[045-223]You had before the sun had thrown   

[045-224]Our shadows on the ground   

[045-225]Discovered that my thoughts, not it,   

[045-226]Are but a narrow pound.”

[045-227]Sang Solomon to Sheba,   

[045-228]And kissed her Arab eyes,   

[045-229]“There’s not a man or woman   

[045-230]Born under the skies   

[045-231]Dare match in learning with us two,   

[045-232]And all day long we have found   

[045-233]There’s not a thing but love can make   

[045-234]The world a narrow pound.”

## The Living Beauty

[045-235]I’ll say and maybe dream I have drawn content﻿—   

[045-236]Seeing that time has frozen up the blood,   

[045-237]The wick of youth being burned and the oil spent﻿—   

[045-238]From beauty that is cast out of a mould   

[045-239]In bronze, or that in dazzling marble appears,   

[045-240]Appears, and when we have gone is gone again,   

[045-241]Being more indifferent to our solitude   

[045-242]Than ’twere an apparition. O heart, we are old,   

[045-243]The living beauty is for younger men,   

[045-244]We cannot pay its tribute of wild tears.

## A Song

[045-245]I thought no more was needed   

[045-246]Youth to prolong   

[045-247]Than dumb-bell and foil   

[045-248]To keep the body young.   

[045-249]Oh, who could have foretold   

[045-250]That the heart grows old?

[045-251]Though I have many words,   

[045-252]What woman’s satisfied,   

[045-253]I am no longer faint   

[045-254]Because at her side?   

[045-255]Oh, who could have foretold   

[045-256]That the heart grows old?

[045-257]I have not lost [[love-in-yeats|desire]]   

[045-258]But the heart that I had;   

[045-259]I thought ’twould burn my body   

[045-260]Laid on the death-bed.   

[045-261]But who could have foretold   

[045-262]That the heart grows old?

## To a Young Beauty

[045-263]Dear fellow-artist, why so free   

[045-264]With every sort of company,   

[045-265]With every Jack and Jill?   

[045-266]Choose your companions from the best;   

[045-267]Who draws a bucket with the rest   

[045-268]Soon topples down the hill.

[045-269]You may, that mirror for a school,   

[045-270]Be passionate, not bountiful   

[045-271]As common beauties may,   

[045-272]Who were not born to keep in trim   

[045-273]With old Ezekiel’s cherubim   

[045-274]But those of Beaujolet.

[045-275]I know what wages beauty gives,   

[045-276]How hard a life her servant lives,   

[045-277]Yet praise the winters gone;   

[045-278]There is not a fool can call me friend,   

[045-279]And I may dine at journey’s end   

[045-280]With Landor and with Donne.

## To a Young Girl

[045-281]My dear, my dear, I know   

[045-282]More than another   

[045-283]What makes your heart beat so;   

[045-284]Not even your own mother   

[045-285]Can know it as I know,   

[045-286]Who broke my heart for her   

[045-287]When the wild thought,   

[045-288]That she denies   

[045-289]And has forgot,   

[045-290]Set all her blood astir   

[045-291]And glittered in her eyes.

## The Scholars

[045-292]Bald heads forgetful of their sins,   

[045-293]Old, learned, respectable bald heads   

[045-294]Edit and annotate the lines   

[045-295]That young men, tossing on their beds,   

[045-296]Rhymed out in love’s despair   

[045-297]To flatter beauty’s ignorant ear.

[045-298]They’ll cough in the ink to the world’s end;   

[045-299]Wear out the carpet with their shoes   

[045-300]Earning respect; have no strange friend;   

[045-301]If they have sinned nobody knows.   

[045-302]Lord, what would they say   

[045-303]Should their Catullus walk that way?

## Tom O’Roughley

[045-304]“Though logic choppers rule the town,   

[045-305]And every man and maid and boy   

[045-306]Has marked a distant object down,   

[045-307]An aimless joy is a pure joy,”   

[045-308]Or so did Tom O’Roughley say   

[045-309]That saw the surges running by,   

[045-310]“And wisdom is a butterfly   

[045-311]And not a gloomy bird of prey.

[045-312]“If little planned is little sinned   

[045-313]But little need the grave distress.   

[045-314]What’s dying but a second wind?   

[045-315]How but in zig-zag wantonness   

[045-316]Could trumpeter Michael be so brave?”   

[045-317]Or something of that sort he said,   

[045-318]“And if my dearest friend were dead   

[045-319]I’d dance a measure on his grave.”

## The Sad Shepherd

[045-320]Shepherd |  That cry’s from the first cuckoo of the year.   

[045-321]I wished before it ceased.  

[045-322]---|---  

[045-323]Goatherd |  Nor bird nor beast   

[045-324]Could make me wish for anything this day,   

[045-325]Being old, but that the old alone might die,   

[045-326]And that would be against God’s Providence.   

[045-327]Let the young wish. But what has brought you here?   

[045-328]Never until this moment have we met   

[045-329]Where my goats browse on the scarce grass or leap   

[045-330]From stone to stone.  

[045-331]Shepherd |  I am looking for strayed sheep;   

[045-332]Something has troubled me and in my trouble   

[045-333]I let them stray. I thought of rhyme alone,   

[045-334]For rhyme can beat a measure out of trouble   

[045-335]And make the daylight sweet once more; but when   

[045-336]I had driven every rhyme into its place   

[045-337]The sheep had gone from theirs.  

[045-338]Goatherd |  I know right well   

[045-339]What turned so good a shepherd from his charge.  

[045-340]Shepherd |  He that was best in every country sport   

[045-341]And every country craft, and of us all   

[045-342]Most courteous to slow age and hasty youth   

[045-343]Is dead.  

[045-344]Goatherd |  The boy that brings my griddle cake   

[045-345]Brought the bare news.  

[045-346]Shepherd |  He had thrown the crook away   

[045-347]And died in the great war beyond the sea.  

[045-348]Goatherd |  He had often played his pipes among my hills,   

[045-349]And when he played it was their loneliness,   

[045-350]The exultation of their stone, that cried   

[045-351]Under his fingers.  

[045-352]Shepherd |  I had it from his mother,   

[045-353]And his own flock was browsing at the door.  

[045-354]Goatherd |  How does she bear her grief? There is not a shepherd   

[045-355]But grows more gentle when he speaks her name,   

[045-356]Remembering kindness done, and how can I   

[045-357]That found when I had neither goat nor grazing   

[045-358]New welcome and [[wisdom-in-yeats|old wisdom]] at her fire   

[045-359]Till winter blasts were gone, but speak of her   

[045-360]Even before his children and his wife.  

[045-361]Shepherd |  She goes about her house erect and calm   

[045-362]Between the pantry and the linen chest,   

[045-363]Or else at meadow or at grazing overlooks   

[045-364]Her labouring men, as though her darling lived,   

[045-365]But for her grandson now; there is no change   

[045-366]But such as I have seen upon her face   

[045-367]Watching our shepherd sports at harvest-time   

[045-368]When her son’s turn was over.  

[045-369]Goatherd |  Sing your song,   

[045-370]I too have rhymed my reveries, but youth   

[045-371]Is hot to show whatever it has found,   

[045-372]And till that’s done can neither work nor wait.   

[045-373]Old goatherds and old goats, if in all else   

[045-374]Youth can excel them in accomplishment,   

[045-375]Are learned in waiting.  

[045-376]Shepherd |  You cannot but have seen   

[045-377]That he alone had gathered up no gear,   

[045-378]Set carpenters to work on no wide table,   

[045-379]On no long bench nor lofty milking shed   

[045-380]As others will, when first they take possession,   

[045-381]But left the house as in his father’s time   

[045-382]As though he knew himself, as it were, a cuckoo,   

[045-383]No settled man. And now that he is gone   

[045-384]There’s nothing of him left but half a score   

[045-385]Of sorrowful, austere, sweet, lofty pipe tunes.  

[045-386]Goatherd |  You have put the thought in rhyme.  

[045-387]Shepherd |  I worked all day   

[045-388]And when ’twas done so little had I done   

[045-389]That maybe “I am sorry” in plain prose   

[045-390]Had sounded better to your mountain fancy.  

[045-391]|  _He sings._  

[045-392]|  “Like the speckled bird that steers   

[045-393]Thousands of leagues oversea,   

[045-394]And runs for a while or a while half-flies   

[045-395]Upon his yellow legs through our meadows,   

[045-396]He stayed for a while; and we   

[045-397]Had scarcely accustomed our ears   

[045-398]To his speech at the break of day,   

[045-399]Had scarcely accustomed our eyes   

[045-400]To his shape at the rinsing pool   

[045-401]Among the evening shadows,   

[045-402]When he vanished from ears and eyes.   

[045-403]I had wished a dear thing on that day   

[045-404]I heard him first, but man is a fool.”  

[045-405]Goatherd |  You sing as always of the natural life,   

[045-406]And I that made like [[song-and-poetry|music]] in my youth   

[045-407]Hearing it now have sighed for that young man   

[045-408]And certain lost companions of my own.  

[045-409]Shepherd |  They say that on your barren mountain ridge   

[045-410]You have measured out the road that the soul treads   

[045-411]When it has vanished from our natural eyes;   

[045-412]That you have talked with apparitions.  

[045-413]Goatherd |  Indeed   

[045-414]My daily thoughts since the first stupor of youth   

[045-415]Have found the path my goat’s feet cannot find.  

[045-416]Shepherd |  Sing, for it may be that your thoughts have plucked   

[045-417]Some medicable herb to make our grief   

[045-418]Less bitter.  

[045-419]Goatherd |  They have brought me from that ridge   

[045-420]Seed pods and flowers that are not all wild poppy.  

[045-421]|  _Sings._  

[045-422]|  “He grows younger every second   

[045-423]That were all his birthdays reckoned   

[045-424]Much too solemn seemed;   

[045-425]Because of what he had dreamed,   

[045-426]Or the ambitions that he served,   

[045-427]Much too solemn and reserved.   

[045-428]Jaunting, journeying   

[045-429]To his own dayspring,   

[045-430]He unpacks the loaded pern[21](endnotes.xhtml#note-21)   

[045-431]Of all ’twas pain or joy to learn,   

[045-432]Of all that he had made.   

[045-433]The outrageous war shall fade;   

[045-434]At some old winding whitethorn root   

[045-435]He’ll practise on the shepherd’s flute,   

[045-436]Or on the close-cropped grass   

[045-437]Court his shepherd lass,   

[045-438]Or run where lads reform our daytime   

[045-439]Till that is their long shouting playtime;   

[045-440]Knowledge he shall unwind   

[045-441]Through victories of the mind,   

[045-442]Till, clambering at the cradle side,   

[045-443]He dreams himself his mother’s pride,   

[045-444]All knowledge lost in trance   

[045-445]Of sweeter ignorance.”  

[045-446]Shepherd |  When I have shut these ewes and this old ram   

[045-447]Into the fold, we’ll to the woods and there   

[045-448]Cut out our rhymes on strips of new-torn bark   

[045-449]But put no name and leave them at her door.   

[045-450]To know the mountain and the valley have grieved   

[045-451]May be a quiet thought to wife and mother,   

[045-452]And children when they spring up shoulder high.  
  
## Lines Written in Dejection

[045-453]When have I last looked on   

[045-454]The round green eyes and the long wavering bodies   

[045-455]Of the dark leopards of the moon?   

[045-456]All the wild witches, those most noble ladies,   

[045-457]For all their broom-sticks and their tears,   

[045-458]Their angry tears, are gone.   

[045-459]The holy centaurs of the hills are vanished;   

[045-460]And I have nothing but harsh sun;
