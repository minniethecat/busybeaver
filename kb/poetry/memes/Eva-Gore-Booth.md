# Eva Gore-Booth

Eva Selina Gore-Booth (1870–1926) was an Irish poet, dramatist, and social reformer. She was the younger sister of Constance Markievicz (née Gore-Booth), the revolutionary who fought in the 1916 Easter Rising. The Gore-Booth sisters grew up at [[Lissadell|Lissadell House]] in Co. Sligo — the great house where the young W. B. Yeats visited in 1894 and which he immortalised in his late poem "In Memory of Eva Gore-Booth and Con Markievicz."

## Yeats's Visits to Lissadell

Yeats visited Lissadell House in 1894, when Eva was twenty-four and Constance twenty-six. The visit made a profound impression on the young poet, who later wrote of the "two girls in silk kimonos, both beautiful, one a gazelle" — the gazelle being Eva. The memory of those visits, charged with youth and beauty, became the subject of one of Yeats's greatest elegies decades later.

## "In Memory of Eva Gore-Booth and Con Markievicz"

Written in 1927 and published in *The Winding Stair and Other Poems* (1933), this elegy contrasts the remembered beauty of the sisters at Lissadell with their later lives — Constance as a revolutionary sentenced to death (commuted) after the Easter Rising, Eva as a social reformer and pacifist in Manchester. The poem's great lament — "The innocent and the beautiful / Have no enemy but time" — is one of Yeats's most quoted lines.

## Life and Work

Eva Gore-Booth moved to Manchester in 1897, where she lived with her lifelong companion Esther Roper. She worked as a suffragist, a pacifist (opposing the First World War), and an advocate for working-class women. She published several volumes of poetry and drama, much of it infused with mystical and religious themes. Her poetry was influenced by Yeats's early work but took a more explicitly political and spiritual direction.
