## Introduction

The phrase "the ceremony of innocence is drowned" [046-397] is one of the most famous lines in W. B. Yeats' poetry, drawn from "The Second Coming" (1919), his great apocalyptic vision of a world breaking apart. The "ceremony of innocence" names a cluster of ideas central to Yeats' mature thought: that civilization depends on ritual, custom, and inherited form; that these forms are fragile; and that when they collapse, what emerges is not freedom but chaos — "mere anarchy" [046-396] loosed upon the world. The concept weaves together Yeats' aristocratic values, his theory of history, and his understanding of the relationship between form and meaning in art and life.

## Origins and Context

Yeats wrote "The Second Coming" in January 1919, in the aftermath of the First World War and at the beginning of the Irish War of Independence. The phrase "ceremony of innocence" condenses a complex of concerns that had been developing throughout his work: the value of "ancient ceremony" [044-1000] as a container for meaning, the threat posed by modern mass politics to inherited forms, and the conviction that innocence — in the sense of a pure, unselfconscious relation to being — is not natural but achieved through discipline and custom. In "A Prayer for My Daughters" (1919), written the same year, the poet reflects on "how but in custom and in ceremony / Are innocence and beauty born" [046-490] — the same pairing of ceremony and innocence, but here as a statement of positive belief rather than elegy. The two poems together articulate the double face of the concept: ceremony is both what we lose and what we must cultivate.

## The Second Coming

In "The Second Coming," the "ceremony of innocence" is what drowns in the "blood-dimmed tide" [046-395] of historical violence. The poem's vision is of a world where the centre cannot hold, where "the best lack all conviction" while "the worst / Are full of passionate intensity" [046-392]. In such a world, ceremony — the ordered forms of social and spiritual life — is swept away, and with it the innocence that ceremony protects. The drowning is not a purification but a destruction: the rough beast that "slouches towards Bethlehem to be born" [046-403] is not the second coming of Christ but its antithesis, a revelation not of redemption but of horror. The "ceremony of innocence" thus stands for everything that civilization must maintain against the recurrent pressure of barbarism — and for everything that, in Yeats' historical vision, must periodically be swept away.

## Radical Innocence

The concept evolves in Yeats' later work into the idea of "radical innocence" [046-479]. In "A Prayer for My Daughter," the poet traces a movement from the drowned ceremony of the public world to the recoverable ceremony of the private: "the soul recovers radical innocence" [046-479] through the discipline of custom and ceremony. Radical innocence is not the innocence of ignorance but the achieved innocence of wisdom — the state of being that comes from having passed through knowledge and returned to a simpler relation with life. "How but in custom and in ceremony / Are innocence and beauty born?" [046-490] the poet asks, locating the source of value not in spontaneous self-expression but in the traditional forms that shape and sustain the self. In this late formulation, ceremony is not a decorative addition to life but its generative principle.

## The Ceremonial Imagination

The "ceremony of innocence" extends beyond this single poem to illuminate Yeats' larger artistic project. His poetry is itself ceremonial: its formal precision, its ritual repetitions, its hieratic tone all enact the principle that meaning requires form. The masks and personae he adopted, the occult systems he constructed, the dramatic rituals he wrote for the Abbey Theatre — all are ceremonies in the Yeatsian sense, disciplines through which innocence (authenticity, vision, truth) becomes possible. To say that "the ceremony of innocence is drowned" is to describe not only a historical crisis but an artistic one: the poet's task is to recover the ceremony, to rebuild the forms through which the soul may "recover radical innocence."
