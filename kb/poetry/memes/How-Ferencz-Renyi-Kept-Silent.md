# How Ferencz Renyi Kept Silent

[022-001]Hungary, 1848

[022-002]We, too, have seen our bravest and our best   

[022-003]To prisons go, and mossy ruin rest   

[022-004]Where homes once whitened vale and mountain crest;   

[022-005]Therefore, O nation of the bleeding breast,   

[022-006]Libations, from the Hungary of the West.

[022-007]Before his tent the General sips his wine,   

[022-008]Waves off the flies, and warms him in the shine.   

[022-009]The Austrian Haynau he, in many lands   

[022-010]Famous, a man of rules, a victor. Stands   

[022-011]Before him one [[water-imagery|well]] guarded, with bound hands;   

[022-012]Schoolmaster he, a dreamer, fiddler, first   

[022-013]In every dance, by children sought. “Accurst,   

[022-014]Thy name is?”   

[022-015]“Renyi.”   

[022-016]“Of?”   

[022-017]“This village.”   

[022-018]“Good!   

[022-019]Hiding the rebels worm in yonder wood   

[022-020]Or yonder mountains. Where? Thou shalt be free﻿—   

[022-021]Silence! Thou shalt be dead!”

[022-022]Now suddenly   

[022-023]The [[soul-in-yeats|spirit]] of young Renyi has grown old.   

[022-024]He turns where, hung like drops of dripping gold,   

[022-025]Flashing and flickering with ever-undulant wing   

[022-026][[About]] a sun-flushed dove-cot, cooing, cling   

[022-027]The doves whose growing forms he’d watched. Not these   

[022-028]He numbers. He a brown farm-house sees,   

[022-029]Where shadow of cherry, and shadow of apple trees,   

[022-030]Enclose a quiet place of beds box-bordered, bees,   

[022-031]Hives, currant bushes. There his kin are. High   

[022-032]Above, the woods where with the soft mild eye   

[022-033]Of her he loved fixed on him full of [[shadow-and-light|light]],   

[022-034]Often he had bent down some bough all bright   

[022-035]With berries. Placid as a homeward bee,   

[022-036]Glad, simple﻿—nay, he sought not mystery,   

[022-037]Nor, gazing forth where life’s sad sickles reap,   

[022-038]Searched the unsearchable﻿—why good men weep;   

[022-039]Why those who do good often be not good,   

[022-040]Why they who will the highest sometimes brood,   

[022-041]Clogged in a marsh where the slow marsh clay clings,   

[022-042]Abolished by a mire of little things,   

[022-043]Untuned by their own striving. If one such   

[022-044]Were here, he would turn death into a crutch;   

[022-045]But this one﻿—this one. Now his head drops low,   

[022-046]Drops on his bosom, sombre, moist and slow.   

[022-047]“Choose!” Restless Haynau’s fingers tapping go.   

[022-048]This sullen peasant spoils the good sunshine,   

[022-049]This sullen peasant spoils the good red wine.   

[022-050]He whispers to a soldier, who goes out﻿—   

[022-051]A neighbouring cricket lifts his shrilly shout   

[022-052]Reiterant. A bird goes by the tent,   

[022-053]A lizard crawls﻿—the two men gaze intent,   

[022-054]As though they’d vowed to measure all its ways.

[022-055]Returns that soldier in the evening rays   

[022-056]Half hid. He brings the peasant’s only kin,   

[022-057]Two women, withered one and small and thin,   

[022-058]Bent low with toil and hoariest years. The other   

[022-059]Of middle age. “His sister here and mother.”   

[022-060]The soldier thus, and Haynau﻿—“Peasant, speak   

[022-061]If these be precious.”

[022-062]“I am old and weak,”   

[022-063]That ancient mother cries, “speak not, my son.   

[022-064]I’m weak, and by the hands shall hold each one   

[022-065]Of my dead children soon, whate’er betide,   

[022-066]For I am old and weak.”

[022-067]And at her side   

[022-068]The sister: “Sell thy country, and the shame   

[022-069]Of traitor evermore is on our name.”   

[022-070]Haynau, the man of system, lifts his hand   

[022-071]Serene. They’re led away, and where a band   

[022-072]Of soldiers ranked is on a grassy spot,   

[022-073]A score of yards off ’neath a willow, shot.

[022-074]“Now hath he kith or kin, or any friend?”   

[022-075]A soldier answers: “By the camp’s far end   

[022-076]I saw a girl afraid to be too near,   

[022-077]Afraid to be too far.” “Ay, bring her here!”

[022-078]Time goes. The flakèd fire of evening crawls   

[022-079]Along the tents, the fields, the village walls.   

[022-080]The hare hath laid asleep her frolic wits,   

[022-081]And every flower above its shadow sits.   

[022-082]“On this embroidered cloud,” the sun hath said,   

[022-083]“A little will I lay my weary head,   

[022-084]Among the gold, the amber, and the red.”   

[022-085]A careful field-mouse finds a fallen crumb;   

[022-086]Now steps draw close, he hides beneath a drum.   

[022-087]That maiden bring they. When the tall red deer   

[022-088]In trouble is, the doe will linger near.   

[022-089]A peasant pale and pretty, her eyes for fear,   

[022-090]Like small brown moths, a-tremble.

[022-091]“Renyi say   

[022-092]Where worm the rebels, or my bullets lay   

[022-093]The young one with the others.” Renyi’s pale   

[022-094]But speechless, and the maiden with long wail   

[022-095]Flings her before him. “Save thyself and me.   

[022-096]Speak, Ferencz, speak. We love each other. See,   

[022-097]I am so young. Dost thou no longer know,   

[022-098]Beloved, how two little years ago   

[022-099]I came the first time to thy village school?   

[022-100]Thou hast forgotten. On the oaken stool   

[022-101]I sat me down beside thee, and I knew   

[022-102]So little. As the months passed by we grew   

[022-103]To love each other. In my prayer-book still   

[022-104]The violets are that on the wooded hill   

[022-105]We gathered. Ferencz, nay, I must not die:   

[022-106]I am to be your wife. A village high   

[022-107]And lost and far in yonder hills I know;   

[022-108]There far away from all we two will go,   

[022-109]And be so happy.”

[022-110]To his hands she clings,   

[022-111]With cries and murmurs. Suddenly he flings   

[022-112]Away her clinging hands, and turns. She throws   

[022-113]Her arms around his feet. The signal goes   

[022-114]From Haynau’s lifted fingers﻿—two draw nigh   

[022-115]And seize her, and thus floats her quivering cry:   

[022-116]“Assassin, my assassin! thou who let’st me die,   

[022-117]I curse thee﻿—curse thee!”

[022-118]Renyi silent stands,   

[022-119]And she is dragged to where the willow bands   

[022-120]With quiet shade its ever dewy-plot.   

[022-121]Noise! and a flash, a momentary blot.   

[022-122]So ends a brain﻿—a world!

[022-123]The smoke goes up,   

[022-124]Creeping along the heavens’ purple cup,   

[022-125]Higher and higher gold with evening light;   

[022-126]It seems to fondle, with a finger bright   

[022-127]And soft, one glimmering star.

[022-128]Renyi has cast   

[022-129]His bonds away, sore struggling.

[022-130]Now at last   

[022-131]Haynau, thine hour has come, thy followers far   

[022-132]Beside the willow.

[022-133]Nay, to yonder star,   

[022-134]Yon bauble of the heavens, he lifts his hands,   

[022-135]And over tillage fields and pasture lands   

[022-136]Where lies the cow at peace beside her calf,   

[022-137]He rushes, rolling from his lips a madman’s laugh.
