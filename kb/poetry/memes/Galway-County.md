## Introduction

County Galway, in the province of Connacht on Ireland's western seaboard, is the county most intimately associated with W. B. Yeats's mature life and work. It was in Galway that he found his most sustained home — Lady Gregory's estate at [[coole-park|Coole Park]], near Gort — and it was in Galway, at [[thoor-ballylee|Thoor Ballylee]], that he purchased and restored his own tower, the setting for some of his greatest poems.

Where [[sligo-county|County Sligo]] was the landscape of Yeats's childhood imagination, Galway was the landscape of his adult achievement. The seven woods of Coole, the lake with its wild swans, the Norman tower at Ballylee, and the stony fields of the western countryside became the concrete imagery through which he explored the great themes of his maturity: age and youth, art and passion, civil war and civilisation.

## Coole Park

Coole Park, the estate of [[lady-gregory|Lady Augusta Gregory]] (1852–1932), was Yeats's spiritual home for over thirty years. He spent summers there from 1897 onward, walking "among the seven woods of Coole" (042-001) and drawing inspiration from its lake, its woods, and its wild swans.

"The [[The-Wild-Swans-at-Coole|Wild Swans at Coole]]" (1917) is the defining Coole poem. Set in "the October twilight" at the lake's edge, it counts the swans — "nine-and-fifty" — and meditates on their unchanging beauty against the poet's own ageing:

The volume *The Wild Swans at Coole* (1919), described in the collected edition as a "reprint of _The Wild Swans at Coole_, printed a year ago on my sister's hand-press" (045-001), represents the transition from Yeats's early, dreamier manner to the harder, more personal voice of his middle period.

Coole Park was demolished in 1941, but its landscape — the lake, the woods, the "autumn beauty" of the trees — survives in the poems.

## Thoor Ballylee

In 1917, Yeats purchased a Norman tower-house near Gort, which he renamed Thoor Ballylee and made his summer home. The tower became the central symbol of his 1928 collection *[[The-Tower|The Tower]]* and the setting for "[[meditations-in-time-of-civil-war|Meditations in Time of Civil War]]" (1923):

> Ballylee, County Galway, (045-003)

The endnotes to the collected poems record that "these poems were written at Thoor Ballylee in 1922, during the civil war" (048-081) and that the tower and its surroundings were "associated by legend, story and tradition with the neighbourhood of Thoor Ballylee or Ballylee Castle" (048-061).

The tower was both a real dwelling and a multivalent symbol: of the poet's isolation, of the Anglo-Irish Ascendancy's embattled position, of the persistence of art through political violence, and of the soul's ascent through the winding stair of spiritual discipline.

## Galway's Folk Traditions in Yeats

The Endnotes to Yeats's collected poems are saturated with Galway folklore — testimony to his decades of listening to the people of the county. "A woman near Gort, in Galway" (048-014) tells of a boy who, after returning from America, "never went to Mass, or to church, or to fairs, or to market" — a sign, in the folk understanding, that he had been touched by the Sidhe. "A man in Galway" (048-017) recounts the story of a bride swept from a boat by a "sudden blast of wind" that the priest alone could withstand.

A woman of Burren reports that the Sidhe "sometimes try to come over the side of the boat in the form of fishes, for they can take their choice shape" (048-023), while another Galway woman adds, "Surely those things are in the sea as well as on land" (048-023). An "old man who was cutting a quickset hedge near Gort, in Galway" (048-024) describes seeing "a girl picking nuts, with her hair hanging down over her shoulders; brown hair; and she had a good, clean face" — the word "clean" being used, Yeats notes, "in its old sense of fresh and comely" (048-025).

These folk testimonies are not mere ornament in Yeats's Endnotes; they form the living tissue of belief that connects his mythological poems to the actual landscape and people of Galway. The "old crane of Gort" (044-524) and the fairy doctor "on the borders of Galway and Clare" (048-032) who explained the battle over the dying are not literary inventions but figures drawn from the oral culture Yeats encountered in the county.

## Galway in Yeats's Poetry

Beyond Coole and Ballylee, Galway appears in Yeats's work in several registers:

- **The Galway Hunt**: "When with the Galway foxhounds he would ride" (045-091) — from "In Memory of Major Robert Gregory", celebrating the athletic aristocracy of the west of Ireland
- **The Rocky Landscape**: "To cold Clare rock and Galway rock and thorn" (045-100) — the stony, sparse terrain of the Burren and its Galway extension
- **Galway as Port**: "Or trading out of Galway into Spain" (044-011) — a reference to Galway city's medieval maritime trade, in the context of "Baile and Aillinn"
- **Folk Culture**: The endnotes are rich in Galway folklore — "A woman near Gort, in Galway, says..." (048-014), "The county Galway people use the word 'clean'..." (048-025), "a Galway blacksmith" (048-029) — reflecting Yeats's deep engagement with the oral traditions of the region

## Landscape as Symbol

The Galway landscape in Yeats's poetry is never merely descriptive. The "cold Clare rock and Galway rock and thorn" (045-100) evoke the austerity of the western terrain, but also the hardness and persistence of the spirit that dwells there. The seven woods of Coole are both a literal grove and a symbol of cultivated beauty — the Anglo-Irish Ascendancy's achievement, fragile and threatened. The tower at Ballylee is both a twelfth-century fortification and the image of the solitary soul, fortified against the world, ascending through the "winding stair" toward wisdom.

In "Coole Park, 1929," Yeats imagines the house and its grounds as a kind of Platonic ideal — a place "where old age cannot find them" (042-007), where the swans and the trees and the "dancer" of memory persist beyond time. This transformation of the actual Galway landscape into a symbolic landscape — real and ideal at once — is the signature of Yeats's mature art.
