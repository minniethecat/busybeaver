# From _The Green Helmet and Other Poems_

## His Dream

[043-001]I swayed upon the gaudy stern   

[043-002]The butt end of a steering oar,   

[043-003]And everywhere that I could turn   

[043-004]A crowd upon a shore.

[043-005]And though I would have hushed the crowd   

[043-006]There was no mother’s son but said,   

[043-007]“What is the figure in a shroud   

[043-008]Upon a gaudy bed?”

[043-009]And after running at the brim   

[043-010]Cried out upon that thing beneath,   

[043-011]—It had such dignity of limb﻿—   

[043-012]By the sweet name of [[death-in-yeats|Death]].

[043-013]Though I’d my finger on my lip,   

[043-014]What could I but take up the song?   

[043-015]And fish and crowd and gaudy ship   

[043-016]Cried out the whole night long,

[043-017]Crying amid the glittering [[water-imagery|sea]],   

[043-018]Naming it with ecstatic breath,   

[043-019]Because it had such dignity   

[043-020]By the sweet name of Death.

## A Woman Homer Sung

[043-021]If any man drew near   

[043-022]When I was young,   

[043-023]I thought, “He holds her dear,”   

[043-024]And shook with hate and fear.   

[043-025]But oh, ’twas bitter wrong   

[043-026]If he could pass her by   

[043-027]With an indifferent eye.

[043-028]Whereon I wrote and wrought,   

[043-029]And now, being grey,   

[043-030]I dream that I have brought   

[043-031]To such a pitch my thought   

[043-032]That coming time can say,   

[043-033]“He shadowed in a glass   

[043-034]What thing her body was.”

[043-035]For she had fiery blood   

[043-036]When I was young,   

[043-037]And trod so sweetly proud   

[043-038]As ’twere upon a cloud,   

[043-039]A woman Homer sung,   

[043-040]That life and letters seem   

[043-041]But an heroic dream.

## The Consolation

[043-042]I had this thought awhile ago,   

[043-043]“My darling cannot understand   

[043-044]What I have done, or what would do   

[043-045]In this blind bitter land.”

[043-046]And I grew weary of the sun   

[043-047]Until my thoughts cleared up again,   

[043-048]Remembering that the best I have done   

[043-049]Was done to make it plain;

[043-050]That every year I have cried, “At length   

[043-051]My darling understands it all,   

[043-052]Because I have come into my strength,   

[043-053]And words obey my call”;

[043-054]That had she done so who can say   

[043-055]What would have shaken from the sieve?   

[043-056]I might have thrown poor words away   

[043-057]And been content to live.

## No Second Troy

[043-058]Why should I blame her that she filled my days   

[043-059]With misery, or that she would of late   

[043-060]Have taught to ignorant men most violent ways,   

[043-061]Or hurled the little streets upon the great,   

[043-062]Had they but courage equal to [[love-in-yeats|desire]]?   

[043-063]What could have made her peaceful with a mind   

[043-064]That nobleness made simple as a fire,   

[043-065]With beauty like a tightened bow, a kind   

[043-066]That is not natural in an age like this,   

[043-067]Being high and solitary and most stern?   

[043-068]Why, what could she have done being what she is?   

[043-069]Was there another Troy for her to burn?

## Reconciliation

[043-070]Some may have blamed you that you took away   

[043-071]The verses that could move them on the day   

[043-072]When, the ears being deafened, the sight of the eyes blind   

[043-073]With lightning you went from me, and I could find   

[043-074]Nothing to make a song about but kings,   

[043-075]Helmets, and swords, and half-forgotten things   

[043-076]That were like memories of you﻿—but now   

[043-077]We’ll out, for the world lives as long ago;   

[043-078]And while we’re in our laughing, weeping fit,   

[043-079]Hurl helmets, crowns, and swords into the pit.   

[043-080]But, dear, cling close to me; since you were gone,   

[043-081]My barren thoughts have chilled me to the bone.

## King and No King

[043-082]“Would it were anything but merely voice!”   

[043-083]The No King cried who after that was King,   

[043-084]Because he had not heard of anything   

[043-085]That balanced with a word is more than noise;   

[043-086]Yet Old Romance being kind, let him prevail   

[043-087]Somewhere or somehow that I have forgot,   

[043-088]Though he’d but cannon﻿—Whereas we that had thought   

[043-089]To have lit upon as clean and sweet a tale   

[043-090]Have been defeated by that pledge you gave   

[043-091]In momentary anger long ago;   

[043-092]And I that have not your faith, how shall I know   

[043-093]That in the blinding [[shadow-and-light|light]] beyond the grave   

[043-094]We’ll find so good a thing as that we have lost?   

[043-095]The hourly kindness, the day’s common speech,   

[043-096]The habitual content of each with each   

[043-097]When neither soul nor body has been crossed.

## Peace

[043-098]Ah, that Time could touch a form   

[043-099]That could show what Homer’s age   

[043-100]Bred to be a hero’s wage.   

[043-101]“Were not all her life but storm,   

[043-102]Would not painters paint a form   

[043-103]Of such noble lines,” I said,   

[043-104]“Such a delicate high head,   

[043-105]All that sternness amid charm,   

[043-106]All that sweetness amid strength?”   

[043-107]Ah, but peace that comes at length,   

[043-108]Came when Time had touched her form.

## Against Unworthy Praise

[043-109]O heart, be at peace, because   

[043-110]Nor knave nor dolt can break   

[043-111]What’s not for their applause,   

[043-112]Being for a woman’s sake.   

[043-113]Enough if the work has seemed,   

[043-114]So did she your strength renew,   

[043-115]A dream that a lion had dreamed   

[043-116]Till the wilderness cried aloud,   

[043-117]A secret between you two,   

[043-118]Between the proud and the proud.

[043-119]What, still you would have their praise!   

[043-120]But here’s a haughtier text,   

[043-121]The labyrinth of her days   

[043-122]That her own strangeness perplexed;   

[043-123]And how what her [[dream-and-vision|dreaming]] gave   

[043-124]Earned slander, ingratitude,   

[043-125]From self-same dolt and knave;   

[043-126]Aye, and worse wrong than these.   

[043-127]Yet she, [[song-and-poetry|singing]] upon her road,   

[043-128]Half lion, half child, is at peace.

## The Fascination of What’s Difficult

[043-129]The fascination of what’s difficult   

[043-130]Has dried the sap out of my veins, and rent   

[043-131]Spontaneous joy and natural content   

[043-132]Out of my heart. There’s something ails our colt   

[043-133]That must, as if it had not holy blood,   

[043-134]Nor on an Olympus leaped from cloud to cloud,   

[043-135]Shiver under the lash, strain, sweat and jolt   

[043-136]As though it dragged road metal. My curse on plays   

[043-137]That have to be set up in fifty ways,   

[043-138]On the day’s war with every knave and dolt,   

[043-139]Theatre business, management of men.   

[043-140]I swear before the dawn comes round again   

[043-141]I’ll find the stable and pull out the bolt.

## A Drinking Song

[043-142]Wine comes in at the mouth   

[043-143]And love comes in at the eye;   

[043-144]That’s all we shall know for truth   

[043-145]Before we grow old and die.   

[043-146]I lift the glass to my mouth,   

[043-147]I look at you, and I sigh.

## The Coming of Wisdom with Time

[043-148]Though leaves are many, the root is one;   

[043-149]Through all the lying days of my youth   

[043-150]I swayed my leaves and flowers in the sun;   

[043-151]Now I may wither into the truth.

## On Hearing That the Students of Our New University Have Joined the Agitation Against Immoral Literature

[043-152]Where, where but here have Pride and Truth,   

[043-153]That long to give themselves for wage,   

[043-154]To shake their wicked sides at youth   

[043-155]Restraining reckless middle-age.

## To a Poet, Who Would Have Me Praise Certain Bad Poets, Imitators of His and Mine

[043-156]You say, as I have often given tongue   

[043-157]In praise of what another’s said or sung,   

[043-158]’Twere politic to do the like by these;   

[043-159]But was there ever dog that praised his fleas?

## The Mask

[043-160]“Put off that mask of [[fire-passion|burning]] gold   

[043-161]With emerald eyes.”   

[043-162]“O no, my dear, you make so bold   

[043-163]To find if hearts be wild and wise,   

[043-164]And yet not cold.”

[043-165]“I would but find what’s there to find,   

[043-166]Love or deceit.”   

[043-167]“It was the mask engaged your mind,   

[043-168]And after set your heart to beat,   

[043-169]Not what’s behind.”

[043-170]“But lest you are my enemy,   

[043-171]I must enquire.”   

[043-172]“O no, my dear, let all that be,   

[043-173]What matter, so there is but fire   

[043-174]In you, in me?”

## Upon a House Shaken by the Land Agitation

[043-175]How should the world be luckier if this house,   

[043-176]Where passion and precision have been one   

[043-177]Time out of mind, became too ruinous   

[043-178]To breed the lidless eye that loves the sun?   

[043-179]And the sweet laughing eagle thoughts that grow   

[043-180]Where wings have memory of wings, and all   

[043-181]That comes of the best knit to the best? Although   

[043-182]Mean roof-trees were the sturdier for its fall,   

[043-183]How should their luck run high enough to reach   

[043-184]The gifts that govern men, and after these   

[043-185]To gradual Time’s last gift, a written speech   

[043-186]Wrought of high laughter, loveliness and ease?

## At the Abbey Theatre

[043-187]Imitated from Ronsard

[043-188]Dear Craoibhin Aoibhin, look into our case.   

[043-189]When we are high and airy hundreds say   

[043-190]That if we hold that flight they’ll leave the place,   

[043-191]While those same hundreds mock another day   

[043-192]Because we have made our art of common things,   

[043-193]So bitterly, you’d dream they longed to look   

[043-194]All their lives through into some drift of wings.   

[043-195]You’ve dandled them and fed them from the book   

[043-196]And know them to the bone; impart to us﻿—   

[043-197]We’ll keep the secret﻿—a new trick to please.   

[043-198]Is there a bridle for this Proteus   

[043-199]That turns and changes like his draughty seas?   

[043-200]Or is there none, most popular of men,   

[043-201]But when they mock us that we mock again?

## These Are the Clouds

[043-202]These are the clouds about the fallen sun,   

[043-203]The majesty that shuts his burning eye:   

[043-204]The weak lay hand on what the strong has done,   

[043-205]Till that be tumbled that was lifted high   

[043-206]And discord follow upon unison,   

[043-207]And all things at one common level lie.   

[043-208]And therefore, friend, if your great race were run   

[043-209]And these things came, so much the more thereby   

[043-210]Have you made greatness your companion,   

[043-211]Although it be for children that you sigh:   

[043-212]These are the clouds about the fallen sun,   

[043-213]The majesty that shuts his burning eye.

## At Galway Races

[043-214]There where the course is,   

[043-215]Delight makes all of the one mind,   

[043-216]The riders upon the galloping horses,   

[043-217]The crowd that closes in behind:   

[043-218]We, too, had good attendance once,   

[043-219]Hearers and hearteners of the work;   

[043-220]Aye, horsemen for companions,   

[043-221]Before the merchant and the clerk   

[043-222]Breathed on the world with timid breath.   

[043-223]Sing on: sometime, and at some new moon,   

[043-224]We’ll learn that sleeping is not death,   

[043-225]Hearing the whole earth change its tune,   

[043-226]Its flesh being wild, and it again   

[043-227]Crying aloud as the race course is,   

[043-228]And we find hearteners among men   

[043-229]That ride upon horses.

## A Friend’s Illness

[043-230]Sickness brought me this   

[043-231]Thought, in that scale of his:   

[043-232]Why should I be dismayed   

[043-233]Though flame had burned the whole   

[043-234]World, as it were a coal,   

[043-235]Now I have seen it weighed   

[043-236]Against a soul?

## All Things Can Tempt Me

[043-237]All things can tempt me from this craft of verse:   

[043-238]One time it was a woman’s face, or worse﻿—   

[043-239]The seeming needs of my fool-driven land;   

[043-240]Now nothing but comes readier to the hand   

[043-241]Than this accustomed toil. When I was young,   

[043-242]I had not given a penny for a song   

[043-243]Did not the poet sing it with such airs   

[043-244]That one believed he had a sword upstairs;   

[043-245]Yet would be now, could I but have my wish,   

[043-246]Colder and dumber and deafer than a fish.

## The Young Man’s Song

[043-247]I whispered, “I am too young.”   

[043-248]And then, “I am old enough”;   

[043-249]Wherefore I threw a penny   

[043-250]To find out if I might love.   

[043-251]“Go and love, go and love, young man,   

[043-252]If the lady be young and fair.”   

[043-253]Ah, penny, brown penny, brown penny,   

[043-254]I am looped in the loops of her hair.

[043-255]Oh, love is the crooked thing,   

[043-256]There is nobody wise enough   

[043-257]To find out all that is in it,   

[043-258]For he would be thinking of love   

[043-259]Till the stars had run away,   

[043-260]And the shadows eaten the moon.   

[043-261]Ah, penny, brown penny, brown penny,   

[043-262]One cannot begin it too soon.
