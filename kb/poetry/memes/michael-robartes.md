# Michael Robartes

A fictional mystic and philosopher invented by W. B. Yeats, who appears as a recurring persona throughout the poet's middle and later work. Robartes serves as a "mask" or anti-self — an imagined figure through whom Yeats could articulate the esoteric philosophical system underpinning poems such as "The Double Vision of Michael Robartes," "The Phases of the Moon," and the prose work *A Vision* (1925). Together with his companion John Aherne, Robartes belongs to the "phantasmagoria" through which Yeats expressed his "convictions about the world" [045-001].

## Introduction

Michael Robartes first appears in Yeats's stories of the 1890s and re-emerges as a central figure in the poems collected in *The Wild Swans at Coole* (1919) and *Michael Robartes and the Dancer* (1921). He is presented as a scholar of the occult who has come into possession of the *Speculum Angelorum et Hominum* ("Mirror of Angels and Men") attributed to Geraldus, and who has decoded its diagrams with the help of "the traditional knowledge of a certain obscure Arab tribe" [046-002]. Through Robartes, Yeats gives dramatic form to the cyclical philosophy of history and personality that he elaborated most fully in *A Vision* — the theory of the twenty-eight phases of the moon, the gyres, and the alternating dominance of opposing human types.

Yeats described Robartes and Aherne as "characters in some stories I wrote years ago, who have once again become a part of the phantasmagoria through which I can alone express my convictions about the world" [045-001]. This admission reveals Robartes's dual nature: he is at once a literary device for distancing Yeats from the more arcane elements of his thought, and a genuine expression of the poet's deepest convictions.

## Key Facts

- **Born**: Fictional — late nineteenth century (in the world of Yeats's stories)
- **Died**: Fictional — no death recorded
- **Role**: Mystic philosopher, student of the *Speculum Angelorum et Hominum*, Yeatsian mask/anti-self
- **Connection to Yeats**: The fictional expositor of the philosophical system behind *A Vision*; a dramatic persona used to articulate Yeats's esoteric thought in poems and prose

## Significance in Yeats's Work

Michael Robartes occupies a unique position in Yeats's imaginative universe as the bridge between poetry and occult philosophy. While Yeats's earlier work drew on Irish mythology and the Celtic Twilight, the Robartes poems represent his mature phase, in which personal symbolism and systematic thought became inseparable from the lyric voice.

### The Double Vision

In "The Double Vision of Michael Robartes," the poem's namesake experiences a visionary encounter on the battlements of a tower — a setting that merges the actual Thoor Ballylee with the symbolic architecture of Yeats's inner world. The vision presents three figures: a girl dancing, a Sphinx, and a Buddha, representing the reconciliation of opposed principles. Robartes himself fades from the scene, leaving only the vision:

> "That Michael Robartes left, you walk in the moon / And though you have passed the best of life still trace / Enthralled by the unconquerable delusion" [045-882]

### The Phases of the Moon

Robartes appears as the speaker in "The Phases of the Moon," a dialogue poem set at Thoor Ballylee in which he expounds the twenty-eight lunar phases governing human personality — the core of the Yeatsian philosophical system. Through Robartes's voice, Yeats could present his most abstract ideas with the authority of dramatic character rather than lyric confession [045-990].

> "All thought becomes an image and the soul / Becomes a body" [045-1041]

### The Speculum Framework

The elaborate framing device — Robartes's discovery of Geraldus's manuscript, supplemented by the wisdom of the Judwalis Arab tribe [048-053] — allowed Yeats to present *A Vision*'s elaborate cosmology not as personal revelation but as edited scholarship. This distancing strategy was essential to Yeats's method: "I have given no account of Robartes himself, nor of his discovery of the explanation of Geraldus' diagrams," he wrote, promising a future volume of Robartes's "letters and table talk" edited by John Aherne [046-002].

## Further Context

### Robartes and the Mask

In Yeats's theory of the mask (*persona*), every artist must cultivate an anti-self — an imagined opposite through which creative work becomes possible. Michael Robartes functions as one of Yeats's most fully realised masks. Where the historical Yeats was an Irish Protestant poet engaged in public life (a Senator of the Irish Free State), Robartes is a reclusive scholar of the occult, utterly absorbed in arcane diagrams and Arab mystical traditions. This opposition was productive: through Robartes, Yeats could speak with conviction about matters — cyclical history, lunar determinism, the geometry of the soul — that would have seemed merely eccentric in his own lyric voice.

### The Aherne-Robartes Dyad

Robartes is inseparable from his companion John Aherne, who serves as his foil and editor. Where Robartes is the visionary, Aherne is the sceptical recorder — a relationship that mirrors the dialectical structure of Yeats's own thought. The two figures together embody the Yeatsian conviction that truth emerges from the conflict of opposites, not from any single perspective.
