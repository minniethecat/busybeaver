# John Millington Synge

John Millington Synge (1871–1909) was the greatest playwright of the Irish Literary Revival and, alongside [[lady-gregory]], W. B. Yeats's most important collaborator at the [[abbey-theatre]]. Yeats met Synge in Paris in 1896 and famously advised him to abandon his life among the literary coteries of the continent and "go to the Aran Islands" — to find his subject in the speech and lives of the Irish western peasantry. The result was a body of dramatic work (*The Playboy of the Western World*, *Riders to the Sea*, *The Well of the Saints*) that transformed Irish theatre.

## The Paris Meeting and the Aran Islands

The encounter in Paris is one of the most consequential moments in Irish literary history. Synge, then twenty-five, was living in the Latin Quarter, studying French literature and writing tepid critical essays. Yeats, visiting the city, recognised in Synge a talent that was miscast in the hothouse atmosphere of continental letters. His advice — "Give up Paris. You will never create anything by reading Racine, and Arthur Symons will always be a better critic of French literature. Go to the Aran Islands. Live there as if you were one of the people themselves; express a life that has never found expression" — redirected Synge's entire artistic trajectory.

Synge took the advice. Over the next five years (1898–1902), he spent his summers on the Aran Islands off the Galway coast, living among the Irish-speaking fishing communities, listening to their stories, absorbing the cadences of their Hiberno-English speech. The experience yielded not only the book *The Aran Islands* (1907) but also the distinctive linguistic texture — at once poetic and colloquial, archaic and immediate — that distinguishes his plays from every other dramatist of the period. The old Aran fisherman's speech, with its biblical rhythms and concrete imagery, became Synge's dramatic instrument [048-032].

## In Yeats's Verse

Synge appears directly in Yeats's late poem "The Municipal Gallery Revisited," where the poet, walking through the gallery of portraits in Dublin, encounters his dead friends one by one. Synge's entry is characteristically understated: "And that enquiring man John Synge comes next" [045-059]. The adjective is exact — Synge's genius was for looking, for listening, for the patient observation of the world that his early training in music and his solitary walks on Aran had cultivated.

## The Playboy Riots and Their Significance

The riots that greeted *The Playboy of the Western World* at its Abbey Theatre premiere in January 1907 were a turning point in Irish cultural history. Nationalist audiences were outraged by Synge's portrayal of Irish rural life — the mention of women in "shifts" (chemises), the suggestion that a community might celebrate a man who believed he had killed his father. The dispute revealed a deep fault line within the Revival itself: between the nationalist demand for an idealized, heroic image of Ireland and the artist's commitment to truth as he saw it. Yeats, who was in Scotland when the riots broke out, returned immediately and famously addressed the audience from the Abbey stage, defending Synge's play against "those that hated it" [050-177].

Yeats later wrote in his essays that the *Playboy* riots confirmed the Abbey Theatre's essential purpose: to disturb, to provoke, to refuse the easy consolations of sentimental nationalism [048-046]. The controversy cemented Synge's position, in Yeats's mind, as the true artist — the one who sees clearly and reports honestly, whatever the cost to popularity.

## Significance

Synge's importance to Yeats was both personal and institutional. Personally, Synge was the friend whose early death in 1909 — from Hodgkin's disease at the age of thirty-seven — was one of the great losses of Yeats's middle age. Yeats described the death as the end of an era: with Synge gone, the Abbey Theatre lost its most original dramatic voice, and the Revival lost the writer who had most fully realised its ambition to create a national literature that was artistically modern rather than merely patriotic.

Institutionally, Synge's plays made the Abbey Theatre a site of genuine artistic daring. *Riders to the Sea* (1904) demonstrated that the one-act tragedy, stripped to its essentials, could achieve the power of classical Greek drama. *The Playboy* (1907) proved that Irish English — the Hiberno-English speech that Synge had absorbed on Aran and in Wicklow — was a magnificent dramatic instrument, capable of lyricism, comedy, and savage irony in a single sentence. *Deirdre of the Sorrows*, unfinished at his death and produced posthumously in 1910, brought Synge's gift for tragedy to the mythological material of the Ulster Cycle.

Yeats wrote of Synge in his *Autobiographies*: "He was the only man I have ever known whom I could not dominate." The remark is a complex tribute — acknowledging Synge's artistic independence while hinting at the tensions that existed between Synge's earthy realism and Yeats's symbolist ambitions. Synge, for his part, admired Yeats's poetry but was sceptical of the mystical system-building that would eventually produce *A Vision*. Their friendship, though genuine, was built on mutual respect for artistic difference rather than doctrinal agreement.
