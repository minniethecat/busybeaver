# Lapis Lazuli

"Lapis Lazuli" is a poem from Yeats's final collection, *Last Poems* (1939). It was written in July 1938, after the poet received as a gift a piece of lapis lazuli carved by a Chinese craftsman — a stone with a mountainous landscape, a temple, and tiny figures ascending. The gift inspired one of Yeats's greatest meditations on the relationship between art and tragedy, and on the "gaiety" that the tragic artist achieves in the face of destruction.

## The Poem

The poem opens with a sharp satire of "hysterical women" who complain that artists should be making political art in a time of crisis (the poem was written on the eve of the Second World War). Yeats's answer is that the tragic arts — Shakespeare's tragedies, Hamlet and Lear — do not end in despair but in "gaiety transfiguring all that dread."

The second section moves to the great tragic artists through history — the Greek sculptors who "made a Trojan it," Michelangelo who "left its sinew in [stone]," and the poets who "were finishing their verses."

The final section turns to the carved stone itself: "Two Chinamen, behind them a third, / Are carved in lapis lazuli." The poet imagines the figures climbing a mountain towards "a little half-way house," looking down on "the tragic scene" — and asks that their eyes, like the tragic artist's, are "gay."

## The Carved Stone

The lapis lazuli stone was a gift from Harry Clifton, a young Englishman whom Yeats had befriended. The carving showed a Chinese landscape with figures ascending a mountain path — an image that, for Yeats, crystallised his lifetime of thinking about art as the human response to the tragedy of existence.
