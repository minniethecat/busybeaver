# Druidism and Celtic Mysticism

The figure of the Druid and the concept of "Druid" consciousness run through W. B. Yeats's poetry as emblems of an ancient Irish wisdom that predates and rivals the Christian order. For Yeats, Druidism was not merely antiquarian interest but a living current within the occult revival of the late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries — a tradition of visionary knowledge that could be recovered through poetry, ritual, and imagination.

## Introduction

Yeats's Druids are not historical reconstructions but symbolic figures — "gray, wood-nurtured, quiet-eyed" [031-008] — who embody an order of knowledge opposed to both modern rationalism and Christian orthodoxy. In Yeats's poetic mythology, the Druids stand at the threshold between the mortal and immortal worlds, custodians of a "Druid dream" [015-210] and a "Druid tune" [031-564] that echoes across Irish history. They represent what the poet called, in a letter of 1888, "the ancient religion of the world" — a pagan wisdom that Christianity had suppressed but never extinguished.

The Druid appears most prominently in "Fergus and the Druid" (from *The Rose*, 1893), a dialogue poem in which King Fergus — weary of the burdens of kingship — seeks from a Druid the gift of dream-knowledge. The exchange dramatises the central tension in Yeats's thought between worldly action and contemplative vision, and the Druid emerges as the guardian of a knowledge that is at once liberating and annihilating.

## Fergus and the Druid

"Fergus and the Druid" is the key text for understanding Yeats's Druidic symbolism. The poem opens with Fergus, the legendary king of Ulster who abandoned his throne to become a poet-hunter, addressing a Druid:

> "The Druid, gray, wood-nurtured, quiet-eyed, / ... / What would you, king of the proud Red Branch kings?" [031-008]

Fergus seeks the "dream" that the Druid carries in a "little bag of dreams" [031-059] — a compressed symbol for the entire Druidic tradition of inner vision. But the Druid warns that the gift is irreversible: once Fergus possesses the dream-knowledge, he can never return to the world of action and kingship. The poem's close — with Fergus wandering, dispossessed of even his own identity — suggests that Druidic wisdom is purchased at the cost of the self.

> "Ah! Druid, Druid, how great webs of sorrow / Lay hidden in that small slate-coloured bag!" [031-070]

## Druidism and the Occult Revival

Yeats's engagement with Druidism was inseparable from his membership in occult societies — the Theosophical Society, the Hermetic Order of the Golden Dawn, and later the Dublin Hermetic Society. These organisations shared a common premise: that beneath the world's exoteric religions lay a single ancient wisdom tradition, of which Druidism was the Irish branch.

The "Druid dream" [015-210] and "Druid swoon" [015-276] in *The Wanderings of Usheen* are not merely poetic metaphors but descriptions of specific altered states that Yeats, through his occult training, believed were accessible to disciplined practitioners. The Druid's "little bag of dreams" [031-059] corresponds to the magical instruments of the Golden Dawn tradition — the symbols and talismans through which the initiate accesses higher planes of consciousness.

> "Druid vapour and make the torches dim" [038-404]

In this line from *The Wind Among the Reeds*, the "Druid vapour" is both literal (the mist of the Irish landscape) and symbolic (the occult medium through which vision becomes possible). The torches dimming suggest the fading of ordinary perception as Druidic sight — what Yeats elsewhere calls "the vision of evil" or "second sight" — takes over.

## Celtic Mysticism and the Literary Revival

Druidism was one strand in a broader Celtic mystical revival that shaped the Irish Literary Renaissance. Alongside Yeats, figures such as George Russell (AE), Standish O'Grady, and Douglas Hyde mined the medieval Irish manuscripts — the *Lebor Gabála Érenn*, the Ulster Cycle, the Fenian Cycle — for evidence of a sophisticated pre-Christian Irish civilisation with its own philosophy, cosmology, and spiritual discipline.

Yeats's contribution was distinctive: rather than merely translating or retelling the old stories, he absorbed their symbolic language and redeployed it in a modern lyric idiom. The Druid in his poems is both a figure from the Irish past and a mask for the modern poet — one who, like Fergus, has traded the world of action for the "Druid dream."
