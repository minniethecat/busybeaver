## Introduction

"The Song of Wandering Aengus" is one of W. B. Yeats's most beloved early poems, first collected in _[[the-wind-among-the-reeds|The Wind Among the Reeds]]_ (1899). The poem reimagines the Irish myth of [[aengus-god|Aengus]], the god of love, as a mortal speaker who goes out to a hazel wood, catches a silver trout that transforms into a "glimmering girl," and spends the rest of his life wandering in search of her. With its incantatory rhythm and luminous closing image — "The silver apples of the moon, / The golden apples of the sun" — it is among the most frequently anthologized of Yeats's lyrics.

## The Poem

> I went out to the hazel wood,
> Because a fire was in my head,
> And cut and peeled a hazel wand,
> And hooked a berry to a thread;
> And when white moths were on the wing,
> And moth-like stars were flickering out,
> I dropped the berry in a stream
> And caught a little silver trout.
>
> When I had laid it on the floor
> I went to blow the fire a-flame,
> But something rustled on the floor,
> And someone called me by my name:
> It had become a glimmering girl
> With apple blossom in her hair
> Who called me by my name and ran
> And faded through the brightening air.
>
> Though I am old with wandering
> Through hollow lands and hilly lands,
> I will find out where she has gone,
> And kiss her lips and take her hands;
> And walk among long dappled grass,
> And pluck till time and times are done
> The silver apples of the moon,
> The golden apples of the sun.
> [038-120] – [038-143]

## Mythological Sources

The poem draws on the Irish myth of Aengus Óg, the god of love and poetry, who was visited in a dream by a beautiful woman and then wandered Ireland searching for her. Yeats significantly transforms the myth: in the traditional telling, Aengus eventually finds the woman, Caer Ibormeith, in the form of a swan on Loch Bel Dragon, and transforms himself into a swan to be with her. In Yeats's version, the quest is ongoing — the speaker is "old with wandering" and has not yet found his beloved. The poem becomes an allegory of the creative life itself: the "fire in my head" that drives the poet outward, the transformative act of making (fishing as poetry), and the eternal pursuit of an ideal that recedes even as it appears.

## Symbols and Imagery

The **hazel wood** is a deeply resonant image in Irish mythology. In the Irish tradition, the nine hazel trees of wisdom dropped their nuts into the Well of Segais, where they were eaten by the Salmon of Knowledge. By setting the poem in a hazel wood, Yeats associates Aengus's quest with the pursuit of _imbas_ — poetic inspiration, visionary knowledge.

The **silver trout** that transforms into a **glimmering girl** is a metamorphosis that condenses several mythological motifs: the fish as symbol of the unconscious or the otherworld, the dangerous beauty of the Sidhe, and the transformative power of vision. The girl "with apple blossom in her hair" evokes both the [[celtic-mythology|Celtic]] otherworld (the Land of Youth with its eternal apple trees) and classical imagery of beauty and youth.

The closing couplet — "The silver apples of the moon, / The golden apples of the sun" — is among the most famous in Yeats's oeuvre. The silver and golden apples unite lunar and solar symbolism, the feminine and masculine principles, the night-world of dream and the day-world of action. They are, in Yeats's symbolic vocabulary, fruits of the imagination, attainable only through a lifetime of wandering and quest.

## Place in Yeats's Development

"The Song of Wandering Aengus" belongs to Yeats's early, symbolist period, heavily influenced by the [[celtic-twilight|Celtic Twilight]] movement and his work collecting Irish folklore. Yet the poem already displays the characteristic Yeatsian note of longing intermixed with resolution — the speaker is old, has not found what he seeks, and yet declares with absolute certainty "I will find out where she has gone." This fusion of desire and will, of dream and determination, would become a hallmark of his mature style.

The poem also initiates the figure of Aengus as a Yeatsian persona who recurs throughout the poet's career, evolving from the love-god of early work into a symbol of the creative imagination in poems like "The Phases of the Moon."
