# Moon Symbolism

The moon is the governing symbol of Yeats's mature philosophical system. In _A Vision_ (1925, revised 1937), the 28 phases of the moon map the entire range of human personality types and historical epochs, from the purely objective (full moon, Phase 15) to the purely subjective (dark moon, Phase 1). The moon's phases are the Great Wheel of incarnation — each soul passes through all 28 phases across successive lives, seeking its antithetical Mask. What began in the early poetry as Romantic lunar atmosphere becomes, in the late work, a complete cosmology.

## Origins and Sources

- **The automatic writing of Georgie Yeats**: Beginning in October 1917, Yeats's wife produced automatic scripts describing a system of 28 lunar phases — the raw material of _A Vision_
- **Traditional lunar symbolism**: The moon as feminine, cyclical, changeable — the "inconstant moon" of Shakespeare, the moon goddess of pagan tradition
- **Astrology**: Traditional astrological associations of the moon with emotion, the unconscious, the body
- **Plotinus and Neoplatonism**: The soul's descent through the planetary spheres and return — the lunar sphere as the closest to Earth, the realm of generation

## Symbolic Meaning

1. **The 28 phases**: Each phase of the moon represents a personality type — a specific ratio of Will to Mask, Creative Mind to Body of Fate. Phase 15 (the full moon) is pure beauty, supernatural, unattainable in life.

2. **The dark of the moon**: Phase 1 and Phase 15 mark the extremes — pure objectivity (complete absorption in the external world) and pure subjectivity (complete retreat into the inner world). Human life oscillates between these poles.

3. **The gyres**: The moon's phases trace a gyre — one millennium moving toward subjectivity, the next toward objectivity. History itself is lunar, cyclical, governed by the same law as individual souls.

4. **Lunar beauty**: The moon in Yeats's poetry maintains its traditional associations — pale, remote, feminine, beautiful — but these are now charged with systematic meaning. A "moonlit" scene in late Yeats is never merely atmospheric.

## Development Across Yeats's Career

**Early period**: The moon is decorative and Romantic — pale light, dream atmosphere, Celtic twilight. It creates mood, not system.

**Middle period (1917–1925)**: The automatic writing sessions with Georgie transform the moon into a philosophical instrument. The 28-phase system is elaborated, and poems like "The Phases of the Moon" dramatize the abstract scheme.

**Late period**: The lunar system is fully integrated into the poetry. "Under Ben Bulben" (1938) includes the phases of the moon in Yeats's epitaphic summary of his beliefs. The moon is no longer merely a symbol — it is the structure of reality itself.
