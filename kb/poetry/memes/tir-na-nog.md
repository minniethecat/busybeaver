## Introduction

Tír na nÓg ("Land of Youth") is the paradisal Otherworld of Irish mythology — a realm beyond the western sea where time stands still, age and death do not exist, and the immortal Tuatha Dé Danann hold court. It is the destination of Oisin (Usheen), son of Fionn mac Cumhaill, in the central narrative of W. B. Yeats's long early poem "[[the-wanderings-of-usheen|The Wanderings of Usheen]]" (1889).

In Yeats's source material — eighteenth- and nineteenth-century Gaelic poems and folk traditions collected by [[lady-gregory|Lady Gregory]] and others — Tír na nÓg is reached by crossing the sea on a magical horse. The fairy woman Niamh, daughter of the king of Tír na nÓg, falls in love with Oisin and carries him there, where he dwells for three hundred years before returning to find Ireland Christianised and diminished.

## Tír na nÓg in "The Wanderings of Usheen"

"The Wanderings of Usheen" (1889), the longest poem in Yeats's early output, is a dramatic dialogue in three parts between the aged Oisin — now returned to Ireland, blind and dependent — and St. Patrick. The poem is Yeats's most sustained engagement with Tír na nÓg and its mythology.

### The Journey

The poem opens with Oisin reminiscing about Niamh's arrival. She describes her homeland in terms of eternal youth and beauty:

> Niam, and my country far [015-050]

Niamh leads Oisin across the western sea on horseback, fleeing the mortal world. Their journey takes them through three islands — the Island of Dancing, the Island of Victories, and the Island of Forgetfulness — before reaching the immortal land itself:

> The immortal desire of immortals we saw in their faces,<br/>
> And sighed in our hearts as we gazed on the vanishing faces. [015-654]

### The Immortals

In Yeats's telling, the immortals of Tír na nÓg are not simply happy; they are remote, emotionally diminished by the absence of mortality. Oisin observes:

> The immortals moved among the fountains [015-370]

and later reflects on the cost of eternal life — a theme that would become central to Yeats's mature thought, where the tension between the mortal world of change and the immortal world of stasis is fundamental to the human condition.

## Tír na nÓg in Irish Mythology

In the broader Irish mythological tradition, Tír na nÓg is one of several Otherworld realms, alongside Mag Mell ("Plain of Delight"), Tír Tairngire ("Land of Promise"), and Emain Ablach ("Isle of Apples"). It is ruled by Manannán mac Lir (god of the sea) and is home to the Tuatha Dé Danann after their withdrawal from the surface world.

The encounter between mortal and immortal — Oisin and Niamh — is the archetypal Irish myth of the hero's journey to the Otherworld, a pattern found across Indo-European mythology (compare Odysseus and Calypso, Gilgamesh and the Garden of the Gods).

### The Celtic Otherworlds Compared

| Realm | Meaning | Ruler | Distinctive Feature |
|-------|---------|-------|---------------------|
| **Tír na nÓg** | Land of Youth | Manannán mac Lir | Eternal youth; beyond the western sea |
| **Mag Mell** | Plain of Delight | Manannán mac Lir | Blissful plain without sickness or death |
| **Tír Tairngire** | Land of Promise | Manannán mac Lir | The "promised land" of Celtic myth |
| **Emain Ablach** | Isle of Apples | Manannán mac Lir | Apple-orchard island; Arthurian Avalon parallel |
| **Tech Duinn** | House of Donn | Donn | The house of the dead; a gathering-place of souls before their journey onward |

These realms are not always sharply distinguished in the sources; Yeats himself tends to use "Faeryland" or "the Country of the Young" as umbrella terms, collapsing the separate Otherworlds into a single undying realm accessible by sea. This conflation is consistent with the practice of the nineteenth-century folklorists from whom Yeats drew his material.

## Yeats's Use of the Myth

For Yeats, Tír na nÓg was not merely a decorative subject from folklore. In the endnotes to his collected poems, he describes the source material:

> ...the last century Gaelic poem about Oisin's journey to the country of the young. [048-026]

and notes that:

> ...a beautiful faery who carried Oisin into Faeryland. [048-002]

The myth of the Land of Youth feeds into several of Yeats's enduring preoccupations: the relationship between Ireland and its pre-Christian past; the tension between mortal passion and immortal stasis; and the figure of the poet-prophet who travels between worlds. The image of a western paradise — reachable by sea — also resonates with Yeats's later poems set in the western Irish landscape of [[connemara|Connemara]] and [[sligo-county|County Sligo]].

The return of Oisin from Tír na nÓg — his discovery that three hundred years have passed, that the Fianna are dead, and that St. Patrick's Christianity has supplanted the old ways — is also Yeats's earliest treatment of the theme of the poet out of time, a figure who would recur in the persona of the Wild Old Wicked Man and in the late self-elegies. The aged Oisin, blind and dependent, recalling the glories of the immortal land while St. Patrick urges him to repent, is a prototype of the Yeatsian speaker caught between worlds.
