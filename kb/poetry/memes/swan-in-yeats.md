## Introduction

The swan is one of the most richly developed animal symbols in W. B. Yeats' poetry. It appears in three distinct but interconnected registers: as a figure of mythological violence and annunciation in "Leda and the Swan," as an emblem of timeless beauty and elegiac change in "The Wild Swans at Coole," and as a Neoplatonic image of the soul's solitary flight in the later philosophical poems. Across these incarnations, the swan embodies the tension between immortal beauty and mortal temporality that lies at the heart of Yeats' symbolic imagination.

## The Mythological Swan: Leda

Yeats' sonnet "Leda and the Swan" (1923) recasts the Greek myth of Zeus's rape of Leda in the form of a swan as a moment of world-historical rupture. The poem's violent opening — "A sudden blow: the great wings beating still" — transforms the swan from a creature of pastoral grace into an agent of overwhelming divine power. Yeats understood the myth as an annunciation parallel to the Christian one: just as the dove's descent began one historical cycle, the swan's violent union with Leda — producing Helen and, ultimately, the Trojan War — inaugurated the classical era. The swan here is not beautiful but terrifying, "the brute blood of the air," a symbol of the divine incursion that shatters human order. In the later poem "Among School Children," Yeats returns to the mythic swan when he writes that "even daughters of the swan can share / Something of every paddler's heritage" [047-687], acknowledging that mythic descent permeates even ordinary life.

## The Wild Swans at Coole

In "The Wild Swans at Coole" (1917), the swan takes on a wholly different character. Here the "nine and fifty swans" [045-010] on the lake at Lady Gregory's estate are figures of unwearied, timeless beauty. The poet, aging and aware of his own mortality, watches the swans "drift on the still water, / Mysterious, beautiful" and contrasts their apparent changelessness with his own sense of loss: "The nineteenth autumn has come upon me / Since I first made my count." In the earlier "Baile and Aillinn," swans already carry this association with immortal beauty — "Two swans came flying up to him" [040-136] in a moment of visionary encounter.

## The Solitary Swan: Soul Symbol

In the later philosophical poems, the swan becomes an image of the soul itself. In the poem "Nineteen Hundred and Nineteen," Yeats writes of the soul that "the swan must fix his eye / Upon a fading gleam" [047-172], using the swan's focused, solitary flight as a figure for the soul's concentration on the eternal. The most explicit use of this Neoplatonic swan-symbol appears when Yeats "compares the solitary soul to a swan" [047-495], and again when "The swan has leaped into the desolate heaven" [047-514] — an image of the soul's departure from the body into the emptiness of the supernatural. This philosophical swan unites the mythological violence of Leda's swan with the elegiac beauty of the Coole swans, yoking terror and transcendence into a single complex symbol.
