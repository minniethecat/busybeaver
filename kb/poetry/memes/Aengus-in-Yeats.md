# Aengus in Yeats

Aengus Óg (Aengus the Young), son of the Dagda and Boann, is the Irish god of love, poetry, and dreams. In W. B. Yeats's poetry, Aengus appears as more than a mythological ornament: he is the "Master of Love" [040-001] who presides over the union of mortal lovers, the dreamer whose "birds" [015-054] symbolise the flight of the imagination, and the inhabitant of a visionary "tower of glass" [042-047] that images the poet's own crystalline art. Across Yeats's career, Aengus provides a mythological figure for the dream-state that Yeats considered essential to poetic creation.

## Aengus in Irish Myth

In the Irish mythological tradition, Aengus is a member of the Tuatha Dé Danann. He is associated with love, youth, and poetic inspiration. His dwelling is Brugh na Bóinne (Newgrange). Four birds — symbols of his kisses — perpetually hover above his head. Yeats knew this material through Standish O'Grady's reconstructions and through the oral folklore of County Sligo, where tales of Aengus and the Tuatha Dé Danann were still current in his childhood.

## "The Song of Wandering Aengus"

Yeats's most celebrated treatment of Aengus is "The Song of Wandering Aengus" (1899), collected in *The Wind Among the Reeds*. The poem recasts the Irish god as a mortal fisherman-seeker who catches "a little silver trout" that transforms into "a glimmering girl" who calls his name and vanishes. The speaker's life becomes a wandering quest "through hollow lands and hilly lands" to find the vanished vision. The poem ends with the promise of eternal union: "The silver apples of the moon, / The golden apples of the sun."

The poem draws on Aengus's mythological role as dreamer and lover: "Where Aengus dreams, from sun to sun, / A loveliness no man has told" [015-209]. Aengus's dreaming — a state of visionary receptivity rather than active pursuit — becomes the model for the poet's own creative stance.

## Aengus in "Baile and Aillinn"

In the long narrative poem "Baile and Aillinn" (1903), Aengus takes a more active role. The poem opens with the declaration: "Baile and Aillinn were lovers, but Aengus, the Master of Love, wishing them to be happy in his own land among the dead, told to each a story of the other's death" [040-001]. Here Aengus is not the passive dreamer but the divine orchestrator of mortal love, engineering the deaths of the lovers so that they may be united in his realm. "While over them birds of Aengus fly" [040-183] — the kiss-birds that are his signature — preside over the lovers' union in death.

## Aengus in "In the Seven Woods"

In the sequence "In the Seven Woods" (1903), Yeats places Aengus within a specifically Irish topography. The poem "In the Seven Woods" mentions "Land-of-the-Tower, where Aengus has thrown the gates apart" [041-143] — a visionary geography that fuses Irish mythological place with the Yeats family estate at Coole Park. The "Land of the Tower" is simultaneously Aengus's mythological domain and Yeats's own Thoor Ballylee, the Norman tower he would later purchase and make the centre of his symbolic system. The image of Aengus throwing "the gates apart" [041-143] is an act of opening, of hospitality, that transforms the tower from a defensive structure into a portal of vision.

This passage is significant for the way it integrates Aengus into Yeats's mature symbolic geography. The tower — which in poems like "The Tower" and "Meditations in Time of Civil War" becomes the master symbol of the poet's achieved self — is here traced back to its mythological origin. Aengus in his "tower of glass" [042-047] and the "Land-of-the-Tower" [041-143] where he throws open the gates are both precursors of the Ballylee tower, linking Yeats's personal architecture to the mythological housing of the Irish gods.

## Aengus in the Later Poetry

In "The Old Age of Queen Maeve" (1903), Aengus appears as a god "crossed in love" [039-076] — a figure who shares in mortal suffering rather than standing above it. Maeve, the formidable queen of Connacht, and Aengus meet in a dream encounter that reveals the god's own vulnerability: "For I am Aengus, and I am crossed in love" [039-076]. The encounter is witnessed by "Aengus of the birds" [039-093], emphasising the continuity of the bird-symbolism across Yeats's career. "Aengus and his sweetheart give their love" [039-138] becomes, in Maeve's recounting, a paradigm of divine-human love that mirrors mortal passion. A later reference places Aengus in his "tower of glass" [042-047] — a visionary architecture that prefigures Yeats's own symbolic tower at Ballylee and links the Irish god to the poet's personal mythology.

## The Bird Symbolism

The "birds of Aengus" [015-054] are among Yeats's most persistent mythological symbols. In Irish tradition, four birds perpetually circled above Aengus's head, representing his kisses. Yeats transforms this into a symbol of poetic flight — the birds of Aengus are the liberated imagination, the dream-images that the poet pursues. In "The Wanderings of Usheen," the birds appear in the visionary landscape: "From where the birds of Aengus wing" [015-054] marks the threshold between mortal and immortal realms. The birds are mentioned in the Endnotes as a possible connection between Irish myth and Yeats's own symbolic system: "what was a hazel wand may have been Aengus, Master of Love" [048-027].

## Aengus and the Feminine Principle

Aengus's relatively rare appearance in the poetry — "Aengus and Adene" [015-049] in "The Wanderings of Usheen," the scattered references across "The Old Age of Queen Maeve" and "Baile and Aillinn" — belies his structural importance. He represents, in Yeats's symbolic vocabulary, a principle that the later system would call the antithetical: the domain of love, dream, art, and subjective vision, opposed to the primary world of politics and action. His "sweetheart" [039-138] who gives her love alongside him, and his status as one "crossed in love" [039-076], align him with the suffering lover rather than the triumphant warrior — a figure closer to the poet's self-conception than the heroic warriors of the Ulster Cycle.

## Aengus and the Dream-Vision

Aengus's mythological identity as a dreamer — "Where Aengus dreams, from sun to sun, / A loveliness no man has told" [015-209] — aligns him with one of Yeats's most persistent convictions: that the dream-state is not an escape from reality but an access to a deeper reality. Throughout his career, Yeats insisted that the poetic imagination operates most powerfully in the threshold state between sleeping and waking, where images from the Great Memory — the Anima Mundi — present themselves to the receptive mind.

Aengus, as the god whose dreams produce "a loveliness no man has told," is the mythological guarantor of this poetic practice. His dreaming is not passive but generative: it creates the images that the waking poet must then pursue through "hollow lands and hilly lands." The structure of "The Song of Wandering Aengus" — a dream-vision, a vanishing, a lifelong quest — is the structure of Yeatsian inspiration itself. The "glimmering girl" who appears and vanishes is the poetic image in its moment of emergence from the unconscious, and the "wandering" that follows is the work of art-making.

This dream-aesthetics connects Aengus to the broader Yeatsian mythology of the Sidhe, the Tuatha Dé Danann, and the Otherworld — all of which are, in Yeats's understanding, names for the same reality that the dreamer accesses: a realm contiguous with the waking world but ordinarily invisible to it. Aengus, as both inhabitant of that realm (in myth) and the dreamer who bridges it (in Yeats's poetic adaptation), is a figure for the poet's own double citizenship in the worlds of matter and spirit.
