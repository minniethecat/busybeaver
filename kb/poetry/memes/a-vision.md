# A Vision

*A Vision* is the strangest, most ambitious, and most personal of W. B. Yeats's prose works — a book that attempts nothing less than a complete symbolic system of human existence. Derived from the automatic writing sessions that Yeats conducted with his wife George Hyde-Lees between 1917 and 1920, *A Vision* presents a cosmology of interpenetrating [[gyres|gyres]], twenty-eight phases of the moon corresponding to twenty-eight types of personality, and a theory of history in which civilisations succeed one another in 2,000-year cycles. First published in a private edition in 1925 and substantially revised in 1937, *A Vision* is the hidden architecture beneath the great poems of Yeats's maturity — the skeleton of thought to which the poetry gives flesh.

## Overview

*A Vision* is not a work of philosophy in the conventional sense. It is a work of myth-making — a deliberate attempt to create, as Yeats put it, "a system of thought that would leave my imagination free to create as it chose." The system is presented not through argument but through a series of nested fictions: Yeats claims to be editing the fragmentary papers of his friend [[michael-robartes]], who in turn received occult knowledge from a mysterious Arabic sect called the Judwalis. The dedicatee of Robartes's letters is [[owen-aherne]], the sceptical auditor who receives "several mathematical diagrams" [048-056] explaining the geometry of the gyres.

This elaborate framing apparatus serves a double purpose. It insulates Yeats from the charge of credulity — the system is presented as discovered rather than invented, and the poet appears as an editor rather than an author — while simultaneously creating an atmosphere of esoteric authenticity. Yeats was not asking his readers to believe in the literal truth of the gyres; he was inviting them to "hold a theory lightly," to "entertain it as a dramatic fiction," and to find in its architecture a structure upon which the imagination could build.

## The Lunar Phases

The twenty-eight phases of the moon, each corresponding to a type of human personality and a stage in the soul's journey, form the psychological core of *A Vision*. The phases range from Phase 1 (the Dark of the Moon — pure objectivity, complete passivity, the state of the saint or the victim) to Phase 15 (the Full Moon — perfect beauty, complete subjectivity, the state of the artist in his moment of creation). The cycle is not merely descriptive but prescriptive: each phase represents a ratio of the Four Faculties (Will, Mask, Creative Mind, Body of Fate), and each soul moves through the phases across multiple incarnations.

Yeats assigned historical figures and literary characters to each phase, creating a kind of typological catalogue of humanity. Phase 12, the phase of the "Forerunner" (Napoleon, Nietzsche), and Phase 17, the phase of the "Daimonic Man" (Dante, Shelley, Landor), were of particular importance to Yeats because he identified himself as a Phase 17 personality and his wife George as Phase 18.

## The Historical Gyres

The historical section of *A Vision* presents a vast panorama of Western civilisation as the intersection of two opposing gyres, each 2,000 years in duration. The primary gyre (objective, democratic, Christian) began with the birth of Christ and is reaching its end in the present era. The antithetical gyre (subjective, aristocratic, pagan) reached its apex in classical Greece and will return in the coming dispensation.

> "cones made up of revolving gyres intersecting each other at various angles" [048-056]

The geometric diagrams that Robartes sends to Aherne illustrate this principle. The gyres are not merely abstract symbols but precise geometrical constructions, and Yeats reproduces them in *A Vision* with detailed captions. The system predicts — or, more accurately, describes in prophetic mode — a "rough beast" slouching toward Bethlehem, the antithetical revelation that will inaugurate the new cycle. This is the conceptual ground of "The Second Coming," Yeats's most famous poem and, not coincidentally, the poem most densely saturated with the language of *A Vision*.

## Themes and Motifs

*A Vision* unites all of Yeats's major themes under a single conceptual roof:

| Theme | Manifestation in A Vision |
|-------|--------------------------|
| **Cyclical History** | The 2,000-year gyres; civilisations rise and fall |
| **The Mask / Anti-Self** | The Will-Mask relation at each lunar phase |
| **Unity of Being** | Phase 15 (Full Moon) — the reconciliation of opposites |
| **Reincarnation** | The soul's journey through the twenty-eight phases |
| **The Daimon** | The spiritual counterpart that guides the soul's choice of incarnation |
| **Fictional Personae** | Robartes and Aherne as vehicles for the system |

## Critical Reception

The first edition of *A Vision* (1925) was reviewed with bewilderment. Few critics knew what to make of a Nobel laureate publishing elaborate occult diagrams. The revised edition (1937) fared somewhat better, partly because the great poems of 1928–1933 (*The Tower*, *The Winding Stair*) had demonstrated that the system was, whatever else it might be, a machine for making great poetry.

Since Yeats's death, *A Vision* has been the subject of extensive scholarly labour. T. R. Henn, Richard Ellmann, and Kathleen Raine established the basic parameters of interpretation; more recent work by Margaret Mills Harper and Catherine E. Paul has reconstructed the manuscript history of the automatic writing sessions and their relationship to the published text. The emerging consensus is that *A Vision* is neither a work of occult revelation nor a work of madness, but a work of what Yeats himself called "mythopoeia" — the deliberate construction of a symbolic language adequate to the complexity of lived experience.
