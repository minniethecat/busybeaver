# W. B. Yeats

William Butler Yeats (13 June 1865 – 28 January 1939) was an Irish poet, playwright, and one of the foremost figures of twentieth-century literature. He was a driving force behind the Irish Literary Revival and a co-founder of the [[Abbey-Theatre|Abbey Theatre]]. He was awarded the Nobel Prize in Literature in 1923 "for his always inspired poetry, which in a highly artistic form gives expression to the spirit of a whole nation."

## Life

Yeats was born in Sandymount, Dublin, the son of the painter John Butler Yeats. His mother, Susan Pollexfen, came from a Sligo merchant family, and the landscapes of Co. Sligo — Ben Bulben, Knocknarea, Lough Gill — shaped his imagination from childhood. He spent his early adult years moving between Dublin, London, and Sligo, and his work is saturated with the tension between these worlds.

## Major Phases

- **Early Work (1889–1899)**: Celtic Twilight poetry, drawing on Irish myth and folklore — *The Wanderings of Usheen*, *The Wind Among the Reeds*
- **Middle Period (1900–1919)**: A plainer, more direct style; engagement with Irish politics and the founding of the Abbey Theatre — *Responsibilities*, *The Wild Swans at Coole*
- **Late Period (1920–1939)**: The great poems of the tower, Byzantium, and the gyres — *The Tower*, *The Winding Stair*, *Last Poems* — widely regarded as among the greatest poetry of the century
