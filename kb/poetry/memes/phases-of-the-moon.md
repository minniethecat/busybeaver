# Phases of the Moon

The twenty-eight phases of the moon constitute the psychological architecture of Yeats's *A Vision* (1925, revised 1937). Each phase represents a distinct type of human personality — defined by the ratio of Will, Mask, Creative Mind, and Body of Fate — and a stage in the soul's journey through successive incarnations. The system is presented as the discovery of the fictional Michael Robartes.

## Definition

The twenty-eight phases of the moon are a symbolic typology mapping the Great Wheel of the soul's incarnations. Each phase is a distinct "moment" in the cycle where the Four Faculties — Will, Mask, Creative Mind, and Body of Fate — achieve a particular equilibrium, producing a characteristic personality type. The Full Moon (Phase 15) represents perfect subjectivity and beauty; the Dark of the Moon (Phase 1), pure objectivity and passivity. Between these poles, the phases describe every possible human disposition.

## Origins

The lunar phases appear in Yeats's system through two distinct channels. In the Endnotes to the poetry, Robartes writes that "there are certain points of stress on outer and inner gyre, a division of each, now into ten, now into twenty-eight, stages or phases" [048-058]. This passage places the phases within the larger geometry of the gyres. In the prefatory verses to *A Vision* addressed to Michael Robartes, Yeats writes: "Twenty-and-eight the phases of the moon" [045-1014]. The phases occupy a liminal position — part of the elaborate fictional apparatus of the prose, yet also entering the poetry as a structuring principle.

## Structure of the Phases

The twenty-eight phases form a complete lunar cycle, from the Dark of the Moon to the Full Moon and back:

| Phase Range | Type | Description |
|-------------|------|-------------|
| Phase 1 | Dark of the Moon | Pure objectivity, complete passivity — the saint or victim |
| Phases 2–7 | Waxing | Subjective life awakening, the Will strengthening |
| Phase 8–12 | First Quarter | The "Forerunner" types — active, shaping, assertive |
| Phase 13–14 | Approaching Full | Near-perfect subjectivity, precursors of beauty |
| Phase 15 | Full Moon | Perfect beauty, complete subjectivity — the artist at creation |
| Phase 16–21 | Waning | Declining subjectivity, the Objective man emergent |
| Phase 22–28 | Last Quarter | Late phases, approaching dissolution and return to Phase 1 |

The four Faculties shift their proportions at each phase, creating a distinct psychological profile for each station. Yeats identified himself as a Phase 17 personality (the "Daimonic Man"), his wife George as Phase 18, and assigned historical figures — Dante, Shelley, Napoleon, Nietzsche — to specific phases as exemplars.

## Yeats's Self-Identification

Phase 17, the "Daimonic Man," was Yeats's own phase — characterised by the search for the anti-self or Mask, the creative tension between the Will and its opposite. Phase 15, the Full Moon, represents the ideal of Unity of Being — the moment when all faculties are in perfect equilibrium. Phase 17, by contrast, is characterised by striving: the Will seeks what it is not, and in that search creates art.

## The Judwalis and the Fictional Apparatus

The phases-of-the-moon doctrine reaches Yeats through the elaborate fictional framework of *A Vision*, in which Michael Robartes serves as the discoverer and transmitter. Robartes claims to have found the doctrine "among the Judwalis,"(048-054) a sect that Yeats presents as an authentic Arabian tradition but that is almost certainly his own invention. The name "Judwalis" combines suggestions of "Jew" and "Druze" with an Arabic-sounding suffix, creating an aura of esoteric antiquity around a personal symbolic system. The endnotes record that Robartes "had little help from the Judwalis either because they cannot grasp the abstractions or because they shun the central myth"(048-058) — a remark that absolves Yeats of the need to document actual sources. The self-conscious fictionality of this apparatus has been a point of critical debate: some readers see it as a protective mask, while others regard it as an elaborate confidence trick. What is certain is that the fictional framework enabled Yeats to build a systematic philosophy of personality without committing to literal belief.

## The Phase System as Psychology and Cosmology

The phases operate on two levels simultaneously: as a typology of individual personalities and as a cosmic law of cyclical transformation. Each phase is a moment in a continuous motion — the soul moves through the phases across successive incarnations, and the historical gyres likewise move through large-scale analogues. This double function explains why the phases have attracted both psychological and historical interpreters. The phases are the hinge between the personal and the cosmic in Yeats's thought — the point at which the doctrine of the mask meets the doctrine of the gyres.

## Scholarly Interpretation

The phases have been read in two main traditions. Kathleen Raine treats them as a genuine esoteric doctrine, tracing their antecedents to Neoplatonic and Hermetic sources. Hazard Adams reads them more as a heuristic fiction — a "supreme fiction" in Wallace Stevens's sense — that Yeats found necessary for his creative work. Both approaches agree that the phases are not reducible to a mere taxonomy of character types: they are the structural armature of Yeats's mature imagination, the system that allowed him to think about the relationship between individual personality and historical change.
