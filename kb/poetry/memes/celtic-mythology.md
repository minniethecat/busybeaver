# Celtic Mythology

**Celtic mythology** provided W. B. Yeats with a deep well of narrative material, symbolic figures, and a cultural framework through which he articulated Irish national identity and his own mystical vision.

## Sources in Yeats's Reading

Yeats's knowledge of Celtic mythology came primarily from scholarly sources. He read Professor John Rhŷs's *Celtic Heathendom* (1886), which documented pre-Christian Celtic religious beliefs and deities (048-029). This scholarly foundation distinguished Yeats's use of Celtic material from the sentimental "Celtic Twilight" popularised by lesser writers. The Endnotes reveal Yeats reading Rhŷs "by the light of Professor Frazer's *Golden Bough*" (048-029), putting together what he "finds there about the boar that killed Diarmuid, and other old Celtic boars and sows" to discern the mythological pattern beneath — that the boar "must be a type of cold and winter doing battle with the summer, or of death battling with life" (048-029).

The stone engravings of Celtic gods described in Rhŷs's work — including a figure holding "what looks like a thunderbolt" — appear in Yeats's notes and influenced his imagery of divine power (048-043).

## The Tuatha Dé Danann in Yeats's System

The Tuatha Dé Danann — "the Tribes of the goddess Danu, or the Sidhe, from *Aes Sidhe*, or *Sluagh Sidhe*, the people of the Faery Hills" (048-013) — occupy the centre of Yeats's mythological imagination. Yeats records that the country people believed the Sidhe "still ride the country as of old" (048-013), and that "*Sidhe* is also Gaelic for wind, and certainly the Sidhe have much to do with the wind" (048-013). When the country people saw leaves whirling on the road, "they bless themselves, because they believe the Sidhe to be passing by" (048-013).

The Tuatha Dé Danann were, in Yeats's reading, not gods in the Christian sense but a diminished divine order — the gods of pagan Ireland who, after their defeat, retreated into the hills and waters. The Endnotes describe them as shape-shifters: they "can take all shapes, and those that are in the waters take often the shape of fish" (048-023). A Galway woman tells Yeats that "there are more of them in the sea than on the land, and they sometimes try to come over the side of the boat in the form of fishes, for they can take their choice shape" (048-023).

## The Mythological Battles

Yeats found in Celtic mythology a recurring pattern of cosmic battle — what he calls "the battle of all things with shadowy decay" (048-032). He identifies four manifestations of this single archetypal conflict:

The first is the primal battle "between the Tuatha De Danaan, the powers of light, and warmth, and fruitfulness, and goodness, and the Fomor, the powers of darkness, and cold, and barrenness, and badness upon the Towery Plain" — which Yeats interprets as "the establishment of the habitable world, the rout of the ancestral darkness" (048-032).

The second is the seasonal battle: the Sidhe fight "in November for the harvest" — "the annual battle of summer and winter" (048-032).

The third is the battle over the dying: Yeats heard of this "both in County Galway and in the Isles of Aran" (048-032), and a fairy doctor explained it as "a battle between the friends and enemies of the dying, the one party trying to take them, the other trying to save them from being taken" (048-032). When "the land of the Sidhe was the only other world" (048-032), such a battle "may once have always accompanied death" (048-032).

The fourth is the Battle of the Black Pig — an eschatological conflict that appears in Irish folk prophecy, which Yeats interprets as "the battle between the manifest world and the ancestral darkness at the end of all things" (048-032).

All four, Yeats concludes, "are one, the battle of all things with shadowy decay" (048-032). This vision of cyclical cosmic conflict directly informs the historical gyres of *A Vision* and the apocalyptic imagery of "The Second Coming."

## Integration with Occult Systems

Yeats synthesised Celtic mythology with other esoteric traditions. He read *Celtic Heathendom* "by the light of Platonic philosophy," finding correspondences between Celtic deities and Neoplatonic spiritual hierarchies (048-029). This syncretic approach allowed Yeats to present Irish mythology not as provincial folklore but as part of a universal esoteric tradition.

The Endnotes show Yeats reading the Tuatha Dé Danann alongside the Golden Dawn's hierarchies, the *Corpus Hermeticum*, and Blake's prophetic books. Celtic mythology, in his hands, becomes not a closed national tradition but a local inflection of a perennial philosophy — a move that simultaneously elevated Irish culture to universal significance and grounded esoteric abstraction in the particular landscape and stories of Ireland.

## In *The Celtic Twilight*

Yeats's 1893 prose collection *[[celtic-twilight\|The Celtic Twilight]]* recorded folk beliefs and supernatural encounters from the Sligo countryside — including the figure of Clooth-na-Bare, a supernatural woman who "went all over the world" seeking the deepest lake (048-015). Hanrahan's pursuit of the "ever-fleeting" supernatural also originates in this collection (048-061). The Endnotes record that "the peasant beauty and the blind poet are Mary Hynes and Raftery, and the incident of the man drowned in Cloone Bog is recorded in my *Celtic Twilight*" (048-061), showing how Yeats's scholarly and folkloric sources interpenetrated.
