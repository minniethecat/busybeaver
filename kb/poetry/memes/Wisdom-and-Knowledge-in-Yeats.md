# Wisdom and Knowledge in Yeats

For Yeats, wisdom was not the accumulation of facts but the hard-earned fruit of conflict — the knowledge that comes through the "anti-self," through suffering, through the discipline of the occult path. This page aggregates wiki entries that explore the traditions of knowledge — philosophical, mystical, and poetic — that inform his work.

## About This Page

Yeats drew on a wide range of knowledge traditions: the Neoplatonism of Plotinus and the Cambridge Platonists, the esoteric wisdom of the Golden Dawn, the "Great Memory" that connects individual minds to a collective past, and the paradoxical insight that comes through the "mask" or anti-self. The entries below cover the philosophers he read, the doctrines he adapted, and the distinctive Yeatsian ideas — the gyre, the Anima Mundi, Unity of Being — that emerged from his lifelong quest for a "philosophy" adequate to his poetic vision.
