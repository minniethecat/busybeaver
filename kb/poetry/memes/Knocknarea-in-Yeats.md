# Knocknarea in Yeats

Knocknarea (from the Irish *Cnoc na Rí* — "Hill of the Kings") is a limestone mountain rising 327 metres above Sligo Bay. Its flat summit is crowned with a massive stone cairn traditionally identified as the burial place of Queen Maeve (Medb) of Connacht, the legendary warrior-queen of the Ulster Cycle. In W. B. Yeats's poetry, Knocknarea is the sacred mountain: the departure-point of the fairy host, the summit where myth and landscape converge, and a constant emblem of the Sligo countryside that formed the poet's imagination.

## Knocknarea and the Sligo Childhood

For the young Yeats, Knocknarea was not a literary symbol but an everyday visual fact. The mountain is visible from Sligo town and dominates the bay; the young Yeats would have seen it daily during his childhood summers at his grandparents' house. The Endnotes recall that "when I was a child at Sligo I could see above my grandfather's house a little column of smoke" [048-052], and though the smoke is not Knocknarea's, the visual memory belongs to the same landscape: the Sligo of childhood, where every view contained a mountain and every mountain contained a story.

This biographical rootedness is fundamental to Knocknarea's poetic charge. Unlike the symbolic mountains of other poets — Shelley's Mont Blanc, Wordsworth's Snowdon — Knocknarea is not encountered in adulthood as a sublime revelation but known from childhood as a familiar landmark. The host that rides from Knocknarea rides from a mountain that the poet saw every summer of his boyhood, and the visionary drama has, for its stage, the landscape of memory. The "birds from Knocknarea" [032-413] that open Yeats's first collection are birds seen with a child's eyes: not generic poetic fowl but the specific swallows and gulls that wheeled above Sligo Bay, rising from a mountain that was simultaneously ordinary and enchanted.

## The Visual Presence

Knocknarea's visual profile — a flat-topped, table-like mountain visible for miles across Sligo Bay — contributes to its symbolic power. Unlike the sharp peak of Ben Bulben to the north, Knocknarea's truncated summit suggests incompletion, a platform waiting for something to rest upon it. The cairn supplies that something: human labour placed upon a natural foundation, myth laid upon geology. This visual character — the mountain as plinth, as pedestal — makes Knocknarea the natural stage for Yeats's fairy drama. When the host rides from Knocknarea, it rides from a visibly myth-charged summit.

## Queen Maeve's Cairn

The cairn on Knocknarea's summit is traditionally the tomb of Queen Maeve — a connection that charges the mountain with mythological resonance. Maeve (Medb), the warrior-queen of Connacht whose cattle-raid (*Táin Bó Cúailnge*) is the central story of the Ulster Cycle, is buried standing up, facing her enemies in Ulster to the north — or so the Sligo country people told Yeats. When the host rides "from Knocknarea," it departs from the tomb of Ireland's most formidable queen, and the conjunction of fairy lore and heroic legend gives the mountain its singular symbolic density.

In "The Old Age of Queen Maeve," Yeats dramatises the aged queen's encounter with Aengus, and Knocknarea — though not named in that poem — stands behind it as Maeve's eternal dwelling. The mountain's cairn is the silent witness to Maeve's long after-living, a "monument of unageing stone" that outlasts the queen's mortal reign.

The Endnotes record a specific piece of Sligo folklore attached to the cairn: "the country people say that the stones, which are the size of a man's head, were put there by a man who wanted to know the size of the moon" [048-015]. This fragment of local tradition — a man piling stones to measure the moon — is at once absurd and profound: absurd because stones cannot measure the moon, profound because it captures the human impulse to build upward, to raise monuments that reach toward celestial bodies. Yeats does not dismiss the "country people's" explanation as superstition; he records it alongside the archaeological fact of the cairn, granting the folk explanation equal standing with scientific description. The cairn, read through this folk narrative, becomes not merely a tomb but a proto-observatory — a monument to the human effort to measure the immeasurable.

The Endnotes confirm the local identification: "Knocknarea is in Sligo" [048-015], anchoring the mythological mountain firmly in the physical geography of Yeats's childhood.

## Knocknarea as Mythic Landscape

Knocknarea's most celebrated poetic appearance is as the launch-point of the fairy host in "The Hosting of the Sidhe," the opening poem of *The Wind Among the Reeds* section:

> The host is riding from Knocknarea
> And over the grave of Clooth-na-Bare [038-001]

The mountain is not merely a scenic landmark: it is the threshold between worlds. From Knocknarea's summit, the Sidhe — the fairy host, the "Ever-living" of Irish mythology — descend into the mortal world. Niamh's voice calls "Away, come away" from Knocknarea, and the invitation to the Otherworld is issued from a physical mountain that Yeats knew from childhood.

## Knocknarea in the Early Poetry

Knocknarea appears already in the earliest Yeats. In "Crossways" (1889), the poem "The Falling of the Leaves" mentions "The birds from Knocknarea" [032-413] — an image that associates the mountain with natural cycles of departure and return. The birds — perhaps the birds of Aengus, perhaps ordinary swallows — rise from Knocknarea as the Sidhe later rise from it, and the mountain is already, in Yeats's first collection, a point of vertical movement, of ascent into the visionary.
