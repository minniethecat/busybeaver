# Dream and Vision

Dream and vision in Yeats's poetry are not mere psychological states but epistemological tools — modes of perception that grant access to the supernatural, the ancestral, and the eternally true. From the "Druid dream of the end of days" ((015-210)) to the dreaming of Aengus, the Celtic god of love and poetry, dream-states dissolve the boundary between the mortal and the immortal, the historical and the mythological.

## Origins and Sources

- **Irish mythology**: Aengus, the god of dreams and love, who "dreams from sun to sun" ((015-209)), embodying the connection between dream and poetic inspiration
- **Druidic tradition**: The Druid dream as prophetic vision — "A Druid dream of the end of days" ((015-210))
- **Romanticism**: The Romantic valorization of dream as creative source, inherited through Blake and Shelley
- **Spiritualism and the occult**: Yeats's séance experiences and Golden Dawn practices, where visions were deliberately induced

## Symbolic Meaning

Dream and vision in Yeats function at multiple levels:

1. **Prophetic vision**: The dream reveals what is to come. In _The Wanderings of Usheen_, dream-states disclose the future of Ireland, the decline of pagan vitality, and the coming of Christianity.

2. **Ancestral memory**: Dreams connect the dreamer to "the dreaming water-fowls" ((001-012)) and by extension to the pre-modern, mythological consciousness of Ireland.

3. **Poetic creation**: The poet is a dreamer who "dreams of battle and love" ((015-389)) while "his heart still dreams" — creative work is itself a form of dreaming.

4. **The hollow dream**: In later poems, dream acquires a more skeptical inflection — "all their beauty's like a hollow dream" ((039-080)). The dream can also be delusion, escapism, the failure to face reality.

## Development Across Yeats's Career

**Early period**: Dream is predominantly mythological — it is the mode of the fairies (Song of the Faeries, 1885), the escape into a pre-Christian Ireland of kings and supernatural beings.

**Middle period**: Dream becomes problematic. The Rose poems ask whether beauty itself is "like a dream" — the question "Who dreamed that beauty passes like a dream?" ((031-163)) can be read as either a rhetorical question (of course it doesn't) or a genuine doubt (perhaps it does).

**Late period (1903–1914)**: Yeats explicitly questions the dream mode. "The Old Age of Queen Maeve" calls it hollow. "In the Seven Woods" dreams of idealized women whose beauty "was folded in dismay" — the dream is shadowed by reality. By "Responsibilities" (1914), the dream has largely given way to the "cold eye" of mature Yeats.
