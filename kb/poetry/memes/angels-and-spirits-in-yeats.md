# Angels and Spirits in Yeats

Angels, spirits, and ghosts pervade Yeats's poetry as emissaries between the
mundane and the transcendent. Unlike the orthodox Christian angelology he
inherited, Yeats's spirits are drawn from a syncretic blend of Hermeticism,
Celtic fairy lore, and his own visionary experiences.

## Definition

In Yeats's cosmos, spirits are real — not metaphors for psychological states.
They are inhabitants of what he called the *Anima Mundi*, the world-soul or
collective unconscious where images and souls persist. Ghosts are the spirits
of the dead, often lingering near the living with "whimpering" or warning voices.
Angels represent higher spiritual intelligences — but in Yeats's system they are
not necessarily benevolent or Christian. The "youngest angel" [002-029] and the
"flaming lute-thronged angelic door" [038-434] both suggest a system where
angels function as cosmic functionaries rather than objects of worship.

## Origins and Sources

- **Primary source**: Hermetic Order of the Golden Dawn ritual practice; Irish fairy tradition
- **Key texts**: "The Hosting of the Sidhe," "The Cold Heaven," "All Souls' Night"

Yeats's membership in the Golden Dawn (from 1890) gave him a structured angelology
derived from Renaissance Hermeticism, Kabbalah, and Rosicrucianism. Simultaneously,
Irish folk tradition — which Yeats collected in *The Celtic Twilight* and *Fairy
and Folk Tales of the Irish Peasantry* — provided a parallel model of spirit
beings (the Sidhe, the *leanhaun shee*, the pooka) that merged easily with
Hermetic hierarchies.

## Development Across Yeats's Career

Early poems treat spirits through the lens of Celtic twilight: "The Hosting
of the Sidhe" (1899) presents the fairy host as a martial ghost-army, and
"who will go drive with Fergus now / And pierce the deep wood's woven shade"
invokes the ghost-Fergus as psychopomp [031-343]. "The Stolen Child" (1886)
makes the fairies both alluring and chilling — they steal children from the
human world into a timeless but inhuman realm.

In the middle and late periods, ghosts become more personal and philosophical.
"A ghost may come," Yeats writes, and the ghost of "All Souls' Night" is a
summoned presence of the dead beloved [047-475]. "His ghost will be gay"
suggests the spirit's liberation from bodily constraint [044-815]. The "ghostly
solitude" of the tower-dweller [047-475] becomes a figure for the poet himself
— alive but already half-spectral, communing with invisible presences.

## Spiritualism, Automatic Writing, and the Instructors

Yeats's engagement with spirits took a decisive turn in October 1917, when
his wife George Hyde-Lees began producing automatic writing — pages of
script produced in trance states that claimed to come from spiritual
"instructors." Yeats spent years organizing and interpreting these
communications, which formed the basis of *A Vision* (1925, revised 1937).
The instructors identified themselves not as angels or ghosts but as
disincarnate intelligences occupying intermediate spiritual states. They
spoke in a deliberately obscure, geometric vocabulary — gyres, cones, phases
of the moon — that Yeats took as evidence of their authenticity: true spirits,
he argued, would not speak with the fluency of the living. The automatic
writing sessions (which continued for years) represent Yeats's most sustained
attempt to bridge the gap between the living and the dead through systematic,
almost scientific, method.

## Angels in A Vision

In the elaborate cosmology of *A Vision*, angels occupy a specific place in the
spiritual hierarchy. They are the Thirteenth Cone — a realm beyond the
twenty-eight phases of the lunar cycle, representing pure subjectivity or pure
objectivity. Unlike the spirits of the dead, who cycle through reincarnation
governed by the phases, angels are permanent — they "stand in the presence of
God." But even this permanence is, for Yeats, a kind of limitation: angels lack
the human capacity for conflict, for the "quarrel with ourselves" out of which
poetry is made. The angelic state is thus both the highest spiritual condition
and, from a human perspective, a kind of sterile perfection. This ambivalence —
reverence for the angelic mixed with suspicion of its inhuman purity — runs
through Yeats's later work and complicates any simple reading of his
spiritualism as credulous or escapist.

## Spirits as Poetic Method

Beyond their doctrinal role in Yeats's occult system, spirits serve a practical
poetic function: they are vehicles for the uncanny. The ghost in Yeats
destabilizes the boundary between inside and outside, past and present, self
and other. In "The Cold Heaven," the speaker sees the sky "burning and yet
the ice burned there" — a vision that is simultaneously natural (a cold winter
sky), supernatural (a ghostly apparition), and psychological (the "ice" of
frozen grief). The poem's power derives from Yeats's refusal to resolve which
register is primary. Spirits thus function in Yeats's mature style as what
the critic Helen Vendler calls "the irrational remainder" — the element in
experience that resists assimilation to rational explanation and that poetry
exists to preserve.
