## Introduction

"Ancestral Houses" is the opening section of "[[meditations-in-time-of-civil-war|Meditations in Time of Civil War]]," the seven-part poem sequence published in _[[The-Tower|The Tower]]_ (1928). Set at [[thoor-ballylee|Thoor Ballylee]], Yeats's Norman tower in Galway, during the [[Irish-Civil-War|Irish Civil War]] (1922–1923), the sequence moves through the poet's dwelling room by room and ends in a visionary encounter with the phantoms of hatred loosed by the conflict. "Ancestral Houses" establishes the sequence's central question: whether beauty is complicit in the violence that makes it possible.

## The Poem

> Surely among a rich man's flowering lawns,
> Amid the rustle of his planted hills,
> Life overflows without ambitious pains;
> And rains down life until the basin spills,
> And mounts more dizzy high the more it rains
> As though to choose whatever shape it wills
> And never stoop to a mechanical,
> Or servile shape, at others' beck and call.
>
> Mere dreams, mere dreams! Yet Homer had not sung
> Had he not found it certain beyond dreams
> That out of life's own self-delight had sprung
> The abounding glittering jet; though now it seems
> As if some marvellous empty sea-shell flung
> Out of the obscure dark of the rich streams,
> And not a fountain, were the symbol which
> Shadows the inherited glory of the rich.
>
> Some violent bitter man, some powerful man
> Called architect and artist in, that they,
> Bitter and violent men, might rear in stone
> The sweetness that all longed for night and day,
> The gentleness none there had ever known;
> But when the master's buried mice can play,
> And maybe the great-grandson of that house,
> For all its bronze and marble, 's but a mouse.
>
> Oh, what if gardens where the peacock strays
> With delicate feet upon old terraces,
> Or else all Juno from an urn displays
> Before the indifferent garden deities;
> Oh, what if levelled lawns and gravelled ways
> Where slippered Contemplation finds his ease
> And Childhood a delight for every sense,
> But take our greatness with our violence!
>
> What if the glory of escutcheoned doors,
> And buildings that a haughtier age designed,
> The pacing to and fro on polished floors
> Amid great chambers and long galleries, lined
> With famous portraits of our ancestors;
> What if those things the greatest of mankind,
> Consider most to magnify, or to bless,
> But take our greatness with our bitterness!
> [047-228] – [047-268]

## Analysis

### The "Rich Man's Flowering Lawns"

The poem opens with an image of almost dreamlike abundance: "Surely among a rich man's flowering lawns, / Amid the rustle of his planted hills, / Life overflows without ambitious pains" [047-229] [047-230] [047-231]. The landscape of the Anglo-Irish great house — the "planted hills," the "levelled lawns and gravelled ways" — appears as a paradise where life streams forth spontaneously, "never stoop[ing] to a mechanical, / Or servile shape" [047-235] [047-236].

But this vision is immediately undercut by the exclamation "Mere dreams, mere dreams!" [047-237]. The organic, self-delighting fountain of life is exposed as a fantasy of the inheriting class. The "real" symbol for inherited wealth, Yeats suggests, is not a fountain but "some marvellous empty sea-shell flung / Out of the obscure dark of the rich streams" [047-241] [047-242] — a beautiful but hollow object, shaped by forces its possessor did not control and does not understand.

### The Violence That Built Sweetness

The poem's most unsettling turn comes in the third stanza: "Some violent bitter man, some powerful man / Called architect and artist in" [047-245] [047-246]. The beauty of the great house — its stone, its symmetry, its cultivated sweetness — was commissioned by violent men seeking to build "The sweetness that all longed for night and day, / The gentleness none there had ever known" [047-248] [047-249]. The great house is, in this reading, a compensation: the violent create beauty to possess a gentleness they themselves lack.

Yet the cycle of inheritance undoes the founder's intention. "When the master's buried mice can play, / And maybe the great-grandson of that house, / For all its bronze and marble, 's but a mouse" [047-250] [047-251] [047-252] — the inheritor is diminished, made small by the very grandeur he inherits. The great house that was meant to transcend violence instead enfeebles its heirs.

### "Our Greatness with Our Violence"

The final two stanzas are structured as a series of rhetorical questions, each beginning "What if...": "What if gardens where the peacock strays... But take our greatness with our violence!" [047-253] [047-260]. "What if the glory of escutcheoned doors... But take our greatness with our bitterness!" [047-261] [047-268].

The parallel construction insists on the inseparability of achievement and cost. The "greatness" of civilization — its art, its architecture, its contemplative leisure — is inextricably bound to the "violence" that founded it and the "bitterness" that sustains it. This is not a condemnation so much as a tragic recognition. Yeats, writing from his own tower — itself a fortified keep from a violent age — does not exempt himself from the indictment. The poet in his tower is the heir to the same ambiguous legacy.

## Place in the Sequence

As the opening movement of "Meditations in Time of Civil War," "Ancestral Houses" sets the thematic ground for the entire sequence. The question it raises — whether beauty depends on violence — echoes through the subsequent sections: through the poet's own house ("My House"), through the [[sato-sword|Sato sword]] on his writing table ("My Table"), through the soldiers on the road and the starling in the crumbling wall ("The Road at My Door," "The Stare's Nest by My Window"), and finally through the phantoms of the dead rising from the contested Irish earth ("I See Phantoms of Hatred...").
