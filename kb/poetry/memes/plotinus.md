# Plotinus

Plotinus (c. 204–270 CE) was the Greek-speaking Egyptian philosopher who founded Neoplatonism, the tradition of idealist thought that would shape Western mysticism for seventeen centuries — and that would become, for W. B. Yeats, the philosophical vocabulary through which he articulated his own system of gyres, lunar phases, and the soul's journey. Yeats's relation to Plotinus was intimate and adversarial: he "chose Plato and Plotinus for a friend" in "The Tower," but in his final poems he "mocked Plotinus' thought" and "cried in Plato's teeth" that the eternal forms could not account for the living body. This double movement — towards Plotinian transcendence and away from it — is one of the great dramas of Yeats's late work.

## Introduction

Plotinus taught in Rome in the third century, elaborating a system in which all existence emanates from a single, ineffable source — the One — through successive levels of reality: Intellect (*Nous*), Soul (*Psyche*), and finally the material world. The soul's task, in Plotinus's teaching, is to ascend back through these levels, stripping away the accretions of embodiment until it achieves union with the One. Yeats encountered Plotinus through Stephen MacKenna's celebrated translation of the *Enneads* (1917–1930), and the philosopher's vision of a universe organised as a hierarchy of spiritual ascent provided the metaphysical architecture for Yeats's *A Vision* (1925) and for the philosophical poems of his maturity.

## Key Facts

- **Born**: c. 204 CE, Lycopolis, Egypt
- **Died**: 270 CE, Campania, Italy
- **Role**: Philosopher, founder of Neoplatonism
- **Connection to Yeats**: Principal philosophical source for the metaphysical system of *A Vision* and the late poems; Yeats's key dialogue partner on the nature of the soul

## Significance in Yeats's Work

Plotinus enters Yeats's poetry most visibly in the philosophical poems of *The Tower* (1928). In "The Tower" (III), Yeats declares his intention to "choose Plato and Plotinus for a friend / Until imagination, ear and eye, / Can be content with argument and deal / In abstract things" [047-249]. The choice is presented as a renunciation — an old man turning away from the sensual world towards the contemplative life that Plotinus championed. But the renunciation is incomplete: even as Yeats invokes Plotinus, the poem pulses with the very sensory richness it claims to be leaving behind.

The rebellion comes in Yeats's final poems. "The Delphic Oracle upon Plotinus" (1939) stages an explicit repudiation: "I mock Plotinus' thought / And cry in Plato's teeth, / Death and life were not / Till man made up the whole" [047-560]. The Plotinian vision of the soul's ascent to a transcendent reality is rejected in favour of an immanent vision — the body, the dance, the "blooming" of the chestnut tree in "Among School Children." Yeats's late position is not a simple rejection of Plotinus but a correction: the eternal must be found *in* the temporal, not beyond it.

Plotinus's doctrine of the soul also shaped Yeats's thinking about death. In his endnotes, Yeats quotes a long passage from Plotinus on the soul's creative power: "soul is the author of all living things, that it has breathed the life into them all … itself formed and ordered this vast heaven" [048-027]. Yeats acknowledged that when he wrote about Plotinus he "forgot that it is something in our own eyes that makes us see them as all transcendence" [048-027] — a recognition that his Plotinus was a constructed figure, shaped by his own imaginative needs.

## References in Yeats's Prose

In *A Vision* (1925), Plotinus's scheme of emanation and return provides the structural model for Yeats's system of gyres and lunar phases — though Yeats adapts the Neoplatonic architecture freely, filling it with Irish mythology, occult symbolism, and his own invented cosmology. The *Daimon*, the *Anima Mundi*, and the doctrine of the soul's descent through the spheres all bear Plotinian traces, filtered through Yeats's Golden Dawn studies and his reading of MacKenna.
