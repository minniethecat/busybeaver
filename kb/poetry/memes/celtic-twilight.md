# The Celtic Twilight

The Celtic Twilight (1893, expanded 1902) is W. B. Yeats's prose collection of Irish folklore stories, fairy encounters, and supernatural tales gathered from the Irish countryside — a cornerstone of his early mythological period and the work that gave its name to the "Celtic Twilight" literary movement.

## Publication Details

- **Type**: prose work / folklore collection
- **First published**: 1893 (expanded edition 1902)
- **Publisher**: Lawrence and Bullen (1893); A. H. Bullen (1902)
- **Significance**: Yeats's first major prose work; established him as a collector and interpreter of Irish oral tradition alongside his poetry

## Overview

The Celtic Twilight collects stories Yeats heard from Irish country people — farmers, storytellers, and seers — about fairies, ghosts, and the supernatural. The book weaves together firsthand accounts with Yeats's own reflections on the relationship between the visible and invisible worlds. It established a prose counterpart to the mythological vision unfolding in his early poems, and its title became the label for an entire generation of Irish literary revival writers.

Yeats returns to the material of The Celtic Twilight in the endnotes to his collected poems, referencing specific stories that connect to his verse. He writes of Clooth-na-Bare, a supernatural figure who "went all over the world seeking a lake deep enough to drown her faery life," noting that he has "written of Clooth-na-Bare in The Celtic Twilight" [048-015]. The poem "The Host of the Air" draws directly on a story collected in the volume about a young man whose bride is taken by the Sidhe.

The book also records Hanrahan the Red, a wandering poet and schoolmaster whose pursuit of a supernatural vision through the bogs of Ireland is recorded in the volume. Yeats references this story in the endnotes, noting that "Hanrahan's pursuit of the phantom deer, hound, and girl through Cloone Bog is recorded in my Celtic Twilight" [048-061]. These stories became a deep well of imagery that Yeats drew upon throughout his career — the fairy doctor, the priest and the fairy, the man who saw the Sidhe ride — all trace back to the encounters recorded in this collection.

## Themes and Motifs

The Celtic Twilight articulates several themes central to Yeats's poetic project:

- **The permeable boundary between worlds**: In story after story, the Sidhe (fairies) cross into the human world, and humans stumble into theirs — a pattern that shapes Yeats's symbolic treatment of vision and reality throughout his poetry.
- **The storyteller as keeper of tradition**: Yeats frames himself as a collector and transmitter of oral culture, positioning the folk narrator as a figure parallel to the poet. This self-conception persists through his later work as he increasingly drew on Irish mythological material.
- **Irish landscape as supernatural terrain**: Specific places — Clooth-na-Bare's lake, Cloone Bog, the hills of Sligo — are charged with supernatural significance, establishing a pattern Yeats would develop in his place-poems throughout his career.
