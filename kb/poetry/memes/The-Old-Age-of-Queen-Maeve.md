# The Old Age of Queen Maeve

[039-001]Maeve the great queen was pacing to and fro,   

[039-002]Between the walls covered with beaten bronze,   

[039-003]In her high house at Cruachan; the long hearth,   

[039-004]Flickering with ash and hazel, but half showed   

[039-005]Where the tired horse-boys lay upon the rushes,   

[039-006]Or on the benches underneath the walls,   

[039-007]In comfortable sleep; all living slept   

[039-008]But that great queen, who more than half the night   

[039-009]Had paced from door to fire and fire to door.   

[039-010]Though now in her old age, in her young age   

[039-011]She had been beautiful in that old way   

[039-012]That’s all but gone; for the proud heart is gone,   

[039-013]And the fool heart of the counting-house fears all   

[039-014]But soft beauty and indolent [[love-in-yeats|desire]].   

[039-015]She could have called over the rim of the world   

[039-016]Whatever woman’s lover had hit her fancy,   

[039-017]And yet had been great bodied and great limbed,   

[039-018]Fashioned to be the mother of strong children;   

[039-019]And she’d had lucky eyes and a high heart,   

[039-020]And wisdom that caught fire like the dried flax,   

[039-021]At need, and made her beautiful and fierce,   

[039-022]Sudden and laughing. O unquiet heart,   

[039-023]Why do you praise another, praising her,   

[039-024]As if there were no tale but your own tale   

[039-025]Worth knitting to a measure of sweet sound?   

[039-026]Have I not bid you tell of that great queen   

[039-027]Who has been buried some two thousand years?

[039-028]When night was at its deepest, a wild goose   

[039-029]Cried from the porter’s lodge, and with long clamour   

[039-030]Shook the ale horns and shields upon their hooks;   

[039-031]But the horse-boys slept on, as though some power   

[039-032]Had filled the house with [[druidism-and-celtic-mysticism|Druid]] heaviness;   

[039-033]And wondering who of the many-changing [[tuatha-de-danaan|Sidhe]]   

[039-034]Had come as in the old times to counsel her,   

[039-035]Maeve walked, yet with slow footfall, being old,   

[039-036]To that small chamber by the outer gate.   

[039-037]The porter slept, although he sat upright   

[039-038]With still and stony limbs and open eyes.   

[039-039]Maeve waited, and when that ear-piercing noise   

[039-040]Broke from his parted lips and broke again,   

[039-041]She laid a hand on either of his shoulders,   

[039-042]And shook him wide awake, and bid him say   

[039-043]Who of the wandering many-changing ones   

[039-044]Had troubled his sleep. But all he had to say   

[039-045]Was that, the air being heavy and the dogs   

[039-046]More still than they had been for a good month,   

[039-047]He had fallen asleep, and, though he had dreamed nothing,   

[039-048]He could remember when he had had fine dreams.   

[039-049]It was before the time of the great war   

[039-050]Over the White-Horned Bull, and the Brown Bull.

[039-051]She turned away; he turned again to sleep   

[039-052]That no god troubled now, and, wondering   

[039-053]What matters were afoot among [[fairy-and-faery-lore|the Sidhe]],   

[039-054]Maeve walked through that great hall, and with a sigh   

[039-055]Lifted the curtain of her sleeping-room,   

[039-056]Remembering that she too had seemed divine   

[039-057]To many thousand eyes, and to her own   

[039-058]One that the generations had long waited   

[039-059]That work too difficult for mortal hands   

[039-060]Might be accomplished. Bunching the curtain up   

[039-061]She saw her husband Ailell sleeping there,   

[039-062]And thought of days when he’d had a straight body,   

[039-063]And of that famous Fergus, Nessa’s husband,   

[039-064]Who had been the lover of her middle life.

[039-065]Suddenly Ailell spoke out of his sleep,   

[039-066]And not with his own voice or a man’s voice,   

[039-067]But with the [[fire-passion|burning]], live, unshaken voice,   

[039-068]Of those that it may be can never age.   

[039-069]He said, “High Queen of Cruachan and Magh Ai,   

[039-070]A king of the Great Plain would speak with you.”   

[039-071]And with glad voice Maeve answered him, “What king   

[039-072]Of the far wandering shadows has come to me?   

[039-073]As in the old days when they would come and go   

[039-074][[About]] my threshold to counsel and to help.”   

[039-075]The parted lips replied, “I seek your help,   

[039-076]For I am [[aengus-god|Aengus]], and I am crossed in love.”   

[039-077]“How may a mortal whose life gutters out   

[039-078]Help them that wander with hand clasping hand,   

[039-079]Their haughty images that cannot wither,   

[039-080]For all their beauty’s like a hollow dream,   

[039-081]Mirrored in streams that neither hail nor rain   

[039-082]Nor the cold North has troubled?” He replied:   

[039-083]“I am from those rivers and I bid you call   

[039-084]The children of the Maines out of sleep,   

[039-085]And set them digging under Bual’s hill.   

[039-086]We shadows, while they uproot his earthy house,   

[039-087]Will overthrow his shadows and carry off   

[039-088]Caer, his blue-eyed daughter that I love.   

[039-089]I helped your fathers when they built these walls,   

[039-090]And I would have your help in my great need,   

[039-091]Queen of high Cruachan.” “I obey your will   

[039-092]With speedy feet and a most thankful heart:   

[039-093]For you have been, O Aengus of the birds,   

[039-094]Our giver of good counsel and good luck.”   

[039-095]And with a groan, as if the mortal breath   

[039-096]Could but awaken sadly upon lips   

[039-097]That happier breath had moved, her husband turned   

[039-098]Face downward, tossing in a troubled sleep;   

[039-099]But Maeve, and not with a slow feeble foot,   

[039-100]Came to the threshold of the painted house,   

[039-101]Where her grandchildren slept, and cried aloud,   

[039-102]Until the pillared dark began to stir   

[039-103]With shouting and the clang of unhooked arms.   

[039-104]She told them of the many-changing ones;   

[039-105]And all that night, and all through the next day   

[039-106]To middle night, they dug into the hill.   

[039-107]At middle night great cats with silver claws,   

[039-108]Bodies of shadow and blind eyes like pearls,   

[039-109]Came up out of the hole, and red-eared hounds   

[039-110]With long white bodies came out of the air   

[039-111]Suddenly, and ran at them and harried them.

[039-112]The Maines’ children dropped their spades, and stood   

[039-113]With quaking joints and terror-strucken faces,   

[039-114]Till Maeve called out: “These are but common men.   

[039-115]The Maines’ children have not dropped their spades,   

[039-116]Because Earth, crazy for its broken power,   

[039-117]Casts up a show and the winds answer it   

[039-118]With holy shadows.” Her high heart was glad,   

[039-119]And when the uproar ran along the grass   

[039-120]She followed with [[shadow-and-light|light]] footfall in the midst,   

[039-121]Till it died out where an old thorn tree stood.

[039-122]Friend of these many years, you too had stood   

[039-123]With equal courage in that whirling rout;   

[039-124]For you, although you’ve not her wandering heart,   

[039-125]Have all that greatness, and not hers alone,   

[039-126]For there is no high story about queens   

[039-127]In any ancient book but tells of you;   

[039-128]And when I’ve heard how they grew old and died,   

[039-129]Or fell into unhappiness, I’ve said:   

[039-130]“She will grow old and die, and she has wept!”   

[039-131]And when I’d write it out anew, the words,   

[039-132]Half crazy with the thought, She too has wept!   

[039-133]Outrun the measure. I’d tell of that great queen   

[039-134]Who stood amid a silence by the thorn   

[039-135]Until two lovers came out of the air   

[039-136]With bodies made out of soft fire. The one,   

[039-137]About whose face birds wagged their fiery wings,   

[039-138]Said: “Aengus and his sweetheart give their thanks   

[039-139]To Maeve and to Maeve’s household, owing all   

[039-140]In owing them the bride-bed that gives peace.”   

[039-141]Then Maeve: “O Aengus, Master of all lovers,   

[039-142]A thousand years ago you held high talk   

[039-143]With the first kings of many-pillared Cruachan.   

[039-144]O when will you grow weary?” They had vanished;   

[039-145]But out of the dark air over her head there came   

[039-146]A murmur of soft words and meeting lips.
