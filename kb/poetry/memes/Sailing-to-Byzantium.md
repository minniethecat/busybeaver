# Sailing to Byzantium

"Sailing to Byzantium" is the opening poem of W. B. Yeats's 1928 collection *The Tower* and is widely regarded as one of the finest poems of the twentieth century. A meditation on aging, the relationship between body and soul, and the consolations of art, the poem traces a spiritual journey from the sensual world of "those dying generations" to the "holy city of Byzantium" — Yeats's symbol for the union of art, intellect, and eternity.

## Publication Details

- **Type**: lyric poem
- **First published**: 1928, in *The Tower* (also appeared in *October Blast*, 1927)
- **Form**: four ottava rima stanzas (eight lines each, iambic pentameter)
- **Setting**: Byzantium (Constantinople/Istanbul), conceived as a symbolic city of art and intellect at the historical apex of civilisation

## The Poem

### I — The Sensual World

The poem opens with a declaration of exclusion: "That is no country for old men" [047-001]. The "country" is the world of biological life — of "The salmon-falls, the mackerel-crowded seas" [047-004], of young lovers in one another's arms, of everything that is "begotten, born, and dies" [047-006]. This is the realm of becoming, not being — a sensual music in which "Monuments of unaging intellect" [047-008] are neglected.

### II — The Aged Man

The second stanza confronts the indignities of age directly: "An aged man is but a paltry thing, / A tattered coat upon a stick" [047-009–010]. The only redemption is for the soul to "clap its hands and sing, and louder sing / For every tatter in its mortal dress" [047-011–012]. There is no "singing school but studying / Monuments of its own magnificence" [047-013–014] — the soul must educate itself through the contemplation of achieved art. And so the speaker has "sailed the seas and come / To the holy city of Byzantium" [047-015–016].

### III — The Sages

The third stanza is a prayer addressed to the "sages standing in God's holy fire / As in the gold mosaic of a wall" [047-017–018] — the figures depicted in the mosaics of Byzantine churches. Yeats calls upon them to "be the singing masters of my soul" [047-020], to "Consume my heart away; sick with desire / And fastened to a dying animal" [047-021–022], and to "gather me / Into the artifice of eternity" [047-023–024].

### IV — The Golden Bird

The final stanza imagines the form the speaker wishes to take after death — not a natural body, but "such a form as Grecian goldsmiths make / Of hammered gold and gold enamelling" [047-027–028]: a mechanical bird singing on a golden bough to "lords and ladies of Byzantium / Of what is past, or passing, or to come" [047-031–032]. This is Yeats's most concentrated image of art as transcendence — the artifice that outlasts nature, the song that survives the singer.

## Yeats's Byzantium

Yeats's Byzantium is not the historical city but a symbol. In *A Vision* (1925), he wrote: "I think that in early Byzantium, maybe never before or since in recorded history, religious, aesthetic and practical life were one." For Yeats, Byzantium circa 550 CE — the age of Justinian and the Hagia Sophia — represented a moment when the " artifice of eternity" and daily life were united, when craftsmen could produce the golden mosaics and mechanical birds that embodied the fusion of spirit and matter the poem celebrates.

The Byzantium symbol recurs in Yeats's later poem "Byzantium" (1930), which offers a darker, more complex vision of the same symbolic city — one haunted by "blood-begotten spirits" and the "mummy-cloth" of the winding sheet.

## Themes

- **Art versus nature**: The golden bird is the poem's central symbol of art's power to transcend the biological cycle of birth, copulation, and death.
- **Aging and the body**: The poem is a fierce, unsentimental reckoning with physical decay — the "dying animal" to which the soul is "fastened."
- **The soul's education**: The journey to Byzantium is a curriculum: the soul must learn to sing by "studying / Monuments of its own magnificence."
