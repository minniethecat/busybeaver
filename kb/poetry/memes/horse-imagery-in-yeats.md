## Introduction

The horse runs through Yeats' poetry as an image of vital energy, mythological power, and the relationship between human will and the forces it seeks to master. From the "horse with bridle of findrinny" [015-025] of the early narrative poems to the "galloping horses" [043-216] of the later philosophical lyrics, the equine image serves as a vehicle for Yeats' exploration of desire, violence, and the artist's struggle to bridle the unruly imagination. The horse in Yeats is never merely an animal — it is a creature of faery, a bearer of warriors, and a figure for that "Proteus" [043-198] of the creative mind that resists every bridle.

## The Horse in Mythological Narrative

The richest concentration of horse imagery appears in "The Wanderings of Usheen" (1889), Yeats' long narrative poem set in the Fenian cycle of Irish mythology. The hero rides "on a horse with bridle of findrinny" [015-025] — a silver-bronze alloy of Irish legend — through the three islands of his supernatural journey. The horse responds to Usheen's will with instinctive intelligence: "when the horse had felt my weight" [015-104] it knows its rider's intent. The horse is the vehicle of passage between worlds, carrying the mortal hero through the "Island of the Living," the "Island of Victories," and the "Island of Forgetfulness." In the climactic moments, the "horse of faery screamed" [015-433], its cry marking the dissolution of the supernatural realm as Usheen's mortal touch breaks the enchantment. The horses of the Fenian warriors appear in the final vision: "a hundred horsemen on the basalt floor" [015-504] and "the Fenian horses; armour torn asunder" [015-614] — the mounted warriors of Irish legend, frozen in their enchanted sleep beneath the earth.

## The Rider and the Bridle

In Yeats' later poetry, the horse-and-rider image becomes an explicit philosophical figure. In "The Faculties of the Soul" sequence, the poet asks: "Is there a bridle for this Proteus / That turns to changes?" [043-198], using the bridle — the instrument of equestrian control — as a metaphor for the artistic and philosophical discipline required to master the shape-shifting imagination. Proteus, the mythological figure of endless metamorphosis, resists every bridle just as the creative mind resists fixed form. The "riders upon the galloping horses" [043-216] in the same poem evoke the Platonic image of the charioteer governing the unruly horses of the soul — reason struggling to control passion. In "Meditations in Time of Civil War," "the rider, the birds that range" [046-209] appears as part of a catalogue of natural and human agents in motion, while in the late poem "The Circus Animals' Desertion," the poet laments that "the horses are dragon-ridden, the nightmare" [047-460] — the noble steeds of imagination now ridden by dark forces, the creative "nightmare" that rides the dreamer rather than being ridden by will.

## The Horse as Vital Force

Throughout Yeats' verse, the horse represents vitality — the life-force that is magnificent but requires discipline. The Fenian "horsemen with their floating hair" [015-012] embody a heroic ideal of untamed energy, while the "bridle-path" [015-771] of the later narrative suggests the narrow course that such energy must follow. In "The Tower," the image of the horse is internalized: the poet's own vital energies become the steed he must master in old age. The "handsome riders" [047-549] who are "garlanded" [047-549] in the late poems represent those who have successfully harnessed their vital powers — the antithesis of the "dragon-ridden" horses of creative exhaustion.
