# Death in Yeats

Death in Yeats's poetry is rarely a terminus. It is more often a threshold — a passage from one state of being to another, wrapped in the imagery of Irish folklore (the fairy death, the "straw-death"), heroic tradition (death in battle), and occult transformation (death as initiation). Yeats's treatment of death evolves from the haunted landscapes of early poems to the stoic confrontation of late works.

## Origins and Sources

- **Irish folklore**: The fairy death — a soul taken by the Sidhe, not truly dead but transported to the Otherworld. The fairy is "soulless" but does not die in the Christian sense.
- **Heroic tradition**: Death as the culmination of a warrior's life — "the deaths of many men" ((040-194)) in Irish saga
- **Occultism**: Death as transformation, the soul's passage through spheres — Golden Dawn teachings on spiritual evolution
- **Romantic death-longing**: The Keatsian desire for dissolution into beauty, transformed by Yeats's harder, more skeptical temperament

## Symbolic Manifestations

1. **The "straw-death"**: A specifically Irish folkloric concept — "waiting in patience the straw-death, croziered one" ((015-776)) — the death associated with Christianity (the crozier) that replaced the heroic pagan death.

2. **Death as sleep**: Drawing on classical and Romantic conventions, death is the "late languor" ((029-014)) from which the speaker writes — a liminal state between life and art.

3. **Death and love**: "death, oh my fair one, will never come" ((009-009)) — death is the shadow that gives love its urgency, the frame that makes mortal beauty precious.

4. **The death-bed**: In late Yeats, the death-bed becomes a stage for final reckoning — "thinking that when you come to the death-bed" ((044-360)).

## Development Across Yeats's Career

**Early period**: Death is folkloric and mythological — the fairy death, the heroic death, death as the end of the pagan world. The _Wanderings of Usheen_ (1889) posits death as the price of progress from pagan to Christian Ireland.

**Middle period**: Death becomes more personal. "Baile and Aillinn" (1903) uses death as the tragic frame for love — two lovers who die believing the other is dead.

**Late period**: Death hardens into stoic confrontation. "Responsibilities" (1914) and later poems treat death as an inevitability that must be faced without illusion. "Under Ben Bulben" would later command: "Cast a cold eye / On life, on death."
