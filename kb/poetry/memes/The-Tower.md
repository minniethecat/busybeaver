# The Tower

## Sailing to Byzantium

### I

[047-001]That is no country for old men. The young   

[047-002]In one another’s arms, birds in the trees,   

[047-003]—Those [[death-in-yeats|dying]] generations﻿—at their song,   

[047-004]The salmon-falls, the mackerel-crowded seas,   

[047-005]Fish flesh or fowl, commend all summer long   

[047-006]Whatever is begotten born and dies.   

[047-007]Caught in that sensual [[song-and-poetry|music]] all neglect   

[047-008]Monuments of unaging intellect.

### II

[047-009]An aged man is but a paltry thing,   

[047-010]A tattered coat upon a stick, unless   

[047-011][[soul-in-yeats|Soul]] clap its hands and sing, and louder sing   

[047-012]For every tatter in its mortal dress,   

[047-013]Nor is there singing school but studying   

[047-014]Monuments of its own magnificence;   

[047-015]And therefore I have sailed the seas and come   

[047-016]To the [[byzantium|holy city of Byzantium]].

### III

[047-017]O sages standing in God’s [[fire-passion|holy fire]]   

[047-018]As in the gold mosaic of a wall,   

[047-019]Come from the holy fire, [[gyres|perne]] in a [[gyres|gyre]],   

[047-020]And be the singing masters of my soul.   

[047-021]Consume my heart away; sick with [[love-in-yeats|desire]]   

[047-022]And fastened to a dying animal   

[047-023]It knows not what it is; and gather me   

[047-024]Into the artifice of [[time-and-eternity|eternity]].

### IV

[047-025]Once out of nature I shall never take   

[047-026]My bodily form from any natural thing,   

[047-027]But such a form as Grecian goldsmiths make   

[047-028]Of hammered gold and gold enamelling   

[047-029]To keep a drowsy emperor awake;   

[047-030]Or set upon a golden bough to sing   

[047-031]To lords and ladies of [[byzantium|Byzantium]]   

[047-032]Of what is past, or passing, or to come.[25](endnotes.xhtml#note-25)

## The Tower

### I

[047-033]What shall I do with this absurdity﻿—   

[047-034]O heart, O troubled heart﻿—this caricature,   

[047-035]Decrepit age that has been tied to me   

[047-036]As to a dog’s tail? Never had I more   

[047-037]Excited, passionate, fantastical   

[047-038]Imagination, nor an ear and eye   

[047-039]That more expected the impossible﻿—   

[047-040]No, not in boyhood when with rod and fly,   

[047-041]Or the humbler worm, I climbed [[ben-bulben-mountain|Ben Bulben]]’s back   

[047-042]And had the livelong summer day to spend.   

[047-043]It seems that I must bid the Muse go pack,   

[047-044]Choose [[plato|Plato]] and [[platonic-tradition|Plotinus]] for a friend   

[047-045]Until imagination, ear and eye,   

[047-046]Can be content with argument and deal   

[047-047]In abstract things; or be derided by   

[047-048]A sort of battered kettle at the heel.

### II[26](endnotes.xhtml#note-26)

[047-049]I pace upon the battlements and stare   

[047-050]On the foundations of a house, or where   

[047-051]Tree, like a sooty finger, starts from the earth;   

[047-052]And send imagination forth   

[047-053]Under the day’s declining beam, and call   

[047-054]Images and memories   

[047-055]From ruin or from ancient trees,   

[047-056]For I would ask a question of them all.

[047-057]Beyond that ridge lived Mrs. French, and once   

[047-058]When every silver candlestick or sconce   

[047-059]Lit up the dark mahogany and the wine,   

[047-060]A serving man that could divine   

[047-061]That most respected lady’s every wish,   

[047-062]Ran and with the garden shears   

[047-063]Clipped an insolent farmer’s ears   

[047-064]And brought them in a little covered dish.

[047-065]Some few remembered still when I was young   

[047-066]A peasant girl commended by a song,   

[047-067]Who’d lived somewhere upon that rocky place,   

[047-068]And praised the colour of her face,   

[047-069]And had the greater joy in praising her,   

[047-070]Remembering that, if walked she there,   

[047-071]Farmers jostled at the fair   

[047-072]So great a glory did the song confer.

[047-073]And certain men, being maddened by those rhymes,   

[047-074]Or else by toasting her a score of times,   

[047-075][[rose-symbol|Rose]] from the table and declared it right   

[047-076]To test their fancy by their sight;   

[047-077]But they mistook the brightness of the moon   

[047-078]For the prosaic [[shadow-and-light|light]] of day﻿—   

[047-079]Music had driven their wits astray﻿—   

[047-080]And one was drowned in the great bog of Cloone.

[047-081]Strange, but the man who made the song was blind,   

[047-082]Yet, now I have considered it, I find   

[047-083]That nothing strange; the tragedy began   

[047-084]With Homer that was a blind man,   

[047-085]And Helen has all living hearts betrayed.   

[047-086]O may the moon and sunlight seem   

[047-087]One inextricable beam,   

[047-088]For if I triumph I must make men mad.

[047-089]And I myself created Hanrahan   

[047-090]And drove him drunk or sober through the dawn   

[047-091]From somewhere in the neighbouring cottages.   

[047-092]Caught by an old man’s juggleries   

[047-093]He stumbled, tumbled, fumbled to and fro   

[047-094]And had but broken knees for hire   

[047-095]And horrible splendour of desire;   

[047-096]I thought it all out twenty years ago:

[047-097]Good fellows shuffled cards in an old bawn;   

[047-098]And when that ancient ruffian’s turn was on   

[047-099]He so bewitched the cards under his thumb   

[047-100]That all, but the one card, became   

[047-101]A pack of hounds and not a pack of cards,   

[047-102]And that he changed into a hare.   

[047-103]Hanrahan rose in frenzy there   

[047-104]And followed up those baying creatures towards﻿—

[047-105]O towards I have forgotten what﻿—enough!   

[047-106]I must recall a man that neither love   

[047-107]Nor music nor an enemy’s clipped ear   

[047-108]Could, he was so harried, cheer;   

[047-109]A figure that has grown so fabulous   

[047-110]There’s not a neighbour left to say   

[047-111]When he finished his dog’s day:   

[047-112]An ancient bankrupt master of this house.

[047-113]Before that ruin came, for centuries,   

[047-114]Rough men-at-arms, cross-gartered to the knees   

[047-115]Or shod in iron, climbed the narrow stairs,   

[047-116]And certain men-at-arms there were   

[047-117]Whose images, in the Great [[memory-in-yeats|Memory]] stored,   

[047-118]Come with loud cry and panting breast   

[047-119]To break upon a sleeper’s rest   

[047-120]While their great wooden dice beat on the board.

[047-121]As I would question all, come all who can;   

[047-122]Come old, necessitous, half-mounted man;   

[047-123]And bring beauty’s blind rambling celebrant;   

[047-124]The red man the juggler sent   

[047-125]Through God-forsaken meadows; Mrs. French,   

[047-126]Gifted with so fine an ear;   

[047-127]The man drowned in a bog’s mire,   

[047-128]When mocking muses chose the country wench.

[047-129]Did all old men and women, rich and poor,   

[047-130]Who trod upon these rocks or passed this door,   

[047-131]Whether in public or in secret rage   

[047-132]As I do now against old age?   

[047-133]But I have found an answer in those eyes   

[047-134]That are impatient to be gone;   

[047-135]Go therefore; but leave Hanrahan   

[047-136]For I need all his mighty memories.

[047-137]Old lecher with a love on every wind   

[047-138]Bring up out of that deep considering mind   

[047-139]All that you have discovered in the grave,   

[047-140]For it is certain that you have   

[047-141]Reckoned up every unforeknown, unseeing   

[047-142]Plunge, lured by a softening eye,   

[047-143]Or by a touch or a sigh,   

[047-144]Into the labyrinth of another’s being;

[047-145]Does the imagination dwell the most   

[047-146]Upon a woman won or woman lost?   

[047-147]If on the lost, admit you turned aside   

[047-148]From a great labyrinth out of pride,   

[047-149]Cowardice, some silly over-subtle thought   

[047-150]Or anything called conscience once;   

[047-151]And that if memory recur, the sun’s   

[047-152]Under eclipse and the day blotted out.

### III[27](endnotes.xhtml#note-27)

[047-153]It is time that I wrote my will;   

[047-154]I choose upstanding men,   

[047-155]That climb the streams until   

[047-156]The [[water-imagery|fountain]] leap, and at dawn   

[047-157]Drop their cast at the side   

[047-158]Of dripping stone; I declare   

[047-159]They shall inherit my pride,   

[047-160]The pride of people that were   

[047-161]Bound neither to Cause nor to State,   

[047-162]Neither to slaves that were spat on,   

[047-163]Nor to the tyrants that spat,   

[047-164]The people of Burke and of Grattan   

[047-165]That gave, though free to refuse﻿—   

[047-166]Pride, like that of the morn,   

[047-167]When the headlong light is loose,   

[047-168]Or that of the fabulous horn,   

[047-169]Or that of the sudden shower   

[047-170]When all streams are dry,   

[047-171]Or that of the hour   

[047-172]When the swan must fix his eye   

[047-173]Upon a fading gleam,   

[047-174]Float out upon a long   

[047-175]Last reach of glittering stream   

[047-176]And there sing his last song.   

[047-177]And I declare my faith;   

[047-178]I mock [[plotinus|Plotinus]]’ thought   

[047-179]And cry in Plato’s teeth,   

[047-180]Death and life were not   

[047-181]Till man made up the whole,   

[047-182]Made lock, stock and barrel   

[047-183]Out of his bitter soul,   

[047-184]Aye, sun and moon and star, all,   

[047-185]And further add to that   

[047-186]That, being dead, we rise,   

[047-187][[dream-and-vision|Dream]] and so create   

[047-188]Translunar Paradise.   

[047-189]I have prepared my peace   

[047-190]With learned Italian things   

[047-191]And the proud stones of Greece,   

[047-192]Poet’s imaginings   

[047-193]And memories of love,   

[047-194]Memories of the words of women,   

[047-195]All those things whereof   

[047-196]Man makes a superhuman,   

[047-197]Mirror-resembling dream.

[047-198]As at the loophole there,   

[047-199]The daws chatter and scream,   

[047-200]And drop twigs layer upon layer.   

[047-201]When they have mounted up,   

[047-202]The mother bird will rest   

[047-203]On their hollow top,   

[047-204]And so warm her wild nest.

[047-205]I leave both faith and pride   

[047-206]To young upstanding men   

[047-207]Climbing the mountain side,   

[047-208]That under bursting dawn   

[047-209]They may drop a fly;   

[047-210]Being of that metal made   

[047-211]Till it was broken by   

[047-212]This sedentary trade.

[047-213]Now shall I make my soul   

[047-214]Compelling it to study   

[047-215]In a learned school   

[047-216]Till the wreck of body,   

[047-217]Slow decay of blood,   

[047-218]Testy delirium   

[047-219]Or dull decrepitude,   

[047-220]Or what worse evil come﻿—   

[047-221]The death of friends, or death   

[047-222]Of every brilliant eye   

[047-223]That made a catch in the breath﻿—   

[047-224]Seem but the clouds of the sky   

[047-225]When the horizon fades;   

[047-226]Or a bird’s sleepy cry   

[047-227]Among the deepening shades.

## Meditations in Time of Civil War[28](endnotes.xhtml#note-28)

### I

[047-228]Ancestral Houses

[047-229]Surely among a rich man’s flowering lawns,   

[047-230]Amid the rustle of his planted hills,   

[047-231]Life overflows without ambitious pains;   

[047-232]And rains down life until the basin spills,   

[047-233]And mounts more dizzy high the more it rains   

[047-234]As though to choose whatever shape it wills   

[047-235]And never stoop to a mechanical,   

[047-236]Or servile shape, at others’ beck and call.

[047-237]Mere dreams, mere dreams! Yet Homer had not sung   

[047-238]Had he not found it certain beyond dreams   

[047-239]That out of life’s own self-delight had sprung   

[047-240]The abounding glittering jet; though now it seems   

[047-241]As if some marvellous empty sea-shell flung   

[047-242]Out of the obscure dark of the rich streams,   

[047-243]And not a fountain, were the symbol which   

[047-244]Shadows the inherited glory of the rich.

[047-245]Some violent bitter man, some powerful man   

[047-246]Called architect and artist in, that they,   

[047-247]Bitter and violent men, might rear in stone   

[047-248]The sweetness that all longed for night and day,   

[047-249]The gentleness none there had ever known;   

[047-250]But when the master’s buried mice can play,   

[047-251]And maybe the great-grandson of that house,   

[047-252]For all its bronze and [[stone-imagery|marble]], ’s but a mouse.

[047-253]Oh, what if gardens where the peacock strays   

[047-254]With delicate feet upon old terraces,   

[047-255]Or else all Juno from an urn displays   

[047-256]Before the indifferent garden deities;   

[047-257]Oh, what if levelled lawns and gravelled ways   

[047-258]Where slippered Contemplation finds his ease   

[047-259]And Childhood a delight for every sense,   

[047-260]But take our greatness with our violence!

[047-261]What if the glory of escutcheoned doors,   

[047-262]And buildings that a haughtier age designed,   

[047-263]The pacing to and fro on polished floors   

[047-264]Amid great chambers and long galleries, lined   

[047-265]With famous portraits of our ancestors;   

[047-266]What if those things the greatest of mankind,   

[047-267]Consider most to magnify, or to bless,   

[047-268]But take our greatness with our bitterness!

### II

[047-269]My House

[047-270]An ancient bridge, and a more ancient tower,   

[047-271]A farmhouse that is sheltered by its wall,   

[047-272]An acre of stony ground,   

[047-273]Where the symbolic rose can break in flower,   

[047-274]Old ragged elms, old thorns innumerable,   

[047-275]The sound of the rain or sound   

[047-276]Of every wind that blows;   

[047-277]The stilted water-hen   

[047-278]Crossing stream again   

[047-279]Scared by the splashing of a dozen cows;

[047-280]A [[winding-stair|winding stair]], a chamber arched with stone,   

[047-281]A grey stone fireplace with an open hearth,   

[047-282]A candle and written page.   

[047-283]“Il Penseroso”’s Platonist toiled on   

[047-284]In some like chamber, shadowing forth   

[047-285]How the daemonic rage   

[047-286]Imagined everything.   

[047-287]Benighted travellers   

[047-288]From markets and from fairs   

[047-289]Have seen his midnight candle glimmering.

[047-290]Two men have founded here. A man-at-arms   

[047-291]Gathered a score of horse and spent his days   

[047-292]In this tumultuous spot,   

[047-293]Where through long wars and sudden night alarms   

[047-294]His dwindling score and he seemed cast-a-ways   

[047-295]Forgetting and forgot;   

[047-296]And I, that after me   

[047-297]My bodily heirs may find,   

[047-298]To exalt a lonely mind,   

[047-299]Befitting emblems of adversity.

### III

[047-300]My Table

[047-301]Two heavy tressels, and a board   

[047-302]Where Sato’s gift, a changeless sword,   

[047-303]By pen and paper lies,   

[047-304]That it may moralise   

[047-305]My days out of their aimlessness.   

[047-306]A bit of an embroidered dress   

[047-307]Covers its wooden sheath.   

[047-308]Chaucer had not drawn breath   

[047-309]When it was forged. In Sato’s house,   

[047-310]Curved like new moon, moon luminous   

[047-311]It lay five hundred years.   

[047-312]Yet if no change appears   

[047-313]No moon; only an aching heart   

[047-314]Conceives a changeless work of art.   

[047-315]Our learned men have urged   

[047-316]That when and where ’twas forged   

[047-317]A marvellous accomplishment,   

[047-318]In painting or in pottery, went   

[047-319]From father unto son   

[047-320]And through the centuries ran   

[047-321]And seemed unchanging like the sword.   

[047-322]Soul’s beauty being most adored,   

[047-323]Men and their business took   

[047-324]The soul’s unchanging look;   

[047-325]For the most rich inheritor,   

[047-326]Knowing that none could pass heaven’s door   

[047-327]That loved inferior art,   

[047-328]Had such an aching heart   

[047-329]That he, although a country’s talk   

[047-330]For silken clothes and stately walk,   

[047-331]Had waking wits; it seemed   

[047-332]Juno’s peacock screamed.

### IV

[047-333]My Descendants

[047-334]Having inherited a vigorous mind   

[047-335]From my old fathers I must nourish dreams   

[047-336]And leave a woman and a man behind   

[047-337]As vigorous of mind, and yet it seems   

[047-338]Life scarce can cast a fragrance on the wind,   

[047-339]Scarce spread a glory to the morning beams,   

[047-340]But the torn petals strew the garden plot;   

[047-341]And there’s but common greenness after that.

[047-342]And what if my descendants lose the flower   

[047-343]Through natural declension of the soul,   

[047-344]Through too much business with the passing hour,   

[047-345]Through too much play, or marriage with a fool?   

[047-346]May this laborious stair and this stark tower   

[047-347]Become a roofless ruin that the owl   

[047-348]May build in the cracked masonry and cry   

[047-349]Her desolation to the desolate sky.

[047-350]The Primum Mobile that fashioned us   

[047-351]Has made the very owls in circles move;   

[047-352]And I, that count myself most prosperous,   

[047-353]Seeing that love and friendship are enough,   

[047-354]For an old neighbour’s friendship chose the house   

[047-355]And decked and altered it for a girl’s love,   

[047-356]And know whatever flourish and decline   

[047-357]These stones remain their monument and mine.

### V

[047-358]The Road at My Door

[047-359]An affable Irregular,   

[047-360]A heavily built Falstaffan man,   

[047-361]Comes cracking jokes of civil war   

[047-362]As though to die by gunshot were   

[047-363]The finest play under the sun.

[047-364]A brown Lieutenant and his men,   

[047-365]Half dressed in national uniform,   

[047-366]Stand at my door, and I complain   

[047-367]Of the foul weather, hail and rain,   

[047-368]A pear tree broken by the storm.

[047-369]I count those feathered balls of soot   

[047-370]The moor-hen guides upon the stream,   

[047-371]To silence the envy in my thought;   

[047-372]And turn towards my chamber, caught   

[047-373]In the cold snows of a dream.

### VI[29](endnotes.xhtml#note-29)

[047-374]The Stare’s Nest by My Window

[047-375]The bees build in the crevices   

[047-376]Of loosening masonry, and there   

[047-377]The mother birds bring grubs and flies.   

[047-378]My wall is loosening; honey-bees   

[047-379]Come build in the empty house of the stare.

[047-380]We are closed in, and the key is turned   

[047-381]On our uncertainty; somewhere   

[047-382]A man is killed, or a house burned,   

[047-383]Yet no clear fact to be discerned:   

[047-384]Come build in the empty house of the stare.

[047-385]A barricade of stone or of wood;   

[047-386]Some fourteen days of civil war;   

[047-387]Last night they trundled down the road   

[047-388]That dead young soldier in his blood:   

[047-389]Come build in the empty house of the stare.

[047-390]We had fed the heart on fantasies,   

[047-391]The heart’s grown brutal from the fare,   

[047-392]More substance in our enmities   

[047-393]Than in our love; oh, honey-bees   

[047-394]Come build in the empty house of the stare.

### VII

[047-395]I See Phantoms of Hatred and of the Heart’s Fullness and of the Coming Emptiness

[047-396]I climb to the tower top and lean upon broken stone,   

[047-397]A mist that is like blown snow is sweeping over all,   

[047-398]Valley, river, and elms, under the light of a moon   

[047-399]That seems unlike itself, that seems unchangeable,   

[047-400]A glittering sword out of the east. A puff of wind   

[047-401]And those white glimmering fragments of the mist sweep by.   

[047-402]Frenzies bewilder, reveries perturb the mind;   

[047-403]Monstrous familiar images swim to the mind’s eye.

[047-404]“Vengeance upon the murderers,” the cry goes up,   

[047-405]“Vengeance for Jacques Molay.”[30](endnotes.xhtml#note-30) In cloud-pale rags, or in lace,   

[047-406]The rage driven, rage tormented, and rage hungry troop,   

[047-407]Trooper belabouring trooper, biting at arm or at face,   

[047-408]Plunges towards nothing, arms and fingers spreading wide   

[047-409]For the embrace of nothing; and I, my wits astray   

[047-410]Because of all that senseless tumult, all but cried   

[047-411]For vengeance on the murderers of Jacques Molay.

[047-412]Their legs long delicate and slender, aquamarine their eyes,   

[047-413]Magical unicorns bear ladies on their backs,   

[047-414]The ladies close their musing eyes. No prophecies,   

[047-415]Remembered out of Babylonian almanacs,   

[047-416]Have closed the ladies’ eyes, their minds are but a pool   

[047-417]Where even longing drowns under its own excess;   

[047-418]Nothing but stillness can remain when hearts are full   

[047-419]Of their own sweetness, bodies of their loveliness.

[047-420]The cloud-pale unicorns, the eyes of aquamarine,   

[047-421]The quivering half-closed eyelids, the rags of cloud or of lace,   

[047-422]Or eyes that rage has brightened, arms it has made lean,   

[047-423]Give place to an indifferent multitude, give place   

[047-424]To brazen hawks.[31](endnotes.xhtml#note-31) Nor self-delighting reverie,   

[047-425]Nor hate of what’s to come, nor pity for what’s gone,   

[047-426]Nothing but grip of claw, and the eye’s complacency,   

[047-427]The innumerable clanging wings that have put out the moon.

[047-428]I turn away and shut the door, and on the stair   

[047-429]Wonder how many times I could have proved my worth   

[047-430]In something that all others understand or share;   

[047-431]But oh, ambitious heart had such a proof drawn forth   

[047-432]A company of friends, a conscience set at ease,   

[047-433]It had but made us pine the more. The abstract joy,   

[047-434]The half read wisdom of daemonic images,   

[047-435]Suffice the ageing man as once the growing boy.

## Nineteen Hundred and Nineteen

### I

[047-436]Many ingenious lovely things are gone   

[047-437]That seemed sheer miracle to the multitude,   

[047-438]Protected from the circle of the moon   

[047-439]That pitches common things about. There stood   

[047-440]Amid the ornamental bronze and stone   

[047-441]An ancient image made of olive wood﻿—   

[047-442]And gone are Phidias’ famous ivories   

[047-443]And all the golden grasshoppers and bees.

[047-444]We too had many pretty toys when young;   

[047-445]A law indifferent to blame or praise,   

[047-446]To bribe or threat; habits that made old wrong   

[047-447]Melt down, as it were wax in the sun’s rays;   

[047-448]Public opinion ripening for so long   

[047-449]We thought it would outlive all future days.   

[047-450]O what fine thought we had because we thought   

[047-451]That the worst rogues and rascals had died out.

[047-452]All teeth were drawn, all ancient tricks unlearned,   

[047-453]And a great army but a showy thing;   

[047-454]What matter that no cannon had been turned   

[047-455]Into a ploughshare; parliament and king   

[047-456]Thought that unless a little powder burned   

[047-457]The trumpeters might burst with trumpeting   

[047-458]And yet it lack all glory; and perchance   

[047-459]The guardsmen’s drowsy chargers would not prance.

[047-460]Now days are dragon-ridden, the nightmare   

[047-461]Rides upon sleep: a drunken soldiery   

[047-462]Can leave the mother, murdered at her door,   

[047-463]To crawl in her own blood, and go scot-free;   

[047-464]The night can sweat with terror as before

[047-935]Madge tells me what I dared not think   

[047-936]When my blood was strong,

[047-945]How such a man pleased women most

[047-949]Stories of the bed of straw

### His Wildness

[047-951]O bid me mount and sail up there   

[047-952]Amid the cloudy wrack,   

[047-953]For Peg and Meg and Paris’ love   

[047-954]That had so straight a back,   

[047-955]Are gone away, and some that stay,   

[047-956]Have changed their silk for sack.

[047-957]Were I but there and none to hear   

[047-958]I’d have a peacock cry   

[047-959]For that is natural to a man

## The Three Monuments

[047-963]They hold their public meetings where   

[047-964]Our most renowned patriots stand,   

[047-965]One among the birds of the air,   

[047-966]A stumpier on either hand;   

[047-967]And all the popular statesmen say   

[047-968]That purity built up the state   

[047-969]And after kept it from decay;   

[047-970]Admonish us to cling to that

## From _Oedipus at Colonus_

### I

[047-975]Endure what life God gives and ask no longer span;   

[047-976]Cease to remember the delights of youth, travel-wearied aged man;   

[047-977]Delight becomes death-longing if all longing else be vain.

### II

[047-978]Even from that delight memory treasures so,   

[047-979]Death, despair, division of families, all entanglements of mankind grow,   

[047-980]As that old wandering beggar and these God-hated children know.

### III

[047-981]In the long echoing street the laughing dancers throng,   

[047-982]The bride is carried to the bridegroom’s chamber through torchlight and tumultuous song;   

[047-983]I celebrate the silent kiss that ends short life or long.

### IV

[047-984]Never to have lived is best, ancient writers say;   

[047-985]Never to have drawn the breath of life, never to have looked into the eye of day;   

[047-986]The second best’s a gay goodnight and quickly turn away.

## The Gift of Harun-al-Rashid[34](endnotes.xhtml#note-34)

[047-987]Kusta ben Luka is my name, I write   

[047-988]To Abd al-Rabban; fellow roysterer once,   

[047-989]Now the good Caliph’s learned Treasurer,   

[047-990]And for no ear but his. Carry this letter   

[047-991]Through the great gallery of the Treasure House   

[047-992]Where banners of the Caliphs hang, night-coloured   

[047-993]But brilliant as the night’s embroidery,   

[047-994]And wait war’s music; pass the little gallery;   

[047-995]Pass books of learning from Byzantium   

[047-996]Written in gold upon a purple stain,   

[047-997]And pause at last, I was about to say,   

[047-998]At the great book of Sappho’s song; but no,   

[047-999]For should you leave my letter there, a boy’s   

[047-1000]Love-lorn, indifferent hands might come upon it   

[047-1001]And let it fall unnoticed to the floor.   

[047-1002]Pause at the Treatise of Parmenides   

[047-1003]And hide it there, for Caliphs to world’s end   

[047-1004]Must keep that perfect, as they keep her song   

[047-1005]So great its fame. When fitting time has passed   

[047-1006]The parchment will disclose to some learned man   

[047-1007]A mystery that else had found no chronicler   

[047-1008]But the wild Bedouin. Though I approve   

[047-1009]Those wanderers that welcomed in their tents   

[047-1010]What great Harun al-Rashid, occupied   

[047-1011]With Persian embassy or Grecian war,   

[047-1012]Must needs neglect; I cannot hide the truth   

[047-1013]That wandering in a desert, featureless   

[047-1014]As air under a wing, can give birds’ wit.   

[047-1015]In after time they will speak much of me   

[047-1016]And speak but fantasy. Recall the year   

[047-1017]When our beloved Caliph put to death   

[047-1018]His Vizir Jaffer for an unknown reason;   

[047-1019]“If but the shirt upon my body knew it   

[047-1020]I’d tear it off and throw it in the fire.”   

[047-1021]That speech was all that the town knew, but he   

[047-1022]Seemed for a while to have grown young again;   

[047-1023]Seemed so on purpose, muttered Jaffer’s friends,   

[047-1024]That none might know that he was conscience struck﻿—   

[047-1025]But that’s a traitor’s thought. Enough for me   

[047-1026]That in the early summer of the year   

[047-1027]The mightiest of the princes of the world   

[047-1028]Came to the least considered of his courtiers;   

[047-1029]Sat down upon the fountain’s marble edge   

[047-1030]One hand amid the goldfish in the pool;   

[047-1031]And thereupon a colloquy took place   

[047-1032]That I commend to all the chroniclers   

[047-1033]To show how violent great hearts can lose   

[047-1034]Their bitterness and find the honey-comb.   

[047-1035]“I have brought a slender bride into the house;   

[047-1036]You know the saying ‘Change the bride with Spring,’   

[047-1037]And she and I, being sunk in happiness,   

[047-1038]Cannot endure to think you tread these paths,   

[047-1039]When evening stirs the jasmine, and yet   

[047-1040]Are brideless.” “I am falling into years.”

[047-1041]“But such as you and I do not seem old   

[047-1042]Like men who live by habit. Every day   

[047-1043]I ride with falcon to the river’s edge   

[047-1044]Or carry the ringed mail upon my back,   

[047-1045]Or court a woman; neither enemy,   

[047-1046]Game-bird, nor woman does the same thing twice;   

[047-1047]And so a hunter carries in the eye   

[047-1048]A mimicry of youth. Can poet’s thought   

[047-1049]That springs from body and in body falls   

[047-1050]Like this pure jet, now lost amid blue sky   

[047-1051]Now bathing lily leaf and fishes’ scale,   

[047-1052]Be mimicry?” “What matter if our souls   

[047-1053]Are nearer to the surface of the body   

[047-1054]Than souls that start no game and turn no rhyme!   

[047-1055]The soul’s own youth and not the body’s youth   

[047-1056]Shows through our lineaments. My candle’s bright,   

[047-1057]My lantern is too loyal not to show   

[047-1058]That it was made in your great father’s reign.”

[047-1059]“And yet the jasmine season warms our blood.”

[047-1060]“Great prince, forgive the freedom of my speech;   

[047-1061]You think that love has seasons, and you think   

[047-1062]That if the spring bear off what the spring gave   

[047-1063]The heart need suffer no defeat; but I   

[047-1064]Who have accepted the Byzantine faith,   

[047-1065]That seems unnatural to Arabian minds,   

[047-1066]Think when I choose a bride I choose for ever;   

[047-1067]And if her eye should not grow bright for mine   

[047-1068]Or brighten only for some younger eye,   

[047-1069]My heart could never turn from daily ruin,   

[047-1070]Nor find a remedy.” “But what if I   

[047-1071]Have lit upon a woman, who so shares   

[047-1072]Your thirst for those old crabbed mysteries,   

[047-1073]So strains to look beyond our life, an eye   

[047-1074]That never knew that strain would scarce seem bright,   

[047-1075]And yet herself can seem youth’s very fountain,   

[047-1076]Being all brimmed with life.” “Were it but true   

[047-1077]I would have found the best that life can give,   

[047-1078]Companionship in those mysterious things   

[047-1079]That make a man’s soul or a woman’s soul   

[047-1080]Itself and not some other soul.” “That love   

[047-1081]Must needs be in this life and in what follows   

[047-1082]Unchanging and at peace, and it is right   

[047-1083]Every philosopher should praise that love.   

[047-1084]But I being none can praise its opposite.   

[047-1085]It makes my passion stronger but to think   

[047-1086]Like passion stirs the peacock and his mate,   

[047-1087]The wild stag and the doe; that mouth to mouth   

[047-1088]Is a man’s mockery of the changeless soul.”   

[047-1089]And thereupon his bounty gave what now   

[047-1090]Can shake more blossom from autumnal chill   

[047-1091]Than all my bursting springtime knew. A girl   

[047-1092]Perched in some window of her mother’s house   

[047-1093]Had watched my daily passage to and fro;   

[047-1094]Had heard impossible history of my past;   

[047-1095]Imagined some impossible history   

[047-1096]Lived at my side; thought time’s disfiguring touch   

[047-1097]Gave but more reason for a woman’s care.   

[047-1098]Yet was it love of me, or was it love   

[047-1099]Of the stark mystery that has dazed my sight,   

[047-1100]Perplexed her fantasy and planned her care?   

[047-1101]Or did the torchlight of that mystery   

[047-1102]Pick out my features in such light and shade   

[047-1103]Two contemplating passions chose one theme   

[047-1104]Through sheer bewilderment? She had not paced   

[047-1105]The garden paths, nor counted up the rooms,   

[047-1106]Before she had spread a book upon her knees   

[047-1107]And asked about the pictures or the text;   

[047-1108]And often those first days I saw her stare   

[047-1109]On old dry writing in a learned tongue,   

[047-1110]On old dry faggots that could never please   

[047-1111]The extravagance of spring; or move a hand   

[047-1112]As if that writing or the figured page   

[047-1113]Were some dear cheek. Upon a moonless night   

[047-1114]I sat where I could watch her sleeping form,   

[047-1115]And wrote by candle-light; but her form moved,   

[047-1116]And fearing that my light disturbed her sleep   

[047-1117]I rose that I might screen it with a cloth.   

[047-1118]I heard her voice, “Turn that I may expound   

[047-1119]What’s bowed your shoulder and made pale your cheek”;   

[047-1120]And saw her sitting upright on the bed;   

[047-1121]Or was it she that spoke or some great Djinn?   

[047-1122]I say that a Djinn spoke. A live-long hour   

[047-1123]She seemed the learned man and I the child;   

[047-1124]Truths without father came, truths that no book   

[047-1125]Of all the uncounted books that I have read,   

[047-1126]Nor thought out of her mind or mine begot,   

[047-1127]Self-born, high-born, and solitary truths,   

[047-1128]Those terrible implacable straight lines   

[047-1129]Drawn through the wandering vegetative dream,   

[047-1130]Even those truths that when my bones are dust   

[047-1131]Must drive the Arabian host. The voice grew still,   

[047-1132]And she lay down upon her bed and slept,   

[047-1133]But woke at the first gleam of day, rose up   

[047-1134]And swept the house and sang about her work   

[047-1135]In childish ignorance of all that passed.   

[047-1136]A dozen nights of natural sleep, and then   

[047-1137]When the full moon swam to its greatest height   

[047-1138]She rose, and with her eyes shut fast in sleep   

[047-1139]Walked through the house. Unnoticed and unfelt   

[047-1140]I wrapped her in a heavy hooded cloak, and she,   

[047-1141]Half running, dropped at the first ridge of the desert   

[047-1142]And there marked out those emblems on the sand   

[047-1143]That day by day I study and marvel at,   

[047-1144]With her white finger. I led her home asleep   

[047-1145]And once again she rose and swept the house   

[047-1146]In childish ignorance of all that passed.   

[047-1147]Even to-day, after some seven years   

[047-1148]When maybe thrice in every moon her mouth   

[047-1149]Murmured the wisdom of the desert Djinns,   

[047-1150]She keeps that ignorance, nor has she now   

[047-1151]That first unnatural interest in my books.   

[047-1152]It seems enough that I am there; and yet   

[047-1153]Old fellow student, whose most patient ear   

[047-1154]Heard all the anxiety of my passionate youth,   

[047-1155]It seems I must buy knowledge with my peace.   

[047-1156]What if she lose her ignorance and so   

[047-1157]Dream that I love her only for the voice,   

[047-1158]That every gift and every word of praise   

[047-1159]Is but a payment for that midnight voice   

[047-1160]That is to age what milk is to a child!   

[047-1161]Were she to lose her love, because she had lost   

[047-1162]Her confidence in mine, or even lose   

[047-1163]Its first simplicity, love, voice and all,   

[047-1164]All my fine feathers would be plucked away   

[047-1165]And I left shivering. The voice has drawn   

[047-1166]A quality of wisdom from her love’s   

[047-1167]Particular quality. The signs and shapes;   

[047-1168]All those abstractions that you fancied were   

[047-1169]From the great treatise of Parmenides;   

[047-1170]All, all those gyres and cubes and midnight things   

[047-1171]Are but a new expression of her body   

[047-1172]Drunk with the bitter sweetness of her youth.   

[047-1173]And now my utmost mystery is out.   

[047-1174]A woman’s beauty is a storm-tossed banner;   

[047-1175]Under it wisdom stands, and I alone﻿—   

[047-1176]Of all Arabia’s lovers I alone﻿—   

[047-1177]Nor dazzled by the embroidery, nor lost   

[047-1178]In the confusion of its night-dark folds,   

[047-1179]Can hear the armed man speak.

## All Souls’ Night

[047-1180]An Epilogue to _[[a-vision|A Vision]]_

[047-1181]Midnight has come and the great Christ Church Bell,   

[047-1182]And may a lesser bell, sound through the room;   

[047-1183]And it is All Souls’ Night,   

[047-1184]And two long glasses brimmed with muscatel   

[047-1185]Bubble upon the table. A ghost may come;   

[047-1186]For it is a ghost’s right,   

[047-1187]His element is so fine   

[047-1188]Being sharpened by his death,   

[047-1189]To drink from the wine-breath   

[047-1190]While our gross palates drink from the whole wine.

[047-1191]I need some mind that, if the cannon sound   

[047-1192]From every quarter of the world, can stay   

[047-1193]Wound in mind’s pondering,   

[047-1194]As mummies in the mummy-cloth are wound;   

[047-1195]Because I have a marvellous thing to say,   

[047-1196]A certain marvellous thing   

[047-1197]None but the living mock,   

[047-1198]Though not for sober ear;   

[047-1199]It may be all that hear   

[047-1200]Should laugh and weep an hour upon the clock.
