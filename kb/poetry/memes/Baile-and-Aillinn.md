# Baile and Aillinn

[040-001]_Argument._ Baile and Aillinn were lovers, but [[aengus-god|Aengus]], the Master of [[love-in-yeats|Love]], wishing them to be happy in his own land among the dead, told to each a story of the other’s death, so that their hearts were broken and they died.

[040-002]I hardly hear the curlew cry,   

[040-003]Nor the grey rush when the wind is high,   

[040-004]Before my thoughts begin to run   

[040-005]On the heir of Ulad, Buan’s son,   

[040-006]Baile, who had the honey mouth;   

[040-007]And that mild woman of the south,   

[040-008]Aillinn, who was King Lugaid’s heir.   

[040-009]Their love was never drowned in care   

[040-010]Of this or that thing, nor grew cold   

[040-011]Because their bodies had grown old.   

[040-012]Being forbid to marry on earth,   

[040-013]They blossomed to immortal mirth.

[040-014][[About]] the time when Christ was born,   

[040-015]When the long wars for the White Horn   

[040-016]And the Brown Bull had not yet come,   

[040-017]Young Baile Honey-Mouth, whom some   

[040-018]Called rather Baile Little-Land,   

[040-019]Rode out of Emain with a band   

[040-020]Of harpers and young men; and they   

[040-021]Imagined, as they struck the way   

[040-022]To many-pastured Muirthemne,   

[040-023]That all things fell out happily,   

[040-024]And there, for all that fools had said,   

[040-025]Baile and Aillinn would be wed.

[040-026]They found an old man running there:   

[040-027]He had ragged long grass-coloured hair;   

[040-028]He had knees that stuck out of his hose;   

[040-029]He had puddle water in his shoes;   

[040-030]He had half a cloak to keep him dry,   

[040-031]Although he had a squirrel’s eye.

[040-032]O wandering birds and rushy beds,   

[040-033]You put such folly in our heads   

[040-034]With all this crying in the wind;   

[040-035]No common love is to our mind,   

[040-036]And our poor Kate or Nan is less   

[040-037]Than any whose unhappiness   

[040-038]Awoke the harp-strings long ago.   

[040-039]Yet they that know all things but know   

[040-040]That all life had to give us is   

[040-041]A child’s laughter, a woman’s kiss.   

[040-042]Who was it put so great a scorn   

[040-043]In the grey reeds that night and morn   

[040-044]Are trodden and broken by the herds,   

[040-045]And in the [[shadow-and-light|light]] bodies of birds   

[040-046]That north wind tumbles to and fro   

[040-047]And pinches among hail and snow?

[040-048]That runner said: “I am from the south;   

[040-049]I run to Baile Honey-Mouth,   

[040-050]To tell him how the girl Aillinn   

[040-051]Rode from the country of her kin,   

[040-052]And old and young men rode with her:   

[040-053]For all that country had been astir   

[040-054]If anybody half as fair   

[040-055]Had chosen a husband anywhere   

[040-056]But where it could see her every day.   

[040-057]When they had ridden a little way   

[040-058]An old man caught the horse’s head   

[040-059]With: ‘You must home again, and wed   

[040-060]With somebody in your own land.’   

[040-061]A young man cried and kissed her hand,   

[040-062]‘O lady, wed with one of us’;   

[040-063]And when no face grew piteous   

[040-064]For any gentle thing she spake,   

[040-065]She fell and died of the heart-break.”

[040-066]Because a lover’s heart’s worn out,   

[040-067]Being tumbled and blown about   

[040-068]By its own blind imagining,   

[040-069]And will believe that anything   

[040-070]That is bad enough to be true, is true,   

[040-071]Baile’s heart was broken in two;   

[040-072]And he being laid upon green boughs,   

[040-073]Was carried to the goodly house   

[040-074]Where the Hound of Ulad sat before   

[040-075]The brazen pillars of his door,   

[040-076]His face bowed low to weep the end   

[040-077]Of the harper’s daughter and her friend.   

[040-078]For although years had passed away   

[040-079]He always wept them on that day,   

[040-080]For on that day they had been betrayed;   

[040-081]And now that Honey-Mouth is laid   

[040-082]Under a cairn of sleepy stone   

[040-083]Before his eyes, he has tears for none,   

[040-084]Although he is carrying stone, but two   

[040-085]For whom the cairn’s but heaped anew.

[040-086]We hold because our memory is   

[040-087]So full of that thing and of this   

[040-088]That out of sight is out of mind.   

[040-089]But the grey rush under the wind   

[040-090]And the grey bird with crooked bill   

[040-091]Have such long memories, that they still   

[040-092]Remember [[deirdre|Deirdre]] and her man;   

[040-093]And when we walk with Kate or Nan   

[040-094]About the windy water side,   

[040-095]Our heart can hear the voices chide.   

[040-096]How could we be so soon content,   

[040-097]Who know the way that Naoise went?   

[040-098]And they have news of Deirdre’s eyes,   

[040-099]Who being lovely was so wise﻿—   

[040-100]Ah! wise, my heart knows [[water-imagery|well]] how wise.

[040-101]Now had that old gaunt crafty one,   

[040-102]Gathering his cloak about him, run   

[040-103]Where Aillinn rode with waiting maids,   

[040-104]Who amid leafy lights and shades   

[040-105]Dreamed of the hands that would unlace   

[040-106]Their bodices in some dim place   

[040-107]When they had come to the marriage bed;   

[040-108]And harpers, pacing with high head   

[040-109]As though their [[song-and-poetry|music]] were enough   

[040-110]To make the savage heart of love   

[040-111]Grow gentle without sorrowing,   

[040-112]Imagining and pondering   

[040-113]Heaven knows what calamity;

[040-114]“Another’s hurried off,” cried he,   

[040-115]“From heat and cold and wind and wave;   

[040-116]They have heaped the stones above his grave   

[040-117]In Muirthemne, and over it   

[040-118]In changeless Ogham letters writ﻿—   

[040-119]"Baile, that was of Rury’s seed".   

[040-120]But the gods long ago decreed   

[040-121]No waiting maid should ever spread   

[040-122]Baile and Aillinn’s marriage bed,   

[040-123]For they should clip and clip again   

[040-124]Where wild bees hive on the Great Plain.   

[040-125]Therefore it is but little news   

[040-126]That put this hurry in my shoes.”

[040-127]Then seeing that he scarce had spoke   

[040-128]Before her love-worn heart had broke,   

[040-129]He ran and laughed until he came   

[040-130]To that high hill the herdsmen name   

[040-131]The Hill Seat of Leighin, because   

[040-132]Some god or king had made the laws   

[040-133]That held the land together there,   

[040-134]In old times among the clouds of the air.

[040-135]That old man climbed; the day grew dim;   

[040-136]Two swans came flying up to him,   

[040-137]Linked by a gold chain each to each,   

[040-138]And with low murmuring laughing speech   

[040-139]Alighted on the windy grass.   

[040-140]They knew him: his changed body was   

[040-141]Tall, proud and ruddy, and light wings   

[040-142]Were hovering over the harp-strings   

[040-143]That Etain, Midhir’s wife, had wove   

[040-144]In the hid place, being crazed by love.

[040-145]What shall I call them? fish that swim,   

[040-146]Scale rubbing scale where light is dim   

[040-147]By a broad water-lily leaf;   

[040-148]Or mice in the one wheaten sheaf   

[040-149]Forgotten at the threshing place;   

[040-150]Or birds lost in the one clear space   

[040-151]Of morning light in a dim sky;   

[040-152]Or, it may be, the eyelids of one eye,   

[040-153]Or the door pillars of one house,   

[040-154]Or two sweet blossoming apple-boughs   

[040-155]That have one shadow on the ground;   

[040-156]Or the two strings that made one sound   

[040-157]Where that wise harper’s finger ran.   

[040-158]For this young girl and this young man   

[040-159]Have happiness without an end,   

[040-160]Because they have made so good a friend.

[040-161]They know all wonders, for they pass   

[040-162]The towery gates of Gorias,   

[040-163]And Findrias and Falias,   

[040-164]And long-forgotten Murias,   

[040-165]Among the giant kings whose hoard,   

[040-166]Cauldron and spear and stone and sword,   

[040-167]Was robbed before earth gave the wheat;   

[040-168]Wandering from broken street to street   

[040-169]They come where some huge watcher is,   

[040-170]And tremble with their love and kiss.

[040-171]They know undying things, for they   

[040-172]Wander where earth withers away,   

[040-173]Though nothing troubles the great streams   

[040-174]But light from the pale stars, and gleams   

[040-175]From the holy orchards, where there is none   

[040-176]But fruit that is of precious stone,   

[040-177]Or apples of the sun and moon.

[040-178]What were our praise to them? They eat   

[040-179]Quiet’s wild heart, like daily meat;   

[040-180]Who when night thickens are afloat   

[040-181]On dappled skins in a glass boat,   

[040-182]Far out under a windless sky;   

[040-183]While over them birds of Aengus fly,   

[040-184]And over the tiller and the prow,   

[040-185]And waving white wings to and fro   

[040-186]Awaken wanderings of light air   

[040-187]To stir their coverlet and their hair.

[040-188]And poets found, old writers say,   

[040-189]A yew tree where his body lay;   

[040-190]But a wild apple hid the grass   

[040-191]With its sweet blossom where hers was;   

[040-192]And being in good heart, because   

[040-193]A better time had come again   

[040-194]After the deaths of many men,   

[040-195]And that long fighting at the ford,   

[040-196]They wrote on tablets of thin board,   

[040-197]Made of the apple and the yew,   

[040-198]All the love stories that they knew.

[040-199]Let rush and bird cry out their fill   

[040-200]Of the harper’s daughter if they will,   

[040-201]Beloved, I am not afraid of her.   

[040-202]She is not wiser nor lovelier,   

[040-203]And you are more high of heart than she,   

[040-204]For all her wanderings over-sea;   

[040-205]But I’d have bird and rush forget   

[040-206]Those other two; for never yet   

[040-207]Has lover lived, but longed to wive   

[040-208]Like them that are no more alive.
