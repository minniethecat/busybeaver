# Celtic Revival

The Celtic Revival — also called the Irish Literary Revival or Irish Renaissance — was a literary and cultural movement that flourished from approximately 1885 to the 1920s. Its central ambition was to create a modern Irish literature in English that was rooted in Irish mythology, folklore, and the Gaelic language tradition, and that could stand as a national literature distinct from English letters.

W. B. Yeats was both the movement's most important poet and its most effective organiser. As a founding member of the Irish Literary Society (London, 1891) and the National Literary Society (Dublin, 1892), and later as a director of the [[Abbey-Theatre|Abbey Theatre]], Yeats worked tirelessly to build the institutional infrastructure of the Revival.

## Key Figures

- **W. B. Yeats** — poet, playwright, and the movement's central figure
- **Lady Gregory** — folklorist, playwright, and Yeats's collaborator at Coole Park
- **J. M. Synge** — playwright whose *The Playboy of the Western World* (1907) caused riots at the Abbey
- **George Russell (AE)** — poet, mystic, and editor of *The Irish Homestead*
- **Douglas Hyde** — scholar of the Irish language, founder of the Gaelic League, and later first President of Ireland
- **Standish O'Grady** — historian whose retellings of Irish myth provided source material for the Revival writers

## Key Institutions

- **The [[Abbey-Theatre|Abbey Theatre]]** (founded 1904) — the national theatre of Ireland, home to the plays of Yeats, Synge, Lady Gregory, and later Sean O'Casey
- **The Gaelic League** (founded 1893) — dedicated to the revival of the Irish language
- **The Irish Literary Society** — London-based organisation promoting Irish writing

## Yeats's Role

Yeats's contribution to the Revival took multiple forms: he edited collections of Irish folklore and myth (*Fairy and Folk Tales of the Irish Peasantry*, 1888; *The Celtic Twilight*, 1893); he drew on Irish legend for his early poetry (*The Wanderings of Usheen*, 1889); he wrote plays based on Irish myth for the Abbey; and he provided the movement's most articulate theoretical statements in essays like "The Celtic Element in Literature" (1897) and "Ireland and the Arts" (1901).
