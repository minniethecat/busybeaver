# Fairy and Faery Lore

The fairy world — the Sidhe, the daoine sídhe, the "faery" host — occupies a central place in W. B. Yeats's poetic imagination, serving at once as authentic Irish folk tradition, symbolist metaphor for the creative mind, and a bridge between the seen and unseen worlds. For Yeats, the fairy faith of the Irish countryside was not quaint superstition but living evidence of an older, more vital relationship between human consciousness and the invisible realm — a "faery" dimension that poetry alone could render visible.

## Introduction

Yeats distinguished carefully between the English "fairy" (diminutive, Victorian, decorative) and the Irish "faery" (dangerous, ancient, real). The distinction was not merely orthographic but ontological. In his poems, fairy and faery appear in distinct registers: the "fairy" of folk ballads — fairy doctors [023-001], fairy hosting [023-009], fairy jigs [021-016] — and the "faery" of high symbolist vision, where the Sidhe are immortal, soulless, and indifferent to mortal categories of good and evil [001-002].

> "But soulless a faery dies, / ... / For soulless a faery dies" [001-002]

This doubleness was essential to Yeats's project. The fairy lore collected in *The Celtic Twilight* (1893) and *Fairy and Folk Tales of the Irish Peasantry* (1888) provided authentic cultural material, while the symbolist "faery" offered a language for the inner life that escaped both Victorian moralism and scientific materialism.

## Irish Sources and the Sidhe

The *aes sídhe* (people of the mounds) of Irish tradition are not the tiny winged fairies of English convention but tall, beautiful, and perilous beings who inhabit a parallel world accessible through ancient burial mounds, lonely hills, and the threshold moments of dusk and dawn. Yeats drew on firsthand accounts collected from Irish country people — stories of faery doctors who could see the hidden host [023-009], of women taken by the Sidhe [048-015], and of faery bushes that must never be cut [048-020].

> "The fairy doctor comes our way / ... / He sees the fairy hosting move" [023-001]

In "The Fairy Doctor" (chapter 023), Yeats dramatises the figure of the folk healer who can perceive the Sidhe's movements invisible to ordinary eyes. The poem's action — curing cattle "fairy-smitten" [023-014] — is grounded in actual Irish folk practice, yet the faery doctor also figures the poet himself, who sees what others cannot.

## Faery as Symbol

In Yeats's mature symbolism, faery becomes a figure for the imagination itself. The Danaan realm of "The Wanderings of Usheen" is a faery country where time passes differently, where "the horse of faery screamed" [015-433] and lovers are bound by rings of "Druid gold" [015-419]. The faery world's characteristic quality — its radical otherness to mortal experience — made it the perfect symbol for the creative state that Yeats, following Blake, saw as the highest human faculty.

The Rose poems of the 1893 collection *The Rose* explicitly merge faery with mystical vision: "Sung by the people of faery over Diarmuid and Grania" [031-230]. The faery song here is not escapism but access to a more intense mode of perception — what Yeats called "the calm of faery" [031-405].

> "How Time may never mar their faery vows" [031-364]

## Faery and the National Imagination

For Yeats, the fairy faith had political as well as poetic implications. The survival of faery belief among the Irish peasantry was evidence of a pre-colonial consciousness that had resisted English rationalism. To write seriously about the Sidhe was to assert that Irish experience could not be reduced to the categories of British empiricism — that Ireland possessed its own epistemology, its own ways of knowing.

The faery poems collected in *Crossways* and *The Rose* thus functioned as acts of cultural recovery. Poems such as "The Stolen Child" (where faeries entice a human child away from the world of weeping), "The Priest and the Fairy" [024-028], and "The Fairy Pedant" [025-001] all dramatise encounters between the faery and human orders — encounters that never resolve neatly into moral allegory.

## Faery and the Occult System

In Yeats's later esoteric system, the Sidhe were integrated into the larger framework of *A Vision*. The faeries were understood as beings of the "intermediate state" — souls that had not completed the cycle of rebirth, existing in a condition between the living and the dead. This theoretical elaboration never displaced the folk material, however; Yeats continued to collect and recount faery anecdotes throughout his life, valuing their concrete particularity as highly as their symbolic resonance.
