# Dance in Yeats

Dance is one of the most persistent and philosophically charged symbols in W. B. Yeats's poetry. Across his career, from the early fairy poems to the great meditations of the later period, dance figures the reconciliation of opposites: stillness and motion, body and soul, art and life, the temporal and the eternal. Yeats's famous line from "Among School Children" — "How can we know the dancer from the dance?" [047-731] — crystallises this symbol, but the motif runs far deeper, appearing in over two dozen passages across the collected poems and extending into his dramatic theory shaped by Japanese Noh theatre.

## Origins and Sources

Yeats's dance symbolism draws on three main sources:

1. **Irish Fairy Tradition**: The *Aos Sí* (fairies) were traditionally depicted dancing in raths and on hillsides, and Yeats absorbed these images from folk tales collected in Sligo and from the poetry of the Celtic Revival.

2. **Japanese Noh Theatre**: Yeats encountered Noh through Ezra Pound in 1913–1916, and its stylised dance-drama — where a single controlled gesture carries immense symbolic weight — profoundly shaped his later conception of dance as a fusion of form and meaning. His *Plays for Dancers* (1916) directly adapted Noh conventions.

3. **Symbolist Aesthetics**: The wider European Symbolist movement (Mallarmé, Maeterlinck) treated dance as the ideal art form — the dancer's body makes the invisible visible, a theme Yeats turned toward his own metaphysical system in *A Vision*.

## Development Across Yeats's Career

### Early Period: Fairy Dance

In the early lyrics, dance belongs primarily to the fairy world. The faery hosts dance upon "the yellow lance" of waves [001-007] and on the "fringed waves" [001-009] in "Song of the Faeries." In "The Wanderings of Usheen" (1889), dance saturates the Otherworld: the immortals dance "to a fitful measure" [015-093], with "dancers leaping in the air" [015-243], "waving arms and wandering dances" [015-263], and a "wild and sudden dance" [015-276] that winds through "the windless woods" [015-304]. Some "danced like shadows on the mountain" [015-372] — a line that prefigures Yeats's later interest in dance as a border-state between substance and shadow.

In the shorter fairy poems, dance takes on a playful but uncanny character: the fairies "dance a fairy jig" [021-016], the swift dancer is celebrated for physical grace — "The swiftest he to dance or fly" [024-046] — and the fairies "dance upon the spear-grass brittle" [024-080]. The rueful speaker of "A Lover's Quarrel Among the Fairies" is sought "in every dance, by children sought" [022-013]. Throughout these early treatments, dance is ecstatic and Otherworldly — associated with the seductive beauty of the Sidhe and the perilous allure of the fairy kingdom.

### Middle Period: Noh and the Symbolic Stage

The 1910s brought a decisive shift through Yeats's encounter with Noh. Dance ceased to be merely depicted and became structural to his dramatic practice. The *Plays for Dancers* — *At the Hawk's Well* (1916), *The Only Jealousy of Emer* (1919), *The Dreaming of the Bones* (1919) — used the dancer's controlled movement as the central expressive device. In the poetry of this period, the dancer figure appears in visionary form: "What a dancer glimmering!" [031-340], a glimpse of perfected, luminous being. In the same poem, the dance becomes associated with ritual celebration: "And no one any leaf-crowned dancer miss" [031-400].

The dancer's technological counterpart appears when Yeats recalls the American modern-dance pioneer: "When Loie Fuller's Chinese dancers enwound" [047-484] — a passage that links Yeats's symbolist dance to the living tradition of modernist performance.

### Late Period: Philosophical Dance

In Yeats's mature philosophy, dance becomes the primary image of what he called "Unity of Being" — the state in which all faculties are integrated and no division exists between the doer and the deed. The dancer so completely inhabits the dance that the two cannot be separated; the art is not something the dancer *does* but something the dancer *is*.

The late poetry develops this into a universal metaphysics of dance. In "A Dialogue of Self and Soul," the body's dance is an affirmation of mortal life against the soul's call to renunciation: the "dancing shoes" are the instruments of earthly joy. In "The Double Vision of Michael Robartes," the dancers are caught at the moment of ecstatic unity: "the laughing dancers throng" [047-981]. The *Supernatural Songs* go further, declaring that "Labour is blossoming or dancing where / The body is not bruised to pleasure soul" [047-724] — dance becomes the image of paradise itself, a state where work and play, body and soul, are indistinguishable.

The most explicit philosophical statement comes in "Among School Children," where dance is the metaphor for the unity toward which all Yeats's thought aspires:

> O chestnut-tree, great-rooted blossomer,
> Are you the leaf, the blossom or the bole?
> O body swayed to music, O brightening glance,
> How can we know the dancer from the dance? [047-731]

Here the dancer-dance unity is presented as equivalent to the tree's organic wholeness — both are images of a state where part and whole, process and product, become one.

## Symbolic Meaning

Dance functions on three symbolic axes in Yeats's work:

1. **Unity of Being**: The dancer embodies the fusion of body and soul, action and identity — the opposite of modern fragmentation and abstraction.
2. **Artistic Creation**: Dance is Yeats's master-metaphor for the creative act itself — the poem, like the dance, cannot be separated from the poet who made it.
3. **Eternal Movement**: Dance figures the cosmic gyre — souls pass through incarnations as dancers pass through figures; "All men are dancers and their tread / Goes to the barbarous clangour of a gong" [047-492].
