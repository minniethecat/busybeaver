# John O'Leary

John O'Leary (1830–1907) was an Irish Fenian, journalist, and nationalist who spent years in English prisons and Parisian exile for his revolutionary activities. For the young W. B. Yeats, O'Leary represented a crucial bridge between the world of literature and the world of Irish nationalism — a figure who combined passionate political conviction with literary cultivation.

## Influence on Yeats

Yeats met O'Leary in 1885, shortly after the older man's return from exile. O'Leary became a mentor to the young poet, introducing him to the Young Ireland poets and encouraging him to write on Irish subjects. Yeats later wrote in his *Autobiographies*:

> "It was through the Fenian movement that I found myself as a young man... John O'Leary was the most important influence on my early intellectual life after my father."

O'Leary's nationalism was cultural as much as political. He believed that an independent Ireland required an independent literature, and he directed Yeats towards Irish mythology, folklore, and history as proper subjects for poetry.

## "September 1913" and "To a Shade"

O'Leary appears in two of Yeats's greatest political poems:

- **"September 1913"**: The refrain "Romantic Ireland's dead and gone / It's with O'Leary in the grave" laments the passing of the heroic, idealistic nationalism O'Leary represented, replaced by what Yeats saw as the mean-spirited commercialism of Catholic middle-class Ireland.
- **"To a Shade"**: The ghost of Charles Stewart Parnell is addressed; O'Leary is invoked as part of the pantheon of heroic figures who have been betrayed by Ireland.

## Legacy

O'Leary occupies a unique place in Yeats's personal mythology — not a mythological symbol but a living connection to the Fenian tradition that gave Irish nationalism its heroic dimension. He was, for Yeats, the type of the nationalist as noble idealist rather than pragmatic politician.
