# The Wanderings of Usheen[1](endnotes.xhtml#note-1)

[015-001]
> “Give me the world if thou wilt, but grant me an asylum for my affections.”

[015-002]
> 

[015-003]
> Tulka

[015-004]To Edwin J. Ellis

##  Book I

[015-005]S. Patric |  You who are bent, and bald, and blind,   

[015-006]With a heavy heart and a wandering mind,   

[015-007]Have known three centuries, poets sing,   

[015-008]Of dalliance with a demon thing.  

[015-009]---|---  

[015-010][[oisin|Usheen]] |  Sad to remember, sick with years,   

[015-011]The swift innumerable spears,   

[015-012]The horsemen with their floating hair,   

[015-013]And bowls of barley, honey, and wine,   

[015-014]And feet of maidens [[dance-in-yeats|dancing]] in tune,   

[015-015]And the white body that lay by mine;   

[015-016]But the tale, though words be lighter than air,   

[015-017]Must live to be old like the wandering moon. Caolte, and Conan, and Finn were there,   

[015-018]When we followed a deer with our baying hounds,   

[015-019]With Bran, Sgeolan, and Lomair,   

[015-020]And passing the Firbolgs’ burial mounds   

[015-021]Came to the cairn-heaped grassy hill   

[015-022]Where passionate Maive is stony still;   

[015-023]And found on the dove-grey edge of the [[water-imagery|sea]]   

[015-024]A pearl-pale, high-born lady, who rode   

[015-025]On a horse with bridle of findrinny;   

[015-026]And like a sunset were her lips,   

[015-027]A stormy sunset on doomed ships;   

[015-028]A citron colour gloomed in her hair,   

[015-029]But down to her feet white vesture flowed,   

[015-030]And with the glimmering crimson glowed   

[015-031]Of many a figured embroidery;   

[015-032]And it was bound with a pearl-pale shell   

[015-033]That wavered like the summer streams,   

[015-034]As her soft bosom rose and fell.  

[015-035]S. Patric |  You are still wrecked among heathen dreams.  

[015-036]Usheen |  “Why do you wind no horn?” she said.   

[015-037]“And every hero droop his head?   

[015-038]The hornless deer is not more sad   

[015-039]That many a peaceful moment had,   

[015-040]More sleek than any granary mouse,   

[015-041]In his own leafy forest house   

[015-042]Among the waving fields of fern:   

[015-043]The hunting of heroes should be glad.” “O pleasant woman,” answered Finn,   

[015-044]“We think on Oscar’s pencilled urn,   

[015-045]And on the heroes lying slain,   

[015-046]On Gavra’s raven-covered plain;   

[015-047]But where are your noble kith and kin,   

[015-048]And into what country do you ride?” “My father and my mother are   

[015-049][[aengus-god|Aengus]] and Adene, and my name   

[015-050]Niam, and my country far   

[015-051]Beyond the tumbling of this tide.” “What dream came with you that you came   

[015-052]Through bitter tide on foam-wet feet?   

[015-053]Did your companion wander away   

[015-054]From where the birds of Aengus wing?” She said, with laughter tender and sweet:   

[015-055]“I have not yet, war-weary king,   

[015-056]Been spoken of with anyone;   

[015-057]Yet now I choose, for these four feet   

[015-058]Ran through the foam and ran to this   

[015-059]That I might have your son to kiss.” “Were there no better than my son   

[015-060]That you through all that foam should run?” “I loved no man, though kings besought   

[015-061]Until the Danaan poets brought,   

[015-062]Rhyme, that rhymed to Usheen’s name,   

[015-063]And now I am dizzy with the thought   

[015-064]Of all that wisdom and the fame,   

[015-065]Of battles broken by his hands,   

[015-066]Of stories builded by his words   

[015-067]That are like coloured Asian birds   

[015-068]At evening in their rainless lands.” O Patric, by your brazen bell,   

[015-069]There was no limb of mine but fell   

[015-070]Into a desperate gulf of love!   

[015-071]“You only will I wed,” I cried,   

[015-072]“And I will make a thousand songs,   

[015-073]And set your name all names above,   

[015-074]And captives bound with leathern thongs   

[015-075]Shall kneel and praise you, one by one,   

[015-076]At evening in my western dun.” “O Usheen, mount by me and ride   

[015-077]To shores by the wash of the tremulous tide,   

[015-078]Where men have heaped no burial mounds,   

[015-079]And the days pass by like a wayward tune,   

[015-080]Where broken faith has never been known,   

[015-081]And the blushes of first love never have flown;   

[015-082]And there I will give you a hundred hounds;   

[015-083]No mightier creatures bay at the moon;   

[015-084]And a hundred robes of murmuring silk,   

[015-085]And a hundred calves and a hundred sheep   

[015-086]Whose long wool whiter than sea froth flows,   

[015-087]And a hundred spears and a hundred bows,   

[015-088]And oil and wine and honey and milk,   

[015-089]And always never-anxious sleep;   

[015-090]While a hundred youths, mighty of limb,   

[015-091]But knowing nor tumult nor hate nor strife,   

[015-092]And a hundred maidens, merry as birds,   

[015-093]Who when they dance to a fitful measure   

[015-094]Have a speed like the speed of the salmon herds,   

[015-095]Shall follow your horn and obey your whim,   

[015-096]And you shall know the Danaan leisure:   

[015-097]And Niam be with you for a wife.”   

[015-098]Then she sighed gently, “It grows late,   

[015-099]Music and love and sleep await,   

[015-100]Where I would be when the white moon climbs,   

[015-101]The red sun falls and the world grows dim.” And then I mounted and she bound me   

[015-102]With her triumphing arms around me,   

[015-103]And whispering to herself enwound me;   

[015-104]But when the horse had felt my weight,   

[015-105]He shook himself and neighed three times:   

[015-106]Caolte, Conan, and Finn came near,   

[015-107]And wept, and raised their lamenting hands,   

[015-108]And bid me stay, with many a tear;   

[015-109]But we rode out from the human lands. In what far kingdom do you go,   

[015-110]Ah, Fenians, with the shield and bow?   

[015-111]Or are you phantoms white as snow,   

[015-112]Whose lips had life’s most prosperous glow?   

[015-113]O you, with whom in sloping valleys,   

[015-114]Or down the dewy forest alleys,   

[015-115]I chased at morn the flying deer,   

[015-116]With whom I hurled the hurrying spear,   

[015-117]And heard the foemen’s bucklers rattle,   

[015-118]And broke the heaving ranks of battle!   

[015-119]And Bran, Sgeolan, and Lomair,   

[015-120]Where are you with your long rough hair?   

[015-121]You go not where the red deer feeds,   

[015-122]Nor tear the foemen from their steeds.  

[015-123]S. Patric |  Boast not, nor mourn with drooping head   

[015-124]Companions long accurst and dead,   

[015-125]And hounds for centuries dust and air.  

[015-126]Usheen |  We galloped over the glossy sea:   

[015-127]I know not if days passed or hours,   

[015-128]And Niam sang continually   

[015-129]Danaan songs, and their dewy showers   

[015-130]Of pensive laughter, unhuman sound,   

[015-131]Lulled weariness, and softly round   

[015-132]My human sorrow her white arms wound. We galloped; now a hornless deer   

[015-133]Passed by us, chased by a phantom hound   

[015-134]All pearly white, save one red ear;   

[015-135]And now a maiden rode like the wind   

[015-136]With an apple of gold in her tossing hand;   

[015-137]And a beautiful young man followed behind   

[015-138]With quenchless eyes and fluttering hair. “Were these two born in the Danaan land,   

[015-139]Or have they breathed the mortal air?”   

[015-140]“Vex them no longer,” Niam said,   

[015-141]And sighing bowed her gentle head,   

[015-142]And sighing laid the pearly tip   

[015-143]Of one long finger on my lip. But now the moon like a white rose shone   

[015-144]In the pale west, and the sun’s rim sank,   

[015-145]And clouds arrayed their rank on rank   

[015-146][[About]] his fading crimson ball:   

[015-147]The floor of Allen’s hosting hall   

[015-148]Was not more level than the sea,   

[015-149]As full of loving fantasy,   

[015-150]And with low murmurs we rode on,   

[015-151]Where many a trumpet-twisted shell   

[015-152]That in immortal silence sleeps   

[015-153]Dreaming of her own melting hues,   

[015-154]Her golds, her ambers, and her blues,   

[015-155]Pierced with soft [[shadow-and-light|light]] the shallowing deeps.   

[015-156]But now a wandering land breeze came   

[015-157]And a far sound of feathery quires;   

[015-158]It seemed to blow from the [[death-in-yeats|dying]] [[fire-passion|flame]],   

[015-159]They seemed to sing in the smouldering fires.   

[015-160]The horse towards the [[song-and-poetry|music]] raced,   

[015-161]Neighing along the lifeless waste;   

[015-162]Like sooty fingers, many a tree   

[015-163][[rose-symbol|Rose]] ever out of the warm sea;   

[015-164]And they were trembling ceaselessly,   

[015-165]As though they all were beating time,   

[015-166]Upon the centre of the sun,   

[015-167]To that low laughing woodland rhyme,   

[015-168]And, now our wandering hours were done,   

[015-169]We cantered to the shore, and knew   

[015-170]The reason of the trembling trees:   

[015-171]Round every branch the song-birds flew,   

[015-172]Or clung thereon like swarming bees;   

[015-173]While round the shore a million stood   

[015-174]Like drops of frozen rainbow light,   

[015-175]And pondered in a soft vain mood   

[015-176]Upon their shadows in the tide,   

[015-177]And told the purple deeps their pride,   

[015-178]And murmured snatches of delight;   

[015-179]And on the shores were many boats   

[015-180]With bending sterns and bending bows,   

[015-181]And carven figures on their prows   

[015-182]Of bitterns, and fish-eating stoats,   

[015-183]And swans with their exultant throats:   

[015-184]And where the wood and waters meet   

[015-185]We tied the horse in a leafy clump,   

[015-186]And Niam blew three merry notes   

[015-187]Out of a little silver trump;   

[015-188]And then an answering whisper flew   

[015-189]Over the bare and woody land,   

[015-190]A whisper of impetuous feet,   

[015-191]And ever nearer, nearer grew;   

[015-192]And from the woods rushed out a band   

[015-193]Of men and maidens, hand in hand,   

[015-194]And singing, singing altogether;   

[015-195]Their brows were white as fragrant milk,   

[015-196]Their cloaks made out of yellow silk,   

[015-197]And trimmed with many a crimson feather;   

[015-198]And when they saw the cloak I wore   

[015-199]Was dim with mire of a mortal shore,   

[015-200]They fingered it and gazed on me   

[015-201]And laughed like murmurs of the sea;   

[015-202]But Niam with a swift distress   

[015-203]Bid them away and hold their peace;   

[015-204]And when they heard her voice they ran   

[015-205]And knelt them, every maid and man   

[015-206]And kissed, as they would never cease,   

[015-207]Her pearl-pale hand and the hem of her dress.   

[015-208]She bade them bring us to the hall   

[015-209]Where Aengus dreams, from sun to sun,   

[015-210]A [[druidism-and-celtic-mysticism|Druid]] dream of the end of days   

[015-211]When the stars are to wane and the world be done. They led us by long and shadowy ways   

[015-212]Where drops of dew in myriads fall,   

[015-213]And tangled creepers every hour   

[015-214]Blossom in some new crimson flower,   

[015-215]And once a sudden laughter sprang   

[015-216]From all their lips, and once they sang   

[015-217]Together, while the dark woods rang,   

[015-218]And made in all their distant parts,   

[015-219]With boom of bees in honey marts,   

[015-220]A rumour of delighted hearts.   

[015-221]And once a maiden by my side   

[015-222]Gave me a harp, and bade me sing,   

[015-223]And touch the laughing silver string;   

[015-224]But when I sang of human joy   

[015-225]A sorrow wrapped each merry face,   

[015-226]And, Patric! by your beard, they wept,   

[015-227]Until one came, a tearful boy;   

[015-228]“A sadder creature never stepped   

[015-229]Than this strange human bard,” he cried;   

[015-230]And caught the silver harp away,   

[015-231]And, weeping over the white strings, hurled   

[015-232]It down in a leaf-hid, hollow place   

[015-233]That kept dim waters from the sky;   

[015-234]And each one said with a long, long sigh,   

[015-235]“O saddest harp in all the world,   

[015-236]Sleep there till the moon and the stars die!” And now still sad we came to where   

[015-237]A beautiful young man dreamed within   

[015-238]A house of wattles, clay, and skin;   

[015-239]One hand upheld his beardless chin;   

[015-240]And one a sceptre flashing out   

[015-241]Wild flames of red and gold and blue,   

[015-242]Like to a merry wandering rout   

[015-243]Of dancers leaping in the air;   

[015-244]And men and maidens knelt them there   

[015-245]And showed their eyes with teardrops dim,   

[015-246]And with low murmurs prayed to him,   

[015-247]And kissed the sceptre with red lips,   

[015-248]And touched it with their finger-tips. He held that flashing sceptre up.   

[015-249]“Joy drowns the twilight in the dew,   

[015-250]And fills with stars night’s purple cup,   

[015-251]And wakes the sluggard seeds of corn,   

[015-252]And stirs the young kid’s budding horn   

[015-253]And makes the infant ferns unwrap,   

[015-254]And for the peewit paints his cap,   

[015-255]And rolls along the unwieldy sun,   

[015-256]And makes the little planets run:   

[015-257]And if joy were not on the earth,   

[015-258]There were an end of change and birth,   

[015-259]And earth and heaven and hell would die,   

[015-260]And in some gloomy barrow lie   

[015-261]Folded like a frozen fly;   

[015-262]Then mock at Death and Time with glances   

[015-263]And waving arms and wandering dances. “Men’s hearts of old were drops of flame   

[015-264]That from the saffron morning came,   

[015-265]Or drops of silver joy that fell   

[015-266]Out of the moon’s pale twisted shell;   

[015-267]But now hearts cry that hearts are slaves,   

[015-268]And toss and turn in narrow caves;   

[015-269]But here there is nor law nor rule,   

[015-270]Nor have hands held a weary tool;   

[015-271]And here there is nor Change nor Death,   

[015-272]But only kind and merry breath,   

[015-273]For joy is God and God is joy.”   

[015-274]With one long glance on maid and boy   

[015-275]And the pale blossom of the moon,   

[015-276]He fell into a Druid swoon. And in a wild and sudden dance   

[015-277]We mocked at Time and Fate and Chance   

[015-278]And swept out of the wattled hall   

[015-279]And came to where the dewdrops fall   

[015-280]Among the foamdrops of the sea,   

[015-281]And there we hushed the revelry;   

[015-282]And, gathering on our brows a frown,   

[015-283]Bent all our swaying bodies down,   

[015-284]And to the waves that glimmer by   

[015-285]That slooping green De Danaan sod   

[015-286]Sang, “God is joy and joy is God.   

[015-287]And things that have grown sad are wicked,   

[015-288]And things that fear the dawn of the morrow   

[015-289]Or the gray wandering osprey Sorrow.” We danced to where in the winding thicket   

[015-290]The damask roses, bloom on bloom,   

[015-291]Like crimson meteors hang in the gloom,   

[015-292]And bending over them softly said,   

[015-293]Bending over them in the dance   

[015-294]With a swift and friendly glance   

[015-295]From dewy eyes: “Upon the dead   

[015-296]Fall the leaves of other roses,   

[015-297]On the dead dim earth encloses:   

[015-298]But never, never on our graves,   

[015-299]Heaped beside the glimmering waves,   

[015-300]Shall fall the leaves of damask roses.   

[015-301]For neither Death nor Change comes near us,   

[015-302]And all listless hours fear us,   

[015-303]And we fear no dawning morrow,   

[015-304]Nor the gray wandering osprey Sorrow.” The dance wound through the windless woods:   

[015-305]The ever-summered solitudes;   

[015-306]Until the tossing arms grew still   

[015-307]Upon the woody central hill;   

[015-308]And, gathered in a panting band,   

[015-309]We flung on high each waving hand,   

[015-310]And sang unto the starry broods.   

[015-311]In our raised eyes there flashed a glow   

[015-312]Of milky brightness to and fro   

[015-313]As thus our song arose: “You stars,   

[015-314]Across your wandering ruby cars   

[015-315]Shake the loose reins: you slaves of God,   

[015-316]He rules you with an iron rod,   

[015-317]He holds you with an iron bond,   

[015-318]Each one woven to the other,   

[015-319]Each one woven to his brother   

[015-320]Like bubbles in a frozen pond;   

[015-321]But we in a lonely land abide   

[015-322]Unchainable as the dim tide,   

[015-323]With hearts that know nor law nor rule,   

[015-324]And hands that hold no wearisome tool,   

[015-325]Folded in love that fears no morrow,   

[015-326]Nor the grey wandering osprey Sorrow.” O Patric! for a hundred years   

[015-327]I chased upon that woody shore   

[015-328]The deer, the badger, and the boar.   

[015-329]O Patric! for a hundred years   

[015-330]At evening on the glimmering sands,   

[015-331]Beside the piled-up hunting spears,   

[015-332]These now outworn and withered hands   

[015-333]Wrestled among the island bands.   

[015-334]O Patric! for a hundred years   

[015-335]We went a-fishing in long boats   

[015-336]With bending sterns and bending bows,   

[015-337]And carven figures on their prows   

[015-338]Of bitterns and fish-eating stoats.   

[015-339]O Patric! for a hundred years   

[015-340]The gentle Niam was my wife;   

[015-341]But now two things devour my life;   

[015-342]The things that most of all I hate;   

[015-343]Fasting and prayers.  

[015-344]S. Patric |  Tell on.  

[015-345]Usheen |  Yes, yes.   

[015-346]For these were ancient Oisin’s fate,   

[015-347]Loosed long ago from heaven’s gate,   

[015-348]For his last days to lie in wait. When one day by the tide I stood,   

[015-349]I found in that forgetfulness   

[015-350]Of dreamy foam a staff of wood   

[015-351]From some dead warrior’s broken lance:   

[015-352]I turned it in my hands; the stains   

[015-353]Of war were on it, and I wept,   

[015-354]Remembering how the Fenians stepped   

[015-355]Along the blood-bedabbled plains,   

[015-356]Equal to good or grievous chance:   

[015-357]Thereon young Niam softly came   

[015-358]And caught my hands, but spake no word   

[015-359]Save only many times my name,   

[015-360]In murmurs, like a frighted bird.   

[015-361]We passed by woods, and lawns of clover,   

[015-362]And found the horse and bridled him,   

[015-363]For we knew well the old was over.   

[015-364]I heard one say, “his eyes grow dim   

[015-365]With all the ancient sorrow of men”;   

[015-366]And wrapped in dreams rode out again   

[015-367]With hoofs of the pale findrinny   

[015-368]Over the glimmering purple sea:   

[015-369]Under the golden evening light.   

[015-370]The immortals moved among the fountains   

[015-371]By rivers and the woods’ old night;   

[015-372]Some danced like shadows on the mountains,   

[015-373]Some wandered ever hand in hand;   

[015-374]Or sat in dreams on the pale strand,   

[015-375]Each forehead like an obscure star   

[015-376]Bent down above each hooked knee,   

[015-377]And sang, and with a dreamy gaze   

[015-378]Watched where the sun in a saffron blaze   

[015-379]Was slumbering half in the sea ways;   

[015-380]And, as they sang, the painted birds   

[015-381]Kept time with their bright wings and feet;   

[015-382]Like drops of honey came their words,   

[015-383]But fainter than a young lamb’s bleat. “An old man stirs the fire to blaze,   

[015-384]In the house of a child, of a friend, of a brother;   

[015-385]He has over-lingered his welcome; the days,   

[015-386]Grown desolate, whisper and sigh to each other;   

[015-387]He hears the storm in the chimney above,   

[015-388]And bends to the fire and shakes with the cold,   

[015-389]While his heart still dreams of battle and love,   

[015-390]And the cry of the hounds on the hills of old. “But we are apart in the grassy places,   

[015-391]Where care cannot trouble the least of our days,   

[015-392]Or the softness of youth be gone from our faces,   

[015-393]Or love’s first tenderness die in our gaze.   

[015-394]The hare grows old as she plays in the sun   

[015-395]And gazes around her with eyes of brightness;   

[015-396]Before the swift things that she dreamed of were done   

[015-397]She limps along in an aged whiteness;   

[015-398]A storm of birds in the Asian trees   

[015-399]Like tulips in the air a-winging,   

[015-400]And the gentle waves of the summer seas,   

[015-401]That raise their heads and wander singing,   

[015-402]Must murmur at last ‘Unjust, unjust’;   

[015-403]And ‘my speed is a weariness,’ falters the mouse,   

[015-404]And the kingfisher turns to a ball of dust,   

[015-405]And the roof falls in of his tunnelled house,   

[015-406]But the love-dew dims our eyes till the day   

[015-407]When God shall come from the sea with a sigh   

[015-408]And bid the stars drop down from the sky,   

[015-409]And the moon like a pale rose wither away.”  
  
##  Book II

[015-410]Usheen |  Now, man of croziers, shadows called our names   

[015-411]And then away, away, like whirling flames;   

[015-412]And now fled by, mist-covered, without sound,   

[015-413]The youth and lady and the deer and hound;   

[015-414]“Gaze no more on the phantoms,” Niam said,   

[015-415]And kissed my eyes, and, swaying her bright head   

[015-416]And her bright body, sang of faery and man   

[015-417]Before God was or my old line began;   

[015-418]Wars shadowy, vast, exultant; faeries of old   

[015-419]Who wedded men with rings of Druid gold;   

[015-420]And how those lovers never turn their eyes   

[015-421]Upon the life that fades and flickers and dies,   

[015-422]But love and kiss on dim shores far away   

[015-423]Rolled round with music of the sighing spray:   

[015-424]But sang no more, as when, like a brown bee   

[015-425]That has drunk full, she crossed the misty sea   

[015-426]With me in her white arms a hundred years   

[015-427]Before this day; for now the fall of tears   

[015-428]Troubled her song. I do not know if days   

[015-429]Or hours passed by, yet hold the morning rays   

[015-430]Shone many times among the glimmering flowers   

[015-431]Woven in her hair, before dark towers   

[015-432]Rose in the darkness, and the white surf gleamed   

[015-433]About them; and the horse of faery screamed   

[015-434]And shivered, knowing the Isle of many Fears,   

[015-435]Nor ceased until white Niam stroked his ears   

[015-436]And named him by sweet names. A foaming tide   

[015-437]Whitened afar with surge, fan-formed and wide,   

[015-438]Burst from a great door marred by many a blow   

[015-439]From mace and sword and pole-axe, long ago   

[015-440]When gods and giants warred. We rode between   

[015-441]The seaweed-covered pillars; and the green   

[015-442]And surging phosphorus alone gave light   

[015-443]On our dark pathway, till a countless flight   

[015-444]Of moonlit steps glimmered; and left and right   

[015-445]Dark statues glimmered over the pale tide   

[015-446]Upon dark thrones. Between the lids of one

[015-800]S. Patric |  Where the flesh of the footsole clingeth on the burning stones is their place;   

[015-801]Where the demons whip them with wires on the burning stones of wide hell,   

[015-802]Watching the blessed ones move far off, and the smile on God’s face,   

[015-803]Between them a gateway of brass, and the howl of the angels who fell.  

[015-804]Usheen |  Put the staff in my hands; for I go to the Fenians, O cleric, to chaunt   

[015-805]The war-songs that roused them of old; they will rise, making clouds with their breath   

[015-806]Innumerable, singing, exultant; the clay underneath them shall pant,   

[015-807]And demons be broken in pieces, and trampled beneath them in death. And demons afraid in their darkness; deep horror of eyes and of wings,   

[015-808]Afraid their ears on the earth laid, shall listen and rise up and weep;   

[015-809]Hearing the shaking of shields and the quiver of stretched bowstrings,   

[015-810]Hearing Hell loud with a murmur, as shouting and mocking we sweep. We will tear out the flaming stones, and batter the gateway of brass   

[015-811]And enter, and none sayeth “No” when there enters the strongly armed guest;   

[015-812]Make clean as a broom cleans, and march on as oxen move over young grass;   

[015-813]Then feast, making converse of wars, and of old wounds, and turn to our rest.  

[015-814]S. Patric |  On the flaming stones, without refuge, the limbs of the Fenians are tost;   

[015-815]None war on the masters of Hell, who could break up the world in their rage;   

[015-816]Kneel, Usheen, wear out the flags and pray for your soul that is lost   

[015-817]Through the demon love of its youth and its godless and passionate age.  

[015-818]Usheen |  Ah, me! to be shaken with coughing and broken with old age and pain,   

[015-819]Without laughter, a show unto children, alone with [[memory-in-yeats|remembrance]] and fear;   

[015-820]All emptied of purple hours as a beggar’s cloak in the rain,   

[015-821]As a hay-cock out on the flood, or a wolf sucked under a weir. It were sad to gaze on the blessed and no man I loved of old there;   

[015-822]I throw down the chain of small stones! when life in my body has ceased,   

[015-823]I will go to Caolte, and Conan, and Bran, Sgeolan, Lomair,
