# Edmund Burke

Edmund Burke (1729–1797) was an Irish-born statesman, political philosopher, and orator, widely regarded as the father of modern conservatism. For W. B. Yeats, Burke represented the highest achievement of the Anglo-Irish intellect — a figure who, though a member of the Protestant Ascendancy, belonged to an Irish tradition of eloquence, public service, and philosophical depth that the poet championed against the narrower currents of modern Irish nationalism.

## Burke in Yeats's Poetry

Yeats invokes Burke most directly in "The Seven Sages" (1933), where the poet names "the people of Burke and of Grattan" [047-164] as the bearers of a distinguished tradition of Irish intellectual and political life. For Yeats, Burke embodied the fusion of Irish roots and European breadth that the poet admired in the eighteenth-century Ascendancy: a figure who participated in the great debates of British and European politics while remaining identifiably Irish.

## Burke and the Ascendancy Ideal

In Yeats's late political imagination, Burke and his contemporaries — Swift, Berkeley, Goldsmith, and Henry Grattan — represented an alternative Irish tradition to the revolutionary nationalism of the nineteenth and twentieth centuries. This tradition valued eloquence, intellect, and cultivated public life over mass politics. Yeats, increasingly disillusioned with the direction of the Irish Free State in which he had served as a senator, turned to the eighteenth-century Ascendancy as a cultural counter-myth — an image of what Irish civilisation might have become.

Burke's particular appeal for Yeats lay in his combination of conservative temperament and imaginative power. Burke's *Reflections on the Revolution in France* (1790) articulated a defence of tradition, inheritance, and organic social order that resonated with Yeats's own deepening conservatism in his later years.
