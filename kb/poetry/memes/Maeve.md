# Queen Maeve

Queen Maeve (Medb in the Old Irish form) is the warrior queen of Connacht in the Ulster Cycle of Irish mythology — the instigator of the Táin Bó Cúailnge (Cattle Raid of Cooley) and one of the most commanding female figures in world mythology. For W. B. Yeats, Maeve was far more than a mythological figure: she was the presiding spirit of Co. Sligo and the west of Ireland, associated with the great cairn on the summit of Knocknarea mountain that was said to be her tomb.

## Yeats's Maeve

Yeats returned to Maeve throughout his career. She appears in his early narrative poem "The Old Age of Queen Maeve" (1903), in which the ageing queen — still passionate, still imperious — seeks out a young lover with the help of the gods. In his late work, she haunts the landscape of "Under Ben Bulben" and the meditations on Knocknarea.

Maeve represented for Yeats a specifically Irish form of feminine power — passionate, martial, sexual, and unbroken by Christian domestication. She was the opposite of the "hysterical women" he mocked in "Lapis Lazuli": a queen who fights, desires, and commands without apology.

## Maeve's Cairn

The great cairn on the summit of Knocknarea, visible for miles across Sligo Bay, is traditionally the tomb of Queen Maeve. Yeats made the cairn a recurring image of permanence and ancestral presence. In "Under Ben Bulben," he imagines her "in her tomb" as a sentinel over the landscape, a figure of the heroic past that watches over the diminished present.
