## Introduction

"The Double Vision of Michael Robartes" is a poem by W. B. Yeats, first collected in _[[The-Wild-Swans-at-Coole|The Wild Swans at Coole]]_ (1919). Set on the grey rock of Cashel — the ancient seat of the Kings of Munster — the poem records a visionary experience in which three figures appear: a Sphinx, a Buddha, and a dancing girl suspended between them. The poem is spoken through the persona of [[michael-robartes|Michael Robartes]], one of Yeats's recurrent mystical _personae_, and serves as a poetic exposition of the philosophical ideas that would later be systematized in _[[a-vision|A Vision]]_ (1925).

## Yeats's Commentary

Yeats provided extensive context for the poem in his endnotes. He explains that he "wrote three stories in which occur the names of Michael Robartes and [[john-aherne|Owen Aherne]]," and that he now "consider[s] that I used the actual names of two friends" [048-053]. Robartes, he writes, "has but lately returned from Mesopotamia where he has partly found and partly thought out much philosophy" [048-053]. The poems — "The Phases of the Moon," "The Double Vision of Michael Robartes," and "Michael Robartes and the Dancer" — "take their place in a phantasmagoria in which I endeavour to explain my philosophy of life and death" [048-053]. Crucially, Yeats acknowledges: "To some extent I wrote them as a text for exposition" [048-053] — the poems were designed to be unpacked, to carry philosophical weight that would only be fully revealed in later prose.

## Structure and Imagery

The poem unfolds in three parts. **Part I** establishes the scene — "On the grey rock of Cashel the mind's eye" — and introduces the "cold spirits" born at the dark of the moon, abstract beings who "do not even feel, so abstract are they, / So dead beyond our death" [045-1219] [045-1232] [045-1233]. These spirits are "obedient to some hidden magical breath" — agents of a larger cosmic pattern [045-1231].

**Part II** presents the central vision: a Sphinx "with woman breast and lion paw" and a Buddha "hand at rest, / Hand lifted up that blest" — and between them a girl dancing [045-1236] [045-1237] [045-1238] [045-1239]. The Sphinx gazes "upon all things known, all things unknown, / In triumph of intellect / With motionless head erect" — representing pure intellectual knowledge [045-1248] [045-1249] [045-1250]. The Buddha's "moonlit eyeballs never moved, / Being fixed on all things loved, all things unloved" — embodying compassion and sorrow, for "those that love are sad" [045-1251] [045-1252] [045-1254]. The dancing girl, "No thought, / Body perfection brought" [045-1257] [045-1258], represents the unselfconscious unity of being that transcends both intellectual abstraction and loving sorrow.

**Part III** shifts to the personal. The speaker recognizes the dancing girl as an image from his own dreams — "That girl my unremembering nights hold fast / Or else my dreams that fly" [045-1268] [045-1269] — and is caught "between the pull / Of the dark moon and the full" [045-1277] [045-1278], between common thought and the "frenzy of our Western seas" [045-1280]. The poem closes with the poet arranging his vision "in a song" in "Cormac's ruined house" — the Rock of Cashel, a place of ancient Irish kingship now in ruins, the perfect setting for a vision that spans Eastern and Western spiritual traditions [045-1286].

## The Double Vision and Yeats's Philosophical System

The poem anticipates the central concepts of _A Vision_. The Sphinx and Buddha, facing each other across the dancing girl, embody the antinomies that Yeats saw as fundamental to consciousness: intellect versus love, abstraction versus compassion, East versus West, the motionless versus the moving. The girl dancing between them represents the unity of being — what Yeats would later call the "Thirteenth Cone" or the sphere of complete reality that transcends all antinomies.

The "double vision" of the title refers both to the twofold nature of the visionary experience (objective symbols seen by the mind's eye, yet carrying subjective emotional force) and to the philosophical principle that truth emerges from the tension of opposites. As Yeats wrote in his endnotes, explaining the Robartes philosophy: "the human soul is always moving outward into the objective world or inward into itself; & this movement is double because the human soul would not be conscious were it not suspended between contraries, the greater the contrast the more intense the consciousness" [048-058].

## The Rock of Cashel

The setting on the Rock of Cashel — Cormac's ruined house — is significant. Cashel was the seat of the Kings of Munster and the site where St. Patrick baptized King Aengus. By setting a syncretic vision combining Egyptian (Sphinx), Indian (Buddha), and Irish (Cashel, the dancing girl) elements in this ruined center of Irish sacred kingship, Yeats creates a symbolic geography that collapses East and West, ancient and modern, into a single moment of vision.
