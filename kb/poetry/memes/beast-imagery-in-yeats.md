## Introduction

Beast and animal imagery runs through W. B. Yeats' poetry as a powerful register for representing forces that exceed human control: the violence of historical change, the irruption of the supernatural into the mundane world, and the animal substrate beneath civilised life. Yeats' beasts range from the apocalyptic to the demonic to the merely creaturely, but they consistently mark a boundary where human order gives way to something older, darker, and more powerful.

## The Rough Beast: Apocalyptic Animality

The most famous beast in Yeats' canon is surely the "rough beast" of "The Second Coming" (1919), whose "hour come round at last" (046-412) announces the end of one historical cycle and the violent birth of another. The beast "slouches towards Bethlehem to be born" (046-413) — an anti-Nativity that inverts Christian eschatology into a vision of terrifying renewal. The rough beast is not evil in any simple sense; it is a force of history, indifferent to human morality, embodying what Yeats called the "fabulous, formless darkness" of the coming age.

The rough beast is preceded in "The Second Coming" by the falcon that "cannot hear the falconer" (046-393) — the first animal image of the poem, and one that establishes the theme of lost control. The falcon's spiralling flight away from its master prefigures the gyre's widening and the collapse of the centre. Together, the falcon and the rough beast form a diptych of animal imagery at the service of apocalyptic vision: the one centrifugal, the other centripetal; the one rising into abstraction, the other lumbering toward incarnation.

## The Sphinx and the Beast of Revelation

The rough beast draws on a specific visual source: "a shape with lion body and the head of a man" (046-405) — the sphinx, which Yeats saw as a symbol of the coming antithetical dispensation. This sphinx-like creature merges Yeats's reading of the Book of Revelation with his own system of historical gyres. In the biblical text, the beast from the abyss is a figure of persecution and blasphemy; in Yeats's reworking, it becomes a figure of necessary historical transformation — terrifying, but not evil. The "gaze blank and pitiless as the sun" (046-407) suggests a force beyond moral judgment, as indifferent and as powerful as the celestial body that governs the day.

## Beast and Demon

In "Michael Robartes and the Dancer," Yeats develops a more philosophically explicit beast-and-demon symbolism. The poem opposes the "crafty demon" and the "loud beast" (046-343) as two aspects of the anti-rational — the demon of intellectual pride and the beast of bodily appetite. The "loud beast" that "ran" (046-357) through history is the force of unthinking violence and desire that civilised order can never fully contain. Yeats asks whether life "belongs to beast or demon" (046-376), framing the animal and the demonic as twin threats to the integrated soul. This dualism draws on the Western philosophical tradition that distinguishes the rational human from both bestial appetite below and demonic pride above.

The beast-demon opposition also resonates with Yeats's reading of Plato. In the *Phaedrus*, the soul is figured as a charioteer driving two horses — one noble, one base. Yeats reworks this classical image into a more violent, more historically charged opposition. The "loud beast" is not merely appetite but the force of collective violence: war, revolution, the mob. It is what breaks through when the "ceremony of innocence is drowned" — the beast that history unleashes when civilised forms collapse.

## The Beast as Historical Force

Beyond its apocalyptic and philosophical registers, the beast in Yeats serves as an image of historical process itself — the blind, amoral energy that drives one era into the next. In "A Dialogue of Self and Soul," the speaker imagines being "always happy like a bird or a beast" (045-1021), using bestial simplicity as a counter-image to the tormented self-consciousness of the human. The beast here represents not violence but freedom from the burden of reflection — an enviable state, if unreachable.

In "Sailing to Byzantium," the aged speaker laments being "fastened to a dying animal" (047-022) — the body itself as beast, the mortal frame that drags the soul toward death. This is perhaps Yeats's most intimate deployment of beast imagery: not the historical beast of apocalypse, nor the philosophical beast of appetite, but the creaturely beast of one's own flesh, aging and failing while the spirit rages on.

## Creaturely Beasts

Yeats also deploys beast imagery in more muted, creaturely registers. In "Crossways," the poet envisions a world "from girl and boy and man and beast" (032-228) — the beast as simply the fourth term in a chain of created being. "The Happy Townland" mentions the "beast of the wilderness, bird of the air" (038-507) in a catalogue of natural freedom. In "The Old Age of Queen Maeve," a god "threw down the beast" (044-197) beneath a beech bole in an act of mythological violence. The goatherd in "Shepherd and Goatherd" declares that "nor bird nor beast" (045-323) can match the complexity of human grief. An "invisible beast or bird" (046-109) terrifies in a visionary moment, its very invisibility amplifying its menace. These creaturely appearances register the presence of the animal as the other against which the human defines itself — but also as a mode of being that, in its simplicity, can seem enviable.
