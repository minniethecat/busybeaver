# Ireland

Ireland is not merely the setting of W. B. Yeats's poetry but its primary subject — the wounded nation whose mythology he recovered, whose landscape he consecrated in verse, whose political struggles he chronicled with ambivalent passion, and whose "terrible beauty" [044-426] he made into one of the permanent phrases of the English language. From the Celtic Twilight of the 1890s to the post-1916 poems of national reckoning, Yeats's engagement with Ireland passed through the phases of romantic nationalism, cultural institution-building, political disillusionment, and finally a hard-won, elegiac love for "Romantic Ireland" [044-426] that was, he knew, already dead at the moment of its being sung.

## Introduction

Yeats was born in Sandymount, Dublin, in 1865, into a Protestant Ascendancy family in an overwhelmingly Catholic country — an identity that shaped his lifelong position as both insider and outsider in Irish life. His father, the painter John Butler Yeats, raised him in a household that valued art above property, and the young Yeats spent formative summers in [[sligo-county|Sligo]], his mother's home county, where he first encountered the folk tales and fairy lore that would become the foundation of his early poetry.

Ireland in Yeats's lifetime was a nation in convulsion: the Land War of the 1880s, the fall of Parnell in 1890, the founding of the [[abbey-theatre]] in 1904, the Home Rule crisis of 1912–14, the Easter Rising of 1916, the War of Independence (1919–1921), the Civil War (1922–1923), and the establishment of the Irish Free State. Yeats was not a spectator to these events: he was a senator of the Free State (1922–1928), a Nobel laureate who was received as the nation's poet (1923), and a controversial public figure whose politics veered from cultural nationalism to a disillusioned, aristocratic disdain for mass democracy.

## Ireland in the Politics of the Poetry

Yeats's political relationship with Ireland was fraught. He was a nationalist who despised the nationalist Catholic middle class; a Protestant who rejected Protestant materialism; a senator who defended the Anglo-Irish Ascendancy while celebrating the mythological Ireland of the pre-Christian *Tuatha Dé Danann*. These contradictions are not reconciled in the poetry — they are the engine of it.

The definitive statement of Yeats's Irish politics comes in "September 1913" (*Responsibilities*, 1914):

> "Romantic Ireland's dead and gone, / It's with O'Leary in the grave" [044-426]

The refrain — repeated three times, each iteration more devastating — is an elegy for an Ireland of heroic possibility that Yeats believed had been extinguished by the "fumbling in a greasy till" of the Catholic middle class. [[john-oleary]], the Fenian exile and mentor of Yeats's youth, stands as the emblem of an aristocratic nationalism of honour and sacrifice, against which the Ireland of 1913 is measured and found wanting.

Yet within three years of writing these lines, the Easter Rising of 1916 proved Yeats wrong — or, rather, proved that "Romantic Ireland" was not dead but had merely been waiting for the right sacrifice to resurrect it. "Easter 1916" (*Michael Robartes and the Dancer*, 1921) is Yeats's great poem of political astonishment: "All changed, changed utterly: / A terrible beauty is born." The phrase "terrible beauty" — an oxymoron that holds awe and horror in perfect suspension — became the definitive English-language formulation of political violence transfigured into art.

## "To Ireland in the Coming Times"

In the dedicatory poem that closes *The Rose* (1893), Yeats made a pledge that he spent the rest of his career fulfilling:

> "That sang to sweeten Ireland's wrong, / Ballad and story, rann and song" [031-535]

The poem — "To Ireland in the Coming Times" — is the manifesto of Yeats's Irish project: to be to Ireland what Dante was to Italy, what Shakespeare was to England. The poet's function is to "sweeten Ireland's wrong" — not to ignore the nation's wounds but to transform them into song, to give suffering the dignity of form. "Made Ireland's heart begin to beat" [031-544] — the claim is staggeringly ambitious, but the poetry that followed over the next forty years did, in fact, make Ireland's heart beat in a new rhythm.

> "And may the thoughts of Ireland brood / Upon a measured quietude" [031-547]

The closing couplet of this early poem already contains, in embryo, the entire trajectory of Yeats's Irish theme: the move from the romantic tumult of the 1890s toward the "measured quietude" of the late work, where political passion has been transfigured into the stillness of the perfected image.
