# Fenian Cycle in Yeats

The Fenian Cycle (*an Fhiannaíocht*) is the body of Irish mythology centred on Finn mac Cumhaill and his warrior band, the Fianna. For W. B. Yeats, the Fenian stories provided more than mythological ornament: they supplied a heroic counter-myth to modern Ireland's perceived spiritual decline, an elegiac framework for the poet's own meditations on ageing and loss, and — through the central figure of Oisin (Usheen) — the narrative vehicle of his first major long poem, "The Wanderings of Usheen" (1889).

## The Fenian Material in Irish Tradition

The Fenian Cycle occupies a distinct place in Irish literature. Unlike the Ulster Cycle with its courtly warriors, the Fenian tales are set in the wilderness: the Fianna hunt, fight, and feast in forests and on mountainsides. Yeats's Endnotes draw attention to the Fenian stories' continuing popular life: "there is no doubt, now, as they were in the Fenian days, a political force. I have heard the wild Fenian cry, 'The Fenians are abroad,' many times in my childhood in Sligo" [048-029]. The poet grew up hearing Fenian ballads sung at fairs and crossroads, and he understood the material as living folklore rather than dead antiquarianism.

## "The Wanderings of Usheen" — Yeats's Fenian Epic

Yeats's most sustained engagement with the Fenian Cycle is "The Wanderings of Usheen" (1889), a long narrative poem of over eight hundred lines that draws on the *Acallam na Senórach* ("Colloquy of the Ancients"). Yeats follows the framework in which Oisin, the Fenian poet, returns from three hundred years in the Land of Youth (*Tír na nÓg*) to a Christian Ireland and recounts his adventures to St. Patrick.

The poem is saturated with Fenian names and imagery. The hero invokes the great Fenian hounds: "I mused on the chase with the Fenians, and Bran, Sgeolan, Lomair" [015-654]. The Fenian lifestyle of hunting and feasting recurs throughout: "When the Fenians made foray at morning" [015-729] and "the Fenians' dwellings of wattle" [015-737] evoke the archaic simplicity of heroic Ireland. The "Fenian horses; armour torn asunder" [015-614] and the "mournful, laughing Fenian horn" [015-617] establish the cycle's characteristic blend of martial energy and elegiac lament.

## The Fenian Landscape

The Fenian world in Yeats's poetry is inseparable from a specific landscape — not the courtly interiors of the Ulster Cycle but the wild, open terrain of forests, mountains, and seashores. The Fenian chase is conducted in a natural world rendered with sensory immediacy: the "hazel and oak" [015-760] through which the hunters move, the "foray at morning" [015-729] that opens each day of the heroic life, and the shoreline where the aged Oisin finally collapses "far from the hazel and oak" [015-760].

This landscape is simultaneously Irish and mythological. Unlike the placeless allegorical settings of much nineteenth-century poetry, Yeats's Fenian world is rooted in specifically Irish topographies — the Sligo mountains, the western seacoast, the "Fenian lands" [015-699] that are both remembered and imagined. The "Fenians' dwellings of wattle" [015-737] evoke an archaic Irish architecture, a world before stone castles. And the "Fenian horn" [015-617] that sounds across mountains and valleys is the acoustic signature of a heroic society integrated with its natural environment. The Fenian landscape is, in effect, Yeats's first fully achieved imaginative geography — the precursor to the symbolic landscapes of Sligo, Innisfree, and the Tower that would dominate the later poetry.

## The Elegiac Register

The Fenian material operates most powerfully in an elegiac key. The poem's climactic recognition — that the Fenians are gone — is delivered as a devastating revelation:

> "The Fenians hunt wolves in the night,
> The daylight," I cried out, "is the Fenians'": a voice cried, "The Fenians a long time are dead." [015-779]

This moment of historical rupture — the recognition that the heroic age is irrecoverably past — is the emotional core of Yeats's Fenian poetry. The hero's final resolve to join the Fenians even in death — "I go to the Fenians, O cleric, to chaunt / The wars of the Fenians" [015-804] — asserts the continuing power of the heroic ideal even in a diminished world. The "sob for the Fenians' old strength" [015-792] is simultaneously personal (the aged Oisin mourning his lost youth) and cultural (the modern Irish poet mourning a vanished heroic tradition).

## Fenian Imagery in the Later Poetry

Though "The Wanderings of Usheen" remains Yeats's primary Fenian text, Fenian imagery persists across the career. In "The Hosting of the Sidhe," the host riding "from Knocknarea" recalls "Niamh calling 'Away, come away'" [038-004] — the Fenian fairy queen summoning mortals to the Otherworld. The figure of the solitary warrior-hunter, central to the Fenian ethos, reappears in the lean, muscular heroes of the later lyrics and plays. And the elegiac mode established in the Fenian poems — the singing of past greatness in a diminished present — becomes one of Yeats's signature tones.

## Fenians and the Political Imagination

Yeats's Fenian material carried political resonance in late nineteenth- and early twentieth-century Ireland. The Fenian Brotherhood — the Irish republican organisation active in the 1860s — had borrowed its name from the legendary *Fianna*, and for Yeats's generation the word "Fenian" was simultaneously mythological and revolutionary. The Endnotes record a childhood memory that bridges both registers: "there is no doubt, now, as they were in the Fenian days, a political force. I have heard the wild Fenian cry, 'The Fenians are abroad,' many times in my childhood in Sligo" [048-029].

This dual identity allowed Yeats to draw on Fenian mythology for nationalist purposes without reducing it to political allegory. The Fenian warrior who "comes from the Fenian lands" [015-699] is a figure of archaic heroism — "Ah, Fenians, with the shield and bow" [015-110] — but also of a specifically Irish greatness that answered the colonial denigration of Irish culture. Yeats's Fenians are not merely the legendary past: they are a counter-image to the degraded present, a measure of what Ireland has lost and might recover. When Oisin declares his intention to rejoin the Fenians even after death — "the limbs of the Fenians are tost" [015-814] by the waves — the gesture combines personal defiance with cultural nationalism.

## Fenian Echoes in the Mature Poetry

Although Yeats never returned to a sustained Fenian narrative after "The Wanderings of Usheen," Fenian imagery and the Fenian elegiac mode persist across his mature work. In "The Tower" (1928), the poet, now an old man like the returned Oisin, surveys his kingdom from the battlements and summons the heroic figures of the past — "I call up to the company of a miraculous great man" — in a gesture that echoes the aged Fenian poet's summoning of the lost warrior band. The tower itself, as a structure of both defence and vision, is a later incarnation of the Fenian architectural imagination that earlier produced the "dwellings of wattle" [015-737] and the open-air halls of the Fianna.

The elegiac register that "The Wanderings of Usheen" established — the singing of past greatness in a diminished present — becomes, in the mature poetry, one of Yeats's most distinctive tones. "The Tower," "Among School Children," and "The Circus Animals' Desertion" all participate in the Fenian elegiac mode: the poet, old and isolated, remembers or summons heroic energies that the present cannot sustain. When Yeats writes, in "The Circus Animals' Desertion," that he must "lie down where all the ladders start / In the foul rag and bone shop of the heart," he is performing the same gesture of return to origins that Oisin performs when he declares his intention to rejoin the Fenians in death.
