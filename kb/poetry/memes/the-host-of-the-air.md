# The Host of the Air

"The Host of the Air" is a lyric poem by W. B. Yeats, first published in *The Wind Among the Reeds* (1899), his most important collection of the Celtic Twilight period. The poem retells, in ballad form, an Irish folk story about the *slua sí* — the fairy host — who steal a mortal bride while her husband-to-be dreams. It is one of Yeats's most achieved early poems, combining the rhythmic simplicity of ballad tradition with the otherworldly atmosphere of Irish fairy lore and a characteristic Yeatsian note of dream-haunted loss.

## The Poem

The ballad tells the story of O'Driscoll, who "drove with a song / The wild duck and the drake / From the tall and the tufted reeds / Of the drear Hart Lake" [038-xxx]. Dreaming of his bride Bridget, he falls into a trance-like state and witnesses a supernatural scene: "young men and young girls / Who danced on a level place / And Bridget his bride among them, / With a sad and a gay face" [038-xxx]. The dancers are the *host of the air* — the fairy company — and they have claimed Bridget for their own.

The ballad's structure builds toward a devastating final image. O'Driscoll, lulled by "a piper piping away" — "never was piping so sad, / And never was piping so gay" — sits among "old men playing at cards / With a twinkling of ancient hands" while his bride is stolen. The "handsomest young man there" carries her away "in his arms," and the last glimpse we have of Bridget is her "long dim hair" [038-xxx] drowning the fairy lover's neck and breast and arms. O'Driscoll "scattered the cards / And out of his dream awoke" — but it is too late. The bride is gone, taken into the otherworld from which mortals do not return.

## Themes

**Dream and reality**: The poem operates in a liminal space between waking and dreaming, where the fairy world and the mortal world interpenetrate. O'Driscoll sings and dreams simultaneously; the fairy piper's music is both "sad" and "gay"; Bridget's face shows the same doubleness. This indeterminacy is the signature of Yeats's Celtic Twilight manner — a poetry that refuses to resolve the question of whether the supernatural is real or imagined, because in the world of Irish folk belief the question itself is wrongly posed.

**The fairy bride motif**: The story of a mortal man who loses his bride to the fairies is among the oldest in Irish folklore, related to the international tale-type of the "fairy lover" and to the Celtic tradition of the *otherworld*, a realm of eternal youth and beauty accessible through dreams, water, or mounds (sídhe). Yeats, who had collected folk tales in Sligo and Galway in the 1880s and 1890s, knew this tradition intimately. His source may have been a story recorded in Douglas Hyde's *Beside the Fire* (1890) or a tale told to him during his folklore-collecting journeys.

**Music and enchantment**: The piper's "piping" — "never was piping so sad, / And never was piping so gay" — is the classic Celtic Otherworld music, a tune that holds the listener in thrall. The doubling of "sad" and "gay" (used here in the older sense of "joyful") captures the essential ambiguity of fairy enchantment: it is beautiful and dangerous, seductive and fatal.

## Rhythm and Sound

The poem's four-stress ballad line, with its alternating rhymes, gives it a hypnotic, incantatory quality. The repetition of key phrases — "her long dim hair" (three times), "sad" and "gay" — produces a trance-like effect that mirrors the content: the reader, like O'Driscoll, is lulled into the dream. The "drear Hart Lake" establishes the poem's tonal register — drear, dim, dream — in a single adjective that looks forward to the "long dim hair" that will become the poem's visual refrain.
