# Wisdom in Yeats

Wisdom in Yeats's poetic system is not the rationality of the Enlightenment but an older, stranger faculty — Druidic, folkloric, "dreaming wisdom" ((031-052)) that comes through intuition, vision, and ancestral memory. It is opposed to "knowledge" in the modern sense: wisdom is "heavy like stone" ((042-026)), "half silence," resistant to articulation, carried in the body and the landscape rather than in books.

## Origins and Sources

- **Druidic tradition**: The wisdom of the pre-Christian Irish priesthood, keepers of oral knowledge, astronomical lore, and poetic craft
- **Folk wisdom**: The "man of wisdom" ((024-040)) in Irish folk tales — the wise peasant, the fairy doctor, the seanchai (storyteller)
- **Occult tradition**: Golden Dawn teachings, where wisdom is initiation into hidden truths, a gradual unveiling
- **Blakean "Wisdom of the Heart"**: Blake's opposition of imaginative wisdom to rational knowledge

## Symbolic Meaning

1. **Dreaming wisdom**: "learn the dreaming wisdom that is yours" ((031-052)) — wisdom comes through dream and vision, not through study. The poet accesses it in states between waking and sleeping.

2. **Ancient vs. modern**: Wisdom belongs to the old days, the pre-modern world — "Remember the wisdom out of the old days" ((038-248)). Modernity has lost it, but the poet can recover it through imagination.

3. **The silence of wisdom**: Wisdom is "heavy like stone, their wisdom being half silence" ((042-026)) — it cannot be fully articulated, only approached through symbol and suggestion.

4. **Love-born wisdom**: "I had the wisdom love brings forth" ((044-857)) — passion, suffering, devotion all generate their own knowledge, distinct from intellectual understanding.

## Development Across Yeats's Career

**Early period**: Wisdom is Druidic and folkloric — the fairies have "wild wisdoms" ((025-029)), the "man of wisdom" appears in folk-ballad contexts. Wisdom belongs to the supernatural and the pre-modern.

**Middle period**: Wisdom becomes increasingly esoteric. "The Secret Rose" equates wisdom with occult initiation — remembering "the wisdom out of the old days" is to remember a lost spiritual tradition.

**Late period**: Wisdom matures into lived experience. "I had the wisdom love brings forth" ((044-857)) — wisdom is no longer only ancient or occult, but also the hard-won knowledge of a life lived fully, including its failures and loves.
