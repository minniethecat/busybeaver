# Tuatha Dé Danann

The Tuatha Dé Danann are the ancient gods of Ireland — "the Tribes of the goddess Danu," as Yeats glosses the name, "or the Sidhe, from *Aes Sidhe*, or *Sluagh Sidhe*, the people of the Faery Hills" [048-010]. For Yeats, these were not literary decorations but living presences. He believed — or chose to believe — that the gods of pre-Christian Ireland still "ride the country as of old," travelling in the whirling wind, abducting brides, fighting invisible battles over the dying, and beckoning mortals into their deathless realm. The Tuatha Dé Danann are the centre of gravity around which Yeats's Irish mythology, folklore, and occultism all turn.

## Definition

In Yeats's usage, "Tuatha Dé Danann" denotes three overlapping entities: (1) **the mythological race** — the divine invaders who, according to the medieval Irish *Lebor Gabála Érenn* (Book of Invasions), conquered Ireland from the Fir Bolg and were themselves later driven underground by the Milesians, becoming the fairy folk of later tradition; (2) **the Sidhe of living folk belief** — the "good people," the fairy hosts whom Yeats's country informants described as riding out on the wind, stealing brides, and casting those they favoured into a magical sleep; and (3) **the symbolic forces** in Yeats's personal system — the Tuatha Dé Danann as "the powers of light, and warmth, and fruitfulness, and goodness," opposed eternally to the Fomor, "the powers of darkness, and cold, and barrenness, and badness" [048-013]. This triple identity — myth, folk belief, symbol — is what gives the Tuatha Dé Danann their peculiar density in Yeats's imagination.

## Origins and Sources

Yeats's knowledge of the Tuatha Dé Danann came through several channels:

- **Medieval Irish literature**: The *Lebor Gabála Érenn*, the *Cath Maige Tuired* (The Battle of Moytura), and the *Tochmarc Étaíne* (The Wooing of Étain) preserved the stories of the divine race — their arrival in Ireland, their battles with the Fomor, their relationships with mortals. Yeats encountered these through Standish O'Grady's retellings and Lady Gregory's *Gods and Fighting Men*.
- **Folk informants**: Yeats collected stories directly from country people in Galway, Sligo, and the Aran Islands. A woman near Gort described a boy who had been "away" among them for seven years; a man in Galway spoke of "the battle over the dying"; an Aran fisherman told of finding blood in his fish-box after the Sidhe fought over his children [048-013].
- **Occult interpretation**: Yeats read the Tuatha Dé Danann through the lens of Hermeticism, seeing them as initiates of a pre-Christian wisdom tradition. "The gods of ancient Ireland" were, in his interpretation, members of an ancient initiatic order whose knowledge survived — in degraded form — in the folk beliefs of the Irish peasantry.

## The Four Battles

In Yeats's most ambitious mythological synthesis — endnote 15 of his collected poems — he identifies four battles as manifestations of a single cosmic conflict:

> "I suggest that the battle between the Tuatha De Danaan, the powers of light, and warmth, and fruitfulness, and goodness, and the Fomor, the powers of darkness, and cold, and barrenness, and badness upon the Towery Plain, was the establishment of the habitable world, the rout of the ancestral darkness; that the battle among the Sidhe for the harvest is the annual battle of summer and winter; that the battle among the Sidhe at a man's death is the battle of life and death; and that the battle of the Black Pig is the battle between the manifest world and the ancestral darkness at the end of all things; and that all these battles are one, the battle of all things with shadowy decay." [048-013]

This fourfold schema — cosmogonic, seasonal, mortal, apocalyptic — is a compressed religious system. It maps the Tuatha Dé Danann onto a cosmic dualism that Yeats found confirmed in the folk beliefs he collected and in the occult traditions he studied.

## The Sidhe and the Living World

Yeats's Tuatha Dé Danann are not remote deities but active presences. In the long prose passage that constitutes endnote 10, he catalogues their interactions with the living world:

- They "ride the country as of old," travelling in whirling winds
- They steal brides "just after their marriage, and sometimes in a blast of wind"
- They cast those they favour into a "magical sleep" that may last for years
- They take the shape of fish to approach boats, and "those that are in the waters beckon to men, and drown them"
- They need "the help of a living person in most of the things they do" — the "away" are not lost to this world but conscripted into service in the Otherworld

The boundary between the Tuatha Dé Danann and the living is permeable, dangerous, and charged with desire. The old Gaelic song of Midher to Etain, which Yeats quotes in full, is an invitation to the deathless realm: "O beautiful woman, come with me to the marvellous land where one listens to a sweet music … Youth does not grow old there" [048-011].
