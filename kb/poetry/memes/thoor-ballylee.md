# Thoor Ballylee

Thoor Ballylee — "Yeats's Tower" — is a 15th-century Norman tower house near Gort, Co. Galway, purchased by Yeats for £35 in 1917. It became his summer home for over a decade and the most important single symbol of his late poetry. "I declare this tower is my symbol," he wrote, and the tower's stone walls, winding stair, stream, and surrounding countryside permeate the poems of _The Tower_ (1928) and _The Winding Stair and Other Poems_ (1933). Thoor Ballylee grounded Yeats's abstract philosophical system in stone and mortar — it was the physical manifestation of his commitment to rootedness, tradition, and the monumental.

## Significance to Yeats

Yeats bought Ballylee shortly after his marriage to Georgie Hyde-Lees in 1917, and the tower became a symbol of his new domestic stability. The "winding stair" that gives its name to his 1933 collection is the literal stair of the tower — narrow, spiraling, connecting the rooms where Yeats and his family lived. The stream that runs beside the tower, the "Ballylee stream," appears repeatedly in the poems as a symbol of passing time and the continuity of life.

The tower also functioned as a connection to Irish history. As a Norman structure, it linked Yeats not to the mythological Gael but to the Anglo-Irish tradition of Swift, Goldsmith, and Burke — the Protestant Ascendancy that he increasingly identified with in his later years. Living in a tower was an act of imaginative affiliation: the poet in his stone fastness, surveying the surrounding countryside, writing poems that would outlast the civilization that produced them.

The tower's stone walls also literalize Yeats's late aesthetic — the hard, monumental, unyielding quality that replaced the dreamy fluidity of the early work. "The Tower" (1928) is a poem about aging, about the body's decay, but the tower itself — "this tower is my symbol" — stands against time, as permanent as art.

## Literary and Historical Context

Thoor Ballylee was one of many Norman tower houses scattered across the Irish countryside — remnants of the medieval Anglo-Norman invasion. By occupying and restoring one, Yeats was making a statement about the legitimacy of the Anglo-Irish presence in Ireland. The tower was not an alien imposition but an authentic part of the Irish landscape, as ancient and rooted as any Gaelic ringfort.

After Yeats's death, the tower fell into disrepair but was later restored as a Yeats museum. It remains a pilgrimage site for readers of his poetry.
