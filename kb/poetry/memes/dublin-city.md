# Dublin

Dublin in W. B. Yeats's work is the city of institutions, intellect, and political crisis — the urban counterweight to the mythological west of [[sligo-county|Sligo]] and [[coole-park]]. It is the city where Yeats co-founded the [[abbey-theatre]] (1904), where he addressed the Young Ireland Society [048-003], where he fought the Hugh Lane Municipal Gallery controversy [048-089], and where the Easter Rising of 1916 transformed the streets he walked into the stage of national tragedy. If [[ireland]] is Yeats's ultimate subject, Dublin is its nerve-centre — the place where the poet's cultural ambitions met the friction of politics, journalism, and public opinion.

## Introduction

Yeats was born in Sandymount, then a coastal suburb of Dublin, in 1865. His family moved to London when he was two, but he returned to Dublin for extended periods — most importantly from 1881 to 1887, when he attended the Metropolitan School of Art and began to write seriously. The Dublin of Yeats's young manhood was a city of extraordinary intellectual energy: the home of Standish O'Grady, Douglas Hyde, John O'Leary, and the Fenian tradition; the headquarters of the Gaelic League and the cultural nationalist movement; and the stage upon which the drama of Irish independence would be played out in the streets.

Yeats's Dublin is not the city of Joyce's *Ulysses* — there is no "dear dirty Dublin" in Yeats, no labyrinth of pubs and quotidian detail. Yeats's Dublin is a city of thresholds and symbolic spaces: the Abbey Theatre stage, the Municipal Gallery, the General Post Office, the drawing-rooms where literary societies met. It is a city experienced primarily through its institutions.

## Dublin and the Literary Revival

Yeats addressed the Young Ireland Society in Dublin early in his career, and the experience shaped his understanding of the relationship between poetry and public life. The lecture, later revised as the essay "Ireland and the Arts," articulates a cultural programme: Irish art must be "as easily understood" [048-003] as the Young Ireland ballads of the 1840s, but it must also be "educated" — a word that, for Yeats, meant steeped in the esoteric and symbolic traditions he himself had cultivated.

The founding of the Abbey Theatre in 1904 was the practical realisation of this programme. The theatre, housed first in the Mechanics' Institute on Abbey Street, became the institutional engine of the Irish dramatic movement. Yeats's own plays — *Cathleen ni Houlihan* (1902), *On Baile's Strand* (1904), *Deirdre* (1907) — were written for the Abbey stage, and the controversies that attended them (the Playboy riots of 1907, the protests against *The Plough and the Stars* in 1926) were Dublin controversies, fought in Dublin newspapers and Dublin streets.

## The Hugh Lane Controversy

One of the defining Dublin episodes of Yeats's career was the long battle over Hugh Lane's collection of modern paintings. Lane, Lady Gregory's nephew, had offered his collection of Impressionist paintings to the city of Dublin on condition that a suitable gallery be built. When the Dublin Corporation refused to fund a gallery, the paintings were removed to London, and a decades-long controversy ensued.

Yeats's involvement was personal and passionate. He wrote poems about the controversy — most famously "To a Wealthy Man Who Promised a Second Subscription to the Dublin Municipal Gallery if It Were Proved the People Wanted Pictures" (1913) — and the episode became, in his mind, a symbol of Dublin's philistinism and ingratitude. The wealthy man of the poem stands for everything Yeats despised in the Dublin middle class: the refusal to risk public money on beauty. "Dublin is the capital of a nation" [048-049] — Yeats's insistence on this point was a demand that Dublin live up to its own significance.

## Dublin and the Easter Rising

The Easter Rising of April 1916 transformed Yeats's relationship with Dublin. The city he had known as the stage of cultural controversy became, overnight, the site of sacrifice. The leaders of the Rising — men Yeats had known personally: [[patrick-pearse]], Thomas MacDonagh, [[john-macbride]] — were executed in Kilmainham Gaol, and the poetry Yeats wrote in response ("Easter 1916," "Sixteen Dead Men," "The Rose Tree") marked a permanent shift in his voice.

Before 1916, Yeats could write of "Romantic Ireland's dead and gone" [044-426] with the scorn of the disappointed idealist. After 1916, the dead had answered him: "A terrible beauty is born" — a beauty inseparable from the Dublin streets where the blood was shed.
