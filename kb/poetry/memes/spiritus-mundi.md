# Spiritus Mundi

The Spiritus Mundi — Latin for "Spirit of the World" — is one of W. B. Yeats's most important occult concepts. Introduced through the fictional persona of Michael Robartes in the Endnotes to Yeats's collected poems, it designates "a general storehouse of images which have ceased to be a property of any personality or spirit" (048-054). The concept provides the metaphysical ground for Yeats's theory of dreams, visions, and poetic inspiration: the images that appear in sleep and in visionary states are not personal memories but are drawn from this transpersonal reservoir.

## Origins and Definition

The Spiritus Mundi is explained in the Endnotes in a long passage attributed to Michael Robartes, who writes to Owen Aherne about the classification of dreams. Robartes distinguishes between the "memory of concrete images" (personal memory) and the "abstract memory," and traces the source of dream images:

> "Those that come in sleep are (1) from the state immediately preceding our birth; (2) from the _Spiritus Mundi_ — that is to say, from a general storehouse of images which have ceased to be a property of any personality or spirit." (048-054)

The concept is Yeats's version of what the Neoplatonists called the *Anima Mundi* (World Soul) and what C. G. Jung would later call the collective unconscious — a shared reservoir of archetypal images accessible to all minds, but most directly to the dreamer and the poet.

## In the Poetry

The Spiritus Mundi enters Yeats's poetry most famously in "The Second Coming," where it is the source of a terrifying vision:

> "When a vast image out of Spiritus Mundi / Troubles my sight" (046-403)

The image that follows — "somewhere in sands of the desert / A shape with lion body and the head of a man" — is the Sphinx-like figure that precedes the "rough beast" slouching toward Bethlehem. The Spiritus Mundi is thus not a neutral storehouse but an active, troubling source of revelation: it delivers images that disrupt, prophesy, and overwhelm the individual consciousness.

The word "troubles" in Yeats's line is significant. The Spiritus Mundi does not merely supply the poet with convenient symbols; it disturbs, intrudes, breaks into ordinary consciousness. The poet is not the master of this faculty but its vessel — a medium through which transpersonal forces speak. This understanding of poetic inspiration as a form of involuntary mediumship aligns with Yeats's lifelong engagement with spiritualism and automatic writing, practices in which the conscious self steps aside to allow other intelligences to communicate.

## The Dream Theory

Robartes's letter develops a sophisticated theory of dream origins. The Spiritus Mundi supplies one class of dream images — those that come from before birth, from the general storehouse — but there is a second source: the "automatic faculty" that reshapes sense impressions "not of ourselves, but of others bound to us by certain emotional links" (048-054). This faculty creates "pattern, balance, etc." and deposits its creations in "a kind of impersonal mirror, often simply called the 'record.'"

This distinction — between the Spiritus Mundi (images from before birth, belonging to no one) and the "record" (images from others living now, preserved impersonally) — creates a nuanced topography of the unconscious that sets Yeats's system apart from simpler theories of collective memory. Robartes insists that "no lover, no husband has ever met in dreams the true image of wife or mistress" (048-054): the beloved who appears in dreams is always a counterfeit, drawn from the general storehouse and shaped by the Over Shadowers — the spirits of the dead connected to the dreamer by emotional bonds. This is the bleakest implication of the Spiritus Mundi doctrine: even the most intimate images of our inner lives are not our own.

## Function in Yeats's System

Within the larger architecture of *A Vision*, the Spiritus Mundi serves several functions:

- **Source of dream images**: It supplies the concrete forms that appear in sleep, which are never from personal memory — "no lover, no husband has ever met in dreams the true image of wife or mistress" (048-054).
- **Medium of the Over Shadowers**: The dead — spirits connected to the living by emotional bonds from past lives — draw forms from the Spiritus Mundi to influence the living, appearing in dreams as "counterfeits" of loved ones.
- **Poetic inspiration**: The poet's access to transpersonal images — the "vast image" of "The Second Coming" — is channelled through the Spiritus Mundi, making poetry a form of mediated revelation rather than mere personal expression.

## Spiritus Mundi and the Great Memory

The Spiritus Mundi is closely related to but distinct from the [[Great-Memory]] — Yeats's term for the ancestral memory preserved in the Irish folk tradition. Where the Great Memory stores the specific myths, legends, and race memories of a particular people, the Spiritus Mundi is universal: it holds forms that belong to no culture, no person, no time. The Endnotes clarify that the Great Memory is Yeats's adaptation of the Celtic doctrine of ancestral recollection, while the Spiritus Mundi is his version of the Neoplatonic World Soul. Both concepts serve Yeats's central conviction that the individual mind is not self-contained but porous — open to influx from realms beyond the personal — but they open onto different dimensions: the Great Memory onto the national past, the Spiritus Mundi onto the cosmic present.
