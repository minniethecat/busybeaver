# The Wind Among the Reeds[8](endnotes.xhtml#note-8)[9](endnotes.xhtml#note-9)

## The Hosting of the Sidhe[10](endnotes.xhtml#note-10)

[038-001]The host is riding from [[knocknarea-mountain|Knocknarea]]   

[038-002]And over the grave of Clooth-na-Bare;   

[038-003]Caolte tossing his [[fire-passion|burning]] hair   

[038-004]And [[niamh-goddess|Niamh]] calling "Away, come away:"   

[038-005]" Empty your heart of its mortal dream.   

[038-006]The winds awaken, the leaves whirl round,   

[038-007]Our cheeks are pale, our hair is unbound,   

[038-008]Our breasts are heaving, our eyes are agleam,   

[038-009]Our arms are waving, our lips are apart;   

[038-010]And if any gaze on our rushing band,   

[038-011]We come between him and the deed of his hand,   

[038-012]We come between him and the hope of his heart.   

[038-013]" The host is rushing ’twixt night and day,   

[038-014]And where is there hope or deed as fair?   

[038-015]Caolte tossing his burning hair,   

[038-016]And Niamh calling "Away, come away".

## The Everlasting Voices

[038-017]O sweet everlasting Voices, be still;   

[038-018]Go to the guards of the heavenly fold   

[038-019]And bid them wander obeying your will   

[038-020]Flame under flame, till Time be no more;   

[038-021]Have you not heard that our hearts are old,   

[038-022]That you call in birds, in wind on the hill,   

[038-023]In shaken boughs, in tide on the shore?   

[038-024]O sweet everlasting Voices, be still.

## The Moods

[038-025]Time drops in decay,   

[038-026]Like a candle burnt out,   

[038-027]And the mountains and woods   

[038-028]Have their day, have their day;   

[038-029]What one in the rout   

[038-030]Of the fire-born moods   

[038-031]Has fallen away?

## The Lover Tells of the Rose in His Heart

[038-032]All things uncomely and broken, all things worn out and old,   

[038-033]The cry of a child by the roadway, the creak of a lumbering cart,   

[038-034]The heavy steps of the ploughman, splashing the wintry mould,   

[038-035]Are wronging your image that blossoms a rose in the deeps of my heart.

[038-036]The wrong of unshapely things is a wrong too great to be told;   

[038-037]I hunger to build them anew and sit on a green knoll apart,   

[038-038]With the earth and the sky and the water, remade, like a casket of gold   

[038-039]For my dreams of your image that blossoms a rose in the deeps of my heart.

## The Host of the Air[11](endnotes.xhtml#note-11)

[038-040]O’Driscoll drove with a song   

[038-041]The wild duck and the drake   

[038-042]From the tall and the tufted reeds   

[038-043]Of the drear Hart Lake.

[038-044]And he saw how the reeds grew dark   

[038-045]At the coming of night tide,   

[038-046]And dreamed of the long dim hair   

[038-047]Of Bridget his bride.

[038-048]He heard while he sang and dreamed   

[038-049]A piper piping away,   

[038-050]And never was piping so sad,   

[038-051]And never was piping so gay.

[038-052]And he saw young men and young girls   

[038-053]Who danced on a level place   

[038-054]And Bridget his bride among them,   

[038-055]With a sad and a gay face.

[038-056]The dancers crowded about him,   

[038-057]And many a sweet thing said,   

[038-058]And a young man brought him red wine   

[038-059]And a young girl white bread.

[038-060]But Bridget drew him by the sleeve,   

[038-061]Away from the merry bands,   

[038-062]To old men playing at cards   

[038-063]With a twinkling of ancient hands.

[038-064]The bread and the wine had a doom,   

[038-065]For these were the host of the air;   

[038-066]He sat and played in a dream   

[038-067]Of her long dim hair.

[038-068]He played with the merry old men   

[038-069]And thought not of evil chance,   

[038-070]Until one bore Bridget his bride   

[038-071]Away from the merry dance.

[038-072]He bore her away in his arms,   

[038-073]The handsomest young man there,   

[038-074]And his neck and his breast and his arms   

[038-075]Were drowned in her long dim hair.

[038-076]O’Driscoll scattered the cards   

[038-077]And out of his dream awoke:   

[038-078]Old men and young men and young girls   

[038-079]Were gone like a drifting smoke;

[038-080]But he heard high up in the air   

[038-081]A piper piping away,   

[038-082]And never was piping so sad,   

[038-083]And never was piping so gay.

## The Fisherman

[038-084]Although you hide in the ebb and flow   

[038-085]Of the pale tide when the moon has set,   

[038-086]The people of coming days will know   

[038-087][[About]] the casting out of my net,   

[038-088]And how you have leaped times out of mind   

[038-089]Over the little silver cords,   

[038-090]And think that you were hard and unkind,   

[038-091]And blame you with many bitter words.

## A Cradle Song

[038-092]The Danaan children laugh, in cradles of wrought gold,   

[038-093]And clap their hands together, and half close their eyes,   

[038-094]For they will ride the North when the ger-eagle flies,   

[038-095]With heavy whitening wings, and a heart fallen cold:   

[038-096]I kiss my wailing child and press it to my breast,   

[038-097]And hear the narrow graves calling my child and me.   

[038-098]Desolate winds that cry over the wandering [[water-imagery|sea]];   

[038-099]Desolate winds that hover in the flaming West;   

[038-100]Desolate winds that beat the doors of Heaven, and beat   

[038-101]The doors of Hell and blow there many a whimpering ghost;   

[038-102]O heart the winds have shaken; the unappeasable host   

[038-103]Is comelier than candles at Mother Mary’s feet.

## Into the Twilight

[038-104]Out-worn heart, in a time out-worn,   

[038-105]Come clear of the nets of wrong and right;   

[038-106]Laugh, heart, again in the grey twilight,   

[038-107]Sigh, heart, again in the dew of the morn.

[038-108]Your mother Eire is always young,   

[038-109]Dew ever shining and twilight grey;   

[038-110]Though hope fall from you and love decay,   

[038-111]Burning in fires of a slanderous tongue.

[038-112]Come, heart, where hill is heaped upon hill:   

[038-113]For there the mystical brotherhood   

[038-114]Of sun and moon and hollow and wood   

[038-115]And river and stream work out their will;

[038-116]And God stands winding His lonely horn,   

[038-117]And time and the world are ever in flight;   

[038-118]And love is less kind than the grey twilight   

[038-119]And hope is less dear than the dew of the morn.

## The Song of Wandering Aengus[12](endnotes.xhtml#note-12)

[038-120]I went out to the hazel wood,   

[038-121]Because a fire was in my head,   

[038-122]And cut and peeled a hazel wand,   

[038-123]And hooked a berry to a thread;   

[038-124]And when white moths were on the wing,   

[038-125]And moth-like stars were flickering out,   

[038-126]I dropped the berry in a stream   

[038-127]And caught a little silver trout.

[038-128]When I had laid it on the floor   

[038-129]I went to blow the fire a-flame,   

[038-130]But something rustled on the floor,   

[038-131]And someone called me by my name:   

[038-132]It had become a glimmering girl   

[038-133]With apple blossom in her hair   

[038-134]Who called me by my name and ran   

[038-135]And faded through the brightening air.

[038-136]Though I am old with wandering   

[038-137]Through hollow lands and hilly lands,   

[038-138]I will find out where she has gone,   

[038-139]And kiss her lips and take her hands;   

[038-140]And walk among long dappled grass,   

[038-141]And pluck till time and times are done   

[038-142]The silver apples of the moon,   

[038-143]The golden apples of the sun.

## The Song of the Old Mother

[038-144]I rise in the dawn, and I kneel and blow   

[038-145]Till the seed of the fire flicker and glow;   

[038-146]And then I must scrub and bake and sweep   

[038-147]Till stars are beginning to blink and peep;   

[038-148]And the young lie long and dream in their bed   

[038-149]Of the matching of ribbons for bosom and head,   

[038-150]And their day goes over in idleness,   

[038-151]And they sigh if the wind but lift a tress:   

[038-152]While I must work because I am old,   

[038-153]And the seed of the fire gets feeble and cold.

## The Heart of the Woman

[038-154]O what to me the little room   

[038-155]That was brimmed up with prayer and rest;   

[038-156]He bade me out into the gloom,   

[038-157]And my breast lies upon his breast.

[038-158]O what to me my mother’s care,   

[038-159]The house where I was safe and warm;   

[038-160]The shadowy blossom of my hair   

[038-161]Will hide us from the bitter storm.

[038-162]O hiding hair and dewy eyes,   

[038-163]I am no more with life and death,   

[038-164]My heart upon his warm heart lies,   

[038-165]My breath is mixed into his breath.

## The Lover Mourns for the Loss of Love

[038-166]Pale brows, still hands and dim hair,   

[038-167]I had a beautiful friend   

[038-168]And dreamed that the old despair   

[038-169]Would end in love in the end:   

[038-170]She looked in my heart one day   

[038-171]And saw your image was there;   

[038-172]She has gone weeping away.

## He Mourns for the Change That Has Come Upon Him and His Beloved and Longs for the End of the World[13](endnotes.xhtml#note-13)

[038-173]Do you not hear me calling, white deer with no horns!   

[038-174]I have been changed to a hound with one red ear;   

[038-175]I have been in the Path of Stones and the Wood of Thorns,   

[038-176]For somebody hid hatred and hope and [[love-in-yeats|desire]] and fear   

[038-177]Under my feet that they follow you night and day.   

[038-178]A man with a hazel wand came without sound;   

[038-179]He changed me suddenly; I was looking another way;   

[038-180]And now my calling is but the calling of a hound;   

[038-181]And Time and Birth and Change are hurrying by.   

[038-182]I would that the Boar without bristles had come from the West   

[038-183]And had rooted the sun and moon and stars out of the sky   

[038-184]And lay in the darkness, grunting, and turning to his rest.

## He Bids His Beloved Be at Peace

[038-185]I hear the Shadowy Horses, their long manes a-shake,   

[038-186]Their hoofs heavy with tumult, their eyes glimmering white;   

[038-187]The North unfolds above them clinging, creeping night,   

[038-188]The East her hidden joy before the morning break,   

[038-189]The West weeps in pale dew and sighs passing away,   

[038-190]The South is pouring down roses of crimson fire:   

[038-191]O vanity of Sleep, Hope, [[dream-and-vision|Dream]], endless Desire,   

[038-192]The Horses of Disaster plunge in the heavy clay:   

[038-193]Beloved, let your eyes half close, and your heart beat   

[038-194]Over my heart, and your hair fall over my breast,   

[038-195]Drowning love’s lonely hour in deep twilight of rest,   

[038-196]And hiding their tossing manes and their tumultuous feet.

## He Reproves the Curlew

[038-197]O curlew, cry no more in the air,   

[038-198]Or only to the waters in the West;   

[038-199]Because your crying brings to my mind   

[038-200]Passion-dimmed eyes and long heavy hair   

[038-201]That was shaken out over my breast:   

[038-202]There is enough evil in the crying of wind.

## He Remembers Forgotten Beauty

[038-203]When my arms wrap you round I press   

[038-204]My heart upon the loveliness   

[038-205]That has long faded from the world;   

[038-206]The jewelled crowns that kings have hurled   

[038-207]In shadowy pools, when armies fled;   

[038-208]The love-tales wrought with silken thread   

[038-209]By dreaming ladies upon cloth   

[038-210]That has made fat the murderous moth;   

[038-211]The roses that of old time were   

[038-212]Woven by ladies in their hair,   

[038-213]The dew-cold lilies ladies bore   

[038-214]Through many a sacred corridor   

[038-215]Where such grey clouds of incense rose   

[038-216]That only the gods’ eyes did not close:   

[038-217]For that pale breast and lingering hand   

[038-218]Come from a more dream-heavy land,   

[038-219]A more dream-heavy hour than this;   

[038-220]And when you sigh from kiss to kiss   

[038-221]I hear white [[eternal-beauty|Beauty]] sighing, too,   

[038-222]For hours when all must fade like dew,   

[038-223]All but the flames, and deep on deep,   

[038-224]Throne over throne where in half sleep,   

[038-225]Their swords upon their iron knees,   

[038-226]Brood her high lonely mysteries.

## A Poet to His Beloved

[038-227]I bring you with reverent hands   

[038-228]The books of my numberless dreams;   

[038-229]White woman that passion has worn   

[038-230]As the tide wears the dove-grey sands,   

[038-231]And with heart more old than the horn   

[038-232]That is brimmed from the pale fire of time:   

[038-233]White woman with numberless dreams   

[038-234]I bring you my passionate rhyme.

## He Gives His Beloved Certain Rhymes

[038-235]Fasten your hair with a golden pin,   

[038-236]And bind up every wandering tress;   

[038-237]I bade my heart build these poor rhymes:   

[038-238]It worked at them, day out, day in,   

[038-239]Building a sorrowful loveliness   

[038-240]Out of the battles of old times.

[038-241]You need but lift a pearl-pale hand,   

[038-242]And bind up your long hair and sigh;   

[038-243]And all men’s hearts must burn and beat;   

[038-244]And candle-like foam on the dim sand,   

[038-245]And stars climbing the dew-dropping sky,   

[038-246]Live but to [[shadow-and-light|light]] your passing feet.

## To His Heart, Bidding It Have No Fear

[038-247]Be you still, be you still, trembling heart;   

[038-248]Remember the wisdom out of the old days:   

[038-249]" Him who trembles before the flame and the flood,   

[038-250]And the winds that blow through the starry ways,   

[038-251]Let the starry winds and the flame and the flood   

[038-252]Cover over and hide, for he has no part   

[038-253]With the lonely, majestical multitude. "

## The Cap and Bells[14](endnotes.xhtml#note-14)

[038-254]The jester walked in the garden:   

[038-255]The garden had fallen still;   

[038-256]He bade his soul rise upward   

[038-257]And stand on her window-sill.

[038-258]It rose in a straight blue garment,   

[038-259]When owls began to call:   

[038-260]It had grown wise-tongued by thinking   

[038-261]Of a quiet and light footfall;

[038-262]But the young queen would not listen;   

[038-263]She rose in her pale night gown;   

[038-264]She drew in the heavy casement   

[038-265]And pushed the latches down.

[038-266]He bade his heart go to her,   

[038-267]When the owls called out no more;   

[038-268]In a red and quivering garment   

[038-269]It sang to her through the door.

[038-270]It had grown sweet-tongued by dreaming,   

[038-271]Of a flutter of flower-like hair;   

[038-272]But she took up her fan from the table   

[038-273]And waved it off on the air.

[038-274]“I have cap and bells,” he pondered,   

[038-275]“I will send them to her and die”;   

[038-276]And when the morning whitened   

[038-277]He left them where she went by.

[038-278]She laid them upon her bosom,   

[038-279]Under a cloud of her hair,   

[038-280]And her red lips sang them a love-song:   

[038-281]Till stars grew out of the air.

[038-282]She opened her door and her window,   

[038-283]And the heart and the soul came through,   

[038-284]To her right hand came the red one,   

[038-285]To her left hand came the blue.

[038-286]They set up a noise like crickets,   

[038-287]A chattering wise and sweet,   

[038-288]And her hair was a folded flower   

[038-289]And the quiet of love in her feet.

## The Valley of the Black Pig[15](endnotes.xhtml#note-15)

[038-290]The dews drop slowly and dreams gather: unknown spears   

[038-291]Suddenly hurtle before my dream-awakened eyes,   

[038-292]And then the clash of fallen horsemen and the cries   

[038-293]Of unknown perishing armies beat about my ears.   

[038-294]We who still labour by the cromlec on the shore,   

[038-295]The grey cairn on the hill, when day sinks drowned in dew,   

[038-296]Being weary of the world’s empires, bow down to you,   

[038-297]Master of the still stars and of the flaming door.

## The Lover Asks Forgiveness Because of His Many Moods

[038-298]If this importunate heart trouble your peace   

[038-299]With words lighter than air,   

[038-300]Or hopes that in mere hoping flicker and cease;   

[038-301]Crumple the rose in your hair;   

[038-302]And cover your lips with odorous twilight and say,   

[038-303]“O Hearts of wind-blown flame!   

[038-304]O Winds, elder than changing of night and day,   

[038-305]That murmuring and longing came,   

[038-306]From [[stone-imagery|marble]] cities loud with tabors of old   

[038-307]In dove-grey faery lands;   

[038-308]From battle banners, fold upon purple fold,   

[038-309]Queens wrought with glimmering hands;   

[038-310]That saw young Niamh hover with love-lorn face   

[038-311]Above the wandering tide;   

[038-312]And lingered in the hidden desolate place,   

[038-313]Where the last Phoenix died   

[038-314]And wrapped the flames above his holy head;   

[038-315]And still murmur and long:   

[038-316]O Piteous Hearts, changing till change be dead   

[038-317]In a tumultuous song”:   

[038-318]And cover the pale blossoms of your breast   

[038-319]With your dim heavy hair,   

[038-320]And trouble with a sigh for all things longing for rest   

[038-321]The odorous twilight there.

## He Tells of a Valley Full of Lovers

[038-322]I dreamed that I stood in a valley, and amid sighs,   

[038-323]For happy lovers passed two by two where I stood;   

[038-324]And I dreamed my lost love came stealthily out of the wood   

[038-325]With her cloud-pale eyelids falling on dream-dimmed eyes:   

[038-326]I cried in my dream, "O women, bid the young men lay"   

[038-327]" Their heads on your knees, and drown their eyes with your hair,   

[038-328]Or remembering hers they will find no other face fair   

[038-329]Till all the valleys of the world have been withered away. "

## He Tells of the Perfect Beauty

[038-330]O cloud-pale eyelids, dream-dimmed eyes,   

[038-331]The poets labouring all their days   

[038-332]To build a perfect beauty in rhyme   

[038-333]Are overthrown by a woman’s gaze   

[038-334]And by the unlabouring brood of the skies:   

[038-335]And therefore my heart will bow, when dew   

[038-336]Is dropping sleep, until God burn time,   

[038-337]Before the unlabouring stars and you.

## He Hears the Cry of the Sedge

[038-338]I wander by the edge   

[038-339]Of this desolate lake   

[038-340]Where wind cries in the sedge:   

[038-341]" Until the axle break   

[038-342]That keeps the stars in their round,   

[038-343]And hands hurl in the deep   

[038-344]The banners of East and West,   

[038-345]And the girdle of light is unbound,   

[038-346]Your breast will not lie by the breast   

[038-347]Of your beloved in sleep. "

## He Thinks of Those Who Have Spoken Evil of His Beloved

[038-348]Half close your eyelids, loosen your hair,   

[038-349]And dream about the great and their pride;   

[038-350]They have spoken against you everywhere,   

[038-351]But weigh this song with the great and their pride;   

[038-352]I made it out of a mouthful of air,   

[038-353]Their children’s children shall say they have lied.

## The Blessed

[038-354]Cumhal called out, bending his head,   

[038-355]Till Dathi came and stood,   

[038-356]With a blink in his eyes at the cave mouth,   

[038-357]Between the wind and the wood.

[038-358]And Cumhal said, bending his knees,   

[038-359]“I have come by the windy way   

[038-360]To gather the half of your blessedness   

[038-361]And learn to pray when you pray.

[038-362]“I can bring you salmon out of the streams   

[038-363]And heron out of the skies.”   

[038-364]But Dathi folded his hands and smiled   

[038-365]With the secrets of God in his eyes.

[038-366]And Cumhal saw like a drifting smoke   

[038-367]All manner of blessed souls,   

[038-368]Women and children, young men with books,   

[038-369]And old men with croziers and stoles.

[038-370]“Praise God and God’s mother,” Dathi said,   

[038-371]“For God and God’s mother have sent   

[038-372]The blessedest souls that walk in the world   

[038-373]To fill your heart with content.”

[038-374]“And which is the blessedest,” Cumhal said,   

[038-375]“Where all are comely and good?   

[038-376]Is it these that with golden thuribles   

[038-377]Are [[song-and-poetry|singing]] about the wood?”

[038-378]“My eyes are blinking,” Dathi said,   

[038-379]“With the secrets of God half blind,   

[038-380]But I can see where the wind goes   

[038-381]And follow the way of the wind;

[038-382]“And blessedness goes where the wind goes,   

[038-383]And when it is gone we are dead;   

[038-384]I see the blessedest soul in the world   

[038-385]And he nods a drunken head.

[038-386]“O blessedness comes in the night and the day   

[038-387]And whither the wise heart knows;   

[038-388]And one has seen in the redness of wine   

[038-389]The Incorruptible [[rose-symbol|Rose]],

[038-390]“That drowsily drops faint leaves on him   

[038-391]And the sweetness of desire,   

[038-392]While time and the world are ebbing away   

[038-393]In twilights of dew and of fire.”

## The Secret Rose[16](endnotes.xhtml#note-16)

[038-394]Far off, most secret, and inviolate Rose,   

[038-395]Enfold me in my hour of hours; where those   

[038-396]Who sought thee in the Holy Sepulchre,   

[038-397]Or in the wine vat, dwell beyond the stir   

[038-398]And tumult of defeated dreams; and deep   

[038-399]Among pale eyelids, heavy with the sleep   

[038-400]Men have named beauty. Thy great leaves enfold   

[038-401]The ancient beards, the helms of ruby and gold   

[038-402]Of the crowned Magi; and the king whose eyes   

[038-403]Saw the Pierced Hands and Rood of elder rise   

[038-404]In [[druidism-and-celtic-mysticism|Druid]] vapour and make the torches dim;   

[038-405]Till vain frenzy awoke and he died; and him   

[038-406]Who met Fand walking among flaming dew   

[038-407]By a grey shore where the wind never blew,   

[038-408]And lost the world and Emer for a kiss;   

[038-409]And him who drove the gods out of their liss,   

[038-410]And till a hundred morns had flowered red,   

[038-411]Feasted and wept the barrows of his dead;   

[038-412]And the proud dreaming king who flung the crown   

[038-413]And sorrow away, and calling bard and clown   

[038-414]Dwelt among wine-stained wanderers in deep woods;   

[038-415]And him who sold tillage, and house, and goods,   

[038-416]And sought through lands and islands numberless years,   

[038-417]Until he found with laughter and with tears,   

[038-418]A woman, of so shining loveliness,   

[038-419]That men threshed corn at midnight by a tress,   

[038-420]A little stolen tress. I, too, await   

[038-421]The hour of thy great wind of love and hate.   

[038-422]When shall the stars be blown about the sky,   

[038-423]Like the sparks blown out of a smithy, and die?   

[038-424]Surely thine hour has come, thy great wind blows,   

[038-425]Far off, most secret, and inviolate Rose?

## Maid Quiet

[038-426]Where has Maid Quiet gone to,   

[038-427]Nodding her russet hood?   

[038-428]The winds that awakened the stars   

[038-429]Are blowing through my blood.   

[038-430]O how could I be so calm   

[038-431]When she rose up to depart?   

[038-432]Now words that called up the lightning   

[038-433]Are hurtling through my heart.

## The Travail of Passion

[038-434]When the flaming lute-thronged angelic door is wide;   

[038-435]When an immortal passion breathes in mortal clay;   

[038-436]Our hearts endure the scourge, the plaited thorns, the way   

[038-437]Crowded with bitter faces, the wounds in palm and side,   

[038-438]The vinegar-heavy sponge, the flowers by Kedron stream;   

[038-439]We will bend down and loosen our hair over you,   

[038-440]That it may drop faint perfume, and be heavy with dew,   

[038-441]Lilies of death-pale hope, roses of passionate dream.

## The Lover Pleads with His Friend for Old Friends

[038-442]Though you are in your shining days,   

[038-443]Voices among the crowd   

[038-444]And new friends busy with your praise,   

[038-445]Be not unkind or proud,   

[038-446]But think about old friends the most:   

[038-447]Time’s bitter flood will rise,   

[038-448]Your beauty perish and be lost   

[038-449]For all eyes but these eyes.

## A Lover Speaks to the Hearers of His Songs in Coming Days

[038-450]O women, kneeling by your altar rails long hence,   

[038-451]When songs I wove for my beloved hide the prayer,   

[038-452]And smoke from this dead heart drifts through the violet air   

[038-453]And covers away the smoke of myrrh and frankincense;   

[038-454]Bend down and pray for all that sin I wove in song,   

[038-455]Till the Attorney for Lost Souls cry her sweet cry,   

[038-456]And call to my beloved and me: “No longer fly   

[038-457]Amid the hovering, piteous, penitential throng.”

## The Poet Pleads with the Elemental Powers[17](endnotes.xhtml#note-17)

[038-458]The Powers whose name and shape no living creature knows   

[038-459]Have pulled the Immortal Rose;   

[038-460]And though the Seven Lights bowed in their dance and wept,   

[038-461]The Polar Dragon slept,   

[038-462]His heavy rings uncoiled from glimmering deep to deep:   

[038-463]When will he wake from sleep?

[038-464]Great Powers of falling wave and wind and windy fire,   

[038-465]With your harmonious choir   

[038-466]Encircle her I love and sing her into peace,   

[038-467]That my old care may cease;   

[038-468]Unfold your flaming wings and cover out of sight   

[038-469]The nets of day and night.

[038-470]Dim Powers of drowsy thought, let her no longer be   

[038-471]Like the pale cup of the sea,   

[038-472]When winds have gathered and sun and moon burned dim   

[038-473]Above its cloudy rim;   

[038-474]But let a gentle silence wrought with music flow   

[038-475]Whither her footsteps go.

## He Wishes His Beloved Were Dead

[038-476]Were you but lying cold and dead,   

[038-477]And lights were paling out of the West,   

[038-478]You would come hither, and bend your head,   

[038-479]And I would lay my head on your breast;   

[038-480]And you would murmur tender words,   

[038-481]Forgiving me, because you were dead:   

[038-482]Nor would you rise and hasten away,   

[038-483]Though you have the will of the wild birds,   

[038-484]But know your hair was bound and wound   

[038-485]About the stars and moon and sun:   

[038-486]O would, beloved, that you lay   

[038-487]Under the dock-leaves in the ground,   

[038-488]While lights were paling one by one.

## He Wishes for the Cloths of Heaven

[038-489]Had I the heavens’ embroidered cloths,   

[038-490]Enwrought with golden and silver light,   

[038-491]The blue and the dim and the dark cloths   

[038-492]Of night and light and the half light,   

[038-493]I would spread the cloths under your feet:   

[038-494]But I, being poor, have only my dreams;   

[038-495]I have spread my dreams under your feet;   

[038-496]Tread softly because you tread on my dreams.

## He Thinks of His Past Greatness When a Part of the Constellation of Heaven

[038-497]I have drunk ale from the Country of the Young   

[038-498]And weep because I know all things now:   

[038-499]I have been a hazel tree and they hung   

[038-500]The Pilot Star and the Crooked Plough   

[038-501]Among my leaves in times out of mind:   

[038-502]I became a rush that horses tread:   

[038-503]I became a man, a hater of the wind,   

[038-504]Knowing one, out of all things, alone, that his head   

[038-505]Would not lie on the breast or his lips on the hair   

[038-506]Of the woman that he loves, until he dies.   

[038-507]O beast of the wilderness, bird of the air,   

[038-508]Must I endure your amorous cries?

## The Fiddler of Dooney

[038-509]When I play on my fiddle in Dooney,   

[038-510]Folk dance like a wave of the sea;   

[038-511]My cousin is priest in Kilvarnet,   

[038-512]My brother in Mocharabuiee.

[038-513]I passed my brother and cousin:   

[038-514]They read in their books of prayer;   

[038-515]I read in my book of songs   

[038-516]I bought at the [[nature-in-yeats|Sligo]] fair.

[038-517]When we come at the end of time,   

[038-518]To Peter sitting in state,   

[038-519]He will smile on the three old spirits,   

[038-520]But call me first through the gate;

[038-521]For the good are always the merry,   

[038-522]Save by an evil chance,   

[038-523]And the merry love the fiddle   

[038-524]And the merry love to dance:

[038-525]And when the folk there spy me,   

[038-526]They will all come up to me,   

[038-527]With “Here is the fiddler of Dooney!”   

[038-528]And dance like a wave of the sea.
