# Constance Markievicz

Constance Georgine Markievicz (1868–1927), born Constance Gore-Booth at Lissadell House in County Sligo, was one of the most extraordinary figures of the Irish revolutionary generation — an Anglo-Irish aristocrat who became a republican militant, a suffragist, a commander in the Easter Rising of 1916, and the first woman elected to the British House of Commons (though, as a Sinn Féin member, she refused to take her seat). For W. B. Yeats, she was both a personal connection — he had known the Gore-Booth sisters at Lissadell in his youth — and a symbol of the transformation of the Anglo-Irish class from which he himself came.

## Early Life and Yeats's Lissadell Connection

The Gore-Booth family belonged to the Anglo-Irish Protestant Ascendancy, landowners at Lissadell in County Sligo, the county Yeats considered his spiritual home. As a young man, Yeats visited Lissadell and encountered the two Gore-Booth sisters — Constance and Eva — before either had taken the paths that would define them. The house and its occupants lodged in Yeats's memory as an image of aristocratic grace: the beautiful sisters in their silk kimonos, the great windows opening to the Atlantic light. This memory would, decades later, become the opening of one of his most powerful elegies.

The young Constance showed no early signs of the revolutionary she would become. She studied art at the Slade School in London and in Paris, where she met and married the Polish count Casimir Markievicz in 1900. It was only after returning to Ireland in 1903 that she was drawn into nationalist politics, joining Sinn Féin and Inghinidhe na hÉireann (Daughters of Ireland), the revolutionary women's organisation founded by Maud Gonne.

## In Yeats's Poetry

Markievicz and her sister Eva Gore-Booth appear directly in Yeats's elegy "In Memory of Eva Gore-Booth and Con Markievicz" (1929), written after Eva's death in 1926. The poem opens with an image of the two sisters at Lissadell in their youth: "The light of evening, Lissadell, / Great windows open to the south, / Two girls in silk kimonos, both / Beautiful, one a gazelle" [035-040]. The gazelle is Eva; the "one" is Constance — and the poem mourns not only the death of youth but the consuming fire of politics that transformed them. The young woman Yeats remembers ("Conspired among the ignorant") becomes the revolutionary who was sentenced to death for her part in the Easter Rising (the sentence was commuted to imprisonment because of her sex).

The poem is haunted by the question of what was lost. Yeats asks whether the "folly" of political commitment — "the folly that man does / Or must suffer" — was worth the cost. But the question is not rhetorical; it is genuinely anguished. Markievicz represents for the late Yeats the dangerous beauty of political commitment: the aristocratic spirit that, turned to revolution, burns through everything that made it beautiful.

The elegy also reflects Yeats's complex attitude toward the revolutionary generation that had remade Ireland. He admired their courage — Markievicz had faced a firing squad without flinching — but he mourned what the "ignorant" fire of politics had consumed. The poem's final image, of the two sisters as "two girls" whose youth was "burnt away," condenses a whole generation's transformation from the graceful certainties of the old Ascendancy world into the violent uncertainties of the new Ireland.

## Historical Role

Markievicz joined the Irish Citizen Army and fought in the St Stephen's Green garrison during the Easter Rising of 1916, serving as second-in-command to Michael Mallin. She was one of the few women to hold a command position in the Rising. During the fighting, she showed conspicuous bravery, and photographs of her in uniform became iconic images of the revolutionary period.

After the Rising, she was sentenced to death — a sentence commuted to life imprisonment on account of her sex; she reportedly told her captors, "I do wish your lot had the decency to shoot me." She was released in the general amnesty of 1917 and was elected to the First Dáil as a Sinn Féin member for Dublin St Patrick's in the 1918 general election — the first woman ever elected to the British House of Commons, though like all Sinn Féin members she refused to take her seat. She served as Minister for Labour in the First Dáil, becoming the first female cabinet minister in Europe. She opposed the Anglo-Irish Treaty of 1921 and fought on the republican side in the Irish Civil War, having aligned herself with the anti-Treaty faction led by Éamon de Valera.

Markievicz died in 1927 at the age of fifty-nine, in a public ward of a Dublin hospital — having given away most of her remaining wealth to the poor. Her funeral was a massive public event, a testament to her standing among Dublin's working class, whom she had served throughout her political career.

## The Yeatsian Paradox

Markievicz poses, for Yeats, a version of the problem that haunts all his late political poetry: the question of whether the transformative fires of history — revolution, war, the leveling of class distinctions — can be reconciled with the aristocratic values of grace, beauty, and cultivated individuality that he cherished. Maud Gonne posed the same question, and Yeats's meditation on Markievicz is in some ways an extension of that earlier, more personal wrestling. But Markievicz is different from Gonne: she is not the beloved but the member of his own class who crossed the line, the aristocrat who became a militant. Her story is, in miniature, the story of the Anglo-Irish Ascendancy itself — its transformation, its absorption into the new Ireland, and the loss that transformation entailed.
