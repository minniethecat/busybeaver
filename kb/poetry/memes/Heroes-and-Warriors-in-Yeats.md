# Heroes and Warriors in Yeats

Yeats returned throughout his career to figures of violent action and impossible courage — Cuchulain tying himself to a stone to die standing, the Fenian warriors of Irish legend, the "heroic" ideal he set against what he saw as the timidity of modern life. This page collects all wiki entries that illuminate the heroic imagination at the heart of his work.

## About This Page

The hero in Yeats is never merely a fighter. Cuchulain is the embodiment of the "dreaming back" after death; the Fenian Cycle provides a mythic template for Irish nationalism; the warrior's tragic joy — fighting a losing battle with style — becomes a model for the artist's own stance. The entries below cover the mythic heroes, the historical revolutionaries Yeats elevated to heroic status, and the recurring image of the horseman, the sword, and the "heroic cry."

For the mythological framework behind these figures, see [[irish-mythology]]. For the political nationalism their stories inspired, see [[Irish-History-and-Politics]].
