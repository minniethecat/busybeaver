Jonathan Swift (30 November 1667 – 19 October 1745) was an Anglo-Irish clergyman, satirist, essayist, and political pamphleteer — author of _Gulliver's Travels_, _A Modest Proposal_, and _The Drapier's Letters_. For W. B. Yeats, Swift was far more than a canonical figure in English literature. He was the first of the "Seven Sages" — the Anglo-Irish writers and thinkers of the eighteenth century whom Yeats celebrated as the true spiritual ancestors of Irish intellectual independence. Yeats read Swift not as an Augustan ironist but as a tragic hero of the imagination: a man of terrible clarity, driven by rage at human folly, who saw through all consolations and lived in a "bitter wisdom" that Yeats came to recognise as his own condition in old age.

## Biographical Summary

Swift was born in Dublin to English parents and educated at Kilkenny College and Trinity College Dublin. He served as secretary to Sir William Temple at Moor Park in England, where he met Esther Johnson (the "Stella" of his journals), and was ordained in the Church of Ireland in 1695. His career was divided between England and Ireland: he was a key figure in the Tory ministry of 1710–1714, editing _The Examiner_ and writing political pamphlets, before being "exiled" to the deanship of St. Patrick's Cathedral in Dublin — a post he had hoped to exchange for a bishopric in England.

It was in Dublin that Swift became the unlikely champion of Irish resistance to English economic exploitation. _The Drapier's Letters_ (1724–1725) mobilised public opposition to Wood's halfpence — a debased coinage scheme — and made Swift a nationalist hero. _A Modest Proposal_ (1729), the most savage satire in the English language, exposed the brutality of English economic policy toward Ireland by pretending to endorse cannibalism as a rational solution to poverty.

Swift's final years were marked by increasing deafness, dizziness, and probable dementia. He died in 1745 and was buried in St. Patrick's Cathedral under an epitaph he wrote himself: "Ubi saeva indignatio ulterius cor lacerare nequit" — "Where savage indignation can no longer tear his heart."

## Yeats and Swift

Yeats's identification with Swift intensified dramatically in his late years. What drew Yeats was not the Augustan satirist — the cool ironist of polite literary convention — but the Swift of "savage indignation," the man who could not reconcile his love of rational order with his knowledge of human depravity. Yeats saw in Swift a mirror of his own predicament: the aging artist, isolated from his society, raging against the degradation of the world he had loved, and transforming that rage into art.

**Swift as Ascendancy hero**: In "The Seven Sages" (1933), Yeats assembles a personal canon of Anglo-Irish heroism: Swift, Berkeley, Burke, Goldsmith, Grattan, and himself. These are not merely writers but "walking mummies" — figures whose intellectual passion persists beyond death:

> "The seventeenth-century madness out of which /
> Our eighteenth-century clarity was born /
> Walked hand in hand with clarity to church."

**Swift and the body**: Yeats was fascinated by Swift's obsession with bodily disgust — the scatological poems, the horror at the female body in "The Lady's Dressing Room," the vision of humanity as "the most pernicious race of little odious vermin that nature ever suffered to crawl upon the surface of the earth." Yeats, in his late poems, shares this disgust: "Old men are the only poets of what's past" is the voice of a man who has looked unflinchingly at the aging body and found no consolation but the "foul rag and bone shop of the heart."

**Swift's epitaph**: Yeats translated Swift's Latin epitaph and incorporated its phrase "saeva indignatio" (savage indignation) into his own self-understanding. In "The Words Upon the Window-Pane" (1934), a one-act play about a séance in which Swift's spirit is contacted, Yeats makes Swift speak of the "folly" of trying to serve Ireland — and the impossibility of doing otherwise.

## Yeats's View of Swift

Yeats's Swift is a deliberately partial reading — he ignores the playful Swift, the Swift of the _Journal to Stella_ and the birthday poems, in favour of the tragic, raging Swift of the late satires and the epitaph. But this partiality is Yeats's method: he reads Swift not as a literary historian but as a poet in search of ancestors, and the Swift he finds is the Swift he needs — a model of how to grow old without consolation, how to transform anger into art, and how to belong to a country that will not have you.
