# Lough Gill

Lough Gill (Irish: _Loch Gile_, "Bright Lake") is a freshwater lake in Co. Sligo, surrounded by wooded hills and containing approximately 20 small islands, the most famous of which is Innisfree. The lake is the central water feature of Yeats's Sligo — the "lake water lapping with low sounds by the shore" that called him from the pavements of London. Along with Ben Bulben and Knocknarea, Lough Gill completes the triad of landscape presences that define Yeats Country.

## Location and Geography

- **Type**: real Irish location (lake)
- **Region**: Co. Sligo, Connacht (extends slightly into Co. Leitrim)
- **Area**: approx. 8 km × 2 km, with numerous wooded islands
- **Key island**: Innisfree (Inis Fraoigh), the setting of Yeats's most famous poem

## Significance to Yeats

Lough Gill was a constant presence in Yeats's Sligo childhood. The lake, with its islands, wooded shores, and the surrounding hills, formed the visual and sensory world that he would later transform into poetry. The sound of water — "lake water lapping" — became one of his most evocative images, carrying the weight of childhood memory, pastoral longing, and the contrast between Irish nature and English urban modernity.

The lake's islands, especially Innisfree, gave Yeats a powerful spatial symbol: the island as refuge, as separate world, as the place where the imagination can live on its own terms. This island symbolism recurs throughout his work and connects Lough Gill to the more fantastical islands of Irish myth (Tir na nÓg, the Isles of the Blessed).

## Literary and Historical Context

Lough Gill has become inseparable from "The Lake Isle of Innisfree" in literary tourism, but it was a meaningful landscape for Yeats well beyond that single poem. The lake and its surroundings represent the west of Ireland as a whole — the region of Gaelic tradition, folk belief, and natural beauty that Yeats and other Revival writers set against the anglicized, urbanized east.
