# Connacht and the West of Ireland

The West of Ireland — its bare hills, its lakes, its mountains with names out of legend — was for Yeats the sacred ground of his poetry. From childhood summers in Sligo to the tower at Ballylee in Galway, from Knocknarea's cairn to Coole Park's woods, Connacht provided the settings, the symbols, and the sense of place that anchor his most enduring work.

## About This Page

Yeats called the West "a place of the imagination." Sligo gave him Ben Bulben and the lake isle of Innisfree; Galway gave him Lady Gregory's Coole Park and his own tower at Ballylee; Knocknarea gave him the cairn of Queen Maeve and a landscape saturated with myth. The entries below cover the real places of Connacht — their geography, their history, and their transformation into the symbolic landscapes of the poems.

For the broader treatment of natural imagery, see [[Nature-and-Landscape-in-Yeats]]. For Sligo in particular, see [[Sligo-in-Yeats]].
