## About This Page

For Yeats, ceremony was not mere formality but a spiritual discipline. His admiration for the Anglo-Irish aristocracy — Lady Gregory, Coole Park, the eighteenth-century Big House — was rooted in a belief that custom and ritual preserve what is finest in a civilization. The "ceremony of innocence" drowned in "The Second Coming" is the same impulse that animates the dancers, horsemen, and scholars of his later verse: a world ordered by grace, not by utility.
