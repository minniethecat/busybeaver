## Introduction

Sato's sword is a key symbolic object in W. B. Yeats's later poetry, particularly in the poems collected in _[[The-Tower|The Tower]]_ (1928). The sword was a gift to Yeats from Junzo Sato, a Japanese scholar and diplomat, and appears as a central emblem in "Meditations in Time of Civil War" and the title poem "The Tower." An ancient Japanese ceremonial blade — "Curved like new moon, moon luminous" — it had "lay five hundred years" in Sato's house before coming to rest on Yeats's writing table at [[thoor-ballylee|Thoor Ballylee]], beside pen and paper [047-309] [047-310] [047-311].

## The Sword in "Meditations in Time of Civil War"

In Section III of "[[meditations-in-time-of-civil-war|Meditations in Time of Civil War]]," subtitled "My Table," Yeats places the sword prominently among his writing implements:

> Two heavy tressels, and a board
> Where Sato's gift, a changeless sword,
> By pen and paper lies,
> That it may moralise
> My days out of their aimlessness.
> [047-301] [047-302] [047-303] [047-304] [047-305]

The sword's purpose is explicitly moral: it sits beside the poet's writing materials to "moralise" his days, to give form and purpose to what might otherwise be aimless. A bit of embroidered dress covers its wooden sheath — a deliberate pairing of the martial and the domestic, the ancient and the intimate [047-306] [047-307].

## The Sword as Symbol of Changeless Art

The sword's antiquity gives it symbolic weight. It was forged before Chaucer drew breath, and in Sato's house it lay for five hundred years, "curved like new moon, moon luminous" [047-308] [047-309] [047-310] [047-311]. From this image Yeats develops a meditation on art and permanence:

> Yet if no change appears
> No moon; only an aching heart
> Conceives a changeless work of art.
> [047-312] [047-313] [047-314]

The paradox is central to Yeats's late aesthetic: the changeless work of art is not found in the world but conceived by the "aching heart" — human passion and longing are the true source of artistic permanence. The sword is not changeless because it is an object, but because it embodies a tradition of craftsmanship passed "from father unto son" through centuries [047-318] [047-319]. This hereditary transmission of skill — "a marvellous accomplishment" — created objects that "seemed unchanging like the sword" [047-317] [047-321].

## Junzo Sato and the Historical Gift

The historical Junzo Sato (1891–1973) was a Japanese philosopher and scholar of Irish literature. He visited Yeats in Ireland in the early 1920s and presented him with the sword as a token of esteem. The blade, a _katana_ or ceremonial sword, dated from approximately the fourteenth century — roughly contemporary with Chaucer. Sato later translated Yeats's work into Japanese and became an important figure in Irish-Japanese literary exchange. The gift embodied for Yeats the continuity of an aristocratic artistic tradition that he saw as vanishing in the modern West but persisting in Japan.

## The Sword and "The Tower"

In the title poem of the volume, Yeats returns to the sword imagery as he contemplates his aging body and the legacies of mind and art. The tower at Ballylee becomes the repository of emblems — the sword among them — that "exalt a lonely mind, / Befitting emblems of adversity" [047-298] [047-299]. The sword belongs to a constellation of tower emblems that includes the candle, the pen, the winding stair, and the stream below — all figures for the creative life lived in isolation and intensity.

## The Changeless and the Changing

The sword's symbolic meaning is inseparable from its relationship to change. "Soul's beauty being most adored, / Men and their business took / The soul's unchanging look" [047-322] [047-323] [047-324]. In an age of revolution and civil war — the poem was written during the [[Irish-Civil-War|Irish Civil War]] — the sword stands for what endures. Yet Yeats is characteristically ambivalent: the "changeless" quality is also a kind of death, a moon that shows "no change" and therefore "no moon" [047-312] [047-313]. True art requires the aching heart — the mortal, changing, passionate self — to conceive something that can endure.
