# Sligo in Yeats

County Sligo, on Ireland's north-west coast, was for W. B. Yeats not merely a place of childhood memory but the foundational landscape of his poetic imagination. "When I was a child at Sligo I could see above my grandfather's house a little column of smoke," he recalls in the Endnotes [048-052], and the sensory world of that Sligo childhood — the fairs, the mountain profiles, the stories told by the servant Mary Battle — fed decades of poetry. Sligo is the "Land of Heart's Desire" from which Yeats's imagination never fully departed.

## Sligo as Spiritual Geography

Yeats's Sligo is a landscape charged with the supernatural. The Endnotes record that Knocknarea mountain, visible from Sligo town, is "in Sligo" and that "the country people say that the stones, which are the size of a man's head, were put there by a man who wanted to know the size of the moon" [048-015]. The "man who lived at Lisadill, in Sligo" who "used to fall down in a fit" [048-029] is recorded as a figure of popular occultism. Sligo was, for Yeats, a landscape where the boundary between natural and supernatural was thin — a place where folklore was not literary affectation but lived reality.

The "Sligo fair" [038-516] where the speaker buys goods appears in the poetry as a site of ordinary commerce that also opens onto the extraordinary: the same fairs where Yeats heard Fenian ballads sung "at the Sligo fair" by "an old woman" [048-029] connected the commercial and the mythological, the everyday and the legendary.

## The Landscape of "The Hosting of the Sidhe"

Sligo's most charged appearance in Yeats's poetry is as the landscape of "The Hosting of the Sidhe" — the poem that opens *The Wind Among the Reeds* section:

> The host is riding from Knocknarea
> And over the grave of Clooth-na-Bare [038-001]

Knocknarea mountain — the flat-topped massif above Sligo Bay, crowned with Queen Maeve's cairn — becomes the point of departure for the fairy host. The Sidhe ride "over the grave of Clooth-na-Bare" (another Sligo landmark) and the poem's summons — "Away, come away" — issues from the Sligo landscape itself. The "birds from Knocknarea" [032-413] appear in the earlier "Crossways" section, already linking the Sligo mountain to visionary flight.

## Sligo and the "Poor Country People"

Yeats's Sligo is a landscape populated by peasants, traders, and storytellers. "The Ballad of Moll Magee" is set among the Sligo poor: "a woman at Ballisodare in County Sligo" [048-018] who murders her child. The poem's sympathy with rural poverty and its violence — "the take to Sligo town to be sold" [032-378] — shows Sligo not only as mythological ground but as a place of real economic life, where carts carry goods to market and women drown infants in desperation.

The Endnotes mention "Father O'Hart, priest of Coloony, Sligo" [048-009] — a folk figure remembered for his eccentricity and his poems, one of the many Sligo characters whose stories fed Yeats's imagination. And "on the open road, near to the Sligo quay" [046-158] appears in the poetry as a specific, localised coordinate — not symbolic but actual.

## Sligo and Irish Mythology

Sligo is also dense with mythological association. Knocknarea mountain is the burial place of Queen Maeve of Connacht, the formidable warrior-queen who dominates the Ulster Cycle and who appears in Yeats's "The Old Age of Queen Maeve." The cairn atop Knocknarea — visible from Sligo town — is a constant physical reminder of the mythological past. When Yeats writes of the "host" riding from Knocknarea, he activates both the visible landmark and its mythological weight: the Sidhe issue from the very mountain where Maeve lies buried.

## Sligo in Yeats's Occult Formation

Sligo's importance to Yeats extends beyond mythology into the occult traditions that shaped his mature thought. The Endnotes record that "a man who lived at Lisadill, in Sligo, used to fall down in a fit and talk of the city of the Sidhe" [048-029] — a figure of the peasant visionary whose trance-states gave Yeats his first encounter with the idea that the supernatural could break into everyday consciousness. The Sligo countryside was, for the young Yeats, a place where second sight was not exceptional but ordinary: the servants told fairy stories as fact, not fancy, and the landscape itself — with its megalithic cairns, its fairy thorn trees, its "gentle" places where the Sidhe were said to dance — was a living map of the invisible world.

This early exposure to popular occultism shaped Yeats's later engagement with the Hermetic Order of the Golden Dawn, Theosophy, and automatic writing. The Sligo peasant's trance-utterance about the "city of the Sidhe" is structurally identical to the mediumistic communications Yeats would later receive from his wife George: in both cases, a human subject in an altered state becomes the channel for otherworldly speech. Sligo provided not just the imagery of Yeats's occult poetry but the experiential model — the conviction that the boundary between worlds was permeable — that underlay his entire symbolic system.

## Sligo and the Politics of the West

Yeats's Sligo is also ideological: the west of Ireland was, in the cultural politics of the Irish Revival, the repository of authentic Irishness — Gaelic-speaking, Catholic or folk-religious, untouched by English urban modernity. Yeats's repeated insistence on Sligo as the source of his imaginative life participates in this Revivalist ideology. The "Sligo fair" [038-516], the "open road, near to the Sligo quay" [046-158], the peasant storytellers and fairy-doctors — all belong to a west that the Revival constructed as the antidote to Anglicisation.

Yet Yeats's Sligo is more than ideology. The specificity of the place-names — Ballisodare, Coloony, Lisadill, Knocknarea — resists abstraction into mere symbol. "That carried the take to Sligo town to be sold" [032-378] records an economic transaction, not a mystical experience. The "take" (a dialect word for the catch or haul) being carted to market is the actual Sligo: a town where things are bought and sold, a port with quays and warehouses, a county of small farms and market fairs. The mythological Sligo and the economic Sligo coexist in the poetry not as contradiction but as dual registration of a single place seen steadily and whole.
