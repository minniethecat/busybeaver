# The Second Coming

"The Second Coming" is a lyric poem by W. B. Yeats, first published in *The Dial* in November 1920 and subsequently collected in *Michael Robartes and the Dancer* (1921). Written in January 1919, in the immediate aftermath of the First World War and at the opening of the Irish War of Independence, it is one of the most anthologised and analysed poems in the English language. Its opening image — "Turning and turning in the widening gyre" [046-392] — and its closing vision of a "rough beast" [046-413] have become part of the shared vocabulary of modern poetry, invoked whenever the collapse of an old order seems imminent.

## The Poem

> Turning and turning in the widening gyre [046-392]  
> The falcon cannot hear the falconer; [046-393]  
> Things fall apart; the centre cannot hold; [046-394]  
> Mere anarchy is loosed upon the world, [046-395]  
> The blood-dimmed tide is loosed, and everywhere [046-396]  
> The ceremony of innocence is drowned; [046-397]  
> The best lack all conviction, while the worst [046-398]  
> Are full of passionate intensity. [046-399]  
>  
> Surely some revelation is at hand; [046-400]  
> Surely the Second Coming is at hand. [046-401]  
> The Second Coming! Hardly are those words out [046-402]  
> When a vast image out of Spiritus Mundi [046-403]  
> Troubles my sight: somewhere in sands of the desert [046-404]  
> A shape with lion body and the head of a man, [046-405]  
> A gaze blank and pitiless as the sun, [046-406]  
> Is moving its slow thighs, while all about it [046-407]  
> Reel shadows of the indignant desert birds. [046-408]  
> The darkness drops again; but now I know [046-409]  
> That twenty centuries of stony sleep [046-410]  
> Were vexed to nightmare by a rocking cradle, [046-411]  
> And what rough beast, its hour come round at last, [046-412]  
> Slouches towards Bethlehem to be born? [046-413]

## Structure and Form

"The Second Coming" is composed in a modified blank verse — unrhymed iambic pentameter — that Yeats deliberately roughens to match the poem's vision of disintegration. The first stanza (lines 1–8) [046-392–399] unfolds as a single long sentence, its syntax mirroring the centrifugal motion it describes: clause piles upon clause as the centre weakens, until the stanza breaks off on "passionate intensity" [046-399] without resolution. The second stanza (lines 9–22) [046-400–413] is longer and more irregular; the line "The Second Coming! Hardly are those words out" [046-402] breaks the pentameter pattern with an exclamation that introduces the poem's visionary turn. Yeats later told his wife that the roughness of the verse was deliberate: the poem should feel "as though it were wrenched out of the body" rather than carefully composed. The only full rhyme in the poem — "sun" [046-406] and "man" [046-405] in the sphinx description — occurs at the visionary climax, binding the two components of the beast into a single sonic unit.

## Themes

The poem's central image — the **widening gyre** [046-392] — draws on Yeats's systematic philosophy of history developed in [[a-vision|A Vision]] (1925). In Yeats's cosmology, history moves not in a line but in spiralling cycles of roughly two thousand years. Each cycle begins with the interpenetration of divine and human realms (the birth of Christ, the annunciation to Leda) and expands outward until the centre can no longer hold. The falcon's loss of contact with the falconer [046-393] condenses this entire system into a single unforgettable image: the dissolution of order pictured as a bird spiralling away from its master's control.

The poem's second movement pivots from diagnosis to prophecy. The speaker, having described the collapse of the present order, turns to the future and sees — emanating from the **Spiritus Mundi** [046-403] or world-soul — a "vast image" [046-403] of a sphinx-like creature, "a shape with lion body and the head of a man" [046-405], whose "gaze blank and pitiless as the sun" [046-406] announces the arrival of a new era. This is the "rough beast" [046-412] whose "hour come round at last" [046-412] — the inversion of the Nativity, an anti-Christ slouching not from the east but "towards Bethlehem to be born" [046-413]. The poem thus enacts the very cycle it describes: the death of one civilisation is the birth-pang of another, and what is born may be terrible.

## Context

Yeats wrote "The Second Coming" in January 1919, a moment of extraordinary historical pressure. The First World War had ended two months earlier, leaving Europe shattered and its old empires — German, Austro-Hungarian, Ottoman, Russian — dissolved or collapsing. In Ireland, the War of Independence began that same month, with the Soloheadbeg ambush on 21 January. The poem's sense of a world where "mere anarchy is loosed" [046-395] and "the best lack all conviction" [046-398] reflects not only Yeats's cyclical philosophy but the immediate experience of a continent in ruins. Yet the poem has proved more durable than its occasion: it has been invoked in the aftermath of every major crisis of the century that followed, from the Second World War to the anxieties of the nuclear age to the upheavals of the twenty-first century.

The poem's composition history offers a revealing glimpse of Yeats's working method. The first prose draft, preserved in Yeats's manuscript book, contains only the germ of the opening image — a falcon lost from its master — written as a reflection on the state of European politics. Only in revision did the gyre imagery from the emerging *A Vision* system fuse with the political meditation, producing the poem's distinctive double vision of history as both immediate crisis and cosmic turning.
