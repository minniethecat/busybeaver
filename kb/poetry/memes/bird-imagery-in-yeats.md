## Introduction

Birds constitute one of the most pervasive and symbolically charged image clusters in the poetry of W. B. Yeats. From the "nine and fifty swans" [045-010] of Coole Park to the "brazen hawks" [047-424] of the later philosophical poems, avian imagery serves as a vehicle for Yeats' deepest meditations on the soul, desire, time, and the natural world. The principal birds of the Yeatsian aviary — swan, hawk, dove, and eagle — each carry distinct symbolic weight, yet together they form a coherent system through which the poet explores the tension between earthly attachment and spiritual transcendence, between the "dove-grey" [038-230] softness of faery and the "yellow-eyed hawk of the mind" [045-574].

Each bird serves as a vehicle for meditation on a different faculty of the human spirit: desire (dove), soul (swan), intellect (hawk), sovereignty (eagle). Together they map an ascent from the earthly to the transcendent.

## Swans

The swan is Yeats' most celebrated bird, dominating "The Wild Swans at Coole" and recurring across his career as an emblem of timeless beauty and the soul's solitude. At Coole, "nine and fifty swans" [045-010] float upon the water, their mystery and permanence contrasting with the poet's own aging. The swan embodies a paradox: it is both of this world and beyond it. In the later poetry, "the swan must fix his eye / Upon a fading gleam" [047-172], and "the solitary soul" is compared "to a swan" [047-495] — a creature that floats upon the dark water of time while belonging to the air and light. The swan's "leap into the desolate heaven" [047-514] images the soul's escape from the cycle of generation. In the early narrative poem "The Wanderings of Usheen," swans appear as creatures of supernatural beauty — "swans with their exultant throats" [015-183] — establishing a motif that would deepen through Yeats' middle and late periods.

## Hawks

If the swan represents the soul in contemplation, the hawk represents the mind in action — predatory, focused, and unsparing. The "yellow-eyed hawk of the mind" [045-574] in the later philosophical poems is the intellect stripped of sentiment, "brazen hawks" [047-424] that wheel above the landscape of thought. In "Cool Park and Ballylee, 1931," the poet calls to "call down the hawk from the air" [045-561], summoning the fierce concentration of mind that the bird represents. The hawk appears also in the political poems: "hawk for news" [046-307] connects the bird's predatory circling to the hungry scan of history. In the early work, a "blue-eyed hawk" [041-148] appears in the mythological narratives, but it is in the later poetry that the hawk finds its full symbolic force as the emblem of the intellectual will — "the sweet laughing eagle thoughts that grow" [043-179] merging eagle and hawk into a single image of sovereign mental power.

## Doves

The dove represents a softer aspect of bird symbolism — love, peace, and domestic tenderness. In "The Rose of the World," the poet addresses "the burnished dove" [032-185] that "groans and sighs" [032-185], connecting the dove to romantic yearning. In "A Prayer for My Daughter," the poet imagines his child growing "like some green laurel / Rooted in one dear perpetual place" while "a sun-flushed dove-cot" [022-026] provides the image of sheltered domestic happiness — "the doves whose growing forms he'd watched" [022-027]. The dove-grey colour that appears throughout Yeats' verse — "dove-grey edge of the sea" [015-023], "dove-grey sands" [038-230], "dove-grey faery lands" [038-307] — associates the dove with the realm of faery and the supernatural, a borderland between worlds that is neither fully earthly nor fully transcendent.

## Eagles

The eagle is the bird of sovereignty and ancient pride. In "The Wanderings of Usheen," "two old eagles, full of ancient pride" [015-480] guard the island of the sleeping warriors, their "few feathers on their dishevelled wings" [015-482] marking them as creatures of primordial power who have outlasted mortal ages. The eagles stand "fearless, nerveless, blind" [015-502], their blindness paradoxically signalling a vision beyond the physical — they are like the ancient Irish gods themselves, diminished but unvanquished. In the later work, the eagle appears in connection with thought and intellect: "the sweet laughing eagle thoughts" [043-179] and the determination to find "the right twigs for an eagle's nest" [044-418] suggest that the eagle symbolizes the highest reach of the mind — noble, solitary, and capable of surveying vast territories from an unmoving height.

## The Symbolic System

Together, these birds form a symbolic hierarchy that maps onto Yeats' larger philosophical concerns. The **dove** occupies the earthly realm of love, domesticity, and the faery borderlands. The **swan** mediates between earth and heaven — floating on water, belonging to air — and images the soul in its transitional state. The **hawk** and **eagle** ascend into pure intellect and visionary power, representing the mind at its most fiercely concentrated and the spirit at its most sovereign. The progression from dove to swan to hawk to eagle traces an arc from the bodily to the intellectual to the transcendent — a scale of being that parallels the Yeatsian journey from the "dove-grey" mists of early Celtic Twilight through the "feathery quires" [015-157] of mythological narrative to the "brazen hawks" of the philosophical last poems.

## Scholarly Interpretation

Critics have noted that Yeats's bird imagery evolves in tandem with his philosophical development. In the early work, birds are decorative elements of the Celtic Twilight; by the middle period, they serve as psychological emblems; in the late poetry, they become vehicles for metaphysical statement. Helen Vendler reads the "yellow-eyed hawk of the mind" as a figure for Yeats's own mature poetic consciousness — fierce, unsentimental, unwilling to turn away from difficult truths. The swan, by contrast, is often read in the tradition of Shelley and Baudelaire as an image of the poet-soul alienated from its element.
