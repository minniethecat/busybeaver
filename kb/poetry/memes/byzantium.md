# Byzantium

**Byzantium** is a central symbol in W. B. Yeats's late poetry, representing an ideal realm where art, intellect, and the soul achieve eternal perfection, freed from the decay of the natural world. It appears most powerfully in two paired poems from his mature period: "Sailing to Byzantium" (1928) and "Byzantium" (1933). In Yeats's symbolic system, Byzantium is not the historical Eastern Roman capital of Justinian but an imagined eternal city — "the holy city of Byzantium" [047-016] — where, as he wrote in *A Vision*, "religious, aesthetic and practical life were one."

## Origins and Sources

Yeats's Byzantium is a composite of historical imagination and symbolic necessity:

1. **Historical Byzantium**: Yeats read accounts of the Emperor's palace at Byzantium under Justinian (6th century CE), where mechanical automata — a golden tree with singing birds — adorned the throne room. The endnote to *The Tower* records this source: "in the Emperor's palace at Byzantium was a tree made of gold and silver, and artificial birds that sang" [048-060].

2. **A Vision**: In Yeats's esoteric system, Byzantium represents a moment in the gyre of history — around 500 CE — when the antithetical (artistic, aristocratic, subjective) civilization achieved its maximum unity before beginning its long decline. This historical Byzantium is the closest earthly approximation to the eternal City of Art.

3. **Personal Symbolism**: For the aging Yeats, Byzantium crystallized the desire to escape the decaying body — "fastened to a dying animal" [047-022] — and enter a realm where the soul could exist as pure artistic form.

## Development: The Two Poems

### "Sailing to Byzantium" (1928)

The first Byzantium poem opens in Ireland, "no country for old men," where the natural world pulses with procreative energy that excludes the aged. The speaker, feeling his body's decay, undertakes an imaginative voyage:

> An aged man is but a paltry thing,
> A tattered coat upon a stick, unless
> Soul clap its hands and sing, and louder sing
> For every tatter in its mortal dress...

The destination is Byzantium, where the speaker prays to "sages standing in God's holy fire" [047-017] to "come from the holy fire, perne in a gyre" [047-019] and consume his heart — burning away the sensual life so that the soul may be gathered "into the artifice of eternity" [047-024].

The poem's final stanza recasts the speaker's desire in the image of the golden bird:

> Once out of nature I shall never take
> My bodily form from any natural thing,
> But such a form as Grecian goldsmiths make
> Of hammered gold and gold enamelling...

This mechanical bird, singing "to lords and ladies of Byzantium / Of what is past, or passing, or to come" [047-031], is the perfected work of art — an artifice that transcends organic mortality while retaining the capacity for song.

### "Byzantium" (1933)

The companion poem, published in *The Winding Stair and Other Poems*, shifts the scene fully into the city. It opens at night: "The unpurged images of day recede; / The Emperor's drunken soldiery are abed." The poem moves through a nocturnal landscape of purgatorial fire, "blood-begotten spirits," and the golden bird performing upon the "star-lit golden bough" — a deliberate echo of the golden bough from Virgil's *Aeneid* that signals Yeats's ambition to rival classical epic.

The poem culminates in the image of dolphins carrying souls across the sea to the "holy city," where the "marble of the dancing floor" breaks "bitter furies of complexity" — the city of art is also a city of resolution, where the chaos of mortal life is shaped into form.

The late reference in the collected poems — "Pass books of learning from Byzantium" [047-995] — shows that the Byzantium concept continued to reverberate through Yeats's imagination until the end of his career, associated not only with art but with the transmission of wisdom.

## Significance

Byzantium is arguably the supreme symbol of Yeats's late work — the destination toward which his entire poetic and philosophical career tended. It synthesizes his preoccupations with aging and mortality, the nature of art, the soul's journey, and the historical gyres. As a symbol, it operates at three levels simultaneously: as a historical reality (Justinian's Constantinople), as a personal vision (the aging poet's escape from the body), and as a metaphysical principle (the unity of being achieved through artifice). The golden bird, singing on its golden bough, is Yeats's answer to Keats's nightingale — an art object, not a natural creature, yet capable of song that outlasts nature.
