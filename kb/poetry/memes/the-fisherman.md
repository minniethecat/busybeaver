# The Fisherman

The Fisherman is the central figure of a 1919 poem from W. B. Yeats's *The Wild Swans at Coole* collection — "a man who does not exist, / A man who is but a dream" [045-555]–[045-556]. He is not a portrait of a real person but a deliberate act of imagination: the poet, in "scorn of this audience" [045-547], conjures an ideal reader against whom to measure his work. The Fisherman embodies the tension between Yeats's artistic ambition and his difficult relationship with the Irish reading public, and stands as one of his most personal statements about the purpose of poetry.

## The Poem

"The Fisherman" opens with a vivid image of the dream-reader: "Although I can see him still / The freckled man who goes / To a grey place on a hill / In grey Connemara clothes / At dawn to cast his flies" [045-521]–[045-525]. The specificity of the image — the freckles, the grey Connemara cloth, the exact time of day — makes the fisherman almost real, even as the poem acknowledges his unreality.

The poem's middle section is a catalogue of the actual audience Yeats despises: "The living men that I hate, / The dead man that I loved, / The craven man in his seat, / The insolent unreproved" [045-533]–[045-536]. The list extends through the "witty man" with jokes "Aimed at the commonest ear" [045-539]–[045-540], the "clever man who cries / The catch-cries of the clown" [045-541]–[045-542], in a crescendo of cultural despair that culminates in "The beating down of the wise / And great Art beaten down" [045-543]–[045-544].

The fisherman is the antidote to this audience — conjured "Suddenly" [045-546] as a deliberate counter-creation. The poem concludes with a vow: "Before I am old / I shall have written him one / Poem maybe as cold / And passionate as the dawn" [045-557]–[045-560]. The poem itself is, of course, that promised work — self-fulfilling in its own creation.

## Symbolic Significance

The Fisherman is a composite figure that draws on several layers of Yeats's thought:

- **The ideal audience**: A "wise and simple man" [045-528] who embodies the traditional Irish peasantry Yeats romanticised — people rooted in folklore, landscape, and oral culture, untouched by the debased public discourse of Dublin.
- **The anti-self**: The Fisherman is a mask Yeats adopts — he is not himself the fisherman, but imagines writing *for* him, which shapes the kind of poet he must become. This connects to the [[mask-theory|Mask Theory]] from *A Vision*.
- **The solitary artist**: The fisherman at dawn, alone on a "grey place on a hill" [045-523], mirrors the poet in his tower at [[thoor-ballylee]] — the figure who withdraws from society to perform his craft.

## The Fisherman in Context

The poem draws on Yeats's actual experiences. The Endnotes record that "The Meditation of the Old Fisherman" was written "from the words of a not very old fisherman at Rosses Point" [048-005], and that an "old Aran fisherman" [048-032] provided material for other poems. Yeats actively sought out the voices of traditional Irish people as a source of poetic material, and the Fisherman figure is the idealised distillation of these encounters.

The "grey Connemara clothes" [045-524] root the figure in a specific western Irish landscape — Connemara in County Galway, a region of rugged beauty that Yeats associated with authentic Irish identity, in contrast to the anglicised Dublin of "the clever man" and "the witty man."
