# Deirdre

Deirdre (1907) is W. B. Yeats's one-act verse tragedy based on the ancient Irish legend of Deirdre of the Sorrows — first performed at the Abbey Theatre during the height of the Irish Literary Revival.

## Publication Details

- **Type**: one-act verse play / tragedy
- **First performed**: 24 November 1906, Abbey Theatre, Dublin
- **First published**: 1907 (in _Poems, 1899–1905_ and separately by A. H. Bullen)
- **Source material**: the ancient Irish tale "The Fate of the Sons of Usnach" from the Ulster Cycle
- **Significance**: one of Yeats's most successful early plays; drew on Irish mythological material for the Abbey Theatre repertoire

## Overview

Deirdre retells the ancient Irish legend of Deirdre of the Sorrows — the woman prophesied at birth to bring ruin upon Ulster, who chooses love with Naoise over her betrothal to the aging King Conchubar. Yeats's version compresses the long narrative into a single intense act set in a remote guesthouse where the returning lovers are trapped. The play focuses on the final hours before the tragedy, as Conchubar's messengers arrive and Deirdre must confront the fate foretold at her birth.

The ancient story of Deirdre occupies a central place in Irish literary tradition, appearing in medieval manuscripts and modern retellings. Yeats notes in his endnotes that the figure of Deirdre appears "in the ancient story of Deirdre, and in modern poems by Ferguson" [048-036], positioning his own dramatic version within a continuous line of Irish adaptations. The play with its concentration on a single tragic heroine in a claustrophobic setting reflects Yeats's evolving dramatic technique — moving away from the sprawling mythological pageants of his earlier plays toward the compressed, ritualistic form he would later develop in his Noh-inspired dance plays.

The mythological Deirdre also enters Yeats's poetry directly. In "Baile and Aillinn" (1903), the doomed lovers remember "Deirdre and her man" [040-092] as a parallel to their own fate, and hear "news of Deirdre's eyes" [040-098] — the celebrated beauty that set the tragedy in motion. These poetic references predate the play and show Yeats returning repeatedly to the Deirdre material across genres.

## Themes and Motifs

- **Fate and prophecy**: The play's tension derives from a prophecy that cannot be escaped — a recurring Yeatsian preoccupation with predestined tragedy and the heroic acceptance of fate.
- **Love versus political order**: Deirdre's choice of personal love over the political marriage arranged by Conchubar echoes the tension between individual desire and social obligation that runs through Yeats's work.
- **Myth as national theater**: Deirdre exemplified the Abbey Theatre's mission to build an Irish national drama from native mythological sources, creating a repertoire distinct from the London stage.

## Critical Reception

Deirdre was one of Yeats's most frequently revived early plays and helped establish the Abbey Theatre's reputation for serious verse drama drawn from Irish sources. Its compressed one-act form anticipated Yeats's later experiments with ritualistic and dance-based drama influenced by Japanese Noh theater.
