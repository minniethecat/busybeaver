# Crossways[5](endnotes.xhtml#note-5)

[032-001]
> “The stars are threshed, and the souls are threshed from the husks.”

[032-002]
> 

[032-003]
> William Blake

[032-004]To A. E.

## The Sad Shepherd

[032-005]There was a man whom Sorrow named his friend   

[032-006]And he, of his high comrade Sorrow [[dream-and-vision|dreaming]],   

[032-007]Went walking with slow steps along the gleaming   

[032-008]And humming sands, where windy surges wend:   

[032-009]And he called loudly to the stars to bend   

[032-010]From their pale thrones and comfort him, but they   

[032-011]Among themselves laugh on and sing alway:   

[032-012]And then the man whom Sorrow named his friend   

[032-013]Cried out, "Dim [[water-imagery|sea]], hear my most piteous story!"   

[032-014]The sea swept on and cried her old cry still,   

[032-015]Rolling along in dreams from hill to hill;   

[032-016]He fled the persecution of her glory   

[032-017]And, in a far-off, gentle valley stopping,   

[032-018]Cried all his story to the dewdrops glistening,   

[032-019]But naught they heard, for they are always listening,   

[032-020]The dewdrops, for the sound of their own dropping.   

[032-021]And then the man whom Sorrow named his friend,   

[032-022]Sought once again the shore, and found a shell,   

[032-023]And thought, "I will my heavy story tell"   

[032-024]" Till my own words, re-echoing, shall send   

[032-025]Their sadness through a hollow, pearly heart;   

[032-026]And my own tale again for me shall sing,   

[032-027]And my own whispering words be comforting,   

[032-028]And lo! my ancient burden may depart.   

[032-029]" Then he sang softly nigh the pearly rim;   

[032-030]But the sad dweller by the sea-ways lone   

[032-031]Changed all he sang to inarticulate moan   

[032-032]Among her wildering whirls, forgetting him.

## The Cloak, the Boat, and the Shoes

[032-033]“What do you make so fair and bright?”

[032-034]“I make the cloak of Sorrow:   

[032-035]O, lovely to see in all men’s sight   

[032-036]Shall be the cloak of Sorrow,   

[032-037]In all men’s sight.”

[032-038]“What do you build with sails for flight?”

[032-039]“I build a boat for Sorrow,   

[032-040]O, swift on the seas all day and night   

[032-041]Saileth the rover Sorrow,   

[032-042]All day and night.”

[032-043]“What do you weave with wool so white?”

[032-044]“I weave the shoes of Sorrow,   

[032-045]Soundless shall be the footfall [[shadow-and-light|light]]   

[032-046]In all men’s ears of Sorrow,   

[032-047]Sudden and light.”

## Anashuya and Vijaya

[032-048]A little Indian temple in the Golden Age. Around it a garden; around that the forest. **Anashuya** , the young priestess, kneeling within the temple.

[032-049]Anashuya |  Send peace on all the lands and flickering corn.﻿—   

[032-050]O, may tranquillity walk by his elbow   

[032-051]When wandering in the forest, if he love   

[032-052]No other.﻿—Hear, and may the indolent flocks   

[032-053]Be plentiful.﻿—And if he love another,   

[032-054]May panthers end him.﻿—Hear, and load our king   

[032-055]With wisdom hour by hour.﻿—May we two stand,   

[032-056]When we are dead, beyond the setting suns,   

[032-057]A little from the other shades apart,   

[032-058]With mingling hair, and play upon one lute.  

[032-059]---|---  

[032-060]Vijaya |  _Entering and throwing a lily at her._ Hail! hail, my Anashuya.  

[032-061]Anashuya |  No: be still.   

[032-062]I, priestess of this temple, offer up   

[032-063]Prayers for the land.  

[032-064]Vijaya |  I will wait here, Amrita.  

[032-065]Anashuya |  By mighty Brahma’s ever rustling robe,   

[032-066]Who is Amrita? Sorrow of all sorrows!   

[032-067]Another fills your mind.  

[032-068]Vijaya |  My mother’s name.  

[032-069]Anashuya |  _Sings, coming out of the temple._ A sad, sad thought went by me slowly:   

[032-070]Sigh, O you little stars! O, sigh and shake your blue apparel!   

[032-071]The sad, sad thought has gone from me now wholly:   

[032-072]Sing, O you little stars! O sing, and raise your rapturous carol   

[032-073]To mighty Brahma, who has made you many as the sands,   

[032-074]And laid you on the gates of evening with his quiet hands.  

[032-075]|  _Sits down on the steps of the temple._  

[032-076]|  Vijaya, I have brought my evening rice;   

[032-077]The sun has laid his chin on the gray wood,   

[032-078]Weary, with all his poppies gathered round him.  

[032-079]Vijaya |  The hour when Kama, full of sleepy laughter,   

[032-080]Rises, and showers abroad his fragrant arrows,   

[032-081]Piercing the twilight with their murmuring barbs.  

[032-082]Anashuya |  See how the sacred old flamingoes come,   

[032-083]Painting with shadow all the [[stone-imagery|marble]] steps:   

[032-084]Aged and wise, they seek their wonted perches   

[032-085]Within the temple, devious walking, made   

[032-086]To wander by their melancholy minds.   

[032-087]Yon tall one eyes my supper; chase him away,   

[032-088]Far, far away. I named him after you.   

[032-089]He is a famous fisher; hour by hour   

[032-090]He ruffles with his bill the minnowed streams.   

[032-091]Ah! there he snaps my rice. I told you so.   

[032-092]Now cuff him off. He’s off! A kiss for you,   

[032-093]Because you saved my rice. Have you no thanks?  

[032-094]Vijaya |  _Sings._ Sing you of her, O first few stars,   

[032-095]Whom Brahma, touching with his finger, praises, for you hold   

[032-096]The van of wandering quiet; ere you be too calm and old,   

[032-097]Sing, turning in your cars,   

[032-098]Sing, till you raise your hands and sigh, and from your car heads peer,   

[032-099]With all your whirling hair, and drop many an azure tear.  

[032-100]Anashuya |  What know the pilots of the stars of tears?  

[032-101]Vijaya |  Their faces are all worn, and in their eyes   

[032-102]Flashes the fire of sadness, for they see   

[032-103]The icicles that famish all the north,   

[032-104]Where men lie frozen in the glimmering snow;   

[032-105]And in the flaming forests cower the lion   

[032-106]And lioness, with all their whimpering cubs;   

[032-107]And, ever pacing on the verge of things,   

[032-108]The phantom, [[eternal-beauty|Beauty]], in a mist of tears;   

[032-109]While we alone have round us woven woods,   

[032-110]And feel the softness of each other’s hand,   

[032-111]Amrita, while﻿—  

[032-112]Anashuya |  _Going away from him._ Ah me, you love another,  

[032-113]|  _Bursting into tears._  

[032-114]|  And may some dreadful ill befall her quick!  

[032-115]Vijaya |  I loved another; now I love no other.   

[032-116]Among the mouldering of ancient woods   

[032-117]You live, and on the village border she,   

[032-118]With her old father the blind wood-cutter;   

[032-119]I saw her standing in her door but now.  

[032-120]Anashuya |  Vijaya, swear to love her never more.  

[032-121]Vijaya |  Ay, ay.  

[032-122]Anashuya |  Swear by the parents of the gods,   

[032-123]Dread oath, who dwell on sacred Himalay,   

[032-124]On the far Golden Peak; enormous shapes,   

[032-125]Who still were old when the great sea was young;   

[032-126]On their vast faces mystery and dreams;   

[032-127]Their hair along the mountains rolled and filled   

[032-128]From year to year by the unnumbered nests   

[032-129]Of aweless birds, and round their stirless feet   

[032-130]The joyous flocks of deer and antelope,   

[032-131]Who never hear the unforgiving hound.   

[032-132]Swear!  

[032-133]Vijaya |  By the parents of the gods, I swear.  

[032-134]Anashuya |  _Sings._ I have forgiven, O new star!   

[032-135]Maybe you have not heard of us, you have come forth so newly,   

[032-136]You hunter of the fields afar!   

[032-137]Ah, you will know my loved one by his hunter’s arrows truly,   

[032-138]Shoot on him shafts of quietness, that he may ever keep   

[032-139]An inner laughter, and may kiss his hands to me in sleep. Farewell, Vijaya. Nay, no word, no word;   

[032-140]I, priestess of this temple, offer up   

[032-141]Prayers for the land.  

[032-142]|  _**Vijaya** goes._  

[032-143]|  O Brahma, guard in sleep   

[032-144]The merry lambs and the complacent kine,   

[032-145]The flies below the leaves, and the young mice   

[032-146]In the tree roots, and all the sacred flocks   

[032-147]Of red flamingo; and my love, Vijaya;   

[032-148]And may no restless fay with fidget finger   

[032-149]Trouble his sleeping: give him dreams of me.  
  
## The Indian Upon God

[032-150]I passed along the water’s edge below the humid trees,   

[032-151]My [[soul-in-yeats|spirit]] rocked in evening light, the rushes round my knees,   

[032-152]My spirit rocked in sleep and sighs; and saw the moorfowl pace   

[032-153]All dripping on a grassy slope, and saw them cease to chase   

[032-154]Each other round in circles, and heard the eldest speak:   

[032-155]" Who holds the world between His bill and made us strong or weak   

[032-156]Is an undying moorfowl, and He lives beyond the sky,   

[032-157]The rains are from His dripping wing, the moonbeams from His eye.   

[032-158]" I passed a little further on and heard a lotus talk:   

[032-159]" Who made the world and ruleth it, He hangeth on a stalk,   

[032-160]For I am in His image made, and all this tinkling tide   

[032-161]Is but a sliding drop of rain between His petals wide.   

[032-162]" A little way within the gloom a roebuck raised his eyes   

[032-163]Brimful of starlight, and he said: "The Stamper of the Skies,"   

[032-164]" He is a gentle roebuck; for how else, I pray, could He   

[032-165]Conceive a thing so sad and soft, a gentle thing like me?   

[032-166]" I passed a little further on and heard a peacock say:   

[032-167]" Who made the grass and made the worms and made my feathers gay,   

[032-168]He is a monstrous peacock, and He waveth all the night   

[032-169]His languid tail above us, lit with myriad spots of light. "

## The Indian to His Love

[032-170]The island dreams under the dawn   

[032-171]And great boughs drop tranquillity;   

[032-172]The peahens dance on a smooth lawn,   

[032-173]A parrot sways upon a tree,   

[032-174]Raging at his own image in the enamelled sea.

[032-175]Here we will moor our lonely ship   

[032-176]And wander ever with woven hands,   

[032-177]Murmuring softly lip to lip,   

[032-178]Along the grass, along the sands,   

[032-179]Murmuring how far away are the unquiet lands:

[032-180]How we alone of mortals are   

[032-181]Hid under quiet bows apart,   

[032-182]While our love grows an Indian star,   

[032-183]A meteor of the [[fire-passion|burning]] heart,   

[032-184]One with the tide that gleams, the wings that gleam and dart,

[032-185]The heavy boughs, the burnished dove   

[032-186]That moans and sighs a hundred days:   

[032-187]How when we die our shades will rove,   

[032-188]When eve has hushed the feathered ways,   

[032-189]With vapoury footsole among the water’s drowsy blaze.

## The Falling of the Leaves

[032-190]Autumn is over the long leaves that love us,   

[032-191]And over the mice in the barley sheaves;   

[032-192]Yellow the leaves of the rowan above us,   

[032-193]And yellow the wet wild-strawberry leaves.

[032-194]The hour of the waning of love has beset us,   

[032-195]And weary and worn are our sad souls now;   

[032-196]Let us part, ere the season of [[love-in-yeats|passion]] forget us,   

[032-197]With a kiss and a tear on thy drooping brow.

## Ephemera

[032-198]“Your eyes that once were never weary of mine   

[032-199]Are bowed in sorrow under their trembling lids,   

[032-200]Because our love is waning.”

[032-201]And then she:   

[032-202]“Although our love is waning, let us stand   

[032-203]By the lone border of the lake once more,   

[032-204]Together in that hour of gentleness   

[032-205]When the poor tired child, Passion, falls asleep:   

[032-206]How far away the stars seem, and how far   

[032-207]Is our first kiss, and ah, how old my heart!”

[032-208]Pensive they paced along the faded leaves,   

[032-209]While slowly he whose hand held hers replied:   

[032-210]“Passion has often worn our wandering hearts.”

[032-211]The woods were round them, and the yellow leaves   

[032-212]Fell like faint meteors in the gloom, and once   

[032-213]A rabbit old and lame limped down the path;   

[032-214]Autumn was over him: and now they stood   

[032-215]On the lone border of the lake once more:   

[032-216]Turning, he saw that she had thrust dead leaves   

[032-217]Gathered in silence, dewy as her eyes,   

[032-218]In bosom and hair. “Ah, do not mourn,” he said,   

[032-219]“That we are tired, for other loves await us;   

[032-220]Hate on and love through unrepining hours.   

[032-221]Before us lies [[time-and-eternity|eternity]]; our souls   

[032-222]Are love, and a continual farewell.”

## The Madness of King Goll

[032-223]I sat on cushioned otter skin:   

[032-224]My word was law from Ith to Emen,   

[032-225]And shook at Invar Amargin   

[032-226]The hearts of the world-troubling seamen,   

[032-227]And drove tumult and war away   

[032-228]From girl and boy and man and beast;   

[032-229]The fields grew fatter day by day,   

[032-230]The wild fowl of the air increased;   

[032-231]And every ancient Ollave said,   

[032-232]While he bent down his fading head,   

[032-233]“He drives away the Northern cold.”   

[032-234]They will not hush, the leaves a-flutter round me, the beech leaves old.

[032-235]I sat and mused and drank sweet wine;   

[032-236]A herdsman came from inland valleys,   

[032-237]Crying, the pirates drove his swine   

[032-238]To fill their dark-beaked hollow galleys.   

[032-239]I called my battle-breaking men,   

[032-240]And my loud brazen battle-cars   

[032-241]From rolling vale and rivery glen;   

[032-242]And under the blinking of the stars   

[032-243]Fell on the pirates by the deep,   

[032-244]And hurled them in the gulf of sleep:   

[032-245]These hands won many a torque of gold.   

[032-246]They will not hush, the leaves a-flutter round me, the beech leaves old.

[032-247]But slowly, as I shouting slew   

[032-248]And trampled in the bubbling mire,   

[032-249]In my most secret spirit grew   

[032-250]A whirling and a wandering fire:   

[032-251]I stood: keen stars above me shone,   

[032-252]Around me shone keen eyes of men:   

[032-253]I laughed aloud and hurried on   

[032-254]By rocky shore and rushy fen;   

[032-255]I laughed because birds fluttered by,   

[032-256]And starlight gleamed, and clouds flew high,   

[032-257]And rushes waved and waters rolled.   

[032-258]They will not hush, the leaves a-flutter round me, the beech leaves old.

[032-259]And now I wander in the woods   

[032-260]When summer gluts the golden bees,   

[032-261]Or in autumnal solitudes   

[032-262]Arise the leopard-coloured trees,   

[032-263]Or when along the wintry strands   

[032-264]The cormorants shiver on their rocks;   

[032-265]I wander on, and wave my hands,   

[032-266]And sing, and shake my heavy locks.   

[032-267]The grey wolf knows me; by one ear   

[032-268]I lead along the woodland deer;   

[032-269]The hares run by me growing bold.   

[032-270]They will not hush, the leaves a-flutter round me, the beech leaves old.

[032-271]I came upon a little town,   

[032-272]That slumbered in the harvest moon,   

[032-273]And passed a-tiptoe up and down,   

[032-274]Murmuring, to a fitful tune,   

[032-275]How I have followed, night and day,   

[032-276]A tramping of tremendous feet,   

[032-277]And saw where this old tympan lay,   

[032-278]Deserted on a doorway seat,   

[032-279]And bore it to the woods with me;   

[032-280]Of some unhuman misery   

[032-281]Our married voices wildly trolled.   

[032-282]They will not hush, the leaves a-flutter round me, the beech leaves old.

[032-283]I sang how, when day’s toil is done,   

[032-284]Orchil shakes out her long dark hair   

[032-285]That hides away the [[death-in-yeats|dying]] sun   

[032-286]And sheds faint odours through the air:   

[032-287]When my hand passed from wire to wire   

[032-288]It quenched, with sound like falling dew,   

[032-289]The whirling and the wandering fire;   

[032-290]But lift a mournful ulalu,   

[032-291]For the kind wires are torn and still,   

[032-292]And I must wander wood and hill   

[032-293]Through summer’s heat and winter’s cold.   

[032-294]They will not hush, the leaves a-flutter round me, the beech leaves old.

## The Stolen Child

[032-295]Where dips the rocky highland   

[032-296]Of Sleuth Wood in the lake,   

[032-297]There lies a leafy island   

[032-298]Where flapping herons wake   

[032-299]The drowsy water rats;   

[032-300]There we’ve hid our faery vats,   

[032-301]Full of berries,   

[032-302]And of reddest stolen cherries.   

[032-303]" Come away, O human child!   

[032-304]To the waters and the wild   

[032-305]With a faery, hand in hand,   

[032-306]For the world’s more full of weeping than you can understand. "

[032-307]Where the wave of moonlight glosses   

[032-308]The dim grey sands with light,   

[032-309]Far off by furthest Rosses   

[032-310]We foot it all the night,   

[032-311]Weaving olden dances,   

[032-312]Mingling hands and mingling glances   

[032-313]Till the moon has taken flight;   

[032-314]To and fro we leap   

[032-315]And chase the frothy bubbles,   

[032-316]While the world is full of troubles   

[032-317]And is anxious in its sleep.   

[032-318]" Come away, O human child!   

[032-319]To the waters and the wild   

[032-320]With a faery, hand in hand,   

[032-321]For the world’s more full of weeping than you can understand. "

[032-322]Where the wandering water gushes   

[032-323]From the hills above Glen-Car,   

[032-324]In pools among the rushes   

[032-325]That scarce could bathe a star,   

[032-326]We seek for slumbering trout   

[032-327]And whispering in their ears   

[032-328]Give them unquiet dreams   

[032-329]Leaning softly out   

[032-330]From ferns that drop their tear   

[032-331]Over the young streams.   

[032-332]" Come away, O human child!   

[032-333]To the waters and the wild   

[032-334]With a faery, hand in hand,   

[032-335]For the world’s more full of weeping than you can understand. "

[032-336]Away with us he’s going,   

[032-337]The solemn-eyed:   

[032-338]He’ll hear no more the lowing   

[032-339]Of the calves on the warm hillside   

[032-340]Or the kettle on the hob   

[032-341]Sing peace into his breast,   

[032-342]Or see the brown mice bob   

[032-343]Round and round the oatmeal-chest.   

[032-344]" For he comes, the human child,   

[032-345]To the waters and the wild   

[032-346]With a faery, hand in hand,   

[032-347]From a world more full of weeping than he can understand. "

## To an Isle in the Water

[032-348]Shy one, shy one,   

[032-349]Shy one of my heart,   

[032-350]She moves in the firelight   

[032-351]Pensively apart.

[032-352]She carries in the dishes,   

[032-353]And lays them in a row.   

[032-354]To an isle in the water   

[032-355]With her would I go.

[032-356]She carries in the candles   

[032-357]And lights the curtained room,   

[032-358]Shy in the doorway   

[032-359]And shy in the gloom;

[032-360]And shy as a rabbit,   

[032-361]Helpful and shy.   

[032-362]To an isle in the water   

[032-363]With her would I fly.

## Down by the Salley Gardens

[032-364]Down by the salley gardens my love and I did meet;   

[032-365]She passed the salley gardens with little snow-white feet.   

[032-366]She bid me take love easy, as the leaves grow on the tree;   

[032-367]But I, being young and foolish, with her would not agree.

[032-368]In a field by the river my love and I did stand,   

[032-369]And on my leaning shoulder she laid her snow-white hand.   

[032-370]She bid me take life easy, as the grass grows on the weirs;   

[032-371]But I was young and foolish, and now am full of tears.

## The Meditation of the Old Fisherman

[032-372]You waves, though you dance by my feet like children at play,   

[032-373]Though you glow and you glance, though you purr and you dart;   

[032-374]In the Junes that were warmer than these are, the waves were more gay,   

[032-375]When I was a boy with never a crack in my heart.

[032-376]The herring are not in the tides as they were of old;   

[032-377]My sorrow! for many a creak gave the creel in the cart   

[032-378]That carried the take to [[sligo-county|Sligo town]] to be sold,   

[032-379]When I was a boy with never a crack in my heart.

[032-380]And ah, you proud maiden, you are not so fair when his oar   

[032-381]Is heard on the water, as they were, the proud and apart,   

[032-382]Who paced in the eve by the nets on the pebbly shore,   

[032-383]When I was a boy with never a crack in my heart.

## The Ballad of Father O’Hart[6](endnotes.xhtml#note-6)

[032-384]Good Father John O’Hart   

[032-385]In penal days rode out   

[032-386]To a shoneen who had free lands   

[032-387]And his own snipe and trout.

[032-388]In trust took he John’s lands;   

[032-389]Sleiveens were all his race;   

[032-390]And he gave them as dowers to his daughters,   

[032-391]And they married beyond their place.

[032-392]But Father John went up,   

[032-393]And Father John went down;   

[032-394]And he wore small holes in his shoes,   

[032-395]And he wore large holes in his gown.

[032-396]All loved him, only the shoneen,   

[032-397]Whom the devils have by the hair,   

[032-398]From the wives, and the cats, and the children,   

[032-399]To the birds in the white of the air.

[032-400]The birds, for he opened their cages   

[032-401]As he went up and down;   

[032-402]And he said with a smile, “Have peace now”;   

[032-403]And he went his way with a frown.

[032-404]But if when any one died   

[032-405]Came keeners hoarser than rooks,   

[032-406]He bade them give over their keening;   

[032-407]For he was a man of books.

[032-408]And these were the works of John,   

[032-409]When weeping score by score,   

[032-410]People came into Coloony;   

[032-411]For he’d died at ninety-four.

[032-412]There was no human keening;   

[032-413]The birds from [[knocknarea-mountain|Knocknarea]]   

[032-414]And the world round Knocknashee   

[032-415]Came keening in that day.

[032-416]The young birds and old birds   

[032-417]Came flying, heavy and sad;   

[032-418]Keening in from Tiraragh,   

[032-419]Keening from Ballinafad;

[032-420]Keening from Inishmurray,   

[032-421]Nor stayed for bite or sup;   

[032-422]This way were all reproved   

[032-423]Who dig old customs up.

## The Ballad of Moll Magee

[032-424]Come round me, little childer;   

[032-425]There, don’t fling stones at me   

[032-426]Because I mutter as I go;   

[032-427]But pity Moll Magee.

[032-428]My man was a poor fisher   

[032-429]With shore lines in the say;   

[032-430]My work was saltin’ herrings   

[032-431]The whole of the long day.

[032-432]And sometimes from the saltin’ shed,
