Automatic writing is the practice through which W. B. Yeats received the core material for his major prose work _[[a-vision]]_ (1925, revised 1937) and, by extension, the symbolic vocabulary of his greatest poems. Between 1917 and 1920, Yeats's wife [[Georgie-Yeats|George Hyde-Lees]] produced nearly 4,000 pages of automatic script — writing without conscious volition while in a trance state — which Yeats interpreted as communications from discarnate "instructors" offering a systematic cosmology of human personality and history.

## Definition

In Yeats's understanding, automatic writing was not a psychological phenomenon (as modern psychology would interpret it — the surfacing of unconscious material) but a genuinely occult practice: a mediumistic technique by which living persons could receive dictated communications from spiritual intelligences. Yeats distinguished between the "Control" (the specific spirit personality speaking through the medium's hand) and the "Interpreter" (Yeats himself, who questioned the Control and shaped the answers into philosophical form). He called these sessions "the Script" and treated the resulting documents as revelations comparable in authority to sacred texts.

## Origins and Sources

- **Primary source**: George Hyde-Lees (Georgie Yeats), who began automatic writing four days after her marriage to Yeats on 20 October 1917, initially as an attempt to distract him from his obsessive thoughts about Maud Gonne
- **Spiritualist tradition**: The practice was widespread in late 19th-century spiritualist circles; Yeats had participated in séances since the 1880s and was familiar with the automatic writing of mediums such as Hester Dowden
- **Golden Dawn training**: Yeats's experience with ritual magic in the [[Golden-Dawn|Hermetic Order of the Golden Dawn]] provided the symbolic framework — correspondences, hierarchies, and cyclic patterns — that allowed him to systematise the raw script into a coherent philosophy
- **Key texts**: _A Vision_ (1925, revised 1937) and the manuscript notebooks at the National Library of Ireland (scheduled for release under Yeats family instructions)

## Development Across Yeats's Career

**Early interest (1880s–1890s)**: Yeats attended his first séance in 1882 at the home of his uncle George Pollexfen. The idea that spirits communicated through human mediums was already familiar. His 1893 essay "The Message of the Folk-lorist" connects folk-belief in fairies to what he called "the one great question" — "whether spirits can communicate with men."

**The marriage and the Script (1917–1920)**: Four days after their wedding, Georgie surprised Yeats by attempting automatic writing. The communicators — later identified as "Thomas of Dorlowicz," "Ameritus," and others — dictated an elaborate system of 28 lunar phases, gyres of history, the Mask and Daimon, and cycles of reincarnation. Sessions typically ran two to three hours, several times per week. Yeats recorded questions and responses in notebooks; Georgie also produced automatic drawing, sleep-talking, and other forms of mediumistic communication.

**_A Vision_ and the system (1920–1937)**: The raw script was organised into _A Vision_ (1925), but the book is heavily mediated — Yeats selected, ordered, interpreted, and framed the communications through the fictional personae of Michael Robartes and Owen Aherne. The 1937 revision reflects further automatic communications received after 1925, as well as Yeats's evolving philosophical interests.

**Impact on the poems**: The automatic writing sessions gave Yeats his mature symbolic vocabulary. Concepts derived directly from the Script — the gyre, the phases of the moon, the Mask, the Daimon — appear throughout the poems of _The Tower_ (1928) and _The Winding Stair and Other Poems_ (1933), as well as in the late _Last Poems_ (1939). Without the automatic writing, there would be no "Sailing to Byzantium," no "Byzantium," no "Leda and the Swan," no "The Second Coming" — or at least not as we know them.

## Symbolic Meaning

The automatic writing itself becomes a symbol in Yeats's late work:

- **The mysterious source**: In Yeats's mythology of creativity, the poet does not invent but receives — he is a medium for the [[Great-Memory]] or _Anima Mundi_. The automatic writing literalises this metaphor: the poet's wife literally receives dictation from invisible beings
- **Unity of the married couple**: The automatic writing was a collaborative act — Georgie wrote, Yeats questioned and interpreted — and Yeats came to see the marriage as a symbolic marriage of conscious and unconscious, male and female principles, the solar and the lunar
- **The occult as method**: Unlike earlier poets who drew on occultism for imagery, Yeats used automatic writing as a practical method of philosophical inquiry. The "supernatural songs" of "A Dialogue of Self and Soul" and the spirits of "Byzantium" are not decorative — they are reportage from a mediumistic reality Yeats believed he had genuinely contacted

## Scholarly Interpretation

Margaret Mills Harper's _Wisdom of Two: The Spiritual and Literary Collaboration of George and W. B. Yeats_ (2006) has been foundational in re-establishing Georgie Yeats as an intellectual co-creator rather than a passive medium. The "Yeatses' Vision" papers project has further reconstructed the manuscript history, showing that the published _A Vision_ is a radical simplification and reordering of a far more chaotic and collaborative process than the book's formal structure suggests.
