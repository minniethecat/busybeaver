# The Sidhe

The Sidhe (Old Irish *Aes Sídhe*, "people of the mounds") are the fairy folk of Irish mythology — the ancient Tuatha Dé Danann who, defeated by the Milesians, retreated into the hollow hills, cairns, and forts of the Irish landscape. For W. B. Yeats, the Sidhe were not mere decorative folklore but a living supernatural presence, central to the poetry of the Celtic Twilight and to his lifelong engagement with Irish folk belief.

## The Sidhe in Irish Tradition

In the traditional lore that Yeats collected and drew upon, the Sidhe are the descendants of the goddess Danu — "the many-changing Sidhe" [039-033] — who inhabit a parallel world interpenetrating the visible landscape. The Endnotes to Yeats's collected poems record detailed folk beliefs: "the Sidhe, from *Aes Sidhe*, or *Sluagh Sidhe*, the host of the air, and *Sluagh Sidhe*, the host of the Sidhe" [048-016]. They ride on the wind, dwell in ancient forts and cairns, and occasionally abduct mortals into their timeless realm.

The Yeats family's own domestic staff included a faery doctor: "A faery doctor has told me that his patients say they are fully happier, but lose the body and blood of the natural world, when they are among the Sidhe" [048-018]. This personal connection between the poet's household and the living fairy tradition gave Yeats's Sidhe poetry an ethnographic authority unusual among literary treatments of folklore.

## "The Hosting of the Sidhe"

The Sidhe enter Yeats's poetry most dramatically in "The Hosting of the Sidhe" (1899), the opening poem of *The Wind Among the Reeds*. The poem imagines the fairy host riding out from Knocknarea across the western Irish landscape, summoning mortals to abandon the natural world:

The fairy wind that drives the host is both seductive and dangerous — "matters were afoot among the Sidhe" [039-053] suggests supernatural events unfolding beyond mortal perception. The host's passage across the Sligo landscape transforms familiar topography into an enchanted geography.

## The Land of the Sidhe

The "land of the Sidhe" [048-032] in Yeats's poetry is not merely an otherworld but a counter-world — an alternative ontology where time moves differently and mortal categories dissolve. The Endnotes observe that "once, when the land of the Sidhe was the only other world" [048-032], the fairy realm offered the sole alternative to earthly existence. The Sídhe engage in spectral battles: "a battle the Sidhe are said to fight when a person is dying" [048-031], suggesting their realm is intimately connected with the threshold between life and death.

The great cairn on Knocknarea, where "a great queen of the western Sidhe, is buried" [048-015], anchors the Sidhe presence to a specific, visitable location. For Yeats, the Sidhe were simultaneously mythological beings and topographical presences — they inhabited the Sligo hills and cairns of his childhood landscape.

## Sidhe and the Yeatsian Supernatural

The Sidhe represent one pole of the Yeatsian supernatural — the folkloric and indigenous Irish tradition, as distinct from the learned occultism of the Golden Dawn or the philosophical system of *A Vision*. In poems like "The Hosting of the Sidhe" and "The Stolen Child," the Sidhe embody a supernatural that is rooted in place, transmitted orally, and inseparable from the Irish landscape. Their "many-changing" [039-033] nature — shape-shifting, elusive, neither fully benevolent nor malevolent — distinguishes them from the more systematic supernatural beings of Yeats's later occult system.

The blending of Sidhe mythology with personal symbolism continues throughout Yeats's career. The "Sidhe" reappear in the figure of the Leanhaun Shee (the fairy mistress), in the wind that blows through the tower poems, and in the persistent sense of an invisible company — "the host of the air" [048-016] — travelling alongside the living. They are, in effect, the tutelary spirits of Yeats's imaginative geography.
