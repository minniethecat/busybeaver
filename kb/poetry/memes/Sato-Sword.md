# Sato Sword

In March 1920, Junzo Sato — a young Japanese scholar and diplomat — presented W. B. Yeats with a samurai sword that had been in his family for over five hundred years. The gift was an act of homage to the poet, and the sword became one of the most resonant symbolic objects in Yeats's later poetry: a physical presence at [[thoor-ballylee|Thoor Ballylee]] and a recurring motif in *The Tower* (1928) and *The Winding Stair and Other Poems* (1933).

## Symbolic Significance

The Sato sword operates on multiple symbolic levels in Yeats's poetry:

- **Ancestral continuity**: The sword had passed through generations of Sato's family — a living link to a tradition of art, honour, and craftsmanship that Yeats contrasted with modern discontinuity. In "A Dialogue of Self and Soul," the sword stands alongside an embroidered tower as an emblem of "the inheritance of a noble house."
- **Art as permanence**: The sword's blade — forged five centuries earlier — embodies the capacity of crafted objects to outlast the civilisations that produce them. This theme connects to Yeats's Byzantium poems, where the golden bird "of hammered gold and gold enamelling" represents art's transcendence of mortality.
- **East-West synthesis**: The sword arriving from Japan to an Irish tower symbolised, for Yeats, a cultural moment in which the great traditions could speak to one another — "measurement began our might," as he wrote in "Under Ben Bulben," imagining a cultural renewal drawn from multiple sources.

## Poems Featuring the Sword

- **"Meditations in Time of Civil War"** — Part III, "My Table," describes the sword lying on the poet's writing table, "an ancient blade" in its embroidered scabbard
- **"A Dialogue of Self and Soul"** — The sword and its silk wrapping appear as tokens of the "self's" attachment to the physical world, contrasted with the "soul's" desire for disembodied purity
- **"Symbols"** — A short poem listing the tower, the winding stair, and the sword as Yeats's central symbols
