# Blood and the Moon

"Blood and the Moon" is a poem from Yeats's 1928 collection *The Tower*. It is one of several poems in that collection that take [[thoor-ballylee|Thoor Ballylee]] — the Norman tower in Co. Galway that Yeats purchased and restored — as both setting and symbol. The poem moves through three distinct phases: a declaration of the tower's power, a meditation on the eighteenth-century Anglo-Irish intellects (Swift, Burke, Berkeley, Goldsmith), and a visionary conclusion in which the tower becomes a symbol of the soul's ascent.

## Structure and Themes

The poem is built around the opposition between "blood" (ancestral power, violence, the body) and "moon" (intellect, purity, the spiritual). The tower — simultaneously a military structure built for defence and a symbol of the upward-striving soul — mediates between these poles.

The central stanza names the great figures of eighteenth-century Anglo-Irish intellect:

> Swift beating on his breast in sibylline frenzy blind
> Because the heart in his blood-sodden breast had dragged him down into mankind,
> Goldsmith deliberately sipping at the honey-pot of his mind...

These figures represent, for Yeats, a moment when Irish intellect achieved European stature — a golden age of "blood" (proud, passionate Protestant Ireland) fertilised by "moon" (pure intellect).

## The Tower Setting

Thoor Ballylee, the Norman tower in Co. Galway that Yeats bought in 1916 for £35, is the literal and symbolic anchor of the poem. The winding stair, the battlements, and the view from the tower's upper window all appear in the poem's imagery. The tower's dual nature — part castle, part ruined monument — embodies the Yeatsian paradox: the blood-built structure that aspires towards the moon's cold perfection.
