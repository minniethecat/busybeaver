# Immortality in Yeats

Immortality in Yeats's poetry takes multiple forms: the timeless youth of the
Celtic Otherworld, the Hermetic doctrine of spiritual transmigration, and the
artifice of eternity achieved through art itself. Yeats was preoccupied with
what lasts beyond the decay of the body.

## Definition

Yeats never settled on a single doctrine of immortality. His early work imagines
it as a mythic condition — the Sidhe and Oisin dwell in an unchanging realm of
beauty outside time. In middle and late work, immortality becomes a philosophical
problem: can the soul persist after death, and if so, in what form? The
immortals of "The Wanderings of Oisin" are ambivalent figures — beautiful but
inhuman, their "immortal desire" [015-654] set against the speaker's longing
for mortal feeling. By "Sailing to Byzantium," immortality is something the
poet must construct: not given by gods but forged in "the artifice of eternity."

## Origins and Sources

- **Primary source**: Celtic mythology (Tír na nÓg), Neoplatonism, Hermeticism
- **Key texts**: "The Wanderings of Oisin," "Sailing to Byzantium," *A Vision*, "Byzantium"

The Celtic tradition offered Yeats the Land of Youth, a literal realm of
immortality. Plotinus and Neoplatonism provided a philosophical framework
for the soul's persistence and return. The Golden Dawn's ritual system added
the concept of spiritual grades and transmigration. Yeats fused these into
his own system in *A Vision*, where souls cycle through incarnations governed
by the phases of the moon.

## Development Across Yeats's Career

"The Wanderings of Oisin" (1889) is Yeats's earliest sustained meditation on
immortality. Oisin lives three hundred years in the immortal islands —
an enchanted existence that becomes a kind of prison. When he returns to
mortal Ireland, the immortals are remembered as remote and strange:
"That in immortal silence sleeps" [015-152], "the immortals moved among the
fountains" [015-370].

The middle period turns to personal immortality through art. "Sailing to
Byzantium" (1928) is the great statement: the aged poet rejects the "dying
generations" of the natural world and seeks translation into "the artifice
of eternity" — a golden bird singing to the Emperor of Byzantium.

In the late poetry, immortality merges with memory and vision. "All Souls'
Night" imagines the dead returning as conscious presences. "Under Ben Bulben"
ends with Yeats's own epitaph: "Cast a cold eye / On life, on death. /
Horseman, pass by!" — a refusal of consolation that is itself a form of
immortality through the poem's endurance. The "immortal, mild, proud
shadows" of "Coole Park, 1929" [042-027] are the great dead made permanent
in art and memory.

## The Immortal Condition as Ambiguous Gift

A consistent thread in Yeats's treatment of immortality is its ambivalence.
The immortal state, in poem after poem, is presented as both desirable and
deeply suspect. In "The Wanderings of Oisin," the hero's three-hundred-year
sojourn in the immortal islands leaves him enervated, cut off from the
passions that define mortal life. The immortals themselves are "remote" and
"strange" — their perfection makes them less than fully alive. This paradox
intensifies in "Sailing to Byzantium," where the speaker's wish to be gathered
"into the artifice of eternity" involves a willing surrender of the body: the
golden bird is eternal but cannot breed, cannot age, cannot die. Immortality,
in Yeats's imagination, is purchased at the cost of the very experiences that
make life meaningful. The tension between longing for permanence and
recognition that transience is the condition of beauty pervades his work from
the earliest poems to the last.

## Reincarnation and the Lunar Cycle

*A Vision* (1925, revised 1937) provides Yeats's most systematic account of
immortality through the doctrine of reincarnation. Souls cycle through
twenty-eight lunar phases, each incarnation governed by the tension between
the Four Faculties (Will, Mask, Creative Mind, Body of Fate). Death, in this
system, is not an ending but a transition between incarnations — a period of
purification in which the soul reviews its completed life and prepares for
the next. The Thirteenth Cone, described in the book's later sections,
represents a state beyond the cycle — the angelic condition in which the
soul escapes reincarnation entirely. But as with the golden bird of "Sailing
to Byzantium," this escape comes at a cost: the Thirteenth Cone is described
as a state of pure being without becoming, a perfection that poetry — which
requires conflict, desire, and loss — can gesture toward but never inhabit.

## Immortality Through Art vs. Immortality Through Memory

Yeats's late work develops a distinction between two modes of immortality.
The first, immortality through art, is the central claim of "Sailing to
Byzantium" and "Byzantium": the poem outlasts the poet, the golden bird
sings to generations unborn. The second, immortality through memory, is
the elegiac mode of "Coole Park, 1929" and "In Memory of Eva Gore-Booth
and Con Markievicz." Here the dead persist not because they have been
transfigured into art but because they are remembered — by the living, by
the landscape, by the house they inhabited. This second mode is humbler but
also more intimate: it does not require the violent transformation of the
body into a golden bird, only the quiet work of recollection across
generations. "Under Ben Bulben" fuses both modes: the epitaph commands the
horseman to "pass by," acknowledging that even the greatest poet must be
forgotten, while the poem itself — by being read — enacts the very
immortality it seems to renounce.
