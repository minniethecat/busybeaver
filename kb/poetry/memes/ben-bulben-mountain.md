# Ben Bulben

Ben Bulben (Irish: _Binn Ghulbain_) is a distinctive table mountain in Co. Sligo, part of the Dartry Mountains, formed from limestone and shale sculpted by glaciation into its characteristic flat-topped profile. In Yeats's poetry, it is the presiding presence of his final resting place — he is buried at Drumcliff churchyard, "under bare Ben Bulben's head," and his epitaph is carved on his headstone there. Ben Bulben bookends his career: the landscape of his childhood summers becomes the site of his imaginative eternal rest.

## Location and Geography

- **Type**: real Irish location (mountain)
- **Region**: Co. Sligo, Connacht
- **Height**: 526m, with near-vertical north face
- **Drumcliff churchyard**: at the foot of the mountain, where Yeats is buried

## Significance to Yeats

Ben Bulben is the presiding genius of Yeats's epitaph poem, "Under Ben Bulben" (1938, published posthumously 1939). The poem is a last will and testament — instructions for the poet's burial, a summation of his beliefs (the gyres, the phases of the moon, the Irish poetic tradition), and the famous epitaph: "Cast a cold eye / On life, on death. / Horseman, pass by!"

The mountain's flat-topped, bare profile is visually striking — visible from much of north Sligo and the Atlantic approaches. For Yeats, it represented the austerity and permanence he sought in his late style: the "bare" mountain, stripped of ornament, massive and immovable, under which the poet could be laid to rest with no more than a few lines of verse to mark his passage.

Yeats's actual burial arrangements are complex: he died in France in January 1939 and was initially buried at Roquebrune-Cap-Martin. His remains were exhumed and reinterred at Drumcliff in 1948, though there is some doubt about whether the exhumed bones were indeed his. The symbolism of the Sligo burial site is what matters: the poet returning to the landscape that made him.

## Literary and Historical Context

Ben Bulben is a major feature of "Yeats Country" — the Sligo landscape that has become a literary pilgrimage site. The mountain's association with the poet's grave has made it one of the most visited literary sites in Ireland, along with Thoor Ballylee and Coole Park.
