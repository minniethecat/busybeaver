# Michael Robartes and the Dancer

## Preface to Michael Robartes and the Dancer

[046-001]A few of these poems may be difficult to understand, perhaps more difficult than I know. Goethe has said that the poet needs all philosophy, but that he must keep it out of his work. After the first few poems I came into possession of [[michael-robartes|Michael Robartes]]’ exposition of the _Speculum Angelorum et Hominum_ of Geraldus, and in the excitement of arranging and editing could no more keep out philosophy than could Goethe himself at certain periods of his life. I have tried to make understanding easy by a couple of notes, which are at any rate much shorter than those Dante wrote on certain of his odes in the _Convito_ , but I may not have succeeded. It is hard for a writer, who has spent much labour upon his style, to remember that thought, which seems to him natural and logical like that style, may be unintelligible to others. The first excitement over, and the thought changed into settled conviction, his interest in simple, that is to say in normal emotion, is always I think increased; he is no longer looking for candlestick and matches but at the objects in the room.

[046-002]I have given no account of Robartes himself, nor of his discovery of the explanation of Geraldus’ diagrams and pictures in the traditional knowledge of a certain obscure Arab tribe, for I hope that my selection from the great mass of his letters and table talk, which I owe to his friend [[john-aherne|John Aherne]], may be published before, or at any rate but soon after this little book, which, like all hand-printed books will take a long time for the setting up and printing off and for the drying of the pages.

[046-003]W. B. Yeats.

## Michael Robartes and the Dancer

[046-004]He |  Opinion is not worth a rush;   

[046-005]In this altar-piece the knight,   

[046-006]Who grips his long spear so to push   

[046-007]That dragon through the fading [[shadow-and-light|light]],   

[046-008]Loved the lady; and it’s plain   

[046-009]The half-dead dragon was her thought,   

[046-010]That every morning rose again   

[046-011]And dug its claws and shrieked and fought.   

[046-012]Could the impossible come to pass   

[046-013]She would have time to turn her eyes,   

[046-014]Her lover thought, upon the glass   

[046-015]And on the instant would grow wise.  

[046-016]---|---  

[046-017]She |  You mean they argued.  

[046-018]He |  Put it so;   

[046-019]But bear in mind your lover’s wage   

[046-020]Is what your looking-glass can show,   

[046-021]And that he will turn green with rage   

[046-022]At all that is not pictured there.  

[046-023]She |  May I not put myself to college?  

[046-024]He |  Go pluck Athena by the hair;   

[046-025]For what mere book can grant a knowledge   

[046-026]With an impassioned gravity   

[046-027]Appropriate to that beating breast,   

[046-028]That vigorous thigh, that [[dream-and-vision|dreaming]] eye?   

[046-029]And may the devil take the rest.  

[046-030]She |  And must no beautiful woman be   

[046-031]Learned like a man?  

[046-032]He |  Paul Veronese   

[046-033]And all his sacred company   

[046-034]Imagined bodies all their days   

[046-035]By the lagoon you love so much,   

[046-036]For proud, soft, ceremonious proof   

[046-037]That all must come to sight and touch;   

[046-038]While Michael Angelo’s Sistine roof   

[046-039]His “Morning” and his “Night” disclose   

[046-040]How sinew that has been pulled tight,   

[046-041]Or it may be loosened in repose,   

[046-042]Can rule by supernatural right   

[046-043]Yet be but sinew.  

[046-044]She |  I have heard said   

[046-045]There is great danger in the body.  

[046-046]He |  Did God in portioning wine and bread   

[046-047]Give man His thought or His mere body?  

[046-048]She |  My wretched dragon is perplexed.  

[046-049]He |  I have principles to prove me right.   

[046-050]It follows from this Latin text   

[046-051]That blest souls are not composite.   

[046-052]And that all beautiful women may   

[046-053]Live in uncomposite blessedness,   

[046-054]And lead us to the like﻿—if they   

[046-055]Will banish every thought, unless   

[046-056]The lineaments that please their view   

[046-057]When the long looking-glass is full,   

[046-058]Even from the foot-sole think it too.  

[046-059]She |  They say such different things at school.  
  
## Solomon and the Witch

[046-060]And thus declared that Arab lady:   

[046-061]“Last night, where under the wild moon   

[046-062]On grassy mattress I had laid me,   

[046-063]Within my arms great Solomon,   

[046-064]I suddenly cried out in a strange tongue   

[046-065]Not his, not mine.” Who understood   

[046-066]What ever had been said, sighed, sung,   

[046-067]Howled, miau-d, barked, brayed, belled, yelled, cried, crowed,   

[046-068]Thereon explained: “A cockerel   

[046-069]Cried from a blossoming apple bough   

[046-070]Three hundred years before the Fall,   

[046-071]And never crew again till now,   

[046-072]And would not now but that he thought,   

[046-073]Chance being at one with Choice at last,   

[046-074]All that the brigand apple brought   

[046-075]And this foul world were dead at last.   

[046-076]He that crowed out [[time-and-eternity|eternity]]   

[046-077]Thought to have crowed it in again.   

[046-078]For though love has a spider’s eye   

[046-079]To find out some appropriate pain,   

[046-080]Aye, though all [[fire-passion|passion]]’s in the glance,   

[046-081]For every nerve: and tests a lover   

[046-082]With cruelties of Choice and Chance;   

[046-083]And when at last that murder’s over   

[046-084]Maybe the bride-bed brings despair   

[046-085]For each an imagined image brings   

[046-086]And finds a real image there;   

[046-087]Yet the world ends when these two things,   

[046-088]Though several, are a single light,   

[046-089]When oil and wick are burned in one;   

[046-090]Therefore a blessed moon last night   

[046-091]Gave Sheba to her Solomon.”

[046-092]“Yet the world stays”: “If that be so,   

[046-093]Your cockerel found us in the wrong   

[046-094]Although he thought it worth a crow.   

[046-095]Maybe an image is too strong   

[046-096]Or maybe is not strong enough.”

[046-097]“The night has fallen; not a sound   

[046-098]In the forbidden sacred grove   

[046-099]Unless a petal hit the ground,   

[046-100]Nor any human sight within it   

[046-101]But the crushed grass where we have lain;   

[046-102]And the moon is wilder every minute.   

[046-103]Oh, Solomon! let us try again.”

## An Image from a Past Life[23](endnotes.xhtml#note-23)

[046-104]He |  Never until this night have I been stirred.   

[046-105]The elaborate star-light has thrown reflections   

[046-106]On the dark stream,   

[046-107]Till all the eddies gleam;   

[046-108]And thereupon there comes that scream   

[046-109]From terrified, invisible beast or bird:   

[046-110]Image of poignant [[memory-in-yeats|recollection]].  

[046-111]---|---  

[046-112]She |  An image of my heart that is smitten through   

[046-113]Out of all likelihood, or reason.   

[046-114]And when at last,   

[046-115]Youth’s bitterness being past,   

[046-116]I had thought that all my days were cast   

[046-117]Amid most lovely places; smitten as though   

[046-118]It had not learned its lesson.  

[046-119]He |  Why have you laid your hands upon my eyes?   

[046-120]What can have suddenly alarmed you   

[046-121]Whereon ’twere best   

[046-122]My eyes should never rest?   

[046-123]What is there but the slowly fading west,   

[046-124]The [[water-imagery|river]] imaging the flashing skies,   

[046-125]All that to this moment charmed you?  

[046-126]She |  A sweetheart from another life floats there   

[046-127]As though she had been forced to linger   

[046-128]From vague distress   

[046-129]Or arrogant loveliness,   

[046-130]Merely to loosen out a tress   

[046-131]Among the starry eddies of her hair   

[046-132]Upon the paleness of a finger.  

[046-133]He |  But why should you grow suddenly afraid   

[046-134]And start﻿—I at your shoulder﻿—   

[046-135]Imagining   

[046-136]That any night could bring   

[046-137]An image up, or anything   

[046-138]Even to eyes that beauty had driven mad,   

[046-139]But images to make me fonder.  

[046-140]She |  Now she has thrown her arms above her head;   

[046-141]Whether she threw them up to flout me,   

[046-142]Or but to find,   

[046-143]Now that no fingers bind,   

[046-144]That her hair streams upon the wind,   

[046-145]I do not know, that know I am afraid   

[046-146]Of the hovering thing night brought me.  
  
## Under Saturn

[046-147]Do not because this day I have grown saturnine   

[046-148]Imagine that lost love, inseparable from my thought   

[046-149]Because I have no other youth, can make me pine;   

[046-150]For how should I forget the wisdom that you brought,   

[046-151]The comfort that you made? Although my wits have gone   

[046-152]On a fantastic ride, my horse’s flanks were spurred   

[046-153]By childish memories of an old cross Pollexfen,   

[046-154]And of a Middleton, whose name you never heard,   

[046-155]And of a red-haired Yeats whose looks, although he died   

[046-156]Before my time, seem like a vivid memory.   

[046-157]You heard that labouring man who had served my people. He said   

[046-158]Upon the open road, near to the [[nature-in-yeats|Sligo]] quay﻿—   

[046-159]No, no, not said, but cried it out﻿—“You have come again   

[046-160]And surely after twenty years it was time to come.”   

[046-161]I am thinking of a child’s vow sworn in vain   

[046-162]Never to leave that valley his fathers called their home.

[046-163]November, 1919.

## Easter, 1916

[046-164]I have met them at close of day   

[046-165]Coming with vivid faces   

[046-166]From counter or desk among grey   

[046-167]Eighteenth-century houses.   

[046-168]I have passed with a nod of the head   

[046-169]Or polite meaningless words,   

[046-170]Or have lingered awhile and said   

[046-171]Polite meaningless words,   

[046-172]And thought before I had done   

[046-173]Of a mocking tale or a gibe   

[046-174]To please a companion   

[046-175]Around the fire at the club,   

[046-176]Being certain that they and I   

[046-177]But lived where motley is worn:   

[046-178]All changed, changed utterly:   

[046-179]A terrible beauty is born.

[046-180]That woman’s days were spent   

[046-181]In ignorant good will,   

[046-182]Her nights in argument   

[046-183]Until her voice grew shrill.   

[046-184]What voice more sweet than hers   

[046-185]When young and beautiful,   

[046-186]She rode to harriers?   

[046-187]This man had kept a school   

[046-188]And rode our winged horse;   

[046-189]This other his helper and friend   

[046-190]Was coming into his force;   

[046-191]He might have won fame in the end,   

[046-192]So sensitive his nature seemed,   

[046-193]So daring and sweet his thought.   

[046-194]This other man I had dreamed   

[046-195]A drunken, vain-glorious lout.   

[046-196]He had done most bitter wrong   

[046-197]To some who are near my heart,   

[046-198]Yet I number him in the song;   

[046-199]He, too, has resigned his part   

[046-200]In the casual comedy;   

[046-201]He, too, has been changed in his turn,   

[046-202]Transformed utterly:   

[046-203]A terrible beauty is born.

[046-204]Hearts with one purpose alone   

[046-205]Through summer and winter seem   

[046-206]Enchanted to a stone   

[046-207]To trouble the living stream.   

[046-208]The horse that comes from the road,   

[046-209]The rider, the birds that range   

[046-210]From cloud to tumbling cloud,   

[046-211]Minute by minute they change;   

[046-212]A shadow of cloud on the stream   

[046-213]Changes minute by minute;   

[046-214]A horse-hoof slides on the brim,   

[046-215]And a horse plashes within it   

[046-216]Where long-legged moor-hens dive,   

[046-217]And hens to moor-cocks call.   

[046-218]Minute by minute they live:   

[046-219]The stone’s in the midst of all.

[046-220]Too long a sacrifice   

[046-221]Can make a stone of the heart.   

[046-222]O when may it suffice?   

[046-223]That is heaven’s part, our part   

[046-224]To murmur name upon name,   

[046-225]As a mother names her child   

[046-226]When sleep at last has come   

[046-227]On limbs that had run wild.   

[046-228]What is it but nightfall?   

[046-229]No, no, not night but death;   

[046-230]Was it needless death after all?   

[046-231]For England may keep faith   

[046-232]For all that is done and said.   

[046-233]We know their dream; enough   

[046-234]To know they dreamed and are dead;   

[046-235]And what if excess of love   

[046-236]Bewildered them till they died?   

[046-237]I write it out in a verse﻿—   

[046-238]MacDonagh and MacBride   

[046-239]And Connolly and Pearse   

[046-240]Now and in time to be,   

[046-241]Wherever green is worn,   

[046-242]Are changed, changed utterly:   

[046-243]A terrible beauty is born.

[046-244]September 25th, 1916.

## Sixteen Dead Men

[046-245]O but we talked at large before   

[046-246]The sixteen men were shot,   

[046-247]But who can talk of give and take,   

[046-248]What should be and what not?   

[046-249]While those dead men are loitering there   

[046-250]To stir the boiling pot.

[046-251]You say that we should still the land   

[046-252]Till Germany’s overcome;   

[046-253]But who is there to argue that   

[046-254]Now Pearse is deaf and dumb?   

[046-255]And is their logic to outweigh   

[046-256]MacDonagh’s bony thumb?

[046-257]How could you dream they’d listen   

[046-258]That have an ear alone   

[046-259]For those new comrades they have found   

[046-260]Lord Edward and Wolfe Tone,   

[046-261]Or meddle with our give and take   

[046-262]That converse bone to bone.

## The Rose Tree

[046-263]“O words are lightly spoken,”   

[046-264]Said Pearse to Connolly,   

[046-265]“Maybe a breath of politic words   

[046-266]Has withered our [[rose-symbol|Rose]] Tree;   

[046-267]Or maybe but a wind that blows   

[046-268]Across the bitter sea.”

[046-269]“It needs to be but watered,”   

[046-270]James Connolly replied,   

[046-271]“To make the green come out again   

[046-272]And spread on every side,   

[046-273]And shake the blossom from the bud   

[046-274]To be the garden’s pride.”

[046-275]“But where can we draw water,”   

[046-276]Said Pearse to Connolly,   

[046-277]“When all the wells are parched away?   

[046-278]O plain as plain can be   

[046-279]There’s nothing but our own red blood   

[046-280]Can make a right Rose Tree.”

## On a Political Prisoner

[046-281]She that but little patience knew,   

[046-282]From childhood on, had now so much   

[046-283]A grey gull lost its fear and flew   

[046-284]Down to her cell and there alit,   

[046-285]And there endured her fingers’ touch   

[046-286]And from her fingers ate its bit.

[046-287]Did she in touching that lone wing   

[046-288]Recall the years before her mind   

[046-289]Became a bitter, an abstract thing,   

[046-290]Her thought some popular enmity:   

[046-291]Blind and leader of the blind   

[046-292]Drinking the foul ditch where they lie?

[046-293]When long ago I saw her ride   

[046-294]Under [[ben-bulben-mountain|Ben Bulben]] to the meet,   

[046-295]The beauty of her country-side   

[046-296]With all youth’s lonely wildness stirred,   

[046-297]She seemed to have grown clean and sweet   

[046-298]Like any [[stone-imagery|rock]]-bred, sea-borne bird:

[046-299]Sea-borne, or balanced on the air   

[046-300]When first it sprang out of the nest   

[046-301]Upon some lofty rock to stare   

[046-302]Upon the cloudy canopy,   

[046-303]While under its storm-beaten breast   

[046-304]Cried out the hollows of the sea.

## The Leaders of the Crowd

[046-305]They must to keep their certainty accuse   

[046-306]All that are different of a base intent;   

[046-307]Pull down established honour; hawk for news   

[046-308]Whatever their loose fantasy invent   

[046-309]And murmur it with bated breath, as though   

[046-310]The abounding gutter had been Helicon   

[046-311]Or calumny a song. How can they know   

[046-312]Truth flourishes where the student’s lamp has shone,   

[046-313]And there alone, that have no solitude?   

[046-314]So the crowd come they care not what may come.   

[046-315]They have loud [[song-and-poetry|music]], hope every day renewed   

[046-316]And heartier loves; that lamp is from the tomb.

## Towards Break of Day

[046-317]Was it the double of my dream   

[046-318]The woman that by me lay   

[046-319]Dreamed, or did we halve a dream   

[046-320]Under the first cold gleam of day?

[046-321]I thought “there is a waterfall   

[046-322]Upon Ben Bulben side,   

[046-323]That all my childhood counted dear;   

[046-324]Were I to travel far and wide   

[046-325]I could not find a thing so dear.”   

[046-326]My memories had magnified   

[046-327]So many times childish delight.

[046-328]I would have touched it like a child   

[046-329]But knew my finger could but have touched   

[046-330]Cold stone and water. I grew wild   

[046-331]Even accusing heaven because   

[046-332]It had set down among its laws:   

[046-333]Nothing that we love over-much   

[046-334]Is ponderable to our touch.

[046-335]I dreamed towards break of day,   

[046-336]The cold blown spray in my nostril.   

[046-337]But she that beside me lay   

[046-338]Had watched in bitterer sleep   

[046-339]The marvellous stag of Arthur,   

[046-340]That lofty white stag, leap   

[046-341]From mountain steep to steep.

## Demon and Beast

[046-342]For certain minutes at the least   

[046-343]That crafty demon and that loud beast   

[046-344]That plague me day and night   

[046-345]Ran out of my sight;   

[046-346]Though I had long pernned in the [[gyres|gyre]],   

[046-347]Between my hatred and [[love-in-yeats|desire]],   

[046-348]I saw my freedom won   

[046-349]And all laugh in the sun.

[046-350]The glittering eyes in a death’s head   

[046-351]Of old Luke Wadding’s portrait said   

[046-352]Welcome, and the Ormonds all   

[046-353]Nodded upon the wall,   

[046-354]And even Stafford smiled as though   

[046-355]It made him happier to know   

[046-356]I understood his plan.   

[046-357]Now that the loud beast ran   

[046-358]There was no portrait in the Gallery   

[046-359]But beckoned to sweet company,   

[046-360]For all men’s thoughts grew clear   

[046-361]Being dear as mine are dear.

[046-362]But soon a tear-drop started up   

[046-363]For aimless joy had made me stop   

[046-364]Beside the little lake   

[046-365]To watch a white gull take   

[046-366]A bit of bread thrown up into the air;   

[046-367]Now [[gyres|gyring]] down and pernning there   

[046-368]He splashed where an absurd   

[046-369]Portly green-pated bird   

[046-370]Shook off the water from his back;   

[046-371]Being no more demoniac   

[046-372]A stupid happy creature   

[046-373]Could rouse my whole nature.

[046-374]Yet I am certain as can be   

[046-375]That every natural victory   

[046-376]Belongs to beast or demon,   

[046-377]That never yet had freeman   

[046-378]Right mastery of natural things,   

[046-379]And that mere growing old, that brings   

[046-380]Chilled blood, this sweetness brought;   

[046-381]Yet have no dearer thought   

[046-382]Than that I may find out a way   

[046-383]To make it linger half a day.

[046-384]O what a sweetness strayed   

[046-385]Through barren Thebaid,   

[046-386]Or by the Mareotic sea   

[046-387]When that exultant Anthony   

[046-388]And twice a thousand more   

[046-389]Starved upon the shore   

[046-390]And withered to a bag of bones:   

[046-391]What had the Caesars but their thrones?

## The Second Coming[24](endnotes.xhtml#note-24)

[046-392]Turning and turning in the widening gyre   

[046-393]The falcon cannot hear the falconer;   

[046-394]Things fall apart; the centre cannot hold;   

[046-395]Mere anarchy is loosed upon the world,   

[046-396]The blood-dimmed tide is loosed, and everywhere   

[046-397]The ceremony of innocence is drowned;   

[046-398]The best lack all conviction, while the worst   

[046-399]Are full of passionate intensity.

[046-400]Surely some revelation is at hand;   

[046-401]Surely the Second Coming is at hand.   

[046-402]The Second Coming! Hardly are those words out   

[046-403]When a vast image out of [[spiritus-mundi|Spiritus Mundi]]   

[046-404]Troubles my sight: somewhere in sands of the desert   

[046-405]A shape with lion body and the head of a man,   

[046-406]A gaze blank and pitiless as the sun,   

[046-407]Is moving its slow thighs, while all about it   

[046-408]Reel shadows of the indignant desert birds.   

[046-409]The darkness drops again; but now I know   

[046-410]That twenty centuries of stony sleep   

[046-411]Were vexed to nightmare by a rocking cradle,   

[046-412]And what rough beast, its hour come round at last,   

[046-413]Slouches towards Bethlehem to be born?

## A Prayer for My Daughter

[046-414]Once more the storm is howling and half hid   

[046-415]Under this cradle-hood and coverlid   

[046-416]My child sleeps on. There is no obstacle   

[046-417]But Gregory’s wood and one bare hill   

[046-418]Whereby the haystack and roof-levelling wind,   

[046-419]Bred on the Atlantic, can be stayed;   

[046-420]And for an hour I have walked and prayed   

[046-421]Because of the great gloom that is in my mind.

[046-422]I have walked and prayed for this young child an hour   

[046-423]And heard the sea-wind scream upon the tower,   

[046-424]And under the arches of the bridge, and scream   

[046-425]In the elms above the flooded stream;   

[046-426]Imagining in excited reverie   

[046-427]That the future years had come,   

[046-428]Dancing to a frenzied drum,   

[046-429]Out of the murderous innocence of the sea.

[046-430]May she be granted beauty and yet not   

[046-431][[eternal-beauty|Beauty]] to make a stranger’s eye distraught,   

[046-432]Or hers before a looking-glass, for such,   

[046-433]Being made beautiful overmuch,   

[046-434]Consider beauty a sufficient end,   

[046-435]Lose natural kindness and maybe   

[046-436]The heart-revealing intimacy   

[046-437]That chooses right and never find a friend.

[046-438]Helen being chosen found life flat and dull   

[046-439]And later had much trouble from a fool,   

[046-440]While that great Queen, that rose out of the spray,   

[046-441]Being fatherless could have her way   

[046-442]Yet chose a bandy-legged smith for man.   

[046-443]It’s certain that fine women eat   

[046-444]A crazy salad with their meat   

[046-445]Whereby the Horn of Plenty is undone.

[046-446]In courtesy I’d have her chiefly learned;   

[046-447]Hearts are not had as a gift but hearts are earned   

[046-448]By those that are not entirely beautiful;   

[046-449]Yet many, that have played the fool   

[046-450]For beauty’s very self, has charm made wise,   

[046-451]And many a poor man that has roved,   

[046-452]Loved and thought himself beloved,   

[046-453]From a glad kindness cannot take his eyes.

[046-454]May she become a flourishing hidden tree   

[046-455]That all her thoughts may like the linnet be,   

[046-456]And have no business but dispensing round   

[046-457]Their magnanimities of sound,   

[046-458]Nor but in merriment begin a chase,   

[046-459]Nor but in merriment a quarrel.   

[046-460]Oh, may she live like some green laurel   

[046-461]Rooted in one dear perpetual place.

[046-462]My mind, because the minds that I have loved,   

[046-463]The sort of beauty that I have approved,   

[046-464]Prosper but little, has dried up of late,   

[046-465]Yet knows that to be choked with hate   

[046-466]May well be of all evil chances chief.   

[046-467]If there’s no hatred in a mind   

[046-468]Assault and battery of the wind

## To Be Carved on a Stone at Ballylee

[046-500]I, the poet William Yeats,   

[046-501]With old mill boards and sea-green slates,   

[046-502]And smithy work from the Gort forge,
