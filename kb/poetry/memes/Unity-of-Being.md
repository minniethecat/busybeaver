## Definition

Unity of Being is Yeats's term for the ideal state in which all human faculties — body, mind, spirit, will, emotion — are integrated into a single harmonious whole, such that no distinction between thinker and thought, doer and deed, remains. It is the psychological and aesthetic counterpart to the Full Moon (Phase 15) in the lunar system of *A Vision*.

## Overview

Unity of Being is one of W. B. Yeats's central philosophical ideals: the complete and harmonious integration of all aspects of human existence — body and soul, thought and action, art and life, the individual and the community. It represents Yeats's answer to what he saw as the fragmentation of modern consciousness, and it animates his most celebrated poetry.

## Origins and Sources

Yeats's Unity of Being draws on a convergence of sources:

- **Neoplatonism**: The Plotinian ideal of the One, in which all division is transcended
- **Goethe**: Goethe's concept of *gegenständliches Denken* (thinking with the object), in which subject and object participate in a single cognitive act
- **Castiglione**: The Renaissance ideal of the complete courtier, accomplished in arms, letters, and arts
- **Blake**: The integration of contraries — "Without Contraries is no progression"

Yeats cites Goethe's authority explicitly: "Goethe has said that the poet needs all philosophy but must keep it out of his work."(046-001)

## The Dancer from the Dance

The most famous expression of Unity of Being in Yeats's verse comes at the close of "Among School Children." After meditating on the great philosophers — Plato, Aristotle, Pythagoras — and their separation from lived experience, Yeats arrives at a vision of wholeness that transcends all division:

> O chestnut tree, great rooted blossomer,
> Are you the leaf, the blossom or the bole?
> O body swayed to music, O brightening glance,
> How can we know the dancer from the dance?(047-728)(047-729)(047-730)(047-731)

The chestnut tree cannot be divided into its parts — the leaf, blossom, and trunk are one living whole. Similarly, the dancer and the dance are inseparable: the art exists only in and through the body's movement.

## Labour as Blossoming

In the same poem, Yeats reimagines labour not as toil but as organic flourishing: "Labour is blossoming or dancing where / The body is not bruised to pleasure soul."(047-724)(047-725) This contrasts with the ascetic traditions that deny the body to exalt the spirit. In Unity of Being, physical existence is not an obstacle to spiritual achievement but its necessary medium.

## The Anti-Self and the Mask

Unity of Being is intimately connected to Yeats's doctrine of the mask — the idea that by adopting an anti-self (a persona opposite to one's natural disposition), one can achieve a fuller, more complete identity. The pursuit of Unity of Being is the pursuit of the mask that completes the self, transforming internal conflict into creative energy.

## Cultural Application

Yeats applied the concept beyond individuals to nations. He saw Irish culture as fragmented — "Religious Ireland…thinks of divine things as a round of duties separated from life," and "political Ireland sees the good citizen but as a man who holds to certain opinions."(048-049) Against this he contrasted the ideal of "the whole being" — a culture in which religion, art, politics, and daily life are integrated. Goethe's fable in *Wilhelm Meister* shows "the futility of all discipline that is not of the whole being."(048-049)

## The Composite Soul

In "A Prayer for My Daughter," Yeats expresses the ideal negatively: "that blest souls are not composite"(046-051) — suggesting that fragmentation of the self is a spiritual failing, while blessedness consists in unity and simplicity of being. The "composite" soul is divided against itself; the "blest" soul is whole.

## The Philosophical Tradition

Yeats's concept draws on a long tradition that seeks to overcome the dualisms of Western thought. In "Among School Children," Yeats names the three foundational philosophers: "Plato thought nature but a spume that plays / Upon a ghostly paradigm of things," "Soldier Aristotle played the taws / Upon the bottom of a king of kings," and "world-famous golden-thighed Pythagoras / Fingered upon a fiddle-stick or strings."(047-718)(047-719)(047-720)(047-721)(047-722) The tone is gently mocking — each philosopher is reduced to a comic gesture that abstracts him from lived bodily experience. Yeats's point is that their systems finally separate thought from the body. The dancer thinks with her body; the chestnut tree does not debate whether it is leaf or root.

## Unity of Being as Social Ideal

For Yeats, Unity of Being was never merely a private ideal. He associated it with whole historical cultures, particularly Renaissance Italy and the Byzantine Empire — societies in which the spiritual, the artistic, and the political were not yet separated into distinct domains. The fragmentation Yeats diagnosed was a distinctly modern affliction, produced by the division of labour, the rise of scientific rationalism, and the decline of aristocratic patronage. "A kind old nun in a white hood"(047-669) who greets the speaker in "Among School Children" is a figure for a world in which religious and educational functions were still fused. Yeats's thinking resists simple nostalgia: the Unity of Being he seeks is not a recovery of the past but a new synthesis, achieved through the very disciplines that modernity has fragmented.

## Scholarly Interpretation

Richard Ellmann, in *The Identity of Yeats*, treats Unity of Being as the master-concept of Yeats's thought — the ideal toward which all his other doctrines (mask, gyres, phases) are oriented. More recent critics, including Denis Donoghue, have questioned whether Unity of Being is a coherent philosophical position or a rhetorical figure for something Yeats felt but could not analyse. The consensus now is that Unity of Being functions as a regulative ideal — a goal that can never be permanently achieved but that gives direction and purpose to both art and life.
