# County Sligo

Sligo is the landscape of Yeats's imagination. Though born in Dublin, he spent his formative childhood summers in Co. Sligo with his maternal grandparents, the Pollexfens, and the west of Ireland became his spiritual and poetic home. Ben Bulben, Lough Gill, Knocknarea, Drumcliff — these places recur throughout his poetry not as mere scenery but as presences, saturated with mythological and personal meaning. Sligo is where Yeats chose to be buried: "Under bare Ben Bulben's head / In Drumcliff churchyard Yeats is laid."

## Location and Geography

- **Type**: real Irish location
- **Region**: Connacht, northwest Ireland
- **Key sites**: Ben Bulben (mountain), Lough Gill (lake), Innisfree (island), Knocknarea (mountain with Queen Maeve's cairn), Drumcliff (churchyard), Rosses Point (coastal village)

## Significance to Yeats

Sligo was not merely the site of childhood holidays — it was the place where Yeats first encountered the Irish folk tradition that would shape his poetry. His uncle George Pollexfen introduced him to the stories of the local people, the fairy lore, the legends of the Sidhe. The landscape itself — the "bare Ben Bulben," the lake isle of Innisfree, the cairn of Queen Maeve on Knocknarea — became the cast of characters in his poetic imagination.

The Pollexfen family's milling and shipping business in Sligo gave Yeats a connection to the practical, Protestant, commercial Ireland that balanced his father's bohemian artistic circles in Dublin. This tension between the dreamy, myth-saturated west and the rational, modernizing east runs throughout his work.

Sligo also represents a specifically Yeatsian version of Irish identity — not the Catholic nationalism of the south nor the Presbyterian industrialism of the north, but an older, stranger Ireland of megalithic monuments, fairy forts, and ancestral memory.

## Literary and Historical Context

Sligo in the late 19th century was a region rich in folk tradition but economically declining. Yeats's poetry preserves a world that was rapidly disappearing — the oral culture of storytelling, fairy belief, and seasonal ritual that had survived from pre-Christian Ireland. His decision to be buried at Drumcliff, under Ben Bulben, was an act of imaginative repossession — the poet claiming the landscape that had claimed him.
