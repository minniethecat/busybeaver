# Irish Free State

The Irish Free State (Saorstát Éireann) was established on 6 December 1922 under the terms of the Anglo-Irish Treaty, which ended the Irish War of Independence (1919–1921). The Treaty granted Ireland dominion status within the British Commonwealth — a compromise that split the Irish nationalist movement and precipitated the Irish Civil War (1922–1923). The Free State existed until 1937, when a new constitution renamed it simply "Ireland" (Éire).

## Yeats and the Free State

Yeats's relationship with the new state was complex and ambivalent. He accepted appointment as a Senator in the Free State's first Seanad Éireann in 1922 and served until 1928 — a role that placed him at the centre of the new nation's political life. His Senate speeches on divorce, censorship, and cultural policy established him as a defender of Protestant minority rights and a critic of what he saw as the Catholic and provincial character of the new state.

The founding of the Free State coincided with some of Yeats's greatest poems. "Meditations in Time of Civil War" (1922–23) was written at [[thoor-ballylee|Thoor Ballylee]] as the violence of the Civil War swept across Ireland. "The Tower" (1928) and "The Winding Stair" (1933) engage repeatedly with the political and spiritual condition of the new nation, often in elegiac or admonitory tones.

## Key Events

- **Anglo-Irish Treaty (December 1921)**: Negotiated by Michael Collins and Arthur Griffith, the Treaty partitioned Ireland (creating Northern Ireland) and established the Free State as a dominion
- **Civil War (1922–1923)**: The pro-Treaty Free State forces defeated the anti-Treaty IRA; Yeats's "Meditations in Time of Civil War" records the poet's reaction to the violence from his tower in Galway
- **Yeats's Senate Service (1922–1928)**: Yeats served as a cultural nationalist voice, speaking on arts funding, the Lane Bequest, and the Coinage Act
- **Constitution of 1937**: The Free State was superseded by a new constitution that claimed sovereignty over all of Ireland
