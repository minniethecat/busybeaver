# A Prayer for My Daughter

"A Prayer for My Daughter" is a lyric poem by W. B. Yeats, composed in June 1919 and first collected in *Michael Robartes and the Dancer* (1921). Written at Thoor Ballylee during a storm — "Once more the storm is howling" [046-489] — as Yeats's infant daughter Anne slept beneath her cradle-hood, the poem is a father's meditation on the world his child will inherit and the virtues that might protect her in it. It is the companion poem to "The Second Coming," written in the same year: where "The Second Coming" envisions a world breaking apart in widening gyres, "A Prayer for My Daughter" asks what "custom and ceremony" [046-490] might sustain a life within such a world.

## The Poem (excerpt)

> Once more the storm is howling and half hid [046-489]  
> Under this cradle-hood and coverlid  
> My child sleeps on. There is no obstacle  
> But Gregory's wood and one bare hill  
> Whereby the haystack and roof-levelling wind,  
> Bred on the Atlantic, can be stayed;  

The opening stanza establishes the setting — the storm battering the tower, the child sleeping — and introduces the central contrast of the poem: the violence of the world outside against the fragile peace of the domestic interior. The "roof-levelling wind" [046-489] is the same force that, in "The Second Coming," has set "mere anarchy" loose upon the world — but here it is held at bay by "Gregory's wood and one bare hill" [046-489], the landscape of Lady Gregory's Coole Park estate.

The poem's subsequent stanzas unfold a series of prayers — or better, hopes — for the daughter. She should have beauty, but not "beauty to make a stranger's eye distraught" [046-489]; excessive beauty, the speaker reflects, was the undoing of Helen of Troy and of Aphrodite herself, who "chose a bandy-legged smith for man" [046-490]. She should learn "courtesy" — "Hearts are not had as a gift but hearts are earned" [046-490]. She should become "a flourishing hidden tree" [046-490], rooted and generous, rather than a public figure buffeted by opinion. The poem's most famous passage is a denunciation of "intellectual hatred" — "the worst" of all evils [046-490] — in which the speaker alludes to Maud Gonne, the "loveliest woman born / Out of the mouth of Plenty's horn" [046-490], whose "opinionated mind" [046-490] traded beauty for the "old bellows full of angry wind" [046-490] of political hatred.

The closing stanza offers the poem's resolution: "how but in custom and in ceremony / Are innocence and beauty born?" [046-490]. The "ceremony of innocence" that is "drowned" in "The Second Coming" is here, in the same year's same voice, proposed as the foundation of a life well lived. "Ceremony" is "a name for the rich horn" [046-490]; "custom" for "the spreading laurel tree" [046-490]. The poem that began with a storm ends with a vision of settled, ceremonious rootedness — the laurel tree that grows in "one dear perpetual place" [046-490].

## Themes

The poem is centrally about the tension between **beauty and danger**, **the public and the private**, **hatred and ceremony**. Yeats's vision of what his daughter should become — a "flourishing hidden tree" [046-490] — is a deliberate rejection of the public life of political passion that had consumed Maud Gonne. The "opinionated mind" [046-490] is the mind that has been drawn into the "thoroughfares" [046-490] of political conflict where "arrogance and hatred are the wares" [046-490]. Against this, Yeats sets the domestic ideal of a house "where all's accustomed, ceremonious" [046-490] — a place of inherited forms, quiet virtues, and rooted love.

The poem's gender politics are unmistakable and, for many readers, troubling. The daughter is imagined as a passive creature of hidden virtue, her life circumscribed by the domestic sphere, while the public world of action and opinion is implicitly masculine and dangerous. Yet the poem's deeper argument transcends its specific prescriptions: it asks, genuinely, what modes of life can survive the storm of history, and answers that only those rooted in "custom and ceremony" — in inherited forms of meaning — can endure "though every face should scowl / And every windy quarter howl" [046-490].

## Context

Anne Butler Yeats, born on 26 February 1919, was Yeats's first child with his wife Georgie Hyde-Lees. The poem was written when Anne was less than four months old, during a period when Yeats was also working on the philosophical system that would become *A Vision* and on the poems that would form the core of his mature achievement. The storm that opens the poem is literal — a gale blowing in off the Atlantic — but also figurative: the storm of the war that had just ended, the storm of the revolution beginning in Ireland, the storm of time itself.
