# Old Age in Yeats

Old age is one of Yeats's most persistent and painful themes: the body's
declension set against the mind's undiminished desire. Across his career, Yeats
returned to the subject with increasing urgency, transforming private rage at
physical decay into some of his greatest poems.

## Definition

Yeats treats old age not as a gentle twilight but as an outrage — the mind
"fastened to a dying animal," as he would write in "Sailing to Byzantium."
Old age in Yeats is defined by the contradiction between decaying body and
raging spirit. The aged speaker of "The Tower" asks, rhetorically: "What shall
I do with this absurdity — / O heart, O troubled heart — this caricature, /
Decrepit age that has been tied to me / As to a dog's tail?" (047-132).

## The Body as "Dying Animal"

Yeats's most concentrated meditation on old age is "Sailing to Byzantium" (1928), which opens with the declaration: "That is no country for old men" (047-001). The poem's first stanza catalogues a world of youthful fecundity — "the young in one another's arms" (047-002), "the salmon-falls, the mackerel-crowded seas" (047-004), "fish, flesh, or fowl" (047-005) — from which the aged speaker is excluded. The phrase "whatever is begotten, born, and dies" (047-006) compresses the entire mortal cycle into a single line, against which the aged speaker's exclusion feels both bitter and strangely privileged.

The second stanza intensifies the theme: "An aged man is but a paltry thing, / A tattered coat upon a stick" (047-009–010). The image is devastating in its reduction — the body as mere clothing, the soul as a scarecrow. The only remedy is art: the soul must "clap its hands and sing, and louder sing" (047-011), studying "monuments of its own magnificence" (047-010). The aged man must become his own artist, singing himself into permanence.

The poem's famous conclusion — the wish to be gathered "into the artifice of eternity" (047-024), transformed into a golden bird "set upon a golden bough to sing" (047-030) — is the ultimate Yeatsian response to old age: not acceptance but transmutation, the rejection of the mortal body for the immortal work of art.

## Origins and Sources

- **Primary source**: Personal experience of aging (Yeats wrote his greatest poems in his fifties and sixties)
- **Key texts**: "Sailing to Byzantium," "The Tower," "Among School Children," "The Spur"

The Yeatsian treatment of old age draws from Renaissance *contemptus mundi*
tradition and from Swift — another aging Irish writer whose late work Yeats
admired. Unlike the consolatory tradition, Yeats refuses resignation: old age,
for him, is a battleground where spirit wars against flesh.

## "The Tower": Old Age as Absurdity

"The Tower" (1928) is Yeats's most sustained confrontation with his own aging. The poem opens with the speaker asking "What shall I do with this absurdity — / O heart, O troubled heart — this caricature, / Decrepit age that has been tied to me / As to a dog's tail?" (047-033–036). The image of age as something "tied to" the self — extrinsic yet inescapable — captures the split between the aging body and the ageless consciousness within.

The poem's response to this "absurdity" is characteristically Yeatsian: not resignation but memory and imagination. The speaker summons the dead from the tower's history, recounts the legends of the countryside, and finally wills his own testament — bequeathing his "faith and pride" to "young upstanding men" (047-187). The tower itself — an ancient Norman structure that Yeats restored — becomes the poem's architectural metaphor for the aged self: ruined, fortified, still standing, still inhabited by the living spirit.

## The Late Rage

Yeats's late poems intensify the theme of old age into outright fury. "The Spur" (1938), one of his last poems, asks: "You think it horrible that lust and rage / Should dance attendance upon my old age; / They were not such a plague when I was young; / What else have I to spur me into song?" The question is rhetorical but devastating: old age, far from extinguishing desire, concentrates it, giving the poet his final and most powerful spur to creation.

This late rage also appears in the figure of the "old lecher with a love on every wind" (047-137) — a self-portrait from "A Prayer for Old Age," where Yeats prays that God will "make me passionate until I die." Old age for the late Yeats is not the end of passion but its purification — desire stripped of its object, burning as pure creative energy.

## Development Across Yeats's Career

In "The Wanderings of Oisin," old age appears through the hero's return: Oisin,
who spent three hundred years in eternal youth in the Land of Youth, returns
to Ireland and instantly ages into a broken old man — "with coughing and broken
with old age and pain" (015-818). The early Yeats frames this as pathos.

The middle period introduces old age more personally. "When You Are Old" (1893)
imagines Maud Gonne grown "old and grey and full of sleep," though the speaker
himself is still young (015-783). By "A Prayer for My Daughter" (1919), the
speaker stands in middle age contemplating his infant daughter's future —
a different register of time's passage.

The late period makes old age the primary subject. "The Tower" (1928) opens
with the speaker asking "What shall I do with this absurdity?" — confronting
his aging body directly (047-132). "Among School Children" places the
sixty-year-old poet among children, measuring the distance between youth
and age. In "A Woman Young and Old," Yeats juxtaposes "in her old age, in her
young age" — the two poles of a woman's life (039-010).
