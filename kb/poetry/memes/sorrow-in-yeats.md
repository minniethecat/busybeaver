# Sorrow in Yeats

Sorrow in Yeats's poetry is neither mere melancholy nor personal complaint; it is
an existential condition tied to human mortality, lost love, and the passage of
time — a fundamental note in his emotional scale that he repeatedly transmutes
into art.

## Definition

For Yeats, sorrow is the price of consciousness. Unlike joy — which he sometimes
treats as divine or superhuman — sorrow is the distinctively human inheritance
of those who know transience and loss. The speaker of "The Wanderings of Oisin"
bears "the ancient sorrow of men" as a defining burden [015-365]. Sorrow is not
to be escaped but transformed: Yeats's poetics consistently turns personal grief
into impersonal, crafted lament.

## Origins and Sources

- **Primary source**: Personal experience (Maud Gonne's rejection, aging, political violence)
- **Key texts**: "The Sorrow of Love," "When You Are Old," "Easter 1916"

The sorrow in Yeats's verse draws from Romantic antecedents — Keats's melancholy,
Shelley's elegiac mode — but is refined through Yeats's own doctrine of the mask
(*Per Amica Silentia Lunae*), which holds that the poet remakes personal suffering
into something impersonal and enduring. Celtic tradition also contributes: the
*sorrowful gaze* of Deirdre-like figures recurs throughout the early work [025-003].

## Development Across Yeats's Career

Early Yeats treats sorrow through mythological and dreamlike displacement: "A
sorrow wrapped each merry face" in "The Ballad of Father O'Hart" [015-225],
distancing grief through folk narrative. In "Fergus and the Druid," the king
casts away his crown specifically "to cast away my sorrow" — and the druid warns
him of "how great webs of sorrow" lie hidden in the visionary life [031-043]
[031-070].

Middle Yeats begins to ground sorrow in historical and erotic loss: "No Second
Troy" transforms unrequited love into political allegory. The late poetry
achieves what Yeats called "tragic joy" — sorrow absorbed into formal mastery
and hard-won acceptance [028-047]. "Lapis Lazuli" makes this explicit: "All
perform their tragic play, / There struts Hamlet, there is Lear" — sorrow
accepted, not resisted.

## Sorrow and the Doctrine of the Mask

Yeats's theory of the mask, articulated in *Per Amica Silentia Lunae* (1918),
provides the philosophical framework for understanding sorrow in his mature work.
The mask doctrine holds that the poet creates an artificial self — a "anti-self"
or "daemon" — that is the opposite of his natural character. Sorrow, in this
scheme, is the raw material that the mask transmutes: personal grief is reformed
into impersonal, crafted lament. When Yeats writes of Maud Gonne's rejection in
"No Second Troy," he does not weep; he recasts her as a tragic heroine — Helen,
Deirdre — whose very refusal becomes the subject of tragic art. The sorrow does not
disappear; it is elevated into a public, ceremonial form. This transformation
of private pain into public art is perhaps Yeats's most enduring poetic
achievement.

## Sorrow and Gender in Yeats's Poetry

A distinct strain in Yeats's treatment of sorrow concerns gender. Women in Yeats's
poems — Maud Gonne, Iseult Gonne, Crazy Jane, Deirdre, the "pale, lamenting
women" of the early verse — frequently embody sorrow in ways that male figures do
not. The male hero (Cuchulain, Fergus) meets sorrow through action and death; the
female figure lives inside it. "Crazy Jane Talks with the Bishop" reverses this
in a late, defiant key: Jane's sorrow is transmuted not into art but into bodily
wisdom — "Love has pitched his mansion in / The place of excrement." This late
turn suggests a revision of the mask doctrine itself: sorrow may be absorbed
not only through aesthetic transformation but through the sheer, stubborn
persistence of mortal life.

## The Sorrow of Love and Its Revisions

"The Sorrow of Love" is a revealing case study in Yeats's evolving treatment of
sorrow. First published in 1893, the poem was radically revised in 1925. The
original was lush, decorative, Pre-Raphaelite in manner; the revision strips
away ornament to leave a harder, more compressed statement. The poem enacts
the very transformation it describes: the youthful sorrow of erotic
disappointment becomes, through revision itself, the mature sorrow of
existential recognition. This self-revision exemplifies Yeats's lifelong
practice of treating his own earlier work as raw material for later
refinement — a kind of poetic archaeology in which sorrow is excavated,
re-examined, and recast.
