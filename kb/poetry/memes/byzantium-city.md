# Byzantium

Byzantium in Yeats's late poetry is a symbolic city — not the historical Constantinople (though that provides the name) but an imagined realm where art defeats nature, where "the monuments of unageing intellect" ((047-025)) stand against the flux of biological existence. It appears most famously in two poems, "Sailing to Byzantium" (1928) and "Byzantium" (1930), which together form Yeats's most concentrated meditation on the relationship between art, aging, and eternity.

## Location and Geography

- **Type**: symbolic / imagined
- **Historical referent**: Constantinople (modern Istanbul), capital of the Eastern Roman (Byzantine) Empire
- **Yeats's Byzantium**: A city of gold mosaic, marble pavements, and mechanical birds — a place where "the sages standing in God's holy fire" have become "the singing-masters of my soul"

## Significance to Yeats

Yeats's Byzantium is constructed out of his reading of Byzantine art history — particularly the mosaics of Hagia Sophia and the accounts of the Imperial court. He imagined Byzantium as a moment in history (c. 550–950 CE) when "religious, aesthetic, and practical life were one," when the artist and the craftsman were indistinguishable, and when the work of art could achieve a perfection that transcended the individual maker.

The two Byzantium poems dramatize the aging poet's desire to escape the "dying generations" of the natural world — "fish, flesh, or fowl" — and transform himself into something permanent: a golden bird "set upon a golden bough to sing / To lords and ladies of Byzantium / Of what is past, or passing, or to come." This is the ultimate Yeatsian fantasy: not to die but to become a work of art.

The city is also the site of purgatorial fire — the "holy fire" of the golden smithies, where the soul is burned free of its biological attachments. Yeats's Byzantium is a place of terrible beauty, where the price of eternity is the loss of the body, of desire, of life itself.

## Literary and Historical Context

Yeats never visited Istanbul. His Byzantium is an entirely bookish creation, drawn from reading W. G. Holmes's _The Age of Justinian and Theodora_, the mosaics reproduced in art histories, and his own philosophical system. The historical inaccuracy (the golden mechanical birds are Yeats's invention, not Byzantine artifacts) is beside the point — Byzantium is a symbol, not a travelogue.

The city also functions as a historical phase in Yeats's gyres: the Byzantine era (roughly 500–1000 CE) represents a moment of unity before the Western gyre fragmented into the chaotic multiplicity of the modern world.
