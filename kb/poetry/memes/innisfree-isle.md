# Innisfree

Innisfree (Irish: *Inis Fraoigh*, "Island of Heather") is a small island in Lough Gill, County Sligo. In Yeats's poetry, it is transformed from a geographical location into one of the most resonant symbols in modern English verse — a place of peace and authenticity, conjured from the "pavements grey" of London as an act of imaginative recovery. "The Lake Isle of Innisfree," first published in 1890 and collected in *The Rose* (1893), remains Yeats's most widely anthologised poem, a deceptively simple lyric that distills the longing for a life close to nature into twelve lines of musical verse.

## Introduction

The poem's origin story is itself a parable of Yeats's method. As he recalled in *Autobiographies*, he was walking down the Strand in London when the sound of water from a shop-window fountain brought back the memory of Lough Gill and the island of Innisfree, which he had seen as a boy from the Sligo shore. The poem was composed in a single burst of homesickness — "the great desire of my childhood," as Yeats later described it — and its power comes from this fusion of precise sensory memory and symbolist technique.

"And I shall have some peace there, for peace comes dropping slow" — the line's hypnotic rhythm enacts what it describes, the long vowels and falling cadence embodying the "slow" peace of the imagined island. The poem's genius is its specificity: "nine bean-rows," "a hive for the honey-bee," "the lake water lapping with low sounds by the shore." These are not abstract pastoral conventions but precise details drawn from the landscape of Yeats's childhood, rendered with a concreteness that makes the invisible island visible.

> "I will arise and go now, and go to Innisfree, / And a small cabin build there, of clay and wattles made" [031-247]

## Significance to Yeats

### The Sligo Connection

Innisfree belongs to the Sligo landscape that Yeats considered his true home. His mother's family, the Pollexfens, were Sligo merchants, and the young Yeats spent long summers there, exploring the shores of Lough Gill, listening to the stories of the local people, and absorbing the landscape that would furnish his poetic imagination for a lifetime. Innisfree was visible from the road between Sligo town and the Pollexfen home — a constant presence on the horizon of his childhood.

The island's transformation into a poetic symbol happened, paradoxically, through distance. Living in London in the late 1880s, Yeats found that the Sligo landscape returned to him with hallucinatory vividness. The fountain in the Strand shop window was the trigger, but the imaginative work had been done years before, in the slow accumulation of sense impressions that the poet's memory had preserved and intensified.

### Pastoral Symbolism

Innisfree represents a specific mode of pastoral desire in Yeats's work: not the elegant artifice of classical pastoral but what might be called the "Irish pastoral" — a vision of self-sufficiency rooted in actual Irish rural practice. The "clay and wattles" cabin, the "nine bean-rows," the "hive for the honey-bee" are not decorative fancies but the real components of a smallholding, scaled to a single life.

The poem's relationship to Thoreau's *Walden* (which Yeats had read) is instructive. Like Thoreau, Yeats imagines a deliberate simplification — a stripping away of urban complexity to recover an authentic relationship with the natural world. But where Thoreau's experiment was actual, Yeats's Innisfree is purely imaginative: he never built the cabin, never planted the beans. The island exists for him — and for the reader — as a possibility held in abeyance, made more powerful by its non-realisation.

> "And I shall have some peace there, for peace comes dropping slow, / Dropping from the veils of the morning to where the cricket sings" [031-247]

### The Musical Achievement

Yeats himself acknowledged the poem's unusual musical quality. He said he had written it "in the rhythm of the wind in the reeds" — an acknowledgment that its hypnotic power owes as much to sound as to sense. The hexameter line (unusual in Yeats) creates a space of unhurried contemplation; the internal rhymes ("day" / "clay," "slow" / "glow") bind the lines into a sonic unity that mimics the peace it describes. When Yeats later recited the poem for BBC radio, listeners commented on its "incantatory" quality — a word that captures its origin in an almost ritual act of imaginative conjuring.

## Literary and Historical Context

Innisfree participates in a long tradition of island-as-utopia in Western literature — from Homer's Ogygia and the Isles of the Blessed in Celtic myth to Thomas More's *Utopia* and Thoreau's *Walden*. But Yeats makes the tradition specifically Irish: the island is not a dream-country but a real place, visible from the Sligo shore, accessible by rowboat, marked on maps. The poem's enduring power comes from this tension — between the mundane reality of a five-acre island in a Connacht lake and the immense symbolic weight it is made to carry.

The poem also belongs to the tradition of the Irish *aisling* (dream-vision), in which a figure representing Ireland appears to the poet in a vision. Innisfree, in this reading, is not just a personal refuge but an image of Ireland itself — not the Ireland of political struggle but an Ireland of the imagination, existing outside history, waiting to be reclaimed through poetry.
