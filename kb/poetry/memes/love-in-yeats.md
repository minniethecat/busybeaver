# Love in Yeats

Love in Yeats's poetry is never merely personal. It is simultaneously: (1) the driving passion of his life, focused on Maud Gonne; (2) a mystical force that transforms the lover, comparable to the alchemical transmutation of base matter into gold; (3) a national allegory, where love for a woman maps onto love for Ireland; and (4) a poetic engine — the "desperate gulf of love" ((015-070)) that generates some of the greatest love poetry in English.

## Origins and Sources

- **Maud Gonne**: The biographical center of Yeats's love poetry. He met her in 1889, proposed multiple times, and was refused. She married John MacBride in 1903. Her presence — as beloved, as unattainable ideal, as Ireland personified — pervades the poetry for over 50 years.
- **Courtly love tradition**: The medieval troubadour convention of the unattainable beloved, the lover ennobled by frustration — Yeats consciously adopted this frame
- **Rosicrucian / alchemical love**: Love as spiritual alchemy, transforming the lover through suffering and devotion
- **Irish nationalist poetry**: The _Róisín Dubh_ tradition, where love for a woman is love for Ireland

## Symbolic Meaning

1. **Love as transformation**: Love changes the lover — "My love, we will go, we will go, I and you" ((009-002)) — the journey of love is a journey of self-transformation through devotion to another.

2. **Love and mortality**: Love is shadowed by death — "death, oh my fair one, will never come" ((009-009)) — the knowledge of mortality gives love its intensity.

3. **Frustrated love as creative source**: Yeats's unrequited love for Gonne produced far more poetry than consummation ever could have. "Our love shall be of yesterday" ((005-014)) — love in memory, in imagination, is more potent than love realized.

4. **Love as folly**: In later poems, Yeats mocks his own romantic posture — "And laugh our love away" ((005-016)) — the aging poet sees the comedy in lifelong devotion to an impossible ideal.

## Development Across Yeats's Career

**Early period (1880s–1890s)**: Love is romantic, mythological, interwoven with fairy-lore and Celtic twilight. The beloved is an idealized figure, barely distinguishable from the Rose or Ireland.

**Middle period (1900–1917)**: Love complicates. Gonne's marriage to MacBride in 1903, the Easter Rising of 1916, and Yeats's own marriage to Georgie Hyde-Lees in 1917 force a reckoning — love is no longer simply the adoration of an ideal, but a negotiation with real, flawed, political human beings.

**Late period (1918–1939)**: Love matures into elegy and distance. "A Prayer for My Daughter" imagines love as protective, gentle, domestic — the opposite of the "desperate gulf" of the early work. The cold eye replaces the burning heart.
