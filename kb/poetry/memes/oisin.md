# Oisin (Usheen)

Legendary Irish poet-warrior of the Fenian Cycle, son of Finn mac Cumhaill, and the central figure of Yeats's longest early narrative poem *The Wanderings of Usheen* (1889). In Yeats's reimagining, Oisin is the aged survivor of the Fianna who recounts his three-century sojourn in the immortal lands with the fairy princess Niamh to St. Patrick — a dialogue that dramatises the conflict between pagan heroic Ireland and the arriving Christian order.

## Introduction

Oisin (pronounced "Uh-sheen," anglicised as Usheen in Yeats's text, also known as Ossian) is one of the most significant figures in Irish mythological poetry. In the Fenian Cycle of Irish mythology, he is the warrior-bard son of Finn mac Cumhaill, gifted with poetic eloquence equal to his martial prowess. Yeats made Oisin the protagonist of his first major work, *The Wanderings of Usheen*, published in 1889 when the poet was twenty-four. The poem, running to over eight hundred lines, adapts the traditional Ossianic dialogue between the last of the Fianna and the Christian saint into a symbolist meditation on youth, age, and the cost of immortal longing [015-005].

The poem opens with Oisin — "bent, and bald, and blind" [015-005] — enduring the preaching of St. Patrick, who dismisses the old warrior's memories as "heathen dreams" [015-035]. What follows is Oisin's extended narrative of how he left the mortal world with Niamh, daughter of Aengus, and passed three hundred years in the Land of Youth, only to return to an Ireland transformed and diminished by the passage of time.

## Key Facts

- **Born**: Unknown (legendary era, Fenian Cycle)
- **Died**: Legendary; in Yeats's telling, shortly after his return from Tir na nOg and baptism by St. Patrick
- **Role**: Warrior-poet of the Fianna; narrator of "The Wanderings of Usheen"
- **Connection to Yeats**: Protagonist of Yeats's first major published poem (1889); the figure through whom Yeats explored the tension between pagan heroic ideals and Christian Ireland

## Significance in Yeats's Work

Oisin occupies a foundational position in Yeats's poetic career. *The Wanderings of Usheen* was the title poem of Yeats's first collection, and its composition marked his deliberate turn toward Irish mythological subject matter — a choice he later described as essential to his artistic identity. The poem draws on the Ossianic lays of the Fenian Cycle, particularly the *Acallam na Senórach* (The Colloquy of the Ancients), but Yeats thoroughly reshapes the source material into a symbolist narrative of desire, loss, and otherworldly enchantment.

Oisin's tale is structured through three "islands" or phases of immortal experience — the Island of Dancing, the Island of Victories, and the Island of Sleep — each representing a different mode of timeless existence that ultimately proves hollow [015-126]. The Danaan realm offers endless pleasure but strips away mortal purpose; Oisin's longing for the comradeship of the Fianna and the struggles of the human world drives him to return to Ireland, where he immediately ages three centuries and finds the heroic age replaced by the "croziers" of the Christian church [015-410].

> "Sad to remember, sick with years, / The swift innumerable spears, / The horsemen with their floating hair, / And bowls of barley, honey, and wine, / And feet of maidens dancing in tune, / And the white body that lay by mine" [015-010]

For Yeats, Oisin embodies a central tension that runs through his entire body of work: the conflict between the dream of eternal beauty and the reality of mortal limitation. The aged poet-warrior, trapped between worlds, becomes a figure for the artist himself — caught between the "Danaan leisure" [015-096] of pure imagination and the demands of history, nation, and the body's inevitable decline. This theme would recur throughout Yeats's career, from the early Celtic Twilight poems through to the Byzantium poems of his maturity.

> "But the tale, though words be lighter than air, / Must live to be old like the wandering moon" [015-016]

## Further Context

### The Ossianic Yeats

Yeats encountered the Oisin material through multiple channels: the Gaelic originals translated by scholars of his circle, the Ossianic controversies of the eighteenth century (Macpherson's *Ossian*), and the broader Celtic Revival's recovery of medieval Irish literature. His Oisin differs significantly from the traditional figure — more self-consciously elegiac, more symbolically freighted — reflecting Yeats's ambition to make Irish mythology serve the purposes of fin-de-siècle symbolism.

The name "Usheen" reflects Yeats's phonetic rendering of the Irish *Oisín* (diminutive of *os*, "young deer"), a spelling he used consistently in the 1889 edition before later editions adopted the more standard "Oisin." The poem's first readers included William Morris and Oscar Wilde, both of whom recognised in the young Yeats's work an authentic new voice in English-language mythopoeic poetry.

### The Fianna Context

In the Fenian Cycle, Oisin is the son of Finn mac Cumhaill, leader of the Fianna — the warrior band that defended Ireland in the legendary past. His companions include Caolte, Conan, Oscar (his son), and the hounds Bran, Sgeolan, and Lomair [015-017]. The Fianna's heroic world — of hunting, feasting, and battle — provides the mortal counterpoint against which the Danaan realm's immortal pleasures are measured and found wanting.
