# Niamh in Yeats

Niamh (pronounced "Neev"), called Niamh of the Golden Hair, is the fairy queen of Tír na nÓg — the Land of Youth — in Irish mythology. She descends from the Otherworld on a white horse, falls in love with Oisin, son of Finn mac Cumhaill, and carries him away to three hundred years of immortal youth and pleasure. In W. B. Yeats's poetry, Niamh is the figure of the Otherworld's seductive call: the summons to abandon the mortal, the temporal, and the political for a realm of eternal beauty. She is, as Yeats writes in the Endnotes, the bearer of the invitation "over the sea with Niamh" (048-026) that transforms a heroic life into a visionary one.

## Niamh in "The Wanderings of Usheen"

Niamh's most extended appearance is in "The Wanderings of Usheen" (1889), Yeats's long narrative poem that adapts the Fenian material. Niamh descends to the mortal world and calls Oisin to the immortal islands:

> And Niamh calling "Away, come away:
> Empty your heart of its mortal dream" (038-004)

The call is irresistible. Niamh embodies the Otherworld itself — beautiful, unearthly, promising eternal youth and love. Yet the poem also registers what is lost in the transport: Oisin leaves behind his father Finn, his Fenian companions, and the mortal world of action and consequence. Niamh's gift is double-edged: immortal beauty that severs the hero from his humanity. "Young Niamh hover with love-lorn face" (038-310) captures her vulnerability — she is not merely the seductress but also, in her way, a figure of longing.

## Niamh and the Sea Symbolism

Niamh's most distinctive attribute in Yeats's symbolic vocabulary is her association with the sea. The Endnotes describe Oisin's journey with her as a crossing: "he is riding over the sea with Niamh" (048-026), and the vision that follows — "he sees amid the waters" — establishes the sea as the medium of visionary transport. Water in Yeats is the element of the unconscious, of dream, of dissolution and rebirth; to cross the sea with Niamh is to leave behind the solid ground of the rational and enter the fluid realm of vision.

This marine dimension distinguishes Niamh from the other fairy queens in Yeats's pantheon. Fand, Niamh's fellow fairy woman, is associated with the sea through her husband Manannán mac Lir, the Irish sea-god, but Niamh's sea-crossing is more fundamental: she herself is the vessel, the medium of transport, the figure who traverses the water and carries the mortal with her. "Over the sea with Niamh" (048-026) is not merely a journey but a transformation — the crossing is also the crossing-over from one mode of being to another.

## Niamh in "The Hosting of the Sidhe"

In "The Hosting of the Sidhe" — the incantatory poem that opens the *Wind Among the Reeds* section — Niamh's voice is the summoning cry that opens and closes the poem:

> And Niamh calling "Away, come away" (038-016)

The phrase appears twice in the poem, framing the vision of the fairy host riding from Knocknarea. Niamh's call — "Away, come away" — becomes the poem's refrain, a hypnotic summons to abandon the heavy, the mortal, the sorrowful for the light, the immortal, the joyous. The call is addressed to the reader as much as to Oisin: it is the poem's invitation to the visionary state.

## Niamh and the Tuatha Dé Danann

Niamh's mythological identity as a member of the Tuatha Dé Danann — the divine race of Irish mythology, also called the Sidhe — is central to her role in Yeats's symbolic system. The Endnotes describe the Sidhe as still riding "the country as of old" (048-013), an immortal presence that persists beneath the surface of modern Ireland. Niamh, as one of their number, belongs to this vanished divine order — simultaneously immortal and diminished, powerful and exiled.

This dual status mirrors the condition of Yeats's own poetic vocation. Niamh descends from the Otherworld to summon a mortal to her realm, but her realm is no longer supreme: it is the hidden Ireland, the underground kingdom, the "country of the young" that exists in parallel with — and in defiance of — the world of colonial modernity. The Endnotes record that "the old Gaelic literature is full of the appeals of the Tuatha De Danaan to mortals whom they would bring into their country" (048-021), with the song of Midher to Etain as "the type of all" (048-021). Niamh's call to Oisin belongs to this ancient pattern: the fairy summons that offers immortality at the cost of mortal belonging.

The Tuatha Dé Danann — "the ancient gods of pagan Ireland" — were, in Yeats's reading, not gods but divine beings who, after their defeat by the Milesians, retreated into the hills and became the fairy host. This dual status — divine but dispossessed — mirrors the condition of Yeats's own poetic vocation in the modern world. To answer Niamh's call is thus to ally oneself with the defeated but undestroyed order of the imagination: the kingdom that has been driven underground but has not ceased to exist. "And Niamh calling 'Away, come away'" (038-016) is, in this reading, not an escape from history but a summons to an alternative history — the history of Ireland's mythic consciousness that persists beneath and against the history of its colonisation.

## Symbolic Significance

In Yeats's symbolic vocabulary, Niamh represents the call of the imagination itself — the summons to abandon the "mortal dream" of ordinary life for the intensified reality of art. Her association with the sea ("over the sea with Niamh" (048-026)) links her to Yeats's water symbolism: the sea as the unconscious, the Otherworld, the realm of dream and vision. To go with Niamh is to cross the water into the visionary state — the state in which poetry is made.

## Niamh and the Fairy Queens

Niamh belongs to a constellation of fairy women in Yeats's poetry — figures who descend from the Otherworld to claim mortal lovers. She is kin to Fand and Laban: "Niamh and Laban and Fand, who could change to an otter or fawn" (041-147). Fand calls Cuchulain away from his mortal wife in "The Only Jealousy of Emer," and all three belong to the tradition of the Leanhaun Shee (fairy lover) of Irish folklore. These figures share a common structure: the fairy woman's love is transformative but costly, granting visionary access at the price of mortal belonging. Niamh is the archetype of this pattern: the first, the most beautiful, and the most ambivalent of Yeats's fairy brides.

The Endnotes amplify this reading by describing the visionary experiences that accompany the approach of the Sidhe to a mortal. The Tuatha Dé Danann "can take all shapes, and those that are in the waters take often the shape of fish" (048-023), and a Galway woman attests that "there are more of them in the sea than on the land" (048-023). Niamh's power thus extends beyond a single mythological figure to encompass the entire tradition of Otherworldly enchantment that Yeats drew upon.
