# Easter, 1916

"Easter, 1916" is one of W. B. Yeats's most important political poems, written in the months following the Easter Rising of April 1916 and first published in 1921 in the collection *Michael Robartes and the Dancer*. The poem elegises the executed leaders of the Rising — men and women Yeats had known personally — while meditating on the nature of political transformation, the cost of fanaticism, and the poet's own ambivalent relationship with Irish nationalism.

## Publication Details

- **Type**: lyric poem / political elegy
- **Written**: September 1916 (dated "September 25th, 1916" in manuscript)
- **First published**: 1921, in *Michael Robartes and the Dancer*
- **Subject**: the Easter Rising of April 1916 and its executed leaders
- **Form**: four stanzas of varying length, with a recurring refrain

## The Poem

The poem opens with Yeats recalling his casual encounters with the future revolutionaries — ordinary Dubliners he passed on the street, exchanged "polite meaningless words" with, and dismissed as inhabiting a world of "motley":

> I have met them at close of day<br/>
> Coming with vivid faces<br/>
> From counter or desk among grey<br/>
> Eighteenth-century houses. [046-164–167]

The first stanza establishes the distance between the poet and his subjects: these are people he knew but did not take seriously, figures from the mundane world of commerce and clerkship who inhabited a realm where "motley is worn" [046-177]. The refrain — "All changed, changed utterly: / A terrible beauty is born" [046-178–179] — is the poem's central paradox.

The second stanza names the individuals, moving from Constance Markievicz ("That woman") to Patrick Pearse ("This man had kept a school"), Thomas MacDonagh ("This other his helper and friend"), and finally John MacBride ("This other man I had dreamed / A drunken, vain-glorious lout") [046-180–195]. The portrait of MacBride — the man who had "done most bitter wrong / To some who are near my heart" [046-196–197] (a reference to MacBride's marriage to Maud Gonne) — is the poem's most personally painful passage. Yet Yeats includes him: "He, too, has been changed in his turn, / Transformed utterly" [046-201–202].

The third stanza meditates on the petrifying effect of single-minded devotion: "Hearts with one purpose alone / Through summer and winter seem / Enchanted to a stone / To trouble the living stream" [046-204–207]. The stone in the stream — fixed, unchanging, obstructing the flow of life — becomes the poem's central metaphor for revolutionary commitment. Yeats asks whether such sacrifice is ultimately life-denying: "Too long a sacrifice / Can make a stone of the heart" [046-220–221].

The final stanza transforms elegy into commemoration. Yeats names the dead — "MacDonagh and MacBride / And Connolly and Pearse" [046-238–239] — and declares that they "Now and in time to be, / Wherever green is worn, / Are changed, changed utterly: / A terrible beauty is born" [046-240–243]. The repetition of the refrain, now freighted with the full weight of the poem's meditation, achieves an effect of terrible finality.

## Historical Context

The Easter Rising began on 24 April 1916, when approximately 1,200 Irish republicans seized key buildings in Dublin and proclaimed an Irish Republic. After six days of fighting, the British army suppressed the Rising. Between 3 and 12 May, fifteen of the Rising's leaders were executed by firing squad at Kilmainham Gaol. The executions transformed public opinion in Ireland: men who had been dismissed as fanatics became martyrs, and the "terrible beauty" of Yeats's refrain captured this transformation with uncanny precision.

Yeats was in England when the Rising occurred. His initial reaction, recorded in letters to friends, was shock and ambivalence. He worried about the loss of life, about the fate of individuals he knew, and about the political implications for Home Rule. The poem, written months later, reflects a long and difficult process of coming to terms with what the Rising meant.

## Themes

- **Transformation through sacrifice**: The poem's central concern is how death transforms the meaning of a life — and how political martyrdom can "change utterly" both the dead and the living.
- **The poet and politics**: Yeats's ambivalence toward the revolutionaries he elegises reflects the tension between aesthetic distance and political engagement that runs through his work.
- **The stone and the stream**: The metaphor of the stone in the "living stream" encapsulates Yeats's fear that revolutionary commitment, however noble, is ultimately life-denying — a fixity that resists the natural flow of change.
