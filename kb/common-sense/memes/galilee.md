# Galilee

## Definition

Galilee is the northern region of ancient [[palestine|Palestine]], centered on the freshwater lake known as the Sea of Galilee (Lake Tiberias). In the [[testament|New Testament]], it is the primary theater of Jesus's ministry: the place of his childhood in [[nazareth|Nazareth]], the site of his first miracle at Cana, the base of operations at [[capernaum|Capernaum]], and the location of several post-resurrection [[phenomena|appearances]].

For Holbach, Galilee functions less as a geographical concept than as a textual test case. By tracing how the four [[missionaries|evangelists]] handle Galilee — where Jesus went, when, and why — he [[demon|demons]]trates that the [[gospel|gospels]] cannot be harmonized into a coherent itinerary. The contradictions are not minor discrepancies of memory but fundamental incompatibilities that reveal the [[gospel|gospels]] as independent human compositions rather than coordinated [[divinity|divine]] testimony.

The geographical contradictions surrounding Galilee are, in Holbach's presentation, one of the clearest [[proof|demonstration]]s that the [[testament|New Testament]] fails the most basic test of historical reliability. If the [[missionaries|evangelists]] could not agree on where Jesus was, they cannot be trusted on what he said or what he meant.

## Historical Context

Galilee in the first century CE was a region of mixed population — Jewish, Gentile, and [[hell|Hell]]enistic — viewed with some suspicion by the religious establishment in [[jerusalem|Jerusalem]]. The phrase "Galilee of the Gentiles" ([[matthew|Matthew]] 4:15) reflects this perception. It was an agricultural and fishing region, relatively distant from the [[temple|Temple]]-centered Judaism of [[judea|Judea]].

By Holbach's era, biblical geography had become a field of scholarly inquiry. Travelers' accounts, archaeological discoveries, and improved cartography allowed scholars to test the [[testament|New Testament]]'s geographical claims against physical reality. Richard Simon, the father of biblical criticism, had already noted discrepancies in the [[gospel|gospels]]' geographical frameworks. Holbach extends this critical tradition by treating the geographical contradictions not as puzzles to be solved through harmonization but as [[proof|evidence]] of the texts' human origins.

The specific contradictions surrounding Galilee — where Jesus went after his baptism, where he first preached, where the post-resurrection [[phenomena|appearances]] occurred — had been noted by earlier skeptics, but Holbach brings them together in a sustained argument that makes the cumulative case impossible to dismiss.

## Core Arguments

**1. The [[gospel|gospels]] contradict each other on Jesus's movements in and around Galilee.**
[[matthew|Matthew]] reports that after his temptation in the [[merit|desert]], Jesus "returned to Galilee, and leaving the city of [[nazareth|Nazareth]], He went to live at [[capernaum|Capernaum]], a maritime city." [[luke|Luke]], however, says "He came at first to [[nazareth|Nazareth]], and afterward went to [[capernaum|Capernaum]]." John reports that Jesus departed for Galilee two days after his baptism and performed his first miracle at Cana. These cannot all be accurate descriptions of the same sequence of [[eve]]nts.

**2. The calling of the first disciples in Galilee is inconsistently narrated.**
The Synoptics report that Jesus, wal[[monarch|king]] along the shore of the Sea of Galilee, saw Simon and Andrew fishing and called them to follow. John reports instead that it was Andrew who first followed Jesus, having been directed to him by John the Baptist — an entirely different location and mechanism for the first discipleship.

**3. The post-resurrection [[phenomena|appearances]] in Galilee are contradictory.**
[[matthew|Matthew]] places the climactic post-resurrection appearance on "a mountain in Galilee" where Jesus had directed the disciples to meet him. [[mark|Mark]] mentions three [[phenomena|appearances]] but does not specify Galilee. [[luke|Luke]] places all [[phenomena|appearances]] in and around [[jerusalem|Jerusalem]]. John alternates between [[jerusalem|Jerusalem]] and the Sea of Galilee. The disagreement about whether the resurrected Jesus appeared in Galilee or [[jerusalem|Jerusalem]] — and to whom and in what order — is not a peripheral detail but a central narrative claim.

**4. The contradictions reveal editorial agendas.**
Each evangelist, Holbach implies, shaped his Galilean geography to serve theological purposes. [[matthew|Matthew]], writing for a Jewish audience, emphasizes Jesus's fulfillment of prophecy in Galilee. [[luke|Luke]], with a more universalist perspective, orients the narrative toward [[jerusalem|Jerusalem]]. John develops an independent tradition. The geographical contradictions are not random errors; they are [[proof|evidence]] that each gospel writer was constructing a narrative, not recording a shared reality.

## In the Text

On the contradictory accounts of Jesus's first retreat:

> "Matthew says that He returned to Galilee, and that leaving the city of Nazareth, He went to live at Capernaum, a maritime city; and Luke says, that He came at first to Nazareth, and afterward went to Capernaum." (ch. on Contradictions)

On the calling of the first disciples by the Sea of Galilee:

> "They contradict each other in regard to the time and manner in which the apostles followed Him; for the first three say that Jesus, passing on the shore of the Sea of Galilee, saw Simon and Andrew his brother." (ch. on Contradictions)

On the women who followed Jesus from Galilee:

> "They contradict each other, also, in regard to the women who followed Jesus from Galilee, for the first three Evangelists say that these women... were looking on at a distance when He was hanged and nailed upon the cross." (ch. on Contradictions)
