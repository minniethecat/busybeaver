# Temperament

## Definition

In Holbach's materialist anthropology, "temperament" denotes the innate physical and psychological constitution that [[universe|nature]] bestows upon each individual at birth — the determinate organization of body and brain from which all passions, [[passion|desire]]s, incli[[nation|nations]], and capacities necessarily arise. It is not a contingent preference or a stylistic peculiarity but the material substrate of character itself. Together with the modifying influence of education, habit, and circumstance, temperament constitutes the complete causal field within which [[eve]]ry human action is determined.

Holbach's use of the term is rigorously anti-[[soul|spirit]]ualist. Temperament is not a property of an immaterial soul but an attribute of the organized physical body: the particular arrangement of fibers, fluids, and nervous sensibility that distinguishes one person from another. "His passions and his [[passion|desire]]s are the necessary results of the temperament which [[universe|nature]] has given him, and of the ideas with which he has been inspired" (ch. 80). There is no "person" behind the temperament who could choose to be otherwise constituted; the temperament is the person, considered as a material cause of action. To be "master of" one's temperament is as impossible as to be master of one's birth — because one does not choose to be born, and one does not choose the constitution with which one is born.

The term therefore functions as a conceptual hinge between Holbach's metaphysics and his [[morality|ethics]]. Metaphysically, temperament is the mechanism through which universal necessity operates at the level of individual psychology. Ethically, it is the fact that refutes [[liberty|free will]], disqualifies theological morality, and explains why religion fails to restrain conduct. In [[eve]]ry domain it touches, temperament asserts the priority of [[universe|nature]] over will, of constitution over choice, of the given over the chosen.

## Historical Context

The concept of temperament has a long prehistory in Western medicine and philosophy. The Hippocratic-Galenic tradition classified human constitutions into four temperaments — sanguine, choleric, melancholic, and phlegmatic — corresponding to the predominance of blood, yellow bile, black bile, or phlegm. This humoral theory, though physiologically obsolete by modern [[model|standard]]s, embedded the crucial materialist insight that character differences have bodily causes. The tradition persisted through the medieval and early modern periods, influencing [[rene-descartes|Descartes]]' *[[passion|Passion]]s of the [[soul|Soul]]* (1649) and the medical literature of the Enlightenment.

What distinguishes Holbach's treatment is his refusal to treat temperament as merely a predisposing factor that a [[liberty|free will]] can overcome. In the [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian anthropology that dominated eighteenth-century moral discourse, temperament was real but secondary: the immaterial soul, endowed with [[liberty|free will]], could resist bodily incli[[nation|nations]] and choose [[morality|virtue]]. [[grace|Grace]] fortified the will against a recalcitrant temperament. Holbach sweeps this entire apparatus aside. Temperament is not one factor among many; it is the primary determinant, and the will — far from [[existence|being]] a free counterforce — is simply temperament modified by experience and motive.

Holbach's position radicalizes a tendency already present in [[john-locke|Locke]] and Condillac. [[john-locke|Locke]]'s *Essay Concerning Human [[mind|Understanding]]* (1689) had argued that the will is determined by uneasiness, not by free indifference, but [[john-locke|Locke]] retained a role for the mind's power to suspend action and examine alternatives. Condillac's *Traité des sensations* (1754) went further, deriving all mental operations from sensation alone, but stopped short of the thoroughgoing [[effect|determinism]] that Holbach would embrace. Holbach's innovation was to close the remaining gap: if all mental states are [[mode|modification]]s of the organized body, and the body operates according to necessary laws, then the will — temperament in action — can be nothing other than determined.

## Core Arguments

**1. Temperament is the necessary product of [[universe|nature]] and the source of all passions.** Human [[existence|being]]s do not choose their [[passion|desire]]s; they experience them as given. "He is not the master of the imagi[[nations|nation]] or the temperament which dominates him; from which it follows, evidently, that man is not the master of the wishes and [[passion|desire]]s which rise in his soul, independently of him" (ch. 80). The passions that appear to arise spontaneously from the self are in fact the wor[[monarch|king]]-out of a constitution one never chose. What one loves, fears, hopes for, and strives toward are all functions of an inherited and unalterable temperament, modified by a history of impressions one did not freely select.

**2. Temperament refutes the doctrine of [[liberty|free will]].** If [[eve]]ry action is the necessary product of temperament and motive, the idea of a will that could have chosen otherwise under identical circumstances is unintelligible. Identical causes produce identical effects; the same temperament, facing the same motives in the same circumstances, will always produce the same action. The subjective experience of free choice is an illusion generated by ignorance of causes. "Thrust into [[earth|the world]] without their wish, provided with a temperament of which they are not the masters, [[soul|anima]]ted by passions and [[passion|desire]]s inherent in their [[universe|nature]]…" (ch. 92) — this, for Holbach, is the real human condition, and the theological claim that we freely choose our [[vice|sin]]s and our salvation is a fiction imposed upon it.

**3. [[theology-index|Divine]] [[attribute|attributes]] are incoherent because [[god|God]] has no temperament.** Human [[morality|virtue]]s — goodness, justice, mercy — are not abstract essences but the results of a particular temperament modified by social life. "Our [[morality|virtue]]s and our perfections are the results of our temperament modified. Has [[god|God]] a temperament like ours? Our good qualities are our habits relative to the [[existence|being]]s in whose society we live. [[god|God]], according to you, is a solitary [[existence|being]]" (ch. 48).

A [[existence|being]] without a body, without passions, without society, cannot possess the qualities that we call [[morality|virtue]]s — because those qualities are relational properties of embodied, social, temperamentally-constituted [[existence|being]]s. Theological language about [[goodness|divine goodness]] is therefore empty: it projects human moral categories onto a [[existence|being]] to which they cannot apply.

**4. Temperament undermines religious morality.** [[sin|Sin]]ce each person "arranges, modifies, and [[suffering|pain]]ts Him agreeably to his taste" — that is, according to his own temperament (ch. 32) — the [[god|God]] whom religious morality is supposed to ground is not a stable foundation but a projection of individual constitution. "To found morality upon a [[god|God]] … whom [[eve]]rybody arranges according to his own temperament and his own interest, is evidently founding morality upon the caprice and upon the imagi[[nations|nation]] of men" (ch. 168). Religious morality is therefore doubly bankrupt: its supposed [[divinity|divine]] source is a projection of human temperament, and its restraining power extends only to those whose temperament already disposes them to [[morality|virtue]].

**5. [[opinion|Opinion]]s follow temperament, not the reverse.** In one of his most psychologically acute observations, Holbach notes that "their opinions do not influence their conduct, except when they conform to their temperament, their passions, and to their interests" (ch. 181).

[[mind|Intellect]]ual convictions are not independent forces that govern behavior; they are adopted, retained, and deployed only when they harmonize with the deeper gravitational pull of temperament. This explains the rarity of consistent conduct, the ease with which believers ignore their own professed principles, and the impotence of religious doctrine to modify behavior except where the underlying temperament is already disposed that way.

**6. Temperament is the mechanism of religious [[persecution|intolerance]].** Hostility between [[sect|sects]] is not primarily a doctrinal phenomenon but a temperamental one. "There is no religious person who, according to his temperament, does not hate, despise, or pity the adherents of a sect different from his own" (ch. 156).

The particular attitude — hate, contempt, or paternalistic pity — varies with individual constitution, but the underlying disposition toward [[persecution|intolerance]] is universal. This is because religion does not suppress temperament; it channels it, and a [[doctrine|dogma]]tic, aggressive, or fearful temperament will produce a correspondingly [[doctrine|dogma]]tic, aggressive, or fearful religiosity.

## In the Text

The fullest statement of Holbach's thesis on temperament as the negation of [[liberty|free will]] appears in chapter LXXX:

> "His passions and his desires are the necessary results of the temperament which nature has given him, and of the ideas with which he has been inspired. He is not the master of the imagination or the temperament which dominates him; from which it follows, evidently, that man is not the master of the wishes and desires which rise in his soul, independently of him." (ch. 80)

The existential consequence — that human [[existence|being]]s are thrust into existence with a constitution they never chose — is drawn with stark clarity in chapter XCII:

> "Thrust into the world without their wish, provided with a temperament of which they are not the masters, animated by passions and desires inherent in their nature…"
>
> (092-002)

The theological implication — that [[attribute|divine attributes]] are projections of human temperament — is stated with surgical precision in chapter XLVIII:

> "Our virtues and our perfections are the results of our temperament modified. Has God a temperament like ours? Our good qualities are our habits relative to the beings in whose society we live. God, according to you, is a solitary being." (ch. 48)

The practical f[[utility]] of religious restraint is captured in the double-edged observation of chapter CLXVI:

> "Religion is a restraint but for those whose temperament or circumstances have already subjected them to reason. The fear of God does not prevent any from committing sin but those who do not wish to sin very much." (ch. 166)

The irreducibility of individual temperament to shared doctrine is noted in chapter XXXII:

> "Each one receives from his parents and his instructors the God which they themselves have received from theirs; only, according to his own temperament, each one arranges, modifies, and paints Him agreeably to his taste." (ch. 32)
