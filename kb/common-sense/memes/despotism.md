# Despotism

## Definition

Despotism, in Holbach's political philosophy, is the exercise of absolute power unrestrained by law, reason, or the consent of the governed. Unlike legitimate government, which derives its authority from the common good, [[tyranny|despotism]] serves only the caprice of the [[monarch|ruler]]. Holbach's distinctive contribution is the argument that [[religion]] and [[tyranny|despotism]] are mutually reinforcing: religion supplies [[supernatural|the supernatural]] justification for arbitrary rule, while the despot supplies the coercive power that protects the [[church]].

The despot, in Holbach's view, is not merely a tyrant in the conventional sense — an [[injustice|evil]] man who abuses power. He is rather someone systematically deceived by [[priest]]s and flatterers into believing that his arbitrary will is [[divinity|divine]]ly sanctioned. "The despot is but an insane man who injures himself" (ch. 157), because by alienating his people he destroys the only foundation of genuine security.

## Historical Context

Holbach wrote in the age of [[europe|Europe]]an [[tyranny|absolutism]]. [[france|France]] under Louis XV exemplified the alliance of throne and altar that Holbach attacks: the [[monarch|monarchy]] claimed [[divine-right|divine right]], and the [[catholic-church|Catholic Church]] provided the ideological framework that made this claim plausible to the masses. The revocation of the [[edict-of-nantes|Edict of Nantes]] (1685) had shown how the alliance of religious and [[government|political authority]] could produce systematic [[persecution]].

[[enlightenment|The Enlightenment]] critique of [[tyranny|despotism]] was most famously articulated by Montesquieu in *The [[spirit|Spirit]] of the [[law|Law]]s* (1748), which distinguished [[monarch|monarchy]] from [[tyranny|despotism]] by the presence or absence of law. Rousseau's *Social Contract* (1762) argued that [[government|sovereignty]] resides in the general will, not in a [[guilt|sin]]gle [[monarch|ruler]]. Holbach extends this critique by focusing on the psychological mechanism — the way religion makes subjects complicit in their own subjugation by [[doctrine|teaching]] them that resistance to authority is resistance to [[god]].

## Core Arguments

**1. [[religion|Religion]] is the strongest support of [[tyranny|despotism]].** "[[christianity|Christianity]]... insinuated itself among the savage and free [[nation|nations]] of [[europe|Europe]] but by showing their chiefs that its principles would favor [[tyranny|despotism]] and place absolute power in their hands" (ch. 146). The doctrine of [[divine-right|divine right]] is not an abuse of [[christianity|Christianity]] but its natural political expression. (146-002)

**2. [[priest|Priest]]s are the systematic enemies of liberty.** "[[priest|Priest]]s in all times have shown themselves supporters of [[tyranny|despotism]], and the enemies of public liberty. Their profession requires vile and submissive slaves, who never have the audacity to [[reason]]" (ch. 157). A thin[[monarch|king]] populace is a threat to priestly power; therefore [[clergy|priests]] [[worship|cult]]ivate submission.

**3. The despot is self-destroying.** "Should not [[eve]]ry rational [[monarch|prince]] perceive that the despot is but an insane man who injures himself? [[will|Will]] not [[eve]]ry enlightened [[monarch|prince]] beware of his flatterers, whose object is to put him to sleep at the edge of the precipice to which they lead him?"
>
> (150-003). True power lies in the love of the people, which [[tyranny|despotism]] forfeits.

**4. [[religion|Religion]] teaches passivity in the face of [[persecution|oppression]].** [[christianity|Christianity]] "was established on the principle that the [[nation|nations]] should renounce the legitimate defense of themselves" (ch. 146). This makes [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian [[nation|nations]] "deprived of the first law of [[nature]], which decrees that man should resist [[injustice|evil]]."

## Relation to Other Concepts

Despotism is the political expression of theological [[authority]]: the same structure of unquestioning submission that religion demands toward [[god]], it also demands toward the [[monarch|ruler]]. [[Priest]]s and despots form an alliance of mutual convenience — the priest legitimates the despot, and the despot enforces the priest's monopoly on [[truth]]. [[Liberty]], by contrast, requires both political and intellectual emancipation: the people must throw off the yoke of both the tyrant and the [[superstition]] that sanctifies him. [[Tyranny]] in the political sphere mirrors [[dogma]] in the intellectual sphere.

## Reception and Critique

Holbach's analysis of [[tyranny|despotism]] was radical for its time but has been criticized for its neglect of institutional mechanisms. While he brilliantly exposes the ideological alliance between throne and altar, he says little about [[temperament|constitution]]al structures, separation of powers, or legal safeguards that might check arbitrary rule independently of [[belief|religious belief]]. Later liberal theorists — from Constant to Tocqueville — argued that [[tyranny|despotism]] could flourish in secular forms, as the totalitarian regimes of the twentieth century would [[demon|demons]]trate. Holbach's contribution remains valuable for its insistence that [[tyranny|despotism]] requires ideological legitimation, not merely force: the tyrant who cannot persuade must constantly coerce, and constant coercion is self-defeating. This insight anticipates Gramsci's concept of hegemony and the broader tradition of ideology critique.
