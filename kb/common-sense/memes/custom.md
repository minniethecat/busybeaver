# Custom

## Definition

In Holbach's analysis of the sources of [[error|religious error]], "[[habit|custom]]" occupies a central but often overlooked role. Custom is the inertial force of social life — the accumulated weight of inherited practices, beliefs, and institutions that shapes human conduct prior to and often in defiance of rational reflection. "The majority of men examine nothing; they allow themselves to be blindly led by [[habit|custom]] and authority; their religious opinions are specially those which they have the least courage and capacity to examine" (122-002). This formulation captures a key Enlightenment insight: error is not primarily a failure of individual reasoning but a social product, transmitted through mechanisms that systematically discourage the exercise of reason.

Holbach's treatment of [[habit|custom]] extends beyond religion to encompass all inherited social forms — laws, institutions, moral codes — that persist through habit rather than through [[demon|demons]]trated [[utility]]. The result is a society governed by "foolish [[habit|custom]]s" and "false opinions" (ch. LXVII) whose very familiarity shields them from scrutiny. Custom is, for Holbach, the epistemological enemy: it is what makes the arbitrary appear necessary and the irrational appear natural.

## Historical Context

The critique of [[habit|custom]] as an enemy of reason has deep roots in Enlightenment thought. [[montaigne|Montaigne]] had already observed the arbitrary power of *coutume* over human belief in the sixteenth century. [[rene-descartes|Descartes]]' method of systematic doubt was, in effect, a procedure for suspending [[habit|custom]] to reach indubitable foundations. By Holbach's era, the argument had become a staple of radical criticism: [[habit|custom]]s, like political institutions, must justify themselves before the tribunal of reason.

What distinguishes Holbach's treatment is its integration into a materialist sociology of religion. Custom is not merely an intellectual vice but a mechanism of social control. The "bad [[habit|custom]] established to prevent the poor from [[existence|being]] instructed and knowing the truth" (Preface, referring to the suppression of Meslier's manuscript) illustrates how [[habit|custom]] operates as an instrument of power. The alliance of [[habit|custom]] with authority — the twin pillars of unreason — ensures that [[error|religious error]] is reproduced across generations with minimal resistance.

## Core Arguments

1. **Custom substitutes habit for exami[[nations|nation]].** The majority of people "allow themselves to be blindly led by [[habit|custom]] and authority" (122-002). When asked to justify their [[belief|religious belief]]s, they can only appeal to the fact that they have always held them — a circular defense that reveals the absence of rational foundation.

2. **Custom conceals the social origins of error.** Human misery is "really due only to the negligence, the ignorance, and to the perversity of their guides, to the folly of their institutions, to their foolish [[habit|custom]]s, to their false opinions, to their unreasonable laws" (ch. LXVII). By attributing [[evil|suffering]] to [[divinity|divine]] will, [[habit|custom]] obscures its remediable social causes.

3. **Custom is stronger than religious conviction.** In practice, social conformity outweighs theological belief: "The [[passion|desire]] to please [[earth|the world]], the current of [[habit|custom]], the fear of [[existence|being]] ridiculed, and of '[[honor|what will they say]]?' have more power than all religious opinions" (139-002). A soldier will risk [[hell|damnation]] to avoid [[honor|dishonor]] — [[demon|demons]]trating that social [[habit|custom]], not religious doctrine, is the true governor of conduct.

4. **Custom perpetuates fear.** Religious fear is itself a [[habit|custom]] — "they have been so early accustomed to tremble [[eve]]n at the name of the [[deity|Deity]], that it has become for them a specter, a goblin, a were-wolf which [[torture|torment]]s them" (ch. CLXXVI). The [[passion|emotion]]al conditioning of childhood creates a lifelong disposition to [[terror|religious terror]] that reason struggles to overcome.

5. **Custom is contingent, not necessary.** "[[religion|Religion]] is a thing of [[habit|custom]] and fashion; we must do as others do" (142-001). The diversity of religious [[habit|custom]]s across [[nation|nations]] proves their arbitrariness: "The French would be to-day as good Mussulmen as they are good [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ians" had they been born under different circumstances. What is merely [[habit|custom]]ary can, in principle, be changed.

## Reception and Critique

Holbach's critique of [[habit|custom]] anticipated later sociological thought while remaining firmly within the normative framework of Enlightenment rationalism. His claim that [[habit|custom]] conceals the social origins of [[evil|suffering]] influenced nineteenth-century critiques of ideology, from Marx's concept of false consciousness to Durkheim's analysis of collective representations.

Critics have objected that Holbach's rationalism underestimates the functional value of [[habit|custom]]. Edmund Burke, writing a generation later, would defend [[habit|custom]] and [[opinion|prejudice]] as accumulated social [[philosophy|wisdom]], embodying the "general bank and capital of [[nation|nations]] and of ages." From this conservative perspective, Holbach's project of subjecting all [[habit|custom]]s to rational scrutiny is not liberation but social dissolution. More recent anthropology has tended to side with the functionalist insight — [[habit|custom]]s often serve purposes invisible to their practitioners — while acknowledging the force of Holbach's normative challenge: the mere fact that a [[habit|custom]] exists does not justify its perpetuation.

## Relation to Other Concepts

Custom is closely linked to [[Education]], which Holbach regards as the proper antidote — "let the mind be filled early with true ideas; let man's reason be [[worship|cult]]ivated; let justice govern him; and there will be no need of opposing to his passions the powerless barrier of the fear of [[god|God]]s" (ch. LXVII). It connects to [[Authority]], the other pillar of unexamined belief. Custom relates to [[Prejudice]], which is [[habit|custom]] internalized as individual disposition. It is the mechanism by which [[Superstition]] is transmitted across generations. Finally, [[habit|custom]] stands in opposition to [[Reason]], whose exercise requires the courage to question what "[[eve]]ryone" believes.

## In the Text

> "The majority of men examine nothing; they allow themselves to be blindly led by custom and authority; their religious opinions are specially those which they have the least courage and capacity to examine; as they do not understand anything about them, they are compelled to be silent or put an end to their reasoning." (122-002)

> "Religion is a thing of custom and fashion; we must do as others do. But, among the many religions in the world, which one ought we to choose? This examination would be too long and too painful; we must then hold to the faith of our fathers, to that of our country, or to that of the prince, who, possessing power, must be the best." (ch. CXLIII)

> "The desire to please the world, the current of custom, the fear of being ridiculed, and of 'what will they say?' have more power than all religious opinions. A warrior with the fear of dishonor, does he not hazard his life in battles every day, even at the risk of incurring eternal damnation?" (ch. CXLIII)
