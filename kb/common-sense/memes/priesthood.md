# Priesthood

## Definition

The **[[clergy|priesthood]]** is the institutional office of religious mediation — the organized system by which a designated class of persons claims exclusive authority to interpret [[divinity|divine]] will, administer sacred [[ritual|rites]], and control the conditions of salvation. In Holbach's analysis, the [[clergy|priesthood]] is not merely a collection of individual [[priest|clergy]] but a *structural apparatus*: a self-perpetuating institution whose power depends on maintaining the inaccessibility of the [[god|sovereign]] it claims to represent, and whose interests systematically diverge from those of the populations it governs.

## The Institution vs. the Individual

A key distinction in Holbach's analysis separates the **office** of [[clergy|priesthood]] from the **persons** who occupy it. Individual [[priest|clergy]] may be [[guilt|sin]]cere, well-intentioned, or [[eve]]n reform-minded — but the institution selects for conformity and self-preservation:

> "Pride and vanity always were and always will be the inherent vices of the priesthood. Is there anything that has a tendency to render men haughty and vain more than the assumption of exercising Heavenly power, of possessing a sacred character, of being the messengers of the Most High?" (002-402)

The structural critique is that the office *itself* corrupts. No individual [[morality|virtue]] can overcome an institutional logic that rewards mystification, punishes transparency, and depends on popular [[belief|credulity]] for its continued existence.

## The Sacerdotal Logic

Holbach identifies a three-part logic that sustains priestly power:

| Element | Function | Stability Mechanism |
|---------|----------|-------------------|
| Inaccessible [[monarch|sovereign]] | [[god|God]] is defined as incomprehensible | Only [[clergy|priests]] can interpret; laity permanently dependent |
| Exclusive mediation | [[priest|Priest]]s control [[ritual|rites]], [[scripture|texts]], salvation conditions | No alternative path to [[divinity|divine]] favor |
| Secular alliance | Priesthood aligns with [[monarch|crowns]] and aristocracies | Mutual protection against popular [[sovereignty|sovereignty]] |

This tripartite structure means the [[clergy|priesthood]] occupies a position of *double mediation*: upward, it claims to speak for [[god|God]] to humanity; downward, it claims to speak for humanity to [[god|God]]. Both directions are monopolized. No one else may perform either function — and the penalty for unauthorized mediation is, historically, [[death]] or [[exile]].

## Priestcraft as a System of Control

Chapter 153 of "Common Sense" is devoted entirely to "The Dominant [[passions|Passions]] and [[crime|Crime]]s of [[priestcraft|Priestcraft]]." Holbach's choice of the term *[[priest|priestcraft]]* — analogous to *witchcraft* — is deliberate. It frames priestly activity not as [[worship|divine service]] but as a *technique of manipulation*:

> "Ambition and avarice were at all times the dominating passions of the priesthood; everywhere the priest places himself above the sovereign and the laws; everywhere we see him occupied but with the interests of his pride, his cupidity, his despotic and vindictive mood." (002-316)

The [[clergy|priesthood]], in this view, is not a [[soul|spirit]]ual calling but a *craft* — a set of teachable techniques for extracting deference and material support from credulous populations. The "[[mysteries]]" of religion are trade secrets; initiation into the [[clergy|priesthood]] is apprenticeship in the arts of imposture.

## Political Supremacy

A recurring theme in Holbach's critique is the [[clergy|priesthood]]'s claim to stand *above* civil authority:

> "Never permitted to them to resist, the priesthood succeeded in perpetuating the misconduct of the kings and the misfortunes of the people; the interests of the nations have been cowardly sacrificed to their chiefs." (002-316)

The [[clergy|priesthood]] does not merely coexist with [[monarch|monarchy]] — it *enables* misrule by providing [[divinity|divine]] sanction for [[sovereign|sovereigns]] who would otherwise lack legitimacy. The doctrine of [[divine-right|divine right]] is, in Holbach's analysis, a priestly invention: [[monarch|king]]s receive their authority "from [[god|God]]" because [[clergy|priests]] have told them so, and in exchange, [[monarch|king]]s protect priestly privileges. The [[nation|people]] are the losers in this arrangement.
