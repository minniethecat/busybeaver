# choice

In Holbach's deterministic philosophy, **choice** — the idea that man freely chooses his actions — is an illusion. Man does not choose to be born, was not consulted about his existence, and is "not free in his choice" but "evidently compelled" by external causes. This denial of free will is central to his materialist worldview. (002-134)

## The Illusion of Free Choice

Holbach's argument against free choice rests on the materialist principle that every event is the necessary consequence of antecedent causes. Man "is not free in his choice; he is evidently compelled to will the choice he makes" by his constitution, his education, his habits, and the circumstances in which he finds himself (002-135; 080-003). The feeling of freedom is merely ignorance of the causes that determine our decisions. What appears as choice is in reality the necessary outcome of forces operating upon us from without and within.

## Divine Choice and Its Problems

Holbach turns the concept of choice against theology itself. If God exists, then His choice of agents and instruments for divine purposes is subject to the same scrutiny as any other decision. Holbach questions why one would not "accuse Him of blundering in His choice of agents and instruments" (002-056; 045-001), given the manifest imperfections of the prophets, apostles, and clergy who claim to speak for Him. The "vocation and the choice which God made of the Patriarchs" (002-497; APP002-002) exemplifies this problem — a perfect being would not select such manifestly imperfect instruments for the communication of divine truth.

## Determinism as Liberation

For Holbach, the recognition that free choice is an illusion is not cause for despair but for liberation. If human beings are determined by their constitution and circumstances, then improving those circumstances — through education, just laws, and rational social organization — is the true path to human flourishing. The theological insistence on free will serves only to justify punishment and to obscure the real, material causes of human suffering and misconduct.

## Selected Passage

Holbach argues that divine choice is no refuge from the problem of disorder — if God chose the agents and instruments of His will, He is accountable for their imperfections:

> Finally, if the order of nature proves the power and the intelligence, disorder ought to prove the weakness, inconstancy, and irrationality of Divinity. You say that God is everywhere; that He fills all space; that nothing was made without Him; that matter could not act without Him as its motor. But in this case you admit that your God is the author of disorder; that it is He who deranges nature; that He is the Father of confusion; that He is in man; and that He moves man at the moment when he sins.
>
> (002-056)
