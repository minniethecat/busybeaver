# Power

## Definition

In Holbach's *Common Sense*, power is not treated as an abstract philosophical category but as a concrete force operating through institutions — above all, through the alliance of church and state. Holbach identifies two forms of power that jointly dominate humanity: *[[soul|spirit]]ual power*, wielded by [[clergy|priests]] who claim authority over con[[knowledge|science]] and eternal destiny, and *temporal power*, exercised by monarchs and magistrates over bodies and property. The "double yoke of [[soul|spirit]]ual and temporal power" (ch. 10) is Holbach's metaphor for the structural [[persecution|oppression]] that keeps populations ignorant, fearful, and obedient. His analysis is distinctively materialist: power is not a [[divinity|divine]] right but a human arrangement, maintained by the manipulation of opinion and the deliberate suppression of reason.

## Historical Context

The 18th century witnessed an intense struggle between ecclesiastical and civil authority across [[europe|Europe]]. The Gallican tradition in [[paris|France]], [[joseph|Joseph]]inism in Austria, and the expulsion of the [[jesuits|Jesuits]] (suppressed by the Pope in 1773 under pressure from Catholic monarchs) all reflected the tension Holbach analyzed. Yet Holbach went further than most of his contemporaries: he did not merely [[desire|want]] to subordinate [[soul|spirit]]ual power to temporal authority, as many absolutist monarchs did, but to expose the very concept of "[[soul|spirit]]ual power" as a usurpation — a fiction maintained by [[clergy|priests]] for their own benefit. This placed him to the left of figures like [[voltaire|Voltaire]], who favored enlightened [[tyranny|despotism]], and aligned him with the radical materialist critique of all hierarchical authority.

## Core Arguments

Holbach advances several interconnected theses about power:

1. **The Double Yoke**: [[humanity|Humanity]] is "oppressed by the double yoke of [[soul|spirit]]ual and temporal power" (ch. 10). These two powers, though often in conflict, ultimately collaborate to keep the people in subjection. When [[monarch|king]]s and [[clergy|priests]] quarrel, it is "always at the expense of the people that peace was restored" (ch. 160).

2. **[[spirit|Spirit]]ual Power as Usurpation**: [[priest|Priest]]s claim a [[divinity|divine]] mandate that no human institution can legitimately possess. By asserting authority over con[[knowledge|science]] and the [[death|afterlife]], they create "a state within [[government|the state]]" — an imperium in imperio — that undermines civil cohesion and makes unified [[government|governance]] impossible.

3. **The [[corruption|Corruption]] of [[temporal|Temporal Power]]**: [[religion|Religion]] does not constrain tyrants; it empowers them. "Civil power in its battles against [[soul|spirit]]ual power, having at length gained the advantage, and the [[clergy|priests]] [[existence|being]] compelled to yield, recognized the [[theology-index|Divine]] right of [[monarch|king]]s" (ch. 160). The doctrine of [[divinity|divine]] right transforms [[government|political authority]] into sacred untouchability.

4. **The Double Legislation**: The union of religion with politics "has necessarily caused a double legislation in the [[politics-index|State]]s. The law of [[god|God]], interpreted by His [[clergy|priests]], is often contrary to the law of the [[monarch|sovereign]] or to the interest of the [[politics-index|State]]" (ch. 164). Citizens are thus caught between incompatible obligations.

5. **Power Maintained by [[ignorance|Ignorance]]**: The entire edifice of [[soul|spirit]]ual and temporal domi[[nations|nation]] rests on keeping the people from thin[[monarch|king]] for themselves. "Man was a pure machine in the hands of his tyrants and his [[clergy|priests]], who alone had the right to regulate his movements" (ch. 10). Enlightenment, therefore, is the antidote to [[persecution|oppression]].

## Reception and Critique

Holbach's analysis of power anticipated many themes of later political thought. His critique of ideology — the way belief-systems serve material interests — prefigures Marx. His analysis of the "double legislation" as a structural contradiction of confessional states anticipates modern secularism. Critics, however, have noted that Holbach's account of power is almost entirely negative: he excels at diagnosing [[persecution|oppression]] but offers little in the way of a constructive political program beyond the general diffusion of [[education|enlightenment]]. His [[religion|faith]] that reason alone can dissolve entrenched power structures may strike modern readers as naive, but it was characteristic of the most radical wing of the French Enlightenment.

## Relation to Other Concepts

Power inter[[sect|sects]] with multiple concepts in Holbach's system. [[tyranny]] is the degenerate form of temporal power unchecked by law or popular consent. [[authority]] is the broader category of legitimate and illegitimate command. [[liberty]] is the condition of [[liberty|freedom]] from the double yoke — the positive goal toward which the critique of power aims. [[priest]] designates the specific class that wields [[soul|spirit]]ual power for its own benefit. [[government]] is the institutional form of temporal power, which can be either just or [[despotism|despot]]ic. [[education]] is the means by which people can be freed from the power of opinion that keeps them servile.

## In the Text

Holbach's most sustained analysis of the power structure appears in chapter 10, where he traces the origins of the double yoke:

> "The human mind, perplexed by these theological opinions, misunderstood itself, doubted its own powers, mistrusted experience, feared truth, disdained its reason, and left it to blindly follow authority. Man was a pure machine in the hands of his tyrants and his priests, who alone had the right to regulate his movements. Always treated as a slave, he had at all times and in all places the vices and dispositions of a slave." (ch. 10)

The conflict between [[soul|spirit]]ual and temporal power, and their ultimate reconciliation at the people's expense, is analyzed in chapter 160:

> "Civil power in its battles against spiritual power, having at length gained the advantage, and the priests being compelled to yield, recognized the Divine right of kings and preached it to the people, reserving to themselves the right to change opinions and to preach revolution, every time that the divine rights of kings did not agree with the divine rights of the clergy." (ch. 160)

The structural problem of double legislation is examined in chapter 164:

> "The union of religion with politics has necessarily caused a double legislation in the States. The law of God, interpreted by His priests, is often contrary to the law of the sovereign or to the interest of the State." (ch. 164)
