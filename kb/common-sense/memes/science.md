# Science

## Definition

Holbach wrote at a moment when "[[knowledge|science]]" in the modern sense was still crystallizing. The term he would have used — *philosophie [[universe|nature]]lle* or simply *la [[knowledge|science]]* — encompassed what we now distinguish as natural [[knowledge|science]], philosophy, and systematic rational inquiry. For Holbach, [[knowledge|science]] is not merely one human activity among others but the paradigmatic form of genuine knowledge: the method of testing claims against [[proof|evidence]], revising conclusions in light of new observations, and refusing to accept propositions on authority alone.

This makes [[knowledge|science]] the structural opponent of [[Theology]]. [[theology|Theology]] claims to know on the basis of revelation; [[knowledge|science]] claims to know on the basis of [[proof|evidence]]. [[theology|Theology]] declares its conclusions infallible; [[knowledge|science]] holds its conclusions provisionally. [[theology|Theology]] demands submission; [[knowledge|science]] invites scrutiny. Holbach's entire project can be understood as the attempt to extend the scientific attitude from [[universe|nature]] to morality, politics, and religion itself.

## Historical Context

The 17th and 18th centuries witnessed a transformation in humanity's [[knowledge|understanding]] of [[nature|the natural world]]. Copernicus, Kepler, and Galileo had dismantled the geocentric [[universe|cosmos]]; [[isaac-newton|Newton]] had shown that the same laws governed the fall of an apple and the orbit of planets; Boyle and Lavoisier were founding modern chemistry. This "Scientific [[revolution|Revolution]]" was not merely an accumulation of discoveries but a methodological revolution: the systematic use of experiment, measurement, and mathematical description to understand [[universe|nature]].

Holbach was a direct participant in this revolution. His salon hosted many of the leading scientific minds of the age. His collaborator Denis Diderot edited the *Encyclopédie*, which aimed to collect all [[knowledge|useful knowledge]] in one work, organized not by theological categories but by the rational structure of human [[knowledge|understanding]]. Holbach himself translated German scientific works into French, including texts on chemistry and mineralogy.

## Core Arguments

**1. Science is the only reliable method of acquiring knowledge.** While [[religion|theology]] claims to know through revelation and tradition, [[knowledge|science]] knows through observation, experiment, and reason. "It is only by experience, by observation, by reason, that we can attain to the knowledge of truth" (ch. XI). Any claim that cannot be tested by these methods is not knowledge but conjecture.

**2. [[theology|Theology]] has historically suppressed [[knowledge|science]].** Holbach documents how "vigilant [[clergy|priests]] were always ready to extinguish systems which could not be made to tally with their interests" (ch. CCIII). Galileo's [[cruelty|persecution]] is the paradigmatic case, but Holbach argues that the [[model|pattern]] is structural: [[eve]]ry advance in scientific [[knowledge|understanding]] has been resisted by those whose authority depends on the old cosmology.

**3. Science serves human welfare; [[religion|theology]] obstructs it.** The practical benefits of [[knowledge|science]] — in medicine, agriculture, navigation, engineering — are evident and universal. [[theology|Theology]], by contrast, has produced "nothing but disputes, hatred, and wars" (ch. CCV). The choice between [[knowledge|science]] and [[religion|theology]] is thus not merely intellectual but practical and moral.

**4. The scientific attitude must be extended to morality and politics.** Holbach's ambition is not merely to defend [[knowledge|science]] against [[religion|theology]] but to apply scientific reasoning to human affairs. [[morality|Morality]] should be based on the study of human [[universe|nature]], not on [[divinity|divine]] commands; politics should be based on the study of social consequences, not on claims of [[divinity|divine]] right.

## Reception and Critique

Holbach's scientism — his conviction that [[knowledge|science]] could replace religion as the foundation of morality and [[society|social order]] — was rejected by many who shared his anticlericalism. [[voltaire|Voltaire]] thought religion necessary for the masses. Rousseau argued that [[knowledge|science]] and the arts had corrupted rather than improved human morals. [[david-hume|Hume]] had already shown the difficulty of deriving "ought" from "is" — a problem Holbach tended to glide over.

Yet Holbach's vision of [[knowledge|science]] as the paradigmatic form of knowledge has been largely vindicated. The natural [[knowledge|science]]s have indeed displaced [[religion|theology]] as the authoritative account of [[universe|nature]]; the scientific method is the gold [[model|standard]] of inquiry in [[eve]]ry field that aspires to knowledge. Whether [[knowledge|science]] can provide a complete foundation for [[morality|ethics]] and politics — Holbach's larger ambition — remains a live question.

## Relation to Other Concepts

Science is the methodological expression of [[Reason]] and the intellectual alternative to [[Theology]]. It depends on [[Evidence]], which [[religion|theology]] replaces with [[Revelation]]. Science is [[worship|cult]]ivated by [[Education]] and suppressed by [[Superstition]]. Its conclusions are always provisional, unlike the fixed [[dogma|dogmas]] of [[Doctrine]]. The scientific revolution that Holbach champions prepared the ground for [[Atheism]] by showing that [[universe|nature]] could be understood without appeal to supernatural causes. Science is allied with [[Philosophy]] in the broad Enlightenment project of replacing [[religion|faith]] with knowledge.
