# Passions

## Definition

In Holbach's materialist psychology, the **passions** are the necessary and irreducible [[desire|drive]]rs of human action. Every human act — virtuous or vicious, rational or irrational — proceeds from passion, understood as the felt urgency of [[passion|desire]] or aversion. This is not a moral condem[[nations|nation]] but a factual description of human [[universe|nature]]: "his passions and his [[passion|desire]]s are the necessary results of the temperament he has received from [[nature|Nature]]." The theological claim that religion restrains the passions is, for Holbach, empirically false — and necessarily so, because supernatural fears cannot compete with natural [[passion|desire]]s.

## Historical Context

The 18th-century debate over the passions was a central axis of Enlightenment [[morality|moral philosophy]]. The Augustinian tradition (inherited by [[jansenists|Jansenism]] and [[pascal|Pascal]]) treated the passions as symptoms of [[sin|original sin]] — corrupt impulses requiring [[divinity|divine]] grace to subdue. Against this, Holbach and his materialist allies (Diderot, Helvétius) argued that the passions are [[phenomena|natural phenomena]] amenable to scientific expla[[nations|nation]] and social regulation. [[david-hume|David Hume]]'s claim that "reason is, and ought only to be the slave of the passions" (1739) supplied philosophical precedent for Holbach's psychological [[effect|determinism]], though Holbach grounded his analysis in physiological [[matter|materialism]] rather than [[david-hume|Hume]]'s empiricism. The key political implication: if passions are determined by temperament and social conditions, then moral reform requires changing conditions, not preaching.

## Core Arguments

Holbach advances four claims about the passions. **First: passions are natural and inevitable** — they arise from the physical [[temperament|constitution]] of the human organism responding to its environment, not from a corrupted will. **Second: passions are morally neutral** — the same passionate energy that produces crime can produce heroism; what matters is the object and direction of passion, not its intensity. **Third: religion cannot restrain the passions** — ch. CXLIII is titled "[[religion|Religion]] Is Certainly Not a [[power|Power]]ful Check Upon the Passions," and Holbach supplies extensive historical [[proof|evidence]]: [[crusades|religious wars]], [[cruelty|persecution]]s, and priestly corruption all [[demon|demons]]trate that supernatural beliefs do not subdue natural [[passion|desire]]s. **Fourth: only countervailing passions restrain passion** — education, law, social approval, and material interest are the real regulators of human behavior, not theological threats.
