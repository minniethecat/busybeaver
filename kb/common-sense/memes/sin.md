# Sin

## Definition

Holbach's critique of [[vice|sin]] is among the most radical elements of his [[morality|moral philosophy]]. Where [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian [[religion|theology]] defines [[vice|sin]] as an [[crime|offense]] against [[god|God]] — a [[crime|transgression]] of [[divinity|divine]] law that merits punishment both temporal and eternal — Holbach argues that the very category is incoherent outside a supernatural framework. If [[god|God]] does not exist, there can be no "[[crime|offense]] against [[god|God]]," and acts traditionally labeled sinful must be evaluated by their actual effects on human welfare. "Physical [[injustice|evil]] commonly passes as the punishment of [[vice|sin]]" (ch. II), but Holbach inverts this: what [[religion|theology]] calls [[vice|sin]] is often natural human behavior that [[clergy|priests]] have arbitrarily prohibited to establish their moral authority.

The doctrine of original sin receives particular scorn. The idea that all humanity is born guilty of [[adam|Adam]]'s [[crime|transgression]], that unbaptized infants merit eternal [[hell|damnation]], and that this [[original-sin|inherited guilt]] requires a [[divinity|divine]] blood sacrifice for its remission — Holbach treats this not as a [[secrecy|mystery]] to be revered but as a system of psychological [[fear|terror]] to be exposed. "[[death|Death]], according to [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian [[religion|theology]], is the penalty of [[vice|sin]]. This doctrine supposes that man was originally immortal, and that it is [[vice|sin]] which has introduced death into [[earth|the world]]" (ch. II).

## Historical Context

The [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian doctrine of [[vice|sin]] developed through centuries of theological elaboration. Augustine's doctrine of original sin, formulated against Pelagius in the 5th century, established that humanity inherited both guilt and corruption from [[adam|Adam]]. Anselm's satis[[sect|faction]] theory (11th century) held that [[vice|sin]] was an infinite [[crime|offense]] against [[god|God]]'s honor requiring an infinite satis[[sect|faction]] — provided by [[jesus-christ|Christ]]'s death. The [[council-of-trent|Council of Trent]] (1545–1563) reaffirmed original sin against Protestant challenges while moderating some of Augustine's more extreme claims about infant [[hell|damnation]].

By Holbach's time, the doctrine of [[vice|sin]] was the psychological foundation of the entire [[religion|religious system]]. Without [[vice|sin]], there was no need for a savior; without a savior, no need for a church; without a church, no need for [[clergy|priests]]. Holbach understood that attac[[monarch|king]] the concept of [[vice|sin]] was to attack the load-bearing wall of [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian [[religion|theology]].

## Core Arguments

**1. Sin is a [[fiction|theological fiction]] with no natural referent.** An action can be harmful, foolish, or cruel, but it cannot be an "[[crime|offense]] against [[god|God]]" unless [[god|God]] exists to be offended. "If [[god|God]] did not save him in the moment when he [[vice|sin]]s, how could man [[vice|sin]]? If [[god|God]] preserves him, [[god|God]], therefore, forces him to live in order to [[vice|sin]]" (ch. II). The concept collapses into contradiction when examined.

**2. The doctrine of [[vice|sin]] is psychologically destructive.** By labeling natural human impulses as sinful, religion creates a permanent state of guilt that only the church can reli[[eve]]. The cycle of [[vice|sin]], guilt, confession, and absolution is a mechanism of control. "When a man has a great [[passion|desire]] to [[vice|sin]], he thinks very little about his [[god|God]]; but when the [[vice|sin]] is committed, he thinks of him again, and his fear revives" (ch. CLXVI).

**3. Sin is used to justify infinite punishment for finite acts.** Holbach finds the proportionality monstrous: a finite [[existence|being]] committing a finite act against an infinite [[existence|being]] somehow merits infinite punishment. This is not justice but "the most terrible cruelty" dressed in theological language. The threat of eternal [[hell|damnation]] for temporal [[vice|sin]]s reveals the coercive function of the doctrine.

**4. The concept of [[vice|sin]] is unnecessary for morality.** Secular [[morality|ethics]] can identify harmful actions, assign responsibility, and prescribe remedies without any appeal to [[vice|sin]]. What [[religion|theology]] calls [[vice|sin]], philosophy calls vice or crime — and can address through education, law, and [[reform|social reform]] rather than through fear of supernatural punishment.

## Reception and Critique

Holbach's elimi[[nations|nation]] of [[vice|sin]] was too radical for most of his contemporaries. [[voltaire|Voltaire]], who shared many of Holbach's anticlerical sentiments, retained a deistic framework in which moral accountability to a [[divinity|divine]] judge remained meaningful. Rousseau argued that the con[[knowledge|science]] was a [[divinity|divine]] voice within, and that guilt before [[god|God]] was essential to moral seriousness. Kant, though no orthodox [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian, preserved a secularized version of original sin in his doctrine of "radical [[injustice|evil]]" — the innate human propensity to subordinate moral law to [[interest|self-interest]].

Later secular thinkers largely vindicated Holbach's approach without always crediting him. Nietzsche's "Genealogy of Morals" traced the concept of [[vice|sin]] to the slave revolt in morality. Freud analyzed guilt as the internalization of social prohibition. [[mode|Mode]]rn secular [[morality|ethics]] treats harmful actions in terms of consequences, rights, and duties without needing the category of [[vice|sin]].

## Relation to Other Concepts

Sin is the theological foundation that supports the entire edifice of [[Religion]]: without [[vice|sin]], there is no need for [[Salvation]], no threat of [[Hell]], and no purchase for [[Priestcraft]]. It is opposed to [[Virtue]], which Holbach defines in naturalistic terms as conduct that promotes human welfare. The fear of [[vice|sin]] is [[worship|cult]]ivated by [[Superstition]] and exploited by [[Authority]]. Sin is connected to [[Guilt]], the psychological state that religion manufactures and then claims to cure. The rejection of [[vice|sin]] is central to [[Morality]] conceived on secular foundations and to [[Reason]], which demands that actions be evaluated by their observable effects rather than by theological categories.
