# Experiment

## Definition

In Holbach's *Common Sense*, "experiment" appears as a term in the empiricist vocabulary that he deploys against religious modes of knowing. Alongside "an astronomer's observations" and "a geometer's calculations," Holbach presents "a chemist's experiments" as exemplary instances of rational inquiry — methods that produce reliable knowledge through the systematic interrogation of nature (002-425). The term is not developed as a philosophical concept in its own right but serves as a synecdoche for the empirical method as a whole, which Holbach treats as the only legitimate path to truth.

## Experiment as Rational Cultivation

Holbach urges humanity to "cultivate his reason, to make experiments" (002-432), positioning experimental inquiry as the active cultivation of the rational faculty. The contrast is with religious modes of thought, which he characterizes as passive and receptive: the believer accepts revealed truths on authority, while the experimenter actively tests nature and draws conclusions from evidence. This distinction maps onto Holbach's broader opposition between reason and superstition. "He fears truth as soon as it is contrary to his interests" (002-432) — and experiment, which brings uncomfortable truths to light, is that which the religious mind most fears.

## Experiment and the Rejection of Authority

The methodological significance of experiment in Holbach's thought extends beyond natural philosophy to ethics and politics. The experimental method — observe, test, conclude — is the antithesis of the authoritarian method — accept, obey, believe. To "make experiments" is to refuse to take claims on trust and to demand evidence. This epistemological stance has radical political implications: if one requires evidence for claims about the natural world, one will also require evidence for claims about divine right, priestly authority, and supernatural sanctions.

## Experiment and the Study of Man

Holbach draws a direct analogy between the experimental investigation of nature and the rational study of humanity. Just as the astronomer, the chemist, and the geometer extend human knowledge by interrogating nature, so too "the study of man, of his nature, of his faculties, would serve to enlighten us concerning our duties" (002-425). The experimental method is unified across domains: the same intellectual habits — observation, systematic testing, critical evaluation of evidence — that produce reliable knowledge about the physical world also yield sound conclusions about morality, politics, and society. The alternative — accepting inherited doctrines on authority — fails equally in both domains.

> As an astronomer's observations, a chemist's experiments, a geometer's calculations, a physician's study of anatomy, serve to extend our knowledge, so also the study of man, of his nature, of his faculties, would serve to enlighten us concerning our duties.
>
> (002-425)
