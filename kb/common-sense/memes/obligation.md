# Obligation

## Definition

Obligation, in Holbach's naturalistic [[morality|ethics]], is the binding character of moral requirements — that which makes certain actions not merely advisable but *required*. Unlike the theological tradition that grounded obligation in [[divinity|divine]] command, Holbach located its source in "the eternal and necessary relations of things among themselves, which have nothing in common with the existence of a [[god|God]]" (ch. 171). Obligation is not an external imposition but an internal consequence of what we are: sensible [[existence|being]]s whose [[universe|nature]] compels us to seek welfare and shun harm, and social [[existence|being]]s whose happiness depends on the regard and cooperation of others. The force of obligation is real — we are genuinely bound — but its source is natural, not supernatural.

## Historical Context

The question of moral obligation was central to Enlightenment [[morality|ethics]]. If, as thinkers from Grotius to [[david-hume|Hume]] had argued, moral principles could be discovered by reason without revelation, what gave those principles their binding force? The theological answer — that [[god|God]]'s will imposes obligation and His sanctions enforce it — seemed increasingly inadequate to philosophers who sought autonomous foundations for [[morality|ethics]]. Holbach's contribution was to argue that obligation arises from the same necessity that governs the physical [[universe|world]]: just as an atom cannot avoid following the laws of motion, a sensible [[existence|being]] cannot avoid [[existence|being]] bound by the conditions of its own [[happiness|well-being]]. This radical naturalization of obligation placed him beyond [[eve]]n [[david-hume|Hume]], who had maintained a role for sentiment, and aligned him with the materialist [[effect|determinism]] of his *System of [[nature|Nature]]*.

## Core Arguments

Holbach advances a systematic case for natural obligation:

1. **Obligation Does Not Require a [[law|Law]]giver**: "Moral obligation implies a law; but this law arises from the eternal and necessary relations of things among themselves, which have nothing in common with the existence of a [[god|God]]" (ch. 171). The concept of law in [[morality|ethics]], as in physics, describes regularities, not commands.

2. **Self-Regarding Obligation Is Inherent in Sensibility**: "The law that compels man not to harm himself, is inherent in the [[universe|nature]] of a sensible [[existence|being]], who... is compelled by his very [[universe|nature]] to seek his welfare and to shun [[injustice|evil]], to love pleasure and to fear [[suffering|pain]]" (ch. 171). No external authority is needed to bind us to self-preservation.

3. **Other-Regarding Obligation Arises from Social [[existence|Existence]]**: We are "compelled to despise those who do them no good, and to detest those who oppose their happiness" (ch. 171). Social sanctions — esteem and contempt, love and hatred — provide the binding force of interpersonal obligations.

4. **Obligation Persists Regardless of [[theology|Theology]]**: "Whether there exists a [[god|God]] or not, whether this [[god|God]] has spoken or not, men's moral duties will always be the same so long as they possess their own [[universe|nature]]; that is to say, so long as they are sensible [[existence|being]]s" (ch. 171).

5. **The Supposed Obligation to [[love|Love]] [[god|God]] Is Incoherent**: The theological claim that we are "under obligation to love [[god|God]]" (ch. 184) founders on the impossibility of loving a [[existence|being]] whose [[attribute|attributes]] are incomprehensible and terrifying.

## Reception and Critique

Holbach's naturalization of obligation was both influential and controversial. Kant's [[morality|ethics]] can be read as an attempt to preserve the binding force of moral law — its character as genuine *obligation* — without recourse to either [[divinity|divine]] command or the naturalistic reduction that Holbach proposed. For Kant, obligation required autonomy of the will, precisely what Holbachian [[effect|determinism]] seemed to deny. Utilitarian thinkers, by contrast, found in Holbach a precursor: the idea that obligation reduces to the natural consequences of actions for [[happiness|well-being]] anticipates Bentham's principle of [[utility]]. Contemporary meta-[[morality|ethics]] revisits Holbach's question — can natural facts generate normative force? — under the rubric of the "is-ought problem," though Holbach himself saw no gap between description and prescription when the description was of human [[universe|nature]] itself.

## Relation to Other Concepts

Obligation is the normative core of Holbach's [[ethics]], the bridge between descriptive facts about human [[universe|nature]] and prescriptive claims about how we should act. It is closely related to [[duty]], which names the specific content of obligation — *what* we are obliged to do — whereas obligation names the binding character — *that* we are bound. It connects to [[necessity]] at the metaphysical level: just as physical [[eve]]nts follow necessarily from their causes, moral obligations follow necessarily from the [[temperament|constitution]] of sensible social [[existence|being]]s. It stands in opposition to [[religion]], which Holbach saw as inventing fictitious obligations to [[god|God]] that displace real obligations to humanity. It also relates to [[morality]] as the total system of obligations and [[virtue]] as the disposition to fulfill them.

## In the Text

The foundational passage on moral obligation appears in chapter 171, where Holbach severs obligation from [[religion|theology]]:

> "We are constantly told that without a God, there can be no moral obligation; that it is necessary for men and for the sovereigns themselves to have a lawgiver sufficiently powerful to compel them to be moral; moral obligation implies a law; but this law arises from the eternal and necessary relations of things among themselves, which have nothing in common with the existence of a God." (ch. 171)

He elaborates the natural basis of self- and other-regarding obligation:

> "The law that compels man not to harm himself, is inherent in the nature of a sensible being... The law which compels a man not to harm others and to do good, is inherent in the nature of sensible beings living in society, who, by their nature, are compelled to despise those who do them no good, and to detest those who oppose their happiness." (ch. 171)

On the independence of obligation from theological beliefs:

> "Whether there exists a God or not, whether this God has spoken or not, men's moral duties will always be the same so long as they possess their own nature; that is to say, so long as they are sensible beings." (ch. 171)
