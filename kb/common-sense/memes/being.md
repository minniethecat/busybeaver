# Being

## Definition

In Holbach's *Common Sense*, the concept of "[[existence|being]]" — particularly as applied to [[god|God]] or a [[god|supreme being]] — is subjected to rigorous philosophical critique. Holbach argues that the term "[[existence|being]]," when predicated of divinity, becomes "but a vague word, which men continually utter, [[existence|being]] able to attach to it only such ideas or qualities as are belied by the facts" (Preface). The theological notion of a [[god|supreme being]] is not a discovery of reason but a projection of human ignorance and fear: an "imaginary [[existence|being]]" whose very incomprehensibility, far from inspiring humility, has [[desire|drive]]n men to [[doctrine|dogma]]tism, [[cruelty|persecution]], and violence. Holbach's critique extends beyond mere a[[divinity|theism]] to a deeper ontological claim: that the concept of a perfect, infinite, intelligent [[existence|being]] is logically impossible and morally disastrous.

## Historical Context

The critique of the concept of [[existence|being]] has deep roots in Western philosophy, from Aristotle's categories through the medieval Scholastic debates over the analogy of [[existence|being]]. By the 18th century, the radical Enlightenment — represented by thinkers such as d'Holbach, Diderot, and Helvétius — had turned the ontological question into a weapon against [[revelation|revealed religion]]. Holbach's innovation was to move the question from the metaphysical to the anthropological plane: instead of as[[monarch|king]] what kind of [[existence|being]] [[god|God]] is, he asked what effects the *idea* of such a [[existence|being]] produces in human societies. His answer was uncompromising: the idea of a [[god|supreme being]] has "caused ravages without number upon the earth" (Preface).

## Core Arguments

Holbach develops several interconnected arguments against the theological concept of [[existence|being]]:

1. **The Vagueness Argument**: The word "[[god|God]]" or "[[god|supreme being]]" is attached to qualities that contradict each other and are belied by observable facts. It is a word without a coherent referent, "a [[existence|being]] of whom not a word can be said without falling into contradictions" (ch. 8).

2. **The Causal Argument from [[evil|Evil]]**: A cause can be judged only by its effects. The [[universe|world]], filled with [[evil|suffering]], disorder, and destruction, does not show [[proof|evidence]] of [[government|governance]] by an intelligent [[existence|being]]. "Everything which takes place in [[earth|the world]] proves to us in the clearest way that it is not governed by an intelligent [[existence|being]]" (054-002).

3. **The [[contradiction|Contradiction]] of [[theology-index|Divine]] [[attribute|Attribute]]s**: The [[attribute|attributes]] ascribed to [[god|God]] — [[infinite|infinity]], immutability, omnipotence, goodness — are irreconcilable with the observed state of [[earth|the world]] and with each other. An immutable [[god|God]] cannot govern a [[universe|world]] of constant revolution; an infinitely good [[god|God]] cannot permit eternal [[evil|suffering]].

4. **The Anthropological Origin**: The notion of an imaginary [[existence|being]] arises from human psychology — specifically from fear and the [[passion|desire]] to understand what remains incomprehensible. The "invincible ignorance" of humanity, rather than discouraging speculation, "does but excite their curiosity" (Preface), leading to [[eve]]r more elaborate and [[doctrine|dogma]]tic fictions.

5. **The Moral Catastrophe**: The idea of a [[god|supreme being]] has not fostered [[morality|virtue]] but instead licensed the worst human cruelties. Men "commit without scruple, as a matter of duty, the greatest crimes" in the service of "a [[existence|being]] who exists only in their imagi[[nations|nation]]" (Preface).

## Reception and Critique

Holbach's radical critique of the concept of [[existence|being]] was immediately controversial. His contemporaries accused him of reducing all metaphysics to psychology and of failing to engage seriously with the sophisticated arguments of theistic philosophers such as [[rene-descartes|Descartes]], [[malebranche|Malebranche]], and [[gottfried-leibniz|Leibniz]]. Yet Holbach's approach anticipated later developments: his insistence on judging concepts by their practical effects foreshadowed pragmatism, and his analysis of religion as a psychological projection influenced Feuerbach, Marx, and Freud. The charge that Holbach dealt only with vulgar [[divinity|theism]] and ignored philosophical [[religion|theology]] has some merit, but it misses his central point — that the [[god|God]] of the philosophers is never the [[god|God]] of the people, and that the practical effects of the concept are what matter for human welfare.

## Relation to Other Concepts

The concept of [[existence|being]] inter[[sect|sects]] with many others in Holbach's system. [[god]] is the specific theological application of the [[existence|being]]-concept that Holbach most directly attacks. [[existence]] is the companion ontological category — Holbach seeks to ground existence claims in material reality rather than abstract speculation. [[nothingness]] is the opposite of [[existence|being]], and Holbach critiques the doctrine of creation *ex nihilo* as another instance of theologians ma[[monarch|king]] coherent thought impossible. [[nature]] represents Holbach's positive [[existence|ontology]]: the realm of real, material [[existence|being]]s governed by necessary laws. [[reason]] is the faculty through which humans can correct their confused notions of [[existence|being]].

## In the Text

Holbach's most concentrated treatment of the concept of [[existence|being]] appears in the Preface, where he traces how a vague word becomes an engine of social destruction:

> "His name having the power to excite in all hearts respect and fear, is found to be but a vague word, which men continually utter, being able to attach to it only such ideas or qualities as are belied by the facts, or which evidently contradict each other. The notion of this imaginary being, or rather the word by which we designate him, would be of no consequence did it not cause ravages without number upon the earth." (Preface)

In chapter 8, he elaborates the logical impossibility of the concept:

> "Does it need, I say, more than simple common sense to understand that the idea of such a being is an idea without model, and that he is himself evidently not a reasonable being? Does it require more than common sense to feel that there is at least delirium and frenzy in hating and tormenting each other for unintelligible opinions of a being of this kind?" (ch. 8)

The [[design|argument from design]] receives a direct refutation in chapter 54:

> "Everything which takes place in the world proves to us in the clearest way that it is not governed by an intelligent being. We can judge of the intelligence of a being but by the means which he employs to accomplish his proposed design." (054-002)
