## Definition

For Holbach, the [[nations|nation]] is the central unit of comparative religious analysis. By observing that [[eve]]ry [[nations|nation]] throughout history has acknowledged some form of divinity — but that the character, [[attribute|attributes]], and commands of these [[gods|divinities]] vary wildly from [[nations|nation]] to [[nations|nation]] — Holbach constructs an empirical argument against the universality of any particular revelation. If [[god|God]] had revealed Himself uniformly to all humanity, all nations would worship the same [[god|God]] in the same way. The fact that they do not [[demon|demons]]trates that gods are local products of [[nations|nation]]al imagi[[nations|nation]], not universal objects of [[divinity|divine]] communication.

The [[nations|nation]] also figures in Holbach's political analysis as the entity most harmed by [[priest|priestcraft]]. [[priest|Priest]]s and [[despotism|despot]]s form an alliance that exploits the [[nations|nation]]; [[nations|nation]]al wealth is diverted to ecclesiastical coffers, [[nations|nation]]al policy is distorted by theological considerations, and [[nations|nation]]al education is replaced by religious indoctri[[nations|nation]].

## Historical Context

The concept of the [[nations|nation]] was undergoing significant transformation in eighteenth-century [[europe|Europe]]. The older sense of "[[nations|nation]]" as a group of people sharing common descent was giving way to a political conception in which the [[nations|nation]] was the source of [[government|sovereignty]]. Rousseau's concept of the general will and the emerging doctrine of popular [[government|sovereignty]] made the [[nations|nation]], not the monarch, the legitimate basis of [[government|political authority]].

Holbach's use of the concept is closer to the anthropological sense. He is interested in nations as empirical units of comparison: the Chinese, the [[india|India]]ns, the [[egypt|Egypt]]ians, the Greeks, the Romans, the Jews, and the [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ians each have their own [[religion|religious system]]s, and the differences between these systems are explicable only if religion is a human product shaped by local circumstances.

## Core Arguments

1. **Religious diversity disproves universal revelation.** If a [[guilt|sin]]gle [[god|God]] had revealed Himself to humanity, all nations would agree about His [[universe|nature]]. The persistent and irreconcilable diversity of [[belief|religious belief]] across nations is decisive [[proof|evidence]] that religions are human inventions.

2. **National character shapes [[nations|nation]]al religion.** A warlike [[nations|nation]] invents a warlike god; a commercial [[nations|nation]] imagines a god who rewards industry; a [[despotism|despot]]ic [[nations|nation]] projects its political structure onto the [[universe|cosmos]] and calls its god a [[monarch|king]]. [[religion|Religion]] reflects the society that produces it.

3. **The alliance of throne and altar exploits the [[nations|nation]].** In [[eve]]ry country, Holbach observes, the [[clergy|priests]] and the [[monarch|ruler]]s cooperate to extract wealth and obedience from the [[nations|nation]]. [[religion|Religion]] provides the ideological justification for political [[persecution|oppression]], and [[politics|political power]] protects [[church|the Church]]'s material interests.

4. **Enlightened nations are the hope of humanity.** Holbach's critique is not pessimistic. [[nations|Nations]] can be educated, and educated nations will reject the fictions that have enslaved them. The progress of [[education|enlightenment]] across the nations of [[europe|Europe]] is, for Holbach, the great hope of his age.

## In the Text

Holbach states the comparative argument plainly:

> "In all ages every nation has acknowledged some kind of divinity. But what does this prove? That men in all ages have been ignorant of the real causes of the phenomena of nature." (ch. CXXXIV)

He observes the link between [[nations|nation]]al character and [[nations|nation]]al religion:

> "There is no nation, no people, which has not its own ideas of the divinity." (ch. CXXXIV)

And he extends the argument to the political consequences:

> "What real benefit can result to a nation in being exterminated by its neighbors on account of difference in their religious opinions?" (ch. CVIII)
