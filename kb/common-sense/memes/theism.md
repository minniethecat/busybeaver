# Theism

## Definition

Theism, in Holbach's usage, encompasses both the [[god|God]] of [[revelation|revealed religion]] (the theologian's [[god|God]]) and the [[god|God]] of natural religion (the deist's or philosophical theist's [[god|God]]). It is the belief in a personal, intelligent, morally concerned [[creator|Creator]] who governs [[nature|the universe]]. Holbach treats [[divinity|theism]] not as a distinct alternative to superstition but as a more refined variant of the same fundamental error: the projection of human [[attribute|attributes]] onto an imaginary cosmic agent. "There is no real difference between natural religion and the most sombre and servile superstition. If the Theist sees [[god|God]] but on the beautiful side, the superstitious man looks upon Him from the most hideous side. The folly of the one is gay of the other is lugubrious; but both are equally delirious" (064-002).

## Historical Context

By Holbach's time, many Enlightenment thinkers had retreated from orthodox [[christianity|Christianity]] to deism — belief in a [[creator|Creator]] [[god|God]] who established natural laws but does not intervene through [[miracle|miracles]] or revelation. [[person-index|Figures]] like [[voltaire|Voltaire]] and Rousseau defended deism as a rational minimum, a purified religion compatible with reason. Holbach, from the radical wing of the Enlightenment, rejected [[eve]]n this. He saw deism/[[divinity|theism]] as an unstable halfway house: once you abandon revelation, [[miracle|miracles]], and priestly authority, what rational grounds remain for believing in any [[god|God]] at all? The deist's "mild Father" and the theologian's "ferocious" judge of souls are, Holbach insists, the same contradiction dressed in different [[passion|emotion]]al tones.

## Core Arguments

**The [[contradiction|Contradiction]] of [[theology-index|Divine]] [[attribute|Attribute]]s.** Whether one accepts the full catalogue of theological [[attribute|attributes]] or the deist's stripped-down list, the concept of [[god|God]] remains incoherent. "[[nothingness|Nothing]] is more evident or more clearly [[demon|demons]]trated than that a [[existence|being]] can not combine qualities so dissimilar, so contradictory, so irreconcilable as those which all the religions of the earth ascribe to [[divinity|Divinity]]" (ch. 109). The theist's [[god|God]] is simultaneously "very good and very wicked, very powerful and very weak, immutable and changeable, perfectly intelligent and perfectly destitute of reason" (ch. 109). The deist attempts to resolve these contradictions by subtracting [[attribute|attributes]] (no [[miracle|miracles]], no particular providence), but the core contradiction — a benevolent [[creator|Creator]] of a [[universe|world]] full of [[evil|suffering]] — remains untouched.

**The Theist's [[god|God]] is as Mysterious as the Theologian's.** Against the deist claim to have arrived at a rational concept of [[god|God]], Holbach retorts: "The [[god|God]] which serves as a foundation to natural religion, to [[divinity|theism]] or to deism, is Himself the greatest [[secrecy|mystery]] to a mind [[desire|wishing]] to dwell upon Him" (ch. 111). The theist may dispense with the [[trinity|Trinity]] and transubstantiation, but the bare idea of an infinite, immaterial, eternal [[existence|being]] who created [[nature|the universe]] *ex nihilo* and governs it with benevolent intention is itself an impenetrable [[secrecy|mystery]]. Theism simply relocates the [[secrecy|mystery]] from revealed [[doctrine|dogma]] to the concept of [[god|God]] itself.

**The Problem of [[evil|Evil]] Undoes Every Theism.** The theist claims his [[god|God]] is "the mildest of [[sovereign|Sovereign]]s" who "fills [[nature|the universe]] with His benefactions." Holbach's response is blunt: "do you not see that [[eve]]rything in this [[universe|world]] contradicts the good qualities which you attribute to your [[god|God]]? In the numerous family of this mild Father I see but unfortunate ones. Under the empire of this just [[sovereign|Sovereign]] I see crime victorious and [[morality|virtue]] in distress" (ch. 116). The deist cannot appeal to [[sin|original sin]] or [[mystery|divine mystery]] to explain [[injustice|evil]] — those are theological devices he has already abandoned. He is left with a [[god|God]] whose creation flatly contradicts his supposed [[attribute|attributes]].

**Theism as Emotional Preference, Not Rational Conclusion.** Holbach diagnoses [[divinity|theism]] as a psychological compromise rather than a philosophical position. The theist recoils from the "ferocious and strange [[god|God]] of [[religion|theology]]" and invents a milder version, but this is aesthetic preference, not reasoning. "Compelled to acknowledge that your good [[god|God]], in contradiction with Himself, distributes with the same hand good and [[injustice|evil]], you will find yourself obliged, in order to justify Him, to send me, as the [[clergy|priests]] would, to the other life. Invent, then, another [[god|God]] than the one of [[religion|theology]], because your [[god|God]] is as contradictory as its [[god|God]] is" (ch. 116). Every [[divinity|theism]] collapses into either [[contradiction|incoherence]], [[secrecy|mystery]]-mongering, or an admission that no [[god|God]] exists at all.

**[[atheism|Atheism]] as the Honest Conclusion.** Holbach's ultimate argument against [[divinity|theism]] is that a[[divinity|theism]] is simply the honest acknowledgment of what reason already [[demon|demons]]trates. Theism survives not because it is rationally compelling but because it is [[passion|emotion]]ally comfortable — it allows one to reject the cruelties of [[revelation|revealed religion]] while retaining the [[hope|consolation]] of cosmic purpose. But this comfort is purchased at the price of intellectual consistency. Theism is, in effect, a refusal to follow one's own reasoning to its conclusion.

## Reception and Critique

Holbach's refusal to grant [[eve]]n deism a rational foothold put him at the far left of Enlightenment thought. [[voltaire|Voltaire]], despite his ferocious anticlericalism, thought Holbach's a[[divinity|theism]] went too far and was socially dangerous. Rousseau's "Profession of [[faith|Faith]] of the Savoyard Vicar" in *Émile* (1762) articulated precisely the deistic position that Holbach rejected. Later critiques of [[divinity|theism]] — from Feuerbach's projection theory to Dawkins's "[[god|God]] hypothesis" — recapitulate many of Holbach's arguments, particularly the claim that [[divinity|theism]] is a psychological crutch rather than an explanatory hypothesis.

## Relation to Other Concepts

Theism is the genus of which [[Theology]] is the systematic elaboration and [[Religion]] the institutional expression. It is opposed by [[Atheism]], which Holbach presents not as a positive doctrine but as the honest refusal to affirm what cannot be [[demon|demons]]trated. Theism is supported by the [[Design]] argument and the concept of [[Providence]], both of which Holbach dismantles independently. The [[passion|emotion]]al root of [[divinity|theism]] is [[Fear]] of [[universe|nature]]'s indifference; its remedy is [[Science]] and [[Philosophy]], which offer genuine [[knowledge|understanding]] in place of consoling fictions.

## In the Text

On the equivalence of [[divinity|theism]] and superstition:

> "There is no real difference between natural religion and the most sombre and servile superstition. If the Theist sees God but on the beautiful side, the superstitious man looks upon Him from the most hideous side. The folly of the one is gay of the other is lugubrious; but both are equally delirious." (064-002)

On the contradiction of [[attribute|divine attributes]]:

> "The theologian's God, as well as the God of the theist, is He not evidently a cause incompatible with the effects attributed to Him? ...we must either invent another God, or conclude that the one which, for so many centuries, has been revealed to mortals, is at the same time very good and very wicked, very powerful and very weak." (ch. 109)

On the theist's [[god|God]] tested against experience:

> "A good God who does evil or who permits it to be done, a God full of equity and in an empire where innocence is so often oppressed; a perfect God who produces but imperfect and wretched works; such a God and His conduct, are they not as great mysteries as that of the incarnation?" (ch. 116)
