# Sect

## Definition

A sect, in Holbach's usage, is any organized religious group distinguished by doctrinal particularities from other groups claiming the same broader tradition.
His analysis focuses not on the theological content of sectarian differences but on their social effects: mutual hatred, [[cruelty|persecution]], and war.

Holbach's central insight is that the very existence of sects is [[proof|evidence]] against [[revelation|divine revelation]].
If [[god|God]] had truly revealed a [[vice|sin]]gle truth to humanity, "He ought at least to speak in the same language to all" (ch. 93).
The proliferation of competing sects — each claiming exclusive access to [[divinity|divine]] truth, each denouncing the others as [[heresy|heretic]]al — is inexplicable on the hypothesis of a benevolent and omnipotent revealer.

It is, however, perfectly explicable on the hypothesis that religion is a human invention.
Different [[worship|cult]]ures, different historical circumstances, and different political interests produce different religious forms.
"Sects see each other very ridiculous and foolish" (ch. 93) because each recognizes the [[absurdity|absurd]]ity in the others' doctrines while remaining blind to its own.

## Historical Context

Eighteenth-century [[europe|Europe]] was still recovering from the Wars of [[religion|Religion]] (1562–1648), in which Catholic-Protestant sectarian conflict had killed millions.
In [[paris|France]], the [[edict-of-nantes|Revocation of the Edict of Nantes]] (1685) had [[desire|drive]]n hundreds of thousands of Huguenots into exile.
Religious [[cruelty|persecution]] was not a historical memory but a living reality.

Within [[christianity|Christianity]] itself, the proliferation of Protestant denominations — Lutheran, Calvinist, Anglican, Anabaptist, Quaker, and dozens more — made Holbach's point visible to any observer.
All claimed to derive their doctrines from the same [[scripture|Bible]], yet they arrived at incompatible conclusions on questions of salvation, [[sacrament|sacraments]], and church [[government|governance]].

Holbach wrote in a context where "toleration" was still a controversial [[model|ideal]].
[[john-locke|Locke]]'s *Letter Concerning [[toleration|Toleration]]* (1689) and [[pierre-bayle|Bayle]]'s arguments for religious liberty had made the philosophical case, but most [[europe|Europe]]an states still maintained established churches and penalized dissent.

## Core Arguments

**1. Sectarian diversity disproves revelation.**
"[[god|God]], having revealed Himself to men, ought at least to speak in the same language to all" (ch. 93).
If revelation were real, it would produce convergence, not fragmentation.
The actual [[model|pattern]] — endless division — points to human fabrication.

**2. Sects [[demon|demons]]trate the arbitrariness of religious truth.**
"The most respected [[mysteries]] in one religion are laughable for another" (ch. 93).
What one sect holds as sacred truth, another dismisses as [[absurdity|absurd]] superstition.
There is no neutral [[model|standard]] by which to adjudicate between them, because each sect's criteria of judgment are internal to its own tradition.

**3. Sectarian hatred is structurally produced by exclusive truth-claims.**
"There is no religious person who, according to his temperament, does not hate, despise, or pity the adherents of a sect different from his own" (ch. 140).
The logic of exclusive revelation is inherently hostile: if I possess the unique truth, your disagreement is not a difference of opinion but rebellion against [[god|God]].

**4. [[politics-index|State]] power amplifies sectarian violence.**
"Every [[monarch|sovereign]] who assumes the protection of a sect or of a religious faction, usually becomes the tyrant of other sects" (ch. 139).
When [[politics|political power]] allies with religious exclusivism, [[cruelty|persecution]] becomes systematic.
"The dominant religion (which is never but that of the [[monarch|sovereign]] and the armies) always makes its superiority felt in a very cruel and injurious manner" (ch. 140).

**5. Even within a [[vice|sin]]gle sect, disagreement is endemic.**
"Never have [[clergy|priests]], of [[eve]]n the same sect, come to an agreement among themselves about the manner of [[knowledge|understanding]] the wishes of a [[god|God]] who has truly revealed Himself" (ch. 93).
The fragmentation does not stop at the denominational level; [[eve]]ry sect generates its own internal controversies, producing further subdivision.

## Reception and Critique

The nineteenth century saw sectarian conflict partially replaced by [[nations|nation]]alist conflict, though religious identity often fused with [[nations|nation]]al identity.
The twentieth century's ecumenical movement within [[christianity|Christianity]] was in part a response to the scandal of division that Holbach diagnosed.

Critics note that Holbach's argument from sectarian diversity proves too much.
Scientific communities also contain competing schools and interpretive disagreements, yet we do not conclude that [[knowledge|science]] is a human fabrication.
The difference, Holbach would reply, is that scientific disputes are [[eve]]ntually resolved by [[proof|evidence]], while theological disputes persist indefinitely because there is no [[proof|evidence]] to resolve them.

## Relation to Other Concepts

Sects are the organizational expression of [[religion]]'s particularity — the universal [[god|God]] worshipped by mutually hostile factions.
Sectarian division is enabled by [[priest]]ly authority, [[vice|sin]]ce each sect's [[priest|clergy]] claims exclusive interpretive competence.

The concept relates to [[toleration|tolerance]] as its antithesis: the more seriously a sect takes its exclusive truth-claims, the less [[toleration|tolerance]] it can extend to others.
Sect is also connected to [[sovereign]]: state protection transforms a sect from one opinion among many into an instrument of political coercion.
