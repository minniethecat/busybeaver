## Definition and Context

In "Common Sense," Holbach presents religious skepticism as the natural and inevitable outcome of rational thought. Rather than a position to be condemned, skepticism appears in his analysis as intellectual honesty — the refusal to accept claims without evidence.

## The Universality of Skepticism

Holbach makes the provocative claim that true belief is far rarer than professed:

> ...all men are either skeptics or atheists, than to pretend that they are... (002-236)

He argues that "to be a skeptic is to lack the motives necessary..." (002-237) for genuine religious conviction — implying that professed believers are either self-deceived or performing belief for social reasons.

## Skepticism and Indifference

Holbach distinguishes religious skepticism from mere indifference:

> ...religious skepticism can be but the effect of a... (002-238)

He clarifies that "indifference itself" should not be "confounded with skepticism" (002-239) — the skeptic has examined and found wanting, while the indifferent person has simply not cared to inquire.
