# Miracle

## Definition

A miracle, in the theological sense critiqued by Holbach, is an [[eve]]nt claimed to violate or suspend the ordinary laws of [[universe|nature]] through direct [[divinity|divine]] intervention. Holbach treats miracles not as [[proof|evidence]] of [[supernatural|the supernatural]] but as symptoms of intellectual failure: when confronted with causes "which, although unknown to us, are no less natural and necessary, we are stupefied, we cry out miracles" (002-054).

## Historical Context

Miracles were central to the self-authentication of [[revelation|revealed religion]] in Holbach's [[europe|Europe]]. The [[catholic-church|Catholic Church]] required attested miracles for [[saint|canonization]]; the biblical narrative — from the parting of the Red Sea to the resurrection of [[jesus-christ|Christ]] — rested on miraculous claims. Protestant and Catholic apologists alike argued that miracles served as [[divinity|divine]] credentials, confirming the authority of revelation. This argument had been powerfully attacked by [[baruch-spinoza|Spinoza]] in the *Tractatus Theologico-Politicus* (1670), which argued that miracles were impossible because natural law was identical with [[divinity|divine]] decree and could not be violated without [[god|God]] contradicting Himself. [[david-hume|David Hume]]'s essay "Of Miracles" (1748) assaulted the credibility of miracle testimony on probabilistic grounds. Holbach synthesizes and popularizes these critiques with characteristic polemical vigor.

## Core Arguments

**1. Miracles are attributed to unknown natural causes.** Holbach argues that miracles are invoked precisely where natural understanding reaches its limit (002-054). The miracle-of-the-gaps argument: wherever causal [[knowledge|understanding]] is incomplete, [[supernatural|the supernatural]] rushes in.

**2. Miracles exploit the taste for obscurity.** "If religion was clear, it would have fewer attractions for the ignorant. They need obscurity, [[mysteries]], fables, miracles, incredible things, which keep their brains perpetually at work" (002-012). This is a psychological analysis: the obscurity of miracles is not a bug but a feature — it engages the credulous mind more deeply than clear truth [[eve]]r could.

**3. Miracle claims fail evidentiary scrutiny.** Holbach challenges the believer to examine miracle reports critically: "If I have the curiosity to examine these miracles, I find that they are tales void of probability, related by suspicious people" (002-245). The [[proof|evidence]] for miracles is hearsay, propagated by interested parties ([[clergy|priests]]) in remote times and places, immune to cross-exami[[nations|nation]].

**4. A self-revealing [[god|God]] would not need miracle proxies.** With characteristic logical sharpness, Holbach asks why an omnipotent [[existence|being]] would rely on intermediaries and ambiguous signs: "Was it not much easier to show Himself, and to explain for Himself?" (002-054). The indirectness of miraculous communication is, for Holbach, [[proof|evidence]] that it is human fabrication.

## Reception and Critique

Holbach's critique of miracles did not go unanswered. [[will|Will]]iam Paley's *A View of the [[evidence|Evidence]]s of [[christianity|Christianity]]* (1794) mounted a detailed defense of miracle testimony. In the twentieth century, C.S. Lewis argued that miracles were not violations of natural law but interventions by a higher law — a position Holbach would have dismissed as verbal evasion. The debate over miracles continues in contemporary philosophy of religion, with figures like Richard Swinburne defending their possibility on probabilistic grounds.

## Relation to Other Concepts

Miracles are the evidentiary pillar of [[Revelation]] and [[Faith]] — without them, [[revelation|revealed religion]] lacks its claim to [[divinity|divine]] authentication. They are opposed to [[Reason]], which demands natural expla[[nations|nation]], and to [[Science]], which proceeds by discovering the laws that miracles supposedly violate. The miracle concept is sustained intellectually by [[Ignorance]] and institutionally by [[Priesthood]]. Holbach links miracles to [[Superstition]], which thrives on the inexplicable.

## The Pagan Precedent for Christian Miracles

Holbach deploys comparative mythology as a weapon against [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian exceptionalism. By tracing [[gospel|Gospel]] narratives to pagan antecedents — the birth, death, and resurrection of Bacchus/Dionysus as the template for [[jesus-christ|Christ]]'s story — he argues that the miracles claimed as [[evidence|proof]] of [[christianity|Christianity]]'s [[divinity|divine]] origin are in fact literary borrowings. The argument does not merely question the historicity of miracles; it provides a naturalistic expla[[nations|nation]] for how the narratives were constructed, removing any need for supernatural [[cause|causation]].

> It is certainly not without reason that we consider these things as lies; for it is easy to see that all these pretended miracles have been invented but by imitating the fables of the Pagan poets. This imitation is so striking that it is impossible to doubt it. The birth of Bacchus, his education, his travels, his exploits, his death, and his resurrection, are visibly the models upon which the history of Christ has been built.
>
> (APP002-006)
