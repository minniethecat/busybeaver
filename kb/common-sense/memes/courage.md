# Courage

## Definition

In Holbach's naturalistic [[morality|ethics]], courage is the disposition to act according to reason despite the threat of negative consequences — social disapproval, legal punishment, or physical harm. Unlike the theological [[morality|virtue]]s, courage requires no [[divinity|divine]] sanction; it arises naturally from a clear [[knowledge|understanding]] of one's interests and a firm commitment to truth.

Holbach's primary [[model|exemplar]] of courage is [[meslier|Jean Meslier]], the priest whose posthumous testament provided the raw material for *Common Sense*. The French [[nation|Nation]]al Convention praised Meslier as "the first priest who had the courage and the honesty to abjure [[error|religious error]]s" — a formulation that links courage directly to [[truth|intellectual honesty]] and the rejection of superstition.

## Holbach's Conception

**1. [[mind|Intellect]]ual courage as the foundation of [[education|enlightenment]].** The courage Holbach values most is not martial but intellectual: the willingness to question [[opinion|received opinion]], to "abjure [[error|religious error]]s" despite the personal cost. Meslier spent his life publicly performing a religion he privately despised, lac[[monarch|king]] the courage to speak openly — but his posthumous manuscript was an act of deferred courage, laying the foundation for others.

**2. The absence of courage perpetuates superstition.** Without courage, "man was, and always remained, a child without experience, a slave without courage, a loggerhead who feared to reason, and who could never escape from the labyrinth into which his ancestors had misled him." Courage is the psychological precondition for the exercise of [[reason]] — without it, fear keeps the mind imprisoned.

**3. Courage is natural, not supernatural.** Holbach does not treat courage as a gift of grace or a fruit of the [[spirit|Spirit]]. It is a natural human capacity, available to anyone who understands their true interests and is willing to act on that [[knowledge|understanding]]. The Convention's praise of Meslier as having "the courage and the honesty" treats these as paired natural [[morality|virtue]]s.

**4. Courage vs. [[zeal|missionary zeal]].** Holbach carefully distinguishes genuine courage from the apparent courage of [[missionaries]] and [[martyr]] — which he analyzes as [[interest|self-interest]] disguised as bravery. The [[missionaries|missionary]] "seeks [[chance|fortune]] by the aid of his doctrine" and risks [[cruelty|persecution]] as an investment. Meslier's courage, by contrast, produced no material reward; it was purely the courage of conviction.

## In the Text

The [[nation|Nation]]al Convention's praise of Meslier:

> "The National Convention sends to the Committee of Public Instruction the proposition made by one of its members to erect a statue to Jean Meslier, curate at Etrepigny, in Champagne, the first priest who had the courage and the honesty to abjure religious errors."

On the absence of courage (ch. I — Apologue):

> "Thus man was, and always remained, a child without experience, a slave without courage, a loggerhead who feared to reason, and who could never escape from the labyrinth into which his ancestors had misled him."

On courage distinguished from [[zeal|missionary zeal]]:

> "We are often surprised and affected at the sight of the generous courage and the disinterested zeal which have led missionaries to preach their doctrine at the risk even of suffering the most rigorous torments. We draw from this love... deductions favorable to the religion which they have proclaimed; but in truth this disinterestedness is only apparent."
>
> (133-002)

## Relation to Other Concepts

Courage is the enabling [[morality|virtue]] for the exercise of [[reason]], [[honesty]], and [[free-thought]]. It stands opposed to [[fear]], [[cowardice]], and [[credulity]] — the psychological states that keep people enslaved to superstition. In Holbach's system, courage is connected to [[virtue]] more broadly and to the specific [[model|exemplar]] of [[meslier]].
