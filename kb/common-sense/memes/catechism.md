# Catechism

## Holbach's Critique

Holbach singles out the **catechism** — the systematic religious instruction of children through question-and-answer formulae — as a particularly insidious institution in *Common Sense*. He observes that "the first question of every catechism was, and will always be, the duty of paying the priests" (002-017). This is not a casual remark but a central element of Holbach's sociology of religion: the catechism's primary social function is to secure the material basis of the clergy by inculcating the obligation of financial support before any other religious duty. Holbach also notes, with dark irony, that religious instruction can produce "an excellent catechism for Beelzebub" (APP007-058) — that is, a curriculum of cruelty and fanaticism dressed in pious language. For Holbach, the catechism exemplifies the way religion colonizes the developing mind, installing dogmas before reason has a chance to develop critical defenses.

## Selected Passage

Holbach identifies the catechism's first question as a tool for preventing independent thought:

> The existence of a God is the basis of all religion. Few people seem to doubt this existence, but this fundamental principle is precisely the one which prevents every mind from reasoning. The first question of every catechism was, and will always be, the most difficult one to answer.
>
> (002-017)
