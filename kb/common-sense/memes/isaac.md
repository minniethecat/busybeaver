# Isaac

## Biography

Isaac (Hebrew: Yitzhak, "he will laugh") is the second of the three biblical patriarchs, the son of [[abraham|Abraham]] and Sarah, born when his mother was ninety years old according to the Book of Genesis. His narrative occupies Genesis 21–28 and is notably sparser than those of his father [[abraham|Abraham]] and his son [[jacob|Jacob]] — Isaac functions primarily as a transitional figure, the bridge between the founding covenant and the twelve tribes. His most dramatic moment comes early: the Akedah, or binding, when [[abraham|Abraham]] is commanded by [[god|God]] to sacrifice him, only for a ram to be substituted at the last moment. Isaac later marries Rebekah, fathers the twins Esau and [[jacob|Jacob]], and is deceived by [[jacob|Jacob]] into giving him the blessing meant for the firstborn.

In Jewish tradition, Isaac is the only patriarch who never leaves the land of [[palestine|Canaan]], earning him the title "the bound one" and ma[[monarch|king]] him, in some midrashic readings, a willing participant in his own near-sacrifice. [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian typology reads the Akedah as a prefiguration of [[jesus-christ|Christ]]'s crucifixion — the beloved son offered by the father. In [[islam|Islam]], Ishaq is a [[prophecy|prophet]], though the Quran's account of the near-sacrifice does not name which son was offered, leading to a longstanding interpretive dispute.

## Role in Religious History

Isaac occupies a structurally crucial but narratively understated position in the [[abraham|Abraham]]ic lineage. He is the sole patriarch whose birth is explicitly miraculous — Sarah's post-menopausal pregnancy — ma[[monarch|king]] his very existence a [[evidence|proof]] of [[divinity|divine]] intervention for believers. His near-sacrifice establishes the paradigm of [[faith|obedient submission]] that all three traditions valorize, and his blessing of [[jacob|Jacob]] over Esau establishes the line of inheritance that leads to the twelve tribes of [[israel|Israel]].

For Holbach's purposes, Isaac is most significant as part of the patriarchal trio — [[abraham|Abraham]], Isaac, and [[jacob|Jacob]] — to whom [[god|God]]'s covenant promises were made. These promises, Holbach argues, were [[demon|demons]]trably never fulfilled: the descendants of these patriarchs never became "as numerous as the stars," and the land they were promised never became their permanent possession. The entire edifice of biblical [[revelation|revelation]] rests on promises that failed, and Isaac is one of the three named recipients of those failed promises.

## Connection to "Common Sense"

Holbach discusses Isaac in *Common Sense* (*Le Bon Sens*, 1772) in two distinct argumentative contexts, both designed to undermine the credibility of [[revelation|revealed religion]].

**1. The sacrifice of Isaac as a pagan parallel.** In his chapter on [[miracle|miracles]], Holbach places the near-sacrifice of Isaac alongside miraculous rescue narratives from pagan antiquity — Castor and Pollux appearing in Roman battles, the [[god|God]]dess Vesta sending a heifer to save Metella, Diana substituting a hind for a human victim:

> "If a ram was miraculously found to be offered as a sacrifice in the place of Isaac, whom his father Abraham was about to sacrifice, the Goddess Vesta also sent a heifer to be sacrificed in the place of Metella, daughter of Metellus: the Goddess Diana sent a hind in the place of Iphigenia." (Appendix 2, §III)

The argument is systematic: [[eve]]ry biblical miracle has a pagan counterpart. If the pagan stories are dismissed as myth, consistency demands the same treatment of the biblical narratives. Isaac's ram is no more credible than Diana's hind — both are the products of a [[superstition|superstitious]] imagi[[nations|nation]] that sees [[divinity|divine]] intervention wherever it wishes.

**2. The unfulfilled patriarchal promises.** In his extended critique of the [[christianity|Christian]] religion, Holbach groups Isaac with [[abraham|Abraham]] and [[jacob|Jacob]] as the recipients of promises that history has refuted:

> "It is certain that the Jewish people, or the people of Israel — which is the only one that can be regarded as having descended from the Patriarchs Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob, and the only ones to whom these promises should have been fulfilled — have never been so numerous that it could be said they had filled the whole earth." (Appendix 4)

The logic is devastating in its simplicity: a [[god|God]] who promises what never occurs either cannot keep his word or does not exist. Isaac, as the middle link in the patriarchal chain, is bound up in this failed promise — his life is the living bridge between the covenant and its non-fulfillment.

> "Now there is no reason to think otherwise of all that has been said by these pretended Holy Patriarchs, Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob, in regard to the Divine Revelations which they claim to have had." (Appendix 4)

## Key Ideas Attributed / Critiqued

Holbach's treatment of Isaac advances three interrelated lines of critique:

**1. Miraculous substitution as mythic [[model|pattern]].** The ram substituted for Isaac belongs to a cross-[[worship|cult]]ural [[model|pattern]] of [[soul|anima]]l substitution in sacrifice narratives. Holbach's comparative method — placing biblical and pagan [[miracle|miracles]] side by side — is designed to show that the biblical story is not unique, not [[revelation|revealed]], but simply one instance of a universal human tendency to invent [[divinity|divine]] rescue stories.

**2. The patriarch as [[proof|evidence]] of failure.** Isaac's historical significance lies in [[existence|being]] the recipient of a [[divinity|divine]] promise. If that promise failed — and Holbach argues the historical record shows it did — then the [[god|God]] who made it either does not exist or is not what the [[testament|biblical texts]] claim. Isaac is thus not a [[evidence|proof]] of [[faith|faith]] but a [[evidence|proof]] of the failure of [[faith|faith]].

**3. The binding as moral horror.** Although Holbach does not dwell on the Akedah at length, his broader critique of [[abraham|Abraham]]'s willingness to sacrifice his son applies equally to the role Isaac plays in the narrative. A [[god|God]] who commands child sacrifice — [[eve]]n if prevented at the last moment — is a [[god|God]] whose commands cannot be the basis of [[morality|morality]].

## Legacy

Isaac's philosophical legacy is largely mediated through the interpretations placed upon the Akedah. Kierkegaard's *[[fear|Fear]] and Trembling* made the binding of Isaac the central text for existentialist reflection on [[faith|faith]] and [[ethics|ethics]], reading [[abraham|Abraham]]'s willingness as a "teleological suspension of the ethical." Where Kierkegaard saw the highest form of religious existence, Holbach saw only [[fanaticism|fanaticism]] dressed in [[bible|scripture]].

In contemporary [[biblical-criticism|biblical criticism]], Isaac is increasingly recognized as a figure whose narrative may preserve traces of a transition from child sacrifice to [[soul|anima]]l substitution in ancient [[israel|Israel]]ite religion — a reading that would, ironically, vindicate Holbach's claim that the biblical narratives reflect human [[worship|cult]]ural evolution rather than [[divinity|divine]] [[revelation|revelation]].
