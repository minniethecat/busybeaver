# Mark

## Biography

Mark (also called Mark the Evangelist, Saint Mark, or John Mark) is identified in [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian tradition as the author of the Second [[gospel|Gospel]] and as a companion of [[peter|Peter]] and [[paul|Paul]]. According to the Acts of the Apostles, John Mark was the son of a [[jerusalem|Jerusalem]] woman named [[mary|Mary]] in whose house the early church gathered; he accompanied [[paul|Paul]] and Barnabas on their first [[missionaries|missionary]] journey but left them at Perga, causing a later rift between [[paul|Paul]] and Barnabas. Later tradition — recorded by Papias of Hierapolis (c. 130 CE) and preserved by Eusebius — holds that Mark served as [[peter|Peter]]'s interpreter in [[rome|Rome]] and composed his [[gospel|Gospel]] based on [[peter|Peter]]'s recollections of [[jesus-christ|Jesus]]'s [[doctrine|teaching]]. Mark's [[gospel|Gospel]] is universally recognized by critical scholars as the earliest of the four canonical [[gospel|Gospel]]s, written around 65–70 CE, and as a primary source for both [[matthew|Matthew]] and [[luke|Luke]].

## Role in Religious History

Mark's [[gospel|Gospel]] is the shortest of the four, is written in a rough, colloquial Greek, and lacks both a birth narrative and — in the earliest manuscripts — a resurrection appearance narrative, ending abruptly at the empty tomb with the women fleeing in fear (Mark 16:8). The so-called "longer ending" (Mark 16:9–20) is a later scribal addition, absent from the earliest manuscripts. Mark portrays [[jesus-christ|Jesus]] in vivid, urgent terms — the word "immediately" recurs throughout — and emphasizes the [[secrecy|mystery]] of [[jesus-christ|Jesus]]'s identity, the "messianic secret" that [[jesus-christ|Jesus]] repeatedly commands others not to disclose.

As the earliest [[gospel|Gospel]], Mark's text is of foundational importance for historical Jesus scholarship. The synoptic problem — the observation that [[matthew|Matthew]], [[mark|Mark]], and [[luke|Luke]] share so much material that some literary relationship must exist among them — is typically resolved by the theory of Markan priority: [[matthew|Matthew]] and [[luke|Luke]] both used Mark as a source, which explains why they share the structure and content that Mark contains, while diverging in the material they add.

## Connection to "Common Sense"

Holbach does not devote individual attention to Mark, but Mark's [[gospel|Gospel]] figures in the catalogue of contradictions that is central to Holbach's attack on biblical inerrancy. Mark's distinctive accounts of the resurrection and the ascension — differing from [[matthew|Matthew]]'s, [[luke|Luke]]'s, and [[john-the-evangelist|John]]'s — are among Holbach's exhibits.

**1. Contradicting resurrection apparitions.** Holbach's systematic enumeration of the resurrection accounts highlights Mark's contributions to the chaos of contradictory testimony:

> "Mark speaks of three apparitions: The first, when He appeared to Mary Magdalene; the second, when He appeared to His two disciples, who went to Emmaus; and the third, when He appeared to His eleven disciples, whom He reproaches for their incredulity." (Chapter 2)

But [[matthew|Matthew]] speaks of two apparitions, [[luke|Luke]] of two, and John of four. The central miracle of [[christianity|Christian]] [[faith|faith]] — the [[eve]]nt that is supposed to vindicate [[eve]]ry claim [[jesus-christ|Jesus]] [[eve]]r made — is reported in four mutually incompatible versions. For Holbach, the conclusion is unavoidable: these are not four independent witnesses to one [[eve]]nt but four different traditions, each shaped by the theological interests of its [[society|community]].

**2. [[contradiction|Contradiction]] on the location of the final apparition.** The [[gospel|Gospel]]s cannot [[eve]]n agree on where the risen [[jesus-christ|Jesus]] appeared to his disciples:

> "Matthew says that it was in Galilee, upon a mountain; Mark says that it was when they were at table; Luke says that He brought them out of Jerusalem as far as Bethany, where He left them by rising to Heaven; and John says that it was in the city of Jerusalem, in a house of which they had closed the doors, and another time upon the borders of the Tiberian Sea." (Chapter 2)

[[galilee|Galilee]], a dining table, Bethany, a locked room in [[jerusalem|Jerusalem]], the Sea of Tiberias — five different settings for the same [[eve]]nt. A police investigator faced with five such incompatible witness statements would not declare the [[eve]]nt confirmed; Holbach suggests the reader apply the same [[model|standard]] to [[gospel|the Gospel]]s.

**3. The problem of the missing ending.** Though Holbach does not mention the textual problem of Mark's ending (the longer ending was accepted as canonical in his era), the fact that the earliest [[gospel|Gospel]] originally ended with the women fleeing in [[fear|terror]] — "for they were afraid" — and that the resurrection [[phenomena|appearances]] had to be added by a later hand, is consistent with Holbach's broader argument: the [[bible|New Testament]] is a layered, composite, humanly-produced text, not a unified [[divinity|divine]] [[revelation|revelation]].

## Key Ideas Attributed / Critiqued

**1. Mark as [[proof|evidence]] of progressive elaboration.** For Holbach, the fact that Mark's [[gospel|Gospel]] is the shortest, the least supernatural in its earliest form, and the source for the later, more elaborate [[gospel|Gospel]]s is significant. It suggests a direction of literary development: from terse, urgent proclamation to elaborated, theologically refined narrative. The later [[gospel|Gospel]]s add birth stories, resurrection [[phenomena|appearances]], ascensions — precisely the elements that elevate a human teacher into a [[divinity|divine]] [[existence|being]].

**2. Immediacy vs. historicity.** Mark's breathless narrative style — [[eve]]nt after [[eve]]nt, "immediately" after "immediately" — conveys urgency but not necessarily accuracy. The absence of a birth narrative, the abrupt original ending, and the small-scale Galilean setting of Jesus's ministry all suggest a tradition at an early stage of development, before the full mythological apparatus had been installed.

**3. Human transmission.** The tradition recorded by Papias — that Mark wrote down [[peter|Peter]]'s recollections, "not in order" — acknowledges the human chain of transmission: from [[jesus-christ|Jesus]] to [[peter|Peter]], from [[peter|Peter]]'s memory to Mark's pen, from Mark's Greek to later redactors and copyists. Each link in the chain is fallible. Holbach's point is that the doctrine of biblical inerrancy asks the reader to ignore [[eve]]ry link in the chain except the first, which it [[attribute|attributes]] to [[god|God]] — a leap that no rational person would make with any other ancient text.

## Legacy

Mark's [[gospel|Gospel]], long neglected in favor of the more theologically developed [[matthew|Matthew]] and [[john-the-evangelist|John]], has received renewed attention in modern critical scholarship precisely because of its primitiveness. The discovery of Markan priority — that Mark, not [[matthew|Matthew]], was the first [[gospel|Gospel]] written — was a watershed in biblical studies. Mark's [[jesus-christ|Jesus]] is more human, more [[passion|emotion]]al, more mysterious than the [[jesus-christ|Christ]] of later [[religion|theology]] — he gets angry, he sighs, he cannot do [[miracle|miracles]] in his hometown because of their [[atheism|unbelief]].

From Holbach's perspective, Mark is the clearest window onto the historical core of the [[jesus-christ|Jesus]] tradition before the mythological overlay accumulated. That the earliest [[gospel|Gospel]] is also the one with the fewest supernatural claims is not, for Holbach, a coincidence: it is what one would expect if [[supernatural|the supernatural]] claims are later inventions.
