# Pride

## Definition

Pride, in Holbach's analysis, is not merely an individual moral failing but the structural vice of organized religion. It is pride that enables a man to claim he speaks for [[god|God]], that [[god|the creator]] of [[nature|the universe]] has entrusted him with sacred [[mysteries]], that those who disagree with his [[religion|theology]] deserve punishment in this [[universe|world]] and the next. "Pride and vanity always were and always will be the inherent vices of the [[clergy|priesthood]]" (ch. CLXXXVIII). The priest does not happen to be proud; pride is constitutive of his role.

Holbach distinguishes between ordinary pride — the universal human tendency to overvalue oneself — and theological pride, which claims [[divinity|divine]] authorization for this overvaluation. The priest's pride is uniquely dangerous because it is shielded from correction: to question the priest is to question [[god|God]]. "Is there anything that has a tendency to render men haughty and vain more than the assumption of exercising [[heaven|Heaven]]ly power, of possessing a sacred character, of [[existence|being]] the messengers of the Most High?" (ch. CLXXXVIII).

The irony Holbach exposes is that theologians accuse unbelievers of pride — "it is pride, and a [[passion|desire]] to distinguish themselves, that makes [[atheism|atheist]]s" — while practicing the most extravagant pride themselves. The [[atheism|atheist]] who confesses ignorance about ultimate questions displays genuine humility; the theologian who claims to know the mind of [[god|God]] displays "frenzied pride." What theologians call humility before [[god|God]] is often pride before men, dressed in the language of submission.

## Historical Context

The analysis of pride as the root of [[vice|sin]] has a long theological pedigree. Augustine made pride the [[sin|original sin]]: it was pride that caused Lucifer's fall and [[adam|Adam]]'s disobedience. The medieval tradition elaborated this into a classification of pride as the deadliest of the seven deadly [[vice|sin]]s, the source from which all others flow.

[[enlightenment|The Enlightenment]] turned this analysis against religion itself. [[pierre-bayle|Bayle]] argued that theological certainty — the claim to possess [[revelation|revealed truth]] — was itself an expression of pride, [[vice|sin]]ce it required ignoring the limits of [[reason|human reason]]. [[baruch-spinoza|Spinoza]]'s critique of anthropomorphism in religion — the projection of human traits onto [[god|God]] — was essentially a critique of pride: man creates [[god|God]] in his own image and then worships the projection.

Holbach radicalizes this inversion. In his hands, the theological category of pride becomes the master expla[[nations|nation]] for [[persecution|religious persecution]]. The wars of religion, the burning of [[heresy|heretic]]s, the suppression of [[knowledge|science]] — all flow from the priest's unwillingness to admit that he might be wrong. Pride is not a [[vice|sin]] against [[god|God]] but a [[vice|sin]] against truth, and the first step toward truth is the humility of admitting ignorance.

## Core Arguments

**1. Pride is the constitutive vice of the [[clergy|priesthood]].** The claim to exercise [[divinity|divine]] power, to possess a sacred character, to be the messenger of the Most High — these are pride institutionalized. "What is more presumptuous than to arm [[nation|nations]] and cause rivers of blood, in order to establish or to defend futile conjectures?" (ch. CLXXXVIII).

**2. [[priest|Priest]]s project their own pride onto unbelievers.** The theological accusation that a[[divinity|theism]] is motivated by pride is itself an expression of pride. "Do not accuse of presumption and vanity those who have the [[vice|sin]]cerity to confess their ignorance" (ch. CLXXXVIII). The [[atheism|atheist]] who admits the limits of human knowledge is more [[mode|modes]]t than the theologian who claims certainty about the inconceivable.

**3. Pride makes religious disagreement violent.** Because the priest cannot admit error — to do so would undermine his claim to [[divinity|divine]] authority — disagreement must be met with force. "What other passion than frenzied pride can render men so ferocious, so vindictive, so devoid of toleration and gentleness?" (ch. CLXXXVIII).

**4. Humility before [[god|God]] is often pride before men.** The saint's self-abasement, his claim to be the most unworthy of [[vice|sin]]ners, his ostentatious renunciation — "Humility in a saint is, as a general rule, but a pride more refined than that of common men" (ch. CLXII).

## Relation to Other Concepts

Pride is the active, aggressive form of [[Vanity]] — vanity wishes to be admired, pride wishes to dominate. Together they constitute the psychological foundation of priestly [[Authority]]. Pride [[desire|drive]]s the [[cruelty|persecution]] of [[Doubt]] and the suppression of [[Reason]], because a proud mind cannot tolerate the suggestion that it might be wrong. The theological accusation that [[Atheism]] is motivated by pride inverts the true relationship: it is theological certainty, not skeptical inquiry, that expresses the will to dominate. Pride is also the enemy of [[Wisdom]], which begins with the acknowledgment of ignorance.

## In the Text

On pride as the constitutive vice of the [[clergy|priesthood]]:

> "Pride and vanity always were and always will be the inherent vices of the priesthood. Is there anything that has a tendency to render men haughty and vain more than the assumption of exercising Heavenly power, of possessing a sacred character, of being the messengers of the Most High?" (ch. CLXXXVIII)

On the projection of pride onto unbelievers:

> "Do not accuse of presumption and vanity those who have the sincerity to confess their ignorance; accuse not of folly those who find it impossible to believe in contradictions. You should blush at the thought of exciting the hatred of the people and the vengeance of the sovereigns against men who do not think as you do upon a Being of whom you have no idea yourselves." (ch. CLXXXVIII)

On the pride hidden in saintly humility:

> "Humility in a saint is, as a general rule, but a pride more refined than that of common men. It must be a very ridiculous vanity which can determine a man to continually war with his own nature!" (ch. CLXII)
