# Malice

## Definition

For Holbach, *malice* is the moral quality that any honest exami[[nations|nation]] of [[earth|the world]] must attribute to a [[god|deity]] who is claimed to be both omnipotent and benevolent. "If He can, and will not, He is guilty of malice which we can not attribute to Him" (ch. II). The presence of [[evil|suffering]] and [[injustice|evil]] in [[earth|the world]] creates an inescapable dilemma: either [[god|God]] is not omnipotent, or [[god|God]] is not benevolent. [[theology|Theology]]'s attempts to escape this dilemma consistently fail, leaving malice as the inescapable conclusion.

Holbach does not beli[[eve]] that [[god|God]] is actually malicious — he believes that no [[god|God]] exists. Rather, his argument is a *reductio ad [[absurdity|absurd]]um*: the concept of a perfect [[god|God]], when confronted with the reality of [[evil|suffering]], forces the conclusion that [[god|God]] is malicious. [[sin|Sin]]ce this conclusion is incompatible with [[god|God]]'s supposed perfection, the premise — that [[god|God]] exists — must be false. "Far from forming a perfect [[god|God]], the theologians have made the most imperfect of [[existence|being]]s" (ch. II).

The concept of malice in Holbach's argument serves as the logical terminus of [[injustice|theodicy]]'s failure. Every attempt to reconcile [[goodness|divine goodness]] with the existence of [[injustice|evil]] either limits [[god|God]]'s power, denies the reality of [[injustice|evil]], or [[attribute|attributes]] [[injustice|evil]] to human [[liberty|free will]] — and each of these moves generates further contradictions that ultimately reduce to the attribution of malice.

## Historical Context

The problem of [[injustice|evil]] — how to reconcile a benevolent, omnipotent [[god|God]] with the existence of [[evil|suffering]] — was already ancient in Holbach's time. [[epicurus|Epicurus]] formulated the classical dilemma in the 3rd century BCE: "Either [[god|God]] [[desire|want]]s to prevent [[injustice|evil]] and cannot, or He can and will not." Augustine's free-will defense and [[gottfried-leibniz|Leibniz]]'s *Theodicy* (1710) were the most influential [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian responses, both arguing that [[injustice|evil]] is compatible with [[god|God]]'s goodness because [[injustice|evil]] is a necessary consequence of [[liberty|free will]] or the best possible [[universe|world]].

The [[lisbon|Lisbon]] earthquake of 1755 shattered Enlightenment optimism about [[providence|divine providence]]. [[voltaire|Voltaire]]'s *Poem on the [[lisbon|Lisbon]] Disaster* and *Candide* (1759) savagely satirized [[gottfried-leibniz|Leibniz]]ian optimism. Holbach went further: he argued that [[evil|the problem of evil]] is not merely an intellectual puzzle but a decisive refutation of [[divinity|theism]]. No amount of theological ingenuity can make a [[existence|being]] who permits infinite [[evil|suffering]] into a [[existence|being]] worthy of worship.

Holbach's innovation was to treat malice not as a difficulty for [[religion|theology]] to solve but as the inevitable shadow of the theological system. The [[demon|devil]], he observed, "was invented to remove all suspicion of malice from [[divinity|Divinity]]" (ch. II), but this invention only proves the powerlessness of its celestial adversary.

## Core Arguments

**1. The dilemma of [[injustice|evil]] admits no escape.** "Either [[god|God]] [[desire|want]]s to prevent [[injustice|evil]], and can not, or He can and will not; or He neither can nor will, or He will and can." (ch. II). Every option leads to a conclusion incompatible with [[perfection|divine perfection]]. The "He can and will not" branch leads directly to malice.

**2. The [[demon|devil]] is a transparent evasion.** "The [[demon|devil]], [[existence|being]] invented to remove all suspicion of malice from [[divinity|Divinity]], proves to us at [[eve]]ry moment the powerlessness or the awkwardness of his celestial Adversary." (ch. II). Inventing a second supernatural agent to take the blame for [[injustice|evil]] does not solve the problem — it merely duplicates it, [[vice|sin]]ce an omnipotent [[god|God]] could destroy the [[demon|devil]] instantly.

**3. The state of [[earth|the world]] refutes [[divinity|divine]] [[goodness|benevolence]].** "You must conclude that your [[god|God]] has more malice than goodness, or you must admit that [[god|God]] was compelled to do what He has done, without [[existence|being]] able to do otherwise." (ch. II). The observable [[universe|world]], with its diseases, disasters, and injustices, is incompatible with [[government|governance]] by a [[existence|being]] who is both perfectly good and all-powerful.

**4. [[theology|Theology]] makes [[god|God]] into a monster.** "[[god|God]] resembles a tyrant who, having put out the eyes of the greater part of his slaves, threw them into a labyrinth, where he has taken the malicious pleasure of watching them wander." (ch. II). The theological [[god|God]] is not a loving father but a cosmic [[despotism|despot]] whose actions, judged by any honest [[model|standard]], are crueler than those of the worst human tyrants.

## Relation to Other Concepts

Malice is the direct consequence of the theological paradox explored in [[Evil]] and [[Cruelty]]. Where [[Punishment]] claims [[justice|divine justice]], malice exposes the sadistic character of eternal [[torture|torment]]. The concept connects to [[Injustice]] — divine malice is the ultimate injustice, [[vice|sin]]ce it proceeds from a [[existence|being]] who claims to be perfectly just. [[Vanity]] and [[Pride]] in human [[existence|being]]s create gods who mirror human vices, including malice, and project them onto the [[universe|cosmos]].

## In the Text

On the dilemma of [[injustice|evil]]:

> "Either God wants to prevent evil, and can not, or He can and will not; or He neither can nor will, or He will and can. If He wants to, without the power, He is impotent; if He can, and will not, He is guilty of malice which we can not attribute to Him." (ch. II)

On [[god|God]] as a monster:

> "God so perfidious and wicked as to create a single man and leave him exposed to the perils of damnation, can not be regarded as a perfect being, but as a monster of nonsense, injustice, malice, and atrocity." (ch. II)

On the [[demon|devil]] as evasion:

> "Manicheism is evidently the support of all religions; but unfortunately the devil, being invented to remove all suspicion of malice from Divinity, proves to us at every moment the powerlessness or the awkwardness of his celestial Adversary." (ch. II)
