# Adam

## Biography

In the Book of Genesis, Adam is the first human [[existence|being]], formed by [[god|God]] from the dust of the earth and [[soul|anima]]ted by [[divinity|divine]] breath. Placed in the Garden of Eden with his companion Eve, he is forbidden to eat from the tree of the knowledge of good and [[injustice|evil]]. Tempted by a [[serpent]] — later identified in [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian tradition with Satan — Eve eats the fruit and gives it to Adam, who also eats. For this [[crime|transgression]], [[god|God]] curses the [[serpent]], subjects Eve to the [[suffering|pain]]s of childbirth and to the rule of her husband, and condemns Adam to a life of toil ending in death: "for dust thou art, and unto dust shalt thou return" (Genesis 3:19). [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian [[religion|theology]], following [[paul|Paul]]'s Epistle to the Romans, interprets Adam's [[guilt|sin]] as the [[sin|original sin]] that corrupted all human [[universe|nature]] and made all subsequent humanity liable to death and [[hell|damnation]], a state from which only the redemptive sacrifice of [[jesus-christ|Jesus Christ]] can rescue the believer.

## Role in Religious History

Adam occupies a foundational position in the theological systems of Judaism, [[christianity|Christianity]], and [[islam|Islam]]. His narrative serves as the expla[[nations|nation]] for why a [[universe|world]] created by a good [[god|God]] is filled with [[evil|suffering]], death, and [[evil|moral evil]]. The doctrine of [[sin|original sin]] — developed most fully by Augustine of Hippo in the fourth and fifth centuries — made Adam's [[guilt|sin]]gle act of disobedience the pivot on which the entire economy of salvation turns: one man's [[guilt|sin]] brought universal condem[[nations|nation]]; one man's [[obedience|obedience]] ([[jesus-christ|Christ]]) brings universal [[salvation|redemption]]. The historical existence of Adam as a literal individual was treated as an essential article of [[religion|faith]] by [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian orthodoxy well into the nineteenth century, and [[eve]]n into the twentieth by Catholic [[doctrine|teaching]] on monogenism — the doctrine that all humanity descends from a [[guilt|sin]]gle pair.

## Connection to "Common Sense"

Holbach mentions Adam and Eve in his sustained argument against the credibility of biblical [[miracle|miracles]]. His treatment is brief but strategically placed in a passage that asks why an omnipotent and benevolent [[god|God]] would perform trivial [[miracle|wonder]]s while neglecting far more urgent moral interventions:

> "It is claimed that He sent to the first beings of the human race, Adam and Eve, a devil, or a simple serpent, to seduce them, and by this means ruin all men. This is not credible! It is claimed, that by a special providence, He prevented the King of Gerais, a Pagan, from committing sin with a strange woman, although there would be no results to follow; and yet He did not prevent Adam and Eve from offending Him and falling into the sin of disobedience — a sin which, according to our Christ-worshipers was to be fatal, and cause the destruction of the human race. This is not credible!"
>
> (002-498)

Holbach's point is one of moral proportionality. The [[god|God]] of the [[bible|Bible]] is described as preventing a pagan [[monarch|king]] from a minor sexual [[crime|transgression]] — an act with no lasting consequence — while standing by as the first couple commits an act that, on the [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian account, would damn [[eve]]ry human [[existence|being]] who [[eve]]r lived. The disproportion is so extreme that, for Holbach, it refutes the narrative: no [[existence|being]] of infinite [[philosophy|wisdom]] and goodness would arrange the history of [[earth|the world]] in this way. The story is not a revelation; it is a [[mythology|myth]].

## Key Ideas Attributed / Critiqued

**1. The [[contradiction|incoherence]] of [[sin|original sin]].** Holbach's critique draws out the logical implications that theologians evade: if [[god|God]] created Adam, [[god|God]] knew Adam would fall; if [[god|God]] knew, [[god|God]] could have prevented it; if [[god|God]] did not prevent it, [[god|God]] willed the fall and the consequent [[hell|damnation]] of billions. The doctrine of [[sin|original sin]], by attempting to explain [[injustice|evil]], ends by attributing it to the very [[god|God]] it claims to exonerate.

**2. Seduction by a [[serpent]].** Holbach's scornful phrasing — "a [[demon|devil]], or a simple [[serpent]]" — highlights the mythological character of the narrative. The idea that the eternal destiny of [[eve]]ry human [[existence|being]] hangs on a conversation between a woman and a tal[[monarch|king]] snake in a garden is, for Holbach, self-evidently fabulous. It belongs to the same category as the myths of [[paganism|pagan]] antiquity, not to a rational account of human origins.

**3. The problem of [[original-sin|inherited guilt]].** Implicit in Holbach's treatment is the moral objection to [[original-sin|inherited guilt]]. A just judge does not punish children for the crimes of their parents. Yet the doctrine of [[sin|original sin]] does exactly that — it makes [[eve]]ry newborn infant guilty of a [[guilt|sin]] committed millennia before their birth and condemns them to eternal [[torture|torment]] unless the remedy of baptism and [[religion|faith]] is applied.

## Legacy

The figure of Adam has undergone a dramatic reinterpretation in the modern era. Advances in evolutionary biology, genetics, and paleoanthropology have made a literal reading of the Genesis creation narrative untenable for most educated people. The [[catholic-church|Catholic Church]], under Pope Pius XII in *Humani Generis* (1950), allowed that the human body might have evolved from pre-existing living matter, while insisting on the special creation of each human soul and the historical reality of a first pair. By the late twentieth and early twenty-first centuries, however, the scientific [[proof|evidence]] for a human population bottleneck no smaller than several thousand individuals made monogenism increasingly diffi[[worship|cult]] to maintain, and many theologians have reinterpreted Adam as a literary or symbolic figure rather than a historical individual. From Holbach's eighteenth-century vantage point, the literal Adam was already an embarrassment to [[reason|reason]]; the subsequent history of [[knowledge|science]] has only reinforced his critique.
