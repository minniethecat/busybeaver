## Definition

In Holbach's usage, "genius" denotes the exceptional individual who, through superior intellect and [[courage|moral courage]], penetrates the illusions maintained by [[priest|priestcraft]] and political [[tyranny|despotism]] and dares to communicate the truths thus discovered to humanity. Genius is not merely a matter of raw intelligence; it requires the [[courage|fortitude]] to withstand [[cruelty|persecution]], the patience to construct arguments that the ordinary mind can follow, and the [[charity|benevolence]] to labor for the good of a species that will often reward its benefactors with ingratitude or violence.

Holbach's conception of genius is embedded in his materialist anthropology. Genius is not a supernatural gift or [[divinity|divine]] inspiration; it is a rare but natural combi[[nations|nation]] of temperament, education, and circumstance. The genius differs from ordinary persons in degree, not in kind — just as some bodies are healthier or stronger than others, some minds are more penetrating and resolute.

## Historical Context

The eighteenth century was preoccupied with the [[universe|nature]] and social role of genius. The word itself shifted meaning during the period: from denoting a tutelary [[soul|spirit]] or innate disposition, it came to designate the creative individual of transcendent ability. Diderot's article "Génie" in the *Encyclopédie* (1757) defined genius as "the breadth of mind, the power of imagi[[nations|nation]], and the activity of the soul" that enables discoveries inaccessible to ordinary intellects.

Holbach's account is less romantic than Diderot's. For Holbach, genius is primarily the capacity to see through error — a critical rather than a creative faculty. The greatest geniuses, in his pantheon, are not artists or poets but philosophers and scientists who have liberated humanity from superstition: [[epicurus|Epicurus]], Lucretius, [[hobbes|Hobbes]], [[baruch-spinoza|Spinoza]], [[pierre-bayle|Bayle]]. Their genius consisted not in creating beautiful fictions but in destroying harmful ones.

## Core Arguments

1. **Genius is a natural phenomenon.** There is nothing supernatural about intellectual excellence. It results from favorable combi[[nation|nations]] of biological endowment, education, and historical circumstance — causes that are in principle fully natural, [[eve]]n if in practice rare.

2. **Genius serves humanity by exposing error.** The history of [[progress|human progress]] is largely the history of geniuses who identified a widely held falsehood and [[demon|demons]]trated its falsity. Copernicus showed that the earth moves; [[baruch-spinoza|Spinoza]] showed that the [[bible|Bible]] is a human document; Holbach hoped to show that religion is a human invention.

3. **Genius is systematically persecuted by religious and political authorities.** The same institutions that benefit from human ignorance — [[clergy|priesthood]]s and [[tyranny|despotism]]s — recognize genius as a threat and seek to suppress it. [[socrates|Socrates]] was executed, Galileo was forced to recant, and freethinkers in Holbach's own circle published anonymously to avoid imprisonment.

4. **The benefits of genius are [[eve]]ntually irreversible.** Once a truth has been [[demon|demons]]trated and disseminated, it cannot be permanently suppressed. Copernican astronomy survived [[inquisition|the Inquisition]]; the Enlightenment survived the censorship of the *ancien régime*. Holbach was optimistic that his own critique of religion would likewise prevail.

## In the Text

Holbach acknowledges the difficulty genius faces:

> "Useful truths bring to those who announce them chains, sufferings, tortures, and death. But their utility survives the persecution of their defenders." (ch. CLX)

He describes the gradual work of [[education|enlightenment]]:

> "Experience and reason as their guides, men of genius have at last dared to shake off the chains of prejudice." (ch. CLXV)

And he identifies the social mechanism by which truth propagates:

> "Truth is like fire; it sparkles, it spreads, it communicates itself, it finally illuminates the whole horizon." (ch. CLXII)
