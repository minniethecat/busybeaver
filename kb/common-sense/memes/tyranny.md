# Tyranny

## Definition

Tyranny, in Holbach's political analysis, is the concentration of unchecked power in the hands of [[monarch|ruler]]s who govern without the consent of the governed and who employ religion as an instrument of social control. It is not merely a defective form of government but the natural political expression of a society in which reason has been suppressed and the people reduced to intellectual dependency. "Tyranny has been, and will always be, the chief source of the corruption of manners and the calamities of [[nation|nations]]." (ch. II). The claim is sweeping and causal: [[despotism|tyranny]] is not one political [[injustice|evil]] among many but the primary generator of social pathology.

## Historical Context

Holbach wrote under the French *ancien régime*, an [[sovereign|absolute monarch]]y supported by an established [[catholic-church|Catholic Church]] — the very alliance of throne and altar his philosophy attacks. Louis XV's reign (1715–1774) had seen the consolidation of royal absolutism alongside persistent ecclesiastical privilege. Censorship was active: Holbach published anonymously and through foreign presses. His contemporary Rousseau offered a different critique in *The Social Contract* (1762), arguing that legitimate government required the general will; Montesquieu's *The [[spirit|Spirit]] of the [[law|Law]]s* (1748) had distinguished [[monarch|monarchy]] from despotism. Holbach collapses that distinction: all arbitrary rule is [[despotism|tyranny]], and religion is its indispensable ally.

## Core Arguments

**1. [[religion|Religion]] and [[despotism|tyranny]] form a symbiotic alliance.** Holbach's analysis is structural, not merely polemical. [[priest|Priest]]s and tyrants need each other: "[[clergy|priests]] deceive them, tyrants corrupt them in order to subjugate them more easily." (ch. II). [[religion|Religion]] provides the ideological justification for absolutism ([[sovereign|divine right of kings]]); absolutism provides the coercive apparatus that protects ecclesiastical power. Neither could survive alone.

**2. Tyranny depends on the suppression of reason.** The tyrant, like the priest, fears an educated populace: "Thus man was, and always remained, a child without experience, a slave without courage, a loggerhead who feared to reason." (ch. II). [[mind|Intellect]]ual dependency and political subjugation are two sides of the same coin.

**3. The tyrant is morally indistinguishable from the theological [[god|God]].** In one of Holbach's most provocative arguments, he draws a direct analogy: "According to theological ideas, [[god|God]] resembles a tyrant who, suspicious and cruel, avaricious of His benefactions and prodigal of His scourges." (ch. II). The [[god|God]] who rules by arbitrary decree, punishes dissent eternally, and demands unquestioning obedience is the celestial model for earthly [[despotism|tyranny]]. To worship such a [[god|God]] is to internalize the values of despotism.

**4. Tyranny is self-perpetuating through corruption.** "Tyranny has been, and will always be, the chief source of the corruption of manners." Tyranny does not merely rule over a corrupted people — it actively corrupts them, creating the intellectual and moral conditions for its own continuation.

## Reception and Critique

Holbach's linkage of religion and [[despotism|tyranny]] influenced the radical wing of [[revolution|the French Revolution]], particularly the Hébertists and the de-[[jesus-christ|Christ]]ianization campaign. More moderate revolutionaries (Mirabeau, Lafayette) sought [[temperament|constitution]]al [[monarch|monarchy]] rather than the abolition Holbach's logic implied. In the nineteenth century, Bakunin and other anarchists extended Holbach's critique: not only religion-allied [[despotism|tyranny]] but [[government|the state]] as such was the enemy. Marx inverted the causal priority: for historical [[matter|materialism]], the economic base determines the political and ideological superstructure — religion is not the cause of [[despotism|tyranny]] but its symptom.

## Relation to Other Concepts

Tyranny is the political manifestation of the epistemological darkness that [[Ignorance]] and [[Superstition]] produce. It is opposed to [[Liberty]], which requires the free exercise of [[Reason]], and to [[Justice]], which arbitrary power cannot deliver. Tyranny depends on the suppression of [[Science]] and [[Education]], and is sustained by the [[Priesthood]], whose alliance with the throne Holbach repeatedly attacks. The tyrant, in Holbach's typology, is the secular counterpart of [[God]] as conceived by [[religion|theology]].

## In the Text

On the alliance of [[clergy|priests]] and tyrants:

> "Priests deceive them, tyrants corrupt them in order to subjugate them more easily. Tyranny has been, and will always be, the chief source of the corruption of manners and the calamities of nations." (ch. II)

On the intellectual subjugation that enables [[despotism|tyranny]]:

> "Thus man was, and always remained, a child without experience, a slave without courage, a loggerhead who feared to reason, and who could never profit by his experience." (ch. II)

On the theological [[god|God]] as the archetype of [[despotism|tyranny]]:

> "According to theological ideas, God resembles a tyrant who, suspicious and cruel, avaricious of His benefactions and prodigal of His scourges... The most sanguinary tyrants, the most ambitious conquerors, would be but feeble images of this God of theology." (ch. II)

On [[despotism|tyranny]] as the primary source of social corruption:

> "Tyranny has been, and will always be, the chief source of the corruption of manners and the calamities of nations." (ch. II)
