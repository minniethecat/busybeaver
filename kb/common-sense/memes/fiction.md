## Definition

In Holbach's lexicon, "fiction" is not merely a literary category but a philosophical verdict. A theological fiction is any doctrine that presents itself as [[revelation|revealed truth]] but has no basis in [[universe|nature]], reason, or verifiable experience.

The doctrine of the [[trinity|Trinity]], the belief in transubstantiation, the claim that a [[god|deity]] became incarnate in a virgin's womb — all are, for Holbach, fictions in the strict sense: stories invented by human [[existence|being]]s and attributed to [[divinity|divine]] authorship.

The word carries a polemical charge. To call a [[belief|religious belief]] a "fiction" is to assert that it was fabricated — that identifiable human interests, not [[divinity|divine]] communication, produced it.

Holbach's use of the term is part of his larger genetic critique of religion: rather than as[[monarch|king]] whether a doctrine is true, he asks how and why it came to be believed. The answer, consistently, is that [[clergy|priests]] invented doctrines to secure their authority and wealth.

## Historical Context

The charge that religion consists of pious fictions has ancient roots. Classical skeptics like Lucretius had described religion as a fabrication that exploits human fear.

In Holbach's era, the critique was revived with new intellectual resources: biblical criticism had exposed the human authorship of [[bible|scripture]]; comparative anthropology had revealed the diversity and mutual contradiction of [[earth|the world]]'s religious fictions; and the [[materialism|materialist philosophy]] of mind had explained how imaginary [[existence|being]]s come to seem real to those who have been taught to fear them from childhood.

The specific phrase "adore [[god|God]] is to adore a fiction" (rendered as a chapter title in the English translation) condenses this tradition into a [[guilt|sin]]gle provocative formula. It was designed to shock pious readers out of their unexamined assumptions — and it did.

## Core Arguments

1. **Religious doctrines are fictions because they lack empirical warrant.** No one has observed a [[trinity|Trinity]], witnessed a transubstantiation, or verified a virgin birth. These doctrines are accepted on the authority of [[clergy|priests]] and texts, not on the [[proof|evidence]] of the senses.

2. **The [[sacred|sacredness]] of a fiction does not make it true.** Holbach acknowledges that religious fictions are held with deep [[guilt|sin]]cerity and [[passion|emotion]]al investment. But the intensity of belief has no bearing on the truth of what is believed. Millions of people have [[guilt|sin]]cerely believed false things.

3. **Fictions serve identifiable social functions.** The fiction of [[punishment|hell]] deters crime more cheaply than a police force. The fiction of [[divine-right|divine right]] legitimates monarchs. The fiction of [[sin|original sin]] makes people feel perpetually indebted to [[clergy|the clergy]] who claim to remedy it. Holbach's analysis of these functions anticipates later sociological critiques of religion.

4. **To adore a fiction is to waste human energy.** [[worship|Worship]] directed at imaginary [[existence|being]]s is worship diverted from real human needs. The time, wealth, and [[passion|emotion]]al devotion spent on fictional [[gods|deities]] could be spent on education, [[knowledge|science]], and the alleviation of [[evil|suffering]].

## Reception and Critique

Holbach's use of "fiction" as a philosophical verdict on religious doctrines has been both influential and contested.

Within the radical Enlightenment tradition, the term became a [[model|standard]] weapon in the anticlerical arsenal — a way of redescribing religious claims that refused to grant them [[eve]]n the dignity of [[existence|being]] "false" (which would imply they were meaningful propositions that happened to be untrue) and insisted instead that they were fabrications from the start.

Nineteenth-century critics of religion, from Ludwig Feuerbach to Karl Marx, extended Holbach's genetic approach without adopting his terminology. Feuerbach's *The [[essence|Essence]] of [[christianity|Christianity]]* (1841) argued that religious doctrines are projections of human [[attribute|attributes]] onto an imaginary [[god|deity]] — a more sophisticated version of Holbach's fiction thesis.

Marx's famous description of religion as "the opium of the people" preserved Holbach's emphasis on the functional role of religious fictions while embedding it in a materialist theory of history that Holbach lacked.

Theological responses to the "fiction" charge have generally taken two forms. The first — associated with Friedrich Schleiermacher and liberal Protestantism — concedes the mythological character of many religious narratives while insisting that they express genuine religious experience that cannot be reduced to priestly fabrication.

The second — associated with Karl Barth and neo-orthodoxy — refuses the Enlightenment's epistemological premises entirely, arguing that [[revelation|divine revelation]] is self-authenticating and cannot be judged by the [[model|standard]]s of [[reason|human reason]].

In literary theory, Holbach's concept of fiction has been complicated by poststructuralist critiques of the fact/fiction distinction. Thinkers like Hayden White and Michel de Certeau have argued that all historical writing — including the secular, scientific historiography that Holbach championed — employs narrative conventions that are "fictional" in a broad sense.

The claim that [[religion|theology]] is fiction while [[knowledge|science]] is fact depends, on this view, on a dichotomy that is itself a rhetorical construction.

Contemporary cognitive [[knowledge|science]] of religion has offered a different kind of complication. Research on the naturalness of [[belief|religious belief]] suggests that supernatural concepts arise spontaneously from ordinary cognitive processes — they are not "invented" by [[clergy|priests]] in any straightforward sense, [[eve]]n if priestly institutions subsequently elaborate and exploit them.

Holbach's genetic critique, in this light, oversimplifies the causal story: religion is not simply imposed from above but emerges from deep structures of human cognition.

## Relation to Other Concepts

Fiction, in Holbach's sense, is the master category under which most specific religious doctrines fall. [[god]], [[supernatural]], [[revelation]], [[miracle]], [[hell]], and [[original-sin]] are all, for Holbach, fictions — human inventions masquerading as [[divinity|divine]] truths.

The concept of fiction thus organizes Holbach's entire critical project: to [[demon|demons]]trate that religious doctrines are fabricated is to [[demon|demons]]trate that they carry no authority.

Fiction is closely related to [[priestcraft]], the institutional mechanism by which fictions are produced and maintained. If fiction is the product, [[priest|priestcraft]] is the production process — the systematic fabrication of religious doctrines for the purpose of social control. The two concepts are complementary and mutually reinforcing.

The concept also relates to [[imagination]], the mental faculty that produces fictions.

Holbach does not condemn imagi[[nations|nation]] as such — he recognizes its role in art, literature, and scientific hypothesis — but he argues that when imagi[[nations|nation]] operates unchecked by reason and [[proof|evidence]], it generates the monsters and [[imagination|chimeras]] that populate [[earth|the world]]'s religions.

Fiction stands in opposition to [[truth]], which for Holbach is established by reason and [[evidence|empirical evidence]]. The criterion that distinguishes truth from fiction is verifiability: true claims can be tested against [[universe|nature]] and experience; fictions cannot. This epistemological criterion is the foundation of Holbach's entire critique of religion.

Finally, fiction relates to [[consolation]], one of the functions that Holbach acknowledges religious fictions can serve.

He does not deny that believing in [[god|God]], [[hope|heaven]], and providence can be psychologically comforting. But he insists that false [[hope|consolation]] is worse than honest despair — that building one's life on fictions, however comforting, is a form of self-[[imposture|deception]] that ultimately deepens the [[evil|suffering]] it pretends to alleviate.

## In the Text

The chapter title itself is the thesis:

> "To Adore God Is to Adore a Fiction." (ch. XXVIII)
>
> (028-001)

Holbach draws out the consequences with characteristic bluntness:

> "It is pretended that even if it should be a fiction, it is advantageous, or at least consoling, to believe in a God. But is the fiction useful? Are the nations in whose minds this fiction is established remarkable for the morality of their conduct?" (ch. XXVIII)

He connects the concept of fiction to the political function of [[priest|priestcraft]]:

> "The ministers of religion have taken great care to make of their God a terrible, capricious, and changeable tyrant; it was necessary for them that He should be thus in order that He might lend Himself to their various interests." (ch. XCVII)
>
> (002-316)
