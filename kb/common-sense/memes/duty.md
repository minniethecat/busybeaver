# Duty

## Definition

In *Common Sense*, Holbach radically re-conceives duty as a secular and natural phenomenon. Rather than obligations imposed by a [[divinity|divine]] lawgiver, duties are the necessary relations that arise among sensible [[existence|being]]s who live in society and seek their own welfare. The concept of duty is stripped of its theological foundation and rebuilt upon the observable facts of human [[universe|nature]]: the [[passion|desire]] for pleasure, the aversion to [[suffering|pain]], and the need for social cooperation. Holbach argues that "if there are no duties between man and his [[god|God]], there exists no religion for man" (ch. 7), using the very idea of duty to undermine the possibility of religion itself — for an infinite [[god|God]] can have no reciprocal relations with finite [[existence|being]]s, and where there are no relations, there can be no duties.

## Historical Context

The 18th-century Enlightenment saw a concerted effort to ground [[morality|ethics]] in reason and human [[universe|nature]] rather than revelation. Thinkers such as [[david-hume|Hume]], Diderot, and Helvétius sought to [[demon|demons]]trate that moral obligations could exist independently of [[belief|religious belief]]. Holbach's treatment of duty extends this project to its most radical conclusion: not only is [[god|God]] unnecessary for morality, but the very concept of duties *toward* [[god|God]] is logically incoherent. This position placed him among the most audacious materialist philosophers of the French Enlightenment, directly challenging the established alliance between throne and altar that grounded political obligation in [[divinity|divine]] right.

## Core Arguments

Holbach advances several interconnected arguments concerning duty:

1. **The Impossibility of Duties Toward [[god|God]]**: An infinite [[existence|being]] cannot have relations with finite [[existence|being]]s. Where there are no relations, there can be no duties. Therefore, religious duty is a contradiction in terms (ch. 7).

2. **Duties Arise from [[nature|Nature]], Not [[divinity|Divinity]]**: "The rules which govern men's conduct spring from their own [[universe|nature]], which they are supposed to know, and not from the [[theology-index|Divine]] [[universe|nature]], of which they have no conception" (ch. 171). [[obligation|Moral obligation]] is a function of human sensibility and social existence.

3. **The Social Foundation of Duty**: Duties exist among sensible [[existence|being]]s living in society who are "compelled to despise those who do them no good, and to detest those who oppose their happiness" (ch. 171). Duty is social reciprocity, not [[divinity|divine]] commandment.

4. **[[religion|Religion]] Corrupts True Duties**: By substituting "chimerical duties" toward [[god|God]] for real obligations to fellow humans, religion makes people "inhuman toward all those who are not of their own way of thin[[monarch|king]]" (ch. 148). Religious duty often contradicts social duty.

5. **Duty and Self-[[interest|Interest]] Converge**: To preserve oneself and flourish in society, one must abstain from excess, do good to others, and avoid harming them — all without needing a "[[god|God]] whom they do not know" or "chimerical fears" (ch. 171).

## Reception and Critique

Holbach's secular theory of duty was received as scandalous by religious authorities but influential among radical Enlightenment circles. Critics objected that without [[divinity|divine]] sanction, moral obligation loses its binding force — the familiar charge that a[[divinity|theism]] leads to moral nihilism. Holbach anticipated this objection, arguing that the sanctions of [[honor|social esteem]], self-preservation, and the natural consequences of action are more real and effective than promises of [[hope|heaven]] or threats of [[punishment|hell]]. Later utilitarian thinkers, particularly Bentham and Mill, would develop similar naturalistic accounts of obligation, though without always acknowledging their debt to Holbach's pioneering formulation.

## Relation to Other Concepts

Duty in Holbach's system is tightly interwoven with several companion concepts. [[morality]] is the broader framework of which duty is the operational component — the specific obligations that arise from moral relations. [[virtue]] names the disposition to fulfill one's duties consistently. [[society]] provides the context in which duties become meaningful, for isolated individuals have no duties. The concept stands in opposition to [[religion]], which Holbach saw as inventing fictitious duties to [[god|God]] that displace real duties to humanity. It also relates to [[conscience]], which Holbach reinterpreted not as the voice of [[god|God]] but as the internalized awareness of social obligations.

## In the Text

Holbach's most concentrated treatment of duty appears in chapter 171, where he argues that moral obligation requires no [[divinity|divine]] lawgiver:

> "The law that compels man not to harm himself, is inherent in the nature of a sensible being, who, no matter how he came into this world, or what can be his fate in another, is compelled by his very nature to seek his welfare and to shun evil, to love pleasure and to fear pain." (ch. 171)

The logical argument against religious duty appears in chapter 7, where [[infinite|the infinite]]-finite gap renders all [[god|God]]-man relations impossible:

> "If God is infinite, no finite being can have communication or any relation with Him. Where there are no relations, there can be no union, no correspondence, no duties. If there are no duties between man and his God, there exists no religion for man." (ch. 7)

Holbach also notes how religion distorts the [[knowledge|understanding]] of true duties:

> "[Religion] has made it their sacred duty to torment, to persecute, to destroy those whose opinions are different from those of the priests. It has forced the same citizens to be continually unjust toward each other, and to rend each other's hearts for the sake of opinions of which neither they nor their guides comprehend anything." (ch. 148)
