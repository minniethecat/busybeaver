# Sacrament

## Definition

In Holbach's critique, a sacrament is a religious [[ritual|ceremony]] that claims to confer supernatural grace through material means — bread, wine, water, oil — but in reality serves only to reinforce priestly authority over the credulous. The sacraments are the paradigmatic case of "puerile ceremonies" that Holbach urges humanity to abandon in favor of "natural things, intelligible objects, sensible truths."

Holbach does not offer a systematic [[religion|theology]] of the sacraments; instead, he attacks them through two powerful strategies: the autobiographical testimony of a priest who despised the [[ritual|rites]] he performed, and the exposure of gospel contradictions that undermine any claim of [[divinity|divine]] institution.

## Holbach's Critique

**1. The performing priest despises the rite.** The autobiographical voice of [[meslier|Jean Meslier]] — the priest whose posthumous testament Holbach published — provides the most damning [[proof|evidence]] against the sacraments. Meslier describes "that superstitious [[mass|Mass]], and those ridiculous administrations of sacraments, especially if I was compelled to perform them with the solemnity which awakened all your piety." The celebrant himself regards the [[ritual|ceremony]] as "ridiculous"; only the laity are deceived.

**2. Meslier's [[conscience|remorse]] reveals the [[charlatan|fraud]].** Meslier's confession that he felt "what [[conscience|remorse]] I had for exciting your [[belief|credulity]]" transforms the sacrament from a channel of grace into an instrument of psychological manipulation. The priest knowingly performs "pious lies" he "despised in my heart."

**3. [[gospel|Gospel]] accounts contradict each other on the [[mass|Eucharist]].** Holbach notes a devastating textual problem: "the first three Evangelists note that [[jesus-christ|Jesus Christ]] instituted the Sacrament of His body and His blood, in the form of [[transubstantiation|bread and wine]]" — yet "John does not mention this mysterious sacrament." Instead, John describes Jesus washing feet and delivering a discourse. If the central ritual of [[christianity|Christianity]] was truly instituted by [[jesus-christ|Christ]], the omission in the Fourth [[gospel|Gospel]] is inexplicable.

**4. Sacraments are instruments of social control.** Through the sacraments, [[clergy|priests]] "grow rich by your sweat and your [[suffering|pain]]s" while showing "disdain" for "their [[mysteries]] and their superstitions." The rituals create dependency while generating revenue.

## In the Text

Meslier's testimony on the [[mass|Mass]] and sacraments:

> "What a disdain I had for my ministry, and particularly for that superstitious Mass, and those ridiculous administrations of sacraments, especially if I was compelled to perform them with the solemnity which awakened all your piety and all your good faith."

On his [[conscience|remorse]] for performing the [[ritual|rites]]:

> "What remorse I had for exciting your credulity! A thousand times upon the point of bursting forth publicly, I was going to open your eyes."

On the gospel contradiction regarding the [[mass|Eucharist]]:

> "The first three Evangelists note that Jesus Christ instituted the Sacrament of His body and His blood, in the form of bread and wine, the same as our Roman Christ-worshipers say; and John does not mention this mysterious sacrament."

## Relation to Other Concepts

Sacrament is the ritual counterpart to [[priestcraft]] — the institutional system that sacraments sustain. It connects to [[mass]], [[piety]], [[credulity]], and [[superstition]]. Holbach contrasts sacramental religion with [[morality]], which requires no ceremonies, and [[nature]], which is the true object of rational reverence. The concept also links to [[meslier]] and [[remorse]].
