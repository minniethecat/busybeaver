# Effect

## Definition

For Holbach, the concept of effect is inseparable from that of cause, and together they form the backbone of his materialist determinism. Every phenomenon in [[universe|nature]], without exception, is the necessary effect of some antecedent cause or combi[[nations|nation]] of causes. "There is no effect without a cause" (ch. XXXVI) — this principle, which theologians themselves affirm, is the foundation of all rational inquiry. But Holbach insists on drawing the conclusion that theologians evade: if [[eve]]ry effect has a cause, then [[nature|the universe]] itself is not an effect of a [[divinity|divine]] cause but the cause of all effects within it.

The corollary is that there is no such thing as [[probability|chance]]. "[[nothingness|Nothing]] in [[universe|nature]] can be made by [[probability|chance]]; all follow fixed laws; these laws are but the necessary union of certain effects with their causes" (ch. XXXVI). What people call [[probability|chance]] is simply ignorance of the true causes at work. An atom colliding with another atom does so not by [[chance|accident]] but by the operation of immutable physical laws. The idea of [[probability|chance]] is a confession of ignorance dressed up as an expla[[nations|nation]].

This has profound theological implications. If [[eve]]ry [[eve]]nt is the necessary effect of natural causes, then [[miracle|miracles]] — defined as [[divinity|divine]] interventions that suspend the ordinary course of [[universe|nature]] — are impossible. A [[god|God]] who intervenes in [[universe|nature]] would be a [[god|God]] who breaks his own laws, which is to say, a [[god|God]] who acts irrationally. Holbach presses the point: the theologian who claims that [[god|God]] is the [[cause|first cause]] of all things must then explain why [[god|God]]'s creation is "full of imperfections" — why the effects attributed to [[wisdom|divine wisdom]] are indistinguishable from the effects of blind necessity.

## Historical Context

Holbach's determinism drew on a century of scientific and philosophical development. [[baruch-spinoza|Spinoza]] had argued that [[eve]]rything follows from the [[divinity|divine nature]] with the same necessity that the angles of a triangle follow from its definition. [[isaac-newton|Newton]] had shown that the motions of planets and falling bodies were governed by universal mathematical laws. Laplace would later crystallize the deterministic [[universe|world]]view in his famous thought experiment about a demon who, knowing the position and momentum of [[eve]]ry particle, could predict the entire future.

Holbach's innovation was to turn determinism into an anti-religious weapon. If [[nature|the universe]] is a closed system of causes and effects, there is no room for [[divinity|divine]] intervention, no "[[design|intelligent design]]" detectable in [[universe|nature]], and no [[liberty|free will]] in the theological sense — only the necessary operation of natural laws upon material bodies. This was [[matter|materialism]] with a polemical edge: the same [[knowledge|science]] that explained the physical [[universe|world]] explained away the need for a supernatural expla[[nations|nation]].

## Core Arguments

**1. The causal principle is universal and admits no exceptions.** "There is no effect without a cause" (ch. XXXVI) applies to [[eve]]rything — including human [[will|volition]], which theologians claim is free. No [[eve]]nt escapes the chain of [[cause|causality]]; to admit an uncaused [[eve]]nt is to abandon reason.

**2. [[chance|Chance]] is not a cause but a confession of ignorance.** "Men may use the word '[[probability|chance]]' to cover their ignorance of the true causes" (ch. XXXVI). The appearance of [[probability|randomness]] reflects only the limits of human knowledge, not a gap in the causal order.

**3. The universe is not an effect of [[god|God]] but the cause of all effects.** "The universe is not an effect; it is the cause of all effects; all the [[existence|being]]s it embraces are the necessary effects of this cause" (ch. XXXVI). The theological concept of a [[cause|first cause]] is unnecessary: [[universe|nature]] is self-causing and self-sustaining.

**4. [[necessity|Determinism]] demolishes [[miracle|miracles]] and [[liberty|free will]].** If [[eve]]ry [[eve]]nt is the necessary effect of natural causes, then [[divinity|divine]] intervention is impossible and human "[[liberty|free will]]" is — properly understood — the operation of internal causes ([[passion|desire]]s, beliefs, temperament) producing necessary effects (actions). Moral responsibility is compatible with determinism, because the causes that produce action include the character and reason of the agent.

## Relation to Other Concepts

Effect is the second term of the cause-effect pair that structures Holbach's entire metaphysics. It is intimately connected to [[Cause]] — the two are conceptually inseparable. [[necessity|Determinism]], the doctrine that all effects follow necessarily from their causes, is the foundation of [[Materialism]] and stands opposed to [[Free-Will]] in the libertarian sense. [[Nature]] is the totality of causes and effects operating according to fixed laws. [[Miracle]] is the theological concept that violates the cause-effect relationship by claiming effects without natural causes. [[Necessity]] is the mode by which causes produce their effects.

## In the Text

On the impossibility of [[probability|chance]]:

> "Nothing in nature can be made by chance; all follow fixed laws; these laws are but the necessary union of certain effects with their causes. An atom of matter does not meet another atom by accident or by hazard; this rencounter is due to permanent laws." (ch. XXXVI)

On [[nature|the universe]] as cause rather than effect:

> "The universe is not an effect; it is the cause of all effects; all the beings it embraces are the necessary effects of this cause which sometimes shows to us its manner of acting, but which often hides from us its way." (ch. XXXVI)

On the [[imposture|deception]] of [[probability|chance]]:

> "Men may use the word 'chance' to cover their ignorance of the true causes; nevertheless, although they may ignore them, these causes act, but by certain laws. There is no effect without a cause." (ch. XXXVI)
