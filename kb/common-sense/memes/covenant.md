# Covenant

## Definition

The covenant, in biblical [[religion|theology]], is the binding agreement between [[god]] and the people of [[israel]] — a pact in which [[god|God]] promises protection, prosperity, and territorial possession in exchange for exclusive worship and obedience. In Holbach's *Common Sense*, the covenant appears primarily through Meslier's *[[testament|Testament]]*, where it serves as a test case for the reliability of [[divinity|divine]] promises.

Meslier's analysis is simple and devastating: the covenant was supposed to be [[eternal|everlasting]] (APP004-014); its terms included perpetual possession of a promised land and the supremacy of the [[israel|Jewish people]] among the [[nation|nations]]; and none of these promises have been fulfilled (APP004-017). The [[israel|Jewish people]] have been scattered, persecuted, and exiled for centuries. The "everlasting covenant" has been empirically falsified, and a [[god|God]] whose promises fail is either not [[god|God]] or not truthful.

## Historical Context

The covenant with [[abraham]] and its renewal through [[moses]] at [[sin|Sin]]ai was the foundational narrative of Jewish identity. [[christianity|Christianity]] claimed to have superseded this covenant — the "new covenant" in [[jesus-christ|Christ]]'s blood rendering the old covenant obsolete. Meslier, a Catholic priest, was writing within a tradition that already treated the old covenant as abolished, but he radicalized this supersessionism into a critique of the very idea of divine covenant-ma[[monarch|king]].

If the old covenant was genuinely established by [[god|God]], its failure would prove either [[divinity|divine]] impotence or [[divinity|divine]] infidelity — neither acceptable to [[religion|theology]]. If it was not established by [[god|God]], then the foundational narrative of the Hebrew [[bible|Bible]] is a human fiction, and the new covenant that claims to fulfill it is fiction built upon fiction. Either way, the concept of a divine covenant collapses.

The historical situation of [[europe|Europe]]an Jewry in the eighteenth century made Meslier's argument politically charged. Jews in [[france|France]] lived under severe legal restrictions; their condition of dispersion and subordi[[nations|nation]] was visible [[eve]]rywhere. Meslier points to this visible condition as [[evidence|proof]] that [[god|God]]'s covenant promises have not been kept.

## Core Arguments

**1. The covenant's terms have been empirically falsified.** The promises were specific: the descendants of [[abraham|Abraham]] would be the "happiest, the holiest, and the most victorious of all the people of the earth," and they would "possess forever the country He should give them." The observable reality is the opposite: "they are now, and have been for a long time, scattered and dispersed over the whole earth, having no country, no dominion, no superiority."

**2. The "[[eternal|everlasting]]" covenant has [[demon|demons]]trably ended.** An everlasting covenant that no longer exists is a contradiction in terms. (APP004-017) "We do not see now, and we have never seen, any [[proof|evidence]] of this covenant; and, on the contrary, they have been for many centuries excluded from the possession of the small country they pretended [[god|God]] had promised them." The absence of [[proof|evidence]] for a covenant that was supposed to be permanent and visible is itself decisive [[proof|evidence]] against it.

**3. The failure of the old covenant undermines the new.** [[christianity|Christianity]] claims that the new covenant fulfills the old. But if the old covenant was never fulfilled — if its promises were never kept — then the new covenant "fulfills" a failed contract. A [[god|God]] who cannot keep his first covenant gives no reason to trust his second.

**4. [[theology-index|Divine]] covenants are a human invention to claim [[divinity|divine]] sanction for territorial ambition.** Meslier's subtext is political: the covenant narrative was constructed by a people see[[monarch|king]] to legitimize their claim to contested land by attributing the grant to [[god|God]]. Every conquering people has invented a [[divinity|divine]] mandate for its conquests. The biblical covenant is simply the most influential example of a universal [[model|pattern]].

## Reception and Critique

The concept of covenant was central to early modern political thought in ways that give Holbach's and Meslier's critique a resonance beyond biblical criticism. In seventeenth-century England, the idea of a covenant between [[god|God]] and a people — and, by analogy, between a [[monarch|sovereign]] and his subjects — was the theological foundation of both royalist and parliamentarian political theory.
[[hobbes|Thomas Hobbes]], in *[[matthew|Levi]]athan* (1651), had redefined the covenant in purely secular terms as the social contract by which individuals surrender natural liberty in exchange for the security provided by [[monarch|sovereign]] power. Robert Filmer, defending [[divinity|divine]]-right [[monarch|monarchy]], had argued that [[government|political authority]] descends from [[god|God]]'s covenant grant of dominion to [[adam|Adam]], not from any compact among men.
The English Civil War was fought, in substantial part, over competing interpretations of what a covenant with [[god|God]] implied for the legitimacy of earthly rule.

Holbach's critique extends the logic of secular contract theory to its limit.
If covenants among human [[existence|being]]s are the product of mutual interest and enforceable only by power, then the claim that [[god|God]] has entered into a covenant with a particular people is either a metaphor (in which case it cannot serve as the basis for [[government|political authority]]) or a claim about a historical [[eve]]nt (in which case it must be subject to the same evidentiary [[model|standard]]s as any other historical claim).
Meslier's [[proof|demonstration]] that the covenant's specific, falsifiable promises have not been fulfilled — the [[israel|Jewish people]] do not possess the promised land, they are not the most victorious of [[nation|nations]], they have been scattered and persecuted — is an application of empirical [[model|standard]]s to a theological claim that had been protected from such scrutiny by the threat of blasphemy.

The theological response to this critique has typically taken two forms. The first is to reinterpret the covenant promises as conditional upon obedience — [[israel|Israel]]'s failure to keep the covenant explains the loss of its promised benefits.
But this raises the question of why an omniscient [[god|God]] would enter into a covenant he knew would be broken, and why the punishment for breach should fall upon generations that had no part in the original agreement. The second response is to [[soul|spirit]]ualize the promises — the "land" is not a territory but a state of [[blessing|blessedness]], the "victory" is not military but [[soul|spirit]]ual.
But this contradicts the plain language of the [[testament|biblical texts]], which describe geographical boundaries and political supremacy in unambiguous terms. A covenant whose terms can be reinterpreted after the fact to mean whatever the believer wishes is not a covenant but a blank check.

## Relation to Other Concepts

The covenant is the foundational concept lin[[monarch|king]] [[divinity|divine]] authority to human political order, and its critique therefore connects directly to Holbach's broader project of dismantling the theological basis of political legitimacy. [[abraham]] is the original recipient of the covenant promise — the patriarch whose descendants were to inherit land, prosperity, and [[divinity|divine]] favor. [[moses]] is the mediator through whom the covenant was renewed and codified into law at [[sin|Sin]]ai.
The status of the [[israel|Jewish people]] as a [[chosen-people]] — a [[nations|nation]] selected by [[god|God]] for special favor — is the claimed consequence of the covenant, and Meslier's empirical refutation of that status is the core of his critique. The historical condition of [[israel]] — scattered, stateless, subordinate — is the visible [[proof|evidence]] that the covenant has failed.

The concept of [[obedience]] is central: the covenant is a bilateral agreement in which [[divinity|divine]] [[blessing]] is conditioned upon human compliance. Holbach's point is that this structure — obey and be rewarded, disobey and be punished — is transparently a human political arrangement projected onto the [[universe|cosmos]].
It is the logic of the earthly [[monarch|sovereign]] who demands loyalty from his subjects and rewards or punishes accordingly. To claim that [[god|God]] operates on the same principle is to reduce [[god|God]] to a monarch and the [[universe|cosmos]] to a [[monarch|king]]dom — an anthropomorphism so crude that no philosophical [[religion|theology]] can defend it once it is made explicit.
The covenant is, in the end, not a [[divinity|divine]] institution but a human contract attributed to [[god|God]] in order to give it an authority that no human contract could possess on its own.
