# Matthias the Apostle

## Biography

Matthias was the apostle chosen by lot to replace Judas Iscariot among the Twelve after Judas's betrayal and death (Acts 1:21–26). Little is known of his subsequent ministry or death; early traditions place his [[missionaries|missionary]] activity in [[judea|Judaea]] or Ethiopia.

## Connection to "Common Sense"

Matthias appears in Holbach's argument not as a biographical subject but as [[proof|evidence]] of competing scriptural traditions in early [[christianity|Christianity]]. Holbach, drawing on the critical scholarship of [[meslier|Jean Meslier]]'s *[[testament|Testament]]*, catalogues the proliferation of [[bible|gospels]] in the first centuries of the church as [[evidence|proof]] that [[bible|scripture]] was a fluid, contested field rather than a stable deposit of [[religion|faith]]:

> "The Marcionites published a Gospel under the name of St. Matthias, in order to confirm their doctrine. The apostles introduced other Scriptures in order to maintain their errors." (Appendix 1)

In a later passage, [[gospel|the Gospel]] of Matthias is listed alongside other apocryphal texts — [[gospel|the Gospel]] of Bartholomew, [[gospel|the Gospel]] of St. James, the Acts of [[peter|Peter]], the Apocalypse of St. Thomas — as writings "all rejected as apocryphal by the Roman Catholics" (002-472). For Holbach, the mere existence of a gospel bearing Matthias's name, promulgated by one [[sect|faction]] and rejected by another, [[demon|demons]]trates that the canonical boundary was drawn by political victory rather than [[divinity|divine]] warrant.

## Key Ideas Attributed / Critiqued

The [[gospel|Gospel]] of St. Matthias is not critiqued for its specific content — its text does not survive — but for what its existence represents: that [[eve]]ry early [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian [[sect]] felt entitled to produce or modify [[bible|scripture]]s to support its doctrinal position. Matthias's gospel stands in Holbach's catalogue alongside those of [[thomas|Thomas]], [[peter|Peter]], and Bartholomew as [[proof|evidence]] that no principled criterion can select one set of texts as authentic when so many competed for authority in the early centuries.
