# Paris

## Overview

Paris in the second half of the 18th century was the intellectual capital of [[europe|Europe]] — the city where the Enlightenment achieved its most radical expression and where, seventeen years after Holbach published "Common Sense" (1772), [[revolution|the French Revolution]] would erupt. With a population of approximately 600,000, Paris was not merely a political and commercial center but the primary site of intellectual production, debate, and dissemi[[nations|nation]] in the Francophone [[universe|world]]. Its salons, cafés, academies, and clandestine publishing networks constituted an infrastructure of [[education|enlightenment]] that made the circulation of heterodox ideas possible despite the formal apparatus of censorship.

## The Holbach Salon

The *coterie holbachique* — Holbach's salon, held regularly at his residence on the Rue Royale [[saint|Saint]]-Roch — was the most radical intellectual gathering in pre-revolutionary Paris. Unlike the more famous salon of Madame Geoffrin, which [[worship|cult]]ivated a polished, moderate discourse, Holbach's table admitted no compromise:

| Feature | Holbach's Salon | Typical Enlightenment Salon |
|---------|----------------|---------------------------|
| Religious views | Open a[[divinity|theism]] | [[mode|Mode]]rate Deism |
| Political stance | Radical, anti-clerical | [[reform|Reform]]ist, cautious |
| Attendees | Diderot, d'Alembert, Helvétius, Grimm, Rousseau (until his break) | Nobility, literati, foreign visitors |
| Tone | Combative, systematic | Polite, conversational |
| Publications | *Système de la [[nature|Nature]]*, *Common Sense* | *Encyclopédie*, literary journals |

The salon functioned as a laboratory in which radical ideas were tested, refined, and prepared for dissemi[[nations|nation]]. Holbach's anonymous publications — he never publicly acknowledged authorship of "Common Sense" or "The System of [[nature|Nature]]" — were circulated through networks centered on this Parisian institution.

## Paris as Publishing Hub

The paradox of Enlightenment Paris was that censorship coexisted with an extraordinarily vibrant clandestine publishing industry. Works banned by the royal censors were printed in Amsterdam, Geneva, or London, smuggled into Paris, and distributed through networks of sympathetic booksellers. Holbach's "Common Sense" was printed abroad (probably in Amsterdam under the imprint of Marc-Michel Rey) and circulated in [[france|France]] under the cover of anonymity. The machinery of censorship, far from suppressing radical ideas, created a lucrative black market for them and enhanced their appeal through the cachet of the forbidden.

## The Geography of Ideas

The physical geography of Paris — the proximity of the Sorbonne ([[religion|theology]]), the Palais-Royal (commerce and politics), the printing houses of the Rue [[saint|Saint]]-Jacques, and the aristocratic residences of the Faubourg [[saint|Saint]]-Germain — created a density of intellectual exchange unmatched anywhere in [[europe|Europe]]. [[concept-index|Ideas]] traveled from Holbach's salon to the printing houses of Amsterdam, to the cafés of the Palais-Royal where they were debated by a literate public, to the [[nation|Nation]]al Assembly where they would be translated into legislation. The spatial concentration of Paris made it a crucible of [[revolution|intellectual revolution]].

## Paris and the Revolution

Holbach died in January 1789, months before the Estates-General convened at Versailles and the revolution he helped prepare intellectually began its political phase. The revolutionaries did not, for the most part, embrace his a[[divinity|theism]] — the Cult of the [[being|Supreme Being]] promoted by Robespierre was Deistic — but the institutional reforms they enacted (the Civil Constitution of the [[clergy|Clergy]], the [[nations|nation]]alization of [[church|Church]] property, the abolition of monastic vows) realized much of the anti-clerical program "Common Sense" had advocated. Paris was the stage on which this drama unfolded: the Bastille, the Tuileries, the Place de la Révolution — locations that became, and remain, symbols of the overthrow of the old alliance of throne and altar.

## In the Text

Holbach does not name Paris directly in "Common Sense," but the city is the implied setting of his intellectual activity — the location from which a critique of universal clerical imposture could be launched precisely because Paris was the one place in [[europe|Europe]] where such a critique could find a sophisticated, receptive audience.
