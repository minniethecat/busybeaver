# Blame

## Definition

In Holbach's *Common Sense*, "blame" appears as a concept deployed against the theological doctrine of divine goodness. Holbach argues that if evil exists in the world, and if God is the creator of all things, then the logical conclusion is to "blame God, then, that man is so perverse" (002-116). This is a direct inversion of the orthodox Christian position, which blames humanity for its own sinfulness. Holbach's move is characteristic of his method: accept the theologian's premises and then follow them to conclusions the theologian cannot accept.

## Blaming God for Human Evil

Holbach systematically assigns responsibility for human suffering to its ultimate theological cause. "In this case ought they not to blame Him for the evils for which they are the victims?" (002-128). The logic is straightforward: if God created human nature, and human nature produces evil actions, then God is the author of evil. The theological attempt to separate God from responsibility — by invoking free will, original sin, or inscrutable providence — is for Holbach an evasion of moral reasoning. Meanwhile, the common people "blame themselves for the disasters which overwhelm them" (002-301), assuming that their own sins have provoked divine wrath — a form of self-blame that Holbach sees as the product of religious manipulation.

## Blame Within Religious Traditions

Holbach observes that blame is not only directed by the religious against unbelievers or by skeptics against God — it also circulates within religion itself, as competing factions deploy blame against one another. Early Christian writers "blamed those who invoked" the saints after their death (003-021), treating the practice of saintly intercession as an illegitimate innovation. The pattern is general: each sect blames every other for corruption, for misunderstanding scripture, for departing from original purity. For Holbach, this intra-religious blame is evidence that no party possesses the certainty it claims. If every tradition can find grounds to blame every other, the entire structure of supernatural authority dissolves into mutual recrimination. Blame, in this sense, is the universal solvent of theological certainty — the tool that each faction wields against its rivals, unaware that it undermines the foundations they all share.

## Historical Cases

Holbach adduces historical examples to support his argument. "Is it credible that Josephus, who blamed the vices of this king, could have praised him?" (002-482) — here the blame is directed at human rulers, but the underlying principle is the same: moral judgment must be applied consistently, whether to kings or to gods. In the appendix, Holbach notes that early Christians "blamed those who invoked" the saints (APP002-027), illustrating that blame is used by all parties in religious disputes as a weapon of moral delegitimation.

> We must blame God, then, that man is so perverse as to render him miserable.
>
> (002-116)
