# Julian the Apostate

## Biography

Flavius Claudius Julianus (331–363 CE), known to [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian posterity as Julian the Apostate, was Roman emperor from 361 to 363 CE. Nephew of Constantine the Great, he was raised [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian but secretly converted to [[pagan|paganism]], studying Neoplatonic philosophy in Athens. Upon becoming emperor, he attempted to restore traditional Roman religion and reverse the [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ianization of the empire — not through [[cruelty|persecution]], but by excluding [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ians from public office and promoting pagan [[temple|temples]] and philosophy. He died campaigning against Persia at age 32; his last words, according to hostile [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian sources, were "Thou hast conquered, Galilean" (*Vicisti, Galilaee*).

## Role in Religious History

Julian is the most famous apostate in [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian history and a unique figure: the last pagan emperor of [[rome|Rome]] and the only one to offer an intellectual defense of [[pagan|paganism]] against [[christianity|Christianity]]. His treatise *Against the Galileans* — preserved only in fragments quoted by his [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian opponents — critiqued the logical inconsistencies of [[scripture|Scripture]] and argued that [[christianity|Christianity]] was an apostasy from Judaism. Julian's program of pagan restoration failed, but his writings remained a potent source for Enlightenment critics of [[christianity|Christianity]], who saw in him a kindred [[soul|spirit]].

## Connection to "Common Sense"

Holbach mentions Julian once, but in remarkable company. In Chapter II, after arguing that [[scripture|Scripture]] is intellectually inferior to secular philosophy, Holbach lists "Xenophon, [[plato|Plato]], [[cicero|Cicero]], the Emperor Antoninus, the Emperor Julian, Virgil, etc." (002-478) as authors plainly "superior... to the books which we are told are inspired of [[god|God]]."

Holbach then delivers his most stinging comparison: "I can truly say that the fables of Aesop, for example, are certainly more ingenious and more instructive than all these rough and poor parables which are related in [[gospel|the Gospel]]s" (Chapter II).

Julian's inclusion in this list is significant: Holbach places a notorious apostate emperor — the "Galilean-conqueror" of [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian legend — among the canonical figures of Western philosophy and literature. The implication is clear: [[eve]]n a pagan who rejected [[christianity|Christianity]] entirely produced work of greater intellectual worth than the inspired authors of [[scripture|Scripture]].
