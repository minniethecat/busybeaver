# Baptism

## Definition

Baptism is the [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian rite of admission, typically involving water, that marks entry into the [[religion|faith]]. In Catholic and Orthodox traditions it is a sacrament believed to cleanse [[sin|original sin]]; in Protestant traditions it is an ordinance symbolizing [[soul|spirit]]ual rebirth.

For Holbach, baptism exemplifies [[eve]]rything wrong with [[ritual|religious ritual]]. It is an external performance that claims supernatural efficacy while producing no observable effect. The water is just water; the words are just words. To beli[[eve]] that a priest's incantation transforms a child's metaphysical status is, in Holbach's framework, to abandon common sense in favor of "chimerical hopes" (ch. 2).

Holbach devotes sustained attention to baptism not as a theological concept in itself, but as a narrative anchor in the [[gospel|gospels]] whose contradictory treatment [[demon|demons]]trates that the [[testament|New Testament]] is a human document, not a [[revelation|divine revelation]]. The disagreements among the [[missionaries|evangelists]] about what happened at and after Jesus's baptism are so severe that they undermine any claim to inspired authorship.

## Historical Context

By Holbach's time, baptism was a contested issue in [[europe|Europe]]an [[christianity|Christianity]]. The [[reformation|Protestant Reformation]] had split over infant baptism: Lutherans and Calvinists retained it, while Anabaptists rejected it in favor of adult (believer's) baptism. The [[catholic-church|Catholic Church]] maintained that baptism was necessary for salvation and that unbaptized infants were excluded from [[hope|heaven]] — a doctrine that caused immense anxiety for parents whose children died in childbirth.

The broader Enlightenment subjected all [[sacrament|sacraments]] to rational scrutiny. If baptism washed away [[vice|sin]], why did the baptized continue to [[vice|sin]]? If it was necessary for salvation, what of the millions who lived before [[jesus-christ|Christ]] or outside [[christianity|Christendom]]? These were not new questions — they had been debated [[vice|sin]]ce [[church|the Church]] Fathers — but the Enlightenment framed them not as [[mysteries]] to be accepted but as contradictions to be resolved.

Holbach's distinctive contribution was to use baptism as a historical-critical wedge. He did not merely question the [[religion|theology]] of baptism; he showed that the gospel accounts of Jesus's own baptism could not be reconciled with each other, and therefore could not be treated as reliable historical records.

## Core Arguments

**1. Baptism as a ritual has no rational connection to its claimed effects.**
Holbach argues that there is no intelligible relationship between pouring water on an infant and cleansing its soul of [[vice|sin]]. The efficacy of the sacrament is asserted by authority, not [[demon|demons]]trated by reason. This makes it a paradigmatic case of superstition: a physical action believed to produce a supernatural result through mechanisms that cannot be observed or tested.

**2. The gospel accounts of Jesus's baptism are contradictory.**
Holbach examines the four [[gospel|gospels]] and finds irreconcilable differences. According to the Synoptics ([[matthew|Matthew]], [[mark|Mark]], [[luke|Luke]]), Jesus was baptized by John and immediately [[desire|drive]]n by the [[spirit|Spirit]] into the [[merit|desert]] for forty days of temptation. According to John's gospel, Jesus departed two days after his baptism for [[galilee|Galilee]], where he performed his first miracle at the wedding at Cana — "more than thirty leagues from the place in which he had been" (ch. on [[contradiction|Contradiction]]s). These cannot both be accurate.

**3. The chronology of Jesus's ministry cannot be reconstructed from the baptism accounts.**
If the Synoptics are correct, Jesus's public ministry lasted about three months from his baptism to his crucifixion — "if we subtract the forty days and forty nights which He passed in the [[merit|desert]] immediately after His baptism, it would follow that the length of His public life from His first preaching till His death, would have lasted but about six weeks" (ch. on [[contradiction|Contradiction]]s). If John's gospel is correct, the ministry lasted at least three years. Such a discrepancy is not a minor detail — it makes the entire narrative framework unreliable.

**4. The contradictions expose the [[gospel|gospels]] as human documents.**
Holbach's point is not merely historical — it is epistemological. If the [[gospel|gospels]] cannot agree on basic facts about Jesus's baptism and its aftermath, they cannot be treated as [[divinity|divine]]ly inspired testimony. They are "simple stories, true or false" (ch. on [[contradiction|Contradiction]]s), written by fallible human [[existence|being]]s with conflicting sources and agendas. The sacrament of baptism rests upon a narrative foundation that crumbles under scrutiny.
