# Accident

## Definition

In Holbach's rigorously determinist universe, there is no such thing as [[chance|accident]] or [[probability|chance]]. "An atom of matter does not meet another atom by [[chance|accident]] or [[probability|chance]]; this meeting is due to immutable laws, which are the necessary consequence of the properties of all [[existence|being]]s and of the elements of which they are composed" (ch. 43). What appears to us as an [[chance|accident]]al or fortuitous [[eve]]nt is simply an [[eve]]nt whose causes lie outside our knowledge — a gap in [[knowledge|understanding]], not a gap in [[cause|causation]].

Holbach's concept of [[chance|accident]] serves a dual polemical function. Negatively, it denies that any [[eve]]nt occurs without a sufficient cause, thereby eliminating the possibility of [[miracle|miracles]], [[divinity|divine]] interventions, or uncaused [[will|volition]]s. Positively, it asserts that [[nature|the universe]] is a [[vice|sin]]gle causal nexus in which [[eve]]ry [[eve]]nt is the necessary product of antecedent conditions. To speak of "the [[chance|accident]]al coming together of atoms" (ch. 43) is to confess one's own ignorance, not to describe a real feature of [[earth|the world]]. The apparent [[probability|randomness]] of natural disasters, human misfortunes, and biological mutations is in [[eve]]ry case the wor[[monarch|king]] out of laws we have not yet mastered.

## Historical Context

The concept of [[chance|accident]] has deep roots in Western philosophy. Aristotle distinguished between substance and [[chance|accident]], where [[chance|accident]]s were properties that a substance could gain or lose without ceasing to be what it is. In the medieval scholastic tradition, [[chance|accident]] took on theological significance: the doctrine of transubstantiation held that the [[chance|accident]]s of [[transubstantiation|bread and wine]] persisted while their substance was transformed into the body and blood of [[jesus-christ|Christ]].

By the eighteenth century, the rise of [[isaac-newton|Newton]]ian physics and the mechanical philosophy had made the concept of [[probability|chance]] increasingly problematic. If [[nature|the universe]] was a vast machine governed by mathematical laws, where could [[chance|accident]] reside? Holbach's position represents the most uncompromising answer: nowhere. His denial of [[chance|accident]] is the metaphysical counterpart of Laplace's later claim that a sufficiently powerful intelligence could predict the entire future from the present state of [[nature|the universe]]. Both express the [[certainty|confidence]] of Enlightenment [[knowledge|science]] that causal [[effect|determinism]] is total and that apparent exceptions are only temporary ignorances.

## Core Arguments

1. **Every [[eve]]nt has a determinate cause.** "In a whirlwind of dust which the wind raises, in the most frightful tempest excited by the storm, there is not a [[vice|sin]]gle particle of dust or water which is placed by [[probability|chance]]" (ch. 43). The apparent [[probability|randomness]] of complex systems is no [[proof|evidence]] of genuine indeterminacy.

2. **[[chance|Chance]] is a word for our ignorance.** When we call an [[eve]]nt [[chance|accident]]al, "we only use this word to cover our ignorance of the natural causes which have operated to produce the effects we see" (ch. 43). [[chance|Chance]] is not a causal force but a confession of epistemic limitation.

3. **The denial of [[chance|accident]] eliminates the possibility of [[miracle|miracles]].** If [[eve]]ry [[eve]]nt has a natural cause, then no [[eve]]nt can be a suspension of natural law by [[divinity|divine]] will. [[miracle|Miracle]]s are simply [[eve]]nts whose causes are unknown to the observer — and the theological claim that they are supernatural is unwarranted.

4. **Human misfortunes are not punishments.** Holbach notes that "inundations, sterility, and the divers [[chance|accident]]s which so often render all human prudence useless" (ch. 52) are natural [[eve]]nts, not acts of [[god|God]]. To interpret them as punishments is to mistake physics for [[religion|theology]].

5. **Ethics must reckon with necessity, not [[probability|chance]].** If human actions are determined and misfortunes are natural, then moral judgment must shift from blame to [[knowledge|understanding]]. The criminal is not a freely choosing agent who happens to do [[injustice|evil]] but a [[existence|being]] whose [[temperament|constitution]] and circumstances have produced harmful conduct.

## Reception and Critique

Holbach's denial of [[chance|accident]] was contested [[eve]]n within the Enlightenment. Diderot, in *D'Alembert's Dream* (1769), entertained a more dynamic and unpredictable vision of [[universe|nature]], in which spontaneous generation and transformative metamorphosis introduced elements of genuine novelty. [[david-hume|Hume]], in the *Enquiry Concerning Human [[mind|Understanding]]* (1748), had already argued that "[[probability|chance]]" is merely a word for an [[eve]]nt whose causes are unknown, but [[david-hume|Hume]] maintained a skeptical caution that Holbach's [[doctrine|dogma]]tic [[effect|determinism]] lacks.

The subsequent development of probability theory and statistical mechanics in the nineteenth century gave the concept of [[probability|chance]] a mathematical respectability that Holbach would have found troubling. Maxwell, Boltzmann, and Gibbs showed that physical laws could be expressed statistically, and the twentieth-century discovery of quantum indeterminacy further complicated the picture. Holbach's [[effect|determinism]] is thus best understood as a regulative [[model|ideal]] of Enlightenment [[knowledge|science]] rather than a settled conclusion of physics.

## Relation to Other Concepts

Accident is the negative pole of [[Necessity]]: to deny [[chance|accident]] is to affirm that necessity governs all [[eve]]nts without exception. The concept is central to Holbach's critique of [[Providence]], [[vice|sin]]ce providential [[government|governance]] implies that [[eve]]nts are directed toward an end, while Holbach insists they are merely the necessary outcomes of blind causes. Accident relates to [[Free Will]] because the libertarian conception of [[liberty|freedom]] requires that human choices be uncaused — which, for Holbach, is equivalent to calling them [[chance|accident]]al. It connects to [[Nature]] as the system of necessary laws that leaves no room for [[probability|chance]], and to [[Evil]] because natural disasters, often interpreted as [[punishment|divine punishment]]s or inexplicable tragedies, are in fact neither: they are the necessary operations of physical causes.
