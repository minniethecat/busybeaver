# Religion and Political Power in Holbach's Thought

## Thesis

In *Common Sense* (1772), Holbach advances a thesis that goes beyond the [[model|standard]] Enlightenment critique of religious superstition: he argues that religion is not merely a set of [[error|false belief]]s but a *political technology* — a system of social control that operates through an enduring alliance between [[clergy|priests]] and monarchs. The [[throne-and-altar]] compact is not an aberration or corruption of religion; it is religion's essential social function.

## The Throne-and-Altar Alliance

Holbach opens *Common Sense* with an allegory that makes this thesis explicit. "This empire is [[earth|the world]]; its monarch is [[god|God]]; His ministers are the [[clergy|priests]]; their subjects are men" (ch. I). The political metaphor is not decorative — it is the book's organizing analytic frame. [[religion|Religion]] is [[government|governance]] by other means: the [[god]] of [[religion|theology]] is functionally a monarch; the [[clergy]] are his ministers; the [[people]] are subjects who must be managed.

The alliance works bidirectionally:
- **[[priest|Priest]]s legitimize monarchs**: By claiming that [[monarch|king]]s rule by [[divine-right|divine right]], [[clergy|the clergy]] transform political submission into religious duty. The [[monarch|sovereign]] becomes [[god|God]]'s representative, and rebellion becomes [[sin]].
- **[[monarch|Monarch]]s protect [[clergy|priests]]**: The state provides the coercive apparatus — laws against [[blasphemy]], suppression of [[heresy]], enforcement of religious uniformity — that prevents rational scrutiny of priestly claims.

Holbach's critique of the [[church]] is thus inseparable from his critique of [[government|the state]]. The two institutions are "united by the same interests" — the [[priesthood]] needs the monarch's sword, and the monarch needs the priest's [[superstition]]-generating power over [[remorse|conscience]].

## The Mechanism: Fear and Ignorance

The alliance operates through two psychological levers:

### Fear
"Frightened and led astray by ignorance, by fear, by calamities" — Holbach's account of the origins of religion is also his account of how it maintains itself. [[terror|Religious terror]] is the psychological foundation of priestly power. The threat of [[hell|eternal punishment]] is the ultimate enforcement mechanism, far more cost-effective than any earthly police force because it operates internally, in the [[remorse|conscience]] of the believer.

### Ignorance
[[priest|Priest]]s deliberately keep people "in ignorance, and deprive them [[eve]]n of the [[passion|desire]] of [[knowledge|understanding]]" their duties. An educated population that understands [[nature]] through [[reason]] would have no need for priestly mediation. The alliance depends on maintaining what Holbach calls a "thick cloud" between the people and the truth about [[nature|the natural world]].

## Historical Evidence in the Text

Holbach draws on the history of [[christianity|Christianity]] to illustrate his thesis:
- The [[council-of-trent]] (1545–1563) represents the institutional moment when [[church|the Church]], threatened by Protestant reform, consolidated its doctrinal authority through conciliar decree rather than rational argument.
- The [[inquisition]] [[demon|demons]]trates the coercive apparatus of the alliance at its most extreme: theological error treated as civil crime, with [[government|the state]] providing the instruments of punishment.
- The [[crusades]] show religion mobilizing populations for political-military objectives under the banner of sacred duty.

## The Contradiction: Why the Alliance Must Fail

Holbach is not merely descriptive — he is predictive. The alliance between throne and altar contains an internal contradiction: [[clergy|priests]] need the people to beli[[eve]] in a [[god|God]] more powerful than any [[monarch|king]], which implicitly relativizes monarchical authority. "If [[god|God]] is infinite, no finite [[existence|being]] can have communication with him, nor relations" (ch. II). This argument cuts both ways: if [[god|God]] is infinite, the monarch cannot claim to represent him; if the monarch claims [[divinity|divine]] authority, [[god|God]] cannot be infinite. The very theological predicate that sustains the alliance — [[divinity|divine]] transcendence — undermines any specific political claim derived from it.

## Implications for Enlightenment Thought

Holbach's analysis represents the most radical version of the Enlightenment critique of religion-as-politics. Where [[voltaire|Voltaire]] advocated for enlightened [[tyranny|despotism]] tempered by Deism, and Rousseau sought a civil religion that would bind citizens to [[government|the state]], Holbach argued that any alliance between supernatural belief and [[government|political authority]] is inherently corrupting. His solution was not reform but abolition: dismantle [[priestcraft]], educate the people, and ground [[government|political authority]] in [[reason]] and [[nature]] rather than [[divinity|divine]] mandate.
