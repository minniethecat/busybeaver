# Wisdom

## Definition

In Holbach's "Common Sense," [[philosophy|wisdom]] is a contested term. Theologians exalt "divine wisdom" as an attribute of [[god|God]] — infinite, inscrutable, and "marvelous only because it is totally impossible for them to understand it themselves" (ch. LXXVIII). But this divine wisdom, when examined, turns out to be a device for silencing rational inquiry: whenever a theological claim is shown to be [[absurdity|absurd]], the theologian retreats to the "immense depths of [[theology-index|Divine]] [[philosophy|wisdom]]" and forbids further exami[[nations|nation]] as insolence.

Holbach's countermove is to reclaim [[philosophy|wisdom]] as a human capacity — the application of reason and experience to life — and to [[demon|demons]]trate that "divine wisdom" as described by theologians is indistinguishable from folly, malice, or indifference. A [[god|God]] whose [[philosophy|wisdom]] produces a [[universe|world]] of "imperfections," where the innocent suffer alongside the guilty, where "His works are full of imperfections, and of little permanence" (ch. I), is not wise by any [[model|standard]] humans can recognize. To call this [[philosophy|wisdom]] is to empty the word of meaning.

## Historical Context

The [[philosophy|wisdom]] tradition in Western thought had two major strands. The first, originating in the Hebrew [[scripture|Bible]] (Proverbs, Ecclesiastes, Job), treated [[philosophy|wisdom]] as both a [[divinity|divine]] attribute and a practical human [[morality|virtue]] — the fear of the Lord is the beginning of [[philosophy|wisdom]], but [[philosophy|wisdom]] also consists in prudential maxims for daily life. The second, originating in Greek philosophy, treated [[philosophy|wisdom]] (sophia) as the highest intellectual [[morality|virtue]], the contemplation of truth for its own sake.

Holbach's intervention was to [[desire|drive]] a wedge between these strands. He accepted the practical value of human [[philosophy|wisdom]] — the [[philosophy|wisdom]] that consists in [[knowledge|understanding]] [[universe|nature]], governing one's passions, and organizing society for human happiness. But he rejected the divine wisdom of [[religion|theology]] as a disguised admission of ignorance. The theologian who says "[[god|God]]'s [[philosophy|wisdom]] is beyond our comprehension" is not praising [[god|God]]; he is confessing that he cannot explain [[earth|the world]] and is forbidding others from trying.

This position was consistent with the Enlightenment's broader revaluation of [[philosophy|wisdom]]. Kant's "Sapere aude!" — dare to be wise, have the courage to use your own [[knowledge|understanding]] — was the motto of the age. Wisdom was not submission to [[mystery|divine mystery]] but the autonomous exercise of reason. Holbach radicalized this: [[philosophy|wisdom]] is not merely independent of [[religion|theology]]; it is incompatible with it.

## Core Arguments

**1. "[[theology-index|Divine]] [[philosophy|wisdom]]" is a euphemism for ignorance.** When theologians invoke the "immense depths of [[theology-index|Divine]] [[philosophy|wisdom]]" and forbid us to "fathom these depths" (ch. LXXVIII), they confess that they cannot account for the conduct of the [[god|God]] they describe. A [[philosophy|wisdom]] that cannot be examined, that produces effects indistinguishable from folly, and that must be protected from rational scrutiny by declaring scrutiny itself a [[vice|sin]] — this is not [[philosophy|wisdom]] at all. "In telling us that it is insolence to call [[god|God]] to the tribunal of our humble reason, in ma[[monarch|king]] it a crime to judge our Master, the theologians only confess the embarrassment in which they find themselves."

**2. The [[universe|world]] shows no [[proof|evidence]] of divine wisdom.** Holbach surveys [[earth|the world]] as theologians describe it — created by an all-wise [[god|God]] for His glory and the good of His creatures — and finds the opposite: "all is created by him, yet [[eve]]nts rarely happen according to his projects. He foresees [[eve]]rything, but his foresight prevents nothing" (ch. I). The [[universe|world]] is full of [[evil|suffering]], disorder, and impermanence. To call this the product of infinite [[philosophy|wisdom]] is to invert the meaning of the word.

**3. [[theology|Theology]] makes [[god|God]] the enemy of human [[philosophy|wisdom]].** The theological claim that "what is folly in the eyes of man, is [[philosophy|wisdom]] before [[god|God]], who is pleased to confound the [[philosophy|wisdom]] of the wise" (ch. CXXXIV) means that [[god|God]] prefers the ignorant to the enlightened. If human [[philosophy|wisdom]] is a gift from [[god|God]] — as theologians also claim — then [[god|God]] punishes people for using His own gift. "Is it not very strange that we can not be the friend of your [[god|God]] but by declaring ourselves the enemy of reason and common sense?"

**4. True [[philosophy|wisdom]] is natural, not supernatural.** Holbach does not deny the value of [[philosophy|wisdom]]; he relocates it. Wisdom is the product of experience, reflection, and the disciplined use of reason — not a gift from [[hope|heaven]] but an acquisition of the human mind. The wise person understands natural [[cause|causation]], recognizes the limits of human knowledge without filling the gaps with superstition, and organizes life for [[happiness|earthly happiness]] rather than for the supposed demands of an invisible tyrant.

## Reception and Critique

Holbach's critique of divine wisdom was part of the broader Enlightenment project of desacralizing theological language. By showing that "[[philosophy|wisdom]]" as applied to [[god|God]] was a honorific without content — a word that signaled reverence while bloc[[monarch|king]] [[knowledge|understanding]] — he anticipated later analytical philosophy's critique of religious language as cognitively meaningless. Ayer's verification principle, applied to [[government|the state]]ment "[[god|God]] is wise," would yield the same result: if no conceivable observation could confirm or disconfirm divine wisdom, [[government|the state]]ment is not a proposition about [[earth|the world]] but an expression of [[passion|emotion]].

More sympathetic religious thinkers — including process theologians like Whitehead and Hartshorne — have accepted Holbach's implicit challenge by reconceiving divine wisdom not as omnipotent foresight but as persuasive lure, a [[philosophy|wisdom]] that works through attraction rather than coercion. Holbach would likely consider this a significant [[progress|improvement]] over the traditional concept, though he would still ask what [[proof|evidence]] supports it.

## Relation to Other Concepts

Wisdom in Holbach's treatment is closely tied to [[Reason]] — [[philosophy|wisdom]] is reason applied to life. It stands in opposition to [[Mystery]], which is the theological device for protecting "divine wisdom" from scrutiny. [[Folly]] is what [[religion|theology]] calls [[reason|human reason]] and what Holbach calls the conduct of the theological [[god|God]]. [[Education]] is the [[worship|cult]]ivation of [[philosophy|wisdom]] through experience and reflection. [[Enlightenment]] is the social condition in which [[philosophy|wisdom]] — the courage to use one's own [[knowledge|understanding]] — is widely exercised.

## In the Text

On [[earth|the world]] as [[proof|evidence]] against divine wisdom:

> "All is created by him, yet events rarely happen according to his projects. He foresees everything, but his foresight prevents nothing. ... His knowledge is admired in the perfection of his works, but his works are full of imperfections, and of little permanence." (ch. I)

On [[religion|theology]]'s confession of embarrassment:

> "In speaking to us incessantly of the immense depths of Divine wisdom, in forbidding us to fathom these depths by telling us that it is insolence to call God to the tribunal of our humble reason, ... the theologians only confess the embarrassment in which they find themselves." (ch. LXXVIII)

On [[god|God]] as enemy of human [[philosophy|wisdom]]:

> "You tell us, O theologians! that 'what is folly in the eyes of men, is wisdom before God, who is pleased to confound the wisdom of the wise.' But do you not pretend that human wisdom is a gift from Heaven? In telling us that this wisdom displeases God, ... you proclaim that your God is but the friend of unenlightened people." (ch. CXXXIV)

On the [[absurdity|absurd]]ity of calling a foolish [[god|God]] wise:

> "A God who punishes the faults which He could have prevented, is a being who lacks wisdom, goodness, and equity. A God of foresight would prevent evil, and in this way would be saved the trouble of punishing it." (ch. LXXIX)
