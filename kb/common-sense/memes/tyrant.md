# Tyrant

## Definition

In Holbach's [[politics|political theology]], the **tyrant** serves a double analytical function. Politically, the tyrant is the [[despotism|absolute rule]]r who governs without law, consent, or accountability — the secular counterpart to the priest. Theologically, the tyrant is the model against which Holbach evaluates the character of [[god|God]]: [[eve]]ry attribute assigned to the [[god|deity]] (omnipotence without accountability, the power to punish eternally for finite [[crime|offense]]s, the demand for praise from [[evil|suffering]] creatures) mirrors the psychology of human [[tyranny|despotism]]. The critique is devastating in its simplicity: if human [[despotism|tyranny]] is morally abhorrent, [[divinity|divine]] [[despotism|tyranny]] is abhorrent multiplied by [[infinite|infinity]].

## Historical Context

Holbach wrote in the shadow of Louis XIV's [[tyranny|absolutism]] and Louis XV's reign, where the doctrine of [[divine-right|divine right]] (*droit divin*) explicitly fused political and theological [[despotism|tyranny]]. The [[monarch|king]] ruled as [[god|God]]'s deputy; rebellion against the [[monarch|king]] was rebellion against [[god|God]]. This throne-altar alliance, which Holbach critiques throughout *Common Sense*, made the critique of [[despotism|tyranny]] simultaneously a political and theological project. The classical sources Holbach draws upon — Tacitus's account of Nero and Domitian, the Roman historians' critique of imperial corruption — supplied the vocabulary for describing arbitrary power. Enlightenment political thought (Montesquieu, Rousseau) provided the theoretical framework: legitimate government rests on consent and law, not force and superstition.

## Core Arguments

Holbach's analysis of [[despotism|tyranny]] operates through three interloc[[monarch|king]] claims. **First: [[god|God]] resembles a tyrant** — theological descriptions of [[divinity|divine]] [[government|governance]] replicate the logic of [[sovereign|absolute monarch]]y: a [[existence|being]] whose will is law, who demands obedience without expla[[nations|nation]], who punishes infinitely for finite [[crime|transgression]]s, and whose favor is arbitrary and inscrutable. **Second: [[despotism|tyranny]] corrupts religion** — political tyrants instrumentalize religion to sanctify their rule, while [[clergy|priests]] sanctify [[despotism|tyranny]] in exchange for state protection. **Third: religion enables [[despotism|tyranny]]** — by [[doctrine|teaching]] subjects that obedience to authority is a [[divinity|divine]] command, religion produces the psychological disposition that makes political [[tyranny|despotism]] possible. The cure is materialist [[effect|determinism]]: [[knowledge|understanding]] that character is shaped by social conditions, not [[predestination|divine decree]], enables the critique of both [[divinity|divine]] and human [[despotism|tyranny]].

## In the Text

> "God resembles a tyrant who, having deprived all his slaves of sight, amuses himself by making them grope in their darkness and stumble against obstacles that he has placed in their path, punishing them cruelly for their falls." (ch. II)

> "The most sanguinary tyrants, Caligula, Nero, Domitian, had at least some motive in tormenting their victims and insulting their sufferings; these motives were, either their own safety, or revenge, or the desire to terrify, or perhaps to give to the universe an example of a power which dares all things. Can God have any of these motives? In tormenting the victims of His wrath, a tyrant does not say that he is good and humane." (ch. LXVI)

> "Is it not calumniating a just God, to say that he is the most unjust tyranny?" (ch. CXXX)

Holbach's rhetorical strategy is to take theological claims at face value and follow them to their logical moral conclusions. If [[god|God]]'s [[government|governance]] operates as described — arbitrary [[predestination|election]], [[hell|eternal punishment]], commands that surpass [[reason|human reason]] — then [[god|God]] is by definition a tyrant. The conclusion is not that humans should find a better concept of [[god|God]], but that the concept of [[god|God]] is a projection of human political experience onto the [[universe|cosmos]].
