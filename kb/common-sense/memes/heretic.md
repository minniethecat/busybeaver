## Definition and Context

Holbach examines the concept of heresy as part of his broader critique of religious intolerance. In "Common Sense," the heretic is presented not as someone who holds false beliefs, but as someone whom religious authorities have marked for persecution because their views threaten ecclesiastical power.

## Persecution of Heretics

Holbach directly addresses the violent impulse behind the label:

> ...you persecute, you devote heretics to torture, because you are... (002-324)

This persecution extends beyond physical violence to social exclusion — the believer is taught that "your neighbor if he is impious, heretical, and incredulous" (002-356) deserves condemnation.

## Mutual Accusation

As with the label "infidel," Holbach observes that heresy accusations are reciprocal among competing sects:

> ...accusing each other of being infidels and heretics, rending and persecuting each other... (002-396)

This reveals that "heresy" is always defined from a particular orthodoxy — what is heretical to one sect is doctrine to another. The "heretical" is simply what the powerful declare it to be: "This doctrine appears heretical, ungodly, and abominable..." (002-394)
