# Atonement

## Definition

Atonement is the [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian doctrine that the death of [[jesus-christ|Jesus Christ]] on the cross satisfied the demands of [[justice|divine justice]], paying the penalty for human [[sin]] and reconciling humanity to [[god|God]]. Holbach treats this doctrine as one of the most revealing [[absurdity|absurd]]ities of [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian [[theology]]: an omnipotent and benevolent [[god|deity]] who requires the torture of his own son before he can forgive his creatures is neither benevolent nor rational.

The doctrine of atonement, in Holbach's reading, exposes the barbaric origins of [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian [[religion|theology]] beneath its moral pretensions. It inherits the logic of ancient sacrificial [[worship|cult]]s — blood for blood, life for life — and elevates it to a cosmic scale while calling it a [[secrecy|mystery]] beyond comprehension.

## Historical Context

The doctrine of atonement developed over centuries of theological debate. Anselm of Canterbury's satisfaction theory (11th century) framed [[jesus-christ|Christ]]'s death as payment of an infinite debt owed to [[divinity|divine]] honor; Calvin's penal substitution (16th century) recast it as [[jesus-christ|Christ]] bearing the punishment deserved by [[guilt|sin]]ners. By Holbach's time, atonement was a settled [[doctrine|dogma]] of both Catholic and Protestant orthodoxy.

Holbach views this theological evolution not as progressive clarification but as successive layers of [[absurdity|absurd]]ity. Each refinement, he argues, only deepened the moral [[contradiction|incoherence]] at the doctrine's core: a father who requires the death of an innocent son as the price of forgiving the guilty is not a model of justice but of [[despotism|tyranny]]. [[enlightenment|The Enlightenment]]'s emphasis on rational [[morality]] made the atonement an increasingly vulnerable target, and Holbach's attack in *Common Sense* represents one of the period's most uncompromising rejections of the doctrine.

## Theological Critique

Holbach's critique follows his broader method of reducing theological [[mysteries]] to their moral implications. If [[god|God]] is all-powerful, why cannot he simply forgive? If he is all-good, why does he demand [[evil|suffering]] as the price of reconciliation? The answer, Holbach suggests, is that the doctrine was not derived from reason but inherited from primitive religions where [[gods|deities]] required blood offerings.

[[christianity|Christianity]] merely elevated this ancient [[model|pattern]] to a cosmic scale — replacing [[soul|anima]]l sacrifice with the death of a [[divinity|divine]] person — and called it a [[secrecy|mystery]]. The doctrine is inseparable from [[sin|original sin]]: without the premise that humanity is fundamentally corrupted, there is no need for a savior to satisfy [[divinity|divine]] wrath. Holbach observes that the entire apparatus — [[guilt|sin]], atonement, [[salvation]] — is circular: it creates a disease in order to sell the cure.

## In the Text

> "A God who is capable of being appeased by prayers, by offerings, by sacrifices, by penitential tears — such a God is a veritable Proteus. His priests show Him now armed with severity, and then full of clemency." (ch. CLXV)

Holbach's use of the Proteus metaphor applies as directly to atonement as to absolution. The same [[god|God]] who demands blood payment for [[guilt|sin]] is also the [[god|God]] who provides the victim — his own son — and accepts the sacrifice as payment to himself. The circularity of this transaction would be recognized as [[absurdity|absurd]] in any context outside of [[religion|theology]].

> "The death of a God would be necessary to redeem man, who without it was lost. Strange remedy, which proves equally the justice and the mercy of the Almighty!" (ch. L)

The irony is unmistakable. The death of [[god|God]] is presented as the solution to a problem that [[god|God]] himself created — the [[hell|damnation]] of humanity for a [[guilt|sin]] committed by the first man. Holbach's technique is to state the doctrine plainly and allow its [[absurdity|absurd]]ity to speak for itself.

## Reception and Critique

Holbach's critique of the atonement belongs to a tradition of rationalist objections to substitutionary soteriology reaching back to Socinus and the Polish Brethren of the sixteenth century. The Socinian argument — that a just [[god|God]] does not require the punishment of an innocent party to forgive the guilty — prefigures Holbach's moral objection, though Holbach goes further in treating the entire framework of [[guilt|sin]] and [[salvation|redemption]] as a clerical invention.

In the twentieth century, the moral critique of atonement was taken up by feminist theologians who argued that the doctrine of substitutionary atonement sanctifies abuse by presenting a father who wills the death of his son as a model of love. While Holbach did not develop this line of argument, his fundamental insight — that the atonement presents a morally repugnant picture of the [[god|deity]] — has proven enduring in secular and liberal theological criticism alike.
