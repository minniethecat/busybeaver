## Penitence as Self-Inflicted Misery

Holbach places the figure of the penitent at the center of his critique of religious morality. "We see in all parts of our globe penitents, hermits, fakirs, fanatics" (002-335) who have been persuaded that self-inflicted suffering is pleasing to God. For Holbach, this is not piety but pathology — a moral system that "is founded upon the opinion that to please the Deity it is necessary to render one's self unhappy upon earth" has inverted the natural purpose of morality, which should promote human happiness rather than human misery.

## The Logic of Penitential Religion

Holbach's analysis reveals a perverse logic at work in the doctrine of penitence. By teaching that suffering now earns reward hereafter, religion creates a demand for its own services: the penitent requires the priest to administer the sacraments of absolution, to prescribe the acts of penance, and to mediate the reconciliation with God. The penitential system thus serves the institutional interests of the clergy even as it imposes genuine suffering on the faithful. "By offerings, by sacrifices, by penitential tears, we have no right to" claim merit (002-347) — the entire apparatus of penitence, in Holbach's view, is a transaction that enriches the priesthood at the expense of human well-being.

## Penitents and the Diversity of Religious Suffering

Holbach draws a comparative portrait of religiously motivated suffering that spans cultures and traditions. The penitents of Christianity find their counterparts in the fakirs of India, the hermits of the desert, and the fanatics of every creed. This universality serves Holbach's argument: penitential practices are not evidence of any particular religion's truth but of a common human susceptibility to the belief that suffering is meritorious. The diversity of penitential forms masks a single underlying error — the substitution of self-punishment for rational virtue.

## From *Common Sense*

> The whole universe is infected more or less with a religious morality which is founded upon the opinion that to please the Deity it is necessary to render one's self unhappy upon earth. We see in all parts of our globe penitents, hermits, fakirs, fanatics, who strive in every way to surpass each other in the art of tormenting themselves.
>
> (002-335)
