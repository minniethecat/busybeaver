# tolerance

Holbach argues that genuine **tolerance** is impossible under religion. "There does not exist yet upon earth a true tolerance," he writes, because "everywhere a jealous God" commands the persecution of unbelievers. For Holbach, sociability, tolerance, and humanity are "the first virtues," yet religion systematically undermines them by demanding intolerance in God's name. (002-321)

## Religion and Intolerance

The connection between religion and intolerance is, for Holbach, structural rather than accidental. He argues that religion "consecrates the most holy obligations, intolerance, and the spirit of persecution" (002-309; 169-002). The jealous God of monotheism demands exclusive allegiance, and this demand translates directly into the persecution of those who do not conform. Every religion that claims a monopoly on divine truth inevitably views dissent as rebellion against God Himself, making persecution not merely permissible but obligatory.

## Tolerance as a Primary Virtue

Against the religious demand for persecution, Holbach sets tolerance as one of the foundational social virtues. He places tolerance alongside sociability and humanity as "these first virtues" (002-323; 156-003) — the qualities that make peaceful coexistence possible. Where religion teaches that the heretic must be burned, natural morality teaches that human beings must learn to live alongside difference. Tolerance is not a concession to weakness but an affirmation of the conditions necessary for any functioning society.

## The Rare Toleration of Enlightened Clergy

Holbach acknowledges that some limited tolerance may be found "among a few priests who are prudent and enlightened" (002-356; 168-006). But this is the exception that proves the rule — tolerance among the clergy arises from worldly prudence, not from religious principle. The system itself, built upon a jealous God and exclusive revelation, points inexorably toward persecution. Individual clerics may resist the logic of their own system, but the system remains fundamentally intolerant.

## Selected Passage

Holbach warns that priests, as educators of princes, systematically instill intolerance as a foundation of royal authority:

> In almost all countries, priests and devout persons are charged with forming the mind and the heart of the young princes destined to govern the nations. What enlightenment can teachers of this stamp give? Filled themselves with prejudices, they will hold up to their pupil superstition as the most important and the most sacred thing, its chimerical duties as the most holy obligations, intolerance, and the spirit of persecution, as the true foundations of his future authority.
>
> (002-309)
