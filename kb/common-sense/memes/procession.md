## Procession as Empty Ritual

Holbach groups religious processions with other external ceremonies that he regards as substitutes for genuine virtue. In *Common Sense*, he lists them among the remedies that the clergy offer for human suffering: "Prayers, sacrifices, processions, offerings, ceremonies, are, according to the priests, the means of appeasing the anger of Heaven" (002-442). For Holbach, all of these external rituals share a common function: they redirect attention from the real causes of human misery — ignorance, tyranny, and exploitation — toward symbolic acts that change nothing.

## The Sociology of Public Ritual

Holbach's analysis of processions reflects his broader materialist approach to religion. Public rituals like processions serve, in his account, to visibly demonstrate the church's social centrality and to reinforce the laity's dependence on clerical mediation. The spectacle of the procession — with its costumes, hierarchy, and choreographed reverence — is a form of social theater that naturalizes ecclesiastical authority by embedding it in sensory experience.

## Ritual and Reform

Within Holbach's Enlightenment framework, the critique of processions is part of a larger argument for replacing ceremonial religion with rational morality. If human beings can be taught that improving their condition requires social and political action rather than ritual performance, then the priesthood loses its claim to indispensable mediation. The procession thus represents, for Holbach, everything that an enlightened society must outgrow.


## From *Common Sense*

> Prayers, sacrifices, processions, offerings, ceremonies, are, we are told, the true means to disarm Celestial fury. But why is Heaven angry? Because men are wicked. Why are men wicked? Because their nature is corrupt.
>
> (002-442)
