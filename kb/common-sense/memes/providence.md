# Providence

## Definition

Providence is the [[doctrine|theological doctrine]] that [[god|God]] benevolently governs [[earth|the world]], providing for human needs and directing [[eve]]nts toward [[divinity|divine]]ly ordained ends. Holbach subjects this doctrine to a devastating empirical critique: if providence exists, the observable [[proof|evidence]] of widespread [[evil|suffering]], natural disaster, and [[evil|moral evil]] renders it indistinguishable from malice or indifference. "We call Providence the generous care which [[divinity|Divinity]] shows in providing for our needs, and in watching over the happiness of its beloved creatures. But, as soon as we look around, we find that [[god|God]] provides for nothing" (ch. 52). The concept is "a word void of sense" — a pious fiction that collapses under the slightest exami[[nations|nation]] of the actual condition of humanity.

## Historical Context

The [[design|argument from design]] and providence was central to eighteenth-century natural [[religion|theology]]. Thinkers like [[will|Will]]iam Paley argued that the complexity and apparent purposefulness of [[universe|nature]] [[proof|evidence]]d a benevolent [[design|Design]]er. The [[lisbon|Lisbon]] earthquake of 1755 — which killed tens of thousands on All [[saint|Saint]]s' Day and destroyed the city's churches while sparing its brothels — dealt a severe blow to providential optimism. [[voltaire|Voltaire]]'s "Poem on the [[lisbon|Lisbon]] Disaster" and *Candide* satirized providential thin[[monarch|king]]. Holbach's contribution was to systematize the critique: providentialism fails on its own terms because the empirical [[universe|world]] contradicts its central claim of [[divinity|divine]] [[goodness|benevolence]].

## Core Arguments

**1. The empirical [[universe|world]] contradicts providential care.** "Providence neglects the greatest part of the inhabitants of this [[universe|world]]. Against a very small number of men, who are supposed to be happy, what a multitude of miserable ones are groaning beneath [[persecution|oppression]], and languishing in misery! Whole [[nation|nations]] are compelled to starve in order to indulge the [[excess|extravagance]]s of a few morose tyrants" (ch. 52).

**2. Providence is a malicious concept when judged by its effects.** If we "judge it by its effects," we find that "these effects are sometimes useful and sometimes injurious to me." A providence that produces both good and [[injustice|evil]] is indistinguishable from no providence at all — or worse, from a malign agency. Even if blessings outnumber [[injustice|evil]]s by ten to one, "against these hundred degrees of goodness, Providence possesses a tenth degree of malignity — which is incompatible with the perfection we suppose it to have" (ch. 52).

**3. The actual condition of humanity requires constant struggle against [[universe|nature]], not reliance on divine care.** "Without labor, man could scarcely live a day. In order to live, I see him obliged to sweat, work, hunt, fish, toil without relaxation; without these secondary causes, the First [[cause|Cause]] (at least in the majority of [[nations|countries]]) could provide for none of his needs" (ch. 52). Providence fails [[eve]]n at basic subsistence.

**4. Natural disasters render providence [[absurdity|absurd]].** [[humanity|Humanity]] is "compelled to ward off the blows which it sends in the form of hurricanes, tempests, frost, hail, inundations, sterility, and the divers [[chance|accident]]s which so often render all their labors useless" (ch. 52). The human race is "continually occupied in protecting itself from the wicked tricks of this Providence."

**5. Providential thin[[monarch|king]] is self-serving and circular.** Holbach notes how a devotee "admired [[theology-index|Divine]] Providence for having wisely made rivers to flow through all the places where men had built large cities" — mista[[monarch|king]] the effect for the cause, and reading [[divinity|divine]] intention into natural facts that require no such expla[[nations|nation]] (ch. 52).

## Relation to Other Concepts

Providence is the theological corollary of the concept of [[God]] as a personal, benevolent agent. It is contradicted by the observable reality of [[Evil]] and [[Suffering]]. The critique of providence directly supports Holbach's broader argument for [[Materialism]] and [[Nature]] as self-sufficient explanatory frameworks. The doctrine of providence serves the political function of legitimizing [[Despotism]] by representing [[monarch|ruler]]s as instruments of [[divinity|divine]] will. [[Reason]] and [[Experience]] are the tools Holbach deploys against providential thin[[monarch|king]].

## In the Text

The chapter on providence is one of Holbach's most concentrated and rhetorically effective critiques:

> "We call Providence the generous care which Divinity shows in providing for our needs, and in watching over the happiness of its beloved creatures. But, as soon as we look around, we find that God provides for nothing. Providence neglects the greatest part of the inhabitants of this world." (ch. 52)

On the impossibility of reconciling providence with [[injustice|evil]]:

> "Let us suppose that the blessings which this Providence makes us enjoy are as one hundred, and that the evils are as ten per cent.; would it not always result that against these hundred degrees of goodness, Providence possesses a tenth degree of malignity? — which is incompatible with the perfection we suppose it to have." (ch. 52)

On the human struggle against providence's "wicked tricks":

> "I see the human race continually occupied in protecting itself from the wicked tricks of this Providence, which is said to be busy with the care of their happiness." (ch. 52)
