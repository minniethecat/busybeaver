# Prophet

## In Holbach's Critique

In Holbach's *Common Sense*, **prophets** are examined as vehicles of religious authority and potential instruments of deception. Holbach acknowledges a distinction: "there are not only false prophets — that is to say, impostors — who have not been sent by the Almighty, but who falsely boast of having received His orders" (002-465). Yet he treats the entire prophetic tradition with skepticism, noting that "the legislators, the priests, and the prophets of the Hebrew religion" (002-438) shaped doctrine for political purposes. Holbach recounts the case of "one of these pretended prophets, named Sedecias" (002-466) as an example of prophetic imposture. The broader point is that claims of divine inspiration are, for Holbach, indistinguishable from fraud — and the faithful who "accept the faith of his Divine prophet" (002-217) do so without rational examination.

## Selected Passage

Holbach highlights the mutual contempt among religions, each convinced of the absurdity of the others' prophets:

> Nothing appears more ridiculous in the eyes of a sensible man than for one denomination to criticize another whose creed is equally foolish. A Christian thinks that the Koran, the Divine revelation announced by Mohammed, is but a tissue of impertinent dreams and impostures injurious to Divinity. The Mohammedan, on his side, treats the Christian as an idolater and a dog; he sees but absurdities in his religion; he imagines he has the right to conquer his country and force him, sword in hand, to accept the faith of his Divine prophet.
>
> (002-217)
