# Incense

In Holbach's rhetoric, incense stands for the act of religious worship — the offering made by believers to a deity whose character, Holbach argues, is "visibly borrowed from the implacable, jealous, vindictive, blood-thirsty, capricious, inconsiderate humor of man" (120-002). The image of incense rising before a savage God crystallizes Holbach's critique: religious devotion is directed toward a projection of humanity's own worst qualities.

## Worship as Self-Adoration

Holbach radicalizes the critique of ritual offering by arguing that in worshiping God, human beings ultimately adore themselves: "In worshiping God, man adores himself" (002-062). The qualities attributed to the deity — power, justice, vengeance — are projections of human attributes, magnified and distorted to the point of unrecognizability. The incense offered to God is thus an offering to a mirror: the worshiper kneels before an idealized and terrifying image of his own nature. This psychological insight transforms the act of burning incense from a pious ritual into an exercise in collective self-deception. The God who receives the incense is, in Holbach's materialist analysis, nothing more than humanity's own reflection, "visibly borrowed from the implacable, jealous, vindictive, blood-thirsty, capricious, inconsiderate humor of man" (120-002).

Holbach opens his discussion of incense and ritual by tracing all worship to barbarous origins:

> All the Gods worshiped by men have a barbarous origin; they were visibly imagined by stupid nations, or were presented by ambitious and cunning legislators to simple and benighted people, who had neither the capacity nor the courage to examine properly the object which, by means of terrors, they were made to worship.
>
> (120-002)
