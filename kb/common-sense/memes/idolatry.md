# Idolatry

## Definition

Idolatry, in its narrow sense, is the worship of idols — physical objects, images, or statues treated as gods or as vessels of [[divinity|divine]] presence. In the [[abraham|Abraham]]ic traditions, idolatry is the paradigmatic [[vice|sin]]: the worship of the creature rather than [[creator|the Creator]].

Holbach radically subverts this framework. He argues that the distinction between idolatrous and "true" worship is a self-serving fiction maintained by [[clergy|priests]] to delegitimize rival religions while obscuring the identical character of their own practices. When a Catholic kneels before a consecrated host believing it to be the body of [[god|God]], or venerates a statue of a saint, or treats a relic as a channel of supernatural power — this is, by any honest definition, idolatry. The Catholic calls the pagan an idolater for worshiping a stone; the pagan could with equal justice call the Catholic an idolater for worshiping a piece of bread.

The concept of idolatry, in Holbach's treatment, thus becomes a tool of analysis rather than an accusation. It exposes the underlying identity of all religious practice: the projection of human fears and [[passion|desire]]s onto material objects, which are then treated as possessing powers they do not have.

## Historical Context

The charge of idolatry had structured religious conflict for centuries. Protestants accused Catholics of idolatry for their veneration of [[saint|saints]], relics, and the consecrated host. Catholics accused Protestants of a different kind of idolatry — bibliolatry, the worship of the [[scripture|Bible]] as a paper pope. Both accused Muslims of idolatry for venerating the Kaaba. Muslims accused [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ians of idolatry for the doctrine of the [[trinity|Trinity]] and the [[incarnation|Incarnation]]. Everyone agreed that pagans, Hindus, and "savages" were idolaters.

[[enlightenment|The Enlightenment]] subjected this merry-go-round of mutual accusation to a deflationary analysis. If [[eve]]ryone is someone else's idolater, perhaps the category itself is incoherent. Perhaps all [[worship|religious worship]] is, at bottom, the same phenomenon: the investment of material objects with imaginary powers, [[desire|drive]]n by the same psychological needs and maintained by the same priestly interests.

Holbach goes further than most of his contemporaries. He does not merely argue for [[toleration|religious toleration]] on the grounds that no one can prove their own worship is not idolatrous. He argues that the "idolatry" label is a mechanism of priestly power: by defining the other as an idolater, the priest consolidates his own authority as the guardian of "true" worship. The concept of idolatry is not a theological distinction but a political weapon.

## Core Arguments

**1. All religions practice what they condemn as idolatry in others.**
"The [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian thinks that the Koran... is but a tissue of impertinent dreams and impostures injurious to [[divinity|Divinity]]. The [[mohammed|Mohammed]]an, on his side, treats the [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian as an idolater and a dog" (116-002). The Protestant "ridicules the Catholic [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian because the latter believes in the [[secrecy|mystery]] of the transubstantiation. He treats him as a fool, as ungodly and idolatrous, because he kneels to worship the bread in which he believes he sees the [[god|God]] of [[nature|the universe]]" (002-217). Each sees the other's idolatry clearly and his own not at all.

**2. [[christianity|Christianity]] itself "has fallen into the errors and idolatry of [[paganism|Paganism]]."**
Holbach explicitly charges [[christianity|Christianity]] with having abandoned its own founding principles and reverted to [[pagan|paganism]]: "It has fallen into the errors and idolatry of [[paganism|Paganism]], as is seen by the idolatrous worship which is offered to its [[god|God]] of dough, to its [[saint|saints]], to their images, and to their relics" (APP006-010). The "[[god|God]] of dough" — the consecrated [[mass|Eucharist]]ic host — is Holbach's most biting image for the idolatrous character of [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian practice.

**3. The idolatry accusation is a tool of priestly power.**
If a priest can define which worship is "true" and which is "idolatrous," he controls access to [[god|God]]. This is a monopoly position of immense political and economic value. "Everywhere the priest places himself above the [[monarch|sovereign]]" by claiming to be the sole legitimate intermediary between humanity and the [[divinity|divine]]. The idolatry charge delegitimizes competitors and consolidates the priest's monopoly.

**4. The psychological mechanism of idolatry is universal.**
Humans [[eve]]rywhere, [[desire|drive]]n by fear of the unknown and [[passion|desire]] for control, invest material objects with imaginary powers. A consecrated host, a saint's relic, a pagan statue, a magical amulet — all serve the same psychological function. "One must be struck by a strange blindness to... open a wide door to all sorts of idolatries, to adore these paste images under the pretext that the [[clergy|priests]] have the power of consecrating them and changing them into [[god|God]]s" (APP007-013).

## In the Text

On the reciprocal accusations of idolatry between religions:

> "The Mohammedan, on his side, treats the Christian as an idolater and a dog; he sees but absurdities in his religion; he imagines he has the right to conquer his country and force him, sword in hand, to accept the faith of his Divine prophet."
>
> (116-002)

On [[christianity|Christianity]]'s reversion to pagan idolatry:

> "It has fallen into the errors and idolatry of Paganism, as is seen by the idolatrous worship which is offered to its God of dough, to its saints, to their images, and to their relics."
>
> (APP006-010)

On the [[absurdity|absurd]]ity of the idolatry distinction:

> "Do not these blind theologians see that it means opening a wide door to all sorts of idolatries, to adore these paste images under the pretext that the priests have the power of consecrating them and changing them into Gods?"
>
> (APP007-013)
