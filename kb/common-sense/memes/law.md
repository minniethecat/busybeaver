# Law

## Definition

In Holbach's *Common Sense*, "law" is fundamentally redefined in naturalistic terms. Rejecting the theological notion of law as [[divinity|divine]] command, Holbach argues that moral and political laws arise from "the eternal and necessary relations of things among themselves" (171-001). These relations are intrinsic to the [[universe|nature]] of sensible [[existence|being]]s living in society and do not depend upon the existence of any supernatural lawgiver. Law, in this view, is not an arbitrary decree imposed from above but a description of the regularities that govern human [[happiness|well-being]] and social cohesion.

The concept operates on two levels. On the moral level, law describes the natural imperative of [[eve]]ry sensible [[existence|being]] to seek pleasure and avoid [[suffering|pain]] — a principle Holbach calls "the first law of [[universe|nature]]" (ch. CXXXVIII). On the political level, law becomes the codified expression of [[utility|social utility]], [[model|ideal]]ly aligned with the interests of the governed rather than the caprice of [[clergy|priests]] or [[despotism|despot]]s. Holbach's treatment of law thus belongs to the Enlightenment tradition of natural jurisprudence, yet pushes further than most contemporaries in its explicit exclusion of [[divinity|divine]] will from the foundation of obligation.

## Historical Context

Holbach wrote *Common Sense* (1772) during the high French Enlightenment, a period marked by fierce debate over the foundations of morality and [[government|political authority]]. The dominant position, defended by apologists of the *ancien régime*, held that moral obligation and civil law both derived ultimately from [[god|God]] — without a [[divinity|divine]] lawgiver, it was argued, there could be no binding duty. This was the position of Bossuet and the Gallican tradition, which grounded royal [[tyranny|absolutism]] in [[divinity|divine]] right.

Holbach's intervention radicalises strands of thought already present in Montesquieu's *De l'esprit des lois* (1748), which had described laws as "the necessary relations deriving from the [[universe|nature]] of things," and in Helvétius's *De l'esprit* (1758), which grounded morality in physical sensibility. But where Montesquieu retained a role for religion as social cement and Helvétius stopped short of open a[[divinity|theism]], Holbach draws the full materialist conclusion: law needs no [[god|God]]. The chapter "[[religion|Religion]] and Its [[supernatural|Supernatural]] [[morality|Morality]] Are Fatal to the [[person-index|People]]" (ch. CLXXII) directly attacks the alliance of throne and altar, arguing that supernatural morality is "directly opposed to man's [[universe|nature]]."

## Core Arguments

1. **Law arises from [[universe|nature]], not from [[divinity|divine]] will.** "[[obligation|Moral obligation]] implies a law; but this law arises from the eternal and necessary relations of things among themselves, which have nothing in common with the existence of a [[god|God]]" (171-001). The rules governing human conduct spring from human [[universe|nature]] itself, which we can know, not from the [[divinity|divine nature]], of which we have no conception.

2. **The foundation of obligation is sensibility.** "The law that compels man not to harm himself, is inherent in the [[universe|nature]] of a sensible [[existence|being]]" (171-001). Because [[eve]]ry human [[existence|being]] seeks welfare and shuns [[injustice|evil]], loves pleasure and fears [[suffering|pain]], moral duties are binding without any theological sanction. "I feel, and another feels, like myself; this is the foundation of all morality" (171-001).

3. **Double legislation is fatal to [[government|the state]].** When [[politics|religion and politics]] unite, "a double legislation" results: "The law of [[god|God]], interpreted by His [[clergy|priests]], is often contrary to the law of the [[monarch|sovereign]] or to the interest of the [[politics-index|State]]" (ch. CLXXIII). [[priest|Priest]]s, claiming to represent the Celestial [[monarch|Monarch]], become "rivals or masters" of temporal [[monarch|sovereign]]s, undermining the unity of law and the stability of [[society|civil society]].

4. **[[supernatural|Supernatural]] morality is injurious to human welfare.** "Whoever has seriously meditated upon religion and its supernatural morality … will become convinced that they are both injurious to the interests of the human race, or directly opposed to man's [[universe|nature]]" (ch. CLXXII). The criterion for evaluating any system of morals is its conformity with human [[universe|nature]] — judged by this [[model|standard]], religious morality fails.

5. **No [[divinity|divine]] legislator is needed for men to know their duties.** "Do men need a [[god|God]] whom they do not know, or an invisible lawgiver, or a mysterious religion, or chimerical fears in order to comprehend that all excess tends ultimately to destroy them?" (171-001). The natural [[passion|desire]] to be loved, to do good, and to avoid hatred and vengeance suffices to ground moral conduct.

## Reception and Critique

Holbach's naturalistic theory of law provoked outrage from religious authorities — *Common Sense* was condemned by the Parlement of [[paris|Paris]] and publicly burned in 1773 — but exercised considerable influence on subsequent radical thought. Jeremy Bentham, though critical of natural-law language, arrived at a similarly secular foundation for jurisprudence in his principle of [[utility]]. In the twentieth century, legal positivists such as H. L. A. Hart would echo Holbach's separation of law from morality and [[divinity|divine]] command, though without Holbach's ambitious claim that [[universe|nature]] itself provides normative guidance.

A persistent critique, articulated already by Rousseau in *Émile*, is that reducing obligation to [[interest|self-interest]] may fail to account for genuinely moral motivations such as sacrifice or impartial justice. Holbach anticipates this objection in part: he insists that doing good to others is a form of enlightened [[interest|self-interest]], [[vice|sin]]ce "doing [[injustice|evil]] is a sure means of incurring their hatred and vengeance" (171-001). Whether this prudential reasoning fully captures the phenomenology of moral obligation remains a live philosophical question.

## Relation to Other Concepts

Law is closely linked to [[Nature]], which in Holbach's system replaces [[god|God]] as the ultimate source of order and regularity. The critique of [[divinity|divine]] law connects directly to [[Religion]], whose "supernatural morality" Holbach condemns as opposed to human [[universe|nature]]. The concept also relates to [[Government]] and [[Authority]], [[vice|sin]]ce Holbach's political theory demands that civil law be aligned with natural human interests rather than priestly or [[despotism|despot]]ic caprice. The emphasis on sensibility as the foundation of obligation ties law to [[Sensation]] and the empiricist epistemology that undergirds Holbach's entire materialist system. Finally, law inter[[sect|sects]] with [[Morality]] and [[Virtue]], which Holbach reconstructs on secular, consequentialist grounds.

## In the Text

> "Moral obligation implies a law; but this law arises from the eternal and necessary relations of things among themselves, which have nothing in common with the existence of a God. The rules which govern men's conduct spring from their own nature, which they are supposed to know, and not from the Divine nature, of which they have no conception." (171-001)

> "The law that compels man not to harm himself, is inherent in the nature of a sensible being, who, no matter how he came into this world, or what can be his fate in another, is compelled by his very nature to seek his welfare and to shun evil, to love pleasure and to fear pain." (171-001)

> "The union of religion with politics has necessarily caused a double legislation in the States. The law of God, interpreted by His priests, is often contrary to the law of the sovereign or to the interest of the State." (ch. CLXXIII)
