# Thomas Woolston

## Identity

[[woolston|Thomas Woolston]] (1668–1733) was an English theologian, Deist, and freethinker — a doctor of Cambridge University who became one of the most controversial religious critics of early eighteenth-century England. In Baron d'Holbach's *Common Sense*, [[woolston|Woolston]] is celebrated as a courageous predecessor in the rational critique of [[christianity|Christianity]], a man who "published and sold publicly at London, in his own house, sixty thousand copies of his Discourses against the [[miracle|miracles]] of [[jesus-christ|Jesus Christ]]" (APP007-021). Holbach positions [[woolston|Woolston]] alongside [[meslier|Jean Meslier]] as a priest who turned against the religion he was ordained to serve, though he judges [[woolston|Woolston]] to be the less radical of the two: "[[woolston|Woolston]] is sometimes indulgent, Meslier never" (APP007-020).

## Biography

[[woolston|Woolston]] was born in Northamptonshire and educated at Sidney Sussex College, Cambridge, where he became a fellow after ta[[monarch|king]] [[sacred|holy]] orders in [[church|the Church]] of England. His early theological work was orthodox; he published *The Old Apology for the [[truth|Truth]] of the [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian [[religion|Religion]]* (1705), a conventional defense of [[christianity|Christianity]]. But by the 1720s, [[woolston|Woolston]] had undergone a dramatic intellectual transformation. Reading the early [[church|Church]] Father [[origen|Origen]], he became convinced that the [[miracle|miracles]] of the [[bible|New Testament]] were not historical [[eve]]nts but allegories — symbolic narratives whose meaning was [[soul|spirit]]ual rather than literal.

Between 1727 and 1729, [[woolston|Woolston]] published his *Six Discourses on the [[miracle|Miracle]]s of Our [[jesus-christ|Saviour]]*, in which he argued that [[gospel|the Gospel]] [[miracle|miracles]], taken literally, were [[absurdity|absurd]], irrational, and unworthy of a [[god|divine being]]. The resurrection of Jesus, he suggested, was a "[[soul|spirit]]ual" [[eve]]nt, not a physical one; the healing [[miracle|miracles]] were allegories of moral transformation. The *Discourses* sold an estimated sixty thousand copies — an extraordinary figure for the period — and provoked a furious response from the established church. [[woolston|Woolston]] was prosecuted for blasphemy, convicted in 1729, fined one hundred pounds, and imprisoned when he could not pay. He died in prison in 1733, a martyr — in the eyes of freethinkers like Holbach — to the cause of rational inquiry.

## Core Arguments

[[woolston|Woolston]]'s central contention was that the [[miracle|miracles]] of Jesus, read literally, are "[[absurdity|absurd]] fables, injurious to divinity." The healing of the blind, the multiplication of loaves and fishes, the wal[[monarch|king]] on water, the resurrection itself — all of these, [[woolston|Woolston]] argued, become incoherent or morally objectionable when treated as historical [[eve]]nts performed by a [[god|divine being]]. Why would an omnipotent [[god|God]] heal one blind man in first-century [[palestine|Palestine]] while leaving millions blind in all subsequent centuries? Why would He multiply loaves for a [[guilt|sin]]gle crowd while permitting famines that kill multitudes? The literal reading, [[woolston|Woolston]] insisted, makes [[god|God]] arbitrary, capricious, and cruel.

[[woolston|Woolston]]'s proposed solution — that the [[miracle|miracles]] were allegories, "ignored during three hundred years by the whole [[roman-empire|Roman Empire]]" and only later literalized when "policy obliged the emperors to adopt the follies of the people" — was itself heterodox, denying as it did the historical foundation of [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian claims. But [[woolston|Woolston]]'s allegorical reading was, in a sense, a half-measure: he sought to save [[christianity|Christianity]] by reinterpreting it, whereas Holbach and Meslier sought to abolish it. This is why Holbach judges [[woolston|Woolston]] to be "sometimes indulgent" — he pulled his punches in ways that Meslier, writing in secret and for posthumous publication, did not.

Holbach's use of [[woolston|Woolston]] in *Common Sense* serves a dual purpose. Substantively, [[woolston|Woolston]]'s arguments against [[miracle|miracles]] provide ammunition for the rationalist critique. Strategically, [[woolston|Woolston]]'s biography — the Cambridge doctor who sold sixty thousand copies of his [[heresy|heretic]]al *Discourses* from his own home, was prosecuted for blasphemy, and died in prison — embodies the alliance of throne and altar that Holbach condemns. [[woolston|Woolston]] is both a source of arguments and a case study in the [[cruelty|persecution]] of reason by ecclesiastical power.

## In the Text

Holbach introduces [[woolston|Woolston]] in the opening biographical sketch of [[meslier|Jean Meslier]] that prefaces *Common Sense*: "While the abbot Meslier naively acknowledged that he did not wish to be burned till after his death, [[woolston|Thomas Woolston]], a doctor of Cambridge, published and sold publicly at London, in his own house, sixty thousand copies of his 'Discourses' against the [[miracle|miracles]] of [[jesus-christ|Jesus Christ]]" (APP007-021). The juxtaposition is carefully constructed: Meslier, the cautious provincial priest who wrote in secret; [[woolston|Woolston]], the bold Cambridge intellectual who published openly and paid the price.

Holbach continues the comparison: "The curate Meslier has gone further yet than [[woolston|Woolston]]; he dares to treat the transport of our [[jesus-christ|Saviour]] by the [[demon|devil]] upon the mountain, the wedding of Cana, the bread and the fishes, as [[absurdity|absurd]] fables, injurious to divinity.... The denunciations of the English priest do not approach those of the Champagne priest. [[woolston|Woolston]] is sometimes indulgent, Meslier never" (APP007-020). Holbach's ran[[monarch|king]] is clear — Meslier is the fuller, more uncompromising freethinker — but [[woolston|Woolston]] is honored as a genuine precursor, a man who risked and lost his liberty for the cause of rational religion.
