# Phenomena

## Definition

In Holbach's *Common Sense*, "phenomena" denotes the observable effects and [[eve]]nts of [[nature|the natural world]] — the totality of what appears to the senses and can be investigated by reason. The term is used consistently and polemically: phenomena are Holbach's evidentiary ground, the data that any adequate expla[[nations|nation]] must account for without recourse to invisible agencies. He insists that "the phenomena of [[universe|nature]] do not prove the existence of a [[god|God]], except to a few forewarned men, to whom has been shown in advance the finger of [[god|God]] in all the objects whose mechanism could embarrass them" (ch. 36). For the unprejudiced observer, phenomena reveal only "permanent and invariable law; nothing but the [[effect|necessary effect]]s of different combi[[nation|nations]] of diversified substance" (ch. 36). The concept thus serves as the empirical keystone of Holbach's naturalistic epistemology: to explain phenomena is to trace them to their natural causes; to invoke [[god|God]] is to abandon expla[[nations|nation]] for oc[[worship|cult]] evasion.

## Historical Context

Holbach's use of "phenomena" reflects the methodological [[materialism|naturalism]] that emerged from the Scientific [[revolution|Revolution]]. [[isaac-newton|Newton]] had [[demon|demons]]trated that celestial and terrestrial phenomena obeyed the same mathematical laws — the *Principia* (1687) insisted that "we are to admit no more causes of natural things than such as are both true and sufficient to explain their appearances." Holbach radicalizes this principle: where [[isaac-newton|Newton]] still required [[god|God]] to explain the initial arrangement of the solar system, Holbach argues that [[eve]]ry phenomenon is fully explicable by matter in motion operating according to invariable laws (ch. 36). The term "phenomena" also carries the weight of the early modern distinction between *phenomena* (appearances) and *noumena* (things-in-themselves), though Holbach, unlike Kant, denies that there is any knowable reality beyond [[nature|the natural world]]. For Holbach, phenomena are not a veil concealing a deeper reality; they are reality itself, and the attempt to peer behind them into [[supernatural|the supernatural]] is intellectual self-[[imposture|deception]] — a point he makes by urging that "by see[[monarch|king]] outside of [[universe|nature]] we can never find the solution of [[universe|nature]]'s problems" (ch. 104).

## Core Arguments

**1. Phenomena attest to natural law, not [[divinity|divine]] agency.** Holbach insists that [[eve]]ry observable effect is the product of material causes operating according to invariable laws. The "[[miracle|wonder]]s of [[universe|nature]]" — whether the motion of the planets, the structure of living organisms, or the processes of generation and decay — are "the [[effect|necessary effect]]s of different combi[[nation|nations]] of diversified substance" (ch. 36). To see design in them is to project human intention onto blind [[cause|causality]]. Only the "forewarned" — those indoctrinated in advance — perceive a [[divinity|divine]] finger in natural phenomena (ch. 36).

**2. Invo[[monarch|king]] [[god|God]] as the cause of phenomena is an appeal to the oc[[worship|cult]].** Holbach confronts the theologian directly: "To say that [[god|God]] is the author of the phenomena that we see, is it not attributing them to an oc[[worship|cult]] cause? What is [[god|God]]? What is a [[soul|spirit]]? They are causes of which we have no idea" (ch. 37). An expla[[nations|nation]] that invokes the inconceivable is no expla[[nations|nation]] at all; it is a confession of ignorance dressed in pious language. [[supernatural|Supernatural]] causes, far from illuminating phenomena, "will but entangle them more and more and make it impossible for you to understand yourselves" (ch. 37).

**3. [[theology|Theology]]'s explanatory failure is systematic, not occasional.** Holbach traces the historical damage done by theological [[cause|causation]] across the entire tradition of philosophy: [[religion|theology]] "substituted supernatural causes, or, rather, causes truly oc[[worship|cult]]" for natural ones; it "explained diffi[[worship|cult]] phenomena by agents more inconceivable than these phenomena; it filled discourse with words void of sense, incapable of giving the reason of things, better suited to obscure than to enlighten" (ch. 201). This is not a failure at the margins of knowledge but a failure at the center — [[religion|theology]] as a discipline is methodologically incapable of explaining anything, because its explanatory appeals are to entities whose properties are unknown and unknowable.

**4. The methodological imperative: remain within [[universe|nature]].** Holbach's positive program is stated with memorable force: "Let us remain in [[universe|nature]] when we [[passion|desire]] to understand its phenomena; let us ignore the causes which are too delicate to be seized by our organs; and let us be assured that by see[[monarch|king]] outside of [[universe|nature]] we can never find the solution of [[universe|nature]]'s problems" (ch. 104). This is a double imperative: first, refuse supernatural expla[[nations|nation]] categorically; second, accept the limits of human [[knowledge|understanding]] without filling the gaps with fictions. Honest ignorance is preferable to false certainty.

**5. The moral phenomena of [[earth|the world]] refute [[divinity|divine]] [[goodness|benevolence]].** If phenomena really proved the existence of an infinitely good [[god|God]], as theologians claim, then the existence of [[injustice|evil]] would be unintelligible. Yet "although this [[universe|world]] is full of [[injustice|evil]]s, although disorder reigns here very often, although men groan [[eve]]ry moment under the fate which oppresses them, we ought to be convinced that these effects are due to a benevolent and immutable [[cause|Cause]]; and many people beli[[eve]] it, or pretend to beli[[eve]] it!" (ch. 54). Holbach's irony is sharp: the theological inference from phenomena to a good [[god|God]] is maintained only by systematically ignoring the most salient phenomena — [[evil|suffering]], disorder, and injustice — or by a willful pretense of belief.

## In the Text

On the naturalistic interpretation of phenomena:

> "The unprejudiced philosopher sees nothing in the wonders of nature but permanent and invariable law; nothing but the necessary effects of different combinations of diversified substance." (036-001)

On the vacuity of supernatural expla[[nations|nation]]:

> "To say that God is the author of the phenomena that we see, is it not attributing them to an occult cause? What is God? What is a spirit? They are causes of which we have no idea. Sages! study nature and her laws; and when you can from them unravel the action of natural causes, do not go in search of supernatural causes, which, very far from enlightening your ideas, will but entangle them more and more and make it impossible for you to understand yourselves." (037-001)

On the methodological imperative of [[materialism|naturalism]]:

> "Let us remain in nature when we desire to understand its phenomena; let us ignore the causes which are too delicate to be seized by our organs; and let us be assured that by seeking outside of nature we can never find the solution of nature's problems." (104-002)

On [[religion|theology]]'s corruption of philosophy through oc[[worship|cult]] [[cause|causation]]:

> "For physical and simple causes, this philosophy substituted supernatural causes, or, rather, causes truly occult; it explained difficult phenomena by agents more inconceivable than these phenomena; it filled discourse with words void of sense, incapable of giving the reason of things, better suited to obscure than to enlighten, and which seem invented but to discourage man, to guard him against the powers of his own mind, to make him distrust the principles of reason and evidence, and to surround the truth with an insurmountable barrier." (ch. 201)
