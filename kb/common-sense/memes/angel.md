# Angel

## Definition

Angels occupy an intermediate position in [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian cosmology between [[god|God]] and humanity — pure [[soul|spirit]]s, created by [[god|God]], capable of moral choice, some of whom fell while others remained [[religion|faith]]ful.
For Holbach, angels are not real [[existence|being]]s but conceptual placeholders.
They are invoked whenever [[religion|theology]] needs to explain something without explaining it: how [[god|God]] communicates with the [[earth|material world]], why there is [[injustice|evil]], what exists beyond human perception.

Holbach treats belief in angels as [[model|exemplar]]y of religion's tendency to multiply invisible entities.
"Aerial [[existence|being]]s, [[soul|spirit]]s, angels, [[demon|demons]], genii, and other phantoms" are "the object of the meditations of our most profound thinkers" (199-002), yet they rest on no [[proof|evidence]] beyond ancient speculation.
[[theology|Theology]] builds entire [[knowledge|science]]s — angelology, [[demon|demonology]] — on foundations of pure imagi[[nations|nation]].

The concept of angels also serves a polemical function in Holbach's argument against [[divinity|divine]] omnipotence.
If [[god|God]] could create angels who remained [[vice|sin]]less, why could He not create [[vice|sin]]less humans?
The asymmetry exposes the [[contradiction|incoherence]] of free-will [[injustice|theodicy]].

## Historical Context

[[belief|Belief]] in angels was universal in eighteenth-century [[christianity|Christianity]].
The [[catholic-church|Catholic Church]] taught that each soul had a guardian angel; angels featured prominently in liturgy, art, and popular devotion.
Protestant confessions generally retained angelology, though they eliminated the [[worship|cult]] of guardian angels.

The intellectual challenge to angelology came from two directions.
Materialist philosophy, which Holbach represented, denied the existence of [[spirit|immaterial substance]]s altogether — angels were impossible a priori because pure [[soul|spirit]] cannot exist.
Biblical criticism, meanwhile, traced the Hebrew *mal'akh* and Greek *angelos* back to their origins as [[divinity|divine]] messengers in [[pagan|polytheist]]ic courts, where a high god needed intermediary [[existence|being]]s to interact with the [[earth|material world]].

## Core Arguments

**1. Angels are hypotheses without [[proof|evidence]].**
"Hypotheses, invented by a few visionists of [[egypt|Memphis]] and of [[babylon|Babylon]], continue to be the basis of a [[knowledge|science]] revered for the obscurity which surrounds it" (199-002).
The entire angelic hierarchy is a legacy of ancient Near Eastern mythology, not revelation.

**2. The existence of [[vice|sin]]less angels defeats free-will [[injustice|theodicy]].**
If angels were created free yet some never [[vice|sin]]ned, then [[liberty|freedom]] does not entail [[vice|sin]].
[[god|God]] could have created humans with the same disposition: "If [[god|God]] could create angels who have not [[vice|sin]]ned, could He not create men [[vice|sin]]less?" (076-002).
The answer reveals the arbitrariness of theological expla[[nation|nations]] of [[injustice|evil]].

**3. Angelic rebellion contradicts [[perfection|divine perfection]].**
That angels "revolted against their Master, and [[eve]]n attempted to expel Him from His throne" (069-002) means [[god|God]]'s creation was flawed from its highest level.
A perfect creator whose most exalted creatures rebel is a [[contradiction|logical contradiction]].

**4. Angels are part of a larger [[model|pattern]] of inventing [[existence|being]]s to fill explanatory gaps.**
From the oyster to the angel, [[religion|theology]] populates a "great chain of [[existence|being]]" that serves no purpose but to make [[nature|the universe]] seem designed.
Holbach argues this is projection, not discovery.

## Reception and Critique

[[belief|Belief]] in angels declined gradually rather than through direct philosophical refutation.
By the nineteenth century, liberal Protestantism had largely demythologized angelology, treating angels as symbolic rather than literal.
Catholic doctrine retained angels but relegated them to an increasingly marginal position in practical piety.

Holbach's conflation of all "aerial [[existence|being]]s" — angels, [[demon|demons]], genii — under the [[vice|sin]]gle heading of mythology is rhetorically effective but historically imprecise.
These categories have distinct origins and functions across religious traditions.
Nevertheless, his core point stands: [[existence|being]]s posited without [[proof|evidence]], invoked only to solve problems created by other unverified posits, are not expla[[nation|nations]] but symptoms of intellectual failure.

## Relation to Other Concepts

Angels are the positive counterpart to [[demon|demons]] — both are "aerial [[existence|being]]s" invented to populate the invisible [[universe|world]].
They relate to [[superstition]] as one of its most elaborate conceptual productions.

The concept of angels is tied to [[spirit]], the claim that [[spirit|immaterial substance]] can exist and act — a claim Holbach rejects as meaningless.
Angels also relate to [[theism]]: the more [[attribute|attributes]] [[religion|theology]] assigns to [[god|God]], the more intermediary [[existence|being]]s it needs to bridge [[god|God]] and the [[earth|material world]].
