# Intolerance

## Definition

Intolerance, in Holbach's analysis, is not a contingent failing of individual believers but a structural feature of revealed [[religion]]. Every religion that claims to possess exclusive [[truth]] about the will of [[god]] necessarily regards those who hold different beliefs as enemies of that god.

[[sin|Sin]]ce an omnipotent [[god|deity]] who punishes [[atheism|unbelief]] in the next [[universe|world]] could not logically regard [[atheism|unbelief]] with indifference in this one, the religious believer is trained to see the [[heresy|heretic]] not merely as mistaken but as impious. Holbach traces the chain from theological premise to political violence: [[divinity|divine]] exclusivism → the duty to defend orthodoxy → the "[[soul|spirit]] of [[cruelty|persecution]]" (Ch. 76) → the burning of [[heresy|heretic]]s and the suppression of [[liberty]].

## Historical Context

Holbach wrote in the aftermath of the French Wars of [[religion|Religion]] and the [[edict-of-nantes|Revocation of the Edict of Nantes]] (1685), which had [[desire|drive]]n hundreds of thousands of Protestants from [[france|France]]. The eighteenth century still witnessed the [[cruelty|persecution]] of religious minorities, the censorship of heterodox writings, and the occasional execution of [[blasphemy|blasphemer]]s.

[[enlightenment|The Enlightenment]]'s campaign for toleration — articulated by [[john-locke|Locke]], [[pierre-bayle|Bayle]], and [[voltaire|Voltaire]] — provided Holbach with an established vocabulary. But where [[john-locke|Locke]] grounded toleration in the [[universe|nature]] of [[charity|Christian charity]] and [[pierre-bayle|Bayle]] in the rights of [[remorse|conscience]], Holbach grounded it in [[materialism]]: if belief is caused by education and circumstance, not chosen freely, then punishing belief is as irrational as punishing a person for the color of their eyes. This argument represents the most radical version of the Enlightenment case for toleration.

## Theological Roots

Holbach locates the ultimate source of [[persecution|intolerance]] in the character attributed to the [[god|deity]]. A god who is jealous, wrathful, and punitive creates worshippers in his image. "A religion founded on the caprice of an unjust and partial [[god|God]]" can only produce "subjects who are, like their [[god|God]], unjust and partial" (Ch. 76).

The priest [[worship|cult]]ivates [[persecution|intolerance]] as "the most [[sacred|holy]] obligation" and "the true foundation of his future authority" (Ch. 76), [[doctrine|teaching]] the [[monarch|prince]] that suppressing [[heresy|religious dissent]] is a sacred duty rather than a political crime. The result is that [[monarch|ruler]]s become "turbulent fanatics" and "tyrants" in service of a theological [[model|ideal]] serving only clerical interests.

## Intolerance and Political Power

Holbach emphasizes that religious intolerance is always mediated through [[politics|political power]]. The priest cannot persecute on his own — he requires the sword of the magistrate. This is why the education of [[monarch]]s is crucial to Holbach's political program. A [[monarch|king]] trained from childhood to regard [[persecution|intolerance]] as a [[morality|virtue]] will "suppress at the instigation of his [[clergy|priests]] all those who think differently from them" (Ch. 76). The alliance of [[throne-and-altar]] is nowhere more visible than in the history of [[persecution|religious persecution]].

## Toleration as an Alternative

Holbach does not merely critique [[persecution|intolerance]] but advocates for its opposite: [[toleration]]. A society governed by [[reason]] rather than revelation would recognize that religious opinions, like all opinions, are the involuntary product of education and circumstance. No one chooses to beli[[eve]] or disbeli[[eve]] — belief is caused, not chosen — and therefore no one deserves punishment for holding a mistaken belief.

## Historical Examples

Holbach's corpus abounds with examples of religious intolerance: the [[inquisition]], the [[cruelty|persecution]] of the [[jesuits]], the [[bartholomew-massacre]], the burning of [[heretic]]s, the suppression of [[free-thought]] by both Catholic and Protestant authorities. His point is that these are not aberrations but the predictable output of a system whose premises — [[revelation|divine revelation]], exclusive truth, [[hell|eternal punishment]] — make [[persecution|intolerance]] a logical duty.

## In the Text

> "A religion founded on the caprice of an unjust and partial God, whose minister is a priest a fanatic or a knave, could only produce subjects who are, like their God, unjust and partial. It is but to flatter the passions of kings and to consecrate their whims, in order to render them the tyrants of their subjects." (Ch. 76)

This passage links [[religion|theology]], [[priest|priestcraft]], and politics in a [[guilt|sin]]gle causal chain. The character of the [[god|deity]] determines the character of the worshipper; the priest shapes the [[monarch|king]]'s [[knowledge|understanding]] of his duties; the [[monarch|king]] becomes a tyrant enforcing theological conformity. Intolerance is not an unfortunate side-effect of religion but a direct consequence of its fundamental premises.

> "The spirit of persecution is as old as the world. It has been the constant principle of all religions." (Ch. 76)

The universality of the claim is deliberate. Holbach refuses to let [[christianity|Christianity]] claim exemption from the history of [[cruelty|persecution]] on the grounds that its founder preached peace. What matters is not the [[doctrine|teaching]]s but the structural logic of exclusive revelation, which in [[eve]]ry religion produces the same result.

## Reception and Critique

Holbach's analysis of [[persecution|intolerance]] as a structural consequence of [[revelation|revealed religion]]'s truth-claims represents one of the most radical arguments of the Enlightenment. It goes beyond the more cautious positions of [[john-locke|Locke]] (who excluded Catholics and [[atheism|atheist]]s from toleration) and [[eve]]n [[voltaire|Voltaire]] (who tempered his critique with deistic commitments). Holbach's materialist [[effect|determinism]] — the claim that belief is caused, not chosen — provides what is arguably the most philosophically rigorous foundation for toleration produced by the Enlightenment, [[guilt|sin]]ce it removes any ground for moral blame directed at the holder of mistaken beliefs.

Contemporary liberal political philosophy has largely adopted Holbach's conclusion — that [[toleration|religious toleration]] should be comprehensive and grounded in secular principles — while rejecting or ignoring his materialist premises. The tension between Holbach's radical foundation and liberalism's more pragmatic justifications for toleration remains a subject of scholarly interest.
