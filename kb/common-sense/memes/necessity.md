# Necessity

## Definition

Necessity occupies a foundational position in Holbach's materialist system. It is the principle that [[eve]]rything in [[nature|the universe]] — [[eve]]ry motion, combi[[nations|nation]], and [[eve]]nt — occurs as the necessary result of antecedent causes acting according to immutable laws. "[[nothingness|Nothing]] in [[universe|nature]] can be made by [[probability|chance]]; all follow fixed laws; these laws are but the necessary union of certain effects with their causes" (ch. 43). For Holbach, necessity is not a metaphysical abstraction but an observable feature of [[nature|the natural world]]: an atom of matter does not encounter another "by [[chance|accident]] or by [[chance|hazard]]" but "due to permanent laws, which cause each [[existence|being]] to act by necessity as it does, and can not act otherwise under the same circumstances." This thoroughgoing [[effect|determinism]] serves as the philosophical engine for his rejection of [[liberty|free will]], [[divinity|divine]] intervention, [[miracle|miracles]], and all forms of supernatural [[cause|causation]].

## Historical Context

Holbach's concept of necessity draws on several Enlightenment and classical sources. From [[baruch-spinoza|Spinoza]] came the idea of a [[vice|sin]]gle substance governed by necessary laws; from the Epicurean tradition revived by Gassendi came atomistic [[matter|materialism]]; from [[isaac-newton|Newton]] came the vision of a universe operating according to uniform physical laws. What distinguished Holbach was his willingness to extend mechanistic necessity from physics to psychology and [[morality|ethics]], arguing that human actions, no less than planetary motions, are determined by prior causes. This placed him at odds with the moderate Enlightenment of [[voltaire|Voltaire]] and Rousseau, who retained a role for [[liberty|free will]] and moral responsibility, and made him a central figure in the radical wing of the French Enlightenment alongside Diderot.

## Core Arguments

Holbach's case for universal necessity proceeds along several lines:

1. **The [[universe|Universe]] Is a [[cause|Cause]], Not an [[effect|Effect]]**: "The universe is not an effect; it is the cause of all effects; all the [[existence|being]]s it embraces are the [[effect|necessary effect]]s of this cause" (ch. 43). There is no need for a [[cause|first cause]] outside [[universe|nature]].

2. **[[chance|Chance]] Is [[ignorance|Ignorance]]**: "Everything is made by [[probability|chance]] for those who do not understand [[universe|nature]], the properties of [[existence|being]]s, and the effects which must necessarily result from the concurrence of certain causes" (ch. 43). The word "[[probability|chance]]" merely covers human ignorance of real causes.

3. **Determinism Without Fatalism**: Necessity is not blind fate but the intelligible operation of natural laws. All [[existence|being]]s act "by necessity as it does, and can not act otherwise under the same circumstances" (ch. 43) — a formulation that preserves the regularity and predictability of [[universe|nature]].

4. **Human Actions Are Necessitated**: Just as physical bodies obey laws of motion, human [[existence|being]]s — as organized material [[existence|being]]s — act according to their [[temperament|constitution]], temperament, and circumstances. This undermines the [[doctrine|theological doctrine]] of [[liberty|free will]].

5. **[[miracle|Miracle]]s Are Impossible**: If [[universe|nature]] operates by necessary laws, then any interruption of those laws (a miracle) is logically impossible. What appears miraculous is merely an effect whose cause we do not yet understand (ch. 44).

## Reception and Critique

Holbach's strict [[effect|determinism]] provoked intense opposition. Critics charged that it eliminated human [[liberty|freedom]] and moral responsibility, reducing persons to automata. Kant's critical philosophy can be read partly as a response to the challenge posed by Holbachian necessity: how to reconcile the deterministic realm of phenomena with the [[liberty|freedom]] required for morality. Within [[matter|materialism]], Marx and Engels admired Holbach's system-building ambition while criticizing its mechanical character. In the 20th century, compatibilist philosophers have revisited Holbach's arguments, noting that his [[effect|determinism]] does not necessarily preclude a meaningful conception of agency — a nuance often overlooked by his contemporaries.

## Relation to Other Concepts

Necessity is the metaphysical backbone of Holbach's entire system. It grounds his rejection of [[god]] — a [[divinity|divine]] creator who intervenes in [[universe|nature]] would violate necessary laws. It undermines [[free-will]], which Holbach saw as an illusion produced by ignorance of causes. It supports [[materialism]] by showing that matter, operating by its own laws, produces all phenomena without external guidance. It relates to [[nature]] as the totality of necessary relations, and to [[cause]] as the mechanism through which necessity operates at the level of individual [[eve]]nts. His treatment of [[miracle]] is a direct corollary of the necessity principle.

## In the Text

The locus classicus for Holbach's doctrine of necessity is chapter 43, where he explicitly rejects [[probability|chance]] and affirms universal [[effect|determinism]]:

> "All bodies, whether organized or not organized, are the necessary results of certain causes, made to produce necessarily the effects which we see. Nothing in nature can be made by chance; all follow fixed laws; these laws are but the necessary union of certain effects with their causes." (ch. 43)

He extends the argument to human cognition, noting that our perception of [[probability|chance]] merely reflects our ignorance:

> "Everything is made by chance for those who do not understand nature, the properties of beings, and the effects which must necessarily result from the concurrence of certain causes. It is not chance that has placed the sun in the center of our planetary system; it is by its very essence, the substance of which it is composed, that it occupies this place." (ch. 43)

In chapter 44, he applies the necessity doctrine to the concept of [[miracle|miracles]]:

> "We call those effects natural which we see, or which are known by experience; and we call those miraculous, the causes of which are unknown to us, and which our experience does not show us. We pronounce effects as necessary in the order of nature as the fall of heavy bodies, as soon as we understand their causes." (ch. 44)
