# Life

## Definition

In Holbach's *Common Sense*, "life" is treated primarily through the critical exami[[nations|nation]] of the [[dogma|theological dogma]] of *another life* — the doctrine of an [[death|afterlife]] in which souls are rewarded or punished. Holbach does not merely deny the existence of an [[death|afterlife]]; he systematically dismantles the arguments offered in its favor, exposes the psychological and political functions it serves, and argues that belief in another life is not consoling but terrifying. The concept is central to his materialist [[morality|ethics]]: if this life is the only life we have, then human happiness must be pursued here and now, through reason, justice, and social cooperation, rather than deferred to an imaginary future state. "The [[doctrine|dogma]] of another life is useful but for those who profit by it at the expense of the credulous public" (107-003).

## Historical Context

[[belief|Belief]] in an [[death|afterlife]] was nearly universal in 18th-century [[europe|Europe]], though its specific form varied across confessions. The [[catholic-church|Catholic Church]] maintained the doctrine of purgatory, [[hope|heaven]], and [[punishment|hell]]; Protestants had simplified the geography but retained the essential structure of postmortem reward and punishment. The radical Enlightenment, however, saw a small but influential group of materialists — La Mettrie, Diderot, Holbach — who denied personal im[[death|mortality]] altogether. Holbach's contribution was to argue not only that the [[death|afterlife]] is unproven, but that the *belief* in an [[death|afterlife]] is actively harmful: it distracts from the real conditions of human welfare and provides [[clergy|priests]] with an instrument of psychological control.

## Core Arguments

Holbach advances a comprehensive critique of the [[doctrine|dogma]] of another life:

1. **No [[evidence|Evidence]]**: "The existence of another life has no other guaranty than the imagi[[nations|nation]] of men, who, in supposing it, have but manifested their [[passion|desire]] to live again" (002-085). The [[death|afterlife]] is a wish, not a discovery.

2. **The [[evidence|Evidence]] from [[earth|Earth]]ly [[suffering|Suffering]]**: The long history of [[suffering|human misery]] — "tyrants, conquerors, heroes; wars, inundations, famines, epidemics" — does not inspire [[certainty|confidence]] in the hidden designs of a benevolent [[god|deity]] preparing a happy [[death|afterlife]]. If [[god|God]] could not or would not remove [[injustice|evil]] from this [[universe|world]], what basis is there for trusting his arrangements for the next?

3. **[[materialism|Materialism]] is More Consoling**: Contrary to the theological claim that [[matter|materialism]] leads to despair, Holbach argues that the fear of eternal [[torture|torment]] is far more afflicting than the prospect of [[death|annihilation]]. "Is not the idea of total [[death|annihilation]] infinitely preferable to the idea of an eternal existence accompanied with [[evil|suffering]] and gnashing of teeth?" (108-004).

4. **The Sociology of the [[immortality|Afterlife]]**: The [[doctrine|dogma]] of another life serves the material interests of [[clergy|the clergy]]. "If men beli[[eve]] that [[eve]]rything is to perish with the body, the geographers of the other [[universe|world]] would evidently lose the [[probability|chance]] of guiding their souls to this unknown abode. They would draw no profits from the hopes with which they feast them, and from the [[fear|terror]]s with which they take care to overwhelm them" (107-003).

5. **This Life is Enough**: The appropriate response to [[death|mortality]] is not to fantasize about another [[universe|world]] but to improve this one. The real foundation of [[morality|virtue]] is [[happiness|earthly happiness]], social reciprocity, and enlightened [[interest|self-interest]] — not the promise of [[heaven|paradise]] or the threat of [[punishment|hell]].

## Reception and Critique

Holbach's denial of an [[death|afterlife]] was among the most scandalous elements of his philosophy. Even many deists and religious skeptics retained some form of belief in postmortem existence, whether as the im[[death|mortality]] of the soul or as a future state of moral reckoning. Holbach's thoroughgoing [[matter|materialism]] placed him beyond [[eve]]n this pale. Critics argued that without the sanctions of the [[death|afterlife]], morality would collapse — the familiar charge of "if there is no [[god|God]], [[eve]]rything is permitted." Holbach replied that this objection got the psychology backwards: people behave morally because of the real consequences of their actions in this life, not because of hypothetical consequences in an imaginary one.

## Relation to Other Concepts

The concept of life connects to a network of related ideas in Holbach's philosophy. [[death]] is the natural terminus of life, and [[matter|materialism]] teaches acceptance of [[death|mortality]] rather than flight into fantasy. [[soul]] is the immaterial entity whose alleged im[[death|mortality]] grounds the [[doctrine|dogma]] of another life — and which Holbach denies exists. [[happiness]] is the proper goal of this life, and religion's deferral of happiness to an [[death|afterlife]] is one of its greatest crimes. [[materialism]] is the philosophical framework that makes sense of life as a material, biological phenomenon without supernatural additions. [[morality]] is grounded in the real conditions of [[earth|earthly existence]], not in postmortem sanctions.

## In the Text

Holbach's systematic demolition of the [[death|afterlife]] [[doctrine|dogma]] spans several chapters. The evidentiary critique is crisp:

> "The existence of another life has no other guaranty than the imagination of men, who, in supposing it, have but manifested their desire to live again, in order to enter upon a purer and more durable state of happiness than that which they enjoy at present." (002-085)

The psychological argument — that [[matter|materialism]] is more consoling than [[religion|theology]] — appears in chapter 108:

> "Those who find the idea of another life so flattering and so sweet, have they then forgotten that this other life, according to them, is to be accompanied by torments for the majority of mortals? Is not the idea of total annihilation infinitely preferable to the idea of an eternal existence accompanied with suffering and gnashing of teeth? The fear of ceasing to be, is it but an evil for the imagination, which alone brought forth the dogma of another life." (108-004)

The sociological critique — cui bono? — is most explicit in chapter 107:

> "If men believe that everything is to perish with the body, the geographers of the other world would evidently lose the chance of guiding their souls to this unknown abode. They would draw no profits from the hopes with which they feast them, and from the terrors with which they take care to overwhelm them." (107-003)
