# Gratitude

## Definition

Gratitude is the recognition of benefit received and the corresponding sentiment of thankfulness toward the benefactor. In religious [[morality|ethics]], gratitude toward [[god|God]] is treated as a fundamental duty: [[vice|sin]]ce [[god|God]] created us and sustains us, we owe Him perpetual thanks.

Holbach challenges this reasoning at its root. He does not deny that gratitude is a genuine and valuable human sentiment when it flows between persons who have actually benefited each other. What he denies is that the concept can coherently be applied to the [[god|God]] of orthodox [[religion|theology]]. In chapter XCIII of "Common Sense," titled "It Is Not True That We Owe Any Gratitude to What We Call [[providence|Providence]]," Holbach undertakes a systematic inventory of human [[evil|suffering]] and concludes that the ledger of existence, fairly audited, shows no net debt of gratitude to any [[god|divine being]].

The argument is characteristically Holbachian in its method: he does not dispute the [[religion|theology]] of gratitude from [[principle|first principles]] but instead presses the empirical question. Look at actual human life — the illnesses, bereavements, anxieties, and [[fear|terror]]s that fill it — and ask whether this is the record of a benefactor deserving thanks.

## Historical Context

The duty of gratitude to [[god|God]] was a staple of eighteenth-century religious discourse. Sermons, devotional manuals, and philosophical treatises all insisted that human [[existence|being]]s should [[worship|cult]]ivate gratitude for the blessings of creation, providence, and [[salvation|redemption]]. The language saturated public life: monarchs were "by the grace of [[god|God]]," harvest festivals gave thanks for [[divinity|divine]] bounty, and [[prayer|prayers]] of thanksgiving were woven into the fabric of daily existence.

[[enlightenment|The Enlightenment]] brought this discourse under scrutiny. Deists like [[voltaire|Voltaire]] retained gratitude toward a benevolent [[creator|Creator]]. [[atheist|Atheist]]s and materialists like Holbach went further: they argued that the concept of [[divinity|divine]] gratitude was not merely unproven but incoherent. To thank a [[existence|being]] who had never [[demon|demons]]trably done anything for you — whose very existence was uncertain — was to direct a natural human sentiment toward an imaginary object.

Holbach's treatment of gratitude is a direct application of his broader materialist [[morality|ethics]]. If morality is grounded in the real relations between real [[existence|being]]s in the real [[universe|world]], then gratitude must be owed to actual benefactors — parents, teachers, friends — not to an invisible and unverifiable [[god|God]]. Redirecting gratitude from real human benefactors to an imaginary [[divinity|divine]] one is not piety; it is a misallocation of moral sentiment.

## Core Arguments

**1. [[existence|Existence]] itself is not an unambiguous benefit.**
Theologians "boast above all that our existence is a blessing. But, alas! how many mortals are really satisfied with their [[mode|mode of existence]]? If life has its sweets, how much of bitterness is mingled with it?" (ch. XCIII). Holbach asks whether most people, given the choice, would consent to relive their lives at the same cost in [[evil|suffering]]. The question is rhetorical; the answer, he implies, is no.

**2. [[life|Life]] is filled with undeserved [[evil|suffering]] that cancels any claim to gratitude.**
"Is not one bitter trouble sufficient to blight all of a sudden the most peaceful and happy life? Is there a great number of men who, if it depended upon them, would wish to begin, at the same sacrifice, the [[suffering|pain]]ful career into which, without their consent, destiny has thrown them?" (ch. XCIII). Gratitude presupposes net benefit. If the benefits of existence are outweighed or [[eve]]n seriously challenged by its inevitable [[evil|suffering]]s, the moral calculus of gratitude collapses.

**3. We did not consent to exist.**
"It did not depend upon us to exist or not to exist" (ch. XCIII). A benefit conferred without the recipient's consent — and one from which the recipient cannot withdraw — is not a gift in any morally meaningful sense. "Would the bird be under such great obligations to the bird-catcher for having caught it in his net and for having put it into his cage, in order to eat it after [[existence|being]] amused with it?" (ch. XCIII). The metaphor is brutal but precise: if [[god|God]] creates us only to suffer and die, we owe Him nothing.

**4. Gratitude should be directed toward real human benefactors.**
Holbach does not reject gratitude as a [[morality|virtue]] — he relocates it. If gratitude is the appropriate response to genuine benefit, then it belongs in the domain of human relations: toward the parents who raised us, the teachers who educated us, the friends who supported us. [[theology|Theology]] appropriates this natural sentiment and redirects it toward a [[existence|being]] who has never [[demon|demons]]trably done anything for anyone. This is not only irrational but morally distorting: it teaches people to thank an invisible [[god|God]] while neglecting the actual human [[existence|being]]s who deserve their appreciation.
