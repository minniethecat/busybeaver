# Isaac Newton

## Biography

Isaac Newton (1643–1727) was an English mathematician, physicist, astronomer, and natural philosopher. His *Philosophiæ Naturalis Principia Mathematica* (1687) formulated the laws of motion and universal gravitation, providing the mathematical framework that dominated physical [[knowledge|science]] until the twentieth century. His work in optics established the composite [[universe|nature]] of white light, and his co-invention of calculus provided mathematics with its most powerful analytical tool. Newton also devoted extensive study to alchemy, [[religion|theology]], and biblical chronology — pursuits that, while less celebrated today, were integral to his self-[[knowledge|understanding]] as a natural philosopher uncovering [[god|God]]'s design in creation.

## Role in Religious History

Newton was a devout, if heterodox, [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian. He rejected the doctrine of the [[trinity|Trinity]], believing it a corruption introduced in the fourth century, and wrote more on [[religion|theology]] than on physics. His religious [[universe|world]]view, however, had a paradoxical effect: by [[demon|demons]]trating that [[nature|the universe]] operates according to immutable mathematical laws, Newton's physics made [[divinity|divine]] intervention seem increasingly unnecessary. The "clockwork universe" — though Newton himself attributed its order to [[god|God]]'s continual [[government|governance]] — became, in the hands of later thinkers, [[proof|evidence]] that [[universe|nature]] required no supernatural supervision. Newton thus inadvertently prepared the ground for the [[matter|materialism]] he would have abhorred.

## Connection to "Common Sense"

Holbach mentions Newton in Chapter XCVI of *Common Sense* as an [[model|exemplar]] of intellectual greatness, contrasting the "infinite distance between the genius of a [[john-locke|Locke]], of a Newton, and that of a peasant, of a Hottentot, or of a Laplander." This reference serves Holbach's argument that human intellectual inequality is a natural fact, produced by differences in organization and environment — not by [[divinity|divine]] favor or the presence of an immaterial soul. For Holbach, Newton's genius [[demon|demons]]trates that [[eve]]n the most extraordinary mental powers are products of material causes: a superior physical organization, rigorous education, and favorable circumstances. Newton's own theistic interpretation of his discoveries is implicitly set aside; what matters to Holbach is that Newton's laws describe a self-regulating [[nature|natural order]] requiring no supernatural expla[[nations|nation]].

> "What an infinite distance between the genius of a Locke, of a Newton, and that of a peasant, of a Hottentot, or of a Laplander!" — Holbach, *Common Sense*, ch. XCVI

## Legacy

Newton's scientific legacy — classical mechanics, calculus, optics — is foundational to modern [[knowledge|science]]. His theological writings, once marginalized, have attracted renewed scholarly interest for what they reveal about the relationship between [[knowledge|science]] and religion in the early modern period. For the French Enlightenment, Newton was a hero of reason whose physics, once stripped of its theological scaffolding, provided the model for a scientific [[knowledge|understanding]] of [[universe|nature]]. Holbach's deployment of Newton illustrates the Enlightenment's selective appropriation of its predecessors: Newton the theist is acknowledged and set aside; Newton the discoverer of natural law is enlisted in the cause of [[matter|materialism]].
