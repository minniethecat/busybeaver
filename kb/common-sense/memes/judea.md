# Judea

## Definition

**Judea** — the mountainous southern region of ancient [[palestine|Palestine]], centered on [[jerusalem|Jerusalem]] — appears in Holbach's "Common Sense" as the historical-geographical stage on which the biblical drama claims to have unfolded. By invo[[monarch|king]] the political realities of Roman Judea ("[[herod|Herod]], [[monarch|king]] of Judea"), Holbach anchors [[scripture|biblical]] claims in verifiable history, then uses that anchor to expose the tension between [[supernatural|the supernatural]] narrative and the mundane political context in which it is set.

## Herod's Kingdom

Holbach references Judea through its most famous client [[monarch|king]]:

> "Augustus, having heard that Herod, king of Judea, had put to death his son, said: 'It is better to be Herod's pig than his son.'" (002-166)

The reference to [[herod|Herod the Great]] (r. 37–4 BCE) — the Roman client [[monarch|king]] notorious for his cruelty and the alleged perpetrator of the [[mass|Mass]]acre of the Innocents — serves a dual function. First, it establishes the political framework: Judea under [[herod|Herod]] was a Roman dependency, its "[[monarch|king]]" a puppet of imperial [[power|power]]. Second, the anecdote about Augustus's quip (drawn from Macrobius's *Saturnalia*) illustrates the kind of secular, ironic perspective on religious claims that Holbach advocates — the Roman emperor did not take Jewish [[religion|religious]] pretensions seriously, and neither should Enlightenment readers.

## The Earthly Stage of the Heavenly Drama

Holbach's broader point about Judea is that [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ianity claims to be anchored in historical [[eve]]nts that occurred in a specific time and place — "suffered under Pontius Pilate," as the [[creed|Creed]] has it — yet the historical record of Roman Judea shows no trace of the cosmic [[eve]]nts the [[gospel|Gospels]] describe. No Roman historian records the darkness at noon, the earthquake, the bodily resurrection of [[saint|saints]] who "appeared to many" in [[jerusalem|Jerusalem]] ([[matthew|Matthew]] 27:52–53). The specificity of the claim ("in Judea, under Pontius Pilate") makes its historical implausibility *more* rather than less apparent. A myth set in a vague "once upon a time" is harder to falsify than one set in a documented Roman province under a documented Roman prefect.

## Judea as Contested Territory

The land of Judea was contested throughout antiquity — by [[babylon|Babylonia]]ns, Persians, Greeks (Seleucids and Ptolemies), and Romans. The Jewish revolts of 66–70 CE and 132–135 CE resulted in the destruction of the [[temple|Second Temple]] and the renaming of the province as Syria Palaestina. Holbach's [[enlightenment|Enlightenment]] readers would have understood Judea as a territory defined by conquest, rebellion, and imperial administration — a far cry from the "[[palestine|Holy Land]]" of pious imagi[[nations|nation]]. The contrast between the sacred geography of [[scripture|Scripture]] and the profane geography of imperial conquest is, for Holbach, the contrast between [[religion|religious]] illusion and historical reality.
