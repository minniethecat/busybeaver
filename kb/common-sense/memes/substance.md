# Substance

## Definition

Substance, in Holbach's [[materialism|materialist philosophy]], is the fundamental reality that underlies all observable phenomena. Against the dualism of Cartesian and theological metaphysics — which posits two kinds of substance, material and [[soul|spirit]]ual — Holbach insists that there is only one substance: matter. Everything that exists, from inorganic bodies to living organisms to the phenomena we call "thought" and "consciousness," is a [[mode|modification]] of [[matter|material substance]] in motion. A substance without extension, without tangible properties, without anything "in common with matter" is literally unthinkable: it is, as Holbach puts it, "an absence of ideas" (021-001).

## Historical Context

The concept of substance stood at the center of early modern metaphysics. [[rene-descartes|Descartes]] had divided reality into *res extensa* (extended substance) and *res cogitans* (thin[[monarch|king]] substance), leaving philosophers with the problem of how two radically different substances could interact. [[baruch-spinoza|Spinoza]] dissolved the problem by positing a [[vice|sin]]gle substance with infinite [[attribute|attributes]], of which thought and extension are two aspects known to us. Holbach, writing in the radical Enlightenment tradition, takes the materialist route: there is only one substance, and it is matter. The "[[soul|spirit]]ual substance" of [[religion|theology]] is a relic of primitive animism, preserved by [[priest|priestcraft]] to maintain intellectual authority over the laity.

## Core Arguments

Holbach advances four principal arguments against [[spirit|immaterial substance]] and for a unified materialist [[existence|ontology]]:

**First: The Unintelligibility of [[spirit|Spirit]].** When theologians are asked what a [[soul|spirit]] is, they reply that it is a substance that is simple, incorporeal, and without extension. But these are all negative properties — they tell us what [[soul|spirit]] is *not*, never what it *is*. Positive predicates can only be drawn from sense experience, which is experience of matter. A substance defined entirely by negations is not a concept at all; it is, Holbach w[[ritual|rites]], "an idea without a model" (021-001).

**Second: The Argument from [[motion|Motion]].** All change in [[nature|the universe]] is motion of material bodies. If [[soul|spirit]]ual substance were to act upon matter, it would have to impart motion — but imparting motion requires contact, and contact requires extension. An unextended substance cannot move an extended one. The hypothesis of [[soul|spirit]] is therefore causally impotent; material motion requires only material causes.

**Third: The Diversity of Substances Explained by Combi[[nations|nation]].** The apparent diversity of [[existence|being]]s — from stones to plants to [[soul|anima]]ls to human minds — does not require multiple kinds of substance. It is fully explained by "different combi[[nation|nations]] of diversified substance" (036-001), that is, by the heterogeneous arrangements of material particles. [[nature|Nature]] is a vast laboratory of material recombi[[nations|nation]], not a hierarchy of ontologically distinct substances.

**Fourth: Dead Substances Engender [[motion|Motion]].** Holbach points to spontaneous chemical reactions — pyrophorus igniting on contact with air, flour and water fermenting when mixed — as [[proof|evidence]] that apparently inert matter contains latent energy. "Thus dead substances engender motion of themselves. [[matter|Matter]] has then the power to move itself, and [[universe|nature]], in order to act, does not need a motor whose essence would hinder its activity" (041-002).

## Reception and Critique

Holbach's substance monism was among the most radical positions of the Enlightenment, going beyond [[eve]]n [[john-locke|Locke]]'s cautious agnosticism about the possibility of thin[[monarch|king]] matter. It influenced later materialists from Diderot to Marx, who adopted the principle that matter is self-moving and requires no external motor. Critics, from [[voltaire|Voltaire]] to later [[model|ideal]]ists, charged that Holbach's eliminativism about consciousness — reducing thought to a property of organized matter — failed to account for the first-person character of experience. The "hard problem" of consciousness, in anachronistic terms, remains a challenge for any substance monism of the kind Holbach advanced.

## Relation to Other Concepts

Substance is the ontological backbone of Holbach's philosophical system. If substance is exclusively material, then [[God]] as an immaterial [[existence|being]] cannot exist. The [[Soul]] as an [[spirit|immaterial substance]] is a contradiction in terms. [[Matter]] is not one substance among others but the only substance. The concept of substance underw[[ritual|rites]] Holbach's [[Materialism]], grounds his critique of [[Theology]], and provides the metaphysical basis for his [[morality|ethics]] — if humans are material [[existence|being]]s, then [[Happiness]] must be pursued through material means in this life, not deferred to an immaterial [[death|afterlife]].

## In the Text

On the unintelligibility of [[soul|spirit]]ual substance:

> "Ask him what he means by a spirit? He will answer, that it is an unknown substance, which is perfectly simple, which has nothing tangible, nothing in common with matter. In good faith, is there any mortal who can form the least idea of such a substance? A spirit in the language of modern theology is then but an absence of ideas." (021-001)

On the self-motion of [[matter|material substance]]:

> "Thus dead substances engender motion of themselves. Matter has then the power to move itself, and nature, in order to act, does not need a motor whose essence would hinder its activity." (041-002)

On substance diversity as material combi[[nations|nation]]:

> "The phenomena of nature do not prove the existence of a God, except to a few forewarned men... The unprejudiced philosopher sees nothing in the wonders of nature but permanent and invariable law; nothing but the necessary effects of different combinations of diversified substance." (036-001)
