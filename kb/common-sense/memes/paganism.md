# Paganism

## Definition

In Holbach's critique of [[christianity|Christianity]], [[pagan|paganism]] serves a crucial comparative function. By [[demon|demons]]trating that [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian miracle claims, doctrines, and institutional structures have exact parallels in the pagan religions that [[christianity|Christianity]] claims to supersede, Holbach undermines the argument from uniqueness. If pagan [[miracle|miracles]] are admitted to be fables, [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian [[miracle|miracles]] — built on the same evidentiary basis — must be judged by the same [[model|standard]].

Holbach does not defend [[pagan|paganism]] as true; he uses it as a mirror to expose [[christianity|Christianity]]'s pretensions. The historical record shows that "during thousands of years Paganism, Poly[[divinity|theism]], and [[idolatry|Idolatry]] have been the religions of [[earth|the world]]" — a fact incompatible with the claim that a benevolent [[god|God]] has guided humanity toward truth.

## Holbach's Arguments

**1. The historical dominance of [[pagan|paganism]] refutes providence.** If [[god|God]] cares about true religion, why did He allow [[pagan|paganism]] to reign for millennia while "the most flourishing [[nation|nations]] had not the least idea of the [[deity|Deity]]"? The long reign of [[pagan|paganism]] is a problem for any [[religion|theology]] that claims [[divinity|divine]] guidance of history.

**2. [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian [[miracle|miracles]] have pagan templates.** In the appended essay on [[miracle|miracles]], Holbach catalogs extensive parallels: "the [[pagan|Pagan]]s had said before them that Remus and Romulus, the founders of [[rome|Rome]], were sons of a god and a virgin"; "the [[pagan|Pagan]]s claim that Epimenides, the philosopher, slept during fifty-seven years"; "the [[pagan|Pagan]]s say that their [[prophecy|oracle]]s have spoken." The conclusion is devastating: "There is certainly a great similarity between the [[pagan|Pagan]] [[miracle|miracles]] and our own."

**3. The ethical critique cuts both ways.** Holbach asks whether [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ians would [[desire|want]] to "imitate the good and great [[jupiter|Jupiter]] of ancient Paganism" — "a rebellious son, who wrests his father's throne from him," "a debauchee and adulterer, an incestuous, intemperate man." But the same critique applies to biblical figures held up as moral [[model|exemplar]]s.

**4. [[christianity|Christianity]] adopted pagan [[model|pattern]]s.** [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian emperors "spread it marvelously in their empire by sword and fire, and founded it upon the ruins of overthrown Paganism." The method of propagation — violence — was inherited, not transcended.

## In the Text

On the historical dominance of [[pagan|paganism]] (ch. LXXXIX):

> "During thousands of years Paganism, Polytheism, and Idolatry have been the religions of the world; we are assured today, that during this period the most flourishing nations had not the least idea of the Deity."
>
> (002-277)

On the comparative method (Of [[miracle|Miracle]]s, ch. III):

> "There is certainly a great similarity between the Pagan miracles and our own. If the former are false, as our doctors of the Church teach, the latter are false also."
>
> (003-021)

On pagan parallels to [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian [[miracle|miracles]]:

> "If they have said that the Holy Virgin, when she gave birth to the Son of God, did not cease to be a virgin, the Pagans had said before them that Remus and Romulus, the founders of Rome, were sons of a god and a virgin."
>
> (APP007-001)

On the problem for [[providence|divine providence]]:

> "If miracles were necessary to establish the Christian religion, miracles were then no less necessary for each new sect; but after the extinction of Paganism the people established a religious principle of going into a frenzy, every time that an opinion was brought forth."
>
> (APP006-007)

## Relation to Other Concepts

Paganism connects to [[idolatry]], [[polytheism]], [[superstition]], and [[miracle]]. The comparative method links it to [[christianity]], [[revelation]], and [[scripture]]. Holbach uses [[pagan|paganism]] to [[demon|demons]]trate that [[religion]] is a universal human phenomenon arising from natural psychological causes, not a response to unique [[divinity|divine]] intervention.
