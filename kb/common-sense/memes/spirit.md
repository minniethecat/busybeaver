# Spirit

## Definition

In Holbach's [[materialism|materialist philosophy]], "[[soul|spirit]]" is the paradigmatic example of a word without a concept — a term that appears to mean something but, when examined, dissolves into [[contradiction|incoherence]]. "To say that [[god|God]] is a [[soul|spirit]] is to speak without saying anything" (ch. XIX). The notion of an immaterial substance is a contradiction in terms: a "substance" is something that exists, but "immaterial" means not consisting of matter — and what exists that is not matter? Holbach argues that no one who uses the word "[[soul|spirit]]" can attach a clear idea to it, and that its function is precisely its lack of clarity: it allows theologians to make claims that cannot be tested or understood.

This critique of spirituality is central to Holbach's broader materialist project. If "[[soul|spirit]]" is meaningless, then so are all doctrines that depend on it: the im[[death|mortality]] of the soul, the existence of a [[soul|spirit]]ual [[god|God]], the distinction between the material body and the immaterial soul. What remains is a [[universe|world]] composed entirely of matter in motion — a [[universe|world]] fully intelligible to [[knowledge|science]] and reason.

## Historical Context

Holbach's rejection of [[soul|spirit]] was part of the 18th-century materialist challenge to Cartesian dualism. [[rene-descartes|Descartes]] had divided reality into two substances — extended matter and thin[[monarch|king]] [[soul|spirit]] — creating a metaphysical framework that seemed to make room for both [[knowledge|science]] (the study of matter) and religion (the study of [[soul|spirit]]). [[hobbes|Hobbes]] had already challenged this by arguing that thin[[monarch|king]] is a material process. La Mettrie's *Man a Machine* (1748) extended [[matter|materialism]] to the human mind. Holbach's *System of [[nature|Nature]]* (1770) provided the most comprehensive statement of materialist metaphysics, and the spiritualism critique in "Common Sense" is its popular, accessible expression.

## Core Arguments

**1. "Spirit" is a word without meaning.** Holbach demands that anyone using the word "[[soul|spirit]]" attach a clear idea to it. What are its properties? How does it act on matter? How can an immaterial substance exist in space? No coherent answers are possible, because the word was invented precisely to be unexaminable — to place [[god|God]] "above reason" where reason cannot touch him.

**2. Spirituality is a chimera.** A whole chapter (ch. XXI) is devoted to this claim. If [[soul|spirit]] has no location, no extension, no parts, and no material properties, it is indistinguishable from nothing. The assertion that [[god|God]] is a [[soul|spirit]] is therefore equivalent to the assertion that [[god|God]] is nothing — or that the speaker does not know what they are tal[[monarch|king]] about.

**3. The mind-body problem is a pseudo-problem created by spiritualism.** Once "[[soul|spirit]]" is abandoned, there is no [[secrecy|mystery]] about how an immaterial soul interacts with a material body — there is no immaterial soul. [[thought|Thought]] is a function of the brain, just as digestion is a function of the stomach.

**4. Spiritualism is politically useful to [[clergy|priests]].** The doctrine that the soul is immaterial and immortal gives [[clergy|priests]] leverage over human conduct: they can threaten [[hell|eternal punishment]] for actions that society might find harmless. "Spirit" is thus not a philosophical concept but a political instrument.

## Reception and Critique

The [[soul|spirit]]ualist response to Holbach was articulated most systematically by [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian Wolff and later by German [[model|ideal]]ists, who argued that Holbach's [[matter|materialism]] begged the question by assuming that only material things exist. Holbach would reply that the burden of [[evidence|proof]] lies with those who assert the existence of immaterial substances — until they can give "[[soul|spirit]]" a clear meaning, [[matter|materialism]] remains the default position. Contemporary philosophy of mind, with its widespread rejection of Cartesian dualism in favor of [[materialism|physicalism]], has largely vindicated Holbach's direction of argument, if not his specific formulations.

## Relation to Other Concepts

Spirit is the metaphysical foundation of [[Theology]], which cannot function without the claim that something immaterial exists. It is the supposed substance of the [[Soul]], which Holbach argues is simply the living body organized in a particular way. The critique of [[soul|spirit]] is connected to the critique of [[God]], [[vice|sin]]ce the only "[[god|God]]" [[religion|theology]] can describe is an immaterial one. Spirit is opposed to [[Nature]] and [[Matter]], which for Holbach constitute the totality of what exists. The rejection of [[soul|spirit]] clears the path for [[Science]], which studies material reality without appeal to immaterial causes.

## In the Text

On the emptiness of "[[soul|spirit]]":

> "To say that God is a Spirit, is to speak without saying any thing; it is to say, I know not what. What idea can be formed of a pure Spirit? It is something of which we can form no idea. Thus, to say that God is a Spirit, is to say, We do not know what God is." (ch. XIX)

On spirituality as a chimera:

> "Spirituality is a chimera. No one has any idea of a spirit; and how can we attribute to God qualities, of which we have no idea? The word spirit presents to the mind only a vague idea of a breath, a vapor, or of any thing whatever that is not tangible. Thus a Spirit is something that has none of the qualities of matter." (ch. XXI)

On the [[absurdity|absurd]]ity of worshipping an inconceivable [[existence|being]]:

> "It would be much more rational to worship the sun than a Spiritual God; at least we see the sun, we feel its benefits; but a Spiritual God is a being that we can neither see, nor feel, nor understand." (ch. XXII)

On the materialist alternative:

> "When we shall demand of a theologian what he understands by a Spirit, he will answer, that it is a substance, which has none of the properties of matter. But what then is it? He knows nothing of it. His ignorance is the measure of his faith." (ch. XXIII)
