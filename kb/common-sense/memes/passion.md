# Passion

## Definition

[[passions|Passions]], in Holbach's naturalistic anthropology, are the affective and conative forces that move human [[existence|being]]s to act — desires, aversions, loves, hatreds, ambitions, and fears. They are not [[sin|sinful]] intrusions into an otherwise rational soul but the very motors of human life, grounded in our material [[temperament|constitution]]. A [[existence|being]] without passions would be a [[existence|being]] without motives — inert, incapable of pursuing any good or avoiding any [[injustice|evil]]. The moral question is not whether we have passions but which passions we [[worship|cult]]ivate and how we direct them.

## Passions as Morally Neutral Forces

Holbach rejects the religious condem[[nations|nation]] of the passions as inherently corrupt. [[passions|Passions]] are [[phenomena|natural phenomena]], like hunger or gravity — they are neither good nor [[injustice|evil]] in themselves. Their moral quality depends on their objects and on the social context in which they operate:

> "The majority of men rarely think of God, or, at least, do not occupy themselves much with Him. The idea of God has so little stability, it is so afflicting, that it can not hold the imagination for a long time... The business man thinks of nothing but his affairs; the courtier of his intrigues; worldly men, women, youth, of their pleasures." (166-001)

This passage, while ostensibly about the ineffectiveness of the idea of [[god|God]], reveals Holbach's clear-eyed view of human motivation: people are moved by their immediate passions — business, intrigue, pleasure — not by abstract theological concepts. The "fear of [[god|God]]" is powerless against human passions because it is itself a weak and unstable passion, easily displaced by stronger, more immediate desires.

## The Nobility of Intellectual Passion

Holbach does not treat all passions as equal. Some passions ennoble and civilize; others degrade and brutalize. The highest passion, in his view, is the love of truth:

> "Every man who loves to retire within himself in order to study and seek for the principles of things, has no very dangerous passions; his greatest passion will be to know the truth, and his greatest ambition to show it to others." (ch. 170)

[[philosophy|Philosophy]] — the systematic pursuit of truth — is itself a passion, one that counteracts the destructive passions that arise from ignorance. The philosopher's passion for knowledge makes him less dangerous to society than the fanatic's passion for [[doctrine|dogma]]. [[reason|Reason]] does not eliminate the passions; it directs them toward beneficial objects.

## Religion and the Misgovernment of Passion

Religious systems, Holbach argues, mismanage the passions. By condemning natural desires as [[sin|sinful]] while simultaneously inflaming the "frenzied pride" of doctrinal certainty, religion produces precisely the destructive outcomes it claims to prevent:

> "What other passion than frenzied pride can render men so ferocious, so vindictive, so devoid of toleration and gentleness? What is more presumptuous than to arm nations and cause rivers of blood, in order to establish or to defend futile conjectures?" (ch. 170)

The passion for religious certainty — the conviction that one possesses exclusive truth — is, in Holbach's analysis, far more dangerous than any "[[sin|sinful]]" natural desire. It is not sensuality but [[doctrine|dogma]]tic pride that has produced the great atrocities of history.

## In the Text

On the weakness of [[fear|religious fear]] against natural passions:

> "The fear of God is powerless against human passions." (ch. 166)

On philosophy as the [[worship|cult]]ivation of noble passion:

> "His greatest passion will be to know the truth, and his greatest ambition to show it to others. Philosophy is beneficial in cultivating the heart and the mind." (ch. 170)

## Relation to Other Concepts

The concept of passion is central to Holbach's ethical [[materialism|naturalism]]. It connects his [[Materialism]] (passions are functions of our material [[temperament|constitution]]) with his critique of [[Religion]] (which mismanages the passions) and his positive vision of [[Morality]] (which should channel passions toward social benefit). The passion for truth links the affective life to [[Reason]] and [[Philosophy]]. The mismanagement of passion by religion produces [[Fanaticism]] and [[Superstition]].
