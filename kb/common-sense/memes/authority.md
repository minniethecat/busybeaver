# Authority

## Definition

Holbach's critique of religious authority extends far beyond particular abuses to the very concept of [[soul|spirit]]ual authority itself. Religious authority claims the right to tell people what to beli[[eve]], how to worship, and how to live — not on the basis of [[demon|demons]]trated [[philosophy|wisdom]] or superior knowledge, but on the basis of a claimed relationship to the [[divinity|divine]] that cannot be verified. "The [[clergy|priests]] treat men like children, who never cavil about the possibilities of the tales which are told them, provided they are amused" (ch. II).

For Holbach, authority in religion shares the structure of authority in politics before the Enlightenment: it is arbitrary, hereditary in effect if not in form, self-serving, and protected from criticism by threats of supernatural punishment. [[enlightenment|The Enlightenment]]'s political critique of [[sovereign|absolute monarch]]y — that legitimate authority requires consent, accountability, and [[demon|demons]]trated benefit — applies with equal force to the church. Religious authority fails all three tests.

## Historical Context

The [[catholic-church|Catholic Church]] of Holbach's time was one of [[europe|Europe]]'s largest landowners, operated its own courts, collected mandatory tithes, and exercised censorship over the printed word. The [[rome|Papal States]] were a [[monarch|sovereign]] territory in central Italy. Bishops sat in the French Estates-General and the English House of Lords. Religious authority was not merely [[soul|spirit]]ual but deeply embedded in political and economic structures.

[[reformation|The Reformation]] had challenged papal authority a quarter-millennium earlier, but it had not eliminated religious authority — it had multiplied it. Protestant state churches claimed the same compulsory power as the [[catholic-church|Catholic Church]], simply transferring ultimate authority from pope to [[monarch|prince]]. Holbach's critique is more radical: it attacks the very idea that any institution should be able to compel belief or enforce religious observance.

## Core Arguments

**1. Religious authority is self-appointed and self-validating.** Who gave [[clergy|priests]] the right to speak for [[god|God]]? The [[clergy|priests]] themselves. They claim [[divinity|divine]] commission, but the only [[proof|evidence]] for this commission is their own assertion, which they back with threats against those who question it. "The [[clergy|priests]] have made [[god|God]] speak; they have made him act; they have interpreted his pretended will. It is therefore the [[clergy|priests]] who should be made to feel that it is their own cause which they plead under the name of [[god|God]]" (ch. XII).

**2. Religious authority depends on keeping people ignorant.** The authority of [[clergy|priests]] requires that people not examine religion too closely. "The people are taught to beli[[eve]], not to examine. They are made to fear doubt as a crime, and to regard the blind submission of the mind as the first of [[morality|virtue]]s" (ch. LXI). A population trained in critical thin[[monarch|king]] would quickly recognize the illegitimacy of religious authority.

**3. Religious authority serves the material interests of its possessors.** Holbach insists on the economic dimension: [[clergy|priests]] "grow rich by your sweat and your [[suffering|pain]]s" (ch. XIII). The claim to [[soul|spirit]]ual authority is a means of extracting wealth, securing leisure, and obtaining immunity from ordinary legal accountability. Theologians are not disinterested seekers of truth but professionals whose livelihood depends on continued public [[belief|credulity]].

**4. Authority should be proportional to [[demon|demons]]trated competence.** In any other domain — medicine, navigation, engineering — we grant authority only to those who have [[demon|demons]]trated their knowledge through reliable results. [[religion|Religion]] demands authority without any comparable [[proof|demonstration]]. Its claimed successes ([[miracle|miracles]], answered [[prayer|prayers]]) are unverifiable; its failures (the persistence of [[injustice|evil]], the [[evil|suffering]] of the innocent) are explained away through ad hoc theological devices.

## Reception and Critique

Holbach's critique of religious authority made him enemies across the entire political spectrum. The church saw him as a threat to its temporal power; the [[monarch|monarchy]] saw his critique of unaccountable authority as potentially applicable to the throne; [[eve]]n some Enlightenment figures thought he went too far in attac[[monarch|king]] all religious authority rather than merely its abuses.

[[mode|Mode]]rn defenders of religious authority argue that Holbach's critique applies only to a caricature — that genuine religious authority is morally earned through [[model|exemplar]]y lives and voluntarily accepted by believers. Holbach would reply that the voluntariness of religious submission is largely an illusion: when children are indoctrinated from in[[imagination|fancy]], when doubt is punished and conformity rewarded, the distinction between voluntary and coerced belief collapses.

## Relation to Other Concepts

Authority is the structural principle of [[Religion]] — the mechanism by which belief is enforced and conformity maintained. It is exercised through [[Priestcraft]], justified by [[Theology]], and accepted through [[Faith]], which is the disposition to beli[[eve]] on authority rather than on [[proof|evidence]]. Authority is opposed to [[Reason]], which demands that claims be evaluated on their merits, and to [[Liberty]], which is the [[liberty|freedom]] to think and act without ecclesiastical constraint. The critique of authority is connected to [[Enlightenment]], the broader movement to replace arbitrary authority with rational justification, and to [[Doubt]], the intellectual [[morality|virtue]] that authority systematically suppresses.
