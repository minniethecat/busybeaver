# Will

## Definition

In Holbach's "Common Sense," the will is not a free, undetermined faculty of the soul but a [[mode|modification]] of the brain produced by motives acting upon a given temperament. Every act of will is the necessary result of the strongest present motive. "Man is not a free agent in any one moment of his life" (ch. 114). The will, like [[eve]]ry other mental operation, is a physical process — the brain responding to internal and external stimuli according to fixed laws.

Holbach's [[effect|determinism]] is absolute: the same individual, placed in exactly the same circumstances with exactly the same motives, would always will the same thing. The feeling of [[liberty|freedom]] is an illusion produced by our ignorance of the causes that determine us. "We do not perceive the chain of causes that act upon us" (ch. 114). The will is free only in the sense that a stone falling is "free" to fall — it acts according to its [[universe|nature]].

## Historical Context

The problem of [[liberty|free will]] was central to 18th-century philosophy. [[rene-descartes|Descartes]] had posited the will as an immaterial faculty capable of acting independently of physical causes. Theologians of all [[sect|denomination]]s insisted on [[liberty|free will]] as the necessary precondition of [[vice|sin]], merit, and [[divinity|divine]] judgment — if human actions are determined, then [[hell|eternal punishment]] for [[vice|sin]] is unjust. [[baruch-spinoza|Spinoza]] had argued for [[effect|determinism]] in the *Ethics* (1677), but his work was widely considered [[atheism|atheist]]ic and dangerous. [[john-locke|Locke]]'s *Essay Concerning Human [[mind|Understanding]]* (1689) had distinguished between "liberty" (the power to act according to the will) and "volition" (the determi[[nations|nation]] of the will itself), but reserved judgment on whether the will is free.

Holbach, writing in the 1770s, radicalized these debates by grounding [[effect|determinism]] in a materialist account of the brain. He drew on the nascent [[knowledge|science]] of neurophysiology — the recognition that mental states depend on physical states of the nervous system — to argue that [[eve]]ry volition is a physical [[eve]]nt with physical causes. This was a direct challenge to the [[doctrine|theological doctrine]] of an immaterial soul whose choices transcend natural [[cause|causation]].

## Core Arguments

**1. The will is determined by motives.** Every act of will has a cause, and that cause is always the strongest present motive acting upon a particular temperament. "To will is to be determined to action by a motive" (ch. 114). The experience of deliberation is simply the oscillation between competing motives before the strongest prevails.

**2. The feeling of [[liberty|freedom]] is ignorance of causes.** We feel free because we are conscious of our volitions but not of the chain of causes that produced them. "The [[liberty|freedom]] of man is a chimera" — the idea that we could have willed otherwise in identical circumstances is unintelligible, [[vice|sin]]ce identical causes produce identical effects.

**3. Free will is a [[fiction|theological fiction]].** The doctrine of [[liberty|free will]] was invented to justify [[punishment|divine punishment]]. If human actions are determined by [[god|God]] (through creation of the soul's [[universe|nature]]), then [[god|God]] is responsible for [[vice|sin]] — an unacceptable conclusion. Theologians therefore posited an undetermined will, ma[[monarch|king]] human [[existence|being]]s solely responsible for their [[vice|sin]]s. Holbach saw this as a transparent evasion: "[[theology|Theology]], to get its [[god|God]] out of the difficulty, has imagined [[liberty|free will]]."

**4. [[necessity|Determinism]] is compatible with moral responsibility.** Holbach did not conclude that [[effect|determinism]] eliminates accountability. [[society|Society]] punishes wrongdoers not because they "deserve" punishment in a metaphysical sense but because punishment modifies future motives and deters harmful behavior. "[[society|Society]] has the right to punish those who trouble it" — this right derives from the social interest in security, not from a [[merit|moral desert]] grounded in [[liberty|free will]].

## Reception and Critique

Holbach's [[effect|determinism]] was among the most uncompromising statements of the Enlightenment. Even sympathetic contemporaries like Diderot found it troubling — if all actions are determined, what becomes of the Enlightenment project of persuasion and reform? Holbach's answer was that persuasion *is* a form of [[cause|causation]]: reading a book, hearing an argument, or witnessing an example changes a person's motives and therefore changes their future volitions. Enlightenment is a causal force.

Kant's later attempt to reconcile [[liberty|freedom]] and [[effect|determinism]] — by positing [[liberty|freedom]] in the noumenal realm and [[effect|determinism]] in the phenomenal — can be read as a response to the Holbachian challenge. Kant admired the rigor of materialist [[effect|determinism]] but could not accept its moral implications.

## Relation to Other Concepts

The will's deterministic [[universe|nature]] connects it to several other Holbachian concepts. [[Reason]] is the faculty that evaluates motives and can become the strongest motive when [[worship|cult]]ivated. [[Education]] shapes the will by furnishing the mind with sound motives. [[Virtue]] is not a free choice to be good but the result of a character formed by reason and social influence. [[Sin]] presupposes [[liberty|free will]] and collapses without it. [[Punishment]] is justified by [[utility|social utility]], not retributive [[merit|desert]]. The will is the mechanism through which [[Society]] shapes individual behavior.

## In the Text

On the determi[[nations|nation]] of the will by motives:

> "Man is not a free agent in any one moment of his life. He is continually modified by causes, whether visible or concealed, that act upon him, and these causes are necessary." (ch. 114)

On the illusion of [[liberty|freedom]]:

> "We believe ourselves free because we are conscious of our actions, but we are ignorant of the causes that make us act. The freedom of man is a chimera." (ch. 114)

On theological motivations for the doctrine of [[liberty|free will]]:

> "Theology, to get its God out of the difficulty of being the author of sin, has imagined that man is free. But in granting man this freedom, it makes him the rival of God." (ch. 115)

On [[effect|determinism]] and [[society|social order]]:

> "If man is not free, society does not cease to have the right to punish him. The punishment that society inflicts is a motive that determines his will." (ch. 116)
