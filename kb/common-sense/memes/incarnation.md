# Incarnation

## Definition

The incar[[nations|nation]] is the [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian doctrine that the second person of the [[trinity|Trinity]] — [[god|God]] the Son — took on human flesh in the person of [[jesus-christ|Jesus Christ]]. In traditional [[religion|theology]] this is the central [[mystery]] of the [[religion|faith]]: [[infinite|the infinite]] becomes finite, the eternal enters time, [[god|the creator]] becomes a creature. Holbach treats the incar[[nations|nation]] not as a unique [[divinity|divine]] intervention but as one instance of a universal [[model|pattern]] in which religions claim that gods have walked among men.

Holbach's strategy is not to disprove the incar[[nations|nation]] directly but to expose its structural similarity to beliefs that [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ians themselves dismiss as [[superstition]]. "All the [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian [[sect|denomination]]s agree in considering as folly the incar[[nations|nation]] of the [[god|God]] of [[india|the Indies]], Vishnu. They contend that the only true incar[[nations|nation]] is that of Jesus, Son of the [[god|God]] of [[nature|the universe]] and of the wife of a carpenter" (ch. 117). The Hindu doctrine of avatar is dismissed as pagan myth; the [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian doctrine is identical in structure but protected by [[worship|cult]]ural familiarity.

## Historical Context

The doctrine of the incar[[nations|nation]] was formulated at the Council of Nicaea (325 CE) and refined at Chalcedon (451 CE), in response to controversies about whether [[jesus-christ|Christ]] was fully [[divinity|divine]], fully human, or some mixture of both. These debates, which occupied [[church|the Church]] for centuries, produced the orthodox formula: [[jesus-christ|Christ]] is one person with two [[universe|nature]]s, [[divinity|divine]] and human, "without confusion, without change, without division, without separation."

Enlightenment critics saw in these metaphysical disputes [[proof|evidence]] that [[religion|theology]] was not a form of [[knowledge]] but a form of imaginative [[fiction]]. [[voltaire|Voltaire]] mocked the incar[[nations|nation]] in his *Philosophical Dictionary*; [[pain|Pain]]e called it a "fable" in *The [[enlightenment|Age of Reason]]*. Holbach's contribution is to link the incar[[nations|nation]] to the broader problem of theological reasoning: once you accept an incomprehensible [[god]], you have forfeited the right to reject any subsequent [[absurdity|absurd]]ity.

## Core Arguments

**1. The incar[[nations|nation]] is structurally identical to pagan myths.** [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ians dismiss Vishnu's incar[[nation|nations]] as folly while accepting their own. "They contend that the only true incar[[nations|nation]] is that of Jesus, Son of the [[god|God]] of [[nature|the universe]] and of the wife of a carpenter" (ch. 117). The irony is that the [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian story is less grand — the [[god|God]] of [[nature|the universe]] takes flesh as the son of a provincial carpenter's wife.

**2. The first [[secrecy|mystery]] licenses all the rest.** Holbach quotes a theologian who concedes: "This first [[secrecy|mystery]], which is no small matter of itself, once admitted, our reason can not suffer violence in admitting all the rest" (ch. 117). Once you accept a creator [[god]] — an immaterial [[existence|being]] who makes matter, an unchanging [[existence|being]] who causes change — you have already abandoned the [[model|standard]]s by which the incar[[nations|nation]] could be rejected.

**3. A [[god|God]] who becomes man is a contradiction.** "An [[almighty|Almighty]] [[god|God]], who was willing to become a mortal man for the love of men, and to shed His blood to the last drop, to save them all, would yet have limited His power to only curing a few diseases" (ch. 206). If [[god|God]] truly became man to save humanity, why did He accomplish so little during His earthly life — healing a few individuals while leaving the universal corruption of [[soul]]s untouched?

**4. The incar[[nations|nation]] makes [[god|God]] incoherent.** A [[god]] who is infinite, perfect, and self-sufficient has no motive to become finite, [[evil|suffering]], and dependent. The act is either unnecessary (if [[god|God]] could save without it) or impossible (if [[god|God]] cannot change without ceasing to be [[god|God]]).

## Relation to Other Concepts

The incar[[nations|nation]] is a special case of the broader category of [[mystery]] — doctrines that [[religion|theology]] declares to be beyond human comprehension while simultaneously asserting their [[truth]] with absolute certainty. It is closely related to [[trinity]] ([[god|God]] as [[trinity|three persons]]), [[atonement]] (the purpose of the incar[[nations|nation]]), and [[miracle]] (the category of [[eve]]nt that the incar[[nations|nation]] represents). The incar[[nations|nation]] is the pivot on which the entire [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian [[theology]] turns: without it there is no [[jesus-christ|Christ]], no atonement, no [[salvation]].

## In the Text

On the structural equivalence of [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian and pagan incar[[nation|nations]]:

> "All the Christian denominations agree in considering as folly the incarnation of the God of the Indies, Vishnu. They contend that the only true incarnation is that of Jesus, Son of the God of the universe and of the wife of a carpenter." (ch. 117)

On the first [[secrecy|mystery]] licensing all others:

> "To us who believe through faith in a true God, an individual substance, there ought to be no trouble in believing everything else. This first mystery, which is no small matter of itself, once admitted, our reason can not suffer violence in admitting all the rest." (ch. 117, quoting a theologian)

On the incar[[nations|nation]]'s practical failure:

> "An Almighty God, who was willing to become a mortal man for the love of men, and to shed His blood to the last drop, to save them all, would yet have limited His power to only curing a few diseases and physical infirmities of a few individuals who were brought to Him; and would not have employed His Divine goodness in curing the infirmities of the soul!" (ch. 206)

## Reception and Critique

Holbach's treatment of the incar[[nations|nation]] forms part of a sustained Enlightenment campaign against the rational coherence of the central [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian [[mysteries]]. His argument that the incar[[nations|nation]] is structurally identical to pagan avatars was not original — Celsus had made the same point in the second century — but Holbach's framing of the issue as one of epistemic consistency gave it new force. If [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ians reject Vishnu's incar[[nations|nation]] on rational grounds, why do they exempt their own? The question, in Holbach's hands, is not just about comparative religion but about the psychology of belief: how [[worship|cult]]ural familiarity protects [[absurdity|absurd]]ities from the critical scrutiny that would instantly destroy them if they were encountered in an unfamiliar context. This line of argument has proven durable. It reappears in the New [[atheist|Atheist]] literature of the early twenty-first century and in the cognitive [[knowledge|science]] of religion, which investigates the mechanisms by which "minimally counterintuitive" concepts — a god who is also a man, a virgin who gives birth — achi[[eve]] [[worship|cult]]ural transmission.
