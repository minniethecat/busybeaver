# Jean Meslier

## Definition

Jean Meslier (1664–1729) was a French Catholic parish priest who, for forty years, performed his ecclesiastical duties while secretly composing a comprehensive [[atheism|atheist]] critique of [[religion]]. His manuscript, *Mémoire des pensées et des sentiments de Jean Meslier* (Memoir of the [[thought|Thought]]s and Sentiments of Jean Meslier), was discovered after his death and circulated clandestinely. Holbach published excerpts in 1762 under the title *Le Bon Sens du [[jean-meslier|Curé Meslier]]* — the text that would become *Common Sense* — ma[[monarch|king]] Meslier the hidden foundation of one of the Enlightenment's most radical works. The French [[nation|Nation]]al Convention later praised him as "the first priest who had the courage and the honesty to abjure [[error|religious error]]s."

## Historical Context

Meslier lived and wrote during the final decades of the *ancien régime*, when the [[catholic-church|Catholic Church]] retained immense institutional power but faced growing intellectual challenges from the early Enlightenment. His lifetime spanned the reign of Louis XIV — the apogee of French [[tyranny|absolutism]] and religious conformity — and the Regency period that followed, when heterodox ideas began to circulate more freely. Unlike the *philosophes* who would follow him, Meslier had no access to salons, publishers, or networks of patronage. He was a village priest in a remote parish, intellectually isolated, conducting his critique of religion in complete solitude. His manuscript circulated only after his death, first among a small circle of radicals and then, through Holbach's adaptation, to a [[europe|Europe]]an audience.

The posthumous publication of Meslier's work in the 1760s coincided with a period of intensified anticlerical publishing in [[france|France]]. Holbach and his circle produced a stream of anonymous or pseudonymous works attac[[monarch|king]] organized religion, and Meslier's testimony — the confession of a priest who had lived the system from within — provided uniquely powerful ammunition. Holbach's decision to strip Meslier's proto-communist politics from his adaptation reflects the strategic calculations of *philosophe* publishing: the critique of religion could be circulated relatively safely, but an attack on private property risked provo[[monarch|king]] repression that would shut down the entire enterprise.

## Life and Secret Dissent

Meslier spent his entire adult life in the village of Étrépigny in Champagne, where by all accounts he was a dutiful and charitable priest. Outwardly conforming to Catholic orthodoxy, he "worked his whole life secretly in order to attack the opinions which he believed to be false." His posthumous testament reveals a man who had concluded that all religions are human inventions used by [[clergy|priests]] and [[monarch|ruler]]s to exploit the [[belief|credulity]] of the masses. The tension between his public role and private convictions exemplifies for Holbach the psychological condition of the honest cleric trapped within a system he knows to be [[charlatan|fraud]]ulent.

Meslier's manuscript — over a thousand pages in its original form — is remarkable not only for its [[atheist|atheism]] but for its social radicalism. He advocated for the abolition of private property, the dissolution of the [[monarch|monarchy]], and the establishment of a communistic [[society|social order]], alongside his demolition of [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian [[religion|theology]]. Holbach, a wealthy baron, prudently stripped the political radicalism from his adaptation, retaining only the anticlerical and anti-[[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian arguments that aligned with his own philosophical program.

## Meslier as Exemplar of Courage

Holbach presents Meslier as the supreme [[model|exemplar]] of intellectual [[courage]]. Unlike the philosophes who attacked religion from positions of relative safety, Meslier risked [[eve]]rything — his livelihood, his social standing, and potentially his life — to record his convictions. The posthumous [[universe|nature]] of his protest does not diminish its moral significance; rather, it [[demon|demons]]trates that a [[guilt|sin]]gle honest witness can, [[eve]]n from beyond the grave, contribute more to human liberation than an army of conformists. Meslier's example serves Holbach's argument that [[priest]]s themselves, if they were permitted to speak freely, would confess that religion is a [[charlatan|fraud]].

## In the Text

Holbach invokes Meslier as the definitive [[evidence|proof]] that [[eve]]n [[clergy|priests]], when honest, reject the system they serve. In his preface, Holbach presents the work as the testament of a man who spent his life inside [[church|the Church]] and found it hollow:

> "A priest, by the name of Meslier, who worked his whole life secretly in order to attack the opinions which he believed to be false, has left us a work under the title of The Common Sense of the Curé Meslier. We have thought that we could not do better than to give to the public a summary of this work, in which a priest, on the brink of the grave, bares his soul and confesses the impostures of the profession he had practiced."

Holbach's strategic use of Meslier's biography — the honest priest who speaks truth only when death frees him from institutional constraint — is a rhetorical masterstroke. It neutralizes the predictable clerical response that [[atheism|atheist]]s are merely libertines who reject religion to escape moral restraint. Meslier was no libertine: he was a priest who had lived a life of service and who, on his deathbed, concluded that the [[religion|faith]] he had preached was a system of [[imposture|deception]].

## Reception and Critique

The figure of Meslier has fascinated historians of the Enlightenment precisely because he complicates the [[model|standard]] narrative. He was not a [[paris|Paris]]ian *philosophe* but a provincial priest; not a deist but an [[atheism|atheist]]; not a reformer but a revolutionary. Some scholars have questioned the extent of Holbach's debt to Meslier, noting that *Common Sense* departs substantially from Meslier's original manuscript in both argument and tone. Others have argued that Meslier's posthumous radicalism — the priest who concealed his true beliefs for forty years — raises uncomfortable questions about the relationship between outward conformity and inner conviction that Holbach's celebratory treatment elides. The most balanced assessment treats Meslier as a genuine precursor whose ideas were selectively appropriated: Holbach took the anticlerical substance while discarding the class politics, adapting a provincial priest's fury into the polished rhetoric of salon [[atheist|atheism]].
