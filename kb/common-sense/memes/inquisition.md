# Inquisition

## Overview

The Inquisition was a network of ecclesiastical tribunals established by the [[catholic-church|Catholic Church]] beginning in the 12th century, tasked with identifying, trying, and punishing [[heresy|heretic]]s — those who held beliefs contrary to [[church|Church]] doctrine. The most notorious expressions were the Medieval Inquisition (founded 1184), the Spanish Inquisition (established 1478), and the Roman Inquisition (established 1542). Methods included interrogation, torture, confiscation of property, and execution by burning at the stake. Holbach's "Common Sense" invokes the Inquisition as a paradigmatic example of how religious institutions, claiming [[divinity|divine]] authority, become instruments of [[persecution|persecution]] and [[cruelty|cruelty]].

## Historical Background

The Inquisition arose from [[church|the Church]]'s perception that heresy threatened both [[soul|spirit]]ual order and social stability. Pope Lucius III's bull *Ad abolendam* (1184) formalized the episcopal obligation to investigate heresy. Pope Gregory IX established the Papal Inquisition in the 1230s, entrusting it largely to the Dominican order. The Spanish Inquisition, authorized by Pope Sixtus IV at the request of Ferdinand and Isabella, operated with particular severity and independence from [[rome|Rome]], targeting *conversos* (Jewish converts to [[christianity|Christianity]]) and later Protestants. The Roman Inquisition, the most directly relevant to Holbach's experience, tried [[galileo|Galileo]] in 1633 and burned Giordano Bruno at the stake in 1600.

## Key Figures and Developments

Torquemada, the first Grand Inquisitor of S[[suffering|pain]] (appointed 1483), became synonymous with inquisitorial severity. The procedures of the Inquisition — secret denunciations, the withholding of charges from the accused, the use of [[torture|torture]] to extract confessions, and the *auto-da-fé* (public [[ritual|ceremony]] of [[confession|penance]] and execution) — were designed to inspire [[fear|terror]] as much as to enforce orthodoxy. The Congregation of the Holy Office in [[rome|Rome]] (the Roman Inquisition) remained active through Holbach's lifetime, condemning books and ideas that contradicted Catholic doctrine.

## Impact on Religion and Thought

The Inquisition's legacy is one of systematic religious coercion. It stifled intellectual inquiry wherever its authority extended, creating an atmosphere in which expressing [[doubt|doubt]] about [[church|Church]] doctrine could cost one's liberty or life. Enlightenment thinkers, writing in the Inquisition's long shadow, saw it as [[evidence|proof]] that religious institutions, when granted temporal power, inevitably become tyrannical. The Inquisition's Index of Prohibited Books, which included works by Enlightenment philosophers, was a direct obstacle to the circulation of ideas that Holbach and his circle sought to promote.

## Connection to "Common Sense"

Holbach invokes the Inquisition both explicitly and implicitly throughout "Common Sense." In his discussion of scriptural authority, he notes that in 1559, "according to the command of the inquisitors of the [[religion|faith]], twelve hundred of these Talmuds were burned in [[rome|Rome]], which were found in a library in the city of Cremona" (ch. II). This episode illustrates his broader argument: that religious authorities do not engage ideas with reason but suppress them with fire. The Inquisition embodies what Holbach considers religion's essential character — the claim that "[[god|God]]" authorizes men to persecute other men:

> "You hate, you persecute, you devote heretics to torture, because you are convinced that your religion alone is true" (002-324).
Holbach's critique of [[persecution|religious persecution]] runs throughout the work. He argues that [[fanaticism|religious fanaticism]] — the conviction of possessing exclusive [[divinity|divine]] truth — is the psychological engine driving inquisitorial cruelty. The Inquisition, in his analysis, is not an aberration but the logical consequence of a belief system that treats dissent as a crime against [[god|God]].

## Legacy

The Inquisition was progressively dismantled during the 18th and 19th centuries — the Spanish Inquisition was abolished in 1834 — but its historical memory remains a powerful symbol of [[intolerance|religious intolerance]]. Holbach's deployment of the Inquisition as an argument against organized religion contributed to the Enlightenment narrative that associated ecclesiastical power with [[cruelty|persecution]] and secular [[government|governance]] with toleration. The term "inquisition" has entered common usage to describe any excessively harsh or intrusive investigation.
