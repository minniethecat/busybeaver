# Pagan

## Definition

"Pagan" (Latin *paganus*, "country-dweller") originally designated the rural populations of the [[roman-empire|Roman Empire]] who continued to practice traditional polytheistic religion after the urban centers had converted to [[christianity|Christianity]]. By Holbach's era, the term had broadened to encompass all pre-[[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian and non-[[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian polytheistic religions of the ancient Mediterranean [[universe|world]].

Holbach deploys the category of "pagan" strategically, not to denigrate [[paganism|ancient religion]] but to expose the structural continuity between paganism and [[christianity|Christianity]]. The [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian who scorns pagan idolatry, Holbach observes, worships a [[god|God]] who is equally invisible, equally anthropomorphic, and equally the product of human imagi[[nations|nation]]. The only difference is that the pagan had many gods and the [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian has one — or, as Holbach would argue, the [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian has many gods under different names (the [[trinity|Trinity]], the [[saint|saints]], [[mary|the Virgin]] [[mary|Mary]] as semi-[[divinity|divine]] intercessor).

## Historical Context

The relationship between [[christianity|Christianity]] and paganism was a central concern of Enlightenment historiography. Edward Gibbon's *Decline and Fall of the [[roman-empire|Roman Empire]]* (1776–1788) treated the [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ianization of [[rome|Rome]] as a catastrophe that destroyed classical civilization. [[voltaire|Voltaire]] subjected both pagan and [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian mythology to the same moc[[monarch|king]] scrutiny.

Holbach's innovation was to argue that the genetic relationship between paganism and [[christianity|Christianity]] was not one of supersession but of inheritance. [[christianity|Christianity]] did not replace paganism — it absorbed and rebranded it. The festivals of the [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian year mapped onto pagan solstice and equinox celebrations. The [[worship|cult]] of [[saint|saints]] reproduced the local [[worship|cult]]s of pagan [[gods|deities]]. The [[religion|theology]] of the [[trinity|Trinity]] had antecedents in Neoplatonic ema[[nations|nation]] theories. For Holbach, the entire [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian system was "paganism baptized."

## Core Arguments

Holbach advances three claims about paganism and its relationship to [[christianity|Christianity]].

First, paganism and [[christianity|Christianity]] share a common psychological origin: fear of the unknown, personified and placated through ritual. The pagan who sacrificed to [[jupiter|Jupiter]] to ensure a good harvest and the [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian who prays to [[god|God]] for the same outcome are engaged in structurally identical behavior.

Second, [[christianity|Christianity]] is not morally superior to paganism. The pagan gods committed adultery, waged war, and demanded human sacrifice. The [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian [[god|God]] — as depicted in [[bible|scripture]] — drowns humanity in a flood, commands genocide in [[palestine|Canaan]], and condemns the majority of humanity to eternal [[torture|torment]]. Holbach sees no moral progress in the transition from [[jupiter|Jupiter]] to Jehovah.

Third, the [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian claim to have replaced paganism through rational persuasion is historically false. [[christianity|Christianity]] spread through imperial decree (Theodosius made it [[government|the state]] religion in 380 CE), through the destruction of pagan [[temple|temples]], and through the criminalization of [[idolatry|pagan worship]]. "The triumph of [[christianity|Christianity]]," Holbach w[[ritual|rites]] in effect, "was the triumph of [[persecution|intolerance]] over [[toleration|tolerance]], of [[cruelty|persecution]] over liberty."

## In the Text

Holbach treats paganism and [[christianity|Christianity]] as continuous in *Common Sense*:

> "What does the Christian religion offer us that the pagan religions did not offer? The same dogmas, the same mysteries, the same ceremonies — only the names have changed." (paraphrase)

> "If we examine without prejudice the religion of the pagans and that of the Christians, we shall find that they differ only in the names of the gods."
