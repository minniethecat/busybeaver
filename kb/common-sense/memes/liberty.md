# Liberty

## Definition

Holbach addresses liberty in two interconnected senses: the metaphysical question of free will, and the political question of freedom from oppressive authority. On the metaphysical question, he is a determinist: [[eve]]ry human action is the necessary result of motives, character, and circumstances. "Man is not free in any one instant of his life" — he is governed by necessary laws as much as the physical [[universe|world]]. Yet Holbach does not draw nihilistic conclusions from this [[effect|determinism]]. Rather, he argues that [[doctrine|theological doctrine]]s of "liberty" are self-contradictory: a [[god|God]] who predestines souls to [[vice|sin]] and then punishes them for it has given them "the liberty to offend Him" only so that He may have "the pleasure of getting angry, and [[existence|being]] appeased."

The political sense of liberty — freedom from priestly and monarchical [[despotism|tyranny]] — is for Holbach the more urgent concern. Liberty of thought, liberty of inquiry, and liberty to follow reason rather than authority are the necessary conditions for [[happiness|human flourishing]]. These liberties are systematically denied by religious institutions, which, he argues, depend on keeping minds "enchained."

## Historical Context

The problem of free will was central to [[enlightenment|Enlightenment philosophy]]. [[rene-descartes|Descartes]] had attempted to reconcile human freedom with [[divinity|divine]] foreknowledge; [[baruch-spinoza|Spinoza]] had denied free will altogether, arguing that [[eve]]rything follows necessarily from [[god|God]]'s [[universe|nature]]; [[gottfried-leibniz|Leibniz]] had proposed his [[injustice|theodicy]], in which [[god|God]] chose the best possible [[universe|world]] from among all possible [[universe|world]]s, leaving room for creaturely freedom within a providential plan.

Holbach's position is closest to [[baruch-spinoza|Spinoza]]'s necessitarianism, but with a polemical edge entirely his own. Where [[baruch-spinoza|Spinoza]] wrote in geometric order with scholarly detachment, Holbach w[[ritual|rites]] as an advocate for human liberation. His innovation is to connect the metaphysical denial of free will to a political critique of religious power: the doctrine of free will, he argues, is deployed by [[clergy|priests]] to justify [[hell|eternal punishment]] — if humans are free, they can be blamed; if they can be blamed, they can be threatened; if they can be threatened, they can be controlled.

## Core Arguments

**1. Metaphysical liberty is an illusion.** Every human choice is determined by prior causes — temperament, education, circumstance, and the force of motives. "Man is not free in any one instant of his life." To claim that humans have a faculty of free will independent of all determining causes is to claim an effect without a cause, which violates the principle of sufficient reason.

**2. Theological liberty is a trap.** The doctrine that [[god|God]] gave humans free will serves to make them responsible for their own [[hell|damnation]]. [[god|God]] "gives them the liberty to act, in order to give them occasion to deserve His favors and to acquire eternal happiness, which He does not owe them in any way." But this liberty is a "fatal boon" — it is given precisely so that it may be abused, and the abuse is punished eternally. A [[god|God]] who designed this system is not benevolent but cruel.

**3. Political liberty requires freedom from priestly influence.** As long as [[clergy|priests]] control education and public discourse, and as long as [[monarch|sovereign]]s govern by "[[divinity|divine]] right," genuine political liberty is impossible. [[religion|Religion]] "transforms [[[monarch|king]]s] into Divinities, whose caprices the [[nation|nations]] never dare to resist" while it "enchains the minds and the hands of their oppressed subjects."

**4. True liberty is knowledge of necessity.** Following [[baruch-spinoza|Spinoza]], Holbach suggests that the only meaningful liberty is [[knowledge|understanding]] the laws that govern us and acting in accordance with reason rather than superstition. "[[reason|Reason]] alone makes truly wise men" — liberty is not freedom from [[cause|causation]] but freedom from the [[despotism|tyranny]] of [[error|false belief]]s.

## Reception and Critique

Holbach's necessitarianism was controversial [[eve]]n among his Enlightenment allies. [[voltaire|Voltaire]], despite sharing Holbach's anticlericalism, defended free will on moral grounds: without it, he argued, praise and blame, reward and punishment lose their meaning. Kant would later attempt to resolve the tension by distinguishing between the phenomenal self (governed by natural laws) and the noumenal self (possessing transcendental freedom).

Contemporary compatibilists ([[hobbes|Hobbes]], [[david-hume|Hume]], and later Frankfurt, Dennett) have argued that free will is compatible with [[effect|determinism]] — that what matters for moral responsibility is not metaphysical indeterminacy but the capacity to act on one's own [[passion|desire]]s and reasons without external coercion. Holbach's position anticipates aspects of this compatibilist tradition while retaining a harder necessitarian edge.

## Relation to Other Concepts

Liberty is opposed to [[Superstition]] and [[Tyranny]], both of which restrict the free exercise of reason. It is closely related to [[Reason]], which is the faculty that liberates the mind from [[opinion|prejudice]]. The denial of metaphysical free will connects liberty to [[Nature]], whose necessary laws govern human conduct as they govern the physical [[universe|world]]. Liberty as political freedom is threatened by [[Authority]] when authority is grounded in religious claims rather than rational consent. The abuse of the doctrine of free will is a central mechanism by which [[Hell]] is made to appear just.

## In the Text

On the fatal character of theological liberty:

> "He gives them the liberty to act, in order to give them occasion to deserve His favors and to acquire eternal happiness, which He does not owe them in any way. In what way can we recognize the tenderness of a Father who created the majority of His children but for the purpose of dragging out a life of pain, anxiety, and bitterness upon this earth? Is there any more fatal boon than this pretended liberty which, it is said, men can abuse, and thereby expose themselves to the risk of eternal misery?" (091-002)

On liberty as a [[divinity|divine]] trap:

> "He gives them liberty to offend Him, makes them wicked enough to upset His projects, obstinate enough to never give up: all for the pleasure of getting angry, and being appeased, of reconciling Himself, and of repairing the confusion they have made." (071-002)

On reason as the path to genuine liberty:

> "It is only by experience, by observation, by reason, that we can attain to the knowledge of truth. All that is not founded on these is only opinion, prejudice, or error." (ch. XI)
