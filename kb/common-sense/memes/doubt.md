# Doubt

## Definition

In Holbach's "Common Sense," doubt occupies an intermediate position between superstition and knowledge. It is neither a [[morality|virtue]] in itself — permanent skepticism about evident truths is intellectually lazy — nor a [[vice|sin]], as theologians claim when they threaten doubters with [[punishment|divine punishment]].

Doubt is the natural consequence of an insufficient exami[[nations|nation]]: "To doubt is to deliberate upon the judgment which we should pass. [[skepticism|Skepticism]] is but a state of indecision which results from a superficial exami[[nations|nation]] of subjects" (123-004).

Applied rigorously to theological claims, doubt is not the final desti[[nations|nation]] but the passage through which the mind must travel to arrive at certainty — the certainty that a [[existence|being]] of contradictory [[attribute|attributes]] cannot exist.

Holbach distinguishes his position from both the theological demand for unquestioning belief and the absolute Pyrrhonism that doubts [[eve]]n sensory [[proof|evidence]]. [[pascal|Pascal]]'s wager — that it is safer to beli[[eve]] in [[god|God]] than not to — is inverted: the truly dangerous position is to accept, without exami[[nations|nation]], the existence of a [[existence|being]] whose supposed [[attribute|attributes]] (infinite goodness, infinite power, infinite justice) are logically incompatible with observable reality.

" Is it possible to doubt [[proof|evidence]]? Sensible people deride, and with reason, an absolute pyrrhonism, and [[eve]]n consider it impossible" (123-003). The non-existence of a contradictory [[existence|being]] is as certain as the non-existence of a square triangle.

## Historical Context

Philosophical doubt had a distinguished pedigree by Holbach's time. [[rene-descartes|Descartes]]' method of systematic doubt (1641) had made doubt the starting point of modern philosophy — though [[rene-descartes|Descartes]] used it to arrive at certainty about [[god|God]], not to dismantle it.

[[pierre-bayle|Bayle]]'s *Historical and Critical Dictionary* (1697) had wielded skeptical arguments against [[dogma|theological dogma]]tism while officially maintaining fi[[theism|deism]]. [[david-hume|Hume]]'s mitigated skepticism distinguished between Pyrrhonian doubt (which doubts [[eve]]rything, including sensory [[proof|evidence]], and is therefore unlivable) and reasonable doubt grounded in probabilistic reasoning.

Holbach's treatment of doubt belongs to the radical wing of this tradition. He rejects both the Cartesian reconciliation of doubt with [[divinity|theism]] and the [[pierre-bayle|Bayle]]an separation of [[religion|faith]] from reason.

For Holbach, doubt about [[god|God]] is not a permanent condition — it is a temporary state resolved by rigorous exami[[nations|nation]] of the theological concept itself. The [[god|God]] of the theologians is not merely unproven; He is logically impossible. To doubt His non-existence, after a full exami[[nations|nation]] of His contradictory [[attribute|attributes]], is as [[absurdity|absurd]] as doubting that two plus two equals four.

## Core Arguments

**1. Theologians use fear to suppress legitimate doubt.** "For the generality of men nothing renders an argument more convincing than fear" (030-001). Theologians threaten doubters with [[hell|eternal punishment]], but this threat "assumes always the thing in question" — it presumes [[god|God]]'s existence to frighten people into believing in [[god|God]]'s existence.

A just [[god|God]] would not punish invincible ignorance; a [[god|God]] who does is not just. "Is not the theologians' manner of reasoning very [[vice|sin]]gular? They create phantoms, they fill them with contradictions, and finally assure us that the safest way is not to doubt the existence of those phantoms, which they have themselves invented."

**2. Doubt is a transitional state, not a desti[[nations|nation]].** Holbach rejects Pyrrhonian skepticism as unlivable: "A man who could doubt his own existence, or that of the sun, would appear very ridiculous."

But he insists that doubt about [[god|God]] is resolved by exami[[nations|nation]], not by an act of [[religion|faith]]. To doubt the non-existence of a [[existence|being]] whose [[attribute|attributes]] destroy each other is "less [[absurdity|absurd]] or less impossible than that of a square triangle." Thorough exami[[nations|nation]] converts doubt into certainty — the certainty of impossibility.

**3. [[dogma|Dogma]]tism arises from suppressed doubt.** [[ignorance|Ignorance]] that refuses to doubt becomes "positive, [[doctrine|dogma]]tic, imperious" (ch. II). The refusal to entertain doubt about theological claims — the insistence on certainty without [[proof|evidence]] — is what produces the [[zeal|fanaticism]] that has "a thousand times, in all parts of our globe" led to slaughter, funeral piles, and crimes committed as matters of duty. Doubt is the prophylactic against [[zeal|fanaticism]].

**4. The skeptical temperament is often laziness, not rigor.** Holbach is critical of those who use skepticism as an excuse to avoid thin[[monarch|king]]: "Doubt arises ordinarily from laziness, weakness, indifference, or incapacity.

To doubt, for many people, is to [[fear|dread]] the trouble of examining things to which one attaches no interest" (ch. CXXXII). True philosophical doubt is active and rigorous; it examines until it reaches certainty. The skeptic who refuses to conclude is not more rigorous than the believer — merely less honest about his intellectual obligations.

## Reception and Critique

Holbach's insistence that doubt must resolve into certainty — specifically, certainty of [[god|God]]'s impossibility — was controversial [[eve]]n among Enlightenment thinkers.

[[voltaire|Voltaire]] remained a deist and considered a[[divinity|theism]] imprudent. Diderot, closer to Holbach's position, nevertheless recognized that the certainty Holbach claimed might exceed what the [[proof|evidence]] could strictly support: the non-existence of [[god|God]] cannot be [[demon|demons]]trated with the same rigor as a mathematical [[evidence|proof]], because the concept of [[god|God]] is vague enough to admit of reformulations that escape any particular refutation.

Kant's critique of rational [[religion|theology]] in the *Critique of Pure [[reason|Reason]]* (1781) took a different path: he argued that reason cannot prove either the existence or the non-existence of [[god|God]], because [[god|God]] is not an object of possible experience.

This was a more radical skepticism than Holbach's — one that denied certainty in either direction — and it shaped subsequent philosophy more decisively. Holbach's [[certainty|confidence]] that doubt about [[god|God]] is resolved by examining contradictions in the concept presupposes that the concept is stable enough to be refuted; if it shifts with each objection, doubt becomes interminable.

## Relation to Other Concepts

Doubt is the intellectual posture that makes Holbach's entire critique possible: without the willingness to doubt received theological opinion, no exami[[nations|nation]] can begin. It connects to [[Reason]] as the faculty that conducts the exami[[nations|nation]] doubt initiates.

[[Fear]] is the [[passion|emotion]] theologians exploit to short-circuit doubt. [[Evidence]] is what doubt demands and [[religion|theology]] cannot provide.

[[Certainty]] is the desti[[nations|nation]] to which doubt leads when exami[[nations|nation]] is thorough. [[Superstition]] is the condition of those who never permitted themselves to doubt.

## In the Text

On the theological use of fear to suppress doubt:

> "Theologians tell us that the safest side must be taken; that nothing is more criminal than incredulity; that God will punish without mercy all those who have the temerity to doubt His existence. ... They create phantoms, they fill them with contradictions, and finally assure us that the safest way is not to doubt the existence of those phantoms, which they have themselves invented." (ch. XXX)

On doubt as insufficient exami[[nations|nation]]:

> "To doubt is to deliberate upon the judgment which we should pass. Skepticism is but a state of indecision which results from a superficial examination of subjects." (ch. CXXXII)

On the certainty that resolves doubt:

> "Is it less extravagant to have uncertainties about the non-existence of an evidently impossible being? Is it more absurd to doubt of one's own existence, than to hesitate upon the impossibility of a being whose qualities destroy each other?" (ch. CXXXII)

On ignorance that refuses to doubt:

> "This ignorance makes them positive, dogmatic, imperious, and causes them to quarrel with all those who oppose doubts to the reveries which their brains have brought forth." (ch. II)
