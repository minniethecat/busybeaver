# Ceremony

## Definition

In Holbach's critique of religion, "[[ritual|ceremony]]" encompasses the entire apparatus of external religious practice — [[ritual|rites]], rituals, sacrifices, [[ritual|observances]], and liturgical formalities — which he regards as remnants of primitive superstition dignified by institutional inertia. Ceremonies, in his analysis, perform a twofold function: they distract the believer from the absence of rational content in religious doctrine, and they reinforce the authority of the priestly caste that alone can administer them correctly. "In all times the ferocity, the stupidity, the folly of savage men were shown in religious [[habit|custom]]s which were often cruel and extravagant" (121-001). Far from [[existence|being]] quaint survivals, these [[habit|custom]]s persist into the "most civilized [[nation|nations]]" and continue to demand the sacrifice of [[reason|human reason]] and [[happiness|well-being]].

Holbach's treatment of [[ritual|ceremony]] is not a call for liturgical reform but a wholesale rejection of external ritual as a legitimate mode of religious or moral life. His alternative — "let the human mind occupy itself with natural things, intelligible objects, sensible truths, and [[knowledge|useful knowledge]]" (Preface) — replaces [[ritual|ceremony]] with [[education|enlightenment]], priestly mediation with individual reason, and empty ritual with practical morality.

## Historical Context

The critique of religious ceremonialism had a long pedigree by Holbach's time. The [[reformation|Protestant Reformation]] had already attacked Catholic liturgy as superstitious pomp, and within Catholicism, Jansenist rigorists had condemned baroque devotional [[excess|excesses]]. [[enlightenment|The Enlightenment]] added a new dimension to this critique: not merely that certain ceremonies were excessive or corrupt, but that the very impulse to substitute ritual for reason was a defining feature of religion as such.

Holbach's specific targets include the [[mass|Catholic Mass]] (the "[[god|God]]-bread" that [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ians worship "as little rational … as that of the most barbarous [[nation|nations]]," (120-003)), the "ridiculous precepts" and "puerile practices" of [[revelation|revealed religion]]s (124-002), and the "sanguinary laws" by which religious authorities punish those who deviate from prescribed ceremonial norms (121-001). His analysis draws on the comparative anthropology of religion developing in the period — the growing awareness, fed by travel literature, that all [[worship|cult]]ures have their own "fetishes" and rituals, undermining any claim to unique [[revelation|revealed truth]].

## Core Arguments

1. **Ceremonies bear the seal of stupidity and [[cruelty|barbarity]].** "In all times the ferocity, the stupidity, the folly of savage men were shown in religious [[habit|custom]]s which were often cruel and extravagant" (121-001). These [[habit|custom]]s are not transcended but perpetuated by modern religions. "Do we not still see human victims offered to [[divinity|Divinity]]?" — the [[cruelty|persecution]] of [[heresy|heretic]]s is the contemporary form of ancient human sacrifice.

2. **Ceremonies are arbitrary and mutually contradictory.** Examining the revealed wishes of [[god|God]] across different religions, Holbach finds "but whimsical ordinances, ridiculous precepts, ceremonies of which we do not understand the aim, puerile practices" (124-002). The sheer diversity and mutual contradiction of [[ritual|religious ceremonies]] refute any claim that they originate from a universal [[revelation|divine revelation]].

3. **Ceremonies serve priestly interests, not human welfare.** The practices ordained by religion are "useful, in fact, to the ministers of [[god|God]], but very onerous to the rest of mankind" (124-002). [[sacrifice|Sacrifice]]s, offerings, expiations — all transfer wealth and power from the laity to [[clergy|the clergy]] while contributing nothing to genuine [[happiness|human flourishing]].

4. **Ceremonies render men unsocial and intolerant.** Religious [[ritual|observances]] "often have a tendency to render men unsocial, disdainful, intolerant, quarrelsome, unjust, inhuman toward all those who have not received either the same [[dreams|revelations]] as they, or the same ordinances, or the same favors from [[heaven|Heaven]]" (124-002). Ceremony marks the boundary between in-group and out-group, justifying [[cruelty|persecution]].

5. **Ceremony should be replaced by [[knowledge|useful knowledge]].** Holbach's conclusion is not reform but replacement: "Tired of an incomprehensible [[religion|theology]], of ridiculous fables, of impenetrable [[mysteries]], of puerile ceremonies, let the human mind occupy itself with natural things, intelligible objects, sensible truths, and [[knowledge|useful knowledge]]" (Preface).

## Reception and Critique

Holbach's blanket condem[[nations|nation]] of [[ritual|religious ritual]] provoked rebuttals from both religious and secular quarters. Catholic apologists argued that external [[ritual|ceremony]] serves legitimate psychological and social functions — embodying communal identity, mar[[monarch|king]] life transitions, and ma[[monarch|king]] abstract doctrine tangible. Rousseau, in the chapter on civil religion in *Du contrat social* (1762), had defended the necessity of civic ceremonies for social cohesion, [[eve]]n while sharing Holbach's [[skepticism|scepticism]] toward priestly religion.

Anthropological thought [[vice|sin]]ce Holbach has largely vindicated his descriptive claim that ceremonies are human universals with pre-[[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian origins, while complicating his normative judgment. Durkheim's *Les formes élémentaires de la vie religieuse* (1912) would argue that ritual is constitutive of social solidarity, not merely a priestly imposition. Holbach's contribution remains valuable, however, as a sharp normative challenge: when ceremonies serve to mark and enforce religious difference, they become — as he argued — engines of [[persecution|intolerance]] rather than [[society|community]].

## Relation to Other Concepts

Ceremony is closely linked to [[Superstition]], of which it is the most visible external expression. It relates to [[Priest]] and [[Priestcraft]], [[vice|sin]]ce the administration of ceremonies is the priest's primary claim to social authority. The critique of [[ritual|ceremony]] connects to [[Worship]], which Holbach regards as an irrational practice — "it would be more rational to worship the sun" (024-001) than an invisible, incomprehensible [[god|God]]. Ceremony also relates to [[Morality]], [[vice|sin]]ce Holbach's fundamental objection is that external ritual substitutes for genuine ethical conduct. Finally, the concept connects to [[Reason]], whose exercise is inhibited by the demand to "submit without exami[[nations|nation]]" to prescribed ritual forms (135-001).

## In the Text

> "In all times the ferocity, the stupidity, the folly of savage men were shown in religious customs which were often cruel and extravagant. A spirit of barbarity has come down to our days; it intrudes itself into the religions which are followed by the most civilized nations." (121-001)

> "Examining the Divine wishes, I find in them, in all countries, but whimsical ordinances, ridiculous precepts, ceremonies of which we do not understand the aim, puerile practices, principles of conduct unworthy of the Monarch of Nature, offerings, sacrifices, expiations, useful, in fact, to the ministers of God, but very onerous to the rest of mankind." (124-002)

> "Tired of an incomprehensible theology, of ridiculous fables, of impenetrable mysteries, of puerile ceremonies, let the human mind occupy itself with natural things, intelligible objects, sensible truths, and useful knowledge." (Preface)
