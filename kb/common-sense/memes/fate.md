## Definition

Holbach opens "Common Sense" (1772) with an observation about the universal human condition: "we see them all continually exposed to sorrows from which it appears they can not escape, and the least satisfied with their fate." (001-001) This opening sets up fate as a central concept in his materialist critique of religion — not as a supernatural destiny ordained by Providence, but as the necessary and indifferent operation of natural laws upon all living beings.

For Holbach, fate is indistinguishable from [[nature|natural]] necessity. The same causal order that produces favorable harvests also produces earthquakes and famines; there is no divine hand selectively doling out blessings and punishments. "The same necessity regulates the fate of all sentient beings — which are born to suffer much, to enjoy little, and to die." (002-079) This is the core of Holbach's anti-Providential argument: the universal distribution of suffering and death proves that fate is blind necessity, not intelligent design.

## Core Arguments

Holbach deploys the concept of fate to advance several arguments against religious worldviews:

**1. Fate is universal and indifferent.** All sentient beings, human and animal alike, are subject to the same necessary laws. The universality of suffering — "everywhere good is side by side with evil; order is replaced by disorder; generation is followed by destruction" (002-079) — refutes the claim that a benevolent God governs the world. The laws of nature have "no mercy to the caprices of fate." (002-076)

**2. Human dissatisfaction with fate drives religious belief.** Holbach opens the book by noting that humans are perennially dissatisfied with their lot. (001-001) This dissatisfaction, rather than pointing toward a divine remedy, is the psychological engine of [[superstition]]: people invent gods precisely because they cannot accept that fate is indifferent to their wishes.

**3. Even human thought is fated.** Holbach extends determinism to the psychological realm: "the opinions and ideas of men are fated." (002-139) If even our beliefs are the necessary products of prior causes, the religious claim that faith is a free choice collapses. Humans believe what their circumstances and education determine them to believe.

**4. Fate in the political realm.** "Power decides the fate of nations." (002-146) Holbach observes that political outcomes are determined by force and material conditions, not by divine providence or the moral worth of rulers. The fate of peoples is in the hands of those who govern — a secular, political analysis that eliminates supernatural causation from history.

**5. The comparative argument from animal suffering.** In a characteristically Enlightenment move, Holbach notes that "the fate of beasts appears to me more supportable" than that of humans tormented by religious fears. (002-197) Animals suffer and die as humans do, but without the added burden of theological terror about an afterlife — a pointed argument against the supposed benevolence of the cosmic order.

## In the Text

Holbach's most concentrated treatment of fate appears in Chapter 2, where he systematically develops the argument from universal necessity against Providence:

> Everything which takes place in the world proves to us in the clearest way that it is not governed by an intelligent being. We can judge of the intelligence of a being but by the means which he employs to accomplish his proposed design. The aim of God, it is said, is the happiness of our race; however, the same necessity regulates the fate of all sentient beings — which are born to suffer much, to enjoy little, and to die.
>
> (002-079)
