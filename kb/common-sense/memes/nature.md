# Nature

## Definition

Nature, in Holbach's system, is the totality of material existence — all that is, governed by invariant laws discoverable through observation and reason. It is the positive alternative to theological metaphysics: where religion posits a transcendent creator who stands outside and above [[earth|the world]], Holbachian [[universe|nature]] is self-contained and self-organizing. "The voice of [[universe|nature]] is intelligible," he w[[ritual|rites]]; "that of falsehood is ambiguous, enigmatical, and mysterious" (ch. II). Nature speaks plainly through the [[proof|evidence]] of the senses; religion speaks murkily through the inventions of the imagi[[nations|nation]].

## Historical Context

The concept of [[universe|nature]] underwent a dramatic transformation during the Scientific [[revolution|Revolution]] and Enlightenment. [[rene-descartes|Descartes]] had divided reality into two substances — thin[[monarch|king]] mind and extended matter — leaving [[universe|nature]] a mechanical system devoid of purpose. [[baruch-spinoza|Spinoza]] went further, identifying [[god|God]] with Nature (*Deus sive Natura*), a pan[[divinity|theism]] that Holbach's [[matter|materialism]] radicalized by eliminating the [[divinity|divine]] element entirely. [[isaac-newton|Newton]] had revealed a universe of mathematical precision, apparently requiring a [[divinity|divine]] clockmaker. Holbach drew the opposite conclusion: the very regularity of natural laws made a supernatural legislator redundant. Nature, operating through [[effect|cause and effect]], was expla[[nations|nation]] enough.

## Core Arguments

**1. Nature is self-sufficient.** Holbach rejects the [[design|argument from design]]: the universe requires no external creator because its laws are intrinsic to matter itself. To posit a [[god|God]] as [[cause|first cause]] merely postpones the question — what caused [[god|God]]? Nature, [[existence|being]] eternal and self-existent, avoids [[infinite|the infinite]] regress.

**2. The concept of "[[divinity|divine nature]]" is incoherent.** Theologians claim [[god|God]]'s [[universe|nature]] is incomprehensible, yet simultaneously assert a detailed knowledge of his [[attribute|attributes]], commands, and intentions. "Everybody tells us that the [[essence|essence of God]] is incomprehensible to man; at the same time they do not hesitate to assign [[attribute|attributes]] to this incomprehensible [[god|God]]" (ch. V). The appeal to [[secrecy|mystery]] is a rhetorical escape hatch, not an expla[[nations|nation]].

**3. Nature provides the model for morality, not revelation.** "They have only to look within themselves, to reflect upon their own [[universe|nature]], to consult their obvious interests, to consider the object of society" (ch. II). Human [[universe|nature]] — our shared needs, [[passion|desire]]s, and capacity for [[evil|suffering]] — provides a surer foundation for [[morality|ethics]] than any revealed text.

**4. [[religion|Religion]] obscures [[universe|nature]].** By inserting invisible agencies between observation and expla[[nations|nation]], [[religion|theology]] makes the natural world seem mysterious when it is not: "all [[universe|nature]] has become an inexplicable enigma for man; the visible [[universe|world]] has disappeared to give place to invisible regions" (ch. II).

## Relation to Other Concepts

Nature is the metaphysical foundation of Holbach's [[Materialism]] — the claim that matter in motion, governed by natural law, is all that exists. It stands in opposition to the [[Superstition]] that populates [[earth|the world]] with invisible agencies, and to the concept of [[God]] as a supernatural creator. It is allied with [[Reason]], which is the human faculty for apprehending [[universe|nature]]'s laws, and with [[Science]], which is the systematic study of those laws. The transition from a theological to a naturalistic [[universe|world]]view is, for Holbach, the central drama of human intellectual history.

## In the Text

On the intelligibility of [[universe|nature]] versus the obscurity of [[religion|theology]]:

> "The voice of nature is intelligible, that of falsehood is ambiguous, enigmatical, and mysterious; the road of truth is straight, that of imposture is oblique and dark; this truth, always necessary to man, is felt by all just minds." (ch. II)

On the self-sufficiency of the natural order:

> "If some being bestowed upon men power, activity, benevolence, strength, it was no doubt the sun, which should be recognized as the father of nature, as the soul of the world, as Divinity. At least one could understand the benefits it bestows." (ch. V)

On [[religion|theology]]'s distortion of natural [[knowledge|understanding]]:

> "By the aid of this systematic nonsense, all nature has become an inexplicable enigma for man; the visible world has disappeared to give place to invisible regions; reason is obliged to give place to imagination, which can lead us only to the land of chimeras." (ch. II)
