# Jean Meslier

## Biography

[[meslier|Jean Meslier]] (also John Meslier) was born in 1664 in the province of Champagne, [[paris|France]]. He served as the Catholic curé (parish priest) of Étrépigny in Champagne for approximately thirty years, from around 1689 until his death in 1733 at the age of fifty-five. According to [[voltaire|Voltaire]]'s biographical preface, Meslier "died in the odor of [[sacred|sanctity]]" — that is, with the outward appearance of a devout priest — "in the year 1733, fifty-five years old" (APP007-020). It is believed that, "disgusted with life, he expressly refused necessary food" during his final illness (APP007-020).

During his lifetime, Meslier performed his priestly duties while privately harboring profound contempt for the doctrines he was compelled to teach. On one occasion, reproached by the Archbishop for supporting oppressed parishioners, Meslier was summoned and "ill-treated with abusive language" — an incident that deepened his resentment toward [[authority|ecclesiastical authority]]. At his death, he gave all his possessions to his parishioners and asked to be buried in his garden.

## Connection to "Common Sense"

After Meslier's death, three manuscript copies of his "[[testament|Testament]]" were discovered (APP007-022) — each "three hundred and sixty-six pages, all written by his hand" (APP007-022). The work was a comprehensive refutation of [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian doctrine. [[voltaire|Voltaire]] reports that copies soon circulated in [[paris|Paris]], "sold at ten Louis-d'or apiece." A dying priest accusing himself "of having professed and taught the [[christianity|Christian religion]], made a deeper impression upon the mind than the '[[thought|Thought]]s of [[pascal|Pascal]]'" (APP007-022).

The text published as "Common Sense" (or "[[superstition|Superstition]] in All Ages") in 1772 was substantially edited and rewritten by Baron d'Holbach, who adapted Meslier's raw manuscript into a systematic philosophical treatise. While Meslier provided the authentic voice of a priest who had renounced his [[religion|faith]], Holbach gave the arguments their Enlightenment polish and philosophical structure. Meslier's original manuscript contained the remarkable declaration:

> "I have seen and recognized the errors, the abuses, the follies, and the wickedness of men. I have hated and despised them. I did not dare say it during my life, but I will say it at least in dying, and after my death."
>
> (APP007-022)

## Key Ideas Attributed / Critiqued

Meslier's testament attacked "[[god|God]], against all religion, against the [[scripture|Bible]] and [[church|the Church]]" using only "the [[scripture|Bible]] itself, Moreri, [[montaigne|Montaigne]], and a few fathers" as sources (APP007-022). His specific targets included:

- The doctrine of [[miracle|miracles]], which he treated as "[[absurdity|absurd]] fables, injurious to divinity"
- The exploitation of the poor by [[clergy|the clergy]]: "How horrible this monopoly! I detest their insatiable cupidity"
- The [[mass|Mass]] and [[sacrament|sacraments]] as "ridiculous administrations"
- The [[christianity|Christian religion]] as responsible for the crimes he witnessed

Meslier was distinctive among critics of religion for the intensity of his condem[[nations|nation]]. As [[voltaire|Voltaire]] observes: "[[woolston|Woolston]] is sometimes indulgent, Meslier never. He was a man profoundly embittered by the crimes he witnessed, for which he holds the [[christianity|Christian religion]] responsible" (APP007-020).

## Legacy

In 1793, during [[revolution|the French Revolution]], the [[nation|Nation]]al Convention decreed the erection of a statue to [[meslier|Jean Meslier]], honoring him as "the first priest who had the courage and the honesty to abjure [[error|religious error]]s." His testament influenced later freethought and socialist traditions. The Holbach-edited version ("Common Sense") circulated widely among 19th-century secularists and was translated into multiple languages, including the English edition by Anna Knoop (1878).
