# Chosen People

## Definition

The concept of the "chosen people" — the claim that the [[god|God]] of [[nature|the universe]] selected one particular [[nations|nation]], [[israel|Israel]], as the exclusive recipient of His revelation, laws, and protection — is subjected by Baron d'Holbach to a sustained rational critique.

Holbach argues that [[divinity|divine]] [[predestination|election]] is historically falsified (the chosen people have been conquered, scattered, and subjugated), morally repugnant (a universal [[god|God]] would not play favo[[ritual|rites]]), and textually unstable (the "true" chosen people are reinterpreted allegorically by [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ians to mean themselves, exposing the arbitrariness of the category).

The doctrine of [[predestination|election]] is, for Holbach, not a [[revelation|revealed truth]] but a political fiction — a claim manufactured by priestly elites to consolidate [[nations|nation]]al identity and legitimize territorial claims.

## Historical Context

The idea of [[israel|Israel]] as [[god|God]]'s chosen people is central to the Hebrew [[bible|Bible]], articulated most explicitly in the covenant narratives of Genesis ([[abraham|Abraham]]), Exodus ([[moses|Moses]] at [[sin|Sin]]ai), and Deuteronomy.

The [[predestination|election]] is conditional upon obedience — blessings for fidelity, curses for apostasy — and the historical books of the Hebrew [[bible|Bible]] interpret [[israel|Israel]]'s political [[chance|fortune]]s through this covenantal lens.

[[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian [[religion|theology]] transformed the concept by "[[soul|spirit]]ualizing" it: the "true [[israel|Israel]]" was no longer the [[israel|Jewish people]] but the [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian [[church|Church]], the "[[israel|Israel]] of [[god|God]]" (Galatians 6:16).

This reinterpretation — which Holbach treats as transparently self-serving — allowed [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ians to claim the promises made to [[israel|Israel]] while consigning the actual [[israel|Jewish people]] to the status of rejected apostates.

Holbach seizes on this contradiction: if the promises to [[israel|Israel]] were [[divinity|divine]]ly guaranteed, their non-fulfillment proves the promises false; if they were allegorical, then the original recipients were deceived and [[bible|scripture]] is duplicitous. Either way, the doctrine collapses under rational scrutiny.

## Core Arguments

Holbach's critique of the chosen people concept proceeds through three claims.

First, the historical [[proof|evidence]] contradicts the promise. [[god|God]] promised [[abraham|Abraham]], [[isaac|Isaac]], and [[jacob|Jacob]] that their descendants would possess the land, flourish, and be a light to the [[nation|nations]]. Instead, they were enslaved in [[egypt|Egypt]], conquered by Assyria and [[babylon|Babylon]], scattered in the Diaspora, and subjected to repeated [[cruelty|persecution]]. The historical condition of the [[israel|Jewish people]] is, for Holbach, an empirical refutation of [[divinity|divine]] [[predestination|election]].

Second, the doctrine of [[predestination|election]] is morally incompatible with the idea of a universal, just [[god|God]]. An impartial [[god|deity]] would not bestow exclusive revelation on "a handful of unfortunate [[existence|being]]s" while leaving "the most flourishing [[nation|nations]]" in ignorance. The particularism of Jewish [[predestination|election]] — and its [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian extension in the doctrine of exclusive salvation through [[jesus-christ|Christ]] — reveals the tribal, human origin of the concept.

Third, the [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian allegorical reinterpretation of [[predestination|election]] exposes the doctrine's intellectual bankruptcy. When [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ians claim that the promises to [[israel|Israel]] were never meant literally — that "the [[israel|Israelites]] after the body" were a mere type of "the [[israel|Israelites]] in [[soul|spirit]]" ([[church|the Church]]) — they concede that the original promises were deceptive.

" It is ridiculous to forge such allegorical meanings," Holbach w[[ritual|rites]]. Either the promises meant what they said (and were false) or they meant something else entirely (and the text misled its original audience).

## Reception and Critique

Holbach's critique of the chosen people concept occupies a complex position in the history of biblical criticism. His argument that the historical misfortunes of the [[israel|Jewish people]] refute the [[predestination|election]] promise was not original — [[baruch-spinoza|Spinoza]] had made a similar case in the *Tractatus Theologico-Politicus* (1670) — but Holbach's polemical sharpening of the argument gave it wider circulation among non-specialist readers.

Jewish responses to this line of argument have varied. [[custom|Tradition]]al interpreters within Judaism have always understood [[predestination|election]] as imposing obligations rather than conferring privileges — the chosen people are chosen for service, not for favoritism — a distinction Holbach's critique largely elides.

[[mode|Mode]]rn Jewish theologians, from [[abraham|Abraham]] Joshua Heschel to Michael Wyschogrod, have reframed [[predestination|election]] as a category of covenantal relationship rather than metaphysical exclusivity, a move that partially addresses Holbach's moral objection while preserving the concept's theological substance.

Within [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian [[religion|theology]], the supersessionist interpretation that Holbach attacks — the claim that [[church|the Church]] has replaced [[israel|Israel]] as the true chosen people — has itself been increasingly repudiated [[guilt|sin]]ce the Holocaust. The Second [[rome|Vatican]] Council's *Nostra Aetate* (1965) explicitly rejected the [[doctrine|teaching]] that Jews are cursed or rejected by [[god|God]], mar[[monarch|king]] a definitive break with the supersessionism that Holbach had identified as contradictory.

In contemporary political philosophy, the chosen people concept has been analyzed as a paradigmatic instance of what Michael Walzer calls "the paradox of [[nations|nation]]al liberation": particularist narratives of [[divinity|divine]] [[predestination|election]], however theologically dubious, have historically functioned as resources for collective identity and resistance against imperial domi[[nations|nation]].

Holbach's universalist critique, in this reading, fails to account for the political [[utility]] of particularist self-[[knowledge|understanding]]s.

## Relation to Other Concepts

The chosen people concept is inseparable from [[covenant]], the biblical agreement whose promises constitute the content of [[predestination|election]].

Holbach's argument that the covenant's non-fulfillment refutes [[predestination|election]] depends on reading covenant promises as [[prophecy|prediction]]s rather than as conditional pledges — a reading that traditional Jewish and [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian interpreters would contest.

The concept is closely related to [[providence]], the doctrine of [[divinity|divine]] [[government|governance]] of history. If providence directs human affairs toward a [[divinity|divine]]ly ordained end, the historical [[evil|suffering]] of the chosen people requires theological expla[[nations|nation]] (the [[prophecy|prophet]]s' answer: punishment for disobedience).

Holbach treats both providence and [[predestination|election]] as ad hoc expla[[nation|nations]] designed to reconcile theological commitment with recalcitrant historical [[proof|evidence]].

Election also connects to [[universalism]], the claim — central to Enlightenment thought — that moral and rational truths are accessible to all human [[existence|being]]s regardless of their particular [[worship|cult]]ural or religious location.

Holbach's critique of [[predestination|election]] is, at bottom, an assertion of universalism against particularism: if truth is universal, the idea that [[god|God]] revealed it exclusively to one small [[nations|nation]] is self-refuting.

Finally, the chosen people concept inter[[sect|sects]] with the political category of [[nationalism]]. Holbach anticipates the later secular critique that claims of [[divinity|divine]] [[predestination|election]] are routinely mobilized to legitimize territorial expansion, ethnic supremacy, and the subjugation of neighboring [[nation|peoples]] — a critique that would be taken up with renewed urgency in the nineteenth and twentieth centuries.

## In the Text

Holbach addresses the chosen people concept in chapter CLXXII of *Common Sense*, where he dismantles the allegorical reinterpretation that [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ians use to salvage the promises: "Our [[jesus-christ|Christ]]-worshipers... reject the literal and natural sense of the words, to give them a mystical and [[soul|spirit]]ual sense which they call allegorical and figurative; claiming, for example, that the [[israel|people of Israel]] and [[judea|Judea]], to whom these promises were made, were not understood as the [[israel|Israelites]] after the body, but the [[israel|Israelites]] in [[soul|spirit]]: that is to say, the [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ians which are the [[israel|Israel]] of [[god|God]], the true chosen people."

He immediately pronounces judgment on this maneuver: "It is easy to see that these [[soul|spirit]]ual and allegorical meanings having only a strange, imaginary sense, [[existence|being]] a subterfuge of the interpreters, can not serve to show the truth or the falsehood of a proposition, or of any promises whatever."
