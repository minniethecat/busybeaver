# Enthusiasm

## Definition

In Holbach's *Common Sense*, "enthusiasm" carries the eighteenth-century meaning of excessive, irrational religious zeal — a state of mind in which emotion overwhelms reason and the believer becomes incapable of evaluating claims critically. Holbach writes that "it can be but blind enthusiasm or folly that can prefer a system of contradictions and absurdities to the most simple and natural ideas" (002-197). Enthusiasm is not merely strong feeling; it is a cognitive state in which the normal operation of reason is suspended, making the believer vulnerable to any doctrine, however absurd, that appeals to their passions.

## Enthusiasm and the Eclipse of Reason

Holbach connects enthusiasm to the systematic distortion of perception. "I see a multitude of evils of which you deny the existence, and which your enthusiasm alone sees as benefits" (002-221). The enthusiast does not merely interpret the world differently — they see a different world, one in which evils become goods and absurdities become profundities. This transformation is not a miracle but a psychological mechanism: "experience teaches me that enthusiasm and interest can blind and mislead men" (002-267). Enthusiasm serves interest — it is the psychological state that enables priests to maintain their authority and believers to maintain their comforting illusions.

## The Inevitable Decline

Holbach observes that religious enthusiasm is inherently unstable. "But very soon this enthusiasm, this supernatural taste for indigence, gives place to the natural desires of the heart" (002-360). The fervor of conversion or revival cannot be sustained against the persistent claims of natural desire — for comfort, for pleasure, for the goods of this world. The enthusiasm that promised to transcend human nature inevitably collapses back into it. This cycle — enthusiasm, exhaustion, and the return of natural desire — is, for Holbach, evidence that religious enthusiasm is a temporary aberration of human psychology, not a glimpse of a higher reality.

## Enthusiasm and the Interests of Priests

Holbach identifies religious enthusiasm — what he calls "ardent zeal" — as one of the core virtues that religion cultivates for instrumental purposes. In his analysis of religious morality, he observes that the virtues prescribed by all religions consist in "a submissive faith, which prevents reasoning; in a devout humility, which assures to the priests the submission of their slaves; in an ardent zeal, when the question of religion is agitated; that is to say, when the interest of these priests is considered" (002-354). Enthusiasm is thus not a spontaneous eruption of piety but a cultivated emotional state that serves institutional ends. When believers are enthusiastic, they do not ask questions; when they are zealous, they defend the interests of the priesthood as though they were their own.
