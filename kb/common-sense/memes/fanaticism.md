# Fanaticism

## Definition

Fanaticism, for Holbach, is the pathological endpoint of [[belief|religious belief]] — a state in which devotion to a supposed [[divinity|divine]] cause becomes "stronger than the love of life" itself. (133-002) It is not merely strong belief but a transformative psychological condition that suspends normal human sentiments of compassion, reason, and self-preservation. The fanatic is one who has been so thoroughly shaped by religious ideology that he willingly sacrifices himself and mercilessly persecutes others, all in the conviction of serving a higher purpose. Holbach's analysis prefigures later psychological and sociological treatments of ideological extremism by grounding [[zeal|fanaticism]] not in demonic possession or moral failing but in the ordinary mechanisms of belief, interest, and social conditioning.

## Historical Context

The 18th century was scarred by the living memory of the Wars of [[religion|Religion]], the [[cruelty|persecution]] of [[heresy|heretic]]s, and the ongoing suppression of dissent by both Catholic and Protestant establishments. The *auto-da-fé*, the [[edict-of-nantes|Revocation of the Edict of Nantes]], and the trial of Jean Calas (which [[voltaire|Voltaire]] famously protested) were recent [[eve]]nts that [[demon|demons]]trated the lethal potential of [[zeal|religious zeal]]. Holbach wrote *Common Sense* in this atmosphere, and his treatment of [[zeal|fanaticism]] is not abstract philosophy but urgent political intervention. By demystifying the psychology of the fanatic and the [[missionaries|missionary]], he sought to disarm the ideological mechanisms that made [[cruelty|persecution]] possible. His chapter on the "[[zeal|fanaticism]] of the [[martyr|martyrs]]" directly targets the hagiographic tradition that celebrated death for [[religion|faith]] as noble and truth-confirming.

## Core Arguments

Holbach develops a multi-layered critique of [[zeal|fanaticism]]:

1. **[[martyr|Martyr]]dom Proves [[nothingness|Nothing]]**: "To die for a religion does not prove it true or [[theology-index|Divine]]; this proves at most that we suppose it to be so. An enthusiast in dying proves nothing but that religious [[zeal|fanaticism]] is often stronger than the love of life" (ch. 133). The courage of [[martyr|martyrs]] is a psychological fact, not an epistemological one.

2. **Missionary [[zeal|Zeal]] Is Self-[[interest|Interest]] in Disguise**: The apparent [[interest|disinterestedness]] of missionaries is an illusion. "A [[missionaries|missionary]] seeks [[chance|fortune]] by the aid of his doctrine; he knows that if he has the good [[chance|fortune]] to retail his commodity, he will become the absolute master of those who accept him as their guide" (ch. 133).

3. **Fanaticism Renders [[person-index|People]] Inhuman**: Fanatics become "inhuman toward all those who are not of their own way of thin[[monarch|king]]" (ch. 148). Religious zeal systematically corrodes the natural bonds of human sympathy.

4. **[[priest|Priest]]s Manufacture Fanatics**: Fanaticism is not spontaneous but [[worship|cult]]ivated by a priestly class that benefits from it. "One fanatical or deceitful priest who has the secret of inspiring the multitude, has often more influence than the [[monarch|sovereign]] himself" (ch. 148).

5. **Fanaticism Is Not [[virtue|Virtue]]**: The self-denial and austerity of the religious fanatic — the "penitents, hermits, fakirs, fanatics, who seem to have studied profoundly the art of [[torture|torment]]ing themselves" — are not morally admirable but psychologically perverse (ch. 160).

## Reception and Critique

Holbach's analysis of [[zeal|fanaticism]] was among his most influential contributions, resonating with the secularizing currents of [[revolution|the French Revolution]] and later with 19th-century anticlericalism. Critics charged that he reduced all religious conviction to pathology, failing to distinguish between benign piety and toxic zeal. [[mode|Mode]]rn scholarship on ideological extremism and radicalization has found unexpected resonances with Holbach's insight that [[zeal|fanaticism]] operates through ordinary psychological mechanisms — [[interest|self-interest]], social recognition, and the [[passion|desire]] for meaning — rather than through exotic mental states. His demystification of [[missionaries|missionary]] motivation anticipated later Marxist and sociological critiques of religious institutions.

## Relation to Other Concepts

Fanaticism stands at the intersection of several Holbachian themes. It is the practical expression of [[superstition]] taken to its extreme — superstition provides the content, [[zeal|fanaticism]] provides the intensity. It is nourished by [[ignorance]], which prevents the fanatic from recognizing the natural causes of his own behavior. It is weaponized by [[priest]]-ly authority, which channels fanatical energy toward institutional ends. It is the opposite of [[reason]], which Holbach saw as the only reliable antidote to zealotry. It connects to [[prejudice]] as the cognitive foundation that makes [[zeal|fanaticism]] possible, and to [[tyranny]] as the political form that [[zeal|fanaticism]] takes when it seizes power.

## In the Text

Holbach's most sustained analysis of [[zeal|fanaticism]] appears in chapter 133, which demystifies the figure of the martyr:

> "To die for a religion does not prove it true or Divine; this proves at most that we suppose it to be so. An enthusiast in dying proves nothing but that religious fanaticism is often stronger than the love of life. An impostor can sometimes die with courage; he makes then, as is said, 'a virtue of necessity.'"
>
> (133-002)

He exposes the economic logic behind [[zeal|missionary zeal]]:

> "A missionary seeks fortune by the aid of his doctrine; he knows that if he has the good fortune to retail his commodity, he will become the absolute master of those who accept him as their guide; he is sure to become the object of their care, of their respect, of their veneration."
>
> (133-002)

In chapter 148, he describes the social consequences of fanatical devotion:

> "[Religion] has made it their sacred duty to torment, to persecute, to destroy those whose opinions are different from those of the priests. It has regarded as the most important and the most sacred thing, its chimerical duties as the most holy obligations, intolerance, and the spirit of persecution, as virtues necessary as they are legitimate."
>
> (148-001)
