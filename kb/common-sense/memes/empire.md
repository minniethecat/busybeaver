# Empire

## Definition

Holbach employs **empire** as a sustained political metaphor for [[divinity|divine]] [[government|governance]]. The universe is figured as "a vast empire governed by a monarch" — but this monarch's conduct is self-contradictory, his decrees are delivered through mutually hostile interpreters, and his subjects live in perpetual confusion about his will. The metaphor accomplishes two critical moves simultaneously: it demystifies theological language by translating it into political terms, and it exposes the practical [[absurdity|absurd]]ity of the [[government|governance]] model that theologians attribute to [[god|God]].

## Historical Context

The metaphor draws on 18th-century French experience with imperial and monarchical [[government|governance]]. The Holy [[roman-empire|Roman Empire]] — famously "neither [[sacred|holy]], nor Roman, nor an empire" ([[voltaire|Voltaire]]) — exemplified the gap between claimed authority and actual chaos. The [[roman-empire|Roman Empire]], which Holbach references explicitly, supplied historical precedent for the political function of religion: "a useful spring to the vast machine of the [[roman-empire|Roman Empire]]" describes how Roman elites instrumentalized religion for social control. The Gallican [[church|Church]]'s complex relationship with papal authority (French [[priest|clergy]] answering to both [[monarch|king]] and pope) mirrored the competing jurisdictions that characterize Holbach's empire metaphor. Gibbon's *Decline and Fall of the [[roman-empire|Roman Empire]]* (1776) was appearing contemporaneously, and Holbach's circle at the salon of Baron d'Holbach was deeply engaged with Gibbon's analysis of [[christianity|Christianity]]'s role in imperial decline.

## Core Arguments

The empire metaphor yields three critical insights. **Epistemological**: subjects of this empire receive contradictory reports about the monarch's will (competing [[bible|scripture]]s, rival theological systems) and have no direct access to verify which is correct. **Moral**: the monarch punishes subjects for failing to obey laws they could not have known, using eternal [[torture|torment]]s that no finite crime could justify. **Political**: the monarch's functionaries ([[clergy|priests]]) claim exclusive interpretative authority, treat each other as [[charlatan|impostor]]s, and profit from the confusion they claim to resolve.

## In the Text

> "There is a vast empire governed by a monarch, whose conduct does but little credit to his wisdom and to the goodness of his heart. The most opposite reports are spread of this prince; his subjects do not know exactly what pleases or displeases him. Those who pretend to be best informed treat each other as impostors and liars; they discredit each other mutually, and while they dispute without and fear, revering orders which they never follow. This empire is the world; this monarch is God." (ch. II)

> "A useful spring to the vast machine of the Roman Empire. By what right will God punish those to whom He has not given the grace to know His laws?" (ch. CXXX)

> "Under the empire of a just God, we find nothing but injustice and disorder." (ch. II)

Holbach's chapter II deploys the empire metaphor as its organizing conceit, wal[[monarch|king]] the reader through the [[absurdity|absurd]]ity of a [[monarch|king]]dom where the monarch's character cannot be discerned from his actions, where ambassadors contradict one another, and where subjects are punished for mis[[knowledge|understanding]] laws they never received.
