## Definition

In "Common Sense" (1772), Holbach defends a naturalistic conception of **human nature** against the Christian doctrine of original sin and fallenness. For Holbach, human nature is not a corrupted state requiring divine repair, but the necessary product of material and social causes operating according to the laws of nature. Religious claims that human nature is inherently depraved serve, in his analysis, to justify ecclesiastical authority and to render believers dependent on a supposed supernatural remedy that never actually materializes. (002-341)

Holbach's treatment of human nature is inseparable from his broader [[materialism]]: humans are physical beings whose inclinations, desires, and limitations arise from their constitution as natural entities, not from a mythological fall from grace. To condemn human nature as sinful is, in Holbach's view, to condemn the creator who supposedly made it — an incoherence at the heart of Christian theology. (002-131)

## Core Arguments

Holbach develops several interconnected arguments concerning human nature:

**1. The injustice of punishing inherent nature.** A just God would not create human beings with inherent weaknesses and then punish them for those very weaknesses. If God made human nature flawed, punishing humans for acting according to that nature is the "most unjust tyranny." (002-131) This anticipates the later philosophical problem of divine justice and theodicy.

**2. Renunciation of human nature as anti-human morality.** Christian virtue, Holbach argues, "consists in a total renunciation of human nature, in a voluntary oblivion of one's reason, in a holy hatred of self." These precepts produce conduct that is "cruel to ourselves and perfectly useless to others." (002-243) Religious morality, by demanding the impossible — the negation of one's own nature — becomes a source of psychological harm rather than ethical guidance.

**3. The failed repair.** Christian theology claims that Christ's death "repaired" human nature and restored it to its original integrity. Holbach points out the obvious: if human nature was truly repaired by the death of a God, why does it remain "still depraved"? The persistence of human imperfection after the supposed redemption is evidence that the doctrine is false. "What becomes of His omnipotence and His victory over the Devil, if it is true that the Devil still holds the empire?" (002-341)

**4. Morality must accord with nature.** A moral code that "contradicts the nature of man is not made for him." (002-340) Holbach insists that ethics must be grounded in the actual characteristics and needs of human beings rather than in supernatural commands that demand the impossible.

**5. Universal belief proves nothing.** The fact that all peoples have believed in "some intelligence superior to human nature" does not transform error into truth. As Holbach quotes Bayle: "Neither general tradition nor the unanimous consent of all men could place any injunction upon truth." (002-225)

## Historical Context

Holbach's defense of human nature belongs to the materialist wing of the French Enlightenment, alongside thinkers such as Diderot and La Mettrie. While Rousseau had argued that civilization corrupts an originally good human nature, and Christianity taught that human nature was corrupted by original sin, Holbach's position is distinctive: human nature is neither originally good nor fallen — it simply *is*, a product of natural causes, and the task of ethics is to work with it rather than against it.

This naturalistic view of human nature drew on the empiricist tradition (Locke's denial of innate ideas) and anticipated later utilitarian and naturalistic approaches to ethics. Holbach's insistence that morality must be compatible with human nature as it actually exists — rather than as theology wishes it to be — is one of his most enduring philosophical contributions.

## In the Text

Holbach addresses human nature across multiple chapters, weaving the theme through his critique of Providence, his analysis of Christian morality, and his late-chapter examination of religious hypocrisy:

> A God who punishes the faults which He could have prevented, is a being who lacks wisdom, goodness, and equity. A God of foresight would prevent evil, and in this way would be saved the trouble of punishing it. A good God would not punish weaknesses which He knows to be inherent in human nature.
>
> (002-131)

On the impossibility of renouncing one's own nature:

> Their virtue consists in a total renunciation of human nature, in a voluntary oblivion of one's reason, in a holy hatred of self; finally, these sublime precepts show us perfection in a conduct cruel to ourselves and perfectly useless to others.
>
> (002-243)

On the failed promise of supernatural repair:

> The Christian assures me that human nature is repaired, that the death of his God has reestablished it in its integrity. How comes it then, that human nature, notwithstanding the death of a God, is still depraved? Is it, then, a pure loss that your God died?
>
> (002-341)
