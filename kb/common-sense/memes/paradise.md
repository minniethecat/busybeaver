# Paradise

## Definition

Paradise, in Holbach's analysis, is the theological promise that functions as the ultimate instrument of social pacification.
The doctrine holds that present [[evil|suffering]] will be compensated by future bliss — that the injustices of this [[universe|world]] are a trial, not a wrong, and that the meek will inherit a [[monarch|king]]dom that exists nowhere on earth.
Holbach treats this promise not as a [[hope|consolation]] but as a [[imposture|deception]]: it is "imaginary" and designed to prevent men from demanding happiness in the only life they actually possess.

The critique operates on multiple levels.
Epistemologically, [[heaven|paradise]] is an assertion without [[proof|evidence]] — "The existence of another life has no other guaranty than the imagi[[nations|nation]] of men, who, in supposing it, have but manifested their [[passion|desire]] to live again" (ch. 56).
Morally, it weakens the impulse to seek justice: if the oppressed beli[[eve]] their reward lies elsewhere, they will not resist their oppressors.
Politically, it serves the powerful by directing popular attention away from earthly grievances toward a future that never arrives.

Holbach's conclusion is blunt: "If [[god|God]] has not been able to render men happier than they are here below, what will become of the hope of a Paradise, where it is pretended that the elect or chosen few will rejoice forever in ineffable happiness?" (ch. 56).

## Historical Context

The [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian doctrine of [[heaven|paradise]] was in the eighteenth century a ubiquitous feature of religious [[education|instruction]], popular preaching, and political rhetoric.
The [[catholic-church|Catholic Church]] taught that [[heaven|paradise]] was the reward of the [[religion|faith]]ful — accessible through the [[sacrament|sacraments]], mediated by [[clergy|the clergy]], and conditional upon obedience to [[authority|ecclesiastical authority]].
The Pope, in the words Holbach quotes with scorn, "possesses the keys of [[heaven|paradise]] and of [[punishment|hell]]" (ch. 69).

[[enlightenment|The Enlightenment]] subjected this doctrine to sustained criticism.
[[voltaire|Voltaire]]'s *Candide* (1759) satirized the [[gottfried-leibniz|Leibniz]]ian optimism that treated all earthly [[injustice|evil]] as necessary for a greater cosmic good.
D'Holbach went further: he denied not merely that [[heaven|paradise]] was just, but that the very concept was coherent.
If [[god|God]] could create [[existence|being]]s incapable of [[vice|sin]]ning in [[hope|heaven]], why did He not do so on earth?
"If the chosen ones are incapable of [[vice|sin]]ning in [[hope|heaven]], could not [[god|God]] have made them such from the beginning?" (ch. 69).

## Core Arguments

**1. Paradise is a groundless fiction.**
Holbach insists that no [[proof|evidence]] supports the existence of an [[death|afterlife]].
The [[passion|desire]] for continued existence proves nothing about its reality: wishful thin[[monarch|king]] does not constitute knowledge.

**2. Paradise is incompatible with a benevolent [[god|God]].**
If [[god|God]] is good, why does He require a lifetime of [[evil|suffering]] as the price of admission to happiness?
"Could He not at least, without bestowing an infinite happiness upon men, communicate to them that degree of happiness of which finite [[existence|being]]s are susceptible?" (ch. 56).

**3. Paradise serves priestly power.**
The [[priest|clergy]] control access to the [[death|afterlife]]: through confession, absolution, and the [[sacrament|sacraments]], they determine who enters [[heaven|paradise]] and who does not.
The doctrine is a financial instrument — "the Pope, who possesses the keys of [[heaven|paradise]] and of [[punishment|hell]]" (ch. 69) — and the keys are not distributed freely.

**4. Paradise reconciles men to injustice.**
"Tired of an unintelligible [[religion|theology]], ridiculous fables, and impenetrable [[mysteries]], let the human mind occupy itself with natural objects, comprehensible truths, and sensible interests" (ch. 2).
The promise of [[heaven|paradise]] diverts attention from the real, pressing needs of this life.

**5. Paradise contradicts the observable [[universe|world]].**
The claim that [[nature|the universe]] is arranged for human benefit is falsified by experience.
"An exalted imagi[[nations|nation]] sees in [[nature|the universe]] but the benefactions of [[heaven|Heaven]]; a calm mind finds good and [[injustice|evil]] in it" (ch. 99).
[[nature|Nature]] is indifferent; [[heaven|paradise]] is a projection of human [[passion|desire]] onto an empty sky.

## Reception and Critique

Holbach's rejection of [[heaven|paradise]] was one of the most scandalous elements of his philosophy for contemporary readers.
The charge that he deprived the [[evil|suffering]] of their only [[hope|consolation]] was made repeatedly by [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian apologists.
The abbé Bergier, Holbach's principal critic, argued that the hope of [[hope|heaven]] was a necessary support for [[morality|virtue]] among those who received no reward in this life.

The Marxist tradition adopted and secularized parts of Holbach's critique: "religion is the opium of the people" echoes the claim that promises of [[hope|heaven]]ly reward pacify the exploited.
Yet the persistence of [[death|afterlife]] belief in secular societies suggests that Holbach may have underestimated the psychological — as distinct from the institutional — sources of the hope for [[heaven|paradise]].

The existentialist tradition (Camus, Sartre) took up Holbach's challenge without accepting his rationalist [[certainty|confidence]]: if [[heaven|paradise]] is an illusion, what meaning can human life possess?

## Relation to Other Concepts

Paradise is the positive pole of the [[hell]] doctrine — the carrot to [[punishment|hell]]'s stick.
Together they form the incentive system that Holbach calls "imaginary."

Paradise is closely tied to [[hope]]: it is hope displaced from the earthly to [[supernatural|the supernatural]], and therefore hope rendered passive rather than active.
It is administered by the [[priest]], who alone can grant or withhold access through the [[sacrament|sacraments]].

The concept is opposed to [[reason]], which demands [[proof|evidence]] for claims about invisible realms, and to [[nature]], which reveals a universe indifferent to human [[passion|desire]]s for justice and compensation.

Paradise functions alongside [[fear]] — the fear of losing [[heaven|paradise]], or of gaining [[punishment|hell]] instead — to sustain the psychological hold of religion over the mind.
