# Conscience

## Definition

Holbach treats con[[knowledge|science]] not as an infallible [[divinity|divine]] voice within each person, nor as an innate moral sense, but as the internalized product of education, habit, and social norms. "Conscience," he w[[ritual|rites]] through [[pascal|Pascal]]'s words, can become the instrument of the worst crimes when it is shaped by a false principle. A religiously-formed con[[knowledge|science]] — one taught that [[god|God]] commands the extermi[[nations|nation]] of [[heresy|heretic]]s, or that eternal torture is justice — becomes a danger to humanity precisely because its bearer acts with the conviction of righteousness.

This psychological account of con[[knowledge|science]] is central to Holbach's critique of religious morality. If con[[knowledge|science]] were a direct communication from [[god|God]], it would speak with one voice across [[worship|cult]]ures and individuals. That it instead reflects the moral education one has received — varying wildly between religions, [[sect|sects]], and epochs — [[demon|demons]]trates its human, social origin. The cruelty licensed by religious con[[knowledge|science]] is not an aberration but a predictable consequence of [[doctrine|teaching]] people that [[god|God]]'s will overrides ordinary human sympathy.

## Historical Context

The concept of con[[knowledge|science]] underwent significant transformation in the early modern period. The medieval scholastic tradition, following Aquinas, had treated con[[knowledge|science]] (*conscientia*) as the application of innate moral knowledge (*synderesis*) to particular cases. [[reformation|The Reformation]] intensified the importance of individual con[[knowledge|science]] — Luther's declaration at Worms, "My con[[knowledge|science]] is captive to the Word of [[god|God]]," made con[[knowledge|science]] the ultimate tribunal, but only when aligned with [[scripture|Scripture]] as interpreted by the reformer.

[[enlightenment|The Enlightenment]] philosophers developed increasingly naturalistic accounts of con[[knowledge|science]]. Shaftesbury and Hutcheson proposed an innate "moral sense." [[david-hume|Hume]] made sympathy the foundation of moral judgment. Rousseau celebrated con[[knowledge|science]] as a "[[divinity|divine]] instinct." Holbach's innovation is to emphasize the dark side of con[[knowledge|science]]: its malleability, its capacity to amplify cruelty when shaped by religious doctrine, and its vulnerability to manipulation by priestly authority. "[[nothingness|Nothing]] is more dangerous than a religion which licenses the ferocity of the people, and justifies in their eyes the blackest crimes."

## Core Arguments

**1. Conscience is acquired, not innate.** The content of con[[knowledge|science]] — what it approves and condemns — is entirely the product of education and social environment. A child raised among cannibals develops a con[[knowledge|science]] that permits cannibalism; a child raised among inquisitors develops a con[[knowledge|science]] that permits torture. There is no universal moral intuition that survives [[worship|cult]]ural transmission intact.

**2. Religious con[[knowledge|science]] is especially dangerous.** When con[[knowledge|science]] is shaped by religious doctrines, it can override natural human sympathy. The believer who thinks he is serving [[god|God]] by persecuting [[heresy|heretic]]s acts "through a false principle of con[[knowledge|science]]" — and therefore "does [[injustice|evil]] so thoroughly and so willingly" that no ordinary restraint can check him.

**3. Conscience is used by the powerful to evade accountability.** Holbach observes that tyrants and wicked [[monarch|prince]]s "whose con[[knowledge|science]] reproaches them" do not reform their conduct; they "bargain with this invisible Judge" or with accommodating [[clergy|priests]] who absolve them. Conscience, when conceived as an account to be settled with [[god|God]] rather than with the victims of one's crimes, becomes a mechanism for moral evasion.

**4. A rational con[[knowledge|science]] must be built on [[proof|evidence]], not authority.** The only reliable con[[knowledge|science]] is one shaped by reason, experience, and genuine concern for human welfare — not by fear of [[punishment|divine punishment]] or hope of [[reward|divine reward]]. Such a con[[knowledge|science]] evaluates actions by their real effects on real people, not by their conformity to revealed commands.

## Reception and Critique

Holbach's naturalistic account of con[[knowledge|science]] anticipates several later developments. Freud's superego — the internalized voice of parental and social authority — is a direct descendant of Holbach's analysis. Nietzsche's critique of "bad con[[knowledge|science]]" as the internalization of cruelty similarly echoes Holbach's concern with the dark potential of religiously-formed con[[knowledge|science]].

Contemporary moral psychology (Haidt, Greene) has provided empirical support for the view that moral judgment is shaped by [[worship|cult]]ural [[education|learning]] and [[passion|emotion]]al conditioning rather than by a universal [[reason|rational faculty]] or [[divinity|divine]] implant. However, the existence of cross-[[worship|cult]]ural moral universals — prohibitions on murder, incest, and betrayal — suggests that Holbach may have overstated the malleability of con[[knowledge|science]].

## Relation to Other Concepts

Conscience is shaped by [[Education]] and [[Religion]], both of which can deform it into an instrument of cruelty. It is closely related to [[Morality]], which Holbach seeks to ground in reason and human welfare rather than [[divinity|divine]] command. A corrupted con[[knowledge|science]] is the psychological mechanism by which [[Superstition]] justifies [[cruelty|persecution]]. The faculty that can correct an erring con[[knowledge|science]] is [[Reason]], which evaluates actions by their real consequences. Conscience is also related to [[Virtue]], which for Holbach consists in acting consistently for human good rather than in obedience to authority.

## In the Text

On the danger of false con[[knowledge|science]]:

> "Never, says Pascal, do we do evil so thoroughly and so willingly as when we do it through a false principle of conscience. Nothing is more dangerous than a religion which licenses the ferocity of the people, and justifies in their eyes the blackest crimes; it puts no limits to their wickedness as soon as they believe it authorized by their God, whose interests, as they are told, can justify all their actions."
>
> (158-002)

On con[[knowledge|science]] as a tool of evasion for the powerful:

> "Many tyrants and wicked princes, whose conscience reproaches them for their negligence or their perversity, far from fearing their God, rather like to bargain with this invisible Judge, who never refuses anything, or with His priests, who are accommodating to the masters of the earth rather than to their subjects."
>
> (002-292)

On religions authorizing crimes:

> "All religions of the world have authorized countless crimes. The Jews, excited by the promises of their God, arrogated to themselves the right of exterminating whole nations. The Christians, following the example of the Jews, have persecuted, tortured, and destroyed those who refused to adopt their doctrines."
>
> (158-002)
