# Hell

## Definition

For Holbach, the doctrine of [[punishment|hell]] is not merely a theological error but the keystone of religious domi[[nations|nation]]. It is the ultimate threat — eternal, inescapable, infinitely terrible — that makes all other religious claims psychologically compelling. "[[religion|Religion]] speaks to us of a [[punishment|hell]] — that is, of a fearful place where, notwithstanding His goodness, [[god|God]] reserves eternal [[torture|torment]]s for the majority of men." The concept is internally contradictory: a [[god|God]] who is infinitely good cannot simultaneously be infinitely cruel, and a [[existence|being]] who undergoes a change from infinite goodness to "implacable fury" cannot be the immutable [[existence|being]] [[religion|theology]] claims.

Holbach's analysis operates on three levels. Theologically, [[punishment|hell]] undermines [[eve]]ry [[divinity|divine]] attribute religion claims to uphold — goodness, justice, immutability. Psychologically, it is the primary mechanism of [[fear|religious fear]], short-circuiting rational inquiry by ma[[monarch|king]] doubt too terrifying to entertain. Sociologically, it is the ultimate instrument of priestly control, [[vice|sin]]ce [[clergy|priests]] alone claim the power to grant or deny escape from it.

## Historical Context

The [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian doctrine of [[punishment|hell]] developed from relatively sparse biblical references into an elaborate system of infernal torture in the medieval period. Aquinas systematized the doctrine, arguing that the [[evil|suffering]] of the damned was not only just but would be witnessed by the saved as part of their beatitude — a claim that horrified Enlightenment critics. [[reformation|The Reformation]] did not soften the doctrine; Calvin's *Institutes* describes the reprobate as "vessels of wrath" predestined to damnation from before creation.

By Holbach's time, however, the doctrine was under sustained attack. The English deists (Tindal, Chubb) had argued that eternal punishment was disproportionate to finite crimes and therefore incompatible with [[justice|divine justice]]. [[david-hume|Hume]] had attacked the reliability of testimony for [[miracle|miracles]] and [[dreams|revelations]], undermining the epistemic basis for [[punishment|hell]]. Holbach's contribution is to synthesize these critiques and connect them to a broader analysis of how the fear of [[punishment|hell]] serves priestly power — it is not just false but *useful* to those who rule through [[fear|terror]].

## Core Arguments

**1. Hell contradicts [[goodness|divine goodness]].** A [[god|God]] who creates the majority of humanity knowing they will suffer eternally is not good. "A [[god|God]] who, after having been infinitely good, becomes infinitely wicked, can He be regarded as an immutable [[existence|being]]?" The attempt to reconcile [[punishment|hell]] with [[goodness|divine goodness]] — by appeal to justice, to [[liberty|free will]], to the demands of [[divinity|divine]] honor — is sophistry designed to preserve a doctrine that is morally repugnant.

**2. Hell is psychologically coercive.** The fear of [[punishment|hell]] is not a rational motive for moral conduct but a [[fear|terror]] that paralyzes the critical faculties. It is the ultimate version of what Holbach identifies as religion's primary psychological mechanism: "[[fear|Fear]] makes slaves; reason alone makes truly wise men." The person who believes in [[punishment|hell]] cannot freely examine his beliefs because the cost of [[existence|being]] wrong is eternal.

**3. Hell serves priestly power.** The doctrine of [[punishment|hell]] gives [[clergy|priests]] a monopoly on salvation. Only through [[church|the Church]], only through the [[sacrament|sacraments]], only through obedience to priestly authority, can the terrifying prospect of [[punishment|hell]] be averted. This monopoly is the source of priestly wealth, political influence, and social control.

**4. The fear of [[punishment|hell]] is weaker than social fear.** In a stri[[monarch|king]] psychological observation, Holbach notes that the fear of [[punishment|hell]] is often less motivating than the fear of social disgrace. A soldier "with the fear of [[honor|dishonor]], does he not [[chance|hazard]] his life in battles [[eve]]ry day, [[eve]]n at the risk of incurring eternal damnation?" This [[demon|demons]]trates that [[punishment|hell]]-belief, however [[vice|sin]]cerely professed, does not govern actual behavior as powerfully as religion claims.

## Reception and Critique

Holbach's critique of [[punishment|hell]] was among his most influential arguments. The 19th century saw a widespread liberal Protestant retreat from the doctrine of eternal punishment — a retreat that Holbach's polemic helped accelerate. Universalism (the doctrine that all will ultimately be saved) gained ground in both Protestant and Catholic [[religion|theology]].

Contemporary theological responses include the "[[liberty|free will]] defense" of [[punishment|hell]] (C.S. Lewis: "the doors of [[punishment|hell]] are locked on the inside"), [[death|annihilation]]ism (the damned are simply destroyed rather than tortured eternally), and continued universalist arguments. Holbach would likely dismiss these as attempts to soften a doctrine whose fundamental purpose — control through fear — remains unchanged.

## Relation to Other Concepts

Hell is the ultimate sanction behind [[Religion]]'s system of rewards and punishments. It depends on the doctrine of [[Liberty]] ([[liberty|free will]]) to appear just: if humans choose freely, they can be held eternally accountable for that choice. The fear of [[punishment|hell]] is the psychological mechanism that sustains [[Faith]] in the face of contrary [[proof|evidence]]. Hell is the opposite of [[Happiness]], which Holbach argues is the natural goal of human life and which religion sacrifices to an imaginary future. The rejection of [[punishment|hell]] is a logical consequence of accepting [[Nature]] as the whole of reality, with no supernatural realm beyond it.

## In the Text

On [[punishment|hell]] as a contradiction of [[goodness|divine goodness]]:

> "Religion speaks to us of a hell — that is, of a fearful place where, notwithstanding His goodness, God reserves eternal torments for the majority of men. Thus, after having rendered mortals very miserable in this world, religion teaches them that God can make them much more wretched in another. They meet our objections by saying, that otherwise the goodness of God would take the place of His justice. But goodness which takes the place of the most terrible cruelty, is not infinite kindness. Besides, a God who, after having been infinitely good, becomes infinitely wicked, can He be regarded as an immutable being? A God filled with implacable fury, is He a God in whom we can find a shadow of charity or goodness?" (061-001)

On the limits of [[punishment|hell]]-fear compared to social fear:

> "A warrior with the fear of dishonor, does he not hazard his life in battles every day, even at the risk of incurring eternal damnation? The most religious persons sometimes show more respect for a servant than for God." (142-001)(142-002)
