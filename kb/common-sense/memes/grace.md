# Grace

## Definition

In [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian [[religion|theology]], "grace" (Latin *gratia*) denotes the unmerited favor and supernatural assistance bestowed by [[god|God]] upon fallen humanity to enable salvation. According to orthodox doctrine, man's [[universe|nature]] is so corrupted by [[sin|original sin]] that he cannot perform any salutary act or turn toward [[god|God]] without the prior infusion of [[divinity|divine]] grace. Grace is thus the indispensable medium through which [[god|God]] elects, justifies, and sanctifies the redeemed.

Holbach subjects this doctrine to a sustained rational critique, arguing that the concept of grace, rather than exalting [[mercy|divine mercy]], exposes the logical [[contradiction|incoherence]] of [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian [[divinity|theism]]. If grace is necessary for moral action, and [[god|God]] dispenses it selectively, then [[god|God]] bears full responsibility for human [[vice|sin]] — and His punishment of the ungraced is not justice but sadistic caprice. Throughout *Common Sense*, Holbach treats grace not as a theological subtopic but as the linchpin that connects the doctrines of [[sin|original sin]], [[liberty|free will]], predesti[[nations|nation]], and eternal [[hell|damnation]] into a [[vice|sin]]gle self-refuting system.

## Historical Context

The theological concept of grace was shaped by centuries of doctrinal controversy, most notably the Pelagian dispute of the fifth century and the [[reform|Reform]]ation-era debates between Protestant and Catholic theologians over the relationship between grace, [[liberty|free will]], and justification. By the eighteenth century, the Jansenist controversy in [[paris|France]] — with its Augustinian emphasis on the necessity and exclusivity of efficacious grace — had rendered the doctrine a live political and intellectual issue. [[pascal|Pascal]]'s *Provincial Letters* (1656–57) had brought these disputes before a literate public, and the question of whether grace was "sufficient" or "efficacious," and whether it could be resisted, remained a matter of fierce ecclesiastical contention.

Holbach inherited this theological vocabulary but redirected it toward radical ends. Where [[jansenists|Jansenists]] and [[jesuits|Jesuits]] quarreled over the mechanics of grace's operation, Holbach questioned the entire framework: if [[god|God]] is infinitely good, why is grace necessary at all? Why does an omnipotent [[existence|being]] not simply create creatures who naturally will the good? His critique belongs to the wider Enlightenment project of subjecting received [[dogma|theological dogma]]s to the tribunal of reason, alongside the work of [[pierre-bayle|Bayle]], [[voltaire|Voltaire]], and Diderot.

## Core Arguments

1. **Grace renders [[god|God]] a capricious tyrant.** If [[god|God]] "distributes His favors and His graces according to His caprice" (002-094), He violates the very contract upon which religion is founded — the expectation that devotion will be rewarded with happiness. A [[god|God]] who grants grace to a few while abandoning the many to [[hell|damnation]] is not a loving father but an arbitrary [[despotism|despot]].

2. **Grace destroys [[liberty|free will]].** Theologians insist that man possesses [[liberty|free will]], yet simultaneously teach that "without grace, man is compelled to do [[injustice|evil]]" (ch. 80).

If a person cannot do good without a supernatural gift that [[god|God]] may choose to withhold, the concept of moral agency collapses. [[reward|Reward]]ing the graced and punishing the ungraced reduces [[justice|divine justice]] to [[absurdity|absurd]]ity: [[god|God]] "punishes him for not having been given the grace, without which it was impossible for him to do better" (ch. 79).

3. **The selective distribution of grace contradicts [[goodness|divine goodness]].** An infinitely good [[existence|being]] would extend the means of salvation to all. The doctrine that "the number of the chosen ones is very small" and that salvation requires "such grace as your [[god|God]] grants to but few" (002-197) portrays [[god|God]] as a respecter of persons, indifferent to the vast majority of His creatures. If grace is necessary for [[morality|virtue]], a good [[god|God]] would give it universally.

4. **Grace functions as an evasion from moral responsibility.** The [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian laity invokes the lack of grace to excuse their failure to live up to [[gospel|Gospel morality]]: "all the [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ians continually admire the morals of [[gospel|the Gospel]], but it is practiced but by a small number of [[saint|saints]]; admired by people who themselves avoid imitating their conduct, under the pretext that they are lac[[monarch|king]] either the power or the grace" (002-334). The doctrine thus provides a convenient rationalization for moral mediocrity while preserving the appearance of piety.

5. **The grace-[[hell|damnation]] nexus makes [[god|God]] a moral monster.** Holbach argues that [[god|God]] "refuses them His grace, in order to have the satis[[sect|faction]] of punishing them eternally for having been hardened, blinded, and not having received the grace which He refused them" (002-405). This is not justice — it is the conduct of a [[existence|being]] "the most odious . . . that the human mind could imagine" (ch. 188).

## Reception and Critique

Holbach's critique of grace was part of a broader materialist attack on [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian anthropology that provoked fierce reactions from both Catholic and Protestant apologists. His contemporaneous critics — including Bergier and Holland — accused him of caricaturing the doctrine by ignoring the distinction between sufficient and efficacious grace, and by collapsing prevenient grace into irresistible grace.

From a theological perspective, the charge that Holbach fails to engage with the nuance of the tradition has some merit. Augustine, Aquinas, and the [[council-of-trent|Council of Trent]] all developed careful distinctions between kinds and operations of grace that Holbach glosses over. However, his essential argument — that *any* system in which a purportedly omnibenevolent [[god|God]] makes salvation depend on a gift He withholds from most people is morally incoherent — does not depend on these intra-theological distinctions. The philosophical problem of grace (commonly called the "problem of [[divinity|divine]] hiddenness" in contemporary philosophy of religion) remains a live area of debate.

## Relation to Other Concepts

Grace is inseparably linked to [[Original Sin]], which supplies the anthropological premise — human [[universe|nature]]'s supposed corruption — that makes grace necessary in the first place. Without the fable of [[original-sin|the Fall]], the entire apparatus of grace, [[salvation|redemption]], and atonement collapses. Grace connects to [[Free Will]] through the paradox that Holbach identifies: if grace is required for morally good action, [[liberty|free will]] is a chimera. The doctrine of grace also inter[[sect|sects]] with [[Salvation]], [[vice|sin]]ce grace is the theological mechanism by which the elect are saved, and with [[God]], whose [[attribute|attributes]] of goodness and justice are rendered contradictory by the selective bestowal of grace. Finally, grace relates to [[Punishment]], [[vice|sin]]ce the [[hell|eternal punishment]] of the ungraced is, for Holbach, the ultimate [[evidence|proof]] of [[injustice|divine injustice]].

## In the Text

> "Is not God the master of His favors? Has He not the right to dispense His benefits? ... In neglecting the happiness of His creatures, in distributing His favors and His graces according to His caprice, and taking back His gifts, does not God violate the contract which serves as a base for all religion?" (ch. 60)

> "According to the principles of theologians themselves, man, in his actual state of corruption, can do nothing but evil, for without Divine grace he has not the strength to do good. ... in punishing man for the evil he does, God punishes him for not having been given the grace, without which it was impossible for him to do better." (002-132)

> "They suppose that, without grace, man is compelled to do evil: and they maintain that God will punish him for not having been given the grace to do good! ... grave Doctors of Divinity assure us that a just God will damn without mercy all those to whom He has not given the grace to know the religion of the Christians." (002-133)

> "You tell us ... God ... refuses them His grace, in order to have the satisfaction of punishing them eternally for having been hardened, blinded, and not having received the grace which He refused them. ... A God who damns eternally must be the most odious Being that the human mind could imagine." (002-405)
