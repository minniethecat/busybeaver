# Solomon

## Biography

Solomon (Hebrew: Shlomo, "peaceful") was, according to the biblical narrative, the son of [[david|David]] and Bathsheba, and the third [[monarch|king]] of a united [[israel|Israel]]. His reign (traditionally dated to the tenth century BCE) is depicted in 1 Kings 1–11 and 2 Chronicles 1–9 as a golden age of wealth, [[philosophy|wisdom]], and imperial expansion. Solomon is credited with building the First [[temple|Temple]] in [[jerusalem|Jerusalem]] — the central sanctuary of [[israel|Israel]]ite worship — and with authoring the biblical books of Proverbs, Ecclesiastes, and the Song of Songs, along with the apocryphal [[wisdom|Wisdom]] of Solomon.

Solomon's legendary [[philosophy|wisdom]] is established in the narrative of the two women who claimed the same infant, each insisting she was the mother. Solomon's command to cut the child in half — and his identification of the true mother as the woman who would rather lose the child than see it killed — became the paradigmatic example of judicial [[philosophy|wisdom]] in Western tradition. The Queen of Sheba's visit, bearing gifts of gold and spices, testifies to Solomon's international [[honor|reputation]].

Yet the biblical narrative also records Solomon's decline: his many foreign wives "turned away his heart" to the worship of other gods, and after his death the [[monarch|king]]dom split into [[israel|Israel]] in the north and Judah in the south. The golden age ended with its architect.

## Role in Religious History

Solomon occupies a unique and contradictory position in biblical tradition. He is at once the builder of the [[temple|Temple]] — the house of [[god|God]] on earth — and the [[monarch|king]] whose idolatry caused the [[monarch|king]]dom's division. He is the author of the [[bible|Bible]]'s [[philosophy|wisdom]] literature and yet, as Holbach notes, "the most incredulous" of biblical writers — a man whose canonical words express doubt about the very doctrines later orthodoxy would insist upon.

The [[gospel|Gospels]] trace [[jesus-christ|Jesus]]'s [[david|David]]ic lineage through Solomon ([[matthew|Matthew]] 1:6–7), ma[[monarch|king]] Solomon a necessary link in the messianic genealogy — a point Holbach seizes upon when [[demon|demons]]trating the contradictions between the Matthean and Lukan genealogies. The [[wisdom|Wisdom]] of Solomon, though relegated to the Apocrypha by Protestant traditions, remained canonical for Catholic and Orthodox [[christianity|Christianity]] and provided crucial textual support for doctrines of the [[immortality|immortality]] of the [[soul|soul]].

## Connection to "Common Sense"

Holbach's discussion of Solomon is brief but strategically devastating. Rather than attac[[monarch|king]] Solomon directly, he deploys Solomon as a witness *against* orthodox [[christianity|Christianity]] — the [[bible|Bible]]'s own wisest author, doubting the [[bible|Bible]]'s own doctrines.

**1. Solomon the skeptic.** In his survey of the biblical canon, Holbach pauses over the [[philosophy|wisdom]] literature and observes:

> "There are, however, in a few of these books, several good teachings and beautiful maxims of morals, as in the Proverbs attributed to Solomon, in the book of Wisdom and of Ecclesiastes; but this same Solomon, the wisest of their writers, is also the most incredulous; he doubts even the immortality of the soul, and concludes his works by saying that there is nothing good but to enjoy in peace the fruit of one's labor."
>
> (002-477)

This is a remarkable argumentative move. Holbach does not need to prove the non-existence of the [[soul|immortal soul]] from outside the text — he can point to Solomon, the [[bible|Bible]]'s own [[philosophy|wisdom]] paragon, expressing the same doubt. If the wisest man in [[bible|scripture]] does not beli[[eve]] in the [[soul|soul]]'s im[[death|mortality]], what authority does the doctrine possess?

**2. The genealogy contradiction.** In his catalogue of biblical contradictions, Holbach notes:

> "The Evangelist Matthew claims that Jesus Christ descended from king David by his son Solomon through Joseph, reputed to be His father; and Luke claims that He is descended from the same David by his son Nathan through Joseph."
>
> (002-480)

Solomon appears here as a link in a broken chain. [[matthew|Matthew]] traces [[jesus-christ|Jesus]] through Solomon; [[luke|Luke]] traces him through Nathan, another son of [[david|David]]. Both cannot be correct, and if the genealogy — the very documentation of [[jesus-christ|Jesus]]'s [[david|Davidic]] legitimacy — is internally contradictory, then the claim it supports is undermined.

**3. Solomon as internal witness.** Holbach's use of Solomon exemplifies his broader strategy of turning the [[bible|Bible]] against itself. He does not argue from the outside that [[bible|scripture]] is unreliable; he finds within [[bible|scripture]] — in Solomon's own words — the seeds of the doubt he wishes to [[worship|cult]]ivate. Solomon becomes, in Holbach's hands, an unwilling ally of the [[materialism|materialist]] critique.

## Key Ideas Attributed / Critiqued

**1. [[wisdom|Wisdom]] is not revelation.** The Proverbs and Ecclesiastes contain, Holbach grants, "good [[doctrine|teaching]]s and beautiful maxims of morals." But this is precisely the point: they are *human* [[philosophy|wisdom]], the accumulated practical knowledge of a [[worship|cult]]ure, dressed in the language of [[divinity|divine]] inspiration. Solomon's [[philosophy|wisdom]] is real — and it is wholly natural.

**2. The internal critic as the most dangerous witness.** Solomon's doubt about the [[immortality|immortality]] of the [[soul|soul]] is, for Holbach, more damaging to orthodox doctrine than any external critique could be. An [[atheism|atheist]] philosopher can be dismissed as [[opinion|prejudice]]d; the [[bible|Bible]]'s own wisest author cannot.

**3. Genealogy as fabricated legitimacy.** The contradictory genealogies reveal the function of such lists: not historical record-keeping but retrospective [[authority|authority]]-construction. Solomon is pressed into service as a legitimizing ancestor, just as the patriarchal promises are pressed into service as a legitimizing origin-story.

## Legacy

Solomon's philosophical and [[worship|cult]]ural legacy is vast. The [[judgment|Judgment]] of Solomon has become a universal archetype of judicial [[philosophy|wisdom]], invoked from legal theory to game theory. Ecclesiastes — "vanity of vanities, all is vanity" — has resonated with skeptics and existentialists across centuries, its author recognized as a kindred [[soul|spirit]] by those who doubt whether human life has transcendent meaning.

In Jewish tradition, Solomon's [[temple|Temple]] remains the symbolic center of religious [[hope|aspiration]]; the Western Wall, a remnant of the [[temple|Temple]]'s retaining wall, is the holiest site in Judaism. In [[islam|Islam]]ic tradition, Suleyman is a [[prophecy|prophet]]-[[monarch|king]] who commands the wind and speaks with [[soul|anima]]ls. In Ethiopian [[christianity|Christianity]], the Solomonic dynasty claimed direct descent from Solomon and the Queen of Sheba until the [[monarch|monarchy]]'s abolition in 1974.

For Holbach's purposes, Solomon is neither temple-builder nor [[prophecy|prophet]]-[[monarch|king]] but the [[bible|Bible]]'s own reluctant materialist — a man who, having tasted all that wealth, power, and [[philosophy|wisdom]] could offer, concluded that "there is nothing good but to enjoy in peace the fruit of one's labor."
