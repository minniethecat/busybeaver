# Pleasure

## Definition

In Holbach's naturalistic [[morality|ethics]], pleasure occupies the position that [[divinity|divine]] command occupies in religious morality: it is the fundamental criterion of value. Pleasure, understood broadly as the satis[[sect|faction]] of natural human needs and [[passion|desire]]s, is not a temptation to be resisted but a guide to be followed. "The universe is but what it can be; all sentient [[existence|being]]s enjoy and suffer here: that is to say, they are moved sometimes in an agreeable, and at other times in a disagreeable way" (ch. 56). On this view, pleasure and [[suffering|pain]] are not moral categories but natural facts — the signals by which [[universe|nature]] directs organisms toward what preserves them and away from what destroys them.

Holbach's rehabilitation of pleasure is a direct challenge to [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian asceticism. The doctrine that earthly pleasures are [[sin|sinful]], that the body is a source of corruption, and that true happiness is attainable only in the [[death|afterlife]] is, in his analysis, a strategy of clerical control. By [[doctrine|teaching]] people to despise the pleasures within their reach, [[clergy|priests]] redirect their hopes — and their offerings — toward a future that only [[church|the Church]] can mediate. Against this, Holbach insists that "in order to be happy, do we need an [[infinite|Infinite]] or [[theology-index|Divine]] happiness?" (ch. 58). [[finite|Finite]] [[existence|being]]s require finite goods — and those goods are available in this [[universe|world]], not the next.

## Historical Context

Holbach's defense of pleasure belongs to a rich tradition of Epicurean revival in early modern thought. Pierre Gassendi had rehabilitated [[epicurus|Epicurus]] for [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian philosophy in the seventeenth century, arguing that Epicurean pleasure (*ataraxia*, or tranquility) was compatible with [[morality|virtue]]. By the mid-eighteenth century, the more radical materialists — La Mettrie, Diderot, Helvétius — had dropped the [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian framework and embraced pleasure as an autonomous ethical principle.

Holbach's distinct contribution was to integrate the [[morality|ethics]] of pleasure into a systematic critique of religion. For Holbach, the religious devaluation of earthly pleasure is not an incidental feature of [[christianity|Christianity]] but its functional core. "All religion is founded upon the happiness which men beli[[eve]] they have a right to expect from the powerful [[existence|being]] which they adore" (ch. 60). By redirecting the expectation of happiness from this [[universe|world]] to the next, religion secures a monopoly on the human hope for [[happiness|well-being]] — and charges a premium, payable to [[clergy|priests]], for access.

## Core Arguments

1. **Pleasure is natural, not [[sin|sinful]].** The capacity to experience pleasure and [[suffering|pain]] is a biological fact, not a consequence of [[original-sin|the Fall]]. "All sentient [[existence|being]]s enjoy and suffer here" (ch. 56) — this is the natural condition of embodied existence, and there is no moral taint in see[[monarch|king]] enjoyment and avoiding [[evil|suffering]].

2. **[[earth|Earth]]ly pleasures are real; [[hope|heaven]]ly happiness is imaginary.** Holbach asks: "In order to be happy, do we need an [[infinite|Infinite]] or [[theology-index|Divine]] happiness?" (ch. 58). The answer is no. [[finite|Finite]] [[existence|being]]s can achi[[eve]] finite happiness through the satis[[sect|faction]] of natural needs — food, shelter, companionship, intellectual stimulation. The promise of infinite bliss is a fantasy that distracts from the real possibilities of finite [[happiness|well-being]].

3. **Pleasure is necessarily mixed with [[suffering|pain]], but this does not invalidate it.** Holbach acknowledges the obvious objection — "Are not his pleasures mingled with [[evil|suffering]]s? Are there many people who are contented with their fate?" (ch. 52). The answer is not to abandon pleasure for asceticism but to accept the mixed character of human existence without postulating a compensatory [[death|afterlife]].

4. **The religious devaluation of pleasure serves priestly power.** By convincing people that earthly pleasures are worthless or [[sin|sinful]], [[clergy|priests]] become the gatekeepers of a deferred happiness accessible only through ecclesiastical mediation. "[[sacrifice|Sacrifice]] your reason; give up experience" (ch. 135) — the command to sacrifice pleasure is part of the same structure of control.

5. **Pleasure, not [[divinity|divine]] command, is the foundation of secular morality.** If human [[existence|being]]s naturally seek pleasure and avoid [[suffering|pain]], [[morality|ethics]] can be grounded in this natural fact rather than in supernatural revelation. The function of morality is to coordinate individual pursuits of pleasure so that they do not conflict — a task for reason and social arrangement, not [[divinity|divine]] legislation.

## Reception and Critique

Holbach's ethical hedonism was controversial [[eve]]n among Enlightenment thinkers. Rousseau, in *Émile*, had warned that the reduction of morality to [[interest|self-interest]] undermined civic [[morality|virtue]]. Kant would later argue that [[morality|ethics]] grounded in pleasure (hypothetical imperatives) could never yield genuine moral obligation (the categorical imperative). The "pleasure" that Holbach celebrates, critics charged, is too thin a foundation for the complexity of moral life.

Utilitarian thinkers — Bentham, Mill — would develop a more sophisticated version of the pleasure principle, distinguishing higher from lower pleasures and incorporating the greatest happiness principle. From the utilitarian perspective, Holbach's contribution was to clear the ground: by demolishing the claim that pleasure is inherently [[sin|sinful]], he made possible a secular [[morality|ethics]] of [[happiness|well-being]]. His limitation was the absence of a systematic calculus for adjudicating conflicts between competing pleasures — the problem that utilitarian philosophy would attempt to solve.

## Relation to Other Concepts

Pleasure is the positive pole of the sensory spectrum whose negative pole is [[Pain]]. Together, they form the natural guidance system that Holbach counterposes to [[Revelation]]. Pleasure connects to [[Happiness]], of which it is the most important component. It relates to [[Morality]], which Holbach seeks to ground in the natural pursuit of pleasure rather than [[divinity|divine]] command. Pleasure is systematically devalued by [[Religion]], which redirects human hope toward deferred, otherworldly gratification. The concept connects to [[Nature]], whose "necessary laws" determine that sentient [[existence|being]]s pursue pleasure and avoid [[suffering|pain]]. Finally, pleasure connects to [[Reason]], which orders and coordinates individual pursuits of pleasure into a coherent [[morality|ethics]].
