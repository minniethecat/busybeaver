# eden

The **Garden of Eden** (terrestrial Paradise) is the biblical paradise where Adam and Eve lived before the Fall. Holbach groups the Eden narrative — a woman formed from a man's rib, a talking serpent — with the fables of Prometheus and Pandora's box, arguing they are equally unworthy of credence. (003-021)

## Eden as Mythological Fiction

Holbach places the Eden story squarely within the category of ancient mythology rather than revealed truth. He categorizes it alongside "fabulous tales similar to that of a woman formed of a man's rib; of the pretended terrestrial Paradise; of a serpent which spoke, which reasoned, and which was more cunning than man" (002-473). For Holbach, these narratives "appear to be fables, as much as those invented about the industry of Prometheus, the box of Pandora, the war of the Giants against the Gods, and similar others which the poets have invented to amuse the men of their time" (002-473). The Christian claim that Eden is historical truth while pagan myths are fiction reflects prejudice rather than rational discrimination.

## Comparative Mythology

Holbach's treatment of Eden exemplifies his broader method of comparative religious critique. By placing the Genesis creation narrative alongside Greek, Egyptian, and Near Eastern myths, he demonstrates that biblical stories share the same structural features as the pagan fables Christians dismiss. A garden with magical trees, a talking animal, and a primordial couple are not unique to the Hebrew Bible — they are common motifs in the mythologies of the ancient Near East. For Holbach, the most rational conclusion is not that one particular set of these stories is divinely revealed while the others are human invention, but rather that all of them are products of the human imagination in its infancy.

## The Theological Function of Eden

The Eden narrative serves an essential theological function: it establishes the doctrine of original sin, which in turn creates the need for redemption. Holbach would recognize this as a classic example of religion inventing a disease in order to sell the cure. The story of the Fall provides the conceptual foundation for the entire Christian economy of sin and salvation — and by undermining the historical credibility of Eden, Holbach undermines the theological superstructure built upon it.

## Selected Passage

Holbach argues that Christian miracles are no more credible than pagan ones — both come from the same source of human credulity:

> There is certainly a great similarity between the Pagan miracles and our own. As it would be great folly to give credence to these pretended miracles of Paganism, it is not any the less so to have faith in those of Christianity, because they all come from the same source of error. It was for this that the Manicheans and the Arians, who existed at the commencement of the Christian Era, derided these pretended miracles performed by the invocation of saints, and blamed those who invoked them after death and honored their relics.
>
> (003-021)
