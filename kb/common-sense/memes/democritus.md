# Democritus

## Biography

Democritus (c. 460 – c. 370 BCE) was a Greek philosopher born in Abdera, Thrace. He traveled widely — reportedly to [[egypt|Egypt]], Persia, and possibly [[india|India]] — before returning to Abdera, where he lived to an advanced age, earning the nickname "the Laughing Philosopher" for his cheerful disposition. He was a student of [[leucippus|Leucippus]], with whom he developed the atomic theory of matter: the doctrine that [[nature|the universe]] consists of an infinite number of indivisible particles (atoms) moving through empty space (the void), and that all observable phenomena — from the formation of [[universe|world]]s to the sensations of the human mind — can be explained by the collisions and combi[[nation|nations]] of these atoms, without recourse to purpose, design, or [[divinity|divine]] intervention.

Democritus was a prolific writer: later catalogues list some seventy works on subjects ranging from physics and cosmology to [[morality|ethics]], mathematics, and music. None of these works survive intact; what is known of his thought comes from fragments quoted by later authors — especially Aristotle, who discussed him extensively, and [[epicurus|Epicurus]], who adopted and modified his atomic theory. Despite his importance in the history of philosophy, Democritus was overshadowed in antiquity by [[plato|Plato]] and Aristotle, and his [[matter|materialism]] was marginalized by the dominance of [[plato|Plato]]nic and [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian [[model|ideal]]ism in the medieval and early modern periods.

## Role in Religious History

Democritus represents, for the Enlightenment, the earliest flowering of a consistently naturalistic [[universe|world]]view — a philosophy that explains [[earth|the world]] without gods, purposes, or supernatural agents. His atomism removed the gods from the [[government|governance]] of [[universe|nature]] and made them, at most, distant spectators — or, as some of his followers held, eliminated them entirely. This made him a heroic figure for the radical wing of the Enlightenment, [[eve]]n as his fragmentary textual remains made him more of a symbol than a direct source.

The revival of atomism in the seventeenth century — through [[pierre-gassendi|Pierre Gassendi]], Robert Boyle, and [[isaac-newton|Isaac Newton]] — prepared the ground for the eighteenth-century materialists. By the time Holbach wrote, Democritus could be invoked as the founding father of a tradition that had survived centuries of ecclesiastical suppression and was now, in the [[enlightenment|Age of Reason]], finally coming into its own.

## Connection to "Common Sense"

Holbach mentions Democritus in a [[guilt|sin]]gle but significant passage — a roll call of the heroes of freethought who dared to challenge the religious consensus of their age:

> "Some ancient and modern philosophers have had the courage to accept experience and reason as their guides, and to shake off the chains of superstition. Lucippe, Democritus, Epicurus, Straton, and some other Greeks, dared to tear away the thick veil of prejudice, and to deliver philosophy from theological fetters."
>
> (204-001)

Holbach contrasts these ancient materialists with the [[model|ideal]]ist tradition of [[plato|Plato]], [[socrates|Socrates]], and [[zeno-of-citium|Zeno]], whose "fabulous conjectures" appealed to "lovers of [[imagination|fancy]]" and crowded out the simpler, more sensible systems of the atomists. For Holbach, the history of philosophy is a long struggle between [[reason|reason]] ([[matter|materialism]], empiricism, [[materialism|naturalism]]) and [[superstition|superstition]] ([[model|ideal]]ism, [[religion|theology]], the appeal to immaterial causes). Democritus is a founding hero of the rationalist camp — a philosopher who understood, two millennia before the Enlightenment, that [[earth|the world]] can be explained without gods.

## Key Ideas Attributed / Critiqued

**1. Atomism as anti-[[theology|theological method]].** Democritus's atomic theory was not merely a physical hypothesis; it was a methodological declaration of independence from theological expla[[nations|nation]]. By positing atoms and void as the ultimate constituents of reality, Democritus eliminated the need for [[divinity|divine]] creation, [[providence|divine providence]], and [[divinity|divine]] intervention. The [[universe|world]] is not a designed artifact; it is the necessary result of matter in motion.

**2. The eclipse of [[matter|materialism]].** Holbach's lament that Democritus's system — "too simple, too sensible, and too stripped of [[miracle|wonder]]s for the lovers of [[imagination|fancy]]" — was displaced by [[plato|Plato]]nic and [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian [[model|ideal]]ism is a compressed history of Western philosophy. The triumph of [[model|ideal]]ism was not, for Holbach, a victory of truth over error; it was a victory of the taste for the marvelous over the demand for [[proof|evidence]].

**3. [[courage|Courage]] and [[cruelty|persecution]].** Holbach's language of "courage" and "daring" is deliberate. Democritus was not a martyr — he lived to old age and was respected in his native Abdera — but his ideas were dangerous in a [[worship|cult]]ure where the gods were woven into the fabric of civic life. To "tear away the thick veil of [[opinion|prejudice]]" was to risk the charge of impiety, a charge that would later destroy [[socrates|Socrates]]. The history of philosophy, in Holbach's telling, is a history of [[courage|intellectual courage]] against the forces of institutionalized [[belief|credulity]].

## Legacy

Democritus's influence on the subsequent history of philosophy is diffi[[worship|cult]] to overstate. His atomic theory, transmitted through [[epicurus|Epicurus]] and Lucretius's poem *De Rerum Natura*, provided the metaphysical framework for the scientific revolution of the seventeenth century. The early modern "corpuscularian" philosophy — held in various forms by Galileo, Gassendi, Boyle, and [[isaac-newton|Newton]] — was a revival and [[mode|modification]] of Democritean atomism, adapted to the requirements of the new experimental [[knowledge|science]].

For the radical Enlightenment — Holbach, Diderot, La Mettrie, and their circle — Democritus was more than a scientific precursor. He was [[evidence|proof]] that a thoroughgoing [[matter|materialism]] was not a modern aberration but the recovery of an ancient and honorable tradition. The philosophical novel had begun with atoms and void; it had been interrupted by two millennia of theological obfuscation; it was now, in the eighteenth century, resuming its proper course.
