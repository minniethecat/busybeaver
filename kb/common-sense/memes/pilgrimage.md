# pilgrimage

A **pilgrimage** is a journey to a sacred place undertaken as an act of religious devotion. Holbach describes a hermit who, filled with gratitude to Allah, vowed to undertake his seventh pilgrimage to Mecca, undeterred by an ongoing war between the Persians and Turks. The narrative serves as a vehicle for Holbach's broader critique of religious self-deception. (002-173)

## The Seventh Pilgrimage

Holbach's dervis had already completed six pilgrimages to Mecca and now vows a seventh, driven by gratitude for what he perceives as divine favor (002-173). The repetition is significant: religious devotion is not satisfied by a single act but demands endless repetition, each journey reinforcing the devotee's conviction of divine care. The dervis's willingness to travel through a war zone — "the war, which then existed between the Persians and the Turks, could not make him defer the execution of his pious enterprise" — illustrates how religious commitment overrides practical judgment.

## The Protected Pilgrim

As the dervis journeys, he experiences what appears to be miraculous protection. "Full of confidence in God, he began his journey; under the inviolable safeguard of a respected garb, he passed through without obstacle the enemies' detachments; far from being molested, he receives at every step marks of veneration from the soldiers of both sides" (002-173). The dervis interprets this as evidence of divine favor — but Holbach's narrative framing suggests a naturalistic explanation: soldiers on both sides of a religious war respect the garb of a holy man. There is no miracle here, only human psychology.

## Pilgrimage and the Cost of Devotion

The economic dimension of pilgrimage does not escape Holbach. The dervis's neighbors "eagerly brought to him every day provisions and presents" in order "to have an interest in his prayers" (002-173). The holy man lives on the charity of others, contributing nothing material to their welfare while offering only the intangible currency of prayer. The pilgrimage system, in Holbach's implicit critique, is an economy of extraction — the devout laborer supports the contemplative traveler, receiving in return only promises of supernatural intercession.


## Selected Passage

Holbach contrasts religion's gloomy view of life as a pilgrimage with a rational philosophy of enjoyment:

> Religion, which is so sadly occupied with its gloomy reveries, represents man to us as but a pilgrim upon earth; it concludes that in order to travel with more safety, he should travel alone; renounce the pleasures which he meets and deprive himself of the amusements which could console him for the fatigues and the weariness of the road.
>
> (002-380)
