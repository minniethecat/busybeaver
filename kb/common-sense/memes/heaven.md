# Heaven

## Definition

In Holbach's analysis, [[hope|heaven]] is the theological promise of eternal reward in exchange for obedience in the present life. It is the positive counterpart to [[Hell]] — together they form the carrot and stick of religious motivation. Holbach argues that the promise of [[hope|heaven]] serves three functions: it comforts the miserable by offering compensation in another [[universe|world]]; it secures obedience to [[authority|religious authority]] by ma[[monarch|king]] present [[evil|suffering]] tolerable; and it devalues [[earth|earthly existence]] by treating it as a mere preparation for the life to come.

"Tired of an incomprehensible [[religion|theology]], of ridiculous fables, of impenetrable [[mysteries]], of puerile ceremonies," Holbach urges his readers to "bring them back to the earth." The promise of [[hope|heaven]], in his view, is not a [[hope|consolation]] but a [[imposture|deception]] — it persuades people to accept injustices they should resist and to neglect the only life they will [[eve]]r actually experience.

## Historical Context

The concept of [[hope|heaven]] has deep roots in the [[abraham|Abraham]]ic traditions. Early Hebrew religion had only a shadowy [[death|afterlife]] (Sheol); the developed doctrine of resurrection and [[hope|heaven]]ly reward emerged during the Second [[temple|Temple]] period and was consolidated in early [[christianity|Christianity]]. Medieval Catholicism elaborated a detailed topography of the [[death|afterlife]]: [[hope|heaven]], [[punishment|hell]], purgatory, and limbo. [[reformation|The Reformation]] simplified this by eliminating purgatory but retained [[hope|heaven]] and [[punishment|hell]] as essential doctrines.

By Holbach's 18th century, [[hope|heaven]] was still a central article of [[religion|faith]], but its credibility was eroding. The new astronomy had dismantled the literal sky above; the new philosophy had questioned how an immaterial soul could experience physical pleasure. Holbach's contribution was to shift the critique from metaphysics to [[morality|ethics]]: the problem with [[hope|heaven]] is not merely that it is implausible but that it is harmful.

## Core Arguments

**1. Heaven devalues the only life we have.** By promising an infinitely better existence after death, the doctrine of [[hope|heaven]] makes people indifferent to improving their condition on earth. Why pursue [[reform|social reform]], why resist [[persecution|oppression]], if "our true home is in [[hope|heaven]]"? Holbach sees this as a mechanism of political pacification.

**2. Heaven is a psychological bribe that corrupts morality.** The believer who acts well to gain [[hope|heaven]] is not genuinely virtuous but merely prudent — a celestial mercenary. True [[morality|virtue]], for Holbach, requires no supernatural reward; it is its own reward, consisting in the satis[[sect|faction]] of contributing to human welfare. "I call [[hope|heaven]] to witness that I also thoroughly despised those who thought differently from me" — the religious appeal to [[hope|heaven]] is a cover for [[persecution|intolerance]].

**3. Heaven is unequally distributed, ma[[monarch|king]] [[justice|divine justice]] arbitrary.** The conditions for entering [[hope|heaven]] — right belief, right baptism, right [[sacrament|sacraments]] — are distributed by [[chance|accident]]s of birth, not by [[merit|moral desert]]. A virtuous pagan born before [[jesus-christ|Christ]] is damned by the same [[god|God]] who saves a repentant murderer baptized on his deathbed. This is not justice but theological caprice.

**4. The promise of [[hope|heaven]] sustains the institutions that Holbach opposes.** [[priest|Priest]]s claim to hold the keys to [[hope|heaven]]; this claimed power is the source of their authority, wealth, and immunity from criticism. The promise of [[hope|heaven]] is thus not merely a theological error but a tool of institutional power.

## Reception and Critique

Holbach's critique of [[hope|heaven]] was seen as dangerous not because it was philosophically novel — [[epicurus|Epicurus]] had made similar arguments in antiquity — but because it reached a popular audience. The fear was that ordinary people, once convinced that no [[hope|heaven]] awaited them, would descend into moral chaos. This was the [[model|standard]] "[[utility|social utility]]" argument: [[eve]]n if [[hope|heaven]] is a fiction, it is a useful fiction that keeps the masses orderly.

Holbach's reply is that this argument proves too much: any lie could be justified by its supposed [[utility]], and in any case the [[proof|evidence]] suggests that belief in [[hope|heaven]] does not make people better. [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian [[europe|Europe]] was no more moral than pagan antiquity; the threat of [[punishment|hell]] and the promise of [[hope|heaven]] had not prevented wars, [[cruelty|persecution]]s, or exploitations.

## Relation to Other Concepts

Heaven is the promised reward of [[Religion]], the positive motivation paired with the negative motivation of [[Hell]]. It is opposed to [[Earth]], which Holbach insists is our only real home and the proper object of our concern. The [[passion|desire]] for [[hope|heaven]] is exploited by [[Priestcraft]], which claims to mediate access to it. Heaven is connected to [[Salvation]] — the process by which one qualifies for [[hope|heaven]]ly entry — and its rejection is central to [[Atheism]], which finds meaning and morality entirely within [[earth|earthly existence]]. The [[hope|heaven]]ly reward distorts [[Virtue]] by ma[[monarch|king]] it instrumental rather than intrinsic.

> "Tired of an incomprehensible theology, of ridiculous fables, of impenetrable mysteries, of puerile ceremonies, let them at last bring them back to the earth." (Preface)

> (002-435)

On religion as a distraction from earthly concerns:

> "Religion is the art of occupying men with things which they will never understand, in order to prevent them from occupying themselves with things which they might understand too well." (ch. CLVI)
>
> (002-295)

On the true [[universe|nature]] of posthumous reward:

> "The hope of another life is a consolation that religion presents to the unfortunate, to help them to support their evils with more patience; but this hope is only a chimera, of which nothing can be affirmed without falling into contradictions." (ch. II)
>
> (002-282)

On the [[atheism|atheist]]'s alternative to [[hope|heaven]]ly reward:

> "The atheist, if he reasons soundly, will be more virtuous than the superstitious man, because he expects nothing after this life. He occupies himself only with his present happiness, and his happiness can only consist in conducting himself well." (ch. CLXXI)
