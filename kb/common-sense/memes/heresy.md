# Heresy

## Definition

Heresy, in Holbach's analysis, is not a meaningful theological category but a political weapon. "Heresy" names whatever beliefs the dominant [[sect|religious faction]] has the power to suppress. The heretic is not someone who has departed from [[truth]] but someone who has departed from the opinions of those who currently hold power. The horror with which orthodoxy regards heresy is manufactured by [[priest]]s who understand that the existence of [[guilt|sin]]cere dissenters undermines their claim to possess exclusive access to [[divinity|divine]] [[truth]].

Holbach's treatment of heresy is characteristically ironic. When [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ians denounce heretics, they forget that [[christianity|Christianity]] itself began as a Jewish heresy and that [[eve]]ry Protestant [[sect|denomination]] was once the heresy of a previous orthodoxy. "Heresy" is a moving target that tracks the distribution of coercive power, not the distribution of [[reason]] or [[evidence]].

## Historical Context

The concept of heresy was formalized in the early [[church|Church]] as it consolidated power against doctrinal rivals — Gnostics, Arians, Donatists, Pelagians. The [[inquisition|Inquisition]] was created specifically to identify and eliminate heresy. The [[reformation|Protestant Reformation]] transformed the meaning of heresy: what had been orthodoxy in [[rome|Rome]] became heresy in Geneva, and vice versa, [[demon|demons]]trating that the category was relative to institutional power.

Holbach wrote in a [[france|France]] where the memory of the Wars of [[religion|Religion]] was still vivid. The St. Bartholomew's Day massacre (1572) had shown that "heresy" was a charge that could justify mass murder. The [[edict-of-nantes|Revocation of the Edict of Nantes]] (1685) had shown that toleration of "heretics" was a policy to be revoked when politically convenient. In this context, Holbach's argument that "heresy" is a meaningless concept was not abstract philosophy but a direct challenge to the ideological foundation of [[persecution]].

## Core Arguments

**1. Heresy is defined by power, not truth.** Who is a heretic depends entirely on who controls the institutions that define orthodoxy. "They are contending, accusing each other of [[existence|being]] infidels and heretics, rending each other with the bitterest fury" (ch. CLXXII). When all [[sect|sects]] call each other heretical, the term loses all cognitive content and becomes a mere expression of hostility.

**2. The [[cruelty|persecution]] of heresy contradicts [[charity|Christian charity]].** "Ask if you must love your neighbor if he is impious, heretical, and incredulous, that is to say, if he does not think as they do? ... Immediately their charity disappears, and the dominating [[priest|clergy]] will tell you that the [[monarch|prince]] carries the sword but to sustain the interests of the Most High" (ch. CLIX). The commandment to love one's neighbor has an unstated exception: love him only if he agrees with you.

**3. The cry of "heresy" is a tool of priestly mobilization.** "[[person-index|People]], to arms! Your [[god|God]]'s cause is at stake! [[heaven|Heaven]] is outraged! [[faith|Faith]] is in danger! Down upon infidelity, blasphemy, and heresy!" (ch. CLXVII). These words "the people never understand," but they reliably produce "civil wars" and "imprisoning men." The concept of heresy is a mechanism for converting theological disagreement into political violence.

**4. [[toleration|Toleration]] is impossible where heresy is a crime.** The only alternative to perpetual religious war is to "permit each one to follow peacefully the worship and the opinions which suit him" (ch. CLVII). As long as heresy is conceived as a crime against [[god]], [[toleration]] will be seen as complicity in [[injustice|evil]].

## Relation to Other Concepts

Heresy is the negative counterpart of [[orthodoxy]]: the one cannot exist without the other. It is policed by the [[inquisition]] and punished by [[torture]] and [[persecution]]. The psychological foundation of the horror of heresy is [[fanaticism]] — the believer's conviction that dissent from his own views is an [[crime|offense]] against the [[divinity|divine]]. [[Toleration]] is the political principle that makes the concept of heresy irrelevant by removing its legal consequences. [[Doubt]], for Holbach, is the intellectual [[morality|virtue]] that inoculates against the charge of heresy: the skeptic cannot be a heretic because he claims no certainty worth killing for.

## In the Text

On heresy as a tool of mobilization:

> "People, to arms! Your God's cause is at stake! Heaven is outraged! Faith is in danger! Down upon infidelity, blasphemy, and heresy! By the magical power of these valiant words, which the people never understand, the priests in all ages were the leaders in the revolts of nations, in dethroning kings, in kindling civil wars, and in imprisoning men." (ch. CLXVII)

On the conflict between heresy-hunting and charity:

> "Ask if you must love your neighbor if he is impious, heretical, and incredulous, that is to say, if he does not think as they do? Ask them if you must tolerate opinions contrary to those which they profess? ... Immediately their charity disappears, and the dominating clergy will tell you that the prince carries the sword but to sustain the interests of the Most High." (ch. CLIX)

On the mutual accusations of heresy among [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian [[sect|sects]]:

> "They are contending, accusing each other of being infidels and heretics, rending each other with the bitterest fury." (ch. CLXXII)

## Reception and Critique

Holbach's reduction of heresy to a power relation was provocative and, in some respects, prescient — it anticipates Foucault's analysis of the knowledge/power nexus and the sociological insight that deviance is defined by those who control institutions of social control. Critics have argued, however, that Holbach's account neglects the [[guilt|sin]]cere theological content of heresy disputes. The Arians and Athanasians were not merely fighting over power; they genuinely disagreed about the [[universe|nature]] of [[jesus-christ|Christ]]. A complete account of heresy must explain why people are willing to die for doctrinal distinctions that, on Holbach's reading, are merely masks for political interest. The most balanced view — developed in the sociology of knowledge tradition — treats heresy as a phenomenon in which theological conviction and institutional power are inseparably intertwined.
