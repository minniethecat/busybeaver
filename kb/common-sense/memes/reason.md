# Reason

## Definition

Reason, in Holbach's "Common Sense," is the human capacity for logical inference, empirical observation, and critical judgment. It is presented not merely as one cognitive tool among many, but as the *only* legitimate means by which human [[existence|being]]s can arrive at truth and govern their conduct. "Let man's reason be [[worship|cult]]ivated," Holbach urges; "let justice govern him; and there will be no need of opposing to his passions the powerless barrier of the fear of [[god|God]]s" (ch. 2). Reason is thus positioned as both an epistemological method and a moral foundation — the alternative to the entire edifice of [[authority|religious authority]].

## Historical Context

The elevation of reason to the supreme arbiter of truth was the defining intellectual project of the [[europe|Europe]]an Enlightenment. [[rene-descartes|Descartes]] had established reason as the starting point of philosophy ("I think, therefore I am"); [[john-locke|Locke]] had grounded knowledge in [[experience|sensory experience]] and rational reflection; [[voltaire|Voltaire]] and the *philosophes* had turned reason into a weapon against ecclesiastical [[doctrine|dogma]]tism. Holbach's contribution is distinctive in its radicalism: where many Enlightenment thinkers sought to reconcile reason with a rationalized, deistic religion, Holbach argued that reason, consistently applied, leads inexorably to a[[divinity|theism]]. The chapter title "Reason Leads Men to Irreligion and to [[atheism|Atheism]]" (ch. 182) states this thesis with characteristic bluntness.

## Core Arguments

**1. Reason is incompatible with [[religion|faith]].** [[faith|Faith]] demands belief without [[proof|evidence]]; reason demands [[proof|evidence]] before belief. These are not complementary paths to truth but mutually exclusive methods. "[[faith|Faith]] is irreconcilable with reason" (ch. 135) is one of Holbach's most consistent refrains.

**2. The suppression of reason enables [[despotism|tyranny]].** Holbach identifies a causal chain: [[clergy|priests]] suppress reason → people become intellectually dependent → tyrants exploit this dependency → society remains corrupt and unhappy. "[[ignorance|Ignorance]] and servitude have a tendency to make men wicked and unhappy. [[science|Science]], reason, liberty, alone can reform them and render them more happy" (ch. 2).

**3. Reason leads to a[[divinity|theism]].** The chapter "Reason Leads Men to Irreligion and to [[atheism|Atheism]]" (ch. 182) argues that any honest rational inquiry into theological claims must conclude that they are unfounded. The idea of [[god|God]] is "an idea without model" — a concept with no empirical referent, sustained only by inherited habit and institutional pressure.

**4. Human [[existence|being]]s are naturally reasonable but have been made irrational by religion.** Holbach locates the problem not in human [[universe|nature]] but in institutions: early humans, frightened by [[phenomena|natural phenomena]], invented gods; [[clergy|priests]] systematized these inventions; and generations of "[[belief|credulity]], preserved by habit, which never reasons" (ch. 2) perpetuated the error. The cure is education: "let the mind be filled early with true ideas."

## Reception and Critique

Holbach's rationalism was extreme [[eve]]n by Enlightenment [[model|standard]]s. [[voltaire|Voltaire]], himself a fierce critic of organized religion, publicly distanced himself from Holbach's [[atheism|atheist]]ic conclusions, preferring [[theism|deism]]. Rousseau argued that reason alone could not provide moral motivation — a critique that anticipated later Romantic and post-Enlightenment reactions. Kant's distinction between "pure reason" (limited to phenomena) and "practical reason" (the domain of morality and [[religion|faith]]) can be read as an attempt to rescue religion from Holbach's critique while preserving reason's authority in [[knowledge|science]].

## Relation to Other Concepts

Reason is the epistemological foundation upon which Holbach's entire critical project rests. It is the faculty that exposes [[God]] as an incoherent concept, that reveals [[Religion]] as a system of imposture, and that grounds [[Morality]] in human [[universe|nature]] rather than [[divinity|divine]] command. It is opposed to [[Faith]], [[Superstition]], and [[Ignorance]], and allied with [[Science]], [[Truth]], and [[Liberty]]. Holbach's [[Materialism]] is the metaphysical expression of reason: a universe governed by natural laws, requiring no supernatural expla[[nations|nation]].

## In the Text

The Apologue (ch. 1) presents the suppression of reason in allegorical form. The invisible monarch's subjects are forbidden to examine his decrees rationally:

> "He is called supremely good, nevertheless not a person but complains of his decrees. He is supposed to be infinitely wise, and in his administration everything seems contrary to reason and good sense." (ch. 1)

Holbach's most concentrated statement of rationalist conviction appears in the opening argument:

> "Let the mind be filled early with true ideas; let man's reason be cultivated; let justice govern him; and there will be no need of opposing to his passions the powerless barrier of the fear of Gods. Men will be good when they are well taught, well governed, chastised or censured for the evil, and justly rewarded for the good." (ch. 2)

On the historical suppression of reason by priestly authority:

> "The human mind, perplexed by these theological opinions, misunderstood itself, doubted its own powers, mistrusted experience, feared truth, disdained its reason, and left it to blindly follow authority. Man was a pure machine in the hands of his tyrants and his priests, who alone had the right to regulate his movements." (ch. 2)
