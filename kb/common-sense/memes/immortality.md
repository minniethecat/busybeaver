# Immortality

## Definition

In Holbach's materialist critique, the im[[death|mortality]] of the soul is not a [[revelation|revealed truth]] but a human invention born of the fear of death and the [[passion|desire]] to survive beyond the grave. The doctrine rests on no empirical foundation: "If I ask what ground we have for supposing that the soul is immortal: they reply, it is because man by his [[universe|nature]] [[passion|desire]]s to be immortal, or to live always" (ch. 101). Holbach treats this as the reversal of sound reasoning — [[passion|desire]] is not [[proof|evidence]], and the intensity of a wish does not make its object real.

The concept of im[[death|mortality]] is inseparable from the prior concept of an immaterial soul. Holbach attacks both together: if the soul is not a distinct substance but merely the organized action of a living body, then the death of the body is the end of consciousness. Immortality is thus not merely unproven but conceptually incoherent within a materialist framework. The doctrine persists, Holbach argues, because it serves the interests of a priestly class that profits from humanity's [[fear|terror]] of [[death|annihilation]].

## Historical Context

The im[[death|mortality]] of the soul was a near-universal doctrine in eighteenth-century [[europe|Europe]], affirmed by Catholic and Protestant alike. [[rene-descartes|Descartes]] had attempted to provide a philosophical foundation for the soul's separability from the body, arguing that the thin[[monarch|king]] substance (res cogitans) could exist independently of extended substance. By the mid-eighteenth century, however, materialist critics including La Mettrie and Diderot had begun to challenge this dualism on empirical and conceptual grounds.

Holbach's treatment of im[[death|mortality]] in *Common Sense* (1772) belongs to this materialist counter-tradition but is distinctive in its directness: he does not content himself with philosophical objections but argues that the doctrine is positively harmful. The belief in an [[death|afterlife]], he contends, devalues the present life and makes humans indifferent to [[happiness|earthly happiness]]. The historical backdrop includes the long debate over [[john-locke|Locke]]'s suggestion that [[god|God]] might superadd the power of thought to matter — a suggestion that, if accepted, would undermine the argument that an immaterial soul is necessary for thought and therefore for im[[death|mortality]].

## Core Arguments

1. **The [[passion|desire]] for im[[death|mortality]] is not [[proof|evidence]] of it.** Holbach observes that "man by his [[universe|nature]] [[passion|desire]]s to be immortal" (ch. 101), but he insists that [[desire|wishing]] does not make true. The universality of the [[passion|desire]] proves only that humans fear death, not that they survive it.

2. **The soul is not a simple, incorruptible substance.** The [[doctrine|dogma]] of im[[death|mortality]] "assumes that the soul is a simple substance, indivisible, and consequently indestructible" (ch. 103). Holbach counters that what we call soul is nothing but the organized functioning of a material body; when the organization dissolves, so does the function.

3. **The doctrine is not consoling but terrifying.** Far from comforting the unfortunate, the idea of im[[death|mortality]] "is made to embitter the present life, and to fill the mind with [[fear|terror]]s for the future" (ch. 108). The threat of [[hell|eternal punishment]] turns im[[death|mortality]] from a promise into a menace.

4. **Immortality serves priestly power.** [[priest|Priest]]s have "principally founded" their authority "upon the opinion of possessing exclusively an [[soul|immortal soul]]" (ch. 100). The doctrine creates a monopoly on the [[death|afterlife]], which [[clergy|the clergy]] exploit for [[universe|world]]ly advantage.

5. **[[materialism|Materialism]] does not degrade humanity.** Holbach preempts the objection that denying im[[death|mortality]] reduces humans to brutes by arguing that [[death|mortality]] gives dignity to the present life: finite existence is more precious, not less, when it is understood to be our only one.

## Reception and Critique

Holbach's rejection of im[[death|mortality]] was among the most incendiary elements of his philosophy. Contemporary critics, including [[voltaire|Voltaire]] — himself no friend of orthodox [[christianity|Christianity]] — recoiled from the claim that consciousness is entirely extinguished at death. [[voltaire|Voltaire]] argued that belief in a future state was a necessary support for morality, without which the [[society|social order]] would collapse. Holbach's reply, implicit throughout his work, is that morality grounded in natural [[interest|self-interest]] and social reciprocity is more reliable than morality dependent on supernatural sanctions.

Kant, in the *Critique of Practical [[reason|Reason]]* (1788), would later treat the im[[death|mortality]] of the soul as a postulate of pure practical reason — not theoretically provable but necessarily presupposed by the moral law. This represented a different strategy for retaining im[[death|mortality]] without the metaphysical baggage Holbach had attacked. The nineteenth-century materialist tradition (Büchner, Vogt, Moleschott) revived Holbach's arguments with updated scientific bac[[monarch|king]] from physiology and evolutionary biology.

## Relation to Other Concepts

Immortality is the theological promise that underw[[ritual|rites]] the threat of [[Hell]]: without an [[death|afterlife]], there is no place for [[hell|eternal punishment]]. It depends logically on the prior concept of [[Soul]], which Holbach dismantles in his critique of [[soul|spirit]]ual substance. The [[passion|desire]] for im[[death|mortality]] is closely linked to [[Fear]], which Holbach identifies as the psychological root of [[belief|religious belief]]. Immortality relates to [[Death]] as its theological denial — the refusal to accept that organisms, once dead, do not return. The concept connects to [[Nature]] because Holbach's case against im[[death|mortality]] rests on the materialist premise that all phenomena, including consciousness, arise from natural causes and cease when those causes cease.
