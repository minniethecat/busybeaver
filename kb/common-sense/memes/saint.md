## Definition

In Catholic [[religion|theology]], a saint is a person recognized by [[church|the Church]] as having lived a life of exceptional holiness and as now residing in [[hope|heaven]], capable of interceding with [[god|God]] on behalf of the [[religion|faith]]ful. The [[worship|cult]] of saints — including veneration, prayer, pilgrimage, and the dedication of churches and feast days — constitutes a major component of Catholic religious practice. Theologians distinguish between *latria* (worship due to [[god|God]] alone) and *dulia* (veneration due to saints), but Holbach dismisses this distinction as a semantic evasion.

For Holbach, saints are simply additional gods added to the pantheon under a different name. The multiplication of saints fragments worship, creates local and [[nations|nation]]al religious loyalties that compete with universal reason, and provides [[clergy|the clergy]] with a vast inventory of miracle stories, relics, and pilgrimage sites that generate revenue and social control.

## Historical Context

The [[worship|cult]] of saints was a major target of Protestant criticism during the [[reform|Reform]]ation, and Holbach's critique inherits much from this tradition. Luther, Calvin, and other [[reform|Reform]]ers had rejected the intercessory role of saints, the veneration of relics, and the practice of pilgrimage as unbiblical corruptions. By the eighteenth century, [[eve]]n within Catholicism, reforming movements sought to curtail the more extravagant expressions of saint-veneration.

Holbach goes further than the Protestant [[reform|Reform]]ers. Where they argued that saint-worship was unbiblical, Holbach argues that it is structurally identical to pagan [[paganism|polytheism]]. The [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian saint is the pagan demigod renamed; the local patron saint is the local tutelary [[god|deity]] in [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian dress; and the multiplication of saints over the centuries [[demon|demons]]trates the same human tendency to invent invisible protectors that produced the gods of antiquity.

## Core Arguments

1. **Saints are demigods under a [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian label.** The distinction between "veneration" of saints and "worship" of [[god|God]] is a verbal trick. In practice, believers pray to saints, make offerings to saints, and attribute supernatural powers to saints — the same behaviors directed at pagan [[gods|deities]].

2. **The multiplication of saints fragments [[authority|religious authority]].** Each [[nations|nation]], city, profession, and disease has its own patron saint. This fragmentation encourages particularism and superstition while undermining any claim to a [[guilt|sin]]gle, universal revelation.

3. **The [[worship|cult]] of saints is a revenue source for [[clergy|the clergy]].** Relics, pilgrimage sites, and saint-specific indulgences generate enormous wealth for [[church|the Church]]. The economic function of the saint-system is as important as its theological function.

4. **Canonization is a political act, not a recognition of holiness.** Who becomes a saint, and when, reflects the political interests of [[church|the Church]] hierarchy, not any objective assessment of [[morality|virtue]].

## In the Text

Holbach treats saints as continuous with pagan [[gods|divinities]]:

> "The Pagans had their household gods, their Lares and Penates; the Christians have their patron saints. The Pagans deified heroes; the Christians canonize them." (ch. CIV)

He connects saint-worship to the political function of religion:

> "Saint Thomas, having doubted, required the evidence of his senses; and Jesus Christ, to convince him, condescended to let him touch his wounds. But where is the saint who has had the same complaisance for those who have doubted after him?" (ch. CXLVII)
