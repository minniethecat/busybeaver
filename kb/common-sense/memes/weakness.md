# Weakness

## Definition

Holbach identifies human weakness as the psychological soil in which religion grows. "Men are imaginary patients, whom interested charlatans take care to encourage in their weakness, in order to have a market for their remedies" (ch. XI). [[religion|Religion]] does not arise from strength — from rational inquiry or empirical discovery — but from the universal human experience of vulnerability, fear, and [[doubt|uncertainty]]. [[priest|Priest]]s and theologians are not discoverers of truth but exploiters of weakness.

The analysis is deliberately psychological rather than theological. Holbach does not refute religious doctrines one by one; he explains why human [[existence|being]]s are motivated to beli[[eve]] them despite their evident [[absurdity|absurd]]ity. Weakness is the chain that binds the believer to the charlatan. "He who from his childhood has had a habit of trembling [[eve]]ry time he heard certain words, needs these words, and needs to tremble" (ch. XI). The fear becomes a psychological need; the remedy becomes an addiction.

This account of weakness has political dimensions. Rulers and [[clergy|priests]] conspire to keep people weak because weakness makes them governable. "Princes and their ministers make a [[secrecy|mystery]] of the art of governing; they are told that man, by the weakness of his [[universe|nature]], is not capable of governing himself" (ch. CXIII). [[religion|Religion]] is not merely a [[hope|consolation]] for weakness — it is an instrument for perpetuating it.

## Historical Context

The analysis of religion as exploitation of human weakness has roots in Epicurean philosophy. Lucretius's *De Rerum Natura* argued that fear of death and ignorance of natural causes generate [[belief|religious belief]], and that knowledge of [[universe|nature]] dispels both. The Roman poet's famous line — *tantum religio potuit suadere malorum* (so great are the [[injustice|evil]]s to which religion can persuade) — captures the Holbachian thesis that religion preys on human frailty.

Machiavelli and [[hobbes|Hobbes]] developed the political dimension: religion as an instrument of rule, effective precisely because it exploits universal psychological vulnerabilities. The French Enlightenment added the language of charlatanism — religious [[charlatan|impostor]]s as [[charlatan|quack]] doctors who invent diseases to sell cures. [[voltaire|Voltaire]]'s campaign against *l'infâme* was partly [[desire|drive]]n by this analogy.

Holbach's originality lies in ma[[monarch|king]] weakness the central explanatory concept. For him, religion is not primarily a set of false propositions but a set of exploitative social relations made possible by psychological vulnerabilities. The cure is not better arguments but stronger minds — education, courage, and the habit of rational inquiry.

## Core Arguments

**1. [[religion|Religion]] exploits pre-existing weakness.** Human [[existence|being]]s are naturally vulnerable to fear, [[doubt|uncertainty]], and the [[fear|terror]] of death. [[priest|Priest]]s "take care to encourage" these weaknesses rather than to dispel them, because weakness creates demand for their services. The patient who recovers no longer needs the physician.

**2. Weakness becomes self-reinforcing.** "The superstitious man [[desire|want]]s to be afraid; his imagi[[nations|nation]] demands it" (ch. XI). The habit of fear creates a psychological dependency — the believer needs objects of fear and feels lost without them. This is why rational argument often fails: the believer is not reasoning badly but satisfying a psychological need.

**3. [[power|Political power]] depends on manufactured weakness.** "Princes and their ministers make a [[secrecy|mystery]] of the art of governing" and tell the people they are "not capable of governing themselves" (ch. CXIII). [[religion|Religion]] provides the ideological cover for this infantilization — the people are weak, [[god|God]]'s representatives are strong, and the [[society|social order]] reflects this cosmic hierarchy.

**4. Weakness is not inherent but [[worship|cult]]ivated.** The human mind is not naturally feeble; it is made feeble by education that discourages inquiry and rewards submission. "[[faith|Faith]] relieves the weakness of the human mind, for whom application is a [[torture|torment]]" (ch. CXXXVIII). The solution is not to offer a better [[religion|faith]] but to strengthen the mind through knowledge.

## Relation to Other Concepts

Weakness is the psychological precondition for [[Superstition]] and [[Faith]]. It is the vulnerability that [[Fear]] exploits and that [[Ignorance]] deepens. [[Education]] is the remedy — the [[worship|cult]]ivation of mental strength that makes charlatans unnecessary. [[Tyranny]] is the political form of weakness-exploitation: [[monarch|ruler]]s who keep subjects weak to keep them compliant. [[Reason]] is what the weak mind cannot exercise, and what the strong mind refuses to surrender.
