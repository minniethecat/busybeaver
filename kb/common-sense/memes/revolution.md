# Revolution

## Definition

"Revolution" in the context of Holbach's "Common Sense" operates on two distinct but interloc[[monarch|king]] levels. On the first, it denotes the intellectual revolution Holbach sought to catalyze: the overthrow of [[authority|religious authority]] in the human mind, the replacement of [[religion|faith]] by reason, and the liberation of morality from theological tutelage. On the second, it gestures toward the political revolution that this intellectual transformation would make possible — and that would erupt seventeen years after the publication of "Common Sense" in the French Revolution of 1789.

## The Intellectual Revolution

Holbach's project is revolutionary in the most fundamental sense: it aims not to reform religion but to abolish it. "The most important of our opinions are those which we have adopted without exami[[nations|nation]]" (ch. II), he w[[ritual|rites]], and it is precisely these unexamined foundations that must be overthrown. The intellectual revolution Holbach en[[dreams|visions]] has three phases:

1. **Destruction**: The systematic exposure of religious claims as incoherent, contradictory, and historically contingent. The Apologue (ch. I) performs this function allegorically; the subsequent chapters perform it analytically.

2. **Reconstruction**: The articulation of a positive alternative — [[Materialism|materialist philosophy]], grounded in [[universe|nature]] and reason — that can supply the explanatory and moral functions religion previously monopolized.

3. **Institutionalization**: The diffusion of enlightened ideas through education, so that future generations are immunized against the "contagion" of superstition.

> "If men were but cultivated by reason, if they were taught to draw their morality from nature and not from imaginary beings, they would be happier and more virtuous." (paraphrase, ch. II)

## The Political Revolution

Holbach did not live to see the French Revolution (he died in January 1789), but his salon — the famous *coterie holbachique* — was one of the crucibles in which revolutionary ideas were forged. The revolutionaries of 1789 did not, for the most part, adopt Holbach's a[[divinity|theism]]; the Cult of the [[being|Supreme Being]] promoted by Robespierre was Deistic rather than [[atheism|atheist]]ic. But the structural relationship Holbach identified between religious and [[government|political authority]] — the throne and the altar as mutually supporting pillars of the old order — was precisely the target the revolutionaries attacked:

| Old Regime Institution | Revolutionary Response |
|-----------------------|----------------------|
| [[theology-index|Divine]] right of [[monarch|king]]s | Popular [[government|sovereignty]] |
| Ecclesiastical courts | Civil [[temperament|constitution]] of [[clergy|the clergy]] |
| Censorship of ideas | Declaration of the [[rights|Rights]] of Man ([[liberty|freedom]] of opinion) |
| Monastic landholdings | [[nation|Nation]]alization of [[church|Church]] property |
| Tithes and indulgences | Abolition of feudal privileges |

## Revolution as Risk

Holbach was aware that intellectual revolution carries risks. To destroy [[belief|religious belief]] without providing a rational alternative is to risk nihilism and despair. This is why the constructive dimension of his project — the positive philosophy of [[Nature]], [[Materialism]], and reason-based morality — is as important as its destructive dimension. He addresses the fear directly:

> "Is not the idea of total annihilation infinitely preferable to the idea of an eternal existence accompanied with suffering and gnashing of teeth?" (ch. CVIII)

The revolutionary wager is that humanity, freed from the [[fear|terror]] of [[punishment|divine punishment]], will not descend into moral chaos but will discover that genuine morality — grounded in human needs, social cooperation, and the observable consequences of actions — is more stable and more humane than the fear-based morality of religion.

## Legacy

The revolution Holbach helped prepare intellectually unfolded over the following two centuries. The separation of church and state, the secularization of education, the rise of scientific [[materialism|naturalism]], and the gradual replacement of theological by empirical [[mode|modes]] of inquiry are all, in their various ways, installments of the intellectual revolution "Common Sense" advocated.
