# Principle

## Definition

Holbach distinguishes sharply between two kinds of principles: those derived from experience and reason, and those imposed by theological authority. A genuine principle, in his view, is a judgment grounded in observation — "all judgment is the effect of experience" (ch. II) — and tested by its capacity to explain phenomena. Theological principles, by contrast, are assertions that demand [[belief|assent]] without [[proof|evidence]] and resist all attempts at verification.

The key diagnostic test for a principle is whether it explains anything or merely restates ignorance in elevated language. When theologians claim that the soul is "the principle of life," they say nothing more than "an unknown force is the invisible principle of imperceptible movements" (ch. CII). This is not expla[[nations|nation]] but tautology: it gives a name to ignorance and then treats the name as if it were knowledge. Genuine principles, Holbach insists, must connect to observable reality — they must arise from experience and return to it in the form of testable [[prophecy|prediction]]s.

The most important religious principle — that [[god|God]] exists and is the [[cause|first cause]] of all things — fails this test completely. "All religious principles are founded upon the idea of a [[god|God]], but it is impossible for men to have any real ideas of a [[existence|being]] who does not act upon any of their senses" (ch. II). A principle that cannot be grounded in any possible experience is not a principle at all; it is a fantasy elevated to the status of an axiom.

## Historical Context

Holbach's critique of theological principles belongs to the empiricist tradition that runs from [[john-locke|Locke]] through [[david-hume|Hume]]. [[john-locke|Locke]] had argued that all ideas derive from sensation and reflection, and that there are no innate principles — a direct challenge to the Cartesian doctrine of innate ideas and to the theological claim that knowledge of [[god|God]] is implanted in [[eve]]ry soul. [[david-hume|Hume]] had sharpened the empiricist criterion into a razor: does this idea correspond to any impression? If not, it is without meaning.

Holbach applied this razor to [[religion|theology]] with ruthless consistency. If the word "[[god|God]]" corresponds to no possible experience — if the idea of an infinite, immaterial, eternal [[existence|being]] cannot be traced to any sensory impression — then [[religion|theology]] is not a [[knowledge|science]] but a verbal construct, a castle built in the air. The "principles" of [[religion|theology]] are not foundations but ornaments: they decorate ignorance rather than dispelling it.

## Core Arguments

**1. All genuine principles derive from experience.** "All judgment is the effect of experience" (ch. II). There are no innate ideas, no a priori truths about [[supernatural|the supernatural]], and no principles that can be known independently of the senses. [[experience|Experience]] is the sole foundation of knowledge.

**2. Religious principles are drawn from nothing.** "Religious principles are drawn from nothing, and are not founded on any real feeling" (ch. II). They are imposed by authority and maintained by habit, not discovered by inquiry. The fact that they have been believed for centuries proves only that they have been taught for centuries, not that they are true.

**3. Theological principles are disguised tautologies.** When theologians say "[[god|God]] is the principle of all things" or "the soul is the principle of life," they are not explaining anything; they are giving a name to their ignorance and then treating the name as an expla[[nations|nation]]. "To say that this soul, which is unknown, is the principle of life, is saying nothing" (ch. CII).

**4. True principles must be revisable by [[proof|evidence]].** A principle that cannot be tested against experience — that must be accepted on authority or not at all — is not a principle but a [[doctrine|dogma]]. The mark of a genuine principle is that it invites verification and survives it; the mark of a theological principle is that it forbids verification and could not survive it.

## Relation to Other Concepts

Principle is the epistemological foundation of Holbach's system. [[Reason]] is the faculty by which principles are derived from experience and tested against it. [[Experience]] is the source of all valid principles. [[Dogma]] is a principle that demands [[belief|assent]] while forbidding verification. [[Truth]] is what a principle yields when it is grounded in experience and confirmed by reason. [[Prejudice]] is a principle absorbed from authority rather than derived from [[proof|evidence]]. [[Knowledge]] is the organized system of principles that have survived empirical testing.

## In the Text

On the empirical origin of all principles:

> "All judgment is the effect of experience; experience teaches us to feel what relations exist between the causes and the effects which we are examining." (ch. II)

On the emptiness of theological principles:

> "All religious principles are founded upon the idea of a God, but it is impossible for men to have any real ideas of a being who does not act upon any of their senses. It follows that religious principles are drawn from nothing, and are not founded on any real feeling." (ch. II)

On the tautology of soul-as-principle:

> "To say that this soul, which is unknown, is the principle of life, is saying nothing, unless that an unknown force is the invisible principle of imperceptible movements." (ch. CII)
