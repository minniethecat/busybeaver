## Definition

In Holbach's materialist [[morality|ethics]], wealth is material abundance — the possession of those goods (food, shelter, clothing, leisure, security) that constitute the conditions of human [[happiness|well-being]]. Unlike [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian moralists who treat wealth as a [[soul|spirit]]ual danger or a mark of [[divinity|divine]] disfavor, Holbach regards the pursuit of prosperity as natural, rational, and in principle virtuous. [[poverty|Poverty]] is not a blessing but a deprivation; wealth is not a [[guilt|sin]] but an achievement.

Holbach's treatment of wealth serves a dual polemical purpose. On the one hand, he critiques [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian morality for [[doctrine|teaching]] that poverty is a [[morality|virtue]] and that the rich are [[soul|spirit]]ually endangered — a doctrine that, he argues, serves the interests of a [[priest|clergy]] that lives comfortably off the labor of the poor. On the other hand, he critiques the actual behavior of the rich, who often use their wealth to support the very religious institutions that teach contempt for material goods. The result is a system in which [[eve]]ryone pretends to despise what [[eve]]ryone in fact pursues.

## Historical Context

The relationship between wealth, [[morality|virtue]], and religion was a central theme of Enlightenment social criticism. Mandeville's *Fable of the Bees* (1714) had scandalized readers by arguing that private vices produce public benefits — that luxury and consumption [[desire|drive]] economic prosperity. [[adam|Adam]] Smith's *Wealth of [[nations|Nations]]* (1776), published the same decade as *Common Sense*, would provide a systematic analysis of how wealth is created through labor, specialization, and trade.

Holbach's contribution to this debate is characteristically polemical. He is less interested in the economic mechanics of wealth creation than in the moral hypocrisy surrounding it. The [[priest|clergy]] preach poverty to the laity while accumulating vast ecclesiastical wealth; the nobility affect contempt for commerce while living on rents extracted from peasants; and the [[christianity|Christian religion]] as a whole teaches that earthly prosperity is worthless while demanding tithes and do[[nation|nations]].

## Core Arguments

1. **Wealth is a natural human good, not a [[soul|spirit]]ual danger.** Human [[existence|being]]s have material needs, and the satis[[sect|faction]] of those needs is the foundation of all other goods. A philosophy that treats prosperity as a temptation rather than an achievement is a philosophy that alienates human [[existence|being]]s from their own [[universe|nature]].

2. **The [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian praise of poverty is hypocritical.** The [[priest|clergy]] who extol the [[morality|virtue]]s of poverty are themselves among the wealthiest members of society. The monasteries, cathedrals, and ecclesiastical estates of [[europe|Europe]] testify to an institution that preaches renunciation while practicing accumulation on a grand scale.

3. **The pursuit of wealth can be socially beneficial.** When people work to improve their material condition, they produce goods and services that benefit others. The merchant, the farmer, and the artisan who seek prosperity are contributing to the general welfare in a way that the monk who takes a vow of poverty is not.

4. **True morality concerns the use of wealth, not its possession.** Holbach does not defend avarice or exploitation. Wealth, like all human goods, must be acquired by just means and used for rational purposes. The problem with ecclesiastical wealth is not merely its quantity but that it is extracted from the poor under false pretenses.

## In the Text

Holbach contrasts [[clergy|the clergy]]'s preaching with their practice:

> "Do they give them in exchange for their wealth and their offerings instruction, useful knowledge, and wise counsels?" (ch. CXLVII)

He describes the [[clergy|priesthood]]'s systematic extraction of wealth:

> "Power, credit, honors, wealth — to the priests! In all countries, who make the greatest figure? The ministers of the Lord." (ch. CXLVII)

And he connects the critique of wealth to the broader theme of priestly hypocrisy:

> "The ministers of religion have taken great care to make of their God a terrible, capricious, and changeable tyrant; it was necessary for them that He should be thus in order that He might lend Himself to their various interests." (ch. XCVII)
