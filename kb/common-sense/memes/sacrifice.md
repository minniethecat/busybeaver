# Sacrifice

## Definition

In Holbach's critique of religion, "sacrifice" names the central transactional mechanism of religious practice: the offering of something valuable — goods, [[soul|anima]]ls, [[eve]]n human lives — to a [[god|deity]] in exchange for favor or [[absolution|forgiveness]]. Holbach traces this practice from its most primitive forms ([[soul|anima]]l and human sacrifice) to its "civilized" equivalents (offerings, expiations, penitential acts, the [[mass|Mass]]), arguing that the underlying logic is identical and universally corrupting. "He who first proclaimed to the [[nation|nations]] that, when man had wronged man, he must ask [[god|God]]'s pardon, appease His wrath by presents, and offer Him sacrifices, obviously subverted the true principles of morality" (ch. 168).

The deeper target of Holbach's critique is the conception of [[god|God]] that sacrifice presupposes. A [[god|deity]] who "enjoys carnage and whose worshipers make it a duty to serve him to his taste" (ch. 72) is a projection of human cruelty, not a revelation of [[goodness|divine goodness]]. Sacrifice degrades both parties to the transaction: it makes [[god|God]] a tyrant who must be bribed, and man a slave who believes morality consists in ritual payment rather than just conduct.

## Historical Context

Holbach wrote in an era when the sacrificial logic of [[christianity|Christianity]] was vividly present. The [[mass|Catholic Mass]] was understood as a re-presentation of [[jesus-christ|Christ]]'s sacrifice on Calvary — an unbloody repetition of the original bloody offering. The doctrine of penal [[atonement|substitutionary atonement]], central to both Catholic and Protestant [[religion|theology]], held that [[jesus-christ|Christ]]'s death was a sacrifice offered to satisfy [[justice|divine justice]], which would otherwise have demanded the [[hell|eternal punishment]] of humanity.

Holbach's critique draws on several intellectual traditions. The *philosophes*' comparative anthropology had documented sacrificial practices across [[worship|cult]]ures, undermining claims to unique revealed origin. [[baruch-spinoza|Spinoza]]'s *Tractatus Theologico-Politicus* had already argued that ceremonial law, including sacrifice, was a political instrument of the ancient Hebrew state, not a universal moral requirement. And the Epicurean tradition, revived by Gassendi, had long mocked the idea that the gods could be influenced by human offerings.

## Core Arguments

1. **Sacrifice presupposes a barbarous conception of [[god|God]].** "Lambs, bulls, children, men, [[heresy|heretic]]s, infidels, [[monarch|king]]s, whole [[nation|nations]], are sacrificed to him" (ch. 72). A [[god|God]] who demands sacrifice is a [[god|God]] modeled on the most savage human tyrants — vindictive, hungry for tribute, appeasable by blood.

2. **Sacrifice corrupts morality.** The logic of ritual atonement "obviously subverted the true principles of morality" (ch. 168). By [[doctrine|teaching]] that wrongs against other humans can be remedied by offerings to [[god|God]], sacrifice severs the connection between [[morality|ethics]] and [[vice|social harm]]. The penitent who pays the priest imagines himself absolved regardless of the injury done to his neighbor.

3. **Sacrifice is useless against an immutable [[god|God]].** Holbach deploys a philosophical argument against the coherence of sacrifice: if [[god|God]] is perfect and unchanging, "neither our actions nor our [[prayer|prayers]] nor our sacrifices could suspend or change His invariable progress and His immutable designs" (ch. 84). Sacrifice presupposes a [[god|God]] who can be moved — but an immutable [[god|God]] cannot be moved by anything.

4. **The sacrifice of reason is the foundational demand of religion.** Beyond material offerings, Holbach identifies a deeper sacrifice: "Sacrifice your reason; give up experience; distrust the testimony of your senses" (ch. 135). Before the priest demands your livestock or your money, he demands your mind.

5. **Sacrifice serves priestly interests.** The offerings, sacrifices, and expiations ordained by religion are "useful, in fact, to the ministers of [[god|God]], but very onerous to the rest of mankind" (ch. 125). Sacrifice is an economic institution — a mechanism for transferring wealth from the laity to [[clergy|the clergy]] under cover of [[divinity|divine]] command.

## Reception and Critique

Holbach's critique of sacrifice was among his most influential arguments. The idea that sacrificial logic corrupts morality by severing [[morality|ethics]] from social consequence was taken up by utilitarian reformers who sought to replace religious [[confession|penance]] with secular criminal justice. The argument that [[jesus-christ|Christ]]'s sacrificial death represents a moral scandal — an innocent punished for the guilty — became a staple of deist and [[atheism|atheist]] polemic.

Theological responses have generally conceded the premise that pagan sacrifice is superstitious while distinguishing [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian sacrifice as qualitatively different — a free self-offering of [[god|God]] Himself, not a human attempt to bribe the [[divinity|divine]]. Whether this distinction withstands Holbach's analysis — which treats the logic of sacrifice, not its particular forms, as the problem — remains contested.

## Relation to Other Concepts

Sacrifice is the practical expression of [[Worship]] — the act by which humans attempt to influence the [[divinity|divine]] through transaction. It connects to [[Ceremony]], which provides the ritual framework for sacrificial practice. Sacrifice relates to [[Morality]], which it systematically corrupts by substituting ritual payment for ethical conduct. It is the economic foundation of [[Priestcraft]] — "useful, in fact, to the ministers of [[god|God]]." Sacrifice connects to [[Reason]], whose surrender is the first and most fundamental sacrifice demanded by religion. The concept also relates to [[Superstition]], of which sacrifice is among the most ancient and universal expressions.

## In the Text

> "Lambs, bulls, children, men, heretics, infidels, kings, whole nations, are sacrificed to him. The zealous servants of this barbarous God go so far as to believe that they are obliged to offer themselves as a sacrifice to him." (ch. 72)

> "He who first proclaimed to the nations that, when man had wronged man, he must ask God's pardon, appease His wrath by presents, and offer Him sacrifices, obviously subverted the true principles of morality. According to these ideas, men imagine that they can obtain from the King of Heaven, as well as from the kings of the earth, permission to be unjust and wicked, or at least pardon for the evil which they might commit." (ch. 168)

> "Neither our actions nor our prayers nor our sacrifices could suspend or change His invariable progress and His immutable designs, from which we are compelled to conclude that all religion would be entirely useless." (ch. 84)
