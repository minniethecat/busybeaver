# Chimera

## Definition

In *Common Sense*, Holbach uses "chimera" (from the Greek mythical fire-breathing creature composed of incongruous parts) as his master category for concepts fabricated by the human imagi[[nations|nation]] that lack any grounding in material reality. For Holbach, a chimera is not merely a falsehood — it is a cognitive construct whose constitutive elements cannot exist together in [[nature|the natural world]]. [[theology|Theology]], in his view, is the systematic production and institutional enforcement of [[imagination|chimeras]]. The concept is deployed across the text as the epistemological hammer that shatters the edifice of [[revelation|revealed religion]].

## Historical Context

Holbach's use of "chimera" inherits from the Enlightenment's broader critique of innate ideas and metaphysical speculation. [[john-locke|Locke]] had argued against innate principles; [[david-hume|Hume]] had challenged the ontological status of [[cause|causality]]; and the French materialists, including Diderot and La Mettrie, had extended empiricism to attack all claims of [[spirit|immaterial substance]]. Holbach's innovation lies in his rhetorical deployment of "chimera" as a unifying epithet: [[spirit|spirituality]], [[liberty|free will]], [[divinity|divine]] intelligence, and theological morality all fall under the same pejorative because they share the same epistemological defect — they are "ideas without a [[model|prototype]]."

The word appears 23 times in the English translation of *Common Sense* (tr. Anna Knoop, 1878), concentrated in chapters dealing with [[spirit|spirituality]] (Chapter XXI), [[divinity|divine]] intelligence (Chapter XLVI), and [[liberty|free will]]. This distribution reveals Holbach's strategic targeting: he uses chimera to attack [[religion|theology]] at its three most vulnerable points — its [[existence|ontology]] ([[soul|spirit]]), its epistemology ([[divinity|divine]] intelligence), and its [[morality|ethics]] ([[liberty|free will]] / moral responsibility).

## Core Arguments

1. **The [[spirit|spirituality]] chimera**: In Chapter XXI, "[[spirit|Spirit]]uality Is a Chimera," Holbach argues that the notion of "[[soul|spirit]]" is semantically empty when divorced from [[matter|material substance]]. The barbarian who speaks of [[soul|spirit]] at least imagines something akin to wind or breath — a material analog. The refined theologian, by contrast, strips the concept of all sensible qualities and is left with a word that denotes nothing. "What can the idea of [[god|God]] represent to us when it is evidently an idea without an object? Is not such an idea as impossible as an effect without a cause?"

2. **The [[divinity|divine]] intelligence chimera**: In Chapter XLVI, "To Adore a [[theology-index|Divine]] Intelligence Is a Chimera," Holbach ties intelligence to embodiment. To have ideas, thoughts, and will requires organs; organs require a body; to act upon bodies requires a body. A pure [[soul|spirit]] cannot think because thin[[monarch|king]] is a material process. The theological synthesis of man and [[god|God]] — theanthropy — "its only result is a chimera, of which nothing can be affirmed without causing the phantom to vanish."

3. **The [[liberty|free will]] chimera**: Man acts by compulsion in a deterministic universe. Whether one is born to [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian or Muslim parents, whether one accepts or rejects their [[doctrine|teaching]]s — none of this depends on a libertarian [[liberty|free will]]. "Man in all things acts by compulsion, and his [[liberty|free will]] is a chimera, [[eve]]n according to the theological system."

4. **Chimeras as instruments of control**: Holbach goes beyond epistemology to a sociological analysis. Legislators and [[clergy|priests]] found it "easier to put the [[nation|nations]] to sleep by fables than to teach them truths." Chimeras are not innocent errors — they are tools of domi[[nations|nation]]. "They needed souls and [[imagination|chimeras]] to populate the imaginary regions which they have discovered in the other life."

5. **The unifying critique of religion**: The entire theological system is reduced to "a long tissue of [[imagination|chimeras]] and contradictions" presenting "only romances devoid of probability, of which the hero himself is made up of qualities impossible to reconcile."

## In the Text

> "What can the idea of God represent to us when it is evidently an idea without an object? Is not such an idea as impossible as an effect without a cause? An idea without a prototype, is it anything but a chimera?"
>
> (004-001)

Holbach equates theological concepts with an effect without a cause — a violation of the fundamental materialist axiom that [[eve]]ry idea must have a sensory origin. A [[god|God]]-idea without a corresponding entity in reality is definitionally a chimera.

> "All religion is but a castle in the air; Theology is but ignorance of natural causes reduced to a system; it is but a long tissue of chimeras and contradictions."
>
> (001-001)

The opening chapter establishes the chimera thesis as the book's organizing principle. [[theology|Theology]] is not a rival expla[[nations|nation]] of [[earth|the world]]; it is a category mistake — the systematization of ignorance dressed as knowledge.

> "What is the result of this combination of man with God, or of this theanthropy? Its only result is a chimera, of which nothing can be affirmed without causing the phantom to vanish which they had taken so much trouble to conjure up."
>
> (047-002)

Theanthropy — the [[doctrine|theological doctrine]] uniting [[divinity|divine]] and human [[universe|nature]]s — is dismissed as a self-contradictory phantom. The very effort to affirm it causes it to dissolve.

## Relation to Other Concepts

- [[phantom]] — a closely related term Holbach uses for theological constructs that vanish under rational scrutiny
- [[superstition]] — [[imagination|chimeras]] are the cognitive content; superstition is the psychological disposition that embraces them
- [[imagination]] — the cognitive faculty that produces [[imagination|chimeras]] when unconstrained by [[experience|sensory experience]] and reason
- [[spirituality]] — the primary target: "[[spirit|Spirit]]uality is a Chimera" is the title and thesis of Chapter XXI
- [[free-will]] — one of Holbach's three principal [[imagination|chimeras]], alongside [[spirit|spirituality]] and [[divinity|divine]] intelligence
