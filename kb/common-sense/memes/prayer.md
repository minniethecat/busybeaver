# Prayer

## Definition

Prayer, in the [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian tradition, is the act of addressing [[god|God]] — to praise, to thank, to confess, and above all to petition.
It is petitionary prayer that Holbach subjects to the most sustained critique.
He argues that as[[monarch|king]] [[god|God]] for favors is not an expression of [[religion|faith]] but an implicit admission that [[divinity|divine]] [[government|governance]] is defective.

"Praying to [[god|God]], as[[monarch|king]] a favor of Him, is to mistrust His vigilant care; to pray [[god|God]] to avert or to suppress an [[injustice|evil]], is to endeavor to put obstacles in the way of His justice" (ch. 87).
If [[god|God]] is perfectly wise and benevolent, His plan for [[earth|the world]] must already be optimal — so what is there to request?
Every prayer is a vote of no [[certainty|confidence]] in providence.

Holbach extends the argument to its logical conclusion: "to implore the assistance of [[god|God]] in our calamities, means to appeal to the very author of these calamities in order to represent to Him our welfare."
The theological [[god|God]] is simultaneously the source of [[evil|suffering]] and the addressee of complaints about [[evil|suffering]] — a contradiction that prayer dramatizes rather than resolves.

## Historical Context

The practice of prayer was universal in eighteenth-century [[christianity|Christianity]].
The [[catholic-church|Catholic Church]] prescribed daily prayers, the liturgy of the hours, and a vast repertoire of devotional prayers addressed to [[god|God]], [[mary|the Virgin]] [[mary|Mary]], and the [[saint|saints]].
Protestant traditions simplified the forms but intensified the expectation of personal, heartfelt prayer.

Philosophical critiques of petitionary prayer were not new.
The Stoics had argued that the wise person does not pray for external goods but only for [[morality|virtue]], which is within one's own power.
[[baruch-spinoza|Spinoza]], in the *Theological-Political Treatise* (1670), treated prayer as a form of superstitious bargaining with an anthropomorphized [[god|deity]].

Holbach's innovation was to combine the Stoic and Spinozist critiques within a popular polemic.
His argument was accessible: [[eve]]ry person who has prayed for recovery from illness, for success in business, or for [[salvation|deliverance]] from danger has experienced the failure of prayer — and the theological excuses for that failure ([[god|God]]'s inscrutable will, the need for perseverance in prayer) are transparent rationalizations.

## Core Arguments

**1. Prayer is an implicit critique of [[divinity|divine]] administration.**
If [[god|God]]'s plan is perfect, why ask Him to change it?
"The prayers which they continually offer to [[heaven|Heaven]], prove to us that they are not at all satisfied with [[god|God]]'s administration" (ch. 87).
Every petition is an act of theological dissent.

**2. Prayer contradicts the doctrine of the best possible [[universe|world]].**
"The optimist, or the one who thinks that [[eve]]rything is good in [[earth|the world]]... if he were consistent, ought never to pray; still less should he expect another [[universe|world]] where men will be happier" (ch. 87).
You cannot simultaneously claim that this is the best of all possible [[universe|world]]s and ask [[god|God]] to improve it.

**3. Prayer appeals to the author of [[evil|suffering]] for relief from [[evil|suffering]].**
"To implore the assistance of [[god|God]] in our calamities, means to appeal to the very author of these calamities" (ch. 87).
If [[god|God]] is the cause of the [[injustice|evil]] from which you seek [[salvation|deliverance]], prayer is a request that [[god|God]] undo His own work — an admission that His work was defective.

**4. Prayer cannot alter necessary natural laws.**
Holbach's [[effect|determinism]] entails that natural [[eve]]nts follow fixed laws.
Praying for rain, for health, or for victory does not change the causal nexus that determines these outcomes.
Prayer is causally inert — an expenditure of mental energy on an impossible transaction.

**5. The practice of prayer persists because it provides psychological comfort, not because it works.**
Holbach recognizes that prayer has a subjective function: it relieves anxiety, creates the illusion of agency in situations of helplessness, and allows the believer to feel that something is [[existence|being]] done.
But psychological comfort is not the same as efficacy, and a practice built on illusion is not benign.

## Reception and Critique

Holbach's critique of prayer was part of a broader Enlightenment rejection of supernatural intervention.
The Deists retained a form of prayer — usually limited to praise and thanksgiving — while rejecting petitionary prayer as superstitious.
Kant treated prayer as having moral value (as an expression of the disposition to fulfill all duties as [[divinity|divine]] commands) while denying its theoretical validity as a means of influencing [[god|God]].

Later theological responses have argued that Holbach misunderstands the function of petitionary prayer.
On Aquinas's account, prayer does not change [[god|God]]'s will but aligns the petitioner's will with [[god|God]]'s — it is a practice of moral formation, not of causal intervention.
Holbach would likely reply that this reinterpretation concedes his central point: whatever prayer is doing psychologically, it is not changing outcomes.

Contemporary empirical studies of intercessory prayer (notably the STEP project, 2006) have found no [[proof|evidence]] that prayer affects medical outcomes — a finding consistent with Holbach's causal argument, though arrived at by different methods.

## Relation to Other Concepts

Prayer is the practical expression of [[worship]] — the act through which the believer addresses the [[god|deity]].
It presupposes [[providence]], the doctrine that [[god|God]] governs [[earth|the world]], which prayer implicitly criticizes.

Prayer is closely related to [[ritual]]: like other rituals, it is a prescribed action whose efficacy is independent of any natural causal mechanism.
It connects to [[superstition]] — the belief that words spoken in the right form to the right [[existence|being]] can alter material reality.

Prayer inter[[sect|sects]] with [[theism]], whose [[contradiction|inconsistency]] Holbach exposes through the prayer critique: a perfect [[god|God]] governing a perfect [[universe|world]] cannot coherently be petitioned for [[progress|improvement]]s.
