# India

## Definition

In Holbach's "Common Sense," **India** functions — alongside [[china|China]] and [[egypt|Egypt]] — as an example of a non-[[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian civilization whose inhabitants, through no fault of their own, are damned by [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian [[religion|theology]] simply because they were born in the wrong place. The Indian who has never heard the [[gospel|Gospel]] is no less damned than the [[paris|Parisian]] who has heard and rejected it. This geographical arbitrariness of salvation is, for Holbach, a *reductio ad [[absurdity|absurd]]um* of any claim to a universal [[revelation|revelation]].

## The Pluralism Problem

Holbach deploys India within a comparative argument about religious diversity:

> "The Indian does not believe one word of what He said to the Chinaman; the Christian, in his turn, rejects what the Indian and the Chinaman accept, and the latter does not believe one word of this more modern revelation, which equally damns the Indian and the Chinaman." (ch. II)

The logic is [[conscience|remorse]]less: if [[god|God]] had revealed a universal truth, that truth would be universally known and universally accessible. Instead, what people beli[[eve]] about [[god|God]] depends overwhelmingly on where and when they were born. The Indian believes what Indian parents and Indian [[priest|priests]] taught; the Chinaman believes what Chinese parents and Chinese sages taught; the [[christianity|Christian]] believes what [[europe|Europe]]an parents and [[church|Christian]] [[clergy|priests]] taught. The distribution of [[religion|religious]] belief follows the distribution of political and [[worship|cult]]ural [[power|power]], not the distribution of [[truth|truth]].

## Indian "Dreamers" and the Origin of Philosophy

Holbach groups Indian [[clergy|priests]] with [[egypt|Egypt]]ian and Chaldean [[clergy|priests]] as the originators of priestly obscurantism:

> "It was among Egyptian, Indian, Chaldean priests; it was in the schools of these dreamers, interested by profession in dethroning human reason, that philosophy was obliged to borrow its first rudiments." (ch. II)

The "Indian dreamers" — likely a reference to the Brahmin priestly class as understood through 18th-century [[europe|Europe]]an accounts of Hinduism — are indicted alongside their [[egypt|Egypt]]ian and Chaldean counterparts as manufacturers of metaphysical obscurity designed to disable [[reason|reason]] and perpetuate priestly authority. [[enlightenment|The Enlightenment]]'s ambivalent relationship with Indian thought is on display here: Holbach uses India as a critical tool against [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ianity (look how many ways there are to be [[religion|religious]]!) while simultaneously dismissing Indian [[religion|religion]] as another species of the same superstition.

## The Argument from Geography

The India example encapsulates one of Holbach's most powerful arguments against [[revelation|revealed religion]]: the argument from geography. If salvation depends on accepting a specific historical [[revelation|revelation]], then salvation is geographically contingent — available to those born in [[jesus-christ|Christ]]endom, unavailable to those born in India, China, or pre-Columbian America. A just [[god|God]] would not make salvation dependent on geographical [[chance|accident]]. Therefore, either [[god|God]] is unjust, or the claim of exclusive [[revelation|revelation]] is false. Holbach's conclusion is the latter.
