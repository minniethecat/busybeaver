# Reward

## Definition

For Holbach, the theological promise of divine reward is a mechanism of control dressed as a moral doctrine. The claim that [[god|God]] rewards the virtuous and punishes the wicked collapses under its own theological premises. If [[god|God]] is the author of all human action — if without grace man can do nothing but [[injustice|evil]] — then rewarding man for good deeds amounts to [[god|God]] rewarding Himself for His own work. "According to such principles, man can merit neither reward nor punishment; in rewarding man for the good he does, [[god|God]] would but recompense Himself" (ch. LXXX).

The doctrine of reward is also empirically empty. The rewards promised — eternal bliss in [[hope|heaven]] — are described in terms so vague that they cannot guide conduct. "The pleasures of [[heaven|Heaven]], of which we can not form an idea," Holbach observes, weigh nothing against the tangible interests of this life (ch. CXLI). A reward that cannot be conceived cannot motivate; it can only distract.

Holbach's deeper point is that the reward-punishment framework is unnecessary for morality. The natural consequences of human action — the esteem of others, the satis[[sect|faction]] of a well-ordered life, the avoidance of social sanction — provide all the motivation that a rational person needs. "The rules which govern men's conduct spring from their own [[universe|nature]]" (ch. CLXXI).

## Historical Context

The concept of divine reward has deep roots in [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian [[religion|theology]], from Augustine's doctrine of grace to the medieval synthesis of Aristotle's [[morality|virtue]] [[morality|ethics]] with [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian eschatology. Aquinas held that beatitude — the vision of [[god|God]] — was the ultimate reward for a life of [[morality|virtue]], a supernatural end exceeding any natural happiness.

[[enlightenment|The Enlightenment]] subjected this framework to sustained critique. [[baruch-spinoza|Spinoza]]'s *Ethics* (1677) argued that [[morality|virtue]] is its own reward and that the expectation of [[divinity|divine]] recompense corrupts genuine moral motivation. [[pierre-bayle|Pierre Bayle]], in his *Historical and Critical Dictionary* (1697), explored the paradox that [[atheism|atheist]]s could be morally upright without any expectation of reward, while believers often behaved badly despite — or because of — their [[certainty|confidence]] in [[divinity|divine]] [[absolution|forgiveness]].

Holbach's contribution was to systematize these critiques within a materialist framework. For him, the reward doctrine was not merely philosophically incoherent but socially harmful: it directed human energy toward an imaginary future at the expense of present [[happiness|well-being]], and it gave [[clergy|priests]] a lever of psychological control over the laity.

## Core Arguments

**1. [[theology-index|Divine]] reward is logically impossible under theological premises.** If [[god|God]] is the sole source of all good action through grace, then rewarding humans for good conduct is [[god|God]] rewarding Himself. The creature contributes nothing that is not already [[god|the creator]]'s doing (ch. LXXX).

**2. [[heaven|Heaven]]ly rewards cannot motivate because they cannot be conceived.** "Men have always but confused ideas of their [[god|God]]; they see Him only in the clouds; they never think of Him when they wish to do wrong" (ch. CXLI). Abstract, imperceptible rewards are powerless against concrete passions and present interests.

**3. Natural consequences are sufficient moral motivators.** Human [[existence|being]]s are constituted to seek pleasure and avoid [[suffering|pain]], to [[passion|desire]] the esteem of others and fear their contempt. These natural sanctions — not supernatural rewards — are the real foundation of moral conduct (ch. CLXXI).

**4. The reward doctrine en[[wealth|riches]] [[clergy|priests]] at the expense of the people.** By positioning themselves as the dispensers of [[divinity|divine]] favor, [[clergy|priests]] convert the promise of [[hope|heaven]]ly reward into earthly wealth and power. "You are, O [[clergy|priests]], rewarded, honored, and paid for deceiving mortals" (ch. CLXXXVII).

## Relation to Other Concepts

Reward is the positive counterpart to [[Punishment]] in the theological apparatus of control. Both depend on the fiction of [[Free Will]], [[vice|sin]]ce without libertarian [[liberty|freedom]] the concepts of merit and demerit collapse. The promise of reward is the chief [[hope|consolation]] offered by religion for earthly [[Suffering]], a [[hope|consolation]] Holbach dismisses as imaginary. The doctrine of reward requires a [[God]] who dispenses it, and functions as a mechanism of [[Authority]] — [[clergy|priests]] control access to [[divinity|divine]] favor. Holbach's alternative foundation for [[morality|ethics]] rests on [[Interest]], the natural pursuit of [[happiness|well-being]] that requires no supernatural sanction.

## In the Text

On the impossibility of merit under theological premises:

> "According to such principles, man can merit neither reward nor punishment; in rewarding man for the good he does, God would but recompense Himself; in punishing man for the evil he does, God punishes him for not having been given the grace, without which it was impossible for him to do better." (ch. LXXX)

On the impotence of [[hope|heaven]]ly rewards against present passions:

> "Whenever ambition, fortune, or pleasure entices them or leads them away, God, and His menaces, and His promises weigh nothing in the balance. The things of this life have for men a degree of certainty, which the most lively faith can never give to the objects of another life." (ch. CXLI)

On morality without divine reward:

> "Whether there exists a God or not, whether this God has spoken or not, men's moral duties will always be the same so long as they possess their own nature; that is to say, so long as they are sensible beings." (ch. CLXXI)
