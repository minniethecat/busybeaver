# René Descartes

## Biography

René Descartes (1596–1650) was a French philosopher, mathematician, and scientist, widely regarded as the founder of modern philosophy. His mechanistic physics and dualistic metaphysics — separating mind from body, and both from [[god|God]] — shaped [[europe|Europe]]an thought for two centuries. His major works include the *Discourse on Method* (1637), *Meditations on First [[philosophy|Philosophy]]* (1641), and *[[principle|Principle]]s of [[philosophy|Philosophy]]* (1644). Descartes's method of systematic doubt and his search for indubitable foundations made him the central figure of seventeenth-century rationalism.

## Role in Religious History

Descartes occupies a complex position in the history of religion and freethought. Though a devout Catholic who attempted to prove the existence of [[god|God]] and the im[[death|mortality]] of the soul through rational argument, his philosophy inadvertently undermined the intellectual authority of [[church|the Church]]. By ma[[monarch|king]] reason, rather than revelation or tradition, the ultimate arbiter of truth, Descartes opened a path that later thinkers — including the materialists and [[atheism|atheist]]s of the French Enlightenment — would follow to conclusions far more radical than his own. His dualism, which separated matter from [[soul|spirit]], was simultaneously a concession to orthodoxy and a liberation of natural [[knowledge|science]] from theological interference.

## Connection to "Common Sense"

Holbach's *Common Sense* references Descartes in two revealing contexts. The biographical preface by [[voltaire|Voltaire]] notes that [[jean-meslier|Jean Meslier]], the apostate priest whose testament Holbach edited, "devoted himself to the system of Descartes" at the seminary — suggesting that Cartesian rationalism, once absorbed, could lead a devout seminarian toward radical doubt ([[life|Life]] of [[meslier|Jean Meslier]], PN 472). In the concluding appendix, Holbach groups Descartes with [[malebranche|Malebranche]] and [[gottfried-leibniz|Leibnitz]] as philosophers "compelled to invent hypotheses and evasions in order to reconcile their discoveries with the reveries and the blunders which religion had rendered sacred" — a judgment that Descartes, despite his [[courage|intellectual courage]], ultimately submitted reason to the demands of [[religion|faith]] (Appendix, PN 6951).
