# Thought

## Definition

In Holbach's Enlightenment vocabulary, "thought" (*la pensée*, also rendered as "reflection" or "reasoning") designates the active exercise of the [[reason|rational faculty]] — not passive reception of ideas but critical scrutiny of [[opinion|received opinion]]. Thought is what distinguishes the philosopher from the believer: "All men accustomed to think, instead of see[[monarch|king]] to injure each other, should unite their efforts in combating errors, in see[[monarch|king]] truth, and especially in dispelling the [[prejudice|prejudices]] from which the [[monarch|sovereign]]s and subjects suffer alike" (191-002). This is not a call for academic speculation but for the systematic application of reason to the beliefs that govern human life.

Holbach's concept of thought has a fundamentally emancipatory structure. To think is to refuse authority, to question [[habit|custom]], to demand [[proof|evidence]]. The [[clergy|priests]] of all religions understand this, which is why they universally demand the sacrifice of thought: "[[sacrifice|Sacrifice]] your reason; give up experience; distrust the testimony of your senses; submit without exami[[nations|nation]] to all that is given to you as coming from [[heaven|Heaven]]" (135-001). The demand to suspend thought is, for Holbach, the defining gesture of [[priest|priestcraft]] — and the act of thin[[monarch|king]] is, correspondingly, the defining act of liberation.

## Historical Context

Holbach's celebration of thought belongs to the mainstream of Enlightenment rationalism. [[rene-descartes|Descartes]]' *cogito* had established thin[[monarch|king]] as the foundational act of philosophy; the *philosophes* extended this primacy of thought from metaphysics to social criticism. Kant's later definition of Enlightenment — *sapere aude*, "dare to know" — captures the same [[soul|spirit]]: the refusal to think for oneself is self-incurred tutelage.

What distinguishes Holbach's treatment is its militancy. Thought is not merely a private intellectual [[morality|virtue]] but a weapon against the institutional structures that suppress it. The "hatred of the latter [[[clergy|ministers of religion]]] for philosophy was never more than professional jealousy" (191-002). The [[priest|clergy]]'s opposition to thought is not doctrinal but economic — thin[[monarch|king]] threatens their monopoly on belief. Holbach's call for thinkers to "unite their efforts" en[[dreams|visions]] an organized intellectual opposition to organized religion.

## Core Arguments

1. **Thought is the antidote to [[opinion|prejudice]].** "[[experience|Experience]], reflection, reason, are the proper remedies to cure ignorance, [[zeal|fanaticism]], and folly" (082-001). Thought operates therapeutically: by subjecting beliefs to rational scrutiny, it dissolves the fantasies generated by fear and ignorance.

2. **[[religion|Religion]] demands the sacrifice of thought.** The universal priestly command is: "[[sacrifice|Sacrifice]] your reason; give up experience; distrust the testimony of your senses; submit without exami[[nations|nation]]" (135-001). [[priest|Priest]]s "do not agree upon any point, except in the necessity of never reasoning" — the suppression of thought is their only common doctrine.

3. **The refusal to think has practical consequences.** "The majority of men examine nothing" (ch. 123); the result is not intellectual neutrality but active complicity in error. Those who do not think become instruments of those who exploit their [[belief|credulity]].

4. **Thinking is a collective enterprise.** "All men accustomed to think ... should unite their efforts in combating errors, in see[[monarch|king]] truth" (191-002). Holbach en[[dreams|visions]] a [[society|community]] of thinkers — what would later be called an intellectual class — whose collective labor advances the cause of [[education|enlightenment]] against the entrenched forces of superstition.

5. **Thought is continuous with sensation.** Following [[john-locke|Locke]] and Condillac, Holbach grounds thought in [[experience|sensory experience]]: "It is from our senses that all our ideas come" (ch. 136). Thinking is not a transcendent faculty but the organization and comparison of sensory data — a natural, material process, not a [[divinity|divine]] spark.

## Reception and Critique

Holbach's rationalism has been criticized from multiple directions. Romantic thinkers argued that his elevation of abstract reason neglected [[passion|emotion]], imagi[[nations|nation]], and the non-rational dimensions of human experience that religion addresses. Nietzsche would later radicalize the critique: the "will to truth" that Holbach celebrated is itself a disguised form of the ascetic [[model|ideal]], a secular continuation of the [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian devaluation of instinct and life.

More sympathetic readers have noted that Holbach's concept of thought is broader than the narrow "rationalism" attributed to him by critics. His emphasis on experience, reflection, and the communal [[universe|nature]] of inquiry anticipates pragmatist and social epistemologies. The point is not that thought can answer [[eve]]ry question but that the alternative — submission to authority without exami[[nations|nation]] — is intellectual and moral abdication.

## Relation to Other Concepts

Thought is the active exercise of [[Reason]], Holbach's fundamental epistemological norm. It is the opposite of [[Prejudice]], which is belief accepted without thought. Thought relates to [[Sensation]] as effect to cause — "it is from our senses that all our ideas come" (ch. 136). It connects to [[Education]], which [[worship|cult]]ivates the habit of thin[[monarch|king]] in the young. Thought is the faculty systematically suppressed by [[Priestcraft]] and [[Authority]]. It is the instrument by which [[Philosophy]] combats [[Superstition]].
