# Peter

## Biography

Peter (also called Simon Peter, Simon bar Jonah, or Cephas) was, according to the [[bible|New Testament]], one of the first disciples called by [[jesus-christ|Jesus]] and the most prominent of the twelve apostles. Originally a Galilean fisherman from Bethsaida, he was called — together with his brother Andrew — while casting nets into the [[galilee|Sea of Galilee]].

[[jesus-christ|Jesus]] renamed him "Peter" (from *Petros*, "rock"), declaring that "upon this rock I will build my church."

Peter appears in all four [[gospel|Gospel]]s and in the Acts of the Apostles as the spokesman of the disciples — impetuous, courageous, and fallible. He confesses [[jesus-christ|Jesus]] as the [[jesus-christ|Christ]], tries to walk on water, cuts off the ear of the high priest's servant at the arrest, and then denies [[jesus-christ|Jesus]] three times before the cock crows. In Acts, he emerges as the leader of the [[jerusalem|Jerusalem]] church, preaching at Pentecost, performing healings, and negotiating the inclusion of Gentiles.

## Role in Religious History

Peter occupies a foundational role in [[christianity|Christian]] [[church|ecclesiastical]] history. Catholic tradition holds that Peter traveled to [[rome|Rome]], led the church there, and was martyred under Nero — crucified upside down at his own request — and that the Bishop of [[rome|Rome]] (the Pope) is his direct successor in a continuous line of apostolic authority.

This claim of Petrine primacy — based on [[jesus-christ|Jesus]]'s words in [[matthew|Matthew]] 16:18 — is the basis for papal authority and has been one of the most consequential institutional claims in Western history. Protestant reformers rejected the papal claim while generally affirming Peter's apostolic authority. The Orthodox churches acknowledge Peter's primacy of honor but reject papal supremacy.

Beyond his role in church polity, Peter is the traditional source for [[mark|Mark]]'s [[gospel|Gospel]] — Papias records that [[mark|Mark]], having become Peter's interpreter, "wrote down accurately, though not in order, all that he remembered of the things said or done by the Lord."

Two [[bible|New Testament]] epistles bear Peter's name, though critical scholarship is divided on their authorship. A substantial body of apocryphal literature — including the Acts of Peter, the Apocalypse of Peter, [[gospel|the Gospel]] of Peter, and the Preaching of Peter — circulated in early [[christianity|Christianity]], some of it widely read before [[existence|being]] excluded from the canon.

## Connection to "Common Sense"

Holbach does not discuss Peter's life or character directly. Peter enters *Common Sense* in two contexts: the catalogue of apocryphal books that blur the boundary of the canon, and the critique of papal authority toward the end of the work.

**1. Apocryphal Petrine literature.** In his survey of the canonical instability of the [[bible|New Testament]], Holbach notes the proliferation of texts attributed to Peter that were accepted by some early [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian communities and rejected by others:

> "The Gospel of St. Bartholomew, that of St. Matthias, of St. Jacques, of St. Peter and of the Apostles, as also the Deeds of St. Peter, his book on Preaching, and that of his Apocalypse... are all rejected as apocryphal by the Roman Catholics." (Chapter 2)
>
> (APP001-015)

For Holbach, the existence of a large body of Petrine literature — [[gospel|Gospel]]s, Acts, Apocalypses, and Preachings all claiming apostolic authorship — raises a problem that the [[church|Church]] does not satisfactorily answer: by what criterion is the canonical [[gospel|Gospel]] of [[mark|Mark]] (which does not [[eve]]n claim apostolic authorship) accepted as [[divinity|divine]], while [[gospel|the Gospel]] of Peter (which explicitly claims to be by the chief apostle) is rejected as apocryphal?

The decision, Holbach implies, was made by human ecclesiastical authorities with theological and political motives — not by any self-evident marker of inspiration.

**2. The fallibility of papal authority.** Holbach addresses the papacy toward the end of Chapter 2 with pointed irony:

> "Indeed, the successor of St. Peter is fallible and the Pope is mortal." (Chapter 2)

The pope claims to be the successor of Peter and to speak with apostolic authority — yet popes have contradicted one another, reversed one another's decisions, and pursued policies that [[eve]]n Catholic historians acknowledge as disastrous.

A chain of succession that runs through fallible, mortal men cannot transmit infallible [[divinity|divine]] authority. If the papacy rests on Peter, and the historical Peter is accessible only through contradictory, composite, and partly legendary sources, then the entire institutional edifice rests on sand rather than rock — a deliberate inversion of [[jesus-christ|Jesus]]'s own metaphor.

**3. Miraculous relics.** Holbach catalogues the [[miracle|miracles]] attributed to the relics of [[saint|saints]], including Peter, with evident scorn:

> "It is said that the staff of St. Peter, that of St. James, and that of St. Bernard performed miracles." (Chapter 2)
>
> (002-500)

The staff of Peter — like the shoe of St. [[honor|Honor]]ius, the cord of St. Francis, and the girdle of St. Melanie — is credited with raising the dead or healing the sick. For Holbach, the relic [[worship|cult]] is the natural terminus of the logic of [[miracle|miracles]]: a material object, authenticated by tradition alone, is believed to act with supernatural power — a claim indistinguishable in form from pagan magic.

## Key Ideas Attributed / Critiqued

**1. Apostolic authority as circular reasoning.** The claim that Peter had authority from [[jesus-christ|Jesus]] rests on a text ([[matthew|Matthew]]'s [[gospel|Gospel]]) whose authority rests on the claim that the [[church|Church]] that Peter founded was [[divinity|divinely]] guided in selecting the canon. The circle is: Peter is authoritative because [[matthew|Matthew]] says so; [[matthew|Matthew]] is authoritative because the [[church|Church]] of Peter's successors says so. Holbach would identify this as a textbook case of circular reasoning used to ground institutional power.

**2. The apocryphal canon as [[proof|evidence]] of arbitrary s[[predestination|election]].** The sheer volume of lost and rejected Petrine literature [[demon|demons]]trates that "apostolic authorship" was not a self-evident criterion for canonicity. Early [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ians could not agree on which Petrine texts were genuine — and the ones that survived, Holbach implies, survived because they served the theological and political interests of the [[sect|faction]] that won.

**3. The successor's fallibility.** The Pope's fallibility is not a theological subtlety but a devastating admission: if the successor to Peter can err, then the [[church|Church]]'s claim to be the unique conduit of [[divinity|divine]] truth is false. A fallible human institution claiming infallibility is, for Holbach, the precise definition of an imposture.

## Legacy

Peter's legacy is immense and contested. For nearly two billion Catholics, he is the first pope, the rock on which the [[church|Church]] is built. For Orthodox [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ians, he is "first among equals" — honored but not supreme.

For Protestants, he is an apostle whose authority was personal and non-transferable. For critical historians, he is a figure about whom very little can be known with certainty beyond his prominence in the earliest Jesus movement.

From Holbach's perspective, Peter illustrates the mechanism by which [[religion|religious]] authority is constructed: a charismatic figure from an obscure Galilean fishing village becomes, through centuries of textual accumulation and institutional elaboration, the foundation for the most enduring institution in Western history — whose head claims to speak for [[god|God]].

The gap between the historical Galilean fisherman and the papal tiara is the measure of what [[religion|religious]] institutions can accomplish through the progressive accumulation of legends and claims.
