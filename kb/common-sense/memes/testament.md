# Testament

## Definition

In Holbach's "Common Sense," the Old and [[bible|New Testament]]s are not [[revelation|divine revelation]]s but human productions — collections of "pretended Visions and [[theology-index|Divine]] [[revelation|Revelation]]s" (Appendix, ch. 4) that would be dismissed as the work of "a fool or a fanatic" if anyone claimed to receive them today. Holbach treats the [[scripture|Bible]] as an object of rational scrutiny rather than reverent submission. His critique operates on three levels: the texts are internally contradictory, historically unreliable, and morally objectionable — they attribute to [[god|God]] actions that would be condemned in any human [[existence|being]].

The concept of "testament" for Holbach is inseparable from the problem of authority. Who testifies, and on what grounds should their testimony be believed? The biblical writers claim to transmit the words of [[god|God]], but they offer no [[proof|evidence]] that could survive [[eve]]n the most basic cross-exami[[nations|nation]]. "In order to give a just idea of it, I beli[[eve]] it is best to say in general, that they are such, that if any one should dare now to boast of similar ones, or wish to make them valued, he would certainly be regarded as a fool or a fanatic" (Appendix, ch. 4).

## Historical Context

Biblical criticism was well underway by Holbach's time. [[baruch-spinoza|Spinoza]]'s *Tractatus Theologico-Politicus* (1670) had argued that the [[scripture|Bible]] should be read like any other book — its authors were human, its composition was historical, and its [[miracle|miracles]] were natural [[eve]]nts misunderstood by superstitious observers. Richard Simon's *Critical History of the [[bible|Old Testament]]* (1678) had [[demon|demons]]trated that the biblical texts existed in multiple manuscript traditions with significant variations. [[pierre-bayle|Pierre Bayle]]'s *Historical and Critical Dictionary* (1697) had catalogued the moral atrocities attributed to [[god|God]] in the [[bible|Old Testament]] and asked how a [[existence|being]] of infinite goodness could command genocide.

Holbach drew on all of these sources but sharpened the polemical edge. Where [[baruch-spinoza|Spinoza]] wrote cautiously (his book was banned), Holbach wrote bluntly: the [[god|God]] of the [[bible|Old Testament]] is "a great savage," and the [[bible|New Testament]]'s central claim — that [[god|God]] became man and was executed to appease His own wrath — is not a [[secrecy|mystery]] to be contemplated but an [[absurdity|absurd]]ity to be rejected. The appendix chapters of "Common Sense" function as a compressed critical commentary on the [[scripture|Bible]]'s most vulnerable claims.

## Core Arguments

**1. The Testaments are contradictory.** The [[god|God]] of the [[bible|Old Testament]] is jealous, wrathful, and tribal; the [[god|God]] of the New is loving, merciful, and universal. These are not two aspects of one consistent character but two incompatible personalities forced into a [[vice|sin]]gle canon. Holbach notes that the "law of [[god|God]], interpreted by His [[clergy|priests]], is often contrary to the law of the [[monarch|sovereign]] or to the interest of the [[politics-index|State]]" (ch. 173) — the contradictions are not merely textual but political.

**2. The Testaments are historically unreliable.** The [[dreams|visions]], prophecies, and [[miracle|miracles]] recorded in the [[scripture|Bible]] are not the kind of testimony that any reasonable person would accept from a contemporary claimant. [[jacob|Jacob]]'s ladder, Balaam's tal[[monarch|king]] donkey, Joshua's stopped sun — these are stories from "ignorant and savage ages" (ch. 119), not [[proof|evidence]] of [[divinity|divine]] intervention.

**3. The Testaments attribute moral atrocities to [[god|God]].** Holbach surveys the [[god|God]] of the [[bible|Old Testament]] — who commands genocide, hardens Pharaoh's heart, and sends bears to maul children who mock a [[prophecy|prophet]] — and asks how such a [[existence|being]] could be worthy of worship. "Men! you worship but a great savage, whom you consider as a model to follow" (ch. 119). The [[bible|New Testament]] improves the moral tone but introduces a new atrocity: the doctrine of eternal [[hell|damnation]], which condemns the majority of humanity to endless [[torture|torment]] for beliefs they could not have held.

**4. The Testaments cannot ground morality.** If the [[scripture|Bible]] is the foundation of [[morality|ethics]], then [[morality|ethics]] rests on a document of uncertain authorship, contradictory content, and morally repugnant commands. Holbach argues that genuine morality must be grounded in human [[universe|nature]] and [[utility|social utility]] — principles that can be investigated rationally without appeal to any "testament."

## Reception and Critique

Holbach's biblical criticism was among the most shoc[[monarch|king]] elements of his work to 18th-century readers, not because its arguments were novel but because they were presented in French rather than Latin and aimed at a general audience. Earlier critics had written for scholars; Holbach wrote for the salon and the coffeehouse. The [[catholic-church|Catholic Church]] placed "Common Sense" on the Index of Prohibited Books, but this only increased its circulation.

The 19th century saw the academic institutionalization of biblical criticism. German scholars like [[david|David]] Friedrich Strauss (*The [[life|Life]] of Jesus, Critically Examined*, 1835) and Julius Wellhausen (*Prolegomena to the History of [[israel|Israel]]*, 1878) developed the documentary hypothesis and [[demon|demons]]trated the composite authorship of the Pentateuch — vindicating Holbach's intuition that the [[scripture|Bible]] was a human document, though with scholarly rigor he did not possess.

## Relation to Other Concepts

The Testament is the textual foundation of [[Christianity]] — the religion Holbach identifies as his primary target. It is connected to [[Revelation]], the theological claim that [[god|God]] has communicated directly with humanity, which Holbach argues is impossible given the [[universe|nature]] of the [[god|God]] described. The Testament's moral content is critiqued through the lens of [[Reason]] and [[Morality]], which Holbach insists must be independent of any particular [[bible|scripture]].

The [[bible|Old Testament]] [[god|God]], whom Holbach calls "the [[god|God]] of the Jews" (ch. 2), is connected to [[Superstition]] as its most influential literary product. The Testament's authority is enforced by [[Priest]], which claims the exclusive right to interpret its obscure and contradictory passages.

## In the Text

On the credibility of biblical [[dreams|visions]]:

> "In order to give a just idea of it, I believe it is best to say in general, that they are such, that if any one should dare now to boast of similar ones, or wish to make them valued, he would certainly be regarded as a fool or a fanatic." (Appendix, ch. 4)

On the [[god|God]] of the [[bible|Old Testament]]:

> "We still continue to kneel before the barbarous God of the Jews, that is to say, of the most stupid, the most credulous, the most savage, the most unsocial nation which ever was on the earth." (ch. 2)

On the contradiction between Testaments and reason:

> "All the Gods worshiped by men have a barbarous origin; they were visibly the productions of ignorant and savage ages. Men! you worship but a great savage, whom you consider as a model to follow." (ch. 119)
