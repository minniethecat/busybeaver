# Merit

## Definition

Holbach defines merit in strictly secular terms: "We can judge of the merit of a system of morals but by its conformity with man's [[universe|nature]]" (ch. CLXXII). Merit is not a theological category — not a score kept by [[god|God]], not a quality of the soul visible only to the [[divinity|divine]] eye — but a practical, observable relation between actions and human welfare. An action has merit to the degree that it promotes the [[happiness|well-being]] of human [[existence|being]]s in this [[universe|world]].

This definition does two things at once. It provides a criterion for evaluating moral systems (a system has merit if it conforms to human [[universe|nature]]) and a criterion for evaluating individual actions (an action has merit if it benefits humanity). Both are naturalistic — they require no reference to [[god|God]], revelation, or the [[death|afterlife]]. "What is merit in man? A way of acting which renders him useful and estimable to his fellows" (ch. LXXXIII).

The theological concept of merit, by contrast, is arbitrary and perverse. It rewards beliefs rather than actions, orthodoxy rather than [[charity|beneficence]]. "The more incredible they are, the more merit he gives himself for believing them" (ch. XIII). The [[belief|credulity]] that [[religion|theology]] calls meritorious is actually a form of intellectual self-harm — the more [[absurdity|absurd]] the belief, the greater the supposed merit in accepting it. This inverts genuine merit: instead of rewarding what benefits humanity, it rewards what degrades the mind.

## Historical Context

The concept of merit in [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian [[religion|theology]] was shaped by the Pelagian controversy of the 5th century. Pelagius argued that human [[existence|being]]s could merit salvation through their own efforts; Augustine insisted that salvation was entirely a gift of [[divinity|divine]] grace, unmerited and unmeritable. The [[catholic-church|Catholic Church]] [[eve]]ntually adopted a compromise — merit is real but dependent on grace — which allowed for the development of an elaborate economy of merits, indulgences, and saintly intercession.

The [[reformation|Protestant Reformation]] rejected this economy. Luther's doctrine of *sola fide* ([[religion|faith]] alone) denied that human actions could merit salvation, ma[[monarch|king]] the entire apparatus of Catholic merit-system theologically invalid. But the [[reform|Reform]]ers retained the concept of merit in a negative form — human [[existence|being]]s merit [[hell|damnation]]; it is only by unmerited grace that any are saved.

Holbach's secularization of merit bypasses this entire theological debate. If merit is simply contribution to human welfare, then the question of whether [[god|God]] rewards merit becomes irrelevant — and the theological systems that make merit depend on belief rather than action are exposed as harmful. "According to such principles, man can merit neither reward nor punishment" (ch. LXXIX) — and this is precisely Holbach's point. The theological concept of merit is incoherent because it depends on an unknowable [[divinity|divine]] will rather than observable human consequences.

## Core Arguments

**1. Merit is judged by conformity with human [[universe|nature]].** "We can judge of the merit of a system of morals but by its conformity with man's [[universe|nature]]" (ch. CLXXII). A moral system that demands what human [[universe|nature]] cannot sustain — celibacy, self-abnegation, hatred of the body — has no merit regardless of its [[divinity|divine]] pretensions.

**2. Theological merit inverts genuine merit.** "The more incredible they are, the more merit he gives himself for believing them" (ch. XIII). In the economy of [[religion|faith]], believing [[absurdity|absurd]]ities is meritorious. In the economy of reason, believing [[absurdity|absurd]]ities is a failure of judgment. [[theology|Theology]] rewards the degradation of the intellect.

**3. Merit without [[liberty|free will]] is incoherent.** If [[god|God]] determines all actions, "man can merit neither reward nor punishment" (ch. LXXIX). The theological concept of merit presupposes [[liberty|free will]], but the theological concept of [[sovereign|divine sovereignty]] denies it. The contradiction is irreconcilable.

**4. Secular merit is socially verifiable.** We can observe whether an action benefits or harms human [[existence|being]]s. We cannot observe whether it pleases [[god|God]]. The secular criterion of merit is public, testable, and corrigible — the theological criterion is private, arbitrary, and incorrigible.

## Relation to Other Concepts

Merit is the secular alternative to [[Grace]] — where grace is unearned [[divinity|divine]] favor, merit is earned through contribution to human welfare. It is the evaluative [[model|standard]] that [[Morality]] requires and that [[Religion]] subverts by rewarding belief over action. [[Virtue]] is the disposition to perform meritorious actions. [[Justice]] is the social arrangement that gives merit its due. [[Free Will]] is the metaphysical precondition that theological merit presupposes but cannot coherently affirm.

On merit judged by human [[universe|nature]]:

> "We can judge of the merit of a system of morals but by its conformity with man's nature. According to this comparison, we have a right to reject it, if we find it detrimental to the welfare of mankind." (ch. CLXXII)
