# Happiness

## Definition

In Holbach's "Common Sense," happiness is not a transcendent state attained in an [[death|afterlife]] but the natural and legitimate goal of human existence in *this* [[universe|world]]. It is defined as the sustained satis[[sect|faction]] of human needs and [[passion|desire]]s within the limits set by [[universe|nature]] and social coexistence. "The aim of [[god|God]], it is said, is the happiness of our race," Holbach notes ironically; "however, the same necessity regulates the fate of all sentient [[existence|being]]s — which are born to suffer much, to enjoy little, and to die" (ch. 2). The gap between the theological promise of happiness and the actual human condition is, for Holbach, another [[evidence|proof]] that religion neither understands nor delivers what it promises.

Holbach's positive thesis is that happiness is achievable — not through prayer, [[confession|penance]], or the hope of [[hope|heaven]], but through the application of reason to human affairs, the establishment of just laws, and the [[worship|cult]]ivation of [[morality|virtue]] grounded in enlightened [[interest|self-interest]]. "[[science|Science]], reason, liberty, alone can reform them and render them more happy" (ch. 2). Happiness is thus a political and educational project, not a [[soul|spirit]]ual one.

## Historical Context

[[enlightenment|The Enlightenment]] reoriented [[morality|ethics]] around human happiness rather than [[divinity|divine]] command. The ancient Epicureans had argued that pleasure — properly understood as the absence of [[suffering|pain]] and mental disturbance — was the highest good; Holbach, like many Enlightenment thinkers, revived and secularized this tradition. Helvétius had argued that [[interest|self-interest]], properly enlightened, was the only reliable motive for moral conduct. Bentham's utilitarianism, which would make the "greatest happiness of the greatest number" the criterion of moral and legal rightness, was [[existence|being]] formulated in the same period. Holbach's distinctive contribution was to argue that religion was not merely irrelevant to human happiness but actively hostile to it — that its promises of [[hope|heaven]]ly bliss were a mechanism for ma[[monarch|king]] people tolerate earthly misery.

## Core Arguments

**1. Happiness is earthly, not [[hope|heaven]]ly.** Holbach rejects the theological deferral of happiness to an [[death|afterlife]] as a cruel [[imposture|deception]]. "The imagi[[nations|nation]] of men, who, in supposing it, have but manifested their [[passion|desire]] to live again, in order to enter upon a purer and more durable state of happiness than that which they enjoy at present" (ch. 2). The doctrine of an [[death|afterlife]] is not a [[revelation|revealed truth]] but a psychological projection of unsatisfied [[passion|desire]].

**2. [[religion|Religion]] undermines happiness.** By [[doctrine|teaching]] people to despise earthly goods, to fear imaginary punishments, and to defer their hopes to an [[death|afterlife]], religion makes people miserable in the only life they actually have. "They silence us by saying that [[god|God]] would not impart to His creatures either the indifference to the [[evil|suffering]]s of others, or [[infinite|the infinite]] happiness which He reserved for Himself alone" (ch. 2).

**3. Happiness requires knowledge and liberty.** [[person-index|People]] cannot be happy when they are ignorant of the causes that affect their well-being or when they are enslaved to tyrants and [[clergy|priests]]. Happiness is not a private mental state but a social achievement that depends on education, just [[government|governance]], and [[liberty|freedom]] of thought.

**4. [[finite|Finite]] [[existence|being]]s need finite happiness.** Holbach argues that the theological demand for "infinite happiness" is a category error. "In order to be happy, do we need an [[infinite|Infinite]] or [[theology-index|Divine]] happiness?" (ch. 2). Human [[existence|being]]s require finite goods appropriate to their finite [[universe|nature]]: health, security, companionship, intellectual stimulation, and [[liberty|freedom]] from [[persecution|oppression]].

## Reception and Critique

Holbach's eudaimonism (happiness-based [[morality|ethics]]) was criticized from multiple directions. Religious apologists accused him of reducing human life to [[soul|anima]]l gratification. Rousseau, though sharing Holbach's critique of civilization's corruptions, argued that true happiness required a transformation of the heart that reason alone could not provide. Kant would later argue that morality could not be founded on happiness, [[vice|sin]]ce happiness was too variable and subjective to serve as a universal principle — the moral law must be grounded in reason alone, independently of incli[[nations|nation]].

## Relation to Other Concepts

Happiness is the end toward which [[Reason]], [[Liberty]], and [[Justice]] are the means. It is obstructed by [[Ignorance]], [[Superstition]], and [[Tyranny]]. Holbach contrasts earthly happiness with the theological promise of [[Heaven]], which he regards as an imaginary [[hope|consolation]] for real [[evil|suffering]]. The pursuit of happiness connects to his [[Morality]], which is grounded in enlightened [[interest|self-interest]] rather than [[divinity|divine]] command, and to his [[Politics]], which judges governments by their contribution to human well-being. The [[Problem of Evil]] is, in effect, the problem of widespread unhappiness in a [[universe|world]] supposedly governed by a benevolent [[god|deity]].
