# inspiration

**Divine inspiration** is the belief that certain individuals receive revelations or guidance directly from God. Holbach treats claims of inspiration with skepticism, arguing that what saints call "inspirations from Heaven" are in fact products of vanity and pride, and that religious history is filled with accounts of "seditious saints" whose inspired zeal caused disruption. (002-338)

## Divine Inspiration and Scripture

Holbach extends his critique of inspiration to the sacred books themselves. He notes that Esdras, a priest of the law, claimed to have corrected and compiled the sacred books "by the inspiration of God Himself."

Yet Holbach responds that "there is no impostor who would not make the same claim" (002-471). The claim of divine inspiration, in his view, is a stock rhetorical device employed by every religious founder and textual redactor.

The subsequent history of scriptural corruption — books lost, burned, mutilated, and forged — further undermines any confidence in the unique inspiration of the surviving texts (002-471; APP001-014).

## Vanity and the Psychology of Inspiration

Holbach provides a psychological explanation for claims of inspiration: human vanity. A saint is driven by "the vanity of performing a role, the desire of distinguishing themselves in the eyes of the stupid vulgar by a strange conduct" (162-003; 169-031).

Pride persuades such individuals that they are extraordinary beings, chosen ones upon whom God looks with special favor. What the believer calls divine inspiration, Holbach diagnoses as "opinions or dreams which his vanity makes him accept as inspirations from Heaven" (162-003).

The annals of every religion, he observes, are filled with accounts of "anxious, intractable, seditious saints" whose inspired zeal has brought devastation rather than enlightenment (162-003).

## The Denial of Conciliar Inspiration

The critique extends to the institutional mechanisms of doctrine. Holbach points out that Church councils "did not maintain that they were of Divine inspiration" (APP004-017), undermining the claim that conciliar formulations carry divine authority.

If even the highest assemblies of the Church do not claim inspiration, the individual prophet's assertion of direct communication from God becomes all the more suspect. The entire structure of revealed religion, Holbach concludes, rests upon unverifiable claims that no rational person would accept in any other domain of life.
Holbach offers a skeptical analysis of saintly inspiration:

> A saint in the world is no more useful than a saint in the desert; the saint has an unhappy, discontented, and often irritable, turbulent disposition; his zeal often obliges him, conscientiously, to disturb society by opinions or dreams which his vanity makes him accept as inspirations from Heaven.
