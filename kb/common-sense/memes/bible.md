# Bible

## Definition

In Holbach's critical framework, the Bible — comprising the Old and New Testaments — is not a [[divinity|divine]]ly inspired text but a human document, compiled from contradictory sources, corrupted by generations of scribes and editors, and employed by [[clergy|priests]] to sustain their authority over the credulous. Holbach approaches the Bible through the lens of Enlightenment textual criticism, treating its claims to [[divinity|divine]] origin as no more credible than those made for the sacred books of any other religion.

The Bible occupies a central place in Holbach's argument because it is the ostensible foundation of [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian belief. If the Bible can be shown to be human in origin, internally contradictory, and textually unreliable, then the entire edifice of [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian doctrine collapses with it.

## Historical Context

Biblical criticism in the eighteenth century was advancing rapidly. Richard Simon's *Critical History of the Old Testament* (1678) had already [[demon|demons]]trated textual variations in biblical manuscripts, though Simon — a Catholic priest — used this to argue for the necessity of [[church|Church]] tradition as an interpretive authority. The English deists (John Toland, Anthony Collins, [[woolston|Thomas Woolston]]) went further, arguing that the Bible contained [[absurdity|absurd]]ities and contradictions incompatible with [[divinity|divine]] authorship. [[baruch-spinoza|Spinoza]]'s *Tractatus Theologico-Politicus* (1670) treated the Bible as a historical document to be studied like any other ancient text.

Holbach's treatment in *Common Sense* and particularly in Meslier's *[[testament|Testament]]* (which Holbach edited and published) represents the radical culmi[[nations|nation]] of this tradition. Drawing on Meslier's detailed biblical scholarship, Holbach catalogues the Bible's textual corruption, its internal contradictions, and — most devastatingly — the historical process by which different [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian [[sect|sects]] selected, rejected, and modified scriptures to suit their doctrinal interests.

## Core Arguments

**1. The biblical canon was shaped by [[sect|faction]]al politics, not [[divinity|divine]] guidance.** Holbach (via Meslier) documents the chaotic process by which the Bible was assembled: the Marcionites rejected the Old Testament entirely; the Ebionites accepted only [[matthew|Matthew]]'s [[gospel|Gospel]]; Carpocrates's followers rejected the whole Old Testament; the Manicheans wrote their own gospel. "Thus there were," as [[jerome|St. Jerome]] admitted, "as many different models as different copies of [[gospel|the Gospel]]s."

**2. The Old Testament [[prophecy|prophet]]s contradict each other.** Holbach observes that the [[prophecy|prophet]]s "reproach each other for spea[[monarch|king]] falsely in the name of [[god|God]]" and all warn "Beware of the false [[prophecy|prophet]]s!" — just as modern charlatans warn against counterfeit products. The prophecies themselves are self-serving [[nations|nation]]al fantasies: "No prophecy in favor of their Jewish [[nations|nation]] was [[eve]]r fulfilled."

**3. Allegorical interpretation is a cover for [[absurdity|absurd]]ity.** Holbach mocks the [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian practice of interpreting Old Testament passages allegorically to make them prefigure New Testament [[eve]]nts. He compares the method to interpreting Don Quixote: one could find equally profound "[[mysteries]] and ridiculous figures" in Cervantes if one approached it with the same hermeneutic [[belief|credulity]]. The entire [[christianity|Christian religion]], he concludes, rests on this "[[absurdity|absurd]] foundation" of forced allegorical readings.

## In the Text

Holbach's critique of biblical textual corruption appears most fully in Meslier's [[testament|Testament]] (ch. II):

> "Generally, authors who transcribe this kind of histories, take the right to enlarge or to retrench all they please, in order to serve their own interests. ... Their saint Jerome, a famous philosopher among them, formally said in several passages of his 'Prologues,' that they had been corrupted and falsified; being, even in his day, in the hands of all kinds of persons, who added and suppressed whatever they pleased; so, 'Thus there were,' said he, 'as many different models as different copies of the Gospels.'" (002-470)

On the self-contradicting [[prophecy|prophet]]s (ch. V):

> "What can be yet a greater proof of the falsity of these pretended prophecies, than the violence with which these prophets reproach each other for speaking falsely in the name of God, reproaches which they claim to make in behalf of God. All of them say, 'Beware of the false prophets!' as the quacks say, 'Beware of the counterfeit pills!'" (APP005-003)

On allegorical [[absurdity|absurd]]ity (ch. VI):

> "We can say that the prophecies of the Old Testament adjusted to the New, would be very absurd and puerile things. ... Who would not laugh at such a ridiculous doctrine? ... It is nevertheless upon this absurd foundation that the whole Christian religion rests."

## Relation to Other Concepts

The Bible connects to [[prophecy]], the predictive claims that Holbach treats as failed [[nations|nation]]al fantasies rather than [[revelation|divine revelation]]. It relates to [[miracle]], [[supernatural|the supernatural]] [[eve]]nts narrated in scripture that Holbach compares to pagan miracle stories. The concept links to [[testament]] — the literary genre through which Meslier (and Holbach) launched their critique of biblical religion — and to [[revelation]], the claim of [[divinity|divine]] communication that Holbach reduces to natural psychological processes.
