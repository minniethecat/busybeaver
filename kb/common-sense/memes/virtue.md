# Virtue

## Definition

Holbach undertakes a radical naturalization of [[morality|virtue]]. Where [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian [[religion|theology]] defines [[morality|virtue]] as conformity to [[divinity|divine]] command — a gift of grace that fallen human [[universe|nature]] cannot achi[[eve]] unaided — Holbach argues that [[morality|virtue]] is a purely human phenomenon: the set of dispositions and actions that promote individual and collective [[happiness|well-being]]. "To be virtuous is to have the disposition to contribute to the welfare of our species" (ch. CIX). Virtue requires no [[revelation|divine revelation]]; it is discoverable by reason, motivated by [[interest|self-interest]] properly understood, and reinforced by social institutions.

This naturalization was profoundly threatening to the religious establishment because it implied that [[atheism|atheist]]s could be virtuous — that morality does not depend on [[theism|belief in God]]. Holbach makes this implication explicit: "The [[atheism|atheist]], if he is a reasoning [[existence|being]], will be more virtuous than the superstitious man, because he has nothing to hope for after this life" and therefore must find his happiness entirely in good conduct here on earth.

## Historical Context

Holbach's naturalization of [[morality|virtue]] belongs to a broader Enlightenment project of secularizing [[morality|ethics]]. Shaftesbury had argued for a "[[conscience|moral sense]]" innate in human [[universe|nature]]. Hutcheson had developed this into a systematic theory of moral sentiments. [[david-hume|Hume]] had argued that moral distinctions derive from sympathy, not reason alone, but still without need of [[divinity|divine]] command. Holbach's contribution was the most radical: he argued not only that [[morality|virtue]] can exist without [[god|God]] but that [[theism|belief in God]] actively corrupts [[morality|virtue]] by substituting fear of punishment for genuine moral motivation.

## Core Arguments

**1. Virtue is natural, not supernatural.** Human [[existence|being]]s are born with needs and [[passion|desire]]s that can be satisfied only in society. Virtue consists in harmonizing individual [[passion|desire]]s with social requirements — a practical problem, not a theological one. "[[morality|Morality]] acquired by philosophy is sufficient to [[morality|virtue]]" (ch. CX).

**2. Self-interest properly understood leads to [[morality|virtue]].** Holbach is a kind of enlightened egoist: he argues that individual happiness is the natural goal of [[eve]]ry human [[existence|being]], but that this goal is unattainable without the cooperation of others. Virtue is therefore the most reliable path to personal happiness — not because it is rewarded in [[hope|heaven]] but because it makes life in society possible and pleasant.

**3. [[religion|Religion]] corrupts [[morality|virtue]].** Religious [[morality|virtue]] is not [[morality|virtue]] at all because it is motivated by fear of punishment and hope of reward, not by genuine concern for human welfare. Worse, religion often labels as virtuous what is actually harmful: fasting, self-mortification, withdrawal from society, [[cruelty|persecution]] of [[heresy|heretic]]s. "The [[morality|virtue]]s that religion recommends are either useless or dangerous to society" (ch. CXXI).

**4. [[atheist|Atheist]]s can be more virtuous than believers.** Because [[atheism|atheist]]s expect no posthumous reward, their virtuous actions are genuinely disinterested in [[supernatural|the supernatural]] sense — they are done for their own sake or for the sake of human welfare. The believer who acts well only to avoid [[punishment|hell]] or gain [[hope|heaven]] is not truly virtuous but merely prudent.

## Reception and Critique

Holbach's claim that [[atheism|atheist]]s can be virtuous was among his most controversial. It provoked refutations from Rousseau, who argued that without belief in an [[death|afterlife]], no one would risk their life for moral principle, and from [[voltaire|Voltaire]], who thought religion necessary for [[society|social order]] [[eve]]n if philosophically indefensible. The subsequent history of secular [[morality|ethics]] — from Mill to [[sin|Sin]]ger — has largely vindicated Holbach's position: moral motivation does not require supernatural belief. Whether [[interest|self-interest]] properly understood can ground the *whole* of morality remains contested.

## Relation to Other Concepts

Virtue is the practical expression of [[Morality]], which is the theoretical framework of right action. It is opposed to [[Sin]], which is the theological category that Holbach finds incoherent outside a supernatural framework. Virtue is [[worship|cult]]ivated by [[Education]], protected by [[Justice]], and undermined by [[Superstition]], which redirects moral energy toward useless or harmful religious practices. The virtuous person exemplifies [[Reason]] in action, and the virtuous society is one that has been freed from the corruption of [[Priestcraft]].

## In the Text

On the naturalness of [[morality|virtue]]:

> "To be virtuous is to have the disposition to contribute to the welfare of our species. To be vicious is to have the disposition to injure it. These definitions are simple, they are founded on the nature of man and of society." (ch. CIX)

On the sufficiency of philosophical morality:

> "We have proved that morality, acquired by philosophy, is quite sufficient to conduct men to virtue; that religion does not render them better; that the fear of God makes nothing but hypocrites, enthusiasts, or fanatics." (ch. CX)

On the uselessness of religious [[morality|virtue]]s:

> "What are the virtues that religion recommends? They are either useless or dangerous to society. It orders man to fly the world, to hate himself, to live in solitude, to macerate his body — all virtues destructive of his being and of society." (ch. CXXI)

On the [[atheism|atheist]]'s [[morality|virtue]]:

> "The atheist, if he reasons soundly, will be more virtuous than the superstitious man, because he expects nothing after this life. He occupies himself only with his present happiness, and his happiness can only consist in conducting himself well." (ch. CLXXI)
