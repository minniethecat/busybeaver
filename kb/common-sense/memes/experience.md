# Experience

## Definition

Experience, in Holbach's "Common Sense," is the process by which human [[existence|being]]s acquire knowledge through the exercise of their senses and practical engagement with the [[earth|material world]]. It stands as the epistemological bedrock of his [[materialism|materialist philosophy]]: "[[eve]]ry principle is a judgment; all judgment is the effect of experience; experience is not acquired but by the exercise of the senses." (ch. II). Unlike the abstract speculations of [[religion|theology]], which Holbach dismisses as "drawn from nothing," experience provides the only reliable path to [[knowledge|understanding]] [[universe|nature]] and human affairs.

## Historical Context

The elevation of experience as the foundation of knowledge was central to the Enlightenment's break with scholastic rationalism and theological authority. [[john-locke|John Locke]]'s *Essay Concerning Human [[mind|Understanding]]* (1689) had argued that the mind at birth is a *tabula rasa*, filled only by sensory experience. [[david-hume|David Hume]] extended this empiricism to its skeptical conclusions, questioning whether [[cause|causality]] itself could be known through experience alone. Holbach draws on this empiricist tradition but gives it a distinctively political inflection: the suppression of experience by priestly authority is, in his analysis, the mechanism by which [[despotism|tyranny]] maintains its grip. When men are taught to "mistrust experience" and "fear truth," they become intellectually dependent and politically docile.

## Core Arguments

**1. Experience is the sole origin of all ideas.** "Every principle is a judgment; all judgment is the effect of experience." Holbach rejects the doctrine of innate ideas entirely. Religious concepts, having no empirical basis, are "drawn from nothing" — mere phantoms of the imagi[[nations|nation]], sustained not by [[proof|evidence]] but by institutional repetition and the suppression of critical inquiry.

**2. The suppression of experience enables [[tyranny|despotism]].** In one of his most forceful passages, Holbach describes the psychological mechanism of intellectual subjugation: "The human mind, perplexed by these theological opinions, misunderstood itself, doubted its own powers, mistrusted experience, feared truth, disdained its reason, and left it to blindly follow authority. Man was a pure machine in the hands of his tyrants and his [[clergy|priests]]." (ch. II). The verb "mistrusted" is revealing — it implies active discouragement, not mere absence of knowledge.

**3. Inexperience produces superstition.** "Ignorant men, attribute to [[soul|spirit]]s all the effects of which their inexperience prevents them from discovering the true causes." (ch. II). The primitive mind, lac[[monarch|king]] accumulated experience, defaults to supernatural expla[[nations|nation]]. Holbach's solution is not ridicule but education: expand experience, and superstition recedes.

**4. Experience refutes theological optimism.** Against the claim that [[providence|divine providence]] ensures universal happiness, Holbach appeals directly to observable reality: "Experience contradicts this sublime revery. In [[earth|the world]] where we are, we see all sentient [[existence|being]]s living and [[evil|suffering]] in the midst of dangers." (ch. II). The appeal to experience is thus not merely epistemological but also moral: it exposes theologies of [[hope|consolation]] as false.

## Reception and Critique

Holbach's radical empiricism was criticized [[eve]]n within Enlightenment circles. Rousseau argued that some moral sentiments — particularly pity — were innate rather than acquired through experience. Kant's critical philosophy sought to identify a priori categories of [[knowledge|understanding]] that structure experience itself, rejecting the empiricist claim that experience is the sole source of knowledge. Later, Hegel would argue that "sense-certainty" is the poorest form of knowledge, requiring dialectical mediation by reason.

## Relation to Other Concepts

Experience is the epistemological foundation that makes [[Reason]] possible: reason processes what experience provides. It is opposed to [[Faith]], which demands belief without experiential [[proof|evidence]], and to [[Revelation]], which claims knowledge from supernatural sources. Experience supports [[Science]] — the systematic organization of experiential knowledge — and underw[[ritual|rites]] Holbach's critique of [[God]] as "an idea without model," a concept with no empirical referent. The suppression of experience is directly linked to the maintenance of [[Tyranny]] and the perpetuation of [[Ignorance]].

## In the Text

Holbach grounds his epistemology in the primacy of sensory experience:

> "Every principle is a judgment; all judgment is the effect of experience; experience is not acquired but by the exercise of the senses: from which it follows that religious principles are drawn from nothing, and are not innate." (ch. II)

On the consequences of suppressing experiential knowledge:

> "The human mind, perplexed by these theological opinions, misunderstood itself, doubted its own powers, mistrusted experience, feared truth, disdained its reason, and left it to blindly follow authority." (ch. II)

On experience as the antidote to superstition:

> "Ignorant men, attribute to spirits all the effects of which their inexperience prevents them from discovering the true causes." (ch. II)
