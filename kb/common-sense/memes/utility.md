# Utility

## Definition

Utility, in Holbach's naturalistic [[morality|ethics]], is the principle that the value of actions, institutions, and beliefs should be measured by their contribution to human welfare and social [[happiness|well-being]]. This forms the foundation of his [[morality|moral philosophy]]: what benefits human [[existence|being]]s is good; what harms them is [[injustice|evil]]. Holbach explicitly rejects the idea that [[morality]] requires religious foundation, arguing instead that the natural [[passion|desire]] for [[happiness]] and aversion to [[pain]] provide a sufficient and universal basis for ethical judgment. The principle of utility thus serves as the bridge between Holbach's materialist metaphysics — which reduces human [[existence|being]]s to natural organisms see[[monarch|king]] [[happiness|well-being]] — and his ethical and political program.

Holbach's formulation anticipates the classical utilitarianism of [[Jeremy Bentham]] (who acknowledged Holbach's influence) and John Stuart Mill, though Holbach is less systematic in his development of the principle. For Holbach, utility is not a calculus to be applied to individual actions but a general criterion for evaluating social institutions — above all, [[religion]], which he judges to be not merely false but positively harmful to human welfare.

## In Holbach's Ethics

Holbach's ethical thought operates on three interconnected claims. First, human [[existence|being]]s are natural organisms whose fundamental [[desire|drive]]s are toward [[pleasure]] and away from [[pain]] — these [[desire|drive]]s are neither [[sin|sinful]] nor virtuous in themselves but simply facts of human [[universe|nature]]. Second, [[morality]] consists in harmonizing individual pursuits of [[happiness|well-being]] with the welfare of others, [[guilt|sin]]ce no person can flourish in isolation. Third, the criterion for judging any institution, [[law]], or [[belief]] is its contribution to this harmonization — its utility.

This framework allows Holbach to mount a devastating critique of religious morality. Religious systems, he argues, substitute arbitrary [[divinity|divine]] commands for the natural measure of human welfare. They praise as virtuous what is useless or harmful (asceticism, [[mortification]], monastic withdrawal) and condemn as [[sin|sinful]] what is natural and beneficial (the pursuit of [[pleasure]], the exercise of [[reason]], the demand for [[justice]]). The doctrine of an [[afterlife]], which Holbach discusses at length, is particularly pernicious because it "renders men indifferent to the things they possess" — the promise of a future [[happiness]] devalues the only life human [[existence|being]]s will [[eve]]r actually experience.

> "If the future is of no real utility to the human race, it is at least of the greatest advantage to those who take upon themselves the responsibility of conducting mankind thither." (ch. CVII)

## Political Utility

Holbach extends the principle of utility from [[morality|ethics]] to politics. A legitimate [[government]] is one that contributes to the welfare of the governed; a government that harms its subjects — through [[tyranny]], [[war]], or the protection of priestly privilege — forfeits its claim to [[authority]]. This aligns Holbach with the social contract tradition of [[Locke]] and [[Rousseau]], but with a distinctively utilitarian inflection: the contract is not a historical [[eve]]nt but an ongoing test of institutional usefulness.

The principle of utility also informs Holbach's analysis of specific political doctrines. The [[divine-right|divine right of kings]] fails the utility test because it protects [[monarch|ruler]]s from accountability, enabling [[persecution|oppression]]. The [[throne-and-altar|throne-and-altar]] alliance fails because it subordinates public welfare to the interests of two self-serving elites. Even [[toleration]], which Holbach supports, is defended on utilitarian grounds — not as a matter of abstract rights but because religious conflict harms social peace and diverts attention from genuine human needs.

Holbach's political application of utility is particularly evident in his discussion of [[law]]. [[law|Law]]s should be judged by their effects on human welfare, not by their conformity to religious doctrine or tradition. A law against [[blasphemy]], for example, serves no genuine social function — it protects no one from harm, advances no legitimate interest, and exists solely to shield [[clergy|the clergy]] from rational criticism. Holbach's utilitarian [[model|standard]] thus becomes a tool for dismantling the legal apparatus of religious coercion.

## Historical Context

Holbach developed his utilitarian [[morality|ethics]] in conversation with — and often in opposition to — several philosophical traditions. From [[epicureanism]], he drew the identification of good with pleasure and [[injustice|evil]] with [[suffering|pain]], though he gave this ancient doctrine a social and political dimension it had lacked. From [[spinozism]], he derived the conviction that human [[universe|nature]] operates according to deterministic laws, ma[[monarch|king]] ethical judgment a matter of [[knowledge|understanding]] causes rather than assigning blame.

Holbach's most direct influence was Claude-Adrien Helvétius, whose *De l'Esprit* (1758) argued that [[interest|self-interest]] is the sole motive of human action and that legislation should align private interest with public good through a system of rewards and punishments. Holbach adopted Helvétius's framework while extending it in a more radically anti-religious direction: where Helvétius allowed that religion might serve as a useful auxiliary to secular morality, Holbach insisted that religion was inherently opposed to utility because it directed human attention toward imaginary goods ([[salvation]], [[heaven]]) at the expense of real ones.

Jeremy Bentham, who lived in [[paris|Paris]] during the 1780s and moved in circles influenced by Holbach's ideas, later acknowledged the French materialists as precursors to his own utilitarian system. The line from Holbach through Bentham to Mill represents one of the central intellectual genealogies of modern secular [[morality|ethics]].

## Critique

Holbach's utilitarian [[morality|ethics]], while historically influential, face several philosophical challenges. First, critics have argued that reducing morality to the pursuit of welfare requires a more precise account of what "welfare" consists in than Holbach provides. His appeal to "happiness" and "utility" operates at a level of generality that leaves diffi[[worship|cult]] cases unresolved — how are competing claims of utility to be weighed when they conflict?

Second, Holbach's assumption that human [[existence|being]]s naturally converge on socially beneficial behavior when freed from religious distortion is arguably optimistic. The "invisible hand" mechanism that [[adam|Adam]] Smith proposed — by which [[interest|self-interest]]ed actions produce collectively beneficial outcomes — is absent from Holbach's system, leaving a gap between individual motivation and social good.

Third, some modern philosophers have questioned whether utility alone provides an adequate foundation for [[rights]]. If rights are justified only by their contribution to aggregate welfare, they may be suspended whenever doing so maximizes utility — a conclusion that Bentham embraced (he called [[rights|natural rights]] "[[absurdity|nonsense]] upon stilts") but that many find morally objectionable. Holbach himself seems torn between a utilitarian calculus and a more robust conception of [[natural-rights|natural rights]], never fully resolving the tension between the two frameworks.
