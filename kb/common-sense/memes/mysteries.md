# Mysteries

## Definition

Religious mysteries, in Baron d'Holbach's analysis, are [[doctrine|theological doctrine]]s deliberately constructed to be incomprehensible to [[reason|human reason]]. Far from [[existence|being]] genuine disclosures of transcendent truths, mysteries function as instruments of intellectual control: by declaring certain propositions to be beyond rational scrutiny, [[clergy|the clergy]] place themselves as the sole authorized interpreters of the [[divinity|divine]], while simultaneously conditioning believers to accept [[absurdity|absurd]]ities without question. Holbach identifies the [[worship|cult]]ivation of [[secrecy|mystery]] as one of the three pillars of [[priest|priestcraft]] — alongside fear and hope — that together produce the psychological state of [[belief|credulity]] upon which organized religion depends.

## Historical Context

The [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian theological tradition had developed an elaborate taxonomy of mysteries — the [[trinity|Trinity]], the [[incarnation|Incarnation]], the [[mass|Eucharist]], the [[atonement|Atonement]] — each declared to be "above reason but not contrary to reason" (Aquinas). The [[council-of-trent|Council of Trent]] (1545–1563) reaffirmed the [[secrecy|mystery]]-status of core Catholic doctrines in explicit opposition to Protestant reformers who sought to make [[bible|scripture]] rationally accessible. By the eighteenth century, the [[secrecy|mystery]]-doctrine had become a primary target of Enlightenment critique. Deists like John Toland argued in *[[christianity|Christianity]] Not Mysterious* (1696) that the [[bible|gospels]] contained no mysteries; radical Enlightenment figures like Holbach went further, arguing that the very category of "[[secrecy|mystery]]" was a priestly invention designed to disable the critical faculties of the laity.

Holbach's treatment of mysteries in *Common Sense* dedicates four consecutive chapters (CXI–CXIV) to the topic, underscoring its importance to his broader polemic. He draws on a classical precedent as well — the epigraph to the [[secrecy|mystery]] chapters quotes Petronius's *Satyricon*, suggesting that priestly exploitation of [[secrecy|mystery]] is not uniquely [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian but a recurring feature of religious institutions across [[worship|cult]]ures.

## Core Arguments

Holbach's first argument is epistemological: a genuine [[secrecy|mystery]] is indistinguishable from an [[absurdity|absurd]]ity. If a doctrine cannot be understood, it cannot be distinguished from a falsehood; if it contradicts the [[proof|evidence]] of the senses — as the doctrine of transubstantiation does — it violates the only reliable instruments of knowledge humans possess. The appeal to [[secrecy|mystery]] is therefore not an expla[[nations|nation]] but a confession of intellectual bankruptcy.

Holbach's second argument is sociological: mysteries create a structural dependency between [[priest|clergy]] and laity. Because the laity cannot understand the mysteries, they must rely on [[clergy|priests]] to explain them; because the expla[[nation|nations]] are themselves incomprehensible, the laity remain permanently dependent. This epistemic hierarchy mirrors and reinforces the political hierarchy of throne and altar: just as subjects must obey [[monarch|king]]s whose authority they cannot question, believers must accept doctrines whose content they cannot comprehend. The common principle is the prohibition of independent thought.

Holbach's third argument is practical and emancipatory. He contrasts the "road of truth" — which is "straight," intelligible, and open to all — with the "road of imposture," which is "oblique and dark." The remedy for mysteries is not better [[religion|theology]] but the total abandonment of theological speculation in favor of [[science|natural philosophy]].

## Reception and Critique

Holbach's analysis of religious mysteries has been recognized as an important contribution to the epistemology of [[belief|religious belief]], though its uncompromising rationalism has drawn criticism. [[peter|Peter]] Gay (*[[enlightenment|The Enlightenment]]: An Interpretation*, Vol. 2, 1969) identifies Holbach's attack on [[secrecy|mystery]] as the logical endpoint of the Enlightenment's demand that all beliefs be warranted by [[proof|evidence]] — a position that, taken to its conclusion, excludes not only [[religion|theology]] but any claim that cannot be adjudicated by empirical observation. This has led critics from the Romantic era onward to charge that Holbach's rationalism is reductive, failing to acknowledge dimensions of human experience — aesthetic, [[passion|emotion]]al, relational — that resist propositional formulation.

A related critique comes from the philosophy of language. Holbach treats "[[secrecy|mystery]]" as synonymous with "[[absurdity|nonsense]]," but this equation depends on a verificationist theory of meaning — the claim that a proposition is meaningful only if it can be empirically verified — that was not rigorously defended until the logical positivists of the early twentieth century. Whether Holbach's Enlightenment predecessors ([[john-locke|Locke]], [[david-hume|Hume]]) provided sufficient grounds for this equation is contested.

Nevertheless, Holbach's functional analysis of [[secrecy|mystery]] as an instrument of social control has been independently rediscovered by later sociological traditions. Pierre Bourdieu's concept of "symbolic power" — the capacity to impose categories of thought that serve the interests of dominant groups — describes the same mechanism Holbach identified in [[clergy|the clergy]]'s monopoly on [[secrecy|mystery]]-interpretation. Michel Foucault's analyses of the interconnection between knowledge and power similarly echo Holbach's claim that epistemological hierarchies (who is authorized to know and interpret) are simultaneously political hierarchies.

## Relation to Other Concepts

Mysteries are the epistemological mechanism that enables the broader system of [[priestcraft]] — without declared-incomprehensible doctrines, [[clergy|the clergy]] would have no exclusive interpretative domain from which to exercise intellectual authority. The concept connects directly to [[transubstantiation]], [[trinity]], and [[incarnation]] as specific instantiations of the [[secrecy|mystery]]-strategy in [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian doctrine. The epistemological critique of mysteries connects to [[reason]] and [[evidence]] — the cognitive instruments that [[secrecy|mystery]] declares inadequate and that Holbach defends as the only reliable path to knowledge. The sociological dimension of mysteries — their function in producing intellectual dependency — connects to [[credulity]] and [[ignorance]], the psychological states that mysteries [[worship|cult]]ivate and require. Holbach contrasts the "oblique and dark" road of [[secrecy|mystery]] with the "straight" road of [[truth]], ma[[monarch|king]] [[secrecy|mystery]] the negative pole of his epistemological binary.

## In the Text

Holbach opens the [[secrecy|mystery]] chapters (CXI–CXIV) with a classical epigraph from Petronius: "Detexit quo dolose Vaticinandi furore sacerdotes mysteria, illis spe ignota, audactur publicant" — "He exposed the cunning fury with which [[clergy|priests]] publish mysteries, unknown to those hopeful of them." This framing establishes that Holbach sees himself as continuing an ancient tradition of exposing [[imposture|priestly deception]].

In these chapters, Holbach catalogs the specific mysteries of [[christianity|Christianity]] — the [[trinity|Trinity]], [[incarnation|Incarnation]], Redemption, and [[mass|Eucharist]] — and argues that each generates [[contradiction|logical contradiction]]s that would be rejected as [[absurdity|absurd]] in any non-religious context.

> "They need obscurity, mysteries, fables, miracles, incredible things, which keep the mind in a perpetual infancy." (002-012)

> "Let the vain chimeras which beset the people be dissipated, and very soon rational opinions will fill the minds of those who were believed destined never to have them." (002-002)
