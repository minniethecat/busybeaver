# Egypt

## Definition

In Holbach's "Common Sense," **Egypt** functions as a recurring anthropological reference point — the [[model|exemplar]]y ancient civilization whose religious practices [[demon|demons]]trate the [[absurdity|absurd]] origins of all [[religion|religious]] worship. The "wise Egyptians" — a phrase dripping with Enlightenment irony — worshiped crocodiles, rats, and onions, providing Holbach with an argument from [[worship|cult]]ural comparison: if [[paris|French]] Catholics mock Egyptian [[idolatry|idolatry]], by what [[model|standard]] is their own worship of a consecrated bread less [[absurdity|absurd]]?

## The Anthropological Argument

Holbach's use of Egypt is part of a broader comparative strategy:

> "If the ancient barbarians have worshiped mountains, rivers, serpents, trees, fetishes of every kind; if the wise Egyptians worshiped crocodiles, rats, onions, do we not see nations who believe themselves wiser than they, worship with reverence a bread, into which they imagine that the divinity has descended?" (ch. II, ch. 120)

The argument is devastating in its simplicity: [[worship|cult]]ural distance makes foreign [[religion|religions]] look [[absurdity|absurd]], but proximity makes one's own invisible. The Egyptian who bows before a crocodile and the [[catholic-church|Catholic]] who genuflects before the [[mass|Eucharist]] are engaged in the same cognitive operation — treating a material object as a [[divinity|divine]] presence. The only difference is familiarity.

## Egyptian Priests and the Origin of Obscurantism

Holbach traces the origin of philosophical obscurantism to the priestly classes of the ancient Near East:

> "It was among Egyptian, Indian, Chaldean priests; it was in the schools of these dreamers, interested by profession in dethroning human reason, that philosophy was obliged to borrow its first rudiments."
>
> (002-435)(ch. II)

Here Egypt is not merely an example of primitive [[superstition|superstition]] but the *source* of a intellectual tradition that Holbach sees as corrupting: the priestly manufacture of obscure doctrines designed to disable [[reason|reason]] and perpetuate dependence on a professional class of interpreters. Greek philosophy — usually celebrated by the [[enlightenment|Enlightenment]] as the dawn of rational thought — is here indicted as having borrowed from Egyptian [[priesthood|priestcraft]] rather than from empirical observation.

## The Comparative Method

Holbach's Egypt exemplifies the Enlightenment's comparative method in the study of [[religion|religion]]. By placing Egyptian, [[india|India]]n, Chaldean, Hebrew, [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian, and [[islam|Islam]]ic practices side by side, he invites the reader to see them all as variations on a [[guilt|sin]]gle human phenomenon: the projection of fears, hopes, and ignorance onto imagined [[divinity|divine]] [[existence|being]]s. Egypt is the oldest and therefore the most transparent case — its [[soul|anima]]l-headed gods and vegetable-[[divinity|deities]] are too obviously [[absurdity|absurd]] to require refutation. The strategy is to make [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ianity look Egyptian: equally human, equally contingent, equally [[absurdity|absurd]].
