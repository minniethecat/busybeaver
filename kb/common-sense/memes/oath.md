# Oath

## Definition

The oath, in Holbach's analysis, is a religious mechanism that purports to bind human conduct by invoking a divine witness. It is the practice of calling upon God to attest to the truth of one's words or the sincerity of one's promises. Holbach treats the oath not as a genuine moral instrument but as a revealing test case for the claim that religion is necessary for social order. If belief in God is what makes the oath binding, then an atheist — who cannot seriously attest the Deity — would be incapable of entering into binding commitments. Holbach argues that this consequence does not follow, because the oath adds nothing to the obligations that already arise from reason, self-interest, and the esteem of one's fellow human beings.

The concept of the oath occupies a strategic position in Holbach's broader argument against the social necessity of religion. The oath is often cited by defenders of religion as proof that society cannot function without belief in a punishing God. By demonstrating that the oath is in fact an empty formality — observed most punctiliously by those most willing to violate it — Holbach turns this argument against its proponents and exposes the psychological mechanisms by which religious institutions claim credit for moral constraints that arise from entirely secular sources.

## Core Arguments

**1. The oath adds no real obligation.** Holbach asks: "Does the oath place us under stronger obligations to the engagements which we make?" (193-001). The answer is no. A person who is willing to lie will also be willing to perjure themselves. The fear of divine punishment adds nothing to the fear of human consequences for someone who already disregards the opinion of their fellow beings. "Whoever dares to lie, will he not dare to perjure himself? He who is base enough to violate his word, or unjust enough to break his promises in contempt of the esteem of men, will not be more faithful for having taken all the Gods as witnesses to his oaths" (193-001).

**2. The oath is most used by those most likely to violate it.** Holbach observes a pointed irony: "Are not princes, of all mortals, the most prompt in taking oaths, and the most prompt in violating them?" (193-001). Those with the greatest power — and therefore the least accountability to their fellow human beings — are precisely those who wrap their commitments in the most solemn religious language. The oath does not restrain power; it decorates it. This observation reflects Holbach's broader critique that religious forms serve the interests of the powerful by providing a veneer of sacredness to commitments they have no intention of keeping.

**3. True obligation comes from human esteem, not divine fear.** Holbach's positive account of obligation is secular and social. We keep our promises because we value the good opinion of those around us and because our long-term self-interest depends on being regarded as trustworthy. The person who has risen above the judgments of other human beings will soon rise above the judgments of God as well: "Those who rank themselves above the judgments of men, will soon put themselves above the judgments of God" (193-001). Divine judgment is no backstop for those who have already escaped human accountability.

## In the Text

The oath is treated most directly in Chapter 193, where Holbach refutes the claim that atheism destroys the bonds of society:

> Atheism, we are told, breaks all social ties. Without belief in God, what becomes of the sacredness of the oath? How can we bind an atheist who can not seriously attest the Deity? But does the oath place us under stronger obligations to the engagements which we make? Whoever dares to lie, will he not dare to perjure himself?
>
> (193-001)
