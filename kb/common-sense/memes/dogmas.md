# Dogmas

## Definition

**Dogmas** are fixed, authoritative doctrines promulgated by religious institutions as [[divinity|divine]]ly [[revelation|revealed truth]]s, demanding unconditional [[belief|assent]] from believers. In Holbach's materialist analysis, [[dogma|dogmas]] are not [[divinity|divine]] communications but human inventions — products of ignorance, fear, and priestly ambition that solidify into compulsory [[creed|articles of faith]] enforced by social pressure and the threat of [[hell|eternal punishment]].

## Historical Context

The term "[[doctrine|dogma]]" derives from the Greek *[[doctrine|dogma]]* (δόγμα), meaning "that which seems good" or "opinion." In the early [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian [[church|Church]], [[dogma|dogmas]] were formalized through ecumenical councils (Nicaea 325 CE, Constantinople 381 CE, etc.) that codified doctrines such as the [[trinity|Trinity]], the [[incarnation|Incarnation]], and [[sin|original sin]]. These formulations emerged not from revelation but from political negotiation among competing theological [[sect|faction]]s — a process Holbach explicitly recognized as human, not [[divinity|divine]]. By the 18th century, the proliferation of [[doctrine|dogma]]tic systems across [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian [[sect|denomination]]s (Catholic, Lutheran, Calvinist, Anglican) [[demon|demons]]trated for Enlightenment thinkers that [[dogma|dogmas]] were [[worship|cult]]ural products, not universal truths: each sect declared its own [[dogma|dogmas]] infallible while denouncing others' as heresy.

## Core Arguments

Holbach's critique of [[dogma|dogmas]] proceeds along three lines. First, **[[dogma|dogmas]] are epistemologically empty** — they are propositions about objects ([[god|God]], soul, [[death|afterlife]]) that lie beyond all possible experience, ma[[monarch|king]] them neither verifiable nor falsifiable. Second, **[[dogma|dogmas]] are psychologically harmful** — the demand for unquestioning belief trains the mind in intellectual submission, weakening the capacity for rational inquiry that Holbach considered essential to [[happiness|human flourishing]]. Third, **[[dogma|dogmas]] are politically instrumental** — [[clergy|priests]] invented the [[doctrine|dogma]] of eternal [[torture|torment]] (066-002) specifically to [[fear|terror]]ize believers into obedience, while [[monarch|sovereign]]s use [[dogma|religious dogma]]s to legitimize [[government|political authority]]. The [[doctrine|dogma]] of the [[absolution|remission of sins]] (ch. CLXV) serves the priestly interest by ma[[monarch|king]] salvation a commodity administered exclusively by [[clergy|the clergy]].

## In the Text

> "The inventors of the dogma of eternal torments in hell, have made of the God whom they call so good, the most detestable of beings. Cruelty in man is the last term of corruption." (066-002)

Holbach devotes multiple chapters to specific [[dogma|dogmas]], treating each as a case study in priestly fabrication. In ch. LXVI he analyzes the [[doctrine|dogma]] of eternal [[punishment|hell]]fire, arguing it transforms [[god|God]] into a cosmic tyrant more cruel than any human [[despotism|despot]]. In ch. CVII–CVIII he critiques the [[doctrine|dogma]] of an [[death|afterlife]] as serving only priestly interests. In ch. CLXV he traces the [[doctrine|dogma]] of the [[absolution|remission of sins]] to its institutional function of binding believers to the sacramental system.

> "I spoke to you as rarely as possible of our unfortunate dogmas. It was necessary that I should acquit myself as a priest of my ministry, but how often have I not suffered within myself when I was forced to preach to you those pious lies which I despised in my heart." (*Testament of Jean Meslier*)

Holbach quotes Meslier's deathbed confession at length in his preface. A lifelong priest admitting to despising the very [[dogma|dogmas]] he preached — this is Holbach's most powerful [[proof|evidence]] that [[dogma|dogmas]] are sustained not by conviction but by institutional inertia and fear of social consequences.
