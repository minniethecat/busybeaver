# Time

## Definition

In Holbach's *Common Sense*, the concept of time is treated primarily through its theological deformation: the [[doctrine|dogma]] of [[eternal|eternity]] and, more specifically, the doctrine of [[hell|eternal punishment]]. While Holbach does not develop a systematic philosophy of time, his critique of the theological use of eternal duration constitutes one of the most rhetorically powerful sections of the work. The argument is both metaphysical and moral: an infinite [[existence|being]] cannot coherently inflict infinite punishment upon finite creatures for finite [[crime|offense]]s, and the very idea of "eternal [[torture|torment]]s" reveals the [[god|God]] of [[religion|theology]] to be "more wicked than the most wicked of men" (066-002). Time, in Holbach's hands, becomes a measure of theological [[absurdity|absurd]]ity — the longer the claimed duration of [[punishment|divine punishment]], the more monstrous the [[god|God]] who administers it.

## Historical Context

The doctrine of [[hell|eternal punishment]] had been a central instrument of ecclesiastical control throughout [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian history. By the 18th century, however, this doctrine faced increasing philosophical criticism. [[gottfried-leibniz|Leibniz]]'s *Theodicy* (1710) had attempted to reconcile eternal [[hell|damnation]] with [[goodness|divine goodness]] through the concept of metaphysical [[injustice|evil]]; more radical thinkers abandoned the attempt at reconciliation altogether. The doctrine of universal salvation (apokatastasis) was revived by some Pietist and rationalist theologians. Holbach stood at the extreme end of this spectrum: for him, the doctrine of eternal [[torture|torment]] was not a diffi[[worship|cult]] theological problem to be solved but a decisive [[evidence|proof]] of religion's moral bankruptcy.

## Core Arguments

Holbach's critique of the theological concept of [[eternal|eternity]] and [[hell|eternal punishment]] proceeds along several lines:

1. **The [[injustice|Injustice]] of [[infinite|Infinite]] [[punishment|Punishment]] for [[finite|Finite]] Offenses**: "Offenses of finite [[existence|being]]s can not be infinite" (066-002). To punish temporal [[crime|transgression]]s with eternal [[evil|suffering]] is disproportionate and unjust by any [[model|standard]] of reason or [[justice|equity]].

2. **The Immutability [[contradiction|Contradiction]]**: A [[god|God]] who is at one moment infinitely good and at another "infinitely wicked" — consigning creatures to eternal [[torture|torment]] — cannot be regarded as immutable. "A [[god|God]] who, after having been infinitely good, becomes infinitely wicked, can He be regarded as an immutable [[existence|being]]?" (061-001).

3. **The Moral Monstrosity of [[eternity|Eternal]] [[pain|Torment]]**: Holbach argues that the [[god|God]] of [[religion|theology]], in punishing creatures eternally, is morally inferior to the worst human tyrants. Even Caligula and Nero had motives for their cruelty — safety, revenge, deterrence. But [[god|God]], needing nothing and fearing nothing, would punish eternally "without fee, without profit, without curiosity, without having anything to fear" (066-002). This is pure, gratuitous sadism.

4. **The Uselessness of [[eternity|Eternal]] [[suffering|Suffering]]**: The [[torture|torment]]s of the [[death|afterlife]] serve no purpose: they cannot warn the living (who cannot witness them), nor reform the damned (for whom "the hour of mercy is passed"), nor protect [[god|God]] (whose "immovable power" and "[[happiness|felicity]]" cannot be threatened). They would exist solely for [[god|God]]'s entertainment — "to amuse Himself and insult the weakness of His creatures" (066-002).

5. **Time as a Measure of [[contradiction|Contradiction]]**: The theological deployment of [[eternal|eternity]] systematically generates contradictions. An eternal [[existence|being]] creates temporal [[existence|being]]s, subjects them to temporal trials, and then punishes their temporal failures with eternal consequences. Each step in this sequence magnifies the initial [[absurdity|absurd]]ity.

## Reception and Critique

Holbach's attack on the doctrine of [[hell|eternal punishment]] was one of the most rhetorically effective sections of *Common Sense*, and it resonated with the broader Enlightenment critique of [[punishment|hell]]. Yet philosophically, Holbach's treatment of time and [[eternal|eternity]] is relatively thin: he does not engage with the sophisticated metaphysical debates about the [[universe|nature]] of time in thinkers such as [[isaac-newton|Newton]], [[gottfried-leibniz|Leibniz]], or Kant. His approach is primarily moral and rhetorical, designed to provoke revulsion at the theological concept rather than to advance a positive metaphysics of temporality. This is both a strength (his moral clarity is bracing) and a limitation (he leaves the positive philosophical question of time almost entirely unaddressed).

## Relation to Other Concepts

Time, in Holbach's treatment, connects to several other central concepts. [[eternity]] is the theological deformation of time — duration extended beyond all finite measure and used as an instrument of [[fear|terror]]. [[infinite]] is the companion attribute that makes the time-concept theologically dangerous: an infinite [[existence|being]] administering infinite duration of [[evil|suffering]]. [[justice]] is the moral [[model|standard]] by which [[hell|eternal punishment]] is judged and found [[desire|want]]ing. [[hell]] is the specific theological institution that eternalizes [[evil|suffering]]. [[death]] is the temporal [[eve]]nt that, in [[materialism|materialist philosophy]], marks the genuine end of individual existence — a doctrine Holbach finds more consoling than the threat of eternal [[torture|torment]].

## In the Text

Holbach's most powerful treatment of time and [[eternal|eternity]] appears in chapter 66, where he compares [[cruelty|divine cruelty]] to that of the worst human tyrants:

> "The inventors of the dogma of eternal torments in hell, have made of the God whom they call so good, the most detestable of beings. Cruelty in man is the last term of corruption... The most sanguinary tyrants, Caligula, Nero, Domitian, had at least some motive in tormenting their victims... Can a God have any of these motives?"
>
> (066-002)

The logical structure of the injustice argument is laid out concisely:

> "I will tell you that offenses of finite beings can not be infinite; that a God who does not want to be offended, can not consent to make His creatures' offenses last for eternity; I will tell you that a God infinitely good, can not be infinitely cruel, nor grant His creatures infinite existence solely for the pleasure of tormenting them forever."
>
> (066-002)

The contradiction between [[goodness|divine goodness]] and [[hell|eternal punishment]] appears in the surrounding chapters:

> "A God filled with implacable fury, is He a God in whom we can find a shadow of charity or goodness?"
>
> (061-001)
