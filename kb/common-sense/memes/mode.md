# Mode

## Definition

Mode, in Holbach's philosophical vocabulary, designates a particular, determinate form or state that substance (matter, [[universe|nature]]) assumes under specific conditions. Borrowed from Spinozist metaphysics, the concept allows Holbach to explain individual [[existence|being]]s — including human [[existence|being]]s — not as independent substances created by [[god|God]], but as temporary modifications of the one eternal, self-existent substance that is [[universe|nature]]. Every mode is a "[[effect|necessary effect]]" of prior modes, governed by immutable laws and requiring no supernatural origin.

## Historical Context

The concept of mode is most closely associated with [[baruch-spinoza|Spinoza]]'s *Ethics* (1677), where modes are defined as "affections of substance" — particular things that exist *in* substance rather than *as* substances. [[baruch-spinoza|Spinoza]] distinguished infinite modes (the laws of [[universe|nature]]) from finite modes (individual things). Holbach, writing a century later, adopts this framework without [[baruch-spinoza|Spinoza]]'s theological language: what [[baruch-spinoza|Spinoza]] called "[[god|God]] or [[nature|Nature]]" (*Deus sive Natura*), Holbach simply calls [[nature|Nature]]. The term "mode" appears less frequently in "Common Sense" than in Holbach's more systematic *System of [[nature|Nature]]* (1770), but the Spinozist framework informs his repeated insistence that all [[existence|being]]s are "necessary results of certain causes."

## Core Arguments

**1. Individual [[existence|being]]s are modes, not substances.** Holbach rejects the idea that individual things or persons are self-subsistent entities. Every [[existence|being]] is a modification of the larger whole: "All bodies, whether organized or not organized, are the necessary results of certain causes, made to produce necessarily the effects which we see" (ch. XLIII).

**2. Modes follow necessary laws.** "[[nothingness|Nothing]] in [[universe|nature]] can be made by [[probability|chance]]; all follow fixed laws; these laws are but the necessary union of certain effects with their causes" (ch. XLIII). A mode is not an arbitrary arrangement but a law-governed expression of [[universe|nature]]'s causal order.

**3. Modes of thin[[monarch|king]] are not exempt from [[cause|causation]].** Holbach extends the mode concept to mental life: human opinions, beliefs, and "modes of thin[[monarch|king]]" (ch. CXCVII) are as determined as physical [[eve]]nts. Religious belief itself is a mode produced by social and psychological causes — habit, fear, and priestly manipulation — not by free rational [[belief|assent]].

**4. The mode framework eliminates the need for creation.** If particular [[existence|being]]s are modifications of eternal substance, there is no need for a moment of creation or a creator. [[nature|Nature]] eternally produces modes; [[nature|the universe]] has no beginning and requires no external cause.

**5. Human diversity reflects modal diversity.** Differences in temperament, opinion, and conduct are explained by the different modes in which human organisms are constituted and situated. "It is because neither their temperament, their interests, nor their habits agree with their sublime theories" that people fail to practice what they preach (ch. CLXXXI).

## Reception and Critique

Holbach's modal metaphysics was largely ignored by mainstream Enlightenment thought, which preferred [[john-locke|Locke]]an empiricism to Spinozist rationalism. The concept was revived in German Idealism — Hegel's dialectic of substance and subject owes much to [[baruch-spinoza|Spinoza]]'s substance-mode distinction — and in twentieth-century French philosophy, particularly Deleuze's reading of [[baruch-spinoza|Spinoza]]. The mode concept remains philosophically productive for materialist approaches to mind, agency, and individuality.

## Relation to Other Concepts

Mode is the metaphysical link between Holbach's [[Materialism]] and his [[Determinism]]. It explains how [[Nature]] can be one (as substance) and many (as modes). It grounds his rejection of [[God]] as a separate substance and of the [[Soul]] as an immortal substance distinct from the body. It connects to [[Cause]]: [[eve]]ry mode is an effect of prior modes, in an unbroken chain of necessary connection.

## In the Text

On the necessary determi[[nations|nation]] of all modes of [[existence|being]]:

> "All bodies, whether organized or not organized, are the necessary results of certain causes, made to produce necessarily the effects which we see. Nothing in nature can be made by chance; all follow fixed laws; these laws are but the necessary union of certain effects with their causes." (ch. XLIII)

On modes of thin[[monarch|king]] and toleration:

> "Let each one be allowed to think as he chooses, but let him not be allowed to annoy others for their mode of thinking. If the chiefs of nations were more just and more sensible, theological opinions would not disturb the public tranquillity." (ch. CXCVII)

On the modal [[universe|nature]] of human dissatis[[sect|faction]]:

> "Are men really satisfied with their mode of existence? If life has its sweets, how many bitternesses are mingled with them!" (ch. II)
