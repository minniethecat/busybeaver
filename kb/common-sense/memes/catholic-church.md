# Catholic Church

## Overview

The Catholic Church (Roman Catholic Church) is the largest [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian [[sect|denomination]], claiming approximately 1.3 billion members [[universe|world]]wide. Headed by the Pope in [[rome|Rome]], it traces its origins to the apostolic [[society|community]] founded by [[Jesus Christ]] in the first century CE. For Holbach's "Common Sense" (1772), the Catholic Church is the primary institutional embodiment of the religious superstition and clerical exploitation that the work systematically attacks.

## Historical Background

The early [[church|Christian church]] evolved from a persecuted sect in the [[rome|Roman Empire]] into the empire's official religion under Constantine (4th century). During the Middle Ages, the papacy became a major [[politics|political power]] in [[europe|Europe]], claiming authority over [[monarch|king]]s and emperors. By the 18th century, [[church|the Church]] had weathered the [[Reformation]] (which cost it northern [[europe|Europe]]) but remained the dominant religious institution in [[paris|France]], where Holbach wrote. The French [[priest|clergy]] constituted the First Estate of the realm and enjoyed extensive privileges, including exemption from taxation and control over education and censorship.

## Structure and Doctrine

The [[church|Church]] is organized hierarchically, with the Pope at its apex, followed by cardinals, archbishops, bishops, and parish [[clergy|priests]]. Its doctrines include the [[trinity|Trinity]], the divinity of [[jesus-christ|Christ]], the [[sacrifice|Atonement]], the [[sacrament|sacraments]] (especially the [[mass|Mass]]), papal infallibility (defined in 1870), and [[church|the Church]]'s role as the exclusive mediator of salvation. The [[priest|clergy]] administer the [[sacrament|sacraments]] and are believed to have the power to transform [[transubstantiation|bread and wine]] into the body and blood of [[jesus-christ|Christ]] during the [[mass|Mass]].

## Critique in "Common Sense"

Voltaire recognized the value of Meslier's priestly testimony:

> The value of the testimony of a catholic priest, who in his last moments recanted the errors of his faith and asked God's pardon for having taught the catholic religion, was fully appreciated by Voltaire, who highly commended this grand work of Meslier.
>
> (APP007-021)

Holbach's critique of the Catholic Church operates on several levels. First, he attacks the [[clergy|priesthood]] as an exploitative class that lives off the [[belief|credulity]] of the poor. In Meslier's words: "How horrible this monopoly! I detest their insatiable cupidity and the signal pleasure such fellows take in railing at the ignorance of those whom they carefully keep in this state of blindness." Second, he condemns the [[mass|Mass]] as a "superstitious" and "ridiculous" ritual that the priest himself despises. Third, he indicts [[church|the Church]]'s moral record, pointing to the crimes committed by religious [[monarch|ruler]]s who "have all allied religion with crime" (ch. CLXXIX).

Holbach argues that [[church|the Church]] deliberately promotes ignorance because "If ignorance is useful to [[clergy|priests]] and to the oppressors of humanity, it is very fatal to society" (ch. CLXXX). The [[church|Church]]'s doctrine of [[punishment|hell]] is presented as a tool of psychological control: "the idea of another life... is of the greatest advantage to those who take upon themselves the responsibility of conducting mankind thither" (ch. CVII).

## Legacy and Modern Relevance

The French [[revolution|Revolution]] (1789) dramatically reduced [[church|the Church]]'s power in [[paris|France]], [[nations|nation]]alizing its property and subjecting [[clergy|the clergy]] to state control. The "Common Sense" tradition contributed to the secularization of French public life, culminating in the 1905 law separating church and state. The work's critique of clerical exploitation and institutionalized ignorance remains influential in secularist thought.
