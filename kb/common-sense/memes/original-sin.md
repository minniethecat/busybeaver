## Definition

Original [[guilt|sin]], in [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian [[religion|theology]], is the doctrine that humanity inherits a state of [[sin|sinful]]ness and guilt from the disobedience of [[adam|Adam]] and Eve in the Garden of Eden. This inherited corruption is held to affect [[eve]]ry human [[existence|being]] from birth, rendering them deserving of [[punishment|divine punishment]] unless remedied by baptism and [[religion|faith]] in [[jesus-christ|Christ]]'s redemptive sacrifice.

The doctrine was developed most influentially by Augustine of Hippo in the fourth and fifth centuries and was reaffirmed by the [[council-of-trent|Council of Trent]] as a foundational [[doctrine|dogma]] of Catholicism.

For Holbach, [[sin|original sin]] is not merely false but morally repugnant. It represents the theological imagi[[nations|nation]] at its most perverse: a doctrine that declares infants guilty of a crime committed before they were born, by ancestors they never met, in a garden that never existed, at the instigation of a tal[[monarch|king]] [[serpent]]. It is, in his view, a fiction designed to make human [[existence|being]]s feel perpetually inadequate and therefore perpetually dependent on [[clergy|the clergy]].

## Historical Context

The doctrine of [[sin|original sin]] was a point of intense controversy in eighteenth-century [[europe|Europe]]. The Jansenist movement within Catholicism had revived a rigorous Augustinian emphasis on human depravity, while Enlightenment thinkers increasingly rejected the notion of inherited guilt as incompatible with any reasonable conception of justice.

Rousseau's critique of [[sin|original sin]] in the *Discourse on Inequality* (1755) was particularly influential, arguing that human [[injustice|evil]] is the product of social institutions, not an innate corruption of [[universe|nature]].

Holbach's treatment is more radical than Rousseau's. Where Rousseau offered a secularized narrative of the fall from primitive innocence into social corruption, Holbach denied that there was any fall at all. Human [[existence|being]]s, for Holbach, are products of [[universe|nature]] — neither originally innocent nor originally [[sin|sinful]], but simply natural organisms whose behavior is determined by their [[temperament|constitution]] and their circumstances.

## Core Arguments

1. **Original [[guilt|sin]] is morally incoherent.** No system of justice, human or [[divinity|divine]], could hold a person responsible for an act committed by a distant ancestor. If a human judge punished the grandchildren of a criminal, we would call it [[despotism|tyranny]]. To attribute such conduct to a perfectly just [[god|God]] is to empty the word "justice" of meaning.

2. **The doctrine contradicts observable human [[universe|nature]].** Infants do not display the marks of depravity. They act according to their natural needs and impulses. The idea that a newborn is "[[sin|sinful]]" is a theological projection, not an empirical observation.

3. **Original [[guilt|sin]] serves a clear institutional function.** By declaring [[eve]]ry human [[existence|being]] guilty from birth, [[church|the Church]] positions itself as the sole dispenser of the remedy — baptism, confession, absolution, and the [[sacrament|sacraments]]. The doctrine creates a permanent market for priestly services.

4. **The Eden narrative is mythological, not historical.** Holbach treats the Genesis story as one among many ancient myths, comparable to the fables of other [[worship|cult]]ures. To build a universal doctrine of human [[universe|nature]] on a folk tale is, he argues, intellectually indefensible.

## Reception and Critique

Holbach's critique of [[sin|original sin]] has been received with varying degrees of engagement across intellectual traditions. Within radical Enlightenment circles, his argument was widely accepted as a definitive refutation: if inherited guilt is incompatible with any coherent conception of justice, then the doctrine collapses under its own moral weight.

Condorcet and other late-Enlightenment thinkers extended Holbach's line of reasoning, arguing that the idea of congenital [[sin|sinful]]ness was an obstacle to educational and penal reform. If children are born depraved, the project of improving human [[existence|being]]s through education is futile from the start.

Theological responses to Holbach's critique have taken several forms. Augustinian defenders argue that Holbach misunderstands [[sin|original sin]] as a forensic imputation of guilt rather than a condition of disordered [[passion|desire]] — a distinction Holbach would likely dismiss as semantic evasion.

More concessive [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian thinkers, from Friedrich Schleiermacher onward, have reframed [[sin|original sin]] as a description of the social transmission of [[sin|sinful]] [[model|pattern]]s rather than a biological inheritance of guilt. This move partially accommodates Holbach's moral objections while preserving the doctrine's pastoral function.

In twentieth-century secular thought, Holbach's critique found an unexpected resonance in existentialist philosophy. Jean-[[paul|Paul]] Sartre's concept of "bad [[religion|faith]]" — the refusal to accept radical [[liberty|freedom]] by attributing one's actions to an immutable [[universe|nature]] — parallels Holbach's charge that the doctrine of [[sin|original sin]] is a device for evading responsibility.

## Relation to Other Concepts

Original [[guilt|sin]] is the theological premise upon which several interconnected doctrines depend. It provides the rationale for [[redemption]]: the claim that [[jesus-christ|Christ]]'s sacrifice was necessary to remedy a universal condition of guilt. Without [[sin|original sin]], the entire economy of [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian salvation — incar[[nations|nation]], atonement, [[sacrament|sacraments]] — loses its rationale, a point Holbach exploits to devastating effect.

The doctrine is closely related to [[free-will]], another concept that Holbach subjects to sustained critique. Augustine's articulation of [[sin|original sin]] was designed to resolve [[evil|the problem of evil]] by attributing human [[evil|suffering]] to human choice. But for Holbach, [[liberty|free will]] is itself a fiction — human actions are determined by natural causes — and [[sin|original sin]] is a theological elaboration upon a prior philosophical error.

Original [[guilt|sin]] also inter[[sect|sects]] with the political concepts of [[authority]] and [[obedience]]. By [[doctrine|teaching]] that human [[existence|being]]s are congenitally flawed and in need of external correction, the doctrine legitimizes hierarchical structures of [[government|governance]] in both church and state.

Finally, [[sin|original sin]] stands in opposition to Holbach's positive conception of [[human-nature]], which he understands as neither inherently good nor inherently [[injustice|evil]] but simply natural — a product of physical [[temperament|constitution]], education, and environment. The rejection of [[sin|original sin]] is foundational to Holbach's entire materialist anthropology.

> "Absurdity of the Theological Fables upon Original Sin and the Existence of the Devil." (ch. LXXIV)
>
> (074-001)

He traces the consequences for human self-[[knowledge|understanding]]:

> "According to the principles of Christianity, poverty itself is a virtue, and the rich man is a criminal in the eyes of God. The Gospel declares that it is easier for a camel to pass through the eye of a needle than for a rich man to enter the kingdom of heaven." (ch. CXLII)
>
> (168-010)

And he explicitly links [[sin|original sin]] to [[evil|the problem of evil]]:

> "It is this system of original sin which serves to justify Providence for all the evils which overwhelm the human race." (ch. LXXIV)
