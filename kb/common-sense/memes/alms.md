## Definition

Alms are charitable donations given to the poor, often as a religious obligation. In [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian doctrine, almsgiving is a work of mercy and a means of obtaining [[divinity|divine]] favor — the [[absolution|forgiveness]] of [[guilt|sin]]s, the accumulation of [[hope|heaven]]ly merit, and ultimately salvation. The practice is rooted in both [[bible|Old Testament]] injunctions to care for the poor and [[bible|New Testament]] [[doctrine|teaching]]s that identify [[jesus-christ|Christ]] with the needy (002-357) ("whatever you did for the least of these, you did for me").

For Holbach, the institution of almsgiving is a mechanism of mystification. It transforms the systematic extraction of wealth by [[clergy|the clergy]] into an occasion for individual piety. The rich give a fraction of their surplus to the poor and are praised as virtuous; the structural causes of poverty — the alliance of throne and altar, the diversion of [[nations|nation]]al wealth to ecclesiastical coffers, the suppression of productive industry — go unexamined. Alms are a substitute for justice.

## Historical Context

Almsgiving was a central practice of medieval [[christianity|Christianity]] and remained important in eighteenth-century Catholic societies. The [[church|Church]] administered vast networks of charitable institutions — hospitals, orphanages, poor-relief funds — funded by do[[nation|nations]] from the [[religion|faith]]ful. These institutions gave [[clergy|the clergy]] enormous social influence: they controlled access to the means of survival for the poorest members of society.

Enlightenment critics argued that charity on this model perpetuated poverty rather than alleviating it. The physiocratic and early liberal emphasis on productive labor and economic growth offered an alternative vision: poverty would be eliminated not by alms but by freeing productive activity from feudal and ecclesiastical constraints. Holbach's critique combines this economic argument with a political one: almsgiving obscures the fact that the wealth [[existence|being]] distributed was extracted from the poor in the first place.

## Core Arguments

1. **Alms are a substitute for justice.** Giving a coin to a beggar assuages the [[remorse|conscience]] of the giver but does nothing to change the conditions that produce beggary. True justice would require the restructuring of social institutions, not individual charity.

2. **The [[priest|clergy]] control the distribution of alms and benefit from doing so.** By administering charitable institutions, [[church|the Church]] positions itself as the indispensable intermediary between the rich and the poor (002-358). Both classes become dependent on [[clergy|the clergy]].

3. **The wealth distributed as alms was originally extracted from the productive classes.** The tithes, do[[nation|nations]], and bequests that fund ecclesiastical charity were taken from peasants and artisans who were told that their contributions were necessary for their salvation (168-008). The [[priest|clergy]] then returns a fraction of this extracted wealth as alms and claims moral credit for the "generosity."

4. **A just society would have no need for alms.** If productive labor were freed from ecclesiastical and feudal restrictions, [[wealth|material prosperity]] would eliminate the poverty that almsgiving claims to address.

## In the Text

Holbach questions what the people receive in exchange for the wealth they give to [[church|the Church]]:

> "Do they give them in exchange for their wealth and their offerings instruction, useful knowledge, and wise counsels?" (ch. CXLVII)

He connects almsgiving to the broader system of priestly extraction:

> "In all countries, who make the greatest figure? The ministers of the Lord. Who have the greatest power, credit, honors, wealth? To the priests!" (ch. CXLVII)
