# Monk

## Holbach's Critique

Holbach reserves particular scorn for **monks** in *Common Sense*, treating them as the most visible example of the economic and moral corruption inherent in organized religion. He observes that in the hierarchy of religious authority, "a curate, a priest, an ignorant monk, will become in the matter of religion the master of one's thoughts" (002-274) — the monk, despite his ignorance and withdrawal from the world, claims dominion over the conscience of others. Holbach traces the historical rise of monasticism to economic motives: monks "became rich and powerful; thus, monks and hermits lived in idleness, and, under the pretext of devoting themselves to Heaven, they lived at the expense of the credulous public" (002-360). The monastery is, in Holbach's analysis, an institution that extracts wealth from society while contributing nothing in return — all under the cover of piety. He also notes the social psychology of monastic authority, recounting an anecdote in which "having spoken hastily to a monk, the latter said to him, arrogantly..." (002-402), illustrating the pride that hides beneath the monk's professed humility.

## Selected Passage

Holbach recounts the story of an Italian monk whose naive sermon inadvertently exposed the economic dependence of the clergy on the devil's continued activity:

> We are told a story of the simple-heartedness of an Italian monk, which does him honor. This good man preaching one day felt obliged to announce to his auditory that, thanks to Heaven, he had at last discovered a sure means of rendering all men happy. "The devil," said he, "tempts men but to have them as comrades of his misery in hell. Let us address ourselves, then, to the Pope, who possesses the keys of paradise and of hell; let us ask him to beseech God, at the head of the whole Church, to reconcile Himself with the devil; to take him back into His favor; to re-establish him in His first rank. This can not fail to put an end to his sinister projects against mankind."
>
> (002-120)
