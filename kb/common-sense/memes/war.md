# war

**War** is one of the great calamities Holbach invokes in his argument against divine Providence. If God is good, why does He "give war"? For Holbach, the existence of war — along with pestilence, famine, and other disasters — is incompatible with the idea of a benevolent, omnipotent deity who cares for humanity. (002-087)

## War as Refutation of Providence

Holbach places war among the recurring disasters that make belief in a providential God rationally impossible. "During this time the nations have, under different forms, experienced many vicissitudes and calamities," he writes; "history shows us that the human race in all ages has been tormented and devastated by tyrants, conquerors, heroes; by wars, inundations, famines, epidemics, etc." (002-087). The sheer scale and constancy of human suffering from war — spanning all ages and all nations — cannot be reconciled with the governance of a being who is both omnipotent and benevolent.

## The Theodicy of Calamity

Theologians attempt to explain war and other disasters as divine punishment for sin. "Calamities, diseases, famines, wars, earthquakes, are the means which God employs to chastise perverse men" (002-128). But Holbach immediately points out the injustice of this explanation: these plagues "fall indiscriminately upon the good and the wicked, upon the impious and the pious, upon the innocent and the guilty" (002-128). A punishment that makes no distinction between the guilty and the innocent is not justice but arbitrary violence — and the God who inflicts it deserves blame, not worship.

## The Dervis and the Wolf

Holbach illustrates his argument with the parable of a holy dervis who undertakes a pilgrimage to Mecca despite an ongoing war between the Persians and Turks (002-173). The dervis passes safely through the war zone, protected by his religious garb, and praises God's goodness — until he reaches a mountaintop and sees a plain covered with over a hundred thousand corpses. A wolf, feasting on the dead, exclaims: "O Allah! how great is Thy kindness for the children of wolves!" For Holbach, this is the true perspective on war: what appears as divine protection to the fortunate devotee appears as divine provision to the predators who feast on the dead. Neither perspective reflects a just and benevolent cosmic order.
