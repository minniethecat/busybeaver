# Existence

## Definition

For Holbach, existence is synonymous with material existence. "Everything that exists is matter: matter is [[eve]]rything" (ch. LX). There is no [[spirit|immaterial substance]], no [[soul|spirit]]ual realm, no disembodied mind, and no [[god|divine being]] existing outside of space and time. To exist is to be somewhere, at some time, composed of some matter, acting and being acted upon according to the laws of motion.

This [[matter|materialism]] is not a speculative metaphysical preference but a methodological principle: whatever cannot be detected by the senses or inferred as a [[cause|necessary cause]] of what the senses detect has no claim on our belief. "We do not know the essence of anything, if by essence we understand that which constitutes its particular [[universe|nature]]. We know matter only by the impressions it makes upon us" (ch. LX). The concept of an immaterial existent is a contradiction in terms — like a square circle or a married bachelor.

## Historical Context

The question of what exists — and whether matter is the whole of existence — was central to 17th and 18th-century philosophy. [[rene-descartes|Descartes]] had divided existence into two substances: *res extensa* (extended substance, matter) and *res cogitans* (thin[[monarch|king]] substance, mind). This dualism preserved a realm for the soul and for [[god|God]] while conceding the [[earth|material world]] to mechanical physics. [[baruch-spinoza|Spinoza]] had collapsed this dualism into a [[vice|sin]]gle substance ([[god|God]]-or-[[nature|Nature]], *Deus sive Natura*), but retained the language of divinity.

Holbach radicalized [[baruch-spinoza|Spinoza]] by eliminating the [[divinity|divine]] language entirely. There is one substance — matter — and [[eve]]rything that exists is a [[mode|modification]] of it. [[thought|Thought]], consciousness, and [[will|volition]] are properties of organized matter (the brain), not of an immaterial soul. "The soul is only the body considered relatively to some of its functions" (ch. CVI).

[[john-locke|Locke]] had opened the door to this conclusion by suggesting — cautiously — that [[god|God]] might have endowed matter with the power of thought. Holbach walked through that door without [[john-locke|Locke]]'s caution: matter *does* think, in its organized form, and there is no need to postulate a supernatural endowment.

## Core Arguments

**1. Existence is material by definition.** To exist is to occupy space and time, to have properties, to act and be acted upon. The concept of an "immaterial existent" is empty of content — we have no experience of such a thing and no coherent concept of what it would be. "To pretend that a being can exist without extension is to pretend that a being can exist nowhere."

**2. Consciousness is a property of organized matter.** The brain produces thought as the liver produces bile — by its physical organization, not by housing an immaterial soul. "The soul is the body itself, considered relatively to some of its functions, or to the faculty it has of feeling and thin[[monarch|king]]" (ch. CVI).

**3. The hypothesis of a [[soul|spirit]]ual realm explains nothing.** Every phenomenon attributed to [[soul|spirit]] or soul — thought, choice, [[passion|emotion]], life itself — is better explained by the physical properties of organized matter. The [[soul|spirit]]ual hypothesis raises more questions than it answers: how does an [[spirit|immaterial substance]] interact with matter? Where does it reside? How does it come into existence and cease to exist?

**4. [[death|Death]] proves the materiality of consciousness.** The fact that consciousness is extinguished by physical damage to the brain — by injury, disease, or death — is the strongest [[proof|evidence]] that consciousness is a function of the brain, not of an [[spirit|immaterial substance]] that would be unaffected by physical changes. "When the body is destroyed, the soul thinks no more."

## Reception and Critique

Holbach's [[matter|materialism]] was considered so dangerous that his works were published pseudonymously (as "[[jean-meslier|Jean Meslier]]" or "Mirabaud") and publicly burned. The charge was not merely that he denied the existence of [[god|God]] and the soul but that he denied the very conceptual space in which [[religion|theology]] operates.

Later materialists, from Büchner and Moleschott in the 19th century to the eliminative materialists of the late 20th century, have continued to defend versions of Holbach's thesis. The "hard problem of consciousness" (Chalmers) represents the most persistent objection: [[eve]]n if thought is correlated with brain states, why is there subjective experience at all? Holbach would likely have dismissed this as a disguised version of dualism — the demand for an expla[[nations|nation]] of "what it is like" presumes that "what it is like" is something over and above brain function.

## Relation to Other Concepts

Existence is the ground of all other Holbachian concepts. [[Nature]] is the totality of what exists — matter in motion in all its forms. [[Spirit]] is the [[fiction|theological fiction]] of immaterial existence, which Holbach rejects. [[Soul]] is a name for certain functions of the organized body, not a separately existing substance. [[Death]] is the cessation of a particular organization of matter. [[Will]] is a physical process in the material brain, not an act of an immaterial faculty. [[God]] is the supreme immaterial existent — and therefore, for Holbach, the supreme impossibility.
