# Belief

## Definition

Holbach distinguishes sharply between belief grounded in [[proof|evidence]] and belief that rests on authority, habit, or [[passion|emotion]]. The first he calls knowledge or reasonable conviction; the second he calls credulity or superstition. The object of his critique is not belief as such but *unevidenced belief* — the acceptance of propositions for which no adequate [[proof|evidence]] exists and which are often contradicted by the available [[proof|evidence]].

This distinction is at the heart of Holbach's epistemology. "It is only by experience, by observation, by reason, that we can attain to the knowledge of truth" (ch. XI). Religious belief fails this [[model|standard]] systematically: its propositions are not testable, its methods of verification are circular, and its primary mechanism of transmission is childhood indoctri[[nations|nation]] rather than rational persuasion. "The people receive their religion from their fathers; they follow it by habit; they would blush to examine it" (ch. LXI).

## Historical Context

[[enlightenment|The Enlightenment]]'s relationship to belief was complex and internally contested. [[john-locke|Locke]] had argued in *The [[reason|Reason]]ableness of [[christianity|Christianity]]* (1695) that [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian doctrine, properly understood, was compatible with reason — that revelation supplemented but did not contradict rational knowledge. The deists went further, arguing that reason alone could establish the essentials of religion and that revelation was at best redundant. [[david-hume|Hume]]'s essay "Of [[miracle|Miracle]]s" (1748) attacked the credibility of testimony for [[miracle|supernatural event]]s, undermining the historical foundations of [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian belief.

Holbach represents the radical endpoint of this trajectory: the claim that religious belief is not merely unsupported by [[proof|evidence]] but is positively irrational — that it persists only because people are prevented from examining it. His innovation is to combine epistemological critique with sociological analysis: religious belief is not just a mistake but a social product, manufactured by [[clergy|priests]] for their benefit and maintained by psychological mechanisms of fear and habit.

## Core Arguments

**1. Religious belief is acquired by habit, not by reason.** [[person-index|People]] do not arrive at religious belief through inquiry; they absorb it in childhood from parents, [[clergy|priests]], and the surrounding [[worship|cult]]ure. "Men are religious because they have been taught to be religious" — the content of their belief is an [[chance|accident]] of geography and family, not a conclusion reached by investigation.

**2. [[fear|Fear]] sustains belief where [[proof|evidence]] cannot.** The psychological engine of religious belief is fear: fear of death, fear of [[evil|suffering]], fear of the unknown, and above all fear of [[hell|eternal punishment]]. "The fear of [[god|God]] troubles only those who have a reason to fear him" (ch. CLXVI). [[fear|Fear]] short-circuits the critical faculties and makes people cling to beliefs that promise safety.

**3. Social conformity reinforces belief.** [[doubt|Doubt]] is punished socially — by ostracism, by legal penalties, by the threat to [[honor|reputation]] and livelihood. Belief is rewarded — by social acceptance, by access to [[society|community]], by the approval of authority. These social mechanisms operate independently of the truth of the beliefs in question; they would sustain any belief system equally well.

**4. Belief should be proportioned to [[proof|evidence]].** Holbach's positive proposal is a version of what later philosophers would call evidentialism: the degree of [[certainty|confidence]] one invests in a proposition should be proportional to the strength of the [[proof|evidence]] supporting it. Religious belief fails this test because the [[proof|evidence]] for its claims — [[miracle|miracles]], [[dreams|revelations]], answered [[prayer|prayers]] — dissolves under scrutiny.

## Reception and Critique

Holbach's critique of religious belief was one of his most influential arguments. It directly anticipates the 19th-century "masters of suspicion" — Marx's claim that religion is a social product, Nietzsche's analysis of belief as a symptom of psychological weakness, Freud's treatment of religious belief as wish-fulfillment and neurosis.

Contemporary epistemology has refined Holbach's crude evidentialism. [[pascal|Pascal]] had already argued that belief could be rational on prudential grounds [[eve]]n without [[proof|evidence]] (the famous "wager"). [[will|Will]]iam James defended the "will to beli[[eve]]" in cases where the [[proof|evidence]] is genuinely ambiguous and the choice is forced and momentous. [[mode|Mode]]rn reformed epistemologists argue that religious belief can be "properly basic" — rational without [[existence|being]] inferred from [[proof|evidence]]. Holbach would likely dismiss all these as sophisticated attempts to justify what reason alone would reject.

## Relation to Other Concepts

Belief is the psychological state that sustains [[Faith]], which is the disposition to beli[[eve]] without [[proof|evidence]]. It is the target of [[Doubt]], the intellectual [[morality|virtue]] that questions [[opinion|received opinion]]. Belief is [[worship|cult]]ivated by [[Education]] when education is controlled by [[authority|religious authority]], and it is undermined by [[Reason]], which demands [[proof|evidence]] for claims. Belief in theological propositions is the foundation of [[Religion]]; its rejection is the starting point of [[Atheism]]. The specific beliefs Holbach critiques include belief in [[God]], belief in the [[Soul]], belief in [[Hell]] and [[Heaven]], and belief in [[Providence]].
