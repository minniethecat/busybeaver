# Testimony

**Testimony** is a central epistemological concept in Holbach's *Common Sense*, governing how claims are evaluated and knowledge is acquired. Holbach draws a fundamental distinction between the **testimony of the senses** — which he regards as the sole reliable foundation of human knowledge — and the **testimony of interested parties** in religious matters, which he considers inherently untrustworthy. The concept also extends to the inward testimony of **conscience** as the internal judge of moral conduct toward fellow beings.

## Definition and Epistemological Role

In Holbach's empiricist framework, testimony refers to the act of bearing witness or providing evidence for a claim. The credibility of any testimony depends entirely on the nature and interests of its source. Holbach grounds his epistemology in sensory experience: ideas arise "only when confirmed by the constant testimony of our senses, which alone give birth to ideas" (002-019; 018-001). This places sensory testimony at the foundation of all genuine knowledge, and by implication, any claim that cannot be verified through the senses — or that demands the rejection of sensory evidence — is epistemologically suspect.

## The Testimony of the Senses

For Holbach, the senses are the only gateway to knowledge. The "constant testimony" of sensory experience provides the raw material from which all ideas are constructed. This commitment to sensory evidence leads to a direct conflict with religious systems that demand belief in supernatural events — events that, by definition, cannot be confirmed through ordinary sensory testimony. Holbach's position is that trusting the senses is not optional but foundational: to doubt sensory testimony is to destroy the very basis of all human certainty.

## Religious Testimony as Unreliable

Holbach's most sustained critique of testimony targets religious claims. He observes that "in the matter of religion, the testimony of interested parties is irrefragable and cannot be questioned" (002-246; 126-002). The clergy, as interested parties who benefit materially and socially from religious belief, are precisely the least trustworthy witnesses. Their testimony should be subjected to the same scrutiny as any other claim, yet religion places it beyond question by declaring it sacred.

This critique extends to the tendency of religious systems to elevate faith above evidence. Holbach notes that faith is presented as having "more weight upon my mind than the suspicious testimony of many men whom I know to be capable of deceiving themselves and of deceiving me" (002-267; 135-003). The religious demand to accept claims on faith — without sensory verification — inverts the proper epistemological order.

## Faith Versus Sensory Evidence

Holbach identifies a fundamental tension in religious epistemology: faith is declared superior to evidence, "preferable to the testimony of the senses" (002-265; 135-001). But if the senses are the only source of knowledge, then any system that demands their sacrifice is demanding intellectual suicide. As Holbach argues, "in warning me to doubt the testimony of my senses, you destroy for me the proof of all truths" (002-268; 135-004). To doubt one's senses is to lose the ability to verify any claim whatsoever.

The problem is compounded by the fact that most people accept religious claims on mere verbal report: "one accepts it at hazard, and believes it upon verbal testimony, without taking the trouble to examine it" (002-273; 137-002). This unexamined acceptance of testimony — passed down through tradition and authority — perpetuates error across generations.

## Evidence Over Inspired Books

Holbach extends his critique to scriptural authority, arguing that rational evidence should always be preferred "rather than upon the testimony of their inspired books" (002-443; 202-003). The claim that a book is divinely inspired is itself a piece of testimony — one that must be evaluated by the same epistemological standards as any other claim. To accept a book's authority on its own say-so is circular reasoning.

## Conscience as Inward Testimony

Holbach offers a positive, naturalistic account of conscience as "the inward testimony which we render to ourselves for having acted in a manner worthy of praise or blame toward our fellow-beings" (002-382; 178-002; APP169-028). Unlike the external and interested testimony of religious authorities, the inward testimony of conscience is direct and requires no intermediary. It is the self's honest accounting of its own conduct — a form of testimony that is verifiable through introspection rather than through blind trust in external claims.

This concept of conscience as inward testimony provides a naturalistic alternative to religious morality: one need not consult priests or sacred texts to know right from wrong; the inward testimony of conscience, properly cultivated through reason and experience, is sufficient.
