# Crusades

## Overview

The Crusades were a series of religious wars initiated, supported, and sometimes directed by the Latin [[church|Church]] between 1095 and 1291, primarily aimed at recovering the [[palestine|Holy Land]] from Muslim rule. Pope Urban II preached the First Crusade at the Council of Clermont in 1095, promising [[soul|spirit]]ual rewards — indulgences — to those who took up arms. The movement produced eight major expeditions to the Eastern Mediterranean, as well as campaigns against pagans in the Baltic, [[heresy|heretic]]s in southern [[france|France]] (the Albigensian Crusade), and political enemies of the papacy. Holbach's "Common Sense" invokes the Crusades as a devastating illustration of religion's capacity to "un[[chain|chain]]" human passions and produce mass violence.

## Historical Background

The immediate trigger for the First Crusade was the Byzantine Emperor Alexios I Komnenos's request for military assistance against the Seljuk Turks, who had conquered much of Anatolia. But the crusading impulse drew on deeper currents: the ideology of [[sacred|holy]] war developed in the [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian West, the [[passion|desire]] to secure pilgrimage routes to [[jerusalem|Jerusalem]], and the papacy's ambition to assert leadership over [[christianity|Christendom]]. The capture of [[jerusalem|Jerusalem]] in 1099 was accompanied by a massacre of its Muslim and Jewish inhabitants. Subsequent crusades were less successful: [[jerusalem|Jerusalem]] was recaptured by Saladin in 1187, and the last Crusader stronghold, Acre, fell in 1291.

## Key Figures and Developments

Pope Urban II launched the crusading movement with his Clermont sermon. [[peter|Peter]] the Hermit led the ill-fated [[person-index|People]]'s Crusade. [[god|God]]frey of Bouillon became the first [[monarch|ruler]] of the Crusader Kingdom of [[jerusalem|Jerusalem]]. [[richard-the-lionheart|Richard the Lionheart]] of England and Saladin, the Ayyubid sultan, became legendary adversaries during the Third Crusade. The Fourth Crusade (1202–1204) notoriously sacked Constantinople, a [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian city, deepening the schism between Eastern and Western churches.

## Impact on Religion and Thought

The Crusades had far-reaching consequences. They intensified hostility between [[christianity|Christianity]] and [[islam|Islam]], a legacy that persists in some forms to the present day. They strengthened the papacy's temporal power and established the precedent of indulgences — the remission of temporal punishment for [[guilt|sin]] in exchange for military service. They also exposed [[europe|Europe]]ans to [[islam|Islam]]ic philosophy, [[knowledge|science]], and commerce, contributing indirectly to the intellectual ferment that culminated in the [[renaissance|Renaissance]] and the [[Enlightenment]]. For Enlightenment critics like Holbach, however, the Crusades were not a story of [[worship|cult]]ural exchange but of [[intolerance|fanaticism]] — [[evidence|proof]] that religion, far from improving human [[morality|morality]], could sanction the worst atrocities when it convinced men they were doing [[god|God]]'s work.

## Connection to "Common Sense"

Holbach's "Common Sense" lists the Crusades among the historical crimes that religion has caused. The [[christianity|Christianity]] overview page in this wiki captures the argument: "Holbach surveys the historical record — crusades against Muslims, inquisitions against [[heresy|heretic]]s, wars between Catholics and Protestants — and concludes that [[christianity|Christianity]] has caused more bloodshed than any pagan religion." Holbach's core question is: "When we complain about the violence and [[injustice|evil]]s which generally religion causes upon earth, we are answered at once, that these [[excess|excesses]] are not due to religion, but that they are the sad effect of men's passions. I would ask, however, what unchained these passions? It is evidently religion" (ch. II). The Crusades, for Holbach, are Exhibit A in this argument: men who might otherwise have lived peacefully were [[desire|drive]]n to cross continents and slaughter strangers because religion convinced them that [[god|God]] willed it.

## Legacy

The Crusades left an enduring mark on [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian-Muslim relations, [[europe|Europe]]an expansionism, and the vocabulary of Western warfare — the word "crusade" continues to be used metaphorically for any zealous campaign. From Holbach's perspective, the Crusades' most important legacy was their [[proof|demonstration]] that religious motivation can override ordinary moral restraints, a theme he develops throughout "Common Sense" in his analysis of [[fanaticism|fanaticism]] and [[persecution|persecution]].
