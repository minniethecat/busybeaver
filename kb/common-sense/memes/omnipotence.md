# Omnipotence

## Definition

Omnipotence is the [[divinity|divine]] attribute of unlimited power — the capacity of [[god|God]] to accomplish anything without constraint. In Holbach's critique, omnipotence is not an attribute to be clarified through [[religion|theology]] but a concept whose logical implications are devastating to [[divinity|theism]]: if [[god|God]] is omnipotent, then all [[injustice|evil]], [[evil|suffering]], and religious confusion in [[earth|the world]] must be either willed or permitted by Him. Holbach uses the concept not to define [[god|God]]'s [[universe|nature]] but to expose the contradiction at the heart of theistic belief — an omnipotent [[existence|being]] would not need the support of men, would not fail to communicate clearly, and would not permit the very conditions theologians use to justify their own existence.

## Historical Context

The [[divinity|divine]] attribute of omnipotence has been a cornerstone of Judeo-[[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian [[religion|theology]] [[guilt|sin]]ce the biblical period, formalized in medieval scholasticism (Aquinas, Anselm) and re-examined during the Enlightenment. The [[problem-of-evil|problem of evil]] — how an omnipotent, omnibenevolent [[god|God]] can permit [[evil|suffering]] — was a central theme of 18th-century philosophical debate, most famously in [[gottfried-leibniz|Leibniz]]'s *Theodicy* (1710) and [[voltaire|Voltaire]]'s satirical response in *Candide* (1759) after the [[lisbon|Lisbon]] earthquake. Holbach entered this debate from the radical materialist position: he did not seek to reconcile omnipotence with [[injustice|evil]] (as [[gottfried-leibniz|Leibniz]] attempted) but to [[demon|demons]]trate that the very concept is incoherent.

## Core Arguments

1. **Omnipotence vs. the existence of [[injustice|evil]]**: If [[god|God]] is omnipotent and good, why does He "tolerate injustice for an instant"? A [[god|God]] who temporizes with [[injustice|evil]] is either not omnipotent or not good. The presence of moral and [[evil|natural evil]] in [[earth|the world]] is, for Holbach, a straightforward empirical refutation of omnipotence.

2. **The scandal of [[divinity|divine]] dependence**: Holbach mocks the idea that an omnipotent [[existence|being]] "needs the support of men." If an omnipotent [[god|God]] [[passion|desire]]d to found a religion, He "would have" done so with irresistible clarity. The very fact that [[clergy|priests]] and theologians must argue, persuade, and coerce on [[god|God]]'s behalf proves the absence of omnipotence.

3. **The crucifixion paradox**: In a passage of blunt rhetorical force, Holbach asks whether it is "a pure loss that your [[god|God]] died." An omnipotent [[existence|being]] cannot die, and a death that fails to achi[[eve]] its stated purpose exposes the limits of [[almighty|divine power]]. The crucifixion, far from [[demon|demons]]trating [[god|God]]'s love, [[demon|demons]]trates the [[contradiction|incoherence]] of attributing omnipotence to a [[existence|being]] who enters history as a victim.

4. **Omnipotence and [[divinity|divine]] communication**: "It is incredible that an Omnipotent and infinitely good [[god|God]]" would deliver His message through "errors and [[imposture|deception]]s." If [[god|God]] [[passion|desire]]s universal salvation, an omnipotent [[existence|being]] would communicate with perfect clarity. The historical reality of religious confusion, competing [[sect|sects]], and theological disputes proves that no omnipotent communicator stands behind revelation.

5. **The [[attribute|attributes]] contradiction**: Omnipotence, omni[[knowledge|science]], and omni[[charity|benevolence]] cannot coexist with the observable [[universe|world]]. Holbach subjects all [[attribute|divine attributes]] to this empirical test: "What [[evidence|proof]]s are given to us of His [[philosophy|wisdom]], goodness, justice, of His omnipotence?" The answer, for him, is none.

## In the Text

> "Theology preaches the omnipotence of its God, and continually places limits upon it." (Chapter LXX)

Holbach identifies the performative contradiction in theological discourse: theologians assert omnipotence in doctrinal statements and then immediately qualify it away when confronted with the realities of [[evil|suffering]], [[liberty|free will]], and religious diversity.

> "Can God tolerate injustice for an instant? To temporize with evil is to be an accomplice to it." (Chapter LXX)

The moral logic of omnipotence is pressed to its conclusion. A [[existence|being]] with unlimited power who permits [[injustice|evil]] is morally indistinguishable from an author of [[injustice|evil]].

> "How can a theologian tell us that a God who is omnipotent does not have the power to make Himself understood?" (Chapter LXX)

The epistemic argument from omnipotence: [[divinity|divine]] communication should be as clear as [[almighty|divine power]] is unlimited. The ambiguity and obscurity of revelation are empirical [[evidence|proof]] against the reality of the [[god|God]] it describes.
