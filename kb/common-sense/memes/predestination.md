# Predestination

## Definition

Predestination is the [[doctrine|theological doctrine]] that [[god|God]] has eternally decreed the fate of [[eve]]ry human soul — some to salvation, others to [[hell|damnation]] — before any individual act of will or merit. In Holbach's critique, predesti[[nations|nation]] is not merely false but morally incoherent: it transforms [[god|God]] into the author of [[injustice|evil]], strips human action of moral significance, and exposes [[justice|divine justice]] as a cruel farce.

Holbach does not oppose predesti[[nations|nation]] from the standpoint of [[liberty|free will]] libertarianism. His own position is determinist — all actions are the [[effect|necessary effect]]s of natural causes. But he argues that theological predesti[[nations|nation]] makes [[god|God]] directly responsible for [[guilt|sin]] in a way that naturalistic [[effect|determinism]] does not, because the [[god|deity]] is said to will and decree the acts He then punishes.

## Holbach's Critique

**1. Predestination makes [[god|God]] the author of [[guilt|sin]].** If [[god|God]] "resolved in His decrees to allow this fall," then "He [[passion|desire]]d it to take place: otherwise it would not have happened." Whatever [[god|God]] foresees and permits, given His omnipotence, He effectively wills. The distinction between "permitting" and "causing" collapses.

**2. [[punishment|Punishment]] for predestined acts is injustice.** The chapter title states the argument plainly: "The Foresight [[attribute|Attribute]]d to [[god|God]], Would Give to [[guilt|Guilt]]y Men Whom He Punishes, the Right to Complain of His [[cruelty|Cruelty]]." If the [[guilt|sin]]ner was foreordained to [[guilt|sin]], punishment is not justice but [[cruelty|persecution]].

**3. The parable of the trap-door.** Holbach offers a memorable thought experiment: imagine a [[existence|being]] who places men in a cell with a trap-door, observes them "incognito," and "cruelly punish[es] all those who in wal[[monarch|king]] should hurt each other" while rewarding "the small number of those to whom the sight was spared." This, he says, is "the ideas which the [[doctrine|dogma]] of gratuitous predesti[[nations|nation]] gives of [[divinity|Divinity]]."

**4. Predestination undermines all moral effort.** If one's eternal fate is already sealed, "what does it matter whether [[god|God]] destined men to happiness or to misery by a previous decree, the effect of His foresight, or by a subsequent decree, the effect of His justice?" In either case, the individual is powerless.

## Relation to Other Concepts

Predestination is the theological counterpart to Holbach's own [[determinism]]. Both deny libertarian [[liberty|free will]], but they differ crucially: naturalistic [[effect|determinism]] traces actions to impersonal causes, while predesti[[nations|nation]] traces them to a personal [[god|deity]] who then judges His own decrees. The concept connects to [[free-will]], [[guilt]], [[punishment]], and [[justice]]. It stands in opposition to [[merit]], [[virtue]], and any coherent account of [[morality]] within a theological framework.
