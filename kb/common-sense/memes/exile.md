# Exile

## Definition

Exile, in Holbach's critique of religious persecution, refers to the banishment or expulsion of individuals from their communities on grounds of religious dissent. Holbach presents exile — alongside imprisonment and execution — as one of the characteristic responses that religious authorities make to disagreement. "You must persecute, imprison, exile, or burn him" (002-356) encapsulates his view of how organized religion handles those who question its dogmas.

The concept serves a specific rhetorical function in "Common Sense": it is evidence that religion, far from being a source of moral improvement, is a primary cause of human suffering and social division. That people are driven from their homes, separated from their families, and stripped of their property because they hold unorthodox beliefs about invisible beings demonstrates the practical inhumanity of theological commitments.

## Historical Context

Religious exile was a living reality in early modern Europe. The revocation of the Edict of Nantes (1685) had driven hundreds of thousands of French Protestants (Huguenots) into exile. The Spanish Inquisition expelled Jews and Muslims. England's religious conflicts produced Puritan exiles to America and Catholic exiles to the continent. Holbach wrote within living memory of these events, and his readers would have recognized exile as a concrete consequence of religious intolerance.

The St. Bartholomew's Day Massacre (1572) — frequently cited by Holbach as the paradigmatic example of religious violence — was preceded by decades of persecution that included exile, forced conversion, and execution of French Protestants.

## Core Arguments

1. **Exile as proof of religion's intolerance**: The fact that religious systems routinely resort to exile demonstrates that they cannot tolerate dissent or rational scrutiny. A truth would not need to banish its critics.

2. **The universal pattern of religious persecution**: Holbach notes that "you must persecute, imprison, exile, or burn him" applies across religions and across history — Catholic against Protestant, Christian against pagan, and so on. The pattern is universal because the underlying psychology of religious certainty is universal (168-006).

3. **Exile as a political tool**: Religious authorities use exile not to protect truth but to eliminate political opposition. The charge of heresy becomes a pretext for removing inconvenient critics (169-049).

## In the Text

Holbach repeatedly invokes exile as evidence of religion's destructive social effects:

> "You must persecute, imprison, exile, or burn him" (002-356)

This formula recurs in multiple chapters:

> "You will find that all religions have said: you must persecute, imprison, exile, or burn him" (168-006, paraphrased)

The appendix contains a parallel formulation (APP169-002).
