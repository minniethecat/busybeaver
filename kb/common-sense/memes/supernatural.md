## Definition

The supernatural, in Holbach's [[materialism|materialist philosophy]], designates that which is claimed to exist or operate outside the order of [[universe|nature]] — gods, [[soul|spirit]]s, [[miracle|miracles]], providence, and [[divinity|divine]] interventions. Holbach denies that the category "supernatural" has any real referent. For him, [[universe|nature]] is the totality of what exists: a self-contained system of matter in motion governed by necessary laws. Anything claimed to transcend [[universe|nature]] is, by definition, a fiction of the human imagi[[nations|nation]]. The supernatural is not a realm one can intelligibly speak of; it is a word invented to name what has never been observed and what contradicts [[eve]]ry known law of physics and experience.

Holbach's critique of the supernatural is not merely a rejection of ghost stories and miracle claims. It is a foundational move in his larger project: to [[demon|demons]]trate that all [[belief|religious belief]] rests upon the supposition that there exists something beyond [[universe|nature]], and that this supposition is incoherent. If [[universe|nature]] is all that exists, then "supernatural" is a word without an object — a sound signifying nothing.

## Historical Context

The critique of the supernatural was a central theme of the Radical Enlightenment. By the mid-eighteenth century, a generation of French materialists — La Mettrie, Diderot, Helvétius, and d'Holbach — had moved beyond the [[theism|deism]] of [[voltaire|Voltaire]] toward a thoroughgoing [[materialism|naturalism]]. Where earlier critics had questioned particular supernatural claims ([[miracle|miracles]], prophecies), Holbach and his circle questioned the very intelligibility of the concept. The supernatural, they argued, was not a hypothesis that happened to be false; it was a category mistake.

This position was continuous with the methodological [[materialism|naturalism]] of the new [[knowledge|science]]. [[isaac-newton|Newton]] had shown that celestial motions obeyed mathematical laws without [[divinity|divine]] intervention; Laplace would later famously declare that he had "no need of that hypothesis" concerning [[god|God]]. Holbach extended this [[materialism|naturalism]] from physics to metaphysics, arguing that the human mind, morality, and society are equally parts of [[universe|nature]] and require no supernatural expla[[nation|nations]].

## Core Arguments

1. **The supernatural is a word without meaning.** If "[[universe|nature]]" denotes the totality of existent things and their laws, then "supernatural" can denote nothing at all. To speak of a "supernatural [[existence|being]]" is like spea[[monarch|king]] of a "square circle" — the terms cancel each other.

2. **[[belief|Belief]] in the supernatural arises from ignorance.** When natural causes are unknown, the human mind invents invisible agents to fill the explanatory gap. Thunder was once attributed to [[jupiter|Jupiter]]; disease to [[demon|demons]]. As knowledge advances, the supernatural retreats. Holbach predicts that a complete [[knowledge|science]] of [[universe|nature]] would leave no room for supernatural expla[[nation|nations]] of any phenomenon.

3. **The supernatural is a tool of political control.** [[priest|Priest]]s and [[monarch|ruler]]s have a vested interest in persuading people that an invisible [[universe|world]] exists, governed by [[existence|being]]s who punish disobedience. The fear of supernatural sanctions makes populations governable. Holbach argues that the doctrine of [[punishment|hell]] is the ultimate instrument of priestly power.

4. **Supernatural morality is fatal to human welfare.** When people beli[[eve]] that their conduct is monitored and judged by invisible [[existence|being]]s, they neglect the natural causes of happiness and [[evil|suffering]]. They pray instead of wor[[monarch|king]], fear imaginary crimes instead of correcting real ones, and persecute those who hold different beliefs about the invisible [[universe|world]].

## In the Text

Holbach opens *Common Sense* with a direct assault on supernatural [[religion|theology]], framing the entire work as a contest between [[universe|nature]] and the supernatural:

> "The universe, this vast assemblage of every thing that exists, presents everywhere matter and motion; the whole offers to our contemplation nothing but an immense and uninterrupted chain of causes and effects." (ch. I)

The text repeatedly treats "supernatural" as synonymous with "chimerical":

> "The fantastic and supernatural notions of theology have succeeded so well in rendering man completely unknown to himself, that he believes himself to be moved by supernatural powers." (ch. CXIV)

And Holbach makes the political dimension explicit:

> "It is from Heaven that the chains have come to fetter the minds of mortals." (ch. CXXII)
