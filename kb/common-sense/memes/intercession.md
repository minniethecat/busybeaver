## The Economics of Intercession

Holbach analyzes intercession — the claim by priests and saints to mediate between believers and God — as an economic transaction dressed in spiritual language. He writes that priests staked their authority on "their prayers and their powerful intercession with the Deity; they became rich" (002-360). The financial enrichment of the clergy is not, in Holbach's analysis, an unfortunate corruption of intercessory doctrine but its intended function: the claim to mediate access to the divine is the priestly class's source of income and power.

## Miraculous Intercession

Holbach extends his critique to the purported miracles attributed to saintly intercession. He observes that alongside "ridiculous cures performed by the intercession of their saints" (003-010), the clergy also manufactured miraculous punishments for those who questioned their authority. For Holbach, the entire apparatus of intercessory miracle claims serves a single purpose: to demonstrate the clergy's unique access to supernatural power and thereby justify their privileged social position.

## The Logic of Mediation

Within Holbach's broader theory, intercession exemplifies a universal pattern in religious systems: the creation of artificial scarcity in access to the divine. By convincing believers that direct communion with God is impossible or dangerous, the priesthood establishes itself as a necessary intermediary. The doctrine of intercession is thus revealed as a power mechanism — one that Holbach believes can be dismantled only by recognizing that no such mediation is necessary.
