# Serpent

## Definition

The serpent of Genesis — the creature that speaks, reasons, and seduces Eve in the Garden of Eden — serves in Baron d'Holbach's critique as a paradigmatic example of biblical mythology. Far from [[existence|being]] a [[revelation|revealed truth]] about a supernatural tempter, the Eden serpent is, in Holbach's analysis, a narrative device borrowed from the common stock of ancient Near Eastern folklore. Its presence in [[bible|scripture]] is not [[proof|evidence]] of [[divinity|divine]] inspiration but of the human, [[worship|cult]]ural, and thoroughly mythological character of the biblical text.

## Historical Context

Serpent symbolism is pervasive in the religious iconography of the ancient Near East and Mediterranean. In Mesopotamian mythology, the serpent appears as a creature of both [[philosophy|wisdom]] and danger — associated with the god Enki, with healing (the caduceus), and with chaos (Tiamat). [[egypt|Egypt]]ian religion featured the serpent Apophis as the enemy of order, while [[palestine|Canaan]]ite [[worship|cult]]s included serpent imagery that archaeologists continue to document. The Genesis serpent thus did not emerge from a theological vacuum; it drew upon a symbolic vocabulary already familiar to the ancient Hebrews and their neighbors.

Early [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian exegesis identified the Eden serpent with Satan ([[revelation|Revelation]] 12:9: "the great dragon... that old serpent, called the D[[injustice|evil]], and Satan"), a connection not explicit in Genesis itself. This identification transformed a folkloric spea[[monarch|king]] [[soul|anima]]l into a cosmic antagonist and became foundational to the [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian doctrine of [[original-sin|the Fall]]. Holbach, following the critical trajectory of [[hobbes|Hobbes]] and [[baruch-spinoza|Spinoza]], treats this entire edifice — folkloric serpent, Satanic identification, cosmic Fall — as a chain of human inventions, each layer added to serve the institutional interests of the priestly class.

## Core Arguments

Holbach's first argument is comparative: the Eden serpent is structurally indistinguishable from the tal[[monarch|king]] [[soul|anima]]ls, magical creatures, and mythical beasts that populate the religious narratives [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ians dismiss as pagan fables. He lists "a serpent which spoke, which reasoned, and which was more cunning than man" alongside "an ass which spoke" (Balaam's ass, Numbers 22) and "the industry of Prometheus" as examples of the same narrative species. If [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ians rightly recognize the mythological character of Greek, Roman, and [[egypt|Egypt]]ian religious tales, consistency demands they apply the same judgment to the Hebrew [[bible|scripture]]s.

His second argument is moral: the serpent story [[attribute|attributes]] to [[god|God]] conduct incompatible with justice or goodness. A [[god|deity]] who permits "a simple serpent" to seduce the first humans, knowing that this seduction will "ruin all men," and then holds all subsequent generations accountable for a [[crime|transgression]] they did not commit, is not a [[god|deity]] worthy of veneration. The serpent, in Holbach's reading, is a theological necessity — a device required to explain how a perfect creation went wrong without implicating [[creator|the Creator]] — and its very convenience as an explanatory mechanism betrays its human fabrication.

Third, Holbach notes the political function of the serpent narrative. By constructing an origin story in which humanity is fallen, guilty, and in need of [[salvation|redemption]], the serpent myth creates the condition of dependency that [[clergy|priests]] exploit. Without the serpent, there is no Fall; without [[original-sin|the Fall]], there is no need for a [[divinity|divine]] savior; without the need for a savior, the entire apparatus of [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian sacerdotal mediation collapses. The serpent is therefore not an incidental detail of the Eden story but its functional center.

## In the Text

Holbach addresses the serpent narrative directly in chapter CXXVII of *Common Sense*, grouping it with other biblical fables: "There is no erudition, no sublime thought, nor any production which surpasses the ordinary capacities of the human mind. On the contrary, we shall see on one side fabulous tales similar to that of a woman formed of a man's rib; of the pretended terrestrial [[paradise|Paradise]]; of a serpent which spoke, which reasoned, and which was more cunning than man."

In chapter CLIV, Holbach sharpens the critique by focusing on the moral [[contradiction|incoherence]] of the temptation narrative. He ridicules the idea that "He sent to the first [[existence|being]]s of the human race, [[adam|Adam]] and Eve, a [[demon|devil]], or a simple serpent, to seduce them, and by this means ruin all men. This is not credible!" The repetition of "this is not credible" throughout the passage functions as a refrain — a rhetorical hammer driving home the rationalist's refusal to accept the mythological as historical.


## Selected Passage

Holbach groups the Eden serpent with other fabulous tales, arguing that all such narrations appear equally mythological:

> There is no erudition, no sublime thought, nor any production which surpasses the ordinary capacities of the human mind. On the contrary, we shall see on one side fabulous tales similar to that of a woman formed of a man's rib; of the pretended terrestrial Paradise; of a serpent which spoke, which reasoned, and which was more cunning than man; of an ass which spoke, and reprimanded its master for ill-treating it; of a universal deluge, and of an ark where animals of all kinds were inclosed; of the confusion of languages and of the division of the nations, without speaking of numerous other useless narrations upon low and frivolous subjects which important authors would scorn to relate.
