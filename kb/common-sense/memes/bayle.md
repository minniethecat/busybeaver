# Bayle

## In Holbach's References

**Pierre Bayle** (1647–1706) was a French philosopher, skeptic, and author of the influential *Historical and Critical Dictionary*. Holbach cites Bayle twice in *Common Sense*. First, he quotes Bayle approvingly: Holbach invokes Bayle's authority on a point about intellectual honesty — the text preserves the citation simply as "[Bayle.]" (002-225), indicating that Bayle's name alone carried weight among enlightened readers. Holbach invokes Bayle to refute the argument from universal consent:

> "Neither general tradition nor the unanimous consent of all men could place any injunction upon truth." [Bayle.] Another wise man said before him, that "an army of philosophers would not be sufficient to change the nature of error and to make it truth." [Averroës]
>
> (002-225)

Second, Holbach places Bayle in a select genealogy of modern freethought: "Among the moderns, Hobbes, Spinoza, Bayle, and others have followed the same path" (002-448) — the path of rational inquiry unconstrained by religious dogma. For Holbach and his contemporaries, Bayle represented the possibility of a rigorously honest philosophy that subjected all claims, including theological ones, to the tribunal of reason. Bayle's argument that a society of atheists could be moral — a scandalous claim in its time — resonates throughout Holbach's own ethical naturalism.
