# Truth

## Definition

In Holbach's "Common Sense," truth is the accurate correspondence between human [[knowledge|understanding]] and objective reality — a relationship that reason alone can establish. Truth is not an abstract or metaphysical [[model|ideal]] but a practical consequence of honest inquiry: "Truth is a cause; it produces necessarily its effect when its impulse is not interrupted by causes which stop its action" (ch. II). This naturalistic conception strips truth of [[mystery|theological mystery]] and repositions it as something accessible to any person willing to employ their rational faculties without [[opinion|prejudice]].

Holbach distinguishes sharply between truth-as-discovery (what reason uncovers through observation and reflection) and truth-as-[[doctrine|dogma]] (what authority declares without [[proof|evidence]]). The former is the foundation of genuine knowledge and moral progress; the latter is an instrument of intellectual enslavement. "Let the mind be filled early with true ideas," he w[[ritual|rites]]; "let man's reason be [[worship|cult]]ivated; let justice govern him; and there will be no need of opposing to his passions the powerless barrier of the fear of [[god|God]]s" (ch. II).

## Historical Context

[[enlightenment|The Enlightenment]] treated truth as both an epistemological goal and a political weapon. Bacon had called for the systematic collection of facts; [[rene-descartes|Descartes]] had demanded certainty through rational method; [[john-locke|Locke]] had grounded knowledge in [[experience|sensory experience]]. By Holbach's era, the French *philosophes* had transformed the pursuit of truth into an explicitly anti-clerical project. Diderot's *Encyclopédie* sought to collect and disseminate all [[knowledge|useful knowledge]], bypassing ecclesiastical gatekeepers. Holbach's contribution was to argue that religious institutions were not merely indifferent to truth but structurally hostile to it — that the entire edifice of [[religion|theology]] was "systematic [[absurdity|nonsense]]" designed to make "the most palpable truths" uncertain (ch. II).

## Core Arguments

**1. Truth is a natural cause with predictable effects.** Holbach adopts a mechanistic epistemology: just as a physical cause produces its effect barring interference, so truth produces [[knowledge|understanding]] in minds not blocked by [[opinion|prejudice]] or [[interest|self-interest]]. "Truth is a cause; it produces necessarily its effect when its impulse is not interrupted by causes which stop its action" (ch. II). The problem is not that truth is weak but that powerful institutions have a vested interest in bloc[[monarch|king]] it.

**2. [[religion|Religion]] systematically suppresses truth.** [[priest|Priest]]s and tyrants conspire to keep people ignorant because "ignorance and servitude have a tendency to make men wicked and unhappy" while "[[knowledge|science]], reason, liberty, alone can reform them" (ch. II). The suppression of truth is not [[chance|accident]]al to religion but constitutive of it: religion demands belief without [[proof|evidence]], which is the opposite of truth-see[[monarch|king]].

**3. Truth is accessible to all through reason.** Holbach rejects the esoteric conception of truth as something available only to a priestly or philosophical elite. Any honest person who "seeks it in good [[religion|faith]]" can arrive at truth through the proper use of reason and experience. The obstacles are institutional, not cognitive.

**4. Truth has moral consequences.** For Holbach, truth-see[[monarch|king]] is not merely an intellectual [[morality|virtue]] but a moral imperative. False beliefs — particularly religious ones — cause real harm: [[cruelty|persecution]], war, wasted lives, and stunted intellects. Truth liberates not just the mind but the whole person.

## Reception and Critique

Holbach's truth-optimism was contested [[eve]]n within the Enlightenment. [[voltaire|Voltaire]], though a fierce critic of clerical authority, doubted that the masses could be trusted with unmediated truth and preferred a rationalized [[theism|deism]] as a social stabilizer. Rousseau argued that the pursuit of truth could corrupt moral sentiment if divorced from con[[knowledge|science]]. Later, Nietzsche would invert Holbach's framework entirely, questioning whether truth itself was anything more than "a mobile army of metaphors" — a critique that, ironically, echoes Holbach's own analysis of theological concepts as "ideas without models."

## Relation to Other Concepts

Truth is the goal toward which [[Reason]] aspires and the casualty of [[Faith]]'s demands. It is opposed to [[Superstition]], [[Ignorance]], and [[Prejudice]], and allied with [[Science]], [[Liberty]], and [[Morality]]. Holbach's concept of truth underpins his critique of [[Theology]] — which he sees as a systematic evasion of truth — and his defense of [[Happiness]], which requires truthful [[knowledge|understanding]] of human [[universe|nature]] and the conditions of [[happiness|well-being]]. The suppression of truth by [[authority|religious authority]] is, in Holbach's analysis, the original crime from which all other social pathologies flow.

## In the Text

Holbach opens his critique by identifying how theological concepts corrupt the most basic truths:

> "By the aid of this systematic nonsense, all nature has become an inexplicable enigma for man; the visible world has disappeared to give place to invisible regions; reason is obliged to give place to imagination." (002-002)

On the natural efficacy of truth when not obstructed:

> "Truth is a cause; it produces necessarily its effect when its impulse is not interrupted by causes which stop its action. It is entirely impossible to have the best arguments accepted by men who are strongly interested in error; who are prejudiced in its favor; who refuse to reflect; but it must necessarily be that truth undeceives the honest souls who seek it in good faith." (002-139)

On the political consequences of truth's suppression:

> "He, like the tyrants of this world, will be offended by the truth, and redouble the weight of His malice and tyranny upon them." (002-158)

On the power of truth against [[miracle|miracles]]:

> "Is a miracle capable of destroying a demonstrated truth? Although a man should see the sun, the stars, and all nature obey his voice, he would not be believed if he cried that all nature was belied by a contradiction." (130-002)
