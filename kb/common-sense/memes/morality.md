# Morality

## Definition

Morality, in Holbach's philosophy, is the set of principles that govern human conduct — not as [[divinity|divine]] commandments handed down through revelation, but as rational inferences from human [[universe|nature]], social needs, and the universal pursuit of happiness. "They have only to look within themselves, to reflect upon their own [[universe|nature]], to consult their obvious interests, to consider the object of society," he w[[ritual|rites]], "and they will easily understand that virtue is an advantage, and that vice is an injury to [[existence|being]]s of their species" (ch. II). Morality is thus a matter of [[wisdom|practical wisdom]], not theological obedience.

## Historical Context

The question of whether morality requires a religious foundation was among the most explosive of the Enlightenment. Orthodox [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian doctrine held that without [[divinity|divine]] law and the threat of [[hell|eternal punishment]], human [[existence|being]]s would descend into license and chaos. The *philosophes* challenged this claim from multiple directions: Shaftesbury and Hutcheson proposed an innate "[[conscience|moral sense]]"; [[david-hume|Hume]] grounded morality in sympathy and social convention; Bentham developed utilitarian calculus. Holbach's contribution was to argue not merely that secular morality was *possible* but that religious morality was *worse* — that the fear of gods produced not virtue but hypocrisy, [[zeal|fanaticism]], and cruelty.

## Core Arguments

**1. Morality does not require [[god|God]].** This is perhaps Holbach's most scandalous claim. He argues that human [[existence|being]]s have natural motives for moral behavior — empathy, [[interest|self-interest]], the [[passion|desire]] for social approval — that are more reliable than the fear of [[punishment|divine punishment]]. "Men will be good when they are well taught, well governed, chastised or censured for the [[injustice|evil]], and justly rewarded for the good" (ch. II).

**2. Religious morality is actively harmful.** Far from ma[[monarch|king]] people better, religion makes them worse: it encourages them to neglect this-[[universe|world]]ly ethics in favor of ritual observance, to persecute others for doctrinal differences, and to excuse cruelty when performed in [[god|God]]'s name.

**3. [[virtue|Virtue]] is its own reward.** Holbach's ethics are eudaimonistic: virtuous behavior conduces to happiness because it aligns the individual with the requirements of social life. "[[virtue|Virtue]] is an advantage" — it makes the virtuous person better off, not just morally superior.

**4. [[education|Education]], not prayer, produces moral behavior.** If morality is a matter of [[knowledge|understanding]] one's true interests and developing good habits, then education — "let the mind be filled early with true ideas" — is the primary moral instrument, displacing religious [[education|instruction]].

## Relation to Other Concepts

Morality is the practical application of [[Reason]] to human conduct. It depends on a correct [[knowledge|understanding]] of [[Nature]] — including human [[universe|nature]] — and is undermined by [[Superstition]], [[Ignorance]], and the fear of imaginary [[God]]s. It is allied with [[Justice]], [[Liberty]], and [[Happiness]], and opposed to the [[Faith]]-based ethics that Holbach sees as producing cruelty and hypocrisy. The concept forms a bridge between Holbach's metaphysical [[materialism|naturalism]] and his social and political program.

## In the Text

Holbach's core argument for secular morality:

> "They have only to look within themselves, to reflect upon their own nature, to consult their obvious interests, to consider the object of society and of each of the members who compose it, and they will easily understand that virtue is an advantage, and that vice is an injury to beings of their species." (ch. II)

On the insufficiency of [[fear|religious fear]] as a moral motivator:

> "Ignorance and servitude have a tendency to make men wicked and unhappy. Science, reason, liberty, alone can reform them and render them more happy; but everything conspires to blind them and to confirm them in their blindness. The priests deceive them, tyrants corrupt them in order to subjugate them more." (ch. II)
