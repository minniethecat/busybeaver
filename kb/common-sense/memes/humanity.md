# Humanity

## Definition

In Holbach's *Common Sense*, "humanity" operates on two interdependent levels.

As a descriptive term — the human race, mankind, human [[universe|nature]] — it designates the human species as a natural phenomenon whose characteristics and capacities are determined entirely by physical organization and environmental circumstance, not by [[divinity|divine]] decree or supernatural destiny.

As a prescriptive [[model|ideal]] — humanity as a moral [[morality|virtue]] — it names the disposition of [[goodness|benevolence]], [[toleration|tolerance]], and rational fellow-feeling that ought to govern relations among members of the species.

Holbach insists that these two dimensions are inseparable. The [[doctrine|theological doctrine]] that human [[universe|nature]] is "depraved" — corrupted by [[sin|original sin]] and requiring supernatural [[salvation|redemption]] — simultaneously falsifies what humanity is and undermines the possibility of genuine humane conduct.

To know what humanity is, one must study the actual human [[existence|being]] as revealed by experience and reason; to practice humanity as a [[morality|virtue]], one must organize society according to the real needs and capacities discovered by that study. A morality that contradicts the [[universe|nature]] of man is, as Holbach repeatedly argues, not made for man at all.

Holbach's conception is grounded in his materialist anthropology. Human [[existence|being]]s differ from other [[soul|anima]]ls only by the greater complexity of their physical organization, which causes them to produce effects of which other species are not capable.

The differences among individual humans — between a [[john-locke|Locke]] and a peasant, between a sage and a tyrant — are equally explicable by variations in physical [[temperament|constitution]], education, and circumstance. There is no immaterial soul that elevates humanity above [[universe|nature]]; there is no innate depravity that degrades it below what [[universe|nature]] has made it. Humanity is what [[universe|nature]], experience, and education make of it.

## Historical Context

Holbach's treatment of humanity belongs to the radical wing of the Enlightenment's rethin[[monarch|king]] of human [[universe|nature]].

The seventeenth and eighteenth centuries had witnessed a profound transformation in conceptions of what it means to be human: from [[rene-descartes|Descartes]]' dualism, which located human dignity in the immaterial soul, to [[john-locke|Locke]]'s empiricism, which traced all ideas to [[experience|sensory experience]], to Rousseau's ambivalent celebration of natural man.

Holbach pushed this trajectory to its materialist conclusion: humanity is entirely a product of [[universe|nature]], and all claims about supernatural origins, destinies, or corruptions are fictions imposed upon an ignorant and credulous species by [[priest|priestcraft]] and [[tyranny|despotism]].

The polemical target of Holbach's anthropology is the [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian doctrine of [[original-sin|the Fall]] — the claim that human [[universe|nature]], though created perfect, became corrupted through [[adam|Adam]]'s [[vice|sin]] and now requires [[divinity|divine]] grace for its repair. Holbach reverses this narrative.

Human [[universe|nature]] is not depraved; the passions that [[religion|theology]] condemns are the very essence of man. What [[religion|theology]] calls depravity is simply the natural operation of human [[passion|desire]]s and aversions, which become harmful only when misdirected by ignorance, [[Superstition]], and oppressive institutions.

The real depravity lies in the theological system itself, which, by [[doctrine|teaching]] men to despise their own [[universe|nature]] and to fear an imaginary tyrant, produces the very misery and vice it claims to remedy.

This argument situates Holbach within a broader Enlightenment project of reclaiming humanity from theological anthropology. Where earlier thinkers like [[pierre-bayle|Pierre Bayle]] and [[voltaire|Voltaire]] had criticized specific doctrines while maintaining a theistic framework, Holbach insisted that no theistic framework could be reconciled with an honest account of human experience. The human race cannot be understood as the object of [[divinity|divine]] [[Providence]] when history [[demon|demons]]trates the opposite: constant [[evil|suffering]], exploitation, and devastation.

## Core Arguments

1. **The human race is not the object and end of creation.** The claim that [[god|God]] created [[nature|the universe]] for humanity's benefit is a flattering illusion contradicted by all available [[proof|evidence]]. If [[earth|the world]] were made for man, man would be happy; instead, mankind is "the continual victim of physical and [[evil|moral evil]]s" (ch. 52). The theological assertion that the human race was included in [[god|God]]'s plan so that "He might have somebody to admire and glorify Him" (ch. 49) collapses under scrutiny — an infinite [[existence|being]] has no need of homage from feeble creatures.

2. **Human [[universe|nature]] is not depraved.** The claim of innate depravity is both false and harmful. [[passion|Passion]]s are not [[vice|sin]]s but "the very essence of man" (ch. 163). A man without passions would be equally indifferent to vice and to [[morality|virtue]]. The task of morality is not to suppress human [[universe|nature]] but to direct it toward useful objects through education, reason, and just institutions. If human [[universe|nature]] were truly corrupted, the fault would lie with [[creator|the Creator]] who either could not or would not render it perfect.

3. **[[materialism|Materialism]] does not debase the human race.** The objection that [[matter|materialism]] reduces man to a "mere machine" is unfounded. The supposed superiority of [[soul|spirit]] over matter is based entirely on ignorance of the soul's [[universe|nature]]; we are more familiar with matter not because it is less noble but because it is less obscure. The real question is whether humanity is more honored by an honest materialist account or by a [[religion|theology]] that depicts men as depraved [[vice|sin]]ners deserving eternal [[torture|torment]].

4. **Humanity as a [[morality|virtue]] requires liberty of thought.** "[[liberty|Liberty]] of thought can alone give to men humanity and grandeur of soul. The notion of a tyrant [[god|God]] can create but abject, angry, quarrelsome, intolerant slaves" (002-320). The moral disposition of humanity — [[goodness|benevolence]], [[toleration|tolerance]], compassion — cannot flourish under a theological system that teaches men to hate those whom [[god|God]] supposedly hates. Religious particularism undermines the universal fellow-feeling that true humanity demands.

5. **Historical [[proof|evidence]] refutes theological claims about humanity.** History "shows us that the human race in all ages has been [[torture|torment]]ed and devastated by tyrants, conquerors, heroes; by wars, inundations, famines, epidemics" (ch. 56). This long catalogue of [[evil|suffering]] is incompatible with the notion of a benevolent providence overseeing human affairs for the good of the species.

## Reception and Critique

Holbach's conception of humanity was received with hostility by the religious establishment of his time and with enthusiasm by radical Enlightenment circles.

His insistence on the natural goodness of human [[universe|nature]] — properly understood — anticipated elements of nineteenth-century humanism and secular [[morality|ethics]] while diverging from Rousseau's more sentimental [[materialism|naturalism]].

Critics have charged that Holbach's materialist reduction of humanity to organized matter fails to account for the qualitative distinctiveness of human experience — consciousness, moral reasoning, aesthetic sensibility. The counter-argument, implicit in Holbach's own work, is that [[secrecy|mystery]] is not expla[[nations|nation]]: invo[[monarch|king]] an immaterial soul does not clarify anything about human [[universe|nature]]; it merely relocates the [[secrecy|mystery]] to an inaccessible realm.

A more substantive criticism concerns the optimism of Holbach's moral vision. If human [[existence|being]]s are entirely products of [[universe|nature]] and education, then the project of reforming humanity through enlightened institutions becomes theoretically possible — but Holbach may have underestimated the depth of resistance that [[prejudice|inherited prejudice]]s and material interests would mount against such reform.

Contemporary readers may find Holbach's universalist language about "the human race" both admirable and insufficiently attentive to the historical and [[worship|cult]]ural specificities that shape different human communities. Nevertheless, his central insight — that humanity's welfare depends on [[knowledge|understanding]] what humanity actually is, rather than what [[religion|theology]] claims it must be — remains a foundational principle of secular humanism.

## Relation to Other Concepts

Humanity is the central anthropological concept in Holbach's system and connects to nearly [[eve]]ry other concept in the work.

It is the subject that [[Theology]] systematically misunderstands and the victim that [[Priest]]craft exploits. The relationship between humanity and [[Providence]] is one of radical opposition: the doctrine of providence is refuted by the actual condition of the human race.

Humanity stands in opposition to [[Tyranny]], whether political or theological — the tyrants and conquerors who "destroy the human race" (ch. 96) are the most detestable [[existence|being]]s in [[universe|nature]] precisely because they are the most destructive to human welfare.

The concept is intimately linked to [[Morality]], [[vice|sin]]ce the only valid morality is one grounded in human [[universe|nature]] as it actually exists. It connects to [[Reason]] as the faculty whose exercise alone can liberate humanity from the [[prejudice|prejudices]] that cause its misery.

Humanity also relates to [[Materialism]], which Holbach defends as the only intellectually honest account of what the human [[existence|being]] is — and therefore the only foundation on which a humane [[morality|ethics]] can be built. Finally, humanity as a [[morality|virtue]] connects to [[Virtue]] itself: the humane person is the virtuous person, and the virtuous society is the humane society.

## In the Text

> "Man, charmed with his own merits, imagines that it is but his own kind that God proposed as the object and the end in the formation of the universe. Upon what is this so flattering opinion based? ... In a word, I see the human race continually occupied in protecting itself from the wicked tricks of this Providence, which is said to be busy with the care of their happiness." (002-064)

Holbach opens his analysis of humanity by dismantling the anthropocentric illusion. The claim that [[nature|the universe]] was made for man is flattering but entirely without [[proof|evidence]]. The actual condition of the human race — toiling perpetually against natural and social calamities — refutes the notion of a benevolent [[providence|Providence]] attending to human welfare.

> "A morality which contradicts the nature of man is not made for him. But you will say that man's nature is depraved. In what consists this pretended depravity? Is it because he has passions? But are not passions the very essence of man? Must he not seek, desire, love that which is, or that which he believes to be, essential to his happiness?" (002-340)

This passage is the foundation of Holbach's moral anthropology. The [[doctrine|theological doctrine]] of depravity is refuted by a simple appeal to observation: what [[religion|theology]] calls corruption is simply the natural operation of human [[passion|desire]]. The remedy is not supernatural grace but rational education that directs passions toward socially beneficial objects.

> "Liberty of thought can alone give to men humanity and grandeur of soul. The notion of a tyrant God can create but abject, angry, quarrelsome, intolerant slaves. Every religion which supposes a God easily irritated, jealous, vindictive ... such a religion becomes necessarily turbulent, unsocial, sanguinary." (ch. 156)

Here Holbach connects the two dimensions of humanity with maximum force. The theological conception of [[god|God]] as a jealous tyrant does not merely produce [[error|false belief]]s — it produces a degraded human character, incapable of the [[toleration|tolerance]] and [[goodness|benevolence]] that constitute true humanity. Only [[liberty|freedom]] of thought can restore to men the humanity that religion has taken from them.
