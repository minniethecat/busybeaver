# Secrecy

## Definition

In Holbach's critique of organized religion, secrecy — the deliberate concealment of doctrines and practices from rational exami[[nations|nation]] — is a central mechanism of priestly control. Religious "[[mysteries]]" are not genuine truths too profound for human [[knowledge|understanding]]; they are fictions deliberately kept obscure to prevent the laity from detecting their [[absurdity|absurd]]ity.

Holbach treats secrecy as an epistemological strategy: by claiming that [[divinity|divine]] matters are "impenetrable [[mysteries]]," [[clergy|the clergy]] place their [[doctrine|teaching]]s beyond the reach of reason and [[proof|evidence]], ma[[monarch|king]] criticism impossible and belief mandatory.

## Holbach's Analysis

**1. Obscurity serves institutional power.** Holbach states bluntly: "If religion was clear, it would have fewer attractions for the ignorant. They need obscurity, [[mysteries]], fables, [[miracle|miracles]], incredible things, which keep their brains perpetually at work." Confusion is not a bug but a feature — it sustains the cognitive dependency of the believer on the priestly interpreter.

**2. "[[mystery|Mystery]]" is an evasion, not an expla[[nations|nation]].** When asked to explain the soul's union with the body, theologians "tell you that they know nothing about it; that it is a mystery to them." But labeling a contradiction a "mystery" does not resolve it — it merely signals that the theory has failed.

**3. [[mystery|Mystery]]-talk shields [[god|God]] from moral critique.** When confronted with [[evil|the problem of evil]], believers retreat to "the designs of [[god|God]] are [[mysteries]]" — a move Holbach rejects as self-defeating. If [[god|God]]'s conduct is incomprehensible, "it is impossible for me to judge whether [[god|God]] is intelligent."

**4. Meslier as secret dissident.** The historical case of [[meslier|Jean Meslier]] — the priest who "worked his whole life secretly in order to attack the opinions he believed false" — illustrates Holbach's point: [[eve]]n [[clergy|the clergy]] themselves, when they possess genuine knowledge, must conceal their true views. Secrecy protects the institution, not the truth.

## In the Text

On the appeal of obscurity (ch. XI):

> "If religion was clear, it would have fewer attractions for the ignorant. They need obscurity, mysteries, fables, miracles, incredible things, which keep their brains perpetually at work."
>
> (002-012)On the "mystery" evasion (ch. LXXVII):

> "We are told that the enormous distance which separates God from men, makes God's conduct necessarily a mystery for us, and that we have no right to interrogate our Master."
>
> (002-124)On the limits of mystery claims:

> "If God's conduct is a mystery to me, it is not made for me. Man can not adore, admire, respect, or imitate a conduct of which everything is impossible to conceive."
>
> (002-124)On the soul-body problem:

> "You will tell us that this harmony is a mystery; and I will tell you that I do not see my soul, that I know and feel but my body."
>
> (002-179)On Meslier's secret dissent:

> "The curate Meslier, the most singular phenomenon ever seen among all the meteors fatal to the Christian religion, worked his whole life secretly in order to attack the opinions he believed false."

## Relation to Other Concepts

Secrecy is the institutional counterpart to [[credulity]] — the one conceals, the other accepts without question. It connects to [[priestcraft]], [[mystery]], [[ignorance]], and [[authority]]. The concept stands in opposition to [[reason]], [[transparency]], and [[knowledge]]. Holbach's call to "let the human mind occupy itself with natural things, intelligible objects, sensible truths" is an explicit rejection of the [[worship|cult]]ure of secrecy.
