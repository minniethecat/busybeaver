# simplicity

Holbach uses **simplicity** in a pejorative sense — the "foolish simplicity" of religious believers who, without noticing the contradiction, call God good even as they attribute the worst calamities (pestilence, war, famine) to His will. For Holbach, this is not piety but logical inconsistency. (002-153)

## Simplicity as Foolish Credulity

The "foolish simplicity" that Holbach criticizes is not the virtue of straightforward honesty but rather an intellectual laziness that refuses to examine religious claims critically. It is "a plain contradiction" (002-153; 089-003) to attribute both benevolence and destructive acts to the same being without recognizing the logical incompatibility. The believer who calls God good while accepting that God sends pestilence and famine has simply failed to think through the implications of their own theological assertions.

## Cultural Simplicity and Religious Manipulation

Holbach draws a comparison between the credulity of religious believers and the simplicity of primitive peoples. "We ridicule the simplicity of some nations whose fashion it is to believe that during eclipses of the moon a dragon is devouring it" (002-184; 102-002). Yet, he suggests, the theological doctrines accepted by supposedly civilized Europeans are no less absurd — merely dressed in more sophisticated language. The same credulity that leads a savage to fear a dragon leads an educated Christian to accept the doctrine of the Trinity.

## Meslier's Simplicity as a Model

In a striking inversion, Holbach praises the simplicity of Jean Meslier, the atheist priest whose testament provided much of the source material for *Common Sense*. Voltaire describes Meslier's writing as "written with great simplicity, which unfortunately resembles weakness" (APP007-038), but adds that "the simplicity of this man, the purity of his morals, and the strength of his reasons should make all Christians blush" (APP007-081). Here simplicity becomes a virtue — the honest, unadorned language of a man who speaks truth without the ornamentation of theological sophistry.
