# Epicurus

## Biography

Epicurus (341–270 BCE) was an ancient Greek philosopher born on the island of Samos to Athenian parents. He founded his school, the Garden, in Athens around 306 BCE, where he taught a comprehensive philosophy combining atomistic physics, empiricist epistemology, and an [[morality|ethics]] centered on the pursuit of *ataraxia* (tranquility). Unlike the other Athenian schools, the Garden admitted women and slaves as equal members. Though Epicurus wrote prolifically — over three hundred works by Diogenes Laërtius's count — only three letters and a collection of maxims survive intact, ma[[monarch|king]] him one of antiquity's most influential but fragmentary thinkers.

## Role in Religious History

Epicurus was antiquity's most systematic critic of super[[theism|natural religion]]. His atomism, inherited from [[democritus|Democritus]] and [[leucippus|Leucippus]], explained the [[universe|cosmos]] entirely in terms of atoms moving through void — no [[divinity|divine]] creation, no providence, no [[soul|immortal soul]]. The gods existed, in Epicurean [[religion|theology]], but they dwelt in blissful indifference in the *intermundia* (spaces between [[universe|world]]s), utterly unconcerned with human affairs. This "[[religion|theology]]" was in practice [[atheist|atheism]] — it removed the gods from any causal role in [[universe|nature]] or morality. Epicurus's most famous religious contribution is his trilemma on [[injustice|evil]], preserved by Lactantius: if [[god|God]] is willing but unable to prevent [[injustice|evil]], He is impotent; if able but unwilling, He is malevolent; if both able and willing, why does [[injustice|evil]] exist?

## Connection to "Common Sense"

Holbach invokes Epicurus at a pivotal moment in *Common Sense*. Having argued that [[god|God]] cannot or will not remove [[injustice|evil]] from the earth, Holbach quotes Lactantius's summary of the Epicurean dilemma in full — a passage that has haunted theistic philosophy for over two thousand years:

> "More than two thousand years ago, according to Lactance, the wise Epicurean said: 'Either God wants to prevent evil, and can not, or He can and will not; or He neither can nor will, or He will and can. If He wants to, without the power, He is impotent; if He can, and will not, He is guilty of malice which we can not attribute to Him; if He neither can nor will, He is both impotent and wicked, and consequently can not be God; if He wishes to and can, whence then comes evil, or why does He not prevent it?'" — Holbach, *Common Sense*, ch. LVII

In the appendix, Holbach places Epicurus at the head of the materialist lineage: "[[leucippus|Lucippe]], [[democritus|Democritus]], Epicurus, Straton, and some other Greeks, dared to tear away the thick veil of [[opinion|prejudice]], and to deliver philosophy from theological fetters." Then, among the moderns: "[[hobbes|Hobbes]], [[baruch-spinoza|Spinoza]], [[pierre-bayle|Bayle]], and others have followed the path of Epicurus." Epicurus is thus the founding father of the tradition Holbach claims to complete — two millennia of thinkers who sought natural, not supernatural, expla[[nation|nations]] of [[earth|the world]].

## Legacy

Epicurus's influence flows through Lucretius's poem *De Rerum Natura*, which transmitted atomism to the Latin West, through Gassendi's seventeenth-century revival of Epicurean physics, and into the French materialist tradition that culminated in Holbach's *Système de la [[nature|Nature]]*. For Holbach, Epicurus was more than a historical reference — he was [[evidence|proof]] that rational, naturalistic philosophy was not a modern aberration but a recovery of the best of ancient thought. The [[problem-of-evil|problem of evil]] that Epicurus formulated, Holbach insists, has never been resolved — and never can be within a theistic framework.
