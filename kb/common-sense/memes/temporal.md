# Temporal Power

## Definition

[[power|Temporal power]], in Holbach's political lexicon, denotes the legitimate civil and governmental authority exercised by [[monarch|sovereign]]s over their subjects in matters of this [[universe|world]] — law, policy, security, and public welfare. It stands in constant tension with *[[soul|spirit]]ual power*, the rival authority claimed by [[clergy|the clergy]] on behalf of a celestial monarch. Holbach's analysis of this conflict is among his most penetrating political insights: by associating the [[deity|Deity]] with government, [[monarch|sovereign]]s unwittingly "accept His ministers as rivals or as masters" (ch. 173). The temporal-[[soul|spirit]]ual distinction becomes the framework for [[knowledge|understanding]] how religion systematically weakens [[government|the state]], creates double legislation, and places "the greatest physical power" in subordi[[nations|nation]] to "the [[soul|spirit]]ual power of opinion" (ch. 173).

## Historical Context

The conflict between temporal and [[soul|spirit]]ual authority defined [[europe|Europe]]an politics from the Investiture Controversy of the 11th century through the [[reform|Reform]]ation and the [[crusades|religious wars]] of the 16th and 17th centuries. By Holbach's time, the Gallican tradition in [[paris|France]] had long asserted the independence of the French crown from papal authority, yet [[clergy|the clergy]] retained vast privileges: exemption from taxation, control over education, censorship powers, and enormous landholdings. Holbach's analysis radicalizes the Gallican position: rather than merely asserting the independence of temporal power, he argues that *any* alliance between [[politics|religion and politics]] is "fatal to the people and to the [[monarch|king]]s" (ch. 173). The solution is not a reformed church-state relationship but the complete separation of religion from [[government|governance]].

## Core Arguments

Holbach's analysis of temporal power proceeds through several key arguments:

1. **The Double Legislation Problem**: "The union of religion with politics has necessarily caused a double legislation in the [[politics-index|State]]s. The law of [[god|God]], interpreted by His [[clergy|priests]], is often contrary to the law of the [[monarch|sovereign]] or to the interest of the [[politics-index|State]]" (ch. 173). Citizens cannot serve two masters.

2. **[[spirit|Spirit]]ual [[power|Power]] Subordinates Temporal Power**: "The temporal [[monarch|prince]]s understand that the greatest physical power is compelled to surrender to the [[soul|spirit]]ual power of opinion" (ch. 173). [[priest|Priest]]s, claiming to speak for [[god|God]], can override [[monarch|king]]s who claim to rule by [[god|God]]'s authority.

3. **[[religion|Religion]] Creates a [[politics-index|State]] Within the [[politics-index|State]]**: The [[priest|clergy]] form a corporation with its own laws, property, and loyalties, answerable not to the [[monarch|sovereign]] but to a foreign [[soul|spirit]]ual authority. This "is most dangerous for a [[monarch|prince]]" because any attempt at reform is met with the claim that "[[god|God]] is never more angry than when the [[theology-index|Divine]] rights, the privileges, the possessions, and the immunities of His [[clergy|priests]] are interfered with" (ch. 173).

4. **[[clergy|Clergy]] Exploit Temporal [[authority|Authority]] Selectively**: [[priest|Priest]]s support the temporal [[monarch|sovereign]] "except when it suits their temporal interests" (ch. 146). Religious obedience is instrumental and conditional.

5. **The [[person-index|People]] Are Divided in Loyalty**: The religious subject considers [[clergy|priests]] "as [[theology-index|Divine]] men" and obeys them more readily "than his temporal superiors, whom he regards as simple mortals" (ch. 188).

## Reception and Critique

Holbach's theory of temporal power influenced the radical phase of [[revolution|the French Revolution]], particularly the Civil Constitution of the [[clergy|Clergy]] (1790) and the subsequent de-[[jesus-christ|Christ]]ianization campaign. The separation of church and state, achieved in [[paris|France]] in 1905, realizes much of Holbach's program, though through political compromise rather than philosophical demolition. Critics have noted that Holbach's analysis underestimates the capacity of modern states to co-opt religious institutions for their own purposes — a phenomenon visible in the established churches of Protestant [[europe|Europe]]. Nevertheless, his core insight that claims of [[soul|spirit]]ual authority inherently rival temporal [[government|sovereignty]] remains relevant in any polity where religion claims jurisdiction over civil matters.

## Relation to Other Concepts

[[power|Temporal power]] is the political dimension of Holbach's broader secularism. It contrasts with [[priest]]-ly authority, which claims a rival jurisdiction on behalf of [[god]]. It relates to [[government]] as the specific institutional form temporal power takes. The conflict between temporal and [[soul|spirit]]ual power mirrors the epistemological conflict between [[reason]] and [[superstition]] — one rules by [[proof|evidence]] and law, the other by [[secrecy|mystery]] and fear. [[society]] suffers when temporal authority is compromised, as the people are taught obedience to a celestial monarch over their earthly [[monarch|sovereign]]. The concept also connects to [[tyranny]], [[vice|sin]]ce the alliance of throne and altar produces a double [[despotism|tyranny]] over the people.

## In the Text

Holbach's clearest exposition of the temporal-[[soul|spirit]]ual conflict appears in chapter 173:

> "The sovereigns of this world in associating the Deity in the government of their realms, in pretending to be His lieutenants and His representatives upon earth, in admitting that they hold their power from Him, must necessarily accept His ministers as rivals or as masters." (ch. 173)

He elaborates on the mechanism by which [[soul|spirit]]ual power overcomes temporal force:

> "The temporal princes understand that the greatest physical power is compelled to surrender to the spiritual power of opinion. Nothing is more difficult than to serve two masters, especially when they do not agree upon what they demand of their subjects." (ch. 173)

On the precarious position of reforming [[monarch|sovereign]]s:

> "Nothing is more dangerous for a prince, than to meddle with ecclesiastical affairs (to put his hands into the holy-water pot), that is to say, to attempt the reform of abuses consecrated by religion. God is never more angry than when the Divine rights, the privileges, the possessions, and the immunities of His priests are interfered with." (ch. 173)
