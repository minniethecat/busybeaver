# Earth

## Definition

In Holbach's "Common Sense," earth is not a [[divinity|divine]] creation but a natural product of physical processes — a planet among planets, governed by the same laws that govern all matter. This is the materialist alternative to the theological picture in which earth is the special stage for a drama of [[vice|sin]] and salvation. "Whence comes man? What is his origin? Is he the result of the fortuitous meeting of atoms? Was the first man formed of the dust of the earth? I do not know! Man appears to me to be a production of [[universe|nature]] like all others she embraces" (ch. 2).

Holbach's insistence on earth as our sole home has an ethical urgency. By endlessly repeating "that the earth is not their true country; that the present life is but a passage; that they were not made to be happy in this [[universe|world]]" (ch. 2), religion persuades people to neglect the only existence they will [[eve]]r have. "Tired of an incomprehensible [[religion|theology]], of ridiculous fables, of impenetrable [[mysteries]], of puerile ceremonies, let them at last bring them back to the earth" (Preface). This is the positive core of Holbach's [[matter|materialism]]: redirect attention from imaginary [[hope|heaven]]s to the real [[universe|world]] where human happiness must be built.

## Historical Context

Holbach wrote in the decade before [[revolution|the French Revolution]], at a moment when the new astronomy had dismantled the ancient [[universe|cosmos]]. Copernicus had shown that earth was not the center of [[nature|the universe]]; Galileo had revealed a moon with mountains and a sun with spots — celestial bodies were not perfect spheres of [[divinity|divine]] light but material objects like earth itself. [[isaac-newton|Newton]] had explained planetary motion through the same gravitational laws that governed falling apples.

The theological picture — [[god|God]] created the earth in six days, placed humanity upon it as the crown of creation, and will one day destroy it in a final judgment — was under strain from [[eve]]ry direction. Fossil discoveries suggested a much older earth than biblical chronology allowed. Reports from the Americas described [[nation|peoples]] who had never heard of the [[scripture|Bible]], raising awkward questions about the universality of revelation.

Holbach synthesized these currents into a comprehensive [[materialism|naturalism]]: earth is a planet, humanity is a species that evolved upon it, and the religions that claim otherwise are "the productions of ignorant and savage ages" (ch. 119). He went further than most of his contemporaries in drawing the ethical conclusion — if earth is our only home, then improving earthly life is the only meaningful moral project.

## Core Arguments

**1. Earth is a material object, not a [[divinity|divine]] artifact.** Holbach treats earth as a physical body subject to natural laws. He notes that "according to the calculations of our chronologists, the earth which we inhabit has existed for six or seven thousand years; during this time the [[nation|nations]] have, under different forms, experienced many vicissitudes" (ch. 2). The chronology he cites is biblical — but his framing treats earth's history as a matter of empirical investigation, not scriptural authority.

**2. The promise of an [[death|afterlife]] devalues the only life we have.** [[religion|Religion]] teaches that "the earth is not their true country" and that "the present life is but a passage." The effect is to make people "neglect their true welfare" in favor of "chimerical hopes." Holbach regards this as a profound moral error: if earth is the only [[universe|world]] we will [[eve]]r inhabit, then improving life on it is the only moral project worth pursuing.

**3. Earth's [[injustice|evil]]s are natural, not supernatural punishments.** Earthquakes, famines, and diseases are physical [[eve]]nts, not [[punishment|divine punishment]]s. "Physical [[injustice|evil]] commonly passes as the punishment of [[vice|sin]]. Calamities, diseases, famines, wars, earthquakes, are the means which [[god|God]] employs to chastise perverse men" (ch. 2). Holbach rejects this [[injustice|theodicy]]: these are [[phenomena|natural phenomena]] that can be understood scientifically and mitigated through human effort — if we stop attributing them to [[god|God]] and start addressing their material causes.

**4. The "gods of the earth" are human tyrants.** Holbach draws an equivalence between the [[hope|heaven]]ly monarch and earthly [[despotism|despot]]s: "Is it surprising, then, that the gods of the earth beli[[eve]] that all is permitted to them, and consider their subjects as vile instruments of their caprices or of their ambition?"
>
> (143-002). Kings who claim to be "the representatives of [[god|God]], His lieutenants upon earth" are freed from accountability, and their subjects are doubly enchained — politically by the [[monarch|sovereign]], [[soul|spirit]]ually by the [[clergy|priests]].

## Reception and Critique

Holbach's earth-centered [[morality|ethics]] influenced the radical Enlightenment's turn toward [[reform|social reform]]. If [[hope|heaven]] is an illusion, then justice must be realized on earth or not at all. This argument was taken up by the [[jacob|Jacob]]ins during [[revolution|the French Revolution]], who replaced the Catholic calendar with a rational one and rededicated churches as [[temple|Temple]]s of [[reason|Reason]].

Romantic critics, from Rousseau onward, objected that Holbach's [[matter|materialism]] reduced the earth to a mechanism and stripped it of the sacred qualities that had inspired art, poetry, and ethical restraint. The earth was not just a rock — it was a home, a mother, a living presence. Holbach would likely reply that treating earth as a material object to be understood and improved is more useful than treating it as a sacred [[secrecy|mystery]] to be worshiped.

In contemporary environmental philosophy, Holbach's [[matter|materialism]] has an ambiguous legacy. On one hand, his rejection of the sacred prepared the way for the instrumental domi[[nations|nation]] of [[universe|nature]] that environmentalists criticize. On the other, his insistence that earth is our only home — that there is no escape hatch to another [[universe|world]] — anticipates the core argument of modern environmentalism: we must care for this planet because there is no other.

## Relation to Other Concepts

Earth stands in opposition to [[Heaven]], which Holbach regards as an imaginary compensation for real [[evil|suffering]]. The doctrine of [[Creation]] is the theological claim that earth was made by [[god|God]] — a claim Holbach rejects as unsupported by [[proof|evidence]] and contradicted by the existence of [[evil|natural evil]]. [[Nature]] is the broader system of which earth is a part — a self-organizing material reality that requires no supernatural expla[[nations|nation]].

Earth connects to [[Politics]] because Holbach argues that the "gods of the earth" ([[monarch|king]]s and [[clergy|priests]]) use the promise of [[hope|heaven]] to pacify populations who might otherwise demand justice. It connects to [[Reason]] because only reason — not revelation — can teach us how the earth actually works and how to improve life upon it.
