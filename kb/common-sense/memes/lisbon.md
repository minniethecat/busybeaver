# Lisbon

## Definition

**Lisbon** — the capital of Portugal — enters Holbach's "Common Sense" through a vignette of popular [[belief|credulity]]: "the bells of Lisbon rang of themselves, without any one knowing how it was done" (ch. II). But the city's deeper significance in the Holbachian universe is indirect: the Lisbon earthquake of November 1, 1755 — which destroyed the city, killed tens of thousands, and notably spared the brothels while collapsing the churches — was the [[eve]]nt that, more than any other, shattered [[enlightenment|Enlightenment]] [[certainty|confidence]] in [[providence|divine providence]].

## The Self-Ringing Bells

Holbach's direct reference to Lisbon is a case study in how [[superstition|superstition]] manufactures [[miracle|miracles]] from ordinary [[eve]]nts:

> "The bells of Lisbon rang of themselves, without any one knowing how it was done; it was thus that the miracle was performed." (002-500)

The anecdote, drawn from a contemporary account of alleged [[miracle|miracles]], illustrates the [[belief|credulity]] that Holbach sees as the raw material of [[religion|religion]]. Bells ring — perhaps from seismic tremors, perhaps from wind, perhaps from pranksters — and the [[superstition|superstitious]] mind immediately [[attribute|attributes]] the [[eve]]nt to [[divinity|divine]] intervention. No investigation is performed; no natural expla[[nations|nation]] is sought. The will to beli[[eve]] precedes and precludes the search for [[truth|truth]].

## The 1755 Earthquake

Although Holbach does not discuss the earthquake directly in "Common Sense," its shadow falls across the text. On All [[saint|Saint]]s' Day, 1755, a massive earthquake struck Lisbon, followed by a tsunami and fires that destroyed much of the city. [[organization-index|Churches]] packed with worshippers collapsed; the city's red-light district survived. For the [[enlightenment|Enlightenment]], Lisbon became the definitive counterexample to philosophical optimism — the doctrine, associated with [[gottfried-leibniz|Leibniz]] and Pope, that this is "the best of all possible [[universe|world]]s."

[[voltaire|Voltaire]]'s "Poem on the Lisbon Disaster" (1756) and *Candide* (1759) made the earthquake the emblem of the problem of [[evil|evil]]: how can a benevolent and omnipotent [[god|God]] permit the indiscriminate slaughter of innocents? Holbach's "Common Sense" (1772) systematizes the critique: if the [[universe|world]] is governed by [[providence|providence]], the [[evidence|empirical evidence]] contradicts the claim. The Lisbon earthquake is not an exception to [[providence|providential]] order — it is the norm, and [[providence|providential]] thin[[monarch|king]] is the desperate attempt to explain away the norm.

## Lisbon as Enlightenment Watershed

The Lisbon earthquake marks a turning point in [[enlightenment|Enlightenment]] thought. Before 1755, the dominant tone of [[europe|Europe]]an philosophy was cautiously optimistic: [[reason|reason]] would prevail, [[government|governance]] would improve, and [[god|God]]'s [[providence|providence]] would be vindicated. After Lisbon, a darker strain emerged — the strain that runs through Holbach, through Sade, through the radical [[enlightenment|Enlightenment]] that abandoned the project of reconciling [[reason|reason]] with [[religion|faith]] and opted instead for the outright rejection of [[religion|religion]].
