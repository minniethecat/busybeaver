# Mystery

## Definition

A [[secrecy|mystery]], in Holbach's analysis, is not a genuine puzzle that invites investigation but a "contradiction, a palpable [[absurdity|absurd]]ity, a notorious impossibility, on which theologians wish to compel men to humbly close the eyes" (ch. 110). It is whatever [[clergy|the clergy]] cannot explain and therefore declares to be beyond expla[[nations|nation]]. The function of [[secrecy|mystery]] is not to mark the limits of human [[knowledge|understanding]] but to immunize religious doctrine against rational scrutiny: if a doctrine is a [[secrecy|mystery]], its incomprehensibility becomes a [[morality|virtue]] rather than a defect. "Every time that we can not see clearly, we are obliged to be guided" (ch. 110) — and guidance, in the [[religion|religious system]], means submission to priestly authority.

## Historical Context

The [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian tradition had long distinguished between truths accessible to natural reason and truths accessible only through revelation — the *mysteria fidei*. Aquinas had argued that some [[divinity|divine]] truths (the [[trinity|Trinity]], the [[incarnation|Incarnation]]) exceed reason but do not contradict it. Holbach radicalizes the Enlightenment critique of this position: [[mysteries]] are not supra-rational but anti-rational. They are not above reason; they are against it. This aligns him with the radical wing of the Enlightenment — Diderot, d'Holbach's salon circle — that rejected [[eve]]n the compromise of [[theism|deism]], insisting that any claim that cannot be rationally justified should be dismissed, not shrouded in the honorific of "[[secrecy|mystery]]."

## Core Arguments

**Mystery Defined as [[contradiction|Contradiction]].** Holbach's definition is deliberately blunt: "a [[secrecy|mystery]] is nothing but a contradiction, a palpable [[absurdity|absurd]]ity, a notorious impossibility" (ch. 110). The [[trinity|Trinity]] is not a profound truth beyond human comprehension; it is a [[contradiction|logical contradiction]] — [[trinity|three persons]] in one substance — that would be rejected immediately in any non-religious context. The [[incarnation|Incarnation]], the [[sacrifice|Atonement]], transubstantiation: each is an assertion that violates the law of non-contradiction, and each is protected by the label "[[secrecy|mystery]]."

**Mystery as Institutional Control.** The social function of [[secrecy|mystery]] is transparent once examined: "It is advantageous for the [[clergy|ministers of religion]] that the people should not comprehend what they are taught. It is impossible for us to examine what we do not comprehend" (ch. 110). If the laity understood doctrine, they would see its [[absurdity|absurd]]ity. Mystery insulates the priestly caste from intellectual challenge by declaring the core doctrines off-limits to reason. [[priest|Priest]]s become necessary mediators between the baffled believer and the incomprehensible [[god|God]].

**Mystery as the [[essence|Essence]] of [[religion|Religion]].** Holbach makes a sweeping claim: "No religion is without [[mysteries]]; [[secrecy|mystery]] is its essence; a religion destitute of [[mysteries]] would be a contradiction of terms" (ch. 111). This is because [[eve]]ry religion posits a [[god|God]] whose [[universe|nature]] is inherently mysterious — invisible, infinite, immaterial — and then compounds the initial obscurity with [[eve]]r more elaborate doctrines. "Every religion announces a concealed [[god|God]], whose essence is a [[secrecy|mystery]]; consequently, it is just as diffi[[worship|cult]] to conceive of His conduct as of the essence of this [[god|God]] Himself" (ch. 112). The foundation is obscure, so the edifice built on it can only grow darker.

**The Aesthetic of Obscurity.** Holbach notes a psychological paradox: the more incomprehensible a doctrine, the more [[divinity|divine]] it appears. "The more obscure a religion is, the more it appears [[divinity|divine]], that is to say, in conformity to the [[universe|nature]] of an invisible [[existence|being]], of whom we have no idea" (ch. 112). This is because "it is the peculiarity of ignorance to prefer the unknown, the concealed, the fabulous, the [[miracle|wonder]]ful, the incredible, [[eve]]n the terrible, to that which is clear, simple, and true" (ch. 112). Mystery feeds the imagi[[nations|nation]] in a way that plain truth cannot, and [[clergy|priests]] exploit this cognitive weakness.

**The Right to Examine.** Against the theological claim that [[god|God]]'s conduct must remain a [[secrecy|mystery]] because of the "enormous distance" between [[creator|Creator]] and creature, Holbach mounts a practical rebuttal: "when my eternal happiness is involved, have I not the right to examine [[god|God]]'s own conduct?" (ch. 77). If a [[god|God]] demands belief on [[suffering|pain]] of [[hell|eternal punishment]], the believer has [[eve]]ry right to scrutinize the terms. A [[god|God]] whose conduct is too mysterious to be judged is not a moral authority but a tyrant who rules by obscurity.

## Relation to Other Concepts

Mystery is the protective s[[punishment|hell]] around [[Dogma]] — [[doctrine|dogma]] provides the content, [[secrecy|mystery]] provides the immunity from criticism. It is the mechanism by which [[Priest]]s maintain authority, the cognitive tool that makes [[Credulity]] a [[morality|virtue]] rather than a vice. The antidote to [[secrecy|mystery]] is [[Science]] and [[Philosophy]] — the systematic application of reason to phenomena, refusing to accept obscurity as a mark of truth. Mystery is also closely related to [[Superstition]]: the superstitious mind actively seeks [[secrecy|mystery]] because it mistakes incomprehensibility for profundity.

## In the Text

On the definition of [[secrecy|mystery]]:

> "What is a mystery? If I examine the thing closely, I discover very soon that a mystery is nothing but a contradiction, a palpable absurdity, a notorious impossibility, on which theologians wish to compel men to humbly close the eyes; in a word, a mystery is whatever our spiritual guides can not explain to us." (ch. 110)

On [[secrecy|mystery]] as institutional power:

> "It is advantageous for the ministers of religion that the people should not comprehend what they are taught. It is impossible for us to examine what we do not comprehend. Every time that we can not see clearly, we are obliged to be guided. If religion was comprehensible, priests would not have so many charges here below." (ch. 110)

On [[secrecy|mystery]] as the essence of religion:

> "The more mysteries a religion has, the more incredible objects it presents to the mind, the better fitted it is to please the imagination of men... The more obscure a religion is, the more it appears divine." (ch. 112)
