# Rome

## Geography and History

Rome is the capital city of Italy, located on the Tiber River in the central-western part of the Italian peninsula. Founded according to legend in 753 BCE, it was the center of the Roman Republic and later the [[roman-empire|Roman Empire]], which at its height controlled the entire Mediterranean ba[[vice|sin]]. After the empire's collapse in the West (476 CE), Rome remained the seat of the [[catholic-church|Papacy]] and the symbolic center of Western [[christianity|Christendom]] throughout the Middle Ages and early modern period. By Holbach's time (18th century), Rome was the capital of the Papal States and the administrative center of the [[catholic-church|Catholic Church]].

## Religious Significance

Rome holds a dual significance in [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian history. It was the city where, according to tradition, the apostles [[peter|Peter]] and [[paul|Paul]] were martyred, and where [[peter|Peter]] established the first [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian [[society|community]] — claims that underpin the papacy's assertion of primacy over all [[church|Christian church]]es. The Pope, as Bishop of Rome, claims direct apostolic succession from [[peter|Peter]]. The Vatican, an independent city-state within Rome [[vice|sin]]ce 1929, houses St. [[peter|Peter]]'s Basilica, the Sistine Chapel, and the administrative apparatus of the [[Catholic Church]].

## Role in "Common Sense"

In Holbach's "Common Sense," Rome appears in the context of early [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian history and the consolidation of ecclesiastical power. [[voltaire|Voltaire]]'s preface notes that the [[miracle|miracles]] of Jesus "were ignored during three hundred years by the whole [[roman-empire|Roman Empire]], and finally passed from the lower class to the palace of the emperors, when policy obliged them to adopt the follies of the people in order the more easily to subjugate them." This passage encapsulates Holbach's view of Rome: the empire that first persecuted, then co-opted, [[christianity|Christianity]] for political purposes.

The [[roman-empire|Roman Empire]]'s adoption of [[christianity|Christianity]] under Constantine is presented not as a triumph of truth but as a political calculation. The implication is that [[church|the Church]] of Rome inherited from the [[roman-empire|Roman Empire]] both its organizational genius and its talent for exploiting popular [[belief|credulity]]. Rome thus symbolizes the fusion of imperial power with [[authority|religious authority]] that Holbach considered the source of so much historical [[evil|suffering]].

## Related Figures and Events

Rome is associated with the early [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian [[martyr|martyrs]] ([[peter|Peter]] and [[paul|Paul]]), the conversion of Emperor Constantine, the establishment of the papacy, and the [[Reformation]] that challenged papal authority. The city also features in the history of the [[Jesuits]], whose founder Ignatius of Loyola established the order's headquarters there. The [[Enlightenment]] thinkers of the 18th century viewed Rome — and the papal court — as the antithesis of reason, progress, and intellectual [[liberty|freedom]].
