# Arabia

## Definition

**Arabia** — the vast peninsula between the Red Sea and the Persian Gulf — enters Holbach's "Common Sense" as the birthplace of [[islam|Islam]], the third [[abraham|Abraham]]ic [[religion|religion]] and the second major target (after [[christianity|Christianity]]) of his critique of revealed [[religion|religion]]. Holbach's treatment of Arabia is characteristically comparative: just as [[christianity|Christianity]] spread from Roman [[judea|Judea]] through imperial adoption, [[islam|Islam]] spread from Arabia through military conquest — and neither mode of propagation provides [[proof|evidence]] of [[divinity|divine]] origin.

## The Sword and the Faith

Holbach's most pointed reference to Arabia concerns the martial character of [[islam|Islam]]ic expansion:

> "Arabians, encouraged by their Divine preceptor, carried the sword and the Koran to the ends of the earth; the gospel was preached by missionaries who risked their lives to propagate it. Both methods prove nothing but the power of religious zeal." (ch. II, ch. 129)

The argument is [[eve]]n-handed in its [[doubt|skepticism]]: whether a [[religion|religion]] spreads by the sword ([[islam|Islam]]) or by [[missionaries|missionary]] persuasion ([[christianity|Christianity]]), the mode of propagation proves nothing about the [[truth|truth]] of the doctrine. The "[[theology-index|Divine]] preceptor" — [[mohammed|Muhammad]] — is treated as a human leader whose followers succeeded through the ordinary mechanisms of military conquest and ideological zeal. Arabia is thus not a "[[sacred|holy]] land" but a point of origin for one more human [[religion|religious]] movement among many.

## The Comparative Frame

Holbach's treatment of Arabia must be understood within his broader comparative project. By placing [[christianity|Christianity]] and [[islam|Islam]] side by side as two Arabian-Near Eastern [[religion|religions]] that conquered [[earth|the world]] — one through the Roman [[government|Empire]], the other through the Caliphate — he relativizes both. The [[paris|French]] Catholic who regards [[islam|Islam]] as a false [[religion|religion]] spread by violence must explain why the same logic does not apply to his own [[religion|faith]], which spread through the Roman [[government|Empire]]'s adoption and subsequent enforcement.

The implicit equivalence is radical: [[christianity|Christianity]] is to the Roman [[government|Empire]] as [[islam|Islam]] is to the Arab conquests. Both are local [[religion|religious]] movements that became global through political and military means, not through the self-evident force of [[truth|truth]].

## Eighteenth-Century European Perspectives

Holbach's treatment of Arabia reflects 18th-century [[europe|Europe]]an assumptions about [[islam|Islam]] and its Prophet. The characterization of [[mohammed|Muhammad]] as a "[[theology-index|Divine]] preceptor" encouraging conquest draws on a long tradition of [[europe|Europe]]an polemic. Yet Holbach's purpose is not to [[guilt|sin]]gle out [[islam|Islam]] for special condem[[nations|nation]] but to use it as a mirror for [[christianity|Christianity]]. If the Arab conquests prove [[islam|Islam]] false, then the Constantinian establishment proves [[christianity|Christianity]] false by the same logic. The blade cuts both ways.
