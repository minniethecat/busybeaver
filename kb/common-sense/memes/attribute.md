# Attribute

## Definition

Attribute, in Holbach's critique of [[religion|theology]], refers to the qualities or perfections that theologians ascribe to [[god|God]] — omnipotence, omni[[knowledge|science]], infinite goodness, immutability, justice, and so forth. Holbach's strategy is not to deny any [[vice|sin]]gle attribute but to [[demon|demons]]trate that the ensemble of divine attributes is logically incoherent: each attribute contradicts the others, and the combined list describes a [[existence|being]] that "can not exist" (ch. 109). The attribute critique is thus a *reductio ad [[absurdity|absurd]]um* of [[divinity|theism]].

## Historical Context

The enumeration and harmonization of divine attributes was a central project of medieval scholasticism. Thomas Aquinas, Maimonides, and Avicenna each developed sophisticated accounts of how attributes like omnipotence and goodness could coexist in a simple, immutable [[existence|being]]. The problem of [[injustice|evil]] — how can an omnipotent, perfectly good [[god|God]] permit [[evil|suffering]]? — has always been the sharpest test of attribute coherence. Holbach does not attempt to solve the problem; he uses its insolubility as [[proof|evidence]] that the very concept of [[god|God]] is "an idle [[imagination|fancy]]" (ch. 109). His approach anticipates the logical problem of [[injustice|evil]] developed by J.L. Mackie in the twentieth century.

## Core Arguments

**1. [[theology-index|Divine]] attributes are mutually contradictory.** "[[nothingness|Nothing]] is more evident or more clearly [[demon|demons]]trated than that a [[existence|being]] can not combine qualities so dissimilar, so contradictory, so irreconcilable as those which all the religions of the earth ascribe to [[divinity|Divinity]]" (ch. 109). Holbach lists the contradictions: [[god|God]] is said to be very good and very wicked, very powerful and very weak, immutable and changeable, perfectly intelligent and perfectly destitute of reason.

**2. Any [[vice|sin]]gle attribute collapses under scrutiny.** "Let us attempt to attribute but a [[vice|sin]]gle quality to [[divinity|Divinity]], and what is said of it will be contradicted immediately by the effects we assign to this cause" (ch. 109). The problem is not merely that the attributes conflict with each other, but that each attribute taken alone conflicts with observed reality.

**3. [[infinite|Infinite]] attributes preclude relation to finite [[existence|being]]s.** If [[god|God]] is infinite, then no finite [[existence|being]] can know, relate to, or have duties toward Him. "To say that [[god|God]] is infinite, is to destroy Him for men, or at least render Him perfectly useless" (ch. 2). The [[infinite|infinity]] attribute alone suffices to make religion impossible.

**4. Attributes are human projections, not [[revelation|divine revelation]]s.** Holbach argues that divine attributes are not discovered through revelation but invented by human imagi[[nations|nation]]: "Every body tells us that the [[essence|essence of God]] is incomprehensible to man; at the same time they do not hesitate to assign attributes to this incomprehensible [[god|God]]" (ch. 5). Theologians first confess ignorance, then proceed to describe the unknowable in minute detail.

**5. The attribute critique extends to all religions.** Holbach is not attac[[monarch|king]] only [[christianity|Christianity]]. "The theologian's [[god|God]], as well as the [[god|God]] of the theist, is He not evidently a cause incompatible with the effects attributed to Him?" (ch. 109). The critique applies universally — to any [[existence|being]] defined by a set of superlative attributes.

## Reception and Critique

The attribute critique was not original to Holbach; it can be traced through [[pierre-bayle|Bayle]], [[david-hume|Hume]], and the clandestine [[atheism|atheist]]ic literature of the early Enlightenment. What distinguishes Holbach is his systematic thoroughness: he does not rest with one or two contradictions but pursues the attribute critique through the entire chain of [[perfection|divine perfection]]s. Theological responses have generally taken two forms: (a) the attributes are analogical, not univocal — they apply to [[god|God]] in a sense we cannot fully comprehend; (b) the attributes describe [[god|God]]'s essence, not His actions ad extra — [[evil|the problem of evil]] reflects free human choices, not [[divinity|divine]] imperfection. Holbach anticipates and rejects both: analogy is "an idea without model" (ch. 7), and [[liberty|free will]] is an illusion.

## Relation to Other Concepts

The attribute critique is the logical engine of Holbach's case against [[God]]. It connects to his epistemology: the attributes are unknowable because [[Finite]] minds cannot comprehend the [[Infinite]]. It supports his [[morality|moral philosophy]]: a [[god|God]] with contradictory attributes cannot serve as a [[Model]] for human conduct. It reinforces his [[Materialism]]: [[matter|material substance]] requires no attributes beyond extension and motion; the multiplication of divine attributes is a symptom of theological fantasy.

## In the Text

Holbach's most comprehensive statement on attribute contradiction:

> "The theologian's God, as well as the God of the theist, is He not evidently a cause incompatible with the effects attributed to Him? In whatever light we may look upon it, we must either invent another God, or conclude that the one which, for so many centuries, has been revealed to mortals, is at the same time very good and very wicked, very powerful and very weak, immutable and changeable, perfectly intelligent and perfectly destitute of reason, of plan, and of means; the friend of order and permitting disorder; very just and very unjust; very skillful and very awkward."
>
> (109-004)

On theologians assigning attributes to an unknowable [[god|God]]:

> "Everybody tells us that the essence of God is incomprehensible to man; at the same time they do not hesitate to assign attributes to this incomprehensible God, and assure us that man can not dispense with a knowledge of this God so impossible to conceive of."
>
> (005-002)
