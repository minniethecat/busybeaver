# Honor

## Definition

Honor, in Holbach's materialist framework, is the natural human concern for reputation, social esteem, and the judgment of others. Unlike religious conceptions that tie honor to [[divinity|divine]] approval or transcendent moral law, Holbach treats honor as an entirely social and psychological phenomenon — the fear of disgrace, ridicule, and the contempt of one's fellow [[existence|being]]s.

For Holbach, honor is not a metaphysical [[morality|virtue]] but a practical restraint grounded in "what will they say?" — the immediate and visible judgment of society. This makes it, in his view, a far more effective check on human conduct than the invisible and merely imagined judgments of a [[god|deity]].

## Historical Context

The concept of honor underwent significant transformation in eighteenth-century Enlightenment thought. In the *ancien régime*, honor was closely tied to aristocratic codes of conduct, dueling, and social rank — a system Montesquieu identified as the principle of monarchical government in *The [[spirit|Spirit]] of the [[law|Law]]s* (1748). [[enlightenment|The Enlightenment]] challenged this aristocratic conception, with thinkers like Rousseau arguing for a more democratic notion of honor based on [[morality|virtue]] and civic worth rather than birth.

Holbach stands at the intersection of these currents. Writing in 1772, he secularizes honor entirely, stripping it of both aristocratic privilege and religious sanction. His treatment follows the materialist logic of Helvétius and Diderot: if all human motivation arises from sensation and [[interest|self-interest]], then honor must be understood as a form of enlightened [[interest|self-interest]] — the [[passion|desire]] to secure the good opinion of others on whom one's [[happiness|well-being]] depends.

## Core Arguments

**1. Visible judgment outweighs invisible judgment.** Holbach's central claim is that people "fear more what they see than what they do not see." The judgments of fellow humans, whose effects one actually experiences, carry more motivational force than the judgments of a [[god|God]] about whom one has only "floating ideas." Even the most devout believer acts differently when observed by another person than when alone — a fact that [[demon|demons]]trates the primacy of social over religious restraint.

**2. The warrior's [[evidence|proof]].** In a stri[[monarch|king]] empirical argument, Holbach points to the soldier who "with the fear of dishonor" [[chance|hazard]]s his life in battle daily, [[eve]]n at the risk of "incurring eternal [[hell|damnation]]." If [[fear|religious fear]] of [[punishment|hell]] were truly the strongest motivator, soldiers would never risk [[hell|damnation]] for the sake of honor. The fact that they routinely do so proves that social incentives outweigh theological ones.

**3. [[religion|Religion]] corrupts the honor of [[monarch|ruler]]s.** Holbach extends his analysis to [[monarch|sovereign]]s, arguing that religion, far from [[existence|being]] a check on [[monarch|prince]]ly power, actually frees [[monarch|ruler]]s from the restraint of honor and [[conscience|remorse]]. By telling [[monarch|prince]]s they are [[god|God]]'s representatives, accountable only to [[hope|heaven]], [[clergy|priests]] "deliver them from fear and [[conscience|remorse]]" — neutralizing the only real checks on arbitrary power.

## In the Text

Holbach's most sustained treatment of honor appears in the chapter titled "Honor Is a More Salutary and a Stronger Check Than [[religion|Religion]]" (ch. CXLII):

> "There is scarcely a man who does not fear more what he sees than what he does not see; the judgments of men, of which he experiences the effects, than the judgments of God, of whom he has but floating ideas. The desire to please the world, the current of custom, the fear of being ridiculed, and of 'what will they say?' have more power than all religious opinions."

He illustrates the point with the example of private versus public behavior (ch. CXLII):

> "A man that firmly believes that God sees everything, knows everything, is everywhere, will, when he is alone, commit actions which he never would do in the presence of the meanest of mortals. Those even who claim to be the most firmly convinced of the existence of a God, act every instant as if they did not believe anything about it."

## Relation to Other Concepts

Honor is connected to [[remorse]] and [[conscience]] — the inward psychological mechanisms that Holbach treats as natural rather than [[divinity|divine]]. It stands in opposition to [[fear]] of supernatural punishment, which Holbach argues is a weaker motivator than social fear. The concept also links to [[virtue]] and [[morality]], which Holbach grounds in [[utility|social utility]] rather than theological command, and to [[despotism]], the political system that flourishes when religion neutralizes the honor-based restraint on [[monarch|ruler]]s.
