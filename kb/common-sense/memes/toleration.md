# Toleration

## Definition

Toleration, in Holbach's political philosophy, is not a concession granted by the powerful to the weak but a logical necessity derived from the [[universe|nature]] of [[belief]] itself. If all beliefs are caused by education, environment, and temperament — if no one freely chooses to beli[[eve]] or disbeli[[eve]] — then punishing someone for holding a mistaken religious opinion is as irrational as punishing them for the color of their eyes. Holbach's defense of toleration is thus grounded not in sentiment but in his materialist [[determinism]]: the [[heresy|heretic]] could not have believed otherwise, and to persecute what could not have been otherwise is to commit an injustice against [[nature]] itself.

## Toleration vs. the Exclusivist God

Holbach identifies the root of [[intolerance]] in the character of the revealed god. A [[god|deity]] who claims exclusive [[truth]], demands universal worship, and punishes [[atheism|unbelief]] with eternal [[damnation]] creates believers who model their earthly conduct on their [[hope|heaven]]ly [[monarch|sovereign]]. "What other passion than frenzied pride can render men so ferocious, so vindictive, so devoid of toleration and gentleness?" (Ch. 96). The answer, for Holbach, is theological certainty — the conviction that one possesses the absolute truth about [[nature|the universe]] and the moral obligation to impose it on others. Toleration can only flourish where theological certainty is replaced by philosophical humility.

## The Political Case for Toleration

Holbach advances a political argument for toleration that anticipates later liberal thought. Religious diversity is an empirical fact of all complex societies. The attempt to impose uniformity through [[cruelty|persecution]] does not eliminate diversity — it merely [[desire|drive]]s dissent underground, produces [[hypocrisy]], and generates political instability. A wise [[monarch|sovereign]], Holbach argues, would recognize that religious opinions are politically irrelevant so long as citizens obey the laws. The [[edict-of-nantes]], which granted limited toleration to French Protestants, exemplifies the kind of practical arrangement that serves the interests of [[government|the state]] far better than the [[cruelty|persecution]] that preceded and followed it.

## Toleration as an Enlightenment Value

Holbach's defense of toleration places him within the mainstream of [[enlightenment]] thought, alongside figures like [[voltaire]], [[john-locke]], and [[pierre-bayle]]. However, his argument is more radical than most: where [[john-locke|Locke]] excludes [[atheism|atheist]]s from toleration on the grounds that they cannot be trusted to keep oaths, and where [[voltaire|Voltaire]] sometimes treats toleration as a prudential compromise, Holbach grounds toleration in metaphysical necessity. Because human actions — including the action of believing — are determined by prior causes, moral blame for incorrect belief is incoherent. Toleration is thus not a policy preference but the only morally intelligible stance.

## Critique of Limited Toleration

Holbach is sharply critical of "toleration" that is merely the absence of active [[cruelty|persecution]] while social and legal disabilities remain. The [[clergy]], he argues, will always prefer [[cruelty|persecution]] when it can be had and toleration only when it cannot. Genuine toleration requires the complete separation of [[authority|religious authority]] from [[politics|political power]] — the dismantling of the [[throne-and-altar]] alliance that enables [[clergy|priests]] to deploy state violence against dissenters. Until religion is treated as a private matter of individual [[remorse|conscience]] rather than a public matter of state enforcement, toleration will remain a fragile and revocable concession.

## In the Text

On theological certainty as the root of [[persecution|intolerance]], and the rarity of genuine toleration:

> "What other passion than frenzied pride can render men so ferocious, so vindictive, so devoid of toleration and gentleness? What is more terrible than the excesses to which men abandon themselves when persuaded that their God demands them?" (Ch. 96)

On the incompatibility of [[divinity|divine]] omnipotence with the tolerance of [[injustice|evil]]:

> "Can God tolerate injustice for an instant? To temporize with injustice when one can prevent it, is to be unjust himself; to tolerate evil which one has the power to prevent, is to consent to it." (Ch. 88)

On the conditional and hypocritical [[universe|nature]] of clerical toleration:

> "Ask them if you must tolerate opinions contrary to those of the Church? They answer you that there is no toleration for the impious, for the enemies of God, for heretics — unless, indeed, they themselves are the ones being persecuted." (Ch. 168)

## Reception and Critique

Holbach's theory of toleration is among the most radical of the Enlightenment, distinguished from its contemporaries by its grounding in determinist metaphysics rather than prudential political calculation. Where [[john-locke|Locke]]'s *Letter Concerning Toleration* (1689) excludes Catholics and [[atheism|atheist]]s, and where [[voltaire|Voltaire]]'s *Treatise on Tolerance* (1763) appeals to shared humanity and historical example, Holbach derives toleration from the premise that belief is not freely chosen. This argument has been taken up in contemporary debates about the [[morality|ethics]] of belief and the role of moral responsibility in religious conviction. Critics such as Charles Taylor have questioned whether determinist arguments for toleration are self-undermining — if all beliefs are determined, the intolerant person's [[cruelty|persecution]] is equally determined, and there is no normative force to the demand that they change. Holbach would likely respond that while the intolerant persecutor could not have done otherwise, society may still protect itself against him, just as it protects itself against a falling rock without blaming the rock.
