# Jerusalem

## Geography and History

Jerusalem is one of the oldest continuously inhabited cities in [[earth|the world]], located in the [[judea|Judea]]n Mountains between the Mediterranean Sea and the Dead Sea. Its recorded history spans over 3,000 years. It has served as the capital of the ancient Kingdoms of [[israel|Israel]] and Judah, a provincial city under the Roman and Byzantine empires, a contested territory during [[crusades|the Crusades]], and a city under Ottoman rule at the time Holbach's "Common Sense" was published. [[sin|Sin]]ce 1948, it has been a focal point of the [[israel|Israel]]i-Palestinian conflict, with both [[israel|Israel]] and [[palestine|Palestine]] claiming it as their capital.

## Religious Significance

Jerusalem is uniquely sacred to the three [[abraham|Abraham]]ic religions. For Judaism, it is the site of the First and Second [[temple|Temple]]s, the Western Wall, and the capital of the ancient [[david|David]]ic [[monarch|king]]dom. For [[christianity|Christianity]], it is the city of Jesus's crucifixion, burial, and resurrection — the [[eve]]nts on which [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian doctrine rests. The [[church|Church]] of the Holy Sepulchre, the Via Dolorosa, and the Garden of Gethsemane are among its [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian [[sacred|holy]] sites. For [[islam|Islam]], it is the site of the Al-Aqsa Mosque and the Dome of the Rock, from which [[mohammed|Muhammad]] is believed to have ascended to [[hope|heaven]].

## Role in "Common Sense"

Holbach's "Common Sense" references Jerusalem primarily in the context of Jewish and [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian sacred history. The work does not devote sustained attention to the city itself but treats the biblical narratives set there — the actions of Jehovah, the ministry and crucifixion of [[Jesus Christ]], the early church — as products of human imagi[[nations|nation]] rather than [[revelation|divine revelation]]. The critique of biblical history implicitly undermines the sacred status that Jerusalem derives from those narratives.

Holbach's broader argument — that religious claims lack historical credibility — applies directly to Jerusalem's significance. The [[miracle|miracles]] reported to have occurred there, the prophecies centered on it, and the [[divinity|divine]] interventions claimed to have happened within its walls are all subjected to the same rational [[doubt|skepticism]] that Holbach applies to religious claims generally.

## Related Figures and Events

Jerusalem is the setting for the ministry, crucifixion, and reported resurrection of [[Jesus Christ]]. It was the site of the apostolic council described in Acts and the center of the early [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian movement before its expansion into the [[Roman Empire]]. The city's destruction by [[rome|Rome]] in 70 CE dispersed the Jewish and [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian communities, contributing to [[christianity|Christianity]]'s transformation from a Jewish sect into a gentile religion centered on [[Rome]].


> Matthew says, in speaking of Jesus, that, it being reported in Jerusalem
> that a new king of the Jews was born, and that the wise men had come to

> told that this new king was born; and that Joseph and the mother of Jesus,

> flight immediately to Egypt, where they stayed until the death of Herod,
> which happened many years afterward.
