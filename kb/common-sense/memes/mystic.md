# Mystic

## Holbach's Critique of Mystical Interpretation

Holbach identifies **mystical** interpretation — the claim that religious texts possess hidden, spiritual meanings beyond their literal sense — as a key mechanism by which religion immunizes itself against rational criticism. He observes that scripture is filled with "parables, in which is found a mystical sense very different from the literal" (002-249). When the literal meaning of a religious text is absurd, immoral, or factually false, the interpreter retreats to allegory: the text was never meant to be taken literally, but contains a "mystical and spiritual sense which the profane could not understand" (APP006-011). For Holbach, this is not interpretation but evasion — a method that allows any text to mean anything, and therefore nothing. He traces the genealogy of this practice back to antiquity: "theological speculations and mystical reveries of the ancients have, even in our days, the making of the law in a great part of the philosophical world" (002-435). Mysticism, in Holbach's analysis, is the intellectual strategy by which priests transform incoherent fables into unfalsifiable mysteries, placing religious claims permanently beyond the reach of reason and evidence.


## Selected Passage

Holbach argues that mystical interpretation is the strategy by which religion evades rational scrutiny:

> The oracles which the Deity has revealed to the nations through His different mediums, are they clear? Alas! there are not two men who understand them alike. Those who explain them to others do not agree among themselves; in order to make them clear, they have recourse to interpretations, to commentaries, to allegories, to parables, in which is found a mystical sense very different from the literal one. Men are needed everywhere to explain the wishes of God, who could not or would not explain Himself clearly to those whom He desired to enlighten.
>
> (002-249)
