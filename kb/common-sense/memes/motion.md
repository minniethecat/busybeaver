# Motion

## Definition

In Holbach's [[materialism|materialist philosophy]], motion is not a secondary quality imposed upon matter from outside but an essential and inherent property of matter itself. Every substance, whether appearing alive or dead, possesses the capacity for movement as a necessary consequence of its existence. "I am compelled to conclude that motion is as essential to matter as its extension, and that it can not be conceived of without it" (041-002). This claim is radical: it eliminates the need for any [[soul|spirit]]ual "first mover" and places the source of all activity squarely within the [[earth|material world]].

Holbach distinguishes between two kinds of motion: the visible motions of macroscopic bodies, and the invisible internal motions that constitute the very [[existence|being]] of substances.

Even objects that appear inert and dead contain within them the potential for motion, which manifests as soon as they are brought into contact with other bodies under the right conditions. "Dead substances engender motion of themselves" (041-002). From the fermentation of flour and water to the combustion of pyrophorus upon exposure to air, Holbach collects examples to [[demon|demons]]trate that motion arises spontaneously from material interactions, not from supernatural intervention.

## Historical Context

The question of motion's origin was among the most consequential in seventeenth- and eighteenth-century [[science|natural philosophy]]. [[rene-descartes|Descartes]] had reduced matter to pure extension, requiring [[god|God]] to impart motion to an otherwise inert universe. [[isaac-newton|Newton]], despite his laws of motion, retained the idea of [[divinity|divine]] intervention to account for the initial impulse and the maintenance of [[order|cosmic order]]. Both positions preserved a role for a transcendent cause.

Holbach's treatment of motion as an essential property of matter draws on a rival tradition extending back to ancient atomism and, closer to his own time, to [[baruch-spinoza|Spinoza]]'s identification of [[god|God]] with [[nature|Nature]].

Toland's *Letters to Serena* (1704) had already argued for the essential activity of matter, and Diderot's *Letter on the Blind* (1749) and *D'Alembert's Dream* (1769) developed similar themes. What distinguishes Holbach is the systematic clarity with which he deploys the argument against theological claims: if motion is inherent in matter, the [[god|God]] hypothesis becomes not merely unproven but strictly unnecessary.

## Core Arguments

1. **Motion is essential to matter, not imposed upon it.** Holbach insists that "matter moves by its own energy, by the necessary result of its heterogeneity" (039-001). The diversity of substances and the variety of their interactions are themselves the source of all motion. No external agent is required; matter is self-moving by its very [[universe|nature]].

2. **All perception and cognition depend on motion.** To perceive anything, our sense organs must be set in motion by external objects. "Without motion I can neither feel, see, distinguish, compare, nor judge the body, nor [[eve]]n occupy my thought with any matter whatever" (041-002).

Vision requires light in motion stri[[monarch|king]] the retina; hearing requires air set in motion by a sonorous body; smell requires particles in motion reaching the olfactory nerve. Motion is thus the universal medium of all knowledge.

3. **The appearance of inertness is deceptive.** [[substance|Substance]]s that "seemed dead or deprived of all energy, take motion of themselves as soon as they are brought within the proper distance to act upon each other" (041-002).

The apparently passive matter around us is merely matter whose potential for motion has not yet been activated by the right conditions. There is no truly inert matter, only matter awaiting the circumstances that will release its inherent activity.

4. **Motion explains all [[nature|natural order]] and apparent disorder.** The regular movements of the oceans, the course of rivers, and the periodical motions of the seas are not [[proof|evidence]] of [[divinity|divine]] [[government|governance]] but "a result of motions necessarily brought on by causes" (044-001).

What appears as order or disorder depends entirely on whether the effects of these motions are favorable or injurious to human interests. [[nature|Nature]] itself is always in order; motion follows fixed laws regardless of human approval.

5. **The [[soul|spirit]]ual motor is an empty hypothesis.** Those who insist matter requires a motor produce an entity -- [[soul|spirit]] -- that is itself "absolutely incomprehensible and contradictory" (040-001) and that has "nothing that is necessary to put it into motion" (040-001).

A [[soul|spirit]]ual [[existence|being]], lac[[monarch|king]] extension and material properties, cannot by definition act upon material bodies. The hypothesis of a [[soul|spirit]]ual motor explains nothing and multiplies [[mysteries]].

## Reception and Critique

Holbach's identification of motion with the essence of matter was influential among radical Enlightenment figures and fed into nineteenth-century [[matter|materialism]]. Marx and Engels drew on this tradition in developing dialectical [[matter|materialism]], where matter in motion becomes the fundamental category. The argument that all perception depends on physical motion anticipated later scientific accounts of sensory physiology.

Critics have raised several objections. Some have argued that Holbach's account of self-motion fails to explain the origin of the specific, law-governed [[model|pattern]]s of motion that characterize [[nature|the universe]] — that eliminating a [[divinity|divine]] author of motion does not itself account for the mathematical regularity of motions.

Others have noted that Holbach, writing before the development of thermodynamics and the concept of energy, lacked the scientific vocabulary to make his case with full precision. Nevertheless, as a philosophical argument against the necessity of a supernatural motor, his treatment of motion remains among the clearest and most forceful statements of materialist metaphysics in the Enlightenment corpus.

## Relation to Other Concepts

Motion is the property that makes [[Matter]] active rather than inert, and is inseparable from it. It is the mechanism through which [[Nature]] produces all the phenomena that theists mistakenly attribute to [[divinity|divine]] agency.

Every [[Cause]] in Holbach's system is ultimately a cause of or through motion — "there is no effect without a cause" (043-001), and the chain of [[cause|causation]] operates entirely through material bodies in motion. Motion connects to [[Universe]], which is not a created effect but the total system of matter in necessary motion, "a cause, not an effect" (039-001). The concept also relates to [[Life]], which Holbach regards as a particular organization of motions, and to [[Perception]], which is entirely dependent on motion communicated from external objects to sensory organs.

## In the Text

> "Is it not more natural and more intelligible to deduce all which exists, from the bosom of matter, whose existence is demonstrated by all our senses, whose effects we feel at every moment, which we see act, move, communicate, motion, and constantly bring living beings into existence, than to attribute the formation of things to an unknown force, to a spiritual being, who can not draw from his ground that which he has not himself, and who, by the spiritual essence claimed for him, is incapable of making anything, and of putting anything in motion?" (022-001)

> "Matter moves by its own energy, by the necessary result of its heterogeneity; the diversity of its movements or of its ways of acting, constitute only the diversity of substances; we distinguish one being from another but by the diversity of the impressions or movements which they communicate to our organs." (039-001)

> "I am compelled to conclude that motion is as essential to matter as its extension, and that it can not be conceived of without it. If one persists in caviling about the evidences which prove to us that motion is an essential property of matter, he must at least acknowledge that substances which seemed dead or deprived of all energy, take motion of themselves as soon as they are brought within the proper distance to act upon each other." (041-002)

> "But this order is only a result of motions necessarily brought on by causes or by circumstances which are sometimes favorable and sometimes injurious to ourselves; we approve the former and find fault with the latter." (044-001)
