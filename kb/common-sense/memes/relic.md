## Relics as Objects of Superstition

In Holbach's analysis of religious practices, the veneration of relics represents a particularly transparent form of superstition. He groups the cult of relics together with image worship as practices that divert reverence from rational morality toward material objects. The faithful "after death honored their relics" (003-021), transforming the remains and possessions of holy figures into objects of devotion — a practice that Holbach sees as continuous with pagan idolatry rather than distinct from it.

## Relics and the Economy of Piety

Holbach's critique of relics extends to their economic function within religious systems. Relics attract pilgrims, generate donations, and enhance the prestige of the institutions that possess them. The material object — a bone, a garment, a fragment — becomes a source of income and influence for the clergy who control access to it. In Holbach's materialist framework, the relic is not merely a spiritual token but an economic asset, and the trade in relics is one more example of how religious institutions convert credulity into wealth.

## Relics in Comparative Perspective

Following his characteristic method, Holbach treats the Christian cult of relics as indistinguishable in principle from pagan practices. The pagans similarly honored the relics of their heroes and saints, and the Christian adoption of relic veneration simply continues an older pattern under a new name. What Christianity condemns as idolatry in other religions, it practices itself under the name of veneration — a distinction without a difference, in Holbach's view.

## Sacred Objects as Instruments of Fraud

Holbach places relic veneration within a broader category of religious fraud: the use of material objects to extract devotion and resources from the credulous. He groups relics together with images as objects to which believers "attribute marvelous virtues" and "address their prayers" (APP006-010), arguing that the veneration of both categories serves the same institutional function. The relic, the image, and the amulet are variations on a single theme — the transformation of ordinary matter into sacred currency. In each case, the clergy stand as intermediaries between the object and its supernatural power, controlling access and interpreting meaning. For Holbach, this arrangement is not an accident of religious history but the structural logic of priestcraft: sacred objects require sacred interpreters, and sacred interpreters require a livelihood.

## From *Common Sense*

> There is certainly a great similarity between the Pagan miracles and our own. As it would be great folly to give credence to these pretended miracles of Paganism, it is not any the less so to have faith in miracles wrought by the power of the Christian religion, and to have no other foundation than the testimony of fanatical or visionary men, who, in order to accredit their mission, pretended to cure the sick, to expel demons, and to raise the dead; and who, after death, honored their relics.
>
> (003-021)
