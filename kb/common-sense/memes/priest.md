# Priest

## Definition

In Holbach's "Common Sense," the priest is not a neutral religious functionary but the primary agent of [[suffering|human misery]] through organized religion. Priests are "charlatans" who manufacture fear, promote [[belief|credulity]], and claim exclusive access to [[divinity|divine]] truth in order to secure wealth, power, and immunity from law. They "devour [[nation|nations]], debase souls, discourage industry, and sow discord under the pretext of the glory of their [[god|God]]" (ch. 169). Far from serving as moral guides, [[clergy|priests]] systematically corrupt [[monarch|prince]]s into tyrants and populations into slaves.

## Historical Context

Holbach wrote during the final decades of the ancien régime, when the [[catholic-church|Catholic Church]] in [[paris|France]] held vast landholdings, collected tithes, controlled education, and exercised censorship. The clergy constituted the First Estate with exemption from most taxes. Holbach's attack on [[clergy|priesthood]] drew on a long tradition of anti-clericalism — from the medieval Waldensians through the [[reform|Reform]]ation — but radicalized it into a full materialist critique: [[clergy|priests]] were not merely corrupt individuals but the necessary product of a system built on supernatural claims. His analysis paralleled that of his contemporary [[voltaire|Voltaire]] ("Écrasez l'infâme") but went further in identifying the structural function of [[clergy|priesthood]] in maintaining political [[tyranny|despotism]].

## Core Arguments

**1. Priests manufacture fear to create dependence.** "[[ignorance|Ignorance]] and fear are the two pivots of all religion" (ch. 10). Priests present [[god|God]] as a "terrible, capricious, and changeable tyrant" so that worshippers "have recourse to them to obtain the means to be quieted" (ch. 169).

**2. Priests are charlatans selling a remedy they themselves do not beli[[eve]] in.** Holbach compares [[clergy|priests]] to "[[charlatan|quack]]s, who [[eve]]ry day would exhibit in a public place, selling their remedies and recommending them as infallible, while we should find them afflicted with the same infirmities which they pretend to cure" (ch. 154). Their own conduct contradicts their [[doctrine|teaching]]s.

**3. Priests turn [[monarch|prince]]s into tyrants.** "In almost all [[nations|countries]], [[clergy|priests]] and devout persons are charged with forming the mind and the heart of the young [[monarch|prince]]s" — filling them with "superstition as the most important and the most sacred thing" and ma[[monarch|king]] them "an imbecile devotee" (ch. 169). The result is a [[tyranny|despotism]] legitimized by [[divinity|divine]] right.

**4. Priests corrupt all morality.** By ma[[monarch|king]] [[morality|virtue]] consist in "a submissive [[religion|faith]], which prevents reasoning" and "a devout humility, which assures to the [[clergy|priests]] the submission of their slaves," religious morality serves exclusively "the advantage of the [[clergy|priests]]" (ch. 168).

**5. Priests stir up hatred and violence while preaching charity.** "Notwithstanding the bloody tragedies which religion has so often caused in this [[universe|world]], we are constantly told that there can be no morality without religion." But [[zeal|religious zeal]] "renders inhuman, and which serves to cover the greatest infamy" (ch. 159).

## Relation to Other Concepts

The concept of priest is inseparable from Holbach's critiques of [[Religion]], [[Theology]], and [[Superstition]]. Priests depend upon popular [[Credulity]] and [[Fear]] to maintain authority. They manufacture the idea of [[God]] as a tyrant to justify their own [[despotism|tyranny]]. The priest-[[monarch|king]] alliance forms the central mechanism of [[Despotism]]. Priests systematically oppose [[Reason]], [[Science]], and [[Liberty]] — all of which threaten their monopoly on truth-claims.

> "The ministers of Heaven have always known how to benefit themselves by the calamities of others; public misery became their element; they made themselves the administrators of the goods of the poor, the distributors of alms, the depositaries of charities; thereby they extended and sustained at all times their power over the unfortunates." (ch. 169, Appendix)

On the priest-tyrant alliance:

> "Priests in all times have shown themselves supporters of despotism, and the enemies of public liberty. Their profession requires vile and submissive slaves, who never have the audacity to reason. In an absolute government, their great object is to secure control of the mind of a weak and stupid prince, in order to make themselves masters of the people." (ch. 150)

On priestly hypocrisy:

> "It is necessary to the priests that we tremble before their God, in order that we have recourse to them to obtain the means to be quieted. No man is a hero to his valet de chambre. It is not surprising that a God clothed by His priests in such a way as to cause others to fear Him, should rarely impose upon those priests themselves." (ch. 153)
>
> (002-316)

> "Religion is the art of occupying limited minds with that which is impossible for them to comprehend." (ch. 6)
