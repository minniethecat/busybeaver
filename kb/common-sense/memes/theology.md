# Theology

## Definition

In Holbach's "Common Sense," [[religion|theology]] is not a legitimate branch of knowledge but the paradigmatic case of pseudo-knowledge — a discourse that appears to reason about a subject while in fact constructing elaborate fictions around an "idea without a model." "Ask a theologian to speak of Him; as soon as he utters one word about Him, the least reflection makes us discover at once that what he says is incompatible with the essence which he [[attribute|attributes]] to Him" (002-027). Theology is thus defined not by its object ([[god|God]]) but by its method: the systematic evasion of empirical constraints, logical consistency, and [[truth|intellectual honesty]].

Holbach's critique of [[religion|theology]] operates at multiple levels. Epistemologically, [[religion|theology]] is vacuous — it produces statements about an entity for which no [[proof|evidence]] exists. Methodologically, it is self-protecting — any apparent contradiction is absorbed into "[[secrecy|mystery]]" rather than acknowledged as refutation. Politically, it is dangerous — theologians constitute a professional class whose material interests depend on the perpetuation of ignorance. "If [[religion|theology]] is a branch of commerce useful to theologians, it has been [[demon|demons]]trated to be superfluous and injurious to the rest of society" (002-428).

## Historical Context

Holbach wrote at a time when [[religion|theology]] still dominated [[europe|Europe]]an intellectual life. The theological faculties at the Sorbonne and other universities exercised censorship powers; books deemed irreligious were burned, and their authors imprisoned. Yet [[religion|theology]]'s intellectual authority was crumbling. [[baruch-spinoza|Spinoza]]'s *Tractatus Theologico-Politicus* (1670) had pioneered historical-critical [[scripture|Bible]] scholarship; [[pierre-bayle|Bayle]]'s *Dictionary* had exposed theological contradictions with devastating irony; the *Encyclopédie* had [[demon|demons]]trated that [[knowledge|useful knowledge]] could be organized without theological foundations. Holbach's contribution was the most radical: not merely to criticize this or that [[doctrine|theological doctrine]], but to argue that [[religion|theology]] *as such* — the very attempt to reason about [[god|God]] — was cognitively bankrupt.

## Core Arguments

**1. Theology is built on an "idea without a model."** Holbach opens his critique by challenging the very concept of [[god|God]]: "An idea without a [[model|prototype]], is it anything but a chimera? Some theologians, however, assure us that the idea of [[god|God]] is innate, or that men have this idea from the time of their birth" (002-003). A discourse without a real referent cannot produce knowledge; it can only produce increasingly elaborate fictions.

**2. Theological language is systematically unintelligible.** When theologians describe [[god|God]] as a "pure [[soul|spirit]]" without extension, they are not saying something profound but nothing at all. "A [[soul|spirit]] in the language of modern [[religion|theology]] is then but an absence of ideas. The idea of [[spirit|spirituality]] is another idea without a model" (ch. II). The term "[[soul|spirit]]" does not clarify; it obscures the fact that no clear concept is [[existence|being]] communicated.

**3. Theology corrupts philosophy and [[knowledge|science]].** "From the most remote period [[religion|theology]] alone regulated the march of philosophy. What aid has it lent it? It changed it into an unintelligible jargon" (ch. CCI). Holbach argues that [[religion|theology]] has historically functioned as a constraint on inquiry, demanding that all conclusions conform to predetermined [[dogma|dogmas]]. "Vigilant [[clergy|priests]] were always ready to extinguish systems which could not be made to tally with their interests" (ch. CCIII).

**4. Theologians have material interests in ignorance.** Holbach's critique is not merely intellectual but sociological: [[religion|theology]] is a profession whose practitioners benefit from the public's [[belief|credulity]]. Theologians "treat men like children, who never cavil about the possibilities of the tales which are told them" (ch. II) because questioning those tales would destroy the theologians' authority and livelihood.

## Reception and Critique

Holbach's radical anti-theologism was rejected [[eve]]n by many Enlightenment sympathizers. [[voltaire|Voltaire]] thought it politically reckless; Rousseau considered it [[soul|spirit]]ually destructive. Yet the trajectory of modern thought has largely vindicated Holbach's epistemological critique: [[religion|theology]] has been progressively excluded from the natural [[knowledge|science]]s, then from philosophy, and increasingly from public justification in pluralistic societies. Contemporary critics argue that Holbach's dismissal of [[religion|theology]] was overly sweeping — that theological traditions contain genuine intellectual resources that cannot be reduced to [[priest|priestcraft]]. Holbach's reply, implicit in his text, is that any theological proposition that can be made intelligible and supported by [[proof|evidence]] ceases to be [[religion|theology]] and becomes philosophy or [[knowledge|science]].

## Relation to Other Concepts

Theology is the primary target of Holbach's critique. It is the intellectual apparatus that sustains belief in [[God]], rationalizes the [[Problem of Evil]], suppresses [[Reason]], and manufactures [[Superstition]]. It stands opposed to [[Science]], which investigates [[universe|nature]] without theological presuppositions, and to [[Truth]], which [[religion|theology]] systematically evades. Holbach's critique of [[religion|theology]] connects directly to his analysis of [[Faith]] (theological conclusions are accepted on [[religion|faith]] precisely because they cannot survive rational scrutiny) and [[Priestcraft]] ([[religion|theology]] is the intellectual product of a professional class with interests opposed to [[education|enlightenment]]).

## In the Text

Holbach's chapter title states his thesis directly: "What Is Theology?" The answer is that it is nothing — a discourse without an object:

> "By metaphysics, God is made a pure spirit, but has modern theology advanced one step further than the theology of the barbarians? They recognized a grand spirit as master of the world." (ch. II)

On the vacuity of theological language:

> "A spirit in the language of modern theology is then but an absence of ideas. The idea of spirituality is another idea without a model." (ch. II)

On the self-defeating character of theological statements:

> "To unsettle the existence of a God, it is only necessary to ask a theologian to speak of Him; as soon as he utters one word about Him, the least reflection makes us discover at once that what he says is incompatible with the essence which he attributes to Him." (002-027)

On [[religion|theology]]'s social cost:

> "If theology is a branch of commerce useful to theologians, it has been demonstrated to be superfluous and injurious to the rest of society." (002-428)

On [[religion|theology]]'s historical damage to philosophy:

> "Half of the efforts that it has cost the human mind to elaborate these religious fables, applied to intelligible things, would have carried the human mind very far. How many famous thinkers, if, instead of occupying themselves with a useless theology, and its impertinent disputes, they had turned their attention upon intelligible and truly important objects!" (ch. CCIII)
