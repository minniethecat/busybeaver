# Justice

## Definition

In Holbach's "Common Sense," justice is examined primarily through the lens of what it cannot be: an attribute of the [[god|God]] described by [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian [[religion|theology]]. "[[theology-index|Divine]] justice, such as our theologians [[suffering|pain]]t it, is, without doubt, a quality intended to make us love [[divinity|Divinity]]" (002-098). Yet when this supposed divine justice is examined honestly, it "so often resembles human injustice" — punishing finite creatures with infinite [[torture|torment]]s for [[crime|offense]]s that an omnipotent [[existence|being]] could have prevented. Holbach's analysis of justice thus proceeds by negation: by [[demon|demons]]trating that the theological concept of justice is incoherent, he clears the ground for a secular conception grounded in [[reason|human reason]], reciprocity, and the requirements of social life.

Holbach's positive conception of justice is naturalistic and political: justice is the set of rules and institutions that allow human [[existence|being]]s to live together peacefully and pursue their happiness without infringing on the equal rights of others. "[[science|Science]], reason, liberty, alone can reform them and render them more happy" (ch. II) — and justice, along with education, is the primary instrument of this [[reform|reformation]].

## Historical Context

[[enlightenment|The Enlightenment]] witnessed a profound transformation in thin[[monarch|king]] about justice. Grotius and Pufendorf had argued that natural law could be derived from human [[universe|nature]] without theological foundations. Montesquieu had analyzed laws as products of climate, [[worship|cult]]ure, and political structure rather than [[divinity|divine]] decree. Beccaria's *On [[crime|Crime]]s and [[punishment|Punishment]]s* (1764) had argued against torture and capital punishment on rational, humanitarian grounds. Holbach's contribution was to take the theological concept of divine justice — particularly the doctrine of eternal [[hell|damnation]] — and subject it to rational scrutiny, [[demon|demons]]trating that what passed for "justice" in [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian [[religion|theology]] was actually a form of infinite cruelty masquerading as righteousness.

## Core Arguments

**1. [[theology-index|Divine]] justice is indistinguishable from injustice.** Holbach argues that the theological characterization of [[god|God]] as "infinitely just" is contradicted by [[eve]]ry observable fact about [[earth|the world]]. An omnipotent [[existence|being]] who permits [[injustice|evil]] "for a time" and then punishes his creatures eternally for faults he could have prevented is not just but monstrous. "They console us in our troubles by saying, that [[god|God]] is patient, and that His justice, although often very slow, is not the less certain. But do you not see, that patience can not be suited to a [[existence|being]] just, immutable, and omnipotent? Can [[god|God]] tolerate injustice for an instant?" (ch. LXXXVIII).

**2. The doctrine of [[hell|eternal punishment]] is incompatible with justice.** [[finite|Finite]] [[crime|offense]]s cannot, by any coherent [[model|standard]] of proportionality, merit infinite punishment. "Far from forming a perfect [[god|God]], the theologians have made the most imperfect" [[existence|being]] — one whose "justice" consists of "the most terrible cruelty" (ch. II). The threat of [[punishment|hell]] is not a manifestation of justice but of psychological [[fear|terror]] deployed for institutional control.

**3. Justice is a human creation for human purposes.** Holbach implies that justice, properly understood, is not a [[divinity|divine]] attribute imperfectly reflected in human law but a human invention designed to solve human problems. Its [[model|standard]]s are human [[model|standard]]s — proportionality, impartiality, consistency — and they make no sense when applied to a [[existence|being]] defined as "beyond human comprehension."

**4. [[religion|Religion]] undermines justice.** By [[doctrine|teaching]] that earthly injustice will be rectified in an [[death|afterlife]], religion reduces the urgency of pursuing justice in this [[universe|world]]. Worse, by claiming [[divinity|divine]] sanction for particular laws and [[monarch|ruler]]s, religion has historically served as a justification for [[despotism|tyranny]]. "If the [[monarch|sovereign]]s had more humanity, charity, and justice, their [[politics-index|State]]s would not be peopled by so many unfortunate ones whose misery becomes impossible to soothe" (ch. CLXIX).

## Reception and Critique

Holbach's critique of divine justice was a direct challenge to one of [[christianity|Christianity]]'s central moral claims. Apologists responded that Holbach misunderstood the [[universe|nature]] of [[vice|sin]] as an [[crime|offense]] against an infinite [[existence|being]], which (they argued) merited infinite punishment. Holbach would likely reply that this only proves his point: the doctrine requires us to abandon all ordinary moral reasoning and accept something we would recognize as monstrous in any other context.

Later philosophers developed secular theories of justice that went far beyond Holbach's primarily negative analysis. Kant grounded justice in the categorical imperative; utilitarians in the maximization of welfare; Rawls in the principles that would be chosen behind a "veil of ignorance." Holbach's contribution was the essential preparatory work of clearing away theological obstacles to thin[[monarch|king]] clearly about justice.

## Relation to Other Concepts

Justice is the social expression of [[Reason]] — the application of [[principle|rational principles]] to the organization of human coexistence. It is opposed to [[Tyranny]], which is the rule of force unchecked by law, and to the [[fiction|theological fiction]] of [[divinity|divine]] [[Providence]], which promises cosmic justice while delivering none. Holbach connects justice to [[Morality]] (justice is the public face of moral principle), [[Liberty]] (there is no liberty without just laws), and the [[Problem of Evil]] (the absence of justice in [[earth|the world]] is itself a form of [[injustice|evil]]). His critique of divine justice connects directly to his critique of [[Hell]] as a concept that degrades rather than expresses justice.

## In the Text

On the [[contradiction|incoherence]] of divine justice:

> "Divine justice, such as our theologians paint it, is, without doubt, a quality intended to make us love Divinity. According to the notions of theology, God possesses this justice; nevertheless, the effects of this justice are nowhere to be seen; it appears to be always belied by the events of this world." (ch. II)

On the incompatibility of patience with perfect justice:

> "They console us in our troubles by saying, that God is patient, and that His justice, although often very slow, is not the less certain. But do you not see, that patience can not be suited to a being just, immutable, and omnipotent? Can God tolerate injustice for an instant? To temporize with an evil that one knows of, evinces either uncertainty, weakness, or connivance." (ch. LXXXVIII)

On the incommensurability of [[divinity|divine]] and human justice:

> "Is it not confounding all our ideas of justice and of injustice, to tell us that what is equitable in God is iniquitous in His creatures? How can we take as a model a being whose Divine perfections are the negation of the human perfections?" (ch. LXXXIX)

On religion's corruption of political justice:

> "If the sovereigns had more humanity, charity, and justice, their States would not be peopled by so many unfortunate ones whose misery becomes impossible to soothe." (ch. CLXIX)
