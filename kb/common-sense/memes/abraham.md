# Abraham

## Biography

Abraham (originally Abram) is the first of the three biblical patriarchs, alongside his son [[isaac|Isaac]] and grandson [[jacob|Jacob]]. According to the Book of Genesis, he was born in Ur of the Chaldeans (in present-day Iraq) and called by [[god|God]] to migrate to [[palestine|Canaan]], where he received a [[divinity|divine]] promise that his descendants would become a great [[nations|nation]]. His life narrative, spanning Genesis 12–25, includes the covenant of circumcision, the destruction of [[sodom|Sodom]] and Gomorrah, the expulsion of Hagar and Ishmael, and the near-sacrifice of [[isaac|Isaac]] — the Akedah, which Kierkegaard would later make the centerpiece of his exploration of [[religion|faith]] and [[morality|ethics]].

In Jewish tradition, Abraham is the founding father of the [[israel|Israel]]ite people and the first monotheist, credited with smashing his father Terah's idols and recognizing the one true [[god|God]] through reason alone. In [[christianity|Christian]] [[religion|theology]], he is the model of [[faith|justification by faith]] — "Abraham believed [[god|God]], and it was counted unto him for righteousness" (Romans 4:3). In [[islam|Islam]], he is Ibrahim, the primal submitter (muslim) to [[god|God]]'s will and the rebuilder of the Kaaba at Mecca.

## Role in Religious History

Abraham occupies a unique position as the claimed ancestor of all three Abrahamic [[religion|faith]]s. Judaism traces its lineage through [[isaac|Isaac]] and [[jacob|Jacob]]; [[christianity|Christianity]] claims [[soul|spirit]]ual descent through [[religion|faith]]; [[islam|Islam]] traces its line through Ishmael, Abraham's firstborn son by Hagar. This shared patrimony has made Abraham a symbol of inter[[religion|faith]] common ground, invoked in ecumenical gestures from the Second [[rome|Vatican]] Council to the Abraham Accords.

Yet the very universality of Abraham's appeal also makes him a target for Holbach's critique. If the same patriarch grounds three mutually incompatible [[revelation|revelation]] systems, each claiming exclusive truth, then the foundational narrative cannot itself validate any one of them. Abraham becomes, in Holbach's hands, not [[evidence|proof]] of [[revelation|divine revelation]] but [[proof|evidence]] of its human construction — a figure so malleable that three rival religions can each claim him as their own.

## Connection to "Common Sense"

Holbach mentions Abraham multiple times in *Common Sense* (*Le Bon Sens*, 1772), always as part of a broader critique of [[revelation|revealed religion]]. He does not treat Abraham as a unique target but as a representative case: the patriarch whose [[divinity|divine]] calling exemplifies the [[model|pattern]] of supernatural claims that crumble under rational scrutiny.

In the chapter on [[revelation|Divine Revelation]], Holbach places Abraham alongside [[isaac|Isaac]] and [[jacob|Jacob]] as one of the "pretended Holy Patriarchs" whose claimed [[dreams|visions]] of [[god|God]] are indistinguishable from delusion or imposture. The [[model|pattern]] Holbach identifies is consistent: a private experience is reported, no independent verification is possible, and the claim is accepted on authority alone.

> "Now there is no reason to think otherwise of all that has been said by these pretended Holy Patriarchs, Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob, in regard to the Divine Revelations which they claim to have had."
>
> (APP004-007)

Holbach's argument is not that Abraham was a liar — it is that the structure of the claim itself is epistemologically vacuous. Any person in any age can claim a private [[divinity|divine]] communication; nothing distinguishes Abraham's reported theophany from the [[dreams|visions]] claimed by "insane fools" in Holbach's own day, except the [[chance|accident]] of scriptural [[saint|canonization]].

On the covenant and the chosen people, Holbach deploys Abraham in a different register: as [[proof|evidence]] that the biblical narrative fails [[eve]]n on its own terms. The promise that Abraham's descendants would be "as numerous as the stars" and rule over the land of [[palestine|Canaan]] was, Holbach observes, never fulfilled:

> "It is certain that the Jewish people, or the people of Israel — which is the only one that can be regarded as having descended from the Patriarchs Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob, and the only ones to whom these promises should have been fulfilled — have never been so numerous that it could be said they had filled the whole earth."
>
> (APP004-015)

The unfulfilled promise is, for Holbach, a decisive argument: a [[revelation|revelation]] whose central prophecy [[demon|demons]]trably fails cannot be [[divinity|divine]].

## Key Ideas Attributed / Critiqued

Holbach [[attribute|attributes]] to Abraham — and through him to the entire biblical tradition — three interconnected claims, each of which he subjects to materialist critique:

**1. Private revelation as public truth.** Abraham's call from [[god|God]] is a private experience — a voice heard by one man, a vision seen by one man. Yet this private experience is offered as the foundation for doctrines that bind millions. Holbach sees no epistemic difference between Abraham's theophany and the [[dreams|visions]] claimed by enthusiasts and madmen in [[eve]]ry age. The acceptance of Abraham's claim rests not on [[proof|evidence]] but on the [[chance|accident]] of its inclusion in a canonized text.

**2. Ethnic [[predestination|election]] as [[divinity|divine]] plan.** The covenant promises to Abraham — land, descendants, blessing — posit a [[divinity|deity]] who plays favo[[ritual|rites]] among [[nation|nations]]. For Holbach, this is transparently a tribal god retroactively elevated to universal status, a projection of [[nations|nation]]al ambition onto the cosmic stage. A truly universal [[god|God]] would not covenant exclusively with one lineage.

**3. [[faith|Faith]] as epistemic submission.** Abraham's willingness to sacrifice [[isaac|Isaac]] on [[divinity|divine]] command — the Akedah — is celebrated in all three Abrahamic traditions as the paradigm of [[faith|faith]]. For Holbach, it is the paradigm of [[fanaticism|fanaticism]]: the surrender of moral judgment and natural affection to an invisible authority whose commands cannot be questioned. If [[god|God]] told you to kill your child, Abraham's example demands obedience; Holbach demands that you question whether the voice is [[divinity|divine]] at all.

## Legacy

Abraham's philosophical legacy extends far beyond the religious traditions that claim him. The Akedah became a central text for existentialism through Kierkegaard's *[[fear|Fear]] and Trembling* (1843), which presented Abraham as a "knight of [[religion|faith]]" whose willingness to sacrifice [[isaac|Isaac]] embodies the "teleological suspension of the ethical." Where Holbach saw [[intolerance|fanaticism]], Kierkegaard saw the highest form of [[faith|religious]] existence — a divergence that marks the fault line between Enlightenment rationalism and existentialist [[faith|theology]].

In contemporary philosophy, Abraham continues to figure in debates about [[divinity|divine]] command [[morality|ethics]], the relationship between [[faith|faith]] and [[reason|reason]], and the epistemology of religious experience. Holbach's critique — that private revelation cannot ground public knowledge — anticipates arguments that would later be developed by [[david-hume|Hume]], Feuerbach, and the logical positivists.
