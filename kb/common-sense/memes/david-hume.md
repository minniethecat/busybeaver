# David Hume

## Biography

David Hume (1711–1776) was a Scottish philosopher, historian, and essayist, widely considered the most important philosopher to write in English. Born in Edinburgh, Hume published his masterwork *A Treatise of [[humanity|Human Nature]]* (1739–40) at the age of twenty-eight, though it "fell dead-born from the press." He later achieved literary fame with his *History of England* and his elegant *Essays*. Hume's radical empiricism — pushing [[john-locke|Locke]]'s principles to their skeptical conclusion that neither [[cause|causality]] nor the self has rational warrant — made him a controversial figure in his own time and a defining influence on subsequent philosophy.

## Role in Religious History

Hume was one of the most devastating critics of religion in the eighteenth century. His essay "Of [[miracle|Miracle]]s" ([[sect|Sect]]ion X of *An Enquiry Concerning [[understanding|Human Understanding]]*, 1748) argued that it is never rational to accept testimony of a miracle over the uniform testimony of natural law. His *Dialogues Concerning Natural [[religion|Religion]]* (published posthumously in 1779) systematically dismantled the [[design|argument from design]], exposing the analogy between [[nature|the universe]] and a human artifact as weak and anthropomorphic. Hume's critiques made him notorious; he was denied academic chairs at Edinburgh and Glasgow on grounds of [[atheist|atheism]]. For the radical Enlightenment, however, Hume was a hero — a philosopher who dared to follow reason wherever it led.

## Connection to "Common Sense"

Holbach cites Hume directly in *Common Sense* as an authority on the [[universe|nature]] of [[god|God]]. After arguing that theologians attribute incompatible properties to their [[god|deity]] — ma[[monarch|king]] "[[god|God]]" an abstract word at best — Holbach invokes Hume's witty observation: "A philosopher has very ingeniously said in spea[[monarch|king]] of theologians, that they have found the solution to the famous problem of Archimedes; a point in the [[hope|heaven]]s from which they move [[earth|the world]]." This allusion transforms the theologians' [[god|God]] into Archimedes' impossible fulcrum — a mathematical fiction with no real existence, invented to explain what would otherwise require honest investigation.

> "A philosopher [David Hume] has very ingeniously said in speaking of theologians, that they have found the solution to the famous problem of Archimedes; a point in the heavens from which they move the world." — Holbach, *Common Sense*, ch. XXVII

Though named only once, Hume's presence pervades *Common Sense*. Holbach's critique of [[miracle|miracles]], his argument that testimony cannot establish [[supernatural|the supernatural]], and his reduction of [[religion|theology]] to psychological projection all echo Humean themes, radicalized and applied with polemical force.

## Legacy

Hume's influence on philosophy is rivaled only by Kant's. His [[doubt|skepticism]] awakened Kant from "[[doctrine|dogma]]tic slumber" and set the agenda for epistemology [[eve]]r [[guilt|sin]]ce. For Holbach and the French materialists, Hume provided the philosophical ammunition to attack [[revelation|revealed religion]] on its own epistemological terms — [[miracle|miracles]], prophecy, and [[divinity|divine]] testimony could not survive empirical scrutiny. Hume himself was more cautious than Holbach, cloa[[monarch|king]] his critiques in irony and dialogue; Holbach, writing from the safety of a pseudonymous press, said openly what Hume implied. Together they represent the moderate and radical wings of the Enlightenment's assault on super[[theism|natural religion]].
