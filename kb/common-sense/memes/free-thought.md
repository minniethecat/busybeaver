# Free Thought

## Definition

Free thought (*libre pensée*) in Baron d'Holbach's philosophy designates the intellectual liberation of the human mind from the authority of [[clergy|priests]], the [[dogma|dogmas]] of [[revelation|revealed religion]], and the political systems that depend upon popular [[belief|credulity]].

Free thought is not merely the absence of censorship — it is the positive exercise of reason applied without restriction to [[eve]]ry domain of human concern, including and especially religion.

A freethinker (*libre penseur*) is one who refuses to accept any proposition on the basis of authority, tradition, or revelation, demanding instead that all claims be established by [[proof|evidence]] and rational argument.

For Holbach, free thought is simultaneously an epistemological method, a moral [[morality|virtue]], and a political act: it is the necessary condition for human emancipation from the "double yoke" of throne and altar.

## Historical Context

The term "freethinker" emerged in English in the late seventeenth century, associated with Deists like John Toland and Anthony Collins who argued for the application of reason to religious questions.

By Holbach's era, the French *libres penseurs* constituted a loose but influential network of radical intellectuals who met in salons, published anonymously or pseudonymously, and circulated forbidden manuscripts.

Holbach's own [[paris|Paris]]ian salon was a major center of this activity, hosting figures such as Denis Diderot, Claude-Adrien Helvétius, and (before their falling-out) Jean-Jacques Rousseau.

The institutional opposition to free thought was formidable. The [[catholic-church|Catholic Church]] maintained an *Index Librorum Prohibitorum*; the French state enforced censorship through the *librairie* and could imprison authors for blasphemy or sedition.

Holbach published most of his radical works under pseudonyms and had them printed abroad, typically in Amsterdam. The very conditions of his authorship illustrate his thesis: free thought is possible only when one evades the institutional mechanisms designed to suppress it.

## Core Arguments

Holbach's first argument for free thought is epistemological: truth can be discovered only through the unrestrained exercise of reason. To forbid inquiry into religion — as both church and state did — is to concede that religion cannot withstand rational scrutiny. "Thanks to religion," Holbach w[[ritual|rites]] bitterly, "it was never permitted to think aloud."

Holbach's second argument is historical. He presents the freethinkers of the past — from ancient Greek philosophers through Renaissance humanists to the English Deists — as a heroic lineage of truth-tellers who advanced human knowledge against the violent resistance of religious institutions.

[[woolston|Thomas Woolston]], the English Deist who died in prison after [[existence|being]] prosecuted for blasphemy, is praised by name in *Common Sense* as a martyr to free inquiry.

Holbach's third argument is moral and political. Free thought is not merely an intellectual luxury but a precondition for [[progress|social progress]].

As long as human minds are "kept in the darkness of superstition" — as long as [[clergy|priests]] control what can and cannot be thought — all other reforms are impossible. Political liberty, scientific [[progress|advancement]], and moral [[progress|improvement]] all presuppose the intellectual liberty to question [[opinion|received opinion]]s.

## Reception and Critique

Holbach's advocacy of free thought has been celebrated and contested in equal measure. In the immediate aftermath of [[revolution|the French Revolution]], freethinkers were hailed as precursors of liberty — Condorcet's *Sketch for a Historical Picture of the [[progress|Progress]] of the Human [[mind|Mind]]* (1795) identified Holbach's circle as a decisive force in the intellectual preparation for 1789.

Conservative critics, from Edmund Burke to [[joseph|Joseph]] de Maistre, saw the matter differently. For them, Holbach's free thought was not liberation but dissolution — the destruction of the moral and religious foundations upon which [[society|social order]] depends.

De Maistre's *Soirées de [[saint|Saint]]-Pétersbourg* (1821) explicitly targeted Holbachian freethought as a philosophy that "dissolves [[eve]]rything and rebuilds nothing."

In the twentieth century, the concept of free thought was taken up by secular humanist movements, who claimed Holbach as a founding figure. The International Humanist and Ethical Union, founded in 1952, traces its intellectual lineage through the French materialists.

Critics within the Marxist tradition, however, have argued that Holbach's free thought is too narrowly intellectual — that it addresses the [[liberty|freedom]] to think without adequately addressing the material conditions (education, leisure, [[liberty|freedom]] from economic necessity) that make thin[[monarch|king]] freely possible for most people.

Postcolonial critics have raised a different concern: Holbach's celebration of [[europe|Europe]]an freethought as the pinnacle of intellectual progress reproduces the very Eurocentrism that Enlightenment universalism was supposed to transcend.

The claim that "the freethinkers of [[france|France]] have taught mankind the truth" — however inspiring in its context — reads differently when [[france|France]] is simultaneously engaged in colonial domi[[nations|nation]].

## Relation to Other Concepts

Free thought is conceptually inseparable from [[reason]], the universal human faculty whose unimpeded exercise it demands. For Holbach, reason is not a specialized philosophical tool but the natural operation of the human mind; free thought is simply reason allowed to do its work without ecclesiastical obstruction.

Free thought stands in direct opposition to [[priestcraft]], the systematic manufacture of [[fiction|religious fiction]]s for social control. If [[priest|priestcraft]] is the disease — the deliberate [[worship|cult]]ivation of ignorance and fear — free thought is the cure. The two concepts define each other dialectically, and Holbach's rhetorical strategy consists largely in exposing [[priest|priestcraft]] so that free thought can take its place.

The concept is also closely tied to [[toleration]], the political condition that makes free thought practically possible.

Holbach recognized, as [[john-locke|John Locke]] had before him, that the [[liberty|freedom]] to think requires legal protection — the right to publish, to assemble, and to dissent from established opinion. Unlike [[john-locke|Locke]], however, Holbach extended toleration to [[atheism|atheist]]s, whom [[john-locke|Locke]] had explicitly excluded.

Finally, free thought is related to [[education]], which Holbach understood as the institutional mechanism for [[worship|cult]]ivating the habit of rational inquiry.

A population trained from childhood to accept [[dogma|dogmas]] on authority will not spontaneously become freethin[[monarch|king]] in adulthood; free thought requires a pedagogy that teaches doubt as a [[morality|virtue]] and authority as a claim to be tested, not obeyed.

## In the Text

Holbach's treatment of free thought in *Common Sense* is characteristically militant. He w[[ritual|rites]] that "the freethinkers of [[france|France]] have taught mankind the truth" (APP007-026) — a statement that is at once a boast, a manifesto, and a call to action.

Throughout the text, he addresses the reader as a potential freethinker who requires only courage to cast off [[prejudice|inherited prejudice]]s.

Holbach identifies specific historical figures as [[model|exemplar]]s of free thought. [[meslier|Jean Meslier (1664–1729)]], the Catholic priest whose posthumously discovered *[[testament|Testament]]* contained a comprehensive [[atheism|atheist]]ic critique of [[christianity|Christianity]], is celebrated as a man who had "the courage and the honesty to abjure [[error|religious error]]s."

By honoring Meslier, Holbach emphasizes that free thought is accessible to anyone willing to exercise their natural reason.

The conclusion of *Common Sense* is an exhortation to free thought: "Let us then leave error to the [[clergy|priests]], and let us hold to truth. Let us listen to reason, which speaks to all men, and which will conduct them to happiness."
