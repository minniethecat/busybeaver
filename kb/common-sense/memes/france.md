# France

## Definition

The **France** of [[paul|Paul]]-Henri Thiry, Baron d'Holbach (1723–1789) was the France of the *ancien régime*: a Catholic [[monarch|monarchy]] in which [[church|Church]] and [[government|State]] were not merely allied but [[temperament|constitution]]ally fused. The [[catholic-church|Catholic Church]] was the largest landowner in the [[monarch|king]]dom, the censor of books, the keeper of civil registries (baptismal certificates functioned as birth certificates), and the arbiter of intellectual orthodoxy. To publish "Common Sense" — an anonymous, pseudonymously-attributed [[atheism|atheist]] manifesto — was a criminal act. Holbach's text is shaped at [[eve]]ry point by the political conditions of its production.

## The Alliance of Throne and Altar

Holbach's critique of [[religion|religion]] is inseparable from his critique of [[monarch|monarchy]]. In [[paris|eighteenth-century France]], the [[church|Church]] legitimized the Crown through the doctrine of [[sovereign|divine right]]; the Crown enforced the [[church|Church's]] monopoly through laws against heresy, blasphemy, and unlicensed printing. This alliance was not incidental but structural:

> "Never permitted to them to resist, the priesthood succeeded in perpetuating the misconduct of the kings and the misfortunes of the people; the interests of the nations have been cowardly sacrificed to their chiefs." (ch. II)

The French context gives Holbach's abstract critique of [[priesthood|priestcraft]] its concrete urgency. He is not writing about [[religion|religion]] in general but about a specific institutional arrangement that he witnessed daily: the [[paris|Paris]] of parishes, bishoprics, monasteries, and the Sorbonne, where ecclesiastical censorship shaped what could be thought and said.

## The Enlightenment Underground

"Common Sense" was published in 1772, during the high [[enlightenment|Enlightenment]], but its radicalism placed it beyond the pale [[eve]]n of reformist circles. While [[voltaire|Voltaire]] and Rousseau could publish under their own names (with occasional exile or book-burning), Holbach's [[atheist|atheism]] was too dangerous for open avowal. "Common Sense" appeared under the pseudonym "the late M. Boulanger" and was printed in Amsterdam, not [[paris|Paris]]. The text circulated in the Enlightenment underground — the network of publishers, booksellers, and readers who distributed prohibited texts across [[europe|Europe]].

This context explains the text's rhetorical strategies: the ventriloquism of [[meslier|Jean Meslier]] (a Catholic priest turned posthumous [[atheism|atheist]]), the attribution to a deceased author, the Dutch imprint. Holbach's France was a place where to speak plainly about [[religion|religion]] was to risk the Bastille.

## From Critique to Revolution

Holbach died in 1789, months before the [[revolution|French Revolution]] began. He did not live to see the Civil Constitution of the [[clergy|Clergy]] (1790), the [[nations|nation]]alization of [[church|Church]] lands, or the de-[[jesus-christ|Christ]]ianization campaigns of 1793–1794. But "Common Sense" was one of the texts that made the [[revolution|Revolution]] possible — not by causing it directly, but by eroding the intellectual prestige of the institutions the [[revolution|Revolution]] would dismantle. The alliance of throne and altar that Holbach critiqued in 1772 was precisely what the [[revolution|Revolution]] destroyed after 1789.
