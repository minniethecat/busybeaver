# Bethlehem

## Geography and History

Bethlehem is a town in the West Bank, approximately 6 miles south of [[jerusalem|Jerusalem]], situated on a limestone ridge in the [[judea|Judea]]n Mountains. In antiquity, it was a small agricultural settlement known as Ephrath. The Hebrew [[bible|Bible]] identifies it as the birthplace of [[david|King David]], from whom it derives its epithet "City of David." Under Roman rule in the first century CE, Bethlehem was a minor village in the province of [[judea|Judea]], administered from [[jerusalem|Jerusalem]]. The [[church|Church]] of the Nativity, built by Constantine in the fourth century over a cave traditionally identified as Jesus's birthplace, remains one of the oldest continuously operating churches in [[earth|the world]].

## Religious Significance

For [[christianity|Christianity]], Bethlehem is second only to [[jerusalem|Jerusalem]] in sacred importance as the birthplace of [[jesus-christ|Jesus Christ]]. Both [[matthew|Matthew]] and [[luke|Luke]] locate Jesus's birth there, lin[[monarch|king]] it to messianic prophecies concerning [[david|David]]'s lineage. For Holbach, however, the Bethlehem nativity stories are not testimony to [[divinity|divine]] fulfillment but [[proof|evidence]] of myth-ma[[monarch|king]] — the [[missionaries|evangelists]] retroactively constructed narratives to fit Hebrew prophecy, a common practice Holbach identifies across religious traditions.

## Role in "Common Sense"

Holbach's "Common Sense" discusses Bethlehem in the context of [[herod|Herod]]'s [[mass|Mass]]acre of the Innocents — [[gospel|the Gospel]] of [[matthew|Matthew]]'s account of [[herod|Herod]] ordering the killing of all male children under two years old "in all the neighborhood of Bethlehem" in an attempt to eliminate the infant Jesus (ch. XI, §Evangelists). Holbach treats this episode not as sacred history but as a moral scandal that fatally undermines the [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian claim of a providential and benevolent [[god|God]].

Holbach points to Matthew's account of Herod's slaughter:

> Herod, fearing that this pretended new king would rob him of his crown some day, caused the murder of all the new-born children under two years, in all the neighborhood of Bethlehem, where he had been told that this new king was born; and that Joseph and the mother of Jesus, having been warned in a dream by an angel, of this wicked intention, took flight immediately to Egypt, where they stayed until the death of Herod, which happened many years afterward.
>
> (002-481)

Holbach argues that the massacre's historical implausibility is self-evident: "as neither the historians of that time speak of it, nor [[josephus|Josephus]], the historian who wrote the life of this [[herod|Herod]], and as the other Evangelists do not mention it, it is evident that the journey of those wise men, guided by a star, this massacre of little children, and this flight to [[egypt|Egypt]], were but [[absurdity|absurd]] falsehoods" (ch. XI). The silence of [[josephus|Josephus]] — who "blamed the vices of this [[monarch|king]]" extensively — is, for Holbach, decisive [[proof|evidence]] that the slaughter never occurred.

Beyond historical criticism, Holbach draws a theological conclusion: if the [[god|God]] of [[christianity|Christianity]] were real, he could have protected the Bethlehem children rather than allowing their slaughter while saving only his own son through an angelic warning. The episode thus serves Holbach's broader argument that the biblical [[god|God]] is either powerless or malevolent — and in either case unworthy of worship.

## Related Figures and Events

Bethlehem is linked to [[herod|Herod the Great]], the Roman client [[monarch|king]] whose order for the massacre is recorded only in [[matthew|Matthew]]. It is also connected to the [[nazareth|Nazareth]]-[[egypt|Egypt]] geographical contradiction in the nativity narratives: [[matthew|Matthew]] has the Holy Family flee from Bethlehem to [[egypt|Egypt]] and later settle in [[nazareth|Nazareth]], while [[luke|Luke]] has them return directly to [[nazareth|Nazareth]] after presenting Jesus at the [[temple|Temple]].
