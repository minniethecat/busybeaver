# Palestine

## Overview

Palestine is the historical region on the eastern shore of the Mediterranean, bounded by the Jordan River to the east, the [[sin|Sin]]ai Peninsula to the south, and the mountains of Lebanon to the north. It has been known by various names — Canaan in the Bronze Age, [[judea|Judea]] and Samaria under the Romans, the Holy Land in [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian tradition — and has been contested by empires and religions for over three millennia.

For Jews, it is the Promised Land, given by [[god|God]] to [[abraham|Abraham]] and his descendants. For [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ians, it is the site of Jesus's incar[[nations|nation]], ministry, crucifixion, and resurrection — the geographical stage of salvation history. For Muslims, it contains [[jerusalem|Jerusalem]], the third holiest city in [[islam|Islam]]. The convergence of three religions on a [[guilt|sin]]gle small territory is, for Holbach, [[proof|evidence]] not of [[divinity|divine]] activity but of the contagious character of religious mythology: one local legend generates others, and claims of [[sacred|sanctity]] accumulate on the same sites because each new religion must appropriate the sacred geography of its predecessors to establish legitimacy.

## In Holbach's Argument

Palestine illustrates several of Holbach's core critical themes. First, the particularity of revelation. If [[god|God]] wished to reveal himself to all humanity, he would not have chosen "a little strip of land in Asia" — a territory smaller than many French provinces — as the exclusive stage for his communications. The [[god|God]] of [[nature|the universe]], Holbach argues, would have revealed himself simultaneously in China, [[india|India]], Persia, and the Americas. That he did not — that he apparently cared only about a [[guilt|sin]]gle small people in a [[guilt|sin]]gle small land — reveals the tribal, human origins of the revelation claim.

Second, the bloody history of Palestine [[demon|demons]]trates that sacred geography does not produce sacred behavior. "The land that is called [[sacred|holy]]," Holbach might observe, "has been soaked in more blood than any other territory of comparable size." Crusaders, conquerors, and competing religious communities have killed one another for centuries over the right to control sites that each tradition claims as uniquely sacred. The [[sacred|sanctity]] of the land has been a license for atrocity, not a restraint upon it.

Third, the archaeological record undermines the biblical narrative. Holbach was writing before the era of systematic biblical archaeology, but his general [[doubt|skepticism]] anticipated later discoveries. The conquest narratives in the Book of Joshua, which describe the [[israel|Israel]]ite destruction of Canaanite cities, are not supported by archaeological [[proof|evidence]]. Jericho, famously, shows no sign of the dramatic destruction described in [[bible|scripture]]. Palestine thus becomes, for the critical mind, a case study in the divergence between theological claim and material [[proof|evidence]].

## Historical Context

In Holbach's era, Palestine was a province of the Ottoman [[empire|Empire]], sparsely populated and economically marginal. It held no strategic significance for the [[europe|Europe]]an powers, and the idea of a Jewish return to the land was a theological concept, not a political program. The transformation of Palestine into a contested [[nations|nation]]al territory — and the [[israel|Israel]]i-Palestinian conflict that has defined the region [[guilt|sin]]ce 1948 — lay far in the future.

Holbach's interest in Palestine was not political in the modern sense but epistemological: he [[desire|want]]ed to show that the place where [[god|God]] allegedly entered human history was, in [[eve]]ry measurable respect, unremarkable. Its inhabitants were not wiser, more virtuous, or more prosperous than those of other lands. Its sacred geography was indistinguishable from profane geography. The only thing that distinguished Palestine was the intensity of the mythological investment that successive religions had made in it — an investment that Holbach understood as a human construction rather than a [[design|divine design]]ation.

## Key Themes

**1. The scandal of particularity.** For Holbach, it is a standing refutation of mono[[divinity|theism]] that the one true [[god|God]] apparently revealed himself only to one small Near Eastern people, in one small Near Eastern land, leaving the rest of humanity — "the most flourishing [[nation|nations]]" — in ignorance for millennia.

**2. [[sacred|Sacred]] geography as human construction.** Holy places become [[sacred|holy]] because people declare them so, not because of any verifiable property they possess. The [[sacred|sanctity]] of Palestine is a social fact, not a natural or supernatural one.

**3. Contested land as contested truth.** That three religions claim the same territory as uniquely sacred [[demon|demons]]trates that contradictory revelation claims cannot all be true — and that the more likely expla[[nations|nation]] is that all three are human inventions that borrow from one another.
