# Infidel

## Holbach's Critique

Holbach analyzes the concept of the **infidel** — the unbeliever, the one outside the faith — as a mechanism of religious exclusion and violence in *Common Sense*. He observes that the cry "Faith is in danger! Down upon infidelity, blasphemy, and heresy!" (002-369) is the universal rallying cry of religious persecution, deployed whenever established doctrine is questioned. The designation of "infidel" licenses violence: religious zeal "animated the nations to capture and to exterminate the infidels for the glory of God" (002-377). Yet Holbach notes the irony that the term is inherently unstable — religious factions spend their energies "accusing each other of being infidels and heretics, rending and persecuting each other without mercy" (002-396). For Holbach, "infidel" is not a description of any actual theological position but a tool of social control: it marks the boundary of the acceptable and justifies the worst excesses of religious cruelty against those on the wrong side of that boundary.

Holbach dramatizes the rallying cry of religious persecution:

> "People, to arms! Your God's cause is at stake! Heaven is outraged! Faith is in danger! Down upon infidelity, blasphemy, and heresy!"
>
> (002-369)
