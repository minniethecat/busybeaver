# Babylon

## Overview

Babylon was one of the great cities of the ancient [[universe|world]], located on the Euphrates River in what is now Iraq (approximately 85 kilometers south of Baghdad). Under Nebuchadnezzar II (r. 605–562 BCE), it was the capital of the Neo-Babylonian Empire and one of the largest and most magnificent cities of antiquity, famous for its walls, its ziggurat (the probable inspiration for the biblical Tower of Babel), and the Hanging Gardens — one of the Seven Wonders of the ancient [[universe|world]].

Babylon's importance for biblical history is that Nebuchadnezzar conquered the Kingdom of Judah in a series of campaigns (597 and 586 BCE), destroyed [[solomon|Solomon]]'s [[temple|Temple]], and deported a substantial portion of the [[judea|Judea]]n population to Babylonia. The Babylonian exile — the period during which the [[judea|Judea]]n elite lived as captives in Babylon — was the formative trauma of Jewish identity and the context in which much of the Hebrew [[bible|Bible]] was composed or redacted.

## In Holbach's Argument

Babylon serves Holbach's argument as empirical counter-[[proof|evidence]] to the claim of [[divinity|divine]] protection. If the [[god|God]] of [[israel|Israel]] had selected the [[israel|Jewish people]] as his own and promised them perpetual possession of the land, the destruction of [[jerusalem|Jerusalem]] and the deportation of its inhabitants by a pagan empire is a direct contradiction. "The [[god|God]] of [[nature|the universe]]," Holbach might ask, "could not protect his chosen people from a Mesopotamian [[monarch|king]]?"

The [[model|standard]] theological response — that the exile was [[punishment|divine punishment]] for [[israel|Israel]]'s disobedience, foretold by the [[prophecy|prophet]]s — does not, for Holbach, solve the problem. [[punishment|Punishment]] that is indistinguishable in its effects from ordinary military defeat is not [[proof|evidence]] of [[divinity|divine]] action but of a belief system that retrospectively reinterprets [[nations|nation]]al catastrophe as [[divinity|divine]] [[punishment|chastisement]]. Any outcome — victory or defeat, prosperity or disaster — can be fitted to the same theological narrative. A theory that explains [[eve]]rything explains nothing.

Holbach traces modern theological dogma directly back to ancient priestly inventions:

> Thus hypotheses, invented by a few visionists of Memphis and of Babylon, continue to be the basis of a science revered for the obscurity which makes it pass as marvelous and Divine. The first legislators of nations were priests; the first mythologists and poets were priests; the first philosophers were priests; the first physicians were priests. In their hands science became a sacred thing, prohibited to the profane.
>
> (002-435)

The exile also creates a historical problem for the authority of [[bible|scripture]]. The Hebrew [[bible|Bible]] as we have it was substantially shaped by the experience of exile — edited, compiled, and in some cases composed in Babylon or under its influence. If the sacred text is a product of a specific political catastrophe, its claim to timeless [[divinity|divine]] authority is compromised. It is, from Holbach's perspective, a document produced by defeated people explaining their defeat in theological terms — not the revealed word of the [[god|God]] who allegedly inflicted that defeat.

## Historical Context

Babylon had a long and complex history before Nebuchadnezzar. It was the center of Hammurabi's empire in the eighteenth century BCE and the source of one of the earliest legal codes. It had been sacked and rebuilt multiple times. Under Nebuchadnezzar's father Nabopolassar, Babylon led the revolt that destroyed the Assyrian [[empire|Empire]] — the power that had conquered the northern Kingdom of [[israel|Israel]] a century earlier.

After the Neo-Babylonian Empire fell to Cyrus the Great of Persia in 539 BCE, Cyrus permitted the exiled [[judea|Judea]]ns to return and rebuild the [[temple|Temple]]. This act of Persian imperial policy was retrospectively interpreted in Jewish [[bible|scripture]] as [[divinity|divine]] [[salvation|deliverance]] — Cyrus is called "the Lord's anointed" in Isaiah 45 — which, for Holbach, only confirms the [[model|pattern]]: political [[eve]]nts are given theological interpretations that serve the interests of the interpreters.

## Key Themes

**1. [[empire|Empire]] as refutation of [[predestination|election]].** The conquest of Judah by Babylon — a pagan empire that did not acknowledge the [[god|God]] of [[israel|Israel]] — [[demon|demons]]trates that [[divinity|divine]] protection is indistinguishable from its absence.

**2. Exile as the formation of [[bible|scripture]].** The fact that the Hebrew [[bible|Bible]] was largely shaped in Babylonian exile — that the sacred text is a product of defeat — undermines its claim to timeless, transcendent authority.

**3. The retrospective character of theological expla[[nations|nation]].** The [[prophecy|prophet]]s "predicted" the exile in terms sufficiently vague to be retrofitted to the [[eve]]nt. Babylon becomes a case study in the retrospective construction of prophecy.
