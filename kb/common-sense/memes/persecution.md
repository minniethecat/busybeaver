# Persecution

## Definition

Persecution, for Holbach, is the natural behavior of religious institutions toward those who dissent from their doctrines. It is not a corruption of religion's true [[soul|spirit]] but its authentic expression — "intolerance, and the [[soul|spirit]] of [[cruelty|persecution]], as the true foundations of his future authority" (ch. CXLVIII). A religion that claims exclusive access to [[divinity|divine]] truth cannot tolerate alternatives, because [[toleration|tolerance]] implies that truth is not exclusive and that error is not damnable. Religious [[cruelty|persecution]] is [[religion|theology]] turned into law.

Holbach distinguishes religious persecution from secular punishment. The secular magistrate punishes actions that harm society. The religious persecutor punishes beliefs that offend [[god|God]] — a [[existence|being]] whose existence is unproven and whose preferences are interpreted by [[interest|self-interest]]ed [[clergy|priests]]. "Instead of sounding the toc[[vice|sin]] of [[cruelty|persecution]] against your adversaries, instead of entertaining the people with foolish disputes, preach to them humane and social [[morality|virtue]]s" (ch. CLXIX). Secular [[toleration|tolerance]] is possible; religious [[toleration|tolerance]] is contradictory because religion's truth-claims are absolute.

The victims of [[cruelty|persecution]] are not only those directly punished. Persecution also silences the living by example — "threatened with the most cruel [[cruelty|persecution]], were called upon to renounce reason and to submit to [[religion|faith]]" (ch. II). The fear of [[cruelty|persecution]] produces intellectual conformity more effectively than [[cruelty|persecution]] itself.

## Historical Context

Religious [[cruelty|persecution]] was a living reality in Holbach's [[paris|France]]. The [[edict-of-nantes|Revocation of the Edict of Nantes]] (1685) had [[desire|drive]]n hundreds of thousands of Huguenots into exile. The last execution for heresy in [[paris|France]] occurred in 1762, and the Calas affair — in which [[voltaire|Voltaire]] famously intervened to exonerate a Protestant father wrongly executed for allegedly murdering his son to prevent conversion — was in 1762, when *Common Sense* was [[existence|being]] drafted. The [[inquisition|Inquisition]] remained active in S[[suffering|pain]] and Portugal.

[[enlightenment|The Enlightenment]]'s case for toleration was built by [[john-locke|Locke]] (*A Letter Concerning [[toleration|Toleration]]*, 1689), [[pierre-bayle|Bayle]] (*Philosophical Commentary*, 1686), and [[voltaire|Voltaire]] (*Treatise on [[toleration|Toleration]]*, 1763). Their arguments were pragmatic ([[cruelty|persecution]] causes civil strife), skeptical (no one can be certain enough to justify coercion), and moral (forced belief is insincere and therefore worthless to [[god|God]]). Holbach added a structural analysis: [[cruelty|persecution]] is not an abuse of religion but its essence, because religion claims exclusive truth enforced by [[punishment|divine punishment]].

## Core Arguments

**1. Persecution is essential to religion, not [[chance|accident]]al.** "[[intolerance|Intolerance]], and the [[soul|spirit]] of [[cruelty|persecution]], as the true foundations of his future authority." (ch. CXLVIII). [[priest|Priest]]s teach intolerance because their power depends on it. A tolerant religion would be a weak religion, because [[toleration|tolerance]] admits that alternatives might be acceptable.

**2. Persecution cripples intellectual progress.** "Threatened with the most cruel [[cruelty|persecution]], were called upon to renounce reason and to submit to [[religion|faith]] — that is to say, to priestly authority." (ch. II). The threat of [[cruelty|persecution]] forces thinkers to self-censor, and a censored mind cannot contribute to the [[progress|advancement]] of knowledge.

**3. All religions persecute when they have power.** "There is not one which has not had firm and zealous defenders who have suffered [[cruelty|persecution]] in order to maintain their religion." (ch. II). Every religion has both persecuted and been persecuted. The victim of one age becomes the persecutor of the next, which proves that [[cruelty|persecution]] is structural, not personal.

**4. Secular authority should suppress religious persecution.** Holbach calls on [[monarch|sovereign]]s to "take the tone of reason, instead of sounding the toc[[vice|sin]] of [[cruelty|persecution]]" — to suppress religious conflict rather than ta[[monarch|king]] sides in it.

## Relation to Other Concepts

Persecution is the practical expression of [[intolerance|Intolerance]] and the enforcement mechanism of [[Imposture]]. It is justified by the theological logic of [[Punishment]] — if [[god|God]] eternally punishes wrong belief, then temporal authorities should punish it too. [[Fanaticism]] provides the [[passion|emotion]]al fuel for [[cruelty|persecution]], and [[Cruelty]] is what [[cruelty|persecution]] becomes when it ceases [[eve]]n to pretend to justice. [[Progress]] is impossible where [[cruelty|persecution]] is active because inquiry is suppressed.

## In the Text

On [[cruelty|persecution]] as the foundation of priestly authority:

> "They will hold up to their pupil superstition as the most important and the most sacred thing, its chimerical duties as the most holy obligations, intolerance, and the spirit of persecution, as the true foundations of his future authority." (ch. CXLVIII)

On the suppression of reason:

> "How could men thus fettered give free flight to their genius, perfect reason — threatened with the most cruel persecution, were called upon to renounce reason and to submit to faith." (ch. II)

On replacing [[cruelty|persecution]] with reason:

> "Take the tone of reason, instead of sounding the tocsin of persecution against your adversaries; instead of entertaining the people with foolish disputes, preach to them humane and social virtues." (ch. CLXIX)
