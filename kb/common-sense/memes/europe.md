# Europe

## Overview

Europe in the 18th century was the theater of a contradiction. It was at once the most thoroughly [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ianized continent — its political borders roughly coterminous with the boundaries of Latin [[christianity|Christendom]], its calendar structured by the liturgical year, its intellectual institutions (universities, academies) founded and sustained by [[church|the Church]] — and the birthplace of the most radical critique [[christianity|Christianity]] had [[eve]]r faced. Holbach's "Common Sense" (1772) was both a product of European civilization and a declaration of war on one of its foundational institutions. [[mind|Understanding]] Europe is essential to [[knowledge|understanding]] what Holbach was attac[[monarch|king]], what resources he drew upon, and what he hoped to transform.

## The Political Geography of Faith

Europe in Holbach's era was a patchwork of confessional states, the legacy of the [[reform|Reform]]ation and the wars of religion that followed it:

This map of confessional diversity was, for Holbach, both [[proof|evidence]] against any [[vice|sin]]gle church's claim to exclusive truth and a practical resource — the existence of competing jurisdictions created spaces of relative toleration where radical ideas could be printed and circulated.

## The Enlightenment's Geography

[[enlightenment|The Enlightenment]] was a pan-European phenomenon, but it took different forms in different [[nations|nation]]al contexts:

1. **[[paris|France]]**: The most radical Enlightenment, centered in [[paris|Paris]]. Holbach, Diderot, Helvétius — systematic [[matter|materialism]] and open a[[divinity|theism]].
2. **Scotland**: [[mode|Mode]]rate Enlightenment. [[david-hume|Hume]]'s [[doubt|skepticism]], Smith's political economy, Robertson's historiography — reformist, not revolutionary.
3. **German [[politics-index|State]]s**: *Aufklärung* — cautious, often university-based, reconciling reason with Protestant [[religion|faith]] (Kant: "religion within the limits of reason alone").
4. **England**: The pioneer that became a spectator. After the Glorious [[revolution|Revolution]] (1688), English intellectual life was less oppositional than the French.

Holbach's Europe was thus intellectually integrated but politically fractured — a continent in which ideas crossed borders more freely than the people who produced them, and in which the safest place to print a book was often not the safest place to read it.

## Europe as Christendom

Holbach's term for Europe's religious identity is implicit but unmistakable: Europe *was* [[christianity|Christendom]] — the territorial extent of [[church|the Church]]'s authority. This identification made his critique simultaneously philosophical and geographical. To attack [[church|the Church]] was to attack the organizing principle of European civilization; to propose an alternative was to propose a re-foundation of Europe on different principles — reason, [[universe|nature]], and a morality derived from human needs rather than [[divinity|divine]] commands.

## Europe After Holbach

The Europe Holbach's ideas helped shape would, over the following two centuries, undergo transformations he could not have anticipated but would have recognized: the secularization of public life, the separation of church and state, the decline of [[authority|clerical power]], and the emergence of a public sphere in which religious claims are subject to rational scrutiny rather than shielded by institutional authority. Contemporary Europe — with its largely secular populations, its legal protections for [[liberty|freedom]] of thought, and its marginalization of theological disputes from political decision-ma[[monarch|king]] — is, in important respects, the Europe Holbach argued for.

## In the Text

Holbach does not name "Europe" directly in "Common Sense," but the continental scope of his critique is implicit in [[eve]]ry chapter. The "vast empire" of the Apologue (ch. I) is a universal allegory, applicable wherever invisible monarchs rule through priestly mediation — which is to say, across the whole of [[christianity|Christendom]].


> The savage inhabitants of Paraguay pretend to be descended from the moon,
> and appear to us as simpletons; the theologians of Europe pretend to be
> descended from a pure spirit. Is this pretension more sensible?
>
> (002-277)
