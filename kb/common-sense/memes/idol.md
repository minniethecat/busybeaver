## Idols as Fabrications of Priestcraft

For Holbach, idols are not merely objects of misguided worship but deliberate fabrications of the priestly class. The priests construct "gigantic idols, whom they have raised to their own grandeur" (002-408) — elevating rulers and deities alike as instruments of their own power. The idol serves a structural function in Holbach's sociology of religion: it is the visible token of invisible superstition, the material expression of a system designed to keep the people in awe and submission.

## The Universality of Idol Worship

Holbach's analysis extends beyond any single religious tradition. He observes that idol worship is a universal phenomenon: "Everywhere on earth we find religious ideas periodically afflicting and rejoicing the people; everywhere we see rites, practices" and the worship of idols spanning from paganism through Moses and into Christianity itself (002-439). This comparative perspective is central to Holbach's argument — idolatry is not a foreign aberration but a recurring pattern that reappears whenever critical reason gives way to priestly authority.

## The Destruction of the Idol

A recurring image in *Common Sense* is the destruction of the idol by the very people who created it. Holbach writes that the tyrant "preaches sedition and demolishes the idol which he has made, when he conceives himself strong enough to do so" (002-312). This image captures a core insight of Holbach's political thought: idols are maintained only as long as they serve the interests of those who erect them. When the balance of power shifts, yesterday's god becomes today's discarded fiction. The idol depends entirely on belief — once belief falters, the idol falls.

## Idols and the Corruption of Morality

Holbach connects idol worship to the broader corruption of morality under religious systems. The flame of religious conflict burns "among Christians and idolaters" alike (002-326), and the most abominable cruelties have been committed in the name of competing idols. By revealing the common mechanism underlying all idolatry, Holbach seeks to expose the absurdity of religious persecution: the persecutor and the persecuted worship different idols, but both are victims of the same fundamental delusion.

## From *Common Sense*

> Have not the ministers of the Lord seen that in pampering the sovereigns, in forging Divine rights for them, and in delivering to them the people, bound hand and foot, they were making tyrants of them, whom one day they would be the first victims? They might have known that authority, once intoxicated, would sooner or later turn against them, and that they had cause to fear that these gigantic idols, whom they have raised to their own grandeur, would one day crush them by their weight.
>
> (002-408)
