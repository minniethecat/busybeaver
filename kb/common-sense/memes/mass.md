# Mass

## Definition

The Mass is the central liturgical ritual of the [[catholic-church|Catholic Church]], celebrated as the reenactment of [[jesus-christ|Christ]]'s Last Supper and the sacramental transformation of [[transubstantiation|bread and wine]] into the body and blood of [[jesus-christ|Christ]] (transubstantiation). In Holbach's materialist critique, the Mass is [[guilt|sin]]gled out as a particularly vivid example of superstitious [[ritual|ceremony]] — a ritual that claims supernatural efficacy while consisting entirely of human inventions and theatrical gestures.

[[meslier|Jean Meslier]], the [[atheism|atheist]] priest whose posthumous testament inspired Holbach's *Common Sense*, described "that superstitious Mass, and those ridiculous administrations of [[sacrament|sacraments]]" with "what a disdain" he felt for his own ministry. The Mass, for both Meslier and Holbach, encapsulates [[eve]]rything wrong with institutional religion: empty ritual presented as [[mystery|divine mystery]], designed to maintain priestly authority over the laity.

## Historical Context

The doctrine of the Mass was a central flashpoint of the [[reformation|Protestant Reformation]]. Martin Luther rejected transubstantiation in favor of consubstantiation; Calvin denied any physical presence of [[jesus-christ|Christ]] in the elements; Zwingli treated the Eucharist as purely symbolic. The [[council-of-trent|Council of Trent]] (1545–1563) reaffirmed transubstantiation as [[doctrine|dogma]], declaring anathema upon anyone who denied it.

By Holbach's time, the Mass remained the most visible and frequently performed Catholic ritual. Every priest was obligated to celebrate Mass daily; [[eve]]ry Catholic was obligated to attend on Sundays and [[sacred|holy]] days. For Enlightenment critics, this mandatory, repetitive, and theologically elaborate [[ritual|ceremony]] exemplified the [[absurdity|irrationality]] and social control inherent in organized religion.

## Core Arguments

**1. The Mass is a "superstitious" and "ridiculous" [[ritual|ceremony]].** Meslier's autobiographical confession — which Holbach prominently features — describes the Mass with undisguised contempt. The priest who performed it daily for thirty years calls it an exercise in "exciting your [[belief|credulity]]" through solemn theatrical display.

**2. [[transubstantiation|Transubstantiation]] is philosophically incoherent.** While Holbach devotes less space to the specific metaphysics of the Eucharist than to more general theological concepts, the materialist framework of *Common Sense* leaves no room for the idea that [[transubstantiation|bread and wine]] can become literal flesh and blood while retaining their sensory appearance. The doctrine violates the principle that matter behaves according to fixed natural laws.

**3. [[ritual|Ritual]] replaces genuine morality.** The Mass exemplifies how religious [[ritual|ceremony]] substitutes for ethical conduct. Believers attend Mass, receive communion, and feel their obligations fulfilled — regardless of their behavior outside the church. Like [[confession]] and [[absolution]], the Mass functions as a mechanism that "gives him the right to follow without [[conscience|remorse]] his vicious practices."

**4. The Mass sustains priestly power.** The doctrine that only ordained [[clergy|priests]] can consecrate the Eucharist creates a dependency relationship between laity and [[priest|clergy]]. Without the priest, there is no Mass; without the Mass (in Catholic [[religion|theology]]), there is no access to [[jesus-christ|Christ]]'s salvific sacrifice. Holbach sees this as a classic example of [[priestcraft]] — institutional arrangements that manufacture dependency on a priestly caste.

## In the Text

The most direct discussion of the Mass appears in the Preface, where Holbach quotes Meslier's deathbed confession:

> "What a disdain I had for my ministry, and particularly for that superstitious Mass, and those ridiculous administrations of sacraments, especially if I was compelled to perform them with the solemnity which awakened all your piety and all your good faith. What remorse I had for exciting your credulity!"

This passage is significant because it comes not from an external critic but from a lifelong Catholic priest — someone who performed the Mass himself for decades and came to see it as empty theater.

## Relation to Other Concepts

The Mass is closely tied to [[sacrament]], [[ritual]], [[priestcraft]], and [[superstition]]. It serves as a concrete example of the broader critique of [[ceremony]] and religious [[ritual]] that runs through *Common Sense*. The Mass also connects to [[transubstantiation]] and Catholic [[dogma]], as well as to the Protestant [[reformation]], which made the [[universe|nature]] of the Eucharist a central point of theological rupture.
