# Temperance

## Definition

Temperance, in the context of Holbach's "Common Sense" (1772), appears primarily through its negation—[[intemperance]]—as part of Holbach's empirical critique of [[religion]]'s claimed moral utility. Theologians argue that religion is necessary as a restraint on the passions of the common people. Holbach counters that the observable evidence does not support this claim: religious belief does not in fact prevent drunkenness, brutality, fraud, or other excesses (002-421).

## Holbach's Critique

Holbach addresses temperance within his larger argument against the social utility of religion. Proponents of established [[religion]] maintain that while enlightened persons may govern themselves by [[reason]], the masses require religious fear to restrain their behavior. Holbach asks whether this claim withstands empirical scrutiny:

> Religion, they tell us, is necessary for the masses; that though enlightened persons may not need restraint upon their opinions, it is necessary at least for the common people, in whom education has not developed reason. Is it true, then, that religion is a restraint for the people? Do we see that this religion prevents them from intemperance, drunkenness, brutality, violence, frauds, and all kinds of excesses?
>
> (002-421)

The implied answer is negative: religion does not produce temperance. Holbach's point is not to deny the value of self-restraint but to challenge the instrumental justification of religion as a necessary prop for public morality.

## Relation to Other Concepts

Temperance relates to Holbach's broader moral philosophy, in which [[virtue]] is grounded in [[reason]] and [[nature]], not in supernatural sanction. The failure of religion to produce temperance supports his argument that morality should be founded on rational self-interest and [[education]] rather than on fear of divine punishment. The concept connects to [[passion]], [[ethics]], and Holbach's critique of [[priest|priestly]] power.
