# Damnation

## Holbach's Critique

The doctrine of **eternal damnation** is, for Holbach, among the most morally repugnant inventions of theology. In *Common Sense*, he argues that "the dogma of eternal damnation" (002-107) transforms the deity into a monster: "If there exists a God who could resolve to punish his creatures throughout eternity for errors which their reason was unable to avoid, would not this God be supremely unjust and supremely cruel?" (002-107). Holbach observes that the threat of damnation creates a form of psychological terrorism: the believer is kept in a state of perpetual anxiety, always "exposed to the perils of damnation" (002-098), never certain of his salvation. He asks pointedly whether any reasonable person would willingly incur "the risk of incurring eternal damnation" (002-285) for the sake of honest inquiry. For Holbach, the doctrine of damnation reveals the core logic of religious morality: it is not based on reason or natural human sympathy but on fear — the threat of infinite punishment for finite transgressions.

## Selected Passage

Holbach denounces the doctrine of eternal damnation as an instrument of cruelty, not divine justice:

> It could have been but the most cruel barbarity, the most notorious imposition, but the blindest ambition which could have created the dogma of eternal damnation. If there exists a God who could be offended or blasphemed, there would not be upon earth any greater blasphemers than those who dare to say that this God is perverse enough to take pleasure in dooming His feeble creatures to useless torments for all eternity.
>
> (002-107)
