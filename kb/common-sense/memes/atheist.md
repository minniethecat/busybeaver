# Atheist

## Definition

In the theological vocabulary of Holbach's era, "[[atheism|atheist]]" was a term of condem[[nations|nation]] — synonymous with [[vice|immorality]], anarchy, and intellectual depravity. Holbach's project in *Common Sense* is, in substantial part, to reverse this valuation: to argue that the [[atheism|atheist]] — one who denies the existence of [[god|God]] on rational grounds — is more honest, more consistent, and frequently more virtuous than the believer whose piety licenses [[cruelty|persecution]].

Holbach distinguishes between two types of [[atheism|atheist]]. The speculative [[atheism|atheist]] denies [[god|God]]'s existence as a philosophical conclusion, arrived at through rational inquiry. The practical [[atheism|atheist]] lives as though [[god|God]] does not exist, regardless of professed belief — and Holbach observes that many of history's worst criminals have been professed believers who acted with the conviction that [[god|God]] authorized their crimes. The speculative [[atheism|atheist]], by contrast, has no [[divinity|divine]] warrant for cruelty and must answer for his actions to the only tribunal that exists: human judgment.

## Historical Context

[[atheism|Atheism]] in [[france|eighteenth-century France]] was a capital [[crime|offense]]. The last person executed for atheism in [[france|France]] was the Chevalier de La Barre in 1766 — tortured, beheaded, and burned with a copy of [[voltaire|Voltaire]]'s *Philosophical Dictionary* nailed to his body. Holbach published his [[atheism|atheist]]ic works anonymously or pseudonymously, printed abroad and smuggled into [[france|France]]. To openly declare oneself an [[atheism|atheist]] was to risk imprisonment, exile, or death.

The intellectual resources for atheism had been accumulating for over a century. [[baruch-spinoza|Spinoza]]'s identification of [[god|God]] with [[universe|nature]] (1670) had provided a metaphysical framework that could be read as [[atheism|atheist]]ic. [[pierre-bayle|Pierre Bayle]] (1697) had argued that a society of [[atheism|atheist]]s could be moral. The clandestine manuscript tradition — texts like *The Treatise of the Three Impostors* — had circulated [[atheism|atheist]]ic arguments among the intellectual underground. Holbach synthesized these resources into a comprehensive, systematic, and unapologetic atheism.

## Core Arguments

Holbach advances four arguments for the moral and intellectual legitimacy of atheism.

First, atheism is the natural terminus of rational inquiry. If reason demands [[proof|evidence]] for belief, and there is no [[proof|evidence]] for [[god|God]]'s existence, then the reasonable conclusion is atheism. The believer who acknowledges the insufficiency of the [[proof|evidence]] but believes anyway — on [[religion|faith]] — has abandoned reason.

Second, atheism is morally superior to [[divinity|theism]]. The theist who commits atrocity in [[god|God]]'s name is more dangerous than the [[atheism|atheist]] who commits atrocity for mundane reasons, because the theist believes his crime is a sacred duty. The [[atheism|atheist]] has no such delusion and can be restrained by ordinary human sanctions.

Third, atheism is intellectually honest in a way that agnosticism is not. The agnostic who says "we cannot know whether [[god|God]] exists" concedes too much — he grants that the proposition "[[god|God]] exists" is a meaningful hypothesis about which [[proof|evidence]] is merely insufficient. Holbach argues that the concept of [[god|God]] is not a hypothesis at all but an incoherent assemblage of contradictory [[attribute|attributes]]. "[[god|God]] does not exist" is not a conjecture but a logical conclusion.

Fourth, atheism is compatible with — indeed conducive to — [[society|social order]]. The fear of [[punishment|divine punishment]] is not necessary for morality; human [[existence|being]]s have natural motives for ethical behavior (sympathy, [[interest|self-interest]], the [[passion|desire]] for social approval) that operate independently of theological belief. A society of [[atheism|atheist]]s would be governed by the same natural incentives that govern all human conduct.

## In the Text

Holbach's defense of atheism pervades *Common Sense*:

> "The atheist is a man who has examined, who has weighed the proofs, and who has found them wanting. (002-236)" (paraphrase)

> "It is not the atheist who is to be feared, but the fanatic who believes himself commissioned by heaven to exterminate those who do not think as he does."
