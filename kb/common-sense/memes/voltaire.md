# Voltaire

## Who

François-Marie Arouet (1694–1778), universally known by his pen name Voltaire, was the most celebrated writer and philosopher of the French Enlightenment. A prolific author of plays, poems, novels, historical works, and philosophical essays, Voltaire wielded his pen against what he called *l'infâme* — the alliance of religious superstition, [[authority|clerical power]], and political [[tyranny|absolutism]]. Holbach, though more radical in his conclusions, drew substantially from the critical toolkit Voltaire had forged: wit as a weapon against [[doctrine|dogma]], historical erudition as a solvent of sacred narratives, and public ridicule as a strategy against institutions too powerful to confront directly.

## What

Voltaire's philosophical contribution to Holbach's project can be summarized under three headings. First, **biblical criticism**: Voltaire's historical exami[[nation|nations]] of [[bible|scripture]] exposed contradictions, anachronisms, and moral atrocities in the biblical text, undermining its claim to [[divinity|divine]] authorship. Second, **anti-clericalism**: Voltaire relentlessly documented the abuses of ecclesiastical power — the [[cruelty|persecution]] of dissenters, the wealth of monasteries, the political machinations of the [[rome|Vatican]] — providing Holbach with an empirical record of religious malfeasance. Third, **Deism as a transitional position**: Voltaire argued for a rational [[creator|Creator]] who established natural laws but does not intervene miraculously — a position Holbach would later reject as philosophically incoherent, arguing that a [[creator|Creator]] without ongoing intervention is indistinguishable from no [[creator|Creator]] at all.

## Why It Matters

Voltaire matters to Holbach's system in three distinct ways:

1. **He cleared the ground**: Voltaire's decades of anti-clerical polemic created the intellectual space within which more radical positions like Holbach's [[atheism|atheist]]ic [[matter|materialism]] could be articulated without immediate suppression.

2. **He normalized critique**: By ma[[monarch|king]] religious criticism fashionable in elite circles, Voltaire shifted the burden of [[evidence|proof]] — it became socially acceptable, [[eve]]n expected, to subject religious claims to rational scrutiny.

3. **He provided the method**: Holbach inherited Voltaire's techniques of satirical exposure, historical debun[[monarch|king]], and the strategic use of wit over solemn refutation. The Apologue in "Common Sense" (ch. I) is a direct descendant of Voltaire's allegorical style.

## Historical Context

Voltaire spent his career in an 18th-century [[paris|France]] where censorship, *lettres de cachet* (arbitrary imprisonment orders), and the combined authority of throne and altar made open a[[divinity|theism]] dangerous. His strategy of indirection — moc[[monarch|king]] religion while professing Deism, attac[[monarch|king]] "superstition" while formally respecting "true religion" — was partly tactical. Holbach, operating from the relative safety of his [[paris|Paris]] salon and publishing anonymously or pseudonymously abroad, could afford to draw the conclusions Voltaire had only implied.

## Key Works Relevant to Holbach

| Work | Year | Relevance |
|------|------|-----------|
| *Dictionnaire philosophique* | 1764 | Alphabetical entries deconstructing biblical narratives and theological concepts |
| *Traité sur la tolérance* | 1763 | Defense of [[toleration|religious toleration]] following the Calas affair |
| *Candide* | 1759 | Satirical demolition of [[gottfried-leibniz|Leibniz]]ian optimism and [[injustice|theodicy]] |
| *Examen important de Milord Bolingbroke* | 1767 | Biblical criticism that Holbach drew upon directly |

These works supplied Holbach with an arsenal of arguments, facts, and rhetorical strategies that "Common Sense" (1772) systematized into a comprehensive [[atheism|atheist]]ic philosophy.

## In the Text

Holbach does not name Voltaire directly in "Common Sense" — a consequence of the work's anonymous publication and radical content. However, the intellectual debt is unmistakable. The critique of biblical morality, the exposure of priestly imposture, and the historical argument that religions are human inventions all trace lineages through Voltaire's corpus. Holbach's radicalization of Voltairean Deism into outright a[[divinity|theism]] represents the logical terminus of the Enlightenment's critical project.


> This work of the honest pastor is the most curious and the most powerful
> thing of the kind which the last century produced. . . . . Paine and
> Voltaire had reserves, but Jean Meslier had none. He keeps nothing back;
> and yet, after all, the wonder is not that there should have been one
> priest who left that testimony at his death, but that all priests do not.
>
> (APP007-020)
