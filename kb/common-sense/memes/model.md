# Model

## Definition

For Holbach, a model is a pattern held up for imitation — an exemplar that shapes how people think, feel, and act. Every society, [[eve]]ry educational system, [[eve]]ry religion proposes models: the saint, the hero, the sage, the martyr. Holbach's critique is that the models offered by [[christianity|Christianity]] are systematically harmful. They valorize [[evil|suffering]] over happiness, obedience over autonomy, and the next [[universe|world]] over this one. "Men! you worship but a great savage, whom you consider as a model to follow" (ch. CXIX).

The alternative, for Holbach, is to derive our models from [[universe|nature]] and reason rather than from [[religion|theology]] and superstition. A good model is one whose imitation promotes human welfare — the honest citizen, the just [[monarch|ruler]], the curious scientist, the compassionate neighbor. These are not dramatic figures; they do not perform [[miracle|miracles]] or endure [[martyr|martyrdom]]. But they make [[earth|the world]] better, and that — not dramatic self-denial — is the measure of a model's worth.

The concept of "model" also appears in Holbach's epistemology. He argues that all our ideas derive from [[experience|sensory experience]]: "All our ideas are but pictures of objects which strike us. What can the idea of [[god|God]] represent to us when it is evidently an idea without an object? Is not such an idea as impossible as an effect without a cause? An idea without a prototype, is it anything but a chimera?" (ch. II). A model, in this sense, is the prototype from which an idea is copied — and an idea without a prototype is an illusion. The idea of [[god|God]], lac[[monarch|king]] any sensory prototype, is precisely such an illusion.

## Historical Context

The 18th century was deeply concerned with models and exemplars. The classical tradition, revived in the Renaissance and still dominant in Holbach's education, presented a gallery of exemplary figures: Cato the Stoic, who chose death over submission to [[despotism|tyranny]]; [[cicero|Cicero]] the orator, who embodied eloquence in the service of the republic; [[socrates|Socrates]] the philosopher, who questioned [[eve]]rything and accepted death rather than renounce his mission. These were secular [[saint|saints]] — models of [[morality|virtue]] derived from reason and history, not from revelation.

[[christianity|Christianity]] offered a competing gallery: [[jesus-christ|Christ]] the [[evil|suffering]] servant, the [[martyr|martyrs]] who died for the [[religion|faith]], the [[saint|saints]] who renounced [[earth|the world]] for the cloister, the ascetics who mortified the flesh to purify the soul. Holbach regarded these models as not merely inferior but actively harmful. The model of [[jesus-christ|Christ]] teaches submission to unjust authority ("render unto Caesar"); the model of the martyr teaches that [[evil|suffering]] is redemptive; the model of the ascetic teaches hatred of the body and [[nature|the natural world]].

[[enlightenment|The Enlightenment]] project of replacing [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian models with secular ones reached its apotheosis in [[revolution|the French Revolution]]'s Cult of [[reason|Reason]] and Cult of the [[being|Supreme Being]], which invented new festivals, new calendars, and new exemplary figures (Marat the martyr, Rousseau the philosopher-legislator). Holbach would not have endorsed all these innovations, but they followed the logic of his argument: if the old models are pernicious, new ones must be created.

## Core Arguments

**1. Religious models are morally pernicious.** The [[god|God]] of the [[scripture|Bible]] — jealous, wrathful, capricious — is offered as the supreme model for human conduct, and the result is predictable: "The [[god|God]]-monarch is but too well imitated by His representatives upon the earth" (ch. II). Kings who model themselves on the [[divinity|divine]] tyrant become tyrants; [[clergy|priests]] who model themselves on the [[divinity|divine]] judge become inquisitors.

**2. An idea without a prototype is a chimera.** Holbach's empiricist epistemology holds that all knowledge derives from [[experience|sensory experience]]. The idea of [[god|God]], lac[[monarch|king]] any sensory prototype, is not a genuine idea at all — it is a word attached to nothing. "Is not such an idea as impossible as an effect without a cause? An idea without a prototype, is it anything but a chimera?" (ch. II).

**3. Good models must be derived from [[universe|nature]] and reason.** The proper models for human conduct are not found in [[bible|scripture]] but in the study of human [[universe|nature]], human needs, and human society. What promotes happiness? What reduces [[evil|suffering]]? What makes cooperation possible? The answers to these questions — not the commands of an invisible [[god|God]] — provide the models by which we should live.

**4. Models shape character, and character shapes society.** Holbach takes seriously the educational function of models. "[[priest|Priest]]s and devout persons are charged with forming the mind and the heart of the young [[monarch|prince]]s destined to govern the [[nation|nations]]" (ch. CLXIX). When these educators hold up religious models — [[saint|saints]], [[martyr|martyrs]], ascetics — they produce [[monarch|ruler]]s who are "imbecile devotees" with "no idea of justice or of injustice." When education holds up rational models, it produces citizens capable of governing themselves.

## Reception and Critique

Holbach's critique of religious models was part of a broader Enlightenment project of secular moral education — a project that shaped the French revolutionary calendar (with its festivals of [[reason|Reason]], [[virtue|Virtue]], and the [[being|Supreme Being]]) and the 19th-century secular education movements (the French Third Republic's *école laïque*, the American common school movement).

Critics have raised several concerns. First, secular models are not obviously more effective than religious ones at motivating moral behavior — the honest citizen is less inspiring than the saint. Second, the replacement of religious models with secular ones can itself become a kind of religion, with its own [[dogma|dogmas]], orthodoxies, and inquisitions — as the revolutionary [[terror|Terror]] [[demon|demons]]trated. Third, Holbach's empiricist epistemology (all ideas from sensory prototypes) may be too restrictive: mathematics, logic, and abstract concepts do not seem to require sensory prototypes in his sense.

## Relation to Other Concepts

Model connects to [[Education]] as the process by which models are transmitted from one generation to the next. It connects to [[Virtue]] as the quality that models are supposed to exemplify — but Holbach insists that true [[morality|virtue]] is secular, not religious. Model is opposed to [[Superstition]], which supplies false models (gods, [[saint|saints]], [[demon|demons]]) that misdirect human conduct.

Model also connects to [[Reason]] as the faculty that evaluates models — that asks whether a proposed exemplar genuinely promotes human welfare or merely serves the interests of [[clergy|priests]] and tyrants. The concept of the model is central to Holbach's [[Politics]]: a good polity requires good models, and good models require a secular, rational education.
