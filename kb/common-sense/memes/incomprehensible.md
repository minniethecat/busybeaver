# Incomprehensible

## Definition

In Holbach's *Common Sense*, the claim that God is "incomprehensible" is one of the central contradictions he identifies in theological reasoning. Theology asserts simultaneously that "the essence of God is incomprehensible to man" and that humans "can not dispense with a knowledge of this God so impossible to conceive of" (002-005). The term "incomprehensible" thus becomes the pivot on which Holbach's entire critique of theological epistemology turns: if God is truly incomprehensible, then all theological claims about God are unfounded; if theological claims about God are legitimate, then God is not incomprehensible. Theology cannot have it both ways.

## The Paradox of Incomprehensibility

Holbach develops the paradox systematically. Theology "has for its object only incomprehensible things" (002-001) — it is a discipline defined by its claim to knowledge of the unknowable. "His qualities are totally incomprehensible; moreover, his ministers disagree among themselves" (001-001) — not only is God said to be beyond comprehension, but the very people who claim to speak for God cannot agree on what God is. The concept of an incomprehensible being who demands belief is, for Holbach, "an absolutely incomprehensible and contradictory being" (002-045). The contradiction is not in the mind of the skeptic but in the concept itself.

## The Universality of the Incomprehensibility Claim

Holbach notes that the claim of divine incomprehensibility is not unique to Christianity but recurs across religious traditions. The same pattern appears whenever theology encounters rational criticism: "the essence of God is incomprehensible to man" (005-002) is not a claim specific to one revelation but a universal defensive maneuver. Every priesthood, when pressed to explain its doctrines, retreats to the assertion that the divine exceeds human understanding. For Holbach, this universality is evidence not of a genuine encounter with transcendence but of a shared rhetorical strategy. If the incomprehensibility claim were evidence of truth rather than of evasion, different religions would not produce incompatible accounts of what lies beyond comprehension. That they all arrive at the same epistemological dead-end, while disagreeing about everything else, suggests that incomprehensibility is not a discovery but a tactic.

## The Function of Incomprehensibility

Holbach argues that incomprehensibility is not an accidental feature of theology but its functional core. By insisting that God is beyond comprehension, theologians insulate their claims from rational scrutiny. "Do you not see that by this means you authorize the most absurd opinions?" (002-125). If the standard of truth is incomprehensibility, then the more absurd a doctrine, the more divine it appears. "Let us conclude, then, that a being that is incomprehensible can be of no use to us" (002-236) — a God that cannot be understood cannot guide action, cannot ground morality, and cannot serve as an object of genuine knowledge.

> The essence of God is incomprehensible to man; at the same time they do not hesitate to assign attributes to this incomprehensible God, and assure us that man can not dispense with a knowledge of this God so impossible to conceive of.
>
> (002-005)
