# Folly

## Definition

Folly, for Holbach, is not mere foolishness but a systematic form of [[absurdity|irrationality]] — the willing or coerced abandonment of reason in favor of beliefs and practices that contradict the [[proof|evidence]] of the senses and the conclusions of rational thought. While folly can be found in all domains of human life, Holbach focuses on its religious instantiation: the credulous acceptance of unverifiable doctrines, the performance of cruel and [[absurdity|absurd]] rituals, and the elevation of what is patently unreasonable to the status of [[wisdom|divine wisdom]]. Religious folly is distinguished from ordinary error by its institutional perpetuation: [[clergy|priests]] have a material interest in maintaining the folly of the laity, and theological systems are built to immunize folly against rational critique.

## Historical Context

Holbach's dissection of folly belongs to the Enlightenment's broader project of tracing the sources of human error. Where Francis Bacon had catalogued the "idols" that distort human [[knowledge|understanding]], and [[voltaire|Voltaire]] had satirized the follies of *l'infâme*, Holbach provides a systematic materialist account: folly is not a [[temperament|constitution]]al defect of the human mind but a product of specific social conditions — [[imposture|priestly deception]], political [[despotism|tyranny]], and the fear induced by ignorance of natural causes. Remove these conditions, and folly dissipates; it has no ontological root in human [[universe|nature]].

## Core Arguments

**The Inversion of [[reason|Reason]].** [[theology|Theology]]'s most pernicious strategy is to rebrand folly as [[philosophy|wisdom]]. Holbach quotes the theological maxim that "what is folly in the eyes of men, is [[philosophy|wisdom]] before [[god|God]], who is pleased to confound the [[philosophy|wisdom]] of the wise" (ch. 134). This rhetorical maneuver allows any [[absurdity|absurd]]ity — incar[[nations|nation]], transubstantiation, eternal [[hell|damnation]] — to be defended as a [[mystery|divine mystery]] beyond human comprehension. The cost is the destruction of the very faculty — reason — that theologians themselves claim is a gift from [[god|God]]. "You proclaim that your [[god|God]] is but the friend of unenlightened people," Holbach retorts, "and that He makes to sensible people a fatal gift" (ch. 134).

**Folly as Social Pathology.** Folly is not [[eve]]nly distributed. The powerful have an interest in the folly of the weak: "the interest of some men and the folly of others" (ch. 92) conspire to perpetuate religious delusion. [[priest|Priest]]s manufacture [[mysteries]] that only they can interpret; [[monarch|king]]s enforce orthodoxies that legitimize their rule. The common people, kept ignorant and fearful, are hostages to a system that benefits from their [[absurdity|irrationality]].

**The Historical Continuity of Savagery.** In a stri[[monarch|king]] anthropological argument, Holbach traces modern religious practices to primitive origins. "In all times the ferocity, the stupidity, the folly of savage men were shown in religious [[habit|custom]]s which were often cruel and extravagant" (ch. 121). Far from [[existence|being]] superseded by [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian civilization, these savage follies were intensified: "[[mode|Mode]]rn [[nation|nations]], at the instigation of their [[clergy|priests]], have [[eve]]n excelled the atrocious folly of the most barbarous [[nation|nations]]" — for savages at least did not persecute people for their opinions or "meddle in thought" (ch. 121).

**The Two Follies.** Holbach distinguishes two temperaments of religious folly, neither more rational than the other: "If the Theist sees [[god|God]] but on the beautiful side, the superstitious man looks upon Him from the most hideous side. The folly of the one is gay of the other is lugubrious; but both are equally delirious" (ch. 65). The cheerful deist and the terrified fanatic are brothers in error; their [[passion|emotion]]s differ, but their shared abandonment of reason is the same.

**Folly as Self-Inflicted [[pain|Misery]].** The ultimate indictment of religious folly is its practical uselessness. "Is there a more deplorable folly, and one that ought more to be abated, than that which, far from doing any good to the human race, does but blind it, cause transports, and render it miserable, depriving it of truth, which alone can soften the rigor of fate?" (ch. 205). A system that promises salvation but delivers only [[evil|suffering]] and intellectual darkness is not merely mistaken — it is actively harmful.

## Relation to Other Concepts

Folly is the epistemic twin of [[Superstition]] — where superstition provides the content ([[error|false belief]]s about supernatural agencies), folly provides the form (the irrational mode of holding those beliefs). Folly is [[worship|cult]]ivated by [[Priest]]s who profit from public ignorance. It is sustained by [[Fear]] of unknown natural causes and by the [[belief|credulity]] that [[Education]] alone can dispel. The antidote to folly is [[Reason]], which Holbach presents not as a cold abstraction but as the practical faculty of judging [[proof|evidence]] correctly and acting on that judgment.

## In the Text

On the inversion of folly into [[philosophy|wisdom]]:

> "You tell us, O theologians! that 'what is folly in the eyes of men, is wisdom before God, who is pleased to confound the wisdom of the wise.' But do you not pretend that human wisdom is a gift from Heaven? In telling us that this wisdom displeases God, is but folly in His eyes, and that He wishes to confound it, you proclaim that your God is but the friend of unenlightened people." (ch. 134)

On the folly of savage religion persisting in civilization:

> "In all times the ferocity, the stupidity, the folly of savage men were shown in religious customs which were often cruel and extravagant... Modern nations, at the instigation of their priests, have even excelled the atrocious folly of the most barbarous nations." (ch. 121)

On religion as the most deplorable folly:

> "Is there a more deplorable folly, and one that ought more to be abated, than that which, far from doing any good to the human race, does but blind it, cause transports, and render it miserable?" (ch. 205)
