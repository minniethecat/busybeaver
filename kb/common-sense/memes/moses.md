# Moses

## Who

Moses is the central figure of the Hebrew [[scripture|Bible]]'s foundational narrative: the infant rescued from the Nile, the shepherd called by a burning bush, the liberator who led the [[israel|Israelites]] out of [[egypt|Egypt]]ian bondage, the lawgiver who received the Ten Commandments on Mount [[sin|Sin]]ai, and the purported author of the Pentateuch (Genesis through Deuteronomy). For Judaism, [[christianity|Christianity]], and [[islam|Islam]] alike, Moses stands as the paradigm of the [[prophecy|prophet]] — a human intermediary through whom [[god|God]] reveals His will and establishes His covenant.

## What

Holbach's treatment of Moses is characteristically double-edged. On one level, he treats the Mosaic narrative as a historical claim subject to rational scrutiny: Are the [[eve]]nts described physically possible? Do they cohere internally? Are they corroborated by independent sources? On another level, he analyzes Moses as a *type* — the paradigmatic example of a "legislator" who claims [[divinity|divine]] sanction for a human legal code, establishing a [[model|pattern]] that subsequent religious founders would replicate.

## The Problem of Mosaic Authorship

Holbach inherits from earlier critics ([[baruch-spinoza|Spinoza]], [[hobbes|Hobbes]], Richard Simon) the observation that the Pentateuch cannot have been written by Moses himself. The text refers to Moses in the third person, describes his death and burial, and contains anachronisms that postdate his life by centuries. This is not a minor scholarly quibble: if the foundational document of Judeo-[[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian revelation is of uncertain authorship — if "Mosaic law" was not in fact given by Moses — the entire chain of [[revelation|divine revelation]] is called into question:

> "The laws of the invisible monarch need interpreters, but those who explain them are always quarreling among themselves about the true way of understanding them; more than this, they do not agree among themselves."
>
> (001-001)

## Miracles and Rational Scrutiny

The Mosaic narrative is dense with [[miracle|supernatural event]]s: the ten plagues, the parting of the Red Sea, water from the rock, manna from [[hope|heaven]]. Holbach applies a uniform [[model|standard]]: any narrative that violates the known regularities of [[universe|nature]] requires extraordinary [[proof|evidence]], and the Mosaic accounts offer none beyond the say-so of a text whose authorship and date are uncertain. He w[[ritual|rites]] that "the most important of our opinions are those which we have adopted without exami[[nations|nation]]; these are the most diffi[[worship|cult]] to be rooted out of our brains" (ch. II) — an observation that applies with particular force to miracle narratives absorbed in childhood.

## Moses as Political Archetype

Holbach's most original contribution is his analysis of Moses as a political operator. The claim of [[revelation|divine revelation]] serves a practical function: it places the law beyond criticism, [[vice|sin]]ce questioning the law becomes questioning [[god|God]]. This [[model|pattern]] — a human "legislator" invo[[monarch|king]] [[divinity|divine]] authority to immunize his commands against rational scrutiny — is, for Holbach, the template that subsequent religious founders have followed:

| Element | Mosaic Pattern | Subsequent Instances |
|---------|---------------|---------------------|
| [[theology-index|Divine]] encounter | Burning bush, [[sin|Sin]]ai | [[paul|Paul]]'s Damascus road, [[mohammed|Muhammad]]'s cave |
| Miraculous credentials | Plagues, Red Sea | Jesus' healings, [[mohammed|Muhammad]]'s night journey |
| Exclusive revelation | "No other gods before me" | "No salvation outside [[church|the Church]]" |
| Hereditary [[clergy|priesthood]] | [[matthew|Levi]]tes | Apostolic succession, imams |

## Historical Evidence

Archaeology and comparative ancient Near Eastern studies have complicated the traditional Mosaic narrative. No [[egypt|Egypt]]ian record mentions the Exodus or the plagues. The law codes of the Pentateuch show stri[[monarch|king]] parallels with earlier Mesopotamian legal collections (e.g., the Code of Hammurabi). The conquest of [[palestine|Canaan]] described in Joshua is not corroborated by archaeological strata. These findings do not, for Holbach's purposes, need to be definitive; it is sufficient that the burden of [[evidence|proof]] lies with those who claim the narrative is historical, and that burden has never been met.

## In the Text

Holbach's Apologue captures the structural problem of Mosaic (and all [[prophecy|prophetic]]) authority:

> "The people subjected to his power have only such ideas of the character and the laws of their invisible sovereign as his ministers give them."
>
> (001-001)
