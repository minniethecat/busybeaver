# Ascension of Jesus

## Overview

The Ascension of Jesus is the [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian doctrine that the risen [[jesus-christ|Christ]], forty days after his [[resurrection|Resurrection]], ascended bodily into [[hope|heaven]] in the presence of his apostles. The [[eve]]nt is recorded explicitly in [[gospel|the Gospel]] of [[luke|Luke]] (24:50–53) and the Acts of the Apostles (1:9–11), and is commemorated in the [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian liturgical calendar. Holbach's "Common Sense" (1772) subjects the Ascension to the same rational scrutiny he applies to the [[resurrection|Resurrection]], exposing contradictions among [[gospel|the Gospel]] writers and the inexplicable silence of [[matthew|Matthew]] and [[john-the-evangelist|John]].

## Historical Background

The Ascension narrative serves a crucial theological function: it marks the transition from [[jesus-christ|Christ]]'s earthly ministry to the era of [[church|the Church]], and it establishes [[jesus-christ|Christ]]'s [[hope|heaven]]ly reign at the right hand of the Father. Early [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian art and liturgy emphasized the Ascension as the glorification of [[jesus-christ|Christ]] and the guarantee of believers' own [[hope|heaven]]ly destiny. However, as with the resurrection accounts, [[gospel|the Gospel]] narratives of the Ascension present chronological and geographical discrepancies that have drawn critical attention [[guilt|sin]]ce antiquity.

## Key Figures and Developments

The Ascension accounts involve [[jesus-christ|Jesus of Nazareth]] and the eleven remaining apostles after Judas's betrayal. The [[gospel|Gospel]] of [[luke|Luke]] places the [[eve]]nt in Bethany, while the Acts of the Apostles (traditionally also attributed to [[luke|Luke]]) places it on the Mount of Olives. Most strikingly, [[gospel|the Gospel]]s of [[matthew|Matthew]] and [[john-the-evangelist|John]] do not mention the Ascension at all — an omission Holbach finds decisive. [[matthew|Matthew]]'s [[gospel|Gospel]] instead records Jesus's final words as a promise of perpetual earthly presence: "I am with you always, [[eve]]n unto the end of [[earth|the world]]" ([[matthew|Matthew]] 28:20).

## Impact on Religion and Thought

The Ascension solidified the [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian conception of [[hope|heaven]] as a physical space to which a physical body could travel, reinforcing the dualistic cosmology that Enlightenment materialists like Holbach rejected. The doctrine also underwrote the authority of [[clergy|the clergy]]: if [[jesus-christ|Christ]] had departed bodily from earth, then [[church|the Church]] hierarchy — the apostles and their successors — became his authorized representatives. The feast of the Ascension, observed forty days after Easter, became a major liturgical celebration in both Eastern and Western [[christianity|Christianity]].

## Connection to "Common Sense"

Holbach devotes a concentrated passage to the Ascension within his larger critique of [[gospel|Gospel]] contradictions. He notes that "neither [[matthew|Matthew]] nor John mentions at all this pretended ascension" — an omission he considers fatal to the claim's credibility. [[matthew|Matthew]], far from describing a departure, records Jesus as promising to "be and remain always with them until the end of [[earth|the world]]." Holbach presses the [[contradiction|inconsistency]]:

> "If all the apostles had really seen their Master gloriously rise to heaven, how could it be possible that Matthew and John, who would have seen it as well as the others, passed in silence such a glorious mystery, and which was so advantageous to their Master, considering that they relate many other circumstances of His life and of His actions which are much less important than this one?" (002-494).
Holbach further observes that [[luke|Luke]] contradicts himself on the timing: [[gospel|the Gospel]] places the Ascension on the day of the resurrection, while Acts places it forty days later. These discrepancies, Holbach argues, are not minor — they go to the core of the [[eve]]nt's historicity. A miracle witnessed by eleven men and recorded as the climax of [[jesus-christ|Christ]]'s earthly career could not, if genuine, have been omitted by half the [[missionaries|evangelists]] and narrated inconsistently by the other half. Holbach's conclusion is characteristically blunt: the Ascension is a "pretended" [[eve]]nt, a human invention serving theological purposes.

## Legacy

Holbach's critique of the Ascension exemplifies the Enlightenment method of internal critique: using the inconsistencies of [[scripture|sacred texts]] themselves to undermine their claims to [[divinity|divine]] authority. [[mode|Mode]]rn biblical scholarship has largely confirmed the textual discrepancies Holbach identified, though mainstream scholars attribute them to the diverse oral and literary traditions behind [[gospel|the Gospel]]s rather than to deliberate fabrication. The Ascension remains an article of [[religion|faith]] for [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ians and a target of rationalist [[doubt|skepticism]] in the tradition Holbach represents.
