# Body

## Definition

In Holbach's [[materialism|materialist philosophy]], the body is not merely the physical housing of an immaterial soul but the entirety of the human [[existence|being]]. "I do not see my soul," he w[[ritual|rites]]; "I know and feel but my body; it is my body which feels, which reflects, which judges, which suffers, and which enjoys, and all of its faculties are the necessary results of its own mechanism or of its organization" (100-002). This position — that all mental operations, including thought, judgment, and [[passion|emotion]], are functions of bodily organisation — places Holbach in the tradition of Epicurean and Lucretian [[matter|materialism]], radicalised through the lens of eighteenth-century physiology.

The [[doctrine|theological doctrine]] of the soul, Holbach argues, is not an expla[[nations|nation]] but an evasion. To claim that an "unknown force is the invisible principle of imperceptible movements" (ch. 102) is to explain nothing. Worse, the denigration of the body in favour of an imaginary soul serves the interests of [[clergy|priests]], who "needed souls and [[imagination|chimeras]] to populate the imaginary regions which they have discovered in the other life" (ch. 107). The affirmation of the body is thus simultaneously a philosophical argument for [[matter|materialism]] and a political argument against [[authority|clerical power]].

## Historical Context

The mind-body problem was at the centre of eighteenth-century philosophical debate. [[rene-descartes|Descartes]]'s radical dualism (res cogitans vs. res extensa) had provoked a series of materialist rejoinders, from La Mettrie's *L'homme machine* (1747) to Diderot's *Le Rêve de d'Alembert* (1769). Holbach's *Système de la [[universe|nature]]* (1770) had already developed a comprehensive materialist [[existence|ontology]]; *Common Sense* (1772) presents a popularised version of the same arguments, targeting the ordinary believer rather than the philosophical specialist.

The specific context for Holbach's emphasis on the body was the Catholic doctrine of the [[soul|immortal soul]], which served as the metaphysical foundation for the entire apparatus of eternal reward and punishment. If the soul dies with the body — if "man dies entirely" (102-004) — then the priest's power to threaten eternal [[hell|damnation]] or promise [[hope|heaven]]ly bliss collapses. Holbach's [[matter|materialism]] is thus not a disinterested metaphysical position but a strategic intervention in the political-theological order of the *ancien régime*.

## Core Arguments

1. **The soul-body union is impossible if they are essentially different.** "If the soul is a substance essentially different from the body, and which can have no affinity with it, their union would be, not a [[secrecy|mystery]], but a thing impossible (100-002)" (ch. 100). Two substances with nothing in common cannot interact — the very idea of union is incoherent.

2. **All mental functions depend on the body.** "Even admitting the existence of this soul, one can not refuse to recognize that it depends wholly on the body, and suffers conjointly with it all the vicissitudes which it experiences itself" (ch. 101). Sickness, ageing, intoxication, and injury all affect thought and feeling — [[proof|evidence]] that mind is a function of matter, not an independent substance.

3. **Man dies entirely.** "[[nothingness|Nothing]] is more evident to him who is not delirious. The human body, after death, is but a mass, incapable of producing any movements the union of which constitutes life" (ch. 102). The claim that the soul separates from the body at death is a conjecture without [[proof|evidence]], motivated by [[passion|desire]] rather than reason.

4. **[[desire|Desire]] for im[[death|mortality]] does not prove im[[death|mortality]].** "If you [[passion|desire]] anything very much, is it sufficient to conclude that this [[passion|desire]] will be fulfilled? By what strange logic do they decide that a thing can not fail to happen because they ardently [[passion|desire]] it to happen?" (ch. 101). The wish for eternal life is a human fantasy, not a metaphysical argument.

5. **The denigration of the body serves priestly power.** "Theologians, no doubt, have had reasons for ma[[monarch|king]] the soul immaterial. They needed souls and [[imagination|chimeras]] to populate the imaginary regions which they have discovered in the other life" (ch. 107). Material souls would be subject to dissolution like all bodies; immaterial souls can be eternally governed by [[clergy|the clergy]].

## Reception and Critique

Holbach's [[matter|materialism]] was among the most radical positions of the Enlightenment, and it drew fire from all quarters — Catholic, Protestant, and [[eve]]n deist. [[voltaire|Voltaire]], despite sharing Holbach's anti-clericalism, rejected materialist [[effect|determinism]] as morally dangerous. Rousseau, in the "Profession of [[faith|Faith]] of the Savoyard Vicar" (*Émile*, 1762), had already defended a sentimental argument for the immateriality and im[[death|mortality]] of the soul against precisely the sort of position Holbach would later articulate.

In the longer term, Holbach's identification of mind with brain function anticipated the trajectory of nineteenth- and twentieth-century neuro[[knowledge|science]], though the specific physiological mechanisms he could invoke were rudimentary. His political analysis of the soul-doctrine — that it serves priestly power rather than truth — has been taken up by subsequent anticlerical traditions and by some strands of Marxist critique of religion.

## Relation to Other Concepts

Body is the material substrate for [[Sensation]], [[vice|sin]]ce all sensory input is received through "the senses or of the material organs of the body" (ch. 101). It relates critically to [[Soul]], whose claimed immateriality and im[[death|mortality]] Holbach systematically dismantles. The concept connects to [[Mind]], which in Holbach's system is not a separate substance but a function of bodily organisation. It also relates to [[Death]], [[vice|sin]]ce the recognition that "man dies entirely" eliminates the theological apparatus of judgment, [[hope|heaven]], and [[punishment|hell]]. Finally, body is tied to [[Nature]], which operates through material causes and effects without supernatural intervention.
