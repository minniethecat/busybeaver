# Salvation

## Definition

Salvation, in the theological systems Holbach critiques, is the deliverance of the human soul from [[vice|sin]] and its eternal consequences through [[divinity|divine]] grace, [[religion|faith]], or sacramental observance. Holbach treats the concept not as a genuine solution to a real human problem but as the manufactured answer to an equally manufactured anxiety: first create the fear of [[Hell]], then offer salvation as the remedy. "We are constantly told of the [[utility|usefulness]] of the [[doctrine|dogma]] of life hereafter," he observes; the promise of salvation is held out as the ultimate incentive for moral conduct — yet the [[nation|nations]] most saturated with this promise are not [[demon|demons]]trably more virtuous than others (ch. 107).

## Historical Context

The doctrine of salvation has been central to [[christianity|Christianity]] from its origins, though the mechanism — [[religion|faith]] alone (Luther), [[religion|faith]] and works (Catholicism), predesti[[nations|nation]] (Calvin) — has been fiercely contested. [[reformation|The Reformation]]'s fragmentation of [[christianity|Christendom]] into competing salvation systems provided Holbach with devastating [[proof|evidence]]: if salvation were a matter of [[revelation|divine revelation]], why had [[god|God]] permitted such irreconcilable disagreement about its terms? [[enlightenment|The Enlightenment]]'s turn toward secular [[morality|ethics]] — the idea that morality could be grounded in human [[universe|nature]] rather than [[reward|divine reward]] — provided the intellectual framework for Holbach's critique. If [[morality|virtue]] is its own reward, the promise of salvation is not merely false but unnecessary.

## Core Arguments

**1. Salvation is a manufactured need.** Holbach argues that the religious concept of salvation follows a predictable commercial logic: [[clergy|priests]] first convince their audience that they are in mortal danger ([[vice|sin]], [[punishment|hell]]), then offer the exclusive remedy ([[religion|faith]], [[sacrament|sacraments]], obedience). "A [[missionaries|missionary]] seeks [[chance|fortune]] by the aid of his doctrine; he knows that if he has the good [[chance|fortune]] to retail his commodity, he will become the absolute master of those who accept him" (ch. 133).

**2. Disagreement about salvation exposes its human origin.** "Never have [[clergy|priests]], of [[eve]]n the same sect, come to an agreement among themselves about the manner of [[knowledge|understanding]] the wishes of a [[god|God]] who has truly revealed himself" (ch. 2). If salvation were a real [[divinity|divine]] offer, its terms would be clear and universally accessible — not an object of endless clerical dispute.

**3. The doctrine of salvation produces [[fear|terror]], not [[morality|virtue]].** Believers are told to "seek salvation with fear and trembling" (ch. 140). This fear-based motivation is antithetical to genuine moral development. A person who does good only to avoid punishment has not become virtuous — merely calculating.

**4. Salvation is politically instrumentalized.** "The arms and the will of the [[monarch|prince]]s alone decided upon the most useful doctrine for the salvation of the [[nation|nations]]" (ch. 139). Throughout history, the official doctrine of salvation has been determined not by theological reasoning but by [[politics|political power]]: whichever [[sect|faction]] controlled [[government|the state]] imposed its version of salvation on the population.

**5. Salvation distracts from earthly [[progress|improvement]].** By directing human attention toward an imagined [[death|afterlife]], the doctrine of salvation reduces the urgency of improving conditions in this life. "Instead of leading the people to salvation, [[clergy|priests]] have always led them to perdition" — by diverting attention from real social problems to imaginary [[soul|spirit]]ual ones.

## Reception and Critique

Holbach's reduction of salvation to a priestly [[certainty|confidence]] trick was characteristically extreme, but not without influence. Ludwig Feuerbach's *The [[essence|Essence]] of [[christianity|Christianity]]* (1841) developed a more systematic version of the argument: theological concepts are projections of human needs and wishes. Karl Marx extended the critique into a political call: the abolition of religion as "the illusory happiness of the people" is required for their "real happiness." More moderate Enlightenment figures — including Jefferson, who compiled his own rationalized [[testament|New Testament]] — sought to salvage the ethical [[doctrine|teaching]]s of Jesus while discarding the soteriological framework.

## Relation to Other Concepts

Salvation is the promised reward that gives [[Faith]] its urgency and [[Dogma]] its authority. It is the solution to a problem created by the doctrine of [[Sin]] and enforced by the fear of [[Hell]]. The promise of salvation serves the interests of [[Authority]] — particularly the [[Priest]] class, who alone claim to control access to it. Holbach contrasts salvation with [[Morality]], arguing that genuine [[morality|virtue]] requires no supernatural incentive, and with [[Happiness]], which should be sought in this life rather than deferred to an imaginary next one.
