# Festival

## Definition

In Holbach's usage, the festival serves as a metaphor for life itself. He compares human existence to "a festival, a conversation, or a visit" — something brief and temporary, but precisely for that reason something to be enjoyed agreeably and sociably. The festival represents the worldly pleasures that religion, "so sadly occupied with its gloomy reveries," urges people to renounce in favor of solitary preparation for an imagined afterlife. Holbach turns this metaphor against its religious usage: if life is as brief as a festival, the rational response is not to withdraw from it but to "act a befitting part in it, agreeable to himself and to others" (177-002).

The term appears within Holbach's broader refutation of the claim that the doctrine of an afterlife is necessary for morality. Religion represents the human being as "but a pilgrim upon earth" who "should travel alone, renounce the pleasures which he meets and deprive himself of the amusements which could console him." Against this ascetic vision, Holbach argues that our brief passage through life is itself reason to make it "easy" through mutual regard and shared enjoyment.

## Core Argument

**Life is brief like a festival — therefore enjoy it, do not renounce it.** Holbach's argument inverts the conventional religious logic. Religion says: life is short, therefore prepare for eternity by renouncing worldly pleasures. Holbach says: life is short, therefore make it pleasant for yourself and others while it lasts. "No matter how short the duration of a festival, of a conversation, or of a visit may be, does not each one of us wish to act a befitting part in it, agreeable to himself and to others? If life is but a passage, let us try to make it easy; it can not be so if we lack the regards of those who travel with us" (177-002). The brevity of life is a reason for sociability, not solitude.

This argument is part of Holbach's secular foundation for morality. We do not need "terrors and fables" (the threat of hell and the promise of heaven) to teach us how to behave. The natural human desire for the approbation and esteem of others — the wish to be agreeable companions in the shared festival of life — is a more reliable moral motivation than religious fear.
