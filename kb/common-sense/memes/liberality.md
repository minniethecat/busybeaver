# liberality

**Liberality** — divine generosity — is a concept Holbach treats with sustained irony. He recounts the story of a devout hermit who exclaims, "O Allah, how great are Thy gifts!" and praises "the benefactions which Thy liberality loads me with," before embarking on a seventh pilgrimage to Mecca. For Holbach, such gratitude is misplaced credulity that ignores the suffering of others. (002-173)

## The Dervis and the Irony of Divine Liberality

Holbach's parable of the dervis near Bagdad is one of the most artistically crafted passages in *Common Sense*. The holy man thanks God incessantly: "O Allah, how ineffable is Thy tenderness toward Thy servants. What have I done to deserve the benefactions which Thy liberality loads me with!" (002-173). He vows to undertake his seventh pilgrimage to Mecca, undeterred by an ongoing war. Passing safely through the war zone, enjoying nature's provisions, he continually praises "the rich and liberal hand of Providence, which is everywhere seen occupied with the welfare of the human race" (002-173).

The irony reaches its peak when the dervis ascends a mountain and sees a plain covered with more than a hundred thousand corpses — the aftermath of a battle. A wolf, gorging on human flesh, exclaims: "O Allah! how great is Thy kindness for the children of wolves! Thy foreseeing wisdom takes care to send infatuation upon these detestable men... these, our destroyers, murder each other, and thus furnish us with sumptuous repasts" (002-173). The wolf's gratitude for divine liberality mirrors the dervis's own — revealing that what each creature calls providence is simply the satisfaction of its own appetites, purchased at the expense of others.

## The Selective Vision of Gratitude

Holbach's parable exposes the selective nature of religious gratitude. The dervis sees divine liberality in the fruits, shade, and safe passage he personally enjoys, while remaining blind to the hundred thousand corpses on the other side of the mountain. Divine liberality, on this account, is simply the tendency of the fortunate to attribute their good luck to supernatural benevolence while ignoring the simultaneous suffering of others. The concept of divine liberality survives only so long as one refuses to look at the full picture.
