# Observation

## Definition

In Holbach's *Common Sense*, "observation" appears as a term in the vocabulary of Enlightenment empiricism, deployed in opposition to religious modes of knowing. Holbach groups "an astronomer's observations, a chemist's experiments, a geometer's calculations" as exemplary instances of rational inquiry — methods that produce reliable knowledge by interrogating nature rather than accepting revelation on authority (002-425). The concept is not developed at length in its own right but serves as a synecdoche for the empirical method as a whole.

## Observation, Experiment, and the Rejection of Authority

Holbach's invocation of observation is polemical rather than methodological. He does not elaborate a philosophy of science; he draws a bright line between two ways of knowing: the empirical (observing, testing, calculating) and the theological (believing, submitting, obeying). Observation produces knowledge that can be verified and shared; theology produces "incomprehensible things" that must be accepted on the word of priests. The astronomer who observes, the chemist who experiments, and the geometer who calculates all practice a form of intellectual self-governance that is the antithesis of religious submission.

## The Chain of Reason

The significance of observation in Holbach's thought extends beyond natural philosophy. "As an astronomer's observations, a chemist's experiments, a geometer's calculations, a physician's study of anatomy, serve to extend our knowledge, so also the study of man, of his nature, of his faculties, would serve to enlighten us concerning our duties" (002-425). Observation of nature leads to knowledge of nature; observation of humanity leads to knowledge of morality. In both cases, the method is the same — look, test, conclude — and the alternative is the same — believe, submit, obey.

## Observation Versus Revelation

Holbach contrasts the method of observation with the demand of religious faith in the starkest terms. Whereas observation requires the exercise of the senses and the critical evaluation of evidence, religion commands the opposite: "Sacrifice your reason; give up experience; distrust the testimony of your senses; submit without examination to all that is given to you as coming from Heaven" (002-265). The two approaches are not merely different but mutually exclusive — to observe is to question authority, and religious authority cannot survive genuine questioning. The "testimony of the senses" is the foundation of all knowledge that can be verified and shared; to distrust it is to abandon the only reliable guide to truth and to surrender the intellect to those who claim access to invisible realities.
