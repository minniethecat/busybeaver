# Devotion

## Definition

Devotion, in Holbach's usage, refers to intense religious commitment characterized by preoccupation with [[soul|spirit]]ual exercises, ritual observance, and [[passion|emotion]]al attachment to [[god|God]]. Far from treating devotion as a [[morality|virtue]], Holbach diagnoses it as a psychological pathology: "Devotion is a disease of the imagi[[nations|nation]], contracted in in[[imagination|fancy]]; the devotee is a hypochondriac, who increases his disease by the use of remedies."

This medicalized framing is characteristic of Enlightenment [[matter|materialism]]. Where traditional [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian thought praised devotion as the highest expression of love for [[god|God]], Holbach sees it as a mental illness — an obsessive-compulsive [[model|pattern]] maintained by the very rituals (prayer, confession, [[mass|Mass]]) that are supposed to cure [[soul|spirit]]ual distress.

## Historical Context

The concept of devotion underwent significant transformation in early modern [[europe|Europe]]. The Catholic [[reform|Reform]]ation had promoted new forms of devotional practice — the [[spirit|Spirit]]ual Exercises of Ignatius of Loyola, the [[worship|cult]] of the [[sacred|Sacred]] Heart, increased frequency of communion — as responses to Protestant critiques. By the eighteenth century, devotional literature was among the most widely printed genres in [[france|France]].

Enlightenment thinkers increasingly pathologized excessive devotion. Medical writers of the period discussed "religious melanc[[sacred|holy]]" and "enthusiasm" as conditions requiring treatment. Holbach's description of devotion as a "disease of the imagi[[nations|nation]]" draws on this emerging medical literature while radicalizing its implications: the cure is not reform of devotional practice but abandonment of religion altogether.

## Core Arguments

**1. Devotion is a psychological disease.** Holbach's central metaphor is medical: devotion is contracted like an infection in childhood and maintained by the very practices that claim to treat it. "The devotee is a hypochondriac, who increases his disease by the use of remedies" — prayer intensifies the anxiety it is supposed to reli[[eve]], because it reinforces the belief system that generates the anxiety.

**2. Devotion absorbs attention that should be directed elsewhere.** A "[[guilt|sin]]cerely religious [[monarch|sovereign]]" becomes a danger to [[government|the state]] because "devotion generally absorbs the attention which the [[monarch|prince]] ought to give to the ruling of his people." The same principle applies to all believers: the time, energy, and [[passion|emotion]]al resources consumed by devotional practice are diverted from real-[[universe|world]] responsibilities.

**3. Devotion often masks [[universe|world]]ly motivations.** Holbach offers a biting psychological observation: "A woman gives herself to [[god|God]] only when [[earth|the world]] no longer [[desire|want]]s her. Her vanity finds in religious devotion a role which occupies her and consoles her for the ruin of her charms." Devotion, in this analysis, is not pure love of [[god|God]] but displaced vanity — a socially acceptable role for those who have lost their place in secular society.

**4. Devotion demands "stupid" submission.** The [[god|God]] of traditional [[divinity|theism]] can be justified only "by conceding to this [[god|God]] the right of the strongest, that is to say, the violation of all rights, or in commanding from men a stupid devotion." Devotion requires the suspension of reason — the believer must accept that [[god|God]]'s justice is incomprehensible and that unquestioning obedience is the highest [[morality|virtue]].

## In the Text

The most concentrated discussion appears in the chapter on the psychological effects of [[belief|religious belief]]:

> "Devotion is a disease of the imagination, contracted in infancy; the devotee is a hypochondriac, who increases his disease by the use of remedies. They do not perceive that they are no better, or that they are just as sick as those who run after the charlatans of another stand."
>
> (002-215)

Holbach's remark about the "[[guilt|sin]]cerely religious [[monarch|sovereign]]" (ch. CLXX) ties devotion to political danger:

> "Credulity always indicates a narrow mind; devotion generally absorbs the attention which the prince ought to give to the ruling of his people. Docile to the suggestions of his priests, he becomes a tyrant."
>
> (002-308)

The diagnosis of feminine devotion as displaced vanity adds a sharp social-psychological dimension to the critique, anticipating later analyses of religion as compensation for [[universe|world]]ly frustrations.

## Relation to Other Concepts

Devotion is the internal, psychological face of [[piety]] (its external, behavioral expression). It stands in contrast to [[reason]], [[skepticism]], and [[atheism]]. The medical framing connects devotion to [[superstition]] and [[fanaticism]] as varieties of the same underlying pathology. The political critique links it to [[tyranny]] and [[priestcraft]].
