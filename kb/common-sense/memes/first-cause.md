# First Cause

The First Cause is a central concept in natural theology, designating God as the ultimate origin and initial mover of all existence. Holbach subjects this argument to sustained rational critique, arguing both that secondary natural causes render the hypothesis of a first cause superfluous and that religion itself, far from being a force for good, is "the first cause of the greatest disorders" in human society (002-391; 182-002; APP169-037).

## Definition and Theological Role

In classical theism, the First Cause argument holds that every effect must have a cause, and the chain of causation cannot regress infinitely — therefore there must exist an uncaused first cause, identified as God. Holbach challenges this reasoning by pointing to the observable operation of natural causes, which "weaken the action of the first cause or render it useless" (002-139; 082-001). If secondary causes are sufficient to explain natural phenomena, the hypothesis of a first cause becomes an unnecessary complication rather than an explanation.

## Natural Law and the Redundancy of a First Cause

Holbach's most powerful argument against the First Cause is empirical rather than metaphysical: the universe, as observed, operates through secondary causes that require no supernatural intervention. Natural laws — gravity, chemical affinity, biological reproduction — account for the regularities of experience without reference to a first mover. Holbach argues that these secondary causes "weaken the action of the first cause or render it useless" (002-139), and he repeats this claim across multiple contexts (082-001), underscoring its centrality to his system. If fire burns, water flows, and plants grow through their own inherent properties, then attributing these processes to an original divine impetus adds nothing to our understanding. The First Cause, in Holbach's materialist framework, is an explanatory placeholder — a name for ignorance rather than a solution to it. Once natural philosophy has identified the immediate causes of phenomena, the hypothesis of a remote supernatural cause becomes, in his view, not merely unnecessary but obstructive: it discourages further inquiry by offering a counterfeit explanation.

## Holbach's Critique

Holbach's most pointed rhetorical move is to invert the concept: rather than God being the first cause of order and goodness, he argues that religion itself "far from rendering men happier, is the first cause of the greatest disorders, and the greatest troubles with which mortals are afflicted" (182-002; APP169-037). The same logic that theologians use to posit a first cause of existence can be turned against them by observing that religious systems are the primary source of persecution, war, and social conflict. For Holbach, a true first cause would be found not in abstract metaphysics but in the observable material conditions and natural laws that govern the universe.

## Philosophical Implications

By rejecting the First Cause argument, Holbach undercuts the foundation of natural theology. If the universe requires no supernatural first cause — if matter in motion, operating according to necessary laws, is sufficient to account for all phenomena — then the idea of God becomes, in Holbach's terms, "an idea without a model, without prototype, without object" (002-007). The First Cause is thus not a solution to the problem of existence but a confession of ignorance dressed in metaphysical language.

Holbach identifies religion itself as the primary source of human misery:

> The rational man becomes incredulous, because he sees that religion, far from rendering men happier, is the first cause of the greatest disorders, and of the permanent calamities with which the human race is afflicted.
>
> (002-391)
