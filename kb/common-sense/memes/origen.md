# Origen of Alexandria

## Biography

Origen of Alexandria (c. 185 – c. 253 CE) was one of the most prolific and influential theologians of the early [[church|Church]]. Born in Alexandria, [[egypt|Egypt]], to a [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian family — his father Leonides was martyred — Origen became head of the Catechetical School of Alexandria at age 18. His major works include *De Principiis* (On First [[principle|Principle]]s), the first systematic [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian [[religion|theology]]; the *Hexapla*, a six-column parallel edition of the [[bible|Old Testament]]; and *Contra Celsum*, a defense of [[christianity|Christianity]] against the pagan philosopher Celsus. Origen's speculative [[religion|theology]], particularly his [[doctrine|teaching]] on the pre-existence of souls and universal salvation (*apokatastasis*), was posthumously condemned at the Second Council of Constantinople (553 CE).

## Role in Religious History

Origen was the pioneer of allegorical biblical interpretation in the [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian tradition. He distinguished three levels of scriptural meaning — literal, moral, and [[soul|spirit]]ual — and argued that many passages of the [[bible|Bible]], if taken literally, are [[absurdity|absurd]] or immoral, and therefore must be interpreted allegorically. This hermeneutic was enormously influential (through Ambrose, [[jerome|Jerome]], and Augustine) but also controversial: critics charged that allegory allowed interpreters to make [[scripture|Scripture]] mean whatever they [[desire|want]]ed, effectively replacing the text with the interpreter's philosophy.

## Connection to "Common Sense"

Holbach's [[guilt|sin]]gle reference to Origen comes in Chapter II, in a catalog of early [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian textual manipulation: "Origen himself, with all his great mind, corrupted the [[scripture|Scripture]]s and forged changes in the allegories which did not suit him, thus corrupting the sense of the [[prophecy|prophet]]s and apostles, and [[eve]]n some of the principal points of doctrine."

Holbach adds that Origen's own books "are now mutilated and falsified; they are but fragments collected by others who have appeared [[guilt|sin]]ce" (Chapter II) — a wry observation that the corrupter's works were themselves corrupted, perfectly illustrating the chain of textual instability that Holbach argues makes [[revelation|divine revelation]] impossible.

The Origen example serves Holbach's broader argument: if [[eve]]n [[church|the Church]]'s most brilliant and learned Father freely altered [[scripture|Scripture]] to fit his philosophical system, what [[certainty|confidence]] can anyone place in the biblical text as a reliable transmission of [[divinity|divine]] truth?
