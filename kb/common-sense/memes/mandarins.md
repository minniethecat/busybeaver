# mandarins

## Definition and Context

The **mandarins** were the scholar-officials of imperial China — a class of magistrates selected through rigorous examination and educated in the Confucian classics. In Holbach's *Common Sense*, they appear not as an ethnographic curiosity but as a political argument: the mandarins demonstrate that enlightened governance is possible without theological commitment. Holbach presents them as "undeceived long ago about the popular religion" — they know its claims are false, yet they do not persecute believers. Their example proves that a stable, prosperous society need not be founded on religious conviction (002-280)(139-005).

## The Chinese Model: Toleration Without Belief

Holbach's portrait of the mandarins serves a specific polemical purpose. He describes China as "a large country which is very flourishing, very productive, thickly populated, and governed by such wise laws, that the most savage conquerors adopted them with respect" (002-280). The mandarins "do not trouble themselves in regard to it, except to watch over it, that the bonzes or priests do not use this religion to disturb the peace of the State" (002-280). This is the model Holbach recommends: the governing class need not share the superstitions of the people, nor need they eradicate them; they need only ensure that priests do not weaponize those superstitions against public order.

## Superstition and the State

The Chinese example allows Holbach to articulate a crucial political principle: "Superstition is never to be feared except when it has the support of princes and soldiers; it is only then that it becomes cruel and sanguinary" (002-280). The mandarins demonstrate the alternative — a governing class that is personally skeptical but institutionally tolerant. The danger lies not in popular credulity but in the alliance between religious authority and state power. "Every sovereign who assumes the protection of a sect or of a religious faction, usually becomes the tyrant of other sects, and makes himself the most cruel perturbator in his kingdom" (002-280). The mandarins, by contrast, protect all sects from each other while believing in none of them.

## Holbach's Use of China as a Polemical Example

Holbach's invocation of China is strategic rather than scholarly. In the eighteenth century, the Chinese system of imperial examinations and its apparent separation of philosophical governance from popular religion fascinated Enlightenment thinkers. Holbach uses China to make an argument that would be dangerous to state directly against Christian Europe: that a society governed by reason rather than revelation is not only possible but demonstrably successful. "The Chinese enjoy blessings and a peace worthy of being envied by many nations which religion divides, ravages, and often destroys" (002-280). The mandarins are not merely an example; they are a reproach.

> Emperors and executioners for a long time sustained the Gods of Rome against the God of the Christians. In the eastern part of Asia, there is a large country which is very flourishing, very productive, thickly populated, and governed by such wise laws, that the most savage conquerors adopted them with respect. It is China! The mandarins or magistrates, undeceived long ago about the popular religion, do not trouble themselves in regard to it, except to watch over it, that the bonzes or priests do not use this religion to disturb the peace of the State.
>
> (002-280)
