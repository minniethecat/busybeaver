# Knowledge

## Definition

Holbach's epistemology is grounded in a radical empiricism: all knowledge derives from the senses, and the only legitimate objects of knowledge are [[phenomena|natural phenomena]] accessible to observation and reason. "It is only by showing them the truth that they can know their best interests and the real motives which will lead them to happiness" (ch. I). Knowledge, in this framework, is not contemplation of the [[divinity|divine]] but practical understanding of the natural and social [[universe|world]] — understanding that directly serves human [[happiness|well-being]].

The corollary is that theological "knowledge" — claims about an incomprehensible [[god|God]], about the [[death|afterlife]], about [[divinity|divine]] purposes — is not knowledge at all but a collection of "vain [[imagination|chimeras]]." "If [[god|God]] is incomprehensible to man, it would seem rational never to think of Him at all; but religion concludes that man is criminal if he ceases for a moment to revere Him" (005-002). The fundamental [[error|epistemological error]] of religion is to claim knowledge where none is possible, and to make this pseudo-knowledge the most important thing in human life.

Holbach calls for a reorientation of intellectual effort from [[hope|heaven]] to earth: "Long enough have the instructors of the people fixed their eyes on [[hope|heaven]]; let them at last bring them back to the earth. Tired of an incomprehensible [[religion|theology]], of ridiculous fables, of impenetrable [[mysteries]], of puerile ceremonies, let the human mind occupy itself with natural things, intelligible objects, sensible truths, and useful knowledge" (ch. I). The adjective "useful" is essential — knowledge is validated by its practical benefits, not by its conformity to [[doctrine|theological doctrine]].

## Historical Context

Holbach's empiricist epistemology draws on [[john-locke|Locke]], who argued that all ideas originate in sensation and reflection, and on Condillac, who radicalized [[john-locke|Locke]] by reducing reflection itself to transformed sensation. From this tradition Holbach inherited the conviction that "innate ideas" — including the idea of [[god|God]] — are fictions, and that the limits of knowledge are the limits of [[experience|sensory experience]].

[[enlightenment|The Enlightenment]] more broadly saw the acquisition and dissemi[[nations|nation]] of knowledge as a moral and political imperative. Diderot's *Encyclopédie* was explicitly designed to "change the common way of thin[[monarch|king]]" by ma[[monarch|king]] useful knowledge accessible. Holbach participated in this project as a contributor to the *Encyclopédie* and as the host of a salon where scientific and philosophical ideas circulated. His defense of knowledge in *Common Sense* is part of this broader campaign against ignorance — which he, like other *philosophes*, identified as the root of superstition and [[despotism|tyranny]].

## Core Arguments

**1. [[theology|Theology]] is not knowledge but ignorance institutionalized.** [[theology|Theology]] "has for its object only incomprehensible things" and "occupies itself but with things unseen" (ch. II). By directing human inquiry toward objects that can never be known, it prevents the acquisition of real knowledge about [[universe|nature]] and society.

**2. Useful knowledge is the remedy for human [[evil|suffering]].** "To annihilate religious [[prejudice|prejudices]], it would be sufficient to show that what is inconceivable to man can not be of any use to him" (ch. I). The [[model|standard]] of [[utility]] replaces the [[model|standard]] of doctrinal orthodoxy: if an idea cannot be translated into practical benefit, it is worthless.

**3. The senses are the only source of knowledge.** All ideas originate in [[experience|sensory experience]]; there are no innate ideas, no [[revelation|divine revelation]]s, no a priori truths accessible to pure reason alone. "Without motion I can neither feel, see, distinguish, compare, nor judge" (ch. XXVII) — [[eve]]n the act of thin[[monarch|king]] depends on physical processes.

**4. [[ignorance|Ignorance]] is not innocence but the cause of vice and [[evil|suffering]].** "Do we pretend to cure mortals of their vices if we do not begin by curing them of their [[prejudice|prejudices]]?" (ch. I). [[ignorance|Ignorance]] is not a passive state but an active cause of harm — it leaves people vulnerable to manipulation by [[clergy|priests]] and tyrants, and unable to identify their true interests.

## Relation to Other Concepts

Knowledge is the positive pole of Holbach's epistemological scheme, opposed to [[Ignorance]] which breeds [[Superstition]] and makes men vulnerable to [[Priest]] craft. Knowledge depends on [[Reason]] as its instrument and [[Experience]] as its source. The dissemi[[nations|nation]] of knowledge through [[Education]] is the precondition for [[Morality]] — people must know their true interests before they can act on them. [[Science]] is the systematic form of useful knowledge, while [[Theology]] is the systematic form of useless pseudo-knowledge.

## In the Text

On the need to redirect inquiry from [[hope|heaven]] to earth:

> "Long enough have the instructors of the people fixed their eyes on heaven; let them at last bring them back to the earth. Tired of an incomprehensible theology, of ridiculous fables, of impenetrable mysteries, of puerile ceremonies, let the human mind occupy itself with natural things, intelligible objects, sensible truths, and useful knowledge." (ch. I)

On the f[[utility]] of theological "knowledge":

> "Everybody tells us that the essence of God is incomprehensible to man; at the same time they do not hesitate to assign attributes to this incomprehensible God, and assure us that man can not dispense with a knowledge of this God so impossible to conceive of. The most important thing for men is that which is the most impossible for them to comprehend." (005-002)

On knowledge and human [[death|mortality]]:

> "Resign yourselves to the order of destiny, which decrees that you, like all other beings, should not endure forever. But what will become of me? you ask! What you were several millions of years ago. You were then, I do not know what; resign yourselves, then, to become again in an instant, I do not know what; what you were then; return peaceably to the universal home from which you came without your knowledge into your material form." (108-002)
