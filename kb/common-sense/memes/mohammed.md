# Mohammed

## Biography

Mohammed (c. 570–632 CE) was the founder of [[islam|Islam]]. Born in Mecca in the [[arabia|Arabian Peninsula]], he worked as a merchant before receiving, at the age of about forty, the first of a series of [[dreams|revelations]] that would be compiled after his death into the Quran — the sacred [[bible|scripture]] of [[islam|Islam]], which Muslims beli[[eve]] to be the literal word of [[god|God]] dictated to Mohammed through the angel Gabriel. His monotheistic preaching met with resistance in [[pagan|polytheist]]ic Mecca, and in 622 CE he migrated with his followers to Medina (the Hijra), an [[eve]]nt that marks the beginning of the [[islam|Islam]]ic calendar. In Medina, Mohammed became not only a religious leader but also a political and military one, and within a decade of his death his followers had conquered the [[arabia|Arabian Peninsula]] and begun expanding into the Byzantine and Persian empires.

## Role in Religious History

Mohammed is unique among the founders of major [[universe|world]] religions in having been, during his lifetime, both a [[prophecy|prophet]] and a head of state. [[islam|Islam]] presents itself not as a new religion but as the restoration of the original mono[[divinity|theism]] of [[abraham|Abraham]], corrupted by Jews (who rejected Jesus) and [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ians (who deified him). The Quran retells many biblical narratives — [[adam|Adam]], Noah, [[abraham|Abraham]], [[moses|Moses]], Jesus — with significant variations, and presents Mohammed as the "Seal of the Prophets," the final and definitive messenger of [[god|God]]. Within a century of Mohammed's death, the [[islam|Islam]]ic empire stretched from S[[suffering|pain]] to [[india|India]], and [[islam|Islam]] had become one of [[earth|the world]]'s great civilizational forces. The relationship between [[christianity|Christianity]] and [[islam|Islam]] has been marked by centuries of polemic, warfare, and occasional mutual influence — from [[crusades|the Crusades]] to the Ottoman conquest of Constantinople to the modern era of globalization and inter[[religion|faith]] dialogue.

## Connection to "Common Sense"

Holbach mentions Mohammed in three distinct contexts, each serving a different argumentative purpose, and together forming a multifaceted critique of [[revelation|revealed religion]] through the lens of comparative religious history.

**1. The self-refuting [[universe|nature]] of inter-religious polemic.** Holbach observes the [[absurdity|absurd]]ity of one [[revelation|revealed religion]] criticizing another when both rest on equally dubious foundations:

> "A Christian thinks that the Koran, the Divine revelation announced by Mohammed, is but a tissue of impertinent dreams and impostures injurious to Divinity. The Mohammedan, on his side, treats the Christian as an idolater and a dog; he sees but absurdities in his religion." (Chapter 64)

For Holbach, both parties are right about each other — and wrong about themselves. The [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian correctly identifies the Quran as a human production; the Muslim correctly identifies [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian doctrines like the [[trinity|Trinity]] and the [[incarnation|Incarnation]] as [[absurdity|absurd]]. But neither applies the same critical [[model|standard]] to their own tradition.

**2. [[religion|Religion]] spreads by the sword, not by the [[spirit|Spirit]].** Holbach treats Mohammed's military success as a case study in how religions actually spread — through conquest and [[politics|political power]], not through rational persuasion or [[divinity|divine]] intervention:

> "Mohammed and his successors, aided by Providence, or by their victorious arms, succeeded in a short time in expelling the Christian religion from a part of Asia, Africa, and even of Europe itself; the Gospel was compelled to surrender to the Koran." (Chapter 153)

The sarcastic reference to "[[providence|Providence]]" is pointed: if military victory is a sign of [[divinity|divine]] favor, then Mohammed's [[god|God]] defeated the [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian [[god|God]] across three continents. The more rational conclusion is that military victory is [[proof|evidence]] of military strength, not theological truth.

**3. [[chance|Chance]], not providence, determines religious affiliation.** Holbach recounts the story of the King of Macassar, who decided to adopt whichever religion's missionaries arrived first — and Mohammed's arrived before [[jesus-christ|Christ]]'s. The anecdote illustrates Holbach's deterministic argument about [[belief|religious belief]]: one's religion is an [[chance|accident]] of birth, geography, and historical circumstance, not a free choice guided by [[reason|reason]].

## Key Ideas Attributed / Critiqued

**1. Mohammed as a natural phenomenon.** Holbach does not demonize Mohammed — that would be the [[christianity|Christian]] approach — nor does he endorse him. He treats Mohammed as a natural historical phenomenon: a charismatic leader whose movement succeeded through the same mechanisms by which all religions succeed (political patronage, military force, the [[belief|credulity]] of the masses). The point is not that [[islam|Islam]] is worse than [[christianity|Christianity]]; it is that both are human inventions.

**2. The symmetry argument.** Holbach's deployment of Mohammed is part of a broader strategy: by setting [[islam|Islam]] and [[christianity|Christianity]] side by side, he forces the reader to notice that they are structurally identical. Each has a revealed book, a founding [[prophecy|prophet]], a set of [[miracle|miracles]], a claim to exclusive truth, and a history of expansion through violence. The [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian who dismisses Mohammed's [[dreams|revelations]] as imposture must explain why the same judgment does not apply to [[moses|Moses]] and Jesus.

**3. The political determi[[nations|nation]] of religion.** The principle illustrated by Mohammed's success is that "the true religion is but the religion of the [[monarch|prince]]; the true [[god|God]] is the [[god|God]] whom the [[monarch|prince]] wishes them to worship" (Chapter 153). This applies equally to [[christianity|Christianity]] under Constantine, to [[islam|Islam]] under the Caliphs, and to any state-sponsored religion in any era.

## Legacy

Mohammed and [[islam|Islam]] have been a persistent presence in [[europe|Europe]]an thought [[guilt|sin]]ce the Middle Ages — often as a polemical foil, occasionally as an object of genuine study. [[enlightenment|The Enlightenment]] saw a shift toward comparative and critical treatments: [[pierre-bayle|Pierre Bayle]] wrote sympathetically about [[islam|Islam]]ic [[toleration|tolerance]], [[voltaire|Voltaire]] used [[islam|Islam]] to satirize [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian orthodoxy, and Edward Gibbon's history of the [[roman-empire|Roman Empire]] devoted careful attention to the rise of [[islam|Islam]]. Holbach's treatment belongs to this tradition but is characteristically more radical: he is not interested in comparing which [[revelation|revealed religion]] is better, because he rejects the category of [[revelation|revealed religion]] altogether.

In the modern era, Mohammed remains a figure of immense significance for over a billion Muslims [[universe|world]]wide. For non-Muslim scholars, the historical-critical study of early [[islam|Islam]] — the dating of Quranic manuscripts, the reconstruction of the Prophet's biography from non-[[islam|Islam]]ic sources, the analysis of [[islam|Islam]]'s emergence from its late-antique Near Eastern context — has become a flourishing field, paralleling the historical-critical study of early [[christianity|Christianity]] that Holbach's generation pioneered.
