# Transubstantiation

## Definition

Transubstantiation is the Catholic doctrine, defined by the Fourth Lateran Council (1215) and reaffirmed at Trent, that during the consecration of the [[mass]], the substance of bread and wine is converted into the literal body and blood of [[jesus-christ]] while the [[chance|accident]]s (appearance, taste, texture) remain unchanged. Holbach seizes upon this doctrine as the ultimate [[proof|demonstration]] of religious [[absurdity|absurd]]ity — a belief that violates [[eve]]ry [[evidence|testimony of the senses]], that contradicts the most basic principles of [[reason]], and that is maintained only by the threat of eternal [[damnation]] for those who doubt.

## Comparative Ridicule

Holbach's treatment of transubstantiation employs a comparative strategy: he notes that different [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian [[sect|denomination]]s regard one another's core doctrines as [[absurdity|absurd]]. "The Protestant... ridicules the Catholic [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian because the latter believes in the [[secrecy|mystery]] of the transubstantiation. He treats him as a fool, as ungodly and idolatrous, because he kneels to worship the bread in which he believes he sees the [[god|God]] of [[nature|the universe]]" (Ch. 56). But the Catholic returns the favor, treating the Protestant as a [[heresy|heretic]] for rejecting what [[church|the Church]] has infallibly declared. Holbach's point is that all these mutual accusations are correct: each sect correctly identifies the [[absurdity|absurd]]ity in [[eve]]ry other sect's beliefs, but is blinded by [[habit|custom]] to the equal [[absurdity|absurd]]ity of its own. The rational observer, positioned outside all [[sect|sects]], recognizes that transubstantiation, the [[trinity]], and [[predestination]] are equally products of human imagi[[nations|nation]].

## The Psychology of Miraculous Belief

Transubstantiation serves Holbach as a case study in how intelligent people can be brought to beli[[eve]] manifest impossibilities. The mechanism is familiar: early childhood indoctri[[nations|nation]], social reinforcement, the threat of supernatural punishment for doubt, and the gradual habituation of the mind to propositions that would strike any unindoctrinated person as transparently false. "All the [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian [[sect|denomination]]s," Holbach observes, "treat each other as impious madmen" (Ch. 56), yet each maintains its own mad beliefs with perfect [[guilt|sin]]cerity. The doctrine of transubstantiation — that a piece of bread is simultaneously bread and the body of [[god|the creator]] of [[nature|the universe]] — [[demon|demons]]trates that there is no belief too [[absurdity|absurd]] to be accepted when backed by the combined force of [[priest]]ly authority, childhood education, and the fear of [[punishment|hell]].

## Pagan Parallels

Holbach places transubstantiation within a broader history of ritual cannibalism and theophagy (god-eating) across [[worship|cult]]ures. [[pagan|Pagan]] religions had their own sacred meals, their own beliefs in the real presence of the [[divinity|divine]] in consecrated objects, their own [[clergy|priests]] who claimed to transform ordinary matter into [[divinity|divine]] substance. The [[mass|Catholic Mass]], Holbach suggests, is simply the most developed and theologically elaborate version of a universal human tendency to beli[[eve]] that the sacred can be ingested. This comparative perspective — placing [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian doctrine alongside [[paganism|pagan]] practice — is Holbach's [[model|standard]] method for dissolving the aura of uniqueness that protects [[belief|religious belief]]s from rational scrutiny.

## Political Function

Beyond its theological [[absurdity|absurd]]ity, transubstantiation serves a political function in Holbach's analysis. The doctrine places the [[laity]] in absolute dependence on the [[clergy]]: only an ordained priest can perform the consecration that transforms bread into [[god|God]]. The [[mass|Mass]] is thus not merely a ritual but an instrument of priestly power — the moment at which the ordinary believer must rely on the professional religious class for access to the [[divinity|divine]]. The claim that a priest can, through words and gestures, create the body of [[god|God]] is the ultimate expression of sacerdotal pretension, and Holbach treats it as such: a power-claim disguised as a [[secrecy|mystery]].

## In the Text

On the mutual ridicule of opposing [[sect|denomination]]s, with transubstantiation as the paradigmatic example:

> "The Protestant Christian, who without scruple worships a man, and who believes firmly in the inconceivable mystery of the Trinity, ridicules the Catholic Christian because the latter believes in the mystery of the transubstantiation. He treats him as a fool, as ungodly and idolatrous, because he kneels to worship the bread in which he believes he sees the God of the universe." (Ch. 116)

On the universality of sacred meals and ritual transformation across [[worship|cult]]ures:

> "The Indians believe that their pagods change the water with which they wash them into some mysterious liquor; they say that they are nourished by the rice which they present them; the form of bread and wine, the same as our Roman Christ-worshipers say; and they imagine that their gods give them the faculty of predicting the future." (Ch. 206, endnote)

On the mutual condem[[nations|nation]] of [[sect|sects]] as "impious madmen" despite each maintaining equally [[absurdity|absurd]] beliefs:

> "All the Christian denominations agree in considering as folly the incarnation of the God of the Indies, Vishnu. They contend that the only true incarnation is that of Jesus, Son of the God of the universe and of the wife of a carpenter." (Ch. 116)

## Reception and Critique

Holbach's treatment of transubstantiation as the paradigmatic case of religious [[belief|credulity]] exemplifies the Enlightenment's strategy of using one religion's critiques of another to undermine all. This method — letting Protestants and Catholics refute each other while the philosopher stands above the fray — was perfected by [[pierre-bayle|Bayle]] in his *Historical and Critical Dictionary* and adopted by [[voltaire|Voltaire]] in his *Philosophical Dictionary*. Holbach's innovation is to apply it not just to particular doctrines but to the very concept of [[secrecy|mystery]]: once one [[secrecy|mystery]] is admitted, all others follow, because the epistemological [[model|standard]] that would exclude them has already been surrendered. This is a version of the "slippery slope" argument that has been deployed by rationalists from [[david-hume|Hume]] to the present day. Catholic apologists have responded by distinguishing between [[mysteries]] that are "above reason" (but not contrary to it) and [[absurdity|absurd]]ities that are "contrary to reason" — but Holbach would reply that this distinction has no non-arbitrary basis, [[guilt|sin]]ce the determi[[nations|nation]] of which [[mysteries]] are above rather than contrary to reason is itself a theological judgment made by the very institution whose claims are in question.
