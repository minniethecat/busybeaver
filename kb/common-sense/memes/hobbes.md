# Thomas Hobbes

## Biography

Thomas Hobbes (1588–1679) was an English philosopher, best known for his political treatise *[[matthew|Levi]]athan* (1651) and for developing one of the earliest systematic materialist philosophies of the modern era. Born prematurely in Westport, Wiltshire — his mother, he said, "gave birth to twins: myself and fear" at news of the Spanish Armada — Hobbes studied at Oxford and served as tutor and secretary to the Cavendish family, which gave him access to the intellectual circles of England and continental [[europe|Europe]]. His philosophy was comprehensively naturalistic: he held that all reality consists of matter in motion, that the human mind is a material phenomenon, and that [[government|political authority]] derives from a social contract, not from [[divine-right|divine right]].

## Role in Religious History

Hobbes was one of the most notorious religious skeptics of the seventeenth century. Though *[[matthew|Levi]]athan* devotes half its length to theological matters and Hobbes professed conformity to [[church|the Church]] of England, his contemporaries widely suspected him of [[atheist|atheism]]. His materialist [[existence|ontology]] left no room for an immaterial soul or a transcendent [[god|God]] who intervenes in [[universe|nature]]. His biblical criticism — arguing that [[moses|Moses]] did not write the Pentateuch and that scriptural authority depends on [[monarch|sovereign]] decree — anticipated the radical biblical scholarship of [[baruch-spinoza|Spinoza]] and the Enlightenment. Parliament investigated him for blasphemy in 1666, and a bill was proposed to burn him as a [[heresy|heretic]].

## Connection to "Common Sense"

Holbach's *Common Sense* invokes Hobbes twice, both times with approval. In the opening pages, Holbach quotes Hobbes's definition of [[religion|theology]] as "the [[monarch|king]]dom of darkness" — a domain where "light is but darkness, [[proof|evidence]] becomes doubtful or false, the impossible becomes credible, reason is an unfaithful guide, and common sense changed into delirium" (Chapter II, PN 901). This Hobbesian characterization perfectly captures Holbach's own framing of [[religion|theology]] as the systematic inversion of reason. In the concluding appendix, Holbach names Hobbes among the modern philosophers who "followed the path of [[epicurus|Epicurus]]" — the materialist tradition that "dared to tear away the thick veil of [[opinion|prejudice]], and to deliver philosophy from theological fetters" — though he adds sorrowfully that this doctrine "found but few votaries in a [[universe|world]] still too much infatuated with fables to listen to reason" (Appendix, PN 6991).

Holbach opens *Common Sense* with Hobbes's memorable characterization of theology:

> There is a science which has for its object only incomprehensible things. Unlike all others, it occupies itself but with things unseen. Hobbes calls it "the kingdom of darkness." In this land all obey laws opposed to those which men acknowledge in the world they inhabit. In this marvelous region light is but darkness, evidence becomes doubtful or false, the impossible becomes credible, reason is an unfaithful guide, and common sense changed into delirium. This science is named Theology, and this Theology is a continual insult to human reason.
>
