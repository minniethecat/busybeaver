# Suffering

## Definition

Suffering, in Holbach's "Common Sense," is the observable reality of human pain and distress — physical, psychological, and social. It functions in the text less as a concept to be defined than as a fact to be confronted: undeniable, universal, and incompatible with the theological claim that [[earth|the world]] is governed by an infinitely good and powerful [[god|God]]. "Are not his pleasures mingled with [[evil|suffering]]s? Are there many people who are contented with their fate?" (ch. II). The rhetorical questions are designed to force the reader toward an uncomfortable conclusion that [[religion|theology]] systematically evades.

## Historical Context

The problem of [[injustice|evil]] — reconciling the existence of [[evil|suffering]] with the [[attribute|attributes]] of an omnipotent, omniscient, and benevolent [[god|God]] — was one of the oldest and most vexing challenges to [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian [[divinity|theism]]. Augustine's free-will defense and [[gottfried-leibniz|Leibniz]]'s [[injustice|theodicy]] (the "best of all possible [[universe|world]]s") had provided sophisticated answers, but the [[lisbon|Lisbon]] earthquake of 1755 shattered Enlightenment optimism. [[voltaire|Voltaire]]'s *Poem on the [[lisbon|Lisbon]] Disaster* (1756) and *Candide* (1759) ridiculed philosophical optimism as callous and empirically indefensible. Holbach, writing after [[voltaire|Voltaire]], uses the raw fact of [[evil|suffering]] not to reform [[injustice|theodicy]] but to demolish its premise: the [[god|God]] who permits such [[evil|suffering]] either does not exist or does not merit worship.

## Core Arguments

**1. Suffering is universal and undeniable.** "In [[earth|the world]] where we are, we see all sentient [[existence|being]]s living and [[evil|suffering]] in the midst of dangers. Man can not step without wounding, [[torture|torment]]ing, crushing other [[existence|being]]s." (ch. II). The universality of [[evil|suffering]] is, for Holbach, an empirical claim — not a theological abstraction but an observable feature of existence.

**2. Suffering refutes [[divinity|divine]] [[goodness|benevolence]].** The argument takes the form of a dilemma: if [[god|God]] is all-powerful, He could prevent [[evil|suffering]]; if He is all-good, He would [[desire|want]] to. [[sin|Sin]]ce [[evil|suffering]] exists, [[god|God]] cannot be both. "Man after all very happy? Are his enjoyments durable? Are not his pleasures mingled with [[evil|suffering]]s?" (ch. II). The answer is assumed to be self-evident.

**3. Theological expla[[nation|nations]] of [[evil|suffering]] are evasions.** When theologians claim that [[evil|suffering]] is a "trial" or a "test," Holbach sees only intellectual dishonesty: "They silence us by saying that [[god|God]] would not impart to His creatures either the indifference to the [[evil|suffering]]s of others, or [[infinite|the infinite]] happiness which He reserved for Himself alone." (ch. II). This "silencing" is the core injustice — [[religion|theology]] deprives sufferers of the right to protest.

**4. The [[god|God]] of [[evil|suffering]] resembles a tyrant, not a father.** Drawing a political analogy characteristic of his thought, Holbach observes: "According to theological ideas, [[god|God]] resembles a tyrant who, suspicious and cruel, avaricious of His benefactions and prodigal of His scourges." (ch. II). The [[evil|suffering]]-inflicting [[god|God]] is not morally distinguishable from the [[despotism|despot]]s Holbach opposes.

## Reception and Critique

Holbach's use of [[evil|suffering]] as an argument against [[divinity|theism]] was anticipated by [[epicurus|Epicurus]] and forcefully restated by Schopenhauer, who made [[evil|suffering]] the central fact of existence in his pessimistic philosophy. Dostoevsky's Ivan Karamazov would later develop a more [[passion|emotion]]ally potent version of the argument from [[evil|suffering]] — not the quantity of pain but the [[evil|suffering]] of innocent children. Contemporary philosophers of religion (Plantinga, Swinburne) have refined free-will and soul-ma[[monarch|king]] theodicies, but Holbach's basic challenge — that the observable facts of [[evil|suffering]] are inconsistent with the claims of benevolent [[divinity|theism]] — remains a central tension in philosophy of religion.

## Relation to Other Concepts

Suffering is the experiential datum that undermines [[Goodness]] as a [[divinity|divine]] attribute, and with it the coherence of [[God]] as traditionally conceived. It is linked to [[Evil]] — [[evil|suffering]] is the subjective experience of [[injustice|evil]] — and to [[Nature]], whose indifference to human welfare Holbach presents as a fact to be accepted rather than a problem to be solved. The existence of [[evil|suffering]] makes [[Happiness]] precarious and underscores the urgency of earthly reform: if this life is all we have, its misery must be addressed here and now, not deferred to an imagined [[death|afterlife]].

## In the Text

On the universality of [[evil|suffering]]:

> "Are not his pleasures mingled with sufferings? Are there many people who are contented with their fate? Is not mankind the continual victim of physical and moral evils?" (ch. II)

On [[evil|suffering]] as a refutation of [[goodness|divine goodness]]:

> "Experience contradicts this sublime revery. In the world where we are, we see all sentient beings living and suffering in the midst of dangers. Man can not step without wounding, tormenting, crushing other beings." (ch. II)

On the theological evasion of [[evil|suffering]]:

> "They silence us by saying that God would not impart to His creatures either the indifference to the sufferings of others, or the infinite happiness which He reserved for Himself alone. How can we be moved by the goodness of a God who is infinitely happy while His creatures are in misery?" (ch. II)

On the political analogy between [[god|God]] and tyrant:

> "According to theological ideas, God resembles a tyrant who, suspicious and cruel... is the most unjust tyranny." (ch. II)
