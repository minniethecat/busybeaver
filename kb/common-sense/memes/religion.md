# Religion

## Definition

Religion, in the sense critiqued by Holbach, is an organized system of doctrines, rituals, and moral prescriptions grounded in the alleged revelation of a [[God|deity]] and administered by a professional [[Clergy|priestly class]]. Holbach distinguishes sharply between the claims religion makes for itself — that it transmits [[divinity|divine]] truth, promotes morality, and offers salvation — and the observable effects it produces in history and society: ignorance, conflict, exploitation, and a systematic crippling of [[reason|human reason]].

## The Psychological Origin

Holbach locates the origin of religion not in [[revelation|divine revelation]] but in universal features of human psychology. Early humans encountered a [[universe|world]] of forces they could not understand or control: storms, disease, death. "Frightened and led astray by ignorance, by fear, by calamities" (ch. II), they personified these forces as willful agents and attempted to propitiate them:

> "Man is every moment forced to recognize that he does not know the natural causes of the effects which he experiences; the uniformity with which he sees these effects happen appears to him to be the work of a supernatural cause." (ch. II)

Religion, on this account, is a primitive explanatory framework — a "[[knowledge|science]] of the ignorant" that fills gaps in [[knowledge|understanding]] with imaginary agents. It persists not because it is true but because it exploits enduring human vulnerabilities: fear of death, [[passion|desire]] for meaning, and the need for cosmic justice.

## Religion as a System of Power

The most distinctive feature of Holbach's critique is its political dimension. Religion is not merely false; it functions as an instrument of domi[[nations|nation]]. The Apologue (ch. I) makes this explicit through allegory: the invisible monarch's "ministers" — the [[clergy|priests]] — control access to him, interpret his will, and profit from the resulting confusion:

> "The people subjected to his power have only such ideas of the character and the laws of their invisible sovereign as his ministers give them; these suit, however, because they themselves have no idea of their master, for his ways are impenetrable."
>
> (001-001)

The structure of [[authority|religious authority]] depends on maintaining a monopoly over the interpretation of the inaccessible. If [[god|God]] were directly accessible to [[eve]]ry believer, [[clergy|priests]] would be unnecessary. The incomprehensibility of [[god|God]] is thus not a doctrinal [[chance|accident]] but a structural requirement of [[authority|clerical power]].

## Effects on Morality and Society

Holbach challenges the claim that religion is necessary for morality. He argues instead that religious morality is systematically distorted:

| Claim | Holbach's Counter-Argument |
|-------|---------------------------|
| Religion promotes [[morality|virtue]] | Religious morality is arbitrary, tied to ritual compliance rather than genuine ethical reasoning |
| Religion deters crime | [[fear|Fear]] of invisible punishment is less effective than visible legal sanctions; religion often excuses crimes (crusades, [[cruelty|persecution]]s) |
| Religion provides [[hope|consolation]] | The [[hope|consolation]]s are illusory — "Is not the idea of total [[death|annihilation]] infinitely preferable to the idea of an eternal existence accompanied with [[evil|suffering]] and gnashing of teeth?" (ch. CVIII) |
| Religion is [[divinity|divine]]ly revealed | Every religion claims exclusive revelation; they cannot all be true, and their mutual contradictions suggest none is |

## Historical Record

Holbach surveys the historical record of religion and finds it to be a chronicle of "quarrels and wars, of massacres and [[cruelty|persecution]]s" (ch. II). The ministers of the same [[god|God]] "discredit and treat each other as [[charlatan|impostor]]s and liars; the decrees and ordinances which they promulgate are obscure; they are enigmas, made not to be understood" (ch. I). Religious diversity is not, for Holbach, a sign of [[soul|spirit]]ual richness but [[proof|evidence]] of the arbitrary, [[worship|cult]]urally contingent [[universe|nature]] of all religions:

> "All which they relate of their hidden prince is but a tissue of contradictions, scarcely a single word that is not contradicted at once."
>
> (001-001)

## Relation to Other Concepts

[[Religion]] is the institutional and [[worship|cult]]ural framework built upon belief in [[God]]. It is the primary target of [[Atheism]]'s critique and the social matrix within which concepts like the [[Soul]], [[Free Will]], and [[divinity|divine]] judgment acquire practical force. Holbach's [[Materialism]] provides the positive philosophical alternative: a [[universe|world]] understood through [[universe|nature]] and reason rather than revelation and authority.

## In the Text

Holbach's Apologue (ch. I) is a sustained allegorical critique of religion as a system of arbitrary, self-serving mediation between humans and the [[divinity|divine]]. On the origins and persistence of [[belief|religious belief]]:

> "The most important of our opinions are those which we have adopted without examination; these are the most difficult to be rooted out of our brains, and these are precisely those which govern the rest of men."
>
> (002-015)
