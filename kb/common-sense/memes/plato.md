# Plato

## Biography

Plato (c. 428–348 BCE) was an Athenian philosopher, a student of [[socrates|Socrates]] and the teacher of Aristotle. Born into an aristocratic Athenian family, he was deeply affected by the execution of [[socrates|Socrates]] in 399 BCE, an [[eve]]nt that shaped his lifelong concern with the relationship between philosophy and politics. After [[socrates|Socrates]]'s death, Plato traveled widely — to Megara, Cyrene, [[egypt|Egypt]], and Sicily — before returning to Athens around 387 BCE to found the Academy, the first institution of higher [[education|learning]] in the Western [[universe|world]], which survived for nearly nine centuries until its closure by the Emperor Justinian in 529 CE.

Plato's written corpus — some thirty-five dialogues and thirteen letters — is the most complete philosophical oeuvre to survive from classical antiquity. In dialogues such as the *Republic*, *Phaedo*, *Symposium*, *Timaeus*, and *Theaetetus*, Plato developed a comprehensive philosophical system encompassing metaphysics (the Theory of Forms), epistemology (knowledge as recollection), [[morality|ethics]] (the Form of the Good), political philosophy (the philosopher-[[monarch|king]]), and cosmology (the [[divinity|divine]] Craftsman or Demiurge). His influence on the subsequent Western tradition is second only to Aristotle's, and his thought was absorbed into [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian [[religion|theology]] through the work of Augustine and the medieval scholastics, who found in Platonic [[model|ideal]]ism a philosophical language for articulating the distinction between the material and the [[soul|spirit]]ual, the temporal and the eternal.

## Role in Religious History

Plato occupies a paradoxical position in the history of religion. He was not a religious founder, but his metaphysical framework — the distinction between a [[universe|world]] of becoming (the material universe, transient and imperfect) and a [[universe|world]] of [[existence|being]] (the Forms, eternal and perfect) — provided the philosophical architecture for [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian [[religion|theology]]'s denigration of the body, the [[earth|material world]], and earthly life in favor of an immaterial soul and a transcendent [[god|God]]. Nietzsche would later famously describe [[christianity|Christianity]] as "Platonism for the people." The Platonic depreciation of the senses as a source of knowledge, the elevation of the invisible over the visible, and the postulation of a realm of eternal truth accessible only to purified reason — all of this was absorbed into [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian thought and became, for the Enlightenment, a primary target of critique.

At the same time, Plato's intellectual seriousness, his insistence that the unexamined life is not worth living, and his willingness to subject conventional opinion to rational scrutiny made him a more ambiguous figure for the radical Enlightenment than, say, a priest or a theologian. He was an enemy in metaphysics but, in some respects, an ally in method.

## Connection to "Common Sense"

Holbach mentions Plato twice, both times in contexts that align him with the [[model|ideal]]ist and supernaturalist camp against the materialist tradition:

**1. The eclipse of [[matter|materialism]].** In the passage celebrating [[democritus|Democritus]] and [[epicurus|Epicurus]], Holbach w[[ritual|rites]]:

> "But their systems, too simple, too sensible, and too stripped of wonders for the lovers of fancy, were obliged to surrender to the fabulous conjectures of Plato, Socrates, and Zeno." (Chapter 204, Continuation)

The judgment is clear. Plato's "fabulous conjectures" — his Forms, his Demiurge, his myths of the [[death|afterlife]], his [[model|ideal]] Republic — are not philosophy but fantasy dressed as philosophy. They satisfy the taste for the marvelous that [[superstition|superstition]] [[worship|cult]]ivates, and for that very reason they triumphed over the austere, unglamorous truth of atomism.

**2. Imitating the gods.** In another passage, Holbach quotes the Platonic maxim — transmitted through [[cicero|Cicero]] — that "[[morality|virtue]] consisted in imitating the gods" (Chapter 183). For Holbach, this is the very worst of ancient [[morality|moral philosophy]]: it ties [[morality|ethics]] to [[religion|theology]], makes human conduct dependent on an imagined [[divinity|divine]] [[model|exemplar]], and distracts from the true foundation of morality, which lies in human [[nature|nature]], human needs, and human society. A morality grounded in [[universe|nature]] teaches us to live well in this [[universe|world]]; a morality grounded in "imitating the gods" teaches us to despise this [[universe|world]] in favor of an imaginary one.

## Key Ideas Attributed / Critiqued

**1. The primacy of the invisible.** Holbach's deepest objection to Plato is metaphysical. The Platonic doctrine that true reality lies in an invisible realm of Forms — and that the visible [[universe|world]] is a mere shadow — inverts the proper relationship between thought and experience. For Holbach, as for all materialists, experience is the source of knowledge, and the [[earth|material world]] is the only [[universe|world]] there is. Plato's philosophy is an escape from reality, not an account of it.

**2. The "fabulous conjectures" charge.** Holbach's epithet is not merely dismissive; it is diagnostic. "Fabulous" means "of the [[universe|nature]] of a fable" — a story, a myth, a fiction. Plato's dialogues are filled with myths (the cave, the chariot of the soul, Er's vision of the [[death|afterlife]]), and Holbach's point is that the myths are not decoration; they are the substance. Strip away the dialectical artistry, and what remains is a system as mythical as any religion.

**3. The ethical consequences.** The morality of "imitating the gods" presupposes gods worth imitating. But if the gods are fictions, then imitating them is imitating fictions — and the actual content of that imitation has historically included asceticism, [[universe|world]]-denial, and indifference to human [[evil|suffering]]. Holbach's alternative — a morality grounded in the natural [[passion|desire]] for happiness and the social necessity of mutual aid — requires no gods and no Forms.

## Legacy

Plato's influence is so pervasive as to be nearly invisible. Every distinction between appearance and reality, body and soul, matter and [[soul|spirit]] that structures Western thought bears the trace of his philosophy. [[enlightenment|The Enlightenment]]'s project of dethroning Plato — of establishing a philosophy grounded in experience, [[universe|nature]], and reason rather than Forms, revelation, and authority — was a multi-generational effort that is still incomplete.

Holbach's critique of Plato, while polemical, belongs to a serious tradition of anti-Platonic thought that includes [[epicurus|Epicurus]] in antiquity, [[hobbes|Hobbes]] and Gassendi in the seventeenth century, and continues through Nietzsche and Dewey in the modern period. The question is not whether Plato was a great philosopher — he was — but whether his greatness consisted in illuminating reality or in constructing an elaborate and seductive fiction. Holbach's answer is unambiguous.
