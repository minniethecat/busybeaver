# Equality

## Definition

In Holbach's "Common Sense," equality operates on two distinct but interconnected planes. On the **natural plane**, equality is a brute fact of existence: [[universe|nature]] "placed on equal footing all the [[existence|being]]s which she produced" (ch. 99). All organisms are equally subject to birth, death, enjoyment, and [[evil|suffering]] — no species, and certainly no human [[existence|being]], occupies a privileged place in the [[order|cosmic order]]. On the **political-legal plane**, equality is a normative demand: laws must "restrain equally the powerful and the weak, the great and the small, the [[monarch|sovereign]] and the subjects" (ch. 144). Holbach's originality lies in welding these two planes together: because [[universe|nature]] makes no distinctions of rank among [[existence|being]]s, human institutions that create hierarchy — above all, theological institutions — are not merely unjust but *unnatural*. Equality is thus not a utopian [[hope|aspiration]] but a return to the natural condition that [[religion|theology]] has obscured.

## Historical Context

Holbach's treatment of equality must be situated within the broader Enlightenment debate over [[rights|natural rights]] and social hierarchy. By the 1770s, when "Common Sense" (originally *Le Bon Sens*) appeared, two rival conceptions of equality were in circulation. The [[john-locke|Locke]]an tradition grounded equality in natural law: all men are born equal in rights because [[god|God]] created them so. Rousseau, in the *Discourse on Inequality* (1755), radicalized this by arguing that inequality itself is the product of civilization — property, law, and organized religion conspire to legitimize what [[universe|nature]] never intended. Holbach borrows from both while rejecting their theological premises. Like [[john-locke|Locke]], he insists on equality before law; like Rousseau, he sees existing inequalities as artificial. But unlike either, he refuses any appeal to [[order|divine order]]. For Holbach, equality requires no theological foundation — [[universe|nature]] itself, understood materialistically, is the sufficient and only necessary ground. This makes Holbach one of the first thinkers to articulate a thoroughly secular egalitarianism, one that would influence later radical Enlightenment figures from Condorcet to Jeremy Bentham.

## Core Arguments

**1. Natural equality refutes human exceptionalism.** The doctrine that humans are the special object of [[divinity|divine]] concern — that [[nature|the universe]] was made *for us* — is the root of theological vanity. Holbach counters: "[[will|Will]] they not see that this [[universe|nature]] has placed on equal footing all the [[existence|being]]s which she produced? [[will|Will]] they not see that all organized [[existence|being]]s are equally made to be born and to die, to enjoy and to suffer?" (ch. 99). Humans are not exempt from natural law; our mental faculties, far from elevating us above other creatures, often render us "more unhappy than the beasts" (ch. 99). This natural egalitarianism is the foundation of Holbach's anti-anthropocentrism: [[nature|the universe]] does not care about us, and the first step toward [[philosophy|wisdom]] is accepting our equal membership in the [[society|community]] of finite, [[evil|suffering]], mortal [[existence|being]]s.

**2. [[theology-index|Divine]] uniqueness is incompatible with [[divinity|divine]] glory.** In a stri[[monarch|king]] argument, Holbach contends that the very concept of [[god|God]] as a unique, unparalleled [[existence|being]] undermines the theological notion of [[divinity|divine]] glory. "A [[existence|being]] who has no equals," he w[[ritual|rites]], "can not be susceptible of glory. Glory can result but from the comparison of his own excellence with that of others" (ch. 49). If [[god|God]] is truly [[vice|sin]]gular — without equal, without rival — then the entire apparatus of worship, praise, and glorification is incoherent. Glory is inherently comparative; a [[existence|being]] with no equals cannot be glorious, only solitary. This argument inverts the traditional theological claim that [[god|God]]'s uniqueness is the source of His majesty: for Holbach, uniqueness destroys the very possibility of majesty.

**3. Theological inequality is a political weapon.** The "unequal combat" between [[god|God]] and humanity — in which an omnipotent [[existence|being]] demands obedience from feeble creatures who cannot possibly resist — is, Holbach argues, the template for all earthly [[despotism|tyranny]] (ch. 70). When [[clergy|priests]] claim to speak for an all-powerful [[god|God]], they naturalize hierarchy: if the [[divinity|divine]]-human relationship is one of absolute inequality, then earthly relationships of domi[[nations|nation]] appear as mere reflections of [[order|cosmic order]]. Holbach's countermove is to insist that "the fear of the [[god|God]]s, religion, the [[fear|terror]]s of another life" are the instruments by which [[monarch|ruler]]s keep subjects in submission (ch. 144). Equality before law is thus not merely a [[reform|political reform]] but a direct assault on the theological foundations of [[tyranny|despotism]].

**4. Equality of ignorance exposes religious pluralism as incoherent.** Holbach deploys an equality argument to cut through inter-[[sect|denomination]]al disputes: [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ians, Muslims, Jews, Hindus, and Chinese alike are "all equally ignorant of what they ought to think about [[god|God]]" (ch. 127). If each religion claims exclusive revelation, yet all disagree, the simplest expla[[nations|nation]] is that none has received any revelation at all. The equality of ignorance across [[worship|cult]]ures is, for Holbach, a decisive argument against [[revelation|revealed religion]]: "it is evident that [[god|God]] did not [[desire|want]] to instruct them" (ch. 127).

## Relation to Other Concepts

Equality is the structural complement to [[liberty]] in Holbach's political thought: liberty requires laws that apply equally, while equality before [[universe|nature]] makes liberty metaphysically possible (if humans were specially created, they would be subject to a special law, i.e., [[religion|theology]]). Equality also grounds Holbach's critique of [[providence]] — if all [[existence|being]]s are equally subject to natural law, there is no room for special [[divinity|divine]] intervention on behalf of any particular creature. The concept inter[[sect|sects]] with [[justice]] in the legal domain (laws must apply equally) and with [[nature]] in the metaphysical domain ([[universe|nature]]'s equal treatment of all [[existence|being]]s is the model for human institutions).
