# Love

## Definition

Holbach's treatment of love in *Common Sense* operates on two distinct levels: a critical deconstruction of the theological demand to love [[god|God]], and an affirmative account of natural human affection grounded in sensibility. Theologically, he argues that the [[god|God]] of orthodox [[christianity|Christianity]] — jealous, punitive, and prepared to damn the majority of humanity to eternal [[torture|torment]] — is an object incapable of inspiring genuine love. Psychologically and ethically, he grounds love in the natural [[temperament|constitution]] of sensible [[existence|being]]s who are "compelled by [their] very [[universe|nature]] to seek [their] welfare and to shun [[injustice|evil]], to love pleasure and to fear [[suffering|pain]]" (ch. 171).

The concept thus participates in Holbach's broader project of relocating the sources of moral motivation from [[divinity|divine]] command to human [[universe|nature]]. Love, properly understood, is not a duty owed to an invisible tyrant but a natural passion arising from the experience of what benefits us and those with whom we live. This redefinition strips love of its theological mystique and restores it to the domain of ordinary human psychology and [[morality|ethics]].

## Historical Context

The commandment to love [[god|God]] — "Thou shalt love the Lord thy [[god|God]] with all thy heart" (Deuteronomy 6:5) — stood at the centre of eighteenth-century [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian piety. Both Catholic and Protestant devotional literature emphasised the [[worship|cult]]ivation of [[divinity|divine]] love as the highest religious [[morality|virtue]]. At the same time, the Enlightenment's turn toward sentiment and sensibility (exemplified by Rousseau's *Julie* and [[adam|Adam]] Smith's *Theory of Moral Sentiments*) was generating secular accounts of human affection as a natural moral capacity.

Holbach draws on the empiricist psychology of [[john-locke|Locke]] and Condillac to argue that love, like all passions, arises from [[experience|sensory experience]] and the pursuit of [[happiness|well-being]]. He pushes this analysis to its anti-theological extreme: if [[god|God]] cannot be sensed and is represented as terrifying, the command to love Him is not merely diffi[[worship|cult]] — it is psychologically impossible. This argument belongs to a broader radical Enlightenment critique, shared by Diderot and d'Holbach's *coterie*, that religious [[passion|emotion]]s are fabricated by [[clergy|priests]] to manipulate the credulous.

## Core Arguments

1. **We cannot love what we do not know.** "How can we love anything we do not know? How can we love a [[existence|being]], the idea of whom is but liable to keep us in anxiety and trouble?" (ch. 62). [[sin|Sin]]ce [[god|God]] is inaccessible to the senses, we can form no adequate idea of Him — and without an idea, love has no object.

2. **A terrifying [[god|God]] cannot be loved.** "It is impossible to love a [[existence|being]], the thought of whom tends to excite [[fear|terror]], and whose judgments make us tremble" (ch. 65). The doctrine of [[hell|eternal punishment]] renders [[divinity|divine]] love psychologically impossible: "No man upon earth can have the least spark of love for a [[god|God]] who holds in reserve eternal, hard, and violent [[punishment|chastisement]]s for ninety-nine hundredths of His children" (ch. 65).

3. **Natural love is rooted in sensibility.** Human [[existence|being]]s are "compelled by [their] very [[universe|nature]] … to love pleasure and to fear [[suffering|pain]]" (ch. 171). Love of others arises naturally from the experience of their [[charity|beneficence]]: "in order to be loved by others, they must do good" (ch. 171). This is a prudential and affective account, not a theological one.

4. **The distinction between true religion and superstition collapses regarding love.** Theologians claim that the truly religious man loves [[god|God]] while the superstitious man merely fears Him. But "does not all religion in reality give us these same ideas of [[god|God]]?" (ch. 63). A [[god|God]] who "chastises with fury" cannot be loved — the distinction is specious.

5. **[[charity|Charity]], as preached by [[clergy|priests]], is a mask for [[persecution|intolerance]].** [[priest|Priest]]s "praise, with emphasis, charity, that tender love of our neighbors" (ch. 167) — yet they teach that one must hate those whom [[god|God]] hates: [[heresy|heretic]]s, infidels, and [[blasphemy|blasphemer]]s. "Ask if you must love your neighbor if he has other religious opinions; they will tell you that for love of the interests of the Most High" hatred is justified (ch. 167).

## Reception and Critique

Holbach's analysis of [[divinity|divine]] love as psychologically impossible was not entirely original — [[pierre-bayle|Pierre Bayle]] had argued similarly in his *Dictionnaire* (1697) that the doctrine of [[punishment|hell]] made genuine love of [[god|God]] incoherent. But Holbach's blunt, polemical formulation gave the argument wider currency in radical circles. [[will|Will]]iam [[god|God]]win and Percy Bysshe Shelley would later echo similar themes in English radical literature.

A [[model|standard]] theological rejoinder, defended by Fénelon and others, was that the "filial fear" of [[god|God]] was compatible with love — just as a child may both love and fear a just father. Holbach dismisses this analogy: "A son can not love his father when he knows he is cruel enough to inflict exquisite [[torture|torment]]s upon him" (ch. 65). The analogy fails because no earthly father punishes his children with eternal torture. Contemporary philosophers of religion continue to debate whether the "problem of [[punishment|hell]]" is logically compatible with [[divinity|divine]] love — a debate that Holbach's chapter effectively inaugurates.

## Relation to Other Concepts

Love is closely tied to [[Fear]], which Holbach identifies as the dominant religious [[passion|emotion]] and the antithesis of genuine love. It relates to [[Morality]], [[vice|sin]]ce natural human affection replaces [[divinity|divine]] command as the foundation of ethical conduct. The concept also connects to [[Happiness]], which Holbach defines as the natural goal of human life — love of pleasure and fear of [[suffering|pain]] [[existence|being]] the twin springs of action. Finally, love inter[[sect|sects]] with [[Sensation]], [[vice|sin]]ce all passions, including love, arise from [[experience|sensory experience]] and the pursuit of [[happiness|well-being]], not from immaterial [[soul|spirit]]ual faculties.
