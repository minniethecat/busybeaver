# Matter

## Definition

Matter, in Holbach's [[materialism|materialist philosophy]], is the fundamental substance of reality — extended, tangible, and capable of motion by its own inherent energy. It is not inert clay awaiting a [[divinity|divine]] potter but an active principle: "Matter moves by its own energy, by the necessary result of its heterogeneity" (ch. XXXIX). Everything that exists — from inorganic bodies to living organisms to human thought — is a product of matter in motion. There is no second substance, no "[[soul|spirit]]" or "soul" that exists independently of material organization.

## The Self-Motion of Matter

Holbach's most radical metaphysical claim is that matter does not require an external mover. Against the theological argument that motion must be imparted to matter by a [[divinity|divine]] [[cause|first cause]], he asserts that motion is inherent in the essence of matter itself:

> "Matter moves by its own energy, by the necessary result of its heterogeneity; the diversity of its movements or of its ways of acting, constitute only the diversity of substances; we distinguish one being from another but by the diversity of the impressions or movements which they communicate to our organs."
>
> (002-044)

This principle eliminates the need for a prime mover. Matter is not passive; it is inherently dynamic. The heterogeneity of material substances — their different compositions and configurations — produces the diversity of motions, forms, and [[existence|being]]s we observe. No external intelligence is required to explain why things move; motion is a property of matter itself.

## Materia Prima: The Bosom of Matter

Holbach appeals to common sense against [[soul|spirit]]ualist metaphysics:

> "Is it not more natural and more intelligible to deduce all which exists, from the bosom of matter, whose existence is demonstrated by all our senses, whose effects we feel at every moment, which we see act, move, communicate motion, and constantly bring living beings into existence, than to attribute the formation of things to an unknown force, to a spiritual being, who can not draw from his ground that which he has not himself?" (ch. XXII)

The argument is one of explanatory parsimony. Matter is known to us directly through sense experience; [[soul|spirit]] is known only through negative definition. To explain the known by the unknown — to say that matter comes from [[soul|spirit]] — is to invert the [[nature|natural order]] of reasoning. Matter is the productive ground of all things; [[soul|spirit]] is an "absence of ideas" (ch. XXI).

## Matter and Thought

A central objection to materialism is that matter cannot think. Holbach turns this objection on its head: thought is precisely what organized matter does. The brain, as a material organ, produces ideas just as the stomach produces digestion. [[spirit|Spirit]]ualists commit a category error when they demand that "matter" in the abstract should think; only concretely organized matter — a living brain — has this capacity. The [[secrecy|mystery]] is not that matter can think but that theologians have invented an [[spirit|immaterial substance]] to explain what matter [[demon|demons]]trably does.

## In the Text

On matter as the source of all existence:

> "Is it not more natural and more intelligible to deduce all which exists, from the bosom of matter, whose existence is demonstrated by all our senses... than to attribute the formation of things to an unknown force?" (ch. XXII)

On the self-sufficiency of matter:

> "The world has always been, its existence is necessary. It is the cause of itself. Nature, whose essence is visibly acting and producing, in order to fulfill her functions, as we see she does, needs no invisible motor far more unknown than herself."
>
> (002-044)

## Relation to Other Concepts

Holbach's concept of matter is the metaphysical foundation for his entire critical project. If matter is self-sufficient and self-moving, then the concept of [[God]] as creator is unnecessary. If all mental activity is a function of material organization, then the [[Soul]] as an [[spirit|immaterial substance]] is a fiction. Matter grounds Holbach's [[Materialism]], provides the ontological basis for [[Nature]] as a self-contained system, and supports his [[morality|ethics]] — if human [[existence|being]]s are material organisms, then [[Happiness]] must be pursued in this [[universe|world]] through material means.
