# Credulity

## Definition

Credulity, for Holbach, is the disposition to accept claims without rational exami[[nations|nation]] or sensory [[proof|evidence]] — the [[habit|mental habit]] that makes [[belief|religious belief]] possible. It is not an innate intellectual [[morality|virtue]] (as theologians claim when they praise "[[religion|faith]]") but a [[worship|cult]]ivated weakness, deliberately instilled by [[clergy|priests]] who "take care to encourage their weakness, in order to have a market for their remedies" (011-001). Credulity transforms the impossible into the credible, the [[absurdity|absurd]] into the [[divinity|divine]], and makes men "overgrown children" who beli[[eve]] that "the more inconceivable things are, the more [[divinity|divine]] they appear" (013-001).

## Historical Context

The critique of [[belief|credulity]] was central to the Enlightenment's self-[[knowledge|understanding]] as an age of reason emerging from an age of [[religion|faith]]. Kant's *sapere aude* ("dare to know") encapsulated the imperative to think for oneself rather than accept authority. Holbach's analysis was distinctive in treating [[belief|credulity]] not as a natural cognitive failing but as a manufactured product — the result of childhood indoctri[[nations|nation]], fear, and the deliberate suppression of critical faculties by a priestly class with a material interest in maintaining [[belief|credulity]]. This anticipated later critiques of ideology, false consciousness, and manufactured consent.

## Core Arguments

**1. [[religion|Religion]] is founded upon [[belief|credulity]].** The opening of Holbach's central chapter declares: "[[religion|Religion]] is founded upon [[belief|credulity]]" (006-001). The entire edifice of theological belief rests not on [[proof|evidence]] or reason but on the willingness to beli[[eve]] without either.

**2. Credulity is [[worship|cult]]ivated by fear.** "[[ignorance|Ignorance]] and fear are the two pivots of all religion" (ch. X). When the mind is frightened, "we beli[[eve]] [[eve]]rything and examine nothing" (ch. IX). [[fear|Fear]] disables the critical faculties that would otherwise reject religious claims.

**3. Credulity becomes self-reinforcing.** "He who from his childhood has had a habit of trembling [[eve]]ry time he heard certain words, needs these words, and needs to tremble" (011-001). The credulous person becomes addicted to fear; "he fears nothing more than having no object to fear."

**4. Credulity increases with [[absurdity|absurd]]ity.** "The more [[absurdity|absurd]] a religion is, and the fuller of marvels, the more power it exerts; the devotee thinks himself obliged to place no limits to his [[belief|credulity]]; the more inconceivable things are, the more [[divinity|divine]] they appear to him; the more incredible they are, the more merit he gives himself for believing them" (013-001).

**5. Credulity is the fundamental "[[morality|virtue]]" of religion because it serves [[clergy|priests]].** "The fundamental [[morality|virtue]] of all religion, that is to say, the most useful one to its ministers, is [[religion|faith]]. It consists in an unlimited [[belief|credulity]], which causes men to beli[[eve]], without exami[[nations|nation]], all that which the interpreters of the [[deity|Deity]] wish them to beli[[eve]]" (ch. CLXIX, Appendix).

**6. [[education|Education]] could dispel [[belief|credulity]] but [[clergy|priests]] prevent this.** The "first legislators of [[nation|nations]] had for their object to dominate. The easiest means of succeeding was to frighten the people and to prevent them from reasoning" (ch. XV). Generations are raised in [[belief|credulity]] because those in power benefit from it.

## Relation to Other Concepts

Credulity is the psychological soil in which [[Superstition]], [[Error]], and [[Religion]] grow. It is deliberately [[worship|cult]]ivated by [[Priest]]s through [[Fear]] and [[Ignorance]]. Its opposite is [[Reason]] — the critical exami[[nations|nation]] of claims against [[proof|evidence]]. [[Liberty]] of thought is the social condition that breaks the cycle of [[belief|credulity]], while [[Education]] (when freed from priestly control) provides the intellectual tools for critical thin[[monarch|king]]. Credulity is particularly associated with [[Faith]] as a theological [[morality|virtue]] that Holbach reinterprets as a vice.

## In the Text

On religion's foundation in [[belief|credulity]]:

> "We are told that Divine qualities are not of a nature to be grasped by limited minds. The natural consequence of this principle ought to be that the Divine qualities are not made to employ limited minds; but religion assures us that limited minds should never lose sight of this inconceivable being, whose qualities can not be grasped by them: from which we see that religion is the art of occupying limited minds with that which is impossible for them to comprehend." (006-001)

On the self-reinforcing [[universe|nature]] of [[belief|credulity]]:

> "He who from his childhood has had a habit of trembling every time he heard certain words, needs these words, and needs to tremble. In this way he is more disposed to listen to the one who encourages his fears than to the one who would dispel his fears. The superstitious man wants to be afraid; his imagination demands it. It seems that he fears nothing more than having no object to fear." (011-001)

On [[absurdity|absurd]]ity as the fuel of [[belief|credulity]]:

> "In the matter of religion, men are but overgrown children. The more absurd a religion is, and the fuller of marvels, the more power it exerts; the devotee thinks himself obliged to place no limits to his credulity; the more inconceivable things are, the more divine they appear to him." (013-001)

## The Epistemological Trap of Credulity

This passage exposes the double bind at the heart of religious epistemology: the believer is told both that [[god|God]] is incomprehensible and that [[knowledge|understanding]] [[god|God]] is the most urgent task of human existence. Holbach diagnoses this contradiction not as a paradox to be resolved through [[religion|faith]] but as a deliberate strategy — religion occupies the mind with the impossible, ensuring perpetual dependence on priestly interpretation.

> We are told that Divine qualities are not of a nature to be grasped by limited minds. The natural consequence of this principle ought to be that the Divine qualities are not made to employ limited minds; but religion assures us that limited minds should never lose sight of this inconceivable being, whose qualities can not be grasped by them: from which we see that religion is the art of occupying limited minds with that which they are not made to comprehend.
>
> (006-001)
