# Imagination

## Definition

In Holbach's "Common Sense," imagi[[nations|nation]] is the faculty that generates ideas without direct sensory input — and, when it operates without the discipline of reason and experience, it becomes the engine of all [[error|religious error]]. "All religious principles are a thing of imagi[[nations|nation]], in which experience and reason have nothing to do" (ch. 109). Imagination is not inherently dangerous; it is essential to art, invention, and [[eve]]n scientific hypothesis. But when it is [[worship|cult]]ivated by [[clergy|priests]], reinforced by habit from infancy, and protected from rational scrutiny by the doctrine of [[mysteries]], it produces a [[universe|world]] of supernatural [[existence|being]]s — gods, [[demon|demons]], [[angel|angels]], [[punishment|hell]]s, [[hope|heaven]]s — that have no existence outside the mind that imagines them.

Holbach's analysis of imagi[[nations|nation]] is central to his materialist epistemology. Every idea, he insists, originates in [[experience|sensory experience]]. [[concept-index|Ideas]] that have no sensory [[model|prototype]] — and the idea of [[god|God]] is the paradigmatic case — are chimeras: "An idea without a [[model|prototype]], is it anything but a chimera?" (ch. 4). Imagination supplies the content that sensation cannot provide, filling the gaps in human knowledge with fictions that are then mistaken for realities.

## Historical Context

The role of imagi[[nations|nation]] in [[belief|religious belief]] had been discussed [[vice|sin]]ce antiquity. Lucretius, in *De Rerum Natura*, attributed [[terror|fear of the gods]] to the mind's tendency to project living agency onto unexplained [[phenomena|natural phenomena]]. [[baruch-spinoza|Spinoza]], in the *Tractatus Theologico-Politicus* (1670), treated [[prophecy|prophetic]] inspiration as a product of vivid imagi[[nations|nation]] rather than [[divinity|divine]] communication. [[david-hume|Hume]]'s *Natural History of [[religion|Religion]]* (1757) traced [[belief|religious belief]] to the imagi[[nations|nation]]'s response to fear, hope, and the unknown.

Holbach synthesized these traditions into a systematic critique. Writing in the 1770s, he drew on the empiricist epistemology of [[john-locke|Locke]] (all ideas from sensation and reflection) and the materialist psychology of La Mettrie and Diderot (mental operations are brain processes). His innovation was to treat imagi[[nations|nation]] not as a special faculty of genius — the Romantic valorization of imagi[[nations|nation]] was just beginning — but as a cognitive vulnerability: the same faculty that produces poetry and art also produces gods. The cure is not to eliminate imagi[[nations|nation]] but to subject it to the discipline of reason and experience.

## Core Arguments

**1. All religious ideas are products of imagi[[nations|nation]].** [[sin|Sin]]ce [[god|God]] does not act upon any of the senses, the idea of [[god|God]] cannot come from sensation. It must come from imagi[[nations|nation]] — and imagi[[nations|nation]], "when once occupied in creating chimeras which astonish or excite it, is incapable of reasoning" (ch. 109). The more a religion stimulates the imagi[[nations|nation]] with [[mysteries]], [[miracle|miracles]], and [[fear|terror]]s, the more it incapacitates the [[reason|rational faculty]] that could expose its falsehoods.

**2. Imagination prefers the obscure to the clear.** Holbach identifies a psychological law: "It is the peculiarity of ignorance to prefer the unknown, the concealed, the fabulous, the [[miracle|wonder]]ful, the incredible, [[eve]]n the terrible, to that which is clear, simple, and true" (ch. 112). The more mysterious a doctrine, the more [[divinity|divine]] it appears precisely because the imagi[[nations|nation]] is more excited by the incomprehensible than by the evident. "[[truth|Truth]] does not give to the imagi[[nations|nation]] such lively play as fiction."

**3. [[habit|Habit]] entrenches imaginative fictions.** The "innate conviction" of [[god|God]]'s existence that theologians invoke is nothing but "the effect of a [[habit|rooted habit]]" imprinted on the imagi[[nations|nation]] in childhood (ch. 109). Repeated exposure to religious imagery and doctrine creates a second [[universe|nature]] that feels like an innate idea but is in fact an acquired [[opinion|prejudice]]. "[[reason|Reason]] can do nothing against habit, which becomes, as is said, a second [[universe|nature]]."

**4. Imagination serves priestly power.** The link between imagi[[nations|nation]] and clerical authority is structural. "The more [[mysteries]] a religion has, the more incredible objects it presents to the mind, the better fitted it is to please the imagi[[nations|nation]] of men" (ch. 112). [[priest|Priest]]s and legislators exploit this — they "invent religions and forge [[mysteries]]" to captivate the imagi[[nations|nation]] and secure submission. Ignorant people "always imagine that he who speaks to them of things which they do not understand, is a very wise and learned man."

## Reception and Critique

Holbach's treatment of imagi[[nations|nation]] as primarily a source of error was contested by Romantic thinkers who valued imagi[[nations|nation]] as a creative and revelatory faculty. Coleridge distinguished "fancy" (mechanical recombi[[nations|nation]] of images) from "imagi[[nations|nation]]" (a creative power that perceives unity in diversity), implicitly challenging Holbach's reduction. The later psychoanalytic tradition — particularly Freud's *The Future of an Illusion* (1927) — returned to Holbachian themes by treating [[belief|religious belief]] as wish-fulfillment, a product of the psyche rather than a response to reality.

Holbach's analysis remains influential in cognitive [[knowledge|science]] of religion, which treats religious concepts as byproducts of ordinary mental faculties — including imagi[[nations|nation]], agency detection, and theory of mind — operating in evolutionarily novel contexts. The Holbachian insight that imagi[[nations|nation]], unchecked by reason and [[proof|evidence]], generates supernatural beliefs is now an empirical research program.

## Relation to Other Concepts

Imagination is Holbach's expla[[nations|nation]] for the origin of religious ideas, placing it at the root of his entire critique. It connects to [[Reason]] as its necessary corrective — reason disciplines imagi[[nations|nation]] by demanding [[proof|evidence]] and logical consistency. [[Education]] is the process of training imagi[[nations|nation]] to operate within the bounds of experience. [[Fear]] and [[Ignorance]] are the [[passion|emotion]]al and cognitive conditions that make imagi[[nations|nation]] susceptible to religious exploitation. [[Superstition]] is imagi[[nations|nation]]'s characteristic product when fear and ignorance collaborate. [[Mystery]] is the device [[clergy|priests]] use to protect imaginative fictions from rational scrutiny.
