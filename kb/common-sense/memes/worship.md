# Worship

## Definition

In Holbach's "Common Sense," worship is the practical expression of [[belief|religious belief]] — the act by which humans address, placate, and honor their gods through ritual, prayer, sacrifice, and submission. Holbach's critique of worship operates at two levels. First, he argues that worship is directed at a fiction: "To adore [[god|God]] is to adore a fiction" (ch. 28). Second, he argues that [[eve]]n if the [[god|God]] of [[religion|theology]] existed, the qualities attributed to Him — cruelty, caprice, injustice — would make Him unworthy of worship. "In worshiping [[god|God]], man adores nothing but himself" (ch. 48), projecting his own [[passion|desire]]s and fears onto an imagined [[existence|being]] and then kneeling before the projection.

The final irony Holbach identifies is that men worship what they do not understand — and that this incomprehensibility is itself taken as a reason for worship. "To adore the profound views of [[wisdom|divine wisdom]], is it not to worship that of which it is impossible for us to form an idea?" (ch. 47). A [[existence|being]] that cannot be conceived cannot be worshiped except as an empty gesture; a [[existence|being]] that could be conceived, given the [[attribute|attributes]] [[religion|theology]] assigns to it, would be a monster.

## Historical Context

Worship in 18th-century [[europe|Europe]] was a pervasive social fact. The [[catholic-church|Catholic Church]]'s liturgical calendar structured the year; [[mass|Mass]] was a weekly or daily obligation; [[saint|saints]]' days, processions, and pilgrimages were woven into the fabric of communal life. The [[reformation|Protestant Reformation]] had simplified worship — stripping away images, [[saint|saints]], and Latin liturgy — but had intensified its interior demands: [[religion|faith]], repentance, personal relationship with [[god|God]].

Holbach wrote in Catholic [[paris|France]], where the political entanglement of worship with [[monarch|monarchy]] (the [[monarch|king]] ruled by [[divinity|divine]] right, and [[church|the Church]] legitimated his authority) made the critique of worship inseparable from the critique of [[tyranny|despotism]]. His argument that worship is directed at a fiction was also an argument that the [[society|social order]] built on that fiction — [[clergy|priests]], churches, tithes, religious courts — was a structure of organized imposture. [[voltaire|Voltaire]]'s "Écrasez l'infâme" ("crush the infamous thing") targeted the same alliance of throne and altar, though [[voltaire|Voltaire]] retained a deistic respect for a creator [[god|God]] who deserved rational gratitude, if not worship in the Catholic sense.

The broader context was the Enlightenment's comparative study of religion. Reports from missionaries and travelers had made [[europe|Europe]]ans aware of the diversity of worship practices across cultures — from Hindu temple rituals to indigenous American ceremonies. This comparative perspective relativized [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian worship: if all [[nation|peoples]] worship, but each worships differently, then worship appears to be a human universal springing from human psychology, not a response to [[revelation|divine revelation]].

## Core Arguments

**1. Worship is directed at a fiction.** [[sin|Sin]]ce [[god|God]] does not exist — or, more precisely, [[vice|sin]]ce the [[god|God]] of [[religion|theology]] is a [[existence|being]] of contradictory [[attribute|attributes]] who cannot exist — worship is an act without an object. "To adore [[god|God]] is to adore a fiction" (ch. 28). The [[temple|temples]], the [[prayer|prayers]], the sacrifices, the kneelings and prostrations — all are addressed to "phantoms" invented by [[clergy|priests]] and sustained by habit.

**2. It would be more rational to worship the sun.** In a provocative move, Holbach argues that sun worship is more rational than the worship of a [[soul|spirit]]ual [[god|God]] (ch. 24). The sun at least [[demon|demons]]trably exists, visibly benefits humanity, and acts according to regular laws. A [[soul|spirit]]ual [[god|God]] — invisible, intangible, and attributed with contradictory qualities — offers none of these advantages. "It would be more rational to worship the sun than a [[soul|spirit]]ual [[god|God]]."

**3. Men worship their own projected image.** Drawing on a tradition that goes back to Xenophanes (who observed that Ethiopian gods are dark-skinned and Thracian gods are fair), Holbach argues that in worshiping [[god|God]], "man adores nothing but himself" (ch. 48). The qualities attributed to [[god|God]] — power, [[philosophy|wisdom]], justice, goodness — are human qualities magnified to [[infinite|infinity]]. Worship is self-adoration by proxy: "In worshiping [[god|God]], man prostrates himself before his own imagi[[nations|nation]]."

**4. All gods have a barbarous origin.** Holbach surveys the history of worship and finds that "all the [[god|God]]s worshiped by men have a barbarous origin; they were visibly the productions of ignorant and savage ages" (ch. 119). The gods of the earliest [[nation|peoples]] were cruel, carnivorous, and bloodthirsty — and the [[god|God]] of modern [[religion|theology]], however much "refined" by philosophy, retains these savage traits: He demands blood sacrifice (the crucifixion of His own son), threatens eternal torture, and exterminates whole [[nation|peoples]]. "Men! you worship but a great savage, whom you consider as a model to follow."

## Reception and Critique

Holbach's critique of worship was among the most offensive parts of his work to contemporary readers, not only because it attacked religion but because it appeared to attack something that [[eve]]n deists and moderate skeptics considered natural and harmless: the human impulse to reverence. Rousseau, in the *Social Contract*, had argued that civil religion was necessary for social cohesion — a people must have some object of collective reverence, [[eve]]n if its metaphysical status is uncertain. Holbach rejected this instrumental defense of worship: a society built on reverence for a fiction is a society governed by the [[clergy|priests]] who control access to that fiction.

The anthropological turn in the study of religion — from Durkheim's *Elementary Forms of Religious [[life|Life]]* (1912) onward — has tended to support Holbach's claim that worship is a social phenomenon: societies worship collective representations of themselves. Durkheim's argument that [[god|God]] is society transfigured echoes Holbach's claim that "in worshiping [[god|God]], man adores nothing but himself," though Durkheim drew less inflammatory conclusions.

## Relation to Other Concepts

Worship is the practical dimension of the theological system Holbach attacks. It connects to [[Superstition]] as its psychological source — worship is superstition enacted. [[Priest]] and [[Priestcraft]] are the institutional mechanisms that organize and profit from worship. [[Sacrifice]] is the concrete act by which worshipers attempt to placate imagined [[gods|deities]]. [[Ritual]] is the repetitive structure that entrenches worship as habit. [[Fear]] is the [[passion|emotion]] that [[desire|drive]]s worship: men worship what they fear, and they fear what imagi[[nations|nation]] has created.

## In the Text

On the fiction that is worshiped:

> "To adore God is to adore a fiction. In worshiping God, man prostrates himself before his own imagination." (ch. 28, XLVIII)

On the [[reason|rationality]] of sun worship compared to [[soul|spirit]]ual worship:

> "It would be more rational to worship the sun than a spiritual God. The sun at least exists; it warms us, it illumines us, it makes the earth fertile. To worship a pure spirit is to worship nothing." (ch. 24)

On the barbarous origin of all gods:

> "All the Gods worshiped by men have a barbarous origin; they were visibly the productions of ignorant and savage ages. Men! you worship but a great savage, whom you consider as a model to follow." (ch. 119)

On worshiping what one cannot understand:

> "To adore the profound views of divine wisdom, is it not to worship that of which it is impossible for us to form an idea? Men worship only what they do not understand." (ch. 47)
