# Creation

## Definition

In Holbach's materialist cosmology, the doctrine of creation is the foundational error from which [[religion|theology]]'s other [[absurdity|absurd]]ities flow. "The [[universe|world]] has not been created, and matter moves by itself" (ch. 39). Holbach denies that [[nature|the universe]] had a beginning in time or that it was brought into [[existence|being]] by a [[divinity|divine]] will. [[matter|Matter]], in his view, is eternal and self-existent; its motion is an inherent property, not an impulse imparted from outside. The concept of creation ex nihilo — the production of something from nothing by an act of will — is, for Holbach, both logically incoherent and empirically empty.

The denial of creation is not a peripheral issue in Holbach's system but its metaphysical cornerstone. If [[nature|the universe]] is eternal, then no creator is needed to explain its existence. If matter moves by its own energy, then no prime mover is required. The entire edifice of natural [[religion|theology]] — the [[design|argument from design]], the cosmological argument, the argument from [[chance|contingency]] — collapses if creation is a fiction. Holbach's concept of creation is thus largely negative: it is the error whose refutation opens the door to a fully naturalistic [[knowledge|understanding]] of [[earth|the world]].

## Historical Context

The [[eternal|eternity]] of [[earth|the world]] was a perennial philosophical question. Aristotle had held that [[nature|the universe]] was eternal, a position that medieval [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian, Jewish, and [[islam|Islam]]ic theologians struggled to reconcile with the Genesis account of creation. Thomas Aquinas argued that creation in time could not be [[demon|demons]]trated by reason and had to be accepted on [[religion|faith]]. By the eighteenth century, the rise of [[isaac-newton|Newton]]ian physics and the discovery of geological deep time through the work of Buffon and others had made the biblical chronology of a six-thousand-year-old [[earth|Earth]] increasingly untenable.

Holbach's denial of creation drew on both ancient [[matter|materialism]] ([[epicurus|Epicurus]], Lucretius) and contemporary [[science|natural philosophy]]. He was particularly influenced by the geological and cosmological speculations of Buffon, who proposed that the [[earth|Earth]] had formed through natural processes over vast timescales. The French materialists also drew on [[baruch-spinoza|Spinoza]]'s identification of [[god|God]] with [[nature|Nature]] (Deus sive Natura), which eliminated the distinction between creator and creation on which orthodox [[religion|theology]] depended. Holbach's originality lies in his willingness to state the conclusion without equivocation: there is no creator, and [[earth|the world]] is self-existent.

## Core Arguments

1. **Creation ex nihilo is inconceivable.** "We are told very often that [[earth|the world]] did not create itself. But [[nature|the universe]] is not a work which has been made, because it is impossible to conceive its creation" (ch. 39). The notion of producing something from nothing is a violation of the most basic principle of reason: from nothing, nothing comes.

2. **[[matter|Matter]] is eternal and self-moving.** "[[matter|Matter]] is eternal; it has always existed; it can neither be annihilated nor created. Its motion is as eternal as its existence" (ch. 39). There is no need for a [[cause|first cause]] because the chain of causes extends infinitely into the past.

3. **The [[universe|world]] shows no signs of benevolent design.** "But, in a [[universe|world]] created expressly for him and governed by an all-powerful [[god|God]], how many reasons has man to complain!" (ch. 51). If [[earth|the world]] were created for human happiness, it is a spectacular failure: [[evil|suffering]], disease, natural disaster, and death afflict [[eve]]ry living creature. The facts of experience refute the hypothesis of a benevolent creator.

4. **The creator would be a monster.** Holbach sharpens the critique: "If [[god|God]], who was supremely happy before [[earth|the world]] was created, had continued to be supremely happy in the created [[universe|world]], why did He not create all men happy?" (ch. 59). An omnipotent [[existence|being]] who creates sentient creatures knowing they will suffer eternally is not benevolent but malevolent.

5. **Creation doctrine is politically motivated.** The claim that [[earth|the world]] was created by [[god|God]] serves to establish a parallel claim: that society was ordered by [[god|God]]. [[priest|Priest]]s and [[monarch|king]]s alike invoke [[divinity|divine]] creation to justify existing hierarchies. "They create gods after their own fashion; they make them speak according to their interests" (ch. 43). The doctrine of creation is as much a tool of power as a metaphysical proposition.

## Reception and Critique

Holbach's denial of creation was among his most scandalous positions, and it drew the most sustained counter-arguments. [[will|Will]]iam Paley's *Natural [[theology|Theology]]* (1802), though published after Holbach's death, can be read as an extended rebuttal: the intricate adaptation of organisms to their environments, Paley argued, is [[proof|evidence]] of design that no honest observer can dismiss. The [[design|argument from design]] would remain the strongest intellectual challenge to [[matter|materialism]] until Darwin's *On the Origin of Species* (1859) provided a naturalistic expla[[nations|nation]] of apparent design through natural s[[predestination|election]].

In the twentieth century, the Big Bang theory gave new life to the cosmological argument by suggesting that [[nature|the universe]] did have a temporal beginning. Holbach's position, however, was never dependent on the empirical details of cosmology: his core claim was not that [[nature|the universe]] is literally eternal in the sense required by steady-state cosmology, but that the concept of a creator is not an expla[[nations|nation]] — it merely relocates the [[secrecy|mystery]] without resolving it.

## Relation to Other Concepts

Creation is the metaphysical foundation that supports the entire theological edifice Holbach seeks to dismantle. Without creation, there is no [[Creator]] and no basis for [[Providence]]. The concept is intimately linked to [[Nature]], which Holbach presents as the eternal, self-organizing alternative to a created universe. It connects to [[Design]] — the argument from apparent purpose in [[universe|nature]] — which Holbach dismisses as an inference from ignorance. Creation relates to [[Evil]] because [[evil|the problem of evil]] is insoluble if [[earth|the world]] was created by a benevolent omnipotent [[existence|being]]; it dissolves if [[earth|the world]] was never created at all. Finally, creation connects to [[Eternity]]: if [[nature|the universe]] is eternal, it requires no beginning and hence no beginner.
