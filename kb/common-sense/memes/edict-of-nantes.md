# Edict of Nantes

## Overview

The Edict of Nantes, signed by King Henry IV of [[paris|France]] in April 1598, granted substantial rights to the Protestant minority (Huguenots) after nearly four decades of religious civil war. The edict allowed Protestants [[toleration|freedom of conscience]], the right to worship in designated locations, access to public offices, and control of certain fortified towns as security. It was the first formal decree of religious [[toleration|toleration]] in a major [[europe|Europe]]an state. Its Revocation in 1685 by Louis XIV — the Edict of Fontainebleau — outlawed Protestantism, destroyed churches, and drove hundreds of thousands of Huguenots into exile. Holbach wrote "Common Sense" in the aftermath of this catastrophe, and the French experience of [[persecution|religious persecution]] is the unstated historical backdrop to his entire critique.

## Historical Background

The French Wars of [[religion|Religion]] (1562–1598) pitted Catholics against Huguenots in a series of conflicts marked by atrocities on both sides, most infamously the [[bartholomew-massacre|St. Bartholomew's Day Massacre]] of 1572. Henry IV, himself a former Protestant who had converted to Catholicism to secure the throne ("[[paris|Paris]] is well worth a [[mass|Mass]]"), negotiated the Edict as a compromise that would end the bloodshed. The settlement held for nearly a century, though tensions persisted. Louis XIV, pursuing religious uniformity as an instrument of [[sovereign|absolute monarch]]y, progressively restricted Protestant rights before revo[[monarch|king]] the Edict entirely. The Revocation triggered a massive exodus: an estimated 200,000–400,000 Huguenots fled to England, the Netherlands, Prussia, Switzerland, and the American colonies, depriving [[france|France]] of skilled artisans, merchants, and soldiers.

## Key Figures and Developments

Henry IV (1553–1610) was the architect of the Edict; his assassination by a Catholic fanatic in 1610 [[demon|demons]]trated the fragility of toleration. Cardinal Richelieu, Louis XIII's chief minister, confirmed the Edict's religious pro[[dreams|visions]] while revo[[monarch|king]] the Huguenots' political and military privileges after the Siege of La Rochelle (1627–1628). Louis XIV (1638–1715), the "Sun King," made religious uniformity a pillar of his reign. His revocation of the Edict was celebrated by the Catholic [[priest|clergy]] but condemned by Enlightenment thinkers as an act of tyrannical [[persecution|intolerance]]. [[voltaire|Voltaire]], who wrote the biographical preface to Holbach's "Common Sense," was deeply engaged with the legacy of the Revocation, famously intervening in the Calas affair (1762), in which a Protestant was wrongfully executed.

## Impact on Religion and Thought

The Edict and its Revocation had profound consequences for [[europe|Europe]]an political thought. The Edict pioneered the concept that civil peace could coexist with religious pluralism — an idea that Enlightenment philosophers would develop into the principle of [[toleration|religious toleration]]. The Revocation, by [[demon|demons]]trating the catastrophic consequences of [[persecution|intolerance]], provided the Enlightenment with its most powerful domestic case study. The exodus of Huguenots also created an international network of religious refugees whose experiences fed into the Enlightenment critique of organized religion. Holbach's circle in [[paris|Paris]] was acutely aware of this history: the last execution for heresy in [[france|France]] occurred in 1762, the very year *Common Sense* was [[existence|being]] drafted.

## Connection to "Common Sense"

Holbach's "Common Sense" does not mention the Edict of Nantes by name, but the French experience of [[persecution|religious persecution]] is the book's unspoken historical referent. As the concept page for [[zeal|Zeal]] observes: "Holbach wrote in the shadow of the French Wars of [[religion|Religion]] (1562–1598), which had [[demon|demons]]trated the destructive power of [[zeal|religious zeal]] on French soil." The [[persecution|Persecution]] concept page elaborates: "The Revocation of the Edict of Nantes (1685) had [[desire|drive]]n hundreds of thousands of Huguenots into exile. The last execution for heresy in [[france|France]] occurred in 1762, and the Calas affair — in which [[voltaire|Voltaire]] famously intervened — was in 1762, when *Common Sense* was [[existence|being]] drafted." Holbach's insistence that religion causes "the most horrible disorders in society" and that "[[cruelty|persecution]]s themselves are considered as a convincing [[evidence|proof]] in favor of the religion that produces them" (ch. CXXXIII) takes on its full meaning only against this French background. When Holbach condemns [[intolerance|religious intolerance]], he is not spea[[monarch|king]] abstractly — he is describing the history of his own country.

## Legacy

The Edict of Nantes is now recognized as a landmark in the history of [[toleration|religious toleration]], anticipating the secular principles of modern liberal states. The Revocation serves as a cautionary tale of the human cost of religious uniformity. For the Enlightenment generation, the Edict and its destruction [[demon|demons]]trated that [[toleration|religious toleration]], once achieved, could not be taken for granted — it required philosophical defense against the persistent forces of [[fanaticism|fanaticism]] and priestly [[authority|authority]]. Holbach's "Common Sense" is one such defense.
