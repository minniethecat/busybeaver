# Conversion

## Holbach's Skepticism

Holbach subjects the phenomenon of religious **conversion** to skeptical analysis in *Common Sense*. He is dismissive of the sincerity of most conversions, observing dryly that "nothing is more rare than sincere conversions" (002-377). For Holbach, the mechanisms of conversion are primarily coercive: conversions occur not because individuals are persuaded by theological arguments but because they are subjected to social pressure, political inducement, or outright violence. He notes with irony that "in a province [they] have caused many conversions. Ah, if I was assisted!" (APP007-070) — suggesting that conversion campaigns are as much about the ambition and resources of the converters as about the convictions of the converted. The theological notion that "in hell is no more conversion, and the hour of mercy is past" (002-106) adds a further dimension to Holbach's critique: the threat of eternal punishment is used to compel "conversion" that is not conversion at all, but coerced compliance driven by fear rather than by authentic change of heart.

## Selected Passage

Holbach skeptically examines the sincerity and social utility of religious conversions:

> Even by the confession of the most ardent defenders of religion and of its usefulness, nothing is more rare than sincere conversions; to which we might add, nothing is more useless to society. Men do not become disgusted with the world until the world is disgusted with them; a woman gives herself to God only when the world no longer wants her.
>
> (002-377)
