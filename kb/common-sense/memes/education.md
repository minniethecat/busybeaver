# Education

## Definition

Education, in Holbach's "Common Sense," is not merely the transmission of information but the deliberate [[worship|cult]]ivation of the rational faculties — the process by which the human mind is "filled early with true ideas" (ch. II) and thereby immunized against superstition. It is the constructive complement to his destructive critique of religion: if [[clergy|priests]] maintain power by keeping minds "in a perpetual in[[imagination|fancy]]" (ch. II), the antidote is education that develops the capacity for independent judgment. "It is by exercise, by habitude, by education, that the human mind is developed." (ch. II).

## Historical Context

[[enlightenment|The Enlightenment]] was, above all, an educational project. Kant's famous definition of Enlightenment — "man's emergence from his self-incurred immaturity" — identified the failure to think for oneself as the central problem education must solve. Rousseau's *Émile* (1762) proposed a radical pedagogy based on natural development rather than rote instruction. The *philosophes* were deeply involved in the production of the *Encyclopédie* (1751–1772), which Diderot and d'Alembert conceived as an instrument of mass intellectual liberation. Holbach's distinctive contribution is to frame education not as an optional [[progress|improvement]] but as an existential necessity: without it, humanity remains enslaved to [[clergy|priests]] and tyrants. With it, "there will be no need of opposing to [man's] passions the powerless barrier of the fear of [[god|God]]s." (ch. II).

## Core Arguments

**1. Education is the foundation of moral conduct.** Holbach inverts the religious claim that morality requires [[divinity|divine]] sanction: "Let the mind be filled early with true ideas; let man's reason be [[worship|cult]]ivated; let justice govern him; and there will be no need of opposing to his passions the powerless barrier of the fear of [[god|God]]s." (ch. II). Proper education produces moral behavior as a natural consequence, without supernatural incentives.

**2. Education combats the psychological infrastructure of [[despotism|tyranny]].** The uneducated mind is plastic and vulnerable: "We receive all the impressions we wish to make on it; education furnishes us with our ideas." (ch. II). This plasticity can be exploited by [[clergy|priests]] — or directed toward enlightenment by philosophers. The battle over education is literally a battle for the human future.

**3. Education is an ongoing process, not a childhood [[eve]]nt.** "It is by exercise, by habitude, by education, that the human mind is developed." The three terms — exercise, habitude, education — suggest that intellectual development is continuous: it is formed through repeated practice and habit, not through a [[vice|sin]]gle moment of instruction.

**4. The prohibition on reasoning is the core mechanism of anti-education.** Holbach identifies the command not to question as the central pedagogical crime of religion: "Even the [[existence|being]] forbidden to reason upon what they tell us, is an education in itself — an education in servitude." (ch. II). The ban on critical thought is itself a form of education — an education in submission.

## Reception and Critique

Holbach's educational optimism was characteristic of Enlightenment thought but has been challenged from several directions. Freudian psychoanalysis suggested that irrational [[desire|drive]]s are not simply the product of bad education but are constitutive of human psychology. The Marxist tradition argued that educational reform alone cannot transform society without changes in the material base. John Dewey's pragmatist pedagogy shared Holbach's commitment to education as social transformation but grounded it in democratic participation rather than in anti-religious polemic.

## Relation to Other Concepts

Education is the practical program that makes [[Reason]] socially effective. It is the direct antagonist of [[Ignorance]], which Holbach identifies as the source of superstition. Education produces [[Liberty]] by creating citizens capable of independent judgment, and it undermines [[Tyranny]] by brea[[monarch|king]] the intellectual dependency on which [[tyranny|despotism]] depends. It is linked to [[Science]] as the institutional form of knowledge production, and to [[Morality]] as the natural consequence of rational [[worship|cult]]ivation.

## In the Text

On education as the foundation of moral society:

> "Let the mind be filled early with true ideas; let man's reason be cultivated; let justice govern him; and there will be no need of opposing to his passions the powerless barrier of the fear of Gods. Men will be good when they are well taught, well governed, chastised or censured for the evil, and justly rewarded for the good." (ch. II)

On the mind's plasticity and the power of education:

> "We receive all the impressions we wish to make on it; education furnishes us with our ideas; even the being forbidden to reason upon what they tell us, is an education in itself." (002-037)

On education as continuous development:

> "It is by exercise, by habitude, by education, that the human mind is developed. It is by the same means that the body is strengthened." (002-170)

On the political consequences of educational failure:

> "Is it not punishing him for having been educated in erroneous opinions? Is it just to punish a man for the education which he has received, and of which he has not been the master?" (002-320)
