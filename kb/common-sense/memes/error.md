# Error

## Definition

Error, in Holbach's epistemology, is not merely factual mistake but a systematic condition deliberately [[worship|cult]]ivated by religious institutions. [[theology|Theology]] is described as "a continual insult to [[reason|human reason]]" (002-001) — a method for ma[[monarch|king]] "the impossible become credible" and "[[proof|evidence]] become doubtful or false." Error is the cognitive state in which men accept theological propositions that contradict [[experience|sensory experience]] and rational inference, a condition maintained by fear, habit, and the deliberate suppression of critical thin[[monarch|king]] from childhood.

## Historical Context

[[enlightenment|The Enlightenment]] treated error as a central problem for philosophy. Francis Bacon's doctrine of idols, [[rene-descartes|Descartes]]'s method of systematic doubt, and [[john-locke|Locke]]'s empiricism all sought to identify and eliminate sources of error. Holbach radicalized this tradition by locating the primary source of error not in the limitations of individual cognition but in a social institution — organized religion — that had a vested interest in keeping minds clouded. This was a political epistemology: error served power. His approach anticipated Marx's later critique of ideology as false consciousness serving class interests and foreshadowed twentieth-century analyses of propaganda.

## Core Arguments

**1. [[theology|Theology]] is a system for producing error.** Holbach describes [[religion|theology]] as a "[[knowledge|science]] which has for its object only incomprehensible things" that "occupies itself but with things unseen." In its domain, "light is but darkness, [[proof|evidence]] becomes doubtful or false, the impossible becomes credible, reason is an unfaithful guide, and common sense changed into delirium" (ch. 2).

**2. Error is perpetuated by childhood indoctri[[nations|nation]].** Error becomes habitual through early exposure: "He who from his childhood has had a habit of trembling [[eve]]ry time he heard certain words, needs these words, and needs to tremble" (ch. 11). The superstitious person "[[desire|want]]s to be afraid; his imagi[[nations|nation]] demands it."

**3. Error compounds through systematic reinforcement.** Religious language itself maintains error: "By frequent repetition of if, but, and perhaps, we succeed in forming an imperfect and broken system which perplexes men's minds to the extent of ma[[monarch|king]] them forget the clearest notions" (ch. 3).

**4. Error is profitable to its purveyors.** [[priest|Priest]]s "take care to encourage their weakness, in order to have a market for their remedies" (ch. 11). Error creates a permanent demand for priestly services.

**5. Only liberty of thought can correct error.** "[[liberty|Liberty]] of thought can alone give to men humanity and grandeur of soul" (ch. 155). Removing the fear-based enforcement of [[dogma|religious dogma]] allows reason to correct accumulated errors.

## Relation to Other Concepts

Error is the cognitive counterpart to [[Superstition]] and the product of [[Credulity]] combined with priestly [[Deception]]. It is the antithesis of [[Truth]], [[Reason]], and [[Science]]. Error is transmitted through [[Education]] when controlled by [[clergy|priests]] and sustained by [[Fear]] of [[punishment|divine punishment]]. The correction of error requires [[Liberty]] of thought and the spread of [[Philosophy]] as a secular alternative to [[religion|theology]].

## In the Text

On [[religion|theology]] as systematic error-production:

> "There is a science which has for its object only incomprehensible things. Unlike all others, it occupies itself but with things unseen. Hobbes calls it 'the kingdom of darkness.' In this land all obey laws opposed to those which men acknowledge in the world they inhabit. In this marvelous region light is but darkness, evidence becomes doubtful or false, the impossible becomes credible, reason is an unfaithful guide, and common sense changed into delirium. This science is named Theology, and this Theology is a continual insult to human reason." (ch. 2)

On theological language as error-generating:

> "By frequent repetition of if, but, and perhaps, we succeed in forming an imperfect and broken system which perplexes men's minds to the extent of making them forget the clearest notions, and to render uncertain the most palpable truths." (ch. 3)

On fear as the mechanism that disables reason:

> "How is it that we have succeeded in persuading reasonable beings that the thing most impossible to understand was the most essential for them. It is because they were greatly frightened; it is because when men are kept in fear they cease to reason; it is because they have been expressly enjoined to distrust their reason." (002-009)
