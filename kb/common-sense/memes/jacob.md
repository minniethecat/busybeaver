# Jacob

## Biography

Jacob (Hebrew: Ya'akov, "heel-grabber" or "supplanter") is the third and final biblical patriarch, the son of [[isaac|Isaac]] and Rebekah, and the grandson of [[abraham|Abraham]]. His narrative occupies Genesis 25–50 and is the most psychologically complex of the patriarchal stories. Jacob deceives his blind father [[isaac|Isaac]] into giving him the blessing meant for his elder twin Esau, flees to Mesopotamia, and on the way experiences his most famous vision: a ladder reaching to [[hope|heaven]] with [[angel|angels]] ascending and descending. At the ford of the Jabbok, he wrestles through the night with a mysterious [[existence|being]] — identified variously as an angel, a man, or [[god|God]] himself — and is renamed [[israel|Israel]], "he who strives with [[god|God]]." His twelve sons become the ancestors of the twelve tribes of [[israel|Israel]].

In Jewish tradition, Jacob is the namesake of the entire people — [[israel|Israel]] is "the children of [[jacob|Jacob]]." In [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian typology, Jacob's ladder prefigures [[jesus-christ|Christ]] as the bridge between [[hope|heaven]] and earth. In [[islam|Islam]], Yaqub is a [[prophecy|prophet]] who counsels patience and trust in [[god|God]].

## Role in Religious History

Jacob is the patriarch in whom the covenant promise becomes a [[nations|nation]]. His twelve sons, born to four women (Leah, Rachel, Bilhah, and Zilpah), become the eponymous ancestors of the twelve tribes. The name-change from Jacob to [[israel|Israel]] — "your name shall be called no more Jacob, but [[israel|Israel]]; for as a [[monarch|prince]] hast thou power with [[god|God]] and with men" — marks the transformation of a family into a people. The entire subsequent history of [[israel|Israel]], from the exodus under [[moses|Moses]] to the [[monarch|monarchy]] under [[david|David]] and [[solomon|Solomon]], traces its legitimacy back to the promises made to Jacob.

For Holbach, this foundational narrative is not sacred history but a case study in how [[revelation|religious]] claims are manufactured. Jacob's [[dreams|visions]] — the ladder, the [[divinity|divine]] promise of land and descendants, the wrestling match — are reported by Jacob alone, accepted on his sole authority, and then retroactively sanctified by tradition. The structure is identical to [[eve]]ry other [[revelation|private revelation]] claim Holbach examines.

## Connection to "Common Sense"

Holbach gives Jacob more sustained attention than either [[isaac|Isaac]] or [[abraham|Abraham]], devoting an extended passage in Appendix 4 to a close reading of Jacob's [[dreams|visions]] — and then ridiculing them as the transparent products of a credulous age.

**1. Jacob's ladder as a dream, not a revelation.** Holbach recounts the Bethel narrative — Jacob's dream of a ladder reaching to [[hope|heaven]], with [[angel|angels]] ascending and descending — and then poses a deflationary question: what distinguishes this dream from any other person's dream?

> "After the death of Isaac, his son, Jacob going one day to Mesopotamia to look for a wife that would suit him, having walked all the day, and being tired from the long distance, desired to rest toward evening; lying upon the ground, with a stone for a pillow, he had a dream in which he saw a ladder placed upon the ground, the top of which reached to Heaven; and beheld the angels of God ascending and descending on it."
>
> (APP004-004)

The details Holbach emphasizes — the exhaustion, the stone pillow, the dream-state — are carefully chosen. A tired man falls asleep, has a vivid dream, and wakes to declare it a [[divinity|divine]] communication. "We can judge by this specimen of the first of these pretended Visions and [[theology-index|Divine]] [[revelation|Revelation]]s," Holbach w[[ritual|rites]], inviting the reader to see nothing more than a dream mistaken for [[revelation|revelation]].

**2. The wrestling match as [[absurdity|absurd]]ity.** Holbach's tone sharpens when he recounts Jacob wrestling with the angel at Peniel:

> "He had, says the Bible, a wrestle with an unknown man during the whole night, until the breaking of the day, and as this man had not been able to subdue him, He asked him who he was. Jacob told Him his name; and He said: 'Thy name shall be called no more Jacob, but Israel; for as a prince hast thou power with God and with men, and hast prevailed.'"
>
> (APP004-005)

The implication is not stated but unmistakable: an omnipotent [[god|God]] who cannot defeat a man in a wrestling match is either not omnipotent or not a [[god|God]]. The story, read literally, collapses into [[absurdity|absurd]]ity — and literal reading is precisely what the [[faith|faithful]] demand.

**3. The patriarchal [[model|pattern]] of unverifiable claims.** Holbach's ultimate point about Jacob is the same as his point about [[abraham|Abraham]] and [[isaac|Isaac]]: all three patriarchs claim private [[divinity|divine]] communications that cannot be verified, and all three are accepted on authority alone:

> "Now there is no reason to think otherwise of all that has been said by these pretended Holy Patriarchs, Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob, in regard to the Divine Revelations which they claim to have had."
>
> (APP004-007)>
> (APP004-007)

The use of the word "pretended" — *prétendus* in the French original — is pointed. Holbach is not arguing that the patriarchs were liars; he is arguing that their claims are indistinguishable from those of "insane fools" who make similar claims in [[eve]]ry age.

## Key Ideas Attributed / Critiqued

**1. Dream and revelation are indistinguishable.** Jacob's ladder narrative collapses the distinction between a vivid dream and a [[divinity|divine]] communication. If the criterion for a [[revelation|revelation]] is simply that the recipient reports having had one, then any dream can become a religion. Holbach's naturalistic reduction — tired man, stone pillow, dream — is designed to make supernatural expla[[nation|nations]] unnecessary.

**2. The name-change as retrospective myth-ma[[monarch|king]].** The renaming of Jacob to [[israel|Israel]] ("he who strives with [[god|God]]") serves a transparent narrative function: it retroactively legitimizes the entire subsequent history of the [[israel|Israel]]ite people as [[divinity|divine]]ly ordained. For Holbach, this is not prophecy but politics — a [[nations|nation]]'s origin-story dressed in [[revelation|supernatural]] language.

**3. The wrestling match as theological self-refutation.** A [[god|God]] who wrestles a man to a draw has abandoned omnipotence. Holbach does not need to say this explicitly — the [[absurdity|absurd]]ity of the image does the argumentative work. The story inadvertently reveals its own human origins: no one inventing a truly omnipotent [[god|God]] would depict him [[existence|being]] held to a stalemate by a mortal.

## Legacy

Jacob's name — [[israel|Israel]] — has outlasted [[eve]]ry empire that sought to destroy the people who bear it, an irony that Holbach's rationalist critique could not have anticipated. The modern state of [[israel|Israel]] takes its name from Jacob's new name, and the wrestling match at Peniel has become a powerful metaphor in Jewish thought for the ongoing struggle with [[divinity|the divine]].

In literary and philosophical traditions, Jacob has been read as the archetype of the trickster-become-patriarch — the deceiver who is himself deceived, the supplanter who is humbled, the man who wrestles [[god|God]] and limps away transformed. Holbach's deflationary reading represents one pole of interpretation; the existentialist reading, in which the struggle itself is the meaning, represents another.
