# Zeno of Citium

## Biography

Zeno of Citium (c. 334–262 BCE) was a [[hell|Hell]]enistic philosopher born in Citium, a Greek colony on Cyprus. According to Diogenes Laërtius, he was a merchant who turned to philosophy after [[existence|being]] shipwrecked in Athens, where he encountered the works of the Cynic Crates, the Megarian Stilpo, and the Academic Polemo. Around 300 BCE, he founded his own school in the Stoa Poikile ([[pain|Pain]]ted Porch) of the Athenian agora, from which the Stoic tradition takes its name. Zeno taught a comprehensive philosophical system encompassing logic, physics, and [[morality|ethics]], unified by the principle that the wise person lives in accordance with [[universe|nature]], which is governed by a rational, providential [[order|divine order]] — the *logos* that pervades and directs the [[universe|cosmos]].

Zeno's writings — including the *Republic*, a radical political utopia — have not survived. His thought is known through later Stoic authors (Seneca, Epictetus, Marcus Aurelius) and through the doxographical summaries of Diogenes Laërtius. Stoicism became, after [[plato|Plato]]nism and Aristotelianism, the third great philosophical system of classical antiquity, and it profoundly influenced Roman thought, early [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian [[morality|ethics]], and the natural law tradition.

## Role in Religious History

Zeno's Stoicism occupies an ambiguous position in the history of religion. On one hand, the Stoic concept of an immanent, rational [[order|divine order]] was among the most philosophically sophisticated theological frameworks of antiquity — one that allowed later [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian thinkers to find in Stoicism a pagan anticipation of their own providential mono[[divinity|theism]]. On the other hand, Stoic physics was thoroughly materialist: [[god|God]], for Zeno and his followers, was a material *pneuma* (fiery breath) that pervaded matter, not a transcendent creator standing outside [[earth|the world]]. This materialist [[religion|theology]] was, in effect, a form of pan[[divinity|theism]] that left no room for [[miracle|miracles]], revelation, or an [[death|afterlife]] of reward and punishment — features that set it at odds with [[christianity|Christianity]] and with the popular religious [[worship|cult]]ure of both the ancient and early modern [[universe|world]]s.

## Connection to "Common Sense"

In the concluding appendix to *Common Sense*, Holbach places Zeno in a telling list alongside [[plato|Plato]] and [[socrates|Socrates]] — the philosophers whose "fabulous conjectures" displaced the materialist systems of [[leucippus|Leucippus]], [[democritus|Democritus]], and [[epicurus|Epicurus]] (Appendix, PN 6990). The ancient atomists' doctrines, Holbach w[[ritual|rites]], were "too simple, too sensible, and too stripped of [[miracle|wonder]]s for the lovers of [[imagination|fancy]]" and were therefore "obliged to surrender to the fabulous conjectures" of the [[model|ideal]]ist tradition. Zeno's providential Stoicism — however materialist its physics — belongs, in Holbach's taxonomy, to the lineage of theological philosophy that subordinated reason to the demands of religion, however sophisticated its rationalizations.
