# Leucippus

## Biography

Leucippus (fl. 5th century BCE) is the most shadowy of the great Greek philosophers. Almost nothing is known of his life — [[eve]]n his birthplace was disputed in antiquity, with Miletus, Abdera, and Elea all claiming him. He was the teacher of [[democritus|Democritus]] and, according to the ancient doxographical tradition, the originator of the atomic theory of matter: the doctrine that [[nature|the universe]] consists of indivisible particles (atoms) moving through empty space (the void), and that all observable phenomena — [[universe|world]]s, bodies, sensations, thoughts — arise from the collisions and combi[[nation|nations]] of these atoms, without purpose, design, or [[divinity|divine]] [[government|governance]].

Leucippus's writings have not survived. His ideas are known only through fragments and testimonia preserved by later authors, principally Aristotle and Theophrastus, who attribute to him the foundational principles of atomism: that "nothing happens at random, but [[eve]]rything for a reason and by necessity." The inseparability of Leucippus's thought from [[democritus|Democritus]]'s — ancient authors routinely refer to "Leucippus and [[democritus|Democritus]]" as a [[guilt|sin]]gle philosophical entity — makes it impossible to determine which specific doctrines originated with the teacher and which with the student.

## Role in Religious History

Leucippus represents the earliest known attempt in the Western tradition to construct a fully naturalistic account of reality — a cosmology that explains the formation of [[universe|world]]s, the [[universe|nature]] of the soul, and the structure of matter without invo[[monarch|king]] gods, [[soul|spirit]]s, or supernatural agents. His atomism was, in effect if not in explicit intention, the first philosophical alternative to religious cosmology. By positing that [[nature|the universe]] operates through mechanical necessity rather than [[divinity|divine]] will, Leucippus opened the conceptual space within which later materialists — [[epicurus|Epicurus]], Lucretius, [[hobbes|Hobbes]], Holbach — would develop their critiques of super[[theism|natural religion]]. The atomists' thoroughgoing [[materialism|naturalism]] made them, in the eyes of later [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian apologists, the original [[atheism|atheist]]s.

## Connection to "Common Sense"

In the concluding appendix to *Common Sense*, Holbach invokes Leucippus (as "Lucippe") at the head of a list of ancient Greek materialists who "dared to tear away the thick veil of [[opinion|prejudice]], and to deliver philosophy from theological fetters" (Appendix, PN 6986). Alongside [[democritus|Democritus]], [[epicurus|Epicurus]], and Straton, Leucippus represents for Holbach the courageous minority who followed "experience and reason as their guides" and "shook off the chains of superstition." Holbach's genealogy of [[matter|materialism]] — from the ancient atomists through the moderns — is, in effect, the philosophical tradition of *Common Sense* itself: a lineage of thinkers who refused to subordinate reason to [[religion|faith]] and [[proof|evidence]] to [[doctrine|dogma]].


## Selected Passage

Holbach names Leucippus among the ancient philosophers who had the courage to follow reason rather than superstition:

> Some ancient and modern philosophers have had the courage to accept experience and reason as their guides, and to shake off the chains of superstition.
