# Perfection

## Definition

The concept of divine perfection is one of the central targets of Holbach's philosophical critique. Theologians ascribe to [[god|God]] "all the perfections" — omnipotence, omni[[knowledge|science]], infinite goodness — yet [[earth|the world]] that this perfect being supposedly created is filled with imperfection, [[evil|suffering]], and [[injustice|evil]]. "The [[universe|world]], it will be said, has all the perfection of which it was susceptible," Holbach notes, but this defense merely relocates the problem: if [[earth|the world]] could not be more perfect, [[god|the creator]]'s power is limited; if it could but was not made so, [[god|the creator]]'s goodness is impeached (ch. LXIX).

Holbach's strategy is not to offer an alternative [[injustice|theodicy]] but to show that the concept of divine perfection is incoherent. A perfect being would create a perfect [[universe|world]], or none at all. The observable [[universe|world]] contains "so many disorders, so many [[evil|suffering]]s, so many vices, so much [[malice|wickedness]]" that the premise of a perfect creator collapses under the weight of the [[proof|evidence]]. "The perfection of [[god|God]] does not show to any more advantage in the creation than in the government of [[earth|the world]]" (ch. LXIX).

The deeper argument is that perfection itself is a human construct — an [[model|ideal]]ized projection of qualities we admire, raised to an infinite degree. We attribute to [[god|God]] what we wish we possessed, and call it perfection. But this projection tells us nothing about any actual [[existence|being]]; it is "an idle [[imagination|fancy]]" generated by the imagi[[nations|nation]] wor[[monarch|king]] on ignorance and fear.

## Historical Context

The problem of [[injustice|evil]] — how to reconcile a perfect, all-powerful, all-good [[god|God]] with the existence of [[evil|suffering]] — had been debated [[vice|sin]]ce [[epicurus|Epicurus]]. The [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian tradition produced elaborate theodicies: Augustine's privation theory ([[injustice|evil]] as the absence of good), Aquinas's distinction between primary and secondary causes, [[gottfried-leibniz|Leibniz]]'s "best of all possible [[universe|world]]s." Holbach was familiar with these arguments through his classical education and his participation in Enlightenment salons.

The [[lisbon|Lisbon]] earthquake of 1755 had galvanized Enlightenment criticism of optimism. [[voltaire|Voltaire]]'s *Candide* (1759) satirized [[gottfried-leibniz|Leibniz]]ian [[injustice|theodicy]] mercilessly. Holbach's critique, published in 1772, was more systematic: rather than mock optimism, he dismantled the concept of perfection itself, showing that it could not be coherently applied to a creator responsible for [[earth|the world]] as it is.

Holbach's argument also draws on the empiricist critique of infinite [[attribute|attributes]]. [[john-locke|Locke]] had argued that our idea of "[[infinite|infinity]]" is merely negative — the ability to add more without end — and tells us nothing about any actually infinite [[existence|being]]. Holbach applies this analysis to divine perfections: "infinite goodness," "infinite power," and "infinite [[philosophy|wisdom]]" are words without referents, generated by negating the limits of human experience.

## Core Arguments

**1. The [[universe|world]]'s imperfection refutes the premise of a perfect creator.** "Do we not see continual contradictions in the works of this [[god|God]] so perfect?" (ch. LXIX). The existence of [[injustice|evil]], [[evil|suffering]], and disorder is incompatible with the conjunction of omnipotence and perfect goodness. Theologians must either limit [[god|God]]'s power (admitting imperfection) or limit [[god|God]]'s goodness (admitting malignity).

**2. Perfection is a human projection, not a discovered attribute.** We "assign [[attribute|attributes]] to this incomprehensible [[god|God]]" (ch. V) by ta[[monarch|king]] human qualities we admire and magnifying them without limit. The result is not knowledge but "vain [[imagination|chimeras]]" — words that sound meaningful but refer to nothing real.

**3. The perfections of different religions contradict each other.** Each religion [[attribute|attributes]] different perfections to its gods; the jealous [[god|God]] of the [[testament|Old Testament]] and the all-[[mercy|merciful God]] of the [[testament|New Testament]] are incompatible. If perfection were a real attribute discovered through reason, it would be uniform across [[worship|cult]]ures; its variability proves it is a [[worship|cult]]ural fabrication.

## Relation to Other Concepts

Perfection is the attribute whose failure defines the theological problem of [[Evil]]. The critique of perfection is central to the broader critique of [[Theology]], which [[attribute|attributes]] perfections to a [[existence|being]] it simultaneously declares incomprehensible — a [[Contradiction]] that vitiates the entire enterprise. The concept of [[Nature]] replaces perfection as an explanatory principle: [[universe|nature]] is what it is, neither perfect nor imperfect, and requires no [[injustice|theodicy]]. [[Morality]] grounded in human [[universe|nature]] replaces the impossible [[model|standard]] of divine perfection with achievable human goods.

## In the Text

On the incompatibility of divine perfection with the observable [[universe|world]]:

> "The perfection of God does not show to any more advantage in the creation than in the government of the world. Do we not see continual contradictions in the works of this God so perfect?" (ch. LXIX)

On the f[[utility]] of theodicies that appeal to perfection:

> "Could not God have at least endowed men with that sort of perfection of which their nature was susceptible? But if God could not render him sinless, why did He take the trouble of creating him?" (ch. LXXIV)

On the projection of human qualities onto the [[divinity|divine]]:

> "His knowledge is admired in the perfection of his works, his goodness in the benefits which he grants to men." (ch. II) — Holbach notes that these are inferences from the very world they are supposed to explain, a circular pattern of reasoning.
