# Excess

## Definition

Excess, in Holbach's moral vocabulary, denotes any human impulse carried beyond the point where it contributes to welfare and into the territory where it causes harm.
The concept is central to his secular [[morality|ethics]]: morality is not about suppressing [[passion|desire]] but about moderating it, directing it toward genuine human goods rather than self-destructive extremes.

Holbach's distinctive contribution is the argument that religion does not restrain excess — it produces new and more dangerous forms of it.
[[fanaticism|Religious fanaticism]], ascetic self-denial, and the passions inflamed by theological controversy are all varieties of excess.
"To be barbarous is to recognize no right but force; it is [[existence|being]] cruel to excess; it is but following one's own caprice" (ch. 2).

The claim that religion restrains the passions is, for Holbach, empirically false.
He points to the "haughty tyrants, oppressive ministers, perfidious courtiers, countless extortioners, unscrupulous magistrates, [[charlatan|impostor]]s, adulterers, libertines" who populate the most religious societies (ch. 2).
Religious belief coexists comfortably with [[eve]]ry form of moral excess.

## Historical Context

Eighteenth-century [[morality|moral philosophy]] was deeply concerned with the regulation of the passions.
The dominant tradition, from Aristotle through Aquinas, treated [[morality|virtue]] as a mean between excess and deficiency.
Holbach accepts this framework but denies that religion is the right instrument for achieving the mean.

The Jansenist controversy in [[paris|France]] had dramatized the question of whether human [[universe|nature]] was capable of moral moderation without [[divinity|divine]] grace.
The [[jansenists|Jansenists]] taught that human [[universe|nature]] was radically corrupt and that only a special gift of grace — granted to few — could enable virtuous conduct.
Holbach inverted this claim: human [[universe|nature]] is not depraved, and the belief in its depravity is itself a form of excess that [[desire|drive]]s people to harmful extremes of self-denial.

The libertine [[worship|cult]]ure of the French aristocracy provided Holbach with abundant examples of the kind of excess he condemned — but he insisted that libertinism and religious [[zeal|fanaticism]] were symmetrical corruptions, both arising from the failure to ground morality in [[interest|rational self-interest]].

## Core Arguments

**1. Religious belief does not restrain the passions.**
"The fear of the Lord is, we are told, the beginning of [[philosophy|wisdom]]" — but Holbach asks us to "open the eyes and to consider what are the morals of the most religious people" (ch. 2).
The [[proof|evidence]] shows that religious societies are no less prone to excess than irreligious ones.

**2. [[religion|Religion]] produces its own characteristic excesses.**
Religious zeal, [[cruelty|persecution]] of [[heresy|heretic]]s, ascetic self-[[torture|torment]], and the "frightful excesses" committed in [[god|God]]'s name are not aberrations but expressions of religion's essential logic.
"[[religion|Religion]], very often leads them into fatal excesses" (ch. 197).

**3. All excess tends to destroy the human [[existence|being]].**
Holbach grounds his critique of excess in a naturalistic [[morality|ethics]]: "all excess tends necessarily to destroy our [[existence|being]] or to render it hateful" (ch. 2).
The criterion is not [[divinity|divine]] command but human welfare — excess is wrong because it harms the person who indulges it and the society that tolerates it.

**4. The appearance of piety enables hidden excess.**
Religious rituals — confession, absolution — allow the [[vice|sin]]ner to "return into his usual excesses, persuading himself all the while that his religion will repair the harm which he has done to himself" (ch. 197).
[[religion|Religion]] functions as a license for excess by offering cheap [[absolution|forgiveness]].

**5. [[mode|Mode]]ration requires rational [[knowledge|understanding]], not supernatural fear.**
"Men, without religion, would feel that all excess tends necessarily to destroy their [[existence|being]]" (ch. 197).
Holbach's alternative to religious restraint is enlightened [[interest|self-interest]]: people who understand the consequences of excess will moderate themselves without needing threats of [[punishment|hell]]fire.

## Reception and Critique

Holbach's argument that religion encourages rather than restrains excess was controversial but not unique.
[[voltaire|Voltaire]] made similar observations about the [[zeal|fanaticism]] of religious enthusiasts.
What distinguished Holbach was his willingness to generalize: it was not just fanatical religion but religion as such that produced excess.

Later critics have noted that Holbach's [[proof|evidence]] is selectively drawn from the worst examples of religious behavior and ignores the moderating role that religious communities have historically played — for instance, in temperance movements and in providing institutional support for self-discipline.

The psychological claim that absolution encourages recidivism (the "moral licensing" effect) has received some support from modern social psychology, though the mechanism is more complex than Holbach assumed.

## Relation to Other Concepts

Excess is the opposite of [[moderation]], which Holbach identifies with [[morality|virtue]] itself.
It is related to [[passion]] — the natural human [[desire|drive]]s that become destructive when unregulated.

Religious excess connects to [[fanaticism]], the specific pathology of [[zeal|religious zeal]] taken to violent extremes.
The concept also relates to [[virtue]]: Holbach's [[virtue|secular virtue]] consists precisely in the avoidance of excess through rational self-[[government|governance]].

Excess inter[[sect|sects]] with [[priest]]: [[clergy|priests]] encourage ascetic excesses (fasting, self-flagellation, celibacy) as marks of holiness while simultaneously profiting from the [[vice|sin]]s that ordinary excess produces through the sale of absolution.
