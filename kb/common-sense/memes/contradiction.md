# Contradiction

## Definition

For Holbach, contradiction is not merely a logical formal concept — it is the fundamental diagnostic tool for exposing error in theological claims. "That which shows us that what implies contradiction can not exist" (ch. CIV) is the principle of non-contradiction itself, which Holbach treats as the bedrock of rational inquiry. A system of thought that generates contradictions is thereby refuted, regardless of its antiquity, authority, or [[passion|emotion]]al appeal.

Theological discourse, Holbach argues, is saturated with contradiction. [[god|God]] is declared incomprehensible, yet theologians "do not hesitate to assign [[attribute|attributes]] to this incomprehensible [[god|God]]" (ch. V). [[religion|Religion]] is proclaimed necessary for all men, yet "it is precisely the one which they understand the least" (ch. CXV). [[god|God]] is perfectly good, yet [[earth|the world]] He created contains "so many disorders, so many [[evil|suffering]]s" (ch. LXIX). Each of these contradictions is not an isolated flaw but a symptom of a systematically incoherent enterprise.

Holbach's method is to accumulate contradictions until the cumulative case is overwhelming. "Compelled to acknowledge that your good [[god|God]], in contradiction with himself, has made man to become the victim of his own [[folly|foolishness]]" (ch. CXXV) — the accumulation of such observations is not rhetorical excess but a systematic [[proof|demonstration]] that theological discourse fails the most basic test of [[reason|rationality]]. A [[vice|sin]]gle contradiction refutes a system; [[religion|theology]] generates them at [[eve]]ry turn.

## Historical Context

The principle of non-contradiction — that the same thing cannot both be and not be in the same respect — was established by Aristotle as the firmest of all principles. Medieval scholasticism built its theological systems on Aristotelian logic, attempting to [[demon|demons]]trate that [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian doctrine was not contradictory when properly understood. Aquinas's *Summa Theologica* is organized around objections and replies, each designed to show that apparent contradictions dissolve under careful analysis.

[[enlightenment|The Enlightenment]]'s critique of religion often took the form of exposing contradictions that scholasticism had papered over. [[pierre-bayle|Bayle]]'s *Historical and Critical Dictionary* (1697) was a compendium of theological contradictions, presented with a show of orthodox piety that did not conceal its corrosive effect. [[voltaire|Voltaire]]'s *Philosophical Dictionary* (1764) made contradiction-hunting a popular literary genre.

Holbach's contribution was to systematize this tradition. Rather than collecting contradictions as isolated curiosities, he argued that contradiction is the necessary product of [[theology|theological method]] — of reasoning about an incomprehensible object using words without fixed meanings. The only remedy is to abandon the enterprise and restrict inquiry to natural objects accessible to observation and reason.

## Core Arguments

**1. Contradiction is [[evidence|proof]] of falsehood.** "That which implies contradiction can not exist" (ch. CIV). This principle, universally admitted, is sufficient to refute any [[doctrine|theological doctrine]] found to contain it. The fact that theologians have spent centuries constructing defenses of contradictory doctrines does not make them true — it makes them "palpable [[absurdity|absurd]]ities."

**2. [[mystery|Mystery]] is contradiction dressed in reverent language.** "A [[secrecy|mystery]] is nothing but a contradiction, a palpable [[absurdity|absurd]]ity, a manifest [[absurdity|nonsense]]" (ch. CXI). Theologians call contradictions "[[mysteries]]" to shield them from rational scrutiny. A religion "destitute of [[mysteries]] would be a contradiction of terms" (ch. CXI) — the very concept of religion requires the acceptance of contradiction.

**3. Contradictions between religions refute all of them equally.** Every religion claims exclusive truth, yet they contradict one another on [[eve]]ry point. If any one were truly revealed by [[god|God]], the rest could not exist. Their mutual contradiction [[demon|demons]]trates that all are human inventions.

**4. The contradictions of [[bible|scripture]] are an additional layer of [[proof|evidence]].** Holbach catalogs contradictions between [[gospel|the Gospel]]s — in genealogies, in resurrection accounts, in the sayings attributed to Jesus — as [[proof|evidence]] that the texts are human documents, not [[revelation|divine revelation]]s. "Contradiction is manifest" (ch. CXXVI) in [[gospel|the Gospel]] narratives themselves.

## Relation to Other Concepts

Contradiction is the logical failure that defines [[Theology]] as a discipline. It is the opposite of [[Reason]], which proceeds by eliminating contradiction through consistent principles. The acceptance of contradiction under the name of [[Mystery]] is what distinguishes religious from rational discourse. [[Proof]] is what [[religion|theology]] claims to offer but cannot, because its doctrines are contradictory. [[Absurdity]] is the practical consequence of contradiction — a claim so incoherent that no rational person can accept it.

## In the Text

On contradiction as the test of falsehood:

> "That which shows us that what implies contradiction can not exist." (ch. CIV)

On [[secrecy|mystery]] as disguised contradiction:

> "By a foolish simplicity, or rather by a plain contradiction of terms, do theologians tell us that a mystery is nothing but a contradiction, a palpable absurdity, a manifest nonsense." (ch. CXI)

On the contradictions in the concept of a good [[god|God]]:

> "Compelled to acknowledge that your good God, in contradiction with himself, has made man to become the victim of his own foolishness." (ch. CXXV)
