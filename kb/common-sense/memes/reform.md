# Reform

## Definition

Reform, for Holbach, is the deliberate [[progress|improvement]] of human institutions through the application of reason. "If [[eve]]rything is necessary, if errors, opinions, and ideas of men are fated, how or why can we pretend to reform them?" (ch. II). Holbach poses this question to preempt the objection that [[effect|determinism]] makes reform futile, then answers it: reform is itself part of the causal chain, and knowledge of necessity enables rather than disables action.

The concept of reform in *Common Sense* operates at multiple levels. It is religious reform — the rejection of [[doctrine|theological doctrine]]s that have corrupted human [[knowledge|understanding]]. It is political reform — the restructuring of society so that it serves human welfare rather than priestly power. And it is moral reform — the reorientation of [[morality|ethics]] from supernatural obedience to natural happiness.

Holbach's [[effect|determinism]] does not undermine reform but grounds it: if human [[existence|being]]s are shaped by their circumstances, then changing those circumstances — through education, good laws, and rational institutions — will change human behavior. Reform is the practical program that follows from a materialist [[knowledge|understanding]] of human [[universe|nature]].

## Historical Context

The 18th century was an age of reform projects. [[enlightenment|The Enlightenment]] program — summed up in Kant's *sapere aude* ("dare to know") — was fundamentally reformist: human institutions could be improved through the application of reason. The *philosophes* proposed reforms to law (Beccaria, Montesquieu), education (Rousseau, Helvétius), economics (the physiocrats, [[adam|Adam]] Smith), and religion ([[voltaire|Voltaire]]'s *Écrasez l'infâme*).

Reform was distinguished from revolution — most *philosophes*, including Holbach, sought change through persuasion and legislation rather than popular uprising. But the logic of radical critique pressed toward revolution, as the French experience after 1789 would [[demon|demons]]trate. Holbach's contemporaries recognized that reforming religion meant reforming the entire [[society|social order]] of which the church was the foundation.

Holbach's specific contribution was to argue that meaningful reform is impossible without first dismantling [[authority|religious authority]]. "[[theology|Theology]] opposed insurmountable barriers to the progress of [[truth|true knowledge]]" (ch. II). Reform that leaves religion intact is reform that leaves the enemy's headquarters standing.

## Core Arguments

**1. Reform requires knowledge of causes.** "The errors of men are the necessary results of their ignorance" (ch. II). Reform is not a matter of moral exhortation but of [[knowledge|understanding]] the causes of error and modifying them — primarily through education and the removal of priestly influence.

**2. Religious reform is the foundation of all other reform.** Every new religion claims to reform a corrupted earlier one, but "does not [[eve]]ry reform suppose that [[god|God]] did not know how at the start to give His religion the required solidity and perfection?" (ch. II). Genuine reform cannot come from within religion, which merely substitutes one set of superstitions for another. It must come from the abolition of [[authority|religious authority]] itself.

**3. Reform is possible because human [[universe|nature]] is malleable.** If men are shaped by their environments, then improving environments will improve men. "All religions, whether new, or ancient ones reformed, are evidently founded on the weakness, the inconstancy, the imprudence, and the malice of the [[deity|Deity]]" (ch. II). Genuine reform must address human [[universe|nature]] through rational institutions, not through appeals to a fictional [[god|deity]].

**4. [[christianity|Christianity]]'s claim to have reformed morals is false.** "The coming of our [[theology-index|Divine]] [[jesus-christ|Saviour]] has produced the most [[miracle|wonder]]ful revolution and the most complete reform in the morals of [[nation|nations]]" (ch. II) — Holbach presents this claim only to demolish it. The [[universe|world]] after [[jesus-christ|Christ]] is no more moral than [[earth|the world]] before him; the supposed moral reform of [[christianity|Christianity]] is a self-serving fiction.

## Relation to Other Concepts

Reform depends on [[Reason]] to diagnose errors and [[Progress]] to measure [[progress|improvement]]. It is obstructed by [[Superstition]], which teaches that present arrangements are [[divinity|divine]]ly ordained. [[Education]] is the primary instrument of reform, and [[Habit]] is the primary obstacle — bad institutions create bad habits, which make bad institutions seem natural. [[Imposture]] is what reform must expose: the deliberate [[imposture|deception]] by which [[clergy|priests]] maintain their power.

## In the Text

On [[effect|determinism]] and reform:

> "If everything is necessary, if errors, opinions, and ideas of men are fated, how or why can we pretend to reform them? The errors of men are the necessary results of their ignorance." (ch. II)

On religious reform as contradiction:

> "Does not every reform suppose that God did not know how at the start to give His religion the required solidity and perfection? To say that God in giving a first law accommodated Himself to the ignorance of the Jews, is to admit that God could not make the Jews as they ought to be." (ch. II)

On reform as human endeavor:

> "All religions, whether new, or ancient ones reformed, are evidently founded on the weakness, the inconstancy, the imprudence, and the malice of the Deity." (ch. II)
