# Council of Trent

## Definition

The **Council of Trent** (1545–1563) was the 19th ecumenical council of the [[catholic-church|Catholic Church]], convoked at Trento (in present-day Italy) as the centerpiece of the Counter-[[reform|Reformation]]. Over 25 sessions spanning eighteen years, the council defined Catholic doctrine on core theological questions — [[scripture|Scripture]] and tradition, original [[vice|sin]], justification, the [[sacrament|sacraments]] — in explicit opposition to Protestant [[doctrine|teaching]]s. For Holbach, the Council of Trent exemplifies how institutional [[religion|religion]] claims authority to settle truth by *decree*: a body of [[priest|clergy]] votes, and metaphysical reality is thereby declared.

## Historical Context

By the 1540s, the Protestant [[reform|Reformation]] had shattered the medieval unity of Western [[christianity|Christendom]]. Martin Luther's doctrine of justification by [[religion|faith]] alone, the spread of [[reform|Reform]]ed [[religion|theology]] under John Calvin, and Henry VIII's break with [[rome|Rome]] had created permanent fractures. The [[catholic-church|Catholic Church]]'s response was twofold: internal reform (the Council) and external action (the [[jesuits|Jesuit]] order, founded 1540, and the [[inquisition|Roman Inquisition]]).

The Council met in three periods (1545–1547, 1551–1552, 1562–1563), interrupted by war, papal succession, and political tensions between the Holy [[roman-empire|Roman Empire]] and [[paris|France]]. Its decrees required papal confirmation and were not fully implemented in many Catholic territories for decades — but they established the doctrinal baseline that would define Catholicism until the Second [[rome|Vatican]] Council (1962–1965).

## Doctrinal Decrees

The Council addressed [[eve]]ry major point of Protestant challenge:

| [[doctrine|Doctrine]] | Tridentine Position | Protestant Challenge |
|----------|-------------------|---------------------|
| [[scripture|Scripture]] and [[custom|Tradition]] | Both are sources of revelation; [[church|Church]] alone interprets | *Sola scriptura* — [[scripture|Scripture]] alone |
| [[original-sin|Original Sin]] | [[baptism|Baptism]] removes guilt but concupiscence remains | Luther: total corruption of human [[universe|nature]] |
| Justification | [[faith|Faith]] *and* works; cooperation with grace | Luther: [[religion|faith]] alone (*sola fide*) |
| [[sacrament|Sacrament]]s | Seven [[sacrament|sacraments]] instituted by [[jesus-christ|Christ]] | Luther: [[baptism|Baptism]] and [[mass|Eucharist]] only |
| [[mass|Eucharist]] | [[transubstantiation|Transubstantiation]] reaffirmed | Varied: consubstantiation, symbolic presence |
| Purgatory | Affirmed; indulgences reformed but retained | Rejected entirely |

These decrees were not theological explorations — they were *legal determi[[nation|nations]]* backed by the claim of [[church|ecclesiastical]] authority to bind [[remorse|conscience]]. For Holbach, this is the essence of the problem: a self-appointed body of men claims the right to define metaphysical reality, and dissent becomes heresy.

## The Citation in "Common Sense"

Holbach's "Common Sense" cites the Council of Trent directly in a footnote to Chapter II:

> "at the Council of Trent.—Sess. 6, Ch. VIII." (002-459)

The reference appears in the context of Holbach's discussion of how [[religion|religious]] doctrines are "enigmas, made not to be understood" — the Council is cited as an example of institutional [[religion]] producing elaborate theological formulations that baffle rather than enlighten. The specific session and chapter referenced (Session 6, Chapter VIII) concerns the doctrine of justification and the manner of preparation for it — precisely the kind of intricate theological machinery that Holbach regards as designed to keep the laity dependent on priestly interpretation.

## The Counter-Reformation Apparatus

The Council of Trent was not an isolated [[eve]]nt but part of a coordinated institutional response that included:

- The [[jesuits|Jesuit]] order (founded 1540), providing a disciplined corps of educators and missionaries
- The [[inquisition|Roman Inquisition]] (established 1542), suppressing doctrinal deviation within Catholic territories
- The Index of Forbidden Books (1559), controlling the circulation of ideas deemed dangerous
- The Tridentine [[creed|Creed]] (1564), requiring public profession of [[religion|faith]] from [[priest|clergy]] and teachers

Together, these instruments created what Holbach would call the "sacerdotal empire" — a comprehensive system for controlling thought, [[remorse|conscience]], and behavior through institutional rather than rational means.
