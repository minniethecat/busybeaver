# Cicero

## Biography

Marcus Tullius Cicero (106–43 BCE) was a Roman statesman, orator, and philosopher. As consul in 63 BCE, he suppressed the Catilinarian conspiracy; his opposition to [[mark|Mark]] Antony later led to his execution. His philosophical works — *De Natura Deorum*, *De Officiis*, *Tusculanae Disputationes* — transmitted Greek philosophy to the Latin West.

Cicero was an Academic Skeptic, holding that certainty in philosophical matters is rare and that religious claims require rational scrutiny. His writings profoundly influenced Enlightenment *philosophes* see[[monarch|king]] classical precedent for the critique of superstition.

## Role in Religious History

Cicero's *De Natura Deorum* (On the [[nature|Nature]] of the [[gods|Gods]]) is one of antiquity's most important critiques of traditional religion. The work stages a debate between Stoic, Epicurean, and Skeptical views of divinity, exposing the contradictions of pagan [[religion|theology]].

Holbach follows a long tradition of freethinkers — [[montaigne|Montaigne]], [[pierre-bayle|Bayle]], [[voltaire|Voltaire]] — who treat Cicero as an ally in the critique of superstition and a model of rational inquiry into religious claims.

## Connection to "Common Sense"

Holbach quotes Cicero once, in Chapter II: "Cicero has said with reason that if [[god|God]] does not make Himself agreeable to man, He can not be his [[god|God]]. *[Nisi Deus homini placuerit, Deus non erit.]* [[goodness|Goodness]] constitutes [[divinity|Divinity]]" (Chapter II).

Holbach uses this Ciceronian principle to argue that [[goodness|divine goodness]] must manifest in actual creature [[happiness|well-being]] — a test the [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian [[god|God]] fails. Cicero's maxim becomes, in Holbach's hands, a logical [[proof|demonstration]] that the [[god|God]] of [[divinity|theism]] cannot exist.

Cicero is also listed by Holbach — alongside [[plato|Plato]], the Emperor Antoninus, the [[julian|Emperor Julian]], and Virgil — among the "profane" (secular) authors whose works are "superior to the books which we are told are inspired of [[god|God]]" (Chapter II). This ran[[monarch|king]] of pagan philosophy above [[scripture|Scripture]] exemplifies Holbach's rhetorical strategy.

## Key Ideas Attributed / Critiqued

Holbach [[attribute|attributes]] to Cicero the thesis that [[goodness|divine goodness]] must manifest in the actual [[happiness|well-being]] of creatures — a test that the [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian [[god|God]], who permits eternal [[hell|damnation]] and earthly [[evil|suffering]], manifestly fails.


> Cicero has said with reason that if God does not make Himself agreeable to
> man, He can not be his God. Goodness constitutes Divinity; this Goodness can
> manifest itself to man only by the advantages he derives from it. As soon as
> he is unfortunate, this Goodness disappears and ceases to be Divinity. An
> infinite Goodness can be neither partial nor exclusive.
>
> (002-095)
