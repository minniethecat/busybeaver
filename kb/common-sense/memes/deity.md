# Deity

## Definition

The Deity, in Holbach's analysis, is not a coherent concept but a cluster of mutually contradictory [[attribute|attributes]] assembled over centuries by theologians who cannot agree among themselves. The [[god|God]] of modern [[religion|theology]] is an abstraction — "a sterile [[existence|being]] who can produce nothing" — stripped of the material agency that [[eve]]n the pagan [[jupiter]] possessed. Holbach's strategy is not to prove that [[god|God]] does not exist but to [[demon|demons]]trate that the concept itself is unintelligible: the [[attribute|attributes]] assigned to the Deity (infinite yet personal, all-powerful yet unable to prevent [[injustice|evil]], perfectly good yet the author of eternal [[damnation]]) cancel each other out, leaving a null term that [[religion|theology]] labels a "[[secrecy|mystery]]" to conceal its emptiness.

The title of Holbach's book — *Common Sense* — signals his method. He insists that the idea of a Deity fails not by the [[model|standard]]s of sophisticated philosophy but by the [[model|standard]]s of ordinary reasoning. The peasant who asks why an almighty [[existence|being]] who [[passion|desire]]s human happiness does not simply make humans happy has already refuted the theologian, who can only reply that the question is impious.

## Historical Context

The concept of [[god|deity]] underwent radical transformation in the century before Holbach wrote. [[rene-descartes|Descartes]] had attempted to prove [[god|God]]'s existence from the idea of perfection; [[baruch-spinoza|Spinoza]] had identified [[god|God]] with [[nature]] itself; the deists had retained a creator [[god|God]] while rejecting revelation; and the materialists — La Mettrie, Diderot, Helvétius — had begun to argue that the concept was superfluous to any adequate expla[[nations|nation]] of [[nature|the natural world]]. Holbach's distinctive contribution was to synthesize these currents into a comprehensive assault on the coherence of [[divinity|theism]] itself.

[[voltaire]] had mocked specific doctrines with devastating wit but remained a deist. [[rousseau]] had displaced [[authority|religious authority]] onto the [[conscience|inner voice]] of [[remorse|conscience]] but retained a sentimental [[divinity|theism]]. Holbach went further than either: the idea of a Deity was not merely unproven but positively harmful. The notion of a [[divinity|divine]] tyrant who demands worship, punishes dissent, and authorizes [[priest]]s to rule in his name was the ideological foundation of [[eve]]ry [[despotism]] on earth.

## Core Arguments

**1. The [[attribute|attributes]] of the Deity are mutually contradictory.** Theologians assign to [[god|God]] properties that cannot coexist. An infinite [[existence|being]] cannot be personal; an immutable [[existence|being]] cannot have will or [[passion|desire]]; a perfectly good [[existence|being]] cannot create [[existence|being]]s destined for eternal [[evil|suffering]]. "Before an almighty [[existence|being]], who never executes that which he [[passion|desire]]s; before a [[existence|being]] supremely good, and who causes but displeasure" — this is the contradictory object of worship that [[religion|theology]] demands.

**2. The idea of the Deity is generated by fear, not reason.** "If fear has created the [[gods|Gods]], fear still holds their empire in the mind of mortals; they have been so early accustomed to tremble [[eve]]n at the name of the Deity, that it has become for them a specter, a goblin, a were-wolf which [[torture|torment]]s them" (ch. 183). The [[fear|terror]] that [[clergy|priests]] [[worship|cult]]ivate is not an [[chance|accident]]al feature of religion but its psychological foundation.

**3. The Deity's character reflects the [[clergy|priests]] who define it.** "The dark colors in which the [[clergy|priests]] [[suffering|pain]]t the Deity revolt men, moving them to hate and reject Him" (ch. 183). The [[god|God]] of [[religion|theology]] is a tyrant because [[clergy|priests]] need a tyrant to threaten their followers into submission. A benevolent [[god|God]] would require no [[clergy|priesthood]] to intercede on behalf of terrified worshipers.

**4. The concept of the Deity has been weaponized to authorize crimes.** "There is no crime which men have not committed in the idea of pleasing the Deity or of appeasing His wrath. The idea of a terrible [[god|God]] who was represented as a [[despotism|despot]], must necessarily have rendered His subjects wicked" (ch. 155). The Deity is not merely a philosophical error but a moral catastrophe: it provides [[divinity|divine]] sanction for human cruelty.

**5. The Deity is invoked to legitimize [[government|political authority]].** "The [[monarch|sovereign]]s of this [[universe|world]] in associating the Deity in the government of their realms, in pretending to be His lieutenants and His representatives upon earth, in admitting that they hold their power from Him — have made themselves dependent upon the [[clergy|priests]]" (ch. 173). The alliance of throne and altar is sustained by the concept of a [[god|God]] who delegates authority to [[monarch|king]]s and [[clergy|priests]] alike.
## Reception and Critique

Holbach's critique of [[god|deity]] is the most radical expression of eighteenth-century [[atheist|atheism]]. While earlier deists like [[voltaire|Voltaire]] had rejected [[christianity|Christianity]] while retaining belief in a creator [[god|God]], and while [[baruch-spinoza|Spinoza]] had identified [[god|God]] with [[universe|nature]] in a way that preserved the language of divinity, Holbach went further: he denied that the concept of [[god|deity]] had any coherent content at all. The word "[[god|God]]," he argued, is not the name of a [[existence|being]] whose existence is in question; it is a sound without meaning, a placeholder for ignorance.

This position was controversial [[eve]]n within Enlightenment circles. [[voltaire|Voltaire]], despite his hostility to organized [[christianity|Christianity]], attacked Holbach's *System of [[nature|Nature]]* as the work of a "philosopher gone mad." Rousseau's profession of [[religion|faith]] in the Savoyard Vicar explicitly rejected [[atheist|atheism]] on moral grounds: without [[god|God]], he argued, there could be no foundation for morality. Holbach's reply, implicit throughout *Common Sense*, is that morality requires no supernatural foundation — it is grounded in the natural human pursuit of happiness and the natural human capacity for sympathy.

Later philosophical developments have both vindicated and challenged Holbach's position. The rise of scientific [[materialism|naturalism]] in the nineteenth and twentieth centuries made his rejection of supernatural [[cause|causation]] the default position of educated opinion in much of the West. At the same time, philosophers like Kant and Kierkegaard developed more sophisticated defenses of [[belief|religious belief]] that Holbach's rather crude epistemological framework — with its simple equation of knowledge with [[experience|sensory experience]] — could not adequately address.

## Relation to Other Concepts

The concept of [[god|deity]] is the central organizing term of the entire critique. Every other concept in Holbach's lexicon is defined, directly or indirectly, by its relation to [[god|deity]]. [[religion]] is the system of practices by which human [[existence|being]]s attempt to relate to a [[god|deity]] they have invented. [[priestcraft]] is the exploitation of belief in [[god|deity]] for political and economic advantage. [[superstition]] is the fear of [[god|deity]] untempered by reason. [[prayer]] is the futile attempt to influence a [[god|deity]] who, if He existed as described, would be beyond influence. [[atheism]] is not a positive doctrine but simply the refusal to affirm what cannot be coherently stated.

The concept is also intimately connected to Holbach's political theory. The alliance of [[throne]] and [[altar]] — of political and [[authority|religious authority]] — is the great theme of the later chapters of *Common Sense*. The [[god|deity]] is the ultimate [[monarch|sovereign]] in whose name earthly [[monarch|sovereign]]s claim to rule; to dethrone the celestial monarch is, by implication, to undermine the legitimacy of the terrestrial one.
