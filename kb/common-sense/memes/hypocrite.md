## The Religious Hypocrite

In Holbach's analysis, religious systems do not merely permit hypocrisy — they actively produce it. By setting up impossible moral standards and threatening eternal punishment for failure, religion creates the conditions in which outward conformity masks inward corruption. Holbach groups the hypocrite alongside other moral failures: "A libertine, a debauchee, a hypocrite, an adulterer, or a thief" (002-388) — treating religious hypocrisy as one species within a broader taxonomy of vice that flourishes under the cover of professed virtue.

## Hypocrisy as Social Mechanism

For Holbach, the hypocrite is not merely an individual moral failure but a systemic product. When religion demands public professions of belief that bear no relation to private conduct, hypocrisy becomes a rational adaptation to an irrational system. The faithful are compelled to perform piety regardless of conviction, and this systematic disconnect between profession and practice corrodes the foundations of genuine morality.

## Beyond Religious Hypocrisy

Holbach's critique extends beyond the individual hypocrite to the institutional hypocrisy of churches that preach humility while amassing wealth, advocate peace while persecuting dissenters, and proclaim truth while suppressing inquiry. The concept of the hypocrite thus serves Holbach as a lens for examining the broader gap between religious profession and institutional practice that is, in his view, characteristic of all organized religion.


## From *Common Sense*

> A libertine, a debauchee, a hypocrite, an adulterer, or a thief will often show us that he has the clearest ideas of morals. Why do they not practice them? It is because neither their temperament, their interests, nor their habits agree with their sublime theories.
>
> (002-388)
