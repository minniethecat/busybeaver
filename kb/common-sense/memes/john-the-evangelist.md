# John the Evangelist

## Biography

John the Evangelist (also called John the Apostle, John the Theologian, or John of Patmos) is identified in [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian tradition as one of the twelve apostles of [[jesus-christ|Jesus]], the brother of James the Greater, and the "beloved disciple" who reclined next to [[jesus-christ|Jesus]] at the Last Supper. He is traditionally credited with the authorship of five [[bible|New Testament]] books: the Fourth [[gospel|Gospel]], three Epistles (1, 2, and 3 John), and the Book of [[revelation|Revelation]] (the Apocalypse). According to tradition, John was the only apostle to die a natural death, living to an advanced age in Ephesus, where he composed his [[gospel|Gospel]] in the final decade of the first century.

Critical scholarship has complicated this traditional picture considerably. Many scholars distinguish between John the Apostle, John the Evangelist (the author of the Fourth [[gospel|Gospel]]), and John of Patmos (the author of [[revelation|Revelation]]), treating them as possibly three distinct figures. The Johannine [[society|community]] that produced [[gospel|the Gospel]] and Epistles appears to have been a distinct strand of early [[christianity|Christianity]], with a higher [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ology and a more developed theological vocabulary than the synoptic tradition.

## Role in Religious History

John's [[gospel|Gospel]] differs markedly from the synoptic [[gospel|Gospel]]s ([[matthew|Matthew]], [[mark|Mark]], [[luke|Luke]]) in both chronology and [[religion|theology]]. Where the synoptics present [[jesus-christ|Jesus]] [[doctrine|teaching]] in parables about the [[monarch|king]]dom of [[god|God]], John presents long theological discourses in which [[jesus-christ|Jesus]] speaks of his own [[divinity|divine]] identity — "I am the way, the truth, and the life," "Before [[abraham|Abraham]] was, I am." The Prologue to John's [[gospel|Gospel]] ("In the beginning was the Word...") became the foundational text for the doctrine of the [[trinity|Trinity]] and for [[christianity|Christian]] [[theology|theology]]'s synthesis with Greek philosophical concepts of the Logos.

The Book of [[revelation|Revelation]], with its vivid apocalyptic imagery of beasts, seals, trumpets, and a new [[jerusalem|Jerusalem]] descending from [[hope|heaven]], has exercised an outsized influence on [[christianity|Christian]] imagi[[nations|nation]] and has been repeatedly mined by millenarian movements across two millennia. Its cryptic symbolism has made it the most contested and variously interpreted book in the [[bible|New Testament]].

## Connection to "Common Sense"

Holbach does not [[guilt|sin]]gle out John the Evangelist for individual attack — his target is the entire biblical canon — but John's writings furnish him with some of his most effective ammunition. The contradictions between John's [[gospel|Gospel]] and the synoptics, and between the various resurrection accounts, are among Holbach's key exhibits in his case against biblical inerrancy.

**1. Chrono[[contradiction|logical contradiction]].** In his catalogue of biblical contradictions, Holbach devotes sustained attention to the incompatibility between John's chronology and that of the synoptics. According to the first three [[missionaries|evangelists]], [[jesus-christ|Jesus]]'s public ministry lasted approximately three months; according to John, it lasted at least three years and three months:

> "According to what the first three Evangelists say, there could be scarcely more than three months from the time of His baptism until His death... and according to what John says, it would have lasted at least three years and three months, because it appears that He might have been three or four times at Jerusalem at the Easter feast which happened but once a year." (Chapter 2)

This is not a trivial discrepancy. If John's chronology is correct, the synoptics are systematically misleading about the entire temporal framework of [[jesus-christ|Jesus]]'s ministry. If the synoptics are correct, John has fabricated a multi-year ministry out of a few months. Either way, the texts cannot all be reliable — and yet [[christianity|Christian]] orthodoxy insists on the [[divinity|divine]] inspiration of all four [[gospel|Gospel]]s.

**2. [[resurrection|Resurrection]] apparition contradictions.** Holbach catalogs the incompatible accounts of [[jesus-christ|Jesus]]'s post-resurrection [[phenomena|appearances]], and John's version multiplies the confusion:

> "Matthew speaks of but two apparitions... Mark speaks of three... Luke speaks of but two apparitions the same as Matthew; and John the Evangelist speaks of four apparitions, and adds to Mark's three, the one which He made to seven or eight of His disciples who were fishing upon the shores of the Tiberian Sea." (Chapter 2)

The central [[eve]]nt of [[christianity|Christian]] [[faith|faith]] — the resurrection — is reported in four mutually incompatible accounts. For Holbach, this is decisive: a [[god|God]] who inspires contradictory accounts of his own decisive act in history is a [[god|God]] who does not exist in the way the [[faith|faithful]] imagine.

**3. The Apocalypse rejected by early [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ians.** Holbach notes that the Book of [[revelation|Revelation]] was itself contested within the early church: "The Ellogians attributed to the [[heresy|heretic]] Corinthus [[gospel|the Gospel]] and the Apocalypse of St. John; this is why they reject them." If [[eve]]n the earliest [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ians could not agree on which books were [[revelation|inspired]], the claim that the canon reflects [[divinity|divine]] guidance collapses into circularity — the canon was determined by the very human processes of ecclesiastical politics.

## Key Ideas Attributed / Critiqued

**1. The Johannine [[jesus-christ|Christ]] as theological inflation.** John's high [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ology — [[jesus-christ|Jesus]] as the pre-existent Logos, the "I am" of the burning bush — represents, for Holbach, not deeper theological insight but the progressive mythological inflation of a human teacher into a [[divinity|divine]] [[existence|being]]. The gap between the synoptic [[jesus-christ|Jesus]] and the Johannine [[jesus-christ|Christ]] is the gap between a man and a myth.

**2. [[contradiction|Contradiction]] as [[proof|evidence]] of human authorship.** The systematic contradictions between John and the synoptics — in chronology, in the content of [[jesus-christ|Jesus]]'s [[doctrine|teaching]], in the resurrection accounts — are for Holbach not problems to be harmonized by ingenious [[theology|theologians]] but [[proof|evidence]] that these are human documents, shaped by the competing interests and traditions of different early [[christianity|Christian]] communities.

**3. Apocalypticism as pathology.** The Book of [[revelation|Revelation]]'s violent imagery and its vision of cosmic destruction are, in Holbach's implicit reading, symptoms of the pathological dimension of [[religion|religious]] imagi[[nations|nation]] — a text that has inspired [[fanaticism|fanaticism]] and cruelty across two millennia, all in the name of a [[god|God]] of love.

## Legacy

John's [[gospel|Gospel]] has been, for many, the most [[soul|spirit]]ually profound book in the [[bible|New Testament]] — the source of some of the most beloved passages in [[christianity|Christian]] [[bible|scripture]] ("[[god|God]] so loved [[earth|the world]]...", "I am the resurrection and the life") and the theological foundation for the doctrine of the [[trinity|Trinity]]. The Johannine literature has inspired a vast tradition of mystical and contemplative [[spirit|spirituality]], from Meister Eckhart to Thomas Merton.

Yet the very features that make John's [[gospel|Gospel]] theologically rich — its developed [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ology, its symbolic and non-literal narrative style — also make it the most vulnerable to Holbach's critique. A [[gospel|Gospel]] that openly departs from the synoptic chronology and presents a [[jesus-christ|Jesus]] who speaks in theological prose rather than parables is, from a critical perspective, the clearest [[proof|evidence]] that [[gospel|the Gospel]]s are not historical records but [[theology|theological]] documents shaped by the communities that produced them.
