# Capernaum

## Overview

Capernaum (Hebrew: כפר נחום, *Kfar Nahum*, "Nahum's village") was a fishing village on the northern shore of the [[galilee|Sea of Galilee]], in the Roman province of [[galilee|Galilee]]. In the [[bible|New Testament]], it serves as the primary setting for Jesus's Galilean ministry — the town where he taught in the synagogue, healed the sick, cast out [[demon|demons]], and called his first disciples ([[peter|Peter]], Andrew, James, and John, all fishermen wor[[monarch|king]] the lake nearby).

Archaeological excavations have identified a first-century synagogue (possibly the one mentioned in [[gospel|the Gospel]]s), a house venerated by early [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ians as [[peter|Peter]]'s dwelling, and [[proof|evidence]] of a [[mode|modes]]t fishing economy. The town was destroyed by an earthquake in the eighth century and was never rebuilt to its former prominence. Today, Capernaum is an archaeological site and pilgrimage desti[[nations|nation]].

## In Holbach's Argument

Capernaum enters Holbach's argument through [[gospel|the Gospel]] accounts of Jesus's [[miracle|miracles]]. The Synoptic [[gospel|Gospel]]s report that Jesus performed numerous healings and exorcisms in Capernaum — the healing of [[peter|Peter]]'s mother-in-law, the centurion's servant, the paralytic lowered through the roof, the man with the withered hand, and others.

Holbach notes the Gospel contradiction regarding Capernaum:

> Matthew says that He returned to Galilee, and that leaving the city of Nazareth, He went to live at Capernaum, a maritime city; and Luke says, that He came at first to Nazareth, and afterward went to Capernaum.
>
> (002-488)

Holbach deploys the Capernaum [[miracle|miracles]] in his broader critique of the claim that [[miracle|miracles]] compel belief. The [[gospel|Gospel]]s record that despite witnessing these [[miracle|wonder]]s, the inhabitants of Capernaum did not universally accept Jesus's messianic claims. In [[matthew|Matthew]] 11:23, Jesus pronounces a curse on the town: "And you, Capernaum, will you be lifted to the [[hope|heaven]]s? No, you will go down to Hades. For if the [[miracle|miracles]] that were performed in you had been performed in Sodom, it would have remained to this day."

For Holbach, this passage is self-defeating. If the [[miracle|miracles]] genuinely occurred — if the blind were made to see and the lame to walk in full public view — belief would have been universal and immediate. That it was not suggests either that the [[miracle|miracles]] did not occur as described, or that the distinction between miracle and natural [[eve]]nt was not as clear to first-century Galileans as later apologists claim. Either way, [[miracle|miracles]] do not function as the irresistible [[proof|evidence]] of [[almighty|divine power]] that theologians present them as [[existence|being]].

## Historical Context

Capernaum exemplifies the gap between theological narrative and historical reality that Holbach exploits throughout *Common Sense*. The [[gospel|Gospel]]s present Capernaum as the stage for cosmic drama — the [[jesus-christ|Son of God]] [[doctrine|teaching]], healing, and casting out [[demon|demons]]. Archaeology reveals a village of perhaps 1,500 inhabitants, its residents engaged in fishing, farming, and small-scale trade.

Holbach's implicit argument is that this disproportion — between the cosmic significance attributed to [[eve]]nts in an obscure Galilean village and the mundane reality of that village — is characteristic of religion generally. [[revelation|Revelation]] always happens in "a corner of [[earth|the world]]," to "a handful of obscure [[existence|being]]s," while the great [[nation|nations]] remain ignorant. A universal [[god|God]], Holbach insists, would not confine his most important communications to a fishing village invisible to the empires of the age.

## Key Themes

**1. The locality of revelation.** Capernaum joins [[bethlehem|Bethlehem]], [[nazareth|Nazareth]], and [[jerusalem|Jerusalem]] as sites where [[revelation|divine revelation]] is claimed to have occurred — all within a small region of the eastern Mediterranean. Holbach asks: why here and not in China, [[india|India]], or [[rome|Rome]], the centers of ancient civilization?

**2. [[miracle|Miracle]]s and belief.** The Capernaum [[miracle|miracles]] illustrate Holbach's argument that [[miracle|miracles]] are not self-authenticating. If the residents of Capernaum witnessed them and remained unconvinced, what obligation do later readers have to accept miracle claims at second hand?

**3. The archaeology of disenchantment.** The excavation of Capernaum — revealing a [[mode|modes]]t fishing village rather than a city transformed by the presence of [[god|God]] incarnate — provides a materialist counter-narrative to [[gospel|the Gospel]] accounts. The stones do not testify to [[miracle|miracles]].
