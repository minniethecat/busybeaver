# Mary

## Biography

Mary of Nazareth (also called the Virgin Mary, the Madonna, or Mary, mother of [[jesus-christ|Jesus]]) is a central figure in [[christianity|Christian]] tradition, identified in the [[bible|New Testament]] as the mother of [[jesus-christ|Jesus]]. According to [[gospel|the Gospel]]s of [[matthew|Matthew]] and [[luke|Luke]], she was a young Jewish woman betrothed to [[joseph|Joseph]], a descendant of King [[david|David]], when an angel announced that she would conceive by the Holy [[spirit|Spirit]] and bear a son who would "save his people from their [[guilt|sin]]s." The [[gospel|Gospel]] of [[luke|Luke]] narrates the annunciation, the visitation to Elizabeth, the journey to [[bethlehem|Bethlehem]] for the census, and the presentation of the infant Jesus at the [[temple|Temple]]. Mary appears intermittently throughout [[gospel|the Gospel]] narratives, most prominently at the wedding at Cana and at the foot of the cross. Catholic and Orthodox traditions elaborate considerably beyond the [[bible|New Testament]]: the doctrines of Mary's Immaculate Conception (that she herself was conceived without [[sin|original sin]]), her perpetual virginity, and her bodily Assumption into [[hope|heaven]].

## Role in Religious History

In [[christianity|Christian]] [[religion|theology]], Mary occupies a unique position. She is not [[divinity|divine]] — the Council of Ephesus (431 CE) adopted the title *Theotokos* ("[[god|God]]-bearer") rather than "Mother of [[god|God]]" in an unqualified sense — but she is the most venerated human [[existence|being]] in the Catholic and Orthodox traditions. The *Ave Maria* ("Hail Mary") prayer, the rosary, and a vast body of Marian art and devotion make her, in practice, one of the most frequently invoked figures in [[christianity|Christian]] piety. Protestant reformers, while affirming the virgin birth, largely rejected the [[worship|cult]] of Mary as an accretion that distracted from [[jesus-christ|Christ]]. Nevertheless, the belief that Mary was a virgin when she conceived Jesus — and that she remained so perpetually — remained a litmus test of [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian orthodoxy across most [[sect|denomination]]s for centuries.

## Connection to "Common Sense"

Holbach does not devote a separate chapter to Mary, but she figures in two of his central lines of attack on [[christianity|Christian]] orthodoxy: the exposure of biblical contradictions and the [[proof|demonstration]] that [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian miracle stories have pagan precedents.

**1. Biblical contradictions.** Holbach's Chapter 2 catalogues the incompatible accounts of the women present at the crucifixion and the resurrection. The [[gospel|Gospel]]s cannot agree on which Mary was where and when:

> "Matthew speaks of but two apparitions: the one when He appeared to Mary Magdalene and to another woman, also named Mary... Mark speaks of three apparitions... Luke speaks of but two apparitions the same as Matthew; and John the Evangelist speaks of four apparitions." (Chapter 2)

If the resurrection is the decisive [[eve]]nt that validates all of [[christianity|Christian]] [[faith|faith]], the fact that the four canonical accounts cannot agree [[eve]]n on how many times the risen [[jesus-christ|Jesus]] appeared — and to whom — is, for Holbach, [[proof|evidence]] that these are conflicting human testimonies, not [[divinity|divine]]ly guaranteed records. Mary, mother of [[jesus-christ|Jesus]], appears in some accounts and is absent from others; the exact list of women at the tomb shifts from [[gospel|Gospel]] to [[gospel|Gospel]]. A [[divinity|divine]]ly inspired text would not produce this confusion.

**2. Virgin birth as borrowed mythology.** In the appendices to *Common Sense*, Holbach explicitly argues that the virgin birth of [[jesus-christ|Jesus]] is not a unique [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian claim but a motif borrowed from pagan mythology. The parallel he draws is precise:

> "Remus and Romulus, the founders of Rome, were miraculously born of a vestal virgin named Ilia, or Silvia, or Rhea Silvia; they had already said that Mars, Argus, Vulcan, and others were born of the goddess Juno without sexual union; and, also, that Minerva, goddess of the sciences, sprang from Jupiter's brain." (Appendix 2)

The logic is straightforward: if a virgin birth proves [[divinity|divine]] origin, then pagan [[gods|deities]] and founders — Romulus, Mars, Minerva — have the same claim as [[jesus-christ|Jesus]]. [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ians dismiss pagan virgin births as myths while insisting that their own is historical fact. Holbach sees this asymmetry as a product of [[worship|cult]]ural conditioning, not [[proof|evidence]].

**3. The annunciation.** Holbach quotes in full the angel's words to [[joseph|Joseph]] and Mary from [[matthew|Matthew]]'s [[gospel|Gospel]] — "[[fear|Fear]] not, Mary, for thou hast found favor with [[god|God]]" — and treats the passage not as sacred revelation but as a specimen of the mythological imagi[[nations|nation]] at work: a dream-narrative indistinguishable in literary form from pagan [[divinity|divine]]-conception stories (Appendix 6).

## Key Ideas Attributed / Critiqued

**1. The virgin birth as pagan parallel.** The core of Holbach's treatment is comparative: the claim that a woman conceived without a human father is not unique to the [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian story and therefore cannot serve as a distinguishing [[evidence|proof]] of [[jesus-christ|Christian]] truth. Every [[worship|cult]]ure, he observes, produces miraculous birth narratives for its founding figures.

**2. Mary as passive vehicle for [[revelation|revelation]].** In the biblical narratives Mary speaks little and acts less. She is presented as a vessel — "be it unto me according to thy word" — rather than as an agent. This passivity is, for Holbach, characteristic of the way [[religion|religious]] systems instrumentalize figures (especially women) as passive recipients of [[divinity|divine]] action, removing human agency from the most consequential moments of the narrative.

**3. The impossibility of verifying the claim.** Holbach's epistemological challenge — how can a claim about a private, miraculous [[eve]]nt (a [[divinity|divine]] conception announced in a dream) be verified by anyone other than the claimant? — applies to Mary's story with particular force. The annunciation is the quintessentially unverifiable [[eve]]nt: a private dream, a supernatural promise, a claim about a woman's body that can neither be confirmed nor disproven by ordinary [[proof|evidence]].

## Legacy

Mary remains one of the most [[worship|cult]]urally significant figures in Western history. The image of the Madonna and Child, the Marian hymns and feasts, the apparitions at Lourdes, Fatima, and Guadalupe — all testify to the depth of Marian devotion across two millennia. Feminist theologians have reinterpreted Mary as a symbol of female empowerment and resistance; others have critiqued the [[worship|cult]] of the Virgin as a vehicle for patriarchal control over female sexuality, setting up an impossible [[model|ideal]] of simultaneous motherhood and virginity.

From Holbach's perspective, the enduring power of Mary as a devotional figure illustrates his broader thesis: [[religion|religious]] imagi[[nations|nation]] generates symbols that meet psychological needs (for a compassionate maternal intercessor, for a pure and [[guilt|sin]]less human [[model|exemplar]]) and then projects these symbols onto history as if they were independent realities. The historical Mary remains inaccessible behind layers of mythological elaboration — but the Mary of devotion reveals a great deal about the human needs that produce [[religion|religion]].
