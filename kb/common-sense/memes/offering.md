# Offering

## Holbach's Analysis

Holbach examines the practice of religious **offerings** — gifts, sacrifices, and material contributions made to deities or their representatives — as a core mechanism of priestly exploitation in *Common Sense*. He catalogs the vocabulary of religious tribute: "offerings, sacrifices, expiations" (002-241) — all supposedly "worthy of the Monarch of Nature" but in reality enriching the clergy. The economic logic is transparent to Holbach: believers are told to "redouble your prayers, your offerings, your sacrifices" (002-303) when facing misfortune, and priests assure them that "Divinity is appeased by prayers, by offerings, by sacrifices, by penitential life" (002-347). The "most useful" result, Holbach notes sardonically, is that "men procure you blessings, offerings, expiations" (002-400) — the priests benefit materially while the faithful receive nothing but promises. For Holbach, the offering is religion's business model: the exchange of tangible goods for intangible assurances, mediated by a clerical class that takes its cut.

## Selected Passage

Holbach examines the revealed ordinances of God and finds in them not wisdom but costly ceremonies designed to enrich the clergy at the expense of the people:

> Examining the Divine wishes, I find in them, in all countries, but whimsical ordinances, ridiculous precepts, ceremonies of which we do not understand the aim, puerile practices, principles of conduct unworthy of the Monarch of Nature, offerings, sacrifices, expiations, useful, in fact, to the ministers of God, but very onerous to the rest of mankind.
>
> (002-241)
