# Piety

## Definition

Piety, in its conventional religious sense, denotes devotion to [[god|God]] and dutiful observance of religious obligations — prayer, worship, ritual participation, and moral conduct motivated by [[divinity|divine]] command. Holbach subjects this concept to withering critique, arguing that what passes for piety is often an external performance that not only fails to produce genuine [[morality|virtue]] but actively undermines it by providing a false substitute.

In *Common Sense*, piety is treated not as a [[morality|virtue]] but as a dangerous illusion. The "blind piety" of the [[religion|faith]]ful makes them vulnerable to manipulation by [[clergy|priests]]; the "fatal consequences of piety" (the title of chapter CLXXVI) include moral complacency, [[cruelty|persecution]] of dissenters, and the diversion of human energy from real-[[universe|world]] [[progress|improvement]] to imaginary obligations.

## Historical Context

The critique of piety as external performance rather than inward [[morality|virtue]] has deep roots in Western thought, from Jesus's condem[[nations|nation]] of the Pharisees ("whitewashed tombs") to Kant's distinction between acting from duty and merely acting in conformity with duty. [[enlightenment|The Enlightenment]] radicalized this critique: where earlier reformers sought to purify piety of its corruptions, thinkers like Holbach argued that piety itself — [[eve]]n in its "purest" form — was a misdirection of human moral energy.

The French context was particularly relevant. The *ancien régime* was built on an alliance of throne and altar; outward displays of piety were social and political obligations as much as religious ones. The [[church|Church]] owned vast lands, collected tithes, and exercised significant [[politics|political power]] — all justified by its role as guardian of piety and morals.

## Core Arguments

**1. Piety is external performance, not genuine [[morality|virtue]].** Holbach draws a sharp distinction between the outward signs of [[devotion|religious devotion]] and actual moral conduct. The pious believer may attend [[mass|Mass]], recite [[prayer|prayers]], and observe fasts while remaining selfish, cruel, or indifferent to human [[evil|suffering]]. [[religion|Religion]] "paralyzes morality" (ch. CLXXV) by redirecting moral attention from real human relationships to imaginary [[divinity|divine]] ones.

**2. "Blind piety" enables priestly exploitation.** In Meslier's autobiographical confession, he describes "abusing the blind piety of those who, by their simplicity, procured them such an easy life." The [[religion|faith]]ful, convinced that piety is the highest [[morality|virtue]], become economically and psychologically dependent on [[clergy|the clergy]] who mediate their relationship with [[god|God]].

**3. Piety has "fatal consequences."** Chapter CLXXVI catalogs the destructive effects of piety: [[cruelty|persecution]] of [[heresy|heretic]]s, [[crusades|religious wars]], the suppression of [[science|scientific inquiry]], and — perhaps most insidiously — the conviction that devotion to [[god|God]] excuses indifference to human welfare. "Even by the confession of the most ardent defenders of religion," Holbach notes, "nothing is more rare than [[guilt|sin]]cere conversions."

**4. Genuine morality requires no piety.** The [[atheist]], Holbach argues in chapter CLXXVIII, "has more motives for acting uprightly" than the believer, because the [[atheism|atheist]]'s moral conduct is grounded in visible, real-[[universe|world]] consequences rather than imaginary posthumous rewards. Piety is not merely unnecessary for [[morality|virtue]] — it is an obstacle to it.

## In the Text

Meslier's confession provides the most vivid portrait of how piety functions as a tool of manipulation:

> "I was compelled to perform [the sacraments] with the solemnity which awakened all your piety and all your good faith. What remorse I had for exciting your credulity!"

The chapter title "Fatal Consequences of Piety" (ch. CLXXVI) encapsulates Holbach's thesis. He argues there that the truly pious person — one who genuinely believes and devotes their life to religious observance — is not a moral [[model|exemplar]] but a victim of [[imposture|deception]] whose energies have been diverted from useful activity.

The reference to the pagan myth of Baucis and Philemon (whose "piety" was rewarded with a temple) serves as an ironic contrast — [[eve]]n the "blind piety" of simple people, Holbach suggests, is exploited by the priestly class who benefit from their devotion.

## Relation to Other Concepts

Piety is the positive face of [[devotion|religious devotion]], contrasted with [[superstition]] (its negative counterpart), [[fanaticism]] (its extreme form), and [[priestcraft]] (the institutional system that exploits it). It connects to [[virtue]], [[morality]], and [[ethics]] — concepts that Holbach seeks to detach from their theological foundations and ground in [[utility|social utility]].
