# Nations

## Definition

In Holbach's *Common Sense*, "[[nation|nations]]" function as the empirical refutation of universal revelation. If a [[guilt|sin]]gle [[god|God]] had revealed himself to all humanity, all [[nation|nations]] would worship him in the same way. The observed fact — that [[eve]]ry nation has its own gods, its own [[scripture|sacred texts]], its own incompatible religious claims — [[demon|demons]]trates that religion is a human product, varying with geography, history, and [[worship|cult]]ure rather than descending from a [[guilt|sin]]gle [[divinity|divine]] source.

Holbach does not treat the nation as a political unit to be analyzed in its own right but as an observational category: the diversity of [[nation|nations]] is the diversity of [[error|religious error]]. "Travel," he effectively invites the reader, "and you will see that what is sacred in one nation is [[absurdity|absurd]] in another."

## Historical Context

Holbach's use of national diversity as an argument against revelation drew on a rich tradition of travel literature and comparative religion. Jesuit missionaries had returned from China, [[india|India]], and the Americas with detailed accounts of non-[[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian civilizations that possessed sophisticated ethical systems without benefit of biblical revelation. The Confucian classics, the Vedas, and the Eddas were becoming available in [[europe|Europe]]an translation.

This comparative [[proof|evidence]] posed a crisis for [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian apologetics. If the Chinese had maintained a civilized society for millennia without knowledge of [[jesus-christ|Christ]], the claim that revelation was necessary for morality was empirically false. If the Hindus worshipped gods unknown to the [[bible|Bible]], the claim that [[god|God]] had revealed himself to all [[nation|peoples]] was [[demon|demons]]trably untrue. Holbach seized on this [[proof|evidence]] with polemical relish: the gods of the [[nation|nations]] are the gods of their inventors.

## Core Arguments

Holbach's argument from national diversity proceeds through three claims.

First, the geographic distribution of religion is indistinguishable from the geographic distribution of human [[worship|cult]]ure. [[gods|Gods]], like languages and [[habit|custom]]s, vary by region. A child born in Mecca becomes a Muslim; a child born in [[rome|Rome]] becomes a Catholic; a child born in Benares becomes a Hindu. Religious affiliation is an [[chance|accident]] of birth, not the result of rational inquiry — and certainly not the result of [[divinity|divine]] guidance.

Second, the mutual incompatibility of national religions refutes the claim that any one of them is uniquely true. Each nation's religion contradicts [[eve]]ry other nation's religion on fundamental points. They cannot all be true; the most parsimonious explanation is that none are.

Third, the fact that [[nation|nations]] fight wars over religious differences — each convinced of its own exclusive truth — [[demon|demons]]trates that religion is not a source of moral [[progress|improvement]] but of division and violence. The gods of the [[nation|nations]] are the excuses the [[nation|nations]] use to kill one another.

## In the Text

Holbach returns repeatedly to the theme of national religious diversity:

> "Every nation had its gods, its worship, its mythology, its oracles, its mysteries — in a word, its religion, which was always absurd, but which was always suited to the interests of those who governed it." (Chapter II, paraphrase)

> "The diversity of religious opinions among men proves that they have in all times been governed by imagination rather than by reason." (Chapter II)
