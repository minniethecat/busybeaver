# Disciple

## In Holbach's Analysis

Holbach treats **discipleship** as the paradigmatic relationship of uncritical intellectual submission in *Common Sense*. His key example is drawn from antiquity: "The disciples of Pythagoras had an implicit faith in their Master's doctrine: 'HE HAS SAID IT!' was for them the solution of all problems" (002-274). Holbach draws a direct parallel between ancient philosophical discipleship and modern religious faith — in both cases, "the majority of men act with as little reason" (002-274). The Christian disciples fare no better in his analysis: "the disciples of this Divine Doctor which His disciples admire so much, and practice so little" (002-344) are presented as failing to live up to even their own master's teachings. For Holbach, discipleship is the mechanism by which authority replaces independent thought — whether in the schools of Pythagoras or in the churches of Christianity.

## Selected Passage

Holbach uses the Pythagorean model of discipleship to illustrate the psychology of uncritical belief:

> The disciples of Pythagoras had an implicit faith in their Master's doctrine: "HE HAS SAID IT!" was for them the solution of all problems. The majority of men act with as little reason. A curate, a priest, an ignorant monk, will become in the matter of religion the master of one's thoughts.
>
> (002-274)
