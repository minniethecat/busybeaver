# The Reformation

## Overview

The Protestant Reformation was a 16th-century religious movement that led to the fragmentation of Western [[christianity|Christianity]]. Initiated by figures such as Martin Luther (1517) and John Calvin, the [[reform|Reform]]ation challenged the authority of the Pope, rejected certain Catholic doctrines and practices, and established Protestant churches across northern [[europe|Europe]]. While the [[reform|Reform]]ation broke the institutional monopoly of the [[Catholic Church]], Holbach's "Common Sense" views it as an incomplete reform that replaced one set of superstitions with another.

## Historical Background

By the early 16th century, the [[catholic-church|Catholic Church]] faced widespread criticism for corruption, the sale of indulgences, and the political ambitions of the papacy. Luther's Ninety-five Theses (1517) catalyzed existing discontent. The movement spread rapidly through the printing press, fragmenting into Lutheran, Calvinist, Anglican, and Anabaptist branches. The resulting religious conflicts — including the French Wars of [[religion|Religion]] and the Thirty Years' War — devastated [[europe|Europe]] and deepened the [[doubt|skepticism]] of Enlightenment thinkers toward all forms of organized religion.

## Key Figures and Developments

Martin Luther (1483–1546) emphasized salvation by [[religion|faith]] alone and the authority of [[scripture|Scripture]] over church tradition. John Calvin (1509–1564) developed a systematic Protestant [[religion|theology]] centered on predesti[[nations|nation]]. In England, Henry VIII broke from [[rome|Rome]] for political reasons, establishing [[church|the Church]] of England. The [[catholic-church|Catholic Church]] responded with the Counter-[[reform|Reform]]ation, including the [[council-of-trent|Council of Trent]] (1545–1563) and the founding of the [[Jesuits]] (1540).

## Impact on Religion and Thought

The Reformation ended the [[catholic-church|Catholic Church]]'s monopoly on Western [[christianity|Christianity]] but did not, in Holbach's view, end religious superstition. Protestant churches retained core [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian doctrines that "Common Sense" considers irrational — the divinity of [[jesus-christ|Christ]], the [[sacrifice|Atonement]], the authority of [[scripture|Scripture]], and the reality of [[miracle|miracles]]. The Reformation did, however, inadvertently advance secular thought by [[demon|demons]]trating that [[authority|religious authority]] could be challenged and that different interpretations of the same [[scripture|sacred texts]] could coexist. This precedent prepared the ground for the more radical critiques of the [[Enlightenment]].

## Connection to "Common Sense"

Holbach's "Common Sense" does not spare Protestantism from its critique. The work condemns all [[revelation|revealed religion]] as equally [[absurdity|absurd]], regardless of [[sect|denomination]]. The Reformation is implicitly present in Holbach's argument that religious di[[dreams|visions]] [[demon|demons]]trate the arbitrary [[universe|nature]] of theological claims: if [[divinity|divine]] truth were clear, there would not be "so many contradictory opinions upon the same subject" among [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ians. The work extends Meslier's critique beyond Catholicism to the entire edifice of [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian belief.

## Legacy

The Reformation's long-term effects include the rise of religious pluralism, the encouragement of literacy (through vernacular [[scripture|Bible]] translation), and the weakening of [[authority|ecclesiastical authority]] over political life. However, from the perspective of "Common Sense," its most significant legacy was [[demon|demons]]trating that religious orthodoxy could be successfully challenged — a lesson that radical Enlightenment thinkers applied more thoroughly than the [[reform|Reform]]ers themselves had intended.
