# Creator

## Definition

The Creator is the theological conception of [[god|God]] as the [[design|intelligent design]]er and [[cause|first cause]] of [[nature|the universe]] — the [[existence|being]] who brought all things into existence out of nothing and who sustains them in [[existence|being]] through His ongoing providence.

Holbach subjects the Creator concept to a sustained critique that operates on three levels. First, he argues that [[earth|the world]] as we actually observe it does not look like the product of a perfect intelligence. Second, he argues that a perfect Creator would be morally incompatible with the [[evil|suffering]] and [[injustice|evil]] that fill creation. Third, he argues that the Creator hypothesis explains nothing — it merely displaces the [[secrecy|mystery]] of existence onto an [[eve]]n more mysterious [[existence|being]] whose own origin is left unexplained.

The Creator, for Holbach, is not a solution to the problem of existence but its evasion. Rather than honestly confronting the fact that [[nature|the universe]] exists as a self-organizing material system, [[religion|theology]] invents an invisible artificer and then declares the problem solved. "What is this [[existence|being]]? What is its origin? What are its [[attribute|attributes]]? How does it act? These questions overwhelm us" (ch. 8).

## Historical Context

The [[design|argument from design]] — that the complexity and order of [[nature|the natural world]] implies an [[design|intelligent design]]er — was the most popular argument for [[god|God]]'s existence in the eighteenth century. [[will|Will]]iam Paley's watchmaker analogy (1802) was its most famous expression, but the argument was ubiquitous in sermons, philosophical texts, and popular apologetics long before Paley. [[isaac-newton|Newton]]'s physics, which revealed a universe of mathematical elegance and lawful regularity, was widely interpreted as [[proof|evidence]] of [[divinity|divine]] craftsmanship.

Holbach wrote before Paley but after [[isaac-newton|Newton]]. He knew the design argument well and attacked it at its empirical base. The [[universe|world]], he insisted, does not look designed — or if it does, the designer is not benevolent and not competent. The "human machine" is "liable to be out of order at any moment, and which would after a while destroy itself" (ch. 2). A mechanic who produced such a device would not be admired; he would be fired.

The problem of [[injustice|evil]] — if [[god|God]] is all-powerful, all-knowing, and all-good, why does [[evil|suffering]] exist? — was already ancient by Holbach's time. [[gottfried-leibniz|Leibniz]]'s *Theodicy* (1710) had attempted to reconcile [[perfection|divine perfection]] with [[universe|world]]ly [[injustice|evil]] by arguing that this is "the best of all possible [[universe|world]]s." [[voltaire|Voltaire]] famously satirized [[gottfried-leibniz|Leibniz]] in *Candide* (1759). Holbach goes further than [[voltaire|Voltaire]]: he does not merely mock [[injustice|theodicy]]; he argues that the very project of reconciling a perfect Creator with an imperfect creation is logically incoherent.

## Core Arguments

**1. The design argument fails on empirical grounds.**
Holbach asks: would you admire the skill of a mechanic who showed you a complicated machine that was "liable to be out of order at any moment, and which would after a while destroy itself"? (ch. 2). The human body, far from [[existence|being]] a masterpiece of design, is vulnerable to countless diseases, ages inexorably, and inevitably dies. The natural [[universe|world]] is full of parasites, predators, earthquakes, and famines. This is not the work of a perfect intelligence.

**2. A perfect Creator is incompatible with an imperfect creation.**
"[[god|God]] intended the happiness of [[angel|angels]] and of men, and He has never succeeded in rendering happy either [[angel|angels]] or men; pride, malice, [[vice|sin]]s, the imperfections of His creatures, have always been opposed to the wishes of the perfect Creator" (ch. 70). This is an [[absurdity|absurd]]ity: an omnipotent [[existence|being]] whose wishes are perpetually frustrated by his own creation is not omnipotent. If the Creator [[desire|want]]ed happy creatures, why did he create them with flaws that guarantee their misery?

**3. [[creation|Creation]] ex nihilo multiplies [[mysteries]] rather than solving them.**
To explain the existence of [[nature|the universe]] by positing a Creator simply pushes the question back one step: who created the Creator? If the Creator is self-existent and uncaused, why cannot [[nature|the universe]] itself be self-existent and uncaused? The Creator hypothesis violates Occam's razor — it introduces an additional, more complex entity to explain what [[matter|materialism]] explains more economically: that matter is eternal and self-organizing.

**4. The Creator's morality is indefensible.**
"Instead of creating men, a perfect [[god|God]] ought to have created only docile and submissive [[angel|angels]]" (ch. 70). A Creator who makes creatures he knows will [[vice|sin]], and then punishes them eternally for the [[vice|sin]] he made them capable of, is not a moral [[existence|being]]. He is, in Holbach's implicit characterization, a sadist. The Creator of [[religion|theology]] is "a [[despotism|despot]] to whom men are subjected but through fear, a master whom they can not interrogate, a totally inaccessible [[monarch|sovereign]]" (ch. 2).
