# Free Will

## Definition

In Holbach's determinist philosophy, [[liberty|free will]] is a chimera. Human [[existence|being]]s beli[[eve]] themselves free because they are conscious of their [[passion|desire]]s but ignorant of the causes that produce those [[passion|desire]]s. "Man is said to be a free agent [[eve]]ry time he finds within himself a will — that is to say, when he experiences a [[passion|desire]] which nothing prevents him from satisfying" (ch. 80). But this experience of unconstrained willing is an illusion: the will itself is determined by temperament, education, habit, and circumstance — all factors beyond the individual's control.

Holbach does not deny that humans make choices; he denies that those choices are free in any metaphysical sense. Every act of will is the [[effect|necessary effect]] of prior causes: "We choose; but are you really free to will or not to will, to [[passion|desire]] or not to [[passion|desire]]? Is not your will necessarily determined by some object, by some motive, by some disposition internal or external?" (ch. 80). The feeling of [[liberty|freedom]] is simply ignorance of the causal chain. A man who is thirsty reaches for water because his [[temperament|constitution]] demands it, and he cannot will himself to be otherwise constituted. Free will is thus not a faculty but an illusion generated by the opacity of our own motives.

## Historical Context

The [[liberty|free will]] debate in eighteenth-century philosophy was framed by the tension between [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian [[religion|theology]] — which required [[liberty|free will]] to justify [[punishment|divine punishment]] — and the rise of mechanical philosophy, which suggested a universe governed by deterministic laws. [[rene-descartes|Descartes]] had attempted to preserve libertarian [[liberty|freedom]] by ma[[monarch|king]] the will infinite while the intellect was finite, but [[baruch-spinoza|Spinoza]]'s more radical [[effect|determinism]] had already challenged this compromise.

Holbach wrote in a French intellectual milieu shaped by both the Spinozist revival and [[john-locke|Locke]]an empiricism. [[john-locke|Locke]]'s chapter "Of [[power|Power]]" in the *Essay Concerning Human [[mind|Understanding]]* (1689) had influentially analyzed the question of liberty, concluding that the will is determined by uneasiness, not by free indifference. Holbach radicalized this analysis by embedding it in a thoroughgoing [[matter|materialism]]: if the soul is nothing but the organized body, and the body obeys physical laws, then the will cannot escape the chain of natural [[cause|causation]]. The political stakes were high: if humans are not free, then the theological basis for [[hell|eternal punishment]] — that [[vice|sin]]ners freely chose [[hell|damnation]] — collapses.

## Core Arguments

1. **The will is determined by motives beyond our control.** "Man is never free. He is guided at each moment of his existence by the necessary laws of his organization and by the circumstances which modify him" (ch. 80). Our choices are always the product of our temperament, our education, our habits, and the situations we find ourselves in — none of which we freely chose.

2. **The experience of [[liberty|freedom]] is ignorance of causes.** We feel free because we are unaware of the multitude of causes that produce our [[passion|desire]]s. "The complex motion which we call a [[will|volition]] is determined by external objects, by internal dispositions, by [[government|the state]] of the brain, by the circulation of the blood, by the condition of the nerves; we are not masters of any of these things."

3. **Apparent choice between alternatives proves nothing.** When a person appears to deliberate and choose, they are simply weighing competing motives until the strongest prevails. "To prove to me that you are free, will have become to you a stronger motive than the [[passion|desire]] to remain seated" (ch. 80). The victor in this internal contest is always the strongest motive at that moment.

4. **Without [[liberty|free will]], [[punishment|divine punishment]] is unjust.** Holbach turns the [[liberty|free will]] doctrine against [[religion|theology]] itself: "If [[god|God]] could prevent the faults which He foresaw, and did not prevent them, it is evident that He consented to them. What becomes of the [[liberty|free will]] of man, if [[god|God]] is the author of all his actions?" (ch. 79). If [[god|God]] is omnipotent and omniscient, human [[liberty|freedom]] is impossible; if humans are not free, [[hell|eternal punishment]] is monstrous.

5. **[[necessity|Determinism]] does not destroy morality.** To the objection that denying [[liberty|free will]] makes humans into mere machines, Holbach replies that society retains the right to restrain harmful conduct: "To take away from man his [[liberty|free will]], is, we are told, to make of him a mere machine. But what does society do when it punishes a criminal? It treats him as a machine that one repairs or destroys when it becomes dangerous" (ch. 83).

## Reception and Critique

Holbach's [[effect|determinism]] provoked strong reactions. Rousseau, in *Émile* (1762), had already argued that the sentiment of [[liberty|freedom]] was irreducible and that to deny it was to deny the most immediate fact of consciousness. Kant would later make [[liberty|freedom]] the cornerstone of his [[morality|moral philosophy]], arguing that while the phenomenal self is determined, the noumenal self is free — a distinction Holbach would have dismissed as metaphysical wordplay.

The nineteenth-century debate over [[liberty|free will]] was heavily shaped by Holbach's arguments, [[eve]]n when they were not cited by name. Schopenhauer's essay "On the Freedom of the Will" (1839) reaches conclusions strikingly similar to Holbach's, though by a different philosophical route. The contemporary compatibilist tradition, from [[david-hume|Hume]] to Dennett, attempts to reconcile [[effect|determinism]] with a meaningful sense of [[liberty|freedom]], while hard determinists continue to press Holbach's case that the feeling of [[liberty|freedom]] is no [[evidence|proof]] of its reality.

## Relation to Other Concepts

Free will stands at the intersection of Holbach's metaphysics and [[morality|ethics]]. It is the linchpin of theological morality: if [[liberty|free will]] is an illusion, then [[Sin]] cannot be freely chosen and [[Hell]] becomes an injustice of cosmic proportions. The denial of [[liberty|free will]] follows directly from Holbach's concept of [[Necessity]] — the universal reign of [[effect|cause and effect]]. It connects to [[Nature]] as the deterministic system in which human action is embedded, and to [[Providence]] because [[divinity|divine]] foreknowledge is incompatible with human [[liberty|freedom]]. The concept also relates to [[Virtue]] and [[Vice]]: if actions are determined, moral evaluation must shift from praise and blame to causes and remedies.
