# Terror

## Definition

Terror occupies a central place in Holbach's critique of religion: it is the [[passion|emotion]]al mechanism through which theological beliefs are implanted, maintained, and exploited.
Where reason cannot persuade, [[fear|terror]] compels.
The idea of an invisible [[god|God]] who judges, punishes, and damns produces a distinctive kind of fear — one directed at an object that cannot be seen, interrogated, or appeased with certainty.

Holbach treats religious terror not as a benign check on human passions but as a systematic deformation of the mind.
"It is impossible to love a [[existence|being]], the thought of whom tends to excite [[fear|terror]], and whose judgments make us tremble" (002-104).
The [[god|God]] of [[religion|theology]] cannot be loved; He can only be feared.
And fear, unlike love, produces submission rather than moral [[progress|improvement]].

The political dimension is explicit: religious terror is manufactured by [[clergy|priests]] and legislators to make populations governable.
"Like nurses who frighten children in order to put them to sleep, ambitious men use the name of the gods to inspire fear in savages; [[fear|terror]] seems well suited to compel them to submit quietly to the yoke" (ch. 2).
Terror is not a side effect of religion — it is its operating principle.

## Historical Context

The eighteenth century inherited a [[christianity|Christianity]] in which fear of [[punishment|divine punishment]] was a [[model|standard]] instrument of moral education.
[[hell|Hell]]fire sermons, the threat of excommunication, and vivid depictions of eternal [[torture|torment]] were routine features of Catholic and Protestant piety alike.
Johnathan Edwards's "[[sin|Sin]]ners in the Hands of an Angry [[god|God]]" (1741) exemplified the [[fear|terror]]-based homiletic tradition that Holbach opposed.

The philosophical case against governing through fear had been made by Montesquieu, who distinguished [[tyranny|despotism]] (rule by fear) from [[monarch|monarchy]] (rule by honor) and republic (rule by [[morality|virtue]]).
Holbach radicalized this analysis by arguing that [[divinity|divine]] [[monarch|monarchy]] was the archetype of [[tyranny|despotism]] — the invisible [[monarch|sovereign]] whose conduct is unintelligible and whose punishments are inescapable.

The political context was urgent.
Louis XV's [[paris|France]] combined religious orthodoxy with absolutist [[government|governance]]; fear of [[god|God]] and fear of the [[monarch|king]] reinforced each other.
Holbach's attack on religious terror was simultaneously an attack on the psychological foundations of the *ancien régime*.

## Core Arguments

**1. Terror makes love of [[god|God]] impossible.**
Holbach insists that the theological [[god|God]] cannot be an object of genuine love.
"Let them not speak to us of a filial or respectful fear mingled with love, which men should have for their [[god|God]]. A son can not love his father when he knows he is cruel enough to inflict exquisite [[torture|torment]]s upon him" (ch. 2).
The attempt to combine love and [[fear|terror]] is a psycho[[contradiction|logical contradiction]].

**2. Religious [[fear|terror]] is a deliberate instrument of social control.**
Legislators and [[clergy|priests]] discovered that invisible punishments were more efficient than visible ones.
"The [[fear|terror]]s of another life are salutary terrors, and calculated to subdue men's passions" (ch. 2) — this is the apologetic defense that Holbach rejects.
The real function of [[fear|terror]] is to make populations docile, not virtuous.

**3. Terror does not improve conduct.**
Holbach asks whether fear of [[god|God]] actually restrains the powerful.
"The idea of an invisible Judge, to whom alone they pretend to be accountable for their actions, should inspire them with [[fear|terror]]! But does this [[fear|terror]] render them more equitable, more humane, less avaricious of the blood and the goods of their subjects?" (ch. 2).
The empirical answer is no: tyrants who profess piety are no less cruel than those who do not.

**4. Panic [[fear|terror]]s paralyze reason.**
"Blinded from childhood by error, held by the invincible ties of opinion, crushed by panic terrors, stupefied at the bosom of ignorance, how could the people understand the true causes of their troubles?" (ch. 2).
Terror keeps populations from recognizing that their real oppressors are earthly, not supernatural.

**5. Secular laws are more effective than supernatural fears.**
"The fear of the [[god|God]]s, religion, the [[fear|terror]]s of another life — these are the metaphysical and supernatural barriers which are opposed to the furious passions of [[monarch|prince]]s! Are these barriers sufficient?" (ch. 2).
Holbach answers that only institutional checks — laws that bind the powerful and the weak alike — can restrain human passions.

## Reception and Critique

Holbach's analysis of religious terror influenced the radical wing of [[revolution|the French Revolution]], particularly the de-[[jesus-christ|Christ]]ianization campaign of 1793–94, which sought to replace fear of [[god|God]] with fear of the law.
Robespierre's Cult of the [[being|Supreme Being]] was in part a response to Holbachian a[[divinity|theism]] — an attempt to retain the [[utility|social utility]] of religious [[passion|emotion]] without the [[religion|theology]].

Later psychological theory has complicated Holbach's picture.
[[will|Will]]iam James argued that [[fear|religious fear]] could be psychologically productive, not merely paralyzing.
Søren Kierkegaard's concept of "fear and trembling" treated religious [[fear|dread]] as a necessary stage of [[soul|spirit]]ual development.

Nevertheless, Holbach's core insight — that invisible [[fear|terror]]s serve visible power structures — has been taken up by later critics of religion, including Marx ("the opium of the people") and Freud (religion as infantile projection of the terrifying father).

## Relation to Other Concepts

Terror is the [[passion|emotion]]al correlate of [[authority]] — the subjective experience of [[existence|being]] subjected to inscrutable power.
It is weaponized by the [[priest]], who claims to mediate between the terrified believer and the terrifying [[god|God]].

Terror is the opposite of [[reason]]: where reason examines and questions, [[fear|terror]] submits and obeys.
It is closely linked to [[superstition]], which thrives in the cognitive environment that [[fear|terror]] creates — one in which critical thought is suspended and any improbable threat seems plausible.

The concept of [[hell]] is [[fear|terror]]'s theological concretization — a place of eternal [[torture|torment]] that gives the abstract fear of [[divinity|divine]] judgment a vivid and unforgettable image.
