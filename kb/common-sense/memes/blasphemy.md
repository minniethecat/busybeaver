# Blasphemy

## Definition

Blasphemy, in Holbach's analysis, is a crime that cannot be coherently defined. If [[god]] is an infinite [[existence|being]], he cannot be "offended" by the words of finite creatures; the very concept of [[divinity|divine]] [[crime|offense]] is an anthropomorphic projection that reduces [[god|God]] to a touchy [[despotism|despot]].
"If there exists a [[god|God]] who could be offended or blasphemed, there would not be upon earth any greater blasphemers than those who dare to say that this [[god|God]] is perverse enough to take pleasure in dooming his weak creatures to [[hell|eternal punishment]] (002-107)." The [[clergy|priests]] who threaten blasphemers with [[hell]] are themselves the greatest blasphemers, for they attribute to [[god|God]] a cruelty that would be condemned in any human tyrant.

Holbach's treatment of blasphemy is characteristic of his broader method: he does not defend the right to blaspheme but denies that the category has any coherent content. The charge of blasphemy is a social weapon, deployed to silence rational criticism by converting an intellectual disagreement into a moral outrage.

## Historical Context

Blasphemy was a capital crime in most [[europe|Europe]]an legal systems until well into the eighteenth century. The last execution for blasphemy in [[france|France]] occurred in 1766, when the Chevalier de La Barre was tortured, beheaded, and burned — with [[voltaire]]'s *Philosophical Dictionary* nailed to his body — for failing to salute a religious procession.
Holbach wrote *Common Sense* in the shadow of this execution, and his critique of blasphemy laws was not abstract philosophy but a response to judicial murder.

The concept of blasphemy was also central to the censorship apparatus that forced Holbach to publish anonymously and abroad. Books deemed blasphemous were burned by the public executioner; their authors, if identified, faced imprisonment or exile.
Holbach's argument that blasphemy is an empty category was, among other things, an argument for [[liberty]] of thought and publication — not because blasphemy should be tolerated, but because the very idea of blasphemy is incoherent and should be abandoned.

## Core Arguments

**1. The concept of blasphemy requires a [[god|God]] capable of [[existence|being]] offended, which is theologically [[absurdity|absurd]].** An infinite, [[perfection|perfect being]] cannot be injured by finite speech. The claim that [[god|God]] is "offended" by [[atheism|unbelief]] or criticism reduces him to the level of a human [[monarch|sovereign]] who demands flattery and punishes disrespect. The theologians who invented the category of blasphemy have constructed a [[god|God]] in their own image — petty, vain, and vindictive.

**2. The true blasphemers are the theologians themselves.** "If there exists a [[god|God]] who could be offended or blasphemed, there would not be upon earth any greater blasphemers than those who dare to say that this [[god|God]] is perverse enough to take pleasure in dooming his weak creatures to [[hell|eternal punishment]]." The doctrine of eternal [[damnation]] is the supreme blasphemy because it [[attribute|attributes]] infinite cruelty to a [[existence|being]] [[religion|theology]] claims is infinitely good.

**3. The cry of "blasphemy" is a mechanism of mass mobilization.** "[[person-index|People]], to arms! Your [[god|God]]'s cause is at stake! [[heaven|Heaven]] is outraged! [[faith|Faith]] is in danger! Down upon infidelity, blasphemy, and [[heresy]]!" (ch. CLXVII). These are not theological propositions but battle cries. The people "never understand" the words, but they reliably produce civil war, [[cruelty|persecution]], and murder. The charge of blasphemy is effective precisely because it bypasses reason and triggers the fanatic's reflex to violence.

**4. The content of "blasphemy" changes as knowledge advances.** Two centuries before Holbach, the claim that the earth revolves around the sun was considered blasphemy "because such a system could not agree with the Holy Book." Once the [[proof|evidence]] became irresistible, theologians quietly abandoned the charge. What was blasphemy yesterday is [[science]] today.
This [[demon|demons]]trates that "blasphemy" is not a stable category referring to a real [[crime|offense]] against [[god|God]] but a label attached to claims that threaten [[authority|ecclesiastical authority]] at a particular historical moment.

**5. Blasphemy laws are instruments of thought control.** By ma[[monarch|king]] certain propositions unutterable on [[suffering|pain]] of death, blasphemy laws protect religious claims from the rational scrutiny to which all other claims are subject.
The effect is to create a protected epistemic zone in which the most implausible assertions — virgin births, bodily resurrections, transubstantiation — cannot be questioned without ris[[monarch|king]] prosecution. This is not the protection of [[sacred|sanctity]] but the protection of [[imposture]].

## Reception and Critique

Holbach wrote *Common Sense* in the immediate aftermath of one of the most notorious judicial atrocities of the French ancien regime.
In 1766, the Chevalier de La Barre, a young man of nineteen, was condemned to death by the Parlement of [[paris|Paris]] for a series of alleged acts of blasphemy: failing to kneel before a Corpus [[jesus-christ|Christ]]i procession, [[guilt|sin]]ging irreverent songs, and possessing a copy of [[voltaire|Voltaire]]'s *Philosophical Dictionary*.
The sentence was gruesome [[eve]]n by the [[model|standard]]s of the time: La Barre was to have his tongue torn out, his right hand cut off, and his body burned at the stake — with [[voltaire|Voltaire]]'s book nailed to his torso. In a gesture of relatively lenient "mercy," the court commuted the sentence to beheading before burning. The execution was carried out in Abbeville on July 1, 1766.

This [[eve]]nt was not an isolated anomaly. Blasphemy prosecutions were a live reality in eighteenth-century [[europe|Europe]]. In Britain, the blasphemy laws had been used against [[woolston|Thomas Woolston]] in 1729 (sentenced to prison for questioning the literal truth of [[jesus-christ|Christ]]'s [[miracle|miracles]]) and would later be used against Thomas [[pain|Pain]]e.
In Geneva, the execution of Michel Servetus for anti-Trinitarian "blasphemy" in 1553 remained within living memory as a symbol of what theological orthodoxy was prepared to do to dissenters. Holbach's argument that blasphemy is an empty category was, first and foremost, an argument against the legal and physical violence that the category was used to justify.

Holbach's deeper philosophical point — that the cry of "blasphemy" is a mechanism for suppressing rational criticism by converting intellectual disagreement into moral outrage — anticipates arguments that would become central to the liberal defense of free expression in the nineteenth century.
John Stuart Mill's harm principle, formulated in *On [[liberty|Liberty]]* (1859), holds that the only justification for restricting speech is the prevention of harm to others. Holbach's argument is [[eve]]n more radical: it denies that speech about [[god|God]] *can* cause harm, because [[god|God]], if he exists as [[religion|theology]] describes him, cannot be harmed.
The crime of blasphemy is a crime without a victim — and a crime without a victim is not a crime but a pretext for [[cruelty|persecution]].
The concept of [[toleration]], as developed by [[john-locke|Locke]] and [[pierre-bayle|Bayle]], had argued that [[heresy|religious dissent]] should be permitted; Holbach's critique of blasphemy goes further, arguing that there is nothing to "tolerate" because there is no coherent [[crime|offense]] to have been committed in the first place.

Later legal developments vindicated Holbach's position, though not always for his reasons. Blasphemy laws were gradually abolished or fell into disuse across the Western [[universe|world]] from the late eighteenth century onward — [[revolution|the French Revolution]] decriminalized blasphemy in 1791, and most [[europe|Europe]]an states followed over the course of the nineteenth and twentieth centuries.
The philosophical justification for abolition, however, has more often rested on Millian arguments about the value of free speech than on Holbachian arguments about the conceptual [[contradiction|incoherence]] of blasphemy.
The distinction matters: a free-speech defense of blasphemy concedes that blasphemy is a meaningful category (speech that genuinely offends religious sensibilities) while arguing that it should be protected nonetheless.
Holbach's argument is that the category itself is [[absurdity|nonsense]], and that granting it conceptual legitimacy — [[eve]]n while defending it on liberal grounds — perpetuates the theologically incoherent idea that an infinite [[existence|being]] can be injured by finite words.

## Relation to Other Concepts

Blasphemy is, in Holbach's lexicon, the legal and social category that protects [[eve]]ry other theological concept from rational scrutiny. Without the threat of prosecution for blasphemy, the [[absurdity|absurd]]ities of [[heresy]] prosecution, [[sacrilege]] laws, and the [[inquisition]] could not have been sustained. [[heresy|Heresy]] is doctrinal error; blasphemy is the verbal expression of that error.
Sacrilege is the physical violation of sacred objects; blasphemy is the verbal violation of sacred concepts. Both are parasitic on the prior claim that certain ideas and objects are "sacred" — a claim that Holbach regards as an imposture from beginning to end.

The connection to [[free-thought]] is essential: free thought is the condition blasphemy laws are designed to prevent, and the history of free thought is the history of individuals who risked the charge of blasphemy in order to think and speak honestly.
Holbach's own career — publishing anonymously, smuggling books across borders, maintaining a perpetual pretense of orthodoxy — was a testament to what the category of blasphemy cost [[eve]]n the most privileged and protected of writers. The concept of [[toleration]] represents the moderate Enlightenment position that blasphemy, while perhaps real, should not be prosecuted.
Holbach represents the radical position that blasphemy, as a category, should be abandoned — not tolerated but dismissed as meaningless. The [[morality|virtue]] of philosophical [[courage]], which Holbach implicitly demands of his readers, is the willingness to speak what one thinks in the face of a charge that has no coherent content but very real consequences.

## In the Text

On the true identity of the blasphemer:

> "If there exists a God who could be offended or blasphemed, there would not be upon earth any greater blasphemers than those who dare to say that this God is perverse enough to take pleasure in dooming his weak creatures to eternal punishment." (002-107)

On blasphemy as a mobilization cry:

> "People, to arms! Your God's cause is at stake! Heaven is outraged! Faith is in danger! Down upon infidelity, blasphemy, and heresy! By the magical power of these valiant words, which the people never understand, the priests in all ages were the leaders in the revolts of nations, in dethroning kings, in kindling civil wars, and in imprisoning men." (002-369)

On blasphemy as a historically unstable category:

> "Does the earth revolve around the sun? Two centuries ago a devout philosopher would have replied that such a thought was blasphemy, because such a system could not agree with the Holy Book, which every Christian reveres as inspired by the Deity Himself." (002-443)

On Meslier's "blasphemies" as rational critique:

> "The 'Last Will of Jean Meslier, — that famous Curate of Etrepigni, — whose apostasy and blasphemies made so strong an impression on the minds of the populace.'" (Voltaire, Preface)
