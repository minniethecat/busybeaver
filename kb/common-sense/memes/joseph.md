# Joseph

## Biography

Joseph (Hebrew: Yosef) is a major figure in the Book of Genesis (chapters 37–50), the eleventh son of [[jacob|Jacob]] and the firstborn of Rachel. According to the biblical narrative, he was his father's favorite son, marked by the gift of a "coat of many colors." His brothers, [[desire|drive]]n by envy, sold him into slavery and told [[jacob|Jacob]] he had been killed by a wild [[soul|anima]]l. Joseph was taken to [[egypt|Egypt]], where he rose from slave to overseer in the house of Potiphar, was falsely accused by Potiphar's wife and imprisoned, and ultimately interpreted Pharaoh's dreams — predicting seven years of plenty followed by seven years of famine. Elevated to vizier of [[egypt|Egypt]], he administered the grain storage that saved [[egypt|Egypt]] and surrounding [[nation|nations]] from starvation, and was [[eve]]ntually reconciled with his brothers and father.

The Joseph narrative is one of the longest continuous stories in Genesis and has been read variously as a [[philosophy|wisdom]] tale, a diaspora novella, and a theological meditation on [[providence|divine providence]] wor[[monarch|king]] through human [[evil|evil]]. Its literary sophistication — the use of dramatic irony, repetitive thematic structures, and psychological realism — sets it apart from the surrounding patriarchal narratives and has led many scholars to date it to the Persian or early [[hell|Hell]]enistic period rather than the patriarchal age it describes.

## Role in Religious History

Joseph occupies a pivotal position in the biblical metanarrative. His descent into [[egypt|Egypt]] sets the stage for the [[israel|Israel]]ite sojourn and [[eve]]ntual enslavement that will require [[moses|Moses]]'s liberation and the Exodus. In [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian typology, Joseph prefigures [[jesus-christ|Christ]]: the beloved son, betrayed by his own, who suffers unjustly and is ultimately exalted to save not only his people but the surrounding [[nation|nations]].

In Jewish tradition, Joseph represents the possibility of [[religion|faith]]ful [[existence|living]] in diaspora — maintaining identity and integrity while achieving success in a foreign [[worship|cult]]ure. His [[egypt|Egypt]]ian name, Zaphenath-paneah, and his marriage to an [[egypt|Egypt]]ian woman make him a liminal figure, both insider and outsider to the [[nations|nation]] he saves.

In [[islam|Islam]]ic tradition, Yusuf is the subject of the Qur'an's twelfth surah, described as "the most beautiful of stories." The Qur'anic account emphasizes Joseph's chastity in the face of Potiphar's wife's seduction and his patient endurance of unjust [[evil|suffering]] as [[model|exemplar]]y of [[prophecy|prophetic]] character.

## Connection to "Common Sense"

Holbach references Joseph in the context of his broader critique of biblical reliability. In the appendix to *Common Sense*, Joseph appears alongside other patriarchs in Holbach's catalog of biblical figures whose claimed [[revelation|divine revelations]] cannot withstand rational exami[[nations|nation]].

The Joseph story, for all its literary merit, presents Holbach with the same epistemological problem as the other patriarchal narratives: a private dream, interpreted by one man, becomes the basis for a claim of [[providence|divine guidance]]. Pharaoh's dream, Joseph's interpretation, and the subsequent elevation of Joseph to vizier are, in Holbach's framework, a chain of claims no more verifiable than the [[dreams|visions]] of [[abraham|Abraham]] or the wrestling of [[jacob|Jacob]].

Holbach's treatment of Joseph is not extensive — the patriarch appears primarily in lists alongside [[abraham|Abraham]], [[isaac|Isaac]], and [[jacob|Jacob]] — but the logic of his critique applies with equal force. If Joseph's dream interpretation proves [[divinity|divine]] inspiration, then [[eve]]ry claimed interpreter of dreams in [[eve]]ry [[worship|cult]]ure makes the same claim on the same [[proof|evidence]]. The biblical canon's acceptance of Joseph's dream and rejection of others' is, for Holbach, an arbitrary act of tradition, not a rational discrimi[[nations|nation]].

> "The vocation and the choice which God made of the Patriarchs, Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob, in order to make for Himself of their posterity a people which He would sanctify and bless above all other peoples of the earth, is a proof of it." (ch. II)

The Joseph story's function in the larger biblical narrative — to explain how the [[israel|Israelites]] came to be in [[egypt|Egypt]] — is, on Holbach's reading, an etiological myth of the sort found in [[eve]]ry [[worship|cult]]ure, dressed in the language of [[providence|providence]] to serve theological ends.

## Key Ideas Attributed / Critiqued

**1. [[providence|Providence]] as retrospective narration.** The Joseph story is the biblical locus classicus of providential [[religion|theology]] — "you meant [[evil|evil]] against me, but [[god|God]] meant it for good" (Genesis 50:20). For Holbach, this is not [[proof|evidence]] of [[providence|divine guidance]] but of the human tendency to reinterpret past [[suffering|suffering]] as part of a meaningful plan. Any outcome can be retroactively described as providential; the description adds nothing to the facts.

**2. Dream interpretation as revelation.** Joseph's rise from prisoner to vizier hinges entirely on his ability to interpret dreams. Holbach would class this with [[eve]]ry other claim of supernatural knowledge: a private experience (Joseph's [[knowledge|understanding]] of the dream's meaning) is offered as public truth (agricultural policy for a [[nations|nation]]). There is no test, no verification, and no criterion by which a true dream interpretation can be distinguished from a false one.

**3. [[theology-index|Divine]] [[predestination|election]] and moral luck.** The Joseph narrative presents the patriarch's [[evil|suffering]] as part of a [[providence|divine plan]]. Holbach sees instead a morally troubling [[model|pattern]]: if [[god|God]] orchestrates Joseph's enslavement to save [[egypt|Egypt]] from famine, then the same [[god|God]] is responsible for the brothers' treachery, Potiphar's wife's false accusation, and years of unjust imprisonment. The [[injustice|theodicy]] that exonerates [[god|God]] for these [[evil|evil]]s by appeal to a greater good is, for Holbach, a rationalization that would not be accepted in any non-religious context.

## Legacy

The Joseph narrative has had an enormous [[worship|cult]]ural [[death|afterlife]], inspiring works by Thomas Mann (*Joseph and His Brothers*), Andrew Lloyd Webber (*Joseph and the Amazing Technicolor Dreamcoat*), and countless visual artists from the Italian Renaissance onward. Its themes of sibling rivalry, unjust [[evil|suffering]], and ultimate reconciliation give it a psychological depth that few other biblical narratives match.

In the history of biblical criticism, the Joseph story has been central to debates about the composition of the Pentateuch. The narrative's literary unity and [[egypt|Egypt]]ian local color set it apart from the surrounding patriarchal material; its relationship to the broader Documentary Hypothesis remains a subject of scholarly discussion.
