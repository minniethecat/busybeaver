## Definition and Context

Holbach identifies the zealot as a recurring figure in religious life — the person whose devotion has become all-consuming, transforming faith from private conviction into public menace. The zealot is, in his analysis, produced by the same psychological mechanisms that generate fanatics and saints.

## The Making of a Zealot

Holbach traces the zealot's psychology to religion's manipulation of human passions:

> ...the materials of which devotees, zealots, fanatics, and saints are made... (002-275)

In his view, the zealot, fanatic, and saint share a common psychological origin — intense religious conditioning that overrides natural human sympathies and rational judgment. The difference between them is one of degree and social labeling rather than fundamental nature.

## Zealotry and Social Harm

For Holbach, religious zeal is not merely a personal disposition but a social danger. The zealot, convinced of divine sanction, becomes capable of acts that would otherwise be recognized as cruel or unjust. The history of religious persecution is, in his account, the history of zealotry given theological justification.

## The Manufacture of Zealots

Holbach's most unsettling claim about zealotry is that it is not spontaneous — it is manufactured. Religious systems systematically produce zealots by isolating adherents from natural human sympathies and substituting divine commands for moral reasoning. The same "materials" — credulity, fear, and the desire for certainty — "of which devotees, zealots, fanatics, and saints are made" (138-002) are shaped by clerical institutions into whatever form serves their interests. The zealot is thus not born but made: a human being whose natural capacity for devotion has been redirected toward destructive ends by a system that benefits from his fanaticism. In Holbach's analysis, to condemn the zealot without condemning the system that creates him is to mistake the symptom for the disease.
