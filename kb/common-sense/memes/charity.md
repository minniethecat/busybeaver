# Charity

## Definition

Charity, in Holbach's critique, is the religious [[morality|virtue]] of alms-giving and love of neighbor that [[christianity|Christianity]] presents as an essential duty. While Holbach acknowledges that assisting the unfortunate is praiseworthy in itself, he argues that Christian charity functions as a systemic [[imposture|deception]] — it addresses the symptoms of misery while leaving its structural causes untouched, and it evaporates entirely when confronted with doctrinal disagreement.

For Holbach, true beneficence would consist not in building hospitals for the poor but in governing justly so that poverty does not arise in the first place. Charity, as practiced by [[church|the Church]], becomes a mechanism for sustaining [[authority|clerical power]] over the miserable rather than a genuine remedy for human [[evil|suffering]].

## Historical Context

The eighteenth century saw the climax of a long debate over the social role of charity. The [[catholic-church|Catholic Church]] had for centuries administered vast charitable institutions — hospitals, orphanages, poor relief — funded by pious do[[nation|nations]] and staffed by religious orders. This system was increasingly criticized by Enlightenment thinkers. [[voltaire|Voltaire]] mocked [[church|the Church]]'s charitable pretensions in *Candide*; the Physiocrats argued that poor relief encouraged idleness; and [[adam|Adam]] Smith, while more nuanced, distinguished between benevolence and justice as distinct [[morality|virtue]]s with different social functions.

Holbach's treatment, in *Common Sense* (1772), represents the most radical Enlightenment critique of religious charity. He applies the same materialist lens to charity that he applies to all religious [[morality|virtue]]s: if actions are determined by natural causes and [[interest|self-interest]], then the charitable institutions of [[church|the Church]] must be analyzed in terms of the power and wealth they generate for [[clergy|the clergy]], not the piety they ostensibly express.

## Core Arguments

**1. Charity disappears at the first sign of dissent.** Holbach observes that Christian charity — the "tender love of our neighbor" — vanishes the moment anyone questions orthodoxy. "Ask them if you must love your neighbor if he is impious, [[heresy|heretic]]al, and incredulous... Immediately their charity disappears." The [[priest|clergy]] will then justify [[cruelty|persecution]], imprisonment, exile, or burning — all in the name of loving one's neighbor.

**2. Prevention is better than alms.** The deeper critique is structural: "Would it not be more humane and more charitable to foresee the misery and to prevent the poor from increasing?" Holbach argues that if religion taught [[monarch|ruler]]s to respect property, govern justly, and avoid oppressive taxation and senseless wars, there would be far fewer mendicants in need of charity. Religious charity, by focusing on remediation rather than prevention, becomes complicit in the very system that produces misery.

**3. Charity as a mechanism of [[authority|clerical power]].** The [[church|Church]] benefits from public misery: "The ministers of [[heaven|Heaven]] have always known how to benefit themselves by the calamities of others; public misery became their element." By positioning themselves as "administrators of the goods of the poor" and "distributors of alms," [[clergy|the clergy]] extend their power over "the most numerous, the most anxious, the most seditious part of society."

## In the Text

Holbach's most concentrated critique appears in the chapter "What Does That [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian Charity Amount To, Such As Theologians Teach and Practice?" (169-049):

> "When we reproach the theologians with the sterility of their religious virtues, they praise, with emphasis, charity, that tender love of our neighbor which Christianity makes an essential duty for its disciples. But, alas! what becomes of this pretended charity as soon as we examine the actions of the Lord's ministers?"

On the structural critique of charity (169-049):

> "If the sovereigns had more humanity, charity, and justice, their States would not be peopled by so many unfortunate ones whose misery becomes impossible to soothe. ... Religion, by its virtues, has but given a change to men; instead of foreseeing evils, it applies but insufficient remedies (169-050)."

On clerical exploitation of charity (ch. CLXIX):

> "Thus the greatest evils are made profitable to the ministers of the Lord."

## Relation to Other Concepts

Charity is closely linked to [[virtue]] and [[morality]], which Holbach grounds in [[utility|social utility]] rather than religious command. It relates to [[justice]] — the [[virtue|secular virtue]] that Holbach considers superior to charity because it addresses causes rather than symptoms. The concept stands in opposition to [[priestcraft]], the institutional system that profits from [[suffering|human misery]], and connects to [[despotism]], the political condition that produces the poverty charity purports to reli[[eve]].
