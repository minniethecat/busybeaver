# Order

## Definition

In "Common Sense," Holbach distinguishes between two conceptions of order. The first, which he defends, is the observable regularity of [[universe|nature]]: the fact that matter in motion produces stable, predictable [[model|pattern]]s — planetary orbits, seasons, biological reproduction, psychological regularities. "The universe is regulated by invariable laws" (ch. LVI). This order is not imposed from without but emerges from the properties of matter itself.

The second conception, which Holbach attacks, is the theological notion of "divine order" — the idea that the regularities of [[universe|nature]] are [[proof|evidence]] of a [[divinity|divine]] lawgiver who designed and maintains [[earth|the world]]. This [[design|argument from design]], advanced most influentially by [[will|Will]]iam Paley and other natural theologians, is for Holbach a logical inversion: we observe order and then imagine an orderer, but the order we observe is simply what matter *does*. The concept of a universe without order is unintelligible — chaos, too, would be a kind of order, just one destructive to life.

## Historical Context

The [[design|argument from design]] was the most popular argument for the existence of [[god|God]] in the 18th century — far more widely invoked than the abstract ontological or cosmological proofs. [[isaac-newton|Newton]]'s physics, by revealing the mathematical elegance of the laws of motion, had seemed to strengthen this argument: if [[nature|the universe]] is a clock, there must be a clockmaker. [[voltaire|Voltaire]], though a fierce critic of organized religion, accepted the design argument. Rousseau's "Profession of [[faith|Faith]] of the Savoyard Vicar" (in *Émile*, 1762) used the order of [[universe|nature]] as the foundation of [[theism|natural religion]].

Holbach's counter-argument was radical: order is not a property that [[universe|nature]] *has* but a relation that observers *perceive*. "Order and disorder are not in [[universe|nature]]; they are only in our mind" (ch. LVII). What we call "order" is simply the arrangement of matter that is compatible with our existence — we call it ordered because we survive in it. A different arrangement would kill us, not violate a [[divinity|divine]] plan.

## Core Arguments

**1. Order is inherent in matter, not imposed by a designer.** The properties of matter — attraction, repulsion, chemical affinity — produce regular [[model|pattern]]s without any external direction. "[[matter|Matter]], by its own energy, produces all the phenomena we admire" (ch. LVII). To argue that order requires an orderer is to beg the question: it assumes that matter is inherently disordered, which experience does not confirm.

**2. The [[design|argument from design]] proves nothing.** The fact that the eye is suited to seeing no more proves [[design|divine design]] than the fact that a river is suited to its banks proves that a surveyor laid out the river's course. Adaptation is the result of natural causes operating over time; we see the survivors and call their features "well-designed" because the alternatives are no longer around to compare.

**3. What we call "disorder" is just order we dislike.** [[earth|Earth]]quakes, diseases, and death are not violations of natural law — they are natural law operating exactly as it always does. Calling them "disorder" or "[[injustice|evil]]" is a human judgment, not a physical fact. "[[nature|Nature]] follows its course without regard to our convenience."

**4. The need for a divine orderer creates more problems than it solves.** If order requires an orderer, then the orderer's own order requires a meta-orderer, leading to infinite regress. Theologians stop the regress by declaring [[god|God]] self-ordered — but if self-order is possible for [[god|God]], why not for matter?

## Reception and Critique

Holbach's critique of the design argument anticipated [[david-hume|Hume]]'s more famous treatment in the *Dialogues Concerning Natural [[religion|Religion]]* (published posthumously in 1779, though written earlier). Both argued that the analogy between [[nature|the universe]] and a human artifact is weak: we have experience of clocks coming from clockmakers, but no experience of universes coming from universe-makers from which to generalize.

Darwin's theory of evolution by natural s[[predestination|election]] (1859) would later provide the mechanism that the 18th-century materialists lacked: a natural process that produces adaptation without a designer. In this sense, Holbach's philosophical argument was vindicated by a scientific discovery he could not have anticipated.

## Relation to Other Concepts

Order is the framework within which Holbach's other concepts operate. [[Nature]], understood as matter in motion obeying fixed laws, produces the order we observe. [[Reason]] is the human faculty that discerns this order. [[Existence]] is matter arranged in some order; there is no existence that is not ordered in some way. The concept of [[Divinity]] arises from the mistaken inference that order requires an orderer. [[Science]] is the systematic study of [[nature|natural order]], and [[Superstition]] is the projection of intentional agency onto natural regularities.
