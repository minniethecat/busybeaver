## Preaching and the Propagation of Error

Holbach devotes sustained attention to the social function of preaching in *Common Sense*. He portrays preaching not as the sincere communication of truth but as a mechanism for transmitting doctrines that cannot withstand rational scrutiny. Missionaries "preach their doctrine at the risk even of their lives" (002-261), but Holbach insists that the willingness to die for a belief proves nothing about its truth: "An enthusiast in dying proves nothing but that religious fanaticism is often stronger than the love of life."

## The Hypocrisy of Preachers

A central theme in Holbach's treatment of preaching is the gap between what preachers profess and how they live. He asks pointedly whether those who preach morality actually practice it: "they teach a devotedness to the will of God, of which there is, perhaps, not a single perfect example among those who preach it to others" (002-388). The religion that "preaches externally but charity, harmony, and forgetfulness of injuries" (002-320) is betrayed by its own advocates, who persecute dissenters and accumulate wealth while counseling poverty to their followers.

## The Social Origins of Preaching

Holbach offers a materialist account of how preaching emerged as an institution. Christianity "was originally preached by beggars and by very wretched persons" (002-357) who, having nothing, promised everything. The poverty of the first preachers was not a virtue but a circumstance — and their promises of heavenly reward were compensation for their lack of earthly goods. Holbach traces the transformation of these humble origins into the wealthy ecclesiastical establishments of his own day, noting that the "successors to these voluntary poor" became "rich and powerful" by selling their prayers and intercession.

## Preaching as Political Instrument

For Holbach, preaching has always served political ends. The clergy "preached it to the people, reserving always the right of granting or refusing absolution from this sacred oath" (002-291) — preaching the Divine right of kings when it served ecclesiastical interests, while retaining the power to release the people from their allegiance when it did not. Preaching, in this analysis, is never neutral; it is always deployed in service of institutional power.

## From *Common Sense*

> We incessantly repeat that the Christian religion is Divine, and we never cease to insist upon the conduct of those who preach them to others. Do they not themselves openly contradict them by their conduct?
>
> (002-388)
