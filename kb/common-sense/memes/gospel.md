# Gospel

## Definition

In Holbach's usage, "Gospel" refers both to the four canonical texts of the [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian [[testament|New Testament]] and, more broadly, to the moral [[doctrine|teaching]]s attributed to [[jesus-christ|Jesus Christ]].
His treatment is uniformly critical: the [[bible|gospels]] are human documents, historically unreliable, internally contradictory, and morally impracticable.

Holbach approaches the [[bible|gospels]] through the lens of Enlightenment textual criticism.
Noting that early [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian communities possessed multiple [[bible|gospels]] with incompatible contents, he argues that the survival of four canonical texts — out of many more — was a matter of political s[[predestination|election]], not [[divinity|divine]] preservation.
The existence of conflicting [[bible|gospels]], each claiming apostolic authority, proves that no [[vice|sin]]gle text carried the unambiguous mark of revelation.

The moral [[doctrine|teaching]]s of the [[bible|gospels]] receive separate treatment.
Holbach acknowledges their surface austerity — the commands to renounce wealth, turn the other cheek, and bless persecutors — but argues that this very rigor is [[proof|evidence]] against their [[divinity|divine]] origin.
A morality "impracticable and beyond the power of man" (162-002) cannot have been designed for human [[existence|being]]s by a benevolent creator who understands human [[universe|nature]].

## Historical Context

Eighteenth-century biblical criticism had already eroded the traditional view of the [[bible|gospels]] as harmonious, eyewitness accounts.
[[pierre-bayle|Pierre Bayle]]'s *Historical and Critical Dictionary* (1697) catalogued discrepancies among gospel narratives.
Richard Simon's critical histories of the Old and [[testament|New Testament]]s showed that the biblical text had a complex history of compilation and transmission.

Deist writers, particularly the English deists (Toland, Tindal, Collins), argued that the [[bible|gospels]] contained a simple rational religion buried under layers of priestly corruption.
Holbach went further than the deists: for him, the gospel narratives were not a corruption of an original pure message but fabrications from the start — "romances" composed by enthusiasts in a credulous age.

The political dimension was acute.
The [[catholic-church|Catholic Church]] in [[paris|France]] claimed its authority from apostolic succession, which depended on the reliability of the gospel accounts of Jesus's commission to [[peter|Peter]].
To attack the [[bible|gospels]]' historical credibility was to attack the foundation of ecclesiastical power.

## Core Arguments

**1. The multiplicity of [[bible|gospels]] proves their human origin.**
Early [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ians used many [[bible|gospels]] beyond the four canonical texts: "The Marcionites published a Gospel under the name of [[matthias|St. Matthias]]... The Manicheans wrote a gospel of their own style" (Appendix 1).
When communities cannot agree on which texts are authentic, the claim that any particular one is [[divinity|divine]]ly inspired becomes arbitrary.

**2. The surviving [[bible|gospels]] were selected by political contest, not by [[divinity|divine]] guidance.**
"It is for [[church|the Church]] and the councils to decide which are the true Gospels. Bodies of [[clergy|priests]], after many disputes, have decided that it is necessary to adhere to the four Gospels and the Epistles of St. [[paul|Paul]]" (Appendix 1).
What is presented as [[revelation|divine revelation]] is in fact the outcome of [[sect|faction]]al struggle among competing [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian groups.

**3. Gospel morality is impracticable.**
"The votaries of [[jesus-christ|Christ]] would like to make us regard as a miracle the establishment of their religion, which is in [[eve]]ry respect contrary to [[universe|nature]], opposed to all the incli[[nation|nations]] of the heart, an enemy to physical pleasures" (162-002).
A moral code that cannot be practiced by ordinary human [[existence|being]]s is not a guide to life but a performance of piety.

**4. The admiration of gospel morality is performative, not practical.**
"All the [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ians continually admire the morals of the Gospel, but it is practiced but by a small number of [[saint|saints]]; admired by people who themselves avoid imitating their conduct" (162-002).
The gap between professed admiration and actual conduct exposes gospel morality as an [[model|ideal]] invoked for display, not a [[model|standard]] enforced in practice.

**5. A society that actually followed gospel morality would collapse.**
"A [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian who would attain perfection ought to [[desire|drive]] away from his mind all that can alienate him from [[hope|heaven]] — his true country... The sublime morality of [[jesus-christ|Christ]], if it were not impracticable, would sever all the ties of society" (162-002).
Holbach's point is not that [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ians are hypoc[[ritual|rites]] (though they are), but that gospel morality is structurally incompatible with [[happiness|human flourishing]].

## Reception and Critique

Holbach's critique of the [[bible|gospels]] was more radical than that of most Enlightenment contemporaries.
[[voltaire|Voltaire]] attacked [[priest|priestcraft]] and superstition but retained a deistic admiration for a purified [[christianity|Christianity]].
Rousseau's "Savoyard Vicar" professed reverence for the moral sublimity of the [[bible|gospels]] [[eve]]n while rejecting their miraculous elements.

Holbach's position — that the [[bible|gospels]] are not just historically unreliable but morally harmful — anticipated the more thoroughgoing critiques of the nineteenth century, including [[david|David]] Friedrich Strauss's *[[life|Life]] of Jesus* (1835) and Nietzsche's attack on [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian morality as slave morality.

Critics have noted that Holbach's reading of gospel [[morality|ethics]] is selective: he emphasizes the impractical commands while ignoring the parables and [[philosophy|wisdom]] sayings that have historically guided [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian communities in practical moral reasoning.
His treatment of gospel morality as a monolithic system understates the diversity of ethical perspectives within the [[testament|New Testament]] itself.

## Relation to Other Concepts

The gospel is the textual foundation of [[christianity]] — to discredit the [[bible|gospels]] is to discredit the religion they authorize.
It is closely related to [[revelation]], the claim that certain texts convey [[divinity|divine]] communication, which Holbach rejects on epistemological grounds.

Gospel morality is an instance of the broader category of religious [[morality]], which Holbach argues is always excessive, anti-natural, and destructive of human welfare.
The gospel's demand for ascetic self-denial relates it to [[virtue]] — or rather to the counterfeit of [[morality|virtue]] that prizes self-[[torture|torment]] over [[utility|social utility]].

The s[[predestination|election]] of the canonical [[bible|gospels]] by church councils implicates the [[priest]] as the arbiter of what counts as sacred text.
The [[bible|gospels]]' contradictions point toward the broader theme of theological [[contradiction]] that Holbach explores throughout his work.
