# Interest

## Definition

In Holbach's "Common Sense," interest — properly understood self-interest — is not a vice but the fundamental spring of all human action. Every person, he argues, acts from the [[passion|desire]] for [[happiness|well-being]] and the aversion to [[evil|suffering]]; this is not a moral failing but a law of [[universe|nature]]. "To know their best interests and the real motives which will lead them to happiness" (ch. II) is the proper aim of [[morality|moral philosophy]]. The problem is not that people pursue their interests, but that they are deceived about what their true interests are.

Holbach distinguishes between enlightened self-interest — which recognizes that individual happiness depends on social cooperation — and the narrow, short-sighted selfishness that religion paradoxically encourages by [[doctrine|teaching]] that earthly interests are contemptible compared to [[hope|heaven]]ly rewards. The [[priest|clergy]], he observes, are masters at exploiting this confusion: "Men are imaginary patients, whom interested charlatans take care to encourage in their weakness, in order to have a market for their remedies" (ch. II).

The concept of "disinterestedness" comes under particular scrutiny. What appears as disinterested zeal — the [[missionaries|missionary]] who risks death to spread his doctrine — is, upon exami[[nations|nation]], a calculated pursuit of reward: "A [[missionaries|missionary]] seeks [[chance|fortune]] by the aid of his doctrine; he knows that if he has the good [[chance|fortune]] to retail his merchandise, he will become the object of his chiefs' [[goodness|benevolence]]" (ch. CXXXIII). No human action, Holbach insists, is [[eve]]r truly disinterested; the question is only whether the interest [[existence|being]] served is rational or illusory.

## Historical Context

Holbach's theory of interest belongs to the Enlightenment tradition of ethical [[materialism|naturalism]], which sought to ground morality in observable human psychology rather than [[divinity|divine]] command. [[hobbes|Hobbes]] had argued that self-preservation was the fundamental human [[desire|drive]]; Mandeville had scandalized readers by claiming that private vices produced public benefits; Helvétius had made self-interest the explicit foundation of all morality. Holbach drew on all three but gave their insights a sharper anti-religious edge.

Where Mandeville celebrated the paradox of private vice yielding public [[morality|virtue]], Holbach insisted that enlightened self-interest leads directly to [[morality|virtue]] — because the individual who understands his true interests recognizes that he needs the goodwill of others to flourish. [[crime|Crime]] and vice are not failures of morality but failures of calculation: the criminal misidentifies his own interest, pursuing a short-term gain at the cost of long-term security and social peace. This position prefigures utilitarian [[morality|ethics]] and the later arguments of Bentham and Mill.

## Core Arguments

**1. Self-interest is the universal motive.** All human action, Holbach argues, can be traced to the pursuit of pleasure or the avoidance of [[suffering|pain]]. This is not a cynical claim but a descriptive one: [[eve]]n the martyr and the ascetic act from interest — the interest in [[hope|heaven]]ly reward or [[honor|social esteem]]. The vocabulary of "disinterestedness" is a disguise that conceals the real motives [[eve]]n from the agent himself.

**2. [[religion|Religion]] exploits the confusion about interest.** By [[doctrine|teaching]] that [[universe|world]]ly interests are [[sin|sinful]] and that true happiness lies in an [[death|afterlife]], religion creates a population that cannot accurately assess its own interests. This confusion is then exploited by "self-interested guides" (ch. CXCVII) — [[clergy|the clergy]] — who profit from the fears they [[worship|cult]]ivate.

**3. Enlightened interest leads to [[morality|virtue]].** When people understand that their [[happiness|well-being]] depends on social cooperation — that "[[morality|virtue]] is an advantage, and that vice is an injury" (ch. II) — they will pursue their interests in ways that benefit others. [[morality|Morality]] does not require the suppression of self-interest; it requires the [[education|enlightenment]] of it.

**4. [[society|Society]] should be organized around real interests.** "If for its own interests society consults reason and justice, it will understand that it need not fear the passions of men" (ch. CXCVII). The task of government is not to make people moral through religion but to align individual interest with collective [[happiness|well-being]] through rational laws and institutions.

## Relation to Other Concepts

Interest is the psychological foundation of Holbach's entire ethical system. It connects to [[Virtue]] — which is enlightened interest in action — and stands opposed to [[Superstition]], which is the systematic [[imposture|deception]] of people about their true interests. [[Reason]] is the faculty that allows a person to calculate genuine interest correctly; [[Prejudice]] is the clouding of that calculation by religious and social conditioning. [[Morality]] without religion is simply the [[knowledge|science]] of human interests properly understood. [[Happiness]] is [[government|the state]] that results when interest is pursued successfully.

## In the Text

On self-interest as the true motive of all action:

> "Men are imaginary patients, whom interested charlatans take care to encourage in their weakness, in order to have a market for their remedies. Physicians who order a great number of remedies for slight diseases, are like priests who hold sinners always in need of supernatural remedies." (ch. II)

On the illusion of disinterestedness:

> "This disinterestedness is only apparent. 'Nothing ventured, nothing gained!' A missionary seeks fortune by the aid of his doctrine; he knows that if he has the good fortune to retail his merchandise, he will become the object of his chiefs' benevolence." (ch. CXXXIII)

On the social function of enlightened interest:

> "If we should succeed in destroying their prejudices, we would but diminish or annihilate the dangerous confidence which they have in self-interested guides, and teach them to beware of those who, under the pretext of religion, very often lead them into fatal excesses." (ch. CXCVII)
