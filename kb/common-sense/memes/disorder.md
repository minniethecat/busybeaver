# disorder

**Disorder** — the apparent chaos and confusion in the world — is a key theme in Holbach's critique of divine providence. He argues that the existence of disorder contradicts the claim that a benevolent, all-powerful God governs the universe: if God is "the friend of order," why is "everything in his universe in a state of confusion and disorder"? (002-028)

## Disorder as Refutation of Providence

Holbach repeatedly returns to the problem of disorder as an empirical refutation of divine government. "The world is full of evils, although disorder reigns here very often" (002-078), he observes, noting that "order is replaced by disorder; generation is followed by destruction" (002-079). A universe governed by a wise and benevolent deity would exhibit regularity and beneficence, but the observable world shows precisely the opposite: "everything is in disorder in a world governed by a God" (002-146). The universal experience of chaos, suffering, and confusion stands as a standing refutation of the providential hypothesis.

## The Subjectivity of Disorder

Holbach complicates the concept by recognizing that what we call disorder may be a function of human perspective rather than an objective feature of reality. "Our organization alone constitutes what we call order and disorder," he argues; "all is always in order; there can be no disorder for nature" (002-054). From the standpoint of the universe itself — considered as a material system operating according to fixed laws — there is no disorder, only the necessary unfolding of causes and effects. What humans experience as chaos is simply the collision of forces that do not happen to serve human interests.

## The Moral Implications

This analysis has important consequences for Holbach's moral and political philosophy. If disorder is not a divine punishment but a natural phenomenon, then human beings bear the responsibility for remedying it. "Who would permit themselves to follow their disordered appetites" (002-131) if they understood that suffering results not from supernatural wrath but from natural causes? The recognition that disorder has material rather than theological origins is the first step toward establishing genuine order through reason, science, and just social organization.

## Selected Passage

The book opens with Holbach's celebrated parable of the invisible monarch — an allegory of humanity's relationship with an unknowable God:

> There is a vast empire governed by a monarch, whose conduct does but confound the minds of his subjects. He desires to be known, loved, respected, and obeyed, but he never shows himself; everything tends to make uncertain the notions which we are able to form about him. The people subjected to his power have only such ideas of the character and the laws of their invisible sovereign as his ministers give them; these suit, however, because they themselves have no idea of their master, for his ways are impenetrable, and his views and his qualities are totally incomprehensible.
>
> (001-001)
