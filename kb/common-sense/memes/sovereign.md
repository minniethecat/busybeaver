# Sovereign

## Definition

In Holbach's vocabulary, "[[monarch|sovereign]]" bridges [[religion|theology]] and politics.
It describes [[god|God]] as the [[despotism|absolute rule]]r of [[nature|the universe]] and earthly monarchs who claim [[divinity|divine]] sanction.

Holbach treats this convergence as [[proof|evidence]] that religious and political [[tyranny|despotism]] share a common logic.
Both demand unconditional obedience without offering intelligible reasons for their commands.

Holbach's [[monarch|sovereign]] is not a figure of legitimate authority but of arbitrary power.
"Absolute [[monarch|sovereign]] of mortals, He distributes happiness or unhappiness, according to His pleasure" (ch. 2).
This is the same language that earthly tyrants use to silence complaint.

The [[monarch|sovereign]] governs through inscrutability and fear rather than through [[demon|demons]]trated competence or consent.
For rational [[existence|being]]s, such a relationship cannot merit allegiance.

Holbach w[[ritual|rites]] that "a [[despotism|despot]] to whom men are subjected but through fear, a master whom they can not interrogate, a totally inaccessible [[monarch|sovereign]], can not merit the homage of intelligent [[existence|being]]s" (ch. 2).
If [[god|God]]'s conduct is a [[secrecy|mystery]], he concludes, "it is not made for me."

## Historical Context

Sovereignty underwent radical transformation in the century before Holbach wrote.
[[hobbes|Hobbes]] theorized the [[monarch|sovereign]] as the artificial person who concentrates all [[government|political authority]].
Bodin defined it as absolute and perpetual power over subjects.

By 1772, Rousseau's *Social Contract* (1762) had reframed [[government|sovereignty]] as the expression of the general will.
This was a radical departure from the personal [[government|sovereignty]] of monarchs.

Holbach wrote at the twilight of French [[tyranny|absolutism]].
Louis XV ruled by [[divinity|divine]] right, backed by the church's ideological justification.
The [[monarch|king]] answered only to [[god|God]]; the people had no right to resist.

The Parlements had been suppressed in 1771, removing the last institutional check on royal power.
Holbach's conflation of [[divinity|divine]] and royal [[government|sovereignty]] was a direct attack on the *ancien régime*'s legitimating ideology.

## Core Arguments

**1. [[theology-index|Divine]] and royal [[tyranny|despotism]] use identical justifications.**
When theologians say [[god|God]]'s power entitles Him to act beyond human moral judgment, they "literally borrow from the language which the ministers of tyrants hold" (ch. 2).
The claim that subjects are worms before the [[monarch|sovereign]] is a political argument in theological dress.

**2. Inaccessible [[government|sovereignty]] cannot merit homage.**
A [[monarch|ruler]] whose conduct is unintelligible, whose decrees cannot be questioned, and whose favor is arbitrary fails the test of legitimate authority.
"If [[god|God]]'s conduct is a [[secrecy|mystery]] to me, it is not made for me" (ch. 2).

**3. [[priest|Priest]]s invented [[government|sovereignty]] to control both [[monarch|king]]s and [[nation|peoples]].**
Religious ideology exalts [[monarch|prince]]s by granting them [[divinity|divine]] right.
At the same time, it subordinates [[monarch|prince]]s to [[clergy|priests]], the sole interpreters of the [[divinity|divine]] will.
"Everywhere the priest places himself above the [[monarch|sovereign]]" (ch. 155).

**4. [[fear|Fear]] of invisible powers cannot substitute for good government.**
Holbach asks what restrains [[monarch|sovereign]]s without fear of [[god|God]].
His answer: "Let them learn how to govern, how to be just, how to respect the rights of the people" (ch. 157).
[[fear|Fear]] of [[punishment|divine punishment]] has never prevented [[despotism|tyranny]]; only institutional checks can do so.

**5. The [[vice|sin]]cere religious [[monarch|sovereign]] is the most dangerous.**
"A [[vice|sin]]cerely religious [[monarch|sovereign]] is generally a very dangerous chief for a [[politics-index|State]]; [[belief|credulity]] always indicates a narrow mind" (ch. 153).
Such a [[monarch|ruler]] becomes "the toy of [[clergy|priests]]' caprices, the abettor of their quarrels, the instrument and the accomplice of their follies."

## Reception and Critique

Holbach's attack on [[government|sovereignty]] was too radical for most contemporaries.
[[voltaire|Voltaire]] shared his anticlericalism but believed in enlightened [[tyranny|absolutism]] — reform from above through an educated monarch.

Rousseau's popular [[government|sovereignty]] offered an alternative without requiring Holbach's a[[divinity|theism]].
The revolutionaries of 1789 built a [[nations|nation]] on popular [[government|sovereignty]], only to see the [[terror|Terror]] revive absolute power in secular form.

Later theorists noted a weakness in Holbach's position.
If all [[government|sovereignty]]-as-command is illegitimate, what replaces it?
Holbach gestures toward rule of law but never develops a full theory of legitimate [[government|political authority]].

## Relation to Other Concepts

Sovereignty is the theological-political form of [[authority]] — authority raised to maximum intensity and shielded from all accountability.
It is the opposite of [[liberty]], which requires power to be limited, divided, and answerable to those subject to it.

Holbach connects [[government|sovereignty]] to the [[priest]]: [[clergy|priests]] manufacture the ideology of divine sovereignty to control both [[monarch|king]]s and [[nation|peoples]].
The concept also relates to [[government]], which should be rational administration rather than sacred command.

Sovereignty's practical consequence is [[tyranny]] — the unchecked domi[[nations|nation]] that absolute power inevitably produces.
Where [[government|sovereignty]] rules, [[law]] and [[rights]] recede, because the [[monarch|sovereign]] stands above all legal restraint.
