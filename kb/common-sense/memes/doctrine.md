# Doctrine

## Definition

In "Common Sense," doctrine is the organized expression of [[belief|religious belief]] — the set of propositions that a particular religion asserts as true and requires its adherents to accept. Holbach's critique is not directed at any [[vice|sin]]gle doctrine but at the entire enterprise of doctrinal religion: the claim that human [[existence|being]]s must accept propositions about invisible realities on the authority of revelation, tradition, or institutional decree, without the support of [[proof|evidence]] or reason.

Holbach proposes a radical principle for evaluating doctrine: "We must judge the doctrine by [[miracle|miracles]], and the [[miracle|miracles]] by the doctrine" — exposing the circular reasoning at the heart of theological apologetics. A doctrine that produces cruelty, [[cruelty|persecution]], or misery cannot be vindicated by appeals to [[miracle|miracles]], because those [[miracle|miracles]] are themselves attested only by the doctrine they are supposed to validate. The only reliable [[model|standard]] for evaluating any doctrine is its observable effect on human welfare.

## Historical Context

The 18th century was an age of doctrinal conflict. [[reformation|The Reformation]] had shattered the doctrinal unity of Western [[christianity|Christendom]], producing centuries of warfare between Catholics and Protestants. Within Protestantism, doctrinal disputes multiplied endlessly — predesti[[nations|nation]] vs. [[liberty|free will]], [[transubstantiation|real presence]] vs. symbolic communion, infant baptism vs. believer's baptism. [[enlightenment|The Enlightenment]]'s response to this chaos was to propose alternative foundations for belief: [[theism|natural religion]] (grounded in reason alone), [[theism|deism]] (a minimal creator without revelation), and finally Holbach's a[[divinity|theism]], which dispensed with doctrine entirely.

## Core Arguments

**1. Doctrine is self-validating through circular reasoning.** Holbach identifies the circular structure of theological argument: "They tell us that we must judge the doctrine by [[miracle|miracles]], and the [[miracle|miracles]] by the doctrine" (ch. LIX). Each half of this formula refers to the other for justification, so the entire system floats free of any independent [[proof|evidence]].

**2. [[concept-index|Doctrines]] are evaluated by their moral consequences, not their theological claims.** Holbach proposes a simple pragmatic test: does a doctrine make people better or worse? [[concept-index|Doctrines]] that inspire [[cruelty|persecution]], justify [[despotism|tyranny]], or encourage cruelty fail this test regardless of their claimed supernatural origin. "The doctrine that tends to make men more sociable, more humane, more moderate, is true; that which produces the contrary effect is false" (ch. LIX).

**3. Doctrinal diversity proves the absence of [[divinity|divine]] guidance.** If [[god|God]] had revealed a [[vice|sin]]gle true doctrine, there would not be thousands of competing [[sect|sects]] each claiming [[divinity|divine]] authority for incompatible propositions. The multiplicity of doctrines is [[proof|evidence]] that they are all human inventions. "If [[god|God]] had spoken, there would be but one religion on earth" (ch. LX).

**4. Doctrinal conformity is enforced by fear, not conviction.** [[person-index|People]] do not beli[[eve]] doctrines because they have examined the [[proof|evidence]]; they beli[[eve]] them because they were taught them in childhood, because [[eve]]ryone around them believes them, and because doubting them is punished. "The people receive their religion from their fathers; they follow it by habit; they would blush to examine it" (ch. LXI).

## Reception and Critique

Holbach's pragmatic criterion — judge doctrine by its fruits — has proved influential beyond his [[atheism|atheist]] conclusions. Liberal [[religion|theology]] in the 19th and 20th centuries increasingly adopted moral criteria for evaluating religious claims, sometimes arriving at positions close to Holbach's without his [[matter|materialism]]. Critics argue that Holbach's criterion is question-begging: it assumes a moral [[model|standard]] independent of doctrine, whereas many religious traditions claim that morality itself derives from doctrine. Holbach would reply that this only [[demon|demons]]trates how far [[religion|theology]] is willing to go to insulate itself from criticism.

## Relation to Other Concepts

Doctrine is the intellectual content of [[Religion]] — what religion asserts as true. It is propagated by [[Theology]], enforced by [[Priestcraft]], and accepted by [[Faith]], which is the disposition to beli[[eve]] without [[proof|evidence]]. Doctrine is opposed to [[Reason]], which demands [[proof|evidence]] and coherence, and to [[Science]], which revises its claims in light of new [[proof|evidence]] rather than claiming infallibility. The specific doctrines Holbach critiques most intensely include [[Hell]] (infinite punishment), [[Providence]] ([[divinity|divine]] [[government|governance]]), and [[Sin]] ([[original-sin|inherited guilt]]). The abandonment of doctrine clears the way for [[Philosophy]], which seeks [[philosophy|wisdom]] through reason rather than revelation.

## In the Text

On the circularity of doctrinal validation:

> "They tell us that we must judge the doctrine by miracles, and the miracles by the doctrine. If the doctrine is false, the miracles are false; if the miracles are true, the doctrine is true. But who will prove to us that the miracles are true? The doctrine. Who will prove to us that the doctrine is true? The miracles. This is what is called a vicious circle." (ch. LIX)
>
> (002-252)
On the pragmatic test of doctrine:

> "Every doctrine that tends to render men more sociable, more humane, more moderate, more benevolent, is a true doctrine. Every doctrine that tends to render them unsociable, inhuman, persecutors, fanatics, is a false doctrine." (ch. LIX)
>
> (002-320)
On doctrinal diversity as [[proof|evidence]] against revelation:

> "If God had spoken, there would be but one religion, one worship, one doctrine on earth. But we see the contrary: there are a thousand religions, all of which contradict each other, and each of which claims to be the only true one." (ch. LX)
>
> (002-118)
On the social function of enforced doctrine:

> "The people are taught to believe, not to examine. They are made to fear doubt as a crime, and to regard the blind submission of the mind as the first of virtues." (ch. LXI)
>
> (002-122)
