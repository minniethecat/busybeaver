# Prejudice

## Definition

Prejudice, for Holbach, is not merely a personal bias but a systematically imposed cognitive constraint: the set of beliefs instilled in childhood before reason has developed, which thereafter operate below the threshold of conscious scrutiny. Theological [[opinion|prejudice]] is the most pernicious form — "men who are strongly interested in error; who are [[opinion|prejudice]]d in its favor; who refuse to reflect" (ch (002-309). 82). Prejudice is the mechanism by which [[Superstition]] perpetuates itself across generations: each cohort of children is filled with the fears and [[dogma|dogmas]] of its predecessors before it can ask whether those beliefs are true.

## Historical Context

The critique of [[opinion|prejudice]] was a foundational theme of the Enlightenment, most famously articulated in Kant's 1784 essay "What is Enlightenment?" (*sapere aude* — dare to know). Francis Bacon's doctrine of the "idols" (1620) had already identified four categories of systematic cognitive error, including the "idols of the theater" — philosophical and theological systems accepted on authority. Holbach's treatment of [[opinion|prejudice]] is distinctive in its political economy: he analyzes [[opinion|prejudice]] not as an [[chance|accident]] of human cognition but as a deliberately maintained system of control. His materialist framework locates the origin of [[opinion|prejudice]] in social institutions rather than in innate cognitive limitations.

## Core Arguments

**1. Prejudice is installed in childhood.** Holbach repeatedly emphasizes the temporal priority of [[opinion|prejudice]] to reason: beliefs implanted "when the mind was least capable of judging" survive into adulthood as unchallenged certainties. [[education|Education]]al systems controlled by [[priest|clergy]] ensure that "the youthful minds of their pupils" are filled with "fables, [[dogma|dogmas]], and puerilities" (ch. 198) rather than with [[knowledge|useful knowledge]].

**2. Prejudice is reinforced by interest.** [[error|Error]] persists not merely through intellectual inertia but because powerful groups have material interests in maintaining it: "It is entirely impossible to have the best arguments accepted by men who are strongly interested in error; who are [[opinion|prejudice]]d in its favor; who refuse to reflect" (ch. 82). [[priest|Priest]]s, tyrants, and the wealthy all benefit from a population that does not question inherited beliefs.

**3. Prejudice multiplies [[evil|suffering]].** Holbach argues that [[opinion|prejudice]] does not merely distort thought — it directly increases [[suffering|human misery]]: "the needs which his imagi[[nations|nation]], his prejudices, and his institutions multiply to his [[torture|torment]]" (ch. 95). Religious [[opinion|prejudice]], in particular, creates anxieties about [[punishment|divine punishment]], [[punishment|hell]], and moral purity that would not exist in a rational society.

**4. Prejudice can be overcome.** Despite its power, [[opinion|prejudice]] is not invincible. Holbach holds that "truth necessarily undeceives the honest souls who seek it in good [[religion|faith]]" (ch. 82). The capacity for reason exists in [[eve]]ry human mind; [[opinion|prejudice]] is a layer of accumulated error that can be stripped away through honest inquiry.

**5. Prejudice and philosophy are opposites.** "The unprejudiced philosopher sees nothing in the [[miracle|wonder]]s of [[universe|nature]] but permanent and invariable law; nothing but the [[effect|necessary effect]]s of different combi[[nation|nations]] of diversified substance" (ch. 2). To be unprejudiced is to approach [[earth|the world]] without pre-formed theological categories — to let [[proof|evidence]], not authority, determine belief.

## Reception and Critique

Holbach's rationalist optimism — his [[certainty|confidence]] that [[opinion|prejudice]] could be eliminated through education — was characteristic of the radical Enlightenment. Critics from Edmund Burke onward argued that [[opinion|prejudice]], understood as inherited moral [[philosophy|wisdom]], was essential to social stability; stripping it away would lead not to liberation but to chaos. Hans-Georg Gadamer's philosophical hermeneutics (1960) offered a more sympathetic rehabilitation: "[[opinion|prejudice]]" (*Vorurteil*) is not necessarily error but the pre-judgment without which [[knowledge|understanding]] cannot begin. Contemporary social psychology has complicated Holbach's picture further: implicit biases operate below conscious awareness in ways that mere rational education may not easily correct.

## Relation to Other Concepts

Prejudice is the cognitive mechanism through which [[Dogma]] and [[Superstition]] are transmitted across generations. It depends on [[Ignorance]] (the absence of countervailing knowledge) and serves [[Authority]] (those who benefit from an unquestioning populace). It is the primary obstacle that [[Reason]] must overcome, and it finds its antidote in [[Education]] and [[Philosophy]]. Prejudice is also closely linked to [[Faith]] — both involve believing without sufficient [[proof|evidence]], but [[opinion|prejudice]] adds the dimension of unexamined inheritance.

## Religion as Inherited Prejudice

Holbach's account of religious transmission is uncompromisingly sociological: [[theism|belief in God]] is not a conclusion reached by reason but an inheritance accepted without scrutiny. The metaphor of religion as "family property" is particularly stri[[monarch|king]] — it reduces the [[divinity|divine]] to a [[worship|cult]]ural asset passed down like land or furniture, its legitimacy derived not from truth but from the authority of tradition and the inertia of [[habit|custom]].

> Religion is handed down from fathers to children as the property of a family with the burdens. Very few people in the world would have a God if care had not been taken to give them one. Each one receives from his parents and from his education a God whom he adopts without examination, and whom he transmits to his own children with the same blind confidence with which he himself received it.
>
> (ch. 32)
