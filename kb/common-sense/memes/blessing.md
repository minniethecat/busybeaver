# Blessing

## Definition

Blessing, in Holbach's analysis, is a theological concept that systematically mis[[attribute|attributes]] the goods of life.
When a harvest is plentiful, when a child is born healthy, when a battle is won — religion teaches that these are blessings from [[god|God]], dispensed through His [[clergy|priests]], and that gratitude and offerings are owed in return.
Holbach reverses the attribution: what are called blessings are in fact the products of [[universe|nature]], labor, and [[probability|chance]], and the practice of blessing is a mechanism for extracting tribute from those who have been taught to mistake natural causes for supernatural gifts.

The critique has a psychological dimension.
"They boast above all that our existence is a blessing" (ch. 91).
But "how many mortals are tired of this blessing" — how many find existence to be not a gift but a burden, marked by "griefs, fears, sicknesses, and bitter disappointments"?
To call existence a blessing is to ignore the actual experience of the majority of human [[existence|being]]s.

The political dimension is equally sharp.
"The follies of men procure you blessings, offerings, expiations" (ch. 183).
The priestly class lives on the offerings that believers make in gratitude for blessings — or in the hope of securing them.
Blessing is a transaction: the priest blesses, the believer pays, and no one can verify whether anything has actually been conferred.

## Historical Context

The practice of priestly benediction was woven into the fabric of eighteenth-century [[europe|Europe]]an life.
Fields were blessed at planting, houses at construction, ships before departure, and the dying at their final hour.
The Roman [[ritual|Ritual]] contained dozens of blessings for [[eve]]ry conceivable object and occasion — candles, water, salt, oil, vestments, bells, and [[soul|anima]]ls.

The theological rationale was that blessings conveyed actual [[soul|spirit]]ual benefits: protection from [[injustice|evil]], remission of venial [[vice|sin]]s, and the impetration of [[divinity|divine]] favors.
The [[council-of-trent|Council of Trent]] had reaffirmed [[church|the Church]]'s power to bless, and the practice was a significant source of clerical income through stipends and offerings.

Holbach's critique must be understood against the background of Enlightenment [[materialism|naturalism]].
If [[earth|the world]] operates according to natural laws — if crops grow because of soil, water, and sunlight, not because a priest blessed the field — then blessing is a category error.
It [[attribute|attributes]] natural effects to supernatural causes, and in doing so, it makes the priest indispensable to activities that require no priest at all.

## Core Arguments

**1. Blessing mis[[attribute|attributes]] natural goods to supernatural agency.**
The farmer's harvest is the product of labor, soil, and climate — not of a priest's benediction.
By [[doctrine|teaching]] that goods come from [[god|God]], religion conceals the real causes of [[happiness|well-being]] and the real agents who produce it.

**2. [[existence|Existence]] is not unambiguously a blessing.**
"Alas! how many mortals are tired of this blessing" (ch. 91).
To call life a blessing is to ignore the [[evil|suffering]] that pervades it — the diseases, the griefs, the injustices.
Only those who are comfortable enough to be insulated from the general misery can [[vice|sin]]cerely call existence a blessing.

**3. Blessings are a revenue stream for [[clergy|the clergy]].**
"The follies of men procure you blessings, offerings, expiations" (ch. 183).
Every blessing carries a fee, and [[clergy|the clergy]] have multiplied the occasions for blessing to multiply the occasions for payment.

**4. [[priest|Priest]]ly blessings are empirically empty.**
Holbach challenges the believer to [[demon|demons]]trate that a blessing has produced any verifiable effect.
The crops that grow on a blessed field grow equally on an unblessed one; the ship that [[vice|sin]]ks despite a priest's benediction [[vice|sin]]ks nonetheless.
The efficacy of blessing is asserted but never [[demon|demons]]trated.

**5. True blessedness is secular.**
"[[philosophy|Philosophy]], morality, legislation, and politics" — not religion — are the foundations of human [[happiness|well-being]] (ch. 197).
A society governed by reason, in which labor is rewarded and knowledge is disseminated, produces real blessings: health, prosperity, and peace.

## Reception and Critique

Holbach's critique of blessing is part of his broader attack on sacramental [[religion|theology]] — the claim that material objects and ritual actions can convey [[soul|spirit]]ual effects.
This critique was influential on later secularism, particularly the French laïcité tradition, which sought to remove [[ritual|religious ritual]] from public life.

Defenders of the practice have argued that blessing serves a social function independent of its supernatural claims: it marks important transitions, expresses communal hope, and provides psychological comfort.
A blessing need not alter the physical [[universe|world]] to alter the subjective experience of those who receive it.

Holbach would likely reply that these functions can be served by secular rituals — by public celebrations, by expressions of gratitude addressed to real benefactors rather than imaginary ones.
The entanglement of blessing with payment, moreover, means that [[eve]]n its social benefits are purchased at the cost of enriching a parasitic class.

## Relation to Other Concepts

Blessing is the positive counterpart to [[curse]]: where blessing confers [[divinity|divine]] favor, curse inflicts [[divinity|divine]] harm, and both are dispensed by the same priestly hands.
It is closely tied to [[gratitude]] — the [[passion|emotion]] that blessing redirects from real benefactors (parents, teachers, fellow citizens) to an imaginary one ([[god|God]]).

Blessing is a form of [[ritual]]: a ceremonial action that claims supernatural efficacy but produces no verifiable effect.
It is administered by the [[priest]], whose monopoly on blessing is a source of both revenue and authority.

The concept inter[[sect|sects]] with [[providence]]: blessings are the [[proof|evidence]] that believers cite for [[providence|divine providence]], just as misfortunes are the [[proof|evidence]] that skeptics cite against it.
Both are selective readings of an indifferent [[universe|nature]].
