# Vanity

## Definition

For Holbach, vanity is the psychological root of [[error|religious error]]. It is vanity that persuades man "that he is the sole center of [[nature|the universe]]; he creates for himself a [[universe|world]] and a [[god|God]]; he thinks himself of sufficient consequence to derange [[universe|nature]] at his will" (ch. XCVIII). This anthropocentric delusion is the cognitive foundation of all [[religion|theology]]: because man cannot bear to be insignificant, he invents a [[god|God]] who cares about his conduct and a [[universe|cosmos]] designed for his benefit.

Vanity operates at two levels in Holbach's analysis. At the individual level, it [[desire|drive]]s the religious believer to imagine that [[god|the creator]] of [[nature|the universe]] takes a personal interest in his [[prayer|prayers]], his rituals, and his moral ledger. At the institutional level, it is the defining vice of the [[clergy|priesthood]]: "[[pride|Pride]] and vanity always were and always will be the inherent vices of the [[clergy|priesthood]]" (ch. CLXXXVIII). The priest's conviction that he exercises [[hope|heaven]]ly power, possesses a sacred character, and speaks for the Most High is vanity raised to a professional principle.

The critique of vanity extends to morality itself. Holbach observes that much of what passes for saintly [[morality|virtue]] is vanity in disguise. The saint who renounces [[earth|the world]], who wages war against his own [[universe|nature]], who [[worship|cult]]ivates an appearance of extraordinary piety — this is not humility but "a pride more refined than that of common men" (ch. CLXII). The religious devotee who gives herself to [[god|God]] only when [[earth|the world]] no longer [[desire|want]]s her is [[desire|drive]]n by vanity, not grace.

## Historical Context

The analysis of vanity as a source of cognitive error has roots in classical philosophy. The Stoics warned against the vanity of attaching excessive importance to one's own affairs. Augustine's *[[confession|Confession]]s* identified pride and self-love as the root of [[vice|sin]], though he located the solution in [[divinity|divine]] grace rather than rational self-correction.

In the early modern period, the critique of vanity became a weapon against religious pretension. [[montaigne|Montaigne]]'s *Essays* (1580) anatomized human vanity with ironic detachment, concluding that man's [[pride|presumption]] in claiming knowledge of [[divinity|divine]] things was the greatest vanity of all. [[pascal|Pascal]], whom Holbach engages extensively, saw human vanity as [[proof|evidence]] of man's fallen greatness — a creature aware of his misery yet unable to stop aspiring. Holbach inverts [[pascal|Pascal]]: vanity is not a mark of lost greatness but a cognitive error to be corrected by philosophy.

[[enlightenment|The Enlightenment]] extended this critique to social institutions. [[voltaire|Voltaire]]'s satires exposed the vanity of [[clergy|priests]], courtiers, and philosophers alike. Rousseau's critique of *amour-propre* — the corrupt form of self-love that depends on the opinion of others — paralleled Holbach's analysis, though Rousseau traced it to civilization itself rather than to religion specifically.

## Core Arguments

**1. Vanity is the psychological foundation of anthropocentric religion.** "Man's vanity persuades him that he is the sole center of [[nature|the universe]]" (ch. XCVIII). The belief that [[god|God]] cares about human conduct, answers [[prayer|prayers]], and designed [[universe|nature]] for human use is vanity projected onto the [[universe|cosmos]].

**2. Vanity is the constitutive vice of the [[clergy|priesthood]].** The assumption of exercising [[divinity|divine]] power, possessing a sacred character, spea[[monarch|king]] for the Most High — these priestly pretensions are vanity institutionalized. "Is there anything that has a tendency to render men haughty and vain more than the assumption of exercising [[heaven|Heaven]]ly power?" (ch. CLXXXVIII).

**3. Religious [[morality|virtue]] is often vanity disguised as humility.** The saint's self-mortification, his war against his own [[universe|nature]], his claim to an extraordinary relationship with [[god|God]] — these are not humility but a pride more refined than that of common men. "It must be a very ridiculous vanity which can determine a man to continually war with his own [[universe|nature]]" (ch. CLXII).

**4. Vanity [[desire|drive]]s [[persecution|religious persecution]].** When [[clergy|priests]] accuse unbelievers of pride, they project their own defining vice. "What other passion than frenzied pride can render men so ferocious, so vindictive, so devoid of toleration and gentleness?" (ch. CLXXXVIII).

## Relation to Other Concepts

Vanity is the psychological source of [[Superstition]] — the belief that cosmic forces are concerned with human affairs. It is closely related to [[Pride]], which is vanity in its aggressive, domineering form. Vanity produces the illusion that man possesses [[Free Will]] and moral significance sufficient to interest an infinite [[existence|being]]. It is the engine of priestly [[Authority]] — the claim to speak for [[god|God]] is vanity's ultimate political expression. Vanity is also the root of [[Ignorance]], because the vain mind cannot acknowledge the limits of its knowledge.

## In the Text

On vanity as the source of anthropocentric delusion:

> "Man's vanity persuades him that he is the sole center of the universe; he creates for himself a world and a God; he thinks himself of sufficient consequence to derange nature at his will." (ch. XCVIII)

On the vanity of the [[clergy|priesthood]]:

> "Pride and vanity always were and always will be the inherent vices of the priesthood. Is there anything that has a tendency to render men haughty and vain more than the assumption of exercising Heavenly power, of possessing a sacred character, of being the messengers of the Most High?" (ch. CLXXXVIII)

On the vanity of saintly self-mortification:

> "The vanity of performing a role, the desire of distinguishing themselves in the eyes of the stupid vulgar by a strange conduct, constitute usually the distinctive characteristics of great saints; pride persuades them that they are extraordinary men, far above human nature." (ch. CLXII)
