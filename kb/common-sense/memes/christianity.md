# Christianity

## Definition

Christianity, as Holbach analyzes it in "Common Sense," is not a [[revelation|revealed truth]] but a human institution — a system of doctrines, rituals, and clerical authority that grew from the apocalyptic preaching of a Jewish reformer into the dominant religion of [[europe|Europe]]. Holbach's critique is comprehensive: Christianity's [[religion|theology]] is incoherent (a [[god|God]] who is three and one, infinite and personal, just and merciful in ways that contradict each other); its history is violent (crusades, inquisitions, [[crusades|religious wars]]); its morality is distorted (it teaches people to despise earthly life and fear a [[god|God]] who holds "in reserve eternal, hard, and violent [[punishment|chastisement]]s for ninety-nine hundredths of His children"); and its social effect is to enrich [[clergy|priests]] at the expense of the credulous.

"Tired of an incomprehensible [[religion|theology]], of ridiculous fables, of impenetrable [[mysteries]], of puerile ceremonies, let them at last bring them back to the earth" (Preface). This is Holbach's program in a sentence: Christianity must be dismantled not because it is merely false but because it is harmful — it distracts from the only life we have and empowers a class of professional deceivers who profit from human fear.

## Historical Context

Holbach wrote at a moment when Christianity's political and intellectual dominance was weakening but still formidable. The [[catholic-church|Catholic Church]] remained the largest landowner in [[paris|France]], the censor of books, and the keeper of the civil registry (baptismal records were birth certificates). To attack Christianity was to attack the institutional foundation of the old regime.

[[reformation|The Reformation]] had splintered Christianity into warring [[sect|sects]] — Catholicism, Lutheranism, Calvinism, Anglicanism, and dozens of smaller movements — each claiming to be the one true church and each denouncing the others as [[heresy|heretic]]al. For Holbach, this mutual hostility was [[proof|evidence]] against Christianity's [[divinity|divine]] origin: a universal [[god|God]] would have revealed a universal religion, not a chaos of competing claims.

[[enlightenment|The Enlightenment]] had already produced powerful critiques of Christianity from within: [[john-locke|Locke]]'s *[[reason|Reason]]ableness of Christianity* (1695) tried to strip away theological accretions and recover a rational core; Toland's *Christianity Not Mysterious* (1696) went further, arguing that [[gospel|the Gospel]] contained nothing above reason. Holbach's innovation was to abandon the project of reforming Christianity and to argue for its outright rejection. Christianity was not a noble [[model|ideal]] corrupted by [[clergy|priests]] — it was corrupt at its source.

## Core Arguments

**1. Christianity is incoherent theologically.** The central [[dogma|dogmas]] of Christianity — the [[trinity|Trinity]], the [[incarnation|Incarnation]], the [[sacrifice|Atonement]] — are not [[mysteries]] to be contemplated but contradictions to be exposed. "There is a [[knowledge|science]] which has for its object only incomprehensible things... This [[knowledge|science]] is named [[theology|Theology]], and this [[theology|Theology]] is a continual insult to [[reason|human reason]]" (ch. II). A [[god|God]] who must become man and die to appease His own wrath is not a [[existence|being]] worthy of worship but a character in a barbarous myth.

**2. Christianity is violent historically.** Holbach surveys the historical record — crusades against Muslims, inquisitions against [[heresy|heretic]]s, wars between Catholics and Protestants — and concludes that Christianity has caused more bloodshed than any pagan religion. "When we complain about the violence and [[injustice|evil]]s which generally religion causes upon earth, we are answered at once, that these [[excess|excesses]] are not due to religion, but that they are the sad effect of men's passions. I would ask, however, what unchained these passions? It is evidently religion" (ch. II).

**3. Christianity corrupts morality.** The [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian [[morality|virtue]]s — [[religion|faith]], hope, charity, humility — are not genuine [[morality|virtue]]s. [[faith|Faith]] is belief without [[proof|evidence]]; hope is deferred to another [[universe|world]]; charity is selective (it extends only to fellow believers); humility is servility toward [[god|God]] and His priestly representatives. True [[morality|virtue]], for Holbach, consists in actions that promote human welfare in this [[universe|world]], not in beliefs that secure personal salvation in the next.

**4. Christianity is institutionally parasitic.** The [[priest|clergy]] constitute a non-productive class that lives off the labor of others by claiming to mediate between humanity and [[god|God]]. "[[priest|Priest]]s in all times have shown themselves supporters of [[tyranny|despotism]], and the enemies of public liberty. Their profession requires vile and submissive slaves, who never have the audacity to reason" (ch. CLXIX). The church is not a [[society|community]] of believers but a system of extraction.

## Reception and Critique

Holbach's attack on Christianity was the most radical of the French Enlightenment — more radical than [[voltaire|Voltaire]]'s [[theism|deism]], more radical than Rousseau's civil religion, more radical [[eve]]n than Diderot's skeptical [[matter|materialism]]. It anticipated the 19th-century critiques of Feuerbach (*The [[essence|Essence]] of Christianity*, 1841), who argued that [[god|God]] was a projection of human qualities, and of Nietzsche, who saw Christianity as a slave morality that devalued life in favor of an imaginary beyond.

Critics have raised several objections. First, Holbach's historical claim — that Christianity is uniquely or disproportionately violent — is diffi[[worship|cult]] to sustain against the record of non-[[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian empires (Mongol, Aztec, Roman) that were equally bloody without [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian motivation. Second, his dismissal of [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian charity overlooks the genuinely humanitarian institutions (hospitals, schools, poor relief) that [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian organizations have built. Third, his reduction of religion to priestly imposture fails to explain why ordinary people — not just [[clergy|priests]] — have found Christianity meaningful and consoling.

## Relation to Other Concepts

Christianity is the institutional form of the theological system Holbach attacks throughout "Common Sense." It connects to [[Theology]] as its intellectual apparatus (the attempt to make the incomprehensible seem profound), to [[Priest]] as its institutional mechanism (the class that profits from [[belief|credulity]]), and to [[Superstition]] as its psychological foundation (the fear and ignorance that make the [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian system credible).

Christianity is opposed to [[Reason]], which Holbach sees as the only reliable guide to truth; to [[Nature]], which operates by invariable laws and requires no supernatural supplement; and to [[Happiness]], which Christianity defers to an [[death|afterlife]] that does not exist.
