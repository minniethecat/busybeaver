# Spinoza

## In Holbach's Reference

**Baruch Spinoza** (1632–1677) was a Dutch philosopher of Portuguese-Jewish descent, one of the most influential figures of the early Enlightenment. In Holbach's *Common Sense*, Spinoza is mentioned only once — but in significant company: "Among the moderns, Hobbes, Spinoza, Bayle, and others have followed the same path" (002-448). The "path" in question is the tradition of rationalist critique of religion. Holbach places Spinoza in a lineage of freethinkers who refused to subordinate reason to revelation.

## The Freethought Tradition

Holbach locates Spinoza within a long intellectual genealogy stretching from antiquity to the modern era. The passage at (002-448) groups Spinoza with the ancient atomists and skeptics as well as moderns Hobbes and Bayle. This lineage is significant to Holbach's argument: it demonstrates that the critique of religion is a persistent tradition of rational inquiry. Holbach's intellectual debt to Spinoza is deeper than the single mention suggests. Spinoza's *Tractatus Theologico-Politicus* (1670) applied systematic rational analysis to scripture, and his pantheistic identification of God with Nature cleared the ground for a fully naturalistic worldview. Bayle, also cited in the same passage, had described Spinoza as a "virtuous atheist," further connecting him to the freethought tradition Holbach champions.
