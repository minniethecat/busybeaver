# Society

## Definition

In Holbach's "Common Sense," society is not a [[divinity|divine]] ordinance or a fall from a state of [[universe|nature]] but the natural and necessary condition of human existence. Human [[existence|being]]s are "born in society" and can flourish only within it. "Man is born in society; he there finds his happiness and his [[happiness|well-being]]" (ch. CXX). Society, for Holbach, is the material and institutional framework within which reason, morality, and justice operate — it is the medium of human life, not an optional arrangement.

Holbach's conception of society is fundamentally naturalistic: society exists because human [[existence|being]]s need one another for survival, flourishing, and happiness. Its proper [[government|governance]] is not by revelation or priestly authority but by laws grounded in an [[knowledge|understanding]] of human [[universe|nature]] and directed toward the common good. "Society has the right to punish those who trouble it," but only within the limits of reason and proportionality — not with the "eternal [[torture|torment]]s" that [[religion|theology]] threatens.

## Historical Context

The 18th century saw the emergence of social theory as a distinct field of inquiry. Montesquieu's *The [[spirit|Spirit]] of the [[law|Law]]s* (1748) had analyzed how climate, geography, and [[habit|custom]] shape social institutions. Rousseau's *Social Contract* (1762) had argued that legitimate [[government|political authority]] derives from the general will. The Scottish Enlightenment — [[david-hume|Hume]], Smith, Ferguson — had developed theories of social evolution from "rude" to "civil" stages. Holbach's contribution was to insist that society must be understood entirely without reference to [[divinity|divine]] purpose: it is a human artifact for human purposes, and its problems admit only human solutions.

## Core Arguments

**1. Society is natural to humanity.** Against the [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian narrative of a prelapsarian state of [[universe|nature]] from which humanity fell, Holbach insists that human [[existence|being]]s are inherently social creatures. The isolated individual is an abstraction; real human [[existence|being]]s live, think, and flourish only in society. There is no "state of [[universe|nature]]" to return to.

**2. Society must be organized by reason, not religion.** "Society of [[saint|saints]] would be impossible" (ch. CXXI) — meaning that a community organized around theological [[morality|virtue]] rather than human needs is a contradiction in terms. [[saint|Saint]]s are defined by their indifference to earthly goods, but society exists precisely to secure those goods. The attempt to organize society on religious principles has historically produced the opposite: [[cruelty|persecution]], war, and [[despotism|tyranny]].

**3. The purpose of society is [[happiness|earthly happiness]].** Holbach rejects the theological view that earthly society is merely a testing-ground for the [[death|afterlife]]. Society's purpose is to enable human [[existence|being]]s to live well *here and now*. Its laws, institutions, and [[habit|custom]]s should be evaluated by this [[model|standard]] alone. "If the [[monarch|sovereign]]s had more humanity, charity, and justice, their [[politics-index|State]]s would not be peopled by so many unfortunate ones."

**4. [[religion|Religion]] undermines society.** By [[doctrine|teaching]] that this [[universe|world]] is a vale of tears and that true happiness awaits in the next, religion systematically devalues the social conditions of [[happiness|human flourishing]]. Worse, by claiming [[divinity|divine]] authority for particular [[monarch|ruler]]s and laws, it immunizes bad government against criticism and reform.

## Reception and Critique

Holbach's social theory was radical for its thoroughgoing [[materialism|naturalism]] — more so than many of his Enlightenment contemporaries who retained a role for [[providence|divine providence]] in social order. Conservative critics charged that without religion as a foundation, society would collapse into moral chaos. Holbach's reply, implicit throughout "Common Sense," is that religion has not prevented moral chaos; it has often caused it, and reason provides a firmer foundation for social order than superstition [[eve]]r did.

Later sociological thought — particularly Durkheim's analysis of religion as society's worship of itself — can be seen as an indirect vindication of Holbach's insight that the sacred is a social product, not a social foundation.

## Relation to Other Concepts

Society is the context within which all other Holbachian concepts find their meaning. [[Justice]] is the set of rules that make society possible. [[Morality]] is the internalization of social requirements. [[Government]] is society's administrative apparatus, and [[Tyranny]] is its corruption. [[Education]] is society's mechanism for transmitting reason and [[morality|virtue]] across generations. Society stands opposed to the anti-social tendencies of [[Superstition]], which teaches individuals to value the next [[universe|world]] over this one, and [[Dogma]], which divides society into warring [[sect|sects]].

## In the Text

On society as the natural condition of humanity:

> "Man is born in society; he there finds his happiness and his well-being. It is then in society that he should seek the remedies for the evils which he experiences." (ch. CXX)
>
> (002-090)

On the impossibility of a saintly society:

> "A society of saints would be impossible. Their virtues, which consist in renouncing all the goods of this world, would be incompatible with the existence of society, which can subsist only by the aid of those goods that the saints despise." (ch. CXXI)
>
> (002-282)

On religion's corrupting effect on social order:

> "Religion, far from being useful to society, is the true source of all the evils with which the human race is afflicted. It divides men instead of uniting them; it makes them enemies instead of brothers." (ch. CLXXII)
>
> (002-366)

On the proper foundation of social authority:

> "Society has the right to punish those who trouble it; but this punishment should be proportioned to the offense, and should have for its object the correction of the guilty and the security of the innocent." (ch. CXXIII)
>
> (002-135)
