## Holbach's Critique of Papal Power

Holbach reserves some of his sharpest criticism for the institution of the Papacy in *Common Sense*. He portrays the Pontiff not as a spiritual shepherd but as an ambitious political actor whose "enterprises" are directed at dominating temporal rulers: "In order to guard themselves against the enterprises of a haughty Pontiff who desired to reign over kings..." (002-291). For Holbach, the Papacy represents the logical endpoint of ecclesiastical ambition — a single figure claiming divine authority to overrule the decisions of legitimate sovereigns.

## The Pontiff as Tyrant

Holbach goes further in characterizing the Pope's historical role, describing "the tyrannical power which a haughty Pontiff formerly exercised over all the sovereigns of Europe" (002-407). This power, in his account, was not spiritual but political — the Pope "possesses the keys of paradise and of hell," a claim that Holbach treats as the ultimate instrument of clerical domination. The Pontiff's claim to control access to salvation gave the Papacy leverage over kings and emperors that no purely political authority could match.

## The Pontiff in Holbach's Sociology of Religion

Within Holbach's broader theory of religion, the Pontiff exemplifies how priesthoods escalate their claims from intercession to absolute spiritual monopoly. The Pope represents the institutional extreme of the same logic that operates in every priest who claims to mediate between humanity and the divine. Holbach's repeated use of "haughty" as the Pontiff's defining adjective underscores his view that papal power is fundamentally a matter of pride and ambition rather than genuine piety.


## From *Common Sense*

> In order to guard themselves against the enterprises of a haughty Pontiff who desired to reign over kings, and in order to protect their persons from the attacks of the credulous people excited by their priests, several princes of Europe pretended to have received their crowns and their rights from God alone.
>
> (002-291)
