# Chance

## Definition

Chance, for Holbach, is not a real force in [[universe|nature]] but an empty word — a linguistic placeholder for causal ignorance.
When we say something happened "by [[probability|chance]]," we confess that we do not understand the laws that produced it.
"Men may use the word '[[probability|chance]]' to cover their ignorance of the true causes; nevertheless, although they may ignore them, these causes act, but by certain laws" (043-001).

This is not a peripheral observation but a cornerstone of Holbach's materialist metaphysics.
If [[eve]]ry [[eve]]nt follows necessarily from prior causes, then nothing can be genuinely random, accidental, or contingent.
The appearance of [[probability|chance]] is always an illusion produced by the limits of human knowledge.

Holbach deploys this argument against the design argument for [[god|God]]'s existence.
Theologians claim that the order of [[universe|nature]] is too complex to have arisen by [[probability|chance]], therefore it must have been designed.
Holbach replies that "[[probability|chance]]" is not a real alternative to design — it is not an expla[[nations|nation]] at all.
The true alternative is natural necessity: [[nature|the universe]] is ordered because the laws of [[universe|nature]] necessarily produce the arrangements we observe.

## Historical Context

The concept of [[probability|chance]] was at the center of eighteenth-century debates about [[effect|determinism]], [[liberty|free will]], and the [[design|argument from design]].
[[isaac-newton|Isaac Newton]]'s physics had revealed a universe of lawful regularity — but [[isaac-newton|Newton]] himself attributed this order to [[divinity|divine]] [[government|governance]], treating the laws of [[universe|nature]] as [[god|God]]'s "sensorium."

The Epicurean revival, particularly through Lucretius's *De Rerum Natura*, offered an alternative: the random swerve (*clinamen*) of atoms produced the variety of natural forms without [[divinity|divine]] intervention.
Holbach rejected this too.
For him, atoms do not swerve randomly — they follow necessary trajectories determined by their essential properties.
"There is no effect without a cause" (043-001).

The design argument, championed by [[will|Will]]iam Paley and others, relied on an implicit dichotomy: either order comes from [[design|intelligent design]] or it comes from [[probability|chance]].
Holbach's contribution was to expose this as a false dichotomy.
The third option — natural necessity — explains order without invo[[monarch|king]] either intelligence or [[probability|randomness]].

Pierre-Simon Laplace would later crystallize this view in his famous reply to Napoleon: "I had no need of that hypothesis" (concerning [[god|God]]).
Holbach's chapter on [[probability|chance]] was an early and forceful statement of the same position.

## Core Arguments

**1. "Chance" is a word for ignorance, not an expla[[nations|nation]].**
"Men may use the word '[[probability|chance]]' to cover their ignorance of the true causes" (ch. 43).
To say that something happened by [[probability|chance]] is to say nothing about how it happened — it is to stop inquiring.

**2. [[nothingness|Nothing]] in [[universe|nature]] occurs without a cause.**
"[[nothingness|Nothing]] in [[universe|nature]] can be made by [[probability|chance]]; all follow fixed laws; these laws are but the necessary union of certain effects with their causes" (ch. 43).
The principle of sufficient reason applies universally; [[eve]]ry [[eve]]nt has a determinate cause.

**3. The universe is not an effect — it is the cause of all effects.**
"The universe is not an effect; it is the cause of all effects; all the [[existence|being]]s it embraces are the [[effect|necessary effect]]s of this cause" (ch. 43).
Theologians ask what caused [[nature|the universe]]; Holbach replies that the question is malformed — [[nature|the universe]] is the totality of causes, not an effect requiring an external cause.

**4. Chance is invoked selectively and inconsistently.**
Believers attribute [[nature|natural order]] to [[god|God]] but natural disorder (earthquakes, diseases) to [[probability|chance]] or [[secrecy|mystery]].
Holbach argues that the same necessity produces both: laws that are regular enough to sustain life and severe enough to destroy it.

**5. The design-vs.-[[probability|chance]] dichotomy is false.**
Theologians argue: "order cannot come from [[probability|chance]], therefore [[god|God]]."
Holbach replies that order comes from the necessary properties of matter — an alternative excluded from the forced choice between design and [[probability|randomness]].

## Reception and Critique

Holbach's rejection of [[probability|chance]] was part of a broader Enlightenment commitment to deterministic [[matter|materialism]].
Laplace's *A Philosophical Essay on Probabilities* (1814) developed the mathematical framework for the view that probability is a measure of ignorance, not a feature of reality.

The discovery of quantum mechanics in the twentieth century challenged the strict [[effect|determinism]] that Holbach assumed.
If fundamental physical processes are genuinely probabilistic, then "[[probability|chance]]" may not be merely a word for ignorance but a feature of [[universe|nature]] itself.
Whether this rehabilitates metaphysical contingency or merely relocates [[effect|determinism]] to a deeper level remains contested.

In evolutionary biology, the role of random mutation has made "[[probability|chance]]" a legitimate scientific concept rather than a confession of ignorance.
Holbach's argument must therefore be understood as a valuable corrective to theological uses of [[probability|chance]], not as the last word on in[[effect|determinism]] in [[universe|nature]].

## Relation to Other Concepts

Chance is the apparent opposite of [[necessity]], but for Holbach the opposition is illusory — what appears as [[probability|chance]] is necessity whose causes we have not yet traced.
It stands opposed to [[design]], the theological claim that order requires an ordering intelligence.

The concept of [[probability|chance]] inter[[sect|sects]] with [[cause]]: Holbach insists on the universality of [[cause|causation]], and [[probability|chance]] is simply [[cause|causation]] whose mechanism is unknown.
It relates to [[nature]], which is the total system of [[cause|necessary cause]]s producing [[effect|necessary effect]]s.

Chance also bears on the problem of [[evil]]: if [[eve]]nts are causally determined, then [[evil|natural evil]]s (earthquakes, diseases) are not [[punishment|divine punishment]]s but necessary consequences of natural laws — a position that undercuts [[injustice|theodicy]] without invo[[monarch|king]] [[probability|randomness]].

## In the Text

> Men may use the word "chance" to cover their ignorance of the true causes; nevertheless, although they may ignore them, these causes act, but by certain laws. There is no effect without a cause. (ch. 43)

> Nothing in nature can be made by chance; all follow fixed laws; these laws are but the necessary union of certain effects with their causes. An atom of matter does not meet another atom by accident or by hazard; this rencounter is due to permanent laws. (ch. 43)

> Everything is made by chance for those who do not understand nature, the properties of beings, and the effects which must necessarily result from the concurrence of certain causes. (ch. 43)

> It is not chance that has placed the sun in the center of our planetary system; it is by its very essence, the substance of which it is composed, that it occupies this place. (ch. 43)
