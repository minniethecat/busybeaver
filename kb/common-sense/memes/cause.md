# Cause

## Definition

Cause, in Holbach's "Common Sense," is the necessary connection between antecedent conditions and their inevitable effects within [[universe|nature]]. Holbach denies the existence of uncaused [[eve]]nts — "There is no effect without a cause" (ch. 43) — and extends this principle to its logical conclusion: [[nature|the universe]] itself is not an effect requiring an external cause, but rather "the cause of all effects" (ch. 43). This reframing eliminates the need for a supernatural First Cause and grounds causation entirely within the [[earth|material world]].

## Historical Context

The concept of causation was central to eighteenth-century debates between theists and materialists. The dominant argument for [[god|God]]'s existence — the cosmological or "First Cause" argument — held that [[eve]]ry effect requires a cause, and the chain of causes must terminate in an uncaused First Cause, identified with [[god|God]]. Holbach's response was radical: he accepted the premise that [[eve]]ry [[eve]]nt has a cause, but denied that [[nature|the universe]] as a whole is an "effect" requiring expla[[nations|nation]]. The universe simply *is*; it is the totality of causes and effects, requiring nothing beyond itself. This position drew on [[baruch-spinoza|Spinoza]]'s substance monism and anticipated Laplace's famous remark to Napoleon that he had "no need of that hypothesis" regarding [[god|God]].

## Core Arguments

**1. The universe is not an effect but the cause of all effects.** Holbach rejects the question "What caused [[nature|the universe]]?" as ill-formed. "The universe is not an effect; it is the cause of all effects; all the [[existence|being]]s it embraces are the [[effect|necessary effect]]s of this cause" (ch. 43). The universe is self-subsistent matter in motion, not a created artifact.

**2. "[[chance|Chance]]" is a word for ignorance, not an expla[[nations|nation]].** "Men may use the word '[[probability|chance]]' to cover their ignorance of the true causes; nevertheless, although they may ignore them, these causes act, but by certain laws" (ch. 43). To invoke [[probability|chance]] is to confess ignorance; to invoke [[god|God]] is to do the same under a different name.

**3. The First Cause argument is theological evasion.** Holbach challenges theologians to "leave to theologians their 'First Cause,' of which [[universe|nature]] has no need in order to produce all the effects which you see" (ch. 40). [[nature|Nature]] is self-sufficient; positing a [[divinity|divine]] cause merely displaces the [[secrecy|mystery]].

**4. Secondary causes suffice for all phenomena.** Holbach ridicules the idea that [[god|God]] must intervene in daily affairs: "Without these secondary causes, the First Cause (at least in the majority of [[nations|countries]]) could provide for none of his needs" (ch. 52). The observation that natural causes explain [[eve]]nts renders [[supernatural|the supernatural]] hypothesis otiose.

**5. [[religion|Religion]] is the first cause of social disorder.** In a stri[[monarch|king]] inversion, Holbach identifies religion itself as a causal agent of [[suffering|human misery]]: "[[religion|Religion]], far from rendering men happier, is the first cause of the greatest disorders, and of the permanent calamities with which the human race is afflicted" (ch. 182).

## Reception and Critique

Holbach's deterministic causation was extreme [[eve]]n for the Enlightenment. [[david-hume|David Hume]] had already undermined the rationalist conception of necessary connection, arguing that causation is observed only as constant conjunction, not logical necessity. Kant responded to [[david-hume|Hume]] by ma[[monarch|king]] causality a category of the [[knowledge|understanding]] — a condition of possible experience rather than a property of things-in-themselves. Later, quantum mechanics would challenge the universality of deterministic causation at the microphysical level. Nevertheless, Holbach's core insight — that explaining [[earth|the world]] does not require a supernatural cause — remains a foundational principle of methodological [[materialism|naturalism]] in [[knowledge|science]].

## Relation to Other Concepts

Causation is the metaphysical backbone of Holbach's entire system. It grounds his [[Materialism]] by ma[[monarch|king]] matter self-moving and self-sufficient. It underpins his rejection of [[God]] as an unnecessary hypothesis. It supports his [[Determinism]] — the view that all [[eve]]nts, including human choices, follow necessarily from prior causes. It is closely related to [[Nature]], [[Necessity]], and [[Order]], and stands in opposition to [[Chance]], [[Miracle]], and [[Creation]].

## In the Text

Holbach's most concentrated statement on causation appears in his chapter on [[probability|chance]]:

> "The universe is not an effect; it is the cause of all effects; all the beings it embraces are the necessary effects of this cause which sometimes shows to us its manner of acting, but which often hides from us its way. Men may use the word 'chance' to cover their ignorance of the true causes; nevertheless, although they may ignore them, these causes act, but by certain laws. There is no effect without a cause."
>
> (043-001)

On the rejection of the First Cause:

> "Come down from an imaginary into a real world; take hold of second causes; leave to theologians their 'First Cause,' of which nature has no need in order to produce all the effects which you see."
>
> (040-001)

On religion as social cause:

> "The rational man becomes incredulous, because he sees that religion, far from rendering men happier, is the first cause of the greatest disorders, and of the permanent calamities with which the human race is afflicted."
>
> (182-002)
