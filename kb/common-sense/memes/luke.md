# Luke

## Biography

Luke (also called Luke the Evangelist or Saint Luke) is identified in [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian tradition as a physician and companion of [[paul|Paul]] the Apostle, and as the author of the Third [[gospel|Gospel]] and the Acts of the Apostles. According to the letter to the Colossians (4:14), [[paul|Paul]] refers to "Luke, the beloved physician," and in the second letter to Timothy (4:11) he notes that "only Luke is with me." Early church tradition — preserved by Irenaeus and Eusebius — holds that Luke was a Gentile convert (the only non-Jewish author in the [[bible|New Testament]] canon), that he was a native of Antioch in Syria, and that he wrote his [[gospel|Gospel]] and Acts while accompanying [[paul|Paul]] on his [[missionaries|missionary]] journeys. Luke's [[gospel|Gospel]] is distinctive among the synoptics for its birth narrative, which includes the annunciation to [[mary|Mary]], the journey to [[bethlehem|Bethlehem]] for the census under Quirinius, the manger, the shepherds, and the presentation at the [[temple|Temple]] — elements entirely absent from [[mark|Mark]] and [[matthew|Matthew]].

## Role in Religious History

Luke's [[gospel|Gospel]] and Acts together constitute approximately one quarter of the [[bible|New Testament]], ma[[monarch|king]] Luke the most prolific contributor to the canon. His [[gospel|Gospel]] emphasizes themes of [[mercy|divine mercy]], concern for the poor and marginalized, and the inclusion of Gentiles in the [[christianity|Christian]] [[society|community]]. The parables of the Good Samaritan and the Prodigal Son appear only in Luke. Acts provides the earliest narrative of the early church's expansion from [[jerusalem|Jerusalem]] to [[rome|Rome]], and it is the primary source for early [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian history — the conversion of [[paul|Paul]], the Council of [[jerusalem|Jerusalem]], and the [[missionaries|missionary]] journeys.

Yet Luke's very distinctiveness creates problems for the doctrine of biblical inerrancy. His birth narrative — with the census of Quirinius requiring [[joseph|Joseph]] and [[mary|Mary]] to travel from [[nazareth|Nazareth]] to [[bethlehem|Bethlehem]] — is historically diffi[[worship|cult]] to reconcile with the known dates of Quirinius's governorship and the reign of [[herod|Herod the Great]]. Luke's genealogy of [[jesus-christ|Jesus]] (Luke 3:23–38) traces through Nathan, son of [[david|David]], rather than through [[solomon|Solomon]] as [[matthew|Matthew]] does, producing two incompatible lists of [[jesus-christ|Jesus]]'s patrilineal ancestors.

## Connection to "Common Sense"

Holbach does not [[guilt|sin]]gle Luke out for personal attack — his target is the composite biblical narrative — but Luke's distinctive contributions to the [[bible|New Testament]] furnish Holbach with contradictions of exactly the kind he deploys to undermine biblical authority.

**1. The genea[[contradiction|logical contradiction]].** Holbach's catalogue of contradictions in Chapter 2 foregrounds the incompatibility between Luke's genealogy and [[matthew|Matthew]]'s:

> "Matthew makes Jesus descend from King David through his son Solomon... and Luke makes Him come down from David through Nathan." (Chapter 2)

Two different lines of descent cannot both be the lineage of the same man. The [[model|standard]] apologetic defense — that one genealogy traces [[joseph|Joseph]]'s line and the other [[mary|Mary]]'s — is nowhere stated in the text and would not resolve the problem [[eve]]n if it were, [[guilt|sin]]ce both genealogies explicitly name [[joseph|Joseph]]. For Holbach, this is not a trivial discrepancy but a [[proof|demonstration]] that [[gospel|the Gospel]] authors fabricated genealogies to establish [[jesus-christ|Jesus]]'s [[david|David]]ic credentials.

**2. [[contradiction|Contradiction]] with [[matthew|Matthew]] on the flight to [[egypt|Egypt]].** Luke's in[[imagination|fancy]] narrative makes no mention of [[herod|Herod]]'s massacre of the innocents, the flight to [[egypt|Egypt]], or the wise men guided by a star — all central to [[matthew|Matthew]]'s account. Instead, Luke has [[joseph|Joseph]] and [[mary|Mary]] return peacefully to [[nazareth|Nazareth]] after the presentation at the [[temple|Temple]]:

> "Luke asserts that Joseph and the mother of Jesus lived peaceably during six weeks in the place where their child Jesus was born... and when the time prescribed by the law for the purification of His mother had arrived, she and Joseph, her husband, carried Him to Jerusalem... after which they returned to Galilee, into their town of Nazareth, where their child Jesus grew every day in grace and in wisdom." (Chapter 2)

Holbach notes that Luke "makes no mention of their flight into [[egypt|Egypt]], nor of the cruelty of [[herod|Herod]] toward the children of the province of [[bethlehem|Bethlehem]]." A massacre of infants — the kind of [[eve]]nt that would be widely recorded — appears in no contemporary source, Jewish or Roman. A historian who narrated [[herod|Herod]]'s atrocities in detail, [[josephus|Josephus]], does not mention it. For Holbach, the silence of Luke and of secular history is further [[proof|evidence]] that [[matthew|Matthew]]'s in[[imagination|fancy]] narrative is a pious fiction.

**3. [[contradiction|Contradiction]] on the ascension.** Holbach catalogues the ascension accounts and finds Luke's version incompatible with the others:

> "They contradict each other in regard to His pretended ascension to heaven; for Luke and Mark say positively that He went to heaven in presence of the eleven apostles, but neither Matthew nor John mentions at all this pretended ascension." (Chapter 2)

Luke narrates the ascension not only in his [[gospel|Gospel]] but also in Acts — yet the two Lukan accounts themselves differ: in [[gospel|the Gospel]], the ascension occurs on Easter [[eve]]ning in Bethany; in Acts, it occurs forty days later on the Mount of Olives. Luke contradicts not only the other [[missionaries|evangelists]] but also himself.

## Key Ideas Attributed / Critiqued

**1. Luke as fabricator of genealogical credentials.** For Holbach, the genealogies of [[matthew|Matthew]] and [[luke|Luke]] are not attempts at accurate record-keeping but theological fabrications designed to establish [[jesus-christ|Jesus]]'s [[david|David]]ic ancestry. The incompatibility of the two lists exposes the enterprise as contrived.

**2. The in[[imagination|fancy]] narratives as later mythological additions.** That [[mark|Mark]] — the earliest [[gospel|Gospel]] — has no birth narrative at all, that [[matthew|Matthew]] adds one with a flight to [[egypt|Egypt]] and a massacre, and that [[luke|Luke]] adds an entirely different one with a census and shepherds, suggests a progressive mythological elaboration. Holbach treats this as a [[model|pattern]]: the earlier the source, the fewer the [[miracle|miracles]]; the later the source, the more elaborate [[supernatural|the supernatural]] claims.

**3. The growth of legend.** Luke's Acts of the Apostles records the growth of the early church through signs and [[miracle|wonder]]s — healings, exorcisms, prison escapes, and [[dreams|visions]]. For Holbach, reading Acts alongside the contradictory [[gospel|Gospel]] accounts yields a consistent picture not of [[divinity|divine]] action in history but of a [[society|community]] generating and accumulating legends in the service of a growing movement.

## Legacy

Luke's [[gospel|Gospel]] has shaped [[christianity|Christian]] imagi[[nations|nation]] profoundly: the [[jesus-christ|Christ]]mas story of shepherds and [[angel|angels]], the parables of the Good Samaritan and the Prodigal Son, and the narrative of the walk to Emmaus are all exclusively Lukan. Luke's Acts remains the foundational text of church history, and his emphasis on the inclusion of Gentiles set the course for a Jewish messianic movement to become a [[universe|world]] [[religion|religion]].

From Holbach's critical perspective, Luke is the clearest example of the progressive mythological inflation of the [[jesus-christ|Jesus]] story. The earliest [[gospel|Gospel]] — [[mark|Mark]] — begins with [[jesus-christ|Jesus]]'s baptism as an adult. [[matthew|Matthew]] adds a miraculous birth and a flight to [[egypt|Egypt]]. Luke adds an annunciation, a census, shepherds, and [[angel|angels]]. [[john-the-evangelist|John]] adds a pre-existent Logos who was with [[god|God]] in the beginning. The direction of development is always toward [[supernatural|the supernatural]], never away from it — which, for Holbach, is precisely what one would expect of myth-ma[[monarch|king]], not of history.
