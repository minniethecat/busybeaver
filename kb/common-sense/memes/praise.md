# Praise

## Definition

In Holbach's *Common Sense*, "praise" appears primarily in the context of praising Providence — the act of thanking God for favorable effects in nature. Holbach treats this praise as a category mistake: humans "praise and thank Providence for its favorable effects" (002-054), but Providence is an imaginary entity, and the praise directed toward it is misdirected. The praise that should go to nature, to human effort, and to social cooperation is instead siphoned off by an invisible and unresponsive recipient.

## The Economy of Divine Praise

Holbach analyzes praise as part of a transactional economy between humans and their gods. The faithful are "filled with the most flattering praises of Providence, whose attentions they assure us are incessantly directed towards supplying the necessary wants of so many weak and ungrateful beings" (002-074). This praise serves a double function: it flatters the deity (securing continued favor) and it reinforces the believer's own conviction (the more one praises, the more real the object of praise seems). But for Holbach, this is a self-deception — praising Providence does not prove Providence exists; it only proves that humans are capable of directing gratitude toward fictions.

## Praise Across Religions

Holbach's critique of praise extends beyond Christianity. He observes a universal pattern: "filled with the most flattering praises of Providence" (052-003) is not peculiar to one religion but common to all — each tradition lavishes praise upon its own conception of the divine, using the same rhetorical forms for incompatible objects. The Roman praised Jupiter for victory in battle; the Christian praised God for the same outcome. For Holbach, this cross-cultural consistency in the form of praise, combined with radical inconsistency in its object, suggests that praise is a projection of human psychology rather than a response to divine reality. The emotion of gratitude is real and natural; its misdirection toward imaginary beings is a cultural achievement of priesthoods everywhere.

## The Irony of Praise

Holbach's treatment of praise is characteristically ironic. He asks, "oh, Father of nature! what praises could be worthy to celebrate thy works?" (002-173) — and the implied answer is: none, because there is no Father of nature to receive them. The lavish language of religious praise, when examined rationally, reveals not the greatness of God but the imaginative capacity of humans to construct objects of devotion. "They praise, with emphasis, charity" (002-356) — but Holbach suspects that the emphasis of the praise is inversely proportional to the reality of the virtue being praised.

> We praise and thank Providence for its favorable effects, without ever perceiving that it is to Nature alone that we owe these benefits.
>
> (002-054)
