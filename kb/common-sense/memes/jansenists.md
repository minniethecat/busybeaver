# Jansenists

## Definition

The **Jansenists** were the adherents of a Catholic reform movement that emerged from the posthumous publication of Cornelius Jansen's *Augustinus* (1640). Centered at the Abbey of Port-Royal near [[paris|Paris]], the movement emphasized radical Augustinian doctrines: the total depravity of human [[nature|nature]] after [[original-sin|the Fall]], the irresistibility of [[grace|divine grace]], and a rigorous moral [[discipline|discipline]] that demanded visible holiness from the [[religion|faith]]ful. The Jansenists were the defining theological adversaries of the [[jesuits|Jesuits]] throughout the 17th and 18th centuries — a conflict that Holbach exploits in "Common Sense" to [[demon|demons]]trate the mutual contradiction of [[religion|religious]] systems.

## The Jesuit-Jansenist Controversy

Holbach references the Jansenists in the context of the century-long theological war between them and the [[jesuits|Jesuits]]:

> "This doctrine appears heretical, ungodly, and abominable to the Jansenists, who, by the revolting severity which they attribute to their God, render Him still less lovable than their adversaries, the Jesuits. The latter, in order to make converts, represent God as a merciful and easily-appeased monarch." (ch. II, citing "Apology, Des Lettres Provinciales," Tome II)

Holbach's argument is devastating in its simplicity: the Jansenists and [[jesuits|Jesuits]] both claim to worship the same [[god|God]], yet they attribute mutually contradictory characters to Him. The Jansenist [[god|God]] is severe, exacting, and predestines most of humanity to [[hell|damnation]]; the Jesuit [[god|God]] is merciful, accommodating, and eager to forgive. Both portraits cannot be true — and the fact that each [[sect|faction]] constructs a [[god|God]] tailored to its own theological system suggests that *neither* portrait is true. The [[god|deity]] is a projection of institutional needs, not a revelation of objective reality.

## Port-Royal and the Politics of Grace

The Abbey of Port-Royal became the intellectual and [[soul|spirit]]ual center of Jansenism, attracting figures of the highest caliber: [[pascal|Blaise Pascal]], whose *Lettres Provinciales* (1656–1657) was the movement's great polemical defense against Jesuit attacks; Antoine Arnauld, the theologian known as "the Great Arnauld"; and Jean Racine, the tragedian, who was educated at the Port-Royal schools.

The movement's political trajectory illustrates the entanglement of [[religion|theology]] and [[government|state power]] that Holbach decries. Louis XIV, urged by his Jesuit confessors and alarmed by the Jansenists' perceived republican sympathies, ordered the destruction of Port-Royal in 1710–1713. The buildings were razed, the cemetery exhumed, and the remaining nuns dispersed. The papal bull *Unigenitus* (1713) formally condemned 101 Jansenist propositions. Yet Jansenism survived as an underground movement, its rigorist [[spirit|spirituality]] shaping French Catholicism well into the 18th century — and its resistance to both papal and royal authority ma[[monarch|king]] it a precursor to the [[revolution|revolutionary]] [[soul|spirit]] that would erupt in 1789.

## The Severity Problem

Holbach's specific critique of Jansenism focuses on the *severity* of its [[god|God]]. The Jansenists, following Augustine and Jansen, taught that:

- [[god|God]] predestines the majority of humanity to eternal [[hell|damnation]]
- [[grace|Divine grace]] is irresistible — those who receive it cannot refuse it
- Those who do not receive it are incapable of any genuinely good act
- The number of the elect is small and fixed from [[eternal|eternity]]

This is, Holbach argues, a theological portrait of "revolting severity" — a [[god|God]] less lovable than the [[jesuits|Jesuit]] version, but no more rationally defensible. Both portraits share the same structural flaw: they attribute to a supposedly infinite and benevolent [[existence|being]] characteristics that would be considered [[vice|vicious]] in any human [[sovereign|ruler]].
