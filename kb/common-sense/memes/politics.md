# Politics

## Definition

Politics, as Holbach treats it, is not a neutral domain of [[government|governance]] but a sphere corrupted at its foundation by religion.
His central claim is unambiguous: "[[religion|Religion]] is fatal to politics; it forms but licentious and perverse [[despotism|despot]]s, as well as abject and unhappy subjects" (ch. 145).
The problem is structural, not [[chance|accident]]al.
When [[monarch|ruler]]s derive their authority from a supernatural source, they become accountable to no earthly check; when subjects are taught that their true homeland is not of this [[universe|world]], they cease to demand justice in the present one.

Holbach distinguishes between politics properly understood — the art of securing the common welfare through rational legislation — and politics as it actually exists under the influence of religion: a system for extracting obedience from populations who have been taught that rebellion against the [[monarch|sovereign]] is rebellion against [[god|God]].
"The union of religion with politics has necessarily caused a double legislation in the [[politics-index|State]]s" (ch. 173).
The law of [[god|God]], interpreted by [[clergy|priests]], is often contrary to the law of the [[monarch|sovereign]] or to the interest of the people.

## Historical Context

Holbach wrote in the final decades of the *ancien régime*, a political order in which the French [[monarch|monarchy]] claimed [[divinity|divine]] right and the [[catholic-church|Catholic Church]] was the largest landowner in the [[monarch|king]]dom.
The alliance of throne and altar was not a metaphor — it was a [[temperament|constitution]]al reality.
Louis XV's coro[[nations|nation]] at Reims included anointment with [[sacred|holy]] oil, and his authority was understood to descend from [[god|God]], not from the consent of the governed.

The eighteenth century had already produced powerful critiques of this arrangement.
Montesquieu's *The [[spirit|Spirit]] of the [[law|Law]]s* (1748) analyzed [[tyranny|despotism]] as a distinct form of government, characterized by rule through fear rather than law.
Rousseau's *Social Contract* (1762) derived political legitimacy from the general will, not from [[divinity|divine]] appointment.
Holbach synthesized these currents into a more radical thesis: religion does not merely fail to improve politics — it actively destroys it.

## Core Arguments

**1. [[religion|Religion]] produces [[despotism|despot]]s, not virtuous [[monarch|ruler]]s.**
Holbach asks whether fear of [[god|God]] restrains [[monarch|prince]]s: "Does not the unjust and cruel manner in which so many [[nation|nations]] are governed here below, furnish the most visible [[evidence|proof]]s, not only of the non-effect produced by the fear of another life, but of the non-existence of a [[providence|Providence]] interested in the fate of the human race?" (ch. 145).
The [[evidence|empirical evidence]] is overwhelming — religious [[monarch|prince]]s are no less cruel than irreligious ones.

**2. [[christianity|Christianity]] was designed to serve tyrants.**
"The [[christianity|Christian religion]], devised for the benefit of tyrants, was established on the principle that the [[nation|nations]] should renounce the legitimate defense of themselves" (ch. 146).
Holbach traces how [[christianity|Christianity]] insinuated itself among barbarian [[monarch|king]]s by offering [[divinity|divine]] sanction for absolute power, and how its ministers moderated these principles only when it suited their temporal interests.

**3. [[theology-index|Divine]] right disarms resistance to real [[injustice|evil]]s.**
"[[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian [[nation|nations]] are deprived of the first law of [[universe|nature]], which decrees that man should resist [[injustice|evil]] and disarm all who attempt to destroy him" (ch. 146).
Subjects who fix their eyes on [[heaven|Heaven]] are prevented "from perceiving the real causes of their [[evil|suffering]]s and from applying the remedies which [[universe|nature]] offers them" (ch. 147).

**4. The union of religion and politics creates competing [[monarch|sovereign]]ties.**
"[[nothingness|Nothing]] is more diffi[[worship|cult]] than to serve two masters, especially when they do not agree upon what they demand of their subjects" (ch. 173).
The [[priest|clergy]] constitute a rival power center — one that commands opinion, owns property, and claims immunity from civil law.

**5. Politics must be secularized to serve human welfare.**
Holbach's positive vision is of a politics oriented toward "the true principles of administration, the aim and the rights of social life, the real interests of men, and the duties which unite them" (ch. 145).
[[religion|Religion]] has prevented [[monarch|ruler]]s from studying these subjects by substituting [[imagination|chimeras]] for knowledge.

## Reception and Critique

Holbach's political a[[divinity|theism]] influenced the radical phase of [[revolution|the French Revolution]], particularly the Civil Constitution of the [[clergy|Clergy]] (1790) and the de-[[jesus-christ|Christ]]ianization campaign.
His argument that religion is structurally allied with [[tyranny|despotism]] was taken up by Marx in his critique of the Prussian state and by anarchist thinkers who extended the critique to all hierarchical authority.

Conservative critics, from Edmund Burke to [[joseph|Joseph]] de Maistre, argued that Holbach underestimated the [[utility|social utility]] of religion — its capacity to bind communities, legitimize law, and provide a moral vocabulary that secular reason cannot easily replace.
Tocqueville later argued that religion in America functioned not as an instrument of [[tyranny|despotism]] but as a voluntary association that checked state power.

[[mode|Mode]]rn secularization theory ([[peter|Peter]] Berger, Charles Taylor) has complicated Holbach's picture: the separation of church and state did not always produce the rational society he envisioned, and the "double legislation" he attacked has proven remarkably durable in hybrid forms.

## Relation to Other Concepts

Politics is the domain in which [[authority]] is exercised, and Holbach's critique of [[authority|religious authority]] is fundamentally a political critique.
The [[priest]] is a political actor — one who claims a jurisdiction independent of and superior to the civil magistrate.

Politics inter[[sect|sects]] with [[despotism]]: religious politics produces [[despotism|despot]]s because it removes the only real check on power, which is the consent and resistance of the governed.
It is opposed to [[reason]], which Holbach sees as the proper foundation of political order — legislation grounded in the study of human needs, not in the supposed will of an invisible monarch.

The concept of [[law]] is central: under religious influence, law becomes arbitrary commandment rather than rational regulation of social life.
