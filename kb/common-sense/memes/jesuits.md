# Jesuits

## Overview

The Society of Jesus (Jesuits) is a Catholic religious order of [[clergy|priests]] and brothers founded by Ignatius of Loyola in 1540 and approved by Pope [[paul|Paul]] III. Known for their vows of obedience to the Pope, their educational institutions, and their [[missionaries|missionary]] activities, the Jesuits became one of the most influential and controversial religious orders in Catholic history. In Holbach's "Common Sense" (1772), the Jesuits appear as [[model|exemplar]]s of the clerical class that profits from and perpetuates religious superstition.

## Historical Background

The Jesuit order was established during the Counter-[[reform|Reform]]ation as a response to the Protestant [[Reformation]]. Its members took a special fourth vow of obedience to the Pope, ma[[monarch|king]] them a mobile and disciplined force for Catholic orthodoxy. Jesuits established schools and universities across [[europe|Europe]], served as confessors to monarchs, and conducted [[missionaries|missionary]] work in Asia, the Americas, and Africa. By the 18th century, their political influence had made them targets of suspicion and [[cruelty|persecution]]. They were expelled from Portugal (1759), [[paris|France]] (1764), and S[[suffering|pain]] (1767), and the order was suppressed by the Pope in 1773 — one year after "Common Sense" was published.

## Structure and Doctrine

The Jesuits are organized under a Superior General (often called the "Black Pope" for the order's influence). Their Constitutions emphasize obedience, adaptability, and intellectual rigor. Jesuit [[spirit|spirituality]], articulated in Loyola's "[[spirit|Spirit]]ual Exercises," stresses discernment, exami[[nations|nation]] of con[[knowledge|science]], and finding [[god|God]] "in all things." The order's educational network made it a major force in the intellectual life of Catholic [[europe|Europe]].

## Critique in "Common Sense"

While "Common Sense" does not devote a separate chapter to the Jesuits, the order appears in the work's broader condem[[nations|nation]] of the clerical system. [[voltaire|Voltaire]]'s biographical preface mentions that among Meslier's books was found "a printed manuscript of the Treatise of Fenelon... upon the existence of [[god|God]] and His [[attribute|attributes]], and the reflections of the Jesuit Tournemine (APP007-045) upon [[atheism|Atheism]], to which treatise he added marginal notes signed by his hand." This detail places the Jesuits among the theologians whose arguments Meslier studied and rejected.

Holbach's critique applies to the Jesuits as part of the general clerical apparatus: men who "laugh at the simplicity of the blind people" and who "grow rich by your sweat and your [[suffering|pain]]s." The order's intellectual sophistication — its engagement with philosophy and [[knowledge|science]] — made it, in Holbach's implicit judgment, more culpable than the ignorant parish [[priest|clergy]], for the Jesuits deployed [[education|learning]] in the service of what he considered systematic [[imposture|deception]].

## Legacy and Modern Relevance

The Jesuit order was restored in 1814 and remains active in education, social justice advocacy, and theological scholarship. Its historical role as the intellectual vanguard of the Counter-[[reform|Reform]]ation makes it a significant case study in the relationship between institutional religion and Enlightenment critique. Holbach's treatment — brief but pointed — exemplifies the Enlightenment's view that educated [[priest|clergy]] were, if anything, more dangerous than ignorant ones because they lent intellectual credibility to "pious lies."
