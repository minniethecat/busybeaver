# Remorse

## Definition

Remorse is the [[suffering|pain]]ful self-awareness that arises when a person recognizes they have acted in ways that harm others or violate shared social norms. In Holbach's materialist framework, [[conscience|remorse]] is not a supernatural "voice of [[god|God]]" spea[[monarch|king]] through conscience, but a natural product of human psychology — the inward fear of having "deserved the hatred of their fellow-[[existence|being]]s."

Unlike theological accounts that treat [[conscience|remorse]] as [[proof|evidence]] of an [[soul|immortal soul]] or [[divinity|divine]] moral law, Holbach understands it as entirely explicable through natural causes: social conditioning, empathy, and the rational calculation of consequences.

## Historical Context

In eighteenth-century Enlightenment thought, the [[universe|nature]] of conscience and moral feeling was a central point of contention between religious and secular thinkers. [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian [[religion|theology]] — both Catholic and Protestant — held that conscience was a [[divinity|divine]] faculty implanted by [[god|God]], a "still small voice" through which [[creator|the Creator]] guided and judged human action. Bishop [[joseph|Joseph]] Butler's *Sermons on [[humanity|Human Nature]]* (1726) famously argued that conscience was supreme among human faculties, a direct representative of [[divinity|divine]] authority within the soul.

The French materialists rejected this framing entirely. Following [[john-locke|Locke]]'s empiricism, they argued that all mental phenomena — including moral sentiments — arise from sensation and reflection. Helvétius, in *De l'Esprit* (1758), traced moral feelings to education and social interest. Holbach extended this line of thought in *Common Sense*, treating [[conscience|remorse]] as a learned response to the fear of social disapproval and legal punishment, not a metaphysical signal.

## Core Arguments

**1. Remorse has natural, not supernatural, causes.** The [[suffering|pain]] of [[conscience|remorse]] arises from the same psychological mechanisms as any other fear — the anticipation of negative consequences. A person feels [[conscience|remorse]] because they fear "the punishments which the laws inflict" and "the contempt" of others, not because a [[god|deity]] has inscribed moral law on their heart.

**2. [[religion|Religion]] undermines genuine [[conscience|remorse]].** Holbach argues that religious practices — confession, absolution, [[baptism|ritual purification]] — actually weaken the natural restraint of [[conscience|remorse]] by offering cheap [[absolution|forgiveness]]. The believer "persuades himself that his periodical homage to [[god|God]] gives him the right to follow without [[conscience|remorse]] his vicious practices."

**3. Princes and [[monarch|ruler]]s are especially vulnerable to religious neutralization of [[conscience|remorse]].** In a stri[[monarch|king]] political argument, Holbach contends that "religion favors the errors of [[monarch|prince]]s, by delivering them from fear and [[conscience|remorse]]." When [[clergy|priests]] tell [[monarch|sovereign]]s they are [[god|God]]'s representatives, [[monarch|ruler]]s are "entitled, without fear and without [[conscience|remorse]], to the errors which are as fatal to themselves as to the [[nation|nations]] which they govern."

**4. True moral restraint comes from visible, not invisible, sanctions.** The only effective checks on human behavior are "true and visible restraints" — law, [[opinion|public opinion]], and the natural fear of social rejection. Imaginary posthumous punishments cannot substitute for real-[[universe|world]] accountability.

## In the Text

Holbach develops his argument about [[conscience|remorse]] most directly in the chapter titled "[[religion|Religion]] Favors the [[error|Error]]s of Princes, by Delivering Them from [[fear|Fear]] and Remorse" (ch. CLI):

> "If the sacerdotal flatteries succeed in perverting princes and changing them into tyrants, the latter on their side necessarily corrupt the priests by their benefits; religion, far from being a restraint upon the sovereigns, entitles them, without fear and without remorse, to the errors which are as fatal to themselves as to the nations which they govern."

In a more psychological register, he describes the natural basis of conscience (ch. CXL):

> "He can fear himself; he can be afraid of the remorse that all those experience whose conscience reproaches them for having deserved the hatred of their fellow-beings. Conscience is the inward knowledge we have of our actions, and of the sentiments they inspire in others."

The autobiographical voice of [[meslier|Jean Meslier]] — the priest whose posthumous testament inspired Holbach — adds personal weight to this argument. Meslier describes celebrating [[sacrament|sacraments]] "with the solemnity which awakened all your piety" while feeling "what [[conscience|remorse]] I had for exciting your [[belief|credulity]]! A thousand times upon the point of bursting forth publicly, I was going to open your eyes."

## Relation to Other Concepts

Remorse is closely linked to [[conscience]] (understood naturalistically), [[guilt]], [[fear]], and [[punishment]]. It stands in opposition to [[absolution]], [[confession]], and [[priestcraft]] — all religious mechanisms that Holbach views as devices for neutralizing the natural restraints that [[conscience|remorse]] provides. The concept also connects to [[virtue]] and [[morality]], which Holbach grounds in [[utility|social utility]] rather than [[divinity|divine]] command.
