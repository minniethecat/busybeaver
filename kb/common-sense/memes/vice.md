# Vice

## Definition

Holbach's treatment of vice is part of his broader project of secularizing morality. In the theological framework, vice is [[guilt|sin]] — a [[crime|transgression]] against [[divinity|divine]] law that offends [[god|God]] and merits punishment in this [[universe|world]] or the next. Holbach rejects this framework entirely. Vice, properly understood, is conduct that causes real harm to oneself or to others — harm that can be observed, measured, and remedied by social means. "[[virtue|Virtue]] is an advantage, and vice is an injury to [[existence|being]]s of their kind" (ch. 2). The criterion is not [[divinity|divine]] displeasure but human [[evil|suffering]].

This redefinition has radical implications. If vice is social harm, then the remedy for vice is not prayer, [[confession|penance]], or supernatural grace, but the reform of social conditions. "Do we pretend to cure mortals of their vices if we do not begin by curing them of their errors?" (ch. 2). The "errors" in question are religious illusions that misidentify the causes of vice — attributing it to [[sin|original sin]] or demonic temptation rather than to ignorance, bad institutions, and the corruption of power.

Holbach is particularly sharp on the vices of the powerful. "Is it not the ambition of [[monarch|king]]s and of the great, their negligence, their vices, their [[persecution|oppression]], to which are generally due sterility, mendacity, wars, contagions, bad morals, and all the multiplied scourges which desolate the earth?" (ch. 188). The theological focus on individual [[guilt|sin]] distracts attention from the systemic vices that cause the most widespread [[evil|suffering]]. [[religion|Religion]], far from restraining the vices of [[monarch|ruler]]s, "by its very principles, gives them a loose rein" (ch. 2) — because it teaches that [[divinity|divine]] [[absolution|forgiveness]] is always available, regardless of the harm done.

## Historical Context

Holbach's secular theory of vice belongs to the Enlightenment project of constructing a morality without [[religion|theology]]. Grotius had argued that natural law would be valid [[eve]]n if [[god|God]] did not exist; [[pierre-bayle|Bayle]] had argued that a society of [[atheism|atheist]]s could be moral; Helvétius had explained [[morality|virtue]] and vice entirely in terms of pleasure and [[suffering|pain]]. Holbach systematized these insights into a comprehensive ethical [[materialism|naturalism]].

The shift from [[guilt|sin]] to vice as the primary moral category was not merely terminological. "[[sin|Sin]]" implies a vertical relationship — an [[crime|offense]] against a superior [[existence|being]] — and the remedy is vertical: confession, absolution, grace. "Vice" implies a horizontal relationship — harm to fellow creatures — and the remedy is horizontal: education, better laws, reformed institutions. This horizontal reorientation of morality was one of the Enlightenment's most consequential innovations, and Holbach was among its most uncompromising advocates.

## Core Arguments

**1. Vice is social harm, not [[divinity|divine]] [[crime|offense]].** "Vice is an injury to [[existence|being]]s of their kind" (ch. 2). The proper measure of moral wrongness is the [[evil|suffering]] it causes to real people in this [[universe|world]], not the displeasure it causes to an invisible [[god|deity]] in another.

**2. [[religion|Religion]] is ineffective against vice.** Despite centuries of [[doctrine|religious teaching]], vice flourishes. "Far from [[existence|being]] a restraint to the passions of [[monarch|king]]s, religion, by its very principles, gives them a loose rein" (ch. 2). The promise of cheap [[absolution|forgiveness]] through ritual undermines the only real deterrent to vice: the natural consequences of harmful action.

**3. The vices of the powerful cause the most [[evil|suffering]].** Holbach directs attention away from personal peccadilloes and toward "the ambition of [[monarch|king]]s and of the great, their negligence, their vices, their [[persecution|oppression]]" (ch. 188) as the real sources of [[suffering|human misery]]. The theological fixation on individual [[guilt|sin]] protects systemic vice from scrutiny.

**4. Vice is cured by reason, not by grace.** "Do we pretend to cure mortals of their vices if we do not begin by curing them of their errors?" (ch. 2). The "errors" are [[error|false belief]]s about [[earth|the world]] — about [[god|God]], the [[death|afterlife]], and the sources of happiness — that lead people to misidentify their true interests. Enlightenment, not absolution, is the remedy for vice.

## Relation to Other Concepts

Vice is the negative pole of Holbach's ethical system, opposed to [[Virtue]] which is conduct that benefits oneself and others. In the theological framework, vice is called [[Sin]] — the same behavior, but judged by a different [[model|standard]]. [[Interest]] is the key to [[knowledge|understanding]] vice: vice results from miscalculating one's true interests, pursuing short-term gain at the cost of long-term [[happiness|well-being]]. [[Reason]] is the faculty that corrects this miscalculation. [[Justice]] is the social arrangement that aligns individual interest with collective good, ma[[monarch|king]] vice unprofitable. [[Morality]] is the [[knowledge|science]] of distinguishing [[morality|virtue]] from vice by reference to real human consequences rather than imaginary [[divinity|divine]] commands.

## In the Text

On vice as social injury:

> "Virtue is an advantage, and that vice is an injury to beings of their kind. To abstain from vice and from crime, not because they will be punished in another world, but because they will be punished in this world." (ch. 2)

On curing vice through reason:

> "Do we pretend to cure mortals of their vices if we do not begin by curing them of their errors? Is it not to vain and imaginary remedies that we must attribute their obstinate continuance in their disorders?" (ch. 2)

On the vices of the powerful:

> "Is it not the ambition of kings and of the great, their negligence, their vices, their oppression, to which are generally due sterility, mendacity, wars, contagions, bad morals, and all the multiplied scourges which desolate the earth?" (ch. 188)
