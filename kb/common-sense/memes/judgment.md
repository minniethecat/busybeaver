# Judgment

## Definition

Judgment in Holbach's *Common Sense* operates on two interrelated levels.

First, it denotes the ordinary human capacity for assessment — the "portion of judgment sufficient to know the most simple truths, to reject the most stri[[monarch|king]] [[absurdity|absurd]]ities, and to be shocked by palpable contradictions" (Preface). This is the faculty that common sense exercises when it evaluates [[proof|evidence]], weighs causes against effects, and distinguishes probable truth from inherited error.

Holbach treats judgment as an empirical faculty: "[[eve]]ry principle is a judgment; all judgment is the effect of experience; experience is not acquired but by the exercise of the senses" (004-001). Judgment is not innate; it is built from sensory encounters with [[earth|the world]].

Second, judgment refers to the [[doctrine|theological doctrine]] of [[divinity|divine]] judgment — [[god|God]]'s supposed evaluation and punishment of human conduct. Holbach subjects this doctrine to sustained critique, arguing that [[divinity|divine]] judgment is a mechanism for manufacturing fear, that it is incompatible with the observable facts of [[earth|the world]], and that the threat of [[hell|eternal punishment]] produces [[fear|terror]] rather than moral [[progress|improvement]].

The two levels converge in Holbach's central epistemological claim: to judge rightly, one must judge by effects; and by this [[model|standard]], the theological notion of [[divinity|divine]] judgment fails entirely.

## Historical Context

The concept of judgment occupied a central place in Enlightenment epistemology.

[[john-locke|Locke]] had argued that judgment is the faculty by which the mind "takes its ideas to agree or disagree" in the absence of [[demon|demons]]trative certainty — ma[[monarch|king]] judgment, not knowledge, the default state of human cognition. [[david-hume|Hume]] radicalized this line of thought by showing that causal judgment rests on [[habit|custom]] and experience rather than rational [[proof|demonstration]]. Holbach's treatment synthesizes these currents: judgment is empirical, fallible, and constrained by the data of the senses.

At the same time, the doctrine of [[divinity|divine]] judgment — the Last Judgment, particular judgment after death, [[god|God]]'s providential judgment in history — had been a cornerstone of [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian [[doctrine|teaching]] for centuries. The [[council-of-trent|Council of Trent]] had reaffirmed the reality of particular judgment in the sixteenth century, and popular preaching throughout the seventeenth and eighteenth centuries emphasized the [[fear|terror]] of [[hell|Hell]] as a motive for repentance.

Holbach's frontal assault on [[divinity|divine]] judgment is one of the most radical aspects of his critique: he denies not only the justice of [[divinity|divine]] judgment but its very coherence, arguing that the observable [[universe|world]] provides no [[proof|evidence]] of a judging intelligence.

## Core Arguments

1. **All judgment is the effect of experience.** Rejecting the Cartesian doctrine of innate ideas, Holbach insists that "[[eve]]ry principle is a judgment; all judgment is the effect of experience" (004-001). There are no judgments prior to sensory encounter with [[earth|the world]]. This principle applies with special force to religious concepts, which, having no sensory [[model|prototype]], cannot produce genuine judgments — only inherited verbal formulas.

2. **A cause must be judged by its effects.** "The logic of common sense teaches us that we should judge a cause but by its effects" (054-001). Applied to the [[god|God]] hypothesis, this criterion is devastating: a [[universe|world]] filled with disorder, [[evil|suffering]], and indiscriminate destruction cannot be the effect of an infinitely good and wise cause. If the observable effects are mixed, the cause must be judged as mixed — or, more radically, as nonexistent. Holbach argues that one cannot claim [[god|God]] is intelligent when "it is impossible for me to judge whether [[god|God]] is intelligent" (054-001) because the effects offer no [[proof|evidence]] of intelligent [[government|governance]].

3. **Human judgments are not free.** In his chapter on [[liberty|free will]], Holbach argues that "our judgments, true or false, are not free; they are necessarily determined by ideas which we have received, or which our mind has formed" (080-003). Judgment is a determinist process: it follows necessarily from the [[proof|evidence]] available to the mind, the temperament of the individual, and the strength of competing motives. The theological notion that [[god|God]] will punish erroneous judgments is therefore [[absurdity|absurd]] — no one chooses his beliefs.

4. **The fear of [[divinity|divine]] judgment is ineffective as a moral restraint.** Holbach observes that "there is scarcely a man who does not fear more what he sees than what he does not see; the judgments of men, of which he experiences the effects, than the judgments of [[god|God]], of whom he has but floating ideas" (142-001). Social judgment — honor, [[honor|reputation]], fear of ridicule — is a far stronger check on conduct than the remote threat of [[punishment|divine punishment]]. The idea of an "invisible Judge" (143-002) is too abstract to compete with the immediate pressures of passion and interest.

5. **Judgment should be based on conduct, not profession.** Holbach proposes a methodological rule for moral assessment: "Let us not judge men by their opinions, or opinions by men; let us judge men by their conduct; and their opinions by their conformity with experience, reason, and their [[utility|usefulness]] for mankind" (181-002). This inverts the religious [[model|standard]], which judges men by doctrinal orthodoxy rather than by their actual effects on human welfare.

## Reception and Critique

Holbach's treatment of judgment provoked fierce opposition from contemporaries who saw it as an assault on the very possibility of moral accountability.

If all judgment is determined, critics objected, then praise and blame lose their meaning. Holbach's response, developed throughout the later chapters on morality, is that society retains the right to restrain harmful conduct regardless of its metaphysical origins: [[effect|determinism]] does not entail permissiveness.

Later thinkers extended Holbach's argument in different directions. Nietzsche's genealogy of moral judgment — the claim that "good" and "[[injustice|evil]]" are historical inventions rather than [[divinity|divine]] decrees — shares Holbach's determi[[nations|nation]] to trace judgment to its [[universe|world]]ly origins. The American pragmatists, particularly [[will|Will]]iam James and John Dewey, echoed Holbach's criterion that beliefs must be judged by their practical effects.

On the theological side, the problem of [[divinity|divine]] judgment that Holbach pressed so forcefully — how can a just [[god|God]] condemn creatures whose judgments are not free? — has never been satisfactorily resolved within orthodox [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian [[religion|theology]].

## Relation to Other Concepts

Judgment is inseparable from [[Reason]], which for Holbach is the proper tribunal before which all claims must be judged: "to challenge reason as a judge of [[religion|faith]], is it not acknowledging that reason is not favorable to [[religion|faith]]?" (135-001).

It is connected to [[Free Will]], [[vice|sin]]ce the determinist account of judgment undermines the theological premise that [[god|God]] can justly punish human choices. Judgment relates to [[Fear]], the [[passion|emotion]]al state that [[divinity|divine]] judgment is designed to produce and that substitutes [[fear|terror]] for rational assessment.

Judgment links to [[Justice]] — Holbach's argument that [[divinity|divine]] judgment is incompatible with genuine justice, [[vice|sin]]ce it punishes finite [[crime|offense]]s with infinite [[torture|torment]]s. It is also tied to [[Skepticism]], which Holbach defines as lac[[monarch|king]] "the motives necessary to establish a judgment" (123-003).

Finally, judgment is the faculty that [[Custom]] systematically disables by substituting inherited habit for critical evaluation.

## In the Text

> "The logic of common sense teaches us that we should judge a cause but by its effects. A cause can not be reputed as constantly good, except when it constantly produces good, useful, and agreeable effects. A cause which produces good at one time, and evil at another, is a cause which is sometimes good and sometimes bad." (054-001)

> "There is scarcely a man who does not fear more what he sees than what he does not see; the judgments of men, of which he experiences the effects, than the judgments of God, of whom he has but floating ideas. The desire to please the world, the current of custom, the fear of being ridiculed, and of 'what will they say?' have more power than all religious opinions." (142-001)

> "Let us not judge men by their opinions, or opinions by men; let us judge men by their conduct; and their opinions by their conformity with experience, reason, and their usefulness for mankind." (181-002)

> "Every principle is a judgment; all judgment is the effect of experience; experience is not acquired but by the exercise of the senses: from which it follows that religious principles are drawn from nothing, and are not innate." (004-001)
