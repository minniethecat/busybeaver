# Torture

## Definition

Torture, in Holbach's analysis, is not merely a method of punishment or interrogation but the logical endpoint of religious [[intolerance]]. When religious authorities claim to possess exclusive [[truth]] about the will of [[god]], those who dissent become not merely mistaken but impious — and the pious are licensed, [[eve]]n obligated, to inflict [[evil|suffering]] on them. "Blind and bigoted [[monarch|prince]]s, you hate, you persecute, you devote [[heresy|heretic]]s to torture, because you are persuaded that these unfortunate ones displease [[god|God]]" (ch. CLVII).

The theological rationale for torture is straightforward: if eternal [[hell]] awaits the unrepentant [[heresy|heretic]], then temporary earthly [[evil|suffering]] that brings about conversion is an act of [[charity]]. Holbach inverts this logic: the fact that [[belief|religious belief]] can motivate people to torture others is [[proof]] that religion is a force for [[cruelty]], not [[morality]].

## Historical Context

The use of torture against [[heresy|religious dissent]]ers reached its institutional peak in the medieval and early modern [[inquisition|Inquisition]]. The papal bull *Ad extirpanda* (1252) authorized the use of torture to extract confessions from [[heresy|heretic]]s. The [[inquisition|Spanish Inquisition]], established in 1478, institutionalized the practice for centuries. In [[france|France]], the torture and execution of [[heresy|heretic]]s continued into Holbach's own century — the last execution for heresy in [[france|France]] occurred in 1762 (the Calas affair, which [[voltaire|Voltaire]] famously contested).

Enlightenment thinkers made the abolition of judicial torture a central demand. Beccaria's *On [[crime|Crime]]s and [[punishment|Punishment]]s* (1764) argued that torture was both cruel and ineffective, producing false confessions rather than [[truth]]. Holbach connects the critique of torture specifically to religion: it is not an aberration but a predictable consequence of a system that teaches that dissent from orthodoxy is a crime against [[god]].

## Core Arguments

**1. Torture for [[god|God]] contradicts [[god|God]]'s supposed goodness.** "Do you not claim that your [[god|God]] is full of kindness? How can you hope to please Him by such barbarous actions which He can not help disapproving of?" (ch. CLVII). The persecutor claims to serve a benevolent [[god|deity]] by committing acts that any benevolent person would condemn.

**2. The authority for torture rests entirely on priestly testimony.** "Who told you that their opinions displease your [[god|God]]? Your [[clergy|priests]] told you! But who guarantees that your [[clergy|priests]] are not deceived themselves or that they do not wish to deceive you? It is these same [[clergy|priests]]!" (ch. CLVII). The entire edifice of religious violence rests on the unverified word of [[priest]]s who have [[eve]]ry [[interest]] in maintaining their monopoly on [[truth]].

**3. The doctrine of [[punishment|hell]] makes earthly torture seem merciful.** If [[god]] will torment the unrepentant for [[eternity]], then burning them at the stake to prompt a last-minute conversion is an act of [[mercy]] — a logic that Holbach regards as monstrous. The problem is not merely that torture is cruel but that [[religion|theology]] provides a framework in which cruelty appears virtuous.

**4. All religions generate [[cruelty|persecution]].** "Every [[nations|nation]]al religion has a tendency to make man vain, unsocial, and wicked" (ch. CLVII). The universal claim of [[revelation|revealed religion]] — that this people, this book, this [[prophecy|prophet]] has the exclusive [[truth]] — makes coexistence with dissent structurally impossible.

## Relation to Other Concepts

Torture is the physical expression of [[intolerance]], which is itself the social expression of theological [[dogma]]. It is enabled by the doctrine of [[hell]] — eternal torture in the next life makes temporal torture in this life seem proportionate, [[eve]]n merciful. The [[priest]] who authorizes torture is the same figure who preaches [[charity]] and [[forgiveness]] from the pulpit. [[Persecution]] is torture systematized and directed against a class of people defined by their beliefs. [[Fanaticism]] is the psychological state that makes the torturer feel [[virtue]] rather than [[remorse]].

## In the Text

On [[monarch|prince]]s who torture at the [[clergy|priests]]' command:

> "Blind and bigoted princes, you hate, you persecute, you devote heretics to torture, because you are persuaded that these unfortunate ones displease God. But do you not claim that your God is full of kindness? How can you hope to please Him by such barbarous actions which He can not help disapproving of?" (ch. CLVII)

On the circular authority of priestly testimony:

> "Who told you that their opinions displease your God? Your priests told you! But who guarantees that your priests are not deceived themselves or that they do not wish to deceive you? It is these same priests!" (ch. CLVII)

On the structural tendency of religion toward cruelty:

> "Every national religion has a tendency to make man vain, unsocial, and wicked; the first step toward humanity is to permit each one to follow peacefully the worship and the opinions which suit him." (ch. CLVII)

## Reception and Critique

Holbach's connection of torture to [[belief|religious belief]] was a powerful polemical move, but it has been criticized for its historical selectivity. Torture existed in non-religious societies and has been practiced extensively by avowedly secular regimes — the twentieth century, the most secular in history, was also the most torture-intensive. This suggests that religion is not a necessary condition for institutionalized cruelty. A more defensible version of Holbach's argument is that religion provides a unique form of moral legitimization for torture: the torturer who acts in [[god|God]]'s name can feel righteous rather than conflicted. This psychological mechanism — what contemporary social psychology calls "moral disengagement" — is the enduring core of Holbach's analysis, [[eve]]n if his claim that religion is the sole or primary cause of torture cannot be sustained.
