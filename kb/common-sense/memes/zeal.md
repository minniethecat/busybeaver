# Zeal

## Definition

Zeal, in Holbach's analysis, is religious fervor stripped of its positive connotations and exposed as a dangerous psychological and social phenomenon. The common view treats zeal as a [[morality|virtue]] — [[proof|evidence]] of [[vice|sin]]cere [[religion|faith]] and commitment to the good. Holbach inverts this: zeal is the passion of the uncritical mind, "the less [[education|enlightenment]] and reason men possess, the more zeal they exhibit for their religion" (ch. CXXXVIII). Zeal is not a sign of [[soul|spirit]]ual depth but of intellectual shallowness.

The [[missionaries|missionary]]'s zeal is particularly suspect. "A [[missionaries|missionary]] seeks [[chance|fortune]] by the aid of his doctrine; he knows that if he has the good [[chance|fortune]] to retail his commodity, he will become the absolute master of those who accept him as their guide" (ch. CXXXIII). What appears as selfless devotion is in fact [[interest|self-interest]]ed ambition — the [[missionaries|missionary]]'s zeal is the zeal of the salesman, not the saint. "These are the true motives which kindle the zeal and the charity of so many [[missionaries|preachers]] and missionaries who travel all over [[earth|the world]]."

Zeal becomes [[intolerance|fanaticism]] when it turns violent. "The [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ians pretend that, with the exception of the [[israel|Jewish people]], the whole human race lived in utter ignorance of its duties toward [[god|God]].... [[christianity|Christianity]], offshoot of Judaism, which was very humble in its obscure origin, became powerful and cruel under the [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian emperors, who, [[desire|drive]]n by a holy zeal, spread it marvelously in their empire by sword and fire" (ch. CXXXIX). The phrase "holy zeal" is bitterly ironic — the zeal is real, but there is nothing [[sacred|holy]] about the slaughter it produces.

## Historical Context

Holbach wrote in the shadow of the French Wars of [[religion|Religion]] (1562–1598), which had [[demon|demons]]trated the destructive power of religious zeal on French soil. The St. Bartholomew's Day massacre (1572), in which thousands of Huguenots were murdered by Catholic mobs, was still within living memory of historical consciousness. The [[edict-of-nantes|Revocation of the Edict of Nantes]] (1685) had renewed the [[cruelty|persecution]] of Protestants, driving hundreds of thousands into exile.

More broadly, the critique of religious zeal was a central theme of Enlightenment thought. [[voltaire|Voltaire]]'s famous motto *Écrasez l'infâme* ("crush the infamous thing") was directed at the [[intolerance|fanaticism]] that religion produced. [[david-hume|Hume]] had analyzed the psychology of zeal in his *Natural History of [[religion|Religion]]*. Holbach's contribution was to connect zeal to the broader structure of [[authority|religious authority]]: zeal is not an aberration but a predictable product of a system that teaches people to value [[religion|faith]] over reason.

## Core Arguments

**1. Zeal is inversely proportional to knowledge.** "The less [[education|enlightenment]] and reason men possess, the more zeal they exhibit for their religion" (ch. CXXXVIII). Zeal fills the space that knowledge would occupy.

**2. Missionary zeal is disguised [[interest|self-interest]].** "[[nothingness|Nothing]] ventured, nothing gained! A [[missionaries|missionary]] seeks [[chance|fortune]] by the aid of his doctrine" (ch. CXXXIII). The appearance of disinterested devotion conceals a calculation of personal advantage.

**3. Zeal is manufactured by [[clergy|priests]].** "Women, aroused by their directors, exhibit very great zeal in opinions of which it is evident they have not the least idea" (ch. CXXXVIII). Zeal is not spontaneous religious feeling but a response to [[priestcraft|clerical manipulation]].

**4. The [[martyr|martyrs]]' zeal proves nothing.** "To die for a religion does not prove it true or [[theology-index|Divine]]; this proves at most that we suppose it to be so" (ch. CXXXIII). The courage of [[martyr|martyrs]] is [[proof|evidence]] of the strength of conviction, not of the truth of what is believed.

## Relation to Other Concepts

Zeal is the active, outward expression of [[Faith]] — the willingness to act on beliefs that have not been examined. It is fueled by [[Ignorance]] and channeled by [[Priest]]s who direct the zeal of their followers toward their own ends. When zeal turns violent, it becomes [[Fanaticism]]. Zeal is the opposite of [[Reason]] — the calm, critical exami[[nations|nation]] that zeal short-circuits. [[Credulity]] is the soil in which zeal grows: those who beli[[eve]] without [[proof|evidence]] act without restraint.

## In the Text

On zeal as the passion of the ignorant:

> "The less enlightenment and reason men possess, the more zeal they exhibit for their religion. In all the religious factions, women, aroused by their directors, exhibit very great zeal in opinions of which it is evident they have not the least idea. In theological quarrels people rush like a ferocious beast upon all those against whom their priest wishes to excite them. Profound ignorance, unlimited credulity, a very weak head, an irritated imagination, these are the materials of which devotees, zealots, fanatics, and saints are made." (ch. CXXXVIII)

On missionary zeal as a profit-see[[monarch|king]] enterprise:

> "A missionary seeks fortune by the aid of his doctrine; he knows that if he has the good fortune to retail his commodity, he will become the absolute master of those who accept him as their guide; he is sure to become the object of their care, of their respect, of their veneration; he has every reason to believe that he will be abundantly provided for. These are the true motives which kindle the zeal and the charity of so many preachers and missionaries who travel all over the world." (ch. CXXXIII)

On the bloody history of [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian zeal:

> "Christianity, offshoot of Judaism, which was very humble in its obscure origin, became powerful and cruel under the Christian emperors, who, driven by a holy zeal, spread it marvelously in their empire by sword and fire, and founded it upon the ruins of overthrown Paganism." (ch. CXXXIX)
