# Skepticism

## Definition

In Holbach's "Common Sense" (1772), [[doubt|skepticism]] is defined as "but a state of indecision which results from a superficial exami[[nations|nation]] of subjects" (ch. 123).

It refers specifically to religious [[doubt|skepticism]] — the suspension of judgment regarding the existence of [[god|God]] and the truth of [[doctrine|theological doctrine]]s. Holbach distinguishes his target sharply from philosophical Pyrrhonism (which doubts [[eve]]n sensory [[proof|evidence]]) and from genuine indifference (which rests on a completed exami[[nations|nation]]).

Religious [[doubt|skepticism]], as Holbach anatomizes it, is not a rigorous philosophical method but a failure of intellectual nerve: "To be a skeptic, is to lack the motives necessary to establish a judgment" (ch. 123). The skeptic sees arguments both for and against [[god|God]]'s existence and, rather than pursuing the question to its logical resolution, retreats into indecision.

Holbach situates [[doubt|skepticism]] between two stable positions — genuine conviction (however misguided) and a[[divinity|theism]] (the conclusion reached by thorough reason). His provocative opening claim sets the stakes: "Perhaps it would be more truthful to say, that all men are either skeptics or [[atheism|atheist]]s, than to pretend that they are firmly convinced of the existence of a [[god|God]]" (ch. 123). For Holbach, few people actually *beli[[eve]]* in [[god|God]] in any intellectually coherent sense; most occupy an uneasy middle ground of unexamined doubt.

## Historical Context

Skepticism as a philosophical tradition had undergone significant revival by Holbach's era.

The rediscovery of Sextus Empiricus in the sixteenth century had furnished early modern philosophy with the arguments of ancient Pyrrhonism, which [[rene-descartes|Descartes]] attempted to overcome through the *cogito* and his [[evidence|proof]]s of [[god|God]]'s existence. By the eighteenth century, [[pierre-bayle|Pierre Bayle]] had wielded skeptical arguments with devastating effect against [[dogma|theological dogma]]tism in his *Historical and Critical Dictionary* (1697), while [[david-hume|David Hume]]'s mitigated [[doubt|skepticism]] (1748) distinguished between unlivable Pyrrhonian doubt and the reasonable suspension of judgment grounded in probabilistic reasoning.

Holbach's treatment of [[doubt|skepticism]], however, belongs to a more radical current. He does not treat [[doubt|skepticism]] as an epistemological problem to be solved (as [[rene-descartes|Descartes]] did) nor as a permanent condition that reason must accommodate (as [[david-hume|Hume]] did).

Instead, he treats religious [[doubt|skepticism]] as a moral and intellectual failing — the product of laziness, weakness, or fear — that can and should be overcome by thorough exami[[nations|nation]]. In this, Holbach aligns with the militant wing of the French Enlightenment, which saw the persistence of [[belief|religious belief]] not as a testament to human [[reason|rationality]] but as [[proof|evidence]] of its suppression. His target is not the skeptic who has examined and concluded nothing can be known, but the skeptic who has *not* examined and therefore concludes nothing at all.

## Core Arguments

**1. Skepticism is the product of insufficient exami[[nations|nation]].** Holbach's central thesis, announced in the chapter title itself, is that "religious [[doubt|skepticism]] can be but the effect of a superficial exami[[nations|nation]] of [[principle|theological principles]], which are in a perpetual contradiction of the clearest and best [[demon|demons]]trated principles" (ch. 123). The skeptic has looked at [[religion|theology]] just enough to see contradictions but not deeply enough to recognize that those contradictions are fatal. Thorough exami[[nations|nation]] would reveal that the concept of [[god|God]] — a [[existence|being]] whose [[attribute|attributes]] mutually destroy each other — is not merely unproven but impossible.

**2. Skepticism is morally suspect.** Holbach does not grant the religious skeptic the dignity of intellectual rigor. "[[doubt|Doubt]] arises ordinarily from laziness, weakness, indifference, or incapacity. To doubt, for many people, is to [[fear|dread]] the trouble of examining things to which one attaches but little interest" (ch. 123). The skeptic avoids the labor of thin[[monarch|king]] through the implications of theological [[contradiction|incoherence]]; [[doubt|skepticism]] becomes "a comfortable cushion" rather than a principled intellectual stance. A person who genuinely cared about the most important question — the existence of [[god|God]] — would not rest in indecision.

**3. Absolute [[doubt|skepticism]] is incoherent and unlivable.** Holbach draws a firm line between his critique of religious [[doubt|skepticism]] and the Pyrrhonian tradition: "Sensible people deride, and with reason, an absolute pyrrhonism, and [[eve]]n consider it impossible. A man who could doubt his own existence, or that of the sun, would appear very ridiculous, or would be suspected of reasoning in bad [[religion|faith]]" (ch. 123).

Some truths — one's own existence, the existence of physical objects, basic logical principles — are beyond reasonable doubt. The non-existence of an impossible [[existence|being]] belongs in this category. To doubt that a stick without two ends cannot exist is no more rational than to doubt one's own existence.

**4. Skepticism is distinct from indifference.** Holbach carefully distinguishes the skeptic from the genuinely indifferent person: "Indifference upon religion can not be confounded with [[doubt|skepticism]]; this indifference itself is founded upon the assurance or upon the probability which we find in believing that religion is not made to interest us" (ch. 123).

The indifferent person has examined religion, concluded it is not worthy of concern, and moved on. The skeptic has examined insufficiently and remains stuck. The indifferent person has reached a conclusion; the skeptic has avoided one. "Those who call themselves skeptics in regard to the fundamental points of religion, are generally but idle and lazy men, who are incapable of examining them" (ch. 123).

**5. Skepticism conceals an impossible epistemology.** Holbach's deeper argument is that the skeptic's posture is epistemologically incoherent when applied to [[religion|theology]]. "Is it less extravagant to have uncertainties about the non-existence of an evidently impossible [[existence|being]]? Is it more [[absurdity|absurd]] to doubt of one's own existence, than to hesitate upon the impossibility of a [[existence|being]] whose qualities destroy each other?" (ch. 123).

The [[attribute|attributes]] theologians assign to [[god|God]] — infinite goodness and infinite power coexisting with infinite [[injustice|evil]], pure [[soul|spirit]] acting on matter, a cause without a cause — are not mysterious but contradictory. Skepticism about the non-existence of such a [[existence|being]] is not caution; it is the failure to apply the same [[model|standard]]s of [[proof|evidence]] and logic that govern all other domains of inquiry.

## In the Text

Holbach devotes the entirety of Chapter 123 to the subject, one of the few chapters in "Common Sense" to treat a [[vice|sin]]gle epistemological posture rather than a [[doctrine|theological doctrine]].

The chapter opens with a provocative challenge to the universality of belief:

> "Perhaps it would be more truthful to say, that all men are either skeptics or atheists, than to pretend that they are firmly convinced of the existence of a God. How can we be assured of the existence of a being whom we never have been able to examine, of whom it is impossible to form any permanent idea, whose different effects upon ourselves prevent us from forming an invariable judgment, of whom no idea can be uniform in two different brains?"
>
> (123-002)

The chapter's rhetorical climax comes in its series of analogies equating [[doubt|skepticism]] about [[god|God]]'s non-existence with [[absurdity|absurd]] forms of doubt:

> "Do we find more probabilities for believing in a spiritual being than for believing in the existence of a stick without two ends? Is the notion of an infinitely good and powerful being who permits an infinity of evils, less absurd or less impossible than that of a square triangle?"
>
> (123-002)

These analogies are not casual rhetorical flourishes. They express Holbach's core epistemological conviction: that the concept of [[god|God]] violates the principle of non-contradiction just as surely as a square triangle does. To remain skeptical about the impossibility of either is to abandon reason itself.

Immediately following Chapter 123, Holbach transitions to "[[revelation|Revelation]] Refuted" (ch. 124), suggesting that the critique of [[doubt|skepticism]] is a clearing operation — once the skeptic's halfway posture is exposed as intellectually unserious, the ground is prepared for a direct assault on [[revelation|revealed religion]] itself.
