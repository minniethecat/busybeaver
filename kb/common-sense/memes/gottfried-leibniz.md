# Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz

## Biography

Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz (1646–1716) was a German philosopher, mathematician, jurist, and diplomat — one of the most comprehensive intellects of the seventeenth century. Independently of [[isaac-newton|Newton]], he developed [[infinite|the infinite]]simal calculus; he designed calculating machines; he served as counselor to the House of Hanover; and he produced a vast philosophical correspondence that engaged the leading minds of [[europe|Europe]]. His philosophical system, expressed in works such as the *Monadology* (1714) and the *Theodicy* (1710), proposed that reality is composed of indivisible, [[spirit|immaterial substance]]s called monads, pre-programmed by [[god|God]] to unfold in a "pre-established harmony" — a universe that is, as he famously argued, "the best of all possible [[universe|world]]s."

## Role in Religious History

Leibniz was a committed [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian who sought throughout his career to reconcile philosophy with [[religion|theology]] and to reunite the Protestant and Catholic churches. His *Theodicy* coined the term for the philosophical defense of [[god|God]]'s goodness in the face of [[injustice|evil]]: Leibniz argued that [[god|God]], [[existence|being]] perfectly good and all-powerful, necessarily chose to create the best possible [[universe|world]] from among all logically possible [[universe|world]]s — and that the [[injustice|evil]] and [[evil|suffering]] we observe are necessary components of that optimal whole. This argument, already controversial in Leibniz's own time, was devastatingly satirized by [[voltaire|Voltaire]] in *Candide* (1759) through the character of Dr. Pangloss, and it became for the radical Enlightenment the definitive example of philosophical ingenuity wasted in the service of [[dogma|theological dogma]].

## Connection to "Common Sense"

Holbach names Leibniz in the concluding appendix to *Common Sense*, grouping him with [[rene-descartes|Descartes]] and [[malebranche|Malebranche]] as philosophers who were "compelled to invent hypotheses and evasions in order to reconcile their discoveries with the reveries and the blunders which religion had rendered sacred" (APP169-092). In Holbach's judgment, Leibniz — for all his intellectual brilliance — exemplifies the tragedy of a great mind deformed by the demand to conform to priestly authority. His "best of all possible [[universe|world]]s" is, in Holbach's materialist view, exactly the kind of "subterfuge" and "mental gymnastics" that [[religion|theology]] forces upon philosophy: a baroque metaphysical construction designed not to explain reality but to protect religious [[opinion|prejudice]] from rational scrutiny.
