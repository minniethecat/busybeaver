# Gods

## Definition

In Holbach's analysis, the plurality of gods across human [[worship|cult]]ures is not a deviation from an original pure mono[[divinity|theism]] but the natural state of [[belief|religious belief]]. Gods are projections of human characteristics onto imaginary [[existence|being]]s: they are "men amplified" — [[existence|being]]s with human passions, [[reason|human reason]]ing, and human limitations, magnified to cosmic scale. The diversity of the gods — their incompatible [[attribute|attributes]], their contradictory commands, their local jurisdictions — is [[proof|evidence]] that they are products of the human imagi[[nations|nation]] rather than [[dreams|revelations]] of a [[guilt|sin]]gle [[divinity|divine]] reality.

Holbach does not distinguish sharply between the gods of [[paganism|polytheism]] and the [[god|God]] of mono[[divinity|theism]]. In his view, the [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian [[god|God]] is simply one more member of the genus "gods" — distinguished by [[existence|being]] stripped of most [[attribute|attributes]] (invisibility, immateriality) but otherwise continuous with the deities of pagan antiquity. The progression from many gods to one [[god|God]] is not, for Holbach, a progression from error to truth but from one form of error to another.

## Historical Context

The comparative study of gods was a central preoccupation of Enlightenment thought. [[david-hume|Hume]]'s *Natural History of [[religion|Religion]]* (1757) had traced the psychological origins of [[paganism|polytheism]] and its gradual evolution toward mono[[divinity|theism]]. [[voltaire|Voltaire]] had catalogued the [[absurdity|absurd]]ities of [[earth|the world]]'s deities with characteristic wit. Holbach's contribution was to radicalize this comparative project: not merely to show that other people's gods were [[absurdity|absurd]], but to show that all gods — including the [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian [[god|God]] — were products of the same psychological and social mechanisms.

Holbach was particularly interested in the structural similarities between [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian [[religion|theology]] and pagan mythology. The [[trinity|Trinity]], the [[incarnation|Incarnation]], [[mary|the Virgin]] Birth, the resurrection of the body — all had pagan precedents that [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian apologists struggled to explain away. For Holbach, the simplest expla[[nations|nation]] was that [[christianity|Christianity]] had borrowed its [[religion|theology]] from the pagan environment in which it developed.

## Core Arguments

Holbach's critique of the gods proceeds through four claims.

First, gods are created in the image of their creators. "Man made [[god|God]] in his own image," Holbach asserts, inverting the biblical formula. Gods reflect the values, fears, and social structures of the societies that worship them (002-284). A warlike [[nations|nation]] has a warlike god; a commercial [[nations|nation]] has a god who blesses prosperity; an authoritarian [[nations|nation]] has a god who demands obedience.

Second, the [[attribute|attributes]] of gods are mutually contradictory. Theologians attribute infinite goodness, infinite power, and infinite justice to their [[god|deity]] — [[attribute|attributes]] that are logically incompatible with the observable state of [[earth|the world]]. A god who is all-good and all-powerful would not permit the amount and distribution of [[evil|suffering]] that exists. The persistent attempt to reconcile these contradictions through [[injustice|theodicy]] is, for Holbach, intellectual evasion.

Third, gods serve identifiable social functions. They legitimate [[government|political authority]] (the [[divine-right|divine right of kings]]) (002-288), enforce moral conformity ([[punishment|divine punishment]] for [[guilt|sin]]ners), and sustain a priestly class that claims exclusive access to [[divinity|divine]] communication (002-316). The persistence of belief in gods is explained by their [[utility|social utility]], not their truth.

Fourth, the fact that gods are believed [[guilt|sin]]cerely does not make them real. Holbach acknowledges the psychological power of [[belief|religious belief]] — the comfort it provides, the fear it instills — but insists that subjective conviction has no bearing on objective truth. Millions of people have [[guilt|sin]]cerely believed in gods that Holbach regards as [[demon|demons]]trably fictional.

## In the Text

Holbach's discussion of the gods pervades *Common Sense*. He catalogs the deities of antiquity alongside the [[god|God]] of [[christianity|Christianity]], treating both as specimens of the same genus:

> "Every nation made for itself gods, or rather every nation had gods made for it by its legislators and its priests." (Chapter II, paraphrase)

> "If we examine the gods of the nations without prejudice, we shall find that they are all equally absurd." (Chapter II)
