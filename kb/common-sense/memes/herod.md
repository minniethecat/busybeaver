# Herod the Great

## Biography

Herod the Great (c. 72–4 BCE) was a Roman client [[monarch|king]] of [[judea|Judea]] who ruled from 37 BCE until his death. Appointed by the Roman Senate and backed by [[mark|Mark]] Antony and later Augustus, Herod governed a territory that included [[judea|Judea]], Samaria, [[galilee|Galilee]], and parts of surrounding regions. He was a prolific builder, responsible for the expansion of the Second [[temple|Temple]] in [[jerusalem|Jerusalem]], the fortress of Masada, and the port city of Caesarea Maritima. Despite his architectural achievements, Herod's reign was marked by paranoia and brutality — he executed several members of his own family, including his wife Mariamne I and three of his sons, fearing conspiracy. The first-century Jewish historian [[josephus|Josephus]], who wrote the most detailed surviving account of Herod's life, documented both his political skill and his murderous domestic cruelty.

## Role in Religious History

In [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian tradition, Herod occupies a uniquely negative place as the [[monarch|ruler]] who, according to [[gospel|the Gospel]] of [[matthew|Matthew]], ordered the [[mass|Mass]]acre of the Innocents — the killing of all male children under two years old in and around [[bethlehem|Bethlehem]] — in an attempt to eliminate the infant [[jesus-christ|Jesus]], whom the Magi had identified as the newborn "[[monarch|king]] of the Jews." The historicity of this [[eve]]nt is widely questioned by scholars: it is recorded only in [[matthew|Matthew]], is absent from the other [[gospel|Gospel]]s, and is not mentioned by [[josephus|Josephus]], who otherwise catalogued Herod's crimes in detail. The episode established Herod as the archetypal tyrant who attempts to thwart [[divinity|divine]] purposes through violence, a motif that has persisted in [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian art, literature, and liturgy.

## Connection to "Common Sense"

Holbach's "Common Sense" references Herod in two distinct contexts, both serving his argument against [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian [[supernatural|supernaturalism]].

The first reference appears in Holbach's discussion of human [[evil|suffering]] and [[providence|divine providence]]. He cites the remark of the Roman emperor Augustus — who, "having heard that Herod, [[monarch|king]] of [[judea|Judea]], had murdered his sons, cried out: 'It would be better to be Herod's pig than his son!'" (ch. IX, §Animals). Holbach uses this aphorism to advance a darkly ironic argument: humans, supposedly the beloved children of [[providence|Providence]], suffer more than [[soul|anima]]ls precisely because of their capacity for [[fear|religious fear]] and cruelty. A pig in Herod's [[judea|Judea]] was safer than Herod's own children — and by extension, a human [[existence|being]] unafflicted by religion would be safer than one [[torture|torment]]ed by eternal [[hell|damnation]] anxiety.

The second and more extensive reference appears in Holbach's critique of [[gospel|Gospel]] reliability. He summarizes [[matthew|Matthew]]'s account: "the [[monarch|king]] Herod, fearing that this pretended new [[monarch|king]] would rob him of his crown some day, caused the murder of all the new-born children under two years, in all the neighborhood of [[bethlehem|Bethlehem]]" (ch. XI, §Evangelists). Holbach then deploys a historical-critical argument: "as neither the historians of that time speak of it, nor [[josephus|Josephus]], the historian who wrote the life of this Herod, and as the other Evangelists do not mention it, it is evident that the journey of those wise men, guided by a star, this massacre of little children, and this flight to [[egypt|Egypt]], were but [[absurdity|absurd]] falsehoods" (ch. XI).

For Holbach, [[josephus|Josephus]]'s silence is decisive. [[josephus|Josephus]] "blamed the vices of this [[monarch|king]]" extensively — his paranoia, his executions of family members, his political ruthlessness. That [[josephus|Josephus]] would record all of Herod's real crimes but omit the most atrocious of all (the systematic slaughter of infants) is, Holbach argues, historically incredible. The massacre, like the guiding star and the angelic dream-warning, is a literary invention designed to heighten the drama of Jesus's nativity.

## Key Ideas Attributed / Critiqued

Holbach [[attribute|attributes]] no specific ideas to Herod — Herod is not a thinker but a figure whose historical role exposes the weakness of [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian claims. Three lines of Holbach's critique emerge from the Herod references:

1. **The moral scandal of [[providence|divine providence]]**. If the [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian [[god|God]] existed, he could have protected the [[bethlehem|Bethlehem]] children from Herod's sword. That he did not — while supposedly warning [[joseph|Joseph]] through an angel — exposes [[providence|divine providence]] as an incoherent concept: [[god|God]] is either unable to save innocents (impotent) or unwilling (malevolent). Neither option is compatible with [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian [[religion|theology]].

2. **The historical unreliability of [[gospel|the Gospel]]s**. [[josephus|Josephus]]'s silence on the massacre, combined with the other [[missionaries|evangelists]]' omission of it, [[demon|demons]]trates that [[matthew|Matthew]]'s nativity account is fiction. Holbach treats this as a representative case — if [[gospel|the Gospel]]s invent the most horrific of Herod's actions, what else did they invent?

3. **The political function of religion**. The Augustus anecdote connects Herod's [[judea|Judea]] to the broader Roman political context. Herod ruled as [[rome|Rome]]'s client, and his cruelty was a political fact, not a religious allegory. Holbach implies that [[politics|political power]] and religious narrative are interwoven from the start of [[christianity|Christianity]]'s story.

Holbach cites the notorious quip of the Emperor Augustus regarding Herod:

> Augustus, having heard that Herod, king of Judea, had murdered his sons, cried out: "It would be better to be Herod's pig than his son!" We can say as much of men; this beloved child of Providence runs much greater risks than all other animals.
