# Indulgence

## In Holbach's Usage

Holbach invokes **indulgence** in two distinct registers in *Common Sense*. First, he appeals to a humane principle of intellectual forbearance: we should "have some indulgence for those who ingenuously confess their ignorance" (002-125). This is the indulgence of the reasonable person who recognizes the limits of human knowledge and does not punish honest doubt. Second, he critiques the theological notion of divine indulgence — the idea that "the Lord can show indulgence to those who are in error" (002-356) — as a mechanism that perpetuates error by offering forgiveness without requiring intellectual reform. Holbach notes sardonically that many people "have more faith in the indulgence of Heaven than in that of earthly powers" (002-292), suggesting that the concept of divine indulgence serves primarily as a psychological comfort that absolves the believer of the hard work of thinking.

Holbach demands that priests extend the same indulgence to skeptics that they demand for their own incomprehensible doctrines:

> If you are as ignorant as we, have some indulgence for those who ingenuously confess that they comprehend nothing of it, or that they see nothing in it Divine. Cease to persecute for opinions which you do not understand yourselves; cease to slander each other for dreams and conjectures which are altogether contradictory.
>
> (002-125)
