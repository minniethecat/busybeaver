# Consent

## Definition

In Holbach's "Common Sense," the term "consent" does not function as a self-standing philosophical concept — it receives no dedicated chapter and no systematic exposition. Rather, it operates as a rhetorical hinge-word deployed at three strategic junctures to expose contradictions in theological reasoning. Each appearance of "consent" marks a moment where Holbach demands that his reader, or his theological opponent, confront the logical entailments of a position they wish to evade. To consent, in the Holbachian idiom, is not a neutral act of permission but an act of [[truth|intellectual honesty]]: it is the demand that one accept — without evasion, without mystification — the consequences of what one claims to beli[[eve]].

The three senses in which "consent" appears in the text are: (1) as **resig[[nations|nation]] to [[death|mortality]]**, the demand that humans consent to their finite [[universe|nature]] rather than cling to illusions of im[[death|mortality]] (ch. 108); (2) as a **test of [[divinity|divine]] character**, an argument that a benevolent [[god|God]] cannot logically consent to [[hell|eternal punishment]] for finite [[crime|offense]]s (ch. 66); and (3) as a **deterministic thought-experiment**, in which [[eve]]n the voluntary-seeming act of consenting to lose a wager is shown to be mechanically determined by the stronger motive (ch. 80). Across all three, consent is the pivot on which Holbach turns the reader from comfortable theological evasion toward uncomfortable rational clarity.

## Historical Context

Holbach's rhetorical use of "consent" must be understood within two broader Enlightenment currents. The first is the tradition of moral and political consent theory, which had been central to early modern philosophy from [[hobbes|Hobbes]] through [[john-locke|Locke]] to Rousseau. In the [[john-locke|Locke]]an tradition, legitimate government depends on the consent of the governed; in the Rousseauian, the general will is the expression of collective consent to the social contract. Holbach was certainly aware of these traditions, and his own political philosophy (expressed more fully in *Système social*, 1773) endorses a version of government by rational consent. In "Common Sense," however, consent is deployed in a darker register: when he demands that the reader "consent" to [[death|mortality]], he is inverting the consensualist tradition to expose the coercive structure of theological belief, which obtains "consent" not through reason but through fear.

The second current is necessitarianism — the doctrine, associated above all with [[baruch-spinoza|Spinoza]], that [[eve]]rything that happens does so necessarily, and that [[liberty|free will]] is an illusion born of ignorance of causes. Holbach was the most prominent French exponent of this view. His argument in chapter 80 — that [[eve]]n the decision to "consent to lose a wager" is itself determined by a stronger motive — is a direct application of Spinozistic necessitarianism in polemical form. Where [[baruch-spinoza|Spinoza]] wrote in axiomatic detachment, Holbach dramatizes the logic as an interpersonal challenge ("But if I consent to lose the wager?"), forcing the reader to experience their own supposed [[liberty|freedom]] as a psychological illusion.

## Core Arguments

**1. Consent to [[death|mortality]] is the rational alternative to theological [[hope|consolation]].** Holbach confronts the claim that the [[death|afterlife]] [[doctrine|dogma]] is "sweet and agreeable" — a comforting illusion that helps the unfortunate endure [[evil|suffering]]. His reply is that the [[passion|desire]] for happiness does not justify belief in falsehoods. The rational response to [[evil|suffering]] is not to invent a compensatory [[death|afterlife]] but to "consent" to the [[nature|natural order]] that decrees [[death|mortality]] for all [[existence|being]]s. This consent is not resig[[nations|nation]]-in-despair but a clear-eyed acceptance that permits one to "return peaceably to the universal home" from which one came (ch. 108). The theological alternative — clinging to im[[death|mortality]] — is not [[hope|consolation]] but the refusal to face reality.

**2. A just [[god|God]] cannot consent to [[hell|eternal punishment]].** Holbach's argument in chapter 66 turns on the internal logic of [[divinity|theism]]: if [[god|God]] is infinitely good and infinitely happy, He cannot be offended by finite creatures. And if He cannot be offended, He cannot "consent" to make those creatures' [[crime|offense]]s last for [[eternal|eternity]]. To assert that [[god|God]] does so consent is to make [[god|God]] "infinitely more wicked than the most wicked of men" (ch. 66). The term "consent" here is precisely chosen: it is not merely that [[god|God]] permits or allows eternal [[torture|torment]], but that the theological system requires [[god|God]] to *actively will* it — to consent to a punishment that serves no purpose except the amusement of the punisher. This is, for Holbach, the mark of cruelty, not justice.

**3. Even the act of consent is determined.** In the wager thought-experiment (ch. 80), Holbach anticipates and dismantles the most intuitive objection to [[effect|determinism]]: the experience of free choice. If I wager that I can freely choose to do or not to do something, and then I "consent to lose the wager" to prove my [[liberty|freedom]], I have only [[demon|demons]]trated that the [[passion|desire]] to prove my [[liberty|freedom]] has become a stronger motive than the [[passion|desire]] to win. Every consent, including the consent to refute [[effect|determinism]], is itself caused by the prevailing motive at that instant. The feeling of [[liberty|freedom]] "is an illusion which may be compared to that of the fly in the fable, which, lighting on the shaft of a heavy wagon, applauded itself as [[desire|drive]]r of the vehicle which carried it" (ch. 80).

## In the Text

On consent as the rational acceptance of [[death|mortality]]:

> "Consent, then, to leave without regret this world, which causes more trouble than pleasure to the majority of you. Resign yourselves to the order of destiny, which decrees that you, like all other beings, should not endure forever. But what will become of me? you ask! What you were several millions of years ago. You were then, I do not know what; resign yourselves, then, to become again in an instant, I do not know what; what you were then; return peaceably to the universal home from which you came without your knowledge into your material form, and pass by without murmuring, like all the beings which surround you!"
>
> (108-002)

On [[divinity|divine]] consent and eternal [[torture|torment]]:

> "I will tell you further, that offenses of finite beings can not be infinite; that a God who does not want to be offended, can not consent to make His creatures' offenses last for eternity; I will tell you that a God infinitely good, can not be infinitely cruel, nor grant His creatures infinite existence solely for the pleasure of tormenting them forever."
>
> (066-002)

On the [[effect|determinism]] behind voluntary consent:

> "If I make the wager to do or not to do a thing, am I not free? Does it not depend upon me to do or not to do it? No; I will answer you, the desire to win the wager will necessarily determine you to do or not to do the thing in question. 'But if I consent to lose the wager?' Then the desire to prove to me that you are free will have become to you a stronger motive than the desire to win the wager; and this motive will necessarily have determined you to do or not to do what was understood between us."
>
> (080-004)
