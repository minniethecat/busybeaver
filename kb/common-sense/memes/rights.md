# Rights

## Definition

Holbach distinguishes between two fundamentally opposed conceptions of rights. "[[theology-index|Divine]] rights" — the claim that [[monarch|king]]s and [[clergy|priests]] derive their authority directly from [[god|God]] — are, in his analysis, a [[fiction|theological fiction]] designed to place [[monarch|ruler]]s beyond accountability. "The fear of the [[god|God]]s, religion, the [[fear|terror]]s of another life — these are the metaphysical and supernatural barriers which are opposed to the furious passions of [[monarch|prince]]s! Are these barriers sufficient?" (ch. CXLV). The answer is clearly no: [[divinity|divine]] right is not a barrier to [[despotism|tyranny]] but its license.

Against this, Holbach sets natural rights — entitlements that arise from human [[universe|nature]] and the requirements of social life. These rights are not granted by [[god|God]] or by [[monarch|king]]s but discovered by reason reflecting on what human [[existence|being]]s need to flourish. "Let them learn how to govern, how to be just, how to respect the rights of the people, to recognize the benefactions of the [[nation|nations]] from whom they obtain their grandeur and power" (ch. CXLVII). Rights, properly understood, flow upward from the people to their [[monarch|ruler]]s — not downward from [[hope|heaven]].

The concept of "divine rights" is not merely false but pernicious: it makes peaceful reform impossible by declaring any challenge to authority a challenge to [[god|God]] Himself. "When men become exasperated, the divine rights (002-292) of tyrants are compelled to yield to the natural rights of their subjects" (ch. CXLVII) — but only after preventable [[evil|suffering]] has made revolution inevitable.

## Historical Context

The language of natural rights was central to Enlightenment political thought. [[john-locke|Locke]] had argued for natural rights to life, liberty, and property; Rousseau had declared that man is born free but [[eve]]rywhere in chains; the American and French [[revolution|Revolution]]s would soon translate these ideas into political declarations. Holbach wrote *Common Sense* in 1772, on the [[eve]] of these revolutions, and his critique of [[divinity|divine]] right participates in the broader assault on [[tyranny|absolutism]].

The specific target of Holbach's argument — the alliance of throne and altar — had been the dominant political arrangement in [[europe|Europe]] [[vice|sin]]ce Constantine. The doctrine of [[sovereign|divine right of kings]], articulated most fully by Bossuet in the seventeenth century, held that royal authority was sanctioned by [[god|God]] and that rebellion was not only treason but [[blasphemy|sacrilege]]. Holbach's counter-argument — that [[divinity|divine]] right is a fiction serving elite interests — was among the most radical political positions available in pre-revolutionary [[paris|France]].

## Core Arguments

**1. [[theology-index|Divine]] right is a fiction that serves power.** Kings and [[clergy|priests]] "pretended to have received their crowns (002-291) and their rights from [[god|God]] alone, and that they should account to Him only for their actions" (ch. CXLVII). This fiction places [[monarch|ruler]]s beyond the reach of their subjects' judgment.

**2. Natural rights are grounded in human needs, not [[divinity|divine]] grant.** "Let them learn to fear men, to submit to the laws of [[justice|equity]], that no one can violate without danger; let these laws restrain equally the powerful and the weak, the great and the small, the [[monarch|sovereign]] and the subjects" (ch. CXLVII). Rights derive from what human [[existence|being]]s require to live together peacefully and prosperously.

**3. [[theology|Theology]] justifies the violation of rights.** The doctrine that [[god|God]]'s justice is different from human justice amounts to "the violation of all rights" (ch. LXXXIX). When theologians say [[god|God]]'s ways are mysterious, they make it impossible to judge whether any act is just or unjust — including the acts of [[monarch|ruler]]s who claim [[divinity|divine]] sanction.

**4. [[religion|Religion]] does not restrain [[despotism|tyranny]].** "From lack of the knowledge of the true principles of administration, the aim and the rights (002-295) of social life, the real interests of men, and the duties which unite them, the [[monarch|prince]]s are become, in almost [[eve]]ry land, licentious, absolute, and perverse" (ch. CXLV). Only secular law and popular vigilance can protect rights.

## Relation to Other Concepts

Rights are closely linked to [[Justice]] — the principle that all persons, including [[monarch|ruler]]s, are subject to law. [[Liberty]] is the condition in which rights are respected. [[Society]] is the framework within which rights are defined and protected. [[Law]] is the instrument that codifies rights and makes them enforceable. [[Power]] without respect for rights degenerates into [[Tyranny]]. The [[fiction|theological fiction]] of divine rights depends on [[Theology]]'s claim to speak for [[god|God]].

## In the Text

On [[divinity|divine]] right as a fiction serving elite interests:

> "Several princes of Europe pretended to have received their crowns and their rights from God alone, and that they should account to Him only for their actions. Civil power in its battles against spiritual power, having at length gained the advantage, and the priests being compelled to yield, recognized the Divine right of kings and preached it to the people, reserving to themselves the right to change opinions and to preach revolution, every time that the divine rights of kings did not agree with the divine rights of the clergy." (ch. CXLVII)

On the violation of all rights by theological reasoning:

> "I hear a multitude of theologians tell me on all sides, that God is infinitely just, but that His justice is not that of men! Of what kind, or of what nature is this Divine justice then? What idea can I form of a justice which so often resembles human injustice? Is it not confounding all our ideas of justice and of injustice, to tell us that what is equitable in God is iniquitous in His creatures?" (ch. LXXXIX)

On the need for secular law to protect rights:

> "Let them learn how to govern, how to be just, how to respect the rights of the people, to recognize the benefactions of the nations from whom they obtain their grandeur and power; let them learn to fear men, to submit to the laws of equity, that no one can violate without danger; let these laws restrain equally the powerful and the weak, the great and the small, the sovereign and the subjects." (ch. CXLVII)
