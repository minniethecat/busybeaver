# Opinion

## Definition

Holbach treats opinion as the central mechanism of social control. Most people, he observes, do not arrive at their beliefs through reason but absorb them from authority — parents, [[clergy|priests]], and [[monarch|prince]]s — before they are capable of critical judgment. "[[prejudice|Prejudice]] tends to confirm in us the opinions of those who are charged with our [[education|instruction]]" (ch. XXXIV). Once installed, these opinions become self-reinforcing: the believer trusts his instructors more than his own reason, and the very prohibition on questioning is taken as [[proof|evidence]] of the doctrine's [[sacred|sanctity]].

The distinction between opinion and knowledge is central to Holbach's epistemology. An opinion is a belief held without adequate [[proof|evidence]] — usually because the believer has been taught from childhood not to demand [[proof|evidence]]. [[knowledge|Knowledge]], by contrast, is belief grounded in experience and verified by reason. [[religion|Religion]], he argues, is almost entirely opinion in this sense: "Whoever will consult common sense upon religious opinions, and will apply to them the ordinary rules of truth, will easily perceive that these opinions have no solid foundation" (ch. II).

The political dimension of opinion is equally important. "Theologians ... have prepared them for opinions which they represented as necessary to the welfare of the [[politics-index|State]]" (ch. II). Opinion is the invisible chain that binds subjects to [[monarch|ruler]]s: those who control the formation of opinion control the society, because people act on what they beli[[eve]] to be true, regardless of whether it is actually true.

## Historical Context

Holbach's analysis of opinion belongs to the Enlightenment's broader campaign against prejudice — literally, pre-judgment. Bacon's doctrine of the idols had identified the systematic sources of error in the human mind; [[john-locke|Locke]] had argued that the mind begins as a blank slate, filled by experience; [[rene-descartes|Descartes]] had made methodical doubt the foundation of philosophy. Holbach synthesized these insights into a political epistemology: the question is not only how individuals form true beliefs, but how societies organize the production of belief.

This concern was shared by contemporaries like Condorcet and [[voltaire|Voltaire]], who saw the struggle against superstition as inseparable from the struggle against [[tyranny|despotism]]. The metaphor of the "chains" of opinion — which must be broken by the "light" of reason — was a commonplace of Enlightenment rhetoric. Holbach's distinctive contribution was to analyze opinion as a system: a closed loop in which authority teaches error, error reinforces authority, and the victim is trained to love his chains.

## Core Arguments

**1. Opinion is formed by authority, not reason.** Most beliefs are acquired in childhood from "those who are charged with our [[education|instruction]]" (ch. XXXIV), before the critical faculties have developed. The authority of the instructor substitutes for the authority of [[proof|evidence]], and the habit of deference persists into adulthood.

**2. Religious opinions have no rational foundation.** When examined by the ordinary [[model|standard]]s of truth — consistency, [[proof|evidence]], coherence with experience — [[doctrine|theological doctrine]]s collapse. They survive only because "the [[existence|being]] forbidden to reason upon what they tell us, does not diminish our [[certainty|confidence]], but contributes often to increase our respect for their opinions" (ch. XXXIV).

**3. Opinion serves [[politics|political power]].** Theologians present their doctrines as "necessary to the welfare of the [[politics-index|State]]" (ch. II), forging an alliance between altar and throne. Opinion is cheaper than force: it is more efficient to make subjects beli[[eve]] they ought to obey than to compel obedience at [[eve]]ry moment.

**4. Free inquiry is the remedy.** The cure for the [[despotism|tyranny]] of opinion is not a different set of opinions but the habit of critical exami[[nations|nation]]. [[reason|Reason]], freely exercised, dissolves prejudice as light dissolves darkness. This is why [[clergy|the clergy]] "fettered him by the ties of opinion" (ch. II) — free minds are ungovernable by theological authority.

## Relation to Other Concepts

Opinion stands at the intersection of Holbach's epistemology and his politics. It is the opposite of [[Reason]], which is belief grounded in [[proof|evidence]] rather than authority. It is the product of [[Education]] — and the purpose of a reformed education is to replace opinion with knowledge. [[Prejudice]] is opinion in its most rigid form: a belief that resists correction by [[proof|evidence]]. [[Superstition]] is the system of religious opinions that most thoroughly enslaves the human mind. [[Liberty]] begins with the [[liberty|freedom]] to form one's own opinions through the exercise of reason.

## In the Text

On the transmission of opinion through education:

> "Prejudice tends to confirm in us the opinions of those who are charged with our instruction. We believe them more skillful than we are; we suppose them thoroughly convinced themselves of the things they teach us." (ch. XXXIV)

On the irrational foundation of religious opinions:

> "Whoever will consult common sense upon religious opinions, and will apply to them the ordinary rules of truth, will easily perceive that these opinions have no solid foundation." (ch. II)

On opinion as social control:

> "Theologians, with the view of making others subservient to their own passions ... fettered him by the ties of opinion, have remained his masters or have only broken his chains to obtain greater control over him." (ch. II)
