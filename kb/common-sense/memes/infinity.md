# Infinity

## Definition

Infinity, in the theological context that Holbach critiques, is the attribute ascribed to God denoting unlimited and boundless existence — infinite power, infinite knowledge, infinite goodness. Holbach argues that the concept of infinity is epistemologically empty: as finite beings, humans cannot form any genuine conception of the infinite. "The idea of infinity is for us an idea without model" (002-007).

For Holbach, attributing infinity to God does not explain anything — it merely replaces one mystery with another, larger mystery. To say "God is infinite" is to say nothing meaningful, since no finite mind can comprehend what infinity entails. The concept functions as a rhetorical device to shut down rational inquiry: when asked to explain divine attributes, theologians retreat to the claim that God's ways are infinite and therefore beyond human understanding.

## Historical Context

The concept of infinity was a major topic in 17th and 18th-century philosophy. Descartes had argued that the human mind's ability to conceive of infinity proved the existence of an infinite God who implanted the idea. Locke and other empiricists countered that the idea of infinity was merely the indefinite extension of finite experiences — we can always imagine adding one more unit, and from this iterative process we derive the notion of the infinite.

Holbach follows the empiricist line but sharpens it into a polemical weapon: if the concept of infinity is merely a mental extrapolation from finite experience, then theological claims about an infinite God have no cognitive content. They are words without corresponding ideas.

## Core Arguments

1. **Infinity as an idea without a model**: Since all human ideas derive from sensory experience of finite objects, the idea of infinity cannot correspond to anything real. It is "an idea without model" — a verbal construction referring to nothing (002-007).

2. **Divine infinity as unintelligible**: Claiming that God is infinite does not make God more comprehensible. "The infinity of God will always place such a being beyond the reach of our understanding" (002-008). theologians use "infinite" to end inquiry, not to illuminate.

3. **The problem of infinite goodness and finite evil**: If God is infinitely good and infinitely powerful, the existence of any evil is logically impossible. "An infinitely powerful being who permits an infinity of evils" is self-contradictory (002-237). The infinite attributes of God are incompatible with observed reality.

## In the Text

Holbach addresses infinity directly in the early sections of Chapter 2:

> "Man is a finite being. The idea of infinity is for us an idea without model" (002-007, paraphrased)

> "In no subject are men less enlightened than in this one, the infinity of God will always place such a being beyond the reach of human intelligence" (002-008, paraphrased)

He returns to the theme when discussing the problem of evil:

> "What could be more absurd than an infinitely powerful being who permits an infinity of evils?" (002-237, paraphrased)
