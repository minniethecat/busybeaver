# Jesus Christ

## Biography

Jesus of Nazareth (c. 4 BCE – c. 30 CE) was a Jewish preacher active in first-century [[judea|Judea]] and [[galilee|Galilee]]. The principal sources for his life are the four canonical [[gospel|Gospel]]s of the [[testament|New Testament]]. According to Christian tradition, he was born in [[bethlehem|Bethlehem]], raised in [[nazareth|Nazareth]], conducted a public ministry in [[galilee|Galilee]] and [[judea|Judea]], was crucified under Pontius Pilate in [[jerusalem|Jerusalem]], and rose from the dead. The [[christianity|Christian religion]] regards him as the Son of God and the Messiah (Christ) whose sacrificial death redeemed humanity from [[vice|sin]].

## Role in Religious History

Jesus Christ is the founder and focal point of [[christianity|Christianity]], the world's largest [[religion]]. His teachings — recorded in the four canonical [[gospel|Gospel]]s — form the doctrinal core of the Christian faith. The [[christianity|Christian]] claim that Jesus was both fully human and fully divine (the [[incarnation|Incarnation]]), that his death atoned for human [[vice|sin]] (the [[atonement|Atonement]]), and that he was raised bodily from the dead (the [[resurrection|Resurrection]]) constitute the central [[mystery|mysteries]] that Baron d'Holbach subjects to sustained rational critique in *Common Sense*.

## Holbach's Critique

Holbach approaches Jesus through the lens of historical-critical skepticism. He does not deny that a first-century Jewish preacher named Jesus existed; what he denies is the [[divinity|divine]] character attributed to him. For Holbach, the Gospel accounts are not reliable historical documents but the products of a community that, decades after the events they narrate, had theological interests in magnifying Jesus's deeds.

Holbach's specific lines of critique include:

**1. The Gospels contradict one another on factual details.** Holbach notes, following earlier critics, that [[matthew|Matthew]] and [[luke|Luke]] give incompatible accounts of Jesus's birth, lineage, and post-resurrection appearances. "If the most serious facts of a religion are related in a contradictory manner by its historians, what credence can we give to them?" (ch. XXXIX).

**2. The miracles attributed to Jesus follow a pattern common to pagan mythology.** Holbach argues that the miracles of Jesus — healing the sick, raising the dead, multiplying food, calming storms — are structurally identical to the miracle stories told about pagan gods and heroes. "The [[pagan|pagans]] had their miracles as well as the [[christianity|Christians]]; the temples of [[esculapius|Esculapius]] (003-002) were filled with the votive offerings of those who believed themselves cured by the aid of the god" (ch. XL) (002-464).

**3. The silence of non-Christian historians is evidence against the Gospel miracles.** Holbach cites the silence of [[josephus|Flavius Josephus]], the first-century Jewish historian, regarding Jesus's public miracles. If Jesus had indeed multiplied loaves before thousands and raised the dead publicly, why does no contemporary non-Christian source mention these events? (ch. XXXIX).

**4. The doctrine of the resurrection rests on unreliable testimony.** The [[resurrection|Resurrection]] — the foundation of Christian faith — is, for Holbach, the weakest link in the Gospel chain. The accounts disagree on who visited the tomb and what they found; no independent witnesses confirm the event; and the resurrected Jesus appears only to his own followers, never to Pilate, the Sanhedrin, or the Roman public. "Is it possible that an event of this importance, which was to serve as the foundation of a new religion, should be attested only by those who are interested?" (ch. XLI).

**5. Jesus's moral teachings are not original.** Holbach argues that the Golden Rule and the command to love one's neighbor predate Jesus and can be found in [[philosophy|pagan moral philosophy]]. What is new in Christianity is not its ethics but its metaphysical claims — claims that Holbach judges to be not only unproven but incoherent (ch. XLII).

## Moral Teachings

The moral teachings attributed to Jesus in the [[gospel|Gospel]]s — the [[bible|Sermon on the Mount]], the parables, the injunctions to love one's enemies and care for the poor — have exercised profound influence on Western moral thought. Holbach acknowledges the ethical content of these teachings but insists they are neither original nor uniquely Christian. Philosophers from [[socrates|Socrates]] to [[seneca|Seneca]] taught similar principles without claiming [[divinity|divine authority]].

Holbach's more fundamental objection is that the moral teachings of Jesus are embedded within a theological framework that is itself immoral: a [[god|God]] who demands the [[sacrifice]] of his own son to satisfy his [[justice|wrath]], who threatens eternal [[punishment|damnation]] for intellectual errors, and who arbitrarily elects some to [[salvation]] and [[predestination|predestines]] others to [[hell]] cannot be a moral exemplar. The ethical content of Jesus's teaching is admirable, but the [[theology|theological system]] in which it is packaged is corrupt.

## In the Text

On the contradictory Gospel accounts of the [[resurrection|Resurrection]]:

> "The four historians of the life of Jesus do not accord in their account of the most important fact of his mission — his resurrection. According to St. [[matthew|Matthew]], the women who visited the sepulchre saw an angel who rolled away the stone; according to St. [[mark|Mark]], the stone was already rolled away and the young man was within the tomb; according to St. [[luke|Luke]], two men in shining garments appeared to them; according to St. [[john-the-evangelist|John]], [[mary|Mary Magdalene]] came alone and found the stone removed, and afterward saw two angels and then Jesus himself. Are these the consistent testimonies of truthful witnesses, or the legends of a credulous community?" (ch. XLI) (002-492).
