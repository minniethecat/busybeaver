## The Incoherence of Blasphemy

Holbach mounts a philosophical challenge to the very concept of blasphemy. He reasons that if God is truly infinite, perfect, and impassible, then no human word or action could possibly offend or injure Him. A being capable of being "offended or blasphemed" (002-107) would be a limited, reactive being — not the God of theology. The concept of blasphemy thus collapses under its own logical weight: it presupposes a God small enough to be wounded by human speech, yet the same theology that criminalizes blasphemy also proclaims God's infinite transcendence.

## Blasphemy as Political Instrument

For Holbach, the category of blasphemy serves a practical rather than theological function. Accusations of blasphemy have historically been wielded to suppress dissent, silence critics, and punish those who question religious authority. "The most cruel barbarity, the most notorious imposition, but the blindest ambition" (002-107) could have created such a doctrine — one that criminalizes honest inquiry while protecting institutional power. Holbach sees in the history of blasphemy prosecutions not the defense of sacred truth but the persecution of inconvenient truth-tellers.

## Freedom of Expression and Religious Criticism

Holbach's treatment of blasphemy connects directly to his broader arguments for intellectual freedom. If no human expression can genuinely offend a perfect Deity, then laws against blasphemy are exposed as laws against free thought — instruments for enforcing conformity rather than protecting the sacred. The restoration of reason, in Holbach's view, requires the abolition of the very category of blasphemy, freeing human beings to examine religious claims with the same critical scrutiny they apply to all other propositions.
