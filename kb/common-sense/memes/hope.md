# Hope

## Definition

Holbach treats hope — specifically the theological hope of eternal happiness in an [[death|afterlife]] — as one of the principal psychological mechanisms by which religion maintains its grip on the human mind. "The hope of a heaven, of a future life, is but imaginary" (ch. 57). It is a consoling fiction, and like all consoling fictions, it exacts a price: by directing human aspiration toward an imaginary future, it diverts energy from the real project of improving the present [[universe|world]].

The critique is not of hope as such — Holbach recognizes that the [[passion|desire]] for happiness is a natural and legitimate human motive — but of false hope, hope that has "no other guaranty than the imagi[[nations|nation]] of men, who, in supposing it, have but manifested their [[passion|desire]] to live again" (ch. 57). True hope is grounded in what reason tells us is possible; false hope is grounded in what fear and [[passion|desire]] make us wish were true.

"The grand policy of theologians is to blow hot and to blow cold, to afflict and to console, to frighten and to reassure" (ch. 111). By first creating the fear of [[punishment|hell]] and then offering the hope of heaven, religion manufactures a need that only it can satisfy — a psychological dependency that keeps believers compliant.

## Historical Context

The critique of otherworldly hope was a staple of radical Enlightenment thought. [[baruch-spinoza|Spinoza]] had argued that the hope of eternal reward and the fear of [[hell|eternal punishment]] are passions that enslave the mind, preventing it from acting rationally. The Epicurean tradition, which Holbach knew through Lucretius and Gassendi, had long identified fear of death and hope of im[[death|mortality]] as the principal sources of religious superstition.

Holbach's innovation was to connect this psychological critique to a social and political analysis. Theological hope is not merely an individual delusion but a mechanism of social control. By [[doctrine|teaching]] the poor to hope for happiness in the next [[universe|world]], religion makes them passive in this one. "They would draw no profits from the hopes with which they feast" (ch. 110) — the promise of future reward costs the powerful nothing and yields them [[eve]]rything: a docile populace that does not demand justice now.

## Core Arguments

**1. The hope of a future life has no rational foundation.** "The existence of another life has no other guaranty than the imagi[[nations|nation]] of men" (ch. 57). It is wishful thin[[monarch|king]] elevated to the status of [[doctrine|dogma]].

**2. Theological hope is a mechanism of social control.** By promising future compensation for present [[evil|suffering]], religion "destroys his courage, whose only hope is complete [[death|annihilation]]" (ch. 111) — it makes the oppressed passive and compliant.

**3. False hope diverts energy from real [[progress|improvement]].** "If [[god|God]] has not been able to render men happier than they are here below, what will become of the hope of a [[paradise|Paradise]]?" (ch. 57). The logical [[contradiction|inconsistency]] of the doctrine — an all-powerful [[god|God]] who cannot make people happy now but will make them happy later — reveals it as an evasion rather than a solution.

**4. Hope and fear are paired instruments of religious control.** "It is but with the hope of happiness that men submit to the empire of a [[god|God]]" (ch. 77). [[religion|Religion]] first threatens, then consoles — creating an [[passion|emotion]]al cycle that keeps the believer dependent on priestly mediation.

## Relation to Other Concepts

Hope is the positive pole of religion's [[passion|emotion]]al manipulation, paired with [[Fear]] as the negative pole. Both depend on [[Faith]] — the willingness to beli[[eve]] without [[proof|evidence]]. The false hope of heaven is contrasted with the real possibility of earthly [[Happiness]]. [[Desire]] is the natural human impulse that theological hope exploits. [[Reason]] is the faculty that distinguishes real hope from illusory hope. [[Immortality]] is the specific form that theological hope takes: the belief that the individual soul survives death.

## In the Text

On the imaginary character of theological hope:

> "The hope of a heaven, of a future life, is but imaginary. The existence of another life has no other guaranty than the imagination of men, who, in supposing it, have but manifested their desire to live again, in order to enter upon a purer and more durable state of happiness than that which they enjoy at present." (ch. 57)

On the logical [[contradiction|inconsistency]] of hoping for a better [[universe|world]] from a [[god|God]] who made this one:

> "If God has not been able to render men happier than they are here below, what will become of the hope of a Paradise, where it is pretended that the elect or chosen few will rejoice forever in ineffable happiness? If God could not or would not remove evil from the earth (the only sojourning place we know of), what reason could we have to presume that He can or will remove it from another world, of which we know nothing?" (ch. 57)

On hope and fear as paired instruments:

> "It is but with the hope of happiness that men submit to the empire of a God. A despot to whom men are subjected but through fear, a master whom they can not interrogate, a totally inaccessible sovereign, can not merit the homage of intelligent beings." (ch. 77)
