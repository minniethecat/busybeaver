# Guilt

## Definition

In Holbach's materialist [[morality|ethics]], guilt is a natural social phenomenon — the awareness that one has violated shared norms and may face consequences — rather than a metaphysical stain on the soul. The feeling of guilt arises from "the contempt of their fellow-[[existence|being]]s" and the fear of punishment, not from [[divinity|divine]] judgment.

Holbach distinguishes between genuine moral guilt (harm done to others) and theological guilt (violation of priest-invented rules). The former has real social consequences; the latter is an instrument of [[authority|clerical power]].

## Holbach's Arguments

Holbach deploys the concept of guilt in several closely related arguments:

**1. [[god|God]]'s own conduct would make Him guilty.** In the famous Epicurean dilemma cited in ch. LVII, Holbach reasons that if [[god|God]] "can, and will not" prevent [[injustice|evil]], "He is guilty of malice which we can not attribute to Him." The logic of guilt applies universally — [[eve]]n to the [[god|deity]] — and by this [[model|standard]] the [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian [[god|God]] fails.

**2. [[predestination|Predestination]] strips guilt of meaning.** If [[god|God]] foreordains all actions, then "guilty men whom He punishes" have "the right to complain of His cruelty." The sinner is merely carrying out the [[predestination|divine decree]]; punishment for predestined acts is not justice but sadism.

**3. Innocent and guilty suffer alike.** In ch. LXXVIII, Holbach observes that plagues and disasters "fall indiscriminately upon the good and the wicked, upon the innocent and the guilty." A universe where [[evil|suffering]] is unrelated to [[merit|moral desert]] refutes the idea of a just [[divinity|divine]] governor.

**4. The [[atonement|Atonement]] inverts guilt.** Holbach finds it [[absurdity|absurd]] that [[god|God]] would sacrifice "His own innocent and sinless Son" to reconcile with mankind "guilty without knowledge of the fault of their parents." No human [[monarch|king]] would be praised for executing his heir to appease himself.

## In the Text

The Epicurean dilemma (ch. LVII) frames guilt as a charge against [[god|God]] Himself:

> "If He can, and will not, He is guilty of malice which we can not attribute to Him; if He neither can nor will, He is both impotent and wicked, and consequently can not be God."
>
> (002-089)On predesti[[nations|nation]] and guilt (ch. LXXIII):

> "If the Divine foresight of the sin of His creatures had been necessary or forced, it might be supposed that God was compelled by His justice to punish the guilty; but God, enjoying the faculty of foresight and the power to predestinate everything, would it not depend upon Himself not to impose upon men these cruel laws?"
>
> (002-118)On indiscriminate [[evil|suffering]] (ch. LXXVIII):

> "Do we not see these plagues fall indiscriminately upon the good and the wicked, upon the impious and the pious, upon the innocent and the guilty?"
>
> (002-128)On the [[absurdity|absurd]]ity of [[atonement|vicarious atonement]] (ch. LXXXIX):

> "What strange ideas of the Divine justice must the Christians have who believe that their God, with the view of reconciling Himself with mankind, guilty without knowledge of the fault of their parents, sacrificed His own innocent and sinless Son!"
>
> (002-155)## Relation to Other Concepts

Guilt is the negative counterpart to [[remorse]], which Holbach analyzes as the inward psychological experience of anticipated social condem[[nations|nation]]. It connects to [[punishment]], [[crime]], [[sin]], and [[justice]]. The concept stands opposed to [[absolution]] and [[confession]], which Holbach sees as mechanisms for evading the natural social consequences of guilt. The predesti[[nations|nation]] argument links guilt to [[free-will]] and [[determinism]].
