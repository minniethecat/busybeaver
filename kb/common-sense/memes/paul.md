# Paul

## Biography

Paul (also called Paul the Apostle, or Saul of Tarsus before his conversion) was, according to the Acts of the Apostles and his own letters, a Pharisaic Jew from Tarsus in Cilicia who initially persecuted the early [[christianity|Christian]] movement before experiencing a dramatic conversion on the road to Damascus.
After this experience — which Paul describes as a [[revelation|revelation]] of the risen [[jesus-christ|Christ]] — he became the most energetic [[missionaries|missionary]] of the early church, underta[[monarch|king]] three major journeys across the eastern Mediterranean, establishing communities in Asia Minor, Macedonia, and Greece, and writing letters of [[education|instruction]] and exhortation that constitute the earliest surviving [[christianity|Christian]] writings.
Paul was [[eve]]ntually arrested in [[jerusalem|Jerusalem]], appealed to Caesar as a Roman citizen, and was taken to [[rome|Rome]], where — according to tradition — he was martyred under Nero around 64–67 CE.

Paul's authentic letters (Romans, 1 and 2 Corinthians, Galatians, Philippians, 1 Thessalonians, and Philemon are universally accepted; several others are disputed) predate [[gospel|the Gospel]]s by at least a decade and provide the earliest witness to [[christianity|Christian]] beliefs.
Paul never knew the historical [[jesus-christ|Jesus]]; his [[jesus-christ|Christ]] is the risen Lord encountered in vision, and his [[religion|theology]] emphasizes justification by [[religion|faith]], [[liberty|freedom]] from the Mosaic law, and the inclusion of Gentiles without circumcision.

## Role in Religious History

Paul is arguably the [[guilt|sin]]gle most consequential figure in [[christianity|Christian]] history after [[jesus-christ|Jesus]] himself — and some would argue more consequential, [[guilt|sin]]ce it was Paul who transformed a Galilean messianic movement within Judaism into a universal [[religion|religion]] open to all [[nation|peoples]]. Without Paul's theological creativity and [[missionaries|missionary]] energy, [[christianity|Christianity]] might have remained a Jewish sect.

Paul's controversies within the early church — recorded in his own letters, particularly Galatians — reveal a movement deeply divided from its earliest days.
Paul's confrontation with [[peter|Peter]] at Antioch over table fellowship with Gentiles, his insistence that Gentile converts need not be circumcised, and his complex relationship with the "pillars" of the [[jerusalem|Jerusalem]] church ([[peter|Peter]], James, and [[john-the-evangelist|John]]) show that what later came to be seen as a unified apostolic witness was, in reality, a field of competing authorities and incompatible [[dreams|visions]] of what the [[jesus-christ|Jesus]] movement should become.

The canon of Paul's letters itself was contested. The Ebionites — Jewish [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ians who observed the Mosaic law — accepted only [[matthew|Matthew]]'s [[gospel|Gospel]] and rejected Paul's epistles entirely. The Marcionites, at the opposite extreme, accepted only [[luke|Luke]]'s [[gospel|Gospel]] and Paul's letters, rejecting the entire [[bible|Old Testament]].

## Connection to "Common Sense"

Like most biblical figures in Holbach's work, Paul does not receive individual treatment but figures as an exhibit in the argument against biblical inerrancy and the stability of the canon.

**1. Contested canonical authority.** Holbach's survey of early [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian sectarian conflict over the canon places Paul at the center:

> "The Marcionites repudiated as bad, the whole of the Old Testament, and rejected the greater part of the four Gospels and the Epistles of St. Paul. The Ebionites accepted but the Gospel of St. Matthew, rejecting the three others, and the Epistles of St. Paul."
>
> (002-471)

The very epistles that most [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ians now consider foundational — Romans, Corinthians, Galatians — were rejected by some of the earliest [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian communities as false or [[heresy|heretic]]al. Conversely, the Marcionites accepted only Paul and [[luke|Luke]], treating the other apostles as corrupters of [[jesus-christ|Jesus]]'s message.
If Paul's writings are [[divinity|divine]]ly inspired, Holbach asks, why did the earliest [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ians — who were closest to the [[eve]]nts — not recognize them as such? And if they could not agree, who is in a position to decide now?

**2. The absence of the historical Jesus in Paul.** Though Holbach does not develop this point explicitly, it is implicit in his broader argument: Paul, who wrote more of the [[bible|New Testament]] than any other [[guilt|sin]]gle author, never met the historical [[jesus-christ|Jesus]].
His entire [[theology|theology]] is based on visionary experience and interpretation of [[bible|scripture]], not on firsthand recollection of [[jesus-christ|Jesus]]'s words and deeds.
If the central claim of [[christianity|Christianity]] is that [[god|God]] entered history in a specific person at a specific time, the fact that the most influential interpreter of that [[eve]]nt had no direct knowledge of it is — from a critical perspective — a severe problem.

**3. Miraculous legends.** In the catalogue of miracle-wor[[monarch|king]] relics, Holbach includes Paul alongside [[peter|Peter]]:

> "St. Paul and St. Pantaleon, having been beheaded, there flowed milk instead of blood."
>
> (002-500)

The detail — milk instead of blood from a beheaded corpse — is presented without commentary, but its placement in a list of patent [[absurdity|absurd]]ities makes Holbach's judgment clear. The same early [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian communities that could not agree on which of Paul's letters were authentic had no difficulty accepting that his severed neck flowed with milk.

## Key Ideas Attributed / Critiqued

**1. Paul as transformer, not transmitter.** Holbach's treatment of the canon implies a view of Paul as a theological innovator rather than a [[religion|faith]]ful transmitter of [[jesus-christ|Jesus]]'s [[doctrine|teaching]]. Paul's [[jesus-christ|Christ]] is a cosmic redeemer whose death and resurrection achi[[eve]] salvation — a figure substantially different from the Galilean teacher of the synoptic [[gospel|Gospel]]s. The religion that emerged was Pauline, not Jesuine.

**2. Canonical instability and institutional power.** The debate between Ebionites (who rejected Paul) and Marcionites (who accepted only Paul) illustrates Holbach's thesis that the canon was decided by [[sect|faction]]al victory, not [[divinity|divine]] guidance. The [[eve]]ntual inclusion of Paul's letters and the rejection of other apostolic traditions was a political outcome of the [[sect|faction]] that won — which then retroactively claimed [[divinity|divine]] sanction for its decisions.

**3. [[faith|Faith]] vs. reason.** Paul's [[religion|theology]] elevates [[faith|faith]] over reason in a way that Holbach would find paradigmatic of the [[religion|religious]] mentality: "the [[folly|foolishness]] of [[god|God]] is wiser than men." For Holbach, this is not a profound theological insight but an open admission that [[religion|religious]] claims cannot withstand rational scrutiny and must therefore declare reason irrelevant.

## Legacy

Paul's influence on Western civilization is diffi[[worship|cult]] to overstate. The doctrine of justification by [[religion|faith]] — central to Lutheran and [[reform|Reform]]ed Protestantism — is Pauline. The [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian [[knowledge|understanding]] of the relationship between law and grace is Pauline. The metaphor of the church as the body of [[jesus-christ|Christ]] is Pauline. Augustine's *[[confession|Confession]]s* turns on a verse from Romans; Luther's [[reform|Reform]]ation turned on another.

From Holbach's perspective, Paul represents the paradigm case of how a religious movement is built: not from the [[doctrine|teaching]]s of a historical founder but from the visionary experiences, theological creativity, and institutional energy of a subsequent interpreter.
Paul never met [[jesus-christ|Jesus]], based his authority on private visionary experiences, wrote letters that early [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ians could not agree were authentic, and yet became — through the victory of his [[sect|faction]] — the most influential voice in the [[bible|New Testament]]. If a [[religion|religion]] can be built from such materials, Holbach suggests, it was built by human hands.
