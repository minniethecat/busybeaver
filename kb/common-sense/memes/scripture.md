# Scripture

## Definition

Scripture, in the volume published as Holbach's *Common Sense* (1772), denotes the collection of texts — the Old and [[testament|New Testament]]s — that [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ians claim as [[divinity|divine]]ly inspired revelation.

The term does not appear in Holbach's own 206 chapters; it occurs exclusively in the appended *[[testament|Testament]] of [[jean-meslier|Jean Meslier]]* and [[voltaire|Voltaire]]'s biographical preface.

In these paratextual materials, "Scripture" functions not as a reverential desig[[nations|nation]] but as an object of forensic critique. Meslier's *[[testament|Testament]]* treats the [[bible|Bible]] as a human document — compiled, corrupted, and contested across centuries by competing [[sect|faction]]s — and deploys the phrase "pretended [[bible|Holy Scriptures]]" as a recurring ironic epithet to underscore the gap between scriptural authority's claimed [[divinity|divine]] origin and its [[demon|demons]]trably human history.

The volume thus presents a layered treatment: Holbach's main text dismantles the philosophical foundations of [[revelation|revealed religion]] ([[god|God]], the soul, [[liberty|free will]], providence) without engaging [[bible|scripture]] directly, while Meslier's *[[testament|Testament]]* supplies the textual-critical ammunition — [[demon|demons]]trating that [[eve]]n on its own terms, the scriptural record cannot sustain the weight placed upon it.

## Historical Context

The status of the [[bible|Bible]] as an authoritative document was the central textual question of the radical Enlightenment.

[[baruch-spinoza|Spinoza]]'s *Tractatus Theologico-Politicus* (1670) had already argued that [[bible|scripture]] must be studied as any other historical document — subject to textual corruption, authorial bias, and political motive. Richard Simon's *Critical History of the [[testament|Old Testament]]* (1678) applied emerging philological methods to [[demon|demons]]trate the composite, redacted character of the biblical text, earning the censure of Bossuet.

By Meslier's lifetime (1664–1733), these critical currents had reached the French [[priest|clergy]] themselves — Meslier, a rural parish priest, composed his *[[testament|Testament]]* using "no other assistance than the [[bible|Bible]] itself, Moreri, [[montaigne|Montaigne]], and a few fathers" ([[life|Life]] of [[jean-meslier|Jean Meslier]]), turning [[church|the Church]]'s own book against its doctrinal claims.

Holbach's decision to publish an edited version of Meslier's manuscript — appending it to his own systematic treatise — was strategically brilliant. Meslier supplied what Holbach's philosophical argument could not: the authentic testimony of a dying priest who, having administered the [[sacrament|sacraments]] for thirty years, renounced the religion whose [[bible|scripture]] he had studied from within. The volume's combined force lies in this double movement: philosophical negation from Holbach, textual demolition from Meslier.

## Core Arguments

**1. The scriptural text has been systematically corrupted.** Meslier marshals patristic testimony to [[demon|demons]]trate that the [[bible|Bible]] in circulation was not the [[bible|Bible]] as originally written.

Even acknowledged [[church|Church]] authorities conceded the point: "their saint [[jerome|Jerome]], a famous philosopher among them, formally said in several passages of his 'Prologues,' that they had been corrupted and falsified; [[existence|being]], [[eve]]n in his day, in the hands of all kinds of persons, who added and suppressed whatever they pleased" ([[testament|Testament]], I: Of [[religion|Religion]]s).

[[jerome|Jerome]]'s admission — "Thus there were as many different models as different copies of [[gospel|the Gospel]]s" — is devastating because it comes from [[church|the Church]]'s own most authoritative translator and editor of [[bible|scripture]]. If the Vulgate's own author could not guarantee textual integrity, no subsequent claim of inerrancy can stand.

The [[testament|Old Testament]] fares no better. Meslier cites Esdras (Ezra), who "testifies himself to having corrected and completed wholly the pretended [[bible|sacred books]] of his law, which had partly been lost and partly corrupted" ([[testament|Testament]], I: Of [[religion|Religion]]s). The admission of loss and reconstruction undermines any claim of continuous, [[religion|faith]]ful transmission.

Later historical [[eve]]nts compounded the problem: "All the books of the law of [[moses|Moses]] and of the [[prophecy|prophet]]s which could be found, were burned in the days of Antiochus" ([[testament|Testament]], I: Of [[religion|Religion]]s). A text twice lost — once to corruption, once to destruction — and twice reconstructed cannot plausibly be the unaltered word of [[god|God]].

**2. The proliferation of competing [[bible|scripture]]s proves human, not [[divinity|divine]], origin.** Meslier's most original argument is his catalog of early [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian [[sect|sects]], each of which produced, rejected, or modified [[bible|scripture]]s to suit its doctrinal commitments.

Marcion and his followers "rejected the books of [[moses|Moses]] and the [[prophecy|prophet]]s, and introduced other fashionable Scriptures" ([[testament|Testament]], I: Of [[religion|Religion]]s). Carpocrates and his followers "rejected the whole of the [[testament|Old Testament]]." The Marcionites "published a [[gospel|Gospel]] under the name of [[matthias|St. Matthias]], in order to confirm their doctrine." The Manicheans "wrote a gospel of their own style, and rejected the Scriptures of the [[prophecy|prophet]]s and the apostles." The Etzaites "sold a certain book which they claimed to have come from [[heaven|Heaven]]; they cut up the other Scriptures according to their [[imagination|fancy]]."

Even [[origen|Origen]] — the greatest biblical scholar of the early [[church|Church]] — does not escape the charge: "[[origen|Origen]] himself, with all his great mind, corrupted the Scriptures and forged changes in the allegories which did not suit him" ([[testament|Testament]], I: Of [[religion|Religion]]s).

The [[model|pattern]] is unmistakable: [[bible|scripture]] was not a stable deposit of [[religion|faith]] received and preserved but a fluid field of textual production, with [[eve]]ry [[sect|faction]] fabricating or altering documents to support its position. Meslier's point is that the mere existence of this textual warfare — [[gospel|gospels]] attributed to Matthias, Thomas, Bartholomew, [[peter|Peter]], and others, all rejected by the [[eve]]ntual winners — makes it impossible to select any one set as authentic on principled grounds. The canonical boundary was drawn by political victory, not [[divinity|divine]] warrant.

**3. Prophetic claims are visionary fantasy or deliberate [[imposture|fraud]].** In the [[testament|Testament]]'s sustained treatment of the [[testament|Old Testament]] [[prophecy|prophet]]s ([[testament|Testament]], V: Of the [[testament|Old Testament]]), Meslier reduces [[prophecy|prophetic]] authority to [[absurdity|absurd]]ity by holding up its most embarrassing specimens.

Meslier asks: "I would like to know how an Ezekiel would be received who should say that [[god|God]] made him eat for his breakfast a roll of parchment; commanded him to be tied like an insane man, and lie three hundred and ninety days upon his right side, and forty days upon his left, and commanded him to eat man's dung upon his bread."

The rhetorical strategy is deliberate: by isolating the most degrading [[prophecy|prophetic]] episodes and as[[monarch|king]] whether any contemporary would credit them, Meslier exposes the double [[model|standard]] by which ancient [[absurdity|absurd]]ities are accepted as revelation while modern equivalents would be dismissed as madness.

The mutual contradiction of [[prophecy|prophet]]s is further [[proof|evidence]] against [[divinity|divine]] inspiration: "All of them say, 'Beware of the false [[prophecy|prophet]]s!' as the [[charlatan|quack]]s say, 'Beware of the counterfeit pills!'" ([[testament|Testament]], V: Of the [[testament|Old Testament]]). And the empirical record of [[prophecy|prophetic]] failure is decisive: "No prophecy in favor of their Jewish [[nations|nation]] was [[eve]]r fulfilled. The number of prophecies which predict the prosperity and the greatness of [[jerusalem|Jerusalem]] is almost innumerable" — yet [[jerusalem|Jerusalem]] lay in ruins, its people scattered. [[prophecy|Prophecy]], on this account, is not [[prophecy|prediction]] but [[hope|consolation]] literature produced by a conquered people.

**4. [[gospel|Gospel]] promises are empirically falsified.** Turning to the [[testament|New Testament]] ([[testament|Testament]], VI: The [[testament|New Testament]]), Meslier tests the promises attributed to Jesus against observable reality.

Jesus promised that "all we ask of [[god|God]] in His name shall be granted, and that if we have [[religion|faith]] as a grain of mustard-seed, we could by one word remove mountains." Meslier notes the obvious: "If this promise is true, nothing appears impossible to our [[jesus-christ|Christ]]-worshipers who have [[religion|faith]] in Jesus. However, the contrary happens."

The promises were made without temporal or geographic limitation — "He who made these promises did not limit them to a certain time, or to certain places, or to certain persons" — yet no believer in Meslier's day could move mountains or obtain whatever was asked.

The [[religion|faith]]ful have devised an escape clause: [[miracle|miracles]] were necessary only to establish [[christianity|Christianity]] and ceased once it was established. Meslier's retort is that this limitation is nowhere stated in the text itself — it is a post-hoc rationalization to explain away falsified [[prophecy|prediction]]s.

**5. Key doctrines lack scriptural foundation.** Perhaps the most telling argument comes in Meslier's treatment of the [[trinity|Trinity]] ([[testament|Testament]], VII: [[error|Error]]s of [[doctrine|Doctrine]]).

After exposing the logical [[contradiction|incoherence]] of [[trinity|three persons]] in one substance, he asks a devastatingly simple question: "from whence they know this, for we do not see in their pretended [[bible|Holy Scriptures]] that any One of these [[theology-index|Divine]] personages reveals any such assertions; how, then, can our [[jesus-christ|Christ]]-worshipers know anything about it?"

The answer — "They speak but according to their ideas and to their hollow imagi[[nation|nations]]" — applies not merely to the [[trinity|Trinity]] but to the entire edifice of [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian doctrine. Scripture, which is supposed to be the sole authoritative source, is silent on doctrines [[church|the Church]] treats as essential. The gap is filled by ecclesiastical invention.

## In the Text

[[voltaire|Voltaire]]'s biographical preface sets the [[passion|emotion]]al register for Meslier's encounter with [[bible|scripture]]:

> "Struck with the difficulties which he found in Scripture, he inveighed against it more bitterly than the Acosta and all the Jews, more than the famous Porphyre, Celse, Iamblique, Julian, Libanius, and all the partisans of human reason." (Life of Jean Meslier)
>
> (APP007-026)

The catalog of ancient critics — pagans, Jews, and apostates — places Meslier in a tradition of rational opposition to scriptural authority that predates [[christianity|Christianity]] itself. [[voltaire|Voltaire]]'s point is that Meslier's bitterness was not the frivolous irreverence of a libertine but the considered conclusion of a priest who had spent his life studying the text he ultimately rejected.

The [[testament|Testament]]'s survey of early [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian scriptural chaos provides the most concentrated textual [[proof|evidence]]:

> "Marcion and his votaries rejected the books of Moses and the prophets, and introduced other fashionable Scriptures. Carpocrates and his followers did the same, and rejected the whole of the Old Testament ... The Marcionites published a Gospel under the name of St. Matthias, in order to confirm their doctrine. The apostles introduced other Scriptures in order to maintain their errors ... The Manicheans wrote a gospel of their own style, and rejected the Scriptures of the prophets and the apostles. The Etzaites sold a certain book which they claimed to have come from Heaven; they cut up the other Scriptures according to their fancy. Origen himself, with all his great mind, corrupted the Scriptures and forged changes in the allegories which did not suit him." (Testament, I: Of Religions)
>
> (APP001-014)

This catalog operates as a *reductio ad [[absurdity|absurd]]um*: if [[bible|scripture]] were [[divinity|divine]], it would be unitary and stable; the historical record of multiplication, rejection, forgery, and allegorical manipulation is the fingerprint of human — and all-too-human — authorship.

On the fundamental question of doctrinal warrant:

> "We do not see in their pretended Holy Scriptures that any One of these Divine personages reveals any such assertions; how, then, can our Christ-worshipers know anything about it? They speak but according to their ideas and to their hollow imaginations." (Testament, VII: Errors of Doctrine and of Morality)
>
> (APP007-004)

The phrase "pretended [[bible|Holy Scriptures]]" — used repeatedly throughout the [[testament|Testament]] — encapsulates Meslier's position: the adjective does the work of demolition. The texts are "pretended" because the claim of [[sacred|sanctity]] is precisely what the [[proof|evidence]] fails to support.
