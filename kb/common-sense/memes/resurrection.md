# Resurrection of Jesus

## Overview

The Resurrection of Jesus is the foundational miracle claim of [[christianity|Christianity]]: the belief that [[jesus-christ|Jesus of Nazareth]], after [[existence|being]] crucified and buried, rose bodily from the dead on the third day. All four canonical [[gospel|Gospel]]s narrate the [[eve]]nt, and [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian [[religion|theology]] treats it as the decisive [[evidence|proof]] of [[jesus-christ|Christ]]'s divinity and the guarantee of believers' own resurrection. In "Common Sense" (1772), Holbach subjects this claim to a systematic rational critique, arguing that [[gospel|the Gospel]] accounts are riddled with irreconcilable contradictions that would be fatal to any ordinary historical testimony.

## Historical Background

The resurrection narrative occupies a central place in early [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian preaching. The Apostle [[paul|Paul]] declared that "if [[jesus-christ|Christ]] be not risen, then is our preaching vain" (1 Corinthians 15:14). The [[gospel|Gospel]] accounts — written decades after the [[eve]]nts they describe — present the resurrection as a historical occurrence witnessed by the disciples. However, the four [[missionaries|evangelists]] disagree on nearly [[eve]]ry detail: the number of women at the tomb, the number of [[angel|angels]] present, the sequence of post-resurrection [[phenomena|appearances]], and the location of these [[eve]]nts. These discrepancies were noted by ancient critics of [[christianity|Christianity]] and became a cornerstone of Enlightenment-era biblical criticism.

## Key Figures and Developments

The resurrection accounts center on [[jesus-christ|Jesus of Nazareth]] and his followers. The [[gospel|Gospel]] writers — [[matthew|Matthew]], [[mark|Mark]], [[luke|Luke]], and [[john-the-evangelist|John]] — each provide a distinct, and often conflicting, version of [[eve]]nts. [[mary|Mary]] Magdalene appears in all four accounts as the first witness of the empty tomb or the risen [[jesus-christ|Christ]]. The eleven remaining apostles ([[peter|Peter]], [[john-the-evangelist|John]], and others) are portrayed as initially skeptical but ultimately convinced by post-resurrection [[phenomena|appearances]]. The doctrine of the resurrection was subsequently elaborated by [[church|Church]] fathers and became the central article of [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian creeds.

## Impact on Religion and Thought

The resurrection claim has had profound consequences. As the purported [[evidence|proof]] of Jesus's divinity, it underw[[ritual|rites]] the entire edifice of [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian [[religion|theology]] — from the [[atonement|Atonement]] to the promise of eternal [[life|life]]. The claim that a dead man physically returned to life also set a precedent for countless subsequent [[miracle|miracle]] stories, each used to validate the authority of a particular [[church|church]], [[sect|sect]], or relic. Holbach's critique targets not merely the historical implausibility of the [[eve]]nt, but the broader [[model|pattern]] by which claims of [[supernatural|the supernatural]] are used to command belief and obedience.

## Connection to "Common Sense"

Holbach devotes sustained attention to the resurrection in his critique of [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian [[bible|scripture]]. He identifies the conflicting [[gospel|Gospel]] accounts as [[proof|evidence]] that the texts cannot be of [[divinity|divine]] origin:

> "They contradict each other upon the pretended apparitions which they relate that Jesus made after His pretended resurrection; for Matthew speaks of but two apparitions... Mark speaks of three apparitions... Luke speaks of but two apparitions the same as Matthew; and John the Evangelist speaks of four apparitions" (002-492).
Holbach emphasizes the word "pretended" repeatedly — "pretended resurrection," "pretended apparitions" — signaling his view that the claim is a fabrication. He further notes the contradiction regarding location: [[matthew|Matthew]] places the [[phenomena|appearances]] in [[galilee|Galilee]], [[mark|Mark]] at a table, [[luke|Luke]] in Bethany, and John in [[jerusalem|Jerusalem]]. Such discrepancies, Holbach argues, would discredit any ordinary historical report; to accept them as [[divinity|divine]]ly inspired truth requires the suspension of [[reason|reason]] itself.

## Legacy

The rationalist critique of the resurrection, of which Holbach's treatment is a representative example, contributed to the broader Enlightenment project of subjecting religious claims to historical and logical scrutiny. [[mode|Mode]]rn biblical scholarship has largely confirmed the existence of the contradictions Holbach identified, though it interprets them differently — as [[proof|evidence]] of independent oral traditions rather than [[charlatan|fraud]]. The resurrection remains a dividing line between traditional [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian belief and secular rationalism, and Holbach's arguments continue to echo in contemporary debates between apologists and skeptics.
