# John Locke

## Biography

John Locke (1632–1704) was an English philosopher and physician, widely regarded as one of the most influential Enlightenment thinkers. Educated at Oxford, Locke studied medicine and [[science|natural philosophy]] before turning to political and epistemological questions. His major works include *An Essay Concerning [[understanding|Human Understanding]]* (1689), which argued that the mind is a *tabula rasa* at birth — all knowledge derives from [[experience|sensory experience]] — and *Two Treatises of [[government|Government]]*, which articulated principles of [[rights|natural rights]], consent of the governed, and the right of revolution that would profoundly shape liberal political thought.

## Role in Religious History

Locke's relationship with religion was complex and transformative. Though a devout [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian, he argued for [[toleration|religious toleration]] in *A Letter Concerning [[toleration|Toleration]]* (1689), contending that belief cannot be compelled by force and that civil government has no authority over souls. He also wrote *The [[reason|Reason]]ableness of [[christianity|Christianity]]* (1695), see[[monarch|king]] to reconcile [[religion|faith]] with reason by stripping away theological accretions. This rationalist approach to religion — subjecting revelation to the test of reason — made Locke a bridge figure between the [[reform|Reform]]ation and the Enlightenment critique of [[revelation|revealed religion]] that Holbach would later radicalize.

## Connection to "Common Sense"

Holbach mentions Locke in *Common Sense* as a benchmark of [[genius|intellectual genius]], contrasting the vast distance "between the genius of a Locke, of a [[isaac-newton|Newton]], and that of a peasant." For Holbach, this difference in intellectual capacity is explained entirely by natural organization — differences in the delicacy of organs, the heat of blood, the suppleness of nerves — rather than by any [[divinity|divine]] endowment or immaterial soul. Locke's empiricist epistemology, though not adopted wholesale by Holbach, provides the philosophical foundation for Holbach's [[matter|materialism]]: if all ideas come from sensation, then the claim of innate religious ideas — and the [[god|God]] who supposedly implanted them — collapses.

> "There are in the human race, beings as different from one another as man is from a horse or a dog. What conformity or resemblance do we find between some men? What an infinite distance between the genius of a Locke, of a Newton, and that of a peasant, of a Hottentot, or of a Laplander!" — Holbach, *Common Sense*, ch. XXVI

Holbach extends Locke's empiricism into a thoroughgoing [[materialism|naturalism]]. Where Locke preserved space for [[revelation|divine revelation]] verified by reason, Holbach closes that door entirely — reason, operating on sensory [[proof|evidence]] alone, finds no warrant for supernatural claims.

## Legacy

Locke's influence on the Enlightenment is diffi[[worship|cult]] to overstate. His empiricism shaped Berkeley, [[david-hume|Hume]], and ultimately Kant; his political theory informed the American and French revolutions. For Holbach and the radical Enlightenment, Locke represented both a model of rational inquiry and a figure to be surpassed — his residual [[divinity|theism]], his defense of [[christianity|Christianity]] as "reasonable," marked the limit of his break with tradition. By citing Locke as a paragon of natural human genius, Holbach simultaneously honors the English philosopher and implicitly criticizes him for stopping short of the [[atheism|atheist]]ic conclusions his own method demanded.
