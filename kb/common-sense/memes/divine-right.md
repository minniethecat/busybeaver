# Divine Right

## Definition

Divine Right, in Holbach's critique, is the political doctrine that monarchs derive their authority directly from [[god|God]], are accountable only to Him, and cannot be legitimately resisted by their subjects. Holbach treats this doctrine not as a theological proposition deserving serious engagement but as an instrument of the [[throne-and-altar]] alliance — a fiction manufactured by [[clergy|priests]] and embraced by [[monarch|king]]s to sanctify arbitrary power. The claim of [[divinity|divine]] authorization is, for Holbach, the most politically consequential of all [[imposture|religious imposture]]s because it transforms [[tyranny]] into a sacred duty and [[obedience]] into a religious obligation.

## Historical Development

The doctrine of divine right received its most influential early-modern formulation in the works of Jean Bodin (*Six Books of the Republic*, 1576) and Jacques-Bénigne Bossuet (*[[politics|Politics]] Drawn from [[scripture|Holy Scripture]]*, 1709), both of whom argued that royal authority was instituted by [[god|God]] and that rebellion was both treason and [[blasphemy|sacrilege]]. In England, the Stuart [[monarch|king]]s — particularly James I and Charles I — asserted divine right against parliamentary claims, leading to the English Civil War (1642–1651) and the execution of Charles I. The Glorious [[revolution|Revolution]] (1688) effectively repudiated divine right in England, replacing it with [[temperament|constitution]]al [[monarch|monarchy]] based on parliamentary consent.

In [[france|France]], however, divine right remained the official ideology of the Bourbon [[monarch|monarchy]] until the [[revolution|Revolution]] of 1789. Louis XIV, the "Sun King," embodied the doctrine in its most absolutist form: *L'État, c'est moi* ("I am [[government|the state]]"). It was this French [[tyranny|absolutism]] — still legally intact when Holbach wrote *Common Sense* in 1772 — that formed the primary target of his critique. Holbach recognized that divine right was not merely a theory of government but the ideological foundation of the entire *ancien régime*, binding political submission to religious [[religion|faith]] in a [[guilt|sin]]gle system of [[authority]].

## Holbach's Critique

Holbach's analysis of divine right proceeds on two fronts. First, he identifies the doctrine as historically contingent — a product of specific political struggles between church and state rather than a timeless principle of [[government]]. As he observes in *Common Sense*, when [[government|civil power]] gained advantage over [[soul|spirit]]ual power, [[clergy|priests]] "recognized the divine right of kings and preached it to the people, reserving to themselves the right to change opinions and to preach revolution, [[eve]]ry time that the [[rights|divine rights]] of [[monarch|king]]s did not agree with the [[rights|divine rights]] of [[clergy|the clergy]]." [[theology-index|Divine]] right is thus exposed as a bargaining chip in an ongoing negotiation between two competing power centers, each see[[monarch|king]] to use the other for its own ends.

Second, Holbach argues that divine right is empirically falsified by the behavior of the very monarchs who claim it. Kings who assert [[divinity|divine]] authorization for their rule routinely violate [[eve]]ry moral principle their supposed [[divinity|divine]] mandate would imply — they wage aggressive wars, oppress their subjects, and bargain cynically with [[clergy|priests]] rather than governing justly. The people, when "reduced to despair, consider the [[rights|divine rights]] of their chiefs as an abuse." Holbach's conclusion is radical: divine right is not merely doubtful but [[demon|demons]]trably false, sustained only by the [[belief|credulity]] that [[priestcraft]] manufactures and the force that [[despotism]] wields.

> "The people, when reduced to despair, consider the divine rights of their chiefs as an abuse. When men become exasperated, the divine rights of tyrants are compelled to yield to the natural rights of their subjects; they have better market with the gods than with men." (ch. II)

## Political Function

Within Holbach's broader political theory, divine right serves as the linchpin of the [[throne-and-altar|throne-and-altar]] system. The throne provides the altar with coercive force and material protection; the altar provides the throne with the ideological justification that transforms brute power into legitimate authority. [[theology-index|Divine]] right is the specific doctrine that accomplishes this transformation — it takes the fact of political domi[[nations|nation]] and clothes it in the language of sacred obligation.

The alliance is not, however, stable. Holbach notes that [[clergy|priests]] have always reserved the right to withdraw their endorsement when royal policies threaten ecclesiastical interests. The divine right they preach to the people can be revoked when [[monarch|king]]s encroach on clerical prerogatives — at which point the same [[priest|clergy]] who preached passive [[obedience]] discover a [[divinity|divine]] mandate for [[revolution]]. This flexibility reveals the doctrine's true [[universe|nature]]: not a fixed principle of political philosophy but an instrument of [[authority|clerical power]], deployed or withdrawn as circumstances demand.

## Contemporary Relevance

Holbach's critique of divine right, while directed at the specific circumstances of Bourbon [[france|France]], articulates a more general argument against the fusion of religious and [[government|political authority]]. The claim that a [[monarch|ruler]]'s power is [[divinity|divine]]ly authorized — whether expressed as the divine right of kings, the Mandate of [[heaven|Heaven]], or theocratic [[government|governance]] — serves the same function across [[worship|cult]]ures: it removes [[government|political authority]] from the sphere of rational criticism and popular [[consent]]. Holbach's alternative is a [[government]] based on the social contract, in which authority derives from the consent of the governed and [[monarch|ruler]]s are accountable to their subjects rather than to an invisible [[god|deity]] whose wishes are conveniently interpreted by a [[interest|self-interest]]ed [[priesthood]].

The doctrine of divine right, though formally abolished in most modern states, persists in residual forms wherever political leaders invoke [[divinity|divine]] authorization for their policies or where religious institutions claim a privileged role in shaping [[law]] and public policy. Holbach's analysis remains relevant to any political order in which the boundary between sacred and secular authority is contested.
