# Atheism

## Definition

Atheism, in the context of Holbach's "Common Sense" (1772), denotes the rejection of belief in a [[god|deity]] based on the insufficiency of [[proof|evidence]] and the [[contradiction|logical contradiction]]s inherent in theological claims. Holbach does not present a[[divinity|theism]] as a positive doctrine but as the natural result of subjecting religious propositions to rational scrutiny. For Holbach, a[[divinity|theism]] is not a metaphysical assertion that "[[god|God]] does not exist" but rather the conclusion that the concept of [[god|God]] lacks coherent meaning and that all purported [[proof|evidence]]s of [[divinity|divine]] existence fail under exami[[nations|nation]].

## Historical Context

The 18th-century Enlightenment witnessed the emergence of systematic atheistic thought in [[europe|Europe]]. While earlier figures such as [[baruch-spinoza|Spinoza]] had challenged orthodox conceptions of [[god|God]], Holbach's "Common Sense" (published under the name of [[jean-meslier|Jean Meslier]], a deceased Catholic priest) represented one of the first comprehensive popular arguments for a[[divinity|theism]]. The book appeared in an era when public profession of a[[divinity|theism]] could result in [[cruelty|persecution]], imprisonment, or death — as [[voltaire|Voltaire]] notes in his biographical preface, Meslier "did not dare say it during my life, but I will say it at least in dying." The work circulated clandestinely in [[paris|Paris]], with copies selling for "ten Louis-d'or apiece."

## Core Arguments

Holbach advances several core arguments for the atheistic position in "Common Sense":

1. **The inconceivability of [[god|God]]**: The concept of a "pure [[soul|spirit]]" acting on matter is incoherent. A [[soul|spirit]]ual [[existence|being]] "can not be intelligent" (ch. XLVI) because thought requires a material substrate.

2. **The problem of [[injustice|evil]]**: The existence of [[evil|suffering]] is incompatible with an omnipotent, benevolent [[god|deity]]. [[theology|Theology]] "makes of its [[god|God]] a monster of [[absurdity|nonsense]], of injustice" (ch. LXII).

3. **The sufficiency of [[universe|nature]]**: Natural phenomena require no supernatural expla[[nations|nation]]. "The [[miracle|wonder]]s of [[universe|nature]] explain themselves by natural causes" (ch. XXXVII).

4. **The moral adequacy of a[[divinity|theism]]**: [[atheist|Atheist]]s have more dependable motives for moral conduct — "the motive of pleasing himself and his fellow-creatures; of living happily and tranquilly" (ch. CLXXVIII) — than the religious, whose con[[knowledge|science]] is "guided by men whose own con[[knowledge|science]] is in error."

## Reception and Critique

Holbach's a[[divinity|theism]] provoked intense controversy. His contemporaries accused [[matter|materialism]] of "debasing to the human race" (ch. CV), arguing that without [[divinity|divine]] accountability, moral order would collapse. Holbach responded by pointing to the actual conduct of religious [[monarch|ruler]]s: "[[nothingness|Nothing]] is rarer than atheistical [[monarch|prince]]s, and nothing more common than very bad and very religious tyrants" (ch. CLXXIX). The work influenced later materialist thinkers including Marx and Diderot, though its uncompromising tone limited its acceptance [[eve]]n among Enlightenment figures.

## Relation to Other Concepts

Atheism in "Common Sense" is inseparable from [[Materialism]] (the doctrine that all phenomena arise from matter in motion), the critique of [[Superstition]] (organized religion's exploitation of fear), and the rejection of [[Providence]] (the claim that a [[god|deity]] governs [[earth|the world]]). Holbach's a[[divinity|theism]] is the logical terminus of his broader naturalistic philosophy.

## In the Text

Holbach devotes multiple chapters to defending the atheistic position. In chapter CLXXVIII, he w[[ritual|rites]]:

> "It is asked what motives has an atheist for doing right. He can have the motive of pleasing himself and his fellow-creatures; of living happily and tranquilly; of making himself loved and respected by men, whose existence and whose dispositions are better known than those of a being impossible to understand."
>
> (178-002)

And in his critique of religious morality:

> "Conscience is the inward testimony which we render to ourselves for having acted in such a manner as to deserve the esteem or the censure of those with whom we associate."
>
> (178-002)

On the question of a[[divinity|theism]] in [[government|governance]], he argues:

> "Can an atheistical king inflict more evil on the world than a Louis XI., a Philip II., a Richelieu, who have all allied religion with crime?"
>
> (179-002)
