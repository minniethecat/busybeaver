# Temple

## Definition

In Holbach's critical vocabulary, the temple represents the physical site of organized superstition — the building where [[clergy|priests]] perform rituals, display purported [[miracle|miracles]], and collect offerings from the credulous. Rather than treating temples as genuinely sacred spaces, Holbach views them as theaters of [[imposture|priestly deception]], regardless of whether they are dedicated to pagan gods or the [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian [[god|God]].

The temple serves a crucial function in Holbach's comparative method: by documenting the miracle claims, votive offerings, and healing accounts associated with pagan temples (those of Venus, Diana, Esculapius, and Apollo), he [[demon|demons]]trates that [[church|Christian church]]es replicate the same [[model|pattern]]s of [[belief|credulity]], the same types of miracle stories, and the same economic exploitation of worshippers.

## Historical Context

Temple worship was central to ancient Mediterranean religion, from the Parthenon in Athens to the Temple of [[jerusalem|Jerusalem]]. [[pagan|Pagan]] temples housed [[worship|cult]] statues, received votive offerings, and were sites of oracular consultation and healing rituals — practices vividly described by ancient authors like Pausanias, [[herod|Herod]]otus, and Livy. Early [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian apologists had to explain why [[church|Christian church]]es, which offered similar promises of miraculous healing and [[divinity|divine]] presence, were different from pagan temples.

Holbach draws on this apologetic tradition and turns it against [[christianity|Christianity]]. Writing in 1772, he follows the comparative method pioneered by earlier freethinkers like [[pierre-bayle|Pierre Bayle]] and the English deists, who catalogued parallels between pagan and [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian religious practices. Holbach's innovation is to use temple comparisons not to argue for a purified [[christianity|Christianity]] but to [[demon|demons]]trate that all religions — pagan, Jewish, [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian, and Muslim — share a common superstitious structure.

## Core Arguments

**1. All temples trade in the same miracle claims.** Holbach systematically compares [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian and pagan temple [[miracle|miracles]]: Minerva producing oil from a rock parallels [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian [[saint|saints]] producing water from rocks; the Palladium of Troy miraculously appearing in its temple parallels [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian images "received from [[heaven|Heaven]]"; the healing [[suffering|pain]]tings in the temple of Esculapius parallel [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian votive offerings for miraculous cures.

**2. The economic function of temples is universal.** [[pagan|Pagan]] temples received rich gifts and offerings; [[church|Christian church]]es do the same. Holbach observes that [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian temples "are ornamented with several pictures and rich gifts which show miraculous cures performed by the intercession of their [[saint|saints]]" — exactly as the temple of Esculapius displayed "many [[suffering|pain]]tings of miraculous cures which he had performed." The temple is a wealth-extraction mechanism regardless of the [[god|deity]] it houses.

**3. Temple [[miracle|miracles]] cancel each other out.** The comparative [[proof|evidence]] undermines any claim to [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian uniqueness. If pagan [[miracle|miracles]] are acknowledged to be [[charlatan|fraud]]ulent or natural, the identical structure of [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian [[miracle|miracles]] suggests the same conclusion. "If our [[jesus-christ|Christ]]-worshipers glorify themselves that their temples and churches are ornamented with several pictures and rich gifts which show miraculous cures," Holbach w[[ritual|rites]], "we also see, or at least we formerly saw in the temple of Esculapius at Epidaurus, many [[suffering|pain]]tings of miraculous cures."

## In the Text

Holbach's most detailed temple comparison appears in the chapter on the similarity between ancient and modern [[miracle|miracles]] (ch. III, Meslier's [[testament|Testament]]):

> "If our Christ-worshipers glorify themselves that their temples and churches are ornamented with several pictures and rich gifts which show miraculous cures performed by the intercession of their saints, we also see, or at least we formerly saw in the temple of Esculapius at Epidaurus, many paintings of miraculous cures which he had performed."

On the fire-wal[[monarch|king]] miracle parallel (ch. III):

> "If our Christ-worshipers claim that several of their saints have been miraculously preserved in the flames without having received any injury to their bodies or their clothing, the Pagans claim that the Holy women of the temple of Diana walked upon burning coals barefooted without burning or hurting their feet, and that the priests of the Goddess Feronie and of Hirpicus walked in the same way upon burning coals in the fires which were made in honor of Apollo."

On the transformation of temples (ch. III):

> "If the angels built a chapel for St. Clement at the bottom of the sea, the little house of Baucis and of Philemon was miraculously changed into a superb temple as a reward of their piety."

## Relation to Other Concepts

The temple connects to [[superstition]] — the universal human tendency to attribute natural [[eve]]nts to supernatural causes, which Holbach sees as manifesting identically across all religions. It relates to [[priestcraft]], the institutional system that operates from temples to manage [[belief|credulity]] and extract wealth. The concept links to [[miracle]], the purported [[miracle|supernatural event]]s that temples display as [[proof|evidence]] of [[almighty|divine power]], and to [[idolatry]], the worship of man-made objects and images housed in temples.
