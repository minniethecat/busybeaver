# Senses

## Definition

The senses, in Holbach's materialist philosophy, are the sole channels through which human beings acquire knowledge of the world. In the opening passages of "Common Sense," Holbach establishes a radical empiricist position: all ideas originate in sensory experience, and nothing can be known that does not act upon the senses (002-003). This sensory epistemology is the foundation of his entire critique of religion — if all knowledge comes from the senses, then claims about invisible deities, immaterial souls, and supernatural realms have no legitimate basis.

Holbach's sensory empiricism draws directly on John Locke and Étienne Bonnot de Condillac, who argued that the mind at birth is a blank slate furnished entirely through experience. Holbach applies this principle with polemical force against theological claims: "All our ideas are but pictures of objects that strike our senses" (002-003).

## Historical Context

The emphasis on sensory experience as the foundation of knowledge was a hallmark of Enlightenment epistemology. Locke's "Essay Concerning Human Understanding" (1689) had argued against innate ideas, and Condillac's "Treatise on Sensations" (1754) developed a systematic sensualist philosophy. Holbach adapted this tradition for polemical purposes: if theology makes claims about entities inaccessible to the senses, those claims are by definition unknowable and therefore without rational warrant.

This position directly opposed the Cartesian doctrine of innate ideas and the theological appeal to revelation as a source of knowledge independent of sensory experience.

## Core Arguments

1. **The sensory origin of all ideas**: Every idea, however abstract, can be traced back to sensory input. Ideas of God, soul, and immortality are merely combinations and distortions of sensory experiences (002-003).

2. **Inaccessibility of the divine to the senses**: A God "inaccessible to all our senses" is by definition unknowable. Claims about his qualities are therefore arbitrary human inventions (002-018).

3. **The testimony of the senses as reliable**: Contrary to theological claims that the senses deceive, Holbach insists that "the constant testimony of our senses" is the only reliable foundation for knowledge (002-019).

4. **Sensory evidence versus revelation**: All supernatural claims require acceptance on authority rather than sensory evidence. Holbach treats this as sufficient reason to reject them: beliefs unsupported by sensory experience are indistinguishable from fables.

## In the Text

Holbach establishes his sensory epistemology at the very beginning of "Common Sense":

> "All our ideas are but pictures of objects which do not act upon any one of their senses" (002-003, paraphrased)

The theme recurs throughout Chapter 2:

> "The constant testimony of our senses, which alone give birth to ideas" (002-019, paraphrased)

> "A being inaccessible to all our senses, and of whose qualities we are entirely ignorant" (002-018, paraphrased)
