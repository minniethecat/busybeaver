# St. Jerome

## Biography

Eusebius Sophronius Hieronymus (c. 347–420 CE), known as St. Jerome, was a Latin priest, theologian, and one of the four great Doctors of the Western [[church|Church]]. Born in Stridon (in modern Croatia or Slovenia), he studied in [[rome|Rome]] and spent formative years as a hermit in the Syrian [[merit|desert]]. His masterwork was the Vulgate — the translation of the [[bible|Bible]] into Latin that became the authoritative text of the Western [[church|Church]] for over a millennium. Jerome was a formidable polemicist, engaging in bitter controversies over [[origen|Origen]]ism, Pelagianism, and monastic practice.

## Role in Religious History

Jerome is, after Augustine, perhaps the most important Latin [[church|Church]] Father. His Vulgate translation [[model|standard]]ized the biblical text for the medieval and early modern West. Yet Jerome was also a candid witness to the textual instability of [[scripture|Scripture]] in his own era. In his prefaces (*Prologues*) to [[gospel|the Gospel]]s, he lamented that the biblical text existed in "as many different forms as there are copies" — a frank admission from a [[church|Church]] Father that Enlightenment skeptics would exploit relentlessly.

## Connection to "Common Sense"

Holbach cites Jerome in Chapter II as an unwilling witness for the prosecution against scriptural reliability. "Their saint Jerome, a famous philosopher among them, formally said in several passages of his 'Prologues' that they had been corrupted and falsified; [[existence|being]], [[eve]]n in his day, in the hands of all kinds of persons, who added and suppressed whatever they pleased." Holbach quotes Jerome's devastating conclusion — "Thus there were as many different models as different copies of [[gospel|the Gospel]]s" — and immediately links Jerome's testimony to that of the priest Esdras, who likewise confessed to having "corrected and completed wholly the pretended [[bible|sacred books]] of his law" after the originals were "partly lost and partly corrupted" (Chapter II).

For Holbach, Jerome is Exhibit A in the case that [[scripture|Scripture]] is a human artifact: if [[church|the Church]]'s own greatest biblical scholar testified that [[gospel|the Gospel]]s had been corrupted beyond a stable text, what basis remains for treating them as [[revelation|divine revelation]]? The argument turns [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian authority against itself — a sig[[universe|nature]] Holbachian strategy.

Holbach cites Jerome's own testimony that the Scriptures were corrupted:

> Generally, authors who transcribe this kind of histories, take the right to enlarge or to retrench all they please, in order to serve their own interests.
>
> (002-470)
