# Obedience

## Definition

Obedience, in Holbach's critique of religion, refers to the theological demand that human beings submit their reason, conduct, and conscience to divine commands as mediated through scripture and clergy. Holbach treats obedience not as a virtue but as a mechanism of control: the concept is deployed by religious authorities to secure compliance without rational justification (002-158).

The concept appears throughout "Common Sense" in close connection with the doctrine of original sin. The "sin of disobedience" — Adam and Eve's transgression in Eden — serves as the mythological foundation for the entire Christian system of guilt, punishment, and redemption. Holbach finds this narrative absurd: an omnipotent deity creates beings and then punishes them eternally for disobedience that he foresaw and could have prevented (APP002-003).

## Historical Context

The demand for obedience to religious authority was a central feature of 18th-century French Catholicism. The Church taught that obedience to God, the king (by divine right), and ecclesiastical hierarchy was a primary virtue. Holbach's radical critique — portraying obedience as a tool of political and priestly control — was politically subversive in a France where church and state were deeply intertwined.

This critique placed Holbach in the tradition of libertine and freethinking writers who questioned the equation of obedience with virtue. His arguments paralleled those of Jean Meslier, whose "Testament" similarly denounced the demand for blind submission to religious authority.

## Core Arguments

1. **Obedience as divine entrapment**: God gives humans commandments, knows they will disobey, and punishes them eternally — making the demand for obedience a form of entrapment rather than a moral requirement (002-498).

2. **Obedience to priests, not God**: What is demanded as obedience to divine will is in practice obedience to clerical interpreters who claim to speak for God. This transfers authority from reason to priestcraft (091-002).

3. **The Eden narrative as moral absurdity**: The foundational story of disobedience in Genesis is, in Holbach's reading, a fable that makes God appear petty and cruel — punishing all humanity for a trivial infraction he could have prevented.

## In the Text

Holbach discusses obedience primarily in theological contexts:

> "How could a just God assure Himself of the love, obedience, and respect of His subjects, who gives them the liberty to act, in order to give them the right to punish them?" (002-158, paraphrased)

The concept of disobedience as the original sin receives sustained criticism:

> "The sin of disobedience — a sin which, according to our theologians, changed the face of the universe and brought death into the world" (APP002-003, paraphrased)
