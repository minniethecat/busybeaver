# Sermon

## Holbach's Treatment

Holbach analyzes the **sermon** as a key instrument of clerical power in *Common Sense*. He notes with irony the technique of the preacher who "puts a good deal of Latin in his sermon" (002-211) — not to instruct but to impress: "Ignorant men always imagine that he who speaks Latin is a great man" (002-211). The use of an arcane language inaccessible to the congregation is, for Holbach, emblematic of the priestly strategy of mystification. Meanwhile, the laity "listen assiduously to sermons; they assist regularly in ceremonies" (002-431) — performing piety while remaining unenlightened. The sermon, in Holbach's account, is not a vehicle of moral education but a performance designed to reinforce the authority of the preacher and the passivity of the audience. True moral instruction would teach people to reason for themselves; the sermon teaches them to defer.

## Selected Passage

Holbach diagnoses the psychology of religious belief as a preference for fiction over truth — a taste that priests and legislators have exploited:

> It is the peculiarity of ignorance to prefer the unknown, the concealed, the fabulous, the wonderful, the incredible, even the terrible, to that which is clear, simple, and true. Truth does not give to the imagination such lively play as fiction, which each one may arrange as he pleases. The vulgar ask nothing better than to listen to fables; priests and legislators, by inventing religions and forging mysteries from them, have served them to their taste.
>
> (002-211)
