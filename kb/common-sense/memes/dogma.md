# Dogma

## Definition

In Holbach's "Common Sense," [[doctrine|dogma]] refers to the set of authoritative doctrines that religious institutions declare to be absolute truths, immune to rational scrutiny or empirical verification. [[dogmas|Dogmas]] are not merely beliefs held in good [[religion|faith]]; they are "obscure, inconceivable, frightful" propositions (ch. 168) deliberately crafted to confound [[reason|human reason]] and render the believer intellectually docile. Holbach treats [[doctrine|dogma]] not as a neutral category of [[belief|religious belief]] but as an instrument of cognitive control: the content of [[doctrine|dogma]] is less important than its function — the suppression of independent thought.

## Historical Context

The critique of religious dogma was central to the Enlightenment project. The seventeenth-century wars of religion had [[demon|demons]]trated the bloodshed that competing [[doctrine|dogma]]tic systems could produce; [[john-locke|Locke]]'s "Letter Concerning [[toleration|Toleration]]" (1689) argued that [[doctrine|dogma]]tic certainty could never justify coercion. By Holbach's time, the [[catholic-church|Catholic Church]]'s [[doctrine|dogma]]tic edifice — from transubstantiation to papal infallibility — had been under sustained attack by the *philosophes* for decades. [[voltaire|Voltaire]]'s cry of *écrasez l'infâme* (crush the infamy) was directed at [[doctrine|dogma]]tic superstition. Holbach's contribution was to argue that [[doctrine|dogma]] was not a corruption of religion but its essential logic: [[eve]]ry religion, by claiming access to [[divinity|divine]] truth without [[proof|evidence]], necessarily produces [[doctrine|dogma]].

## Core Arguments

**1. Dogma is designed to disable reason.** Holbach argues that the obscurity of [[doctrine|dogma]] is functional, not [[chance|accident]]al: "the dogmas are obscure, inconceivable, frightful, and, thereby, very liable to cause the imagi[[nations|nation]] to wander, and to render the common man more docile to those who wish to domineer over him" (ch. 168). A doctrine that cannot be understood cannot be rationally challenged.

**2. Dogmatic systems serve priestly and [[politics|political power]].** "The more we consider the dogmas and the principles of religion, the more we shall be convinced that their only aim is to give advantage to tyrants and [[clergy|priests]]; not having the least regard for the good of society" (ch. 147). Dogma creates a dependency relationship: believers need priestly interpretation to navigate the incomprehensible.

**3. The [[doctrine|dogma]] of [[hell|eternal punishment]] is morally incoherent.** Holbach [[vice|sin]]gles out the doctrine of [[punishment|hell]] as particularly revealing of [[doctrine|dogma]]'s true character: "The inventors of the [[doctrine|dogma]] of eternal [[torture|torment]]s in [[punishment|hell]], have made of the [[god|God]] whom they call so good, the most detestable of [[existence|being]]s" (ch. 66). A [[doctrine|dogma]] that [[attribute|attributes]] infinite cruelty to an infinitely good [[existence|being]] is logically self-refuting.

**4. Competing dogmas expose each other.** Holbach notes with irony that "the dogmas, ceremonies, the morality and the [[morality|virtue]]s which all religions of [[earth|the world]] prescribe, are visibly calculated only to extend the power or to increase the emoluments of the founders and of the ministers of these religions" (ch. 168). Every religion condemns the dogmas of [[eve]]ry other religion as superstition — and all are correct about their rivals.

**5. Dogma entrenches itself through education.** "Instead of filling the youthful minds of their pupils with fables, dogmas, and puerilities," Holbach demands, "why not invite or oblige the [[clergy|priests]] to teach them true things, and so make of them citizens useful to their country?" (ch. 198). The perpetuation of [[doctrine|dogma]] depends on capturing minds before reason has developed.

## Reception and Critique

Holbach's total rejection of religious dogma was extreme [[eve]]n for the Enlightenment. Deists like [[voltaire|Voltaire]] argued that a purified, rational religion could exist without [[doctrine|dogma]]tic excess. Rousseau, while critical of institutional religion, believed that civil religion — a minimal set of civic dogmas — was necessary for social cohesion. Later thinkers developed more nuanced positions: John Stuart Mill defended [[liberty|freedom]] of thought against [[doctrine|dogma]]tic imposition but acknowledged that provisional certainties were necessary for practical life. Karl Marx extended the critique of [[doctrine|dogma]] into a general theory of ideology: ruling ideas are always the ideas of the ruling class.

## Relation to Other Concepts

Dogma is the epistemological mechanism by which [[Religion]] maintains its hold on the human mind. It depends upon [[Ignorance]] ([[doctrine|dogma]] requires believers who do not question) and produces [[Superstition]] (fear-based compliance with irrational demands). Dogma is the antithesis of [[Reason]], which demands [[proof|evidence]] and logical coherence. It serves the interests of [[Authority]] — both priestly and political — and is perpetuated through the suppression of [[Education]]. The critique of [[doctrine|dogma]] is inseparable from Holbach's arguments against [[Faith]] and [[Revelation]].

## In the Text

Holbach's most concentrated analysis of [[doctrine|dogma]]'s social function appears in his discussion of how religions differentiate themselves:

> "The dogmas are obscure, inconceivable, frightful, and, thereby, very liable to cause the imagination to wander, and to render the common man more docile to those who wish to domineer over him."

> (002-354)

On the moral [[contradiction|incoherence]] of [[hell|eternal punishment]]:

> "The inventors of the dogma of eternal torments in hell, have made of the God whom they call so good, the most detestable of beings. Cruelty in man is the last term of corruption."
>
> (002-106)

On [[doctrine|dogma]] as a tool of power:

> "The more we consider the dogmas and the principles of religion, the more we shall be convinced that their only aim is to give advantage to tyrants and priests; not having the least regard for the good of society."
>
> (002-301)

Holbach's plea for liberation from [[doctrine|dogma]]:

> "Priests! lay aside your idle fancies, your unintelligible dogmas, your despicable quarrels; banish to imaginary regions these phantoms, which could be of use to you only in the infancy of nations; take the tone of reason."
>
> (002-412)
