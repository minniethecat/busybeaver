# Missionaries

## Definition

In Holbach's analysis, a missionary is not a selfless servant of [[divinity|divine]] truth but an entrepreneur of belief — someone who risks present comfort for the prospect of future dominion. The apparent courage and [[interest|disinterestedness]] of missionaries, which believers cite as [[proof|evidence]] for their religion's truth, dissolves under materialist scrutiny into ordinary [[interest|self-interest]].

Holbach's treatment of missionaries appears in a chapter explicitly titled to refute the argument from [[martyr|martyrdom]] and missionary sacrifice: "The [[fanaticism|Fanaticism]] of the [[martyr|Martyr]]s, the [[interest|Interest]]ed [[zeal|Zeal]] of Missionaries, Prove in Nowise the [[truth|Truth]] of [[religion|Religion]]."

## Holbach's Argument

**1. Missionary sacrifice is apparent, not real.** Holbach grants that missionaries display "generous courage" and "disinterested zeal" in preaching "at the risk [[eve]]n of [[evil|suffering]] the most rigorous [[torture|torment]]s." But this appearance of selflessness is deceptive: "in truth this [[interest|disinterestedness]] is only apparent."

**2. "[[nothingness|Nothing]] ventured, nothing gained!"** The core of Holbach's analysis is an economic metaphor. The missionary "seeks [[chance|fortune]] by the aid of his doctrine; he knows that if he has the good [[chance|fortune]] to retail his commodity, he will become the absolute master of those who accept him as their guide." The risk-reward calculus explains missionary behavior without invo[[monarch|king]] [[divinity|divine]] inspiration.

**3. The rewards are material, not [[soul|spirit]]ual.** A successful missionary "becomes the object of their [[certainty|confidence]], of their respect, of their veneration; he has [[eve]]ry reason to beli[[eve]] that he will be abundantly provided for. These are the true motives which kindle the zeal and the charity of so many preachers and missionaries who travel all over [[earth|the world]]."

**4. Miraculous protection that fails.** Holbach notes a further [[contradiction|inconsistency]]: missionaries supposedly "protected by a [[god|God]], invested with His [[theology-index|Divine]] [[power|Power]], and enjoying the gift of [[miracle|miracles]], could not perform the simple miracle of escaping from the cruelty of their persecutors." A [[god|God]] who "sacrifices unworthily His missionaries to the interests of His ambition" is not a benevolent protector but a cosmic exploiter.

## In the Text

On the apparent courage of missionaries (ch. CXXXIII):

> "We are often surprised and affected at the sight of the generous courage and the disinterested zeal which have led missionaries to preach their doctrine at the risk even of suffering the most rigorous torments."
>
> (002-261)

On the true motive:

> "Nothing ventured, nothing gained! A missionary seeks fortune by the aid of his doctrine; he knows that if he has the good fortune to retail his commodity, he will become the absolute master of those who accept him as their guide."
>
> (002-261)

On the material rewards of missionary success:

> "He becomes the object of their confidence, of their respect, of their veneration; he has every reason to believe that he will be abundantly provided for. These are the true motives which kindle the zeal and the charity of so many preachers and missionaries who travel all over the world."
>
> (002-096)

On [[god|God]]'s failure to protect His agents:

> "How can we believe that missionaries, protected by a God, invested with His Divine Power, and enjoying the gift of miracles, could not perform the simple miracle of escaping from the cruelty of their persecutors?"
>
> (002-258)

## Relation to Other Concepts

Missionaries are the active agents of [[priestcraft]], spreading the doctrines that sustain [[authority|clerical power]]. Their zeal connects to [[fanaticism]], [[martyr]] and [[martyrdom]], and the psychology of [[belief]]. Holbach contrasts missionary [[interest|self-interest]] with genuine [[virtue]] and [[courage]], which he grounds in natural sentiment rather than religious enthusiasm.
