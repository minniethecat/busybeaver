# Injustice

## Definition

Injustice is, for Holbach, the most powerful empirical argument against the existence of the [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian [[god|God]]. If [[god|God]] is omnipotent and perfectly just, why does injustice flourish? "Under the empire of a just [[god|God]], we find nothing but injustice and violence. [[power|Power]] decides the fate of [[nation|nations]]. Equity seems to be banished from the earth" (ch. LXXXVI). Theologians respond that [[god|God]]'s justice operates on a timescale beyond human comprehension — but this response, Holbach argues, is an evasion that makes justice meaningless.

The logic is simple and devastating: "Can [[god|God]] tolerate injustice for an instant? To temporize with an evil that one knows of, evinces either [[doubt|uncertainty]], weakness, or collusion; to tolerate evil which one has the power to prevent, is to consent that evil should be committed" (ch. LXXXVIII). A [[existence|being]] who permits injustice when he could prevent it is not just — regardless of what theologians call him. The word "justice" has a meaning, and that meaning cannot survive [[existence|being]] applied to a [[existence|being]] whose actions are the opposite of just.

The theological response — that [[god|God]]'s justice is not like human justice — is, for Holbach, an admission of defeat. "Is it not confounding all our ideas of justice and of injustice, to tell us that what is equitable in [[god|God]] is iniquitous in His creatures?" (ch. LXXXIX). If [[justice|divine justice]] has no relationship to human justice, then the word has been emptied of content, and calling [[god|God]] "just" is mere noise.

## Historical Context

The [[problem-of-evil|problem of evil]] — how to reconcile the existence of a good and omnipotent [[god|God]] with the existence of [[evil|suffering]] — had been the central challenge to [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian [[religion|theology]] [[vice|sin]]ce [[epicurus|Epicurus]] formulated it in antiquity. Augustine's free-will defense and [[gottfried-leibniz|Leibniz]]'s optimism (satirized by [[voltaire|Voltaire]] in *Candide*) were the most influential attempts at theodicy. Holbach's treatment belongs to a tradition of radical rejection: like [[pierre-bayle|Bayle]] before him, he argues that no theodicy works and that the failure of all theodicies is [[proof|evidence]] against [[god|God]]'s existence.

The specific target of chapters LXII–LXXXIX of *Common Sense* is the Calvinist doctrine of predesti[[nations|nation]], which Holbach presents as the logical conclusion of all attempts to reconcile [[justice|divine justice]] with human [[evil|suffering]]: [[god|God]] creates people knowing they will be damned, and then punishes them eternally for [[existence|being]] what He made them. This is "a monster of [[absurdity|nonsense]], injustice, malice, and atrocity" (ch. LXII).

## Core Arguments

**1. The existence of injustice disproves a just [[god|God]].** "Can [[god|God]] tolerate injustice for an instant?" (ch. LXXXVIII). A [[existence|being]] who permits what he could prevent is complicit — and an omnipotent [[existence|being]] can prevent [[eve]]rything.

**2. Theodicy makes [[god|God]] a monster.** The [[god|God]] of predesti[[nations|nation]] "has created the majority of men with the view only of punishing them eternally" (ch. LXII). This is not justice but "the blackest injustice" (ch. LXXX).

**3. "[[theology-index|Divine]] justice" is a contradiction in terms.** If [[god|God]]'s justice has no relationship to human justice, the word "justice" has lost its meaning. "What idea can I form of a justice which so often resembles human injustice?" (ch. LXXXIX).

**4. [[prayer|Prayer]] is an implicit admission of divine injustice.** "Praying to [[god|God]], as[[monarch|king]] a favor of Him, is to mistrust His vigilant care; to pray [[god|God]] to avert or to suppress an evil, is to endeavor to put obstacles in the way of His justice" (ch. LXXXVII). Those who pray reveal that they do not actually beli[[eve]] [[god|God]]'s administration is just.

## Relation to Other Concepts

Injustice is the central problem that destabilizes the entire theological system. It exposes the contradiction between [[god|God]]'s supposed [[Goodness]] and the observable state of [[earth|the world]]. The doctrine of [[Free Will]] is the [[model|standard]] theological attempt to shift responsibility for injustice from [[god|God]] to man — an attempt Holbach rejects. Injustice in this [[universe|world]] is supposedly remedied by [[Heaven]] and [[Hell]] — but an omnipotent [[god|God]] who needs an [[death|afterlife]] to correct the injustices of this life is a [[god|God]] who could have prevented those injustices in the first place. True [[Justice]] is possible only in a secular framework where law and institutions constrain the powerful.

On the incompatibility of injustice with a just [[god|God]]:

> "Under the empire of a just God, we find nothing but injustice and violence. Power decides the fate of nations. Equity seems to be banished from the earth; a small number of men enjoy with impunity the repose, the fortunes, the liberty, and the life of all the others. Everything is in disorder in a world governed by a God of whom it is said that disorder displeases Him exceedingly."
>
> (002-146)(ch. LXXXVI)

On [[divinity|divine]] [[toleration|tolerance]] of injustice as complicity:

> "Can God tolerate injustice for an instant? To temporize with an evil that one knows of, evinces either uncertainty, weakness, or collusion; to tolerate evil which one has the power to prevent, is to consent that evil should be committed."
>
> (002-150)(ch. LXXXVIII)

On the predestinarian [[god|God]] as a monster:

> "A God so perfidious and wicked as to create a single man and leave him exposed to the perils of damnation, can not be regarded as a perfect being, but as a monster of nonsense, injustice, malice, and atrocity. Far from forming a perfect God, the theologians have made the most imperfect of beings."
>
> (002-098)(ch. LXII)
