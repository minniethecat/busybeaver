# Incredulity

## Definition

In Holbach's *Common Sense*, "incredulity" is the theological term for the refusal to accept religious claims on insufficient evidence. Holbach observes that "nothing is more criminal than incredulity" in the eyes of theologians, who insist "that God will punish without mercy all those who have the temerity to doubt His existence" (002-033). The concept is central to Holbach's analysis of how religion enforces belief: not through rational persuasion but through the threat of punishment directed at those who exercise their rational faculty. Incredulity is criminalized doubt — the prohibition of epistemic conscientiousness.

## The Criminalization of Doubt

Holbach's treatment of incredulity exposes the circular logic of religious coercion. The theologian threatens the incredulous with divine punishment, but "they must commence by proving to our satisfaction the existence of a God, before telling us that it is horrible to doubt or to deny it" (002-033). The threat presumes the very thing that the incredulous person doubts. To punish someone for incredulity is to punish them for failing to believe a claim that has not been established — a practice that, in any domain other than religion, would be recognized as tyrannical. "My incredulity increases if I am told that this God is goodness itself" (002-258) — the more the theologian insists on God's benevolence while threatening eternal punishment for honest doubt, the more reason there is to doubt.

## Incredulity as Rational Response

Holbach recasts incredulity as a virtue rather than a vice. "How can we help our incredulity, when we see principles about which the most learned men can not agree?" (002-396). When the evidence is absent, the arguments contradictory, and the authorities divided, incredulity is not perversity — it is the natural and proper response of a rational mind. "The opinions of man do not depend upon himself; no one can believe or disbelieve at will" (002-320) — belief is not a choice but a conclusion forced (or not forced) by evidence. To criminalize incredulity is to criminalize the honest operation of the human mind.

## The Necessity of Incredulity

Holbach pushes his defense of incredulity beyond mere toleration to active necessity. If human well-being depends on acting on true beliefs rather than false ones, then incredulity toward unproven claims is not optional but obligatory. To accept claims without evidence is to risk acting on falsehoods — and where those falsehoods concern eternal salvation, the risk of error is infinite. The theologian who demands belief on insufficient grounds is asking the believer to gamble everything on a claim that cannot be verified. For Holbach, the rational response to such a demand is not faith but incredulity — and the moral response is to refuse to participate in a system that criminalizes the honest exercise of reason.

> Nothing is more criminal than incredulity; that God will punish without mercy all those who have the temerity to doubt His existence.
