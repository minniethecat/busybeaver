# Divinity

## Definition

In "Common Sense," Holbach treats the concept of divinity not as a datum of revelation or a [[salvation|deliverance]] of reason but as a human construction — a projection of human [[attribute|attributes]] onto the [[universe|cosmos]], magnified to [[infinite|infinity]] and stripped of human limitations. "If we wish to obtain an idea of [[god|God]], we must, in spite of ourselves, make a man" (ch. 61). Every divine attribute — power, knowledge, goodness, justice — is a human quality pushed to its conceptual limit. Divinity is anthropology disguised as [[religion|theology]].

Holbach's critique of divinity operates on two levels. On the epistemological level, he argues that we have no faculty for cognizing the divine: all our ideas come from sense experience, and we have no sense experience of anything divine. On the moral level, he argues that the concept of divinity has been a disaster for humanity: it has been used to sanctify [[despotism|tyranny]], to suppress reason, and to divert human energies from the [[progress|improvement]] of earthly life to the propitiation of imaginary [[existence|being]]s.

## Historical Context

The 18th century was not short of critiques of organized religion, but most Enlightenment thinkers stopped short of denying the existence of [[god|God]]. [[voltaire|Voltaire]] was a deist. Rousseau was a sentimental theist. Even [[david-hume|Hume]], in the *Dialogues*, left open the possibility of a minimal, non-interventionist [[god|deity]].

Holbach was different. In both *The System of [[nature|Nature]]* (1770) and *Common Sense* (1772), he argued that the very concept of divinity is incoherent. His critique was not directed primarily at the abuses of religion but at theism itself — the claim that a [[god|divine being]] exists at all. He was, in the terminology of the time, an "[[atheism|atheist]]" — and among the first to defend [[atheist|atheism]] openly in print (albeit pseudonymously).

This placed Holbach beyond the pale [[eve]]n for many of his Enlightenment colleagues. [[voltaire|Voltaire]], who hosted Holbach at his table, nonetheless publicly denounced [[atheist|atheism]] as dangerous to [[society|social order]]. Diderot, who shared many of Holbach's conclusions, was more cautious in his published work.

## Core Arguments

**1. Divinity is a human projection.** Every [[worship|cult]]ure's gods resemble that [[worship|cult]]ure's [[model|ideal]]ized humans. "Man has made [[god|God]] in his own image" — the [[attribute|divine attributes]] are human [[attribute|attributes]] writ large. [[omnipotence|Omnipotence]] is power without limit; omni[[knowledge|science]] is knowledge without ignorance; perfect goodness is goodness without selfishness. We arrive at these concepts by ta[[monarch|king]] a human quality and negating its human limitations — but we have no independent experience of any such unlimited quality.

**2. We have no faculty for knowing the divine.** All knowledge comes from the senses. We have no "divine sense" and no [[reason|rational faculty]] that can transcend the [[earth|material world]] to grasp an immaterial [[existence|being]]. "To speak of [[god|God]] is to speak of a [[existence|being]] of whom we cannot form any idea" (ch. 62). The theologian claims knowledge of [[god|God]] but cannot explain how he obtained it.

**3. [[theology-index|Divine]] [[attribute|attributes]] are mutually contradictory.** The [[model|standard]] [[attribute|divine attributes]] cannot all be true simultaneously. If [[god|God]] is omnipotent and perfectly good, why does [[injustice|evil]] exist? If [[god|God]] is omniscient, how can human [[existence|being]]s have [[liberty|free will]]? If [[god|God]] is infinite, how can he be a person? These are not [[mysteries]] to be accepted in [[religion|faith]] but contradictions that reveal the [[contradiction|incoherence]] of the concept.

**4. The moral cost of divinity is catastrophic.** [[belief|Belief]] in a [[god|divine being]] has not made humanity more moral. On the contrary: it has divided people into warring [[sect|sects]], justified [[cruelty|persecution]] ("killing for the love of [[god|God]]"), and directed moral energy away from human welfare toward ritual observance. "The idea of a [[god|God]] has made men tremble, but it has not made them better" (ch. 63).

## Reception and Critique

Holbach's critique of divinity was the most radical produced by the Enlightenment. Later [[atheism|atheist]] arguments — Feuerbach's projection theory, Marx's "opium of the people," Nietzsche's "[[god|God]] is dead," Dawkins's "[[god|God]] Delusion" — all have roots in Holbach's systematic demolition.

The most persistent objection is the "something rather than nothing" argument: why does anything exist at all? Holbach's answer was that this question arises from a category mistake — existence is not a predicate that some things have and others lack. [[matter|Matter]] has always existed in some form; the demand for a cause of existence-as-such assumes that "nothing" is the default state, which there is no reason to beli[[eve]].

## Relation to Other Concepts

Divinity is the concept around which Holbach's entire critique turns. [[Religion]] is the organized worship of divinity. [[Superstition]] is the fear-[[desire|drive]]n belief in divine agency. [[Theology]] is the pseudo-[[knowledge|science]] that pretends to study divinity. [[Atheism]] is the rejection of divinity — the position Holbach himself defends. [[Nature]] is the alternative to divinity as an explanatory principle. [[Reason]] is the faculty whose proper exercise leads away from divinity. [[Morality]] must be rebuilt on secular foundations once the divine foundation is removed.
