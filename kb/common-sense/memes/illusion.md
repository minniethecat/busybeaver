# Illusion

## Definition

In Holbach's *Common Sense*, "illusion" denotes the systematic deception by which religion presents agreeable falsehoods to the human mind, seducing it away from the pursuit of truth and real happiness. Holbach describes humans as allowing "themselves to be seduced by the illusions which skillful impostors persuade them are necessary" (002-246). The concept is central to his epistemology of religion: belief is not freely chosen but imposed through the manipulation of desire — humans believe in gods and afterlives not because the evidence supports them, but because the illusions are "sweet and agreeable" (002-194).

## The Mechanism of Religious Illusion

Holbach locates the power of religious illusion in its appeal to human wishes rather than to human reason. The illusion of free will is a paradigmatic case: "I feel myself free. It is an illusion which may be compared to that of a fly" (002-136) — just as a fly on a ship's mast believes itself to be steering the vessel, so humans mistake the absence of visible constraint for genuine freedom. Religious systems exploit this cognitive vulnerability systematically. They offer narratives that flatter human vanity — that we are the center of creation, that our actions have cosmic significance, that death is not annihilation — and these narratives feel true precisely because they satisfy desires that reason cannot fulfill.

## Illusion Versus Truth

The remedy for illusion is the difficult work of reason. Holbach calls religion "their dreams, their illusions, their reveries; and it will be found that they are destitute of foundation" (002-476). The pursuit of truth requires abandoning the consolations of illusion: "If this should be an illusion, is it not a sweet and agreeable one?" (002-194). Holbach's answer is an emphatic no — the sweetness of illusion is purchased at the cost of real human welfare, and the philosopher's task is to awaken humanity from its self-imposed dream.

## The Political Function of Religious Illusion

Holbach identifies the creators of religious illusion not as innocent dreamers but as "ambitious impostors" who "took exclusive possession of the minds of men" (002-323). The illusions are not accidental byproducts of human psychology; they are instruments deliberately crafted to secure power. By persuading people that their true interests lie in an invisible world governed by priestly intermediaries, these impostors acquire authority over the visible one. The illusion is thus doubly deceptive: it conceals not only the truth about nature and humanity but also the material interests of those who benefit from the deception. The priest who preaches renunciation of worldly goods lives comfortably on tithes; the sovereign who claims divine right rules without accountability.

> They are but men, speaking to men, who allow themselves to be seduced by the illusions which skillful impostors persuade them are necessary.
