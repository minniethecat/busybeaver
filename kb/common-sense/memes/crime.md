# Crime

## Definition

In Holbach's "Common Sense," crime is reconceived within a deterministic framework that denies [[liberty|free will]] and moral responsibility in the theological sense. Men do not choose to be criminals through an inexplicable exercise of libertarian [[liberty|freedom]]; they become criminals because of the social, educational, and institutional conditions that shape them. "[[religion|Religion]] concludes that man is criminal if he ceases for a moment to revere Him" (002-005) — but Holbach inverts this logic: it is society, not [[god|God]], that creates crime, and it is society, not [[punishment|divine punishment]], that must address it.

The theological concept of crime as [[vice|sin]] — an offense against [[god|God]] that merits supernatural punishment — is, for Holbach, doubly pernicious. It misidentifies the source of crime (human [[universe|nature]] corrupted by [[sin|original sin]], rather than human [[existence|being]]s shaped by bad institutions) and it proposes an ineffective remedy (repentance and [[divinity|divine]] [[absolution|forgiveness]], rather than [[reform|social reform]]). "A greedy, unjust, tyrannical government multiplies misery; the rigor of taxes produces discouragement, idleness, [[poverty|indigence]], which, on their part, produce robbery, murders, and all kinds of crime" (Appendix, ch. CLXIX).

## Historical Context

In 18th-century [[paris|France]], crime was punished with spectacular brutality. The penal code included torture, branding, the galleys, and public execution by hanging, beheading, or brea[[monarch|king]] on the wheel. [[punishment|Punishment]] was designed to be [[model|exemplar]]y — to terrify the population into obedience by [[demon|demons]]trating the [[monarch|sovereign]]'s power over life and death.

This system was coming under critical scrutiny during Holbach's lifetime. Cesare Beccaria's *On Crimes and [[punishment|Punishment]]s* (1764) — one of the most influential books of the Enlightenment — argued that punishment should be proportionate, certain, and swift, not cruel and arbitrary; that the purpose of punishment was deterrence, not vengeance; and that torture and capital punishment were both unjust and ineffective. Holbach absorbed Beccaria's arguments and extended them: if human actions are determined by motives, then the very concept of criminal responsibility must be rethought.

Holbach was not alone in connecting crime to social conditions. Montesquieu had argued in *The [[spirit|Spirit]] of the [[law|Law]]s* (1748) that different forms of government produced different [[model|pattern]]s of crime. Rousseau had argued that private property was the source of most social ills. Holbach's distinctive contribution was to insist that religion — far from preventing crime — actually contributed to it by [[doctrine|teaching]] people to fear imaginary punishments while neglecting the real causes of human [[evil|suffering]].

## Core Arguments

**1. Crime is caused by social conditions, not [[liberty|free will]].** Human [[existence|being]]s act from motives, and motives are determined by experience. "All our ideas are but pictures of objects which strike us" (002-005). A person raised in poverty, ignorance, and corruption will predictably develop criminal habits — not because their will is free but because their circumstances have shaped it. To punish such a person without changing the conditions that produced them is not justice but cruelty.

**2. [[religion|Religion]] does not prevent crime.** The fear of [[punishment|hell]] and the promise of [[hope|heaven]] are supposed to deter crime, but the [[evidence|empirical evidence]] suggests otherwise. "Does not the unjust and cruel manner in which so many [[nation|nations]] are governed here below, furnish the most visible [[evidence|proof]]s... of the non-existence of a [[providence|Providence]] interested in the fate of the human race?" (002-295). Religious [[monarch|prince]]s are no less criminal than irreligious ones; religious populations are no less prone to violence.

**3. The theological concept of "[[vice|sin]]" manufactures artificial crimes.** [[religion|Religion]] creates categories of offense that have no basis in [[vice|social harm]] — heresy, blasphemy, [[blasphemy|sacrilege]], impiety — and punishes them more severely than actual crimes against persons and property. The [[inquisition|Inquisition]] tortured and burned people not for theft or murder but for holding [[heresy|unorthodox belief]]s. This is not justice but institutionalized [[cruelty|persecution]].

**4. Crime prevention requires [[reform|social reform]], not supernatural threats.** If crime has material causes, it requires material remedies: good education, just laws, equitable distribution of resources, and institutions that reward productive behavior rather than punishing poverty. "If the [[monarch|sovereign]]s had more humanity, charity, and justice, their [[politics-index|State]]s would not be peopled by so many unfortunate ones whose misery becomes impossible to soothe" (Appendix, ch. CLXIX).

## Reception and Critique

Holbach's deterministic approach to crime was taken up by 19th-century positivist criminology, particularly Cesare Lombroso and Enrico Ferri, who sought to identify the biological and social causes of criminal behavior. The Italian positivist school argued, as Holbach had, that punishment should be tailored to the offender's dangerousness rather than to the abstract gravity of the offense — a principle that influenced the development of indeterminate sentencing and parole.

Critics have argued that [[effect|determinism]], taken to its logical conclusion, undermines the entire system of criminal justice. If no one is freely responsible for their actions, on what basis can we punish anyone? Holbach's reply would be that punishment should be reconceived as social defense rather than retribution: we restrain dangerous people to protect society, not to exact metaphysical vengeance. This utilitarian approach has been influential but remains controversial — many people feel that justice requires something more than deterrence and incapacitation.

## Relation to Other Concepts

Crime connects to [[Free-Will]], which Holbach denies — if human actions are determined, the traditional concept of [[guilt|criminal guilt]] collapses. It connects to [[Punishment]], which must be reconceived as social defense rather than [[divinity|divine]] or retributive justice. Crime is produced by [[Society]] and its institutions, particularly by bad government and economic inequality. The theological parallel to crime is [[Sin]] — an artificial category of offense against [[god|God]] that Holbach regards as a tool of priestly control.

Crime also connects to [[Justice]], which for Holbach is a this-[[universe|world]]ly [[morality|virtue]] concerned with human welfare, not a [[divinity|divine]] attribute distributed in an [[death|afterlife]]. The prevention of crime is a political problem, not a religious one.
