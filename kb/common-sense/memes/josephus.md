# Flavius Josephus

## Biography

Flavius Josephus (c. 37 – c. 100 CE) was a Jewish historian and military leader. Born Yosef ben Matityahu in [[jerusalem|Jerusalem]] to a priestly family, he initially fought against [[rome|Rome]] in the First Jewish–Roman War but surrendered and became a Roman citizen, ta[[monarch|king]] the name Flavius Josephus. His major works — *The Jewish War* (c. 75 CE) and *Antiquities of the Jews* (c. 94 CE) — are among the most important sources for the history of first-century [[judea|Judea]] and the context of early [[christianity|Christianity]].

## Role in Religious History

Josephus occupies a unique position in [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian apologetics and its critique. The *Testimonium Flavianum*, a brief passage in *Antiquities* about Jesus, has been hotly debated: most scholars consider it at least partially interpolated by later [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian scribes. More significantly for critics of [[christianity|Christianity]], Josephus's detailed biography of [[herod|Herod the Great]] and his comprehensive accounts of Jewish affairs make no mention of several [[eve]]nts central to [[gospel|the Gospel]] narratives — a silence that Enlightenment skeptics weaponized.

## Connection to "Common Sense"

Holbach's *Common Sense* invokes Josephus three times in a [[guilt|sin]]gle chapter (Chapter II), deploying him as a critical witness *against* [[gospel|Gospel]] reliability.

First, Holbach notes that Josephus, after recounting the great [[miracle|miracles]] of his [[nations|nation]], "immediately diminishes their credibility" by declaring that each reader is free to beli[[eve]] as they choose — revealing, Holbach argues, that [[eve]]n the historian did not beli[[eve]] his own miracle stories (Chapter II).

Second, and more devastatingly, Holbach observes that neither the historians of [[herod|Herod]]'s time "nor Josephus, the historian who wrote the life of this [[herod|Herod]]" say anything about the [[mass|Mass]]acre of the Innocents. [[sin|Sin]]ce Josephus "blamed the vices of this [[monarch|king]]" in detail, his silence on such a "dark and detestable action" proves, Holbach concludes, that [[gospel|the Gospel]] account is "an [[absurdity|absurd]] falsehood" (Chapter II). This argument — the argument from silence by the best-placed contemporary historian — is a cornerstone of Holbach's historical critique of [[gospel|the Gospel]]s.


## Selected Passage

Holbach exposes contradictions between the Gospel accounts of Jesus's early life:

> On the contrary, Luke asserts that Joseph and the mother of Jesus lived peaceably during six weeks in the place where their child Jesus was born; that He was circumcised according to the law of the Jews, eight days after His birth; and when the time prescribed by the law for the purification of His mother had arrived, she and Joseph, her husband, carried Him to Jerusalem in order to present Him to God in His temple, and to offer at the same time a sacrifice which was ordained by God's law; after which they returned to Galilee, into their town of Nazareth, where their child Jesus grew every day
