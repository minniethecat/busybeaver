# Daniel

## Biography

Daniel is the protagonist of the biblical book that bears his name, presented as a young Jewish noble taken into [[babylon|Babylonia]]n captivity after Nebuchadnezzar's conquest of [[jerusalem|Jerusalem]] (c. 605 BCE). In the narrative, Daniel rises to high office in the [[babylon|Babylonia]]n and Persian courts through his skill in interpreting dreams and his unwavering fidelity to Jewish law.

The book describes a series of legendary episodes: Daniel interprets Nebuchadnezzar's dream of a colossal statue representing successive empires; his three companions survive the fiery furnace; he reads the writing on the wall at Belshazzar's feast; he emerges unharmed from a den of lions into which he was thrown for praying to his [[god|God]] in defiance of a royal edict. The second half of the book contains apocalyptic [[dreams|visions]] of future [[monarch|king]]doms, cosmic conflict, and the final triumph of [[god|God]]'s people.

Critical biblical scholarship dates the book to the Maccabean period (c. 165 BCE), four centuries after the [[eve]]nts it purports to describe. It is widely regarded as the latest book of the Hebrew [[bible|Bible]], composed as resistance literature during the [[cruelty|persecution]] of Jews under Antiochus IV Epiphanes.

## Connection to Holbach's Critique

Holbach does not discuss Daniel at length — the book belongs to the [[bible|Old Testament]], which receives less detailed attention in *Common Sense* than the New — but the character of Daniel exemplifies several of his general arguments about biblical narrative.

First, the miraculous episodes in Daniel are indistinguishable in form from pagan legend. A man surviving a night in a lions' den through [[divinity|divine]] intervention is structurally identical to tales of heroes protected by gods in Greek, [[babylon|Babylonia]]n, or Persian mythology. The claim that Daniel's [[salvation|deliverance]] is historical while analogous pagan stories are fabulous rests on no criterion other than the believer's prior commitment to the truth of [[bible|scripture]].

Second, the apocalyptic [[dreams|visions]] of Daniel represent a genre — predictive prophecy through symbolic imagery — that Holbach regards as inherently deceptive. The [[dreams|visions]] are sufficiently vague to be retrospectively fitted to a wide range of historical [[eve]]nts, and the practice of "interpreting" them has generated endless and mutually contradictory readings. If [[divinity|divine]] communication were genuinely intended to inform, Holbach asks, why would it take a form that guarantees confusion?

Third, the historical setting of Daniel — the [[babylon|Babylonia]]n exile — is itself a refutation of the claim that [[israel|Israel]] was [[god|God]]'s chosen people, [[divinity|divine]]ly protected in their land. The exile [[demon|demons]]trates, for Holbach, that the Jews were subject to the same historical forces as all other [[nation|nations]]: conquest, displacement, and imperial domi[[nations|nation]]. A people uniquely protected by [[god|God]] would not spend generations in captivity.

## Key Ideas

**1. [[prophecy|Prophecy]] as retrojection.** The detailed "[[prophecy|prediction]]s" in Daniel — the succession of empires from [[babylon|Babylon]] through Persia to Greece — are accurate up to the Maccabean period and inaccurate thereafter, suggesting that the author was writing history in the guise of prophecy. This technique, common in ancient literature, is treated by Holbach as a form of pious [[charlatan|fraud]].

**2. Miraculous [[salvation|deliverance]] as folkloric motif.** The lions' den narrative appears in variants across ancient Near Eastern literature. Its presence in [[bible|scripture]] marks it not as a unique act of [[god|God]] but as one instance of a widely distributed narrative [[model|pattern]].

**3. The exile as [[proof|evidence]] against [[predestination|election]].** Daniel's situation — a conquered Jew serving foreign [[monarch|king]]s — is the lived refutation of the promise that [[israel|Israel]] would possess the land in security. The continued existence of the [[israel|Jewish people]] is attributed by [[bible|scripture]] to [[divinity|divine]] [[religion|faith]]fulness; for Holbach, it is a function of ordinary historical processes of [[worship|cult]]ural persistence.
