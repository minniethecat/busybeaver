# Immutability

## Definition

Immutability, in the context of Holbach's "Common Sense" (1772), denotes the theological doctrine that [[god|God]] is unchanging in His essence, will, and character. Theologians assert that God possesses immutability as one of His incommunicable attributes—qualities unique to the divine nature that cannot be shared with created beings (002-251)(129-002). Holbach subjects this claim to rational scrutiny and finds it incoherent: if God is immutable, He cannot change His plan, forgive sins, respond to [[prayer]], or grant [[miracle]]-working power to human agents.

## Historical Context

The doctrine of divine immutability originates in classical theism and was systematized by medieval scholastic theology. By the eighteenth century, it had become a standard tenet of Christian apologetics. Holbach confronts this doctrine within the broader Enlightenment critique of revealed [[religion]], arguing that the attributes assigned to God by theologians are not derived from observation of the natural world but are instead metaphysical abstractions that collapse under examination.

## Core Arguments

Holbach advances several interrelated arguments against divine immutability:

**The Argument from Miracles.** If God is immutable, He cannot communicate to human beings the power to create new causes capable of producing effects contrary to the ordinary course of nature. An unchangeable God cannot delegate a power that He Himself does not possess (002-251)(129-002). The claim that prophets and saints performed miracles is therefore incompatible with the doctrine of an immutable deity.

**The Argument from Vicissitude.** The observable world is characterized by continual instability, revolution, and change. If this world is governed by an immutable God powerful enough to give solidity to His works, why is everything in perpetual flux? Holbach asks: "How can we attribute to an immutable God, powerful enough to give solidity to His works, the government of a world where everything is in a continual vicissitude?" (002-080)

**The Argument from Evil.** If God is both immutable and infinitely good, He should constantly produce good effects. Yet the world is full of evils. A cause that produces good at one time and evil at another cannot be consistently good. The existence of [[evil]] and [[disorder]] in the world contradicts the notion of an immutable, benevolent cause (002-078)(002-056).

**The Argument from Freedom.** Theologians claim God is free, yet also immutable. Holbach asks: if God is self-sufficient, why create beings who suffer? An immutable being cannot begin in time and then change His mind—yet creation itself implies a change from non-creation to creation (002-093).

## Relation to Other Concepts

Immutability is closely linked to other contested divine attributes in Holbach's critique. The problem of [[evil]] arises because an immutable and benevolent God should prevent suffering but does not. The concept of [[miracle]] is undermined because an immutable being cannot suspend natural laws. [[prayer|Prayer]] and [[atonement]] are rendered futile if God's will cannot be altered by human entreaty. The attribute of immutability thus serves as a lever for Holbach's broader argument that the theological conception of [[god|God]] is a fabric of contradictions.

## In the Text

> An attempt is made to persuade us that men who have been favored by the Most High have received from Him the power to perform miracles; but in order to perform a miracle, it is necessary to have the faculty of creating new causes capable of producing effects opposed to those which ordinary causes can produce. Can we realize how God can give to men the inconceivable power of creating causes out of nothing? Can it be believed that an unchangeable God can communicate to man the power to change or rectify His plan, a power which, according to His essence, an immutable being can not have himself?
>
> (002-251)

> You pretend that God is immutable! But what is it that occasions the continual instability in this world, which you claim as His empire? Is any state subject to more frequent and cruel revolutions than that of this unknown monarch? How can we attribute to an immutable God, powerful enough to give solidity to His works, the government of a world where everything is in a continual vicissitude?
>
> (002-080)
