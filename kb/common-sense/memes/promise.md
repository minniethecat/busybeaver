# Promise

## Definition

In Holbach's *Common Sense*, "promise" is analyzed primarily as a theological instrument of control. God is portrayed as a being who makes lavish promises — of eternal happiness, of salvation, of the remission of sins — but delivers nothing in the present life. "Be faithful to your promises, and we will love you, we will bless you, in spite of this world" (002-094). The divine promise is a deferred transaction: obedience now, reward later. But the "later" never arrives, and the promise functions to secure present compliance without present accountability.

## The Empty Promise

Holbach argues that divine promises are structurally empty. "God, and His menaces, and His promises weigh nothing in the balance against the slightest hope of present happiness" (002-283). Human beings are motivated by tangible, immediate rewards; a God who offers only distant and invisible benefits cannot compete with the pleasures and pains of actual life. The theological system compensates for this weakness by elevating the stakes — the promise of eternal bliss and the threat of eternal torment are designed to outweigh any earthly consideration through sheer magnitude. But Holbach observes that even these infinite promises fail in practice: "they promise themselves they will practice it some day, and they do not practice it" (002-350).

## Historical Consequences of Divine Promises

Holbach traces the historical damage wrought by divine promises. "The Jews, excited by the promises of their God, arrogated to themselves the right of despoiling nations" (002-326) — a divine promise, believed sincerely, becomes a license for conquest and expropriation. The mechanism is general: when a people believes it has been promised dominion by God, ordinary moral constraints give way to sacred mandate. Holbach extends this analysis to Christianity: the promise of salvation has authorized countless crimes, as those who believe themselves assured of divine favor feel entitled to impose that favor on others by force. The promise is not merely empty — it is actively destructive, converting religious hope into political violence.

## Religion and the Promise of Remission

A distinctive feature of Holbach's analysis is the connection between divine promises and moral license. "Religion which expiates, and which promises the remission of crimes" (002-347) does not restrain immorality — it enables it. If sins can be forgiven through confession, absolution, or piety, then the promise of forgiveness functions as a permission slip for wrongdoing. "He does not promise us salvation but on condition of renouncing our reason" (002-394) — the promise is not a gift but a transaction in which human autonomy is the price.
