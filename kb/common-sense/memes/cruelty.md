# Cruelty

## Definition

Cruelty, for Holbach, is the hidden essence of the theological [[god|God]]. Theologians present [[god|God]] as infinitely good, but the doctrines they attach to Him — eternal [[punishment|hell]], indiscriminate punishment, the [[hell|damnation]] of the majority of humanity — describe a [[existence|being]] of incomprehensible cruelty. "The inventors of the [[doctrine|dogma]] of eternal [[torture|torment]]s in [[punishment|hell]], have made of the [[god|God]] whom they call so good, the most detestable of [[existence|being]]s" (ch. LXVI). The problem is not that theologians have misunderstood a benevolent [[god|God]] but that the concept of [[god|God]] they have constructed is inherently cruel.

Holbach's analysis distinguishes two forms of cruelty. The first is divine cruelty — the attribution to [[god|God]] of punishments that would be considered sadistic if inflicted by any human agent. He asks whether there is "in [[universe|nature]] a man so cruel as to wish in cold blood to [[torture|torment]], I do not say his fellow-[[existence|being]]s, but any sentient [[existence|being]] whatever, without fee, without profit, without curiosity, without having anything to fear" (ch. LXVI). Every human tyrant had a motive for cruelty — safety, revenge, intimidation. [[god|God]]'s cruelty, by contrast, is gratuitous — eternal [[torture|torment]] inflicted on [[existence|being]]s who cannot threaten His power or disturb His [[happiness|felicity]].

The second form is human cruelty conducted in [[god|God]]'s name. "There is no cruelty which they do not practice upon each other under this specious pretext" (ch. CC). Religious wars, inquisitions, the burning of [[heresy|heretic]]s — all are cruelty sanctified by [[religion|theology]]. The cruelty of beasts is [[desire|drive]]n by hunger; "the cruelty of man against man has no other motive than the vanity of his masters and the folly of his impertinent [[prejudice|prejudices]]" (ch. XCVII).

## Historical Context

The problem of divine cruelty is as old as mono[[divinity|theism]]. The Hebrew [[scripture|Bible]] contains numerous accounts of [[god|God]] commanding or committing acts that, if performed by humans, would be condemned as atrocities — the flood, the destruction of Sodom, the command to slaughter the [[palestine|Canaan]]ites. Marcion, in the second century, was so troubled by the contrast between the violent [[god|God]] of the [[testament|Old Testament]] and the loving [[god|God]] of the New that he concluded they must be different [[existence|being]]s.

[[enlightenment|The Enlightenment]] brought new urgency to this critique. The [[lisbon|Lisbon]] earthquake of 1755, which killed tens of thousands on All [[saint|Saint]]s' Day, prompted [[voltaire|Voltaire]] to ask how a benevolent [[god|God]] could permit such indiscriminate destruction. His *Candide* (1759) satirized the [[gottfried-leibniz|Leibniz]]ian [[injustice|theodicy]] that this is "the best of all possible [[universe|world]]s." Holbach went further: the problem is not that [[god|God]] permits cruelty but that the theological concept of [[god|God]] is itself cruel. A [[existence|being]] who creates creatures foreknowing their eternal [[hell|damnation]] is not good in any recognizable sense of the word.

The historical record of [[persecution|religious persecution]] provided Holbach with endless examples of human cruelty sanctified by [[religion|theology]]. The [[crusades|Crusades]], [[inquisition|the Inquisition]], the Wars of [[religion|Religion]] — in each case, men committed atrocities they would never have contemplated without the conviction that they were doing [[god|God]]'s work.

## Core Arguments

**1. The theological [[god|God]] is a [[existence|being]] of gratuitous cruelty.** "Cruelty in man is the last term of corruption," yet the [[god|God]] of [[religion|theology]] inflicts eternal [[torture|torment]] without motive — not for safety, revenge, or intimidation, but for the mere amusement of the [[torture|torment]]or (ch. LXVI).

**2. [[theology-index|Divine]] cruelty exceeds any human cruelty.** Even the worst human tyrants — Caligula, Nero, Domitian — had motives for their cruelty. [[god|God]]'s cruelty, [[existence|being]] eternal and without corrective purpose, is worse. "[[god|God]], in the exercise of His eternal vengeance, would have no other aim than to amuse Himself and insult the weakness of His creatures" (ch. LXVI).

**3. [[religion|Religion]] licenses human cruelty.** "There is no cruelty which they do not practice upon each other under this specious pretext" (ch. CC). The conviction of acting for [[god|God]] removes the natural inhibitions against inflicting [[evil|suffering]].

**4. Human cruelty exceeds [[soul|anima]]l cruelty because it is gratuitous.** "The cruelty of beasts against other species is caused by hunger, the need of nourishment; the cruelty of man against man has no other motive than the vanity of his masters and the folly of his impertinent [[prejudice|prejudices]]" (ch. XCVII).

## Relation to Other Concepts

Cruelty is the practical consequence of the doctrines of [[Punishment]] and [[Hell]] — if punishment is eternal and purposeless, it is cruelty by definition. Cruelty is the opposite of [[Goodness]], and its attribution to [[God]] makes the theological concept of [[god|God]] self-contradictory. The cruelty of religious [[Tyranny]] is cruelty sanctified by [[Authority]]. [[Vanity]] and [[Pride]] are the psychological sources of religious cruelty — the unwillingness to tolerate disagreement. The [[Fanaticism]]'s [[evil|suffering]] is cruelty inflicted by those who claim to act for a [[god|God]] of love.

## In the Text

On [[god|God]] as the most detestable of [[existence|being]]s:

> "The inventors of the dogma of eternal torments in hell, have made of the God whom they call so good, the most detestable of beings. Cruelty in man is the last term of corruption. There is no sensitive soul but is moved and revolts at the recital alone of the torments which the greatest criminal endures; but cruelty merits the greater indignation when we consider it gratuitous or without motive." (066-002)

On the purposelessness of divine cruelty:

> "I appeal to the whole human race! Is there in nature a man so cruel as to wish in cold blood to torment, I do not say his fellow-beings, but any sentient being whatever, without fee, without profit, without curiosity, without having anything to fear?" (066-002)

On religious cruelty among men:

> "There is no cruelty which they do not practice upon each other under this specious pretext." (ch. CC)
