# Martyr

## Definition

A martyr, in [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian [[religion|theology]], is a person who suffers [[cruelty|persecution]] and death for bearing witness to their [[religion|faith]]. The blood of the martyrs has traditionally been invoked as [[proof|evidence]] of [[christianity|Christianity]]'s truth — the argument [[existence|being]] that no one would die for a lie. Holbach devotes two chapters (CXXXII and CXXXIII) to dismantling this claim, arguing that martyrdom proves nothing except the intensity of the martyr's delusion and the cruelty of the [[god|God]] who permits such [[evil|suffering]].

For Holbach, the martyr is not a hero of [[religion|faith]] but a victim of [[fanaticism]] — someone whose natural courage has been misdirected by [[belief|religious belief]] into self-destructive devotion. "The courage of a martyr, [[soul|anima]]ted by the idea of [[paradise|Paradise]], is not any more supernatural than the courage of a warrior, inspired with the idea of glory."

## Historical Context

The [[worship|cult]] of the martyrs was central to early [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian identity. Accounts of martyrdom — from the stoning of Stephen to the execution of [[peter|Peter]] and [[paul|Paul]] — circulated widely and were read liturgically on [[saint|saints]]' feast days. The Apologists (Justin Martyr, Tertullian) had made the argument from martyrdom a cornerstone of [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian apologetics: "The blood of the martyrs is the seed of [[church|the Church]]."

By Holbach's time, this argument had been challenged from multiple directions. [[david-hume|David Hume]], in his essay "Of [[miracle|Miracle]]s" (1748), had pointed out that martyrdom is found in many religions and proves only the [[guilt|sin]]cerity of belief, not its truth. [[voltaire|Voltaire]] frequently mocked the [[belief|credulity]] of martyrdom narratives. Holbach's contribution is to fold the critique of martyrdom into his broader materialist argument: the martyr is a natural, not supernatural, phenomenon — explicable entirely through psychology and social conditioning.

## Core Arguments

**1. The blood of the martyrs testifies against [[christianity|Christianity]], not for it.** In chapter CXXXII, Holbach inverts the traditional apologetic argument. If the first apostles "suffered unheard-of [[torture|torment]]s" to spread their message, this proves not that their message was true but that the [[god|God]] who permitted their [[evil|suffering]] is "a [[god|God]] of injustice, hateful, and sanguinary."

**2. Martyrdom is universal, not unique to [[christianity|Christianity]].** "What difference do we find between an Iroquois who [[guilt|sin]]gs while he is burned by a slow fire, and the martyr St. [[law|Law]]rence, who while upon the gridiron insults his tyrant?" Every religion has its martyrs; [[eve]]ry [[nations|nation]] has warriors who die for their cause. The willingness to die proves nothing about the truth of one's beliefs.

**3. Martyrdom is fueled by the promise of [[heaven|paradise]], not truth.** The martyr's courage is not supernatural — it is "[[soul|anima]]ted by the idea of [[paradise|Paradise]]," just as "the courage of a warrior" is "inspired with the idea of glory or held to duty by the fear of disgrace." Both are natural psychological phenomena, neither requiring [[divinity|divine]] expla[[nations|nation]].

**4. Martyrs are victims, not heroes.** The [[missionaries|preachers]] of a new doctrine "succumb because they are not the strongest." The apostles did not choose martyrdom freely — they were executed because they lacked [[politics|political power]]. The [[church|Church]] later canonized their deaths as victories, transforming a political defeat into a theological triumph.

**5. Each religion claims its own martyrs.** "Each one exclaims: 'It is I!' Every one claims the same [[evidence|proof]]s; each one speaks of his [[miracle|miracles]], his [[saint|saints]], his [[prophecy|prophet]]s, his martyrs." The fact that mutually contradictory religions all invoke martyrdom as [[evidence|proof]] [[demon|demons]]trates the worthlessness of the argument.

## In the Text

The core of Holbach's argument appears in chapters CXXXII and CXXXIII. From chapter CXXXII:

> "A religion which boasts of having caused the death of many martyrs, and which informs us that its founders have suffered for its extension unheard-of torments, can not be the religion of a benevolent, equitable, and merciful God."

From chapter CXXXIII:

> "To die for a religion does not prove it true or Divine; this proves, at most, that it is considered true. A fanatic proves nothing, except that he is a fanatic."

The comparison between [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian martyr and Iroquois warrior is particularly stri[[monarch|king]]:

> "What difference do we find between an Iroquois who sings while he is burned by a slow fire, and the martyr St. Lawrence, who while upon the gridiron insults his tyrant?"

## Relation to Other Concepts

Martyrdom is the extreme expression of [[fanaticism]] and the ultimate product of [[devotion]] and [[piety]]. It connects to [[persecution]] (the social mechanism that creates martyrs), [[priestcraft]] (the institutional system that celebrates and exploits their deaths), and [[paradise]] (the promised reward that motivates self-sacrifice). Holbach's naturalistic psychology of martyrdom links it to [[courage]] and [[honor]], which he treats as equally natural but less destructive when directed toward [[universe|world]]ly rather than [[hope|heaven]]ly goals.
