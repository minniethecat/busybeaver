# canon

In Catholic theology, **canonization** is the process by which the Church formally declares a deceased person to be a saint. Holbach treats canonization narratives as examples of religious credulity, noting that the accounts are filled with absurd miracles invented in imitation of pagan fables. (002-500)

## The Fabrication of Miracles

Holbach devotes a substantial passage to cataloging the miracles associated with canonized saints, presenting them as evidence of systematic fabrication. "We should never come to an end if we had to report all this idle talk," he writes; "there is no subject, however vain, frivolous, and even ridiculous, on which the authors of these 'LIVES OF THE SAINTS' do not take pleasure in heaping miracles upon miracles, for they are skillful in forging absurd falsehoods" (002-500). The sheer volume and absurdity of the miracle claims — from St. Francis commanding swallows to obey him to the soul of St. Benedict ascending to Heaven "clothed with a precious cloak and surrounded by burning lamps" — serve, in Holbach's view, as their own refutation.

## Miracles as Pagan Imitation

Holbach identifies a pattern: the miracles attributed to Christian saints follow the same templates as pagan wonder-tales. The shoe of St. Honorius raising a dead man, the staff of St. Peter performing miracles, the cord of St. Francis working wonders — all belong to the same genre of magical thinking that produced the fables of antiquity. "For in these lives we nearly everywhere read that these pretended blessed ones cured diseases and infirmities, expelled the devils wherever they encountered them, solely in the name of Jesus or by the sign of the cross" (002-500). The only difference between a pagan miracle and a Christian one, Holbach suggests, is the name invoked.

## The Bells of Lisbon

Among the many miracles Holbach catalogs, the canonization of St. Antoine de Padua provides a particularly vivid example: "the day of the canonization of St. Antoine de Padua, all the bells of the city of Lisbon rang of themselves, without any one knowing how it was done" (002-500). For Holbach, such stories require no elaborate refutation — their inherent absurdity is sufficient. That the Church presents them as grounds for declaring a person a saint reveals the credulity at the heart of institutional religion.
