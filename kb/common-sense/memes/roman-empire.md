# Roman Empire

## Definition

The **Roman Empire** — the vast Mediterranean political entity that at its height stretched from Britain to Mesopotamia — is referenced in Holbach's "Common Sense" in two distinct registers: as the political "machine" within which [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ianity spread and was [[eve]]ntually adopted for reasons of state, and as an example (through figures like Marcus Aurelius) of [[virtue|virtue]] possible without [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian [[revelation|revelation]]. The [[empire|Empire]] serves Holbach's argument that [[religion|religion]] is shaped by, and in turn shapes, [[politics|political power]] — not [[providence|divine providence]].

## Christianity's Imperial Adoption

The Preface to "Common Sense" (by [[voltaire|Voltaire]]) traces the trajectory of [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ianity from obscure sect to imperial religion:

> "Absurd fables, injurious to divinity, which were ignored during three hundred years by the whole Roman Empire, and finally passed from the lower class to the palace of the emperors, when policy obliged them to adopt the follies of the people in order the more easily to subjugate them." (Preface)

This is the Enlightenment's political reading of [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ianization: Constantine's adoption of [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ianity was not a [[miracle|miraculous]] conversion but a calculated act of statecraft. The [[empire|Empire]] needed ideological cohesion; [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ianity, already popular among the urban lower classes, provided it. What had been "ignored" for three centuries became useful, and therefore became official. [[religion|Religion]], in this reading, is an instrument of [[government|governance]] — adopted not because it is true, but because it is useful.

## The Imperial Machine

Holbach uses the Roman Empire as a metaphor for the *systemic* [[universe|nature]] of social organization. In Chapter 83, he w[[ritual|rites]]:

> "Marcus Aurelius was a very useful spring to the vast machine of the Roman Empire. By what right will a machine despise another machine, whose springs would facilitate its own play?" (083-002)

The passage is part of Holbach's [[determinism|determinist]] argument: human [[existence|being]]s are "springs" in the social machine, their [[virtue|virtue]] or [[vice]] measured by their contribution to collective [[happiness|well-being]]. Marcus Aurelius — the Stoic emperor who persecuted [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ians — is held up as a model of [[virtue|virtue]] achieved without [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ianity. The implication is pointed: the best Roman emperor was a [[paganism|pagan]].

## Gibbon and Enlightenment Historiography

Holbach's analysis of the Roman Empire draws on a broader Enlightenment historiographical tradition exemplified by Edward Gibbon's *The History of the Decline and Fall of the Roman Empire* (1776–1789). Gibbon's argument — that [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ianity was a significant cause of the Empire's decline, promoting otherworldly concerns over civic [[virtue|virtue]] and monastic withdrawal over public engagement — resonates with Holbach's thesis that [[religion|religion]] diverts human energy from improving this [[universe|world]] to preparing for an imaginary next one.

## The Empire as a Cautionary Structure

For Holbach, the Roman Empire also serves as a structural analogy for the "sacerdotal empire" of the [[priesthood|priesthood]]. Just as Roman emperors claimed universal temporal authority, the [[church|Church]] claims universal [[soul|spirit]]ual authority — and, Holbach argues, the [[soul|spirit]]ual claim is the more dangerous, because it cannot be checked by [[reason|reason]] or resisted by force without incurring the charge of [[sacrilege|impiety]]. The Roman Empire fell; the sacerdotal empire, Holbach hopes, will fall too — but only when the populations it governs learn to think for themselves.
