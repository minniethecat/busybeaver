## Definition

Poverty, in Holbach's materialist [[morality|ethics]], is material deprivation — the lack of those goods necessary for human [[happiness|well-being]]. Unlike [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian moralists who extol poverty as a [[soul|spirit]]ual [[morality|virtue]] ("blessed are the poor"), Holbach insists that poverty is an [[injustice|evil]] to be overcome, not a state to be celebrated. The [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian praise of poverty is, in his analysis, a piece of ideological manipulation: it persuades the poor to accept their condition as [[divinity|divine]]ly ordained while [[clergy|the clergy]] who preach this doctrine live in comfort.

Holbach distinguishes between voluntary simplicity (which may be a rational choice under certain conditions) and involuntary poverty (which is always a harm). His critique is directed at a social system in which the producers of wealth — peasants, artisans, laborers — live in poverty while the non-producers — [[priest|clergy]], nobles, and idle rentiers — live in abundance.

## Historical Context

The [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian valorization of poverty has deep roots in [[gospel|the Gospel]]s ("Blessed are the poor in [[soul|spirit]]," "It is easier for a camel to pass through the eye of a needle") and was reinforced by the mendicant orders of the Middle Ages. The Franciscans, in particular, made poverty a central [[soul|spirit]]ual [[model|ideal]], arguing that [[jesus-christ|Christ]] and the apostles owned no property.

Enlightenment critics challenged this [[model|ideal]] on both economic and moral grounds. The Physiocrats and early classical economists argued that poverty was not a [[soul|spirit]]ual condition but an economic problem amenable to rational solutions. Holbach's contribution is to emphasize the hypocrisy of a system in which those who preach poverty are themselves wealthy, and to argue that material [[happiness|well-being]] is a legitimate human aim.

## Core Arguments

1. **Poverty is a [[evil|natural evil]], not a [[soul|spirit]]ual good.** Human [[existence|being]]s have material needs, and the frustration of those needs causes real [[evil|suffering]]. A morality that treats this [[evil|suffering]] as meritorious is a morality that has inverted natural values.

2. **The [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian praise of poverty is hypocritical.** The [[priest|clergy]] who tell the poor that their poverty is a blessing are themselves among the wealthiest members of society. The doctrine serves the interests of those who benefit from the labor of the poor.

3. **Poverty is maintained by the alliance of throne and altar.** The political and religious establishments cooperate to keep the producing classes in a state of material dependence. [[religion|Religion]] provides the ideological justification; [[government|the state]] provides the coercive enforcement.

4. **The elimi[[nations|nation]] of poverty requires the elimi[[nations|nation]] of [[priest|priestcraft]].** As long as a parasitic [[priest|clergy]] extracts wealth from the productive classes under the cover of religious obligation, poverty will persist. Material prosperity requires intellectual liberation.

## In the Text

Holbach traces poverty to [[divinity|divine]] permission within the theological framework, exposing the contradiction:

> "He permits or sends famines, poverty, calamities, and wars. How can you reconcile these scourges with the goodness of a God?" (ch. CXXXI)

He attacks the [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian valorization of poverty directly:

> "According to the principles of Christianity, poverty itself is a virtue, and the rich man is a criminal in the eyes of God." (ch. CXLII)

And he identifies the political function of this doctrine:

> "Poverty of spirit was that of which religion made always the greatest use. It was necessary to render men poor in spirit in order to lead them." (ch. CXLII)
