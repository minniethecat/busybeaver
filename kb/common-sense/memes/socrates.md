# Socrates

## Biography

Socrates (c. 470–399 BCE) was an Athenian philosopher whose method of inquiry — the Socratic elenchus, or cross-exami[[nations|nation]] — laid the foundation for Western [[morality|ethics]] and epistemology. He wrote nothing himself; his ideas survive through the dialogues of his students [[plato|Plato]] and Xenophon, and through the testimony of contemporaries like Aristophanes. Sentenced to death by the Athenian democracy on charges of impiety and corrupting the youth, Socrates accepted the hemlock rather than flee, becoming a martyr for philosophical integrity. His life and death established the model of the philosopher as one who questions [[opinion|received opinion]] and follows reason wherever it leads — [[eve]]n to death.

## Role in Religious History

Though Socrates was executed for impiety, he was not an [[atheism|atheist]] in any modern sense. He spoke of a *daimonion* — an inner [[divinity|divine]] voice — and frequently invoked the gods in conversation. His philosophical legacy, however, proved deeply subversive to traditional Greek religion. By subjecting [[eve]]ry claim about the gods to rational scrutiny, Socrates established the principle that [[belief|religious belief]] must answer to reason — a principle that would, centuries later, fuel the Enlightenment's critique of revelation. Early [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian apologists, notably Justin [[martyr|Martyr]], claimed Socrates as a "[[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian before [[jesus-christ|Christ]]," recognizing his moral seriousness while repudiating his [[pagan|polytheist]]ic context.

## Connection to "Common Sense"

In *Common Sense*, Holbach places Socrates within the genealogy of [[model|ideal]]ist philosophy that, in his view, betrayed the materialist insights of earlier Greek thinkers. In the Appendix, Holbach w[[ritual|rites]] that the sensible systems of [[democritus|Democritus]], [[epicurus|Epicurus]], and Straton "were obliged to surrender to the fabulous conjectures of [[plato|Plato]], Socrates, and [[zeno-of-citium|Zeno]]." For Holbach, Socrates — filtered through [[plato|Plato]] — represents the moment when Greek philosophy turned away from materialist expla[[nations|nation]] toward a [[universe|world]] of invisible Forms and immaterial principles, a move that Holbach sees as preparing the ground for [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian [[religion|theology]]. Holbach's critique is not of Socrates personally but of the [[model|ideal]]ist tradition he inadvertently enabled: a philosophy that privileges invisible realities over the [[proof|evidence]] of the senses.

> "Lucippe, Democritus, Epicurus, Straton, and some other Greeks, dared to tear away the thick veil of prejudice, and to deliver philosophy from theological fetters. But their systems, too simple, too sensible, and too stripped of wonders for the lovers of fancy, were obliged to surrender to the fabulous conjectures of Plato, Socrates, and Zeno." — Holbach, *Common Sense*, Appendix

## Legacy

Socrates's influence on Western thought is incalculable. Through [[plato|Plato]], he shaped the entire [[model|ideal]]ist tradition; through the Stoics and Cynics, he inspired schools of practical [[morality|ethics]]; through the Enlightenment, his commitment to rational inquiry became a model for [[courage|intellectual courage]]. Holbach's dismissal of Socrates as part of the "fabulous conjectures" tradition is a strategic move in a larger polemic: by tracing [[model|ideal]]ism from Socrates through [[plato|Plato]] to [[christianity|Christianity]], Holbach constructs a genealogy of error that his own [[materialism|materialist philosophy]] is meant to correct. In this sense, Socrates serves as a foil — the philosopher who, despite his brilliance, pointed philosophy in what Holbach considered the wrong direction.
