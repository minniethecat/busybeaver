# Throne and Altar

## Definition

"Throne and Altar" is the central political metaphor in Baron d'Holbach's critique of organized religion. It denotes the strategic alliance between [[government|political authority]] (the throne, representing [[monarch|monarchy]] and [[government|the state]]) and [[authority|religious authority]] (the altar, representing the church and [[priest|clergy]]). For Holbach, these two institutions are not natural allies but mutually reinforcing systems of [[persecution|oppression]]: the throne provides the altar with coercive force and material protection, while the altar provides the throne with [[divinity|divine]] legitimation and popular obedience. This symbiosis constitutes what Holbach regards as the fundamental political pathology of pre-Enlightenment [[europe|Europe]] — a self-perpetuating compact in which [[monarch|king]]s rule by "[[divine-right|divine right]]" and [[clergy|priests]] preach submission to [[monarch|king]]s as a religious duty.

## Historical Context

The throne-and-altar alliance reached its classical formulation in the [[europe|Europe]]an *ancien régime*, particularly in Catholic [[france|France]] where the coro[[nations|nation]] of [[monarch|king]]s at Reims and the doctrine of the [[divine-right|divine right of kings]] fused monarchical and [[authority|ecclesiastical authority]] into a [[guilt|sin]]gle ideological apparatus. The Concordat of Bologna (1516) gave French [[monarch|king]]s control over episcopal appointments, binding the hierarchy of the church directly to the interests of the crown. By Holbach's time, this alliance faced sustained challenge from Enlightenment philosophers who argued that both institutions sustained themselves through popular ignorance.

Holbach wrote *Common Sense* (1772) in the decades preceding [[revolution|the French Revolution]], when the alliance between the Bourbon [[monarch|monarchy]] and the [[catholic-church|Catholic Church]] remained formally intact but was increasingly contested by the *philosophes*. The expulsion of the [[jesuits|Jesuits]] from [[france|France]] (1764) [[demon|demons]]trated that [[eve]]n within the alliance, tensions could erupt when [[authority|clerical power]] threatened royal prerogative. Holbach's radical innovation was to argue that the throne and altar were not merely circumstantially allied but *structurally* interdependent — neither could survive without the [[belief|credulity]] the other [[worship|cult]]ivated.

## Core Arguments

Holbach's central argument is that the throne-and-altar alliance operates through a division of labor in the production of submission. The [[priest|clergy]] manufacture [[doctrine|theological doctrine]]s — [[sin|original sin]], [[hell|eternal punishment]], [[divinity|divine]] judgment — that render the human mind fearful, docile, and habituated to unquestioning obedience. The monarch then harvests this [[worship|cult]]ivated docility for political purposes: a population trained to submit to an invisible, incomprehensible [[god|God]] will also submit to a visible, coercive [[monarch|sovereign]].

The second argument is epistemological. The alliance maintains itself by preventing rational inquiry. [[priest|Priest]]s declare theological [[mysteries]] beyond the reach of [[reason|human reason]]; [[monarch|king]]s declare political matters beyond the competence of subjects. Both authorities converge on the same principle: *do not think, but beli[[eve]] and obey*. Holbach counters that the free exercise of reason dissolves both theological and political mystification simultaneously — to question the authority of [[clergy|priests]] is inevitably to question the authority of [[monarch|king]]s, and vice versa.

The third argument is historical and comparative. Holbach observes that wherever the throne-and-altar alliance is strongest — in Catholic absolutist states — the population is most miserable and most ignorant. Conversely, he points to societies where priestly power has been curtailed (England after the Glorious [[revolution|Revolution]]; the Dutch Republic) as [[proof|evidence]] that political liberty and religious [[doubt|skepticism]] advance together. The implication is radical: genuine [[reform|political reform]] requires the total dismantling of [[authority|ecclesiastical authority]].

## Reception and Critique

Holbach's throne-and-altar thesis has received sustained attention in scholarship on the origins of modern secularism. Jonathan [[israel|Israel]] (*Democratic Enlightenment*, 2011) identifies Holbach's formulation as the most uncompromising version of the Radical Enlightenment critique — going beyond [[voltaire|Voltaire]]'s pragmatic anti-clericalism and Montesquieu's institutional analysis to assert that [[monarch|monarchy]] and [[clergy|priesthood]] are structurally inseparable and must fall together. [[israel|Israel]] argues this thesis directly influenced the [[jacob|Jacob]]in phase of [[revolution|the French Revolution]], particularly the de-[[jesus-christ|Christ]]ianization campaign of 1793–94.

Critics have raised several objections. The first is empirical: the English case, which Holbach himself cites approvingly, [[demon|demons]]trates that [[monarch|monarchy]] and established religion can coexist in a [[temperament|constitution]]al framework without producing the [[despotism|despot]]ic fusion he describes. The Hanoverian settlement retained a state church and a hereditary [[monarch|monarchy]] while developing parliamentary government and [[toleration|religious toleration]]. Second, the 19th-century experience of Catholic-majority states (Belgium, post-1870 [[france|France]]) shows that throne-and-altar alliances can be broken without abolishing either institution — secularization can proceed through disestablishment rather than destruction. Charles Taylor (*A Secular Age*, 2007) makes the broader point that Holbach's analysis treats religion exclusively as an instrument of social control, which flattens the genuine [[soul|spirit]]ual motivations that have historically [[desire|drive]]n resistance to state power (abolitionism, civil rights movements, liberation [[religion|theology]]).

Nevertheless, contemporary scholars of authoritarianism have returned to Holbach's insight. The alliance of political authoritarianism with religious establishments — whether in Tsarist Russia's "Orthodoxy, Autocracy, [[nation|Nation]]ality" or in modern variants — [[demon|demons]]trates the enduring relevance of his core claim: that the fusion of sacred and [[government|political authority]] produces a distinctive and particularly resilient form of [[tyranny|despotism]].

## Relation to Other Concepts

The throne-and-altar concept functions as Holbach's master analytical framework connecting his epistemology to his politics. It directly depends on [[priestcraft]] — the systematic [[priestcraft|clerical manipulation]] that produces the [[belief|credulity]] upon which the alliance rests. Without a population trained by [[clergy|priests]] in intellectual submission, monarchs would lack the raw material for political domi[[nations|nation]]. The concept connects to [[tyrant]] and [[despotism]] as the political outcomes of the alliance — when the throne is unrestrained by law and the altar by reason, the result is the arbitrary power that Holbach calls [[despotism|tyranny]]. Through its analysis of how religion legitimizes [[government|political authority]], throne-and-altar connects to [[divine-right]] and [[sovereign]] — the specific doctrines through which sacred and secular power are fused. The concept's negative example is [[toleration]] — the political arrangement Holbach advocates, in which religious institutions are subordinated to civil peace rather than elevated as partners in [[government|governance]]. The [[mandarins]] of China, who permitted [[superstition|popular superstition]] while preventing [[priest|clergy]] from disturbing [[government|the state]], provide Holbach's positive counter-model.

## In the Text

Holbach illustrates the throne-altar alliance through concrete historical examples. He invokes the [[bartholomew-massacre|St. Bartholomew's Day Massacre]] (1572) as the paradigmatic case: a [[monarch|king]] (Charles IX), guided by priestly counsellors and a queen mother [[soul|anima]]ted by Catholic zeal, authorized the slaughter of thousands of his Protestant subjects.

> "Can an atheistical king inflict more evil on the world than a Louis XI., a Philip II., a St. Bartholomew's Day Massacre?"
>
> (179-002)

The rhetorical question is devastating — religious monarchs, not [[atheism|atheist]]s, have authored the worst crimes of [[europe|Europe]]an history.

He also analyzes the figure of the "[[atheism|atheist]] on a throne" as a thought-experiment. Even granting the worst caricature of a [[monarch|ruler]] without religious scruples, Holbach argues such a figure could hardly exceed the cruelty of conspicuously pious monarchs. Philip II of S[[suffering|pain]], who presided over [[inquisition|the Inquisition]] and the [[cruelty|persecution]] of Protestants in the Netherlands while maintaining ostentatious Catholic devotion, serves as the counter-example: religion did not restrain his [[despotism|tyranny]] but *enabled* it by sanctifying his power.

In Chapter CLXIV, Holbach extends the metaphor to ancient [[pagan|paganism]], noting that [[jupiter|Jupiter]] himself — the model for [[divinity|divine]] [[monarch|king]]ship — was a "rebellious son, who wrests his father's throne from him and then mutilates his body" (160-002). The origin-story of [[divinity|divine]] authority is itself an act of violent usurpation, undermining any claim that monarchs derive their legitimacy from a morally perfect source.
