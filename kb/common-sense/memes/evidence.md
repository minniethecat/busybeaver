# Evidence

## Definition

For Holbach, [[proof|evidence]] is the foundation of all legitimate belief. "Evidence can be relied on only when it is confirmed by constant experience" (ch. XVIII). This is a thoroughgoing empiricism — beliefs are warranted only when supported by [[experience|sensory experience]] repeatedly confirmed. [[theology|Theology]] inverts this principle: "[[faith|Faith]], according to theologians, is consent without [[proof|evidence]]" (ch. CXXXV). The demand to beli[[eve]] without [[proof|evidence]] is not a higher [[soul|spirit]]ual achievement but a confession that theological claims cannot survive rational scrutiny.

Holbach's concept of [[proof|evidence]] has both a positive and negative dimension. Positively, [[proof|evidence]] from the senses and reason is the sole route to knowledge. Negatively, the absence of [[proof|evidence]] for a proposition is decisive grounds for rejecting it. Theologians attempt to make the absence of [[proof|evidence]] into a [[morality|virtue]] — calling it "[[religion|faith]]" — but this is simply an evasion of the only [[model|standard]]s by which truth can be distinguished from falsehood.

The [[proof|evidence]] [[model|standard]] is the great leveler: it applies equally to all claims regardless of their antiquity, authority, or [[passion|emotion]]al appeal. No proposition is exempt. "I will not sacrifice my reason, because this reason alone enables me to distinguish good from [[injustice|evil]], the true from the false" (002-266).

## Historical Context

The privileging of [[proof|evidence]] over authority was a defining commitment of the Enlightenment. [[john-locke|Locke]]'s *Essay Concerning Human [[mind|Understanding]]* (1689) argued that probable knowledge is proportional to the [[proof|evidence]] for it — a direct challenge to [[revelation|revealed religion]]'s claim to certainty without [[proof|evidence]]. [[david-hume|Hume]]'s *Enquiry Concerning Human [[mind|Understanding]]* (1748) made the famous declaration that "a wise man proportions his belief to the [[proof|evidence]]," concluding that [[miracle|miracles]] cannot be believed because the [[proof|evidence]] for uniform natural law always outweighs testimony for a violation.

French [[matter|materialism]] radicalized this empiricism. Diderot and d'Alembert's *Encyclopédie* (1751–1772) was itself an exercise in ma[[monarch|king]] knowledge rest on [[proof|evidence]] rather than authority. Holbach's contribution was to apply the [[proof|evidence]] [[model|standard]] relentlessly to [[eve]]ry theological claim, [[demon|demons]]trating that none could meet it. His method was not to argue against religion on metaphysical grounds but to demand [[proof|evidence]] and observe that none was forthcoming.

Theologians had long maintained that some truths exceed reason and must be accepted on authority. [[enlightenment|The Enlightenment]]'s counter-move was not to deny that some things exceed reason but to insist that the claim of revelation itself must be supported by [[proof|evidence]] — and that no such [[proof|evidence]] exists.

## Core Arguments

**1. Evidence is the only test of truth.** "Evidence can be relied on only when it is confirmed by constant experience" (ch. XVIII). A claim unsupported by sensory [[proof|evidence]] and rational [[proof|demonstration]] is indistinguishable from a falsehood. [[theology|Theology]] has no special exemption from this universal [[model|standard]].

**2. [[faith|Faith]] without [[proof|evidence]] is a confession of falsehood.** When theologians define [[religion|faith]] as "consent without [[proof|evidence]]" (ch. CXXXV), they admit that their doctrines cannot meet the [[model|standard]] by which all other beliefs are judged. They then present this failure as a [[morality|virtue]] — "sacrifice your reason; give up experience; distrust the testimony of your senses" — but this is simply the demand to abandon the only instruments for detecting error.

**3. The absence of [[proof|evidence]] for [[miracle|miracles]] is itself decisive.** "How can these pretended [[miracle|miracles]] be the [[proof|evidence]]s of truth?" (ch. CCII). [[miracle|Miracle]] claims are never supported by the kind of [[proof|evidence]] that would be required for any extraordinary claim — multiple independent witnesses, controlled conditions, absence of interested motives. The [[proof|evidence]] for [[miracle|miracles]] is testimony from credulous ages, recorded by partisans, and accepted only by those already committed to the religion in question.

**4. Evidence-based reasoning is the path to human [[progress|improvement]].** The rejection of [[proof|evidence]] in favor of authority has retarded moral and intellectual progress. "The principles of reason and [[proof|evidence]]" (ch. CCI) are what enable human [[existence|being]]s to improve their condition — to surround truth with clarity rather than obscurity.

## Relation to Other Concepts

Evidence is the epistemological criterion that distinguishes [[Reason]] from [[Faith]]. Where [[religion|faith]] demands belief without [[proof|evidence]], reason demands [[proof|evidence]] proportional to the claim. [[Proof]] is the formal structure of [[proof|evidence]] — a chain of reasoning from established premises to a conclusion. [[Truth]] is [[government|the state]] that [[proof|evidence]] properly understood produces. [[Mystery]] is what [[religion|theology]] substitutes for [[proof|evidence]] when none can be produced. The rejection of [[proof|evidence]] as a [[model|standard]] is the defining move of all [[Superstition]].

## In the Text

On [[religion|faith]] as belief without [[proof|evidence]]:

> "Faith, according to theologians, is consent without evidence. From this it follows that religion exacts that we should firmly believe, without evidence, in propositions which are often improbable or opposed to reason." (ch. CXXXV)

On the refusal to sacrifice reason:

> "I will not sacrifice my reason, because this reason alone enables me to distinguish good from evil, the true from the false." (ch. CXXXV)

On [[proof|evidence]] and constant experience:

> "Evidence can be relied on only when it is confirmed by constant experience." (ch. XVIII)
