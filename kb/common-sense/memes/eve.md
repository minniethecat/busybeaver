# Eve

## Definition

Eve, in the biblical narrative of Genesis, is the first woman, formed from [[adam|Adam]]'s rib and placed with him in the Garden of Eden. In Baron d'Holbach's materialist critique, Eve belongs to the category of mythological fictions — narrative inventions borrowed from older Near Eastern sources and repurposed by Hebrew [[clergy|priests]] to construct a theologically serviceable account of human origins.

Holbach treats the Eden story, including Eve's creation and her interaction with the [[serpent]], not as sacred history but as one of many "fabulous tales" that reveal the human, rather than [[divinity|divine]], origin of [[bible|scripture]].

## Historical Context

The Genesis creation narrative, including the story of Eve, draws upon Mesopotamian and [[palestine|Canaan]]ite mythological traditions that predate the Hebrew [[bible|scripture]]s by centuries. The motif of a woman formed from a man's body, a [[heaven|paradise]] garden, a tal[[monarch|king]] [[soul|anima]]l, and a fatal [[crime|transgression]] all have parallels in Sumerian, [[babylon|Babylonia]]n, and [[egypt|Egypt]]ian literature.

Eighteenth-century biblical critics — most notably [[pierre-bayle|Pierre Bayle]] and the English Deists — had already begun to document these parallels, arguing that the Pentateuch was a composite human document rather than [[revelation|divine revelation]].

Holbach wrote within this critical tradition. By the 1770s, the question of Mosaic authorship had been seriously challenged; the documentary hypothesis was ta[[monarch|king]] shape; and the Genesis narratives were increasingly read as mythology rather than history. Holbach's contribution was to integrate this scholarship into a popular and polemical format, using the Eve story as a rhetorical weapon against the [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian claim that [[bible|scripture]] was dictated by [[god|God]].

## Core Arguments

Holbach's critique of the Eve narrative operates on three levels. First, the story is structurally identical to pagan myths — "as much as those invented about the industry of Prometheus, the box of Pandora, the war of the Giants against the [[gods|Gods]]" — and therefore cannot claim unique [[divinity|divine]] origin. A woman formed from a rib, a spea[[monarch|king]] [[serpent]], and a magical garden are narrative elements indistinguishable from the fables [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ians dismiss in Greek and Roman mythology.

Second, the moral logic of the Eve story is incoherent. An omniscient [[god|God]] creates [[existence|being]]s He knows will disobey, permits a "simple [[serpent]]" to seduce them, and then punishes not only the transgressors but all their descendants for an act they did not commit. "He sent to the first [[existence|being]]s of the human race, [[adam|Adam]] and Eve, a [[demon|devil]], or a simple [[serpent]], to seduce them, and by this means ruin all men. This is not credible!"

Third, the Eve story illustrates Holbach's broader thesis about [[priest|priestcraft]]. By inventing a narrative in which the first woman's disobedience brings universal calamity, [[clergy|priests]] manufacture a universal guilt that only they — through their [[sacrament|sacraments]], their authority, and their interpretation — can remediate. Eve is not merely a fictional character; she is a functional instrument of ecclesiastical power.

## Reception and Critique

Holbach's treatment of Eve as a mythological fiction has had a complex reception history. Within Enlightenment circles, his demythologizing approach was widely shared — [[voltaire|Voltaire]] and Diderot made similar arguments — and the nineteenth-century development of biblical higher criticism largely vindicated Holbach's claim that Genesis draws upon older Near Eastern sources.

Feminist biblical scholarship in the twentieth century has engaged Holbach's critique from a different angle. Thinkers like Phyllis Trible and Elisabeth Schüssler Fiorenza have argued that the Eve narrative, read against its patriarchal reception history, can be recovered as a text about human responsibility and relationality rather than female culpability. Holbach's flat-footed dismissal of the story as a "fable" — while politically useful in his context — fails to engage the hermeneutical complexity that feminist readers have uncovered.

[[custom|Tradition]]al [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian apologetics has responded to Holbach's critique by distinguishing between the theological truth of Genesis and its literary genre. The claim that Genesis is "myth" in the sense of "false story" conflates myth as a literary form with myth as a truth-claim — a category error, on this view, that Holbach's polemical purposes lead him to commit. Whether Genesis is "true" depends on what kind of truth it claims to convey, and ancient Near Eastern narrative conventions differ from modern historiographical ones.

In literary studies, the Eve narrative has been analyzed as a foundational text in Western literature's representation of women, knowledge, and [[crime|transgression]]. Holbach's reading, which treats the story as simple [[priest|priestcraft]], underestimates its [[worship|cult]]ural productivity — the Eve figure has been reinterpreted, reimagined, and contested across two millennia of art, literature, and philosophy, a fact that complicates any straightforward dismissal of the narrative as a merely instrumental fiction.

## Relation to Other Concepts

Eve is inextricably linked to [[original-sin]], the doctrine of [[original-sin|inherited guilt]] that depends upon her [[crime|transgression]] in Eden. Holbach's critique of [[sin|original sin]] and his critique of the Eve narrative are mutually reinforcing: if Eve is a mythological fiction, the doctrine built upon her actions is doubly unfounded.

The figure of Eve is also related to [[serpent]], the spea[[monarch|king]] tempter whose presence in the narrative Holbach treats as decisive [[proof|evidence]] of its mythological character. A tal[[monarch|king]] [[soul|anima]]l, in Holbach's materialist universe, is a narrative marker of fiction, not a [[existence|being]] that could exist in [[nature|the natural world]].

Eve connects to [[misogyny]] in the history of [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian thought. The patristic tradition — Tertullian's infamous address to women as "the [[demon|devil]]'s gateway," Augustine's association of Eve with carnality — used the Genesis narrative to justify the subordi[[nations|nation]] of women. Holbach's critique of Eve, while primarily epistemological, has implications for this patriarchal tradition: if the story that authorizes misogyny is a fiction, the misogyny it authorizes loses its theological warrant.

The concept also relates to [[mythology]], the comparative category into which Holbach places the Genesis account. By treating the Eve story as one myth among many — comparable to Pandora, to Ishtar, to the Sumerian goddess Ninti ("lady of the rib") — Holbach relativizes the [[bible|Bible]]'s claim to unique revelation.

Finally, Eve inter[[sect|sects]] with [[biblical-criticism]], the scholarly discipline that Holbach's polemical arguments anticipated and that would, in the nineteenth and twentieth centuries, provide detailed philological and historical [[proof|evidence]] for the composite, human-authored character of the Pentateuch.

## In the Text

Holbach addresses the Eve narrative directly in *Common Sense*, grouping it with other biblical stories that "appear to be fables." He w[[ritual|rites]] dismissively of "a woman formed of a man's rib; of the pretended terrestrial [[paradise|Paradise]]; of a [[serpent]] which spoke, which reasoned, and which was more cunning than man" — placing the Eden narrative alongside "an ass which spoke" and "a universal deluge" as [[proof|evidence]] that [[bible|scripture]] contains "no erudition, no sublime thought" beyond "the ordinary capacities of the human mind" (ch. CXXVII).

In a separate passage, Holbach sharpens his critique by focusing on the moral implications. He contrasts [[god|God]]'s alleged intervention to prevent a pagan [[monarch|king]] from [[guilt|sin]]ning "although there would be no results to follow" with His failure to prevent "[[adam|Adam]] and Eve from offending Him and falling into the [[guilt|sin]] of disobedience — a [[guilt|sin]] which, according to our [[jesus-christ|Christ]]-worshipers was to be fatal, and cause the destruction of the human race" (ch. CLIV).
