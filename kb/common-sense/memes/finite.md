# Finite

## Definition

The finite, in Holbach's "Common Sense," designates the bounded, limited, and conditioned [[mode|mode of existence]] proper to all known [[existence|being]]s, including human [[existence|being]]s. Holbach's argument is deceptively simple: human [[existence|being]]s are finite; [[god|God]] is said to be infinite; but "if [[god|God]] is infinite, no finite [[existence|being]] can have communication or any relation with Him" (ch. VII). From this premise he draws the radical conclusion that religion — which presumes relation between finite humans and infinite [[god|God]] — is an [[absurdity|absurd]]ity.

## Historical Context

The finite-infinite distinction has deep roots in Western philosophy. Anaximander spoke of the *apeiron* (the unlimited); medieval scholastics debated whether finite intellects could know an infinite [[god|God]] through analogy; [[rene-descartes|Descartes]] grounded his [[evidence|proof]]s of [[god|God]] in the idea that a finite mind could not generate the idea of an infinite [[existence|being]]. Holbach inverts this tradition: rather than treating the gap between finite and infinite as a problem to be solved by revelation or grace, he treats it as a logical [[proof|demonstration]] that the entire framework is incoherent. His position radicalizes the nominalist critique that concepts applied to [[god|God]] are merely verbal.

## Core Arguments

**1. Finite and infinite are relationally incommensurable.** "Where there are no relations, there can be no union, no correspondence, no duties. If there are no duties between man and his [[god|God]], there exists no religion for man" (ch. VII). The ontological gap is unbridgeable — not by [[religion|faith]], not by grace, not by analogy.

**2. The idea of [[infinite|infinity]] is "an idea without model."** "The idea of [[infinite|infinity]] is for us an idea without model, without [[model|prototype]], without object" (ch. VII). [[mind|Human mind]]s, [[existence|being]] finite, can form no genuine concept of [[infinite|the infinite]]; the word is used without corresponding mental content.

**3. [[life|Future life]] does not solve the problem.** Even if humans were "more enlightened" in an [[death|afterlife]], "the [[infinite|infinity]] of [[god|God]] will always place such a distance between his idea and the limited mind of man, that he will not be able to conceive of [[god|God]] any more in a [[hope|future life]] than in the present" (ch. VIII). The problem is structural, not a matter of insufficient information.

**4. The finite-infinite gap annihilates religion.** "Thus by saying that [[god|God]] is infinite, you annihilate, from that moment, all religion for man, who is a finite [[existence|being]]" (ch. VII). The very attribute that theologians invoke to magnify [[god|God]]'s greatness is, on analysis, what makes [[god|God]] irrelevant to human life.

**5. Even superior intelligences fare no better.** Holbach extends the argument: "[[angel|Angel]]s, [[angel|archangels]], [[angel|seraphim]]s, and [[saint|saints]] — can have no more complete notions of [[god|God]] than has man, who does not understand anything about Him" (ch. VIII). No finite intellect, however elevated, can bridge the gap.

## Reception and Critique

Holbach's use of the finite-infinite distinction reflects the influence of [[baruch-spinoza|Spinoza]], for whom finite [[mode|modes]] are [[mode|modification]]s of infinite substance. However, Holbach deploys the argument for [[atheism|atheist]]ic rather than pantheistic conclusions. Critics have noted that the argument proves too much: if no relation between finite and infinite is possible, then Holbach's own claims about what an infinite [[god|God]] would or would not entail are equally impossible. The argument has been revived in contemporary philosophy of religion by those who argue that [[attribute|divine attributes]] generate paradoxes of infinitude.

## Relation to Other Concepts

The finite-infinite distinction is foundational to Holbach's critique of [[God]] and [[Religion]]. It connects to his epistemology — finite minds know only through [[Experience]] and [[Reason]], not revelation. It supports his turn toward [[Nature]] as the proper object of human knowledge: [[universe|nature]], unlike [[god|God]], is finite and knowable. It aligns with his [[Materialism]]: material [[existence|being]]s are finite, extended, and interact causally; an infinite immaterial [[existence|being]] cannot enter this causal network.

## In the Text

The argument is stated with exceptional concision in chapter VII:

> "If God is infinite, no finite being can have communication or any relation with Him. Where there are no relations, there can be no union, no correspondence, no duties. If there are no duties between man and his God, there exists no religion for man. Thus by saying that God is infinite, you annihilate, from that moment, all religion for man, who is a finite being." (ch. VII)

On the impossibility of bridging the gap [[eve]]n in an [[death|afterlife]]:

> "If God is an infinite being, there can be neither in the actual world or in another any proportion between man and his God; thus the idea of God will never enter the human mind. In the supposition of a life where men will be more enlightened than in this one, the infinity of God will always place such a distance between his idea and the limited mind of man, that he will not be able to conceive of God any more in a future life than in the present." (ch. VIII)
