# David

## Biography

David (c. 1040–970 BCE, according to traditional chronology) was the second [[monarch|king]] of the United Kingdom of [[israel|Israel]], succeeding Saul. According to the Books of Samuel, he rose from shepherd to giant-slayer (Goliath), to court musician for the [[torture|torment]]ed Saul, to outlaw chief, and finally to [[monarch|king]] over a unified [[israel|Israel]]. His reign is portrayed as the golden age of [[israel|Israel]]ite [[monarch|monarchy]]: he captured [[jerusalem|Jerusalem]] and made it his capital, brought the Ark of the [[covenant|Covenant]] there, and established a dynasty that would rule Judah for four centuries.

The biblical portrait of David is unusually complex for an ancient royal biography. He is at once the "man after [[god|God]]'s own heart" and an adulterer who arranges the death of Uriah the Hittite to cover his liaison with Bathsheba. His later years are marked by family catastrophe — the rape of his daughter Tamar, the rebellion of his son Absalom, and the execution of his son Adonijah by his successor [[solomon|Solomon]].

In Jewish tradition, David is the sweet [[guilt|sin]]ger of [[israel|Israel]], the author of the Psalms, and the father of the messianic line. In [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian [[religion|theology]], [[jesus-christ|Jesus]] is "son of David" — the fulfillment of the promise that David's throne would be established forever. In [[islam|Islam]], Dawud is a [[prophecy|prophet]] and [[monarch|king]] who received the Zabur (Psalms) and judged with [[justice|justice]].

## Role in Religious History

The Davidic covenant — the promise that David's dynasty would endure forever (2 Samuel 7) — is the theological linchpin connecting [[israel|Israel]]ite [[monarch|monarchy]] to Jewish messianism and [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ology. When the [[prophecy|prophet]]s of the exile spoke of a future [[monarch|king]] from David's line who would restore [[israel|Israel]], they were drawing on this promise. When the [[gospel|Gospels]] open with genealogies tracing [[jesus-christ|Jesus]]'s descent from David, they are claiming that the promise has been fulfilled.

The centrality of the Davidic promise makes it a natural target for Holbach's critique. If the messianic claim depends on Davidic descent, then the reliability of the genealogical record becomes a question of [[proof|evidence]], not [[faith|faith]]. Holbach subjects the gospel genealogies to the same scrutiny he applies to other biblical claims: they are contradictory ([[matthew|Matthew]] and [[luke|Luke]] give incompatible Davidic lineages for [[jesus-christ|Jesus]]), historically unverifiable, and functionally indistinguishable from the legendary genealogies by which [[monarch|ruler]]s in [[eve]]ry [[worship|cult]]ure have claimed [[divinity|divine]] sanction.

## Connection to "Common Sense"

Holbach references David in the context of his exami[[nations|nation]] of biblical prophecy and the messianic claims of [[christianity|Christianity]]. His treatment is characteristically deflationary: David is not discussed as a historical figure in his own right but as a link in the chain of [[prophecy|prophetic]] claims that Holbach is dismantling.

The logic of Holbach's approach is straightforward. [[christianity|Christianity]] claims that [[jesus-christ|Jesus]] is the promised [[jesus-christ|Messiah]]. This claim depends on prophecies made to and about David in the Hebrew [[bible|Bible]]. If those prophecies can be shown to be ambiguous, retroactively applied, or based on unreliable texts, then the messianic claim collapses. David, as the anchor of messianic prophecy, is thus implicated in Holbach's broader critique of [[revelation|revealed religion]].

On the genealogical problem:

> "They contradict each other in regard to the time and manner in which the apostles followed Him."
>
> (002-489)

While this passage specifically addresses the calling of the apostles, Holbach applies the same genealogical [[doubt|skepticism]] to the Davidic lineage. Two incompatible genealogies cannot both be true; if neither can be verified independently, the claim of Davidic descent reduces to an article of [[faith|faith]] rather than a fact of history.

## Key Ideas Attributed / Critiqued

**1. Davidic messianism as retroactive prophecy.** Holbach's general critique of prophecy applies with particular force to the Davidic promises. The Hebrew [[bible|Bible]]'s references to a future Davidic [[monarch|king]] are, on a naturalistic reading, political hopes for the restoration of the [[monarch|monarchy]] after the [[babylon|Babylonia]]n exile. The [[christianity|Christian]] reinterpretation of these texts as [[prophecy|prediction]]s of [[jesus-christ|Jesus]] is, in Holbach's framework, a case study in how ambiguous ancient texts are made to say whatever later interpreters need them to say.

**2. Genealogy as theological argument.** Both [[matthew|Matthew]] and [[luke|Luke]] trace [[jesus-christ|Jesus]]'s lineage to David, but through entirely different lines ([[solomon|Solomon]] vs. Nathan) and with different names. Holbach sees in this contradiction not a minor discrepancy but a fatal flaw: if the central historical claim about [[jesus-christ|Jesus]] — his Davidic descent — cannot be established from the sources, then the theological superstructure built upon it has no foundation.

**3. [[sacred|Sacred]] [[monarch|king]]ship as political ideology.** The biblical portrayal of David as [[god|God]]'s anointed serves, on Holbach's reading, the same function as royal ideology in [[eve]]ry ancient Near Eastern [[worship|cult]]ure: it legitimizes a political dynasty by claiming [[divinity|divine]] sanction. David is not uniquely "chosen"; he is one instance of a universal [[model|pattern]] of [[monarch|ruler]]s claiming [[god|God]]'s favor.

## Legacy

David's [[worship|cult]]ural legacy is vast and extends far beyond [[religion|religion]]. Michelangelo's *David* (1504) reimagined the biblical [[monarch|king]] as a Renaissance [[model|ideal]] of human potential. The David story has been retold in literature from the medieval [[secrecy|mystery]] plays to [[joseph|Joseph]] [[hell|Hell]]er's *[[god|God]] Knows* (1984). The Psalms attributed to David remain among the most widely read and translated texts in [[universe|world]] literature.

In historical scholarship, the existence and extent of David's [[monarch|king]]dom have been subjects of intense debate [[guilt|sin]]ce the 1990s, with minimalist scholars arguing that David was at most a local chieftain and maximalists defending the biblical portrayal of a substantial United [[monarch|Monarch]]y. The Tel Dan stele (9th century BCE), which refers to the "House of David," provides the only extrabiblical archaeological [[proof|evidence]] for a Davidic dynasty.
