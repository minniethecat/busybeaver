# Pythagoras

## Definition and Context

**Pythagoras** (c. 570–495 BCE) was an ancient Greek philosopher and mathematician, the founder of the Pythagorean school. In Holbach's *Common Sense*, Pythagoras appears in two argumentative contexts, both critical: he serves as an example of how discipleship fosters uncritical acceptance of authority, and he is grouped with Plato as a thinker who acquired his ideas through priestly initiation rather than through independent reason. Holbach's references to Pythagoras are not biographical but polemical — they advance his broader critique of religious and philosophical authority. (002-274)(002-435)

## In Holbach's Argument

Holbach invokes Pythagoras in two distinct passages:

**The Disciples' Unquestioning Faith.** In Chapter CXXXVIII, "Faith Takes Root but in Weak, Ignorant, or Indolent Minds," Holbach writes: "The disciples of Pythagoras had an implicit faith in their Master's doctrine: 'HE HAS SAID IT!' was for them the solution of all problems. The majority of men act with as little reason. A curate, a priest, an ignorant monk, will become in the matter of religion the master of one's thoughts." (138-001) The analogy is direct: just as the Pythagoreans substituted their master's authority for their own judgment, so too do religious believers defer to priests rather than think for themselves. Faith, Holbach argues, "relieves the weakness of the human mind, for whom application is commonly a very painful work." (138-001)

**Philosophy's Debt to Priestcraft.** In a broader passage on the history of human knowledge, Holbach argues that ancient philosophy was not an independent rational enterprise but a derivative of priestly traditions: "If the ministers of the Gods sometimes made useful discoveries, they always took care to hide them in enigmas and to envelope them in shadows of mystery. The Pythagorases and the Platos, in order to acquire some futile attainments, were obliged to crawl to the feet of the priests, to become initiated into their mysteries, to submit to the tests which they desired to impose upon them." (199-002) For Holbach, this genealogy undermines the authority of both ancient philosophy and the theological traditions that inherited its methods. Philosophy "instead of enlightening the mind, it blinded it, and turned it away from useful objects." (199-002)

## Significance

Holbach's use of Pythagoras illustrates his historical method: he traces religious and philosophical ideas back to their origins not to celebrate them but to expose their human, all-too-human sources. By showing that even the revered figures of antiquity — Pythagoras, Plato — were indebted to priestly mystification, Holbach undermines the claim that religion or philosophy possesses a transcendent, divine origin. The Pythagorean motto *autos epha* ("he himself said it") becomes, in Holbach's hands, the emblem of all uncritical submission to authority, whether philosophical or religious.


## Selected Passage

Holbach uses the Pythagorean motto as the emblem of all uncritical submission to authority:

> The disciples of Pythagoras had an implicit faith in their Master's doctrine: "HE HAS SAID IT!" was for them the solution of all problems. The majority of men act with as little reason. A curate, a priest, an ignorant monk, will become in the matter of religion the master of one's thoughts. Faith relieves the weakness of the human mind, for whom application is commonly a very painful work; it is much easier to rely upon others than to examine for one's self; examination being slow and difficult, it is usually unpleasant to ignorant and stupid minds as well as to very ardent ones; this is, no doubt, why faith finds so many partisans.
>
> (002-274)
