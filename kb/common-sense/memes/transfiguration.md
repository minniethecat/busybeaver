# Transfiguration of Jesus

## Overview

The Transfiguration is a [[gospel|Gospel]] [[eve]]nt in which Jesus is transfigured on a mountain before [[peter|Peter]], James, and John, his face shining and his clothes becoming dazzling white, while [[moses|Moses]] and Elijah appear beside him ([[matthew|Matthew]] 17:1–8, [[mark|Mark]] 9:2–8, [[luke|Luke]] 9:28–36). In [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian [[religion|theology]], the [[eve]]nt prefigures the resurrection and confirms Jesus's [[divinity|divine]] identity. For Holbach, it serves a very different purpose: the Transfiguration's placement in [[gospel|the Gospel]] chronology is [[proof|evidence]] of the texts' [[contradiction|incoherence]] and human fabrication.

## Connection to "Common Sense"

Holbach seizes on the Transfiguration not for its theological significance but for what its timing reveals about [[gospel|the Gospel]] narratives as historical documents. In a sustained critique of gospel chronology, he notes that the [[missionaries|evangelists]] compress Jesus's entire public ministry — his preaching, [[miracle|miracles]], passion, and death — into an impossibly short timeframe:

> "The Evangelists speak of no other year following, and it appears even by the narration of His acts that He performed them consecutively immediately after His baptism, and in a very short time, during which we see but an interval of six days before his Transfiguration; during these six days we do not see that He did anything." (Chapter 2, "On the Gospels")

Holbach's arithmetic is devastating: subtract forty days in the [[merit|desert]] from the three months after baptism, and Jesus's public ministry lasted "but about six weeks." For a figure whose [[doctrine|teaching]]s were supposedly transforming [[earth|the world]], this compressed timeline is, Holbach implies, historically [[absurdity|absurd]]. The Transfiguration thus becomes not a moment of [[divinity|divine]] glory but a timestamp exposing the [[missionaries|evangelists]]' indifference to chronological coherence — and by extension, their unreliability as historical witnesses.

## Significance in Holbach's Critique

The Transfiguration functions in Holbach's argument as a specific instance of a general principle: [[gospel|the Gospel]] writers did not intend to produce consistent history, and the seams in their narratives — chronological, geographical, and doctrinal — are visible to any reader who approaches them without the [[pride|presumption]] of [[divinity|divine]] inspiration. The six-day gap before the Transfiguration is one such seam.
