# Revelation

## Definition

Revelation, in Holbach's usage, is the claim that [[god|divine being]]s have communicated truths to humanity through chosen intermediaries — [[prophecy|prophet]]s, apostles, [[saint|saints]] — whose pronouncements must thereafter be accepted as infallible. Holbach treats revelation as an epistemological scandal: it demands belief without [[proof|evidence]], on the authority of messengers whose credentials cannot be independently verified. Every revealed religion claims exclusive access to [[divinity|divine]] truth, yet "[[eve]]ry new revelation ought to be considered false; [[eve]]ry change in a revealed religion indicates that [[god|God]] has not revealed Himself, or that He has deceived men" (ch. CXXVII). The mutual contradiction of [[dreams|revelations]] is Holbach's primary argument against the very concept.

## Historical Context

The status of revelation was the central theological-epistemological question of the Enlightenment. The Deist solution — [[god|God]] revealed Himself through [[universe|nature]] and reason, not through particular historical [[eve]]nts or texts — was an attempt to retain [[divinity|divine]] authorship of [[nature|the universe]] while abandoning the scandal of particularity. Lessing's "ugly broad ditch" (*der garstige breite Graben*, 1777) articulated the core problem: [[chance|accident]]al truths of history can never become the [[evidence|proof]] of necessary truths of reason. Holbach goes further than the Deists: he denies not merely the reliability of particular [[dreams|revelations]] but the coherence of the concept itself. A [[god|God]] who reveals Himself to some and not others is not a universal father but a capricious tribal [[god|deity]].

## Core Arguments

**1. Revelations contradict each other.** Holbach catalogues the mutual exclusions of revealed religions: "A [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian thinks that the Koran, the [[theology-index|Divine]] revelation announced by [[mohammed|Mohammed]], is but a tissue of impertinent dreams ... The [[mohammed|Mohammed]]an, on his side, treats the [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian as an idolater and a dog" (ch. II). If divine revelation were a real phenomenon, its content would be consistent across time and [[worship|cult]]ure. The actual record — contradictory, [[worship|cult]]urally specific, mutually hostile — points to human authorship.

**2. A just [[god|God]] would not reveal Himself partially.** Holbach argues that the selectivity of revelation is incompatible with [[justice|divine justice]]: "Does not [[eve]]ry special revelation announce an unjust, partial, and malicious [[god|God]]? If He condescends to show Himself to some men, He takes care to keep all the others in invincible ignorance of His [[divinity|divine]] intentions" (ch. CXXIV). A [[god|God]] who provides salvific knowledge to a small minority while condemning the vast majority to ignorance is not benevolent.

**3. Revelation changes over time — and change implies imperfection.** "Every new revelation ought to be considered false; [[eve]]ry change in a revealed religion indicates that [[god|God]] has not revealed Himself, or that He has deceived men" (ch. CXXVII). An omniscient [[god|God]] would get it right the first time. The historical record of progressive revelation — Judaism, [[christianity|Christianity]], [[islam|Islam]] — each claiming to supersede and correct the previous — is [[proof|evidence]] of human theological evolution, not [[divinity|divine]] communication.

**4. Revelation is a tool of political ambition.** The individuals who claim to receive [[dreams|revelations]] are invariably those who stand to gain power from them. Holbach treats the [[prophecy|prophet]] not as a mystic but as an entrepreneur: the revelation is the product, and the [[religion|faith]]ful are the market.

**5. Natural reason makes revelation unnecessary.** If [[god|God]] exists and [[desire|want]]s humans to know the truth, He has already provided the means: [[reason|human reason]] and the observable natural [[universe|world]]. Special revelation is not merely unreliable — it is redundant.

## Reception and Critique

Holbach's total rejection of revelation placed him at the [[atheism|atheist]]ic extreme of the Enlightenment spectrum. Deists like Thomas [[pain|Pain]]e (*The [[enlightenment|Age of Reason]]*, 1794) shared his critique of particular [[dreams|revelations]] while maintaining that [[universe|nature]] itself was a universal divine revelation. Kant's *[[religion|Religion]] within the Boundaries of Mere [[reason|Reason]]* (1793) attempted to translate revealed doctrines into moral allegories, preserving their ethical content while abandoning their historical claims. In the twentieth century, Karl Barth's neo-orthodoxy would reassert the centrality of revelation against liberal [[religion|theology]], arguing that the scandal of particularity was precisely the point — [[god|God]]'s [[liberty|freedom]] to reveal Himself as He chooses.

## Relation to Other Concepts

Revelation is the claimed source of [[Dogma]] — [[eve]]ry [[doctrine|dogma]] is ultimately justified by an appeal to revelation. It is the epistemological foundation of [[Faith]], which is defined as belief in revealed truths without independent verification. Revelation is opposed to [[Reason]], which demands [[proof|evidence]] accessible to all rather than testimony restricted to a few. It produces [[Superstition]] when the content of alleged [[dreams|revelations]] generates fear and irrational practice. Holbach sees revelation as serving the interests of [[Authority]] — the priestly class that claims exclusive access to [[divinity|divine]] communication.

## In the Text

On the mutual cancellation of [[dreams|revelations]]:

> "A Christian thinks that the Koran, the Divine revelation announced by Mohammed, is but a tissue of impertinent dreams and impostures injurious to Divinity. The Mohammedan, on his side, treats the Christian as an idolater and a dog." (ch. II)

On the injustice of partial revelation:

> "Does not every special revelation announce an unjust, partial, and malicious God? If He condescends to show Himself to some men, He takes care to keep all the others in invincible ignorance of His divine intentions." (ch. CXXIV)

On the self-refuting [[universe|nature]] of progressive revelation:

> "If, in the economy of all Divine revelations, I am unable to recognize either the wisdom, the goodness, or the equity of a God ... every new revelation ought to be considered false; every change in a revealed religion indicates that God has not revealed Himself, or that He has deceived men." (ch. CXXVI–CXXVII)
