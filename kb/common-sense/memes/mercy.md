# Mercy

## Definition

[[theology-index|Divine]] mercy, in Holbach's analysis, is a concept that collapses under its own weight.
The theological [[god|God]] is said to be merciful — to forgive [[vice|sin]]s, to temper justice with compassion, to open "the treasures of [[theology-index|Divine]] mercy" to the repentant [[vice|sin]]ner (ch. 69).
Yet this same [[god|God]] "will punish without mercy all those who have the temerity to doubt His existence, or His [[attribute|attributes]], or His justice, or His goodness" (ch. 36).
The contradiction is not superficial but foundational: a [[existence|being]] who punishes without mercy those He created with the capacity to doubt cannot be called merciful in any sense recognizable to human morality.

Holbach's critique proceeds by exposing the arbitrariness of the doctrine.
Mercy is dispensed not according to any principle of [[merit|desert]] but according to inscrutable decrees.
"[[god|God]] will damn without mercy all those to whom He has not given the grace to know Him and to beli[[eve]] in Him" (ch. 78).
The "hour of mercy" passes for some and not for others — and no one can know in advance whether they are among the elect or the reprobate.
This is not mercy; it is caprice dressed in theological language.

The political dimension is inescapable: by convincing men that mercy is a [[divinity|divine]] attribute administered by [[clergy|priests]], [[clergy|the clergy]] secure for themselves the power to dispense or withhold it.
"The treasures of [[theology-index|Divine]] mercy reassure the greatest criminals, who have reason to hope that [[eve]]n the most atrocious [[vice|sin]]s will be effaced by the tears of repentance" (ch. 160).
Mercy is a commodity, and [[clergy|the clergy]] are its brokers.

## Historical Context

The doctrine of divine mercy was central to eighteenth-century Catholic practice.
The sacrament of [[confession|penance]] — confession, contrition, absolution — was the ordinary mechanism through which mercy was dispensed.
The Jubilee indulgence, the [[prayer|prayers]] for the dead in purgatory, and the intercession of [[saint|saints]] all presupposed a [[god|God]] whose mercy could be activated through priestly mediation.

The [[reformation|Protestant Reformation]] had contested the clerical monopoly on mercy — Luther's doctrine of justification by [[religion|faith]] alone removed the priest from the transaction — but had preserved the underlying concept of a merciful God whose grace is freely given to the elect.
Holbach's critique is more radical than Luther's: he denies not the mode of access to mercy but the coherence of the concept itself.

The philosophical context included [[pierre-bayle|Bayle]]'s reflections on [[evil|the problem of evil]] and [[gottfried-leibniz|Leibniz]]'s *Theodicy* (1710), which attempted to reconcile divine mercy with the existence of [[evil|suffering]].
Holbach follows [[pierre-bayle|Bayle]] in arguing that the reconciliation fails: a [[god|God]] who is both merciful and omnipotent would not permit the [[injustice|evil]]s that actually exist.

## Core Arguments

**1. [[theology-index|Divine]] mercy is arbitrary, not principled.**
"Conversion and the hour of mercy is passed; from which it follows, that those who live in invincible ignorance would be punished for not [[existence|being]] converted" (ch. 69).
Mercy is dispensed or withheld according to no discernible rule — it is favoritism, not justice.

**2. Mercy contradicts the observable facts of human [[evil|suffering]].**
If [[god|God]] is merciful, why does He permit His representatives on earth to [[torture|torment]] their subjects?
Holbach points to the "licentious, absolute, and perverse" [[monarch|prince]]s who govern without justice while claiming [[divinity|divine]] sanction (ch. 145).

**3. Merciful repentance is a license to [[vice|sin]].**
"The treasures of [[theology-index|Divine]] mercy reassure the greatest criminals, who have reason to hope that [[eve]]n the most atrocious [[vice|sin]]s will be effaced by the tears of repentance" (ch. 160).
The doctrine of mercy undermines morality by providing an escape hatch from the consequences of [[crime|wrongdoing]].

**4. Mercy is a priestly revenue stream.**
"By imploring [[theology-index|Divine]] mercy; their con[[knowledge|science]] rests in peace when a priest assures them that [[heaven|Heaven]] is quieted by [[vice|sin]]cere repentance, which is very useless to [[earth|the world]]; this priest consoles them in the name of [[deity|Deity]], if they consent in reparation of their faults to divide with His ministers the fruits of their plunderings" (ch. 170).
Mercy is sold, not given.

**5. True mercy is secular.**
Holbach implies that genuine mercy — compassion for [[evil|suffering]], [[absolution|forgiveness]] of wrongs — belongs to the domain of human relations, not to the imaginary transactions between guilty mortals and an invisible judge.
By relocating mercy to [[hope|heaven]], religion has impoverished it on earth.

## Reception and Critique

Holbach's critique of divine mercy was taken up by later anticlerical writers, particularly in the French tradition.
Anatole [[paris|France]]'s satires of ecclesiastical venality echo Holbach's charge that mercy is a commodity sold by [[clergy|priests]].

Theological defenders have argued that Holbach misunderstands the doctrine: divine mercy, in orthodox [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian [[religion|theology]], is not arbitrary but flows from [[god|God]]'s [[universe|nature]] as love, and is always available to the [[vice|sin]]cerely repentant.
The conditions of access — [[religion|faith]], repentance, the [[sacrament|sacraments]] — are not arbitrary restrictions but the means by which mercy is received.

The deeper challenge Holbach poses concerns the relationship between mercy and power.
If mercy is the prerogative of a [[monarch|sovereign]] who can choose to punish or forgive, then the merciful God is structurally identical to the [[sovereign|absolute monarch]] — and the critique of [[tyranny|absolutism]] extends to the celestial throne.

## Relation to Other Concepts

Mercy is the alleged counterweight to [[divinity|divine]] [[justice]]: where justice demands punishment, mercy grants [[absolution|forgiveness]].
But the balance is incoherent — a perfectly just [[existence|being]] would not need mercy, and a merciful [[existence|being]] who punishes without mercy is a contradiction.

Mercy is administered by the [[priest]], who alone can declare [[vice|sin]]s forgiven and open access to [[divinity|divine]] pardon.
It is closely tied to [[confession]], the institutional mechanism through which mercy is dispensed.

The concept of [[punishment]] is mercy's shadow: those to whom mercy is not extended face eternal [[torture|torment]].
Mercy and [[hell]] are two sides of the same theological coin — the promise of one implies the threat of the other.

Mercy is opposed to [[reason]], which demands that moral concepts have determinate content and consistent application.
