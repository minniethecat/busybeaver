# Almighty

## Definition

"Almighty" designates the attribute of unlimited power that [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian [[religion|theology]] assigns to [[god]]. In Holbach's analysis, this attribute stands in direct contradiction to [[eve]]ry observable fact about [[earth|the world]].
An almighty [[existence|being]] who [[passion|desire]]s human happiness would simply make humans happy — "does it cost Him any more to say, 'Let [[eve]]rything remain in order! — let all my subjects be good, innocent, fortunate!' than to say, 'Let them be wicked, [[sin|sinful]], and miserable!'?" (061-001) The question requires no theological sophistication to ask and no theological answer can satisfy it.

Holbach's treatment of [[divinity|divine]] omnipotence is structured as a dilemma: either [[god|God]] is almighty and does not [[passion|desire]] human welfare (in which case he is malevolent), or he [[passion|desire]]s human welfare but cannot achi[[eve]] it (in which case he is not almighty). The theologian who claims both omnipotence and [[charity|benevolence]] is asserting a [[contradiction|logical contradiction]]. The "[[secrecy|mystery]]" to which [[religion|theology]] retreats is not an expla[[nations|nation]] but an evasion.

## Historical Context

The problem of reconciling [[divinity|divine]] omnipotence with the existence of [[evil]] — the [[injustice|theodicy]] problem — had been central to [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian philosophy [[guilt|sin]]ce [[augustine]] and received its classic formulation in [[gottfried-leibniz|Leibniz]]'s *Theodicy* (1710), which argued that this is "the best of all possible [[universe|world]]s." The [[lisbon|Lisbon]] earthquake of 1755 shattered the optimism of [[gottfried-leibniz|Leibniz]]'s solution for an entire generation of [[europe|Europe]]an thinkers. [[voltaire]]'s *Candide* (1759) mercilessly satirized the claim that observable catastrophes are compatible with an almighty and benevolent [[god|God]].

Holbach radicalized this critique. Where [[voltaire]] mocked [[injustice|theodicy]] while retaining deistic belief, Holbach argued that the concept of omnipotence itself was incoherent. An almighty [[existence|being]] who creates imperfect creatures and then punishes them for their imperfection is not a [[god|God]] but a tyrant. If [[god|the creator]] is almighty, all defects in creation are defects of [[god|the creator]]'s will or capacity — and neither option is compatible with the theological claim of [[perfection|divine perfection]].

## Core Arguments

**1. [[omnipotence|Omnipotence]] is incompatible with observable imperfection.** "Man, regarded as the masterpiece, as the most marvelous work of [[divinity|Divinity]], is full of imperfections which render him disagreeable in the eyes of the Almighty workman who has formed him. (068-001)" If the workman is almighty, why does his masterpiece displease him? The only answers — that [[god|God]] could not make man better (denying omnipotence) or would not (denying [[charity|benevolence]]) — are both fatal to [[religion|theology]].

**2. An almighty [[god|God]] could achi[[eve]] his ends without [[evil|suffering]].** "If [[god|God]] is Almighty, does it cost Him any more to say, 'Let [[eve]]rything remain in order! — let all my subjects be good, innocent, fortunate!' than to say, 'Let them be wicked, [[sin|sinful]], and miserable!'?" The [[evil|suffering]] that [[religion|theology]] interprets as [[punishment|chastisement]] is unnecessary for an omnipotent [[existence|being]], who could achi[[eve]] moral order by fiat rather than by [[torture|torment]].

**3. The failure of [[christianity|Christianity]] refutes the claim of [[divinity|divine]] omnipotence.** "Their [[god|God]], [[jesus-christ|Jesus Christ]], preached without fruit, without success; His Almighty will still finds in men a resistance, over which this [[god|God]] either can not or does not wish to triumph." (164-002) If Jesus was [[god|God]] incarnate with almighty power, why did his [[doctrine|teaching]] fail to transform humanity? Either the power is not almighty, or the claim of [[divinity|divine]] identity is false.

**4. The appeal to "Almighty power" is a placeholder for ignorance.** When asked how an immaterial [[soul|spirit]] can act upon matter, theologians reply "that it is a [[secrecy|mystery]] to them; that this combi[[nations|nation]] is the effect of the Almighty power." "Almighty power" is invoked precisely where rational expla[[nations|nation]] fails — it is not a solution but a verbal gesture that marks the boundary of theological knowledge.

**5. [[revelation|Revelation]] through [[evil|suffering]] is incompatible with omnipotence.** An almighty [[god|God]] [[desire|wishing]] to found a religion "would not permit that men charged with revealing His will should be misused." The [[martyr|martyrdom]] of the apostles and the [[cruelty|persecution]] of the early [[church|Church]] are [[proof|evidence]] not of [[divinity|divine]] truth but of [[divinity|divine]] absence: an omnipotent [[existence|being]] would protect his messengers, or find a means of communication that did not require their torture and death.

## Reception and Critique

Holbach's critique of [[divinity|divine]] omnipotence stands within a philosophical tradition reaching back to antiquity. The earliest and most enduring formulation of the problem is [[epicurus|Epicurus]]'s paradox, preserved by Lactantius: "If [[god|God]] is willing to prevent [[injustice|evil]] but not able, then he is not omnipotent. If he is able but not willing, then he is malevolent.
If he is both able and willing, then whence comes [[injustice|evil]]?" Holbach does not merely repeat this ancient dilemma — he radicalizes it. For [[epicurus|Epicurus]], the paradox was a reason to doubt the gods' involvement in human affairs; for Holbach, it is a reason to deny that the concept of divine power has any coherent meaning at all.
An "almighty" [[existence|being]] who cannot be detected in [[earth|the world]], whose interventions are indistinguishable from natural causes, and whose grand projects (creation, revelation, [[salvation|redemption]]) all fail by any empirical measure, is not a [[existence|being]] whose existence is in question — it is a word without a referent.

Holbach's argument that [[divinity|divine]] omnipotence is refuted by the failure of [[christianity|Christianity]] was particularly provocative. The claim that Jesus "preached without fruit, without success" and that his "Almighty will still finds in men a resistance" was not merely a theological criticism but a direct challenge to the historical narrative on which [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian identity rested.
If the incar[[nations|nation]] of almighty power could not convert [[earth|the world]] in two millennia, what reason was there to beli[[eve]] that the power in question was almighty, or indeed [[divinity|divine]] at all? Contemporary critics responded that Holbach confused power with compulsion — that an omnipotent [[god|God]] might choose to permit free resistance rather than compel obedience.
But Holbach's reply was already built into his argument: an almighty [[existence|being]] who "chooses" to permit [[evil|suffering]] and failure that he could prevent is not respecting [[liberty|freedom]] but inflicting misery, and the moral character of such a [[existence|being]] is indistinguishable from indifference or cruelty.

Later philosophical engagement with the problem of omnipotence has both strengthened and complicated Holbach's position. The logical [[problem-of-evil|problem of evil]], refined by J.L.
Mackie and others in the twentieth century, formalized the contradiction Holbach identified between omnipotence, omni[[charity|benevolence]], and the existence of [[injustice|evil]] — and many philosophers now regard this as a successful dis[[evidence|proof]] of the existence of a [[god|God]] with all three [[attribute|attributes]].
At the same time, the concept of omnipotence has been subjected to conceptual analysis that Holbach did not attempt: can an omnipotent [[existence|being]] create a stone so heavy he cannot lift it? Can he change the past? Can he make 2 + 2 equal 5?
These paradoxes suggest that "omnipotence" may be logically incoherent quite apart from any [[evidence|empirical evidence]] — a conclusion that would, if accepted, vindicate Holbach's claim that the term is not a genuine concept but a placeholder for an unexamined cluster of incompatible assertions.

## Relation to Other Concepts

The concept of omnipotence is the claimed attribute around which Holbach's entire critique of divine power is organized. [[god]] is defined by [[religion|theology]] as an almighty [[existence|being]]; if the attribute of omnipotence cannot be coherently ascribed, the concept of [[god|God]] collapses.
The comparison with [[jupiter]] is central to Holbach's argument: the material [[jupiter|Jupiter]] of the ancients at least had a kind of power one could imagine — he could move, build, destroy, and propagate [[existence|being]]s — whereas the "almighty" [[god|God]] of modern [[religion|theology]] is a pure [[soul|spirit]] whose alleged power has no mechanism and produces no detectable effects.
The pagan god could do something; the [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian [[god|God]], stripped of material [[attribute|attributes]], can do nothing.

The connection to [[providence]] is equally important. [[providence|Providence]] is the claim that [[god|God]]'s almighty power is exercised in the [[government|governance]] of [[earth|the world]] — that [[eve]]nts unfold according to a [[divinity|divine]] plan.
Holbach's critique of omnipotence undermines providence at its foundation: if the power attributed to [[god|God]] cannot be shown to produce any effect distinguishable from natural [[cause|causation]], then talk of "[[divinity|divine]] [[government|governance]]" is empty.
The existence of [[evil]] — physical [[evil|suffering]], moral corruption, natural catastrophe — is the empirical refutation of both divine power and [[goodness|divine goodness]] simultaneously. [[prayer]] is the practice by which believers attempt to activate divine power through petition; if the [[existence|being]] addressed is either impotent or indifferent, prayer is a futile gesture.
The entire enterprise of [[theodicy]] — the attempt to reconcile omnipotence with the existence of [[injustice|evil]] — is, on Holbach's analysis, an intellectual [[charlatan|fraud]] designed to preserve a concept that observation has already falsified.

## In the Text

On the [[contradiction|logical contradiction]] between omnipotence and human [[evil|suffering]]:

> "If God is Almighty, does it cost Him any more to say, 'Let everything remain in order!' — 'let all my subjects be good, innocent, fortunate!' — than to say, 'Let them be wicked, sinful, and miserable!'?" (ch. CXXXII)

On the imperfection of the Almighty's workmanship:

> "Man, regarded as the masterpiece, as the most marvelous work of Divinity, is full of imperfections which render him disagreeable in the eyes of the Almighty workman who has formed him; this surprising work becomes often so revolting and so odious to its Author, that He feels Himself compelled to throw it in the fire." (ch. CXXXII)

On the failure of [[jesus-christ|Christ]]'s almighty will:

> "Their God, Jesus Christ, preached without fruit, without success; His Almighty will still finds in men a resistance, over which this God either can not or does not wish to triumph." (ch. CLXIV)

On "Almighty power" as a placeholder for ignorance:

> "They will tell you that they know nothing about it; that it is a mystery to them; that this combination is the effect of the Almighty power." (ch. CIV)
