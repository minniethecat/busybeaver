# Dreams

## Definition

In Holbach's materialist epistemology, dreams — and the wa[[monarch|king]] "visions" that resemble them — are natural products of the human brain, not channels of [[divinity|divine]] communication. The claim that a religious founder received revelation through dreams or ecstatic states is not [[proof|evidence]] of supernatural contact but of ordinary psychological processes misinterpreted by credulous minds.

Holbach uses "dreams" as a term of epistemological dismissal: religious doctrines are "dreams and conjectures which are altogether contradictory." The word groups together all claims to private revelation — visions, prophecies, ecstatic states, [[conscience|inner voice]]s — and reduces them to a [[guilt|sin]]gle mundane expla[[nations|nation]]: the brain generating content without external referent.

## Holbach's Analysis

**1. Dreams substitute for [[proof|evidence]].** In the absence of "intelligible and truly useful things," religion offers "dreams and conjectures" — mental content that cannot be verified, tested, or debated on rational grounds. The appeal to private revelation immunizes belief from criticism while providing no reason for anyone else to accept it.

**2. Mutual dismissal exposes the [[model|pattern]].** Holbach observes that adherents of different religions dismiss each other's revelations as dreams. A [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian "thinks that the Koran, the [[theology-index|Divine]] revelation announced by [[mohammed|Mohammed]], is but a tissue of impertinent dreams and impostures injurious to [[divinity|Divinity]]." The [[mohammed|Mohammed]]an "treats the [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian as an idolater and a dog." Each side correctly identifies the other's "revelation" as mere mental content — but fails to apply the same [[model|standard]] to its own.

**3. [[saint|Saint]]s and visionaries disturb society.** Holbach notes that the "saint has an unhappy, discontented, and often irritable, turbulent disposition; his zeal often obliges him, conscientiously, to disturb society by opinions or dreams which his vanity makes him accept as inspirations from [[heaven|Heaven]]." The psychological profile of the visionary is one of vanity and instability, not [[divinity|divine]] contact.

**4. All religions are filled with such accounts.** "The annals of all religions are filled with accounts of anxious, intractable, seditious [[saint|saints]], who have distinguished themselves" — the universality of vision claims across [[worship|cult]]ures is [[proof|evidence]] that they arise from a shared human psychology, not from unique [[divinity|divine]] interventions.
