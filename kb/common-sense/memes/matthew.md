# Matthew

## Biography

Matthew (also called Levi) is identified in [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian tradition as one of the twelve apostles of [[jesus-christ|Jesus]] and the author of the First [[gospel|Gospel]]. Before his calling, he was a tax collector (publican) in [[capernaum|Capernaum]] — a profession despised by first-century Jews as collaborationist and ritually unclean. The [[gospel|Gospel]] of Matthew, traditionally dated to around 80–85 CE, was written for a predominantly Jewish-[[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian audience and emphasizes [[jesus-christ|Jesus]] as the fulfillment of [[bible|Old Testament]] prophecy, the new [[moses|Moses]] who delivers a new law from a mountain (the Sermon on the Mount).

The [[gospel|Gospel]] of Matthew is the longest of the four canonical [[gospel|Gospel]]s and the most structured, organized around five major discourses that mirror the five books of the Torah. It contains some of the most influential passages in [[christianity|Christian]] tradition — the Beatitudes, the Lord's [[prayer|Prayer]], the Great Commission ("Go ye therefore and teach all [[nation|nations]]") — and the only canonical account of the visit of the Magi, the flight into [[egypt|Egypt]], and the massacre of the innocents.

## Role in Religious History

Matthew's [[gospel|Gospel]] has been, historically, the most widely read and liturgically used of the four [[gospel|Gospel]]s. Its structured [[doctrine|teaching]] blocks made it [[model|ideal]] for catechetical [[education|instruction]], and its emphasis on [[jesus-christ|Jesus]] as the fulfillment of Jewish prophecy made it the bridge text between the Old and [[bible|New Testament]]s. The early church father Papias (c. 100 CE) records that "Matthew compiled the sayings [of Jesus] in the Hebrew language," though [[gospel|the Gospel]] that survives is in Greek and appears to draw on [[mark|Mark]]'s [[gospel|Gospel]] as a source — leading most critical scholars to doubt that the apostle Matthew was its direct author.

The genealogy with which Matthew opens his [[gospel|Gospel]] — tracing [[jesus-christ|Jesus]]'s lineage from [[abraham|Abraham]] through [[david|David]] and [[solomon|Solomon]] to [[joseph|Joseph]] — is one of the most consequential passages in the [[bible|New Testament]], establishing [[jesus-christ|Jesus]]'s [[david|Davidic]] credentials. It is also, for Holbach, a key exhibit in the case against biblical reliability.

## Connection to "Common Sense"

Holbach references Matthew repeatedly in his catalogue of biblical contradictions, using the First [[gospel|Gospel]] as a primary source for his [[proof|demonstration]] that the [[bible|New Testament]] documents cannot be reconciled with each other. Matthew is not attacked directly — Holbach's target is the claim of [[revelation|divine inspiration]], not the individual evangelist — but Matthew's text provides some of the most stri[[monarch|king]] examples of the problem.

**1. The contradictory genealogies.** Holbach's most pointed use of Matthew involves the genealogy:

> "The Evangelist Matthew claims that Jesus Christ descended from king David by his son Solomon through Joseph, reputed to be His father; and Luke claims that He is descended from the same David by his son Nathan through Joseph." (Chapter 2 / Appendix 1)

The contradiction is stark and irreconcilable: two different sons of [[david|David]] ([[solomon|Solomon]] and Nathan), two different lines of descent, both terminating in [[joseph|Joseph]]. If the genealogies are [[revelation|divinely inspired]], [[god|God]] cannot keep his own family records straight. If they are human constructions — competing attempts by different early [[christianity|Christian]] communities to establish [[jesus-christ|Jesus]]'s [[david|Davidic]] pedigree — their contradictions are exactly what one would expect from fallible human authors.

**2. The flight into [[egypt|Egypt]].** Matthew's in[[imagination|fancy]] narrative includes the visit of the Magi, [[herod|Herod]]'s massacre of the innocents, and the [[sacred|holy]] family's flight into [[egypt|Egypt]] — [[eve]]nts that appear nowhere in the other [[gospel|Gospel]]s. [[luke|Luke]]'s in[[imagination|fancy]] narrative (the census of Quirinius, the shepherds, the presentation in the [[temple|Temple]]) is entirely different and incompatible with Matthew's. Holbach does not dwell on this discrepancy in detail, but it forms part of his broader case: [[gospel|the Gospel]]s cannot [[eve]]n agree on the circumstances of [[jesus-christ|Jesus]]'s birth.

**3. The resurrection accounts.** Matthew reports two post-resurrection apparitions — one to [[mary|Mary]] Magdalene and another [[mary|Mary]], and one to the eleven disciples on a mountain in [[galilee|Galilee]]. This conflicts with [[luke|Luke]] (who places the [[phenomena|appearances]] in [[jerusalem|Jerusalem]]), with [[mark|Mark]], and with [[john-the-evangelist|John the Evangelist]] (who reports four apparitions in various locations). For Holbach, this is not a minor [[contradiction|inconsistency]] but a fatal one: the central miracle of [[christianity|Christianity]] is attested in mutually contradictory accounts.

## Key Ideas Attributed / Critiqued

**1. Genealogy as retrospective legitimation.** Matthew's genealogy is, for Holbach, a transparent attempt to establish [[jesus-christ|Jesus]]'s credentials by manufacturing a lineage. That a different lineage was manufactured by [[luke|Luke]]'s [[society|community]] simply reveals the mechanism: genealogy in the ancient [[universe|world]] was not historical record-keeping but political [[theology|theology]].

**2. [[prophecy|Prophecy]]-fulfillment as circular reasoning.** Matthew's characteristic formula — "this was done that it might be fulfilled which was spoken by the [[prophecy|prophet]]" — structures his entire [[gospel|Gospel]]. For Holbach, this is circular: [[gospel|the Gospel]] writer shapes the narrative to fit the prophecy, then cites the fit as [[proof|evidence]] of [[revelation|divine inspiration]]. The [[model|pattern]] is transparent once noticed, yet generations of believers have accepted it as [[evidence|proof]].

**3. [[contradiction|Contradiction]] as the mark of human authorship.** Matthew's contradictions with [[luke|Luke]] and [[john-the-evangelist|John the Evangelist]] are not problems to be solved by harmonization but [[proof|evidence]] to be acknowledged. They [[demon|demons]]trate that [[gospel|the Gospel]]s are human documents, produced by different communities with different theological agendas — and that the doctrine of [[revelation|inerrancy]] is indefensible.

## Legacy

Matthew's [[gospel|Gospel]] has shaped Western civilization more profoundly than almost any other [[guilt|sin]]gle text. The Sermon on the Mount — "Blessed are the poor in [[soul|spirit]]," "Turn the other cheek," "[[love|Love]] your enemies" — has provided moral vocabulary for two millennia of ethical reflection, from Augustine to Tolstoy to Gandhi to Martin Luther King Jr. The parable of the sheep and the goats ("inasmuch as ye have done it unto one of the least of these my brethren, ye have done it unto me") has motivated countless works of [[charity|charity]] and social [[justice|justice]].

For Holbach, none of this moral content requires [[divinity|divine]] authorship. That Matthew's [[gospel|Gospel]] contains beautiful ethical [[doctrine|teaching]]s proves only that human [[existence|being]]s are capable of producing beautiful ethical [[doctrine|teaching]]s — a point Holbach is happy to concede. The claim he contests is not that the [[bible|Bible]] contains [[philosophy|wisdom]] but that its [[philosophy|wisdom]] is [[revelation|supernaturally guaranteed]].
