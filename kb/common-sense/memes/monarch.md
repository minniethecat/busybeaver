# Monarch

## Definition

The monarch, in Holbach's political analysis, occupies a pivotal position between two competing sources of authority: the [[people]] whose welfare he is supposed to serve, and the [[clergy]] who claim to speak for [[god]]. Holbach opens his central argument with an extended metaphor: "There is a vast empire governed by a monarch, whose conduct does but confound the minds of his subjects" (Ch. 13). This invisible monarch — who never shows himself, who communicates through contradictory intermediaries, and whose laws require endless interpretation — is, of course, the [[god|God]] of [[revelation|revealed religion]]. The metaphor works in both directions: just as an earthly monarch who behaved as [[god|God]] allegedly behaves would be considered a tyrant, so too the [[hope|heaven]]ly monarch, when examined rationally, reveals himself as a [[despotism|despot]] unworthy of worship.

## The Monarch and the Priest

Holbach's deepest political concern is the education of monarchs. From earliest childhood, the future ruler is placed in the hands of [[clergy|priests]] who "teach him to regard as the most important and the most sacred thing, its chimerical duties as the most [[sacred|holy]] obligations, [[persecution|intolerance]], and the [[soul|spirit]] of [[cruelty|persecution]], as the true foundations of his future authority" (Ch. 76). The priest does not seek to make the monarch wise or just — he seeks to make him a "turbulent fanatic" and a "tyrant," a ruler who will deploy state violence in service of theological ends. The result is the [[throne-and-altar]] alliance: the priest legitimizes the monarch's rule by declaring it [[divinity|divine]]ly ordained, and the monarch enforces the priest's orthodoxy with the sword of [[government|the state]].

## The Invisible Monarch as Theological Critique

The extended allegory of the invisible monarch (Ch. 13) is one of Holbach's most effective rhetorical devices. He describes a kingdom whose ruler never appears, whose edicts are delivered through quarreling ambassadors who contradict one another, whose laws are "enigmas, made not to be understood or [[divinity|divine]]d by the subjects for whose [[education|instruction]] they were intended" (Ch. 13), and whose punishment for disobedience is both infinite and invisible. Any reasonable person, Holbach suggests, would regard such a monarch as a fiction invented by the ambassadors for their own enrichment. The transparent application to the [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian [[god|God]] — whose "ambassadors" are the quarreling [[sect|sects]] of the [[clergy]] — needs no elaboration.

## Earthly vs. Heavenly Sovereignty

Holbach draws a systematic contrast between good earthly [[government|governance]] and the model of [[sovereign|divine sovereignty]]. A good human monarch is visible, accessible, consistent in his decrees, and proportionate in his punishments. The [[divinity|divine]] monarch, by contrast, is invisible, inaccessible, contradictory in his revealed commands, and infinite in his retribution. The implication is clear: if earthly [[government|sovereignty]] is judged by its effects on human welfare, [[sovereign|divine sovereignty]] fails [[eve]]ry test. "How can we attribute to an immutable [[god|God]], powerful enough to make all hearts as he pleased, the bizarre conduct of a monarch who does [[eve]]rything to be disobeyed?" (Ch. 29). The very concept of [[divinity|divine]] monarchy, Holbach argues, is a projection of human political arrangements onto the [[universe|cosmos]] — and a particularly bad political arrangement at that.

## The Monarch and Toleration

Holbach argues that a truly enlightened monarch would recognize religious diversity as politically harmless and [[persecution|religious persecution]] as politically destructive. The [[france]] of his own day, where Catholic and Protestant had slaughtered each other for centuries, [[demon|demons]]trated the f[[utility]] of attempting to impose religious uniformity by force. The monarch who serves his people rather than his [[clergy|priests]] would practice [[toleration]] — not as a grudging concession but as a principle of [[government|governance]] grounded in the recognition that "the laws of the invisible monarch need interpreters, but those who explain them are always quarreling among themselves" (Ch. 13).

## In the Text

On the invisible monarch who governs through contradictory intermediaries:

> "There is a vast empire governed by a monarch, whose conduct does but confound the minds of his subjects. He desires to be known, loved, respected, and obeyed, but he never shows himself; everything tends to make uncertain the notions which we are able to form about him."
>
> (001-001)(Ch. 13)

On the religious monarch as a public scourge:

> "A sincerely religious sovereign is generally a very dangerous chief for a State; credulity always indicates a narrow mind; devotion generally absorbs the attention which the prince ought to give to the ruling of his people. ... A religious bigot at the head of an empire, is one of the greatest scourges which Heaven in its fury could have sent upon earth." (Ch. 169)

On the education of princes by [[clergy|priests]], producing [[intolerance|fanaticism]] instead of [[philosophy|wisdom]]:

> "In almost all countries, priests and devout persons are charged with forming the mind and the heart of the young princes destined to govern the nations. ... They will hold up to their pupil superstition as the most important and the most sacred thing, its chimerical duties as the most holy obligations, intolerance, and the spirit of persecution, as the true foundations of his future authority; they will try to make him a chief of party, a turbulent fanatic, and a tyrant." (Ch. 169)

On the true definition of an enlightened sovereign, independent of religious claims:

> "An enlightened sovereign is he who understands his true interests; he knows they are united to those of his nation; he knows that a prince can be neither great, nor powerful, nor beloved, nor respected, so long as he will command but miserable slaves; he knows that equity, benevolence, and vigilance will give him more real rights over men than fabulous titles which claim to come from Heaven."
>
> (002-314)(Ch. 152)

## Reception and Critique

Holbach's treatment of monarchy participates in a broader Enlightenment discourse on [[government|sovereignty]] that extends from [[hobbes|Hobbes]]'s *[[matthew|Levi]]athan* to Rousseau's *Social Contract*. Unlike [[hobbes|Hobbes]], who saw [[sovereign|absolute monarch]]y as a rational solution to [[government|the state]] of [[universe|nature]], Holbach treats monarchy as a problem to be solved — a form of political organization that is easily corrupted by priestly influence. His allegory of the invisible monarch (Ch. 13) anticipates Feuerbach's projection theory of religion by half a century: the [[divinity|divine]] monarch is simply the earthly monarch writ large, a human political arrangement projected onto the [[universe|cosmos]] and then used to legitimize earthly [[despotism|tyranny]]. This analysis has been influential in the Marxist tradition, which adopted Holbach's critique of the throne-altar alliance as a model for [[knowledge|understanding]] ideology as a tool of class domi[[nations|nation]]. Critics, however, have noted that Holbach's analysis of monarchy assumes a sharp separation between political and [[authority|religious authority]] that did not exist in the *ancien régime* — the French monarch was himself a sacred figure, not merely the priest's client. Jonathan [[israel|Israel]]'s *Radical Enlightenment* (2001) positions Holbach's political writings as a bridge between [[baruch-spinoza|Spinoza]]'s critique of theocracy and the revolutionary republicanism of the 1790s.
