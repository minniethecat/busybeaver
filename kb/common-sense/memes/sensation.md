# Sensation

## Definition

Sensation is the cornerstone of Holbach's empiricist epistemology. In *Common Sense*, he argues that all ideas are "but pictures of objects which strike us" through the senses (ch. 4). There is no innate knowledge, no [[divinity|divine]]ly implanted ideas, and no faculty of pure reason independent of sensory input. "Every principle is a judgment; all judgment is the effect of experience; experience is not acquired but by the exercise of the senses" (ch. 4). This [[john-locke|Locke]]an-sensationalist position serves as the epistemological foundation for Holbach's entire materialist system: because all knowledge comes through the senses, any claim that cannot be traced to [[experience|sensory experience]] — including all theological claims about [[god|God]], the soul, and the [[death|afterlife]] — is cognitively empty.

Holbach's defence of sensation is simultaneously a defence of reason and experience against the encroachments of [[religion|faith]]. The [[clergy|priests]] of all religions, he observes, demand the same thing: "[[sacrifice|Sacrifice]] your reason; give up experience; distrust the testimony of your senses; submit without exami[[nations|nation]] to all that is given to you as coming from [[heaven|Heaven]]" (ch. 135). Holbach refuses this triple sacrifice, insisting that reason, experience, and the senses are "the only guides which [[[god|God]]] [[passion|desire]]d to give us" (ch. 136) — and that a benevolent [[god|deity]] would not have given us guides destined to deceive.

## Historical Context

Holbach's sensationalism inherits directly from [[john-locke|John Locke]]'s *Essay Concerning Human [[mind|Understanding]]* (1689), which had argued that the mind is a *tabula rasa* at birth and that all ideas derive from sensation and reflection. Condillac's *Traité des sensations* (1754) radicalised this position by eliminating reflection as an independent source of ideas, reducing all mental operations to transformed sensations. Holbach follows Condillac in this radical sensationalism.

The defence of the senses against theological [[skepticism|scepticism]] had particular urgency in eighteenth-century [[paris|France]], where Catholic apologists routinely invoked the fallibility of the senses to undermine [[certainty|confidence]] in natural reason and to carve out space for [[revelation|revealed truth]]. The argument that the senses deceive (optical illusions, dreams, madness) was a [[model|standard]] trope of apologetics, deployed to justify the submission of reason to [[authority|ecclesiastical authority]]. Holbach's counter-argument — that the senses correct their own errors through repeated application, and that to distrust the senses is to destroy the [[proof|evidence]] for revelation itself — is a deft inversion of the traditional apologetic.

## Core Arguments

1. **All ideas come from the senses.** "All our ideas are but pictures of objects which strike us" (ch. 4). The idea of [[god|God]], having no sensory [[model|prototype]], is "an idea without an object" — a chimera. "An idea without a [[model|prototype]], is it anything but a chimera?" (ch. 4).

2. **Religious principles are not innate.** Some theologians claim that the idea of [[god|God]] is innate, but "[[eve]]ry principle is a judgment; all judgment is the effect of experience; experience is not acquired but by the exercise of the senses: from which it follows that religious principles are drawn from nothing, and are not innate" (ch. 4).

3. **To distrust the senses is to destroy all knowledge.** "In warning me to doubt the testimony of my senses, you destroy for me the [[evidence|proof]]s of all religion. If men can be dupes of their imagi[[nations|nation]], if their senses are deceivers, why would you have me beli[[eve]] in the [[miracle|miracles]] which made an impression upon the deceiving senses of our ancestors?" (ch. 135). The apologetic argument from sensory fallibility is self-defeating.

4. **The senses can correct their own errors.** Holbach concedes that the senses sometimes deceive — the eye shows the sun as smaller than the earth — but "experience, which is only the repeated application of the senses, teaches me that objects continually diminish by reason of their distance; it is by these means that I reach the conclusion that the sun is much larger than the earth" (ch. 135). The senses contain their own corrective mechanism through cumulative experience.

5. **[[reason|Reason]], experience, and the senses are our only guides.** "To pretend that reason can deceive us, is to say that truth can be false, that [[utility|usefulness]] can be injurious" (ch. 136). If the senses are "[[religion|faith]]less guides," then [[god|God]] has left us without any means of knowing truth — an unjust arrangement incompatible with [[goodness|divine goodness]].

## Reception and Critique

Holbach's radical sensationalism was influential among the *Idéologues* of the [[revolution|Revolution]]ary period, particularly Destutt de Tracy and Cabanis, who developed materialist theories of mind based on sensory physiology. In the nineteenth century, the positivism of Auguste Comte would recapitulate the empiricist rejection of theological knowledge-claims, though without Holbach's militant anti-clerical rhetoric.

A recurring philosophical objection to radical sensationalism is that it cannot account for abstract concepts (mathematics, logic, morality) that do not seem to be "pictures of objects." Holbach, following Condillac, would reply that abstraction is itself a transformed sensation — but the adequacy of this reduction remains contested. Kant's critical philosophy, developed in the decade after Holbach's death, would argue that sensory input requires *a priori* categories of [[knowledge|understanding]] to become knowledge — a position that challenges the sensationalist claim that "experience is not acquired but by the exercise of the senses."

## Relation to Other Concepts

Sensation is the epistemological foundation for Holbach's entire system. It grounds [[Nature]], [[vice|sin]]ce knowledge of natural laws comes through sensory observation. It supports [[Reason]], which is not an independent faculty but "nothing else but the knowledge of the useful and the true" acquired through experience. It connects to [[Body]], [[vice|sin]]ce sensation is a function of material organs, not of an immaterial soul. It relates to [[Experience]], which is "only the repeated application of the senses." Finally, it underpins [[Law]], [[vice|sin]]ce moral obligation arises from the felt experience of pleasure and [[suffering|pain]] — the testimony of sensibility, not [[divinity|divine]] command.

## In the Text

> "All our ideas are but pictures of objects which strike us. What can the idea of God represent to us when it is evidently an idea without an object? Is not such an idea as impossible as an effect without a cause? An idea without a prototype, is it anything but a chimera?" (ch. 4)

> "Sacrifice your reason; give up experience; distrust the testimony of your senses; submit without examination to all that is given to you as coming from Heaven. This is the usual language of all the priests of the world; they do not agree upon any point, except in the necessity of never reasoning when they present principles to us which they claim as the most important to our happiness." (ch. 135)

> "I will not distrust my senses. I do not ignore the fact that they can sometimes lead me into error; but on the other hand, I know that they do not deceive me always. … In warning me to doubt the testimony of my senses, you destroy for me the proofs of all religion." (ch. 135)
