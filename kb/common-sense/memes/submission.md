# Submission

## Definition and Role in Holbach's Critique

In Holbach's *Common Sense*, submission is a central concept in his analysis of the psychological and social mechanisms of religion. Holbach identifies submission — particularly "blind submission" to religious authority — as both the demand that religion makes of believers and the condition that sustains priestly power. The concept appears in two interconnected forms: the intellectual submission that requires believers to accept doctrines without evidence, and the social submission that ensures obedience to ecclesiastical authority (002-265)(002-354).

## Blind Submission and the Rejection of Reason

For Holbach, religious faith is fundamentally an act of submission that is antithetical to reason. He defines faith, following the theologians' own account, as "consent without evidence," and observes that "religion exacts that we should firmly believe, without evidence, in propositions which are often improbable or opposed to reason" (002-265). The demand that believers renounce their rational faculties is, Holbach argues, an implicit acknowledgment that reason and faith are irreconcilable. The priests' imperative — "Sacrifice your reason; give up experience; distrust the testimony of your senses; submit without examination to all that is given to you as coming from Heaven" — is, he contends, the universal language of religious authority (002-265). This blind submission, Holbach concludes, elevates priestly authority above evidence itself and above the testimony of the senses.

## Submission as Social Control

Holbach extends his analysis of submission from the intellectual to the social domain. In a sweeping assessment of religious institutions, he argues that the dogmas, ceremonies, and moral teachings of all religions "are visibly calculated only to extend the power or to increase the emoluments of the founders and of the ministers of these religions" (002-354). Religious morality, in his account, is designed to produce a "submissive faith, which prevents reasoning" and a "devout humility, which assures to the priests the submission of their slaves" (002-354). The concept of submission thus reveals the functional role of religion: it is a system of social control that secures obedience by discouraging independent thought and cultivating habitual deference to ecclesiastical authority.

## The Psychology of Submission

Holbach also examines the psychological motivations that lead individuals to submit. He observes that "it is but with the hope of happiness that men submit to the empire of a God" (002-124), identifying the desire for future reward as the emotional lever that induces submission. Yet this hope, Holbach contends, is exploited: the God to whom men submit is characterized as "a despot to whom men are subjected but through fear, a master whom they can not interrogate, a totally inaccessible sovereign" who "can not merit the homage of intelligent beings" (002-124). The submission demanded by religion is thus doubly illegitimate — it rests on fear rather than reason, and it serves the interests of priests rather than the genuine welfare of believers.

> Faith, according to theologians, is consent without evidence. From this it follows that religion exacts that we should firmly believe, without evidence, in propositions which are often improbable or opposed to reason. But to challenge reason as a judge of faith, is it not acknowledging that reason can not agree with faith? As the ministers of religion have determined to banish reason, they must have felt the impossibility of reconciling reason with faith, which is visibly but a blind submission to those priests whose authority, in many minds, appears to be of a greater importance than evidence itself, and preferable to the testimony of the senses.
>
