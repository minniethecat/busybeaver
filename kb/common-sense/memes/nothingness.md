# Nothingness

## Definition

In Holbach's [[materialism|materialist philosophy]], the concept of "nothingness" serves a dual function. Negatively, it marks the limit of intelligibility: theological discourse about [[god|God]], the soul, and creation, when pressed, resolves into negations — statements about what [[god|God]] is *not* rather than what He *is*. Positively, the axiom that "nothing comes from nothing" (*ex nihilo nihil fit*) anchors Holbach's commitment to the [[eternal|eternity]] and self-sufficiency of matter: [[nature|the universe]] requires no creator because it has always existed.

The most radical application of the concept appears in Holbach's critique of [[attribute|divine attributes]]. After stripping away all anthropomorphic predicates — [[god|God]] is not corporeal, not temporal, not spatial, not passionate, not limited — theologians are left with a residue of pure negation. "By negations accumulated one upon another, they beli[[eve]] they form a real [[existence|being]], while under their own eyes their abstractions, their negations, become annihilated. A substance which possesses nothing that we know … can it be anything but nothingness?" (ch. 33). The concept of [[god|God]], on this analysis, is not merely false but semantically empty — a word designating nothing real.

## Historical Context

The argument that theological language reduces to "nothing" has a long philosophical pedigree. The ancient atomists — [[leucippus|Leucippus]], [[democritus|Democritus]], [[epicurus|Epicurus]] — had affirmed that nothing comes from nothing and nothing perishes into nothing, ma[[monarch|king]] [[nature|the universe]] eternal and uncreated. Lucretius' *De Rerum Natura* transmitted this argument to the early modern period, where it was taken up by materialists from [[hobbes|Hobbes]] to Diderot.

Holbach's specific contribution was to turn the "nothingness" argument against the [[religion|theology]] of his own day. Cartesian philosophy had accepted that [[god|God]]'s essence transcends human comprehension, retreating to a negative [[religion|theology]] in which [[god|God]] could be described only by what He is not — not finite, not material, not extended. For Holbach, this theological strategy was self-defeating. As he w[[ritual|rites]], the theologian "tells us what He is not. By negations accumulated one upon another, they beli[[eve]] they form a real [[existence|being]] … A substance which possesses nothing that we know … can it be anything but nothingness?" (ch. 33). The Cartesian [[god|God]], stripped of all positive predicates, collapses into the void from which it was meant to save [[earth|the world]].

## Core Arguments

1. **[[god|God]]-talk reduces to negation and nothingness.** When theologians insist [[god|God]] is utterly unlike anything we know, they empty the concept of content: "By negations accumulated one upon another, they beli[[eve]] they form a real [[existence|being]], while under their own eyes their abstractions, their negations, become annihilated" (ch. 33).

2. **The infinite [[god|God]] has nothing in common with finite creatures.** Holbach's key logical point: "If [[god|God]] is infinite, no finite [[existence|being]] can have communication with him, nor relations. Where there is no relation, there can be no union, no connection, no duties, no worship" (ch. 28). Infinity entails irrelevance — a [[god|God]] with whom we can have nothing in common is indistinguishable from no [[god|God]] at all.

3. **Religious principles are drawn from nothing.** Examining the foundations of [[revelation|revealed religion]], Holbach concludes that "religious principles are drawn from nothing, and are not proportionate to our views" (ch. 29). The claim to [[divinity|divine]] origin conceals an absence of rational justification.

4. **To adore [[god|God]] is to adore nothing.** In his most provocative formulation, Holbach w[[ritual|rites]]: "To adore [[god|God]] is to adore nothing but fictions of one's own brain, or rather, it is to adore nothing" (ch. 34). [[worship|Worship]] directed at an incomprehensible entity is worship without an object.

5. **Nothing comes from nothing — matter is eternal.** The Lucretian axiom grounds Holbach's [[matter|materialism]]: if [[nature|the universe]] required a creator, that creator would in turn require a creator, ad infinitum. The only coherent stopping point is the recognition that matter has always existed, requiring no external cause.

## Reception and Critique

Holbach's "nothingness" argument provoked sustained theological responses. Critics pointed out that negative [[religion|theology]] has a respectable pedigree — Maimonides, Pseudo-Dionysius, and Aquinas all employed it — and that the inability to describe [[god|God]] positively does not entail that the concept is vacuous. The analogy of [[existence|being]] (*analogia entis*) was developed precisely to avoid the dilemma Holbach posed.

Philosophically, Holbach's argument depends on a verificationist theory of meaning that would later be articulated more rigorously by the logical positivists. If a proposition has no empirically determinable content, it is — on this view — cognitively meaningless. Whether Holbach would have endorsed the stronger verificationist thesis is debatable; his argument functions more as a rhetorical *reductio* than a systematic semantic theory.

## Relation to Other Concepts

Nothingness is the conceptual antithesis of [[Being]], which Holbach treats as the fundamental predicate of material existence. It connects to [[Creation]], the doctrine that Holbach rejects — the claim that a [[god|God]] brought [[nature|the universe]] into [[existence|being]] from nothing. Nothingness relates to [[Infinity]] and [[Eternity]]: if [[god|God]] is infinite and eternal, He has nothing in common with finite, temporal creatures. The argument that "to adore [[god|God]] is to adore nothing" links to [[Worship]], which becomes an empty gesture when its object is unintelligible. Finally, nothingness connects to [[Death]], which Holbach treats not as transition to another realm but as the dissolution of the organized material system that constitutes a living [[existence|being]] — a return to the material whole, not an entry into nothingness.

## In the Text

> "To pretend that we can attribute to this phantom theological qualities, moral virtues, a character, a conduct, is, without doubt, to reason in a very strange manner. In truth, to adore God is to adore nothing but fictions of one's own brain, or rather, it is to adore nothing." (ch. 34)

> "By negations accumulated one upon another, they believe they form a real being, while under their own eyes their abstractions, their negations, become annihilated. A substance which possesses nothing that we know, or of which we can affirm nothing, which excludes all idea, can it be anything but nothingness?" (ch. 33)

> "If God is infinite, no finite being can have communication with him, nor relations. Where there is no relation, there can be no union, no connection, no duties, no worship. Saying that God is infinite, you annihilate, from that moment, all relation between Him and His creatures." (ch. 28)
