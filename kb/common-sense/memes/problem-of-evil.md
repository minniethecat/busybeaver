# Problem of Evil

## Definition

The Problem of Evil, as deployed in Baron d'Holbach's *Common Sense*, is the refutation of [[goodness|divine goodness]], power, and [[philosophy|wisdom]] based on the observable existence of [[evil|suffering]], disorder, and [[evil|moral evil]] in [[earth|the world]]. Holbach presents the problem not as a speculative theological puzzle but as an empirical refutation: if [[god|God]] is infinitely good, He would wish to prevent [[injustice|evil]]; if infinitely powerful, He could prevent [[injustice|evil]]; if infinitely wise, He would know how to prevent [[injustice|evil]]. The actual existence of [[injustice|evil]] therefore [[demon|demons]]trates that at least one of these [[attribute|divine attributes]] must be false — and Holbach concludes that all three are human projections onto a [[nature|Nature]] that is entirely indifferent to human welfare. This argument constitutes one of the central pillars of his materialist [[atheist|atheism]].

## Historical Context

The problem of evil has a long philosophical genealogy stretching from [[epicurus|Epicurus]] (who first formulated it as a logical trilemma) through Augustine, Aquinas, and [[gottfried-leibniz|Leibniz]]. [[gottfried-leibniz|Leibniz]]'s *Théodicée* (1710) — which coined the term "[[injustice|theodicy]]" and argued that this is "the best of all possible [[universe|world]]s" — was famously satirized by [[voltaire|Voltaire]] in *Candide* (1759) after the 1755 [[lisbon|Lisbon]] earthquake killed tens of thousands. Holbach wrote in the aftermath of this debate, radicalizing the Enlightenment critique: unlike [[voltaire|Voltaire]], who retained a deistic belief in a distant [[creator|Creator]], Holbach argued that [[evil|the problem of evil]] *dissolved* entirely once one abandoned the hypothesis of a [[divinity|divine]] intelligence governing [[universe|nature]]. [[evil|Evil]] is not a problem to be explained but [[proof|evidence]] that the [[god|God]]-hypothesis is false.

Holbach dedicated multiple chapters of *Common Sense* to the problem: Chapters LV ("[[god|God]] Can Not Be Called Immutable"), LVI ("[[evil|Evil]] and Good Are the Necessary [[effect|Effect]]s of Natural [[cause|Cause]]s"), LXXII ("It Is Absurd to Say That [[evil|Evil]] Does Not Come from [[god|God]]"), and LXXXVI ("All [[evil|Evil]], All Disorder, All [[sin|Sin]], Can Be [[attribute|Attribute]]d But to [[god|God]]"). This extended treatment signals the problem's centrality to his demolition of natural [[religion|theology]].

## Core Arguments

Holbach's first argument is the logical [[contradiction|inconsistency]] of [[attribute|divine attributes]] with observed reality. A [[god|God]] who is immutable (unchanging) cannot will one thing at one time and another at another — yet the biblical [[god|God]] first creates a perfect [[universe|world]], then destroys it with a flood, then promises never to do so again. A [[god|God]] who is perfectly good cannot be the author of a [[universe|world]] in which [[sentient-being|sentient beings]] suffer disease, famine, war, and natural disaster. A [[god|God]] who is omniscient cannot have created [[existence|being]]s He knew would [[guilt|sin]] and then punish them eternally for acting as He foresaw they would. Each [[divinity|divine]] attribute collapses under the weight of [[evidence|empirical evidence]].

Holbach's second argument is that all theological attempts to resolve [[evil|the problem of evil]] — [[liberty|free will]] defenses, "mysterious ways" appeals, [[sin|original sin]] narratives — merely relocate the problem without solving it. If [[injustice|evil]] results from human [[liberty|free will]], why did [[god|God]] create [[existence|being]]s He knew would misuse it? If [[injustice|evil]] is a [[secrecy|mystery]] beyond human comprehension, why claim to know that [[god|God]] is good? If [[injustice|evil]] entered [[earth|the world]] through [[adam|Adam]]'s [[guilt|sin]], why did [[god|God]] permit the [[serpent]] to tempt Eve, and why are all subsequent generations punished for an ancestor's [[crime|transgression]]? Holbach systematically exposes each [[injustice|theodicy]] as an evasion rather than an expla[[nations|nation]].

Holbach's third and most radical argument is the naturalization of [[injustice|evil]]. What theologians call "[[injustice|evil]]" — [[suffering|pain]], death, [[evil|suffering]] — are simply the [[effect|necessary effect]]s of natural causes operating according to deterministic laws. A volcano erupts not because [[god|God]] is angry but because geological pressures reach a critical threshold. A child dies of disease not because of [[sin|original sin]] but because microorganisms proliferate in certain conditions. To call these [[eve]]nts "[[injustice|evil]]" is to impose a human moral category onto value-neutral natural processes. The true error, Holbach argues, is not failing to reconcile [[injustice|evil]] with [[god|God]] but positing [[god|God]] in the first place.

## Reception and Critique

Holbach's version of [[evil|the problem of evil]] has been both praised for its uncompromising clarity and critiqued for its philosophical limitations. Alan Charles Kors (*D'Holbach's Coterie*, 1976) notes that Holbach's treatment, while rhetorically powerful, does not engage seriously with the most sophisticated theodicies of his own era — [[gottfried-leibniz|Leibniz]]'s argument that [[injustice|evil]] is a necessary condition of the greatest possible good, or [[malebranche|Malebranche]]'s [[injustice|theodicy]] of general laws. Holbach treats all theodicies as equally worthless and dispatches them with a [[guilt|sin]]gle logical schema, which some critics consider philosophically dismissive.

A deeper critique comes from the tradition of post-Holocaust [[religion|theology]]. Holbach's [[certainty|confidence]] that [[evil|the problem of evil]] *dissolves* by eliminating [[god|God]] does not address the existential question: if [[universe|nature]] is indifferent, why does human [[evil|suffering]] matter? Holbach's answer — that [[evil|suffering]] matters because humans care about it and can ameliorate it — is arguably sufficient, but it does not supply the metaphysical grounding that many find necessary for moral commitments. Dostoevsky's Ivan Karamazov, who "returns his ticket" to a [[god|God]] who permits the [[evil|suffering]] of children, arrives at a position structurally similar to Holbach's, but without the Enlightenment optimism about [[progress|human progress]].

Contemporary philosophers of religion have recognized Holbach as an important precursor to the "evidential" problem of evil articulated by [[will|Will]]iam Rowe (1979): the sheer quantity and distribution of apparently pointless [[evil|suffering]] constitutes [[proof|evidence]] against the existence of [[god|God]], [[eve]]n if no [[contradiction|logical contradiction]] is proved. Holbach's empirical approach — cataloguing actual instances of [[evil|suffering]] rather than relying on logical deduction — anticipates this modern development.

## Relation to Other Concepts

The problem of evil is the conceptual linchpin connecting Holbach's metaphysics to his [[morality|ethics]]. It connects to [[goodness]], which Holbach reconstructs on naturalistic foundations once [[goodness|divine goodness]] is refuted — if [[god|God]] is not good, goodness must be redefined in terms of human welfare. The argument depends on the concept of [[determinism]], which dissolves the free-will [[injustice|theodicy]] by showing that all actions, including [[sin|sinful]] ones, are [[effect|necessary effect]]s of prior causes. The problem of evil supplies the evidential basis for Holbach's critique of [[providence]] — if there is no benevolent [[divinity|divine]] [[government|governance]], the observed disorder of [[earth|the world]] is exactly what [[matter|materialism]] predicts. The concept connects to [[divine-attributes]] (omnipotence, omni[[knowledge|science]], omni[[charity|benevolence]]), each of which Holbach subjects to empirical scrutiny. Finally, the naturalization of [[injustice|evil]] as value-neutral causal necessity connects to [[nature]] — the material totality that requires no supernatural expla[[nations|nation]] and imposes no moral order on its operations.

## In the Text

Holbach addresses [[evil|the problem of evil]] through a sustained critique of specific [[doctrine|theological doctrine]]s. On [[sin|original sin]] (Chapter LXXIV), he w[[ritual|rites]] that the doctrine is an "[[absurdity|absurd]]ity" that makes [[god|God]] the true author of all human [[crime|transgression]]s: if [[god|God]] created human [[universe|nature]] and human [[universe|nature]] is [[sin|sinful]], then [[god|God]] created [[guilt|sin]]. On [[punishment|divine punishment]] (Chapter LXXIX), he notes the injustice of a [[existence|being]] who "punishes the faults which He could have prevented." The entire apparatus of [[guilt|sin]], guilt, and [[salvation|redemption]] is exposed as a clerical invention designed to manufacture a disease ([[guilt|sin]]) for which only [[clergy|the clergy]] possess the cure ([[sacrament|sacraments]] and absolution).

Holbach also turns [[evil|the problem of evil]] into an argument for [[atheist|atheism]]'s moral superiority. If [[god|God]] does not exist, [[injustice|evil]] is simply mis[[chance|fortune]] to be remedied by human effort, [[knowledge|science]], and [[reform|social reform]]. If [[god|God]] does exist and permits [[injustice|evil]], then [[god|God]] is malevolent and unworthy of worship. In either case, human [[existence|being]]s should turn their attention away from futile [[prayer|prayers]] and toward practical amelioration of [[evil|suffering]].

> "Men are unhappy only because they are ignorant; they are ignorant only because everything conspires to prevent them from being enlightened."
