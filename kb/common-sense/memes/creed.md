## Definition

A creed is a formal, authoritative statement of religious doctrine whose acceptance is required for membership in a [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian [[society|community]]. The most influential creeds — the Apostles' Creed, the Nicene Creed, and the Athanasian Creed — were formulated in the early centuries of [[christianity|Christianity]] to define orthodoxy and exclude heresy. Recitation of the creed is a central act of [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian worship in most [[sect|denomination]]s.

For Holbach, the creed exemplifies the coercive logic of [[revelation|revealed religion]]. Believers are required to affirm propositions that are not merely unproven but often unintelligible — that [[god|God]] is [[trinity|three persons]] in one substance, that a virgin gave birth, that bread becomes flesh — and the penalty for refusal is eternal [[hell|damnation]]. No secular institution would be permitted to demand [[belief|assent]] to incomprehensible propositions on [[suffering|pain]] of death; religion does so as a matter of course. The creed is not an invitation to [[knowledge|understanding]] but a test of submission.

## Historical Context

The formulation of creeds in the early [[church|Church]] was [[desire|drive]]n by doctrinal controversy. The Council of Nicaea (325 CE) produced the Nicene Creed to settle the Arian controversy over the divinity of [[jesus-christ|Christ]]; the [[council-of-trent|Council of Trent]] (1545–1563) reaffirmed the creeds against Protestant challenges. By Holbach's time, creedal orthodoxy was enforced by both ecclesiastical and civil authority — heresy was a crime as well as a [[guilt|sin]].

[[enlightenment|The Enlightenment]] subjected the creeds to a new kind of scrutiny. Biblical critics [[demon|demons]]trated that the doctrines codified in the creeds could not be found in their developed form in the [[bible|New Testament]]; they were products of centuries of theological elaboration. For Holbach, this historical [[proof|demonstration]] confirmed what reason already knew: creeds are human inventions, not [[divinity|divine]] communications.

## Core Arguments

1. **Creeds demand [[belief|assent]] to the unintelligible.** The propositions of the Nicene Creed — "[[god|God]] from [[god|God]], Light from Light, true [[god|God]] from true [[god|God]], begotten not made, consubstantial with the Father" — are not statements that can be understood and evaluated. They are formulas to be repeated, and the repetition is the [[evidence|proof]] of [[religion|faith]].

2. **The penalty for disbelief reveals the coercive [[universe|nature]] of religion.** If the propositions of the creed were reasonable, belief would not need to be compelled by threats of [[hell|eternal punishment]]. The severity of the penalty is proportional to the weakness of the [[proof|evidence]].

3. **Creedal uniformity serves political purposes.** A population that can be made to agree on incomprehensible theological propositions can also be made to agree on political propositions. The habit of intellectual submission [[worship|cult]]ivated by creedal religion is valuable to [[despotism|despot]]ic governments.

4. **The historical development of creeds proves their human origin.** The creeds were formulated by councils of bishops over centuries of debate and political maneuvering. They bear the marks of the historical circumstances that produced them, not the marks of [[divinity|divine]] authorship.

## In the Text

Holbach does not discuss the creeds in isolation, but his analysis of the [[council-of-trent|Council of Trent]] illustrates the mechanism of creedal imposition:

> "The Council of Trent was assembled to fix the dogmas of the Church; it decided upon questions which had divided the theologians during several centuries. But did it convince them? Not at all." (ch. CXXV)

He connects doctrinal imposition to political control:

> "Religion was made only for the purpose of keeping the people in obedience under the yoke of the priests and the kings." (ch. CXXIV)
