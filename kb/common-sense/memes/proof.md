# Proof

## Definition

Holbach applies the [[model|standard]] of rational proof — demonstration through evidence and logical inference — to the claims of religion and finds them [[desire|want]]ing at [[eve]]ry turn. "Where, then, is the [[evidence|proof]] that [[god|God]] did [[eve]]r show Himself to men or speak to them?" (ch. CXXV). The demand for [[evidence|proof]] is not an arbitrary philosophical preference; it follows from the [[universe|nature]] of the claims themselves. If religion is "the most important thing for men" (ch. CXV), then it ought to be the best [[demon|demons]]trated of all things. That it is instead the most disputed is, for Holbach, decisive evidence against it.

The argument from the absence of [[evidence|proof]] is not merely negative. Holbach observes that the very structure of [[authority|religious authority]] — a priestly caste that alone has access to the sanctuary, that alone interprets [[divinity|divine]] intentions for the unlearned — is designed to prevent independent verification. "It was never permitted to the people to go to the sanctuary; the ministers of the [[god|God]]s always alone had the right to report to them what transpired" (ch. CXXV). The absence of public [[evidence|proof]] is a feature of the system, not an [[chance|accident]].

In place of theological proof-by-authority, Holbach advocates an empirical [[model|standard]]: claims about [[earth|the world]] must be tested against experience and reason. "The most important thing for men is that which is the most impossible for them to comprehend" (ch. V) — this paradox defines the theological enterprise and condemns it.

## Historical Context

Holbach's emphasis on [[evidence|proof]] belongs to the Enlightenment's broader epistemological turn toward evidentialism. [[john-locke|Locke]] had argued that the degree of [[belief|assent]] should be proportioned to the strength of the evidence, and that belief without sufficient evidence is a moral failing. [[david-hume|Hume]]'s critique of [[miracle|miracles]] — that no testimony is sufficient to establish a miracle unless its falsehood would be more miraculous than the fact it seeks to establish — set a high bar for supernatural [[evidence|proof]] claims.

Holbach applies this evidentialist framework systematically to [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian doctrine. Unlike earlier skeptics who questioned particular [[evidence|proof]]s of [[god|God]]'s existence while preserving room for [[religion|faith]], Holbach argues that the demand for [[evidence|proof]] is universal and the failure to meet it is total. The [[evidence|proof]]s offered — design arguments, [[miracle|miracles]], fulfilled prophecies — are all found to rest on circular reasoning or unverifiable testimony.

This approach reflects the broader Enlightenment project of bringing all claims under the jurisdiction of reason. As Kant would later put it, [[education|enlightenment]] is man's emergence from self-incurred immaturity — the inability to use one's [[knowledge|understanding]] without the guidance of another. Holbach's demand for [[evidence|proof]] is an assertion of intellectual maturity against the tutelage of [[clergy|priests]].

## Core Arguments

**1. The importance of a claim is inversely proportional to the [[toleration|tolerance]] for its [[existence|being]] unproven.** If religion truly determined [[salvation|eternal salvation]], a just [[god|God]] would have made its truth "the clearest, the most evident, and the best [[demon|demons]]trated of all things" (ch. CXV). That it is instead "precisely the one which they understand the least" is a contradiction that refutes the premise of [[goodness|divine goodness]].

**2. The [[evidence|proof]]s offered for [[god|God]]'s existence are circular.** "They beli[[eve]] they see the most convincing [[evidence|proof]] of His existence in [[nature|the universe]]" (ch. II), but this argument assumes what it seeks to prove — that order requires an orderer, that design implies a designer. The natural [[universe|world]], properly understood, explains itself through its own laws.

**3. [[miracle|Miracle]]s are not [[evidence|proof]]s but tales void of probability.** [[miracle|Miracle]] claims come from "suspicious people, who had the greatest interest in ma[[monarch|king]] others beli[[eve]] that they were sent from [[hope|heaven]]" (ch. CXXVI). The interestedness of the witnesses, combined with the improbability of the [[eve]]nts, makes miracle testimony worthless as [[evidence|proof]].

**4. The burden of [[evidence|proof]] lies on those who assert supernatural claims.** "What can be yet a greater [[evidence|proof]] of the falsity of these pretended [[dreams|revelations]]" than their mutual contradiction and the historical violence they have generated? (ch. XXII). Extraordinary claims require extraordinary evidence, and religion provides none.

## Relation to Other Concepts

Proof is the epistemological [[model|standard]] that [[Theology]] systematically fails to meet. It is the instrument of [[Reason]] applied to claims about the [[divinity|divine]]. The absence of [[evidence|proof]] for religious claims leaves [[Faith]] as the only alternative foundation — a foundation Holbach identifies with [[Credulity]]. The demand for [[evidence|proof]] aligns with [[Knowledge]] as the fruit of empirical investigation, and opposes [[Superstition]] which thrives where the demand for [[evidence|proof]] is suppressed. [[Evidence]] is what [[evidence|proof]] rests upon; [[Certainty]] is what [[evidence|proof]] aims to produce.

## In the Text

On the absence of public [[evidence|proof]] in [[religion|religious system]]s:

> "How did God show Himself? Did He Himself promulgate His laws? Did He speak to men with His own mouth? I am told that God did not show Himself to a whole nation, but that He employed always the organism of a few favored persons, who took the care to teach and to explain His intentions to the unlearned." (125-003)

On the contradiction between the importance of religion and its obscurity:

> "If religion was necessary to all men, it ought to be intelligible to all men. If this religion was the most important thing for them, the goodness of God, it seems, ought to make it for them the clearest, the most evident, and the best demonstrated of all things." (115-001)

On the unproven [[universe|nature]] of supernatural claims:

> "They have been for many centuries contesting this matter without understanding each other; they still fight, they still dispute, and probably they will dispute as long as they blindly follow the guidance of their imagination." (ch. VIII)
