# Probability

## Definition

Probability and chance, in Holbach's [[materialism|materialist philosophy]], are not positive features of reality but confessions of ignorance. When people call an [[eve]]nt "probable" or attribute it to "chance," they are not describing a genuine indeterminacy in [[universe|nature]] but rather acknowledging the limits of their own knowledge. As Holbach argues in *Common Sense*, "Men may use the word 'chance' to cover their ignorance of the true causes; nevertheless, although they may ignore them, these causes act, but by certain laws. There is no effect without a cause" (043-001). This position represents a thoroughgoing [[effect|determinism]]: [[eve]]ry [[eve]]nt in [[nature|the universe]] is the necessary consequence of antecedent causes operating according to fixed natural laws. Probability is thus an epistemic category — a measure of human [[doubt|uncertainty]] — rather than an ontological one.

## Holbach's Critique of Chance

Holbach devotes an entire chapter (XLIII) to refuting the notion that [[nature|the universe]] or human [[existence|being]]s could be the products of chance. His argument proceeds from a simple premise: "[[nothingness|Nothing]] in [[universe|nature]] can be made by chance; all follow fixed laws; these laws are but the necessary union of certain effects with their causes." If an atom collides with another atom, it does so not by [[chance|accident]] but because both atoms are obeying deterministic laws of motion. The appearance of randomness arises only because we cannot trace the full causal chain.

This critique serves a dual purpose in Holbach's broader argument. First, it undermines the [[design|teleological argument]] for [[god|God]]'s existence — the claim that the order of [[nature|the universe]] requires a [[design|divine design]]er. Holbach counters that order arises necessarily from the operation of natural laws, not from supernatural intervention. Second, it targets the religious mindset that sees [[divinity|divine]] [[providence]] in [[eve]]ry [[eve]]nt while dismissing inconvenient outcomes as "mysterious" or "[[chance|accident]]al." The believer's selective invocation of chance and [[providence]] reveals, for Holbach, the intellectual bankruptcy of [[theology]].

## The Political Function of "Providence"

Holbach identifies a political dimension to the concept of chance. The [[priest|clergy]] teach that [[eve]]nts are governed by [[divinity|divine]] [[providence]] when the outcome favors their interests, but they invoke "[[secrecy|mystery]]" or "inscrutable [[divinity|divine]] will" when [[eve]]nts contradict their claims. The ordinary believer, unable to distinguish genuine [[cause|causation]] from [[priestcraft|priestly]] interpretation, is left in a state of intellectual dependency. The concept of chance thus becomes an instrument of social control: "religion presents to all the different [[nation|nations]] of the earth only romances devoid of probability, of which the hero himself is made up of qualities impossible to reconcile" (ch. II).

Holbach's alternative is a [[universe]] governed entirely by necessary laws, knowable through [[science]] and [[reason]]. In such a universe, the word "chance" becomes superfluous — a placeholder for causes we have not yet identified but which are no less real or deterministic for our ignorance of them.

## In Holbach's Materialist System

Probability and [[effect|determinism]] occupy a central place in Holbach's materialist [[philosophy]]. If [[nature|the universe]] is composed entirely of matter in motion, governed by unchanging laws, then [[eve]]ry physical and mental [[eve]]nt — including human [[choice]] and [[morality]] — is causally determined. This has profound implications for Holbach's [[morality|ethics]]: if human actions are determined by [[education]], environment, and [[habit]], then moral [[progress|improvement]] must proceed not by preaching [[free-will|free will]] but by reforming the social and intellectual conditions that shape human [[behavior]].

The deterministic rejection of chance also underpins Holbach's critique of [[miracle|miracles]]. A miracle is, by definition, a suspension of natural law — an [[eve]]nt that would not have occurred through ordinary causal sequences. But if natural laws are fixed and universal, [[miracle|miracles]] are impossible. Reports of miraculous [[eve]]nts are either misunderstandings of [[phenomena|natural phenomena]] or deliberate fabrications by a [[priesthood]] that profits from popular [[belief|credulity]].
