# Thomas Woolston

## Biography

Thomas Woolston (1668–1733) was an English theologian and fellow of Sidney Sussex College, Cambridge. Ordained as an Anglican [[priest|clergy]]man, he became increasingly skeptical of orthodox [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian doctrine and turned to allegorical interpretation of [[bible|scripture]]. His most famous work, *Six Discourses on the [[miracle|Miracle]]s of Our [[jesus-christ|Saviour]]* (1727–1729), argued that the [[miracle|miracles]] recorded in the [[bible|New Testament]] were not literal historical [[eve]]nts but allegorical representations of [[soul|spirit]]ual truths. For this, Woolston was prosecuted for blasphemy, fined, and imprisoned until his death. He became a cause célèbre among English freethinkers and was widely discussed in French Enlightenment circles.

## Role in Religious History

Woolston occupies a distinctive niche in the history of religious criticism: he was a believing [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian who denied the literal truth of [[gospel|the Gospel]] [[miracle|miracles]]. This paradoxical position — [[religion|faith]] without miracle — placed him at odds with both orthodox defenders of [[supernatural|supernaturalism]] and radical materialists who rejected [[christianity|Christianity]] entirely. His prosecution under England's blasphemy laws made him a symbol of clerical [[cruelty|persecution]] and intellectual [[martyr|martyrdom]]. The sheer scale of his readership — sixty thousand copies of his *Discourses* were reportedly sold from his own house — [[demon|demons]]trated a public [[desire|appetite]] for rationalist critique of [[bible|scripture]] that the established churches could neither satisfy nor suppress.

## Connection to "Common Sense"

Holbach discusses Woolston at length in the Preface to *Common Sense*, comparing him favorably with [[meslier|Jean Meslier]]. He notes with admiration that Woolston "published and sold publicly at London, in his own house, sixty thousand copies of his 'Discourses' against the [[miracle|miracles]] of [[jesus-christ|Jesus Christ]]" — presenting this as [[proof|evidence]] that [[eve]]n ordained [[priest|clergy]]men recognized the [[absurdity|absurd]]ity of [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian miracle claims. Yet Holbach also observes that "Woolston is sometimes indulgent, Meslier never" — acknowledging that Woolston's allegorical approach left room for a [[soul|spirit]]ualized [[christianity|Christianity]] that Meslier's thoroughgoing [[matter|materialism]] did not. For Holbach, Woolston's career proves that rational scrutiny of [[bible|scripture]] leads inexorably toward the rejection of [[supernatural|supernaturalism]], [[eve]]n when the critic tries to preserve a kernel of [[religion|faith]].

> "While the abbot Meslier naively acknowledged that he did not wish to be burned till after his death, Thomas Woolston, a doctor of Cambridge, published and sold publicly at London, in his own house, sixty thousand copies of his 'Discourses' against the miracles of Jesus Christ." — Holbach, *Common Sense*, Preface

> "Woolston is sometimes indulgent, Meslier never. He was a man profoundly embittered by the crimes he witnessed, for which he holds the Christian religion responsible." — Holbach, *Common Sense*, Preface (citing Voltaire)

## Legacy

Woolston's influence was felt more in [[france|France]] than in England. His *Discourses* were translated and widely read among the philosophes, and his prosecution became a textbook case of clerical [[persecution|intolerance]]. [[voltaire|Voltaire]] discussed him alongside Meslier and other critics of [[christianity|Christianity]]; Holbach's Preface ensures that English-spea[[monarch|king]] readers of *Common Sense* would recognize Woolston as an ally in the war against superstition. In the longer history of biblical criticism, Woolston stands as a transitional figure — too [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian for the materialists, too skeptical for the orthodox — whose attempt to reconcile [[religion|faith]] with rational critique helped make possible the more thoroughgoing rejection that followed.
