# Infinite

## Definition

The infinite, in Holbach's "Common Sense," is the concept of unlimited, unbounded, and unconditioned [[existence|being]] — the central attribute that theologians ascribe to [[god|God]] to signify His transcendence of all finite limitations. Holbach's argument is that this very attribute, far from exalting [[god|God]], makes [[god|God]] impossible for human [[existence|being]]s to know, relate to, or worship. "To say that [[god|God]] is infinite, is to destroy Him for men, or at least render Him perfectly useless" (ch. 2). The infinite is thus a theological self-defeat: the attribute meant to express [[divinity|divine]] greatness is the one that severs any possible connection between [[god|God]] and humanity.

## Historical Context

The concept of the infinite underwent a dramatic transformation in the early modern period. For ancient and medieval thought, the infinite (*apeiron*, *infinitum*) was typically associated with imperfection — matter without form, potentiality without actuality. The finite and determinate was superior. This hierarchy was inverted by Neoplatonism and [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian [[religion|theology]], which made infinity a [[perfection|divine perfection]]. [[rene-descartes|Descartes]] then placed the idea of the infinite at the foundation of his [[evidence|proof]]s of [[god|God]]: the finite human mind cannot be the source of the idea of an infinite [[existence|being]], so [[god|God]] must have implanted it. Holbach accepts the Cartesian premise that the infinite is incomprehensible to finite minds, but draws the opposite conclusion: "The idea of infinity is for us an idea without model, without [[model|prototype]], without object" (ch. 7).

## Core Arguments

**1. The infinite is incomprehensible to finite minds.** "If [[god|God]] is infinite, no finite [[existence|being]] can have communication or any relation with Him" (ch. 7). The gap is not one of degree but of kind — no amount of [[education|learning]], revelation, or grace can make the infinite accessible to the finite.

**2. The idea of infinity has no object.** "The idea of infinity is for us an idea without model, without [[model|prototype]], without object" (ch. 7). Holbach is ma[[monarch|king]] a semantic point: a word that refers to nothing conceivable is not a genuine concept but an empty vocalization.

**3. [[theology-index|Divine]] infinity annihilates religion.** "Thus by saying that [[god|God]] is infinite, you annihilate, from that moment, all religion for man, who is a finite [[existence|being]]" (ch. 7). The attribute theologians deploy to elevate [[god|God]] is, on analysis, the very attribute that makes [[god|God]] irrelevant.

**4. No future state can bridge the gap.** Even in a hypothetical [[death|afterlife]] of greater [[education|enlightenment]], "the infinity of [[god|God]] will always place such a distance between his idea and the limited mind of man, that he will not be able to conceive of [[god|God]] any more in a [[hope|future life]] than in the present" (ch. 8). The problem is logical, not epistemological.

**5. Infinite [[attribute|attributes]] collapse under their own weight.** Theologians describe [[god|God]] as infinitely good, infinitely powerful, infinitely wise — but "Where is the infinite power of a [[existence|being]] who can do but a very few things to please me? Where is the infinite kindness of a [[existence|being]] who is indifferent to my welfare? Where is the infinite goodness of a [[existence|being]] who, having it in his power to confer upon me infinite good, does not [[eve]]n give me a finite one?" (ch. 2). Each infinite attribute is refuted by the observed state of [[earth|the world]].

## Reception and Critique

Holbach's argument that the infinite is unknowable draws on a long skeptical tradition. But it is vulnerable to the reply that mathematics deals successfully with the infinite (calculus, set theory), and that concepts may be meaningful without [[existence|being]] fully comprehensible. A theist might respond that "infinite" means "not finite" — a negative definition that finite minds can grasp without comprehending infinity positively. Holbach would likely reply that a purely negative definition cannot ground worship, obligation, or religion. The debate continues in contemporary philosophy of religion, particularly in discussions of [[divinity|divine]] simplicity and apophatic [[religion|theology]].

## Relation to Other Concepts

The infinite is the [[divinity|divine]] [[Attribute]] that Holbach weaponizes against [[divinity|theism]]. It is the conceptual ground of the [[Finite]]-infinite gap that severs human-[[divinity|divine]] relations. It is what makes [[God]] "an idea without model." It stands in opposition to [[Nature]], which is finite, knowable, and law-governed. It connects to Holbach's critique of [[Imagination]]: the infinite is a product of imagi[[nations|nation]] unchecked by [[Experience]] and [[Reason]].

## In the Text

The core argument from chapter 7:

> "Religion unites man with God or puts them in communication; but do you say that God is infinite? If God is infinite, no finite being can have communication or any relation with Him. Where there are no relations, there can be no union, no correspondence, no duties. If there are no duties between man and his God, there exists no religion for man. Thus by saying that God is infinite, you annihilate, from that moment, all religion for man, who is a finite being." (ch. 7)

On the infinite as a useless concept:

> "To say that God is infinite, is to destroy Him for men, or at least render Him perfectly useless. To say that God is infinite, is to say that He is not made for His creatures." (ch. 2)

On the failure of infinite [[attribute|attributes]] in practice:

> "Where is the infinite power of a being who can do but a very few things to please me? Where is the infinite kindness of a being who is indifferent to my welfare?" (ch. 2)
