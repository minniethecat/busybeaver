# God

## Definition and Centrality

The concept of God is the foundational pillar of all theological systems. As Holbach w[[ritual|rites]], "the existence of a God is the basis of all religion" (ch. 2). Without this premise, the entire edifice of [[belief|religious belief]] — revelation, [[clergy|priesthood]], morality grounded in [[divinity|divine]] command, and the promise of an [[death|afterlife]] — collapses. Yet Holbach argues that this foundational concept is precisely the weakest link in the theological chain: "Few people seem to doubt the existence of a God" (ch. 2), but this near-universal belief conceals a profound absence of any clear, consistent, or communicable idea of what "God" actually means.

## The Incomprehensibility Argument

Holbach's central critique is that the concept of God is inherently self-defeating. Theologians insist that God is infinite, yet they also claim that religion "unites man with God or puts them in communication" (ch. 2). These two assertions cannot both be true:

> "If God is infinite, no finite being can have communication with him, nor relations. Where there is no relation, there can be no union, no connection, no duties, no worship. Saying that God is infinite, you annihilate, from that moment, all relation between Him and His creatures." (002-007)

This argument has a clear logical structure: [[infinite|infinity]] entails incomprehensibility; an incomprehensible entity cannot be the object of any human duty, worship, or relationship. The very predicates theologians assign to God — [[infinite|infinity]], incomprehensibility, transcendence — make religious practice incoherent.

## The Contradiction of Divine Attributes

Holbach systematically catalogs the contradictions between the [[attribute|attributes]] traditionally ascribed to God. The Apologue (ch. 1) presents this critique in allegorical form:

> "He is called supremely good, nevertheless not a person but complains of his decrees. He is supposed to be infinitely wise, and in his administration everything seems contrary to reason and good sense. They boast of his justice, and the best of his subjects are generally the least favored. We are assured that he sees everything, yet his presence remedies nothing."

| [[attribute|Attribute]] | Claimed | Observed |
|-----------|---------|----------|
| [[goodness|Goodness]] | Supremely good | Universal complaint about [[divinity|divine]] decrees |
| [[wisdom|Wisdom]] | [[infinite|Infinite]]ly wise | Administration contrary to reason |
| [[justice|Justice]] | Perfect justice | The best are least favored |
| Omni[[knowledge|science]] | Sees [[eve]]rything | His presence remedies nothing |
| [[order|Order]] | Friend of order | [[universe|Universe]] in confusion and disorder |
| [[providence|Providence]] | Foresees [[eve]]rything | Foresight prevents nothing |

This table crystallizes Holbach's core argument: if God has these [[attribute|attributes]], the observable [[universe|world]] — his alleged creation — is the best [[proof|evidence]] against him.

## Origins of the Idea

Holbach traces the idea of God not to revelation but to human psychology. Early humans, "frightened and led astray by ignorance, by fear, by calamities," personified the unknown forces of [[universe|nature]] and projected human qualities onto them. The "inconceivable" was mistaken for the "[[divinity|divine]]": "the more inconceivable things are, the more [[divinity|divine]] they appear to him; the more incredible they are, the more merit he believes he gains in crediting them" (ch. 2). The concept of God is thus, for Holbach, a historically contingent product of ignorance, not a necessary rational inference.

## Historical Evolution

The concept has undergone radical transformations across [[worship|cult]]ures and epochs — from the anthropomorphic tribal [[gods|deities]] of the ancient Hebrews, to the abstract philosophical God of Greek metaphysics, to the [[trinity|triune God]] of [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian [[religion|theology]], to the watchmaker God of Deism. Holbach notes that [[eve]]n within [[christianity|Christianity]], conceptions of God are irreconcilable across [[sect|sects]] and centuries. The "metaphysical God is a workman without hands; he is able to act without organs" (ch. 2) — a conceptual mutation so far removed from anything human experience can verify that it becomes, in practice, indistinguishable from nothing.

## Relation to Other Concepts

The concept of [[God]] stands at the apex of Holbach's critical targets. It is the premise from which [[Religion]] derives its authority, the source of the [[doctrine|theological doctrine]]s attacked by [[Atheism]], and the imagined guarantor of the [[Soul]]'s im[[death|mortality]]. Holbach's [[Materialism]] is in part developed as the positive alternative to theistic metaphysics: a self-sufficient universe requiring no external creator.

## In the Text

Holbach's Apologue opens the work with a sustained analogy between an invisible, contradictory monarch and the theological God:

> "This empire is the world; its monarch is God; His ministers are the priests; their subjects are men." (ch. 1)

On the [[contradiction|incoherence]] of [[divinity|divine]] [[infinite|infinity]]:

> "If God is an infinite being, there can be neither in the actual world or in a future world any relation, any point of contact between man and the divinity. Thus, it evidently follows that the idea of God will not be better suited to man in the other life than in the present." (ch. 2)
