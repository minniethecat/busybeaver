# Apostle

## In Holbach's Usage

Holbach uses the term **apostle** in two contrasting senses in *Common Sense*. In its historical sense, he observes that "history informs me that the first apostles, founders or reformers of religions" (002-258) attracted disciples and built movements through persuasion rather than force — though he notes drily that "they are not the strongest; the apostles usually practice a perilous trade" (002-263). In a polemical reversal, Holbach reclaims the term for the Enlightenment project: he urges his readers to become "the apostles of reason, the lights of the world" (002-412). This rhetorical move — appropriating religious language for secular purposes — is characteristic of Holbach's strategy: he does not merely attack religion but offers an alternative, calling for a new kind of apostolic mission devoted to reason and critical inquiry rather than to faith and submission.

## Selected Passage

Holbach challenges priests to abandon superstition and embrace reason, reclaiming the language of apostleship for the Enlightenment:

> Priests! lay aside your idle fancies, your unintelligible dogmas, your despicable quarrels; take the tone of reason, instead of sounding the tocsin of persecution against your adversaries; become the apostles of reason, the lights of the nations, the defenders of liberty, reformers of abuses, the friends of truth, and we will bless you, we will honor you, we will love you.
>
> (002-412)
