# Blaise Pascal

## Biography

Blaise Pascal (1623–1662) was a French mathematician, physicist, inventor, and religious philosopher. A child prodigy, he made foundational contributions to projective geometry, probability theory, and the physics of fluids. In the 1650s, after a profound religious experience, Pascal turned his intellectual energies toward [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian apologetics, joining the Jansenist movement centered at [[jansenists|Port-Royal]]. His unfinished *Pensées* ([[thought|Thought]]s), published posthumously in 1670, became one of the most influential works of French literature and [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian philosophy.

## Role in Religious History

Pascal is remembered as one of [[christianity|Christianity]]'s most eloquent defenders. His "Wager" argument — that it is rational to beli[[eve]] in [[god|God]] because the potential reward of eternal life outweighs the finite cost of belief — remains a landmark in the philosophy of religion. Pascal also clashed with the [[jesuits|Jesuits]] in his *Lettres provinciales*, satirizing Jesuit casuistry and defending Jansenist [[religion|theology]]. His fusion of mathematical rigor with religious conviction gave Catholic apologetics a new intellectual respectability in the early modern period, influencing thinkers from Bossuet to Kierkegaard.

## Connection to "Common Sense"

Holbach devotes the entire Chapter CXXX of *Common Sense* to refuting Pascal's reasoning on [[miracle|miracles]]. The target is Pascal's circular method: "judge the doctrine by [[miracle|miracles]], and the [[miracle|miracles]] by the doctrine." Holbach presents this as a "defective and ridiculous circle" — [[guilt|sin]]ce [[eve]]ry religion claims admirable doctrine backed by [[miracle|miracles]], Pascal's test cannot distinguish true from false revelation. For Holbach, Pascal exemplifies how [[eve]]n a brilliant mind, once captured by religious [[opinion|prejudice]], produces arguments that collapse under rational scrutiny.

> "Pascal, in order to disembarrass us, says very gravely, that we must judge the doctrine by miracles, and the miracles by the doctrine; that doctrine judges the miracles, and the miracles judge the doctrine. If there exists a defective and ridiculous circle, it is no doubt in this fine reasoning of one of the greatest defenders of the Christian religion." — Holbach, *Common Sense*, ch. CXXX

Holbach further questions whether any miracle, however spectacular, can overturn [[demon|demons]]trated truth. He asks: could a miracle-worker convince us that 2+2≠4, or that an infinite [[god|God]] became a finite man? Pascal's own mathematical genius is implicitly turned against his apologetics — reason and [[proof|demonstration]], not circular appeals to [[miracle|miracles]], are the only reliable guides to truth.

## Legacy

Pascal's intellectual legacy is twofold. In mathematics and [[knowledge|science]], his contributions remain foundational — probability theory, Pascal's Triangle, and the principle of the hydraulic press bear his name. In religious thought, the *Pensées* continue to inspire [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian existentialists and apologists. Yet Holbach's critique in *Common Sense* foreshadows the Enlightenment's broader rejection of Pascal's fi[[theism|deism]]. By targeting Pascal — the most mathematically rigorous of [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian apologists — Holbach signals that no appeal to [[miracle|miracles]] or paradox can survive rational exami[[nations|nation]]. Pascal thus stands as a test case for the Enlightenment's central thesis: that reason, not [[religion|faith]], is the proper arbiter of truth.
