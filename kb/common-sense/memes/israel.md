# Israel

## Definition

Israel, in Holbach's analysis, is the paradigmatic "chosen people" — a [[nations|nation]] whose history was retroactively rewritten by [[priest]]ly editors to conform to the narrative of [[divinity|divine]] [[predestination|election]]. Holbach examines the biblical account of Israel not as sacred history but as a human document that reveals the mechanisms by which religious identity is constructed: a small people "distinguished from others by superstitious and [[absurdity|absurd]] [[habit|custom]]s" invents a mythology of [[divinity|divine]] favor to legitimize its laws and territorial claims (Ch. 124). The very concept of a chosen people, Holbach argues, is a template for religious [[intolerance]] — if one [[nations|nation]] is uniquely favored by [[god]], all others are by definition excluded.

## Etymology and Biblical Context

The name Israel ("he who struggles with [[god|God]]") originates in the [[genesis]] narrative where [[jacob]] wrestles with a [[god|divine being]] and is renamed: "Thy name shall be called no more [[jacob|Jacob]], but Israel; for as a [[monarch|prince]] hast thou power with [[god|God]] and with men, and hast prevailed" (Gen. 32:28). Holbach treats this episode as one of many biblical passages that reveal the anthropomorphic and tribal character of the [[god|deity]] — a god who physically wrestles with a man and loses, yet is simultaneously proclaimed omnipotent.

## Israel as Political Archetype

Holbach's interest in Israel is not antiquarian but analytical. The Israelite theocracy — a state governed directly by [[divinity|divine]] law as interpreted by [[clergy|priests]] — serves as his model for [[knowledge|understanding]] how [[authority|religious authority]] transforms political institutions into instruments of [[authority|clerical power]]. The people of Israel were "the only one that can be regarded as having descended from the first man who was created by [[god|God]] himself" (Ch. 122), and this claim of unique descent became the justification for conquest, exclusion, and the suppression of neighboring [[nation|peoples]]. Holbach sees in this [[model|pattern]] the template for all subsequent religious polities, where a claim to [[divinity|divine]] [[predestination|election]] licenses political violence.

## Spiritual vs. Fleshly Israel

Holbach notes a crucial semantic shift in the [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian reinterpretation of Israel. Early [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian apologists, unable to reconcile the failure of [[bible|Old Testament]] prophecies with the continued subjugation of the Jewish people, redefined Israel to mean not the literal descendants of [[abraham]] but "the Israelites in [[soul|spirit]]: that is to say, the [[christianity|Christians]] which are the Israel of [[god|God]], the true chosen people" (Ch. 122). This act of reinterpretation — turning a fleshly promise into a [[soul|spirit]]ual one — reveals, for Holbach, the fundamental dishonesty of theological reasoning: when prophecies fail to materialize, the meaning is simply changed rather than the prophecy abandoned.

## Critique and Legacy

Holbach's treatment of Israel participates in a broader Enlightenment critique of biblical history. By treating Israel as a human [[nations|nation]] whose [[scripture|sacred texts]] are products of political editing rather than [[revelation|divine revelation]], he anticipates the methods of later biblical criticism. His argument is ultimately about the psychology of [[belief]]: the Israelites believed themselves chosen because their [[clergy|priests]] told them so, and this belief — however factually unfounded — proved powerful enough to sustain a [[nations|nation]]al identity across millennia. The case of Israel thus [[demon|demons]]trates, for Holbach, that the most durable political formations are those that successfully merge [[nations|nation]]al identity with religious mythology.

## Reception and Critique

Holbach's treatment of Israel as a human [[nations|nation]] whose sacred history is politically constructed participates in a critical tradition that runs from [[baruch-spinoza|Spinoza]]'s *Tractatus Theologico-Politicus* (1670) through to modern biblical scholarship. [[baruch-spinoza|Spinoza]] had already argued that the Hebrew [[bible|Bible]] was a human document produced by multiple authors in specific historical circumstances, and Holbach applies this method to the doctrine of [[predestination|election]]. The claim that Israel is the "most stupid, the most credulous, the most savage, the most unsocial [[nations|nation]]" (Ch. 121) is characteristic of the Enlightenment's polemical style, but it also reflects a genuine analytical insight: that the moral character attributed to [[god|God]] in the Hebrew [[bible|Bible]] — jealous, vindictive, tribal — mirrors the social conditions of the people who first worshipped him. Later scholars such as Julius Wellhausen and Max Weber would develop this insight into systematic accounts of the relationship between Israelite religion and Israelite social structure. Holbach's contribution is to have recognized, a century earlier, that the biblical narrative is not a record of [[divinity|divine]] action but a projection of human political experience.
