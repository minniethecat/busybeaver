# Judaism

## Definition

Judaism, in Baron d'Holbach's critical genealogy of religion, occupies an ambivalent position: it is simultaneously the parent of [[christianity|Christianity]] and the living refutation of [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian claims to uniqueness. Holbach treats Judaism not as a [[divinity|divine]]ly revealed precursor to the [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian dispensation but as a human artifact — the tribal religion of a "handful of unfortunate [[existence|being]]s" (002-277) whose [[bible|scripture]]s, laws, and prophecies bear all the marks of their provincial origin. By exposing Judaism's human character, Holbach aims to undermine [[christianity|Christianity]]'s claim to represent its [[divinity|divine]]ly ordained fulfillment.

## Historical Context

Holbach's analysis of Judaism must be understood against the background of eighteenth-century debates about the relationship between the Old and [[bible|New Testament]]s. Orthodox [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian [[religion|theology]] maintained that Judaism was a [[divinity|divine]]ly instituted preparation for [[christianity|Christianity]] — that the Hebrew [[prophecy|prophet]]s foretold [[jesus-christ|Christ]], that Mosaic law prefigured [[gospel|the Gospel]], and that the [[israel|Jewish people]]'s rejection of Jesus was a tragic but prophesied apostasy. This "typological" reading of the Hebrew [[bible|Bible]] had been the church's [[model|standard]] hermeneutic [[guilt|sin]]ce the patristic era.

By Holbach's time, this framework was under attack from multiple directions. Deists like John Toland and [[matthew|Matthew]] Tindal argued that the [[bible|Old Testament]] contained no clear prophecies of [[jesus-christ|Christ]] and that [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian typological readings were arbitrary impositions on the Hebrew text. More radical critics — [[baruch-spinoza|Spinoza]] above all — had suggested that the Pentateuch was not written by [[moses|Moses]] but compiled by later scribes, and that the Hebrew religion was a political instrument for governing an ancient Near Eastern people. Holbach synthesizes these critiques into a popular polemic: Judaism is not [[revelation|divine revelation]] but a human institution, and [[christianity|Christianity]] is not its fulfillment but its derivative.

## Core Arguments

Holbach's first argument is that Judaism, as a tribal religion, is incompatible with the idea of a universal [[god|God]]. If [[god|God]] intended a universal religion, why would He reveal Himself exclusively to "a handful of unfortunate [[existence|being]]s" in a remote corner of the ancient [[universe|world]] while leaving "the most flourishing [[nation|nations]]" — China, [[india|India]], Greece, [[rome|Rome]] — in ignorance? The particularity of Jewish revelation is, for Holbach, [[evidence|proof]] of its human origin: a universal [[god|God]] would produce a universal revelation; a local revelation implies a local, human authorship.

His second argument concerns the moral and intellectual quality of the Hebrew [[bible|scripture]]s. Holbach presents the [[bible|Old Testament]] as a collection of "fabulous tales," "superstitious practices," and "senseless distinctions" concerning pure and impure [[soul|anima]]ls, sacrifices, and purifications — "no more respectable than those of the most idolatrous [[nation|nations]]." The [[god|God]] of the Hebrew [[bible|Bible]] is jealous, vindictive, and partial; His laws include "the most horrible" pro[[dreams|visions]]; and His chosen people are depicted as stubborn, rebellious, and repeatedly punished. A [[god|God]] who cannot manage His own chosen [[nations|nation]] without constant violence and coercion is not a [[god|God]] worthy of worship.

Third, Holbach argues that [[christianity|Christianity]]'s dependence on Judaism is logically fatal to [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian claims. "[[christianity|Christianity]] is an impiety, if it is true that Judaism as a religion really emanated from a Holy, Immutable, [[almighty|Almighty]], and Foreseeing [[god|God]]." If the Mosaic law was perfect and [[divinity|divine]]ly given, [[jesus-christ|Christ]]'s abrogation of it implies either that [[god|God]] changed His mind (inconstancy), that the original law was defective (impotence), or that [[jesus-christ|Christ]]'s reforms were illegitimate (impiety).

## Reception and Critique

Holbach's treatment of Judaism has been the subject of substantial scholarly debate, particularly regarding its relationship to the broader Enlightenment discourse on Jews and Judaism. Jonathan [[israel|Israel]] (*Radical Enlightenment*, 2001) argues that Holbach's critique, while harsh, belongs to a tradition of Radical Enlightenment thought that was fundamentally anti-clerical and anti-theological rather than anti-Semitic — its target was the religious claims made on the basis of the Hebrew [[bible|scripture]]s, not the [[israel|Jewish people]] as an ethnic or [[worship|cult]]ural group. Holbach's praise of [[baruch-spinoza|Spinoza]], a excommunicated Jew, as a model of rational [[morality|virtue]] supports this reading.

Other scholars are more critical. [[adam|Adam]] Sutcliffe (*Judaism and Enlightenment*, 2003) notes that Holbach inherits and reproduces [[voltaire|Voltaire]]'s deeply negative characterization of the ancient Hebrews as a barbarous and superstitious people, [[eve]]n while he differs from [[voltaire|Voltaire]] in not extending this critique to a racial theory of Jewish character. Leon Poliakov (*The History of Anti-Semitism*, Vol. 3, 1975) argues that Enlightenment critiques of Judaism, including Holbach's, supplied secular rationalizations for anti-Jewish [[opinion|prejudice]] that persisted into the modern era, [[eve]]n where the authors' intentions were anti-religious rather than anti-Semitic.

A distinct line of critique concerns Holbach's historical method. His treatment of Judaism relies entirely on the biblical text as transmitted by [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian tradition; he makes no reference to rabbinic literature, medieval Jewish philosophy, or the lived experience of Jewish communities in early modern [[europe|Europe]]. Judaism for Holbach is not a living tradition but a textual object to be refuted — a limitation that modern scholars of religion consider a significant methodological weakness in Enlightenment critiques of religion more generally.

## Relation to Other Concepts

Judaism occupies the root position in Holbach's genealogy of [[error|religious error]], ma[[monarch|king]] it foundational to several other concepts in his system. It directly generates [[christianity]], which Holbach presents as a reformed Judaism that retains its parent's fatal flaws — exclusivism, scriptural authority, and the claim to unique [[divinity|divine]] favor — while adding new [[absurdity|absurd]]ities ([[incarnation|Incarnation]], [[trinity|Trinity]]) of its own. The concept of the [[chosen-people]] originates in the Hebrew [[bible|scripture]]s and exemplifies the particularist logic that Holbach regards as incompatible with a truly universal [[god|deity]]. Judaism's [[bible|scripture]]s provide the primary target for Holbach's analysis of the [[bible]] as a human document, and its legal-moral system provides the foundation for his critique of [[divine-law]] as an incoherent concept. The Hebrew [[prophecy|prophet]]s serve as a case study in [[prophecy]] — human claims of [[divinity|divine]] communication that are indistinguishable from delusion or imposture. Judaism's historical trajectory — from tribal [[worship|cult]] to imperial religion to persecuted minority — illustrates the broader argument that [[religion|religious system]]s are shaped by political and social conditions, not [[divinity|divine]] guidance.

## In the Text

Holbach addresses Judaism most pointedly in chapter CXXXI of *Common Sense*:

> "Christianity is an impiety, if it is true that Judaism as a religion really emanated from a Holy, Immutable, Almighty, and Foreseeing God. Christ's religion implies either defects in the law that God Himself gave by Moses, or impotence or malice in this God who could not, or would not make the Jews as they ought to be to please Him." (002-256)

The trilemma is designed to be inescapable: whichever horn the [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian apologist chooses — defective law, impotent [[god|God]], or malicious [[god|God]] — the conclusion is incompatible with orthodox [[religion|theology]].

In chapter CLVII, Holbach extends the critique historically:

> "Christianity, offshoot of Judaism, which was very humble in its obscure origin, became powerful and cruel under the Christian emperors, who, driven by a holy zeal, spread it marvelously in their empire by sword and fire."

Judaism is presented as the root from which [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian [[persecution|intolerance]] grows: the exclusivism, the claim to unique [[divinity|divine]] favor, and the impulse to coerce the unbeliever all have their origin in the Hebrew [[bible|scripture]]s.
