# Materialism

## Definition

Materialism, as defended in Holbach's "Common Sense" (1772), is the thesis that [[eve]]rything which exists is material in [[universe|nature]] and that all phenomena — including thought, consciousness, and what is called "soul" — are effects of matter in motion. Holbach rejects the dualistic distinction between [[soul|spirit]] and matter, arguing that "all which exists springs from the bosom of matter" (ch. XXII) and that the concept of an [[spirit|immaterial substance]] is incoherent.

## Historical Context

Materialism in 18th-century [[paris|France]] emerged from the scientific revolution of the preceding century. [[rene-descartes|Descartes]]' mechanical philosophy, despite his own dualism, had opened the door to materialist expla[[nation|nations]] of [[universe|nature]]. By Holbach's time, thinkers such as La Mettrie ("L'Homme Machine," 1747) had argued that mental processes were wholly dependent on bodily states. Holbach systematized these insights into a comprehensive philosophical position, applying them specifically to the critique of religious claims about the soul, [[liberty|free will]], and the [[death|afterlife]].

## Core Arguments

Holbach presents several key arguments for [[matter|materialism]]:

1. **The primacy of matter**: "All which exists springs from the bosom of matter" (ch. XXII). Everything we observe is material; the postulation of an [[spirit|immaterial substance]] adds nothing to expla[[nations|nation]].

2. **The dependence of thought on body**: [[thought|Thought]] arises from the organization of matter, not from an independent "soul." The "most simple movements of our bodies are, for [[eve]]ry thin[[monarch|king]] man, enigmas as diffi[[worship|cult]] to [[divinity|divine]] as thought" (ch. CVI).

3. **The problem with [[soul|spirit]]ual substance**: Theologians give superiority to "[[soul|spirit]]" over matter only because they cannot define [[soul|spirit]]. "The contempt which our metaphysicians show for matter, comes from the fact that familiarity breeds contempt" (ch. CVI).

4. **[[matter|Matter]] is self-moving**: "The [[universe|world]] has not been created, and matter moves by itself" (ch. XXXIX). [[motion|Motion]] is inherent to matter, not imparted by an external agent.

## Reception and Critique

Critics charged that [[matter|materialism]] "makes of man a mere machine, which is considered very debasing to the human race" (105-001). They argued that without belief in an [[soul|immortal soul]], humans would descend into despair and moral chaos. Holbach countered that the supposed [[hope|consolation]]s of [[soul|spirit]]ual doctrine were illusory: "Is not the idea of total [[death|annihilation]] infinitely preferable to the idea of an eternal existence accompanied with [[evil|suffering]] and gnashing of teeth?" (ch. CVIII). He further argued that the churches needed immaterial souls to populate their imaginary [[hope|heaven]]s and [[punishment|hell]]s, drawing profit from the hopes and fears of the credulous.

## Relation to Other Concepts

Materialism is the metaphysical foundation of Holbach's [[Atheism]]. It grounds his rejection of the [[Soul]] as an independent substance, his critique of [[Free Will]] as an "idle [[imagination|fancy]]" (ch. LXXX), and his naturalistic account of [[Nature]] as a self-sufficient system requiring no creator.

## In the Text

Holbach defends [[matter|materialism]] in a sustained sequence of chapters (CV–CVIII). He w[[ritual|rites]]:

> "Materialism, it is objected, makes of man a mere machine, which is considered very debasing to the human race. But will the human race be more honored when it can be said that man acts by the secret impulsions of a spirit, or a certain something which animates him without his knowing how?" (105-001)

On the theological motivation for im[[matter|materialism]]:

> "Theologians, no doubt, have had reasons for making the soul immaterial. They needed souls and chimeras to populate the imaginary regions which they have discovered in the other life." (107-003)
