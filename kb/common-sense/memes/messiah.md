# Messiah

## Holbach's Treatment

Holbach addresses the concept of the **Messiah** — the promised deliverer of Jewish and Christian tradition — with characteristic skepticism in *Common Sense*. He notes that Pascal's influential argument for religious belief invokes the Messiah: "The Messiah, according to Pascal, [See Thoughts of Pascal]" (002-343). But Holbach dismisses this appeal to authority, treating Pascal's reasoning as circular — one must already believe in the Scriptures to accept their messianic prophecies as evidence. Holbach further observes that claims to messianic identity were historically common: among those who "claimed to be the true promised Messiah" was "a certain Judas of Galilee" (APP007-008), alongside others. The proliferation of messianic claimants demonstrates, for Holbach, that the concept of a promised deliverer is a recurring human fantasy, not a unique divine revelation — and that the identification of any particular figure as the Messiah is a matter of historical circumstance rather than theological truth.


## Selected Passage

Holbach challenges Pascal's exalted vision of the Messiah's transformative power:

> If, closing our eyes upon all that transpires in this world, we should rely upon the votaries of the Christian religion, we would believe that the coming of our Divine Saviour has produced the most wonderful revolution and the most complete reform in the morals of nations. The Messiah, according to Pascal, [See Thoughts of Pascal] ought of Himself alone to produce a great, select, and holy people; conducting and nourishing it, and introducing it into the place of repose and sanctity, rendering it holy to God, making it the temple of God, saving it from the wrath of God, delivering it from the servitude of sin, giving laws to this people, engraving these laws upon their hearts, offering Himself to God for them, crushing the head of the serpent, etc. This great man has forgotten to show us the people upon whom His Divine Messiah has produced the miraculous effects of which He speaks with so much emphasis; so far, it seems, they do not exist upon the earth!
>
> (002-343)
