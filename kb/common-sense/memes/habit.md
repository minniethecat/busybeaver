# Habit

## Definition

Holbach's analysis of habit is one of his most original contributions to the psychology of religion. Religious belief, he argues, is not primarily maintained by argument or [[proof|evidence]] but by "a rooted habit, which, ma[[monarch|king]] them close their eyes against the most stri[[monarch|king]] truths, causes them to fall back, in spite of themselves, into their ancient [[prejudice|prejudices]]" (ch. CX). Habit operates below the level of conscious reasoning, ma[[monarch|king]] beliefs feel natural and inevitable [[eve]]n when they are groundless.

The mechanism is straightforward: "He who from his childhood has had a habit of trembling [[eve]]ry time he heard certain words, has need of these words, and feels himself uneasy when he is prevented from hearing them" (ch. XIII). Religious language — the names of [[god|God]], the formulas of prayer, the rituals of worship — conditions the mind through repetition to respond with reverence and fear before it is capable of rational evaluation. By the time the believer reaches adulthood, the habit is so deeply rooted that "reason can do nothing against habit, which becomes, as is said, a second [[universe|nature]]" (ch. CX).

This analysis has radical implications. If [[belief|religious belief]] is maintained by habit rather than reason, then the theologian's elaborate [[evidence|proof]]s and arguments are not the real foundation of [[religion|faith]] — they are post-hoc rationalizations of beliefs acquired through childhood conditioning. The target of critique shifts from theological arguments (which can be refuted) to the psychological mechanisms that make arguments seem unnecessary. Brea[[monarch|king]] the hold of religion requires brea[[monarch|king]] the habit — exposing the mind to rational inquiry until "second [[universe|nature]]" is replaced by first [[universe|nature]].

## Historical Context

Holbach's treatment of habit draws on the empiricist psychology of his era. [[john-locke|Locke]] had analyzed the association of ideas — how two ideas repeatedly experienced together come to seem necessarily connected. [[david-hume|Hume]] expanded this into a general theory of mental association, arguing that custom and habit, not reason, are "the great guide of human life." Holbach applies this empiricist psychology specifically to [[belief|religious belief]], ma[[monarch|king]] explicit what [[david-hume|Hume]] had left implicit.

The ancient skeptics had also identified habit as a source of belief. Sextus Empiricus listed "custom and law" among the criteria by which people actually live, as opposed to philosophical criteria of truth. [[montaigne|Montaigne]], drawing on this tradition, had observed that "we are [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ians by the same title that we are Perigordins or Germans" — by the [[chance|accident]] of birth and upbringing, not by rational conviction.

Holbach's innovation is to treat habit not as a neutral psychological mechanism but as a tool of social control. [[priest|Priest]]s and [[monarch|ruler]]s "take advantage of the [[belief|credulity]]" (ch. XI) of the people by implanting religious habits in childhood, knowing that these habits will resist rational correction throughout adult life. The critique of habit is thus a political critique: [[belief|religious belief]] persists not despite its [[absurdity|irrationality]] but because of it, [[vice|sin]]ce irrational beliefs are the hardest to dislodge.

## Core Arguments

**1. Habit, not argument, is the foundation of [[belief|religious belief]].** [[person-index|People]] do not arrive at [[religion|faith]] through rational inquiry; they absorb it "from childhood" (ch. XIII) through repetition, ritual, and [[passion|emotion]]al conditioning. By the time they can reason, the habit is already fixed.

**2. Habit makes contradiction invisible.** "[[reason|Reason]] can do nothing against habit" (ch. CX) because habit operates pre-rationally. A believer can accept contradictory doctrines without discomfort because habit has made the contradictions familiar and therefore unnoticed.

**3. Habit is the mechanism of intellectual servitude.** "[[credulity|Credulity]], preserved by habit, which never reasons, and revered solely because it has never been examined" (ch. VII) — the church maintains power not by winning arguments but by preventing them from occurring. Habit is the barrier that keeps the mind from [[eve]]n as[[monarch|king]] the questions that would expose error.

**4. Brea[[monarch|king]] habit is the first step toward intellectual [[liberty|freedom]].** The cure for superstition is not argument alone but the disruption of habitual [[model|pattern]]s of thought. "Let the human mind occupy itself with natural things, intelligible objects, sensible truths, and [[knowledge|useful knowledge]]" (ch. I) — replacing religious habit with the habit of rational inquiry.

## Relation to Other Concepts

Habit is the psychological mechanism that sustains [[Credulity]] and prevents the exercise of [[Reason]]. It is the instrument through which [[Superstition]] is transmitted across generations. Habit explains why [[Faith]] persists in the face of [[Contradiction]] and why [[Proof]] seems unnecessary to believers. The brea[[monarch|king]] of intellectual habit is the precondition for acquiring [[Knowledge]]. Habit is related to [[Education]] as cause to cure — only systematic education in rational inquiry can displace the habits implanted by religious upbringing.
