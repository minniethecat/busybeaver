# Essence

## Definition

Essence, in the theological context that Holbach critiques, refers to the fundamental [[universe|nature]] or "what-it-is" of a [[existence|being]] — particularly the "essence of God" that theologians claim is both incomprehensible to [[reason|human reason]] and yet the most important object of human knowledge. Holbach exposes this as a fatal contradiction: "[[eve]]rybody tells us that the essence of God is incomprehensible to man; at the same time they do not hesitate to assign [[attribute|attributes]] to this incomprehensible [[god|God]], and assure us that man can not dispense with a knowledge of this [[god|God]] so impossible to conceive of" (002-005). If the divine essence is unknowable, [[eve]]ry theological claim about [[god|God]] is groundless; if it is knowable, theologians must explain how limited minds access [[infinite|the infinite]].

## Historical Context

The concept of essence has roots in Aristotelian metaphysics, where it denotes the set of properties that make a thing what it is, as distinct from its [[chance|accident]]al [[attribute|attributes]]. Medieval scholasticism, particularly Thomas Aquinas, developed an elaborate [[religion|theology]] of divine essence — [[god|God]]'s essence *is* His existence (*ipsum esse subsistens*), and all [[attribute|divine attributes]] (omnipotence, omni[[knowledge|science]], etc.) are identical with the divine essence. Holbach, writing in the empiricist tradition of [[john-locke|Locke]] and drawing on [[baruch-spinoza|Spinoza]]'s critique of anthropomorphic [[religion|theology]], rejected the entire framework. For Holbach, an "essence" without sensory content was an "idea without a [[model|prototype]]" — a chimera.

## Core Arguments

**1. The incomprehensibility of divine essence makes [[religion|theology]] impossible.** "If [[god|God]] is incomprehensible to man, it would seem rational never to think of Him at all; but religion concludes that man is criminal if he ceases for a moment to revere Him" (005-002). The most important thing is the most impossible to comprehend — a paradox that reveals [[religion|theology]]'s [[absurdity|irrationality]].

**2. Claims about divine essence are incoherent.** "All our ideas are but pictures of objects which strike us. What can the idea of [[god|God]] represent to us when it is evidently an idea without an object? Is not such an idea as impossible as an effect without a cause? An idea without a [[model|prototype]], is it anything but a chimera?" (002-003).

**3. The infinite essence cannot relate to the finite.** "If [[god|God]] is infinite, no finite [[existence|being]] can have communication or any relation with Him. Where there are no relations, there can be no union, no correspondence, no duties. If there are no duties between man and his [[god|God]], there exists no religion for man" (007-001). Infinity annihilates religion by ma[[monarch|king]] [[divinity|divine]]-human relation impossible.

**4. The innateness claim fails.** Some theologians assert the idea of [[god|God]] is innate. Holbach counters: "Every principle is a judgment; all judgment is the effect of experience; experience is not acquired but by the exercise of the senses: from which it follows that religious principles are drawn from nothing, and are not innate" (004-001).

**5. Essence claims are projections.** What theologians call "divine essence" is merely human qualities magnified and projected onto an imaginary [[existence|being]]. The [[attribute|attributes]] assigned to [[god|God]] — will, intelligence, goodness — are human [[attribute|attributes]]; to claim they exist in an incomprehensible [[existence|being]] is to speak without meaning.

## Relation to Other Concepts

Essence is closely tied to the concepts of [[God]], [[Attribute]], and [[Infinite]]. The critique of essence supports Holbach's [[Materialism]]: only material [[existence|being]]s with sensible properties have knowable essences. The theological misuse of "essence" exemplifies the broader [[model|pattern]] of [[Error]] and [[Superstition]]. The concept stands in opposition to [[Experience]] and [[Reason]], which demand that ideas be grounded in sensory [[proof|evidence]].

## In the Text

On the contradiction of the incomprehensible essence:

> "Everybody tells us that the essence of God is incomprehensible to man; at the same time they do not hesitate to assign attributes to this incomprehensible God, and assure us that man can not dispense with a knowledge of this God so impossible to conceive of. The most important thing for men is that which is the most impossible for them to comprehend." (005-002)

On the impossibility of ideas without sensory objects:

> "All our ideas are but pictures of objects which strike us. What can the idea of God represent to us when it is evidently an idea without an object? Is not such an idea as impossible as an effect without a cause? An idea without a prototype, is it anything but a chimera?" (004-001)

On [[infinite|the infinite]] essence annihilating religion:

> "If God is infinite, no finite being can have communication or any relation with Him... Thus by saying that God is infinite, you annihilate, from that moment, all religion for man, who is a finite being." (007-001)
