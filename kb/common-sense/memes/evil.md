# Evil

## Definition

In Holbach's "Common Sense," [[injustice|evil]] encompasses both moral evil (human cruelty, injustice, and vice) and natural evil (disease, disaster, and suffering). Rather than treating [[injustice|evil]] as a theological puzzle to be solved — the classic "problem of [[injustice|evil]]" that challenges belief in an all-powerful, all-good [[god|God]] — Holbach deploys the sheer pervasiveness of [[injustice|evil]] as a positive argument *against* the existence of any such [[existence|being]]. "We shall always see [[injustice|evil]], imperfections, and follies resulting from a cause claimed to be full of goodness, of perfections, and of [[philosophy|wisdom]]" (ch. II). The fact of [[injustice|evil]] is not a [[secrecy|mystery]] to be accepted on [[religion|faith]]; it is [[proof|evidence]] to be weighed in the scales of reason.

## Historical Context

The problem of [[injustice|evil]] had been debated [[vice|sin]]ce antiquity. [[epicurus|Epicurus]] formulated it with devastating clarity: if [[god|God]] is willing to prevent [[injustice|evil]] but unable, he is not omnipotent; if able but unwilling, he is malevolent; if both able and willing, whence comes [[injustice|evil]]? [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian [[religion|theology]] responded with [[injustice|theodicy]] — attempts to justify [[god|God]]'s goodness despite [[injustice|evil]] — most influentially in Augustine ([[injustice|evil]] as privation of good) and [[gottfried-leibniz|Leibniz]] (this is the "best of all possible [[universe|world]]s"). The [[lisbon|Lisbon]] earthquake of 1755 shattered [[gottfried-leibniz|Leibniz]]ian optimism for many Enlightenment thinkers; [[voltaire|Voltaire]]'s *Candide* satirized it mercilessly. Holbach goes further than [[voltaire|Voltaire]]: he does not merely mock [[injustice|theodicy]] but uses [[injustice|evil]] as a [[proof|logical proof]] *against* the [[attribute|divine attributes]], arguing that a [[universe|world]] containing so much suffering cannot possibly be the product of a [[existence|being]] that is simultaneously all-powerful, all-knowing, and all-good.

## Core Arguments

**1. Evil is incompatible with [[perfection|divine perfection]].** Holbach presents a trilemma: if [[god|God]] is all-good, He would [[desire|want]] to prevent [[injustice|evil]]; if all-powerful, He could prevent it; if all-knowing, He would know of it. Evil exists. Therefore at least one [[divinity|divine]] attribute must be false. "A cause can not be reputed as constantly good, except when it constantly produces good, useful, and agreeable effects. A cause which produces good at one time, and [[injustice|evil]] at another, is a cause which is sometimes good and sometimes bad" (002-073).

**2. The scale of [[injustice|evil]] dwarfs the scale of good.** Against the theological claim that good outweighs [[injustice|evil]] in [[earth|the world]], Holbach presses the empirical case: "Man's cup is full of joy and of bitterness; [[eve]]rywhere good is side by side with [[injustice|evil]]; order is replaced by disorder; generation is followed by destruction" (ch. II). Even if good predominated one hundred to ten, a [[god|God]] with "a tenth degree of malignity" would still be imperfect.

**3. Religious expla[[nation|nations]] of [[injustice|evil]] are evasions.** The claim that "the designs of [[god|God]] are [[mysteries]]" is not an expla[[nations|nation]] but an admission of failure. If we cannot understand [[god|God]]'s purposes, we have no basis for calling Him good. "It would be to judge, to admire, to adore that of which we can form no idea" (ch. II).

**4. [[religion|Religion]] itself is a major source of [[injustice|evil]].** Holbach argues that [[persecution|religious persecution]], war, and the corruption of reason are not aberrations but direct consequences of theological belief. "Never do we do [[injustice|evil]] so thoroughly and so willingly as when we do it through a false principle of con[[knowledge|science]]" (ch. CLXIX, quoting [[pascal|Pascal]]). Religious conviction licenses cruelties that secular [[interest|self-interest]] would never justify.

## Reception and Critique

Theologians responded that Holbach's argument begged the question by assuming human [[model|standard]]s of goodness apply to [[god|God]] — a critique Holbach anticipated and rejected. If [[goodness|divine goodness]] is incommensurable with human goodness, then calling [[god|God]] "good" is meaningless. Later philosophers have nuanced the problem: [[david-hume|Hume]]'s *Dialogues Concerning Natural [[religion|Religion]]* (published posthumously in 1779) developed similar arguments with greater philosophical sophistication, while contemporary formulations distinguish between the "logical" problem of [[injustice|evil]] ([[contradiction|inconsistency]]) and the "evidential" problem (improbability), the latter of which remains a live philosophical debate.

## Relation to Other Concepts

Evil is the central empirical datum in Holbach's case against [[God]] and [[Theology]]. It is the consequence of [[Ignorance]] and [[Superstition]] when they govern human conduct, and the thing that [[Reason]] and [[Justice]] exist to mitigate. The problem of [[injustice|evil]] connects directly to Holbach's critique of [[Providence]] — if [[god|God]]'s providence watches over human happiness, why is [[earth|the world]] filled with misery? — and to his defense of secular [[Morality]], which addresses [[injustice|evil]] through human effort rather than [[divinity|divine]] intervention. Holbach also connects [[injustice|evil]] to [[Sin]], arguing that the theological concept of [[vice|sin]] creates guilt without addressing the real causes of human suffering.

## In the Text

Holbach states the core incompatibility with characteristic bluntness:

> "We shall always see evil, imperfections, and follies resulting from a cause claimed to be full of goodness, of perfections, and of wisdom." (ch. II)

On the quantitative argument against [[goodness|divine goodness]]:

> "Let us suppose that the blessings which this Providence makes us enjoy are as one hundred, and that the evils are as ten per cent.; would it not always result that against these hundred degrees of goodness, Providence possesses a tenth degree of malignity? — which is incompatible with the perfection we attribute to it." (ch. II)

On religion as a source of [[injustice|evil]]:

> "Never do we do evil so thoroughly and so willingly as when we do it through a false principle of conscience." (ch. CLXIX, quoting Pascal)

On the empirical reality of suffering:

> "Is not mankind the continual victim of physical and moral evils? This human machine, which is shown to us as the masterpiece of the Creator's industry, has it not a thousand ways of deranging itself?" (ch. II)
