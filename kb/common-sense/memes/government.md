# Government

## Definition

Holbach's political philosophy is grounded in a contractual conception of government: political authority derives not from [[divinity|divine]] grant but from the consent and welfare of the governed. "[[sovereign|Sovereign]]s, it is true, call themselves the representatives of [[god|God]], His lieutenants upon earth." But this claim is not a justification of their authority — it is a usurpation of it. The true function of government is to protect the lives, liberties, and property of its subjects, and any government that fails in this function, or that actively oppresses its people, has forfeited its legitimacy.

Holbach's critique of government focuses on two interrelated corruptions. First, the doctrine of [[divinity|divine]] right transforms [[monarch|king]]s into "Divinities, whose caprices the [[nation|nations]] never dare to resist." Second, the alliance between throne and altar — between political and [[authority|religious authority]] — creates a system of mutual reinforcement in which [[clergy|priests]] sanctify [[despotism|tyranny]] and tyrants enforce orthodoxy. The result is that "almost [[eve]]rywhere, men are governed by tyrants, who make use of religion but to brutalize their slaves."

## Historical Context

Holbach wrote in the last decades of the *ancien régime*, when the French [[monarch|monarchy]] still claimed [[divinity|divine]] right and the [[catholic-church|Catholic Church]] was woven into the fabric of the state. The political theory of [[tyranny|absolutism]], developed by Bodin in the 16th century and refined by Bossuet under Louis XIV, held that [[monarch|king]]s derived their authority directly from [[god|God]] and were accountable only to Him — not to their subjects, not to parliaments, and not to law.

[[enlightenment|The Enlightenment]] offered a competing theory: the social contract. [[hobbes|Hobbes]] had argued that sovereignty arose from the people's consent (however irrevocable once given). [[john-locke|Locke]] had argued that government was a trust that could be revoked when the trustee violated its terms. Rousseau was developing his theory of the general will. Holbach's contribution is to bring the anticlerical critique to bear on political theory: the problem is not only that [[monarch|king]]s claim [[divinity|divine]] right, but that religion provides the ideological apparatus that makes this claim credible and that disarms resistance.

## Core Arguments

**1. [[theology-index|Divine]] right is a [[imposture|fraud]] that enables [[despotism|tyranny]].** The claim that [[monarch|king]]s rule by [[divinity|divine]] appointment serves to place them beyond accountability. If a [[monarch|king]] is answerable only to [[god|God]], and if [[clergy|priests]] (who alone interpret [[god|God]]'s will) can always be bought or intimidated, then there is no earthly check on royal power. "Far from [[existence|being]] a restraint to the passions of [[monarch|king]]s, religion, by its very principles, gives them a loose rein. It transforms them into Divinities."

**2. The alliance of throne and altar oppresses the people.** Holbach describes a system in which [[clergy|priests]] teach the people that resistance to [[despotism|tyranny]] is resistance to [[god|God]], while [[monarch|king]]s enforce [[church|the Church]]'s monopoly on salvation. This alliance is not incidental but structural: each institution needs the other. Kings need religion to sanctify their authority; [[clergy|priests]] need state power to enforce their [[dogma|dogmas]] and collect their tithes.

**3. Government should serve human welfare, not [[divinity|divine]] glory.** The purpose of government is this-[[universe|world]]ly: to protect life, liberty, and property; to administer justice; to promote the general welfare. A government that sacrifices its people's happiness to the "glory of [[god|God]]" — or to the glory of the [[monarch|king]], which religion conflates with [[divinity|divine]] glory — has inverted the proper order of political values.

**4. The social contract is the only legitimate basis of authority.** Following [[john-locke|Locke]] and anticipating the revolutionaries of 1789, Holbach argues that political authority rests on consent. When "men become exasperated, the [[rights|divine rights]] of tyrants are compelled to yield to the [[rights|natural rights]] of their subjects." The people have "better market with the gods than with men" — meaning that when [[evil|suffering]] becomes intolerable, [[eve]]n religious sanctions cannot prevent revolt.

## Reception and Critique

Holbach's political thought was, in his lifetime, too radical to publish openly. *Common Sense* and *The System of [[nature|Nature]]* were published anonymously and circulated clandestinely. After [[revolution|the French Revolution]], his ideas — particularly the critique of the throne-altar alliance — became mainstream in republican political thought, though his a[[divinity|theism]] remained controversial.

Contemporary political philosophers have criticized the social contract tradition on several grounds: its historical inaccuracy (there was never an actual contract), its atomistic individualism, and its neglect of the ways in which political identity is shaped by communities, traditions, and institutions that are not chosen. Holbach would likely concede the historical point while insisting on the normative one: whatever the origins of government, its legitimacy depends on its conduct.

## Relation to Other Concepts

Government is the political expression of [[Authority]], which becomes [[Tyranny]] when unchecked by consent and reason. It is corrupted by its alliance with [[Religion]] and the [[Priesthood]], which provide ideological cover for [[persecution|oppression]]. Good government requires [[Reason]] in the [[monarch|sovereign]] and [[Education]] in the people — without an educated public, there is no check on power. The purpose of government is to secure [[Liberty]] and [[Happiness]], not to enforce religious orthodoxy. Government's legitimacy derives from the consent of the governed, not from [[God]] or the claims of [[divinity|divine]] right.

## In the Text

On the corruption of government by religion:

> "Sovereigns, it is true, call themselves the representatives of God, His lieutenants upon earth. But does the fear of a more powerful master than themselves make them attend to the welfare of the peoples that Providence has confided to their care? ... Far from being a restraint to the passions of kings, religion, by its very principles, gives them a loose rein. It transforms them into Divinities, whose caprices the nations never dare to resist. At the same time that it unchains princes and breaks for them the ties of the social pact, it enchains the minds and the hands of their oppressed subjects." (143-002)

On the natural limits of [[divinity|divine]] right:

> "The people, when reduced to despair, consider the divine rights of their chiefs as an abuse. When men become exasperated, the divine rights of tyrants are compelled to yield to the natural rights of their subjects; they have better market with the gods than with men." (002-292)

On the function of good government:

> "It is the duty of sovereigns to watch over the education of their subjects, to encourage useful arts, to reward talents, to repress the enterprises of superstition and fanaticism, and to protect the liberty of thought." (ch. CXCVI)
