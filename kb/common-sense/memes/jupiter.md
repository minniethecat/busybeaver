# Jupiter

## Definition

Jupiter, the chief god of ancient Roman religion, appears in Holbach's *Common Sense* not as an object of belief but as a polemical device for exposing the [[contradiction|incoherence]] of modern [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian [[divinity|theism]]. Holbach's argument is strikingly counterintuitive: the pagan Jupiter, for all his mythological [[absurdity|absurd]]ity, was conceptually superior to the [[god]] of the theologians because at least Jupiter could *act*.
"The material Jupiter of the ancients could move, build up, destroy, and propagate [[existence|being]]s similar to himself; but the [[god|God]] of modern [[religion|theology]] is a sterile [[existence|being]] who can produce nothing." The pagan [[god|deity]], however crude, had material agency.
The [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian [[god|deity]], stripped by philosophy of all material [[attribute|attributes]], has become an abstraction — a "pure [[soul|spirit]]" that, by definition, can have no effect on a [[earth|material world]].

This comparison is not an endorsement of [[pagan|paganism]] but a [[proof|demonstration]] that theological "refinement" has made the concept of [[god|God]] worse, not better. The ancients at least had a god who could do something; the moderns have a god who, by the logic of their own abstractions, can do nothing at all.

## Historical Context

The comparison between pagan and [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian [[gods|deities]] was a [[model|standard]] trope of Enlightenment polemics. The deists had argued that pagan mythology was a corruption of an original pure [[religion]]; the orthodox countered that pagan gods were [[demon]]s or human inventions. Holbach's innovation was to reverse the evaluative hierarchy.
The pagan gods were [[absurdity|absurd]], but their [[absurdity|absurd]]ity was at least consistent with their materiality. The [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian [[god|God]] was incoherent because theologians had stripped him of [[eve]]ry attribute that could make the concept meaningful while retaining the [[passion|emotion]]al force of a personal [[god|deity]].

This argument drew on the emerging [[knowledge|science]] of comparative religion. Scholars like Charles de Brosses (*Du [[worship|cult]]e des dieux fétiches*, 1760) had begun to treat [[paganism|pagan religions]] not as demonic counterfeits of true religion but as human [[worship|cult]]ural products amenable to historical analysis. Holbach extends this naturalistic approach to [[christianity|Christianity]] itself, treating the virgin birth and the resurrection as structurally identical to the mythological tales of Jupiter, Minerva, and Ganymede.

## Core Arguments

**1. The pagan Jupiter had material agency; the [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian [[god|God]] has none.** "The material Jupiter of the ancients could move, build up, destroy, and propagate [[existence|being]]s similar to himself." A god who can act in [[earth|the world]] is at least a coherent concept, [[eve]]n if false. A "pure [[soul|spirit]]" who "holds [[nature|the universe]] in his hand" without having hands — who "moves matter without having movement himself" — is not a refinement but a nullity.

**2. [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian doctrines are structurally identical to pagan myths.** The birth of Minerva from Jupiter's brain — "she came out of it, all armed, by means of a blow which this god gave to his own head" — is no more [[absurdity|absurd]] than the [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian claim that [[god|God]] became man through a virgin birth. The transportation of Ganymede to [[hope|heaven]] is no more incredible than the ascension of [[jesus-christ|Christ]].
Holbach's point is not that [[pagan|paganism]] is true but that [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ians have no principled basis for accepting their own [[miracle|miracles]] while moc[[monarch|king]] the [[miracle|miracles]] of others.

**3.
The "good and great Jupiter" exposes [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian morality.** Holbach asks whether we should "imitate the good and great Jupiter of ancient [[paganism|Paganism]]" and answers with a devastating portrait: Jupiter was "a rebellious son, who wrests his father's throne from him and then mutilates him." The [[vice|immorality]] attributed to pagan gods by their own mythology is undeniable — but the [[vice|immorality]] attributed to the [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian [[god|God]] (commanding genocide, creating [[hell]], predestining souls to [[damnation]]) is greater, not lesser.
The difference is that [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ians refuse to acknowledge what their own texts record.

**4. The transition from Jupiter to the [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian [[god|God]] was a loss, not a gain.** Ancient [[nation|peoples]] conceived of their gods in material terms because they had not yet learned to disembody their abstractions. The resulting [[religion|theology]] was crude but at least it made contact with experience.
[[mode|Mode]]rn [[religion|theology]], by insisting that [[god|God]] is [[soul|spirit]] without body, parts without passions, timeless without succession — has created a concept so remote from experience that it can neither explain nor be explained by anything in [[earth|the world]].

## Reception and Critique

Holbach's use of Jupiter as a polemical device reflects the comparative method that was gaining ground in Enlightenment religious criticism. [[pierre-bayle|Pierre Bayle]], in his *Historical and Critical Dictionary* (1697), had already treated pagan and [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian theological claims as parallel phenomena amenable to the same rational scrutiny.
Bernard de Fontenelle's *De l'origine des fables* (1724) argued that myths arise [[eve]]rywhere from the same psychological sources — [[miracle|wonder]] at [[phenomena|natural phenomena]], the personification of forces, the gradual elevation of ancestors into gods.
Holbach extends this comparative logic to its most radical conclusion: not merely that pagan and [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian beliefs share a common psychological origin, but that the pagan formulation was in certain respects more coherent than the [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian one.

This argument was genuinely shoc[[monarch|king]] to eighteenth-century readers. The orthodox position held that [[pagan|paganism]] was a demonic parody of true religion — that Satan had inspired false myths in anticipation of [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian doctrine so as to discredit it in advance.
Holbach turns this framework on its head: if the [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian [[god|God]] is an immaterial [[soul|spirit]] who cannot act upon matter, while Jupiter is a material [[existence|being]] who can, then Jupiter is the more coherent concept, and [[christianity|Christianity]]'s claim to represent theological progress over [[pagan|paganism]] is precisely backward.
The [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian [[god|God]] is not a refinement of Jupiter but an evaporation of him — the same concept stripped of [[eve]]ry attribute that made it capable of interacting with [[earth|the world]].

Critics of Holbach's approach have noted that his comparison flattens important theological distinctions. The [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian doctrine of [[divinity|divine]] simplicity — that [[god|God]] is not composed of parts and does not possess [[attribute|attributes]] distinct from his essence — represents a genuinely different conceptual framework from pagan [[paganism|polytheism]], not merely a more abstract version of it.
Holbach's reduction of all [[god|deity]]-concepts to a [[guilt|sin]]gle structural type, while rhetorically effective, may oversimplify the intellectual history.
Defenders of Holbach, however, can reply that the question is not whether [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian [[religion|theology]] has sophisticated internal distinctions but whether any of them succeed in ma[[monarch|king]] the concept of an immaterial agent coherent — and on that question, Holbach's challenge remains unanswered.

## Relation to Other Concepts

Jupiter functions in Holbach's argument as the material counterpart to the abstract [[god]] of [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian [[religion|theology]].
The relationship is explicitly evaluative: the material [[god|deity]] is superior because he can act, while the immaterial [[god|deity]] is "sterile" and "can produce nothing." This connects Jupiter directly to the attribute of [[almighty]] power — the pagan god's power, however limited by mythology, is at least imaginable as a force in [[earth|the world]], whereas the [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian [[god|God]]'s alleged omnipotence is asserted without any mechanism by which it could be exercised.
The concept of [[paganism]] is not, for Holbach, a term of simple condem[[nations|nation]]. [[pagan|Pagan]] religion was superstitious, but its superstition was honest — it did not pretend to be anything other than what it was. [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian [[religion|theology]], by contrast, wraps the same anthropomorphic projections in philosophical abstractions that make them harder to critique.

The link to [[superstition]] is essential. Holbach does not exempt [[pagan|paganism]] from the charge of superstition — [[zeus]] and Jupiter are both products of the same human tendency to personify natural forces and project human passions onto the [[universe|cosmos]].
But the move from Jupiter to the [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian [[god|God]] is not a move from superstition to truth; it is a move from one form of superstition to another, and arguably to a worse form, because the abstraction makes the superstition immune to empirical correction.
The connection to the [[roman-empire]] is also significant: Jupiter was the god of a conquering empire whose political structures [[christianity|Christianity]] inherited and whose theological categories it absorbed.
The imperial [[worship|cult]] of Jupiter Optimus Maximus and the [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian [[worship|cult]] of [[god|God]] [[almighty|Almighty]] are structurally parallel attempts to place a [[guilt|sin]]gle supreme [[god|deity]] at the apex of a hierarchical [[universe|cosmos]] — and, by implication, to place a [[guilt|sin]]gle supreme [[monarch|ruler]] at the apex of a hierarchical society.

## In the Text

On the material agency of Jupiter versus the sterility of the modern [[god|God]]:

> "The material Jupiter of the ancients could move, build up, destroy, and propagate beings similar to himself; but the God of modern theology is a sterile being who can produce nothing." (023-001)

On the mythological character of [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian doctrine:

> "Minerva, goddess of the sciences, sprang from Jupiter's brain, and she came out of it, all armed, by means of a blow which this god gave to his own head. If our Christ-worshipers claim that these things are incredible and impossible, they might be driven into a corner by referring to their own miracles." (ch. XXIII ff.)

On the morality of the pagan gods:

> "Shall we imitate the good and great Jupiter of ancient Paganism? To imitate such a God would be to take as a model a rebellious son, who wrests his father's throne from him and then mutilates him." (160-002)
