# Altar

## The Altar as Political Symbol

In Holbach's *Common Sense*, the **altar** functions less as a description of ritual furniture and more as a political metonym — it stands for the institutional power of organized religion, particularly in its alliance with the state. Holbach observes that "even of the ministers at the altars, nothing is more rare than" (002-423) genuine virtue — the priests who serve at altars are, in his account, the least likely to practice the morality they preach. The altar represents the sacred authority that "broke the bondage of the altar, and severed the shackles of superstition" (APP007-026) — a liberation Holbach celebrates. But he also notes how the altar has been used to sanctify power: Abraham "builded he an altar unto the Lord" (APP004-004) as an act of obedience that Holbach finds emblematic of religion's demand that human loyalty be redirected from earthly society to an invisible master. The "throne and altar" alliance is, for Holbach, the central political fact of European civilization — and the altar is its indispensable symbolic partner.

## Selected Passage

Holbach questions the sincerity of religious practice, noting that even priests admit how rare genuine piety is:

> By the confession even of the ministers at the altars, nothing is more rare than the interior and spiritual religion, which is alone capable of regulating the life of man, and of triumphing over his inclinations.
>
> (002-423)
