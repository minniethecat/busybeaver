# Prophecy

## Definition

Prophecy, in Holbach's critical analysis, is the claim to foretell future [[eve]]nts through [[divinity|divine]] inspiration — a claim he regards as either the product of "visionaries and fanatics" acting under the impulsions of their own passions, or of deliberate "[[charlatan|impostor]]s" who exploit popular [[belief|credulity]] for power and profit. Holbach denies that any genuine prophecy exists, arguing that all purported prophecies are either vague enough to be retrospectively fitted to [[eve]]nts, manifestly false, or forged after the fact.

For Holbach, the failure of biblical prophecy is a decisive argument against the [[divinity|divine]] inspiration of [[bible|scripture]]. If [[god|God]] had truly inspired the prophets, their predictions would have been clear, specific, and fulfilled. That they are none of these things [[demon|demons]]trates their purely human origin.

## Historical Context

Prophecy was a central concern of Enlightenment religious criticism. The [[catholic-church|Catholic Church]] had traditionally cited [[bible|Old Testament]] prophecies — particularly those purportedly fulfilled in the life of Jesus — as [[evidence|proof]] of [[christianity|Christianity]]'s [[divinity|divine]] origin. The fulfillment of prophecy was one of the [[model|standard]] apologetic arguments taught in seminaries and defended in theological treatises throughout the seventeenth and eighteenth centuries.

The critique of prophecy developed in stages. [[baruch-spinoza|Spinoza]], in the *Tractatus Theologico-Politicus* (1670), argued that the biblical prophets were not philosophers but men of vivid imagi[[nations|nation]] who expressed moral truths in imaginative language. Anthony Collins's *Discourse of the Grounds and [[reason|Reason]]s of the [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian [[religion|Religion]]* (1724) subjected the argument from prophecy to rigorous logical scrutiny, arguing that allegorical interpretation made prophecy unfalsifiable. Holbach, building on both, uses Meslier's detailed biblical scholarship to subject the prophetic books to withering ridicule, treating the prophets not as inspired visionaries but as the ancient equivalent of village charlatans.

## Core Arguments

**1. Prophets are either madmen or [[charlatan|fraud]]s.** Holbach offers a stark dichotomy: biblical prophets were "visionaries and fanatics, who acted and spoke according to the impulsions of their ruling passions" or "[[charlatan|impostor]]s who feigned to be prophets, and who, in order to more easily deceive the ignorant and simple-minded, boasted of acting and spea[[monarch|king]] by the [[spirit|Spirit]] of [[god|God]]." Both expla[[nation|nations]] are naturalistic; neither requires [[divinity|divine]] intervention.

**2. Prophets denounce each other as false.** The most damning [[proof|evidence]], for Holbach, is internal to the [[bible|Bible]] itself. "All of them say, 'Beware of the false prophets!' as the [[charlatan|quack]]s say, 'Beware of the counterfeit pills!'" The mutual accusations among prophets [[demon|demons]]trate that prophecy was a contested, politicized practice — not a reliable channel of [[divinity|divine]] communication.

**3. No prophecy was [[eve]]r fulfilled.** Holbach makes the blunt empirical claim: "No prophecy in favor of their Jewish [[nations|nation]] was [[eve]]r fulfilled. The number of prophecies which predict the prosperity and the greatness of [[jerusalem|Jerusalem]] is almost innumerable." He explains these prophecies as the natural psychological compensation of a defeated people: "it is very natural that a subdued and captive people should comfort themselves in their real [[suffering|affliction]]s by imaginary hopes."

**4. Allegorical interpretation makes prophecy meaningless.** [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian apologists claim prophecies are fulfilled in a "[[soul|spirit]]ual" or allegorical sense. Holbach replies that this method can make any text "predict" anything: if we interpreted Don Quixote allegorically, we would find the same "[[mysteries]] and ridiculous figures." A claim that is unfalsifiable in principle is not a claim at all.

## In the Text

Holbach's critique of prophecy appears in Meslier's [[testament|Testament]], in the section on the [[bible|Old Testament]] (ch. V):

> "Let us see, then, who these pretended prophets are, and if we ought to consider them as important as our Christ-worshipers pretend they are. These men were but visionaries and fanatics, who acted and spoke according to the impulsions of their ruling passions, and who imagined that it was the Spirit of God by which they spoke and acted; or they were impostors who feigned to be prophets."

On the mutual accusations among prophets (ch. V):

> "What can be yet a greater proof of the falsity of these pretended prophecies, than the violence with which these prophets reproach each other for speaking falsely in the name of God, reproaches which they claim to make in behalf of God. All of them say, 'Beware of the false prophets!' as the quacks say, 'Beware of the counterfeit pills!'"

On the most clearly falsified prophecy (ch. VI):

> "The most false and the most ridiculous prophecy ever made is that of Jesus, in Luke, where it is pretended that there will be signs in the sun and in the moon, and that the Son of Man will appear in a cloud to judge men; and this is predicted for the generation living at that time. Has it come to pass? Did the Son of Man appear in a cloud?"

## Relation to Other Concepts

Prophecy is closely linked to [[bible]], the text whose authority depends on prophetic claims, and to [[revelation]], the broader category of alleged [[divinity|divine]] communication that Holbach reduces to natural causes. It connects to [[miracle]], the other major category of supernatural [[proof|evidence]] that Holbach subjects to comparative critique, and to [[superstition]], the universal human tendency to attribute natural [[eve]]nts to supernatural agency. The concept stands in opposition to [[reason]], the Enlightenment faculty that Holbach champions as the proper guide to truth.
