# Schism

## Definition

A schism is a formal division within a religious [[society|community]] — typically a church — resulting in two or more mutually hostile organizations, each claiming to be the legitimate bearer of the original tradition. Unlike heresy, which is a doctrinal deviation, schism is a rupture in institutional communion: the parties may agree on doctrine while disagreeing on authority, or they may diverge on both. Holbach treats schism as a diagnostic symptom: it reveals that [[authority|religious authority]], far from [[existence|being]] a transparent channel of [[divinity|divine]] will, is a site of irreducible human competition.

## The Mechanism of Division

Holbach's Apologue (ch. I) describes the structural conditions that make schism inevitable in any religion based on unverifiable revelation:

> "His ministers disagree among themselves in regard to the orders which they pretend emanated from the sovereign whose organs they claim to be; they announce them diversely in each province of the empire; they discredit and treat each other as impostors and liars."

The logic is straightforward: if [[revelation|divine revelation]] is inaccessible to direct verification, then [[eve]]ry claim to interpret it authoritatively is contestable. There is no procedure — no experiment, no observation, no rational deduction — that can settle a dispute between two claimants to [[divinity|divine]] authority. The result is an escalating spiral of mutual anathematization, each party denouncing the other as [[charlatan|impostor]]s while insisting on its own exclusive legitimacy.

## Major Historical Schisms

| Schism | Date | Parties | Precipitating Issue |
|--------|------|---------|-------------------|
| East-West (Great) Schism | 1054 | Roman Catholic vs. Eastern Orthodox | Papal authority, *filioque* clause |
| Western (Papal) Schism | 1378–1417 | [[rome|Rome]] vs. Avignon papacies | Legitimacy of papal [[predestination|election]] |
| [[reformation|Protestant Reformation]] | 1517–1648 | Catholic vs. Lutheran/[[reform|Reform]]ed/Anglican | Indulgences, sola scriptura, authority |
| English [[reform|Reform]]ation | 1534 | Roman Catholic vs. [[church|Church]] of England | Papal authority vs. royal supremacy |

Each of these di[[dreams|visions]] generated centuries of conflict — theological polemics, political wars, [[cruelty|persecution]]s, and executions — all conducted in the name of a [[god|God]] whose ministers "do not agree among themselves" (ch. I).

## Holbach's Diagnosis

Holbach's analysis of schism has three dimensions:

1. **Epistemological**: The root cause is the [[universe|nature]] of theological claims themselves. When "the decrees and ordinances which they promulgate are obscure; they are enigmas, made not to be understood or [[divinity|divine]]d" (ch. I), conflict over interpretation is not an [[chance|accident]] but a necessity.

2. **Sociological**: Schisms are fundamentally competitions for power, prestige, and revenue between rival elites. The doctrinal differences are often pretexts — "a tissue of contradictions" — that rationalize conflicts [[desire|drive]]n by material and institutional interests.

3. **Therapeutic**: The cure for schism is not ecumenism or doctrinal clarification but the abandonment of the premise that generates it — the belief that any human institution has privileged access to [[divinity|divine]] will. When morality is "drawn from [[universe|nature]] and not from imaginary [[existence|being]]s," the entire machinery of schism and religious conflict becomes obsolete.

## Schism as Evidence Against Revelation

Holbach deploys the fact of schism as an argument against the claim of [[revelation|divine revelation]]. A benevolent and omnipotent [[god|God]] who wished to communicate with humanity would, presumably, communicate clearly. The actual history of religion — a chronicle of "quarrels and wars, of massacres and [[cruelty|persecution]]s" (ch. II) conducted over the interpretation of obscure texts — is strong [[proof|evidence]] that no such communication has occurred. The "enigmas" of revelation are exactly what we would expect if religious texts were human products, and exactly what we would not expect if they were [[divinity|divine]] communications.

## In the Text

The Apologue captures the schismatic dynamic with precision:

> "The laws of the invisible monarch need interpreters, but those who explain them are always quarreling among themselves about the true way of understanding them." (ch. I)
