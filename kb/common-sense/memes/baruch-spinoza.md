# Baruch Spinoza

## Biography

Baruch Spinoza (1632–1677) was born into a Portuguese-Jewish family in Amsterdam. A brilliant student of Hebrew [[bible|scripture]] and philosophy, he was excommunicated from the synagogue at age twenty-three for his unorthodox views. He spent the rest of his life as a lens-grinder, living frugally while producing a body of work that transformed [[europe|Europe]]an philosophy. His *Theological-Political Treatise* (1670) was one of the first works to apply systematic rational analysis to the [[bible|Bible]], arguing that [[bible|scripture]] is a human document shaped by historical circumstances. His *Ethics*, published posthumously in 1677, presented a complete metaphysical system in geometric form, identifying [[god|God]] with [[nature|Nature]] (*Deus sive Natura*) and denying supernatural intervention.

## Role in Religious History

Spinoza occupies a unique position in religious history: he was denounced as an [[atheism|atheist]] by contemporaries, yet his philosophy is saturated with the language of [[god|God]]. His pan[[divinity|theism]] — the identification of [[god|God]] with the [[nature|natural order]] — was seen by orthodox Jews and [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ians alike as a thinly disguised [[atheist|atheism]]. The *Theological-Political Treatise* was banned throughout [[europe|Europe]], and Spinoza became synonymous with the most dangerous kind of [[atheism|irreligion]]. Yet his biblical criticism, which treated [[bible|scripture]] as a historical document to be interpreted like any other ancient text, laid the groundwork for modern hermeneutics. [[enlightenment|The Enlightenment]]'s project of subjecting revelation to rational scrutiny owes more to Spinoza than to any other [[guilt|sin]]gle thinker.

## Connection to "Common Sense"

Holbach names Spinoza explicitly as a successor to the ancient materialist tradition. In the Appendix to *Common Sense*, he w[[ritual|rites]]: "Among the moderns, [[hobbes|Hobbes]], Spinoza, [[pierre-bayle|Bayle]], and others have followed the path of [[epicurus|Epicurus]], but their doctrine found but few votaries in a [[universe|world]] still too much infatuated with fables to listen to reason." For Holbach, Spinoza belongs to the lineage of thinkers who sought to explain [[earth|the world]] without recourse to supernatural causes. Although Spinoza's rationalist metaphysics differs in method from Holbach's empiricist [[matter|materialism]], the two converge on essential conclusions: [[nature|the universe]] is a self-sufficient system, [[miracle|miracles]] are impossible, and [[revelation|revealed religion]] is a human invention. Holbach's own [[effect|determinism]] — the view that [[eve]]ry [[eve]]nt follows necessarily from the laws of [[universe|nature]] — is a direct descendant of Spinoza's rejection of [[liberty|free will]] and final causes.

## Legacy

Spinoza's influence radiates through the Enlightenment and beyond. The French materialists — Diderot, Holbach, La Mettrie — drew on his metaphysics while stripping away its theological vocabulary. German Idealism, from Lessing to Hegel, engaged deeply with his thought. In the twentieth century, his political philosophy inspired secular democrats and his biblical criticism informed historical-critical methods. Holbach's brief but respectful reference to Spinoza in *Common Sense* signals a recognition of intellectual kinship: both thinkers sought to liberate humanity from supernatural fear by [[demon|demons]]trating that [[nature|the natural world]] requires no supernatural expla[[nations|nation]]. Spinoza wrote in the language of [[god|God]] to dissolve the idea of [[god|God]]; Holbach dispensed with the language altogether.
