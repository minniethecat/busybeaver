# The Enlightenment

## Overview

The Enlightenment (French: Siècle des Lumières, "Century of Lights") was an intellectual and [[worship|cult]]ural movement that swept through [[europe|Europe]] and the Americas from approximately 1685 to 1815. Its core principle was the elevation of [[reason|human reason]], empirical observation, and scientific method over tradition, authority, and [[revelation|revealed religion]]. The movement produced foundational works in philosophy, political theory, and natural [[knowledge|science]], and culminated in [[revolution|political revolution]]s in America (1776) and [[paris|France]] (1789). Holbach's "Common Sense" (1772) is a representative document of the radical wing of the French Enlightenment.

## Historical Background

The Enlightenment built upon the Scientific [[revolution|Revolution]] of the 17th century, which had [[demon|demons]]trated the explanatory power of natural laws ([[isaac-newton|Newton]]'s physics, Galileo's astronomy). The [[crusades|religious wars]] of the 16th and 17th centuries had discredited [[doctrine|dogma]]tic [[persecution|intolerance]] in the eyes of many intellectuals. Key precursors included [[rene-descartes|Descartes]] (rationalism), [[john-locke|Locke]] (empiricism), and [[baruch-spinoza|Spinoza]] (biblical criticism). In [[paris|France]], the Enlightenment found its voice in the salons of [[paris|Paris]], where philosophers, scientists, and literate nobles discussed radical ideas outside the constraints of church and state censorship.

## Key Figures and Developments

The French Enlightenment's major figures included [[voltaire|Voltaire]] (who wrote the biographical preface to "Common Sense"), Denis Diderot (editor of the Encyclopédie), Jean-Jacques Rousseau, Montesquieu, and Baron d'Holbach. Holbach's [[paris|Paris]] salon was a center of radical materialist and [[atheism|atheist]]ic thought. The Encyclopédie (1751–1772) sought to compile all human knowledge from a secular perspective. Clandestine manuscripts — including Meslier's "[[testament|Testament]]" — circulated widely, challenging religious orthodoxy.

## Impact on Religion and Thought

The Enlightenment subjected [[christianity|Christianity]] to the most systematic critique it had faced [[vice|sin]]ce antiquity. Thinkers questioned the reliability of [[testament|biblical texts]], the morality of church doctrines, the [[reason|rationality]] of [[miracle|miracles]], and the legitimacy of priestly authority. Holbach's "Common Sense" represents the culmi[[nations|nation]] of this critique: a work that attacks "[[god|God]], against all religion, against the [[scripture|Bible]] and [[church|the Church]]" (as [[voltaire|Voltaire]] described Meslier's manuscript). The movement promoted [[theism|deism]] among moderates and a[[divinity|theism]] among radicals, while advocating [[toleration|religious toleration]] and the separation of church and state.

## Connection to "Common Sense"

Holbach's "Common Sense" is a product of the late Enlightenment at its most uncompromising. The work synthesizes decades of clandestine religious criticism into a popular, accessible format. Its publication under Meslier's name — a deceased priest — gave the arguments an authenticity that a living philosopher could not match. The work embodies the Enlightenment conviction that "[[philosophy|Philosophy]] is beneficial in [[worship|cult]]ivating the heart and the mind" and that "man, deprived of reason and intelligence, is a savage, who is liable at any moment to be led into crime" (ch. CLXXX).

## Legacy

The Enlightenment's emphasis on reason, [[evidence|empirical evidence]], and secular morality shaped modern democratic institutions, scientific [[worship|cult]]ure, and religious [[doubt|skepticism]]. Holbach's specific arguments — particularly his critique of the moral foundations of [[christianity|Christianity]] and his defense of a[[divinity|theism]] as compatible with [[morality|virtue]] — anticipated secular humanist movements of the 19th and 20th centuries.
