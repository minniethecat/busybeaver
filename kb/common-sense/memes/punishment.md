# Punishment

## Definition

[[theology-index|Divine]] punishment is, for Holbach, the most transparently incoherent doctrine in the theological system. The claim that a perfectly good and omnipotent [[god|God]] punishes his creatures eternally for actions he both foreknew and could have prevented is not a [[secrecy|mystery]] to be accepted but a contradiction to be rejected. "Physical [[injustice|evil]] commonly passes as the punishment of [[vice|sin]]," Holbach observes, yet these calamities "fall indiscriminately upon the good and the wicked, upon the impious and the pious, upon the innocent and the guilty" (ch. LXXVIII). A punishment that does not discriminate is not punishment but random [[evil|suffering]].

The doctrine is incoherent at [[eve]]ry level of analysis. If [[god|God]] is the author of human [[universe|nature]], then human [[sin|sinful]]ness is [[god|God]]'s work, and punishing it is punishing His own creation for [[existence|being]] what He made it. If [[god|God]] foreknows all actions, then he creates [[existence|being]]s knowing they will be damned — an act of cruelty, not justice. "If [[god|God]] had the foresight of the future, did He not foresee the fall of His creatures whom He had destined to happiness? If He resolved in His decrees to allow this fall, there is no doubt that He [[passion|desire]]d it to take place" (ch. LXXIII).

The eternal duration of punishment compounds the injustice. [[finite|Finite]] [[crime|transgression]]s by finite [[existence|being]]s, committed under conditions of ignorance and passion that [[god|God]] himself designed, are met with infinite torture. This is not justice but "the most terrible cruelty" — a punishment without purpose, [[vice|sin]]ce "in hell is no more conversion, and the hour of mercy is passed" (ch. LXVI).

## Historical Context

The doctrine of [[hell|eternal punishment]] developed gradually in [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian [[religion|theology]]. Early [[christianity|Christianity]] inherited Jewish notions of [[divinity|divine]] judgment but transformed them through the concept of hell as eternal conscious [[torture|torment]]. Augustine's *City of [[god|God]]* systematized the doctrine, arguing that [[hell|eternal punishment]] was just because [[vice|sin]] against an infinite [[god|God]] deserves infinite retribution. Aquinas refined this by distinguishing between the finite act and [[infinite|the infinite]] dignity of the [[existence|being]] against whom it was committed.

[[enlightenment|The Enlightenment]] mounted a sustained assault on this doctrine. [[pierre-bayle|Pierre Bayle]]'s article "Manicheans" in his *Dictionary* argued that [[evil|the problem of evil]] made divine punishment philosophically indefensible. [[voltaire|Voltaire]]'s poem on the [[lisbon|Lisbon]] earthquake (1755) asked how a just [[god|God]] could indiscriminately destroy the innocent and the guilty. Holbach's contribution was to extend this critique into a comprehensive argument: the punishment doctrine is not merely mysterious but logically self-contradictory, and its social function is not moral [[progress|improvement]] but psychological [[fear|terror]].

## Core Arguments

**1. [[theology-index|Divine]] punishment is indiscriminate and therefore unjust.** Calamities, diseases, and disasters "fall indiscriminately upon the good and the wicked, upon the innocent and the guilty" (ch. LXXVIII). A punishment that does not distinguish between the virtuous and the vicious is not justice.

**2. [[eternity|Eternal]] punishment is disproportionate and purposeless.** [[finite|Finite]] [[crime|transgression]]s met with infinite torture serve no corrective purpose. "In hell is no more conversion, and the hour of mercy is passed; from which it follows, that [[god|God]], in the exercise of His eternal vengeance, would have no other aim than to amuse Himself" (ch. LXVI).

**3. Punishment contradicts foreknowledge and omnipotence.** A [[god|God]] who foresees [[vice|sin]] and creates the [[vice|sin]]ner anyway, then punishes him for acting as foreseen, is not a judge but a trap-setter. "If [[god|God]] had the foresight of the future, did He not foresee the fall of His creatures?" (ch. LXXIII).

**4. [[fear|Fear]] of punishment is ineffective as a moral restraint.** "The fear of [[god|God]] is powerless against human passions" (ch. CLXVI). Those most confident in hell's reality — [[clergy|priests]] and devout believers — are not [[demon|demons]]trably more virtuous than [[atheism|atheist]]s.

## Relation to Other Concepts

Punishment is the negative pole of the theological [[Reward]]-and-punishment system, both of which depend on the fiction of [[Free Will]]. The doctrine of [[hell|eternal punishment]] is the chief content of the concept of [[Hell]], and it functions as the primary mechanism of [[Fear]]-based social control. Punishment is allegedly administered by a just [[God]], but Holbach shows it makes [[god|God]] monstrous. The doctrine of punishment is incompatible with [[divinity|divine]] [[Goodness]] and [[Justice]], and the fear it generates is the engine of [[Superstition]] and priestly [[Authority]].

## In the Text

On the indiscriminate [[universe|nature]] of divine punishment:

> "Physical evil commonly passes as the punishment of sin. Calamities, diseases, famines, wars, earthquakes, are the means which God employs to chastise perverse men. However, do we not see these plagues fall indiscriminately upon the good and the wicked, upon the impious and the pious, upon the innocent and the guilty?" (ch. LXXVIII)

On the purposelessness of eternal [[torture|torment]]:

> "God, in the exercise of His eternal vengeance, would have no other aim than to amuse Himself and insult the weakness of His creatures. I appeal to the whole human race! Is there in nature a man so cruel as to wish in cold blood to torment, I do not say his fellow-beings, but any sentient being whatever, without fee, without profit, without curiosity, without having anything to fear?" (ch. LXVI)

On foreknowledge and the right to punish:

> "If God had the foresight of the future, did He not foresee the fall of His creatures whom He had destined to happiness? If He resolved in His decrees to allow this fall, there is no doubt that He desired it to take place: otherwise it would not have happened." (ch. LXXIII)
