## Definition and Context

In "Common Sense," Holbach examines the concept of infallibility as a crucial pillar of religious authority. Religious institutions, he argues, present their teachings as infallible decrees that must not be questioned — a strategy designed to bypass rational scrutiny and compel submission.

## Infallible Decrees

Holbach describes how the believer is conditioned to accept religious pronouncements as beyond challenge:

> ...himself, whose oracles are for him infallible decrees, to contradict which would be the greatest of crimes. (002-229)

This conditioning, in Holbach's view, is central to maintaining ecclesiastical power over the individual conscience.

## Infallible Remedies

Holbach extends his critique to the remedies offered by religion, which are promoted as infallible solutions to moral and existential anxiety:

> ...they are in the religious pharmacy infallible receipts for calming the conscience... (002-347)

He suggests that "bodies and recommending them as infallible, while we should find them after all powerless" (002-317) — the claim of infallibility masks ineffectiveness.

> ...pretend to make use of them as infallible testimony to prove the truth... (APP004-017)
