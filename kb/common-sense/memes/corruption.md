# Corruption

## Definition

**Corruption** in Holbach's system is not merely individual moral failure but a structural consequence of theological belief and priestly institutions. [[religion|Religion]] corrupts at three levels: it corrupts *thought* by replacing empirical reasoning with [[doctrine|dogma]], it corrupts *character* by training believers in intellectual submission and fear, and it corrupts *society* by sanctifying unjust hierarchies and providing [[divinity|divine]] cover for exploitation. The concept forms a bridge between Holbach's epistemology (how religion distorts cognition) and his political theory (how distorted cognition enables political [[persecution|oppression]]).

## Historical Context

The language of corruption pervades 18th-century French political discourse, from Rousseau's critique of civilization (*Discourse on the Arts and [[science|Science]]s*, 1750) to the parliamentary opposition's attacks on court venality. Holbach draws on this vocabulary but gives it a distinctive materialist foundation: corruption is not a fall from an original state of grace or innocence, but the inevitable effect of social institutions that reward [[imposture|deception]] and punish honesty. The [[catholic-church|Catholic Church]]'s wealth and [[politics|political power]] in *ancien régime* [[france|France]] — bishops as feudal lords, monasteries as landowners, indulgences as commodities — provided [[evidence|empirical evidence]] for Holbach's claim that religious institutions systematically corrupt both their members and the societies they dominate. The [[reformation|Protestant Reformation]]'s critique of papal corruption supplied precedent, but Holbach radicalized it: the problem is not corrupt popes but the very structure of priestly mediation.

## Core Arguments

Holbach identifies three mechanisms of corruption. **Epistemological corruption**: religion corrupts the natural operation of the human mind by demanding belief without [[proof|evidence]], training the intellect to accept contradictions and defer to authority rather than exercise reason. **Moral corruption**: the doctrine that [[guilt|sin]]s can be remitted by priestly absolution (ch. 165) corrupts the natural [[conscience|moral sense]] by ma[[monarch|king]] [[morality|virtue]] a transaction rather than a disposition. **Political corruption**: "[[clergy|priests]] deceive them, tyrants corrupt them in order to subjugate them more easily" — the alliance of priest and [[monarch|prince]] creates a self-reinforcing system in which each corrupts the population to serve the other's interests. [[pride|Pride]], [[pride|presumption]], and corruption of the heart (188-005) complete the diagnosis: religion fosters moral pride that masks genuine corruption of character.

## In the Text

> "These are the true sources of the corruption of habits, to which religion opposes but feeble barriers, which are soon overthrown." (002-037)

> "Priests deceive them, tyrants corrupt them in order to subjugate them more easily. Tyranny has been, and will always be, the chief source of the corruption of morals." (ch. 2)

> "The dogma of the remission of sins has been invented by priests to corrupt the human heart, to make crime easy, and to annihilate remorse." (ch. 165)

Holbach's chapter 188 explicitly treats pride, [[pride|presumption]], and corruption of the heart as a unified syndrome: religious pride convinces believers they possess unique access to [[divinity|divine]] truth, which in turn justifies contempt for outsiders and corruption of the natural sentiments of compassion and justice.
