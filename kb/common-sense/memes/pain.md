# Pain

## Definition

In Holbach's [[materialism|materialist philosophy]], [[suffering|pain]] is not a [[punishment|divine punishment]] or a test of [[religion|faith]] but a necessary and inescapable consequence of sentient existence.

Pain arises from the physical [[temperament|constitution]] of living [[existence|being]]s interacting with a universe governed by natural causes, not by [[design|intelligent design]].

"Certain objects produce in us the feeling of [[suffering|pain]]; therefore our [[universe|nature]] compels us to hate them, and incites us to remove them" (002-138). This definition anchors [[suffering|pain]] in the [[earth|material world]]: it is a sensory signal that [[desire|drive]]s organisms to avoid harm, not a mystical [[suffering|affliction]].

Holbach draws a sharp distinction between the natural experience of [[suffering|pain]] and the theological exploitation of it. Pain, in itself, is neither good nor [[injustice|evil]] in a cosmic sense — it is simply a fact of existence, woven into the fabric of a universe that operates through blind necessity.

What Holbach condemns is the religious impulse to sanctify [[suffering|pain]], to present [[evil|suffering]] as meritorious, and to threaten eternal [[torture|torment]] as a tool of social control. The concept of [[suffering|pain]] thus operates on two levels in the text: as a phenomenon of [[universe|nature]] and as an instrument of theological domi[[nations|nation]].

## Historical Context

The problem of [[suffering|pain]] occupied a central place in eighteenth-century philosophy.

The [[lisbon|Lisbon]] earthquake of 1755 had shaken [[europe|Europe]]an [[certainty|confidence]] in a benevolent [[nature|natural order]], prompting [[voltaire|Voltaire]]'s *Candide* (1759) to mock [[gottfried-leibniz|Leibniz]]ian optimism with savage irony.

Holbach wrote *Common Sense* in 1772, at the height of this crisis: the traditional [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian answer — that [[evil|suffering]] is a consequence of [[sin|original sin]], permitted by a just [[god|God]] for inscrutable purposes — no longer satisfied a public exposed to Enlightenment criticism.

Holbach radicalized the critique. Where [[voltaire|Voltaire]] satirized and [[david-hume|Hume]] questioned, Holbach attacked directly. He argued that the existence of [[suffering|pain]] was not merely a difficulty for [[religion|theology]] but a decisive refutation of it: an infinitely good and all-powerful [[god|God]] is logically incompatible with the [[evil|suffering]] of [[eve]]n the smallest sentient creature.

This argument, later formalized as the logical problem of [[injustice|evil]], appears in Holbach in its most uncompromising form. The intellectual context also included La Mettrie's materialist account of sensation and Diderot's naturalistic [[morality|ethics]], both of which influenced Holbach's treatment of [[suffering|pain]] as a physical phenomenon rather than a [[soul|spirit]]ual one.

## Core Arguments

1. **Pain is a necessary feature of sentient life.** Holbach insists that all [[existence|being]]s capable of sensation are "born to suffer much, to enjoy little, and to die" (002-079). This is not a moral claim but a descriptive one: the [[temperament|constitution]] of living bodies in a [[universe|world]] of physical causes makes [[suffering|pain]] unavoidable. There is no design behind it and no purpose served by it.

2. **The existence of [[evil|suffering]] refutes [[goodness|divine goodness]].** In one of the most forceful passages in the book, Holbach w[[ritual|rites]] that "an [[soul|anima]]l, a mite, which suffers, furnishes invincible arguments against [[theology-index|Divine]] [[providence|Providence]] and its infinite benefactions" (ch. 61). If [[god|God]] were both infinitely good and infinitely powerful, no creature could suffer. [[sin|Sin]]ce creatures do suffer, [[religion|theology]]'s [[god|God]] cannot exist.

3. **Pain is governed by natural necessity, not [[liberty|free will]].** Holbach argues that humans "can not love [[suffering|pain]] nor detest pleasure" (ch. 80). When a person appears to choose [[suffering|pain]] over pleasure, they are in fact choosing a lesser [[suffering|pain]] in hope of a greater pleasure. The avoidance of [[suffering|pain]] and pursuit of pleasure are necessary laws of human [[universe|nature]], not choices made by a [[liberty|free will]].

4. **[[theology|Theology]] exploits [[suffering|pain]] as an instrument of control.** The doctrine of eternal [[torture|torment]] in [[punishment|hell]] transforms [[god|God]] into "a perverse and cruel tyrant without aim" (ch. 66). By threatening infinite [[evil|suffering]] for finite [[crime|offense]]s, [[religion|theology]] uses the fear of [[suffering|pain]] to subjugate minds and enrich [[clergy|priests]]. The [[death|afterlife]] becomes a realm of perpetual torture invented for [[universe|world]]ly profit.

5. **Religious asceticism sanctifies useless [[evil|suffering]].** Holbach condemns the [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian injunction to "hate pleasure, to seek [[evil|suffering]]s, and to despise themselves" (ch. 160). By ma[[monarch|king]] [[suffering|pain]] into a [[morality|virtue]], religion inverts the [[nature|natural order]], causing people to [[torture|torment]] themselves for the glory of a [[god|God]] whose goodness they simultaneously proclaim.

## Reception and Critique

Holbach's treatment of [[suffering|pain]] drew fierce opposition from contemporaries who saw it as a counsel of despair.

Rousseau, though himself a critic of institutional religion, would have rejected Holbach's denial of any moral significance to [[evil|suffering]]; for Rousseau, [[evil|suffering]] could be ennobling and instructive. The Catholic apologist Nicolas-Sylvestre Bergier responded directly to Holbach, arguing that [[suffering|pain]] serves [[divinity|divine]] purposes beyond human comprehension.

Later philosophical traditions have engaged with Holbach's argument in different ways. The utilitarian tradition, from Bentham onward, accepted Holbach's premise that [[suffering|pain]] is the fundamental [[injustice|evil]] to be minimized, but rejected his determinist fatalism by insisting that human action can and should reduce [[evil|suffering]].

Nietzsche, in a different register, attacked the [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian valorization of [[evil|suffering]] on grounds that partly echo Holbach's, though Nietzsche's alternative — the affirmation of [[evil|suffering]] as a condition of greatness — would have been alien to Holbach's humanitarian hedonism.

Contemporary critics sometimes charge that Holbach's [[matter|materialism]] cannot ground the normative claim that [[evil|suffering]] is bad — if [[suffering|pain]] is merely a necessary physical [[eve]]nt, why should we seek to avoid it? Holbach's implicit answer is that the imperative to avoid [[suffering|pain]] is itself a necessary law of [[universe|nature]], not a moral prescription requiring metaphysical justification.

## Relation to Other Concepts

Pain is the negative pole of Holbach's hedonistic psychology, standing in opposition to [[Pleasure]]. While pleasure is the object of natural [[passion|desire]], [[suffering|pain]] is the object of natural aversion; together they constitute the motivational structure of all sentient life.

Pain is closely tied to [[Evil]], [[vice|sin]]ce for Holbach what we call [[injustice|evil]] is simply the experience of [[suffering|pain]] as it affects us.

The concept also relates to [[Hell]], which Holbach treats as the theological apotheosis of [[suffering|pain]] — the transformation of a natural phenomenon into an instrument of eternal [[fear|terror]].

Pain connects to [[Providence]] because the ubiquity of [[evil|suffering]] is, for Holbach, the decisive empirical refutation of providential [[government|governance]]. It links to [[Nature]] as the source of both pleasure and [[suffering|pain]] through the blind operation of physical causes.

Finally, [[suffering|pain]] relates to [[Superstition]], which Holbach sees as feeding on and amplifying human fear of [[evil|suffering]].

## In the Text

> "Are not his pleasures mingled with sufferings? Are there many people who are contented with their fate? Is not mankind the continual victim of physical and moral evils? This human machine, which is shown to us as the masterpiece of the Creator's industry, has it not a thousand ways of deranging itself?" (ch. 51)

> "An animal, a mite, which suffers, furnishes invincible arguments against Divine Providence and its infinite benefactions." (002-095)

> "The inventors of the dogma of eternal torments in hell, have made of the God whom they call so good, the most detestable of beings. Cruelty in man is the last term of corruption. There is no sensitive soul but is moved and revolts at the recital alone of the torments which the greatest criminal endures; but cruelty merits the greater indignation when we consider it gratuitous or without motive." (002-106)

> "He can not love pain nor detest pleasure. Man, it will be said, sometimes prefers pain to pleasure; but then, he prefers a passing pain in the hope of procuring a greater and more durable pleasure." (002-134)
