# Eternal

## Definition

"Eternal" is among the most frequently invoked [[attribute|divine attributes]] in theological discourse, yet Holbach treats it as a term empty of cognitive content. To say that [[god|God]] is eternal is to say that [[god|God]] has always existed and will always exist — a claim that cannot be verified, falsified, or [[eve]]n coherently imagined. "[[eternity|Eternity]]," Holbach w[[ritual|rites]] in effect, "is a word that men have invented to cover their ignorance of origins."

The concept of the eternal functions in [[religion|theology]] as a stopping rule for expla[[nations|nation]]. When asked why [[nature|the universe]] exists, the theologian answers: because [[god|God]] created it. When asked why [[god|God]] exists, the theologian answers: [[god|God]] is eternal — [[god|God]] has no cause. The regress of expla[[nation|nations]] is terminated by a word. Holbach regards this as intellectually no different from saying "it is a [[secrecy|mystery]]" — a confession of ignorance dressed in metaphysical language.

## Historical Context

The philosophical problem of eternity has roots in ancient Greek thought. Parmenides argued that true [[existence|being]] must be eternal and unchanging. [[plato|Plato]]'s Forms were eternal archetypes existing outside time. Aristotle's Unmoved Mover was an eternal [[existence|being]] whose existence explained the motion of the [[universe|cosmos]] without itself requiring expla[[nations|nation]].

[[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian [[religion|theology]] absorbed these Greek concepts and applied them to the biblical [[god|God]]. Augustine, in the *[[confession|Confession]]s*, wrestled with the question of what [[god|God]] was doing "before" creation — answering that time itself was created, so the question is incoherent. Thomas Aquinas developed a sophisticated metaphysics of [[divinity|divine]] eternity as timelessness rather than endless duration.

Holbach cuts through this tradition with characteristic directness: the eternity of [[god|God]] is not a solved philosophical problem but an evasion. The concept of a timeless [[existence|being]] is no more coherent than the concept of a square circle. It is a word to which no clear idea corresponds.

## Core Arguments

First, eternity is unverifiable. No observation could confirm that a [[existence|being]] has existed forever. The claim is not merely false — it is not [[eve]]n a candidate for truth or falsehood, because it makes no contact with possible experience.

Second, eternity is used to terminate inquiry rather than to advance it. "[[god|God]] is eternal" is the theological equivalent of "because I said so." It answers the question "why does anything exist?" by refusing to answer it — by positing a [[existence|being]] that is, by definition, exempt from the demand for expla[[nations|nation]].

Third, if it is acceptable for [[god|God]] to be eternal — to exist without cause — it is equally acceptable for [[nature|the universe]] to be eternal. The materialist hypothesis — that matter has always existed — is no more mysterious than the theological hypothesis — that [[god|God]] has always existed. And [[matter|materialism]] has the advantage of not multiplying entities beyond necessity: it explains the observable (matter) with the observable (matter in motion).

## In the Text

Holbach's treatment of eternity is woven through his critique of [[attribute|divine attributes]]:

> "They will tell you that God is eternal, that He has existed from all eternity. But what is eternity? A duration which has no limits. Can you comprehend a being who has always existed?" (paraphrase of passages throughout)

> "To say that God is eternal is to say nothing — or rather, it is to say that we do not know what God is, and that we cover our ignorance with a word." (Chapter V, paraphrase)
