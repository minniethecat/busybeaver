# Michel de Montaigne

## Biography

Michel de Montaigne (1533–1592) was a French nobleman, magistrate, and philosopher whose *Essais* (1580–1592) invented the essay as a literary form and established a distinctive mode of philosophical inquiry: personal, skeptical, and relentlessly curious. Born into a wealthy Bordeaux family, Montaigne served as a magistrate and mayor of Bordeaux before retiring to his estate to write. His essays range over morality, education, friendship, death, and — most relevant for Holbach's purposes — the [[belief|credulity]] of humankind and the dubiousness of reported [[miracle|miracles]]. Montaigne's motto, "Que sais-je?" (What do I know?), captured his commitment to intellectual humility and suspicion of [[doctrine|dogma]]tic certainty.

## Role in Religious History

Montaigne lived through the French Wars of [[religion|Religion]] (1562–1598) and witnessed firsthand the destruction wrought by sectarian certainty. His skeptical philosophy was, in part, a response to this violence: if neither Catholic nor Protestant could claim [[demon|demons]]trative certainty about [[god|God]]'s will, then toleration became the only rational policy. Though Montaigne remained outwardly a practicing Catholic, his essays subtly undermined [[eve]]ry claim to religious certainty. His famous "Apology for Raymond Sebond" — ostensibly a defense of natural [[religion|theology]] — became, through its relentless deployment of skeptical arguments, a demolition of the very project it purported to defend. This technique of subversive orthodoxy made Montaigne a model for later Enlightenment writers who needed to veil their critiques of religion.

## Connection to "Common Sense"

Holbach cites Montaigne in Chapter CXXX of *Common Sense* — the same chapter devoted to refuting [[pascal|Pascal]]. The reference is brief but strategically placed: in a discussion of [[moses|Moses]]'s diminished credibility when he invites readers to "beli[[eve]] what they choose," Holbach notes that this "gives occasion to the more judicious to regard the histories which speak of this kind of things as fabulous narrations. [See Montaigne, and the author of the 'Apology for Great Men.']" For Holbach, Montaigne represents the tradition of skeptical judgment that refuses to accept miraculous claims at face value. Montaigne is not a materialist — he is too skeptical for any system — but his corrosive doubt clears the ground for Holbach's more constructive project of explaining [[earth|the world]] without supernatural intervention.

> "It also gives occasion to the more judicious to regard the histories which speak of this kind of things as fabulous narrations. [See Montaigne, and the author of the 'Apology for Great Men.']" — Holbach, *Common Sense*, ch. CXXX

## Legacy

Montaigne's influence extends through the entire modern tradition of skeptical and humanistic thought. [[rene-descartes|Descartes]] wrestled with his [[doubt|skepticism]]; [[pascal|Pascal]] feared and admired him; the Enlightenment philosophes claimed him as a forerunner. For Holbach, Montaigne is a valuable authority — not because he arrived at materialist conclusions (he did not), but because his method of skeptical inquiry makes [[dogma|religious dogma]] impossible to sustain. In the genealogy of Enlightenment critique, Montaigne represents the moment when [[europe|Europe]]an thought began to turn from theological authority toward the [[proof|evidence]] of experience and the judgment of the individual mind. Holbach's citation of Montaigne acknowledges this debt while also signaling his own departure: where Montaigne doubted, Holbach affirmed; where Montaigne suspended judgment, Holbach pronounced sentence upon religion.
