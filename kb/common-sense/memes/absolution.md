# Absolution

## Definition

Absolution, in Holbach's critique, is the sacerdotal power to remit [[guilt|sin]]s — a power that [[clergy|priests]] claim to exercise on behalf of [[god|God]] but that Holbach identifies as a human invention designed to control the credulous. Rather than restraining vice, the promise of absolution "encourages the great number to commit [[injustice|evil]]" (ch. CLXV).

The doctrine rests on the assumption that a priest can speak for [[god|God]] — an assumption Holbach regards as both unfounded and socially destructive. The believer who accepts that a fellow mortal can forgive [[guilt|sin]]s on [[divinity|divine]] authority has surrendered moral autonomy to a class of intermediaries whose interests oppose those of genuine [[morality]].

## Historical Context

Absolution developed gradually in the early [[church|Christian church]], reaching its mature form in the medieval sacrament of [[confession|penance]]. By Holbach's century, auricular [[confession]] followed by priestly absolution was a well-established mechanism of social control.

The sale of indulgences — monetized remission — had been a principal grievance of the [[reform|Reform]]ation. Holbach inherits that critique but radicalizes it: the problem is not abuse of absolution but the concept itself. Whether gratis or sold, the claim that one man can forgive another's [[guilt|sin]]s on behalf of [[god|God]] is, for Holbach, an imposture no reform can salvage.

## Theological Function

Holbach analyzes absolution as part of a broader "religious pharmacy" stocked with "infallible receipts for calming the [[remorse|conscience]]" (ch. CLXV). The mechanism is straightforward: the believer [[guilt|sin]]s, experiences guilt, and seeks absolution through priestly mediation — [[prayer|prayers]], offerings, sacrifices, or penitential tears.

The cycle repeats indefinitely, reinforcing both the believer's dependence on the priest and the priest's claim to supernatural authority. Holbach notes that the supposed immutability of [[god|God]] is contradicted by this system: the [[god|deity]] is presented now as wrathful, now as merciful, depending on what serves "the present interests" of [[clergy|the clergy]] and the [[guilt|sin]]ner.

## Moral Consequences

The promise of absolution fundamentally undermines [[morality]]. A person who believes [[guilt|sin]]s can be wiped away by a priestly formula has little incentive to refrain from [[crime|wrongdoing]]. The natural restraint of [[remorse]] — a healthy psychological mechanism — is systematically weakened by a religious apparatus that offers to remove guilt without genuine moral reform.

This stands in opposition to Holbach's conception of [[virtue]] as habitually acting for the welfare of society. Through the lens of [[Priest]]craft, absolution is one of the most effective instruments of [[authority|clerical power]]. The priest who can forgive [[guilt|sin]]s holds the keys to both this [[universe|world]] and the next, serving the double purpose of enriching [[clergy|the clergy]] and securing their dominance.

## In the Text

> "Every religion which expiates, and which promises the remission of crimes, if it restrains any, it encourages the great number to commit evil. Notwithstanding His immutability, God is, in all the religions of this world, a veritable Proteus. His priests show Him now armed with severity, and then full of clemency and gentleness; now cruel and pitiless, and then easily reconciled by the repentance and the tears of the sinners."
>
> (165-003)

Holbach's central argument takes its most concentrated form here. The metaphor of [[god|God]] as Proteus — the shape-shifting sea [[god|deity]] of Greek mythology — is deliberately provocative: the supposedly immutable creator is, in practice, whatever the priest needs him to be.

> "When a man has a great desire to sin, he thinks very little about his God; more than this, whatever crimes he may have committed, he always flatters himself that this God will mitigate the severity of his punishments. No mortal seriously believes that his conduct can damn him."
>
> (165-003)

This observation cuts to the heart of Holbach's case. [[theology-index|Divine]] punishment is, in practice, no deterrent — because the same system that threatens punishment also promises a way out. The believer calculates that reconciliation will be available afterward.

## Reception and Critique

Holbach's analysis of absolution as a mechanism of moral corruption anticipates later critiques of sacerdotal mediation by thinkers from Feuerbach to Nietzsche. Feuerbach's argument in *The [[essence|Essence]] of [[christianity|Christianity]]* (1841) — that the priest functions as a surrogate [[remorse|conscience]], relieving the believer of moral responsibility — extends Holbach's insight into a general theory of religious alie[[nations|nation]]. Nietzsche's critique of the "priestly type" in *The Genealogy of Morals* (1887) similarly identifies the power to forgive [[guilt|sin]]s as a technology of psychological domi[[nations|nation]].

Contemporary scholarship on Holbach has noted that his treatment of absolution exemplifies his broader method: reducing a [[mystery|theological mystery]] to its observable social function. Alan Charles Kors, in *D'Holbach's Coterie* (1976), observes that Holbach's critique of Catholic sacramental practice was among the most radical of the Enlightenment, going beyond [[voltaire|Voltaire]]'s calls for reform to demand the abolition of sacerdotal authority altogether. The argument that absolution encourages rather than restrains crime remains a staple of secular critiques of confessional practice.
