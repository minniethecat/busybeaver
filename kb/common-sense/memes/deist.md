## Definition and Context

Holbach addresses deism as a significant position in the religious landscape of the Enlightenment. The deist rejects the specific dogmas, rituals, and clerical authority of organized religion while maintaining belief in a supreme being — a God of reason and nature rather than revelation.

## The Deist's God

Holbach critically examines what kind of God the deist actually worships:

> ...should we imitate? Is it the deist's God? But even this God can... (002-330)

In Holbach's analysis, the deist's conception of God, while more rational than that of traditional theism, still falls short of a fully naturalistic understanding. The deist has discarded the external trappings of religion but retained what Holbach sees as its fundamental error — the positing of a supernatural agent behind natural phenomena.

## Holbach's Argument Against Deism

Holbach's critique of deism goes beyond rejecting its theology: he argues that deism is an intellectually unstable position. The deist has taken the first step of reason — rejecting priests, sacraments, and revelation — but stops short of the conclusion that reason demands. If reason is sufficient to expose the absurdities of revealed religion, Holbach asks, why should it not also suffice to expose the incoherence of a disembodied creator? The deist's God "can not act otherwise than by general and immutable laws" (160-001), rendering the hypothesis of a personal deity indistinguishable in practice from a purely naturalistic universe. For Holbach, deism is not a refuge from superstition but a way station on the road to atheism — and a way station at which no consistent thinker should linger.

## Deism and the Enlightenment

Deism represented a transitional stage in Enlightenment thought, where many thinkers sought to reconcile reason with a minimal form of religious belief. Holbach's work pushes beyond this compromise, arguing that consistency requires abandoning supernatural explanations entirely.
