# Progress

## Definition

Progress, in Holbach's lexicon, is the accumulation of knowledge and the improvement of human institutions through rational inquiry over time. "[[nature|Nature]] follows constantly the same progress; that is to say, the same causes produce the same effects, as long as their action is not interrupted by other causes" (ch. II). [[nature|Nature]] is regular and predictable, and the task of human knowledge is to understand its progress — its lawful unfolding — so that society can be organized in harmony with it.

Holbach distinguishes between natural progress and social progress. Natural progress is the deterministic unfolding of causes and effects in the physical [[universe|world]]. Social progress is the improvement of human institutions — law, education, government — through the application of natural knowledge. The two are connected: [[knowledge|understanding]] [[universe|nature]]'s regularities enables the design of institutions that work with, rather than against, human [[universe|nature]].

[[theology|Theology]] is the great enemy of progress because it substitutes fictitious expla[[nation|nations]] for natural ones. "[[theology|Theology]] opposed insurmountable barriers to the progress of [[truth|true knowledge]]; it seemed to be occupied but with the care to keep the [[nation|nations]] and their chiefs in the most profound ignorance of their true interests" (ch. II). So long as human [[existence|being]]s attribute [[eve]]nts to [[divinity|divine]] will rather than natural causes, they cannot understand [[earth|the world]] well enough to improve it.

## Historical Context

The idea of progress was a defining feature of the Enlightenment, mar[[monarch|king]] a break from the classical and [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian view of history as cyclical or declining. Fontenelle's *Digression on the Ancients and [[mode|Mode]]rns* (1688) argued that modern thinkers could surpass the ancients because knowledge is cumulative. Turgot's *A Philosophical Review of the Successive Advances of the Human [[mind|Mind]]* (1750) gave progress a theoretical framework as the gradual actualization of human potential. Condorcet's *Sketch for a Historical Picture of the Progress of the Human [[mind|Mind]]* (1795) would later make progress the organizing principle of history itself.

Holbach shared this optimism but grounded it in [[matter|materialism]]. Progress is not a metaphysical force or [[divinity|divine]] plan — it is the result of applying the same methods that work in [[science|natural philosophy]] to social and political questions. His hostility to religion was fueled by the conviction that [[religion|theology]] was the chief obstacle to progress: as long as [[clergy|priests]] controlled education and censored inquiry, the advancement of knowledge would be blocked at its source.

## Core Arguments

**1. Progress follows natural law.** "[[nature|Nature]] follows constantly the same progress; that is to say, the same causes produce the same effects." (ch. II). Progress is not miraculous or mysterious; it is the predictable result of [[knowledge|understanding]] causes and modifying them to produce better effects.

**2. [[theology|Theology]] obstructs progress.** "Incapable of perfecting itself, [[religion|theology]] opposed insurmountable barriers to the progress of [[truth|true knowledge]]." (ch. II). Theological doctrines are immune to correction by [[proof|evidence]], which makes them permanent obstacles to the advancement of [[knowledge|understanding]].

**3. Resources wasted on religion could fund progress.** "What resources for the public benefit, for encouraging the progress of the [[knowledge|science]]s and the advancement of knowledge, for the education of youth, are presented to well-meaning [[monarch|sovereign]]s through so many monasteries." (ch. II). The wealth locked up in religious institutions is wealth diverted from human improvement.

**4. Progress requires free inquiry.** "How could men thus fettered give free flight to their genius, perfect reason?" (ch. II). The threat of [[cruelty|persecution]] forces thinkers to self-censor, and progress is impossible where inquiry is not free.

## Relation to Other Concepts

Progress is the measured outcome of [[Reform]] — reform is the action, progress is the result. It requires [[Reason]] as its engine and [[Evidence]] as its fuel. [[Education]] is the transmission mechanism by which progress is sustained across generations. [[Superstition]] and [[Imposture]] are the primary obstacles, keeping populations ignorant so that [[clergy|priests]] can control them. [[Knowledge]] is the stock that progress accumulates.
