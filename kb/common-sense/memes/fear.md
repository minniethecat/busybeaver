# Fear

## Definition

In Holbach's psychological analysis of religion, fear is the original source of all [[belief|religious belief]]. "Fear was the first parent of the gods" (ch. 142). Early humans, ignorant of natural causes and vulnerable to the elements, experienced terror at thunderstorms, earthquakes, disease, and death. Unable to understand these phenomena, they attributed them to invisible, powerful agents who needed to be propitiated. [[religion|Religion]], in this account, is not a revelation from above but a projection from below — the institutionalization of primitive terror.

Holbach's analysis is naturalistic and psychological: he does not argue that religion is a deliberate [[imposture|fraud]] (though he believes [[clergy|priests]] later exploit it as such) but that it arises naturally from the intersection of human vulnerability and human ignorance. "If the ignorance of [[universe|nature]] gave birth to the gods, the knowledge of [[universe|nature]] is calculated to destroy them" (ch. 142). Enlightenment — [[knowledge|understanding]] the natural causes of what once seemed supernatural — is the cure for the fear that creates religion.

## Historical Context

The idea that fear creates gods was not original to Holbach. The Roman poet Lucretius had written in *De Rerum Natura* that "fear gave birth to the gods" (*primus in orbe deos fecit timor*). The Greek historian Polybius had argued that religion was a political invention to control the masses through fear of invisible watchers. [[hobbes|Hobbes]], in *[[matthew|Levi]]athan* (1651), had traced religion to "fear of things invisible."

What was distinctive in Holbach's treatment was its integration into a comprehensive materialist psychology. Fear is not just one cause of religion among others — it is the *primary* cause, and all other religious motivations (gratitude for blessings, hope for an [[death|afterlife]], [[passion|desire]] for cosmic justice) are secondary formations built on the foundation of primitive terror. Even the love of [[god|God]], which theologians describe as the highest religious [[passion|emotion]], is for Holbach a disguised form of fear: the believer loves [[god|God]] because he fears [[god|God]]'s punishment.

## Core Arguments

**1. Fear is the original source of religion.** The earliest religious acts — sacrifices, [[prayer|prayers]], rituals — were attempts to placate feared invisible powers. "The first theologians of [[earth|the world]] were fear and ignorance" (ch. 142). Humans did not begin by reasoning about the existence of a [[cause|first cause]]; they began by trembling at thunder.

**2. [[ignorance|Ignorance]] magnifies fear.** Fear is proportional to ignorance of causes. An eclipse terrifies those who do not know what causes it; it amazes those who do. "If men ceased to fear, they would cease to be religious" (ch. 143). The progress of [[knowledge|science]] is inversely proportional to the intensity of religious fear — as expla[[nations|nation]] advances, terror recedes.

**3. [[priest|Priest]]s exploit fear to maintain power.** Once religion is established, the priestly class has [[eve]]ry incentive to perpetuate and intensify the fear that sustains it. "The [[clergy|priests]], to render themselves necessary, have done [[eve]]rything to render men fearful" (ch. 144). The doctrines of [[hell|eternal punishment]], [[sin|original sin]], and [[divinity|divine]] wrath are instruments of psychological control — they keep believers in a state of perpetual anxiety that only the [[clergy|priesthood]] can reli[[eve]].

**4. Fear corrupts morality.** Actions performed out of fear — of [[punishment|divine punishment]] or hope of [[reward|divine reward]] — have no moral worth. "[[morality|Morality]] founded on fear is the morality of slaves" (ch. 145). True [[morality|virtue]] is the product of reason and social sympathy, not of terror before an invisible tyrant.

## Reception and Critique

Holbach's fear theory of religion influenced the 19th-century "scientific" study of religion. Feuerbach, in *The [[essence|Essence]] of [[christianity|Christianity]]* (1841), developed the projection theory further: not only fear but also human wishes and [[model|ideal]]s are projected onto the [[divinity|divine]]. Marx's famous dictum that religion is "the opium of the people" echoes Holbach's analysis of religion as a response to [[evil|suffering]] — a [[hope|consolation]] that prevents people from addressing the real causes of their misery.

Later anthropology complicated the simple fear → religion model. Durkheim argued that religion is primarily a social bonding mechanism, not an individual psychological response. Max Weber showed that religion can be [[desire|drive]]n by hope and meaning-ma[[monarch|king]] as much as by fear. Contemporary cognitive [[knowledge|science]] of religion (Boyer, Atran) identifies multiple cognitive modules — agency detection, contagion avoidance, teleological reasoning — that contribute to [[belief|religious belief]].

Holbach's model, while oversimplified, captures a genuine dimension of religious psychology. The fear of death, in particular, remains one of the strongest predictors of [[belief|religious belief]] across [[worship|cult]]ures.

## Relation to Other Concepts

Fear connects to many Holbachian concepts. [[Ignorance]] is fear's precondition — we fear what we do not understand. [[Superstition]] is the cognitive expression of fear — the belief in invisible agents. [[Hell]] is fear institutionalized as doctrine — the ultimate punishment for disobedience. [[Priesthood]] exploits fear for social control. [[Reason]] and [[Science]] are fear's antidotes — they replace unknown causes with known ones. [[Death]] is the ultimate object of fear and the ultimate [[desire|drive]]r of religious [[hope|consolation]].
