# China

## Definition

China occupies a distinctive strategic role in Baron d'Holbach's *Common Sense* as the empirical counter-example that refutes [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian claims of universal revelation and providential [[government|governance]]. Holbach invokes China — an ancient, sophisticated, and enduring civilization that developed elaborate systems of [[morality|ethics]], [[government|governance]], and philosophy entirely outside the biblical tradition — to [[demon|demons]]trate that morality and [[society|social order]] do not require [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian revelation. If [[god|God]] revealed Himself universally to all [[nation|nations]], why did China — with its "very flourishing, very productive, thickly populated" society — receive no word of [[gospel|the Gospel]] until [[europe|Europe]]an missionaries arrived? For Holbach, the Chinese case exposes the parochialism of [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian universalism and provides a positive model of a society in which [[government|the state]] has successfully subordinated religious institutions to civil peace.

## Historical Context

[[europe|Europe]]an knowledge of China in Holbach's time was mediated primarily through Jesuit [[missionaries|missionary]] accounts, particularly those of Matteo Ricci (1552–1610) and his successors, who presented China as a civilization of extraordinary antiquity, literacy, and moral refinement. The Jesuit strategy of [[worship|cult]]ural accommodation — presenting [[christianity|Christianity]] as compatible with Confucian [[morality|ethics]] — generated a rich literature that paradoxically supplied Enlightenment *philosophes* with ammunition against [[christianity|Christianity]] itself. If the Chinese had achieved high civilization without biblical revelation, then biblical revelation was unnecessary for civilization.

The Chinese [[ceremony|Rites]] Controversy (1630s–1742), in which the [[rome|Vatican]] condemned Jesuit accommodations of Confucian ancestor veneration as idolatrous, further exposed [[christianity|Christianity]]'s incompatibility with non-[[europe|Europe]]an [[worship|cult]]ures. By Holbach's era, China had become a [[model|standard]] reference point in Enlightenment polemics: [[voltaire|Voltaire]] opened his *Essai sur les mœurs* (1756) with China as the model of enlightened [[tyranny|despotism]]; [[gottfried-leibniz|Leibniz]] had earlier advocated [[worship|cult]]ural exchange with China. Holbach's specific contribution was to weaponize the Chinese example not merely against [[europe|Europe]]an *superstition* but against the very claim that [[providence|divine providence]] governed human history.

## Core Arguments

Holbach's first argument is the argument from civilizational exclusion. The [[god|God]] of [[christianity|Christianity]] is said to have revealed Himself for the salvation of all humanity, yet the vast population of China — one of [[earth|the world]]'s oldest continuous civilizations — lived for thousands of years without access to this revelation. Either [[god|God]] did not wish to save the Chinese (which contradicts universal [[charity|benevolence]]) or He could not reach them (which contradicts omnipotence) or the claim of universal revelation is false. The Chinese case makes any particularist revelation thesis empirically untenable.

Holbach's second argument concerns the relation between religion and civil order. He observes that the Chinese mandarins (scholar-officials), "undeceived long ago about the popular religion," maintained a policy of tolerant oversight: they permitted [[superstition|popular superstition]] but prevented the Buddhist and Taoist [[priest|clergy]] ("bonzes") from disturbing the peace of [[government|the state]]. This model — religion tolerated but politically subordinated — is precisely the inversion of the [[europe|Europe]]an [[throne-and-altar]] alliance. It [[demon|demons]]trates that a stable, prosperous society does not require a state church and that secular [[government|governance]] produces superior outcomes.

Holbach's third argument is moral and psychological. The Chinese [[demon|demons]]trate that human [[existence|being]]s, unaided by [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian revelation, can develop elaborate codes of [[morality|ethics]], produce great works of literature and philosophy, and maintain complex social institutions across millennia. The implication is devastating to the [[missionaries|missionary]] claim that non-[[jesus-christ|Christ]]ians languish in moral darkness: if this is darkness, what is the superior light that [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian [[europe|Europe]] has produced? Holbach lets the question hang unanswered.

## Reception and Critique

Holbach's use of China as a polemical weapon against [[christianity|Christianity]] has been evaluated by modern scholarship as both insightful and limited. Jonathan [[israel|Israel]] (*Radical Enlightenment*, 2001) positions Holbach's [[sin|Sin]]ophilia within the broader Radical Enlightenment project of deploying non-[[europe|Europe]]an civilizations as counter-models to [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian [[europe|Europe]] — a strategy shared by [[pierre-bayle|Bayle]], Diderot, and [[voltaire|Voltaire]], but which Holbach radicalized by ma[[monarch|king]] the Chinese case the centerpiece of an argument against [[providence|divine providence]] itself, not merely against clerical corruption. [[david|David]] Mungello (*The Great Encounter of China and the West*, 1999) notes that Enlightenment thinkers, Holbach included, constructed an [[model|ideal]]ized "philosophical China" that served [[europe|Europe]]an polemical needs more than it reflected Chinese reality. The Confucian state [[worship|cult]], ancestor veneration, and popular Daoist-Buddhist practices were far more religiously saturated than Holbach's rationalist mandarin model suggests. Nevertheless, Simon Kow (*China in Early Enlightenment Political [[thought|Thought]]*, 2017) argues that Holbach's instrumental use of China was methodologically consistent with his materialist epistemology: if all knowledge comes from experience, then the existence of a non-[[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian civilization of equal or superior attainment is a brute empirical fact that [[religion|theology]] must explain or abandon its universalist claims.

A more fundamental critique concerns Holbach's implicit Eurocentrism. While he deploys China against [[christianity|Christianity]], his [[model|ideal]]ized mandarin [[government|governance]] model — "wise laws" adopted "with respect" by conquerors — rests on the same orientalist tropes of Chinese stasis and immemorial antiquity that later 19th-century critics would weaponize against China's capacity for progress. Holbach does not engage with Chinese philosophy on its own terms (Confucian metaphysics, Daoist [[existence|ontology]]) but rather as negative [[proof|evidence]] against [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian revelation. His China is a mirror held up to [[europe|Europe]], not a civilization understood in its own categories.

## Relation to Other Concepts

China functions in Holbach's argument as the geographical-instantiation of the concept of [[religious-pluralism]] — the observable fact that different civilizations produce different [[religion|religious system]]s, each claiming exclusive truth. It connects directly to [[nations]], which generalizes the argument from China to all [[nation|peoples]]: [[eve]]ry [[nations|nation]] has its own gods, which proves gods are local products, not universal [[dreams|revelations]]. The mandarin policy of subordinating [[priest|clergy]] to civil order provides a concrete model of [[toleration]] — not as theological [[morality|virtue]] but as political prudence — and stands in contrast to [[throne-and-altar]], the [[europe|Europe]]an alliance of political and religious power that Holbach seeks to dismantle. China's exclusion from biblical history connects the concept to [[providence]] — if there is a universal [[divinity|divine]] plan, why was China not included? — and to [[revelation]], whose particularity is exposed by the Chinese counter-example. The Jesuit [[missionaries|missionary]] encounter with China supplies the historical material for Holbach's analysis of [[missionaries]] as ambitious agents of [[worship|cult]]ural imperialism rather than [[guilt|sin]]cere bearers of [[divinity|divine]] truth.

## In the Text

Holbach's principal discussion of China appears in a remarkable passage that contrasts [[god|God]]'s supposed universal providence with the actual distribution of religious knowledge:

> "God has spoken differently to each nation of the globe which we inhabit. The Indian does not believe one word of what He said to the Chinaman; the Mohammedan considers what He has told to the Christian as fables; the Jew considers the Mohammedan and the Christian as sacrilegious corruptors of the Holy Law... Each one exclaims: 'It is I!' Every one claims the same proofs; each one speaks of his miracles, his saints, his prophets, his martyrs. Sensible men answer, that they are all delirious."

This passage is followed immediately by the description of China as a flourishing society governed by "such wise laws, that the most savage conquerors adopted them with respect." Holbach notes pointedly that [[christianity|Christianity]] was "banished as dangerous" from China — a judgment he implicitly endorses by observing that the mandarins acted to prevent [[clergy|the clergy]] from using religion "to disturb the peace of the [[politics-index|State]]."
