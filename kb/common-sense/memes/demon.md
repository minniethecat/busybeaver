# Demon

## Definition

Demons, in the [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian [[religion|theology]] Holbach critiques, are fallen [[angel|angels]] — [[soul|spirit]]ual [[existence|being]]s created good by [[god|God]] who freely chose to rebel and now work to corrupt humanity and oppose [[divinity|divine]] purposes.
Holbach treats demonology as a particularly revealing symptom of [[religion|theology]]'s intellectual bankruptcy.
The doctrine of demons is an attempt to solve [[evil|the problem of evil]] by inventing a second, malevolent agency to blame for the [[evil|suffering]] that a benevolent [[god|God]] supposedly permits.

Holbach argues that demons are logically incoherent.
If [[god|God]] is omnipotent, why does He permit demonic opposition to His plans?
If [[god|God]] created demons knowing they would rebel, why did He create them?
"[[god|God]] made [[earth|the world]] to be the theater of [[honor|dishonor]]ing wars with His creatures; He created [[angel|angels]], men, demons, wicked spirits, but as adversaries, against whom He could exercise His power" (ch. 70).
This is not the conduct of a [[perfection|perfect being]] but of a playwright who manufactures conflict for dramatic effect.

The concept of demons also serves a social function: it provides religious authorities with an external enemy to blame for human [[evil|suffering]] and moral failure.
As long as people fear demons, they remain dependent on the priestly class that claims power over them.

## Historical Context

Demonology was a living concern in eighteenth-century [[europe|Europe]].
Witch trials, though declining, had not entirely ceased; the last legal execution for witchcraft in [[paris|France]] occurred in 1745.
The Catholic ritual of exorcism remained in active use, and demonic possession was widely accepted as a genuine phenomenon by both Catholic and Protestant authorities.

The intellectual challenge to demonology came from several quarters.
Balthasar Bekker's *The World Bewitched* (1691) had argued that [[soul|spirit]]s cannot act on matter — a Cartesian argument that Holbach radicalized.
The medical profession increasingly reinterpreted possession as mental illness.
By Holbach's time, educated opinion was shifting toward naturalistic expla[[nation|nations]], but popular belief in demons remained widespread.

## Core Arguments

**1. Demons are inventions to externalize [[injustice|evil]].**
Rather than confront [[evil|the problem of evil]] honestly — as [[proof|evidence]] against [[divinity|divine]] [[goodness|benevolence]] — [[religion|theology]] invents demons to serve as scapegoats.
This is an ad hoc solution that creates more problems than it solves.

**2. The demonic rebellion makes [[god|God]] look impotent or malevolent.**
[[god|God]] "created [[angel|angels]], men, demons, wicked spirits, but as adversaries" (ch. 70).
Either [[god|God]] could not prevent their rebellion (impotence) or chose not to ([[malice|malevolence]]).
Neither option is compatible with the theological definition of [[god|God]].

**3. Demonology is continuous with pagan mythology.**
Holbach groups demons with "aerial [[existence|being]]s, [[soul|spirit]]s, [[angel|angels]], genii, and other phantoms" (ch. 76) — all products of the same ancient mythological imagi[[nations|nation]].
[[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian demonology is not revelation but a baptized version of pre-existing pagan beliefs about malevolent [[soul|spirit]]s.

**4. [[fear|Fear]] of demons serves priestly power.**
The more terrifying the invisible [[universe|world]], the more people depend on [[clergy|the clergy]] for protection.
Exorcism, [[sacrament|sacraments]], and priestly intercession are marketed as defenses against demonic attack.
The [[clergy|priesthood]] thus has a material interest in maintaining demon-belief.

## Reception and Critique

Demonology declined in mainstream [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian [[religion|theology]] over the nineteenth and twentieth centuries, though the process was uneven.
Liberal [[religion|theology]] reinterpreted demons as symbols of psychological or social [[injustice|evil]].
The [[catholic-church|Catholic Church]] maintained the reality of demonic possession but centralized exorcism under strict ecclesiastical control.

[[mode|Mode]]rn defenders of [[divinity|theism]] rarely invoke demons, effectively conceding Holbach's point that demonology is an embarrassment to rational [[religion|theology]].
The problem of [[injustice|evil]] is now addressed through free-will defenses and soul-ma[[monarch|king]] theodicies rather than through personified evil spirits.

Holbach might observe that these modern theodicies face the same structural problem as demonology: they explain [[injustice|evil]] by positing entities or processes whose existence is unverifiable and whose necessity is asserted rather than demonstrated.

## Relation to Other Concepts

Demons are the negative counterpart to [[angel|angels]] — both are "aerial [[existence|being]]s" invented to populate the invisible realm.
They relate to [[superstition]] as one of its most socially consequential products.

The concept of demons is intimately connected to [[fear]], the psychological foundation on which priestly authority rests.
Demons provide the threat; the [[priest]] provides the protection.
Demonology also relates to [[theism]]: the more a [[religion|theology]] struggles to reconcile [[goodness|divine goodness]] with earthly [[evil|suffering]], the more it needs demons to absorb the explanatory burden.
