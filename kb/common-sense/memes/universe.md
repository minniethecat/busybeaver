# Universe

## Definition

The universe, in Holbach's usage, is the comprehensive totality of all that exists — synonymous with "[[earth|the world]]" and closely related to "[[nature|Nature]]." It is not a created artifact but a necessary, eternal, and self-sustaining system. "The world has always been, its existence is necessary. It is the cause of itself" (ch. XXXIX). The universe is not an effect requiring an external cause; it is itself the ultimate cause, the ground of all particular causes and effects.

## The Universe Is Eternal and Uncreated

Holbach rejects the creationist premise that [[nature|the universe]] must have had a beginning. The argument that "there is no effect without a cause" and that "[[earth|the world]] did not create itself" mistakenly treats [[nature|the universe]] as an effect rather than recognizing it as the totality of causes:

> "But the universe is a cause, not an effect; it is not a work, has not been made, because it was impossible that it should be made."
>
> (039-001)

If [[nature|the universe]] is the sum total of all that exists, then there is nothing outside it that could have created it. To speak of the "creation of [[nature|the universe]]" is a category mistake — it presupposes a standpoint outside existence from which existence could be brought into [[existence|being]]. No such standpoint exists. The universe has always existed because its non-existence is inconceivable: there is no time at which nothing existed, for time itself presupposes existence.

## Order Without an Orderer

The theological [[design|argument from design]] claims that the order observable in [[nature|the universe]] proves the existence of an intelligent creator. Holbach turns this argument on its head: order is not something imposed on [[nature|the universe]] from without but the necessary result of natural laws operating consistently:

> "Nature follows constantly the same progress; that is to say, the same causes produce the same effects, as long as their action is not interrupted by other causes."
>
> (044-002)

What we call "order" is simply the regular behavior of matter in motion. When causes produce effects that benefit us, we praise [[providence|divine providence]]; when they produce effects that harm us — earthquakes, volcanoes, famines — we call them disorders or [[mysteries]]. But both are equally necessary: "[[earth|Earth]]quakes, volcanoes, inundations, contagions, and famines are effects as necessary in the order of nature as the fall of heavy bodies" (ch. XLIV). The universe is always in order; only our interests create the illusion of disorder.

## The Universe and Confusion

In the Apologue (ch. I), Holbach deploys the alleged disorder of [[nature|the universe]] as [[proof|evidence]] against [[divinity|divine]] [[government|governance]]:

> "It is said that he is the friend of order, and everything in his universe is in a state of confusion and disorder; all is created by him, yet events rarely happen according to his projects."
>
> (001-001)

If a perfectly wise and powerful [[god|God]] created and governs [[nature|the universe]], the observable state of [[earth|the world]] — with its [[evil|suffering]], injustice, and chaos — becomes the strongest argument against his existence. The universe, taken as [[proof|evidence]], refutes the claim that a benevolent intelligence directs it.

## In the Text

On [[nature|the universe]] as self-existent:

> "Nature, whose essence is visibly acting and producing, in order to fulfill her functions, as we see she does, needs no invisible motor far more unknown than herself."
>
> (039-001)

On order as a natural rather than [[divinity|divine]] phenomenon:

> "The universe is always in order; there can be no disorder for it. Our organization alone is suffering if we complain about disorder." (ch. XLIV)

## Relation to Other Concepts

The concept of [[nature|the universe]] as eternal and self-organizing is the cosmological expression of Holbach's [[Materialism]]. It stands in direct opposition to the theological concept of [[God]] as creator and to the [[Order]] argument for [[divinity|divine]] existence. The [[eternal|eternity]] of [[nature|the universe]] is the macrocosmic counterpart to the self-motion of [[Matter]] at the microcosmic level, and both converge in [[Nature]] as the all-encompassing reality that renders supernatural expla[[nation|nations]] superfluous.
