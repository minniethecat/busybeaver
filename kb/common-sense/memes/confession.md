# Confession

## Definition

Confession, in Holbach's analysis, is not a sacrament of reconciliation but an instrument of power.
The title of chapter 170 states his thesis directly: "Confession, that golden mine for the [[clergy|priests]], has destroyed the true principles of morality."
The practice of auricular confession — in which the [[religion|faith]]ful are required to disclose their [[vice|sin]]s to a priest who then grants or withholds absolution — is, Holbach argues, a triple corruption: it subverts morality, en[[wealth|riches]] [[clergy|the clergy]], and grants [[vice|sin]]ners a cheap escape from the consequences of their actions.

The argument proceeds from [[principle|first principles]].
[[morality|Morality]], properly understood, is "founded upon the relations, the needs, and the constant interests of the inhabitants of the earth" (ch. 170).
When a wrong is done, it is a wrong against another person — and it can only be repaired by restitution to that person, not by rituals addressed to an invisible third party.
Confession severs this connection: "He who first proclaimed to the [[nation|nations]] that, when man had wronged man, he must ask [[god|God]]'s pardon, appease His wrath by presents, and offer Him sacrifices, obviously subverted the true principles of morality" (ch. 170).

The political dimension is explicit.
Confession gives the priest access to the inner life of [[eve]]ry member of the [[society|community]] — their secrets, their fears, their [[crime|transgression]]s.
This is a surveillance apparatus more intimate and more comprehensive than any police force could achi[[eve]].
And it is self-financing: the penitent pays for the privilege of [[existence|being]] surveilled.

## Historical Context

The requirement of annual confession was established by the Fourth Lateran Council (1215) and reaffirmed by the [[council-of-trent|Council of Trent]] (1551).
By the eighteenth century, the confessional was a fixed feature of Catholic parish life.
[[priest|Priest]]s were bound by the seal of confession, but the information they gathered — about political dissent, heresy, sexual conduct — made them de facto agents of social control.

The [[reformation|Protestant Reformation]] had abolished mandatory auricular confession, but Holbach's critique goes beyond the [[reform|Reform]]ers'.
Luther retained confession as a voluntary practice and valued it as a means of applying [[gospel|the Gospel]] to the troubled con[[knowledge|science]].
Holbach rejects the entire framework: the problem is not who hears the confession or whether it is mandatory but the very idea that [[vice|sin]]s against men can be pardoned by a [[god|God]] through a priest.

The economic dimension was especially salient in eighteenth-century [[paris|France]], where [[church|the Church]] extracted substantial revenue through offerings, masses for the dead, and do[[nation|nations]] made under the influence of confessional advice.
Holbach's phrase "that golden mine" is literal: confession was a financial institution.

## Core Arguments

**1. Confession inverts morality.**
True morality requires that wrongs be repaired — that the thief restore what was stolen, that the slanderer retract the lie.
Confession teaches instead that the wrong can be expunged by a transaction with [[god|God]], mediated by a priest.
"Mortals imagine that they can, with impunity, injure each other by ma[[monarch|king]] a suitable reparation to the [[almighty|Almighty]] [[being|Being]]" (ch. 170).

**2. Confession emboldens crime.**
"Is there anything more liable to encourage [[malice|wickedness]] and to embolden to crime, than to persuade men that there exists an invisible [[existence|being]] who has the right to pardon injustice, rapine, perfidy, and all the outrages they can inflict upon society?" (ch. 170).
Confession is not a deterrent — it is an enabler.

**3. Confession en[[wealth|riches]] [[clergy|the clergy]] at the expense of the laity.**
The priest "consoles them in the name of [[deity|Deity]], if they consent in reparation of their faults to divide with His ministers the fruits of their plunderings, of their [[imposture|fraud]]s, and of their [[malice|wickedness]]" (ch. 170).
[[absolution|Absolution]] is a commodity, and the confessional is the point of sale.

**4. Confession subordinates morality to religion.**
"[[morality|Morality]] united to religion, becomes necessarily subordinate to it. In the mind of a religious person, [[god|God]] must be preferred to His creatures; 'It is better to obey Him than men!'" (ch. 170).
When [[divinity|divine]] commandments conflict with human obligations, religion teaches that [[god|God]] takes precedence — but "the interests of [[heaven|Heaven]] are evidently the interests of the ministers of [[heaven|Heaven]]."

**5. The power to pardon implies the power to command.**
Holbach poses a devastating question: "He who has the power to pardon crimes, has He not the right to order them committed?" (ch. 170).
If the priest can absolve any [[vice|sin]], the priest can authorize any action — and history, Holbach implies, shows that [[clergy|priests]] have done exactly this.

## Reception and Critique

The abolition of mandatory confession was one of the demands of the more radical [[sect|faction]]s of [[revolution|the French Revolution]], though it was never systematically implemented.
The practice survived the [[revolution|Revolution]] and continues in the [[catholic-church|Catholic Church]] to the present day.

Defenders of confession have argued that Holbach caricatures the practice: the requirement of [[vice|sin]]cere contrition and the firm purpose of amendment means that confession is not a license to [[vice|sin]] but a call to conversion.
The seal of confession, moreover, protects the penitent from the very surveillance Holbach alleges.

Psychological perspectives have offered a more sympathetic reading of confession: the act of verbalizing [[crime|wrongdoing]] to another person can have therapeutic value, regardless of the theological framework.
Holbach would likely reply that therapy does not require the fiction of [[divinity|divine]] absolution or the mediation of a priestly caste.

## Relation to Other Concepts

Confession is the institutional mechanism through which [[mercy]] is dispensed.
The priest who hears confessions is the [[priest]] in his most intimate and powerful role — not the public preacher but the private interrogator.

Confession is structurally linked to [[sin]]: it presupposes a category of [[crime|offense]]s against [[god|God]] that can be cleansed by ritual rather than repaired by restitution.
It is opposed to [[morality]], which Holbach defines as the system of obligations that arise from human relations, not from [[divinity|divine]] commands.

Confession is related to [[secrecy]]: the confessional is a site of privileged knowledge, where the priest learns what no civil authority could discover, and this knowledge is power.
