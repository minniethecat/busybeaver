# Goodness

## Definition

In Holbach's "Common Sense," goodness is not denied as a human [[morality|virtue]] but is relentlessly examined as a [[divinity|divine]] attribute. The core of his argument is logical: if [[god|God]] is infinitely good, infinite in power, and the author of all things, then the existence of any [[evil|suffering]] whatsoever is incompatible with that goodness. "One unfortunate [[existence|being]] alone would be sufficient to annihilate an unlimited goodness" (060-002). Goodness that coexists with the infliction of indiscriminate [[evil|suffering]] is not goodness at all — it is a contradiction in terms, a "goodness which takes the place of the most terrible cruelty" (060-002).

Holbach's method is not to disprove [[god|God]] directly but to show that the [[god|God]] described by theologians cannot possess the [[attribute|attributes]] they assign to Him. If [[earth|the world]] contains [[injustice|evil]] — and it manifestly does — then [[god|God]] cannot be simultaneously all-good, all-powerful, and [[god|the creator]] of all things. Theologians resolve this trilemma by sacrificing one attribute at a time, never all three, and the attribute most frequently sacrificed is goodness — rebranded as "justice," "trial," or "mysterious love."

## Historical Context

The problem of [[injustice|evil]] was already ancient when Holbach wrote. [[epicurus|Epicurus]] had formulated it in the third century BCE: "If [[god|God]] is willing to prevent [[injustice|evil]] but not able, then He is not omnipotent. If He is able but not willing, then He is malevolent. If He is both able and willing, then whence comes [[injustice|evil]]?" Augustine's free-will defense ([[injustice|evil]] originates in the misuse of creaturely [[liberty|freedom]]) and [[gottfried-leibniz|Leibniz]]'s [[injustice|theodicy]] (this is the best of all possible [[universe|world]]s) were the dominant [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian responses by the 18th century.

Holbach addressed both. Against the free-will defense, he argued — as developed more fully in his treatment of [[Will]] — that [[liberty|free will]] is a chimera; all human actions are determined by motives acting on temperament. If [[god|God]] created human [[universe|nature]], He created the motives and the temperament; He is therefore the author of the [[vice|sin]]s He punishes.

Against [[gottfried-leibniz|Leibniz]]ian optimism, Holbach responded with the brute fact of [[evil|suffering]]: the [[lisbon|Lisbon]] earthquake of 1755 had killed tens of thousands indiscriminately — pious and impious alike — and no "best possible [[universe|world]]" argument could make that compatible with divine goodness.

Holbach's treatment of goodness is thus part of a broader Enlightenment assault on [[injustice|theodicy]], alongside [[voltaire|Voltaire]]'s *Poem on the [[lisbon|Lisbon]] Disaster* (1756) and [[david-hume|Hume]]'s *Dialogues Concerning Natural [[religion|Religion]]* (published posthumously in 1779 but written earlier). What distinguishes Holbach is his refusal to soften the conclusion: the [[god|God]] of [[religion|theology]] is not merely "inscrutable" but logically impossible.

## Core Arguments

**1. Indiscriminate [[evil|suffering]] refutes divine goodness.** Calamities, diseases, famines, wars, and earthquakes "fall indiscriminately upon the good and the wicked, upon the impious and the pious, upon the innocent and the guilty" (ch. LXXVIII). A [[god|God]] who treats the just and the unjust identically cannot be called just; a [[god|God]] who inflicts [[evil|suffering]] on the innocent cannot be called good. The attempt to console the afflicted by directing them to the very [[god|God]] who afflicted them is a cruel [[absurdity|absurd]]ity.

**2. [[infinite|Infinite]] goodness is incompatible with [[hell|eternal punishment]].** The doctrine of [[punishment|hell]] — that [[god|God]] reserves "eternal [[torture|torment]]s for the majority of men" (060-002) — transforms an allegedly good [[existence|being]] into an infinitely cruel one. A [[god|God]] who, after rendering mortals miserable in this life, makes them infinitely more miserable in the next, has abandoned goodness for "implacable fury." The theological claim that divine goodness must yield to [[justice|divine justice]] is a transparent evasion: "goodness which takes the place of the most terrible cruelty, is not infinite kindness."

**3. The free-will defense fails.** If [[god|God]] created human [[universe|nature]] with its present weaknesses, then He is responsible for the [[vice|sin]]s that arise from those weaknesses. To punish [[existence|being]]s for acting according to the [[universe|nature]] He gave them — a [[universe|nature]] they could not alter — is not justice but [[despotism|tyranny]]. A truly good [[god|God]] "would not punish weaknesses which He knows to be inherent in human [[universe|nature]]" (ch. LXXIX).

**4. Goodness must be experienced, not merely asserted.** Holbach approvingly cites [[cicero|Cicero]]: "If [[god|God]] does not make Himself agreeable to man, He cannot be his [[god|God]]" (ch. LX). Goodness that is invisible in practice — goodness that no living creature can perceive — is indistinguishable from non-existence. "Goodness constitutes [[divinity|Divinity]]; this Goodness can manifest itself to man only by the advantages he derives from it. As soon as he is unfortunate, this Goodness disappears and ceases to be [[divinity|Divinity]]."

## Reception and Critique

Holbach's argument against divine goodness was among the most forceful of the Enlightenment, and it drew sharp responses. Catholic apologists accused him of ignoring the distinction between physical and [[evil|moral evil]] — the former [[existence|being]] a consequence of the latter, mediated by [[sin|original sin]]. Holbach anticipated this reply: if [[sin|original sin]] is itself part of [[god|God]]'s design, [[god|God]] remains the ultimate author of all [[evil|suffering]] that follows from it.

Later theodicies — notably John Hick's "soul-ma[[monarch|king]]" [[injustice|theodicy]] in the 20th century — argued that [[evil|suffering]] is necessary for moral and [[soul|spirit]]ual development. Holbach would likely have responded that this makes [[god|God]] a cruel pedagogue who tortures His pupils for their own good, and that it fails to account for [[evil|suffering]] that destroys rather than develops (the death of children, the devastation of innocents).

The broader philosophical significance of Holbach's critique is its methodological [[materialism|naturalism]]: he does not concede any ground to revelation or [[secrecy|mystery]]. If a concept (divine goodness) is logically incoherent with observable facts (the existence of [[evil|suffering]]), then the concept must be abandoned — not preserved by appeals to "inscrutable depths" or "[[mysteries]] of [[religion|faith]]." This commitment to rational consistency, applied without exception to theological claims, is the hallmark of Holbach's [[matter|materialism]].

## Relation to Other Concepts

Goodness in Holbach's system is the attribute whose logical impossibility most directly undermines [[divinity|theism]]. If [[god|God]] is not good, He is not [[god|God]] — "Goodness constitutes [[divinity|Divinity]]" — yet goodness is the attribute most visibly contradicted by human experience. This connects goodness to several other Holbachian critiques: [[Evil]] provides the factual premise ([[evil|suffering]] exists); [[Justice]] provides the normative [[model|standard]] that [[divinity|divine]] conduct fails to meet; [[Sin]] is the [[fiction|theological fiction]] invented to blame humans for [[god|God]]'s design; [[Punishment]] becomes unjustifiable when the punisher created the conditions for the crime; [[Order]] is the appearance the theologians invoke to prove goodness, while ignoring the disorder that disproves it.

## In the Text

On the contradiction between divine goodness and human [[evil|suffering]]:

> "Do we not see these plagues fall indiscriminately upon the good and the wicked, upon the impious and the pious, upon the innocent and the guilty? How can we be made to admire, in this proceeding, the justice and the goodness of a being, the idea of whom appears so consoling to the unfortunate?" (078-002)

On the incompatibility of infinite goodness with any [[evil|suffering]]:

> "An infinite Goodness can be neither partial nor exclusive. If God is infinitely good, He owes happiness to all His creatures; one unfortunate being alone would be sufficient to annihilate an unlimited goodness." (060-002)

On the [[absurdity|absurd]]ity of consoling the afflicted with the [[god|God]] who afflicted them:

> "Unfortunate father! you console yourself in the bosom of Providence for the loss of a cherished child or of a wife, who made your happiness! Alas! do you not see that your God has killed them? Your God has rendered you miserable; and you want Him to console you for the fearful blows He has inflicted upon you." (078-002)

On the transformation of goodness into cruelty:

> "A God who, after having been infinitely good, becomes infinitely wicked, can He be regarded as an immutable being? A God filled with implacable fury, is He a God in whom we can find a shadow of charity or goodness?" (060-002)
