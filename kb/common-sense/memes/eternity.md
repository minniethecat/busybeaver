# Eternity

## Definition

In Holbach's philosophy, [[eternal|eternity]] is a concept weaponized by [[religion|theology]] to magnify human [[fear|terror]]. While [[eternal|eternity]] in the abstract sense — infinite temporal duration — is a legitimate metaphysical concept ([[nature|the universe]] itself, in Holbach's view, is eternal), its theological application to posthumous punishment is a moral abomi[[nations|nation]]. "The inventors of the [[doctrine|dogma]] of eternal [[torture|torment]]s in [[punishment|hell]], have made of the [[god|God]] whom they call so good, the most detestable of [[existence|being]]s" (002-106). Eternity, when joined to punishment, transforms a finite fault into an infinite penalty — a disproportion so vast that it can only be the product of human cruelty, not [[justice|divine justice]].

Holbach distinguishes between the [[eternal|eternity]] of matter (which is a descriptive claim about [[nature|the universe]]) and the [[eternal|eternity]] of punishment (which is a prescriptive tool of social control). The former is a reasonable inference from the principle that nothing comes from nothing; the latter is an unreasonable invention designed to make humans submit to priestly authority through fear. The concept of [[hell|eternal punishment]] is not a [[revelation|revealed truth]] but a psychological weapon: "[[cruelty|Cruelty]] in man is the last term of corruption; but cruelty merits the greater indig[[nations|nation]] when we consider it gratuitous or without motive" (002-106).

## Historical Context

The doctrine of [[hell|eternal punishment]] had been a central feature of [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian [[religion|theology]] [[vice|sin]]ce Augustine, who argued in *The City of [[god|God]]* that the [[torture|torment]]s of the damned were both literally endless and justly deserved by [[sin|original sin]]. The medieval imagi[[nations|nation]], culminating in Dante's *Inferno*, elaborated the physical details of eternal [[evil|suffering]] with lurid precision. By the eighteenth century, however, this tradition was under pressure from multiple directions: Enlightenment humanitarianism recoiled from the cruelty of the doctrine, while rationalist criticism questioned its coherence with [[divinity|divine]] [[goodness|benevolence]].

Holbach's treatment of [[eternal|eternity]] belongs to a broader campaign against the doctrine of [[punishment|hell]] that included [[pierre-bayle|Pierre Bayle]]'s skeptical arguments and [[voltaire|Voltaire]]'s satirical attacks. What distinguishes Holbach is his systematic connection of [[hell|eternal punishment]] to the interests of [[clergy|the clergy]]: [[clergy|priests]], he argues, invent [[punishment|hell]] to make themselves indispensable as mediators between terrified humans and the [[god|God]] they have made terrifying. The doctrine also had political resonance in pre-revolutionary [[paris|France]], where the alliance of throne and altar used the threat of eternal [[hell|damnation]] to discourage rebellion against established authority.

## Core Arguments

1. **[[eternal|Eternal]] punishment is infinitely disproportionate.** A finite [[crime|offense]], committed by a finite [[existence|being]] during a finite lifespan, cannot merit infinite punishment. "If there exists a [[god|God]] who could be offended or outraged by the actions of men, He ought to be too great to attach infinite importance to the conduct of such puny creatures" (ch. 66). The disproportion between crime and penalty is not merely unjust — it is [[absurdity|absurd]].

2. **A benevolent [[god|God]] cannot ordain eternal [[evil|suffering]].** "A [[god|God]] infinitely good, can not be susceptible of hatred or of vengeance; He can not take pleasure in dooming His feeble creatures to useless [[torture|torment]]s for all [[eternal|eternity]]" (ch. 66). The concept of a loving father who tortures his children eternally is a contradiction in terms.

3. **[[eternal|Eternal]] punishment serves no corrective purpose.** [[punishment|Punishment]] in human justice aims at deterrence, rehabilitation, or the protection of society. [[eternal|Eternal]] punishment serves none of these ends: "[[god|God]], in the exercise of His eternal vengeance, would have no other aim than to glut His cruelty without [[utility]]" (ch. 66). It is punishment for its own sake — that is, it is cruelty.

4. **The threat of [[eternal|eternity]] is a tool of priestly control.** By manufacturing the threat of [[eternal|everlasting]] [[torture|torment]], [[clergy|priests]] create the very anxiety they then claim to reli[[eve]]. "Is not the [[doctrine|dogma]] of the im[[death|mortality]] of the soul made to embitter the present life, and to fill the mind with [[fear|terror]]s for the future?" (ch. 108). Eternity, as preached by [[clergy|the clergy]], is not a doctrine but an extortion racket.

5. **Eternity, properly understood, belongs to [[universe|nature]], not [[religion|theology]].** The universe itself is eternal — matter has always existed and always will. But this [[eternal|eternity]] is impersonal and indifferent to human conduct. The theological appropriation of [[eternal|eternity]] for purposes of reward and punishment is a distortion of a legitimate metaphysical concept.

## Reception and Critique

Holbach's attack on [[hell|eternal punishment]] drew less direct rebuttal than his cosmological arguments, perhaps because the cruelty of the doctrine was embarrassing [[eve]]n to many believers. The gradual softening of [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian eschatology in the nineteenth and twentieth centuries — the rise of [[death|annihilation]]ism, the decline of fire-and-brimstone preaching, and the increasing discomfort with the idea of eternal conscious [[torture|torment]] — represents a tacit vindication of Holbach's moral critique, [[eve]]n among those who would never accept his [[matter|materialism]].

Philosophically, Holbach's argument raises the question of whether [[eternal|eternity]] can be meaningfully applied to any conscious state. If consciousness is a temporal process — a succession of mental states — then the idea of an unchanging eternal consciousness may be incoherent. Holbach gestures toward this line of argument but does not develop it, preferring to emphasize the moral dimension.

## Relation to Other Concepts

Eternity is most directly tied to [[Hell]], the place of [[hell|eternal punishment]] that Holbach considers the cruelest invention of [[religion|theology]]. It connects to [[Time]] as its infinite extension, and to [[Nature]] as the genuinely eternal reality in Holbach's cosmology. The concept is linked to [[Immortality]] because the doctrine of the [[soul|immortal soul]] provides the metaphysical substrate for [[hell|eternal punishment]]: without a soul that survives death, there is nothing to punish eternally. Eternity also relates to [[Justice]] because infinite punishment for finite [[crime|transgression]]s represents the absolute negation of justice. Finally, it connects to [[Fear]], the psychological mechanism through which the threat of eternal [[evil|suffering]] is made to operate on human minds.
