## Schism as Religious Origin

Holbach makes the provocative claim in *Common Sense* that the founders of religions are themselves schismatics — religious dissenters who broke from the orthodoxies of their time. He writes: "Moses was but an Egyptian schismatic, Christianity is but a reformed Judaism" (002-439). This genealogical argument serves Holbach's broader project of naturalizing religion: if every faith originates as a schism from an earlier faith, then the claim of any religion to unique divine origin collapses. Each new orthodoxy is simply the successful schism of yesterday.

## The Politics of Schism

For Holbach, the charge of "schismatic" is fundamentally a political weapon wielded by established churches against challengers. The label serves to delegitimize religious innovation while obscuring the fact that the dominant church itself began as a schismatic movement. Holbach's analysis anticipates later sociological theories of religious organization, which view the heretic-schismatic distinction as a function of institutional power rather than doctrinal truth.

## Schism and Toleration

Holbach's treatment of schism connects directly to his arguments for religious toleration. If every religion originates in schism, then the persecution of schismatics is not only cruel but logically inconsistent — it is the persecution of the very mechanism by which all religions come into existence. Holbach uses this historical analysis to undermine the moral authority of churches that claim the right to suppress dissent.
