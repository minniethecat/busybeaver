# Desire

## Definition

Desire is the natural incli[[nations|nation]] of a sentient [[existence|being]] toward an object it perceives as beneficial, or away from an object it perceives as harmful. In Holbach's materialist psychology, [[passion|desire]] is not a corruption of human [[universe|nature]] but its fundamental principle: "This same [[universe|nature]] compels me to avoid, to remove, and to combat the one, and to seek, to [[passion|desire]], and to procure the other" (ch. 98). Every human action, from the pursuit of food to the quest for philosophical truth, is motivated by [[passion|desire]] in some form. A [[existence|being]] without [[passion|desire]] would be a [[existence|being]] without will, incapable of action.

## The Desire to Dominate: Origin of Religion

Holbach offers a strikingly political genealogy of religion, tracing it not to [[revelation|divine revelation]] or to the natural yearning for transcendence, but to the [[passion|desire]] of early legislators to control populations:

> "The first legislators of nations had for their object to dominate. The easiest means of succeeding was to frighten the people and to prevent them from reasoning; they led them by tortuous paths in order that they should not perceive the designs of their guides." (015-001)

The title of this chapter is explicit: "All religion was born of the [[passion|desire]] to dominate." [[religion|Religion]], in this analysis, is not a response to the sacred but an instrument of social control — a system of fear and [[secrecy|mystery]] designed to make populations governable. The [[passion|desire]] for power, channeled through the invention of gods and afterlives, is the hidden motor of religious history.

## Desire as the Foundation of Ethics

Holbach's [[materialism|naturalism]] grounds [[morality|ethics]] in [[passion|desire]] rather than in [[divinity|divine]] command. What is good for a human [[existence|being]] is what satisfies genuine human [[passion|desire]]s and promotes [[happiness|well-being]]; what is bad is what frustrates them. This does not mean that [[eve]]ry passing impulse should be indulged — reason must discriminate between [[passion|desire]]s that lead to lasting happiness and those that produce transient pleasure followed by lasting harm. But the [[model|standard]] of value is, ultimately, human [[passion|desire]] itself:

> "In a world where everything is from necessity, a God who remedies nothing, and allows things to follow their own course, is He anything else but destiny or necessity personified? It is a deaf God who can effect no change on the general laws to which He is subjected Himself. What do I care for the infinite power of a being who can do but a very few things to please me?"
>
> (056-002)

The rhetorical force of this passage depends on the legitimacy of [[passion|desire]] as a moral criterion: if [[god|God]] cannot satisfy human [[passion|desire]]s — cannot improve the human condition — then [[god|God]] is irrelevant to [[morality|ethics]]. Desire, not [[divinity|divine]] will, is the measure of value.

## Desire and the Afterlife

Holbach argues that the [[passion|desire]] for cosmic justice — for the wicked to be punished and the virtuous rewarded — is the psychological root of the [[death|afterlife]] doctrine:

> "The existence of another life has no other guaranty than the imagination of men, who, in supposing it, have but manifested their desire." (ch. 101)

Human [[existence|being]]s [[passion|desire]] that the injustices of this [[universe|world]] be corrected; they project this [[passion|desire]] into an imaginary future state. But a wish, however earnestly felt, is not [[proof|evidence]]. The doctrine of the [[death|afterlife]] is, in Holbach's analysis, wish-fulfillment — a consoling fantasy generated by the [[passion|desire]] for justice rather than a conclusion supported by any observation or argument.

## In the Text

On religion as political manipulation:

> "All religion was born of the desire to dominate." (ch. 15)

On [[passion|desire]] as the motor of action:

> "This same nature compels me to avoid, to remove, and to combat the one, and to seek, to desire, and to procure the other." (ch. 98)

On the [[death|afterlife]] as projected [[passion|desire]]:

> "The existence of another life has no other guaranty than the imagination of men, who, in supposing it, have but manifested their desire." (ch. 101)

## Relation to Other Concepts

Desire is the psychological foundation of Holbach's entire ethical and political critique. It connects his [[Materialism]] ([[passion|desire]] is a function of our material [[temperament|constitution]]) with his genealogy of [[Religion]] (born of the [[passion|desire]] to dominate), his critique of the [[Soul]] and [[death|afterlife]] (projections of [[passion|desire]]), and his positive vision of [[Morality]] and [[Happiness]] (the satis[[sect|faction]] of properly ordered [[passion|desire]]s). The concept also links to [[Passion]] ([[passion|desire]] intensified and stabilized) and [[Fear]] (the [[passion|desire]] to avoid).
