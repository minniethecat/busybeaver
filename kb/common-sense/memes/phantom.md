# Phantom

## Definition

In Holbach's *Common Sense*, "phantoms" are the imaginary beings manufactured by religious systems to frighten the credulous into submission. Priests "create phantoms, they fill them with contradictions" in order to keep the human mind occupied with invisible terrors rather than with the real conditions of happiness on earth (002-033). The concept of phantoms is central to Holbach's explanation of how religion sustains itself: not through rational persuasion but through psychological manipulation — the systematic cultivation of fear directed at entities that do not exist.

## Religious Phantoms as Instruments of Control

Holbach repeatedly returns to the image of phantoms to describe the invisible beings that populate religious cosmologies. These include "angels, demons, genii, and other phantoms, which are the object of the researches and the worship of the credulous" (002-435). Their function is political: "he would banish to imaginary regions these phantoms, which could be of use to you only by frightening you" (002-412). By directing human attention and anxiety toward imaginary beings, priests divert energy away from the examination of their own authority. The man who "passes his life in trembling at phantoms which are made but to intimidate him" (002-391) cannot think clearly about the natural causes of his suffering or the human agents who profit from his credulity.

## The Fragility of Phantoms

A distinctive feature of Holbach's phantom metaphor is its implicit account of why religious concepts are so resistant to rational refutation yet so fragile when examined. Phantoms are "chimera, of which nothing can be affirmed without causing the phantom to vanish which they had taken so much pains to establish" (002-061). The very act of trying to make a phantom definite — of specifying its properties, its location, its mode of action — causes it to dissolve. This is why theology retreats perpetually into vagueness and mystery: precision is fatal to phantoms. The theologian's insistence that God is incomprehensible is not a confession of humility but a survival strategy for concepts that cannot survive scrutiny. Holbach's philosophical program — to demand clarity, to insist on evidence, to "affirm" only what can be demonstrated — is therefore not merely a methodological preference but a solvent for the entire phantom-populated cosmos of religion.

## The Remedy: Reason

The antidote to phantoms is not another supernatural system but the exercise of reason. Holbach calls for removing "its clouds and phantoms that we may find the sources of our happiness" (002-453). The task of philosophy is to dispel imaginary terrors so that humans can attend to the real, material conditions of their well-being. Those who "entertain them with fables, phantoms, intrigues, observances, idle ceremonies" (002-422) are the enemies of human flourishing — not its guardians.

> They create phantoms, they fill them with contradictions, and the human mind, which loves to go astray, finds itself perfectly satisfied.
>
> (002-033)
