# Nazareth

## Geography and History

Nazareth is a town in the Lower [[galilee|Galilee]] region of present-day northern [[israel|Israel]], approximately 15 miles west of the [[galilee|Sea of Galilee]] and 6 miles west of Mount Tabor. In the first century CE, it was a small Jewish village of perhaps 200–400 inhabitants, economically dependent on the nearby administrative center of Sepphoris. Nazareth is not mentioned in the Hebrew [[bible|Bible]], the Talmud, or any non-[[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian source from the first century, and its historical obscurity has been cited by critics of [[christianity|Christianity]] as problematic for [[gospel|Gospel]] historicity.

## Religious Significance

For [[christianity|Christianity]], Nazareth is venerated as the town where the Annunciation occurred — the angel Gabriel's announcement to [[mary|the Virgin]] [[mary|Mary]] that she would conceive [[jesus-christ|Jesus]] ([[luke|Luke]] 1:26–38). It is also identified as the place where Jesus spent his childhood and early adulthood before beginning his public ministry, earning him the epithet "[[jesus-christ|Jesus of Nazareth]]." The modern city is home to the Basilica of the Annunciation, one of [[christianity|Christianity]]'s most significant pilgrimage sites.

## Role in "Common Sense"

Holbach's "Common Sense" discusses Nazareth in the context of [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian [[bible|scripture]]'s internal contradictions. He observes that [[luke|Luke]]'s [[gospel|Gospel]] states that [[joseph|Joseph]] and [[mary|Mary]], after presenting the infant Jesus at the [[temple|Temple]] in [[jerusalem|Jerusalem]], "returned to [[galilee|Galilee]], into their town of Nazareth, where their child Jesus grew [[eve]]ry day in grace and in [[philosophy|wisdom]]" — ma[[monarch|king]] no mention of any flight into [[egypt|Egypt]] (ch. XI, §Evangelists). [[luke|Luke]] further records that the family made annual Passover pilgrimages to [[jerusalem|Jerusalem]], which is inconsistent with a multi-year exile in [[egypt|Egypt]].

Holbach contrasts this with [[matthew|Matthew]]'s account, which has the Holy Family fleeing to [[egypt|Egypt]] to escape [[herod|Herod]]'s massacre of the innocents. He argues that "if what the Evangelist said had been true," the historian [[josephus|Josephus]], who "blamed the vices of this [[monarch|king]]," would not have "been silent on such a dark and detestable action" (ch. XI, §Evangelists). For Holbach, the Nazareth-[[egypt|Egypt]] contradiction is not a minor geographical discrepancy but [[proof|evidence]] that [[gospel|the Gospel]] narratives are "[[absurdity|absurd]] falsehoods" invented without concern for consistency.

The Nazareth references in "Common Sense" serve Holbach's broader project of [[demon|demons]]trating that [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian [[bible|scripture]]s, far from [[existence|being]] [[divinity|divine]]ly inspired, bear the marks of human fabrication — contradictory, historically unreliable, and unworthy of the [[belief|credence]] believers grant them.

## Related Figures and Events

Nazareth is inseparably linked to [[jesus-christ|Jesus Christ]], who is repeatedly called "[[jesus-christ|Jesus of Nazareth]]" in the [[bible|New Testament]] (a detail Holbach does not dispute, using it only to anchor his critique of [[gospel|Gospel]] geography). The town also appears in relation to the [[galilee|Galilee]] ministry — [[matthew|Matthew]] states that Jesus "leaving the city of Nazareth, went to live at [[capernaum|Capernaum]]," while [[luke|Luke]] says "he came at first to Nazareth, and afterward went to [[capernaum|Capernaum]]" (ch. XI).


## Selected Passage

Holbach points out contradictions in the Gospel accounts of Jesus's early movements:

> In regard to the place of His first retreat after His departure from the desert, Matthew says that He returned to Galilee, and that leaving the city of Nazareth, He went to live at Capernaum, a maritime city; and Luke says, that He came at first to Nazareth, and afterward went to Capernaum.
