# Politics Index

This page aggregates all entries tagged with `politics` — the sphere where Holbach's anti-clerical philosophy meets its practical application. The central thesis of *Common Sense* is that religion and [[politics|political power]] form a mutually reinforcing system of [[persecution|oppression]]: the [[throne-and-altar]] alliance. [[priest|Priest]]s sanctify royal authority, and [[monarch|king]]s enforce priestly [[doctrine|dogma]]. The entries below cover Holbach's analysis of this partnership, his critique of [[theocracy]] and [[divine-right]], and his vision of a secular political order grounded in [[natural-law]], [[toleration]], and the [[separation-of-church-and-state]].

## Key Sub-Themes

### The Throne-and-Altar Alliance

Holbach's core political insight: religious and [[government|political authority]] are structurally interdependent. Entries include [[throne-and-altar]], [[divine-right]], [[theocracy]], [[established-church]], [[civil-religion]], and [[confessional-state]].

### Secular Governance

Holbach's positive vision of political order free from clerical domi[[nations|nation]]: [[toleration]], [[separation-of-church-and-state]], [[secularism]], [[natural-law]], [[freedom-of-conscience]], and [[freedom-of-expression]].

### Power and Oppression

The mechanisms by which religion sustains political domi[[nations|nation]]: [[authority]], [[obedience]], [[submission]], [[fear]], [[ignorance]], and the manipulation of [[hope]] and [[terror]].

### Reform and Revolution

Holbach's reformist — not revolutionary — politics: [[reform]], [[progress]], [[enlightenment]], [[public-education]], and the role of the philosopher as public [[remorse|conscience]].
