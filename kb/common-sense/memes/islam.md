# Islam

## Definition

Islam, in Holbach's analysis, serves a crucial comparative function: it [[demon|demons]]trates that the features he critiques in [[christianity|Christianity]] — [[prophecy|prophetic]] claims, revealed [[bible|scripture]], miraculous narratives, priestly authority — are not unique to one religion but structural features of [[revelation|revealed religion]] as such.

Holbach invokes Islam not to [[guilt|sin]]gle it out for special condem[[nations|nation]] but to establish a [[model|pattern]]. Wherever one finds a [[prophecy|prophet]], a sacred book, and a class of interpreters claiming [[divinity|divine]] authority, one finds the same machinery of [[Superstition]] and control. The existence of multiple competing [[dreams|revelations]], each claiming exclusive truth, is itself a [[proof|demonstration]] that none can be what it claims to be.

## Historical Context

Holbach wrote in an era when [[europe|Europe]]an knowledge of Islam was shaped by centuries of polemical literature — the same crusading mentality that had produced hostile caricatures of [[mohammed|Muhammad]] as an [[charlatan|impostor]] and the Quran as a [[charlatan|fraud]]. Holbach drew on this tradition's critical tools while redirecting them: his target was not Islam per se but the shared structure of [[revelation|revealed religion]].

The comparative study of religion was in its in[[imagination|fancy]] in the eighteenth century, and Holbach's treatment of Islam is less an exercise in scholarship than a rhetorical strategy. By showing that [[christianity|Christianity]] and Islam share the same building blocks — [[prophecy|prophet]]s, [[bible|scripture]]s, [[miracle|miracles]], [[clergy|priests]] — he undermines the claim that any one revelation is uniquely [[divinity|divine]]. If the mechanism is the same, the product is the same, regardless of the label.

## Comparative Method

Holbach's treatment of Islam exemplifies his comparative method. Just as [[christianity|Christianity]] spread from Roman [[palestine]] through imperial patronage and popular [[belief|credulity]], Islam spread from [[arabia|Arabia]] through military conquest and the appeal of a simplified mono[[divinity|theism]]. Both religions claim a founder who received [[divinity|divine]] communications; both possess a sacred text alleged to be of supernatural origin.

The "marvelous" elements of Islam — the splitting of the moon, the night journey, the angelic dictation of the Quran — are structurally identical to the [[miracle|miracles]] claimed by [[christianity|Christianity]]. Rather than arguing against [[christianity|Christianity]] from within its own assumptions, Holbach treats it as one instance of a universal phenomenon: the human invention of gods and religions.

## Political Dimension

Holbach notes a further parallel between Islam and [[christianity|Christianity]] in their political functions. Both religions have served to legitimize [[tyranny]]: the Islamic caliphate and the [[jesus-christ|Christ]]ian [[divine-right|divine right of kings]] are different names for the same arrangement. Religious authority sanctifies [[politics|political power]], and [[politics|political power]] enforces religious conformity.

The [[crusades|Crusades]] — [[christianity|Christianity]]'s military response to Islam — [[demon|demons]]trate that religions whose ostensible message is peace become engines of violence when their claims to exclusive truth are challenged. Holbach's point is not to favor one religion over another but to show that [[revelation|revealed religion]] per se is incompatible with peaceful coexistence.

## In the Text

> "All the religions of the world are evidently the work of men; they have been invented by legislators and by poets, and afterward propagated by enthusiasts and priests." (ch. CXLVI)

This passage articulates Holbach's universal thesis. Islam, [[christianity|Christianity]], [[judaism|Judaism]], and [[paganism]] are all products of the same human machinery — legislators see[[monarch|king]] [[society|social order]], poets embellishing with marvels, enthusiasts generating fervor, and [[clergy|priests]] institutionalizing control over successive generations.

> "The same spirit has presided at the formation of all religions; the same passions, the same fears, the same credulity, the same ignorance, the same roguery." (ch. CXLVII)

The repetition of "the same" five times is rhetorically deliberate. Holbach denies [[christianity|Christianity]] any qualitative distinction from Islam or any other religion. The uniformity of religious phenomena across [[worship|cult]]ures is, for him, decisive [[proof|evidence]] that religion is a human product rather than a [[divinity|divine]] communication.

## Reception and Critique

Holbach's comparative treatment of Islam has been assessed in two distinct registers by modern scholarship. On one hand, his use of Islam as a rhetorical mirror for [[christianity|Christianity]] — revealing the shared structural features of all [[revelation|revealed religion]]s — represents an early instance of the comparative study of religion, anticipating the more systematic approaches of nineteenth-century scholarship. On the other hand, Holbach's treatment relies on the same stereotyped representations of Islam that characterized [[europe|Europe]]an Orientalist discourse, and he shows little interest in Islam on its own terms rather than as a polemical instrument against [[christianity|Christianity]].

Jonathan [[israel|Israel]], in *Radical Enlightenment* (2001), notes that Holbach's comparative method was unusually systematic for its time and contributed to the development of a naturalistic history of religion. Edward Said's critique of Orientalism (1978) does not address Holbach directly but provides a framework for [[knowledge|understanding]] the limitations of his comparative approach. The tension between Holbach's universalizing ambition and his reliance on received [[europe|Europe]]an stereotypes of Islam remains a productive site for scholarly analysis.
