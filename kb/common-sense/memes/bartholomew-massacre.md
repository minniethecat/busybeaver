# St. Bartholomew's Day Massacre

## Overview

The St. Bartholomew's Day Massacre (August 24, 1572) was the coordinated killing of thousands of French Protestants (Huguenots) in [[paris|Paris]] and subsequently across [[france|France]], carried out by Catholic mobs with the approval of the royal court. The massacre began in the early hours of the feast of St. Bartholomew, targeting Huguenot nobles who had gathered in [[paris|Paris]] for the wedding of the Protestant Henry of Navarre to the Catholic Margaret of Valois — a marriage intended to reconcile [[france|France]]'s [[sect|religious faction]]s.

The violence spread from [[paris|Paris]] to the provinces and continued for weeks. Contemporary estimates placed the death toll between 5,000 and 30,000. Pope Gregory XIII reportedly celebrated the news with a Te Deum mass and had a commemorative medal struck. For Enlightenment thinkers, the massacre became the definitive symbol of what religious conviction can justify when unchecked by humane sentiment.

## Historical Context

The massacre occurred during the French Wars of [[religion|Religion]] (1562–1598), a series of civil wars between the Catholic majority and the Huguenot minority. The immediate trigger was the attempted assassination of Admiral Gaspard de Coligny, the Huguenot military leader, on August 22. When the assassination failed, Catherine de' Medici — the queen mother and de facto [[monarch|ruler]] — feared Huguenot retaliation and apparently authorized the preemptive killing of the Huguenot leadership gathered in [[paris|Paris]].

What began as a targeted political assassination escalated into a general massacre as Catholic [[paris|Paris]]ians turned on their Huguenot neighbors. The violence was not spontaneous — it followed identifiable [[model|pattern]]s of organized killing directed by the Catholic League and local officials. Houses were marked in advance; militia units were mobilized; the city gates were closed to prevent escape. The massacre had the character of a planned operation rather than a popular riot.

## Core Arguments

Holbach invokes the St. Bartholomew's Day Massacre as the definitive refutation of the claim that religion improves morality. His argument proceeds through three claims.

First, the massacre was not an aberration but the logical outcome of a belief system that divides humanity into the saved and the damned. When eternal consequences are attached to doctrinal disagreement, violence against [[heresy|heretic]]s becomes not merely permissible but meritorious. The killers acted "in the name of the [[god|God]] of mercy."

Second, the massacre [[demon|demons]]trates the impotence of religious morality in constraining human cruelty. The same [[church|Church]] that taught "thou shalt not kill" celebrated the killing of [[heresy|heretic]]s with public thanksgiving. Religious morality is, for Holbach, entirely subordinate to institutional interest: it forbids what threatens priestly power and permits what advances it.

Third, the massacre illustrates the complicity of [[government|political authority]] in religious violence. The French crown did not merely tolerate the killing — it directed it. The "throne and altar" alliance that Holbach critiques throughout *Common Sense* reached its murderous apogee in the August days of 1572.

## In the Text

Holbach references the Bartholomew Massacre among the catalogue of religious crimes in the early chapters of *Common Sense*:

> "The massacre of St. Bartholomew, the crimes of Ireland, the crusades against the Albigenses, the butcheries of the New World... have a thousand times, in all parts of our globe, been produced by religious zeal." (Chapter II, paraphrase)

The massacre appears alongside [[crusades|the Crusades]], [[inquisition|the Inquisition]], and the conquest of the Americas as [[proof|evidence]] that religion does not restrain human cruelty — it licenses and sanctifies it. Men who would hesitate to harm a neighbor for personal gain will kill enthusiastically when they beli[[eve]] [[god|God]] commands it.

## Legacy

The Bartholomew Massacre became a central reference point in Enlightenment polemics against [[intolerance|religious intolerance]]. [[voltaire|Voltaire]], who was born nearly a century after the [[eve]]nt, reportedly observed an annual fast on its anniversary and used it repeatedly in his writings against the [[catholic-church|Catholic Church]].

In the nineteenth century, the massacre was reinterpreted by anticlerical French republicans as emblematic of the dangers of clerical influence in politics. The memory of 1572 was mobilized in the campaigns that led to the 1905 French law separating church and state.

[[mode|Mode]]rn historians have complicated the narrative of the massacre as purely religious violence, emphasizing the political, economic, and social dimensions of the French Wars of [[religion|Religion]]. The killing of 1572 was simultaneously an act of [[zeal|religious zeal]], a political coup, and an explosion of social tensions in a city where Catholic and Protestant populations lived in close proximity.

From Holbach's perspective, this scholarly nuance would be beside the point. Whether the killers were motivated by [[guilt|sin]]cere [[religion|faith]], political calculation, or a mixture of both, the fact remains that religion provided the ideological framework that transformed murder into a sacred duty. "[[crime|Crime]]," Holbach w[[ritual|rites]], "has never been committed with more [[certainty|confidence]] than when it has been believed to be agreeable to [[god|God]]."
