# Thomas Paine

Thomas Paine (1737–1809) was an English-born political philosopher and revolutionary whose writings — particularly *Common Sense* and *The Age of [[reason|Reason]]* — helped ignite the American Revolution and later articulated a powerful case for deism and freethought. Robert Ingersoll regarded Paine as a founding father of American liberty whose contributions had been unjustly erased from public memory.

## 生平

Born in Thetford, England, Paine emigrated to the American colonies in 1774 and quickly became the most influential pamphleteer of the Revolutionary era. His *Common Sense* (1776) sold hundreds of thousands of copies and transformed colonial grievances into a coherent argument for independence.

Ingersoll noted that "when Thomas Paine was in favor of human liberty, Wesley was against it" — contrasting Paine's revolutionary commitment to freedom with the conservative religious establishment of his day [015-023]. For Ingersoll, the verdict of history was unambiguous: "The day has come when Thomas Paine will be remembered with Washington, Franklin and Jefferson" [004-167].

## 主要观点与贡献

Paine's political philosophy rested on natural rights, popular sovereignty, and a fierce opposition to hereditary privilege. His later work *The Age of [[reason|Reason]]* (1794) subjected the [[bible|Bible]] to rational criticism and advocated deism — the belief in a Creator knowable through nature and reason rather than revelation. This book made Paine a hero to freethinkers but earned him vilification from orthodox Christians.

Ingersoll also highlighted Paine's abolitionism: "Thomas Paine attacked the institution with all the intensity and passion of his nature" [022-185]. Alongside John Adams and other founders, Paine regarded slavery with "horror" — a moral stance that Ingersoll used to shame the [[christianity|Christianity]] of his own day for its accommodation of the slave system.

## Ingersoll 的评价

Ingersoll's admiration for Paine was unreserved. He lamented that "the American people will wonder that their fathers could have been guilty of such base" ingratitude toward Paine, whose contributions to American independence had been deliberately minimized because of his religious views [004-167].

For Ingersoll, Paine represented the highest synthesis of political courage and [[reason|intellect]]ual honesty — a man who risked his reputation and safety to speak truth to power, both political and ecclesiastical.

## 历史影响

Paine's influence on American political thought was profound, though his religious writings made him a pariah in his own time. The freethought movement of the 19th century — of which Ingersoll was the leading American voice — reclaimed Paine as a foundational figure. Ingersoll's insistence that Paine deserved a place "with Washington, Franklin and Jefferson" was part of a broader effort to rehabilitate the secular, Enlightenment roots of the American republic.
