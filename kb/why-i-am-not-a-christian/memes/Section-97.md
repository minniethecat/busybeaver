[096-001]The great column of his argument should be unbroken.  He can adorn


[096-002]it with vines and flowers, but they should not be in such profusion


[096-003]as to hide the column.  He should give variety of episode by


[096-004]illustrations, but they should be used only for the purpose of


[096-005]adding strength to the argument.  The man who wishes to become an


[096-006]orator should study language.  He should know the deeper meaning


[096-007]of words.  He should understand the vigor and velocity of verbs


[096-008]and the color of adjectives.  He should know how to sketch a scene,


[096-009]to paint a picture, to give life and action.  He should be a poet


[096-010]and a dramatist, a painter and an actor.  He should cultivate his


[096-011]imagination.  He should become familiar with the great poetry and


[096-012]fiction, with splendid and heroic deeds.  He should be a student


[096-013]of Shakespeare.  He should read and devour the great plays.  From


[096-014]Shakespeare he could learn the art of expression, of compression,


[096-015]and all the secrets of the head and heart.


[096-016]The great orator is full of variety--of surprises.  Like a juggler,


[096-017]he keeps the colored balls in the air.  He expresses himself in


[096-018]pictures.  His speech is a panorama.  By continued change he holds


[096-019]the attention.  The interest does not flag.  He does not allow


[096-020]himself to be anticipated.  A picture is shown but once.  So, an


[096-021]orator should avoid the commonplace.  There should be no stuffing,


[096-022]no filling.  He should put no cotton with his silk, no common metals


[096-023]with his gold.  He should remember that "gilded dust is not as good


[096-024]as dusted gold."  The great orator is honest, sincere.  He does


[096-025]not pretend.  His brain and heart go together.  Every drop of his


[096-026]blood is convinced.  Nothing is forced.  He knows exactly what he


[096-027]wishes to do--knows when he has finished it, and stops.


[096-028]Only a great orator knows when and how to close.  Most speakers go


[096-029]on after they are through.  They are satisfied only with a "lame


[096-030]and impotent conclusion."  Most speakers lack variety.  They travel


[096-031]a straight and dusty road.  The great orator is full of episode.


[096-032]He convinces and charms by indirection.  He leaves the road, visits


[096-033]the fields, wanders in the woods, listens to the murmurs of springs,


[096-034]the songs of birds.  He gathers flowers, scales the crags and comes


[096-035]back to the highway refreshed, invigorated.  He does not move in


[096-036]a straight line.  He wanders and winds like a stream.


[096-037]Of course, no one can tell a man what to do to become an orator.


[096-038]The great orator has that wonderful thing called presence.  He has


[096-039]that strange something known as magnetism.  He must have a flexible,


[096-040]musical voice, capable of expressing the pathetic, the humorous,


[096-041]the heroic.  His body must move in unison with his thought.  He


[096-042]must be a reasoner, a logician.  He must have a keen sense of humor


[096-043]--of the laughable.  He must have wit, sharp and quick.  He must


[096-044]have sympathy.  His smiles should be the neighbors of his tears.


[096-045]He must have imagination.  He should give eagles to the air, and


[096-046]painted moths should flutter in the sunlight.


[096-047]While I cannot tell a man what to do to become an orator, I can


[096-048]tell him a few things not to do.  There should be no introduction


[096-049]to an oration.  The orator should commence with his subject.  There


[096-050]should be no prelude, no flourish, no apology, no explanation.  He


[096-051]should say nothing about himself.  Like a sculptor, he stands by


[096-052]his block of stone.  Every stroke is for a purpose.  As he works


[096-053]the form begins to appear.  When the statue is finished the workman


[096-054]stops.  Nothing is more difficult than a perfect close.  Few poems,


[096-055]few pieces of music, few novels end well.  A good story, a great


[096-056]speech, a perfect poem should end just at the proper point.  The


[096-057]bud, the blossom, the fruit.  No delay.  A great speech is a


[096-058]crystallization in its logic, an efflorescence in its poetry.


[096-059]I have not heard many speeches.  Most of the great speakers in our


[096-060]country were before my time.  I heard Beecher, and he was an orator.


[096-061]He had imagination, humor and intensity.  His brain was as fertile


[096-062]as the valleys of the tropics.  He was too broad, too philosophic,


[096-063]too poetic for the pulpit.  Now and then, he broke the fetters of


[096-064]his creed, escaped from his orthodox prison, and became sublime.


[096-065]Theodore Parker was an orator.  He preached great sermons.  His


[096-066]sermons on "Old Age" and "Webster," and his address on "Liberty"


[096-067]were filled with great thoughts, marvelously expressed.  When he


[096-068]dealt with human events, with realities, with things he knew, he


[096-069]was superb.  When he spoke of freedom, of duty, of living to the


[096-070]ideal, of mental integrity, he seemed inspired.


[096-071]Webster I never heard.  He had great qualities; force, dignity,


[096-072]clearness, grandeur; but, after all, he worshiped the past.  He


[096-073]kept his back to the sunrise.  There was no dawn in his brain.  He


[096-074]was not creative.  He had no spirit of prophecy.  He lighted no


[096-075]torch.  He was not true to his ideal.  He talked sometimes as though


[096-076]his head was among the stars, but he stood in the gutter.  In the


[096-077]name of religion he tried to break the will of Stephen Girard--to


[096-078]destroy the greatest charity in all the world; and in the name of


[096-079]the same religion he defended the Fugitive Slave Law.  His purpose


[096-080]was the same in both cases.  He wanted office.  Yet he uttered a


[096-081]few very great paragraphs, rich with thought, perfectly expressed.


[096-082]Clay I never heard, but he must have had a commanding presence, a


[096-083]chivalric bearing, an heroic voice.  He cared little for the past.


[096-084]He was a natural leader, a wonderful talker--forcible, persuasive,


[096-085]convincing.  He was not a poet, not a master of metaphor, but he


[096-086]was practical.  He kept in view the end to be accomplished.  He


[096-087]was the opposite of Webster.  Clay was the morning, Webster the


[096-088]evening.  Clay had large views, a wide horizon.  He was ample,


[096-089]vigorous, and a little tyrannical.


[096-090]Benton was thoroughly commonplace.  He never uttered an inspired


[096-091]word.  He was an intense egoist.  No subject was great enough to


[096-092]make him forget himself.  Calhoun was a political Calvinist--narrow,


[096-093]logical, dogmatic.  He was not an orator.  He delivered essays,


[096-094]not orations.  I think it was in 1851 that Kossuth visited this


[096-095]country.  He was an orator.  There was no man, at that time, under


[096-096]our flag, who could speak English as well as he.  In the first


[096-097]speech I read of Kossuth's was this line:  "Russia is the rock


[096-098]against which the sigh for freedom breaks."  In this you see the


[096-099]poet, the painter, the orator.


[096-100]S. S. Prentiss was an orator, but, with the recklessness of a


[096-101]gamester, he threw his life away.  He said profound and beautiful


[096-102]things, but he lacked application.  He was uneven, disproportioned,


[096-103]saying ordinary things on great occasions, and now and then, without


[096-104]the slightest provocation, uttering the sublimest and most beautiful


[096-105]thoughts.


[096-106]In my judgment, Corwin was the greatest orator of them all.  He


[096-107]had more arrows in his quiver.  He had genius.  He was full of


[096-108]humor, pathos, wit, and logic.  He was an actor.  His body talked.


[096-109]His meaning was in his eyes and lips.  Gov. O. P. Morton of Indiana


[096-110]had the greatest power of statement of any man I ever heard.  All


[096-111]the argument was in his statement.  The facts were perfectly grouped.


[096-112]The conclusion was a necessity.


[096-113]The best political speech I ever heard was made by Gov. Richard J.


[096-114]Oglesby of Illinois.  It had every element of greatness--reason,


[096-115]humor, wit, pathos, imagination, and perfect naturalness.  That


[096-116]was in the grand years, long ago.  Lincoln had reason, wonderful


[096-117]humor, and wit, but his presence was not good.  His voice was poor,


[096-118]his gestures awkward--but his thoughts were profound.  His speech


[096-119]at Gettysburg is one of the masterpieces of the world.  The word


[096-120]"here" is used four or five times too often.  Leave the "heres"


[096-121]out, and the speech is perfect.


[096-122]Of course, I have heard a great many talkers, but orators are few


[096-123]and far between.  They are produced by victorious nations--born in


[096-124]the midst of great events, of marvelous achievements.  They utter


[096-125]the thoughts, the aspirations of their age.  They clothe the children


[096-126]of the people in the gorgeous robes of giants.  The interpret the


[096-127]dreams.  With the poets, they prophesy.  They fill the future with


[096-128]heroic forms, with lofty deeds.  They keep their faces toward the


[096-129]dawn--toward the ever-coming day.


[096-130]--_New York Sun_, April, 1898.


[096-131]JOHN RUSSELL YOUNG AND EXPANSION.


[096-132]_Question_.  You knew John Russell Young, Colonel?


[096-133]_Answer_.  Yes, I knew him well and we were friends for many years.


[096-134]He was a wonderfully intelligent man--knew something about everything,


[096-135]had read most books worth reading.  He was one of the truest friends.


[096-136]He had a genius for friendship.  He never failed to do a favor when


[096-137]he could, and he never forgot a favor.  He had the genius of


[096-138]gratitude.  His mind was keen, smooth, clear, and he really loved


[096-139]to think.  I had the greatest admiration for his character and I


[096-140]was shocked when I read of his death.  I did not know that he had


[096-141]been ill.  All my heart goes out to his wife--a lovely woman, now


[096-142]left alone with her boy.  After all, life is a fearful thing at


[096-143]best.  The brighter the sunshine the deeper the shadow.


[096-144]_Question_.  Are you in favor of expansion?


[096-145]_Answer_.  Yes, I have always wanted more--I love to see the Republic


[096-146]grow.  I wanted the Sandwich Islands, wanted Porto Rico, and I want


[096-147]Cuba if the Cubans want us.  I want the Philippines if the Filipinos


[096-148]want us--I do not want to conquer and enslave those people.  The


[096-149]war on the Filipinos is a great mistake--a blunder--almost a crime.


[096-150]If the President had declared his policy, then, if his policy was


[096-151]right, there was no need of war.  The President should have told


[096-152]the Filipinos just exactly what he wanted.  It is a small business,


[096-153]after Dewey covered Manila Bay with glory, to murder a lot of half-


[096-154]armed savages.  We had no right to buy, because Spain had no right


[096-155]to sell the Philippines.  We acquired no rights on those islands


[096-156]by whipping Spain.


[096-157]_Question_.  Do you think the President should have stated his


[096-158]policy in Boston the other day?


[096-159]_Answer_.  Yes, I think it would be better if he would unpack his


[096-160]little budget--I like McKinley, but I liked him just as well before


[096-161]he was President.  He is a good man, not because he is President,


[096-162]but because he is a man--you know that real honor must be earned--


[096-163]people cannot give honor--honor is not alms--it is wages.  So, when


[096-164]a man is elected President the best thing he can do is to remain


[096-165]a natural man.  Yes, I wish McKinley would brush all his advisers


[096-166]to one side and say his say; I believe his say would be right.


[096-167]Now, don't change this interview and make me say something mean


[096-168]about McKinley, because I like him.  The other day, in Chicago, I


[096-169]had an interview and I wrote it out.  In that "interview" I said


[096-170]a few things about the position of Senator Hoar.  I tried to show


[096-171]that he was wrong--but I took pains to express by admiration for


[096-172]Senator Hoar.  When the interview was published I was made to say


[096-173]that Senator Hoar was a mud-head.  I never said or thought anything


[096-174]of the kind.  Don't treat me as that Chicago reporter did.


[096-175]_Question_.  What do you think of Atkinson's speech?


[096-176]_Answer_.  Well, some of it is good--but I never want to see the


[096-177]soldiers of the Republic whipped.  I am always on our side.


[096-178]--_The Press_, Philadelphia, February 20, 1899.

[096-180][* As an incident in the life of any one favored with the


[096-181]privilege, a visit to the home of Col. Robert G. Ingersoll


[096-182]is certain to be recalled as a most pleasant and profitable


[096-183]experience.  Although not a sympathizer with the great


[096-184]Agnostic's religious views, yet I have long admired his


[096-185]ability, his humor, his intellectual honesty and courage.


[096-186]And it was with gratification that I accepted the good


[096-187]offices of a common friend who recently offered to introduce


[096-188]me to the Ingersoll domestic circle in Gramercy Park.  Here


[096-189]I found the genial Colonel, surrounded by his children, his


[096-190]grandchildren, and his amiable wife, whose smiling greeting


[096-191]dispelled formality and breathed "Welcome" in every


[096-192]syllable.  The family relationship seemed absolutely ideal--


[096-193]the very walls emitting an atmosphere of art and music, of


[096-194]contentment and companionship, of mutual trust, happiness


[096-195]and generosity.
