[051-001]_Question_.  I fear, Colonel, that I have surprised you into agreeing


[051-002]with a clergyman.  The following are the points made by the Rev.


[051-003]Dr. Abbott in his editorial on the theatre, and it seems to me that


[051-004]you and he think very much alike--on that subject.  The points are


[051-005]these:


[051-006]1.  It is not the function of the drama to teach moral lessons.


[051-007]2.  A moral lesson neither makes nor mars either a drama or a novel.


[051-008]3.  The moral quality of a play does not depend upon the result.


[051-009]4.  The real function of the drama is like that of the novel--not


[051-010]to amuse, not to excite; but to portray life, and so minister to


[051-011]it.  And as virtue and vice, goodness and evil, are the great


[051-012]fundamental facts of life, they must, in either serious story or


[051-013]serious play, be portrayed.  If they are so portrayed that the vice


[051-014]is alluring and the virtue repugnant, the play or story is immoral;


[051-015]if so portrayed that the vice is repellant and the virtue alluring,


[051-016]they play or story is moral.


[051-017]5.  The church has no occasion to ask the theatre to preach; though


[051-018]if it does preach we have a right to demand that its ethical


[051-019]doctrines be pure and high.  But we have a right to demand that in


[051-020]its pictures of life it so portrays vice as to make it abhorrent,


[051-021]and so portrays virtue as to make it attractive.


[051-022]_Answer_.  I agree in most of what you have read, though I must


[051-023]confess that to find a minister agreeing with me, or to find myself


[051-024]agreeing with a minister, makes me a little uncertain.  All art,


[051-025]in my judgment, is for the sake of expression--equally true of the


[051-026]drama as of painting and sculpture.  No poem touches the human


[051-027]heart unless it touches the universal.  It must, at some point,


[051-028]move in unison with the great ebb and flow of things.  The same is


[051-029]true of the play, of a piece of music or a statue.  I think that


[051-030]all real artists, in all departments, touch the universal and when


[051-031]they do the result is good; but the result need not have been a


[051-032]consideration.  There is an old story that at first there was a


[051-033]temple erected upon the earth by God himself; that afterward this


[051-034]temple was shivered into countless pieces and distributed over the


[051-035]whole earth, and that all the rubies and diamonds and precious


[051-036]stones since found are parts of that temple.  Now, if we could


[051-037]conceive of a building, or of anything involving all Art, and that


[051-038]it had been scattered abroad, then I would say that whoever find


[051-039]and portrays truthfully a thought, an emotion, a truth, has found


[051-040]and restored one of the jewels.


[051-041]--_Dramatic Mirror_, New York, April 21, 1888.


[051-042]PROTECTION AND FREE TRADE.


[051-043]_Question_.  Do you take much interest in politics, Colonel


[051-044]Ingersoll?


[051-045]_Answer_.  I take as much interest in politics as a Republican


[051-046]ought who expects nothing and who wants nothing for himself.  I


[051-047]want to see this country again controlled by the Republican party.


[051-048]The present administration has not, in my judgment, the training


[051-049]and the political intelligence to decide upon the great economic


[051-050]and financial questions.  There are a great many politicians and


[051-051]but few statesmen.  Here, where men have to be elected every two


[051-052]or six years, there is hardly time for the officials to study


[051-053]statesmanship--they are busy laying pipes and fixing fences for


[051-054]the next election.  Each one feels much like a monkey at a fair,


[051-055]on the top of a greased pole, and puts in the most of his time


[051-056]dodging stones and keeping from falling.  I want to see the party


[051-057]in power best qualified, best equipped, to administer the


[051-058]Government.


[051-059]_Question_.  What do you think will be the particular issue of the


[051-060]coming campaign?


[051-061]_Answer_.  That question has already been answered.  The great


[051-062]question will be the tariff.  Mr. Cleveland imagines that the


[051-063]surplus can be gotten rid of by a reduction of the tariff.  If the


[051-064]reduction is so great as to increase the demand for foreign articles,


[051-065]the probability is that the surplus will be increased.  The surplus


[051-066]can surely be done away with by either of two methods; first make


[051-067]the tariff prohibitory; second, have no tariff.  But if the tariff


[051-068]is just at that point where the foreign goods could pay it and yet


[051-069]undersell the American so as to stop home manufactures, then the


[051-070]surplus would increase.


[051-071]As a rule we can depend on American competition to keep prices at


[051-072]a reasonable rate.  When that fails we have at all times the


[051-073]governing power in our hands--that is to say, we can reduce the


[051-074]tariff.  In other words, the tariff is not for the benefit of the


[051-075]manufacturer--the protection is not for the mechanic or the capitalist


[051-076]--it is for the whole country.  I do not believe in protecting silk


[051-077]simply to help the town of Paterson, but I am for the protection


[051-078]of the manufacture, because, in my judgment, it helps the entire


[051-079]country, and because I know that it has given us a far better


[051-080]article of silk at a far lower price than we obtained before the


[051-081]establishment of those factories.


[051-082]I believe in the protection of every industry that needs it, to


[051-083]the end that we may make use of every kind of brain and find use


[051-084]for all human capacities.  In this way we will produce greater and


[051-085]better people.  A nation of agriculturalists or a nation of mechanics


[051-086]would become narrow and small, but where everything is done, then


[051-087]the brain is cultivated on every side, from artisan to artist.


[051-088]That is to say, we become thinkers as well as workers; muscle and


[051-089]mind form a partnership.


[051-090]I don't believe that England is particularly interested in the


[051-091]welfare of the United States.  It never seemed probable to me that


[051-092]men like Godwin Smith sat up nights fearing that we in some way


[051-093]might injure ourselves.  To use a phrase that will be understood


[051-094]by theologians at least, we ought to "copper" all English advice.


[051-095]The free traders say that there ought to be no obstructions placed


[051-096]by governments between buyers and sellers.  If we want to make the


[051-097]trade, of course there should be no obstruction, but if we prefer


[051-098]that Americans should trade with Americans--that Americans should


[051-099]make what Americans want--then, so far as trading with foreigners


[051-100]is concerned, there ought to be an obstruction.


[051-101]I am satisfied that the United States could get along if the rest


[051-102]of the world should be submerged, and I want to see this country


[051-103]in such a condition that it can be independent of the rest of


[051-104]mankind.


[051-105]There is more mechanical genius in the United States than in the


[051-106]rest of the world, and this genius has been fostered and developed


[051-107]by protection.  The Democracy wish to throw all this away--to make


[051-108]useless this skill, this ingenuity, born of generations of application


[051-109]and thought.  These deft and marvelous hands that create the


[051-110]countless things of use and beauty to be worth no more than the


[051-111]common hands of ignorant delvers and shovelers.  To the extent that


[051-112]thought is mingled with labor, labor becomes honorable and its


[051-113]burden lighter.


[051-114]Thousands of millions of dollars have been invested on the faith


[051-115]of this policy--millions and millions of people are this day earning


[051-116]their bread by reason of protection, and they are better housed


[051-117]and better fed and better clothed than any other workmen on the


[051-118]globe.


[051-119]The intelligent people of this country will not be satisfied with


[051-120]President Cleveland's platform--with his free trade primer.  They


[051-121]believe in good wages for good work, and they know that this is


[051-122]the richest nation in the world.  The Republic is worth at least


[051-123]sixty billion dollars.  This vast sum is the result of labor, and


[051-124]this labor has been protected either directly or indirectly.  This


[051-125]vast sum has been made by the farmer, the mechanic, the laborer,


[051-126]the miner, the inventor.


[051-127]Protection has given work and wages to the mechanic and a market


[051-128]to the farmer.  The interests of all laborers in America--all men


[051-129]who work--are identical.  If the farmer pays more for his plow he


[051-130]gets more for his plowing.  In old times, when the South manufactured


[051-131]nothing and raised only raw material--for the reason that its labor


[051-132]was enslaved and could not be trusted with education enough to


[051-133]become skillful--it was in favor of free trade; it wanted to sell


[051-134]the raw material to England and buy the manufactured article where


[051-135]it could buy the cheapest.  Even under those circumstances it was


[051-136]a short-sighted and unpatriotic policy.  Now everything is changing


[051-137]in the South.  They are beginning to see that he who simply raises


[051-138]raw material is destined to be forever poor.  For instance, the


[051-139]farmer who sells corn will never get rich; the farmer should sell


[051-140]pork and beef and horses.  So a nation, a State, that parts with


[051-141]its raw material, loses nearly all the profits, for the reason that


[051-142]the profit rises with the skill requisite to produce.  It requires


[051-143]only brute strength to raise cotton; it requires something more to


[051-144]spin it, to weave it, and the more beautiful the fabric the greater


[051-145]the skill, and consequently the higher the wages and the greater


[051-146]the profit.  In other words, the more thought is mingled with labor


[051-147]the more valuable is the result.


[051-148]Besides all this, protection is the mother of economy; the cheapest


[051-149]at last, no matter whether the amount paid is less or more.  It is


[051-150]far better for us to make glass than to sell sand to other countries;


[051-151]the profit on sand will be exceedingly small.


[051-152]The interests of this country are united; they depend upon each


[051-153]other.  You destroy one and the effect upon all the rest may be


[051-154]disastrous.  Suppose we had free trade to-day, what would become


[051-155]of the manufacturing interests to-morrow?  The value of property


[051-156]would fall thousands of millions of dollars in an instant.  The


[051-157]fires would die out in thousands and thousands of furnaces,


[051-158]innumerable engines would stop, thousands and thousands would stop


[051-159]digging coal and iron and steel.  What would the city that had been


[051-160]built up by the factories be worth?  What would be the effect on


[051-161]farms in that neighborhood?  What would be the effect on railroads,


[051-162]on freights, on business--what upon the towns through which they


[051-163]passed?  Stop making iron in Pennsylvania, and the State would be


[051-164]bankrupt in an hour.  Give us free trade, and New Jersey, Connecticut


[051-165]and many other States would not be worth one dollar an acre.


[051-166]If a man will think of the connection between all industries--of


[051-167]the dependence and inter-dependence of each on all; of the subtle


[051-168]relations between all human pursuits--he will see that to destroy


[051-169]some of the grand interest makes financial ruin and desolation.


[051-170]I am not talking now about a tariff that is too high, because that


[051-171]tariff does not produce a surplus--neither am I asking to have that


[051-172]protected which needs no protection--I am only insisting that all


[051-173]the industries that have been fostered and that need protection


[051-174]should be protected, and that we should turn our attention to the


[051-175]interests of our own country, letting other nations take care of


[051-176]themselves.  If every American would use only articles produced by


[051-177]Americans--if they would wear only American cloth, only American


[051-178]silk--if we would absolutely stand by each other, the prosperity


[051-179]of this nation would be the marvel of human history.  We can live


[051-180]at home, and we have now the ingenuity, the intelligence, the


[051-181]industry to raise from nature everything that a nation needs.


[051-182]_Question_.  What have you to say about the claim that Mr. Cleveland


[051-183]does not propose free trade?


[051-184]_Answer_.  I suppose that he means what he said.  His argument was


[051-185]all for free trade, and he endeavored to show to the farmer that


[051-186]he lost altogether more money by protection, because he paid a


[051-187]higher price for manufactured articles and received no more for


[051-188]what he had to sell.  This certainly was an argument in favor of


[051-189]free trade.  And there is no way to decrease the surplus except to


[051-190]prohibit the importation of foreign articles, which certainly Mr.


[051-191]Cleveland is not in favor of doing, or to reduce the tariff to a


[051-192]point so low that no matter how much may be imported the surplus


[051-193]will be reduced.  If the message means anything it means free trade,


[051-194]and if there is any argument in it it is an argument in favor of


[051-195]absolutely free trade.  The party, not willing to say "free trade"


[051-196]uses the word "reform."  This is simply a mask and a pretence.


[051-197]The party knows that the President made a mistake.  The party,


[051-198]however, is so situated that it cannot get rid of Cleveland, and


[051-199]consequently must take him with his mistake--they must take him


[051-200]with his message, and then show that all he intended by "free trade"


[051-201]was "reform."
