Reason, as understood in the [[freethought]] tradition and articulated by [[robert-ingersoll|Robert G. Ingersoll]], is the primary faculty by which human beings discover [[truth]], evaluate evidence, and form judgments about the natural and moral world. Ingersoll placed reason at the center of his philosophy, famously declaring it "the only torch" of humanity — a direct challenge to the authority of [[bible|scripture]], [[christianity|ecclesiastical tradition]], and revealed religion. Unlike faith, which Ingersoll regarded as a surrender of intellectual autonomy, reason demands observation, skepticism, and the constant testing of propositions against experience.

## Ingersoll's Defense of Reason

Throughout his interviews and writings, Ingersoll consistently argued that reason is not merely one path to knowledge among many, but the *only* reliable path. He grounded his epistemology in three sources:

> On reason, observation, experience, upon the discoveries of the great and intelligent of all ages, I pin my faith. [P01-028]

This triad — reason, observation, and experience — recurs throughout his work. For Ingersoll, reason was inseparable from [[science|scientific inquiry]]: both demanded evidence, both rejected arguments from authority, and both reserved the right to revise conclusions when new [[truth|fact]]s emerged.

Ingersoll recognized that religious orthodoxy viewed reason with suspicion, precisely because rational inquiry threatened doctrinal certainties:

> Recollect that reason is a dangerous thing. You had better not depend on it. [016-033]

This ironic warning captures the tension at the heart of Ingersoll's critique of [[christianity|Christianity]]: faith systems, he argued, survive only by persuading adherents to abandon their critical faculties. He saw the demand that believers "throw away your reason" [015-171] as the central intellectual crime of organized religion.

## The Biblical/Christian View of Reason

In the [[bible|biblical]] tradition, reason occupies an ambivalent position. The [[bible|Old Testament]] wisdom literature (Proverbs, Ecclesiastes) praises understanding and discernment, but the [[bible|New Testament]] — particularly the Pauline epistles — frequently contrasts human wisdom unfavorably with divine revelation. Paul's declaration that "the wisdom of this world is foolishness with [[god|God]]" (1 Corinthians 3:19) established a theological framework in which reason was subordinate to faith.

| Tradition | View of Reason | Key Text/Source |
|-----------|---------------|-----------------|
| [[bible|Old Testament]] Wisdom | Reason and wisdom are divine gifts; the "fear of the Lord is the beginning of wisdom" | Proverbs 1:7 |
| Pauline [[christianity|Christianity]] | Human reason is insufficient; faith transcends rational understanding | 1 Corinthians 1:18–25 |
| Medieval Scholasticism | Reason and revelation are complementary; reason serves theology | Thomas Aquinas, *Summa Theologica* |
| Reformation | *Sola scriptura* — scripture alone, not reason, is the final authority | [[martin-luther|Luther]], Calvin |
| [[freethought|Freethought]] / Ingersoll | Reason is primary; revelation and scripture must be judged by reason, not the reverse | Ingersoll, *Interviews* |

Ingersoll rejected the scholastic synthesis entirely. In his view, reason was not the handmaid of theology but its judge. Any doctrine that could not withstand rational scrutiny — [[hell|eternal punishment]], [[resurrection|bodily resurrection]], [[slavery|biblical slavery]] — was, on that basis alone, unworthy of belief.

## Relation to Other Concepts

Reason does not exist in isolation in Ingersoll's thought. It forms a network of interdependent concepts:

- **Reason and [[truth]]**: [[truth|Truth]] is what reason discovers. Ingersoll held that no truth could be genuinely harmful and no error genuinely beneficial — a conviction that drove his public critiques of religious dogma.
- **Reason and [[science]]**: Science is reason applied systematically to the natural world. Ingersoll celebrated scientists like [[darwin|Charles Darwin]] and [[humboldt|Alexander von Humboldt]] as exemplars of the rational spirit.
- **Reason and [[superstition]]**: [[superstition|Superstition]] is what remains when reason is abandoned. Ingersoll traced religious superstition to humanity's pre-scientific attempts to explain natural phenomena.
- **Reason and [[liberty]]**: Intellectual liberty — the freedom to think, question, and dissent — is the necessary condition for reason to flourish. Ingersoll tied religious orthodoxy to intellectual [[slavery]] and reason to emancipation.

## Later Influence

Ingersoll's elevation of reason influenced subsequent freethought and secular humanist movements. His formulation — that reason, not faith, is the proper foundation for ethics and knowledge — anticipated the New Atheism of the late 20th and early 21st centuries. Figures from [[bertrand-russell|Bertrand Russell]] to [[christopher-hitchens|Christopher Hitchens]] echoed his core argument: that beliefs held on insufficient evidence are not virtuous but intellectually irresponsible.
