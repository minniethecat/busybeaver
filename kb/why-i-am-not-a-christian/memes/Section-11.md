[010-001]_Question_.  What do you think of civil service reform?


[010-002]_Answer_.  I am in favor of it.  I want such civil service reform


[010-003]that all the offices will be filled with good and competent


[010-004]Republicans.  The majority should rule, and the men who are in


[010-005]favor of the views of the majority should hold the offices.  I am


[010-006]utterly opposed to the idea that a party should show its liberality


[010-007]at the expense of its principles.  Men holding office can afford


[010-008]to take their chances with the rest of us.  If they are Democrats,


[010-009]they should not expect to succeed when their party is defeated.


[010-010]I believe that there are enough good and honest Republicans in this


[010-011]country to fill all the offices, and I am opposed to taking any


[010-012]Democrats until the Republican supply is exhausted.


[010-013]Men should not join the Republican party to get office.  Such men


[010-014]are contemptible to the last degree.  Neither should a Republican


[010-015]administration compel a man to leave the party to get a Federal


[010-016]appointment.  After a great battle has been fought I do not believe


[010-017]that the victorious general should reward the officers of the


[010-018]conquered army.  My doctrine is, rewards for friends.


[010-019]--_The Commercial_, Cincinnati, Ohio, December 6, 1880.


[010-020]MR. BEECHER, MOSES AND THE NEGRO.


[010-021]_Question_.  Mr. Beecher is here.  Have you seen him?


[010-022]_Answer_.  No, I did not meet Mr. Beecher.  Neither did I hear him


[010-023]lecture.  The fact is, that long ago I made up my mind that under


[010-024]no circumstances would I attend any lecture or other entertainment


[010-025]given at Lincoln Hall.  First, because the hall has been denied


[010-026]me, and secondly, because I regard it as extremely unsafe.  The


[010-027]hall is up several stories from the ground, and in case of the


[010-028]slightest panic, in my judgment, many lives would be lost.  Had it


[010-029]not been for this, and for the fact that the persons owning it


[010-030]imagined that because they had control, the brick and mortar had


[010-031]some kind of holy and sacred quality, and that this holiness is of


[010-032]such a wonderful character that it would not be proper for a man


[010-033]in that hall to tell his honest thoughts, I would have heard him.


[010-034]_Question_.  Then I assume that you and Mr. Beecher have made up?


[010-035]_Answer_.  There is nothing to be made up for so far as I know.


[010-036]Mr. Beecher has treated me very well, and, I believe, a little too


[010-037]well for his own peace of mind.  I have been informed that some


[010-038]members of Plymouth Church felt exceedingly hurt that their pastor


[010-039]should so far forget himself as to extend the right hand of fellowship


[010-040]to one who differs from him upon what they consider very essential


[010-041]points in theology.  You see I have denied with all my might, a


[010-042]great many times, the infamous doctrine of eternal punishment.  I


[010-043]have also had the temerity to suggest that I did not believe that


[010-044]a being of infinite justice and mercy was the author of all that


[010-045]I find in the Old Testament.  As, for instance, I have insisted


[010-046]that God never commanded anybody to butcher women or to cut the


[010-047]throats of prattling babes.  These orthodox gentlemen have rushed


[010-048]to the rescue of Jehovah by insisting that he did all these horrible


[010-049]things.  I have also maintained that God never sanctioned or upheld


[010-050]human slavery; that he never would make one child to own and beat


[010-051]another.


[010-052]I have also expressed some doubts as to whether this same God ever


[010-053]established the institution of polygamy.  I have insisted that the


[010-054]institution is simply infamous; that it destroys the idea of home;


[010-055]that it turns to ashes the most sacred words in our language, and


[010-056]leaves the world a kind of den in which crawl the serpents of


[010-057]selfishness and lust.  I have been informed that after Mr. Beecher


[010-058]had treated me kindly a few members of his congregation objected,


[010-059]and really felt ashamed that he had so forgotten himself.  After


[010-060]that, Mr. Beecher saw fit to give his ideas of the position I had


[010-061]taken.  In this he was not exceedingly kind, nor was his justice


[010-062]very conspicuous.  But I cared nothing about that, not the least.


[010-063]As I have said before, whenever Mr. Beecher says a good thing I


[010-064]give him credit.  Whenever he does an unfair or unjust thing I


[010-065]charge it to the account of his religion.  I have insisted, and I


[010-066]still insist, that Mr. Beecher is far better than his creed.  I do


[010-067]not believe that he believes in the doctrine of eternal punishment.


[010-068]Neither do I believe that he believes in the literal truth of the


[010-069]Scriptures.  And, after all, if the Bible is not true, it is hardly


[010-070]worth while to insist upon its inspiration.  An inspired lie is


[010-071]not better than an uninspired one.  If the Bible is true it does


[010-072]not need to be inspired.  If it is not true, inspiration does not


[010-073]help it.  So that after all it is simply a question of fact.  Is


[010-074]it true?  I believe Mr. Beecher stated that one of my grievous


[010-075]faults was that I picked out the bad things in the Bible.  How an


[010-076]infinitely good and wise God came to put bad things in his book


[010-077]Mr. Beecher does not explain.  I have insisted that the Bible is


[010-078]not inspired, and, in order to prove that, have pointed out such


[010-079]passages as I deemed unworthy to have been written even by a


[010-080]civilized man or a savage.  I certainly would not endeavor to prove


[010-081]that the Bible is uninspired by picking out its best passages.  I


[010-082]admit that there are many good things in the Bible.  The fact that


[010-083]there are good things in it does not prove its inspiration, because


[010-084]there are thousands of other books containing good things, and yet


[010-085]no one claims they are inspired.  Shakespeare's works contain a


[010-086]thousand times more good things than the Bible, but no one claims


[010-087]he was an inspired man.  It is also true that there are many bad


[010-088]things in Shakespeare--many passages which I wish he had never


[010-089]written.  But I can excuse Shakespeare, because he did not rise


[010-090]absolutely above his time.  That is to say, he was a man; that is


[010-091]to say, he was imperfect.  If anybody claimed now that Shakespeare


[010-092]was actually inspired, that claim would be answered by pointing to


[010-093]certain weak or bad or vulgar passages in his works.  But every


[010-094]Christian will say that it is a certain kind of blasphemy to impute


[010-095]vulgarity or weakness to God, as they are all obliged to defend


[010-096]the weak, the bad and the vulgar, so long as they insist upon the


[010-097]inspiration of the Bible.  Now, I pursued the same course with the


[010-098]Bible that Mr. Beecher has pursued with me.  Why did he want to


[010-099]pick out my bad things?  Is it possible that he is a kind of vulture


[010-100]that sees only the carrion of another?  After all, has he not


[010-101]pursued the same method with me that he blames me for pursuing in


[010-102]regard to the Bible?  Of course he must pursue that method.  He


[010-103]could not object to me and then point out passages that were not


[010-104]objectionable.  If he found fault he had to find faults in order


[010-105]to sustain his ground.  That is exactly what I have done with


[010-106]Scriptures--nothing more and nothing less.  The reason I have thrown


[010-107]away the Bible is that in many places it is harsh, cruel, unjust,


[010-108]coarse, vulgar, atrocious, infamous.  At the same time, I admit


[010-109]that it contains many passages of an excellent and splendid character


[010-110]--many good things, wise sayings, and many excellent and just laws.


[010-111]But I would like to ask this:  Suppose there were no passages in


[010-112]the Bible except those upholding slavery, polygamy and wars of


[010-113]extermination; would anybody then claim that it was the word of


[010-114]God?  I would like to ask if there is a Christian in the world who


[010-115]would not be overjoyed to find that every one of these passages


[010-116]was an interpolation?  I would also like to ask Mr. Beecher if he


[010-117]would not be greatly gratified to find that after God had written


[010-118]the Bible the Devil had got hold of it, and interpolated all these


[010-119]passages about slavery, polygamy, the slaughter of women and babes


[010-120]and the doctrine of eternal punishment?  Suppose, as a matter of


[010-121]fact, the Devil did get hold of it; what part of the Bible would


[010-122]Mr. Beecher pick out as having been written by the Devil?  And if


[010-123]he picks out these passages could not the Devil answer him by


[010-124]saying, "You, Mr. Beecher, are like a vulture, a kind of buzzard,


[010-125]flying through the tainted air of inspiration, and pouncing down


[010-126]upon the carrion.  Why do you not fly like a dove, and why do you


[010-127]not have the innocent ignorance of the dove, so that you could


[010-128]light upon a carcass and imagine that you were surrounded by the


[010-129]perfume of violets?"  The fact is that good things in a book do


[010-130]not prove that it is inspired, but the presence of bad things does


[010-131]prove that it is not.


[010-132]_Question_.  What was the real difficulty between you and Moses,


[010-133]Colonel, a man who has been dead for thousands of years?


[010-134]_Answer_.  We never had any difficulty.  I have always taken pains


[010-135]to say that Moses had nothing to do with the Pentateuch.  Those


[010-136]books, in my judgment, were written several centuries after Moses


[010-137]had become dust in his unknown sepulchre.  No doubt Moses was quite


[010-138]a man in his day, if he ever existed at all.  Some people say that


[010-139]Moses is exactly the same as "law-giver;" that is to say, as


[010-140]Legislature, that is to say as Congress.  Imagine somebody in the


[010-141]future as regarding the Congress of the United States as one person!


[010-142]And then imagine that somebody endeavoring to prove that Congress


[010-143]was always consistent.  But, whether Moses lived or not makes but


[010-144]little difference to me.  I presume he filled the place and did


[010-145]the work that he was compelled to do, and although according to


[010-146]the account God had much to say to him with regard to the making


[010-147]of altars, tongs, snuffers and candlesticks, there is much left


[010-148]for nature still to tell.  Thinking of Moses as a man, admitting


[010-149]that he was above his fellows, that he was in his day and generation


[010-150]a leader, and, in a certain narrow sense, a patriot, that he was


[010-151]the founder of the Jewish people; that he found them barbarians


[010-152]and endeavored to control them by thunder and lightning, and found


[010-153]it necessary to pretend that he was in partnership with the power


[010-154]governing the universe; that he took advantage of their ignorance


[010-155]and fear, just as politicians do now, and as theologians always


[010-156]will, still, I see no evidence that the man Moses was any nearer


[010-157]to God than his descendants, who are still warring against the


[010-158]Philistines in every civilized part of the globe.  Moses was a


[010-159]believer in slavery, in polygamy, in wars of extermination, in


[010-160]religious persecution and intolerance and in almost everything that


[010-161]is now regarded with loathing, contempt and scorn.  The Jehovah of


[010-162]whom he speaks violated, or commands the violation of at least nine


[010-163]of the Ten Commandments he gave.  There is one thing, however, that


[010-164]can be said of Moses that cannot be said of any person who now


[010-165]insists that he was inspired, and that is, he was in advance of his


[010-166]time.


[010-167]_Question_.  What do you think of the Buckner Bill for the colonization


[010-168]of the negroes in Mexico?


[010-169]_Answer_.  Where does Mr. Buckner propose to colonize the white


[010-170]people, and what right has he to propose the colonization of six


[010-171]millions of people?  Should we not have other bills to colonize


[010-172]the Germans, the Swedes, the Irish, and then, may be, another bill


[010-173]to drive the Chinese into the sea?  Where do we get the right to


[010-174]say that the negroes must emigrate?
