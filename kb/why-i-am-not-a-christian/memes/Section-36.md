[035-001]_Question_.  Why do the theological seminaries find it difficult


[035-002]to get students?


[035-003]_Answer_.  I was told last spring, at New Haven, that the "theologs,"


[035-004]as they call the young men there being fitted for the ministry,


[035-005]were not regarded as intellectual by all the other students.  The


[035-006]orthodox pulpit has no rewards for genius.  It has rewards only for


[035-007]stupidity, for belief--not for investigation, not for thought; and


[035-008]the consequence is that young men of talent avoid the pulpit.  I


[035-009]think I heard the other day that of all the students at Harvard


[035-010]only nine are preparing for the ministry.  The truth is, the ministry


[035-011]is not regarded as an intellectual occupation.  The average church


[035-012]now consists of women and children.  Men go to please their wives,


[035-013]or stay at home and subscribe to please their wives; and the wives


[035-014]are beginning to think, and many of them are staying at home.  Many


[035-015]of them now prefer the theatre or the opera or the park or the


[035-016]seashore or the forest or the companionship of their husbands and


[035-017]children at home.


[035-018]_Question_.  How does the religious state of California compare


[035-019]with the rest of the Union?


[035-020]_Answer_.  I find that sensible people everywhere are about the


[035-021]same, and the proportion of Freethinkers depends on the proportion


[035-022]of sensible folks.  I think that California has her full share of


[035-023]sensible people.  I find everywhere the best people and the brightest


[035-024]people--the people with the most heart and the best brain--all


[035-025]tending toward free thought.  Of course, a man of brain cannot


[035-026]believe the miracles of the Old and New Testaments.  A man of heart


[035-027]cannot believe in the doctrine of eternal pain.  We have found that


[035-028]other religions are like ours, with precisely the same basis, the


[035-029]same idiotic miracles, the same Christ or Saviour.  It will hardly


[035-030]do to say that all others like ours are false, and ours the only


[035-031]true one, when others substantially like it are thousands of years


[035-032]older.  We have at last found that a religion is simply an effort


[035-033]on the part of man to account for what he sees, what he experiences,


[035-034]what he feels, what he fears, and what he hopes.  Every savage has


[035-035]his philosophy.  That is his religion and his science.


[035-036]The religions of to-day are the sciences of the past; and it may


[035-037]be that the sciences of to-day will be the religions of the future,


[035-038]and that other sciences will be as far beyond them as the science


[035-039]of to-day is beyond the religion of to-day.  As a rule, religion


[035-040]is a sanctified mistake, and heresy a slandered fact.  In other


[035-041]words, the human mind grows--and as it grows it abandons the old,


[035-042]and the old gets its revenge by maligning the new.


[035-043]--_The San Franciscan_, San Francisco, October 4, 1884.


[035-044]BLAINE'S DEFEAT.


[035-045]_Question_.  Colonel, the fact that you took no part in the late


[035-046]campaign, is a subject for general comment, and knowing your former


[035-047]enthusiastic advocacy and support of Blaine, the people are somewhat


[035-048]surprised, and would like to know why?


[035-049]_Answer_.  In the first place, it was generally supposed that Blaine


[035-050]needed no help.  His friends were perfectly confident.  They counted


[035-051]on a very large Catholic support.  The Irish were supposed to be


[035-052]spoiling to vote for Blaine and Logan.  All the Protestant ministers


[035-053]were also said to be solid for the ticket.  Under these circumstances


[035-054]it was hardly prudent for me to say much.


[035-055]I was for Blaine in 1876.  In 1880 I was for Garfield, and in 1884


[035-056]I was for Gresham or Harlan.  I believed then and I believe now


[035-057]that either one of these men could have been elected.  Blaine is


[035-058]an exceedingly able man, but he made some mistakes and some very


[035-059]unfortunate utterances.  I took no part in the campaign; first,


[035-060]because there was no very important issue, no great principle at


[035-061]stake, and second, I thought that I had done enough, and, third,


[035-062]because I wanted to do something else.


[035-063]_Question_.  What, in your opinion, were the causes for Blaine's


[035-064]defeat?


[035-065]_Answer_.  First, because of dissension in the party.  Second,


[035-066]because party ties have grown weak.  Third, the Prohibition vote.


[035-067]Fourth, the Delmonico dinner--too many rich men.  Fifth, the Rev.


[035-068]Dr. Burchard with his Rum, Romanism and Rebellion.  Sixth, giving


[035-069]too much attention to Ohio and not enough to New York.  Seventh,


[035-070]the unfortunate remark of Mr. Blaine, that "the State cannot get


[035-071]along without the Church."  Eighth, the weakness of the present


[035-072]administration.  Ninth, the abandonment by the party of the colored


[035-073]people of the South.  Tenth, the feeling against monopolies, and


[035-074]not least, a general desire for a change.


[035-075]_Question_.  What, in your opinion, will be the result of Cleveland's


[035-076]election and administration upon the general political and business


[035-077]interests of the country?


[035-078]_Answer_.  The business interests will take care of themselves.


[035-079]A dollar has the instinct of self-preservation largely developed.


[035-080]The tariff will take care of itself.  No State is absolutely for


[035-081]free trade.  In each State there is an exception.  The exceptions


[035-082]will combine, as they always have.  Michigan will help Pennsylvania


[035-083]take care of iron, if Pennsylvania will help Michigan take care of


[035-084]salt and lumber.  Louisiana will help Pennsylvania and Michigan if


[035-085]they help her take care of sugar.  Colorado, California and Ohio


[035-086]will help the other States if they will help them about wool--and


[035-087]so I might make a tour of the States, ending with Vermont and maple


[035-088]sugar.  I do not expect that Cleveland will do any great harm.


[035-089]The Democrats want to stay in power, and that desire will give


[035-090]security for good behavior.


[035-091]_Question_.  Will he listen to or grant any demands made of him by


[035-092]the alleged Independent Republicans of New York, either in his


[035-093]appointments or policies?


[035-094]_Answer_.  Of this I know nothing.  The Independents--from what I


[035-095]know of them--will be too modest to claim credit or to ask office.


[035-096]They were actuated by pure principle.  They did what they did to


[035-097]purify the party, so that they could stay in it.  Now that it has


[035-098]been purified they will remain, and hate the Democratic party as


[035-099]badly as ever.  I hardly think that Cleveland would insult their


[035-100]motives by offering loaves and fishes.  All they desire is the


[035-101]approval of their own consciences.


[035-102]--_The Commonwealth_, Topeka, Kansas, November 21, 1884.


[035-103]BLAINE'S DEFEAT.


[035-104]_Question_.  How do you account for the defeat of Mr. Blaine?


[035-105]_Answer_.  How do I account for the defeat of Mr. Blaine?  I will


[035-106]answer:  St. John, the Independents, Burchard, Butler and Cleveland


[035-107]did it.  The truth is that during the war a majority of the people,


[035-108]counting those in the South, were opposed to putting down the


[035-109]Rebellion by force.  It is also true that when the Proclamation of


[035-110]Emancipation was issued a majority of the people, counting the


[035-111]whole country, were opposed to it, and it is also true that when


[035-112]the colored people were made citizens a majority of the people,


[035-113]counting the whole country, were opposed to it.


[035-114]Now, while, in my judgment, an overwhelming majority of the whole


[035-115]people have honestly acquiesced in the result of the war, and are


[035-116]now perfectly loyal to the Union, and have also acquiesced in the


[035-117]abolition of slavery, I doubt very much whether they are really in


[035-118]favor of giving the colored man the right to vote.  Of course they


[035-119]have not the power now to take that right away, but they feel


[035-120]anything but kindly toward the party that gave the colored man that


[035-121]right.  That is the only result of the war that is not fully accepted


[035-122]by the South and by many Democrats of the North.


[035-123]Another thing, the Republican party was divided--divided too by


[035-124]personal hatreds.  The party was greatly injured by the decision


[035-125]of the Supreme Court in which the Civil Rights Bill was held void.


[035-126]Now, a great many men who kept with the Republican party, did so


[035-127]because they believed that that party would protect the colored


[035-128]man in the South, but as soon as the Court decided that all the


[035-129]laws passed were unconstitutional, these men felt free to vote for


[035-130]the other side, feeling that it would make no difference.  They


[035-131]reasoned this way:  If the Republican party cannot defend the


[035-132]colored people, why make a pretence that excites hatred on one side


[035-133]and disarms the other?  If the colored people have to depend upon


[035-134]the State for protection, and the Federal Government cannot interfere,


[035-135]why say any more about it?


[035-136]I think that these men made a mistake and our party made a mistake


[035-137]in accepting without protest a decision that was far worse than


[035-138]the one delivered in the case of Dred Scott.  By accepting this


[035-139]decision the most important issue was abandoned.  The Republican


[035-140]party must take the old ground that it is the duty of the Federal


[035-141]Government to protect the citizens, and that it cannot simply leave


[035-142]that duty to the State.  It must see to it that the State performs


[035-143]that duty.


[035-144]_Question_.  Have you seen the published report that Dorsey claims


[035-145]to have paid you one hundred thousand dollars for your services in


[035-146]the Star Route Cases?


[035-147]_Answer_.  I have seen the report, but Dorsey never said anything


[035-148]like that.


[035-149]_Question_.  Is there no truth in the statement, then?


[035-150]_Answer_.  Well, Dorsey never said anything of the kind.


[035-151]_Question_.  Then you do not deny that you received such an enormous


[035-152]fee?


[035-153]_Answer_.  All I say is that Dorsey did not say I did.*


[035-154]--_The Commercial_, Louisville, Kentucky, October 24, 1884.


[035-155][* Col. Ingersoll has been so criticised and maligned for


[035-156]defending Mr. Dorsey in the Star Route cases, and so


[035-157]frequently charged with having received an enormous fee,


[035-158]that I think it but simple justice to his memory to say that


[035-159]he received no such fee, and that the ridiculously small


[035-160]sums he did receive were much more than offset by the amount


[035-161]he had to pay as indorser of Mr. Dorsey's paper. --C. F.


[035-162]FARRELL.]


[035-163]PLAGIARISM AND POLITICS.


[035-164]_Question_.  What have you to say about the charges published in


[035-165]this morning's _Herald_ to the effect that you copied your lecture


[035-166]about "Mistakes of Moses" from a chapter bearing the same title in


[035-167]a book called Hittell's "Evidences against Christianity"?


[035-168]_Answer_.  All I have to say is that the charge is utterly false.


[035-169]I will give a thousand dollars reward to any one who will furnish


[035-170]a book published before my lecture, in which that lecture can be


[035-171]found.  It is wonderful how malicious the people are who love their


[035-172]enemies.  This charge is wholly false, as all others of like nature


[035-173]are.  I do not have to copy the writings of others.  The Christians


[035-174]do not seem to see that they are constantly complimenting me by


[035-175]saying that what I write is so good that I must have stolen it.


[035-176]Poor old orthodoxy!


[035-177]_Question_.  What is your opinion of the incoming administration,


[035-178]and how will it affect the country?


[035-179]_Answer_.  I feel disposed to give Cleveland a chance.  If he does


[035-180]the fair thing, then it is the duty of all good citizens to say


[035-181]so.  I do not expect to see the whole country go to destruction


[035-182]because the Democratic party is in power.  Neither do I believe


[035-183]that business is going to suffer on that account.  The times are


[035-184]hard, and I fear will be much harder, but they would have been


[035-185]substantially the same if Blaine had been elected.  I wanted the


[035-186]Republican party to succeed and fully expected to see Mr. Blaine


[035-187]President, but I believe in making the best of what has happened.


[035-188]I want no office, I want good government--wise legislation.  I


[035-189]believe in protection, but I want the present tariff reformed and


[035-190]I hope the Democrats will be wise enough to do so.


[035-191]_Question_.  How will the Democratic victory affect the colored


[035-192]people in the South?


[035-193]_Answer_.  Certainly their condition will not be worse than it has


[035-194]been.  The Supreme Court decided that the Civil Rights Bill was


[035-195]unconstitutional and that the Federal Government cannot interfere.


[035-196]That was a bad decision and our party made a mistake in not protesting


[035-197]against it.  I believe it to be the duty of the Federal Government


[035-198]to protect all its citizens, at home as well as abroad.  My hope


[035-199]is that there will be a division in the Democratic party.  That


[035-200]party has something now to divide.  At last it has a bone, and


[035-201]probably the fighting will commence.  I hope that some new issue


[035-202]will take color out of politics, something about which both white


[035-203]and colored may divide.  Of course nothing would please me better


[035-204]than to see the Democratic party become great and grand enough to


[035-205]give the colored people their rights.
