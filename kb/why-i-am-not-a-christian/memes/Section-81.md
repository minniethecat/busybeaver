[080-001]The Sunday newspaper, I think, is the best of any day in the week.


[080-002]That paper keeps hundreds and thousands at home.  You can find in


[080-003]it information about almost everything in the world.  One of the


[080-004]great Sunday papers will keep a family busy reading almost all day.


[080-005]Now, I do not wonder that the ministers are so opposed to the Sunday


[080-006]newspaper, and so they are opposed to anything calculated to decrease


[080-007]the attendance at church.  Why, they want all the parks, all the


[080-008]museums, all the libraries closed on Sunday, and they want the


[080-009]World's Fair closed on Sunday.


[080-010]Now, I am in favor of Sunday; in fact, I am perfectly willing to


[080-011]have two of them a week, but I want Sunday as a day of recreation


[080-012]and pleasure.  The fact is we ought not to work hard enough during


[080-013]the week to require a day of rest.  Every day ought to be so arranged


[080-014]that there would be time for rest from the labor of that day.


[080-015]Sunday is a good day to get business out of your mind, to forget


[080-016]the ledger and the docket and the ticker, to forget profits and


[080-017]losses, and enjoy yourself.  It is a good day to go to the art


[080-018]museums, to look at pictures and statues and beautiful things, so


[080-019]that you may feel that there is something in this world besides


[080-020]money and mud.  It is a good day, is Sunday, to go to the libraries


[080-021]and spend a little time with the great and splendid dead, and to


[080-022]go to the cemetery and think of those who are sleeping there, and


[080-023]to give a little thought to the time when you, too, like them, will


[080-024]fall asleep.  I think it is a good day for almost anything except


[080-025]going to church.  There is no need of that; everybody knows the


[080-026]story, and if a man has worked hard all the week, you can hardly


[080-027]call it recreation if he goes to church Sunday and hears that his


[080-028]chances are ninety-nine in a hundred in favor of being eternally


[080-029]damned.


[080-030]So it is I am in favor of having the World's Fair open on Sunday.


[080-031]It will be a good day to look at the best the world has produced;


[080-032]a good day to leave the saloons and commune for a little while with


[080-033]the mighty spirits that have glorified this world.  Sunday is a


[080-034]good day to leave the churches, where they teach that man has become


[080-035]totally depraved, and look at the glorious things that have been


[080-036]wrought by these depraved beings.  Besides all this, it is the day


[080-037]of days for the working man and working woman, for those who have


[080-038]to work all the week.  In New York an attempt was made to open the


[080-039]Metropolitan Museum of Art on Sunday, and the pious people opposed


[080-040]it.  They thought it would interfere with the joy of heaven if


[080-041]people were seen in the park enjoying themselves on Sunday, and


[080-042]they also held that nobody would visit the Museum if it were opened


[080-043]on Sunday; that the "common people" had no love for pictures and


[080-044]statues and cared nothing about art.  The doors were opened, and


[080-045]it was demonstrated that the poor people, the toilers and workers,


[080-046]did want to see such things on Sunday, and now more people visit


[080-047]the Museum on Sunday than on all the other days of the week put


[080-048]together.  The same is true of the public libraries.  There is


[080-049]something to me infinitely pharisaical, hypocritical and farcical


[080-050]in this Sunday nonsense.  The rich people who favor keeping Sunday


[080-051]"holy," have their coachman drive them to church and wait outside


[080-052]until the services end.  What do they care about the coachman's


[080-053]soul?  While they are at church their cooks are busy at home getting


[080-054]dinner ready.  What do they care for the souls of cooks?  The whole


[080-055]thing is pretence, and nothing but pretence.  It is the instinct


[080-056]of business.  It is the competition of the gospel shop with other


[080-057]shops and places of resort.


[080-058]The ministers, of course, are opposed to all shows except their


[080-059]own, for they know that very few will come to see or hear them and


[080-060]the choice must be the church or nothing.


[080-061]I do not believe that one day can be more holy than another unless


[080-062]more joyous than another.  The holiest day is the happiest day--


[080-063]the day on which wives and children and men are happiest.  In that


[080-064]sense a day can be holy.


[080-065]Our idea of the Sabbath is from the Puritans, and they imagined


[080-066]that a man has to be miserable in order to excite the love of God.


[080-067]We have outgrown the old New England Sabbath--the old Scotch horror.


[080-068]The Germans have helped us and have set a splendid example.  I do


[080-069]not see how a poor workingman can go to church for recreation--I


[080-070]mean an orthodox church.  A man who has hell here cannot be benefitted


[080-071]by being assured that he is likely to have hell hereafter.  The


[080-072]whole business I hold in perfect abhorrence.


[080-073]They tell us that God will not prosper us unless we observe the


[080-074]Sabbath.  The Jews kept the Sabbath and yet Jehovah deserted them,


[080-075]and they are a people without a nation.  The Scotch kept Sunday;


[080-076]they are not independent.  The French never kept Sunday, and yet


[080-077]they are the most prosperous nation in Europe.


[080-078]--_Commercial Gazette_, Cincinnati, Ohio, May 2, 1892.


[080-079]AUTHORS.


[080-080]_Question_.  Who, in your opinion, is the greatest novelist who


[080-081]has written in the English language?


[080-082]_Answer_.  The greatest novelist, in my opinion, who has ever


[080-083]written in the English language, was Charles Dickens.  He was the


[080-084]greatest observer since Shakespeare.  He had the eyes that see,


[080-085]the ears that really hear.  I place him above Thackeray.  Dickens


[080-086]wrote for the home, for the great public.  Thackeray wrote for the


[080-087]clubs.  The greatest novel in our language--and it may be in any


[080-088]other--is, according to my ideas, "A Tale of Two Cities."  In that,


[080-089]are philosophy, pathos, self-sacrifice, wit, humor, the grotesque


[080-090]and the tragic.  I think it is the most artistic novel that I have


[080-091]read.  The creations of Dickens' brain have become the citizens of


[080-092]the world.


[080-093]_Question_.  What is your opinion of American writers?


[080-094]_Answer_.  I think Emerson was a fine writer, and he did this world


[080-095]a great deal of good, but I do not class him with the first.  Some


[080-096]of his poetry is wonderfully good and in it are some of the deepest


[080-097]and most beautiful lines.  I think he was a poet rather than a


[080-098]philosopher.  His doctrine of compensation would be delightful if


[080-099]it had the facts to support it.


[080-100]Of course, Hawthorne was a great writer.  His style is a little


[080-101]monotonous, but the matter is good.  "The Marble Faun" is by far


[080-102]his best effort.  I shall always regret that Hawthorne wrote the


[080-103]life of Franklin Pierce.


[080-104]Walt Whitman will hold a high place among American writers.  His


[080-105]poem on the death of Lincoln, entitled "When Lilacs Last in the


[080-106]Dooryard Bloom'd," is the greatest ever written on this continent.


[080-107]He was a natural poet and wrote lines worthy of America.  He was


[080-108]the poet of democracy and individuality, and of liberty.  He was


[080-109]worthy of the great Republic.


[080-110]_Question_.  What about Henry George's books?


[080-111]_Answer_.  Henry George wrote a wonderful book and one that arrested


[080-112]the attention of the world--one of the greatest books of the century.


[080-113]While I do not believe in his destructive theories, I gladly pay


[080-114]a tribute to his sincerity and his genius.


[080-115]_Question_.  What do you think of Bellamy?


[080-116]_Answer_.  I do not think what is called nationalism of the Bellamy


[080-117]kind is making any particular progress in this country.  We are


[080-118]believers in individual independence, and will be, I hope, forever.


[080-119]Boston was at one time the literary center of the country, but the


[080-120]best writers are not living here now.  The best novelists of our


[080-121]country are not far from Boston.  Edgar Fawcett lives in New York.


[080-122]Howells was born, I believe, in Ohio, and Julian Hawthorne lives


[080-123]in New Jersey or in Long Island.  Among the poets, James Whitcomb


[080-124]Riley is a native of Indiana, and he has written some of the


[080-125]daintiest and sweetest things in American literature.  Edgar Fawcett


[080-126]is a great poet.  His "Magic Flower" is as beautiful as anything


[080-127]Tennyson has ever written.  Eugene Field of Chicago, has written


[080-128]some charming things, natural and touching.


[080-129]Westward the star of literature takes its course.


[080-130]--_The Star_, Kansas City, Mo., May 26, 1892.


[080-131]INEBRIETY.*


[080-132][* Published from notes found among Colonel Ingersoll's


[080-133]papers, evidently written soon after the discovery of the


[080-134]"Keeley Cure."]


[080-135]_Question_.  Do you consider inebriety a disease, or the result of


[080-136]diseased conditions?


[080-137]_Answer_.  I believe that by a long and continuous use of stimulants,


[080-138]the system gets in such a condition that it imperatively demands


[080-139]not only the usual, but an increased stimulant.  After a time,


[080-140]every nerve becomes hungry, and there is in the body of the man a


[080-141]cry, coming from every nerve, for nourishment.  There is a kind of


[080-142]famine, and unless the want is supplied, insanity is the result.


[080-143]This hunger of the nerves drowns the voice of reason--cares nothing


[080-144]for argument--nothing for experience--nothing for the sufferings


[080-145]of others--nothing for anything, except for the food it requires.


[080-146]Words are wasted, advice is of no possible use, argument is like


[080-147]reasoning with the dead.  The man has lost the control of his will


[080-148]--it has been won over to the side of the nerves.  He imagines that


[080-149]if the nerves are once satisfied he can then resume the control of


[080-150]himself.  Of course, this is a mistake, and the more the nerves


[080-151]are satisfied, the more imperative is their demand.  Arguments are


[080-152]not of the slightest force.  The knowledge--the conviction--that


[080-153]the course pursued is wrong, has no effect.  The man is in the


[080-154]grasp of appetite.  He is like a ship at the mercy of wind and wave


[080-155]and tide.  The fact that the needle of the compass points to the


[080-156]north has no effect--the compass is not a force--it cannot battle


[080-157]with the wind and tide--and so, in spite of the fact that the needle


[080-158]points to the north, the ship is stranded on the rocks.


[080-159]So the fact that the man knows that he should not drink has not


[080-160]the slightest effect upon him.  The sophistry of passion outweighs


[080-161]all that reason can urge.  In other words, the man is the victim


[080-162]of disease, and until the disease is arrested, his will is not his


[080-163]own.  He may wish to reform, but wish is not will.  He knows all


[080-164]of the arguments in favor of temperance--he knows all about the


[080-165]distress of wife and child--all about the loss of reputation and


[080-166]character--all about the chasm toward which he is drifting--and


[080-167]yet, not being the master of himself, he goes with the tide.


[080-168]For thousands of years society has sought to do away with inebriety


[080-169]by argument, by example, by law; and yet millions and millions have


[080-170]been carried away and countless thousands have become victims of


[080-171]alcohol.  In this contest words have always been worthless, for


[080-172]the reason that no argument can benefit a man who has lost control


[080-173]of himself.


[080-174]_Question_.  As a lawyer, will you express an opinion as to the


[080-175]moral and legal responsibility of a victim of alcoholism?


[080-176]_Answer_.  Personally, I regard the moral and legal responsibility


[080-177]of all persons as being exactly the same.  All persons do as they


[080-178]must.  If you wish to change the conduct of an individual you must


[080-179]change his conditions--otherwise his actions will remain the same.


[080-180]We are beginning to find that there is no effect without a cause,


[080-181]and that the conduct of individuals is not an exception to this


[080-182]law.  Every hope, every fear, every dream, every virtue, every


[080-183]crime, has behind it an efficient cause.  Men do neither right nor


[080-184]wrong by chance.  In the world of fact and in the world of conduct,


[080-185]as well as in the world of imagination, there is no room, no place,


[080-186]for chance.
