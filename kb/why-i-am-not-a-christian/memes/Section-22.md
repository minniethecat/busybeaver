[021-001]_Question_.  But who will win?


[021-002]_Answer_.  The present indications are favorable to Judge Hoadly.


[021-003]It is an off year.  The Ohio leaders on one side are not in perfect


[021-004]harmony.  The Germans are afraid, and they generally vote the


[021-005]Democratic ticket when in doubt.  The effort to enforce the Sunday


[021-006]law, to close the gardens, to make one day in the week desolate


[021-007]and doleful, will give the Republicans a great deal of hard work.


[021-008]_Question_.  How about Illinois?


[021-009]_Answer_.  Republican always.  The Supreme Court of Illinois has


[021-010]just made a good decision.  That Court decided that a contract made


[021-011]on Sunday can be enforced.  In other words, that Sunday is not holy


[021-012]enough to sanctify fraud.  You can rely on a State with a Court


[021-013]like that.  There is very little rivalry in Illinois.  I think that


[021-014]General Oglesby will be the next Governor.  He is one of the best


[021-015]men in that State or any other.


[021-016]_Question_.  What about Indiana?


[021-017]_Answer_.  In that State I think General Gresham is the coming man.


[021-018]He was a brave soldier, an able, honest judge, and he will fill


[021-019]with honor any position he may be placed in.  He is an excellent


[021-020]lawyer, and has as much will as was ever put in one man.  McDonald


[021-021]is the most available man for the Democrats.  He is safe and in


[021-022]every respect reliable.  He is without doubt the most popular man


[021-023]in his party.


[021-024]_Question_.  Well, Colonel, what are you up to?


[021-025]_Answer_.  Nothing.  I am surrounded by sand, sea and sky.  I listen


[021-026]to music, bathe in the surf and enjoy myself.  I am wondering why


[021-027]people take interest in politics; why anybody cares about anything;


[021-028]why everybody is not contented; why people want to climb the greased


[021-029]pole of office and then dodge the brickbats of enemies and rivals;


[021-030]why any man wishes to be President, or a member of Congress, or in


[021-031]the Cabinet, or do anything except to live with the ones he loves,


[021-032]and enjoy twenty-four hours every day.  I wonder why all New York


[021-033]does not come to Long Beach and hear Schreiner's Band play the


[021-034]music of Wagner, the greatest of all composers.  Finally, in the


[021-035]language of Walt Whitman, "I loaf and invite my soul."


[021-036]--_The Herald_, New York, July 1, 1883.


[021-037]THE INTERVIEWER.


[021-038]_Question_.  What do you think of newspaper interviewing?


[021-039]_Answer_.  I believe that James Redpath claims to have invented


[021-040]the "interview."  This system opens all doors, does away with


[021-041]political pretence, batters down the fortifications of dignity and


[021-042]official importance, pulls masks from solemn faces, compels everybody


[021-043]to show his hand.  The interviewer seems to be omnipresent.  He is


[021-044]the next man after the accident.  If a man should be blown up he


[021-045]would likely fall on an interviewer.  He is the universal interrogation


[021-046]point.  He asks questions for a living.  If the interviewer is fair


[021-047]and honest he is useful, if the other way, he is still interesting.


[021-048]On the whole, I regard the interviewer as an exceedingly important


[021-049]person.  But whether he is good or bad, he has come to stay.  He


[021-050]will interview us until we die, and then ask the "friends" a few


[021-051]questions just to round the subject off.


[021-052]_Question_.  What do you think of the tendency of newspapers is at


[021-053]present?


[021-054]_Answer_.  The papers of the future, I think, will be "news" papers.


[021-055]The editorial is getting shorter and shorter.  The paragraphist is


[021-056]taking the place of the heavy man.  People rather form their own


[021-057]opinions from the facts.  Of course good articles will always find


[021-058]readers, but the dreary, doleful, philosophical dissertation has


[021-059]had its day.  The magazines will fall heir to such articles; then


[021-060]religious weeklies will take them up, and then they will cease


[021-061]altogether.


[021-062]_Question_.  Do you think the people lead the newspapers, or do


[021-063]the newspapers lead them?


[021-064]_Answer_.  The papers lead and are led.  Most papers have for sale


[021-065]what people want to buy.  As a rule the people who buy determine


[021-066]the character of the thing sold.  The reading public grow more


[021-067]discriminating every year, and, as a result, are less and less


[021-068]"led."  Violent papers--those that most freely attack private


[021-069]character--are becoming less hurtful, because they are losing their


[021-070]own reputations.  Evil tends to correct itself.  People do not


[021-071]believe all they read, and there is a growing tendency to wait and


[021-072]hear from the other side.


[021-073]_Question_.  Do newspapers to-day exercise as much influence as


[021-074]they did twenty-five years ago?


[021-075]_Answer_.  More, by the facts published, and less, by editorials.


[021-076]As we become more civilized we are governed less by persons and


[021-077]more by principles--less by faith and more by fact.  The best of


[021-078]all leaders is the man who teaches people to lead themselves.


[021-079]_Question_.  What would you define public opinion to be?


[021-080]_Answer_.  First, in the widest sense, the opinion of the majority,


[021-081]including all kinds of people.  Second, in a narrower sense, the


[021-082]opinion of the majority of the intellectual.  Third, in actual


[021-083]practice, the opinion of those who make the most noise.  Fourth,


[021-084]public opinion is generally a mistake, which history records and


[021-085]posterity repeats.


[021-086]_Question_.  What do you regard as the result of your lectures?


[021-087]_Answer_.  In the last fifteen years I have delivered several


[021-088]hundred lectures.  The world is growing more and more liberal every


[021-089]day.  The man who is now considered orthodox, a few years ago would


[021-090]have been denounced as an Infidel.  People are thinking more and


[021-091]believing less.  The pulpit is losing influence.  In the light of


[021-092]modern discovery the creeds are growing laughable.  A theologian


[021-093]is an intellectual mummy, and excites attention only as a curiosity.


[021-094]Supernatural religion has outlived its usefulness.  The miracles


[021-095]and wonders of the ancients will soon occupy the same tent.  Jonah


[021-096]and Jack the Giant Killer, Joshua and Red Riding Hood, Noah and


[021-097]Neptune, will all go into the collection of the famous Mother


[021-098]Hubbard.


[021-099]--_The Morning Journal_, New York, July 3, 1883.


[021-100]POLITICS AND PROHIBITION.


[021-101]_Question_.  What do you think of the result in Ohio?


[021-102]_Answer_.  In Ohio prohibition did more harm to the Republican


[021-103]chances than anything else.  The Germans hold the Republicans


[021-104]responsible.  The German people believe in personal liberty.  They


[021-105]came to America to get it, and they regard any interference in the


[021-106]manner or quantity of their food and drink as an invasion of personal


[021-107]rights.  They claim they are not questions to be regulated by law,


[021-108]and I agree with them.  I believe that people will finally learn


[021-109]to use spirits temperately and without abuse, but teetotalism is


[021-110]intemperance in itself, which breeds resistance, and without


[021-111]destroying the rivulet of the appetite only dams it and makes it


[021-112]liable to break out at any moment. You can prevent a man from


[021-113]stealing by tying his hands behind him, but you cannot make him


[021-114]honest.  Prohibition breeds too many spies and informers, and makes


[021-115]neighbors afraid of each other.  It kills hospitality.  Again, the


[021-116]Republican party in Ohio is endeavoring to have Sunday sanctified


[021-117]by the Legislature.  The working people want freedom on Sunday.


[021-118]They wish to enjoy themselves, and all laws now making to prevent


[021-119]innocent amusement, beget a spirit of resentment among the common


[021-120]people.  I feel like resenting all such laws, and unless the


[021-121]Republican party reforms in that particular, it ought to be defeated.


[021-122]I regard those two things as the principal causes of the Republican


[021-123]party's defeat in Ohio.


[021-124]_Question_.  Do you believe that the Democratic success was due to


[021-125]the possession of reverse principles?


[021-126]_Answer_.  I do not think that the Democratic party is in favor of


[021-127]liberty of thought and action in these two regards, from principle,


[021-128]but rather from policy.  Finding the course pursued by the Republicans


[021-129]unpopular, they adopted the opposite mode, and their success is a


[021-130]proof of the truth of what I contend.  One great trouble in the


[021-131]Republican party is bigotry.  The pulpit is always trying to take


[021-132]charge.  The same thing exists in the Democratic party to a less


[021-133]degree.  The great trouble here is that its worst element--Catholicism


[021-134]--is endeavoring to get control.


[021-135]_Question_.  What causes operated for the Republican success in


[021-136]Iowa?


[021-137]_Answer_.  Iowa is a prohibition State and almost any law on earth


[021-138]as against anything to drink, can be carried there.  There are no


[021-139]large cities in the State and it is much easier to govern, but even


[021-140]there the prohibition law is bound to be a failure.  It will breed


[021-141]deceit and hypocrisy, and in the long run the influence will be bad.


[021-142]_Question_.  Will these two considerations cut any figure in the


[021-143]presidential campaign of 1884?


[021-144]_Answer_.  The party, as a party, will have nothing to do with


[021-145]these questions.  These matters are local.  Whether the Republicans


[021-146]are successful will depend more upon the country's prosperity.  If


[021-147]things should be generally in pretty good shape in 1884, the people


[021-148]will allow the party to remain in power.  Changes of administration


[021-149]depend a great deal on the feeling of the country.  If crops are


[021-150]bad and money is tight, the people blame the administration, whether


[021-151]it is responsible or not.  If a ship going down the river strikes


[021-152]a snag, or encounters a storm, a cry goes up against the captain.


[021-153]It may not have been his fault, but he is blamed, all the same,


[021-154]and the passengers at once clamor for another captain.  So it is


[021-155]in politics.


[021-156]If nothing interferes between this and 1884, the Republican party


[021-157]will continue.  Otherwise it will be otherwise.  But the principle


[021-158]of prosperity as applied to administrative change is strong.  If


[021-159]the panic of 1873 had occurred in 1876 there would have been no


[021-160]occasion for a commission to sit on Tilden.  If it had struck us


[021-161]in 1880, Hancock would have been elected.  Neither result would


[021-162]have its occasion in the superiority of the Democratic party, but


[021-163]in the belief that the Republican party was in some vague way


[021-164]blamable for the condition of things, and there should be a change.


[021-165]The Republican party is not as strong as it used to be.  The old


[021-166]leaders have dropped out and no persons have yet taken their places.


[021-167]Blaine has dropped out, and is now writing a book.  Conkling dropped


[021-168]out and is now practicing law, and so I might go on enumerating


[021-169]leaders who have severed their connection with the party and are


[021-170]no longer identified with it.


[021-171]_Question_.  What is your opinion regarding the Republican nomination


[021-172]for President?


[021-173]_Answer_.  My belief is that the Republicans will have to nominate


[021-174]some man who has not been conspicuous in any faction, and upon whom


[021-175]all can unite.  As a consequence he must be a new man.  The Democrats


[021-176]must do the same.  They must nominate a new man.  The old ones have


[021-177]been defeated so often that they start handicapped with their own


[021-178]histories, and failure in the past is very poor raw material out


[021-179]of which to manufacture faith for the future.  My own judgment is


[021-180]that for the Democrats, McDonald is as strong a man as they can


[021-181]get.  He is a man of most excellent sense and would be regarded as


[021-182]a safe man.  Tilden?  He is dead, and he occupies no stronger place


[021-183]in the general heart than a graven image.  With no magnetism, he


[021-184]has nothing save his smartness to recommend him.


[021-185]_Question_.  What are your views, generally expressed, on the


[021-186]tariff?


[021-187]_Answer_.  There are a great many Democrats for protection and a


[021-188]great many for so-called free trade.  I think the large majority


[021-189]of American people favor a reasonable tariff for raising our revenue


[021-190]and protecting our manufactures.  I do not believe in tariff for


[021-191]revenue only, but for revenue and protection.  The Democrats would


[021-192]have carried the country had they combined revenue and incidental


[021-193]protection.


[021-194]_Question_.  Are they rectifying the error now?


[021-195]_Answer_.  I believe they are, already.  They will do it next fall.


[021-196]If they do not put it in their platform they will embody it in


[021-197]their speeches.  I do not regard the tariff as a local, but a


[021-198]national issue, notwithstanding Hancock inclined to the belief that


[021-199]it was the former.
