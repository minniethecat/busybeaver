[089-001]As a result of this, all the literature of the world has changed,


[089-002]so that now in all departments the thoughts of women are taken into


[089-003]consideration, and women have thoughts, because they are the


[089-004]intellectual equals of men.


[089-005]There are no statesmen in this country the equals of Harriet


[089-006]Martineau; probably no novelists the equals of George Eliot or


[089-007]George Sand, and I think Ouida the greatest living novelist.  I


[089-008]think her "Ariadne" is one of the greatest novels in the English


[089-009]language.  There are few novels better than "Consuelo," few poems


[089-010]better than "Mother and Poet."


[089-011]So in all departments women are advancing; some of them have taken


[089-012]the highest honors at medical colleges; others are prominent in the


[089-013]sciences, some are great artists, and there are several very fine


[089-014]sculptors, &c., &c.


[089-015]So you can readily see what my opinion is on that point.


[089-016]I am in favor of giving woman all the domain she conquers, and as


[089-017]the world becomes civilized the domain that she can conquer will


[089-018]steadily increase.


[089-019]_Question_.  But, Colonel, is there no danger of greatly interfering


[089-020]with a woman's duties as wife and mother?


[089-021]_Answer_.  I do not think that it is dangerous to think, or that


[089-022]thought interferes with love or the duties of wife or mother.  I


[089-023]think the contrary is the truth; the greater the brain the greater


[089-024]the power to love, the greater the power to discharge all duties


[089-025]and obligations, so I have no fear for the future.  About women


[089-026]voting I don't care; whatever they want to do they have my consent.


[089-027]--_The Democrat_, Grand Rapids, Michigan, 1894.


[089-028]PROFESSOR SWING.


[089-029]_Question_.  Since you were last in this city, Colonel, a distinguished


[089-030]man has passed away in the person of Professor Swing.  The public


[089-031]will be interested to have your opinion of him.


[089-032]_Answer_.  I think Professor Swing did a great amount of good.  He


[089-033]helped to civilize the church and to humanize the people.  His


[089-034]influence was in the right direction--toward the light.  In his


[089-035]youth he was acquainted with toil, poverty, and hardship; his road


[089-036]was filled with thorns, and yet he lived and scattered flowers in


[089-037]the paths of many people.  At first his soul was in the dungeon of


[089-038]a savage creed, where the windows were very small and closely


[089-039]grated, and though which struggled only a few rays of light.  He


[089-040]longed for more light and for more liberty, and at last his fellow-


[089-041]prisoners drove him forth, and from that time until his death he


[089-042]did what he could to give light and liberty to the souls of men.


[089-043]He was a lover of nature, poetic in his temperament, charitable


[089-044]and merciful.  As an orator he may have lacked presence, pose and


[089-045]voice, but he did not lack force of statement or beauty of expression.


[089-046]He was a man of wide learning, of great admiration of the heroic


[089-047]and tender.  He did what he could to raise the standard of character,


[089-048]to make his fellow-men just and noble.  He lost the provincialism


[089-049]of his youth and became in a very noble sense a citizen of the


[089-050]world.  He understood that all the good is not in our race or in


[089-051]our religion--that in every land there are good and noble men, self-


[089-052]denying and lovely women, and that in most respects other religions


[089-053]are as good as ours, and in many respects better.  This gave him


[089-054]breadth of intellectual horizon and enlarged his sympathy for the


[089-055]failures of the world.  I regard his death as a great loss, and


[089-056]his life as a lesson and inspiration.


[089-057]--_Inter-Ocean_, Chicago, October 13, 1894.


[089-058]SENATOR SHERMAN AND HIS BOOK.*


[089-059][* No one is better qualified than Robert G. Ingersoll to


[089-060]talk about Senator Sherman's book and the questions it


[089-061]raises in political history.  Mr. Ingersoll was for years a


[089-062]resident of Washington and a next-door neighbor to Mr.


[089-063]Sherman; he was for an even longer period the intimate


[089-064]personal friend of James G. Blaine; he knew Garfield from


[089-065]almost daily contact, and of the Republican National


[089-066]Conventions concerning which Senator Sherman has raised


[089-067]points of controversy Mr. Ingersoll can say, as the North


[089-068]Carolinian said of the Confederacy: "Part of whom I am


[089-069]which."


[089-070]He placed Blaine's name before the convention at Cincinnati


[089-071]in 1876.  He made the first of the three great nominating


[089-072]speeches in convention history, Conkling and Garfield making


[089-073]the others in 1880.


[089-074]The figure of the Plumed Knight which Mr. Ingersoll created


[089-075]to characterize Mr. Blaine is part of the latter's memory.


[089-076]At Chicago, four years later, when Garfield, dazed by the


[089-077]irresistible doubt of the convention, was on the point of


[089-078]refusing that in the acceptance of which he had no voluntary


[089-079]part, Ingersoll was the adviser who showed him that duty to


[089-080]Sherman required no such action.]


[089-081]_Question_.  What do you think of Senator Sherman's book--especially


[089-082]the part about Garfield?


[089-083]_Answer_.  Of course, I have only read a few extracts from Mr.


[089-084]Sherman's reminiscences, but I am perfectly satisfied that the


[089-085]Senator is mistaken about Garfield's course.  The truth is that


[089-086]Garfield captured the convention by his course from day to day,


[089-087]and especially by the speech he made for Sherman.  After that


[089-088]speech, and it was a good one, the best Garfield ever made, the


[089-089]convention said, "Speak for yourself, John."


[089-090]It was perfectly apparent that if the Blaine and Sherman forces


[089-091]should try to unite, Grant would be nominated.  It had to be Grant


[089-092]or a new man, and that man was Garfield.  It all came about without


[089-093]Garfield's help, except in the way I have said.  Garfield even went


[089-094]so far as to declare that under no circumstances could he accept,


[089-095]because he was for Sherman, and honestly for him.  He told me that


[089-096]he would not allow his name to go before the convention.  Just


[089-097]before he was nominated I wrote him a note in which I said he was


[089-098]about to be nominated, and that he must not decline.  I am perfectly


[089-099]satisfied that he acted with perfect honor, and that he did his


[089-100]best for Sherman.


[089-101]_Question_.  Mr. Sherman expresses the opinion that if he had had


[089-102]the "moral strength" of the Ohio delegation in his support he would


[089-103]have been nominated?


[089-104]_Answer_.  We all know that while Senator Sherman had many friends,


[089-105]and that while many thought he would make an excellent President,


[089-106]still there was but little enthusiasm among his followers.  Sherman


[089-107]had the respect of the party, but hardly the love.


[089-108]_Question_.  In his book the Senator expresses the opinion that he


[089-109]was quite close to the nomination in 1888, when Mr. Quay was for


[089-110]him.  Do you think that is so, Mr. Ingersoll?


[089-111]_Answer_.  I think Mr. Sherman had a much better chance in 1888


[089-112]than in 1880, but as a matter of fact, he never came within hailing


[089-113]distance of success at any time.  He is not of the nature to sway


[089-114]great bodies of men.  He lacks the power to impress himself upon


[089-115]others to such an extent as to make friends of enemies and devotees


[089-116]of friends.  Mr. Sherman has had a remarkable career, and I think


[089-117]that he ought to be satisfied with what he has achieved.


[089-118]_Question_.  Mr. Ingersoll, what do you think defeated Blaine for


[089-119]the nomination in 1876?


[089-120]_Answer_.  On the first day of the convention at Cincinnati it was


[089-121]known that Blaine was the leading candidate.  All of the enthusiasm


[089-122]was for him.  It was soon known that Conkling, Bristow or Morton


[089-123]could not be nominated, and that in all probability Blaine would


[089-124]succeed.  The fact that Blaine had been attacked by vertigo, or


[089-125]had suffered from a stroke of apoplexy, gave an argument to those


[089-126]who opposed him, and this was used with great effect.  After Blaine


[089-127]was put in nomination, and before any vote was taken, the convention


[089-128]adjourned, and during the night a great deal of work was done.


[089-129]The Michigan delegation was turned inside out and the Blaine forces


[089-130]raided in several States.  Hayes, the dark horse, suddenly developed


[089-131]speed, and the scattered forces rallied to his support.  I have


[089-132]always thought that if a ballot could have been taken on the day


[089-133]Blaine was put in nomination he would have succeeded, and yet he


[089-134]might have been defeated for the nomination anyway.


[089-135]Blaine had the warmest friends and the bitterest enemies of any


[089-136]man in the party.  People either loved or hated him.  He had no


[089-137]milk-and-water friends and no milk-and-water enemies.


[089-138]_Question_.  If Blaine had been nominated at Cincinnati in 1876


[089-139]would he have made a stronger candidate than Hayes did?


[089-140]_Answer_.  If he had been nominated then, I believe that he would


[089-141]have been triumphantly elected.  Mr. Blaine's worst enemies would


[089-142]not have supported Tilden, and thousands of moderate Democrats


[089-143]would have given their votes to Blaine.


[089-144]_Question_.  Mr. Ingersoll, do you think that Mr. Blaine wanted


[089-145]the nomination in 1884, when he got it?


[089-146]_Answer_.  In 1883, Mr. Blaine told me that he did not want the


[089-147]nomination.  I said to him:  "Is that honest?"  He replied that he


[089-148]did not want it, that he was tired of the whole business.  I said:


[089-149]"If you do not want it; if you have really reached that conclusion,


[089-150]then I think you will get it."  He laughed, and again said:  "I do


[089-151]not want it."  I believe that he spoke exactly as he then felt.


[089-152]_Question_.  What do you think defeated Mr. Blaine at the polls in


[089-153]1884?


[089-154]_Answer_.  Blaine was a splendid manager for another man, a great


[089-155]natural organizer, and when acting for others made no mistake; but


[089-156]he did not manage his own campaign with ability.  He made a succession


[089-157]of mistakes.  His suit against the Indianapolis editor; his letter


[089-158]about the ownership of certain stocks; his reply to Burchard and


[089-159]the preachers, in which he said that history showed the church


[089-160]could get along without the state, but the state could not get


[089-161]along without the church, and this in reply to the "Rum, Romanism and


[089-162]Rebellion" nonsense; and last, but not least, his speech to the


[089-163]millionaires in New York--all of these things weakened him.  As a


[089-164]matter of fact many Catholics were going to support Blaine, but


[089-165]when they saw him fooling with the Protestant clergy, and accepting


[089-166]the speech of Burchard, they instantly turned against him.  If he


[089-167]had never met Burchard, I think he would have been elected.  His


[089-168]career was something like that of Mr. Clay; he was the most popular


[089-169]man of his party and yet----


[089-170]_Question_.  How do you account for Mr. Blaine's action in allowing


[089-171]his name to go before the convention at Minneapolis in 1892?


[089-172]_Answer_.  In 1892, Mr. Blaine was a sick man, almost worn out; he


[089-173]was not his former self, and he was influenced by others.  He seemed


[089-174]to have lost his intuition; he was misled, yet in spite of all


[089-175]defeats, no name will create among Republicans greater enthusiasm


[089-176]than that of James G. Blaine.  Millions are still his devoted,


[089-177]unselfish and enthusiastic friends and defenders.


[089-178]--_The Globe-Democrat_, St. Louis, October 27, 1895.


[089-179]REPLY TO THE CHRISTIAN ENDEAVORERS.


[089-180]_Question_.  How were you affected by the announcement that the


[089-181]united prayers of the Salvationists and Christian Endeavorers were


[089-182]to be offered for your conversion?


[089-183]_Answer_.  The announcement did not affect me to any great extent.


[089-184]I take it for granted that the people praying for me are sincere


[089-185]and that they have a real interest in my welfare.  Of course, I


[089-186]thank them one and all.  At the same time I can hardly account for


[089-187]what they did.  Certainly they would not ask God to convert me


[089-188]unless they thought the prayer could be answered.  And if their


[089-189]God can convert me of course he can convert everybody.  Then the


[089-190]question arises why he does not do it.  Why does he let millions


[089-191]go to hell when he can convert them all.  Why did he not convert


[089-192]them all before the flood and take them all to heaven instead of


[089-193]drowning them and sending them all to hell.  Of course these


[089-194]questions can be answered by saying that God's ways are not our


[089-195]ways.  I am greatly obliged to these people.  Still, I feel about


[089-196]the same, so that it would be impossible to get up a striking


[089-197]picture of "before and after."  It was good-natured on their part


[089-198]to pray for me, and that act alone leads me to believe that there


[089-199]is still hope for them.  The trouble with the Christian Endeavorers


[089-200]is that they don't give my arguments consideration.  If they did


[089-201]they would agree with me.  It seemed curious that they would advise


[089-202]divine wisdom what to do, or that they would ask infinite mercy to


[089-203]treat me with kindness.  If there be a God, of course he knows what


[089-204]ought to be done, and will do it without any hints from ignorant


[089-205]human beings.  Still, the Endeavorers and the Salvation people may


[089-206]know more about God than I do.  For all I know, this God may need


[089-207]a little urging.  He may be powerful but a little slow; intelligent


[089-208]but sometimes a little drowsy, and it may do good now and then to


[089-209]call his attention to the facts.  The prayers did not, so far as


[089-210]I know, do me the least injury or the least good.  I was glad to


[089-211]see that the Christians are getting civilized.  A few years ago


[089-212]they would have burned me.  Now they pray for me.
