[081-001]_Question_.  In the case of an inebriate who has committed a crime,


[081-002]what do you think of the common judicial opinion that such a criminal


[081-003]is as deserving of punishment as a person not inebriated?


[081-004]_Answer_.  I see no difference.  Believing as I do that all persons


[081-005]act as they must, it makes not the slightest difference whether


[081-006]the person so acting is what we call inebriated, or sane, or insane


[081-007]--he acts as he must.


[081-008]There should be no such thing as punishment.  Society should protect


[081-009]itself by such means as intelligence and humanity may suggest, but


[081-010]the idea of punishment is barbarous.  No man ever was, no man ever


[081-011]will be, made better by punishment.  Society should have two objects


[081-012]in view:  First, the defence of itself, and second, the reformation


[081-013]of the so-called criminal.


[081-014]The world has gone on fining, imprisoning, torturing and killing


[081-015]the victims of condition and circumstance, and condition and


[081-016]circumstance have gone on producing the same kind of men and women


[081-017]year after year and century after century--and all this is so


[081-018]completely within the control of cause and effect, within the scope


[081-019]and jurisdiction of universal law, that we can prophesy the number


[081-020]of criminals for the next year--the thieves and robbers and murderers


[081-021]--with almost absolute certainty.


[081-022]There are just so many mistakes committed every year--so many crimes


[081-023]--so many heartless and foolish things done--and it does not seem


[081-024]to be--at least by the present methods--possible to increase or


[081-025]decrease the number.


[081-026]We have thousands and thousands of pulpits, and thousands of


[081-027]moralists, and countless talkers and advisers, but all these sermons,


[081-028]and all the advice, and all the talk, seem utterly powerless in


[081-029]the presence of cause and effect.  Mothers may pray, wives may


[081-030]weep, children may starve, but the great procession moves on.


[081-031]For thousands of years the world endeavored to save itself from


[081-032]disease by ceremonies, by genuflections, by prayers, by an appeal


[081-033]to the charity and mercy of heaven--but the diseases flourished


[081-034]and the graveyards became populous, and all the ceremonies and all


[081-035]the prayers were without the slightest effect.  We must at last


[081-036]recognize the fact, that not only life, but conduct, has a physical


[081-037]basis.  We must at last recognize the fact that virtue and vice,


[081-038]genius and stupidity, are born of certain conditions.


[081-039]_Question_.  In which way do you think the reformation or reconstruction


[081-040]of the inebriate is to be effected--by punishment, by moral suasion,


[081-041]by seclusion, or by medical treatment?


[081-042]_Answer_.  In the first place, punishment simply increases the


[081-043]disease.  The victim, without being able to give the reasons, feels


[081-044]that punishment is unjust, and thus feeling, the effect of the


[081-045]punishment cannot be good.


[081-046]You might as well punish a man for having the consumption which he


[081-047]inherited from his parents, or for having a contagious disease


[081-048]which was given to him without his fault, as to punish him for


[081-049]drunkenness.  No one wishes to be unhappy--no one wishes to destroy


[081-050]his own well-being.  All persons prefer happiness to unhappiness,


[081-051]and success to failure,  Consequently, you might as well punish a


[081-052]man for being unhappy, and thus increase his unhappiness, as to


[081-053]punish him for drunkenness.  In neither case is he responsible for


[081-054]what he suffers.


[081-055]Neither can you cure this man by what is called moral suasion.


[081-056]Moral suasion, if it amounts to anything, is the force of argument


[081-057]--that is to say, the result of presenting the facts to the victim.


[081-058]Now, of all persons in the world, the victim knows the facts.  He


[081-059]knows not only the effect upon those who love him, but the effect


[081-060]upon himself.  There are no words that can add to his vivid


[081-061]appreciation of the situation.  There is no language so eloquent


[081-062]as the sufferings of his wife and children.  All these things the


[081-063]drunkard knows, and knows perfectly, and knows as well as any other


[081-064]human being can know.  At the same time, he feels that the tide


[081-065]and current of passion are beyond his power.  He feels that he


[081-066]cannot row against the stream.


[081-067]There is but one way, and that is, to treat the drunkard as the


[081-068]victim of a disease--treat him precisely as you would a man with


[081-069]a fever, as a man suffering from smallpox, or with some form of


[081-070]indigestion.  It is impossible to talk a man out of consumption,


[081-071]or to reason him out of typhoid fever.  You may tell him that he


[081-072]ought not to die, that he ought to take into consideration the


[081-073]condition in which he would leave his wife.  You may talk to him


[081-074]about his children--the necessity of their being fed and educated


[081-075]--but all this will have nothing to do with the progress of the


[081-076]disease.  The man does not wish to die--he wishes to live--and yet,


[081-077]there will come a time in his disease when even that wish to live


[081-078]loses its power to will, and the man drifts away on the tide,


[081-079]careless of life or death.


[081-080]So it is with drink.  Every nerve asks for a stimulant.  Every drop


[081-081]of blood cries out for assistance, and in spite of all argument,


[081-082]in spite of all knowledge, in this famine of the nerves, a man


[081-083]loses the power of will.  Reason abdicates the throne, and hunger


[081-084]takes its place.


[081-085]_Question_.  Will you state your reasons for your belief?


[081-086]_Answer_.  In the first place, I will give a reason for my unbelief


[081-087]in what is called moral suasion and in legislation.


[081-088]As I said before, for thousands and thousands of years, fathers


[081-089]and mothers and daughters and sisters and brothers have been


[081-090]endeavoring to prevent the ones they love from drink, and yet, in


[081-091]spite of everything, millions have gone on and filled at last a


[081-092]drunkard's grave.  So, societies have been formed all over the


[081-093]world.  But the consumption of ardent spirits has steadily increased.


[081-094]Laws have been passed in nearly all the nations of the world upon


[081-095]the subject, and these laws, so far as I can see, have done but


[081-096]little, if any, good.


[081-097]And the same old question is upon us now:  What shall be done with


[081-098]the victims of drink?  There have been probably many instances in


[081-099]which men have signed the pledge and have reformed.  I do not say


[081-100]that it is not possible to reform many men, in certain stages, by


[081-101]moral suasion.  Possibly, many men can be reformed in certain


[081-102]stages, by law; but the per cent. is so small that, in spite of


[081-103]that per cent., the average increases.  For these reasons, I have


[081-104]lost confidence in legislation and in moral suasion.  I do not say


[081-105]what legislation may do by way of prevention, or what moral suasion


[081-106]may do in the same direction, but I do say that after man have


[081-107]become the victims of alcohol, advice and law seem to have lost


[081-108]their force.


[081-109]I believe that science is to become the savior of mankind.  In


[081-110]other words, every appetite, every excess, has a physical basis,


[081-111]and if we only knew enough of the human system--of the tides and


[081-112]currents of thought and will and wish--enough of the storms of


[081-113]passion--if we only knew how the brain acts and operates--if we


[081-114]only knew the relation between blood and thought, between thought


[081-115]and act--if we only knew the conditions of conduct, then we could,


[081-116]through science, control the passions of the human race.


[081-117]When I first heard of the cure of inebriety through scientific


[081-118]means, I felt that the morning star had risen in the east--I felt


[081-119]that at last we were finding solid ground.  I did not accept--being


[081-120]of a skeptical turn of mind--all that I heard as true.  I preferred


[081-121]to hope, and wait.  I have waited, until I have seen men, the


[081-122]victims of alcohol, in the very gutter of disgrace and despair,


[081-123]lifted from the mire, rescued from the famine of desire, from the


[081-124]grasp of appetite.  I have seen them suddenly become men--masters


[081-125]and monarchs of themselves.


[081-126]MIRACLES, THEOSOPHY AND SPIRITUALISM.


[081-127]_Question_.  Do you believe that there is such a thing as a miracle,


[081-128]or that there has ever been?


[081-129]_Answer_.  Mr. Locke was in the habit of saying:  "Define your


[081-130]terms."  So the first question is, What is a miracle?  If it is


[081-131]something wonderful, unusual, inexplicable, then there have been


[081-132]many miracles.  If you mean simply that which is inexplicable, then


[081-133]the world is filled with miracles; but if you mean by a miracle,


[081-134]something contrary to the facts in nature, then it seems to me that


[081-135]the miracle must be admitted to be an impossibility.  It is like


[081-136]twice two are eleven in mathematics.


[081-137]If, again, we take the ground of some of the more advanced clergy,


[081-138]that a miracle is in accordance with the facts in nature, but with


[081-139]facts unknown to man, then we are compelled to say that a miracle


[081-140]is performed by a divine sleight-of-hand; as, for instance, that


[081-141]our senses are deceived; or, that it is perfectly simple to this


[081-142]higher intelligence, while inexplicable to us.  If we give this


[081-143]explanation, then man has been imposed upon by a superior intelligence.


[081-144]It is as though one acquainted with the sciences--with the action


[081-145]of electricity--should excite the wonder of savages by sending


[081-146]messages to his partner.  The savage would say, "A miracle;" but


[081-147]the one who sent the message would say, "There is no miracle; it


[081-148]is in accordance with facts in nature unknown to you."  So that,


[081-149]after all, the word miracle grows in the soil of ignorance.


[081-150]The question arises whether a superior intelligence ought to impose


[081-151]upon the inferior.  I believe there was a French saint who had his


[081-152]head cut off by robbers, and this saint, after the robbers went


[081-153]away, got up, took his head under his arm and went on his way until


[081-154]he found friends to set it on right.  A thing like this, if it


[081-155]really happened, was a miracle.


[081-156]So it may be said that nothing is much more miraculous than the


[081-157]fact that intelligent men believe in miracles.  If we read in the


[081-158]annals of China that several thousand years ago five thousand people


[081-159]were fed on one sandwich, and that several sandwiches were left


[081-160]over after the feast, there are few intelligent men--except, it


[081-161]may be, the editors of religious weeklies--who would credit the


[081-162]statement.  But many intelligent people, reading a like story in


[081-163]the Hebrew, or in the Greek, or in a mistranslation from either of


[081-164]these languages, accept the story without a doubt.


[081-165]So if we should find in the records of the Indians that a celebrated


[081-166]medicine-man of their tribe used to induce devils to leave crazy


[081-167]people and take up their abode in wild swine, very few people would


[081-168]believe the story.


[081-169]I believe it is true that the priest of one religion has never had


[081-170]the slightest confidence in the priest of any other religion.


[081-171]My own opinion is, that nature is just as wonderful one time as


[081-172]another; that that which occurs to-day is just as miraculous as


[081-173]anything that ever happened; that nothing is more wonderful than


[081-174]that we live--that we think--that we convey our thoughts by speech,


[081-175]by gestures, by pictures.


[081-176]Nothing is more wonderful than the growth of grass--the production


[081-177]of seed--the bud, the blossom and the fruit.  In other words, we


[081-178]are surrounded by the inexplicable.


[081-179]All that happens in conformity with what we know, we call natural;


[081-180]and that which is said to have happened, not in conformity with


[081-181]what we know, we say is wonderful; and that which we believe to


[081-182]have happened contrary to what we know, we call the miraculous.


[081-183]I think the truth is, that nothing ever happened except in a natural


[081-184]way; that behind every effect has been an efficient cause, and that


[081-185]this wondrous procession of causes and effects has never been, and


[081-186]never will be, broken.  In other words, there is nothing superior


[081-187]to the universe--nothing that can interfere with this procession


[081-188]of causes and effects.  I believe in no miracles in the theological


[081-189]sense.  My opinion is that the universe is, forever has been, and


[081-190]forever will be, perfectly natural.
