[032-001]_Question_.  Would the Catholicism of General Sherman's family


[032-002]affect his chances for the presidency?


[032-003]_Answer_.  I do not think the religion of the family should have


[032-004]any weight one way or the other.  It would make no difference with


[032-005]me; although I hate Catholicism with all my heart, I do not hate


[032-006]Catholics.  Some people might be so prejudiced that they would not


[032-007]vote for a man whose wife belongs to the Catholic Church; but such


[032-008]people are too narrow to be consulted.  General Sherman says that


[032-009]he wants no office.  In that he shows his good sense.  He is a


[032-010]great man and a great soldier.  He has won laurels enough for one


[032-011]brow.  He has the respect and admiration of the nation, and does


[032-012]not need the presidency to finish his career.  He wishes to enjoy


[032-013]the honors he has won and the rest he deserves.


[032-014]_Question_.  What is your opinion of Matthew Arnold?


[032-015]_Answer_.  He is a man of talent, well educated, a little fussy,


[032-016]somewhat sentimental, but he is not a genius.  He is not creative.


[032-017]He is a critic--not an originator.  He will not compare with


[032-018]Emerson.


[032-019]--_The Journal_, Kansas City, Missouri, February 23, 1884.


[032-020]SWEARING AND AFFIRMING.


[032-021]_Question_.  What is the difference in the parliamentary oath of


[032-022]this country which saves us from such a squabble as they have had


[032-023]in England over the Bradlaugh case?


[032-024]_Answer_.  Our Constitution provides that a member of Congress may


[032-025]swear or affirm.  The consequence is that we can have no such


[032-026]controversy as they have had in England.  The framers of our


[032-027]Constitution wished forever to divorce church and state.  They knew


[032-028]that it made no possible difference whether a man swore or affirmed,


[032-029]or whether he swore and affirmed to support the Constitution.  All


[032-030]the Federal officers who went into the Rebellion had sworn or affirmed


[032-031]to support the Constitution.  All that did no good.  The entire


[032-032]oath business is a mistake.  I think it would be a thousand times


[032-033]better to abolish all oaths in courts of justice.  The oath allows


[032-034]a rascal to put on the garments of solemnity, the mask of piety,


[032-035]while he tells a lie.  In other words, the oath allows the villain


[032-036]to give falsehood the appearance of truth.  I think it would be


[032-037]far better to let each witness tell his story and leave his evidence


[032-038]to the intelligence of the jury and judge.  The trouble about an


[032-039]oath is that its tendency is to put all witnesses on an equality;


[032-040]the jury says, "Why, he swore to it."  Now, if the oath were


[032-041]abolished, the jury would judge all testimony according to the


[032-042]witness, and then the evidence of one man of good reputation would


[032-043]outweigh the lies of thousands of nobodies.


[032-044]It was at one time believed that there was something miraculous in


[032-045]the oath, that it was a kind of thumbscrew that would torture the


[032-046]truth out of a rascal, and at one time they believed that if a man


[032-047]swore falsely he might be struck by lightning or paralyzed.  But


[032-048]so many people have sworn to lies without having their health


[032-049]impaired that the old superstition has very little weight with the


[032-050]average witness.  I think it would be far better to let every man


[032-051]tell his story; let him be cross-examined, let the jury find out


[032-052]as much as they can of his character, of his standing among his


[032-053]neighbors--then weigh his testimony in the scale of reason.  The


[032-054]oath is born of superstition, and everything born of superstition


[032-055]is bad.  The oath gives the lie currency; it gives it for the moment


[032-056]the ring of true metal, and the ordinary average juror is imposed


[032-057]upon and justice in many instances defeated.  Nothing can be more


[032-058]absurd than the swearing of a man to support the Constitution.


[032-059]Let him do what he likes.  If he does not support the Constitution,


[032-060]the probability is that his constituents will refuse to support


[032-061]him.  Every man who swears to support the Constitution swears to


[032-062]support it as he understands it, and no two understand it exactly


[032-063]alike.  Now, if the oath brightened a man's intellect or added to


[032-064]his information or increased his patriotism or gave him a little


[032-065]more honesty, it would be a good thing--but it doesn't.  And as a


[032-066]consequence it is a very useless and absurd proceeding.  Nothing


[032-067]amuses me more in a court than to see one calf kissing the tanned


[032-068]skin of another.


[032-069]--_The Courier_, Buffalo, New York, May 19, 1884.


[032-070]REPLY TO A BUFFALO CRITIC.


[032-071]_Question_.  What have you to say in reply to the letter in to-


[032-072]day's _Times_ signed R. H. S.?


[032-073]_Answer_.  I find that I am accused of "four flagrant wrongs," and


[032-074]while I am not as yet suffering from the qualms of conscience, nor


[032-075]do I feel called upon to confess and be forgiven, yet I have


[032-076]something to say in self-defence.


[032-077]As to the first objection made by your correspondent, namely, that


[032-078]my doctrine deprives people of the hope that after this life is


[032-079]ended they will meet their fathers, mothers, sisters and brothers,


[032-080]long since passed away, in the land beyond the grave, and there


[032-081]enjoy their company forever, I have this to say:  If Christianity


[032-082]is true we are not quite certain of meeting our relatives and


[032-083]friends where we can enjoy their company forever.  If Christianity


[032-084]is true most of our friends will be in hell.  The ones I love best


[032-085]and whose memory I cherish will certainly be among the lost.  The


[032-086]trouble about Christianity is that it is infinitely selfish.  Each


[032-087]man thinks that if he can save his own little, shriveled, microscopic


[032-088]soul, that is enough.  No matter what becomes of the rest.


[032-089]Christianity has no consolation for a generous man.  I do not wish


[032-090]to go to heaven if the ones who have given me joy are to be lost.


[032-091]I would much rather go with them.  The only thing that makes life


[032-092]endurable in this world is human love, and yet, according to


[032-093]Christianity, that is the very thing we are not to have in the


[032-094]other world.  We are to be so taken up with Jesus and the angels,


[032-095]that we shall care nothing about our brothers and sisters that have


[032-096]been damned.  We shall be so carried away with the music of the


[032-097]harp that we shall not even hear the wail of father or mother.


[032-098]Such a religion is a disgrace to human nature.


[032-099]As to the second objection,--that society cannot be held together


[032-100]in peace and good order without hell and a belief in eternal torment,


[032-101]I would ask why an infinitely wise and good God should make people


[032-102]of so poor and mean a character that society cannot be held together


[032-103]without scaring them.  Is it possible that God has so made the


[032-104]world that the threat of eternal punishment is necessary for the


[032-105]preservation of society?


[032-106]The writer of the letter also says that it is necessary to believe


[032-107]that if a man commits murder here he is destined to be punished in


[032-108]hell for the offence.  This is Christianity.  Yet nearly every


[032-109]murderer goes directly from the gallows to God.  Nearly every


[032-110]murderer takes it upon himself to lecture the assembled multitude


[032-111]who have gathered to see him hanged, and invite them to meet him


[032-112]in heaven.  When the rope is about his neck he feels the wings


[032-113]growing.  That is the trouble with the Christian doctrine.  Every


[032-114]murderer is told he may repent and go to heaven, and have the


[032-115]happiness of seeing his victim in hell.  Should heaven at any time


[032-116]become dull, the vein of pleasure can be re-thrilled by the sight


[032-117]of his victim wriggling on the gridiron of God's justice.  Really,


[032-118]Christianity leads men to sin on credit.  It sells rascality on


[032-119]time and tells all the devils they can have the benefit of the


[032-120]gospel bankrupt act.


[032-121]The next point in the letter is that I do not preach for the benefit


[032-122]of mankind, but for the money which is the price of blood.  Of


[032-123]course it makes no difference whether I preach for money or not.


[032-124]That is to say, it makes no difference to the preached.  The


[032-125]arguments I advance are either good or bad.  If they are bad they


[032-126]can easily be answered by argument.  If they are not they cannot


[032-127]be answered by personalities or by ascribing to me selfish motives.


[032-128]It is not a personal matter.  It is a matter of logic, of sense--


[032-129]not a matter of slander, vituperation or hatred.  The writer of


[032-130]the letter, R. H. S., may be an exceedingly good person, yet that


[032-131]will add no weight to his or her argument.  He or she may be a very


[032-132]bad person, but that would not weaken the logic of the letter, if


[032-133]it had any logic to begin with.  It is not for me to say what my


[032-134]motives are in what I do or say; it must be left to the judgment


[032-135]of mankind.  I presume I am about as bad as most folks, and as good


[032-136]as some, but my goodness or badness has nothing to do with the


[032-137]question.  I may have committed every crime in the world, yet that


[032-138]does not make the story of the flood reasonable, nor does it even


[032-139]tend to show that the three gentlemen in the furnace were not


[032-140]scorched.  I may be the best man in the world, yet that does not


[032-141]go to prove that Jonah was swallowed by the whale.  Let me say


[032-142]right here that if there is another world I believe that every soul


[032-143]who finds the way to that shore will have an everlasting opportunity


[032-144]to do right--of reforming.  My objection to Christianity is that


[032-145]it is infinitely cruel, infinitely selfish, and I might add infinitely


[032-146]absurd.  I deprive no one of any hope unless you call the expectation


[032-147]of eternal pain a hope.


[032-148]_Question_.  Have you read the Rev. Father Lambert's "Notes on


[032-149]Ingersoll," and if so, what have you to say of them or in reply to


[032-150]them?


[032-151]_Answer_.  I have read a few pages or paragraphs of that pamphlet,


[032-152]and do not feel called upon to say anything.  Mr. Lambert has the


[032-153]same right to publish his ideas that I have, and the readers must


[032-154]judge.  People who believe his way will probably think that he has


[032-155]succeeded in answering me.  After all, he must leave the public to


[032-156]decide.  I have no anxiety about the decision.  Day by day the


[032-157]people are advancing, and in a little while the sacred superstitions


[032-158]of to-day will be cast aside with the foolish myths and fables of


[032-159]the pagan world.


[032-160]As a matter of fact there can be no argument in favor of the


[032-161]supernatural.  Suppose you should ask if I had read the work of


[032-162]that gentleman who says that twice two are five.  I should answer


[032-163]you that no gentleman can prove that twice two are five; and yet


[032-164]this is exactly as easy as to prove the existence of the supernatural.


[032-165]There are no arguments in favor of the supernatural.  There are


[032-166]theories and fears and mistakes and prejudices and guesses, but no


[032-167]arguments--plenty of faith, but no facts; plenty of divine revelation,


[032-168]but no demonstration.  The supernatural, in my judgment, is a


[032-169]mistake.  I believe in the natural.


[032-170]--_The Times_, Buffalo, New York, May 19, 1884.
