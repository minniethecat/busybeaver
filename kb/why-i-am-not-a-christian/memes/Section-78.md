[077-001]Now, understand, I am not finding fault with any of these religions


[077-002]or with any of these ministers.  These religions and these ministers


[077-003]are the necessary and natural products of sufficient causes.


[077-004]Mankind has traveled from barbarism to what we now call civilization,


[077-005]by many paths, all of which under the circumstances, were absolutely


[077-006]necessary; and while I think the individual does as he must, I


[077-007]think the same of the church, of the corporation, and of the nation,


[077-008]and not only of the nation, but of the whole human race.  Consequently


[077-009]I have no malice and no prejudices.  I have likes and dislikes.


[077-010]I do not blame a gourd for not being a cantaloupe, but I like


[077-011]cantaloupes.  So I do not blame the old hard-shell Presbyterian


[077-012]for not being a philosopher, but I like philosophers.  So to wind


[077-013]it all up with regard to the tendency of modern thought, or as to


[077-014]the outcome of what you call religion, my own belief is that what


[077-015]is known as religion will disappear from the human mind.  And by


[077-016]"religion" I mean the supernatural.  By "religion" I mean living


[077-017]in this world for another, or living in this world to gratify some


[077-018]supposed being, whom we never saw and about whom we know nothing,


[077-019]and of whose existence we know nothing.  In other words, religion


[077-020]consists of the duties we are supposed to owe to the first great


[077-021]cause, and of certain things necessary for us to do here to insure


[077-022]happiness hereafter.  These ideas, in my judgment, are destined to


[077-023]perish, and men will become convinced that all their duties are


[077-024]within their reach, and that obligations can exist only between


[077-025]them and other sentient beings.  Another idea, I think, will force


[077-026]itself upon the mind, which is this:  That he who lives the best


[077-027]for this world lives the best for another if there be one.  In


[077-028]other words, humanity will take the place of what is called


[077-029]"religion."  Science will displace superstition, and to do justice


[077-030]will be the ambition of man.


[077-031]My creed is this:  Happiness is the only good.  The place to be


[077-032]happy is here.  The time to be happy is now.  The way to be happy


[077-033]is to make others so.


[077-034]_Question_.  What is going to take the place of the pulpit?


[077-035]_Answer_.  I have for a long time wondered why somebody didn't


[077-036]start a church on a sensible basis.  My idea is this:  There are,


[077-037]of course, in every community, lawyers, doctors, merchants, and


[077-038]people of all trades and professions who have not the time during


[077-039]the week to pay any particular attention to history, poetry, art,


[077-040]or song.  Now, it seems to me that it would be a good thing to have


[077-041]a church and for these men to employ a man of ability, of talent,


[077-042]to preach to them Sundays, and let this man say to his congregation:


[077-043]"Now, I am going to preach to you for the first few Sundays--eight


[077-044]or ten or twenty, we will say--on the art, poetry, and intellectual


[077-045]achievements of the Greeks."  Let this man study all the week and


[077-046]tell his congregation Sunday what he has ascertained.  Let him give


[077-047]to his people the history of such men as Plato, as Socrates, what


[077-048]they did; of Aristotle, of his philosophy; of the great Greeks,


[077-049]their statesmen, their poets, actors, and sculptors, and let him


[077-050]show the debt that modern civilization owes to these people.  Let


[077-051]him, too, give their religions, their mythology--a mythology that


[077-052]has sown the seed of beauty in every land.  Then let him take up


[077-053]Rome.  Let him show what a wonderful and practical people they


[077-054]were; let him give an idea of their statesmen, orators, poets,


[077-055]lawyers--because probably the Romans were the greatest lawyers.


[077-056]And so let him go through with nation after nation, biography after


[077-057]biography, and at the same time let there be a Sunday school


[077-058]connected with this church where the children shall be taught


[077-059]something of importance.  For instance, teach them botany, and when


[077-060]a Sunday is fair, clear, and beautiful, let them go into the fields


[077-061]and woods with their teachers, and in a little while they will


[077-062]become acquainted with all kinds of tress and shrubs and flowering


[077-063]plants.  They could also be taught entomology, so that every bug


[077-064]would be interesting, for they would see the facts in science--


[077-065]something of use to them.  I believe that such a church and such


[077-066]a Sunday school would at the end of a few years be the most


[077-067]intelligent collection of people in the United States.  To teach


[077-068]the children all of these things and to teach their parents, too,


[077-069]the outlines of every science, so that every listener would know


[077-070]something of geology, something of astronomy, so that every member


[077-071]could tell the manner in which they find the distance of a star--


[077-072]how much better that would be than the old talk about Abraham,


[077-073]Isaac, and Jacob, and quotations from Haggai and Zephaniah, and


[077-074]all this eternal talk about the fall of man and the Garden of Eden,


[077-075]and the flood, and the atonement, and the wonders of Revelation!


[077-076]Even if the religious scheme be true, it can be told and understood


[077-077]as well in one day as in a hundred years.  The church says, "He


[077-078]that hath ears to hear let him hear."  I say:  "He that hath brains


[077-079]to think, let him think."  So, too, the pulpit is being displaced


[077-080]by what we call places of amusement, which are really places where


[077-081]men go because they find there is something which satisfies in a


[077-082]greater or less degree the hunger of the brain.  Never before was


[077-083]the theatre as popular as it is now.  Never before was so much


[077-084]money lavished upon the stage as now.  Very few men having their


[077-085]choice would go to hear a sermon, especially of the orthodox kind,


[077-086]when they had a chance to see a great actor.


[077-087]The man must be a curious combination who would prefer an orthodox


[077-088]sermon, we will say, to a concert given by Theodore Thomas.  And


[077-089]I may say in passing that I have great respect for Theodore Thomas,


[077-090]because it was he who first of all opened to the American people


[077-091]the golden gates of music.  He made the American people acquainted


[077-092]with the great masters, and especially with Wagner, and it is a


[077-093]debt that we shall always owe him.  In this day the opera--that is


[077-094]to say, music in every form--is tending to displace the pulpit.


[077-095]The pulpits have to go in partnership with music now.  Hundreds of


[077-096]people have excused themselves to me for going to church, saying


[077-097]they have splendid music.  Long ago the Catholic Church was forced


[077-098]to go into partnership not only with music, but with painting and


[077-099]with architecture.  The Protestant Church for a long time thought


[077-100]it could do without these beggarly elements, and the Protestant


[077-101]Church was simply a dry-goods box with a small steeple on top of


[077-102]it, its walls as bleak and bare and unpromising as the creed.  But


[077-103]even Protestants have been forced to hire a choir of ungodly people


[077-104]who happen to have beautiful voices, and they, too, have appealed


[077-105]to the organ.  Music is taking the place of creed, and there is


[077-106]more real devotional feeling summoned from the temple of the mind


[077-107]by great music than by any sermon ever delivered.  Music, of all


[077-108]other things, gives wings to thought and allows the soul to rise


[077-109]above all the pains and troubles of this life, and to feel for a


[077-110]moment as if it were absolutely free, above all clouds, destined


[077-111]to enjoy forever.  So, too, science is beckoning with countless


[077-112]hands.  Men of genius are everywhere beckoning men to discoveries,


[077-113]promising them fortunes compared with which Aladdin's lamp was weak


[077-114]and poor.  All these things take men from the church; take men from


[077-115]the pulpit.  In other words, prosperity is the enemy of the pulpit.


[077-116]When men enjoy life, when they are prosperous here, they are in


[077-117]love with the arts, with the sciences, with everything that gives


[077-118]joy, with everything that promises plenty, and they care nothing


[077-119]about the prophecies of evil that fall from the solemn faces of


[077-120]the parsons.  They look in other directions.  They are not thinking


[077-121]about the end of the world.  They hate the lugubrious, and they


[077-122]enjoy the sunshine of to-day.  And this, in my judgment, is the


[077-123]highest philosophy:  First, do not regret having lost yesterday;


[077-124]second, do not fear that you will lose to-morrow; third, enjoy to-


[077-125]day.


[077-126]Astrology was displaced by astronomy.  Alchemy and the black art


[077-127]gave way to chemistry.  Science is destined to take the place of


[077-128]superstition.  In my judgment, the religion of the future will be


[077-129]Reason.


[077-130]--_The Tribune_, Chicago, Illinois, November, 1891.


[077-131]WOMAN SUFFRAGE, HORSE RACING, AND MONEY.


[077-132]_Question_.  What are your opinions on the woman's suffrage


[077-133]question?


[077-134]_Answer_.  I claim no right that I am not willing to give to my


[077-135]wife and daughters, and to the wives and daughters of other men.


[077-136]We shall never have a generation of great men until we have a


[077-137]generation of great women.  I do not regard ignorance as the


[077-138]foundation of virtue, or uselessness as one of the requisites of


[077-139]a lady.  I am a believer in equal rights.  Those who are amenable


[077-140]to the laws should have a voice in making the laws.  In every


[077-141]department where woman has had an equal opportunity with man, she


[077-142]has shown that she has equal capacity.


[077-143]George Sand was a great writer, George Eliot one of the greatest,


[077-144]Mrs. Browning a marvelous poet--and the lyric beauty of her "Mother


[077-145]and Poet" is greater than anything her husband ever wrote--Harriet


[077-146]Martineau a wonderful woman, and Ouida is probably the greatest


[077-147]living novelist, man or woman.  Give the women a chance.


[077-148][The Colonel's recent election as a life member of the Manhattan


[077-149]Athletic Club, due strangely enough to a speech of his denouncing


[077-150]certain forms of sport, was referred to, and this led him to express


[077-151]his contempt for prize-fighting, and then he said on the subject of


[077-152]horse-racing:  ]


[077-153]The only objection I have to horse racing is its cruelty.  The whip


[077-154]and spur should be banished from the track.  As long as these are


[077-155]used, the race track will breed a very low and heartless set of


[077-156]men.  I hate to see a brute whip and spur a noble animal.  The good


[077-157]people object to racing, because of the betting, but bad people,


[077-158]like myself, object to the cruelty.  Men are not forced to bet.


[077-159]That is their own business, but the poor horse, straining every


[077-160]nerve, does not ask for the lash and iron.  Abolish torture on the


[077-161]track and let the best horse win.


[077-162]_Question_.  What do you think of the Chilian insult to the United


[077-163]States flag?


[077-164]_Answer_.  In the first place, I think that our Government was


[077-165]wrong in taking the part of Balmaceda.  In the next place, we made


[077-166]a mistake in seizing the Itata.  America should always side with


[077-167]the right.  We should care nothing for the pretender in power, and


[077-168]Balmaceda was a cruel, tyrannical scoundrel.  We should be with


[077-169]the people everywhere.  I do not blame Chili for feeling a little


[077-170]revengeful.  We ought to remember that Chili is weak, and nations,


[077-171]like individuals, are sensitive in proportion that they are weak.


[077-172]Let us trust Chili just as we would England.  We are too strong to


[077-173]be unjust.


[077-174]_Question_.  How do you stand on the money question?


[077-175]_Answer_.  I am with the Republican party on the question of money.


[077-176]I am for the use of gold and silver both, but I want a dollar's


[077-177]worth of silver in a silver dollar.  I do not believe in light money,


[077-178]or in cheap money, or in poor money.  These are all contradictions


[077-179]in terms.  Congress cannot fix the value of money.  The most it


[077-180]can do is to fix its debt paying power.  It is beyond the power of


[077-181]any Congress to fix the purchasing value of what it may be pleased


[077-182]to call money.  Nobody knows, so far as I know, why people want


[077-183]gold.  I do not know why people want silver.  I do not know how


[077-184]gold came to be money; neither do I understand the universal desire,


[077-185]but it exists, and we take things as we find them.  Gold and silver


[077-186]make up, you may say, the money of the world, and I believe in


[077-187]using the two metals.  I do not believe in depreciating any American


[077-188]product; but as value cannot be absolutely fixed by law, so far as


[077-189]the purchasing power is concerned, and as the values of gold and


[077-190]silver vary, neither being stable any more than the value of wheat


[077-191]or corn is stable, I believe that legislation should keep pace


[077-192]within a reasonable distance at least, of the varying values, and


[077-193]that the money should be kept as nearly equal as possible.  Of


[077-194]course, there is one trouble with money to-day, and that is the


[077-195]use of the word "dollar."  It has lost its meaning.  So many


[077-196]governments have adulterated their own coin, and as many have


[077-197]changed weights, that the word "dollar" has not to-day an absolute,


[077-198]definite, specific meaning.  Like individuals, nations have been


[077-199]dishonest.  The only time the papal power had the right to coin


[077-200]money--I believe it was under Pius IX., when Antonelli was his


[077-201]minister--the coin of the papacy was so debased that even orthodox


[077-202]Catholics refused to take it, and it had to be called in and minted


[077-203]by the French Empire, before even the Italians recognized it as


[077-204]money.  My own opinion is, that either the dollar must be absolutely


[077-205]defined--it must be the world over so many grains of pure gold, or


[077-206]so many grains of pure silver--or we must have other denominations


[077-207]for our money, as for instance, ounces, or parts of ounces, and


[077-208]the time will come, in my judgment, when there will be a money of


[077-209]the world, the same everywhere; because each coin will contain


[077-210]upon its face the certificate of a government that it contains such


[077-211]a weight--so many grains or so many ounces--of a certain metal.


[077-212]I, for one, want the money of the United States to be as good as


[077-213]that of any other country.  I want its gold and silver exactly what


[077-214]they purport to be; and I want the paper issued by the Government


[077-215]to be the same as gold.  I want its credit so perfectly established


[077-216]that it will be taken in every part of the habitable globe.  I am


[077-217]with the Republican party on the question of money, also on the


[077-218]question of protection, and all I hope is that the people of this


[077-219]country will have sense enough to defend their own interests.
