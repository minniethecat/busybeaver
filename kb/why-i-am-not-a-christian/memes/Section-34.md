[033-001]"'If any person shall willfully, premeditatedly and


[033-002]despitefully blaspheme or speak loosely and profanely of


[033-003]Almighty God, Christ Jesus, the Holy Spirit, or the


[033-004]Scriptures of Truth, such person, on conviction thereof,


[033-005]shall be sentenced to pay a fine not exceeding one hundred


[033-006]dollars, and undergo an imprisonment not exceeding three


[033-007]months, or either, at the discretion of the court.'"


[033-008]Last evening Colonel Ingersoll sat in the dining room at


[033-009]Guy's Hotel, just in from New York City.  When told of the


[033-010]plans of Mr. Torrence and his friends, he laughed and said:]


[033-011]I did not suppose that anybody was idiotic enough to want me arrested


[033-012]for blasphemy.  It seems to me that an infinite Being can take care


[033-013]of himself without the aid of any agent of a Bible society.  Perhaps


[033-014]it is wrong for me to be here while the Methodist Conference is in


[033-015]session.  Of course no one who differs from the Methodist ministers


[033-016]should ever visit Philadelphia while they are here.  I most humbly


[033-017]hope to be forgiven.


[033-018]_Question_.  What do you think of the law of 1860?


[033-019]_Answer_.  It is exceedingly foolish.  Surely, there is no need


[033-020]for the Legislature of Pennsylvania to protect an infinite God,


[033-021]and why should the Bible be protected by law?  The most ignorant


[033-022]priest can hold Darwin up to orthodox scorn.  This talk of the Rev.


[033-023]Mr. Torrence shows that my lectures are needed; that religious


[033-024]people do not know what real liberty is.  I presume that the law


[033-025]of 1860 is an old one re-enacted.  It is a survival of ancient


[033-026]ignorance and bigotry, and no one in the Legislature thought it


[033-027]worth while to fight it.  It is the same as the law against swearing,


[033-028]both are dead letters and amount to nothing.  They are not enforced


[033-029]and should not be.  Public opinion will regulate such matters.  If


[033-030]all who take the name of God in vain were imprisoned there would


[033-031]not be room in the jails to hold the ministers.  They speak of God


[033-032]in the most flippant and snap-your-fingers way that can be conceived


[033-033]of.  They speak to him as though he were an intimate chum, and


[033-034]metaphorically slap him on the back in the most familiar way


[033-035]possible.


[033-036]_Question_.  Have you ever had any similar experiences before?


[033-037]_Answer_.  Oh, yes--threats have been made, but I never was arrested.


[033-038]When Mr. Torrence gets cool he will see that he has made a mistake.


[033-039]People in Philadelphia have been in the habit of calling the citizens


[033-040]of Boston bigots--but there is more real freedom of thought and


[033-041]expression in Boston than in almost any other city of the world.


[033-042]I think that as I am to suffer in hell forever, Mr. Torrence ought


[033-043]to be satisfied and let me have a good time here.  He can amuse


[033-044]himself through all eternity by seeing me in hell, and that ought


[033-045]to be enough to satisfy, not only an agent, but the whole Bible


[033-046]society.  I never expected any trouble in this State, and most


[033-047]sincerely hope that Mr. Torrence will not trouble me and make the


[033-048]city a laughing stock.


[033-049]Philadelphia has no time to waste in such foolish things.  Let the


[033-050]Bible take its chances with other books.  Let everybody feel that


[033-051]he has the right freely to express his opinions, provided he is


[033-052]decent and kind about it.  Certainly the Christians now ought to


[033-053]treat Infidels as well as Penn did Indians.


[033-054]Nothing could be more perfectly idiotic than in this day and


[033-055]generation to prosecute any man for giving his conclusions upon


[033-056]any religious subject.  Mr. Torrence would have had Huxley and


[033-057]Haeckel and Tyndall arrested; would have had Humboldt and John


[033-058]Stuart Mill and Harriet Martineau and George Eliot locked up in the


[033-059]city jail.  Mr. Torrence is a fossil from the old red sandstone of


[033-060]a mistake.  Let him rest.  To hear these people talk you would


[033-061]suppose that God is some petty king, some Liliputian prince, who


[033-062]was about to be dethroned, and who was nearly wild for recruits.


[033-063]_Question_.  But what would you do if they should make an attempt


[033-064]to arrest you?


[033-065]_Answer_.  Nothing, except to defend myself in court.


[033-066]--_Philadelphia Press_, May 24, 1884.


[033-067]POLITICS AND BRITISH COLUMBIA.


[033-068]_Question_.  I understand that there was some trouble in connection


[033-069]with your lecture in Victoria, B. C.  What are the facts?


[033-070]_Answer_.  The published accounts, as circulated by the Associated


[033-071]Press, were greatly exaggerated.  The affair was simply this:  The


[033-072]authorities endeavored to prevent the lecture.  They refused the


[033-073]license, on the ground that the theatre was unsafe, although it


[033-074]was on the ground floor, had many exits and entrances, not counting


[033-075]the windows.  The theatre was changed to meet the objections of


[033-076]the fire commissioner, and the authorities expressed their satisfaction


[033-077]and issued the license.  Afterward further objection was raised,


[033-078]and on the night of the lecture, when the building was about two-


[033-079]thirds full, the police appeared and said that the lecture would


[033-080]not be allowed to be delivered, because the house was unsafe.


[033-081]After a good deal of talk, the policeman in authority said that


[033-082]there should be another door, whereupon my friends, in a few minutes,


[033-083]made another door with an ax and a saw, the crowd was admitted and


[033-084]the lecture was delivered.  The audience was well-behaved, intelligent


[033-085]and appreciative.  Beyond some talking in the hall, and the natural


[033-086]indignation of those who had purchased tickets and were refused


[033-087]admittance, there was no disturbance.  I understand that those who


[033-088]opposed the lecture are now heartily ashamed of the course pursued.


[033-089]_Question_.  Are you going to take any part in the campaign?


[033-090]_Answer_.  It is not my intention to make any political speeches.


[033-091]I have made a good many in the past, and, in my judgment, have done


[033-092]my part.  I have no other interest in politics than every citizen


[033-093]should have.  I want that party to triumph which, in my judgment,


[033-094]represents the best interests of the country.  I have no doubt


[033-095]about the issue of the election.  I believe that Mr. Blaine will


[033-096]be the next President.  But there are plenty of talkers, and I


[033-097]really think that I have earned a vacation.


[033-098]_Question_.  What do you think Cleveland's chances are in New York?


[033-099]_Answer_.  At this distance it is hard to say.  The recent action


[033-100]of Tammany complicates matters somewhat.  But my opinion is that


[033-101]Blaine will carry the State.  I had a letter yesterday from that


[033-102]State, giving the opinion of a gentleman well informed, that Blaine


[033-103]would carry New York by no less than fifty thousand majority.


[033-104]_Question_.  What figure will Butler cut in the campaign?


[033-105]_Answer_.  I hardly think that Butler will have many followers on


[033-106]the 4th of November.  His forces will gradually go to one side or


[033-107]the other.  It is only when some great principle is at stake that


[033-108]thousands of men are willing to vote with a known minority.


[033-109]_Question_.  But what about the Prohibitionists?


[033-110]_Answer_.  They have a very large following.  They are fighting


[033-111]for something they believe to be of almost infinite consequence,


[033-112]and I can readily understand how a Prohibitionist is willing to be


[033-113]in the minority.  It may be well enough for me to say here, that


[033-114]my course politically is not determined by my likes or dislikes of


[033-115]individuals.  I want to be governed by principles, not persons.


[033-116]If I really thought that in this campaign a real principle was at


[033-117]stake, I should take part.  The only great question now is protection,


[033-118]and I am satisfied that it is in no possible danger.


[033-119]_Question_.  Not even in the case of a Democratic victory?


[033-120]_Answer_.  Not even in the event of a Democratic victory.  No State


[033-121]in the Union is for free trade.  Every free trader has an exception.


[033-122]These exceptions combined, control the tariff legislation of this


[033-123]country, and if the Democrats were in power to-day, with the control


[033-124]of the House and Senate and Executive, the exceptions would combine


[033-125]and protect protection.  As long as the Federal Government collects


[033-126]taxes or revenue on imports, just so long these revenues will be


[033-127]arranged to protect home manufactures.


[033-128]_Question_.  You said that if there were a great principle at stake,


[033-129]you would take part in the campaign.  You think, then, that there


[033-130]is no great principle involved?


[033-131]_Answer_.  If it were a matter of personal liberty, I should take


[033-132]part.  If the Republican party had stood by the Civil Rights Bill,


[033-133]I should have taken part in the present campaign.


[033-134]_Question_.  Still, I suppose we can count on you as a Republican?


[033-135]_Answer_.  Certainly, I am a Republican.


[033-136]--_Evening Post_, San Francisco, California, September 16, 1884.


[033-137]INGERSOLL CATECHISED.


[033-138]_Question_.  Does Christianity advance or retard civilization?


[033-139]_Answer_.  If by Christianity you mean the orthodox church, then


[033-140]I unhesitatingly answer that it does retard civilization, always


[033-141]has retarded it, and always will.  I can imagine no man who can be


[033-142]benefitted by being made a Catholic or a Presbyterian or a Baptist


[033-143]or a Methodist--or, in other words, by being made an orthodox


[033-144]Christian.  But by Christianity I do not mean morality, kindness,


[033-145]forgiveness, justice.  Those virtues are not distinctively Christian.


[033-146]They are claimed by Mohammedans and Buddhists, by Infidels and


[033-147]Atheists--and practiced by some of all classes.  Christianity


[033-148]consists of the miraculous, the marvelous, and the impossible.


[033-149]The one thing that I most seriously object to in Christianity is


[033-150]the doctrine of eternal punishment.  That doctrine subverts every


[033-151]idea of justice.  It teaches the infinite absurdity that a finite


[033-152]offence can be justly visited by eternal punishment.  Another


[033-153]serious objection I have is, that Christianity endeavors to destroy


[033-154]intellectual liberty.  Nothing is better calculated to retard


[033-155]civilization than to subvert the idea of justice.  Nothing is better


[033-156]calculated to retain barbarism than to deny to every human being


[033-157]the right to think.  Justice and Liberty are the two wings that


[033-158]bear man forward.  The church, for a thousand years, did all within


[033-159]its power to prevent the expression of honest thought; and when


[033-160]the church had power, there was in this world no civilization.  We


[033-161]have advanced just in the proportion that Christianity has lost


[033-162]power.  Those nations in which the church is still powerful are


[033-163]still almost savage--Portugal, Spain, and many others I might name.


[033-164]Probably no country is more completely under the control of the


[033-165]religious idea than Russia.  The Czar is the direct representative


[033-166]of God.  He is the head of the church, as well as of the state.


[033-167]In Russia every mouth is a bastille and every tongue a convict.


[033-168]This Russian pope, this representative of God, has on earth his


[033-169]hell (Siberia), and he imitates the orthodox God to the extent of


[033-170]his health and strength.


[033-171]Everywhere man advances as the church loses power.  In my judgment,


[033-172]Ireland can never succeed until it ceases to be Catholic; and there


[033-173]can be no successful uprising while the confessional exists.  At


[033-174]one time in New England the church had complete power.  There was


[033-175]then no religious liberty.  And so we might make a tour of the


[033-176]world, and find that superstition always has been, is, and forever


[033-177]will be, inconsistent with human advancement.


[033-178]_Question_.  Do not the evidences of design in the universe prove


[033-179]a Creator?


[033-180]_Answer_.  If there were any evidences of design in the universe,


[033-181]certainly they would tend to prove a designer, but they would not


[033-182]prove a Creator.  Design does not prove creation.  A man makes a


[033-183]machine.  That does not prove that he made the material out of


[033-184]which the machine is constructed.  You find the planets arranged


[033-185]in accordance with what you call a plan.  That does not prove that


[033-186]they were created.  It may prove that they are governed, but it


[033-187]certainly does not prove that they were created.  Is it consistent


[033-188]to say that a design cannot exist without a designer, but that a


[033-189]designer can?  Does not a designer need a design as much as a design


[033-190]needs a designer?  Does not a Creator need a Creator as much as


[033-191]the thing we think has been created?  In other words, is not this


[033-192]simply a circle of human ignorance?  Why not say that the universe


[033-193]has existed from eternity, as well as to say that a Creator has


[033-194]existed from eternity?  And do you not thus avoid at least one


[033-195]absurdity by saying that the universe has existed from eternity,


[033-196]instead of saying that it was created by a Creator who existed from


[033-197]eternity?  Because if your Creator existed from eternity, and


[033-198]created the universe, there was a time when he commenced; and back


[033-199]of that, according to Shelley, is "an eternity of idleness."
