Immortality — the belief that the human soul or consciousness persists after bodily death — was a doctrine that [[robert-ingersoll|Robert G. Ingersoll]] subjected to sustained critical scrutiny. While never denying the *possibility* of an afterlife, Ingersoll argued that the doctrine of immortality, as taught by orthodox [[christianity|Christianity]], rested on no evidence and served primarily as an instrument of control — the promise of [[hell|eternal punishment]] for unbelievers and heavenly reward for the faithful. His position exemplifies the agnostic principle: that claims lacking evidence deserve no assent.

## Ingersoll's Critique of Immortality

Ingersoll approached the question of immortality with characteristic [[reason|intellect]]ual honesty. He began by examining what the [[bible|Bible]] actually said on the subject — and found surprisingly little. In the [[bible|Old Testament]], he observed, the doctrine of immortality is barely present:

> The Old Testament shows man how he lost immortality. According to Genesis, God prevented man from putting forth his hand and eating of the Tree of Life. (009-002)

The [[bible|New Testament]], in Ingersoll's reading, offered no clearer warrant. He noted that "the [[bible|New Testament]] has but little on the subject" and found the scattered references contradictory — in one place believers are told to "seek for immortality," a strange instruction if souls are already immortal by nature (009-008).

This thin textual basis did not, by itself, disprove immortality. But it undermined the certainty with which churches preached eternal torment as the alternative to belief. Ingersoll's concern was not to convince believers to abandon hope of another life, but to expose the moral coercion embedded in the doctrine:

> I do not deny immortality, nor would I endeavor to shake the belief of anybody in another life. What I am endeavoring to do is to put out the fires of hell. If we cannot have heaven without hell, I am in favor of abolishing heaven. (009-016)

For Ingersoll, the question was ethical before it was metaphysical. A heaven purchased at the cost of eternal suffering for others was, in his view, worse than oblivion.

## Ingersoll's Personal View

When pressed for his own opinion, Ingersoll offered a modest, three-part response that captured his naturalistic outlook on existence:

> My opinion of immortality is this: First — I live, and that of itself is infinitely wonderful. Second — There was a time when I was not, and after I was not, I was. Third — There will be a time when I shall not be, and it will be exactly as it was before I was. (009-021)

This argument — that non-existence after death is no more troubling than non-existence before birth — became a classic formulation in [[freethought|freethought]] literature. Ingersoll found the [[truth|fact]] of having lived at all remarkable enough; the demand for endless continuation struck him as greedy rather than reasonable.

## The Biblical Doctrine

The [[bible|Bible]] presents an evolving — and Ingersoll would say contradictory — picture of the afterlife. The [[bible|Old Testament]] speaks mostly of Sheol, a shadowy underworld, rather than the heaven-and-hell dichotomy of later Christian theology. Only in the late prophetic books (Daniel 12:2) does a clear resurrection doctrine emerge.

| Source | View of Afterlife |
|--------|-------------------|
| [[bible|Old Testament]] (Torah) | Sheol — neutral underworld, no clear reward/punishment |
| [[bible|Old Testament]] (Prophets) | Emerging resurrection hope (Daniel, Isaiah) |
| [[bible|New Testament]] (Gospels) | Heaven and hell; eternal life through Christ |
| [[bible|New Testament]] (Paul) | Resurrection body; "absent from the body, present with the Lord" |
| Ingersoll | No evidence; agnostic; the wonder of this life is sufficient |

Ingersoll noted that the churches' emphasis on immortality was a relatively late development: "If the churches advocated immortality, if they advocated eternal justice... they would be in harmony with the aspirations of the human heart" (009-026). But the actual doctrine, with its threat of hell for unbelievers, poisoned the consolation it claimed to offer.
