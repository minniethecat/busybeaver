[049-001]_Answer_.  Not in the least.  I am perfectly willing that all


[049-002]civilized people should know my opinions on any question in which


[049-003]others than myself can have any interest.


[049-004]I was anxious, in the first place, that the defendants should have


[049-005]a fair and impartial trial.  The worst form of anarchy is when a


[049-006]judge violates his conscience and bows to a popular demand.  A


[049-007]court should care nothing for public opinion.  An honest judge


[049-008]decides the law, not as it ought to be, but as it is, and the state


[049-009]of the public mind throws no light upon the question of what the


[049-010]law then is.


[049-011]I thought that some of the rulings on the trial of the Anarchists


[049-012]were contrary to law.  I think so still.  I have read the opinion


[049-013]of the Supreme Court of Illinois, and while the conclusion reached


[049-014]by that tribunal is the law of that case, I was not satisfied with


[049-015]the reasons given, and do not regard the opinion as good law.


[049-016]There is no place for an Anarchist in the United States.  There is


[049-017]no excuse for any resort to force; and it is impossible to use


[049-018]language too harsh or too bitter in denouncing the spirit of anarchy


[049-019]in this country.  But, no matter how bad a man is, he has the


[049-020]right to be fairly tried; and if he cannot be fairly tried, then


[049-021]there is anarchy on the bench.  So I was opposed to the execution


[049-022]of these men.  I thought it would have been far better to commute


[049-023]the punishment to imprisonment, and I said so; and I not only said


[049-024]so, but I wrote a letter to Governor Oglesby, in which I urged the


[049-025]commutation of the death sentence.  In my judgment, a great mistake


[049-026]was made.  I am on the side of mercy, and if I ever make mistakes,


[049-027]I hope they will all be made on that side.  I have not the slightest


[049-028]sympathy with the feeling of revenge.  Neither have I ever admitted,


[049-029]and I never shall, that every citizen has not the right to give


[049-030]his opinion on all that may be done by any servant of the people,


[049-031]by any judge, or by any court, by any officer--however small or


[049-032]however great.  Each man in the United States is a sovereign, and


[049-033]a king can freely speak his mind.


[049-034]Words were put in my mouth that I never uttered with regard to the


[049-035]Anarchists.  I never said that they were saints, or that they would


[049-036]be martyrs.  What I said was that they would be regarded as saints


[049-037]and martyrs by many people if they were executed, and that has


[049-038]happened which I said would happen.  I am, so far as I know, on


[049-039]the side of the right.  I wish, above all things, for the preservation


[049-040]of human liberty.  This Government is the best, and we should not


[049-041]lose confidence in liberty.  Property is of very little value in


[049-042]comparison with freedom.  A civilization that rests on slavery is


[049-043]utterly worthless.  I do not believe in sacrificing all there is


[049-044]of value in the human heart, or in the human brain, for the


[049-045]preservation of what is called property, or rather, on account of


[049-046]the fear that what is called "property" may perish.  Property is


[049-047]in no danger while man is free.  It is the freedom of man that


[049-048]gives value to property.  It is the happiness of the human race


[049-049]that creates what we call value.  If we preserve liberty, the spirit


[049-050]of progress, the conditions of development, property will take care


[049-051]of itself.


[049-052]_Question_.  The Christian press during the past few months has


[049-053]been very solicitous as to your health, and has reported you weak


[049-054]and feeble physically, and not only so, but asserts that there is


[049-055]a growing disposition on your part to lay down your arms, and even


[049-056]to join the church.


[049-057]_Answer_.  I do not think the Christian press has been very solicitous


[049-058]about my _health_.  Neither do I think that my health will ever


[049-059]add to theirs.  The fact is, I am exceedingly well, and my throat


[049-060]is better than it has been for many years.  Any one who imagines


[049-061]that I am disposed to lay down my arms can read by Reply to Dr.


[049-062]Field in the November number of the _North American Review_.  I


[049-063]see no particular difference in myself, except this; that my hatred


[049-064]of superstition becomes a little more and more intense; on the


[049-065]other hand, I see more clearly, that all the superstitions were


[049-066]naturally produced, and I am now satisfied that every man does as


[049-067]he must, including priests and editors of religious papers.


[049-068]This gives me hope for the future.  We find that certain soil, with


[049-069]a certain amount of moisture and heat, produces good corn, and we


[049-070]find when the soil is poor, or when the ground is too wet, or too


[049-071]dry, that no amount of care can, by any possibility, produce good


[049-072]corn.  In other words, we find that the fruit, that is to say, the


[049-073]result, whatever it may be, depends absolutely upon the conditions.


[049-074]This being so, we will in time find out the conditions that produce


[049-075]good, intelligent, honest men.  This is the hope for the future.


[049-076]We shall know better than to rely on what is called reformation,


[049-077]or regeneration, or a resolution born of ignorant excitement.  We


[049-078]shall rely, then, on the eternal foundation--the fact in nature--


[049-079]that like causes produce like results, and that good conditions


[049-080]will produce good people.


[049-081]_Question_.  Every now and then some one challenges you to a


[049-082]discussion, and nearly every one who delivers lectures, or speeches,


[049-083]attacking you, or your views, says that you are afraid publicly to


[049-084]debate these questions.  Why do you not meet these men, and why do


[049-085]you not answer these attacks?


[049-086]_Answer_.  In the first place, it would be a physical impossibility


[049-087]to reply to all the attacks that have been made--to all the "answers."


[049-088]I receive these attacks, and these answers, and these lectures


[049-089]almost every day.  Hundreds of them are delivered every year.  A


[049-090]great many are put in pamphlet form, and, of course, copies are


[049-091]received by me.  Some of them I read, at least I look them over,


[049-092]and I have never yet received one worthy of the slightest notice,


[049-093]never one in which the writer showed the slightest appreciation of


[049-094]the questions under discussion.  All these pamphlets are about the


[049-095]same, and they could, for the matter, have all been produced by


[049-096]one person.  They are impudent, shallow, abusive, illogical, and


[049-097]in most respects, ignorant.  So far as the lecturers are concerned,


[049-098]I know of no one who has yet said anything that challenges a reply.


[049-099]I do not think a single paragraph has been produced by any of the


[049-100]gentlemen who have replied to me in public, that is now remembered


[049-101]by reason of its logic or beauty.  I do not feel called upon to


[049-102]answer any argument that does not at least appear to be of value.


[049-103]Whenever any article appears worthy of an answer, written in a kind


[049-104]and candid spirit, it gives me pleasure to reply.


[049-105]I should like to meet some one who speaks by authority, some one


[049-106]who really understands his creed, but I cannot afford to waste time


[049-107]on little priests or obscure parsons or ignorant laymen.


[049-108]--_The Truth Seeker_, New York, January 14, 1888.


[049-109]ROSCOE CONKLING.


[049-110]_Question_.  What is Mr. Conkling's place in the political history


[049-111]of the United States?


[049-112]_Answer_.  Upon the great questions Mr. Conkling has been right.


[049-113]During the war he was always strong and clear, unwavering and


[049-114]decided.  His position was always known.  He was right on


[049-115]reconstruction, on civil rights, on the currency, and, so far as


[049-116]I know, on all important questions.  He will be remembered as an


[049-117]honest, fearless man.  He was admired for his known integrity.  He


[049-118]was never even suspected of being swayed by an improper consideration.


[049-119]He was immeasurably above purchase.


[049-120]His popularity rested upon his absolute integrity.  He was not


[049-121]adapted for a leader, because he would yield nothing.  He had no


[049-122]compromise in his nature.  He went his own road and he would not


[049-123]turn aside for the sake of company.  His individuality was too


[049-124]marked and his will too imperious to become a leader in a republic.


[049-125]There is a great deal of individuality in this country, and a leader


[049-126]must not appear to govern and must not demand obedience.  In the


[049-127]Senate he was a leader.  He settled with no one.


[049-128]_Question_.  What essentially American idea does he stand for?


[049-129]_Answer_.  It is a favorite saying in this country that the people


[049-130]are sovereigns.  Mr. Conkling felt this to be true, and he exercised


[049-131]what he believed to be his rights.  He insisted upon the utmost


[049-132]freedom for himself.  He settled with no one but himself.  He stands


[049-133]for individuality--for the freedom of the citizen, the independence


[049-134]of the man.  No lord, no duke, no king was ever prouder of his


[049-135]title or his place than Mr. Conkling was of his position and his


[049-136]power.  He was thoroughly American in every drop of his blood.


[049-137]_Question_.  What have you to say about his having died with sealed


[049-138]lips?


[049-139]_Answer_.  Mr. Conkling was too proud to show wounds.  He did not


[049-140]tell his sorrows to the public.  It seemed sufficient to him to


[049-141]know the facts himself.  He seemed to have great confidence in


[049-142]time, and he had the patience to wait.  Of course he could have


[049-143]told many things that would have shed light on many important


[049-144]events, but for my part I think he acted in the noblest way.


[049-145]He was a striking and original figure in our politics.  He stood


[049-146]alone.  I know of no one like him.  He will be remembered as a


[049-147]fearless and incorruptible statesman, a great lawyer, a magnificent


[049-148]speaker, and an honest man.


[049-149]--_The Herald_, New York, April 19, 1888.


[049-150]THE CHURCH AND THE STAGE.


[049-151]_Question_.  I have come to talk with you a little about the drama.


[049-152]Have you any decided opinions on that subject?


[049-153]_Answer_.  Nothing is more natural than imitation.  The little


[049-154]child with her doll, telling it stories, putting words in its mouth,


[049-155]attributing to it the feelings of happiness and misery, is the


[049-156]simple tendency toward the drama.  Little children always have


[049-157]plays, they imitate their parents, they put on the clothes of their


[049-158]elders, they have imaginary parties, carry on conversation with


[049-159]imaginary persons, have little dishes filled with imaginary food,


[049-160]pour tea and coffee out of invisible pots, receive callers, and


[049-161]repeat what they have heard their mothers say.  This is simply the


[049-162]natural drama, an exercise of the imagination which always has been


[049-163]and which, probably, always will be, a source of great pleasure.


[049-164]In the early days of the world nothing was more natural than for


[049-165]the people to re-enact the history of their country--to represent


[049-166]the great heroes, the great battles, and the most exciting scenes


[049-167]the history of which has been preserved by legend.  I believe this


[049-168]tendency to re-enact, to bring before the eyes the great, the


[049-169]curious, and pathetic events of history, has been universal.  All


[049-170]civilized nations have delighted in the theatre, and the greatest


[049-171]minds in many countries have been devoted to the drama, and, without


[049-172]doubt, the greatest man about whom we know anything devoted his


[049-173]life to the production of plays.


[049-174]_Question_.  I would like to ask you why, in your opinion as a


[049-175]student of history, has the Protestant Church always been so bitterly


[049-176]opposed to the theatre?


[049-177]_Answer_.  I believe the early Christians expected the destruction


[049-178]of the world.  They had no idea of remaining here, in the then


[049-179]condition of things, but for a few days.  They expected that Christ


[049-180]would come again, that the world would be purified by fire, that


[049-181]all the unbelievers would be burned up and that the earth would


[049-182]become a fit habitation for the followers of the Saviour.


[049-183]Protestantism became as ascetic as the early Christians.  It is


[049-184]hard to conceive of anybody believing in the "Five Points" of John


[049-185]Calvin going to any place of amusement.  The creed of Protestantism


[049-186]made life infinitely sad and made man infinitely responsible.


[049-187]According to this creed every man was liable at any moment to be


[049-188]summoned to eternal pain; the most devout Christian was not absolutely


[049-189]sure of salvation.  This life was a probationary one.  Everybody


[049-190]was considered as waiting on the dock of time, sitting on his trunk,


[049-191]expecting the ship that was to bear him to an eternity of good or


[049-192]evil--probably evil.  They were in no state of mind to enjoy


[049-193]burlesque or comedy, and, so far as tragedy was concerned, their


[049-194]own lives and their own creeds were tragic beyond anything that


[049-195]could by any possibility happen in this world.  A broken heart was


[049-196]nothing to be compared with a damned soul; the afflictions of a


[049-197]few years, with the flames of eternity.  This, to say the least of


[049-198]it, accounts, in part, for the hatred that Protestantism always


[049-199]bore toward the stage.  Of course, the churches have always regarded


[049-200]the theatre as a rival and have begrudged the money used to support


[049-201]the stage.  You know that Macaulay said the Puritans objected to


[049-202]bear-baiting, not because they pitied the bears, but because they


[049-203]hated to see the people enjoy themselves.  There is in this at


[049-204]least a little truth.  Orthodox religion has always been and always


[049-205]will be the enemy of happiness.  This world is not the place for


[049-206]enjoyment.  This is the place to suffer.  This is the place to


[049-207]practice self-denial, to wear crowns of thorns; the other world is


[049-208]the place for joy, provided you are fortunate enough to travel the


[049-209]narrow, grass-grown path.  Of course, wicked people can be happy


[049-210]here.  People who care nothing for the good of others, who live


[049-211]selfish and horrible lives, are supposed by Christians to enjoy


[049-212]themselves; consequently, they will be punished in another world.


[049-213]But whoever carried the cross of decency, and whoever denied himself


[049-214]to that degree that he neither stole nor forged nor murdered, will


[049-215]be paid for this self-denial in another world.  And whoever said


[049-216]that he preferred a prayer-meeting with five or six queer old men


[049-217]and two or three very aged women, with one or two candles, and who


[049-218]solemnly affirmed that he enjoyed that far more than he could a


[049-219]play of Shakespeare, was expected with much reason, I think, to be


[049-220]rewarded in another world.
