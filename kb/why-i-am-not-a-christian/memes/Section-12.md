[011-001]All such schemes will, in my judgment, prove utterly futile.


[011-002]Perhaps the history of the world does not give an instance of the


[011-003]emigration of six millions of people.  Notwithstanding the treatment


[011-004]that Ireland has received from England, which may be designated as


[011-005]a crime of three hundred years, the Irish still love Ireland.  All


[011-006]the despotism in the world will never crush out of the Irish heart


[011-007]the love of home--the adoration of the old sod.  The negroes of


[011-008]the South have certainly suffered enough to drive them into other


[011-009]countries; but after all, they prefer to stay where they were born.


[011-010]They prefer to live where their ancestors were slaves, where fathers


[011-011]and mothers were sold and whipped; and I don't believe it will be


[011-012]possible to induce a majority of them to leave that land.  Of


[011-013]course, thousands may leave, and in process of time millions may


[011-014]go, but I don't believe emigration will ever equal their natural


[011-015]increase.  As the whites of the South become civilized the reason


[011-016]for going will be less and less.


[011-017]I see no reason why the white and black men cannot live together


[011-018]in the same land, under the same flag.  The beauty of liberty is


[011-019]you cannot have it unless you give it away, and the more you give


[011-020]away the more you have.  I know that my liberty is secure only


[011-021]because others are free.


[011-022]I am perfectly willing to live in a country with such men as


[011-023]Frederick Douglass and Senator Bruce.  I have always preferred a


[011-024]good, clever black man to a mean white man, and I am of the opinion


[011-025]that I shall continue in that preference.  Now, if we could only


[011-026]have a colonization bill that would get rid of all the rowdies,


[011-027]all the rascals and hypocrites, I would like to see it carried out,


[011-028]thought some people might insist that it would amount to a repudiation


[011-029]of the national debt and that hardly enough would be left to pay


[011-030]the interest.  No, talk as we will, the colored people helped to


[011-031]save this Nation.  They have been at all times and in all places


[011-032]the friends of our flag; a flag that never really protected them.


[011-033]And for my part, I am willing that they should stand forever beneath


[011-034]that flag, the equal in rights of all other people.  Politically,


[011-035]if any black men are to be sent away, I want it understood that


[011-036]each one is to be accompanied by a Democrat, so that the balance


[011-037]of power, especially in New York, will not be disturbed.


[011-038]_Question_.  I notice that leading Republican newspapers are advising


[011-039]General Garfield to cut loose from the machine in politics; what


[011-040]do you regard as the machine?


[011-041]_Answer_.  All defeated candidates regard the persons who defeated


[011-042]them as constituting a machine, and always imagine that there is


[011-043]some wicked conspiracy at the bottom of the machine.  Some of the


[011-044]recent reformers regard the people who take part in the early stages


[011-045]of a political campaign--who attend caucuses and primaries, who


[011-046]speak of politics to their neighbors, as members and parts of the


[011-047]machine, and regard only those as good and reliable American citizens


[011-048]who take no part whatever, simply reserving the right to grumble


[011-049]after the work has been done by others.  Not much can be accomplished


[011-050]in politics without an organization, and the moment an organization


[011-051]is formed, and, you might say, just a little before, leading spirits


[011-052]will be developed.  Certain men will take the lead, and the weaker


[011-053]men will in a short time, unless they get all the loaves and fishes,


[011-054]denounce the whole thing as a machine, and, to show how thoroughly


[011-055]and honestly they detest the machine in politics, will endeavor to


[011-056]organize a little machine themselves.  General Garfield has been


[011-057]in politics for many years.  He knows the principal men in both


[011-058]parties.  He knows the men who have not only done something, but


[011-059]who are capable of doing something, and such men will not, in my


[011-060]opinion, be neglected.  I do not believe that General Garfield will


[011-061]do any act calculated to divide the Republican party.  No thoroughly


[011-062]great man carries personal prejudice into the administration of


[011-063]public affairs.  Of course, thousands of people will be prophesying


[011-064]that this man is to be snubbed and another to be paid; but, in my


[011-065]judgment, after the 4th of March most people will say that General


[011-066]Garfield has used his power wisely and that he has neither sought


[011-067]nor shunned men simply because he wished to pay debts--either of


[011-068]love or hatred.


[011-069]--Washington correspondent, _Brooklyn Eagle_, January 31, 1881.


[011-070]HADES, DELAWARE AND FREETHOUGHT.


[011-071]_Question_.  Now that a lull has come in politics, I thought I


[011-072]would come and see what is going on in the religious world?


[011-073]_Answer_.  Well, from what little I learn, there has not been much


[011-074]going on during the last year.  There are five hundred and twenty-


[011-075]six Congregational Churches in Massachusetts, and two hundred of


[011-076]these churches have not received a new member for an entire year,


[011-077]and the others have scarcely held their own.  In Illinois there


[011-078]are four hundred and eighty-three Presbyterian Churches, and they


[011-079]have now fewer members than they had in 1879, and of the four


[011-080]hundred and eighty-three, one hundred and eighty-three have not


[011-081]received a single new member for twelve months.  A report has been


[011-082]made, under the auspices of the Pan-Presbyterian Council, to the


[011-083]effect that there are in the whole world about three millions of


[011-084]Presbyterians.  This is about one-fifth of one per cent. of the


[011-085]inhabitants of the world.  The probability is that of the three


[011-086]million nominal Presbyterians, not more than two or three hundred


[011-087]thousand actually believe the doctrine, and of the two or three


[011-088]hundred thousand, not more than five or six hundred have any true


[011-089]conception of what the doctrine is.  As the Presbyterian Church


[011-090]has only been able to induce one-fifth of one per cent. of the


[011-091]people to even call themselves Presbyterians, about how long will


[011-092]it take, at this rate, to convert mankind?  The fact is, there


[011-093]seems to be a general lull along the entire line, and just at


[011-094]present very little is being done by the orthodox people to keep


[011-095]their fellow-citizens out of hell.


[011-096]_Question_.  Do you really think that the orthodox people now


[011-097]believe in the old doctrine of eternal punishment, and that they


[011-098]really think there is a kind of hell that our ancestors so carefully


[011-099]described?


[011-100]_Answer_.  I am afraid that the old idea is dying out, and that


[011-101]many Christians are slowly giving up the consolations naturally


[011-102]springing from the old belief.  Another terrible blow to the old


[011-103]infamy is the fact that in the revised New Testament the word Hades


[011-104]has been substituted.  As nobody knows exactly what Hades means,


[011-105]it will not be quite so easy to frighten people at revivals by


[011-106]threatening them with something that they don't clearly understand.


[011-107]After this, when the impassioned orator cries out that all the


[011-108]unconverted will be sent to Hades, the poor sinners, instead of


[011-109]getting frightened, will begin to ask each other what and where


[011-110]that is.  It will take many years of preaching to clothe that word


[011-111]in all the terrors and horrors, pains, and penalties and pangs of


[011-112]hell.  Hades is a compromise.  It is a concession to the philosophy


[011-113]of our day.  It is a graceful acknowledgment to the growing spirit


[011-114]of investigation, that hell, after all, is a barbaric mistake.


[011-115]Hades is the death of revivals.  It cannot be used in song.  It


[011-116]won't rhyme with anything with the same force that hell does.  It


[011-117]is altogether more shadowy than hot.  It is not associated with


[011-118]brimstone and flame.  It sounds somewhat indistinct, somewhat


[011-119]lonesome, a little desolate, but not altogether uncomfortable.


[011-120]For revival purposes, Hades is simply useless, and few conversions


[011-121]will be made in the old way under the revised Testament.


[011-122]_Question_.  Do you really think that the church is losing ground?


[011-123]_Answer_.  I am not, as you probably know, connected with any


[011-124]orthodox organization, and consequently have to rely upon them for


[011-125]my information.  If they can be believed, the church is certainly


[011-126]in an extremely bad condition.  I find that the Rev. Dr. Cuyler,


[011-127]only a few days ago, speaking of the religious condition of Brooklyn


[011-128]--and Brooklyn, you know, has been called the City of Churches--


[011-129]states that the great mass of that Christian city was out of Christ,


[011-130]and that more professing Christians went to the theatre than to


[011-131]the prayer meeting. This, certainly, from their standpoint, is a


[011-132]most terrible declaration.  Brooklyn, you know, is one of the great


[011-133]religious centres of the world--a city in which nearly all the


[011-134]people are engaged either in delivering or in hearing sermons; a


[011-135]city filled with the editors of religious periodicals; a city of


[011-136]prayer and praise; and yet, while prayer meetings are free, the


[011-137]theatres, with the free list entirely suspended, catch more Christians


[011-138]than the churches; and this happens while all the pulpits thunder


[011-139]against the stage, and the stage remains silent as to the pulpit.


[011-140]At the same meeting in which the Rev. Dr. Cuyler made his astounding


[011-141]statements the Rev. Mr. Pentecost was the bearer of the happy news


[011-142]that four out of five persons living in the city of Brooklyn were


[011-143]going down to hell with no God and with no hope.  If he had read


[011-144]the revised Testament he would have said "Hades," and the effect


[011-145]of the statement would have been entirely lost.  If four-fifths of


[011-146]the people of that great city are destined to eternal pain, certainly


[011-147]we cannot depend upon churches for the salvation of the world.  At


[011-148]the meeting of the Brooklyn pastors they were in doubt as to whether


[011-149]they should depend upon further meetings, or upon a day of fasting


[011-150]and prayer for the purpose of converting the city.


[011-151]In my judgment, it would be much better to devise ways and means


[011-152]to keep a good many people from fasting in Brooklyn.  If they had


[011-153]more meat, they could get along with less meeting.  If fasting


[011-154]would save a city, there are always plenty of hungry folks even in


[011-155]that Christian town.  The real trouble with the church of to-day


[011-156]is, that it is behind the intelligence of the people.  Its doctrines


[011-157]no longer satisfy the brains of the nineteenth century; and if the


[011-158]church proposes to hold its power, it must lose its superstitions.


[011-159]The day of revivals is gone.  Only the ignorant and unthinking can


[011-160]hereafter be impressed by hearing the orthodox creed.  Fear has in


[011-161]it no reformatory power, and the more intelligent the world grows


[011-162]the more despicable and contemptible the doctrine of eternal misery


[011-163]will become.  The tendency of the age is toward intellectual liberty,


[011-164]toward personal investigation.  Authority is no longer taken for


[011-165]truth.  People are beginning to find that all the great and good


[011-166]are not dead--that some good people are alive, and that the


[011-167]demonstrations of to-day are fully equal to the mistaken theories


[011-168]of the past.


[011-169]_Question_.  How are you getting along with Delaware?


[011-170]_Answer_.  First rate.  You know I have been wondering where Comegys


[011-171]came from, and at last I have made the discovery.  I was told the


[011-172]other day by a gentleman from Delaware that many years ago Colonel


[011-173]Hazelitt died; that Colonel Hazelitt was an old Revolutionary


[011-174]officer, and that when they were digging his grave they dug up


[011-175]Comegys.  Back of that no one knows anything of his history.  The


[011-176]only thing they know about him certainly, is, that he has never


[011-177]changed one of his views since he was found, and that he never


[011-178]will.  I am inclined to think, however, that he lives in a community


[011-179]congenial to him.  For instance, I saw in a paper the other day


[011-180]that within a radius of thirty miles around Georgetown, Delaware,


[011-181]there are about two hundred orphan and friendless children.  These


[011-182]children, it seems, were indentured to Delaware farmers by the


[011-183]managers of orphan asylums and other public institutions in and


[011-184]about Philadelphia.  It is stated in the paper, that:
