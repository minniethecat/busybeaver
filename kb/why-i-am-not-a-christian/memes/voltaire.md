# Voltaire

Voltaire (1694–1778), the pen name of François-Marie Arouet, was the central figure of the French Enlightenment — a prolific writer, philosopher, and fierce critic of religious intolerance. In Robert Ingersoll's lectures, Voltaire appears repeatedly as a benchmark of [[reason|intellect]]ual courage and a counter-example to the moral claims of Protestant orthodoxy.

## 生平

Born into a middle-class Parisian family, Voltaire was educated by Jesuits before embarking on a literary career that would see him exiled, imprisoned in the Bastille, and eventually celebrated across Europe. His works spanned philosophy, history, poetry, plays, and an enormous correspondence that made him the most famous [[reason|intellect]]ual of his age.

Ingersoll noted that Voltaire's influence endured powerfully after his death, observing that "twenty years after the death of Voltaire there were millions less than when he was born" — a reference to the decline of religious persecution in the wake of Enlightenment thought [014-085].

## 主要观点与贡献

Voltaire's philosophy centered on religious toleration, freedom of speech, and the separation of church and state. His famous rallying cry — "Écrasez l'infâme" (Crush the infamous thing) — targeted clerical abuse and superstition. His *Philosophical Dictionary* appeared on Ingersoll's recommended reading list alongside works by Lecky, Buechner, and Paine [039-109].

Ingersoll also cited Voltaire's famous quip about [[reason|intellect]]ual envy: "It had gotten even with him by the invention of a rival system with which to destroy his" — a remark on how Newton's achievements provoked theological counter-attacks [015-008]. Voltaire was a master of using wit as a weapon against dogma.

## Ingersoll 的评价

Ingersoll made direct comparisons between Voltaire and [[martin-luther|Martin Luther]]. Where [[martin-luther|Luther]]'s conversation was "exceedingly low and vulgar," Voltaire represented the refined reason of the Enlightenment [014-078]. He framed the comparison starkly: "Protestantism is not holding its own with Catholicism" partly because figures like Voltaire had shown that neither confession could withstand rational scrutiny [014-098].

When asked whether one needed French to appreciate Voltaire and Hugo, Ingersoll dismissed the idea: "To say that a knowledge of French is necessary in order to appreciate Voltaire or Hugo is nonsensical" [083-012] [083-015]. Great ideas, he insisted, transcended language barriers — a claim that also served to elevate Voltaire to universal stature.

## 历史影响

Voltaire's legacy as the premier Enlightenment critic of religious authority made him a natural hero for 19th-century freethinkers like Ingersoll. His insistence that morality does not require religious belief, and that tolerance is the foundation of civil society, became core tenets of the freethought movement. Ingersoll treated Voltaire not merely as a historical figure but as a living argument — proof that the finest minds had found organized religion wanting.
