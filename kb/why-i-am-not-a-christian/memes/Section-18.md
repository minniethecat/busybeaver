[017-001]_Question_.  You most certainly do not see any foundation for the


[017-002]accusations of preachers like Sunderland, Newman and Power, _et


[017-003]al_, that the teaching of a secular liberalism has had anything to


[017-004]do with the shaping of Guiteau's character or the actions of his


[017-005]vagabond life or the inciting to his murderous deeds?


[017-006]_Answer_.  I do not think that the sermon of Mr. Power was in good


[017-007]taste.  It is utterly foolish to charge the "Stalwarts" with


[017-008]committing or inciting the crime against the life of the President.


[017-009]Ministers, though, as a rule, know but little of public affairs,


[017-010]and they always account for the actions of people they do not like


[017-011]or agree with, by attributing to them the lowest and basest motives.


[017-012]This is the fault of the pulpit--always has been, and probably


[017-013]always will be.  The Rev. Dr. Newman of New York, tells us that


[017-014]the crime of Guiteau shows three things:  First, that ignorant men


[017-015]should not be allowed to vote; second, that foreigners should not


[017-016]be allowed to vote; and third, that there should not be so much


[017-017]religious liberty.


[017-018]It turns out, first, the Guiteau is not an ignorant man; second,


[017-019]that he is not a foreigner; and third, that he is a Christian.


[017-020]Now, because an intelligent American Christian tries to murder the


[017-021]President, this person says we ought to do something with ignorant


[017-022]foreigners and Infidels.  This is about the average pulpit logic.


[017-023]Of course, all the ministers hate to admit the Guiteau was a


[017-024]Christian; that he belonged to the Young Men's Christian Association,


[017-025]or at least was generally found in their rooms; that he was a


[017-026]follower of Moody and Sankey, and probably instrumental in the


[017-027]salvation of a great many souls.  I do not blame them for wishing


[017-028]to get rid of this record.  What I blame them for is that they are


[017-029]impudent enough to charge the crime of Guiteau upon Infidelity.


[017-030]Infidels and Atheists have often killed tyrants.  They have often


[017-031]committed crimes to increase the liberty of mankind; but the history


[017-032]of the world will not show an instance where an Infidel or an


[017-033]Atheist has assassinated any man in the interest of human slavery.


[017-034]Of course, I am exceedingly glad that Guiteau is not an Infidel.


[017-035]I am glad that he believes the Bible, glad that he has delivered


[017-036]lectures against what he calls Infidelity, and glad that he has


[017-037]been working for years with the missionaries and evangelists of


[017-038]the United States.  He is a man of small brain, badly balanced.


[017-039]He believes the Bible to be the word of God.  He believes in the


[017-040]reality of heaven and hell.  He believes in the miraculous.  He is


[017-041]surrounded by the supernatural, and when a man throws away his


[017-042]reason, of course no one can tell what he will do.  He is liable


[017-043]to become a devotee or an assassin, a saint or a murderer; he may


[017-044]die in a monastery or in a penitentiary.


[017-045]_Question_.  According to your view, then, the species of fanaticism


[017-046]taught in sectarian Christianity, by which Guiteau was led to assert


[017-047]that Garfield dead would be better off then living--being in Paradise


[017-048]--is more responsible than office seeking or political factionalism


[017-049]for his deed?


[017-050]_Answer_.  Guiteau seemed to think that the killing of the President


[017-051]would only open the gates of Paradise to him, and that, after all,


[017-052]under such circumstances, murder was hardly a crime.  This same


[017-053]kind of reasoning is resorted to in the pulpit to account for death.


[017-054]If Guiteau had succeeded in killing the President, hundreds of


[017-055]ministers would have said, "After all, it may be that the President


[017-056]has lost nothing; it may be that our loss is his eternal gain; and


[017-057]although it seems cruel that Providence should allow a man like


[017-058]him to be murdered, still, it may have been the very kindest thing


[017-059]that could have been done for him."  Guiteau reasoned in this way,


[017-060]and probably convinced himself, judging from his own life, that


[017-061]this world was, after all, of very little worth.  We are apt to


[017-062]measure others by ourselves.  Of course, I do not think Christianity


[017-063]is responsible for this crime.  Superstition may have been, in part


[017-064]--probably was.  But no man believes in Christianity because he


[017-065]thinks it sanctions murder.  At the same time, an absolute belief


[017-066]in the Bible sometimes produces the worst form of murder.  Take


[017-067]that of Mr. Freeman, of Poeasset, who stabbed his little daughter


[017-068]to the heart in accordance with what he believed to be the command


[017-069]of God.  This poor man imitated Abraham; and, for that matter,


[017-070]Jehovah himself.  There have been in the history of Christianity


[017-071]thousands and thousands of such instances, and there will probably


[017-072]be many thousands more that have been and will be produced by


[017-073]throwing away our own reason and taking the word of some one else


[017-074]--often a word that we do not understand.


[017-075]_Question_.  What is your opinion as to the effect of praying for


[017-076]the recovery of the President, and have you any confidence that


[017-077]prayers are answered?


[017-078]_Answer_.  My opinion as to the value of prayer is well known.  I


[017-079]take it that every one who prays for the President shows at least


[017-080]his sympathy and good will.  Personally, I have no objection to


[017-081]anybody's praying.  Those who think their prayers are answered


[017-082]should pray.  For all who honestly believe this, and who honestly


[017-083]implore their Deity to watch over, protect, and save the life of


[017-084]the President, I have only the kindliest feelings.


[017-085]It may be that a few will pray to be seen of men; but I suppose


[017-086]that most people on a subject like this are honest.  Personally,


[017-087]I have not the slightest idea of the existence of the supernatural.


[017-088]Prayer may affect the person who prays.  It may put him in such a


[017-089]frame of mind that he can better bear disappointment than if he


[017-090]had not prayed; but I cannot believe that there is any being who


[017-091]hears and answers prayer.


[017-092]When we remember the earthquakes that have devoured, the pestilences


[017-093]that have covered the earth with corpses, and all the crimes and


[017-094]agonies that have been inflicted upon the good and weak by the bad


[017-095]and strong, it does not seem possible that anything can be accomplished


[017-096]by prayer.  I do not wish to hurt the feelings of anyone, but I


[017-097]imagine that I have a right to my own opinion.  If the President


[017-098]gets well it will be because the bullet did not strike an absolutely


[017-099]vital part; it will be because he has been well cared for; because


[017-100]he has had about him intelligent and skillful physicians, men who


[017-101]understood their profession.  No doubt he has received great support


[017-102]from the universal expression of sympathy and kindness.  The


[017-103]knowledge that fifty millions of people are his friends has given


[017-104]him nerve and hope.  Some of the ministers, I see, think that God


[017-105]was actually present and deflected the ball.  Another minister


[017-106]tells us that the President would have been assassinated in a


[017-107]church, but that God determined not to allow so frightful a crime


[017-108]to be committed in so sacred an edifice.  All this sounds to me


[017-109]like perfect absurdity--simple noise.  Yet, I presume that those


[017-110]who talk in this way are good people and believe what they say.


[017-111]Of course, they can give no reason why God did not deflect the ball


[017-112]when Lincoln was assassinated.  The truth is, the pulpit first


[017-113]endeavors to find out the facts, and then to make a theory to fit


[017-114]them.  Whoever believes in a special providence must, of necessity,


[017-115]by illogical and absurd; because it is impossible to make any


[017-116]theological theory that some facts will not contradict.


[017-117]_Question_.  Won't you give us, then, Colonel, your analysis of


[017-118]this act, and the motives leading to it?


[017-119]_Answer_.  I think Guiteau wanted an office and was refused.  He


[017-120]became importunate.  He was, substantially, put out of the White


[017-121]House.  He became malicious.  He made up his mind to be revenged.


[017-122]This, in my judgment, is the diagnosis of his case.  Since he has


[017-123]been in jail he has never said one word about having been put out


[017-124]of the White House; he is lawyer enough to know he must not furnish


[017-125]any ground for malice.  He is a miserable, malicious and worthless


[017-126]wretch, infinitely egotistical, imagines that he did a great deal


[017-127]toward the election of Garfield, and upon being refused the house


[017-128]a serpent of malice coiled in his heart, and he determined to be


[017-129]revenged.  That is all!


[017-130]_Question_.  Do you, in any way, see any reason or foundation for


[017-131]the severe and bitter criticisms made against the Stalwart leaders


[017-132]in connection with this crime?  As you are well known to be a friend


[017-133]of the administration, while not unfriendly to Mr. Conkling and


[017-134]those acting with him, would you mind giving the public your opinion


[017-135]on this point?


[017-136]_Answer_.  Of course, I do not hold Arthur, Conkling and Platt


[017-137]responsible for Guiteau's action.  In the first excitement a thousand


[017-138]unreasonable things were said; and when passion has possession of


[017-139]the brain, suspicion is a welcome visitor.


[017-140]I do not think that any friend of the administration really believes


[017-141]Conkling, Platt and Arthur responsible in the slightest degree.


[017-142]Conkling wished to prevent the appointment of Robertson.  The


[017-143]President stood by his friend.  One thing brought on another, Mr.


[017-144]Conkling petulantly resigned, and made the mistake of his life.


[017-145]There was a good deal of feeling, but, of course, no one dreamed


[017-146]that the wretch, Guiteau, was lying in wait for the President's


[017-147]life.  In the first place, Guiteau was on the President's side,


[017-148]and was bitterly opposed to Conkling.  Guiteau did what he did from


[017-149]malice and personal spite.  I think the sermon preached last Sunday


[017-150]in the Campbellite Church was unwise, ill advised, and calculated


[017-151]to make enemies instead of friends.  Mr. Conkling has been beaten.


[017-152]He has paid for the mistake he made.  If he can stand it, I can;


[017-153]and why should there be any malice on the subject?  Exceedingly


[017-154]good men have made mistakes, and afterward corrected them.


[017-155]_Question_.  Is it not true, Colonel Ingersoll, that the lesson of


[017-156]this deed is to point the real and overwhelming need of re-knitting


[017-157]and harmonizing the factions?


[017-158]_Answer_.  There is hardly enough faction left for "knitting."


[017-159]The party is in harmony now.  All that is necessary is to stop


[017-160]talking.  The people of this country care very little as to who


[017-161]holds any particular office.  They wish to have the Government


[017-162]administered in accordance with certain great principles, and they


[017-163]leave the fields, the shops, and the stores once in four years,


[017-164]for the purpose of attending to that business.  In the meantime,


[017-165]politicians quarrel about offices.  The people go on.  They plow


[017-166]fields, they build homes, they open mines, they enrich the world,


[017-167]they cover our country with prosperity, and enjoy the aforesaid


[017-168]quarrels.  But when the time comes, these gentlemen are forgotten.


[017-169]Principles take the place of politicians, and the people settle


[017-170]these questions for themselves.


[017-171]--_Sunday Gazette_, Washington, D. C., July 24, 1881.


[017-172]DISTRICT SUFFRAGE.


[017-173]_Question_.  You have heretofore incidentally expressed yourself


[017-174]on the matter of local suffrage in the District of Columbia.  Have


[017-175]you any objections to giving your present views of the question?


[017-176]_Answer_.  I am still in favor of suffrage in the District.  The


[017-177]real trouble is, that before any substantial relief can be reached,


[017-178]there must be a change in the Constitution of the United States.


[017-179]The mere right to elect aldermen and mayors and policemen is of no


[017-180]great importance.  It is a mistake to take all political power from


[017-181]the citizens of the District.  Americans want to help rule the


[017-182]country.  The District ought to have at least one Representative


[017-183]in Congress, and should elect one presidential elector.  The people


[017-184]here should have a voice.  They should feel that they are a part


[017-185]of this country.  They should have the right to sue in all Federal


[017-186]courts, precisely as though they were citizens of a State.  This


[017-187]city ought to have half a million of inhabitants.  Thousands would


[017-188]come here every year from every part of the Union, were it not for


[017-189]the fact that they do not wish to become political nothings.  They


[017-190]think that citizenship is worth something, and they preserve it by


[017-191]staying away from Washington.  This city is a "flag of truce" where


[017-192]wounded and dead politicians congregate; the Mecca of failures,


[017-193]the perdition of claimants, the purgatory of seekers after place,


[017-194]and the heaven only of those who neither want nor do anything.


[017-195]Nothing is manufactured, no solid business is done in this city,


[017-196]and there never will be until energetic, thrifty people wish to


[017-197]make it their home, and they will not wish that until the people


[017-198]of the District have something like the rights and political


[017-199]prospects of other citizens.  It is hard to see why the right to


[017-200]representation should be taken from citizens living in the Capital


[017-201]of the Nation.  The believers in free government should believe in


[017-202]a free capital.
