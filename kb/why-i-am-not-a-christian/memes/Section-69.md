[068-001]Christianity is the most heartless of all religions--the most


[068-002]unforgiving, the most revengeful.  According to the Episcopalian


[068-003]belief, God becomes the eternal prosecutor of his own children.


[068-004]I know of no creed believed by any tribe, not excepting the tribes


[068-005]where cannibalism is practiced, that is more heartless, more inhuman


[068-006]than this.  To find that the creed is false is like being roused


[068-007]from a frightful dream, in which hundreds of serpents are coiled


[068-008]about you, in which their eyes, gleaming with hatred, are fixed on


[068-009]you, and finding the world bathed in sunshine and the songs of


[068-010]birds in your ears and those you love about you.


[068-011]--_New York World_, November 18, 1888.


[068-012]WORKING GIRLS.


[068-013]_Question_.  What is your opinion of the work undertaken by the


[068-014]_World_ in behalf of the city slave girl?


[068-015]_Answer_.  I know of nothing better for a great journal to do.


[068-016]The average girl is so helpless, and the greed of the employer is


[068-017]such, that unless some newspaper or some person of great influence


[068-018]comes to her assistance, she is liable not simply to be imposed


[068-019]upon, but to be made a slave.  Girls, as a rule, are so anxious to


[068-020]please, so willing to work, that they bear almost every hardship


[068-021]without complaint.  Nothing is more terrible than to see the rich


[068-022]living on the work of the poor.  One can hardly imagine the utter


[068-023]heartlessness of a man who stands between the wholesale manufacturer


[068-024]and the wretched women who make their living--or rather retard


[068-025]their death--by the needle.  How a human being can consent to live


[068-026]on this profit, stolen from poverty, is beyond my imagination.


[068-027]These men, when known, will be regarded as hyenas and jackals.


[068-028]They are like the wild beasts which follow herds of cattle for the


[068-029]purpose of devouring those that are injured or those that have


[068-030]fallen by the wayside from weakness.


[068-031]_Question_.  What effect has unlimited immigration on the wages of


[068-032]women?


[068-033]_Answer_.  If our country were overpopulated, the effect of


[068-034]immigration would be to lessen wages, for the reason that the


[068-035]working people of Europe are used to lower wages, and have been in


[068-036]the habit of practicing an economy unknown to us.  But this country


[068-037]is not overpopulated.  There is plenty of room for several hundred


[068-038]millions more.  Wages, however, are too low in the United States.


[068-039]The general tendency is to leave the question of labor to what is


[068-040]called the law of supply and demand.  My hope is that in time we


[068-041]shall become civilized enough to know that there is a higher law,


[068-042]or rather a higher meaning in the law of supply and demand, than


[068-043]is now perceived.  Year after year what are called the necessaries


[068-044]of life increase.  Many things now regarded as necessaries were


[068-045]formerly looked upon as luxuries. So, as man becomes civilized, he


[068-046]increases what may be called the necessities of his life.  When


[068-047]perfectly civilized, one of the necessities of his life will be


[068-048]that the lives of others shall be of some value to them.  A good


[068-049]man is not happy so long as he knows that other good men and women


[068-050]suffer for raiment and for food, and have no roof but the sky, no


[068-051]home but the highway.  Consequently what is called the law of supply


[068-052]and demand will then have a much larger meaning.


[068-053]In nature everything lives upon something else.  Life feeds upon


[068-054]life.  Something is lying in wait for something else, and even the


[068-055]victim is weaving a web or crouching for some other victim, and


[068-056]the other victim is in the same business--watching for something


[068-057]else.  The same is true in the human world--people are living on


[068-058]each other; the cunning obtain the property of the simple; wealth


[068-059]picks the pockets of poverty; success is a highwayman leaping from


[068-060]the hedge.  The rich combine, the poor are unorganized, without


[068-061]the means to act in concert, and for that reason become the prey


[068-062]of combinations and trusts.  The great questions are:  Will man


[068-063]ever be sufficiently civilized to be honest?  Will the time ever


[068-064]come when it can truthfully be said that right is might?  The lives


[068-065]of millions of people are not worth living, because of their


[068-066]ignorance and poverty, and the lives of millions of others are not


[068-067]worth living, on account of their wealth and selfishness.  The


[068-068]palace without justice, without charity, is as terrible as the


[068-069]hovel without food.


[068-070]_Question_.  What effect has the woman's suffrage movement had on


[068-071]the breadwinners of the country?


[068-072]_Answer_.  I think the women who have been engaged in the struggle


[068-073]for equal rights have done good for women in the direction of


[068-074]obtaining equal wages for equal work.  There has also been for many


[068-075]years a tendency among women in our country to become independent


[068-076]--a desire to make their own living--to win their own bread.  So


[068-077]many husbands are utterly useless, or worse, that many women hardly


[068-078]feel justified in depending entirely on a husband for the future.


[068-079]They feel somewhat safer to know how to do something and earn a


[068-080]little money themselves.  If men were what they ought to be, few


[068-081]women would be allowed to labor--that is to say, to toil.  It should


[068-082]be the ambition of every healthy and intelligent man to take care


[068-083]of, to support, to make happy, some woman.  As long as women bear


[068-084]the burdens of the world, the human race can never attain anything


[068-085]like a splendid civilization.  There will be no great generation


[068-086]of men until there has been a great generation of women.  For my


[068-087]part, I am glad to hear this question discussed--glad to know that


[068-088]thousands of women take some interest in the fortunes and in the


[068-089]misfortunes of their sisters.


[068-090]The question of wages for women is a thousand times more important


[068-091]than sending missionaries to China or to India.  There is plenty


[068-092]for missionaries to do here.  And by missionaries I do not mean


[068-093]gentlemen and ladies who distribute tracts or quote Scripture to


[068-094]people out of work.  If we are to better the condition of men and


[068-095]women we must change their surroundings.  The tenement house breeds


[068-096]a moral pestilence.  There can be in these houses no home, no


[068-097]fireside, no family, for the reason that there is no privacy, no


[068-098]walls between them and the rest of the world.  There is no sacredness,


[068-099]no feeling, "this is ours."


[068-100]_Question_.  Might not the rich do much?


[068-101]_Answer_.  It would be hard to overestimate the good that might be


[068-102]done by the millionaires if they would turn their attention to


[068-103]sending thousands and thousands into the country or to building


[068-104]them homes miles from the city, where they could have something


[068-105]like privacy, where the family relations could be kept with some


[068-106]sacredness.  Think of the "homes" in which thousands and thousands


[068-107]of young girls are reared in our large cities.  Think of what they


[068-108]see and what they hear; of what they come in contact with.  How is


[068-109]it possible for the virtues to grow in the damp and darkened


[068-110]basements?  Can we expect that love and chastity and all that is


[068-111]sweet and gentle will be produced in these surroundings, in cellars


[068-112]and garrets, in poverty and dirt?  The surroundings must be changed.


[068-113]_Question_.  Are the fathers and brothers blameless who allow young


[068-114]girls to make coats, cloaks and vests in an atmosphere poisoned by


[068-115]the ignorant and low-bred?


[068-116]_Answer_.  The same causes now brutalizing girls brutalize their


[068-117]fathers and brothers, and the same causes brutalize the ignorant


[068-118]and low-lived that poison the air in which these girls are made to


[068-119]work.  It is hard to pick out one man and say that he is to blame,


[068-120]or one woman and say that the fault is hers.  We must go back of


[068-121]all this.  In my opinion, society raises its own failures, its own


[068-122]criminals, its own wretches of every sort and kind.  Great pains


[068-123]are taken to raise these crops.  The seeds, it may be, were sown


[068-124]thousands of years ago, but they were sown, and the present is the


[068-125]necessary child of all the past.  If the future is to differ from


[068-126]the present, the seeds must now be sown.  It is not simply a question


[068-127]of charity, or a question of good nature, or a question of what we


[068-128]call justice--it is a question of intelligence.  In the first place,


[068-129]I suppose that it is the duty of every human being to support


[068-130]himself--first, that he may not become a burden upon others, and


[068-131]second, that he may help others.  I think all people should be


[068-132]taught never, under any circumstances, if by any possibility they


[068-133]can avoid it, to become a burden.  Every one should be taught the


[068-134]nobility of labor, the heroism and splendor of honest effort.  As


[068-135]long as it is considered disgraceful to labor, or aristocratic not


[068-136]to labor, the world will be filled with idleness and crime, and


[068-137]with every possible moral deformity.


[068-138]_Question_.  Has the public school system anything to do with the


[068-139]army of pupils who, after six years of study, willingly accept the


[068-140]injustice and hardship imposed by capital?


[068-141]_Answer_.  The great trouble with the public school is that many


[068-142]things are taught that are of no immediate use.  I believe in manual


[068-143]training schools.  I believe in the kindergarten system.  Every


[068-144]person ought to be taught how to do something--ought to be taught


[068-145]the use of their hands.  They should endeavor to put in palpable


[068-146]form the ideas that they gain.  Such an education gives them a


[068-147]confidence in themselves, a confidence in the future--gives them


[068-148]a spirit and feeling of independence that they do not now have.


[068-149]Men go through college studying for many years, and when graduated


[068-150]have not the slightest conception of how to make a living in any


[068-151]department of human effort.  Thousands of them are to-day doing


[068-152]manual labor and doing it very poorly, whereas, if they had been


[068-153]taught the use of tools, the use of their hands, they would derive


[068-154]a certain pleasure from their work.  It is splendid to do anything


[068-155]well.  One can be just as poetic working with iron and wood as


[068-156]working with words and colors.


[068-157]_Question_.  What ought to be done, or what is to be the end?


[068-158]_Answer_.  The great thing is for the people to know the facts.


[068-159]There are thousands and millions of splendid and sympathetic people


[068-160]who would willingly help, if they only knew; but they go through


[068-161]the world in such a way that they know but little of it.  They go


[068-162]to their place of business; they stay in their offices for a few


[068-163]hours; they go home; they spend the evening there or at a club;


[068-164]they come in contact with the well-to-do, with the successful, with


[068-165]the satisfied, and they know nothing of the thousands and millions


[068-166]on every side.  They have not the least idea how the world lives,


[068-167]how it works, how it suffers.  They read, of course, now and then,


[068-168]some paragraph in which the misfortune of some wretch is set forth,


[068-169]but the wretch is a kind of steel engraving, an unreal shadow, a


[068-170]something utterly unlike themselves.  The real facts should be


[068-171]brought home, the sympathies of men awakened, and awakened to such


[068-172]a degree that they will go and see how these people live, see how


[068-173]they work, see how they suffer.


[068-174]_Question_.  Does exposure do any good?


[068-175]_Answer_.  I hope that _The World_ will keep on.  I hope that it


[068-176]will express every horror that it can, connected with the robbery


[068-177]of poor and helpless girls, and I hope that it will publish the


[068-178]names of all the robbers it can find, and the wretches who oppress


[068-179]the poor and who live upon the misfortunes of women.


[068-180]The crosses of this world are mostly born by wives, by mothers and


[068-181]by daughters.  Their brows are pierced by thorns.  They shed the


[068-182]bitterest tears.  They live and suffer and die for others.  It is


[068-183]almost enough to make one insane to think of what woman, in the


[068-184]years of savagery and civilization, has suffered.  Think of the


[068-185]anxiety and agony of motherhood.  Maternity is the most pathetic


[068-186]fact in the universe.  Think how helpless girls are.  Think of the


[068-187]thorns in the paths they walk--of the trials, the temptations, the


[068-188]want, the misfortune, the dangers and anxieties that fill their


[068-189]days and nights.  Every true man will sympathize with woman, and


[068-190]will do all in his power to lighten her burdens and increase the


[068-191]sunshine of her life.
