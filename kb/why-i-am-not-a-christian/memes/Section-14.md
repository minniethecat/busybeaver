[013-001]_Question_.  Shall you sue the Opera House management for breach


[013-002]of contract?


[013-003]_Answer_.  I guess not; but I may pay Lansing something for


[013-004]advertising my lecture.  I suppose Mr. Wilcox (who controls the


[013-005]Opera House) did what he thought was right.  I hear he is a good


[013-006]man.  He probably got a little frightened and began to think about


[013-007]the day of judgment.  He could not help it, and I cannot help


[013-008]laughing at him.


[013-009]_Question_.  Those in Meriden who most strongly oppose you are


[013-010]radical Republicans.  Is it not a fact that you possess the confidence


[013-011]and friendship of some of the most respected leaders of that party?


[013-012]_Answer_.  I think that all the respectable ones are friends of


[013-013]mine.  I am a Republican because I believe in the liberty of the


[013-014]body, and I am an Infidel because I believe in the liberty of the


[013-015]mind.  There is no need of freeing cages.  Let us free the birds.


[013-016]If Mr. Lansing knew me, he would be a great friend.  He would


[013-017]probably annoy me by the frequency and length of his visits.


[013-018]_Question_.  During the recent presidential campaign did any


[013-019]clergymen denounce you for your teachings, that you are aware of?


[013-020]_Answer_.  Some did, but they would not if they had been running


[013-021]for office on the Republican ticket.


[013-022]_Question_.  What is most needed in our public men?


[013-023]_Answer_.  Hearts and brains.


[013-024]_Question_.  Would people be any more moral solely because of a


[013-025]disbelief in orthodox teaching and in the Bible as an inspired


[013-026]book, in your opinion?


[013-027]_Answer_.  Yes; if a man really believes that God once upheld


[013-028]slavery; that he commanded soldiers to kill women and babes; that


[013-029]he believed in polygamy; that he persecuted for opinion's sake;


[013-030]that he will punish forever, and that he hates an unbeliever, the


[013-031]effect in my judgment will be bad.  It always has been bad.  This


[013-032]belief built the dungeons of the Inquisition.  This belief made


[013-033]the Puritan murder the Quaker, and this belief has raised the devil


[013-034]with Mr. Lansing.


[013-035]_Question_.  Do you believe there will ever be a millennium, and


[013-036]if so how will it come about?


[013-037]_Answer_.  It will probably start in Meriden, as I have been informed


[013-038]that Lansing is going to leave.


[013-039]_Question_.  Is there anything else bearing upon the question at


[013-040]issue or that would make good reading, that I have forgotten, that


[013-041]you would like to say?


[013-042]_Answer_.  Yes.  Good-bye.


[013-043]--_The Sunday Union_, New Haven, Conn., April 10, 1881.


[013-044]BEACONSFIELD, LENT AND REVIVALS.


[013-045]_Question_.  What have you to say about the attack of Dr. Buckley


[013-046]on you, and your lecture?


[013-047]_Answer_.  I never heard of Dr. Buckley until after I had lectured


[013-048]in Brooklyn.  He seems to think that it was extremely ill bred in


[013-049]me to deliver a lecture on the "Liberty of Man, Woman and Child,"


[013-050]during Lent.  Lent is just as good as any other part of the year,


[013-051]and no part can be too good to do good.  It was not a part of my


[013-052]object to hurt the feelings of the Episcopalians and Catholics.


[013-053]If they think that there is some subtle relation between hunger


[013-054]and heaven, or that faith depends upon, or is strengthened by


[013-055]famine, or that veal, during Lent, is the enemy of virtue, or that


[013-056]beef breeds blasphemy, while fish feeds faith--of course, all this


[013-057]is nothing to me.  They have a right to say that vice depends upon


[013-058]victuals, sanctity on soup, religion on rice and chastity on cheese,


[013-059]but they have no right to say that a lecture on liberty is an insult


[013-060]to them because they are hungry.  I suppose that Lent was instituted


[013-061]in memory of the Savior's fast.  At one time it was supposed that


[013-062]only a divine being could live forty days without food.  This


[013-063]supposition has been overthrown.


[013-064]It has been demonstrated by Dr. Tanner to be utterly without


[013-065]foundation.  What possible good did it do the world for Christ to


[013-066]go without food for forty days?  Why should we follow such an


[013-067]example?  As a rule, hungry people are cross, contrary, obstinate,


[013-068]peevish and unpleasant.  A good dinner puts a man at peace with


[013-069]all the world--makes him generous, good natured and happy.  He


[013-070]feels like kissing his wife and children.  The future looks bright.


[013-071]He wants to help the needy.  The good in him predominates, and he


[013-072]wonders that any man was ever stingy or cruel.  Your good cook is


[013-073]a civilizer, and without good food, well prepared, intellectual


[013-074]progress is simply impossible.  Most of the orthodox creeds were


[013-075]born of bad cooking.  Bad food produced dyspepsia, and dyspepsia


[013-076]produced Calvinism, and Calvinism is the cancer of Christianity.


[013-077]Oatmeal is responsible for the worst features of Scotch Presbyterianism.


[013-078]Half cooked beans account for the religion of the Puritans.  Fried


[013-079]bacon and saleratus biscuit underlie the doctrine of State Rights.


[013-080]Lent is a mistake, fasting is a blunder, and bad cooking is a crime.


[013-081]_Question_.  It is stated that you went to Brooklyn while Beecher


[013-082]and Talmage were holding revivals, and that you did so for the


[013-083]purpose of breaking them up.  How is this?


[013-084]_Answer_.  I had not the slightest idea of interfering with the


[013-085]revivals.  They amounted to nothing.  They were not alive enough


[013-086]to be killed.  Surely one lecture could not destroy two revivals.


[013-087]Still, I think that if all the persons engaged in the revivals had


[013-088]spent the same length of time in cleaning the streets, the good


[013-089]result would have been more apparent.  The truth is, that the old


[013-090]way of converting people will have to be abandoned.  The Americans


[013-091]are getting hard to scare, and a revival without the "scare" is


[013-092]scarcely worth holding.  Such maniacs as Hammond and the "Boy


[013-093]Preacher" fill asylums and terrify children.  After saying what he


[013-094]has about hell, Mr. Beecher ought to know that he is not the man


[013-095]to conduct a revival.  A revival sermon with hell left out--with


[013-096]the brimstone gone--with the worm that never dies, dead, and the


[013-097]Devil absent--is the broadest farce.  Mr. Talmage believes in the


[013-098]ancient way.  With him hell is a burning reality.  He can hear the


[013-099]shrieks and groans.  He is of that order of mind that rejoices in


[013-100]these things.  If he could only convince others, he would be a


[013-101]great revivalist.  He cannot terrify, he astonishes.  He is the


[013-102]clown of the horrible--one of Jehovah's jesters.  I am not responsible


[013-103]for the revival failure in Brooklyn.  I wish I were.  I would have


[013-104]the happiness of knowing that I had been instrumental in preserving


[013-105]the sanity of my fellow-men.


[013-106]_Question_.  How do you account for these attacks?


[013-107]_Answer_.  It was not so much what I said that excited the wrath


[013-108]of the reverend gentlemen as the fact that I had a great house.


[013-109]They contrasted their failure with my success.  The fact is, the


[013-110]people are getting tired of the old ideas.  They are beginning to


[013-111]think for themselves.  Eternal punishment seems to them like eternal


[013-112]revenge.  They see that Christ could not atone for the sins of


[013-113]others; that belief ought not to be rewarded and honest doubt


[013-114]punished forever; that good deeds are better than bad creeds, and


[013-115]that liberty is the rightful heritage of every soul.


[013-116]_Question_.  Were you an admirer of Lord Beaconsfield?


[013-117]_Answer_.  In some respects.  He was on our side during the war,


[013-118]and gave it as his opinion that the Union would be preserved.  Mr.


[013-119]Gladstone congratulated Jefferson Davis on having founded a new


[013-120]nation.  I shall never forget Beaconsfield for his kindness, nor


[013-121]Gladstone for his malice.  Beaconsfield was an intellectual gymnast,


[013-122]a political athlete, one of the most adroit men in the world.  He


[013-123]had the persistence of his race.  In spite of the prejudices of


[013-124]eighteen hundred years, he rose to the highest position that can


[013-125]be occupied by a citizen.  During his administration England again


[013-126]became a Continental power and played her game of European chess.


[013-127]I have never regarded Beaconsfield as a man controlled by principle,


[013-128]or by his heart.  He was strictly a politician.  He always acted


[013-129]as though he thought the clubs were looking at him.  He knew all


[013-130]the arts belonging to his trade.  He would have succeeded anywhere,


[013-131]if by "succeeding" is meant the attainment of position and power.


[013-132]But after all, such men are splendid failures.  They give themselves


[013-133]and others a great deal of trouble--they wear the tinsel crown of


[013-134]temporary success and then fade from public view.  They astonish


[013-135]the pit, they gain the applause of the galleries, but when the


[013-136]curtain falls there is nothing left to benefit mankind.  Beaconsfield


[013-137]held convictions somewhat in contempt.  He had the imagination of


[013-138]the East united with the ambition of an Englishman.  With him, to


[013-139]succeed was to have done right.


[013-140]_Question_.  What do you think of him as an author?


[013-141]_Answer_.  Most of his characters are like himself--puppets moved


[013-142]by the string of self-interest.  The men are adroit, the women


[013-143]mostly heartless.  They catch each other with false bait.  They


[013-144]have great worldly wisdom.  Their virtue and vice are mechanical.


[013-145]They have hearts like clocks--filled with wheels and springs.  The


[013-146]author winds them up.  In his novels Disraeli allows us to enter


[013-147]the greenroom of his heart.  We see the ropes, the pulleys and the


[013-148]old masks.  In all things, in politics and in literature, he was


[013-149]cold, cunning, accurate, able and successful.  His books will, in


[013-150]a little while, follow their author to their grave.  After all,


[013-151]the good will live longest.


[013-152]--Washington correspondent, _Brooklyn Eagle_, April 24, 1881.


[013-153]ANSWERING THE NEW YORK MINISTERS.*


[013-154][* Ever since Colonel Ingersoll began the delivery of his


[013-155]lecture called _The Great Infidels_, the ministers of the


[013-156]country have made him the subject of special attack.  One


[013-157]week ago last Sunday the majority of the leading ministers


[013-158]in New York made replies to Ingersoll's latest lecture.


[013-159]What he has to say to these replies will be found in a


[013-160]report of an interview with Colonel Ingersoll.


[013-161]No man is harder to pin down for a long talk than the


[013-162]Colonel.  He is so beset with visitors and eager office


[013-163]seekers anxious for help, that he can hardly find five


[013-164]minutes unoccupied during an entire day. Through the shelter


[013-165]of a private room and the guardianship of a stout colored


[013-166]servant, the Colonel was able to escape the crowd of seekers


[013-167]after his personal charity long enough to give some time to


[013-168]answer some of the ministerial arguments advanced against


[013-169]him in New York.]


[013-170]_Question_.  Have you seen the attacks made upon you by certain


[013-171]ministers of New York, published in the _Herald_ last Sunday?


[013-172]_Answer_.  Yes, I read, or heard read, what was in Monday's _Herald_.


[013-173]I do not know that you could hardly call them attacks.  They are


[013-174]substantially a repetition of what the pulpit has been saying for


[013-175]a great many hundred years, and what the pulpit will say just so


[013-176]long as men are paid for suppressing truth and for defending


[013-177]superstition.  One of these gentlemen tells the lambs of his flock


[013-178]that three thousand men and a few women--probably with quite an


[013-179]emphasis on the word "Few"--gave one dollar each to hear their


[013-180]Maker cursed and their Savior ridiculed.  Probably nothing is so


[013-181]hard for the average preacher to bear as the fact that people are


[013-182]not only willing to hear the other side, but absolutely anxious to


[013-183]pay for it.  The dollar that these people paid hurt their feelings


[013-184]vastly more than what was said after they were in.  Of course, it


[013-185]is a frightful commentary on the average intellect of the pulpit


[013-186]that a minister cannot get so large an audience when he preaches


[013-187]for nothing, as an Infidel can draw at a dollar a head.  If I


[013-188]depended upon a contribution box, or upon passing a saucer that


[013-189]would come back to the stage enriched with a few five cent pieces,


[013-190]eight or ten dimes, and a lonesome quarter, these gentlemen would,


[013-191]in all probability, imagine Infidelity was not to be feared.
