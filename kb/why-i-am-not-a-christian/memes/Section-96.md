[095-001]To feel that I have given as much joy as the circus fills me with


[095-002]pleasure.  What chance would the Rev. Dr. Banks stand against a


[095-003]circus?


[095-004]The reverend gentleman has done me a great honor, and I tender him


[095-005]my sincere thanks.


[095-006]_Question_.  Dr. Banks says that you write only one lecture a year,


[095-007]while preachers write a brand new one every week--that if you did


[095-008]that people would tire of you.  What have you to say to that?


[095-009]_Answer_.  It may be that great artists paint only one picture a


[095-010]year, and it may be that sign painters can do several jobs a day.


[095-011]Still, I would not say that the sign painters were superior to the


[095-012]artists.  There is quite a difference between a sculptor and a


[095-013]stone-cutter.


[095-014]There are thousands of preachers and thousands and thousands of


[095-015]sermons preached every year.  Has any orthodox minister in the year


[095-016]1898 given just one paragraph to literature?  Has any orthodox


[095-017]preacher uttered one great thought, clothed in perfect English that


[095-018]thrilled the hearers like music--one great strophe that became one


[095-019]of the treasures of memory?


[095-020]I will make the question a little clearer.  Has any orthodox


[095-021]preacher, or any preacher in an orthodox pulpit uttered a paragraph


[095-022]of what may be called sculptured speech since Henry Ward Beecher


[095-023]died?  I do not wonder that the sermons are poor.  Their doctrines


[095-024]have been discussed for centuries.  There is little chance for


[095-025]originality; they not only thresh old straw, but the thresh straw


[095-026]that has been threshed a million times--straw in which there has


[095-027]not been a grain of wheat for hundreds of years.  No wonder that


[095-028]they have nervous prostration.  No wonder that they need vacations,


[095-029]and no wonder that their congregations enjoy the vacations as keenly


[095-030]as the ministers themselves.  Better deliver a real good address


[095-031]fifty-two times than fifty-two poor ones--just for the sake of


[095-032]variety.


[095-033]_Question_.  Dr. Banks says that the tendency at present is not


[095-034]toward Agnosticism, but toward Christianity.  What is your opinion?


[095-035]_Answer_.  When I was a boy "Infidels" were very rare.  A man who


[095-036]denied the inspiration of the Bible was regarded as a monster.


[095-037]Now there are in this country millions who regard the Bible as the


[095-038]work of ignorant and superstitious men.  A few years ago the Bible


[095-039]was the standard.  All scientific theories were tested by the Bible.


[095-040]Now science is the standard and the Bible is tested by that.


[095-041]Dr. Banks did not mention the names of the great scientists who


[095-042]are or were Christians, but he probably thought of Laplace, Humboldt,


[095-043]Haeckel, Huxley, Spencer, Tyndall, Darwin, Helmholtz and Draper.


[095-044]When he spoke of Christian statesmen he likely thought of Jefferson,


[095-045]Franklin, Washington, Paine and Lincoln--or he may have thought of


[095-046]Pierce, Fillmore and Buchanan.


[095-047]But, after all, there is no argument in names.  A man is not


[095-048]necessarily great because he holds office or wears a crown or talks


[095-049]in a pulpit.  Facts, reasons, are better than names.  But it seems


[095-050]to me that nothing can be plainer than that the church is losing


[095-051]ground--that the people are discarding the creeds and that superstition


[095-052]has passed the zenith of its power.


[095-053]_Question_.  Dr. Banks says that Christ did not mention the Western


[095-054]Hemisphere because God does nothing for men that they can do for


[095-055]themselves.  What have you to say?


[095-056]_Answer_.  Christ said nothing about the Western Hemisphere because


[095-057]he did not know that it existed.  He did not know the shape of the


[095-058]earth.  He was not a scientist--never even hinted at any science--


[095-059]never told anybody to investigate--to think.  His idea was that


[095-060]this life should be spent in preparing for the next.  For all the


[095-061]evils of this life, and the next, faith was his remedy.


[095-062]I see from the report in the paper that Dr. Banks, after making


[095-063]the remarks about me preached a sermon on "Herod the Villain in


[095-064]the Drama of Christ."  Who made Herod?  Dr. Banks will answer that


[095-065]God made him.  Did God know what Herod would do?  Yes.  Did he know


[095-066]that he would cause the children to be slaughtered in his vain


[095-067]efforts to kill the infant Christ?  Yes.  Dr. Banks will say that


[095-068]God is not responsible for Herod because he gave Herod freedom.


[095-069]Did God know how Herod would use his freedom?  Did he know that he


[095-070]would become the villain in the drama of Christ?  Yes.  Who, then,


[095-071]is really responsible for the acts of Herod?


[095-072]If I could change a stone into a human being, and if I could give


[095-073]this being freedom of will, and if I knew that if I made him he


[095-074]would murder a man, and if with that knowledge I made him, and he


[095-075]did commit a murder, who would be the real murderer?


[095-076]Will Dr. Banks in his fifty-two sermons of next year show that his


[095-077]God is not responsible for the crimes of Herod?


[095-078]No doubt Dr. Banks is a good man, and no doubt he thinks that


[095-079]liberty of thought leads to hell, and honestly believes that all


[095-080]doubt comes from the Devil.  I do not blame him.  He thinks as he


[095-081]must.  He is a product of conditions.


[095-082]He ought to be my friend because I am doing the best I can to


[095-083]civilize his congregation.


[095-084]--_The Plain Dealer_, Cleveland, Ohio, 1898.


[095-085]CUBA--ZOLA AND THEOSOPHY.


[095-086]_Question_.  What do you think, Colonel, of the Cuban question?


[095-087]_Answer_.  What I know about this question is known by all.  I


[095-088]suppose that the President has information that I know nothing


[095-089]about.  Of course, all my sympathies are with the Cubans.  They


[095-090]are making a desperate--an heroic struggle for their freedom.  For


[095-091]many years they have been robbed and trampled under foot.  Spain


[095-092]is, and always has been, a terrible master--heartless and infamous.


[095-093]There is no language with which to tell what Cuba has suffered.


[095-094]In my judgment, this country should assist the Cubans.  We ought


[095-095]to acknowledge the independence of that island, and we ought to


[095-096]feed the starving victims of Spain.  For years we have been helping


[095-097]Spain.  Cleveland did all he could to prevent the Cubans from


[095-098]getting arms and men.  This was a criminal mistake--a mistake that


[095-099]even Spain did not appreciate.  All this should instantly be


[095-100]reversed, and we should give aid to Cuba.  The war that Spain is


[095-101]waging shocks every civilized man.  Spain has always been the same.


[095-102]In Holland, in Peru, in Mexico, she was infinitely cruel, and she


[095-103]is the same to-day.  She loves to torture, to imprison, to degrade,


[095-104]to kill.  Her idea of perfect happiness is to shed blood.  Spain


[095-105]is a legacy of the Dark Ages.  She belongs to the den, the cave


[095-106]period.  She has no business to exist.  She is a blot, a stain on


[095-107]the map of the world.  Of course there are some good Spaniards,


[095-108]but they are not in control.


[095-109]I want Cuba to be free.  I want Spain driven from the Western World.


[095-110]She has already starved five hundred thousand Cubans--poor, helpless


[095-111]non-combatants.  Among the helpless she is like a hyena--a tiger


[095-112]among lambs.  This country ought to stop this gigantic crime.  We


[095-113]should do this in the name of humanity--for the sake of the starving,


[095-114]the dying.


[095-115]_Question_.  Do you think we are going to have war with Spain?


[095-116]_Answer_.  I do not think there will be war.  Unless Spain is


[095-117]insane, she will not attack the United States.  She is bankrupt.


[095-118]No nation will assist her.  A civilized nation would be ashamed to


[095-119]take her hand, to be her friend.  She has not the power to put down


[095-120]the rebellion in Cuba.  How then can she hope to conquer this


[095-121]country?  She is full of brag and bluster.  Of course she will play


[095-122]her hand for all it is worth, so far as talk goes.  She will double


[095-123]her fists and make motions.  She will assume the attitude of war,


[095-124]but she will never fight.  Should she commence hostilities, the


[095-125]war would be short.  She would lose her navy.  The little commerce


[095-126]she has would be driven from the sea.  She would drink to the dregs


[095-127]the cup of humiliation and disgrace.  I do not believe that Spain


[095-128]is insane enough to fire upon our flag.  I know that there is


[095-129]nothing too mean, too cruel for her to do, but still she must have


[095-130]sense enough to try and save her own life.  No, I think there will


[095-131]be no war, but I believe that Cuba will be free.  My opinion is


[095-132]that the Maine was blown up from the outside--blown up by Spanish


[095-133]officers, and I think the report of the Board will be to that


[095-134]effect.  Such a crime ought to redden even the cheeks of Spain.


[095-135]As soon as this fact is known, other nations will regard Spain with


[095-136]hatred and horror.  If the Maine was destroyed by Spain we will


[095-137]ask for indemnity.  The people insist that the account be settled


[095-138]and at once.  Possibly we may attack Spain.  There is the only


[095-139]danger of war.  We must avenge that crime.  The destruction of two


[095-140]hundred and fifty-nine Americans must be avenged.  Free Cuba must


[095-141]be their monument.  I hope for the sake of human nature that the


[095-142]Spanish did not destroy the Maine.  I hope it was the result of an


[095-143]accident.  I hope there is to be no war, but Spain must be driven


[095-144]from the New World.


[095-145]_Question_.  What about Zola's trial and conviction?


[095-146]_Answer_.  It was one of the most infamous trials in the history


[095-147]of the world.  Zola is a great man, a genius, the best man in


[095-148]France.  His trial was a travesty on justice.  The judge acted like


[095-149]a bandit.  The proceedings were a disgrace to human nature.  The


[095-150]jurors must have been ignorant beasts.  The French have disgraced


[095-151]themselves.  Long live Zola.


[095-152]_Question_.  Having expressed yourself less upon the subject of


[095-153]Theosophy than upon other religious beliefs, and as Theosophy denies


[095-154]the existence of a God as worshiped by Christianity, what is your


[095-155]idea of the creed?


[095-156]_Answer_.  Insanity.  I think it is a mild form of delusion and


[095-157]illusion; vague, misty, obscure, half dream, mixed with other


[095-158]mistakes and fragments of facts--a little philosophy, absurdity--


[095-159]a few impossibilities--some improbabilities--some accounts of events


[095-160]that never happened--some prophecies that will not come to pass--


[095-161]a structure without foundation.  But the Theosophists are good


[095-162]people; kind and honest.  Theosophy is based on the supernatural


[095-163]and is just as absurd as the orthodox creeds.


[095-164]--_The Courier-Journal_, Louisville, Ky., February, 1898.


[095-165]HOW TO BECOME AN ORATOR.


[095-166]_Question_.  What advice would you give to a young man who was


[095-167]ambitious to become a successful public speaker or orator?


[095-168]_Answer_.  In the first place, I would advise him to have something


[095-169]to say--something worth saying--something that people would be glad


[095-170]to hear.  This is the important thing.  Back of the art of speaking


[095-171]must be the power to think.  Without thoughts words are empty


[095-172]purses.  Most people imagine that almost any words uttered in a


[095-173]loud voice and accompanied by appropriate gestures, constitute an


[095-174]oration.  I would advise the young man to study his subject, to


[095-175]find what others had thought, to look at it from all sides.  Then


[095-176]I would tell him to write out his thoughts or to arrange them in


[095-177]his mind, so that he would know exactly what he was going to say.


[095-178]Waste no time on the how until you are satisfied with the what.


[095-179]After you know what you are to say, then you can think of how it


[095-180]should be said.  Then you can think about tone, emphasis, and


[095-181]gesture; but if you really understand what you say, emphasis, tone,


[095-182]and gesture will take care of themselves.  All these should come


[095-183]from the inside.  They should be in perfect harmony with the


[095-184]feelings.  Voice and gesture should be governed by the emotions.


[095-185]They should unconsciously be in perfect agreement with the sentiments.


[095-186]The orator should be true to his subject, should avoid any reference


[095-187]to himself.
