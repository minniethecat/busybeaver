[002-001]_Question_.  If the ordinance exempts scientific, literary and


[002-002]historical lectures, as it is said it does, will not that exempt


[002-003]you?


[002-004]_Answer_.  Yes, all my lectures are historical; that is, I speak


[002-005]of many things that have happened.  They are scientific because


[002-006]they are filled with facts, and they are literary of course.  I


[002-007]can conceive of no address that is neither historical nor scientific,


[002-008]except sermons.  They fail to be historical because they treat of


[002-009]things that never happened and they are certainly not scientific,


[002-010]as they contain no facts.


[002-011]_Question_.  Suppose they arrest you what will you do?


[002-012]_Answer_.  I will examine the law and if convicted will pay the


[002-013]fine, unless I think I can reverse the case by appeal.  Of course


[002-014]I would like to see all these foolish laws wiped from the statute


[002-015]books.  I want the law so that everybody can do just as he pleases


[002-016]on Sunday, provided he does not interfere with the rights of others.


[002-017]I want the Christian, the Jew, the Deist and the Atheist to be


[002-018]exactly equal before the law.  I would fight for the right of the


[002-019]Christian to worship God in his own way just as quick as I would


[002-020]for the Atheist to enjoy music, flowers and fields.  I hope to see


[002-021]the time when even the poor people can hear the music of the finest


[002-022]operas on Sunday.  One grand opera with all its thrilling tones,


[002-023]will do more good in touching and elevating the world than ten


[002-024]thousand sermons on the agonies of hell.


[002-025]_Question_.  Have you ever been interfered with before in delivering


[002-026]Sunday lectures?


[002-027]_Answer_.  No, I postponed a lecture in Baltimore at the request


[002-028]of the owners of a theatre because they were afraid some action


[002-029]might be taken.  That is the only case.  I have delivered lectures


[002-030]on Sunday in the principal cities of the United States, in New


[002-031]York, Boston, Buffalo, Chicago, San Francisco, Cincinnati and many


[002-032]other places.  I lectured here last winter; it was on Sunday and


[002-033]I heard nothing of its being contrary to law.  I always supposed


[002-034]my lectures were good enough to be delivered on the most sacred


[002-035]days.


[002-036]--_The Leader_, Pittsburg, Pa., October 27, 1879.


[002-037][* The manager of the theatre, where Col. Ingersoll


[002-038]lectured, was fined fifty dollars which Col. Ingersoll


[002-039]paid.]


[002-040]POLITICAL AND RELIGIOUS.


[002-041]_Question_.  What do you think about the recent election, and what


[002-042]will be its effect upon political matters and the issues and


[002-043]candidates of 1880?


[002-044]_Answer_.  I think the Republicans have met with this almost


[002-045]universal success on account, first, of the position taken by the


[002-046]Democracy on the currency question; that is to say, that party was


[002-047]divided, and was willing to go in partnership with anybody, whatever


[002-048]their doctrines might be, for the sake of success in that particular


[002-049]locality.  The Republican party felt it of paramount importance


[002-050]not only to pay the debt, but to pay it in that which the world


[002-051]regards as money.  The next reason for the victory is the position


[002-052]assumed by the Democracy in Congress during the called session.


[002-053]The threats they then made of what they would do in the event that


[002-054]the executive did not comply with their demands, showed that the


[002-055]spirit of the party had not been chastened to any considerable


[002-056]extent by the late war.  The people of this country will not, in


[002-057]my judgment, allow the South to take charge of this country until


[002-058]they show their ability to protect the rights of citizens in their


[002-059]respective States.


[002-060]_Question_.  Then, as you regard the victories, they are largely


[002-061]due to a firm adherence to principle, and the failure of the


[002-062]Democratic party is due to their abandonment of principle, and


[002-063]their desire to unite with anybody and everything, at the sacrifice


[002-064]of principle, to attain success?


[002-065]_Answer_.  Yes.  The Democratic party is a general desire for office


[002-066]without organization.  Most people are Democrats because they hate


[002-067]something, most people are Republicans because they love something.


[002-068]_Question_.  Do you think the election has brought about any


[002-069]particular change in the issues that will be involved in the campaign


[002-070]of 1880?


[002-071]_Answer_.  I think the only issue is who shall rule the country.


[002-072]_Question_.  Do you think, then, the question of State Rights, hard


[002-073]or soft money and other questions that have been prominent in the


[002-074]campaign are practically settled, and so regarded by the people?


[002-075]_Answer_.  I think the money question is, absolutely.  I think the


[002-076]question of State Rights is dead, except that it can still be used


[002-077]to defeat the Democracy.  It is what might be called a convenient


[002-078]political corpse.


[002-079]_Question_.  Now, to leave the political field and go to the


[002-080]religious at one jump--since your last visit here much has been


[002-081]said and written and published to the effect that a great change,


[002-082]or a considerable change at least, had taken place in your religious,


[002-083]or irreligious views.  I would like to know if that is so?


[002-084]_Answer_.  The only change that has occurred in my religious views


[002-085]is the result of finding more and more arguments in favor of my


[002-086]position, and, as a consequence, if there is any difference, I am


[002-087]stronger in my convictions than ever before.


[002-088]_Question_.  I would like to know something of the history of your


[002-089]religious views?


[002-090]_Answer_.  I may say right here that the Christian idea that any


[002-091]God can make me his friend by killing mine is about a great mistake


[002-092]as could be made.  They seem to have the idea that just as soon as


[002-093]God kills all the people that a person loves, he will then begin


[002-094]to love the Lord.  What drew my attention first to these questions


[002-095]was the doctrine of eternal punishment.  This was so abhorrent to


[002-096]my mind that I began to hate the book in which it was taught.


[002-097]Then, in reading law, going back to find the origin of laws, I


[002-098]found one had to go but a little way before the legislator and


[002-099]priest united.  This led me to a study of a good many of the


[002-100]religions of the world.  At first I was greatly astonished to find


[002-101]most of them better than ours.  I then studied our own system to


[002-102]the best of my ability, and found that people were palming off upon


[002-103]children and upon one another as the inspired word of God a book


[002-104]that upheld slavery, polygamy and almost every other crime.  Whether


[002-105]I am right or wrong, I became convinced that the Bible is not an


[002-106]inspired book; and then the only question for me to settle was as


[002-107]to whether I should say what I believed or not.  This really was


[002-108]not the question in my mind, because, before even thinking of such


[002-109]a question, I expressed my belief, and I simply claim that right


[002-110]and expect to exercise it as long as I live.  I may be damned for


[002-111]it in the next world, but it is a great source of pleasure to me


[002-112]in this.


[002-113]_Question_.  It is reported that you are the son of a Presbyterian


[002-114]minister?


[002-115]_Answer_.  Yes, I am the son of a New School Presbyterian minister.


[002-116]_Question_.  About what age were you when you began this investigation


[002-117]which led to your present convictions?


[002-118]_Answer_.  I cannot remember when I believed the Bible doctrine of


[002-119]eternal punishment.  I have a dim recollection of hating Jehovah


[002-120]when I was exceedingly small.


[002-121]_Question_.  Then your present convictions began to form themselves


[002-122]while you were listening to the teachings of religion as taught by


[002-123]your father?


[002-124]_Answer_.  Yes, they did.


[002-125]_Question_.  Did you discuss the matter with him?


[002-126]_Answer_.  I did for many years, and before he died he utterly gave


[002-127]up the idea that this life is a period of probation.  He utterly


[002-128]gave up the idea of eternal punishment, and before he died he had


[002-129]the happiness of believing that God was almost as good and generous


[002-130]as he was himself.


[002-131]_Question_.  I suppose this gossip about a change in your religious


[002-132]views arose or was created by the expression used at your brother's


[002-133]funeral, "In the night of death hope sees a star and listening love


[002-134]can hear the rustle of a wing"?


[002-135]_Answer_.  I never willingly will destroy a solitary human hope.


[002-136]I have always said that I did not know whether man was or was not


[002-137]immortal, but years before my brother died, in a lecture entitled


[002-138]"The Ghosts," which has since been published, I used the following


[002-139]words:  "The idea of immortality, that like a sea has ebbed and


[002-140]flowed in the human heart, with its countless waves of hope and


[002-141]fear, beating against the shores and rocks of time and fate, was


[002-142]not born of any book, nor of any creed, nor of any religion.  It


[002-143]was born of human affection, and it will continue to ebb and flow


[002-144]beneath the mists and clouds of doubt and darkness as long as love


[002-145]kisses the lips of death.  It is the rainbow--Hope, shining upon


[002-146]the tears of grief."


[002-147]_Question_.  The great objection to your teaching urged by your


[002-148]enemies is that you constantly tear down, and never build up?


[002-149]_Answer_.  I have just published a little book entitled, "Some


[002-150]Mistakes of Moses," in which I have endeavored to give most of the


[002-151]arguments I have urged against the Pentateuch in a lecture I


[002-152]delivered under that title.  The motto on the title page is, "A


[002-153]destroyer of weeds, thistles and thorns is a benefactor, whether


[002-154]he soweth grain or not."  I cannot for my life see why one should


[002-155]be charged with tearing down and not rebuilding simply because he


[002-156]exposes a sham, or detects a lie.  I do not feel under any obligation


[002-157]to build something in the place of a detected falsehood.  All I


[002-158]think I am under obligation to put in the place of a detected lie


[002-159]is the detection.  Most religionists talk as if mistakes were


[002-160]valuable things and they did not wish to part with them without a


[002-161]consideration.  Just how much they regard lies worth a dozen I do


[002-162]not know.  If the price is reasonable I am perfectly willing to


[002-163]give it, rather than to see them live and give their lives to the


[002-164]defence of delusions.  I am firmly convinced that to be happy here


[002-165]will not in the least detract from our happiness in another world


[002-166]should we be so fortunate as to reach another world; and I cannot


[002-167]see the value of any philosophy that reaches beyond the intelligent


[002-168]happiness of the present.  There may be a God who will make us


[002-169]happy in another world.  If he does, it will be more than he has


[002-170]accomplished in this.  I suppose that he will never have more than


[002-171]infinite power and never have less than infinite wisdom, and why


[002-172]people should expect that he should do better in another world than


[002-173]he has in this is something that I have never been able to explain.


[002-174]A being who has the power to prevent it and yet who allows thousands


[002-175]and millions of his children to starve; who devours them with


[002-176]earthquakes; who allows whole nations to be enslaved, cannot in my


[002-177]judgment be implicitly be depended upon to do justice in another


[002-178]world.


[002-179]_Question_.  How do the clergy generally treat you?


[002-180]_Answer_.  Well, of course there are the same distinctions among


[002-181]clergymen as among other people.  Some of them are quite respectable


[002-182]gentlemen, especially those with whom I am not acquainted.  I think


[002-183]that since the loss of my brother nothing could exceed the


[002-184]heartlessness of the remarks made by the average clergyman.  There


[002-185]have been some noble exceptions, to whom I feel not only thankful


[002-186]but grateful; but a very large majority have taken this occasion


[002-187]to say most unfeeling and brutal things.  I do not ask the clergy


[002-188]to forgive me, but I do request that they will so act that I will


[002-189]not have to forgive them.  I have always insisted that those who


[002-190]love their enemies should at least tell the truth about their


[002-191]friends, but I suppose, after all, that religion must be supported


[002-192]by the same means as those by which it was founded.  Of course,


[002-193]there are thousands of good ministers, men who are endeavoring to


[002-194]make the world better, and whose failure is no particular fault of


[002-195]their own.  I have always been in doubt as to whether the clergy


[002-196]were a necessary or an unnecessary evil.
