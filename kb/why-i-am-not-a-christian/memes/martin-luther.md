# Martin Luther

Martin Luther (1483–1546) was a German theologian, Augustinian monk, and the seminal figure of the Protestant Reformation. His challenge to papal authority — famously launched with the Ninety-five Theses in 1517 — fractured Western Christendom. In Robert Ingersoll's lectures, however, Luther appears not as a hero of religious liberty but as a cautionary example: the Reformation broke from Rome but preserved much of the superstition and intolerance it claimed to reform.

## 生平

Born in Eisleben, Saxony, Luther entered the Augustinian order and became a professor of theology at the University of Wittenberg. His protest against the sale of indulgences escalated into a broad challenge to papal authority, leading to his excommunication in 1521. His translation of the [[bible|Bible]] into German helped standardize the language and made scripture accessible to lay readers.

Ingersoll's assessment of Luther was unsparing. He noted that "Luther himself gave consent to a person high in office to have two wives, but prudently suggested to him that he had better keep it as still as possible" — using Luther's moral flexibility on bigamy to undermine claims of Protestant moral superiority [014-104].

## 主要观点与贡献

Luther's theological innovations — salvation by faith alone (*sola fide*), the priesthood of all believers, and the primacy of scripture (*sola scriptura*) — reshaped [[christianity|Christianity]]. But Ingersoll emphasized the darker elements of Luther's worldview: "Luther was, also, a believer in a personal Devil. He thought that deformed children had been begotten by an evil spirit" [014-110]. For Ingersoll, these beliefs exposed the Reformation as a rearrangement of superstition rather than its abolition.

When Luther confronted the Copernican revolution, he answered not with argument but with authority: "Luther answered Copernicus with a passage of Scripture, and he answered him to the satis[[truth|fact]]ion of orthodox ignorance" [033-022]. The image is deliberate — Luther silencing science with scripture, exactly the pattern Ingersoll spent his career opposing.

Ingersoll also quoted Luther's most damning pronouncement on toleration: "Universal toleration is universal error, and universal error is universal hell" [014-119]. In a single sentence, Luther revealed that the Reformation had not embraced the freedom of conscience that later freethinkers would demand.

## Ingersoll 的评价

Ingersoll directly compared Luther with [[voltaire|Voltaire]], and the contrast was devastating for the Reformer. He observed that "of Luther there were more Catholics than when he was born. And that twenty years after the death of [[voltaire|Voltaire]] there were millions less" [014-085]. The metric was historical effect: [[voltaire|Voltaire]]'s Enlightenment reduced religious persecution; Luther's Reformation had not.

Ingersoll also criticized Luther's personal character, noting that "in his ordinary conversation he was exceedingly low and vulgar, and that no respectable English publisher could be found who would soil" his pages with Luther's language [014-078]. Even Luther's sermons were "almost unrivaled for vulgarity" [050-013]. This was not mere ad hominem — Ingersoll's point was that the man Protestants revered as a moral exemplar fell far short of the standards even his own admirers would apply.

## 历史影响

Luther's Reformation permanently altered the religious and political landscape of Europe. But Ingersoll's treatment of Luther served a specific polemical purpose: to demonstrate that Protestantism was not a progressive force but merely a different form of the same theological authoritarianism. The true break with religious intolerance, Ingersoll argued, came not from Luther but from the [[voltaire|Enlightenment]] — from thinkers who subjected all religious claims to rational scrutiny rather than simply replacing one authority with another.
