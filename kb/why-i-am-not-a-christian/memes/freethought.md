Freethought — the position that beliefs about the natural and moral world should be formed through [[reason]], evidence, and logical inquiry rather than accepted on the authority of scripture, tradition, or revelation — was the [[reason|intellect]]ual tradition to which [[robert-ingersoll|Robert G. Ingersoll]] devoted his public life. Known as "[[robert-ingersoll|The Great Agnostic]]," Ingersoll was the most prominent American spokesman for freethought in the late nineteenth century, traveling the country to deliver lectures that drew thousands and arguing that [[truth]] was best pursued with an open mind and a skeptical disposition.

## Ingersoll's Vision of Free Thought

For Ingersoll, free thought was not merely a right but a civilizational force. He saw [[reason|intellect]]ual liberty as the engine of human progress and organized [[religion]] as its principal obstacle:

> Civilization is the child of free thought. (008-089)

This conviction animated his critique of [[christianity|orthodox Christianity]]. Ingersoll did not object to religious belief as such — he defended the right of Spiritualists and others to hold unconventional views — but to the demand that certain propositions be accepted without examination. He insisted that "he has the same right to free thought" as any believer, and that no institution had authority to circumscribe the human mind (006-154).

Ingersoll's freethought was constructive, not merely critical. He celebrated the achievements of [[science]], literature, and philosophy as fruits of the unfettered [[reason|intellect]]. Figures like [[shakespeare|Shakespeare]] and [[darwin|Darwin]] were, in his view, exemplars of what the human mind could produce when liberated from theological constraint. The "free thought" Ingersoll championed was the method of all genuine inquiry — whether in the laboratory, the library, or the public square.

## Freethought and Religion

Ingersoll drew a sharp line between freethought and organized religion, but he did not equate religion with superstition in every case. He acknowledged that "the Spiritualists believe in free thought, in freedom of" conscience (090-056), and he defended their right to hold unconventional beliefs. His objection was never to belief itself, but to the claim of infallibility — the assertion that some book or institution was beyond rational scrutiny.

This distinction placed Ingersoll in the tradition of Enlightenment freethinkers — [[humboldt|Alexander von Humboldt]], [[voltaire|Voltaire]], [[thomas-paine|Thomas Paine]] — who argued that [[reason|intellect]]ual progress required the right to question authority. Ingersoll updated this tradition for post-Civil War America, applying its methods to the [[bible|Bible]], Christian doctrine, and the church's role in public life.

| Freethought Principle | Ingersoll's Application |
|----------------------|------------------------|
| Beliefs require evidence | The [[bible|Bible]] cannot be accepted as revelation without proof |
| Authority is not a reason | The church's say-so does not make a doctrine true |
| Skepticism is virtuous | Doubt is the engine of inquiry; certainty without evidence is arrogance |
| Intellectual liberty is a right | Every person has "the same right to free thought" (006-154) |

## Relation to Key Ingersoll Concepts

Freethought in Ingersoll's framework is inseparable from a network of related commitments:

- **Freethought and [[reason]]**: [[reason|Reason]] is the tool; freethought is the stance. One cannot think freely without reason, and reason is wasted without the freedom to follow it.
- **Freethought and [[science]]**: Science is freethought applied to nature — hypothesis, experiment, revision — the same method Ingersoll urged be applied to theology.
- **Freethought and [[superstition]]**: [[superstition|Superstition]] is what fills the vacuum when free thought is suppressed. "Civilization is the child of free thought" — its absence leaves only inherited error.
- **Freethought and [[immortality]]**: The freethinker examines the evidence for an [[immortality|afterlife]] and, finding none, withholds belief — not out of hostility to hope, but out of fidelity to [[reason|intellect]]ual honesty.

## Later Influence

Ingersoll's freethought lectures were among the most attended public events of his era, drawing crowds that sometimes exceeded those of the nation's most prominent clergymen. His oratory and published works influenced subsequent generations of secularists, humanists, and religious skeptics. The tradition he represented — agnostic, rational, committed to free inquiry — anticipated the secular humanist movement of the twentieth century and the New Atheism of the twenty-first. Figures from [[bertrand-russell|Bertrand Russell]] to [[christopher-hitchens|Christopher Hitchens]] worked within the [[reason|intellect]]ual framework Ingersoll helped establish: that beliefs held on insufficient evidence are unworthy of respect, and that the freedom to question is the foundation of all genuine knowledge.
