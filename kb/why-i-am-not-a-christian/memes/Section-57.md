[056-001]The platform, except upon this question of free trade, means very


[056-002]little.  There are other features in it which I have not at present


[056-003]time to examine, but shall do so hereafter.  I want to take it up


[056-004]point by point and find really what it means, what its scope is,


[056-005]and what the intentions were of the gentlemen who made it.


[056-006]But it may be proper to say here, that in my judgment it is a very


[056-007]weak and flimsy document, as Victor Hugo would say, "badly cut and


[056-008]badly sewed."


[056-009]Of course, I know that the country will exist whatever party may


[056-010]be in power.  I know that all our blessings do not come from laws,


[056-011]or from the carrying into effect of certain policies, and probably


[056-012]I could pay no greater compliment to any country than to say that


[056-013]even eight years of Democratic rule cannot materially affect her


[056-014]destiny.


[056-015]--_New York Press_, June 10, 1888.


[056-016]THE REPUBLICAN PLATFORM OF 1888.


[056-017]_Question_.  What do you think of the signs of the times so far as


[056-018]the campaign has progressed?


[056-019]_Answer_.  The party is now going through a period of misrepresentation.


[056-020]Every absurd meaning that can be given to any combination of words


[056-021]will be given to every plank of the platform.  In the heat of


[056-022]partisan hatred every plank will look warped and cracked.  A great


[056-023]effort is being made to show that the Republican party is in favor


[056-024]of intemperance,--that the great object now is to lessen the price


[056-025]of all intoxicants and increase the cost of all the necessaries of


[056-026]life.  The papers that are for nothing but reform of everything


[056-027]and everybody except themselves, are doing their utmost to show


[056-028]that the Republican party is the enemy of honesty and temperance.


[056-029]The other day, at a Republican ratification meeting, I stated among


[056-030]other things, that we could not make great men and great women


[056-031]simply by keeping them out of temptation--that nobody would think


[056-032]of tying the hands of a person behind them and then praise him for


[056-033]not picking pockets; that great people were great enough to withstand


[056-034]temptation, and in that connection I made this statement:  "Temperance


[056-035]goes hand in hand with liberty"--the idea being that when a chain


[056-036]is taken from the body an additional obligation is perceived by


[056-037]the mind.  These good papers--the papers that believe in honest


[056-038]politics--stated that I said:  "Temperance goes hand in hand with


[056-039]liquor."  This was not only in the reports of the meeting, but this


[056-040]passage was made the subject of several editorials.  It hardly


[056-041]seems possible that any person really thought that such a statement


[056-042]had been expressed.  The Republican party does not want free whiskey


[056-043]--it wants free men; and a great many people in the Republican


[056-044]party are great enough to know that temperance does go hand in hand


[056-045]with liberty; they are great enough to know that all legislation


[056-046]as to what we shall eat, as to what we shall drink, and as to


[056-047]wherewithal we shall be clothed, partakes of the nature of petty,


[056-048]irritating and annoying tyranny.  They also know that the natural


[056-049]result is to fill a country with spies, hypocrites and pretenders,


[056-050]and that when a law is not in accordance with an enlightened public


[056-051]sentiment, it becomes either a dead letter, or, when a few fanatics


[056-052]endeavor to enforce it, a demoralizer of courts, of juries and of


[056-053]people.


[056-054]The attack upon the platform by temperance people is doing no harm,


[056-055]for the reason that long before November comes these people will


[056-056]see the mistake they have made.  It seems somewhat curious that


[056-057]the Democrats should attack the platform if they really believe


[056-058]that it means free whiskey.


[056-059]The tax was levied during the war.  It was a war measure.  The


[056-060]Government was _in extremis_, and for that reason was obliged to


[056-061]obtain a revenue from every possible article of value.  The war is


[056-062]over; the necessity has disappeared; consequently the Government


[056-063]should return to the methods of peace.  We have too many Government


[056-064]officials.  Let us get rid of collectors and gaugers and inspectors.


[056-065]Let us do away with all this machinery, and leave the question to


[056-066]be settled by the State.  If the temperance people themselves would


[056-067]take a second thought, they would see that when the Government


[056-068]collects eighty or ninety million dollars from a tax on whiskey,


[056-069]the traffic becomes entrenched, it becomes one of the pillars of


[056-070]the State, one of the great sources of revenue.  Let the States


[056-071]attend to this question, and it will be a matter far easier to deal


[056-072]with.


[056-073]The Prohibitionists are undoubtedly honest, and their object is to


[056-074]destroy the traffic, to prevent the manufacture of whiskey.  Can


[056-075]they do this as long as the Government collects ninety million


[056-076]dollars per annum from that one source?  If there is anything


[056-077]whatever in this argument, is it not that the traffic pays a bribe


[056-078]of ninety million dollars a year for its life?  Will not the farmers


[056-079]say to the temperance men:  "The distilleries pay the taxes, the


[056-080]distilleries raise the price of corn; is it not better for the


[056-081]General Government to look to another direction for its revenues


[056-082]and leave the States to deal as they may see proper with this


[056-083]question?"


[056-084]With me, it makes no difference what is done with the liquor--


[056-085]whether it is used in the arts or not--it is a question of policy.


[056-086]There is no moral principle involved on our side of the question,


[056-087]to say the least of it.  If it is a crime to make and sell intoxicating


[056-088]liquors, the Government, by licensing persons to make and sell,


[056-089]becomes a party to the crime.  If one man poisons another, no matter


[056-090]how much the poison costs, the crime is the same; and if the person


[056-091]from whom the poison was purchased knew how it was to be used, he


[056-092]is also a murderer.


[056-093]There have been many reformers in this world, and they have seemed


[056-094]to imagine that people will do as they say.  They think that you


[056-095]can use people as you do bricks or stones; that you can lay them


[056-096]up in walls and they will remain where they are placed; but the


[056-097]truth is, you cannot do this.  The bricks are not satisfied with


[056-098]each other--they go away in the night--in the morning there is no


[056-099]wall.  Most of these reformers go up what you might call the Mount


[056-100]Sinai of their own egotism, and there, surrounded by the clouds of


[056-101]their own ignorance, they meditate upon the follies and the frailties


[056-102]of their fellow-men and then come down with ten commandments for


[056-103]their neighbors.


[056-104]All this talk about the Republican platform being in favor of


[056-105]intemperance, so far as the Democratic party is concerned, is pure,


[056-106]unadulterated hypocrisy--nothing more, nothing less.  So far as


[056-107]the Prohibitionists are concerned, they may be perfectly honest,


[056-108]but, if they will think a moment, they will see how perfectly


[056-109]illogical they are.  No one can help sympathizing with any effort


[056-110]honestly made to do away with the evil of intemperance.  I know


[056-111]that many believe that these evils can be done away with by


[056-112]legislation.  While I sympathize with the objects that these people


[056-113]wish to attain, I do not believe in the means they suggest.  As


[056-114]life becomes valuable, people will become temperate, because they


[056-115]will take care of themselves.  Temperance is born of the countless


[056-116]influences of civilization.  Character cannot be forced upon anybody;


[056-117]it is a growth, the seeds of which are within.  Men cannot be forced


[056-118]into real temperance any more than they can be frightened into real


[056-119]morality.  You may frighten a man to that degree that he will not


[056-120]do a certain thing, but you cannot scare him badly enough to prevent


[056-121]his wanting to do that thing.  Reformation begins on the inside,


[056-122]and the man refrains because he perceives that he ought to refrain,


[056-123]not because his neighbors say that he ought to refrain.  No one


[056-124]would think of praising convicts in jail for being regular at their


[056-125]meals, or for not staying out nights; and it seems to me that when


[056-126]the Prohibitionists--when the people who are really in favor of


[056-127]temperance--look the ground all over they will see that it is far


[056-128]better to support the Republican party than to throw their votes


[056-129]away; and the Republicans will see that it is simply a proposition


[056-130]to go back to the original methods of collecting revenue for the


[056-131]Government--that it is simply abandoning the measures made necessary


[056-132]by war, and that it is giving to the people the largest liberty


[056-133]consistent with the needs of the Government, and that it is only


[056-134]leaving these questions where in time of peace they properly belong


[056-135]--to the States themselves.


[056-136]_Question_.  Do you think that the Knights of Labor will cut any


[056-137]material figure in this election?


[056-138]_Answer_.  The Knights of Labor will probably occupy substantially


[056-139]the same position as other laborers and other mechanics.  If they


[056-140]clearly see that the policy advocated by the Republican party is


[056-141]to their interest, that it will give them better wages than the


[056-142]policy advocated by the Democrats, then they will undoubtedly


[056-143]support our ticket.  There is more or less irritation between


[056-144]employers and employed.  All men engaged in manufacturing and


[056-145]neither good nor generous.  Many of them get work for as little as


[056-146]possible, and sell its product for all they can get.  It is impossible


[056-147]to adopt a policy that will not by such people be abused.  Many of


[056-148]them would like to see the working man toil for twelve hours or


[056-149]fourteen or sixteen in each day.  Many of them wonder why they need


[056-150]sleep or food, and are perfectly astonished when they ask for pay.


[056-151]In some instances, undoubtedly, the working men will vote against


[056-152]their own interests simply to get even with such employers.


[056-153]Some laboring men have been so robbed, so tyrannized over, that


[056-154]they would be perfectly willing to feel for the pillars and take


[056-155]a certain delight in a destruction that brought ruin even to


[056-156]themselves.  Such manufacturers, however, I believe to be in a


[056-157]minority, and the laboring men, under the policy of free trade,


[056-158]would be far more in their power.  When wages fall below a certain


[056-159]point, then comes degradation, loss of manhood, serfdom and slavery.


[056-160]If any man has the right to vote for his own interests, certainly


[056-161]the man who labors is that man, and every working man having in


[056-162]his will a part of the sovereignty of this nation, having within


[056-163]him a part of the lawmaking power, should have the intelligence


[056-164]and courage to vote for his own interests; he should vote for good


[056-165]wages; he should vote for a policy that would enable him to lay


[056-166]something by for the winter of his life, that would enable him to


[056-167]earn enough to educate his children, enough to give him a home and


[056-168]a fireside.


[056-169]He need not do this in anger or for revenge, but because it is


[056-170]just, because it is right, and because the working people are in


[056-171]a majority.  They ought to control the world, because they have


[056-172]made the world what it is.  They have given everything there is of


[056-173]value.  Labor plows every field, builds every house, fashions


[056-174]everything of use, and when that labor is guided by intelligence


[056-175]the world is prosperous.


[056-176]He who thinks good thoughts is a laborer--one of the greatest.


[056-177]The man who invented the reaper will be harvesting the fields for


[056-178]thousands of years to come.  If labor is abused in this country


[056-179]the laborers have it within their power to defend themselves.


[056-180]All my sympathies are with the men who toil.  I shed very few tears


[056-181]over bankers and millionaires and corporations--they can take care


[056-182]of themselves.  My sympathies are with the man who has nothing to


[056-183]sell but his strength; nothing to sell but his muscle and his


[056-184]intelligence; who has no capital except that which his mother gave


[056-185]him--a capital he must sell every day; my sympathies are with him;


[056-186]and I want him to have a good market; and I want it so that he can


[056-187]sell the work for more than enough to take care of him to-morrow.
