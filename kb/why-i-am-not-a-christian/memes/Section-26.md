[025-001]I believe it was Christ who commanded his disciples to leave father


[025-002]and mother; not only to leave them, but to desert them; and not


[025-003]only to desert father and mother, but to desert wives and children.


[025-004]It is also told of Christ that he said that he came to set fathers


[025-005]against children and children against fathers.  Strange that a


[025-006]follower of his should object to a man differing in opinion from


[025-007]his parents!  The truth is, logic knows nothing of consanguinity;


[025-008]facts have no relatives but other facts; and these facts do not


[025-009]depend upon the character of the person who states them, or upon


[025-010]the position of the discoverer.  And this leads me to another branch


[025-011]of the same subject.


[025-012]The ministers are continually saying that certain great men--kings,


[025-013]presidents, statesmen, millionaires--have believed in the inspiration


[025-014]of the Bible.  Only the other day, I read a sermon in which Carlyle


[025-015]was quoted as having said that "the Bible is a noble book."  That


[025-016]all may be and yet the book not be inspired.  But what is the simple


[025-017]assertion of Thomas Carlyle worth?  If the assertion is based upon


[025-018]a reason, then it is worth simply the value of the reason, and the


[025-019]reason is worth just as much without the assertion, but without


[025-020]the reason the assertion is worthless.  Thomas Carlyle thought,


[025-021]and solemnly put the thought in print, that his father was a greater


[025-022]man than Robert Burns.  His opinion did Burns no harm, and his


[025-023]father no good.  Since reading his "Reminiscences," I have no great


[025-024]opinion of his opinion.  In some respects he was undoubtedly a


[025-025]great man, in others a small one.


[025-026]No man should give the opinion of another as authority and in place


[025-027]of fact and reason, unless he is willing to take all the opinions


[025-028]of that man.  An opinion is worth the warp and woof of fact and


[025-029]logic in it and no more.  A man cannot add to the truthfulness of


[025-030]truth.  In the ordinary business of life, we give certain weight


[025-031]to the opinion of specialists--to the opinion of doctors, lawyers,


[025-032]scientists, and historians.  Within the domain of the natural, we


[025-033]take the opinions of our fellow-men; but we do not feel that we


[025-034]are absolutely bound by these opinions.  We have the right to re-


[025-035]examine them, and if we find they are wrong we feel at liberty to


[025-036]say so.  A doctor is supposed to have studied medicine; to have


[025-037]examined and explored the questions entering into his profession;


[025-038]but we know that doctors are often mistaken.  We also know that


[025-039]there are many schools of medicine; that these schools disagree


[025-040]with one another, and that the doctors of each school disagree with


[025-041]one another.  We also know that many patients die, and so far as


[025-042]we know, these patients have not come back to tell us whether the


[025-043]doctors killed them or not.  The grave generally prevents a


[025-044]demonstration.  It is exactly the same with the clergy.  They have


[025-045]many schools of theology, all despising each other.  Probably no


[025-046]two members of the same church exactly agree.  They cannot demonstrate


[025-047]their propositions, because between the premise and the logical


[025-048]conclusion or demonstration, stands the tomb.  A gravestone marks


[025-049]the end of theology.  In some cases, the physician can, by a post-


[025-050]mortem examination, find what killed the patient, but there is no


[025-051]theological post-mortem.  It is impossible, by cutting a body open,


[025-052]to find where the soul has gone; or whether baptism, or the lack


[025-053]of it, had the slightest effect upon final destiny.  The church,


[025-054]knowing that there are no facts beyond the coffin, relies upon


[025-055]opinions, assertions and theories.  For this reason it is always


[025-056]asking alms of distinguished people.  Some President wishes to be


[025-057]re-elected, and thereupon speaks about the Bible as "the corner-


[025-058]stone of American Liberty."  This sentence is a mouth large enough


[025-059]to swallow any church, and from that time forward the religious


[025-060]people will be citing that remark of the politician to substantiate


[025-061]the inspiration of the Scriptures.


[025-062]The man who accepts opinions because they have been entertained by


[025-063]distinguished people, is a mental snob.  When we blindly follow


[025-064]authority we are serfs.  When our reason is convinced we are freemen.


[025-065]It is rare to find a fully rounded and complete man.  A man may be


[025-066]a great doctor and a poor mechanic, a successful politician and a


[025-067]poor metaphysician, a poor painter and a good poet.


[025-068]The rarest thing in the world is a logician--that is to say, a man


[025-069]who knows the value of a fact.  It is hard to find mental proportion.


[025-070]Theories may be established by names, but facts cannot be demonstrated


[025-071]in that way.  Very small people are sometimes right, and very great


[025-072]people are sometimes wrong.  Ministers are sometimes right.


[025-073]In all the philosophies of the world there are undoubtedly


[025-074]contradictions and absurdities.  The mind of man is imperfect and


[025-075]perfect results are impossible.  A mirror, in order to reflect a


[025-076]perfect picture, a perfect copy, must itself be perfect.  The mind


[025-077]is a little piece of intellectual glass the surface of which is


[025-078]not true, not perfect.  In consequence of this, every image is more


[025-079]or less distorted.  The less we know, the more we imagine that we


[025-080]can know; but the more we know, the smaller seems the sum of


[025-081]knowledge.  The less we know, the more we expect, the more we hope


[025-082]for, and the more seems within the range of probability.  The less


[025-083]we have, the more we want.  There never was a banquet magnificent


[025-084]enough to gratify the imagination of a beggar.  The moment people


[025-085]begin to reason about what they call the supernatural, they seem


[025-086]to lose their minds.  People seem to have lost their reason in


[025-087]religious matters, very much as the dodo is said to have lost its


[025-088]wings; they have been restricted to a little inspired island, and


[025-089]by disuse their reason has been lost.


[025-090]In the Jewish Scriptures you will find simply the literature of


[025-091]the Jews.  You will find there the tears and anguish of captivity,


[025-092]patriotic fervor, national aspiration, proverbs for the conduct of


[025-093]daily life, laws, regulations, customs, legends, philosophy and


[025-094]folly.  These books, of course, were not written by one man, but


[025-095]by many authors.  They do not agree, having been written in different


[025-096]centuries, under different circumstances.  I see that Mr. Beecher


[025-097]has at last concluded that the Old Testament does not teach the


[025-098]doctrine of immortality.  He admits that from Mount Sinai came no


[025-099]hope for the dead.  It is very curious that we find in the Old


[025-100]Testament no funeral service.  No one stands by the dead and predicts


[025-101]another life.  In the Old Testament there is no promise of another


[025-102]world.  I have sometimes thought that while the Jews were slaves


[025-103]in Egypt, the doctrine of immortality became hateful.  They built


[025-104]so many tombs; they carried so many burdens to commemorate the


[025-105]dead; the saw a nation waste its wealth to adorn its graves, and


[025-106]leave the living naked to embalm the dead, that they concluded the


[025-107]doctrine was a curse and never should be taught.


[025-108]_Question_.  If the Jews did not believe in immortality, how do


[025-109]you account for the allusions made to witches and wizards and things


[025-110]of that nature?


[025-111]_Answer_.  When Saul visited the Witch of Endor, and she, by some


[025-112]magic spell, called up Samuel, the prophet said:  "Why hast thou


[025-113]disquieted me, to call me up?"  He did not say:  Why have you called


[025-114]me from another world?  The idea expressed is:  I was asleep, why


[025-115]did you disturb that repose which should be eternal?  The ancient


[025-116]Jews believed in witches and wizards and familiar spirits; but they


[025-117]did not seem to think that these spirits had once been men and


[025-118]women.  They spoke to them as belonging to another world, a world


[025-119]to which man would never find his way.  At that time it was supposed


[025-120]that Jehovah and his angels lived in the sky, but that region was


[025-121]not spoken of as the destined home of man.  Jacob saw angels going


[025-122]up and down the ladder, but not the spirits of those he had known.


[025-123]There are two cases where it seems that men were good enough to be


[025-124]adopted into the family of heaven.  Enoch was translated, and Elijah


[025-125]was taken up in a chariot of fire.  As it is exceedingly cold at


[025-126]the height of a few miles, it is easy to see why the chariot was


[025-127]of fire, and the same fact explains another circumstance--the


[025-128]dropping of the mantle.  The Jews probably believed in the existence


[025-129]of other beings--that is to say, in angels and gods and evil spirits


[025-130]--and that they lived in other worlds--but there is no passage


[025-131]showing that they believed in what we call the immortality of the


[025-132]soul.


[025-133]_Question_.  Do you believe, or disbelieve, in the immortality of


[025-134]the soul?


[025-135]_Answer_.  I neither assert nor deny; I simply admit that I do not


[025-136]know.  Upon that subject I am absolutely without evidence.  This


[025-137]is the only world that I was ever in.  There may be spirits, but


[025-138]I have never met them, and do not know that I would recognize a


[025-139]spirit.  I can form no conception of what is called spiritual life.


[025-140]It may be that I am deficient in imagination, and that ministers


[025-141]have no difficulty in conceiving of angels and disembodied souls.


[025-142]I have not the slightest idea how a soul looks, what shape it is,


[025-143]how it goes from one place to another, whether it walks or flies.


[025-144]I cannot conceive of the immaterial having form; neither can I


[025-145]conceive of anything existing without form, and yet the fact that


[025-146]I cannot conceive of a thing does not prove that the thing does


[025-147]not exist, but it does prove that I know nothing about it, and that


[025-148]being so, I ought to admit my ignorance.  I am satisfied of a good


[025-149]many things that I do not know.  I am satisfied that there is no


[025-150]place of eternal torment.  I am satisfied that that doctrine has


[025-151]done more harm than all the religious ideas, other than that, have


[025-152]done good.  I do not want to take any hope from any human heart.


[025-153]I have no objection to people believing in any good thing--no


[025-154]objection to their expecting a crown of infinite joy for every


[025-155]human being.  Many people imagine that immortality must be an


[025-156]infinite good; but, after all, there is something terrible in the


[025-157]idea of endless life.  Think of a river that never reaches the sea;


[025-158]of a bird that never folds its wings; of a journey that never ends.


[025-159]Most people find great pleasure in thinking about and in believing


[025-160]in another world.  There the prisoner expects to be free; the slave


[025-161]to find liberty; the poor man expects wealth; the rich man happiness;


[025-162]the peasant dreams of power, and the king of contentment.  They


[025-163]expect to find there what they lack here.  I do not wish to destroy


[025-164]these dreams.  I am endeavoring to put out the everlasting fires.


[025-165]A good, cool grave is infinitely better than the fiery furnace of


[025-166]Jehovah's wrath.  Eternal sleep is better than eternal pain.  For


[025-167]my part I would rather be annihilated than to be an angel, with


[025-168]all the privileges of heaven, and yet have within my breast a heart


[025-169]that could be happy while those who had loved me in this world were


[025-170]in perdition.


[025-171]I most sincerely hope that the future life will fulfill all splendid


[025-172]dreams; but in the religion of the present day there is no joy.


[025-173]Nothing is so devoid of comfort, when bending above our dead, as


[025-174]the assertions of theology unsupported by a single fact.  The


[025-175]promises are so far away, and the dead are so near.  From words


[025-176]spoken eighteen centuries ago, the echoes are so weak, and the


[025-177]sounds of the clods on the coffin are so loud.  Above the grave


[025-178]what can the honest minister say?  If the dead were not a Christian,


[025-179]what then?  What comfort can the orthodox clergyman give to the


[025-180]widow of an honest unbeliever?  If Christianity is true, the other


[025-181]world will be worse than this.  There the many will be miserable,


[025-182]only the few happy; there the miserable cannot better their condition;


[025-183]the future has no star of hope, and in the east of eternity there


[025-184]can never be a dawn.
