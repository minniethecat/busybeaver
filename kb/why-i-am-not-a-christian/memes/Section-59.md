[058-001]There are two kinds of people that I have no use for--leaders and


[058-002]followers.  The leader should be principle; the leader should be


[058-003]a great object to be accomplished.  The follower should be the man


[058-004]dedicated to the accomplishment of a noble end.  He who simply


[058-005]follows persons gains no honor and is incapable of giving honor


[058-006]even to the one he follows.  There are certain things to be


[058-007]accomplished and these things are the leaders.  We want in this


[058-008]country an American system; we wish to carry into operation, into


[058-009]practical effect, ideas, policies, theories in harmony with our


[058-010]surroundings.


[058-011]This is a great country filled with intelligent, industrious,


[058-012]restless, ambitious people.  Millions came here because they were


[058-013]dissatisfied with the laws, the institutions, the tyrannies, the


[058-014]absurdities, the poverty, the wretchedness and the infamous spirit


[058-015]of caste found in the Old World.  Millions of these people are


[058-016]thinking for themselves, and only the people who can teach, who


[058-017]can give new facts, who can illuminate, should be regarded as


[058-018]political benefactors.  This country is, in my judgment, in all


[058-019]that constitutes true greatness, the nearest civilized of any


[058-020]country.  Only yesterday the German Empire robbed a woman of her


[058-021]child; this was done as a political necessity.  Nothing is taken


[058-022]into consideration except some move on the political chess-board.


[058-023]The feelings of a mother are utterly disregarded; they are left


[058-024]out of the question; they are not even passed upon.  They are


[058-025]naturally ignored, because in these governments only the unnatural


[058-026]is natural.


[058-027]In our political life we have substantially outgrown the duel.


[058-028]There are some small, insignificant people who still think it


[058-029]important to defend a worthless reputation on the field of "honor,"


[058-030]but for respectable members of the Senate, of the House, of the


[058-031]Cabinet, to settle a political argument with pistols would render


[058-032]them utterly contemptible in this country; that is to say, the


[058-033]opinion that governs, that dominates in this country, holds the


[058-034]duel in abhorrence and in contempt.  What could be more idiotic,


[058-035]absurd, childish, than the duel between Boulanger and Floquet?


[058-036]What was settled?  It needed no duel to convince the world that


[058-037]Floquet is a man of courage.  The same may be said of Boulanger.


[058-038]He has faced death upon many fields.  Why, then, resort to the


[058-039]duel?  If Boulanger's wound proves fatal, that certainly does not


[058-040]tend to prove that Floquet told the truth, and if Boulanger recovers,


[058-041]it does not tend to prove that he did not tell the truth.


[058-042]Nothing is settled.  Two men controlled by vanity, that individual


[058-043]vanity born of national vanity, try to kill each other; the public


[058-044]ready to reward the victor; the cause of the quarrel utterly ignored;


[058-045]the hands of the public ready to applaud the successful swordsman


[058-046]--and yet France is called a civilized nation.  No matter how


[058-047]serious the political situation may be, no matter if everything


[058-048]depends upon one man, that man is at the mercy of anyone in opposition


[058-049]who may see fit to challenge him.  The greatest general at the head


[058-050]of their armies may be forced to fight a duel with a nobody.  Such


[058-051]ideas, such a system, keeps a nation in peril and makes every cause,


[058-052]to a greater or less extent, depend upon the sword or the bullet


[058-053]of a criminal.


[058-054]--_The Press_, New York, July 16, 1888.


[058-055]THE MILLS BILL.


[058-056]_Question_.  What, in your opinion, is the significance of the vote


[058-057]on the Mills Bill recently passed in the House?  In this I find


[058-058]there were one hundred and sixty-two for it, and one hundred and


[058-059]forty-nine against it; of these, two Republicans voted for, and


[058-060]five Democrats against.


[058-061]_Answer_.  In the first place, I think it somewhat doubtful whether


[058-062]the bill could have been passed if Mr. Randall had been well.  His


[058-063]sickness had much to do with this vote.  Had he been present to


[058-064]have taken care of his side, to have kept his forces in hand, he,


[058-065]in my judgment, taking into consideration his wonderful knowledge


[058-066]of parliamentary tactics, would have defeated this bill.


[058-067]It is somewhat hard to get the average Democrat, in the absence of


[058-068]his leader, to throw away the prospect of patronage.  Most members


[058-069]of Congress have to pay tolerably strict attention to their political


[058-070]fences.  The President, although clinging with great tenacity to


[058-071]the phrase "civil service," has in all probability pulled every


[058-072]string he could reach for the purpose of compelling the Democratic


[058-073]members not only to stand in line, but to answer promptly to their


[058-074]names.  Every Democrat who has shown independence has been stepped


[058-075]on just to the extent he could be reached; but many members, had


[058-076]the leader been on the floor--and a leader like Randall--would have


[058-077]followed him.


[058-078]There are very few congressional districts in the United States not


[058-079]intensely Democratic where the people want nothing protected.


[058-080]There are a few districts where nothing grows except ancient


[058-081]politics, where they cultivate only the memory of what never ought


[058-082]to have been, where the subject of protection has not yet reached.


[058-083]The impudence requisite to pass the Mills Bill is something


[058-084]phenomenal.  Think of the Representatives from Louisiana saying to


[058-085]the ranchmen of the West and to the farmers of Ohio that wool must


[058-086]be on the free list, but that for the sake of preserving the sugar


[058-087]interest of Louisiana and a little portion of Texas, all the rest


[058-088]of the United States must pay tribute.


[058-089]Everybody admits that Louisiana is not very well adapted by nature


[058-090]for raising sugar, for the reason that the cane has to be planted


[058-091]every year, and every third year the frost puts in an appearance


[058-092]just a little before the sugar.  Now, while I think personally that


[058-093]the tariff on sugar has stimulated the inventive genius of the


[058-094]country to find other ways of producing that which is universally


[058-095]needed; and while I believe that it will not be long until we shall


[058-096]produce every pound of sugar that we consume, and produce it cheaper


[058-097]than we buy it now, I am satisfied that in time and at no distant


[058-098]day sugar will be made in this country extremely cheap, not only


[058-099]from beets, but from sorghum and corn, and it may be from other


[058-100]products.  At the same time this is no excuse for Louisiana, neither


[058-101]is it any excuse for South Carolina asking for a tariff on rice,


[058-102]and at the same time wishing to leave some other industry in the


[058-103]United States, in which many more millions have been invested,


[058-104]absolutely without protection.


[058-105]Understand, I am not opposed to a reasonable tariff on rice, provided


[058-106]it is shown that we can raise rice in this country cheaply and at


[058-107]a profit to such an extent as finally to become substantially


[058-108]independent of the rest of the world.  What I object to is the


[058-109]impudence of the gentleman who is raising the rice objecting to


[058-110]the protection of some other industry of far greater importance


[058-111]than his.


[058-112]After all, the whole thing must be a compromise.  We must act


[058-113]together for the common good.  If we wish to make something at the


[058-114]expense of another State we must allow that State to make something


[058-115]at our expense, or at least we must be able to show that while it


[058-116]is for our benefit it is also for the benefit of the country at


[058-117]large.  Everybody is entitled to have his own way up to the point


[058-118]that his way interferes with somebody else.  States are like


[058-119]individuals--their rights are relative--they are subordinated to


[058-120]the good of the whole country.


[058-121]For many years it has been the American policy to do all that


[058-122]reasonably could be done to foster American industry, to give scope


[058-123]to American ingenuity and a field for American enterprise--in other


[058-124]words, a future for the United States.


[058-125]The Southern States were always in favor of something like free


[058-126]trade.  They wanted to raise cotton for Great Britain--raw material


[058-127]for other countries.  At that time their labor was slave labor,


[058-128]and they could not hope ever to have skilled labor, because skilled


[058-129]labor cannot be enslaved.  The Southern people knew at that time


[058-130]that if a man was taught enough of mathematics to understand


[058-131]machinery, to run locomotives, to weave cloth; it he was taught


[058-132]enough of chemistry even to color calico, it would be impossible


[058-133]to keep him a slave.  Education always was and always will be an


[058-134]abolitionist.  The South advocated a system of harmony with slavery,


[058-135]in harmony with ignorance--that is to say, a system of free trade,


[058-136]under which it might raise its raw material.  It could not hope to


[058-137]manufacture, because by making its labor intelligent enough to


[058-138]manufacture it would lose it.


[058-139]In the North, men are working for themselves, and as I have often


[058-140]said, they were getting their hands and heads in partnership.


[058-141]Every little stream that went singing to the sea was made to turn


[058-142]a thousand wheels; the water became a spinner and a weaver; the


[058-143]water became a blacksmith and ran a trip hammer; the water was


[058-144]doing the work of millions of men.  In other words, the free people


[058-145]of the North were doing what free people have always done, going


[058-146]into partnership with the forces of nature.  Free people want good


[058-147]tools, shapely, well made--tools with which the most work can be


[058-148]done with the least strain.


[058-149]Suppose the South had been in favor of protection; suppose that


[058-150]all over the Southern country there had been workshops, factories,


[058-151]machines of every kind; suppose that her people had been as ingenious


[058-152]as the people of the North; suppose that her hands had been as deft


[058-153]as those that had been accustomed to skilled labor; then one of


[058-154]two things would have happened; either the South would have been


[058-155]too intelligent to withdraw from the Union, or, having withdrawn,


[058-156]it would have had the power to maintain its position.  My opinion


[058-157]is that is would have been too intelligent to withdraw.


[058-158]When the South seceded it had no factories.  The people of the


[058-159]South had ability, but it was not trained in the direction then


[058-160]necessary.  They could not arm and equip their men; they could not


[058-161]make their clothes; they could not provide them with guns, with


[058-162]cannon, with ammunition, and with the countless implements of


[058-163]destruction.  They had not the ingenuity; they had not the means;


[058-164]they could not make cars to carry their troops, or locomotives to


[058-165]draw them; they had not in their armies the men to build bridges


[058-166]or to supply the needed transportation.  They had nothing but cotton


[058-167]--that is to say, raw material.  So that you might say that the


[058-168]Rebellion has settled the question as to whether a country is better


[058-169]off and more prosperous, and more powerful, and more ready for war,


[058-170]that is filled with industries, or one that depends simply upon


[058-171]the production of raw material.


[058-172]There is another thing in this connection that should never be


[058-173]forgotten--at least, not until after the election in November, and


[058-174]then if forgotten, should be remembered at every subsequent election


[058-175]--and that is, that the Southern Confederacy had in its Constitution


[058-176]the doctrine of free trade.  Among other things it was fighting


[058-177]for free trade.  As a matter of fact, John C. Calhoun was fighting


[058-178]for free trade; the nullification business was in the interest of


[058-179]free trade.


[058-180]The Southern people are endeavoring simply to accomplish, with the


[058-181]aid of New York, what they failed to accomplish on the field.  The


[058-182]South is as "solid" to-day as in 1863.  It is now for free trade,


[058-183]and it purposes to carry the day by the aid of one or two Northern


[058-184]States.  History is repeating itself.  It was the same for many


[058-185]years, up to the election of Abraham Lincoln.


[058-186]Understand me, I do not blame the South for acting in accordance


[058-187]with its convictions, but the North ought not to be misled.  The


[058-188]North ought to understand what the issue is.  The South has a


[058-189]different idea of government--it is afraid of what it calls


[058-190]"centralization"--it is extremely sensitive about what are called


[058-191]"State Rights" or the sovereignty of the State.  But the North


[058-192]believes in a Union that is united.  The North does not expect to


[058-193]have any interest antagonistic to the Union.  The North has no


[058-194]mental reservation.  The North believes in the Government and in


[058-195]the Federal system, and the North believes that when a State is


[058-196]admitted into the Union it becomes a part--an integral part--of


[058-197]the Nation; that there was a welding, that the State, so far as


[058-198]sovereignty is concerned, is lost in the Union, and that the people


[058-199]of that State become citizens of the whole country.
