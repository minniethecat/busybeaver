[057-001]I believe that no corporation should be allowed to exist except


[057-002]for the benefit of the whole people.  The Government should always


[057-003]act for the benefit of all, and when the Government gives a part


[057-004]of its power to an aggregation of individuals, the accomplishment


[057-005]of some public good should justify the giving of that power; and


[057-006]whenever a corporation becomes subversive of the very end for which


[057-007]it was created, the Government should put an end to its life.


[057-008]So I believe that after these matters, these issues have been


[057-009]discussed--when something is understood about the effect of a


[057-010]tariff, the effect of protection, the laboring people of this


[057-011]country will be on the side of the Republican party.  The Republican


[057-012]party is always trying to do something--trying to take a step in


[057-013]advance.  Persons who care for nothing except themselves--who wish


[057-014]to make no effort except for themselves--are its natural enemies.


[057-015]_Question_.  What do you think of Mr. Mills' Fourth of July speech


[057-016]on his bill?


[057-017]_Answer_.  Certain allowances should always be made for the Fourth


[057-018]of July.  What Mr. Mills says with regard to free trade depends,


[057-019]I imagine, largely on where he happens to be.  You remember the


[057-020]old story about the _Moniteur_.  When Napoleon escaped from Elba


[057-021]that paper said:  "The ogre has escaped."  And from that moment


[057-022]the epithets grew a little less objectionable as Napoleon advanced,


[057-023]and at last the _Moniteur_ cried out:  "The Emperor has reached


[057-024]Paris."  I hardly believe that Mr. Mills would call his bill in


[057-025]Texas a war tariff measure.  He might commence in New York with


[057-026]that description, but as he went South that language, in my judgment,


[057-027]would change, and when he struck the Brazos I think the bill would


[057-028]be described as the nearest possible approach to free trade.


[057-029]Mr. Mills takes the ground that if raw material comes here free of


[057-030]duty, then we can manufacture that raw material and compete with


[057-031]other countries in the markets of the world--that is to say, under


[057-032]his bill.  Now, other countries can certainly get the raw material


[057-033]as cheaply as we can, especially those countries in which the raw


[057-034]material is raised; and if wages are less in other countries than


[057-035]in ours, the raw material being the same, the product must cost


[057-036]more with us than with them.  Consequently we cannot compete with


[057-037]foreign countries simply by getting the raw material at the same


[057-038]price; we must be able to manufacture it as cheaply as they, and


[057-039]we can do that only by cutting down the wages of the American


[057-040]workingmen.  Because, to have raw material at the same price as


[057-041]other nations, is only a part of the problem.  The other part is


[057-042]how cheaply can we manufacture it?  And that depends upon wages.


[057-043]If wages are twenty-five cents a day, then we can compete with


[057-044]those nations where wages are twenty-five cents a day; but if our


[057-045]wages are five or six times as high, then the twenty-five cent


[057-046]labor will supply the market.  There is no possible way of putting


[057-047]ourselves on an equality with other countries in the markets of


[057-048]the world, except by putting American labor on an equality with


[057-049]the other labor of the world.  Consequently, we cannot obtain a


[057-050]foreign market without lessening our wages.  No proposition can be


[057-051]plainer than this.


[057-052]It cannot be said too often that the real prosperity of a country


[057-053]depends upon the well-being of those who labor.  That country is


[057-054]not prosperous where a few are wealthy and have all the luxuries


[057-055]that the imagination can suggest, and where the millions are in


[057-056]want, clothed in rags, and housed in tenements not fit for wild


[057-057]beasts.  The value of our property depends on the civilization of


[057-058]our people.  If the people are happy and contented, if the workingman


[057-059]receives good wages, then our houses and our farms are valuable.


[057-060]If the people are discontented, if the workingmen are in want, then


[057-061]our property depreciates from day to day, and national bankruptcy


[057-062]will only be a question of time.


[057-063]If Mr. Mills has given a true statement with regard to the measure


[057-064]proposed by him, what relation does that measure bear to the


[057-065]President's message?  What has it to do with the Democratic platform?


[057-066]If Mr. Mills has made no mistake, the President wrote a message


[057-067]substantially in favor of free trade.  The Democratic party ratified


[057-068]and indorsed that message, and at the same time ratified and indorsed


[057-069]the Mills bill.  Now, the message was for free trade, and the Mills


[057-070]bill, according to Mr. Mills, is for the purpose of sustaining the


[057-071]war tariff.  They have either got the wrong child or the wrong


[057-072]parents.


[057-073]_Question_.  I see that some people are objecting to your taking


[057-074]any part in politics, on account of your religious opinion?


[057-075]_Answer_.  The Democratic party has always been pious.  If it is


[057-076]noted for anything it is for its extreme devotion.  You have no


[057-077]idea how many Democrats wear out the toes of their shoes praying.


[057-078]I suppose that in this country there ought to be an absolute divorce


[057-079]between church and state and without any alimony being allowed to


[057-080]the church; and I have always supposed that the Republican party


[057-081]was perfectly willing that anybody should vote its ticket who


[057-082]believed in its principles.  The party was not established, as I


[057-083]understand it, in the interest of any particular denomination; it


[057-084]was established to promote and preserve the freedom of the American


[057-085]citizen everywhere.  Its first object was to prevent the spread of


[057-086]human slavery; its second object was to put down the Rebellion and


[057-087]preserve the Union; its third object was the utter destruction of


[057-088]human slavery everywhere, and its fourth object is to preserve not


[057-089]only the fruit of all that it has won, but to protect American


[057-090]industry to the end that the Republic may not only be free, but


[057-091]prosperous and happy.  In this great work all are invited to join,


[057-092]no matter whether Catholics or Presbyterians or Methodists or


[057-093]Infidels--believers or unbelievers.  The object is to have a majority


[057-094]of the people of the United States in favor of human liberty, in


[057-095]favor of justice and in favor of an intelligent American policy.


[057-096]I am not what is called strictly orthodox, and yet I am liberal


[057-097]enough to vote for a Presbyterian, and if a Presbyterian is not


[057-098]liberal enough to stand by a Republican, no matter what his religious


[057-099]opinions may be, then the Presbyterian is not as liberal as the


[057-100]Republican party, and he is not as liberal as an unbeliever; in


[057-101]other words, he is not a manly man.


[057-102]I object to no man who is running for office on the ticket of my


[057-103]party on account of his religious convictions.  I care nothing


[057-104]about the church of which he is a member.  That is his business.


[057-105]That is an individual matter--something with which the State has


[057-106]no right to interfere--something with which no party can rightfully


[057-107]have anything to do.  These great questions are left open to


[057-108]discussion.  Every church must take its chance in the open field


[057-109]of debate.  No belief has the right to draw the sword--no dogma


[057-110]the right to resort to force.  The moment a church asks for the


[057-111]help of the State, it confesses its weakness, it confesses its


[057-112]inability to answer the arguments against it.


[057-113]I believe in the absolute equality before the law, of all religions


[057-114]and all metaphysical theories; and I would no more control those


[057-115]things by law than I would endeavor to control the arts and the


[057-116]sciences by legislation.  Man admires the beautiful, and what is


[057-117]beautiful to one may not be to another, and this inequality or this


[057-118]difference cannot be regulated by law.


[057-119]The same is true of what is called religious belief.  I am willing


[057-120]to give all others every right that I claim for myself, and if they


[057-121]are not willing to give me the rights they claim for themselves,


[057-122]they are not civilized.


[057-123]No man acknowledges the truth of my opinions because he votes the


[057-124]same ticket that I do, and I certainly do not acknowledge the


[057-125]correctness of the opinions of others because I vote the Republican


[057-126]ticket.  We are Republicans together.  Upon certain political


[057-127]questions we agree, upon other questions we disagree--and that is


[057-128]all.  Only religious people, who have made up their minds to vote


[057-129]the Democratic ticket, will raise an objection of this kind, and


[057-130]they will raise the objection simply as a pretence, simply for the


[057-131]purpose of muddying the water while they escape.


[057-132]Of course there may be some exceptions.  There are a great many


[057-133]insane people out of asylums.  If the Republican party does not


[057-134]stand for absolute intellectual liberty, it had better disband.


[057-135]And why should we take so much pains to free the body, and then


[057-136]enslave the mind?  I believe in giving liberty to both.  Give every


[057-137]man the right to labor, and give him the right to reap the harvest


[057-138]of his toil.  Give every man the right to think, and to reap the


[057-139]harvest of his brain--that is to say, give him the right to express


[057-140]his thoughts.


[057-141]--_New York Press_, July 8, 1888.


[057-142]JAMES G. BLAINE AND POLITICS.


[057-143]_Question_.  I see that there has lately been published a long


[057-144]account of the relations between Mr. Blaine and yourself, and the


[057-145]reason given for your failure to support him for the nomination in


[057-146]1884 and 1888?


[057-147]_Answer_.  Every little while some donkey writes a long article


[057-148]pretending to tell all that happened between Mr. Blaine and myself.


[057-149]I have never seen any article on the subject that contained any


[057-150]truth.  They are always the invention of the writer or of somebody


[057-151]who told him.  The last account is more than usually idiotic.  An


[057-152]unpleasant word has never passed between Mr. Blaine and myself.


[057-153]We have never had any falling out.  I never asked Mr. Blaine's


[057-154]influence for myself.  I never asked President Hayes or Garfield


[057-155]or Arthur for any position whatever, and I have never asked Mr.


[057-156]Cleveland for any appointment under the civil service.


[057-157]With regard to the German Mission, about which so much has been


[057-158]said, all that I ever did in regard to that was to call on Secretary


[057-159]Evarts and inform him that there was no place in the gift of the


[057-160]administration that I would accept.  I could not afford to throw


[057-161]away a good many thousand dollars a year for the sake of an office.


[057-162]So I say again that I never asked, or dreamed of asking, any such


[057-163]favor of Mr. Blaine.  The favors have been exactly the other way--


[057-164]from me, and not from him.  So there is not the slightest truth in


[057-165]the charge that there was some difference between our families.


[057-166]I have great respect for Mrs. Blaine, have always considered her


[057-167]an extremely good and sensible woman; our relations have been of


[057-168]the friendliest character, and such relations have always existed


[057-169]between all the members of both families, so far as I know.  Nothing


[057-170]could be more absurd that the charge that there was some feeling


[057-171]growing out of our social relations.  We do not depend upon others


[057-172]to help us socially; we need no help, and if we did we would not


[057-173]accept it.  The whole story about there having been any lack of


[057-174]politeness or kindness is without the slightest foundation.


[057-175]In 1884 I did not think that Mr. Blaine could be elected.  I thought


[057-176]the same at the Chicago convention this year.  I know that he has


[057-177]a great number of ardent admirers and of exceedingly self-denying


[057-178]and unselfish friends.  I believe that he has more friends than


[057-179]any other man in the Republican party; but he also has very bitter


[057-180]enemies--enemies with influence.  Taking this into consideration,


[057-181]and believing that the success of the party was more important than


[057-182]the success of any individual, I was in favor of nominating some


[057-183]man who would poll the entire Republican vote.  This feeling did


[057-184]not grow out of any hostility to any man, but simply out of a desire


[057-185]for Republican success.  In other words, I endeavored to take an


[057-186]unprejudiced view of the situation.  Under no circumstances would


[057-187]I underrate the ability and influence of Mr. Blaine, nor would I


[057-188]endeavor to deprecate the services he has rendered to the Republican


[057-189]party and to the country.  But by this time it ought to be understood


[057-190]that I belong to no man, that I am the proprietor of myself.
