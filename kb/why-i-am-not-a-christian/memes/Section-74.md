[073-001]_Answer_.  Yes; I can.  Shakespeare died in 1616, and the quarto


[073-002]was published in 1623, seven years after he was dead.  Under these


[073-003]circumstances I think Shakespeare ought to be excused, even by


[073-004]those who attack him with the greatest bitterness, for not having a copy


[073-005]of the book.  There is, however, another side to his.  Bacon did


[073-006]not die until long after the quarto was published.  Did he have a


[073-007]copy?  Did he mention the copy in his will?  Did he ever mention


[073-008]the quarto in any letter, essay, or in any way?  He left a library,


[073-009]was there a copy of the plays in it?  Has there ever been found a


[073-010]line from any play or sonnet in his handwriting?  Bacon left his


[073-011]writings, his papers, all in perfect order, but no plays, no sonnets,


[073-012]said nothing about plays--claimed nothing on their behalf.  This


[073-013]is the other side.  Now, there is still another thing.  The edition


[073-014]of 1623 was published by Shakespeare's friends, Heminge and Condell.


[073-015]They knew him--had been with him for years, and they collected most


[073-016]of his plays and put them in book form.


[073-017]Ben Jonson wrote a preface, in which he placed Shakespeare above


[073-018]all the other poets--declared that he was for all time.


[073-019]The edition of 1623 was gotten up by actors, by the friends and


[073-020]associates of Shakespeare, vouched for by dramatic writers--by


[073-021]those who knew him.  This is enough.


[073-022]_Question_.  How do you explain the figure:  "His soul, like Mazeppa,


[073-023]was lashed naked to the wild horse of every fear and love and hate"?


[073-024]Mr. Donnelly does not understand you.


[073-025]_Answer_.  It hardly seems necessary to explain a thing as simple


[073-026]and plain as that.  Men are carried away by some fierce passion--


[073-027]carried away in spite of themselves as Mazeppa was carried by the


[073-028]wild horse to which he was lashed.  Whether the comparison is good


[073-029]or bad it is at least plain.  Nothing could tempt me to call Mr.


[073-030]Donnelly's veracity in question.  He says that he does not understand


[073-031]the sentence and I most cheerfully admit that he tells the exact


[073-032]truth.


[073-033]_Question_.  Mr. Donnelly says that you said:  "Where there is


[073-034]genius, education seems almost unnecessary," and he denounces your


[073-035]doctrine as the most abominable doctrine ever taught.  What have


[073-036]you to say to that?


[073-037]_Answer_.  In the first place, I never made the remark.  In the


[073-038]next place, it may be well enough to ask what education is.  Much


[073-039]is taught in colleges that is of no earthly use; much is taught


[073-040]that is hurtful.  There are thousands of educated men who never


[073-041]graduated from any college or university.  Every observant, thoughtful


[073-042]man is educating himself as long as he lives.  Men are better then


[073-043]books.  Observation is a great teacher.  A man of talent learns


[073-044]slowly.  He does not readily see the necessary relation that one


[073-045]fact bears to another.  A man of genius, learning one fact, instantly


[073-046]sees hundreds of others.  It is not necessary for such a man to


[073-047]attend college.  The world is his university.  Every man he meets


[073-048]is a book--every woman a volume every fact a torch--and so without


[073-049]the aid of the so-called schools he rises to the very top.


[073-050]Shakespeare was such a man.


[073-051]_Question_.  Mr. Donnelly says that:  "The biggest myth ever on


[073-052]earth was Shakespeare, and that if Francis Bacon had said to the


[073-053]people, I, Francis Bacon, a gentleman of gentlemen, have been taking


[073-054]in secret my share of the coppers and shillings taken at the door


[073-055]of those low playhouses, he would have been ruined.  If he had put


[073-056]the plays forth simply as poetry it would have ruined his legal


[073-057]reputation."  What do you think of this?


[073-058]_Answer_.  I hardly think that Shakespeare was a myth.  He was


[073-059]certainly born, married, lived in London, belonged to a company of


[073-060]actors; went back to Stratford, where he had a family, and died.


[073-061]All these things do not as a rule happen to myths.  In addition to


[073-062]this, those who knew him believed him to be the author of the plays.


[073-063]Bacon's friends never suspected him.  I do not think it would have


[073-064]hurt Bacon to have admitted that he wrote "Lear" and "Othello,"


[073-065]and that he was getting "coppers and shillings" to which he was


[073-066]justly entitled.  Certainly not as much as for him to have written


[073-067]this, which if fact, though not in exact form, he did write:  "I,


[073-068]Francis Bacon, a gentleman of gentlemen, have been taking coppers


[073-069]and shillings to which I was not entitled--but which I received as


[073-070]bribes while sitting as a judge."  He has been excused for two


[073-071]reasons.  First, because his salary was small, and, second, because


[073-072]it was the custom for judges to receive presents.


[073-073]Bacon was a lawyer.  He was charged with corruption--with having


[073-074]taken bribes, with having sold his decisions.  He knew what the


[073-075]custom was and knew how small his salary was.  But he did not plead


[073-076]the custom in his defense.  He did not mention the smallness of


[073-077]the salary.  He confessed that he was guilty--as charged.  His


[073-078]confession was deemed too general and he was called upon by the


[073-079]Lords to make a specific confession.  This he did.  He specified


[073-080]the cases in which he had received the money and told how much,


[073-081]and begged for mercy.  He did not make his confession, as Mr.


[073-082]Donnelly is reported to have said, to get his fine remitted.  The


[073-083]confession was made before the fine was imposed.


[073-084]Neither do I think that the theatre in which the plays of Shakespeare


[073-085]were represented could or should be called a "low play house."


[073-086]The fact that "Othello," "Lear," "Hamlet," "Julius Caesar," and


[073-087]the other great dramas were first played in that playhouse made it


[073-088]the greatest building in the world.  The gods themselves should


[073-089]have occupied seats in that theatre, where for the first time the


[073-090]greatest productions of the human mind were put upon the stage.


[073-091]--_The Tribune_, Minneapolis, Minn., May 31, 1891.


[073-092]GROWING OLD GRACEFULLY, AND PRESBYTERIANISM.


[073-093]_Question_.  How have you acquired the art of growing old


[073-094]gracefully?


[073-095]_Answer_.  It is very hard to live a great while without getting


[073-096]old, and it is hardly worth while to die just to keep young.  It


[073-097]is claimed that people with certain incomes live longer than those


[073-098]who have to earn their bread.  But the income people have a stupid


[073-099]kind of life, and though they may hang on a good many years, they


[073-100]can hardly be said to do much real living.  The best you can say


[073-101]is, not that they lived so many years, but that it took them so


[073-102]many years to die.  Some people imagine that regular habits prolong


[073-103]life, but that depends somewhat on the habits.  Only the other day


[073-104]I read an article written by a physician, in which regular habits


[073-105]--good ones, were declared to be quite dangerous.


[073-106]Where life is perfectly regular, all the wear and tear comes on


[073-107]the same nerves--every blow falls on the same place.  Variety, even


[073-108]in a bad direction, is a great relief.  But living long has nothing


[073-109]to do with getting old gracefully.  Good nature is a great enemy


[073-110]of wrinkles, and cheerfulness helps the complexion.  If we could


[073-111]only keep from being annoyed at little things, it would add to the


[073-112]luxury of living.  Great sorrows are few, and after all do not


[073-113]affect us as much as the many irritating, almost nothings that


[073-114]attack from every side.  The traveler is bothered more with dust


[073-115]than mountains.  It is a great thing to have an object in life--


[073-116]something to work for and think for.  If a man thinks only about


[073-117]himself, his own comfort, his own importance, he will not grow old


[073-118]gracefully.  More and more his spirit, small and mean, will leave


[073-119]its impress on his face, and especially in his eyes.  You look at


[073-120]him and feel that there is no jewel in the casket; that a shriveled


[073-121]soul is living in a tumble-down house.


[073-122]The body gets its grace from the mind.  I suppose that we are all


[073-123]more or less responsible for our looks.  Perhaps the thinker of


[073-124]great thoughts, the doer of noble deeds, moulds his features in


[073-125]harmony with his life.


[073-126]Probably the best medicine, the greatest beautifier in the world,


[073-127]is to make somebody else happy.  I have noticed that good mothers


[073-128]have faces as serene as a cloudless day in June, and the older the


[073-129]serener.  It is a great thing to know the relative importance of


[073-130]things, and those who do, get the most out of life.  Those who take


[073-131]an interest in what they see, and keep their minds busy are always


[073-132]young.


[073-133]The other day I met a blacksmith who has given much attention to


[073-134]geology and fossil remains.  He told me how happy he was in his


[073-135]excursions.  He was nearly seventy years old, and yet he had the


[073-136]enthusiasm of a boy.  He said he had some very fine specimens,


[073-137]"but," said he, "nearly every night I dream of finding perfect


[073-138]ones."


[073-139]That man will keep young as long as he lives.  As long as a man


[073-140]lives he should study.  Death alone has the right to dismiss the


[073-141]school.  No man can get too much knowledge.  In that, he can have


[073-142]all the avarice he wants, but he can get too much property.  If


[073-143]the business men would stop when they got enough, they might have


[073-144]a chance to grow old gracefully.  But the most of them go on and


[073-145]on, until, like the old stage horse, stiff and lame, they drop dead


[073-146]in the road.  The intelligent, the kind, the reasonably contented,


[073-147]the courageous, the self-poised, grow old gracefully.


[073-148]_Question_.  Are not the restraints to free religious thought being


[073-149]worn away, as the world grows older, and will not the recent attacks


[073-150]of the religious press and pulpit upon the unorthodoxy of Dr.


[073-151]Briggs, Rev. R. Heber Newton and the prospective Episcopal bishop


[073-152]of Massachusetts, Dr. Phillips Brooks, and others, have a tendency


[073-153]still further to extend this freedom?


[073-154]_Answer_.  Of course the world is growing somewhat wiser--getting


[073-155]more sense day by day.  It is amazing to me that any human being


[073-156]or beings ever wrote the Presbyterian creed.  Nothing can be more


[073-157]absurd--more barbaric than that creed.  It makes man the sport of


[073-158]an infinite monster, and yet good people, men and women of ability,


[073-159]who have gained eminence in almost every department of human effort,


[073-160]stand by this creed as if it were filled with wisdom and goodness.


[073-161]They really think that a good God damns his poor ignorant children


[073-162]just for his own glory, and that he sends people to perdition, not


[073-163]for any evil in them, but to the praise of his glorious justice.


[073-164]Dr. Briggs has been wicked enough to doubt this phase of God's


[073-165]goodness, and Dr. Bridgman was heartless enough to drop a tear in


[073-166]hell.  Of course they have no idea of what justice really is.


[073-167]The Presbyterian General Assembly that has just adjourned stood by


[073-168]Calvinism.  The "Five Points" are as sharp as ever.  The members


[073-169]of that assembly--most of them--find all their happiness in the


[073-170]"creed."  They need no other amusement.  If they feel blue they


[073-171]read about total depravity--and cheer up.  In moments of great


[073-172]sorrow they think of the tale of non-elect infants, and their hearts


[073-173]overflow with a kind of joy.


[073-174]They cannot imagine why people wish to attend the theatre when they


[073-175]can read the "Confession of Faith," or why they should feel like


[073-176]dancing after they do read it.


[073-177]It is very sad to think of the young men and women who have been


[073-178]eternally ruined by witnessing the plays of Shakespeare, and it is


[073-179]also sad to think of the young people, foolish enough to be happy,


[073-180]keeping time to the pulse of music, waltzing to hell in loving


[073-181]pairs--all for the glory of God, and to the praise of his glorious


[073-182]justice.  I think, too, of the thousands of men and women who, while


[073-183]listening to the music of Wagner, have absolutely forgotten the


[073-184]Presbyterian creed, and who for a little while have been as happy


[073-185]as if the creed had never been written.  Tear down the theatres,


[073-186]burn the opera houses, break all musical instruments, and then let


[073-187]us go to church.
