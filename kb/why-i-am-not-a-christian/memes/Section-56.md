[055-001]_Question_.  What do you think of the Democratic platform?


[055-002]_Answer_.  Mr. Watterson was kind enough to say that before they


[055-003]took the roof off of the house they were going to give the occupants


[055-004]a chance to get out.  By the "house" I suppose he means the great


[055-005]workshop of America.  By the "roof" he means protection; and by


[055-006]the "occupants" the mechanics.  He is not going to turn them out


[055-007]at once, or take the roof off in an instant, but this is to be done


[055-008]gradually.


[055-009]In other words, they will remove it shingle by shingle or tile by


[055-010]tile, until it becomes so leaky or so unsafe that the occupants--


[055-011]that is to say, the mechanics, will leave the building.


[055-012]The first thing in the platform is a reaffirmation of the platform


[055-013]of 1884, and an unqualified endorsement of President Cleveland's


[055-014]message on the tariff.  And if President Cleveland's message has


[055-015]any meaning whatever, it means free trade--not instantly, it may


[055-016]be--but that is the object and the end to be attained.  All his


[055-017]reasoning, if reasoning it can be called, is in favor of absolute


[055-018]free trade.  The issue is fairly made--shall American labor be


[055-019]protected, or must the American laborer take his chances with the


[055-020]labor market of the world?  Must he stand upon an exact par with


[055-021]the laborers of Belgium and England and Germany, not only, but with


[055-022]the slaves and serfs of other countries?  Must he be reduced to


[055-023]the diet of the old country?  Is he to have meat on holidays and


[055-024]a reasonably good dinner on Christmas, and live the rest of the


[055-025]year on crusts, crumbs, scraps, skimmed milk, potatoes, turnips,


[055-026]and a few greens that he can steal from the corners of fences?  Is


[055-027]he to rely for meat, on poaching, and then is he to be transported


[055-028]to some far colony for the crime of catching a rabbit?  Are our


[055-029]workingmen to wear wooden shoes?


[055-030]Now, understand me, I do not believe that the Democrats think that


[055-031]free trade would result in disaster.  Their minds are so constituted


[055-032]that they really believe that free trade would be a great blessing.


[055-033]I am not calling in question their honesty.  I am simply disputing


[055-034]the correctness of their theory.  It makes no difference, as a


[055-035]matter of fact, whether they are honest or dishonest.  Free trade


[055-036]established by honest people would be just as injurious as if


[055-037]established by dishonest people.  So there is no necessity of


[055-038]raising the question of intention.  Consequently, I admit that they


[055-039]are doing the best they know now.  This is not admitting much, but


[055-040]it is something, as it tends to take from the discussion all ill


[055-041]feeling.


[055-042]We all know that the tariff protects special interests in particular


[055-043]States.  Louisiana is not for free trade.  It may be for free trade


[055-044]in everything except sugar.  It is willing that the rest of the


[055-045]country should pay an additional cent or two a pound on sugar for


[055-046]its benefit, and while receiving the benefit it does not wish to


[055-047]bear its part of the burden.  If the other States protect the sugar


[055-048]interests in Louisiana, certainly that State ought to be willing


[055-049]to protect the wool interest in Ohio, the lead and hemp interest


[055-050]in Missouri, the lead and wool interest in Colorado, the lumber


[055-051]interest in Minnesota, the salt and lumber interest in Michigan,


[055-052]the iron interest in Pennsylvania, and so I might go on with a list


[055-053]of the States--because each one has something that it wishes to


[055-054]have protected.


[055-055]It sounds a little strange to hear a Democratic convention cry out


[055-056]that the party "is in favor of the maintenance of an indissoluble


[055-057]union of free and indestructible States."  Only a little while ago


[055-058]the Democratic party regarded it as the height of tyranny to coerce


[055-059]a free State.  Can it be said that a State is "free" that is


[055-060]absolutely governed by the Nation?  Is a State free that can make


[055-061]no treaty with any other State or country--that is not permitted


[055-062]to coin money or to declare war?  Why should such a State be called


[055-063]free?  The truth is that the States are not free in that sense.


[055-064]The Republican party believes that this is a Nation and that the


[055-065]national power is the highest, and that every citizen owes the


[055-066]highest allegiance to the General Government and not to his State.


[055-067]In other words, we are not Virginians or Mississippians or Delawareans


[055-068]--we are Americans.  The great Republic is a free Nation, and the


[055-069]States are but parts of that Nation.  The doctrine of State


[055-070]Sovereignty was born of the institution of slavery.  In the history


[055-071]of our country, whenever anything wrong was to be done, this doctrine


[055-072]of State Sovereignty was appealed to.  It protected the slave-trade


[055-073]until the year 1808.  It passed the Fugitive Slave Law.  It made


[055-074]every citizen in the North a catcher of his fellow-man--made it


[055-075]the duty of free people to enslave others.  This doctrine of State


[055-076]Rights was appealed to for the purpose of polluting the Territories


[055-077]with the institution of slavery.  To deprive a man of his liberty,


[055-078]to put him back into slavery, State lines were instantly obliterated;


[055-079]but whenever the Government wanted to protect one of its citizens


[055-080]from outrage, then the State lines became impassable barriers, and


[055-081]the sword of justice fell in twain across the line of a State.


[055-082]People forget that the National Government is the creature of the


[055-083]people.  The real sovereign is the people themselves.  Presidents


[055-084]and congressmen and judges are the creatures of the people.  If we


[055-085]had a governing class--if men were presidents or senators by virtue


[055-086]of birth--then we might talk about the danger of centralization;


[055-087]but if the people are sufficiently intelligent to govern themselves,


[055-088]they will never create a government for the destruction of their


[055-089]liberties, and they are just as able to protect their rights in


[055-090]the General Government as they are in the States.  If you say that


[055-091]the sovereignty of the State protects labor, you might as well say


[055-092]that the sovereignty of the county protects labor in the State and


[055-093]that the sovereignty of the town protects labor in the county.


[055-094]Of all subjects in the world the Democratic party should avoid


[055-095]speaking of "a critical period of our financial affairs, resulting


[055-096]from over taxation."  How did taxation become necessary?  Who


[055-097]created the vast debt that American labor must pay?  Who made this


[055-098]taxation of thousands of millions necessary?  Why were the greenbacks


[055-099]issued?  Why were the bonds sold?  Who brought about "a critical


[055-100]period of our financial affairs"?  How has the Democratic party


[055-101]"averted disaster"?  How could there be a disaster with a vast


[055-102]surplus in the treasury?  Can you find in the graveyard of nations


[055-103]this epitaph:  "Died of a Surplus"?  Has any nation ever been known


[055-104]to perish because it had too much gold and too much silver, and


[055-105]because its credit was better than that of any other nation on the


[055-106]earth?  The Democrats seem to think--and it is greatly to their


[055-107]credit--that they have prevented the destruction of the Government


[055-108]when the treasury was full--when the vaults were overflowing.  What


[055-109]would they have done had the vaults been empty?  Let them wrestle


[055-110]with the question of poverty; let them then see how the Democratic


[055-111]party would succeed.  When it is necessary to create credit, to


[055-112]inspire confidence, not only in our own people, but in the nations


[055-113]of the world--which of the parties is best adapted for the task?


[055-114]The Democratic party congratulates itself that it has not been


[055-115]ruined by a Republican surplus!  What good boys we are!  We have


[055-116]not been able to throw away our legacy!


[055-117]Is it not a little curious that the convention plumed itself on


[055-118]having paid out more for pensions and bounties to the soldiers and


[055-119]sailors of the Republic than was ever paid before during an equal


[055-120]period?  It goes wild in its pretended enthusiasm for the President


[055-121]who has vetoed more pension bills than all the other Presidents put


[055-122]together.


[055-123]The platform informs us that "the Democratic party has adopted and


[055-124]consistently pursued and affirmed a prudent foreign policy, preserving


[055-125]peace with all nations."  Does it point with pride to the Mexican


[055-126]fiasco, or does it rely entirely upon the great fishery triumph?


[055-127]What has the administration done--what has it accomplished in the


[055-128]field of diplomacy?


[055-129]When we come to civil service, about how many Federal officials


[055-130]were at the St. Louis convention?  About how many have taken part


[055-131]in the recent nominations?  In other words, who has been idle?


[055-132]We have recently been told that the wages of workingmen are just


[055-133]as high in the old country as in this, when you take into consideration


[055-134]the cost of living.  We have always been told by all the free trade


[055-135]papers and orators, that the tariff has no bearing whatever upon


[055-136]wages, and yet, the Democrats have not succeeded in convincing


[055-137]themselves.  I find in their platform this language:  "A fair and


[055-138]careful revision of our tax laws, with due allowance for the


[055-139]difference between the wages of American and foreign labor, must


[055-140]promote and encourage every branch of such industries and enterprises


[055-141]by giving them the assurance of an extended market and steady and


[055-142]continuous operations."


[055-143]It would seem from this that the Democratic party admits that wages


[055-144]are higher here than in foreign countries.  Certainly they do not


[055-145]mean to say that they are lower.  If they are higher here than in


[055-146]foreign countries, the question arises, why are they higher?  If


[055-147]you took off the tariff, the presumption is that they would be as


[055-148]low here as anywhere else, because this very Democratic convention


[055-149]says:  "A fair and careful revision of our tax laws, with due


[055-150]allowance for the difference between wages."  In other words, they


[055-151]would keep tariff enough on to protect our workingmen from the low


[055-152]wages of the foreigner--consequently, we have the admission of the


[055-153]Democratic party that in order to keep wages in this country higher


[055-154]than they are in Belgium, in Italy, in England and in Germany, we


[055-155]must protect home labor.  Then follows the _non sequitur_, which


[055-156]is a Democratic earmark.  They tell us that by keeping a tariff,


[055-157]"making due allowance for the difference between wages, all the


[055-158]industries and enterprises would be encouraged and promoted by


[055-159]giving them the assurance of an extended market."  What does the


[055-160]word "extended" mean?  If it means anything, it means a market in


[055-161]other countries.  In other words, we will put the tariff so low


[055-162]that the wages of American workingmen will be so low that he can


[055-163]compete with the laborers of other countries; otherwise his market


[055-164]could not be "extended."  What does this mean?  There is evidently


[055-165]a lack of thought here.  The two things cannot be accomplished in


[055-166]that way.  If the tariff raises American wages, the American cannot


[055-167]compete in foreign markets with the men who work for half the price.


[055-168]What may be the final result is another question.  American industry


[055-169]properly protected, American genius properly fostered, may invent


[055-170]ways and means--such wonderful machinery, such quick, inexpensive


[055-171]processes, that in time American genius may produce at a less rate


[055-172]than any other country, for the reason that the laborers of other


[055-173]countries will not be as intelligent, will not be as independent,


[055-174]will not have the same ambition.


[055-175]Fine phrases will not deceive the people of this country.  The


[055-176]American mechanic already has a market of sixty millions of people,


[055-177]and, as I said before, the best market in the world.  This country


[055-178]is now so rich, so prosperous, that it is the greatest market of


[055-179]the earth, even for luxuries.  It is the best market for pictures,


[055-180]for works of art.  It is the best market for music and song.  It


[055-181]is the best market for dramatic genius, and it is the best market


[055-182]for skilled labor, the best market for common labor, and in this


[055-183]country the poor man to-day has the best chance--he can look forward


[055-184]to becoming the proprietor of a home, of some land, to independence,


[055-185]to respectability, and to an old age without want and without


[055-186]disgrace.
