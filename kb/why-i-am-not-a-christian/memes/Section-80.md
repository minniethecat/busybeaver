[079-001]--_The News_, Indianapolis, Indiana, February 18, 1892.


[079-002]PERSONAL MAGNETISM AND THE SUNDAY QUESTION.


[079-003][Colonel Ingersoll was a picturesque figure as he sat in his


[079-004]room at the Gibson House yesterday, while the balmy May


[079-005]breeze blew through the open windows, fluttered the lace


[079-006]curtains and tossed the great Infidel's snowy hair to and


[079-007]fro.  The Colonel had come in from New York during the


[079-008]morning and the keen white sunlight of a lovely May day


[079-009]filled his heart with gladness.  After breakfast, the man


[079-010]who preaches the doctrine of the Golden Rule and the Gospel


[079-011]of Humanity and the while chaffs the gentlemen of the


[079-012]clerical profession, was in a fine humor.  He was busy with


[079-013]cards and callers, but not too busy to admire the vase full


[079-014]of freshly-picked spring flowers that stood on the mantel,


[079-015]and wrestled with clouds of cigar smoke, to see which


[079-016]fragrance should dominate the atmosphere.


[079-017]To a reporter of _The Commercial Gazette_, the Colonel spoke


[079-018]freely and interestingly upon a variety of subjects, from


[079-019]personal magnetism in politics to mob rule in Tennessee.  He


[079-020]had been interested in Colonel Weir's statement about the


[079-021]lack of gas in Exposition Hall, at the 1876 convention, and


[079-022]when asked if he believed there was any truth in the stories


[079-023]that the gas supply had been manipulated so as to prevent


[079-024]the taking of a ballot after he had placed James G. Blaine


[079-025]in nomination, he replied:  ]


[079-026]All I can say is, that I heard such a story the day after the


[079-027]convention, but I do not know whether or not it is true.  I have


[079-028]always believed, that if a vote had been taken that evening, Blaine


[079-029]would have been nominated, possibly not as the effect of my speech,


[079-030]but the night gave time for trafficking, and that is always dangerous


[079-031]in a convention.  I believed then that Blaine ought to have been


[079-032]nominated, and that it would have been a very wise thing for the


[079-033]party to have done.  That he was not the candidate was due partly


[079-034]to accident and partly to political traffic, but that is one of


[079-035]the bygones, and I believe there is an old saying to the effect


[079-036]that even the gods have no mastery over the past.


[079-037]_Question_.  Do you think that eloquence is potent in a convention


[079-038]to set aside the practical work of politics and politicians?


[079-039]_Answer_.  I think that all the eloquence in the world cannot affect


[079-040]a trade if the parties to the contract stand firm, and when people


[079-041]have made a political trade they are not the kind of people to be


[079-042]affected by eloquence.  The practical work of the world has very


[079-043]little to do with eloquence.  There are a great many thousand stone


[079-044]masons to one sculptor, and houses and walls are not constructed


[079-045]by sculptors, but by masons.  The daily wants of the world are


[079-046]supplied by the practical workers, by men of talent, not by men of


[079-047]genius, although in the world of invention, genius has done more,


[079-048]it may be, than the workers themselves.  I fancy the machinery now


[079-049]in the world does the work of many hundreds of millions; that there


[079-050]is machinery enough now to do several times the work that could be


[079-051]done by all the men, women and children of the earth.  The genius


[079-052]who invented the reaper did more work and will do more work in the


[079-053]harvest field than thousands of millions of men, and the same may


[079-054]be said of the great engines that drive the locomotives and the


[079-055]ships.  All these marvelous machines were made by men of genius,


[079-056]but they are not the men who in fact do the work.


[079-057][This led the Colonel to pay a brilliant tribute to the great


[079-058]orators of ancient and modern times, the peer of all of them being


[079-059]Cicero.  He dissected and defined oratory and eloquence, and


[079-060]explained with picturesque figures, wherein the difference between


[079-061]them lay.  As he mentioned the magnetism of public speakers, he was


[079-062]asked as to his opinion of the value of personal magnetism in


[079-063]political life.]


[079-064]It may be difficult to define what personal magnetism is, but I


[079-065]think it may be defined in this way:  You don't always feel like


[079-066]asking a man whom you meet on the street what direction you should


[079-067]take to reach a certain point.  You often allow three or four to


[079-068]pass, before you meet one who seems to invite the question.  So,


[079-069]too, there are men by whose side you may sit for hours in the cars


[079-070]without venturing a remark as to the weather, and there are others


[079-071]to whom you will commence talking the moment you sit down.  There


[079-072]are some men who look as if they would grant a favor, men toward


[079-073]whom you are unconsciously drawn, men who have a real human look,


[079-074]men with whom you seem to be acquainted almost before you speak,


[079-075]and that you really like before you know anything about them.  It


[079-076]may be that we are all electric batteries; that we have our positive


[079-077]and our negative poles; it may be that we need some influence that


[079-078]certain others impart, and it may be that certain others have that


[079-079]which we do not need and which we do not want, and the moment you


[079-080]think that, you feel annoyed and hesitate, and uncomfortable, and


[079-081]possibly hateful.


[079-082]I suppose there is a physical basis for everything.  Possibly the


[079-083]best test of real affection between man and woman, or of real


[079-084]friendship between man and woman, is that they can sit side by


[079-085]side, for hours maybe, without speaking, and yet be having a really


[079-086]social time, each feeling that the other knows exactly what they


[079-087]are thinking about.  Now, the man you meet and whom you would not


[079-088]hesitate a moment to ask a favor of, is what I call a magnetic man.


[079-089]This magnetism, or whatever it may be, assists in making friends,


[079-090]and of course is a great help to any one who deals with the public.


[079-091]Men like a magnetic man even without knowing him, perhaps simply


[079-092]having seen him.  There are other men, whom the moment you shake


[079-093]hands with them, you feel you want no more; you have had enough.


[079-094]A sudden chill runs up the arm the moment your hand touches theirs,


[079-095]and finally reaches the heart; you feel, if you had held that hand


[079-096]a moment longer, an icicle would have formed in the brain.  Such


[079-097]people lack personal magnetism.  These people now and then thaw


[079-098]out when you get thoroughly acquainted with them, and you find that


[079-099]the ice is all on the outside, and then you come to like them very


[079-100]well, but as a rule first impressions are lasting.  Magnetism is


[079-101]what you might call the climate of a man.  Some men, and some women,


[079-102]look like a perfect June day, and there are others who, while the


[079-103]look quite smiling, yet you feel that the sky is becoming overcast,


[079-104]and the signs all point to an early storm.  There are people who


[079-105]are autumnal--that is to say, generous.  They have had their harvest,


[079-106]and have plenty to spare.  Others look like the end of an exceedingly


[079-107]hard winter--between the hay and grass, the hay mostly gone and


[079-108]the grass not yet come up.  So you will see that I think a great


[079-109]deal of this thing that is called magnetism.  As I said, there are


[079-110]good people who are not magnetic, but I do not care to make an


[079-111]Arctic expedition for the purpose of discovering the north pole of


[079-112]their character.  I would rather stay with those who make me feel


[079-113]comfortable at the first.


[079-114][From personal magnetism to the lynching Saturday morning down at


[079-115]Nashville, Tennessee, was a far cry, but when Colonel Ingersoll


[079-116]was asked what he thought of mob law, whether there was any


[079-117]extenuation, any propriety and moral effect resultant from it, he


[079-118]quickly answered:  ]


[079-119]I do not believe in mob law at any time, among any people.  I


[079-120]believe in justice being meted out in accordance with the forms of


[079-121]law.  If a community violates that law, why should not the individual?


[079-122]The example is bad.  Besides all that, no punishment inflicted by


[079-123]a mob tends to prevent the commission of crime.  Horrible punishment


[079-124]hardens the community, and that in itself produces more crime.


[079-125]There seems to be a sort of fascination in frightful punishments,


[079-126]but, to say the least of it, all these things demoralize the


[079-127]community.  In some countries, you know, they whip people for petty


[079-128]offences.  The whipping, however, does no good, and on the other


[079-129]hand it does harm; it hardens those who administer the punishment


[079-130]and those who witness it, and it degrades those who receive it.


[079-131]There will be but little charity in the world, and but little


[079-132]progress until men see clearly that there is no chance in the world


[079-133]of conduct any more than in the physical world.


[079-134]Back of every act and dream and thought and desire and virtue and


[079-135]crime is the efficient cause.  If you wish to change mankind, you


[079-136]must change the conditions.  There should be no such thing as


[079-137]punishment.  We should endeavor to reform men, and those who cannot


[079-138]be reformed should be placed where they cannot injure their fellows.


[079-139]The State should never take revenge any more than the community


[079-140]should form itself into a mob and take revenge.  This does harm,


[079-141]not good.  The time will come when the world will no more think of


[079-142]sending men to the penitentiary for stealing, as a punishment, that


[079-143]it will for sending a man to the penitentiary because he has


[079-144]consumption.  When that time comes, the object will be to reform


[079-145]men; to prevent crime instead of punishing it, and the object then


[079-146]will be to make the conditions such that honest people will be the


[079-147]result, but as long as hundreds of thousands of human beings live


[079-148]in tenements, as long as babes are raised in gutters, as long as


[079-149]competition is so sharp that hundreds of thousands must of necessity


[079-150]be failures, just so long as society gets down on its knees before


[079-151]the great and successful thieves, before the millionaire thieves,


[079-152]just so long will it have to fill the jails and prisons with the


[079-153]little thieves.  When the "good time" comes, men will not be judged


[079-154]by the money they have accumulated, but by the uses they make of


[079-155]it.  So men will be judged, not according to their intelligence,


[079-156]but by what they are endeavoring to accomplish with their intelligence.


[079-157]In other words, the time will come when character will rise above


[079-158]all.  There is a great line in Shakespeare that I have often quoted,


[079-159]and that cannot be quoted too often:  "There is no darkness but


[079-160]ignorance."  Let the world set itself to work to dissipate this


[079-161]darkness; let us flood the world with intellectual light.  This


[079-162]cannot be accomplished by mobs or lynchers.  It must be done by


[079-163]the noblest, by the greatest, and by the best.


[079-164][The conversation shifting around to the Sunday question; the


[079-165]opening of the World's Fair on Sunday, the attacks of the pulpit


[079-166]upon the Sunday newspapers, the opening of parks and museums and


[079-167]libraries on Sunday, Colonel Ingersoll waxed eloquent, and in answer


[079-168]to many questions uttered these paragraphs:  ]


[079-169]Of course, people will think that I have some prejudice against


[079-170]the parsons, but really I think the newspaper press is of far more


[079-171]importance in the world than the pulpit.  If I should admit in a


[079-172]kind of burst of generosity, and simply for the sake of making a


[079-173]point, that the pulpit can do some good, how much can it do without


[079-174]the aid of the press?  Here is a parson preaching to a few ladies


[079-175]and enough men, it may be, to pass the contribution box, and all


[079-176]he says dies within the four walls of that church.  How many


[079-177]ministers would it take to reform the world, provided I again admit


[079-178]in a burst of generosity, that there is any reforming power in what


[079-179]they preach, working along that line?
