The [[resurrection]] of Jesus Christ — the claim that a man executed by Roman authorities rose from the dead and ascended into heaven — is the central miracle of [[christianity|Christian]] theology. The Apostle Paul declared that "if Christ has not been raised, your faith is futile" (1 Corinthians 15:17). For [[robert-ingersoll|Robert G. Ingersoll]], this made the resurrection the ideal test case for his epistemology: if [[christianity|Christianity]]'s foundational claim could not withstand rational scrutiny, the entire edifice collapsed. His forensic examination of the resurrection evidence became one of his most effective public arguments for [[freethought|freethought]].

## Ingersoll's Forensic Analysis

Ingersoll approached the resurrection accounts not as a theologian but as a lawyer evaluating witness testimony. He noted that the gospel narratives — the sole sources for the resurrection claim — were written decades after the alleged event by unknown authors who were not themselves eyewitnesses:

> Certainly none of the gospels were written until after his resurrection; and if he made that doctrine known after his resurrection, and before his ascension, it should have been in the gospels. It is not. [005-015, 005-016, 005-017]

This was a precise legal observation: if the risen Christ had forty days between resurrection and ascension to instruct his disciples, why did none of the gospel writers record his explanation of the [[trinity]] or any other developed Christian doctrine? The silence, Ingersoll argued, was evidence of later theological invention.

His conclusion was characteristically blunt:

> There is not a particle of evidence of the resurrection and ascension of Jesus Christ. [019-050]

## The Evidence Problem

Ingersoll identified several specific failures in the resurrection evidence:

1. **No contemporary accounts**: The earliest written gospel (Mark) dates to approximately 70 CE — forty years after the alleged events. No Roman, Greek, or Jewish source from the 30s CE mentions a resurrection.
2. **Conflicting gospel narratives**: The four gospels disagree on who visited the tomb, what they found there, and to whom the risen Jesus first appeared.
3. **Failure to appear to hostile witnesses**: Ingersoll noted that the risen Christ appeared only to his followers, never to Pilate, Caiaphas, or any neutral or hostile observer who might have recorded the event independently.
4. **The wrong audience**: "He should have shown himself to the Pharisees and the Romans" [019-057] — to the very people who had demanded his execution and could have verified the miracle.

## The Bodily Resurrection

When questioned directly about the doctrine, Ingersoll did not soften his position:

> I do not believe in the resurrection of the body. It seems to me an impossibility. [082-106]

He approached the question from a materialist perspective: if the atoms that compose a human body are recycled through the ecosystem — becoming part of plants, animals, and other humans — the literal reassembly of each individual's body at the end of time was not merely improbable but physically incoherent:

> If the atoms are given to the first men who used them, to whom shall they belong at the resurrection? [082-126]

| Evidentiary Problem | Ingersoll's Observation |
|--------------------|-----------------------|
| Late authorship | Gospels written decades after events |
| No independent corroboration | No Roman or Jewish sources confirm |
| Internal contradictions | Gospel accounts disagree on details |
| Wrong audience | Appeared only to followers, never to skeptics |
| Physical impossibility | Atoms recycled through ecosystem |

## Significance for Freethought

The resurrection debate encapsulated Ingersoll's broader method: take [[christianity|Christianity]]'s most important claim, apply the same standards of evidence one would use in a courtroom or a laboratory, and observe that it fails. If the resurrection — the very linchpin of Christian faith — could not survive rational examination, then [[reason]] was a more reliable guide to [[truth]] than revelation.

The resurrection also illustrated Ingersoll's distinction between hope and knowledge. He did not mock the [[immortality|hope for life after death]]; he insisted only that hope should not be confused with demonstrated [[truth|fact]]. A doctrine accepted on faith was not a truth discovered but a wish indulged.
