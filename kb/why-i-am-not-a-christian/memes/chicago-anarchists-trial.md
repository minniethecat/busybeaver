# Chicago Anarchists Trial

The trial of the Chicago anarchists — often referred to as the Haymarket trial — was one of the most controversial legal proceedings in 19th-century America. Following the Haymarket bombing of 1886, eight men were tried and convicted, with several sentenced to death. [[robert-ingersoll|Robert G. Ingersoll]], an experienced trial lawyer, offered a sharp critique of the fairness of the proceedings.

## Ingersoll's Assessment of the Trial

When asked for his opinion of "the trial of the Chicago Anarchists and their chances for a new trial," Ingersoll responded with lawyerly precision [047-042]. He did not defend the anarchists' political philosophy — he explicitly stated that he did "not uphold the Anarchists" or their methods [047-094]. However, he insisted that even the most unpopular defendants deserved due process.

Ingersoll argued that the trial had been tainted by public hysteria. He observed that "there never was a trial as shamelessly conducted" as this one had been in certain respects [027-048]. The presiding judge, in his view, had failed in the fundamental duty of impartiality:

> A judge should either sit between the parties as an impartial arbiter, or he should not sit at all. [047-051]

He questioned "whether these men had a fair trial" and suggested that the atmosphere of fear surrounding anarchism had poisoned the proceedings [047-064].

## Criticism of the Verdict

Ingersoll's primary concern was procedural. He believed that "some of the rulings on the trial of the Anarchists" were legally indefensible [049-011]. The defendants were convicted not for acts they had committed but for ideas they had expressed — a precedent that Ingersoll found deeply troubling for a free society.

He noted that "the worst form of anarchy is" the government's own lawlessness when it abandons fair procedure in the name of public safety [049-005]. The trial, in his analysis, illustrated the danger of allowing fear to override the protections that the legal system was designed to provide.

## Legacy and Principle

Ingersoll's defense of the anarchists' right to a fair trial was consistent with his broader commitment to free expression. He had spent his career defending unpopular views, and he recognized that the right to a fair hearing applied equally to those whose ideas he found abhorrent. The trial of the Chicago anarchists served as a test case for the principle that justice must be blind to popular sentiment.
