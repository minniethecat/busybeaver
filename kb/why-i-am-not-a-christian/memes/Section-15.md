[014-001]The churches were all open on that Sunday, and all could go who


[014-002]desired.  Yet they were not full, and the pews were nearly as empty


[014-003]of people as the pulpit of ideas.  The truth is, the story is


[014-004]growing old, the ideas somewhat moss-covered, and everything has


[014-005]a wrinkled and withered appearance.  This gentleman says that these


[014-006]people went to hear their Maker cursed and their Savior ridiculed.


[014-007]Is it possible that in a city where so many steeples pierce the


[014-008]air, and hundreds of sermons are preached every Sunday, there are


[014-009]three thousand men, and a few women, so anxious to hear "their


[014-010]Maker cursed and their Savior ridiculed" that they are willing to


[014-011]pay a dollar each?  The gentleman knew that nobody cursed anybody's


[014-012]Maker.  He knew that the statement was utterly false and without


[014-013]the slightest foundation.  He also knew that nobody had ridiculed


[014-014]the Savior of anybody, but, on the contrary, that I had paid a


[014-015]greater tribute to the character of Jesus Christ than any minister


[014-016]in New York has the capacity to do.  Certainly it is not cursing


[014-017]the Maker of anybody to say that the God described in the Old


[014-018]Testament is not the real God.  Certainly it is not cursing God to


[014-019]declare that the real God never sanctioned slavery or polygamy, or


[014-020]commanded wars of extermination, or told a husband to separate from


[014-021]his wife if she differed with him in religion.  The people who say


[014-022]these things of God--if there is any God at all--do what little


[014-023]there is in their power, unwittingly of course, to destroy his


[014-024]reputation.  But I have done something to rescue the reputation of


[014-025]the Deity from the slanders of the pulpit.  If there is any God,


[014-026]I expect to find myself credited on the heavenly books for my


[014-027]defence of him.  I did say that our civilization is due not to


[014-028]piety, but to Infidelity.  I did say that every great reformer had


[014-029]been denounced as an Infidel in his day and generation.  I did say


[014-030]that Christ was an Infidel, and that he was treated in his day very


[014-031]much as the orthodox preachers treat an honest man now.  I did say


[014-032]that he was tried for blasphemy and crucified by bigots.  I did


[014-033]say that he hated and despised the church of his time, and that he


[014-034]denounced the most pious people of Jerusalem as thieves and vipers.


[014-035]And I suggested that should he come again he might have occasion


[014-036]to repeat the remarks that he then made.  At the same time I admitted


[014-037]that there are thousands and thousands of Christians who are


[014-038]exceedingly good people.  I never did pretend that the fact that


[014-039]a man was a Christian even tended to show that he was a bad man.


[014-040]Neither have I ever insisted that the fact that a man is an Infidel


[014-041]even tends to show what, in other respects, his character is.  But


[014-042]I always have said, and I always expect to say, that a Christian


[014-043]who does not believe in absolute intellectual liberty is a curse


[014-044]to mankind, and that an Infidel who does believe in absolute


[014-045]intellectual liberty is a blessing to this world.  We cannot expect


[014-046]all Infidels to be good, nor all Christians to be bad, and we might


[014-047]make some mistakes even if we selected these people ourselves.  It


[014-048]is admitted by the Christians that Christ made a great mistake when


[014-049]he selected Judas.  This was a mistake of over eight per cent.


[014-050]Chaplain Newman takes pains to compare some great Christians with


[014-051]some great Infidels.  He compares Washington with Julian, and


[014-052]insists, I suppose, that Washington was a great Christian.  Certainly


[014-053]he is not very familiar with the history of Washington, or he never


[014-054]would claim that he was particularly distinguished in his day for


[014-055]what is generally known as vital piety.  That he went through the


[014-056]ordinary forms of Christianity nobody disputes.  That he listened


[014-057]to sermons without paying any particular attention to them, no one


[014-058]will deny.  Julian, of course, was somewhat prejudiced against


[014-059]Christianity, but that he was one of the greatest men of antiquity


[014-060]no one acquainted with the history of Rome can honestly dispute.


[014-061]When he was made emperor he found at the palace hundreds of gentlemen


[014-062]who acted as barbers, hair-combers, and brushers for the emperor.


[014-063]He dismissed them all, remarking that he was able to wash himself.


[014-064]These dismissed office-holders started the story that he was dirty


[014-065]in his habits, and a minister of the nineteenth century was found


[014-066]silly enough to believe the story.  Another thing that probably


[014-067]got him into disrepute in that day, he had no private chaplains.


[014-068]As a matter of fact, Julian was forced to pretend that he was a


[014-069]Christian in order to save his life.  The Christians of that day


[014-070]were of such a loving nature that any man who differed with them


[014-071]was forced to either fall a victim to their ferocity or seek safety


[014-072]in subterfuge.  The real crime that Julian committed, and the only


[014-073]one that has burned itself into the very heart and conscience of


[014-074]the Christian world, is, that he transferred the revenues of the


[014-075]Christian churches to heathen priests.  Whoever stands between a


[014-076]priest and his salary will find that he has committed the unpardonable


[014-077]sin commonly known as the sin against the Holy Ghost.


[014-078]This gentleman also compares Luther with Voltaire.  If he will read


[014-079]the life of Luther by Lord Brougham, he will find that in his


[014-080]ordinary conversation he was exceedingly low and vulgar, and that


[014-081]no respectable English publisher could be found who would soil


[014-082]paper with the translation.  If he will take the pains to read an


[014-083]essay by Macaulay, he will find that twenty years after the death


[014-084]of Luther there were more Catholics than when he was born.  And


[014-085]that twenty years after the death of Voltaire there were millions


[014-086]less than when he was born.  If he will take just a few moments to


[014-087]think, he will find that the last victory of Protestantism was in


[014-088]Holland; that there has never been one since, and will never be


[014-089]another.  If he would really like to think, and enjoy for a few


[014-090]moments the luxury of having an idea, let him ponder for a little


[014-091]while over the instructive fact that languages having their root


[014-092]in the Latin have generally been spoken in Catholic countries, and


[014-093]that those languages having their root in the ancient German are


[014-094]now mostly spoken by people of Protestant proclivities.  It may


[014-095]occur to him, after thinking of this a while, that there is something


[014-096]deeper in the question than he has as yet perceived.  Luther's last


[014-097]victory, as I said before, was in Holland; but the victory of


[014-098]Voltaire goes on from day to day.  Protestantism is not holding


[014-099]its own with Catholicism, even in the United States.  I saw the


[014-100]other day the statistics, I believe, of the city of Chicago, showing


[014-101]that, while the city had increased two or three hundred per cent.,


[014-102]Protestantism had lagged behind at the rate of twelve per cent.


[014-103]I am willing for one, to have the whole question depend upon a


[014-104]comparison of the worth and work of Voltaire and Luther.  It may


[014-105]be, too, that the gentleman forgot to tell us that Luther himself


[014-106]gave consent to a person high in office to have two wives, but


[014-107]prudently suggested to him that he had better keep it as still as


[014-108]possible.  Luther was, also, a believer in a personal Devil.  He


[014-109]thought that deformed children had been begotten by an evil spirit.


[014-110]On one occasion he told a mother that, in his judgment, she had


[014-111]better drown her child; that he had no doubt that the Devil was


[014-112]its father.  This same Luther made this observation:  "Universal


[014-113]toleration is universal error, and universal error is universal


[014-114]hell."  From this you will see that he was an exceedingly good man,


[014-115]but mistaken upon many questions.  So, too, he laughed at the


[014-116]Copernican system, and wanted to know if those fool astronomers


[014-117]could undo the work of God.  He probably knew as little about


[014-118]science as the reverend gentleman does about history.


[014-119]_Question_.  Does he compare any other Infidels with Christians?


[014-120]_Answer_.  Oh, yes; he compares Lord Bacon with Diderot.  I have


[014-121]never claimed that Diderot was a saint.  I have simply insisted


[014-122]that he was a great man; that he was grand enough to say that


[014-123]"incredulity is the beginning of philosophy;" that he had sense


[014-124]enough to know that the God described by the Catholics and Protestants


[014-125]of his day was simply an impossible monster; and that he also had


[014-126]the brain to see that the little selfish heaven occupied by a few


[014-127]monks and nuns and idiots they had fleeced, was hardly worth going


[014-128]to; in other words, that he was a man of common sense, greatly in


[014-129]advance of his time, and that he did what he could to increase the


[014-130]sum of human enjoyment to the end that there might be more happiness


[014-131]in this world.


[014-132]The gentleman compares him with Lord Bacon, and yet, if he will


[014-133]read the trials of that day--I think in the year 1620--he will find


[014-134]that the Christian Lord Bacon, the pious Lord Bacon, was charged


[014-135]with receiving pay for his opinions, and, in some instances, pay


[014-136]from both sides; that the Christian Lord Bacon, at first upon his


[014-137]honor as a Christian lord, denied the whole business; that afterward


[014-138]the Christian Lord Bacon, upon his honor as a Christian lord,


[014-139]admitted the truth of the whole business, and that, therefore, the


[014-140]Christian Lord Bacon was convicted and sentenced to pay a fine of


[014-141]forty thousand pounds, and rendered infamous and incapable of


[014-142]holding any office.  Now, understand me, I do not think Bacon took


[014-143]bribes because he was a Christian, because there have been many


[014-144]Christian judges perfectly honest; but, if the statement of the


[014-145]reverend gentlemen of New York is true, his being a Christian did


[014-146]not prevent his taking bribes.  And right here allow me to thank


[014-147]the gentleman with all my heart for having spoken of Lord Bacon in


[014-148]this connection.  I have always admired the genius of Bacon, and


[014-149]have always thought of his fall with an aching heart, and would


[014-150]not now have spoken of his crime had not his character been flung


[014-151]in my face by a gentleman who asks his God to kill me for having


[014-152]expressed my honest thought.


[014-153]The same gentleman compares Newton with Spinoza.  In the first


[014-154]place, there is no ground of parallel.  Newton was a very great


[014-155]man and a very justly celebrated mathematician.  As a matter of


[014-156]fact, he is not celebrated for having discovered the law of


[014-157]gravitation.  That was known for thousands of years before he was


[014-158]born; and if the reverend gentleman would read a little more he


[014-159]would find that Newton's discovery was not that there is such a


[014-160]law as gravitation, but that bodies attract each other "with a


[014-161]force proportional directly to the quantity of matter they contain,


[014-162]and inversely to the squares of their distances."  I do not think


[014-163]he made the discoveries on account of his Christianity.  Laplace


[014-164]was certainly in many respects as great a mathematician and


[014-165]astronomer, but he was not a Christian.


[014-166]Descartes was certainly not much inferior to Newton as a mathematician,


[014-167]and thousands insist that he was his superior; yet he was not a


[014-168]Christian.  Euclid, if I remember right, was not a Christian, and


[014-169]yet he had quite a turn for mathematics.  As a matter of fact,


[014-170]Christianity got its idea of algebra from the Mohammedans, and,


[014-171]without algebra, astronomical knowledge of to-day would have been


[014-172]impossible.  Christianity did not even invent figures.  We got


[014-173]those from the Arabs.  The very word "algebra" is Arabic.  The


[014-174]decimal system, I believe, however, was due to a German, but whether


[014-175]he was a Christian or not, I do not know.
