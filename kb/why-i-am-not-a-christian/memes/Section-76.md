[075-001]This creed is a relic of the Middle Ages.  It has in it the malice,


[075-002]the malicious logic, the total depravity, the utter heartlessness


[075-003]of John Calvin, and it gives me great pleasure to say that no


[075-004]Presbyterian was ever as bad as his creed.  And here let me say,


[075-005]as I have said many times, that I do not hate Presbyterians--because


[075-006]among them I count some of my best friends--but I hate Presbyterianism.


[075-007]And I cannot illustrate this any better than by saying, I do not


[075-008]hate a man because he has the rheumatism, but I hate the rheumatism


[075-009]because it has a man.


[075-010]The Presbyterian Church is growing, and is growing because, as I


[075-011]said at first, there is a universal tendency in the mind of man to


[075-012]harmonize all that he knows or thinks he knows.  This growth may


[075-013]be delayed.  The buds of heresy may be kept back by the north wind


[075-014]of Princeton and by the early frost called Patton.  In spite of


[075-015]these souvenirs of the Dark Ages, the church must continue to grow.


[075-016]The theologians who regard theology as something higher than a


[075-017]trade, tend toward Liberalism.  Those who regard preaching as a


[075-018]business, and the inculcation of sentiment as a trade, will stand


[075-019]by the lowest possible views.  They will cling to the letter and


[075-020]throw away the spirit.  They prefer the dead limb to a new bud or


[075-021]to a new leaf.  They want no more sap.  They delight in the dead


[075-022]tree, in its unbending nature, and they mistake the stiffness of


[075-023]death for the vigor and resistance of life.


[075-024]Now, as with Dr. Briggs, so with Dr. Bridgman, although it seems


[075-025]to me that he has simply jumped from the frying-pan into the fire;


[075-026]and why he should prefer the Episcopal creed to the Baptist, is


[075-027]more than I can imagine.  The Episcopal creed is, in fact, just as


[075-028]bad as the Presbyterian.  It calmly and with unruffled brow, utters


[075-029]the sentence of eternal punishment on the majority of the human


[075-030]race, and the Episcopalian expects to be happy in heaven, with his


[075-031]son or daughter or his mother or wife in hell.


[075-032]Dr. Bridgman will find himself exactly in the position of the Rev.


[075-033]Mr. Newton, provided he expresses his thought.  But I account for


[075-034]the Bridgmans and for the Newtons by the fact that there is still


[075-035]sympathy in the human heart, and that there is still intelligence


[075-036]in the human brain.  For my part, I am glad to see this growth in


[075-037]the orthodox churches, and the quicker they revise their creeds


[075-038]the better.


[075-039]I oppose nothing that is good in any creed--I attack only that


[075-040]which is ignorant, cruel and absurd, and I make the attack in the


[075-041]interest of human liberty, and for the sake of human happiness.


[075-042]_Question_.  What do you think of the action of the Presbyterian


[075-043]General Assembly at Detroit, and what effect do you think it will


[075-044]have on religious growth?


[075-045]_Answer_.  That General Assembly was controlled by the orthodox


[075-046]within the church, by the strict constructionists and by the


[075-047]Calvinists; by gentlemen who not only believe the creed, not only


[075-048]believe that a vast majority of people are going to hell, but are


[075-049]really glad of it; by gentlemen who, when they feel a little blue,


[075-050]read about total depravity to cheer up, and when they think of the


[075-051]mercy of God as exhibited in their salvation, and the justice of


[075-052]God as illustrated by the damnation of others, their hearts burst


[075-053]into a kind of efflorescence of joy.


[075-054]These gentlemen are opposed to all kinds of amusements except


[075-055]reading the Bible, the Confession of Faith, and the creed, and


[075-056]listening to Presbyterian sermons and prayers.  All these things


[075-057]they regard as the food of cheerfulness.  They warn the elect


[075-058]against theatres and operas, dancing and games of chance.


[075-059]Well, if their doctrine is true, there ought to be no theatres,


[075-060]except exhibitions of hell; there ought to be no operas, except


[075-061]where the music is a succession of wails for the misfortunes of


[075-062]man.  If their doctrine is true, I do not see how any human being


[075-063]could ever smile again--I do not see how a mother could welcome


[075-064]her babe; everything in nature would become hateful; flowers and


[075-065]sunshine would simply tell us of our fate.


[075-066]My doctrine is exactly the opposite of this.  Let us enjoy ourselves


[075-067]every moment that we can.  The love of the dramatic is universal.


[075-068]The stage has not simply amused, but it has elevated mankind.  The


[075-069]greatest genius of our world poured the treasures of his soul into


[075-070]the drama.  I do not believe that any girl can be corrupted, or


[075-071]that any man can be injured, by becoming acquainted with Isabella


[075-072]or Miranda or Juliet or Imogen, or any of the great heroines of


[075-073]Shakespeare.


[075-074]So I regard the opera as one of the great civilizers.  No one can


[075-075]listen to the symphonies of Beethoven, or the music of Schubert,


[075-076]without receiving a benefit.  And no one can hear the operas of


[075-077]Wagner without feeling that he has been ennobled and refined.


[075-078]Why is it the Presbyterians are so opposed to music in the world,


[075-079]and yet expect to have so much in heaven?  Is not music just as


[075-080]demoralizing in the sky as on the earth, and does anybody believe


[075-081]that Abraham or Isaac or Jacob, ever played any music comparable


[075-082]to Wagner?


[075-083]Why should we postpone our joy to another world?  Thousands of


[075-084]people take great pleasure in dancing, and I say let them dance.


[075-085]Dancing is better than weeping and wailing over a theology born of


[075-086]ignorance and superstition.


[075-087]And so with games of chance.  There is a certain pleasure in playing


[075-088]games, and the pleasure is of the most innocent character.  Let


[075-089]all these games be played at home and children will not prefer the


[075-090]saloon to the society of their parents.  I believe in cards and


[075-091]billiards, and would believe in progressive euchre, were it more


[075-092]of a game--the great objection to it is its lack of complexity.


[075-093]My idea is to get what little happiness you can out of this life,


[075-094]and to enjoy all sunshine that breaks through the clouds of


[075-095]misfortune.  Life is poor enough at best.  No one should fail to


[075-096]pick up every jewel of joy that can be found in his path.  Every


[075-097]one should be as happy as he can, provided he is not happy at the


[075-098]expense of another, and no person rightly constituted can be happy


[075-099]at the expense of another.


[075-100]So let us get all we can of good between the cradle and the grave;


[075-101]all that we can of the truly dramatic; all that we can of music;


[075-102]all that we can of art; all that we can of enjoyment; and if, when


[075-103]death comes, that is the end, we have at least made the best of


[075-104]this life; and if there be another life, let us make the best of


[075-105]that.


[075-106]I am doing what little I can to hasten the coming of the day when


[075-107]the human race will enjoy liberty--not simply of body, but liberty


[075-108]of mind.  And by liberty of mind I mean freedom from superstition,


[075-109]and added to that, the intelligence to find out the conditions of


[075-110]happiness; and added to that, the wisdom to live in accordance with


[075-111]those conditions.


[075-112]--_The Morning Advertiser_, New York, June 12, 1891.


[075-113]THE TENDENCY OF MODERN THOUGHT.


[075-114]_Question_.  Do you regard the Briggs trial as any evidence of the


[075-115]growth of Liberalism in the church itself?


[075-116]_Answer_.  When men get together, and make what they call a creed,


[075-117]the supposition is that they then say as nearly as possible what


[075-118]they mean and what they believe.  A written creed, of necessity,


[075-119]remains substantially the same.  In a few years this creed ceases


[075-120]to give exactly the new shade of thought.  Then begin two processes,


[075-121]one of destruction and the other of preservation.  In every church,


[075-122]as in every party, and as you may say in every corporation, there


[075-123]are two wings--one progressive, the other conservative.  In the


[075-124]church there will be a few, and they will represent the real


[075-125]intelligence of the church, who become dissatisfied with the creed,


[075-126]and who at first satisfy themselves by giving new meanings to old


[075-127]words.  On the other hand, the conservative party appeals to


[075-128]emotions, to memories, and to the experiences of their fellow-


[075-129]members, for the purpose of upholding the old dogmas and the old


[075-130]ideas; so that each creed is like a crumbling castle.  The


[075-131]conservatives plant ivy and other vines, hoping that their leaves


[075-132]will hide the cracks and erosions of time; but the thoughtful see


[075-133]beyond these leaves and are satisfied that the structure itself is


[075-134]in the process of decay, and that no amount of ivy can restore the


[075-135]crumbling stones.


[075-136]The old Presbyterian creed, when it was first formulated, satisfied


[075-137]a certain religious intellect.  At that time people were not very


[075-138]merciful.  They had no clear conceptions of justice.  Their lives


[075-139]were for the most part hard; most of them suffered the pains and


[075-140]pangs of poverty; nearly all lived in tyrannical governments and


[075-141]were the sport of nobles and kings.  Their idea of God was born of


[075-142]their surroundings.  God, to them, was an infinite king who delighted


[075-143]in exhibitions of power.  At any rate, their minds were so constructed


[075-144]that they conceived of an infinite being who, billions of years


[075-145]before the world was, made up his mind as to whom he would save


[075-146]and whom he would damn.  He not only made up his mind as to the


[075-147]number he would save, and the number that should be lost, but he


[075-148]saved and damned without the slightest reference to the character


[075-149]of the individual.  They believed then, and some pretend to believe


[075-150]still, that God damns a man not because he is bad, and that he


[075-151]saves a man not because he is good, but simply for the purpose of


[075-152]self-glorification as an exhibition of his eternal justice.  It


[075-153]would be impossible to conceive of any creed more horrible than


[075-154]that of the Presbyterians.  Although I admit--and I not only admit


[075-155]but I assert--that the creeds of all orthodox Christians are


[075-156]substantially the same, the Presbyterian creed says plainly what


[075-157]it means.  There is no hesitation, no evasion.  The horrible truth,


[075-158]so-called, is stated in the clearest possible language.  One would


[075-159]think after reading this creed, that the men who wrote it not only


[075-160]believed it, but were really glad it was true.


[075-161]Ideas of justice, of the use of power, of the use of mercy, have


[075-162]greatly changed in the last century.  We are beginning dimly to


[075-163]see that each man is the result of an infinite number of conditions,


[075-164]of an infinite number of facts, most of which existed before he


[075-165]was born.  We are beginning dimly to see that while reason is a


[075-166]pilot, each soul navigates the mysterious sea filled with tides


[075-167]and unknown currents set in motion by ancestors long since dust.


[075-168]We are beginning to see that defects of mind are transmitted


[075-169]precisely the same as defects of body, and in my judgment the time


[075-170]is coming when we shall not more think of punishing a man for


[075-171]larceny than for having the consumption.  We shall know that the


[075-172]thief is a necessary and natural result of conditions, preparing,


[075-173]you may say, the field of the world for the growth of man.  We


[075-174]shall no longer depend upon accident and ignorance and providence.


[075-175]We shall depend upon intelligence and science.


[075-176]The Presbyterian creed is no longer in harmony with the average


[075-177]sense of man.  It shocks the average mind.  It seems too monstrous


[075-178]to be true; too horrible to find a lodgment in the mind of the


[075-179]civilized man.  The Presbyterian minister who thinks, is giving


[075-180]new meanings to the old words.  The Presbyterian minister who feels,


[075-181]also gives new meanings to the old words.  Only those who neither


[075-182]think nor feel remain orthodox.
