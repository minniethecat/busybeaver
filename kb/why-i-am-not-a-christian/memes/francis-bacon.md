# Francis Bacon

Francis Bacon (1561–1626), Viscount St Alban, was an English philosopher, statesman, and essayist whose advocacy of empirical method — observation and experiment over deductive reasoning — made him a foundational figure in the development of modern science. Ingersoll mentioned Bacon in connection with the [[shakespeare|Shakespeare]] authorship question and as an illustration of how social class determined [[reason|intellect]]ual credibility in Elizabethan England.

## 生平

Bacon rose to the highest offices of the English state, serving as Lord Chancellor under James I, before his career ended in disgrace over bribery charges. His philosophical works, including *Novum Organum* (1620) and *The Advancement of Learning* (1605), articulated a new method for investigating nature that rejected scholastic Aristotelianism in favor of inductive reasoning from observed [[truth|fact]]s.

## 主要观点与贡献

Bacon's core innovation was the idea that knowledge should be built from the ground up — from careful observation to general principles — rather than deduced from religious or philosophical authority. This empiricist program, though Bacon himself made few actual scientific discoveries, provided the philosophical charter for the Scientific Revolution.

Ingersoll's reference to Bacon came in a discussion of the [[shakespeare|Shakespeare]] authorship controversy. He argued that Elizabethan class prejudice made it impossible for a man of [[shakespeare|Shakespeare]]'s low birth to be acknowledged as a literary genius: "If Francis Bacon had said to the people, I, Francis Bacon, a gentleman of gentlemen, have been taking in secret my share of the coppers and shillings taken at the door of those low playhouses, he would have been ruined" [073-052] [073-053]. Bacon, unlike [[shakespeare|Shakespeare]], had the social standing to escape disgrace — but only by concealing his theatrical involvement.

## Ingersoll 的评价

Ingersoll did not discuss Bacon's philosophy directly. His interest was in what Bacon's case revealed about social hierarchy and [[reason|intellect]]ual attribution. A gentleman like Bacon could not afford to be publicly associated with the popular theater, while [[shakespeare|Shakespeare]] — the actual playwright — was dismissed because of his humble origins. Ingersoll used this asymmetry to expose the role of class in determining who received credit for [[reason|intellect]]ual achievement.

## 历史影响

Bacon's empiricist method became the dominant epistemology of modern science, though his direct influence on working scientists was less than that of figures like Galileo or Newton. Ingersoll's glancing treatment reflects the [[truth|fact]] that Bacon was not a central figure in the religious debates that occupied the freethought movement — but his mention of Bacon in connection with the [[shakespeare|Shakespeare]] question shows how Ingersoll wove literary, scientific, and social threads together in his larger argument against authority based on status rather than evidence.
