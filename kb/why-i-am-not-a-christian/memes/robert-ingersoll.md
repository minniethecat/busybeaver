# Robert G. Ingersoll

Robert Green Ingersoll (1833–1899), widely known as Colonel Ingersoll, was the most prominent American freethinker and agnostic of the 19th century. A Civil War veteran, accomplished lawyer, and celebrated orator, Ingersoll traveled the country delivering lectures that challenged [[christianity|orthodox Christianity]] and advocated for reason, science, and human freedom.

## Career and Public Life

Ingersoll served as a colonel in the Union Army during the Civil War and later became one of the most successful trial lawyers in America. His true fame, however, came from his public lectures on religion, science, and politics. He was described as "a picturesque figure" whose eloquence drew large audiences across the country [079-003].

His speaking tours were demanding. Reports noted that during "the past exciting three weeks Colonel Ingersoll has been a busy" figure, constantly traveling and speaking [016-132]. Despite the controversy his views generated, he maintained a reputation for personal integrity and [[reason|intellect]]ual honesty.

## Religious Views

Ingersoll's religious philosophy centered on agnosticism and a rejection of supernatural claims. He did not deny the possibility of a deity but insisted that no religion had provided sufficient evidence for its doctrines. Contemporary observers noted that many did "not know Colonel Ingersoll's religious views precisely" because his position was more nuanced than simple atheism [006-153].

He was a fierce critic of biblical literalism and the doctrine of [[hell|eternal punishment]]. His method was to subject religious claims to the same standards of evidence applied in law and science. When asked about temperance, politics, and social issues, he consistently grounded his answers in human welfare rather than divine command.

## Relationship with Henry Ward Beecher

One of the most notable relationships in Ingersoll's public life was his [[reason|intellect]]ual engagement with [[henry-ward-beecher|Henry Ward Beecher]], the famous Brooklyn preacher. Despite their theological differences, Ingersoll respected Beecher, saying that "[[henry-ward-beecher|Mr. Beecher]] is far better than his creed" and that "whenever [[henry-ward-beecher|Mr. Beecher]] says a good thing I" acknowledge it [010-063] [010-066].

The two men represented opposing poles of American religious thought — Beecher the liberal Christian, Ingersoll the agnostic — yet they maintained a relationship of mutual respect. Ingersoll observed that "evidently [[henry-ward-beecher|Mr. Beecher]] has been growing" toward more liberal views [027-053], and he acknowledged Beecher as "a man of great genius" [027-075].

## Political Engagement

Ingersoll was active in Republican politics, and his support was sought by candidates. He was asked about his presidential preferences and whether he was "for Henry" (referring to [[henry-ward-beecher|Henry Ward Beecher]] or a political candidate) [044-019]. He was also questioned about socialism and other political movements of his era [044-119].
