[004-001]_Question_.  Where do you think it is necessary the Republican


[004-002]candidate should come from to insure success?


[004-003]_Answer_.  Somewhere out of Ohio.  I think it will go to Maine,


[004-004]and for this reason:  First of all, Blaine is certainly a competent


[004-005]man of affairs, a man who knows what to do at the time; and then


[004-006]he has acted in such a chivalric way ever since the convention at


[004-007]Cincinnati, that those who opposed him most bitterly, now have for


[004-008]him nothing but admiration.  I think John Sherman is a man of


[004-009]decided ability, but I do not believe the American people would


[004-010]make one brother President, while the other is General of the Army.


[004-011]It would be giving too much power to one family.


[004-012]_Question_.  What are your conclusions as to the future of the


[004-013]Democratic party?


[004-014]_Answer_.  I think the Democratic party ought to disband.  I think


[004-015]they would be a great deal stronger disbanded, because they would


[004-016]get rid of their reputation without decreasing.


[004-017]_Question_.  But if they will not disband?


[004-018]_Answer_.  Then the next campaign depends undoubtedly upon New York


[004-019]and Indiana.  I do not see how they can very well help nominating


[004-020]a man from Indiana, and by that I mean Hendricks.  You see the


[004-021]South has one hundred and thirty-eight votes, all supposed to be


[004-022]Democratic; with the thirty-five from New York and fifteen from


[004-023]Indiana they would have just three to spare.  Now, I take it, that


[004-024]the fifteen from Indiana are just about as essential as the thirty-


[004-025]five from New York.  To lack fifteen votes is nearly as bad as


[004-026]being thirty-five short, and so far as drawing salary is concerned


[004-027]it is quite as bad.  Mr. Hendricks ought to know that he holds the


[004-028]key to Indiana, and that there cannot be any possibility of carrying


[004-029]this State for Democracy without him.  He has tried running for


[004-030]the vice-presidency, which is not much of a place anyhow--I would


[004-031]about as soon be vice-mother-in-law--and my judgment is that he


[004-032]knows exactly the value of his geographical position.  New York is


[004-033]divided to that degree that it would be unsafe to take a candidate


[004-034]from that State; and besides, New York has become famous for


[004-035]furnishing defeated candidates for the Democracy.  I think the man


[004-036]must come from Indiana.


[004-037]_Question_.  Would the Democracy of New York unite on Seymour?


[004-038]_Answer_.  You recollect what Lincoln said about the powder that


[004-039]had been shot off once.  I do not remember any man who has once


[004-040]made a race for the presidency and been defeated ever being again


[004-041]nominated.


[004-042]_Question_.  What about Bayard and Hancock as candidates?


[004-043]_Answer_.  I do not see how Bayard could possibly carry Indiana,


[004-044]while his own State is too small and too solidly Democratic.  My


[004-045]idea of Bayard is that he has not been good enough to be popular,


[004-046]and not bad enough to be famous.  The American people will never


[004-047]elect a President from a State with a whipping-post.  As to General


[004-048]Hancock, you may set it down as certain that the South will never


[004-049]lend their aid to elect a man who helped to put down the Rebellion.


[004-050]It would be just the same as the effort to elect Greeley.  It cannot


[004-051]be done.  I see, by the way, that I am reported as having said that


[004-052]David Davis, as the Democratic candidate, could carry Illinois.


[004-053]I did say that in 1876, he could have carried it against Hayes;


[004-054]but whether he could carry Illinois in 1880 would depend altogether


[004-055]upon who runs against him.  The condition of things has changed


[004-056]greatly in our favor since 1876.


[004-057]--_The Journal_, Indianapolis, Ind., November, 1879.


[004-058]POLITICS, RELIGION AND THOMAS PAINE.


[004-059]_Question_.  You have traveled about this State more or less,


[004-060]lately, and have, of course, observed political affairs here.  Do


[004-061]you think that Senator Logan will be able to deliver this State to


[004-062]the Grant movement according to the understood plan?


[004-063]_Answer_.  If the State is really for Grant, he will, and if it is


[004-064]not, he will not.  Illinois is as little "owned" as any State in


[004-065]this Union.  Illinois would naturally be for Grant, other things


[004-066]being equal, because he is regarded as a citizen of this State,


[004-067]and it is very hard for a State to give up the patronage naturally


[004-068]growing out of the fact that the President comes from that State.


[004-069]_Question_.  Will the instructions given to delegates be final?


[004-070]_Answer_.  I do not think they will be considered final at all;


[004-071]neither do I think they will be considered of any force.  It was


[004-072]decided at the last convention, in Cincinnati, that the delegates


[004-073]had a right to vote as they pleased; that each delegate represented


[004-074]the district of the State that sent him.  The idea that a State


[004-075]convention can instruct them as against the wishes of their


[004-076]constituents smacks a little too much of State sovereignty.  The


[004-077]President should be nominated by the districts of the whole country,


[004-078]and not by massing the votes by a little chicanery at a State


[004-079]convention, and every delegate ought to vote what he really believes


[004-080]to be the sentiment of his constituents, irrespective of what the


[004-081]State convention may order him to do.  He is not responsible to


[004-082]the State convention, and it is none of the State convention's


[004-083]business.  This does not apply, it may be, to the delegates at


[004-084]large, but to all the others it certainly must apply.  It was so


[004-085]decided at the Cincinnati convention, and decided on a question


[004-086]arising about this same Pennsylvania delegation.


[004-087]_Question_.  Can you guess as to what the platform in going to


[004-088]contain?


[004-089]_Answer_.  I suppose it will be a substantial copy of the old one.


[004-090]I am satisfied with the old one with one addition.  I want a plank


[004-091]to the effect that no man shall be deprived of any civil or political


[004-092]right on account of his religious or irreligious opinions.  The


[004-093]Republican party having been foremost in freeing the body ought to


[004-094]do just a little something now for the mind.  After having wasted


[004-095]rivers of blood and treasure uncounted, and almost uncountable, to


[004-096]free the cage, I propose that something ought to be done for the


[004-097]bird.  Every decent man in the United States would support that


[004-098]plank.  People should have a right to testify in courts, whatever


[004-099]their opinions may be, on any subject.  Justice should not shut


[004-100]any door leading to truth, and as long as just views neither affect


[004-101]a man's eyesight or his memory, he should be allowed to tell his


[004-102]story.  And there are two sides to this question, too.  The man is


[004-103]not only deprived of his testimony, but the commonwealth is deprived


[004-104]of it.  There should be no religious test in this country for


[004-105]office; and if Jehovah cannot support his religion without going


[004-106]into partnership with a State Legislature, I think he ought to give


[004-107]it up.


[004-108]_Question_.  Is there anything new about religion since you were


[004-109]last here?


[004-110]_Answer_.  Since I was here I have spoken in a great many cities,


[004-111]and to-morrow I am going to do some missionary work at Milwaukee.


[004-112]Many who have come to scoff have remained to pray, and I think that


[004-113]my labors are being greatly blessed, and all attacks on me so far


[004-114]have been overruled for good.  I happened to come in contact with


[004-115]a revival of religion, and I believe what they call an "outpouring"


[004-116]at Detroit, under the leadership of a gentleman by the name of


[004-117]Pentecost.  He denounced me as God's greatest enemy.  I had always


[004-118]supposed that the Devil occupied that exalted position, but it


[004-119]seems that I have, in some way, fallen heir to his shoes.  Mr.


[004-120]Pentecost also denounced all business men who would allow any


[004-121]advertisements or lithographs of mine to hang in their places of


[004-122]business, and several of these gentlemen thus appealed to took the


[004-123]advertisements away.  The result of all this was that I had the


[004-124]largest house that ever attended a lecture in Detroit.  Feeling


[004-125]that ingratitude is a crime, I publicly returned thanks to the


[004-126]clergy for the pains they had taken to give me an audience.  And


[004-127]I may say, in this connection, that if the ministers do God as


[004-128]little good as they do me harm, they had better let both of us


[004-129]alone.  I regard them as very good, but exceedingly mistaken men.


[004-130]They do not come much in contact with the world, and get most of


[004-131]their views by talking with the women and children of their


[004-132]congregations.  They are not permitted to mingle freely with society.


[004-133]They cannot attend plays nor hear operas.  I believe some of them


[004-134]have ventured to minstrel shows and menageries, where they confine


[004-135]themselves strictly to the animal part of the entertainment.  But,


[004-136]as a rule, they have very few opportunities of ascertaining what


[004-137]the real public opinion is.  They read religious papers, edited by


[004-138]gentlemen who know as little about the world as themselves, and


[004-139]the result of all this is that they are rather behind the times.


[004-140]They are good men, and would like to do right if they only knew


[004-141]it, but they are a little behind the times.  There is an old story


[004-142]told of a fellow who had a post-office in a small town in North


[004-143]Carolina, and he being the only man in the town who could read, a


[004-144]few people used to gather in the post-office on Sunday, and he


[004-145]would read to them a weekly paper that was published in Washington.


[004-146]He commenced always at the top of the first column and read right


[004-147]straight through, articles, advertisements, and all, and whenever


[004-148]they got a little tired of reading he would make a mark of red


[004-149]ochre and commence at that place the next Sunday.  The result was


[004-150]that the papers came a great deal faster than he read them, and it


[004-151]was about 1817 when they struck the war of 1812.  The moment they


[004-152]got to that, every one of them jumped up and offered to volunteer.


[004-153]All of which shows that they were patriotic people, but a little


[004-154]show, and somewhat behind the times.


[004-155]_Question_.  How were you pleased with the Paine meeting here, and


[004-156]its results?


[004-157]_Answer_.  I was gratified to see so many people willing at last


[004-158]to do justice to a great and a maligned man.  Of course I do not


[004-159]claim that Paine was perfect.  All I claim is that he was a patriot


[004-160]and a political philosopher; that he was a revolutionist and an


[004-161]agitator; that he was infinitely full of suggestive thought, and


[004-162]that he did more than any man to convince the people of American


[004-163]not only that they ought to separate from Great Britain, but that


[004-164]they ought to found a representative government.  He has been


[004-165]despised simply because he did not believe the Bible.  I wish to


[004-166]do what I can to rescue his name from theological defamation.  I


[004-167]think the day has come when Thomas Paine will be remembered with


[004-168]Washington, Franklin and Jefferson, and that the American people


[004-169]will wonder that their fathers could have been guilty of such base


[004-170]ingratitude.


[004-171]--_Chicago Times_, February 8, 1880.


[004-172]REPLY TO CHICAGO CRITICS.


[004-173]_Question_.  Have you read the replies of the clergy to your recent


[004-174]lecture in this city on "What Must we do to be Saved?" and if so


[004-175]what do you think of them?


[004-176]_Answer_.  I think they dodge the point.  The real point is this:


[004-177]If salvation by faith is the real doctrine of Christianity, I asked


[004-178]on Sunday before last, and I still ask, why didn't Matthew tell


[004-179]it?  I still insist that Mark should have remembered it, and I


[004-180]shall always believe that Luke ought, at least, to have noticed


[004-181]it.  I was endeavoring to show that modern Christianity has for


[004-182]its basis an interpolation.  I think I showed it.  The only gospel


[004-183]on the orthodox side is that of John, and that was certainly not


[004-184]written, or did not appear in its present form, until long after


[004-185]the others were written.
