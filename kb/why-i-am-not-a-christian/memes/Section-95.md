[094-001]_Answer_.  I do not care about the text-book question.  But I am


[094-002]in favor of the public school.  Nothing should be taught that


[094-003]somebody does not know.  No superstitions--nothing but science.


[094-004]_Question_.  There has been a good deal said lately about your


[094-005]suicide theology, Colonel.  Do you still believe that suicide is


[094-006]justifiable?


[094-007]_Answer_.  Certainly.  When a man is useless to himself and to


[094-008]others he has a right to determine what he will do about living.


[094-009]The only thing to be considered is a man's obligation to his fellow-


[094-010]beings and to himself.  I don't take into consideration any


[094-011]supernatural nonsense.  If God wants a man to stay here he ought


[094-012]to make it more comfortable for him.


[094-013]_Question_.  Since you expounded your justification of suicide,


[094-014]Colonel, I believe you have had some cases of suicide laid at your


[094-015]door?


[094-016]_Answer_.  Oh, yes.  Every suicide that has happened since that


[094-017]time has been charged to me.  I don't know how the people account


[094-018]for the suicides before my time.  I have not yet heard of my being


[094-019]charged with the death of Cato, but that may yet come to pass.  I


[094-020]was reading the other day that the rate of suicide in Germany is


[094-021]increasing.  I suppose my article has been translated into German.


[094-022]_Question_.  How about lying, Colonel?  Is it ever right to lie?


[094-023]_Answer_.  Of course, sometimes.  In war when a man is captured by


[094-024]the enemy he ought to lie to them to mislead them.  What we call


[094-025]strategy is nothing more than lies.  For the accomplishment of a


[094-026]good end, for instance, the saving of a woman's reputation, it is


[094-027]many times perfectly right to lie.  As a rule, people ought to tell


[094-028]the truth.  If it is right to kill a man to save your own life it


[094-029]certainly ought to be right to fool him for the same purpose.  I


[094-030]would rather be deceived than killed, wouldn't you?


[094-031]--_The Inter-Ocean_, Chicago, Illinois, March, 1897.


[094-032]A VISIT TO SHAW'S GARDEN.


[094-033]_Question_.  I was told that you came to St. Louis on your wedding


[094-034]trip some thirty years ago and went to Shaw's Garden?


[094-035]_Answer_.  Yes; we were married on the 13th of February, 1862.  We


[094-036]were here in St. Louis, and we did visit Shaw's Garden, and we


[094-037]thought it perfectly beautiful.  Afterward we visited the Kew


[094-038]Gardens in London, but our remembrance of Shaw's left Kew in the


[094-039]shade.


[094-040]Of course, I have been in St. Louis many times, my first visit


[094-041]being, I think, in 1854.  I have always liked the town.  I was


[094-042]acquainted at one time with a great many of your old citizens.


[094-043]Most of them have died, and I know but few of the present generation.


[094-044]I used to stop at the old Planter's House, and I was there quite


[094-045]often during the war.  In those days I saw Hackett as Falstaff,


[094-046]the best Falstaff that ever lived.  Ben de Bar was here then, and


[094-047]the Maddern sisters, and now the daughter of one of the sisters,


[094-048]Minnie Maddern Fiske, is one of the greatest actresses in the world.


[094-049]She has made a wonderful hit in New York this season.  And so the


[094-050]ebb and flow of life goes on--the old pass and the young arrive.


[094-051]"Death and progress!"  It may be that death is, after all, a great


[094-052]blessing.  Maybe it gives zest and flavor to life, ardor and flame


[094-053]to love.  At the same time I say, "long life" to all my friends.


[094-054]I want to live--I get great happiness out of life.  I enjoy the


[094-055]company of my friends.  I enjoy seeing the faces of the ones I


[094-056]love.  I enjoy art and music.  I love Shakespeare and Burns; love


[094-057]to hear the music of Wagner; love to see a good play.  I take


[094-058]pleasure in eating and sleeping.  The fact is, I like to breathe.


[094-059]I want to get all the happiness out of life that I can.  I want to


[094-060]suck the orange dry, so that when death comes nothing but the


[094-061]peelings will be left, and so I say:  "Long life!"


[094-062]--_The Republic_, St. Louis, April 11, 1897.


[094-063]THE VENEZUELAN BOUNDARY DISCUSSION AND THE WHIPPING-POST.


[094-064]_Question_.  What is your opinion as to the action of the President


[094-065]on the Venezuelan matter?


[094-066]_Answer_.  In my judgment, the President acted in haste and without


[094-067]thought.  It may be said that it would have been well enough for


[094-068]him to have laid the correspondence before Congress and asked for


[094-069]an appropriation for a commission to ascertain the facts, to the


[094-070]end that our Government might intelligently act.  There was no


[094-071]propriety in going further than that.  To almost declare war before


[094-072]the facts were known was a blunder--almost a crime.  For my part,


[094-073]I do not think the Monroe doctrine has anything to do with the


[094-074]case.  Mr. Olney reasons badly, and it is only by a perversion of


[094-075]facts, and an exaggeration of facts, and by calling in question


[094-076]the motives of England that it is possible to conclude that the


[094-077]Monroe doctrine has or can have anything to do with the controversy.


[094-078]The President went out of his way to find a cause of quarrel.


[094-079]Nobody doubts the courage of the American people, and we for that


[094-080]reason can afford to be sensible and prudent.  Valor and discretion


[094-081]should go together.  Nobody doubts the courage of England.


[094-082]America and England are the leading nations, and in their keeping,


[094-083]to a great extent, is the glory of the future.  They should be at


[094-084]peace.  Should a difference arise it should be settled without


[094-085]recourse to war.


[094-086]Fighting settles nothing but the relative strength.  No light is


[094-087]thrown on the cause of the conflict--on the question or fact that


[094-088]caused the war.


[094-089]_Question_.  Do you think that there is any danger of war?


[094-090]_Answer_.  If the members of Congress really represent the people,


[094-091]then there is danger.  But I do not believe the people will really


[094-092]want to fight about a few square miles of malarial territory in


[094-093]Venezuela--something in which they have no earthly or heavenly


[094-094]interest.  The people do not wish to fight for fight's sake.  When


[094-095]they understand the question they will regard the administration


[094-096]as almost insane.


[094-097]The message has already cost us more than the War of 1812 or the


[094-098]Mexican war, or both.  Stocks and bonds have decreased in value


[094-099]several hundred millions, and the end is not yet.  It may be that


[094-100]it will, on account of the panic, be impossible for the Government


[094-101]to maintain the gold standard--the reserve.  Then gold would command


[094-102]a premium, the Government would be unable to redeem the greenbacks,


[094-103]and the result would be financial chaos, and all this the result


[094-104]of Mr. Cleveland's curiosity about a boundary line between two


[094-105]countries, in neither of which we have any interest, and this


[094-106]curiosity has already cost us more than both countries, including


[094-107]the boundary line, are worth.


[094-108]The President made a great mistake.  So did the House and Senate,


[094-109]and the poor people have paid a part of the cost.


[094-110]_Question_.  What is your opinion of the Gerry Whipping Post bill?


[094-111]_Answer_.  I see that it has passed the Senate, and yet I think it


[094-112]is a disgrace to the State.  How the Senators can go back to torture,


[094-113]to the Dark Ages, to the custom of savagery, is beyond belief.  I


[094-114]hope that the House is nearer civilized, and that the infamous bill


[094-115]will be defeated.  If, however, the bill should pass, then I hope


[094-116]Governor Morton will veto it.


[094-117]Nothing is more disgusting, more degrading, than the whipping-post.


[094-118]It degrades the whipped and the whipper.  It degrades all who


[094-119]witness the flogging.  What kind of a person will do the whipping?


[094-120]Men who would apply the lash to the naked backs of criminals would


[094-121]have to be as low as the criminals, and probably a little lower.


[094-122]The shadow of the whipping-post does not fall on any civilized


[094-123]country, and never will.  The next thing we know Mr. Gerry will


[094-124]probably introduce some bill to brand criminals on the forehead or


[094-125]cut off their ears and slit their noses.  This is in the same line,


[094-126]and is born of the same hellish spirit.  There is no reforming


[094-127]power in torture, in bruising and mangling the flesh.


[094-128]If the bill becomes a law, I hope it will provide that the lash


[094-129]shall be applied by Mr. Gerry and his successors in office.  Let


[094-130]these pretended enemies of cruelty enjoy themselves.  If the bill


[094-131]passes, I presume Mr. Gerry could get a supply of knouts from


[094-132]Russia, as that country has just abolished the whipping-post.


[094-133]--_The Journal_, New York, December 24, 1895.


[094-134]COLONEL SHEPARD'S STAGE HORSES.*


[094-135][* One of Colonel Shepard's equine wrecks was picked up on


[094-136]Fifth avenue yesterday by the Prevention of Cruelty Society,


[094-137]and was laid up for repairs.  The horse was about twenty-


[094-138]eight years old, badly foundered, and its leg was cut and


[094-139]bleeding.  It was the leader of three that had been hauling


[094-140]a Fifth avenue stage, and, according to the Society's


[094-141]agents, was in about as bad a condition as a horse could be


[094-142]and keep on his feet.  The other two horses were little


[094-143]better, neither of them being fit to drive.


[094-144]Colonel Shepard's scrawny nags have long been an eyesore to


[094-145]Colonel Robert G. Ingersoll, who is compelled to see them


[094-146]from his windows at number 400 Fifth avenue.  He said last


[094-147]night:]


[094-148]It might not be in good taste for me to say anything about Colonel


[094-149]Shepard's horses.  He might think me prejudiced.  But I am satisfied


[094-150]horses cannot live on faith or on the substance of things hoped


[094-151]for.  It is far better for the horse, to feed him without praying,


[094-152]than to pray without feeding him.  It is better to be kind even to


[094-153]animals, than to quote Scripture in small capitals.  Now, I am not


[094-154]saying anything against Colonel Shepard.  I do not know how he


[094-155]feeds his horses.  If he is as good and kind as he is pious, then


[094-156]I have nothing to say.  Maybe he does not allow the horses to break


[094-157]the Sabbath by eating.  They are so slow that they make one think


[094-158]of a fast.  They put me in mind of the Garden of Eden--the rib


[094-159]story.  When I watch them on the avenue I, too, fall to quoting


[094-160]Scripture, and say, "Can these dry bones live?"  Still, I have a


[094-161]delicacy on this subject; I hate to think about it, and I think


[094-162]the horses feel the same way.


[094-163]--_Morning Advertiser_, New York, January 21, 1892.


[094-164]A REPLY TO THE REV. L. A. BANKS.


[094-165]_Question_.  Have you read the remarks made about you by the Rev.


[094-166]Mr. Banks, and what do you think of what he said?


[094-167]_Answer_.  The reverend gentleman pays me a great compliment by


[094-168]comparing me to a circus.  Everybody enjoys the circus.  They love


[094-169]to see the acrobats, the walkers on the tight rope, the beautiful


[094-170]girls on the horses, and they laugh at the wit of the clowns.  They


[094-171]are delighted with the jugglers, with the music of the band.  They


[094-172]drink the lemonade, eat the colored popcorn and laugh until they


[094-173]nearly roll off their seats.  Now the circus has a few animals so


[094-174]that Christians can have an excuse for going.  Think of the joy


[094-175]the circus gives to the boys and girls.  They look at the show


[094-176]bills, see the men and women flying through the air, bursting


[094-177]through paper hoops, the elephants standing on their heads, and


[094-178]the clowns, in curious clothes, with hands on their knees and open


[094-179]mouths, supposed to be filled with laughter.


[094-180]All the boys and girls for many miles around know the blessed day.


[094-181]They save their money, obey their parents, and when the circus


[094-182]comes they are on hand.  They see the procession and then they see


[094-183]the show.  They are all happy.  No sermon ever pleased them as


[094-184]much, and in comparison even the Sunday school is tame and dull.
