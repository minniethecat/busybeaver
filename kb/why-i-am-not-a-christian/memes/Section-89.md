[088-001]_Answer_.  In this country I see no need of secret political


[088-002]societies.  I think it better to fight in the open field.  I am a


[088-003]believer in religious liberty, in allowing all sects to preach


[088-004]their doctrines and to make as many converts as they can.  As long


[088-005]as we have free speech and a free press I think there is no danger


[088-006]of the country being ruled by any church.  The Catholics are much


[088-007]better than their creed, and the same can be said of nearly all


[088-008]members of orthodox churches.  A majority of American Catholics


[088-009]think a great deal more of this country than they do of their


[088-010]church.  When they are in good health they are on our side.  It is


[088-011]only when they are very sick that they turn their eyes toward Rome.


[088-012]If they were in the majority, of course, they would destroy all


[088-013]other churches and imprison, torture and kill all Infidels.  But


[088-014]they will never be in the majority.  They increase now only because


[088-015]Catholics come in from other countries.  In a few years that supply


[088-016]will cease, and then the Catholic Church will grow weaker every


[088-017]day.  The free secular school is the enemy of priestcraft and


[088-018]superstition, and the people of this country will never consent to


[088-019]the destruction of that institution.  I want no man persecuted on


[088-020]account of his religion.


[088-021]_Question_.  If there is no beatitude, or heaven, how do you account


[088-022]for the continual struggle in every natural heart for its own


[088-023]betterment?


[088-024]_Answer_.  Man has many wants, and all his efforts are the children


[088-025]of wants.  If he wanted nothing he would do nothing.  We civilize


[088-026]the savage by increasing his wants, by cultivating his fancy, his


[088-027]appetites, his desires.  He is then willing to work to satisfy


[088-028]these new wants.  Man always tries to do things in the easiest way.


[088-029]His constant effort is to accomplish more with less work.  He


[088-030]invents a machine; then he improves it, his idea being to make it


[088-031]perfect.  He wishes to produce the best.  So in every department


[088-032]of effort and knowledge he seeks the highest success, and he seeks


[088-033]it because it is for his own good here in this world.  So he finds


[088-034]that there is a relation between happiness and conduct, and he


[088-035]tries to find out what he must do to produce the greatest enjoyment.


[088-036]This is the basis of morality, of law and ethics.  We are so


[088-037]constituted that we love proportion, color, harmony.  This is the


[088-038]artistic man.  Morality is the harmony and proportion of conduct--


[088-039]the music of life.  Man continually seeks to better his condition


[088-040]--not because he is immortal--but because he is capable of grief


[088-041]and pain, because he seeks for happiness.  Man wishes to respect


[088-042]himself and to gain the respect of others.  The brain wants light,


[088-043]the heart wants love.  Growth is natural.  The struggle to overcome


[088-044]temptation, to be good and noble, brave and sincere, to reach, if


[088-045]possible, the perfect, is no evidence of the immortality of the


[088-046]soul or of the existence of other worlds.  Men live to excel, to


[088-047]become distinguished, to enjoy, and so they strive, each in his


[088-048]own way, to gain the ends desired.


[088-049]_Question_.  Do you believe that the race is growing moral or


[088-050]immoral?


[088-051]_Answer_.  The world is growing better.  There is more real liberty,


[088-052]more thought, more intelligence than ever before.  The world was


[088-053]never so charitable or generous as now.  We do not put honest


[088-054]debtors in prison, we no longer believe in torture.  Punishments


[088-055]are less severe.  We place a higher value on human life.  We are


[088-056]far kinder to animals.  To this, however, there is one terrible


[088-057]exception.  The vivisectors, those who cut, torture, and mutilate


[088-058]in the name of science, disgrace our age.  They excite the horror


[088-059]and indignation of all good people.  Leave out the actions of those


[088-060]wretches, and animals are better treated than ever before.  So


[088-061]there is less beating of wives and whipping of children.  The whip


[088-062]in no longer found in the civilized home.  Intelligent parents now


[088-063]govern by kindness, love and reason.  The standard of honor is


[088-064]higher than ever.  Contracts are more sacred, and men do nearer as


[088-065]they agree.  Man has more confidence in his fellow-man, and in the


[088-066]goodness of human nature.  Yes, the world is getting better, nobler


[088-067]and grander every day.  We are moving along the highway of progress


[088-068]on our way to the Eden of the future.


[088-069]_Question_.  Are the doctrines of Agnosticism gaining ground, and


[088-070]what, in your opinion, will be the future of the church?


[088-071]_Answer_.  The Agnostic is intellectually honest.  He knows the


[088-072]limitations of his mind.  He is convinced that the questions of


[088-073]origin and destiny cannot be answered by man.  He knows that he


[088-074]cannot answer these questions, and he is candid enough to say so.


[088-075]The Agnostic has good mental manners.  He does not call belief or


[088-076]hope or wish, a demonstration.  He knows the difference between


[088-077]hope and belief--between belief and knowledge--and he keeps these


[088-078]distinctions in his mind.  He does not say that a certain theory


[088-079]is true because he wishes it to be true.  He tries to go according


[088-080]to evidence, in harmony with facts, without regard to his own


[088-081]desires or the wish of the public.  He has the courage of his


[088-082]convictions and the modesty of his ignorance.  The theologian is


[088-083]his opposite.  He is certain and sure of the existence of things


[088-084]and beings and worlds of which there is, and can be, no evidence.


[088-085]He relies on assertion, and in all debate attacks the motive of


[088-086]his opponent instead of answering his arguments.  All savages know


[088-087]the origin and destiny of man.  About other things they know but


[088-088]little.  The theologian is much the same.  The Agnostic has given


[088-089]up the hope of ascertaining the nature of the "First Cause"--the


[088-090]hope of ascertaining whether or not there was a "First Cause."  He


[088-091]admits that he does not know whether or not there is an infinite


[088-092]Being.  He admits that these questions cannot be answered, and so


[088-093]he refuses to answer.  He refuses also to pretend.  He knows that


[088-094]the theologian does not know, and he has the courage to say so.


[088-095]He knows that the religious creeds rest on assumption, supposition,


[088-096]assertion--on myth and legend, on ignorance and superstition, and


[088-097]that there is no evidence of their truth.  The Agnostic bends his


[088-098]energies in the opposite direction.  He occupies himself with this


[088-099]world, with things that can be ascertained and understood.  He


[088-100]turns his attention to the sciences, to the solution of questions


[088-101]that touch the well-being of man.  He wishes to prevent and cure


[088-102]diseases; to lengthen life; to provide homes and raiment and food


[088-103]for man; to supply the wants of the body.


[088-104]He also cultivates the arts.  He believes in painting and sculpture,


[088-105]in music and the drama--the needs of the soul.  The Agnostic believes


[088-106]in developing the brain, in cultivating the affections, the tastes,


[088-107]the conscience, the judgment, to the end that man may be happy in


[088-108]this world.  He seeks to find the relation of things, the condition


[088-109]of happiness.  He wishes to enslave the forces of nature to the


[088-110]end that they may perform the work of the world.  Back of all


[088-111]progress are the real thinkers; the finders of facts, those who


[088-112]turn their attention to the world in which we live.  The theologian


[088-113]has never been a help, always a hindrance.  He has always kept his


[088-114]back to the sunrise.  With him all wisdom was in the past.  He


[088-115]appealed to the dead.  He was and is the enemy of reason, of


[088-116]investigation, of thought and progress.  The church has never given


[088-117]"sanctuary" to a persecuted truth.


[088-118]There can be no doubt that the ideas of the Agnostic are gaining


[088-119]ground.  The scientific spirit has taken possession of the intellectual


[088-120]world.  Theological methods are unpopular to-day, even in theological


[088-121]schools.  The attention of men everywhere is being directed to the


[088-122]affairs of this world, this life.  The gods are growing indistinct,


[088-123]and, like the shapes of clouds, they are changing as they fade.


[088-124]The idea of special providence has been substantially abandoned.


[088-125]People are losing, and intelligent people have lost, confidence in


[088-126]prayer.  To-day no intelligent person believes in miracles--a


[088-127]violation of the facts in nature.  They may believe that there used


[088-128]to be miracles a good while ago, but not now.  The "supernatural"


[088-129]is losing its power, its influence, and the church is growing weaker


[088-130]every day.


[088-131]The church is supported by the people, and in order to gain the


[088-132]support of the people it must reflect their ideas, their hopes and


[088-133]fears.  As the people advance, the creeds will be changed, either


[088-134]by changing the words or giving new meanings to the old words.


[088-135]The church, in order to live, must agree substantially with those


[088-136]who support it, and consequently it will change to any extent that


[088-137]may be necessary.  If the church remains true to the old standards


[088-138]then it will lose the support of progressive people, and if the


[088-139]people generally advance the church will die.  But my opinion is


[088-140]that it will slowly change, that the minister will preach what the


[088-141]members want to hear, and that the creed will be controlled by the


[088-142]contribution box.  One of these days the preachers may become


[088-143]teachers, and when that happens the church will be of use.


[088-144]_Question_.  What do you regard as the greatest of all themes in


[088-145]poetry and song?


[088-146]_Answer_.  Love and Death. The same is true of the greatest music.


[088-147]In "Tristan and Isolde" is the greatest music of love and death.


[088-148]In Shakespeare the greatest themes are love and death.  In all real


[088-149]poetry, in all real music, the dominant, the triumphant tone, is


[088-150]love, and the minor, the sad refrain, the shadow, the background,


[088-151]the mystery, is death.


[088-152]_Question_.  What would be your advice to an intelligent young man


[088-153]just starting out in life?


[088-154]_Answer_.  I would say to him:  "Be true to your ideal.  Cultivate


[088-155]your heart and brain.  Follow the light of your reason.  Get all


[088-156]the happiness out of life that you possibly can.  Do not care for


[088-157]power, but strive to be useful.  First of all, support yourself so


[088-158]that you may not be a burden to others.  If you are successful, if


[088-159]you gain a surplus, use it for the good of others.  Own yourself


[088-160]and live and die a free man.  Make your home a heaven, love your


[088-161]wife and govern your children by kindness.  Be good natured,


[088-162]cheerful, forgiving and generous.  Find out the conditions of


[088-163]happiness, and then be wise enough to live in accordance with them.


[088-164]Cultivate intellectual hospitality, express your honest thoughts,


[088-165]love your friends, and be just to your enemies."


[088-166]--_New York Herald_, September 16, 1894.


[088-167]WOMAN AND HER DOMAIN.


[088-168]_Question_.  What is your opinion of the effect of the multiplicity


[088-169]of women's clubs as regards the intellectual, moral and domestic


[088-170]status of their members?


[088-171]_Answer_.  I think that women should have clubs and societies, that


[088-172]they should get together and exchange ideas.  Women, as a rule,


[088-173]are provincial and conservative.  They keep alive all the sentimental


[088-174]mistakes and superstitions.  Now, if they can only get away from


[088-175]these, and get abreast with the tide of the times, and think as


[088-176]well as feel, it will be better for them and their children.  You


[088-177]know St. Paul tells women that if they want to know anything they


[088-178]must ask their husbands.  For many centuries they have followed


[088-179]this orthodox advice, and of course they have not learned a great


[088-180]deal, because their husbands could not answer their questions.


[088-181]Husbands, as a rule, do not know a great deal, and it will not do


[088-182]for every wife to depend on the ignorance of her worst half.  The


[088-183]women of to-day are the great readers, and no book is a great


[088-184]success unless it pleases the women.
