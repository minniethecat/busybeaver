[022-001]--_The Times_, Chicago, Illinois, October 13, 1883.


[022-002]THE REPUBLICAN DEFEAT IN OHIO.


[022-003]_Question_.  What is your explanation of the Republican disaster


[022-004]last Tuesday?


[022-005]_Answer_.  Too much praying and not enough paying, is my explanation


[022-006]of the Republican defeat.


[022-007]_First_.  I think the attempt to pass the Prohibition Amendment


[022-008]lost thousands of votes.  The people of this country, no matter


[022-009]how much they may deplore the evils of intemperance, are not yet


[022-010]willing to set on foot a system of spying into each other's affairs.


[022-011]They know that prohibition would need thousands of officers--that


[022-012]it would breed informers and spies and peekers and skulkers by the


[022-013]hundred in every county.  They know that laws do not of themselves


[022-014]make good people.  Good people make good laws.  Americans do not


[022-015]wish to be temperate upon compulsion.  The spirit that resents


[022-016]interference in these matters is the same spirit that made and


[022-017]keeps this a free country.  All this crusade and prayer-meeting


[022-018]business will not do in politics.  We must depend upon the countless


[022-019]influences of civilization, upon science, art, music--upon the


[022-020]softening influences of kindness and argument.  As life becomes


[022-021]valuable people will take care of it.  Temperance upon compulsion


[022-022]destroys something more valuable than itself--liberty.  I am for


[022-023]the largest liberty in all things.


[022-024]_Second_.  The Prohibitionists, in my opinion, traded with Democrats.


[022-025]The Democrats were smart enough to know that prohibition could not


[022-026]carry, and that they could safely trade.  The Prohibitionists were


[022-027]insane enough to vote for their worst enemies, just for the sake


[022-028]of polling a large vote for prohibition, and were fooled as usual.


[022-029]_Thirdly_.  Certain personal hatreds of certain Republican politicians.


[022-030]These were the causes which led to Republican defeat in Ohio.


[022-031]_Question_.  Will it necessitate the nomination of an Ohio Republican


[022-032]next year?


[022-033]_Answer_.  I do not think so.  Defeat is apt to breed dissension,


[022-034]and on account of that dissension the party will have to take a


[022-035]man from some other State.  One politician will say to another,


[022-036]"You did it," and another will reply, "You are the man who ruined


[022-037]the party."  I think we have given Ohio her share; certainly she


[022-038]has given us ours.


[022-039]_Question_.  Will this reverse seriously affect Republican chances


[022-040]next year?


[022-041]_Answer_.  If the country is prosperous next year, if the crops


[022-042]are good, if prices are fair, if Pittsburg is covered with smoke,


[022-043]if the song of the spindle is heard in Lowell, if stocks are healthy,


[022-044]the Republicans will again succeed.  If the reverse as to crops


[022-045]and forges and spindles, then the Democrats will win.  It is a


[022-046]question of "chich-bugs," and floods and drouths.


[022-047]_Question_.  Who, in your judgment, would be the strongest man the


[022-048]Republicans could put up?


[022-049]_Answer_.  Last year I thought General Sherman, but he has gone to


[022-050]Missouri, and now I am looking around.  The first day I find out


[022-051]I will telegraph you.


[022-052]--_The Democrat_, Dayton, Ohio, October 15, 1883.


[022-053]THE CIVIL RIGHTS BILL.


[022-054]_Question_.  What do you think of the recent opinion of the Supreme


[022-055]Court touching the rights of the colored man?


[022-056]_Answer_.  I think it is all wrong.  The intention of the framers


[022-057]of the amendment, by virtue of which the law was passed, was that


[022-058]no distinction should be made in inns, in hotels, cars, or in


[022-059]theatres; in short, in public places, on account of color, race,


[022-060]or previous condition.  The object of the men who framed that


[022-061]amendment to the Constitution was perfectly clear, perfectly well


[022-062]known, perfectly understood.  They intended to secure, by an


[022-063]amendment to the fundamental law, what had been fought for by


[022-064]hundreds of thousands of men.  They knew that the institution of


[022-065]slavery had cost rebellion; the also knew that the spirit of caste


[022-066]was only slavery in another form.  They intended to kill that


[022-067]spirit.  Their object was that the law, like the sun, should shine


[022-068]upon all, and that no man keeping a hotel, no corporation running


[022-069]cars, no person managing a theatre should make any distinction on


[022-070]account of race or color.  This amendment is above all praise.  It


[022-071]was the result of a moral exaltation, such as the world never before


[022-072]had seen.  There were years during the war, and after, when the


[022-073]American people were simply sublime; when their generosity was


[022-074]boundless; when they were willing to endure any hardship to make


[022-075]this an absolutely free country.


[022-076]This decision of the Supreme Court puts the best people of the


[022-077]colored race at the mercy of the meanest portion of the white race.


[022-078]It allows a contemptible white man to trample upon a good colored


[022-079]man.  I believe in drawing a line between good and bad, between


[022-080]clean and unclean, but I do not believe in drawing a color line


[022-081]which is as cruel as the lash of slavery.


[022-082]I am willing to be on an equality in all hotels, in all cars, in


[022-083]all theatres, with colored people.  I make no distinction of race.


[022-084]Those make the distinction who cannot afford not to.  If nature


[022-085]has made no distinction between me and some others, I do not ask


[022-086]the aid of the Legislature.  I am willing to associate with all


[022-087]good, clean persons, irrespective of complexion.


[022-088]This decision virtually gives away one of the great principles for


[022-089]which the war was fought.  It carries the doctrine of "State Rights"


[022-090]to the Democratic extreme, and renders necessary either another


[022-091]amendment or a new court.


[022-092]I agree with Justice Harlan.  He has taken a noble and patriotic


[022-093]stand.  Kentucky rebukes Massachusetts!  I am waiting with some


[022-094]impatience--impatient because I anticipate a pleasure--for his


[022-095]dissenting opinion.  Only a little while ago Justice Harlan took


[022-096]a very noble stand on the Virginia Coupon cases, in which was


[022-097]involved the right of a State to repudiate its debts.  Now he has


[022-098]taken a stand in favor of the civil rights of the colored man; and


[022-099]in both instances I think he is right.


[022-100]This decision may, after all, help the Republican party.  A decision


[022-101]of the Supreme Court aroused the indignation of the entire North,


[022-102]and I hope the present decision will have a like effect.  The good


[022-103]people of this country will not be satisfied until every man beneath


[022-104]the flag, without the slightest respect to his complexion, stands


[022-105]on a perfect equality before the law with every other.  Any government


[022-106]that makes a distinction on account of color, is a disgrace to the


[022-107]age in which we live.  The idea that a man like Frederick Douglass


[022-108]can be denied entrance to a car, that the doors of a hotel can be


[022-109]shut in his face; that he may be prevented from entering a theatre;


[022-110]the idea that there shall be some ignominious corner into which


[022-111]such a man can be thrown simply by a decision of the Supreme Court!


[022-112]This idea is simply absurd.


[022-113]_Question_.  What remains to be done now, and who is going to do it?


[022-114]_Answer_.  For a good while people have been saying that the


[022-115]Republican party has outlived its usefulness; that there is very


[022-116]little difference now between the parties; that there is hardly


[022-117]enough left to talk about.  This decision opens the whole question.


[022-118]This decision says to the Republican party, "Your mission is not


[022-119]yet ended.  This is not a free country.  Our flag does not protect


[022-120]the rights of a human being."  This decision is the tap of a drum.


[022-121]The old veterans will fall into line.  This decision gives the


[022-122]issue for the next campaign, and it may be that the Supreme Court


[022-123]has builded wiser than it knew.  This is a greater question than


[022-124]the tariff or free trade.  It is a question of freedom, of human


[022-125]rights, of the sacredness of humanity.


[022-126]The real Americans, the real believers in Liberty, will give three


[022-127]cheers for Judge Harlan.


[022-128]One word more.  The Government is bound to protect its citizens,


[022-129]not only when they are away from home, but when they are under the


[022-130]flag.  In time of war the Government has a right to draft any


[022-131]citizen; to put that citizen in the line of battle, and compel him


[022-132]to fight for the nation.  If the Government when imperiled has the


[022-133]right to compel a citizen, whether white or black, to defend with


[022-134]his blood the flag, that citizen, when imperiled, has the right to


[022-135]demand protection from the Nation.  The Nation cannot then say,


[022-136]"You must appeal to your State."  If the citizen must appeal to


[022-137]the State for redress, then the citizen should defend the State


[022-138]and not the General Government, and the doctrine of State Rights


[022-139]then becomes complete.


[022-140]--_The National Republican_, Washington, D. C., October 17, 1883.


[022-141]JUSTICE HARLAN AND THE CIVIL RIGHTS BILL.


[022-142]_Question_.  What do you think of Justice Harlan's dissenting


[022-143]opinion in the Civil Rights case?


[022-144]_Answer_.  I have just read it and think it admirable in every


[022-145]respect.  It is unanswerable.  He has given to words their natural


[022-146]meaning.  He has recognized the intention of the framers of the


[022-147]recent amendments.  There is nothing in this opinion that is


[022-148]strained, insincere, or artificial.  It is frank and manly.  It is


[022-149]solid masonry, without crack or flaw.  He does not resort to legal


[022-150]paint or putty, or to verbal varnish or veneer.  He states the


[022-151]position of his brethren of the bench with perfect fairness, and


[022-152]overturns it with perfect ease.  He has drawn an instructive parallel


[022-153]between the decisions of the olden time, upholding the power of


[022-154]Congress to deal with individuals in the interests of slavery, and


[022-155]the power conferred on Congress by the recent amendments.  He has


[022-156]shown by the old decisions, that when a duty is enjoined upon


[022-157]Congress, ability to perform it is given; that when a certain end


[022-158]is required, all necessary means are granted.  He also shows that


[022-159]the Fugitive Slave Acts of 1793 and of 1850, rested entirely upon


[022-160]the implied power of Congress to enforce a master's rights; and


[022-161]that power was once implied in favor of slavery against human


[022-162]rights, and implied from language shadowy, feeble and uncertain


[022-163]when compared with the language of the recent amendments.  He has


[022-164]shown, too, that Congress exercised the utmost ingenuity in devising


[022-165]laws to enforce the master's claim.  Implication was held ample to


[022-166]deprive a human being of his liberty, but to secure freedom, the


[022-167]doctrine of implication is abandoned.  As a foundation for wrong,


[022-168]implication was their rock.  As a foundation for right, it is now


[022-169]sand.  Implied power then was sufficient to enslave, while power


[022-170]expressly given is now impotent to protect.


[022-171]_Question_.  What do you think of the use he has made of the Dred


[022-172]Scott decision?


[022-173]_Answer_.  Well, I think he has shown conclusively that the present


[022-174]decision, under the present circumstances, is far worse than the


[022-175]Dred Scott decision was under the then circumstances.  The Dred


[022-176]Scott decision was a libel upon the best men of the Revolutionary


[022-177]period.  That decision asserted broadly that our forefathers regarded


[022-178]the negroes as having no rights which white men were bound to


[022-179]respect; that the negroes were merely merchandise, and that that


[022-180]opinion was fixed and universal in the civilized portion of the


[022-181]white race, and that no one thought of disputing it.  Yet Franklin


[022-182]contended that slavery might be abolished under the preamble of


[022-183]the Constitution.  Thomas Jefferson said that if the slave should


[022-184]rise to cut the throat of his master, God had no attribute that


[022-185]would side against the slave.  Thomas Paine attacked the institution


[022-186]with all the intensity and passion of his nature.  John Adams


[022-187]regarded the institution with horror.  So did every civilized man,


[022-188]South and North.


[022-189]Justice Harlan shows conclusively that the Thirteenth Amendment


[022-190]was adopted in the light of the Dred Scott decision; that it


[022-191]overturned and destroyed, not simply the decision, but the reasoning


[022-192]upon which it was based; that it proceeded upon the ground that


[022-193]the colored people had rights that white men were bound to respect,


[022-194]not only, but that the Nation was bound to protect.  He takes the


[022-195]ground that the amendment was suggested by the condition of that


[022-196]race, which had been declared by the Supreme Court of the United


[022-197]States to have no rights which white men were bound to respect; that


[022-198]it was made to protect people whose rights had been invaded, and


[022-199]whose strong arms had assisted in the overthrow of the Rebellion;


[022-200]that it was made for the purpose of putting these men upon a legal


[022-201]authority with white citizens.
