[050-001]_Question_.  Do you think that church people were justified in


[050-002]their opposition to the drama in the days when Congreve, Wycherley


[050-003]and Ben Jonson were the popular favorites?


[050-004]_Answer_.  In that time there was a great deal of vulgarity in many


[050-005]of the plays.  Many things were said on the stage that the people


[050-006]of this age would not care to hear, and there was not very often


[050-007]enough wit in the saying to redeem it.  My principal objection to


[050-008]Congreve, Wycherley and most of their contemporaries is that the


[050-009]plays were exceedingly poor and had not much in them of real,


[050-010]sterling value.  The Puritans, however, did not object on account


[050-011]of the vulgarity; that was not the honest objection.  No play was


[050-012]ever put upon the English stage more vulgar then the "Table Talk"


[050-013]of Martin Luther, and many sermons preached in that day were almost


[050-014]unrivaled for vulgarity.  The worst passages in the Old Testament


[050-015]were quoted with a kind of unction that showed a love for the


[050-016]vulgar.  And, in my judgment, the worst plays were as good as the


[050-017]sermons, and the theatre of that time was better adapted to civilize


[050-018]mankind, to soften the human heart, and to make better men and


[050-019]better women, than the pulpit of that day.  The actors, in my


[050-020]judgment, were better people than the preachers.  They had in them


[050-021]more humanity, more real goodness and more appreciation of beauty,


[050-022]of tenderness, of generosity and of heroism.  Probably no religion


[050-023]was ever more thoroughly hateful than Puritanism.  But all religionists


[050-024]who believe in an eternity of pain would naturally be opposed to


[050-025]everything that makes this life better; and, as a matter of fact,


[050-026]orthodox churches have been the enemies of painting, of sculpture,


[050-027]of music and the drama.


[050-028]_Question_.  What, in your estimation, is the value of the drama


[050-029]as a factor in our social life at the present time?


[050-030]_Answer_.  I believe that the plays of Shakespeare are the most


[050-031]valuable things in the possession of the human race.  No man can


[050-032]read and understand Shakespeare without being an intellectually


[050-033]developed man.  If Shakespeare could be as widely circulated as


[050-034]the Bible--if all the Bible societies would break the plates they


[050-035]now have and print Shakespeare, and put Shakespeare in all the


[050-036]languages of the world, nothing would so raise the intellectual


[050-037]standard of mankind.  Think of the different influence on men


[050-038]between reading Deuteronomy and "Hamlet" and "King Lear"; between


[050-039]studying Numbers and the "Midsummer Night's Dream"; between pondering


[050-040]over the murderous crimes and assassinations in Judges, and studying


[050-041]"The Tempest" or "As You Like It."  Man advances as he develops


[050-042]intellectually.  The church teaches obedience.  The man who reads


[050-043]Shakespeare has his intellectual horizon enlarged.  He begins to


[050-044]think for himself, and he enjoys living in a new world.  The


[050-045]characters of Shakespeare become his acquaintances.  He admires


[050-046]the heroes, the philosophers; he laughs with the clowns, and he


[050-047]almost adores the beautiful women, the pure, loving, and heroic


[050-048]women born of Shakespeare's heart and brain.  The stage has amused


[050-049]and instructed the world.  It had added to the happiness of mankind.


[050-050]It has kept alive all arts.  It is in partnership with all there


[050-051]is of beauty, of poetry, and expression.  It goes hand in hand with


[050-052]music, with painting, with sculpture, with oratory, with philosophy,


[050-053]and history.  The stage has humor.  It abhors stupidity.  It despises


[050-054]hypocrisy.  It holds up to laughter the peculiarities, the


[050-055]idiosyncrasies, and the little insanities of mankind.  It thrusts


[050-056]the spear of ridicule through the shield of pretence.  It laughs


[050-057]at the lugubrious and it has ever taught and will, in all probability,


[050-058]forever teach, that Man is more than a title, and that human love


[050-059]laughs at all barriers, at all the prejudices of society and caste


[050-060]that tend to keep apart two loving hearts.


[050-061]_Question_.  What is your opinion of the progress of the drama in


[050-062]educating the artistic sense of the community as compared with the


[050-063]progress of the church as an educator of the moral sentiment?


[050-064]_Answer_.  Of course, the stage is not all good, nor is--and I say


[050-065]this with becoming modesty--the pulpit all bad.  There have been


[050-066]bad actors and there have been good preachers.  There has been no


[050-067]improvement in plays since Shakespeare wrote.  There has been great


[050-068]improvement in theatres, and the tendency seems to me be toward


[050-069]higher artistic excellence in the presentation of plays.  As we


[050-070]become slowly civilized we will constantly demand more artistic


[050-071]excellence.  There will always be a class satisfied with the lowest


[050-072]form of dramatic presentation, with coarse wit, with stupid but


[050-073]apparent jokes, and there will always be a class satisfied with


[050-074]almost anything; but the class demanding the highest, the best,


[050-075]will constantly increase in numbers, and the other classes will,


[050-076]in all probability, correspondingly decrease.  The church has ceased


[050-077]to be an educator.  In an artistic direction it never did anything


[050-078]except in architecture, and that ceased long ago.  The followers


[050-079]of to-day are poor copyists.  The church has been compelled to be


[050-080]a friend of, or rather to call in the assistance of, music.  As a


[050-081]moral teacher, the church always has been and always will be a


[050-082]failure.  The pulpit, to use the language of Frederick Douglass,


[050-083]has always "echoed the cry of the street."  Take our own history.


[050-084]The church was the friend of slavery.  That institution was defended


[050-085]in nearly every pulpit.  The Bible was the auction-block on which


[050-086]the slave-mother stood while her child was sold from her arms.


[050-087]The church, for hundreds of years, was the friend and defender of


[050-088]the slave-trade.  I know of no crime that has not been defended by


[050-089]the church, in one form or another.  The church is not a pioneer;


[050-090]it accepts a new truth, last of all, and only when denial has become


[050-091]useless.  The church preaches the doctrine of forgiveness.  This


[050-092]doctrine sells crime on credit.  The idea that there is a God who


[050-093]rewards and punishes, and who can reward, if he so wishes, the


[050-094]meanest and vilest of the human race, so that he will be eternally


[050-095]happy, and can punish the best of the human race, so that he will


[050-096]be eternally miserable, is subversive of all morality.  Happiness


[050-097]ought to be the result of good actions.  Happiness ought to spring


[050-098]from the seed a man sows himself.  It ought not to be a reward, it


[050-099]ought to be a consequence, and there ought to be no idea that there


[050-100]is any being who can step between action and consequence.  To preach


[050-101]that a man can abuse his wife and children, rob his neighbors,


[050-102]slander his fellow-citizens, and yet, a moment or two before he


[050-103]dies, by repentance become a glorified angel is, in my judgment,


[050-104]immoral.  And to preach that a man can be a good man, kind to his


[050-105]wife and children, an honest man, paying his debts, and yet, for


[050-106]the lack of a certain belief, the moment after he is dead, be sent


[050-107]to an eternal prison, is also immoral.  So that, according to my


[050-108]opinion, while the church teaches men many good things, it also


[050-109]teaches doctrines subversive of morality.  If there were not in


[050-110]the whole world a church, the morality of man, in my judgment, would


[050-111]be the gainer.


[050-112]_Question_.  What do you think of the treatment of the actor by


[050-113]society in his social relations?


[050-114]_Answer_.  For a good many years the basis of society has been the


[050-115]dollar.  Only a few years ago all literary men were ostracized


[050-116]because they had no money; neither did they have a reading public.


[050-117]If any man produced a book he had to find a patron--some titled


[050-118]donkey, some lauded lubber, in whose honor he could print a few


[050-119]well-turned lies on the fly-leaf.  If you wish to know the degradation


[050-120]of literature, read the dedication written by Lord Bacon to James


[050-121]I., in which he puts him beyond all kings, living and dead--beyond


[050-122]Caesar and Marcus Aurelius.  In those days the literary man was a


[050-123]servant, a hack.  He lived in Grub Street.  He was only one degree


[050-124]above the sturdy vagrant and the escaped convict.  Why was this?


[050-125]He had no money and he lived in an age when money was the fountain


[050-126]of respectability.  Let me give you another instance:  Mozart,


[050-127]whose brain was a fountain of melody, was forced to eat at table


[050-128]with coachmen, with footmen and scullions.  He was simply a servant


[050-129]who was commanded to make music for a pudding-headed bishop.  The


[050-130]same was true of the great painters, and of almost all other men


[050-131]who rendered the world beautiful by art, and who enriched the


[050-132]languages of mankind.  The basis of respectability was the dollar.


[050-133]Now that the literary man has an intelligent public he cares nothing


[050-134]for the ignorant patron.  The literary man makes money.  The world


[050-135]is becoming civilized and the literary man stands high.  In England,


[050-136]however, if Charles Darwin had been invited to dinner, and there


[050-137]had been present some sprig of nobility, some titled vessel holding


[050-138]the germs of hereditary disease, Darwin would have been compelled


[050-139]to occupy a place beneath him.  But I have hopes even for England.


[050-140]The same is true of the artist.  The man who can now paint a picture


[050-141]by which he receives from five thousand to fifty thousand dollars,


[050-142]is necessarily respectable.  The actor who may realize from one to


[050-143]two thousand dollars a night, or even more, is welcomed in the


[050-144]stupidest and richest society.  So with the singers and with all


[050-145]others who instruct and amuse mankind.  Many people imagine that


[050-146]he who amuses them must be lower than they.  This, however, is


[050-147]hardly possible.  I believe in the aristocracy of the brain and


[050-148]heart; in the aristocracy of intelligence and goodness, and not


[050-149]only appreciate but admire the great actor, the great painter, the


[050-150]great sculptor, the marvelous singer.  In other words, I admire


[050-151]all people who tend to make this life richer, who give an additional


[050-152]thought to this poor world.


[050-153]_Question_.  Do you think this liberal movement, favoring the better


[050-154]class of plays, inaugurated by the Rev. Dr. Abbott, will tend to


[050-155]soften the sentiment of the orthodox churches against the stage?


[050-156]_Answer_.  I have not read what Dr. Abbott has written on this


[050-157]subject.  From your statement of his position, I think he entertains


[050-158]quite a sensible view, and, when we take into consideration that


[050-159]he is a minister, a miraculously sensible view.  It is not the


[050-160]business of the dramatist, the actor, the painter or the sculptor


[050-161]to teach what the church calls morality.  The dramatist and the


[050-162]actor ought to be truthful, ought to be natural--that is to say,


[050-163]truthfully and naturally artistic.  He should present pictures of


[050-164]life properly chosen, artistically constructed; an exhibition of


[050-165]emotions truthfully done, artistically done.  If vice is presented


[050-166]naturally, no one will fall in love with vice.  If the better


[050-167]qualities of the human heart are presented naturally, no one can


[050-168]fail to fall in love with them.  But they need not be presented


[050-169]for that purpose.  The object of the artist is to present truthfully


[050-170]and artistically.  He is not a Sunday school teacher.  He is not


[050-171]to have the moral effect eternally in his mind.  It is enough for


[050-172]him to be truly artistic.  Because, as I have said, a great many


[050-173]times, the greatest good is done by indirection.  For instance, a


[050-174]man lives a good, noble, honest and lofty life.  The value of that


[050-175]life would be destroyed if he kept calling attention to it--if he


[050-176]said to all who met him, "Look at me!" he would become intolerable.


[050-177]The truly artistic speaks of perfection; that is to say, of harmony,


[050-178]not only of conduct, but of harmony and proportion in everything.


[050-179]The pulpit is always afraid of the passions, and really imagines


[050-180]that it has some influence on men and women, keeping them in the


[050-181]path of virtue.  No greater mistake was ever made.  Eternally


[050-182]talking and harping on that one subject, in my judgment, does harm.


[050-183]Forever keeping it in the mind by reading passages from the Bible,


[050-184]by talking about the "corruption of the human heart," of the "power


[050-185]of temptation," of the scarcity of virtue, of the plentifulness of


[050-186]vice--all these platitudes tend to produce exactly what they are


[050-187]directed against.
