[016-001]What did God mean when he said, If a man strike his servant so he


[016-002]dies, he should not be punished, because his servant was his money?


[016-003]Passages like these can be quoted beyond the space that any paper


[016-004]is willing to give.  Yet the Rev. Dr. Fulton denies that the Old


[016-005]Testament upholds slavery.  I would like to ask him if the Old


[016-006]Testament is in favor of religious toleration?  If God wrote the


[016-007]Old Testament and afterward came upon the earth as Jesus Christ,


[016-008]and taught a new religion, and the Jews crucified him, was this


[016-009]not in accordance with his own law, and was he not, after all, the


[016-010]victim of himself?


[016-011]_Question_.  What about the other ministers?


[016-012]_Answer_.  Well, I see in the _Herald_ that some ten have said that


[016-013]they would reply to me.  I have selected the two, simply because


[016-014]they came first.  I think they are about as poor as any; and you


[016-015]know it is natural to attack those who are the easiest answered.


[016-016]All these ministers are now acting as my agents, and are doing me


[016-017]all the good they can by saying all the bad things about me they


[016-018]can think of.  They imagine that their congregations have not grown,


[016-019]and they talk to them as though they were living in the seventeenth


[016-020]instead of the nineteenth century.  The truth is, the pews are


[016-021]beyond the pulpit, and the modern sheep are now protecting the


[016-022]shepherds.


[016-023]_Question_.  Have you noticed a great change in public sentiment


[016-024]in the last three or four years?


[016-025]_Answer_.  Yes, I think there are ten times as many Infidels to-


[016-026]day as there were ten years ago.  I am amazed at the great change


[016-027]that has taken place in public opinion.  The churches are not


[016-028]getting along well.  There are hundreds and hundreds who have not


[016-029]had a new member in a year.  The young men are not satisfied with


[016-030]the old ideas.  They find that the church, after all, is opposed


[016-031]to learning; that it is the enemy of progress; that it says to


[016-032]every young man, "Go slow.  Don't allow your knowledge to puff you


[016-033]up.  Recollect that reason is a dangerous thing.  You had better


[016-034]be a little ignorant here for the sake of being an angel hereafter,


[016-035]than quite a smart young man and get damned at last."  The church


[016-036]warns them against Humboldt and Darwin, and tells them how much


[016-037]nobler it is to come from mud than from monkeys; that they were


[016-038]made from mud.  Every college professor is afraid to tell what he


[016-039]thinks, and every student detects the cowardice.  The result is


[016-040]that the young men have lost confidence in the creeds of the day


[016-041]and propose to do a little thinking for themselves.  They still


[016-042]have a kind of tender pity for the old folks, and pretend to believe


[016-043]some things they do not, rather than hurt grandmother's feelings.


[016-044]In the presence of the preachers they talk about the weather or


[016-045]other harmless subjects, for fear of bruising the spirit of their


[016-046]pastor.  Every minister likes to consider himself as a brave shepherd


[016-047]leading the lambs through the green pastures and defending them at


[016-048]night from Infidel wolves.  All this he does for a certain share


[016-049]of the wool.  Others regard the church as a kind of social


[016-050]organization, as a good way to get into society.  They wish to


[016-051]attend sociables, drink tea, and contribute for the conversion of


[016-052]the heathen.  It is always so pleasant to think that there is


[016-053]somebody worse than you are, whose reformation you can help pay


[016-054]for.  I find, too, that the young women are getting tired of the


[016-055]old doctrines, and that everywhere, all over this country, the


[016-056]power of the pulpit wanes and weakens.  I find in my lectures that


[016-057]the applause is just in proportion to the radicalism of the thought


[016-058]expressed.  Our war was a great educator, when the whole people of


[016-059]the North rose up grandly in favor of human liberty.  For many


[016-060]years the great question of human rights was discussed from every


[016-061]stump.  Every paper was filled with splendid sentiments.  An


[016-062]application of those doctrines--doctrines born in war--will forever


[016-063]do away with the bondage of superstition.  When man has been free


[016-064]in body for a little time, he will become free in mind, and the


[016-065]man who says, "I have a equal right with other men to work and reap


[016-066]the reward of my labor," will say, "I have, also, an equal right


[016-067]to think and reap the reward of my thought."


[016-068]In old times there was a great difference between a clergyman and


[016-069]a layman.  The clergyman was educated; the peasant was ignorant.


[016-070]The tables have been turned.  The thought of the world is with the


[016-071]laymen.  They are the intellectual pioneers, the mental leaders,


[016-072]and the ministers are following on behind, predicting failure and


[016-073]disaster, sighing for the good old times when their word ended


[016-074]discussion.  There is another good thing, and that is the revision


[016-075]of the Bible.  Hundreds of passages have been found to be


[016-076]interpolations, and future revisers will find hundreds more.  The


[016-077]foundation crumbles.  That book, called the basis of all law and


[016-078]civilization, has to be civilized itself.  We have outgrown it.


[016-079]Our laws are better; our institutions grander; our objects and aims


[016-080]nobler and higher.


[016-081]_Question_.  Do many people write to you upon this subject; and


[016-082]what spirit do they manifest?


[016-083]_Answer_.  Yes, I get a great many anonymous letters--some letters


[016-084]in which God is asked to strike me dead, others of an exceedingly


[016-085]insulting character, others almost idiotic, others exceedingly


[016-086]malicious, and others insane, others written in an exceedingly good


[016-087]spirit, winding up with the information that I must certainly be


[016-088]damned.  Others express wonder that God allowed me to live at all,


[016-089]and that, having made the mistake, he does not instantly correct


[016-090]it by killing me.  Others prophesy that I will yet be a minister


[016-091]of the gospel; but, as there has never been any softening of the


[016-092]brain in our family, I imagine that the prophecy will never by


[016-093]fulfilled.  Lately, on opening a letter and seeing that it is upon


[016-094]this subject, and without a signature, I throw it aside without


[016-095]reading.  I have so often found them to be so grossly ignorant,


[016-096]insulting and malicious, that as a rule I read them no more.


[016-097]_Question_.  Of the hundreds of people who call upon you nearly


[016-098]every day to ask your help, do any of them ever discriminate against


[016-099]you on account of your Infidelity?


[016-100]_Answer_.  No one who has asked a favor of me objects to my religion,


[016-101]or, rather, to my lack of it.  A great many people do come to me


[016-102]for assistance of one kind or another.  But I have never yet asked


[016-103]a man or woman whether they were religious or not, to what church


[016-104]they belonged, or any questions upon the subject.  I think I have


[016-105]done favors for persons of most denominations.  It never occurs to


[016-106]me whether they are Christians or Infidels.  I do not care.  Of


[016-107]course, I do not expect that Christians will treat me the same as


[016-108]though I belonged to their church.  I have never expected it.  In


[016-109]some instances I have been disappointed.  I have some excellent


[016-110]friends who disagree with me entirely upon the subject of religion.


[016-111]My real opinion is that secretly they like me because I am not a


[016-112]Christian, and those who do not like me envy the liberty I enjoy.


[016-113]--New York correspondent, _Chicago Times_, May 29, 1881.


[016-114]GUITEAU AND HIS CRIME.*


[016-115][* Our "Royal Bob" was found by _The Gazette_, in the


[016-116]gloaming of a delicious evening, during the past week,


[016-117]within the open portals of his friendly residence, dedicated


[016-118]by the gracious presence within to a simple and cordial


[016-119]hospitality, to the charms of friendship and the freedom of


[016-120]an abounding comradeship.  With intellectual and untrammeled


[016-121]life, a generous, wise and genial host, whoever enters finds


[016-122]a welcome, seasoned with kindly wit and Attic humor, a


[016-123]poetic insight and a delicious frankness which renders an


[016-124]evening there a veritable symposium.  The wayfarer who


[016-125]passes is charmed, and he who comes frequently, goes always


[016-126]away with delighted memories.


[016-127]What matters it that we differ? such as he and his make our


[016-128]common life the sweeter.  An hour or two spent in the


[016-129]attractive parlors of the Ingersoll homestead, amid that


[016-130]rare group, lends a newer meaning to the idea of home and a


[016-131]more secure beauty to the fact of family life.  During the


[016-132]past exciting three weeks Colonel Ingersoll has been a busy


[016-133]man.  He holds no office.  No position could lend him an


[016-134]additional crown and even recognition is no longer


[016-135]necessary.  But it has been well that amid the first fierce


[016-136]fury of anger and excitement, and the subsequent more bitter


[016-137]if not as noble outpouring of faction's suspicions and


[016-138]innuendoes, that so manly a man, so sagacious a counsellor,


[016-139]has been enabled to hold so positive a balance.  Cabinet


[016-140]officers, legal functionaries, detectives, citizens--all


[016-141]have felt the wise, humane instincts, and the capacious


[016-142]brain of this marked man affecting and influencing for this


[016-143]fair equipoise and calmer judgment.


[016-144]Conversing freely on the evening of this visit, Colonel


[016-145]Ingersoll, in the abundance of his pleasure at the White


[016-146]House news, submitted to be interviewed, and with the


[016-147]following result.]


[016-148]_Question_.  By-the-way, Colonel, you knew Guiteau slightly, we


[016-149]believe.  Are you aware that it has been attempted to show that


[016-150]some money loaned or given him by yourself was really what he


[016-151]purchased the pistol with?


[016-152]_Answer_.  I knew Guiteau slightly; I saw him for the first time


[016-153]a few days after the inauguration.  He wanted a consulate, and


[016-154]asked me to give him a letter to Secretary Blaine.  I refused, on


[016-155]the ground that I didn't know him.  Afterwards he wanted me to lend


[016-156]him twenty-five dollars, and I declined.  I never loaned him a


[016-157]dollar in the world.  If I had, I should not feel that I was guilty


[016-158]of trying to kill the President.  On the principle that one would


[016-159]hold the man guilty who had innocently loaned the money with which


[016-160]he bought the pistol, you might convict the tailor who made his


[016-161]clothes.  If he had had no clothes he would not have gone to the


[016-162]depot naked, and the crime would not have been committed.  It is


[016-163]hard enough for the man who did lend him the money to lose that,


[016-164]without losing his reputation besides.  Nothing can exceed the


[016-165]utter absurdity of what has been said upon this subject.


[016-166]_Question_.  How did Guiteau impress you and what have you remembered,


[016-167]Colonel, of his efforts to reply to your lectures?


[016-168]_Answer_.  I do not know that Guiteau impressed me in any way.  He


[016-169]appeared like most other folks in search of a place or employment.


[016-170]I suppose he was in need.  He talked about the same as other people,


[016-171]and claimed that I ought to help him because he was from Chicago.


[016-172]The second time he came to see me he said that he hoped I had no


[016-173]prejudice against him on account of what he had said about me.  I


[016-174]told him that I never knew he had said anything against me.  I


[016-175]suppose now that he referred to what he had said in his lectures.


[016-176]He went about the country replying to me.  I have seen one or two


[016-177]of his lectures.  He used about the same arguments that Mr. Black


[016-178]uses in his reply to my article in the _North American Review_,


[016-179]and denounced me in about the same terms.  He is undoubtedly a man


[016-180]who firmly believes in the Old Testament, and has no doubt concerning


[016-181]the New.  I understand that he puts in most of his time now reading


[016-182]the Bible and rebuking people who use profane language in his


[016-183]presence.
