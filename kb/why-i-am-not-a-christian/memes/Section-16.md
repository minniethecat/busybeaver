[015-001]We find that the Chinese calculated eclipses long before Christ


[015-002]was born; and, exactness being the rule at that time, there is an


[015-003]account of two astronomers having been beheaded for failing to tell


[015-004]the coming of an eclipse to the minute; yet they were not Christians.


[015-005]There is another fact connected with Newton, and that is that he


[015-006]wrote a commentary on the Book of Revelation.  The probability is


[015-007]that a sillier commentary was never written.  It was so perfectly


[015-008]absurd and laughable that some one--I believe it was Voltaire--said


[015-009]that while Newton had excited the envy of the intellectual world


[015-010]by his mathematical accomplishments, it had gotten even with him


[015-011]the moment his commentaries were published.  Spinoza was not a


[015-012]mathematician, particularly.  He was a metaphysician, an honest


[015-013]thinker, whose influence is felt, and will be felt so long as these


[015-014]great questions have the slightest interest for the human brain.


[015-015]He also compares Chalmers with Hume.  Chalmers gained his notoriety


[015-016]from preaching what are known as the astronomical sermons, and, I


[015-017]suppose, was quite a preacher in his day.


[015-018]But Hume was a thinker, and his works will live for ages after Mr.


[015-019]Chalmers' sermons will have been forgotten.  Mr. Chalmers has never


[015-020]been prominent enough to have been well known by many people.  He


[015-021]may have been an exceedingly good man, and derived, during his


[015-022]life, great consolation from a belief in the damnation of infants.


[015-023]Mr. Newman also compares Wesley with Thomas Paine.  When Thomas


[015-024]Paine was in favor of human liberty, Wesley was against it.  Thomas


[015-025]Paine wrote a pamphlet called "Common Sense," urging the colonies


[015-026]to separate themselves from Great Britain.  Wesley wrote a treatise


[015-027]on the other side.  He was the enemy of human liberty; and if his


[015-028]advice could have been followed we would have been the colonies of


[015-029]Great Britain still.  We never would have had a President in need


[015-030]of a private chaplain.  Mr. Wesley had not a scientific mind.  He


[015-031]preached a sermon once on the cause and cure of earthquakes, taking


[015-032]the ground that earthquakes were caused by sins, and that the only


[015-033]way to stop them was to believe on the Lord Jesus Christ.  He also


[015-034]laid down some excellent rules for rearing children, that is, from


[015-035]a Methodist standpoint.  His rules amounted to about this:


[015-036]_First_.  Never give them what they want.


[015-037]_Second_.  Never give them what you intend to give them, at the time


[015-038]they want it.


[015-039]_Third_.  Break their wills at the earliest possible moment.


[015-040]Mr. Wesley made every family an inquisition, every father and mother


[015-041]inquisitors, and all the children helpless victims.  One of his


[015-042]homes would give an exceedingly vivid idea of hell.  At the same


[015-043]time, Mr. Wesley was a believer in witches and wizards, and knew


[015-044]all about the Devil.  At his request God performed many miracles.


[015-045]On several occasions he cured his horse of lameness.  On others,


[015-046]dissipated Mr. Wesley's headaches.  Now and then he put off rain


[015-047]on account of a camp meeting, and at other times stopped the wind


[015-048]blowing at the special request of Mr. Wesley.  I have no doubt that


[015-049]Mr. Wesley was honest in all this,--just as honest as he was


[015-050]mistaken.  And I also admit that he was the founder of a church


[015-051]that does extremely well in new countries, and that thousands of


[015-052]Methodists have been exceedingly good men.  But I deny that he ever


[015-053]did anything for human liberty.  While Mr. Wesley was fighting the


[015-054]Devil and giving his experience with witches and wizards, Thomas


[015-055]Paine helped to found a free nation, helped to enrich the air with


[015-056]another flag.  Wesley was right on one thing, though.  He was


[015-057]opposed to slavery, and, I believe, called it the sum of all


[015-058]villainies.  I have always been obliged to him for that.  I do not


[015-059]think he said it because he was a Methodist; but Methodism, as he


[015-060]understood it, did not prevent his saying it, and Methodism as


[015-061]others understood it, did not prevent men from being slaveholders,


[015-062]did not prevent them from selling babes from mothers, and in the


[015-063]name of God beating the naked back of toil.  I think, on the whole,


[015-064]Paine did more for the world than Mr. Wesley.  The difference


[015-065]between an average Methodist and an average Episcopalian is not


[015-066]worth quarreling about.  But the difference between a man who


[015-067]believes in despotism and one who believes in liberty is almost


[015-068]infinite.  Wesley changed Episcopalians into Methodists; Paine


[015-069]turned lickspittles into men.  Let it be understood, once for all,


[015-070]that I have never claimed that Paine was perfect.  I was very glad


[015-071]that the reverend gentleman admitted that he was a patriot and the


[015-072]foe of tyrants; that he sympathized with the oppressed, and befriended


[015-073]the helpless; that he favored religious toleration, and that he


[015-074]weakened the power of the Catholic Church.  I am glad that he made


[015-075]these admissions.  Whenever it can be truthfully said of a man that


[015-076]he loved his country, hated tyranny, sympathized with the oppressed,


[015-077]and befriended the helpless, nothing more is necessary.  If God


[015-078]can afford to damn such a man, such a man can afford to be damned.


[015-079]While Paine was the foe of tyrants, Christians were the tyrants.


[015-080]When he sympathized with the oppressed, the oppressed were the


[015-081]victims of Christians.  When he befriended the helpless, the helpless


[015-082]were the victims of Christians.  Paine never founded an inquisition;


[015-083]never tortured a human being; never hoped that anybody's tongue


[015-084]would be paralyzed, and was always opposed to private chaplains.


[015-085]It might be well for the reverend gentleman to continue his


[015-086]comparisons, and find eminent Christians to put, for instance,


[015-087]along with Humboldt, the Shakespeare of science; somebody by the


[015-088]side of Darwin, as a naturalist; some gentleman in England to stand


[015-089]with Tyndall, or Huxley; some Christian German to stand with Haeckel


[015-090]and Helmholtz.  May be he knows some Christian statesman that he


[015-091]would compare with Gambetta.  I would advise him to continue his


[015-092]parallels.


[015-093]_Question_.  What have you to say of the Rev. Dr. Fulton?


[015-094]_Answer_.  The Rev. Dr. Fulton is a great friend of mine.  I am


[015-095]extremely sorry to find that he still believes in a personal Devil,


[015-096]and I greatly regret that he imagines that this Devil has so much


[015-097]power that he can take possession of a human being and deprive God


[015-098]of their services.  It is in sorrow and not in anger, that I find


[015-099]that he still believes in this ancient superstition.  I also regret


[015-100]that he imagines that I am leading young men to eternal ruin.  It


[015-101]occurs to me that if there is an infinite God, he ought not to


[015-102]allow anybody to lead young men to eternal ruin.  If anything I


[015-103]have said, or am going to say, has a tendency to lead young men to


[015-104]eternal ruin, I hope that if there is a God with the power to


[015-105]prevent me, that he will use it.  Dr. Fulton admits that in politics


[015-106]I am on the right side.  I presume he makes this concession because


[015-107]he is a Republican.  I am in favor of universal education, of


[015-108]absolute intellectual liberty.  I am in favor, also, of equal rights


[015-109]to all.  As I have said before we have spent millions and millions


[015-110]of dollars and rivers of blood to free the bodies of men; in other


[015-111]words, we have been freeing the cages.  My proposition now is to


[015-112]give a little liberty to the birds.  I am not willing to stop where


[015-113]a man can simply reap the fruit of his hand.  I wish him, also, to


[015-114]enjoy the liberty of his brain.  I am not against any truth in the


[015-115]New Testament.  I did say that I objected to religion because it


[015-116]made enemies and not friends.  The Rev. Dr. says that is one reason


[015-117]why he likes religion.  Dr. Fulton tells me that the Bible is the


[015-118]gift of God to man.  He also tells me that the Bible is true, and


[015-119]that God is its author.  If the Bible is true and God is its author,


[015-120]then God was in favor of slavery four thousand years ago.  He was


[015-121]also in favor of polygamy and religious intolerance.  In other


[015-122]words, four thousand years ago he occupied the exact position the


[015-123]Devil is supposed to occupy now.  If the Bible teaches anything it


[015-124]teaches man to enslave his brother, that is to say, if his brother


[015-125]is a heathen.  The God of the Bible always hated heathens.  Dr.


[015-126]Fulton also says that the Bible is the basis of all law.  Yet, if


[015-127]the Legislature of New York would re-enact next winter the Mosaic


[015-128]code, the members might consider themselves lucky if they were not


[015-129]hung upon their return home.  Probably Dr. Fulton thinks that had


[015-130]it not been for the Ten Commandments, nobody would ever have thought


[015-131]that stealing was wrong.  I have always had an idea that men objected


[015-132]to stealing because the industrious did not wish to support the


[015-133]idle; and I have a notion that there has always been a law against


[015-134]murder, because a large majority of people have always objected to


[015-135]being murdered.  If he will read his Old Testament with care, he


[015-136]will find that God violated most of his own commandments--all except


[015-137]that "Thou shalt worship no other God before me," and, may be, the


[015-138]commandment against work on the Sabbath day.  With these two


[015-139]exceptions I am satisfied that God himself violated all the rest.


[015-140]He told his chosen people to rob the Gentiles; that violated the


[015-141]commandment against stealing.  He said himself that he had sent


[015-142]out lying spirits; that certainly was a violation of another


[015-143]commandment.  He ordered soldiers to kill men, women and babes;


[015-144]that was a violation of another.  He also told them to divide the


[015-145]maidens among the soldiers; that was a substantial violation of


[015-146]another.  One of the commandments was that you should not covet


[015-147]your neighbor's property.  In that commandment you will find that


[015-148]a man's wife is put on an equality with his ox.  Yet his chosen


[015-149]people were allowed not only to covet the property of the Gentiles,


[015-150]but to take it.  If Dr. Fulton will read a little more, he will


[015-151]find that all the good laws in the Decalogue had been in force in


[015-152]Egypt a century before Moses was born.  He will find that like laws


[015-153]and many better ones were in force in India and China, long before


[015-154]Moses knew what a bulrush was.  If he will think a little while,


[015-155]he will find that one of the Ten Commandments, the one on the


[015-156]subject of graven images, was bad.  The result of that was that


[015-157]Palestine never produced a painter, or a sculptor, and that no Jew


[015-158]became famous in art until long after the destruction of Jerusalem.


[015-159]A commandment that robs a people of painting and statuary is not


[015-160]a good one.  The idea of the Bible being the basis of law is almost


[015-161]too silly to be seriously refuted.  I admit that I did say that


[015-162]Shakespeare was the greatest man who ever lived; and Dr. Fulton


[015-163]says in regard to this statement, "What foolishness!"  He then


[015-164]proceeds to insult his audience by telling them that while many of


[015-165]them have copies of Shakespeare's works in their houses, they have


[015-166]not read twenty pages of them.  This fact may account for their


[015-167]attending his church and being satisfied with that sermon.  I do


[015-168]not believe to-day that Shakespeare is more influential than the


[015-169]Bible, but what influence Shakespeare has, is for good.  No man


[015-170]can read it without having his intellectual wealth increased.  When


[015-171]you read it, it is not necessary to throw away your reason.  Neither


[015-172]will you be damned if you do not understand it.  It is a book that


[015-173]appeals to everything in the human brain.  In that book can be


[015-174]found the wisdom of all ages.  Long after the Bible has passed out


[015-175]of existence, the name of Shakespeare will lead the intellectual


[015-176]roster of the world.  Dr. Fulton says there is not one work in the


[015-177]Bible that teaches that slavery or polygamy is right.  He also


[015-178]states that I know it.  If language has meaning--if words have


[015-179]sense, or the power to convey thought,--what did God mean when he


[015-180]told the Israelites to buy of the heathen round about, and that


[015-181]the heathen should be their bondmen and bondmaids forever?
