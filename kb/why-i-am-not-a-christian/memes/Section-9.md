[008-001]_Answer_.  My opinion is that the ideas the North fought for upon


[008-002]the field have at last triumphed at the ballot-box.  For several


[008-003]years after the Rebellion was put down the Southern ideas traveled


[008-004]North.  We lost West Virginia, New Jersey, Connecticut, New York


[008-005]and a great many congressional districts in other States.  We lost


[008-006]both houses of Congress and every Southern State.  The Southern


[008-007]ideas reached their climax in 1876.  In my judgment the tide has


[008-008]turned, and hereafter the Northern idea is going South.  The young


[008-009]men are on the Republican side.  The old Democrats are dying.  The


[008-010]cradle is beating the coffin.  It is a case of life and death, and


[008-011]life is ahead.  The heirs outnumber the administrators.


[008-012]_Question_.  What kind of a President will Garfield make?


[008-013]_Answer_.  My opinion is that he will make as good a President as


[008-014]this nation ever had.  He is fully equipped.  He is a trained


[008-015]statesman.  He has discussed all the great questions that have


[008-016]arisen for the last eighteen years, and with great ability.  He is


[008-017]a thorough scholar, a conscientious student, and takes an exceedingly


[008-018]comprehensive survey of all questions.  He is genial, generous and


[008-019]candid, and has all the necessary qualities of heart and brain to


[008-020]make a great President.  He has no prejudices.  Prejudice is the


[008-021]child and flatterer of ignorance.  He is firm, but not obstinate.


[008-022]The obstinate man wants his own way; the firm man stands by the


[008-023]right.  Andrew Johnson was obstinate--Lincoln was firm.


[008-024]_Question_.  How do you think he will treat the South?


[008-025]_Answer_.  Just the same as the North.  He will be the President


[008-026]of the whole country.  He will not execute the laws by the compass,


[008-027]but according to the Constitution.  I do not speak for General


[008-028]Garfield, nor by any authority from his friends.  No one wishes to


[008-029]injure the South.  The Republican party feels in honor bound to


[008-030]protect all citizens, white and black.  It must do this in order


[008-031]to keep its self-respect.  It must throw the shield of the Nation


[008-032]over the weakest, the humblest and the blackest citizen.  Any other


[008-033]course is suicide.  No thoughtful Southern man can object to this,


[008-034]and a Northern Democrat knows that it is right.


[008-035]_Question_.  Is there a probability that Mr. Sherman will be retained


[008-036]in the Cabinet?


[008-037]_Answer_.  I have no knowledge upon that question, and consequently


[008-038]have nothing to say.  My opinion about the Cabinet is, that General


[008-039]Garfield is well enough acquainted with public men to choose a


[008-040]Cabinet that will suit him and the country.  I have never regarded


[008-041]it as the proper thing to try and force a Cabinet upon a President.


[008-042]He has the right to be surrounded by his friends, by men in whose


[008-043]judgment and in whose friendship he has the utmost confidence, and


[008-044]I would no more think of trying to put some man in the Cabinet that


[008-045]I would think of signing a petition that a man should marry a


[008-046]certain woman.  General Garfield will, I believe, select his own


[008-047]constitutional advisers, and he will take the best he knows.


[008-048]_Question_.  What, in your opinion, is the condition of the Democratic


[008-049]party at present?


[008-050]_Answer_.  It must get a new set of principles, and throw away its


[008-051]prejudices.  It must demonstrate its capacity to govern the country


[008-052]by governing the States where it is in power.  In the presence of


[008-053]rebellion it gave up the ship.  The South must become Republican


[008-054]before the North will willingly give it power; that is, the great


[008-055]ideas of nationality are greater than parties, and if our flag is


[008-056]not large enough to protect every citizen, we must add a few more


[008-057]stars and stripes.  Personally I have no hatreds in this matter.


[008-058]The present is not only the child of the past, but the necessary


[008-059]child.  A statesman must deal with things as they are.  He must


[008-060]not be like Gladstone, who divides his time between foreign wars


[008-061]and amendments to the English Book of Common Prayer.


[008-062]_Question_.  How do you regard the religious question in politics?


[008-063]_Answer_.  Religion is a personal matter--a matter that each


[008-064]individual soul should be allowed to settle for itself.  No man


[008-065]shod in the brogans of impudence should walk into the temple of


[008-066]another man's soul.  While every man should be governed by the


[008-067]highest possible considerations of the public weal, no one has the


[008-068]right to ask for legal assistance in the support of his particular


[008-069]sect.  If Catholics oppose the public schools I would not oppose


[008-070]them because they are Catholics, but because I am in favor of the


[008-071]schools.  I regard the public school as the intellectual bread of


[008-072]life.  Personally I have no confidence in any religion that can be


[008-073]demonstrated only to children.  I suspect all creeds that rely


[008-074]implicitly on mothers and nurses.  That religion is the best that


[008-075]commends itself the strongest to men and women of education and


[008-076]genius.  After all, the prejudices of infancy and the ignorance of


[008-077]the aged are a poor foundation for any system of morals or faith.


[008-078]I respect every honest man, and I think more of a liberal Catholic


[008-079]than of an illiberal Infidel.  The religious question should be


[008-080]left out of politics.  You might as well decide questions of art


[008-081]and music by a ward caucus as to govern the longings and dreams of


[008-082]the soul by law.  I believe in letting the sun shine whether the


[008-083]weeds grow or not.  I can never side with Protestants if they try


[008-084]to put Catholics down by law, and I expect to oppose both of these


[008-085]until religious intolerance is regarded as a crime.


[008-086]_Question_.  Is the religious movement of which you are the chief


[008-087]exponent spreading?


[008-088]_Answer_.  There are ten times as many Freethinkers this year as


[008-089]there were last.  Civilization is the child of free thought.  The


[008-090]new world has drifted away from the rotting wharf of superstition.


[008-091]The politics of this country are being settled by the new ideas of


[008-092]individual liberty; and parties and churches that cannot accept


[008-093]the new truths must perish.  I want it perfectly understood that


[008-094]I am not a politician.  I believe in liberty and I want to see the


[008-095]time when every man, woman and child will enjoy every human right.


[008-096]The election is over, the passions aroused by the campaign will


[008-097]soon subside, the sober judgment of the people will, in my opinion,


[008-098]indorse the result, and time will indorse the indorsement.


[008-099]--_The Evening Express_, New York City, November 19, 1880.


[008-100]MIRACLES AND IMMORTALITY.


[008-101]_Question_.  You have seen some accounts of the recent sermon of


[008-102]Dr. Tyng on "Miracles," I presume, and if so, what is your opinion


[008-103]of the sermon, and also what is your opinion of miracles?


[008-104]_Answer_.  From an orthodox standpoint, I think the Rev. Dr. Tyng


[008-105]is right.  If miracles were necessary eighteen hundred years ago,


[008-106]before scientific facts enough were known to overthrow hundreds


[008-107]and thousands of passages in the Bible, certainly they are necessary


[008-108]now.  Dr. Tyng sees clearly that the old miracles are nearly worn


[008-109]out, and that some new ones are absolutely essential.  He takes


[008-110]for granted that, if God would do a miracle to found his gospel,


[008-111]he certainly would do some more to preserve it, and that it is in


[008-112]need of preservation about now is evident.  I am amazed that the


[008-113]religious world should laugh at him for believing in miracles.  It


[008-114]seems to me just as reasonable that the deaf, dumb, blind and lame,


[008-115]should be cured at Lourdes as at Palestine.  It certainly is no


[008-116]more wonderful that the law of nature should be broken now than


[008-117]that it was broken several thousand years ago.  Dr. Tyng also has


[008-118]this advantage.  The witnesses by whom he proves these miracles


[008-119]are alive.  An unbeliever can have the opportunity of cross-


[008-120]examination.  Whereas, the miracles in the New Testament are


[008-121]substantiated only by the dead.  It is just as reasonable to me


[008-122]that blind people receive their sight in France as that devils were


[008-123]made to vacate human bodies in the holy land.


[008-124]For one I am exceedingly glad that Dr. Tyng has taken this position.


[008-125]It shows that he is a believer in a personal God, in a God who is


[008-126]attending a little to the affairs of this world, and in a God who


[008-127]did not exhaust his supplies in the apostolic age.  It is refreshing


[008-128]to me to find in this scientific age a gentleman who still believes


[008-129]in miracles.  My opinion is that all thorough religionists will have


[008-130]to take the ground and admit that a supernatural religion must be


[008-131]supernaturally preserved.


[008-132]I have been asking for a miracle for several years, and have in a


[008-133]very mild, gentle and loving way, taunted the church for not


[008-134]producing a little one.  I have had the impudence to ask any number


[008-135]of them to join in a prayer asking anything they desire for the


[008-136]purpose of testing the efficiency of what is known as supplication.


[008-137]They answer me by calling my attention to the miracles recorded in


[008-138]the New Testament.  I insist, however, on a new miracle, and,


[008-139]personally, I would like to see one now.  Certainly, the Infinite


[008-140]has not lost his power, and certainly the Infinite knows that


[008-141]thousands and hundreds of thousands, if the Bible is true, are now


[008-142]pouring over the precipice of unbelief into the gulf of hell.  One


[008-143]little miracle would save thousands.  One little miracle in Pittsburg,


[008-144]well authenticated, would do more good than all the preaching ever


[008-145]heard in this sooty town.  The Rev. Dr. Tyng clearly sees this,


[008-146]and he has been driven to the conclusion, first, that God can do


[008-147]miracles; second, that he ought to, third, that he has.  In this


[008-148]he is perfectly logical.  After a man believes the Bible, after he


[008-149]believes in the flood and in the story of Jonah, certainly he ought


[008-150]not to hesitate at a miracle of to-day.  When I say I want a miracle,


[008-151]I mean by that, I want a good one.  All the miracles recorded in


[008-152]the New Testament could have been simulated.  A fellow could have


[008-153]pretended to be dead, or blind, or dumb, or deaf.  I want to see


[008-154]a good miracle.  I want to see a man with one leg, and then I want


[008-155]to see the other leg grow out.


[008-156]I would like to see a miracle like that performed in North Carolina.


[008-157]Two men were disputing about the relative merits of the salve they


[008-158]had for sale.  One of the men, in order to demonstrate that his


[008-159]salve was better than any other, cut off a dog's tail and applied


[008-160]a little of the salve to the stump, and, in the presence of the


[008-161]spectators, a new tail grew out.  But the other man, who also had


[008-162]salve for sale, took up the piece of tail that had been cast away,


[008-163]put a little salve at the end of that, and a new dog grew out, and


[008-164]the last heard of those parties they were quarrelling as to who


[008-165]owned the second dog.  Something like that is what I call a miracle.


[008-166]_Question_.  What do you believe about the immortality of the soul?


[008-167]Do you believe that the spirit lives as an individual after the


[008-168]body is dead?


[008-169]_Answer_.  I have said a great many times that it is no more


[008-170]wonderful that we should live again than that we do live.  Sometimes


[008-171]I have thought it not quite so wonderful for the reason that we


[008-172]have a start.  But upon that subject I have not the slightest


[008-173]information.  Whether man lives again or not I cannot pretend to


[008-174]say.  There may be another world and there may not be.  If there


[008-175]is another world we ought to make the best of it after arriving


[008-176]there.  If there is not another world, or if there is another world,


[008-177]we ought to make the best of this.  And since nobody knows, all


[008-178]should be permitted to have their opinions, and my opinion is that


[008-179]nobody knows.
