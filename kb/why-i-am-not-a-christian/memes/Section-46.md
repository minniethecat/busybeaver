[045-001]I have sometimes thought that I should like to see the laboring


[045-002]men in power, so that they would realize how little, after all,


[045-003]can be done by law.  All that any man should ask, so far as the


[045-004]Government is concerned, is a fair chance to compete with his


[045-005]neighbors.  Personally, I am for the abolition of all special


[045-006]privileges that are not for the general good.  My principal hope


[045-007]of the future is the civilization of my race; the development not


[045-008]only of the brain, but of the heart.  I believe the time will come


[045-009]when we shall stop raising failures, when we shall know something


[045-010]of the laws governing human beings.  I believe the time will come


[045-011]when we shall not produce deformed persons, natural criminals.  In


[045-012]other words, I think the world is going to grow better and better.


[045-013]This may not happen to this nation or to what we call our race,


[045-014]but it may happen to some other race, and all that we do in the


[045-015]right direction hastens that day and that race.


[045-016]_Question_.  Do you think that the old parties are about to die?


[045-017]_Answer_.  It is very hard to say.  The country is not old enough


[045-018]for tables of mortality to have been calculated upon parties.  I


[045-019]suppose a party, like anything else, has a period of youth, of


[045-020]manhood and decay.  The Democratic party is not dead.  Some men


[045-021]grow physically strong as they grow mentally weak.  The Democratic


[045-022]party lived out of office, and in disgrace, for twenty-five years,


[045-023]and lived to elect a President.  If the Democratic party could live


[045-024]on disgrace for twenty-five years it now looks as though the


[045-025]Republican party, on the memory of its glory and of its wonderful


[045-026]and unparalleled achievements, might manage to creep along for a


[045-027]few years more.


[045-028]--_New York World_, October 26, 1886.


[045-029]HENRY GEORGE AND SOCIALISM.


[045-030]_Question_.  What is your opinion of the result of the election?


[045-031]_Answer_.  I find many dead on the field whose faces I recognize.


[045-032]I see that Morrison has taken a "horizontal" position.  Free trade


[045-033]seems to have received an exceedingly black eye.  Carlisle, in my


[045-034]judgment, one of the very best men in Congress, has been defeated


[045-035]simply because he is a free trader, and I suppose you can account


[045-036]for Hurd's defeat in the same way.  The people believe in protection


[045-037]although they generally admit that the tariff ought to be reformed.


[045-038]I believe in protecting "infant industries," but I do not believe


[045-039]in rocking the cradle when the infant is seven feet high and wears


[045-040]number twelve boots.


[045-041]_Question_.  Do you sympathize with the Socialists, or do you think


[045-042]that the success of George would promote socialism?


[045-043]_Answer_.  I have said frequently that if I lived in Russia I should


[045-044]in all probability be a Nihilist.  I can conceive of no government


[045-045]that would not be as good as that of Russia, and I would consider


[045-046]_no_ government far preferable to that government.  Any possible


[045-047]state of anarchy is better than organized crime, because in the


[045-048]chaos of anarchy justice may be done by accident, but in a government


[045-049]organized for the perpetuation of slavery, and for the purpose of


[045-050]crushing out of the human brain every noble thought, justice does


[045-051]not live.  In Germany I would probably be a Socialist--to this


[045-052]extent, that I would want the political power honestly divided


[045-053]among the people.  I can conceive of no circumstance in which I


[045-054]could support Bismarck.  I regard Bismarck as a projection of the


[045-055]Middle Ages, as a shadow that has been thrown across the sunlight


[045-056]of modern civilization, and in that shadow grow all the bloodless


[045-057]crimes.  Now, in Ireland, of course, I believe in home rule.  In


[045-058]this country I am an Individualist.  The political power here is


[045-059]equally divided.  Poverty and wealth have the same power at the


[045-060]ballot-box.  Intelligence and ignorance are on an equality here,


[045-061]simply because all men have a certain interest in the government


[045-062]where they live.  I hate above all other things the tyranny of a


[045-063]government.  I do not want a government to send a policeman along


[045-064]with me to keep me from buying eleven eggs for a dozen.  I will


[045-065]take care of myself.  I want the people to do everything they can


[045-066]do, and the Government to keep its hands off, because if the


[045-067]Government attends to all these matters the people lose manhood,


[045-068]and in a little while become serfs, and there will arise some strong


[045-069]mind and some powerful hand that will reduce them to actual slavery.


[045-070]So I am in favor or personal liberty to the largest extent.  Whenever


[045-071]the Government grants privileges to the few, these privileges should


[045-072]be for the benefit of the many, and when they cease to be for the


[045-073]benefit of the many, they should be taken from the few and used by


[045-074]the government itself for the benefit of the whole people.  And I


[045-075]want to see in this country the Government so administered that


[045-076]justice will be done to all as nearly as human institutions can


[045-077]produce such a result.  Now, I understand that in any state of


[045-078]society there will be failures.  We have failures among the working


[045-079]people.  We have had some failures in Congress.  I will not mention


[045-080]the names, because your space is limited.  There have been failures


[045-081]in the pulpit, at the bar; in fact, in every pursuit of life you


[045-082]will presume we shall have failures with us for a great while; at


[045-083]least until the establishment of the religion of the body, when we


[045-084]shall cease to produce failures; and I have faith enough in the


[045-085]human race to believe that that time will come, but I do not expect


[045-086]it during my life.


[045-087]_Question_.  What do you think of the income tax as a step toward


[045-088]the accomplishment of what you desire?


[045-089]_Answer_.  There are some objections to an income tax.  First, the


[045-090]espionage that it produces on the part of the Government.  Second,


[045-091]the amount of perjury that it annually produces.  Men hate to have


[045-092]their business inquired into if they are not doing well.  They


[045-093]often pay a very large tax to make their creditors think they are


[045-094]prosperous.  Others by covering up, avoid the tax.  But I will say


[045-095]this with regard to taxation:  The great desideratum is stability.


[045-096]If we tax only the land, and that were the only tax, in a little


[045-097]while every other thing, and the value of every other thing, would


[045-098]adjust itself in relation to that tax, and perfect justice would


[045-099]be the result.  That is to say, if it were stable long enough the


[045-100]burden would finally fall upon the right backs in every department.


[045-101]The trouble with taxation is that it is continually changing--not


[045-102]waiting for the adjustment that will naturally follow provided it


[045-103]is stable.  I think the end, so far as land is concerned, could be


[045-104]reached by cumulative taxation--that is to say, a man with a certain


[045-105]amount of land paying a very small per cent., with more land, and


[045-106]increased per cent., and let that per cent. increase rapidly enough


[045-107]so that no man could afford to hold land that he did not have a


[045-108]use for.  So I believe in cumulative taxation in regard to any kind


[045-109]of wealth.  Let a man worth ten million dollars pay a greater per


[045-110]cent. than one worth one hundred thousand, because he is able to


[045-111]pay it.  The other day a man was talking to me about having the


[045-112]dead pay the expenses of the Government; that whenever a man died


[045-113]worth say five million dollars, one million should go to the


[045-114]Government; that if he died worth ten million dollars, three millions


[045-115]should go to the Government; if he died worth twenty million dollars,


[045-116]eight million should go to the Government, and so on.  He said that


[045-117]in this way the expenses of the Government could be borne by the


[045-118]dead.  I should be in favor of cumulative taxation upon legacies--


[045-119]the greater the legacy, the greater the per cent. of taxation.


[045-120]But, of course, I am not foolish enough to suppose that I understand


[045-121]these questions.  I am giving you a few guesses.  My only desire


[045-122]is to guess right.  I want to see the people of this world live


[045-123]for this world, and I hope the time will come when a civilized man


[045-124]will understand that he cannot be perfectly happy while anybody


[045-125]else is miserable; that a perfectly civilized man could not enjoy


[045-126]a dinner knowing that others were starving; that he could not enjoy


[045-127]the richest robes if he knew that some of his fellow-men in rags


[045-128]and tatters were shivering in the blast.  In other words, I want


[045-129]to carry out the idea there that I have so frequently uttered with


[045-130]regard to the other world; that is, that no gentleman angel could


[045-131]be perfectly happy knowing that somebody else was in hell.


[045-132]_Question_.  What are the chances for the Republican party in 1888?


[045-133]_Answer_.  If it will sympathize with the toilers, as it did with


[045-134]the slaves; if it will side with the needy; if it will only take


[045-135]the right side it will elect the next President.  The poor should


[045-136]not resort to violence; the rich should appeal to the intelligence


[045-137]of the working people.  These questions cannot be settled by envy


[045-138]and scorn.  The motto of both parties should be:  "Come, let us


[045-139]reason together."  The Republican party was the grandest organization


[045-140]that ever existed.  It was brave, intelligent and just.  It sincerely


[045-141]loved the right.  A certificate of membership was a patent of


[045-142]nobility.  If it will only stand by the right again, its victorious


[045-143]banner will float over all the intelligent sons of toil.


[045-144]--_The Times_, Chicago, Illinois, November 4, 1886.


[045-145]REPLY TO THE REV. B. F. MORSE.*


[045-146][* At the usual weekly meeting of the Baptist ministers at


[045-147]the Publication Rooms yesterday, the Rev. Dr. B. F. Morse


[045-148]read an essay on "Christianity vs. Materialism."  His


[045-149]contention was that all nature showed that design, not


[045-150]evolution, was its origin.


[045-151]In his concluding remarks Dr. Morse said that he knew from


[045-152]unquestionable authority, that Robert G. Ingersoll did not


[045-153]believe what he uttered in his lectures, and that to get out


[045-154]of a financial embarrassment he looked around for a money


[045-155]making scheme that could be put into immediate execution.


[045-156]To lecture against Christianity was the most rapid way of


[045-157]giving him the needed cash and, what was quite as acceptable


[045-158]to him, at the same time, notoriety.]


[045-159]This aquatic or web-footed theologian who expects to go to heaven


[045-160]by diving is not worth answering.  Nothing can be more idiotic than


[045-161]to answer an argument by saying he who makes it does not believe


[045-162]it.  Belief has nothing to do with the cogency or worth of an


[045-163]argument.  There is another thing.  This man, or rather this


[045-164]minister, says that I attacked Christianity simply to make money.


[045-165]Is it possible that, after preachers have had the field for eighteen


[045-166]hundred years, the way to make money is to attack the clergy?  Is


[045-167]this intended as a slander against me or the ministers?


[045-168]The trouble is that my arguments cannot be answered.  All the


[045-169]preachers in the world cannot prove that slavery is better than


[045-170]liberty.  They cannot show that all have not an equal right to


[045-171]think.  They cannot show that all have not an equal right to express


[045-172]their thoughts.  They cannot show that a decent God will punish a


[045-173]decent man for making the best guess he can.  This is all there is


[045-174]about it.


[045-175]--_The Herald_, New York, December 14, 1886.


[045-176]INGERSOLL ON McGLYNN.


[045-177]The attitude of the Roman Catholic Church in Dr. McGlynn's case is


[045-178]consistent with the history and constitution of the Catholic Church


[045-179]--perfectly consistent with its ends, its objects, and its means--


[045-180]and just as perfectly inconsistent with intellectual liberty and


[045-181]the real civilization of the human race.
