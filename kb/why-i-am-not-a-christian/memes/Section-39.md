[038-001]_Question_.  Do you think that the American people are seeking


[038-002]after truth, or do they want to be amused?


[038-003]_Answer_.  We have all kinds.  Thousands are earnestly seeking for


[038-004]the truth.  They are looking over the old creeds, they are studying


[038-005]the Bible for themselves, they have the candor born of courage,


[038-006]they are depending upon themselves instead of on the clergy.  They


[038-007]have found out that the clergy do not know; that their sources of


[038-008]information are not reliable; that, like the politicians, many


[038-009]ministers preach one way and talk another.  The doctrine of eternal


[038-010]pain has driven millions from the church.  People with good hearts


[038-011]cannot get consolation out of that cruel lie.  The ministers


[038-012]themselves are getting ashamed to call that doctrine "the tidings


[038-013]of great joy."  The American people are a serious people.  They


[038-014]want to know the truth.  They fell that whatever the truth may be


[038-015]they have the courage to hear it.  The American people also have


[038-016]a sense of humor.  They like to see old absurdities punctured and


[038-017]solemn stupidity held up to laughter.  They are, on the average,


[038-018]the most intelligent people on the earth.  They can see the point.


[038-019]Their wit is sharp, quick and logical.  Nothing amuses them more


[038-020]that to see the mask pulled from the face of sham.  The average


[038-021]American is generous, intelligent, level-headed, manly, and good-


[038-022]natured.


[038-023]_Question_.  What, in your judgment, is the source of the greatest


[038-024]trouble among men?


[038-025]_Answer_.  Superstition.  That has caused more agony, more tears,


[038-026]persecution and real misery than all other causes combined.  The


[038-027]other name for superstition is ignorance.  When men learn that all


[038-028]sin is a mistake, that all dishonesty is a blunder, that even


[038-029]intelligent selfishness will protect the rights of others, there


[038-030]will be vastly more happiness in this world.  Shakespeare says that


[038-031]"There is no darkness but ignorance."  Sometime man will learn that


[038-032]when he steals from another, he robs himself--that the way to be


[038-033]happy is to make others so, and that it is far better to assist


[038-034]his fellow-man than to fast, say prayers, count beads or build


[038-035]temples to the Unknown.  Some people tell us that selfishness is


[038-036]the only sin, but selfishness grows in the soil of ignorance.


[038-037]After all, education is the great lever, and the only one capable


[038-038]of raising mankind.  People ignorant of their own rights are ignorant


[038-039]of the rights of others.  Every tyrant is the slave of ignorance.


[038-040]_Question_.  How soon do you think we would have the millennium if


[038-041]every person attended strictly to his own business?


[038-042]_Answer_.  Now, if every person were intelligent enough to know


[038-043]his own business--to know just where his rights ended and the rights


[038-044]of others commenced, and then had the wisdom and honesty to act


[038-045]accordingly, we should have a very happy world.  Most people like


[038-046]to control the conduct of others.  They love to write rules, and


[038-047]pass laws for the benefit of their neighbors, and the neighbors


[038-048]are pretty busy at the same business.  People, as a rule, think


[038-049]that they know the business of other people better than they do


[038-050]their own.  A man watching others play checkers or chess always


[038-051]thinks he sees better moves than the players make.  When all people


[038-052]attend to their own business they will know that a part of their


[038-053]own business is to increase the happiness of others.


[038-054]_Question_.  What is causing the development of this country?


[038-055]_Answer_.  Education, the free exchange of ideas, inventions by


[038-056]which the forces of nature become our servants, intellectual


[038-057]hospitality, a willingness to hear the other side, the richness of


[038-058]our soil, the extent of our territory, the diversity of climate


[038-059]and production, our system of government, the free discussion of


[038-060]political questions, our social freedom, and above all, the fact


[038-061]that labor is honorable.


[038-062]_Question_.  What is your opinion of the religious tendency of the


[038-063]people of this country?


[038-064]_Answer_.  Using the word religion in its highest and best sense,


[038-065]the people are becoming more religious.  We are far more religious


[038-066]--using the word in its best sense--than when we believed in human


[038-067]slavery, but we are not as orthodox as we were then.  We have more


[038-068]principle and less piety.  We care more for the right and less for


[038-069]the creed.  The old orthodox dogmas are mouldy.  You will find moss


[038-070]on their backs.  They are only brought out when a new candidate


[038-071]for the ministry is to be examined.  Only a little while ago in


[038-072]New York a candidate for the Presbyterian pulpit was examined and


[038-073]the following is a part of the examination:


[038-074]_Question_.  "Do you believe in eternal punishment, as set forth


[038-075]in the confession of faith?"


[038-076]_Answer_.  (With some hesitation) "Yes, I do."


[038-077]_Question_.  "Have you preached on that subject lately?"


[038-078]_Answer_.  "No.  I prepared a sermon on hell, in which I took the


[038-079]ground that the punishment of the wicked will be endless, and have


[038-080]it with me."


[038-081]_Question_.  "Did you deliver it?"


[038-082]_Answer_.  "No.  I thought that my congregation would not care to


[038-083]hear it.  The doctrine is rather unpopular where I have been


[038-084]preaching, and I was afraid I might do harm, so I have not delivered


[038-085]it yet."


[038-086]_Question_.  "But you believe in eternal damnation, do you not?"


[038-087]_Answer_.  "O yes, with all my heart."


[038-088]He was admitted, and the admission proves the dishonesty of the


[038-089]examiners and the examined.  The new version of the Old and New


[038-090]Testaments has done much to weaken confidence in the doctrine of


[038-091]inspiration.  It has occurred to a good many that if God took the


[038-092]pains to inspire men to write the Bible, he ought to have inspired


[038-093]others to translate it correctly.  The general tendency today is


[038-094]toward science, toward naturalism, toward what is called Infidelity,


[038-095]but is in fact fidelity.  Men are in a transition state, and the


[038-096]people, on the average, have more real good, sound sense to-day


[038-097]than ever before.  The church is losing its power for evil.  The


[038-098]old chains are wearing out, and new ones are not being made.  The


[038-099]tendency is toward intellectual freedom, and that means the final


[038-100]destruction of the orthodox bastille.


[038-101]_Question_.  What is your opinion of General Grant as he stands


[038-102]before the people to-day?


[038-103]_Answer_.  I have always regarded General Grant as the greatest


[038-104]soldier this continent has produced.  He is to-day the most


[038-105]distinguished son of the Republic.  The people have the greatest


[038-106]confidence in his ability, his patriotism and his integrity.  The


[038-107]financial disaster impoverished General Grant, but he did not stain


[038-108]the reputation of the grand soldier who led to many victories the


[038-109]greatest army that ever fought for the liberties of man.


[038-110]--_Iowa State Register_, May 23, 1885.


[038-111]HELL OR SHEOL AND OTHER SUBJECTS.


[038-112]_Question_.  Colonel, have you read the revised Testament?


[038-113]_Answer_.  Yes, but I don't believe the work has been fairly done.


[038-114]The clergy are not going to scrape the butter off their own bread.


[038-115]The clergy are offensive partisans, and those of each denomination


[038-116]will interpret the Scriptures their way.  No Baptist minister would


[038-117]countenance a "Revision" that favored sprinkling, and no Catholic


[038-118]priest would admit that any version would be correct that destroyed


[038-119]the dogma of the "real presence."  So I might go through all the


[038-120]denominations.


[038-121]_Question_.  Why was the word sheol introduced in place of hell,


[038-122]and how do you like the substitute?


[038-123]_Answer_.  The civilized world has outgrown the vulgar and brutal


[038-124]hell of their fathers and founders of the churches.  The clergy


[038-125]are ashamed to preach about sulphurous flames and undying worms.


[038-126]The imagination of the world has been developed, the heart has


[038-127]grown tender, and the old dogma of eternal pain shocks all civilized


[038-128]people.  It is becoming disgraceful either to preach or believe in


[038-129]such a beastly lie.  The clergy are beginning to think that it is


[038-130]hardly manly to frighten children with a detected falsehood.  Sheol


[038-131]is a great relief.  It is not so hot as the old place.  The nights


[038-132]are comfortable, and the society is quite refined.  The worms are


[038-133]dead, and the air reasonably free from noxious vapors.  It is a


[038-134]much worse word to hold a revival with, but much better for every


[038-135]day use.  It will hardly take the place of the old word when people


[038-136]step on tacks, put up stoves, or sit on pins; but for use at church


[038-137]fairs and mite societies it will do about as well.  We do not need


[038-138]revision; excision is what we want.  The barbarism should be taken


[038-139]out of the Bible.  Passages upholding polygamy, wars of extermination,


[038-140]slavery, and religious persecution should not be attributed to a


[038-141]perfect God.  The good that is in the Bible will be saved for man,


[038-142]and man will be saved from the evil that is in that book.  Why


[038-143]should we worship in God what we detest in man?


[038-144]_Question_.  Do you think the use of the word sheol will make any


[038-145]difference to the preachers?


[038-146]_Answer_.  Of course it will make no difference with Talmage.  He


[038-147]will make sheol just as hot and smoky and uncomfortable as hell,


[038-148]but the congregations will laugh instead of tremble.  The old


[038-149]shudder has gone.  Beecher had demolished hell before sheol was


[038-150]adopted.  According to his doctrine of evolution hell has been


[038-151]slowly growing cool.  The cindered souls do not even perspire.


[038-152]Sheol is nothing to Mr. Beecher but a new name for an old mistake.


[038-153]As for the effect it will have on Heber Newton, I cannot tell,


[038-154]neither can he, until he asks his bishop.  There are people who


[038-155]believe in witches and madstones and fiat money, and centuries


[038-156]hence it may be that people will exist who will believe as firmly


[038-157]in hell as Dr. Shedd does now.


[038-158]_Question_.  What about Beecher's sermons on "Evolution"?


[038-159]_Answer_.  Beecher's sermons on "Evolution" will do good.  Millions


[038-160]of people believe that Mr. Beecher knows at least as much as the


[038-161]other preachers, and if he regards the atonement as a dogma with


[038-162]a mistake for a foundation, they may conclude that the whole system


[038-163]is a mistake.  But whether Mr. Beecher is mistaken or not, people


[038-164]know that honesty is a good thing, that gratitude is a virtue, that


[038-165]industry supports the world, and that whatever they believe about


[038-166]religion they are bound by every conceivable obligation to be just


[038-167]and generous.  Mr. Beecher can no more succeed in reconciling


[038-168]science and religion, than he could in convincing the world that


[038-169]triangles and circles are exactly the same.  There is the same


[038-170]relation between science and religion that there is between astronomy


[038-171]and astrology, between alchemy and chemistry, between orthodoxy


[038-172]and common sense.


[038-173]_Question_.  Have you read Miss Cleveland's book?  She condemns


[038-174]George Eliot's poetry on the ground that it has no faith in it,


[038-175]nothing beyond.  Do you imagine she would condemn Burns or Shelley


[038-176]for that reason?


[038-177]_Answer_.  I have not read Miss Cleveland's book; but, if the author


[038-178]condemns the poetry of George Eliot, she has made a mistake.  There


[038-179]is no poem in our language more beautiful than "The Lovers," and


[038-180]none loftier or purer than "The Choir Invisible."  There is no


[038-181]poetry in the "beyond."  The poetry is here--here in this world,


[038-182]where love is in the heart.  The poetry of the beyond is too far


[038-183]away, a little too general.  Shelley's "Skylark" was in our sky,


[038-184]the daisy of Burns grew on our ground, and between that lark and


[038-185]that daisy is room for all the real poetry of the earth.


[038-186]--_Evening Record_, Boston, Mass., 1885.


[038-187]INTERVIEWING, POLITICS AND SPIRITUALISM.


[038-188]_Question_.  What is your opinion of the peculiar institution of


[038-189]American journalism known as interviewing?


[038-190]_Answer_.  If the interviewers are fair, if they know how to ask


[038-191]questions of a public nature, if they remember what is said, or


[038-192]write it at the time, and if the interviewed knows enough to answer


[038-193]questions in a way to amuse or instruct the public, then interviewing


[038-194]is a blessing.  But if the representative of the press asks questions,


[038-195]either impudent or unimportant, and the answers are like the


[038-196]questions, then the institution is a failure.  When the journalist


[038-197]fails to see the man he wishes to interview, or when the man refuses


[038-198]to be interviewed, and thereupon the aforesaid journalist writes


[038-199]up an interview, doing the talking for both sides, the institution


[038-200]is a success.  Such interviews are always interesting, and, as a


[038-201]rule, the questions are to the point and the answers perfectly


[038-202]responsive.  There is probably a little too much interviewing, and


[038-203]to many persons are asked questions upon subjects about which they


[038-204]know nothing.  Mr. Smith makes some money in stocks or pork, visits


[038-205]London, and remains in that city for several weeks.  On his return


[038-206]he is interviewd as to the institutions, laws and customs of the


[038-207]British Empire.  Of course such an interview is exceedingly


[038-208]instructive.  Lord Affanaff lands at the dock in North River, is


[038-209]driven to a hotel in a closed carriage, is interviewed a few minutes


[038-210]after by a representative of the _Herald_ as to his view of the


[038-211]great Republic based upon what he has seen.  Such an interview is


[038-212]also instructive.  Interviews with candidates as to their chances


[038-213]of election is another favorite way of finding out their honest


[038-214]opinion, but people who rely on those interviews generally lose


[038-215]their bets.  The most interesting interviews are generally denied.


[038-216]I have been expecting to see an interview with the Rev. Dr. Leonard


[038-217]on the medicinal properties of champagne and toast, or the relation


[038-218]between old ale and modern theology, and as to whether prohibition


[038-219]prohibits the Prohibitionists.
