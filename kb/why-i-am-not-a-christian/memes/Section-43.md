[042-001]Only a few years ago slavery was upheld by the same Bible.  Slavery


[042-002]having been abolished, the passages in the inspired volume upholding


[042-003]it have been mostly forgotten, but polygamy lives, and the polygamists,


[042-004]with great volubility, repeat the passages in their favor.  We send


[042-005]our missionaries to Utah, with their Bibles, to convert the Mormons.


[042-006]The Mormons show, by these very Bibles, that God is on their side.


[042-007]Nothing remain now for the missionaries except to get back their


[042-008]Bibles and come home.  The preachers do not appeal to the Bible


[042-009]for the purpose of putting down Mormonism.  They say:  "Send the


[042-010]army."  If the people of this country could only be honest; if they


[042-011]would only admit that the Old Testament is but the record of a


[042-012]barbarous people; if the Samson of the nineteenth century would


[042-013]not allow its limbs to be bound by the Delilah of superstition, it


[042-014]could with one blow destroy this monster.  What shall we say of


[042-015]the moral force of Christianity, when it utterly fails in the


[042-016]presence of Mormonism?  What shall we say of a Bible that we dare


[042-017]not read to a Mormon as an argument against legalized lust, or as


[042-018]an argument against illegal lust?


[042-019]I am opposed to polygamy.  I want it exterminated by law; but I


[042-020]hate to see the exterminators insist that God, only a few thousand


[042-021]years ago, was as bad as the Mormons are to-day.  In my judgment,


[042-022]such a God ought to be exterminated.


[042-023]_Question_.  What do you think of men like the Rev. Henry Ward


[042-024]Beecher and the Rev. R. Heber Newton?  Do they deserve any credit


[042-025]for the course they have taken?


[042-026]_Answer_.  Mr. Beecher is evidently endeavoring to shore up the


[042-027]walls of the falling temple.  He sees the cracks; he knows that


[042-028]the building is out of plumb; he feels that the foundation is


[042-029]insecure.  Lies can take the place of stones only so long as they


[042-030]are thoroughly believed.  Mr. Beecher is trying to do something to


[042-031]harmonize superstition and science.  He is reading between the


[042-032]lines.  He has discovered that Darwin is only a later Saint Paul,


[042-033]or that Saint Paul was the original Darwin.  He is endeavoring to


[042-034]make the New Testament a scientific text-book.  Of course he will


[042-035]fail.  But his intentions are good.  Thousands of people will read


[042-036]the New Testament with more freedom than heretofore.  They will


[042-037]look for new meanings; and he who looks for new meanings will not


[042-038]be satisfied with the old ones.  Mr. Beecher, instead of strengthening


[042-039]the walls, will make them weaker.


[042-040]There is no harmony between religion and science.  When science


[042-041]was a child, religion sought to strangle it in the cradle.  Now


[042-042]that science has attained its youth, and superstition is in its


[042-043]dotage, the trembling, palsied wreck says to the athlete:  "Let us


[042-044]be friends."  It reminds me of the bargain the cock wished to make


[042-045]with the horse:  "Let us agree not to step on each other's feet."


[042-046]Mr. Beecher, having done away with hell, substitutes annihilation.


[042-047]His doctrine at present is that only a fortunate few are immortal,


[042-048]and that the great mass return to dreamless dust.  This, of course,


[042-049]is far better than hell, and is a great improvement on the orthodox


[042-050]view.  Mr. Beecher cannot believe that God would make such a mistake


[042-051]as to make men doomed to suffer eternal pain.  Why, I ask, should


[042-052]God give life to men whom he knows are unworthy of life?  Why should


[042-053]he annihilate his mistakes?  Why should he make mistakes that need


[042-054]annihilation?


[042-055]It can hardly be said that Mr. Beecher's idea is a new one.  It


[042-056]was taught, with an addition, thousands of years ago, in India,


[042-057]and the addition almost answers my objection.  The old doctrine


[042-058]was that only the soul that bears fruit, only the soul that bursts


[042-059]into blossom, will at the death of the body rejoin the Infinite,


[042-060]and that all other souls--souls not having blossomed--will go back


[042-061]into low forms and make the journey up to man once more, and should


[042-062]they then blossom and bear fruit, will be held worthy to join the


[042-063]Infinite, but should they again fail, they again go back; and this


[042-064]process is repeated until they do blossom, and in this way all


[042-065]souls at last become perfect.  I suggest that Mr. Beecher make at


[042-066]least this addition to his doctrine.


[042-067]But allow me to say that, in my judgment, Mr. Beecher is doing


[042-068]great good.  He may not convince many people that he is right, but


[042-069]he will certainly convince a great many people that Christianity


[042-070]is wrong.


[042-071]_Question_.  In what estimation do you hold Charles Watts and Samuel


[042-072]Putnam, and what do you think of their labors in the cause of


[042-073]Freethought?


[042-074]_Answer_.  Mr. Watts is an extremely logical man, with a direct


[042-075]and straightforward manner and mind.  He has paid great attention


[042-076]to what is called "Secularism."  He thoroughly understands


[042-077]organization, and he is undoubtedly one of the strongest debaters


[042-078]in the field.  He has had great experience.  He has demolished more


[042-079]divines than any man of my acquaintance.  I have read several of


[042-080]his debates.  In discussion he is quick, pertinent, logical, and,


[042-081]above all, good natured.


[042-082]There is not in all he says a touch of malice.  He can afford to


[042-083]be generous to his antagonists, because he is always the victor,


[042-084]and is always sure of the victory.  Last winter wherever I went,


[042-085]I heard the most favorable accounts of Mr. Watts.  All who heard


[042-086]him were delighted.


[042-087]Mr. Putnam is one of the most thorough believers in intellectual


[042-088]liberty in the world.  He believes with all his heart, is full of


[042-089]enthusiasm, ready to make any sacrifice, and to endure any hardship.


[042-090]Had he lived a few years ago, he would have been a martyr.  He has


[042-091]written some of the most stirring appeals to the Liberals of this


[042-092]country that I have ever read.  He believes that Freethought has


[042-093]a future; that the time is coming when the superstitions of the


[042-094]world will either be forgotten, or remembered--some of them with


[042-095]smiles--most of them with tears.  Mr. Putnam, although endowed with


[042-096]a poetic nature, with poetic insight, clings to the known, builds


[042-097]upon the experience of man, and believes in fancies only when they


[042-098]are used as the wings of a fact.  I have never met a man who appeared


[042-099]to be more thoroughly devoted to the great cause of mental freedom.


[042-100]I have read his books with great interest, and find in them many


[042-101]pages filled with philosophy and pathos.  I have met him often and


[042-102]I never heard him utter a harsh word about any human being.  His


[042-103]good nature is as unfailing as the air.  His abilities are of the


[042-104]highest order.  It is a positive pleasure to meet him.  He is so


[042-105]enthusiastic, so unselfish, so natural, so appreciative of others,


[042-106]so thoughtful for the cause, and so careless of himself, that he


[042-107]compels the admiration of every one who really loves the just and


[042-108]true.


[042-109]--_The Truth Seeker_, New York, September 5, 1885.


[042-110]THE PRESIDENT AND SENATE.


[042-111]_Question_.  What have you to say with reference to the respective


[042-112]attitudes of the President and Senate?


[042-113]_Answer_.  I don't think there is any doubt as to the right of the


[042-114]Senate to call on the President for information.  Of course that


[042-115]means for what information he has.  When a duty devolves upon two


[042-116]persons, one of them has no right to withhold any facts calculated


[042-117]to throw any light on the question that both are to decide.  The


[042-118]President cannot appoint any officer who has to be confirmed by


[042-119]the Senate; he can simply nominate.  The Senate cannot even suggest


[042-120]a name; it can only pass upon the person nominated.  If it is called


[042-121]upon for counsel and advice, how can it give advice without knowing


[042-122]the facts and circumstances?  The President must have a reason for


[042-123]wishing to make a change.  He should give that reason to the Senate


[042-124]without waiting to be asked.  He has assured the country that he


[042-125]is a civil service reformer; that no man is to be turned out because


[042-126]he is a Republican, and no man appointed because he is a Democrat.


[042-127]Now, the Senate has given the President an opportunity to prove


[042-128]that he has acted as he has talked.  If the President feels that


[042-129]he is bound to carry out the civil-service law, ought not the Senate


[042-130]to feel in the same way?  Is it not the duty of the Senate to see


[042-131]to it that the President does not, with its advice and consent,


[042-132]violate the civil service law?  Is the consent of the Senate a mere


[042-133]matter of form?  In these appointments the President is not


[042-134]independent of or above the Senate; they are equal, and each has


[042-135]the right to be "honor bright" with the other, at least.


[042-136]As long as this foolish law is unrepealed it must be carried out.


[042-137]Neither party is in favor of civil service reform, and never was.


[042-138]The Republican party did not carry it out, and did not intend to.


[042-139]The President has the right to nominate.  Under the law as it is


[042-140]now, when the President wants to appoint a clerk, or when one of


[042-141]his secretaries wants one, four names are sent, and from these four


[042-142]names a choice has to be made.  This is clearly an invasion of the


[042-143]rights of the Executive.  If they have the right to compel the


[042-144]President to choose from four, why not from three, or two?  Why


[042-145]not name the one, and have done with it?  The law is worse than


[042-146]unconstitutional--it is absurd.


[042-147]But in this contest the Senate, in my judgment, is right.  In my


[042-148]opinion, by the time Cleveland goes out most of the offices will


[042-149]be filled with Democrats.  If the Republicans succeed next time,


[042-150]I know, and everybody knows, that they will never rest easy until


[042-151]they get the Democrats out.  They will shout "offensive partisanship."


[042-152]The truth is, the theory is wrong.  Every citizen should take an


[042-153]interest in politics.  A good man should not agree to keep silent


[042-154]just for the sake of an office.  A man owes his best thoughts to


[042-155]his country.  If he ought to defend his country in time of war,


[042-156]and under certain circumstances give his life for it, can we say


[042-157]that in time of peace he is under no obligation to discharge what


[042-158]he believes to be a duty, if he happens to hold an office?  Must


[042-159]he sell his birthright for the sake of being a doorkeeper?  The


[042-160]whole doctrine is absurd and never will be carried out.


[042-161]_Question_.  What do you think as to the presidential race?


[042-162]_Answer_.  That is a good way off.  I think the people can hardly


[042-163]be roused to enthusiasm by the old names.  Our party must take


[042-164]another step forward.  We cannot live on what we have done; we must


[042-165]seek power for the sake, not of power, but for the accomplishment


[042-166]of a purpose.  We must reform the tariff.  We must settle the


[042-167]question of silver.  We must have sense enough to know what the


[042-168]country needs, and courage enough to tell it.  By reforming the


[042-169]tariff, I mean protect that and that only that needs protection--


[042-170]laws for the country and not for the few.  We want honest money;


[042-171]we want a dollar's worth of gold in a silver dollar, and a dollar's


[042-172]worth of silver in a gold dollar.  We want to make them of equal


[042-173]value.  Bi-metallism does not mean that eighty cents' worth of


[042-174]silver is worth one hundred in gold.  The Republican party must


[042-175]get back its conscience and be guided by it in deciding the questions


[042-176]that arise.  Great questions are pressing for solution.  Thousands


[042-177]of working people are in want.  Business is depressed.  The future


[042-178]is filled with clouds.  What does the Republican party propose?


[042-179]Must we wait for mobs to inaugurate reform?  Must we depend on


[042-180]police or statesmen?  Should we wait and crush by brute force or


[042-181]should we prevent?
