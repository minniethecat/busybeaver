# Paul the Apostle

**Paul the Apostle** (c. 5–64 CE), also known as Saint Paul and Paul of Tarsus, was an early Christian missionary and theologian. In "Why I Am Not a Christian," [[robert-ingersoll|Robert G. Ingersoll]] examines Paul's role in shaping Christian doctrine, particularly the concept of resurrection.

## In Ingersoll's Writings

### Paul in the Book of Acts

Ingersoll devotes considerable attention to the biblical account of Paul in the Book of Acts. He recounts the episode of "Paul having been mobbed in the cit" (005-034) of Jerusalem, and notes that "when he had given him license, Paul stood on the stairs and" (005-040) addressed the crowd. Ingersoll examines "the speech of Paul, wherein he gives an account" (005-043) of his conversion experience.

Ingersoll expresses skepticism about Paul's account, noting that "it is a little curious to me that Paul" (005-044) would need to recount his conversion in such detail if the events were as miraculous as claimed. He also questions whether the circumstances in Jerusalem at that time, "or Paul would not have addressed the" (005-053) crowd in the manner described, align with the biblical narrative.

### Doctrine of Resurrection

Paul's theology of resurrection is a central point of Ingersoll's critique. He cites "the doctrine of Paul when he says: 'Behold, I show'" (019-039) a mystery concerning the resurrection of the dead. Ingersoll questions whether Paul genuinely believed in a physical resurrection, noting that "Paul certainly" (019-046) had a conception of resurrection that differed from later Christian orthodoxy.

### Paul and Darwin

In a striking rhetorical move, Ingersoll draws a parallel between Paul and [[charles-darwin|Charles Darwin]], suggesting that "Darwin is only a later Saint Paul" (042-032) or "that Saint Paul was the original Darwin" (042-033). This analogy serves to naturalize Paul's religious innovations as evolutionary adaptations of ideas rather than divine revelations.
