[062-001]--_New York World_, August 5, 1888.


[062-002]WOMAN'S RIGHT TO DIVORCE.


[062-003]_Question_.  Col. Robert G. Ingersoll, the great Agnostic, has


[062-004]always been an ardent defender of the sanctity of the home and of


[062-005]the marriage relation.  Apropos of the horrible account of a man's


[062-006]tearing out the eyes of his wife at Far Rockaway last week, Colonel


[062-007]Ingersoll was asked what recourse a woman had under such


[062-008]circumstances?


[062-009]_Answer_.  I read the account, and I don't remember of ever having


[062-010]read anything more perfectly horrible and cruel.  It is impossible


[062-011]for me to imagine such a monster, or to account for such an inhuman


[062-012]human being.  How a man could deprive a human being of sight, except


[062-013]where some religious question is involved, is beyond my comprehension.


[062-014]We know that for many centuries frightful punishments were inflicted,


[062-015]and inflicted by the pious, by the theologians, by the spiritual


[062-016]minded, and by those who "loved their neighbors as themselves."


[062-017]We read the accounts of how the lids of men's eyes were cut off


[062-018]and then the poor victims tied where the sum would shine upon their


[062-019]lifeless orbs; of others who were buried alive; of others staked


[062-020]out on the sands of the sea, to be drowned by the rising tide; of


[062-021]others put in sacks filled with snakes.  Yet these things appeared


[062-022]far away, and we flattered ourselves that, to a great degree, the


[062-023]world had outgrown these atrocities; and now, here, near the close


[062-024]of the nineteenth century, we find a man--a husband--cruel enough


[062-025]to put out the eyes of the woman he swore to love, protect and


[062-026]cherish.  This man has probably been taught that there is forgiveness


[062-027]for every crime, and now imagines that when he repents there will


[062-028]be more joy in heaven over him than over ninety and nine good and


[062-029]loving husbands who have treated their wives in the best possible


[062-030]manner, and who, instead of tearing out their eyes, have filled


[062-031]their lives with content and covered their faces with kisses.


[062-032]_Question_.  You told me, last week, in a general way, what society


[062-033]should do with the husband in such a case as that.  I would like


[062-034]to ask you to-day, what you think society ought to do with the wife


[062-035]in such a case, or what ought the wife to be permitted to do for


[062-036]herself?


[062-037]_Answer_.  When we take into consideration the crime of the man


[062-038]who blinded his wife, it is impossible not to think of the right


[062-039]of divorce.  Many people insist that marriage is an indissoluble


[062-040]tie; that nothing can break it, and that nothing can release either


[062-041]party from the bond.  Now, take this case at Far Rockaway.  One


[062-042]year ago the husband tore out one of his wife's eyes.  Had she then


[062-043]good cause for divorce?  Is it possible that an infinitely wise


[062-044]and good God would insist on this poor, helpless woman remaining


[062-045]with the wild beast, her husband?  Can anyone imagine that such a


[062-046]course would add to the joy of Paradise, or even tend to keep one


[062-047]harp in tune?  Can the good of society require the woman to remain?


[062-048]She did remain, and the result is that the other eye has been torn


[062-049]from its socket by the hands of the husband.  Is she entitled to


[062-050]a divorce now?  And if she is granted one, is virtue in danger,


[062-051]and shall we lose the high ideal of home life?  Can anything be


[062-052]more infamous than to endeavor to make a woman, under such


[062-053]circumstances, remain with such a man?  It may be said that she


[062-054]should leave him--that they should live separate and apart.  That


[062-055]is to say, that this woman should be deprived of a home; that she


[062-056]should not be entitled to the love of man; that she should remain,


[062-057]for the rest of her days, worse than a widow.  That is to say, a


[062-058]wife, hiding, keeping out of the way, secreting herself from the


[062-059]hyena to whom she was married.  Nothing, in my judgment, can exceed


[062-060]the heartlessness of a law or of a creed that would compel this


[062-061]woman to remain the wife of this monster.  And it is not only cruel,


[062-062]but it is immoral, low, vulgar.


[062-063]The ground has been taken that woman would lose her dignity if


[062-064]marriages were dissoluble.  Is it necessary to lose your freedom


[062-065]in order to retain your character, in order to be womanly or manly?


[062-066]Must a woman in order to retain her womanhood become a slave, a


[062-067]serf, with a wild beast for a master, or with society for a master,


[062-068]or with a phantom for a master?  Has not the married woman the


[062-069]right of self-defence?  Is it not the duty of society to protect


[062-070]her from her husband?  If she owes no duty to her husband; if it


[062-071]is impossible for her to feel toward him any thrill of affection,


[062-072]what is there of marriage left?  What part of the contract remains


[062-073]in force?  She is not to live with him, because she abhors him.


[062-074]She is not to remain in the same house with him, for fear he may


[062-075]kill her.  What, then, are their relations?  Do they sustain any


[062-076]relation except that of hunter and hunted--that is, of tyrant and


[062-077]victim?  And is it desirable that this relation should be rendered


[062-078]sacred by a church?  Is it desirable to have families raised under


[062-079]such circumstances?  Are we really in need of the children born of


[062-080]such parents?  If the woman is not in fault, does society insist


[062-081]that her life should be wrecked?  Can the virtue of others be


[062-082]preserved only by the destruction of her happiness, and by what


[062-083]might be called her perpetual imprisonment?  I hope the clergy who


[062-084]believe in the sacredness of marriage--in the indissolubility of


[062-085]the marriage tie--will give their opinions on this case.  I believe


[062-086]that marriage is the most important contract that human beings can


[062-087]make.  I always believe that a man will keep his contract; that a


[062-088]woman, in the highest sense, will keep hers,  But suppose the man


[062-089]does not.  Is the woman still bound?


[062-090]Is there no mutuality?  What is a contract?  It is where one party


[062-091]promises to do something in consideration that the other party will


[062-092]do something.  That is to say, there is a consideration on both


[062-093]sides, moving from one to the other.  A contract without consideration


[062-094]is null and void; and a contract duly entered into, where the


[062-095]consideration of one party is withheld, is voidable, and can be


[062-096]voided by the party who has kept, or who is willing to keep, the


[062-097]contract.  A marriage without love is bad enough.  But what can we


[062-098]say of a marriage where the parties hate each other?  Is there any


[062-099]morality in this--any virtue?  Will any decent person say that a


[062-100]woman, true, good and loving, should be compelled to live with a


[062-101]man she detests, compelled to be the mother of his children?  Is


[062-102]there a woman in the world who would not shrink from this herself?


[062-103]And is there a woman so heartless and so immoral that she would


[062-104]force another to bear what she would shudderingly avoid?  Let us


[062-105]bring these questions home.  In other words, let us have some sense,


[062-106]some feeling, some heart--and just a little brain.  Marriages are


[062-107]made by men and women.  They are not made by the State, and they


[062-108]are not made by the gods.  By this time people should learn that


[062-109]human happiness is the foundation of virtue--the foundation of


[062-110]morality.  Nothing is moral that does not tend to the well-being


[062-111]of sentient beings.  Nothing is virtuous the result of which is


[062-112]not a human good.  The world has always been living for phantoms,


[062-113]for ghosts, for monsters begotten by ignorance and fear.  The world


[062-114]should learn to live for itself.  Man should, by this time, be


[062-115]convinced that all the reasons for doing right, and all the reasons


[062-116]for doing wrong, are right here in this world--all within the


[062-117]horizon of this life.  And besides, we should have imagination to


[062-118]put ourselves in the place of another.  Let a man suppose himself


[062-119]a helpless wife, beaten by a brute who believes in the indissolubility


[062-120]of marriage.  Would he want a divorce?


[062-121]I suppose that very few people have any adequate idea of the


[062-122]sufferings of women and children; of the number of wives who tremble


[062-123]when they hear the footsteps of a returning husband; of the number


[062-124]of children who hide when they hear the voice of a father.  Very


[062-125]few people know the number of blows that fall on the flesh of the


[062-126]helpless every day.  Few know the nights of terror passed by mothers


[062-127]holding young children at their breasts.  Compared with this, the


[062-128]hardships of poverty, borne by those who love each other, are


[062-129]nothing.  Men and women, truly married, bear the sufferings of


[062-130]poverty.  They console each other; their affection gives to the


[062-131]heart of each perpetual sunshine.  But think of the others!  I have


[062-132]said a thousand times that the home is the unit of good government.


[062-133]When we have kind fathers and loving mothers, then we shall have


[062-134]civilized nations, and not until then.  Civilization commences at


[062-135]the hearthstone.  When intelligence rocks the cradle--when the


[062-136]house is filled with philosophy and kindness--you will see a world


[062-137]a peace.  Justice will sit in the courts, wisdom in the legislative


[062-138]halls, and over all, like the dome of heaven, will be the spirit


[062-139]of Liberty!


[062-140]_Question_.  What is your idea with regard to divorce?


[062-141]_Answer_.  My idea is this:  As I said before, marriage is the most


[062-142]sacred contract--the most important contract--that human beings can


[062-143]make.  As a rule, the woman dowers the husband with her youth--with


[062-144]all she has.  From this contract the husband should never be released


[062-145]unless the wife has broken a condition; that is to say, has failed


[062-146]to fulfill the contract of marriage.  On the other hand, the woman


[062-147]should be allowed a divorce for the asking.  This should be granted


[062-148]in public, precisely as the marriage should be in public.  Every


[062-149]marriage should be known.  There should be witnesses, to the end


[062-150]that the character of the contract entered into should be understood;


[062-151]and as all marriage records should be kept, so the divorce should


[062-152]be open, public and known.  The property should be divided by a


[062-153]court of equity, under certain regulations of law.  If there are


[062-154]children, they should be provided for through the property and the


[062-155]parents.  People should understand that men and women are not


[062-156]virtuous by law.  They should comprehend the fact that law does


[062-157]not create virtue--that law is not the foundation, the fountain,


[062-158]of love.  They should understand that love is in the human heart,


[062-159]and that real love is virtuous.  People who love each other will


[062-160]be true to each other.  The death of love is the commencement of


[062-161]vice.  Besides this, there is a public opinion that has great


[062-162]weight.  When that public opinion is right, it does a vast amount


[062-163]of good, and when wrong, a great amount of harm.  People marry, or


[062-164]should marry, because it increases the happiness of each and all.


[062-165]But where the marriage turns out to have been a mistake, and where


[062-166]the result is misery, and not happiness, the quicker they are


[062-167]divorced the better, not only for themselves, but for the community


[062-168]at large.  These arguments are generally answered by some donkey


[062-169]braying about free love, and by "free love" he means a condition


[062-170]of society in which there is no love.  The persons who make this


[062-171]cry are, in all probability, incapable of the sentiment, of the


[062-172]feeling, known as love.  They judge others by themselves, and they


[062-173]imagine that without law there would be no restraint.


[062-174]What do they say of natural modesty?  Do they forget that people


[062-175]have a choice?  Do they not understand something of the human heart,


[062-176]and that true love has always been as pure as the morning star?


[062-177]Do they believe that by forcing people to remain together who


[062-178]despise each other they are adding to the purity of the marriage


[062-179]relation?  Do they not know that all marriage is an outward act,


[062-180]testifying to that which has happened in the heart?  Still, I always


[062-181]believe that words are wasted on such people.  It is useless to


[062-182]talk to anybody about music who is unable to distinguish one tune


[062-183]from another.  It is useless to argue with a man who regards his


[062-184]wife as his property, and it is hardly worth while to suggest


[062-185]anything to a gentleman who imagines that society is so constructed


[062-186]that it really requires, for the protection of itself, that the


[062-187]lives of good and noble women should be wrecked,  I am a believer


[062-188]in the virtue of women, in the honesty of man.  The average woman


[062-189]is virtuous; the average man is honest, and the history of the


[062-190]world shows it.  If it were not so, society would be impossible.


[062-191]I don't mean by this that most men are perfect, but what I mean is


[062-192]this:  That there is far more good than evil in the average human


[062-193]being, and that the natural tendency of most people is toward the


[062-194]good and toward the right.  And I most passionately deny that the


[062-195]good of society demands that any good person should suffer.  I do


[062-196]not regard government as a Juggernaut, the wheels of which must,


[062-197]of necessity, roll over and crush the virtuous, the self-denying


[062-198]and the good.  My doctrine is the exact opposite of what is known


[062-199]as free love.  I believe in the marriage of true minds and of true


[062-200]hearts.  But I believe that thousands of people are married who do


[062-201]not love each other.  That is the misfortune of our century.  Other


[062-202]things are taken into consideration--position, wealth, title and


[062-203]the thousand things that have nothing to do with real affection.


[062-204]Where men and women truly love each other, that love, in my judgment,


[062-205]lasts as long as life.  The greatest line that I know of in the


[062-206]poetry of the world is in the 116th sonnet of Shakespeare:  "Love


[062-207]is not love which alters when it alteration finds."
