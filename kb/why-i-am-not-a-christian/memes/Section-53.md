[052-001]_Question_.  Who do you think ought to be nominated at Chicago?


[052-002]_Answer_.  Personally, I am for General Gresham.  I am saying


[052-003]nothing against the other prominent candidates.  They have their


[052-004]friends, and many of them are men of character and capacity, and


[052-005]would make good Presidents.  But I know of no man who has a better


[052-006]record than Gresham, and of no man who, in my judgment, would


[052-007]receive a larger number of votes.  I know of no Republican who


[052-008]would not support Judge Gresham.  I have never heard one say that


[052-009]he had anything against him or know of any reason why he should


[052-010]not be voted for.  He is a man of great natural capacity.  He is


[052-011]candid and unselfish.  He has for many years been engaged in the


[052-012]examination and decision of important questions, of good principles,


[052-013]and consequently he has a trained mind.  He knows how to take hold


[052-014]of a question, to get at a fact, to discover in a multitude of


[052-015]complications the real principle--the heart of the case.  He has


[052-016]always been a man of affairs.  He is not simply a judge--that is


[052-017]to say, a legal pair of scales--he knows the effect of his decision


[052-018]on the welfare of communities--he is not governed entirely by


[052-019]precedents--he has opinions of his own.  In the next place, he is


[052-020]a man of integrity in all the relations of life.  He is not a seeker


[052-021]after place, and, so far as I know, he has done nothing for the


[052-022]purpose of inducing any human being to favor his nomination.  I


[052-023]have never spoken to him on the subject.


[052-024]In the West he has developed great strength, in fact, his popularity


[052-025]has astonished even his best friends.  The great mass of people


[052-026]want a perfectly reliable man--one who will be governed by his best


[052-027]judgment and by a desire to do the fair and honorable thing.  It


[052-028]has been stated that the great corporations might not support him


[052-029]with much warmth for the reason that he has failed to decide certain


[052-030]cases in their favor.  I believe that he has decided the law as he


[052-031]believed it to be, and that he has never been influenced in the


[052-032]slightest degree, by the character, position, or the wealth of the


[052-033]parties before him.  It may be that some of the great financiers,


[052-034]the manipulators, the creators of bonds and stocks, the blowers of


[052-035]financial bubbles, will not support him and will not contribute


[052-036]any money for the payment of election expenses, because they are


[052-037]perfectly satisfied that they could not make any arrangements with


[052-038]him to get the money back, together with interest thereon, but the


[052-039]people of this country are intelligent enough to know what that


[052-040]means, and they will be patriotic enough to see to it that no man


[052-041]needs to bow or bend or cringe to the rich to attain the highest


[052-042]place.


[052-043]The possibility is that Mr. Blaine could have been nominated had


[052-044]he not withdrawn, but having withdrawn, of course the party is


[052-045]released.  Others were induced to become candidates, and under


[052-046]these circumstances Mr. Blaine has hardly the right to change his


[052-047]mind, and certainly other persons ought not to change it for him.


[052-048]_Question_.  Do you think that the friends of Gresham would support


[052-049]Blaine if he should be nominated?


[052-050]_Answer_.  Undoubtedly they would.  If they go into convention they


[052-051]must abide the decision.  It would be dishonorable to do that which


[052-052]you would denounce in others.  Whoever is nominated ought to receive


[052-053]the support of all good Republicans.  No party can exist that will


[052-054]not be bound by its own decision.  When the platform is made, then


[052-055]is the time to approve or reject.  The conscience of the individual


[052-056]cannot be bound by the action of party, church or state.  But when


[052-057]you ask a convention to nominate your candidate, you really agree


[052-058]to stand by the choice of the convention.  Principles are of more


[052-059]importance than candidates.  As a rule, men who refuse to support


[052-060]the nominee, while pretending to believe in the platform, are giving


[052-061]an excuse for going over to the enemy.  It is a pretence to cover


[052-062]desertion.  I hope that whoever may be nominated at Chicago will


[052-063]receive the cordial support of the entire party, of every man who


[052-064]believes in Republican principles, who believes in good wages for


[052-065]good work, and has confidence in the old firms of "Mind and Muscle,"


[052-066]of "Head and Hand."


[052-067]--_New York Press_, May 27, 1888.


[052-068]LABOR, AND TARIFF REFORM.


[052-069]_Question_.  What, in your opinion, is the condition of labor in


[052-070]this country as compared with that abroad?


[052-071]_Answer_.  In the first place, it is self-evident that if labor


[052-072]received more in other lands than in this the tide of emigration


[052-073]would be changed.  The workingmen would leave our shores.  People


[052-074]who believe in free trade are always telling us that the laboring


[052-075]man is paid much better in Germany than in the United States, and


[052-076]yet nearly every ship that comes from Germany is crammed with


[052-077]Germans, who, for some unaccountable reason, prefer to leave a


[052-078]place where they are doing well and come to one where they must do


[052-079]worse.


[052-080]The same thing can be said of Denmark and Sweden, of England,


[052-081]Scotland, Ireland and of Italy.  The truth is, that in all those


[052-082]lands the laboring man can earn just enough to-day to do the work


[052-083]of to-morrow; everything he earns is required to get food enough


[052-084]in his body and rags enough on his back to work from day to day,


[052-085]to toil from week to week.  There are only three luxuries within


[052-086]his reach--air, light, and water; probably a fourth might be added


[052-087]--death.


[052-088]In those countries the few own the land, the few have the capital,


[052-089]the few make the laws, and the laboring man is not a power.  His


[052-090]opinion in neither asked nor heeded.  The employers pay as little


[052-091]as they can.  When the world becomes civilized everybody will want


[052-092]to pay what things are worth, but now capital is perfectly willing


[052-093]that labor shall remain at the starvation line.  Competition on


[052-094]every hand tends to put down wages.  The time will come when the


[052-095]whole community will see that justice is economical.  If you starve


[052-096]laboring men you increase crime; you multiply, as they do in England,


[052-097]workhouses, hospitals and all kinds of asylums, and these public


[052-098]institutions are for the purpose of taking care of the wrecks that


[052-099]have been produced by greed and stinginess and meanness--that is


[052-100]to say, by the ignorance of capital.


[052-101]_Question_.  What effect has the protective tariff on the condition


[052-102]of labor in this country?


[052-103]_Answer_.  To the extent that the tariff keeps out the foreign


[052-104]article it is a direct protection to American labor.  Everything


[052-105]in this country is on a larger scale than in any other.  There is


[052-106]far more generosity among the manufacturers and merchants and


[052-107]millionaires and capitalists of the United States than among those


[052-108]of any other country, although they are bad enough and mean enough


[052-109]here.


[052-110]But the great thing for the laboring man in the United States is


[052-111]that he is regarded as a man.  He is a unit of political power.


[052-112]His vote counts just as much as that of the richest and most


[052-113]powerful.  The laboring man has to be consulted.  The candidate


[052-114]has either to be his friend or to pretend to be his friend, before


[052-115]he can succeed.  A man running for the presidency could not say


[052-116]the slightest word against the laboring man, or calculated to put


[052-117]a stain upon industry, without destroying every possible chance of


[052-118]success.  Generally, every candidate tries to show that he is a


[052-119]laboring man, or that he was a laboring man, or that his father


[052-120]was before him.  There is in this country very little of the spirit


[052-121]of caste--the most infamous spirit that ever infested the heartless


[052-122]breast of the brainless head of a human being.


[052-123]_Question_.  What will be the effect on labor of a departure in


[052-124]American policy in the direction of free trade?


[052-125]_Answer_.  If free trade could be adopted to-morrow there would be


[052-126]an instant shrinkage of values in this country.  Probably the


[052-127]immediate loss would equal twenty billion dollars--that is to say,


[052-128]one-third of the value of the country.  No one can tell its extent.


[052-129]All thing are so interwoven that to destroy one industry cripples


[052-130]another, and the influence keeps on until it touches the circumference


[052-131]of human interests.


[052-132]I believe that labor is a blessing.  It never was and never will


[052-133]be a curse.  It is a blessed thing to labor for your wife and


[052-134]children, for your father and mother, and for the ones you love.


[052-135]It is a blessed thing to have an object in life--something to do--


[052-136]something to call into play your best thoughts, to develop your


[052-137]faculties and to make you a man.  How beautiful, how charming, are


[052-138]the dreams of the young mechanic, the artist, the musician, the


[052-139]actor and the student.  How perfectly stupid must be the life of


[052-140]a young man with nothing to do, no ambition, no enthusiasm--that


[052-141]is to say, nothing of the divine in him; the young man with an


[052-142]object in life, of whose brain a great thought, a great dream has


[052-143]taken possession, and in whose heart there is a great, throbbing


[052-144]hope.  He looks forward to success--to wife, children, home--all


[052-145]the blessings and sacred joys of human life.  He thinks of wealth


[052-146]and fame and honor, and of a long, genial, golden, happy autumn.


[052-147]Work gives the feeling of independence, of self-respect.  A man


[052-148]who does something necessarily puts a value on himself.  He feels


[052-149]that he is a part of the world's force.  The idler--no matter what


[052-150]he says, no matter how scornfully he may look at the laborer--in


[052-151]his very heart knows exactly what he is; he knows that he is a


[052-152]counterfeit, a poor worthless imitation of a man.


[052-153]But there is a vast difference between work and what I call "toil."


[052-154]What must be the life of a man who can earn only one dollar or two


[052-155]dollars a day?  If this man has a wife and a couple of children


[052-156]how can the family live?  What must they eat?  What must they wear?


[052-157]From the cradle to the coffin they are ignorant of any luxury of


[052-158]life.  If the man is sick, if one of the children dies, how can


[052-159]doctors and medicines be paid for?  How can the coffin or the grave


[052-160]be purchased?  These people live on what might be called "the snow


[052-161]line"--just at that point where trees end and the mosses begin.


[052-162]What are such lives worth?  The wages of months would hardly pay


[052-163]for the ordinary dinner of the family of a rich man.  The savings


[052-164]of a whole life would not purchase one fashionable dress, or the


[052-165]lace on it.  Such a man could not save enough during his whole life


[052-166]to pay for the flowers of a fashionable funeral.


[052-167]And yet how often hundreds of thousands of persons, who spend


[052-168]thousands of dollars every year on luxuries, really wonder why the


[052-169]laboring people should complain.  They are astonished when a car


[052-170]driver objects to working fourteen hours a day.  Men give millions


[052-171]of dollars to carry the gospel to the heathen, and leave their own


[052-172]neighbors without bread; and these same people insist on closing


[052-173]libraries and museums of art on Sunday, and yet Sunday is the only


[052-174]day that these institutions can be visited by the poor.


[052-175]They even want to stop the street cars so that these workers, these


[052-176]men and women, cannot go to the parks or the fields on Sunday.


[052-177]They want stages stopped on fashionable avenues so that the rich


[052-178]may not be disturbed in their prayers and devotions.


[052-179]The condition of the workingman, even in America, is bad enough.


[052-180]If free trade will not reduce wages what will?  If manufactured


[052-181]articles become cheaper the skilled laborers of America must work


[052-182]cheaper or stop producing the articles.  Every one knows that most


[052-183]of the value of a manufactured article comes from labor.  Think of


[052-184]the difference between the value of a pound of cotton and a pound


[052-185]of the finest cotton cloth; between a pound of flax and enough


[052-186]point lace to weigh a pound; between a few ounces of paint, two or


[052-187]three yards of canvas and a great picture; between a block of stone


[052-188]and a statue!  Labor is the principal factor in price; when the


[052-189]price falls wages must go down.
