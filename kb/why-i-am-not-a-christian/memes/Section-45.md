[044-001]_Question_.  Do you think the Republican party should take a decided


[044-002]stand on the temperance issue?


[044-003]_Answer_.  I do; and that decided stand should be that temperance


[044-004]is an individual question, something with which the State and Nation


[044-005]have nothing to do.  Temperance is a thing that the law cannot


[044-006]control.  You might as well try to control music, painting, sculpture,


[044-007]or metaphysics, as the question of temperance.  As life becomes


[044-008]more valuable, people will learn to take better care of it.  There


[044-009]is something more to be desired even than temperance, and that is


[044-010]liberty.  I do not believe in putting out the sun because weeds


[044-011]grow.  I should rather have some weeds than go without wheat and


[044-012]corn.  The Republican party should represent liberty and individuality;


[044-013]it should keep abreast of the real spirit of the age; the Republican


[044-014]party ought to be intelligent enough to know that progress has been


[044-015]marked not by the enactment of new laws, but by the repeal of old


[044-016]ones.


[044-017]--_Evening Traveler_, Boston, October, 1886.


[044-018]HENRY GEORGE AND LABOR.


[044-019]_Question_.  It is said, Colonel Ingersoll, that you are for Henry


[044-020]George?


[044-021]_Answer_.  Of course; I think it the duty of the Republicans to


[044-022]defeat the Democracy--a solemn duty--and I believe that they have


[044-023]a chance to elect George; that is to say, an opportunity to take


[044-024]New York from their old enemy.  If the Republicans stand by George


[044-025]he will succeed.  All the Democratic factions are going to unite


[044-026]to beat the workingmen.  What a picture!  Now is the time for the


[044-027]Republicans to show that all their sympathies are not given to


[044-028]bankers, corporations and millionaires.  They were on the side of


[044-029]the slave--they gave liberty to millions.  Let them take another


[044-030]step and extend their hands to the sons of toil.


[044-031]My heart beats with those who bear the burdens of this poor world.


[044-032]_Question_.  Do you not think that capital is entitled to


[044-033]protection?


[044-034]_Answer_.  I am in favor of accomplishing all reforms in a legal


[044-035]and orderly way, and I want the laboring people of this country to


[044-036]appeal to the ballot.  All classes and all interests must be content


[044-037]to abide the result.


[044-038]I want the laboring people to show that they are intelligent enough


[044-039]to stand by each other.  Henry George is their natural leader.


[044-040]Let them be true to themselves by being true to him.  The great


[044-041]questions between capital and labor must be settled peaceably.


[044-042]There is no excuse for violence, and no excuse for contempt and


[044-043]scorn.  No country can be prosperous while the workers want and


[044-044]the idlers waste.  Those who do the most should have the most.


[044-045]There is no civilized country, so far as I know, but I believe


[044-046]there will be, and I want to hasten they day when the map of the


[044-047]world will give the boundaries of that blessed land.


[044-048]_Question_.  Do you agree with George's principles?  Do you believe


[044-049]in socialism?


[044-050]_Answer_.  I do not understand that George is a Socialist.  He is


[044-051]on the side of those that work--so am I.  He wants to help those


[044-052]that need help--so do I.  The rich can take care of themselves.


[044-053]I shed no tears over the miseries of capital.  I think of the men


[044-054]in mines and factories, in huts, hovels and cellars; of the poor


[044-055]sewing women; of the poor, the hungry and the despairing.  The


[044-056]world must be made better through intelligence.  I do not go with


[044-057]the destroyers, with those that hate the successful, that hate the


[044-058]generous, simply because they are rich.  Wealth is the surplus


[044-059]produced by labor, and the wealth of the world should keep the


[044-060]world from want.


[044-061]--_New York Herald_, October 13, 1886.


[044-062]LABOR QUESTION AND SOCIALISM.


[044-063]_Question_.  What do you think of Henry George for mayor?


[044-064]_Answer_.  Several objections have been urged, not to what Mr.


[044-065]George has done, but to what Mr. George has thought, and he is the


[044-066]only candidate up to this time against whom a charge of this


[044-067]character could be made.  Among other things, he seems to have


[044-068]entertained an idea to the effect that a few men should not own


[044-069]the entire earth; that a child coming into the world has a right


[044-070]to standing room, and that before he walks, his mother has a right


[044-071]to standing room while she holds him.  He insists that if it were


[044-072]possible to bottle the air, and sell it as we do mineral water, it


[044-073]would be hardly fair for the capitalists of the world to embark in


[044-074]such a speculation, especially where millions were allowed to die


[044-075]simply because they were not able to buy breath at "pool prices."


[044-076]Mr. George seems to think that the time will come when capital will


[044-077]be intelligent enough and civilized enough to take care of itself.


[044-078]He has a dream that poverty and crime and all the evils that go


[044-079]hand in hand with partial famine, with lack of labor, and all the


[044-080]diseases born of living in huts and cellars, born of poor food and


[044-081]poor clothing and of bad habits, will disappear, and that the world


[044-082]will be really fit to live in.  He goes so far as to insist that


[044-083]men ought to have more than twenty-three or twenty-four dollars a


[044-084]month for digging coal, and that they ought not to be compelled to


[044-085]spend that money in the store or saloon of the proprietor of the


[044-086]mine.  He has also stated on several occasions that a man ought


[044-087]not to drive a street car for sixteen or eighteen hours a day--that


[044-088]even a street-car driver ought to have the privilege now and then


[044-089]of seeing his wife, or at least one of the children, awake.  And


[044-090]he has gone so far as to say that a letter-carrier ought not to


[044-091]work longer in each day for the United States than he would for a


[044-092]civilized individual.


[044-093]To people that imagine that this world is already perfection; that


[044-094]the condition of no one should be bettered except their own, these


[044-095]ideas seem dangerous.  A man who has already amassed a million,


[044-096]and who has no fear for the future, and who says:  "I will employ


[044-097]the cheapest labor and make men work as long as they can possibly


[044-098]endure the toil," will regard Mr. George as an impractical man.


[044-099]It is very probable that all of us will be dead before all the


[044-100]theories of Mr. George are put in practice.  Some of them, however,


[044-101]may at some time benefit mankind; and so far as I am concerned, I


[044-102]am willing to help hasten the day, although it may not come while


[044-103]I live.  I do not know that I agree with many of the theories of


[044-104]Mr. George.  I know that I do not agree with some of them.  But


[044-105]there is one thing in which I do agree with him, and that is, in


[044-106]his effort to benefit the human race, in his effort to do away with


[044-107]some of the evils that now afflict mankind.  I sympathize with him


[044-108]in his endeavor to shorten the hours of labor, to increase the well-


[044-109]being of laboring men, to give them better houses, better food,


[044-110]and in every way to lighten the burdens that now bear upon their


[044-111]bowed backs.  It may be that very little can be done by law, except


[044-112]to see that they are not absolutely abused; to see that the mines


[044-113]in which they work are supplied with air and with means of escape


[044-114]in time of danger; to prevent the deforming of children by forcing


[044-115]upon them the labor of men; to shorten the hours of toil, and to


[044-116]give all laborers certain liens, above all other claims, for their


[044-117]work.  It is easy to see that in this direction something may be


[044-118]done by law.


[044-119]_Question_.  Colonel Ingersoll, are you a Socialist?


[044-120]_Answer_.  I am an Individualist instead of a Socialist.  I am a


[044-121]believer in individuality and in each individual taking care of


[044-122]himself, and I want the Government to do just as little as it can


[044-123]consistently with the safety of the nation, and I want as little


[044-124]law as possible--only as much as will protect life, reputation and


[044-125]property by punishing criminals and by enforcing honest contracts.


[044-126]But if a government gives privileges to a few, the few must not


[044-127]oppress the many.  The Government has no right to bestow any


[044-128]privilege upon any man or upon any corporation, except for the


[044-129]public good.  That which is a special privilege to the few, should


[044-130]be a special benefit to the many.  And whenever the privileged few


[044-131]abuse the privilege so that it becomes a curse to the many, the


[044-132]privilege, whatever it is, should be withdrawn.  I do not pretend


[044-133]to know enough to suggest a remedy for all the evils of society.


[044-134]I doubt if one human mind could take into consideration the almost


[044-135]infinite number of factors entering into such a problem.  And this


[044-136]fact that no one knows, is the excuse for trying.  While I may not


[044-137]believe that a certain theory will work, still, if I feel sure it


[044-138]will do no harm, I am willing to see it tried.


[044-139]_Question_.  Do you think that Mr. George would make a good mayor?


[044-140]_Answer_.  I presume he would.  He is a thoughtful, prudent man.


[044-141]His reputation for honesty has never, so far as I know, been called


[044-142]in question.  It certainly does not take a genius to be mayor of


[044-143]New York.  If so, there have been some years when there was hardly


[044-144]a mayor.  I take it that a clear-headed, honest man, whose only


[044-145]object is to do his duty, and with courage enough to stand by his


[044-146]conscience, would make a good mayor of New York or of any other


[044-147]city.


[044-148]_Question_.  Are you in sympathy with the workingmen and their


[044-149]objects?


[044-150]_Answer_.  I am in sympathy with laboring men of all kinds, whether


[044-151]they labor with hand or brain.  The Knights of Labor, I believe,


[044-152]do not allow a lawyer to become a member.  I am somewhat wider in


[044-153]my sympathies.  No men in the world struggle more heroically; no


[044-154]men in the world have suffered more, or carried a heavier cross,


[044-155]or worn a sharper crown of thorns, than those that have produced


[044-156]what we call the literature of our race.  So my sympathies extend


[044-157]all the way from hod-carriers to sculptors; from well-diggers to


[044-158]astronomers.  If the objects of the laboring men are to improve


[044-159]their condition without injuring others; to have homes and firesides,


[044-160]and wives and children; plenty to eat, good clothes to wear; to


[044-161]develop their minds, to educate their children--in short, to become


[044-162]prosperous and civilized, I sympathize with them, and hope they


[044-163]will succeed.  I have not the slightest sympathy with those that


[044-164]wish to accomplish all these objects through brute force.  A Nihilist


[044-165]may be forgiven in Russia--may even be praised in Russia; a Socialist


[044-166]may be forgiven in Germany; and certainly a Home-ruler can be


[044-167]pardoned in Ireland, but in the United States there is no place


[044-168]for Anarchist, Socialist or Dynamiter.  In this country the political


[044-169]power has been fairly divided.  Poverty has just as many votes as


[044-170]wealth.  No man can be so poor as not to have a ballot; no man is


[044-171]rich enough to have two; and no man can buy another vote, unless


[044-172]somebody is mean enough and contemptible enough to sell; and if he


[044-173]does sell his vote, he never should complain about the laws or


[044-174]their administration.  So the foolish and the wise are on an


[044-175]equality, and the political power of this country is divided so


[044-176]that each man is a sovereign.


[044-177]Now, the laboring people are largely in the majority in this country.


[044-178]If there are any laws oppressing them, they should have them


[044-179]repealed.  I want the laboring people--and by the word "laboring"


[044-180]now, I include only the men that they include by that word--to


[044-181]unite; I want them to show that they have the intelligence to act


[044-182]together, and sense enough to vote for a friend.  I want them to


[044-183]convince both the other great parties that they cannot be purchased.


[044-184]This will be an immense step in the right direction.
