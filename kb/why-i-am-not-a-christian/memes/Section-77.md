[076-001]For many years the Christian world has been engaged in examining


[076-002]the religions of other peoples, and the Christian scholars have


[076-003]had but little trouble in demonstrating the origin of Mohammedanism


[076-004]and Buddhism and all other isms except ours.  After having examined


[076-005]other religions in the light of science, it occurred to some of


[076-006]our theologians to examine their own doctrine in the same way, and


[076-007]the result has been exactly the same in both cases.  Dr. Briggs,


[076-008]as I believe, is a man of education.  He is undoubtedly familiar


[076-009]with other religions, and has, to some extent at least, made himself


[076-010]familiar with the sacred books of other people.  Dr. Briggs knows


[076-011]that no human being knows who wrote a line of the Old Testament.


[076-012]He knows as well as he can know anything, for instance, that Moses


[076-013]never wrote one word of the books attributed to him.  He knows that


[076-014]the book of Genesis was made by putting two or three stories


[076-015]together.  He also knows that it is not the oldest story, but was


[076-016]borrowed.  He knows that in this book of Genesis there is not one


[076-017]word adapted to make a human being better, or to shed the slightest


[076-018]light on human conduct.  He knows, if he knows anything, that the


[076-019]Mosaic Code, so-called, was, and is, exceedingly barbarous and not


[076-020]adapted to do justice between man and man, or between nation and


[076-021]nation.  He knows that the Jewish people pursued a course adapted


[076-022]to destroy themselves; that they refused to make friends with their


[076-023]neighbors; that they had not the slightest idea of the rights of


[076-024]other people; that they really supposed that the earth was theirs,


[076-025]and that their God was the greatest God in the heavens.  He also


[076-026]knows that there are many thousands of mistakes in the Old Testament


[076-027]as translated.  He knows that the book of Isaiah is made up of


[076-028]several books.  He knows the same thing in regard to the New


[076-029]Testament.  He also knows that there were many other books that


[076-030]were once considered sacred that have been thrown away, and that


[076-031]nobody knows who wrote a solitary line of the New Testament.


[076-032]Besides all this, Dr. Briggs knows that the Old and New Testaments


[076-033]are filled with interpolations, and he knows that the passages of


[076-034]Scripture which have been taken as the foundation stones for creeds,


[076-035]were written hundreds of years after the death of Christ.  He knows


[076-036]well enough that Christ never said:  "I came not to bring peace,


[076-037]but a sword."  He knows that the same being never said:  "Thou art


[076-038]Peter, and on this rock will I build my church."  He knows, too,


[076-039]that Christ never said:  "Whosoever believes shall be saved, and


[076-040]whosoever believes not shall be damned."  He knows that these were


[076-041]interpolations.  He knows that the sin against the Holy Ghost is


[076-042]another interpolation.  He knows, if he knows anything, that the


[076-043]gospel according to John was written long after the rest, and that


[076-044]nearly all of the poison and superstition of orthodoxy is in that


[076-045]book.  He knows also, if he knows anything, that St. Paul never


[076-046]read one of the four gospels.


[076-047]Knowing all these things, Dr. Briggs has had the honesty to say


[076-048]that there was some trouble about taking the Bible as absolutely


[076-049]inspired in word and punctuation.  I do not think, however, that


[076-050]he can maintain his own position and still remain a Presbyterian


[076-051]or anything like a Presbyterian.  He takes the ground, I believe,


[076-052]that there are three sources of knowledge:  First, the Bible;


[076-053]second, the church; third, reason.  It seems to me that reason


[076-054]should come first, because if you say the Bible is a source of


[076-055]authority, why do you say it?  Do you say this because your reason


[076-056]is convinced that it is?  If so, then reason is the foundation of


[076-057]that belief.  If, again, you say the church is a source of authority,


[076-058]why do you say so?  It must be because its history convinces your


[076-059]reason that it is.  Consequently, the foundation of that idea is


[076-060]reason.  At the bottom of this pyramid must be reason, and no man


[076-061]is under any obligation to believe that which is unreasonable to


[076-062]him.  He may believe things that he cannot prove, but he does not


[076-063]believe them because they are unreasonable.  He believes them


[076-064]because he thinks they are not unreasonable, not impossible, not


[076-065]improbable.  But, after all, reason is the crucible in which every


[076-066]fact must be placed, and the result fixes the belief of the


[076-067]intelligent man.


[076-068]It seems to me that the whole Presbyterian creed must come down


[076-069]together.  It is a scheme based upon certain facts, so-called.


[076-070]There is in it the fall of man.  There is in it the scheme of the


[076-071]atonement, and there is the idea of hell, eternal punishment, and


[076-072]the idea of heaven, eternal reward; and yet, according to their


[076-073]creed, hell is not a punishment and heaven is not a reward.  Now,


[076-074]if we do away with the fall of man we do away with the atonement;


[076-075]then we do away with all supernatural religion.  Then we come back


[076-076]to human reason.  Personally, I hope that the Presbyterian Church


[076-077]will be advanced enough and splendid enough to be honest, and if


[076-078]it is honest, all the gentlemen who amount to anything, who assist


[076-079]in the trial of Dr. Briggs, will in all probability agree with him,


[076-080]and he will be acquitted.  But if they throw aside their reason,


[076-081]and remain blindly orthodox, then he will be convicted.  To me it


[076-082]is simply miraculous that any man should imagine that the Bible is


[076-083]the source of truth.  There was a time when all scientific facts


[076-084]were measured by the Bible.  That time is past, and now the believers


[076-085]in the Bible are doing their best to convince us that it is in


[076-086]harmony with science.  In other words, I have lived to see a change


[076-087]of standards.  When I was a boy, science was measured by the Bible.


[076-088]Now the Bible is measured by science.  This is an immense step.


[076-089]So it is impossible for me to conceive what kind of a mind a man


[076-090]has, who finds in the history of the church the fact that it has


[076-091]been a source of truth.  How can any one come to the conclusion


[076-092]that the Catholic Church has been a source of truth, a source of


[076-093]intellectual light?  How can anyone believe that the church of John


[076-094]Calvin has been a source of truth?  If its creed is not true, if


[076-095]its doctrines are mistakes, if its dogmas are monstrous delusions,


[076-096]how can it be said to have been a source of truth?


[076-097]My opinion is that Dr. Briggs will not be satisfied with the step


[076-098]he has taken.  He has turned his face a little toward the light.


[076-099]The farther he walks the harder it will be for him to turn back.


[076-100]The probability is that the orthodox will turn him out, and the


[076-101]process of driving out men of thought and men of genius will go on


[076-102]until the remnant will be as orthodox as they are stupid.


[076-103]_Question_.  Do you think mankind is drifting away from the


[076-104]supernatural?


[076-105]_Answer_.  My belief is that the supernatural has had its day.


[076-106]The church must either change or abdicate.  That is to say, it must


[076-107]keep step with the progress of the world or be trampled under foot.


[076-108]The church as a power has ceased to exist.  To-day it is a matter


[076-109]of infinite indifference what the pulpit thinks unless there comes


[076-110]the voice of heresy from the sacred place.  Every orthodox minister


[076-111]in the United States is listened to just in proportion that he


[076-112]preaches heresy.  The real, simon-pure, orthodox clergyman delivers


[076-113]his homilies to empty benches, and to a few ancient people who know


[076-114]nothing of the tides and currents of modern thought.  The orthodox


[076-115]pulpit to-day has no thought, and the pews are substantially in


[076-116]the same condition.  There was a time when the curse of the church


[076-117]whitened the face of a race, but now its anathema is the food of


[076-118]laughter.


[076-119]_Question_.  What, in your judgment, is to be the outcome of the


[076-120]present agitation in religious circles?


[076-121]_Answer_.  My idea is that people more and more are declining the


[076-122]postponement of happiness to another world.  The general tendency


[076-123]is to enjoy the present.  All religions have taught men that the


[076-124]pleasures of this world are of no account; that they are nothing


[076-125]but husks and rags and chaff and disappointment; that whoever


[076-126]expects to be happy in this world makes a mistake; that there is


[076-127]nothing on the earth worth striving for; that the principal business


[076-128]of mankind should be to get ready to be happy in another world;


[076-129]that the great occupation is to save your soul, and when you get


[076-130]it saved, when you are satisfied that you are one of the elect,


[076-131]then pack up all your worldly things in a very small trunk, take


[076-132]it to the dock of time that runs out into the ocean of eternity,


[076-133]sit down on it, and wait for the ship of death.  And of course each


[076-134]church is the only one that sells a through ticket which can be


[076-135]depended on.  In all religions, as far as I know, is an admixture


[076-136]of asceticism, and the greater the quantity, the more beautiful


[076-137]the religion has been considered,  The tendency of the world to-


[076-138]day is to enjoy life while you have it; it is to get something out


[076-139]of the present moment; and we have found that there are things


[076-140]worth living for even in this world.  We have found that a man can


[076-141]enjoy himself with wife and children; that he can be happy in the


[076-142]acquisition of knowledge; that he can be very happy in assisting


[076-143]others; in helping those he loves; that there is some joy in poetry,


[076-144]in science and in the enlargement and development of the mind; that


[076-145]there is some delight in music and in the drama and in the arts.


[076-146]We are finding, poor as the world is, that it beats a promise the


[076-147]fulfillment of which is not to take place until after death.  The


[076-148]world is also finding out another thing, and that is that the


[076-149]gentlemen who preach these various religions, and promise these


[076-150]rewards, and threaten the punishments, know nothing whatever of


[076-151]the subject; that they are as blindly ignorant as the people they


[076-152]pretend to teach, and the people are as blindly ignorant as the


[076-153]animals below them.  We have finally concluded that no human being


[076-154]has the slightest conception of origin or of destiny, and that this


[076-155]life, not only in its commencement but in its end, is just as


[076-156]mysterious to-day as it was to the first man whose eyes greeted


[076-157]the rising sun.  We are no nearer the solution of the problem than


[076-158]those who lived thousands of years before us, and we are just as


[076-159]near it as those who will live millions of years after we are dead.


[076-160]So many people having arrived at the conclusion that nobody knows


[076-161]and that nobody can know, like sensible folks they have made up


[076-162]their minds to enjoy life.  I have often said, and I say again,


[076-163]that I feel as if I were on a ship not knowing the port from which


[076-164]it sailed, not knowing the harbor to which it was going, not having


[076-165]a speaking acquaintance with any of the officers, and I have made


[076-166]up my mind to have as good a time with the other passengers as


[076-167]possible under the circumstances.  If this ship goes down in mid-


[076-168]sea I have at least made something, and if it reaches a harbor of


[076-169]perpetual delight I have lost nothing, and I have had a happy


[076-170]voyage.  And I think millions and millions are agreeing with me.
