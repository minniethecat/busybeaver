[078-001]--_The Inter-Ocean_, Chicago, Illinois, October 27, 1891.


[078-002]MISSIONARIES.


[078-003]_Question_.  What is your opinion of foreign missions?


[078-004]_Answer_.  In the first place, there seems to be a pretty good


[078-005]opening in this country for missionary work.  We have a good many


[078-006]Indians who are not Methodists.  I have never known one to be


[078-007]converted.  A good many have been killed by Christians, but their


[078-008]souls have not been saved.  Maybe the Methodists had better turn


[078-009]their attention to the heathen of our own country.  Then we have


[078-010]a good many Mormons who rely on the truth of the Old Testament and


[078-011]follow the example of Abraham, Isaac and Jacob.  It seems to me


[078-012]that the Methodists better convert the Mormons before attacking


[078-013]the tribes of Central Africa.  There is plenty of work to be done


[078-014]right here.  A few good bishops might be employed for a time in


[078-015]converting Dr. Briggs and Professor Swing, to say nothing of other


[078-016]heretical Presbyterians.


[078-017]There is no need of going to China to convert the Chinese.  There


[078-018]are thousands of them here.  In China our missionaries will tell


[078-019]the followers of Confucius about the love and forgiveness of


[078-020]Christians, and when the Chinese come here they are robbed, assaulted,


[078-021]and often murdered.  Would it not be a good thing for the Methodists


[078-022]to civilize our own Christians to such a degree that they would


[078-023]not murder a man simply because he belongs to another race and


[078-024]worships other gods?


[078-025]So, too, I think it would be a good thing for the Methodists to go


[078-026]South and persuade their brethren in that country to treat the


[078-027]colored people with kindness.  A few efforts might be made to


[078-028]convert the "White-caps" in Ohio, Indiana and some other States.


[078-029]My advice to the Methodists is to do what little good they can


[078-030]right here and now.  It seems cruel to preach to the heathen a


[078-031]gospel that is dying out even here, and fill their poor minds with


[078-032]the absurd dogmas and cruel creeds that intelligent men have outgrown


[078-033]and thrown away.


[078-034]Honest commerce will do a thousand times more good than all the


[078-035]missionaries on earth.  I do not believe that an intelligent Chinaman


[078-036]or an intelligent Hindoo has ever been or ever will be converted


[078-037]into a Methodist.  If Methodism is good we need it here, and if it


[078-038]is not good, do not fool the heathen with it.


[078-039]--_The Press_, Cleveland, Ohio, November 12, 1891.


[078-040]MY BELIEF AND UNBELIEF.*


[078-041][* Col. Robert G. Ingersoll was in Toledo for a few hours


[078-042]yesterday afternoon on railroad business.  Whatever Mr.


[078-043]Ingersoll says is always read with interest, for besides the


[078-044]independence of his averments, his ideas are worded in a way


[078-045]that in itself is attractive.


[078-046]While in the court room talking with some of the officials


[078-047]and others, he was saying that in this world there is rather


[078-048]an unequal distribution of comforts, rewards, and


[078-049]punishments.  For himself, he had fared pretty well.  He


[078-050]stated that during the thirty years he has been married


[078-051]there have been fifteen to twenty of his relatives under the


[078-052]same roof, but never had there been in his family a death or


[078-053]a night's loss of sleep on account of sickness.


[078-054]"The Lord has been pretty good to you," suggested Marshall


[078-055]Wade.


[078-056]"Well, I've been pretty good to him," he answered.]


[078-057]_Question_.  I have heard people in discussing yourself and your


[078-058]views, express the belief that way down in the depths of your mind


[078-059]you are not altogether a "disbeliever."  Are they in any sense


[078-060]correct?


[078-061]_Answer_.  I am an unbeliever, and I am a believer.  I do not


[078-062]believe in the miraculous, the supernatural, or the impossible.


[078-063]I do not believe in the "Mosaic" account of the creation, or in


[078-064]the flood, or the Tower of Babel, or that General Joshua turned


[078-065]back the sun or stopped the earth.  I do not believe in the Jonah


[078-066]story, or that God and the Devil troubled poor Job.  Neither do I


[078-067]believe in the Mt. Sinai business, and I have my doubts about the


[078-068]broiled quails furnished in the wilderness.  Neither do I believe


[078-069]that man is wholly depraved.  I have not the least faith in the


[078-070]Eden, snake and apple story.  Neither do I believe that God is an


[078-071]eternal jailer; that he is going to be the warden of an everlasting


[078-072]penitentiary in which the most of men are to be eternally tormented.


[078-073]I do not believe that any man can be justly punished or rewarded


[078-074]on account of his belief.


[078-075]But I do believe in the nobility of human nature.  I believe in


[078-076]love and home, and kindness and humanity.  I believe in good


[078-077]fellowship and cheerfulness, in making wife and children happy.


[078-078]I believe in good nature, in giving to others all the rights that


[078-079]you claim for yourself.  I believe in free thought, in reason,


[078-080]observation and experience.  I believe in self-reliance and in


[078-081]expressing your honest thought.  I have hope for the whole human


[078-082]race.  What will happen to one, will, I hope, happen to all, and


[078-083]that, I hope, will be good.  Above all, I believe in Liberty.


[078-084]--_The Blade_, Toledo, Ohio, January 9, 1892.


[078-085]MUST RELIGION GO?


[078-086]_Question_.  What is your idea as to the difference between honest


[078-087]belief, as held by honest religious thinkers, and heterodoxy?


[078-088]_Answer_.  Of course, I believe that there are thousands of men


[078-089]and women who honestly believe not only in the improbable, not only


[078-090]in the absurd, but in the impossible.  Heterodoxy, so-called,


[078-091]occupies the half-way station between superstition and reason.  A


[078-092]heretic is one who is still dominated by religion, but in the east


[078-093]of whose mind there is a dawn.  He is one who has seen the morning


[078-094]star; he has not entire confidence in the day, and imagines in some


[078-095]way that even the light he sees was born of the night.  In the mind


[078-096]of the heretic, darkness and light are mingled, the ties of


[078-097]intellectual kindred bind him to the night, and yet he has enough


[078-098]of the spirit of adventure to look toward the east.  Of course, I


[078-099]admit that Christians and heretics are both honest; a real Christian


[078-100]must be honest and a real heretic must be the same.  All men must


[078-101]be honest in what they think; but all men are not honest in what


[078-102]they say.  In the invisible world of the mind every man is honest.


[078-103]The judgment never was bribed.  Speech may be false, but conviction


[078-104]is always honest.  So that the difference between honest belief,


[078-105]as shared by honest religious thinkers and heretics, is a difference


[078-106]of intelligence.  It is the difference between a ship lashed to


[078-107]the dock, and on making a voyage; it is the difference between


[078-108]twilight and dawn--that is to say, the coming of the sight and the


[078-109]coming of the morning.


[078-110]_Question_.  Are women becoming freed from the bonds of sectarianism?


[078-111]_Answer_.  Women are less calculating than men.  As a rule they do


[078-112]not occupy the territory of compromise.  They are natural extremists.


[078-113]The woman who is not dominated by superstition is apt to be absolutely


[078-114]free, and when a woman has broken the shackles of superstition,


[078-115]she has no apprehension, no fears.  She feels that she is on the


[078-116]open sea, and she cares neither for wind nor wave.  An emancipated


[078-117]woman never can be re-enslaved.  Her heart goes with her opinions,


[078-118]and goes first.


[078-119]_Question_.  Do you consider that the influence of religion is


[078-120]better than the influence of Liberalism upon society, that is to


[078-121]say, is society less or more moral, is vice more or less


[078-122]conspicuous?


[078-123]_Answer_.  Whenever a chain is broken an obligation takes its place.


[078-124]There is and there can be no responsibility without liberty.  The


[078-125]freer a man is, the more responsible, the more accountable he feels;


[078-126]consequently the more liberty there is, the more morality there


[078-127]is.  Believers in religion teach us that God will reward men for


[078-128]good actions, but men who are intellectually free, know that the


[078-129]reward of a good action cannot be given by any power, but that it


[078-130]is the natural result of the good action.  The free man, guided by


[078-131]intelligence, knows that his reward is in the nature of things,


[078-132]and not in the caprice even of the Infinite.  He is not a good and


[078-133]faithful servant, he is an intelligent free man.


[078-134]The vicious are ignorant; real morality is the child of intelligence;


[078-135]the free and intelligent man knows that every action must be judged


[078-136]by its consequences; he knows that if he does good he reaps a good


[078-137]harvest; he knows that if he does evil he bears a burden, and he


[078-138]knows that these good and evil consequences are not determined by


[078-139]an infinite master, but that they live in and are produced by the


[078-140]actions themselves.


[078-141]--_Evening Advertiser_, New York, February 6, 1892.


[078-142]WORD PAINTING AND COLLEGE EDUCATION.


[078-143]_Question_.  What is the history of the speech delivered here in


[078-144]1876?  Was it extemporaneous?


[078-145]_Answer_.  It was not born entirely of the occasion.  It took me


[078-146]several years to put the thoughts in form--to paint the pictures


[078-147]with words.  No man can do his best on the instant.  Iron to be


[078-148]beaten into perfect form has to be heated several times and turned


[078-149]upon the anvil many more, and hammered long and often.


[078-150]You might as well try to paint a picture with one sweep of the


[078-151]brush, or chisel a statue with one stroke, as to paint many pictures


[078-152]with words, without great thought and care.  Now and then, while


[078-153]a man is talking, heated with his subject, a great thought, sudden


[078-154]as a flash of lightning, illumines the intellectual sky, and a


[078-155]great sentence clothed in words of purple, falls, or rather rushes,


[078-156]from his lips--but a continuous flight is born, not only of


[078-157]enthusiasm, but of long and careful thought.  A perfect picture


[078-158]requires more details, more lights and shadows, than the mind can


[078-159]grasp at once, or on the instant.  Thoughts are not born of chance.


[078-160]They grow and bud and blossom, and bear the fruit of perfect form.


[078-161]Genius is the soil and climate, but the soil must be cultivated,


[078-162]and the harvest is not instantly after the planting.  It takes time


[078-163]and labor to raise and harvest a crop from that field called the


[078-164]brain.


[078-165]_Question_.  Do you think young men need a college education to


[078-166]get along?


[078-167]_Answer_.  Probably many useless things are taught in colleges.


[078-168]I think, as a rule, too much time is wasted learning the names of


[078-169]the cards without learning to play a game.  I think a young man


[078-170]should be taught something that he can use--something he can sell.


[078-171]After coming from college he should be better equipped to battle


[078-172]with the world--to do something of use.  A man may have his brain


[078-173]stuffed with Greek and Latin without being able to fill his stomach


[078-174]with anything of importance.  Still, I am in favor of the highest


[078-175]education.  I would like to see splendid schools in every State,


[078-176]and then a university, and all scholars passing a certain examination


[078-177]sent to the State university free, and then a United States


[078-178]university, the best in the world, and all graduates of the State


[078-179]universities passing a certain examination sent to the United States


[078-180]university free.  We ought to have in this country the best library,


[078-181]the best university, the best school of design in the world; and


[078-182]so I say, more money for the mind.


[078-183]_Question_.  Was the peculiar conduct of the Rev. Dr. Parkhurst,


[078-184]of New York, justifiable, and do you think that it had a tendency


[078-185]to help morality?


[078-186]_Answer_.  If Christ had written a decoy letter to the woman to


[078-187]whom he said:  "Go and sin no more," and if he had disguised himself


[078-188]and visited her house and had then lodged a complaint against her


[078-189]before the police and testified against her, taking one of his


[078-190]disciples with him, I do not think he would have added to his


[078-191]reputation.
