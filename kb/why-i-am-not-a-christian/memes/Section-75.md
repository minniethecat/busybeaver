[074-001]I am not at all surprised that the General Assembly took up this


[074-002]progressive euchre matter.  The word "progressive" is always


[074-003]obnoxious to the ministers.  Euchre under another name might go.


[074-004]Of course, progressive euchre is a kind of gambling.  I knew a


[074-005]young man, or rather heard of him, who won at progressive euchre


[074-006]a silver spoon.  At first this looks like nothing, almost innocent,


[074-007]and yet that spoon, gotten for nothing, sowed the seed of gambling


[074-008]in that young man's brain.  He became infatuated with euchre, then


[074-009]with cards in general, then with draw-poker in particular,--then


[074-010]into Wall Street.  He is now a total wreck, and has the impudence


[074-011]to say that is was all "pre-ordained."  Think of the thousands and


[074-012]millions that are being demoralized by games of chance, by marbles


[074-013]--when they play for keeps--by billiards and croquet, by fox and


[074-014]geese, authors, halma, tiddledywinks and pigs in clover.  In all


[074-015]these miserable games, is the infamous element of chance--the raw


[074-016]material of gambling.  Probably none of these games could be played


[074-017]exclusively for the glory of God.  I agree with the Presbyterian


[074-018]General Assembly, if the creed is true, why should anyone try to


[074-019]amuse himself?  If there is a hell, and all of us are going there,


[074-020]there should never be another smile on the human face.  We should


[074-021]spend our days in sighs, our nights in tears.  The world should go


[074-022]insane.  We find strange combinations--good men with bad creeds,


[074-023]and bad men with good ones--and so the great world stumbles along.


[074-024]--_The Blade_, Toledo, Ohio, June 4, 1891.


[074-025]CREEDS.


[074-026]There is a natural desire on the part of every intelligent human


[074-027]being to harmonize his information--to make his theories agree--in


[074-028]other words, to make what he knows, or thinks he knows, in one


[074-029]department, agree and harmonize with what he knows, or thinks he


[074-030]knows, in every other department of human knowledge.


[074-031]The human race has not advanced in line, neither has it advanced


[074-032]in all departments with the same rapidity.  It is with the race as


[074-033]it is with an individual.  A man may turn his entire attention to


[074-034]some one subject--as, for instance, to geology--and neglect other


[074-035]sciences.  He may be a good geologist, but an exceedingly poor


[074-036]astronomer; or he may know nothing of politics or of political


[074-037]economy.  So he may be a successful statesman and know nothing of


[074-038]theology.  But if a man, successful in one direction, takes up some


[074-039]other question, he is bound to use the knowledge he has on one


[074-040]subject as a kind of standard to measure what he is told on some


[074-041]other subject.  If he is a chemist, it will be natural for him,


[074-042]when studying some other question, to use what he knows in chemistry;


[074-043]that is to say, he will expect to find cause and effect everywhere


[074-044]--succession and resemblance.  He will say:  It must be in all


[074-045]other sciences as in chemistry--there must be no chance.  The


[074-046]elements have no caprice.  Iron is always the same.  Gold does not


[074-047]change.  Prussic acid is always poison--it has no freaks.  So he


[074-048]will reason as to all facts in nature.  He will be a believer in


[074-049]the atomic integrity of all matter, in the persistence of gravitation.


[074-050]Being so trained, and so convinced, his tendency will be to weigh


[074-051]what is called new information in the same scales that he has been


[074-052]using.


[074-053]Now, for the application of this.  Progress in religion is the


[074-054]slowest, because man is kept back by sentimentality, by the efforts


[074-055]of parents, by old associations.  A thousand unseen tendrils are


[074-056]twining about him that he must necessarily break if he advances.


[074-057]In other departments of knowledge inducements are held out and


[074-058]rewards are promised to the one who does succeed--to the one who


[074-059]really does advance--to the one who discovers new facts.  But in


[074-060]religion, instead of rewards being promised, threats are made.


[074-061]The man is told that he must not advance; that if he takes a step


[074-062]forward, it is at the peril of his soul; that if he thinks and


[074-063]investigates, he is in danger of exciting the wrath of God.


[074-064]Consequently religion has been of the slowest growth.  Now, in most


[074-065]departments of knowledge, man has advanced; and coming back to the


[074-066]original statement--a desire to harmonize all that we know--there


[074-067]is a growing desire on the part of intelligent men to have a religion


[074-068]fit to keep company with the other sciences.


[074-069]Our creeds were made in times of ignorance.  They suited very well


[074-070]a flat world, and a God who lived in the sky just above us and who


[074-071]used the lightning to destroy his enemies.  This God was regarded


[074-072]much as a savage regarded the head of his tribe--as one having the


[074-073]right to reward and punish.  And this God, being much greater than


[074-074]a chief of the tribe, could give greater rewards and inflict greater


[074-075]punishments.  They knew that the ordinary chief, or the ordinary


[074-076]king, punished the slightest offence with death.  They also knew


[074-077]that these chiefs and kings tortured their victims as long as the


[074-078]victims could bear the torture.  So when they described their God,


[074-079]they gave this God power to keep the tortured victim alive forever


[074-080]--because they knew that the earthly chief, or the earthly king,


[074-081]would prolong the life of the tortured for the sake of increasing


[074-082]the agonies of the victim.  In those savage days they regarded


[074-083]punishment as the only means of protecting society.  In consequence


[074-084]of this they built heaven and hell on an earthly plan, and they


[074-085]put God--that is to say the chief, that is to say the king--on a


[074-086]throne like an earthly king.


[074-087]Of course, these views were all ignorant and barbaric; but in that


[074-088]blessed day their geology and astronomy were on a par with their


[074-089]theology.  There was a harmony in all departments of knowledge, or


[074-090]rather of ignorance.  Since that time there has been a great advance


[074-091]made in the idea of government--the old idea being that the right


[074-092]to govern came from God to the king, and from the king to his


[074-093]people.  Now intelligent people believe that the source of authority


[074-094]has been changed, and that all just powers of government are derived


[074-095]from the consent of the governed.  So there has been a great advance


[074-096]in the philosophy of punishment--in the treatment of criminals.


[074-097]So, too, in all the sciences.  The earth is no longer flat; heaven


[074-098]is not immediately above us; the universe has been infinitely


[074-099]enlarged, and we have at last found that our earth is but a grain


[074-100]of sand, a speck on the great shore of the infinite.  Consequently


[074-101]there is a discrepancy, a discord, a contradiction between our


[074-102]theology and the other sciences.  Men of intelligence feel this.


[074-103]Dr. Briggs concluded that a perfectly good and intelligent God


[074-104]could not have created billions of sentient beings, knowing that


[074-105]they were to be eternally miserable.  No man could do such a thing,


[074-106]had he the power, without being infinitely malicious.  Dr. Briggs


[074-107]began to have a little hope for the human race--began to think that


[074-108]maybe God is better than the creed describes him.


[074-109]And right here it may be well enough to remark that no one has ever


[074-110]been declared a heretic for thinking God bad.  Heresy has consisted


[074-111]in thinking God better than the church said he was.  The man who


[074-112]said God will damn nearly everybody, was orthodox.  The man who


[074-113]said God will save everybody, was denounced as a blaspheming wretch,


[074-114]as one who assailed and maligned the character of God.  I can


[074-115]remember when the Universalists were denounced as vehemently and


[074-116]maliciously as the Atheists are to-day.


[074-117]Now, Dr. Briggs is undoubtedly an intelligent man.  He knows that


[074-118]nobody on earth knows who wrote the five books of Moses.  He knows


[074-119]that they were not written until hundreds of years after Moses was


[074-120]dead.  He knows that two or more persons were the authors of Isaiah.


[074-121]He knows that David did not write to exceed three or four of the


[074-122]Psalms.  He knows that the Book of Job is not a Jewish book.  He


[074-123]knows that the Songs of Solomon were not written by Solomon.  He


[074-124]knows that the Book of Ecclesiastes was written by a Freethinker.


[074-125]He also knows that there is not in existence to-day--so far as


[074-126]anybody knows--any of the manuscripts of the Old or New Testaments.


[074-127]So about the New Testament, Dr. Briggs knows that nobody lives who


[074-128]has ever seen an original manuscript, or who ever saw anybody that


[074-129]did see one, or that claims to have seen one.  He knows that nobody


[074-130]knows who wrote Matthew or Mark or Luke or John.  He knows that


[074-131]John did not write John, and that that gospel was not written until


[074-132]long after John was dead.  He knows that no one knows who wrote


[074-133]the Hebrews.  He also knows that the Book of Revelation is an insane


[074-134]production.  Dr. Briggs also knows the way in which these books came


[074-135]to be canonical, and he knows that the way was no more binding than


[074-136]a resolution passed by a political convention.  He also knows that


[074-137]many books were left out that had for centuries equal authority


[074-138]with those that were put in.  He also knows that many passages--


[074-139]and the very passages upon which many churches are founded--are


[074-140]interpolations.  He knows that the last chapter of Mark, beginning


[074-141]with the sixteenth verse to the end, is an interpolation; and he


[074-142]also knows that neither Matthew nor Mark nor Luke ever said one


[074-143]word about the necessity of believing on the Lord Jesus Christ, or


[074-144]of believing anything--not one word about believing the Bible or


[074-145]joining the church, or doing any particular thing in the way of


[074-146]ceremony to insure salvation.  He knows that according to Matthew,


[074-147]God agreed to forgive us when we would forgive others.  Consequently


[074-148]he knows that there is not one particle of what is called modern


[074-149]theology in Matthew, Mark, or Luke.  He knows that the trouble


[074-150]commenced in John, and that John was not written until probably


[074-151]one hundred and fifty years--possibly two hundred years--after


[074-152]Christ was dead.  So he also knows that the sin against the Holy


[074-153]Ghost is an interpolation; that "I came not to bring peace but a


[074-154]sword," if not an interpolation, is an absolute contradiction.


[074-155]So, too, he knows that the promise to forgive in heaven what the


[074-156]disciples should forgive on earth, is an interpolation; and that


[074-157]if its not an interpolation, it is without the slightest sense in


[074-158]fact.


[074-159]Knowing these things, and knowing, in addition to what I have


[074-160]stated, that there are thirty thousand or forty thousand mistakes


[074-161]in the Old Testament, that there are a great many contradictions


[074-162]and absurdities, than many of the laws are cruel and infamous, and


[074-163]could have been made only by a barbarous people, Dr. Briggs has


[074-164]concluded that, after all, the torch that sheds the serenest and


[074-165]divinest light is the human reason, and that we must investigate


[074-166]the Bible as we do other books.  At least, I suppose he has reached


[074-167]some such conclusion.  He may imagine that the pure gold of


[074-168]inspiration still runs through the quartz and porphyry of ignorance


[074-169]and mistake, and that all we have to do is to extract the shining


[074-170]metal by some process that may be called theological smelting; and


[074-171]if so I have no fault to find.  Dr. Briggs has taken a step in


[074-172]advance--that is to say, the tree is growing, and when the tree


[074-173]grows, the bark splits; when the new leaves come the old leaves


[074-174]are rotting on the ground.


[074-175]The Presbyterian creed is a very bad creed.  It has been the


[074-176]stumbling-block, not only of the head, but of the heart for many


[074-177]generations.  I do not know that it is, in fact, worse than any


[074-178]other orthodox creed; but the bad features are stated with an


[074-179]explicitness and emphasized with a candor that render the creed


[074-180]absolutely appalling.  It is amazing to me that any man ever wrote


[074-181]it, or that any set of men ever produced it.  It is more amazing


[074-182]to me that any human being ever believed in it.  It is still more


[074-183]amazing that any human being ever thought it wicked not to believe


[074-184]it.  It is more amazing still, than all the others combined, that


[074-185]any human being ever wanted it to be true.
