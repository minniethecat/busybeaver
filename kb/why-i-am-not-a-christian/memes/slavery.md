The institution of [[slavery]] — and the [[bible|Bible]]'s explicit sanction of it — was one of [[robert-ingersoll|Robert G. Ingersoll]]'s most powerful arguments against the claim that scripture provided a reliable moral guide. Ingersoll observed that the same [[bible|Bible]] used by pro-slavery preachers to justify human bondage was also the book that Christians held up as the supreme authority on [[morality]]. If the [[bible|Bible]] could be cited in defense of owning, beating, and selling human beings, Ingersoll argued, then it could not be the infallible word of a just [[god|God]]. The moral [[truth]] was that slavery was wrong — and the [[bible|Bible]], judged by that truth, was morally defective.

## Ingersoll's Critique

Ingersoll's argument was straightforward and devastating: the [[god|God]] of the [[bible|Old Testament]] explicitly permitted slavery, regulated its practice, and never once condemned it. If [[god|God]] was the author of moral law, His silence on chattel slavery spoke louder than any commandment:

> The real God never sanctioned slavery or polygamy, or the slaughter of women and babies. [014-019]

But this was a statement about what the "real [[god|God]]" *would* do — not about what the biblical [[god|God]] actually did. Ingersoll was candid about the text:

> The Old Testament upholds slavery. [016-005]

He pressed the point relentlessly in public debates: the [[bible|Bible]] contained detailed regulations for buying, selling, and beating slaves (Leviticus 25:44–46; Exodus 21:20–21), but nowhere commanded their emancipation. If the [[bible|Bible]] was [[god|God]]'s word, then:

> God was in favor of slavery four thousand years ago. [015-120]

## The American Context

In the United States, the [[bible|Bible]] was the primary [[reason|intellect]]ual weapon of the pro-slavery [[truth|fact]]ion. Southern theologians developed elaborate biblical defenses of the institution, citing both [[bible|Old Testament]] precedent and [[bible|New Testament]] passages (Ephesians 6:5, Colossians 3:22) in which Paul instructed slaves to obey their masters. Ingersoll noted the grim irony:

> My principal objections to orthodox religion are two — slavery here and hell hereafter. [006-120]

This pairing was deliberate. Just as the doctrine of [[hell|eternal punishment]] revealed a cruel [[god|God]] in the next world, the biblical endorsement of slavery revealed a cruel [[god|God]] in this one. A religion that could produce both doctrines, Ingersoll argued, was morally bankrupt.

| Biblical Passage | Content | Ingersoll's Assessment |
|-----------------|---------|----------------------|
| Leviticus 25:44–46 | Permission to buy and own foreign slaves as property | Explicit endorsement of chattel slavery |
| Exodus 21:20–21 | Master not punished if slave survives a beating | Regulation, not condemnation |
| Ephesians 6:5 | "Slaves, obey your earthly masters" | [[bible|New Testament]] reinforces the institution |
| 1 Timothy 6:1–2 | Slaves should honor masters; Christian masters still own slaves | [[christianity|Christianity]] accommodated slavery; did not abolish it |

## Moral Reasoning vs. Scriptural Authority

The slavery debate provided Ingersoll with his clearest case study in the superiority of [[reason|moral reasoning]] over scriptural authority. Abolitionists who argued against slavery on biblical grounds were forced into tortured hermeneutics — claiming that the [[bible|Bible]]'s slavery passages didn't *really* mean what they plainly said. But abolitionists who argued on the basis of human rights and natural justice needed no such contortions. They simply declared slavery wrong and the [[bible|Bible]] mistaken.

Ingersoll's conclusion was that moral progress came not from better [[bible|Bible]] reading but from the gradual triumph of human sympathy over textual authority. The abolition of slavery was not a Christian achievement but a human one — achieved, in many cases, despite the [[bible|Bible]]'s clear teachings.
