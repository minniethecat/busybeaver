[028-001]_Question_.  What should be done with the surplus revenue?


[028-002]_Answer_.  My answer to that is, reduce internal revenue taxation


[028-003]until the present surplus is exhausted, and then endeavor so to


[028-004]arrange your tariff that you will not produce more than you need.


[028-005]I think the easiest question to grapple with on this earth is a


[028-006]surplus of money.


[028-007]I do not believe in distributing it among the States.  I do not think


[028-008]there could be a better certificate of the prosperity of our country


[028-009]than the fact that we are troubled with a surplus revenue; that we


[028-010]have the machinery for collecting taxes in such perfect order, so


[028-011]ingeniously contrived, that it cannot be stopped; that it goes


[028-012]right on collecting money, whether we want it or not; and the


[028-013]wonderful thing about it is that nobody complains.  If nothing else


[028-014]can be done with the surplus revenue, probably we had better pay


[028-015]some of our debts.  I would suggest, as a last resort, to pay a


[028-016]few honest claims.


[028-017]_Question_.  Are you getting nearer to or farther away from God,


[028-018]Christianity and the Bible?


[028-019]_Answer_.  In the first place, as Mr. Locke so often remarked, we


[028-020]will define our terms.  If by the word "God" is meant a person, a


[028-021]being, who existed before the creation of the universe, and who


[028-022]controls all that is, except himself, I do not believe in such a


[028-023]being; but if by the word God is meant all that is, that is to say,


[028-024]the universe, including every atom and every star, then I am a


[028-025]believer.  I suppose the word that would nearest describe me is


[028-026]"Pantheist."  I cannot believe that a being existed from eternity,


[028-027]and who finally created this universe after having wasted an eternity


[028-028]in idleness; but upon this subject I know just as little as anybody


[028-029]ever did or ever will, and, in my judgment, just as much.  My


[028-030]intellectual horizon is somewhat limited, and, to tell you the


[028-031]truth, this is the only world that I was ever in.  I am what might


[028-032]be called a representative of a rural district, and, as a matter


[028-033]of fact, I know very little about the district.  I believe it was


[028-034]Confucius who said:  "How should I know anything about another


[028-035]world when I know so little of this?"


[028-036]The greatest intellects of the world have endeavored to find words


[028-037]to express their conception of God, of the first cause, or of the


[028-038]science of being, but they have never succeeded.  I find in the


[028-039]old Confession of Faith, in the old Catechism, for instance, this


[028-040]description:  That God is a being without body, parts or passions.


[028-041]I think it would trouble anybody to find a better definition of


[028-042]nothing.  That describes a vacuum, that is to say, that describes


[028-043]the absence of everything.  I find that theology is a subject that


[028-044]only the most ignorant are certain about, and that the more a man


[028-045]thinks, the less he knows.


[028-046]From the Bible God, I do not know that I am going farther and


[028-047]farther away.  I have been about as far as a man could get for many


[028-048]years.  I do not believe in the God of the Old Testament.


[028-049]Now, as to the next branch of your question, Christianity.


[028-050]The question arises, What is Christianity?  I have no objection to


[028-051]the morality taught as a part of Christianity, no objection to its


[028-052]charity, its forgiveness, its kindness; no objection to its hope


[028-053]for this world and another, not the slightest, but all these things


[028-054]do not make Christianity.  Mohammed taught certain doctrines that


[028-055]are good, but the good in the teachings of Mohammed is not Mohammedism.


[028-056]When I speak of Christianity I speak of that which is distinctly


[028-057]Christian.  For instance, the idea that the Infinite God was born


[028-058]in Palestine, learned the carpenter's trade, disputed with the


[028-059]parsons of his time, excited the wrath of the theological bigots,


[028-060]and was finally crucified; that afterward he was raised from the


[028-061]dead, and that if anybody believes this he will be saved and if he


[028-062]fails to believe it, he will be lost; in other words, that which


[028-063]is distinctly Christian in the Christian system, is its supernaturalism,


[028-064]its miracles, its absurdity.  Truth does not need to go into


[028-065]partnership with the supernatural.  What Christ said is worth the


[028-066]reason it contains.  If a man raises the dead and then says twice


[028-067]two are five, that changes no rule in mathematics.  If a multiplication


[028-068]table was divinely inspired, that does no good.  The question is,


[028-069]is it correct?  So I think that in the world of morals, we must


[028-070]prove that a thing is right or wrong by experience, by analogy,


[028-071]not by miracles.  There is no fact in physical science that can be


[028-072]supernaturally demonstrated.  Neither is there any fact in the


[028-073]moral world that could be substantiated by miracles.  Now, then,


[028-074]keeping in mind that by Christianity I mean the supernatural in


[028-075]that system, of course I am just as far away from it as I can get.


[028-076]For the man Christ I have respect.  He was an infidel in his day,


[028-077]and the ministers of his day cried out blasphemy, as they have been


[028-078]crying ever since, against every person who has suggested a new


[028-079]thought or shown the worthlessness of an old one.


[028-080]Now, as to the third part of the question, the Bible.  People say


[028-081]that the Bible is inspired.  Well, what does inspiration mean?


[028-082]Did God write it?  No; but the men who did write it were guided by


[028-083]the Holy Spirit.  Very well.  Did they write exactly what the Holy


[028-084]Spirit wanted them to write?  Well, religious people say, yes.  At


[028-085]the same time they admit that the gentlemen who were collecting,


[028-086]or taking down in shorthand what was said, had to use their own


[028-087]words.  Now, we all know that the same words do not have the same


[028-088]meaning to all people.  It is impossible to convey the same thoughts


[028-089]to all minds by the same language, and it is for that reason that


[028-090]the Bible has produced so many sects, not only disagreeing with


[028-091]each other, but disagreeing among themselves.


[028-092]We find, then, that it is utterly impossible for God (admitting


[028-093]that there is one) to convey the same thoughts in human language


[028-094]to all people.  No two persons understand the same language alike.


[028-095]A man's understanding depends upon his experience, upon his capacity,


[028-096]upon the particular bent of his mind--in fact, upon the countless


[028-097]influences that have made him what he is.  Everything in nature


[028-098]tells everyone who sees it a story, but that story depends upon


[028-099]the capacity of the one to whom it is told.  The sea says one thing


[028-100]to the ordinary man, and another thing to Shakespeare.  The stars


[028-101]have not the same language for all people.  The consequence is that


[028-102]no book can tell the same story to any two persons.  The Jewish


[028-103]Scriptures are like other books, written by different men in


[028-104]different ages of the world, hundreds of years apart, filled with


[028-105]contradictions.  They embody, I presume, fairly enough, the wisdom


[028-106]and ignorance, the reason and prejudice, of the times in which they


[028-107]were written.  They are worth the good that is in them, and the


[028-108]question is whether we will take the good and throw the bad away.


[028-109]There are good laws and bad laws.  There are wise and foolish


[028-110]sayings.  There are gentle and cruel passages, and you can find a


[028-111]text to suit almost any frame of mind; whether you wish to do an


[028-112]act of charity or murder a neighbor's babe, you will find a passage


[028-113]that will exactly fit the case.  So that I can say that I am still


[028-114]for the reasonable, for the natural; and am still opposed to the


[028-115]absurd and supernatural.


[028-116]_Question_.  Is there any better or more ennobling belief than


[028-117]Christianity; if so, what is it?


[028-118]_Answer_.  There are many good things, of course, in every religion,


[028-119]or they would not have existed; plenty of good precepts in


[028-120]Christianity, but the thing that I object to more than all others


[028-121]is the doctrine of eternal punishment, the idea of hell for many


[028-122]and heaven for the few.  Take from Christianity the doctrine of


[028-123]eternal punishment and I have no particular objection to what is


[028-124]generally preached.  If you will take that away, and all the


[028-125]supernatural connected with it, I have no objection; but that


[028-126]doctrine of eternal punishment tends to harden the human heart.


[028-127]It has produced more misery than all the other doctrines in the


[028-128]world.  It has shed more blood; it has made more martyrs.  It has


[028-129]lighted the fires of persecution and kept the sword of cruelty wet


[028-130]with heroic blood for at least a thousand years.  There is no crime


[028-131]that that doctrine has not produced.  I think it would be impossible


[028-132]for the imagination to conceive of a worse religion than orthodox


[028-133]Christianity--utterly impossible; a doctrine that divides this


[028-134]world, a doctrine that divides families, a doctrine that teaches


[028-135]the son that he can be happy, with his mother in perdition; the


[028-136]husband that he can be happy in heaven while his wife suffers the


[028-137]agonies of hell.  This doctrine is infinite injustice, and tends


[028-138]to subvert all ideas of justice in the human heart.  I think it


[028-139]would be impossible to conceive of a doctrine better calculated to


[028-140]make wild beasts of men than that; in fact, that doctrine was born


[028-141]of all the wild beast there is in man.  It was born of infinite


[028-142]revenge.


[028-143]Think of preaching that you must believe that a certain being was


[028-144]the son of God, no matter whether your reason is convinced or not.


[028-145]Suppose one should meet, we will say on London Bridge, a man clad


[028-146]in rags, and he should stop us and say, "My friend, I wish to talk


[028-147]with you a moment.  I am the rightful King of Great Britain," and


[028-148]you should say to him, "Well, my dinner is waiting; I have no time


[028-149]to bother about who the King of England is," and then he should


[028-150]meet another and insist on his stopping while the pulled out some


[028-151]papers to show that he was the rightful King of England, and the


[028-152]other man should say, "I have got business here, my friend; I am


[028-153]selling goods, and I have no time to bother my head about who the


[028-154]King of England is.  No doubt you are the King of England, but you


[028-155]don't look like him."  And then suppose he stops another man, and


[028-156]makes the same statement to him, and the other man should laugh at


[028-157]him and say, "I don't want to hear anything on this subject; you


[028-158]are crazy; you ought to go to some insane asylum, or put something


[028-159]on your head to keep you cool."  And suppose, after all, it should


[028-160]turn out that the man was King of England, and should afterward


[028-161]make his claim good and be crowned in Westminster.  What would we


[028-162]think of that King if he should hunt up the gentlemen that he met


[028-163]on London Bridge, and have their heads cut off because they had


[028-164]no faith that he was the rightful heir?  And what would we think


[028-165]of a God now who would damn a man eighteen hundred years after the


[028-166]event, because he did not believe that he was God at the time he


[028-167]was living in Jerusalem; not only damn the fellows that he met and


[028-168]who did not believe him, but gentlemen who lived eighteen hundred


[028-169]years afterward, and who certainly could have known nothing of the


[028-170]facts except from hearsay?


[028-171]The best religion, after all, is common sense; a religion for this


[028-172]world, one world at a time, a religion for to-day.  We want a


[028-173]religion that will deal in questions in which we are interested.


[028-174]How are we to do away with crime?  How are we to do away with


[028-175]pauperism?  How are we to do away with want and misery in every


[028-176]civilized country?  England is a Christian nation, and yet about


[028-177]one in six in the city of London dies in almshouses, asylums,


[028-178]prisons, hospitals and jails.  We, I suppose, are a civilized


[028-179]nation, and yet all the penitentiaries are crammed; there is want


[028-180]on every hand, and my opinion is that we had better turn our


[028-181]attention to this world.
