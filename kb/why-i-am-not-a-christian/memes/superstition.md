Superstition — belief maintained without evidence and often sustained by fear — was a central category in [[robert-ingersoll|Robert G. Ingersoll]]'s critique of organized [[christianity|religion]]. Ingersoll did not dismiss all religious sentiment as superstitious; he distinguished between religion as compassionate moral inquiry and religion as the uncritical transmission of fear-based dogmas. The latter, in his vocabulary, was superstition — and it was the primary obstacle to [[reason|intellect]]ual and moral progress.

## Ingersoll on Superstition and Religion

Ingersoll frequently equated what passed for [[christianity|orthodox Christianity]] with superstition. He argued that doctrines like [[hell|eternal punishment]], biblical inerrancy, and [[immortality|the afterlife]] were believed not because they were supported by evidence but because they had been inherited from a pre-scientific age:

> What you call religion is simply superstition. Religion as it is now... (034-049)

This was not a blanket condemnation of religious feeling. Ingersoll praised the moral teachings he found in many traditions and acknowledged that religion could inspire genuine compassion. What made a belief superstitious, in his analysis, was its independence from [[reason]] and evidence — its survival through authority and fear rather than demonstration and inquiry.

The metaphor Ingersoll returned to was bondage: superstition enslaved the mind, and [[freethought|free thought]] emancipated it. He described the project of freethought as an effort to "do away with the bondage of superstition" and to liberate humanity from "ignorance and superstition" which "are based upon the idea" that authority should replace inquiry (016-063, 030-143).

## Superstition and the Bible

Ingersoll traced many superstitious beliefs to the [[bible|Bible]] itself. He argued that the legal codes of the [[bible|Old Testament]] — with their rules about ritual purity, Sabbath observance, and sacrificial atonement — were products of the same superstitious mentality found in other ancient cultures. The difference, in his view, was not that biblical superstitions were true but that they were protected from criticism by the doctrine of divine inspiration:

> ...were passed by superstition, and the enlightened people of... (001-169)

For Ingersoll, the [[truth|fact]] that biblical laws had been "passed by superstition" disqualified them as moral authorities for the present. He saw no reason why rules devised by Iron Age pastoralists should govern the conduct of nineteenth-century Americans, and he regarded the attempt to enforce such rules as a form of cultural regression.

## The Decline of Superstition

Despite his sharp critique, Ingersoll was optimistic about superstition's trajectory. He believed that the advance of [[science]], the spread of literacy, and the growth of democratic institutions were gradually eroding the foundations of supernatural belief:

> ...in a little while the sacred superstitions... (032-157)

He predicted that "the Delilah of superstition" would eventually be shorn of its power — not through persecution of believers but through the patient work of education and public argument (030-028). The "enlightened people" of each generation, he observed, discarded more and more of the superstitions their ancestors had regarded as essential.

This optimism was grounded in Ingersoll's faith in [[truth]] — his conviction that truth, once discovered, could never be permanently suppressed. Superstition survived, he argued, only where inquiry was forbidden; wherever [[freethought|free thought]] was permitted, superstition withered.

## Relation to Ingersoll's Core Concepts

- **Superstition and [[reason]]**: [[reason|Reason]] is the antidote to superstition. Where reason is permitted to operate, superstition cannot survive.
- **Superstition and [[freethought]]**: [[freethought|Freethought]] is the deliberate practice of examining beliefs and discarding those that lack evidence — the opposite of superstitious acceptance.
- **Superstition and [[hell]]**: The doctrine of hell is, for Ingersoll, superstition's most harmful product — a belief sustained entirely by fear, with no rational warrant.
- **Superstition and [[science]]**: Science is the institutional embodiment of reason; its success in explaining nature is the strongest argument against superstitious worldviews.
- **Superstition and [[bible]]**: The [[bible|Bible]], in Ingersoll's reading, is a repository of ancient superstitions elevated to divine status — "everything born of superstition" includes scripture itself (032-054).
