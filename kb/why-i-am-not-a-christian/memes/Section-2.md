[001-001]I do not believe the trans-continental commerce of this country


[001-002]should depend on one railroad.  I want new territories opened.  I


[001-003]want to see American steamships running to all the great ports of


[001-004]the world.  I want to see our flag flying on all the seas and in


[001-005]all the harbors.  We have the best country, and, in my judgment,


[001-006]the best people in the world, and we ought to be the most prosperous


[001-007]nation on the earth.


[001-008]_Question_.  Then you only consider the Greenback movement a


[001-009]temporary thing?


[001-010]_Answer_.  Yes; I do not believe that there is anything permanent


[001-011]in anything that is not sound, that has not a perfectly sound


[001-012]foundation, and I mean sound, sound in every sense of that word.


[001-013]It must be wise and honest.  We have plenty of money; the trouble


[001-014]is to get it.  If the Greenbackers will pass a law furnishing all


[001-015]of us with collaterals, there certainly would be no trouble about


[001-016]getting the money.  Nothing can demonstrate more fully the


[001-017]plentifulness of money than the fact that millions of four per


[001-018]cent. bonds have been taken in the United States.  The trouble is,


[001-019]business is scarce.


[001-020]_Question_.  But do you not think the Greenback movement will help


[001-021]the Democracy to success in 1880?


[001-022]_Answer_.  I think the Greenback movement will injure the Republican


[001-023]party much more than the Democratic party.  Whether that injury


[001-024]will reach as far as 1880 depends simply upon one thing.  If


[001-025]resumption--in spite of all the resolutions to the contrary--


[001-026]inaugurates an era of prosperity, as I believe and hope it will,


[001-027]then it seems to me that the Republican party will be as strong in


[001-028]the North as in its palmiest days.  Of course I regard most of the


[001-029]old issues as settled, and I make this statement simply because I


[001-030]regard the financial issue as the only living one.


[001-031]Of course, I have no idea who will be the Democratic candidate,


[001-032]but I suppose the South will be solid for the Democratic nominee,


[001-033]unless the financial question divides that section of the country.


[001-034]_Question_.  With a solid South do you not think the Democratic


[001-035]nominee will stand a good chance?


[001-036]_Answer_.  Certainly, he will stand the best chance if the Democracy


[001-037]is right on the financial question; if it will cling to its old


[001-038]idea of hard money, he will.  If the Democrats will recognize that


[001-039]the issues of the war are settled, then I think that party has the


[001-040]best chance.


[001-041]_Question_.  But if it clings to soft money?


[001-042]_Answer_.  Then I think it will be beaten, if by soft money it


[001-043]means the payment of one promise with another.


[001-044]_Question_.  You consider Greenbackers inflationists, do you not?


[001-045]_Answer_.  I suppose the Greenbackers to be the party of inflation.


[001-046]I am in favor of inflation produced by industry.  I am in favor of


[001-047]the country being inflated with corn, with wheat, good houses,


[001-048]books, pictures, and plenty of labor for everybody.  I am in favor


[001-049]of being inflated with gold and silver, but I do not believe in


[001-050]the inflation of promise, expectation and speculation.  I sympathize


[001-051]with every man who is willing to work and cannot get it, and I


[001-052]sympathize to that degree that I would like to see the fortunate


[001-053]and prosperous taxed to support his unfortunate brother until labor


[001-054]could be found.


[001-055]The Greenback party seems to think credit is just as good as gold.


[001-056]While the credit lasts this is so; but the trouble is, whenever it


[001-057]is ascertained that the gold is gone or cannot be produced the


[001-058]credit takes wings.  The bill of a perfectly solvent bank may


[001-059]circulate for years.  Now, because nobody demands the gold on that


[001-060]bill it doesn't follow that the bill would be just as good without


[001-061]any gold behind it.  The idea that you can have the gold whenever


[001-062]you present the bill gives it its value.  To illustrate:  A poor


[001-063]man buys soup tickets.  He is not hungry at the time of purchase,


[001-064]and will not be for some hours.  During those hours the Greenback


[001-065]gentlemen argue that there is no use of keeping any soup on hand


[001-066]with which to redeem these tickets, and from this they further


[001-067]argue that if they can be good for a few hours without soup, why


[001-068]not forever?  And they would be, only the holder gets hungry.


[001-069]Until he is hungry, of course, he does not care whether any soup


[001-070]is on hand or not, but when he presents his ticket he wants his


[001-071]soup, and the idea that he can have the soup when he does present


[001-072]the ticket gives it its value.  And so I regard bank notes, without


[001-073]gold and silver, as of the same value as tickets without soup.


[001-074]--_The Post_, Washington, D. C., 1878.


[001-075]THE PRE-MILLENNIAL CONFERENCE.


[001-076]_Question_.  What do you think of the Pre-Millennial Conference


[001-077]that was held in New York City recently?


[001-078]_Answer_.  Well, I think that all who attended it were believers


[001-079]in the Bible, and any one who believes in prophecies and looks to


[001-080]their fulfillment will go insane.  A man that tries from Daniel's


[001-081]ram with three horns and five tails and his deformed goats to


[001-082]ascertain the date of the second immigration of Christ to this


[001-083]world is already insane.  It all shows that the moment we leave


[001-084]the realm of fact and law we are adrift on the wide and shoreless


[001-085]sea of theological speculation.


[001-086]_Question_.  Do you think there will be a second coming?


[001-087]_Answer_.  No, not as long as the church is in power.  Christ will


[001-088]never again visit this earth until the Freethinkers have control.


[001-089]He will certainly never allow another church to get hold of him.


[001-090]The very persons who met in New York to fix the date of his coming


[001-091]would despise him and the feeling would probably be mutual.  In


[001-092]his day Christ was an Infidel, and made himself unpopular by


[001-093]denouncing the church as it then existed.  He called them liars,


[001-094]hypocrites, thieves, vipers, whited sepulchres and fools.  From


[001-095]the description given of the church in that day, I am afraid that


[001-096]should he come again, he would be provoked into using similar


[001-097]language.  Of course, I admit there are many good people in the


[001-098]church, just as there were some good Pharisees who were opposed to


[001-099]the crucifixion.


[001-100]--_The Express_, Buffalo, New York, Nov. 4th, 1878.


[001-101]THE SOLID SOUTH AND RESUMPTION.


[001-102]_Question_.  Colonel, to start with, what do you think of the solid


[001-103]South?


[001-104]_Answer_.  I think the South is naturally opposed to the Republican


[001-105]party; more, I imagine, to the name, than to the personnel of the


[001-106]organization.  But the South has just as good friends in the


[001-107]Republican party as in the Democratic party.  I do not think there


[001-108]are any Republicans who would not rejoice to see the South prosperous


[001-109]and happy.  I know of none, at least.  They will have to get over


[001-110]the prejudices born of isolation.  We lack direct and constant


[001-111]communication.  I do not recollect having seen a newspaper from


[001-112]the Gulf States for a long time.  They, down there, may imagine


[001-113]that the feeling in the North is the same as during the war.  But


[001-114]it certainly is not.  The Northern people are anxious to be friendly;


[001-115]and if they can be, without a violation of their principles, they


[001-116]will be.  Whether it be true or not, however, most of the Republicans


[001-117]of the North believe that no Republican in the South is heartily


[001-118]welcome in that section, whether he goes there from the North, or


[001-119]is a Southern man.  Personally, I do not care anything about partisan


[001-120]politics.  I want to see every man in the United States guaranteed


[001-121]the right to express his choice at the ballot-box, and I do not


[001-122]want social ostracism to follow a man, no matter how he may vote.


[001-123]A solid South means a solid North.  A hundred thousand Democratic


[001-124]majority in South Carolina means fifty thousand Republican majority


[001-125]in New York in 1880.  I hope the sections will never divide, simply


[001-126]as sections.  But if the Republican party is not allowed to live


[001-127]in the South, the Democratic party certainly will not be allowed


[001-128]to succeed in the North.  I want to treat the people of the South


[001-129]precisely as though the Rebellion had never occurred.  I want all


[001-130]that wiped from the slate of memory, and all I ask of the Southern


[001-131]people is to give the same rights to the Republicans that we are


[001-132]willing to give to them and have given to them.


[001-133]_Question_.  How do you account for the results of the recent


[001-134]elections?


[001-135]_Answer_.  The Republican party won the recent election simply


[001-136]because it was for honest money, and it was in favor of resumption.


[001-137]And if on the first of January next, we resume all right, and


[001-138]maintain resumption, I see no reason why the Republican party should


[001-139]not succeed in 1880.  The Republican party came into power at the


[001-140]commencement of the Rebellion, and necessarily retained power until


[001-141]its close; and in my judgment, it will retain power so long as in


[001-142]the horizon of credit there is a cloud of repudiation as large as


[001-143]a man's hand.


[001-144]_Question_.  Do you think resumption will work out all right?


[001-145]_Answer_.  I do.  I think that on the first of January the greenback


[001-146]will shake hands with gold on an equality, and in a few days


[001-147]thereafter will be worth just a little bit more.  Everything has


[001-148]resumed, except the Government.  All the property has resumed, all


[001-149]the lands, bonds and mortgages and stocks.  All these things resumed


[001-150]long ago--that is to say, they have touched the bottom.  Now, there


[001-151]is no doubt that the party that insists on the Government paying


[001-152]all its debts will hold control, and no one will get his hand on


[001-153]the wheel who advocates repudiation in any form.  There is one


[001-154]thing we must do, though.  We have got to put more silver in our


[001-155]dollars.  I do not think you can blame the New York banks--any bank


[001-156]--for refusing to take eighty-eight cents for a dollar.  Neither


[001-157]can you blame any depositor who puts gold in the bank for demanding


[001-158]gold in return.  Yes, we must have in the silver dollar a dollar's


[001-159]worth of silver.


[001-160]--_The Commercial_, Cincinnati, Ohio, November, 1878.


[001-161]THE SUNDAY LAWS OF PITTSBURG.*


[001-162]_Question_.  Colonel, what do you think of the course the Mayor


[001-163]has pursued toward you in attempting to stop your lecture?


[001-164]_Answer_.  I know very little except what I have seen in the morning


[001-165]paper.  As a general rule, laws should be enforced or repealed;


[001-166]and so far as I am personally concerned, I shall not so much complain


[001-167]of the enforcing of the law against Sabbath breaking as of the fact


[001-168]that such a law exists.  We have fallen heir to these laws.  They


[001-169]were passed by superstition, and the enlightened people of to-day


[001-170]should repeal them.  Ministers should not expect to fill their


[001-171]churches by shutting up other places.  They can only increase their


[001-172]congregations by improving their sermons.  They will have more


[001-173]hearers when they say more worth hearing.  I have no idea that the


[001-174]Mayor has any prejudice against me personally and if he only enforces


[001-175]the law, I shall have none against him.  If my lectures were free


[001-176]the ministers might have the right to object, but as I charge one


[001-177]dollar admission and they nothing, they ought certainly be able to


[001-178]compete with me.


[001-179]_Question_.  Don't you think it is the duty of the Mayor, as chief


[001-180]executive of the city laws, to enforce the ordinances and pay no


[001-181]attention to what the statutes say?


[001-182]_Answer_.  I suppose it to be the duty of the Mayor to enforce the


[001-183]ordinance of the city and if the ordinance of the city covers the


[001-184]same ground as the law of the State, a conviction under the ordinance


[001-185]would be a bar to prosecution under the State law.
