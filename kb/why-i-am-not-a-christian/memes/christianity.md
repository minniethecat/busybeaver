# Christianity

The Christian religion as examined and critiqued by [[robert-ingersoll|Robert G. Ingersoll]] in his collected interviews. Ingersoll, known as "[[robert-ingersoll|The Great Agnostic]]," devoted much of his public life to challenging orthodox Christian doctrines, questioning the authority of the [[bible|Bible]], and advocating for [[freethought|free thought]].

## Ingersoll's Core Critique

Ingersoll's central argument against orthodox Christianity was that it rested on fear rather than reason. He maintained that "no man believes in Christianity because he investigates it — he believes because he was taught it" [017-064]. For Ingersoll, genuine religious inquiry required [[reason|intellect]]ual honesty, which he found incompatible with the dogmatic claims of the Christian church.

He was particularly critical of the doctrine of [[hell|eternal punishment]], observing that this was "the only honest consolation that Christianity can have for the bereaved" — the promise that non-believing loved ones would suffer eternally [018-197]. In his view, the Christian promise of heaven for believers and hell for unbelievers was morally indefensible.

## The Bible as Foundation

Ingersoll regarded the [[bible|Bible]] as the cornerstone of Christian belief, but argued that its authority was undermined by internal contradictions and morally problematic passages. He observed that both supporters and opponents of slavery had appealed to scripture for justification:

> the Bible except those upholding slavery — it seemed to me that if the Bible was inspired the Devil must have got hold of it. [010-112]

He challenged the doctrine of biblical inspiration by noting that "there are many good things in the [[bible|Bible]]" but also "ten times more good things than the [[bible|Bible]]" in other books, yet "no one claims" those books to be divinely inspired [010-086].

## Calvinism and Its Effects

Ingersoll reserved his harshest criticism for Calvinism, which he described as "the cancer of Christianity" [013-076]. He believed that the Calvinist doctrine of predestination corrupted moral reasoning. He pointed to the case of Charles Guiteau, who assassinated President Garfield, as an example of "sectarian Christianity, by which Guiteau was led to believe he was doing [[god|God]]'s will" [017-046].

## Modern Christianity

Despite his criticisms, Ingersoll acknowledged that modern Christianity had evolved beyond its earlier forms. He noted that "modern Christianity has for" its basis a more humane and tolerant ethic, influenced by broader cultural progress rather than fidelity to scripture [004-181]. He argued that the moral improvements in Christian societies were "not growing out of Christianity, but out of the politeness of" secular civilization [018-203].
