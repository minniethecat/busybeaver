[063-001]_Question_.  Why do you make such a distinction between the rights


[063-002]of man and the rights of women?


[063-003]_Answer_.  The woman has, as her capital, her youth, her beauty.


[063-004]We will say that she is married at twenty or twenty-five.  In a


[063-005]few years she has lost her beauty.  During these years the man, so


[063-006]far as capacity to make money is concerned--to do something--has


[063-007]grown better and better.  That is to say, his chances have improved;


[063-008]hers have diminished.  She has dowered him with the Spring of her


[063-009]life, and as her life advances her chances decrease.  Consequently,


[063-010]I would give her the advantage, and I would not compel her to remain


[063-011]with him against her will.  It seems to me far worse to be a wife


[063-012]upon compulsion than to be a husband upon compulsion.  Besides


[063-013]this, I have a feeling of infinite tenderness toward mothers.  The


[063-014]woman that bears children certainly should not be compelled to live


[063-015]with a man whom she despises.  The suffering is enough when the


[063-016]father of the child is to her the one man of all the world.  Many


[063-017]people who have a mechanical apparatus in their breasts that assists


[063-018]in the circulation of what they call blood, regard these views as


[063-019]sentimental.  But when you take sentiment out of the world nothing


[063-020]is left worth living for, and when you get sentiment out of the


[063-021]heart it is nothing more or less than a pump, an old piece of rubber


[063-022]that has acquired the habit of contracting and dilating.  But I


[063-023]have this consolation:  The people that do not agree with me are


[063-024]those that do not understand me.


[063-025]--_New York World_, 1888.


[063-026]SECULARISM.


[063-027]_Question_.  Colonel, what is your opinion of Secularism?  Do you


[063-028]regard it as a religion?


[063-029]_Answer_.  I understand that the word Secularism embraces everything


[063-030]that is of any real interest or value to the human race.  I take


[063-031]it for granted that everybody will admit that well-being is the


[063-032]only good; that is to say, that it is impossible to conceive of


[063-033]anything of real value that does not tend either to preserve or to


[063-034]increase the happiness of some sentient being.  Secularism, therefore,


[063-035]covers the entire territory.  It fills the circumference of human


[063-036]knowledge and of human effort.  It is, you may say, the religion


[063-037]of this world; but if there is another world, it is necessarily


[063-038]the religion of that, as well.


[063-039]Man finds himself in this world naked and hungry.  He needs food,


[063-040]raiment, shelter.  He finds himself filled with almost innumerable


[063-041]wants.  To gratify these wants is the principal business of life.


[063-042]To gratify them without interfering with other people is the course


[063-043]pursued by all honest men.


[063-044]Secularism teaches us to be good here and now.  I know nothing


[063-045]better than goodness.  Secularism teaches us to be just here and


[063-046]now.  It is impossible to be juster than just.


[063-047]Man can be as just in this world as in any other, and justice must


[063-048]be the same in all worlds.  Secularism teaches a man to be generous,


[063-049]and generosity is certainly as good here as it can be anywhere


[063-050]else. Secularism teaches a man to be charitable, and certainly


[063-051]charity is as beautiful in this world and in this short life as it


[063-052]could be were man immortal.


[063-053]But orthodox people insist that there is something higher than


[063-054]Secularism; but, as a matter of fact, the mind of man can conceive


[063-055]of nothing better, nothing higher, nothing more spiritual, than


[063-056]goodness, justice, generosity, charity.  Neither has the mind of


[063-057]men been capable of finding a nobler incentive to action than human


[063-058]love.  Secularism has to do with every possible relation.  It says


[063-059]to the young man and to the young woman:  "Don't marry unless you


[063-060]can take care of yourselves and your children."  It says to the


[063-061]parents:  "Live for your children; put forth every effort to the


[063-062]end that your children may know more than you--that they may be


[063-063]better and grander than you."  It says:  "You have no right to


[063-064]bring children into the world that you are not able to educate and


[063-065]feed and clothe."  It says to those who have diseases that can be


[063-066]transmitted to children:  "Do not marry; do not become parents; do


[063-067]not perpetuate suffering, deformity, agony, imbecility, insanity,


[063-068]poverty, wretchedness."


[063-069]Secularism tells all children to do the best they can for their


[063-070]parents--to discharge every duty and every obligation.  It defines


[063-071]the relation that should exist between husband and wife; between


[063-072]parent and child; between the citizen and the Nation.  And not only


[063-073]that, but between nations.


[063-074]Secularism is a religion that is to be used everywhere, and at all


[063-075]times--that is to be taught everywhere and practiced at all times.


[063-076]It is not a religion that is so dangerous that it must be kept out


[063-077]of the schools; it is not a religion that is so dangerous that it


[063-078]must be kept out of politics.  It belongs in the schools; it belongs


[063-079]at the polls.  It is the business of Secularism to teach every


[063-080]child; to teach every voter.  It is its business to discuss all


[063-081]political problems, and to decide all questions that affect the


[063-082]rights or the happiness of a human being.


[063-083]Orthodox religion is a firebrand; it must be kept out of the schools;


[063-084]it must be kept out of politics.  All the churches unite in saying


[063-085]that orthodox religion is not for every day use.  The Catholics


[063-086]object to any Protestant religion being taught to children.


[063-087]Protestants object to any Catholic religion being taught to


[063-088]children.  But the Secularist wants his religion taught to all;


[063-089]and his religion can produce no feeling, for the reason that it


[063-090]consists of facts--of truths.  And all of it is important; important


[063-091]for the child, important for the parent, important for the politician


[063-092]--for the President--for all in power; important to every legislator,


[063-093]to every professional man, to every laborer and every farmer--that


[063-094]is to say, to every human being.


[063-095]The great benefit of Secularism is that is appeals to the reason


[063-096]of every man.  It asks every man to think for himself.  It does


[063-097]not threaten punishment if a man thinks, but it offers a reward,


[063-098]for fear that he will not think.  It does not say, "You will be


[063-099]damned in another world if you think."  But it says, "You will be


[063-100]damned in this world if you do not think."


[063-101]Secularism preserves the manhood and the womanhood of all.  It says


[063-102]to each human being:  "Stand upon your own feet.  Count one!


[063-103]Examine for yourself.  Investigate, observe, think.  Express your


[063-104]opinion.  Stand by your judgment, unless you are convinced you are


[063-105]wrong, and when you are convinced, you can maintain and preserve


[063-106]your manhood or womanhood only by admitting that you were wrong."


[063-107]It is impossible that the whole world should agree on one creed.


[063-108]It may be impossible that any two human beings can agree exactly


[063-109]in religious belief.  Secularism teaches that each one must take


[063-110]care of himself, that the first duty of man is to himself, to the


[063-111]end that he may be not only useful to himself, but to others.  He


[063-112]who fails to take care of himself becomes a burden; the first duty


[063-113]of man is not to be a burden.


[063-114]Every Secularist can give a reason for his creed.  First of all,


[063-115]he believes in work--taking care of himself.  He believes in the


[063-116]cultivation of the intellect, to the end that he may take advantage


[063-117]of the forces of nature--to the end that he may be clothed and fed


[063-118]and sheltered.


[063-119]He also believes in giving to every other human being every right


[063-120]that he claims for himself.  He does not depend on prayer.  He has


[063-121]no confidence in ghosts or phantoms.  He knows nothing of another


[063-122]world, and knows just as little of a First Cause.  But what little


[063-123]he does know, he endeavors to use, and to use for the benefit of


[063-124]himself and others.


[063-125]He knows that he sustains certain relations to other sentient


[063-126]beings, and he endeavors to add to the aggregate of human joy.  He


[063-127]is his own church, his own priest, his own clergyman and his own


[063-128]pope.  He decides for himself; in other words, he is a free man.


[063-129]He also has a Bible, and this Bible embraces all the good and true


[063-130]things that have been written, no matter by whom, or in what


[063-131]language, or in what time.  He accepts everything that he believes


[063-132]to be true, and rejects all that he thinks is false.  He knows that


[063-133]nothing is added to the probability of an event, because there has


[063-134]been an account of it written and printed.


[063-135]All that has been said that is true is part of his Bible.  Every


[063-136]splendid and noble thought, every good word, every kind action--


[063-137]all these you will find in his Bible.  And, in addition to these,


[063-138]all that is absolutely known--that has been demonstrated--belongs


[063-139]to the Secularist.  All the inventions, machines--everything that


[063-140]has been of assistance to the human race--belongs to his religion.


[063-141]The Secularist is in possession of everything that man has.  He is


[063-142]deprived only of that which man never had.  The orthodox world


[063-143]believes in ghosts and phantoms, in dreams and prayers, in miracles


[063-144]and monstrosities; that is to say, in modern theology.  But these


[063-145]things do not exist, or if they do exist, it is impossible for a


[063-146]human being to ascertain the fact.  Secularism has no "castles in


[063-147]Spain."  It has no glorified fog.  It depends upon realities, upon


[063-148]demonstrations; and its end and aim is to make this world better


[063-149]every day--to do away with poverty and crime, and to cover the


[063-150]world with happy and contended homes.


[063-151]Let me say, right here, that a few years ago the Secular Hall at


[063-152]Leicester, England, was opened by a speech from George Jacob


[063-153]Holyoake, entitled, "Secularism as a Religion."  I have never read


[063-154]anything better on the subject of Secularism than this address.


[063-155]It is so clear and so manly that I do not see how any human being


[063-156]can read it without becoming convinced, and almost enraptured.


[063-157]Let me quote a few lies from this address:--


[063-158]"The mind of man would die if it were not for Thought, and were


[063-159]Thought suppressed, God would rule over a world of idiots.


[063-160]"Nature feeds Thought, day and night, with a million hands.


[063-161]"To think is a duty, because it is a man's duty not to be a fool.


[063-162]"If man does not think himself, he is an intellectual pauper, living


[063-163]upon the truth acquired by others, and making no contribution


[063-164]himself in return.  He has no ideas but such as he obtains by 'out-


[063-165]door relief,' and he goes about the world with a charity mind.


[063-166]"The more thinkers there are in the world, the more truth there is


[063-167]in the world.


[063-168]"Progress can only walk in the footsteps of Conviction.


[063-169]"Coercion in thought is not progress, it reduces to ignominious


[063-170]pulp the backbone of the mind.


[063-171]"By Religion I mean the simple creed of deed and duty, by which a


[063-172]man seeks his own welfare in his own way, with an honest and fair


[063-173]regard to the welfare and ways of others.


[063-174]"In these thinking and practical days, men demand a religion of


[063-175]daily life, which stands on a business footing."


[063-176]I think nothing could be much better than the following, which


[063-177]shows the exact relation that orthodox religion sustains to the


[063-178]actual wants of human beings:


[063-179]"The Churches administer a system of Foreign Affairs.


[063-180]"Secularism dwells in a land of its own.  It dwells in a land of


[063-181]Certitude.


[063-182]"In the Kingdom of Thought there is no conquest over man, but over


[063-183]foolishness only."


[063-184]I will not quote more, but hope all who read this will read the


[063-185]address of Mr. Holyoake, who has, in my judgment, defined Secularism


[063-186]with the greatest possible clearness.
