[047-001]It is a curious kind of a spectacle to see a few hundred people


[047-002]paying a few thousand dollars a year for the purpose of hearing


[047-003]these great problems discussed:  "Was Adam the first man?"  "Who


[047-004]was Cain's wife?"  "Has anyone seen a map of the land of Nod?"


[047-005]"Where are the four rivers that ran murmuring through the groves


[047-006]of Paradise?"  "Who was the snake?  How did he walk?  What language


[047-007]did he speak?"  This turns a church into a kind of nursery, makes


[047-008]a cradle of each pew, and gives to each member a rattle with which


[047-009]he can amuse what he calls his mind.


[047-010]The great theologians of Andover--the gentlemen who wear the brass


[047-011]collars furnished by the dead founder--have been disputing among


[047-012]themselves as to what is to become of the heathen who fortunately


[047-013]died before meeting any missionary from that institution.  One can


[047-014]almost afford to be damned hereafter for the sake of avoiding the


[047-015]dogmas of Andover here.  Nothing more absurd and childish has ever


[047-016]happened--not in the intellectual, but in the theological world.


[047-017]There is no need of the Freethinkers saying anything at present.


[047-018]The work is being done by the church members themselves.  They are


[047-019]beginning to ask questions of the clergy.  They are getting tired


[047-020]of the old ideas--tired of the consolations of eternal pain--tired


[047-021]of hearing about hell--tired of hearing the Bible quoted or talked


[047-022]about--tired of the scheme of redemption--tired of the Trinity, of


[047-023]the plenary inspiration of the barbarous records of a barbarous


[047-024]people--tired of the patriarchs and prophets--tired of Daniel and


[047-025]the goats with three horns, and the image with the clay feet, and


[047-026]the little stone that rolled down the hill--tired of the mud man


[047-027]and the rib woman--tired of the flood of Noah, of the astronomy of


[047-028]Joshua, the geology of Moses--tired of Kings and Chronicles and


[047-029]Lamentations--tired of the lachrymose Jeremiah--tired of the


[047-030]monstrous, the malicious, and the miraculous.  In short, they are


[047-031]beginning to think.  They have bowed their necks to the yoke of


[047-032]ignorance and fear and impudence and superstition, until they are


[047-033]weary.  They long to be free.  They are tired of the services--


[047-034]tired of the meaningless prayers--tired of hearing each other say,


[047-035]"Hear us, good Lord"--tired of the texts, tired of the sermons,


[047-036]tired of the lies about spontaneous combustion as a punishment for


[047-037]blasphemy, tired of the bells, and they long to hear the doxology


[047-038]of superstition.  They long to have Common Sense lift its hands in


[047-039]benediction and dismiss the congregation.


[047-040]--_Brooklyn Citizen_, April, 1886.


[047-041]TRIAL OF THE CHICAGO ANARCHISTS.


[047-042]_Question_.  What do you think of the trial of the Chicago Anarchists


[047-043]and their chances for a new trial?


[047-044]_Answer_.  I have paid some attention to the evidence and to the


[047-045]rulings of the court, and I have read the opinion of the Supreme


[047-046]Court of Illinois, in which the conviction is affirmed.  Of course


[047-047]these men were tried during a period of great excitement--tried


[047-048]when the press demanded their conviction--when it was asserted that


[047-049]society was on the edge of destruction unless these men were hanged.


[047-050]Under such circumstances, it is not easy to have a fair and impartial


[047-051]trial.  A judge should either sit beyond the reach of prejudice,


[047-052]in some calm that storms cannot invade, or he should be a kind of


[047-053]oak that before any blast he would stand erect.  It is hard to find


[047-054]such a place as I have suggested and not easy to find such a man.


[047-055]We are all influenced more or less by our surroundings, by the


[047-056]demands and opinions and feelings and prejudices of our fellow-


[047-057]citizens.  There is a personality made up of many individuals known


[047-058]as society.  This personality has prejudices like an individual.


[047-059]It often becomes enraged, acts without the slightest sense, and


[047-060]repents at its leisure.  It is hard to reason with a mob whether


[047-061]organized or disorganized, whether acting in the name of the law


[047-062]or of simple brute force.  But in any case, where people refuse to


[047-063]be governed by reason, they become a mob.


[047-064]_Question_.  Do you not think that these men had a fair trial?


[047-065]_Answer_.  I have no doubt that the court endeavored to be fair--


[047-066]no doubt that Judge Gary is a perfectly honest, upright man, but


[047-067]I think his instructions were wrong.  He instructed the jury to


[047-068]the effect that where men have talked in a certain way, and where


[047-069]the jury believed that the result of such talk might be the commission


[047-070]of a crime, that such men are responsible for that crime.  Of


[047-071]course, there is neither law nor sense in an instruction like this.


[047-072]I hold that it must have been the intention of the man making the


[047-073]remark, or publishing the article, or doing the thing--it must have


[047-074]been his intention that the crime should be committed.  Men differ


[047-075]as to the effect of words, and a man may say a thing with the best


[047-076]intentions the result of which is a crime, and he may say a thing


[047-077]with the worst of intentions and the result may not be a crime.


[047-078]The Supreme Court of Illinois seemed to have admitted that the


[047-079]instructions were wrong, but took the ground that it made no


[047-080]difference with the verdict.  This is a dangerous course for the


[047-081]court of last resort to pursue; neither is it very complimentary


[047-082]to the judge who tried the case, that his instructions had no effect


[047-083]upon the jury.  Under the instructions of the court below, any man


[047-084]who had been arrested with the seven Anarchists and of whom it


[047-085]could be proved that he had ever said a word in favor of any change


[047-086]in government, or of other peculiar ideas, no matter whether he


[047-087]knew of the meeting at the Haymarket or not, would have been


[047-088]convicted.


[047-089]I am satisfied that the defendant Fielden never intended to harm


[047-090]a human being.  As a matter of fact, the evidence shows that he


[047-091]was making a speech in favor of peace at the time of the occurrence.


[047-092]The evidence also shows that he was an exceedingly honest, industrious,


[047-093]and a very poor and philanthropic man.


[047-094]_Question_.  Do you uphold the Anarchists?


[047-095]_Answer_.  Certainly not.  There is no place in this country for


[047-096]the Anarchist.  The source of power here is the people, and to


[047-097]attack the political power is to attack the people.  If the laws


[047-098]are oppressive, it is the fault of the oppressed.  If the laws


[047-099]touch the poor and leave them without redress, it is the fault of


[047-100]the poor.  They are in a majority.  The men who work for their


[047-101]living are the very men who have the power to make every law that


[047-102]is made in the United States.  There is no excuse for any resort


[047-103]to violence in this country.  The boycotting by trades unions and


[047-104]by labor organizations is all wrong.  Let them resort to legal


[047-105]methods and to no other.  I have not the slightest sympathy with


[047-106]the methods that have been pursued by Anarchists, or by Socialists,


[047-107]or by any other class that has resorted to force or intimidation.


[047-108]The ballot-box is the place to assemble.  The will of the people


[047-109]can be made known in that way, and their will can be executed.  At


[047-110]the same time, I think I understand what has produced the Anarchist,


[047-111]the Socialist, and the agitator.  In the old country, a laboring


[047-112]man, poorly clad, without quite enough to eat, with a wife in rags,


[047-113]with a few children asking for bread--this laboring man sees the


[047-114]idle enjoying every luxury of this life; he sees on the breast of


[047-115]"my lady" a bonfire of diamonds; he sees "my lord" riding in his


[047-116]park; he sees thousands of people who from the cradle to the grave


[047-117]do no useful act; add nothing to the intellectual or the physical


[047-118]wealth of the world; he sees labor living in the tenement house,


[047-119]in the hut; idleness and nobility in the mansion and the palace;


[047-120]the poor man a trespasser everywhere except upon the street, where


[047-121]he is told to "move on," and in the dusty highways of the country.


[047-122]That man naturally hates the government--the government of the few,


[047-123]the government that lives on the unpaid labor of the many, the


[047-124]government that takes the child from the parents, and puts him in


[047-125]the army to fight the child of another poor man and woman in some


[047-126]other country.  These Anarchists, these Socialists, these agitators,


[047-127]have been naturally produced.  All the things of which I have spoken


[047-128]sow in the breast of poverty the seeds of hatred and revolution.


[047-129]These poor men, hunted by the officers of the law, cornered,


[047-130]captured, imprisoned, excite the sympathy of other poor men, and


[047-131]if some are dragged to the gallows and hanged, or beheaded by the


[047-132]guillotine, they become saints and martyrs, and those who sympathize


[047-133]with them feel that they have the power, and only the power of


[047-134]hatred--the power of riot, of destruction--the power of the torch,


[047-135]of revolution, that is to say, of chaos and anarchy.  The injustice


[047-136]of the higher classes makes the lower criminal.  Then there is


[047-137]another thing.  The misery of the poor excites in many noble breasts


[047-138]sympathy, and the men who thus sympathize wish to better the


[047-139]condition of their fellows.  At first they depend upon reason, upon


[047-140]calling the attention of the educated and powerful to the miseries


[047-141]of the poor.  Nothing happens, no result follows.  The Juggernaut


[047-142]of society moves on, and the wretches are still crushed beneath


[047-143]the great wheels.  These men who are really good at first, filled


[047-144]with sympathy, now become indignant--they are malicious, then


[047-145]destructive and criminal.  I do not sympathize with these methods,


[047-146]but I do sympathize with the general object that all good and


[047-147]generous people seek to accomplish--namely, to better the condition


[047-148]of the human race.  Only the other day, in Boston, I said that we


[047-149]ought to take into consideration the circumstances under which the


[047-150]Anarchists were reared; that we ought to know that every man is


[047-151]necessarily produced; that man is what he is, not by accident, but


[047-152]necessity; that society raises its own criminals--that it plows


[047-153]the soil and cultivates and harvests the crop.  And it was telegraphed


[047-154]that I had defended anarchy.  Nothing was ever further from my


[047-155]mind.  There is no place, as I said before, for anarchy in the


[047-156]United States.  In Russia it is another question; in Germany another


[047-157]question.  Every country that is governed by the one man, or governed


[047-158]by the few, is the victim of anarchy.  That _is_ anarchy.  That is


[047-159]the worst possible form of socialism.  The definition of socialism


[047-160]given by its bitterest enemy is, that idlers wish to live on the


[047-161]labor and on the money of others.  Is not this definition--a


[047-162]definition given in hatred--a perfect definition of every monarchy


[047-163]and of nearly every government in the world?  That is to say:  The


[047-164]idle few live on the labor and the money of others.


[047-165]_Question_.  Will the Supreme Court take cognizance of this case


[047-166]and prevent the execution of the judgment?


[047-167]_Answer_.  Of course it is impossible for me to say.  At the same


[047-168]time, judging from the action of Justice Miller in the case of _The


[047-169]People vs. Maxwell_, it seems probable that the Supreme Court may


[047-170]interfere, but I have not examined the question sufficiently to


[047-171]form an opinion.  My feeling about the whole matter is this:  That


[047-172]it will not tend to answer the ideas advanced by these men, to hang


[047-173]them.  Their execution will excite sympathy among thousands and


[047-174]thousands of people who have never examined and knew nothing of


[047-175]the theories advanced by the Anarchists, or the Socialists, or


[047-176]other agitators.  In my judgment, supposing the men to be guilty,


[047-177]it is far better to imprison them.  Less harm will be done the


[047-178]cause of free government.  We are not on the edge of any revolution.


[047-179]No other government is as firmly fixed as ours.  No other government


[047-180]has such a broad and splendid foundation.  We have nothing to fear.


[047-181]Courage and safety can afford to be generous--can afford to act


[047-182]without haste and without the feeling of revenge.  So, for my part,


[047-183]I hope that the sentence may be commuted, and that these men, if


[047-184]found guilty at last, may be imprisoned.  This course is, in my


[047-185]judgment, the safest to pursue.  It may be that I am led to this


[047-186]conclusion, because of my belief that every man does as he must.


[047-187]This belief makes me charitable toward all the world.  This belief


[047-188]makes me doubt the wisdom of revenge.  This belief, so far as I am


[047-189]concerned, blots from our language the word "punishment."  Society


[047-190]has a right to protect itself, and it is the duty of society to


[047-191]reform, in so far as it may be possible, any member who has committed


[047-192]what is called a crime.  Where the criminal cannot be reformed,


[047-193]and the safety of society can be secured by his imprisonment, there


[047-194]is no possible excuse for destroying his life.  After these six or


[047-195]seven men have been, in accordance with the forms of law, strangled


[047-196]to death, there will be a few pieces of clay, and about them will


[047-197]gather a few friends, a few admirers--and these pieces will be


[047-198]buried, and over the grave will be erected a monument, and those


[047-199]who were executed as criminals will be regarded by thousands as


[047-200]saints.  It is far better for society to have a little mercy.  The


[047-201]effect upon the community will be good.  If these men are imprisoned,


[047-202]people will examine their teachings without prejudice.  If they


[047-203]are executed, seen through the tears of pity, their virtues, their


[047-204]sufferings, their heroism, will be exaggerated; others may emulate


[047-205]their deeds, and the gulf between the rich and the poor will be


[047-206]widened--a gulf that may not close until it has devoured the noblest


[047-207]and the best.
