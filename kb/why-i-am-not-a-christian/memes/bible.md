# Bible

The Bible, comprising the Old and New Testaments, was a primary target of [[robert-ingersoll|Robert G. Ingersoll]]'s religious criticism. He approached the Bible not as divine revelation but as a human document to be evaluated by the same standards applied to any other book.

## Ingersoll's View of Biblical Authority

Ingersoll made his position on the Bible clear from the outset of his interviews. When asked whether his views of religion were based on the Bible, he answered:

> I regard the Bible, especially the Old Testament, the same as I do most other books — to be judged by its contents. [P01-015]

He had read the Bible attentively and concluded that it was "not an inspired book" [002-105]. His method was straightforward: he applied the same critical standards to the Bible that one would apply to any text claiming divine origin.

## The Problem of Inspiration

Ingersoll challenged the doctrine of inspiration by pointing to morally objectionable passages. He argued that if the Bible were truly inspired by a benevolent [[god|God]], it should not contain commandments to commit atrocities or endorse slavery. He noted:

> I have insisted that the Bible is uninspired by picking out the bad things in the Bible. [010-081]

He was willing to acknowledge the presence of wisdom in the Bible, observing that "there are many good things in the Bible" [010-082]. However, he pointed out that the existence of good passages does not prove divine inspiration — other books contain equal or greater wisdom without claiming supernatural origin. "[[shakespeare|Shakespeare]] has ten times more good things than the Bible, but no one claims" divine inspiration for [[shakespeare|Shakespeare]] [010-086].

## The Devil's Hand in Scripture

Ingersoll deployed a provocative rhetorical strategy when confronting defenders of biblical inerrancy. He asked what part of the Bible would be left if one removed the passages that:

> the Bible except those upholding slavery — it seemed to me that if the Bible was inspired the Devil must have got hold of it. [010-112]

This argument forced believers to either defend morally indefensible passages or admit to a selective reading of scripture — a selectivity that Ingersoll saw as proof that human moral judgment, not divine revelation, was the true arbiter.

## The Need for Revision

Ingersoll predicted that the Bible would need continual revision to remain relevant. He observed that "the Bible must be revised again" because "most of" its content could not withstand modern moral scrutiny [012-127]. He saw the history of biblical interpretation as a gradual retreat from literal readings toward allegorical ones, driven by moral progress rather than theological insight.
