[084-001]_Question_.  Can you offer any explanation of the extraordinary


[084-002]phenomena such as Henry J. Newton has had produced at his own house


[084-003]under his own supervision?


[084-004]_Answer_.  In the first place, I don't believe that anything such


[084-005]as you describe has ever happened.  I do not believe that a medium


[084-006]ever passed into and out of a triple-locked iron cage.  Neither do


[084-007]I believe that any spirits were able to throw shoes and wraps out


[084-008]of the cage; neither do I believe that any apparitions ever rose


[084-009]from the floor, or that anything you relate has ever happened.


[084-010]The best explanation I can give of these wonderful occurrences is


[084-011]the following:  A little boy and girl were standing in a doorway


[084-012]holding hands.  A gentleman passing, stopped for a moment and said


[084-013]to the little girl:  "What relation is the little boy to you?" and


[084-014]she replied, "We had the same father and we had the same mother,


[084-015]but I am not his sister and he is not my brother."  This at first


[084-016]seemed to be quite a puzzle, but it was exceedingly plain when the


[084-017]answer was known:  The little girl lied.


[084-018]_Question_.  Have you had any experience with spirit photography,


[084-019]spirit physicians, or spirit lawyers?


[084-020]_Answer_.  I was shown at one time several pictures said to be the


[084-021]photographs of living persons surrounded by the photographs of


[084-022]spirits.  I examined them very closely, and I found evidence in


[084-023]the photographs themselves that they were spurious.  I took it for


[084-024]granted that light is the same everywhere, and that it obeys the


[084-025]angle of incidence in all worlds and at all times.  In looking at


[084-026]the spirit photographs I found, for instance, that in the photograph


[084-027]of the living person the shadows fell to the right, and that in


[084-028]the photographs of the ghosts, or spirits, supposed to have been


[084-029]surrounding the living person at the time the picture was taken,


[084-030]the shadows did not fall in the same direction, sometimes in the


[084-031]opposite direction, never at the same angle even when the general


[084-032]direction was the same.  This demonstrated that the photographs of


[084-033]the spirits and of the living persons were not taken at the same


[084-034]time.  So much for photographs.


[084-035]I have had no experience with spirit physicians.  I was once told


[084-036]by a lawyer who came to employ me in a will case, that a certain


[084-037]person had made a will giving a large amount of money for the


[084-038]purpose of spreading the gospel of Spiritualism, but that the will


[084-039]had been lost and than an effort was then being made to find it,


[084-040]and they wished me to take certain action pending the search, and


[084-041]wanted my assistance.  I said to him:  "If Spiritualism be true,


[084-042]why not ask the man who made the will what it was and also what


[084-043]has become of it.  If you can find that out from the departed, I


[084-044]will gladly take a retainer in the case; otherwise, I must decline."


[084-045]I have had no other experience with the lawyers.


[084-046]_Question_.  If you were to witness phenomena that seemed inexplicable


[084-047]by natural laws, would you be inclined to favor Spiritualism?


[084-048]_Answer_.  I would not.  If I should witness phenomena that I could


[084-049]not explain, I would leave the phenomena unexplained.  I would not


[084-050]explain them because I did not understand them, and say they were


[084-051]or are produced by spirits.  That is no explanation, and, after


[084-052]admitting that we do not know and that we cannot explain, why should


[084-053]we proceed to explain?  I have seen Mr. Kellar do things for which


[084-054]I cannot account.  Why should I say that he has the assistance of


[084-055]spirits?  All I have a right to say is that I know nothing about


[084-056]how he does them.  So I am compelled to say with regard to many


[084-057]spiritualistic feats, that I am ignorant of the ways and means.


[084-058]At the same time, I do not believe that there is anything supernatural


[084-059]in the universe.


[084-060]_Question_.  What is your opinion of Spiritualism and Spiritualists?


[084-061]_Answer_.  I think the Spiritualism of the present day is certainly


[084-062]in advance of the Spiritualism of several centuries ago.  Persons


[084-063]who now deny Spiritualism and hold it in utter contempt insist that


[084-064]some eighteen or nineteen centuries ago it had possession of the


[084-065]world; that miracles were of daily occurrence; that demons, devils,


[084-066]fiends, took possession of human beings, lived in their bodies,


[084-067]dominated their minds.  They believe, too, that devils took possession


[084-068]of the bodies of animals.  They also insist that a wish could


[084-069]multiply fish.  And, curiously enough, the Spiritualists of our


[084-070]time have but little confidence in the phenomena of eighteen hundred


[084-071]years ago; and, curiously enough, those who believe in the Spiritualism


[084-072]of eighteen hundred years ago deny the Spiritualism of to-day.  I


[084-073]think the Spiritualists of to-day have far more evidence of their


[084-074]phenomena than those who believe in the wonderful things of eighteen


[084-075]centuries ago.  The Spiritualists of to-day have living witnesses,


[084-076]which is something.  I know a great many Spiritualists that are


[084-077]exceedingly good people, and are doing what they can to make the


[084-078]world better.  But I think they are mistaken.


[084-079]_Question_.  Do you believe in spirit entities, whether manifestible


[084-080]or not?


[084-081]_Answer_.  I believe there is such a thing as matter.  I believe


[084-082]there is a something called force.  The difference between force


[084-083]and matter I do not know.  So there is something called consciousness.


[084-084]Whether we call consciousness an entity or not makes no difference


[084-085]as to what it really is.  There is something that hears, sees and


[084-086]feels, a something that takes cognizance of what happens in what


[084-087]we call the outward world.  No matter whether we call this something


[084-088]matter or spirit, it is something that we do not know, to say the


[084-089]least of it, all about.  We cannot understand what matter is.  It


[084-090]defies us, and defies definitions.  So, with what we call spirit,


[084-091]we are in utter ignorance of what it is.  We have some little


[084-092]conception of what we mean by it, and of what others mean, but as


[084-093]to what it really is no one knows.  It makes no difference whether


[084-094]we call ourselves Materialists or Spiritualists, we believe in all


[084-095]there is, no matter what you call it.  If we call it all matter,


[084-096]then we believe that matter can think and hope and dream.  If we


[084-097]call it all spirit, then we believe that spirit has force, that it


[084-098]offers a resistance; in other words, that it is, in one of its


[084-099]aspects, what we call matter.  I cannot believe that everything


[084-100]can be accounted for by motion or by what we call force, because


[084-101]there is something that recognizes force.  There is something that


[084-102]compares, that thinks, that remembers; there is something that


[084-103]suffers and enjoys; there is something that each one calls himself


[084-104]or herself, that is inexplicable to himself or herself, and it


[084-105]makes no difference whether we call this something mind or soul,


[084-106]effect or entity, it still eludes us, and all the words we have


[084-107]coined for the purpose of expressing our knowledge of this something,


[084-108]after all, express only our desire to know, and our efforts to


[084-109]ascertain.  It may be that if we would ask some minister, some one


[084-110]who has studied theology, he would give us a perfect definition.


[084-111]The scientists know nothing about it, and I know of no one who


[084-112]does, unless it be a theologian.


[084-113]--_The Globe-Democrat_, St. Louis, Mo., 1893.

[084-116]_Chatham Street Theater, New York City, N. Y., where Robert G.


[084-117]Ingersoll was baptized in 1836 by his father, the Rev. John Ingersoll,


[084-118]who temporarily preached at the theatre, his church having been


[084-119]destroyed by fire_.


[084-120]_Question_.  What place does the theatre hold among the arts?


[084-121]_Answer_.  Nearly all the arts unite in the theatre, and it is the


[084-122]result of the best, the highest, the most artistic, that man can do.


[084-123]In the first place, there must be the dramatic poet.  Dramatic


[084-124]poetry is the subtlest, profoundest, the most intellectual, the


[084-125]most passionate and artistic of all.  Then the stage must be


[084-126]prepared, and there is work for the architect, the painter and


[084-127]sculptor.  Then the actors appear, and they must be gifted with


[084-128]imagination, with a high order of intelligence; they must have


[084-129]sympathies quick and deep, natures capable of the greatest emotion,


[084-130]dominated by passion.  They must have impressive presence, and all


[084-131]that is manly should meet and unite in the actor; all that is


[084-132]womanly, tender, intense and admirable should be lavishly bestowed


[084-133]on the actress.  In addition to all this, actors should have the


[084-134]art of being natural.


[084-135]Let me explain what I mean by being natural.  When I say that an


[084-136]actor is natural, I mean that he appears to act in accordance with


[084-137]his ideal, in accordance with his nature, and that he is not an


[084-138]imitator or a copyist--that he is not made up of shreds and patches


[084-139]taken from others, but that all he does flows from interior fountains


[084-140]and is consistent with his own nature, all having in a marked degree


[084-141]the highest characteristics of the man.  That is what I mean by


[084-142]being natural.


[084-143]The great actor must be acquainted with the heart, must know the


[084-144]motives, ends, objects and desires that control the thoughts and


[084-145]acts of men.  He must be familiar with many people, including the


[084-146]lowest and the highest, so that he may give to others, clothed with


[084-147]flesh and blood, the characters born of the poet's brain.  The


[084-148]great actor must know the relations that exist between passion and


[084-149]voice, gesture and emphasis, expression and pose.  He must speak


[084-150]not only with his voice, but with his body.  The great actor must


[084-151]be master of many arts.


[084-152]Then comes the musician.  The theatre has always been the home of


[084-153]music, and this music must be appropriate; must, or should, express


[084-154]or supplement what happens on the stage; should furnish rest and


[084-155]balm for minds overwrought with tragic deeds.  To produce a great


[084-156]play, and put it worthily upon the stage, involves most arts, many


[084-157]sciences and nearly all that is artistic, poetic and dramatic in


[084-158]the mind of man.


[084-159]_Question_.  Should the drama teach lessons and discuss social


[084-160]problems, or should it give simply intellectual pleasure and furnish


[084-161]amusement?


[084-162]_Answer_.  Every great play teaches many lessons and touches nearly


[084-163]all social problems.  But the great play does this by indirection.


[084-164]Every beautiful thought is a teacher; every noble line speaks to


[084-165]the brain and heart.  Beauty, proportion, melody suggest moral


[084-166]beauty, proportion in conduct and melody in life.  In a great play


[084-167]the relations of the various characters, their objects, the means


[084-168]adopted for their accomplishment, must suggest, and in a certain


[084-169]sense solve or throw light on many social problems, so that the


[084-170]drama teaches lessons, discusses social problems and gives intellectual


[084-171]pleasure.


[084-172]The stage should not be dogmatic; neither should its object be


[084-173]directly to enforce a moral.  The great thing for the drama to do,


[084-174]and the great thing it has done, and is doing, is to cultivate the


[084-175]imagination.  This is of the utmost importance.  The civilization


[084-176]of man depends upon the development, not only of the intellect,


[084-177]but of the imagination.  Most crimes of violence are committed by


[084-178]people who are destitute of imagination.  People without imagination


[084-179]make most of the cruel and infamous creeds.  They were the persecutors


[084-180]and destroyers of their fellow-men.  By cultivating the imagination,


[084-181]the stage becomes one of the greatest teachers.  It produces the


[084-182]climate in which the better feelings grow; it is the home of the


[084-183]ideal.  All beautiful things tend to the civilization of man.  The


[084-184]great statues plead for proportion in life, the great symphonies


[084-185]suggest the melody of conduct, and the great plays cultivate the


[084-186]heart and brain.
