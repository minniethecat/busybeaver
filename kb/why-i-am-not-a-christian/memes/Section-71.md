[070-001]No one would enjoy a sudden sunset--we want the clouds of gold that


[070-002]float in the azure sea.  No one would enjoy a sudden sunrise--we


[070-003]are in love with the morning star, with the dawn that modestly


[070-004]heralds the day and draws aside, with timid hands, the curtains of


[070-005]the night.  In other words, we want sequence, proportion, logic,


[070-006]beauty.


[070-007]There are several actors in this country who are in perfect accord


[070-008]with nature--who appear to make no effort--whose acting seems to


[070-009]give them joy and rest.  We do well what we do easily.  It is a


[070-010]great mistake to exhaust yourself, instead of the subject.  All


[070-011]great actors "fill the stage" because they hold the situation.


[070-012]You see them and nothing else.


[070-013]_Question_.  Speaking of American actors, Colonel, I believe you


[070-014]are greatly interested in the playing of Miss Marlowe, and have


[070-015]given your opinion of her as Parthenia; what do you think of her


[070-016]Julia and Viola?


[070-017]_Answer_.  A little while ago I saw Miss Marlowe as Julia, in "The


[070-018]Hunchback."  We must remember the limitations of the play.  Nothing


[070-019]can excel the simplicity, the joyous content of the first scene.


[070-020]Nothing could be more natural than the excitement produced by the


[070-021]idea of leaving what you feel to be simple and yet good, for what


[070-022]you think is magnificent, brilliant and intoxicating.  It is only


[070-023]in youth that we are willing to make this exchange.  One does not


[070-024]see so clearly in the morning of life when the sun shines in his


[070-025]eyes.  In the afternoon, when the sun is behind him, he sees better


[070-026]--he is no longer dazzled.  In old age we are not only willing,


[070-027]but anxious, to exchange wealth and fame and glory and magnificence,


[070-028]for simplicity.  All the palaces are nothing compared with our


[070-029]little cabin, and all the flowers of the world are naught to the


[070-030]wild rose that climbs and blossoms by the lowly window of content.


[070-031]Happiness dwells in the valleys with the shadows.


[070-032]The moment Julia is brought in contact with wealth, she longs for


[070-033]the simple--for the true love of one true man.  Wealth and station


[070-034]are mockeries.  These feelings, these emotions, Miss Marlowe rendered


[070-035]not only with look and voice and gesture, but with every pose of


[070-036]her body; and when assured that her nuptials with the Earl could


[070-037]be avoided, the only question in her mind was as to the absolute


[070-038]preservation of her honor--not simply in fact, but in appearance,


[070-039]so that even hatred could not see a speck upon the shining shield


[070-040]of her perfect truth.  In this scene she was perfect--everything


[070-041]was forgotten except the desire to be absolutely true.


[070-042]So in the scene with Master Walter, when he upbraids her for


[070-043]forgetting that she is about to meet her father, when excusing her


[070-044]forgetfulness on the ground that he has been to her a father.


[070-045]Nothing could exceed the delicacy and tenderness of this passage.


[070-046]Every attitude expressed love, gentleness, and a devotion even unto


[070-047]death.  One felt that there could be no love left for the father


[070-048]she expected to meet--Master Walter had it all.


[070-049]A greater Julia was never on the stage--one in whom so much passion


[070-050]mingled with so much purity.  Miss Marlowe never "o'ersteps the


[070-051]modesty of nature."  She maintains proportion.  The river of her


[070-052]art flows even with the banks.


[070-053]In Viola, we must remember the character--a girl just rescued from


[070-054]the sea--disguised as a boy--employed by the Duke, whom she instantly


[070-055]loves--sent as his messenger to woo another for him--Olivia enamored


[070-056]of the messenger--forced to a duel--mistaken for her brother by


[070-057]the Captain, and her brother taken for herself by Olivia--and yet,


[070-058]in the midst of these complications and disguises, she remains a


[070-059]pure and perfect girl--these circumstances having no more real


[070-060]effect upon her passionate and subtle self than clouds on stars.


[070-061]When Malvolio follows and returns the ring the whole truth flashes


[070-062]upon her.  She is in love with Orsino--this she knows.  Olivia,


[070-063]she believes, is in love with her.  The edge of the situation, the


[070-064]dawn of this entanglement, excites her mirth.  In this scene she


[070-065]becomes charming--an impersonation of Spring.  Her laughter is as


[070-066]natural and musical as the song of a brook.  So, in the scene with


[070-067]Olivia in which she cries, "Make me a willow cabin at your gate!"


[070-068]she is the embodiment of grace, and her voice is as musical as the


[070-069]words, and as rich in tone as they are in thought.


[070-070]In the duel with Sir Andrew she shows the difference between the


[070-071]delicacy of woman and the cowardice of man.  She does the little


[070-072]that she can, not for her own sake, but for the sake of her disguise


[070-073]--she feels that she owes something to her clothes.


[070-074]But I have said enough about this actress to give you an idea of


[070-075]one who is destined to stand first in her profession.


[070-076]We will now come back to the real question.  I am in favor of


[070-077]protecting the American actor.  I regard the theatre as the civilizer


[070-078]of man.  All the arts united upon the stage, and the genius of the


[070-079]race has been lavished on this mimic world.


[070-080]--_New York Star_, December 23, 1888.


[070-081]LIBERALS AND LIBERALISM.


[070-082]_Question_.  What do you think of the prospects of Liberalism in


[070-083]this country?


[070-084]_Answer_.  The prospects of Liberalism are precisely the same as


[070-085]the prospects of civilization--that is to say, of progress.  As


[070-086]the people become educated, they become liberal.  Bigotry is the


[070-087]provincialism of the mind.  Men are bigoted who are not acquainted


[070-088]with the thoughts of others.  They have been taught one thing, and


[070-089]have been made to believe that their little mental horizon is the


[070-090]circumference of all knowledge.  The bigot lives in an ignorant


[070-091]village, surrounded by ignorant neighbors.  This is the honest


[070-092]bigot.  The dishonest bigot may know better, but he remains a bigot


[070-093]because his salary depends upon it.  A bigot is like a country that


[070-094]has had no commerce with any other.  He imagines that in his little


[070-095]head there is everything of value.  When a man becomes an intellectual


[070-096]explorer, an intellectual traveler, he begins to widen, to grow


[070-097]liberal.  He finds that the ideas of others are as good as and often


[070-098]better than his own.  The habits and customs of other people throw


[070-099]light on his own, and by this light he is enabled to discover at


[070-100]least some of his own mistakes.  Now the world has become acquainted.


[070-101]A few years ago, a man knew something of the doctrines of his own


[070-102]church.  Now he knows the creeds of others, and not only so, but


[070-103]he has examined to some extent the religions of other nations.  He


[070-104]finds in other creeds all the excellencies that are in his own,


[070-105]and most of the mistakes.  In this way he learns that all creeds


[070-106]have been produced by men, and that their differences have been


[070-107]accounted for by race, climate, heredity--that is to say, by a


[070-108]difference in circumstances.  So we now know that the cause of


[070-109]Liberalism is the cause of civilization.  Unless the race is to be


[070-110]a failure, the cause of Liberalism must succeed.  Consequently, I


[070-111]have the same faith in that cause that I have in the human race.


[070-112]_Question_.  Where are the most Liberals, and in what section of


[070-113]the country is the best work for Liberalism being done?


[070-114]_Answer_.  The most Liberals are in the most intelligent section


[070-115]of the United States.  Where people think the most, there you will


[070-116]find the most Liberals; where people think the least, you will find


[070-117]the most bigots.  Bigotry is produced by feeling--Liberalism by


[070-118]thinking--that is to say, the one is a prejudice, the other a


[070-119]principle.  Every geologist, every astronomer, every scientist, is


[070-120]doing a noble work for Liberalism.  Every man who finds a fact,


[070-121]and demonstrates it, is doing work for the cause.  All the literature


[070-122]of our time that is worth reading is on the liberal side.  All the


[070-123]fiction that really interests the human mind is with us.  No one


[070-124]cares to read the old theological works.  Essays written by professors


[070-125]of theological colleges are regarded, even by Christians, with a


[070-126]kind of charitable contempt.  When any demonstration of science is


[070-127]attacked by a creed, or a passage of Scripture, all the intelligent


[070-128]smile.  For these reasons I think that the best work for Liberalism


[070-129]is being done where the best work for science is being done--where


[070-130]the best work for man is being accomplished.  Every legislator that


[070-131]assists in the repeal of theological laws is doing a great work


[070-132]for Liberalism.


[070-133]_Question_.  In your opinion, what relation do Liberalism and


[070-134]Prohibition bear to each other?


[070-135]_Answer_.  I do not think they have anything to do with each other.


[070-136]They have nothing in common except this:  The Prohibitionists, I


[070-137]presume, are endeavoring to do what they can for temperance; so


[070-138]all intelligent Liberals are doing what they can for the cause of


[070-139]temperance.  The Prohibitionist endeavors to accomplish his object


[070-140]by legislation--the Liberalist by education, by civilization, by


[070-141]example, by persuasion.  The method of the Liberalist is good, that


[070-142]of the Prohibitionist chimerical and fanatical.


[070-143]_Question_.  Do you think that Liberals should undertake a reform


[070-144]in the marriage and divorce laws and relations?


[070-145]_Answer_.  I think that Liberals should do all in their power to


[070-146]induce people to regard marriage and divorce in a sensible light,


[070-147]and without the slightest reference to any theological ideas.  They


[070-148]should use their influence to the end that marriage shall be


[070-149]considered as a contract--the highest and holiest that men and


[070-150]women can make.  And they should also use their influence to have


[070-151]the laws of divorce based on this fundamental idea,--that marriage


[070-152]is a contract.  All should be done that can be done by law to uphold


[070-153]the sacredness of this relation.  All should be done that can be


[070-154]done to impress upon the minds of all men and all women their duty


[070-155]to discharge all the obligations of the marriage contract faithfully


[070-156]and cheerfully.  I do not believe that it is to the interest of


[070-157]the State or of the Nation, that people should be compelled to live


[070-158]together who hate each other, or that a woman should be bound to


[070-159]a man who has been false and who refuses to fulfill the contract


[070-160]of marriage.  I do not believe that any man should call upon the


[070-161]police, or upon the creeds, or upon the church, to compel his wife


[070-162]to remain under his roof, or to compel a woman against her will to


[070-163]become the mother of his children.  In other words, Liberals should


[070-164]endeavor to civilize mankind, and when men and women are civilized,


[070-165]the marriage question, and the divorce question, will be settled.


[070-166]_Question_.  Should Liberals vote on Liberal issues?


[070-167]_Answer_.  I think that, other things being anywhere near equal,


[070-168]Liberals should vote for men who believe in liberty, men who believe


[070-169]in giving to others the rights they claim for themselves--that is


[070-170]to say, for civilized men, for men of some breadth of mind.  Liberals


[070-171]should do what they can to do away with all the theological


[070-172]absurdities.


[070-173]_Question_.  Can, or ought, the Liberals and Spiritualists to unite?


[070-174]_Answer_.  All people should unite where they have objects in


[070-175]common.  They can vote together, and act together, without believing


[070-176]the same on all points.  A Liberal is not necessarily a Spiritualist,


[070-177]and a Spiritualist is not necessarily a Liberal.  If Spiritualists


[070-178]wish to liberalize the Government, certainly Liberals would be glad


[070-179]of their assistance, and if Spiritualists take any step in the


[070-180]direction of freedom, the Liberals should stand by them to that


[070-181]extent.


[070-182]_Question_.  Which is the more dangerous to American institutions


[070-183]--the National Reform Association (God-in-the-Constitution party)


[070-184]or the Roman Catholic Church?


[070-185]_Answer_.  The Association and the Catholic Church are dangerous


[070-186]according to their power.  The Catholic Church has far more power


[070-187]than the Reform Association, and is consequently far more dangerous.


[070-188]The God-in-the-Constitution association is weak, fanatical, stupid,


[070-189]and absurd.  What God are we to have in the Constitution?  Whose


[070-190]God?  If we should agree to-morrow to put God in the Constitution,


[070-191]the question would then be:  Which God?  On that question, the


[070-192]religious world would fall out.  In that direction there is no


[070-193]danger.  But the Roman Catholic Church is the enemy of intellectual


[070-194]liberty.  It is the enemy of investigation.  It is the enemy of


[070-195]free schools.  That church always has been, always will be, the


[070-196]enemy of freedom.  It works in the dark.  When in a minority it is


[070-197]humility itself--when in power it is the impersonation of arrogance.


[070-198]In weakness it crawls--in power it stands erect, and compels its


[070-199]victims to fall upon their faces.  The most dangerous institution


[070-200]in this world, so far as the intellectual liberty of man is concerned,


[070-201]is the Roman Catholic Church.  Next to that is the Protestant


[070-202]Church.
