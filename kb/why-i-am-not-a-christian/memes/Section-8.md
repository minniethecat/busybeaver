[007-001]_Question_.  Do I understand you to imply that there will be a


[007-002]neutral policy, as it were, towards the South?


[007-003]_Answer_.  No, I think that there will be nothing neutral about


[007-004]it.  I think that the next administration will be one-sided--that


[007-005]is, it will be on the right side.  I know of no better definition


[007-006]for a compromise than to say it is a proceeding in which hypocrites


[007-007]deceive each other.  I do not believe that the incoming administration


[007-008]will be neutral in anything.  The American people do not like


[007-009]neutrality.  They would rather a man were on the wrong side than


[007-010]on neither.  And, in my judgment, there is no paper so utterly


[007-011]unfair, malicious and devilish, as one that claims to be neutral.


[007-012]No politician is as bitter as a neutral politician.  Neutrality is


[007-013]generally used as a mask to hide unusual bitterness.  Sometimes it


[007-014]hides what it is--nothing.  It always stands for hollowness of head


[007-015]or bitterness of heart, sometimes for both.  My idea is--and that


[007-016]is the only reason I have the right to express it--that General


[007-017]Garfield believes in the platform adopted by the Republican party.


[007-018]He believes in free speech, in honest money, in divorce of church


[007-019]and state, and he believes in the protection of American citizens


[007-020]by the Federal Government wherever the flag flies.  He believes


[007-021]that the Federal Government is as much bound to protect the citizen


[007-022]at home as abroad.  I believe he will do the very best he can to


[007-023]carry these great ideas into execution and make them living realities


[007-024]in the United States.  Personally, I have no hatred toward the


[007-025]Southern people.  I have no hatred toward any class.  I hate tyranny,


[007-026]no matter whether it is South or North; I hate hypocrisy, and I


[007-027]hate above all things, the spirit of caste.  If the Southern people


[007-028]could only see that they gained as great a victory in the Rebellion


[007-029]as the North did, and some day they will see it, the whole question


[007-030]would be settled.  The South has reaped a far greater benefit from


[007-031]being defeated than the North has from being successful, and I


[007-032]believe some day the South will be great enough to appreciate that


[007-033]fact.  I have always insisted that to be beaten by the right is to


[007-034]be a victor.  The Southern people must get over the idea that they


[007-035]are insulted simply because they are out-voted, and they ought by


[007-036]this time to know that the Republicans of the North, not only do


[007-037]not wish them harm, but really wish them the utmost success.


[007-038]_Question_.  But has the Republican party all the good and the


[007-039]Democratic all the bad?


[007-040]_Answer_.  No, I do not think that the Republican party has all


[007-041]the good, nor do I pretend that the Democratic party has all the


[007-042]bad; though I may say that each party comes pretty near it.  I


[007-043]admit that there are thousands of really good fellows in the


[007-044]Democratic party, and there are some pretty bad people in the


[007-045]Republican party.  But I honestly believe that within the latter


[007-046]are most of the progressive men of this country.  That party has


[007-047]in it the elements of growth.  It is full of hope.  It anticipates.


[007-048]The Democratic party remembers. It is always talking about the


[007-049]past.  It is the possessor of a vast amount of political rubbish,


[007-050]and I really believe it has outlived its usefulness.  I firmly


[007-051]believe that your editor, Mr. Hutchings, could start a better


[007-052]organization, if he would only turn his attention to it.  Just


[007-053]think for a moment of the number you could get rid of by starting


[007-054]a new party.  A hundred names will probably suggest themselves to


[007-055]any intelligent Democrat, the loss of which would almost insure


[007-056]success.  Some one has said that a tailor in Boston made a fortune


[007-057]by advertising that he did not cut the breeches of Webster's statue.


[007-058]A new party by advertising that certain men would not belong to


[007-059]it, would have an advantage in the next race.


[007-060]_Question_.  What, in your opinion, were the causes which led to


[007-061]the Democratic defeat?


[007-062]_Answer_.  I think the nomination of English was exceedingly


[007-063]unfortunate.  Indiana, being an October State, the best man in that


[007-064]State should have been nominated either for President or Vice-


[007-065]President.  Personally, I know nothing of Mr. English, but I have


[007-066]the right to say that he was exceedingly unpopular.  That was


[007-067]mistake number one.  Mistake number two was putting a plank in the


[007-068]platform insisting upon a tariff for revenue only.  That little


[007-069]word "only" was one of the most frightful mistakes ever made by a


[007-070]political party.  That little word "only" was a millstone around


[007-071]the neck of the entire campaign.  The third mistake was Hancock's


[007-072]definition of the tariff.  It was exceedingly unfortunate, exceedingly


[007-073]laughable, and came just in the nick of time.  The fourth mistake


[007-074]was the speech of Wade Hampton, I mean the speech that the Republican


[007-075]papers claim he made.  Of course I do not know, personally, whether


[007-076]it was made or not.  If made, it was a great mistake.  Mistake


[007-077]number five was made in Alabama, where they refused to allow a


[007-078]Greenbacker to express his opinion.  That lost the Democrats enough


[007-079]Greenbackers to turn the scale in Maine, and enough in Indiana to


[007-080]change that election.  Mistake number six was in the charges made


[007-081]against General Garfield.  They were insisted upon, magnified and


[007-082]multiplied until at last the whole thing assumed the proportions


[007-083]of a malicious libel.  This was a great mistake, for the reason


[007-084]that a number of Democrats in the United States had most heartily


[007-085]and cordially indorsed General Garfield as a man of integrity and


[007-086]great ability.  Such indorsements had been made by the leading


[007-087]Democrats of the North and South, among them Governor Hendricks


[007-088]and many others I might name.  Jere Black had also certified to


[007-089]the integrity and intellectual grandeur of General Garfield, and


[007-090]when afterward he certified to the exact contrary, the people


[007-091]believed that it was a persecution.  The next mistake, number seven,


[007-092]was the Chinese letter.  While it lost Garfield California, Nevada,


[007-093]and probably New Jersey, it did him good in New York.  This letter


[007-094]was the greatest mistake made, because a crime is greater than a


[007-095]mistake.  These, in my judgment, are the principal mistakes made


[007-096]by the Democratic party in the campaign.  Had McDonald been on the


[007-097]ticket the result might have been different, or had the party united


[007-098]on some man in New York, satisfactory to the factions, it might


[007-099]have succeeded.  The truth, however, is that the North to-day is


[007-100]Republican, and it may be that had the Democratic party made no


[007-101]mistakes whatever the result would have been the same.  But that


[007-102]mistakes were made is now perfectly evident to the blindest partisan.


[007-103]If the ticket originally suggested, Seymour and McDonald, had been


[007-104]nominated on an unobjectionable platform, the result might have


[007-105]been different. One of the happiest days in my life was the day on


[007-106]which the Cincinnati convention did not nominate Seymour and did


[007-107]nominate English.  I regard General Hancock as a good soldier, but


[007-108]not particularly qualified to act as President.  He has neither


[007-109]the intellectual training nor the experience to qualify him for


[007-110]that place.


[007-111]_Question_.  You have doubtless heard of a new party, Colonel.


[007-112]What is your idea in regard to it?


[007-113]_Answer_.  I have heard two or three speak of a new party to be


[007-114]called the National party, or National Union party, but whether


[007-115]there is anything in such a movement I have no means of knowing.


[007-116]Any party in opposition to the Republican, no matter what it may


[007-117]be called, must win on a new issue, and that new issue will determine


[007-118]the new party.  Parties cannot be made to order.  They must grow.


[007-119]They are the natural offspring of national events.  They must embody


[007-120]certain hopes, they must gratify, or promise to gratify, the feelings


[007-121]of a vast number of people.  No man can make a party, and if a new


[007-122]party springs into existence it will not be brought forth to gratify


[007-123]the wishes of a few, but the wants of the many.  It has seemed to


[007-124]me for years that the Democratic party carried too great a load in


[007-125]the shape of record; that its autobiography was nearly killing it


[007-126]all the time, and that if it could die just long enough to assume


[007-127]another form at the resurrection, just long enough to leave a grave


[007-128]stone to mark the end of its history, to get a cemetery back of


[007-129]it, that it might hope for something like success.  In other words,


[007-130]that there must be a funeral before there can be victory.  Most of


[007-131]its leaders are worn out.  They have become so accustomed to defeat


[007-132]that they take it as a matter of course; they expect it in the


[007-133]beginning and seem unconsciously to work for it.  There must be


[007-134]some new ideas, and this only can happen when the party as such


[007-135]has been gathered to its fathers.  I do not think that the advice


[007-136]of Senator Hill will be followed.  He is willing to kill the


[007-137]Democratic party in the South if we will kill the Republican party


[007-138]in the North.  This puts me in mind of what the rooster said to


[007-139]the horse:  "Let us agree not to step on each other's feet."


[007-140]_Question_.  Your views of the country's future and prospects must


[007-141]naturally be rose colored?


[007-142]_Answer_.  Of course, I look at things through Republican eyes and


[007-143]may be prejudiced without knowing it.  But it really seems to me


[007-144]that the future is full of great promise.  The South, after all,


[007-145]is growing more prosperous.  It is producing more and more every


[007-146]year, until in time it will become wealthy.  The West is growing


[007-147]almost beyond the imagination of a speculator, and the Eastern and


[007-148]Middle States are much more than holding their own.  We have now


[007-149]fifty millions of people and in a few years will have a hundred.


[007-150]That we are a Nation I think is now settled.  Our growth will be


[007-151]unparalleled.  I myself expect to live to see as many ships on the


[007-152]Pacific as on the Atlantic.  In a few years there will probably be


[007-153]ten millions of people living along the Rocky and Sierra Mountains.


[007-154]It will not be long until Illinois will find her market west of


[007-155]her.  In fifty years this will be the greatest nation on the earth,


[007-156]and the most populous in the civilized world.  China is slowly


[007-157]awakening from the lethargy of centuries.  It will soon have the


[007-158]wants of Europe, and America will supply those wants.  This is a


[007-159]nation of inventors and there is more mechanical ingenuity in the


[007-160]United States than on the rest of the globe.  In my judgment this


[007-161]country will in a short time add to its customers hundreds of


[007-162]millions of the people of the Celestial Empire.  So you see, to


[007-163]me, the future is exceedingly bright.  And besides all this, I must


[007-164]not forget the thing that is always nearest my heart.  There is


[007-165]more intellectual liberty in the United States to-day than ever


[007-166]before.  The people are beginning to see that every citizen ought


[007-167]to have the right to express himself freely upon every possible


[007-168]subject.  In a little while, all the barbarous laws that now disgrace


[007-169]the statute books of the States by discriminating against a man


[007-170]simply because he is honest, will be repealed, and there will be


[007-171]one country where all citizens will have and enjoy not only equal


[007-172]rights, but all rights.  Nothing gratifies me so much as the growth


[007-173]of intellectual liberty.  After all, the true civilization is where


[007-174]every man gives to every other, every right that he claims for


[007-175]himself.


[007-176]--_The Post_, Washington, D. C., November 14, 1880.
