[087-001]So I would like to see the Sandwich Islands annexed to the United


[087-002]States.  They are a good way from San Francisco and our Western


[087-003]shore, but they are nearer to us than they are to any other nation.


[087-004]I think they would be of great importance.  They would tend to


[087-005]increase the Asiatic trade, and they certainly would be important


[087-006]in case of war.  We should have fortifications on those islands


[087-007]that no naval power could take.


[087-008]Some objection has been made on the ground that under our system


[087-009]the people of those islands would have to be represented in Congress.


[087-010]I say yes, represented by a delegate until the islands become a


[087-011]real part of the country, and by that time, there would be several


[087-012]hundred thousand Americans living there, capable of sending over


[087-013]respectable members of Congress.


[087-014]Now, I think that Mr. Cleveland has made a very great mistake.


[087-015]First, I think he was mistaken as to the facts in the Sandwich


[087-016]Islands; second, as to the Constitution of the United States, and


[087-017]thirdly, as to the powers of the President of the United States.


[087-018]_Question_.  In your experience as a lawyer what was the most unique


[087-019]case in which you were ever engaged?


[087-020]_Answer_.  The Star Route trial.  Every paper in the country, but


[087-021]one, was against the defence, and that one was a little sheet owned


[087-022]by one of the defendants.  I received a note from a man living in


[087-023]a little town in Ohio criticizing me for defending the accused.


[087-024]In reply I wrote that I supposed he was a sensible man and that


[087-025]he, of course, knew what he was talking about when he said the


[087-026]accused were guilty; that the Government needed just such men as


[087-027]he, and that he should come to the trial at once and testify.  The


[087-028]man wrote back:  "Dear Colonel:  I am a ---- fool."


[087-029]_Question_.  Will the church and the stage ever work together for


[087-030]the betterment of the world, and what is the province of each?


[087-031]_Answer_.  The church and stage will never work together.  The


[087-032]pulpit pretends that fiction is fact.  The stage pretends that


[087-033]fiction is fact.  The pulpit pretence is dishonest--that of the


[087-034]stage is sincere.  The actor is true to art, and honestly pretends


[087-035]to be what he is not.  The actor is natural, if he is great, and


[087-036]in this naturalness is his truth and his sincerity.  The pulpit is


[087-037]unnatural, and for that reason untrue.  The pulpit is for another


[087-038]world, the stage for this.  The stage is good because it is natural,


[087-039]because it portrays real and actual life; because "it holds the


[087-040]mirror up to nature."  The pulpit is weak because it too often


[087-041]belittles and demeans this life; because it slanders and calumniates


[087-042]the natural and is the enemy of joy.


[087-043]--_The Inter-Ocean_, Chicago, February 2, 1894.


[087-044]ORATORS AND ORATORY.*


[087-045][* It was at his own law office in New York City that I had


[087-046]my talk with that very notable American, Col. Robert G.


[087-047]Ingersoll.  "Bob" Ingersoll, Americans call him


[087-048]affectionately; in a company of friends it is "The Colonel."


[087-049]A more interesting personality it would be hard to find, and


[087-050]those who know even a little of him will tell you that a


[087-051]bigger-hearted man probably does not live.  Suppose a well-


[087-052]knit frame, grown stouter than it once was, and a fine,


[087-053]strong face, with a vivid gleam in the eyes, a deep,


[087-054]uncommonly musical voice, clear cut, decisive, and a manner


[087-055]entirely delightful, yet tinged with a certain reserve.


[087-056]Introduce a smoking cigar, the smoke rising in little curls


[087-057]and billows, then imagine a rugged sort of picturesqueness


[087-058]in dress, and you get, not by any means the man, but, still,


[087-059]some notion of "Bob" Ingersoll.


[087-060]Colonel Ingersoll stands at the front of American orators.


[087-061]The natural thing, therefore, was that I should ask him--a


[087-062]master in the art--about oratory.  What he said I shall give


[087-063]in his own words precisely as I took them down from his


[087-064]lips, for in the case of such a good commander of the old


[087-065]English tongue that is of some importance.  But the


[087-066]wonderful limpidness, the charming pellucidness of Ingersoll


[087-067]can only be adequately understood when you also have the


[087-068]finishing touch of his facile voice.]


[087-069]_Question_.  I should be glad if you would tell me what you think


[087-070]the differences are between English and American oratory?


[087-071]_Answer_.  There is no difference between the real English and the


[087-072]real American orator.  Oratory is the same the world over.  The


[087-073]man who thinks on his feet, who has the pose of passion, the face


[087-074]that thought illumines, a voice in harmony with the ideals expressed,


[087-075]who has logic like a column and poetry like a vine, who transfigures


[087-076]the common, dresses the ideals of the people in purple and fine


[087-077]linen, who has the art of finding the best and noblest in his


[087-078]hearers, and who in a thousand ways creates the climate in which


[087-079]the best grows and flourishes and bursts into blossom--that man is


[087-080]an orator, no matter of what time, of what country.


[087-081]_Question_.  If you were to compare individual English and American


[087-082]orators--recent or living orators in particular--what would you say?


[087-083]_Answer_.  I have never heard any of the great English speakers,


[087-084]and consequently can pass no judgment as to their merits, except


[087-085]such as depends on reading.  I think, however, the finest paragraph


[087-086]ever uttered in Great Britain was by Curran in his defence of Rowan.


[087-087]I have never read one of Mr. Gladstone's speeches, only fragments.


[087-088]I think he lacks logic.  Bright was a great speaker, but he lacked


[087-089]imagination and the creative faculty.  Disraeli spoke for the clubs,


[087-090]and his speeches were artificial.  We have had several fine speakers


[087-091]in America.  I think that Thomas Corwin stands at the top of the


[087-092]natural orators.  Sergeant S. Prentiss, the lawyer, was a very


[087-093]great talker; Henry Ward Beecher was the greatest orator that the


[087-094]pulpit has produced.  Theodore Parker was a great orator.  In this


[087-095]country, however, probably Daniel Webster occupies the highest


[087-096]place in general esteem.


[087-097]_Question_.  Which would you say are the better orators, speaking


[087-098]generally, the American people or the English people?


[087-099]_Answer_.  I think Americans are, on the average, better talkers


[087-100]than the English.  I think England has produced the greatest


[087-101]literature of the world; but I do not think England has produced


[087-102]the greatest orators of the world.  I know of no English orator


[087-103]equal to Webster or Corwin or Beecher.


[087-104]_Question_.  Would you mind telling me how it was you came to be


[087-105]a public speaker, a lecturer, an orator?


[087-106]_Answer_.  We call this America of ours free, and yet I found it


[087-107]was very far from free.  Our writers and our speakers declared that


[087-108]here in America church and state were divorced.  I found this to


[087-109]be untrue.  I found that the church was supported by the state in


[087-110]many ways, that people who failed to believe certain portions of


[087-111]the creeds were not allowed to testify in courts or to hold office.


[087-112]It occurred to me that some one ought to do something toward making


[087-113]this country intellectually free, and after a while I thought that


[087-114]I might as well endeavor to do this as wait for another.  This is


[087-115]the way in which I came to make speeches; it was an action in favor


[087-116]of liberty.  I have said things because I wanted to say them, and


[087-117]because I thought they ought to be said.


[087-118]_Question_.  Perhaps you will tell me your methods as a speaker,


[087-119]for I'm sure it would be interesting to know them?


[087-120]_Answer_.  Sometimes, and frequently, I deliver a lecture several


[087-121]times before it is written.  I have it taken by a shorthand writer,


[087-122]and afterward written out.  At other times I have dictated a lecture,


[087-123]and delivered it from manuscript.  The course pursued depends on


[087-124]how I happen to feel at the time.  Sometimes I read a lecture, and


[087-125]sometimes I deliver lectures without any notes--this, again,


[087-126]depending much on how I happen to feel.  So far as methods are


[087-127]concerned, everything should depend on feeling.  Attitude, gestures,


[087-128]voice, emphasis, should all be in accord with and spring from


[087-129]feeling, from the inside.


[087-130]_Question_.  Is there any possibility of your coming to England,


[087-131]and, I need hardly add, of your coming to speak?


[087-132]_Answer_.  I have thought of going over to England, and I may do


[087-133]so.  There is an England in England for which I have the highest


[087-134]possible admiration, the England of culture, of art, of principle.


[087-135]--_The Sketch_, London, Eng., March 21, 1894.


[087-136]CATHOLICISM AND PROTESTANTISM.  THE POPE, THE A. P. A., AGNOSTICISM


[087-137]AND THE CHURCH.


[087-138]_Question_.  Which do you regard as the better, Catholicism or


[087-139]Protestantism?


[087-140]_Answer_.  Protestantism is better than Catholicism because there


[087-141]is less of it.  Protestantism does not teach that a monk is better


[087-142]than a husband and father, that a nun is holier than a mother.


[087-143]Protestants do not believe in the confessional.  Neither do they


[087-144]pretend that priests can forgive sins.  Protestantism has fewer


[087-145]ceremonies and less opera bouffe, clothes, caps, tiaras, mitres,


[087-146]crooks and holy toys.  Catholics have an infallible man--an old


[087-147]Italian.  Protestants have an infallible book, written by Hebrews


[087-148]before they were civilized.  The infallible man is generally wrong,


[087-149]and the infallible book is filled with mistakes and contradictions.


[087-150]Catholics and Protestants are both enemies of intellectual freedom


[087-151]--of real education, but both are opposed to education enough to


[087-152]make free men and women.


[087-153]Between the Catholics and Protestants there has been about as much


[087-154]difference as there is between crocodiles and alligators.  Both


[087-155]have done the worst they could, both are as bad as they can be,


[087-156]and the world is getting tired of both.  The world is not going to


[087-157]choose either--both are to be rejected.


[087-158]_Question_.  Are you willing to give your opinion of the Pope?


[087-159]_Answer_.  It may be that the Pope thinks he is infallible, but I


[087-160]doubt it.  He may think that he is the agent of God, but I guess


[087-161]not.  He may know more than other people, but if he does he has


[087-162]kept it to himself.  He does not seem satisfied with standing in


[087-163]the place and stead of God in spiritual matters, but desires temporal


[087-164]power.  He wishes to be Pope and King.  He imagines that he has


[087-165]the right to control the belief of all the world; that he is the


[087-166]shepherd of all "sheep" and that the fleeces belong to him.  He


[087-167]thinks that in his keeping is the conscience of mankind.  So he


[087-168]imagines that his blessing is a great benefit to the faithful and


[087-169]that his prayers can change the course of natural events.  He is


[087-170]a strange mixture of the serious and comical.  He claims to represent


[087-171]God, and admits that he is almost a prisoner.  There is something


[087-172]pathetic in the condition of this pontiff.  When I think of him,


[087-173]I think of Lear on the heath, old, broken, touched with insanity,


[087-174]and yet, in his own opinion, "every inch a king."


[087-175]The Pope is a fragment, a remnant, a shred, a patch of ancient


[087-176]power and glory.  He is a survival of the unfittest, a souvenir of


[087-177]theocracy, a relic of the supernatural.  Of course he will have a


[087-178]few successors, and they will become more and more comical, more


[087-179]and more helpless and impotent as the world grows wise and free.


[087-180]I am not blaming the Pope.  He was poisoned at the breast of his


[087-181]mother.  Superstition was mingled with her milk.  He was poisoned


[087-182]at school--taught to distrust his reason and to live by faith.


[087-183]And so it may be that his mind was so twisted and tortured out of


[087-184]shape that he now really believes that he is the infallible agent


[087-185]of an infinite God.
