[069-001]_Question_.  Is there any remedy?


[069-002]_Answer_.  I have always wondered that the great corporations have


[069-003]made no provisions for their old and worn out employees.  It seems


[069-004]to me that not only great railway companies, but great manufacturing


[069-005]corporations, ought to provide for their workmen.  Many of them


[069-006]are worn out, unable longer to work, and they are thrown aside like


[069-007]old clothes.  They find their way to the poorhouses or die in


[069-008]tenements by the roadside.  This seems almost infinitely heartless.


[069-009]Men of great wealth, engaged in manufacturing, instead of giving


[069-010]five hundred thousand dollars for a library, or a million dollars


[069-011]for a college, ought to put this money aside, invest it in bonds


[069-012]of the Government, and the interest ought to be used in taking care


[069-013]of the old, of the helpless, of those who meet with accidents in


[069-014]their work.  Under our laws, if an employee is caught in a wheel


[069-015]or in a band, and his arm or leg is torn off, he is left to the


[069-016]charity of the community, whereas the profits of the business ought


[069-017]to support him in his old age.  If employees had this feeling--that


[069-018]they were not simply working for that day, not simply working while


[069-019]they have health and strength, but laying aside a little sunshine


[069-020]for the winter of age--if they only felt that they, by their labor,


[069-021]were creating a fireside in front of which their age and helplessness


[069-022]could sit, the feeling between employed and employers would be a


[069-023]thousand times better.  On the great railways very few people know


[069-024]the number of the injured, of those who lose their hands or feet,


[069-025]of those who contract diseases riding on the tops of freight trains


[069-026]in snow and sleet and storm; and yet, when these men become old


[069-027]and helpless through accident, they are left to shift for themselves.


[069-028]The company is immortal, but the employees become helpless.  Now,


[069-029]it seems to me that a certain per cent. should be laid aside, so


[069-030]that every brakeman and conductor could feel that he was providing


[069-031]for himself, as well as for his fellow-workmen, so that when the


[069-032]dark days came there would be a little light.


[069-033]The men of wealth, the men who control these great corporations--


[069-034]these great mills--give millions away in ostentatious charity.


[069-035]They send missionaries to foreign lands.  They endow schools and


[069-036]universities and allow the men who earned the surplus to die in


[069-037]want.  I believe in no charity that is founded on robbery.  I have


[069-038]no admiration for generous highwaymen or extravagant pirates.  At


[069-039]the foundation of charity should be justice.  Let these men whom


[069-040]others have made wealthy give something to their workmen--something


[069-041]to those who created their fortunes.  This would be one step in


[069-042]the right direction.  Do not let it be regarded as charity--let it


[069-043]be regarded as justice.


[069-044]--_New York World_, December 2, 1888.


[069-045]PROTECTION FOR AMERICAN ACTORS.


[069-046]_Question_.  It is reported that you have been retained as counsel


[069-047]for the Actors' Order of Friendship--the Edwin Forrest Lodge of


[069-048]New York, and the Shakespeare Lodge of Philadelphia--for the purpose


[069-049]of securing the necessary legislation to protect American actors--


[069-050]is that so?


[069-051]_Answer_.  Yes, I have been retained for that purpose, and the


[069-052]object is simply that American actors may be put upon an equal


[069-053]footing with Americans engaged in other employments.  There is a


[069-054]law now which prevents contractors going abroad and employing


[069-055]mechanics or skilled workmen, and bringing them to this country to


[069-056]take the places of our citizens.


[069-057]No one objects to the English, German and French mechanics coming


[069-058]with their wives and children to this country and making their


[069-059]homes here.  Our ports are open, and have been since the foundation


[069-060]of this Government.  Wages are somewhat higher in this country than


[069-061]in any other, and the man who really settles here, who becomes, or


[069-062]intends to become an American citizen, will demand American wages.


[069-063]But if a manufacturer goes to Europe, he can make a contract there


[069-064]and bring hundreds and thousands of mechanics to this country who


[069-065]will work for less wages than the American, and a law was passed


[069-066]to prevent the American manufacturer, who was protected by a tariff,


[069-067]from burning the laborer's candle at both ends.  That is to say,


[069-068]we do not wish to give him the American price, by means of a tariff,


[069-069]and then allow him to go to Europe and import his labor at the


[069-070]European price.


[069-071]In the law, actors were excepted, and we now find the managers are


[069-072]bringing entire companies from the old county, making contracts


[069-073]with them there, and getting them at much lower prices than they


[069-074]would have had to pay for American actors.


[069-075]No one objects to a foreign actor coming here for employment, but


[069-076]we do not want an American manager to go there, and employ him to


[069-077]act here.  No one objects to the importation of a star.  We wish


[069-078]to see and hear the best actors in the world.  But the rest of the


[069-079]company--the support--should be engaged in the United States, if


[069-080]the star speaks English.


[069-081]I see that it is contended over in England, that English actors


[069-082]are monopolizing the American stage because they speak English,


[069-083]while the average American actor does not.  The real reason is that


[069-084]the English actor works for less money--he is the cheaper article.


[069-085]Certainly no one will accuse the average English actor of speaking


[069-086]English.  The hemming and hawing, the aristocratic stutter, the


[069-087]dropping of h's and picking them up at the wrong time, have never


[069-088]been popular in the United States, except by way of caricature.


[069-089]Nothing is more absurd than to take the ground that the English


[069-090]actors are superior to the American.  I know of no English actor


[069-091]who can for a moment be compared with Joseph Jefferson, or with


[069-092]Edwin Booth, or with Lawrence Barrett, or with Denman Thompson,


[069-093]and I could easily name others.


[069-094]If English actors are so much better than American, how is it that


[069-095]an American star is supported by the English?  Mary Anderson is


[069-096]certainly an American actress, and she is supported by English


[069-097]actors.  Is it possible that the superior support the inferior?


[069-098]I do not believe that England has her equal as an actress.  Her


[069-099]Hermione is wonderful, and the appeal to Apollo sublime.  In Perdita


[069-100]she "takes the winds of March with beauty."  Where is an actress


[069-101]on the English stage the superior of Julia Marlowe in genius, in


[069-102]originality, in naturalness?


[069-103]Is there any better Mrs. Malaprop than Mrs. Drew, and better Sir


[069-104]Anthony than John Gilbert?  No one denies that the English actors


[069-105]and actresses are great.  No one will deny that the plays of


[069-106]Shakespeare are the greatest that have been produced, and no one


[069-107]wishes in any way to belittle the genius of the English people.


[069-108]In this country the average person speaks fairly good English, and


[069-109]you will find substantially the same English spoken in most of the


[069-110]country; whereas in England there is a different dialect in almost


[069-111]every county, and most of the English people speak the language as


[069-112]if was not their native tongue.  I think it will be admitted that


[069-113]the English write a good deal better than they speak, and that


[069-114]their pronunciation is not altogether perfect.


[069-115]These things, however, are not worth speaking of.  There is no


[069-116]absolute standard.  They speak in the way that is natural to them,


[069-117]and we in the way that is natural to us.  This difference furnishes


[069-118]no foundation for a claim of general superiority.  The English


[069-119]actors are not brought here on account of their excellence, but on


[069-120]account of their cheapness.  It requires no great ability to play


[069-121]the minor parts, or the leading roles in some plays, for that


[069-122]matter.  And yet acting is a business, a profession, a means of


[069-123]getting bread.


[069-124]We protect our mechanics and makers of locomotives and of all other


[069-125]articles.  Why should we not protect, by the same means, the actor?


[069-126]You may say that we can get along without actors.  So we can get


[069-127]along without painters, without sculptors and without poets.  But


[069-128]a nation that gets along without these people of genius amounts to


[069-129]but little.  We can do without music, without players and without


[069-130]composers; but when we take art and poetry and music and the theatre


[069-131]out of the world, it becomes an exceedingly dull place.


[069-132]Actors are protected and cared for in proportion that people are


[069-133]civilized.  If the people are intelligent, educated, and have


[069-134]imaginations, they enjoy the world of the stage, the creations of


[069-135]poets, and they are thrilled by great music, and, as a consequence,


[069-136]respect the dramatist, the actor and the musician.


[069-137]_Question_.  It is claimed that an amendment to the law, such as


[069-138]is desired, will interfere with the growth of art?


[069-139]_Answer_.  No one is endeavoring to keep stars from this country.


[069-140]If they have American support, and the stars really know anything,


[069-141]the American actors will get the benefit.  If they bring their


[069-142]support with them, the American actor is not particularly benefitted,


[069-143]and the star, when the season is over, takes his art and his money


[069-144]with him.


[069-145]Managers who insist on employing foreign support are not sacrificing


[069-146]anything for art.  Their object is to make money.  They care nothing


[069-147]for the American actor--nothing for the American drama.  They look


[069-148]for the receipts.  It is the sheerest cant to pretend that they


[069-149]are endeavoring to protect art.


[069-150]On the 26th of February, 1885, a law was passed making it unlawful


[069-151]"for any person, company, partnership or corporation, in any manner


[069-152]whatsoever, to prepay the transportation, or in any way assist or


[069-153]encourage the importation or emigration of any alien or aliens into


[069-154]the United States, under contract or agreement, parol or special,


[069-155]previous to the importation or emigration of such aliens to perform


[069-156]labor or services of any kind the United States."


[069-157]By this act it was provided that its provisions should not apply


[069-158]to professional actors, artists, lecturers or singers, in regard


[069-159]to persons employed strictly as personal or domestic servants.


[069-160]The object now in view is so to amend the law that its provision


[069-161]shall apply to all actors except stars.


[069-162]_Question_.  In this connection there has been so much said about


[069-163]the art of acting--what is your idea as to that art?


[069-164]_Answer_.  Above all things in acting, there must be proportion.


[069-165]There are no miracles in art or nature.  All that is done--every


[069-166]inflection and gesture--must be in perfect harmony with the


[069-167]circumstances.  Sensationalism is based on deformity, and bears


[069-168]the same relation to proportion that caricature does to likeness.


[069-169]The stream that flows even with its banks, making the meadows green,


[069-170]delights us ever; the one that overflows surprises for a moment.


[069-171]But we do not want a succession of floods.


[069-172]In acting there must be natural growth, not sudden climax.  The


[069-173]atmosphere of the situation, the relation sustained to others,


[069-174]should produce the emotions.  Nothing should be strained.  Beneath


[069-175]domes there should be buildings, and buildings should have foundations.


[069-176]There must be growth.  There should be the bud, the leaf, the


[069-177]flower, in natural sequence.  There must be no leap from naked


[069-178]branches to the perfect fruit.


[069-179]Most actors depend on climax--they save themselves for the supreme


[069-180]explosion.  The scene opens with a slow match and ends when the


[069-181]spark reaches the dynamite.  So, most authors fill the first act


[069-182]with contradictions and the last with explanations.  Plots and


[069-183]counter-plots, violence and vehemence, perfect saints and perfect


[069-184]villains--that is to say, monsters, impelled by improbable motives,


[069-185]meet upon the stage, where they are pushed and pulled for the sake


[069-186]of the situation, and where everything is so managed that the fire


[069-187]reaches the powder and the explosion is the climax.


[069-188]There is neither time, nor climate, nor soil, in which the emotions


[069-189]and intentions may grow.  No land is plowed, no seed is sowed, no


[069-190]rain falls, no light glows--the events are all orphans.
