[006-001]--_Chicago Tribune_, September 30, 1880.


[006-002]THE REPUBLICAN VICTORY.


[006-003]_Question_.  Do you really think, Colonel, that the country has


[006-004]just passed through a crisis?


[006-005]_Answer_.  Yes; there was a crisis and a great one.  The question


[006-006]was whether a Northern or Southern idea of the powers and duties


[006-007]of the Federal Government was to prevail.  The great victory of


[006-008]yesterday means that the Rebellion was not put down on the field


[006-009]of war alone, but that we have conquered in the realm of thought.


[006-010]The bayonet has been justified by argument.  No party can ever


[006-011]succeed in this country that even whispers "State Sovereignty."


[006-012]That doctrine has become odious.  The sovereignty of the State


[006-013]means a Government without power, and citizens without protection.


[006-014]_Question_.  Can you see any further significance in the present


[006-015]Republican victory other than that the people do not wish to change


[006-016]the general policy of the present administration?


[006-017]_Answer_.  Yes; the people have concluded that the lips of America


[006-018]shall be free.  There never was free speech at the South, and there


[006-019]never will be until the people of that section admit that the Nation


[006-020]is superior to the State, and that all citizens have equal rights.


[006-021]I know of hundreds who voted the Republican ticket because they


[006-022]regarded the South as hostile to free speech.  The people were


[006-023]satisfied with the financial policy of the Republicans, and they


[006-024]feared a change.  The North wants honest money--gold and silver.


[006-025]The people are in favor of honest votes, and they feared the


[006-026]practices of the Democratic party.  The tissue ballot and shotgun


[006-027]policy made them hesitate to put power in the hands of the South.


[006-028]Besides, the tariff question made thousands and thousands of votes.


[006-029]As long as Europe has slave labor, and wherever kings and priests


[006-030]rule, the laborer will be substantially a slave.  We must protect


[006-031]ourselves.  If the world were free, trade would be free, and the


[006-032]seas would be the free highways of the world.  The great objects


[006-033]of the Republican party are to preserve all the liberty we have,


[006-034]protect American labor, and to make it the undisputed duty of the


[006-035]Government to protect every citizen at home and abroad.


[006-036]_Question_.  What do you think was the main cause of the Republican


[006-037]sweep?


[006-038]_Answer_.  The wisdom of the Republicans and the mistakes of the


[006-039]Democrats.  The Democratic party has for twenty years underrated


[006-040]the intelligence, the patriotism and the honesty of the American


[006-041]people.  That party has always looked upon politics as a trade,


[006-042]and success as the last act of a cunning trick.  It has had no


[006-043]principles, fixed or otherwise.  It has always been willing to


[006-044]abandon everything but its prejudices.  It generally commences


[006-045]where it left off and then goes backward.  In this campaign English


[006-046]was a mistake, Hancock was another.  Nothing could have been more


[006-047]incongruous than yoking a Federal soldier with a peace-at-any-price


[006-048]Democrat.  Neither could praise the other without slandering himself,


[006-049]and the blindest partisan could not like them both.  But, after


[006-050]all, I regard the military record of English as fully equal to the


[006-051]views of General Hancock on the tariff.  The greatest mistake that


[006-052]the Democratic party made was to suppose that a campaign could be


[006-053]fought and won by slander.  The American people like fair play and


[006-054]they abhor ignorant and absurd vituperation.  The continent knew


[006-055]that General Garfield was an honest man; that he was in the grandest


[006-056]sense a gentleman; that he was patriotic, profound and learned;


[006-057]that his private life was pure; that his home life was good and


[006-058]kind and true, and all the charges made and howled and screeched


[006-059]and printed and sworn to harmed only those who did the making and


[006-060]the howling, the screeching and the swearing.  I never knew a man


[006-061]in whose perfect integrity I had more perfect confidence, and in


[006-062]less than one year even the men who have slandered him will agree


[006-063]with me.


[006-064]_Question_.  How about that "personal and confidential letter"?


[006-065](The Morey letter.)


[006-066]_Answer_.  It was as stupid, as devilish, as basely born as


[006-067]godfathered.  It is an exploded forgery, and the explosion leaves


[006-068]dead and torn upon the field the author and his witnesses.


[006-069]_Question_.  Is there anything in the charge that the Republican


[006-070]party seeks to change our form of government by gradual centralization?


[006-071]_Answer_.  Nothing whatever.  We want power enough in the Government


[006-072]to protect, not to destroy, the liberties of the people.  The


[006-073]history of the world shows that burglars have always opposed an


[006-074]increase of the police.


[006-075]--_New York Herald_, November 5, 1880.


[006-076]INGERSOLL AND BEECHER.*


[006-077][* The sensation created by the speech of the Rev. Henry


[006-078]Ward Beecher at the Academy of Music, in Brooklyn, when he


[006-079]uttered a brilliant eulogy of Col. Robert Ingersoll and


[006-080]publicly shook hands with him has not yet subsided.  A


[006-081]portion of the religious world is thoroughly stirred up at


[006-082]what it considers a gross breach of orthodox propriety.


[006-083]This feeling is especially strong among the class of


[006-084]positivists who believe that


[006-085]"An Atheist's laugh's a poor exchange For Deity offended."


[006-086]Many believe that Mr. Beecher is at heart in full sympathy


[006-087]and accord with Ingersoll's teachings, but has not courage


[006-088]enough to say so at the sacrifice of his pastoral position.


[006-089]The fact that these two men are the very head and front of


[006-090]their respective schools of thought makes the matter an


[006-091]important one.  The denouncement of the doctrine of eternal


[006-092]punishment, followed by the scene at the Academy, has about


[006-093]it an aroma of suggestiveness that might work much harm


[006-094]without an explanation.  Since Colonel Ingersoll's recent


[006-095]attack upon the _personnel_ of the clergy through the


[006-096]"Shorter Catechism" the pulpit has been remarkably silent


[006-097]regarding the great atheist.  "Is the keen logic and broad


[006-098]humanity of Ingersoll converting the brain and heart of


[006-099]Christendom?" was recently asked. Did the hand that was


[006-100]stretched out to him on the stage of the Academy reach


[006-101]across the chasm which separates orthodoxy from infidelity?


[006-102]Desiring to answer the last question if possible, a _Herald_


[006-103]reporter visited Mr. Beecher and Colonel Ingersoll to learn


[006-104]their opinion of each other.  Neither of the gentlemen was


[006-105]aware that the other was being interviewed.]


[006-106]_Question_.  What is your opinion of Mr. Beecher?


[006-107]_Answer_.  I regard him as the greatest man in any pulpit of the


[006-108]world.  He treated me with a generosity that nothing can exceed.


[006-109]He rose grandly above the prejudices supposed to belong to his


[006-110]class, and acted as only a man could act without a chain upon his


[006-111]brain and only kindness in his heart.


[006-112]I told him that night that I congratulated the world that it had


[006-113]a minister with an intellectual horizon broad enough and a mental


[006-114]sky studded with stars of genius enough to hold all creeds in scorn


[006-115]that shocked the heart of man.  I think that Mr. Beecher has


[006-116]liberalized the English-speaking people of the world.


[006-117]I do not think he agrees with me.  He holds to many things that I


[006-118]most passionately deny.  But in common, we believe in the liberty


[006-119]of thought.


[006-120]My principal objections to orthodox religion are two--slavery here


[006-121]and hell hereafter.  I do not believe that Mr. Beecher on these


[006-122]points can disagree with me.  The real difference between us is--


[006-123]he says God, I say Nature.  The real agreement between us is--we


[006-124]both say--Liberty.


[006-125]_Question_.  What is his forte?


[006-126]_Answer_.  He is of a wonderfully poetic temperament.  In pursuing


[006-127]any course of thought his mind is like a stream flowing through


[006-128]the scenery of fairyland.  The stream murmurs and laughs while the


[006-129]banks grow green and the vines blossom.


[006-130]His brain is controlled by his heart.  He thinks in pictures.  With


[006-131]him logic means mental melody.  The discordant is the absurd.


[006-132]For years he has endeavored to hide the dungeon of orthodoxy with


[006-133]the ivy of imagination.  Now and then he pulls for a moment the


[006-134]leafy curtain aside and is horrified to see the lizards, snakes,


[006-135]basilisks and abnormal monsters of the orthodox age, and then he


[006-136]utters a great cry, the protest of a loving, throbbing heart.


[006-137]He is a great thinker, a marvelous orator, and, in my judgment,


[006-138]greater and grander than any creed of any church.


[006-139]Besides all this, he treated me like a king.  Manhood is his forte,


[006-140]and I expect to live and die his friend.


[006-141]BEECHER ON INGERSOLL.


[006-142]_Question_.  What is your opinion of Colonel Ingersoll?


[006-143]_Answer_.  I do not think there should be any misconception as to


[006-144]my motive for indorsing Mr. Ingersoll.  I never saw him before that


[006-145]night, when I clasped his hand in the presence of an assemblage of


[006-146]citizens.  Yet I regard him as one of the greatest men of this age.


[006-147]_Question_.  Is his influence upon the world good or otherwise?


[006-148]_Answer_.  I am an ordained clergyman and believe in revealed


[006-149]religion.  I am, therefore, bound to regard all persons who do not


[006-150]believe in revealed religion as in error.  But on the broad platform


[006-151]of human liberty and progress I was bound to give him the right


[006-152]hand of fellowship.  I would do it a thousand times over.  I do


[006-153]not know Colonel Ingersoll's religious views precisely, but I have


[006-154]a general knowledge of them.  He has the same right to free thought


[006-155]and free speech that I have.  I am not that kind of a coward who


[006-156]has to kick a man before he shakes hands with him.  If I did so I


[006-157]would have to kick the Methodists, Roman Catholics and all other


[006-158]creeds.  I will not pitch into any man's religion as an excuse for


[006-159]giving him my hand.  I admire Ingersoll because he is not afraid


[006-160]to speak what he honestly thinks, and I am only sorry that he does


[006-161]not think as I do.  I never heard so much brilliancy and pith put


[006-162]into a two hour speech as I did on that night.  I wish my whole


[006-163]congregation had been there to hear it.  I regret that there are


[006-164]not more men like Ingersoll interested in the affairs of the nation.


[006-165]I do not wish to be understood as indorsing skepticism in any form.


[006-166]--_New York Herald_, November 7, 1880.


[006-167]POLITICAL.


[006-168]_Question_.  Is it true, as rumored, that you intend to leave


[006-169]Washington and reside in New York?


[006-170]_Answer_.  No, I expect to remain here for years to come, so far


[006-171]as I can now see.  My present intention is certainly to stay here


[006-172]during the coming winter.


[006-173]_Question_.  Is this because you regard Washington as the pleasantest


[006-174]and most advantageous city for a residence?


[006-175]_Answer_.  Well, in the first place, I dislike to move.  In the


[006-176]next place, the climate is good.  In the third place, the political


[006-177]atmosphere has been growing better of late, and when you consider


[006-178]that I avoid one dislike and reap the benefits of two likes, you


[006-179]can see why I remain.


[006-180]_Question_.  Do you think that the moral atmosphere will improve


[006-181]with the political atmosphere?


[006-182]_Answer_.  I would hate to say that this city is capable of any


[006-183]improvement in the way of morality.  We have a great many churches,


[006-184]a great many ministers, and, I believe, some retired chaplains, so


[006-185]I take it that the moral tone of the place could hardly be bettered.


[006-186]One majority in the Senate might help it.  Seriously, however, I


[006-187]think that Washington has as high a standard of morality as any


[006-188]city in the Union.  And it is one of the best towns in which to


[006-189]loan money without collateral in the world.


[006-190]_Question_.  Do you know this from experience?


[006-191]_Answer_.  This I have been told [was the solemn answer.]


[006-192]_Question_.  Do you think that the political features of the incoming


[006-193]administration will differ from the present?


[006-194]_Answer_.  Of course, I have no right to speak for General Garfield.


[006-195]I believe his administration will be Republican, at the same time


[006-196]perfectly kind, manly, and generous.  He is a man to harbor no


[006-197]resentment.  He knows that it is the duty of statesmanship to remove


[006-198]causes of irritation rather then punish the irritated.
