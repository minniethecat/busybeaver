[012-001]"Many of these farmers are rough task-masters, and if a boy fails


[012-002]to perform the work of an adult, he is almost certain to be cruelly


[012-003]treated, half starved, and in the coldest weather wretchedly clad.


[012-004]If he does the work, his life is not likely to be much happier,


[012-005]for as a rule he will receive more kicks than candy.  The result


[012-006]in either case is almost certain to be wrecked constitutions,


[012-007]dwarfed bodies, rounded shoulders, and limbs crippled or rendered


[012-008]useless by frost or rheumatism.  The principal diet of these boys


[012-009]is corn pone.  A few days ago, Constable W. H. Johnston went to


[012-010]the house of Reuben Taylor, and on entering the sitting room his


[012-011]attention was attracted by the moans of its only occupant, a little


[012-012]colored boy, who was lying on the hearth in front of the fireplace.


[012-013]The boy's head was covered with ashes from the fire, and he did


[012-014]not pay the slightest attention to the visitor, until Johnston


[012-015]asked what made him cry.  Then the little fellow sat up and drawing


[012-016]on old rag off his foot said, 'Look there.'  The sight that met


[012-017]Johnston's eye was horrible beyond description.  The poor boy's


[012-018]feet were so horribly frozen that the flesh had dropped off the


[012-019]toes until the bones protruded.  The flesh on the sides, bottoms,


[012-020]and tops of his feet was swollen until the skin cracked in many


[012-021]places, and the inflamed flesh was sloughing off in great flakes.


[012-022]The frost-bitten flesh extended to his knees, the joints of which


[012-023]were terribly inflamed.  The right one had already begun suppurating.


[012-024]This poor little black boy, covered with nothing but a cotton shirt,


[012-025]drilling pants, a pair of nearly worn out brogans and a battered


[012-026]old hat, on the morning of December 30th, the coldest day of the


[012-027]season, when the mercury was seventeen degrees below zero, in the


[012-028]face of a driving snow storm, was sent half a mile from home to


[012-029]protect his master's unshucked corn from the depredations of


[012-030]marauding cows and crows.  He remained standing around in the snow


[012-031]until four o'clock, then he drove the cows home, received a piece


[012-032]of cold corn pone, and was sent out in the snow again to chop stove


[012-033]wood till dark.  Having no bed, he slept that night in front of


[012-034]the fireplace, with his frozen feet buried in the ashes.  Dr. C.


[012-035]H. Richards found it necessary to cut off the boy's feet as far


[012-036]back as the ankle and the instep."


[012-037]This was but one case in several.  Personally, I have no doubt that


[012-038]Mr. Reuben Taylor entirely agrees with Chief Justice Comegys on


[012-039]the great question of blasphemy, and probably nothing would so


[012-040]gratify Mr. Reuben Taylor as to see some man in a Delaware jail


[012-041]for the crime of having expressed an honest thought.  No wonder


[012-042]that in the State of Delaware the Christ of intellectual liberty


[012-043]has been crucified between the pillory and the whipping-post.  Of


[012-044]course I know that there are thousands of most excellent people in


[012-045]that State--people who believe in intellectual liberty, and who


[012-046]only need a little help--and I am doing what I can in that direction


[012-047]--to repeal the laws that now disgrace the statute book of that


[012-048]little commonwealth.  I have seen many people from that State lately


[012-049]who really wish that Colonel Hazelitt had never died.


[012-050]_Question_.  What has the press generally said with regard to the


[012-051]action of Judge Comegys?  Do they, so far as you know, justify his


[012-052]charge?


[012-053]_Answer_.  A great many papers having articles upon the subject


[012-054]have been sent to me.  A few of the religious papers seem to think


[012-055]that the Judge did the best he knew, and there is one secular paper


[012-056]called the _Evening News_, published at Chester, Pa., that thinks


[012-057]"that the rebuke from so high a source of authority will have a


[012-058]most excellent effect, and will check religious blasphemers from


[012-059]parading their immoral creeds before the people."  The editor of


[012-060]this paper should at once emigrate to the State of Delaware, where


[012-061]he properly belongs.  He is either a native of Delaware, or most


[012-062]of his subscribers are citizens of that country; or, it may be that


[012-063]he is a lineal descendant of some Hessian, who deserted during the


[012-064]Revolutionary war.  Most of the newspapers in the United States


[012-065]are advocates of mental freedom.  Probably nothing on earth has


[012-066]been so potent for good as an untrammeled, fearless press.  Among


[012-067]the papers of importance there is not a solitary exception.  No


[012-068]leading journal in the United States can be found upon the side of


[012-069]intellectual slavery.  Of course, a few rural sheets edited by


[012-070]gentlemen, as Mr. Greeley would say, "whom God in his inscrutable


[012-071]wisdom had allowed to exist," may be found upon the other side,


[012-072]and may be small enough, weak enough and mean enough to pander to


[012-073]the lowest and basest prejudices of their most ignorant subscribers.


[012-074]These editors disgrace their profession and exert about the same


[012-075]influence upon the heads as upon the pockets of their subscribers


[012-076]--that is to say, they get little and give less.


[012-077]_Question_.  Do you not think after all, the people who are in


[012-078]favor of having you arrested for blasphemy, are acting in accordance


[012-079]with the real spirit of the Old and New Testaments?


[012-080]_Answer_.  Of course, they act in exact accordance with many of


[012-081]the commands in the Old Testament, and in accordance with several


[012-082]passages in the New.  At the same time, it may be said that they


[012-083]violate passages in both.  If the Old Testament is true, and if it


[012-084]is the inspired word of God, of course, an Infidel ought not be


[012-085]allowed to live; and if the New Testament is true, an unbeliever


[012-086]should not be permitted to speak.  There are many passages, though,


[012-087]in the New Testament, that should protect even an Infidel.  Among


[012-088]them is this:  "Do unto others as ye would that others should do


[012-089]unto you."  But that is a passage that has probably had as little


[012-090]effect upon the church as any other in the Bible.  So far as I am


[012-091]concerned, I am willing to adopt that passage, and I am willing to


[012-092]extend to every other human being every right that I claim for


[012-093]myself.  If the churches would act upon this principle, if they


[012-094]would say--every soul, every mind, may think and investigate for


[012-095]itself; and around all, and over all, shall be thrown the sacred


[012-096]shield of liberty, I should be on their side.


[012-097]_Question_.  How do you stand with the clergymen, and what is their


[012-098]opinion of you and of your views?


[012-099]_Answer_.  Most of them envy me; envy my independence; envy my


[012-100]success; think that I ought to starve; that the people should not


[012-101]hear me; say that I do what I do for money, for popularity; that I


[012-102]am actuated by hatred of all that is good and tender and holy in


[012-103]human nature; think that I wish to tear down the churches, destroy


[012-104]all morality and goodness, and usher in the reign of crime and


[012-105]chaos.  They know that shepherds are unnecessary in the absence of


[012-106]wolves, and it is to their interest to convince their sheep that


[012-107]they, the sheep, need protection.  This they are willing to give


[012-108]them for half the wool.  No doubt, most of these minsters are


[012-109]honest, and are doing what they consider their duty.  Be this as


[012-110]it may, they feel the power slipping from their hands.  They know


[012-111]that the idea is slowly growing that they are not absolutely


[012-112]necessary for the protection of society.  They know that the


[012-113]intellectual world cares little for what they say, and that the


[012-114]great tide of human progress flows on careless of their help or


[012-115]hindrance.  So long as they insist upon the inspiration of the


[012-116]Bible, they are compelled to take the ground that slavery was once


[012-117]a divine institution; they are forced to defend cruelties that


[012-118]would shock the heart of a savage, and besides, they are bound to


[012-119]teach the eternal horror of everlasting punishment.


[012-120]They poison the minds of children; they deform the brain and pollute


[012-121]the imagination by teaching the frightful and infamous dogma of


[012-122]endless misery.  Even the laws of Delaware shock the enlightened


[012-123]public of to-day.  In that State they simply fine and imprison a


[012-124]man for expressing his honest thoughts; and yet, if the churches


[012-125]are right, God will damn a man forever for the same offence.  The


[012-126]brain and heart of our time cannot be satisfied with the ancient


[012-127]creeds.  The Bible must be revised again.  Most of the creeds must


[012-128]be blotted out.  Humanity must take the place of theology.


[012-129]Intellectual liberty must stand in every pulpit.  There must be


[012-130]freedom in all the pews, and every human soul must have the right


[012-131]to express its honest thought.


[012-132]--Washington correspondent, _Brooklyn Eagle_, March 19, 1881.


[012-133]A REPLY TO THE REV. MR. LANSING.*


[012-134][* Rev. Isaac J. Lansing of Meriden, Conn., recently


[012-135]denounced Col. Robert G. Ingersoll from the pulpit of the


[012-136]Meriden Methodist Church, and had the Opera House closed


[012-137]against him.  This led a _Union_ reporter to show Colonel


[012-138]Ingersoll what Mr. Lansing had said and to interrogate him


[012-139]with the following result.]


[012-140]_Question_.  Did you favor the sending of obscene matter through


[012-141]the mails as alleged by the Rev. Mr. Lansing?


[012-142]_Answer_.  Of course not, and no honest man ever thought that I


[012-143]did.  This charge is too malicious and silly to be answered.  Mr.


[012-144]Lansing knows better.  He has made this charge many times and he


[012-145]will make it again.


[012-146]_Question_.  Is it a fact that there are thousands of clergymen in


[012-147]the country whom you would fear to meet in fair debate?


[012-148]_Answer_.  No; the fact is I would like to meet them all in one.


[012-149]The pulpit is not burdened with genius.  There a few great men


[012-150]engaged in preaching, but they are not orthodox.  I cannot conceive


[012-151]that a Freethinker has anything to fear from the pulpit, except


[012-152]misrepresentation.  Of course, there are thousands of ministers


[012-153]too small to discuss with--ministers who stand for nothing in the


[012-154]church--and with such clergymen I cannot afford to discuss anything.


[012-155]If the Presbyterians, or the Congregationalists, or the Methodists


[012-156]would select some man, and endorse him as their champion, I would


[012-157]like to meet him in debate.  Such a man I will pay to discuss with


[012-158]me.  I will give him most excellent wages, and pay all the expenses


[012-159]at the discussion besides.  There is but one safe course for the


[012-160]ministers--they must assert.  They must declare.  They must swear


[012-161]to it and stick to it, but they must not try to reason.


[012-162]_Question_.  You have never seen Rev. Mr. Lansing.  To the people


[012-163]of Meriden and thereabouts he is well-known.  Judging from what


[012-164]has been told you of his utterances and actions, what kind of a


[012-165]man would you take him to be?


[012-166]_Answer_.  I would take him to be a Christian.  He talks like one,


[012-167]and he acts like one.  If Christianity is right, Lansing is right.


[012-168]If salvation depends upon belief, and if unbelievers are to be


[012-169]eternally damned, then an Infidel has no right to speak.  He should


[012-170]not be allowed to murder the souls of his fellow-men.  Lansing does


[012-171]the best he knows how.  He thinks that God hates an unbeliever,


[012-172]and he tries to act like God.  Lansing knows that he must have the


[012-173]right to slander a man whom God is to eternally damn.


[012-174]_Question_.  Mr. Lansing speaks of you as a wolf coming with fangs


[012-175]sharpened by three hundred dollars a night to tear the lambs of


[012-176]his flock.  What do you say to that?


[012-177]_Answer_.  All I have to say is, that I often get three times that


[012-178]amount, and sometimes much more.  I guess his lambs can take care


[012-179]of themselves.  I am not very fond of mutton anyway.  Such talk


[012-180]Mr. Lansing ought to be ashamed of.  The idea that he is a shepherd


[012-181]--that he is on guard--is simply preposterous.  He has few sheep


[012-182]in his congregation that know as little on the wolf question as


[012-183]he does.  He ought to know that his sheep support him--his sheep


[012-184]protect him; and without the sheep poor Lansing would be devoured


[012-185]by the wolves himself.
