[085-001]_Answer_.  The modern French drama, so far as I am acquainted with


[085-002]it, is a disease.  It deals with the abnormal.  It is fashioned


[085-003]after Balzac.  It exhibits moral tumors, mental cancers and all


[085-004]kinds of abnormal fungi,--excrescences.  Everything is stood on


[085-005]its head; virtue lives in the brothel; the good are the really bad


[085-006]and the worst are, after all, the best.  It portrays the exceptional,


[085-007]and mistakes the scum-covered bayou for the great river.  The French


[085-008]dramatists seem to think that the ceremony of marriage sows the


[085-009]seed of vice.  They are always conveying the idea that the virtuous


[085-010]are uninteresting, rather stupid, without sense and spirit enough


[085-011]to take advantage of their privilege.  Between the greatest French


[085-012]plays and the greatest English plays of course there is no comparison.


[085-013]If a Frenchman had written the plays of Shakespeare, Desdemona


[085-014]would have been guilty, Isabella would have ransomed her brother


[085-015]at the Duke's price, Juliet would have married the County Paris,


[085-016]run away from him, and joined Romeo in Mantua, and Miranda would


[085-017]have listened coquettishly to the words of Caliban.  The French


[085-018]are exceedingly artistic.  They understand stage effects, love the


[085-019]climax, delight in surprises, especially in the improbable; but


[085-020]their dramatists lack sympathy and breadth of treatment.  They are


[085-021]provincial.  With them France is the world.  They know little of


[085-022]other countries.  Their plays do not touch the universal.


[085-023]_Question_.  What are your feelings in reference to idealism on


[085-024]the stage?


[085-025]_Answer_.  The stage ought to be the home of the ideal; in a word,


[085-026]the imagination should have full sway.  The great dramatist is a


[085-027]creator; he is the sovereign, and governs his own world.  The


[085-028]realist is only a copyist.  He does not need genius.  All he wants


[085-029]is industry and the trick of imitation.  On the stage, the real


[085-030]should be idealized, the ordinary should be transfigured; that is,


[085-031]the deeper meaning of things should be given.  As we make music of


[085-032]common air, and statues of stone, so the great dramatist should


[085-033]make life burst into blossom on the stage.  A lot of words, facts,


[085-034]odds and ends divided into acts and scenes do not make a play.


[085-035]These things are like old pieces of broken iron that need the heat


[085-036]of the furnace so that they may be moulded into shape.  Genius is


[085-037]that furnace, and in its heat and glow and flame these pieces,


[085-038]these fragments, become molten and are cast into noble and heroic


[085-039]forms.  Realism degrades and impoverishes the stage.


[085-040]_Question_.  What attributes should an actor have to be really


[085-041]great?


[085-042]_Answer_.  Intelligence, imagination, presence; a mobile and


[085-043]impressive face; a body that lends itself to every mood in appropriate


[085-044]pose, one that is oak or willow, at will; self-possession; absolute


[085-045]ease; a voice capable of giving every shade of meaning and feeling,


[085-046]an intuitive knowledge or perception of proportion, and above all,


[085-047]the actor should be so sincere that he loses himself in the character


[085-048]he portrays.  Such an actor will grow intellectually and morally.


[085-049]The great actor should strive to satisfy himself--to reach his own


[085-050]ideal.


[085-051]_Question_.  Do you enjoy Shakespeare more in the library than


[085-052]Shakespeare interpreted by actors now on the boards?


[085-053]_Answer_.  I enjoy Shakespeare everywhere.  I think it would give


[085-054]me pleasure to hear those wonderful lines spoken even by phonographs.


[085-055]But Shakespeare is greatest and best when grandly put upon the


[085-056]stage.  There you know the connection, the relation, the circumstances,


[085-057]and these bring out the appropriateness and the perfect meaning of


[085-058]the text.  Nobody in this country now thinks of Hamlet without


[085-059]thinking of Booth.  For this generation at least, Booth is Hamlet.


[085-060]It is impossible for me to read the words of Sir Toby without seeing


[085-061]the face of W. F. Owen.  Brutus is Davenport, Cassius is Lawrence


[085-062]Barrett, and Lear will be associated always in my mind with Edwin


[085-063]Forrest.  Lady Macbeth is to me Adelaide Ristori, the greatest


[085-064]actress I ever saw.  If I understood music perfectly, I would much


[085-065]rather hear Seidl's orchestra play "Tristan," or hear Remenyi's


[085-066]matchless rendition of Schubert's "Ave Maria," than to read the


[085-067]notes.


[085-068]Most people love the theatre.  Everything about it from stage to


[085-069]gallery attracts and fascinates.  The mysterious realm, behind the


[085-070]scenes, from which emerge kings and clowns, villains and fools,


[085-071]heroes and lovers, and in which they disappear, is still a fairyland.


[085-072]As long as man is man he will enjoy the love and laughter, the


[085-073]tears and rapture of the mimic world.


[085-074]_Question_.  Is it because we lack men of genius or because our


[085-075]life is too material that no truly great American plays have been


[085-076]written?


[085-077]_Answer_.  No great play has been written since Shakespeare; that


[085-078]is, no play has been written equal to his.  But there is the same


[085-079]reason for that in all other countries, including England, that


[085-080]there is in this country, and that reason is that Shakespeare has


[085-081]had no equal.


[085-082]America has not failed because life in the Republic is too material.


[085-083]Germany and France, and, in fact, all other nations, have failed


[085-084]in the same way.  In the sense in which I am speaking, Germany has


[085-085]produced no great play.


[085-086]In the dramatic world Shakespeare stands alone.  Compared with him,


[085-087]even the classic is childish.


[085-088]There is plenty of material for plays.  The Republic has lived a


[085-089]great play--a great poem--a most marvelous drama.  Here, on our


[085-090]soil, have happened some of the greatest events in the history of


[085-091]the world.


[085-092]All human passions have been and are in full play here, and here


[085-093]as elsewhere, can be found the tragic, the comic, the beautiful,


[085-094]the poetic, the tears, the smiles, the lamentations and the laughter


[085-095]that are the necessary warp and woof with which to weave the living


[085-096]tapestries that we call plays.


[085-097]We are beginning.  We have found that American plays must be American


[085-098]in spirit.  We are tired of imitations and adaptations.  We want


[085-099]plays worthy of the great Republic.  Some good work has recently


[085-100]been done, giving great hope for the future.  Of course the realistic


[085-101]comes first; afterward the ideal.  But here in America, as in all


[085-102]other lands, love is the eternal passion that will forever hold


[085-103]the stage.  Around that everything else will move.  It is the sun.


[085-104]All other passions are secondary.  Their orbits are determined by


[085-105]the central force from which they receive their light and meaning.


[085-106]Love, however, must be kept pure.


[085-107]The great dramatist is, of necessity, a believer in virtue, in


[085-108]honesty, in courage and in the nobility of human nature.  He must


[085-109]know that there are men and women that even a God could not corrupt;


[085-110]such knowledge, such feeling, is the foundation, and the only


[085-111]foundation, that can support the splendid structure, the many


[085-112]pillared stories and the swelling dome of the great drama.


[085-113]--_The New York Dramatic Mirror_, December 26, 1891.


[085-114]WOMAN.


[085-115]It takes a hundred men to make an encampment, but one woman can


[085-116]make a home.  I not only admire woman as the most beautiful object


[085-117]ever created, but I reverence her as the redeeming glory of humanity,


[085-118]the sanctuary of all the virtues, the pledge of all perfect qualities


[085-119]of heart and head.  It is not just or right to lay the sins of men


[085-120]at the feet of women.  It is because women are so much better than


[085-121]men that their faults are considered greater.


[085-122]The one thing in this world that is constant, the one peak that


[085-123]rises above all clouds, the one window in which the light forever


[085-124]burns, the one star that darkness cannot quench, is woman's love.


[085-125]It rises to the greatest heights, it sinks to the lowest depths,


[085-126]it forgives the most cruel injuries.  It is perennial of life, and


[085-127]grows in every climate.  Neither coldness nor neglect, harshness


[085-128]nor cruelty, can extinguish it.  A woman's love is the perfume of


[085-129]the heart.


[085-130]This is the real love that subdues the earth; the love that has


[085-131]wrought all the miracles of art, that gives us music all the way


[085-132]from the cradle song to the grand closing symphony that bears the


[085-133]soul away on wings of fire.  A love that is greater than power,


[085-134]sweeter than life and stronger than death.


[085-135]STRIKES, EXPANSION AND OTHER SUBJECTS.


[085-136]_Question_.  What have you to say in regard to the decision of


[085-137]Judge Billings in New Orleans, that strikes which interfere with


[085-138]interstate commerce, are illegal?


[085-139]_Answer_.  As a rule, men have a right to quit work at any time


[085-140]unless there is some provision to the contrary in their contracts.


[085-141]They have not the right to prevent other men from taking their


[085-142]places.  Of course I do not mean by this that strikers may not use


[085-143]persuasion and argument to prevent other men from filling their


[085-144]places.  All blacklisting and refusing to work with other men is


[085-145]illegal and punishable.  Of course men may conspire to quit work,


[085-146]but how is it to be proved?  One man can quit, or five hundred men


[085-147]can quit together, and nothing can prevent them.  The decisions of


[085-148]Judge Ricks and Judge Billings are an acknowledgment, at least, of


[085-149]the principle of public control or regulation of railroads and of


[085-150]commerce generally.  The railroads, which run for private profit,


[085-151]are public carriers, and the public has a vested interest in them


[085-152]as such.  The same principle applies to the commerce of the country


[085-153]and can be dealt with by the courts in the same way.  It is unlikely,


[085-154]however, that Judge Billings' decision will have any lasting effect


[085-155]upon organized labor.  Law cannot be enforced against such vast


[085-156]numbers of people, especially when they have the general sympathy.


[085-157]Nearly all strikes have been illegal, but the numbers involved have


[085-158]made the courts powerless.


[085-159]_Question_.  Are you in favor of the annexation of Canada?


[085-160]_Answer_.  Yes, if Canada is.  We do not want that country unless


[085-161]that country wants us.  I do not believe it to the interests of


[085-162]Canada to remain a province.  Canada should either be an independent


[085-163]nation, or a part of a nation.  Now Canada is only a province--with


[085-164]no career--with nothing to stimulate either patriotism or great


[085-165]effort.  Yes, I hope that Canada will be annexed.


[085-166]By all means annex the Sandwich Islands, too.  I believe in


[085-167]territorial expansion.  A prosperous farmer wants the land next him,


[085-168]and a prosperous nation ought to grow.  I believe that we ought to


[085-169]hold the key to the Pacific and its commerce.  We want to be prepared


[085-170]at all points to defend our interests from the greed and power of


[085-171]England.


[085-172]We are going to have a navy, and we want that navy to be of use in


[085-173]protecting our interests the world over.  And we want interests to


[085-174]protect.


[085-175]It is a splendid feeling--this feeling of growth.  By the annexation


[085-176]of these islands we open new avenues to American adventure, and


[085-177]the tendency is to make our country greater and stronger.  The West


[085-178]Indian Islands ought to be ours, and some day our flag will float


[085-179]there.  This country must not stop growing.


[085-180]_Question_.  Is the spirit of patriotism declining in America?


[085-181]_Answer_.  There has been no decline in the spirit of American


[085-182]patriotism; in fact, it has increased rather then otherwise as the


[085-183]nation has grown older, stronger, more prosperous, more glorious.


[085-184]If there were occasion to demonstrate the truth of this statement


[085-185]it would be quickly demonstrated.  Let an attack be made upon the


[085-186]American flag, and you will very quickly find out how genuine is


[085-187]the patriotic spirit of Americans.


[085-188]I do not think either that there has been a decline in the celebration


[085-189]of the Fourth of July.  The day is probably not celebrated with as


[085-190]much burning of gunpowder and shooting of fire crackers in the


[085-191]large cities as formerly, but it is celebrated with as much enthusiasm


[085-192]as ever all through the West, and the feeling of rejoicing over


[085-193]the anniversary of the day is as great and strong as ever.  The


[085-194]people are tired of celebrating with a great noise and I am glad


[085-195]of it.
