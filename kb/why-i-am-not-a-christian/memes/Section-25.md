[024-001]Everything was done to demoralize, frighten and overawe judges,


[024-002]witnesses and jurors.  I do not pretend to say who was responsible,


[024-003]possibly I am not an impartial judge.  I was deeply interested at


[024-004]the time, and felt all of these things, rather than reasoned about


[024-005]them.


[024-006]Possibly I cannot give a perfectly unbiased opinion.  Personally,


[024-007]I have no feeling now upon the subject.


[024-008]The Department of Justice, in spite of its methods, did not succeed.


[024-009]That was enough for me.  I think, however, when the country knows


[024-010]the facts, that the people will not approve of what was done.  I


[024-011]do not believe in trying cases in the newspapers before they are


[024-012]submitted to jurors.  That is a little too early.  Neither do I


[024-013]believe in trying them in the newspapers after the verdicts have


[024-014]been rendered.  That is a little too late.


[024-015]_Question_.  What are Mr. Blaine's chances for the presidency?


[024-016]_Answer_.  My understanding is that Mr. Blaine is not a candidate


[024-017]for the nomination; that he does not wish his name to be used in


[024-018]that connection.  He ought to have been nominated in 1876, and if


[024-019]he were a candidate, he would probably have the largest following;


[024-020]but my understanding is, that he does not, in any event, wish to


[024-021]be a candidate.  He is a man perfectly familiar with the politics


[024-022]of this country, knows its history by heart, and is in every respect


[024-023]probably as well qualified to act as its Chief Magistrate as any


[024-024]man in the nation.  He is a man of ideas, of action, and has positive


[024-025]qualities.  He would not wait for something to turn up, and things


[024-026]would not have to wait long for him to turn them up.


[024-027]_Question_.  Who do you think will be nominated at Chicago?


[024-028]_Answer_.  Of course I have not the slightest idea who will be


[024-029]nominated.  I may have an opinion as to who ought to be nominated,


[024-030]and yet I may be greatly mistaken in that opinion.  There are


[024-031]hundreds of men in the Republican party, any one of whom, if elected,


[024-032]would make a good, substantial President, and there are many


[024-033]thousands of men about whom I know nothing, any one of whom would


[024-034]in all probability make a good President.  We do not want any man


[024-035]to govern this country.  This country governs itself.  We want a


[024-036]President who will honestly and faithfully execute the laws, who


[024-037]will appoint postmasters and do the requisite amount of handshaking


[024-038]on public occasions, and we have thousands of men who can discharge


[024-039]the duties of that position.  Washington is probably the worst


[024-040]place to find out anything definite upon the subject of presidential


[024-041]booms.  I have thought for a long time that one of the most valuable


[024-042]men in the country was General Sherman.  Everybody knows who and


[024-043]what he is.  He has one great advantage--he is a frank and outspoken


[024-044]man.  He has opinions and he never hesitates about letting them be


[024-045]known.  There is considerable talk about Judge Harlan.  His dissenting


[024-046]opinion in the Civil Rights case has made every colored man his


[024-047]friend, and I think it will take considerable public patronage to


[024-048]prevent a good many delegates from the Southern States voting for


[024-049]him.


[024-050]_Question_.  What are your present views on theology?


[024-051]_Answer_.  Well, I think my views have not undergone any change


[024-052]that I know of.  I still insist that observation, reason and


[024-053]experience are the things to be depended upon in this world.  I


[024-054]still deny the existence of the supernatural.  I still insist that


[024-055]nobody can be good for you, or bad for you; that you cannot be


[024-056]punished for the crimes of others, nor rewarded for their virtues.


[024-057]I still insist that the consequences of good actions are always


[024-058]good, and those of bad actions always bad.  I insist that nobody


[024-059]can plant thistles and gather figs; neither can they plant figs


[024-060]and gather thistles.  I still deny that a finite being can commit


[024-061]an infinite sin; but I continue to insist that a God who would


[024-062]punish a man forever is an infinite tyrant.  My views have undergone


[024-063]no change, except that the evidence of that truth constantly


[024-064]increases, and the dogmas of the church look, if possible, a little


[024-065]absurder every day.  Theology, you know, is not a science.  It


[024-066]stops at the grave; and faith is the end of theology.  Ministers


[024-067]have not even the advantage of the doctors; the doctors sometimes


[024-068]can tell by a post-mortem examination whether they killed the man


[024-069]or not; but by cutting a man open after he is dead, the wisest


[024-070]theologians cannot tell what has become of his soul, and whether


[024-071]it was injured or helped by a belief in the inspiration of the


[024-072]Scriptures.  Theology depends on assertion for evidence, and on


[024-073]faith for disciples.


[024-074]--_The Tribune_, Denver, Colorado, January 17, 1886.


[024-075]MORALITY AND IMMORTALITY.


[024-076]_Question_.  I see that the clergy are still making all kinds of


[024-077]charges against you and your doctrines.


[024-078]_Answer_.  Yes.  Some of the charges are true and some of them are


[024-079]not.  I suppose that they intend to get in the vicinity of veracity,


[024-080]and are probably stating my belief as it is honestly misunderstood


[024-081]by them.  I admit that I have said and that I still think that


[024-082]Christianity is a blunder.  But the question arises, What is


[024-083]Christianity?  I do not mean, when I say that Christianity is a


[024-084]blunder, that the morality taught by Christians is a mistake.


[024-085]Morality is not distinctively Christian, any more than it is


[024-086]Mohammedan.  Morality is human, it belongs to no ism, and does not


[024-087]depend for a foundation upon the supernatural, or upon any book,


[024-088]or upon any creed.  Morality is itself a foundation.  When I say


[024-089]that Christianity is a blunder, I mean all those things distinctively


[024-090]Christian are blunders.  It is a blunder to say that an infinite


[024-091]being lived in Palestine, learned the carpenter's trade, raised


[024-092]the dead, cured the blind, and cast out devils, and that this God


[024-093]was finally assassinated by the Jews.  This is absurd.  All these


[024-094]statements are blunders, if not worse.  I do not believe that Christ


[024-095]ever claimed that he was of supernatural origin, or that he wrought


[024-096]miracles, or that he would rise from the dead.  If he did, he was


[024-097]mistaken--honestly mistaken, perhaps, but still mistaken.


[024-098]The morality inculcated by Mohammed is good.  The immorality


[024-099]inculcated by Mohammed is bad.  If Mohammed was a prophet of God,


[024-100]it does not make the morality he taught any better, neither does


[024-101]it make the immorality any better or any worse.


[024-102]By this time the whole world ought to know that morality does not


[024-103]need to go into partnership with miracles.  Morality is based upon


[024-104]the experience of mankind.  It does not have to learn of inspired


[024-105]writers, or of gods, or of divine persons.  It is a lesson that


[024-106]the whole human race has been learning and learning from experience.


[024-107]He who upholds, or believes in, or teaches, the miraculous, commits


[024-108]a blunder.


[024-109]Now, what is morality?  Morality is the best thing to do under the


[024-110]circumstances.  Anything that tends to the happiness of mankind is


[024-111]moral.  Anything that tends to unhappiness is immoral.  We apply


[024-112]to the moral world rules and regulations as we do in the physical


[024-113]world.  The man who does justice, or tries to do so--who is honest


[024-114]and kind and gives to others what he claims for himself, is a moral


[024-115]man.  All actions must be judged by their consequences.  Where the


[024-116]consequences are good, the actions are good.  Where the consequences


[024-117]are bad, the actions are bad; and all consequences are learned from


[024-118]experience.  After we have had a certain amount of experience, we


[024-119]then reason from analogy.  We apply our logic and say that a certain


[024-120]course will bring destruction, another course will bring happiness.


[024-121]There is nothing inspired about morality--nothing supernatural.


[024-122]It is simply good, common sense, going hand in hand with kindness.


[024-123]Morality is capable of being demonstrated.  You do not have to take


[024-124]the word of anybody; you can observe and examine for yourself.


[024-125]Larceny is the enemy of industry, and industry is good; therefore


[024-126]larceny is immoral.  The family is the unit of good government;


[024-127]anything that tends to destroy the family is immoral.  Honesty is


[024-128]the mother of confidence; it united, combines and solidifies society.


[024-129]Dishonesty is disintegration; it destroys confidence; it brings


[024-130]social chaos; it is therefore immoral.


[024-131]I also admit that I regard the Mosaic account of the creation as


[024-132]an absurdity--as a series of blunders.  Probably Moses did the best


[024-133]he could.  He had never talked with Humboldt or Laplace.  He knew


[024-134]nothing of geology or astronomy.  He had not the slightest suspicion


[024-135]of Kepler's Three Laws.  He never saw a copy of Newton's Principia.


[024-136]Taking all these things into consideration, I think Moses did the


[024-137]best he could.


[024-138]The religious people say now that "days" did not mean days.  Of


[024-139]these "six days" they make a kind of telescope, which you can push


[024-140]in or draw out at pleasure.  If the geologists find that more time


[024-141]was necessary they will stretch them out.  Should it turn out that


[024-142]the world is not quite as old as some think, they will push them


[024-143]up.  The "six days" can now be made to suit any period of time.


[024-144]Nothing can be more childish, frivolous or contradictory.


[024-145]Only a few years ago the Mosaic account was considered true, and


[024-146]Moses was regarded as a scientific authority.  Geology and astronomy


[024-147]were measured by the Mosaic standard.  The opposite is now true.


[024-148]The church has changed; and instead of trying to prove that modern


[024-149]astronomy and geology are false, because they do not agree with


[024-150]Moses, it is now endeavoring to prove that the account by Moses is


[024-151]true, because it agrees with modern astronomy and geology.  In


[024-152]other words, the standard has changed; the ancient is measured by


[024-153]the modern, and where the literal statement in the Bible does not


[024-154]agree with modern discoveries, they do not change the discoveries,


[024-155]but give new meanings to the old account.  We are not now endeavoring


[024-156]to reconcile science with the Bible, but to reconcile the Bible


[024-157]with science.


[024-158]Nothing shows the extent of modern doubt more than the eagerness


[024-159]with which Christians search for some new testimony.  Luther answered


[024-160]Copernicus with a passage of Scripture, and he answered him to the


[024-161]satisfaction of orthodox ignorance.


[024-162]The truth is that the Jews adopted the stories of Creation, the


[024-163]Garden of Eden, Forbidden Fruit, and the Fall of Man.  They were


[024-164]told by older barbarians than they, and the Jews gave them to us.


[024-165]I never said that the Bible is all bad.  I have always admitted


[024-166]that there are many good and splendid things in the Jewish Scriptures,


[024-167]and many bad things.  What I insist is that we should have the


[024-168]courage and the common sense to accept the good, and throw away


[024-169]the bad.  Evil is not good because found in good company, and truth


[024-170]is still truth, even when surrounded by falsehood.


[024-171]_Question_.  I see that you are frequently charged with disrespect


[024-172]toward your parents--with lack of reverence for the opinions of


[024-173]your father?


[024-174]_Answer_.  I think my father and mother upon several religious


[024-175]questions were mistaken.  In fact, I have no doubt that they were;


[024-176]but I never felt under the slightest obligation to defend my father's


[024-177]mistakes.  No one can defend what he thinks is a mistake, without


[024-178]being dishonest.  That is a poor way to show respect for parents.


[024-179]Every Protestant clergyman asks men and women who had Catholic


[024-180]parents to desert the church in which they were raised.  They have


[024-181]no hesitation in saying to these people that their fathers and


[024-182]mothers were mistaken, and that they were deceived by priests and


[024-183]popes.


[024-184]The probability is that we are all mistaken about almost everything;


[024-185]but it is impossible for a man to be respectable enough to make a


[024-186]mistake respectable.  There is nothing remarkably holy in a blunder,


[024-187]or praiseworthy in stubbing the toe of the mind against a mistake.


[024-188]Is it possible that logic stands paralyzed in the presence of


[024-189]paternal absurdity?  Suppose a man has a bad father; is he bound


[024-190]by the bad father's opinion, when he is satisfied that the opinion


[024-191]is wrong?  How good does a father have to be, in order to put his


[024-192]son under obligation to defend his blunders?  Suppose the father


[024-193]thinks one way, and the mother the other; what are the children to


[024-194]do?  Suppose the father changes his opinion; what then?  Suppose


[024-195]the father thinks one way and the mother the other, and they both


[024-196]die when the boy is young; and the boy is bound out; whose mistakes


[024-197]is he then bound to follow?  Our missionaries tell the barbarian


[024-198]boy that his parents are mistaken, that they know nothing, and that


[024-199]the wooden god is nothing but a senseless idol.  They do not hesitate


[024-200]to tell this boy that his mother believed lies, and hugged, it may


[024-201]be to her dying heart, a miserable delusion.  Why should a barbarian


[024-202]boy cast reproach upon his parents?
