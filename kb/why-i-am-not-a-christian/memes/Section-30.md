[029-001]Christianity is charitable; Christianity spends a great deal of


[029-002]money; but I am somewhat doubtful as to the good that is accomplished.


[029-003]There ought to be some way to prevent crime; not simply to punish


[029-004]it.  There ought to be some way to prevent pauperism, not simply


[029-005]to relieve temporarily a pauper, and if the ministers and good


[029-006]people belonging to the churches would spend their time investigating


[029-007]the affairs of this world and let the New Jerusalem take care of


[029-008]itself, I think it would be far better.


[029-009]The church is guilty of one great contradiction.  The ministers


[029-010]are always talking about worldly people, and yet, were it not for


[029-011]worldly people, who would pay the salary?  How could the church


[029-012]live a minute unless somebody attended to the affairs of this world?


[029-013]The best religion, in my judgment, is common sense going along hand


[029-014]in hand with kindness, and not troubling ourselves about another


[029-015]world until we get there.  I am willing for one, to wait and see


[029-016]what kind of a country it will be.


[029-017]_Question_.  Does the question of the inspiration of Scriptures


[029-018]affect the beauty and benefits of Christianity here and hereafter?


[029-019]_Answer_.  A belief in the inspiration of the Scriptures has done,


[029-020]in my judgment, great harm.  The Bible has been the breastwork for


[029-021]nearly everything wrong.  The defenders of slavery relied on the


[029-022]Bible.  The Bible was the real auction block on which every negro


[029-023]stood when he was sold.  I never knew a minister to preach in favor


[029-024]of slavery that did not take his text from the Bible.  The Bible


[029-025]teaches persecution for opinion's sake.  The Bible--that is the


[029-026]Old Testament--upholds polygamy, and just to the extent that men,


[029-027]through the Bible, have believed that slavery, religious persecution,


[029-028]wars of extermination and polygamy were taught by God, just to that


[029-029]extent the Bible has done great harm.  The idea of inspiration


[029-030]enslaves the human mind and debauches the human heart.


[029-031]_Question_.  Is not Christianity and the belief in God a check upon


[029-032]mankind in general and thus a good thing in itself?


[029-033]_Answer_.  This, again, brings up the question of what you mean by


[029-034]Christianity, but taking it for granted that you mean by Christianity


[029-035]the church, then I answer, when the church had almost absolute


[029-036]authority, then the world was the worst.


[029-037]Now, as to the other part of the question, "Is not a belief in God


[029-038]a check upon mankind in general?"  That is owing to what kind of


[029-039]God the man believes in.  When mankind believed in the God of the


[029-040]Old Testament, I think that belief was a bad thing; the tendency


[029-041]was bad.  I think that John Calvin patterned after Jehovah as nearly


[029-042]as his health and strength would permit.  Man makes God in his own


[029-043]image, and bad men are not apt to have a very good God if they make


[029-044]him.  I believe it is far better to have a real belief in goodness,


[029-045]in kindness, in honesty and in mankind than in any supernatural


[029-046]being whatever.  I do not suppose it would do any harm for a man


[029-047]to believe in a real good God, a God without revenge, a God that


[029-048]was not very particular in having a man believe a doctrine whether


[029-049]he could understand it or not.  I do not believe that a belief of


[029-050]that kind would do any particular harm.


[029-051]There is a vast difference between the God of John Calvin and the


[029-052]God of Henry Ward Beecher, and a great difference between the God


[029-053]of Cardinal Pedro Gonzales de Mendoza and the God of Theodore


[029-054]Parker.


[029-055]_Question_.  Well, Colonel, is the world growing better or worse?


[029-056]_Answer_.  I think better in some respects and worse in others;


[029-057]but on the whole, better.  I think that while events, like the


[029-058]pendulum of a clock, go backward and forward, man, like the hands,


[029-059]goes forward.  I think there is more reason and less religion, more


[029-060]charity and less creed.  I think the church is improving.  Ministers


[029-061]are ashamed to preach the old doctrines with the old fervor.  There


[029-062]was a time when the pulpit controlled the pews.  It is so no longer.


[029-063]The pews know what they want, and if the minister does not furnish


[029-064]it they discharge him and employ another.  He is no longer an


[029-065]autocrat; he must bring to the market what his customers are willing


[029-066]to buy.


[029-067]_Question_.  What are you going to do to be saved?


[029-068]_Answer_.  Well, I think I am safe, anyway.  I suppose I have a


[029-069]right to rely on what Matthew says, that if I will forgive others


[029-070]God will forgive me.  I suppose if there is another world I shall


[029-071]be treated very much as I treat others.  I never expect to find


[029-072]perfect bliss anywhere; maybe I should tire of it if I should.


[029-073]What I have endeavored to do has been to put out the fires of an


[029-074]ignorant and cruel hell; to do what I could to destroy that dogma;


[029-075]to destroy the doctrine that makes the cradle as terrible as the


[029-076]coffin.


[029-077]--_The Denver Republican_, Denver, Colorado, January 17, 1884.


[029-078]THE OATH QUESTION.


[029-079]_Question_.  I suppose that your attention has been called to the


[029-080]excitement in England over the oath question, and you have probably


[029-081]wondered that so much should have been made of so little?


[029-082]_Answer_.  Yes; I have read a few articles upon the subject,


[029-083]including one by Cardinal Newman.  It is wonderful that so many


[029-084]people imagine that there is something miraculous in the oath.


[029-085]They seem to regard it as a kind of verbal fetich, a charm, an "open


[029-086]sesame" to be pronounced at the door of truth, a spell, a kind of


[029-087]moral thumbscrew, by means of which falsehood itself is compelled


[029-088]to turn informer.


[029-089]The oath has outlived its brother, "the wager of battle."  Both


[029-090]were born of the idea that God would interfere for the right and


[029-091]for the truth.  Trial by fire and by water had the same origin.


[029-092]It was once believed that the man in the wrong could not kill the


[029-093]man in the right; but, experience having shown that he usually did,


[029-094]the belief gradually fell into disrepute.  So it was once thought


[029-095]that a perjurer could not swallow a piece of sacramental bread;


[029-096]but, the fear that made the swallowing difficult having passed


[029-097]away, the appeal to the corsned was abolished.  It was found that


[029-098]a brazen or a desperate man could eat himself out of the greatest


[029-099]difficulty with perfect ease, satisfying the law and his own hunger


[029-100]at the same time.


[029-101]The oath is a relic of barbarous theology, of the belief that a


[029-102]personal God interferes in the affairs of men; that some God protects


[029-103]innocence and guards the right.  The experience of the world has


[029-104]sadly demonstrated the folly of that belief.  The testimony of a


[029-105]witness ought to be believed, not because it is given under the


[029-106]solemnities of an oath, but because it is reasonable.  If unreasonable


[029-107]it ought to be thrown aside.  The question ought not to be, "Has


[029-108]this been sworn to?" but, "Is this true?"  The moment evidence is


[029-109]tested by the standard of reason, the oath becomes a useless


[029-110]ceremony.  Let the man who gives false evidence be punished as the


[029-111]lawmaking power may prescribe.  He should be punished because he


[029-112]commits a crime against society, and he should be punished in this


[029-113]world.  All honest men will tell the truth if they can; therefore,


[029-114]oaths will have no effect upon them.  Dishonest men will not tell


[029-115]the truth unless the truth happens to suit their purpose; therefore,


[029-116]oaths will have no effect upon them.  We punish them, not for


[029-117]swearing to a lie, but for telling it, and we can make the punishment


[029-118]for telling the falsehood just as severe as we wish.  If they are


[029-119]to be punished in another world, the probability is that the


[029-120]punishment there will be for having told the falsehood here.  After


[029-121]all, a lie is made no worse by an oath, and the truth is made no


[029-122]better.


[029-123]_Question_.  You object then to the oath.  Is your objection based


[029-124]on any religious grounds, or on any prejudice against the ceremony


[029-125]because of its religious origin; or what is your objection?


[029-126]_Answer_.  I care nothing about the origin of the ceremony.  The


[029-127]objection to the oath is this:  It furnishes a falsehood with a


[029-128]letter of credit.  It supplies the wolf with sheep's clothing and


[029-129]covers the hands of Jacob with hair.  It blows out the light, and


[029-130]in the darkness Leah is taken for Rachel.  It puts upon each witness


[029-131]a kind of theological gown.  This gown hides the moral rags of the


[029-132]depraved wretch as well as the virtues of the honest man.  The oath


[029-133]is a mask that falsehood puts on, and for a moment is mistaken for


[029-134]truth.  It gives to dishonesty the advantage of solemnity.  The


[029-135]tendency of the oath is to put all testimony on an equality.  The


[029-136]obscure rascal and the man of sterling character both "swear," and


[029-137]jurors who attribute a miraculous quality to the oath, forget the


[029-138]real difference in the men, and give about the same weight to the


[029-139]evidence of each, because both were "sworn."  A scoundrel is


[029-140]delighted with the opportunity of going through a ceremony that


[029-141]gives importance and dignity to his story, that clothes him for


[029-142]the moment with respectability, loans him the appearance of


[029-143]conscience, and gives the ring of true coin to the base metal.  To


[029-144]him the oath is a shield.  He is in partnership, for a moment, with


[029-145]God, and people who have no confidence in the witness credit the


[029-146]firm.


[029-147]_Question_.  Of course you know the religionists insist that people


[029-148]are more likely to tell the truth when "sworn," and that to take


[029-149]away the oath is to destroy the foundation of testimony?


[029-150]_Answer_.  If the use of the oath is defended on the ground that


[029-151]religious people need a stimulus to tell the truth, then I am


[029-152]compelled to say that religious people have been so badly educated


[029-153]that they mistake the nature of the crime.


[029-154]They should be taught that to defeat justice by falsehood is the


[029-155]real offence.  Besides, fear is not the natural foundation of


[029-156]virtue.  Even with religious people fear cannot always last.


[029-157]Ananias and Sapphira have been dead so long, and since their time


[029-158]so many people have sworn falsely without affecting their health


[029-159]that the fear of sudden divine vengeance no longer pales the cheek


[029-160]of the perjurer.  If the vengeance is not sudden, then, according


[029-161]to the church, the criminal will have plenty of time to repent; so


[029-162]that the oath no longer affects even the fearful.  Would it not be


[029-163]better for the church to teach that telling the falsehood is the


[029-164]real crime, and that taking the oath neither adds to nor takes from


[029-165]its enormity?  Would it not be better to teach that he who does


[029-166]wrong must suffer the consequences, whether God forgives him or not?


[029-167]He who tries to injure another may or may not succeed, but he cannot


[029-168]by any possibility fail to injure himself.  Men should be taught


[029-169]that there is no difference between truth-telling and truth-swearing.


[029-170]Nothing is more vicious than the idea that any ceremony or form of


[029-171]words--hand-lifting or book-kissing--can add, even in the slightest


[029-172]degree, to the perpetual obligation every human being is under to


[029-173]speak the truth.


[029-174]The truth, plainly told, naturally commends itself to the intelligent.


[029-175]Every fact is a genuine link in the infinite chain, and will agree


[029-176]perfectly with every other fact.  A fact asks to be inspected, asks


[029-177]to be understood.  It needs no oath, no ceremony, no supernatural


[029-178]aid.  It is independent of all the gods.  A falsehood goes in


[029-179]partnership with theology, and depends on the partner for success.


[029-180]To show how little influence for good has been attributed to the


[029-181]oath, it is only necessary to say that for centuries, in the


[029-182]Christian world, no person was allowed to testify who had the


[029-183]slightest pecuniary interest in the result of a suit.
