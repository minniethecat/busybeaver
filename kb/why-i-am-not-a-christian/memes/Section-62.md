[061-001]My own belief is that the system in vogue now in regard to the


[061-002]treatment of criminals in many States produces more crime than it


[061-003]prevents.  Take, for instance, the Southern States.  There is hardly


[061-004]a chapter in the history of the world the reading of which could


[061-005]produce greater indignation than the history of the convict system


[061-006]in many of the Southern States.  These convicts are hired out for


[061-007]the purpose of building railways, or plowing fields, or digging


[061-008]coal, and in some instances the death-rate has been over twelve


[061-009]per cent. a month.  The evidence shows that no respect was paid to


[061-010]the sexes--men and women were chained together indiscriminately.


[061-011]The evidence also shows that for the slightest offences they were


[061-012]shot down like beasts.  They were pursued by hounds, and their


[061-013]flesh was torn from their bones.


[061-014]So in some of the Northern prisons they have what they call the


[061-015]weighing machine--an infamous thing, and he who uses it commits as


[061-016]great a crime as the convict he punishes could have committed.


[061-017]All these things are degrading, debasing, and demoralizing.  There


[061-018]is no need of any such punishment in any penitentiary.  Let the


[061-019]punishment be of such kind that the convict is responsible himself.


[061-020]For instance, if the convict refuses to obey a reasonable rule he


[061-021]can be put into a cell.  He can be fed when he obeys the rule.


[061-022]If he goes hungry it is his own fault.  It depends upon himself to


[061-023]say when he shall eat.  Or he may be placed in such a position that


[061-024]if he does not work--if he does not pump--the water will rise and


[061-025]drown him.  If the water does rise it is his fault.  Nobody pours


[061-026]it upon him.  He takes his choice.


[061-027]These are suggested as desperate cases, but I can imagine no case


[061-028]where what is called corporal punishment should be inflicted, and


[061-029]the reason I am against it is this:  I am opposed to any punishment


[061-030]that cannot be inflicted by a gentleman.  I am opposed to any


[061-031]punishment the infliction of which tends to harden and debase the


[061-032]man who inflicts it.  I am for no laws that have to be carried out


[061-033]by human curs.


[061-034]Take, for instance, the whipping-post.  Nothing can be more degrading.


[061-035]The man who applies the lash is necessarily a cruel and vulgar man,


[061-036]and the oftener he applies it the more and more debased he will


[061-037]become.  The whole thing can be stated in the one sentence:  I am


[061-038]opposed to any punishment that cannot be inflicted by a gentleman,


[061-039]and by "gentleman" I mean a self-respecting, honest, generous man.


[061-040]_Question_.  What do you think of the efficacy or the propriety of


[061-041]punishing criminals by solitary confinement?


[061-042]_Answer_.  Solitary confinement is a species of torture.  I am


[061-043]opposed to all torture.  I think the criminal should not be punished.


[061-044]He should be reformed, if he is capable of reformation.  But,


[061-045]whatever is done, it should not be done as a punishment.  Society


[061-046]should be too noble, too generous, to harbor a thought of revenge.


[061-047]Society should not punish, it should protect itself only.  It should


[061-048]endeavor to reform the individual.  Now, solitary confinement does


[061-049]not, I imagine, tend to the reformation of the individual.  Neither


[061-050]can the person in that position do good to any human being.  The


[061-051]prisoner will be altogether happier when his mind is engaged, when


[061-052]his hands are busy, when he has something to do.  This keeps alive


[061-053]what we call cheerfulness.  And let me say a word on this point.


[061-054]I don't believe that the State ought to steal the labor of a convict.


[061-055]Here is a man who has a family.  He is sent to the penitentiary.


[061-056]He works from morning till night.  Now, in my judgment, he ought


[061-057]to be paid for the labor over and above what it costs to keep him.


[061-058]That money should be sent to his family.  That money should be


[061-059]subject, at least, to his direction.  If he is a single man, when


[061-060]he comes out of the penitentiary he should be given his earnings,


[061-061]and all his earnings, so that he would not have the feeling that


[061-062]he had been robbed.  A statement should be given to him to show what


[061-063]it had cost to keep him and how much his labor had brought and the


[061-064]balance remaining in his favor.  With this little balance he could


[061-065]go out into the world with something like independence.  This little


[061-066]balance would be a foundation for his honesty--a foundation for a


[061-067]resolution on his part to be a man.  But now each one goes out with


[061-068]the feeling that he has not only been punished for the crime which


[061-069]he committed, but that he has been robbed of the results of his


[061-070]labor while there.


[061-071]The idea is simply preposterous that the people sent to the


[061-072]penitentiary should live in idleness.  They should have the benefit


[061-073]of their labor, and if you give them the benefit of their labor


[061-074]they will turn out as good work as if they were out of the


[061-075]penitentiary.  They will have the same reason to do their best.


[061-076]Consequently, poor articles, poorly constructed things, would not


[061-077]come into competition with good articles made by free people outside


[061-078]of the walls.


[061-079]Now many mechanics are complaining because work done in the


[061-080]penitentiaries is brought into competition with their work.  But


[061-081]the only reason that convict work is cheaper is because the poor


[061-082]wretch who does it is robbed.  The only reason that the work is


[061-083]poor is because the man who does it has no interest in its being


[061-084]good. If he had the profit of his own labor he would do the best


[061-085]that was in him, and the consequence would be that the wares


[061-086]manufactured in the prisons would be as good as those manufactured


[061-087]elsewhere.  For instance, we will say here are three or four men


[061-088]working together.  They are all free men.  One commits a crime and


[061-089]he is sent to the penitentiary.  Is it possible that his companions


[061-090]would object to his being paid for honest work in the penitentiary?


[061-091]And let me say right here, all labor is honest.  Whoever makes a


[061-092]useful thing, the labor is honest, no matter whether the work is


[061-093]done in a penitentiary or in a palace; in a hovel or the open field.


[061-094]Wherever work is done for the good of others, it is honest work.


[061-095]If the laboring men would stop and think, they would know that they


[061-096]support everybody.  Labor pays all the taxes.  Labor supports all


[061-097]the penitentiaries.  Labor pays the warden.  Labor pays everything,


[061-098]and if the convicts are allowed to live in idleness labor must pay


[061-099]their board.  Every cent of tax is borne by the back of labor.  No


[061-100]matter whether your tariff is put on champagne and diamonds, it


[061-101]has to be paid by the men and women who work--those who plow in


[061-102]the fields, who wash and iron, who stand by the forge, who run the


[061-103]cars and work in the mines, and by those who battle with the waves


[061-104]of the sea.  Labor pays every bill.


[061-105]There is one little thing to which I wish to call the attention of


[061-106]all who happen to read this interview, and that is this:  Undoubtedly


[061-107]you think of all criminals with horror and when you hear about them


[061-108]you are, in all probability, filled with virtuous indignation.


[061-109]But, first of all, I want you to think of what you have in fact


[061-110]done.  Secondly, I want you to think of what you have wanted to


[061-111]do.  Thirdly, I want you to reflect whether you were prevented from


[061-112]doing what you wanted to do by fear or by lack of opportunity.


[061-113]Then perhaps you will have more charity.


[061-114]_Question_.  What do you think of the new legislation in the State


[061-115]changing the death penalty to death by electricity?


[061-116]_Answer_.  If death by electricity is less painful than hanging,


[061-117]then the law, so far as that goes, is good.  There is not the


[061-118]slightest propriety in inflicting upon the person executed one


[061-119]single unnecessary pang, because that partakes of the nature of


[061-120]revenge--that is to say, of hatred--and, as a consequence, the


[061-121]State shows the same spirit that the criminal was animated by when


[061-122]he took the life of his neighbor.  If the death penalty is to be


[061-123]inflicted, let it be done in the most humane way.  For my part, I


[061-124]should like to see the criminal removed, if he must be removed,


[061-125]with the same care and with the same mercy that you would perform


[061-126]a surgical operation.  Why inflict pain?  Who wants it inflicted?


[061-127]What good can it, by any possibility, do?  To inflict unnecessary


[061-128]pain hardens him who inflicts it, hardens each among those who


[061-129]witness it, and tends to demoralize the community.


[061-130]_Question_.  Is it not the fact that punishments have grown less


[061-131]and less severe for many years past?


[061-132]_Answer_.  In the old times punishment was the only means of


[061-133]reformation.  If anybody did wrong, punish him.  If people still


[061-134]continued to commit the same offence, increase the punishment; and


[061-135]that went on until in what they call "civilized countries" they


[061-136]hanged people, provided they stole the value of one shilling.  But


[061-137]larceny kept right on.  There was no diminution.  So, for treason,


[061-138]barbarous punishments were inflicted.  Those guilty of that offence


[061-139]were torn asunder by horses; their entrails were cut out of them


[061-140]while they were yet living and thrown into their faces; their bodies


[061-141]were quartered and their heads were set on pikes above the gates


[061-142]of the city.  Yet there was a hundred times more treason then than


[061-143]now.  Every time a man was executed and mutilated and tortured in


[061-144]this way the seeds of other treason were sown.


[061-145]So in the church there was the same idea.  No reformation but by


[061-146]punishment.  Of course in this world the punishment stopped when


[061-147]the poor wretch was dead.  It was found that that punishment did


[061-148]not reform, so the church said:  "After death it will go right on,


[061-149]getting worse and worse, forever and forever."  Finally it was


[061-150]found that this did not tend to the reformation of mankind.  Slowly


[061-151]the fires of hell have been dying out.  The climate has been changing


[061-152]from year to year.  Men have lost confidence in the power of the


[061-153]thumbscrew, the fagot, and the rack here, and they are losing


[061-154]confidence in the flames of perdition hereafter.  In other words,


[061-155]it is simply a question of civilization.


[061-156]When men become civilized in matters of thought, they will know


[061-157]that every human being has the right to think for himself, and the


[061-158]right to express his honest thought.  Then the world of thought


[061-159]will be free.  At that time they will be intelligent enough to know


[061-160]that men have different thoughts, that their ways are not alike,


[061-161]because they have lived under different circumstances, and in that


[061-162]time they will also know that men act as they are acted upon.  And


[061-163]it is my belief that the time will come when men will no more think


[061-164]of punishing a man because he has committed the crime of larceny


[061-165]than they will think of punishing a man because he has the consumption.


[061-166]In the first case they will endeavor to reform him, and in the


[061-167]second case they will endeavor to cure him.


[061-168]The intelligent people of the world, many of them, are endeavoring


[061-169]to find out the great facts in Nature that control the dispositions


[061-170]of men.  So other intelligent people are endeavoring to ascertain


[061-171]the facts and conditions that govern what we call health, and what


[061-172]we call disease, and the object of these people is finally to


[061-173]produce a race without disease of flesh and without disease of


[061-174]mind.  These people look forward to the time when there need to be


[061-175]neither hospitals nor penitentiaries.
