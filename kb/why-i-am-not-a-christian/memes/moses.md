# Moses

**Moses** is the central prophetic figure of the Torah and the traditional author of the Pentateuch. In "Why I Am Not a Christian," [[robert-ingersoll|Robert G. Ingersoll]] subjects Moses to sustained critical examination, referencing his earlier work "Mistakes of Moses" as part of his broader critique of biblical authority.

## In Ingersoll's Writings

### "Mistakes of Moses"

Ingersoll opens his discussion by referencing his earlier lecture: "Mistakes of Moses, in which I have endeavored" (002-150) to demonstrate errors and moral problems in the Pentateuch. This establishes his credentials as a longtime critic of Mosaic authorship and authority.

### Historical Existence

Ingersoll questions whether Moses was a historical figure at all. He notes the "difficulty between you and Moses" (010-132) — the problem of reconciling the biblical account with historical evidence. He observes that the Pentateuch was "written several centuries after Moses" (010-136) would have lived, pointing to Moses' "unknown sepulchre" (010-137) as further evidence against the traditional account.

Ingersoll concedes that "no doubt Moses was quite" (010-137) influential as a legendary figure, but argues that this does not establish his historicity. He states that "Moses is exactly the same as 'law-g'" (010-139) — lawgiver — a role common to many ancient cultures, not unique to Hebrew tradition.

### Moral Assessment

Ingersoll evaluates Moses as a moral teacher, noting: "whether Moses lived or not makes but" (010-143) little difference to his argument. He acknowledges that "thinking of Moses as a man, admitting" (010-148) his existence for the sake of argument, one can still find his moral code deficient by modern standards.

He concludes that he can "see no evidence that the man Moses was any nearer" (010-156) to divine truth than other ancient lawgivers. "Moses was a" (010-158) product of his time, Ingersoll argues, and his laws reflect the primitive moral understanding of the ancient Near East rather than eternal divine wisdom.
