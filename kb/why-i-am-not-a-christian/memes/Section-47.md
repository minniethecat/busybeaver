[046-001]When a man becomes a Catholic priest, he has been convinced that


[046-002]he ought not to think for himself upon religious questions.  He


[046-003]has become convinced that the church is the only teacher--that he


[046-004]has a right to think only to enforce its teachings.  From that


[046-005]moment he is a moral machine.  The chief engineer resides at Rome,


[046-006]and he gives his orders through certain assistant engineers until


[046-007]the one is reached who turns the crank, and the machine has nothing


[046-008]to do one way or the other.  This machine is paid for giving up


[046-009]his liberty by having machines under him who have also given up


[046-010]theirs.  While somebody else turns his crank, he has the pleasure


[046-011]of turning a crank belonging to somebody below him.


[046-012]Of course, the Catholic Church is supposed to be the only perfect


[046-013]institution on earth.  All others are not only imperfect, but


[046-014]unnecessary.  All others have been made either by man, or by the


[046-015]Devil, or by a partnership, and consequently cannot be depended


[046-016]upon for the civilization of man.


[046-017]The Catholic Church gets its power directly from God, and is the


[046-018]only institution now in the world founded by God.  There was never


[046-019]any other, so far as I know, except polygamy and slavery and a


[046-020]crude kind of monarchy, and they have been, for the most part,


[046-021]abolished.


[046-022]The Catholic Church must be true to itself.  It must claim everything,


[046-023]and get what it can.  It alone is infallible.  It alone has all


[046-024]the wisdom of this world.  It alone has the right to exist.  All


[046-025]other interests are secondary.  To be a Catholic is of the first


[046-026]importance.  Human liberty is nothing.  Wealth, position, food,


[046-027]clothing, reputation, happiness--all these are less than worthless


[046-028]compared with what the Catholic Church promises to the man who will


[046-029]throw all these away.


[046-030]A priest must preach what his bishop tells him.  A bishop must


[046-031]preach what his archbishop tells him.  The pope must preach what


[046-032]he says God tells him.


[046-033]Dr. McGlynn cannot make a compromise with the Catholic Church.  It


[046-034]never compromises when it is in the majority.


[046-035]I do not mean by this that the Catholic Church is worse than any


[046-036]other.  All are alike in this regard.  Every sect, no matter how


[046-037]insignificant; every church, no matter how powerful, asks precisely


[046-038]the same thing from every member--that is to say, a surrender of


[046-039]intellectual freedom.  The Catholic Church wants the same as the


[046-040]Baptist, the Presbyterian, and the Methodist--it wants the whole


[046-041]earth.  It is ambitious to be the one supreme power.  It hopes to


[046-042]see the world upon its knees, with all its tongues thrust out for


[046-043]wafers.  It has the arrogance of humility and the ferocity of


[046-044]universal forgiveness.  In this respect it resembles every other


[046-045]sect.  Every religion is a system of slavery.


[046-046]Of course, the religionists say that they do not believe in


[046-047]persecution; that they do not believe in burning and hanging and


[046-048]whipping or loading with chains a man simply because he is an


[046-049]Infidel.  They are willing to leave all this with God, knowing that


[046-050]a being of infinite goodness will inflict all these horrors and


[046-051]tortures upon an honest man who differs with the church.


[046-052]In case Dr. McGlynn is deprived of his priestly functions, it is


[046-053]hard to say what effect it will have upon his church and the labor


[046-054]party in the country.


[046-055]So long as a man believes that a church has eternal joy in store


[046-056]for him, so long as he believes that a church holds within its hand


[046-057]the keys of heaven and hell, it will be hard to make him trade off


[046-058]the hope of everlasting happiness for a few good clothes and a


[046-059]little good food and higher wages here.  He finally thinks that,


[046-060]after all, he had better work for less and go a little hungry, and


[046-061]be an angel forever.


[046-062]I hope, however, that a good many people who have been supporting


[046-063]the Catholic Church by giving tithes of the wages of weariness will


[046-064]see, and clearly see, that Catholicism is not their friend; that


[046-065]the church cannot and will not support them; that, on the contrary,


[046-066]they must support the church.  I hope they will see that all the


[046-067]prayers have to be paid for, although not one has ever been answered.


[046-068]I hope they will perceive that the church is on the side of wealth


[046-069]and power, that the mitre is the friend of the crown, that the


[046-070]altar is the sworn brother of the throne.  I hope they will finally


[046-071]know that the church cares infinitely more for the money of the


[046-072]millionaire than for the souls of the poor.


[046-073]Of course, there are thousands of individual exceptions.  I am


[046-074]speaking of the church as an institution, as a corporation--and


[046-075]when I say the church, I include all churches.  It is said of


[046-076]corporations in general, that they have no soul, and it may truthfully


[046-077]be said of the church that it has less than any other.  It lives


[046-078]on alms.  It gives nothing for what it gets.  It has no sympathy.


[046-079]Beggars never weep over the misfortunes of other beggars.


[046-080]Nothing could give me more pleasure than to see the Catholic Church


[046-081]on the side of human freedom; nothing more pleasure than to see


[046-082]the Catholics of the world--those who work and weep and toil--


[046-083]sensible enough to know that all the money paid for superstition


[046-084]is worse than lost.  I wish they could see that the counting of


[046-085]beads, and the saying of prayers and celebrating of masses, and


[046-086]all the kneelings and censer-swingings and fastings and bell-ringing,


[046-087]amount to less than nothing--that all these things tend only to


[046-088]the degradation of mankind.  It is hard, I know, to find an antidote


[046-089]for a poison that was mingled with a mother's milk.


[046-090]The laboring masses, so far as the Catholics are concerned, are


[046-091]filled with awe and wonder and fear about the church.  This fear


[046-092]began to grow while they were being rocked in their cradles, and


[046-093]they still imagine that the church has some mysterious power; that


[046-094]it is in direct communication with some infinite personality that


[046-095]could, if it desired, strike then dead, or damn their souls forever.


[046-096]Persons who have no such belief, who care nothing for popes or


[046-097]priests or churches or heavens or hells or devils or gods, have


[046-098]very little idea of the power of fear.


[046-099]The old dogmas filled the brain with strange monsters.  The soul


[046-100]of the orthodox Christian gropes and wanders and crawls in a kind


[046-101]of dungeon, where the strained eyes see fearful shapes, and the


[046-102]frightened flesh shrinks from the touch of serpents.


[046-103]The good part of Christianity--that is to say, kindness, morality


[046-104]--will never go down.  The cruel part ought to go down.  And by


[046-105]the cruel part I mean the doctrine of eternal punishment--of allowing


[046-106]the good to suffer for the bad--allowing innocence to pay the debt


[046-107]of guilt.  So the foolish part of Christianity--that is to say,


[046-108]the miraculous--will go down.  The absurd part must perish.  But


[046-109]there will be no war about it as there was in France.  Nobody


[046-110]believes enough in the foolish part of Christianity now to fight


[046-111]for it.  Nobody believes with intensity enough in miracles to


[046-112]shoulder a musket.  There is probably not a Christian in New York


[046-113]willing to fight for any story, no matter if the story is so old


[046-114]that it is covered with moss.  No mentally brave and intelligent


[046-115]man believes in miracles, and no intelligent man cares whether


[046-116]there was a miracle or not, for the reason that every intelligent


[046-117]man knows that the miraculous has no possible connection with the


[046-118]moral.  "Thou shalt not steal," is just as good a commandment if


[046-119]it should turn out that the flood was a drouth.  "Thou shalt not


[046-120]murder," is a good and just and righteous law, and whether any


[046-121]particular miracle was ever performed or not has nothing to do with


[046-122]the case.  There is no possible relation between these things.


[046-123]I am on the side not only of the physically oppressed, but of the


[046-124]mentally oppressed.  I hate those who put lashes on the body, and


[046-125]I despise those who put the soul in chains.  In other words, I am


[046-126]in favor of liberty.  I do not wish that any man should be the


[046-127]slave of his fellow-men, or that the human race should be the slaves


[046-128]of any god, real or imaginary.  Man has the right to think for


[046-129]himself, to work for himself, to take care of himself, to get bread


[046-130]for himself, to get a home for himself.  He has a right to his own


[046-131]opinion about God, and heaven and hell; the right to learn any art


[046-132]or mystery or trade; the right to work for whom he will, for what


[046-133]he will, and when he will.


[046-134]The world belongs to the human race.  There is to be no war in this


[046-135]country on religious opinions, except a war of words--a conflict


[046-136]of thoughts, of facts; and in that conflict the hosts of superstition


[046-137]will go down.  They may not be defeated to-day, or to-morrow, or


[046-138]next year, or during this century, but they are growing weaker day


[046-139]by day.


[046-140]This priest, McGlynn, has the courage to stand up against the


[046-141]propaganda.  What would have been his fate a few years ago?  What


[046-142]would have happened to him in Spain, in Portugal, in Italy--in any


[046-143]other country that was Catholic--only a few years ago?  Yet he


[046-144]stands here in New York, he refuses to obey God's vicegerent; he


[046-145]freely gives his mind to an archbishop; he holds the holy Inquisition


[046-146]in contempt.  He has done a great thing.  He is undoubtedly an


[046-147]honest man.  He never should have been a Catholic.  He has no


[046-148]business in that church.  He has ideas of his own--theories, and


[046-149]seems to be governed by principles.  The Catholic Church is not


[046-150]his place.  If he remains, he must submit, he must kneel in the


[046-151]humility of abjectness; he must receive on the back of his independence


[046-152]the lashes of the church.  If he remains, he must ask the forgiveness


[046-153]of slaves for having been a man.  If he refuses to submit, the


[046-154]church will not have him.  He will be driven to take his choice--


[046-155]to remain a member, humiliated, shunned, or go out into the great,


[046-156]free world a citizen of the Republic, with the rights, responsibilities,


[046-157]and duties of an American citizen.


[046-158]I believe that Dr. McGlynn is an honest man, and that he really


[046-159]believes in the land theories of Mr. George.  I have no confidence


[046-160]in his theories, but I have confidence that he is actuated by the


[046-161]best and noblest motives.


[046-162]_Question_.  Are you to go on the lecture platform again?


[046-163]_Answer_.  I expect to after a while.  I am now waiting for the


[046-164]church to catch up.  I got so far ahead that I began almost to


[046-165]sympathize with the clergy.  They looked so helpless and talked in


[046-166]such a weak, wandering, and wobbling kind of way that I felt as


[046-167]though I had been cruel.  From the papers I see that they are busy


[046-168]trying to find out who the wife of Cain was.  I see that the Rev.


[046-169]Dr. Robinson, of New York, is now wrestling with that problem.  He


[046-170]begins to be in doubt whether Adam was the first man, whether Eve


[046-171]was the first woman; suspects that there were other races, and that


[046-172]Cain did not marry his sister, but somebody else's sister, and that


[046-173]the somebody else was not Cain's brother.  One can hardly over-


[046-174]estimate the importance of these questions, they have such a direct


[046-175]bearing on the progress of the world.  If it should turn out that


[046-176]Adam was the first man, or that he was not the first man, something


[046-177]might happen--I am not prepared to say what, but it might.
