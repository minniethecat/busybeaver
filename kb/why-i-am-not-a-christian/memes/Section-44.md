[043-001]The toilers demand that eight hours should constitute a day's work.


[043-002]Upon this question what does our party say?  Labor saving machines


[043-003]ought to lighten the burdens of the laborers.  It will not do to


[043-004]say "over production" and keep on inventing machines and refuse to


[043-005]shorten the hours.  What does our party say?  The rich can take


[043-006]care of themselves if the mob will let them alone, and there will


[043-007]be no mob if there is no widespread want.  Hunger is a communist.


[043-008]The next candidate of the Republican party must be big enough and


[043-009]courageous enough to answer these questions.  If we find that kind


[043-010]of a candidate we shall succeed--if we do not, we ought not.


[043-011]--_Chicago Inter-Ocean_, February, 1886.


[043-012]ATHEISM AND CITIZENSHIP.


[043-013]_Question_.  Have you noticed the decision of Mr. Nathaniel Jarvis,


[043-014]Jr., clerk of the Naturalization Bureau of the Court of Common


[043-015]Pleas, that an Atheist cannot become a citizen?


[043-016]_Answer_.  Yes, but I do not think it necessary for a man to be a


[043-017]theist in order to become or to remain a citizen of this country.


[043-018]The various laws, from 1790 up to 1828, provided that the person


[043-019]wishing to be naturalized might make oath or affirmation.  The


[043-020]first exception you will find in the Revised Statutes of the United


[043-021]States passed in 1873-74, section 2,165, as follows:--"An alien


[043-022]may be admitted to become a citizen of the United States in the


[043-023]following manner, and not otherwise:--First, he shall declare on


[043-024]oath, before a Circuit or District Court of the United States,


[043-025]etc."  I suppose Mr. Jarvis felt it to be his duty to comply with


[043-026]this section.  In this section there is nothing about affirmation


[043-027]--only the word "oath" is used--and Mr. Jarvis came to the conclusion


[043-028]that an Atheist could not take an oath, and, therefore, could not


[043-029]declare his intention legally to become a citizen of the United


[043-030]States.  Undoubtedly Mr. Jarvis felt it his duty to stand by the


[043-031]law and to see to it that nobody should become a citizen of this


[043-032]country who had not a well defined belief in the existence of a


[043-033]being that he could not define and that no man has ever been able


[043-034]to define.  In other words, that he should be perfectly convinced


[043-035]that there is a being "without body, parts or passions," who presides


[043-036]over the destinies of this world, and more especially those of New


[043-037]York in and about that part known as City Hall Park.


[043-038]_Question_.  Was not Mr. Jarvis right in standing by the law?


[043-039]_Answer_.  If Mr. Jarvis is right, neither Humboldt nor Darwin


[043-040]could have become a citizen of the United States.  Wagner, the


[043-041]greatest of musicians, not being able to take an oath, would have


[043-042]been left an alien.  Under this ruling Haeckel, Spencer and Tyndall


[043-043]would be denied citizenship--that is to say, the six greatest men


[043-044]produced by the human race in the nineteenth century, were and are


[043-045]unfit to be citizens of the United States.  Those who have placed


[043-046]the human race in debt cannot be citizens of the Republic.  On the


[043-047]other hand, the ignorant wife beater, the criminal, the pauper


[043-048]raised in the workhouse, could take the necessary oath and would


[043-049]be welcomed by New York "with arms outstretched as she would fly."


[043-050]_Question_.  You have quoted one statute.  Is there no other


[043-051]applicable to this case?


[043-052]_Answer_.  I am coming to that.  If Mr. Jarvis will take the pains


[043-053]to read not only the law of naturalization in section 2,165 of the


[043-054]Revised Statutes of the United States, but the very first chapter


[043-055]in the book, "Title I.," he will find in the very first section


[043-056]this sentence:  "The requirements of any 'oath' shall be deemed


[043-057]complied with by making affirmation in official form."  This applies


[043-058]to section 2,165.  Of course an Atheist can affirm, and the statute


[043-059]provides that wherever an oath is required affirmation may be made.


[043-060]_Question_.  Did you read the recent action of Judge O'Gorman, of


[043-061]the Superior Court, in refusing naturalization papers to an applicant


[043-062]because he had not read the Constitution of the United States?


[043-063]_Answer_.  I did.  The United States Constitution is a very important


[043-064]document, a good, sound document, but it is talked about a great


[043-065]deal more than it is read.  I'll venture that you may commence at


[043-066]the Battery to interview merchants and other business men about


[043-067]the Constitution and you will talk with a hundred before you will


[043-068]find one who has ever read it.


[043-069]--_New York Herald_, August 8, 1886.


[043-070]THE LABOR QUESTION.


[043-071]_Question_.  What is your remedy, Colonel, for the labor troubles


[043-072]of the day?


[043-073]_Answer_.  One remedy is this:  I should like to see the laboring


[043-074]men succeed.  I should like to see them have a majority in Congress


[043-075]and with a President of their own.  I should like to see this so


[043-076]that they could satisfy themselves how little, after all, can be


[043-077]accomplished by legislation.  The moment responsibility should


[043-078]touch their shoulders they would become conservative.  They would


[043-079]find that making a living in this world is an individual affair,


[043-080]and that each man must look out for himself.  They would soon find


[043-081]that the Government cannot take care of the people.  The people


[043-082]must support the Government.  Everything cannot be regulated by


[043-083]law.  The factors entering into this problem are substantially


[043-084]infinite and beyond the intellectual grasp of any human being.


[043-085]Perhaps nothing in the world will convince the laboring man how


[043-086]little can be accomplished by law until there is opportunity of


[043-087]trying.  To discuss the question will do good, so I am in favor of


[043-088]its discussion.  To give the workingmen a trial will do good, so


[043-089]I am in favor of giving them a trial.


[043-090]_Question_.  But you have not answered my question:  I asked you


[043-091]what could be done, and you have told me what could not be done.


[043-092]Now, is there not some better organization of society that will


[043-093]help in this trouble?


[043-094]_Answer_.  Undoubtedly.  Unless humanity is a failure, society will


[043-095]improve from year to year and from age to age.  There will be, as


[043-096]the years go by, less want, less injustice, and the gifts of nature


[043-097]will be more equally divided, but there will never come a time when


[043-098]the weak can do as much as the strong, or when the mentally weak


[043-099]can accomplish as much as the intellectually strong.  There will


[043-100]forever be inequality in society; but, in my judgment, the time


[043-101]will come when an honest, industrious person need not want.  In my


[043-102]judgment, that will come, not through governmental control, not


[043-103]through governmental slavery, not through what is called Socialism,


[043-104]but through liberty and through individuality.  I can conceive of


[043-105]no greater slavery than to have everything done by the Government.


[043-106]I want free scope given to individual effort.  In time some things


[043-107]that governments have done will be removed.  The creation of a


[043-108]nobility, the giving of vast rights to corporations, and the


[043-109]bestowment of privileges on the few will be done away with.  In


[043-110]other words, governmental interference will cease and man will be


[043-111]left more to himself.  The future will not do away with want by


[043-112]charity, which generally creates more want than it alleviates, but


[043-113]by justice and intelligence.  Shakespeare says, "There is no darkness


[043-114]but ignorance," and it might be added that ignorance is the mother


[043-115]of most suffering.


[043-116]--_The Enquirer_, Cincinnati, Ohio, September 30, 1886.


[043-117]RAILROADS AND POLITICS.


[043-118]_Question_.  You are intimately acquainted with the great railroad


[043-119]managers and the great railroad systems, and what do you think is


[043-120]the great need of the railways to-day?


[043-121]_Answer_.  The great need of the railroads to-day is more business,


[043-122]more cars, better equipments, better pay for the men and less


[043-123]gambling in Wall Street.


[043-124]_Question_.  Is it your experience that public men usually ride on


[043-125]passes?


[043-126]_Answer_.  Yes, whenever they can get them.  Passes are for the


[043-127]rich.  Only those are expected to pay who can scarcely afford it.


[043-128]Nothing shortens a journey, nothing makes the road as smooth,


[043-129]nothing keeps down the dust and keeps out the smoke like a pass.


[043-130]_Question_.  Don't you think that the pass system is an injustice


[043-131]--that is, that ordinary travelers are taxed for the man who rides


[043-132]on a pass?


[043-133]_Answer_.  Certainly, those who pay, pay for those who do not.


[043-134]This is one of the misfortunes of the obscure.  It is so with


[043-135]everything.  The big fish live on the little ones.


[043-136]_Question_.  Are not parallel railroads an evil?


[043-137]_Answer_.  No, unless they are too near together.  Competition does


[043-138]some good and some harm, but it must exist.  All these things must


[043-139]be left to take care of themselves.  If the Government interferes


[043-140]it is at the expense of the manhood and liberty of the people.


[043-141]_Question_.  But wouldn't it be better for the people if the


[043-142]railroads were managed by the Government as is the Post-Office?


[043-143]_Answer_.  No, everything that individual can do should be left to


[043-144]them.  If the Government takes charge of the people they become


[043-145]weak and helpless.  The people should take charge of the Government.


[043-146]Give the folks a chance.


[043-147]_Question_.  In the next presidential contest what will be the main


[043-148]issue?


[043-149]_Answer_.  The Maine issue!


[043-150]_Question_.  Would you again refuse to take the stump for Mr. Blaine


[043-151]if he should be renominated, and if so, why?


[043-152]_Answer_.  I do not expect to take the stump for anybody.  Mr.


[043-153]Blaine is probably a candidate, and if he is nominated there will


[043-154]be plenty of people on the stump--or fence--or up a tree or somewhere


[043-155]in the woods.


[043-156]_Question_.  What are the most glaring mistakes of Cleveland's


[043-157]administration?


[043-158]_Answer_.  First, accepting the nomination.  Second, taking the


[043-159]oath of office.  Third, not resigning.


[043-160]--_Times Star_, Cincinnati, September 30, 1886.


[043-161]PROHIBITION.


[043-162]_Question_.  How much importance do you attach to the present


[043-163]prohibition movement?


[043-164]_Answer_.  No particular importance.  I am opposed to prohibition


[043-165]and always have been, and hope always to be.  I do not want the


[043-166]Legislature to interfere in these matters.  I do not believe that


[043-167]the people can be made temperate by law.  Men and women are not


[043-168]made great and good by the law.  There is no good in the world that


[043-169]cannot be abused.  Prohibition fills the world with spies and


[043-170]tattlers, and, besides that, where a majority of the people are


[043-171]not in favor of it the law will not be enforced; and where a majority


[043-172]of the people are in favor of it there is not much need of the law.


[043-173]Where a majority are against it, juries will violate their oath,


[043-174]and witnesses will get around the truth, and the result is


[043-175]demoralization.  Take wine and malt liquors out of the world and


[043-176]we shall lose a vast deal of good fellowship; the world would lose


[043-177]more than it would gain.  There is a certain sociability about wine


[043-178]that I should hate to have taken from the earth.  Strong liquors


[043-179]the folks had better let alone.  If prohibition succeeds, and wines


[043-180]and malt liquors go, the next thing will be to take tobacco away,


[043-181]and the next thing all other pleasures, until prayer meetings will


[043-182]be the only places of enjoyment.


[043-183]_Question_.  Do you care to say who your choice is for Republican


[043-184]nominee for President in 1888?


[043-185]_Answer_.  I now promise that I will answer this question either


[043-186]in May or June, 1888.  At present my choice is not fixed, and is


[043-187]liable to change at any moment, and I need to leave it free, so


[043-188]that it can change from time to time as the circumstances change.


[043-189]I will, however, tell you privately that I think it will probably


[043-190]be a new man, somebody on whom the Republicans can unite.  I have


[043-191]made a good many inquiries myself to find out who this man is to


[043-192]be, but in every instance the answer has been determined by the


[043-193]location in which the gentleman lived who gave the answer.  Let us


[043-194]wait.
