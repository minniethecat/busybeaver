[082-001]Whenever a religion has been founded among barbarians and ignorant


[082-002]people, the founder has appealed to miracle as a kind of credential


[082-003]--as an evidence that he is in partnership with some higher power.


[082-004]The credulity of savagery made this easy.  But at last we have


[082-005]discovered that there is no necessary relation between the miraculous


[082-006]and the moral.  Whenever a man's reason is developed to that point


[082-007]that he sees the reasonableness of a thing, he needs no miracle to


[082-008]convince him.  It is only ignorance or cunning that appeals to the


[082-009]miraculous.


[082-010]There is another thing, and that is this:  Truth relies upon itself


[082-011]--that is to say, upon the perceived relation between itself and


[082-012]all other truths.  If you tell the facts, you need not appeal to


[082-013]a miracle.  It is only a mistake or a falsehood, that needs to be


[082-014]propped and buttressed by wonders and miracles.


[082-015]_Question_.  What is your explanation of the miracles referred to


[082-016]in the Old and New Testaments?


[082-017]_Answer_.  In the first place, a miracle cannot be explained.  If


[082-018]it is a real miracle, there is no explanation.  If it can be


[082-019]explained, then the miracle disappears, and the thing was done in


[082-020]accordance with the facts and forces of nature.


[082-021]In a time when not one it may be in thousands could read or write,


[082-022]when language was rude, and when the signs by which thoughts were


[082-023]conveyed were few and inadequate, it was very easy to make mistakes,


[082-024]and nothing is more natural than for a mistake to grow into a


[082-025]miracle.  In an ignorant age, history for the most part depended


[082-026]upon memory.  It was handed down from the old in their dotage, to


[082-027]the young without judgment.  The old always thought that the early


[082-028]days were wonderful--that the world was wearing out because they


[082-029]were.  The past looked at through the haze of memory, became


[082-030]exaggerated, gigantic.  Their fathers were stronger than they, and


[082-031]their grandfathers far superior to their fathers, and so on until


[082-032]they reached men who had the habit of living about a thousand years.


[082-033]In my judgment, everything in the Old Testament contrary to the


[082-034]experience of the civilized world, is false.  I do not say that


[082-035]those who told the stories knew that they were false, or that those


[082-036]who wrote them suspected that they were not true.  Thousands and


[082-037]thousands of lies are told by honest stupidity and believed by


[082-038]innocent credulity.  Then again, cunning takes advantage of ignorance,


[082-039]and so far as I know, though all the history of the world a good


[082-040]many people have endeavored to make a living without work.


[082-041]I am perfectly convinced of the integrity of nature--that the


[082-042]elements are eternally the same--that the chemical affinities and


[082-043]hatreds know no shadow of turning--that just so many atoms of one


[082-044]kind combine with so many atoms of another, and that the relative


[082-045]numbers have never changed and never will change.  I am satisfied


[082-046]that the attraction of gravitation is a permanent institution; that


[082-047]the laws of motion have been the same that they forever will be.


[082-048]There is no chance, there is no caprice.  Behind every effect is


[082-049]a cause, and every effect must in its turn become a cause, and only


[082-050]that is produced which a cause of necessity produces.


[082-051]_Question_.  What do you think of Madame Blavatsky and her school


[082-052]of Theosophists?  Do you believe Madame Blavatsky does or has done


[082-053]the wonderful things related of her?  Have you seen or known of


[082-054]any Theosophical or esoteric marvels?


[082-055]_Answer_.  I think wonders are about the same in this country that


[082-056]they are in India, and nothing appears more likely to me simply


[082-057]because it is surrounded with the mist of antiquity.  In my judgment,


[082-058]Madame Blavatsky has never done any wonderful things--that is to


[082-059]say, anything not in perfect accordance with the facts of nature.


[082-060]I know nothing of esoteric marvels.  In one sense, everything that


[082-061]exists is a marvel, and the probability is that if we knew the


[082-062]history of one grain of sand we would know the history of the


[082-063]universe.  I regard the universe as a unit.  Everything that happens


[082-064]is only a different aspect of that unit.  There is no room for the


[082-065]marvelous--there is no space in which it can operate--there is no


[082-066]fulcrum for its lever.  The universe is already occupied with the


[082-067]natural.  The ground is all taken.


[082-068]It may be that all these people are perfectly honest, and imagine


[082-069]that they have had wonderful experiences.  I know but little of


[082-070]the Theosophists--but little of the Spiritualists.  It has always


[082-071]seemed to me that the messages received by Spiritualists are


[082-072]remarkably unimportant--that they tell us but little about the


[082-073]other world, and just as little about this--that if all the messages


[082-074]supposed to have come from angelic lips, or spiritual lips, were


[082-075]destroyed, certainly the literature of the world would lose but


[082-076]little.  Some of these people are exceedingly intelligent, and


[082-077]whenever they say any good thing, I imagine that it was produced


[082-078]in their brain, and that it came from no other world.  I have no


[082-079]right to pass upon their honesty.  Most of them may be sincere.


[082-080]It may be that all the founders of religions have really supposed


[082-081]themselves to be inspired--believed that they held conversations


[082-082]with angels and Gods.  It seems to be easy for some people to get


[082-083]in such a frame of mind that their thoughts become realities, their


[082-084]dreams substances, and their very hopes palpable.


[082-085]Personally, I have no sort of confidence in these messages from


[082-086]the other world.  There may be mesmeric forces--there may be an


[082-087]odic force.  It may be that some people can tell of what another


[082-088]is thinking.  I have seen no such people--at least I am not acquainted


[082-089]with them--and my own opinion is that no such persons exist.


[082-090]_Question_.  Do you believe the spirits of the dead come back to


[082-091]earth?


[082-092]_Answer_.  I do not.  I do not say that the spirits do not come


[082-093]back.  I simply say that I know nothing on the subject.  I do not


[082-094]believe in such spirits, simply for the reason that I have no


[082-095]evidence upon which to base such a belief.  I do not say there are


[082-096]no such spirits, for the reason that my knowledge is limited, and


[082-097]I know of no way of demonstrating the non-existence of spirits.


[082-098]It may be that man lives forever, and it may be that what we call


[082-099]life ends with what we call death.  I have had no experience beyond


[082-100]the grave, and very little back of birth.  Consequently, I cannot


[082-101]say that I have a belief on this subject.  I can simply say that


[082-102]I have no knowledge on this subject, and know of no fact in nature


[082-103]that I would use as the corner-stone of a belief.


[082-104]_Question_.  Do you believe in the resurrection of the body?


[082-105]_Answer_.  My answer to that is about the same as to the other


[082-106]question.  I do not believe in the resurrection of the body.  It


[082-107]seems to me an exceedingly absurd belief--and yet I do not know.


[082-108]I am told, and I suppose I believe, that the atoms that are in me


[082-109]have been in many other people, and in many other forms of life,


[082-110]and I suppose at death the atoms forming my body go back to the


[082-111]earth and are used in countless forms.  These facts, or what I


[082-112]suppose to be facts, render a belief in the resurrection of the


[082-113]body impossible to me.


[082-114]We get atoms to support our body from what we eat.  Now, if a


[082-115]cannibal should eat a missionary, and certain atoms belonging to


[082-116]the missionary should be used by the cannibal in his body, and the


[082-117]cannibal should then die while the atoms of the missionary formed


[082-118]part of his flesh, to whom would these atoms belong in the morning


[082-119]of the resurrection?


[082-120]Then again, science teaches us that there is a kind of balance


[082-121]between animal and vegetable life, and that probably all men and


[082-122]all animals have been trees, and all trees have been animals; so


[082-123]that the probability is that the atoms that are now in us have


[082-124]been, as I said in the first place, in millions of other people.


[082-125]Now, if this be so, there cannot be atoms enough in the morning of


[082-126]the resurrection, because, if the atoms are given to the first men,


[082-127]that belonged to the first men when they died, there will certainly


[082-128]be no atoms for the last men.


[082-129]Consequently, I am compelled to say that I do not believe in the


[082-130]resurrection of the body.*


[082-131][* From notes found among Colonel Ingersoll's papers.]


[082-132]TOLSTOY AND LITERATURE.


[082-133]_Question_.  What is your opinion of Count Leo Tolstoy?


[082-134]_Answer_.  I have read Tolstoy.  He is a curious mixture of simplicity


[082-135]and philosophy.  He seems to have been carried away by his conception


[082-136]of religion.  He is a non-resistant to such a degree that he asserts


[082-137]that he would not, if attacked, use violence to preserve his own


[082-138]life or the life of a child.  Upon this question he is undoubtedly


[082-139]insane.


[082-140]So he is trying to live the life of a peasant and doing without


[082-141]the comforts of life!  This is not progress.  Civilization should


[082-142]not endeavor to bring about equality by making the rich poor or


[082-143]the comfortable miserable.  This will not add to the pleasures of


[082-144]the rich, neither will it feed the hungry, not clothe the naked.


[082-145]The civilized wealthy should endeavor to help the needy, and help


[082-146]them in a sensible way, not through charity, but through industry;


[082-147]through giving them opportunities to take care of themselves.  I


[082-148]do not believe in the equality that is to be reached by pulling


[082-149]the successful down, but I do believe in civilization that tends


[082-150]to raise the fallen and assists those in need.


[082-151]Should we all follow Tolstoy's example and live according to his


[082-152]philosophy the world would go back to barbarism; art would be lost;


[082-153]that which elevates and refines would be destroyed; the voice of


[082-154]music would become silent, and man would be satisfied with a rag,


[082-155]a hut, a crust.  We do not want the equality of savages.


[082-156]No, in civilization there must be differences, because there is a


[082-157]constant movement forward.  The human race cannot advance in line.


[082-158]There will be pioneers, there will be the great army, and there


[082-159]will be countless stragglers.  It is not necessary for the whole


[082-160]army to go back to the stragglers, it is better that the army should


[082-161]march forward toward the pioneers.


[082-162]It may be that the sale of Tolstoy's works is on the increase in


[082-163]America, but certainly the principles of Tolstoy are gaining no


[082-164]foothold here.  We are not a nation of non-resistants.  We believe


[082-165]in defending our homes.  Nothing can exceed the insanity of non-


[082-166]resistance.  This doctrine leaves virtue naked and clothes vice in


[082-167]armor; it gives every weapon to the wrong and takes every shield


[082-168]from the right.  I believe that goodness has the right of self-


[082-169]defence.  As a matter of fact, vice should be left naked and virtue


[082-170]should have all the weapons.  The good should not be a flock of


[082-171]sheep at the mercy of every wolf.  So, I do not accept Tolstoy's


[082-172]theory of equality as a sensible solution of the labor problem.


[082-173]The hope of this world is that men will become civilized to that


[082-174]degree that they cannot be happy while they know that thousands of


[082-175]their fellow-men are miserable.


[082-176]The time will come when the man who dwells in a palace will not be


[082-177]happy if Want sits upon the steps at his door.  No matter how well


[082-178]he is clothed himself he will not enjoy his robes if he sees others


[082-179]in rags, and the time will come when the intellect of this world


[082-180]will be directed by the heart of this world, and when men of genius


[082-181]and power will do what they can for the benefit of their fellow-


[082-182]men.  All this is to come through civilization, through experience.
