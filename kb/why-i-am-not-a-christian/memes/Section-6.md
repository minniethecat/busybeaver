[005-001]I know very well that the Catholic Church claimed during the Dark


[005-002]Ages, and still claims, that references had been made to the gospels


[005-003]by persons living in the first, second, and third centuries; but


[005-004]I believe such manuscripts were manufactured by the Catholic Church.


[005-005]For many years in Europe there was not one person in twenty thousand


[005-006]who could read and write.  During that time the church had in its


[005-007]keeping the literature of our world.  They interpolated as they


[005-008]pleased.  They created.  They destroyed.  In other words, they did


[005-009]whatever in their opinion was necessary to substantiate the faith.


[005-010]The gentlemen who saw fit to reply did not answer the question,


[005-011]and I again call upon the clergy to explain to the people why, if


[005-012]salvation depends upon belief on the Lord Jesus Christ, Matthew


[005-013]didn't mention it.  Some one has said that Christ didn't make known


[005-014]this doctrine of salvation by belief or faith until after his


[005-015]resurrection.  Certainly none of the gospels were written until


[005-016]after his resurrection; and if he made that doctrine known after


[005-017]his resurrection, and before his ascension, it should have been


[005-018]in Matthew, Mark, and Luke, as well as in John.


[005-019]The replies of the clergy show that they have not investigated the


[005-020]subject; that they are not well acquainted with the New Testament.


[005-021]In other words, they have not read it except with the regulation


[005-022]theological bias.


[005-023]There is one thing I wish to correct here.  In an editorial in the


[005-024]_Tribune_ it was stated that I had admitted that Christ was beyond


[005-025]and above Buddha, Zoroaster, Confucius, and others.  I did not say


[005-026]so.  Another point was made against me, and those who made it seemed


[005-027]to think it was a good one.  In my lecture I asked why it was that


[005-028]the disciples of Christ wrote in Greek, whereas, if fact, they


[005-029]understood only Hebrew.  It is now claimed that Greek was the


[005-030]language of Jerusalem at that time; that Hebrew had fallen into


[005-031]disuse; that no one understood it except the literati and the highly


[005-032]educated.  If I fell into an error upon this point it was because


[005-033]I relied upon the New Testament.  I find in the twenty-first chapter


[005-034]of the Acts an account of Paul having been mobbed in the city of


[005-035]Jerusalem; that he was protected by a chief captain and some


[005-036]soldiers; that, while upon the stairs of the castle to which he


[005-037]was being taken for protection, he obtained leave from the captain


[005-038]to speak unto the people.  In the fortieth verse of that chapter


[005-039]I find the following:


[005-040]"And when he had given him license, Paul stood on the stairs and


[005-041]beckoned with the hand unto the people.  And when there was made


[005-042]a great silence, he spake unto them in the Hebrew tongue, saying,"


[005-043]And then follows the speech of Paul, wherein he gives an account of


[005-044]his conversion.  It seems a little curious to me that Paul, for


[005-045]the purpose of quieting a mob, would speak to that mob in an unknown


[005-046]language.  If I were mobbed in the city of Chicago, and wished to


[005-047]defend myself with an explanation, I certainly would not make that


[005-048]explanation in Choctaw, even if I understood that tongue.  My


[005-049]present opinion is that I would speak in English; and the reason


[005-050]I would speak in English is because that language is generally


[005-051]understood in this city, and so I conclude from the account in the


[005-052]twenty-first chapter of the Acts that Hebrew was the language of


[005-053]Jerusalem at that time, or Paul would not have addressed the mob


[005-054]in that tongue.


[005-055]_Question_.  Did you read Mr. Courtney's answer?


[005-056]_Answer_.  I read what Mr. Courtney read from others, and think


[005-057]some of his quotations very good; and have no doubt that the authors


[005-058]will feel complimented by being quoted.  There certainly is no need


[005-059]of my answering Dr. Courtney; sometime I may answer the French


[005-060]gentlemen from whom he quoted.


[005-061]_Question_.  But what about there being "belief" in Matthew?


[005-062]_Answer_.  Mr. Courtney says that certain people were cured of


[005-063]diseases on account of faith.  Admitting that mumps, measles, and


[005-064]whooping-cough could be cured in that way, there is not even a


[005-065]suggestion that salvation depended upon a like faith.  I think he


[005-066]can hardly afford to rely upon the miracles of the New Testament


[005-067]to prove his doctrine.  There is one instance in which a miracle


[005-068]was performed by Christ without his knowledge; and I hardly think


[005-069]that even Mr. Courtney would insist that any faith could have been


[005-070]great enough for that.  The fact is, I believe that all these


[005-071]miracles were ascribed to Christ long after his death, and that


[005-072]Christ never, at any time or place, pretended to have any supernatural


[005-073]power whatever.  Neither do I believe that he claimed any supernatural


[005-074]origin.  He claimed simply to be a man; no less, no more.  I do


[005-075]not believe Mr. Courtney is satisfied with his own reply.


[005-076]_Question_.  And now as to Prof. Swing?


[005-077]_Answer_.  Mr. Swing has been out of the orthodox church so long


[005-078]that he seems to have forgotten the reasons for which he left it.


[005-079]I do not believe there is an orthodox minister in the city of


[005-080]Chicago who will agree with Mr. Swing that salvation by faith is


[005-081]no longer preached.  Prof. Swing seems to think it of no importance


[005-082]who wrote the gospel of Matthew.  In this I agree with him.  Judging


[005-083]from what he said there is hardly difference enough of opinion


[005-084]between us to justify a reply on his part.  He, however, makes one


[005-085]mistake.  I did not in the lecture say one word about tearing down


[005-086]churches.  I have no objection to people building all the churches


[005-087]they wish.  While I admit it is a pretty sight to see children on


[005-088]a morning in June going through the fields to the country church,


[005-089]I still insist that the beauty of that sight does not answer the


[005-090]question how it is that Matthew forgot to say anything about


[005-091]salvation through Christ.  Prof. Swing is a man of poetic temperament,


[005-092]but this is not a poetic question.


[005-093]_Question_.  How did the card of Dr. Thomas strike you?


[005-094]_Answer_.  I think the reply of Dr. Thomas is in the best possible


[005-095]spirit.  I regard him to-day as the best intellect in the Methodist


[005-096]denomination.  He seems to have what is generally understood as a


[005-097]Christian spirit.  He has always treated me with perfect fairness,


[005-098]and I should have said long ago many grateful things, had I not


[005-099]feared I might hurt him with his own people.  He seems to be by


[005-100]nature a perfectly fair man; and I know of no man in the United


[005-101]States for whom I have a profounder respect.  Of course, I don't


[005-102]agree with Dr. Thomas.  I think in many things he is mistaken.


[005-103]But I believe him to be perfectly sincere.  There is one trouble


[005-104]about him--he is growing; and this fact will no doubt give great


[005-105]trouble to many of his brethren.  Certain Methodist hazel-brush


[005-106]feel a little uneasy in the shadow of this oak.  To see the difference


[005-107]between him and some others, all that is necessary is to read his


[005-108]reply, and then read the remarks made at the Methodist ministers'


[005-109]meeting on the Monday following.  Compared with Dr. Thomas, they


[005-110]are as puddles by the sea.  There is the same difference that there


[005-111]is between sewers and rivers, cesspools and springs.


[005-112]_Question_.  What have you to say to the remarks of the Rev. Dr.


[005-113]Jewett before the Methodist ministers' meeting?


[005-114]_Answer_.  I think Dr. Jewett is extremely foolish.  I did not say


[005-115]that I would commence suit against a minister for libel.  I can


[005-116]hardly conceive of a proceeding that would be less liable to produce


[005-117]a dividend.  The fact about it is, that the Rev. Mr. Jewett seems


[005-118]to think anything true that he hears against me.  Mr. Jewett is


[005-119]probably ashamed of what he said by this time.  He must have known


[005-120]it to be entirely false.  It seems to me by this time even the most


[005-121]bigoted should lose their confidence in falsehood.  Of course there


[005-122]are times when a falsehood well told bridges over quite a difficulty,


[005-123]but in the long run you had better tell the truth, even if you swim


[005-124]the creek.  I am astonished that these ministers were willing to


[005-125]exhibit their wounds to the world.  I supposed of course I would


[005-126]hit some, but I had no idea of wounding so many.


[005-127]_Question_.  Mr. Crafts stated that you were in the habit of swearing


[005-128]in company and before your family?


[005-129]_Answer_.  I often swear.  In other words, I take the name of God


[005-130]in vain; that is to say, I take it without any practical thing


[005-131]resulting from it, and in that sense I think most ministers are


[005-132]guilty of the same thing.  I heard an old story of a clergyman who


[005-133]rebuked a neighbor for swearing, to whom the neighbor replied, "You


[005-134]pray and I swear, but as a matter of fact neither of us means


[005-135]anything by it."  As to the charge that I am in the habit of using


[005-136]indecent language in my family, no reply is needed.  I am willing


[005-137]to leave that question to the people who know us both.  Mr. Crafts


[005-138]says he was told this by a lady.  This cannot by any possibility


[005-139]be true, for no lady will tell a falsehood.  Besides, if this woman


[005-140]of whom he speaks was a lady, how did she happen to stay where


[005-141]obscene language was being used?  No lady ever told Mr. Crafts any


[005-142]such thing.  It may be that a lady did tell him that I used profane


[005-143]language.  I admit that I have not always spoken of the Devil in


[005-144]a respectful way; that I have sometimes referred to his residence


[005-145]when it was not a necessary part of the conversation, and that a


[005-146]divers times I have used a good deal of the terminology of the


[005-147]theologian when the exact words of the scientist might have done


[005-148]as well.  But if by swearing is meant the use of God's name in


[005-149]vain, there are very few preachers who do not swear more than I


[005-150]do, if by "in vain" is meant without any practical result.  I leave


[005-151]Mr. Crafts to cultivate the acquaintance of the unknown lady,


[005-152]knowing as I do, that after they have talked this matter over again


[005-153]they will find that both have been mistaken.


[005-154]I sincerely regret that clergymen who really believe that an infinite


[005-155]God is on their side think it necessary to resort to such things


[005-156]to defeat one man.  According to their idea, God is against me,


[005-157]and they ought to have confidence in this infinite wisdom and


[005-158]strength to suppose that he could dispose of one man, even if they


[005-159]failed to say a word against me.  Had you not asked me I should


[005-160]have said nothing to you on these topics.  Such charges cannot hurt


[005-161]me.  I do not believe it possible for such men to injure me.  No


[005-162]one believes what they say, and the testimony of such clergymen


[005-163]against an Infidel is no longer considered of value.  I believe it


[005-164]was Goethe who said, "I always know that I am traveling when I hear


[005-165]the dogs bark."


[005-166]_Question_.  Are you going to make a formal reply to their sermons?


[005-167]_Answer_.  Not unless something better is done than has been.  Of


[005-168]course, I don't know what another Sabbath may bring forth.  I am


[005-169]waiting.  But of one thing I feel perfectly assured; that no man


[005-170]in the United States, or in the world, can account for the fact,


[005-171]if we are to be saved only by faith in Christ, that Matthew forgot


[005-172]it, that Luke said nothing about it, and that Mark never mentioned


[005-173]it except in two passages written by _another_ person.  Until that


[005-174]is answered, as one grave-digger says to the other in "Hamlet," I


[005-175]shall say, "Ay, tell me that and unyoke."  In the meantime I wish


[005-176]to keep on the best terms with all parties concerned.  I cannot


[005-177]see why my forgiving spirit fails to gain their sincere praise.
