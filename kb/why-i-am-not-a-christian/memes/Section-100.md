[099-001]There is certainly nothing in the play calculated to please the


[099-002]pulpit.  There is a clergyman who is pious and heartless.  John


[099-003]Storm is the only Christian, and he is crazy.  When Glory accepts


[099-004]him at last, you not only feel, but you know she has acted the


[099-005]fool.  The lord in the piece is a dog, and the real gentleman is


[099-006]the chap that runs the music hall.  How the play can please the


[099-007]pulpit I do not see.  Storm's whole career is a failure.  His


[099-008]followers turn on him like wild beasts.  His religion is a divine


[099-009]and diabolical dream.  With him murder is one of the means of


[099-010]salvation.  Mr. Caine has struck Christianity a stinging blow


[099-011]between the eyes.  He has put two preachers on the stage, one a


[099-012]heartless hypocrite and the other a madman.  Certainly I am not


[099-013]prejudiced in favor of Christianity, and yet I enjoyed the play.


[099-014]If Mr. Caine says he is trying to bring the stage and the pulpit


[099-015]together, then he is a humorist, with the humor of Rabelais.


[099-016]_Question_.  What do recent exhibitions in this city, of scenes


[099-017]from the life of Christ, indicate with regard to the tendencies of


[099-018]modern art?


[099-019]_Answer_.  Nothing.  Some artists love the sombre, the melancholy,


[099-020]the hopeless.  They enjoy painting the bowed form, the tear-filled


[099-021]eyes.  To them grief is a festival.  There are people who find


[099-022]pleasure in funerals.  They love to watch the mourners.  The falling


[099-023]clods make music.  They love the silence, the heavy odors, the


[099-024]sorrowful hymns and the preacher's remarks.  The feelings of such


[099-025]people do not indicate the general trend of the human mind.  Even


[099-026]a poor artist may hope for success if he represents something in


[099-027]which many millions are deeply interested, around which their


[099-028]emotions cling like vines.  A man need not be an orator to make a


[099-029]patriotic speech, a speech that flatters his audience.  So, an


[099-030]artist need not be great in order to satisfy, if his subject appeals


[099-031]to the prejudice of those who look at his pictures.


[099-032]I have never seen a good painting of Christ.  All the Christs that


[099-033]I have seen lack strength and character.  They look weak and


[099-034]despairing.  They are all unhealthy.  They have the attitude of


[099-035]apology, the sickly smile of non-resistance.  I have never seen an


[099-036]heroic, serene and triumphant Christ.  To tell the truth, I never


[099-037]saw a great religious picture.  They lack sincerity.  All the angels


[099-038]look almost idiotic.  In their eyes is no thought, only the innocence


[099-039]of ignorance.


[099-040]I think that art is leaving the celestial, the angelic, and is


[099-041]getting in love with the natural, the human.  Troyon put more genius


[099-042]in the representation of cattle than Angelo and Raphael did in


[099-043]angels.  No picture has been painted of heaven that is as beautiful


[099-044]as a landscape by Corot.  The aim of art is to represent the


[099-045]realities, the highest and noblest, the most beautiful.  The Greeks


[099-046]did not try to make men like gods, but they made gods like men.


[099-047]So that great artists of our day go to nature.


[099-048]_Question_.  Is it not strange that, with one exception, the most


[099-049]notable operas written since Wagner are by Italian composers instead


[099-050]of German?


[099-051]_Answer_.  For many years German musicians insisted that Wagner


[099-052]was not a composer.  They declared that he produced only a succession


[099-053]of discordant noises.  I account for this by the fact that the


[099-054]music of Wagner was not German.  His countrymen could not understand


[099-055]it.  They had to be educated.  There was no orchestra in Germany


[099-056]that could really play "Tristan and Isolde."  Its eloquence, its


[099-057]pathos, its shoreless passion was beyond them.  There is no reason


[099-058]to suppose that Germany is to produce another Wagner.  Is England


[099-059]expected to give us another Shakespeare?


[099-060]--_The Sun_, New York, March 19, 1899.


[099-061]CAPITAL PUNISHMENT AND THE WHIPPING-POST.


[099-062]_Question_.  What do you think of Governor Roosevelt's decision in


[099-063]the case of Mrs. Place?


[099-064]_Answer_.  I think the refusal of Governor Roosevelt to commute


[099-065]the sentence of Mrs. Place is a disgrace to the State.  What a


[099-066]spectacle of man killing a woman--taking a poor, pallid, frightened


[099-067]woman, strapping her to a chair and then arranging the apparatus


[099-068]so she can be shocked to death.  Many call this a Christian country.


[099-069]A good many people who believe in hell would naturally feel it


[099-070]their duty to kill a wretched, insane woman.


[099-071]Society has a right to protect itself, but this can be done by


[099-072]imprisonment, and it is more humane to put a criminal in a cell


[099-073]than in a grave.  Capital punishment degrades and hardens a


[099-074]community and it is a work of savagery.  It is savagery.  Capital


[099-075]punishment does not prevent murder, but sets an example--an example


[099-076]by the State--that is followed by its citizens.  The State murders


[099-077]its enemies and the citizen murders his.  Any punishment that


[099-078]degrades the punished, must necessarily degrade the one inflicting


[099-079]the punishment.  No punishment should be inflicted by a human being


[099-080]that could not be inflicted by a gentleman.


[099-081]For instance, take the whipping-post.  Some people are in favor of


[099-082]flogging because they say that some offences are of such a frightful


[099-083]nature that flogging is the only punishment.  They forget that the


[099-084]punishment must be inflicted by somebody, and that somebody is a


[099-085]low and contemptible cur.  I understand that John G. Shortall,


[099-086]president of the Humane Society of Illinois, has had a bill introduced


[099-087]into the Legislature of the State for the establishment of the


[099-088]whipping-post.


[099-089]The shadow of that post would disgrace and darken the whole State.


[099-090]Nothing could be more infamous, and yet this man is president of


[099-091]the Humane Society.  Now, the question arises, what is humane about


[099-092]this society?  Certainly not its president.  Undoubtedly he is


[099-093]sincere.  Certainly no man would take that position unless he was


[099-094]sincere.  Nobody deliberately pretends to be bad, but the idea of


[099-095]his being president of the Humane Society is simply preposterous.


[099-096]With his idea about the whipping-post he might join a society of


[099-097]hyenas for the cultivation of ferocity, for certainly nothing short


[099-098]of that would do justice to his bill.  I have too much confidence


[099-099]in the legislators of that State, and maybe my confidence rests in


[099-100]the fact that I do not know them, to think that the passage of such


[099-101]a bill is possible.  If it were passed I think I would be justified


[099-102]in using the language of the old Marylander, who said, "I have


[099-103]lived in Maryland fifty years, but I have never counted them, and


[099-104]my hope is, that God won't."


[099-105]_Question_.  What did you think of the late Joseph Medill?


[099-106]_Answer_.  I was not very well acquainted with Mr. Medill.  I had


[099-107]a good many conversations with him, and I was quite familiar with


[099-108]his work.  I regard him as the greatest editor of the Northwestern


[099-109]States and I am not sure that there was a greater one in the country.


[099-110]He was one of the builders of the Republican party.  He was on the


[099-111]right side of the great question of Liberty.  He was a man of strong


[099-112]likes and I may say dislikes.  He never surrendered his personality.


[099-113]The atom called Joseph Medill was never lost in the aggregation


[099-114]known as the Republican party.  He was true to that party when it


[099-115]was true to him.  As a rule he traveled a road of his own and he


[099-116]never seemed to have any doubt about where the road led.  I think


[099-117]that he was an exceedingly useful man.  I think the only true


[099-118]religion is usefulness.  He was a very strong writer, and when


[099-119]touched by friendship for a man, or a cause, he occasionally wrote


[099-120]very great paragraphs, and paragraphs full of force and most


[099-121]admirably expressed.


[099-122]--_The Tribune_, Chicago, March 19, 1899.


[099-123]EXPANSION AND TRUSTS.*


[099-124][* This was Colonel Ingersoll's last interview.]


[099-125]I am an expansionist.  The country has the land hunger and expansion


[099-126]is popular.  I want all we can honestly get.


[099-127]But I do not want the Philippines unless the Filipinos want us,


[099-128]and I feel exactly the same about the Cubans.


[099-129]We paid twenty millions of dollars to Spain for the Philippine


[099-130]Islands, and we knew that Spain had no title to them.


[099-131]The question with me is not one of trade or convenience; it is a


[099-132]question of right or wrong.  I think the best patriot is the man


[099-133]who wants his country to do right.


[099-134]The Philippines would be a very valuable possession to us, in view


[099-135]of their proximity to China.  But, however desirable they may be,


[099-136]that cuts no figure.  We must do right.  We must act nobly toward


[099-137]the Filipinos, whether we get the islands or not.


[099-138]I would like to see peace between us and the Filipinos; peace


[099-139]honorable to both; peace based on reason instead of force.


[099-140]If control had been given to Dewey, if Miles had been sent to


[099-141]Manila, I do not believe that a shot would have been fired at the


[099-142]Filipinos, and that they would have welcomed the American flag.


[099-143]_Question_.  Although you are not in favor of taking the Philippines


[099-144]by force, how do you regard the administration in its conduct of


[099-145]the war?


[099-146]_Answer_.  They have made many mistakes at Washington, and they


[099-147]are still making many.  If it has been decided to conquer the


[099-148]Filipinos, then conquer them at once.  Let the struggle not be


[099-149]drawn out and the drops of blood multiplied.  The Republican party


[099-150]is being weakened by inaction at the Capital.  If the war is not


[099-151]ended shortly, the party in power will feel the evil effects at


[099-152]the presidential election.


[099-153]_Question_.  In what light do you regard the Philippines as an


[099-154]addition to the territory of the United States?


[099-155]_Answer_.  Probably in the future, and possibly in the near future,


[099-156]the value of the islands to this country could hardly be calculated.


[099-157]The division of China which is bound to come, will open a market


[099-158]of four hundred millions of people.  Naturally a possession close


[099-159]to the open doors of the East would be of an almost incalculable


[099-160]value to this country.


[099-161]It might perhaps take a long time to teach the Chinese that they


[099-162]need our products.  But suppose that the Chinese came to look upon


[099-163]wheat in the same light that other people look upon wheat and its


[099-164]product, bread?  What an immense amount of grain it would take to


[099-165]feed four hundred million hungry Chinamen!


[099-166]The same would be the case with the rest of our products.  So you


[099-167]will perhaps agree with me in my view of the immense value of the


[099-168]islands if they could but be obtained by honorable means.


[099-169]_Question_.  If the Democratic party makes anti-imperialism the


[099-170]prominent plank in its platform, what effect will it have on the


[099-171]party's chance for success?


[099-172]_Answer_.  Anti-imperialism, as the Democratic battle-cry, would


[099-173]greatly weaken a party already very weak.  It is the most unpopular


[099-174]issue of the day.  The people want expansion.  The country is


[099-175]infected with patriotic enthusiasm.  The party that tries to resist


[099-176]the tidal wave will be swept away.  Anybody who looks can see.


[099-177]Let a band at any of the summer resorts or at the suburban breathing


[099-178]spots play a patriotic air.  The listeners are electrified, and


[099-179]they rise and off go their hats when "The Star-Spangled Banner" is


[099-180]struck up.  Imperialism cannot be fought with success.


[099-181]_Question_.  Will the Democratic party have a strong issue in its


[099-182]anti-trust cry?


[099-183]_Answer_.  In my opinion, both parties will nail anti-trust planks


[099-184]in their platforms.  But this talk is all bosh with both parties.


[099-185]Neither one is honest in its cry against trusts.  The one making


[099-186]the more noise in this direction may get the votes of some unthinking


[099-187]persons, but every one who is capable of reading and digesting what


[099-188]he reads, knows full well that the leaders of neither party are


[099-189]sincere and honest in their demonstrations against the trusts.
