[064-001]_Question_.  What, in your opinion, are the best possible means to


[064-002]spread this gospel or religion of Secularism?


[064-003]_Answer_.  This can only be done by the cultivation of the mind--


[064-004]only through intelligence--because we are fighting only the monsters


[064-005]of the mind.  The phantoms whom we are endeavoring to destroy do


[064-006]not exist; they are all imaginary.  They live in that undeveloped


[064-007]or unexplored part of the mind that belongs to barbarism.


[064-008]I have sometimes thought that a certain portion of the mind is


[064-009]cultivated so that it rises above the surrounding faculties and is


[064-010]like some peak that has lifted itself above the clouds, while all


[064-011]the valleys below are dark or dim with mist and cloud.  It is in


[064-012]this valley-region, amid these mists, beneath these clouds, that


[064-013]these monsters and phantoms are born.  And there they will remain


[064-014]until the mind sheds light--until the brain is developed.


[064-015]One exceedingly important thing is to teach man that his mind has


[064-016]limitations; that there are walls that he cannot scale--that he


[064-017]cannot pierce, that he cannot dig under.  When a man finds the


[064-018]limitations of his own mind, he knows that other people's minds


[064-019]have limitations.  He, instead of believing what the priest says,


[064-020]he asks the priest questions.  In a few moments he finds that the


[064-021]priest has been drawing on his imagination for what is beyond the


[064-022]wall.  Consequently he finds that the priest knows no more than


[064-023]he, and it is impossible that he should know more than he.


[064-024]An ignorant man has not the slightest suspicion of what a superior


[064-025]man may do.  Consequently, he is liable to become the victim of


[064-026]the intelligent and cunning.  A man wholly unacquainted with


[064-027]chemistry, after having been shown a few wonders, is ready to


[064-028]believe anything.  But a chemist who knows something of the


[064-029]limitations of that science--who knows what chemists have done and


[064-030]who knows the nature of things--cannot be imposed upon.  When no


[064-031]one can be imposed upon, orthodox religion cannot exist.  It is an


[064-032]imposture, and there must be impostors and there must be victims,


[064-033]or the religion cannot be a success.


[064-034]Secularism cannot be a success, universally, as long as there is


[064-035]an impostor or a victim.  This is the difference:  The foundation


[064-036]of orthodox religion is imposture.  The foundation of Secularism


[064-037]is demonstration.  Just to the extent that a man knows, he becomes


[064-038]a Secularist.


[064-039]_Question_.  What do you think of the action of the Knights of


[064-040]Labor in Indiana in turning out one of their members because he


[064-041]was an Atheist, and because he objected to the reading of the Bible


[064-042]at lodge meetings?


[064-043]_Answer_.  In my judgment, the Knights of Labor have made a great


[064-044]mistake.  They want liberty for themselves--they feel that, to a


[064-045]certain extent, they have been enslaved and robbed.  If they want


[064-046]liberty, they should be willing to give liberty to others.  Certainly


[064-047]one of their members has the same right to his opinion with regard


[064-048]to the existence of a God, that the other members have to theirs.


[064-049]I do not blame this man for doubting the existence of a Supreme


[064-050]Being, provided he understands the history of liberty.  When a man


[064-051]takes into consideration the fact that for many thousands of years


[064-052]labor was unpaid, nearly all of it being done by slaves, and that


[064-053]millions and hundreds of millions of human beings were bought and


[064-054]sold the same as cattle, and that during all that time the religions


[064-055]of the world upheld the practice, and the priests of the countless


[064-056]unknown gods insisted that the institution of slavery was divine--


[064-057]I do not wonder that he comes to the conclusion that, perhaps,


[064-058]after all, there is no Supreme Being--at least none who pays any


[064-059]particular attention to the affairs of this world.


[064-060]If one will read the history of the slave-trade, of the cruelties


[064-061]practiced, of the lives sacrificed, of the tortures inflicted, he


[064-062]will at least wonder why "a God of infinite goodness and wisdom"


[064-063]did not interfere just a little; or, at least, why he did not deny


[064-064]that he was in favor of the trade.  Here, in our own country,


[064-065]millions of men were enslaved, and hundreds and thousands of


[064-066]ministers stood up in their pulpits, with their Bibles in front of


[064-067]them, and proceeded to show that slavery was about the only


[064-068]institution that they were absolutely certain was divine.  And they


[064-069]proved it by reading passages from this very Bible that the Knights


[064-070]of Labor in Indiana are anxious to have read in their meetings.


[064-071]For their benefit, let me call their attention to a few passages,


[064-072]and suggest that, hereafter, they read those passages at every


[064-073]meeting, for the purpose of convincing all the Knights that the


[064-074]Lord is on the side of those who work for a living:--


[064-075]"Both thy bondsmen and thy bondsmaids which thou shalt have, shall


[064-076]be of the heathen round about you; of them shall ye buy bondsmen


[064-077]and bondmaids.


[064-078]"Moreover, of the children of the strangers that do sojourn among


[064-079]you, of them shall ye buy, and of their families which are with


[064-080]you, which they begat in your land; and they shall be your


[064-081]possession.


[064-082]"And ye shall take them as an inheritance, for your children after


[064-083]you to inherit them for a possession.  They shall be your bondsmen


[064-084]forever."


[064-085]Nothing seems more natural to me than that a man who believes that


[064-086]labor should be free, and that he who works should be free, should


[064-087]come to the conclusion that the passages above quoted are not


[064-088]entirely on his side.  I don't see why people should be in favor


[064-089]of free bodies who are not also in favor of free minds.  If the


[064-090]mind is to remain in imprisonment, it is hardly worth while to free


[064-091]the body.  If the man has the right to labor, he certainly has the


[064-092]right to use his mind, because without mind he can do no labor.


[064-093]As a rule, the more mind he has, the more valuable his labor is,


[064-094]and the freer his mind is the more valuable he is.


[064-095]If the Knights of Labor expect to accomplish anything in this world,


[064-096]they must do it by thinking.  They must have reason on their side,


[064-097]and the only way they can do anything by thinking is to allow each


[064-098]other to think.  Let all the men who do not believe in the inspiration


[064-099]of the Bible, leave the Knights of Labor and I do not know how many


[064-100]would be left.  But I am perfectly certain that those left will


[064-101]accomplish very little, simply from their lack of sense.


[064-102]Intelligent clergymen have abandoned the idea of plenary inspiration.


[064-103]The best ministers in the country admit that the Bible is full of


[064-104]mistakes, and while many of them are forced to say that slavery is


[064-105]upheld by the Old Testament they also insist that slavery was and


[064-106]is, and forever will be wrong.  What had the Knights of Labor to


[064-107]do with a question of religion?  What business is it of theirs who


[064-108]believes or disbelieves in the religion of the day?  Nobody can


[064-109]defend the rights of labor without defending the right to think.


[064-110]I hope that in time these Knights will become intelligent enough


[064-111]to read in their meetings something of importance; something that


[064-112]applies to this century; something that will throw a little light


[064-113]on questions under discussion at the present time.  The idea of


[064-114]men engaged in a kind of revolution reading from Leviticus,


[064-115]Deuteronomy and Haggai, for the purpose of determining the rights


[064-116]of workingmen in the nineteenth century!  No wonder such men have


[064-117]been swallowed by the whale of monopoly.  And no wonder that,


[064-118]while that are in the belly of this fish, they insist on casting


[064-119]out a man with sense enough to understand the situation!  The


[064-120]Knights of Labor have made a mistake and the sooner they reverse


[064-121]their action the better for all concerned.  Nothing should be taught


[064-122]in this world that somebody does not know.


[064-123]--_Secular Thought_, Toronto, Canada, August 25, 1888.


[064-124]SUMMER RECREATION--MR. GLADSTONE.


[064-125]_Question_.  What is the best philosophy of summer recreation?


[064-126]_Answer_.  As a matter of fact, no one should be overworked.


[064-127]Recreation becomes necessary only when a man has abused himself or


[064-128]has been abused.  Holidays grew out of slavery.  An intelligent


[064-129]man ought not to work so hard to-day that he is compelled to rest


[064-130]to-morrow.  Each day should have its labor and its rest.  But in


[064-131]our civilization, if it can be called civilization, every man is


[064-132]expected to devote himself entirely to business for the most of


[064-133]the year and by that means to get into such a state of body and


[064-134]mind that he requires, for the purpose of recreation, the


[064-135]inconveniences, the poor diet, the horrible beds, the little towels,


[064-136]the warm water, the stale eggs and the tough beef of the average


[064-137]"resort."  For the purpose of getting his mental and physical


[064-138]machinery in fine working order, he should live in a room for two


[064-139]or three months that is about eleven by thirteen; that is to say,


[064-140]he should live in a trunk, fight mosquitoes, quarrel with strangers,


[064-141]dispute bills, and generally enjoy himself; and this is supposed


[064-142]to be the philosophy of summer recreation.  He can do this, or he


[064-143]can go to some extremely fashionable resort where his time is taken


[064-144]up in making himself and family presentable.


[064-145]Seriously, there are few better summer resorts than New York City.


[064-146]If there were no city here it would be the greatest resort for the


[064-147]summer on the continent; with its rivers, its bay, with its wonderful


[064-148]scenery, with the winds from the sea, no better could be found.


[064-149]But we cannot in this age of the world live in accordance with


[064-150]philosophy.  No particular theory can be carried out.  We must live


[064-151]as we must; we must earn our bread and we must earn it as others


[064-152]do, and, as a rule, we must work when others work.  Consequently,


[064-153]if we are to take any recreation we must follow the example of


[064-154]others; go when they go and come when they come.  In other words,


[064-155]man is a social being, and if one endeavors to carry individuality


[064-156]to an extreme he must suffer the consequences.  So I have made up


[064-157]my mind to work as little as I can and to rest as much as I can.


[064-158]_Question_.  What is your opinion of Mr. Gladstone as a controversialist?


[064-159]_Answer_.  Undoubtedly Mr. Gladstone is a man of great talent, of


[064-160]vast and varied information, and undoubtedly he is, politically


[064-161]speaking, at least, one of the greatest men in England--possibly


[064-162]the greatest.  As a controversialist, and I suppose by that you


[064-163]mean on religious questions, he is certainly as good as his cause.


[064-164]Few men can better defend the indefensible than Mr. Gladstone.


[064-165]Few men can bring forward more probabilities in favor of the


[064-166]impossible, then Mr. Gladstone.  He is, in my judgment, controlled


[064-167]in the realm of religion by sentiment; he was taught long ago


[064-168]certain things as absolute truths and he has never questioned them.


[064-169]He has had all he can do to defend them.  It is of but little use


[064-170]to attack sentiment with argument, or to attack argument with


[064-171]sentiment.  A question of sentiment can hardly be discussed; it is


[064-172]like a question of taste.  A man is enraptured with a landscape by


[064-173]Corot; you cannot argue him out of his rapture; the sharper the


[064-174]criticism the greater his admiration, because he feels that it is


[064-175]incumbent upon him to defend the painter who has given him so much


[064-176]real pleasure.  Some people imagine that what they think ought to


[064-177]exist must exist, and that what they really desire to be true is


[064-178]true.  We must remember that Mr. Gladstone has been what is called


[064-179]a deeply religions man all his life.  There was a time when he


[064-180]really believed it to be the duty of the government to see to it


[064-181]that the citizens were religious; when he really believed that no


[064-182]man should hold any office or any position under the government


[064-183]who was not a believer in the established religion; who was not a


[064-184]defender of the parliamentary faith.  I do not know whether he has


[064-185]ever changed his opinions upon these subjects or not.  There is


[064-186]not the slightest doubt as to his honesty, as to his candor.  He


[064-187]says what he believes, and for his belief he gives the reasons that


[064-188]are satisfactory to him.  To me it seems impossible that miracles


[064-189]can be defended.  I do not see how it is possible to bring forward


[064-190]any evidence that any miracle was ever performed; and unless miracles


[064-191]have been performed, Christianity has no basis as a system.  Mr.


[064-192]Hume took the ground that it was impossible to substantiate a


[064-193]miracle, for the reason that it is more probable that the witnesses


[064-194]are mistaken, or are dishonest, than that a fact in nature should


[064-195]be violated.  For instance:  A man says that a certain time, in a


[064-196]certain locality, the attraction of gravitation was suspended; that


[064-197]there were several moments during which a cannon ball weighed


[064-198]nothing, during which when dropped from the hand, or rather when


[064-199]released from the hand, it refused to fall and remained in the air.


[064-200]It is safe to say that no amount of evidence, no number of witnesses,


[064-201]could convince an intelligent man to-day that such a thing occurred.


[064-202]We believe too thoroughly in the constancy of nature.  While men


[064-203]will not believe witnesses who testify to the happening of miracles


[064-204]now, they seem to have perfect confidence in men whom they never


[064-205]saw, who have been dead for two thousand years.  Of course it is


[064-206]known that Mr. Gladstone has published a few remarks concerning my


[064-207]religious views and that I have answered him the best I could.  I


[064-208]have no opinion to give as to that controversy; neither would it


[064-209]be proper for me to say what I think of the arguments advanced by


[064-210]Mr. Gladstone in addition to what I have already published.  I am


[064-211]willing to leave the controversy where it is, or I am ready to


[064-212]answer any further objections that Mr. Gladstone may be pleased to


[064-213]urge.
