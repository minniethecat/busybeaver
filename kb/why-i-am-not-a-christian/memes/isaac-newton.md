# Isaac Newton

**Isaac Newton** (1642–1727) was an English mathematician, physicist, and natural philosopher. In "Why I Am Not a Christian," [[robert-ingersoll|Robert G. Ingersoll]] discusses Newton in the context of scientific genius, religious orthodoxy, and the relationship between intellectual achievement and personal belief.

## In Ingersoll's Writings

### Newton and Spinoza

Ingersoll references a comparison between Newton and [[baruch-spinoza|Spinoza]], rejecting the parallel: "there is no ground of parallel. Newton was a very great" (014-154) scientist, but Ingersoll argues that Newton's scientific achievements do not validate his theological views. He notes that "Newton's discovery was not that there" (014-159) was no God, but rather pertained to natural laws.

### Mathematical Genius

Ingersoll acknowledges Newton's intellectual stature, noting that even Spinoza was "certainly not much inferior to Newton as a mathematician" (014-166). This comparison serves Ingersoll's argument that intellectual greatness in one domain does not guarantee sound judgment in matters of religion.

### Religious Orthodoxy and Envy

Ingersoll observes that "Newton had excited the envy of the i" (015-009) — a reference to the professional jealousies surrounding Newton's career. He also mentions "another fact connected with Newton, and that is that he" (015-005) was an orthodox Protestant, using this to illustrate that even great scientists can hold religious beliefs that Ingersoll considers unfounded.

### Newton and Heber Newton

Ingersoll also references a figure named Heber Newton (038-153), a later American clergyman, drawing a contrast between the scientific Newton and his namesake in the religious sphere.
