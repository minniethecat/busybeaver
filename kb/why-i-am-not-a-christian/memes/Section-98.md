[097-001]But my chief desire was to elicit Colonel Ingersoll's


[097-002]personal views on questions related to the New Thought and


[097-003]its attitude on matters on which he is known to have very


[097-004]decided opinions.  My request for a private chat was


[097-005]cordially granted. During the conversation that ensued--(the


[097-006]substance of which is presented to the readers of _Mind_ in


[097-007]the following paragraphs, with the Colonel's consent)--I was


[097-008]impressed most deeply, not by the force of his arguments,


[097-009]but by the sincerity of his convictions.  Among some of his


[097-010]more violent opponents, who presumably lack other


[097-011]opportunities of becoming known, it is the fashion to accuse


[097-012]Ingersoll of having really no belief in his own opinions.


[097-013]But, if he convinced me of little else, he certainly,


[097-014]without effort, satisfied my mind that this accusation is a


[097-015]slander. Utterly mistaken in his views he may be; but if so,


[097-016]his errors are more honest than many of those he points out


[097-017]in the King James version of the Bible.  If his pulpit


[097-018]enemies could talk with this man by his own fireside, they


[097-019]would pay less attention to Ingersoll himself and more to


[097-020]what he says. They would consider his _meaning_, rather than


[097-021]his motive.


[097-022]As the Colonel is the most conspicuous denunciator of


[097-023]intolerance and bigotry in America, he has been inevitably


[097-024]the greatest victim of these obstacles to mental freedom.


[097-025]"To answer Ingersoll" is the pet ambition of many a young


[097-026]clergyman--the older ones have either acquired prudence or


[097-027]are broad enough to concede the utility of even Agnostics in


[097-028]the economy of evolution.  It was with the very subject that


[097-029]we began our talk--the uncharitableness of men, otherwise


[097-030]good, in their treatment of those whose religious views


[097-031]differ from their own.]


[097-032]_Question_.  What is your conception of true intellectual hospitality?


[097-033]As Truth can brook no compromises, has it not the same limitations


[097-034]that surround social and domestic hospitality?


[097-035]_Answer_.  In the republic of mind we are all equals.  Each one is


[097-036]sceptered and crowned.  Each one is the monarch of his own realm.


[097-037]By "intellectual hospitality" I mean the right of every one to


[097-038]think and to express his thought.  It makes no difference whether


[097-039]his thought is right or wrong.  If you are intellectually hospitable


[097-040]you will admit the right of every human being to see for himself;


[097-041]to hear with his own ears, see with his own eyes, and think with


[097-042]his own brain.  You will not try to change his thought by force,


[097-043]by persecution, or by slander.  You will not threaten him with


[097-044]punishment--here or hereafter.  You will give him your thought,


[097-045]your reasons, your facts; and there you will stop.  This is


[097-046]intellectual hospitality.  You do not give up what you believe to


[097-047]be the truth; you do not compromise.  You simply give him the


[097-048]liberty you claim for yourself.  The truth is not affected by your


[097-049]opinion or by his.  Both may be wrong.  For many years the church


[097-050]has claimed to have the "truth," and has also insisted that it is


[097-051]the duty of every man to believe it, whether it is reasonable to


[097-052]him or not.  This is bigotry in its basest form.  Every man should


[097-053]be guided by his reason; should be true to himself; should preserve


[097-054]the veracity of his soul.  Each human being should judge for himself.


[097-055]The man that believes that all men have this right is intellectually


[097-056]hospitable.


[097-057]_Question_.  In the sharp distinction between theology and religion


[097-058]that is now recognized by many theologians, and in the liberalizing


[097-059]of the church that has marked the last two decades, are not most


[097-060]of your contentions already granted?  Is not the "lake of fire and


[097-061]brimstone" an obsolete issue?


[097-062]_Answer_.  There has been in the last few years a great advance.


[097-063]The orthodox creeds have been growing vulgar and cruel.  Civilized


[097-064]people are shocked at the dogma of eternal pain, and the belief in


[097-065]hell has mostly faded away.  The churches have not changed their


[097-066]creeds.  They still pretend to believe as they always have--but


[097-067]they have changed their tone.  God is now a father--a friend.  He


[097-068]is no longer the monster, the savage, described in the Bible.  He


[097-069]has become somewhat civilized.  He no longer claims the right to


[097-070]damn us because he made us.  But in spite of all the errors and


[097-071]contradictions, in spite of the cruelties and absurdities found in


[097-072]the Scriptures, the churches still insist that the Bible is


[097-073]_inspired_.  The educated ministers admit that the Pentateuch was


[097-074]not written by Moses; that the Psalms were not written by David;


[097-075]that Isaiah was the work of at least three; that Daniel was not


[097-076]written until after the prophecies mentioned in that book had been


[097-077]fulfilled; that Ecclesiastes was not written until the second


[097-078]century after Christ; that Solomon's Song was not written by Solomon;


[097-079]that the book of Esther is of no importance; and that no one knows,


[097-080]or pretends to know, who were the authors of Kings, Samuel,


[097-081]Chronicles, or Job.  And yet these same gentlemen still cling to


[097-082]the dogma of inspiration!  It is no longer claimed that the Bible


[097-083]is true--but _inspired_.


[097-084]_Question_.  Yet the sacred volume, no matter who wrote it, is a


[097-085]mine of wealth to the student and the philosopher, is it not?


[097-086]Would you have us discard it altogether?


[097-087]_Answer_.  Inspiration must be abandoned, and the Bible must take


[097-088]its place among the books of the world.  It contains some good


[097-089]passages, a little poetry, some good sense, and some kindness; but


[097-090]its philosophy is frightful.  In fact, if the book had never existed


[097-091]I think it would have been far better for mankind.  It is not enough


[097-092]to give up the Bible; that is only the beginning.  The _supernatural_


[097-093]must be given up.  It must be admitted that Nature has no master;


[097-094]that there never has been any interference from without; that man


[097-095]has received no help from heaven; and that all the prayers that


[097-096]have ever been uttered have died unanswered in the heedless air.


[097-097]The religion of the supernatural has been a curse.  We want the


[097-098]religion of usefulness.


[097-099]_Question_.  But have you no use whatever for prayer--even in the


[097-100]sense of aspiration--or for faith, in the sense of confidence in


[097-101]the ultimate triumph of the right?


[097-102]_Answer_.  There is a difference between wishing, hoping, believing,


[097-103]and--knowing.  We can wish without evidence or probability, and we


[097-104]can wish for the impossible--for what we believe can never be.  We


[097-105]cannot hope unless there is in the mind a possibility that the


[097-106]thing hoped for can happen.  We can believe only in accordance with


[097-107]evidence, and we know only that which has been demonstrated.  I


[097-108]have no use for prayer; but I do a good deal of wishing and hoping.


[097-109]I hope that some time the right will triumph--that Truth will gain


[097-110]the victory; but I have no faith in gaining the assistance of any


[097-111]god, or of any supernatural power.  I never pray.


[097-112]_Question_.  However fully materialism, as a philosophy, may accord


[097-113]with the merely human _reason_, is it not wholly antagonistic to


[097-114]the instinctive faculties of the mind?


[097-115]_Answer_.  Human reason is the final arbiter.  Any system that does


[097-116]not commend itself to the reason must fall.  I do not know exactly


[097-117]what you mean by _materialism_.  I do not know what matter is.  I


[097-118]am satisfied, however, that without matter there can be no force,


[097-119]no life, no thought, no reason.  It seems to me that mind is a form


[097-120]of force, and force cannot exist apart from matter.  If it is said


[097-121]that God created the universe, then there must have been a time


[097-122]when he commenced to create.  If at that time there was nothing in


[097-123]existence but himself, how could he have exerted any force?  Force


[097-124]cannot be exerted except in opposition to force.  If God was the


[097-125]only existence, force could not have been exerted.


[097-126]_Question_.  But don't you think, Colonel, that the materialistic


[097-127]philosophy, even in the light of your own interpretation, is


[097-128]essentially pessimistic?


[097-129]_Answer_.  I do not consider it so.  I believe that the pessimists


[097-130]and the optimists are both right.  This is the worst possible world,


[097-131]and this is the best possible world--because it is as it must be.


[097-132]The present is the child, and the necessary child, of all the past.


[097-133]_Question_.  What have you to say concerning the operations of the


[097-134]Society for Psychical Research?  Do not its facts and conclusions


[097-135]prove, if not immortality, at least the continuity of life beyond


[097-136]the grave?  Are the millions of Spiritualists deluded?


[097-137]_Answer_.  Of course I have heard and read a great deal about the


[097-138]doings of the Society; so, I have some knowledge as to what is


[097-139]claimed by Spiritualists, by Theosophists, and by all other believers


[097-140]in what are called "spiritual manifestations."  Thousands of


[097-141]wonderful tings have been established by what is called "evidence"


[097-142]--the testimony of good men and women.  I have seen things done


[097-143]that I could not explain, both by mediums and magicians.  I also


[097-144]know that it is easy to deceive the senses, and that the old saying


[097-145]"that seeing is believing" is subject to many exceptions.  I am


[097-146]perfectly satisfied that there is, and can be, no force without


[097-147]matter; that everything that is--all phenomena--all actions and


[097-148]thoughts, all exhibitions of force, have a material basis--that


[097-149]nothing exists,--ever did, or ever will exist, apart from matter.


[097-150]So I am satisfied that no matter ever existed, or ever will, apart


[097-151]from force.


[097-152]We think with the same force with which we walk.  For every action


[097-153]and for every thought, we draw upon the store of force that we have


[097-154]gained from air and food.  We create no force; we borrow it all.


[097-155]As force cannot exist apart from matter, it must be used _with_


[097-156]matter.  It travels only on material roads.  It is impossible to


[097-157]convey a thought to another without the assistance of matter.  No


[097-158]one can conceive of the use of one of our senses without substance.


[097-159]No one can conceive of a thought in the absence of the senses.


[097-160]With these conclusions in my mind--in my brain--I have not the


[097-161]slightest confidence in "spiritual manifestations," and do not


[097-162]believe that any message has ever been received from the dead.


[097-163]The testimony that I have heard--that I have read--coming even from


[097-164]men of science--has not the slightest weight with me.  I do not


[097-165]pretend to see beyond the grave.  I do not say that man is, or is


[097-166]not, immortal.  All I say is that there is no evidence that we live


[097-167]again, and no demonstration that we do not.  It is better ignorantly


[097-168]to hope than dishonestly to affirm.


[097-169]_Question_.  And what do you think of the modern development of


[097-170]metaphysics--as expressed outside of the emotional and semi-


[097-171]ecclesiastical schools?  I refer especially to the power of mind


[097-172]in the curing of disease--as demonstrated by scores of drugless


[097-173]healers.


[097-174]_Answer_.  I have no doubt that the condition of the mind has some


[097-175]effect upon the health.  The blood, the heart, the lungs answer--


[097-176]respond to--emotion.  There is no mind without body, and the body


[097-177]is affected by thought--by passion, by cheerfulness, by depression.


[097-178]Still, I have not the slightest confidence in what is called "mind


[097-179]cure."  I do not believe that thought, or any set of ideas, can


[097-180]cure a cancer, or prevent the hair from falling out, or remove a


[097-181]tumor, or even freckles.  At the same time, I admit that cheerfulness


[097-182]is good and depression bad.  But I have no confidence in what you


[097-183]call "drugless healers."  If the stomach is sour, soda is better


[097-184]than thinking.  If one is in great pain, opium will beat meditation.


[097-185]I am a believer in what you call "drugs," and when I am sick I send


[097-186]for a physician.  I have no confidence in the supernatural.  Magic


[097-187]is not medicine.


[097-188]_Question_.  One great object of this movement, is to make religion


[097-189]scientific--an aid to intellectual as well as spiritual progress.


[097-190]Is it not thus to be encouraged, and destined to succeed--even


[097-191]though it prove the reality and supremacy of the spirit and the


[097-192]secondary importance of the flesh?
