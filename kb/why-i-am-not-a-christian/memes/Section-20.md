[019-001]_Question_.  Do you not think that the Bible has consolation for


[019-002]those who have lost their friends?


[019-003]_Answer_.  There is about the Old Testament this strange fact--I


[019-004]find in it no burial service.  There is in it, I believe, from the


[019-005]first mistake in Genesis to the last curse in Malachi, not one word


[019-006]said over the dead as to their place and state.  When Abraham died,


[019-007]nobody said:  "He is still alive--he is in another world."  When


[019-008]the prophets passed away, not one word was said as to the heaven


[019-009]to which they had gone.  In the Old Testament, Saul inquired of


[019-010]the witch, and Samuel rose.  Samuel did not pretend that he had


[019-011]been living, or that he was alive, but asked:  "Why hast thou


[019-012]disquieted me?"  He did not pretend to have come from another world.


[019-013]And when David speaks of his son, saying that he could not come


[019-014]back to him, but that he, David, could go to his son, that is but


[019-015]saying that he, too, must die.  There is not in the Old Testament


[019-016]one hope of immortality.  It is expressly asserted that there is


[019-017]no difference between the man and beast--that as the one dieth so


[019-018]dieth the other.  There is one little passage in Job which commentators


[019-019]have endeavored to twist into a hope of immortality.  Here is a


[019-020]book of hundreds and hundreds of pages, and hundreds and hundreds


[019-021]of chapters--a revelation from God--and in it one little passage,


[019-022]which, by a mistranslation, is tortured into saying something about


[019-023]another life.  And this is the Old Testament.  I have sometimes


[019-024]thought that the Jews, when slaves in Egypt, were mostly occupied


[019-025]in building tombs for mummies, and that they became so utterly


[019-026]disgusted with that kind of work, that the moment they founded a


[019-027]nation for themselves they went out of the tomb business.  The


[019-028]Egyptians were believers in immortality, and spent almost their


[019-029]entire substance upon the dead.  The living were impoverished to


[019-030]enrich the dead.  The grave absorbed the wealth of Egypt.  The


[019-031]industry of a nation was buried.  Certainly the Old Testament has


[019-032]nothing clearly in favor of immortality.  In the New Testament we


[019-033]are told about the "kingdom of heaven,"--that it is at hand--and


[019-034]about who shall be worthy, but it is hard to tell what is meant by


[019-035]the kingdom of heaven.  The kingdom of heaven was apparently to be


[019-036]in this world, and it was about to commence.  The Devil was to be


[019-037]chained for a thousand years, the wicked were to be burned up, and


[019-038]Christ and his followers were to enjoy the earth.  This certainly


[019-039]was the doctrine of Paul when he says:  "Behold, I show you a


[019-040]mystery; We shall not all _sleep_, but we shall all be _changed_.


[019-041]In a moment, in the twinkling of an eye, at the last trump; for


[019-042]the trumpet shall sound, and the _dead_ shall be _raised_ incorruptible,


[019-043]and _we_ shall be _changed_.  For this corruptible must put on


[019-044]incorruption, and this mortal must put on immortality."  According


[019-045]to this doctrine, those who were alive were to be changed, and


[019-046]those who had died were to be raised from the dead.  Paul certainly


[019-047]did not refer to any other world beyond this.  All these things


[019-048]were to happen here.  The New Testament is made up of the fragments


[019-049]of many religions.  It is utterly inconsistent with itself; and


[019-050]there is not a particle of evidence of the resurrection and ascension


[019-051]of Christ--neither in the nature of things could there be.  It is


[019-052]a thousand times more probable that people were mistaken than that


[019-053]such things occurred.  If Christ really rose from the dead, he


[019-054]should have shown himself, not simply to his disciples, but to the


[019-055]very men who crucified him--to Herod, to the high priest, to Pilate.


[019-056]He should have made a triumphal entry into Jerusalem after his


[019-057]resurrection, instead of before.  He should have shown himself to


[019-058]the Sadducees,--to those who denied the existence of spirit.  Take


[019-059]from the New Testament its doctrine of eternal pain--the idea that


[019-060]we can please God by acts of self-denial that can do no good to


[019-061]others--take away all its miracles, and I have no objection to all


[019-062]the good things in it--no objection to the hope of a future life,


[019-063]if such a hope is expressed--not the slightest.  And I would not


[019-064]for the world say anything to take from any mind a hope in which


[019-065]dwells the least comfort, but a doctrine that dooms a large majority


[019-066]of mankind to eternal flames ought not to be called a consolation.


[019-067]What I say is, that the writers of the New Testament knew no more


[019-068]about the future state than I do, and no less.  The horizon of life


[019-069]has never been pierced.  The veil between time and what is called


[019-070]eternity, has never been raised, so far as I know; and I say of


[019-071]the dead what all others must say if they say only what they know.


[019-072]There is no particular consolation in a guess.  Not knowing what


[019-073]the future has in store for the human race, it is far better to


[019-074]prophesy good than evil.  It is better to hope that the night has


[019-075]a dawn, that the sky has a star, than to build a heaven for the


[019-076]few, and a hell for the many.  It is better to leave your dead in


[019-077]doubt than in fire--better that they should sleep in shadow than


[019-078]in the lurid flames of perdition.  And so I say, and always have


[019-079]said, let us hope for the best.  The minister asks:  "What right


[019-080]have you to hope?  It is sacrilegious in you!"  But, whether the


[019-081]clergy like it or not, I shall always express my real opinion, and


[019-082]shall always be glad to say to those who mourn:  "There is in death,


[019-083]as I believe, nothing worse than sleep.  Hope for as much better


[019-084]as you can.  Under the seven-hued arch let the dead rest."  Throw


[019-085]away the Bible, and you throw away the fear of hell, but the hope


[019-086]of another life remains, because the hope does not depend upon a


[019-087]book--it depends upon the heart--upon human affection.  The fear,


[019-088]so far as this generation is concerned, is born of the book, and


[019-089]that part of the book was born of savagery.  Whatever of hope is


[019-090]in the book is born, as I said before, of human affection, and the


[019-091]higher our civilization the greater the affection.  I had rather


[019-092]rest my hope of something beyond the grave upon the human heart,


[019-093]than upon what they call the Scriptures, because there I find


[019-094]mingled with the hope of something good the threat of infinite


[019-095]evil.  Among the thistles, thorns and briers of the Bible is one


[019-096]pale and sickly flower of hope.  Among all its wild beasts and


[019-097]fowls, only one bird flies heavenward.  I prefer the hope without


[019-098]the thorns, without the briers, thistles, hyenas, and serpents.


[019-099]_Question_.  Do you not know that it is claimed that immortality


[019-100]was brought to light in the New Testament, that that, in fact, was


[019-101]the principal mission of Christ?


[019-102]_Answer_.  I know that Christians claim that the doctrine of


[019-103]immortality was first taught in the New Testament.  They also claim


[019-104]that the highest morality was found there.  Both these claims are


[019-105]utterly without foundation.  Thousands of years before Christ was


[019-106]born--thousands of years before Moses saw the light--the doctrine


[019-107]of immortality was preached by the priests of Osiris and Isis.


[019-108]Funeral discourses were pronounced over the dead, ages before


[019-109]Abraham existed.  When a man died in Egypt, before he was taken


[019-110]across the sacred lake, he had a trial.  Witnesses appeared, and


[019-111]if he had done anything wrong, for which he had not done restitution,


[019-112]he was not taken across the lake.  The living friends, in disgrace,


[019-113]carried the body back, and it was buried outside of what might be


[019-114]called consecrated ground, while the ghost was supposed to wander


[019-115]for a hundred years.  Often the children of the dead would endeavor


[019-116]to redeem the poor ghost by acts of love and kindness.  When he


[019-117]came to the spirit world there was the god Anubis, who weighed his


[019-118]heart in the scales of eternal justice, and if the good deed


[019-119]preponderated he entered the gates of Paradise; if the evil, he


[019-120]had to go back to the world, and be born in the bodies of animals


[019-121]for the purpose of final purification.  At last, the good deeds


[019-122]would outweigh the evil, and, according to the religion of Egypt,


[019-123]the latch-string of heaven would never be drawn in until the last


[019-124]wanderer got home.  Immortality was also taught in India, and, in


[019-125]fact, in all the countries of antiquity.  Wherever men have loved,


[019-126]wherever they have dreamed, wherever hope has spread its wings,


[019-127]the idea of immortality has existed.  But nothing could be worse


[019-128]than the immortality promised in the New Testament--admitting that


[019-129]it is so promised--eternal joy side by side with eternal pain.


[019-130]Think of living forever, knowing that countless millions are


[019-131]suffering eternal pain!  How much better it would be for God to


[019-132]commit suicide and let all life and motion cease!  Christianity


[019-133]has no consolation except for the Christian, and if a Christian


[019-134]minister endeavors to console the widow of an unbeliever he must


[019-135]resort, not to his religion, but to his sympathy--to the natural


[019-136]promptings of the heart.  He is compelled to say:  "After all, may


[019-137]be God is not so bad as we think," or, "May be your husband was


[019-138]better than he appeared; perhaps somehow, in some way, the dear


[019-139]man has squeezed in; he was a good husband, he was a kind father,


[019-140]and even if he is in hell, may be he is in the temperate zone,


[019-141]where they have occasional showers, and where, if the days are hot,


[019-142]the nights are reasonably cool."  All I ask of Christian ministers


[019-143]is to tell what they believe to be the truth--not to borrow ideas


[019-144]from the pagans--not to preach the mercy born of unregenerate


[019-145]sympathy.  Let them tell their real doctrines.  If they will do


[019-146]that, they will not have much influence.  If orthodox Christianity


[019-147]is true, a large majority of the man who have made this world fit


[019-148]to live in are now in perdition.  A majority of the Revolutionary


[019-149]soldiers have been damned.  A majority of the man who fought for


[019-150]the integrity of this Union--a majority who were starved at Libby


[019-151]and Andersonville are now in hell.


[019-152]_Question_.  Do you deny the immortality of the soul?


[019-153]_Answer_.  I have never denied the immortality of the soul.  I have


[019-154]simply been honest.  I have said:  "I do not know."  Long ago, in


[019-155]my lecture on "The Ghosts," I used the following language:  "The


[019-156]idea of immortality, that like a sea has ebbed and flowed in the


[019-157]human heart, with its countless waves of hope and fear beating


[019-158]against the shores and rocks of time and fate, was not born of any


[019-159]book, nor of any creed, nor of any religion.  It was born of human


[019-160]affection, and it will continue to ebb and flow beneath the mists


[019-161]and clouds of doubt and darkness as long as love kisses the lips


[019-162]of death.  It is the rainbow Hope, shining upon the tears of grief."


[019-163]--_The Post_, Washington, D. C., April 30, 1883.

[019-166]concerning President Arthur, Attorney-General Brewster and


[019-167]divers other people, which will be found presented herewith


[019-168]in characteristically piquant style.  With his family, the


[019-169]eloquent advocate has a cottage here, and finds brain and


[019-170]body rest and refreshment in the tumbling waves. This noon,


[019-171]in the height of a tremendous thunder storm, I bumped


[019-172]against his burly figure in the roaring crest, and, after


[019-173]the first shock had passed, determined to utilize the

[019-175]were in the bathing houses, and comfort was more certain


[019-176]where we were than anywhere else.  The Colonel is an expert


[019-177]swimmer and as a floater he cannot be beaten. He was


[019-178]floating when we bumped.  Spouting a pint of salt water from


[019-179]his mouth, he nearly choked with laughter as in answer to my
