[066-001]Temperance men say that it is wise, in so far as possible, to remove


[066-002]temptation from our fellow-men.


[066-003]Let us look at this in regard to other matters.  How do we do away


[066-004]with larceny?  We cannot remove property.  We cannot destroy the


[066-005]money of the world to keep people from stealing some of it.  In


[066-006]other words, we cannot afford to make the world valueless to prevent


[066-007]larceny.  All strength by which temptation is resisted must come


[066-008]from the inside.  Virtue does not depend upon the obstacles to be


[066-009]overcome; virtue depends upon what is inside of the man.  A man is


[066-010]not honest because the safe of the bank is perfectly secure.  Upon


[066-011]the honest man the condition of the safe has no effect.  We will


[066-012]never succeed in raising great and splendid people by keeping them


[066-013]out of temptation.  Great people withstand temptation.  Great people


[066-014]have what may be called moral muscle, moral force.  They are poised


[066-015]within themselves.  They understand their relations to the world.


[066-016]The best possible foundation for honesty is the intellectual


[066-017]perception that dishonesty can, under no circumstances, be a good


[066-018]investment--that larceny is not only wicked, but foolish--not only


[066-019]criminal, but stupid--that crimes are committed only by fools.


[066-020]On every hand there is what is called temptation.  Every man has


[066-021]the opportunity of doing wrong.  Every man, in this country, has


[066-022]the opportunity of drinking too much, has the opportunity of


[066-023]acquiring the opium habit, has the opportunity of taking morphine


[066-024]every day--in other words, has the opportunity of destroying himself.


[066-025]How are they to be prevented?  Most of them are prevented--at least


[066-026]in a reasonable degree--and they are prevented by their intelligence,


[066-027]by their surroundings, by their education, by their objects and


[066-028]aims in life, by the people they love, by the people who love them.


[066-029]No one will deny the evils of intemperance, and it is hardly to be


[066-030]wondered at that people who regard only one side--who think of the


[066-031]impoverished and wretched, of wives and children in want, of desolate


[066-032]homes--become the advocates of absolute prohibition.  At the same


[066-033]time, there is a philosophic side, and the question is whether more


[066-034]good cannot be done by moral influence, by example, by education,


[066-035]by the gradual civilization of our fellow-men, than in any other


[066-036]possible way.  The greatest things are accomplished by indirection.


[066-037]In this way the idea of force, of slavery, is avoided.  The person


[066-038]influenced does not feel that he has been trampled upon, does not


[066-039]regard himself as a victim--he feels rather as a pupil, as one who


[066-040]receives a benefit, whose mind has been enlarged, whose life has


[066-041]been enriched--whereas the direct way of "Thou shalt not" produces


[066-042]an antagonism--in other words, produces the natural result of "I


[066-043]will."


[066-044]By removing one temptation you add strength to others.  By depriving


[066-045]a man of one stimulant, as a rule, you drive him to another, and


[066-046]the other may be far worse than the one from which he has been


[066-047]driven.  We have hundreds of laws making certain things misdemeanors,


[066-048]which are naturally right.


[066-049]Thousands of people, honest in most directions, delight in outwitting


[066-050]the Government--derive absolute pleasure from getting in a few


[066-051]clothes and gloves and shawls without the payment of duty.  Thousands


[066-052]of people buy things in Europe for which they pay more than they


[066-053]would for the same things in America, and then exercise their


[066-054]ingenuity in slipping them through the custom-house.


[066-055]A law to have real force must spring from the nature of things,


[066-056]and the justice of this law must be generally perceived, otherwise


[066-057]it will be evaded.


[066-058]The temperance people themselves are playing into the hands of the


[066-059]very party that would refuse to count their votes.  Allow the


[066-060]Democrats to remain in power, allow the Democrats to be controlled


[066-061]by the South, and a large majority might be in favor of temperance


[066-062]legislation, and yet the votes would remain uncounted.  The party


[066-063]of reform has a great interest in honest elections, and honest


[066-064]elections must first be obtained as the foundation of reform.  The


[066-065]Prohibitionists can take their choice between these parties.  Would


[066-066]it not be far better for the Prohibitionists to say:  "We will vote


[066-067]for temperance men; we will stand with the party that is the nearest


[066-068]in favor of what we deem to be the right"?  They should also take


[066-069]into consideration that other people are as honest as they; that


[066-070]others disbelieve in prohibition as honestly as they believe in


[066-071]it, and that other people cannot leave their principles to vote


[066-072]for prohibition; and they must remember, that these other people


[066-073]are in the majority.


[066-074]Mr. Fisk knows that he cannot be elected President--knows that it


[066-075]is impossible for him to carry any State in the Union.  He also


[066-076]knows that in nearly every State in the Union--probably in all--a


[066-077]majority of the people believe in stimulants.  Why not work with


[066-078]the great and enlightened majority?  Why rush to the extreme for


[066-079]the purpose not only of making yourself useless but hurtful?


[066-080]No man in the world is more opposed to intemperance than I am.  No


[066-081]man in the world feels more keenly the evils and the agony produced


[066-082]by the crime of drunkenness.  And yet I would not be willing to


[066-083]sacrifice liberty, individuality, and the glory and greatness of


[066-084]individual freedom, to do away with all the evils of intemperance.


[066-085]In other words, I believe that slavery, oppression and suppression


[066-086]would crowd humanity into a thousand deformities, the result of


[066-087]which would be a thousand times more disastrous to the well-being


[066-088]of man.  I do not believe in the slave virtues, in the monotony of


[066-089]tyranny, in the respectability produced by force.  I admire the


[066-090]men who have grown in the atmosphere of liberty, who have the pose


[066-091]of independence, the virtues of strength, of heroism, and in whose


[066-092]hearts is the magnanimity, the tenderness, and the courage born of


[066-093]victory.


[066-094]--_New York World_, October 21, 1888.


[066-095]ROBERT ELSMERE.


[066-096]Why do people read a book like "Robert Elsmere," and why do they


[066-097]take any interest in it?  Simply because they are not satisfied


[066-098]with the religion of our day.  The civilized world has outgrown


[066-099]the greater part of the Christian creed.  Civilized people have


[066-100]lost their belief in the reforming power of punishment.  They find


[066-101]that whips and imprisonment have but little influence for good.


[066-102]The truth has dawned upon their minds that eternal punishment is


[066-103]infinite cruelty--that it can serve no good purpose and that the


[066-104]eternity of hell makes heaven impossible.  That there can be in


[066-105]this universe no perfectly happy place while there is a perfectly


[066-106]miserable place--that no infinite being can be good who knowingly


[066-107]and, as one may say, willfully created myriads of human beings,


[066-108]knowing that they would be eternally miserable.  In other words,


[066-109]the civilized man is greater, tenderer, nobler, nearer just than


[066-110]the old idea of God.  The ideal of a few thousand years ago is far


[066-111]below the real of to-day.  No good man now would do what Jehovah


[066-112]is said to have done four thousand years ago, and no civilized


[066-113]human being would now do what, according to the Christian religion,


[066-114]Christ threatens to do at the day of judgment.


[066-115]_Question_.  Has the Christian religion changed in theory of late


[066-116]years, Colonel Ingersoll?


[066-117]_Answer_.  A few years ago the Deists denied the inspiration of


[066-118]the Bible on account of its cruelty.  At the same time they worshiped


[066-119]what they were pleased to call the God of Nature.  Now we are


[066-120]convinced that Nature is as cruel as the Bible; so that, if the


[066-121]God of Nature did not write the Bible, this God at least has caused


[066-122]earthquakes and pestilence and famine, and this God has allowed


[066-123]millions of his children to destroy one another.  So that now we


[066-124]have arrived at the question--not as to whether the Bible is inspired


[066-125]and not as to whether Jehovah is the real God, but whether there


[066-126]is a God or not.  The intelligence of Christendom to-day does not


[066-127]believe in an inspired art or an inspired literature.  If there be


[066-128]an infinite God, inspiration in some particular regard would be a


[066-129]patch--it would be the puttying of a crack, the hiding of a defect


[066-130]--in other words, it would show that the general plan was defective.


[066-131]_Question_.  Do you consider any religion adequate?


[066-132]_Answer_.  A good man, living in England, drawing a certain salary


[066-133]for reading certain prayers on stated occasions, for making a few


[066-134]remarks on the subject of religion, putting on clothes of a certain


[066-135]cut, wearing a gown with certain frills and flounces starched in


[066-136]an orthodox manner, and then looking about him at the suffering


[066-137]and agony of the world, would not feel satisfied that he was doing


[066-138]anything of value for the human race.  In the first place, he would


[066-139]deplore his own weakness, his own poverty, his inability to help


[066-140]his fellow-men.  He would long every moment for wealth, that he


[066-141]might feed the hungry and clothe the naked--for knowledge, for


[066-142]miraculous power, that he might heal the sick and the lame and that


[066-143]he might give to the deformed the beauty of proportion.  He would


[066-144]begin to wonder how a being of infinite goodness and infinite power


[066-145]could allow his children to die, to suffer, to be deformed by


[066-146]necessity, by poverty, to be tempted beyond resistance; how he


[066-147]could allow the few to live in luxury, and the many in poverty and


[066-148]want, and the more he wondered the more useless and ironical would


[066-149]seem to himself his sermons and his prayers.  Such a man is driven


[066-150]to the conclusion that religion accomplishes but little--that it


[066-151]creates as much want as it alleviates, and that it burdens the


[066-152]world with parasites.  Such a man would be forced to think of the


[066-153]millions wasted in superstition.  In other words, the inadequacy,


[066-154]the uselessness of religion would be forced upon his mind.  He


[066-155]would ask himself the question:  "Is it possible that this is a


[066-156]divine institution?  Is this all that man can do with the assistance


[066-157]of God?  Is this the best?"


[066-158]_Question_.  That is a perfectly reasonable question, is it not,


[066-159]Colonel Ingersoll?


[066-160]_Answer_.  The moment a man reaches the point where he asks himself


[066-161]this question he has ceased to be an orthodox Christian.  It will


[066-162]not do to say that in some other world justice will be done.  If


[066-163]God allows injustice to triumph here, why not there?


[066-164]Robert Elsmere stands in the dawn of philosophy.  There is hardly


[066-165]light enough for him to see clearly; but there is so much light


[066-166]that the stars in the night of superstition are obscured.


[066-167]_Question_.  You do not deny that a religious belief is a comfort?


[066-168]_Answer_.  There is one thing that it is impossible for me to


[066-169]comprehend.  Why should any one, when convinced that Christianity


[066-170]is a superstition, have or feel a sense of loss?  Certainly a man


[066-171]acquainted with England, with London, having at the same time


[066-172]something like a heart, must feel overwhelmed by the failure of


[066-173]what is known as Christianity.  Hundreds of thousands exist there


[066-174]without decent food, dwelling in tenements, clothed with rags,


[066-175]familiar with every form of vulgar vice, where the honest poor eat


[066-176]the crust that the vicious throw away.  When this man of intelligence,


[066-177]of heart, visits the courts; when he finds human liberty a thing


[066-178]treated as of no value, and when he hears the judge sentencing


[066-179]girls and boys to the penitentiary--knowing that a stain is being


[066-180]put upon them that all the tears of all the coming years can never


[066-181]wash away--knowing, too, and feeling that this is done without the


[066-182]slightest regret, without the slightest sympathy, as a mere matter


[066-183]of form, and that the judge puts this brand of infamy upon the


[066-184]forehead of the convict just as cheerfully as a Mexican brands his


[066-185]cattle; and when this man of intelligence and heart knows that


[066-186]these poor people are simply the victims of society, the unfortunates


[066-187]who stumble and over whose bodies rolls the Juggernaut--he knows


[066-188]that there is, or at least appears to be, no power above or below


[066-189]working for righteousness--that from the heavens is stretched no


[066-190]protecting hand.  And when a man of intelligence and heart in


[066-191]England visits the workhouse, the last resting place of honest


[066-192]labor; when he thinks that the young man, without any great


[066-193]intelligence, but with a good constitution, starts in the morning


[066-194]of his life for the workhouse, and that it is impossible for the


[066-195]laboring man, one who simply has his muscle, to save anything; that


[066-196]health is not able to lay anything by for the days of disease--when


[066-197]the man of intelligence and heart sees all this, he is compelled


[066-198]to say that the civilization of to-day, the religion of to-day,


[066-199]the charity of to-day--no matter how much of good there may be


[066-200]behind them or in them, are failures.
