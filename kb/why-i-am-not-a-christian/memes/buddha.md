# Buddha

**Buddha** (Siddhartha Gautama) was the founder of [[buddhism|Buddhism]], one of the world's major religions. In "Why I Am Not a Christian," Robert G. Ingersoll references Buddha multiple times in the context of comparative religion and the historical development of moral teachings.

## In Ingersoll's Writings

Ingersoll places Buddha alongside other major religious figures, noting that he stands "above Buddha, Zoroaster, Confucius, and others" (005-025) in certain arguments about Christ. This rhetorical move situates Buddha within a pantheon of pre-Christian moral teachers.

### Historical Precedence

Ingersoll emphasizes that Buddha predates Christianity and that many Christian moral teachings have precedents in earlier traditions. He observes that "long before Christ lived Buddha taught the" (086-127) same principles that Christians later claimed as uniquely their own. This argument supports Ingersoll's broader thesis that Christian morality is derivative rather than original.

### Popular Influence

On the question of religious truth and popularity, Ingersoll notes that "Buddha not only had, but has more followers" (026-036) than Christ at certain points in history. This statistic serves his argument that numerical success does not validate theological truth claims.

### Christian Attitudes

Ingersoll critiques the dismissive attitude of Christians toward other faiths, observing that "by Buddha, the Christian laughs" (034-143). He uses this observation to highlight what he sees as religious chauvinism and intellectual inconsistency among Christian believers.
