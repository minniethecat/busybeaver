[060-001]Without taking into account whether the man is or is not responsible,


[060-002]still society has the right of self-defence.  Whether that right


[060-003]of self-defence goes to the extent of taking life, depends, I


[060-004]imagine, upon the circumstances in which society finds itself


[060-005]placed.  A thousand men on a ship form a society.  If a few men


[060-006]should enter into a plot for the destruction of the ship, or for


[060-007]turning it over to pirates, or for poisoning and plundering the


[060-008]most of the passengers--if the passengers found this out certainly


[060-009]they would have the right of self-defence.  They might not have


[060-010]the means to confine the conspirators with safety.  Under such


[060-011]circumstances it might be perfectly proper for them to destroy


[060-012]their lives and to throw their worthless bodies into the sea.  But


[060-013]what society has the right to do depends upon the circumstances.


[060-014]Now, in my judgment, society has the right to do two things--to


[060-015]protect itself and to do what it can to reform the individual.


[060-016]Society has no right to take revenge; no right to torture a convict;


[060-017]no right to do wrong because some individual has done wrong.  I am


[060-018]opposed to all corporal punishment in penitentiaries.  I am opposed


[060-019]to anything that degrades a criminal or leaves upon him an unnecessary


[060-020]stain, or puts upon him any stain that he did not put upon himself.


[060-021]Most people defend capital punishment on the ground that the man


[060-022]ought to be killed because he has killed another.  The only real


[060-023]ground for killing him, even if that be good, is not that he has


[060-024]killed, but that he may kill.  What he has done simply gives evidence


[060-025]of what he may do, and to prevent what he may do, instead of to


[060-026]revenge what he has done, should be the reason given.


[060-027]Now, there is another view.  To what extent does it harden the


[060-028]community for the Government to take life?  Don't people reason in


[060-029]this way:  That man ought to be killed; the Government, under the


[060-030]same circumstances, would kill him, therefore I will kill him?


[060-031]Does not the Government feed the mob spirit--the lynch spirit?


[060-032]Does not the mob follow the example set by the Government?  The


[060-033]Government certainly cannot say that it hangs a man for the purpose


[060-034]of reforming him.  Its feelings toward that man are only feelings


[060-035]of revenge and hatred.  These are the same feelings that animate


[060-036]the lowest and basest mob.


[060-037]Let me give you an example.  In the city of Bloomington, in the


[060-038]State of Illinois, a man confined in the jail, in his efforts to


[060-039]escape, shot and, I believe, killed the jailer.  He was pursued,


[060-040]recaptured, brought back and hanged by a mob.  The man who put the


[060-041]rope around his neck was then under indictment for an assault to


[060-042]kill and was out on bail, and after the poor wretch was hanged


[060-043]another man climbed the tree and, in a kind of derision, put a


[060-044]piece of cigar between the lips of the dead man.  The man who did


[060-045]this had also been indicted for a penitentiary offence and was then


[060-046]out on bail.


[060-047]I mention this simply to show the kind of people you find in mobs.


[060-048]Now, if the Government had a greater and nobler thought; if the


[060-049]Government said:  "We will reform; we will not destroy; but if the


[060-050]man is beyond reformation we will simply put him where he can do


[060-051]no more harm," then, in my judgment, the effect would be far better.


[060-052]My own opinion is, that the effect of an execution is bad upon the


[060-053]community--degrading and debasing.  The effect is to cheapen human


[060-054]life; and, although a man is hanged because he has taken human


[060-055]life, the very fact that his life is taken by the Government tends


[060-056]to do away with the idea that human life is sacred.


[060-057]Let me give you an illustration.  A man in the city of Washington


[060-058]went to Alexandria, Va., for the purpose of seeing a man hanged


[060-059]who had murdered an old man and a woman for the purpose of getting


[060-060]their money.  On his return from that execution he came through


[060-061]what is called the Smithsonian grounds.  This was on the same day,


[060-062]late in the evening.  There he met a peddler, whom he proceeded to


[060-063]murder for his money.  He was arrested in a few hours, in a little


[060-064]while was tried and convicted, and in a little while was hanged.


[060-065]And another man, present at this second execution, went home on


[060-066]that same day, and, in passing by a butcher-shop near his house,


[060-067]went in, took from the shop a cleaver, went into his house and


[060-068]chopped his wife's head off.


[060-069]This, I say, throws a little light upon the effect of public


[060-070]executions.  In the Cignarale case, of course the sentence should


[060-071]have been commuted.  I think, however, that she ought not to be


[060-072]imprisoned for life.  From what I read of the testimony I think


[060-073]she should have been pardoned.


[060-074]It is hard, I suppose, for a man fully to understand and enter into


[060-075]the feelings of a wife who has been trampled upon, abused, bruised,


[060-076]and blackened by the man she loved--by the man who made to her the


[060-077]vows of eternal affection.  The woman, as a rule, is so weak, so


[060-078]helpless.  Of course, it does not all happen in a moment.  It comes


[060-079]on as the night comes.  She notices that he does not act quite as


[060-080]affectionately as he formerly did.  Day after day, month after


[060-081]month, she feels that she is entering a twilight.  But she hopes


[060-082]that she is mistaken, and that the light will come again.  The


[060-083]gloom deepens, and at last she is in midnight--a midnight without


[060-084]a star.  And this man, whom she once worshiped, is now her enemy--


[060-085]one who delights to trample upon every sentiment she has--who


[060-086]delights in humiliating her, and who is guilty of a thousand nameless


[060-087]tyrannies.  Under these circumstances, it is hardly right to hold


[060-088]that woman accountable for what she does.  It has always seemed to


[060-089]me strange that a woman so circumstanced--in such fear that she


[060-090]dare not even tell her trouble--in such fear that she dare not even


[060-091]run away--dare not tell a father or a mother, for fear that she


[060-092]will be killed--I say, that in view of all this, it has always


[060-093]seemed strange to me that so few husbands have been poisoned.


[060-094]The probability is that society raises its own criminals.  It plows


[060-095]the land, sows the seed, and harvests the crop.  I believe that


[060-096]the shadow of the gibbet will not always fall upon the earth.  I


[060-097]believe the time will come when we shall know too much to raise


[060-098]criminals--know too much to crowd those that labor into the dens


[060-099]and dungeons that we call tenements, while the idle live in palaces.


[060-100]The time will come when men will know that real progress means the


[060-101]enfranchisement of the whole human race, and that our interests


[060-102]are so united, so interwoven, that the few cannot be happy while


[060-103]the many suffer; so that the many cannot be happy while the few


[060-104]suffer; so that none can be happy while one suffers.  In other


[060-105]words, it will be found that the human race is interested in each


[060-106]individual.  When that time comes we will stop producing criminals;


[060-107]we will stop producing failures; we will not leave the next generation


[060-108]to chance; we will not regard the gutter as a proper nursery for


[060-109]posterity.


[060-110]People imagine that if the thieves are sent to the penitentiary,


[060-111]that is the last of the thieves; that if those who kill others are


[060-112]hanged, society is on a safe and enduring basis.  But the trouble


[060-113]is here:  A man comes to your front door and you drive him away.


[060-114]You have an idea that that man's case is settled.  You are mistaken.


[060-115]He goes to the back door.  He is again driven away.  But the case


[060-116]is not settled.  The next thing you know he enters at night.  He


[060-117]is a burglar.  He is caught; he is convicted; he is sent to the


[060-118]penitentiary, and you imagine that the case is settled.  But it is


[060-119]not.  You must remember that you have to keep all the agencies


[060-120]alive for the purpose of taking care of these people.  You have to


[060-121]build and maintain your penitentiaries, your courts of justice;


[060-122]you have to pay your judges, your district attorneys, your juries,


[060-123]you witnesses, your detectives, your police--all these people must


[060-124]be paid.  So that, after all, it is a very expensive way of settling


[060-125]this question.  You could have done it far more cheaply had you


[060-126]found this burglar when he was a child; had you taken his father


[060-127]and mother from the tenement house, or had you compelled the owners


[060-128]to keep the tenement clean; or if you had widened the streets, if


[060-129]you had planted a few trees, if you had had plenty of baths, if


[060-130]you had had a school in the neighborhood.  If you had taken some


[060-131]interest in this family--some interest in this child--instead of


[060-132]breaking into houses, he might have been a builder of houses.


[060-133]There is, and it cannot be said too often, no reforming influence


[060-134]in punishment; no reforming power in revenge.  Only the best of


[060-135]men should be in charge of penitentiaries; only the noblest minds


[060-136]and the tenderest hearts should have the care of criminals.


[060-137]Criminals should see from the first moment that they enter a


[060-138]penitentiary that it is filled with the air of kindness, full of


[060-139]the light of hope.  The object should be to convince every criminal


[060-140]that he has made a mistake; that he has taken the wrong way; that


[060-141]the right way is the easy way, and that the path of crime never


[060-142]did and never can lead to happiness; that that idea is a mistake,


[060-143]and that the Government wishes to convince him that he has made a


[060-144]mistake; wishes to open his intellectual eyes; wishes so to educate


[060-145]him, so to elevate him, that he will look back upon what he has


[060-146]done, only with horror.  This is reformation.  Punishment is not.


[060-147]When the convict is taken to Sing Sing or to Auburn, and when a


[060-148]striped suit of clothes is put upon him--that is to say, when he


[060-149]is made to feel the degradation of his position--no step has been


[060-150]taken toward reformation.  You have simply filled his heart with


[060-151]hatred.  Then, when he has been abused for several years, treated


[060-152]like a wild beast, and finally turned out again in the community,


[060-153]he has no thought, in a majority of cases, except to "get even"


[060-154]with those who have persecuted him.  He feels that it is a


[060-155]persecution.


[060-156]_Question_.  Do you think that men are naturally criminals and


[060-157]naturally virtuous?


[060-158]_Answer_.  I think that man does all that he does naturally--that


[060-159]is to say, a certain man does a certain act under certain circumstances,


[060-160]and he does this naturally.  For instance, a man sees a five dollar


[060-161]bill, and he knows that he can take it without being seen.  Five


[060-162]dollars is no temptation to him.  Under the circumstances it is


[060-163]not natural that he should take it.  The same man sees five million


[060-164]dollars, and feels that he can get possession of it without detection.


[060-165]If he takes it, then under the circumstances, that was natural to


[060-166]him.  And yet I believe there are men above all price, and that no


[060-167]amount of temptation or glory or fame could mislead them.  Still,


[060-168]whatever man does, is or was natural to him.


[060-169]Another view of the subject is this:  I have read that out of fifty


[060-170]criminals who had been executed it was found, I believe, in nearly


[060-171]all the cases, that the shape of the skull was abnormal.  Whether


[060-172]this is true or not, I don't know; but that some men have a tendency


[060-173]toward what we call crime, I believe.  Where this has been ascertained,


[060-174]then, it seems to me, such men should be placed where they cannot


[060-175]multiply their kind.  Women who have a criminal tendency should be


[060-176]placed where they cannot increase their kind.  For hardened criminals


[060-177]--that is to say, for the people who make crime a business--it


[060-178]would probably be better to separate the sexes; to send the men to


[060-179]one island, the women to another.  Let them be kept apart, to the


[060-180]end that people with criminal tendencies may fade from the earth.


[060-181]This is not prompted by revenge.  This would not be done for the


[060-182]purpose of punishing these people, but for the protection of society


[060-183]--for the peace and happiness of the future.
