# Assassination of James A. Garfield

The assassination of President James A. Garfield by Charles Guiteau on July 2, 1881, was a national tragedy that [[robert-ingersoll|Robert G. Ingersoll]] analyzed through the lens of his critique of religious fanaticism. Ingersoll had known Garfield personally and had supported his presidential campaign, and he briefly knew Guiteau as well.

## Ingersoll's Connection

Ingersoll was acquainted with both the victim and the assassin. When asked, "you knew Guiteau slightly," Ingersoll confirmed: "I knew Guiteau slightly; I saw him for the first time" in a professional context [016-152]. He noted that Guiteau "did not impress me in any way" — an unremarkable figure who gave no warning of the violence to come [016-168].

As for Garfield, Ingersoll had been an active supporter. He described Garfield as "an honest man" and a person of "integrity and" [[reason|intellect]]ual stature [006-055] [007-085]. He had campaigned for Garfield in the 1880 election and believed Garfield would be a capable president.

## Guiteau's Religious Motivation

Ingersoll's analysis of the assassination focused on the role of religious belief in motivating Guiteau. He identified "sectarian [[christianity|Christianity]], by which Guiteau was led to assert" that his act was divinely sanctioned [017-046]. Guiteau "seemed to think that the killing" of the President was [[god|God]]'s will [017-050], reasoning that if Garfield were removed, political harmony would result.

Ingersoll pointed out the uncomfortable [[truth|fact]] for religious critics: Guiteau "is not an ignorant man" — he was literate, articulate, and his actions were shaped by a coherent (if delusional) religious worldview [017-018]. The ministers "hate to admit that Guiteau was a" Christian, preferring to blame his act on infidelity or secularism [017-023]. Ingersoll noted with satis[[truth|fact]]ion that he was "exceedingly glad that Guiteau is not an Infidel" — the assassination could not be pinned on freethinkers [017-034].

## Broader Implications

The crime demonstrated, in Ingersoll's view, three things: "First, the" danger of religious certainty; second, the moral bankruptcy of blaming secularism for violence committed by believers; and third, the need to judge individuals by their actions rather than their professed creed [017-014].

Ingersoll argued that Guiteau "wanted an office and was refused" the patronage position he sought, and that his religious justification was a rationalization for personal grievance [017-119]. The assassination was not caused by infidelity, nor by [[christianity|Christianity]] per se, but by the combination of personal disappointment and a theological framework that allowed Guiteau to believe he was carrying out divine judgment.
