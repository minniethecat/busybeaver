[083-001]Men, after a time, will find the worthlessness of great wealth;


[083-002]they will find it is not splendid to excite envy in others.  So,


[083-003]too, they will find that the happiness of the human race is so


[083-004]interdependent and so interwoven, that finally the interest of


[083-005]humanity will be the interest of the individual.


[083-006]I know that at present the lives of many millions are practically


[083-007]without value, but in my judgment, the world is growing a little


[083-008]better every day.  On the average, men have more comforts, better


[083-009]clothes, better food, more books and more of the luxuries of life


[083-010]than ever before.


[083-011]_Question_.  It is said that properly to appreciate Rousseau,


[083-012]Voltaire, Hugo and other French classics, a thorough knowledge of


[083-013]the French language is necessary.  What is your opinion?


[083-014]_Answer_.  No; to say that a knowledge of French is necessary in


[083-015]order to appreciate Voltaire or Hugo is nonsensical.  For a student


[083-016]anxious to study the works of these masters, to set to work to


[083-017]learn the language of the writers would be like my building a flight


[083-018]of stairs to go down to supper.  The stairs are already there.


[083-019]Some other person built them for me and others who choose to use


[083-020]them.


[083-021]Men have spent their lives in the study of the French and English,


[083-022]and have given us Voltaire, Hugo and all other works of French


[083-023]classics, perfect in sentiment and construction as the originals


[083-024]are.  Macaulay was a great linguist, but he wrote no better than


[083-025]Shakespeare, and Burns wrote perfect English, though virtually


[083-026]uneducated.  Good writing is a matter of genius and heart; reading


[083-027]is application and judgment.


[083-028]I am of the opinion that Wilbur's English translation of "Les


[083-029]Miserables" is better than Hugo's original, as a literary


[083-030]masterpiece.


[083-031]What a grand novel it is!  What characters, Jean Valjean and Javert!


[083-032]_Question_.  Which in your opinion is the greatest English novel?


[083-033]_Answer_.  I think the greatest novel ever written in English is


[083-034]"A Tale of Two Cities," by Dickens.  It is full of philosophy; its


[083-035]incidents are dramatically grouped.  Sidney Carton, the hero, is


[083-036]a marvelous creation and a marvelous character.  Lucie Manette is


[083-037]as delicate as the perfume of wild violets, and cell 105, North


[083-038]Tower, and scenes enacted there, almost touch the region occupied


[083-039]by "Lear."  There, too, Mme. Defarge is the impersonation of the


[083-040]French Revolution, and the nobleman of the chateau with his fine


[083-041]features changed to stone, and the messenger at Tellson's Bank


[083-042]gnawing the rust from his nails; all there are the creations of


[083-043]genius, and these children of fiction will live as long as Imagination


[083-044]spreads her many-colored wings in the mind of man.


[083-045]_Question_.  What do you think of Pope?


[083-046]_Answer_.  Pope!  Alexander Pope, the word-carpenter, a mechanical


[083-047]poet, or stay--rather a "digital poet;" that fits him best--one of


[083-048]those fellows who counts his fingers to see that his verse is in


[083-049]perfect rhythm.  His "Essay on Man" strikes me as being particularly


[083-050]defective.  For instance:


[083-051]"All discord, harmony not understood,


[083-052]All partial evil, universal good,"


[083-053]from the first epistle of his "Essay on Man."  Anything that is


[083-054]evil cannot by any means be good, and anything partial cannot be


[083-055]universal.


[083-056]We see in libraries ponderous tomes labeled "Burke's Speeches."


[083-057]No person ever seems to read them, but he is now regarded as being


[083-058]in his day a great speaker, because now no one has pluck enough to


[083-059]read his speeches.  Why, for thirty years Burke was known in


[083-060]Parliament as the "Dinner Bell"--whenever he rose to speak, everybody


[083-061]went to dinner.


[083-062]--_The Evening Express_, Buffalo, New York, October 6, 1892.


[083-063]WOMAN IN POLITICS.


[083-064]_Question_.  What do you think of the influence of women in


[083-065]politics?


[083-066]_Answer_.  I think the influence of women is always good in politics,


[083-067]as in everything else.  I think it the duty of every woman to


[083-068]ascertain what she can in regard to her country, including its


[083-069]history, laws and customs.  Woman above all others is a teacher.


[083-070]She, above all others, determines the character of children; that


[083-071]is to say, of men and women.


[083-072]There is not the slightest danger of women becoming too intellectual


[083-073]or knowing too much.  Neither is there any danger of men knowing


[083-074]too much.  At least, I know of no men who are in immediate peril


[083-075]from that source.  I am a firm believer in the equal rights of


[083-076]human beings, and no matter what I think as to what woman should


[083-077]or should not do, she has the same right to decide for herself that


[083-078]I have to decide for myself.  If women wish to vote, if they wish


[083-079]to take part in political matters, if they wish to run for office,


[083-080]I shall do nothing to interfere with their rights.  I most cheerfully


[083-081]admit that my political rights are only equal to theirs.


[083-082]There was a time when physical force or brute strength gave pre-


[083-083]eminence.  The savage chief occupied his position by virtue of his


[083-084]muscle, of his courage, on account of the facility with which he


[083-085]wielded a club.  As long as nations depend simply upon brute force,


[083-086]the man, in time of war, is, of necessity, of more importance to


[083-087]the nation than woman, and as the dispute is to be settled by


[083-088]strength, by force, those who have the strength and force naturally


[083-089]settle it.  As the world becomes civilized, intelligence slowly


[083-090]takes the place of force, conscience restrains muscle, reason enters


[083-091]the arena, and the gladiator retires.


[083-092]A little while ago the literature of the world was produced by men,


[083-093]and men were not only the writers, but the readers.  At that time


[083-094]the novels were coarse and vulgar.  Now the readers of fiction are


[083-095]women, and they demand that which they can read, and the result is


[083-096]that women have become great writers.  The women have changed our


[083-097]literature, and the change has been good.


[083-098]In every field where woman has become a competitor of man she has


[083-099]either become, or given evidence that she is to become, his equal.


[083-100]My own opinion is that woman is naturally the equal of man and that


[083-101]in time, that is to say, when she has had the opportunity and the


[083-102]training, she will produce in the world of art as great pictures,


[083-103]as great statues, and in the world of literature as great books,


[083-104]dramas and poems as man has produced or will produce.


[083-105]There is nothing very hard to understand in the politics of a


[083-106]country.  The general principles are for the most part simple.  It


[083-107]is only in the application that the complexity arises, and woman,


[083-108]I think, by nature, is as well fitted to understand these things


[083-109]as man.  In short, I have no prejudice on this subject.  At first,


[083-110]women will be more conservative than men; and this is natural.


[083-111]Women have, through many generations, acquired the habit of


[083-112]submission, of acquiescence.  They have practiced what may be called


[083-113]the slave virtues--obedience, humility--so that some time will be


[083-114]required for them to become accustomed to the new order of things,


[083-115]to the exercise of greater freedom, acting in accordance with


[083-116]perceived obligation, independently of authority.


[083-117]So I say equal rights, equal education, equal advantages.  I hope


[083-118]that woman will not continue to be the serf of superstition; that


[083-119]she will not be the support of the church and priest; that she will


[083-120]not stand for the conservation of superstition, but that in the


[083-121]east of her mind the sun of progress will rise.


[083-122]_Question_.  In your lecture on Voltaire you made a remark about


[083-123]the government of ministers, and you stated that if the ministers


[083-124]of the city of New York had to power to make the laws most people


[083-125]would prefer to live in a well regulated penitentiary.  What do


[083-126]you mean by this?


[083-127]_Answer_.  Well, as a rule, ministers are quite severe.  They have


[083-128]little patience with human failures.  They are taught, and they


[083-129]believe and they teach, that man is absolutely master of his own


[083-130]fate.  Besides, they are believers in the inspiration of the


[083-131]Scriptures, and the laws of the Old Testament are exceedingly


[083-132]severe.  Nearly every offence was punished by death.  Every offence


[083-133]was regarded as treason against Jehovah.


[083-134]In the Pentateuch there is no pity.  If a man committed some offence


[083-135]justice was not satisfied with his punishment, but proceeded to


[083-136]destroy his wife and children.  Jehovah seemed to think that crime


[083-137]was in the blood; that it was not sufficient to kill the criminal,


[083-138]but to prevent future crimes you should kill his wife and babes.


[083-139]The reading of the Old Testament is calculated to harden the heart,


[083-140]to drive the angel of pity from the breast, and to make man a


[083-141]religious savage.  The clergy, as a rule, do not take a broad and


[083-142]liberal view of things.  They judge every offence by what they


[083-143]consider would be the result if everybody committed the same offence.


[083-144]They do not understand that even vice creates obstructions for


[083-145]itself, and that there is something in the nature of crime the


[083-146]tendency of which is to defeat crime, and I might add in this place


[083-147]that the same seems to be true of excessive virtue.  As a rule,


[083-148]the clergy clamor with great zeal for the execution of cruel laws.


[083-149]Let me give an instance in point:  In the time of George III., in


[083-150]England, there were two hundred and twenty-three offences punishable


[083-151]with death.  From time to time this cruel code was changed by Act


[083-152]of Parliament, yet no bishop sitting in the House of Lords ever


[083-153]voted in favor of any one of these measures.  The bishops always


[083-154]voted for death, for blood, against mercy and against the repeal


[083-155]of capital punishment.  During all these years there were some


[083-156]twenty thousand or more of the established clergy, and yet, according


[083-157]to John Bright, no voice was ever raised in any English pulpit


[083-158]against the infamous criminal code.


[083-159]Another thing:  The orthodox clergy teach that man is totally


[083-160]depraved; that his inclination is evil; that his tendency is toward


[083-161]the Devil.  Starting from this as a foundation, of course every


[083-162]clergyman believes every bad thing said of everybody else.  So,


[083-163]when some man is charged with a crime, the clergyman taking into


[083-164]consideration the fact that the man is totally depraved, takes it


[083-165]for granted that he must be guilty.  I am not saying this for the


[083-166]purpose of exciting prejudice against the clergy.  I am simply


[083-167]showing what is the natural result of a certain creed, of a belief


[083-168]in universal depravity, or a belief in the power and influence of


[083-169]a personal Devil.  If the clergy could have their own way they


[083-170]would endeavor to reform the world by law.  They would re-enact


[083-171]the old statutes of the Puritans.  Joy would be a crime.  Love


[083-172]would be an offence.  Every man with a smile on his face would be


[083-173]suspected, and a dimple in the cheek would be a demonstration of


[083-174]depravity.


[083-175]In the trial of a cause it is natural for a clergyman to start with


[083-176]the proposition, "The defendant is guilty;" and then he says to


[083-177]himself, "Let him prove himself innocent."  The man who has not


[083-178]been poisoned with the creed starts out with the proposition, "The


[083-179]defendant is innocent; let the State prove that he is guilty."


[083-180]Consequently, I say that if I were defending a man whom I knew to


[083-181]be innocent, I would not have a clergyman on the jury if I could


[083-182]help it.


[083-183]--_New York Advertiser_, December 24, 1893.


[083-184]SPIRITUALISM.


[083-185]_Question_.  Have you investigated Spiritualism, and what has been


[083-186]your experience?


[083-187]_Answer_.  A few years ago I paid some attention to what is called


[083-188]Spiritualism, and was present when quite mysterious things were


[083-189]supposed to have happened.  The most notable seance that I attended


[083-190]was given by Slade, at which slate-writing was done.  Two slates


[083-191]were fastened together, with a pencil between them, and on opening


[083-192]the slates certain writing was found.  When the writing was done


[083-193]it was impossible to tell.  So, I have been present when it was


[083-194]claimed that certain dead people had again clothed themselves in


[083-195]flesh and were again talking in the old way.  In one instance, I


[083-196]think, George Washington claimed to be present.  On the same evening


[083-197]Shakespeare put in an appearance.  It was hard to recognize


[083-198]Shakespeare from what the spirit said, still I was assured by the


[083-199]medium that there was no mistake as to the identity.
