[059-001]_Question_.  I see that by the vote two of the five Democrats who


[059-002]voted for protection, and one of the two Republicans who voted for


[059-003]free trade, were New Yorkers.  What do you think is the significance


[059-004]of this fact in relation to the question as to whether New York


[059-005]will join the South in the opposition to the industries of the


[059-006]country?


[059-007]_Answer_.  In the city of New York there are a vast number of men


[059-008]--importers, dealers in foreign articles, representatives of foreign


[059-009]houses, of foreign interests, of foreign ideas.  Of course most of


[059-010]these people are in favor of free trade.  They regard New York as


[059-011]a good market; beyond that they have not the slightest interest in


[059-012]the United States.  They are in favor of anything that will give


[059-013]them a large profit, or that will allow them to do the same business


[059-014]with less capital, or that will do them any good without the


[059-015]slightest regard as to what the effect may be on this country as


[059-016]a nation.  They come from all countries, and they expect to remain


[059-017]here until their fortunes are made or lost and all their ideas are


[059-018]moulded by their own interests.  Then, there are a great many


[059-019]natives who are merchants in New York and who deal in foreign goods,


[059-020]and they probably think--some of them--that it would be to their


[059-021]interest to have free trade, and they will probably vote according


[059-022]to the ledger.  With them it is a question of bookkeeping.  Their


[059-023]greed is too great to appreciate the fact that to impoverish


[059-024]customers destroys trade.


[059-025]At the same time, New York, being one of the greatest manufacturing


[059-026]States of the world, will be for protection, and the Democrats of


[059-027]New York who voted for protection did so, not only because the


[059-028]believed in it themselves, but because their constituents believe


[059-029]in it, and the Republicans who voted the other way must have


[059-030]represented some district where the foreign influence controls.


[059-031]The people of this State will protect their own industries.


[059-032]_Question_.  What will be the fate of the Mills Bill in the Senate?


[059-033]_Answer_.  I think that unless the Senate has a bill prepared


[059-034]embodying Republican ideals, a committee should be appointed, not


[059-035]simply to examine the Mills Bill, but to get the opinions and the


[059-036]ideas of the most intelligent manufacturers and mechanics in this


[059-037]country.  Let the questions be thoroughly discussed, and let the


[059-038]information thus obtained be given to the people; let it be published


[059-039]from day to day; let the laboring man have his say, let the


[059-040]manufacturer give his opinion; let the representatives of the


[059-041]principal industries be heard, so that we may vote intelligently,


[059-042]so that the people may know what they are doing.


[059-043]A great many industries have been attacked.  Let them defend


[059-044]themselves.  Public property should not be taken for Democratic


[059-045]use without due process of law.


[059-046]Certainly it is not the business of a Republican Senate to pull


[059-047]the donkey of the Democrats out of the pit; the dug the pit, and


[059-048]we have lost no donkey.


[059-049]I do not think the Senate called upon to fix up this Mills Bill,


[059-050]to rectify its most glaring mistakes, and then for the sake of


[059-051]saving a little, give up a great deal.  What we have got is safe


[059-052]until the Democrats have the power to pass a bill.  We can protect


[059-053]our rights by not passing their bills.  In other words, we do not


[059-054]wish to practice any great self-denial simply for the purpose of


[059-055]insuring Democratic success.  If the bill is sent back to the House,


[059-056]no matter in what form, if it still has the name "Mills Bill" I


[059-057]think the Democrats will vote for it simply to get out of their


[059-058]trouble.  They will have the President's message left.


[059-059]But I do hope that the Senate will investigate this business.  It


[059-060]is hardly fair to ask the Senate to take decided and final action


[059-061]upon this bill in the last days of the session.  There is no time


[059-062]to consider it unless it is instantly defeated.  This would probably


[059-063]be a safe course, and yet, by accident, there may be some good


[059-064]things in this bill that ought to be preserved, and certainly the


[059-065]Democratic party ought to regard it as a compliment to keep it long


[059-066]enough to read it.


[059-067]The interests involved are great--there are the commercial and


[059-068]industrial interests of sixty millions of people.  These questions


[059-069]touch the prosperity of the Republic.  Every person under the flag


[059-070]has a direct interest in the solution of these questions.  The end


[059-071]that is now arrived at, the policy now adopted, may and probably


[059-072]will last for many years.  One can hardly overestimate the immensity


[059-073]of the interests at stake.  A man dealing with his own affairs


[059-074]should take time to consider; he should give himself the benefit


[059-075]of his best judgment.  When acting for others he should do no less.


[059-076]The Senators represent, or should represent, not only their own


[059-077]views, but above these things they represent the material interests


[059-078]of their constituents, of their States, and to this trust they must


[059-079]be true, and in order to be true, they must understand the material


[059-080]interests of their States, and in order to be faithful, they must


[059-081]understand how the proposed changes in the tariff will affect these


[059-082]interests.  This cannot be done in a moment.


[059-083]In my judgment, the best way is for the Senate, through the proper


[059-084]committee, to hear testimony, to hear the views of intelligent men,


[059-085]of interested men, of prejudiced men--that is to say, they should


[059-086]look at the question from all sides.


[059-087]_Question_.  The Senate is almost tied; do you think that any


[059-088]Republicans are likely to vote in the interest of the President's


[059-089]policy at this session?


[059-090]_Answer_.  Of course I cannot pretend to answer that question from


[059-091]any special knowledge, or on any information that others are not


[059-092]in possession of.  My idea is simply this:  That a majority of the


[059-093]Senators are opposed to the President's policy.  A majority of the


[059-094]Senate will, in my judgment, sustain the Republican policy; that


[059-095]is to say, they will stand by the American system.  A majority of


[059-096]the Senate, I think, know that it will be impossible for us to


[059-097]compete in the markets of the world with those nations in which


[059-098]labor is far cheaper than it is in the United States, and that when


[059-099]you make the raw material just the same, you have not overcome the


[059-100]difference in labor, and until this is overcome we cannot successfully


[059-101]compete in the markets of the world with those countries where


[059-102]labor is cheaper.  And there are only two ways to overcome this


[059-103]difficulty--either the price of labor must go up in the other


[059-104]countries or must go down in this.  I do not believe that a majority


[059-105]of the Senate can be induced to vote for a policy that will decrease


[059-106]the wages of American workingmen.


[059-107]There is this curious thing:  The President started out blowing


[059-108]the trumpet of free trade.  It gave, as the Democrats used to say,


[059-109]"no uncertain sound."  He blew with all his might.  Messrs. Morrison,


[059-110]Carlisle, Mills and many others joined the band.  When the Mills


[059-111]Bill was introduced it was heralded as the legitimate offspring of


[059-112]the President's message.  When the Democratic convention at St.


[059-113]Louis met, the declaration was made that the President's message,


[059-114]the Mills Bill, the Democratic platform of 1884 and the Democratic


[059-115]platform of 1888, were all the same--all segments of one circle;


[059-116]in fact, they were like modern locomotives--"all the parts


[059-117]interchangeable."  As soon as the Republican convention met, made


[059-118]its platform and named its candidates, it is not free trade, but


[059-119]freer trade; and now Mr. Mills, in the last speech that he was


[059-120]permitted to make in favor of his bill, endeavored to show that it


[059-121]was a high protective tariff measure.


[059-122]This is what lawyers call "a departure in pleading."  That is to


[059-123]say, it is a case that ought to be beaten on demurrer.


[059-124]--_New York Press_, July 29, 1888.


[059-125]SOCIETY AND ITS CRIMINALS*


[059-126][* Col. Robert G. Ingersoll was greatly interested in


[059-127]securing for Chiara Cignarale a commutation of the death


[059-128]sentence to imprisonment for life.  In view of the fact that


[059-129]the great Agnostic has made a close study of capital


[059-130]punishment, a reporter for the _World_ called upon him a day


[059-131]or two ago for an interview touching modern reformatory


[059-132]measures and the punishment of criminals.  Speaking


[059-133]generally on the subject Colonel Ingersoll said:]


[059-134]I suppose that society--that is to say, a state or a nation--has


[059-135]the right of self-defence.  It is impossible to maintain society--


[059-136]that is to say, to protect the rights of individuals in life, in


[059-137]property, in reputation, and in the various pursuits known as trades


[059-138]and professions, without in some way taking care of those who


[059-139]violate these rights.  The principal object of all government should


[059-140]be to protect those in the right from those in the wrong.  There


[059-141]are a vast number of people who need to be protected who are unable,


[059-142]by reason of the defects in their minds and by the countless


[059-143]circumstances that enter into the question of making a living, to


[059-144]protect themselves.  Among the barbarians there was, comparatively


[059-145]speaking, but little difference.  A living was made by fishing and


[059-146]hunting.  These arts were simple and easily learned.  The principal


[059-147]difference in barbarians consisted in physical strength and courage.


[059-148]As a consequence, there were comparatively few failures.  Most men


[059-149]were on an equality.  Now that we are somewhat civilized, life has


[059-150]become wonderfully complex.  There are hundreds of arts, trades,


[059-151]and professions, and in every one of these there is great


[059-152]competition.


[059-153]Besides all this, something is needed every moment.  Civilized man


[059-154]has less credit than the barbarian.  There is something by which


[059-155]everything can be paid for, including the smallest services.


[059-156]Everybody demands payment, and he who fails to pay is a failure.


[059-157]Owing to the competition, owing to the complexity of modern life,


[059-158]owing to the thousand things that must be known in order to succeed


[059-159]in any direction, on either side of the great highway that is called


[059-160]Progress, are innumerable wrecks.  As a rule, failure in some honest


[059-161]direction, or at least in some useful employment, is the dawn of


[059-162]crime.  People who are prosperous, people who by reasonable labor


[059-163]can make a reasonable living, who, having a little leisure can lay


[059-164]in a little for the winter that comes to all, are honest.


[059-165]As a rule, reasonable prosperity is virtuous.  I don't say great


[059-166]prosperity, because it is very hard for the average man to withstand


[059-167]extremes.  When people fail under this law, or rather this fact,


[059-168]of the survival of the fittest, they endeavor to do by some illegal


[059-169]way that which they failed to do in accordance with law.  Persons


[059-170]driven from the highway take to the fields, and endeavor to reach


[059-171]their end or object in some shorter way, by some quicker path,


[059-172]regardless of its being right or wrong.


[059-173]I have said this much to show that I regard criminals as unfortunates.


[059-174]Most people regard those who violate the law with hatred.  They do


[059-175]not take into consideration the circumstances.  They do not believe


[059-176]that man is perpetually acted upon.  They throw out of consideration


[059-177]the effect of poverty, of necessity, and above all, of opportunity.


[059-178]For these reasons they regard criminals with feelings of revenge.


[059-179]They wish to see them punished.  They want them imprisoned or


[059-180]hanged.  They do not think the law has been vindicated unless


[059-181]somebody has been outraged.  I look at these things from an entirely


[059-182]different point of view.  I regard these people who are in the


[059-183]clutches of the law not only as unfortunates, but, for the most


[059-184]part, as victims.  You may call them victims of nature, or of


[059-185]nations, or of governments; it makes no difference, they are victims.


[059-186]Under the same circumstances the very persons who punish them would


[059-187]be punished.  But whether the criminal is a victim or not, the


[059-188]honest man, the industrious man, has the right to defend the product


[059-189]of his labor.  He who sows and plows should be allowed to reap,


[059-190]and he who endeavors to take from him his harvest is what we call


[059-191]a criminal; and it is the business of society to protect the honest


[059-192]from the dishonest.
