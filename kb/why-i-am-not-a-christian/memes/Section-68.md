[067-001]A few years ago people were satisfied when the minister said:  "All


[067-002]this will be made even in another world; a crust-eater here will


[067-003]sit at the head of the banquet there, and the king here will beg


[067-004]for the crumbs that fall from the table there."  When this was


[067-005]said, the poor man hoped and the king laughed.  A few years ago


[067-006]the church said to the slave:  "You will be free in another world,


[067-007]and your freedom will be made glorious by the perpetual spectacle


[067-008]of your master in hell."  But the people--that is, many of the


[067-009]people--are no longer deceived by what once were considered fine


[067-010]phrases.  They have suffered so much that they no longer wish to


[067-011]see others suffer and no longer think of the suffering of others


[067-012]as a source of joy to themselves.  The poor see that the eternal


[067-013]starvation of kings and queens in another world will be no compensation


[067-014]for what they have suffered there.  The old religions appear vulgar


[067-015]and the ideas of rewards and punishments are only such as would


[067-016]satisfy a cannibal chief or one of his favorites.


[067-017]_Question_.  Do you think the Christian religion has made the world


[067-018]better?


[067-019]_Answer_.  For many centuries there has been preached and taught


[067-020]in an almost infinite number of ways a supernatural religion.


[067-021]During all this time the world has been in the care of the Infinite,


[067-022]and yet every imaginable vice has flourished, every imaginable pang


[067-023]has been suffered, and every injustice has been done.  During all


[067-024]these years the priests have enslaved the minds, and the kings the


[067-025]bodies, of men.  The priests did what they did in the name of God,


[067-026]and the kings appeal to the same source of authority.  Man suffered


[067-027]as long as he could.  Revolution, reformation, was simply a re-


[067-028]action, a cry from the poor wretch that was between the upper and


[067-029]the nether millstone.  The liberty of man has increased just in


[067-030]the proportion that the authority of the gods has decreased.  In


[067-031]other words, the wants of man, instead of the wishes of God, have


[067-032]inaugurated what we call progress, and there is this difference:


[067-033]Theology is based upon the narrowest and intensest form of selfishness.


[067-034]Of course, the theologian knows, the Christian knows, that he can


[067-035]do nothing for God; consequently all that he does must be and is


[067-036]for himself, his object being to win the approbation of this God,


[067-037]to the end that he may become a favorite.  On the other side, men


[067-038]touched not only by their own misfortunes, but by the misfortunes


[067-039]of others, are moved not simply by selfishness, but by a splendid


[067-040]sympathy with their fellow-men.


[067-041]_Question_.  Christianity certainly fosters charity?


[067-042]_Answer_.  Nothing is more cruel than orthodox theology, nothing


[067-043]more heartless than a charitable institution.  For instance, in


[067-044]England, think for a moment of the manner in which charities are


[067-045]distributed, the way in which the crust is flung at Lazarus.  If


[067-046]that parable could be now retold, the dogs would bite him.  The


[067-047]same is true in this country.  The institution has nothing but


[067-048]contempt for the one it relieves.  The people in charge regard the


[067-049]pauper as one who has wrecked himself.  They feel very much as a


[067-050]man would feel rescuing from the water some hare-brained wretch


[067-051]who had endeavored to swim the rapids of Niagara--the moment they


[067-052]reach him they begin to upbraid him for being such a fool.  This


[067-053]course makes charity a hypocrite, with every pauper for its enemy.


[067-054]Mrs. Ward compelled Robert Elsmere to perceive, in some slight


[067-055]degree, the failure of Christianity to do away with vice and


[067-056]suffering, with poverty and crime.  We know that the rich care but


[067-057]little for the poor.  No matter how religious the rich may be, the


[067-058]sufferings of their fellows have but little effect upon them.  We


[067-059]are also beginning to see that what is called charity will never


[067-060]redeem this world.


[067-061]The poor man willing to work, eager to maintain his independence,


[067-062]knows that there is something higher than charity--that is to say,


[067-063]justice.  He finds that many years before he was born his country


[067-064]was divided out between certain successful robbers, flatterers,


[067-065]cringers and crawlers, and that in consequence of such division


[067-066]not only he himself, but a large majority of his fellow-men are


[067-067]tenants, renters, occupying the surface of the earth only at the


[067-068]pleasure of others.  He finds, too, that these people who have done


[067-069]nothing and who do nothing, have everything, and that those who do


[067-070]everything have but little.  He finds that idleness has the money


[067-071]and that the toilers are compelled to bow to the idlers.  He finds


[067-072]also that the young men of genius are bribed by social distinctions


[067-073]--unconsciously it may be--but still bribed in a thousand ways.


[067-074]He finds that the church is a kind of waste-basket into which are


[067-075]thrown the younger sons of titled idleness.


[067-076]_Question_.  Do you consider that society in general has been made


[067-077]better by religious influences?


[067-078]_Answer_.  Society is corrupted because the laurels, the titles,


[067-079]are in the keeping and within the gift of the corrupters.  Christianity


[067-080]is not an enemy of this system--it is in harmony with it.  Christianity


[067-081]reveals to us a universe presided over by an infinite autocrat--a


[067-082]universe without republicanism, without democracy--a universe where


[067-083]all power comes from one and the same source, and where everyone


[067-084]using authority is accountable, not to the people, but to this


[067-085]supposed source of authority.  Kings reign by divine right.  Priests


[067-086]are ordained in a divinely appointed way--they do not get their


[067-087]office from man.  Man is their servant, not their master.


[067-088]In the story of Robert Elsmere all there is of Christianity is left


[067-089]except the miraculous.  Theism remains, and the idea of a protecting


[067-090]Providence is left, together with a belief in the immeasurable


[067-091]superiority of Jesus Christ.  That is to say, the miracles are


[067-092]discarded for lack of evidence, and only for lack of evidence; not


[067-093]on the ground that they are impossible, not on the ground that they


[067-094]impeach and deny the integrity of cause and effect, not on the


[067-095]ground that they contradict the self-evident proposition that an


[067-096]effect must have an efficient cause, but like the Scotch verdict,


[067-097]"not proven."  It is an effort to save and keep in repair the


[067-098]dungeons of the Inquisition for the sake of the beauty of the vines


[067-099]that have overrun them.  Many people imagine that falsehoods may


[067-100]become respectable on account of age, that a certain reverence goes


[067-101]with antiquity, and that if a mistake is covered with the moss of


[067-102]sentiment it is altogether more credible than a parvenu fact.  They


[067-103]endeavor to introduce the idea of aristocracy into the world of


[067-104]thought, believing, and honestly believing, that a falsehood long


[067-105]believed is far superior to a truth that is generally denied.


[067-106]_Question_.  If Robert Elsmere's views were commonly adopted what


[067-107]would be the effect?


[067-108]_Answer_.  The new religion of Elsmere is, after all, only a system


[067-109]of outdoor relief, an effort to get successful piracy to give up


[067-110]a larger per cent. for the relief of its victims.  The abolition


[067-111]of the system is not dreamed of.  A civilized minority could not


[067-112]by any possibility be happy while a majority of the world were


[067-113]miserable.  A civilized majority could not be happy while a minority


[067-114]were miserable.  As a matter of fact, a civilized world could not


[067-115]be happy while one man was really miserable.  At the foundation of


[067-116]civilization is justice--that is to say, the giving of an equal


[067-117]opportunity to all the children of men.  Secondly, there can be no


[067-118]civilization in the highest sense until sympathy becomes universal.


[067-119]We must have a new definition for success.  We must have new ideals.


[067-120]The man who succeeds in amassing wealth, who gathers money for


[067-121]himself, is not a success.  It is an exceedingly low ambition to


[067-122]be rich to excite the envy of others, or for the sake of the vulgar


[067-123]power it gives to triumph over others.  Such men are failures.  So


[067-124]the man who wins fame, position, power, and wins these for the sake


[067-125]of himself, and wields this power not for the elevation of his


[067-126]fellow-men, but simply to control, is a miserable failure.  He may


[067-127]dispense thousands of millions in charity, and his charity may be


[067-128]prompted by the meanest part of his nature--using it simply as a


[067-129]bait to catch more fish and to prevent the rising tide of indignation


[067-130]that might overwhelm him.  Men who steal millions and then give a


[067-131]small percentage to the Lord to gain the praise of the clergy and


[067-132]to bring the salvation of their souls within the possibilities of


[067-133]imagination, are all failures.


[067-134]Robert Elsmere gains our affection and our applause to the extent


[067-135]that he gives up what are known as orthodox views, and his wife


[067-136]Catherine retains our respect in the proportion that she lives the


[067-137]doctrine that Elsmere preaches.  By doing what she believes to be


[067-138]right, she gains our forgiveness for her creed.  One is astonished


[067-139]that she can be as good as she is, believing as she does.  The


[067-140]utmost stretch of our intellectual charity is to allow the old wine


[067-141]to be put in a new bottle, and yet she regrets the absence of the


[067-142]old bottle--she really believes that the bottle is the important


[067-143]thing--that the wine is but a secondary consideration.  She misses


[067-144]the label, and not having perfect confidence in her own taste, she


[067-145]does not feel quite sure that the wine is genuine.


[067-146]_Question_.  What, on the whole, is your judgment of the book?


[067-147]_Answer_.  I think the book conservative.  It is an effort to save


[067-148]something--a few shreds and patches and ravelings--from the wreck.


[067-149]Theism is difficult to maintain.  Why should we expect an infinite


[067-150]Being to do better in another world than he has done and is doing


[067-151]in this?  If he allows the innocent to suffer here, why not there?


[067-152]If he allows rascality to succeed in this world, why not in the


[067-153]next?  To believe in God and to deny his personality is an exceedingly


[067-154]vague foundation for a consolation.  If you insist on his personality


[067-155]and power, then it is impossible to account for what happens.  Why


[067-156]should an infinite God allow some of his children to enslave others?


[067-157]Why should he allow a child of his to burn another child of his,


[067-158]under the impression that such a sacrifice was pleasing to him?


[067-159]Unitarianism lacks the motive power.  Orthodox people who insist


[067-160]that nearly everybody is going to hell, and that it is their duty


[067-161]to do what little they can to save their souls, have what you might


[067-162]call a spur to action.  We can imagine a philanthropic man engaged


[067-163]in the business of throwing ropes to persons about to go over the


[067-164]falls of Niagara, but we can hardly think of his carrying on the


[067-165]business after being convinced that there are no falls, or that


[067-166]people go over them in perfect safety.  In this country the question


[067-167]has come up whether all the heathen are bound to be damned unless


[067-168]they believe in the gospel.  Many admit that the heathen will be


[067-169]saved if they are good people, and that they will not be damned


[067-170]for not believing something that they never heard.  The really


[067-171]orthodox people--that is to say, the missionaries--instantly see


[067-172]that this doctrine destroys their business.  They take the ground


[067-173]that there is but one way to be saved--you must believe on the Lord


[067-174]Jesus Christ--and they are willing to admit, and cheerfully to


[067-175]admit, that the heathen for many generations have gone in an unbroken


[067-176]column down to eternal wrath.  And they not only admit this, but


[067-177]insist upon it, to the end that subscriptions may not cease.  With


[067-178]them salary and salvation are convertible terms.


[067-179]The tone of this book is not of the highest.  Too much stress is


[067-180]laid upon social advantages--too much respect for fashionable folly


[067-181]and for ancient absurdity.  It is hard for me to appreciate the


[067-182]feelings of one who thinks it difficult to give up the consolations


[067-183]of the gospel.  What are the consolations of the Church of England?


[067-184]It is a religion imposed upon the people by authority.  It is the


[067-185]gospel at the mouth of a cannon, at the point of a bayonet, enforced


[067-186]by all authority, from the beadle to the Queen.  It is a parasite


[067-187]living upon tithes--these tithes being collected by the army and


[067-188]navy.  It produces nothing--is simply a beggar--or rather an


[067-189]aggregation of beggars.  It teaches nothing of importance.  It


[067-190]discovers nothing.  It is under obligation not to investigate.  It


[067-191]has agreed to remain stationary not only, but to resist all


[067-192]innovation.  According to the creed of this church, a very large


[067-193]proportion of the human race is destined to suffer eternal pain.


[067-194]This does not interfere with the quiet, with the serenity and repose


[067-195]of the average clergyman.  They put on their gowns, they read the


[067-196]service, they repeat the creed and feel that their duty has been


[067-197]done.  How any one can feel that he is giving up something of value


[067-198]when he finds that the Episcopal creed is untrue is beyond my


[067-199]imagination.  I should think that every good man and woman would


[067-200]overflow with joy, that every heart would burst into countless


[067-201]blossoms the moment the falsity of the Episcopal creed was


[067-202]established.
