[P01-012]THE BIBLE AND A FUTURE LIFE


[P01-013]_Question_.  Colonel, are your views of religion based upon the


[P01-014]Bible?


[P01-015]_Answer_.  I regard the Bible, especially the Old Testament, the


[P01-016]same as I do most other ancient books, in which there is some truth,


[P01-017]a great deal of error, considerable barbarism and a most plentiful


[P01-018]lack of good sense.


[P01-019]_Question_.  Have you found any other work, sacred or profane,


[P01-020]which you regard as more reliable?


[P01-021]_Answer_.  I know of no book less so, in my judgment.


[P01-022]_Question_.  You have studied the Bible attentively, have you not?


[P01-023]_Answer_.  I have read the Bible.  I have heard it talked about a


[P01-024]good deal, and am sufficiently well acquainted with it to justify


[P01-025]my own mind in utterly rejecting all claims made for its divine


[P01-026]origin.


[P01-027]_Question_.  What do you base your views upon?


[P01-028]_Answer_.  On reason, observation, experience, upon the discoveries


[P01-029]in science, upon observed facts and the analogies properly growing


[P01-030]out of such facts.  I have no confidence in anything pretending to


[P01-031]be outside, or independent of, or in any manner above nature.


[P01-032]_Question_.  According to your views, what disposition is made of


[P01-033]man after death?


[P01-034]_Answer_.  Upon that subject I know nothing.  It is no more wonderful


[P01-035]that man should live again than he now lives; upon that question


[P01-036]I know of no evidence.  The doctrine of immortality rests upon


[P01-037]human affection.  We love, therefore we wish to live.


[P01-038]_Question_.  Then you would not undertake to say what becomes of


[P01-039]man after death?


[P01-040]_Answer_.  If I told or pretended to know what becomes of man after


[P01-041]death, I would be as dogmatic as are theologians upon this question.


[P01-042]The difference between them and me is, I am honest.  I admit that


[P01-043]I do not know.


[P01-044]_Question_.  Judging by your criticism of mankind, Colonel, in your


[P01-045]recent lecture, you have not found his condition very satisfactory?


[P01-046]_Answer_.  Nature, outside of man, so far as I know, is neither


[P01-047]cruel nor merciful.  I am not satisfied with the present condition


[P01-048]of the human race, nor with the condition of man during any period


[P01-049]of which we have any knowledge.  I believe, however, the condition


[P01-050]of man is improved, and this improvement is due to his own exertions.


[P01-051]I do not make nature a being.  I do not ascribe to nature


[P01-052]intentions.


[P01-053]_Question_.  Is your theory, Colonel, the result of investigation


[P01-054]of the subject?


[P01-055]_Answer_.  No one can control his own opinion or his own belief.


[P01-056]My belief was forced upon me by my surroundings.  I am the product


[P01-057]of all circumstances that have in any way touched me.  I believe


[P01-058]in this world.  I have no confidence in any religion promising joys


[P01-059]in another world at the expense of liberty and happiness in this.


[P01-060]At the same time, I wish to give others all the rights I claim for


[P01-061]myself.


[P01-062]_Question_.  If I asked for proofs for your theory, what would you


[P01-063]furnish?


[P01-064]_Answer_.  The experience of every man who is honest with himself,


[P01-065]every fact that has been discovered in nature.  In addition to


[P01-066]these, the utter and total failure of all religionists in all


[P01-067]countries to produce one particle of evidence showing the existence


[P01-068]of any supernatural power whatever, and the further fact that the


[P01-069]people are not satisfied with their religion.  They are continually


[P01-070]asking for evidence.  They are asking it in every imaginable way.


[P01-071]The sects are continually dividing.  There is no real religious


[P01-072]serenity in the world.  All religions are opponents of intellectual


[P01-073]liberty.  I believe in absolute mental freedom.  Real religion with


[P01-074]me is a thing not of the head, but of the heart; not a theory, not


[P01-075]a creed, but a life.


[P01-076]_Question_.  What punishment, then, is inflicted upon man for his


[P01-077]crimes and wrongs committed in this life?


[P01-078]_Answer_.  There is no such thing as intellectual crime.  No man


[P01-079]can commit a mental crime.  To become a crime it must go beyond


[P01-080]thought.


[P01-081]_Question_.  What punishment is there for physical crime?


[P01-082]_Answer_.  Such punishment as is necessary to protect society and


[P01-083]for the reformation of the criminal.


[P01-084]_Question_.  If there is only punishment in this world, will not


[P01-085]some escape punishment?


[P01-086]_Answer_.  I admit that all do not seem to be punished as they


[P01-087]deserve.  I also admit that all do not seem to be rewarded as they


[P01-088]deserve; and there is in this world, apparently, as great failures


[P01-089]in matter of reward as in matter of punishment.  If there is another


[P01-090]life, a man will be happier there for acting according to his


[P01-091]highest ideal in this.  But I do not discern in nature any effort


[P01-092]to do justice.


[P01-093]--_The Post_, Washington, D. C., 1878.


[P01-094]MRS. VAN COTT, THE REVIVALIST


[P01-095]_Question_.  I see, Colonel, that in an interview published this


[P01-096]morning, Mrs. Van Cott (the revivalist), calls you "a poor barking


[P01-097]dog."  Do you know her personally?


[P01-098]_Answer_.  I have never met or seen her.


[P01-099]_Question_.  Do you know the reason she applied the epithet?


[P01-100]_Answer_.  I suppose it to be the natural result of what is called


[P01-101]vital piety; that is to say, universal love breeds individual


[P01-102]hatred.


[P01-103]_Question_.  Do you intend making any reply to what she says?


[P01-104]_Answer_.  I have written her a note of which this is a copy:


[P01-105]_Buffalo, Feb. 24th, 1878._


[P01-106]MRS. VAN COTT;


[P01-107]My dear Madam:--Were you constrained by the love of Christ to call


[P01-108]a man who has never injured you "a poor barking dog?"  Did you make


[P01-109]this remark as a Christian, or as a lady?  Did you say these words


[P01-110]to illustrate in some faint degree the refining influence upon


[P01-111]women of the religion you preach?


[P01-112]What would you think of me if I should retort, using your language,


[P01-113]changing only the sex of the last word?


[P01-114]I have the honor to remain,


[P01-115]Yours truly,


[P01-116]R. G. INGERSOLL


[P01-117]_Question_.  Well, what do you think of the religious revival system


[P01-118]generally?


[P01-119]_Answer_.  The fire that has to be blown all the time is a poor


[P01-120]thing to get warm by.  I regard these revivals as essentially


[P01-121]barbaric.  I think they do no good, but much harm, they make innocent


[P01-122]people think they are guilty, and very mean people think they are


[P01-123]good.


[P01-124]_Question_.  What is your opinion concerning women as conductors


[P01-125]of these revivals?


[P01-126]_Answer_.  I suppose those engaged in them think they are doing


[P01-127]good.  They are probably honest.  I think, however, that neither


[P01-128]men nor women should be engaged in frightening people into heaven.


[P01-129]That is all I wish to say on the subject, as I do not think it


[P01-130]worth talking about.


[P01-131]--_The Express_, Buffalo, New York, Feb., 1878.


[P01-132]EUROPEAN TRIP AND GREENBACK QUESTION


[P01-133]_Question_.  What did you do on your European trip, Colonel?


[P01-134]_Answer_.  I went with my family from New York to Southampton,


[P01-135]England, thence to London, and from London to Edinburgh.  In Scotland


[P01-136]I visited every place where Burns had lived, from the cottage where


[P01-137]he was born to the room where he died.  I followed him from the


[P01-138]cradle to the coffin.  I went to Stratford-upon-Avon for the purpose


[P01-139]of seeing all that I could in any way connected with Shakespeare;


[P01-140]next to London, where we visited again all the places of interest,


[P01-141]and thence to Paris, where we spent a couple of weeks in the


[P01-142]Exposition.


[P01-143]_Question_.  And what did you think of it?


[P01-144]_Answer_.  So far as machinery--so far as the practical is concerned,


[P01-145]it is not equal to ours in Philadelphia; in art it is incomparably


[P01-146]beyond it.  I was very much gratified to find so much evidence in


[P01-147]favor of my theory that the golden age in art is in front of us;


[P01-148]that mankind has been advancing, that we did not come from a perfect


[P01-149]pair and immediately commence to degenerate.  The modern painters


[P01-150]and sculptors are far better and grander than the ancient.  I think


[P01-151]we excel in fine arts as much as we do in agricultural implements.


[P01-152]Nothing pleased me more than the painting from Holland, because


[P01-153]they idealized and rendered holy the ordinary avocations of life.


[P01-154]They paint cottages with sweet mothers and children; they paint


[P01-155]homes.  They are not much on Ariadnes and Venuses, but they paint


[P01-156]good women.


[P01-157]_Question_.  What did you think of the American display?


[P01-158]_Answer_.  Our part of the Exposition is good, but nothing to what


[P01-159]is should and might have been, but we bring home nearly as many


[P01-160]medals as we took things.  We lead the world in machinery and in


[P01-161]ingenious inventions, and some of our paintings were excellent.


[P01-162]_Question_.  Colonel, crossing the Atlantic back to America, what


[P01-163]do you think of the Greenback movement?


[P01-164]_Answer_.  In regard to the Greenback party, in the first place,


[P01-165]I am not a believer in miracles.  I do not believe that something


[P01-166]can be made out of nothing.  The Government, in my judgment, cannot


[P01-167]create money; the Government can give its note, like an individual,


[P01-168]and the prospect of its being paid determines its value.  We have


[P01-169]already substantially resumed.  Every piece of property that has


[P01-170]been shrinking has simply been resuming.  We expended during the


[P01-171]war--not for the useful, but for the useless, not to build up, but


[P01-172]to destroy--at least one thousand million dollars.  The Government


[P01-173]was an enormous purchaser; when the war ceased the industries of


[P01-174]the country lost their greatest customer.  As a consequence there


[P01-175]was a surplus of production, and consequently a surplus of labor.


[P01-176]At last we have gotten back, and the country since the war has


[P01-177]produced over and above the cost of production, something near the


[P01-178]amount that was lost during the war.  Our exports are about two


[P01-179]hundred million dollars more than our imports, and this is a healthy


[P01-180]sign.  There are, however, five or six hundred thousand men,


[P01-181]probably, out of employment; as prosperity increases this number


[P01-182]will decrease.  I am in favor of the Government doing something to


[P01-183]ameliorate the condition of these men.  I would like to see


[P01-184]constructed the Northern and Southern Pacific railroads; this would


[P01-185]give employment at once to many thousands, and homes after awhile


[P01-186]to millions.  All the signs of the times to me are good.  The


[P01-187]wretched bankrupt law, at last, is wiped from the statute books,


[P01-188]and honest people in a short time can get plenty of credit.  This


[P01-189]law should have been repealed years before it was.  It would have


[P01-190]been far better to have had all who have gone into bankruptcy during


[P01-191]these frightful years to have done so at once.


[P01-192]_Question_.  What will be the political effect of the Greenback


[P01-193]movement?


[P01-194]_Answer_.  The effect in Maine has been to defeat the Republican


[P01-195]party.  I do not believe any party can permanently succeed in the


[P01-196]United States that does not believe in and advocate actual money.


[P01-197]I want to see the greenback equal with gold the world round.  A


[P01-198]money below par keeps the people below par.  No man can possibly


[P01-199]be proud of a country that is not willing to pay its debts.  Several


[P01-200]of the States this fall may be carried by the Greenback party, but


[P01-201]if I have a correct understanding of their views, that party cannot


[P01-202]hold any State for any great length of time.  But all the men of


[P01-203]wealth should remember that everybody in the community has got, in


[P01-204]some way, to be supported.  I want to see them so that they can


[P01-205]support themselves by their own labor.  In my judgment real prosperity


[P01-206]will begin with actual resumption, because confidence will then


[P01-207]return.  If the workingmen of the United States cannot make their


[P01-208]living, cannot have the opportunity to labor, they have got to be


[P01-209]supported in some way, and in any event, I want to see a liberal


[P01-210]policy inaugurated by the Government.  I believe in improving rivers


[P01-211]and harbors.
