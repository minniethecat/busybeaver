[040-001]--_Philadelphia Times_, September 25, 1885.


[040-002]SOME LIVE TOPICS.


[040-003]_Question_.  Shall you attend the Albany Freethought Convention?


[040-004]_Answer_.  I have agreed to be present not only, but to address


[040-005]the convention, on Sunday, the 13th of September.  I am greatly


[040-006]gratified to know that the interest in the question of intellectual


[040-007]liberty is growing from year to year.  Everywhere I go it seems to


[040-008]be the topic of conversation.  No matter upon what subject people


[040-009]begin to talk, in a little while the discussion takes a religious


[040-010]turn, and people who a few moments before had not the slightest


[040-011]thought of saying a word about the churches, or about the Bible,


[040-012]are giving their opinions in full.  I hear discussions of this kind


[040-013]in all the public conveyances, at the hotels, on the piazzas at


[040-014]the seaside--and they are not discussions in which I take any part,


[040-015]because I rarely say anything upon these questions except in public,


[040-016]unless I am directly addressed.


[040-017]There is a general feeling that the church has ruled the world long


[040-018]enough.  People are beginning to see that no amount of eloquence,


[040-019]or faith, or erudition, or authority, can make the records of


[040-020]barbarism satisfactory to the heart and brain of this century.


[040-021]They have also found that a falsehood in Hebrew in no more credible


[040-022]than in plain English.  People at last are beginning to be satisfied


[040-023]that cruel laws were never good laws, no matter whether inspired


[040-024]or uninspired.  The Christian religion, like every other religion


[040-025]depending upon inspired writings, is wrecked upon the facts of


[040-026]nature.  So long as inspired writers confined themselves to the


[040-027]supernatural world; so long as they talked about angels and Gods


[040-028]and heavens and hells; so long as they described only things that


[040-029]man has never seen, and never will see, they were safe, not from


[040-030]contradiction, but from demonstration.  But these writings had to


[040-031]have a foundation, even for their falsehoods, and that foundation


[040-032]was in Nature.  The foundation had to be something about which


[040-033]somebody knew something, or supposed they knew something.  They


[040-034]told something about this world that agreed with the then general


[040-035]opinion.  Had these inspired writers told the truth about Nature--


[040-036]had they said that the world revolved on its axis, and made a


[040-037]circuit about the sun--they could have gained no credence for their


[040-038]statements about other worlds.  They were forced to agree with


[040-039]their contemporaries about this world, and there is where they made


[040-040]the fundamental mistake.  Having grown in knowledge, the world has


[040-041]discovered that these inspired men knew nothing about this earth;


[040-042]that the inspired books are filled with mistakes--not only mistakes


[040-043]that we can contradict, but mistakes that we can demonstrate to be


[040-044]mistakes.  Had they told the truth in their day, about this earth,


[040-045]they would not have been believed about other worlds, because their


[040-046]contemporaries would have used their own knowledge about this world


[040-047]to test the knowledge of these inspired men.  We pursue the same


[040-048]course; and what we know about this world we use as the standard,


[040-049]and by that standard we have found that the inspired men knew


[040-050]nothing about Nature as it is.  Finding that they were mistaken


[040-051]about this world, we have no confidence in what they have said


[040-052]about another.  Every religion has had its philosophy about this


[040-053]world, and every one has been mistaken.  As education becomes


[040-054]general, as scientific modes are adopted, this will become clearer


[040-055]and clearer, until "ignorant as inspiration" will be a comparison.


[040-056]_Question_.  Have you seen the memorial to the New York Legislature,


[040-057]to be presented this winter, asking for the repeal of such laws as


[040-058]practically unite church and state?


[040-059]_Answer_.  I have seen a memorial asking that church property be


[040-060]taxed like other property; that no more money should be appropriated


[040-061]from the public treasury for the support of institutions managed


[040-062]by and in the interest of sectarian denominations; for the repeal


[040-063]of all laws compelling the observance of Sunday as a religious day.


[040-064]Such memorials ought to be addressed to the Legislatures of all


[040-065]the States.  The money of the public should only be used for the


[040-066]benefit of the public.  Public money should not be used for what


[040-067]a few gentlemen think is for the benefit of the public.  Personally,


[040-068]I think it would be for the benefit of the public to have Infidel


[040-069]or scientific--which is the same thing--lectures delivered in every


[040-070]town, in every State, on every Sunday; but knowing that a great


[040-071]many men disagree with me on this point, I do not claim that such


[040-072]lectures ought to be paid for with public money.  The Methodist


[040-073]Church ought not to be sustained by taxation, nor the Catholic,


[040-074]nor any other church.  To relieve their property from taxation is


[040-075]to appropriate money, to the extent of that tax, for the support


[040-076]of that church.  Whenever a burden is lifted from one piece of


[040-077]property, it is distributed over the rest of the property of the


[040-078]State, and to release one kind of property is to increase the tax


[040-079]on all other kinds.


[040-080]There was a time when people really supposed the churches were


[040-081]saving souls from the eternal wrath of a God of infinite love.


[040-082]Being engaged in such a philanthropic work, and at the time nobody


[040-083]having the courage to deny it--the church being all-powerful--all


[040-084]other property was taxed to support the church; but now the more


[040-085]civilized part of the community, being satisfied that a God of


[040-086]infinite love will not be eternally unjust, feel as though the


[040-087]church should support herself.  To exempt the church from taxation


[040-088]is to pay a part of the priest's salary.  The Catholic now objects


[040-089]to being taxed to support a school in which his religion is not


[040-090]taught.  He is not satisfied with the school that says nothing on


[040-091]the subject of religion.  He insists that it is an outrage to tax


[040-092]him to support a school where the teacher simply teaches what he


[040-093]knows.  And yet this same Catholic wants his church exempted from


[040-094]taxation, and the tax of an Atheist or of a Jew increased, when he


[040-095]teaches in his untaxed church that the Atheist and Jew will both


[040-096]be eternally damned!  Is it possible for impudence to go further?


[040-097]I insist that no religion should be taught in any school supported


[040-098]by public money; and by religion I mean superstition.  Only that


[040-099]should be taught in a school that somebody can learn and that


[040-100]somebody can know.  In my judgment, every church should be taxed


[040-101]precisely the same as other property.  The church may claim that


[040-102]it is one of the instruments of civilization and therefore should


[040-103]be exempt.  If you exempt that which is useful, you exempt every


[040-104]trade and every profession.  In my judgment, theatres have done


[040-105]more to civilize mankind than churches; that is to say, theatres


[040-106]have done something to civilize mankind--churches nothing.  The


[040-107]effect of all superstition has been to render men barbarous.  I do


[040-108]not believe in the civilizing effects of falsehood.


[040-109]There was a time when ministers were supposed to be in the employ


[040-110]of God, and it was thought that God selected them with great care


[040-111]--that their profession had something sacred about it.  These ideas


[040-112]are no longer entertained by sensible people.  Ministers should be


[040-113]paid like other professional men, and those who like their preaching


[040-114]should pay for the preach.  They should depend, as actors do, upon


[040-115]their popularity, upon the amount of sense, or nonsense, that they


[040-116]have for sale.  They should depend upon the market like other


[040-117]people, and if people do not want to hear sermons badly enough to


[040-118]build churches and pay for them, and pay the taxes on them, and


[040-119]hire the preacher, let the money be diverted to some other use.


[040-120]The pulpit should no longer be a pauper.  I do not believe in


[040-121]carrying on any business with the contribution box.  All the


[040-122]sectarian institutions ought to support themselves.  These should


[040-123]be no Methodist or Catholic or Presbyterian hospitals or orphan


[040-124]asylums.  All these should be supported by the State.  There is no


[040-125]such thing as Catholic charity, or Methodist charity.  Charity


[040-126]belongs to humanity, not to any particular form of faith or religion.


[040-127]You will find as charitable people who never heard of religion, as


[040-128]you can find in the church.  The State should provide for those


[040-129]who ought to be provided for.  A few Methodists beg of everybody


[040-130]they meet--send women with subscription papers, asking money from


[040-131]all classes of people, and nearly everybody gives something from


[040-132]politeness, or to keep from being annoyed; and when the institution


[040-133]is finished, it is pointed at as the result of Methodism.


[040-134]Probably a majority of the people in this country suppose that


[040-135]there was no charity in the world until the Christian religion was


[040-136]founded.  Great men have repeated this falsehood, until ignorance


[040-137]and thoughtlessness believe it.  There were orphan asylums in China,


[040-138]in India, and in Egypt thousands of years before Christ was born;


[040-139]and there certainly never was a time in the history of the whole


[040-140]world when there was less charity in Europe than during the centuries


[040-141]when the Church of Christ had absolute power.  There were hundreds


[040-142]of Mohammedan asylums before Christianity had built ten in the


[040-143]entire world.


[040-144]All institutions for the care of unfortunate people should be


[040-145]secular--should be supported by the State.  The money for the


[040-146]purpose should be raised by taxation, to the end that the burden


[040-147]may be borne by those able to bear it.  As it is now, most of the


[040-148]money is paid, not by the rich, but by the generous, and those most


[040-149]able to help their needy fellow citizens are the very ones who do


[040-150]nothing.  If the money is raised by taxation, then the burden will


[040-151]fall where it ought to fall, and these institutions will no longer


[040-152]be supported by the generous and emotional, and the rich and stingy


[040-153]will no longer be able to evade the duties of citizenship and of


[040-154]humanity.


[040-155]Now, as to the Sunday laws, we know that they are only spasmodically


[040-156]enforced.  Now and then a few people are arrested for selling papers


[040-157]or cigars.  Some unfortunate barber is grabbed by a policeman


[040-158]because he has been caught shaving a Christian, Sunday morning.


[040-159]Now and then some poor fellow with a hack, trying to make a dollar


[040-160]or two to feed his horses, or to take care of his wife and children,


[040-161]is arrested as though he were a murderer.  But in a few days the


[040-162]public are inconvenienced to that degree that the arrests stop and


[040-163]business goes on in its accustomed channels, Sunday and all.


[040-164]Now and then society becomes so pious, so virtuous, that people


[040-165]are compelled to enter saloons by the back door; others are compelled


[040-166]to drink beer with the front shutters up; but otherwise the stream


[040-167]that goes down the thirsty throats is unbroken.  The ministers have


[040-168]done their best to prevent all recreation on the Sabbath.  They


[040-169]would like to stop all the boats on the Hudson, and on the sea--


[040-170]stop all the excursion trains.  They would like to compel every


[040-171]human being that lives in the city of New York to remain within


[040-172]its limits twenty-four hours every Sunday.  They hate the parks;


[040-173]they hate music; they hate anything that keeps a man away from


[040-174]church.  Most of the churches are empty during the summer, and now


[040-175]most of the ministers leave themselves, and give over the entire


[040-176]city to the Devil and his emissaries.  And yet if the ministers had


[040-177]their way, there would be no form of human enjoyment except prayer,


[040-178]signing subscription papers, putting money in contribution boxes,


[040-179]listening to sermons, reading the cheerful histories of the Old


[040-180]Testament, imagining the joys of heaven and the torments of hell.


[040-181]The church is opposed to the theatre, is the enemy of the opera,


[040-182]looks upon dancing as a crime, hates billiards, despises cards,


[040-183]opposes roller-skating, and even entertains a certain kind of


[040-184]prejudice against croquet.
