[027-001]Polygamy exists.  All laws upon the subject should take that fact


[027-002]into consideration, and punishment should be provided for offences


[027-003]thereafter committed.  The children of Mormons should be legitimized.


[027-004]In other words, in attempting to settle this question, we should


[027-005]accomplish all the good possible, with the least possible harm.


[027-006]I agree mostly with Mr. Beecher, and I utterly disagree with the


[027-007]Rev. Mr. Newman.  Mr. Newman wants to kill and slay.  He does not


[027-008]rely upon Christianity, but upon brute force.  He has lost his


[027-009]confidence in example, and appeals to the bayonet.  Mr. Newman had


[027-010]a discussion with one of the Mormon elders, and was put to ignominious


[027-011]flight; no wonder that he appeals to force.  Having failed in


[027-012]argument, he calls for artillery; having been worsted in the appeal


[027-013]to Scripture, he asks for the sword.  He says, failing to convert,


[027-014]let us kill; and he takes this position in the name of the religion


[027-015]of kindness and forgiveness.


[027-016]Strange that a minister now should throw away the Bible and yell


[027-017]for a bayonet; that he should desert the Scriptures and call for


[027-018]soldiers; that he should lose confidence in the power of the Spirit


[027-019]and trust in a sword.  I recommend that Mormonism be done away with


[027-020]by distributing the Old Testament throughout Utah.


[027-021]_Question_.  What do you think of the investigation of the Department


[027-022]of Justice now going on?


[027-023]_Answer_.  The result, in my judgment, will depend on its thoroughness.


[027-024]If Mr. Springer succeeds in proving exactly what the Department of


[027-025]Justice did, the methods pursued, if he finds out what their spies


[027-026]and detectives and agents were instructed to do, then I think the


[027-027]result will be as disastrous to the Department as beneficial to


[027-028]the country.  The people seem to have forgotten that a little while


[027-029]after the first Star Route trial three of the agents of the Department


[027-030]of Justice were indicted for endeavoring to bribe the jury.  They


[027-031]forget that Mr. Bowen, an agent of the Department of Justice, is


[027-032]a fugitive, because he endeavored to bribe the foreman of the jury.


[027-033]They seem to forget that the Department of Justice, in order to


[027-034]cover its own tracks, had the foreman of the jury indicted because


[027-035]one of its agents endeavored to bribe him.  Probably this investigation


[027-036]will nudge the ribs of the public enough to make people remember


[027-037]these things.  Personally, I have no feelings on the subject.  It


[027-038]was enough for me that we succeeded in thwarting its methods, in


[027-039]spite of the detectives, spies, and informers.


[027-040]The Department is already beginning to dissolve.  Brewster Cameron


[027-041]has left it, and as a reward has been exiled to Arizona.  Mr.


[027-042]Brewster will probably be the next to pack his official valise.


[027-043]A few men endeavored to win popularity by pursuing a few others,


[027-044]and thus far they have been conspicuous failures.  MacVeagh and


[027-045]James are to-day enjoying the oblivion earned by misdirected energy,


[027-046]and Mr. Brewster will soon keep them company.  The history of the


[027-047]world does not furnish an instance of more flagrant abuse of power.


[027-048]There never was a trial as shamelessly conducted by a government.


[027-049]But, as I said before, I have no feeling now except that of pity.


[027-050]_Question_.  I see that Mr. Beecher is coming round to your views


[027-051]on theology?


[027-052]_Answer_.  I would not have the egotism to say that he was coming


[027-053]round to my views, but evidently Mr. Beecher has been growing.


[027-054]His head has been instructed by his heart; and if a man will allow


[027-055]even the poor plant of pity to grow in his heart he will hold in


[027-056]infinite execration all orthodox religion.  The moment he will


[027-057]allow himself to think that eternal consequences depend upon human


[027-058]life; that the few short years we live in the world determine for


[027-059]an eternity the question of infinite joy or infinite pain; the


[027-060]moment he thinks of that he will see that it is an infinite absurdity.


[027-061]For instance, a man is born in Arkansas and lives there to be


[027-062]seventeen or eighteen years of age, is it possible that he can be


[027-063]truthfully told at the day of judgment that he had a fair chance?


[027-064]Just imagine a man being held eternally responsible for his conduct


[027-065]in Delaware!  Mr. Beecher is a man of great genius--full of poetry


[027-066]and pathos.  Every now and then he is driven back by the orthodox


[027-067]members of his congregation toward the old religion, and for the


[027-068]benefit of those weak disciples he will preach what is called "a


[027-069]doctrinal sermon;" but before he gets through with it, seeing that


[027-070]it is infinitely cruel, he utters a cry of horror, and protests


[027-071]with all the strength of his nature against the cruelty of the


[027-072]creed.  I imagine that he has always thought that he was under


[027-073]great obligation to Plymouth Church, but the truth is that the


[027-074]church depends upon him; that church gets its character from Mr.


[027-075]Beecher.  He has done a vast deal to ameliorate the condition of


[027-076]the average orthodox mind.  He excites the envy of the mediocre


[027-077]minister, and he excites the hatred of the really orthodox, but he


[027-078]receives the approbation of good and generous men everywhere.  For


[027-079]my part, I have no quarrel with any religion that does not threaten


[027-080]eternal punishment to very good people, and that does not promise


[027-081]eternal reward to very bad people.  If orthodox Christianity is


[027-082]true, some of the best people I know are going to hell, and some


[027-083]of the meanest I have ever known are either in heaven or on the


[027-084]road.  Of course, I admit that there are thousands and millions of


[027-085]good Christians--honest and noble people, but in my judgment, Mr.


[027-086]Beecher is the greatest man in the world who now occupies a pulpit.

[027-088]Speaking of a man's living in Delaware, a young man, some time ago,


[027-089]came up to me on the street, in an Eastern city and asked for money.


[027-090]"What is your business," I asked.  "I am a waiter by profession."


[027-091]"Where do you come from?"  "Delaware."  "Well, what was the matter


[027-092]--did you drink, or cheat your employer, or were you idle?"  "No."


[027-093]"What was the trouble?"  "Well, the truth is, the State is so small


[027-094]they don't need any waiters; they all reach for what they want."


[027-095]_Question_.  Do you not think there are some dangerous tendencies


[027-096]in Liberalism?


[027-097]_Answer_.  I will first state this proposition:  The credit system


[027-098]in morals, as in business, breeds extravagance.  The cash system


[027-099]in morals, as well as in business, breeds economy.  We will suppose


[027-100]a community in which everybody is bound to sell on credit, and in


[027-101]which every creditor can take the benefit of the bankrupt law every


[027-102]Saturday night, and the constable pays the costs.  In my judgment


[027-103]that community would be extravagant as long as the merchants lasted.


[027-104]We will take another community in which everybody has to pay cash,


[027-105]and in my judgment that community will be a very economical one.


[027-106]Now, then, let us apply this to morals.  Christianity allows


[027-107]everybody to sin on a credit, and allows a man who has lived, we


[027-108]will say sixty-nine years, what Christians are pleased to call a


[027-109]worldly life, an immoral life.  They allow him on his death-bed,


[027-110]between the last dose of medicine and the last breath, to be


[027-111]converted, and that man who has done nothing except evil, becomes


[027-112]an angel.  Here is another man who has lived the same length of


[027-113]time, doing all the good he possibly could do, but not meeting with


[027-114]what they are pleased to call "a change of heart;" he goes to a


[027-115]world of pain.  Now, my doctrine is that everybody must reap exactly


[027-116]what he sows, other things being equal.  If he acts badly he will


[027-117]not be very happy; if he acts well he will not be very sad.  I


[027-118]believe in the doctrine of consequences, and that every man must


[027-119]stand the consequences of his own acts.  It seems to me that that


[027-120]fact will have a greater restraining influence than the idea that


[027-121]you can, just before you leave this world, shift your burden on to


[027-122]somebody else.  I am a believer in the restraining influences of


[027-123]liberty, because responsibility goes hand in hand with freedom.


[027-124]I do not believe that the gallows is the last step between earth


[027-125]and heaven.  I do not believe in the conversion and salvation of


[027-126]murderers while their innocent victims are in hell.  The church


[027-127]has taught so long that he who acts virtuously carries a cross,


[027-128]and that only sinners enjoy themselves, that it may be that for a


[027-129]little while after men leave the church they may go to extremes


[027-130]until they demonstrate for themselves that the path of vice is the


[027-131]path of thorns, and that only along the wayside of virtue grow the


[027-132]flowers of joy.  The church has depicted virtue as a sour, wrinkled


[027-133]termagant; an old woman with nothing but skin and bones, and a


[027-134]temper beyond description; and at the same time vice has been


[027-135]painted in all the voluptuous outlines of a Greek statue.  The


[027-136]truth is exactly the other way.  A thing is right because it pays;


[027-137]a thing is wrong because it does not; and when I use the word


[027-138]"pays," I mean in the highest and noblest sense.


[027-139]--_The Daily News_, Denver, Colorado, January 17, 1884.


[027-140]FREE TRADE AND CHRISTIANITY.


[027-141]_Question_.  Who will be the Republican nominee for President?


[027-142]_Answer_.  The correct answer to this question would make so many


[027-143]men unhappy that I have concluded not to give it.


[027-144]_Question_.  Has not the Democracy injured itself irretrievably by


[027-145]permitting the free trade element to rule it?


[027-146]_Answer_.  I do not think that the Democratic party weakened itself


[027-147]by electing Carlisle, Speaker.  I think him an excellent man, an


[027-148]exceedingly candid man, and one who will do what he believes ought


[027-149]to be done.  I have a very high opinion of Mr. Carlisle.  I do not


[027-150]suppose any party in this country is really for free trade.  I find


[027-151]that all writers upon the subject, no matter which side they are


[027-152]on, are on that side with certain exceptions.  Adam Smith was in


[027-153]favor of free trade, with a few exceptions, and those exceptions


[027-154]were in matters where he thought it was for England's interest not


[027-155]to have free trade.  The same may be said of all writers.  So far


[027-156]as I can see, the free traders have all the arguments and the


[027-157]protectionists all the facts.  The free trade theories are splendid,


[027-158]but they will not work; the results are disastrous.  We find by


[027-159]actual experiment that it is better to protect home industries.


[027-160]It was once said that protection created nothing but monopoly; the


[027-161]argument was that way, but the facts are not.  Take, for instance,


[027-162]steel rails; when we bought them of England we paid one hundred


[027-163]and twenty-five dollars a ton.  I believe there was a tariff of


[027-164]twenty-eight or twenty-nine dollars a ton, and yet in spite of all


[027-165]the arguments going to show that protection would simply increase


[027-166]prices in America, would simply enrich the capitalists and impoverish


[027-167]the consumer, steel rails are now produced, I believe, right here


[027-168]in Colorado for forty-two dollars a ton.


[027-169]After all, it is a question of labor; a question of prices that


[027-170]shall be paid the laboring man; a question of what the laboring


[027-171]man shall eat; whether he shall eat meat or soup made from the


[027-172]bones.  Very few people take into consideration the value of raw


[027-173]material and the value of labor.  Take, for instance, your ton of


[027-174]steel rails worth forty-two dollars.  The iron in the earth is not


[027-175]worth twenty-five cents.  The coal in the earth and the lime in


[027-176]the ledge together are not worth twenty-five cents.  Now, then, of


[027-177]the forty-two dollars, forty-one and a half is labor.  There is


[027-178]not two dollars' worth of raw material in a locomotive worth fifteen


[027-179]thousand dollars.  By raw material I mean the material in the earth.


[027-180]There is not in the works of a watch which will sell for fifteen


[027-181]dollars, raw material of the value of one-half cent.  All the rest


[027-182]is labor.  A ship, a man-of-war that costs one million dollars--


[027-183]the raw material in the earth is not worth, in my judgment, one


[027-184]thousand dollars.  All the rest is labor.  If there is any way to


[027-185]protect American labor, I am in favor of it.  If the present tariff


[027-186]does not do it, then I am in favor of changing to one that will.


[027-187]If the Democratic party takes a stand for free trade or anything


[027-188]like it, they will need protection; they will need protection at


[027-189]the polls; that is to say, they will meet only with defeat and


[027-190]disaster.
