[071-001]_Question_.  What is your opinion of the Christian religion and


[071-002]the Christian Church?


[071-003]_Answer_.  My opinion upon this subject is certainly well known.


[071-004]The Christian Church is founded upon miracles--that is to say, upon


[071-005]impossibilities.  Of course, there is a great deal that is good in


[071-006]the creeds of the churches, and in the sermons delivered by its


[071-007]ministers; but mixed with this good is much that is evil.  My


[071-008]principal objection to orthodox religion is the dogma of eternal


[071-009]pain.  Nothing can be more infamously absurd.  All civilized men


[071-010]should denounce it--all women should regard it with a kind of


[071-011]shuddering abhorrence.


[071-012]--_Secular Thought_, Toronto, Canada, 1888.


[071-013]POPE LEO XIII.


[071-014]_Question_.  Do you agree with the views of Pope Leo XIII. as


[071-015]expressed in _The Herald_ of last week?


[071-016]_Answer_.  I am not personally acquainted with Leo XIII., but I


[071-017]have not the slightest idea that he loves Americans or their country.


[071-018]I regard him as an enemy of intellectual liberty.  He tells us that


[071-019]where the church is free it will increase, and I say to him that


[071-020]where others are free it will not.  The Catholic Church has increased


[071-021]in this country by immigration and in no other way.  Possibly the


[071-022]Pope is willing to use his power for the good of the whole people,


[071-023]Protestants and Catholics, and to increase their prosperity and


[071-024]happiness, because by this he means that he will use his power to


[071-025]make Catholics out of Protestants.


[071-026]It is impossible for the Catholic Church to be in favor of mental


[071-027]freedom.  That church represents absolute authority.  Its members


[071-028]have no right to reason--no right to ask questions--they are called


[071-029]upon simply to believe and to pay their subscriptions.


[071-030]_Question_.  Do you agree with the Pope when he says that the result


[071-031]of efforts which have been made to throw aside Christianity and


[071-032]live without it can be seen in the present condition of society--


[071-033]discontent, disorder, hatred and profound unhappiness?


[071-034]_Answer_.  Undoubtedly the people of Europe who wish to be free


[071-035]are discontented.  Undoubtedly these efforts to have something like


[071-036]justice done will bring disorder.  Those in power will hate those


[071-037]who are endeavoring to drive them from their thrones.  If the people


[071-038]now, as formerly, would bear all burdens cheerfully placed upon


[071-039]their shoulders by church and state--that is to say, if they were


[071-040]so enslaved mentally that they would not even have sense enough to


[071-041]complain, then there would be what the Pope might call "peace and


[071-042]happiness"--that is to say, the peace of ignorance, and the happiness


[071-043]of those who are expecting pay in another world for their agonies


[071-044]endured in this.


[071-045]Of course, the revolutionaries of Europe are not satisfied with


[071-046]the Catholic religion; neither are they satisfied with the Protestant.


[071-047]Both of these religions rest upon authority.  Both discourage


[071-048]reason.  Both say "Let him that hath ears to hear, hear," but


[071-049]neither say let him that hath brains to think, think.


[071-050]Christianity has been thoroughly tried, and it is a failure.  Nearly


[071-051]every church has upheld slavery, not only of the body, but of the


[071-052]mind.  When Christian missionaries invade what they call a heathen


[071-053]country, they are followed in a little while by merchants and


[071-054]traders, and in a few days afterward by the army.  The first real


[071-055]work is to kill the heathen or steal their lands, or else reduce


[071-056]them to something like slavery.


[071-057]I have no confidence in the reformation of this world by churches.


[071-058]Churches for the most part exist, not for this world, but for


[071-059]another.  They are founded upon the supernatural, and they say:


[071-060]"Take no thought for the morrow; put your trust in your Heavenly


[071-061]Father and he will take care of you."  On the other hand, science


[071-062]says:  "You must take care of yourself, live for the world in which


[071-063]you happen to be--if there is another, live for that when you get


[071-064]there."


[071-065]_Question_.  What do you think of the plan to better the condition


[071-066]of the workingmen, by committees headed by bishops of the Catholic


[071-067]Church, in discussing their duties?


[071-068]_Answer_.  If the bishops wish to discuss with anybody about duties


[071-069]they had better discuss with the employers, instead of the employed.


[071-070]This discussion had better take place between the clergy and the


[071-071]capitalist.  There is no need of discussing this question with the


[071-072]poor wretches who cannot earn more than enough to keep their souls


[071-073]in their bodies.  If the Catholic Church has so much power, and if


[071-074]it represents God on earth, let it turn its attention to softening


[071-075]the hearts of capitalists, and no longer waste its time in preaching


[071-076]patience to the poor slaves who are now bearing the burdens of the


[071-077]world.


[071-078]_Question_.  Do you agree with the Pope that:  "Sound rules of life


[071-079]must be founded on religion"?


[071-080]_Answer_.  I do not.  Sound rules of life must be founded on the


[071-081]experience of mankind.  In other words, we must live for this world.


[071-082]Why should men throw away hundreds and thousands of millions of


[071-083]dollars in building cathedrals and churches, and paying the salaries


[071-084]of bishops and priests, and cardinals and popes, and get no possible


[071-085]return for all this money except a few guesses about another world


[071-086]--those guesses being stated as facts--when every pope and priest


[071-087]and bishop knows that no one knows the slightest thing on the


[071-088]subject.  Superstition is the greatest burden borne by the industry


[071-089]of the world.


[071-090]The nations of Europe to-day all pretend to be Christian, yet


[071-091]millions of men are drilled and armed for the purpose of killing


[071-092]other Christians.  Each Christian nation is fortified to prevent


[071-093]other Christians from devastating their fields.  There is already


[071-094]a debt of about twenty-five thousand millions of dollars which has


[071-095]been incurred by Christian nations, because each one is afraid of


[071-096]every other, and yet all say:  "It is our duty to love our enemies."


[071-097]This world, in my judgment, is to be reformed through intelligence


[071-098]--through development of the mind--not by credulity, but by


[071-099]investigation; not by faith in the supernatural, but by faith in


[071-100]the natural.  The church has passed the zenith of her power.  The


[071-101]clergy must stand aside.  Scientists must take their places.


[071-102]_Question_.  Do you agree with the Pope in attacking the present


[071-103]governments of Europe and the memories of Mazzini and Saffi?


[071-104]_Answer_.  I do not.  I think Mazzini was of more use to Italy than


[071-105]all the popes that ever occupied the chair of St. Peter--which, by


[071-106]the way, was not his chair.  I have a thousand times more regard


[071-107]for Mazzini, for Garibaldi, for Cavour, than I have for any gentleman


[071-108]who pretends to be the representative of God.


[071-109]There is another objection I have to the Pope, and that is that he


[071-110]was so scandalized when a monument was reared in Rome to the memory


[071-111]of Giordano Bruno.  Bruno was murdered about two hundred and sixty


[071-112]years ago by the Catholic Church, and such has been the development


[071-113]of the human brain and heart that on the very spot where he was


[071-114]murdered a monument rises to his memory.


[071-115]But the vicar of God has remained stationary, and he regards this


[071-116]mark of honor to one of the greatest and noblest of the human race


[071-117]as an act of blasphemy.  The poor old man acts as if America had


[071-118]never been discovered--as if the world were still flat--and as if


[071-119]the stars had been made out of little pieces left over from the


[071-120]creation of the world and stuck in the sky simply to beautify the


[071-121]night.


[071-122]But, after all, I do not blame this Pope.  He is the victim of his


[071-123]surroundings.  He was never married.  His heart was never softened


[071-124]by wife or children.  He was born that way, and, to tell you the


[071-125]truth, he has my sincere sympathy.  Let him talk about America and


[071-126]stay in Italy.


[071-127]--_The Herald_, New York, April 22, 1890.


[071-128]THE SACREDNESS OF THE SABBATH.


[071-129]_Question_.  What do you think of the sacredness of the Sabbath?


[071-130]_Answer_.  I think all days, all times and all seasons are alike


[071-131]sacred.  I think the best day in a man's life is the day that he


[071-132]is truly the happiest.  Every day in which good is done to humanity


[071-133]is a holy day.


[071-134]If I were to make a calendar of sacred days, I would put down the


[071-135]days in which the greatest inventions came to the mind of genius;


[071-136]the days when scattered tribes became nations; the days when good


[071-137]laws were passed; the days when bad ones were repealed; the days


[071-138]when kings were dethroned, and the people given their own; in other


[071-139]words, every day in which good has been done; in which men and


[071-140]women have truly fallen in love, days in which babes were born


[071-141]destined to change the civilization of the world.  These are all


[071-142]sacred days; days in which men have fought for the right, suffered


[071-143]for the right, died for the right; all days in which there were


[071-144]heroic actions for good.  The day when slavery was abolished in


[071-145]the United States is holier than any Sabbath by reason of "divine


[071-146]consecration."


[071-147]Of course, I care nothing about the sacredness of the Sabbath


[071-148]because it was hallowed in the Old Testament, or because of that


[071-149]day Jehovah is said to have rested from his labors.  A space of


[071-150]time cannot be sacred, any more than a vacuum can be sacred, and


[071-151]it is rendered sacred by deeds done in it, and not in and of itself.


[071-152]If we should finally invent some means of traveling by which we


[071-153]could go a thousand miles a day, a man could escape Sunday all his


[071-154]life by traveling West.  He could start Monday, and stay Monday


[071-155]all the time.  Or, if he should some time get near the North Pole,


[071-156]he could walk faster than the earth turns and thus beat Sunday all


[071-157]the while.


[071-158]_Question_.  Should not the museums and art galleries be thrown


[071-159]open to the workingmen free on Sunday?


[071-160]_Answer_.  Undoubtedly.  In all civilized countries this is done,


[071-161]and I believe it would be done in New York, only it is said that


[071-162]money has been given on condition that the museums should be kept


[071-163]closed on Sundays.  I have always heard it said that large sums


[071-164]will be withheld by certain old people who have the prospect of


[071-165]dying in the near future if the museums are open on Sunday.


[071-166]This, however, seems to me a very poor and shallow excuse.  Money


[071-167]should not be received under such conditions.  One of the curses


[071-168]of our country has been the giving of gifts to colleges on certain


[071-169]conditions.  As, for instance, the money given to Andover by the


[071-170]original founder on the condition that a certain creed be taught,


[071-171]and other large amounts have been given on a like condition.  Now,


[071-172]the result of this is that the theological professor must teach


[071-173]what these donors have indicated, or go out of the institution; or


[071-174]--and this last "or" is generally the trouble--teach what he does


[071-175]not believe, endeavoring to get around it by giving new meaning to


[071-176]old words.


[071-177]I think the cause of intellectual progress has been much delayed


[071-178]by these conditions put in the wills of supposed benefactors, so


[071-179]that after they are dead they can rule people who have the habit


[071-180]of being alive.  In my opinion, a corpse is a poor ruler, and after


[071-181]a man is dead he should keep quiet.


[071-182]Of course all that he did will live, and should be allowed to have


[071-183]its natural effect.  If he was a great inventor or discoverer, or


[071-184]if he uttered great truths, these became the property of the world;


[071-185]but he should not endeavor, after he is dead, to rule the living


[071-186]by conditions attached to his gifts.


[071-187]All the museums and libraries should be opened, not only to


[071-188]workingmen, but to all others.  If to see great paintings, great


[071-189]statues, wonderful works of art; if to read the thoughts of the


[071-190]greatest men--if these things tend to the civilization of the race,


[071-191]then they should be put as nearly as possible within the reach of


[071-192]all.
