[093-001]What good can it do God to keep people married who hate each other?


[093-002]What good can it do the community to keep such people together?


[093-003]_Question_.  Do you consider marriage a contract or a sacrament?


[093-004]_Answer_.  Marriage is the most important contract that human beings


[093-005]can make.  No matter whether it is called a contract or a sacrament,


[093-006]it remains the same.  A true marriage is a natural concord or


[093-007]agreement of souls--a harmony in which discord is not even imagined.


[093-008]It is a mingling so perfect that only one seems to exist.  All


[093-009]other considerations are lost.  The present seems eternal.  In this


[093-010]supreme moment there is no shadow, or the shadow is as luminous as


[093-011]light.


[093-012]When two beings thus love, thus united, this is the true marriage


[093-013]of soul and soul.  The idea of contract is lost.  Duty and obligation


[093-014]are instantly changed into desire and joy, and two lives, like


[093-015]uniting streams, flow on as one.


[093-016]This is real marriage.


[093-017]Now, if the man turns out to be a wild beast, if he destroys the


[093-018]happiness of the wife, why should she remain his victim?


[093-019]If she wants a divorce, she should have it.  The divorce will not


[093-020]hurt God or the community.  As a matter of fact, it will save a


[093-021]life.


[093-022]No man not poisoned by superstition will object to the release of


[093-023]an abused wife.  In such a case only savages can object to divorce.


[093-024]The man who wants courts and legislatures to force a woman to live


[093-025]with him is a monster.


[093-026]_Question_.  Do you believe that the divorced should be allowed to


[093-027]marry again?


[093-028]_Answer_.  Certainly.  Has the woman whose rights have been outraged


[093-029]no right to build another home?  Must this woman, full of kindness,


[093-030]affection and health, be chained until death releases her?  Is


[093-031]there no future for her?  Must she be an outcast forever?  Can she


[093-032]never sit by her own hearth, with the arms of her children about


[093-033]her neck, and by her side a husband who loves and protects her?


[093-034]There are no two sides to this question.


[093-035]All human beings should be allowed to correct their mistakes.  If


[093-036]the wife has flagrantly violated the contract of marriage, the


[093-037]husband should be given a divorce.  If the wife wants a divorce,


[093-038]if she loathes her husband, if she no longer loves him, then the


[093-039]divorce should be granted.


[093-040]It is immoral for a woman to live as the wife of a man whom she


[093-041]abhors.  The home should be pure.  Children should be well-born.


[093-042]Their parents should love one another.


[093-043]Marriages are made by men and women, not by society, not by the


[093-044]state, not by the church, not by the gods.  Nothing is moral, that


[093-045]does not tend to the well-being of sentient beings.


[093-046]The good home is the unit of good government.  The hearthstone is


[093-047]the corner-stone of civilization.  Society is not interested in


[093-048]the preservation of hateful homes.  It is not to the interest of


[093-049]society that good women should be enslaved or that they should


[093-050]become mothers by husbands whom they hate.


[093-051]Most of the laws about divorce are absurd or cruel, and ought to


[093-052]be repealed.


[093-053]--_The Herald_, New York, February, 1897.


[093-054]MUSIC, NEWSPAPERS, LYNCHING AND ARBITRATION.


[093-055]_Question_.  How do you enjoy staying in Chicago?


[093-056]_Answer_.  Well, I am about as happy as a man can be when he is


[093-057]away from home.  I was at the opera last night.  I am always happy


[093-058]when I hear the music of Wagner interpreted by such a genius as


[093-059]Seidl.  I do not believe there is a man in the world who has in


[093-060]his brain and heart more of the real spirit of Wagner than Anton


[093-061]Seidl.  He knows how to lead, how to phrase and shade, how to rush


[093-062]and how to linger, and to express every passion and every mood.  So


[093-063]I was happy last night to hear him.  Then I heard Edouard de Reszke,


[093-064]the best of bass singers, with tones of a great organ, and others


[093-065]soft and liquid, and Jean de Reszke, a great tenor, who sings the


[093-066]"Swan Song" as though inspired; and I liked Bispham, but hated his


[093-067]part.  He is a great singer; so is Mme. Litvinne.


[093-068]So, I can say that I am enjoying Chicago.  In fact, I always did.


[093-069]I was here when the town was small, not much more than huts and


[093-070]hogs, lumber and mud; and now it is one of the greatest of cities.


[093-071]It makes me happy just to think of the difference.  I was born the


[093-072]year Chicago was incorporated.  In my time matches were invented.


[093-073]Steam navigation became really useful.  The telegraph was invented.


[093-074]Gas was discovered and applied to practical uses, and electricity


[093-075]was made known in its practical workings to mankind.  Thus, it is


[093-076]seen the world is progressing; men are becoming civilized.  But


[093-077]the process of civilization even now is slow.  In one or two thousand


[093-078]years we may hope to see a vast improvement in man's condition.


[093-079]We may expect to have the employer so far civilized that he will


[093-080]not try to make money for money's sake, but in order that he may


[093-081]apply it to good uses, to the amelioration of his fellow-man's


[093-082]condition.  We may also expect the see the workingman, the employee,


[093-083]so far civilized that he will know it is impossible and undesirable


[093-084]for him to attempt to fix the wages paid by his employer.  We may


[093-085]in a thousand or more years reasonably expect that the employee


[093-086]will be so far civilized and become sufficiently sensible to know


[093-087]that strikes and threats and mob violence can never improve his


[093-088]condition.  Altruism is nonsense, craziness.


[093-089]_Question_.  Is Chicago as liberal, intellectually, as New York?


[093-090]_Answer_.  I think so.  Of course you will find thousands of free,


[093-091]thoughtful people in New York--people who think and want others to


[093-092]do the same.  So, there are thousands of respectable people who


[093-093]are centuries behind the age.  In other words, you will find all


[093-094]kinds.  I presume the same is true of Chicago.  I find many liberal


[093-095]people here, and some not quite so liberal.


[093-096]Some of the papers here seem to be edited by real pious men.  On


[093-097]last Tuesday the _Times-Herald_ asked pardon of its readers for


[093-098]having given a report of my lecture.  That editor must be pious.


[093-099]In the same paper, columns were given to the prospective prize-


[093-100]fight at Carson City.  All the news about the good Corbett and the


[093-101]orthodox Fitzsimmons--about the training of the gentlemen who are


[093-102]going to attack each others' jugulars and noses; who are expected


[093-103]to break jaws, blacken eyes, and peel foreheads in a few days, to


[093-104]settle the question of which can bear the most pounding.  In this


[093-105]great contest and in all its vulgar details, the readers of the


[093-106]_Times-Herald_ are believed by the editor of that religious daily


[093-107]to take great interest.


[093-108]The editor did not ask the pardon of his readers for giving so much


[093-109]space to the nose-smashing sport.  No!  He knew that would fill


[093-110]their souls with delight, and, so knowing, he reached the correct


[093-111]conclusion that such people would not enjoy anything I had said.


[093-112]The editor did a wise thing and catered to a large majority of his


[093-113]readers.  I do not think that we have as religious a daily paper


[093-114]in New York as the _Times-Herald_.  So the editor of the _Times-


[093-115]Herald_ took the ground that men with little learning, in youth,


[093-116]might be agnostic, but as they grew sensible they would become


[093-117]orthodox.  When he wrote that he was probably thinking of Humboldt


[093-118]and Darwin, of Huxley and Haeckel.  May be Herbert Spencer was in


[093-119]his mind, but I think that he must have been thinking of a few boys


[093-120]in his native village.


[093-121]_Question_.  What do you think about prize-fighting anyway?


[093-122]_Answer_.  Well, I think that prize-fighting is worse, if possible,


[093-123]than revival meetings.  Next to fighting to kill, as they did in


[093-124]the old Roman days, I think the modern prize-fight is the most


[093-125]disgusting and degrading of exhibitions.  All fights, whether cock-


[093-126]fights, bull-fights or pugilistic encounters, are practiced and


[093-127]enjoyed only by savages.  No matter what office they hold, what


[093-128]wealth or education they have, they are simply savages.  Under no


[093-129]possible circumstances would I witness a prize-fight or a bull-


[093-130]fight or a dog-fight.  The Marquis of Queensbury was once at my


[093-131]house, and I found his opinions were the same as mine.  Everyone


[093-132]thinks that he had something to do with the sport of prize-fighting,


[093-133]but he did not, except to make some rules once for a college boxing


[093-134]contest.  He told me that he never saw but one prize-fight in his


[093-135]life, and that it made him sick.


[093-136]_Question_.  How are you on the arbitration treaty?


[093-137]_Answer_.  I am for it with all my heart.  I have read it, and read


[093-138]it with care, and to me it seems absolutely fair.  England and


[093-139]America should set an example to the world.  The English-speaking


[093-140]people have reason enough and sense enough, I hope, to settle their


[093-141]differences by argument--by reason.  Let us get the wild beast out


[093-142]of us.  Two great nations like England and America appealing to


[093-143]force, arguing with shot and shell!  What is education worth?  Is


[093-144]what we call civilization a sham?  Yes, I believe in peace, in


[093-145]arbitration, in settling disputes like reasonable, human beings.


[093-146]All that war can do is to determine who is the stronger.  It throws


[093-147]no light on any question, addresses no argument.  There is a point


[093-148]to a bayonet, but no logic.  After the war is over the victory does


[093-149]not tell which nation was right.  Civilized men take their differences


[093-150]to courts or arbitrators.  Civilized nations should do the same.


[093-151]There ought to be an international court.


[093-152]Let every man do all he can to prevent war--to prevent the waste,


[093-153]the cruelties, the horrors that follow every flag on every field


[093-154]of battle.  It is time that man was human--time that the beast was


[093-155]out of his heart.


[093-156]_Question_.  What do you think of McKinley's inaugural?


[093-157]_Answer_.  It is good, honest, clear, patriotic and sensible.


[093-158]There is one thing in it that touched me; I agree with him that


[093-159]lynching has to be stopped.  You see that now we are citizens of


[093-160]the United States, not simply of the State in which we happen to


[093-161]live.  I take the ground that it is the business of the United


[093-162]States to protect its citizens, not only when they are in some


[093-163]other country, but when they are at home.  The United States cannot


[093-164]discharge this obligation by allowing the States to do as they


[093-165]please.  Where citizens are being lynched the Government should


[093-166]interfere.  If the Governor of some barbarian State says that he


[093-167]cannot protect the lives of citizens, then the United States should,


[093-168]if it took the entire Army and Navy.


[093-169]_Question_.  What is your opinion of charity organizations?


[093-170]_Answer_.  I think that the people who support them are good and


[093-171]generous--splendid--but I have a poor opinion of the people in


[093-172]charge.  As a rule, I think they are cold, impudent and heartless.


[093-173]There is too much circumlocution, or too many details and too little


[093-174]humanity.  The Jews are exceedingly charitable.  I think that in


[093-175]New York the men who are doing the most for their fellow-men are


[093-176]Jews.  Nathan Strauss is trying to feed the hungry, warm the cold,


[093-177]and clothe the naked.  For the most part, organized charities are,


[093-178]I think, failures.  A real charity has to be in the control of a


[093-179]good man, a real sympathetic, a sensible man, one who helps others


[093-180]to help themselves.  Let a hungry man go to an organized society


[093-181]and it requires several days to satisfy the officers that the man


[093-182]is hungry.  Meanwhile he will probably starve to death.
