# Mohammed

**Mohammed** (c. 570–632 CE) was the founder of [[islam|Islam]]. In "Why I Am Not a Christian," [[robert-ingersoll|Robert G. Ingersoll]] discusses Mohammed as a comparative case study in religious morality, historical success, and the distinction between a teacher's personal character and the doctrines attributed to them.

## In Ingersoll's Writings

### Morality and Mohammed

Ingersoll addresses the moral teachings associated with Mohammed directly: "The morality inculcated by Mohammed is good. The immorality inculcated by Mohammed is bad" (024-098–024-099). This balanced assessment reflects Ingersoll's broader methodology of evaluating religious figures by their actual teachings rather than by inherited prejudice. He further asks: "If Mohammed was a pro" (024-099) — suggesting the question of whether Mohammed's personal character should affect the evaluation of Islamic moral principles.

### Historical Success

On the question of whether numerical success validates religious truth, Ingersoll observes: "the success of Mohammed is unparalleled" (026-042). He acknowledges the rapid spread of Islam as a historical fact, but argues that this "success is not a demonstration" (026-037) of divine truth. This parallels his treatment of [[christianity|Christianity]]'s own historical expansion.

### Teachings vs. Teacher

Ingersoll makes an important analytical distinction: "Mohammed taught certain doctrines that" (028-054) may or may not be original to him, and "the good in the teachings of Mohammed is not Mohammedism" (028-055). This separation of valuable moral content from the personality cult surrounding a religious founder is a recurring theme in Ingersoll's critique of all organized religions.

### Cultural Contributions

Ingersoll acknowledges the intellectual contributions of Islamic civilization, noting that Europe derived "its idea of algebra from the Mohammedans" (014-170). He also references "Mohammedan asylums" (040-142) as examples of charitable institutions that predated Christian equivalents.
