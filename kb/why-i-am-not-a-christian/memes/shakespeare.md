[[william-shakespeare|William Shakespeare]] (1564–1616) occupies a singular place in the thought of [[robert-ingersoll|Robert G. Ingersoll]], who repeatedly invoked the playwright as the supreme example of human genius operating without divine assistance. For Ingersoll, Shakespeare was a living refutation of the claim that greatness requires [[bible|scriptural]] inspiration: here was a mortal man — imperfect, unordained, writing for the commercial stage — who produced works of such depth and [[truth]] that they surpassed, in Ingersoll's judgment, anything in sacred literature. Ingersoll visited Shakespeare's birthplace [P01-139] and spoke of him with a reverence most contemporaries reserved for prophets and saints.

## Ingersoll's Assessment

Ingersoll's admiration for Shakespeare was not casual. He ranked him above all other human beings:

> Shakespeare was the greatest man who ever lived. [015-162]

This was not hyperbole in context. Ingersoll elaborated that while most households owned a [[bible|Bible]], Shakespeare's actual influence — unlike scripture's — was entirely for good:

> But what influence Shakespeare has, is for good. No man ever was, or ever will be, made better by the reading of the cruel and contemptible things in the Old Testament. [015-169]

The contrast was deliberate and polemical: the [[bible|Bible]], which claimed divine authorship, contained commands to commit genocide and laws permitting [[slavery]]; Shakespeare, who claimed nothing, wrote plays that expanded human sympathy and illuminated the human condition.

Ingersoll acknowledged Shakespeare's imperfections candidly — "many passages which I wish he had never written" [010-088] — but considered this honesty a strength of the secular worldview:

> I can excuse Shakespeare, because he did not rise above his time and claim to be perfect. If anybody claimed now that Shakespeare's works were inspired, I would denounce him as an impostor. [010-089, 010-091]

## Why Shakespeare Mattered to Freethought

For the [[freethought]] movement, Shakespeare served as a crucial exhibit in the case for human capacity. Orthodox [[christianity|Christianity]] taught that humanity was fallen, depraved, and incapable of producing genuine goodness without divine grace. Shakespeare demonstrated otherwise.

| Quality | Biblical Claim | Shakespeare's Example |
|---------|---------------|----------------------|
| Moral insight | Requires divine revelation | Profound understanding of human nature, unaided |
| Literary excellence | Scripture as supreme text | Works equal or superior in depth and beauty |
| Influence on character | Scripture makes people better | Shakespeare's influence "is for good" |
| Claim to authority | "Thus saith the Lord" | No supernatural claim; judged on merit alone |

This framework allowed Ingersoll to [[reason|reason]] that if a mortal playwright could produce works of enduring moral and aesthetic value, the doctrine of total depravity was false, and the claim that humanity needed divine revelation for moral guidance was unnecessary.

## Enduring Influence

Ingersoll predicted that Shakespeare's reputation would only grow with time:

> After all the thrones have been destroyed, at the intellectual assembly of the world, when the roll is called, the name of Shakespeare will lead the intellectual list. [015-175]

This prophecy reflected Ingersoll's conviction that [[reason|intellect]]ual achievement — not military conquest, not political power, not religious authority — was humanity's highest calling. In placing a playwright at the summit of human accomplishment, Ingersoll made an implicit argument about what civilization should value.
