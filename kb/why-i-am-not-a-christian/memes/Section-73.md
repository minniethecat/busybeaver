[072-001]The man who works eight or ten or twelve hours a day has not time


[072-002]during the six days of labor to visit libraries or museums.  Sunday


[072-003]is his day of leisure, his day of recreation, and on that day he


[072-004]should have the privilege, and he himself should deem it a right


[072-005]to visit all the public libraries and museums, parks and gardens.


[072-006]In other words, I think the laboring man should have the same rights


[072-007]on Sundays, to say the least of it, that wealthy people have on


[072-008]other days.  The man of wealth has leisure.  He can attend these


[072-009]places on any day he may desire; but necessity being the master of


[072-010]the poor man, Sunday is his one day for such a purpose.  For men


[072-011]of wealth to close the museums and libraries on that day, shows


[072-012]that they have either a mistaken idea as to the well-being of their


[072-013]fellow-men, or that they care nothing about the rights of any except


[072-014]the wealthy.


[072-015]Personally, I have no sort of patience with the theological snivel


[072-016]and drivel about the sacredness of the Sabbath.  I do not understand


[072-017]why they do not accept the words of their own Christ, namely, that


[072-018]"the Sabbath was made for man, and not man for the Sabbath."


[072-019]The hypocrites of Judea were great sticklers for the Sabbath, and


[072-020]the orthodox Christians of New York are exactly the same.  My own


[072-021]opinion is that a man who has been at work all the week, in the


[072-022]dust and heat, can hardly afford to waste his Sunday in hearing an


[072-023]orthodox sermon--a sermon that gives him the cheerful intelligence


[072-024]that his chances for being damned are largely in the majority.  I


[072-025]think it is far better for the workingman to go out with his family


[072-026]in the park, into the woods, to some German garden, where he can


[072-027]hear the music of Wagner, or even the waltzes of Strauss, or to


[072-028]take a boat and go down to the shore of the sea.  I think than in


[072-029]summer a few waves of the ocean are far more refreshing then all


[072-030]the orthodox sermons of the world.


[072-031]As a matter of fact, I believe the preachers leave the city in the


[072-032]summer and let the Devil do his worst.  Whether it is believed that


[072-033]the Devil has less power in warm weather, I do not know.  But I do


[072-034]know that, as the mercury rises, the anxiety about souls decreases,


[072-035]and the hotter New York becomes, the cooler hell seems to be.


[072-036]I want the workingman, no matter what he works at--whether at


[072-037]doctoring people, or trying law suits, or running for office--to


[072-038]have a real good time on Sunday.  He, of course, must be careful


[072-039]not to interfere with the rights of others.  He ought not to play


[072-040]draw-poker on the steps of a church; neither should he stone a


[072-041]Chinese funeral, nor go to any excesses; but all the week long he


[072-042]should have it in his mind:  Next Sunday I am going to have a good


[072-043]time.  My wife and I and the children are going to have a happy


[072-044]time.  I am going out with the girl I like; or my young man is


[072-045]going to take me to the picnic.  And this thought, and this hope,


[072-046]of having a good time on Sunday--of seeing some great pictures at


[072-047]the Metropolitan Art Gallery--together with a good many bad ones--


[072-048]will make work easy and lighten the burden on the shoulders of toil.


[072-049]I take a great interest, too, in the working women--particularly


[072-050]in the working woman.  I think that every workingman should see to


[072-051]it that every working woman has a good time on Sunday.  I am no


[072-052]preacher.  All I want is that everybody should enjoy himself in a


[072-053]way that he will not and does not interfere with the enjoyment of


[072-054]others.


[072-055]It will not do to say that we cannot trust the people.  Our Government


[072-056]is based upon the idea that the people can be trusted, and those


[072-057]who say that the workingmen cannot be trusted, do not believe in


[072-058]Republican or Democratic institutions.  For one, I am perfectly


[072-059]willing to trust the working people of the country.  I do, every


[072-060]day.  I trust the engineers on the cars and steamers.  I trust the


[072-061]builders of houses.  I trust all laboring men every day of my life,


[072-062]and if the laboring people of the country were not trustworthy--if


[072-063]they were malicious or dishonest--life would not be worth living.


[072-064]--_The Journal_, New York, June 6, 1890.


[072-065]THE WEST AND SOUTH.


[072-066]_Question_.  Do you think the South will ever equal or surpass the


[072-067]West in point of prosperity?


[072-068]_Answer_.  I do not.  The West has better soil and more of the


[072-069]elements of wealth.  It is not liable to yellow fever; its rivers


[072-070]have better banks; the people have more thrift, more enterprise,


[072-071]more political hospitality; education is more general; the people


[072-072]are more inventive; better traders, and besides all this, there is


[072-073]no race problem.  The Southern people are what their surroundings


[072-074]made them, and the influence of slavery has not yet died out.  In


[072-075]my judgment the climate of the West is superior to that of the


[072-076]South.  The West has good, cold winters, and they make people a


[072-077]little more frugal, prudent and industrious.  Winters make good


[072-078]homes, cheerful firesides, and, after all, civilization commences


[072-079]at the hearthstone.  The South is growing, and will continue to


[072-080]grow, but it will never equal the West.  The West is destined to


[072-081]dominate the Republic.


[072-082]_Question_.  Do you consider the new ballot-law adapted to the


[072-083]needs of our system of elections?  If not, in what particulars does


[072-084]it require amendment?


[072-085]_Answer_.  Personally I like the brave and open way.  The secret


[072-086]ballot lacks courage.  I want people to know just how I vote.  The


[072-087]old _viva voce_ way was manly and looked well.  Every American


[072-088]should be taught that he votes as a sovereign--an emperor--and he


[072-089]should exercise the right in a kingly way.  But if we must have


[072-090]the secret ballot, then let it be secret indeed, and let the crowd


[072-091]stand back while the king votes.


[072-092]_Question_.  What do you think of the service pension movement?


[072-093]_Answer_.  I see that there is a great deal of talk here in Indiana


[072-094]about this service pension movement.  It has always seemed to me


[072-095]that the pension fund has been frittered away.  Of what use is it


[072-096]to give a man two or three dollars a month?  If a man is rich why


[072-097]should he have any pension?  I think it would be better to give


[072-098]pensions only to the needy, and then give them enough to support


[072-099]them.  If the man was in the army a day or a month, and was uninjured,


[072-100]and can make his own living, or has enough, why should he have a


[072-101]pension?  I believe in giving to the wounded and disabled and poor,


[072-102]with a liberal hand, but not to the rich.  I know that the nation


[072-103]could not pay the men who fought and suffered.  There is not money


[072-104]enough in the world to pay the heroes for what they did and endured


[072-105]--but there is money enough to keep every wounded and diseased


[072-106]soldier from want.  There is money enough to fill the lives of


[072-107]those who gave limbs or health for the sake of the Republic, with


[072-108]comfort and happiness.  I would also like to see the poor soldier


[072-109]taken care of whether he was wounded or not, but I see no propriety


[072-110]in giving to those who do not need.


[072-111]--_The Journal_, Indianapolis, Indiana, June 21, 1890.


[072-112]THE WESTMINSTER CREED AND OTHER SUBJECTS.


[072-113]_Question_.  What do you think of the revision of the Westminster


[072-114]creed?


[072-115]_Answer_.  I think that the intelligence and morality of the age


[072-116]demand the revision.  The Westminster creed is infamous.  It makes


[072-117]God an infinite monster, and men the most miserable of beings.


[072-118]That creed has made millions insane.  It has furrowed countless


[072-119]cheeks with tears.  Under its influence the sentiments and sympathies


[072-120]of the heart have withered.  This creed was written by the worst


[072-121]of men.  The civilized Presbyterians do not believe it.  The


[072-122]intelligent clergyman will not preach it, and all good men who


[072-123]understand it, hold it in abhorrence.  But the fact is that it is


[072-124]just as good as the creed of any orthodox church.  All these creeds


[072-125]must be revised.  Young America will not be consoled by the doctrine


[072-126]of eternal pain.  Yes, the creeds must be revised or the churches


[072-127]will be closed.


[072-128]_Question_.  What do you think of the influence of the press on


[072-129]religion?


[072-130]_Answer_.  If you mean on orthodox religion, then I say the press


[072-131]is helping to destroy it.  Just to the extent that the press is


[072-132]intelligent and fearless, it is and must be the enemy of superstition.


[072-133]Every fact in the universe is the enemy of every falsehood.  The


[072-134]press furnishes food for, and excites thought.  This tends to the


[072-135]destruction of the miraculous and absurd.  I regard the press as


[072-136]the friend of progress and consequently the foe of orthodox religion.


[072-137]The old dogmas do not make the people happy.  What is called religion


[072-138]is full of fear and grief.  The clergy are always talking about


[072-139]dying, about the grave and eternal pain.  They do not add to the


[072-140]sunshine of life.  If they could have their way all the birds would


[072-141]stop singing, the flowers would lose their color and perfume, and


[072-142]all the owls would sit on dead trees and hoot, "Broad is the road


[072-143]that leads to death."


[072-144]_Question_.  If you should write your last sentence on religious


[072-145]topics what would be your closing?


[072-146]_Answer_.  I now in the presence of death affirm and reaffirm the


[072-147]truth of all that I have said against the superstitions of the


[072-148]world.  I would say at least that much on the subject with my last


[072-149]breath.


[072-150]_Question_.  What, in your opinion, will be Browning's position in


[072-151]the literature of the future?


[072-152]_Answer_.  Lower than at present.  Mrs. Browning was far greater


[072-153]than her husband.  He never wrote anything comparable to "Mother


[072-154]and Poet."  Browning lacked form, and that is as great a lack in


[072-155]poetry as it is in sculpture.  He was the author of some great


[072-156]lines, some great thoughts, but he was obscure, uneven and was


[072-157]always mixing the poetic with the commonplace.  To me he cannot be


[072-158]compared with Shelley or Keats, or with our own Walt Whitman.  Of


[072-159]course poetry cannot be very well discussed.  Each man knows what


[072-160]he likes, what touches his heart and what words burst into blossom,


[072-161]but he cannot judge for others.  After one has read Shakespeare,


[072-162]Burns and Byron, and Shelley and Keats; after he has read the


[072-163]"Sonnets" and the "Daisy" and the "Prisoner of Chillon" and the


[072-164]"Skylark" and the "Ode to the Grecian Urn"--the "Flight of the


[072-165]Duchess" seems a little weak.


[072-166]--_The Post-Express_, Rochester, New York, June 23, 1890.


[072-167]SHAKESPEARE AND BACON.


[072-168]_Question_.  What is your opinion of Ignatius Donnelly as a literary


[072-169]man irrespective of his Baconian theory?


[072-170]_Answer_.  I know that Mr. Donnelly enjoys the reputation of being


[072-171]a man of decided ability and that he is regarded by many as a great


[072-172]orator.  He is known to me through his Baconian theory, and in that


[072-173]of course I have no confidence.  It is nearly as ingenious as


[072-174]absurd.  He has spent great time, and has devoted much curious


[072-175]learning to the subject, and has at last succeeded in convincing


[072-176]himself that Shakespeare claimed that which he did not write, and


[072-177]that Bacon wrote that which he did not claim.  But to me the theory


[072-178]is without the slightest foundation.


[072-179]_Question_.  Mr. Donnelly asks:  "Can you imagine the author of


[072-180]such grand productions retiring to that mud house in Stratford to


[072-181]live without a single copy of the quarto that has made his name


[072-182]famous?"  What do you say?
