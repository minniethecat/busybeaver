[018-001]_Question_.  Are there any valid reasons why the constitutional


[018-002]limitations to the elective franchise in the District of Columbia


[018-003]should not be removed by an amendment to that instrument?


[018-004]_Answer_.  I cannot imagine one.  If our Government is founded upon


[018-005]a correct principle there can be no objection urged against suffrage


[018-006]in the District that cannot, with equal force, be urged against


[018-007]every part of the country.  If freedom is dangerous here, it is


[018-008]safe nowhere.  If a man cannot be trusted in the District, he is


[018-009]dangerous in the State.  We do not trust the place where the man


[018-010]happens to be; we trust the man.  The people of this District cannot


[018-011]remain in their present condition without becoming dishonored.


[018-012]The idea of allowing themselves to be governed by commissioners,


[018-013]in whose selection they have no part, is monstrous.  The people


[018-014]here beg, implore, request, ask, pray, beseech, intercede, crave,


[018-015]urge, entreat, supplicate, memorialize and most humbly petition,


[018-016]but they neither vote nor demand.  They are not allowed to enter


[018-017]the Temple of Liberty; they stay in the lobby or sit on the steps.


[018-018]_Question_.  They say Paris is France, because her electors or


[018-019]citizens control that municipality.  Do you foresee any danger of


[018-020]centralization in the full enfranchisement of the citizens of


[018-021]Washington?


[018-022]_Answer_.  There was a time when the intelligence of France was in


[018-023]Paris.  The country was besotted, ignorant, Catholic; Paris was


[018-024]alive, educated, Infidel, full of new theories, of passion and


[018-025]heroism.  For two hundred years Paris was an athlete chained to a


[018-026]corpse.  The corpse was the rest of France.  It is different now,


[018-027]and the whole country is at last filling with light.  Besides,


[018-028]Paris has two millions of people.  It is filled with factories.


[018-029]It is not only the intellectual center, but the center of money


[018-030]and business as well.  Let the _Corps Legislatif_ meet anywhere,


[018-031]and Paris will continue to be in a certain splendid sense--France.


[018-032]Nothing like that can ever happen here unless you expect Washington


[018-033]to outstrip New York, Philadelphia and Chicago.  If allowing the


[018-034]people of the District of Columbia to vote was the only danger to


[018-035]the Republic, I should be politically the happiest of men.  I think


[018-036]it somewhat dangerous to deprive even one American citizen of the


[018-037]right to govern himself.


[018-038]_Question_.  Would you have Government clerks and officials appointed


[018-039]to office here given the franchise in the District? and should


[018-040]this, if given, include the women clerks?


[018-041]_Answer_.  Citizenship should be determined here as in the States.


[018-042]Clerks should not be allowed to vote unless their intention is to


[018-043]make the District their home.  When I make a government I shall


[018-044]give one vote to each family.  The unmarried should not be represented


[018-045]except by parents.  Let the family be the unit of representation.


[018-046]Give each hearthstone a vote.


[018-047]_Question_.  How do you regard the opposition of the local clergy


[018-048]and of the Bourbon Democracy to enfranchising the citizens of the


[018-049]District?


[018-050]_Answer_.  I did not know that the clergy did oppose it.  If, as


[018-051]you say, they do oppose it because they fear it will extend the


[018-052]liquor traffic, I think their reason exceedingly stupid.  You cannot


[018-053]make men temperate by shutting up a few of the saloons and leaving


[018-054]others wide open.  Intemperance must be met with other weapons.


[018-055]The church ought not to appeal to force.  What would the clergy of


[018-056]Washington think should the miracle of Cana be repeated in their


[018-057]day?  Had they been in that country, with their present ideas, what


[018-058]would they have said?  After all there is a great deal of philosophy


[018-059]in the following:  "Better have the whole world voluntarily drunk


[018-060]then sober on compulsion."  Of course the Bourbons object.  Objecting


[018-061]is the business of a Bourbon.  He always objects.  If he does not


[018-062]understand the question he objects because he does not, and if he


[018-063]does understand he objects because he does.  With him the reason


[018-064]for objecting is the fact that he does.


[018-065]_Question_.  What effect, if any, would the complete franchise to


[018-066]our citizens have upon real estate and business in Washington?


[018-067]_Answer_.  If the people here had representation according to


[018-068]numbers--if the avenues to political preferment were open--if men


[018-069]here could take part in the real government of the country, if they


[018-070]could bring with them all their rights, this would be a great and


[018-071]splendid Capital.  We ought to have here a University, the best in


[018-072]the world, a library second to none, and here should be gathered


[018-073]the treasures of American art.  The Federal Government has been


[018-074]infinitely economical in the direction of information.  I hope the


[018-075]time will come when our Government will give as much to educate


[018-076]two men as to kill one.


[018-077]--_The Capital_, Washington, D. C., December 18, 1881.


[018-078]FUNERAL OF JOHN G. MILLS AND IMMORTALITY.*


[018-079][* Robert G. Ingersoll rarely takes the trouble to answer


[018-080]critics. His recent address over the dead body of his friend


[018-081]John G. Mills has called forth a storm of denunciation from


[018-082]nearly every pulpit in the country.  The writer called at


[018-083]the Colonel's office in New York Avenue yesterday and asked


[018-084]him to reply to some of the points made against him.


[018-085]Reluctantly he assented.]


[018-086]_Question_.  Have you seen the recent clerical strictures upon your


[018-087]doctrines?


[018-088]_Answer_.  There are always people kind enough to send me anything


[018-089]they have the slightest reason to think I do not care to read.


[018-090]They seem to be animated by a missionary spirit, and apparently


[018-091]want to be in a position when they see me in hell to exclaim:  "You


[018-092]can't blame me.  I sent you all the impudent articles I saw, and


[018-093]if you died unconverted it was no fault of mine."


[018-094]_Question_.  Did you notice that a Washington clergyman said that


[018-095]the very fact that you were allowed to speak at the funeral was in


[018-096]itself a sacrilege, and that you ought to have been stopped?


[018-097]_Answer_.  Yes, I saw some such story.  Of course, the clergy regard


[018-098]marriages and funerals as the perquisites of the pulpit, and they


[018-099]resent any interference on the part of the pews.  They look at


[018-100]these matters from a business point of view.  They made the same


[018-101]cry against civil marriages.  They denied that marriage was a


[018-102]contract, and insisted that it was a sacrament, and that it was


[018-103]hardly binding unless a priest had blessed it.  They used to bury


[018-104]in consecrated ground, and had marks upon the graves, so that


[018-105]Gabriel might know the ones to waken.  The clergy wish to make


[018-106]themselves essential.  They must christen the babe--this gives them


[018-107]possession of the cradle.  They must perform the ceremony of marriage


[018-108]--this gives them possession of the family.  They must pronounce


[018-109]the funeral discourse--this gives them possession of the dead.


[018-110]Formerly they denied baptism to the children of the unbeliever,


[018-111]marriage to him who denied the dogmas of the church, and burial to


[018-112]honest men.  The church wishes to control the world, and wishes to


[018-113]sacrifice this world for the next.  Of course I am in favor of the


[018-114]utmost liberty upon all these questions.  When a Presbyterian dies,


[018-115]let a follower of John Calvin console the living by setting forth


[018-116]the "Five Points."  When a Catholic becomes clay, let a priest


[018-117]perform such ceremonies as his creed demands, and let him picture


[018-118]the delights of purgatory for the gratification of the living.


[018-119]And when one dies who does not believe in any religion, having


[018-120]expressed a wish that somebody say a few words above his remains,


[018-121]I see no reason why such a proceeding should be stopped, and, for


[018-122]my part, I see no sacrilege in it.  Why should the reputations of


[018-123]the dead, and the feelings of those who live, be placed at the


[018-124]mercy of the ministers?  A man dies not having been a Christian,


[018-125]and who, according to the Christian doctrine, is doomed to eternal


[018-126]fire.  How would an honest Christian minister console the widow


[018-127]and the fatherless children?  How would he dare to tell what he


[018-128]claims to be truth in the presence of the living?  The truth is,


[018-129]the Christian minister in the presence of death abandons his


[018-130]Christianity.  He dare not say above the coffin, "the soul that


[018-131]once inhabited this body is now in hell."  He would be denounced


[018-132]as a brutal savage.  Now and then a minister at a funeral has been


[018-133]brave enough and unmannerly enough to express his doctrine in all


[018-134]its hideousness of hate.  I was told that in Chicago, many years


[018-135]ago, a young man, member of a volunteer fire company, was killed


[018-136]by the falling of a wall, and at the very moment the wall struck


[018-137]him he was uttering a curse.  He was a brave and splendid man.  An


[018-138]orthodox minister said above his coffin, in the presence of his


[018-139]mother and mourning friends, that he saw no hope for the soul of


[018-140]that young man.  The mother, who was also orthodox, refused to have


[018-141]her boy buried with such a sermon--stopped the funeral, took the


[018-142]corpse home, engaged a Universalist preacher, and, on the next day


[018-143]having heard this man say that there was no place in the wide


[018-144]universe of God without hope, and that her son would finally stand


[018-145]among the redeemed, this mother laid her son away, put flowers upon


[018-146]his grave, and was satisfied.


[018-147]_Question_.  What have you to say to the charge that you are


[018-148]preaching the doctrine of despair and hopelessness, when they have


[018-149]the comforting assurances of the Christian religion to offer?


[018-150]_Answer_.  All I have to say is this:  If the Christian religion


[018-151]is true, as commonly preached--and when I speak of Christianity,


[018-152]I speak of the orthodox Christianity of the day--if that be true,


[018-153]those whom I have loved the best are now in torment.  Those to whom


[018-154]I am most deeply indebted are now suffering the vengeance of God.


[018-155]If this religion be true, the future is of no value to me.  I care


[018-156]nothing about heaven, unless the ones I love and have loved are


[018-157]there.  I know nothing about the angels.  I might not like them,


[018-158]and they might not like me.  I would rather meet there the ones


[018-159]who have loved me here--the ones who would have died for me, and


[018-160]for whom I would have died; and if we are to be eternally divided


[018-161]--not because we differed in our views of justice, not because we


[018-162]differed about friendship or love or candor, or the nobility of


[018-163]human action, but because we differed in belief about the atonement


[018-164]or baptism or the inspiration of the Scriptures--and if some of us


[018-165]are to be in heaven, and some in hell, then, for my part, I prefer


[018-166]eternal sleep.  To me the doctrine of annihilation is infinitely


[018-167]more consoling, than the probable separation preached by the orthodox


[018-168]clergy of our time.  Of course, even if there be a God, I like


[018-169]persons that I know, better than I can like him--we have more in


[018-170]common--I know more about them; and how is it possible for me to


[018-171]love the infinite and unknown better than the ones I know?  Why


[018-172]not have the courage to say that if there be a God, all I know


[018-173]about him I know by knowing myself and my friends--by knowing


[018-174]others?  And, after all, is not a noble man, is not a pure woman,


[018-175]the finest revelation we have of God--if there be one?  Of what


[018-176]use is it to be false to ourselves?  What moral quality is there


[018-177]in theological pretence?  Why should a man say that he loves God


[018-178]better than he does his wife or his children or his brother or his


[018-179]sister or his warm, true friend?  Several ministers have objected


[018-180]to what I said about my friend Mr. Mills, on the ground that it


[018-181]was not calculated to console the living.  Mr. Mills was not a


[018-182]Christian.  He denied the inspiration of the Scriptures.  He believed


[018-183]that restitution was the best repentance, and that, after all, sin


[018-184]is a mistake.  He was not a believer in total depravity, or in the


[018-185]atonement.  He denied these things.  He was an unbeliever.  Now,


[018-186]let me ask, what consolation could a Christian minister have given


[018-187]to his family?  He could have said to the widow and the orphans,


[018-188]to the brother and sister:  "Your husband, your father, your brother,


[018-189]is now in hell; dry your tears; weep not for him, but try and save


[018-190]yourselves.  He has been damned as a warning to you, care no more


[018-191]for him, why should you weep over the grave of a man whom God thinks


[018-192]fit only to be eternally tormented?  Why should you love the memory


[018-193]of one whom God hates?"  The minister could have said:  "He had an


[018-194]opportunity--he did not take it.  The life-boat was lowered--he


[018-195]would not get in--he has been drowned, and the waves of God's wrath


[018-196]will sweep over him forever."  This is the consolation of Christianity


[018-197]and the only honest consolation that Christianity can have for the


[018-198]widow and orphans of an unbeliever.  Suppose, however, that the


[018-199]Christian minister has too tender a heart to tell what he believes


[018-200]to be the truth--then he can say to the sorrowing friends:  "Perhaps


[018-201]the man repented before he died; perhaps he is not in hell, perhaps


[018-202]you may meet him in heaven;" and this "perhaps" is a consolation


[018-203]not growing out of Christianity, but out of the politeness of the


[018-204]preacher--out of paganism.
