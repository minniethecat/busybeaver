[037-001]_Answer_.  I think that the church would persecute to-day if it


[037-002]had the power, just as it persecuted in the past.  We are indebted


[037-003]for nearly all our religious liberty to the hypocrisy of the church.


[037-004]The church does not believe.  Some in the church do, and if they


[037-005]had the power, they would torture and burn as of yore.  Give the


[037-006]Presbyterian Church the power, and it would not allow an Infidel


[037-007]to live.  Give the Methodist Church the power and the result would


[037-008]be the same.  Give the Catholic Church the power--just the same.


[037-009]No church in the United States would be willing that any other


[037-010]church should have the power.  The only men who are to be angels


[037-011]in the next world are the ones who cannot be trusted with human


[037-012]liberty in this; and the man who are destined to live forever in


[037-013]hell are the only gentlemen with whom human liberty is safe.  Why


[037-014]should Christians refuse to persecute in this world, when their


[037-015]God is going to in the next?


[037-016]--_Mail and Express_, New York, January 12, 1885.


[037-017]CLEVELAND AND HIS CABINET.


[037-018]_Question_.  What do you think of Mr. Cleveland's Cabinet?


[037-019]_Answer_.  It is a very good Cabinet.  Some objections have been


[037-020]made to Mr. Lamar, but I think he is one of the very best.  He is


[037-021]a man of ability, of unquestioned integrity, and is well informed


[037-022]on national affairs.  Ever since he delivered his eulogy on the


[037-023]life and services of Sumner, I have had great respect for Mr. Lamar.


[037-024]He is far beyond most of his constituents, and has done much to


[037-025]destroy the provincial prejudices of Mississippi.  He will without


[037-026]doubt make an excellent Secretary of the Interior.  The South has


[037-027]no better representative man, and I believe his appointment will,


[037-028]in a little while, be satisfactory to the whole country.  Bayard


[037-029]stands high in his party, and will certainly do as well as his


[037-030]immediate predecessor.  Nothing could be better than the change in


[037-031]the Department of Justice.  Garland is an able lawyer, has been an


[037-032]influential Senator and will, in my judgment, make an excellent


[037-033]Attorney-General.  The rest of the Cabinet I know little about,


[037-034]but from what I hear I believe they are men of ability and that


[037-035]they will discharge their duties well.  Mr. Vilas has a great


[037-036]reputation in Wisconsin, and is one of the best and most forcible


[037-037]speakers in the country.


[037-038]_Question_.  Will Mr. Cleveland, in your opinion, carry out the


[037-039]civil service reform he professes to favor?


[037-040]_Answer_.  I have no reason to suspect even that he will not.  He


[037-041]has promised to execute the law, and the promise is in words that


[037-042]do not admit of two interpretations.  Of course he is sincere.  He


[037-043]knows that this course will save him a world of trouble, and he


[037-044]knows that it makes no difference about the politics of a copyist.


[037-045]All the offices of importance will in all probability be filled by


[037-046]Democrats.  The President will not put himself in the power of his


[037-047]opponents.  If he is to be held responsible for the administration


[037-048]he must be permitted to choose his own assistants.  This is too


[037-049]plain to talk about.  Let us give Mr. Cleveland a fair show--and


[037-050]let us expect success instead of failure.  I admit that many


[037-051]Presidents have violated their promises.  There seems to be something


[037-052]in the atmosphere of Washington that breeds promise and prevents


[037-053]performance.  I suppose it is some kind of political malarial


[037-054]microbe.  I hope that some political Pasteur will, one of these


[037-055]days, discover the real disease so that candidates can be vaccinated


[037-056]during the campaign.  Until them, presidential promises will be


[037-057]liable to a discount.


[037-058]_Question_.  Is the Republican party dead?


[037-059]_Answer_.  My belief is that the next President will be a Republican,


[037-060]and that both houses will be Republican in 1889.  Mr. Blaine was


[037-061]defeated by an accident--by the slip of another man's tongue.  But


[037-062]it matters little what party is in power if the Government is


[037-063]administered upon correct principles, and if the Democracy adopt


[037-064]the views of the Republicans and carry out Republican measures, it


[037-065]may be that they can keep in power--otherwise--otherwise.  If the


[037-066]Democrats carry out real Democratic measures, then their defeat is


[037-067]certain.


[037-068]_Question_.  Do you think that the era of good feeling between the


[037-069]North and the South has set in with the appointment of ex-rebels


[037-070]to the Cabinet?


[037-071]_Answer_.  The war is over.  The South failed.  The Nation succeeded.


[037-072]We should stop talking about South and North.  We are one people,


[037-073]and whether we agree or disagree one destiny awaits us.  We cannot


[037-074]divide.  We must live together.  We must trust each other.  Confidence


[037-075]begets confidence.  The whole country was responsible for slavery.


[037-076]Slavery was rebellion.  Slavery is dead--so is rebellion.  Liberty


[037-077]has united the country and there is more real union, national


[037-078]sentiment to-day, North and South, than ever before.


[037-079]_Question_.  It is hinted that Mr. Tilden is really the power behind


[037-080]the throne.  Do you think so?


[037-081]_Answer_.  I guess nobody has taken the hint.  Of course Mr. Tilden


[037-082]has retired from politics.  The probability is that many Democrats


[037-083]ask his advice, and some rely on his judgment.  He is regarded as


[037-084]a piece of ancient wisdom--a phenomenal persistence of the Jeffersonian


[037-085]type--the connecting link with the framers, founders and fathers.


[037-086]The power behind the throne is the power that the present occupant


[037-087]supposes will determine who the next occupant shall be.


[037-088]_Question_.  With the introduction of the Democracy into power,


[037-089]what radical changes will take place in the Government, and what


[037-090]will be the result?


[037-091]_Answer_.  If the President carries out his inaugural promises


[037-092]there will be no radical changes, and if he does not there will be


[037-093]a very radical change at the next presidential election.  The


[037-094]inaugural is a very good Republican document.  There is nothing in


[037-095]it calculated to excite alarm.  There is no dangerous policy


[037-096]suggested--no conceited vagaries--nothing but a plain statement of


[037-097]the situation and the duty of the Chief Magistrate as understood


[037-098]by the President.  I think that the inaugural surprised the Democrats


[037-099]and the Republicans both, and if the President carries out the


[037-100]program he has laid down he will surprise and pacify a large majority


[037-101]of the American people.


[037-102]--_Mail and Express_, New York, March 10, 1885.


[037-103]RELIGION, PROHIBITION, AND GEN. GRANT.


[037-104]_Question_.  What do you think of prohibition, and what do you


[037-105]think of its success in this State?


[037-106]_Answer_.  Few people understand the restraining influence of


[037-107]liberty.  Moderation walks hand in hand with freedom.  I do not


[037-108]mean the freedom springing from the sudden rupture of restraint.


[037-109]That kind of freedom usually rushes to extremes.


[037-110]People must be educated to take care of themselves, and this


[037-111]education must commence in infancy.  Self-restraint is the only


[037-112]kind that can always be depended upon.  Of course intemperance is


[037-113]a great evil.  It causes immense suffering--clothes wives and


[037-114]children in rags, and is accountable for many crimes, particularly


[037-115]those of violence.  Laws to be of value must be honestly enforced.


[037-116]Laws that sleep had better be dead.  Laws to be enforced must be


[037-117]honestly approved of and believed in by a large majority of the


[037-118]people.  Unpopular laws make hypocrites, perjurers and official


[037-119]shirkers of duty.  And if to the violation of such laws severe


[037-120]penalties attach, they are rarely enforced.  Laws that create


[037-121]artificial crimes are the hardest to carry into effect.  You can


[037-122]never convince a majority of people that it is as bad to import


[037-123]goods without paying the legal duty as to commit larceny.  Neither


[037-124]can you convince a majority of people that it is a crime or sin,


[037-125]or even a mistake, to drink a glass of wine or beer.  Thousands and


[037-126]thousands of people in this State honestly believe that prohibition


[037-127]is an interference with their natural rights, and they feel justified


[037-128]in resorting to almost any means to defeat the law.


[037-129]In this way people become somewhat demoralized.  It is unfortunate


[037-130]to pass laws that remain unenforced on account of their unpopularity.


[037-131]People who would on most subjects swear to the truth do not hesitate


[037-132]to testify falsely on a prohibition trial.  In addition to this,


[037-133]every known device is resorted to, to sell in spite of the law,


[037-134]and when some want to sell and a great many want to buy, considerable


[037-135]business will be done, while there are fewer saloons and less liquor


[037-136]sold in them.  The liquor is poorer and the price is higher.  The


[037-137]consumer has to pay for the extra risk.  More liquor finds its way


[037-138]to homes, more men buy by the bottle and gallon.  In old times


[037-139]nearly everybody kept a little rum or whiskey on the sideboard.


[037-140]The great Washingtonian temperance movement drove liquor out of


[037-141]the home and increased the taverns and saloons.  Now we are driving


[037-142]liquor back to the homes.  In my opinion there is a vast difference


[037-143]between distilled spirits and the lighter drinks, such as wine and


[037-144]beer.  Wine is a fireside and whiskey a conflagration.  These


[037-145]lighter drinks are not unhealthful and do not, as I believe, create


[037-146]a craving for stronger beverages.  You will, I think, find it almost


[037-147]impossible to enforce the present law against wine and beer.  I


[037-148]was told yesterday that there are some sixty places in Cedar Rapids


[037-149]where whiskey is sold.  It takes about as much ceremony to get a


[037-150]drink as it does to join the Masons, but they seem to like the


[037-151]ceremony.  People seem to take delight in outwitting the State when


[037-152]it does not involve the commission of any natural offence, and when


[037-153]about to be caught, may not hesitate to swear falsely to the extent


[037-154]of "don't remember," or "can't say positively," or "can't swear


[037-155]whether it was whiskey or not."


[037-156]One great trouble in Iowa is that the politicians, or many of them


[037-157]who openly advocate prohibition, are really opposed to it.  They


[037-158]want to keep the German vote, and they do not want to lose native


[037-159]Republicans.  They feel a "divided duty" to ride both horses.  This


[037-160]causes the contrast between their conversation and their speeches.


[037-161]A few years ago I took dinner with a gentleman who had been elected


[037-162]Governor of one of our States on the Prohibition ticket.  We had


[037-163]four kinds of wine during the meal, and a pony of brandy at the


[037-164]end.  Prohibition will never be a success until it prohibits the


[037-165]Prohibitionists.  And yet I most sincerely hope and believe that


[037-166]the time will come when drunkenness shall have perished from the


[037-167]earth.  Let us cultivate the love of home.  Let husbands and wives


[037-168]and children be companions.  Let them seek amusements together.


[037-169]If it is a good place for father to go, it is a good place for


[037-170]mother and the children.  I believe that a home can be made more


[037-171]attractive than a saloon.  Let the boys and girls amuse themselves


[037-172]at home--play games, study music, read interesting books, and let


[037-173]the parents be their playfellows.  The best temperance lecture, in


[037-174]the fewest words, you will find in Victor Hugo's great novel "Les


[037-175]Miserables."  The grave digger is asked to take a drink.  He refuses


[037-176]and gives this reason:  "The hunger of my family is the enemy of


[037-177]my thirst."


[037-178]_Question_.  Many people wonder why you are out of politics.  Will


[037-179]you give your reasons?


[037-180]_Answer_.  A few years ago great questions had to be settled.  The


[037-181]life of the nation was at stake.  Later the liberty of millions of


[037-182]slaves depended upon the action of the Government.  Afterward


[037-183]reconstruction and the rights of citizens pressed themselves upon


[037-184]the people for solution.  And last, the preservation of national


[037-185]honor and credit.  These questions did not enter into the last


[037-186]campaign.  They had all been settled, and properly settled, with


[037-187]the one exception of the duty of the nation to protect the colored


[037-188]citizens.  The Supreme Court settled that, at least for a time,


[037-189]and settled it wrong.  But the Republican party submitted to the


[037-190]civil rights decision, and so, as between the great parties, that


[037-191]question did not arise.  This left only two questions--protection


[037-192]and office.  But as a matter of fact, all Republicans were not for


[037-193]our present system of protection, and all Democrats were not against


[037-194]it.  On that question each party was and is divided.  On the other


[037-195]question--office--both parties were and are in perfect harmony.


[037-196]Nothing remains now for the Democrats to do except to give a


[037-197]"working" definition of "offensive partisanship."
