[003-001]_Question_.  I would like to have a positive expression of your


[003-002]views as to a future state?


[003-003]_Answer_.  Somebody asked Confucius about another world, and his


[003-004]reply was:  "How should I know anything about another world when


[003-005]I know so little of this?"  For my part, I know nothing of any


[003-006]other state of existence, either before or after this, and I have


[003-007]never become personally acquainted with anybody that did.  There


[003-008]may be another life, and if there is, the best way to prepare for


[003-009]it is by making somebody happy in this.  God certainly cannot afford


[003-010]to put a man in hell who has made a little heaven in this world.


[003-011]I propose simply to take my chances with the rest of the folks,


[003-012]and prepare to go where the people I am best acquainted with will


[003-013]probably settle.  I cannot afford to leave the great ship and sneak


[003-014]off to shore in some orthodox canoe.  I hope there is another life,


[003-015]for I would like to see how things come out in the world when I am


[003-016]dead.  There are some people I would like to see again, and hope


[003-017]there are some who would not object to seeing me; but if there is


[003-018]no other life I shall never know it.  I do not remember a time when


[003-019]I did not exist; and if, when I die, that is the end, I shall not


[003-020]know it, because the last thing I shall know is that I am alive,


[003-021]and if nothing is left, nothing will be left to know that I am


[003-022]dead; so that so far as I am concerned I am immortal; that is to


[003-023]say, I cannot recollect when I did not exist, and there never will


[003-024]be a time when I shall remember that I do not exist.  I would like


[003-025]to have several millions of dollars, and I may say that I have a


[003-026]lively hope that some day I may be rich, but to tell you the truth


[003-027]I have very little evidence of it.  Our hope of immortality does


[003-028]not come from any religion, but nearly all religions come from that


[003-029]hope.  The Old Testament, instead of telling us that we are immortal,


[003-030]tells us how we lost immortality.  You will recollect that if Adam


[003-031]and Eve could have gotten to the Tree of Life, they would have


[003-032]eaten of its fruit and would have lived forever; but for the purpose


[003-033]of preventing immortality God turned them out of the Garden of


[003-034]Eden, and put certain angels with swords or sabres at the gate to


[003-035]keep them from getting back.  The Old Testament proves, if it proves


[003-036]anything--which I do not think it does--that there is no life after


[003-037]this; and the New Testament is not very specific on the subject.


[003-038]There were a great many opportunities for the Saviour and his


[003-039]apostles to tell us about another world, but they did not improve


[003-040]them to any great extent; and the only evidence, so far as I know,


[003-041]about another life is, first, that we have no evidence; and,


[003-042]secondly, that we are rather sorry that we have not, and wish we


[003-043]had.  That is about my position.


[003-044]_Question_.  According to your observation of men, and your reading


[003-045]in relation to the men and women of the world and of the church,


[003-046]if there is another world divided according to orthodox principles


[003-047]between the orthodox and heterodox, which of the two that are known


[003-048]as heaven and hell would contain, in your judgment, the most good


[003-049]society?


[003-050]_Answer_.  Since hanging has got to be a means of grace, I would


[003-051]prefer hell.  I had a thousand times rather associate with the


[003-052]Pagan philosophers than with the inquisitors of the Middle Ages.


[003-053]I certainly should prefer the worst man in Greek or Roman history


[003-054]to John Calvin; and I can imagine no man in the world that I would


[003-055]not rather sit on the same bench with than the Puritan fathers and


[003-056]the founders of orthodox churches.  I would trade off my harp any


[003-057]minute for a seat in the other country.  All the poets will be in


[003-058]perdition, and the greatest thinkers, and, I should think, most of


[003-059]the women whose society would tend to increase the happiness of


[003-060]man; nearly all the painters, nearly all the sculptors, nearly all


[003-061]the writers of plays, nearly all the great actors, most of the best


[003-062]musicians, and nearly all the good fellows--the persons who know


[003-063]stories, who can sing songs, or who will loan a friend a dollar.


[003-064]They will mostly all be in that country, and if I did not live


[003-065]there permanently, I certainly would want it so I could spend my


[003-066]winter months there.  But, after all, what I really want to do is


[003-067]to destroy the idea of eternal punishment.  That doctrine subverts


[003-068]all ideas of justice.  That doctrine fills hell with honest men,


[003-069]and heaven with intellectual and moral paupers.  That doctrine


[003-070]allows people to sin on credit.  That doctrine allows the basest


[003-071]to be eternally happy and the most honorable to suffer eternal


[003-072]pain.  I think of all doctrines it is the most infinitely infamous,


[003-073]and would disgrace the lowest savage; and any man who believes it,


[003-074]and has imagination enough to understand it, has the heart of a


[003-075]serpent and the conscience of a hyena.


[003-076]_Question_.  Your objective point is to destroy the doctrine of


[003-077]hell, is it?


[003-078]_Answer_.  Yes, because the destruction of that doctrine will do


[003-079]away with all cant and all pretence.  It will do away with all


[003-080]religious bigotry and persecution.  It will allow every man to


[003-081]think and to express his thought.  It will do away with bigotry in


[003-082]all its slimy and offensive forms.


[003-083]--_Chicago Tribune_, November 14, 1879.


[003-084]POLITICS AND GEN. GRANT


[003-085]_Question_.  Some people have made comparisons between the late


[003-086]Senators O. P. Morton and Zach. Chandler.  What did you think of


[003-087]them, Colonel?


[003-088]_Answer_.  I think Morton had the best intellectual grasp of a


[003-089]question of any man I ever saw.  There was an infinite difference


[003-090]between the two men.  Morton's strength lay in proving a thing;


[003-091]Chandler's in asserting it.  But Chandler was a strong man and no


[003-092]hypocrite.


[003-093]_Question_.  Have you any objection to being interviewed as to your


[003-094]ideas of Grant, and his position before the people?


[003-095]_Answer_.  I have no reason for withholding my views on that or


[003-096]any other subject that is under public discussion.  My idea is that


[003-097]Grant can afford to regard the presidency as a broken toy.  It


[003-098]would add nothing to his fame if he were again elected, and would


[003-099]add nothing to the debt of gratitude which the people feel they


[003-100]owe him.  I do not think he will be a candidate.  I do not think


[003-101]he wants it.  There are men who are pushing him on their own account.


[003-102]Grant was a great soldier.  He won the respect of the civilized


[003-103]world.  He commanded the largest army that ever fought for freedom,


[003-104]and to make him President would not add a solitary leaf to the


[003-105]wreath of fame already on his brow; and should he be elected, the


[003-106]only thing he could do would be to keep the old wreath from fading.


[003-107]I do not think his reputation can ever be as great in any direction


[003-108]as in the direction of war.  He has made his reputation and has


[003-109]lived his great life.  I regard him, confessedly, as the best


[003-110]soldier the Anglo-Saxon blood has produced.  I do not know that it


[003-111]necessarily follows because he is a great soldier he is great in


[003-112]other directions.  Probably some of the greatest statesmen in the


[003-113]world would have been the worst soldiers.


[003-114]_Question_.  Do you regard him as more popular now than ever before?


[003-115]_Answer_.  I think that his reputation is certainly greater and


[003-116]higher than when he left the presidency, and mainly because he has


[003-117]represented this country with so much discretion and with such


[003-118]quiet, poised dignity all around the world.  He has measured himself


[003-119]with kings, and was able to look over the heads of every one of


[003-120]them.  They were not quite as tall as he was, even adding the crown


[003-121]to their original height.  I think he represented us abroad with


[003-122]wonderful success.  One thing that touched me very much was, that


[003-123]at a reception given him by the workingmen of Birmingham, after he


[003-124]had been received by royalty, he had the courage to say that that


[003-125]reception gave him more pleasure than any other.  He has been


[003-126]throughout perfectly true to the genius of our institutions, and


[003-127]has not upon any occasion exhibited the slightest toadyism.  Grant


[003-128]is a man who is not greatly affected by either flattery or abuse.


[003-129]_Question_.  What do you believe to be his position in regard to


[003-130]the presidency?


[003-131]_Answer_.  My own judgment is that he does not care.  I do not


[003-132]think he has any enemies to punish, and I think that while he was


[003-133]President he certainly rewarded most of his friends.


[003-134]_Question_.  What are your views as to a third term?


[003-135]_Answer_.  I have no objection to a third term on principle, but


[003-136]so many men want the presidency that it seems almost cruel to give


[003-137]a third term to anyone.


[003-138]_Question_.  Then, if there is no objection to a third term, what


[003-139]about a fourth?


[003-140]_Answer_.  I do not know that that could be objected to, either.


[003-141]We have to admit, after all, that the American people, or at least


[003-142]a majority of them, have a right to elect one man as often as they


[003-143]please.  Personally, I think it should not be done unless in the


[003-144]case of a man who is prominent above the rest of his fellow-citizens,


[003-145]and whose election appears absolutely necessary.  But I frankly


[003-146]confess I cannot conceive of any political situation where one man


[003-147]is a necessity.  I do not believe in the one-man-on-horseback idea,


[003-148]because I believe in all the people being on horseback.


[003-149]_Question_.  What will be the effect of the enthusiastic receptions


[003-150]that are being given to General Grant?


[003-151]_Answer_.  I think these ovations show that the people are resolved


[003-152]not to lose the results of the great victories of the war, and that


[003-153]they make known this determination by their attention to General


[003-154]Grant.  I think that if he goes through the principal cities of


[003-155]this country the old spirit will be revived everywhere, and whether


[003-156]it makes him President or not the result will be to make the election


[003-157]go Republican.  The revival of the memories of the war will bring


[003-158]the people of the North together as closely as at any time since


[003-159]that great conflict closed, not in the spirit of hatred, or malice


[003-160]or envy, but in generous emulation to preserve that which was fairly


[003-161]won.  I do not think there is any hatred about it, but we are


[003-162]beginning to see that we must save the South ourselves, and that


[003-163]that is the only way we can save the nation.


[003-164]_Question_.  But suppose they give the same receptions in the South?


[003-165]_Answer_.  So much the better.


[003-166]_Question_.  Is there any split in the solid South?


[003-167]_Answer_.  Some of the very best people in the South are apparently


[003-168]disgusted with following the Democracy any longer, and would hail


[003-169]with delight any opportunity they could reasonably take advantage


[003-170]of to leave the organization, if they could do so without making


[003-171]it appear that they were going back on Southern interests, and this


[003-172]opportunity will come when the South becomes enlightened, and sees


[003-173]that it has no interests except in common with the whole country.


[003-174]That I think they are beginning to see.


[003-175]_Question_.  How do you like the administration of President Hayes?


[003-176]_Answer_.  I think its attitude has greatly improved of late.


[003-177]There are certain games of cards--pedro, for instance, where you


[003-178]can not only fail to make something, but be set back.  I think that


[003-179]Hayes's veto messages very nearly got him back to the commencement


[003-180]of the game--that he is now almost ready to commence counting, and


[003-181]make some points.  His position before the country has greatly


[003-182]improved, but he will not develop into a dark horse.  My preference


[003-183]is, of course, still for Blaine.
