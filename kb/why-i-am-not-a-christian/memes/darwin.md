[[charles-darwin|Charles Darwin]] (1809–1882) was, for [[robert-ingersoll|Robert G. Ingersoll]], the preeminent scientific figure of the nineteenth century — a man whose [[reason|rational]] investigation of nature had done more to liberate the human mind than centuries of theological pronouncement. Ingersoll placed Darwin alongside [[shakespeare|Shakespeare]] in the pantheon of unaided human genius, and defended his theory of evolution by natural selection against the clerical attacks that greeted its publication. Where orthodox [[christianity|Christians]] saw a threat to [[bible|biblical]] authority, Ingersoll saw the [[truth]] prevailing over [[superstition]].

## Ingersoll's Defense of Darwin

Ingersoll was an early and outspoken public defender of Darwin at a time when the theory of evolution was still bitterly contested in American religious culture. He noted that clergy across denominations united in opposition to the new science:

> He warns them against [[humboldt|Humboldt]] and Darwin, and tells them how much better it would be for them to read the Bible than to investigate for themselves. [016-036]

This observation captured Ingersoll's broader critique: religious authorities did not merely disagree with Darwin — they sought to prevent their followers from engaging with his ideas at all. The priest, Ingersoll noted, "can hold Darwin up to orthodox scorn" [033-022] without ever fairly representing what Darwin actually wrote.

Ingersoll recognized the cultural magnitude of Darwin's achievement:

> The laurel of the nineteenth century is on Darwin's brow. [098-018]

He characterized the acceptance of evolution as a decisive victory for scientific [[truth]] over scriptural literalism:

> Darwin and Haeckel and Huxley and Tyndall — these are the glittering stars of truth. This is Darwin's victory. He gained it not by the sword, but by intellectual force. [098-081, 098-083]

## Darwin and Religion

The relationship between Darwin's science and [[christianity|Christian]] doctrine was a central concern of Ingersoll's public work. He rejected attempts to harmonize evolution with Genesis:

> There is an inexpressible meanness in the attempt to show that Darwin and Moses can be harmonized. [036-129]

For Ingersoll, such harmonization was [[reason|intellect]]ually dishonest: either the biblical creation account was literally true (in which case Darwin was wrong), or Darwin was right (in which case Genesis was mythology). The attempt to hold both positions simultaneously — to claim that evolution was "[[god|God]]'s method of creation" — struck Ingersoll as a face-saving evasion rather than a serious [[reason|intellect]]ual position.

He also noted the irony of religious figures belatedly claiming Darwin as one of their own:

> He has discovered that Darwin is only a later Saint Paul, or that Saint Paul was the original Darwin. [042-032]

## The Darwinian Legacy in Freethought

Darwin's significance for the [[freethought]] movement extended beyond biology. His work demonstrated a methodology — patient observation, hypothesis formation, evidence-based revision — that Ingersoll wished to see applied to religion, [[morality]], and social organization. If the origin of species could be explained without invoking a Creator, then perhaps the origin of conscience could be explained without invoking a divine lawgiver.

| Domain | Pre-Darwinian View | Post-Darwinian View |
|--------|-------------------|---------------------|
| Origin of species | Special creation by [[god|God]] | Natural selection over deep time |
| Human uniqueness | Created in [[god|God]]'s image | A product of the same evolutionary processes |
| Morality | Divine command | Evolved social instinct |
| Scripture | Literal history | Mythology incompatible with science |
