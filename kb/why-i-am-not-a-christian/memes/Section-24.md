[023-001]Justice Harland also shows that while legislation of Congress to


[023-002]enforce a master's right was upheld by implication, the rights of


[023-003]the negro do not depend upon that doctrine; that the Thirteenth


[023-004]Amendment does not rest upon implication, or upon inference; that


[023-005]by its terms it places the power in Congress beyond the possibility


[023-006]of a doubt--conferring the power to enforce the amendment by


[023-007]appropriate legislation in express terms; and he also shows that


[023-008]the Supreme Court has admitted that legislation for that purpose


[023-009]may be direct and primary.  Had not the power been given in express


[023-010]terms, Justice Harlan contends that the sweeping declaration that


[023-011]neither slavery nor involuntary servitude shall exist would by


[023-012]implication confer the power.  He also shows conclusively that,


[023-013]under the Thirteenth Amendment, Congress has the right by appropriate


[023-014]legislation to protect the colored people against the deprivation


[023-015]of any right on account of their race, and that Congress is not


[023-016]necessarily restricted, under the Thirteenth Amendment, to legislation


[023-017]against slavery as an institution, but that power may be exerted


[023-018]to the extent of protecting the race from discrimination in respect


[023-019]to such rights as belong to freemen, where such discrimination is


[023-020]based on race or color.


[023-021]If Justice Harlan is wrong the amendments are left without force


[023-022]and Congress without power.  No purpose can be assigned for their


[023-023]adoption.  No object can be guessed that was to be accomplished.


[023-024]They become words, so arranged that they sound like sense, but when


[023-025]examined fall meaninglessly apart.  Under the decision of the


[023-026]Supreme Court they are Quaker cannon--cloud forts--"property" for


[023-027]political stage scenery--coats of mail made of bronzed paper--


[023-028]shields of gilded pasteboard--swords of lath.


[023-029]_Question_.  Do you wish to say anything as to the reasoning of


[023-030]Justice Harlan on the rights of colored people on railways, in inns


[023-031]and theatres?


[023-032]_Answer_.  Yes, I do.  That part of the opinion is especially


[023-033]strong.  He shows conclusively that a common carrier is in the


[023-034]exercise of a sort of public office and has public duties to perform,


[023-035]and that he cannot exonerate himself from the performance of these


[023-036]duties without the consent of the parties concerned.  He also shows


[023-037]that railroads are public highways, and that the railway company


[023-038]is the agent of the State, and that a railway, although built by


[023-039]private capital, is just as public in its nature as though constructed


[023-040]by the State itself.  He shows that the railway is devoted to public


[023-041]use, and subject to be controlled by the State for the public


[023-042]benefit, and that for these reasons the colored man has the same


[023-043]rights upon the railway that he has upon the public highway.


[023-044]Justice Harlan shows that the same law is applicable to inns that


[023-045]is applicable to railways; that an inn-keeper is bound to take all


[023-046]travelers if he can accommodate them; that he is not to select his


[023-047]guests; that he has not right to say to one "you may come in," and


[023-048]to another "you shall not;" that every one who conducts himself in


[023-049]a proper manner has a right to be received.  He shows conclusively


[023-050]that an inn-keeper is a sort of public servant; that he is in the


[023-051]exercise of a _quasi_ public employment, that he is given special


[023-052]privileges, and charged with duties of a public character.


[023-053]As to theatres, I think his argument most happy.  It is this:


[023-054]Theatres are licensed by law.  The authority to maintain them comes


[023-055]from the public.  The colored race being a part of the public,


[023-056]representing the power granting the license, why should the colored


[023-057]people license a manager to open his doors to the white man and


[023-058]shut them in the face of the black man?  Why should they be compelled


[023-059]to license that which they are not permitted to enjoy?  Justice


[023-060]Harlan shows that Congress has the power to prevent discrimination


[023-061]on account of race or color on railways, at inns, and in places of


[023-062]public amusements, and has this power under the Thirteenth


[023-063]Amendment.


[023-064]In discussing the Fourteenth Amendment, Justice Harlan points out


[023-065]that a prohibition upon a State is not a power in Congress or the


[023-066]National Government, but is simply a denial of power to the State;


[023-067]that such was the Constitution before the Fourteenth Amendment.


[023-068]He shows, however, that the Fourteenth Amendment presents the first


[023-069]instance in our history of the investiture of Congress with


[023-070]affirmative power by legislation to enforce an express prohibition


[023-071]upon the States.  This is an important point.  It is stated with


[023-072]great clearness, and defended with great force.  He shows that the


[023-073]first clause of the first section of the Fourteenth Amendment is


[023-074]of a distinctly affirmative character, and that Congress would have


[023-075]had the power to legislate directly as to that section simply by


[023-076]implication, but that as to that as well as the express prohibitions


[023-077]upon the States, express power to legislate was given.


[023-078]There is one other point made by Justice Harlan which transfixes


[023-079]as with a spear the decision of the Court.  It is this:  As soon


[023-080]as the Thirteenth and Fourteenth Amendments were adopted the colored


[023-081]citizen was entitled to the protection of section two, article


[023-082]four, namely:  "The citizens of each State shall be entitled to


[023-083]all the privileges and immunities of citizens of the several States."


[023-084]Now, suppose a colored citizen of Mississippi moves to Tennessee.


[023-085]Then, under the section last quoted, he would immediately become


[023-086]invested with all the privileges and immunities of a white citizen


[023-087]of Tennessee.  Although denied these privileges and immunities in


[023-088]the State from which he emigrated, in the State to which he immigrates


[023-089]he could not be discriminated against on account of his color under


[023-090]the second section of the fourth article.  Now, is it possible that


[023-091]he gets additional rights by immigration?  Is it possible that the


[023-092]General Government is under a greater obligation to protect him in


[023-093]a State of which he is not a citizen than in a State of which he


[023-094]is a citizen?  Must he leave home for protection, and after he has


[023-095]lived long enough in the State to which he immigrates to become a


[023-096]citizen there, must he again move in order to protect his rights?


[023-097]Must one adopt the doctrine of peripatetic protection--the doctrine


[023-098]that the Constitution is good only _in transitu_, and that when


[023-099]the citizen stops, the Constitution goes on and leaves him without


[023-100]protection?


[023-101]Justice Harlan shows that Congress had the right to legislate


[023-102]directly while that power was only implied, but that the moment


[023-103]this power was conferred in express terms, then according to the


[023-104]Supreme Court, it was lost.


[023-105]There is another splendid definition given by Justice Harlan--a


[023-106]line drawn as broad as the Mississippi.  It is the distinction


[023-107]between the rights conferred by a State and rights conferred by


[023-108]the Nation.  Admitting that many rights conferred by a State cannot


[023-109]be enforced directly by Congress, Justice Harlan shows that rights


[023-110]granted by the Nation to an individual may be protected by direct


[023-111]legislation.  This is a distinction that should not be forgotten,


[023-112]and it is a definition clear and perfect.


[023-113]Justice Harlan has shown that the Supreme Court failed to take into


[023-114]consideration the intention of the framers of the amendment; failed


[023-115]to see that the powers of Congress were given by express terms and


[023-116]did not rest upon implication; failed to see that the Thirteenth


[023-117]Amendment was broad enough to cover the Civil Rights Act; failed


[023-118]to see that under the three amendments rights and privileges were


[023-119]conferred by the Nation on citizens of the several States, and that


[023-120]these rights are under the perpetual protection of the General


[023-121]Government, and that for their enforcement Congress has the right


[023-122]to legislate directly; failed to see that all implications are now


[023-123]in favor of liberty instead of slavery; failed to comprehend that


[023-124]we have a new nation with a new foundation, with different objects,


[023-125]ends, and aims, for the attainment of which we use different means


[023-126]and have been clothed with greater powers; failed to see that the


[023-127]Republic changed front; failed to appreciate the real reasons for


[023-128]the adoption of the amendments, and failed to understand that the


[023-129]Civil Rights Act was passed in order that a citizen of the United


[023-130]States might appeal from local prejudice to national justice.


[023-131]Justice Harlan shows that it was the object to accomplish for the


[023-132]black man what had been accomplished for the white man--that is,


[023-133]to protect all their rights as free men and citizens; and that the


[023-134]one underlying purpose of the amendments and of the congressional


[023-135]legislation has been to clothe the black race with all the rights


[023-136]of citizenship, and to compel a recognition of their rights by


[023-137]citizens and States--that the object was to do away with class


[023-138]tyranny, the meanest and basest form of oppression.


[023-139]If Justice Harlan was wrong in his position, then, it may truthfully


[023-140]be said of the three amendments that:


[023-141]"The law hath bubbles as the water has,


[023-142]And these are of them."


[023-143]The decision of the Supreme Court denies the protection of the


[023-144]Nation to the citizens of the Nation.  That decision has already


[023-145]borne fruit--the massacre at Danville.  The protection of the Nation


[023-146]having been withdrawn, the colored man was left to the mercy of


[023-147]local prejudices and hatreds.  He is without appeal, without redress.


[023-148]The Supreme Court tells him that he must depend upon his enemies


[023-149]for justice.


[023-150]_Question_.  You seem to agree with all that Justice Harlan has


[023-151]said, and to have the greatest admiration for his opinion?


[023-152]_Answer_.  Yes, a man rises from reading this dissenting opinion


[023-153]refreshed, invigorated, and strengthened.  It is a mental and moral


[023-154]tonic.  It was produced after a clear head had held conference with


[023-155]a good heart.  It will furnish a perfectly clear plank, without


[023-156]knot or wind-shake, for the next Republican platform.  It is written


[023-157]in good plain English, and ornamented with good sound sense.  The


[023-158]average man can and will understand its every word.  There is no


[023-159]subterfuge in it.


[023-160]Each position is taken in the open field.  There is no resort to


[023-161]quibbles or technicalities--no hiding.  Nothing is secreted in the


[023-162]sleeve--no searching for blind paths--no stooping and looking for


[023-163]ancient tracks, grass-grown and dim.  Each argument travels the


[023-164]highway--"the big road."  It is logical.  The facts and conclusions


[023-165]agree, and fall naturally into line of battle.  It is sincere and


[023-166]candid--unpretentious and unanswerable.  It is a grand defence of


[023-167]human rights--a brave and manly plea for universal justice.  It


[023-168]leaves the decision of the Supreme Court without argument, without


[023-169]reason, and without excuse.  Such an exhibition of independence,


[023-170]courage and ability has won for Justice Harlan the respect and


[023-171]admiration of "both sides," and places him in the front rank of


[023-172]constitutional lawyers.


[023-173]--_The Inter-Ocean_, Chicago, Illinois, November 29, 1883.


[023-174]POLITICS AND THEOLOGY.


[023-175]_Question_.  What is your opinion of Brewster's administration?


[023-176]_Answer_.  I hardly think I ought to say much about the administration


[023-177]of Mr. Brewster.  Of course many things have been done that I


[023-178]thought, and still think, extremely bad; but whether Mr. Brewster


[023-179]was responsible for the things done, or not, I do not pretend to


[023-180]say.  When he was appointed to his present position, there was


[023-181]great excitement in the country about the Star Route cases, and


[023-182]Mr. Brewster was expected to prosecute everybody and everything to


[023-183]the extent of the law; in fact, I believe he was appointed by reason


[023-184]of having made such a promise.  At that time there were hundreds


[023-185]of people interested in exaggerating all the facts connected with


[023-186]the Star Route cases, and when there were no facts to be exaggerated,


[023-187]they made some, and exaggerated them afterward.  It may be that


[023-188]the Attorney-General was misled, and he really supposed that all


[023-189]he heard was true.  My objection to the administration of the


[023-190]Department of Justice is, that a resort was had to spies and


[023-191]detectives.  The battle was not fought in the open field.  Influences


[023-192]were brought to bear.  Nearly all departments of the Government


[023-193]were enlisted.  Everything was done to create a public opinion in


[023-194]favor of the prosecution.  Everything was done that the cases might


[023-195]be decided on prejudice instead of upon facts.
