[098-001]_Answer_.  When religion becomes scientific, it ceases to be religion


[098-002]and becomes science.  Religion is not intellectual--it is emotional.


[098-003]It does not appeal to the reason.  The founder of a religion has


[098-004]always said:  "Let him that hath ears to hear, hear!"  No founder


[098-005]has said:  "Let him that hath brains to think, think!"  Besides,


[098-006]we need not trouble ourselves about "spirit" and "flesh."  We know


[098-007]that we know of no spirit--without flesh.  We have no evidence that


[098-008]spirit ever did or ever will exist apart from flesh.  Such existence


[098-009]is absolutely inconceivable.  If we are going to construct what


[098-010]you call a "religion," it must be founded on observed and known


[098-011]facts.  Theories, to be of value, must be in accord with all the


[098-012]facts that are known; otherwise they are worthless.  We need not


[098-013]try to get back of facts or behind the truth.  The _why_ will


[098-014]forever elude us.  You cannot move your hand quickly enough to


[098-015]grasp your image back of the mirror.


[098-016]--_Mind_, New York, March, 1899.


[098-017]THIS CENTURY'S GLORIES.


[098-018]The laurel of the nineteenth century is on Darwin's brow.  This


[098-019]century has been the greatest of all.  The inventions, the discoveries,


[098-020]the victories on the fields of thought, the advances in nearly


[098-021]every direction of human effort are without parallel in human


[098-022]history.  In only two directions have the achievements of this


[098-023]century been excelled.  The marbles of Greece have not been equalled.


[098-024]They still occupy the niches dedicated to perfection.  They sculptors


[098-025]of our century stand before the miracles of the Greeks in impotent


[098-026]wonder.  They cannot even copy.  They cannot give the breath of


[098-027]life to stone and make the marble feel and think.  The plays of


[098-028]Shakespeare have never been approached.  He reached the summit,


[098-029]filled the horizon.  In the direction of the dramatic, the poetic,


[098-030]the human mind, in my judgment, in Shakespeare's plays reached its


[098-031]limit.  The field was harvested, all the secrets of the heart were


[098-032]told.  The buds of all hopes blossomed, all seas were crossed and


[098-033]all the shores were touched.


[098-034]With these two exceptions, the Grecian marbles and the Shakespeare


[098-035]plays, the nineteenth century has produced more for the benefit of


[098-036]man than all the centuries of the past.  In this century, in one


[098-037]direction, I think the mind has reached the limit.  I do not believe


[098-038]the music of Wagner will ever be excelled.  He changed all passions,


[098-039]longing, memories and aspirations into tones, and with subtle


[098-040]harmonies wove tapestries of sound, whereon were pictured the past


[098-041]and future, the history and prophecy of the human heart.  Of course


[098-042]Copernicus, Galileo, Newton and Kepler laid the foundations of


[098-043]astronomy.  It may be that the three laws of Kepler mark the highest


[098-044]point in that direction that the mind has reached.


[098-045]In the other centuries there is now and then a peak, but through


[098-046]ours there runs a mountain range with Alp on Alp--the steamship


[098-047]that has conquered all the seas; the railway, with its steeds of


[098-048]steel with breath of flame, covers the land; the cables and


[098-049]telegraphs, along which lightning is the carrier of thought, have


[098-050]made the nations neighbors and brought the world to every home;


[098-051]the making of paper from wood, the printing presses that made it


[098-052]possible to give the history of the human race each day; the reapers,


[098-053]mowers and threshers that superseded the cradles, scythes and


[098-054]flails; the lighting of streets and houses with gas and incandescent


[098-055]lamps, changing night into day; the invention of matches that made


[098-056]fire the companion of man; the process of making steel, invented


[098-057]by Bessemer, saving for the world hundreds of millions a year; the


[098-058]discovery of anesthetics, changing pain to happy dreams and making


[098-059]surgery a science; the spectrum analysis, that told us the secrets


[098-060]of the suns; the telephone, that transports speech, uniting lips


[098-061]and ears; the phonograph, that holds in dots and marks the echoes


[098-062]of our words; the marvelous machines that spin and weave, that


[098-063]manufacture the countless things of use, the marvelous machines,


[098-064]whose wheels and levers seem to think; the discoveries in chemistry,


[098-065]the wave theory of light, the indestructibility of matter and force;


[098-066]the discovery of microbes and bacilli, so that now the plague can


[098-067]be stayed without the assistance of priests.


[098-068]The art of photography became known, the sun became an artist, gave


[098-069]us the faces of our friends, copies of the great paintings and


[098-070]statues, pictures of the world's wonders, and enriched the eyes of


[098-071]poverty with the spoil of travel, the wealth of art.  The cell


[098-072]theory was advanced, embryology was studied and science entered


[098-073]the secret house of life.  The biologists, guided by fossil forms,


[098-074]followed the paths of life from protoplasm up to man.  Then came


[098-075]Darwin with the "Origin of Species," "Natural Selection," and the


[098-076]"Survival of the Fittest."  From his brain there came a flood of


[098-077]light.  The old theories grew foolish and absurd.  The temple of


[098-078]every science was rebuilt.  That which had been called philosophy


[098-079]became childish superstition.  The prison doors were opened and


[098-080]millions of convicts, of unconscious slaves, roved with joy over


[098-081]the fenceless fields of freedom.  Darwin and Haeckel and Huxley


[098-082]and their fellow-workers filled the night of ignorance with the


[098-083]glittering stars of truth.  This is Darwin's victory.  He gained


[098-084]the greatest victory, the grandest triumph.  The laurel of the


[098-085]nineteenth century is on his brow.


[098-086]_Question_.  How does the literature of to-day compare with that


[098-087]of the first half of the century, in your opinion?


[098-088]_Answer_.  There is now no poet of laughter and tears, of comedy


[098-089]and pathos, the equal of Hood.  There is none with the subtle


[098-090]delicacy, the aerial footstep, the flame-like motion of Shelley;


[098-091]none with the amplitude, sweep and passion, with the strength and


[098-092]beauty, the courage and royal recklessness of Byron.  The novelists


[098-093]of our day are not the equals of Dickens.  In my judgment, Dickens


[098-094]wrote the greatest of all novels.  "The Tale of Two Cities" is the


[098-095]supreme work of fiction.  Its philosophy is perfect.  The characters


[098-096]stand out like living statues.  In its pages you find the blood


[098-097]and flame, the ferocity and self-sacrifice of the French Revolution.


[098-098]In the bosom of the Vengeance is the heart of the horror.  In 105,


[098-099]North Tower, sits one whom sorrow drove beyond the verge, rescued


[098-100]from death by insanity, and we see the spirit of Dr. Manette


[098-101]tremblingly cross the great gulf that lies between the night of


[098-102]dreams and the blessed day, where things are as they seem, as a


[098-103]tress of golden hair, while on his hands and cheeks fall Lucie's


[098-104]blessed tears.  The story is filled with lights and shadows, with


[098-105]the tragic and grotesque. While the woman knits, while the heads


[098-106]fall, Jerry Cruncher gnaws his rusty nails and his poor wife "flops"


[098-107]against his business, and prim Miss Pross, who in the desperation


[098-108]and terror of love held Mme. Defarge in her arms and who in the


[098-109]flash and crash found that her burden was dead, is drawn by the


[098-110]hand of a master.  And what shall I say of Sidney Carton?  Of his


[098-111]last walk?  Of his last ride, holding the poor girl by the hand?


[098-112]Is there a more wonderful character in all the realm of fiction?


[098-113]Sidney Carton, the perfect lover, going to his death for the love


[098-114]of one who loves another.  To me the three greatest novels are "The


[098-115]Tale of Two Cities," by Dickens, "Les Miserables," by Hugo, and


[098-116]"Ariadne," by Ouida.


[098-117]"Les Miserables" is full of faults and perfections.  The tragic is


[098-118]sometimes pushed to the grotesque, but from the depths it brings


[098-119]the pearls of truth.  A convict becomes holier than the saint, a


[098-120]prostitute purer than the nun.  This book fills the gutter with


[098-121]the glory of heaven, while the waters of the sewer reflect the


[098-122]stars.


[098-123]In "Ariadne" you find the aroma of all art.  It is a classic dream.


[098-124]And there, too, you find the hot blood of full and ample life.


[098-125]Ouida is the greatest living writer of fiction.  Some of her books


[098-126]I do not like.  If you wish to know what Ouida really is, read


[098-127]"Wanda," "The Dog of Flanders," "The Leaf in a Storm."  In these


[098-128]you will hear the beating of her heart.


[098-129]Most of the novelists of our time write good stories.  They are


[098-130]ingenious, the characters are well drawn, but they lack life,


[098-131]energy.  They do not appear to act for themselves, impelled by


[098-132]inner force.  They seem to be pushed and pulled.  The same may be


[098-133]said of the poets.  Tennyson belongs to the latter half of our


[098-134]century.  He was undoubtedly a great writer.  He had no flame or


[098-135]storm, no tidal wave, nothing volcanic.  He never overflowed the


[098-136]banks.  He wrote nothing as intense, as noble and pathetic as the


[098-137]"Prisoner of Chillon;" nothing as purely poetic as "The Skylark;"


[098-138]nothing as perfect as the "Grecian Urn," and yet he was one of the


[098-139]greatest of poets.  Viewed from all sides he was far greater than


[098-140]Shelley, far nobler than Keats.  In a few poems Shelley reached


[098-141]almost the perfect, but many are weak, feeble, fragmentary, almost


[098-142]meaningless.  So Keats in three poems reached a great height--in


[098-143]"St. Agnes' Eve," "The Grecian Urn," and "The Nightingale"--but


[098-144]most of his poetry is insipid, without thought, beauty or sincerity.


[098-145]We have had some poets ourselves.  Emerson wrote many poetic and


[098-146]philosophic lines.  He never violated any rule.  He kept his passions


[098-147]under control and generally "kept off the grass."  But he uttered


[098-148]some great and splendid truths and sowed countless seeds of


[098-149]suggestion.  When we remember that he came of a line of New England


[098-150]preachers we are amazed at the breadth, the depth and the freedom


[098-151]of his thought.


[098-152]Walt Whitman wrote a few great poems, elemental, natural--poems


[098-153]that seem to be a part of nature, ample as the sky, having the


[098-154]rhythm of the tides, the swing of a planet.


[098-155]Whitcomb Riley has written poems of hearth and home, of love and


[098-156]labor worthy of Robert Burns.  He is the sweetest, strongest singer


[098-157]in our country and I do not know his equal in any land.


[098-158]But when we compare the literature of the first half of this century


[098-159]with that of the last, we are compelled to say that the last, taken


[098-160]as a whole, is best.  Think of the volumes that science has given


[098-161]to the world.  In the first half of this century, sermons, orthodox


[098-162]sermons, were published and read.  Now reading sermons is one of


[098-163]the lost habits.  Taken as a whole, the literature of the latter


[098-164]half of our century is better than the first.  I like the essays


[098-165]of Prof. Clifford.  They are so clear, so logical that they are


[098-166]poetic.  Herbert Spencer is not simply instructive, he is charming.


[098-167]He is full of true imagination.  He is not the slave of imagination.


[098-168]Imagination is his servant.  Huxley wrote like a trained swordsman.


[098-169]His thrusts were never parried.  He had superb courage.  He never


[098-170]apologized for having an opinion.  There was never on his soul the


[098-171]stain of evasion.  He was as candid as the truth.  Haeckel is a


[098-172]great writer because he reveres a fact, and would not for his life


[098-173]deny or misinterpret one.  He tells what he knows with the candor


[098-174]of a child and defends his conclusions like a scientist, a philosopher.


[098-175]He stands next to Darwin.


[098-176]Coming back to fiction and poetry, I have great admiration for


[098-177]Edgar Fawcett.  There is in his poetry thought, beauty and philosophy.


[098-178]He has the courage of his thought.  He knows our language, the


[098-179]energy of verbs, the color of adjectives.  He is in the highest


[098-180]sense an artist.


[098-181]_Question_.  What do you think of Hall Caine's recent efforts to


[098-182]bring about a closer union between the stage and pulpit?


[098-183]_Answer_.  Of course, I am not certain as to the intentions of Mr.


[098-184]Caine.  I saw "The Christian," and it did not seem to me that the


[098-185]author was trying to catch the clergy.
