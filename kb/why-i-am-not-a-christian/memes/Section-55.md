[054-001]--_New York Press_, June 3, 1888.


[054-002]CLEVELAND AND THURMAN.


[054-003]_Question_.  What do you think of the Democratic nominations?


[054-004]_Answer_.  In the first place, I hope that this campaign is to be


[054-005]fought on the issues involved, and not on the private characters


[054-006]of the candidates.  All that they have done as politicians--all


[054-007]measures that they have favored or opposed--these are the proper


[054-008]subjects of criticism; in all other respects I think it better to


[054-009]let the candidates alone.  I care but little about the private


[054-010]character of Mr. Cleveland or of Mr. Thurman.  The real question


[054-011]is, what do they stand for?  What policy do they advocate?  What


[054-012]are the reasons for and against the adoption of the policy they


[054-013]propose?


[054-014]I do not regard Cleveland as personally popular.  He has done


[054-015]nothing, so far as I know, calculated to endear him to the popular


[054-016]heart.  He certainly is not a man of enthusiasm.  He has said


[054-017]nothing of a striking or forcible character.  His messages are


[054-018]exceedingly commonplace.  He is not a man of education, of wide


[054-019]reading, of refined tastes, or of general cultivation.  He has some


[054-020]firmness and a good deal of obstinacy, and he was exceedingly


[054-021]fortunate in his marriage.


[054-022]Four years ago he was distinctly opposed to a second term.  He was


[054-023]then satisfied that no man should be elected President more than


[054-024]once.  He was then fearful that a President might use his office,


[054-025]his appointing power, to further his own ends instead of for the


[054-026]good of the people.  He started, undoubtedly, with that idea in


[054-027]his mind.  He was going to carry out the civil service doctrine to


[054-028]the utmost.  But when he had been President a few months he was


[054-029]exceedingly unpopular with his party.  The Democrats who elected


[054-030]him had been out of office for twenty-five years.  During all those


[054-031]years they had watched the Republicans sitting at the national


[054-032]banquet.  Their appetites had grown keener and keener, and they


[054-033]expected when the 4th of March, 1885, came that the Republicans


[054-034]would be sent from the table and that they would be allowed to tuck


[054-035]the napkins under their chins.  The moment Cleveland got at the


[054-036]head of the table he told his hungry followers that there was


[054-037]nothing for them, and he allowed the Republicans to go on as usual.


[054-038]In a little while he began to hope for a second term, and gradually


[054-039]the civil service notion faded from his mind.  He stuck to it long


[054-040]enough to get the principal mugwump papers committed to him and to


[054-041]his policy; long enough to draw their fire and to put them in a


[054-042]place where they could not honorably retreat without making themselves


[054-043]liable to the charge of having fought only for the loaves and


[054-044]fishes.  As a matter of fact, no men were hungrier for office than


[054-045]the gentlemen who had done so much for civil service reform.  They


[054-046]were so earnest in the advocacy of that principle that they insisted


[054-047]that only their followers should have place; but the real rank and


[054-048]file, the men who had been Democrats through all the disastrous


[054-049]years, and who had prayed and fasted, became utterly disgusted with


[054-050]Mr. Cleveland's administration and they were not slow to express


[054-051]their feelings.  Mr. Cleveland saw that he was in danger of being


[054-052]left with no supporters, except a few who thought themselves too


[054-053]respectable really to join the Democratic party.  So for the last


[054-054]two years, and especially the last year, he turned his attention


[054-055]to pacifying the real Democrats.  He is not the choice of the


[054-056]Democratic party.  Although unanimously nominated, I doubt if he


[054-057]was the unanimous choice of a single delegate.


[054-058]Another very great mistake, I think, has been made by Mr. Cleveland.


[054-059]He seems to have taken the greatest delight in vetoing pension


[054-060]bills, and they seem to be about the only bills he has examined,


[054-061]and he has examined them as a lawyer would examine the declaration,


[054-062]brief or plea of his opponent.  He has sought for technicalities,


[054-063]to the end that he might veto these bills.  By this course he has


[054-064]lost the soldier vote, and there is no way by which he can regain


[054-065]it.  Upon this point I regard the President as exceedingly weak.


[054-066]He has shown about the same feeling toward the soldier now that he


[054-067]did during the war.  He was not with them then either in mind or


[054-068]body.  He is not with them now.  His sympathies are on the other


[054-069]side.  He has taken occasion to show his contempt for the Democratic


[054-070]party again and again.  This certainly will not add to his strength.


[054-071]He has treated the old leaders with great arrogance.  He has cared


[054-072]nothing for their advice, for their opinions, or for their feelings.


[054-073]The principal vestige of monarchy or despotism in our Constitution


[054-074]is the veto power, and this has been more liberally used by Mr.


[054-075]Cleveland than by any other President.  This shows the nature of


[054-076]the man and how narrow he is, and through what a small intellectual


[054-077]aperture he views the world.  Nothing is farther from true democracy


[054-078]than this perpetual application of the veto power.  As a matter of


[054-079]fact, it should be abolished, and the utmost that a President should


[054-080]be allowed to do, would be to return a bill with his objections,


[054-081]and the bill should then become a law upon being passed by both


[054-082]houses by a simple majority.  This would give the Executive the


[054-083]opportunity of calling attention to the supposed defects, and


[054-084]getting the judgment of Congress a second time.


[054-085]I am perfectly satisfied that Mr. Cleveland is not popular with


[054-086]his party.  The noise and confusion of the convention, the cheers


[054-087]and cries, were all produced and manufactured for effect and for


[054-088]the purpose of starting the campaign.


[054-089]Now, as to Senator Thurman.  During the war he occupied substantially


[054-090]the same position occupied by Mr. Cleveland.  He was opposed to


[054-091]putting down the Rebellion by force, and as I remember it, he rather


[054-092]justified the people of the South for going with their States.


[054-093]Ohio was in favor of putting down the Rebellion, yet Mr. Thurman,


[054-094]by some peculiar logic of his own, while he justified Southern


[054-095]people for going into rebellion because they followed their States,


[054-096]justified himself for not following his State.  His State was for


[054-097]the Union.  His State was in favor of putting down rebellion.  His


[054-098]State was in favor of destroying slavery.  Certainly, if a man is


[054-099]bound to follow his State, he is equally bound when the State is


[054-100]right.  It is hardly reasonable to say that a man is only bound to


[054-101]follow his State when his State is wrong; yet this was really the


[054-102]position of Senator Thurman.


[054-103]I saw the other day that some gentlemen in this city had given as


[054-104]a reason for thinking that Thurman would strengthen the ticket,


[054-105]that he had always been right on the financial question.  Now, as


[054-106]a matter of fact, he was always wrong.  When it was necessary for


[054-107]the Government to issue greenbacks, he was a hard money man--he


[054-108]believed in the mint drops--and if that policy had been carried


[054-109]out, the Rebellion could not have been suppressed.  After the


[054-110]suppression of the Rebellion, and when hundreds and hundreds of


[054-111]millions of greenbacks were afloat, and the Republican party proposed


[054-112]to redeem them in gold, and to go back--as it always intended to


[054-113]do--to hard money--to a gold and silver basis--then Senator Thurman,


[054-114]holding aloft the red bandanna, repudiated hard money, opposed


[054-115]resumption, and came out for rag currency as being the best.  Let


[054-116]him change his ideas--put those first that he had last--and you


[054-117]might say that he was right on the currency question; but when the


[054-118]country needed the greenback he was opposed to it, and when the


[054-119]country was able to redeem the greenback, he was opposed to it.


[054-120]It gives me pleasure to say that I regard Senator Thurman as a man


[054-121]of ability, and I have no doubt that he was coaxed into his last


[054-122]financial position by the Democratic party, by the necessities of


[054-123]Ohio, and by the force and direction of the political wind.  No


[054-124]matter how much respectability he adds to the ticket, I do not


[054-125]believe that he will give any great strength.  In the first place,


[054-126]he is an old man.  He has substantially finished his career.  Young


[054-127]men cannot attach themselves to him, because he has no future.


[054-128]His following is not an army of the young and ambitious--it is


[054-129]rather a funeral procession.  Yet, notwithstanding this fact, he


[054-130]will furnish most of the enthusiasm for this campaign--and that


[054-131]will be done with his handkerchief.  The Democratic banner is


[054-132]Thurman's red bandanna.  I do not believe that it will be possible


[054-133]for the Democracy to carry Ohio by reason of Thurman's nomination,


[054-134]and I think the failure to nominate Gray or some good man from that


[054-135]State, will lose Indiana.  So, while I have nothing to say against


[054-136]Senator Thurman, nothing against his integrity or his ability,


[054-137]still, under the circumstances, I do not think his nomination a


[054-138]strong one.


[054-139]_Question_.  Do you think that the nominations have been well


[054-140]received throughout the United States?


[054-141]_Answer_.  Not as well as in England.  I see that all the Tory


[054-142]papers regard the nominations as excellent--especially that of


[054-143]Cleveland.  Every Englishman who wants Ireland turned into a


[054-144]penitentiary, and every Irishman to be treated as a convict, is


[054-145]delighted with the action of the St. Louis convention.  England


[054-146]knows what she wants.  Her market is growing small.  A few years


[054-147]ago she furnished manufactured articles to a vast portion of the


[054-148]world.  Millions of her customers have become ingenious enough to


[054-149]manufacture many things that they need, so the next thing England


[054-150]did was to sell them the machinery.  Now they are beginning to make


[054-151]their own machinery.  Consequently, English trade is falling off.


[054-152]She must have new customers.  Nothing would so gratify her as to


[054-153]have sixty millions of Americans buy her wares.  If she could see


[054-154]our factories still and dead; if she could put out the fires of


[054-155]our furnaces and forges; there would come to her the greatest


[054-156]prosperity she has ever known.  She would fatten on our misfortunes


[054-157]--grow rich and powerful and arrogant upon our poverty.  We would


[054-158]become her servants.  We would raise the raw material with ignorant


[054-159]labor and allow her children to reap all the profit of its manufacture,


[054-160]and in the meantime to become intelligent and cultured while we


[054-161]grew poor and ignorant.


[054-162]The greatest blow that can be inflicted upon England is to keep


[054-163]her manufactured articles out of the United States.  Sixty millions


[054-164]of Americans buy and use more than five hundred millions of Asiatics


[054-165]--buy and use more than all of China, all of India and all of


[054-166]Africa.  One civilized man has a thousand times the wants of a


[054-167]savage or of a semi-barbarian.  Most of the customers of England


[054-168]want a few yards of calico, some cheap jewelry, a little powder,


[054-169]a few knives and a few gallons of orthodox rum.


[054-170]To-day the United States is the greatest market in the world.  The


[054-171]commerce between the States is almost inconceivable in its immensity.


[054-172]In order that you may have some idea of the commerce of this country,


[054-173]it is only necessary to remember one fact.  We have railroads enough


[054-174]engaged in this commerce to make six lines around the globe.  The


[054-175]addition of a million Americans to our population gives us a better


[054-176]market than a monopoly of ten millions of Asiatics.  England, with


[054-177]her workhouses, with her labor that barely exists, wishes this


[054-178]market, and wishes to destroy the manufactures of America, and she


[054-179]expects Irish-Americans to assist her in this patriotic business.


[054-180]Now, as to the enthusiasm in this country.  I fail to see it.  The


[054-181]nominations have fallen flat.  It has been known for a long time


[054-182]that Cleveland was to be nominated.  That has all been discounted,


[054-183]and the nomination of Judge Thurman has been received in a quite


[054-184]matter-of-fact way.  It may be that his enthusiasm was somewhat


[054-185]dampened by what might be called the appearance above the horizon


[054-186]of the morning star of this campaign--Oregon.  What a star to rise


[054-187]over the work of the St. Louis convention!  What a prophecy for


[054-188]Democrats to commence business with!  Oregon, with the free trade


[054-189]issue, seven thousand to eight thousand Republican majority--the


[054-190]largest ever given by that State--Oregon speaks for the Pacific


[054-191]Coast.
