[053-001]I do not claim that protection is for the benefit of any particular


[053-002]class, but that it is for the benefit not only of that particular


[053-003]class, but of the entire country.  In England the common laborer


[053-004]expects to spend his old age in some workhouse.  He is cheered


[053-005]through all his days of toil, through all his years of weariness,


[053-006]by the prospect of dying a respectable pauper.  The women work as


[053-007]hard as the men.  They toil in the iron mills.  They make nails,


[053-008]they dig coal, they toil in the fields.


[053-009]In Europe they carry the hod, they work like beasts and with beasts,


[053-010]until they lose almost the semblance of human beings--until they


[053-011]look inferior to the animals they drive.  On the labor of these


[053-012]deformed mothers, of these bent and wrinkled girls, of little boys


[053-013]with the faces of old age, the heartless nobility live in splendor


[053-014]and extravagant idleness.  I am not now speaking of the French


[053-015]people, as France is the most prosperous country in Europe.


[053-016]Let us protect our mothers, our wives and our children from the


[053-017]deformity of toil, from the depths of poverty.


[053-018]_Question_.  Is not the ballot an assurance to the laboring man


[053-019]that he can get fair treatment from his employer?


[053-020]_Answer_.  The laboring man in this country has the political power,


[053-021]provided he has the intelligence to know it and the intelligence


[053-022]to use it.  In so far as laws can assist labor, the workingman has


[053-023]it in his power to pass such laws; but in most foreign lands the


[053-024]laboring man has really no voice.  It is enough for him to work


[053-025]and wait and suffer and emigrate.  He can take refuge in the grave


[053-026]or go to America.


[053-027]In the old country, where people have been taught that all blessing


[053-028]come from the king, it is very natural for the poor to believe the


[053-029]other side of that proposition--that is to say, all evils come from


[053-030]the king, from the government.  They are rocked in the cradle of


[053-031]this falsehood.  So when they come to this country, if they are


[053-032]unfortunate, it is natural for them to blame the Government.


[053-033]The discussion of these questions, however, has already done great


[053-034]good.  The workingman is becoming more and more intelligent.  He


[053-035]is getting a better idea every day of the functions and powers and


[053-036]limitations of government, and if the problem is ever worked out--


[053-037]and by "problem" I mean the just and due relations that should


[053-038]exist between labor and capital--it will be worked out here in


[053-039]America.


[053-040]_Question_.  What assurance has the American laborer that he will


[053-041]not be ultimately swamped by foreign immigration?


[053-042]_Answer_.  Most of the immigrants that come to American come because


[053-043]they want a home.  Nearly every one of them is what you may call


[053-044]"land hungry."  In his country, to own a piece of land was to be


[053-045]respectable, almost a nobleman.  The owner of a little land was


[053-046]regarded as the founder of a family--what you might call a "village


[053-047]dynasty."  When they leave their native shores for America, their


[053-048]dream is to become a land owner--to have fields, to own trees, and


[053-049]to listen to the music of their own brooks.


[053-050]The moment they arrive the mass of them seek the West, where land


[053-051]can be obtained.  The great Northwest now is being filled with


[053-052]Scandinavian farmers, with persons from every part of Germany--in


[053-053]fact from all foreign countries--and every year they are adding


[053-054]millions of acres to the plowed fields of the Republic.  This land


[053-055]hunger, this desire to own a home, to have a field, to have flocks


[053-056]and herds, to sit under your own vine and fig tree, will prevent


[053-057]foreign immigration from interfering to any hurtful degree with


[053-058]the skilled workmen of America.  These land owners, these farmers,


[053-059]become consumers of manufactured articles.  They keep the wheels


[053-060]and spindles turning and the fires in the forges burning.


[053-061]_Question_.  What do you think of Cleveland's message?


[053-062]_Answer_.  Only the other day I read a speech made by the Hon.


[053-063]William D. Kelley, of Pennsylvania, upon this subject, in which he


[053-064]says in answer to what he calls "the puerile absurdity of President


[053-065]Cleveland's assumption" that the duty is always added to the cost,


[053-066]not only of imported commodities, but to the price of like commodities


[053-067]produced in this country, "that the duties imposed by our Government


[053-068]on sugar reduced to _ad valorem_ were never so high as now, and


[053-069]the price of sugar was never in this country so low as it is now."


[053-070]He also showed that this tax on sugar has made it possible for us


[053-071]to produce sugar from other plants and he gives the facts in relation


[053-072]to corn sugar.


[053-073]We are now using annually nineteen million bushels of corn for the


[053-074]purpose of making glucose or corn sugar.  He shows that in this


[053-075]industry alone there has been a capital invested of eleven million


[053-076]dollars; that seven hundred and thirty-two thousand acres of land


[053-077]are required to furnish the supply, and that this one industry now


[053-078]gives employment to about twenty-two thousand farmers, about five


[053-079]thousand laborers in factories, and that the annual value of this


[053-080]product of corn sugar is over seventeen million dollars.


[053-081]He also shows what we may expect from the cultivation of the beet.


[053-082]I advise every one to read that speech, so that they may have some


[053-083]idea of the capabilities of this country, of the vast wealth asking


[053-084]for development, of the countless avenues opened for ingenuity,


[053-085]energy and intelligence.


[053-086]_Question_.  Does the protective tariff cheapen the prices of


[053-087]commodities to the laboring man?


[053-088]_Answer_.  In this there are involved two questions.  If the tariff


[053-089]is so low that the foreign article is imported, of course this


[053-090]tariff is added to the cost and must be paid by the consumer; but


[053-091]if the protective tariff is so high that the importer cannot pay


[053-092]it, and as a consequence the article is produced in America, then


[053-093]it depends largely upon competition whether the full amount of the


[053-094]tariff will be added to the article.  As a rule, competition will


[053-095]settle that question in America, and the article will be sold as


[053-096]cheaply as the producers can afford.


[053-097]For instance:  If there is a tariff, we will say of fifty cents on


[053-098]a pair of shoes, and this tariff is so low that the foreign article


[053-099]can afford to pay it, then that tariff, of course, must be paid by


[053-100]the consumer.  But suppose the tariff was five dollars on a pair


[053-101]of shoes--that is to say, absolutely prohibitory--does any man in


[053-102]his senses say that five dollars would be added to each pair of


[053-103]American shoes?  Of course, the statement is the answer.


[053-104]I think it is the duty of the laboring man in this country, first,


[053-105]thoroughly to post himself upon these great questions, to endeavor


[053-106]to understand his own interest as well as the interest of his


[053-107]country, and if he does, I believe he will arrive at the conclusion


[053-108]that it is far better to have the country filled with manufacturers


[053-109]than to be employed simply in the raising of raw material.  I think


[053-110]he will come to the conclusion that we had better have skilled


[053-111]labor here, and that it is better to pay for it than not to have


[053-112]it.  I think he will find that it is better for America to be


[053-113]substantially independent of the rest of the world.  I think he


[053-114]will conclude that nothing is more desirable than the development


[053-115]of American brain, and that nothing better can be raised than great


[053-116]and splendid men and women.  I think he will conclude that the


[053-117]cloud coming from the factories, from the great stacks and chimneys,


[053-118]is the cloud on which will be seen, and always seen, the bow of


[053-119]American promise.


[053-120]_Question_.  What have you to say about tariff reform?


[053-121]_Answer_.  I have this to say:  That the tariff is for the most


[053-122]part the result of compromises--that is, one State wishing to have


[053-123]something protected agrees to protect something else in some other


[053-124]State, so that, as a matter of fact, many things are protected that


[053-125]need no protection, and many things are unprotected that ought to


[053-126]be cared for by the Government.


[053-127]I am in favor of a sensible reform of the tariff--that is to say,


[053-128]I do not wish to put it in the power of the few to practice extortion


[053-129]upon the many.  Congress should always be wide awake, and whenever


[053-130]there is any abuse it should be corrected.  At the same time, next


[053-131]to having the tariff just--next in importance is to have it stable.


[053-132]It does us great injury to have every dollar invested in manufactures


[053-133]frightened every time Congress meets.  Capital should feel secure.


[053-134]Insecurity calls for a higher interest, wants to make up for the


[053-135]additional risk, whereas, when a dollar feels absolutely certain


[053-136]that it is well invested, that it is not to be disturbed, it is


[053-137]satisfied with a very low rate of interest.


[053-138]The present agitation--the message of President Cleveland upon


[053-139]these questions--will cost the country many hundred millions of


[053-140]dollars.


[053-141]_Question_.  I see that some one has been charging that Judge


[053-142]Gresham is an Infidel?


[053-143]_Answer_.  I have known Judge Gresham for many years, and of course


[053-144]have heard him talk upon many subjects, but I do not remember ever


[053-145]discussing with him a religious topic.  I only know that he believes


[053-146]in allowing every man to express his opinions, and that he does


[053-147]not hate a man because he differs with him.  I believe that he


[053-148]believes in intellectual hospitality, and that he would give all


[053-149]churches equal rights, and would treat them all with the utmost


[053-150]fairness.  I regard him as a fair-minded, intelligent and honest


[053-151]man, and that is enough for me.  I am satisfied with the way he


[053-152]acts, and care nothing about his particular creed.  I like a manly


[053-153]man, whether he agrees with me or not.  I believe that President


[053-154]Garfield was a minister of the Church of the Disciples--that made


[053-155]no difference to me.  Mr. Blaine is a member of some church in


[053-156]Augusta--I care nothing for that.  Whether Judge Gresham belongs


[053-157]to any church, I do not know.  I never asked him, but I know he


[053-158]does not agree with me by a large majority.


[053-159]In this country, where a divorce has been granted between church


[053-160]and state, the religious opinions of candidates should be let alone.


[053-161]To make the inquiry is a piece of impertinence--a piece of impudence.


[053-162]I have voted for men of all persuasions and expect to keep right


[053-163]on, and if they are not civilized enough to give me the liberty


[053-164]they ask for themselves, why I shall simply set them an example of


[053-165]decency.


[053-166]_Question_.  What do you think of the political outlook?


[053-167]_Answer_.  The people of this country have a great deal of


[053-168]intelligence.  Tariff and free trade and protection and home


[053-169]manufactures and American industries--all these things will be


[053-170]discussed in every schoolhouse of the country, and in thousands


[053-171]and thousands of political meetings, and when next November comes


[053-172]you will see the Democratic party overthrown and swept out of power


[053-173]by a cyclone.  All other questions will be lost sight of.  Even


[053-174]the Prohibitionists would rather drink beer in a prosperous country


[053-175]than burst with cold water and hard times.


[053-176]The preservation of what we have will be the great question.  This


[053-177]is the richest country and the most prosperous country, and I


[053-178]believe that the people have sense enough to continue the policy


[053-179]that has given them those results.  I never want to see the


[053-180]civilization of the Old World, or rather the barbarism of the Old


[053-181]World, gain a footing on this continent.  I am an American.  I


[053-182]believe in American ideas--that is to say, in equal rights, and in


[053-183]the education and civilization of all the people.
