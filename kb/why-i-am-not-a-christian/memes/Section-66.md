[065-001]In my judgment, the "Age of Faith" is passing away.  We are living


[065-002]in a time of demonstration.


[065-003][NOTE:  From an unfinished interview found among Colonel Ingersoll's


[065-004]papers.]


[065-005]PROHIBITION.


[065-006]It has been decided in many courts in various States that the


[065-007]traffic in liquor can be regulated--that it is a police question.


[065-008]It has been decided by the courts in Iowa that its manufacture and


[065-009]sale can be prohibited, and, not only so, but that a distillery or


[065-010]a brewery may be declared a nuisance and may legally be abated,


[065-011]and these decisions have been upheld by the Supreme Court of the


[065-012]United States.  Consequently, it has been settled by the highest


[065-013]tribunal that States have the power either to regulate or to prohibit


[065-014]the sale of intoxicating liquors, and not only so, but that States


[065-015]have the power to destroy breweries and distilleries without making


[065-016]any compensation to owners.


[065-017]So it has always been considered within the power of the State to


[065-018]license the selling of intoxicating liquors.  In other words, this


[065-019]question is one that the States can decide for themselves.  It is


[065-020]not, and it should not be, in my judgment, a Federal question.  It


[065-021]is something with which the United States has nothing to do.  It


[065-022]belongs to the States; and where a majority of the people are in


[065-023]favor of prohibition and pass laws to that effect, there is nothing


[065-024]in the Constitution of the United States that interferes with such


[065-025]action.


[065-026]The remaining question, then, is not a question of power, but a


[065-027]question of policy, and at the threshold of this question is another:


[065-028]Can prohibitory laws be enforced?  There are to-day in Kansas,--a


[065-029]prohibition State--more saloons, that is to say, more places in


[065-030]which liquor is sold, than there are in Georgia, a State without


[065-031]prohibition legislation.  There are more in Nebraska, according to


[065-032]the population, more in Iowa, according to the population, than in


[065-033]many of the States in which there is the old license system.  You


[065-034]will find that the United States has granted more licenses to


[065-035]wholesale and retail dealers in these prohibition States,--according


[065-036]to the population,--than in many others in which prohibition has


[065-037]not been adopted.


[065-038]These facts tend to show that it is not enough for the Legislature


[065-039]to say:  "Be it enacted."  Behind every law there must be an


[065-040]intelligent and powerful public opinion.  A law, to be enforced,


[065-041]must be the expression of such powerful and intelligent opinion;


[065-042]otherwise it becomes a dead letter; it is avoided; judges continue


[065-043]the cases, juries refuse to convict, and witnesses are not particular


[065-044]about telling the truth.  Such laws demoralize the community, or,


[065-045]to put it in another way, demoralized communities pass such laws.


[065-046]_Question_.  What do you think of the prohibitory movement on


[065-047]general principles?


[065-048]_Answer_.  The trouble is that when a few zealous men, intending


[065-049]to reform the world, endeavor to enforce unpopular laws, they are


[065-050]compelled to resort to detectives, to a system of espionage.  For


[065-051]the purpose of preventing the sale of liquors somebody has to watch.


[065-052]Eyes and ears must become acquainted with keyholes.  Every neighbor


[065-053]suspects every other.  A man with a bottle or demijohn is followed.


[065-054]Those who drink get behind doors, in cellars and garrets.  Hypocrisy


[065-055]becomes substantially universal.  Hundreds of people become suddenly


[065-056]afflicted with a variety of diseases, for the cure of which alcohol


[065-057]in some form is supposed to be indispensable.  Malaria becomes general,


[065-058]and it is perfectly astonishing how long a few pieces of Peruvian


[065-059]bark will last, and how often the liquor can be renewed without


[065-060]absorbing the medicinal qualities of the bark.  The State becomes


[065-061]a paradise for patent medicine--the medicine being poor whiskey


[065-062]with a scientific name.


[065-063]Physicians become popular in proportion as liquor of some kind


[065-064]figures in their prescriptions.  Then in the towns clubs are formed,


[065-065]the principal object being to establish a saloon, and in many


[065-066]instances the drug store becomes a favorite resort, especially on


[065-067]Sundays.


[065-068]There is, however, another side to this question.  It is this:


[065-069]Nothing in the world is more important than personal liberty.  Many


[065-070]people are in favor of blotting out the sun to prevent the growth


[065-071]of weeds.  This is the mistake of all prohibitory fanaticism.


[065-072]_Question_.  What is true temperance, Colonel Ingersoll?


[065-073]_Answer_.  Men have used stimulants for many thousand years, and


[065-074]as much is used to-day in various forms as in any other period of


[065-075]the world's history.  They are used with more prudence now than


[065-076]ever before, for the reason that the average man is more intelligent


[065-077]now than ever before.  Intelligence has much to do with temperance.


[065-078]The barbarian rushes to the extreme, for the reason that but little,


[065-079]comparatively, depends upon his personal conduct or personal habits.


[065-080]Now the struggle for life is so sharp, competition is so severe,


[065-081]that few men can succeed who carry a useless burden.  The business


[065-082]men of our country are compelled to lead temperate lives, otherwise


[065-083]their credit is gone.  Men of wealth, men of intelligence, do not


[065-084]wish to employ intemperate physicians.  They are not willing to


[065-085]trust their health or their lives with a physician who is under


[065-086]the influence of liquor.  The same is true of business men in regard


[065-087]to their legal interests.  They insist upon having sober attorneys;


[065-088]they want the counsel of a sober man.  So in every department.  On


[065-089]the railways it is absolutely essential that the engineer, that


[065-090]the conductor, the train dispatcher and every other employee, in


[065-091]whose hands are the lives of men, should be temperate.  The


[065-092]consequence is that under the law of the survival of the fittest,


[065-093]the intemperate are slowly but surely going to the wall; they are


[065-094]slowly but surely being driven out of employments of trust and


[065-095]importance.  As we rise in the scale of civilization we continually


[065-096]demand better and better service.  We are continually insisting


[065-097]upon better habits, upon a higher standard of integrity, of fidelity.


[065-098]These are the causes, in my judgment, that are working together in


[065-099]the direction of true temperance.


[065-100]_Question_.  Do you believe the people can be made to do without


[065-101]a stimulant?


[065-102]_Answer_.  The history of the world shows that all men who have


[065-103]advanced one step beyond utter barbarism have used some kind of


[065-104]stimulant.  Man has sought for it in every direction.  Every savage


[065-105]loves it.  Everything has been tried.  Opium has been used by many


[065-106]hundreds of millions.  Hasheesh has filled countless brains with


[065-107]chaotic dreams, and everywhere that civilization has gone the blood


[065-108]of the grape has been used.  Nothing is easier now to obtain than


[065-109]liquor.  In one bushel of corn there are at least five gallons--


[065-110]four can easily be extracted.  All starch, all sugars, can be


[065-111]changed almost instantly into alcohol.  Every grain that grows has


[065-112]in it the intoxicating principle, and, as a matter of fact, nearly


[065-113]all of the corn, wheat, sugar and starch that man eats is changed


[065-114]into alcohol in his stomach.  Whether man can be compelled to do


[065-115]without a stimulant is a question that I am unable to answer.  Of


[065-116]one thing I am certain:  He has never yet been compelled to do


[065-117]without one.  The tendency, I think, of modern times is toward a


[065-118]milder stimulant than distilled liquors.  Whisky and brandies are


[065-119]too strong; wine and beer occupy the middle ground.  Wine is a


[065-120]fireside, whisky a conflagration.


[065-121]It seems to me that it would be far better if the Prohibitionists


[065-122]would turn their attention toward distilled spirits.  If they were


[065-123]willing to compromise, the probability is that they would have


[065-124]public opinion on their side.  If they would say:  "You may have


[065-125]all the beer and all the wine and cider you wish, and you can drink


[065-126]them when and where you desire, but the sale of distilled spirits


[065-127]shall be prohibited," it is possible that this could be carried


[065-128]out in good faith in many if not in most of the States--possibly


[065-129]in all.  We all know the effect of wine, even when taken in excess,


[065-130]is nothing near as disastrous as the effect of distilled spirits.


[065-131]Why not take the middle ground?  The wine drinkers of the old


[065-132]country are not drunkards.  They have been drinking wine for


[065-133]generations.  It is drunk by men, women and children.  It adds to


[065-134]the sociability of the family.  It does not separate the husband


[065-135]from the rest, it keeps them all together, and in that view is


[065-136]rather a benefit than an injury.  Good wine can be raised as cheaply


[065-137]here as in any part of the world.  In nearly every part of our


[065-138]country the grape grows and good wine can be made.  If our people


[065-139]had a taste for wine they would lose the taste for stronger drink,


[065-140]and they would be disgusted with the surroundings of the stronger


[065-141]drink.


[065-142]The same may be said in favor of beer.  As long as the Prohibitionists


[065-143]make no distinction between wine and whisky, between beer and


[065-144]brandy, just so long they will be regarded by most people as


[065-145]fanatics.


[065-146]The Prohibitionists cannot expect to make this question a Federal


[065-147]one.  The United States has no jurisdiction of this subject.


[065-148]Congress can pass no laws affecting this question that could have


[065-149]any force except in such parts of our country as are not within


[065-150]the jurisdiction of States.  It is a question for the States and


[065-151]not for the Federal Government.  The Prohibitionists are simply


[065-152]throwing away their votes.  Let us suppose that we had a Prohibition


[065-153]Congress and a Prohibition President--what steps could be taken to


[065-154]do away with drinking in the city of New York?  What steps could


[065-155]be taken in any State of this Union?  What could by any possibility


[065-156]be done?


[065-157]A few years ago the Prohibitionists demanded above all things that


[065-158]the tax be taken from distilled spirits, claiming at that time that


[065-159]such a tax made the Government a partner in vice.


[065-160]Now when the Republican party proposes under certain circumstances


[065-161]to remove that tax, the Prohibitionists denounce the movement as


[065-162]one in favor of intemperance.  We have also been told that the tax


[065-163]on whisky should be kept for the reason that it increases the price,


[065-164]and that an increased price tends to make a temperate people; that


[065-165]if the tax is taken off, the price will fall and the whole country


[065-166]start on the downward road to destruction.  Is it possible that


[065-167]human nature stands on such slippery ground?  It is possible that


[065-168]our civilization to-day rests upon the price of alcohol, and that,


[065-169]should the price be reduced, we would all go down together?  For


[065-170]one, I cannot entertain such a humiliating and disgraceful view of


[065-171]human nature.  I believe that man is destined to grow greater,


[065-172]grander and nobler.  I believe that no matter what the cost of


[065-173]alcohol may be, life will grow too valuable to be thrown away.


[065-174]Men hold life according to its value.  Men, as a rule, only throw


[065-175]away their lives when they are not worth keeping.  When life becomes


[065-176]worth living it will be carefully preserved and will be hoarded to


[065-177]the last grain of sand that falls through the glass of time.


[065-178]_Question_.  What is the reason for so much intemperance?


[065-179]_Answer_.  When many people are failures, when they are distanced


[065-180]in the race, when they fall behind, when they give up, when they


[065-181]lose ambition, when they finally become convinced that they are


[065-182]worthless, precisely as they are in danger of becoming dishonest.


[065-183]In other words, having failed in the race of life on the highway,


[065-184]they endeavor to reach to goal by going across lots, by crawling


[065-185]through the grass.  Disguise this matter as we may, all people are


[065-186]not successes, all people have not the brain or the muscle or the


[065-187]moral stamina necessary to succeed.  Some fall in one way, some in


[065-188]another; some in the net of strong drink, some in the web of


[065-189]circumstances and others in a thousand ways, and the world itself


[065-190]cannot grow better unless the unworthy fail.  The law is the survival


[065-191]of the fittest, that is to say, the destruction of the unfit.


[065-192]There is no scheme of morals, no scheme of government, no scheme


[065-193]of charity, that can reverse this law.  If it could be reversed,


[065-194]then the result would be the survival of the unfittest, the speedy


[065-195]end of which would be the extinction of the human race.
