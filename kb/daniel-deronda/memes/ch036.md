[036-001]Seeing the group they made this evening, one could hardly wish them to

[036-002]change their way of life. They were all alike small, and so in due

[036-003]proportion to their miniature rooms. Mrs. Meyrick was reading aloud

[036-004]from a French book; she was a lively little woman, half French, half

[036-005]Scotch, with a pretty articulateness of speech that seemed to make

[036-006]daylight in her hearer’s understanding. Though she was not yet fifty,

[036-007]her rippling hair, covered by a quakerish net cap, was chiefly gray,

[036-008]but her eyebrows were brown as the bright eyes below them; her black

[036-009]dress, almost like a priest’s cassock with its rows of buttons, suited

[036-010]a neat figure hardly five feet high. The daughters were to match the

[036-011]mother, except that Mab had [[Hans-Meyrick|Hans]]’ light hair and complexion, with a

[036-012]bossy, irregular brow, and other quaintnesses that reminded one of him.

[036-013]Everything about them was compact, from the firm coils of their hair,

[036-014]fastened back _à la Chinoise_, to their gray skirts in Puritan

[036-015]nonconformity with the fashion, which at that time would have demanded

[036-016]that four feminine circumferences should fill all the free space in the

[036-017]front parlor. All four, if they had been wax-work, might have been

[036-018]packed easily in a fashionable lady’s traveling trunk. Their faces

[036-019]seemed full of speech, as if their minds had been shelled, after the

[036-020]manner of horse-chestnuts, and become brightly visible. The only large

[036-021]thing of its kind in the room was Hafiz, the Persian cat, comfortably

[036-022]poised on the brown leather back of a chair, and opening his large eyes

[036-023]now and then to see that the lower animals were not in any mischief.

[036-024]The book Mrs. Meyrick had before her was Erckmann-Chatrian’s _Historie

[036-025]d’un Conscrit_. She had just finished reading it aloud, and Mab, who

[036-026]had let her work fall on the ground while she stretched her head

[036-027]forward and fixed her eyes on the reader, exclaimed,

[036-028]“I think that is the finest story in the world.”

[036-029]“Of course, Mab!” said Amy, “it is the last you have heard. Everything

[036-030]that pleases you is the best in its turn.”

[036-031]“It is hardly to be called a story,” said Kate. “It is a bit of history

[036-032]brought near us with a strong telescope. We can see the soldiers’

[036-033]faces: no, it is more than that—we can hear everything—we can almost

[036-034]hear their hearts beat.”

[036-035]“I don’t care what you call it,” said Mab, flirting away her thimble.

[036-036]“Call it a chapter in Revelations. It makes me want to do something

[036-037]good, something grand. It makes me so sorry for everybody. It makes me

[036-038]like Schiller—I want to take the world in my arms and kiss it. I must

[036-039]kiss you instead, little mother!” She threw her arms round her mother’s

[036-040]neck.

[036-041]“Whenever you are in that mood, Mab, down goes your work,” said Amy.

[036-042]“It would be doing something good to finish your cushion without

[036-043]soiling it.”

[036-044]“Oh—oh—oh!” groaned Mab, as she stooped to pick up her work and

[036-045]thimble. “I wish I had three wounded conscripts to take care of.”

[036-046]“You would spill their beef tea while you were talking,” said Amy.

[036-047]“Poor Mab! don’t be hard on her,” said the mother. “Give me the

[036-048]embroidery now, child. You go on with your enthusiasm, and I will go on

[036-049]with the pink and white poppy.”

[036-050]“Well, ma, I think you are more caustic than Amy,” said Kate, while she

[036-051]drew her head back to look at her drawing.

[036-052]“Oh—oh—oh!” cried Mab again, rising and stretching her arms. “I wish

[036-053]something wonderful would happen. I feel like the deluge. The waters of

[036-054]the great deep are broken up, and the windows of heaven are opened. I

[036-055]must sit down and play the scales.”

[036-056]Mab was opening the piano while the others were laughing at this

[036-057]climax, when a cab stopped before the house, and there forthwith came a

[036-058]quick rap of the knocker.

[036-059]“Dear me!” said Mrs. Meyrick, starting up, “it is after ten, and Phœbe

[036-060]is gone to bed.” She hastened out, leaving the parlor door open.

[036-061]“[[Daniel-Deronda|Mr. Deronda]]!” The girls could hear this exclamation from their mamma.

[036-062]Mab clasped her hands, saying in a loud whisper, “There now! something

[036-063]_is_ going to happen.” Kate and Amy gave up their work in amazement.

[036-064]But Deronda’s tone in reply was so low that they could not hear his

[036-065]words, and Mrs. Meyrick immediately closed the parlor door.

[036-066]“I know I am trusting to your goodness in a most extraordinary way,”

[036-067]Deronda went on, after giving his brief narrative; “but you can imagine

[036-068]how helpless I feel with a young creature like this on my hands. I

[036-069]could not go with her among strangers, and in her nervous state I

[036-070]should dread taking her into a house full of servants. I have trusted

[036-071]to your mercy. I hope you will not think my act unwarrantable.”

[036-072]“On the contrary. You have honored me by trusting me. I see your

[036-073]difficulty. Pray bring her in. I will go and prepare the girls.”

[036-074]While Deronda went back to the cab, Mrs. Meyrick turned into the parlor

[036-075]again and said: “Here is somebody to take care of instead of your

[036-076]wounded conscripts, Mab: a poor girl who was going to drown herself in

[036-077]despair. Mr. Deronda found her only just in time to save her. He

[036-078]brought her along in his boat, and did not know what else it would be

[036-079]safe to do with her, so he has trusted us and brought her here. It

[036-080]seems she is a Jewess, but quite refined, he says—knowing Italian and

[036-081][[art|music]].”

[036-082]The three girls, wondering and expectant, came forward and stood near

[036-083]each other in mute confidence that they were all feeling alike under

[036-084]this appeal to their [[sympathy|compassion]]. Mab looked rather awe-stricken, as if

[036-085]this answer to her wish were something preternatural.

[036-086]Meanwhile Deronda going to the door of the cab where the pale face was

[036-087]now gazing out with roused observation, said, “I have brought you to

[036-088]some of the kindest people in the world: there are daughters like you.

[036-089]It is a happy home. Will you let me take you to them?”

[036-090]She stepped out obediently, putting her hand in his and forgetting her

[036-091]hat; and when Deronda led her into the full light of the parlor where

[036-092]the four little women stood awaiting her, she made a picture that would

[036-093]have stirred much duller sensibilities than theirs. At first she was a

[036-094]little dazed by the sudden light, and before she had concentrated her

[036-095]glance he had put her hand into the mother’s. He was inwardly rejoicing

[036-096]that the Meyricks were so small: the dark-curled head was the highest

[036-097]among them. The poor wanderer could not be afraid of these gentle faces

[036-098]so near hers: and now she was looking at each of them in turn while the

[036-099]mother said, “You must be weary, poor child.”

[036-100]“We will take care of you—we will comfort you—we will love you,”

[036-101]cried Mab, no longer able to restrain herself, and taking the small

[036-102]right hand caressingly between both her own. This gentle welcoming

[036-103]warmth was penetrating the bewildered one: she hung back just enough to

[036-104]see better the four faces in front of her, whose good will was being

[036-105]reflected in hers, not in any smile, but in that undefinable change

[036-106]which tells us that anxiety is passing in contentment. For an instant

[036-107]she looked up at Deronda, as if she were referring all this mercy to

[036-108]him, and then again turning to Mrs. Meyrick, said with more

[036-109]collectedness in her sweet tones than he had heard before,

[036-110]“I am a stranger. I am a Jewess. You might have thought I was wicked.”

[036-111]“No, we are sure you are good,” burst out Mab.

[036-112]“We think no evil of you, poor child. You shall be safe with us,” said

[036-113]Mrs. Meyrick. “Come now and sit down. You must have some food, and then

[036-114]you must go to rest.”

[036-115]The stranger looked up again at Deronda, who said,

[036-116]“You will have no more fears with these friends? You will rest

[036-117]to-night?”

[036-118]“Oh, I should not fear. I should rest. I think these are the

[036-119]ministering angels.”

[036-120]Mrs. Meyrick wanted to lead her to seat, but again hanging back gently,

[036-121]the poor weary thing spoke as if with a scruple at being received

[036-122]without a further account of herself.

[036-123]“My name is [[Mirah-Lapidoth|Mirah Lapidoth]]. I am come a long way, all the way from

[036-124]Prague by myself. I made my escape. I ran away from dreadful things. I

[036-125]came to find my mother and brother in [[london|London]]. I had been taken from my

[036-126]mother when I was little, but I thought I could find her again. I had

[036-127]trouble—the houses were all gone—I could not find her. It has been a

[036-128]long while, and I had not much money. That is why I am in distress.”

[036-129]“Our mother will be good to you,” cried Mab. “See what a nice little

[036-130]mother she is!”

[036-131]“Do sit down now,” said Kate, moving a chair forward, while Amy ran to

[036-132]get some tea.

[036-133]Mirah resisted no longer, but seated herself with perfect grace,

[036-134]crossing her little feet, laying her hands one over the other on her

[036-135]lap, and looking at her friends with placid reverence; whereupon Hafiz,

[036-136]who had been watching the scene restlessly came forward with tail erect

[036-137]and rubbed himself against her ankles. Deronda felt it time to go.

[036-138]“Will you allow me to come again and inquire—perhaps at five

[036-139]to-morrow?” he said to Mrs. Meyrick.

[036-140]“Yes, pray; we shall have had time to make acquaintance then.”

[036-141]“Good-bye,” said Deronda, looking down at Mirah, and putting out his

[036-142]hand. She rose as she took it, and the moment brought back to them both

[036-143]strongly the other moment when she had first taken that outstretched

[036-144]hand. She lifted her eyes to his and said with reverential fervor, “The

[036-145]God of our fathers bless you and deliver you from all evil as you have

[036-146]delivered me. I did not believe there was any man so good. None before

[036-147]have thought me worthy of the best. You found me poor and miserable,

[036-148]yet you have given me the best.”

[036-149]Deronda could not speak, but with silent adieux to the Meyricks,

[036-150]hurried away.

[036-151]BOOK III.—MAIDENS CHOOSING.

[036-152]CHAPTER XIX.

[036-153]“I pity the man who can travel from Dan to Beersheba, and say, ‘’Tis

[036-154]all barren’: and so it is: and so is all the world to him who will not

[036-155]cultivate the fruits it offers.”—STERNE: _Sentimental Journey_.

[036-156]To say that Deronda was romantic would be to misrepresent him; but

[036-157]under his calm and somewhat self-repressed exterior there was a fervor

[036-158]which made him easily find poetry and romance among the events of

[036-159]everyday life. And perhaps poetry and romance are as plentiful as ever

[036-160]in the world except for those phlegmatic natures who I suspect would in

[036-161]any age have regarded them as a dull form of erroneous thinking. They

[036-162]exist very easily in the same room with the microscope and even in

[036-163]railway carriages: what banishes them in the vacuum in gentlemen and

[036-164]lady passengers. How should all the apparatus of heaven and earth, from

[036-165]the farthest firmament to the tender bosom of the mother who nourished

[036-166]us, make poetry for a mind that had no movements of awe and tenderness,

[036-167]no sense of fellowship which thrills from the near to the distant, and

[036-168]back again from the distant to the near?

[036-169]To Deronda this event of finding Mirah was as heart-stirring as

[036-170]anything that befell Orestes or Rinaldo. He sat up half the night,

[036-171]living again through the moments since he had first discerned Mirah on

[036-172]the river-brink, with the fresh and fresh vividness which belongs to

[036-173]emotive memory. When he took up a book to try and dull this urgency of

[036-174]inward vision, the printed words were no more than a network through

[036-175]which he saw and heard everything as clearly as before—saw not only

[036-176]the actual events of two hours, but possibilities of what had been and

[036-177]what might be which those events were enough to feed with the warm

[036-178]blood of passionate hope and fear. Something in his own experience

[036-179]caused Mirah’s search after her mother to lay hold with peculiar force

[036-180]on his imagination. The first prompting of sympathy was to aid her in

[036-181]her search: if given persons were extant in London there were ways of

[036-182]finding them, as subtle as scientific experiment, the right machinery

[036-183]being set at work. But here the mixed feelings which belonged to

[036-184]Deronda’s kindred experience naturally transfused themselves into his

[036-185]anxiety on behalf of Mirah.
