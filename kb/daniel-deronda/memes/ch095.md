[095-001]“I don’t hold with you there, Miller,” said Goodwin, the inlayer, more

[095-002]concerned to carry on the subject than to wait for a word from the new

[095-003]guest. “For either you mean so many sorts of things by ideas that I get

[095-004]no knowledge by what you say, any more than if you said light was a

[095-005]cause; or else you mean a particular sort of ideas, and then I go

[095-006]against your meaning as too narrow. For, look at it in one way, all

[095-007]actions men put a bit of thought into are ideas—say, sowing seed, or

[095-008]making a canoe, or baking clay; and such ideas as these work themselves

[095-009]into life and go on growing with it, but they can’t go apart from the

[095-010]material that set them to work and makes a medium for them. It’s the

[095-011]nature of wood and stone yielding to the knife that raises the idea of

[095-012]shaping them, and with plenty of wood and stone the shaping will go on.

[095-013]I look at it, that such ideas as are mixed straight away with all the

[095-014]other elements of life are powerful along with ’em. The slower the

[095-015]mixing, the less power they have. And as to the causes of social

[095-016]change, I look at it in this way—ideas are a sort of parliament, but

[095-017]there’s a commonwealth outside and a good deal of the commonwealth is

[095-018]working at change without knowing what the parliament is doing.”

[095-019]“But if you take ready mixing as your test of power,” said Pash, “some

[095-020]of the least practical ideas beat everything. They spread without being

[095-021]understood, and enter into the language without being thought of.”

[095-022]“They may act by changing the distribution of gases,” said Marrables;

[095-023]“instruments are getting so fine now, men may come to register the

[095-024]spread of a theory by observed changes in the atmosphere and

[095-025]corresponding changes in the nerves.”

[095-026]“Yes,” said Pash, his dark face lighting up rather impishly, “there is

[095-027]the idea of nationalities; I dare say the wild asses are snuffing it,

[095-028]and getting more gregarious.”

[095-029]“You don’t share that idea?” said [[Daniel-Deronda|Deronda]], finding a piquant

[095-030]incongruity between Pash’s sarcasm and the strong stamp of race on his

[095-031]features.

[095-032]“Say, rather, he does not share that spirit,” said [[Mordecai]], who had

[095-033]turned a melancholy glance on Pash. “Unless nationality is a feeling,

[095-034]what force can it have as an idea?”

[095-035]“Granted, Mordecai,” said Pash, quite good-humoredly. “And as the

[095-036]feeling of nationality is dying, I take the idea to be no better than a

[095-037]ghost, already walking to announce the death.”

[095-038]“A sentiment may seem to be dying and yet revive into strong life,”

[095-039]said Deronda. “Nations have revived. We may live to see a great

[095-040]outburst of force in the Arabs, who are being inspired with a new zeal.”

[095-041]“Amen, amen,” said Mordecai, looking at Deronda with a delight which

[095-042]was the beginning of recovered energy: his attitude was more upright,

[095-043]his face was less worn.

[095-044]“That may hold with backward nations,” said Pash, “but with us in

[095-045]Europe the sentiment of nationality is destined to die out. It will

[095-046]last a little longer in the quarters where oppression lasts, but

[095-047]nowhere else. The whole current of progress is setting against it.”

[095-048]“Ay,” said Buchan, in a rapid thin Scotch tone which was like the

[095-049]letting in of a little cool air on the conversation, “ye’ve done well

[095-050]to bring us round to the point. Ye’re all agreed that societies

[095-051]change—not always and everywhere—but on the whole and in the long

[095-052]run. Now, with all deference, I would beg t’ observe that we have got

[095-053]to examine the nature of changes before we have a warrant to call them

[095-054]progress, which word is supposed to include a bettering, though I

[095-055]apprehend it to be ill-chosen for that purpose, since mere motion

[095-056]onward may carry us to a bog or a precipice. And the questions I would

[095-057]put are three: Is all change in the direction of progress? if not, how

[095-058]shall we discern which change is progress and which not? and thirdly,

[095-059]how far and in what way can we act upon the course of change so as to

[095-060]promote it where it is beneficial, and divert it where it is injurious?”

[095-061]But Buchan’s attempt to impose his method on the talk was a failure.

[095-062]Lily immediately said,

[095-063]“Change and progress are merged in the idea of development. The laws of

[095-064]development are being discovered, and changes taking place according to

[095-065]them are necessarily progressive; that is to say, it we have any notion

[095-066]of progress or improvement opposed to them, the notion is a mistake.”

[095-067]“I really can’t see how you arrive at that sort of certitude about

[095-068]changes by calling them development,” said Deronda. “There will still

[095-069]remain the degrees of inevitableness in relation to our own will and

[095-070]acts, and the degrees of wisdom in hastening or retarding; there will

[095-071]still remain the danger of mistaking a tendency which should be

[095-072]resisted for an inevitable law that we must adjust ourselves to,—which

[095-073]seems to me as bad a superstition or false god as any that has been set

[095-074]up without the ceremonies of philosophizing.”

[095-075]“That is a truth,” said Mordecai. “Woe to the men who see no place for

[095-076]resistance in this generation! I believe in a growth, a passage, and a

[095-077]new unfolding of life whereof the seed is more perfect, more charged

[095-078]with the elements that are pregnant with diviner form. The life of a

[095-079]people grows, it is knit together and yet expanded, in joy and sorrow,

[095-080]in thought and action; it absorbs the thought of other nations into its

[095-081]own forms, and gives back the thought as new wealth to the world; it is

[095-082]a power and an organ in the great body of the nations. But there may

[095-083]come a check, an arrest; memories may be stifled, and love may be faint

[095-084]for the lack of them; or memories may shrink into withered relics—the

[095-085]soul of a people, whereby they know themselves to be one, may seem to

[095-086]be dying for want of common action. But who shall say, ‘The fountain of

[095-087]their life is dried up, they shall forever cease to be a nation?’ Who

[095-088]shall say it? Not he who feels the life of his people stirring within

[095-089]his own. Shall he say, ‘That way events are wending, I will not

[095-090]resist?’ His very soul is resistance, and is as a seed of fire that may

[095-091]enkindle the souls of multitudes, and make a new pathway for events.”

[095-092]“I don’t deny patriotism,” said Gideon, “but we all know you have a

[095-093]particular meaning, Mordecai. You know Mordecai’s way of thinking, I

[095-094]suppose.” Here Gideon had turned to Deronda, who sat next to him, but

[095-095]without waiting for an answer he went on. “I’m a rational Jew myself. I

[095-096]stand by my people as a sort of family relations, and I am for keeping

[095-097]up our worship in a rational way. I don’t approve of our people getting

[095-098]baptised, because I don’t believe in a Jew’s conversion to the Gentile

[095-099]part of [[religion|Christianity]]. And now we have political equality, there’s no

[095-100]excuse for a pretense of that sort. But I am for getting rid of all of

[095-101]our superstitions and exclusiveness. There’s no reason now why we

[095-102]shouldn’t melt gradually into the populations we live among. That’s the

[095-103]order of the day in point of progress. I would as soon my children

[095-104]married Christians as Jews. And I’m for the old maxim, ‘A man’s country

[095-105]is where he’s well off.’”

[095-106]“That country’s not so easy to find, Gideon,” said the rapid Pash, with

[095-107]a shrug and grimace. “You get ten shillings a-week more than I do, and

[095-108]have only half the number of children. If somebody will introduce a

[095-109]brisk trade in watches among the ‘Jerusalem wares,’ I’ll go—eh,

[095-110]Mordecai, what do you say?”

[095-111]Deronda, all ear for these hints of Mordecai’s opinion, was inwardly

[095-112]wondering at his persistence in coming to this club. For an

[095-113]enthusiastic spirit to meet continually the fixed indifference of men

[095-114]familiar with the object of his enthusiasm is the acceptance of a slow

[095-115]martyrdom, beside which the fate of a missionary tomahawked without any

[095-116]considerate rejection of his doctrines seems hardly worthy of

[095-117][[sympathy|compassion]]. But Mordecai gave no sign of shrinking: this was a moment

[095-118]of spiritual fullness, and he cared more for the utterance of his faith

[095-119]than for its immediate reception. With a fervor which had no temper in

[095-120]it, but seemed rather the rush of feeling in the opportunity of speech,

[095-121]he answered Pash:,

[095-122]“What I say is, let every man keep far away from the brotherhood and

[095-123]inheritance he despises. Thousands on thousands of our race have mixed

[095-124]with the Gentiles as Celt with Saxon, and they may inherit the blessing

[095-125]that belongs to the Gentile. You cannot follow them. You are one of the

[095-126]multitudes over this globe who must walk among the nations and be known

[095-127]as Jews, and with words on their lips which mean, ‘I wish I had not

[095-128]been born a Jew, I disown any bond with the long travail of my race, I

[095-129]will outdo the Gentile in mocking at our separateness,’ they all the

[095-130]while feel breathing on them the breath of contempt because they are

[095-131]Jews, and they will breathe it back poisonously. Can a fresh-made

[095-132]garment of citizenship weave itself straightway into the flesh and

[095-133]change the slow deposit of eighteen centuries? What is the citizenship

[095-134]of him who walks among a people he has no hardy kindred and fellowship

[095-135]with, and has lost the sense of brotherhood with his own race? It is a

[095-136]charter of selfish ambition and rivalry in low greed. He is an alien of

[095-137]spirit, whatever he may be in form; he sucks the blood of mankind, he

[095-138]is not a man, sharing in no loves, sharing in no subjection of the

[095-139]soul, he mocks it all. Is it not truth I speak, Pash?”

[095-140]“Not exactly, Mordecai,” said Pash, “if you mean that I think the worse

[095-141]of myself for being a Jew. What I thank our fathers for is that there

[095-142]are fewer blockheads among us than among other races. But perhaps you

[095-143]are right in thinking the Christians don’t like me so well for it.”

[095-144]“Catholics and Protestants have not liked each other much better,” said

[095-145]the genial Gideon. “We must wait patiently for prejudices to die out.

[095-146]Many of our people are on a footing with the best, and there’s been a

[095-147]good filtering of our blood into high families. I am for making our

[095-148]expectations rational.”

[095-149]“And so am I!” said Mordecai, quickly, leaning forward with the

[095-150]eagerness of one who pleads in some decisive crisis, his long, thin

[095-151]hands clasped together on his lap. “I, too, claim to be a rational Jew.

[095-152]But what is it to be rational—what is it to feel the light of the

[095-153]divine reason growing stronger within and without? It is to see more

[095-154]and more of the hidden bonds that bind and consecrate change as a

[095-155]dependent growth—yea, consecrate it with kinship: the past becomes my

[095-156]parent and the future stretches toward me the appealing arms of

[095-157]children. Is it rational to drain away the sap of special kindred that

[095-158]makes the families of men rich in interchanged wealth, and various as

[095-159]the forests are various with the glory of the cedar and the palm? When

[095-160]it is rational to say, ‘I know not my father or my mother, let my

[095-161]children be aliens to me, that no prayer of mine may touch them,’ then

[095-162]it will be rational for the Jew to say, ‘I will seek to know no

[095-163]difference between me and the Gentile, I will not cherish the prophetic

[095-164]consciousness of our nationality—let the Hebrew cease to be, and let

[095-165]all his memorials be antiquarian trifles, dead as the wall-paintings of

[095-166]a conjectured race. Yet let his child learn by rote the speech of the

[095-167]Greek, where he abjures his fellow-citizens by the bravery of those who

[095-168]fought foremost at Marathon—let him learn to say that was noble in the

[095-169]Greek, that is the spirit of an immortal nation! But the Jew has no

[095-170]memories that bind him to action; let him laugh that his nation is

[095-171]degraded from a nation; let him hold the monuments of his law which

[095-172]carried within its frame the breath of social justice, of charity, and

[095-173]of household sanctities—let him hold the energy of the prophets, the

[095-174]patient care of the Masters, the fortitude of martyred generations, as

[095-175]mere stuff for a professorship. The business of the Jew in all things

[095-176]is to be even as the rich Gentile.’”
