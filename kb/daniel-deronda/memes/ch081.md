[081-001]She did not turn away her glance or let her eyelids fall, but a change

[081-002]came over her face—that subtle change in nerve and muscle which will

[081-003]sometimes give a childlike expression even to the elderly: it is the

[081-004]subsidence of self-assertion.

[081-005]“Shall I lead you back?” said [[Daniel-Deronda|Deronda]], gently, turning and offering her

[081-006]his arm again. She took it silently, and in that way they came in sight

[081-007]of [[Henleigh-Grandcourt|Grandcourt]], who was walking slowly near their former place.

[081-008][[Gwendolen-Harleth|Gwendolen]] went up to him and said, “I am ready to go now. Mr. Deronda

[081-009]will excuse us to Lady Mallinger.”

[081-010]“Certainly,” said Deronda. “Lord and Lady Pentreath disappeared some

[081-011]time ago.”

[081-012]Grandcourt gave his arm in silent compliance, nodding over his shoulder

[081-013]to Deronda, and Gwendolen too only half turned to bow and say,

[081-014]“Thanks.” The husband and wife left the gallery and paced the corridors

[081-015]in silence. When the door had closed on them in the boudoir, Grandcourt

[081-016]threw himself into a chair and said, with undertoned peremptoriness,

[081-017]“Sit down.” She, already in the expectation of something unpleasant,

[081-018]had thrown off her burnous with nervous unconsciousness, and

[081-019]immediately obeyed. Turning his eyes toward her, he began,

[081-020]“Oblige me in future by not showing whims like a mad woman in a play.”

[081-021]“What do you mean?” said Gwendolen.

[081-022]“I suppose there is some understanding between you and Deronda about

[081-023]that thing you have on your wrist. If you have anything to say to him,

[081-024]say it. But don’t carry on a telegraphing which other people are

[081-025]supposed not to see. It’s damnably vulgar.”

[081-026]“You can know all about the necklace,” said Gwendolen, her angry pride

[081-027]resisting the nightmare of fear.

[081-028]“I don’t want to know. Keep to yourself whatever you like.” Grandcourt

[081-029]paused between each sentence, and in each his speech seemed to become

[081-030]more preternaturally distinct in its inward tones. “What I care to know

[081-031]I shall know without your telling me. Only you will please to behave as

[081-032]becomes my wife. And not make a spectacle of yourself.”

[081-033]“Do you object to my talking to Mr. Deronda?”

[081-034]“I don’t care two straws about Deronda, or any other conceited

[081-035]hanger-on. You may talk to him as much as you like. He is not going to

[081-036]take my place. You are my wife. And you will either fill your place

[081-037]properly—to the world and to me—or you will go to the devil.”

[081-038]“I never intended anything but to fill my place properly,” said

[081-039]Gwendolen, with bitterest [[shame|mortification]] in her soul.

[081-040]“You put that thing on your wrist, and hid it from me till you wanted

[081-041]him to see it. Only fools go into that deaf and dumb talk, and think

[081-042]they’re secret. You will understand that you are not to compromise

[081-043]yourself. Behave with dignity. That’s all I have to say.”

[081-044]With that last word Grandcourt rose, turned his back to the fire and

[081-045]looked down on her. She was mute. There was no reproach that she dared

[081-046]to fling back at him in return for these insulting admonitions, and the

[081-047]very reason she felt them to be insulting was that their purport went

[081-048]with the most absolute dictate of her pride. What she would least like

[081-049]to incur was the making a fool of herself and being compromised. It was

[081-050]futile and irrelevant to try and explain that Deronda too had only been

[081-051]a monitor—the strongest of all monitors. Grandcourt was contemptuous,

[081-052]not jealous; contemptuously certain of all the subjection he cared for.

[081-053]Why could she not rebel and defy him? She longed to do it. But she

[081-054]might as well have tried to defy the texture of her nerves and the

[081-055]palpitation of her heart. Her husband had a ghostly army at his back,

[081-056]that could close round her wherever she might turn. She sat in her

[081-057]splendid attire, like a white image of helplessness, and he seemed to

[081-058]gratify himself with looking at her. She could not even make a

[081-059]passionate exclamation, or throw up her arms, as she would have done in

[081-060]her maiden days. The sense of his scorn kept her still.

[081-061]“Shall I ring?” he said, after what seemed to her a long while. She

[081-062]moved her head in assent, and after ringing he went to his

[081-063]dressing-room.

[081-064]Certain words were gnawing within her. “The wrong you have done me will

[081-065]be your own curse.” As he closed the door, the bitter tears rose, and

[081-066]the gnawing words provoked an answer: “Why did you put your fangs into

[081-067]me and not into him?” It was uttered in a whisper, as the tears came up

[081-068]silently. But she immediately pressed her handkerchief against her

[081-069]eyes, and checked her tendency to sob.

[081-070]The next day, recovered from the shuddering fit of this evening scene,

[081-071]she determined to use the charter which Grandcourt had scornfully given

[081-072]her, and to talk as much as she liked with Deronda; but no

[081-073]opportunities occurred, and any little devices she could imagine for

[081-074]creating them were rejected by her pride, which was now doubly active.

[081-075]Not toward Deronda himself—she was singularly free from alarm lest he

[081-076]should think her openness wanting in dignity: it was part of his power

[081-077]over her that she believed him free from all misunderstanding as to the

[081-078]way in which she appealed to him; or rather, that he should

[081-079]misunderstand her had never entered into her mind. But the last morning

[081-080]came, and still she had never been able to take up the dropped thread

[081-081]of their talk, and she was without devices. She and Grandcourt were to

[081-082]leave at three o’clock. It was too irritating that after a walk in the

[081-083]grounds had been planned in Deronda’s hearing, he did not present

[081-084]himself to join in it. Grandcourt was gone with [[Sir-Hugo-Mallinger|Sir Hugo]] to King’s

[081-085]Topping, to see the old manor-house; others of the gentlemen were

[081-086]shooting; she was condemned to go and see the decoy and the water-fowl,

[081-087]and everything else that she least wanted to see, with the ladies, with

[081-088]old Lord Pentreath and his anecdotes, with Mr. Vandernoodt and his

[081-089]admiring manners. The irritation became too strong for her; without

[081-090]premeditation, she took advantage of the winding road to linger a

[081-091]little out of sight, and then set off back to the house, almost running

[081-092]when she was safe from observation. She entered by a side door, and the

[081-093]library was on her left hand; Deronda, she knew, was often there; why

[081-094]might she not turn in there as well as into any other room in the

[081-095]house? She had been taken there expressly to see the illuminated family

[081-096]tree, and other remarkable things—what more natural than that she

[081-097]should like to look in again? The thing most to be feared was that the

[081-098]room would be empty of Deronda, for the door was ajar. She pushed it

[081-099]gently, and looked round it. He was there, writing busily at a distant

[081-100]table, with his back toward the door (in fact, Sir Hugo had asked him

[081-101]to answer some constituents’ letters which had become pressing). An

[081-102]enormous log fire, with the scent of Russia from the books, made the

[081-103]great room as warmly odorous as a private chapel in which the censers

[081-104]have been swinging. It seemed too daring to go in—too rude to speak

[081-105]and interrupt him; yet she went in on the noiseless carpet, and stood

[081-106]still for two or three minutes, till Deronda, having finished a letter,

[081-107]pushed it aside for signature, and threw himself back to consider

[081-108]whether there were anything else for him to do, or whether he could

[081-109]walk out for the chance of meeting the party which included Gwendolen,

[081-110]when he heard her voice saying, “Mr. Deronda.”

[081-111]It was certainly startling. He rose hastily, turned round, and pushed

[081-112]away his chair with a strong expression of surprise.

[081-113]“Am I wrong to come in?” said Gwendolen.

[081-114]“I thought you were far on your walk,” said Deronda.

[081-115]“I turned back,” said Gwendolen.

[081-116]“Do you intend to go out again? I could join you now, if you would

[081-117]allow me.”

[081-118]“No; I want to say something, and I can’t stay long,” said Gwendolen,

[081-119]speaking quickly in a subdued tone, while she walked forward and rested

[081-120]her arms and muff on the back of the chair he had pushed away from him.

[081-121]“I want to tell you that it is really so—I can’t help feeling [[conscience|remorse]]

[081-122]for having injured others. That was what I meant when I said that I had

[081-123]done worse than gamble again and pawn the necklace again—something

[081-124]more injurious, as you called it. And I can’t alter it. I am punished,

[081-125]but I can’t alter it. You said I could do many things. Tell me again.

[081-126]What should you do—what should you feel if you were in my place?”

[081-127]The hurried directness with which she spoke—the absence of all her

[081-128]little airs, as if she were only concerned to use the time in getting

[081-129]an answer that would guide her, made her appeal unspeakably touching.

[081-130]Deronda said, “I should feel something of what you feel—deep sorrow.”

[081-131]“But what would you try to do?” said Gwendolen, with urgent quickness.

[081-132]“Order my life so as to make any possible amends, and keep away from

[081-133]doing any sort of injury again,” said Deronda, catching her sense that

[081-134]the time for speech was brief.

[081-135]“But I can’t—I can’t; I must go on,” said Gwendolen, in a passionate

[081-136]loud whisper. “I have thrust out others—I have made my gain out of

[081-137]their loss—tried to make it—tried. And I must go on. I can’t alter

[081-138]it.”

[081-139]It was impossible to answer this instantaneously. Her words had

[081-140]confirmed his conjecture, and the situation of all concerned rose in

[081-141]swift images before him. His feeling for those who had been thrust out

[081-142]sanctioned her [[guilt|remorse]]; he could not try to nullify it, yet his heart

[081-143]was full of pity for her. But as soon as he could he answered—taking

[081-144]up her last words,

[081-145]“That is the bitterest of all—to wear the yoke of our own wrong-doing.

[081-146]But if you submitted to that as men submit to maiming or life-long

[081-147]incurable disease?—and made the unalterable wrong a reason for more

[081-148]effort toward a good, that may do something to counterbalance the evil?

[081-149]One who has committed irremediable errors may be scourged by that

[081-150]consciousness into a higher course than is common. There are many

[081-151]examples. Feeling what it is to have spoiled one life may well make us

[081-152]long to save other lives from being spoiled.”

[081-153]“But you have not wronged any one, or spoiled their lives,” said

[081-154]Gwendolen, hastily. “It is only others who have wronged _you_.”

[081-155]Deronda colored slightly, but said immediately—“I suppose our keen

[081-156]feeling for ourselves might end in giving us a keen feeling for others,

[081-157]if, when we are suffering acutely, we were to consider that others go

[081-158]through the same sharp experience. That is a sort of remorse before

[081-159]commission. Can’t you understand that?”

[081-160]“I think I do—now,” said Gwendolen. “But you were right—I _am_

[081-161]selfish. I have never thought much of any one’s feelings, except my

[081-162]mother’s. I have not been fond of people. But what can I do?” she went

[081-163]on, more quickly. “I must get up in the morning and do what every one

[081-164]else does. It is all like a dance set beforehand. I seem to see all

[081-165]that can be—and I am tired and sick of it. And the world is all

[081-166]confusion to me”—she made a gesture of disgust. “You say I am

[081-167]ignorant. But what is the good of trying to know more, unless life were

[081-168]worth more?”

[081-169]“This good,” said Deronda promptly, with a touch of indignant severity,

[081-170]which he was inclined to encourage as his own safeguard; “life _would_

[081-171]be worth more to you: some real knowledge would give you an interest in

[081-172]the world beyond the small drama of personal desires. It is the curse

[081-173]of your life—forgive me—of so many lives, that all passion is spent

[081-174]in that narrow round, for want of ideas and sympathies to make a larger

[081-175]home for it. Is there any single occupation of mind that you care about

[081-176]with passionate delight or even independent interest?”
