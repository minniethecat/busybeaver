# Suicide

> "I fancied myself putting it under my pillow. But I never did." — [[Gwendolen-Harleth|Gwendolen Harleth]]

The motif of suicide runs through George Eliot's *Daniel Deronda* (1876) as a persistent dark thread, surfacing in the thoughts and near-actions of multiple characters. Far from being merely sensational, Eliot uses the threat of self-destruction to explore the psychological limits of guilt, despair, and social entrapment.

## Gwendolen's Confession

The most sustained treatment of suicidal ideation comes in Gwendolen's confession to [[Daniel-Deronda|Deronda]] in the climactic scene at [[Genoa]] (Chapter 54). She reveals that after learning the truth about [[Lydia-Glasher|Mrs. Glasher]], she kept a small sharp dagger — "long like a willow leaf in a silver sheath" — locked in her dressing case[^1].

Gwendolen's relationship with the dagger is complex and psychologically precise. She never uses it; she never even looks at it again after locking it away. Instead, it haunts her imagination, a constant possibility she simultaneously dreads and is drawn to. When she finally drops the key into the sea from the yacht, she frames it as an attempt at deliverance — yet immediately begins thinking of how she might open the drawer without the key[^2].

## The Boat Scene: Murderous Wishes and the Death Wish

The most psychologically complex passage in the novel concerns the moments before [[Henleigh-Grandcourt|Grandcourt]]'s drowning. Gwendolen confesses that she "did kill him in my thoughts" — that during the long, silent sail, her hatred crystallized into murderous fantasy. When the gust of wind strikes Grandcourt and he goes overboard, Gwendolen experiences the horror of seeing her wish realized outside her mind[^3].

Her subsequent guilt is not for an act she committed (she did not push him; she was frozen) but for having wished it. This is suicide of conscience — the destruction of moral selfhood through the discovery of one's own murderous capacity.

## Mirah's Near-Drowning

[[Mirah-Lapidoth|Mirah]]'s story begins with an attempted suicide. Fleeing her father [[Lapidoth]], who had tried to sell her into a degrading situation, she comes to London and, in despair, attempts to drown herself in the Thames. Deronda rescues her at the novel's opening — a scene that establishes both his role as rescuer and the novel's interest in the psychological extremity of women with no viable escape[^4].

Mirah's survival and subsequent recovery represent the novel's argument that despair can be overcome through community, meaningful work, and spiritual connection — the Jewish world she rediscovers in London saves her as much as Deronda's physical rescue.

## Eliot's Moral Framework

Eliot treats suicide not as sin but as a symptom of social failure. Gwendolen and Mirah are driven to the edge not by innate weakness but by circumstances that offer them no dignified path. The governess's fate, the abusive father, the entrapment of a hateful marriage — these are the true villains.

The novel's prescription is implicit but clear: the cure for despair is purpose. Deronda finds his purpose in [[Judaism]] and the [[zionism|Zionist]] cause; Mirah finds hers in art and community; Gwendolen's redemption, at the novel's end, remains uncertain — but Deronda's final counsel to her insists that she can turn her fear into a safeguard, her guilt into a source of moral seriousness.
