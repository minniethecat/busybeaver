# Ezra Cohen

Ezra Cohen is a Jewish pawnbroker and shopkeeper in the East End of London, whose household provides shelter to [[Mordecai]] and serves as a point of entry for [[Daniel-Deronda]] into the Anglo-Jewish community. His shop, with the name proudly displayed above the window, anchors the Jewish presence in the novel's London scenes.

## The Shop and the Name

The name "Ezra Cohen" above the shop window functions as a motif of Jewish visibility and ordinariness in Eliot's London. Deronda first encounters it with the peculiar fascination that marks his unconscious draw toward Jewish identity:

## Character and Temperament

Cohen is presented as practical, unpoetic, and thoroughly ordinary. Deronda's assessment encapsulates the character:

> this Ezra Cohen to be the most unpoetic Jew h… [070-187]

He is a businessman concerned with the daily operations of his shop, lacking the spiritual grandeur that Mordecai embodies. Eliot's narrator reinforces this with a reflective judgment:

> other form of aristocracy. Ezra Cohen was not clad in the sublime [093-115]

Yet his very ordinariness serves Eliot's purpose: to show that Jewish identity in England encompasses the full range of human types, from the mystical to the mercantile. Cohen's unpoetic nature is not a flaw but a form of authentic representation.

## The Cohen Household

Ezra Cohen presides over a bustling family home that becomes the novel's most detailed domestic portrait of Anglo-Jewish life. Under his roof live his wife, his children — including [[Jacob-Cohen|Jacob]] and [[adelaide-rebekah]] — and the lodger Mordecai:

> had taken up his abode under Ezra Cohen's roof, [086-063]

The household serves as a waypoint in Deronda's journey and as the setting for the novel's culminating domestic scene — the wedding-feast of [[Mirah-Lapidoth|Mirah]] and Deronda [147-176].

## Thematic Significance

Through Ezra Cohen, Eliot explores the tension between commercial assimilation and spiritual distinctiveness in Anglo-Jewish identity. His shop, with its name proudly displayed, represents a form of Jewish visibility that is neither ashamed nor messianic — simply present. He embodies a Jewishness rooted in daily life and economic participation, broadening the novel's portrait beyond the visionary Mordecai and the assimilated [[Daniel-Charisi]].
