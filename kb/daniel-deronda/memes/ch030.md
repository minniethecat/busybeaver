[030-001]“Mr. Fraser, how was it that the popes and cardinals always had so many

[030-002]nephews?”

[030-003]The tutor, an able young Scotchman, who acted as [[Sir-Hugo-Mallinger|Sir Hugo Mallinger]]’s

[030-004]secretary, roused rather unwillingly from his political economy,

[030-005]answered with the clear-cut emphatic chant which makes a truth doubly

[030-006]telling in Scotch utterance,

[030-007]“Their own children were called nephews.”

[030-008]“Why?” said [[Daniel-Deronda|Deronda]].

[030-009]“It was just for the propriety of the thing; because, as you know very

[030-010]well, priests don’t marry, and the children were illegitimate.”

[030-011]Mr. Fraser, thrusting out his lower lip and making his chant of the

[030-012]last word the more emphatic for a little impatience at being

[030-013]interrupted, had already turned his eyes on his book again, while

[030-014]Deronda, as if something had stung him, started up in a sitting

[030-015]attitude with his back to the tutor.

[030-016]He had always called Sir Hugo Mallinger his uncle, and when it once

[030-017]occurred to him to ask about his father and mother, the baronet had

[030-018]answered, “You lost your father and mother when you were quite a little

[030-019]one; that is why I take care of you.” Daniel then straining to discern

[030-020]something in that early twilight, had a dim sense of having been kissed

[030-021]very much, and surrounded by thin, cloudy, scented drapery, till his

[030-022]fingers caught in something hard, which hurt him, and he began to cry.

[030-023]Every other memory he had was of the little world in which he still

[030-024]lived. And at that time he did not mind about learning more, for he was

[030-025]too fond of Sir Hugo to be sorry for the loss of unknown parents. Life

[030-026]was very delightful to the lad, with an uncle who was always indulgent

[030-027]and cheerful—a fine man in the bright noon of life, whom Daniel

[030-028]thought absolutely perfect, and whose place was one of the finest in

[030-029]England, at once historical, romantic, and home-like: a picturesque

[030-030]architectural outgrowth from an abbey, which had still remnants of the

[030-031]old monastic trunk. [[diplow-hall|Diplow]] lay in another county, and was a

[030-032]comparatively landless place which had come into the family from a rich

[030-033]lawyer on the female side who wore the perruque of the restoration;

[030-034]whereas the Mallingers had the grant of Monk’s Topping under Henry the

[030-035]Eighth, and ages before had held the neighboring lands of King’s

[030-036]Topping, tracing indeed their origin to a certain Hugues le Malingre,

[030-037]who came in with the Conqueror—and also apparently with a sickly

[030-038]complexion which had been happily corrected in his descendants. Two

[030-039]rows of these descendants, direct and collateral, females of the male

[030-040]line, and males of the female, looked down in the gallery over the

[030-041]cloisters on the nephew Daniel as he walked there: men in armor with

[030-042]pointed beards and arched eyebrows, pinched ladies in hoops and ruffs

[030-043]with no face to speak of; grave-looking men in black velvet and stuffed

[030-044]hips, and fair, frightened women holding little boys by the hand;

[030-045]smiling politicians in magnificent perruques, and ladies of the

[030-046]prize-animal kind, with rosebud mouths and full eyelids, according to

[030-047]Lely; then a generation whose faces were revised and embellished in the

[030-048]taste of Kneller; and so on through refined editions of the family

[030-049]types in the time of Reynolds and Romney, till the line ended with Sir

[030-050]Hugo and his younger brother Henleigh. This last had married Miss

[030-051][[Henleigh-Grandcourt|Grandcourt]], and taken her name along with her estates, thus making a

[030-052]junction between two equally old families, impaling the three Saracens’

[030-053]heads proper and three bezants of the one with the tower and falcons

[030-054]_argent_ of the other, and, as it happened, uniting their highest

[030-055]advantages in the prospects of that Henleigh Mallinger Grandcourt who

[030-056]is at present more of an acquaintance to us than either Sir Hugo or his

[030-057]nephew Daniel Deronda.

[030-058]In Sir Hugo’s youthful portrait with rolled collar and high cravat, Sir

[030-059]Thomas Lawrence had done justice to the agreeable alacrity of

[030-060]expression and sanguine temperament still to be seen in the original,

[030-061]but had done something more than justice in slightly lengthening the

[030-062]nose, which was in reality shorter than might have been expected in a

[030-063]Mallinger. Happily the appropriate nose of the family reappeared in his

[030-064]younger brother, and was to be seen in all its refined regularity in

[030-065]his nephew Mallinger Grandcourt. But in the nephew Daniel Deronda the

[030-066]family faces of various types, seen on the walls of the gallery, found

[030-067]no reflex. Still he was handsomer than any of them, and when he was

[030-068]thirteen might have served as model for any painter who wanted to image

[030-069]the most memorable of boys: you could hardly have seen his face

[030-070]thoroughly meeting yours without believing that human creatures had

[030-071]done nobly in times past, and might do more nobly in time to come. The

[030-072]finest childlike faces have this consecrating power, and make us

[030-073]shudder anew at all the grossness and basely-wrought griefs of the

[030-074]world, lest they should enter here and defile.

[030-075]But at this moment on the grass among the rose-petals, Daniel Deronda

[030-076]was making a first acquaintance with those griefs. A new idea had

[030-077]entered his mind, and was beginning to change the aspect of his

[030-078]habitual feelings as happy careless voyagers are changed with the sky

[030-079]suddenly threatened and the thought of danger arises. He sat perfectly

[030-080]still with his back to the tutor, while his face expressed rapid inward

[030-081]transition. The deep blush, which had come when he first started up,

[030-082]gradually subsided; but his features kept that indescribable look of

[030-083]subdued activity which often accompanies a new mental survey of

[030-084]familiar facts. He had not lived with other boys, and his mind showed

[030-085]the same blending of child’s ignorance with surprising knowledge which

[030-086]is oftener seen in bright girls. Having read [[shakespeare-in-deronda|Shakespeare]] as well as a

[030-087]great deal of history, he could have talked with the wisdom of a

[030-088]bookish child about men who were born out of wedlock and were held

[030-089]unfortunate in consequence, being under disadvantages which required

[030-090]them to be a sort of heroes if they were to work themselves up to an

[030-091]equal standing with their legally born brothers. But he had never

[030-092]brought such knowledge into any association with his own lot, which had

[030-093]been too easy for him ever to think about it—until this moment when

[030-094]there had darted into his mind with the magic of quick comparison, the

[030-095]possibility that here was the secret of his own birth, and that the man

[030-096]whom he called uncle was really his father. Some children, even younger

[030-097]than Daniel, have known the first arrival of care, like an ominous

[030-098]irremovable guest in their tender lives, on the discovery that their

[030-099]parents, whom they had imagined able to buy everything, were poor and

[030-100]in hard money troubles. Daniel felt the presence of a new guest who

[030-101]seemed to come with an enigmatic veiled face, and to carry

[030-102]dimly-conjectured, dreaded revelations. The ardor which he had given to

[030-103]the imaginary world in his books suddenly rushed toward his own history

[030-104]and spent its pictorial energy there, explaining what he knew,

[030-105]representing the unknown. The uncle whom he loved very dearly took the

[030-106]aspect of a father who held secrets about him—who had done him a

[030-107]wrong—yes, a wrong: and what had become of his mother, for whom he

[030-108]must have been taken away?—Secrets about which he, Daniel, could never

[030-109]inquire; for to speak or to be spoken to about these new thoughts

[030-110]seemed like falling flakes of fire to his imagination. Those who have

[030-111]known an impassioned childhood will understand this dread of utterance

[030-112]about any shame connected with their parents. The impetuous advent of

[030-113]new images took possession of him with the force of fact for the first

[030-114]time told, and left him no immediate power for the reflection that he

[030-115]might be trembling at a fiction of his own. The terrible sense of

[030-116]collision between a strong rush of feeling and the dread of its

[030-117]betrayal, found relief at length in big slow tears, which fell without

[030-118]restraint until the voice of Mr. Fraser was heard saying:

[030-119]“Daniel, do you see that you are sitting on the bent pages of your

[030-120]book?”

[030-121]Daniel immediately moved the book without turning round, and after

[030-122]holding it before him for an instant, rose with it and walked away into

[030-123]the open grounds, where he could dry his tears unobserved. The first

[030-124]shock of suggestion past, he could remember that he had no certainty

[030-125]how things really had been, and that he had been making conjectures

[030-126]about his own history, as he had often made stories about Pericles or

[030-127]Columbus, just to fill up the blanks before they became famous. Only

[030-128]there came back certain facts which had an obstinate reality, almost

[030-129]like the fragments of a bridge, telling you unmistakably how the arches

[030-130]lay. And again there came a mood in which his conjectures seemed like a

[030-131]doubt of religion, to be banished as an offense, and a mean prying

[030-132]after what he was not meant to know; for there was hardly a delicacy of

[030-133]feeling this lad was not capable of. But the summing-up of all his

[030-134]fluctuating experience at this epoch was, that a secret impression had

[030-135]come to him which had given him something like a new sense in relation

[030-136]to all the elements of his life. And the idea that others probably knew

[030-137]things concerning which they did not choose to mention, set up in him a

[030-138]premature reserve which helped to intensify his inward experience. His

[030-139]ears open now to words which before that July day would have passed by

[030-140]him unnoted; and round every trivial incident which imagination could

[030-141]connect with his suspicions, a newly-roused set of feelings were ready

[030-142]to cluster themselves.

[030-143]One such incident a month later wrought itself deeply into his life.

[030-144]Daniel had not only one of those thrilling boy voices which seem to

[030-145]bring an idyllic heaven and earth before our eyes, but a fine musical

[030-146]instinct, and had early made out accompaniments for himself on the

[030-147]piano, while he sang from memory. Since then he had had some teaching,

[030-148]and Sir Hugo, who delighted in the boy, used to ask for his [[art|music]] in

[030-149]the presence of guests. One morning after he had been [[music|singing]] “Sweet

[030-150]Echo” before a small party of gentlemen whom the rain had kept in the

[030-151]house, the baronet, passing from a smiling remark to his next neighbor

[030-152]said:

[030-153]“Come here, Dan!”

[030-154]The boy came forward with unusual reluctance. He wore an embroidered

[030-155]holland blouse which set off the rich coloring of his head and throat,

[030-156]and the resistant gravity about his mouth and eyes as he was being

[030-157]smiled upon, made their beauty the more impressive. Every one was

[030-158]admiring him.

[030-159]“What do you say to being a great singer? Should you like to be adored

[030-160]by the world and take the house by storm, like Mario and Tamberlik?”

[030-161]Daniel reddened instantaneously, but there was a just perceptible

[030-162]interval before he answered with angry decision,

[030-163]“No; I should hate it!”

[030-164]“Well, well, well!” said Sir Hugo, with surprised kindliness intended

[030-165]to be soothing. But Daniel turned away quickly, left the room, and

[030-166]going to his own chamber threw himself on the broad window-sill, which

[030-167]was a favorite retreat of his when he had nothing particular to do.

[030-168]Here he could see the rain gradually subsiding with gleams through the

[030-169]parting clouds which lit up a great reach of the park, where the old

[030-170]oaks stood apart from each other, and the bordering wood was pierced

[030-171]with a green glade which met the eastern sky. This was a scene which

[030-172]had always been part of his home—part of the dignified ease which had

[030-173]been a matter of course in his life. And his ardent clinging nature had

[030-174]appropriated it all with affection. He knew a great deal of what it was

[030-175]to be a gentleman by inheritance, and without thinking much about

[030-176]himself—for he was a boy of active perceptions and easily forgot his

[030-177]own existence in that of Robert Bruce—he had never supposed that he

[030-178]could be shut out from such a lot, or have a very different part in the

[030-179]world from that of the uncle who petted him. It is possible (though not

[030-180]greatly believed in at present) to be fond of poverty and take it for a

[030-181]bride, to prefer scoured deal, red quarries and whitewash for one’s

[030-182]private surroundings, to delight in no splendor but what has open doors

[030-183]for the whole nation, and to glory in having no privileges except such

[030-184]as nature insists on; and noblemen have been known to run away from

[030-185]elaborate ease and the option of idleness, that they might bind

[030-186]themselves for small pay to hard-handed labor. But Daniel’s tastes were

[030-187]altogether in keeping with his nurture: his disposition was one in

[030-188]which everyday scenes and habits beget not _ennui_ or rebellion, but

[030-189]delight, affection, aptitudes; and now the lad had been stung to the

[030-190]quick by the idea that his uncle—perhaps his father—thought of a

[030-191]career for him which was totally unlike his own, and which he knew very

[030-192]well was not thought of among possible destinations for the sons of

[030-193]English gentlemen. He had often stayed in [[london|London]] with Sir Hugo, who to

[030-194]indulge the boy’s ear had carried him to the [[opera-as-art|opera]] to hear the great

[030-195]tenors, so that the image of a singer taking the house by storm was

[030-196]very vivid to him; but now, spite of his musical gift, he set himself

[030-197]bitterly against the notion of being dressed up to sing before all

[030-198]those fine people, who would not care about him except as a wonderful

[030-199]toy. That Sir Hugo should have thought of him in that position for a

[030-200]moment, seemed to Daniel an unmistakable proof that there was something

[030-201]about his birth which threw him out from the class of gentlemen to

[030-202]which the baronet belonged. Would it ever be mentioned to him? Would

[030-203]the time come when his uncle would tell him everything? He shrank from

[030-204]the prospect: in his imagination he preferred ignorance. If his father

[030-205]had been wicked—Daniel inwardly used strong words, for he was feeling

[030-206]the injury done him as a maimed boy feels the crushed limb which for

[030-207]others is merely reckoned in an average of accidents—if his father had

[030-208]done any wrong, he wished it might never be spoken of to him: it was

[030-209]already a cutting thought that such knowledge might be in other minds.

[030-210]Was it in Mr. Fraser’s? probably not, else he would not have spoken in

[030-211]that way about the pope’s nephews. Daniel fancied, as older people do,

[030-212]that every one else’s consciousness was as active as his own on a

[030-213]matter which was vital to him. Did Turvey the valet know?—and old Mrs.

[030-214]French the housekeeper?—and Banks the bailiff, with whom he had ridden

[030-215]about the farms on his pony?—And now there came back the recollection

[030-216]of a day some years before when he was drinking Mrs. Banks’s whey, and

[030-217]Banks said to his wife with a wink and a cunning laugh, “He features

[030-218]the mother, eh?” At that time little Daniel had merely thought that

[030-219]Banks made a silly face, as the common farming men often did, laughing

[030-220]at what was not laughable; and he rather resented being winked at and

[030-221]talked of as if he did not understand everything. But now that small

[030-222]incident became information: it was to be reasoned on. How could he be

[030-223]like his mother and not like his father? His mother must have been a

[030-224]Mallinger, if Sir Hugo were his uncle. But no! His father might have

[030-225]been Sir Hugo’s brother and have changed his name, as Mr. Henleigh

[030-226]Mallinger did when he married Miss Grandcourt. But then, why had he

[030-227]never heard Sir Hugo speak of his brother Deronda, as he spoke of his

[030-228]brother Grandcourt? Daniel had never before cared about the family

[030-229]tree—only about that ancestor who had killed three Saracens in one

[030-230]encounter. But now his mind turned to a cabinet of estate-maps in the

[030-231]library, where he had once seen an illuminated parchment hanging out,

[030-232]that Sir Hugo said was the family tree. The phrase was new and odd to

[030-233]him—he was a little fellow then—hardly more than half his present

[030-234]age—and he gave it no precise meaning. He knew more now and wished

[030-235]that he could examine that parchment. He imagined that the cabinet was

[030-236]always locked, and longed to try it. But here he checked himself. He

[030-237]might be seen: and he would never bring himself near even a silent

[030-238]admission of the sore that had opened in him.
