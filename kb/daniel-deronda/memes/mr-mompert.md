# Mr. Mompert

Mr. Mompert (also referred to as Bishop Mompert) is a clergyman who offers [[Gwendolen-Harleth]] a position in his household after the collapse of her family's fortune. Though he never appears directly in the novel, the prospect of the Mompert situation serves as a crucial plot device, representing the narrow path of respectability available to a young woman without money.

## The Mompert Offer

When Gwendolen's family is financially ruined by the failure of Grapnell and Co., her uncle [[Mr-Gascoigne]] arranges a position for her in the Mompert household. The situation is presented as a respectable and even advantageous one: "It is really a home, with a continuance of education" [051-173]. Mr. Gascoigne emphasizes his personal connection to the Momperts as an advantage for Gwendolen.

Yet Gwendolen experiences the prospect with visceral dread. The name "Mompert" itself becomes "a sort of Mumbo-jumbo" [055-094] in her imagination — a symbol of the constrained, dependent life she has always sought to escape. Her mother reports that "Mrs. Mompert wishes to see you before making an absolute engagement" [051-173], a formality that Gwendolen finds humiliating.

## Symbolic Significance

The Mompert situation crystallizes the novel's critique of the limited options for women in Victorian society. Gwendolen, who has imagined herself a queen of society, faces a future of "Mrs. Mompert's supervision" [055-090] — a phrase that captures the loss of autonomy and glamour. The bishop's household, with "three daughters" [051-173], represents domestic confinement rather than the freedom Gwendolen craved.

The Mompert option functions as the "before" against which marriage to [[Henleigh-Grandcourt]] appears as escape — a juxtaposition that Eliot uses to deepen the tragedy of Gwendolen's choice.
