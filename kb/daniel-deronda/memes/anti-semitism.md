# Anti-Semitism

> The prejudice against Jews is a persistent undercurrent in *Daniel Deronda*, shaping the experiences of every Jewish character and motivating Mordecai's vision of a restored Jewish national life.

Anti-Semitism in George Eliot's *Daniel Deronda* (1876) operates as both explicit social prejudice and an invisible structural force that constrains the lives of Jewish characters. Through her sympathetic portrayal of [[Mordecai]], [[Mirah-Lapidoth|Mirah]], and the Cohen family, Eliot challenged the anti-Semitic stereotypes prevalent in Victorian English society and literature.

## Social Prejudice in the Novel

The novel registers anti-Semitism at multiple social levels. At the upper-class gatherings, Jewishness is treated as an exotic curiosity or a barely concealed stigma. [[Catherine-Arrowpoint|Catherine Arrowpoint]]'s family reacts with horror when she intends to marry the Jewish musician [[Herr-Klesmer|Klesmer]], revealing the depth of aristocratic prejudice against even celebrated Jewish artists[^1].

The [[Ezra-Cohen|Cohen family]], who run a pawnshop in a poor London district, embody the stereotype that English society attached to Jews — yet Eliot humanizes them, showing their domestic life, their observance of [[Judaism]], and their kindness in sheltering [[Mordecai]].

## Structural Exclusion

Beyond individual prejudice, the novel depicts systemic barriers. Jewish characters face exclusion from professions, social circles, and the implicit category of "Englishness." [[Mordecai]] articulates this when he speaks of "the yoke of the nations" and the humiliations borne by "our people" through centuries of dispersion[^2].

[[Daniel-Deronda|Deronda]]'s discovery of his own Jewish heritage forces him to confront what this identity means in a Christian-dominated society — not just spiritually, but in terms of how he will be perceived and treated by others.

## Eliot's Philo-Semitic Project

Eliot wrote *Daniel Deronda* partly as a corrective to the anti-Semitic caricatures common in Victorian fiction (notably Dickens's Fagin in *Oliver Twist*). Her notebook research into Jewish history, customs, and Kabbalistic thought informed her portrait of [[Mordecai]] as a visionary intellectual rather than a stereotype[^3].

The novel's treatment of [[Judaism]] as a living, evolving tradition — rather than a superseded precursor to Christianity — was itself a challenge to theological anti-Semitism. Mordecai's insistence on the continuing vitality of Jewish thought directly rebuts Christian supersessionist narratives.

## Relation to the Zionist Vision

Anti-Semitism is the negative force that, in the novel's logic, drives the positive vision of Jewish national restoration. [[Mordecai]]'s proto-Zionist philosophy — that the Jewish people must gather in their ancestral land — is presented as a response to the impossibility of full acceptance in European societies. As he says, merely tolerating Jews while expecting them to abandon their identity is another form of prejudice: "We must wait patiently for prejudices to die out."
