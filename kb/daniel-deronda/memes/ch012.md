[012-001]CHAPTER VII.

[012-002]“_Perigot_. As the bonny lasse passed by,

[012-003]_Willie_. Hey, ho, bonnilasse!

[012-004]_P_. She roode at me with glauncing eye,

[012-005]_W_. As clear as the crystal glasse.

[012-006]_P_. All as the sunny beame so bright,

[012-007]_W_. Hey, ho, the sunnebeame!

[012-008]_P_. Glaunceth from Phoebus’ face forthright,

[012-009]_W_. So love into thy heart did streame.”

[012-010]—SPENSER: _Shepard’s Calendar_.

[012-011]“The kindliest symptom, yet the most alarming crisis in the ticklish

[012-012]state of youth; the nourisher and destroyer of hopeful wits; * * * the

[012-013]servitude above freedom; the gentle mind’s religion; the liberal

[012-014]superstition.”—CHARLES LAMB.

[012-015]The first sign of the unimagined snow-storm was like the transparent

[012-016]white cloud that seems to set off the blue. Anna was in the secret of

[012-017][[Rex-Gascoigne|Rex]]’s feeling; though for the first time in their lives he had said

[012-018]nothing to her about what he most thought of, and he only took it for

[012-019]granted that she knew it. For the first time, too, Anna could not say

[012-020]to Rex what was continually in her mind. Perhaps it might have been a

[012-021]pain which she would have had to conceal, that he should so soon care

[012-022]for some one else more than for herself, if such a feeling had not been

[012-023]thoroughly neutralized by doubt and anxiety on his behalf. Anna admired

[012-024]her cousin—would have said with simple sincerity, “[[Gwendolen-Harleth|Gwendolen]] is always

[012-025]very good to me,” and held it in the order of things for herself to be

[012-026]entirely subject to this cousin; but she looked at her with mingled

[012-027]fear and distrust, with a puzzled contemplation as of some wondrous and

[012-028]beautiful animal whose nature was a mystery, and who, for anything Anna

[012-029]knew, might have an appetite for devouring all the small creatures that

[012-030]were her own particular pets. And now Anna’s heart was sinking under

[012-031]the heavy conviction which she dared not utter, that Gwendolen would

[012-032]never care for Rex. What she herself held in tenderness and reverence

[012-033]had constantly seemed indifferent to Gwendolen, and it was easier to

[012-034]imagine her scorning Rex than returning any tenderness of his. Besides,

[012-035]she was always thinking of being something extraordinary. And poor Rex!

[012-036]Papa would be angry with him if he knew. And of course he was too young

[012-037]to be in love in that way; and she, Anna had thought that it would be

[012-038]years and years before any thing of that sort came, and that she would

[012-039]be Rex’s housekeeper ever so long. But what a heart must that be which

[012-040]did not return his love! Anna, in the prospect of his suffering, was

[012-041]beginning to dislike her too fascinating cousin.

[012-042]It seemed to her, as it did to Rex, that the weeks had been filled with

[012-043]a tumultuous life evident to all observers: if he had been questioned

[012-044]on the subject he would have said that he had no wish to conceal what

[012-045]he hoped would be an engagement which he should immediately tell his

[012-046]father of: and yet for the first time in his life he was reserved not

[012-047]only about his feelings but—which was more remarkable to Anna—about

[012-048]certain actions. She, on her side, was nervous each time her father or

[012-049]mother began to speak to her in private lest they should say anything

[012-050]about Rex and Gwendolen. But the elders were not in the least alive to

[012-051]this agitating drama, which went forward chiefly in a sort of pantomime

[012-052]extremely lucid in the minds thus expressing themselves, but easily

[012-053]missed by spectators who were running their eyes over the _Guardian_ or

[012-054]the _Clerical Gazette_, and regarded the trivialities of the young ones

[012-055]with scarcely more interpretation than they gave to the action of

[012-056]lively ants.

[012-057]“Where are you going, Rex?” said Anna one gray morning when her father

[012-058]had set off in his carriage to the sessions, Mrs. Gascoigne with him,

[012-059]and she had observed that her brother had on his antigropelos, the

[012-060]utmost approach he possessed to a hunting equipment.

[012-061]“Going to see the hounds throw off at the Three Barns.”

[012-062]“Are you going to take Gwendolen?” said Anna, timidly.

[012-063]“She told you, did she?”

[012-064]“No, but I thought—Does papa know you are going?”

[012-065]“Not that I am aware of. I don’t suppose he would trouble himself about

[012-066]the matter.”

[012-067]“You are going to use his horse?”

[012-068]“He knows I do that whenever I can.”

[012-069]“Don’t let Gwendolen ride after the hounds, Rex,” said Anna, whose

[012-070]fears gifted her with second-sight.

[012-071]“Why not?” said Rex, smiling rather provokingly.

[012-072]“Papa and mamma and aunt Davilow all wish her not to. They think it is

[012-073]not right for her.”

[012-074]“Why should you suppose she is going to do what is not right?”

[012-075]“Gwendolen minds nobody sometimes,” said Anna getting bolder by dint of

[012-076]a little anger.

[012-077]“Then she would not mind me,” said Rex, perversely making a joke of

[012-078]poor Anna’s anxiety.

[012-079]“Oh Rex, I cannot bear it. You will make yourself very unhappy.” Here

[012-080]Anna burst into tears.

[012-081]“Nannie, Nannie, what on earth is the matter with you?” said Rex, a

[012-082]little impatient at being kept in this way, hat on and whip in hand.

[012-083]“She will not care for you one bit—I know she never will!” said the

[012-084]poor child in a sobbing whisper. She had lost all control of herself.

[012-085]Rex reddened and hurried away from her out of the hall door, leaving

[012-086]her to the miserable consciousness of having made herself disagreeable

[012-087]in vain.

[012-088]He did think of her words as he rode along; they had the unwelcomeness

[012-089]which all unfavorable fortune-telling has, even when laughed at; but he

[012-090]quickly explained them as springing from little Anna’s tenderness, and

[012-091]began to be sorry that he was obliged to come away without soothing

[012-092]her. Every other feeling on the subject, however, was quickly merged in

[012-093]a resistant belief to the contrary of hers, accompanied with a new

[012-094]determination to prove that he was right. This sort of certainty had

[012-095]just enough kinship to doubt and uneasiness to hurry on a confession

[012-096]which an untouched security might have delayed.

[012-097]Gwendolen was already mounted and riding up and down the avenue when

[012-098]Rex appeared at the gate. She had provided herself against

[012-099]disappointment in case he did not appear in time by having the groom

[012-100]ready behind her, for she would not have waited beyond a reasonable

[012-101]time. But now the groom was dismissed, and the two rode away in

[012-102]delightful freedom. Gwendolen was in her highest spirits, and Rex

[012-103]thought that she had never looked so lovely before; her figure, her

[012-104]long white throat, and the curves of her cheek and chin were always set

[012-105]off to perfection by the compact simplicity of her riding dress. He

[012-106]could not conceive a more perfect girl; and to a youthful lover like

[012-107]Rex it seems that the fundamental identity of the good, the true and

[012-108]the beautiful, is already extant and manifest in the object of his

[012-109]love. Most observers would have held it more than equally accountable

[012-110]that a girl should have like impressions about Rex, for in his handsome

[012-111]face there was nothing corresponding to the undefinable stinging

[012-112]quality—as it were a trace of demon ancestry—which made some

[012-113]beholders hesitate in their admiration of Gwendolen.

[012-114]It was an exquisite January morning in which there was no threat of

[012-115]rain, but a gray sky making the calmest background for the charms of a

[012-116]mild winter scene—the grassy borders of the lanes, the hedgerows

[012-117]sprinkled with red berries and haunted with low twitterings, the purple

[012-118]bareness of the elms, the rich brown of the furrows. The horses’ hoofs

[012-119]made a musical chime, accompanying their young voices. She was laughing

[012-120]at his equipment, for he was the reverse of a dandy, and he was

[012-121]enjoying her laughter; the freshness of the morning mingled with the

[012-122]freshness of their youth; and every sound that came from their clear

[012-123]throats, every glance they gave each other, was the bubbling outflow

[012-124]from a spring of joy. It was all morning to them, within and without.

[012-125]And thinking of them in these moments one is tempted to that futile

[012-126]sort of wishing—if only things could have been a little otherwise

[012-127]then, so as to have been greatly otherwise after—if only these two

[012-128]beautiful young creatures could have pledged themselves to each other

[012-129]then and there, and never through life have swerved from that pledge!

[012-130]For some of the goodness which Rex believed in was there. Goodness is a

[012-131]large, often a prospective word; like harvest, which at one stage when

[012-132]we talk of it lies all underground, with an indeterminate future; is

[012-133]the germ prospering in the darkness? at another, it has put forth

[012-134]delicate green blades, and by-and-by the trembling blossoms are ready

[012-135]to be dashed off by an hour of rough wind or rain. Each stage has its

[012-136]peculiar blight, and may have the healthy life choked out of it by a

[012-137]particular action of the foul land which rears or neighbors it, or by

[012-138]damage brought from foulness afar.

[012-139]“Anna had got it into her head that you would want to ride after the

[012-140]hounds this morning,” said Rex, whose secret associations with Anna’s

[012-141]words made this speech seem quite perilously near the most momentous of

[012-142]subjects.

[012-143]“Did she?” said Gwendolen, laughingly. “What a little clairvoyant she

[012-144]is!”

[012-145]“Shall you?” said Rex, who had not believed in her intending to do it

[012-146]if the elders objected, but confided in her having good reasons.

[012-147]“I don’t know. I can’t tell what I shall do till I get there.

[012-148]Clairvoyants are often wrong: they foresee what is likely. I am not

[012-149]fond of what is likely: it is always dull. I do what is unlikely.”

[012-150]“Ah, there you tell me a secret. When once I knew what people in

[012-151]general would be likely to do, I should know you would do the opposite.

[012-152]So you would have come round to a likelihood of your own sort. I shall

[012-153]be able to calculate on you. You couldn’t surprise me.”

[012-154]“Yes, I could. I should turn round and do what was likely for people in

[012-155]general,” said Gwendolen, with a musical laugh.

[012-156]“You see you can’t escape some sort of likelihood. And

[012-157]contradictoriness makes the strongest likelihood of all. You must give

[012-158]up a plan.”

[012-159]“No, I shall not. My plan is to do what pleases me.” (Here should any

[012-160]young lady incline to imitate Gwendolen, let her consider the set of

[012-161]her head and neck: if the angle there had been different, the chin

[012-162]protrusive, and the cervical vertebrae a trifle more curved in their

[012-163]position, ten to one Gwendolen’s words would have had a jar in them for

[012-164]the sweet-natured Rex. But everything odd in her speech was humor and

[012-165]pretty banter, which he was only anxious to turn toward one point.)

[012-166]“Can you manage to feel only what pleases you?” said he.

[012-167]“Of course not; that comes from what other people do. But if the world

[012-168]were pleasanter, one would only feel what was pleasant. Girls’ lives

[012-169]are so stupid: they never do what they like.”

[012-170]“I thought that was more the case of the men. They are forced to do

[012-171]hard things, and are often dreadfully bored, and knocked to pieces too.

[012-172]And then, if we love a girl very dearly we want to do as she likes, so

[012-173]after all you have your own way.”

[012-174]“I don’t believe it. I never saw a married woman who had her own way.”

[012-175]“What should you like to do?” said Rex, quite guilelessly, and in real

[012-176]anxiety.

[012-177]“Oh, I don’t know!—go to the North Pole, or ride steeple-chases, or go

[012-178]to be a queen in the East like Lady Hester Stanhope,” said Gwendolen,

[012-179]flightily. Her words were born on her lips, but she would have been at

[012-180]a loss to give an answer of deeper origin.

[012-181]“You don’t mean you would never be married?”

[012-182]“No; I didn’t say that. Only when I married, I should not do as other

[012-183]women do.”
