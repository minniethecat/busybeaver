[077-001][[Gwendolen-Harleth|Gwendolen]], indeed, with all that gnawing trouble in her consciousness,

[077-002]had hardly for a moment dropped the sense that it was her part to bear

[077-003]herself with dignity, and appear what is called happy. In disclosure of

[077-004]disappointment or sorrow she saw nothing but a [[shame|humiliation]] which would

[077-005]have been vinegar to her wounds. Whatever her husband might have come

[077-006]at last to be to her, she meant to wear the yoke so as not to be

[077-007]pitied. For she did think of the coming years with presentiment: she

[077-008]was frightened at [[Henleigh-Grandcourt|Grandcourt]]. The poor thing had passed from her

[077-009]girlish sauciness of superiority over this inert specimen of personal

[077-010]distinction into an amazed perception of her former ignorance about the

[077-011]possible mental attitude of a man toward the woman he sought in

[077-012]marriage—of her present ignorance as to what their life with each

[077-013]other might turn into. For novelty gives immeasurableness to fear, and

[077-014]fills the early time of all sad changes with phantoms of the future.

[077-015]Her little coquetries, voluntary or involuntary, had told on Grandcourt

[077-016]during courtship, and formed a medium of communication between them,

[077-017]showing him in the light of a creature such as she could understand and

[077-018]manage: but marriage had nullified all such interchange, and Grandcourt

[077-019]had become a blank uncertainty to her in everything but this, that he

[077-020]would do just what he willed, and that she had neither devices at her

[077-021]command to determine his will, nor any rational means of escaping it.

[077-022]What had occurred between them and her wearing the diamonds was

[077-023]typical. One evening, shortly before they came to the Abbey, they were

[077-024]going to dine at Brackenshaw Castle. Gwendolen had said to herself that

[077-025]she would never wear those diamonds: they had horrible words clinging

[077-026]and crawling about them, as from some bad dream, whose images lingered

[077-027]on the perturbed sense. She came down dressed in her white, with only a

[077-028]streak of gold and a pendant of emeralds, which Grandcourt had given

[077-029]her, round her neck, and the little emerald stars in her ears.

[077-030]Grandcourt stood with his back to the fire and looked at her as she

[077-031]entered.

[077-032]“Am I altogether as you like?” she said, speaking rather gaily. She was

[077-033]not without enjoyment in this occasion of going to Brackenshaw Castle

[077-034]with her new dignities upon her, as men whose affairs are sadly

[077-035]involved will enjoy dining out among persons likely to be under a

[077-036]pleasant mistake about them.

[077-037]“No,” said Grandcourt.

[077-038]Gwendolen felt suddenly uncomfortable, wondering what was to come. She

[077-039]was not unprepared for some struggle about the diamonds; but suppose he

[077-040]were going to say, in low, contemptuous tones, “You are not in any way

[077-041]what I like.” It was very bad for her to be secretly hating him; but it

[077-042]would be much worse when he gave the first sign of hating her.

[077-043]“Oh, mercy!” she exclaimed, the pause lasting till she could bear it no

[077-044]longer. “How am I to alter myself?”

[077-045]“Put on the diamonds,” said Grandcourt, looking straight at her with

[077-046]his narrow glance.

[077-047]Gwendolen paused in her turn, afraid of showing any emotion, and

[077-048]feeling that nevertheless there was some change in her eyes as they met

[077-049]his. But she was obliged to answer, and said as indifferently as she

[077-050]could, “Oh, please not. I don’t think diamonds suit me.”

[077-051]“What you think has nothing to do with it,” said Grandcourt, his _sotto

[077-052]voce_ imperiousness seeming to have an evening quietude and finish,

[077-053]like his toilet. “I wish you to wear the diamonds.”

[077-054]“Pray excuse me; I like these emeralds,” said Gwendolen, frightened in

[077-055]spite of her preparation. That white hand of his which was touching his

[077-056]whisker was capable, she fancied, of clinging round her neck and

[077-057]threatening to throttle her; for her fear of him, mingling with the

[077-058]vague foreboding of some retributive calamity which hung about her

[077-059]life, had reached a superstitious point.

[077-060]“Oblige me by telling me your reason for not wearing the diamonds when

[077-061]I desire it,” said Grandcourt. His eyes were still fixed upon her, and

[077-062]she felt her own eyes narrowing under them as if to shut out an

[077-063]entering pain.

[077-064]Of what use was the rebellion within her? She could say nothing that

[077-065]would not hurt her worse than submission. Turning slowly and covering

[077-066]herself again, she went to her dressing-room. As she reached out the

[077-067]diamonds, it occurred to her that her unwillingness to wear them might

[077-068]have already raised a suspicion in Grandcourt that she had some

[077-069]knowledge about them which he had not given her. She fancied that his

[077-070]eyes showed a delight in torturing her. How could she be defiant? She

[077-071]had nothing to say that would touch him—nothing but what would give

[077-072]him a more painful grasp on her consciousness.

[077-073]“He delights in making the dogs and horses quail: that is half his

[077-074]pleasure in calling them his,” she said to herself, as she opened the

[077-075]jewel-case with a shivering sensation.

[077-076]“It will come to be so with me; and I shall quail. What else is there

[077-077]for me? I will not say to the world, ‘Pity me.’”

[077-078]She was about to ring for her maid when she heard the door open behind

[077-079]her. It was Grandcourt who came in.

[077-080]“You want some one to fasten them,” he said, coming toward her.

[077-081]She did not answer, but simply stood still, leaving him to take out the

[077-082]ornaments and fasten them as he would. Doubtless he had been used to

[077-083]fasten them on some one else. With a bitter sort of sarcasm against

[077-084]herself, Gwendolen thought, “What a privilege this is, to have robbed

[077-085]another woman of!”

[077-086]“What makes you so cold?” said Grandcourt, when he had fastened the

[077-087]last ear-ring. “Pray put plenty of furs on. I hate to see a woman come

[077-088]into a room looking frozen. If you are to appear as a bride at all,

[077-089]appear decently.”

[077-090]This marital speech was not exactly persuasive, but it touched the

[077-091]quick of Gwendolen’s pride and forced her to rally. The words of the

[077-092]bad dream crawled about the diamonds still, but only for her: to others

[077-093]they were brilliants that suited her perfectly, and Grandcourt inwardly

[077-094]observed that she answered to the rein.

[077-095]“Oh, yes, mamma, quite happy,” Gwendolen had said on her return to

[077-096][[diplow-hall|Diplow]]. “Not at all disappointed in [[ryelands|Ryelands]]. It is a much finer place

[077-097]than this—larger in every way. But don’t you want some more money?”

[077-098]“Did you not know that Mr. Grandcourt left me a letter on your

[077-099]wedding-day? I am to have eight hundred a year. He wishes me to keep

[077-100][[Offendene]] for the present, while you are at Diplow. But if there were

[077-101]some pretty cottage near the park at Ryelands we might live there

[077-102]without much expense, and I should have you most of the year, perhaps.”

[077-103]“We must leave that to Mr. Grandcourt, mamma.”

[077-104]“Oh, certainly. It is exceedingly handsome of him to say that he will

[077-105]pay the rent for Offendene till June. And we can go on very

[077-106]well—without any man-servant except Crane, just for out-of-doors. Our

[077-107]good Merry will stay with us and help me to manage everything. It is

[077-108]natural that Mr. Grandcourt should wish me to live in a good style of

[077-109]house in your neighborhood, and I cannot decline. So he said nothing

[077-110]about it to you?”

[077-111]“No; he wished me to hear it from you, I suppose.”

[077-112]Gwendolen in fact had been very anxious to have some definite knowledge

[077-113]of what would be done for her mother, but at no moment since her

[077-114]marriage had she been able to overcome the difficulty of mentioning the

[077-115]subject to Grandcourt. Now, however, she had a sense of [[duty|obligation]]

[077-116]which would not let her rest without saying to him, “It is very good of

[077-117]you to provide for mamma. You took a great deal on yourself in marrying

[077-118]a girl who had nothing but relations belonging to her.”

[077-119]Grandcourt was smoking, and only said carelessly, “Of course I was not

[077-120]going to let her live like a gamekeeper’s mother.”

[077-121]“At least he is not mean about money,” thought Gwendolen, “and mamma is

[077-122]the better off for my marriage.”

[077-123]She often pursued the comparison between what might have been, if she

[077-124]had not married Grandcourt, and what actually was, trying to persuade

[077-125]herself that life generally was barren of satisfaction, and that if she

[077-126]had chosen differently she might now have been looking back with a

[077-127]regret as bitter as the feeling she was trying to argue away. Her

[077-128]mother’s dullness, which used to irritate her, she was at present

[077-129]inclined to explain as the ordinary result of woman’s experience. True,

[077-130]she still saw that she would “manage differently from mamma;” but her

[077-131]management now only meant that she would carry her troubles with

[077-132]spirit, and let none suspect them. By and by she promised herself that

[077-133]she should get used to her heart-sores, and find excitements that would

[077-134]carry her through life, as a hard gallop carried her through some of

[077-135]the morning hours. There was gambling: she had heard stories at

[077-136][[Leubronn]] of fashionable women who gambled in all sorts of ways. It

[077-137]seemed very flat to her at this distance, but perhaps if she began to

[077-138]gamble again, the passion might awake. Then there was the pleasure of

[077-139]producing an effect by her appearance in society: what did celebrated

[077-140]beauties do in town when their husbands could afford display? All men

[077-141]were fascinated by them: they had a perfect equipage and toilet, walked

[077-142]into public places, and bowed, and made the usual answers, and walked

[077-143]out again, perhaps they bought china, and practiced accomplishments. If

[077-144]she could only feel a keen appetite for those pleasures—could only

[077-145]believe in pleasure as she used to do! Accomplishments had ceased to

[077-146]have the exciting quality of promising any pre-eminence to her; and as

[077-147]for fascinated gentlemen—adorers who might hover round her with

[077-148]languishment, and diversify married life with the romantic stir of

[077-149]mystery, passion, and danger, which her French reading had given her

[077-150]some girlish notion of—they presented themselves to her imagination

[077-151]with the fatal circumstance that, instead of fascinating her in return,

[077-152]they were clad in her own weariness and disgust. The admiring male,

[077-153]rashly adjusting the expression of his features and the turn of his

[077-154]conversation to her supposed tastes, had always been an absurd object

[077-155]to her, and at present seemed rather detestable. Many courses are

[077-156]actually pursued—follies and sins both convenient and

[077-157]inconvenient—without pleasure or hope of pleasure; but to solace

[077-158]ourselves with imagining any course beforehand, there must be some

[077-159]foretaste of pleasure in the shape of appetite; and Gwendolen’s

[077-160]appetite had sickened. Let her wander over the possibilities of her

[077-161]life as she would, an uncertain shadow dogged her. Her confidence in

[077-162]herself and her destiny had turned into [[conscience|remorse]] and dread; she trusted

[077-163]neither herself nor her future.

[077-164]This hidden helplessness gave fresh force to the hold [[Daniel-Deronda|Deronda]] had from

[077-165]the first taken on her mind, as one who had an unknown standard by

[077-166]which he judged her. Had he some way of looking at things which might

[077-167]be a new footing for her—an inward safeguard against possible events

[077-168]which she dreaded as stored-up retribution? It is one of the secrets in

[077-169]that change of mental poise which has been fitly named conversion, that

[077-170]to many among us neither heaven nor earth has any revelation till some

[077-171]personality touches theirs with a peculiar influence, subduing them

[077-172]into receptiveness. It had been Gwendolen’s habit to think of the

[077-173]persons around her as stale books, too familiar to be interesting.

[077-174]Deronda had lit up her attention with a sense of novelty: not by words

[077-175]only, but by imagined facts, his influence had entered into the current

[077-176]of that self-suspicion and self-blame which awakens a new consciousness.

[077-177]“I wish he could know everything about me without my telling him,” was

[077-178]one of her thoughts, as she sat leaning over the end of a couch,

[077-179]supporting her head with her hand, and looking at herself in a

[077-180]mirror—not in admiration, but in a sad kind of companionship. “I wish

[077-181]he knew that I am not so contemptible as he thinks me; that I am in

[077-182]deep trouble, and want to be something better if I could.” Without the

[077-183]aid of sacred ceremony or costume, her feelings had turned this man,

[077-184]only a few years older than herself, into a priest; a sort of trust

[077-185]less rare than the fidelity that guards it. Young reverence for one who

[077-186]is also young is the most coercive of all: there is the same level of

[077-187]temptation, and the higher motive is believed in as a fuller force—not

[077-188]suspected to be a mere residue from weary experience.
