# Justice

Justice in *[[Daniel-Deronda|Daniel Deronda]]* [[opera-as-art|opera]]tes as both a social ideal and a narrative principle — the moral law by which [[reputation|character]]s receive what their choices have earned them.

## Overview

Eliot structures the novel as a kind of moral court, in which every significant action invites judgment. "The shadow of justice" (072-137) falls across every [[reputation|character]], though its [[opera-as-art|opera]]tion is subtle rather than mechanical. Unlike the plot-[[ambition|drive]]n justice of Dickens or the providential justice of Charlotte Brontë, Eliot's justice is psychological: [[reputation|character]]s suffer the consequences of their choices not through external punishment but through the internal logic of their own natures.

## Role in the Novel

Justice in the novel is inseparable from sympathy. "A pity which is the shadow of love" (072-137) modifies pure justice with [[sympathy|compassion]], reflecting Eliot's conviction that justice without sympathy is cruelty. [[Daniel-Deronda|Deronda]] embodies this fusion: he judges [[Gwendolen-Harleth|Gwendolen]]'s choices but never condemns her, extending "the larger justice which makes gratitude" (111-102). The novel's treatment of [[Henleigh-Grandcourt|Grandcourt]] — allowed to die without redemption, his will punishing [[Gwendolen-Harleth|Gwendolen]] from beyond the grave — represents justice at its most austere.

## Expression Through Characters

### Gwendolen Harleth
[[Gwendolen-Harleth|Gwendolen]]'s fate is the novel's central enactment of justice. She knowingly marries [[Henleigh-Grandcourt|Grandcourt]] despite knowing about [[Lydia-Glasher|Lydia Glasher]]'s claim, and the novel exacts justice through her psychological torment. "The justice of hindering another from" a wrong act (063-024) becomes, in her case, the justice of suffering the consequences of a wrong already committed.

### Daniel Deronda
[[Daniel-Deronda|Deronda]]'s justice is restorative rather than punitive. He seeks to do "justice to my account of [[Mordecai]]" (103-081), to do justice to his Jewish [[inheritance|heritage]], to do justice to [[Mirah-Lapidoth|Mirah]]'s trust in him. His is the justice of recognition — seeing others clearly and acting accordingly.

## Key Passages

> The shadow of justice, a pity which is the shadow of love. (072-137)

> That larger justice which makes gratitude. (111-102)

> Do me the justice to believe that I was not indifferent. (090-077)

## Historical Context

Victorian debates about justice ranged from utilitarian reform (Bentham) to the idealist philosophy of T.H. Green, who influenced Eliot. The novel reflects the period's growing concern with social justice — the recognition that legal equality was insufficient without economic and cultural justice — particularly through the Jewish plot, which argues for the justice of national self-determination.
