[023-001]“I suppose you sent my card and inquiries.”

[023-002]“I went myself at four, and said you were sure to be there shortly.

[023-003]They would suppose some accident prevented you from fulfilling the

[023-004]intention. Especially if you go to-day.”

[023-005]Silence for a couple of minutes. Then [[Henleigh-Grandcourt|Grandcourt]] said, “What men are

[023-006]invited here with their wives?”

[023-007][[Lush]] drew out a note-book. “The Captain and Mrs. Torrington come next

[023-008]week. Then there are Mr. Hollis and Lady Flora, and the Cushats and the

[023-009]Gogoffs.”

[023-010]“Rather a ragged lot,” remarked Grandcourt, after a while. “Why did you

[023-011]ask the Gogoffs? When you write invitations in my name, be good enough

[023-012]to give me a list, instead of bringing down a giantess on me without my

[023-013]knowledge. She spoils the look of the room.”

[023-014]“You invited the Gogoffs yourself when you met them in [[paris|Paris]].”

[023-015]“What has my meeting them in Paris to do with it? I told you to give me

[023-016]a list.”

[023-017]Grandcourt, like many others, had two remarkably different voices.

[023-018]Hitherto we have heard him speaking in a superficial interrupted drawl

[023-019]suggestive chiefly of languor and _ennui_. But this last brief speech

[023-020]was uttered in subdued inward, yet distinct, tones, which Lush had long

[023-021]been used to recognize as the expression of a peremptory will.

[023-022]“Are there any other couples you would like to invite?”

[023-023]“Yes; think of some decent people, with a daughter or two. And one of

[023-024]your damned musicians. But not a comic fellow.”

[023-025]“I wonder if [[Herr-Klesmer|Klesmer]] would consent to come to us when he leaves

[023-026][[quetcham-hall|Quetcham]]. Nothing but first-class [[art|music]] will go down with Miss

[023-027]Arrowpoint.”

[023-028]Lush spoke carelessly, but he was really seizing an opportunity and

[023-029]fixing an observant look on Grandcourt, who now for the first time,

[023-030]turned his eyes toward his companion, but slowly and without speaking

[023-031]until he had given two long luxuriant puffs, when he said, perhaps in a

[023-032]lower tone than ever, but with a perceptible edge of contempt,

[023-033]“What in the name of nonsense have I to do with [[Catherine-Arrowpoint|Miss Arrowpoint]] and her

[023-034]music?”

[023-035]“Well, something,” said Lush, jocosely. “You need not give yourself

[023-036]much trouble, perhaps. But some forms must be gone through before a man

[023-037]can marry a million.”

[023-038]“Very likely. But I am not going to marry a million.”

[023-039]“That’s a pity—to fling away an opportunity of this sort, and knock

[023-040]down your own plans.”

[023-041]“_Your_ plans, I suppose you mean.”

[023-042]“You have some debts, you know, and things may turn out inconveniently,

[023-043]after all. The heirship is not _absolutely_ certain.”

[023-044]Grandcourt did not answer, and Lush went on.

[023-045]“It really is a fine opportunity. The father and mother ask for nothing

[023-046]better, I can see, and the daughter’s looks and manners require no

[023-047]allowances, any more than if she hadn’t a sixpence. She is not

[023-048]beautiful; but equal to carrying any rank. And she is not likely to

[023-049]refuse such prospects as you can offer her.”

[023-050]“Perhaps not.”

[023-051]“The father and mother would let you do anything you like with them.”

[023-052]“But I should not like to do anything with them.”

[023-053]Here it was Lush who made a little pause before speaking again, and

[023-054]then he said in a deep voice of remonstrance, “Good God, Grandcourt!

[023-055]after your experience, will you let a whim interfere with your

[023-056]comfortable settlement in life?”

[023-057]“Spare your oratory. I know what I am going to do.”

[023-058]“What?” Lush put down his cigar and thrust his hands into his side

[023-059]pockets, as if he had to face something exasperating, but meant to keep

[023-060]his temper.

[023-061]“I am going to marry the other girl.”

[023-062]“Have you fallen in love?” This question carried a strong sneer.

[023-063]“I am going to marry her.”

[023-064]“You have made her an offer already, then?”

[023-065]“No.”

[023-066]“She is a young lady with a will of her own, I fancy. Extremely well

[023-067]fitted to make a rumpus. She would know what she liked.”

[023-068]“She doesn’t like you,” said Grandcourt, with the ghost of a smile.

[023-069]“Perfectly true,” said Lush, adding again in a markedly sneering tone.

[023-070]“However, if you and she are devoted to each other, that will be

[023-071]enough.”

[023-072]Grandcourt took no notice of this speech, but sipped his coffee, rose,

[023-073]and strolled out on the lawn, all the dogs following him.

[023-074]Lush glanced after him a moment, then resumed his cigar and lit it, but

[023-075]smoked slowly, consulting his beard with inspecting eyes and fingers,

[023-076]till he finally stroked it with an air of having arrived at some

[023-077]conclusion, and said in a subdued voice,

[023-078]“Check, old boy!”

[023-079]Lush, being a man of some ability, had not known Grandcourt for fifteen

[023-080]years without learning what sort of measures were useless with him,

[023-081]though what sort might be useful remained often dubious. In the

[023-082]beginning of his career he held a fellowship, and was near taking

[023-083]orders for the sake of a college living, but, not being fond of that

[023-084]prospect, accepted instead the office of traveling companion to a

[023-085]marquess, and afterward to young Grandcourt, who had lost his father

[023-086]early, and who found Lush so convenient that he had allowed him to

[023-087]become prime minister in all his more personal affairs. The habit of

[023-088]fifteen years had made Grandcourt more and more in need of Lush’s

[023-089]handiness, and Lush more and more in need of the lazy luxury to which

[023-090]his transactions on behalf of Grandcourt made no interruption worth

[023-091]reckoning. I cannot say that the same lengthened habit had intensified

[023-092]Grandcourt’s want of respect for his companion since that want had been

[023-093]absolute from the beginning, but it had confirmed his sense that he

[023-094]might kick Lush if he chose—only he never did choose to kick any

[023-095]animal, because the act of kicking is a compromising attitude, and a

[023-096]gentleman’s dogs should be kicked for him. He only said things which

[023-097]might have exposed himself to be kicked if his confidant had been a man

[023-098]of independent spirit. But what son of a vicar who has stinted his wife

[023-099]and daughters of calico in order to send his male offspring to Oxford,

[023-100]can keep an independent spirit when he is bent on dining with high

[023-101]discrimination, riding good horses, living generally in the most

[023-102]luxuriant honey-blossomed clover—and all without working? Mr. Lush had

[023-103]passed for a scholar once, and had still a sense of scholarship when he

[023-104]was not trying to remember much of it; but the bachelor’s and other

[023-105]arts which soften manners are a time-honored preparation for sinecures;

[023-106]and Lush’s present comfortable provision was as good a sinecure in not

[023-107]requiring more than the odor of departed learning. He was not

[023-108]unconscious of being held kickable, but he preferred counting that

[023-109]estimate among the peculiarities of Grandcourt’s character, which made

[023-110]one of his incalculable moods or judgments as good as another. Since in

[023-111]his own opinion he had never done a bad action, it did not seem

[023-112]necessary to consider whether he should be likely to commit one if his

[023-113]love of ease required it. Lush’s love of ease was well-satisfied at

[023-114]present, and if his puddings were rolled toward him in the dust, he

[023-115]took the inside bits and found them relishing.

[023-116]This morning, for example, though he had encountered more annoyance

[023-117]than usual, he went to his private sitting-room and played a good hour

[023-118]on the violoncello.

[023-119]CHAPTER XIII.

[023-120]“Philistia, be thou glad of me!”

[023-121]Grandcourt having made up his mind to marry [[Gwendolen-Harleth|Miss Harleth]], showed a

[023-122]power of adapting means to ends. During the next fortnight there was

[023-123]hardly a day on which by some arrangement or other he did not see her,

[023-124]or prove by emphatic attentions that she occupied his thoughts. His

[023-125]cousin, Mrs. Torrington, was now doing the honors of his house, so that

[023-126][[Mrs-Davilow|Mrs. Davilow]] and Gwendolen could be invited to a large party at [[diplow-hall|Diplow]]

[023-127]in which there were many witnesses how the host distinguished the

[023-128]dowerless beauty, and showed no solicitude about the heiress. The

[023-129]world—I mean [[Rex-Gascoigne|Mr. Gascoigne]] and all the families worth speaking of

[023-130]within visiting distance of [[pennicote-rectory|Pennicote]]—felt an assurance on the subject

[023-131]which in the rector’s mind converted itself into a resolution to do his

[023-132]duty by his niece and see that the settlements were adequate. Indeed

[023-133]the wonder to him and Mrs. Davilow was that the offer for which so many

[023-134]suitable occasions presented themselves had not been already made; and

[023-135]in this wonder Grandcourt himself was not without a share. When he had

[023-136]told his resolution to Lush he had thought that the affair would be

[023-137]concluded more quickly, and to his own surprise he had repeatedly

[023-138]promised himself in a morning that he would to-day give Gwendolen the

[023-139]opportunity of accepting him, and had found in the evening that the

[023-140]necessary formality was still unaccomplished. This remarkable fact

[023-141]served to heighten his determination on another day. He had never

[023-142]admitted to himself that Gwendolen might refuse him, but—heaven help

[023-143]us all!—we are often unable to act on our certainties; our objection

[023-144]to a contrary issue (were it possible) is so strong that it rises like

[023-145]a spectral illusion between us and our certainty; we are rationally

[023-146]sure that the blind worm can not bite us mortally, but it would be so

[023-147]intolerable to be bitten, and the creature has a biting look—we

[023-148]decline to handle it.

[023-149]He had asked leave to have a beautiful horse of his brought for

[023-150]Gwendolen to ride. Mrs. Davilow was to accompany her in the carriage,

[023-151]and they were to go to Diplow to lunch, Grandcourt conducting them. It

[023-152]was a fine mid-harvest time, not too warm for a noonday ride of five

[023-153]miles to be delightful; the poppies glowed on the borders of the

[023-154]fields, there was enough breeze to move gently like a social spirit

[023-155]among the ears of uncut corn, and to wing the shadow of a cloud across

[023-156]the soft gray downs; here the sheaves were standing, there the horses

[023-157]were straining their muscles under the last load from a wide space of

[023-158]stubble, but everywhere the green pasture made a broader setting for

[023-159]the corn-fields, and the cattle took their rest under wide branches.

[023-160]The road lay through a bit of country where the dairy-farms looked much

[023-161]as they did in the days of our forefathers—where peace and permanence

[023-162]seemed to find a home away from the busy change that sent the railway

[023-163]train flying in the distance.

[023-164]But the spirit of peace and permanence did not penetrate poor Mrs.

[023-165]Davilow’s mind so as to overcome her habit of uneasy foreboding.

[023-166]Gwendolen and Grandcourt cantering in front of her, and then slackening

[023-167]their pace to a conversational walk till the carriage came up with them

[023-168]again, made a gratifying sight; but it served chiefly to keep up the

[023-169]conflict of hopes and fears about her daughter’s lot. Here was an

[023-170]irresistible opportunity for a lover to speak and put an end to all

[023-171]uncertainties, and Mrs. Davilow could only hope with trembling that

[023-172]Gwendolen’s decision would be favorable. Certainly if Rex’s love had

[023-173]been repugnant to her, Mr. Grandcourt had the advantage of being in

[023-174]complete contrast with Rex; and that he had produced some quite novel

[023-175]impression on her seemed evident in her marked abstinence from

[023-176]satirical observations, nay, her total silence about his

[023-177]characteristics, a silence which Mrs. Davilow did not dare to break.

[023-178]“Is he a man she would be happy with?”—was a question that inevitably

[023-179]arose in the mother’s mind. “Well, perhaps as happy as she would be

[023-180]with any one else—or as most other women are”—was the answer with

[023-181]which she tried to quiet herself; for she could not imagine Gwendolen

[023-182]under the influence of any feeling which would make her satisfied in

[023-183]what we traditionally call “mean circumstances.”

[023-184]Grandcourt’s own thought was looking in the same direction: he wanted

[023-185]to have done with the uncertainty that belonged to his not having

[023-186]spoken. As to any further uncertainty—well, it was something without

[023-187]any reasonable basis, some quality in the air which acted as an

[023-188]irritant to his wishes.
