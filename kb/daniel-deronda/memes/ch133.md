[133-001][[Hans-Meyrick|Hans]] could not but interpret this invitation as a sign of pardon. He

[133-002]had not enough understanding of what [[Mirah-Lapidoth|Mirah]]’s nature had been wrought

[133-003]into by her early experience, to divine how the very strength of her

[133-004]late excitement had made it pass more quickly into the resolute

[133-005]acceptance of pain. When he had said, “If you will let me,” and they

[133-006]went in together, half his grief was gone, and he was spinning a little

[133-007]romance of how his devotion might make him indispensable to Mirah in

[133-008]proportion as [[Daniel-Deronda|Deronda]] gave his devotion elsewhere. This was quite fair,

[133-009]since his friend was provided for according to his own heart; and on

[133-010]the question of [[religion|Judaism]] Hans felt thoroughly fortified:—who ever heard

[133-011]in tale or history that a woman’s love went in the track of her race

[133-012]and religion? Moslem and Jewish damsels were always attracted toward

[133-013]Christians, and now if Mirah’s heart had gone forth too precipitately

[133-014]toward Deronda, here was another case in point. Hans was wont to make

[133-015]merry with his own arguments, to call himself a Giaour, and antithesis

[133-016]the sole clue to events; but he believed a little in what he laughed

[133-017]at. And thus his bird-like hope, constructed on the lightest

[133-018]principles, soared again in spite of heavy circumstances.

[133-019]They found [[Mordecai]] looking singularly happy, holding a closed letter

[133-020]in his hand, his eyes glowing with a quiet triumph which in his

[133-021]emaciated face gave the idea of a conquest over assailing death. After

[133-022]the greeting between him and Hans, Mirah put her arm round her

[133-023]brother’s neck and looked down at the letter in his hand, without the

[133-024]courage to ask about it, though she felt sure that it was the cause of

[133-025]his happiness.

[133-026]“A letter from Daniel Deronda,” said Mordecai, answering her look.

[133-027]“Brief—only saying that he hopes soon to return. Unexpected claims

[133-028]have detained him. The promise of seeing him again is like the bow in

[133-029]the cloud to me,” continued Mordecai, looking at Hans; “and to you it

[133-030]must be a gladness. For who has two friends like him?”

[133-031]While Hans was answering Mirah slipped away to her own room; but not to

[133-032]indulge in any outburst of the passion within her. If the angels, once

[133-033]supposed to watch the toilet of women, had entered the little chamber

[133-034]with her and let her shut the door behind them, they would only have

[133-035]seen her take off her hat, sit down and press her hands against her

[133-036]temples as if she had suddenly reflected that her head ached; then rise

[133-037]to dash cold water on her eyes and brow and hair till her backward

[133-038]curls were full of crystal beads, while she had dried her brow and

[133-039]looked out like a freshly-opened flower from among the dewy tresses of

[133-040]the woodland; then give deep sighs of relief, and putting on her little

[133-041]slippers, sit still after that action for a couple of minutes, which

[133-042]seemed to her so long, so full of things to come, that she rose with an

[133-043]air of recollection, and went down to make tea.

[133-044]Something of the old life had returned. She had been used to remember

[133-045]that she must learn her part, must go to rehearsal, must act and sing

[133-046]in the evening, must hide her feelings from her father; and the more

[133-047]painful her life grew, the more she had been used to hide. The force of

[133-048]her nature had long found its chief action in resolute endurance, and

[133-049]to-day the violence of feeling which had caused the first jet of anger

[133-050]had quickly transformed itself into a steady facing of trouble, the

[133-051]well-known companion of her young years. But while she moved about and

[133-052]spoke as usual, a close observer might have discerned a difference

[133-053]between this apparent calm, which was the effect of restraining energy,

[133-054]and the sweet genuine calm of the months when she first felt a return

[133-055]of her infantine happiness.

[133-056]Those who have been indulged by fortune and have always thought of

[133-057]calamity as what happens to others, feel a blind incredulous rage at

[133-058]the reversal of their lot, and half believe that their wild cries will

[133-059]alter the course of the storm. Mirah felt no such surprise when

[133-060]familiar Sorrow came back from brief absence, and sat down with her

[133-061]according to the old use and wont. And this habit of expecting trouble

[133-062]rather than joy, hindered her from having any persistent belief in

[133-063]opposition to the probabilities which were not merely suggested by

[133-064]Hans, but were supported by her own private knowledge and long-growing

[133-065]presentiment. An attachment between Deronda and [[Gwendolen-Harleth|Mrs. Grandcourt]], to end

[133-066]in their future marriage, had the aspect of a certainty for her

[133-067]feeling. There had been no fault in him: facts had ordered themselves

[133-068]so that there was a tie between him and this woman who belonged to

[133-069]another world than hers and Ezra’s—nay, who seemed another sort of

[133-070]being than Deronda, something foreign that would be a disturbance in

[133-071]his life instead of blending with it. Well, well—but if it could have

[133-072]been deferred so as to make no difference while Ezra was there! She did

[133-073]not know all the momentousness of the relation between Deronda and her

[133-074]brother, but she had seen, and instinctively felt enough to forebode

[133-075]its being incongruous with any close tie to Mrs. [[Henleigh-Grandcourt|Grandcourt]]; at least

[133-076]this was the clothing that Mirah first gave to her mortal repugnance.

[133-077]But in the still, quick action of her consciousness, thoughts went on

[133-078]like changing states of sensation unbroken by her habitual acts; and

[133-079]this inward language soon said distinctly that the mortal repugnance

[133-080]would remain even if Ezra were secured from loss.

[133-081]“What I have read about and sung about and seen acted, is happening to

[133-082]me—this that I am feeling is the love that makes jealousy;” so

[133-083]impartially Mirah summed up the charge against herself. But what

[133-084]difference could this pain of hers make to any one else? It must remain

[133-085]as exclusively her own, and hidden, as her early yearning and devotion

[133-086]to her lost mother. But unlike that devotion, it was something that she

[133-087]felt to be a misfortune of her nature—a discovery that what should

[133-088]have been pure gratitude and reverence had sunk into selfish pain, that

[133-089]the feeling she had hitherto delighted to pour out in words was

[133-090]degraded into something she was ashamed to betray—an absurd longing

[133-091]that she who had received all and given nothing should be of importance

[133-092]where she was of no importance—an angry feeling toward another woman

[133-093]who possessed the good she wanted. But what notion, what vain reliance

[133-094]could it be that had lain darkly within her and was now burning itself

[133-095]into sight as disappointment and jealousy? It was as if her soul had

[133-096]been steeped in poisonous passion by forgotten dreams of deep sleep,

[133-097]and now flamed out in this unaccountable misery. For with her waking

[133-098]reason she had never entertained what seemed the wildly unfitting

[133-099]thought that Deronda could love her. The uneasiness she had felt before

[133-100]had been comparatively vague and easily explained as part of a general

[133-101]regret that he was only a visitant in her and her brother’s world, from

[133-102]which the world where his home lay was as different as a portico with

[133-103]lights and lacqueys was different from the door of a tent, where the

[133-104]only splendor came from the mysterious inaccessible stars. But her

[133-105]feeling was no longer vague: the cause of her pain—the image of Mrs.

[133-106]Grandcourt by Deronda’s side, drawing him farther and farther into the

[133-107]distance, was as definite as pincers on her flesh. In the Psyche-mould

[133-108]of Mirah’s frame there rested a fervid quality of emotion, sometimes

[133-109]rashly supposed to require the bulk of a Cleopatra; her impressions had

[133-110]the thoroughness and tenacity that give to the first selection of

[133-111]passionate feeling the character of a life-long faithfulness. And now a

[133-112]selection had declared itself, which gave love a cruel heart of

[133-113]jealousy: she had been used to a strong repugnance toward certain

[133-114]objects that surrounded her, and to walk inwardly aloof from them while

[133-115]they touched her sense. And now her repugnance concentrated itself on

[133-116]Mrs. Grandcourt, of whom she involuntarily conceived more evil than she

[133-117]knew. “I could bear everything that used to be—but this is worse—this

[133-118]is worse,—I used not to have horrible feelings!” said the poor child

[133-119]in a loud whisper to her pillow. Strange that she should have to pray

[133-120]against any feeling which concerned Deronda!

[133-121]But this conclusion had been reached through an evening spent in

[133-122]attending to Mordecai, whose exaltation of spirit in the prospect of

[133-123]seeing his friend again, disposed him to utter many thoughts aloud to

[133-124]Mirah, though such communication was often interrupted by intervals

[133-125]apparently filled with an inward utterance that animated his eyes and

[133-126]gave an occasional silent action to his lips. One thought especially

[133-127]occupied him.

[133-128]“Seest thou, Mirah,” he said once, after a long silence, “the _Shemah_,

[133-129]wherein we briefly confess the divine Unity, is the chief devotional

[133-130]exercise of the Hebrew; and this made our religion the fundamental

[133-131]religion for the whole world; for the divine Unity embraced as its

[133-132]consequence the ultimate unity of mankind. See, then—the nation which

[133-133]has been scoffed at for its separateness, has given a binding theory to

[133-134]the human race. Now, in complete unity a part possesses the whole as

[133-135]the whole possesses every part: and in this way human life is tending

[133-136]toward the image of the Supreme Unity: for as our life becomes more

[133-137]spiritual by capacity of thought, and joy therein, possession tends to

[133-138]become more universal, being independent of gross material contact; so

[133-139]that in a brief day the soul of man may know in fuller volume the good

[133-140]which has been and is, nay, is to come, than all he could possess in a

[133-141]whole life where he had to follow the creeping paths of the senses. In

[133-142]this moment, my sister, I hold the joy of another’s future within me: a

[133-143]future which these eyes will not see, and which my spirit may not then

[133-144]recognize as mine. I recognize it now, and love it so, that I can lay

[133-145]down this poor life upon its altar and say: ‘Burn, burn indiscernibly

[133-146]into that which shall be, which is my love and not me.’ Dost thou

[133-147]understand, Mirah?”

[133-148]“A little,” said Mirah, faintly, “but my mind is too poor to have felt

[133-149]it.”

[133-150]“And yet,” said Mordecai, rather insistently, “women are specially

[133-151]framed for the love which feels possession in renouncing, and is thus a

[133-152]fit image of what I mean. Somewhere in the later _Midrash_, I think, is

[133-153]the story of a Jewish maiden who loved a Gentile king so well, that

[133-154]this was what she did:—she entered into prison and changed clothes

[133-155]with the woman who was beloved by the king, that she might deliver that

[133-156]woman from death by dying in her stead, and leave the king to be happy

[133-157]in his love which was not for her. This is the surpassing love, that

[133-158]loses self in the object of love.”

[133-159]“No, Ezra, no,” said Mirah, with low-toned intensity, “that was not it.

[133-160]She wanted the king when she was dead to know what she had done, and

[133-161]feel that she was better than the other. It was her strong self,

[133-162]wanting to conquer, that made her die.”

[133-163]Mordecai was silent a little, and then argued,

[133-164]“That might be, Mirah. But if she acted so, believing the king would

[133-165]never know.”

[133-166]“You can make the story so in your mind, Ezra, because you are great,

[133-167]and like to fancy the greatest that could be. But I think it was not

[133-168]really like that. The Jewish girl must have had jealousy in her heart,

[133-169]and she wanted somehow to have the first place in the king’s mind. That

[133-170]is what she would die for.”

[133-171]“My sister, thou hast read too many plays, where the writers delight in

[133-172]showing the human passions as indwelling demons, unmixed with the

[133-173]relenting and devout elements of the soul. Thou judgest by the plays,

[133-174]and not by thy own heart, which is like our mother’s.”
