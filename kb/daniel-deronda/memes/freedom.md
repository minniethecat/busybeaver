# Freedom

Freedom in *[[Daniel-Deronda|Daniel Deronda]]* is [[Gwendolen-Harleth|Gwendolen Harleth]]'s deepest desire and most painful loss. George Eliot examines freedom not as a political abstraction but as a psychological condition — the capacity to act from one's own nature rather than from compulsion.

## Overview

[[Gwendolen-Harleth|Gwendolen]]'s [[reputation|character]] is defined by her "horror of being classed" (013-031) with other dependent women. Her "superior freedom" (014-086) — in laughter, in opinion, in the exercise of her beauty — is her most cherished possession. The novel's tragedy is that this freedom is illusory: [[Gwendolen-Harleth|Gwendolen]] has no economic independence, no intellectual discipline, and no moral framework. Her "freedom" is the freedom of a leaf in the wind.

The novel contrasts [[Gwendolen-Harleth|Gwendolen]]'s false freedom with the genuine freedom of [[reputation|character]]s who have accepted [[duty|obligation]]. [[Daniel-Deronda|Deronda]]'s discovery of his Jewish identity, [[Mirah-Lapidoth|Mirah]]'s commitment to her art, and even [[Catherine-Arrowpoint|Catherine Arrowpoint]]'s choice of [[Herr-Klesmer|Klesmer]] represent freedom through commitment — the paradox at the heart of Eliot's moral vision.

## Role in the Novel

Freedom is the value [[Gwendolen-Harleth|Gwendolen]] invokes to justify every reckless choice. She "was not going to renounce her freedom" (024-010) by marrying, until economic necessity forces her to. Her marriage to [[Henleigh-Grandcourt|Grandcourt]] is the negation of freedom — he controls her movements, her speech, and finally her conscience. The novel traces the path from the false freedom of self-assertion through the bondage of a bad marriage to the genuine freedom of moral self-knowledge.

[[Daniel-Deronda|Deronda]]'s arc inverts this pattern. He begins the novel in apparent freedom — wealthy, unattached, free to travel — but discovers that true freedom requires the acceptance of inheritance and [[duty|obligation]]. "Servitude above freedom" (012-013) becomes his motto: the willing subordination of self to a cause larger than self.

## Expression Through Characters

### Gwendolen Harleth
[[Gwendolen-Harleth|Gwendolen]]'s entire moral arc is a study in the nature of freedom. "She would assert her freedom by doing" (009-162) — act first, consider consequences later. Her marriage to [[Henleigh-Grandcourt|Grandcourt]] teaches her that freedom without moral foundation is slavery to impulse. Her final state — "I will try; I will be better" — is the paradox of freedom: she is freest when she submits to moral [[duty|obligation]].

### Daniel Deronda
[[Daniel-Deronda|Deronda]]'s freedom is the freedom of vocation. He is "free" in the novel's opening — free to travel, free from family [[duty|obligation]]s — but this freedom feels empty. His discovery of his Jewish identity and his acceptance of [[Mordecai]]'s [[vocation|mission]] represent the novel's ideal: freedom as the capacity to choose one's [[duty|obligation]]s.

## Key Passages

> She would assert her freedom by doing whatever seemed to her a defiance of authority. (009-162)

> Her horror of being classed with other women, and compelled to follow a prescribed destiny. (013-031)

> She was not going to renounce her freedom, and allow herself to be disciplined into a condition she despised. (024-010)

> Servitude above freedom; the gentle mind's religion. (012-013)

## Historical Context

The Victorian period was the great age of the "Woman Question" — debates about female education, [[wealth|property]] rights, and political participation. The Married Women's Property Act (1870, 1882) was passed during the years Eliot was thinking about and writing *[[Daniel-Deronda|Daniel Deronda]]*. [[Gwendolen-Harleth|Gwendolen]]'s predicament — economically dependent, legally subordinate to her husband, her "freedom" a social [[art|performance]] — reflects these contemporary realities with painful precision.
