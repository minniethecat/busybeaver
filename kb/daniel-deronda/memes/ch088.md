[088-001]Mab felt herself beginning to cry, and all the three girls held [[Herr-Klesmer|Klesmer]]

[088-002]adorable. Mrs. Meyrick took a long breath.

[088-003]But straightway the frown came again, the long hand, back uppermost,

[088-004]was stretched out in quite a different sense to touch with finger-tip

[088-005]the back of [[Mirah-Lapidoth|Mirah]]’s, and with protruded lip he said,

[088-006]“Not for great tasks. No high roofs. We are no skylarks. We must be

[088-007]modest.” Klesmer paused here. And Mab ceased to think him adorable: “as

[088-008]if Mirah had shown the least sign of conceit!”

[088-009]Mirah was silent, knowing that there was a specific opinion to be

[088-010]waited for, and Klesmer presently went on—“I would not advise—I would

[088-011]not further your [[music|singing]] in any larger space than a private

[088-012]drawing-room. But you will do there. And here in [[london|London]] that is one of

[088-013]the best careers open. Lessons will follow. Will you come and sing at a

[088-014]private concert at my house on Wednesday?”

[088-015]“Oh, I shall be grateful,” said Mirah, putting her hands together

[088-016]devoutly. “I would rather get my bread in that way than by anything

[088-017]more public. I will try to improve. What should I work at most?”

[088-018]Klesmer made a preliminary answer in noises which sounded like words

[088-019]bitten in two and swallowed before they were half out, shaking his

[088-020]fingers the while, before he said, quite distinctly, “I shall introduce

[088-021]you to Astorga: he is the foster-father of good singing and will give

[088-022]you advice.” Then addressing Mrs. Meyrick, he added, “[[Catherine-Arrowpoint|Mrs. Klesmer]] will

[088-023]call before Wednesday, with your permission.”

[088-024]“We shall feel that to be a great kindness,” said Mrs. Meyrick.

[088-025]“You will sing to her,” said Klesmer, turning again to Mirah. “She is a

[088-026]thorough musician, and has a soul with more ears to it than you will

[088-027]often get in a musician. Your singing will satisfy her:

[088-028]‘Vor den Wissenden sich stellen;’

[088-029]you know the rest?”

[088-030]“‘Sicher ist’s in allen Fällen.’”

[088-031]said Mirah, promptly. And Klesmer saying “Schön!” put out his hand

[088-032]again as a good-by.

[088-033]He had certainly chosen the most delicate way of praising Mirah, and

[088-034]the Meyrick girls had now given him all their esteem. But imagine Mab’s

[088-035]feeling when suddenly fixing his eyes on her, he said decisively, “That

[088-036]young lady is musical, I see!” She was a mere blush and sense of

[088-037]scorching.

[088-038]“Yes,” said Mirah, on her behalf. “And she has a touch.”

[088-039]“Oh, please, Mirah—a scramble, not a touch,” said Mab, in anguish,

[088-040]with a horrible fear of what the next thing might be: this dreadful

[088-041]divining personage—evidently Satan in gray trousers—might order her

[088-042]to sit down to the piano, and her heart was like molten wax in the

[088-043]midst of her. But this was cheap payment for her amazed joy when

[088-044]Klesmer said benignantly, turning to Mrs. Meyrick, “Will she like to

[088-045]accompany Miss [[Lapidoth]] and hear the [[art|music]] on Wednesday?”

[088-046]“There could hardly be a greater pleasure for her,” said Mrs. Meyrick.

[088-047]“She will be most glad and grateful.”

[088-048]Thereupon Klesmer bowed round to the three sisters more grandly than

[088-049]they had ever been bowed to before. Altogether it was an amusing

[088-050]picture—the little room with so much of its diagonal taken up in

[088-051]Klesmer’s magnificent bend to the small feminine figures like images a

[088-052]little less than life-size, the grave Holbein faces on the walls, as

[088-053]many as were not otherwise occupied, looking hard at this stranger who

[088-054]by his face seemed a dignified contemporary of their own, but whose

[088-055]garments seemed a deplorable mockery of the human form.

[088-056]Mrs. Meyrick could not help going out of the room with Klesmer and

[088-057]closing the door behind her. He understood her, and said with a

[088-058]frowning nod,

[088-059]“She will do: if she doesn’t attempt too much and her voice holds out,

[088-060]she can make an income. I know that is the great point: [[Daniel-Deronda|Deronda]] told

[088-061]me. You are taking care of her. She looks like a good girl.”

[088-062]“She is an angel,” said the warm-hearted woman.

[088-063]“No,” said Klesmer, with a playful nod; “she is a pretty Jewess: the

[088-064]angels must not get the credit of her. But I think she has found a

[088-065]guardian angel,” he ended, bowing himself out in this amiable way.

[088-066]The four young creatures had looked at each other mutely till the door

[088-067]banged and Mrs. Meyrick re-entered. Then there was an explosion. Mab

[088-068]clapped her hands and danced everywhere inconveniently; Mrs. Meyrick

[088-069]kissed Mirah and blessed her; Amy said emphatically, “We can never get

[088-070]her a new dress before Wednesday!” and Kate exclaimed, “Thank heaven my

[088-071]table is not knocked over!”

[088-072]Mirah had reseated herself on the music-stool without speaking, and the

[088-073]tears were rolling down her cheeks as she looked at her friends.

[088-074]“Now, now, Mab!” said Mrs. Meyrick; “come and sit down reasonably and

[088-075]let us talk?”

[088-076]“Yes, let us talk,” said Mab, cordially, coming back to her low seat

[088-077]and caressing her knees. “I am beginning to feel large again. [[Hans-Meyrick|Hans]] said

[088-078]he was coming this afternoon. I wish he had been here—only there would

[088-079]have been no room for him. Mirah, what are you looking sad for?”

[088-080]“I am too happy,” said Mirah. “I feel so full of gratitude to you all;

[088-081]and he was so very kind.”

[088-082]“Yes, at last,” said Mab, sharply. “But he might have said something

[088-083]encouraging sooner. I thought him dreadfully ugly when he sat frowning,

[088-084]and only said, ‘_Con_tinue.’ I hated him all the long way from the top

[088-085]of his hair to the toe of his polished boot.”

[088-086]“Nonsense, Mab; he has a splendid profile,” said Kate.

[088-087]“_Now_, but not _then_. I cannot bear people to keep their minds

[088-088]bottled up for the sake of letting them off with a pop. They seem to

[088-089]grudge making you happy unless they can make you miserable beforehand.

[088-090]However, I forgive him everything,” said Mab, with a magnanimous air,

[088-091]“but he has invited me. I wonder why he fixed on me as the musical one?

[088-092]Was it because I have a bulging forehead, ma, and peep from under it

[088-093]like a newt from under a stone?”

[088-094]“It was your way of listening to the singing, child,” said Mrs.

[088-095]Meyrick. “He has magic spectacles and sees everything through them,

[088-096]depend upon it. But what was that German quotation you were so ready

[088-097]with, Mirah—you learned puss?”

[088-098]“Oh, that was not learning,” said Mirah, her tearful face breaking into

[088-099]an amused smile. “I said it so many times for a lesson. It means that

[088-100]it is safer to do anything—singing or anything else—before those who

[088-101]know and understand all about it.”

[088-102]“That was why you were not one bit frightened, I suppose,” said Amy.

[088-103]“But now, what we have to talk about is a dress for you on Wednesday.”

[088-104]“I don’t want anything better than this black merino,” said Mirah,

[088-105]rising to show the effect. “Some white gloves and some new _bottines_.”

[088-106]She put out her little foot, clad in the famous felt slipper.

[088-107]“There comes Hans,” said Mrs. Meyrick. “Stand still, and let us hear

[088-108]what he says about the dress. Artists are the best people to consult

[088-109]about such things.”

[088-110]“You don’t consult me, ma,” said Kate, lifting up her eyebrow with a

[088-111]playful complainingness. “I notice mothers are like the people I deal

[088-112]with—the girls’ doings are always priced low.”

[088-113]“My dear child, the boys are such a trouble—we could never put up with

[088-114]them, if we didn’t make believe they were worth more,” said Mrs.

[088-115]Meyrick, just as her boy entered. “Hans, we want your opinion about

[088-116]Mirah’s dress. A great event has happened. Klesmer has been here, and

[088-117]she is going to sing at his house on Wednesday among grand people. She

[088-118]thinks this dress will do.”

[088-119]“Let me see,” said Hans. Mirah in her childlike way turned toward him

[088-120]to be looked at; and he, going to a little further distance, knelt with

[088-121]one knee on a hassock to survey her.

[088-122]“This would be thought a very good stage-dress for me,” she said,

[088-123]pleadingly, “in a part where I was to come on as a poor Jewess and sing

[088-124]to fashionable Christians.”

[088-125]“It would be effective,” said Hans, with a considering air; “it would

[088-126]stand out well among the fashionable _chiffons_.”

[088-127]“But you ought not to claim all the poverty on your side, Mirah,” said

[088-128]Amy. “There are plenty of poor Christians and dreadfully rich Jews and

[088-129]fashionable Jewesses.”

[088-130]“I didn’t mean any harm,” said Mirah. “Only I have been used to

[088-131]thinking about my dress for parts in plays. And I almost always had a

[088-132]part with a plain dress.”

[088-133]“That makes me think it questionable,” said Hans, who had suddenly

[088-134]become as fastidious and conventional on this occasion as he had

[088-135]thought Deronda was, apropos of the Berenice-pictures. “It looks a

[088-136]little too theatrical. We must not make you a _rôle_ of the poor

[088-137]Jewess—or of being a Jewess at all.” Hans had a secret desire to

[088-138]neutralize the Jewess in private life, which he was in danger of not

[088-139]keeping secret.

[088-140]“But it is what I am really. I am not pretending anything. I shall

[088-141]never be anything else,” said Mirah. “I always feel myself a Jewess.”

[088-142]“But we can’t feel that about you,” said Hans, with a devout look.

[088-143]“What does it signify whether a perfect woman is a Jewess or not?”

[088-144]“That is your kind way of praising me; I never was praised so before,”

[088-145]said Mirah, with a smile, which was rather maddening to Hans and made

[088-146]him feel still more of a cosmopolitan.

[088-147]“People don’t think of me as a British Christian,” he said, his face

[088-148]creasing merrily. “They think of me as an imperfectly handsome young

[088-149]man and an unpromising painter.”

[088-150]“But you are wandering from the dress,” said Amy. “If that will not do,

[088-151]how are we to get another before Wednesday? and to-morrow Sunday?”

[088-152]“Indeed this will do,” said Mirah, entreatingly. “It is all real, you

[088-153]know,” here she looked at Hans—“even if it seemed theatrical. Poor

[088-154]Berenice sitting on the ruins—any one might say that was theatrical,

[088-155]but I know that this is just what she would do.”

[088-156]“I am a scoundrel,” said Hans, overcome by this misplaced trust. “That

[088-157]is my invention. Nobody knows that she did that. Shall you forgive me

[088-158]for not saying so before?”

[088-159]“Oh, yes,” said Mirah, after a momentary pause of surprise. “You knew

[088-160]it was what she would be sure to do—a Jewess who had not been

[088-161]faithful—who had done what she did and was penitent. She could have no

[088-162]joy but to afflict herself; and where else would she go? I think it is

[088-163]very beautiful that you should enter so into what a Jewess would feel.”

[088-164]“The Jewesses of that time sat on ruins,” said Hans, starting up with a

[088-165]sense of being checkmated. “That makes them convenient for pictures.”

[088-166]“But the dress—the dress,” said Amy; “is it settled?”

[088-167]“Yes; is it not?” said Mirah, looking doubtfully at Mrs. Meyrick, who

[088-168]in her turn looked up at her son, and said, “What do you think, Hans?”

[088-169]“That dress will not do,” said Hans, decisively. “She is not going to

[088-170]sit on ruins. You must jump into a cab with her, little mother, and go

[088-171]to Regent Street. It’s plenty of time to get anything you like—a black

[088-172]silk dress such as ladies wear. She must not be taken for an object of

[088-173]charity. She has talents to make people indebted to her.”

[088-174]“I think it is what Mr. Deronda would like—for her to have a handsome

[088-175]dress,” said Mrs. Meyrick, deliberating.

[088-176]“Of course it is,” said Hans, with some sharpness. “You may take my

[088-177]word for what a gentleman would feel.”

[088-178]“I wish to do what Mr. Deronda would like me to do,” said Mirah,

[088-179]gravely, seeing that Mrs. Meyrick looked toward her; and Hans, turning

[088-180]on his heel, went to Kate’s table and took up one of her drawings as if

[088-181]his interest needed a new direction.
