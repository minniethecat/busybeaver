[007-001]It would be a little hard to blame the rector of [[pennicote-rectory|Pennicote]] that in the

[007-002]course of looking at things from every point of view, he looked at

[007-003][[Gwendolen-Harleth|Gwendolen]] as a girl likely to make a brilliant marriage. Why should he

[007-004]be expected to differ from his contemporaries in this matter, and wish

[007-005]his niece a worse end of her charming maidenhood than they would

[007-006]approve as the best possible? It is rather to be set down to his credit

[007-007]that his feelings on the subject were entirely good-natured. And in

[007-008]considering the relation of means to ends, it would have been mere

[007-009]folly to have been guided by the exceptional and idyllic—to have

[007-010]recommended that Gwendolen should wear a gown as shabby as Griselda’s

[007-011]in order that a marquis might fall in love with her, or to have

[007-012]insisted that since a fair maiden was to be sought, she should keep

[007-013]herself out of the way. [[Rex-Gascoigne|Mr. Gascoigne]]’s calculations were of the kind

[007-014]called rational, and he did not even think of getting a too frisky

[007-015]horse in order that Gwendolen might be threatened with an accident and

[007-016]be rescued by a man of property. He wished his niece well, and he meant

[007-017]her to be seen to advantage in the best society of the neighborhood.

[007-018]Her uncle’s intention fell in perfectly with Gwendolen’s own wishes.

[007-019]But let no one suppose that she also contemplated a brilliant marriage

[007-020]as the direct end of her witching the world with her grace on

[007-021]horseback, or with any other accomplishment. That she was to be married

[007-022]some time or other she would have felt obliged to admit; and that her

[007-023]marriage would not be of a middling kind, such as most girls were

[007-024]contented with, she felt quietly, unargumentatively sure. But her

[007-025]thoughts never dwelt on marriage as the fulfillment of her ambition;

[007-026]the dramas in which she imagined herself a heroine were not wrought up

[007-027]to that close. To be very much sued or hopelessly sighed for as a bride

[007-028]was indeed an indispensable and agreeable guarantee of womanly power;

[007-029]but to become a wife and wear all the domestic fetters of that

[007-030]condition, was on the whole a vexatious necessity. Her observation of

[007-031]matrimony had inclined her to think it rather a dreary state in which a

[007-032]woman could not do what she liked, had more children than were

[007-033]desirable, was consequently dull, and became irrevocably immersed in

[007-034]humdrum. Of course marriage was social promotion; she could not look

[007-035]forward to a single life; but promotions have sometimes to be taken

[007-036]with bitter herbs—a peerage will not quite do instead of leadership to

[007-037]the man who meant to lead; and this delicate-limbed sylph of twenty

[007-038]meant to lead. For such passions dwell in feminine breasts also. In

[007-039]Gwendolen’s, however, they dwelt among strictly feminine furniture, and

[007-040]had no disturbing reference to the advancement of learning or the

[007-041]balance of the constitution; her knowledge being such as with no sort

[007-042]of standing-room or length of lever could have been expected to move

[007-043]the world. She meant to do what was pleasant to herself in a striking

[007-044]manner; or rather, whatever she could do so as to strike others with

[007-045]admiration and get in that reflected way a more ardent sense of living,

[007-046]seemed pleasant to her fancy.

[007-047]“Gwendolen will not rest without having the world at her feet,” said

[007-048]Miss Merry, the meek governess: hyperbolical words which have long come

[007-049]to carry the most moderate meanings; for who has not heard of private

[007-050]persons having the world at their feet in the shape of some half-dozen

[007-051]items of flattering regard generally known in a genteel suburb? And

[007-052]words could hardly be too wide or vague to indicate the prospect that

[007-053]made a hazy largeness about poor Gwendolen on the heights of her young

[007-054]self-exultation. Other people allowed themselves to be made slaves of,

[007-055]and to have their lives blown hither and thither like empty ships in

[007-056]which no will was present. It was not to be so with her; she would no

[007-057]longer be sacrificed to creatures worth less than herself, but would

[007-058]make the very best of the chances that life offered her, and conquer

[007-059]circumstances by her exceptional cleverness. Certainly, to be settled

[007-060]at [[Offendene]], with the notice of Lady Brackenshaw, the [[the-archery-club|archery club]],

[007-061]and invitations to dine with the Arrowpoints, as the highest lights in

[007-062]her scenery, was not a position that seemed to offer remarkable

[007-063]chances; but Gwendolen’s confidence lay chiefly in herself. She felt

[007-064]well equipped for the mastery of life. With regard to much in her lot

[007-065]hitherto, she held herself rather hardly dealt with, but as to her

[007-066]“education,” she would have admitted that it had left her under no

[007-067]disadvantages. In the school-room her quick mind had taken readily that

[007-068]strong starch of unexplained rules and disconnected facts which saves

[007-069]ignorance from any painful sense of limpness; and what remained of all

[007-070]things knowable, she was conscious of being sufficiently acquainted

[007-071]with through novels, plays and poems. [[About]] her French and [[art|music]], the

[007-072]two justifying accomplishments of a young lady, she felt no ground for

[007-073]uneasiness; and when to all these qualifications, negative and

[007-074]positive, we add the spontaneous sense of capability some happy persons

[007-075]are born with, so that any subject they turn their attention to

[007-076]impresses them with their own power of forming a correct judgment on

[007-077]it, who can wonder if Gwendolen felt ready to manage her own destiny?

[007-078]There were many subjects in the world—perhaps the majority—in which

[007-079]she felt no interest, because they were stupid; for subjects are apt to

[007-080]appear stupid to the young as light seems dull to the old; but she

[007-081]would not have felt at all helpless in relation to them if they had

[007-082]turned up in conversation. It must be remembered that no one had

[007-083]disputed her power or her general superiority. As on the arrival at

[007-084]Offendene, so always, the first thought of those about her had been,

[007-085]what will Gwendolen think?—if the footman trod heavily in creaking

[007-086]boots, or if the laundress’s work was unsatisfactory, the maid said,

[007-087]“This will never do for Miss Harleth”; if the wood smoked in the

[007-088]bedroom fire-place, [[Mrs-Davilow|Mrs. Davilow]], whose own weak eyes suffered much from

[007-089]this inconvenience, spoke apologetically of it to Gwendolen. If, when

[007-090]they were under the stress of traveling, she did not appear at the

[007-091]breakfast table till every one else had finished, the only question

[007-092]was, how Gwendolen’s coffee and toast should still be of the hottest

[007-093]and crispest; and when she appeared with her freshly-brushed

[007-094]light-brown hair streaming backward and awaiting her mamma’s hand to

[007-095]coil it up, her large brown eyes glancing bright as a wave-washed onyx

[007-096]from under their long lashes, it was always she herself who had to be

[007-097]tolerant—to beg that Alice who sat waiting on her would not stick up

[007-098]her shoulders in that frightful manner, and that Isabel, instead of

[007-099]pushing up to her and asking questions, would go away to Miss Merry.

[007-100]Always she was the princess in exile, who in time of famine was to have

[007-101]her breakfast-roll made of the finest-bolted flour from the seven thin

[007-102]ears of wheat, and in a general decampment was to have her silver fork

[007-103]kept out of the baggage. How was this to be accounted for? The answer

[007-104]may seem to lie quite on the surface:—in her beauty, a certain

[007-105]unusualness about her, a decision of will which made itself felt in her

[007-106]graceful movements and clear unhesitating tones, so that if she came

[007-107]into the room on a rainy day when everybody else was flaccid and the

[007-108]use of things in general was not apparent to them, there seemed to be a

[007-109]sudden, sufficient reason for keeping up the forms of life; and even

[007-110]the waiters at hotels showed the more alacrity in doing away with

[007-111]crumbs and creases and dregs with struggling flies in them. This potent

[007-112]charm, added to the fact that she was the eldest daughter, toward whom

[007-113]her mamma had always been in an apologetic state of mind for the evils

[007-114]brought on her by a step-father, may seem so full a reason for

[007-115]Gwendolen’s domestic empire, that to look for any other would be to ask

[007-116]the reason of daylight when the sun is shining. But beware of arriving

[007-117]at conclusions without comparison. I remember having seen the same

[007-118]assiduous, apologetic attention awarded to persons who were not at all

[007-119]beautiful or unusual, whose firmness showed itself in no very graceful

[007-120]or euphonious way, and who were not eldest daughters with a tender,

[007-121]timid mother, compunctious at having subjected them to inconveniences.

[007-122]Some of them were a very common sort of men. And the only point of

[007-123]resemblance among them all was a strong determination to have what was

[007-124]pleasant, with a total fearlessness in making themselves disagreeable

[007-125]or dangerous when they did not get it. Who is so much cajoled and

[007-126]served with trembling by the weak females of a household as the

[007-127]unscrupulous male—capable, if he has not free way at home, of going

[007-128]and doing worse elsewhere? Hence I am forced to doubt whether even

[007-129]without her potent charm and peculiar filial position Gwendolen might

[007-130]not still have played the queen in exile, if only she had kept her

[007-131]inborn energy of egoistic desire, and her power of inspiring fear as to

[007-132]what she might say or do. However, she had the charm, and those who

[007-133]feared her were also fond of her; the fear and the fondness being

[007-134]perhaps both heightened by what may be called the iridescence of her

[007-135]character—the play of various, nay, contrary tendencies. For Macbeth’s

[007-136]rhetoric about the impossibility of being many opposite things in the

[007-137]same moment, referred to the clumsy necessities of action and not to

[007-138]the subtler possibilities of feeling. We cannot speak a loyal word and

[007-139]be meanly silent; we cannot kill and not kill in the same moment; but a

[007-140]moment is wide enough for the loyal and mean desire, for the outlash of

[007-141]a murderous thought and the sharp backward stroke of repentance.

[007-144]Values itself so highly, that to her

[007-145]All matter else seems weak.”

[007-146]—_Much Ado About Nothing._

[007-147]Gwendolen’s reception in the neighborhood fulfilled her uncle’s

[007-148]expectations. From Brackenshaw Castle to the Firs at Wanchester, where

[007-149]Mr. Quallon the banker kept a generous house, she was welcomed with

[007-150]manifest admiration, and even those ladies who did not quite like her,

[007-151]felt a comfort in having a new, striking girl to invite; for hostesses

[007-152]who entertain much must make up their parties as ministers make up

[007-153]their cabinets, on grounds other than personal liking. Then, in order

[007-154]to have Gwendolen as a guest, it was not necessary to ask any one who

[007-155]was disagreeable, for Mrs. Davilow always made a quiet, picturesque

[007-156]figure as a chaperon, and Mr. Gascoigne was everywhere in request for

[007-157]his own sake.

[007-158]Among the houses where Gwendolen was not quite liked, and yet invited,

[007-159]was [[quetcham-hall|Quetcham Hall]]. One of her first invitations was to a large

[007-160]dinner-party there, which made a sort of general introduction for her

[007-161]to the society of the neighborhood; for in a select party of thirty and

[007-162]of well-composed proportions as to age, few visitable families could be

[007-163]entirely left out. No youthful figure there was comparable to

[007-164]Gwendolen’s as she passed through the long suite of rooms adorned with

[007-165]light and flowers, and, visible at first as a slim figure floating

[007-166]along in white drapery, approached through one wide doorway after

[007-167]another into fuller illumination and definiteness. She had never had

[007-168]that sort of promenade before, and she felt exultingly that it befitted

[007-169]her: any one looking at her for the first time might have supposed that

[007-170]long galleries and lackeys had always been a matter of course in her

[007-171]life; while her cousin Anna, who was really more familiar with these

[007-172]things, felt almost as much embarrassed as a rabbit suddenly deposited

[007-173]in that well-lit-space.
