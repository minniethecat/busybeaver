[128-001]“Yes, I should have known you,” said [[Daniel-Deronda|Deronda]], mournfully. “The outside

[128-002]change is not great. I should have seen at once that it was you, and

[128-003]that you had gone through some great sorrow.”

[128-004]“Don’t wish now that you had never seen me; don’t wish that,” said

[128-005][[Gwendolen-Harleth|Gwendolen]], imploringly, while the tears gathered.

[128-006]“I should despise myself for wishing it,” said Deronda. “How could I

[128-007]know what I was wishing? We must find our duties in what comes to us,

[128-008]not in what we imagine might have been. If I took to foolish wishing of

[128-009]that sort, I should wish—not that I had never seen you, but that I had

[128-010]been able to save you from this.”

[128-011]“You have saved me from worse,” said Gwendolen, in a sobbing voice. “I

[128-012]should have been worse if it had not been for you. If you had not been

[128-013]good, I should have been more wicked than I am.”

[128-014]“It will be better for me to go now,” said Deronda, worn in spirit by

[128-015]the perpetual strain of this scene. “Remember what we said of your

[128-016]task—to get well and calm before other friends come.”

[128-017]He rose as he spoke, and she gave him her hand submissively. But when

[128-018]he had left her she sank on her knees, in hysterical crying. The

[128-019]distance between them was too great. She was a banished soul—beholding

[128-020]a possible life which she had sinned herself away from.

[128-021]She was found in this way, crushed on the floor. Such grief seemed

[128-022]natural in a poor lady whose husband had been drowned in her presence.

[128-023]BOOK VIII.—FRUIT AND SEED.

[128-024]CHAPTER LVIII.

[128-025]“Much adoe there was, God wot;

[128-026]He wold love and she wold not.”

[128-027]—NICHOLAS BRETON.

[128-028]Extension, we know, is a very imperfect measure of things; and the

[128-029]length of the sun’s journeying can no more tell us how life has

[128-030]advanced than the acreage of a field can tell us what growths may be

[128-031]active within it. A man may go south, and, stumbling over a bone, may

[128-032]meditate upon it till he has found a new starting-point for anatomy; or

[128-033]eastward, and discover a new key to language telling a new story of

[128-034]races; or he may head an expedition that opens new continental

[128-035]pathways, get himself maimed in body, and go through a whole heroic

[128-036]poem of resolve and endurance; and at the end of a few months he may

[128-037]come back to find his neighbors grumbling at the same parish grievance

[128-038]as before, or to see the same elderly gentleman treading the pavement

[128-039]in discourse with himself, shaking his head after the same percussive

[128-040]butcher’s boy, and pausing at the same shop-window to look at the same

[128-041]prints. If the swiftest thinking has about the pace of a greyhound, the

[128-042]slowest must be supposed to move, like the limpet, by an apparent

[128-043]sticking, which after a good while is discerned to be a slight

[128-044]progression. Such differences are manifest in the variable intensity

[128-045]which we call human experience, from the revolutionary rush of change

[128-046]which makes a new inner and outer life, to that quiet recurrence of the

[128-047]familiar, which has no other epochs than those of hunger and the

[128-048]heavens.

[128-049]Something of this contrast was seen in the year’s experience which had

[128-050]turned the brilliant, self-confident Gwendolen Harleth of the Archery

[128-051]Meeting into the crushed penitent impelled to confess her unworthiness

[128-052]where it would have been her happiness to be held worthy; while it had

[128-053]left her family in [[pennicote-rectory|Pennicote]] without deeper change than that of some

[128-054]outward habits, and some adjustment of prospects and intentions to

[128-055]reduced income, fewer visits, and fainter compliments. The rectory was

[128-056]as pleasant a home as before: and the red and pink peonies on the lawn,

[128-057]the rows of hollyhocks by the hedges, had bloomed as well this year as

[128-058]last: the rector maintained his cheerful confidence in the good will of

[128-059]patrons and his resolution to deserve it by diligence in the

[128-060]fulfillment of his duties, whether patrons were likely to hear of it or

[128-061]not; doing nothing solely with an eye to promotion except, perhaps, the

[128-062]writing of two ecclesiastical articles, which having no signature, were

[128-063]attributed to some one else, except by the patrons who had a special

[128-064]copy sent them, and these certainly knew the author but did not read

[128-065]the articles. The rector, however, chewed no poisonous cud of suspicion

[128-066]on this point: he made marginal notes on his own copies to render them

[128-067]a more interesting loan, and was gratified that the Archdeacon and

[128-068]other authorities had nothing to say against the general tenor of his

[128-069]argument. Peaceful authorship!—living in the air of the fields and

[128-070]downs, and not in the thrice-breathed breath of criticism—bringing no

[128-071]Dantesque leanness; rather, assisting nutrition by complacency, and

[128-072]perhaps giving a more suffusive sense of achievement than the

[128-073]production of a whole _Divina Commedia_. Then there was the father’s

[128-074]recovered delight in his favorite son, which was a happiness

[128-075]outweighing the loss of eighteen hundred a year. Of whatever nature

[128-076]might be the hidden change wrought in [[Rex-Gascoigne|Rex]] by the disappointment of his

[128-077]first love, it was apparently quite secondary to that evidence of more

[128-078]serious ambition which dated from the family misfortune; indeed, Mr.

[128-079]Gascoigne was inclined to regard the little affair which had caused him

[128-080]so much anxiety the year before as an evaporation of superfluous

[128-081]moisture, a kind of finish to the baking process which the human dough

[128-082]demands. Rex had lately come down for a summer visit to the rectory,

[128-083]bringing Anna home, and while he showed nearly the old liveliness with

[128-084]his brothers and sisters, he continued in his holiday the habits of the

[128-085]eager student, rising early in the morning and shutting himself up

[128-086]early in the evenings to carry on a fixed course of study.

[128-087]“You don’t repent the choice of the law as a profession, Rex?” said his

[128-088]father.

[128-089]“There is no profession I would choose before it,” said Rex. “I should

[128-090]like to end my life as a first-rate judge, and help to draw up a code.

[128-091]I reverse the famous dictum. I should say, ‘Give me something to do

[128-092]with making the laws, and let who will make the songs.’”

[128-093]“You will have to stow in an immense amount of rubbish, I

[128-094]suppose—that’s the worst of it,” said the rector.

[128-095]“I don’t see that law-rubbish is worse than any other sort. It is not

[128-096]so bad as the rubbishy literature that people choke their minds with.

[128-097]It doesn’t make one so dull. Our wittiest men have often been lawyers.

[128-098]Any orderly way of looking at things as cases and evidence seems to me

[128-099]better than a perpetual wash of odds and ends bearing on nothing in

[128-100]particular. And then, from a higher point of view, the foundations and

[128-101]the growth of law make the most interesting aspects of philosophy and

[128-102]history. Of course there will be a good deal that is troublesome,

[128-103]drudging, perhaps exasperating. But the great prizes in life can’t be

[128-104]won easily—I see that.”

[128-105]“Well, my boy, the best augury of a man’s success in his profession is

[128-106]that he thinks it the finest in the world. But I fancy it so with most

[128-107]work when a man goes into it with a will. Brewitt, the blacksmith, said

[128-108]to me the other day that his ’prentice had no mind to his trade; ‘and

[128-109]yet, sir,’ said Brewitt, ‘what would a young fellow have if he doesn’t

[128-110]like the blacksmithing?”

[128-111]The rector cherished a fatherly delight, which he allowed to escape him

[128-112]only in moderation. Warham, who had gone to India, he had easily borne

[128-113]parting with, but Rex was that romance of later life which a man

[128-114]sometimes finds in a son whom he recognizes as superior to himself,

[128-115]picturing a future eminence for him according to a variety of famous

[128-116]examples. It was only to his wife that he said with decision: “Rex will

[128-117]be a distinguished man, Nancy, I am sure of it—as sure as Paley’s

[128-118]father was about his son.”

[128-119]“Was Paley an old bachelor?” said Mrs. Gascoigne.

[128-120]“That is hardly to the point, my dear,” said the rector, who did not

[128-121]remember that irrelevant detail. And Mrs. Gascoigne felt that she had

[128-122]spoken rather weakly.

[128-123]This quiet trotting of time at the rectory was shared by the group who

[128-124]had exchanged the faded dignity of [[Offendene]] for the low white house

[128-125]not a mile off, well enclosed with evergreens, and known to the

[128-126]villagers, as “Jodson’s.” [[Mrs-Davilow|Mrs. Davilow]]’s delicate face showed only a

[128-127]slight deepening of its mild melancholy, her hair only a few more

[128-128]silver lines, in consequence of the last year’s trials; the four girls

[128-129]had bloomed out a little from being less in the shade; and the good

[128-130]Jocosa preserved her serviceable neutrality toward the pleasures and

[128-131]glories of the world as things made for those who were not “in a

[128-132]situation.”

[128-133]The low narrow drawing-room, enlarged by two quaint projecting windows,

[128-134]with lattices wide open on a July afternoon to the scent of monthly

[128-135]roses, the faint murmurs of the garden, and the occasional rare sound

[128-136]of hoofs and wheels seeming to clarify the succeeding silence, made

[128-137]rather a crowded, lively scene, Rex and Anna being added to the usual

[128-138]group of six. Anna, always a favorite with her younger cousins, had

[128-139]much to tell of her new experience, and the acquaintances she had made

[128-140]in [[london|London]], and when on her first visit she came alone, many questions

[128-141]were asked her about Gwendolen’s house in Grosvenor Square, what

[128-142]Gwendolen herself had said, and what any one else had said about

[128-143]Gwendolen. Had Anna been to see Gwendolen after she had known about the

[128-144]yacht? No:—an answer which left speculation free concerning everything

[128-145]connected with that interesting unknown vessel beyond the fact that

[128-146]Gwendolen had written just before she set out to say that Mr.

[128-147][[Henleigh-Grandcourt|Grandcourt]] and she were going yachting on the Mediterranean, and again

[128-148]from Marseilles to say that she was sure to like the yachting, the

[128-149]cabins were very elegant, and she would probably not send another

[128-150]letter till she had written quite a long diary filled with _dittos_.

[128-151]Also, this movement of Mr. and Mrs. Grandcourt had been mentioned in

[128-152]“the newspaper;” so that altogether this new phase of Gwendolen’s

[128-153]exalted life made a striking part of the sisters’ romance, the

[128-154]book-devouring Isabel throwing in a corsair or two to make an adventure

[128-155]that might end well.

[128-156]But when Rex was present, the girls, according to instructions, never

[128-157]started this fascinating topic, and to-day there had only been animated

[128-158]descriptions of the Meyricks and their extraordinary Jewish friends,

[128-159]which caused some astonished questioning from minds to which the idea

[128-160]of live Jews, out of a book, suggested a difference deep enough to be

[128-161]almost zoological, as of a strange race in Pliny’s Natural History that

[128-162]might sleep under the shade of its own ears. Bertha could not imagine

[128-163]what Jews believed now; and she had a dim idea that they rejected the

[128-164]Old Testament since it proved the New; Miss Merry thought that [[Mirah-Lapidoth|Mirah]]

[128-165]and her brother could “never have been properly argued with,” and the

[128-166]amiable Alice did not mind what the Jews believed, she was sure she

[128-167]“couldn’t bear them.” Mrs. Davilow corrected her by saying that the

[128-168]great Jewish families who were in society were quite what they ought to

[128-169]be both in London and [[paris|Paris]], but admitted that the commoner unconverted

[128-170]Jews were objectionable; and Isabel asked whether Mirah talked just as

[128-171]they did, or whether you might be with her and not find out that she

[128-172]was a Jewess.

[128-173]Rex, who had no partisanship with the Israelites, having made a

[128-174]troublesome acquaintance with the minutiae of their ancient history in

[128-175]the form of “cram,” was amusing himself by playfully exaggerating the

[128-176]notion of each speaker, while Anna begged them all to understand that

[128-177]he was only joking, when the laughter was interrupted by the bringing

[128-178]in of a letter for Mrs. Davilow. A messenger had run with it in great

[128-179]haste from the rectory. It enclosed a telegram, and as Mrs. Davilow

[128-180]read and re-read it in silence and agitation, all eyes were turned on

[128-181]her with anxiety, but no one dared to speak. Looking up at last and

[128-182]seeing the young faces “painted with fear,” she remembered that they

[128-183]might be imagining something worse than the truth, something like her

[128-184]own first dread which made her unable to understand what was written,
