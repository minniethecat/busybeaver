[143-001]“You laughed at the mystery of my journey to Italy, [[Hans-Meyrick|Hans]],” he began.

[143-002]“It was for an object that touched my happiness at the very roots. I

[143-003]had never known anything about my parents, and I really went to [[genoa|Genoa]]

[143-004]to meet my mother. My father has been long dead—died when I was an

[143-005]infant. My mother was the daughter of an eminent Jew; my father was her

[143-006]cousin. Many things had caused me to think of this origin as almost a

[143-007]probability before I set out. I was so far prepared for the result that

[143-008]I was glad of it—glad to find myself a Jew.”

[143-009]“You must not expect me to look surprised, [[Daniel-Deronda|Deronda]],” said Hans, who had

[143-010]changed his attitude, laying one leg across the other and examining the

[143-011]heel of his slipper.

[143-012]“You knew it?”

[143-013]“My mother told me. She went to the house the morning after you had

[143-014]been there—brother and sister both told her. You may imagine we can’t

[143-015]rejoice as they do. But whatever you are glad of, I shall come to be

[143-016]glad of in the end—_when_ exactly the end may be I can’t predict,”

[143-017]said Hans, speaking in a low tone, which was as usual with him as it

[143-018]was to be out of humor with his lot, and yet bent on making no fuss

[143-019]about it.

[143-020]“I quite understand that you can’t share my feeling,” said Deronda;

[143-021]“but I could not let silence lie between us on what casts quite a new

[143-022]light over my future. I have taken up some of [[Mordecai]]’s ideas, and I

[143-023]mean to try and carry them out, so far as one man’s efforts can go. I

[143-024]dare say I shall by and by travel to the East and be away for some

[143-025]years.”

[143-026]Hans said nothing, but rose, seized his palette and began to work his

[143-027]brush on it, standing before his picture with his back to Deronda, who

[143-028]also felt himself at a break in his path embarrassed by Hans’s

[143-029]embarrassment.

[143-030]Presently Hans said, again speaking low, and without turning, “Excuse

[143-031]the question, but does [[Gwendolen-Harleth|Mrs. Grandcourt]] know of all this?”

[143-032]“No; and I must beg of you, Hans,” said Deronda, rather angrily, “to

[143-033]cease joking on that subject. Any notions you have are wide of the

[143-034]truth—are the very reverse of the truth.”

[143-035]“I am no more inclined to joke than I shall be at my own funeral,” said

[143-036]Hans. “But I am not at all sure that you are aware what are my notions

[143-037]on that subject.”

[143-038]“Perhaps not,” said Deronda. “But let me say, once for all, that in

[143-039]relation to Mrs. [[Henleigh-Grandcourt|Grandcourt]], I never have had, and never shall have the

[143-040]position of a lover. If you have ever seriously put that interpretation

[143-041]on anything you have observed, you are supremely mistaken.”

[143-042]There was silence a little while, and to each the silence was like an

[143-043]irritating air, exaggerating discomfort.

[143-044]“Perhaps I have been mistaken in another interpretation, also,” said

[143-045]Hans, presently.

[143-046]“What is that?”

[143-047]“That you had no wish to hold the position of a lover toward another

[143-048]woman, who is neither wife nor widow.”

[143-049]“I can’t pretend not to understand you, Meyrick. It is painful that our

[143-050]wishes should clash. I hope you will tell me if you have any ground for

[143-051]supposing that you would succeed.”

[143-052]“That seems rather a superfluous inquiry on your part, Deronda,” said

[143-053]Hans, with some irritation.

[143-054]“Why superfluous?”

[143-055]“Because you are perfectly convinced on the subject—and probably have

[143-056]had the very best evidence to convince you.”

[143-057]“I will be more frank with you than you are with me,” said Deronda,

[143-058]still heated by Hans’ show of temper, and yet sorry for him. “I have

[143-059]never had the slightest evidence that I should succeed myself. In fact,

[143-060]I have very little hope.”

[143-061]Hans looked round hastily at his friend, but immediately turned to his

[143-062]picture again.

[143-063]“And in our present situation,” said Deronda, hurt by the idea that

[143-064]Hans suspected him of insincerity, and giving an offended emphasis to

[143-065]his words, “I don’t see how I can deliberately make known my feeling to

[143-066]her. If she could not return it, I should have embittered her best

[143-067]comfort; for neither she nor I can be parted from her brother, and we

[143-068]should have to meet continually. If I were to cause her that sort of

[143-069]pain by an unwilling betrayal of my feeling, I should be no better than

[143-070]a mischievous animal.”

[143-071]“I don’t know that I have ever betrayed _my_ feeling to her,” said

[143-072]Hans, as if he were vindicating himself.

[143-073]“You mean that we are on a level, then; you have no reason to envy me.”

[143-074]“Oh, not the slightest,” said Hans, with bitter irony. “You have

[143-075]measured my conceit and know that it out-tops all your advantages.”

[143-076]“I am a nuisance to you, Meyrick. I am sorry, but I can’t help it,”

[143-077]said Deronda, rising. “After what passed between us before, I wished to

[143-078]have this explanation; and I don’t see that any pretensions of mine

[143-079]have made a real difference to you. They are not likely to make any

[143-080]pleasant difference to myself under present circumstances. Now the

[143-081]father is there—did you know that the father is there?”

[143-082]“Yes. If he were not a Jew I would permit myself to damn him—with

[143-083]faint praise, I mean,” said Hans, but with no smile.

[143-084]“She and I meet under greater constraint than ever. Things might go on

[143-085]in this way for two years without my getting any insight into her

[143-086]feeling toward me. That is the whole state of affairs, Hans. Neither

[143-087]you nor I have injured the other, that I can see. We must put up with

[143-088]this sort of rivalry in a hope that is likely enough to come to

[143-089]nothing. Our friendship can bear that strain, surely.”

[143-090]“No, it can’t,” said Hans, impetuously, throwing down his tools,

[143-091]thrusting his hands into his coat-pockets, and turning round to face

[143-092]Deronda, who drew back a little and looked at him with amazement. Hans

[143-093]went on in the same tone,

[143-094]“Our friendship—my friendship—can’t bear the strain of behaving to

[143-095]you like an ungrateful dastard and grudging you your happiness. For you

[143-096]_are_ the happiest dog in the world. If [[Mirah-Lapidoth|Mirah]] loves anybody better than

[143-097]her brother, _you are the man_.”

[143-098]Hans turned on his heel and threw himself into his chair, looking up at

[143-099]Deronda with an expression the reverse of tender. Something like a

[143-100]shock passed through Deronda, and, after an instant, he said,

[143-101]“It is a good-natured fiction of yours, Hans.”

[143-102]“I am not in a good-natured mood. I assure you I found the fact

[143-103]disagreeable when it was thrust on me—all the more, or perhaps all the

[143-104]less, because I believed then that your heart was pledged to the

[143-105]duchess. But now, confound you! you turn out to be in love in the right

[143-106]place—a Jew—and everything eligible.”

[143-107]“Tell me what convinced you—there’s a good fellow,” said Deronda,

[143-108]distrusting a delight that he was unused to.

[143-109]“Don’t ask. Little mother was witness. The upshot is, that Mirah is

[143-110]jealous of the duchess, and the sooner you relieve your mind the

[143-111]better. There! I’ve cleared off a score or two, and may be allowed to

[143-112]swear at you for getting what you deserve—which is just the very best

[143-113]luck I know of.”

[143-114]“God bless you, Hans!” said Deronda, putting out his hand, which the

[143-115]other took and wrung in silence.

[143-116]CHAPTER LXVIII.

[143-117]“All thoughts, all passions, all delights,

[143-118]Whatever stirs this mortal frame,

[143-119]All are but ministers of Love,

[143-120]And feed his sacred flame.”

[143-121]—COLERIDGE.

[143-122]Deronda’s eagerness to confess his love could hardly have had a

[143-123]stronger stimulus than Hans had given it in his assurance that Mirah

[143-124]needed relief from jealousy. He went on his next visit to Ezra with the

[143-125]determination to be resolute in using—nay, in requesting—an

[143-126]opportunity of private conversation with her. If she accepted his love,

[143-127]he felt courageous about all other consequences, and as her betrothed

[143-128]husband he would gain a protective authority which might be a desirable

[143-129]defense for her in future difficulties with her father. Deronda had not

[143-130]observed any signs of growing restlessness in [[Lapidoth]], or of

[143-131]diminished desire to recommend himself; but he had forebodings of some

[143-132]future struggle, some [[shame|mortification]], or some intolerable increase of

[143-133]domestic disquietude in which he might save Ezra and Mirah from being

[143-134]helpless victims.

[143-135]His forebodings would have been strengthened if he had known what was

[143-136]going on in the father’s mind. That amount of restlessness, that

[143-137]desultoriness of attention, which made a small torture to Ezra, was to

[143-138]Lapidoth an irksome submission to restraint, only made bearable by his

[143-139]thinking of it as a means of by-and-by securing a well-conditioned

[143-140]freedom. He began with the intention of awaiting some really good

[143-141]chance, such as an opening for getting a considerable sum from Deronda;

[143-142]but all the while he was looking about curiously, and trying to

[143-143]discover where Mirah deposited her money and her keys. The imperious

[143-144]gambling desire within him, which carried on its activity through every

[143-145]other occupation, and made a continuous web of imagination that held

[143-146]all else in its meshes, would hardly have been under the control of a

[143-147]contracted purpose, if he had been able to lay his hands on any sum

[143-148]worth capturing. But Mirah, with her practical clear-sightedness,

[143-149]guarded against any frustration of the promise she had given to Ezra,

[143-150]by confiding all money, except what she was immediately in want of, to

[143-151]Mrs. Meyrick’s care, and Lapidoth felt himself under an irritating

[143-152]completeness of supply in kind as in a lunatic asylum where everything

[143-153]was made safe against him. To have opened a desk or drawer of Mirah’s,

[143-154]and pocketed any bank-notes found there, would have been to his mind a

[143-155]sort of domestic appropriation which had no disgrace in it; the degrees

[143-156]of [[freedom|liberty]] a man allows himself with other people’s property being

[143-157]often delicately drawn, even beyond the boundary where the law begins

[143-158]to lay its hold—which is the reason why spoons are a safer investment

[143-159]than mining shares. Lapidoth really felt himself injuriously treated by

[143-160]his daughter, and thought that he ought to have had what he wanted of

[143-161]her other earnings as he had of her apple-tart. But he remained

[143-162]submissive; indeed, the indiscretion that most tempted him, was not any

[143-163]insistence with Mirah, but some kind of appeal to Deronda. Clever

[143-164]persons who have nothing else to sell can often put a good price on

[143-165]their absence, and Lapidoth’s difficult search for devices forced upon

[143-166]him the idea that his family would find themselves happier without him,

[143-167]and that Deronda would be willing to advance a considerable sum for the

[143-168]sake of getting rid of him. But, in spite of well-practiced hardihood,

[143-169]Lapidoth was still in some awe of Ezra’s imposing friend, and deferred

[143-170]his purpose indefinitely.

[143-171]On this day, when Deronda had come full of a gladdened consciousness,

[143-172]which inevitably showed itself in his air and speech, Lapidoth was at a

[143-173]crisis of discontent and longing that made his mind busy with schemes

[143-174]of freedom, and Deronda’s new amenity encouraged them. This

[143-175]preoccupation was at last so strong as to interfere with his usual

[143-176]show of interest in what went forward, and his persistence in sitting

[143-177]by even when there was reading which he could not follow. After sitting

[143-178]a little while, he went out to smoke and walk in the square, and the

[143-179]two friends were all the easier. Mirah was not at home, but she was

[143-180]sure to be in again before Deronda left, and his eyes glowed with a

[143-181]secret anticipation: he thought that when he saw her again he should

[143-182]see some sweetness of recognition for himself to which his eyes had

[143-183]been sealed before. There was an additional playful affectionateness in

[143-184]his manner toward Ezra.

[143-185]“This little room is too close for you, Ezra,” he said, breaking off

[143-186]his reading. “The week’s heat we sometimes get here is worse than the

[143-187]heat in Genoa, where one sits in the shaded coolness of large rooms.
