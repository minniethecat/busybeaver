[033-001][[Hans-Meyrick|Hans]] in his right mind, however, was a lovable creature, and in [[Daniel-Deronda|Deronda]]

[033-002]he had happened to find a friend who was likely to stand by him with

[033-003]the more constancy, from [[sympathy|compassion]] for these brief aberrations that

[033-004]might bring a long repentance. Hans, indeed, shared Deronda’s rooms

[033-005]nearly as much as he used his own: to Deronda he poured himself out on

[033-006]his studies, his affairs, his hopes; the poverty of his home, and his

[033-007]love for the creatures there; the itching of his fingers to draw, and

[033-008]his determination to fight it away for the sake of getting some sort of

[033-009]a plum that he might divide with his mother and the girls. He wanted no

[033-010]confidence in return, but seemed to take Deronda as an Olympian who

[033-011]needed nothing—an egotism in friendship which is common enough with

[033-012]mercurial, expansive natures. Deronda was content, and gave Meyrick all

[033-013]the interest he claimed, getting at last a brotherly anxiety about him,

[033-014]looking after him in his erratic moments, and contriving by adroitly

[033-015]delicate devices not only to make up for his friend’s lack of pence,

[033-016]but to save him from threatening chances. Such friendship easily

[033-017]becomes tender: the one spreads strong sheltering wings that delight in

[033-018]spreading, the other gets the warm protection which is also a delight.

[033-019]Meyrick was going in for a classical scholarship, and his success, in

[033-020]various ways momentous, was the more probable from the steadying

[033-021]influence of Deronda’s friendship.

[033-022]But an imprudence of Meyrick’s, committed at the beginning of the

[033-023]autumn term, threatened to disappoint his hopes. With his usual

[033-024]alternation between unnecessary expense and self-privation, he had

[033-025]given too much money for an old engraving which fascinated him, and to

[033-026]make up for it, had come from [[london|London]] in a third-class carriage with his

[033-027]eyes exposed to a bitter wind and any irritating particles the wind

[033-028]might [[ambition|drive]] before it. The consequence was a severe inflammation of the

[033-029]eyes, which for some time hung over him the threat of a lasting injury.

[033-030]This crushing trouble called out all Deronda’s readiness to devote

[033-031]himself, and he made every other occupation secondary to that of being

[033-032]companion and eyes to Hans, working with him and for him at his

[033-033]classics, that if possible his chance of the classical scholarship

[033-034]might be saved. Hans, to keep the knowledge of his suffering from his

[033-035]mother and sisters, alleged his work as a reason for passing the

[033-036]Christmas at Cambridge, and his friend stayed up with him.

[033-037]Meanwhile Deronda relaxed his hold on his mathematics, and Hans,

[033-038]reflecting on this, at length said: “Old fellow, while you are hoisting

[033-039]me you are risking yourself. With your mathematical cram one may be

[033-040]like Moses or Mohammed or somebody of that sort who had to cram, and

[033-041]forgot in one day what it had taken him forty to learn.”

[033-042]Deronda would not admit that he cared about the risk, and he had really

[033-043]been beguiled into a little indifference by double sympathy: he was

[033-044]very anxious that Hans should not miss the much-needed scholarship, and

[033-045]he felt a revival of interest in the old studies. Still, when Hans,

[033-046]rather late in the day, got able to use his own eyes, Deronda had

[033-047]tenacity enough to try hard and recover his lost ground. He failed,

[033-048]however; but he had the satisfaction of seeing Meyrick win.

[033-049]Success, as a sort of beginning that urged completion, might have

[033-050]reconciled Deronda to his university course; but the emptiness of all

[033-051]things, from politics to pastimes, is never so striking to us as when

[033-052]we fail in them. The loss of the personal triumph had no severity for

[033-053]him, but the sense of having spent his time ineffectively in a mode of

[033-054]working which had been against the grain, gave him a distaste for any

[033-055]renewal of the process, which turned his imagined project of quitting

[033-056]Cambridge into a serious intention. In speaking of his intention to

[033-057]Meyrick he made it appear that he was glad of the turn events had

[033-058]taken—glad to have the balance dip decidedly, and feel freed from his

[033-059]hesitations; but he observed that he must of course submit to any

[033-060]strong objection on the part of [[Sir-Hugo-Mallinger|Sir Hugo]].

[033-061]Meyrick’s joy and gratitude were disturbed by much uneasiness. He

[033-062]believed in Deronda’s alleged preference, but he felt keenly that in

[033-063]serving him Daniel had placed himself at a disadvantage in Sir Hugo’s

[033-064]opinion, and he said mournfully, “If you had got the scholarship, Sir

[033-065]Hugo would have thought that you asked to leave us with a better grace.

[033-066]You have spoiled your luck for my sake, and I can do nothing to amend

[033-067]it.”

[033-068]“Yes, you can; you are to be a first-rate fellow. I call that a

[033-069]first-rate investment of my luck.”

[033-070]“Oh, confound it! You save an ugly mongrel from drowning, and expect

[033-071]him to cut a fine figure. The poets have made tragedies enough about

[033-072]signing one’s self over to wickedness for the sake of getting something

[033-073]plummy; I shall write a tragedy of a fellow who signed himself over to

[033-074]be good, and was uncomfortable ever after.”

[033-075]But Hans lost no time in secretly writing the history of the affair to

[033-076]Sir Hugo, making it plain that but for Deronda’s generous devotion he

[033-077]could hardly have failed to win the prize he had been working for.

[033-078]The two friends went up to town together: Meyrick to rejoice with his

[033-079]mother and the girls in their little home at Chelsea; Deronda to carry

[033-080]out the less easy task of opening his mind to Sir Hugo. He relied a

[033-081]little on the baronet’s general tolerance of eccentricities, but he

[033-082]expected more opposition than he met with. He was received with even

[033-083]warmer kindness than usual, the failure was passed over lightly, and

[033-084]when he detailed his reasons for wishing to quit the university and go

[033-085]to study abroad, Sir Hugo sat for some time in a silence which was

[033-086]rather meditative than surprised. At last he said, looking at Daniel

[033-087]with examination, “So you don’t want to be an Englishman to the

[033-088]backbone after all?”

[033-089]“I want to be an Englishman, but I want to understand other points of

[033-090]view. And I want to get rid of a merely English attitude in studies.”

[033-091]“I see; you don’t want to be turned out in the same mould as every

[033-092]other youngster. And I have nothing to say against your doffing some of

[033-093]our national prejudices. I feel the better myself for having spent a

[033-094]good deal of my time abroad. But, for God’s sake, keep an English cut,

[033-095]and don’t become indifferent to bad tobacco! And, my dear boy, it is

[033-096]good to be unselfish and generous; but don’t carry that too far. It

[033-097]will not do to give yourself to be melted down for the benefit of the

[033-098]tallow-trade; you must know where to find yourself. However, I shall

[033-099]put no veto on your going. Wait until I can get off Committee, and I’ll

[033-100]run over with you.”

[033-101]So Deronda went according to his will. But not before he had spent some

[033-102]hours with Hans Meyrick, and been introduced to the mother and sisters

[033-103]in the Chelsea home. The shy girls watched and registered every look of

[033-104]their brother’s friend, declared by Hans to have been the salvation of

[033-105]him, a fellow like nobody else, and, in fine, a brick. They so

[033-106]thoroughly accepted Deronda as an ideal, that when he was gone the

[033-107]youngest set to work, under the criticism of the two elder girls, to

[033-108]paint him as Prince Camaralzaman.

[033-109]CHAPTER XVII.

[033-110]“This is true the poet sings,

[033-111]That a sorrow’s crown of sorrow

[033-112]Is remembering happier things.”

[033-113]—TENNYSON: _In Memoriam_.

[033-114]On a fine evening near the end of July, Deronda was rowing himself on

[033-115]the Thames. It was already a year or more since he had come back to

[033-116]England, with the understanding that his education was finished, and

[033-117]that he was somehow to take his place in English society; but though,

[033-118]in deference to Sir Hugo’s wish, and to fence off idleness, he had

[033-119]begun to read law, this apparent decision had been without other result

[033-120]than to deepen the roots of indecision. His old love of boating had

[033-121]revived with the more force now that he was in town with the

[033-122]Mallingers, because he could nowhere else get the same still seclusion

[033-123]which the river gave him. He had a boat of his own at Putney, and

[033-124]whenever Sir Hugo did not want him, it was his chief holiday to row

[033-125]till past sunset and come in again with the stars. Not that he was in a

[033-126]sentimental stage; but he was in another sort of contemplative mood

[033-127]perhaps more common in the young men of our day—that of questioning

[033-128]whether it were worth while to take part in the battle of the world: I

[033-129]mean, of course, the young men in whom the unproductive labor of

[033-130]questioning is sustained by three or five per cent, on capital which

[033-131]somebody else has battled for. It puzzled Sir Hugo that one who made a

[033-132]splendid contrast with all that was sickly and puling should be

[033-133]hampered with ideas which, since they left an accomplished Whig like

[033-134]himself unobstructed, could be no better than spectral illusions;

[033-135]especially as Deronda set himself against authorship—a vocation which

[033-136]is understood to turn foolish thinking into funds.

[033-137]Rowing in his dark-blue shirt and skull-cap, his curls closely clipped,

[033-138]his mouth beset with abundant soft waves of beard, he bore only

[033-139]disguised traces of the seraphic boy “trailing clouds of glory.” Still,

[033-140]even one who had never seen him since his boyhood might have looked at

[033-141]him with slow recognition, due perhaps to the peculiarity of the gaze

[033-142]which [[Gwendolen-Harleth|Gwendolen]] chose to call “dreadful,” though it had really a very

[033-143]mild sort of scrutiny. The voice, sometimes audible in subdued snatches

[033-144]of song, had turned out merely a high baritone; indeed, only to look at

[033-145]his lithe, powerful frame and the firm gravity of his face would have

[033-146]been enough for an experienced guess that he had no rare and ravishing

[033-147]tenor such as nature reluctantly makes at some sacrifice. Look at his

[033-148]hands: they are not small and dimpled, with tapering fingers that seem

[033-149]to have only a deprecating touch: they are long, flexible,

[033-150]firmly-grasping hands, such as Titian has painted in a picture where he

[033-151]wanted to show the combination of refinement with force. And there is

[033-152]something of a likeness, too, between the faces belonging to the

[033-153]hands—in both the uniform pale-brown skin, the perpendicular brow, the

[033-154]calmly penetrating eyes. Not seraphic any longer: thoroughly

[033-155]terrestrial and manly; but still of a kind to raise belief in a human

[033-156]dignity which can afford to recognize poor relations.

[033-157]Such types meet us here and there among average conditions; in a

[033-158]workman, for example, whistling over a bit of measurement and lifting

[033-159]his eyes to answer our question about the road. And often the grand

[033-160]meanings of faces as well as of written words may lie chiefly in the

[033-161]impressions that happen just now to be of importance in relation to

[033-162]Deronda, rowing on the Thames in a very ordinary equipment for a young

[033-163]Englishman at leisure, and passing under Kew Bridge with no thought of

[033-164]an adventure in which his appearance was likely to play any part. In

[033-165]fact, he objected very strongly to the notion, which others had not

[033-166]allowed him to escape, that his appearance was of a kind to draw

[033-167]attention; and hints of this, intended to be complimentary, found an

[033-168]angry resonance in him, coming from mingled experiences, to which a

[033-169]clue has already been given. His own face in the glass had during many

[033-170]years associated for him with thoughts of some one whom he must be

[033-171]like—one about whose character and lot he continually wondered, and

[033-172]never dared to ask.

[033-173]In the neighborhood of Kew Bridge, between six and seven o’clock, the

[033-174]river was no solitude. Several persons were sauntering on the

[033-175]towing-path, and here and there a boat was plying. Deronda had been

[033-176]rowing fast to get over this spot, when, becoming aware of a great

[033-177]barge advancing toward him, he guided his boat aside, and rested on his

[033-178]oar within a couple of yards of the river-brink. He was all the while

[033-179]unconsciously continuing the low-toned chant which had haunted his

[033-180]throat all the way up the river—the gondolier’s song in the _Otello_,

[033-181]where Rossini has worthily set to [[art|music]] the immortal words of [[dantes-divine-comedy|Dante]],
