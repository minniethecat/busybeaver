[044-001]“It would be too hard—impossible—you could not carry it through. I am

[044-002]not worth what you would have to encounter. I will not accept the

[044-003]sacrifice. It would be thought a _mésalliance_ for you and I should be

[044-004]liable to the worst accusations.”

[044-005]“Is it the accusations you are afraid of? I am afraid of nothing but

[044-006]that we should miss the passing of our lives together.”

[044-007]The decisive word had been spoken: there was no doubt concerning the

[044-008]end willed by each: there only remained the way of arriving at it, and

[044-009][[Catherine-Arrowpoint|Catherine]] determined to take the straightest possible. She went to her

[044-010]father and mother in the library, and told them that she had promised

[044-011]to marry [[Herr-Klesmer|Klesmer]].

[044-012]Mrs. Arrowpoint’s state of mind was pitiable. Imagine Jean Jacques,

[044-013]after his essay on the corrupting influence of the arts, waking up among

[044-014]children of nature who had no idea of grilling the raw bone they offered

[044-015]him for breakfast with the primitive couvert of a flint knife; or Saint

[044-016]Just, after fervidly denouncing all recognition of pre-eminence,

[044-017]receiving a vote of thanks for the unbroken mediocrity of his speech,

[044-018]which warranted the dullest patriots in delivering themselves at equal

[044-019]length. Something of the same sort befell the authoress of “Tasso,” when

[044-020]what she had safely demanded of the dead [[Leonora-Halm-Eberstein|Leonora]] was enacted by her own

[044-021]Catherine. It is hard for us to live up to our own eloquence, and keep

[044-022]pace with our winged words, while we are treading the solid earth and

[044-023]are liable to heavy dining. Besides, it has long been understood that

[044-024]the proprieties of literature are not those of practical life. Mrs.

[044-025]Arrowpoint naturally wished for the best of everything. She not only

[044-026]liked to feel herself at a higher level of literary sentiment than the

[044-027]ladies with whom she associated; she wished not to be behind them in any

[044-028]point of social consideration. While Klesmer was seen in the light of a

[044-029]patronized musician, his peculiarities were picturesque and acceptable:

[044-030]but to see him by a sudden flash in the light of her son-in-law gave her

[044-031]a burning sense of what the world would say. And the poor lady had been

[044-032]used to represent her Catherine as a model of excellence.

[044-033]Under the first shock she forgot everything but her anger, and snatched

[044-034]at any phrase that would serve as a weapon.

[044-035]“If Klesmer has presumed to offer himself to you, your father shall

[044-036]horsewhip him off the premises. Pray, speak, Mr. Arrowpoint.”

[044-037]The father took his cigar from his mouth, and rose to the occasion by

[044-038]saying, “This will never do, Cath.”

[044-039]“Do!” cried Mrs. Arrowpoint; “who in their senses ever thought it would

[044-040]do? You might as well say poisoning and strangling will not do. It is a

[044-041]comedy you have got up, Catherine. Else you are mad.”

[044-042]“I am quite sane and serious, mamma, and Herr Klesmer is not to blame.

[044-043]He never thought of my marrying him. I found out that he loved me, and

[044-044]loving him, I told him I would marry him.”

[044-045]“Leave that unsaid, Catherine,” said Mrs. Arrowpoint, bitterly. “Every

[044-046]one else will say that for you. You will be a public fable. Every one

[044-047]will say that you must have made an offer to a man who has been paid to

[044-048]come to the house—who is nobody knows what—a gypsy, a Jew, a mere

[044-049]bubble of the earth.”

[044-050]“Never mind, mamma,” said Catherine, indignant in her turn. “We all

[044-051]know he is a genius—as Tasso was.”

[044-052]“Those times were not these, nor is Klesmer Tasso,” said Mrs.

[044-053]Arrowpoint, getting more heated. “There is no sting in _that_ sarcasm,

[044-054]except the sting of undutifulness.”

[044-055]“I am sorry to hurt you, mamma. But I will not give up the happiness of

[044-056]my life to ideas that I don’t believe in and customs I have no respect

[044-057]for.”

[044-058]“You have lost all sense of duty, then? You have forgotten that you are

[044-059]our only child—that it lies with you to place a great property in the

[044-060]right hands?”

[044-061]“What are the right hands? My grandfather gained the property in trade.”

[044-062]“Mr. Arrowpoint, _will_ you sit by and hear this without speaking?”

[044-063]“I am a gentleman, Cath. We expect you to marry a gentleman,” said the

[044-064]father, exerting himself.

[044-065]“And a man connected with the institutions of this country,” said the

[044-066]mother. “A woman in your position has serious duties. Where duty and

[044-067]inclination clash, she must follow duty.”

[044-068]“I don’t deny that,” said Catherine, getting colder in proportion to

[044-069]her mother’s heat. “But one may say very true things and apply them

[044-070]falsely. People can easily take the sacred word duty as a name for what

[044-071]they desire any one else to do.”

[044-072]“Your parent’s desire makes no duty for you, then?”

[044-073]“Yes, within reason. But before I give up the happiness of my life—”

[044-074]“Catherine, Catherine, it will not be your happiness,” said Mrs.

[044-075]Arrowpoint, in her most raven-like tones.

[044-076]“Well, what seems to me my happiness—before I give it up, I must see

[044-077]some better reason than the wish that I should marry a nobleman, or a

[044-078]man who votes with a party that he may be turned into a nobleman. I

[044-079]feel at [[freedom|liberty]] to marry the man I love and think worthy, unless some

[044-080]higher duty forbids.”

[044-081]“And so it does, Catherine, though you are blinded and cannot see it.

[044-082]It is a woman’s duty not to lower herself. You are lowering yourself.

[044-083]Mr. Arrowpoint, will you tell your daughter what is her duty?”

[044-084]“You must see, Catherine, that Klesmer is not the man for you,” said

[044-085]Mr. Arrowpoint. “He won’t do at the head of estates. He has a deuced

[044-086]foreign look—is an unpractical man.”

[044-087]“I really can’t see what that has to do with it, papa. The land of

[044-088]England has often passed into the hands of foreigners—Dutch soldiers,

[044-089]sons of foreign women of bad character:—if our land were sold

[044-090]to-morrow it would very likely pass into the hands of some foreign

[044-091]merchant on ’Change. It is in everybody’s mouth that successful

[044-092]swindlers may buy up half the land in the country. How can I stem that

[044-093]tide?”

[044-094]“It will never do to argue about marriage, Cath,” said Mr. Arrowpoint.

[044-095]“It’s no use getting up the subject like a parliamentary question. We

[044-096]must do as other people do. We must think of the nation and the public

[044-097]good.”

[044-098]“I can’t see any public good concerned here, papa,” said Catherine.

[044-099]“Why is it to be expected of any heiress that she should carry the

[044-100]property gained in trade into the hands of a certain class? That seems

[044-101]to be a ridiculous mishmash of superannuated customs and false

[044-102]ambition. I should call it a public evil. People had better make a new

[044-103]sort of public good by changing their ambitions.”

[044-104]“That is mere sophistry, Catherine,” said Mrs. Arrowpoint. “Because you

[044-105]don’t wish to marry a nobleman, you are not obliged to marry a

[044-106]mountebank or a charlatan.”

[044-107]“I cannot understand the application of such words, mamma.”

[044-108]“No, I dare say not,” rejoined Mrs. Arrowpoint, with significant scorn.

[044-109]“You have got to a pitch at which we are not likely to understand each

[044-110]other.”

[044-111]“It can’t be done, Cath,” said Mr. Arrowpoint, wishing to substitute a

[044-112]better-humored reasoning for his wife’s impetuosity. “A man like

[044-113]Klesmer can’t marry such a property as yours. It can’t be done.”

[044-114]“It certainly will not be done,” said Mrs. Arrowpoint, imperiously.

[044-115]“Where is the man? Let him be fetched.”

[044-116]“I cannot fetch him to be insulted,” said Catherine. “Nothing will be

[044-117]achieved by that.”

[044-118]“I suppose you would wish him to know that in marrying you he will not

[044-119]marry your fortune,” said Mrs. Arrowpoint.

[044-120]“Certainly; if it were so, I should wish him to know it.”

[044-121]“Then you had better fetch him.”

[044-122]Catherine only went into the [[art|music]]-room and said, “Come.” She felt no

[044-123]need to prepare Klesmer.

[044-124]“Herr Klesmer,” said Mrs. Arrowpoint, with a rather contemptuous

[044-125]stateliness, “it is unnecessary to repeat what has passed between us

[044-126]and our daughter. Mr. Arrowpoint will tell you our resolution.”

[044-127]“Your marrying is out of the question,” said Mr. Arrowpoint, rather too

[044-128]heavily weighted with his task, and standing in an embarrassment

[044-129]unrelieved by a cigar. “It is a wild scheme altogether. A man has been

[044-130]called out for less.”

[044-131]“You have taken a base advantage of our confidence,” burst in Mrs.

[044-132]Arrowpoint, unable to carry out her purpose and leave the burden of

[044-133]speech to her husband.

[044-134]Klesmer made a low bow in silent irony.

[044-135]“The pretension is ridiculous. You had better give it up and leave the

[044-136]house at once,” continued Mr. Arrowpoint. He wished to do without

[044-137]mentioning the money.

[044-138]“I can give up nothing without reference to your daughter’s wish,” said

[044-139]Klesmer. “My engagement is to her.”

[044-140]“It is useless to discuss the question,” said Mrs. Arrowpoint. “We

[044-141]shall never consent to the marriage. If Catherine disobeys us we shall

[044-142]disinherit her. You will not marry her fortune. It is right you should

[044-143]know that.”

[044-144]“Madam, her fortune has been the only thing I have had to regret about

[044-145]her. But I must ask her if she will not think the sacrifice greater

[044-146]than I am worthy of.”

[044-147]“It is no sacrifice to me,” said Catherine, “except that I am sorry to

[044-148]hurt my father and mother. I have always felt my fortune to be a

[044-149]wretched fatality of my life.”

[044-150]“You mean to defy us, then?” said Mrs. Arrowpoint.

[044-151]“I mean to marry Herr Klesmer,” said Catherine, firmly.

[044-152]“He had better not count on our relenting,” said Mrs. Arrowpoint, whose

[044-153]manners suffered from that impunity in insult which has been reckoned

[044-154]among the privileges of women.

[044-155]“Madam,” said Klesmer, “certain reasons forbid me to retort. But

[044-156]understand that I consider it out of the power of either of you, or of

[044-157]your fortune, to confer on me anything that I value. My rank as an

[044-158]artist is of my own winning, and I would not exchange it for any other.

[044-159]I am able to maintain your daughter, and I ask for no change in my life

[044-160]but her companionship.”

[044-161]“You will leave the house, however,” said Mrs. Arrowpoint.

[044-162]“I go at once,” said Klesmer, bowing and quitting the room.

[044-163]“Let there be no misunderstanding, mamma,” said Catherine; “I consider

[044-164]myself engaged to Herr Klesmer, and I intend to marry him.”

[044-165]The mother turned her head away and waved her hand in sign of dismissal.

[044-166]“It’s all very fine,” said Mr. Arrowpoint, when Catherine was gone;

[044-167]“but what the deuce are we to do with the property?”

[044-168]“There is Harry Brendall. He can take the name.”

[044-169]“Harry Brendall will get through it all in no time,” said Mr.

[044-170]Arrowpoint, relighting his cigar.

[044-171]And thus, with nothing settled but the determination of the lovers,

[044-172]Klesmer had left [[quetcham-hall|Quetcham]].

[044-173]CHAPTER XXIII.

[044-174]Among the heirs of Art, as is the division of the promised land, each

[044-175]has to win his portion by hard fighting: the bestowal is after the

[044-176]manner of prophecy, and is a title without possession. To carry the

[044-177]map of an ungotten estate in your pocket is a poor sort of copyhold.

[044-178]And in fancy to cast his shoe over Eden is little warrant that a man

[044-179]shall ever set the sole of his foot on an acre of his own there.

[044-180]The most obstinate beliefs that mortals entertain about themselves are

[044-181]such as they have no evidence for beyond a constant, spontaneous

[044-182]pulsing of their self-satisfaction—as it were a hidden seed of

[044-183]madness, a confidence that they can move the world without precise

[044-184]notion of standing-place or lever.

[044-185]“Pray go to church, mamma,” said [[Gwendolen-Harleth|Gwendolen]] the next morning. “I prefer

[044-186]seeing Herr Klesmer alone.” (He had written in reply to her note that

[044-187]he would be with her at eleven.)

[044-188]“That is hardly correct, I think,” said [[Mrs-Davilow|Mrs. Davilow]], anxiously.

[044-189]“Our affairs are too serious for us to think of such nonsensical

[044-190]rules,” said Gwendolen, contemptuously. “They are insulting as well as

[044-191]ridiculous.”
