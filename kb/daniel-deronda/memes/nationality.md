# Nationality

**Nationality** is a central concept in *Daniel Deronda*, explored primarily through the novel's engagement with Jewish identity and the emerging idea of national consciousness in 19th-century Europe.

## The Jewish National Question

The novel's latter half turns on the question of whether the Jews constitute a nationality or merely a religious community. [[Mordecai]] is the principal advocate for Jewish nationality, arguing that Jewish identity transcends religion to encompass a distinct national consciousness.[^1] His vision anticipates the Zionist movement that would emerge in the decades following the novel's publication.

> "Unless nationality is a feeling, I think the feeling of nationality is dying." [095-036]

## Nationality and Assimilation

Eliot stages a debate between competing views of nationality. Characters like Pash argue that national sentiment is fading in modern Europe, while [[Mordecai]] insists on its enduring power.[^2] The question is whether Jews should assimilate into the nations where they live or maintain a separate national identity—a question that [[Daniel-Deronda]] must answer for himself.[^3]

> In Europe the sentiment of nationality is destined to die out. It will be merged in the sentiment of humanity. [095-045]

## The Philosophical Debate

The novel embeds the nationality debate within a broader philosophical context. Pash's cosmopolitanism—the belief that national sentiment will yield to universal "sentiment of humanity"—is presented as an enlightened but ultimately insufficient response to the Jewish question.[^6] Mordecai counters that a living nationality cannot be reduced to abstract universalism: "unless our race takes on again the character of a nationality," the Jewish contribution to human civilization will be lost.[^7]

The debate is not merely political; it touches on the nature of identity itself. Mordecai is described as "a frail incorporation of the national consciousness"—his very body is imagined as vessel for a collective identity that transcends individual existence.[^8]

## Religion, Nation, and Peoplehood

The novel explores the relationship between national and religious identity. [[Daniel-Deronda]]'s grandfather, Daniel Charisi, "looked at a national religion by daylight, and saw it in its relation to national life"—a vision that treats Judaism as the expression of a people's collective existence rather than a private faith.[^9] This conception of a "national faith" as "national covenant" reshapes Deronda's understanding of what it means to be Jewish.[^10]

## Deronda's Choice

[[Daniel-Deronda]]'s discovery of his Jewish birth forces him to confront the question of nationality directly. His eventual decision to embrace Jewish nationality and work toward the restoration of a Jewish homeland represents the novel's answer to the nationalist question.[^4] This choice transforms nationality from an abstract concept into a lived commitment.[^5]
