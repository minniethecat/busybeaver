# Klesmer's Compositions

The fictional musical compositions of [[Herr-Klesmer]] stand as the novel's gold standard of artistic authenticity — works created not for applause or social advancement but from an inner necessity that tolerates no compromise. Through Klesmer's music, [[art|George Eliot]] articulates her most demanding vision of what it means to be an artist: a vocation that consumes the self and transcends the social distinctions of class, nationality, and religion.

## Overview

Klesmer's compositions are never described in technical detail — Eliot wisely avoids the trap of attempting to represent music in prose. Instead, they are defined by their effect: the awe they inspire, the standard they set, the social barriers they dismantle. Klesmer appears as "the musical Magus" [PN=009-121] whose mere presence widens the horizon of musical experience for those who hear him. His compositions represent the European art-music tradition at its most demanding — Beethovenian in their seriousness, Lisztian in their virtuosity, and distinctly modern in their harmonic language. Within the world of the novel, Klesmer's music is the artistic equivalent of moral truth: it does not negotiate.

## Role in the Novel

Klesmer's compositions serve three functions in the novel's moral-aesthetic scheme. First, they establish an **absolute standard** — the uncompromising yardstick against which Gwendolen's amateurism and Catherine Arrowpoint's earnestness are both measured. Second, they provide the **vehicle for social critique**: Klesmer's marriage to the aristocratic Catherine across barriers of class and ethnicity demonstrates that artistic value creates its own social order. Third, they embody the novel's **ideal of vocation**: Klesmer's total dedication to his art models the fusion of identity and purpose that Deronda must find in the Jewish national cause.

> "You cannot lead in art; it would be too cruel to a great musician. You cannot rule in music; you can only follow." [PN=008-171]

## Connection to Characters

### Herr Klesmer

Klesmer lives his compositions — they are not separate from his identity but its fullest expression. His severity as a judge of others' musical claims derives from the severity with which he judges himself. "I know that I am not to be called a great musician," he tells Catherine, yet the novel makes clear that his standards are higher than anyone else's — and that his self-assessment is itself a mark of genuine artistic conscience [PN=008-171]. His compositions are the outward sign of an inward dedication that the novel treats with almost religious reverence.

### Catherine Arrowpoint

Catherine's relationship to Klesmer's music is one of recognition and devotion. She is a serious amateur pianist who understands what Klesmer's compositions demand — technically, emotionally, morally. Her choice to marry Klesmer despite her parents' objections is an act of artistic as well as romantic commitment: she is choosing not just a man but a standard of value. In marrying Klesmer, she marries his music and all it represents.

### Gwendolen Harleth

Gwendolen's encounter with Klesmer's compositions — and his devastating assessment of her own musical abilities — is the moment her fantasy of independence through art collapses. She has "no talent — only liking for music" [PN=009-003], and Klesmer's compositions, by their mere existence, expose the gap between her pretension and her capacity. His music represents a world of genuine achievement that her social accomplishments cannot touch.

## Thematic Significance

Klesmer's compositions embody the novel's argument that genuine art is a form of moral truth. Eliot, who believed that the artist's primary duty was to see and represent reality without flinching, uses Klesmer's music as a model for what fiction itself should be: uncompromising, demanding, and ultimately redemptive. The compositions also serve as a bridge between the novel's aesthetic and political themes: Klesmer's ability to transcend social prejudice through artistic achievement prefigures Deronda's vision of a Jewish national renaissance that will redeem a people through collective purpose.
