[015-001]But when the door opened and she whose presence he was longing for

[015-002]entered, there came over him suddenly and mysteriously a state of

[015-003]tremor and distrust which he had never felt before. Miss [[Gwendolen-Harleth|Gwendolen]],

[015-004]simple as she stood there, in her black silk, cut square about the

[015-005]round white pillar of her throat, a black band fastening her hair which

[015-006]streamed backward in smooth silky abundance, seemed more queenly than

[015-007]usual. Perhaps it was that there was none of the latent fun and

[015-008]tricksiness which had always pierced in her greeting of [[Rex-Gascoigne|Rex]]. How much

[015-009]of this was due to her presentiment from what he had said yesterday

[015-010]that he was going to talk of love? How much from her desire to show

[015-011]regret about his accident? Something of both. But the wisdom of ages

[015-012]has hinted that there is a side of the bed which has a malign influence

[015-013]if you happen to get out on it; and this accident befalls some charming

[015-014]persons rather frequently. Perhaps it had befallen Gwendolen this

[015-015]morning. The hastening of her toilet, the way in which Bugle used the

[015-016]brush, the quality of the shilling serial mistakenly written for her

[015-017]amusement, the probabilities of the coming day, and, in short, social

[015-018]institutions generally, were all objectionable to her. It was not that

[015-019]she was out of temper, but that the world was not equal to the demands

[015-020]of her fine organism.

[015-021]However it might be, Rex saw an awful majesty about her as she entered

[015-022]and put out her hand to him, without the least approach to a smile in

[015-023]eyes or mouth. The fun which had moved her in the evening had quite

[015-024]evaporated from the image of his accident, and the whole affair seemed

[015-025]stupid to her. But she said with perfect propriety, “I hope you are not

[015-026]much hurt, Rex; I deserve that you should reproach me for your

[015-027]accident.”

[015-028]“Not at all,” said Rex, feeling the soul within him spreading itself

[015-029]like an attack of illness. “There is hardly any thing the matter with

[015-030]me. I am so glad you had the pleasure: I would willingly pay for it by

[015-031]a tumble, only I was sorry to break the horse’s knees.”

[015-032]Gwendolen walked to the hearth and stood looking at the fire in the

[015-033]most inconvenient way for conversation, so that he could only get a

[015-034]side view of her face.

[015-035]“My father wants me to go to Southampton for the rest of the vacation,”

[015-036]said Rex, his baritone trembling a little.

[015-037]“Southampton! That’s a stupid place to go to, isn’t it?” said

[015-038]Gwendolen, chilly.

[015-039]“It would be to me, because you would not be there.” Silence.

[015-040]“Should you mind about me going away, Gwendolen?”

[015-041]“Of course. Every one is of consequence in this dreary country,” said

[015-042]Gwendolen, curtly. The perception that poor Rex wanted to be tender

[015-043]made her curl up and harden like a sea-anemone at the touch of a finger.

[015-044]“Are you angry with me, Gwendolen? Why do you treat me in this way all

[015-045]at once?” said Rex, flushing, and with more spirit in his voice, as if

[015-046]he too were capable of being angry.

[015-047]Gwendolen looked round at him and smiled. “Treat you? Nonsense! I am

[015-048]only rather cross. Why did you come so very early? You must expect to

[015-049]find tempers in dishabille.”

[015-050]“Be as cross with me as you like—only don’t treat me with

[015-051]indifference,” said Rex, imploringly. “All the happiness of my life

[015-052]depends on your loving me—if only a little—better than any one else.”

[015-053]He tried to take her hand, but she hastily eluded his grasp and moved

[015-054]to the other end of the hearth, facing him.

[015-055]“Pray don’t make love to me! I hate it!” she looked at him fiercely.

[015-056]Rex turned pale and was silent, but could not take his eyes off her,

[015-057]and the impetus was not yet exhausted that made hers dart death at him.

[015-058]Gwendolen herself could not have foreseen that she should feel in this

[015-059]way. It was all a sudden, new experience to her. The day before she had

[015-060]been quite aware that her cousin was in love with her; she did not mind

[015-061]how much, so that he said nothing about it; and if any one had asked

[015-062]her why she objected to love-making speeches, she would have said,

[015-063]laughingly, “Oh I am tired of them all in the books.” But now the life

[015-064]of passion had begun negatively in her. She felt passionately averse to

[015-065]this volunteered love.

[015-066]To Rex at twenty the joy of life seemed at an end more absolutely than

[015-067]it can do to a man at forty. But before they had ceased to look at each

[015-068]other, he did speak again.

[015-069]“Is that last word you have to say to me, Gwendolen? Will it always be

[015-070]so?”

[015-071]She could not help seeing his wretchedness and feeling a little regret

[015-072]for the old Rex who had not offended her. Decisively, but yet with some

[015-073]return of kindness, she said,

[015-074]“[[About]] making love? Yes. But I don’t dislike you for anything else.”

[015-075]There was just a perceptible pause before he said a low “good-bye,” and

[015-076]passed out of the room. Almost immediately after, she heard the heavy

[015-077]hall door bang behind him.

[015-078][[Mrs-Davilow|Mrs. Davilow]], too, had heard Rex’s hasty departure, and presently came

[015-079]into the drawing-room, where she found Gwendolen seated on the low

[015-080]couch, her face buried, and her hair falling over her figure like a

[015-081]garment. She was sobbing bitterly. “My child, my child, what is it?”

[015-082]cried the mother, who had never before seen her darling struck down in

[015-083]this way, and felt something of the alarmed anguish that women, feel at

[015-084]the sight of overpowering sorrow in a strong man; for this child had

[015-085]been her ruler. Sitting down by her with circling arms, she pressed her

[015-086]cheek against Gwendolen’s head, and then tried to draw it upward.

[015-087]Gwendolen gave way, and letting her head rest against her mother, cried

[015-088]out sobbingly, “Oh, mamma, what can become of my life? there is nothing

[015-089]worth living for!”

[015-090]“Why, dear?” said Mrs. Davilow. Usually she herself had been rebuked by

[015-091]her daughter for involuntary signs of despair.

[015-092]“I shall never love anybody. I can’t love people. I hate them.”

[015-093]“The time will come, dear, the time will come.”

[015-094]Gwendolen was more and more convulsed with sobbing; but putting her

[015-095]arms round her mother’s neck with an almost painful clinging, she said

[015-096]brokenly, “I can’t bear any one to be very near me but you.”

[015-097]Then the mother began to sob, for this spoiled child had never shown

[015-098]such dependence on her before: and so they clung to each other.

[015-099]CHAPTER VIII.

[015-100]What name doth Joy most borrow

[015-101]When life is fair?

[015-102]“To-morrow.”

[015-103]What name doth best fit Sorrow

[015-104]In young despair?

[015-105]“To-morrow.”

[015-106]There was a much more lasting trouble at the rectory. Rex arrived there

[015-107]only to throw himself on his bed in a state of apparent apathy,

[015-108]unbroken till the next day, when it began to be interrupted by more

[015-109]positive signs of illness. Nothing could be said about his going to

[015-110]Southampton: instead of that, the chief thought of his mother and Anna

[015-111]was how to tend this patient who did not want to be well, and from

[015-112]being the brightest, most grateful spirit in the household, was

[015-113]metamorphosed into an irresponsive, dull-eyed creature who met all

[015-114]affectionate attempts with a murmur of “Let me alone.” His father

[015-115]looked beyond the crisis, and believed it to be the shortest way out of

[015-116]an unlucky affair; but he was sorry for the inevitable suffering, and

[015-117]went now and then to sit by him in silence for a few minutes, parting

[015-118]with a gentle pressure of his hand on Rex’s blank brow, and a “God

[015-119]bless you, my boy.” Warham and the younger children used to peep round

[015-120]the edge of the door to see this incredible thing of their lively

[015-121]brother being laid low; but fingers were immediately shaken at them to

[015-122][[ambition|drive]] them back. The guardian who was always there was Anna, and her

[015-123]little hand was allowed to rest within her brother’s, though he never

[015-124]gave it a welcoming pressure. Her soul was divided between anguish for

[015-125]Rex and reproach of Gwendolen.

[015-126]“Perhaps it is wicked of me, but I think I never _can_ love her again,”

[015-127]came as the recurrent burden of poor little Anna’s inward monody. And

[015-128]even Mrs. Gascoigne had an angry feeling toward her niece which she

[015-129]could not refrain from expressing (apologetically) to her husband.

[015-130]“I know of course it is better, and we ought to be thankful that she is

[015-131]not in love with the poor boy; but really. Henry, I think she is hard;

[015-132]she has the heart of a coquette. I can not help thinking that she must

[015-133]have made him believe something, or the disappointment would not have

[015-134]taken hold of him in that way. And some blame attaches to poor Fanny;

[015-135]she is quite blind about that girl.”

[015-136]Mr. Gascoigne answered imperatively: “The less said on that point the

[015-137]better, Nancy. I ought to have been more awake myself. As to the boy,

[015-138]be thankful if nothing worse ever happens to him. Let the thing die out

[015-139]as quickly as possible; and especially with regard to Gwendolen—let it

[015-140]be as if it had never been.”

[015-141]The rector’s dominant feeling was that there had been a great escape.

[015-142]Gwendolen in love with Rex in return would have made a much harder

[015-143]problem, the solution of which might have been taken out of his hands.

[015-144]But he had to go through some further difficulty.

[015-145]One fine morning Rex asked for his bath, and made his toilet as usual.

[015-146]Anna, full of excitement at this change, could do nothing but listen

[015-147]for his coming down, and at last hearing his step, ran to the foot of

[015-148]the stairs to meet him. For the first time he gave her a faint smile,

[015-149]but it looked so melancholy on his pale face that she could hardly help

[015-150]crying.

[015-151]“Nannie!” he said gently, taking her hand and leading her slowly along

[015-152]with him to the drawing-room. His mother was there, and when she came

[015-153]to kiss him, he said: “What a plague I am!”

[015-154]Then he sat still and looked out of the bow-window on the lawn and

[015-155]shrubs covered with hoar-frost, across which the sun was sending faint

[015-156]occasional gleams:—something like that sad smile on Rex’s face, Anna

[015-157]thought. He felt as if he had had a resurrection into a new world, and

[015-158]did not know what to do with himself there, the old interests being

[015-159]left behind. Anna sat near him, pretending to work, but really watching

[015-160]him with yearning looks. Beyond the garden hedge there was a road where

[015-161]wagons and carts sometimes went on field-work: a railed opening was

[015-162]made in the hedge, because the upland with its bordering wood and clump

[015-163]of ash-trees against the sky was a pretty sight. Presently there came

[015-164]along a wagon laden with timber; the horses were straining their grand

[015-165]muscles, and the driver having cracked his whip, ran along anxiously to

[015-166]guide the leader’s head, fearing a swerve. Rex seemed to be shaken into

[015-167]attention, rose and looked till the last quivering trunk of the timber

[015-168]had disappeared, and then walked once or twice along the room. Mrs.

[015-169]Gascoigne was no longer there, and when he came to sit down again,

[015-170]Anna, seeing a return of speech in her brother’s eyes, could not resist

[015-171]the impulse to bring a little stool and seat herself against his knee,

[015-172]looking up at him with an expression which seemed to say, “Do speak to

[015-173]me.” And he spoke.

[015-174]“I’ll tell you what I’m thinking of, Nannie. I will go to Canada, or

[015-175]somewhere of that sort.” (Rex had not studied the character of our

[015-176]colonial possessions.)

[015-177]“Oh, Rex, not for always!”
