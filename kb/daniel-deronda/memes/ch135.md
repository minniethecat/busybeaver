[135-001][[Mirah-Lapidoth|Mirah]] felt herself under a temptation which she must try to overcome.

[135-002]She answered, obliging herself to look at him again,

[135-003]“I don’t like to deny you what you ask, father; but I have given a

[135-004]promise not to do things for you in secret. It _is_ hard to see you

[135-005]looking needy; but we will bear that for a little while; and then you

[135-006]can have new clothes, and we can pay for them.” Her practical sense

[135-007]made her see now what was Mrs. Meyrick’s wisdom in exacting a promise

[135-008]from her.

[135-009][[Lapidoth]]’s good humor gave way a little. He said, with a sneer, “You

[135-010]are a hard and fast young lady—you have been learning useful

[135-011]virtues—keeping promises not to help your father with a pound or two

[135-012]when you are getting money to dress yourself in silk—your father who

[135-013]made an idol of you, and gave up the best part of his life to providing

[135-014]for you.”

[135-015]“It seems cruel—I know it seems cruel,” said Mirah, feeling this a

[135-016]worse moment than when she meant to drown herself. Her lips were

[135-017]suddenly pale. “But, father, it is more cruel to break the promises

[135-018]people trust in. That broke my mother’s heart—it has broken [[Mordecai|Ezra]]’s

[135-019]life. You and I must eat now this bitterness from what has been. Bear

[135-020]it. Bear to come in and be cared for as you are.”

[135-021]“To-morrow, then,” said Lapidoth, almost turning on his heel away from

[135-022]this pale, trembling daughter, who seemed now to have got the

[135-023]inconvenient world to back her; but he quickly turned on it again, with

[135-024]his hands feeling about restlessly in his pockets, and said, with some

[135-025]return to his appealing tone, “I’m a little cut up with all this,

[135-026]Mirah. I shall get up my spirits by to-morrow. If you’ve a little money

[135-027]in your pocket, I suppose it isn’t against your promise to give me a

[135-028]trifle—to buy a cigar with.”

[135-029]Mirah could not ask herself another question—could not do anything

[135-030]else than put her cold trembling hands in her pocket for her

[135-031]_portemonnaie_ and hold it out. Lapidoth grasped it at once, pressed

[135-032]her fingers the while, said, “Good-bye, my little girl—to-morrow

[135-033]then!” and left her. He had not taken many steps before he looked

[135-034]carefully into all the folds of the purse, found two half-sovereigns

[135-035]and odd silver, and, pasted against the folding cover, a bit of paper

[135-036]on which Ezra had inscribed, in a beautiful Hebrew character, the name

[135-037]of his mother, the days of her birth, marriage, and death, and the

[135-038]prayer, “May Mirah be delivered from evil.” It was Mirah’s liking to

[135-039]have this little inscription on many articles that she used. The father

[135-040]read it, and had a quick vision of his marriage day, and the bright,

[135-041]unblamed young fellow he was at that time; teaching many things, but

[135-042]expecting by-and-by to get money more easily by writing; and very fond

[135-043]of his beautiful bride Sara—crying when she expected him to cry, and

[135-044]reflecting every phase of her feeling with mimetic susceptibility.

[135-045]Lapidoth had traveled a long way from that young self, and thought of

[135-046]all that this inscription signified with an unemotional memory, which

[135-047]was like the ocular perception of a touch to one who has lost the sense

[135-048]of touch, or like morsels on an untasting palate, having shape and

[135-049]grain, but no flavor. Among the things we may gamble away in a lazy

[135-050]selfish life is the capacity for ruth, compunction, or any unselfish

[135-051]regret—which we may come to long for as one in slow death longs to

[135-052]feel laceration, rather than be conscious of a widening margin where

[135-053]consciousness once was. Mirah’s purse was a handsome one—a gift to

[135-054]her, which she had been unable to reflect about giving away—and

[135-055]Lapidoth presently found himself outside of his reverie, considering

[135-056]what the purse would fetch in addition to the sum it contained, and

[135-057]what prospect there was of his being able to get more from his daughter

[135-058]without submitting to adopt a penitential form of life under the eyes

[135-059]of that formidable son. On such a subject his susceptibilities were

[135-060]still lively.

[135-061]Meanwhile Mirah had entered the house with her power of reticence

[135-062]overcome by the cruelty of her pain. She found her brother quietly

[135-063]reading and sifting old manuscripts of his own, which he meant to

[135-064]consign to [[Daniel-Deronda|Deronda]]. In the reaction from the long effort to master

[135-065]herself, she fell down before him and clasped his knees, sobbing, and

[135-066]crying, “Ezra, Ezra!”

[135-067]He did not speak. His alarm for her spending itself on conceiving the

[135-068]cause of her distress, the more striking from the novelty in her of

[135-069]this violent manifestation. But Mirah’s own longing was to be able to

[135-070]speak and tell him the cause. Presently she raised her hand, and still

[135-071]sobbing, said brokenly,

[135-072]“Ezra, my father! our father! He followed me. I wanted him to come in.

[135-073]I said you would let him come in. And he said No, he would not—not

[135-074]now, but to-morrow. And he begged for money from me. And I gave him my

[135-075]purse, and he went away.”

[135-076]Mirah’s words seemed to herself to express all the misery she felt in

[135-077]them. Her brother found them less grievous than his preconceptions, and

[135-078]said gently, “Wait for calm, Mirah, and then tell me all,”—putting off

[135-079]her hat and laying his hands tenderly on her head. She felt the

[135-080]soothing influence, and in a few minutes told him as exactly as she

[135-081]could all that had happened.

[135-082]“He will not come to-morrow,” said Mordecai. Neither of them said to

[135-083]the other what they both thought, namely, that he might watch for

[135-084]Mirah’s outgoings and beg from her again.

[135-085]“Seest thou,” he presently added, “our lot is the lot of Israel. The

[135-086]grief and the glory are mingled as the smoke and the flame. It is

[135-087]because we children have inherited the good that we feel the evil.

[135-088]These things are wedded for us, as our father was wedded to our mother.”

[135-089]The surroundings were of Brompton, but the voice might have come from a

[135-090]Rabbi transmitting the sentences of an elder time to be registered in

[135-091]_Babli_—by which (to our ears) affectionate-sounding diminutive is

[135-092]meant the voluminous Babylonian Talmud. “The Omnipresent,” said a

[135-093]Rabbi, “is occupied in making marriages.” The levity of the saying lies

[135-094]in the ear of him who hears it; for by marriages the speaker meant all

[135-095]the wondrous combinations of the universe whose issue makes our good

[135-096]and evil.

[135-097]CHAPTER LXIII.

[135-098]“Moses, trotz seiner Befeindung der Kunst, dennoch selber ein großer

[135-099]Künstler war und den wahren Künstlergeist besaß. Nur war dieser

[135-100]Künstlergeist bei ihm, wie bei seinen ägyptischen Landsleuten, nur

[135-101]auf das Kolossale und Unverwüstliche gerichtet. Aber nicht wie die

[135-102]Ägypter formierte er seine Kunstwerke aus Backstein und Granit,

[135-103]sondern er baute Menschenpyramiden, er meisselte Menschenobelisken,

[135-104]er nahm einen armen Hirtenstamm und schuf daraus ein Volk, das

[135-105]ebenfalls den Jahrhunderten trotzen sollte . . . er schuf

[135-106]Israel!”—HEINE: _Geständnisse_.

[135-107]Imagine the difference in Deronda’s state of mind when he left England

[135-108]and when he returned to it. He had set out for [[genoa|Genoa]] in total

[135-109]uncertainty how far the actual bent of his wishes and affections would

[135-110]be encouraged—how far the claims revealed to him might draw him into

[135-111]new paths, far away from the tracks his thoughts had lately been

[135-112]pursuing with a consent of desire which uncertainty made dangerous. He

[135-113]came back with something like a discovered charter warranting the

[135-114]inherited right that his ambition had begun to yearn for: he came back

[135-115]with what was better than freedom—with a duteous bond which his

[135-116]experience had been preparing him to accept gladly, even if it had been

[135-117]attended with no promise of satisfying a secret passionate longing

[135-118]never yet allowed to grow into a hope. But now he dared avow to himself

[135-119]the hidden selection of his love. Since the hour when he left the house

[135-120]at Chelsea in full-hearted silence under the effect of Mirah’s farewell

[135-121]look and words—their exquisite appealingness stirring in him that

[135-122]deep-laid care for womanhood which had begun when his own lip was like

[135-123]a girl’s—her hold on his feeling had helped him to be blameless in

[135-124]word and deed under the difficult circumstances we know of. There

[135-125]seemed no likelihood that he could ever woo this creature who had

[135-126]become dear to him amidst associations that forbade wooing; yet she had

[135-127]taken her place in his soul as a beloved type—reducing the power of

[135-128]other fascination and making a difference in it that became deficiency.

[135-129]The influence had been continually strengthened. It had lain in the

[135-130]course of poor [[Gwendolen-Harleth|Gwendolen]]’s lot that her dependence on Deronda tended to

[135-131]rouse in him the enthusiasm of self-martyring pity rather than of

[135-132]personal love, and his less constrained tenderness flowed with the

[135-133]fuller stream toward an indwelling image in all things unlike

[135-134]Gwendolen. Still more, his relation to Mordecai had brought with it a

[135-135]new nearness to Mirah which was not the less agitating because there

[135-136]was no apparent change in his position toward her; and she had

[135-137]inevitably been bound up in all the thoughts that made him shrink from

[135-138]an issue disappointing to her brother. This process had not gone on

[135-139]unconsciously in Deronda: he was conscious of it as we are of some

[135-140]covetousness that it would be better to nullify by encouraging other

[135-141]thoughts than to give it the insistency of confession even to

[135-142]ourselves: but the jealous fire had leaped out at [[Hans-Meyrick|Hans]]’s pretensions,

[135-143]and when his mother accused him of being in love with a Jewess any

[135-144]evasion suddenly seemed an infidelity. His mother had compelled him to

[135-145]a decisive acknowledgment of his love, as Joseph Kalonymos had

[135-146]compelled him to a definite expression of his resolve. This new state

[135-147]of decision wrought on Deronda with a force which surprised even

[135-148]himself. There was a release of all the energy which had long been

[135-149]spent in self-checking and suppression because of doubtful conditions;

[135-150]and he was ready to laugh at his own impetuosity when, as he neared

[135-151]England on his way from [[mainz|Mainz]], he felt the remaining distance more and

[135-152]more of an obstruction. It was as if he had found an added soul in

[135-153]finding his ancestry—his judgment no longer wandering in the mazes of

[135-154]impartial sympathy, but choosing, with that partiality which is man’s

[135-155]best strength, the closer fellowship that makes sympathy

[135-156]practical—exchanging that bird’s eye reasonableness which soars to

[135-157]avoid preference and loses all sense of quality for the generous

[135-158]reasonableness of drawing shoulder to shoulder with men of like

[135-159]inheritance. He wanted now to be again with Mordecai, to pour forth

[135-160]instead of restraining his feeling, to admit agreement and maintain

[135-161]dissent, and all the while to find Mirah’s presence without the

[135-162]embarrassment of obviously seeking it, to see her in the light of a new

[135-163]possibility, to interpret her looks and words from a new

[135-164]starting-point. He was not greatly alarmed about the effect of Hans’s

[135-165]attentions, but he had a presentiment that her feeling toward himself

[135-166]had from the first lain in a channel from which it was not likely to be

[135-167]diverted into love. To astonish a woman by turning into her lover when

[135-168]she has been thinking of you merely as a Lord Chancellor is what a man

[135-169]naturally shrinks from: he is anxious to create an easier transition.

[135-170]What wonder that Deronda saw no other course than to go straight from

[135-171]the [[london|London]] railway station to the lodgings in that small square in

[135-172]Brompton? Every argument was in favor of his losing no time. He had

[135-173]promised to run down the next day to see Lady Mallinger at the Abbey,

[135-174]and it was already sunset. He wished to deposit the precious chest with

[135-175]Mordecai, who would study its contents, both in his absence and in

[135-176]company with him; and that he should pay this visit without pause would

[135-177]gratify Mordecai’s heart. Hence, and for other reasons, it gratified

[135-178]Deronda’s heart. The strongest tendencies of his nature were rushing in

[135-179]one current—the fervent affectionateness which made him delight in

[135-180]meeting the wish of beings near to him, and the imaginative need of

[135-181]some far-reaching relation to make the horizon of his immediate, daily

[135-182]acts. It has to be admitted that in this classical, romantic,

[135-183]world-historic position of his, bringing as it were from its

[135-184]hiding-place his hereditary armor, he wore—but so, one must suppose,

[135-185]did the most ancient heroes, whether Semitic or Japhetic—the summer

[135-186]costume of his contemporaries. He did not reflect that the drab tints

[135-187]were becoming to him, for he rarely went to the expense of such

[135-188]thinking; but his own depth of coloring, which made the becomingness,

[135-189]got an added radiance in the eyes, a fleeting and returning glow in the

[135-190]skin, as he entered the house wondering what exactly he should find. He

[135-191]made his entrance as noiseless as possible.
