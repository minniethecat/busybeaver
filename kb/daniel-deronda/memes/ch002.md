[002-001]In the evening the same room was more stiflingly heated, was brilliant

[002-002]with gas and with the costumes of ladies who floated their trains along

[002-003]it or were seated on the ottomans.

[002-004]The Nereid in sea-green robes and silver ornaments, with a pale

[002-005]sea-green feather fastened in silver falling backward over her green

[002-006]hat and light brown hair, was [[Gwendolen-Harleth|Gwendolen Harleth]]. She was under the

[002-007]wing, or rather soared by the shoulder, of the lady who had sat by her

[002-008]at the [[gambling|roulette]]-table; and with them was a gentleman with a white

[002-009]mustache and clipped hair: solid-browed, stiff and German. They were

[002-010]walking about or standing to chat with acquaintances, and Gwendolen was

[002-011]much observed by the seated groups.

[002-012]“A striking girl—that Miss Harleth—unlike others.”

[002-013]“Yes, she has got herself up as a sort of serpent now—all green and

[002-014]silver, and winds her neck about a little more than usual.”

[002-015]“Oh, she must always be doing something extraordinary. She is that kind

[002-016]of girl, I fancy. Do you think her pretty, Mr. Vandernoodt?”

[002-017]“Very. A man might risk hanging for her—I mean a fool might.”

[002-018]“You like a _nez retroussé_, then, and long narrow eyes?”

[002-019]“When they go with such an _ensemble_.”

[002-020]“The _ensemble du serpent_?”

[002-021]“If you will. Woman was tempted by a serpent; why not man?”

[002-022]“She is certainly very graceful; but she wants a tinge of color in her

[002-023]cheeks. It is a sort of Lamia beauty she has.”

[002-024]“On the contrary, I think her complexion one of her chief charms. It is

[002-025]a warm paleness; it looks thoroughly healthy. And that delicate nose

[002-026]with its gradual little upward curve is distracting. And then her

[002-027]mouth—there never was a prettier mouth, the lips curled backward so

[002-028]finely, eh, Mackworth?”

[002-029]“Think so? I cannot endure that sort of mouth. It looks so

[002-030]self-complacent, as if it knew its own beauty—the curves are too

[002-031]immovable. I like a mouth that trembles more.”

[002-032]“For my part, I think her odious,” said a dowager. “It is wonderful

[002-033]what unpleasant girls get into vogue. Who are these Langens? Does

[002-034]anybody know them?”

[002-035]“They are quite _comme il faut_. I have dined with them several times

[002-036]at the _Russie_. The baroness is English. Miss Harleth calls her

[002-037]cousin. The girl herself is thoroughly well-bred, and as clever as

[002-038]possible.”

[002-039]“Dear me! and the baron?”.

[002-040]“A very good furniture picture.”

[002-041]“Your baroness is always at the roulette-table,” said Mackworth. “I

[002-042]fancy she has taught the girl to gamble.”

[002-043]“Oh, the old woman plays a very sober game; drops a ten-franc piece

[002-044]here and there. The girl is more headlong. But it is only a freak.”

[002-045]“I hear she has lost all her winnings to-day. Are they rich? Who knows?”

[002-046]“Ah, who knows? Who knows that about anybody?” said Mr. Vandernoodt,

[002-047]moving off to join the Langens.

[002-048]The remark that Gwendolen wound her neck about more than usual this

[002-049]evening was true. But it was not that she might carry out the serpent

[002-050]idea more completely: it was that she watched for any chance of seeing

[002-051][[Daniel-Deronda|Deronda]], so that she might inquire about this stranger, under whose

[002-052]measuring gaze she was still wincing. At last her opportunity came.

[002-053]“Mr. Vandernoodt, you know everybody,” said Gwendolen, not too eagerly,

[002-054]rather with a certain languor of utterance which she sometimes gave to

[002-055]her clear soprano. “Who is that near the door?”

[002-056]“There are half a dozen near the door. Do you mean that old Adonis in

[002-057]the George the Fourth wig?”

[002-058]“No, no; the dark-haired young man on the right with the dreadful

[002-059]expression.”

[002-060]“Dreadful, do you call it? I think he is an uncommonly fine fellow.”

[002-061]“But who is he?”

[002-062]“He is lately come to our hotel with [[Sir-Hugo-Mallinger|Sir Hugo Mallinger]].”

[002-063]“Sir Hugo Mallinger?”

[002-064]“Yes. Do you know him?”

[002-065]“No.” (Gwendolen colored slightly.) “He has a place near us, but he

[002-066]never comes to it. What did you say was the name of that gentleman near

[002-067]the door?”

[002-068]“Deronda—Mr. Deronda.”

[002-069]“What a delightful name! Is he an Englishman?”

[002-070]“Yes. He is reported to be rather closely related to the baronet. You

[002-071]are interested in him?”

[002-072]“Yes. I think he is not like young men in general.”

[002-073]“And you don’t admire young men in general?”

[002-074]“Not in the least. I always know what they will say. I can’t at all

[002-075]guess what this Mr. Deronda would say. What _does_ he say?”

[002-076]“Nothing, chiefly. I sat with his party for a good hour last night on

[002-077]the terrace, and he never spoke—and was not smoking either. He looked

[002-078]bored.”

[002-079]“Another reason why I should like to know him. I am always bored.”

[002-080]“I should think he would be charmed to have an introduction. Shall I

[002-081]bring it about? Will you allow it, baroness?”

[002-082]“Why not?—since he is related to Sir Hugo Mallinger. It is a new

[002-083]_rôle_ of yours, Gwendolen, to be always bored,” continued Madame von

[002-084]Langen, when Mr. Vandernoodt had moved away. “Until now you have always

[002-085]seemed eager about something from morning till night.”

[002-086]“That is just because I am bored to death. If I am to leave off play I

[002-087]must break my arm or my collar-bone. I must make something happen;

[002-088]unless you will go into Switzerland and take me up the Matterhorn.”

[002-089]“Perhaps this Mr. Deronda’s acquaintance will do instead of the

[002-090]Matterhorn.”

[002-091]“Perhaps.”

[002-092]But Gwendolen did not make Deronda’s acquaintance on this occasion. Mr.

[002-093]Vandernoodt did not succeed in bringing him up to her that evening, and

[002-094]when she re-entered her own room she found a letter recalling her home.

[002-095]CHAPTER II.

[002-096]This man contrives a secret ’twixt us two,

[002-097]That he may quell me with his meeting eyes

[002-098]Like one who quells a lioness at bay.

[002-099]This was the letter Gwendolen found on her table:

[002-100]DEAREST CHILD.—I have been expecting to hear from you for a week. In

[002-101]your last you said the Langens thought of leaving [[Leubronn]] and going

[002-102]to Baden. How could you be so thoughtless as to leave me in

[002-103]uncertainty about your address? I am in the greatest anxiety lest this

[002-104]should not reach you. In any case, you were to come home at the end of

[002-105]September, and I must now entreat you to return as quickly as

[002-106]possible, for if you spent all your money it would be out of my power

[002-107]to send you any more, and you must not borrow of the Langens, for I

[002-108]could not repay them. This is the sad truth, my child—I wish I could

[002-109]prepare you for it better—but a dreadful calamity has befallen us

[002-110]all. You know nothing about business and will not understand it; but

[002-111]Grapnell & Co. have failed for a million, and we are totally

[002-112]ruined—your aunt Gascoigne as well as I, only that your uncle has his

[002-113]benefice, so that by putting down their carriage and getting interest

[002-114]for the boys, the family can go on. All the property our poor father

[002-115]saved for us goes to pay the liabilities. There is nothing I can call

[002-116]my own. It is better you should know this at once, though it rends my

[002-117]heart to have to tell it you. Of course we cannot help thinking what a

[002-118]pity it was that you went away just when you did. But I shall never

[002-119]reproach you, my dear child; I would save you from all trouble if I

[002-120]could. On your way home you will have time to prepare yourself for the

[002-121]change you will find. We shall perhaps leave [[Offendene]] at once, for we

[002-122]hope that Mr. Haynes, who wanted it before, may be ready to take it

[002-123]off my hands. Of course we cannot go to the rectory—there is not a

[002-124]corner there to spare. We must get some hut or other to shelter us,

[002-125]and we must live on your uncle Gascoigne’s charity, until I see what

[002-126]else can be done. I shall not be able to pay the debts to the

[002-127]tradesmen besides the servants’ wages. Summon up your fortitude, my

[002-128]dear child; we must resign ourselves to God’s will. But it is hard to

[002-129]resign one’s self to Mr. Lassman’s wicked recklessness, which they say

[002-130]was the cause of the failure. Your poor sisters can only cry with me

[002-131]and give me no help. If you were once here, there might be a break in

[002-132]the cloud—I always feel it impossible that you can have been meant

[002-133]for poverty. If the Langens wish to remain abroad, perhaps you can put

[002-134]yourself under some one else’s care for the journey. But come as soon

[002-135]as you can to your afflicted and loving mamma,

[002-136]FANNY DAVILOW.

[002-137]The first effect of this letter on Gwendolen was half-stupefying. The

[002-138]implicit confidence that her destiny must be one of luxurious ease,

[002-139]where any trouble that occurred would be well clad and provided for,

[002-140]had been stronger in her own mind than in her mamma’s, being fed there

[002-141]by her youthful blood and that sense of superior claims which made a

[002-142]large part of her consciousness. It was almost as difficult for her to

[002-143]believe suddenly that her position had become one of poverty and of

[002-144]humiliating dependence, as it would have been to get into the strong

[002-145]current of her blooming life the chill sense that her death would

[002-146]really come. She stood motionless for a few minutes, then tossed off

[002-147]her hat and automatically looked in the glass. The coils of her smooth

[002-148]light-brown hair were still in order perfect enough for a ball-room;

[002-149]and as on other nights, Gwendolen might have looked lingeringly at

[002-150]herself for pleasure (surely an allowable indulgence); but now she took

[002-151]no conscious note of her reflected beauty, and simply stared right

[002-152]before her as if she had been jarred by a hateful sound and was waiting

[002-153]for any sign of its cause. By-and-by she threw herself in the corner of

[002-154]the red velvet sofa, took up the letter again and read it twice

[002-155]deliberately, letting it at last fall on the ground, while she rested

[002-156]her clasped hands on her lap and sat perfectly still, shedding no

[002-157]tears. Her impulse was to survey and resist the situation rather than

[002-158]to wail over it. There was no inward exclamation of “Poor mamma!” Her

[002-159]mamma had never seemed to get much enjoyment out of life, and if

[002-160]Gwendolen had been at this moment disposed to feel pity she would have

[002-161]bestowed it on herself—for was she not naturally and rightfully the

[002-162]chief object of her mamma’s anxiety too? But it was anger, it was

[002-163]resistance that possessed her; it was bitter vexation that she had lost

[002-164]her gains at roulette, whereas if her luck had continued through this

[002-165]one day she would have had a handsome sum to carry home, or she might

[002-166]have gone on playing and won enough to support them all. Even now was

[002-167]it not possible? She had only four napoleons left in her purse, but she

[002-168]possessed some ornaments which she could sell: a practice so common in

[002-169]stylish society at German baths that there was no need to be ashamed of

[002-170]it; and even if she had not received her mamma’s letter, she would

[002-171]probably have decided to get money for an Etruscan necklace which she

[002-172]happened not to have been wearing since her arrival; nay, she might

[002-173]have done so with an agreeable sense that she was living with some

[002-174]intensity and escaping humdrum. With ten louis at her disposal and a

[002-175]return of her former luck, which seemed probable, what could she do

[002-176]better than go on playing for a few days? If her friends at home

[002-177]disapproved of the way in which she got the money, as they certainly

[002-178]would, still the money would be there. Gwendolen’s imagination dwelt on

[002-179]this course and created agreeable consequences, but not with unbroken

[002-180]confidence and rising certainty as it would have done if she had been

[002-181]touched with the gambler’s mania. She had gone to the roulette-table

[002-182]not because of passion, but in search of it: her mind was still sanely

[002-183]capable of picturing balanced probabilities, and while the chance of

[002-184]winning allured her, the chance of losing thrust itself on her with

[002-185]alternate strength and made a vision from which her pride sank

[002-186]sensitively. For she was resolved not to tell the Langens that any

[002-187]misfortune had befallen her family, or to make herself in any way

[002-188]indebted to their [[sympathy|compassion]]; and if she were to part with her jewelry

[002-189]to any observable extent, they would interfere by inquiries and

[002-190]remonstrances. The course that held the least risk of intolerable

[002-191]annoyance was to raise money on her necklace early in the morning, tell

[002-192]the Langens that her mother desired her immediate return without giving

[002-193]a reason, and take the train for Brussels that evening. She had no maid

[002-194]with her, and the Langens might make difficulties about her returning

[002-195]home, but her will was peremptory.
