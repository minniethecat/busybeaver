[106-001]“Ah, the dear head, the dear head!” said [[Mordecai]], in a low loving

[106-002]tone, laying his thin hand gently on the curls.

[106-003]“You are very ill, Ezra,” said [[Mirah-Lapidoth|Mirah]], sadly looking at him with more

[106-004]observation.

[106-005]“Yes, dear child, I shall not be long with you in the body,” was the

[106-006]quiet answer.

[106-007]“Oh, I will love you and we will talk to each other,” said Mirah, with

[106-008]a sweet outpouring of her words, as spontaneous as bird-notes. “I will

[106-009]tell you everything, and you will teach me:—you will teach me to be a

[106-010]good Jewess—what she would have liked me to be. I shall always be with

[106-011]you when I am not working. For I work now. I shall get money to keep

[106-012]us. Oh, I have had such good friends.”

[106-013]Mirah until now had quite forgotten that any one was by, but here she

[106-014]turned with the prettiest attitude, keeping one hand on her brother’s

[106-015]arm while she looked at Mrs. Meyrick and [[Daniel-Deronda|Deronda]]. The little mother’s

[106-016]happy emotion in witnessing this meeting of brother and sister had

[106-017]already won her to Mordecai, who seemed to her really to have more

[106-018]dignity and refinement than she had felt obliged to believe in from

[106-019]Deronda’s account.

[106-020]“See this dear lady!” said Mirah. “I was a stranger, a poor wanderer,

[106-021]and she believed in me, and has treated me as a daughter. Please give

[106-022]my brother your hand,” she added, beseechingly, taking Mrs. Meyrick’s

[106-023]hand and putting it in Mordecai’s, then pressing them both with her own

[106-024]and lifting them to her lips.

[106-025]“The Eternal Goodness has been with you,” said Mordecai. “You have

[106-026]helped to fulfill our mother’s prayer.”

[106-027]“I think we will go now, shall we?—and return later,” said Deronda,

[106-028]laying a gentle pressure on Mrs. Meyrick’s arm, and she immediately

[106-029]complied. He was afraid of any reference to the facts about himself

[106-030]which he had kept back from Mordecai, and he felt no uneasiness now in

[106-031]the thought of the brother and sister being alone together.

[106-032]CHAPTER XLVIII.

[106-033]’Tis hard and ill-paid task to order all things beforehand by the rule

[106-034]of our own security, as is well hinted by Machiavelli concerning

[106-035]Cæsar Borgia, who, saith he, had thought of all that might occur on

[106-036]his father’s death, and had provided against every evil chance save

[106-037]only one: it had never come into his mind that when his father died,

[106-038]his own death would quickly follow.

[106-039][[Henleigh-Grandcourt|Grandcourt]]’s importance as a subject of this realm was of the grandly

[106-040]passive kind which consists in the inheritance of land. Political and

[106-041]social movements touched him only through the wire of his rental, and

[106-042]his most careful biographer need not have read up on

[106-043]Schleswig-Holstein, the policy of Bismarck, trade-unions, household

[106-044]suffrage, or even the last commercial panic. He glanced over the best

[106-045]newspaper columns on these topics, and his views on them can hardly be

[106-046]said to have wanted breadth, since he embraced all Germans, all

[106-047]commercial men, and all voters liable to use the wrong kind of soap,

[106-048]under the general epithet of “brutes;” but he took no action on these

[106-049]much-agitated questions beyond looking from under his eyelids at any

[106-050]man who mentioned them, and retaining a silence which served to shake

[106-051]the opinions of timid thinkers.

[106-052]But Grandcourt, within his own sphere of interest, showed some of the

[106-053]qualities which have entered into triumphal diplomacy of the wildest

[106-054]continental sort.

[106-055]No movement of [[Gwendolen-Harleth|Gwendolen]] in relation to Deronda escaped him. He would

[106-056]have denied that he was jealous; because jealousy would have implied

[106-057]some doubt of his own power to hinder what he had determined against.

[106-058]That his wife should have more inclination to another man’s society

[106-059]than to his own would not pain him: what he required was that she

[106-060]should be as fully aware as she would have been of a locked hand-cuff,

[106-061]that her inclination was helpless to decide anything in contradiction

[106-062]with his resolve. However much of vacillating whim there might have

[106-063]been in his entrance on matrimony, there was no vacillating in his

[106-064]interpretation of the bond. He had not repented of his marriage; it had

[106-065]really brought more of aim into his life, new objects to exert his will

[106-066]upon; and he had not repented of his choice. His taste was fastidious,

[106-067]and Gwendolen satisfied it: he would not have liked a wife who had not

[106-068]received some elevation of rank from him; nor one who did not command

[106-069]admiration by her mien and beauty; nor one whose nails were not of the

[106-070]right shape; nor one the lobe of whose ear was at all too large and

[106-071]red; nor one who, even if her nails and ears were right, was at the

[106-072]same time a ninny, unable to make spirited answers. These requirements

[106-073]may not seem too exacting to refined contemporaries whose own ability

[106-074]to fall in love has been held in suspense for lack of indispensable

[106-075]details; but fewer perhaps may follow him in his contentment that his

[106-076]wife should be in a temper which would dispose her to fly out if she

[106-077]dared, and that she should have been urged into marrying him by other

[106-078]feelings than passionate attachment. Still, for those who prefer

[106-079]command to love, one does not see why the habit of mind should change

[106-080]precisely at the point of matrimony.

[106-081]Grandcourt did not feel that he had chosen the wrong wife; and having

[106-082]taken on himself the part of husband, he was not going in any way to be

[106-083]fooled, or allow himself to be seen in a light that could be regarded

[106-084]as pitiable. This was his state of mind—not jealousy; still, his

[106-085]behavior in some respects was as like jealousy as yellow is to yellow,

[106-086]which color we know may be the effect of very different causes.

[106-087]He had come up to town earlier than usual because he wished to be on

[106-088]the spot for legal consultation as to the arrangements of his will, the

[106-089]transference of mortgages, and that transaction with his uncle about

[106-090]the succession to [[diplow-hall|Diplow]], which the bait of ready money, adroitly

[106-091]dangled without importunity, had finally won him to agree upon. But

[106-092]another acceptable accompaniment of his being in town was the

[106-093]presentation of himself with the beautiful bride whom he had chosen to

[106-094]marry in spite of what other people might have expected of him. It is

[106-095]true that Grandcourt went about with the sense that he did not care a

[106-096]languid curse for any one’s admiration: but this state of not-caring,

[106-097]just as much as desire, required its related object—namely, a world of

[106-098]admiring or envying spectators: for if you are fond of looking stonily

[106-099]at smiling persons—the persons must be and they must smile—a

[106-100]rudimentary truth which is surely forgotten by those who complain of

[106-101]mankind as generally contemptible, since any other aspect of the race

[106-102]must disappoint the voracity of their contempt. Grandcourt, in town for

[106-103]the first time with his wife, had his non-caring abstinence from curses

[106-104]enlarged and diversified by splendid receptions, by conspicuous rides

[106-105]and drives, by presentations of himself with her on all distinguished

[106-106]occasions. He wished her to be sought after; he liked that “fellows”

[106-107]should be eager to talk with her and escort her within his observation;

[106-108]there was even a kind of lofty coquetry on her part that he would not

[106-109]have objected to. But what he did not like were her ways in relation to

[106-110]Deronda.

[106-111]After the musical party at Lady Mallinger’s, when Grandcourt had

[106-112]observed the dialogue on the settee as keenly as [[Hans-Meyrick|Hans]] had done, it was

[106-113]characteristic of him that he named Deronda for invitation along with

[106-114]the Mallingers, tenaciously avoiding the possible suggestion to

[106-115]anybody concerned that Deronda’s presence or absence could be of the

[106-116]least importance to him; and he made no direct observation to Gwendolen

[106-117]on her behavior that evening, lest the expression of his disgust should

[106-118]be a little too strong to satisfy his own pride. But a few days

[106-119]afterward he remarked, without being careful of the _à propos_,

[106-120]“Nothing makes a woman more of a gawky than looking out after people

[106-121]and showing tempers in public. A woman ought to have good manners. Else

[106-122]it’s intolerable to appear with her.”

[106-123]Gwendolen made the expected application, and was not without alarm at

[106-124]the notion of being a gawky. For she, too, with her melancholy distaste

[106-125]for things, preferred that her distaste should include admirers. But

[106-126]the sense of overhanging rebuke only intensified the strain of

[106-127]expectation toward any meeting with Deronda. The novelty and excitement

[106-128]of her town life was like the hurry and constant change of foreign

[106-129]travel; whatever might be the inward despondency, there was a programme

[106-130]to be fulfilled, not without gratification to many-sided self. But, as

[106-131]always happens with a deep interest, the comparatively rare occasions

[106-132]on which she could exchange any words with Deronda had a diffusive

[106-133]effect in her consciousness, magnifying their communication with each

[106-134]other, and therefore enlarging the place she imagined it to have in his

[106-135]mind. How could Deronda help this? He certainly did not avoid her;

[106-136]rather he wished to convince her by every delicate indirect means that

[106-137]her confidence in him had not been indiscreet since it had not lowered

[106-138]his respect. Moreover he liked being near her—how could it be

[106-139]otherwise? She was something more than a problem: she was a lovely

[106-140]woman, for the turn of whose mind and fate he had a care which, however

[106-141]futile it might be, kept soliciting him as a [[duty|responsibility]], perhaps

[106-142]all the more that, when he dared to think of his own future, he saw it

[106-143]lying far away from this splendid sad-hearted creature, who, because he

[106-144]had once been impelled to arrest her attention momentarily, as he might

[106-145]have seized her arm with warning to hinder her from stepping where

[106-146]there was danger, had turned to him with a beseeching persistent need.

[106-147]One instance in which Grandcourt stimulated a feeling in Gwendolen that

[106-148]he would have liked to suppress without seeming to care about it, had

[106-149]relation to Mirah. Gwendolen’s inclination lingered over the project of

[106-150]the [[music|singing]] lessons as a sort of obedience to Deronda’s advice, but day

[106-151]followed day with that want of perceived leisure which belongs to lives

[106-152]where there is no work to mark off intervals; and the continual

[106-153]liability to Grandcourt’s presence and surveillance seemed to flatten

[106-154]every effort to the level of the boredom which his manner expressed;

[106-155]his negative mind was as diffusive as fog, clinging to all objects, and

[106-156]spoiling all contact.

[106-157]But one morning when they were breakfasting, Gwendolen, in a recurrent

[106-158]fit of determination to exercise the old spirit, said, dallying

[106-159]prettily over her prawns without eating them,

[106-160]“I think of making myself accomplished while we are in town, and having

[106-161]singing lessons.”

[106-162]“Why?” said Grandcourt, languidly.

[106-163]“Why?” echoed Gwendolen, playing at sauciness; “because I can’t eat

[106-164]_pâté de foie gras_ to make me sleepy, and I can’t smoke, and I can’t

[106-165]go to the club to make me like to come away again—I want a variety of

[106-166]_ennui_. What would be the most convenient time, when you are busy with

[106-167]your lawyers and people, for me to have lessons from that little

[106-168]Jewess, whose singing is getting all the rage.”

[106-169]“Whenever you like,” said Grandcourt, pushing away his plate, and

[106-170]leaning back in his chair while he looked at her with his most

[106-171]lizard-like expression and, played with the ears of the tiny spaniel on

[106-172]his lap (Gwendolen had taken a dislike to the dogs because they fawned

[106-173]on him).

[106-174]Then he said, languidly, “I don’t see why a lady should sing. Amateurs

[106-175]make fools of themselves. A lady can’t risk herself in that way in

[106-176]company. And one doesn’t want to hear squalling in private.”

[106-177]“I like frankness: that seems to me a husband’s great charm,” said

[106-178]Gwendolen, with her little upward movement of her chin, as she turned

[106-179]her eyes away from his, and lifting a prawn before her, looked at the

[106-180]boiled ingenuousness of its eyes as preferable to the lizard’s. “But;”

[106-181]she added, having devoured her [[shame|mortification]], “I suppose you don’t

[106-182]object to Miss [[Lapidoth]]’s singing at our party on the fourth? I thought

[106-183]of engaging her. Lady Brackenshaw had her, you know: and the Raymonds,

[106-184]who are very particular about their [[art|music]]. And Mr. Deronda, who is a

[106-185]musician himself and a first-rate judge, says there is no singing in

[106-186]such good taste as hers for a drawing-room. I think his opinion is an

[106-187]authority.”
