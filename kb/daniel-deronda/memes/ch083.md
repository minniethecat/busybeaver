[083-001]“But these are as impossible as the legs of Raphael’s Alcibiades,” said

[083-002][[Daniel-Deronda|Deronda]].

[083-003]“Then they are good ideally,” said [[Hans-Meyrick|Hans]]. “Agrippa’s legs were possibly

[083-004]bad; I idealize that and make them impossibly bad. [[art|Art]], my Eugenius,

[083-005]must intensify. But never mind the legs now: the third sketch in the

[083-006]series is Berenice exulting in the prospects of being Empress of Rome,

[083-007]when the news has come that Vespasian is declared Emperor and her lover

[083-008]Titus his successor.”

[083-009]“You must put a scroll in her mouth, else people will not understand

[083-010]that. You can’t tell that in a picture.”

[083-011]“It will make them feel their ignorance then—an excellent æsthetic

[083-012]effect. The fourth is, Titus sending Berenice away from Rome after she

[083-013]has shared his palace for ten years—both reluctant, both sad—_invitus

[083-014]invitam_, as Suetonius hath it. I’ve found a model for the Roman brute.”

[083-015]“Shall you make Berenice look fifty? She must have been that.”

[083-016]“No, no; a few mature touches to show the lapse of time. Dark-eyed

[083-017]beauty wears well, hers particularly. But now, here is the fifth:

[083-018]Berenice seated lonely on the ruins of Jerusalem. That is pure

[083-019]imagination. That is what ought to have been—perhaps was. Now, see how

[083-020]I tell a pathetic negative. Nobody knows what became of her—that is

[083-021]finely indicated by the series coming to a close. There is no sixth

[083-022]picture.” Here Hans pretended to speak with a gasping sense of

[083-023]sublimity, and drew back his head with a frown, as if looking for a

[083-024]like impression on Deronda. “I break off in the Homeric style. The

[083-025]story is chipped off, so to speak, and passes with a ragged edge into

[083-026]nothing—_le néant_; can anything be more sublime, especially in

[083-027]French? The vulgar would desire to see her corpse and burial—perhaps

[083-028]her will read and her linen distributed. But now come and look at this

[083-029]on the easel. I have made some way there.”

[083-030]“That beseeching attitude is really good,” said Deronda, after a

[083-031]moment’s contemplation. “You have been very industrious in the

[083-032]Christmas holidays; for I suppose you have taken up the subject since

[083-033]you came to [[london|London]].” Neither of them had yet mentioned [[Mirah-Lapidoth|Mirah]].

[083-034]“No,” said Hans, putting touches to his picture, “I made up my mind to

[083-035]the subject before. I take that lucky chance for an augury that I am

[083-036]going to burst on the world as a great painter. I saw a splendid woman

[083-037]in the Trastevere—the grandest women there are half Jewesses—and she

[083-038]set me hunting for a fine situation of a Jewess at Rome. Like other men

[083-039]of vast learning, I ended by taking what lay on the surface. I’ll show

[083-040]you a sketch of the Trasteverina’s head when I can lay my hands on it.”

[083-041]“I should think she would be a more suitable model for Berenice,” said

[083-042]Deronda, not knowing exactly how to express his discontent.

[083-043]“Not a bit of it. The model ought to be the most beautiful Jewess in

[083-044]the world, and I have found her.”

[083-045]“Have you made yourself sure that she would like to figure in that

[083-046]character? I should think no woman would be more abhorrent to her. Does

[083-047]she quite know what you are doing?”

[083-048]“Certainly. I got her to throw herself precisely into this attitude.

[083-049]Little mother sat for Gessius Florus, and Mirah clasped her knees.”

[083-050]Here Hans went a little way off and looked at the effect of his touches.

[083-051]“I dare say she knows nothing about Berenice’s history,” said Deronda,

[083-052]feeling more indignation than he would have been able to justify.

[083-053]“Oh, yes, she does—ladies’ edition. Berenice was a fervid patriot, but

[083-054]was beguiled by love and ambition into attaching herself to the

[083-055]arch-enemy of her people. Whence the Nemesis. Mirah takes it as a

[083-056]tragic parable, and cries to think what the penitent Berenice suffered

[083-057]as she wandered back to Jerusalem and sat desolate amidst desolation.

[083-058]That was her own phrase. I couldn’t find it in my heart to tell her I

[083-059]invented that part of the story.”

[083-060]“Show me your Trasteverina,” said Deronda, chiefly in order to hinder

[083-061]himself from saying something else.

[083-062]“Shall you mind turning over that folio?” said Hans. “My studies of

[083-063]heads are all there. But they are in confusion. You will perhaps find

[083-064]her next to a crop-eared undergraduate.”

[083-065]After Deronda had been turning over the drawings a minute or two, he

[083-066]said,

[083-067]“These seem to be all Cambridge heads and bits of country. Perhaps I

[083-068]had better begin at the other end.”

[083-069]“No; you’ll find her about the middle. I emptied one folio into

[083-070]another.”

[083-071]“Is this one of your undergraduates?” said Deronda, holding up a

[083-072]drawing. “It’s an unusually agreeable face.”

[083-073]“That! Oh, that’s a man named Gascoigne—[[Rex-Gascoigne|Rex Gascoigne]]. An uncommonly

[083-074]good fellow; his upper lip, too, is good. I coached him before he got

[083-075]his scholarship. He ought to have taken honors last Easter. But he was

[083-076]ill, and has had to stay up another year. I must look him up. I want to

[083-077]know how he’s going on.”

[083-078]“Here she is, I suppose,” said Deronda, holding up a sketch of the

[083-079]Trasteverina.

[083-080]“Ah,” said Hans, looking at it rather contemptuously, “too coarse. I

[083-081]was unregenerate then.”

[083-082]Deronda was silent while he closed the folio, leaving the Trasteverina

[083-083]outside. Then clasping his coat-collar, and turning toward Hans, he

[083-084]said, “I dare say my scruples are excessive, Meyrick, but I must ask

[083-085]you to oblige me by giving up this notion.”

[083-086]Hans threw himself into a tragic attitude, and screamed, “What! my

[083-087]series—my immortal Berenice series? Think of what you are saying,

[083-088]man—destroying, as [[miltons-paradise-lost|Milton]] says, not a life but an immortality. Wait

[083-089]before you answer, that I may deposit the implements of my art and be

[083-090]ready to uproot my hair.”

[083-091]Here Hans laid down his pencil and palette, threw himself backward into

[083-092]a great chair, and hanging limply over the side, shook his long hair

[083-093]over his face, lifted his hooked fingers on each side his head, and

[083-094]looked up with comic terror at Deronda, who was obliged to smile, as he

[083-095]said,

[083-096]“Paint as many Berenices as you like, but I wish you could feel with

[083-097]me—perhaps you will, on reflection—that you should choose another

[083-098]model.”

[083-099]“Why?” said Hans, standing up, and looking serious again.

[083-100]“Because she may get into such a position that her face is likely to be

[083-101]recognized. Mrs. Meyrick and I are anxious for her that she should be

[083-102]known as an admirable singer. It is right, and she wishes it, that she

[083-103]should make herself independent. And she has excellent chances. One

[083-104]good introduction is secured already, and I am going to speak to

[083-105][[Herr-Klesmer|Klesmer]]. Her face may come to be very well known, and—well, it is

[083-106]useless to attempt to explain, unless you feel as I do. I believe that

[083-107]if Mirah saw the circumstances clearly, she would strongly object to

[083-108]being exhibited in this way—to allowing herself to be used as a model

[083-109]for a heroine of this sort.”

[083-110]As Hans stood with his thumbs in the belt of his blouse, listening to

[083-111]this speech, his face showed a growing surprise melting into amusement,

[083-112]that at last would have its way in an explosive laugh: but seeing that

[083-113]Deronda looked gravely offended, he checked himself to say, “Excuse my

[083-114]laughing, Deronda. You never gave me an advantage over you before. If

[083-115]it had been about anything but my own pictures, I should have swallowed

[083-116]every word because you said it. And so you actually believe that I

[083-117]should get my five pictures hung on the line in a conspicuous position,

[083-118]and carefully studied by the public? Zounds, man! cider-cup and conceit

[083-119]never gave me half such a beautiful dream. My pictures are likely to

[083-120]remain as private as the utmost hypersensitiveness could desire.”

[083-121]Hans turned to paint again as a way of filling up awkward pauses.

[083-122]Deronda stood perfectly still, recognizing his mistake as to publicity,

[083-123]but also conscious that his repugnance was not much diminished. He was

[083-124]the reverse of satisfied either with himself or with Hans; but the

[083-125]power of being quiet carries a man well through moments of

[083-126]embarrassment. Hans had a reverence for his friend which made him feel

[083-127]a sort of shyness at Deronda’s being in the wrong; but it was not in

[083-128]his nature to give up anything readily, though it were only a whim—or

[083-129]rather, especially if it were a whim, and he presently went on,

[083-130]painting the while,

[083-131]“But even supposing I had a public rushing after my pictures as if they

[083-132]were a railway series including nurses, babies and bonnet-boxes, I

[083-133]can’t see any justice in your objection. Every painter worth

[083-134]remembering has painted the face he admired most, as often as he could.

[083-135]It is a part of his soul that goes out into his pictures. He diffuses

[083-136]its influence in that way. He puts what he hates into a caricature. He

[083-137]puts what he adores into some sacred, heroic form. If a man could paint

[083-138]the woman he loves a thousand times as the _Stella Maris_ to put courage

[083-139]into the sailors on board a thousand ships, so much the more honor to

[083-140]her. Isn’t that better than painting a piece of staring immodesty and

[083-141]calling it by a worshipful name?”

[083-142]“Every objection can be answered if you take broad ground enough, Hans:

[083-143]no special question of conduct can be properly settled in that way,”

[083-144]said Deronda, with a touch of peremptoriness. “I might admit all your

[083-145]generalities, and yet be right in saying you ought not to publish

[083-146]Mirah’s face as a model for Berenice. But I give up the question of

[083-147]publicity. I was unreasonable there.” Deronda hesitated a moment.

[083-148]“Still, even as a private affair, there might be good reasons for your

[083-149]not indulging yourself too much in painting her from the point of view

[083-150]you mention. You must feel that her situation at present is a very

[083-151]delicate one; and until she is in more [[freedom|independence]], she should be kept

[083-152]as carefully as a bit of Venetian glass, for fear of shaking her out of

[083-153]the safe place she is lodged in. Are you quite sure of your own

[083-154]discretion? Excuse me, Hans. My having found her binds me to watch over

[083-155]her. Do you understand me?”

[083-156]“Perfectly,” said Hans, turning his face into a good-humored smile.

[083-157]“You have the very justifiable opinion of me that I am likely to

[083-158]shatter all the glass in my way, and break my own skull into the

[083-159]bargain. Quite fair. Since I got into the scrape of being born,

[083-160]everything I have liked best has been a scrape either for myself or

[083-161]somebody else. Everything I have taken to heartily has somehow turned

[083-162]into a scrape. My painting is the last scrape; and I shall be all my

[083-163]life getting out of it. You think now I shall get into a scrape at

[083-164]home. No; I am regenerate. You think I must be over head and ears in

[083-165]love with Mirah. Quite right; so I am. But you think I shall scream and

[083-166]plunge and spoil everything. There you are mistaken—excusably, but

[083-167]transcendently mistaken. I have undergone baptism by immersion. Awe

[083-168]takes care of me. Ask the little mother.”

[083-169]“You don’t reckon a hopeless love among your scrapes, then,” said

[083-170]Deronda, whose voice seemed to get deeper as Hans’s went higher.

[083-171]“I don’t mean to call mine hopeless,” said Hans, with provoking

[083-172]coolness, laying down his tools, thrusting his thumbs into his belt,

[083-173]and moving away a little, as if to contemplate his picture more

[083-174]deliberately.

[083-175]“My dear fellow, you are only preparing misery for yourself,” said

[083-176]Deronda, decisively. “She would not marry a Christian, even if she

[083-177]loved him. Have you heard her—of course you have—heard her speak of

[083-178]her people and her religion?”

[083-179]“That can’t last,” said Hans. “She will see no Jew who is tolerable.

[083-180]Every male of that race is insupportable—‘insupportably

[083-181]advancing’—his nose.”
