# Cohen Family

The Cohen family are a working-class Jewish household in London's East End, whose shop and home provide the novel's most detailed and sympathetic portrait of Anglo-Jewish domestic life. Comprising Ezra Cohen the pawnbroker, his wife, their children — including young [[Jacob-Cohen|Jacob]] — and the grandmother [[adelaide-rebekah]], they shelter [[Mordecai]] under their roof and become, through their ordinariness, a radical assertion of Jewish normalcy in Victorian fiction.

## The Cohen Household

The Cohen home is both residence and place of business — a shop with the name "Ezra Cohen" lettered above the window [069-049]. It is a space of bustling commerce and family warmth, where the sacred and the mercantile coexist without contradiction. [[Daniel-Deronda|Deronda]] visits repeatedly, drawn at first by curiosity and later by a deepening connection to the Jewish world the Cohens represent:

> his meditated second visit to Ezra Cohen's [069-120]

Deronda's first approach to the shop is marked by a peculiar intensity of feeling — "he particularly desired not to see the name of Cohen above the shop-window" [069-021] — an aversion that the narrator presents as symptomatic of his unconscious resistance to Jewish identification. Yet he returns, and the name that initially troubled him becomes the portal to his Jewish education.

The household's most significant role is as shelter for Mordecai, who lodges with them during his final illness:

> had taken up his abode under Ezra Cohen's roof [086-063]

## Key Scenes

### The Sabbath at the Cohens

Eliot's most detailed domestic portrait of the Cohens occurs during the Sabbath. When Deronda visits on a Friday evening, Ezra Cohen greets him with a reminder that reframes the visit within Jewish temporal rhythms: "this evening is the Sabbath, young gentleman" [071-005]. Deronda acknowledges the observation: "Yes; and your Sabbath begins early at this season" [071-010], a reply that reveals his growing familiarity with Jewish practice.

The Sabbath scenes at the Cohen household are rich with the texture of domestic ritual. [[adelaide-rebekah]] is dressed in her "Sabbath frock" [071-038, 071-039], a detail that grounds religious observance in the material culture of childhood. The meal, the family gathering, and the presence of the lodger Mordecai — who "had no handsome Sabbath frock" [071-193] — together create a tableau of Jewish life that is neither sentimentalised nor exoticised.

Eliot's allusion to Heine's *Prinzessin Sabbath* [071-084] in this context elevates the domestic scene, linking the Cohens' ordinary Friday evening to a broader tradition of Jewish poetry and mysticism. The Sabbath princess descends equally upon the pawnbroker's home and the scholar's study.

### The Wedding-Feast

The Cohen household provides the setting for the novel's culminating domestic scene — the wedding-feast of Mirah and Deronda:

> the wedding-feast was the entire Cohen family, with the one exception of [147-157]

This gathering brings together all the novel's Jewish characters in a moment of communal celebration. The Sabbath frock that appeared in an earlier domestic scene returns — "the Sabbath frock, maintained throughout" [147-177] — creating a symbolic continuity between the weekly rhythm of Jewish observance and this singular moment of communal joy. The Cohen family's role as hosts underscores their centrality to the novel's representation of Jewish community: the wedding of the novel's protagonists is celebrated not in a synagogue or a grand hall but in the home of a pawnbroker.

## A Portrait of Anglo-Jewish Life

Eliot's depiction of the Cohen family was groundbreaking for its time. Rather than the caricatures common in Victorian literature, the Cohens are portrayed as a real family — sometimes comic, sometimes tender, always human. Ezra Cohen jokes with his customers [070-138], makes amends for his bluntness [070-157], and presides over his household with an unpretentious warmth. He is "not clad in the sublime" [093-115], yet his very ordinariness serves Eliot's purpose: to show that Jewish identity in England encompasses the full range of human experience, from the mystical to the mercantile.

The family's domestic rituals — the Sabbath meal, the care of children, the pragmatic kindness shown to a dying lodger — normalise Jewish life for readers who might never have encountered it outside the pages of a novel. Ezra Cohen's unpoetic nature is not a flaw but a form of authentic representation: the Jewish people, Eliot insists, include pawnbrokers as well as prophets.
