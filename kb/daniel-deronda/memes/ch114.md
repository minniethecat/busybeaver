[114-001]But [[Daniel-Deronda|Deronda]] had recovered his fuller self. He was recalling his

[114-002]sensibilities to what life had been and actually was for her whose best

[114-003]years were gone, and who with the signs of suffering in her frame was

[114-004]now exerting herself to tell him of a past which was not his alone but

[114-005]also hers. His habitual shame at the acceptance of events as if they

[114-006]were his only, helped him even here. As he looked at his mother

[114-007]silently after her last words, his face regained some of its

[114-008]penetrative calm; yet it seemed to have a strangely agitating influence

[114-009]over her: her eyes were fixed on him with a sort of fascination, but

[114-010]not with any repose of maternal delight.

[114-011]“Forgive me, if I speak hastily,” he said, with diffident gravity. “Why

[114-012]have you resolved now on disclosing to me what you took care to have me

[114-013]brought up in ignorance of? Why—since you seem angry that I should be

[114-014]glad?”

[114-015]“Oh—the reasons of our actions!” said the Princess, with a ring of

[114-016]something like sarcastic scorn. “When you are as old as I am, it will

[114-017]not seem so simple a question—‘Why did you do this?’ People talk of

[114-018]their motives in a cut and dried way. Every woman is supposed to have

[114-019]the same set of motives, or else to be a monster. I am not a monster,

[114-020]but I have not felt exactly what other women feel—or say they feel,

[114-021]for fear of being thought unlike others. When you reproach me in your

[114-022]heart for sending you away from me, you mean that I ought to say I felt

[114-023]about you as other women say they feel about their children. I did

[114-024]_not_ feel that. I was glad to be freed from you. But I did well for

[114-025]you, and I gave you your father’s fortune. Do I seem now to be revoking

[114-026]everything?—Well, there are reasons. I feel many things that I cannot

[114-027]understand. A fatal illness has been growing in me for a year. I shall

[114-028]very likely not live another year. I will not deny anything I have

[114-029]done. I will not pretend to love where I have no love. But shadows are

[114-030]rising round me. Sickness makes them. If I have wronged the dead—I

[114-031]have but little time to do what I left undone.”

[114-032]The varied transitions of tone with which this speech was delivered

[114-033]were as perfect as the most accomplished actress could have made them.

[114-034]The speech was in fact a piece of what may be called sincere acting;

[114-035]this woman’s nature was one in which all feeling—and all the more when

[114-036]it was tragic as well as real—immediately became matter of conscious

[114-037]representation: experience immediately passed into drama, and she acted

[114-038]her own emotions. In a minor degree this is nothing uncommon, but in

[114-039]the Princess the acting had a rare perfection of physiognomy, voice,

[114-040]and gesture. It would not be true to say that she felt less because of

[114-041]this double consciousness: she felt—that is, her mind went

[114-042]through—all the more, but with a difference; each nucleus of pain or

[114-043]pleasure had a deep atmosphere of the excitement or spiritual

[114-044]intoxication which at once exalts and deadens. But Deronda made no

[114-045]reflection of this kind. All his thoughts hung on the purport of what

[114-046]his mother was saying; her tones and her wonderful face entered into

[114-047]his agitation without being noted. What he longed for with an awed

[114-048]desire was to know as much as she would tell him of the strange mental

[114-049]conflict under which it seemed he had been brought into the world; what

[114-050]his compassionate nature made the controlling idea within him were the

[114-051]suffering and the confession that breathed through her later words, and

[114-052]these forbade any further question, when she paused and remained

[114-053]silent, with her brow knit, her head turned a little away from him, and

[114-054]her large eyes fixed as if on something incorporeal. He must wait for

[114-055]her to speak again. She did so with strange abruptness, turning her

[114-056]eyes upon him suddenly, and saying more quickly,

[114-057]“[[Sir-Hugo-Mallinger|Sir Hugo]] has written much about you. He tells me you have a wonderful

[114-058]mind—you comprehend everything—you are wiser than he is with all his

[114-059]sixty years. You say you are glad to know that you were born a Jew. I

[114-060]am not going to tell you that I have changed my mind about that. Your

[114-061]feelings are against mine. You don’t thank me for what I did. Shall you

[114-062]comprehend your mother, or only blame her?”

[114-063]“There is not a fibre within me but makes me wish to comprehend her,”

[114-064]said Deronda, meeting her sharp gaze solemnly. “It is a bitter reversal

[114-065]of my longing to think of blaming her. What I have been most trying to

[114-066]do for fifteen years is to have some understanding of those who differ

[114-067]from myself.”

[114-068]“Then you have become unlike your grandfather in that.” said the

[114-069]mother, “though you are a young copy of him in your face. He never

[114-070]comprehended me, or if he did, he only thought of fettering me into

[114-071]obedience. I was to be what he called ‘the Jewish woman’ under pain of

[114-072]his curse. I was to feel everything I did not feel, and believe

[114-073]everything I did not believe. I was to feel awe for the bit of

[114-074]parchment in the _mezuza_ over the door; to dread lest a bit of butter

[114-075]should touch a bit of meat; to think it beautiful that men should bind

[114-076]the _tephillin_ on them, and women not,—to adore the wisdom of such

[114-077]laws, however silly they might seem to me. I was to love the long

[114-078]prayers in the ugly [[the-synagogue|synagogue]], and the howling, and the gabbling, and

[114-079]the dreadful fasts, and the tiresome feasts, and my father’s endless

[114-080]discoursing about our people, which was a thunder without meaning in my

[114-081]ears. I was to care forever about what Israel had been; and I did not

[114-082]care at all. I cared for the wide world, and all that I could represent

[114-083]in it. I hated living under the shadow of my father’s strictness.

[114-084]Teaching, teaching for everlasting—‘this you must be,’ ‘that you must

[114-085]not be’—pressed on me like a frame that got tighter and tighter as I

[114-086]grew. I wanted to live a large life, with freedom to do what every one

[114-087]else did, and be carried along in a great current, not obliged to care.

[114-088]Ah!”—here her tone changed to one of a more bitter incisiveness—“you

[114-089]are glad to have been born a Jew. You say so. That is because you have

[114-090]not been brought up as a Jew. That separateness seems sweet to you

[114-091]because I saved you from it.”

[114-092]“When you resolved on that, you meant that I should never know my

[114-093]origin?” said Deronda, impulsively. “You have at least changed in your

[114-094]feeling on that point.”

[114-095]“Yes, that was what I meant. That is what I persevered in. And it is

[114-096]not true to say that I have changed. Things have changed in spite of

[114-097]me. I am still the same [[Leonora-Halm-Eberstein|Leonora]]”—she pointed with her forefinger to

[114-098]her breast—“here within me is the same desire, the same will, the same

[114-099]choice, _but_”—she spread out her hands, palm upward, on each side of

[114-100]her, as she paused with a bitter compression of her lip, then let her

[114-101]voice fall into muffled, rapid utterance—“events come upon us like

[114-102]evil enchantments: and thoughts, feelings, apparitions in the darkness

[114-103]are events—are they not? I don’t consent. We only consent to what we

[114-104]love. I obey something tyrannic”—she spread out her hands again—“I am

[114-105]forced to be withered, to feel pain, to be dying slowly. Do I love

[114-106]that? Well, I have been forced to obey my dead father. I have been

[114-107]forced to tell you that you are a Jew, and deliver to you what he

[114-108]commanded me to deliver.”

[114-109]“I beseech you to tell me what moved you—when you were young, I

[114-110]mean—to take the course you did,” said Deronda, trying by this

[114-111]reference to the past to escape from what to him was the heart-rending

[114-112]piteousness of this mingled suffering and defiance. “I gather that my

[114-113]grandfather opposed your bent to be an artist. Though my own experience

[114-114]has been quite different, I enter into the painfulness of your

[114-115]struggle. I can imagine the hardship of an enforced renunciation.”

[114-116]“No,” said the Princess, shaking her head and folding her arms with an

[114-117]air of decision. “You are not a woman. You may try—but you can never

[114-118]imagine what it is to have a man’s force of genius in you, and yet to

[114-119]suffer the slavery of being a girl. To have a pattern cut out—‘this is

[114-120]the Jewish woman; this is what you must be; this is what you are wanted

[114-121]for; a woman’s heart must be of such a size and no larger, else it must

[114-122]be pressed small, like Chinese feet; her happiness is to be made as

[114-123]cakes are, by a fixed receipt.’ That was what my father wanted. He

[114-124]wished I had been a son; he cared for me as a make-shift link. His

[114-125]heart was set on his [[religion|Judaism]]. He hated that Jewish women should be

[114-126]thought of by the Christian world as a sort of ware to make public

[114-127]singers and actresses of. As if we were not the more enviable for that!

[114-128]That is a chance of escaping from bondage.”

[114-129]“Was my grandfather a learned man?” said Deronda, eager to know

[114-130]particulars that he feared his mother might not think of.

[114-131]She answered impatiently, putting up her hand, “Oh, yes,—and a clever

[114-132]physician—and good: I don’t deny that he was good. A man to be admired

[114-133]in a play—grand, with an iron will. Like the old Foscari before he

[114-134]pardons. But such men turn their wives and daughters into slaves. They

[114-135]would rule the world if they could; but not ruling the world, they

[114-136]throw all the weight of their will on the necks and souls of women. But

[114-137]nature sometimes thwarts them. My father had no other child than his

[114-138]daughter, and she was like himself.”

[114-139]She had folded her arms again, and looked as if she were ready to face

[114-140]some impending attempt at mastery.

[114-141]“Your father was different. Unlike me—all lovingness and affection. I

[114-142]knew I could rule him; and I made him secretly promise me, before I

[114-143]married him, that he would put no hindrance in the way of my being an

[114-144]artist. My father was on his deathbed when we were married: from the

[114-145]first he had fixed his mind on my marrying my cousin Ephraim. And when

[114-146]a woman’s will is as strong as the man’s who wants to govern her, half

[114-147]her strength must be [[secrecy|concealment]]. I meant to have my will in the end,

[114-148]but I could only have it by seeming to obey. I had an awe of my

[114-149]father—always I had had an awe of him: it was impossible to help it. I

[114-150]hated to feel awed—I wished I could have defied him openly; but I

[114-151]never could. It was what I could not imagine: I could not act it to

[114-152]myself that I should begin to defy my father openly and succeed. And I

[114-153]never would risk failure.”

[114-154]This last sentence was uttered with an abrupt emphasis, and she paused

[114-155]after it as if the words had raised a crowd of remembrances which

[114-156]obstructed speech. Her son was listening to her with feelings more and

[114-157]more highly mixed; the first sense of being repelled by the frank

[114-158]coldness which had replaced all his preconceptions of a mother’s tender

[114-159]joy in the sight of him; the first impulses of indignation at what

[114-160]shocked his most cherished emotions and principles—all these busy

[114-161]elements of collision between them were subsiding for a time, and

[114-162]making more and more room for that effort at just allowance and that

[114-163]admiration of a forcible nature whose errors lay along high pathways,

[114-164]which he would have felt if, instead of being his mother, she had been

[114-165]a stranger who had appealed to his sympathy. Still it was impossible to

[114-166]be dispassionate: he trembled lest the next thing she had to say would

[114-167]be more repugnant to him than what had gone before: he was afraid of

[114-168]the strange coercion she seemed to be under to lay her mind bare: he

[114-169]almost wished he could say, “Tell me only what is necessary,” and then

[114-170]again he felt the fascination which made him watch her and listen to

[114-171]her eagerly. He tried to recall her to particulars by asking,
