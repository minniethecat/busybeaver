[084-001]“She may rejoin her family. That is what she longs for. Her mother and

[084-002]brother are probably strict Jews.”

[084-003]“I’ll turn proselyte, if she wishes it,” said [[Hans-Meyrick|Hans]], with a shrug and a

[084-004]laugh.

[084-005]“Don’t talk nonsense, Hans. I thought you professed a serious love for

[084-006]her,” said [[Daniel-Deronda|Deronda]], getting heated.

[084-007]“So I do. You think it desperate, but I don’t.”

[084-008]“I know nothing; I can’t tell what has happened. We must be prepared

[084-009]for surprises. But I can hardly imagine a greater surprise to me than

[084-010]that there should have seemed to be anything in [[Mirah-Lapidoth|Mirah]]’s sentiments for

[084-011]you to found a romantic hope on.” Deronda felt that he was too

[084-012]contemptuous.

[084-013]“I don’t found my romantic hopes on a woman’s sentiments,” said Hans,

[084-014]perversely inclined to be the merrier when he was addressed with

[084-015]gravity. “I go to science and philosophy for my romance. Nature

[084-016]designed Mirah to fall in love with me. The amalgamation of races

[084-017]demands it—the mitigation of human ugliness demands it—the affinity

[084-018]of contrasts assures it. I am the utmost contrast to Mirah—a bleached

[084-019]Christian, who can’t sing two notes in tune. Who has a chance against

[084-020]me?”

[084-021]“I see now; it was all _persiflage_. You don’t mean a word you say,

[084-022]Meyrick,” said Deronda, laying his hand on Meyrick’s shoulder, and

[084-023]speaking in a tone of cordial relief. “I was a wiseacre to answer you

[084-024]seriously.”

[084-025]“Upon my honor I do mean it, though,” said Hans, facing round and

[084-026]laying his left hand on Deronda’s shoulder, so that their eyes fronted

[084-027]each other closely. “I am at the confessional. I meant to tell you as

[084-028]soon as you came. My mother says you are Mirah’s guardian, and she

[084-029]thinks herself responsible to you for every breath that falls on Mirah

[084-030]in her house. Well, I love her—I worship her—I won’t despair—I mean

[084-031]to deserve her.”

[084-032]“My dear fellow, you can’t do it,” said Deronda, quickly.

[084-033]“I should have said, I mean to try.”

[084-034]“You can’t keep your resolve, Hans. You used to resolve what you would

[084-035]do for your mother and sisters.”

[084-036]“You have a right to reproach me, old fellow,” said Hans, gently.

[084-037]“Perhaps I am ungenerous,” said Deronda, not apologetically, however.

[084-038]“Yet it can’t be ungenerous to warn you that you are indulging mad,

[084-039]Quixotic expectations.”

[084-040]“Who will be hurt but myself, then?” said Hans, putting out his lip. “I

[084-041]am not going to say anything to her unless I felt sure of the answer. I

[084-042]dare not ask the oracles: I prefer a cheerful caliginosity, as Sir

[084-043]Thomas Browne might say. I would rather run my chance there and lose,

[084-044]than be sure of winning anywhere else. And I don’t mean to swallow the

[084-045]poison of despair, though you are disposed to thrust it on me. I am

[084-046]giving up wine, so let me get a little drunk on hope and [[pride|vanity]].”

[084-047]“With all my heart, if it will do you any good,” said Deronda, loosing

[084-048]Hans’s shoulder, with a little push. He made his tone kindly, but his

[084-049]words were from the lip only. As to his real feeling he was silenced.

[084-050]He was conscious of that peculiar irritation which will sometimes

[084-051]befall the man whom others are inclined to trust as a mentor—the

[084-052]irritation of perceiving that he is supposed to be entirely off the

[084-053]same plane of desire and temptation as those who confess to him. Our

[084-054]guides, we pretend, must be sinless: as if those were not often the

[084-055]best teachers who only yesterday got corrected for their mistakes.

[084-056]Throughout their friendship Deronda had been used to Hans’s egotism,

[084-057]but he had never before felt intolerant of it: when Hans, habitually

[084-058]pouring out his own feelings and affairs, had never cared for any

[084-059]detail in return, and, if he chanced to know any, had soon forgotten

[084-060]it. Deronda had been inwardly as well as outwardly indulgent—nay,

[084-061]satisfied. But now he had noted with some indignation, all the stronger

[084-062]because it must not be betrayed, Hans’s evident assumption that for any

[084-063]danger of rivalry or jealousy in relation to Mirah, Deronda was not as

[084-064]much out of the question as the angel Gabriel. It is one thing to be

[084-065]resolute in placing one’s self out of the question, and another to

[084-066]endure that others should perform that exclusion for us. He had

[084-067]expected that Hans would give him trouble: what he had not expected was

[084-068]that the trouble would have a strong element of personal feeling. And

[084-069]he was rather ashamed that Hans’s hopes caused him uneasiness in spite

[084-070]of his well-warranted conviction that they would never be fulfilled.

[084-071]They had raised an image of Mirah changing; and however he might

[084-072]protest that the change would not happen, the protest kept up the

[084-073]unpleasant image. Altogether poor Hans seemed to be entering into

[084-074]Deronda’s experience in a disproportionate manner—going beyond his

[084-075]part of rescued prodigal, and rousing a feeling quite distinct from

[084-076]compassionate affection.

[084-077]When Deronda went to Chelsea he was not made as comfortable as he ought

[084-078]to have been by Mrs. Meyrick’s evident release from anxiety about the

[084-079]beloved but incalculable son. Mirah seemed livelier than before, and

[084-080]for the first time he saw her laugh. It was when they were talking of

[084-081]Hans, he being naturally the mother’s first topic. Mirah wished to know

[084-082]if Deronda had seen Mr. Hans going through a sort of character piece

[084-083]without changing his dress.

[084-084]“He passes from one figure to another as if he were a bit of flame

[084-085]where you fancied the figures without seeing them,” said Mirah, full of

[084-086]her subject; “he is so wonderfully quick. I used never to like comic

[084-087]things on the stage—they were dwelt on too long; but all in one minute

[084-088]Mr. Hans makes himself a blind bard, and then Rienzi addressing the

[084-089]Romans, and then an [[opera-as-art|opera]]-dancer, and then a desponding young

[084-090]gentleman—I am sorry for them all, and yet I laugh, all in one”—here

[084-091]Mirah gave a little laugh that might have entered into a song.

[084-092]“We hardly thought that Mirah could laugh till Hans came,” said Mrs.

[084-093]Meyrick, seeing that Deronda, like herself, was observing the pretty

[084-094]picture.

[084-095]“Hans seems in great force just now,” said Deronda in a tone of

[084-096]congratulation. “I don’t wonder at his enlivening you.”

[084-097]“He’s been just perfect ever since he came back,” said Mrs. Meyrick,

[084-098]keeping to herself the next clause—“if it will but last.”

[084-099]“It is a great happiness,” said Mirah, “to see the son and brother come

[084-100]into this dear home. And I hear them all talk about what they did

[084-101]together when they were little. That seems like heaven, and to have a

[084-102]mother and brother who talk in that way. I have never had it.”

[084-103]“Nor I,” said Deronda, involuntarily.

[084-104]“No?” said Mirah, regretfully. “I wish you had. I wish you had had

[084-105]every good.” The last words were uttered with a serious ardor as if

[084-106]they had been part of a litany, while her eyes were fixed on Deronda,

[084-107]who with his elbow on the back of his chair was contemplating her by

[084-108]the new light of the impression she had made on Hans, and the

[084-109]possibility of her being attracted by that extraordinary contrast. It

[084-110]was no more than what had happened on each former visit of his, that

[084-111]Mirah appeared to enjoy speaking of what she felt very much as a little

[084-112]girl fresh from school pours forth spontaneously all the long-repressed

[084-113]chat for which she has found willing ears. For the first time in her

[084-114]life Mirah was among those whom she entirely trusted, and her original

[084-115]visionary impression that Deronda was a divinely-sent messenger hung

[084-116]about his image still, stirring always anew the disposition to reliance

[084-117]and openness. It was in this way she took what might have been the

[084-118]injurious flattery of admiring attention into which her helpless

[084-119]dependence had been suddenly transformed. Every one around her watched

[084-120]for her looks and words, and the effect on her was simply that of

[084-121]having passed from a trifling imprisonment into an exhilarating air

[084-122]which made speech and action a delight. To her mind it was all a gift

[084-123]from others’ goodness. But that word of Deronda’s implying that there

[084-124]had been some lack in his life which might be compared with anything

[084-125]she had known in hers, was an entirely new inlet of thought about him.

[084-126]After her first expression of sorrowful surprise she went on,

[084-127]“But Mr. Hans said yesterday that you thought so much of others you

[084-128]hardly wanted anything for yourself. He told us a wonderful story of

[084-129]Buddha giving himself to the famished tigress to save her and her

[084-130]little ones from starving. And he said you were like Buddha. That is

[084-131]what we all imagine of you.”

[084-132]“Pray don’t imagine that,” said Deronda, who had lately been finding

[084-133]such suppositions rather exasperating. “Even if it were true that I

[084-134]thought so much of others, it would not follow that I had no wants for

[084-135]myself. When Buddha let the tigress eat him he might have been very

[084-136]hungry himself.”

[084-137]“Perhaps if he was starved he would not mind so much about being

[084-138]eaten,” said Mab, shyly.

[084-139]“Please don’t think that, Mab; it takes away the beauty of the action,”

[084-140]said Mirah.

[084-141]“But if it were true, Mirah?” said the rational Amy, having a

[084-142]half-holiday from her teaching; “you always take what is beautiful as

[084-143]if it were true.”

[084-144]“So it is,” said Mirah, gently. “If people have thought what is the

[084-145]most beautiful and the best thing, it must be true. It is always there.”

[084-146]“Now, Mirah, what do you mean?” said Amy.

[084-147]“I understand her,” said Deronda, coming to the rescue.

[084-148]“It is a truth in thought though it may never have been carried out in

[084-149]action. It lives as an idea. Is that it?” He turned to Mirah, who was

[084-150]listening with a blind look in her lovely eyes.

[084-151]“It must be that, because you understand me, but I cannot quite

[084-152]explain,” said Mirah, rather abstractedly—still searching for some

[084-153]expression.

[084-154]“But _was_ it beautiful for Buddha to let the tiger eat him?” said Amy,

[084-155]changing her ground. “It would be a bad pattern.”

[084-156]“The world would get full of fat tigers,” said Mab.

[084-157]Deronda laughed, but defended the myth. “It is like a passionate word,”

[084-158]he said; “the exaggeration is a flash of fervor. It is an extreme image

[084-159]of what is happening every day—the transmutation of self.”

[084-160]“I think I can say what I mean, now,” said Mirah, who had not heard the

[084-161]intermediate talk. “When the best thing comes into our thoughts, it is

[084-162]like what my mother has been to me. She has been just as really with me

[084-163]as all the other people about me—often more really with me.”

[084-164]Deronda, inwardly wincing under this illustration, which brought other

[084-165]possible realities about that mother vividly before him, presently

[084-166]turned the conversation by saying, “But we must not get too far away

[084-167]from practical matters. I came, for one thing, to tell of an interview

[084-168]I had yesterday, which I hope Mirah will find to have been useful to

[084-169]her. It was with [[Herr-Klesmer|Klesmer]], the great pianist.”

[084-170]“Ah?” said Mrs. Meyrick, with satisfaction. “You think he will help

[084-171]her?”

[084-172]“I hope so. He is very much occupied, but has promised to fix a time

[084-173]for receiving and hearing Miss [[Lapidoth]], as we must learn to call

[084-174]her”—here Deronda smiled at Mirah—“If she consents to go to him.”

[084-175]“I shall be very grateful,” said Mirah. “He wants to hear me sing,

[084-176]before he can judge whether I ought to be helped.”

[084-177]Deronda was struck with her plain sense about these matters of

[084-178]practical concern.

[084-179]“It will not be at all trying to you, I hope, if Mrs. Meyrick will

[084-180]kindly go with you to Klesmer’s house.”

[084-181]“Oh, no, not at all trying. I have been doing that all my life—I mean,

[084-182]told to do things that others may judge of me. And I have gone through

[084-183]a bad trial of that sort. I am prepared to bear it, and do some very

[084-184]small thing. Is Klesmer a severe man?”
