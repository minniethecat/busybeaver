[035-001]“I have some biscuits—should you like them?” said [[Daniel-Deronda|Deronda]].

[035-002]“No; I cannot eat. I had still some money left to buy bread.”

[035-003]He began to ply his oar without further remark, and they went along

[035-004]swiftly for many minutes without speaking. She did not look at him, but

[035-005]was watching the oar, leaning forward in an attitude of repose, as if

[035-006]she were beginning to feel the comfort of returning warmth and the

[035-007]prospect of life instead of death. The twilight was deepening; the red

[035-008]flush was all gone and the little stars were giving their answer one

[035-009]after another. The moon was rising, but was still entangled among the

[035-010]trees and buildings. The light was not such that he could distinctly

[035-011]discern the expression of her features or her glance, but they were

[035-012]distinctly before him nevertheless—features and a glance which seemed

[035-013]to have given a fuller meaning for him to the human face. Among his

[035-014]anxieties one was dominant: his first impression about her, that her

[035-015]mind might be disordered, had not been quite dissipated: the project of

[035-016]suicide was unmistakable, and given a deeper color to every other

[035-017]suspicious sign. He longed to begin a conversation, but abstained,

[035-018]wishing to encourage the confidence that might induce her to speak

[035-019]first. At last she did speak.

[035-020]“I like to listen to the oar.”

[035-021]“So do I.”

[035-022]“If you had not come, I should have been dead now.”

[035-023]“I cannot bear you to speak of that. I hope you will never be sorry

[035-024]that I came.”

[035-025]“I cannot see how I shall be glad to live. The _maggior dolore_ and the

[035-026]_miseria_ have lasted longer than the _tempo felice_.” She paused and

[035-027]then went on dreamily,—“_Dolore—miseria_—I think those words are

[035-028]alive.”

[035-029]Deronda was mute: to question her seemed an unwarrantable freedom; he

[035-030]shrank from appearing to claim the authority of a benefactor, or to

[035-031]treat her with the less reverence because she was in distress. She went

[035-032]on musingly,

[035-033]“I thought it was not wicked. Death and life are one before the

[035-034]Eternal. I know our fathers slew their children and then slew

[035-035]themselves, to keep their souls pure. I meant it so. But now I am

[035-036]commanded to live. I cannot see how I shall live.”

[035-037]“You will find friends. I will find them for you.”

[035-038]She shook her head and said mournfully, “Not my mother and brother. I

[035-039]cannot find them.”

[035-040]“You are English? You must be—speaking English so perfectly.”

[035-041]She did not answer immediately, but looked at Deronda again, straining

[035-042]to see him in the double light. Until now she had been watching the

[035-043]oar. It seemed as if she were half roused, and wondered which part of

[035-044]her impression was dreaming and which waking. Sorrowful isolation had

[035-045]benumbed her sense of reality, and the power of distinguishing outward

[035-046]and inward was continually slipping away from her. Her look was full of

[035-047]wondering timidity such as the forsaken one in the desert might have

[035-048]lifted to the angelic vision before she knew whether his message was in

[035-049]anger or in pity.

[035-050]“You want to know if I am English?” she said at last, while Deronda was

[035-051]reddening nervously under a gaze which he felt more fully than he saw.

[035-052]“I want to know nothing except what you like to tell me,” he said,

[035-053]still uneasy in the fear that her mind was wandering. “Perhaps it is

[035-054]not good for you to talk.”

[035-055]“Yes, I will tell you. I am English-born. But I am a Jewess.”

[035-056]Deronda was silent, inwardly wondering that he had not said this to

[035-057]himself before, though any one who had seen delicate-faced Spanish

[035-058]girls might simply have guessed her to be Spanish.

[035-059]“Do you despise me for it?” she said presently in low tones, which had

[035-060]a sadness that pierced like a cry from a small dumb creature in fear.

[035-061]“Why should I?” said Deronda. “I am not so foolish.”

[035-062]“I know many Jews are bad.”

[035-063]“So are many Christians. But I should not think it fair for you to

[035-064]despise me because of that.”

[035-065]“My mother and brother were good. But I shall never find them. I am

[035-066]come a long way—from abroad. I ran away; but I cannot tell you—I

[035-067]cannot speak of it. I thought I might find my mother again—God would

[035-068]guide me. But then I despaired. This morning when the light came, I

[035-069]felt as if one word kept sounding within me—Never! never! But now—I

[035-070]begin—to think—” her words were broken by rising sobs—“I am

[035-071]commanded to live—perhaps we are going to her.”

[035-072]With an outburst of weeping she buried her head on her knees. He hoped

[035-073]that this passionate weeping might relieve her excitement. Meanwhile he

[035-074]was inwardly picturing in much embarrassment how he should present

[035-075]himself with her in Park Lane—the course which he had at first

[035-076]unreflectingly determined on. No one kinder and more gentle than Lady

[035-077]Mallinger; but it was hardly probable that she would be at home; and he

[035-078]had a shuddering sense of a lackey staring at this delicate, sorrowful

[035-079]image of womanhood—of glaring lights and fine staircases, and perhaps

[035-080]chilling suspicious manners from lady’s maid and housekeeper, that

[035-081]might scare the mind already in a state of dangerous susceptibility.

[035-082]But to take her to any other shelter than a home already known to him

[035-083]was not to be contemplated: he was full of fears about the issue of the

[035-084]adventure which had brought on him a [[duty|responsibility]] all the heavier for

[035-085]the strong and agitating impression this childlike creature had made on

[035-086]him. But another resource came to mind: he could venture to take her to

[035-087]Mrs. Meyrick’s—to the small house at Chelsea—where he had been often

[035-088]enough since his return from abroad to feel sure that he could appeal

[035-089]there to generous hearts, which had a romantic readiness to believe in

[035-090]innocent need and to help it. [[Hans-Meyrick|Hans Meyrick]] was safe away in Italy, and

[035-091]Deronda felt the comfort of presenting himself with his charge at a

[035-092]house where he would be met by a motherly figure of quakerish neatness,

[035-093]and three girls who hardly knew of any evil closer to them than what

[035-094]lay in history-books, and dramas, and would at once associate a lovely

[035-095]Jewess with Rebecca in _Ivanhoe_, besides thinking that everything they

[035-096]did at Deronda’s request would be done for their idol, Hans. The vision

[035-097]of the Chelsea home once raised, Deronda no longer hesitated.

[035-098]The rumbling thither in the cab after the stillness of the water seemed

[035-099]long. Happily his charge had been quiet since her fit of weeping, and

[035-100]submitted like a tired child. When they were in the cab, she laid down

[035-101]her hat and tried to rest her head, but the jolting movement would not

[035-102]let it rest. Still she dozed, and her sweet head hung helpless, first

[035-103]on one side, then on the other.

[035-104]“They are too good to have any fear about taking her in,” thought

[035-105]Deronda. Her person, her voice, her exquisite utterance, were one

[035-106]strong appeal to belief and tenderness. Yet what had been the history

[035-107]which had brought her to this desolation? He was going on a strange

[035-108]errand—to ask shelter for this waif. Then there occurred to him the

[035-109]beautiful story Plutarch somewhere tells of the Delphic women: how when

[035-110]the Maenads, outworn with their torch-lit wanderings, lay down to sleep

[035-111]in the market-place, the matrons came and stood silently round them to

[035-112]keep guard over their slumbers; then, when they waked, ministered to

[035-113]them tenderly and saw them safely to their own borders. He could trust

[035-114]the women he was going to for having hearts as good.

[035-115]Deronda felt himself growing older this evening and entering on a new

[035-116]phase in finding a life to which his own had come—perhaps as a rescue;

[035-117]but how to make sure that snatching from death was rescue? The moment

[035-118]of finding a fellow-creature is often as full of mingled doubt and

[035-119]exultation as the moment of finding an idea.

[035-120]CHAPTER XVIII.

[035-121]Life is a various mother: now she dons

[035-122]Her plumes and brilliants, climbs the marble stairs

[035-123]With head aloft, nor ever turns her eyes

[035-124]On lackeys who attend her; now she dwells

[035-125]Grim-clad, up darksome alleys, breathes hot gin,

[035-126]And screams in pauper riot.

[035-127]But to these

[035-128]She came a frugal matron, neat and deft,

[035-129]With cheerful morning thoughts and quick device

[035-130]To find the much in little.

[035-131]Mrs. Meyrick’s house was not noisy: the front parlor looked on the

[035-132]river, and the back on gardens, so that though she was reading aloud to

[035-133]her daughters, the window could be left open to freshen the air of the

[035-134]small double room where a lamp and two candles were burning. The

[035-135]candles were on a table apart for Kate, who was drawing illustrations

[035-136]for a publisher; the lamp was not only for the reader but for Amy and

[035-137]Mab, who were embroidering satin cushions for “the great world.”

[035-138]Outside, the house looked very narrow and shabby, the bright light

[035-139]through the holland blind showing the heavy old-fashioned window-frame;

[035-140]but it is pleasant to know that many such grim-walled slices of space

[035-141]in our foggy [[london|London]] have been and still are the homes of a culture the

[035-142]more spotlessly free from vulgarity, because poverty has rendered

[035-143]everything like display an impersonal question, and all the grand shows

[035-144]of the world simply a spectacle which rouses petty rivalry or vain

[035-145]effort after possession.

[035-146]The Meyricks’ was a home of that kind: and they all clung to this

[035-147]particular house in a row because its interior was filled with objects

[035-148]always in the same places, which, for the mother held memories of her

[035-149]marriage time, and for the young ones seemed as necessary and

[035-150]uncriticised a part of their world as the stars of the Great Bear seen

[035-151]from the back windows. Mrs. Meyrick had borne much stint of other

[035-152]matters that she might be able to keep some engravings specially

[035-153]cherished by her husband; and the narrow spaces of wall held a world

[035-154]history in scenes and heads which the children had early learned by

[035-155]heart. The chairs and tables were also old friends preferred to new.

[035-156]But in these two little parlors with no furniture that a broker would

[035-157]have cared to cheapen except the prints and piano, there was space and

[035-158]apparatus for a wide-glancing, nicely-select life, opened to the

[035-159]highest things in [[art|music]], painting and poetry. I am not sure that in the

[035-160]times of greatest scarcity, before Kate could get paid-work, these

[035-161]ladies had always had a servant to light their fires and sweep their

[035-162]rooms; yet they were fastidious in some points, and could not believe

[035-163]that the manners of ladies in the fashionable world were so full of

[035-164]coarse selfishness, petty quarreling, and slang as they are represented

[035-165]to be in what are called literary photographs. The Meyricks had their

[035-166]little oddities, streaks of eccentricity from the mother’s blood as

[035-167]well as the father’s, their minds being like mediæval houses with

[035-168]unexpected recesses and openings from this into that, flights of steps

[035-169]and sudden outlooks.

[035-170]But mother and daughters were all united by a triple bond—family love;

[035-171]admiration for the finest work, the best action; and habitual industry.

[035-172]Hans’ desire to spend some of his money in making their lives more

[035-173]luxurious had been resisted by all of them, and both they and he had

[035-174]been thus saved from regrets at the threatened triumphs of his yearning

[035-175]for art over the attractions of secured income—a triumph that would

[035-176]by-and-by oblige him to give up his fellowship. They could all afford

[035-177]to laugh at his Gavarni-caricatures and to hold him blameless in

[035-178]following a natural bent which their unselfishness and [[freedom|independence]] had

[035-179]left without obstacle. It was enough for them to go on in their old

[035-180]way, only having a grand treat of [[opera-as-art|opera]]-going (to the gallery) when

[035-181]Hans came home on a visit.
