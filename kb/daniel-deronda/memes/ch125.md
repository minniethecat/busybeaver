[125-001]“It is come, it is come! He is dead!”

[125-002]“Hush, hush!” said [[Daniel-Deronda|Deronda]], in a tone of authority; “quiet yourself.”

[125-003]Then to the men who were assisting her, “I am a connection of this

[125-004]lady’s husband. If you will get her on to the _Italia_ as quickly as

[125-005]possible, I will undertake everything else.”

[125-006]He stayed behind to hear from the remaining boatman that her husband

[125-007]had gone down irrecoverably, and that his boat was left floating empty.

[125-008]He and his comrade had heard a cry, had come up in time to see the lady

[125-009]jump in after her husband, and had got her out fast enough to save her

[125-010]from much damage.

[125-011]After this, Deronda hastened to the hotel to assure himself that the

[125-012]best medical help would be provided; and being satisfied on this point,

[125-013]he telegraphed the event to [[Sir-Hugo-Mallinger|Sir Hugo]], begging him to come forthwith,

[125-014]and also to [[Rex-Gascoigne|Mr. Gascoigne]], whose address at the rectory made his

[125-015]nearest known way of getting the information to [[Gwendolen-Harleth|Gwendolen]]’s mother.

[125-016]Certain words of Gwendolen’s in the past had come back to him with the

[125-017]effectiveness of an inspiration: in moments of agitated confession she

[125-018]had spoken of her mother’s presence, as a possible help, if she could

[125-019]have had it.

[125-020]CHAPTER LVI.

[125-021]“The pang, the curse with which they died,

[125-022]Had never passed away:

[125-023]I could not draw my eyes from theirs,

[125-024]Nor lift them up to pray.”

[125-025]—COLERIDGE.

[125-026]Deronda did not take off his clothes that night. Gwendolen, after

[125-027]insisting on seeing him again before she would consent to be undressed,

[125-028]had been perfectly quiet, and had only asked him, with a whispering,

[125-029]repressed eagerness, to promise that he would come to her when she sent

[125-030]for him in the morning. Still, the possibility that a change might come

[125-031]over her, the danger of a supervening feverish condition, and the

[125-032]suspicion that something in the late catastrophe was having an effect

[125-033]which might betray itself in excited words, acted as a foreboding

[125-034]within him. He mentioned to her attendant that he should keep himself

[125-035]ready to be called if there were any alarming change of symptoms,

[125-036]making it understood by all concerned that he was in communication with

[125-037]her friends in England, and felt bound meanwhile to take all care on

[125-038]her behalf—a position which it was the easier for him to assume,

[125-039]because he was well known to [[Henleigh-Grandcourt|Grandcourt]]’s valet, the only old servant

[125-040]who had come on the late voyage.

[125-041]But when fatigue from the strangely various emotion of the day at last

[125-042]sent Deronda to sleep, he remained undisturbed except by the morning

[125-043]dreams, which came as a tangled web of yesterday’s events, and finally

[125-044]waked him, with an image drawn by his pressing anxiety.

[125-045]Still, it was morning, and there had been no summons—an augury which

[125-046]cheered him while he made his toilet, and reflected that it was too

[125-047]early to send inquiries. Later, he learned that she had passed a too

[125-048]wakeful night, but had shown no violent signs of agitation, and was at

[125-049]last sleeping. He wondered at the force that dwelt in this creature, so

[125-050]alive to dread; for he had an irresistible impression that even under

[125-051]the effects of a severe physical shock she was mastering herself with a

[125-052]determination of [[secrecy|concealment]]. For his own part, he thought that his

[125-053]sensibilities had been blunted by what he had been going through in the

[125-054]meeting with his mother: he seemed to himself now to be only fulfilling

[125-055]claims, and his more passionate sympathy was in abeyance. He had lately

[125-056]been living so keenly in an experience quite apart from Gwendolen’s

[125-057]lot, that his present cares for her were like a revisiting of scenes

[125-058]familiar in the past, and there was not yet a complete revival of the

[125-059]inward response to them.

[125-060]Meanwhile he employed himself in getting a formal, legally recognized

[125-061]statement from the fishermen who had rescued Gwendolen. Few details

[125-062]came to light. The boat in which Grandcourt had gone out had been found

[125-063]drifting with its sail loose, and had been towed in. The fishermen

[125-064]thought it likely that he had been knocked overboard by the flapping of

[125-065]the sail while putting about, and that he had not known how to swim;

[125-066]but, though they were near, their attention had been first arrested by

[125-067]a cry which seemed like that of a man in distress, and while they were

[125-068]hastening with their oars, they heard a shriek from the lady, and saw

[125-069]her jump in.

[125-070]On re-entering the hotel, Deronda was told that Gwendolen had risen,

[125-071]and was desiring to see him. He was shown into a room darkened by

[125-072]blinds and curtains, where she was seated with a white shawl wrapped

[125-073]round her, looking toward the opening door like one waiting uneasily.

[125-074]But her long hair was gathered up and coiled carefully, and, through

[125-075]all, the blue stars in her ears had kept their place: as she started

[125-076]impulsively to her full height, sheathed in her white shawl, her face

[125-077]and neck not less white, except for a purple line under her eyes, her

[125-078]lips a little apart with the peculiar expression of one accused and

[125-079]helpless, she looked like the unhappy ghost of that Gwendolen Harleth

[125-080]whom Deronda had seen turning with firm lips and proud self-possession

[125-081]from her losses at the [[gambling|gaming]] table. The sight pierced him with pity,

[125-082]and the effects of all their past relations began to revive within him.

[125-083]“I beseech you to rest—not to stand,” said Deronda, as he approached

[125-084]her; and she obeyed, falling back into her chair again.

[125-085]“Will you sit down near me?” she said. “I want to speak very low.”

[125-086]She was in a large arm-chair, and he drew a small one near to her side.

[125-087]The action seemed to touch her peculiarly: turning her pale face full

[125-088]upon his, which was very near, she said, in the lowest audible tone,

[125-089]“You know I am a guilty woman?”

[125-090]Deronda himself turned paler as he said, “I know nothing.” He did not

[125-091]dare to say more.

[125-092]“He is dead.” She uttered this with the same undertoned decision.

[125-093]“Yes,” said Deronda, in a mournful suspense which made him reluctant to

[125-094]speak.

[125-095]“His face will not be seen above the water again,” said Gwendolen, in a

[125-096]tone that was not louder, but of a suppressed eagerness, while she held

[125-097]both her hands clenched.

[125-098]“No.”

[125-099]“Not by any one else—only by me—a dead face—I shall never get away

[125-100]from it.”

[125-101]It was with an inward voice of desperate self-repression that she spoke

[125-102]these last words, while she looked away from Deronda toward something

[125-103]at a distance from her on the floor. She was seeing the whole

[125-104]event—her own acts included—through an exaggerating medium of

[125-105]excitement and horror? Was she in a state of delirium into which there

[125-106]entered a sense of concealment and necessity for self-repression? Such

[125-107]thoughts glanced through Deronda as a sort of hope. But imagine the

[125-108]conflict of feeling that kept him silent. She was bent on confession,

[125-109]and he dreaded hearing her confession. Against his better will he

[125-110]shrank from the task that was laid on him: he wished, and yet rebuked

[125-111]the wish as cowardly, that she could bury her secrets in her own bosom.

[125-112]He was not a priest. He dreaded the weight of this woman’s soul flung

[125-113]upon his own with imploring dependence. But she spoke again, hurriedly,

[125-114]looking at him,

[125-115]“You will not say that I ought to tell the world? you will not say that

[125-116]I ought to be disgraced? I could not do it. I could not bear it. I

[125-117]cannot have my mother know. Not if I were dead. I could not have her

[125-118]know. I must tell you; but you will not say that any one else should

[125-119]know.”

[125-120]“I can say nothing in my ignorance,” said Deronda, mournfully, “except

[125-121]that I desire to help you.”

[125-122]“I told you from the beginning—as soon as I could—I told you I was

[125-123]afraid of myself.” There was a piteous pleading in the low murmur in

[125-124]which Deronda turned his ear only. Her face afflicted him too much. “I

[125-125]felt a hatred in me that was always working like an evil

[125-126]spirit—contriving things. Everything I could do to free myself came

[125-127]into my mind; and it got worse—all things got worse. That is why I

[125-128]asked you to come to me in town. I thought then I would tell you the

[125-129]worst about myself. I tried. But I could not tell everything. And _he_

[125-130]came in.”

[125-131]She paused, while a shudder passed through her; but soon went on.

[125-132]“I will tell you everything now. Do you think a woman who cried, and

[125-133]prayed, and struggled to be saved from herself, could be a murderess?”

[125-134]“Great God!” said Deronda, in a deep, shaken voice, “don’t torture me

[125-135]needlessly. You have not murdered him. You threw yourself into the

[125-136]water with the impulse to save him. Tell me the rest afterward. This

[125-137]death was an accident that you could not have hindered.”

[125-138]“Don’t be impatient with me.” The tremor, the childlike beseeching in

[125-139]these words compelled Deronda to turn his head and look at her face.

[125-140]The poor quivering lips went on. “You said—you used to say—you felt

[125-141]more for those who had done something wicked and were miserable; you

[125-142]said they might get better—they might be scourged into something

[125-143]better. If you had not spoken in that way, everything would have been

[125-144]worse. I _did_ remember all you said to me. It came to me always. It

[125-145]came to me at the very last—that was the reason why I—But now, if you

[125-146]cannot bear with me when I tell you everything—if you turn away from

[125-147]me and forsake me, what shall I do? Am I worse than I was when you

[125-148]found me and wanted to make me better? All the wrong I have done was in

[125-149]me then—and more—and more—if you had not come and been patient with

[125-150]me. And now—will you forsake me?”

[125-151]Her hands, which had been so tightly clenched some minutes before, were

[125-152]now helplessly relaxed and trembling on the arm of her chair. Her

[125-153]quivering lips remained parted as she ceased speaking. Deronda could

[125-154]not answer; he was obliged to look away. He took one of her hands, and

[125-155]clasped it as if they were going to walk together like two children: it

[125-156]was the only way in which he could answer, “I will not forsake you.”

[125-157]And all the while he felt as if he were putting his name to a blank

[125-158]paper which might be filled up terribly. Their attitude, his adverted

[125-159]face with its expression of a suffering which he was solemnly resolved

[125-160]to undergo, might have told half the truth of the situation to a

[125-161]beholder who had suddenly entered.

[125-162]That grasp was an entirely new experience to Gwendolen: she had never

[125-163]before had from any man a sign of tenderness which her own being had

[125-164]needed, and she interpreted its powerful effect on her into a promise

[125-165]of inexhaustible patience and constancy. The stream of renewed strength

[125-166]made it possible for her to go on as she had begun—with that fitful,

[125-167]wandering confession where the sameness of experience seems to nullify

[125-168]the sense of time or of order in events. She began again in a

[125-169]fragmentary way,

[125-170]“All sorts of contrivances in my mind—but all so difficult. And I

[125-171]fought against them—I was terrified at them—I saw his dead

[125-172]face”—here her voice sank almost to a whisper close to Deronda’s

[125-173]ear—“ever so long ago I saw it and I wished him to be dead. And yet it

[125-174]terrified me. I was like two creatures. I could not speak—I wanted to

[125-175]kill—it was as strong as thirst—and then directly—I felt beforehand

[125-176]I had done something dreadful, unalterable—that would make me like an

[125-177]evil spirit. And it came—it came.”

[125-178]She was silent a moment or two, as if her memory had lost itself in a

[125-179]web where each mesh drew all the rest.
