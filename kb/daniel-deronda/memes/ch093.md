[093-001]A more plausible reason for putting discipleship out of the question

[093-002]was the strain of visionary excitement in [[Mordecai]], which turned his

[093-003]wishes into overmastering impressions, and made him read outward facts

[093-004]as fulfillment. Was such a temper of mind likely to accompany that wise

[093-005]estimate of consequences which is the only safeguard from fatal error,

[093-006]even to ennobling motive? But it remained to be seen whether that rare

[093-007]conjunction existed or not in Mordecai: perhaps his might be one of the

[093-008]natures where a wise estimate of consequences is fused in the fires of

[093-009]that passionate belief which determines the consequences it believes

[093-010]in. The inspirations of the world have come in that way too: even

[093-011]strictly-measuring science could hardly have got on without that

[093-012]forecasting ardor which feels the agitations of discovery beforehand,

[093-013]and has a [[religion|faith]] in its preconception that surmounts many failures of

[093-014]experiment. And in relation to human motives and actions, passionate

[093-015]belief has a fuller efficacy. Here enthusiasm may have the validity of

[093-016]proof, and happening in one soul, give the type of what will one day be

[093-017]general.

[093-018]At least, [[Daniel-Deronda|Deronda]] argued, Mordecai’s visionary excitability was hardly

[093-019]a reason for concluding beforehand that he was not worth listening to

[093-020]except for pity’s sake. Suppose he had introduced himself as one of the

[093-021]strictest reasoners. Do they form a body of men hitherto free from

[093-022]false conclusions and illusory speculations? The driest argument has

[093-023]its hallucinations, too hastily concluding that its net will now at

[093-024]last be large enough to hold the universe. Men may dream in

[093-025]demonstrations, and cut out an illusory world in the shape of axioms,

[093-026]definitions, and propositions, with a final exclusion of fact signed

[093-027]Q.E.D. No formulas for thinking will save us mortals from mistake in

[093-028]our imperfect apprehension of the matter to be thought about. And since

[093-029]the unemotional intellect may carry us into a mathematical dreamland

[093-030]where nothing is but what is not, perhaps an emotional intellect may

[093-031]have absorbed into its passionate vision of possibilities some truth of

[093-032]what will be—the more comprehensive massive life feeding theory with

[093-033]new material, as the sensibility of the artist seizes combinations

[093-034]which science explains and justifies. At any rate, presumptions to the

[093-035]contrary are not to be trusted. We must be patient with the inevitable

[093-036]makeshift of our human thinking, whether in its sum total or in the

[093-037]separate minds that have made the sum. Columbus had some impressions

[093-038]about himself which we call superstitions, and used some arguments

[093-039]which we disapprove; but he had also some sound physical conceptions,

[093-040]and he had the passionate patience of genius to make them tell on

[093-041]mankind. The world has made up its mind rather contemptuously about

[093-042]those who were deaf to Columbus.

[093-043]“My contempt for them binds me to see that I don’t adopt their mistake

[093-044]on a small scale,” said Deronda, “and make myself deaf with the

[093-045]assumption that there cannot be any momentous relation between this Jew

[093-046]and me, simply because he has clad it in illusory notions. What I can

[093-047]be to him, or he to me, may not at all depend on his persuasion about

[093-048]the way we came together. To me the way seems made up of plainly

[093-049]discernible links. If I had not found [[Mirah-Lapidoth|Mirah]], it is probable that I

[093-050]should not have begun to be specially interested in the Jews, and

[093-051]certainly I should not have gone on that loitering search after an Ezra

[093-052]Cohen which made me pause at Ram’s book-shop and ask the price of

[093-053]_Maimon_. Mordecai, on his side, had his visions of a disciple, and he

[093-054]saw me by their light; I corresponded well enough with the image his

[093-055]longing had created. He took me for one of his race. Suppose that his

[093-056]impression—the elderly Jew at Frankfort seemed to have something like

[093-057]it—suppose in spite of all presumptions to the contrary, that his

[093-058]impression should somehow be proved true, and that I should come

[093-059]actually to share any of the ideas he is devoted to? This is the only

[093-060]question which really concerns the effect of our meeting on my life.

[093-061]“But if the issue should be quite different?—well, there will be

[093-062]something painful to go through. I shall almost inevitably have to be

[093-063]an active cause of that poor fellow’s crushing disappointment. Perhaps

[093-064]this issue is the one I had need prepare myself for. I fear that no

[093-065]tenderness of mine can make his suffering lighter. Would the

[093-066]alternative—that I should not disappoint him—be less painful to me?”

[093-067]Here Deronda wavered. Feelings had lately been at work within him which

[093-068]had very much modified the reluctance he would formerly have had to

[093-069]think of himself as probably a Jew. And, if you like, he was romantic.

[093-070]That young energy and spirit of adventure which have helped to create

[093-071]the world-wide legions of youthful heroes going to seek the hidden

[093-072]tokens of their birth and its inheritance of tasks, gave him a certain

[093-073]quivering interest in the bare possibility that he was entering on a

[093-074]like track —all the more because the track was one of thought as well

[093-075]as action.

[093-076]“The bare possibility.” He could not admit it to be more. The belief

[093-077]that his father was an Englishman only grew firmer under the weak

[093-078]assaults of unwarranted doubt. And that a moment should ever come in

[093-079]which that belief was declared a delusion, was something of which

[093-080]Deronda would not say, “I should be glad.” His life-long affection for

[093-081][[Sir-Hugo-Mallinger|Sir Hugo]], stronger than all his resentment, made him shrink from

[093-082]admitting that wish.

[093-083]Which way soever the truth might lie, he repeated to himself what he

[093-084]had said to Mordecai—that he could not without farther reasons

[093-085]undertake to hasten its discovery. Nay, he was tempted now to regard

[093-086]his uncertainty as a condition to be cherished for the present. If

[093-087]further intercourse revealed nothing but illusions as what he was

[093-088]expected to share in, the want of any valid evidence that he was a Jew

[093-089]might save Mordecai the worst shock in the refusal of fraternity. It

[093-090]might even be justifiable to use the uncertainty on this point in

[093-091]keeping up a suspense which would induce Mordecai to accept those

[093-092]offices of friendship that Deronda longed to urge on him.

[093-093]These were the meditations that busied Deronda in the interval of four

[093-094]days before he could fulfill his promise to call for Mordecai at Ezra

[093-095]Cohen’s, Sir Hugo’s demands on him often lasting to an hour so late as

[093-096]to put the evening expedition to Holborn out of the question.

[093-097]CHAPTER XLII.

[093-098]“Wenn es eine Stufenleiter von Leiden giebt, so hat Israel die höchste

[093-099]Staffel erstiegen; wen die Dauer der Schmerzen und die Geduld, mit

[093-100]welcher sie ertragen werden, adeln, so nehmen es die Juden mit den

[093-101]Hochgeborenen aller Länder auf; wenn eine Literatur reich genannt

[093-102]wird, die wenige klassische Trauerspiele besitzt, welcher Platz

[093-103]gebührt dann einer Tragodie die anderthalb Jahrtausende wahrt,

[093-104]gedichtet und dargestellt von den Helden selber?”—ZUNZ: _Die

[093-105]Synagogale Poesie des Mittelalters._

[093-106]“If there are ranks in suffering, Israel takes precedence of all the

[093-107]nations—if the duration of sorrows and the patience with which they

[093-108]are borne ennoble, the Jews are among the aristocracy of every land—if

[093-109]a literature is called rich in the possession of a few classic

[093-110]tragedies, what shall we say to a National Tragedy lasting for fifteen

[093-111]hundred years, in which the poets and the actors were also the heroes?”

[093-112]Deronda had lately been reading that passage of Zunz, and it occurred

[093-113]to him by way of contrast when he was going to the Cohens, who

[093-114]certainly bore no obvious stamp of distinction in sorrow or in any

[093-115]other form of aristocracy. Ezra Cohen was not clad in the sublime

[093-116]pathos of the martyr, and his taste for money-getting seemed to be

[093-117]favored with that success which has been the most exasperating

[093-118]difference in the greed of Jews during all the ages of their

[093-119]dispersion. This Jeshurun of a pawnbroker was not a symbol of the great

[093-120]Jewish tragedy; and yet was there not something typical in the fact

[093-121]that a life like Mordecai’s—a frail incorporation of the national

[093-122]consciousness, breathing with difficult breath—was nested in the

[093-123]self-gratulating ignorant prosperity of the Cohens?

[093-124]Glistening was the gladness in their faces when Deronda reappeared

[093-125]among them. Cohen himself took occasion to intimate that although the

[093-126]diamond ring, let alone a little longer, would have bred more money, he

[093-127]did not mind _that_—not a sixpence—when compared with the pleasure of

[093-128]the women and children in seeing a young gentleman whose first visit

[093-129]had been so agreeable that they had “done nothing but talk of it ever

[093-130]since.” Young Mrs. Cohen was very sorry that baby was asleep, and then

[093-131]very glad that Adelaide was not yet gone to bed, entreating Deronda not

[093-132]to stay in the shop, but to go forthwith into the parlor to see “mother

[093-133]and the children.” He willingly accepted the invitation, having

[093-134]provided himself with portable presents; a set of paper figures for

[093-135]Adelaide, and an ivory cup and ball for Jacob.

[093-136]The grandmother had a pack of cards before her and was making “plates”

[093-137]with the children. A plate had just been thrown down and kept itself

[093-138]whole.

[093-139]“Stop!” said Jacob, running to Deronda as he entered. “Don’t tread on

[093-140]my plate. Stop and see me throw it up again.”

[093-141]Deronda complied, exchanging a smile of understanding with the

[093-142]grandmother, and the plate bore several tossings before it came to

[093-143]pieces; then the visitor was allowed to come forward and seat himself.

[093-144]He observed that the door from which Mordecai had issued on the former

[093-145]visit was now closed, but he wished to show his interest in the Cohens

[093-146]before disclosing a yet stronger interest in their singular inmate.

[093-147]It was not until he had Adelaide on his knee, and was setting up the

[093-148]paper figures in their dance on the table, while Jacob was already

[093-149]practicing with the cup and ball, that Deronda said,

[093-150]“Is Mordecai in just now?”

[093-151]“Where is he, Addy?” said Cohen, who had seized an interval of business

[093-152]to come and look on.

[093-153]“In the workroom there,” said his wife, nodding toward the closed door.

[093-154]“The fact is, sir,” said Cohen, “we don’t know what’s come to him this

[093-155]last day or two. He’s always what I may call a little touched, you

[093-156]know”—here Cohen pointed to his own forehead—“not quite so rational

[093-157]in all things, like you and me; but he’s mostly wonderful regular and

[093-158]industrious so far as a poor creature can be, and takes as much delight

[093-159]in the boy as anybody could. But this last day or two he’s been moving

[093-160]about like a sleep-walker, or else sitting as still as a wax figure.”

[093-161]“It’s the disease, poor dear creature,” said the grandmother, tenderly.

[093-162]“I doubt whether he can stand long against it.”

[093-163]“No; I think its only something he’s got in his head,” said Mrs. Cohen

[093-164]the younger. “He’s been turning over writing continually, and when I

[093-165]speak to him it takes him ever so long to hear and answer.”

[093-166]“You may think us a little weak ourselves,” said Cohen, apologetically.

[093-167]“But my wife and mother wouldn’t part with him if he was a still worse

[093-168]incumbrance. It isn’t that we don’t know the long and short of matters,

[093-169]but it’s our principle. There’s fools do business at a loss and don’t

[093-170]know it. I’m not one of ’em.”

[093-171]“Oh, Mordecai carries a blessing inside him,” said the grandmother.

[093-172]“He’s got something the matter inside him,” said Jacob, coming up to

[093-173]correct this erratum of his grandmother’s. “He said he couldn’t talk to

[093-174]me, and he wouldn’t have a bit o’ bun.”

[093-175]“So far from wondering at your feeling for him,” said Deronda, “I

[093-176]already feel something of the same sort myself. I have lately talked to

[093-177]him at Ram’s book-shop—in fact, I promised to call for him here, that

[093-178]we might go out together.”
