[117-001]I am less convinced that my society makes amends to [[Mordecai]] for your

[117-002]absence, but another substitute occasionally comes in the form of

[117-003]Jacob Cohen. It is worth while to catch our prophet’s expression when

[117-004]he has that remarkable type of young Israel on his knee, and pours

[117-005]forth some Semitic inspiration with a sublime look of melancholy

[117-006]patience and devoutness. Sometimes it occurs to Jacob that Hebrew will

[117-007]be more edifying to him if he stops his ears with his palms, and

[117-008]imitates the venerable sounds as heard through that muffled medium.

[117-009]When Mordecai gently draws down the little fists and holds them fast,

[117-010]Jacob’s features all take on an extraordinary activity, very much as

[117-011]if he was walking through a menagerie and trying to imitate every

[117-012]animal in turn, succeeding best with the owl and the peccary. But I

[117-013]dare say you have seen something of this. He treats me with the

[117-014]easiest familiarity, and seems in general to look at me as a second-hand

[117-015]Christian commodity, likely to come down in price; remarking on

[117-016]my disadvantages with a frankness which seems to imply some thoughts

[117-017]of future purchase. It is pretty, though, to see the change in him if

[117-018][[Mirah-Lapidoth|Mirah]] happens to come in. He turns child suddenly—his age usually

[117-019]strikes one as being like the Israelitish garments in the desert,

[117-020]perhaps near forty, yet with an air of recent production. But, with

[117-021]Mirah, he reminds me of the dogs that have been brought up by women,

[117-022]and remain manageable by them only. Still, the dog is fond of Mordecai

[117-023]too, and brings sugar-plums to share with him, filling his own mouth

[117-024]to rather an embarrassing extent, and watching how Mordecai deals with

[117-025]a smaller supply. Judging from this modern Jacob at the age of six, my

[117-026]astonishment is that his race has not bought us all up long ago, and

[117-027]pocketed our feebler generations in the form of stock and scrip, as so

[117-028]much slave property. There is one Jewess I should not mind being slave

[117-029]to. But I wish I did not imagine that Mirah gets a little sadder, and

[117-030]tries all the while to hide it. It is natural enough, of course, while

[117-031]she has to watch the slow death of this brother, whom she has taken to

[117-032]worshipping with such looks of loving devoutness that I am ready to

[117-033]wish myself in his place.

[117-034]For the rest, we are a little merrier than usual. [[Rex-Gascoigne|Rex Gascoigne]]—you

[117-035]remember a head you admired among my sketches, a fellow with a good

[117-036]upper lip, reading law—has got some rooms in town now not far off us,

[117-037]and has had a neat sister (upper lip also good) staying with him the

[117-038]last fortnight. I have introduced them both to my mother and the

[117-039]girls, who have found out from Miss Gascoigne that she is cousin to

[117-040]your Vandyke duchess!!! I put the notes of exclamation to mark the

[117-041]surprise that the information at first produced on my feeble

[117-042]understanding. On reflection I discovered that there was not the least

[117-043]ground for surprise, unless I had beforehand believed that nobody

[117-044]could be anybody’s cousin without my knowing it. This sort of

[117-045]surprise, I take it, depends on a liveliness of the spine, with a more

[117-046]or less constant nullity of brain. There was a fellow I used to meet

[117-047]at Rome who was in an effervescence of surprise at contact with the

[117-048]simplest information. Tell him what you would—that you were fond of

[117-049]easy boots—he would always say, “No! are you?” with the same energy

[117-050]of wonder: the very fellow of whom pastoral Browne wrote

[117-051]prophetically,

[117-052]“A wretch so empty that if e’er there be

[117-053]In nature found the least vacuity

[117-054]’Twill be in him.”

[117-055]I have accounted for it all—he had a lively spine.

[117-056]However, this cousinship with the duchess came out by chance one day

[117-057]that Mirah was with them at home and they were talking about the

[117-058]Mallingers. _Apropos_; I am getting so important that I have

[117-059]rival invitations. Gascoigne wants me to go down with him to his

[117-060]father’s rectory in August and see the country round there. But I

[117-061]think self-interest well understood will take me to Topping Abbey, for

[117-062][[Sir-Hugo-Mallinger|Sir Hugo]] has invited me, and proposes—God bless him for his rashness!

[117-063]—that I should make a picture of his three daughters sitting on a

[117-064]bank—as he says, in the Gainsborough style. He came to my studio the

[117-065]other day and recommended me to apply myself to portrait. Of course I

[117-066]know what that means.—“My good fellow, your attempts at the historic

[117-067]and poetic are simply pitiable. Your brush is just that of a

[117-068]successful portrait-painter—it has a little truth and a great

[117-069]facility in falsehood—your idealism will never do for gods and

[117-070]goddesses and heroic story, but it may fetch a high price as flattery.

[117-071]Fate, my friend, has made you the hinder wheel—_rota posterior

[117-072]curras, et in axe secundo_—run behind, because you can’t help it.”

[117-073]—What great effort it evidently costs our friends to give us these

[117-074]candid opinions! I have even known a man to take the trouble to call,

[117-075]in order to tell me that I had irretrievably exposed my want of

[117-076]judgment in treating my subject, and that if I had asked him we would

[117-077]have lent me his own judgment. Such was my ingratitude and my

[117-078]readiness at composition, that even while he was speaking I inwardly

[117-079]sketched a Last Judgment with that candid friend’s physiognomy on the

[117-080]left. But all this is away from Sir Hugo, whose manner of implying

[117-081]that one’s gifts are not of the highest order is so exceedingly

[117-082]good-natured and comfortable that I begin to feel it an advantage not

[117-083]to be among those poor fellows at the tip-top. And his kindness to me

[117-084]tastes all the better because it comes out of his love for you, old

[117-085]boy. His chat is uncommonly amusing. By the way, he told me that your

[117-086]Vandyke duchess is gone with her husband yachting to the Mediterranean.

[117-087]I bethink me that it is possible to land from a yacht, or to be taken

[117-088]on to a yacht from the land. Shall you by chance have an opportunity of

[117-089]continuing your theological discussion with the fair Supralapsarian—I

[117-090]think you said her tenets were of that complexion? Is Duke Alphonso

[117-091]also theological?—perhaps an Arian who objects to triplicity. (Stage

[117-092]direction. While D. is reading, a profound scorn gathers in his face

[117-093]till at the last word he flings down the letter, grasps his coat-collar

[117-094]in a statuesque attitude and so remains with a look generally

[117-095]tremendous, throughout the following soliloquy, “O night, O blackness,

[117-096]etc., etc.”)

[117-097]Excuse the brevity of this letter. You are not used to more from me

[117-098]than a bare statement of facts, without comment or digression. One

[117-099]fact I have omitted—that the Klesmers on the eve of departure have

[117-100]behaved magnificently, shining forth as might be expected from the

[117-101]planets of genius and fortune in conjunction. Mirah is rich with their

[117-102]oriental gifts.

[117-103]What luck it will be if you come back and present yourself at the

[117-104]Abbey while I am there! I am going to behave with consummate

[117-105]discretion and win golden opinions, But I shall run up to town now and

[117-106]then, just for a peep into Gad Eden. You see how far I have got in

[117-107]Hebrew lore—up with my Lord Bolingbroke, who knew no Hebrew, but

[117-108]“understood that sort of learning and what is writ about it.” If Mirah

[117-109]commanded, I would go to a depth below the tri-literal roots. Already

[117-110]it makes no difference to me whether the points are there or not. But

[117-111]while her brother’s life lasts I suspect she would not listen to a

[117-112]lover, even one whose “hair is like a flock of goats on Mount

[117-113]Gilead”—and I flatter myself that few heads would bear that trying

[117-114]comparison better than mine. So I stay with my hope among the

[117-115]orchard-blossoms.—Your devoted,

[117-116]HANS MEYRICK.

[117-117]Some months before, this letter from [[Hans-Meyrick|Hans]] would have divided [[Daniel-Deronda|Deronda]]’s

[117-118]thoughts irritatingly: its romancing, about Mirah would have had an

[117-119]unpleasant edge, scarcely anointed with any commiseration for his

[117-120]friend’s probable disappointment. But things had altered since March.

[117-121]Mirah was no longer so critically placed with regard to the Meyricks,

[117-122]and Deronda’s own position had been undergoing a change which had just

[117-123]been crowned by the revelation of his birth. The new opening toward the

[117-124]future, though he would not trust in any definite visions, inevitably

[117-125]shed new lights, and influenced his mood toward past and present;

[117-126]hence, what Hans called his hope now seemed to Deronda, not a

[117-127]mischievous unreasonableness which roused his indignation, but an

[117-128]unusually persistent bird-dance of an extravagant fancy, and he would

[117-129]have felt quite able to pity any consequent suffering of his friend’s,

[117-130]if he had believed in the suffering as probable. But some of the busy

[117-131]thought filling that long day, which passed without his receiving any

[117-132]new summons from his mother, was given to the argument that Hans

[117-133]Meyrick’s nature was not one in which love could strike the deep roots

[117-134]that turn disappointment into sorrow: it was too restless, too readily

[117-135]excitable by novelty, too ready to turn itself into imaginative

[117-136]material, and wear its grief as a fantastic costume. “Already he is

[117-137]beginning to play at love: he is taking the whole affair as a comedy,”

[117-138]said Deronda to himself; “he knows very well that there is no chance

[117-139]for him. Just like him—never opening his eyes on any possible

[117-140]objection I could have to receive his outpourings about Mirah. Poor old

[117-141]Hans! If we were under a fiery hail together he would howl like a

[117-142]Greek, and if I did not howl too it would never occur to him that I was

[117-143]as badly off as he. And yet he is tender-hearted and affectionate in

[117-144]intention, and I can’t say that he is not active in imagining what goes

[117-145]on in other people—but then he always imagines it to fit his own

[117-146]inclination.”

[117-147]With this touch of causticity Deronda got rid of the slight heat at

[117-148]present raised by Hans’s naive expansiveness. The nonsense about

[117-149][[Gwendolen-Harleth|Gwendolen]], conveying the fact that she was gone yachting with her

[117-150]husband, only suggested a disturbing sequel to his own strange parting

[117-151]with her. But there was one sentence in the letter which raised a more

[117-152]immediate, active anxiety. Hans’s suspicion of a hidden sadness in

[117-153]Mirah was not in the direction of his wishes, and hence, instead of

[117-154]distrusting his observation here, Deronda began to conceive a cause for

[117-155]the sadness. Was it some event that had occurred during his absence, or

[117-156]only the growing fear of some event? Was it something, perhaps

[117-157]alterable, in the new position which had been made for her? Or—had

[117-158]Mordecai, against his habitual resolve, communicated to her those

[117-159]peculiar cherished hopes about him, Deronda, and had her quickly

[117-160]sensitive nature been hurt by the discovery that her brother’s will or

[117-161]tenacity of visionary conviction had acted coercively on their

[117-162]friendship—been hurt by the fear that there was more of pitying

[117-163]self-suppression than of equal regard in Deronda’s relation to him? For

[117-164]amidst all Mirah’s quiet renunciation, the evident thirst of soul with

[117-165]which she received the tribute of equality implied a corresponding pain

[117-166]if she found that what she had taken for a purely reverential regard

[117-167]toward her brother had its mixture of condescension.

[117-168]In this last conjecture of Deronda’s he was not wrong as to the quality

[117-169]in Mirah’s nature on which he was founding—the latent protest against

[117-170]the treatment she had all her life being subject to until she met him.

[117-171]For that gratitude which would not let her pass by any notice of their

[117-172]acquaintance without insisting on the depth of her debt to him, took

[117-173]half its fervor from the keen comparison with what others had thought

[117-174]enough to render to her. Deronda’s affinity in feeling enabled him to

[117-175]penetrate such secrets. But he was not near the truth in admitting the

[117-176]idea that Mordecai had broken his characteristic reticence. To no soul

[117-177]but Deronda himself had he yet breathed the history of their relation

[117-178]to each other, or his confidence about his friend’s origin: it was not

[117-179]only that these subjects were for him too sacred to be spoken of

[117-180]without weighty reason, but that he had discerned Deronda’s shrinking

[117-181]at any mention of his birth; and the severity of reserve which had

[117-182]hindered Mordecai from answering a question on a private affair of the

[117-183]Cohen family told yet more strongly here.
