[100-001]“What! going to [[ryelands|Ryelands]] again?” said [[Rex-Gascoigne|Mr. Gascoigne]].

[100-002]“No, we are going to town,” said [[Gwendolen-Harleth|Gwendolen]], beginning to break up a

[100-003]piece of bread, but putting no morsel into her mouth.

[100-004]“It is rather early to go to town,” said Mrs. Gascoigne, “and Mr.

[100-005][[Henleigh-Grandcourt|Grandcourt]] not in Parliament.”

[100-006]“Oh, there is only one more day’s hunting to be had, and Henleigh has

[100-007]some business in town with lawyers, I think,” said Gwendolen. “I am

[100-008]very glad. I shall like to go to town.”

[100-009]“You will see your house in Grosvenor Square,” said [[Mrs-Davilow|Mrs. Davilow]]. She

[100-010]and the girls were devouring with their eyes every movement of their

[100-011]goddess, soon to vanish.

[100-012]“Yes,” said Gwendolen, in a tone of assent to the interest of that

[100-013]expectation. “And there is so much to be seen and done in town.”

[100-014]“I wish, my dear Gwendolen,” said Mr. Gascoigne, in a kind of cordial

[100-015]advice, “that you would use your influence with Mr. Grandcourt to

[100-016]induce him to enter Parliament. A man of his position should make his

[100-017]weight felt in politics. The best judges are confident that the

[100-018]ministry will have to appeal to the country on this question of further

[100-019]Reform, and Mr. Grandcourt should be ready for the opportunity. I am

[100-020]not quite sure that his opinions and mine accord entirely; I have not

[100-021]heard him express himself very fully. But I don’t look at the matter

[100-022]from that point of view. I am thinking of your husband’s standing in

[100-023]the country. And he has now come to that stage of life when a man like

[100-024]him should enter into public affairs. A wife has great influence with

[100-025]her husband. Use yours in that direction, my dear.”

[100-026]The rector felt that he was acquitting himself of a duty here, and

[100-027]giving something like the aspect of a public benefit to his niece’s

[100-028]match. To Gwendolen the whole speech had the flavor of bitter comedy.

[100-029]If she had been merry, she must have laughed at her uncle’s explanation

[100-030]to her that he had not heard Grandcourt express himself very fully on

[100-031]politics. And the wife’s great influence! General maxims about husbands

[100-032]and wives seemed now of a precarious usefulness. Gwendolen herself had

[100-033]once believed in her future influence as an omnipotence in

[100-034]managing—she did not know exactly what. But her chief concern at

[100-035]present was to give an answer that would be felt appropriate.

[100-036]“I should be very glad, uncle. But I think Mr. Grandcourt would not

[100-037]like the trouble of an election—at least, unless it could be without

[100-038]his making speeches. I thought candidates always made speeches.”

[100-039]“Not necessarily—to any great extent,” said Mr. Gascoigne. “A man of

[100-040]position and weight can get on without much of it. A county member need

[100-041]have very little trouble in that way, and both out of the House and in

[100-042]it is liked the better for not being a speechifier. Tell Mr. Grandcourt

[100-043]that I say so.”

[100-044]“Here comes Jocosa with my chocolate after all,” said Gwendolen,

[100-045]escaping from a promise to give information that would certainly have

[100-046]been received in a way inconceivable to the good rector, who, pushing

[100-047]his chair a little aside from the table and crossing his leg, looked as

[100-048]well as if he felt like a worthy specimen of a clergyman and magistrate

[100-049]giving experienced advice. Mr. Gascoigne had come to the conclusion

[100-050]that Grandcourt was a proud man, but his own self-love, calmed through

[100-051]life by the consciousness of his general value and personal advantages,

[100-052]was not irritable enough to prevent him from hoping the best about his

[100-053]niece’s husband because her uncle was kept rather haughtily at a

[100-054]distance. A certain aloofness must be allowed to the representative of

[100-055]an old family; you would not expect him to be on intimate terms even

[100-056]with abstractions. But Mrs. Gascoigne was less dispassionate on her

[100-057]husband’s account, and felt Grandcourt’s haughtiness as something a

[100-058]little blameable in Gwendolen.

[100-059]“Your uncle and Anna will very likely be in town about Easter,” she

[100-060]said, with a vague sense of expressing a slight discontent. “Dear Rex

[100-061]hopes to come out with honors and a fellowship, and he wants his father

[100-062]and Anna to meet him in [[london|London]], that they may be jolly together, as he

[100-063]says. I shouldn’t wonder if Lord Brackenshaw invited them, he has been

[100-064]so very kind since he came back to the Castle.”

[100-065]“I hope my uncle will bring Ann to stay in Grosvenor Square,” said

[100-066]Gwendolen, risking herself so far, for the sake of the present moment,

[100-067]but in reality wishing that she might never be obliged to bring any of

[100-068]her family near Grandcourt again. “I am very glad of Rex’s good

[100-069]fortune.”

[100-070]“We must not be premature, and rejoice too much beforehand,” said the

[100-071]rector, to whom this topic was the happiest in the world, and

[100-072]altogether allowable, now that the issue of that little affair about

[100-073]Gwendolen had been so satisfactory. “Not but that I am in

[100-074]correspondence with impartial judges, who have the highest hopes about

[100-075]my son, as a singularly clear-headed young man. And of his excellent

[100-076]disposition and principle I have had the best evidence.”

[100-077]“We shall have him a great lawyer some time,” said Mrs. Gascoigne.

[100-078]“How very nice!” said Gwendolen, with a concealed scepticism as to

[100-079]niceness in general, which made the word quite applicable to lawyers.

[100-080]“Talking of Lord Brackenshaw’s kindness,” said Mrs. Davilow, “you don’t

[100-081]know how delightful he has been, Gwendolen. He has begged me to

[100-082]consider myself his guest in this house till I can get another that I

[100-083]like—he did it in the most graceful way. But now a house has turned

[100-084]up. Old Mr. Jodson is dead, and we can have his house. It is just what

[100-085]I want; small, but with nothing hideous to make you miserable thinking

[100-086]about it. And it is only a mile from the Rectory. You remember the low

[100-087]white house nearly hidden by the trees, as we turn up the lane to the

[100-088]church?”

[100-089]“Yes, but you have no furniture, poor mamma,” said Gwendolen, in a

[100-090]melancholy tone.

[100-091]“Oh, I am saving money for that. You know who has made me rather rich,

[100-092]dear,” said Mrs. Davilow, laying her hand on Gwendolen’s. “And Jocosa

[100-093]really makes so little do for housekeeping—it is quite wonderful.”

[100-094]“Oh, please let me go up-stairs with you and arrange my hat, mamma,”

[100-095]said Gwendolen, suddenly putting up her hand to her hair and perhaps

[100-096]creating a desired disarrangement. Her heart was swelling, and she was

[100-097]ready to cry. Her mother _must_ have been worse off, if it had not been

[100-098]for Grandcourt. “I suppose I shall never see all this again,” said

[100-099]Gwendolen, looking round her, as they entered the black and yellow

[100-100]bedroom, and then throwing herself into a chair in front of the glass

[100-101]with a little groan as of bodily fatigue. In the resolve not to cry she

[100-102]had become very pale.

[100-103]“You are not well, dear?” said Mrs. Davilow.

[100-104]“No; that chocolate has made me sick,” said Gwendolen, putting up her

[100-105]hand to be taken.

[100-106]“I should be allowed to come to you if you were ill, darling,” said

[100-107]Mrs. Davilow, rather timidly, as she pressed the hand to her bosom.

[100-108]Something had made her sure to-day that her child loved her—needed her

[100-109]as much as ever.

[100-110]“Oh, yes,” said Gwendolen, leaning her head against her mother, though

[100-111]speaking as lightly as she could. “But you know I never am ill. I am as

[100-112]strong as possible; and you must not take to fretting about me, but

[100-113]make yourself as happy as you can with the girls. They are better

[100-114]children to you than I have been, you know.” She turned up her face

[100-115]with a smile.

[100-116]“You have always been good, my darling. I remember nothing else.”

[100-117]“Why, what did I ever do that was good to you, except marry Mr.

[100-118]Grandcourt?” said Gwendolen, starting up with a desperate resolve to be

[100-119]playful, and keep no more on the perilous edge of agitation. “And I

[100-120]should not have done that unless it had pleased myself.” She tossed up

[100-121]her chin, and reached her hat.

[100-122]“God forbid, child! I would not have had you marry for my sake. Your

[100-123]happiness by itself is half mine.”

[100-124]“Very well,” said Gwendolen, arranging her hat fastidiously, “then you

[100-125]will please to consider that you are half happy, which is more than I

[100-126]am used to seeing you.” With the last words she again turned with her

[100-127]old playful smile to her mother. “Now I am ready; but oh, mamma, Mr.

[100-128]Grandcourt gives me a quantity of money, and expects me to spend it,

[100-129]and I can’t spend it; and you know I can’t bear charity children and

[100-130]all that; and here are thirty pounds. I wish the girls would spend it

[100-131]for me on little things for themselves when you go to the new house.

[100-132]Tell them so.” Gwendolen put the notes into her mother’s hands and

[100-133]looked away hastily, moving toward the door.

[100-134]“God bless you, dear,” said Mrs. Davilow. “It will please them so that

[100-135]you should have thought of them in particular.”

[100-136]“Oh, they are troublesome things; but they don’t trouble me now,” said

[100-137]Gwendolen, turning and nodding playfully. She hardly understood her own

[100-138]feeling in this act toward her sisters, but at any rate she did not

[100-139]wish it to be taken as anything serious. She was glad to have got out

[100-140]of the bedroom without showing more signs of emotion, and she went

[100-141]through the rest of her visit and all the good-byes with a quiet

[100-142]propriety that made her say to herself sarcastically as she rode away,

[100-143]“I think I am making a very good Mrs. Grandcourt.”

[100-144]She believed that her husband had gone to [[gadsmere|Gadsmere]] that day—had

[100-145]inferred this, as she had long ago inferred who were the inmates of

[100-146]what he had described as “a dog-hutch of a place in a black country;”

[100-147]and the strange conflict of feeling within her had had the

[100-148]characteristic effect of sending her to [[Offendene]] with a tightened

[100-149]resolve—a form of excitement which was native to her.

[100-150]She wondered at her own contradictions. Why should she feel it bitter

[100-151]to her that Grandcourt showed concern for the beings on whose account

[100-152]she herself was undergoing [[conscience|remorse]]? Had she not before her marriage

[100-153]inwardly determined to speak and act on their behalf?—and since he had

[100-154]lately implied that he wanted to be in town because he was making

[100-155]arrangements about his will, she ought to have been glad of any sign

[100-156]that he kept a conscience awake toward those at Gadsmere; and yet, now

[100-157]that she was a wife, the sense that Grandcourt was gone to Gadsmere was

[100-158]like red heat near a burn. She had brought on herself this indignity in

[100-159]her own eyes—this [[shame|humiliation]] of being doomed to a terrified silence

[100-160]lest her husband should discover with what sort of consciousness she

[100-161]had married him; and as she had said to [[Daniel-Deronda|Deronda]], she “must go on.”

[100-162]After the intense moments of secret hatred toward this husband who from

[100-163]the very first had cowed her, there always came back the spiritual

[100-164]pressure which made submission inevitable. There was no effort at

[100-165]freedoms that would not bring fresh and worse humiliation. Gwendolen

[100-166]could dare nothing except an impulsive action—least of all could she

[100-167]dare premeditatedly a vague future in which the only certain condition

[100-168]was indignity. In spite of [[guilt|remorse]], it still seemed the worst result of

[100-169]her marriage that she should in any way make a spectacle of herself;

[100-170]and her humiliation was lightened by her thinking that only Mrs.

[100-171]Glasher was aware of the fact which caused it. For Gwendolen had never

[100-172]referred the interview at the Whispering Stones to [[Lush]]’s agency; her

[100-173]disposition to vague terror investing with shadowy omnipresence any

[100-174]threat of fatal power over her, and so hindering her from imagining

[100-175]plans and channels by which news had been conveyed to the woman who had

[100-176]the poisoning skill of a sorceress. To Gwendolen’s mind the secret lay

[100-177]with [[Lydia-Glasher|Mrs. Glasher]], and there were words in the horrible letter which

[100-178]implied that Mrs. Glasher would dread disclosure to the husband, as

[100-179]much as the usurping Mrs. Grandcourt.
