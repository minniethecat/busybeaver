# Governess

> "I would rather emigrate than be a governess." — [[Gwendolen-Harleth|Gwendolen Harleth]]

The governess in George Eliot's *Daniel Deronda* (1876) functions as more than a profession — it is the shadow that haunts [[Gwendolen-Harleth|Gwendolen]]'s consciousness, the feared fate that drives her decisions and ultimately pushes her toward a disastrous marriage. The novel uses the governess question to explore the limited economic options available to Victorian gentlewomen and the social anxieties surrounding class, gender, and labor.

## The Governess in Victorian England

The governess occupied an anomalous social position in Victorian society: she was a lady by birth and education, yet a paid employee. She dined with the family but was not of the family; she educated the children of the gentry but had no independent social standing of her own. As Eliot knew from her own experience — she had briefly considered governessing before turning to writing — the position was a form of genteel servitude, offering neither the dignity of independent labor nor the security of family life.

## Gwendolen's Dread

For [[Gwendolen-Harleth|Gwendolen]], the prospect of becoming a governess is a nightmare. When the collapse of Grapnell & Co. destroys her family's fortune, her uncle [[Rex-Gascoigne|Mr. Gascoigne]] arranges a position with [[Mrs-Mompert|Mrs. Mompert]], a clergyman's wife who runs a small school. Gwendolen's horror at this prospect — "the dead level of being a governess" — is not mere vanity but a recognition of what the position would cost her[^1].

Gwendolen has been raised to value personal freedom, beauty, and social display. The governess's life would deny her all of these: she would be invisible, subordinate, and denied any independent sphere of action. Her desperate alternative — marrying [[Henleigh-Grandcourt|Grandcourt]] despite knowing he has a mistress and children — is the result of a system that offers women no dignified path between the poles of marriage and menial dependence.

## Miss Merry: The Silent Governess

The novel already contains a governess figure in **Miss Merry**, the timid woman who serves the Davilow household. [[Miss-Merry|Miss Merry]] is described as "the meek governess" whose presence is barely registered by the narrative — a pointed illustration of the governess's social invisibility[^2]. She functions as a living warning to Gwendolen of what awaits her: a life of quiet erasure.

## The Economic Trap

Eliot's treatment of the governess question is part of a broader feminist critique running through the novel. Women of the gentry class were educated for display, not for labor; when financial disaster struck, they discovered that their accomplishments — music, languages, deportment — had no market value beyond the governess market, where supply far exceeded demand.

Gwendolen's attempt to become a singer or actress, and her interview with [[Herr-Klesmer|Klesmer]], dramatizes the gap between amateur "accomplishments" and professional skill. Klesmer's devastating honesty — she is too old, too untrained, too far from excellence — leaves her with no escape from the governess fate except marriage[^3].

## The Feminist Reading

The governess plot in *Daniel Deronda* aligns with Eliot's broader interest in the "Woman Question." Like [[Mirah-Lapidoth|Mirah]], who supports herself through singing, Gwendolen seeks independence through talent rather than marriage. But where Mirah has genuine artistic gifts nurtured from childhood, Gwendolen has only the superficial polish of a lady's education. The contrast underscores Eliot's argument that women need genuine education and economic opportunities — not just accomplishments and marriage prospects.
