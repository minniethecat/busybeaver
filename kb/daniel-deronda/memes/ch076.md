[076-001]“Do you take off your hat to horses?” said [[Henleigh-Grandcourt|Grandcourt]], with a slight

[076-002]sneer.

[076-003]“Why not?” said [[Daniel-Deronda|Deronda]], covering himself. He had really taken off the

[076-004]hat automatically, and if he had been an ugly man might doubtless have

[076-005]done so with impunity; ugliness having naturally the air of involuntary

[076-006]exposure, and beauty, of display.

[076-007][[Gwendolen-Harleth|Gwendolen]]’s confusion was soon merged in the survey of the horses,

[076-008]which Grandcourt politely abstained from appraising, languidly

[076-009]assenting to [[Sir-Hugo-Mallinger|Sir Hugo]]’s alternate depreciation and eulogy of the same

[076-010]animal, as one that he should not have bought when he was younger, and

[076-011]piqued himself on his horses, but yet one that had better qualities

[076-012]than many more expensive brutes.

[076-013]“The fact is, stables dive deeper and deeper into the pocket nowadays,

[076-014]and I am very glad to have got rid of that _démangeaison_,” said Sir

[076-015]Hugo, as they were coming out.

[076-016]“What is a man to do, though?” said Grandcourt. “He must ride. I don’t

[076-017]see what else there is to do. And I don’t call it riding to sit astride

[076-018]a set of brutes with every deformity under the sun.”

[076-019]This delicate diplomatic way of characterizing Sir Hugo’s stud did not

[076-020]require direct notice; and the baronet, feeling that the conversation

[076-021]had worn rather thin, said to the party generally, “Now we are going to

[076-022]see the cloister—the finest bit of all—in perfect preservation; the

[076-023]monks might have been walking there yesterday.”

[076-024]But Gwendolen had lingered behind to look at the kenneled blood-hounds,

[076-025]perhaps because she felt a little dispirited; and Grandcourt waited for

[076-026]her.

[076-027]“You had better take my arm,” he said, in his low tone of command; and

[076-028]she took it.

[076-029]“It’s a great bore being dragged about in this way, and no cigar,” said

[076-030]Grandcourt.

[076-031]“I thought you would like it.”

[076-032]“Like it!—one eternal chatter. And encouraging those ugly

[076-033]girls—inviting one to meet such monsters. How that _fat_ Deronda can

[076-034]bear looking at her——”

[076-035]“Why do you call him a _fat_? Do you object to him so much?”

[076-036]“Object? no. What do I care about his being a _fat_? It’s of no

[076-037]consequence to me. I’ll invite him to [[diplow-hall|Diplow]] again if you like.”

[076-038]“I don’t think he would come. He is too clever and learned to care

[076-039]about _us_,” said Gwendolen, thinking it useful for her husband to be

[076-040]told (privately) that it was possible for him to be looked down upon.

[076-041]“I never saw that make much difference in a man. Either he is a

[076-042]gentleman, or he is not,” said Grandcourt.

[076-043]That a new husband and wife should snatch a moment’s _tête-à-tête_ was

[076-044]what could be understood and indulged; and the rest of the party left

[076-045]them in the rear till, re-entering the garden, they all paused in that

[076-046]cloistered court where, among the falling rose-petals thirteen years

[076-047]before, we saw a boy becoming acquainted with his first sorrow. This

[076-048]cloister was built of a harder stone than the church, and had been in

[076-049]greater safety from the wearing weather. It was a rare example of a

[076-050]northern cloister with arched and pillared openings not intended for

[076-051]glazing, and the delicately-wrought foliage of the capitals seemed

[076-052]still to carry the very touches of the chisel. Gwendolen had dropped

[076-053]her husband’s arm and joined the other ladies, to whom Deronda was

[076-054]noticing the delicate sense which had combined freedom with accuracy in

[076-055]the imitation of natural forms.

[076-056]“I wonder whether one oftener learns to love real objects through their

[076-057]representations, or the representations through the real objects,” he

[076-058]said, after pointing out a lovely capital made by the curled leaves of

[076-059]greens, showing their reticulated under-side with the firm gradual

[076-060]swell of its central rib. “When I was a little fellow these capitals

[076-061]taught me to observe and delight in the structure of leaves.”

[076-062]“I suppose you can see every line of them with your eyes shut,” said

[076-063]Juliet Fenn.

[076-064]“Yes. I was always repeating them, because for a good many years this

[076-065]court stood for me as my only image of a convent, and whenever I read

[076-066]of monks and monasteries, this was my scenery for them.”

[076-067]“You must love this place very much,” said Miss Fenn, innocently, not

[076-068]thinking of inheritance. “So many homes are like twenty others. But

[076-069]this is unique, and you seem to know every cranny of it. I dare say you

[076-070]could never love another home so well.”

[076-071]“Oh, I carry it with me,” said Deronda, quietly, being used to all

[076-072]possible thoughts of this kind. “To most men their early home is no

[076-073]more than a memory of their early years, and I’m not sure but they have

[076-074]the best of it. The image is never marred. There’s no disappointment in

[076-075]memory, and one’s exaggerations are always on the good side.”

[076-076]Gwendolen felt sure that he spoke in that way out of delicacy to her

[076-077]and Grandcourt—because he knew they must hear him; and that he

[076-078]probably thought of her as a selfish creature who only cared about

[076-079]possessing things in her own person. But whatever he might say, it must

[076-080]have been a secret hardship to him that any circumstances of his birth

[076-081]had shut him out from the inheritance of his father’s position; and if

[076-082]he supposed that she exulted in her husband’s taking it, what could he

[076-083]feel for her but scornful pity? Indeed it seemed clear to her that he

[076-084]was avoiding her, and preferred talking to others—which nevertheless

[076-085]was not kind in him.

[076-086]With these thoughts in her mind she was prevented by a mixture of pride

[076-087]and timidity from addressing him again, and when they were looking at

[076-088]the rows of quaint portraits in the gallery above the cloisters, she

[076-089]kept up her air of interest and made her vivacious remarks without any

[076-090]direct appeal to Deronda. But at the end she was very weary of her

[076-091]assumed spirits, and as Grandcourt turned into the billiard-room, she went

[076-092]to the pretty boudoir which had been assigned to her, and shut herself

[076-093]up to look melancholy at her ease. No chemical process shows a more

[076-094]wonderful activity than the transforming influence of the thoughts we

[076-095]imagine to be going on in another. Changes in theory, religion,

[076-096]admirations, may begin with a suspicion of dissent or disapproval, even

[076-097]when the grounds of disapproval are but matter of searching conjecture.

[076-098]Poor Gwendolen was conscious of an uneasy, transforming process—all

[076-099]the old nature shaken to its depths, its hopes spoiled, its pleasures

[076-100]perturbed, but still showing wholeness and strength in the will to

[076-101]reassert itself. After every new shock of [[shame|humiliation]] she tried to

[076-102]adjust herself and seize her old supports—proud [[secrecy|concealment]], trust in

[076-103]new excitements that would make life go by without much thinking; trust

[076-104]in some deed of reparation to nullify her self-blame and shield her

[076-105]from a vague, ever-visiting dread of some horrible calamity; trust in

[076-106]the hardening effect of use and wont that would make her indifferent to

[076-107]her miseries.

[076-108]Yes—miseries. This beautiful, healthy young creature, with her

[076-109]two-and-twenty years and her gratified ambition, no longer felt

[076-110]inclined to kiss her fortunate image in the glass. She looked at it

[076-111]with wonder that she could be so miserable. One belief which had

[076-112]accompanied her through her unmarried life as a self-cajoling

[076-113]superstition, encouraged by the subordination of every one about

[076-114]her—the belief in her own power of dominating—was utterly gone.

[076-115]Already, in seven short weeks, which seemed half her life, her husband

[076-116]had gained a mastery which she could no more resist than she could have

[076-117]resisted the benumbing effect from the touch of a torpedo. Gwendolen’s

[076-118]will had seemed imperious in its small girlish sway; but it was the

[076-119]will of a creature with a large discourse of imaginative fears: a

[076-120]shadow would have been enough to relax its hold. And she had found a

[076-121]will like that of a crab or a boa-constrictor, which goes on pinching

[076-122]or crushing without alarm at thunder. Not that Grandcourt was without

[076-123]calculation of the intangible effects which were the chief means of

[076-124]mastery; indeed, he had a surprising acuteness in detecting that

[076-125]situation of feeling in Gwendolen which made her proud and rebellious

[076-126]spirit dumb and helpless before him.

[076-127]She had burned [[Lydia-Glasher|Lydia Glasher]]’s letter with an instantaneous terror lest

[076-128]other eyes should see it, and had tenaciously concealed from Grandcourt

[076-129]that there was any other cause of her violent hysterics than the

[076-130]excitement and fatigue of the day: she had been urged into an implied

[076-131]falsehood. “Don’t ask me—it was my feeling about everything—it was

[076-132]the sudden change from home.” The words of that letter kept repeating

[076-133]themselves, and hung on her consciousness with the weight of a

[076-134]prophetic doom. “I am the grave in which your chance of happiness is

[076-135]buried as well as mine. You had your warning. You have chosen to injure

[076-136]me and my children. He had meant to marry me. He would have married me

[076-137]at last, if you had not broken your word. You will have your

[076-138]punishment. I desire it with all my soul. Will you give him this letter

[076-139]to set him against me and ruin us more—me and my children? Shall you

[076-140]like to stand before your husband with these diamonds on you, and these

[076-141]words of mine in his thoughts and yours? Will he think you have any

[076-142]right to complain when he has made you miserable? You took him with

[076-143]your eyes open. The willing wrong you have done me will be your curse.”

[076-144]The words had nestled their venomous life within her, and stirred

[076-145]continually the vision of the scene at the Whispering Stones. That

[076-146]scene was now like an accusing apparition: she dreaded that Grandcourt

[076-147]should know of it—so far out of her sight now was that possibility she

[076-148]had once satisfied herself with, of speaking to him about Mrs. Glasher

[076-149]and her children, and making them rich amends. Any endurance seemed

[076-150]easier than the mortal humiliation of confessing that she knew all

[076-151]before she married him, and in marrying him had broken her word. For

[076-152]the reasons by which she had justified herself when the marriage

[076-153]tempted her, and all her easy arrangement of her future power over her

[076-154]husband to make him do better than he might be inclined to do, were now

[076-155]as futile as the burned-out lights which set off a child’s pageant. Her

[076-156]sense of being blameworthy was exaggerated by a dread both definite and

[076-157]vague. The definite dread was lest the veil of secrecy should fall

[076-158]between her and Grandcourt, and give him the right to taunt her. With

[076-159]the reading of that letter had begun her husband’s empire of fear.

[076-160]And her husband all the while knew it. He had not, indeed, any distinct

[076-161]knowledge of her broken promise, and would not have rated highly the

[076-162]effect of that breach on her conscience; but he was aware not only of

[076-163]what [[Lush]] had told him about the meeting at the Whispering Stones, but

[076-164]also of Gwendolen’s concealment as to the cause of her sudden illness.

[076-165]He felt sure that Lydia had enclosed something with the diamonds, and

[076-166]that this something, whatever it was, had at once created in Gwendolen

[076-167]a new repulsion for him and a reason for not daring to manifest it. He

[076-168]did not greatly mind, or feel as many men might have felt, that his

[076-169]hopes in marriage were blighted: he had wanted to marry Gwendolen, and

[076-170]he was not a man to repent. Why should a gentleman whose other

[076-171]relations in life are carried on without the luxury of sympathetic

[076-172]feeling, be supposed to require that kind of condiment in domestic

[076-173]life? What he chiefly felt was that a change had come over the

[076-174]conditions of his mastery, which, far from shaking it, might establish

[076-175]it the more thoroughly. And it was established. He judged that he had

[076-176]not married a simpleton unable to perceive the impossibility of escape,

[076-177]or to see alternative evils: he had married a girl who had spirit and

[076-178]pride enough not to make a fool of herself by forfeiting all the

[076-179]advantages of a position which had attracted her; and if she wanted

[076-180]pregnant hints to help her in making up her mind properly he would take

[076-181]care not to withhold them.
