[054-001]“I was disappointed not to find you at [[Leubronn]],” he began, his usual

[054-002]broken drawl having just a shade of amorous languor in it. “The place

[054-003]was intolerable without you. A mere kennel of a place. Don’t you think

[054-004]so?”

[054-005]“I can’t judge what it would be without myself,” said [[Gwendolen-Harleth|Gwendolen]],

[054-006]turning her eyes on him, with some recovered sense of mischief. “_With_

[054-007]myself I like it well enough to have stayed longer, if I could. But I

[054-008]was obliged to come home on account of family troubles.”

[054-009]“It was very cruel of you to go to Leubronn,” said [[Henleigh-Grandcourt|Grandcourt]], taking

[054-010]no notice of the troubles, on which Gwendolen—she hardly knew

[054-011]why—wished that there should be a clear understanding at once. “You

[054-012]must have known that it would spoil everything: you knew you were the

[054-013]heart and soul of everything that went on. Are you quite reckless about

[054-014]me?”

[054-015]It would be impossible to say “yes” in a tone that would be taken

[054-016]seriously; equally impossible to say “no;” but what else could she say?

[054-017]In her difficulty, she turned down her eyelids again and blushed over

[054-018]face and neck. Grandcourt saw her in a new phase, and believed that she

[054-019]was showing her inclination. But he was determined that she should show

[054-020]it more decidedly.

[054-021]“Perhaps there is some deeper interest? Some attraction—some

[054-022]engagement—which it would have been only fair to make me aware of? Is

[054-023]there any man who stands between us?”

[054-024]Inwardly the answer framed itself. “No; but there is a woman.” Yet how

[054-025]could she utter this? Even if she had not promised that woman to be

[054-026]silent, it would have been impossible for her to enter on the subject

[054-027]with Grandcourt. But how could she arrest his wooing by beginning to

[054-028]make a formal speech—“I perceive your intention—it is most

[054-029]flattering, etc.”? A fish honestly invited to come and be eaten has a

[054-030]clear course in declining, but how if it finds itself swimming against

[054-031]a net? And apart from the network, would she have dared at once to say

[054-032]anything decisive? Gwendolen had not time to be clear on that point. As

[054-033]it was, she felt compelled to silence, and after a pause, Grandcourt

[054-034]said,

[054-035]“Am I to understand that some one else is preferred?”

[054-036]Gwendolen, now impatient of her own embarrassment, determined to rush

[054-037]at the difficulty and free herself. She raised her eyes again and said

[054-038]with something of her former clearness and defiance, “No”—wishing him

[054-039]to understand, “What then? I may not be ready to take _you_.” There was

[054-040]nothing that Grandcourt could not understand which he perceived likely

[054-041]to affect his _amour propre_.

[054-042]“The last thing I would do, is to importune you. I should not hope to

[054-043]win you by making myself a bore. If there were no hope for me, I would

[054-044]ask you to tell me so at once, that I might just ride away to—no

[054-045]matter where.”

[054-046]Almost to her own astonishment, Gwendolen felt a sudden alarm at the

[054-047]image of Grandcourt finally riding away. What would be left her then?

[054-048]Nothing but the former dreariness. She liked him to be there. She

[054-049]snatched at the subject that would defer any decisive answer.

[054-050]“I fear you are not aware of what has happened to us. I have lately had

[054-051]to think so much of my mamma’s troubles, that other subjects have been

[054-052]quite thrown into the background. She has lost all her fortune, and we

[054-053]are going to leave this place. I must ask you to excuse my seeming

[054-054]preoccupied.”

[054-055]In eluding a direct appeal Gwendolen recovered some of her

[054-056]self-possession. She spoke with dignity and looked straight at

[054-057]Grandcourt, whose long, narrow, impenetrable eyes met hers, and

[054-058]mysteriously arrested them: mysteriously; for the subtly-varied drama

[054-059]between man and woman is often such as can hardly be rendered in words

[054-060]put together like dominoes, according to obvious fixed marks. The word

[054-061]of all work, Love, will no more express the myriad modes of mutual

[054-062]attraction, than the word Thought can inform you what is passing

[054-063]through your neighbor’s mind. It would be hard to tell on which

[054-064]side—Gwendolen’s or Grandcourt’s—the influence was more mixed. At

[054-065]that moment his strongest wish was to be completely master of this

[054-066]creature—this piquant combination of maidenliness and mischief: that

[054-067]she knew things which had made her start away from him, spurred him to

[054-068]triumph over that repugnance; and he was believing that he should

[054-069]triumph. And she—ah, piteous equality in the need to dominate!—she

[054-070]was overcome like the thirsty one who is drawn toward the seeming water

[054-071]in the desert, overcome by the suffused sense that here in this man’s

[054-072]homage to her lay the rescue from helpless subjection to an oppressive

[054-073]lot.

[054-074]All the while they were looking at each other; and Grandcourt said,

[054-075]slowly and languidly, as if it were of no importance, other things

[054-076]having been settled,

[054-077]“You will tell me now, I hope, that [[Mrs-Davilow|Mrs. Davilow]]’s loss of fortune will

[054-078]not trouble you further. You will trust me to prevent it from weighing

[054-079]upon her. You will give me the claim to provide against that.”

[054-080]The little pauses and refined drawlings with which this speech was

[054-081]uttered, gave time for Gwendolen to go through the dream of a life. As

[054-082]the words penetrated her, they had the effect of a draught of wine,

[054-083]which suddenly makes all things easier, desirable things not so wrong,

[054-084]and people in general less disagreeable. She had a momentary phantasmal

[054-085]love for this man who chose his words so well, and who was a mere

[054-086]incarnation of delicate homage. Repugnance, dread, scruples—these were

[054-087]dim as remembered pains, while she was already tasting relief under the

[054-088]immediate pain of hopelessness. She imagined herself already springing

[054-089]to her mother, and being playful again. Yet when Grandcourt had ceased

[054-090]to speak, there was an instant in which she was conscious of being at

[054-091]the turning of the ways.

[054-092]“You are very generous,” she said, not moving her eyes, and speaking

[054-093]with a gentle intonation.

[054-094]“You accept what will make such things a matter of course?” said

[054-095]Grandcourt, without any new eagerness. “You consent to become my wife?”

[054-096]This time Gwendolen remained quite pale. Something made her rise from

[054-097]her seat in spite of herself and walk to a little distance. Then she

[054-098]turned and with her hands folded before her stood in silence.

[054-099]Grandcourt immediately rose too, resting his hat on the chair, but

[054-100]still keeping hold of it. The evident hesitation of this destitute girl

[054-101]to take his splendid offer stung him into a keenness of interest such

[054-102]as he had not known for years. None the less because he attributed her

[054-103]hesitation entirely to her knowledge about [[Lydia-Glasher|Mrs. Glasher]]. In that

[054-104]attitude of preparation, he said,

[054-105]“Do you command me to go?” No familiar spirit could have suggested to

[054-106]him more effective words.

[054-107]“No,” said Gwendolen. She could not let him go: that negative was a

[054-108]clutch. She seemed to herself to be, after all, only drifted toward the

[054-109]tremendous decision—but drifting depends on something besides the

[054-110]currents when the sails have been set beforehand.

[054-111]“You accept my devotion?” said Grandcourt, holding his hat by his side

[054-112]and looking straight into her eyes, without other movement. Their eyes

[054-113]meeting in that way seemed to allow any length of pause: but wait as

[054-114]long as she would, how could she contradict herself? What had she

[054-115]detained him for? He had shut out any explanation.

[054-116]“Yes,” came as gravely from Gwendolen’s lips as if she had been

[054-117]answering to her name in a court of justice. He received it gravely,

[054-118]and they still looked at each other in the same attitude. Was there

[054-119]ever such a way before of accepting the bliss-giving “Yes”? Grandcourt

[054-120]liked better to be at that distance from her, and to feel under a

[054-121]ceremony imposed by an indefinable prohibition that breathed from

[054-122]Gwendolen’s bearing.

[054-123]But he did at length lay down his hat and advance to take her hand,

[054-124]just pressing his lips upon it and letting it go again. She thought his

[054-125]behavior perfect, and gained a sense of freedom which made her almost

[054-126]ready to be mischievous. Her “Yes” entailed so little at this moment

[054-127]that there was nothing to screen the reversal of her gloomy prospects;

[054-128]her vision was filled by her own release from the Momperts, and her

[054-129]mother’s release from Sawyer’s Cottage. With a happy curl of the lips,

[054-130]she said,

[054-131]“Will you not see mamma? I will fetch her.”

[054-132]“Let us wait a little,” said Grandcourt, in his favorite attitude,

[054-133]having his left forefinger and thumb in his waist-coat pocket, and with

[054-134]his right hand caressing his whisker, while he stood near Gwendolen and

[054-135]looked at her—not unlike a gentleman who has a felicitous introduction

[054-136]at an evening party.

[054-137]“Have you anything else to say to me?” said Gwendolen, playfully.

[054-138]“Yes—I know having things said to you is a great bore,” said

[054-139]Grandcourt, rather sympathetically.

[054-140]“Not when they are things I like to hear.”

[054-141]“Will it bother you to be asked how soon we can be married?”

[054-142]“I think it will, to-day,” said Gwendolen, putting up her chin saucily.

[054-143]“Not to-day, then, but to-morrow. Think of it before I come to-morrow.

[054-144]In a fortnight—or three weeks—as soon as possible.”

[054-145]“Ah, you think you will be tired of my company,” said Gwendolen. “I

[054-146]notice when people are married the husband is not so much with his wife

[054-147]as when they are engaged. But perhaps I shall like that better, too.”

[054-148]She laughed charmingly.

[054-149]“You shall have whatever you like,” said Grandcourt.

[054-150]“And nothing that I don’t like?—please say that; because I think I

[054-151]dislike what I don’t like more than I like what I like,” said

[054-152]Gwendolen, finding herself in the woman’s paradise, where all her

[054-153]nonsense is adorable.

[054-154]Grandcourt paused; these were subtilties in which he had much

[054-155]experience of his own. “I don’t know—this is such a brute of a world,

[054-156]things are always turning up that one doesn’t like. I can’t always

[054-157]hinder your being bored. If you like to ride Criterion, I can’t hinder

[054-158]his coming down by some chance or other.”

[054-159]“Ah, my friend Criterion, how is he?”

[054-160]“He is outside: I made the groom ride him, that you might see him. He

[054-161]had the side-saddle on for an hour or two yesterday. Come to the window

[054-162]and look at him.”

[054-163]They could see the two horses being taken slowly round the sweep, and

[054-164]the beautiful creatures, in their fine grooming, sent a thrill of

[054-165]exultation through Gwendolen. They were the symbols of command and

[054-166]luxury, in delightful contrast with the ugliness of poverty and

[054-167][[shame|humiliation]] at which she had lately been looking close.

[054-168]“Will you ride Criterion to-morrow?” said Grandcourt. “If you will,

[054-169]everything shall be arranged.”

[054-170]“I should like it of all things,” said Gwendolen. “I want to lose

[054-171]myself in a gallop again. But now I must go and fetch mamma.”

[054-172]“Take my arm to the door, then,” said Grandcourt, and she accepted.

[054-173]Their faces were very near each other, being almost on a level, and he

[054-174]was looking at her. She thought his manners as a lover more agreeable

[054-175]than any she had seen described. She had no alarm lest he meant to kiss

[054-176]her, and was so much at her ease, that she suddenly paused in the

[054-177]middle of the room and said half archly, half earnestly,

[054-178]“Oh, while I think of it—there is something I dislike that you can

[054-179]save me from. I do _not_ like [[Lush|Mr. Lush]]’s company.”

[054-180]“You shall not have it. I’ll get rid of him.”

[054-181]“You are not fond of him yourself?”

[054-182]“Not in the least. I let him hang on me because he has always been a

[054-183]poor devil,” said Grandcourt, in an _adagio_ of utter indifference.

[054-184]“They got him to travel with me when I was a lad. He was always that

[054-185]coarse-haired kind of brute—sort of cross between a hog and a

[054-186]_dilettante_.”
