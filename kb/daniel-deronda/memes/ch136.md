[136-001]It was the evening of that same afternoon on which [[Mirah-Lapidoth|Mirah]] had had the

[136-002]interview with her father. [[Mordecai]], penetrated by her grief, and also

[136-003]the sad memories which the incident had awakened, had not resumed his

[136-004]task of sifting papers: some of them had fallen scattered on the floor

[136-005]in the first moments of anxiety, and neither he nor Mirah had thought

[136-006]of laying them in order again. They had sat perfectly still together,

[136-007]not knowing how long; while the clock ticked on the mantel-piece, and

[136-008]the light was fading, Mirah, unable to think of the food that she ought

[136-009]to have been taking, had not moved since she had thrown off her

[136-010]dust-cloak and sat down beside Mordecai with her hand in his, while he

[136-011]had laid his head backward, with closed eyes and difficult breathing,

[136-012]looking, Mirah thought, as he would look when the soul within him could

[136-013]no longer live in its straitened home. The thought that his death might

[136-014]be near was continually visiting her when she saw his face in this way,

[136-015]without its vivid animation; and now, to the rest of her grief, was

[136-016]added the regret that she had been unable to control the violent

[136-017]outburst which had shaken him. She sat watching him—her oval cheeks

[136-018]pallid, her eyes with the sorrowful brilliancy left by young tears, her

[136-019]curls in as much disorder as a just-awakened child’s—watching that

[136-020]emaciated face, where it might have been imagined that a veil had been

[136-021]drawn never to be lifted, as if it were her dead joy which had left her

[136-022]strong enough to live on in sorrow. And life at that moment stretched

[136-023]before Mirah with more than a repetition of former sadness. The shadow

[136-024]of the father was there, and more than that, a double bereavement—of

[136-025]one living as well as one dead.

[136-026]But now the door was opened, and while none entered, a well-known voice

[136-027]said: “[[Daniel-Deronda|Daniel Deronda]]—may he come in?”

[136-028]“Come! come!” said Mordecai, immediately rising with an irradiated face

[136-029]and opened eyes—apparently as little surprised as if he had seen

[136-030]Deronda in the morning, and expected this evening visit; while Mirah

[136-031]started up blushing with confused, half-alarmed expectation.

[136-032]Yet when Deronda entered, the sight of him was like the clearness after

[136-033]rain: no clouds to come could hinder the cherishing beam of that

[136-034]moment. As he held out his right hand to Mirah, who was close to her

[136-035]brother’s left, he laid his other hand on Mordecai’s right shoulder,

[136-036]and stood so a moment, holding them both at once, uttering no word, but

[136-037]reading their faces, till he said anxiously to Mirah, “Has anything

[136-038]happened?—any trouble?”

[136-039]“Talk not of trouble now,” said Mordecai, saving her from the need to

[136-040]answer. “There is joy in your face—let the joy be ours.”

[136-041]Mirah thought, “It is for something he cannot tell us.” But they all

[136-042]sat down, Deronda drawing a chair close in front of Mordecai.

[136-043]“That is true,” he said, emphatically. “I have a joy which will remain

[136-044]to us even in the worst trouble. I did not tell you the reason of my

[136-045]journey abroad, Mordecai, because—never mind—I went to learn my

[136-046]parentage. And you were right. I am a Jew.”

[136-047]The two men clasped hands with a movement that seemed part of the flash

[136-048]from Mordecai’s eyes, and passed through Mirah like an electric shock.

[136-049]But Deronda went on without pause, speaking from Mordecai’s mind as

[136-050]much as from his own,

[136-051]“We have the same people. Our souls have the same vocation. We shall

[136-052]not be separated by life or by death.”

[136-053]Mordecai’s answer was uttered in Hebrew, and in no more than a loud

[136-054]whisper. It was in the liturgical words which express the religious

[136-055]bond: “Our God and the God of our fathers.”

[136-056]The weight of feeling pressed too strongly on that ready-winged speech

[136-057]which usually moved in quick adaptation to every stirring of his fervor.

[136-058]Mirah fell on her knees by her brother’s side, and looked at his now

[136-059]illuminated face, which had just before been so deathly. The action was

[136-060]an inevitable outlet of the violent reversal from despondency to a

[136-061]gladness which came over her as solemnly as if she had been beholding a

[136-062]religious rite. For the moment she thought of the effect on her own

[136-063]life only through the effect on her brother.

[136-064]“And it is not only that I am a Jew,” Deronda went on, enjoying one of

[136-065]those rare moments when our yearnings and our acts can be completely

[136-066]one, and the real we behold is our ideal good; “but I come of a strain

[136-067]that has ardently maintained the fellowship of our race—a line of

[136-068]Spanish Jews that has borne many students and men of practical power.

[136-069]And I possess what will give us a sort of communion with them. My

[136-070]grandfather, Daniel Charisi, preserved manuscripts, family records

[136-071]stretching far back, in the hope that they would pass into the hands of

[136-072]his grandson. And now his hope is fulfilled, in spite of attempts to

[136-073]thwart it by hiding my parentage from me. I possess the chest

[136-074]containing them, with his own papers, and it is down below in this

[136-075]house. I mean to leave it with you, Mordecai, that you may help me to

[136-076]study the manuscripts. Some of them I can read easily enough—those in

[136-077]Spanish and Italian. Others are in Hebrew, and, I think, Arabic; but

[136-078]there seem to be Latin translations. I was only able to look at them

[136-079]cursorily while I stayed at [[mainz|Mainz]]. We will study them together.”

[136-080]Deronda ended with that bright smile which, beaming out from the

[136-081]habitual gravity of his face, seemed a revelation (the reverse of the

[136-082]continual smile that discredits all expression). But when this happy

[136-083]glance passed from Mordecai to rest on Mirah, it acted like a little

[136-084]too much sunshine, and made her change her attitude. She had knelt

[136-085]under an impulse with which any personal embarrassment was incongruous,

[136-086]and especially any thoughts about how [[Gwendolen-Harleth|Mrs. Grandcourt]] might stand to

[136-087]this new aspect of things—thoughts which made her color under

[136-088]Deronda’s glance, and rise to take her seat again in her usual posture

[136-089]of crossed hands and feet, with the effort to look as quiet as

[136-090]possible. Deronda, equally sensitive, imagined that the feeling of

[136-091]which he was conscious, had entered too much into his eyes, and had

[136-092]been repugnant to her. He was ready enough to believe that any

[136-093]unexpected manifestation might spoil her feeling toward him—and then

[136-094]his precious relation to brother and sister would be marred. If Mirah

[136-095]could have no love for him, any advances of love on his part would make

[136-096]her wretched in that continual contact with him which would remain

[136-097]inevitable.

[136-098]While such feelings were pulsating quickly in Deronda and Mirah,

[136-099]Mordecai, seeing nothing in his friend’s presence and words but a

[136-100]blessed fulfillment, was already speaking with his old sense of

[136-101]enlargement in utterance,

[136-102]“Daniel, from the first, I have said to you, we know not all the

[136-103]pathways. Has there not been a meeting among them, as of the operations

[136-104]in one soul, where an idea being born and breathing draws the elements

[136-105]toward it, and is fed and glows? For all things are bound together in

[136-106]that Omnipresence which is the place and habitation of the world, and

[136-107]events are of a glass wherethrough our eyes see some of the pathways.

[136-108]And if it seems that the erring and unloving wills of men have helped

[136-109]to prepare you, as Moses was prepared, to serve your people the better,

[136-110]that depends on another order than the law which must guide our

[136-111]footsteps. For the evil will of man makes not a people’s good except by

[136-112]stirring the righteous will of man; and beneath all the clouds with

[136-113]which our thought encompasses the Eternal, this is clear—that a people

[136-114]can be blessed only by having counsellors and a multitude whose will

[136-115]moves in obedience to the laws of justice and love. For see, now, it

[136-116]was your loving will that made a chief pathway, and resisted the effect

[136-117]of evil; for, by performing the duties of brotherhood to my sister, and

[136-118]seeking out her brother in the flesh, your soul has been prepared to

[136-119]receive with gladness this message of the Eternal, ‘behold the

[136-120]multitude of your brethren.’”

[136-121]“It is quite true that you and Mirah have been my teachers,” said

[136-122]Deronda. “If this revelation had been made to me before I knew you

[136-123]both, I think my mind would have rebelled against it. Perhaps I should

[136-124]have felt then—‘If I could have chosen, I would not have been a Jew.’

[136-125]What I feel now is—that my whole being is a consent to the fact. But

[136-126]it has been the gradual accord between your mind and mine which has

[136-127]brought about that full consent.”

[136-128]At the moment Deronda was speaking, that first evening in the book-shop

[136-129]was vividly in his remembrance, with all the struggling aloofness he

[136-130]had then felt from Mordecai’s prophetic confidence. It was his nature

[136-131]to delight in satisfying to the utmost the eagerly-expectant soul,

[136-132]which seemed to be looking out from the face before him, like the

[136-133]long-enduring watcher who at last sees the mountain signal-flame; and

[136-134]he went on with fuller fervor,

[136-135]“It is through your inspiration that I have discerned what may be my

[136-136]life’s task. It is you who have given shape to what, I believe, was an

[136-137]inherited yearning—the effect of brooding, passionate thoughts in many

[136-138]ancestors—thoughts that seem to have been intensely present in my

[136-139]grandfather. Suppose the stolen offspring of some mountain tribe

[136-140]brought up in a city of the plain, or one with an inherited genius for

[136-141]painting, and born blind—the ancestral life would lie within them as a

[136-142]dim longing for unknown objects and sensations, and the spell-bound

[136-143]habit of their inherited frames would be like a cunningly-wrought

[136-144]musical instrument, never played on, but quivering throughout in uneasy

[136-145]mysterious meanings of its intricate structure that, under the right

[136-146]touch, gives [[art|music]]. Something like that, I think, has been my

[136-147]experience. Since I began to read and know, I have always longed for

[136-148]some ideal task, in which I might feel myself the heart and brain of a

[136-149]multitude—some social captainship, which would come to me as a duty,

[136-150]and not be striven for as a personal prize. You have raised the image

[136-151]of such a task for me—to bind our race together in spite of heresy.

[136-152]You have said to me—‘Our religion united us before it divided us—it

[136-153]made us a people before it made Rabbanites and Karaites.’ I mean to try

[136-154]what can be done with that union—I mean to work in your spirit.

[136-155]Failure will not be ignoble, but it would be ignoble for me not to try.”

[136-156]“Even as my brother that fed at the breasts of my mother,” said

[136-157]Mordecai, falling back in his chair with a look of exultant repose, as

[136-158]after some finished labor.

[136-159]To estimate the effect of this ardent outpouring from Deronda we must

[136-160]remember his former reserve, his careful avoidance of premature assent

[136-161]or delusive encouragement, which gave to this decided pledge of himself

[136-162]a sacramental solemnity, both for his own mind and Mordecai’s. On Mirah

[136-163]the effect was equally strong, though with a difference: she felt a

[136-164]surprise which had no place in her brother’s mind, at Deronda’s

[136-165]suddenly revealed sense of nearness to them: there seemed to be a

[136-166]breaking of day around her which might show her other facts unlike her

[136-167]forebodings in the darkness. But after a moment’s silence Mordecai

[136-168]spoke again,

[136-169]“It has begun already—the marriage of our souls. It waits but the

[136-170]passing away of this body, and then they who are betrothed shall unite

[136-171]in a stricter bond, and what is mine shall be thine. Call nothing mine

[136-172]that I have written, Daniel; for though our masters delivered rightly

[136-173]that everything should be quoted in the name of him that said it—and

[136-174]their rule is good—yet it does not exclude the willing marriage which

[136-175]melts soul into soul, and makes thought fuller as the clear waters are

[136-176]made fuller, where the fullness is inseparable and the clearness is

[136-177]inseparable. For I have judged what I have written, and I desire the

[136-178]body that I gave my thought to pass away as this fleshly body will

[136-179]pass; but let the thought be born again from our fuller soul which

[136-180]shall be called yours.”
