[050-001]She took out the torn handkerchief from her pocket again, and wrapped

[050-002]it deliberately round the necklace. [[Mrs-Davilow|Mrs. Davilow]] observed the action

[050-003]with some surprise, but the tone of her last words discouraged her from

[050-004]asking any question.

[050-005]The “feeling” [[Gwendolen-Harleth|Gwendolen]] spoke of with an air of tragedy was not to be

[050-006]explained by the mere fact that she was going to be a governess: she

[050-007]was possessed by a spirit of general disappointment. It was not simply

[050-008]that she had a distaste for what she was called on to do: the distaste

[050-009]spread itself over the world outside her penitentiary, since she saw

[050-010]nothing very pleasant in it that seemed attainable by her even if she

[050-011]were free. Naturally her grievances did not seem to her smaller than

[050-012]some of her male contemporaries held theirs to be when they felt a

[050-013]profession too narrow for their powers, and had an _à priori_

[050-014]conviction that it was not worth while to put forth their latent

[050-015]abilities. Because her education had been less expensive than theirs,

[050-016]it did not follow that she should have wider emotions or a keener

[050-017]intellectual vision. Her griefs were feminine; but to her as a woman

[050-018]they were not the less hard to bear, and she felt an equal right to the

[050-019]Promethean tone.

[050-020]But the movement of mind which led her to keep the necklace, to fold it

[050-021]up in the handkerchief, and rise to put it in her _nécessaire_, where

[050-022]she had first placed it when it had been returned to her, was more

[050-023]peculiar, and what would be called less reasonable. It came from that

[050-024]streak of superstition in her which attached itself both to her

[050-025]confidence and her terror—a superstition which lingers in an intense

[050-026]personality even in spite of theory and science; any dread or hope for

[050-027]self being stronger than all reasons for or against it. Why she should

[050-028]suddenly determine not to part with the necklace was not much clearer

[050-029]to her than why she should sometimes have been frightened to find

[050-030]herself in the fields alone: she had a confused state of emotion about

[050-031][[Daniel-Deronda|Deronda]]—was it [[pride|wounded pride]] and resentment, or a certain awe and

[050-032]exceptional trust? It was something vague and yet mastering, which

[050-033]impelled her to this action about the necklace. There is a great deal

[050-034]of unmapped country within us which would have to be taken into account

[050-035]in an explanation of our gusts and storms.

[050-036]CHAPTER XXV.

[050-037]How trace the why and wherefore in a mind reduced to the barrenness of

[050-038]a fastidious egoism, in which all direct desires are dulled, and have

[050-039]dwindled from motives into a vacillating expectation of motives: a

[050-040]mind made up of moods, where a fitful impulse springs here and there

[050-041]conspicuously rank amid the general weediness? ’Tis a condition apt to

[050-042]befall a life too much at large, unmoulded by the pressure of

[050-043][[duty|obligation]]. _Nam deteriores omnes sumus licentiæ_, or, as a more

[050-044]familiar tongue might deliver it, _“As you like” is a bad finger-post._

[050-045]Potentates make known their intentions and affect the funds at a small

[050-046]expense of words. So when [[Henleigh-Grandcourt|Grandcourt]], after learning that Gwendolen had

[050-047]left [[Leubronn]], incidentally pronounced that resort of fashion a beastly

[050-048]hole, worse than Baden, the remark was conclusive to [[Lush|Mr. Lush]] that his

[050-049]patron intended straightway to return to [[diplow-hall|Diplow]]. The execution was sure

[050-050]to be slower than the intention, and, in fact, Grandcourt did loiter

[050-051]through the next day without giving any distinct orders about

[050-052]departure—perhaps because he discerned that Lush was expecting them:

[050-053]he lingered over his toilet, and certainly came down with a faded

[050-054]aspect of perfect distinction which made fresh complexions and hands

[050-055]with the blood in them, seem signs of raw vulgarity; he lingered on the

[050-056]terrace, in the gambling-rooms, in the reading-room, occupying himself

[050-057]in being indifferent to everybody and everything around him. When he

[050-058]met Lady Mallinger, however, he took some trouble—raised his hat,

[050-059]paused, and proved that he listened to her recommendation of the waters

[050-060]by replying, “Yes; I heard somebody say how providential it was that

[050-061]there always happened to be springs at gambling places.”

[050-062]“Oh, that was a joke,” said innocent Lady Mallinger, misled by

[050-063]Grandcourt’s languid seriousness, “in imitation of the old one about

[050-064]the towns and the rivers, you know.”

[050-065]“Ah, perhaps,” said Grandcourt, without change of expression. Lady

[050-066]Mallinger thought this worth telling to [[Sir-Hugo-Mallinger|Sir Hugo]], who said, “Oh, my

[050-067]dear, he is not a fool. You must not suppose that he can’t see a joke.

[050-068]He can play his cards as well as most of us.”

[050-069]“He has never seemed to me a very sensible man,” said Lady Mallinger,

[050-070]in excuse of herself. She had a secret objection to meeting Grandcourt,

[050-071]who was little else to her than a large living sign of what she felt to

[050-072]be her failure as a wife—the not having presented Sir Hugo with a son.

[050-073]Her constant reflection was that her husband might fairly regret his

[050-074]choice, and if he had not been very good might have treated her with

[050-075]some roughness in consequence, gentlemen naturally disliking to be

[050-076]disappointed.

[050-077]Deronda, too, had a recognition from Grandcourt, for which he was not

[050-078]grateful, though he took care to return it with perfect civility. No

[050-079]reasoning as to the foundations of custom could do away with the

[050-080]early-rooted feeling that his birth had been attended with injury for

[050-081]which his father was to blame; and seeing that but for this injury

[050-082]Grandcourt’s prospects might have been his, he was proudly resolute not

[050-083]to behave in any way that might be interpreted into irritation on that

[050-084]score. He saw a very easy descent into mean unreasoning rancor and

[050-085]triumph in others’ frustration; and being determined not to go down

[050-086]that ugly pit, he turned his back on it, clinging to the kindlier

[050-087]affections within him as a possession. Pride certainly helped him

[050-088]well—the pride of not recognizing a disadvantage for one’s self which

[050-089]vulgar minds are disposed to exaggerate, such as the shabby equipage of

[050-090]poverty: he would not have a man like Grandcourt suppose himself envied

[050-091]by him. But there is no guarding against interpretation. Grandcourt did

[050-092]believe that Deronda, poor devil, who he had no doubt was his cousin by

[050-093]the father’s side, inwardly winced under their mutual position;

[050-094]wherefore the presence of that less lucky person was more agreeable to

[050-095]him than it would otherwise have been. An imaginary envy, the idea that

[050-096]others feel their comparative deficiency, is the ordinary _cortège_ of

[050-097]egoism; and his pet dogs were not the only beings that Grandcourt liked

[050-098]to feel his power over in making them jealous. Hence he was civil

[050-099]enough to exchange several words with Deronda on the terrace about the

[050-100]hunting round Diplow, and even said, “You had better come over for a

[050-101]run or two when the season begins.”

[050-102]Lush, not displeased with delay, amused himself very well, partly in

[050-103]gossiping with Sir Hugo and in answering his questions about

[050-104]Grandcourt’s affairs so far as they might affect his willingness to

[050-105]part with his interest in Diplow. Also about Grandcourt’s personal

[050-106]entanglements, the baronet knew enough already for Lush to feel

[050-107]released from silence on a sunny autumn day, when there was nothing

[050-108]more agreeable to do in lounging promenades than to speak freely of a

[050-109]tyrannous patron behind his back. Sir Hugo willingly inclined his ear

[050-110]to a little good-humored scandal, which he was fond of calling _traits

[050-111]de mœurs_; but he was strict in keeping such communications from

[050-112]hearers who might take them too seriously. Whatever knowledge he had of

[050-113]his nephew’s secrets, he had never spoken of it to Deronda, who

[050-114]considered Grandcourt a pale-blooded mortal, but was far from wishing

[050-115]to hear how the red corpuscles had been washed out of him. It was

[050-116]Lush’s policy and inclination to gratify everybody when he had no

[050-117]reason to the contrary; and the baronet always treated him well, as one

[050-118]of those easy-handled personages who, frequenting the society of

[050-119]gentlemen, without being exactly gentlemen themselves, can be the more

[050-120]serviceable, like the second-best articles of our wardrobe, which we

[050-121]use with a comfortable freedom from anxiety.

[050-122]“Well, you will let me know the turn of events,” said Sir Hugo, “if

[050-123]this marriage seems likely to come off after all, or if anything else

[050-124]happens to make the want of money pressing. My plan would be much

[050-125]better for him than burdening [[ryelands|Ryelands]].”

[050-126]“That’s true,” said Lush, “only it must not be urged on him—just

[050-127]placed in his way that the scent may tickle him. Grandcourt is not a

[050-128]man to be always led by what makes for his own interest; especially if

[050-129]you let him see that it makes for your interest too. I’m attached to

[050-130]him, of course. I’ve given up everything else for the sake of keeping

[050-131]by him, and it has lasted a good fifteen years now. He would not easily

[050-132]get any one else to fill my place. He’s a peculiar character, is

[050-133]Henleigh Grandcourt, and it has been growing on him of late years.

[050-134]However, I’m of a constant disposition, and I’ve been a sort of

[050-135]guardian to him since he was twenty; an uncommonly fascinating fellow

[050-136]he was then, to be sure—and could be now, if he liked. I’m attached to

[050-137]him; and it would be a good deal worse for him if he missed me at his

[050-138]elbow.”

[050-139]Sir Hugo did not think it needful to express his sympathy or even

[050-140]assent, and perhaps Lush himself did not expect this sketch of his

[050-141]motives to be taken as exact. But how can a man avoid himself as a

[050-142]subject in conversation? And he must make some sort of decent toilet in

[050-143]words, as in cloth and linen. Lush’s listener was not severe: a member

[050-144]of Parliament could allow for the necessities of verbal toilet; and the

[050-145]dialogue went on without any change of mutual estimate.

[050-146]However, Lush’s easy prospect of indefinite procrastination was cut off

[050-147]the next morning by Grandcourt’s saluting him with the question,

[050-148]“Are you making all the arrangements for our starting by the [[paris|Paris]]

[050-149]train?”

[050-150]“I didn’t know you meant to start,” said Lush, not exactly taken by

[050-151]surprise.

[050-152]“You might have known,” said Grandcourt, looking at the burned length

[050-153]of his cigar, and speaking in that lowered tone which was usual with

[050-154]him when he meant to express disgust and be peremptory. “Just see to

[050-155]everything, will you? and mind no brute gets into the same carriage

[050-156]with us. And leave my P. P. C. at the Mallingers’.”

[050-157]In consequence they were at Paris the next day; but here Lush was

[050-158]gratified by the proposal or command that he should go straight on to

[050-159]Diplow and see that everything was right, while Grandcourt and the

[050-160]valet remained behind; and it was not until several days later that

[050-161]Lush received the telegram ordering the carriage to the Wanchester

[050-162]station.

[050-163]He had used the interim actively, not only in carrying out Grandcourt’s

[050-164]orders about the stud and household, but in learning all he could of

[050-165]Gwendolen, and how things were going on at [[Offendene]]. What was the

[050-166]probable effect that the news of the family misfortunes would have on

[050-167]Grandcourt’s fitful obstinacy he felt to be quite incalculable. So far

[050-168]as the girl’s poverty might be an argument that she would accept an

[050-169]offer from him now in spite of any previous coyness, it might remove

[050-170]that bitter objection to risk a repulse which Lush divined to be one of

[050-171]Grandcourt’s deterring motives; on the other hand, the certainty of

[050-172]acceptance was just “the sort of thing” to make him lapse hither and

[050-173]thither with no more apparent will than a moth. Lush had had his patron

[050-174]under close observation for many years, and knew him perhaps better

[050-175]than he knew any other subject; but to know Grandcourt was to doubt

[050-176]what he would do in any particular case. It might happen that he would

[050-177]behave with an apparent magnanimity, like the hero of a modern French

[050-178]drama, whose sudden start into moral splendor after much lying and

[050-179]meanness, leaves you little confidence as to any part of his career

[050-180]that may follow the fall of the curtain. Indeed, what attitude would

[050-181]have been more honorable for a final scene than that of declining to

[050-182]seek an heiress for her money, and determining to marry the attractive

[050-183]girl who had none? But Lush had some general certainties about

[050-184]Grandcourt, and one was that of all inward movements those of

[050-185]generosity were least likely to occur in him. Of what use, however, is

[050-186]a general certainty that an insect will not walk with his head

[050-187]hindmost, when what you need to know is the play of inward stimulus

[050-188]that sends him hither and thither in a network of possible paths? Thus

[050-189]Lush was much at fault as to the probable issue between Grandcourt and

[050-190]Gwendolen, when what he desired was a perfect confidence that they

[050-191]would never be married. He would have consented willingly that

[050-192]Grandcourt should marry an heiress, or that he should marry Mrs.

[050-193]Glasher: in the one match there would have been the immediate abundance

[050-194]that prospective heirship could not supply, in the other there would

[050-195]have been the security of the wife’s gratitude, for Lush had always

[050-196]been [[Lydia-Glasher|Mrs. Glasher]]’s friend; and that the future Mrs. Grandcourt should

[050-197]not be socially received could not affect his private comfort. He would

[050-198]not have minded, either, that there should be no marriage in question

[050-199]at all; but he felt himself justified in doing his utmost to hinder a

[050-200]marriage with a girl who was likely to bring nothing but trouble to her

[050-201]husband—not to speak of annoyance if not ultimate injury to her

[050-202]husband’s old companion, whose future Mr. Lush earnestly wished to make

[050-203]as easy as possible, considering that he had well deserved such

[050-204]compensation for leading a dog’s life, though that of a dog who enjoyed

[050-205]many tastes undisturbed, and who profited by a large establishment. He

[050-206]wished for himself what he felt to be good, and was not conscious of

[050-207]wishing harm to any one else; unless perhaps it were just now a little

[050-208]harm to the inconvenient and impertinent Gwendolen. But the

[050-209]easiest-humored of luxury and [[art|music]], the toad-eater the least liable to

[050-210]nausea, must be expected to have his susceptibilities. And Mr. Lush was

[050-211]accustomed to be treated by the world in general as an apt, agreeable

[050-212]fellow: he had not made up his mind to be insulted by more than one

[050-213]person.
