[022-001]The appropriateness of the event seemed an augury, and as [[Gwendolen-Harleth|Gwendolen]]

[022-002]stood up for the quadrille with [[Henleigh-Grandcourt|Grandcourt]], there was a revival in her

[022-003]of the exultation—the sense of carrying everything before her, which

[022-004]she had felt earlier in the day. No man could have walked through the

[022-005]quadrille with more irreproachable ease than Grandcourt; and the

[022-006]absence of all eagerness in his attention to her suited his partner’s

[022-007]taste. She was now convinced that he meant to distinguish her, to mark

[022-008]his admiration of her in a noticeable way; and it began to appear

[022-009]probable that she would have it in her power to reject him, whence

[022-010]there was a pleasure in reckoning up the advantages which would make

[022-011]her rejection splendid, and in giving Mr. Grandcourt his utmost value.

[022-012]It was also agreeable to divine that this exclusive selection of her to

[022-013]dance with, from among all the unmarried ladies present, would attract

[022-014]observation; though she studiously avoided seeing this, and at the end

[022-015]of the quadrille walked away on Grandcourt’s arm as if she had been one

[022-016]of the shortest sighted instead of the longest and widest sighted of

[022-017]mortals. They encountered [[Catherine-Arrowpoint|Miss Arrowpoint]], who was standing with Lady

[022-018]Brackenshaw and a group of gentlemen. The heiress looked at Gwendolen

[022-019]invitingly and said, “I hope you will vote with us, Miss Harleth, and

[022-020]Mr. Grandcourt too, though he is not an archer.” Gwendolen and

[022-021]Grandcourt paused to join the group, and found that the voting turned

[022-022]on the project of a picnic archery meeting to be held in Cardell Chase,

[022-023]where the evening entertainment would be more poetic than a ball under

[022-024]chandeliers—a feast of sunset lights along the glades and through the

[022-025]branches and over the solemn tree-tops.

[022-026]Gwendolen thought the scheme delightful—equal to playing Robin Hood

[022-027]and Maid Marian: and Mr. Grandcourt, when appealed to a second time,

[022-028]said it was a thing to be done; whereupon [[Lush|Mr. Lush]], who stood behind

[022-029]Lady Brackenshaw’s elbow, drew Gwendolen’s notice by saying with a

[022-030]familiar look and tone to Grandcourt, “[[diplow-hall|Diplow]] would be a good place for

[022-031]the meeting, and more convenient: there’s a fine bit between the oaks

[022-032]toward the north gate.”

[022-033]Impossible to look more unconscious of being addressed than Grandcourt;

[022-034]but Gwendolen took a new survey of the speaker, deciding, first, that

[022-035]he must be on terms of intimacy with the tenant of Diplow, and,

[022-036]secondly, that she would never, if she could help it, let him come

[022-037]within a yard of her. She was subject to physical antipathies, and Mr.

[022-038]Lush’s prominent eyes, fat though not clumsy figure, and strong black

[022-039]gray-besprinkled hair of frizzy thickness, which, with the rest of his

[022-040]prosperous person, was enviable to many, created one of the strongest

[022-041]of her antipathies. To be safe from his looking at her, she murmured to

[022-042]Grandcourt, “I should like to continue walking.”

[022-043]He obeyed immediately; but when they were thus away from any audience,

[022-044]he spoke no word for several minutes, and she, out of a half-amused,

[022-045]half-serious inclination for experiment, would not speak first. They

[022-046]turned into the large conservatory, beautifully lit up with Chinese

[022-047]lamps. The other couples there were at a distance which would not have

[022-048]interfered with any dialogue, but still they walked in silence until

[022-049]they had reached the farther end where there was a flush of pink light,

[022-050]and the second wide opening into the ball-room. Grandcourt, when they

[022-051]had half turned round, paused and said languidly,

[022-052]“Do you like this kind of thing?”

[022-053]If the situation had been described to Gwendolen half an hour before,

[022-054]she would have laughed heartily at it, and could only have imagined

[022-055]herself returning a playful, satirical answer. But for some mysterious

[022-056]reason—it was a mystery of which she had a faint wondering

[022-057]consciousness—she dared not be satirical: she had begun to feel a wand

[022-058]over her that made her afraid of offending Grandcourt.

[022-059]“Yes,” she said, quietly, without considering what “kind of thing” was

[022-060]meant—whether the flowers, the scents, the ball in general, or this

[022-061]episode of walking with Mr. Grandcourt in particular. And they returned

[022-062]along the conservatory without farther interpretation. She then

[022-063]proposed to go and sit down in her old place, and they walked among

[022-064]scattered couples preparing for the waltz to the spot where Mrs.

[022-065]Davilow had been seated all the evening. As they approached it her seat

[022-066]was vacant, but she was coming toward it again, and, to Gwendolen’s

[022-067]shuddering annoyance, with Mr. Lush at her elbow. There was no avoiding

[022-068]the confrontation: her mamma came close to her before they had reached

[022-069]the seats, and, after a quiet greeting smile, said innocently,

[022-070]“Gwendolen, dear, let me present Mr. Lush to you.” Having just made the

[022-071]acquaintance of this personage, as an intimate and constant companion

[022-072]of Mr. Grandcourt’s, [[Mrs-Davilow|Mrs. Davilow]] imagined it altogether desirable that

[022-073]her daughter also should make the acquaintance.

[022-074]It was hardly a bow that Gwendolen gave—rather, it was the slightest

[022-075]forward sweep of the head away from the physiognomy that inclined

[022-076]itself toward her, and she immediately moved toward her seat, saying,

[022-077]“I want to put on my burnous.” No sooner had she reached it, than Mr.

[022-078]Lush was there, and had the burnous in his hand: to annoy this

[022-079]supercilious young lady, he would incur the offense of forestalling

[022-080]Grandcourt; and, holding up the garment close to Gwendolen, he said,

[022-081]“Pray, permit me?” But she, wheeling away from him as if he had been a

[022-082]muddy hound, glided on to the ottoman, saying, “No, thank you.”

[022-083]A man who forgave this would have much Christian feeling, supposing he

[022-084]had intended to be agreeable to the young lady; but before he seized

[022-085]the burnous Mr. Lush had ceased to have that intention. Grandcourt

[022-086]quietly took the drapery from him, and Mr. Lush, with a slight bow,

[022-087]moved away. “You had perhaps better put it on,” said Mr. Grandcourt,

[022-088]looking down on her without change of expression.

[022-089]“Thanks; perhaps it would be wise,” said Gwendolen, rising, and

[022-090]submitting very gracefully to take the burnous on her shoulders.

[022-091]After that, Mr. Grandcourt exchanged a few polite speeches with Mrs.

[022-092]Davilow, and, in taking leave, asked permission to call at [[Offendene]]

[022-093]the next day. He was evidently not offended by the insult directed

[022-094]toward his friend. Certainly Gwendolen’s refusal of the burnous from

[022-095]Mr. Lush was open to the interpretation that she wished to receive it

[022-096]from Mr. Grandcourt. But she, poor child, had no design in this action,

[022-097]and was simply following her antipathy and inclination, confiding in

[022-098]them as she did in the more reflective judgments into which they

[022-099]entered as sap into leafage. Gwendolen had no sense that these men were

[022-100]dark enigmas to her, or that she needed any help in drawing conclusions

[022-101]about them—Mr. Grandcourt at least. The chief question was, how far

[022-102]his character and ways might answer her wishes; and unless she were

[022-103]satisfied about that, she had said to herself that she would not accept

[022-104]his offer.

[022-105]Could there be a slenderer, more insignificant thread in human history

[022-106]than this consciousness of a girl, busy with her small inferences of

[022-107]the way in which she could make her life pleasant?—in a time, too,

[022-108]when ideas were with fresh vigor making armies of themselves, and the

[022-109]universal kinship was declaring itself fiercely; when women on the

[022-110]other side of the world would not mourn for the husbands and sons who

[022-111]died bravely in a common cause, and men stinted of bread on our side of

[022-112]the world heard of that willing loss and were patient: a time when the

[022-113]soul of man was walking to pulses which had for centuries been beating

[022-114]in him unfelt, until their full sum made a new life of terror or of joy.

[022-115]What in the midst of that mighty drama are girls and their blind

[022-116]visions? They are the Yea or Nay of that good for which men are

[022-117]enduring and fighting. In these delicate vessels is borne onward

[022-118]through the ages the treasure of human affections.

[022-119]CHAPTER XII.

[022-120]“O gentlemen, the time of life is short;

[022-121]To spend that shortness basely were too long,

[022-122]If life did ride upon a dial’s point,

[022-123]Still ending at the arrival of an hour.”

[022-124]—SHAKESPEARE: _Henry IV_.

[022-125]On the second day after the archery meeting, Mr. Henleigh Mallinger

[022-126]Grandcourt was at his breakfast-table with Mr. Lush. Everything around

[022-127]them was agreeable: the summer air through the open windows, at which

[022-128]the dogs could walk in from the old green turf on the lawn; the soft,

[022-129]purplish coloring of the park beyond, stretching toward a mass of

[022-130]bordering wood; the still life in the room, which seemed the stiller

[022-131]for its sober antiquated elegance, as if it kept a conscious, well-bred

[022-132]silence, unlike the restlessness of vulgar furniture.

[022-133]Whether the gentlemen were agreeable to each other was less evident.

[022-134]Mr. Grandcourt had drawn his chair aside so as to face the lawn, and

[022-135]with his left leg over another chair, and his right elbow on the table,

[022-136]was smoking a large cigar, while his companion was still eating. The

[022-137]dogs—half-a-dozen of various kinds were moving lazily in and out,

[022-138]taking attitudes of brief attention—gave a vacillating preference

[022-139]first to one gentleman, then to the other; being dogs in such good

[022-140]circumstances that they could play at hunger, and liked to be served

[022-141]with delicacies which they declined to put in their mouths; all except

[022-142]Fetch, the beautiful liver-colored water-spaniel, which sat with its

[022-143]forepaws firmly planted and its expressive brown face turned upward,

[022-144]watching Grandcourt with unshaken constancy. He held in his lap a tiny

[022-145]Maltese dog with a tiny silver collar and bell, and when he had a hand

[022-146]unused by cigar or coffee-cup, it rested on this small parcel of animal

[022-147]warmth. I fear that Fetch was jealous, and wounded that her master gave

[022-148]her no word or look; at last it seemed that she could bear this neglect

[022-149]no longer, and she gently put her large silky paw on her master’s leg.

[022-150]Grandcourt looked at her with unchanged face for half a minute, and

[022-151]then took the trouble to lay down his cigar while he lifted the

[022-152]unimpassioned Fluff close to his chin and gave it caressing pats, all

[022-153]the while gravely watching Fetch, who, poor thing, whimpered

[022-154]interruptedly, as if trying to repress that sign of discontent, and at

[022-155]last rested her head beside the appealing paw, looking up with piteous

[022-156]beseeching. So, at least, a lover of dogs must have interpreted Fetch,

[022-157]and Grandcourt kept so many dogs that he was reputed to love them; at

[022-158]any rate, his impulse to act just in that way started from such an

[022-159]interpretation. But when the amusing anguish burst forth in a howling

[022-160]bark, Grandcourt pushed Fetch down without speaking, and, depositing

[022-161]Fluff carelessly on the table (where his black nose predominated over a

[022-162]salt-cellar), began to look to his cigar, and found, with some

[022-163]annoyance against Fetch as the cause, that the brute of a cigar

[022-164]required relighting. Fetch, having begun to wail, found, like others of

[022-165]her sex, that it was not easy to leave off; indeed, the second howl was

[022-166]a louder one, and the third was like unto it.

[022-167]“Turn out that brute, will you?” said Grandcourt to Lush, without

[022-168]raising his voice or looking at him—as if he counted on attention to

[022-169]the smallest sign.

[022-170]And Lush immediately rose, lifted Fetch, though she was rather heavy,

[022-171]and he was not fond of stooping, and carried her out, disposing of her

[022-172]in some way that took him a couple of minutes before he returned. He

[022-173]then lit a cigar, placed himself at an angle where he could see

[022-174]Grandcourt’s face without turning, and presently said,

[022-175]“Shall you ride or [[ambition|drive]] to [[quetcham-hall|Quetcham]] to-day?”

[022-176]“I am not going to Quetcham.”

[022-177]“You did not go yesterday.”
