[073-001]A various party had been invited to meet the new couple; the old

[073-002]aristocracy was represented by Lord and Lady Pentreath; the old gentry

[073-003]by young Mr. and Mrs. Fitzadam of the Worcestershire branch of the

[073-004]Fitzadams; politics and the public good, as specialized in the cider

[073-005]interest, by Mr. Fenn, member for West Orchards, accompanied by his two

[073-006]daughters; Lady Mallinger’s family, by her brother, Mr. Raymond, and

[073-007]his wife; the useful bachelor element by Mr. Sinker, the eminent

[073-008]counsel, and by Mr. Vandernoodt, whose acquaintance [[Sir-Hugo-Mallinger|Sir Hugo]] had found

[073-009]pleasant enough at [[Leubronn]] to be adopted in England.

[073-010]All had assembled in the drawing-room before the new couple appeared.

[073-011]Meanwhile, the time was being passed chiefly in noticing the

[073-012]children—various little Raymonds, nephews and nieces of Lady

[073-013]Mallinger’s with her own three girls, who were always allowed to appear

[073-014]at this hour. The scene was really delightful—enlarged by full-length

[073-015]portraits with deep back-grounds, inserted in the cedar

[073-016]paneling—surmounted by a ceiling that glowed with the rich colors of

[073-017]the coats of arms ranged between the sockets—illuminated almost as

[073-018]much by the red fire of oak-boughs as by the pale wax-lights—stilled

[073-019]by the deep-piled carpet and by the high English breeding that subdues

[073-020]all voices; while the mixture of ages, from the white-haired Lord and

[073-021]Lady Pentreath to the four-year-old Edgar Raymond, gave a varied charm

[073-022]to the living groups. Lady Mallinger, with fair matronly roundness and

[073-023]mildly prominent blue eyes, moved about in her black velvet, carrying a

[073-024]tiny white dog on her arm as a sort of finish to her costume; the

[073-025]children were scattered among the ladies, while most of the gentlemen

[073-026]were standing rather aloof, conversing with that very moderate vivacity

[073-027]observable during the long minutes before dinner. [[Daniel-Deronda|Deronda]] was a little

[073-028]out of the circle in a dialogue fixed upon him by Mr. Vandernoodt, a

[073-029]man of the best Dutch blood imported at the revolution: for the rest,

[073-030]one of those commodious persons in society who are nothing particular

[073-031]themselves, but are understood to be acquainted with the best in every

[073-032]department; close-clipped, pale-eyed, _nonchalant_, as good a foil as

[073-033]could well be found to the intense coloring and vivid gravity of

[073-034]Deronda.

[073-035]He was talking of the bride and bridegroom, whose appearance was being

[073-036]waited for. Mr. Vandernoodt was an industrious gleaner of personal

[073-037]details, and could probably tell everything about a great philosopher

[073-038]or physicist except his theories or discoveries; he was now implying

[073-039]that he had learned many facts about [[Henleigh-Grandcourt|Grandcourt]] since meeting him at

[073-040]Leubronn.

[073-041]“Men who have seen a good deal of life don’t always end by choosing

[073-042]their wives so well. He has had rather an anecdotic history—gone

[073-043]rather deep into pleasures, I fancy, lazy as he is. But, of course, you

[073-044]know all about him.”

[073-045]“No, really,” said Deronda, in an indifferent tone. “I know little more

[073-046]of him than that he is Sir Hugo’s nephew.”

[073-047]But now the door opened and deferred any satisfaction of Mr.

[073-048]Vandernoodt’s communicativeness.

[073-049]The scene was one to set off any figure of distinction that entered on

[073-050]it, and certainly when Mr. and [[Gwendolen-Harleth|Mrs. Grandcourt]] entered, no beholder

[073-051]could deny that their figures had distinction. The bridegroom had

[073-052]neither more nor less easy perfection of costume, neither more nor less

[073-053]well-cut impassibility of face, than before his marriage. It was to be

[073-054]supposed of him that he would put up with nothing less than the best in

[073-055]outward equipment, wife included; and the bride was what he might have

[073-056]been expected to choose. “By George, I think she’s handsomer, if

[073-057]anything!” said Mr. Vandernoodt. And Deronda was of the same opinion,

[073-058]but he said nothing. The white silk and diamonds—it may seem strange,

[073-059]but she did wear diamonds on her neck, in her ears, in her hair—might

[073-060]have something to do with the new imposingness of her beauty, which

[073-061]flashed on him as more unquestionable if not more thoroughly

[073-062]satisfactory than when he had first seen her at the [[gambling|gaming]]-table. Some

[073-063]faces which are peculiar in their beauty are like original works of

[073-064]art: for the first time they are almost always met with question. But

[073-065]in seeing Gwendolen at [[diplow-hall|Diplow]], Deronda had discerned in her more than

[073-066]he had expected of that tender appealing charm which we call womanly.

[073-067]Was there any new change since then? He distrusted his impressions; but

[073-068]as he saw her receiving greetings with what seemed a proud cold

[073-069]quietude and a superficial smile, there seemed to be at work within her

[073-070]the same demonic force that had possessed her when she took him in her

[073-071]resolute glance and turned away a loser from the gaming-table. There

[073-072]was no time for more of a conclusion—no time even for him to give his

[073-073]greeting before the summons to dinner.

[073-074]He sat not far from opposite to her at table, and could sometimes hear

[073-075]what she said in answer to Sir Hugo, who was at his liveliest in

[073-076]conversation with her; but though he looked toward her with the

[073-077]intention of bowing, she gave him no opportunity of doing so for some

[073-078]time. At last Sir Hugo, who might have imagined that they had already

[073-079]spoken to each other, said, “Deronda, you will like to hear what Mrs.

[073-080]Grandcourt tells me about your favorite [[Herr-Klesmer|Klesmer]].”

[073-081]Gwendolen’s eyelids had been lowered, and Deronda, already looking at

[073-082]her, thought he discovered a quivering reluctance as she was obliged to

[073-083]raise them and return his unembarrassed bow and smile, her own smile

[073-084]being one of the lip merely. It was but an instant, and Sir Hugo

[073-085]continued without pause,

[073-086]“The Arrowpoints have condoned the marriage, and he is spending the

[073-087]Christmas with his bride at [[quetcham-hall|Quetcham]].”

[073-088]“I suppose he will be glad of it for the sake of his wife, else I dare

[073-089]say he would not have minded keeping at a distance,” said Deronda.

[073-090]“It’s a sort of troubadour story,” said Lady Pentreath, an easy,

[073-091]deep-voiced old lady; “I’m glad to find a little romance left among us.

[073-092]I think our young people now are getting too worldly wise.”

[073-093]“It shows the Arrowpoints’ good sense, however, to have adopted the

[073-094]affair, after the fuss in the paper,” said Sir Hugo. “And disowning

[073-095]your own child because of a _mésalliance_ is something like disowning

[073-096]your one eye: everybody knows it’s yours, and you have no other to make

[073-097]an appearance with.”

[073-098]“As to _mésalliance_, there’s no blood on any side,” said Lady

[073-099]Pentreath. “Old Admiral Arrowpoint was one of Nelson’s men, you know—a

[073-100]doctor’s son. And we all know how the mother’s money came.”

[073-101]“If they were any _mésalliance_ in the case, I should say it was on

[073-102]Klesmer’s side,” said Deronda.

[073-103]“Ah, you think it is a case of the immortal marrying the mortal. What

[073-104]is your opinion?” said Sir Hugo, looking at Gwendolen.

[073-105]“I have no doubt that Herr Klesmer thinks himself immortal. But I dare

[073-106]say his wife will burn as much incense before him as he requires,” said

[073-107]Gwendolen. She had recovered any composure that she might have lost.

[073-108]“Don’t you approve of a wife burning incense before her husband?” said

[073-109]Sir Hugo, with an air of jocoseness.

[073-110]“Oh, yes,” said Gwendolen, “if it were only to make others believe in

[073-111]him.” She paused a moment and then said with more gayety, “When Herr

[073-112]Klesmer admires his own genius, it will take off some of the absurdity

[073-113]if his wife says Amen.”

[073-114]“Klesmer is no favorite of yours, I see,” said Sir Hugo.

[073-115]“I think very highly of him, I assure you,” said Gwendolen. “His genius

[073-116]is quite above my judgment, and I know him to be exceedingly generous.”

[073-117]She spoke with the sudden seriousness which is often meant to correct

[073-118]an unfair or indiscreet sally, having a bitterness against Klesmer in

[073-119]her secret soul which she knew herself unable to justify. Deronda was

[073-120]wondering what he should have thought of her if he had never heard of

[073-121]her before: probably that she put on a little hardness and defiance by

[073-122]way of concealing some painful consciousness—if, indeed, he could

[073-123]imagine her manners otherwise than in the light of his suspicion. But

[073-124]why did she not recognize him with more friendliness?

[073-125]Sir Hugo, by way of changing the subject, said to her, “Is not this a

[073-126]beautiful room? It was part of the refectory of the Abbey. There was a

[073-127]division made by those pillars and the three arches, and afterward they

[073-128]were built up. Else it was half as large again originally. There used

[073-129]to be rows of Benedictines sitting where we are sitting. Suppose we

[073-130]were suddenly to see the lights burning low and the ghosts of the old

[073-131]monks rising behind all our chairs!”

[073-132]“Please don’t!” said Gwendolen, with a playful shudder. “It is very

[073-133]nice to come after ancestors and monks, but they should know their

[073-134]places and keep underground. I should be rather frightened to go about

[073-135]this house all alone. I suppose the old generations must be angry with

[073-136]us because we have altered things so much.”

[073-137]“Oh, the ghosts must be of all political parties,” said Sir Hugo. “And

[073-138]those fellows who wanted to change things while they lived and couldn’t

[073-139]do it must be on our side. But if you would not like to go over the

[073-140]house alone, you will like to go in company, I hope. You and Grandcourt

[073-141]ought to see it all. And we will ask Deronda to go round with us. He is

[073-142]more learned about it than I am.” The baronet was in the most

[073-143]complaisant of humors.

[073-144]Gwendolen stole a glance at Deronda, who must have heard what Sir Hugo

[073-145]said, for he had his face turned toward them helping himself to an

[073-146]_entrée_; but he looked as impassive as a picture. At the notion of

[073-147]Deronda’s showing her and Grandcourt the place which was to be theirs,

[073-148]and which she with painful emphasis remembered might have been his

[073-149](perhaps, if others had acted differently), certain thoughts had rushed

[073-150]in—thoughts repeated within her, but now returning on an occasion

[073-151]embarrassingly new; and was conscious of something furtive and awkward

[073-152]in her glance which Sir Hugo must have noticed. With her usual

[073-153]readiness of resource against betrayal, she said, playfully, “You don’t

[073-154]know how much I am afraid of Mr. Deronda.”

[073-155]“How’s that? Because you think him too learned?” said Sir Hugo, whom

[073-156]the peculiarity of her glance had not escaped.

[073-157]“No. It is ever since I first saw him at Leubronn. Because when he came

[073-158]to look on at the roulette-table, I began to lose. He cast an evil eye

[073-159]on my play. He didn’t approve it. He has told me so. And now whatever I

[073-160]do before him, I am afraid he will cast an evil eye upon it.”

[073-161]“Gad! I’m rather afraid of him myself when he doesn’t approve,” said

[073-162]Sir Hugo, glancing at Deronda; and then turning his face toward

[073-163]Gwendolen, he said less audibly, “I don’t think ladies generally object

[073-164]to have his eyes upon them.” The baronet’s small chronic complaint of

[073-165]facetiousness was at this moment almost as annoying to Gwendolen as it

[073-166]often was to Deronda.

[073-167]“I object to any eyes that are critical,” she said, in a cool, high

[073-168]voice, with a turn of her neck. “Are there many of these old rooms left

[073-169]in the Abbey?”

[073-170]“Not many. There is a fine cloistered court with a long gallery above

[073-171]it. But the finest bit of all is turned into stables. It is part of the

[073-172]old church. When I improved the place I made the most of every other

[073-173]bit; but it was out of my reach to change the stables, so the horses

[073-174]have the benefit of the fine old choir. You must go and see it.”

[073-175]“I shall like to see the horses as well as the building,” said

[073-176]Gwendolen.

[073-177]“Oh, I have no stud to speak of. Grandcourt will look with contempt at

[073-178]my horses,” said Sir Hugo. “I’ve given up hunting, and go on in a

[073-179]jog-trot way, as becomes an old gentlemen with daughters. The fact is,

[073-180]I went in for doing too much at this place. We all lived at Diplow for

[073-181]two years while the alterations were going on: Do you like Diplow?”
