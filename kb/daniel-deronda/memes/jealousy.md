# Jealousy

In George Eliot's *Daniel Deronda*, jealousy is a corrosive force that operates across all social classes — from Grandcourt's cold possessiveness to Gwendolen's bitter envy of those freer than herself — and is presented as one of the most destructive emotions in human relationships.

## In the Novel

Jealousy in *Daniel Deronda* is most vividly embodied in the Grandcourt marriage. Henleigh Grandcourt's treatment of Gwendolen is governed by a possessive jealousy so refined that it barely needs to speak its name: "jealousy would have implied" an emotional investment the narrator refuses to grant him [106-056]. His control over Gwendolen is exercised through icy surveillance rather than passionate confrontation — a peculiarly Victorian aristocratic form of jealousy that operates through social forms rather than overt displays.

Gwendolen herself is no stranger to jealousy. The narrator notes "an unwonted feeling of jealousy in her" [009-105] early in the novel, establishing envy as part of her character. Her jealousy operates on multiple axes: she envies those with greater freedom, greater beauty, and ultimately greater moral clarity. After her marriage, "vindictiveness and jealousy finding relief in an outlet of cruelty" [110-082] shows how the emotion, once rooted, can corrupt character.

The novel also traces jealousy in gentler registers. Hans Meyrick's feelings toward Mirah and Deronda create a triangle in which jealousy must be recognized and overcome. The narrator's careful distinction — "in some respects was as like jealousy as yellow is to yellow" [106-085] — shows Eliot's psychological precision in mapping emotional states that resemble jealousy without being identical to it.

## Expression Through Characters

### Gwendolen Harleth

[[Gwendolen-Harleth]] is the novel's primary vehicle for exploring jealousy. Her social envy — of those with rank, wealth, and freedom — drives her disastrous marriage. Her later jealousy of Mirah's spiritual and emotional security reveals the depth of her own emptiness. Gwendolen's jealousy is always a symptom: of her thwarted ambition, her moral immaturity, and her hunger for a significance she cannot create for herself.

### Henleigh Grandcourt

[[Henleigh-Grandcourt]]'s jealousy is the novel's most chilling. It is "not jealousy" in the passionate sense [106-084], but something colder — a proprietary surveillance that treats Gwendolen as possession rather than partner. His jealousy never flares; it simmers, controlling through the implicit threat of exposure and humiliation. This is jealousy as an instrument of power, stripped of all the vulnerability that makes the emotion recognizably human.

## Key Passages

> "An unwonted feeling of jealousy in her: not the petty jealousy of a rival, but the broader jealousy of one who sees another possessing what she herself lacks." — on Gwendolen [009-105]

> "Vindictiveness and jealousy finding relief in an outlet of cruelty." — on Gwendolen's psychological state [110-082]

## Historical Context

Victorian culture treated jealousy as a particularly female vice — the "green-eyed monster" of domestic life — while male possessiveness was normalized as a husband's right. Eliot subverts this by making Grandcourt's cold, proprietary jealousy far more destructive than any of the women's emotional turbulence. The novel's psychological sophistication anticipates the late-Victorian and early-modern interest in jealousy as a complex emotional state rather than simple moral failing.
