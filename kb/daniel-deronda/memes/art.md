# Art

Art in *[[Daniel-Deronda|Daniel Deronda]]* — primarily music — functions as the novel's test of authenticity. George Eliot distinguishes sharply between art as social decoration ([[Gwendolen-Harleth|Gwendolen's]] "liking for music" [PN=009-003]) and art as sacred vocation ([[Herr-Klesmer|Klesmer's]] demanding standard, [[Mirah-Lapidoth|Mirah's]] devotion), using the contrast to illuminate the moral difference between appearance and reality. The novel's [[klesmers-compositions|musical scenes]] at [[quetcham-hall|Quetcham Hall]] establish the terms of this argument with uncompromising clarity.

## Overview

The novel treats music as its primary artistic domain, and the music scenes at [[quetcham-hall|Quetcham Hall]] — where [[Herr-Klesmer|Herr Klesmer]] holds court — establish the terms of the argument. [[Herr-Klesmer|Klesmer]], "the musical Magus" [PN=009-121], represents art as uncompromising truth: he tells [[Gwendolen-Harleth|Gwendolen]] that her amateur accomplishments are worthless, and he tells [[Catherine-Arrowpoint|Catherine Arrowpoint]]'s parents that his artistic vocation outweighs their social objections. Against this standard, the novel measures everyone who claims an artistic identity.

Art is also linked to the novel's Jewish plot through [[Mirah-Lapidoth|Mirah Lapidoth]], whose [[music|singing]] is not a social accomplishment but a necessity — both economic and spiritual. [[Mirah-Lapidoth|Mirah]]'s art is inseparable from her identity, in contrast to [[Gwendolen-Harleth|Gwendolen]]'s art, which is inseparable from her [[pride|vanity]]. The novel thus uses art as a moral lens: how [[reputation|character]]s relate to art reveals how they relate to truth.

## Role in the Novel

Art provides the novel's most concentrated examination of vocation and authenticity. [[Herr-Klesmer|Klesmer]]'s pronouncements establish a standard of artistic seriousness that measures every [[reputation|character]]'s pretensions. [[Gwendolen-Harleth|Gwendolen]]'s fantasy of becoming a singer is exposed as fantasy precisely because she lacks the dedication [[Herr-Klesmer|Klesmer]] demands.

The [[quetcham-hall|Quetcham]] scenes use music-making as social theater: [[Gwendolen-Harleth|Gwendolen]]'s performance, [[Catherine-Arrowpoint|Catherine Arrowpoint]]'s earnest [[music|musicianship]], [[Herr-Klesmer|Klesmer]]'s terrifying honesty. The Arrowpoint-[[Herr-Klesmer|Klesmer]] marriage plot — in which [[Catherine-Arrowpoint|Catherine]] chooses the Jewish musician over her parents' objections — uses art as the vehicle for the novel's argument that genuine value transcends social convention.

[[Mirah-Lapidoth|Mirah]]'s art represents the integration of talent and identity. Her voice is a gift she works to develop; her [[music|singing]] is her livelihood and her self-expression. Unlike [[Gwendolen-Harleth|Gwendolen]], who wants art to admire her, [[Mirah-Lapidoth|Mirah]] wants to serve art — and through art, to serve something larger than herself.

## Expression Through Characters

### Julius Klesmer

[[Herr-Klesmer|Klesmer]] is the novel's artistic conscience — a figure of almost frightening integrity. His judgments are absolute: "You cannot rule in music; you can only follow" [PN=008-171]. His marriage to [[Catherine-Arrowpoint|Catherine Arrowpoint]], across barriers of class and ethnicity, demonstrates that artistic vocation creates its own aristocracy. [[Herr-Klesmer|Klesmer]] embodies Eliot's conviction that true art demands everything and tolerates nothing less.

### Mirah Lapidoth

[[Mirah-Lapidoth|Mirah]]'s art is presented as natural and unpretentious. Her [[music|singing]] carries the emotional truth of her Jewish identity — her performances of [[hebrew-melodies|Hebrew melodies]] connect the novel's artistic theme to its national theme. [[Mirah-Lapidoth|Mirah]] studies seriously, works as a professional singer, and treats her gift as a [[duty|responsibility]]. She is the novel's demonstration that art can be both a livelihood and a spiritual practice.

### Gwendolen Harleth

[[Gwendolen-Harleth|Gwendolen]]'s relationship to art reveals her [[reputation|character]]'s central limitation. She has "no talent — only liking for music" [PN=009-003], and her desire to become a singer after her financial ruin is exposed by [[Herr-Klesmer|Klesmer]] as a fantasy born of desperation, not dedication. [[Gwendolen-Harleth|Gwendolen]] wants what art can give her (admiration, [[freedom|independence]], identity) without the sacrifice art demands. Her failure at art prefigures her eventual moral education.

## Historical Context

The Victorian period witnessed a revolution in the status of music and musicians in England. The founding of the Royal Academy of [[music|Music]] (1822), the rise of the public concert, and the cult of virtuoso performers (Liszt, Paganini) transformed music from a domestic accomplishment into a professional career. The figure of the "musical genius" — inspired, uncompromising, socially ambiguous — was a recognizable type, and [[Herr-Klesmer|Klesmer]] draws on this cultural script while also subverting it through his Jewish identity.

Eliot's own relationship to music was profound: she played piano, attended concerts regularly, and saw in music a model for the emotional truth-telling she attempted in fiction. *[[Daniel-Deronda|Daniel Deronda]]*, with its sustained attention to musical performance and its use of [[Herr-Klesmer|Klesmer]] as artistic conscience, is her most musically saturated novel.
