[080-001]The next couple that passed were also worth looking at. Lady Pentreath

[080-002]had said, “I shall stand up for one dance, but I shall choose my

[080-003]partner. [[Daniel-Deronda|Mr. Deronda]], you are the youngest man, I mean to dance with

[080-004]you. Nobody is old enough to make a good pair with me. I must have a

[080-005]contrast.” And the contrast certainly set off the old lady to the

[080-006]utmost. She was one of those women who are never handsome till they are

[080-007]old, and she had had the wisdom to embrace the beauty of age as early

[080-008]as possible. What might have seemed harshness in her features when she

[080-009]was young, had turned now into a satisfactory strength of form and

[080-010]expression which defied wrinkles, and was set off by a crown of white

[080-011]hair; her well-built figure was well covered with black drapery, her

[080-012]ears and neck comfortably caressed with lace, showing none of those

[080-013]withered spaces which one would think it a pitiable condition of

[080-014]poverty to expose. She glided along gracefully enough, her dark eyes

[080-015]still with a mischievous smile in them as she observed the company. Her

[080-016]partner’s young richness of tint against the flattened hues and rougher

[080-017]forms of her aged head had an effect something like that of a fine

[080-018]flower against a lichenous branch. Perhaps the tenants hardly

[080-019]appreciated this pair. Lady Pentreath was nothing more than a straight,

[080-020]active old lady: Mr. Deronda was a familiar figure regarded with

[080-021]friendliness; but if he had been the heir, it would have been regretted

[080-022]that his face was not as unmistakably English as [[Sir-Hugo-Mallinger|Sir Hugo]]’s.

[080-023][[Henleigh-Grandcourt|Grandcourt]]’s appearance when he came up with Lady Mallinger was not

[080-024]impeached with foreignness: still the satisfaction in it was not

[080-025]complete. It would have been matter of congratulation if one who had

[080-026]the luck to inherit two old family estates had had more hair, a fresher

[080-027]color, and a look of greater animation; but that fine families dwindled

[080-028]off into females, and estates ran together into the single heirship of

[080-029]a mealy-complexioned male, was a tendency in things which seemed to be

[080-030]accounted for by a citation of other instances. It was agreed that Mr.

[080-031]Grandcourt could never be taken for anything but what he was—a born

[080-032]gentleman; and that, in fact, he looked like an heir. Perhaps the

[080-033]person least complacently disposed toward him at that moment was Lady

[080-034]Mallinger, to whom going in procession up this country-dance with

[080-035]Grandcourt was a blazonment of herself as the infelicitous wife who had

[080-036]produced nothing but daughters, little better than no children, poor

[080-037]dear things, except for her own fondness and for Sir Hugo’s wonderful

[080-038]goodness to them. But such inward discomfort could not prevent the

[080-039]gentle lady from looking fair and stout to admiration, or her full blue

[080-040]eyes from glancing mildly at her neighbors. All the mothers and fathers

[080-041]held it a thousand pities that she had not had a fine boy, or even

[080-042]several—which might have been expected, to look at her when she was

[080-043]first married.

[080-044]The gallery included only three sides of the quadrangle, the fourth

[080-045]being shut off as a lobby or corridor: one side was used for dancing,

[080-046]and the opposite side for the supper-table, while the intermediate part

[080-047]was less brilliantly lit, and fitted with comfortable seats. Later in

[080-048]the evening [[Gwendolen-Harleth|Gwendolen]] was in one of these seats, and Grandcourt was

[080-049]standing near her. They were not talking to each other: she was leaning

[080-050]backward in her chair, and he against the wall; and Deronda, happening

[080-051]to observe this, went up to ask her if she had resolved not to dance

[080-052]any more. Having himself been doing hard duty in this way among the

[080-053]guests, he thought he had earned the right to sink for a little while

[080-054]into the background, and he had spoken little to Gwendolen since their

[080-055]conversation at the piano the day before. Grandcourt’s presence would

[080-056]only make it the easier to show that pleasure in talking to her even

[080-057]about trivialities which would be a sign of friendliness; and he

[080-058]fancied that her face looked blank. A smile beamed over it as she saw

[080-059]him coming, and she raised herself from her leaning posture. Grandcourt

[080-060]had been grumbling at the _ennui_ of staying so long in this stupid

[080-061]dance, and proposing that they should vanish: she had resisted on the

[080-062]ground of politeness—not without being a little frightened at the

[080-063]probability that he was silently angry with her. She had her reason

[080-064]for staying, though she had begun to despair of the opportunity for the

[080-065]sake of which she had put the old necklace on her wrist. But now at

[080-066]last Deronda had come.

[080-067]“Yes; I shall not dance any more. Are you not glad?” she said, with

[080-068]some gayety, “you might have felt obliged humbly to offer yourself as a

[080-069]partner, and I feel sure you have danced more than you like already.”

[080-070]“I will not deny that,” said Deronda, “since you have danced as much as

[080-071]you like.”

[080-072]“But will you take trouble for me in another way, and fetch me a glass

[080-073]of that fresh water?”

[080-074]It was but a few steps that Deronda had to go for the water. Gwendolen

[080-075]was wrapped in the lightest, softest of white woolen burnouses, under

[080-076]which her hands were hidden. While he was gone she had drawn off her

[080-077]glove, which was finished with a lace ruffle, and when she put up her

[080-078]hand to take the glass and lifted it to her mouth, the

[080-079]necklace-bracelet, which in its triple winding adapted itself clumsily

[080-080]to her wrist, was necessarily conspicuous. Grandcourt saw it, and saw

[080-081]that it was attracting Deronda’s notice.

[080-082]“What is that hideous thing you have got on your wrist?” said the

[080-083]husband.

[080-084]“That?” said Gwendolen, composedly, pointing to the turquoises, while

[080-085]she still held the glass; “it is an old necklace I like to wear. I lost

[080-086]it once, and someone found it for me.”

[080-087]With that she gave the glass again to Deronda, who immediately carried

[080-088]it away, and on returning said, in order to banish any consciousness

[080-089]about the necklace,

[080-090]“It is worth while for you to go and look out at one of the windows on

[080-091]that side. You can see the finest possible moonlight on the stone

[080-092]pillars and carving, and shadows waving across it in the wind.”

[080-093]“I should like to see it. Will you go?” said Gwendolen, looking up at

[080-094]her husband.

[080-095]He cast his eyes down at her, and saying, “No, Deronda will take you,”

[080-096]slowly moved from his leaning attitude, and walked away.

[080-097]Gwendolen’s face for a moment showed a fleeting vexation: she resented

[080-098]this show of indifference toward her. Deronda felt annoyed, chiefly for

[080-099]her sake; and with a quick sense, that it would relieve her most to

[080-100]behave as if nothing peculiar had occurred, he said, “Will you take my

[080-101]arm and go, while only servants are there?” He thought that he

[080-102]understood well her action in drawing his attention to the necklace:

[080-103]she wished him to infer that she had submitted her mind to rebuke—her

[080-104]speech and manner had from the first fluctuated toward that

[080-105]submission—and that she felt no lingering resentment. Her evident

[080-106]confidence in his interpretation of her appealed to him as a peculiar

[080-107]claim.

[080-108]When they were walking together, Gwendolen felt as if the annoyance

[080-109]which had just happened had removed another film of reserve from

[080-110]between them, and she had more right than before to be as open as she

[080-111]wished. She did not speak, being filled with the sense of silent

[080-112]confidence, until they were in front of the window looking out on the

[080-113]moonlit court. A sort of bower had been made round the window, turning

[080-114]it into a recess. Quitting his arm, she folded her hands in her

[080-115]burnous, and pressed her brow against the glass. He moved slightly

[080-116]away, and held the lapels of his coat with his thumbs under the collar

[080-117]as his manner was: he had a wonderful power of standing perfectly

[080-118]still, and in that position reminded one sometimes of [[dantes-divine-comedy|Dante]]’s _spiriti

[080-119]magni con occhi tardi e gravi_. (Doubtless some of these danced in

[080-120]their youth, doubted of their own vocation, and found their own times

[080-121]too modern.) He abstained from remarking on the scene before them,

[080-122]fearing that any indifferent words might jar on her: already the calm

[080-123]light and shadow, the ancient steadfast forms, and aloofness enough

[080-124]from those inward troubles which he felt sure were agitating her. And

[080-125]he judged aright: she would have been impatient of polite conversation.

[080-126]The incidents of the last minute or two had receded behind former

[080-127]thoughts which she had imagined herself uttering to Deronda, which now

[080-128]urged themselves to her lips. In a subdued voice, she said,

[080-129]“Suppose I had gambled again, and lost the necklace again, what should

[080-130]you have thought of me?”

[080-131]“Worse than I do now.”

[080-132]“Then you are mistaken about me. You wanted me not to do that—not to

[080-133]make my gain out of another’s loss in that way—and I have done a great

[080-134]deal worse.”

[080-135]“I can’t imagine temptations,” said Deronda. “Perhaps I am able to

[080-136]understand what you mean. At least I understand self-reproach.” In

[080-137]spite of preparation he was almost alarmed at Gwendolen’s precipitancy

[080-138]of confidence toward him, in contrast with her habitual resolute

[080-139][[secrecy|concealment]].

[080-140]“What should you do if you were like me—feeling that you were wrong

[080-141]and miserable, and dreading everything to come?” It seemed that she was

[080-142]hurrying to make the utmost use of this opportunity to speak as she

[080-143]would.

[080-144]“That is not to be amended by doing one thing only—but many,” said

[080-145]Deronda, decisively.

[080-146]“What?” said Gwendolen, hastily, moving her brow from the glass and

[080-147]looking at him.

[080-148]He looked full at her in return, with what she thought was severity. He

[080-149]felt that it was not a moment in which he must let himself be tender,

[080-150]and flinch from implying a hard opinion.

[080-151]“I mean there are many thoughts and habits that may help us to bear

[080-152]inevitable sorrow. Multitudes have to bear it.”

[080-153]She turned her brow to the window again, and said impatiently, “You

[080-154]must tell me then what to think and what to do; else why did you not

[080-155]let me go on doing as I liked and not minding? If I had gone on

[080-156]gambling I might have won again, and I might have got not to care for

[080-157]anything else. You would not let me do that. Why shouldn’t I do as I

[080-158]like, and not mind? Other people do.” Poor Gwendolen’s speech expressed

[080-159]nothing very clearly except her irritation.

[080-160]“I don’t believe you would ever get not to mind,” said Deronda, with

[080-161]deep-toned decision. “If it were true that baseness and cruelty made an

[080-162]escape from pain, what difference would that make to people who can’t

[080-163]be quite base or cruel? Idiots escape some pain; but you can’t be an

[080-164]idiot. Some may do wrong to another without [[conscience|remorse]]; but suppose one

[080-165]does feel [[guilt|remorse]]? I believe you could never lead an injurious

[080-166]life—all reckless lives are injurious, pestilential—without feeling

[080-167]remorse.” Deronda’s unconscious fervor had gathered as he went on: he

[080-168]was uttering thoughts which he had used for himself in moments of

[080-169]painful meditation.

[080-170]“Then tell me what better I can do,” said Gwendolen, insistently.

[080-171]“Many things. Look on other lives besides your own. See what their

[080-172]troubles are, and how they are borne. Try to care about something in

[080-173]this vast world besides the gratification of small selfish desires. Try

[080-174]to care for what is best in thought and action—something that is good

[080-175]apart from the accidents of your own lot.”

[080-176]For an instant or two Gwendolen was mute. Then, again moving her brow

[080-177]from the glass, she said,

[080-178]“You mean that I am selfish and ignorant.”

[080-179]He met her fixed look in silence before he answered firmly—“You will

[080-180]not go on being selfish and ignorant!”
