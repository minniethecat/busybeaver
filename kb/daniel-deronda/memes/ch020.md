[020-001][[Gwendolen-Harleth|Gwendolen]] met Lady Brackenshaw’s gracious smile without blushing (which

[020-002]only came to her when she was taken by surprise), but with a charming

[020-003]gladness of expression, and then bent with easy grace to have the star

[020-004]fixed near her shoulder. That little ceremony had been over long enough

[020-005]for her to have exchanged playful speeches and received congratulations

[020-006]as she moved among the groups who were now interesting themselves in

[020-007]the results of the scoring; but it happened that she stood outside

[020-008]examining the point of an arrow with rather an absent air when Lord

[020-009]Brackenshaw came up to her and said,

[020-010]“Miss Harleth, here is a gentleman who is not willing to wait any

[020-011]longer for an introduction. He has been getting [[Mrs-Davilow|Mrs. Davilow]] to send me

[020-012]with him. Will you allow me to introduce [[Sir-Hugo-Mallinger|Mr. Mallinger]] [[Henleigh-Grandcourt|Grandcourt]]?”

[020-013]BOOK II.—MEETING STREAMS.

[020-014]CHAPTER XI.

[020-015]The beginning of an acquaintance whether with persons or things is to

[020-016]get a definite outline for our ignorance.

[020-017]Mr. Grandcourt’s wish to be introduced had no suddenness for Gwendolen;

[020-018]but when Lord Brackenshaw moved aside a little for the prefigured

[020-019]stranger to come forward and she felt herself face to face with the

[020-020]real man, there was a little shock which flushed her cheeks and

[020-021]vexatiously deepened with her consciousness of it. The shock came from

[020-022]the reversal of her expectations: Grandcourt could hardly have been

[020-023]more unlike all her imaginary portraits of him. He was slightly taller

[020-024]than herself, and their eyes seemed to be on a level; there was not the

[020-025]faintest smile on his face as he looked at her, not a trace of

[020-026]self-consciousness or anxiety in his bearing: when he raised his hat he

[020-027]showed an extensive baldness surrounded with a mere fringe of

[020-028]reddish-blonde hair, but he also showed a perfect hand; the line of

[020-029]feature from brow to chin undisguised by beard was decidedly handsome,

[020-030]with only moderate departures from the perpendicular, and the slight

[020-031]whisker too was perpendicular. It was not possible for a human aspect

[020-032]to be freer from grimace or solicitous wrigglings: also it was perhaps

[020-033]not possible for a breathing man wide awake to look less animated. The

[020-034]correct Englishman, drawing himself up from his bow into rigidity,

[020-035]assenting severely, and seemed to be in a state of internal drill,

[020-036]suggests a suppressed vivacity, and may be suspected of letting go with

[020-037]some violence when he is released from parade; but Grandcourt’s bearing

[020-038]had no rigidity, it inclined rather to the flaccid. His complexion had

[020-039]a faded [[justice|fairness]] resembling that of an actress when bare of the

[020-040]artificial white and red; his long narrow gray eyes expressed nothing

[020-041]but indifference. Attempts at description are stupid: who can all at

[020-042]once describe a human being? Even when he is presented to us we only

[020-043]begin that knowledge of his appearance which must be completed by

[020-044]innumerable impressions under differing circumstances. We recognize the

[020-045]alphabet; we are not sure of the language. I am only mentioning the

[020-046]point that Gwendolen saw by the light of a prepared contrast in the

[020-047]first minutes of her meeting with Grandcourt: they were summed up in

[020-048]the words, “He is not ridiculous.” But forthwith Lord Brackenshaw was

[020-049]gone, and what is called conversation had begun, the first and constant

[020-050]element in it being that Grandcourt looked at Gwendolen persistently

[020-051]with a slightly exploring gaze, but without change of expression, while

[020-052]she only occasionally looked at him with a flash of observation a

[020-053]little softened by coquetry. Also, after her answers there was a longer

[020-054]or shorter pause before he spoke again.

[020-055]“I used to think archery was a great bore,” Grandcourt began. He spoke

[020-056]with a fine accent, but with a certain broken drawl, as of a

[020-057]distinguished personage with a distinguished cold on his chest.

[020-058]“Are you converted to-day?” said Gwendolen.

[020-059](Pause, during which she imagined various degrees and modes of opinion

[020-060]about herself that might be entertained by Grandcourt.)

[020-061]“Yes, since I saw you shooting. In things of this sort one generally

[020-062]sees people missing and simpering.”

[020-063]“I suppose you are a first-rate shot with a rifle.”

[020-064](Pause, during which Gwendolen, having taken a rapid observation of

[020-065]Grandcourt, made a brief graphic description of him to an indefinite

[020-066]hearer.)

[020-067]“I have left off shooting.”

[020-068]“Oh, then, you are a formidable person. People who have done things once

[020-069]and left them off make one feel very contemptible, as if one were using

[020-070]cast-off fashions. I hope you have not left off all follies, because I

[020-071]practice a great many.”

[020-072](Pause, during which Gwendolen made several interpretations of her own

[020-073]speech.)

[020-074]“What do you call follies?”

[020-075]“Well, in general, I think, whatever is agreeable is called a folly. But

[020-076]you have not left off hunting, I hear.”

[020-077](Pause, wherein Gwendolen recalled what she had heard about

[020-078]Grandcourt’s position, and decided that he was the most

[020-079]aristocratic-looking man she had ever seen.)

[020-080]“One must do something.”

[020-081]“And do you care about the turf?—or is that among the things you have

[020-082]left off?”

[020-083](Pause, during which Gwendolen thought that a man of extremely calm,

[020-084]cold manners might be less disagreeable as a husband than other men,

[020-085]and not likely to interfere with his wife’s preferences.)

[020-086]“I run a horse now and then; but I don’t go in for the thing as some

[020-087]men do. Are you fond of horses?”

[020-088]“Yes, indeed; I never like my life so well as when I am on horseback,

[020-089]having a great gallop. I think of nothing. I only feel myself strong

[020-090]and happy.”

[020-091](Pause, wherein Gwendolen wondered whether Grandcourt would like what

[020-092]she said, but assured herself that she was not going to disguise her

[020-093]tastes.)

[020-094]“Do you like danger?”

[020-095]“I don’t know. When I am on horseback I never think of danger. It seems

[020-096]to me that if I broke my bones I should not feel it. I should go at

[020-097]anything that came in my way.”

[020-098](Pause during which Gwendolen had run through a whole hunting season

[020-099]with two chosen hunters to ride at will.)

[020-100]“You would perhaps like tiger-hunting or pig-sticking. I saw some of

[020-101]that for a season or two in the East. Everything here is poor stuff

[020-102]after that.”

[020-103]“_You_ are fond of danger, then?”

[020-104](Pause, wherein Gwendolen speculated on the probability that the men of

[020-105]coldest manners were the most adventurous, and felt the strength of her

[020-106]own insight, supposing the question had to be decided.)

[020-107]“One must have something or other. But one gets used to it.”

[020-108]“I begin to think I am very fortunate, because everything is new to me:

[020-109]it is only that I can’t get enough of it. I am not used to anything

[020-110]except being dull, which I should like to leave off as you have left

[020-111]off shooting.”

[020-112](Pause, during which it occurred to Gwendolen that a man of cold and

[020-113]distinguished manners might possibly be a dull companion; but on the

[020-114]other hand she thought that most persons were dull, that she had not

[020-115]observed husbands to be companions—and that after all she was not

[020-116]going to accept Grandcourt.)

[020-117]“Why are you dull?”

[020-118]“This is a dreadful neighborhood. There is nothing to be done in it.

[020-119]That is why I practiced my archery.”

[020-120](Pause, during which Gwendolen reflected that the life of an unmarried

[020-121]woman who could not go about and had no command of anything must

[020-122]necessarily be dull through all degrees of comparison as time went on.)

[020-123]“You have made yourself queen of it. I imagine you will carry the first

[020-124]prize.”

[020-125]“I don’t know that. I have great rivals. Did you not observe how well

[020-126][[Catherine-Arrowpoint|Miss Arrowpoint]] shot?”

[020-127](Pause, wherein Gwendolen was thinking that men had been known to

[020-128]choose some one else than the woman they most admired, and recalled

[020-129]several experiences of that kind in novels.)

[020-130]“Miss Arrowpoint. No—that is, yes.”

[020-131]“Shall we go now and hear what the scoring says? Every one is going to

[020-132]the other end now—shall we join them? I think my uncle is looking

[020-133]toward me. He perhaps wants me.”

[020-134]Gwendolen found a relief for herself by thus changing the situation:

[020-135]not that the _tête-à-tête_ was quite disagreeable to her; but while it

[020-136]lasted she apparently could not get rid of the unwonted flush in her

[020-137]cheeks and the sense of surprise which made her feel less mistress of

[020-138]herself than usual. And this Mr. Grandcourt, who seemed to feel his own

[020-139]importance more than he did hers—a sort of unreasonableness few of us

[020-140]can tolerate—must not take for granted that he was of great moment to

[020-141]her, or that because others speculated on him as a desirable match she

[020-142]held herself altogether at his beck. How Grandcourt had filled up the

[020-143]pauses will be more evident hereafter.

[020-144]“You have just missed the gold arrow, Gwendolen,” said [[Rex-Gascoigne|Mr. Gascoigne]].

[020-145]“Miss Juliet Fenn scores eight above you.”

[020-146]“I am very glad to hear it. I should have felt that I was making myself

[020-147]too disagreeable—taking the best of everything,” said Gwendolen, quite

[020-148]easily.

[020-149]It was impossible to be jealous of Juliet Fenn, a girl as middling as

[020-150]midday market in everything but her archery and plainness, in which

[020-151]last she was noticeable like her father: underhung and with receding

[020-152]brow resembling that of the more intelligent fishes. (Surely,

[020-153]considering the importance which is given to such an accident in female

[020-154]offspring, marriageable men, or what the new English calls “intending

[020-155]bridegrooms,” should look at themselves dispassionately in the glass,

[020-156]since their natural selection of a mate prettier than themselves is not

[020-157]certain to bar the effect of their own ugliness.)

[020-158]There was now a lively movement in the mingling groups, which carried

[020-159]the talk along with it. Every one spoke to every one else by turns, and

[020-160]Gwendolen, who chose to see what was going on around her now, observed

[020-161]that Grandcourt was having [[Herr-Klesmer|Klesmer]] presented to him by some one unknown

[020-162]to her—a middle-aged man, with dark, full face and fat hands, who

[020-163]seemed to be on the easiest terms with both, and presently led the way

[020-164]in joining the Arrowpoints, whose acquaintance had already been made by

[020-165]both him and Grandcourt. Who this stranger was she did not care much to

[020-166]know; but she wished to observe what was Grandcourt’s manner toward

[020-167]others than herself. Precisely the same: except that he did not look

[020-168]much at Miss Arrowpoint, but rather at Klesmer, who was speaking with

[020-169]animation—now stretching out his long fingers horizontally, now

[020-170]pointing downward with his forefinger, now folding his arms and

[020-171]tossing his mane, while he addressed himself first to one and then to

[020-172]the other, including Grandcourt, who listened with an impassive face

[020-173]and narrow eyes, his left forefinger in his waistcoat-pocket, and his

[020-174]right slightly touching his thin whisker.

[020-175]“I wonder which style Miss Arrowpoint admires most,” was a thought that

[020-176]glanced through Gwendolen’s mind, while her eyes and lips gathered

[020-177]rather a mocking expression. But she would not indulge her sense of

[020-178]amusement by watching, as if she were curious, and she gave all her

[020-179]animation to those immediately around her, determined not to care

[020-180]whether Mr. Grandcourt came near her again or not.

[020-181]He did come, however, and at a moment when he could propose to

[020-182]conduct Mrs. Davilow to her carriage, “Shall we meet again in the

[020-183]ball-room?” she said as he raised his hat at parting. The “yes” in

[020-184]reply had the usual slight drawl and perfect gravity.

[020-185]“You were wrong for once, Gwendolen,” said Mrs. Davilow, during their

[020-186]few minutes’ [[ambition|drive]] to the castle.

[020-187]“In what, mamma?”

[020-188]“[[About]] Mr. Grandcourt’s appearance and manners. You can’t find anything

[020-189]ridiculous in him.”

[020-190]“I suppose I could if I tried, but I don’t want to do it,” said

[020-191]Gwendolen, rather pettishly; and her mother was afraid to say more.

[020-192]It was the rule on these occasions for the ladies and gentlemen to dine

[020-193]apart, so that the dinner might make a time of comparative ease and

[020-194]rest for both. Indeed, the gentlemen had a set of archery stories about

[020-195]the epicurism of the ladies, who had somehow been reported to show a

[020-196]revolting masculine judgment in venison, even asking for the fat—a

[020-197]proof of the frightful rate at which corruption might go on in women,

[020-198]but for severe social restraint, and every year the amiable Lord

[020-199]Brackenshaw, who was something of a _gourmet_, mentioned Byron’s

[020-200]opinion that a woman should never be seen eating,—introducing it with

[020-201]a confidential—“The fact is” as if he were for the first time

[020-202]admitting his concurrence in that sentiment of the refined poet.
