[021-001]In the ladies’ dining-room it was evident that [[Gwendolen-Harleth|Gwendolen]] was not a

[021-002]general favorite with her own sex: there were no beginnings of intimacy

[021-003]between her and other girls, and in conversation they rather noticed

[021-004]what she said than spoke to her in free exchange. Perhaps it was that

[021-005]she was not much interested in them, and when left alone in their

[021-006]company had a sense of empty benches. Mrs. Vulcany once remarked that

[021-007]Miss Harleth was too fond of the gentlemen; but we know that she was

[021-008]not in the least fond of them—she was only fond of their homage—and

[021-009]women did not give her homage. The exception to this willing aloofness

[021-010]from her was [[Catherine-Arrowpoint|Miss Arrowpoint]], who often managed unostentatiously to be

[021-011]by her side, and talked to her with quiet friendliness.

[021-012]“She knows, as I do, that our friends are ready to quarrel over a

[021-013]husband for us,” thought Gwendolen, “and she is determined not to enter

[021-014]into the quarrel.”

[021-015]“I think Miss Arrowpoint has the best manners I ever saw,” said Mrs.

[021-016]Davilow, when she and Gwendolen were in a dressing-room with Mrs.

[021-017]Gascoigne and Anna, but at a distance where they could have their talk

[021-018]apart.

[021-019]“I wish I were like her,” said Gwendolen.

[021-020]“Why? Are you getting discontented with yourself, Gwen?”

[021-021]“No; but I am discontented with things. She seems contented.”

[021-022]“I am sure you ought to be satisfied to-day. You must have enjoyed the

[021-023]shooting. I saw you did.”

[021-024]“Oh, that is over now, and I don’t know what will come next,” said

[021-025]Gwendolen, stretching herself with a sort of moan and throwing up her

[021-026]arms. They were bare now; it was the fashion to dance in the archery

[021-027]dress, throwing off the jacket; and the simplicity of her white

[021-028]cashmere with its border of pale green set off her form to the utmost.

[021-029]A thin line of gold round her neck, and the gold star on her breast,

[021-030]were her only ornaments. Her smooth soft hair piled up into a grand

[021-031]crown made a clear line about her brow. Sir Joshua would have been glad

[021-032]to take her portrait; and he would have had an easier task than the

[021-033]historian at least in this, that he would not have had to represent the

[021-034]truth of change—only to give stability to one beautiful moment.

[021-035]“The dancing will come next,” said [[Mrs-Davilow|Mrs. Davilow]] “You are sure to enjoy

[021-036]that.”

[021-037]“I shall only dance in the quadrille. I told Mr. Clintock so. I shall

[021-038]not waltz or polk with any one.”

[021-039]“Why in the world do you say that all on a sudden?”

[021-040]“I can’t bear having ugly people so near me.”

[021-041]“Whom do you mean by ugly people?”

[021-042]“Oh, plenty.”

[021-043]“Mr. Clintock, for example, is not ugly.” Mrs. Davilow dared not

[021-044]mention [[Henleigh-Grandcourt|Grandcourt]].

[021-045]“Well, I hate woolen cloth touching me.”

[021-046]“Fancy!” said Mrs. Davilow to her sister who now came up from the other

[021-047]end of the room. “Gwendolen says she will not waltz or polk.”

[021-048]“She is rather given to whims, I think,” said Mrs. Gascoigne, gravely.

[021-049]“It would be more becoming in her to behave as other young ladies do on

[021-050]such an occasion as this; especially when she has had the advantage of

[021-051]first-rate dancing lessons.”

[021-052]“Why should I dance if I don’t like it, aunt? It is not in the

[021-053]catechism.”

[021-054]“My _dear_!” said Mrs. Gascoigne, in a tone of severe check, and Anna

[021-055]looked frightened at Gwendolen’s daring. But they all passed on without

[021-056]saying any more.

[021-057]Apparently something had changed Gwendolen’s mood since the hour of

[021-058]exulting enjoyment in the archery-ground. But she did not look the

[021-059]worse under the chandeliers in the ball-room, where the soft splendor

[021-060]of the scene and the pleasant odors from the conservatory could not but

[021-061]be soothing to the temper, when accompanied with the consciousness of

[021-062]being preeminently sought for. Hardly a dancing man but was anxious to

[021-063]have her for a partner, and each whom she accepted was in a state of

[021-064]melancholy remonstrance that she would not waltz or polk.

[021-065]“Are you under a vow, Miss Harleth?”—“Why are you so cruel to us

[021-066]all?”—“You waltzed with me in February.”—“And you who waltz so

[021-067]perfectly!” were exclamations not without piquancy for her. The ladies

[021-068]who waltzed naturally thought that Miss Harleth only wanted to make

[021-069]herself particular; but her uncle when he overheard her refusal,

[021-070]supported her by saying,

[021-071]“Gwendolen has usually good reasons.” He thought she was certainly more

[021-072]distinguished in not waltzing, and he wished her to be distinguished.

[021-073]The archery ball was intended to be kept at the subdued pitch that

[021-074]suited all dignities clerical and secular; it was not an escapement for

[021-075]youthful high spirits, and he himself was of opinion that the

[021-076]fashionable dances were too much of a romp.

[021-077]Among the remonstrant dancing men, however, Mr. Grandcourt was not

[021-078]numbered. After standing up for a quadrille with Miss Arrowpoint, it

[021-079]seemed that he meant to ask for no other partner. Gwendolen observed

[021-080]him frequently with the Arrowpoints, but he never took an opportunity

[021-081]of approaching her. [[Rex-Gascoigne|Mr. Gascoigne]] was sometimes speaking to him; but

[021-082]Mr. Gascoigne was everywhere. It was in her mind now that she would

[021-083]probably after all not have the least trouble about him: perhaps he had

[021-084]looked at her without any particular admiration, and was too much used

[021-085]to everything in the world to think of her as more than one of the

[021-086]girls who were invited in that part of the country. Of course! It was

[021-087]ridiculous of elders to entertain notions about what a man would do,

[021-088]without having seen him even through a telescope. Probably he meant to

[021-089]marry Miss Arrowpoint. Whatever might come, she, Gwendolen, was not

[021-090]going to be disappointed: the affair was a joke whichever way it

[021-091]turned, for she had never committed herself even by a silent confidence

[021-092]in anything Mr. Grandcourt would do. Still, she noticed that he did

[021-093]sometimes quietly and gradually change his position according to hers,

[021-094]so that he could see her whenever she was dancing, and if he did not

[021-095]admire her—so much the worse for him.

[021-096]This movement for the sake of being in sight of her was more direct

[021-097]than usual rather late in the evening, when Gwendolen had accepted

[021-098][[Herr-Klesmer|Klesmer]] as a partner; and that wide-glancing personage, who saw

[021-099]everything and nothing by turns, said to her when they were walking,

[021-100]“Mr. Grandcourt is a man of taste. He likes to see you dancing.”

[021-101]“Perhaps he likes to look at what is against his taste,” said

[021-102]Gwendolen, with a light laugh; she was quite courageous with Klesmer

[021-103]now. “He may be so tired of admiring that he likes disgust for variety.”

[021-104]“Those words are not suitable to your lips,” said Klesmer, quickly,

[021-105]with one of his grand frowns, while he shook his hand as if to banish

[021-106]the discordant sounds.

[021-107]“Are you as critical of words as of [[art|music]]?”

[021-108]“Certainly I am. I should require your words to be what your face and

[021-109]form are—always among the meanings of a noble music.”

[021-110]“That is a compliment as well as a correction. I am obliged for both.

[021-111]But do you know I am bold enough to wish to correct _you_, and require

[021-112]you to understand a joke?”

[021-113]“One may understand jokes without liking them,” said the terrible

[021-114]Klesmer. “I have had [[opera-as-art|opera]] books sent me full of jokes; it was just

[021-115]because I understood them that I did not like them. The comic people

[021-116]are ready to challenge a man because he looks grave. ‘You don’t see the

[021-117]witticism, sir?’ ‘No, sir, but I see what you meant.’ Then I am what we

[021-118]call ticketed as a fellow without _esprit_. But, in fact,” said

[021-119]Klesmer, suddenly dropping from his quick narrative to a reflective

[021-120]tone, with an impressive frown, “I am very sensible to wit and humor.”

[021-121]“I am glad you tell me that,” said Gwendolen, not without some

[021-122]wickedness of intention. But Klesmer’s thoughts had flown off on the

[021-123]wings of his own statement, as their habit was, and she had the

[021-124]wickedness all to herself. “Pray, who is that standing near the

[021-125]card-room door?” she went on, seeing there the same stranger with whom

[021-126]Klesmer had been in animated talk on the archery ground. “He is a

[021-127]friend of yours, I think.”

[021-128]“No, no; an amateur I have seen in town; [[Lush]], a Mr. Lush—too fond of

[021-129]Meyerbeer and Scribe—too fond of the mechanical-dramatic.”

[021-130]“Thanks. I wanted to know whether you thought his face and form

[021-131]required that his words should be among the meanings of noble music?”

[021-132]Klesmer was conquered, and flashed at her a delightful smile which made

[021-133]them quite friendly until she begged to be deposited by the side of her

[021-134]mamma.

[021-135]Three minutes afterward her preparations for Grandcourt’s indifference

[021-136]were all canceled. Turning her head after some remark to her mother,

[021-137]she found that he had made his way up to her.

[021-138]“May I ask if you are tired of dancing, Miss Harleth?” he began,

[021-139]looking down with his former unperturbed expression.

[021-140]“Not in the least.”

[021-141]“Will you do me the honor—the next—or another quadrille?”

[021-142]“I should have been very happy,” said Gwendolen looking at her card,

[021-143]“but I am engaged for the next to Mr. Clintock—and indeed I perceive

[021-144]that I am doomed for every quadrille; I have not one to dispose of.”

[021-145]She was not sorry to punish Mr. Grandcourt’s tardiness, yet at the same

[021-146]time she would have liked to dance with him. She gave him a charming

[021-147]smile as she looked up to deliver her answer, and he stood still

[021-148]looking down at her with no smile at all.

[021-149]“I am unfortunate in being too late,” he said, after a moment’s pause.

[021-150]“It seemed to me that you did not care for dancing,” said Gwendolen. “I

[021-151]thought it might be one of the things you had left off.”

[021-152]“Yes, but I have not begun to dance with you,” said Grandcourt. Always

[021-153]there was the same pause before he took up his cue. “You make dancing a

[021-154]new thing, as you make archery.”

[021-155]“Is novelty always agreeable?”

[021-156]“No, no—not always.”

[021-157]“Then I don’t know whether to feel flattered or not. When you had once

[021-158]danced with me there would be no more novelty in it.”

[021-159]“On the contrary, there would probably be much more.”

[021-160]“That is deep. I don’t understand.”

[021-161]“It is difficult to make Miss Harleth understand her power?” Here

[021-162]Grandcourt had turned to Mrs. Davilow, who, smiling gently at her

[021-163]daughter, said,

[021-164]“I think she does not generally strike people as slow to understand.”

[021-165]“Mamma,” said Gwendolen, in a deprecating tone, “I am adorably stupid,

[021-166]and want everything explained to me—when the meaning is pleasant.”

[021-167]“If you are stupid, I admit that stupidity is adorable,” returned

[021-168]Grandcourt, after the usual pause, and without change of tone. But

[021-169]clearly he knew what to say.

[021-170]“I begin to think that my cavalier has forgotten me,” Gwendolen

[021-171]observed after a little while. “I see the quadrille is being formed.”

[021-172]“He deserves to be renounced,” said Grandcourt.

[021-173]“I think he is very pardonable,” said Gwendolen.

[021-174]“There must have been some misunderstanding,” said Mrs. Davilow. “Mr.

[021-175]Clintock was too anxious about the engagement to have forgotten it.”

[021-176]But now Lady Brackenshaw came up and said, “Miss Harleth, Mr. Clintock

[021-177]has charged me to express to you his deep regret that he was obliged to

[021-178]leave without having the pleasure of dancing with you again. An express

[021-179]came from his father, the archdeacon; something important; he was to

[021-180]go. He was _au désespoir_.”

[021-181]“Oh, he was very good to remember the engagement under the

[021-182]circumstances,” said Gwendolen. “I am sorry he was called away.” It was

[021-183]easy to be politely sorrowful on so felicitous an occasion.

[021-184]“Then I can profit by Mr. Clintock’s misfortune?” said Grandcourt. “May

[021-185]I hope that you will let me take his place?”

[021-186]“I shall be very happy to dance the next quadrille with you.”
