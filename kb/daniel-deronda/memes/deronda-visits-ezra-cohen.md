# Deronda Visits Ezra Cohen's Shop

Seeking information about Mirah Lapidoth's family, Daniel Deronda visits Ezra Cohen's pawnshop in a Jewish quarter of London — and there encounters Mordecai, a dying scholar whose visionary talk of a restored Jewish nation will transform Deronda's life.

## Context

Deronda has been searching for Mirah's long-lost mother and brother, driven by a combination of sympathy for Mirah and his own growing fascination with Jewish life and thought. His inquiries have led him to Ezra Cohen, a prosperous pawnbroker whose name keeps appearing in connection with Mirah's family [069-021]. Deronda is uncertain what he will find — he fears Cohen might be an unsavory character, unworthy of connection with Mirah [069-022]. The name over the shop window confirms he has found the right place [069-049, 069-050].

## The Event

Deronda makes his second meditated visit to the shop, having previously scouted the location [069-120]. Entering through the door of Ezra Cohen's establishment, he finds himself in a world utterly different from the drawing rooms of his upbringing [070-037]. The shop is a pawnbroker's — filled with the pledged possessions of the poor, presided over by the prosperous and pragmatic Ezra Cohen [070-187, 071-067].

But it is not Ezra Cohen who changes Deronda's life. In a back room, Deronda encounters Mordecai — Ezra's consumptive lodger and distant relative. Mordecai is a scholar and visionary who has been waiting, with prophetic certainty, for a successor to arrive [071-168, 071-174]. The moment Deronda enters, Mordecai recognizes him as the one he has foreseen — the young man who will carry forward his vision of a restored Jewish nation [071-175, 071-192].

The Cohens, meanwhile, treat Mordecai with a mixture of familial tolerance and incomprehension. They feed him, house him, and indulge his eccentricities without understanding his intellectual passion [071-200, 072-037]. The scene captures the complexity of Anglo-Jewish life — the tension between material prosperity and spiritual aspiration, between assimilation and tradition.

## Participants

- **Daniel Deronda**: The visitor; he arrives seeking practical information but finds a spiritual calling
- **Mordecai**: The visionary; his recognition of Deronda as his destined successor transforms a chance encounter into a moment of prophetic fulfillment
- **Ezra Cohen**: The pawnbroker; his worldly success and family-centered life represent one model of Jewish identity
- **Jacob Cohen**: Ezra's young son, whose presence humanizes the domestic scene
- **Adelaide Cohen**: Ezra's wife, who manages the household with practical competence

## Consequences

This visit initiates the most significant relationship in Deronda's spiritual development. Mordecai's vision gives Deronda a purpose that transcends his personal uncertainty — a mission to contribute to the preservation and renewal of Jewish national life. The encounter also connects Deronda practically to Mirah's world, as Mordecai is her long-lost brother. The pawnshop, a place of material transactions, becomes the site of a spiritual transmission that will determine the course of Deronda's future.

## Thematic Significance

The pawnshop visit dramatizes the novel's argument that the sacred can be found in the most unlikely places. Mordecai, dying of consumption in a pawnbroker's back room, embodies a spiritual intensity that Deronda has never encountered in fashionable drawing rooms. The scene also illustrates George Eliot's interest in the diversity of Jewish experience — from Ezra Cohen's commercial pragmatism to Mordecai's prophetic idealism.
