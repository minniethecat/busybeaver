# Class

In George Eliot's *Daniel Deronda*, social class is the invisible architecture that shapes every character's destiny — from Gwendolen Harleth's disastrous marriage of convenience to Daniel Deronda's discovery that his bloodline crosses the boundary English society most disdained.

## Overview

Class in *Daniel Deronda* is both omnipresent and unstable. The novel maps Victorian society with sociological precision: the aristocracy (Lord Brackenshaw, Grandcourt), the gentry (the Gascoignes, the Arrowpoints), the professional class (Klesmer, the Meyricks), and the commercial class (Ezra Cohen). Each stratum has its own codes, its own permissible marriages, and its own relationship to the novel's central question: what makes a life worth living?

Eliot's treatment of class is characteristically double-edged. She respects the "time-honored" social forms that "touched all classes within a certain circuit" [016-145] while exposing the cruelty those forms enable. Grandcourt's class position allows him to destroy Gwendolen with perfect social impunity — his manners are impeccable, his tyranny invisible to his peers. The narrator notes how Daniel Deronda's uncertain birth "threw him out from the class of gentlemen" [030-201], a precarious position that prepares him for his eventual embrace of an identity outside the English class system entirely.

> "Threw him out from the class of gentlemen to which he was born." — on Deronda's uncertain birth [030-201]

Klesmer's marriage to Catherine Arrowpoint is the novel's most explicit challenge to class boundaries. When he declares that "nothing but first-class music" [023-026] will satisfy Miss Arrowpoint, he redefines class from birth to merit — an argument the novel ultimately endorses through the dignity it grants his marriage.

The commercial class, represented by Ezra Cohen's pawnshop and family, occupies an ambiguous position. The Cohens are materially comfortable but socially marginal — respectable enough to do business with gentlemen, yet excluded from the drawing rooms where decisions about marriage and status are made. Eliot treats them with ethnographic precision rather than condescension, noting the "strange blending of the commercial with the domestic" in their household [098-012].

> "The tie between the classes had not been broken." — narrator, on the persistence of social connection despite hierarchy [016-145]

## The Class Structure in Daniel Deronda

| Stratum | Representatives | Social Currency | Limitations |
|---------|----------------|-----------------|-------------|
| Aristocracy | Grandcourt, Lord Brackenshaw | Land, title, manners | Moral emptiness, dynastic constraint |
| Gentry | Gascoignes, Arrowpoints | Education, clerical livings, social connection | Financial precarity, status anxiety |
| Professional/Artistic | Klesmer, Meyricks | Talent, education, moral seriousness | Social exclusion, economic vulnerability |
| Commercial | Ezra Cohen, pawnbrokers | Capital, practical skill | Social marginality, gentile contempt |

## Expression Through Characters

### Gwendolen Harleth

[[Gwendolen-Harleth]]'s tragedy is fundamentally a class tragedy. She is raised to expect a life of aristocratic ease but lacks the fortune to secure it. Her marriage to Grandcourt is the logical outcome of a class system that offers women of her rank only one career: advantageous marriage. Her horror of "being classed with Mrs. Gadsby" [013-031] reveals how class anxiety governs even her rebellion.

### Herr Klesmer

[[Julius-Klesmer|Herr Klesmer]] is the novel's class-transgressor, a foreign musician who marries an heiress not for her money but for artistic partnership. His insistence that "high class of school" [041-179] is a matter of artistic standards rather than social rank offers the novel's most radical vision of class transcended through vocation.

### Daniel Deronda

Deronda occupies a uniquely unstable class position. Raised as a gentleman by [[Sir-Hugo-Mallinger]] but uncertain of his birth, he inhabits a social category that is "thrown out from the class of gentlemen" [030-201]. This ambiguity proves generative: freed from the certainties of class identity, Deronda can recognize merit and value across social boundaries — in the Cohens' pawnshop, in Mordecai's attic, in Mirah's vulnerability. His eventual embrace of Jewish identity is simultaneously an exit from the English class system.

## Historical Context

Victorian England was a society acutely conscious of class while also experiencing its slow transformation. The Reform Acts of 1832 and 1867 expanded the franchise; industrial wealth challenged landed wealth; and railways, newspapers, and urbanization blurred old local hierarchies. *Daniel Deronda* was published in 1876, the height of what historians call the "age of equipoise" — a moment when the old aristocratic order still held social prestige but was increasingly challenged by meritocratic and democratic forces.

Eliot's treatment of class reflects this transitional moment, honoring tradition while insisting on the primacy of individual worth. The novel's most radical class argument is embedded in its treatment of Judaism: by making her hero a Jew, Eliot asserts that moral and spiritual aristocracy can exist entirely outside the English class system. Mordecai — poor, foreign, dying — possesses a "moral grandeur" that Grandcourt, with all his wealth and lineage, cannot approach.
