[134-001][[Mirah-Lapidoth|Mirah]] made no answer.

[134-002]CHAPTER LXII.

[134-003]“Das Glück ist eine leichte Dirne,

[134-004]Und weilt nicht gern am selben Ort;

[134-005]Sie streicht das Haar dir von der Stirne

[134-006]Und küsst dich rasch und flattert fort

[134-007]Frau Unglück hat im Gegentheile

[134-008]Dich liebefest an’s Herz gedrückt;

[134-009]Sie sagt, sie habe keine Eile,

[134-010]Setzt sich zu dir an’s Bett und strickt.”

[134-011]—HEINE.

[134-012]Something which Mirah had lately been watching for as the fulfilment of

[134-013]a threat, seemed now the continued visit of that familiar sorrow which

[134-014]had lately come back, bringing abundant luggage.

[134-015]Turning out of Knightsbridge, after [[music|singing]] at a charitable morning

[134-016]concert in a wealthy house, where she had been recommended by [[Herr-Klesmer|Klesmer]],

[134-017]and where there had been the usual groups outside to see the departing

[134-018]company, she began to feel herself dogged by footsteps that kept an

[134-019]even pace with her own. Her concert dress being simple black, over

[134-020]which she had thrown a dust cloak, could not make her an object of

[134-021]unpleasant attention, and render walking an imprudence; but this

[134-022]reflection did not occur to Mirah: another kind of alarm lay uppermost

[134-023]in her mind. She immediately thought of her father, and could no more

[134-024]look round than if she had felt herself tracked by a ghost. To turn and

[134-025]face him would be voluntarily to meet the rush of emotions which

[134-026]beforehand seemed intolerable. If it were her father he must mean to

[134-027]claim recognition, and he would oblige her to face him. She must wait

[134-028]for that compulsion. She walked on, not quickening her pace—of what

[134-029]use was that?—but picturing what was about to happen as if she had the

[134-030]full certainty that the man behind her was her father; and along with

[134-031]her picturing went a regret that she had given her word to Mrs. Meyrick

[134-032]not to use any [[secrecy|concealment]] about him. The regret at last urged her, at

[134-033]least, to try and hinder any sudden betrayal that would cause her

[134-034]brother an unnecessary shock. Under the pressure of this motive, she

[134-035]resolved to turn before she reached her own door, and firmly will the

[134-036]encounter instead of merely submitting to it. She had already reached

[134-037]the entrance of the small square where her home lay, and had made up

[134-038]her mind to turn, when she felt her embodied presentiment getting

[134-039]closer to her, then slipping to her side, grasping her wrist, and

[134-040]saying, with a persuasive curl of accent, “Mirah!”

[134-041]She paused at once without any start; it was the voice she expected,

[134-042]and she was meeting the expected eyes. Her face was as grave as if she

[134-043]had been looking at her executioner, while his was adjusted to the

[134-044]intention of soothing and propitiating her. Once a handsome face, with

[134-045]bright color, it was now sallow and deep-lined, and had that peculiar

[134-046]impress of impudent suavity which comes from courting favor while

[134-047]accepting disrespect. He was lightly made and active, with something of

[134-048]youth about him which made the signs of age seem a disguise; and in

[134-049]reality he was hardly fifty-seven. His dress was shabby, as when she

[134-050]had seen him before. The presence of this unreverend father now, more

[134-051]than ever, affected Mirah with the mingled anguish of shame and grief,

[134-052]repulsion and pity—more than ever, now that her own world was changed

[134-053]into one where there was no comradeship to fence him from scorn and

[134-054]contempt.

[134-055]Slowly, with a sad, tremulous voice, she said, “It is you, father.”

[134-056]“Why did you run away from me, child?” he began with rapid speech which

[134-057]was meant to have a tone of tender remonstrance, accompanied with

[134-058]various quick gestures like an abbreviated finger-language. “What were

[134-059]you afraid of? You knew I never made you do anything against your will.

[134-060]It was for your sake I broke up your engagement in the Vorstadt,

[134-061]because I saw it didn’t suit you, and you repaid me by leaving me to

[134-062]the bad times that came in consequence. I had made an easier engagement

[134-063]for you at the Vorstadt Theater in Dresden: I didn’t tell you, because

[134-064]I wanted to take you by surprise. And you left me planted

[134-065]there—obliged to make myself scarce because I had broken contract.

[134-066]That was hard lines for me, after I had given up everything for the

[134-067]sake of getting you an education which was to be a fortune to you. What

[134-068]father devoted himself to his daughter more than I did to you? You know

[134-069]how I bore that disappointment in your voice, and made the best of it:

[134-070]and when I had nobody besides you, and was getting broken, as a man

[134-071]must who has had to fight his way with his brains—you chose that time

[134-072]to leave me. Who else was it you owed everything to, if not to me? and

[134-073]where was your feeling in return? For what my daughter cared, I might

[134-074]have died in a ditch.”

[134-075][[Lapidoth]] stopped short here, not from lack of invention, but because he

[134-076]had reached a pathetic climax, and gave a sudden sob, like a woman’s,

[134-077]taking out hastily an old yellow silk handkerchief. He really felt that

[134-078]his daughter had treated him ill—a sort of sensibility which is

[134-079]naturally strong in unscrupulous persons, who put down what is owing to

[134-080]them, without any _per contra_. Mirah, in spite of that sob, had energy

[134-081]enough not to let him suppose that he deceived her. She answered more

[134-082]firmly, though it was the first time she had ever used accusing words

[134-083]to him.

[134-084]“You know why I left you, father; and I had reason to distrust you,

[134-085]because I felt sure that you had deceived my mother. If I could have

[134-086]trusted you, I would have stayed with you and worked for you.”

[134-087]“I never meant to deceive your mother, Mirah,” said Lapidoth, putting

[134-088]back his handkerchief, but beginning with a voice that seemed to

[134-089]struggle against further sobbing. “I meant to take you back to her, but

[134-090]chances hindered me just at the time, and then there came information

[134-091]of her death. It was better for you that I should stay where I was, and

[134-092]your brother could take care of himself. Nobody had any claim on me but

[134-093]you. I had word of your mother’s death from a particular friend, who

[134-094]had undertaken to manage things for me, and I sent him over money to

[134-095]pay expenses. There’s one chance to be sure—” Lapidoth had quickly

[134-096]conceived that he must guard against something unlikely, yet

[134-097]possible—“he may have written me lies for the sake of getting the

[134-098]money out of me.”

[134-099]Mirah made no answer; she could not bear to utter the only true one—“I

[134-100]don’t believe one word of what you say”—and she simply showed a wish

[134-101]that they should walk on, feeling that their standing still might draw

[134-102]down unpleasant notice. Even as they walked along, their companionship

[134-103]might well have made a passer-by turn back to look at them. The figure

[134-104]of Mirah, with her beauty set off by the quiet, careful dress of an

[134-105]English lady, made a strange pendant to this shabby, foreign-looking,

[134-106]eager, and gesticulating man, who withal had an ineffaceable jauntiness

[134-107]of air, perhaps due to the bushy curls of his grizzled hair, the

[134-108]smallness of his hands and feet, and his light walk.

[134-109]“You seem to have done well for yourself, Mirah? _You_ are in no want,

[134-110]I see,” said the father, looking at her with emphatic examination.

[134-111]“Good friends who found me in distress have helped me to get work,”

[134-112]said Mirah, hardly knowing what she actually said, from being occupied

[134-113]with what she would presently have to say. “I give lessons. I have sung

[134-114]in private houses. I have just been singing at a private concert.” She

[134-115]paused, and then added, with significance, “I have very good friends,

[134-116]who know all about me.”

[134-117]“And you would be ashamed they should see your father in this plight?

[134-118]No wonder. I came to England with no prospect, but the chance of

[134-119]finding you. It was a mad quest; but a father’s heart is

[134-120]superstitious—feels a loadstone drawing it somewhere or other. I might

[134-121]have done very well, staying abroad: when I hadn’t you to take care of,

[134-122]I could have rolled or settled as easily as a ball; but it’s hard being

[134-123]lonely in the world, when your spirit’s beginning to break. And I

[134-124]thought my little Mirah would repent leaving her father when she came

[134-125]to look back. I’ve had a sharp pinch to work my way; I don’t know what

[134-126]I shall come down to next. Talents like mine are no use in this

[134-127]country. When a man’s getting out at elbows nobody will believe in him.

[134-128]I couldn’t get any decent employ with my appearance. I’ve been obliged

[134-129]to get pretty low for a shilling already.”

[134-130]Mirah’s anxiety was quick enough to imagine her father’s sinking into a

[134-131]further degradation, which she was bound to hinder if she could. But

[134-132]before she could answer his string of inventive sentences, delivered

[134-133]with as much glibness as if they had been learned by rote, he added

[134-134]promptly,

[134-135]“Where do you live, Mirah?”

[134-136]“Here, in this square. We are not far from the house.”

[134-137]“In lodgings?”

[134-138]“Yes.”

[134-139]“Any one to take care of you?”

[134-140]“Yes,” said Mirah again, looking full at the keen face which was turned

[134-141]toward hers—“my brother.”

[134-142]The father’s eyelids fluttered as if the lightning had come across

[134-143]them, and there was a slight movement of the shoulders. But he said,

[134-144]after a just perceptible pause: “[[Mordecai|Ezra]]? How did you know—how did you

[134-145]find him?”

[134-146]“That would take long to tell. Here we are at the door. My brother

[134-147]would not wish me to close it on you.”

[134-148]Mirah was already on the doorstep, but had her face turned toward her

[134-149]father, who stood below her on the pavement. Her heart had begun to

[134-150]beat faster with the prospect of what was coming in the presence of

[134-151]Ezra; and already in this attitude of giving leave to the father whom

[134-152]she had been used to obey—in this sight of him standing below her,

[134-153]with a perceptible shrinking from the admission which he had been

[134-154]indirectly asking for, she had a pang of the peculiar, sympathetic

[134-155][[shame|humiliation]] and shame—the stabbed heart of reverence—which belongs to

[134-156]a nature intensely filial.

[134-157]“Stay a minute, _Liebchen_,” said Lapidoth, speaking in a lowered tone;

[134-158]“what sort of man has Ezra turned out?”

[134-159]“A good man—a wonderful man,” said Mirah, with slow emphasis, trying

[134-160]to master the agitation which made her voice more tremulous as she went

[134-161]on. She felt urged to prepare her father for the complete penetration

[134-162]of himself which awaited him. “But he was very poor when my friends

[134-163]found him for me—a poor workman. Once—twelve years ago—he was strong

[134-164]and happy, going to the East, which he loved to think of; and my mother

[134-165]called him back because—because she had lost me. And he went to her,

[134-166]and took care of her through great trouble, and worked for her till she

[134-167]died—died in grief. And Ezra, too, had lost his health and strength.

[134-168]The cold had seized him coming back to my mother, because she was

[134-169]forsaken. For years he has been getting weaker—always poor, always

[134-170]working—but full of knowledge, and great-minded. All who come near him

[134-171]honor him. To stand before him is like standing before a prophet of

[134-172]God”—Mirah ended with difficulty, her heart throbbing—“falsehoods are

[134-173]no use.”

[134-174]She had cast down her eyes that she might not see her father while she

[134-175]spoke the last words—unable to bear the ignoble look of frustration

[134-176]that gathered in his face. But he was none the less quick in invention

[134-177]and decision.

[134-178]“Mirah, _Liebchen_,” he said, in the old caressing way, “shouldn’t you

[134-179]like me to make myself a little more respectable before my son sees me?

[134-180]If I had a little sum of money, I could fit myself out and come home to

[134-181]you as your father ought, and then I could offer myself for some decent

[134-182]place. With a good shirt and coat on my back, people would be glad

[134-183]enough to have me. I could offer myself for a courier, if I didn’t look

[134-184]like a broken-down mountebank. I should like to be with my children,

[134-185]and forget and forgive. But you have never seen your father look like

[134-186]this before. If you had ten pounds at hand—or I could appoint you to

[134-187]bring it me somewhere—I could fit myself out by the day after

[134-188]to-morrow.”
