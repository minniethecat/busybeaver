[061-001]Yet, in fact, [[Henleigh-Grandcourt|Grandcourt]] had never disentangled himself from Mrs.

[061-002]Glasher. His passion for her had been the strongest and most lasting he

[061-003]had ever known; and though it was now as dead as the [[art|music]] of a cracked

[061-004]flute, it had left a certain dull disposedness, which, on the death of

[061-005]her husband three years before, had prompted in him a vacillating

[061-006]notion of marrying her, in accordance with the understanding often

[061-007]expressed between them during the days of his first ardor. At that

[061-008]early time Grandcourt would willingly have paid for the freedom to be

[061-009]won by a divorce; but the husband would not oblige him, not wanting to

[061-010]be married again himself, and not wishing to have his domestic habits

[061-011]printed in evidence.

[061-012]The altered poise which the years had brought in [[Lydia-Glasher|Mrs. Glasher]] was just

[061-013]the reverse. At first she was comparatively careless about the

[061-014]possibility of marriage. It was enough that she had escaped from a

[061-015]disagreeable husband and found a sort of bliss with a lover who had

[061-016]completely fascinated her—young, handsome, amorous, and living in the

[061-017]best style, with equipage and conversation of the kind to be expected

[061-018]in young men of fortune who have seen everything. She was an

[061-019]impassioned, vivacious woman, fond of adoration, exasperated by five

[061-020]years of marital rudeness; and the sense of release was so strong upon

[061-021]her that it stilled anxiety for more than she actually enjoyed. An

[061-022]equivocal position was of no importance to her then; she had no envy

[061-023]for the honors of a dull, disregarded wife: the one spot which spoiled

[061-024]her vision of her new pleasant world, was the sense that she left her

[061-025]three-year-old boy, who died two years afterward, and whose first tones

[061-026]saying “mamma” retained a difference from those of the children that

[061-027]came after. But now the years had brought many changes besides those in

[061-028]the contour of her cheek and throat; and that Grandcourt should marry

[061-029]her had become her dominant desire. The equivocal position which she

[061-030]had not minded about for herself was now telling upon her through her

[061-031]children, whom she loved with a devotion charged with the added passion

[061-032]of atonement. She had no repentance except in this direction. If

[061-033]Grandcourt married her, the children would be none the worse off for

[061-034]what had passed: they would see their mother in a dignified position,

[061-035]and they would be at no disadvantage with the world: her son could be

[061-036]made his father’s heir. It was the yearning for this result which gave

[061-037]the supreme importance to Grandcourt’s feeling for her; her love for

[061-038]him had long resolved itself into anxiety that he should give her the

[061-039]unique, permanent claim of a wife, and she expected no other happiness

[061-040]in marriage than the satisfaction of her maternal love and

[061-041]pride—including her pride for herself in the presence of her children.

[061-042]For the sake of that result she was prepared even with a tragic

[061-043]firmness to endure anything quietly in marriage; and she had acuteness

[061-044]enough to cherish Grandcourt’s flickering purpose negatively, by not

[061-045]molesting him with passionate appeals and with scene-making. In her, as

[061-046]in every one else who wanted anything of him, his incalculable turns,

[061-047]and his tendency to harden under beseeching, had created a reasonable

[061-048]dread:—a slow discovery, of which no presentiment had been given in

[061-049]the bearing of a youthful lover with a fine line of face and the

[061-050]softest manners. But reticence had necessarily cost something to this

[061-051]impassioned woman, and she was the bitterer for it. There is no

[061-052]quailing—even that forced on the helpless and injured—which has not

[061-053]an ugly obverse: the withheld sting was gathering venom. She was

[061-054]absolutely dependent on Grandcourt; for though he had been always

[061-055]liberal in expenses for her, he had kept everything voluntary on his

[061-056]part; and with the goal of marriage before her, she would ask for

[061-057]nothing less. He had said that he would never settle anything except by

[061-058]will; and when she was thinking of alternatives for the future it often

[061-059]occurred to her that, even if she did not become Grandcourt’s wife, he

[061-060]might never have a son who would have a legitimate claim on him, and

[061-061]the end might be that her son would be made heir to the best part of

[061-062]his estates. No son at that early age could promise to have more of his

[061-063]father’s physique. But her becoming Grandcourt’s wife was so far from

[061-064]being an extravagant notion of possibility, that even [[Lush]] had

[061-065]entertained it, and had said that he would as soon bet on it as on any

[061-066]other likelihood with regard to his familiar companion. Lush, indeed,

[061-067]on inferring that Grandcourt had a preconception of using his residence

[061-068]at [[diplow-hall|Diplow]] in order to win [[Catherine-Arrowpoint|Miss Arrowpoint]], had thought it well to fan

[061-069]that project, taking it as a tacit renunciation of the marriage with

[061-070]Mrs. Glasher, which had long been a mark for the hovering and wheeling

[061-071]of Grandcourt’s caprice. But both prospects had been negatived by

[061-072][[Gwendolen-Harleth|Gwendolen]]’s appearance on the scene; and it was natural enough for Mrs.

[061-073]Glasher to enter with eagerness into Lush’s plan of hindering that new

[061-074]danger by setting up a barrier in the mind of the girl who was being

[061-075]sought as a bride. She entered into it with an eagerness which had

[061-076]passion in it as well as purpose, some of the stored-up venom

[061-077]delivering itself in that way.

[061-078]After that, she had heard from Lush of Gwendolen’s departure, and the

[061-079]probability that all danger from her was got rid of; but there had been

[061-080]no letter to tell her that the danger had returned and had become a

[061-081]certainty. She had since then written to Grandcourt, as she did

[061-082]habitually, and he had been longer than usual in answering. She was

[061-083]inferring that he might intend coming to [[gadsmere|Gadsmere]] at the time when he

[061-084]was actually on the way; and she was not without hope—what

[061-085]construction of another’s mind is not strong wishing equal to?—that a

[061-086]certain sickening from that frustrated courtship might dispose him to

[061-087]slip the more easily into the old track of intention.

[061-088]Grandcourt had two grave purposes in coming to Gadsmere: to convey the

[061-089]news of his approaching marriage in person, in order to make this first

[061-090]difficulty final; and to get from Lydia his mother’s diamonds, which

[061-091]long ago he had confided to her and wished her to wear. Her person

[061-092]suited diamonds, and made them look as if they were worth some of the

[061-093]money given for them. These particular diamonds were not mountains of

[061-094]light—they were mere peas and haricots for the ears, neck and hair;

[061-095]but they were worth some thousands, and Grandcourt necessarily wished

[061-096]to have them for his wife. Formerly when he had asked Lydia to put them

[061-097]into his keeping again, simply on the ground that they would be safer

[061-098]and ought to be deposited at the bank, she had quietly but absolutely

[061-099]refused, declaring that they were quite safe; and at last had said, “If

[061-100]you ever marry another woman I will give them up to her: are you going

[061-101]to marry another woman?” At that time Grandcourt had no motive which

[061-102]urged him to persist, and he had this grace in him, that the

[061-103]disposition to exercise power either by cowing or disappointing others

[061-104]or exciting in them a rage which they dared not express—a disposition

[061-105]which was active in him as other propensities became languid—had

[061-106]always been in abeyance before Lydia. A severe interpreter might say

[061-107]that the mere facts of their relation to each other, the melancholy

[061-108]position of this woman who depended on his will, made a standing

[061-109]banquet for his delight in dominating. But there was something else

[061-110]than this in his forbearance toward her: there was the surviving though

[061-111]metamorphosed effect of the power she had had over him; and it was this

[061-112]effect, the fitful dull lapse toward solicitations that once had the

[061-113]zest now missing from life, which had again and again inclined him to

[061-114]espouse a familiar past rather than rouse himself to the expectation of

[061-115]novelty. But now novelty had taken hold of him and urged him to make

[061-116]the most of it.

[061-117]Mrs. Glasher was seated in the pleasant room where she habitually

[061-118]passed her mornings with her children round her. It had a square

[061-119]projecting window and looked on broad gravel and grass, sloping toward

[061-120]a little brook that entered the pool. The top of a low, black cabinet,

[061-121]the old oak table, the chairs in tawny leather, were littered with the

[061-122]children’s toys, books and garden garments, at which a maternal lady in

[061-123]pastel looked down from the walls with smiling indulgence. The children

[061-124]were all there. The three girls, seated round their mother near the

[061-125]window, were miniature portraits of her—dark-eyed, delicate-featured

[061-126]brunettes with a rich bloom on their cheeks, their little nostrils and

[061-127]eyebrows singularly finished as if they were tiny women, the eldest

[061-128]being barely nine. The boy was seated on the carpet at some distance,

[061-129]bending his blonde head over the animals from a Noah’s ark, admonishing

[061-130]them separately in a voice of threatening command, and occasionally

[061-131]licking the spotted ones to see if the colors would hold. Josephine,

[061-132]the eldest, was having her French lesson; and the others, with their

[061-133]dolls on their laps, sat demurely enough for images of the Madonna.

[061-134]Mrs. Glasher’s toilet had been made very carefully—each day now she

[061-135]said to herself that Grandcourt might come in. Her head, which, spite

[061-136]of emaciation, had an ineffaceable beauty in the fine profile, crisp

[061-137]curves of hair, and clearly-marked eyebrows, rose impressively above

[061-138]her bronze-colored silk and velvet, and the gold necklace which

[061-139]Grandcourt had first clasped round her neck years ago. Not that she had

[061-140]any pleasure in her toilet; her chief thought of herself seen in the

[061-141]glass was, “How changed!”—but such good in life as remained to her she

[061-142]would keep. If her chief wish were fulfilled, she could imagine herself

[061-143]getting the comeliness of a matron fit for the highest rank. The little

[061-144]faces beside her, almost exact reductions of her own, seemed to tell of

[061-145]the blooming curves which had once been where now was sunken pallor.

[061-146]But the children kissed the pale cheeks and never found them deficient.

[061-147]That love was now the one end of her life.

[061-148]Suddenly Mrs. Glasher turned away her head from Josephine’s book and

[061-149]listened. “Hush, dear! I think some one is coming.”

[061-150]Henleigh the boy jumped up and said, “Mamma, is it the miller with my

[061-151]donkey?”

[061-152]He got no answer, and going up to his mamma’s knee repeated his

[061-153]question in an insistent tone. But the door opened, and the servant

[061-154]announced Mr. Grandcourt. Mrs. Glasher rose in some agitation. Henleigh

[061-155]frowned at him in disgust at his not being the miller, and the three

[061-156]little girls lifted up their dark eyes to him timidly. They had none of

[061-157]them any particular liking for this friend of mamma’s—in fact, when he

[061-158]had taken Mrs. Glasher’s hand and then turned to put his other hand on

[061-159]Henleigh’s head, that energetic scion began to beat the friend’s arm

[061-160]away with his fists. The little girls submitted bashfully to be patted

[061-161]under the chin and kissed, but on the whole it seemed better to send

[061-162]them into the garden, where they were presently dancing and chatting

[061-163]with the dogs on the gravel.

[061-164]“How far are you come?” said Mrs. Glasher, as Grandcourt put away his

[061-165]hat and overcoat.

[061-166]“From Diplow,” he answered slowly, seating himself opposite her and

[061-167]looking at her with an unnoting gaze which she noted.

[061-168]“You are tired, then.”

[061-169]“No, I rested at the Junction—a hideous hole. These railway journeys

[061-170]are always a confounded bore. But I had coffee and smoked.”

[061-171]Grandcourt drew out his handkerchief, rubbed his face, and in returning

[061-172]the handkerchief to his pocket looked at his crossed knee and blameless

[061-173]boot, as if any stranger were opposite to him, instead of a woman

[061-174]quivering with a suspense which every word and look of his was to

[061-175]incline toward hope or dread. But he was really occupied with their

[061-176]interview and what it was likely to include. Imagine the difference in

[061-177]rate of emotion between this woman whom the years had worn to a more

[061-178]conscious dependence and sharper eagerness, and this man whom they were

[061-179]dulling into a more neutral obstinacy.
