# Pride

Pride in *[[Daniel-Deronda|Daniel Deronda]]* is [[Gwendolen-Harleth|Gwendolen Harleth]]'s defining quality — the "wounded pride" (003-093) that structures her response to every reversal, and the psychological barrier that must be breached before moral growth can begin.

## Overview

Eliot treats pride not as a simple vice but as a tragic necessity for [[Gwendolen-Harleth|Gwendolen]]. In a world that offers women no power except through marriage and beauty, pride is [[Gwendolen-Harleth|Gwendolen]]'s only weapon: "her pride had felt a" mortal wound (046-026) at every intimation of dependency. Pride protects her from the full recognition of her powerlessness. But it also isolates her from the sympathy that could save her.

## Role in the Novel

Pride [[ambition|drive]]s [[Gwendolen-Harleth|Gwendolen]]'s most consequential choices. Her decision to marry [[Henleigh-Grandcourt|Grandcourt]] is, in part, a refusal to be humiliated by poverty; her resistance to his cruelty is sustained by pride even as it deepens her suffering. The novel traces pride's transformation: initially a defense against external judgment, it becomes, through [[Daniel-Deronda|Deronda]]'s influence, a standard she applies to herself. "Her wounded pride could not rest" (047-001) — this restlessness is the engine of her moral development.

## Expression Through Characters

### Gwendolen Harleth
Pride is so central to [[Gwendolen-Harleth|Gwendolen]] that it functions almost as a second [[reputation|character]]. Her "resistant pride" (031-162) flares at every challenge to her [[freedom|autonomy]]. The novel's opening tableau — [[Gwendolen-Harleth|Gwendolen]] at the [[gambling|roulette]] table, refusing to show distress at losing — is a study in pride as [[art|performance]]. Her eventual confession to [[Daniel-Deronda|Deronda]] marks the moment pride yields to something larger: the capacity to be seen as she truly is.

### Other Characters
[[Henleigh-Grandcourt|Grandcourt]]'s pride is colder — the pride of absolute entitlement. [[Daniel-Deronda|Deronda]]'s pride, in contrast, is nearly absent, replaced by a sympathetic imagination that leaves no room for self-regard. The contrast between [[Gwendolen-Harleth|Gwendolen]]'s wounded pride, [[Henleigh-Grandcourt|Grandcourt]]'s cruel pride, and [[Daniel-Deronda|Deronda]]'s selflessness maps the novel's moral spectrum.

## Key Passages

> Vexation of wounded pride. A large corner of her consciousness was filled with this. (003-093)

## Pride and Moral Growth

The novel's opening scene establishes pride as [[Gwendolen-Harleth|Gwendolen]]'s defining trait: "Her pride had felt a terrible knife-edge" (046-026) when she first sensed her [[freedom|powerlessness]], and "a large corner of her consciousness" (003-093) was always occupied by the vexation of wounded pride. Yet Eliot does not treat pride as a fixed defect to be eliminated. Instead, pride is the raw material from which moral seriousness can be forged — if it can be redirected from self-defense toward self-examination.

The turning point comes when [[Gwendolen-Harleth|Gwendolen]] begins to experience "resistant pride" (031-162) not against external threats to her dignity but against her own moral complacency. This is the novel's finest psychological insight about pride: it is not overcome by humiliation but by being given a worthier object. [[Daniel-Deronda|Deronda]] understands this instinctively. His own pride — "the pride of not recognizing a disadvantage for one's self which vulgar minds are disposed to exaggerate" (050-087) — is the model: a pride that refuses self-pity and channels self-regard toward moral purpose. When he appeals to [[Gwendolen-Harleth|Gwendolen]], he is calling her pride to follow his example — pride that once served vanity can, under the right influence, serve conscience.

The [[Henleigh-Grandcourt|Grandcourt]] marriage tests this transformation. Grandcourt's pride is the novel's counter-example — "the pride of the household" (004-062) that treats others as possessions. His pride never evolves because it never encounters a standard higher than his own will. The contrast makes [[Gwendolen-Harleth|Gwendolen]]'s progress legible: wounded pride that resists is better than pride that never questions itself.

## Historical Context

Victorian culture placed pride in an ambiguous moral position. Christian teaching condemned it as the deadliest sin; Romanticism celebrated it as the mark of individual integrity. Eliot, [[reputation|character]]istically, does neither: she treats pride as a psychological fact, a necessary stage in moral development that must be transcended but cannot be simply abolished.
