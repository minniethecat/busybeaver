# Adelaide Rebekah

Adelaide Rebekah is the young daughter of [[Ezra-Cohen|Ezra Cohen]], a child whose vivid presence in the domestic scenes of the Cohen household brings warmth and specificity to the novel's portrayal of Anglo-Jewish family life.

## Role in the Novel

Adelaide Rebekah appears in the domestic interiors of the Cohen household, one of several children whose ordinary liveliness contrasts with the spiritual intensity of [[Mordecai|Mordecai Cohen]]'s prophetic visions. She serves, along with her brother [[Jacob-Cohen|Jacob]], to ground the Cohen scenes in the tangible reality of family life — meals, clothing, childhood rivalries, and the small dramas of the nursery.

Her presence is one of the ways Eliot insists on the full humanity of her Jewish characters. Adelaide Rebekah is not a symbol or a type; she is a specific child with a "miniature crinoline" and braided hair, whose tears at her brother's departure are rendered with unsentimental clarity [071-046, 104-109].

## The Cohen Household

The domestic world Adelaide Rebekah inhabits is rendered with ethnographic richness. On the Sabbath evening, the family gathers around the table in a scene that includes "each of the family, including Adelaide Rebekah, who stood on the" counterpane [071-183]. Her mother introduces her by her full name — "Adelaide Rebekah is her name" [071-035] — with a formality that bespeaks both maternal pride and the ritual quality of domestic introductions. The child herself participates in the family's multilingual idiom, attempting to reproduce the phrase "I'll have Shabbes fyock on" [071-037] in a moment of linguistic imitation that Eliot renders phonetically.

The grandmother's kitchen becomes another site of Adelaide Rebekah's presence: she is "seated on the grandmother's knee" [104-072], an image of intergenerational continuity. Even Jacob's attempts to "terrify Adelaide Rebekah" [086-185] are recorded as part of the ordinary texture of sibling life — teasing, not cruelty. In the novel's final domestic tableau, Adelaide Rebekah appears one more time, "in a new" dress [147-176], a small detail that marks the passage of time and the ongoingness of family life beyond the novel's central dramas.

## Character Traits

### Personality

Adelaide Rebekah is presented as a spirited child, capable of both playful imitation — she mimics adult speech patterns in her family's multilingual household — and genuine emotional response. She "always cried when her brother" departed [104-109], a detail that establishes her capacity for attachment and sorrow within the novel's larger emotional register.

### Appearance

The narrator offers one of the novel's few detailed physical descriptions of a child in rendering Adelaide Rebekah: "her miniature crinoline" and "braided amber" hair [071-046, 071-100]. These details situate her within the material culture of Victorian childhood while suggesting her family's attention to her appearance.

## Relationships

- **[[Ezra-Cohen]]**: Her father, the pawnbroker under whose roof the family lives.
- **[[Jacob-Cohen]]**: Her brother, whose departures prompt her tears [104-109].
- **Mrs. Cohen**: Her mother, who introduces her by name: "Adelaide Rebekah is her name" [071-035].
- **[[Mordecai]]**: The visionary lodger in her family's home, whose spiritual intensity exists alongside the ordinary childhood world she inhabits.

## Character Arc

Adelaide Rebekah undergoes no character development — she is too young, her role too confined to domestic scenes. Her presence in the novel is significant precisely because she does not need to change. She represents the ordinary continuity of Jewish family life that will persist whether or not the novel's protagonists achieve their destinies.

## Thematic Significance

Through Adelaide Rebekah, Eliot insists on the value of the ordinary. Her childhood world — the miniature crinoline, the braided hair, the tears at a brother's departure — represents a mode of being that the novel's grand themes might otherwise overshadow. She is a reminder that the Jewish life Deronda initially disdains [070-187] is rich with its own poetry — the poetry of children growing up in a household where the Sabbath is observed and family bonds are real.
