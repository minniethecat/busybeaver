[108-001]“She told you so—did she?” returned [[Henleigh-Grandcourt|Grandcourt]], with a more thoroughly

[108-002]distilled sneer.

[108-003][[Gwendolen-Harleth|Gwendolen]] was mute. The daring anger within her was turned into the

[108-004]rage of dumbness. What reasons for her belief could she give? All the

[108-005]reasons that seemed so strong and living within her—she saw them

[108-006]suffocated and shrivelled up under her husband’s breath. There was no

[108-007]proof to give, but her own impression, which would seem to him her own

[108-008]folly. She turned her head quickly away from him and looked angrily

[108-009]toward the end of the room: she would have risen, but he was in her way.

[108-010]Grandcourt saw his advantage. “It’s of no consequence so far as her

[108-011][[music|singing]] goes,” he said, in his superficial drawl. “You can have her to

[108-012]sing, if you like.” Then, after a pause, he added in his lowest

[108-013]imperious tone, “But you will please to observe that you are not to go

[108-014]near that house again. As my wife, you must take my word about what is

[108-015]proper for you. When you undertook to be Mrs. Grandcourt, you undertook

[108-016]not to make a fool of yourself. You have been making a fool of yourself

[108-017]this morning; and if you were to go on as you have begun, you might

[108-018]soon get yourself talked of at the clubs in a way you would not like.

[108-019]What do _you_ know about the world? You have married _me_, and must be

[108-020]guided by my opinion.”

[108-021]Every slow sentence of that speech had a terrific mastery in it for

[108-022]Gwendolen’s nature. If the low tones had come from a physician telling

[108-023]her that her symptoms were those of a fatal disease, and

[108-024]prognosticating its course, she could not have been more helpless

[108-025]against the argument that lay in it. But she was permitted to move now,

[108-026]and her husband never again made any reference to what had occurred

[108-027]this morning. He knew the force of his own words. If this white-handed

[108-028]man with the perpendicular profile had been sent to govern a difficult

[108-029]colony, he might have won reputation among his contemporaries. He had

[108-030]certainly ability, would have understood that it was safer to

[108-031]exterminate than to cajole superseded proprietors, and would not have

[108-032]flinched from making things safe in that way.

[108-033]Gwendolen did not, for all this, part with her recovered

[108-034][[religion|faith]];—rather, she kept it with a more anxious tenacity, as a

[108-035]Protestant of old kept his bible hidden or a Catholic his crucifix,

[108-036]according to the side favored by the civil arm; and it was

[108-037]characteristic of her that apart from the impression gained concerning

[108-038][[Daniel-Deronda|Deronda]] in that visit, her imagination was little occupied with [[Mirah-Lapidoth|Mirah]]

[108-039]or the eulogised brother. The one result established for her was, that

[108-040]Deronda had acted simply as a generous benefactor, and the phrase

[108-041]“reading Hebrew” had fleeted unimpressively across her sense of

[108-042]hearing, as a stray stork might have made its peculiar flight across

[108-043]her landscape without rousing any surprised reflection on its natural

[108-044]history.

[108-045]But the issue of that visit, as it regarded her husband, took a

[108-046]strongly active part in the process which made an habitual conflict

[108-047]within her, and was the cause of some external change perhaps not

[108-048]observed by any one except Deronda. As the weeks went on bringing

[108-049]occasional transient interviews with her, he thought that he perceived

[108-050]in her an intensifying of her superficial hardness and resolute

[108-051]display, which made her abrupt betrayals of agitation the more marked

[108-052]and disturbing to him.

[108-053]In fact, she was undergoing a sort of discipline for the refractory

[108-054]which, as little as possible like conversion, bends half the self with

[108-055]a terrible strain, and exasperates the unwillingness of the other half.

[108-056]Grandcourt had an active divination rather than discernment of

[108-057]refractoriness in her, and what had happened about Mirah quickened his

[108-058]suspicion that there was an increase of it dependent on the occasions

[108-059]when she happened to see Deronda: there was some “confounded nonsense”

[108-060]between them: he did not imagine it exactly as flirtation, and his

[108-061]imagination in other branches was rather restricted; but it was

[108-062]nonsense that evidently kept up a kind of simmering in her mind—an

[108-063]inward action which might become disagreeable outward. Husbands in the

[108-064]old time are known to have suffered from a threatening devoutness in

[108-065]their wives, presenting itself first indistinctly as oddity, and ending

[108-066]in that mild form of lunatic asylum, a nunnery: Grandcourt had a vague

[108-067]perception of threatening moods in Gwendolen which the unity between

[108-068]them in his views of marriage required him peremptorily to check. Among

[108-069]the means he chose, one was peculiar, and was less ably calculated than

[108-070]the speeches we have just heard.

[108-071]He determined that she should know the main purport of the will he was

[108-072]making, but he could not communicate this himself, because it involved

[108-073]the fact of his relation to [[Lydia-Glasher|Mrs. Glasher]] and her children; and that

[108-074]there should be any overt recognition of this between Gwendolen and

[108-075]himself was supremely repugnant to him. Like all proud, closely-wrapped

[108-076]natures, he shrank from explicitness and detail, even on trivialities,

[108-077]if they were personal: a valet must maintain a strict reserve with him

[108-078]on the subject of shoes and stockings. And clashing was intolerable to

[108-079]him; his habitual want was to put collision out of the question by the

[108-080]quiet massive pressure of his rule. But he wished Gwendolen to know

[108-081]that before he made her an offer it was no secret to him that she was

[108-082]aware of his relations with Lydia, her previous knowledge being the

[108-083]apology for bringing the subject before her now. Some men in his place

[108-084]might have thought of writing what he wanted her to know, in the form

[108-085]of a letter. But Grandcourt hated writing: even writing a note was a

[108-086]bore to him, and he had long been accustomed to have all his writing

[108-087]done by [[Lush]]. We know that there are persons who will forego their own

[108-088]obvious interest rather than do anything so disagreeable as to write

[108-089]letters; and it is not probable that these imperfect utilitarians would

[108-090]rush into manuscript and syntax on a difficult subject in order to save

[108-091]another’s feelings. To Grandcourt it did not even occur that he should,

[108-092]would, or could write to Gwendolen the information in question; and the

[108-093]only medium of communication he could use was Lush, who, to his mind,

[108-094]was as much of an implement as pen and paper. But here too Grandcourt

[108-095]had his reserves, and would not have uttered a word likely to encourage

[108-096]Lush in an impudent sympathy with any supposed grievance in a marriage

[108-097]which had been discommended by him. Who that has a confidant escapes

[108-098]believing too little in his penetration, and too much in his

[108-099]discretion? Grandcourt had always allowed Lush to know his external

[108-100]affairs indiscriminately—irregularities, debts, want of ready money;

[108-101]he had only used discrimination about what he would allow his confidant

[108-102]to say to him; and he had been so accustomed to this human tool, that

[108-103]the having him at call in [[london|London]] was a recovery of lost ease. It

[108-104]followed that Lush knew all the provisions of the will more exactly

[108-105]than they were known to the testator himself.

[108-106]Grandcourt did not doubt that Gwendolen, since she was a woman who

[108-107]could put two and two together, knew or suspected Lush to be the

[108-108]contriver of her interview with Lydia, and that this was the reason why

[108-109]her first request was for his [[exile|banishment]]. But the bent of a woman’s

[108-110]inferences on mixed subjects which excites mixed passions is not

[108-111]determined by her capacity for simple addition; and here Grandcourt

[108-112]lacked the only organ of thinking that could have saved him from

[108-113]mistake—namely, some experience of the mixed passions concerned. He

[108-114]had correctly divined one-half of Gwendolen’s dread—all that related

[108-115]to her personal pride, and her perception that his will must conquer

[108-116]hers; but the remorseful half, even if he had known of her broken

[108-117]promise, was as much out of his imagination as the other side of the

[108-118]moon. What he believed her to feel about Lydia was solely a tongue-tied

[108-119]jealousy, and what he believed Lydia to have written with the jewels

[108-120]was the fact that she had once been used to wearing them, with other

[108-121]amenities such as he imputed to the intercourse with jealous women. He

[108-122]had the triumphant certainty that he could aggravate the jealousy and

[108-123]yet smite it with a more absolute dumbness. His object was to engage

[108-124]all his wife’s egoism on the same side as his own, and in his

[108-125]employment of Lush he did not intend an insult to her: she ought to

[108-126]understand that he was the only possible envoy. Grandcourt’s view of

[108-127]things was considerably fenced in by his general sense, that what

[108-128]suited him others must put up with. There is no escaping the fact that

[108-129]want of sympathy condemns us to corresponding stupidity. Mephistopheles

[108-130]thrown upon real life, and obliged to manage his own plots, would

[108-131]inevitably make blunders.

[108-132]One morning he went to Gwendolen in the boudoir beyond the back

[108-133]drawing-room, hat and gloves in hand, and said with his best-tempered,

[108-134]most persuasive drawl, standing before her and looking down on her as

[108-135]she sat with a book on her lap,

[108-136]“A—Gwendolen, there’s some business about property to be explained. I

[108-137]have told Lush to come and explain it to you. He knows all about these

[108-138]things. I am going out. He can come up now. He’s the only person who

[108-139]can explain. I suppose you’ll not mind.”

[108-140]“You know that I do mind,” said Gwendolen, angrily, starting up. “I

[108-141]shall not see him.” She showed the intention to dart away to the door.

[108-142]Grandcourt was before her, with his back toward it. He was prepared for

[108-143]her anger, and showed none in return, saying, with the same sort of

[108-144]remonstrant tone that he might have used about an objection to dining

[108-145]out,

[108-146]“It’s no use making a fuss. There are plenty of brutes in the world

[108-147]that one has to talk to. People with any _savoir vivre_ don’t make a

[108-148]fuss about such things. Some business must be done. You can’t expect

[108-149]agreeable people to do it. If I employ Lush, the proper thing for you

[108-150]is to take it as a matter of course. Not to make a fuss about it. Not

[108-151]to toss your head and bite your lips about people of that sort.”

[108-152]The drawling and the pauses with which this speech was uttered gave

[108-153]time for crowding reflections in Gwendolen, quelling her resistance.

[108-154]What was there to be told her about property? This word had certain

[108-155]dominant associations for her, first with her mother, then with Mrs.

[108-156]Glasher and her children. What would be the use if she refused to see

[108-157]Lush? Could she ask Grandcourt to tell her himself? That might be

[108-158]intolerable, even if he consented, which it was certain he would not,

[108-159]if he had made up his mind to the contrary. The [[shame|humiliation]] of standing

[108-160]an obvious prisoner, with her husband barring the door, was not to be

[108-161]borne any longer, and she turned away to lean against a cabinet, while

[108-162]Grandcourt again moved toward her.

[108-163]“I have arranged for Lush to come up now, while I am out,” he said,

[108-164]after a long organ stop, during which Gwendolen made no sign. “Shall I

[108-165]tell him he may come?”

[108-166]Yet another pause before she could say “Yes”—her face turned obliquely

[108-167]and her eyes cast down.

[108-168]“I shall come back in time to ride, if you like to get ready,” said

[108-169]Grandcourt. No answer. “She is in a desperate rage,” thought he. But

[108-170]the rage was silent, and therefore not disagreeable to him. It followed

[108-171]that he turned her chin and kissed her, while she still kept her

[108-172]eyelids down, and she did not move them until he was on the other side

[108-173]of the door.

[108-174]What was she to do? Search where she would in her consciousness, she

[108-175]found no plea to justify a plaint. Any romantic illusions she had had

[108-176]in marrying this man had turned on her power of using him as she liked.

[108-177]He was using her as he liked.
