[120-001]She put her arms on her son’s shoulders at once, and kissed him on both

[120-002]cheeks, then seated herself among her cushions with an air of assured

[120-003]firmness and dignity unlike her fitfulness in their first interview,

[120-004]and told [[Daniel-Deronda|Deronda]] to sit down by her. He obeyed, saying, “You are quite

[120-005]relieved now, I trust?”

[120-006]“Yes, I am at ease again. Is there anything more that you would like to

[120-007]ask me?” she said, with the matter of a queen rather than of a mother.

[120-008]“Can I find the house in [[genoa|Genoa]] where you used to live with my

[120-009]grandfather?” said Deronda.

[120-010]“No,” she answered, with a deprecating movement of her arm, “it is

[120-011]pulled down—not to be found. But about our family, and where my father

[120-012]lived at various times—you will find all that among the papers in the

[120-013]chest, better than I can tell you. My father, I told you, was a

[120-014]physician. My mother was a Morteira. I used to hear all those things

[120-015]without listening. You will find them all. I was born amongst them

[120-016]without my will. I banished them as soon as I could.”

[120-017]Deronda tried to hide his pained feeling, and said, “Anything else that

[120-018]I should desire to know from you could only be what it is some

[120-019]satisfaction to your own feeling to tell me.”

[120-020]“I think I have told you everything that could be demanded of me,” said

[120-021]the Princess, looking coldly meditative. It seemed as if she had

[120-022]exhausted her emotion in their former interview. The fact was, she had

[120-023]said to herself, “I have done it all. I have confessed all. I will not

[120-024]go through it again. I will save myself from agitation.” And she was

[120-025]acting out that scheme.

[120-026]But to Deronda’s nature the moment was cruel; it made the filial

[120-027]yearning of his life a disappointed pilgrimage to a shrine where there

[120-028]were no longer the symbols of sacredness. It seemed that all the woman

[120-029]lacking in her was present in him, as he said, with some tremor in his

[120-030]voice,

[120-031]“Then are we to part and I never be anything to you?”

[120-032]“It is better so,” said the Princess, in a softer, mellower voice.

[120-033]“There could be nothing but hard duty for you, even if it were possible

[120-034]for you to take the place of my son. You would not love me. Don’t deny

[120-035]it,” she said, abruptly, putting up her hand. “I know what is the

[120-036]truth. You don’t like what I did. You are angry with me. You think I

[120-037]robbed you of something. You are on your grandfather’s side, and you

[120-038]will always have a condemnation of me in your heart.”

[120-039]Deronda felt himself under a ban of silence. He rose from his seat by

[120-040]her, preferring to stand, if he had to obey that imperious prohibition

[120-041]of any tenderness. But his mother now looked up at him with a new

[120-042]admiration in her glance, saying,

[120-043]“You are wrong to be angry with me. You are the better for what I did.”

[120-044]After pausing a little, she added, abruptly, “And now tell me what you

[120-045]shall do?”

[120-046]“Do you mean now, immediately,” said Deronda; “or as to the course of

[120-047]my future life?”

[120-048]“I mean in the future. What difference will it make to you that I have

[120-049]told you about your birth?”

[120-050]“A very great difference,” said Deronda, emphatically. “I can hardly

[120-051]think of anything that would make a greater difference.”

[120-052]“What shall you do then?” said the Princess, with more sharpness. “Make

[120-053]yourself just like your grandfather—be what he wished you—turn

[120-054]yourself into a Jew like him?”

[120-055]“That is impossible. The effect of my education can never be done away

[120-056]with. The Christian sympathies in which my mind was reared can never

[120-057]die out of me,” said Deronda, with increasing tenacity of tone. “But I

[120-058]consider it my duty—it is the impulse of my feeling—to identify

[120-059]myself, as far as possible, with my hereditary people, and if I can see

[120-060]any work to be done for them that I can give my soul and hand to I

[120-061]shall choose to do it.”

[120-062]His mother had her eyes fixed on him with a wondering speculation,

[120-063]examining his face as if she thought that by close attention she could

[120-064]read a difficult language there. He bore her gaze very firmly,

[120-065]sustained by a resolute opposition, which was the expression of his

[120-066]fullest self. She bent toward him a little, and said, with a decisive

[120-067]emphasis,

[120-068]“You are in love with a Jewess.”

[120-069]Deronda colored and said, “My reasons would be independent of any such

[120-070]fact.”

[120-071]“I know better. I have seen what men are,” said the Princess,

[120-072]peremptorily. “Tell me the truth. She is a Jewess who will not accept

[120-073]any one but a Jew. There _are_ a few such,” she added, with a touch of

[120-074]scorn.

[120-075]Deronda had that objection to answer which we all have known in

[120-076]speaking to those who are too certain of their own fixed

[120-077]interpretations to be enlightened by anything we may say. But besides

[120-078]this, the point immediately in question was one on which he felt a

[120-079]repugnance either to deny or affirm. He remained silent, and she

[120-080]presently said,

[120-081]“You love her as your father loved me, and she draws you after her as I

[120-082]drew him.”

[120-083]Those words touched Deronda’s filial imagination, and some tenderness

[120-084]in his glance was taken by his mother as an assent. She went on with

[120-085]rising passion: “But I was leading him the other way. And now your

[120-086]grandfather is getting his revenge.”

[120-087]“Mother,” said Deronda, remonstrantly, “don’t let us think of it in

[120-088]that way. I will admit that there may come some benefit from the

[120-089]education you chose for me. I prefer cherishing the benefit with

[120-090]gratitude, to dwelling with resentment on the injury. I think it would

[120-091]have been right that I should have been brought up with the

[120-092]consciousness that I was a Jew, but it must always have been a good to

[120-093]me to have as wide an instruction and sympathy as possible. And now,

[120-094]you have restored me my inheritance—events have brought a fuller

[120-095]restitution than you could have made—you have been saved from robbing

[120-096]my people of my service and me of my duty: can you not bring your whole

[120-097]soul to consent to this?”

[120-098]Deronda paused in his pleading: his mother looked at him listeningly,

[120-099]as if the cadence of his voice were taking her ear, yet she shook her

[120-100]head slowly. He began again, even more urgently.

[120-101]“You have told me that you sought what you held the best for me: open

[120-102]your heart to relenting and love toward my grandfather, who sought what

[120-103]he held the best for you.”

[120-104]“Not for me, no,” she said, shaking her head with more absolute denial,

[120-105]and folding her arms tightly. “I tell you, he never thought of his

[120-106]daughter except as an instrument. Because I had wants outside his

[120-107]purpose, I was to be put in a frame and tortured. If that is the right

[120-108]law for the world, I will not say that I love it. If my acts were

[120-109]wrong—if it is God who is exacting from me that I should deliver up

[120-110]what I withheld—who is punishing me because I deceived my father and

[120-111]did not warn him that I should contradict his trust—well, I have told

[120-112]everything. I have done what I could. And _your_ soul consents. That is

[120-113]enough. I have after all been the instrument my father wanted.—‘I

[120-114]desire a grandson who shall have a true Jewish heart. Every Jew should

[120-115]rear his family as if he hoped that a Deliverer might spring from it.’”

[120-116]In uttering these last sentences the Princess narrowed her eyes, waved

[120-117]her head up and down, and spoke slowly with a new kind of chest-voice,

[120-118]as if she were quoting unwillingly.

[120-119]“Were those my grandfather’s words?” said Deronda.

[120-120]“Yes, yes; and you will find them written. I wanted to thwart him,”

[120-121]said the Princess, with a sudden outburst of the passion she had shown

[120-122]in the former interview. Then she added more slowly, “You would have me

[120-123]love what I have hated from the time I was so high”—here she held her

[120-124]left hand a yard from the floor.—“That can never be. But what does it

[120-125]matter? His yoke has been on me, whether I loved it or not. You are the

[120-126]grandson he wanted. You speak as men do—as if you felt yourself wise.

[120-127]What does it all mean?”

[120-128]Her tone was abrupt and scornful. Deronda, in his pained feeling, and

[120-129]under the solemn urgency of the moment, had to keep a clutching

[120-130]remembrance of their relationship, lest his words should become cruel.

[120-131]He began in a deep entreating tone:

[120-132]“Mother, don’t say that I feel myself wise. We are set in the midst of

[120-133]difficulties. I see no other way to get any clearness than by being

[120-134]truthful—not by keeping back facts which may—which should carry

[120-135][[duty|obligation]] within them—which should make the only guidance toward

[120-136]duty. No wonder if such facts come to reveal themselves in spite of

[120-137]concealments. The effects prepared by generations are likely to triumph

[120-138]over a contrivance which would bend them all to the satisfaction of

[120-139]self. Your will was strong, but my grandfather’s trust which you

[120-140]accepted and did not fulfill—what you call his yoke—is the expression

[120-141]of something stronger, with deeper, farther-spreading roots, knit into

[120-142]the foundations of sacredness for all men. You renounced me—you still

[120-143]banish me—as a son”—there was an involuntary movement of indignation

[120-144]in Deronda’s voice—“But that stronger Something has determined that I

[120-145]shall be all the more the grandson whom also you willed to annihilate.”

[120-146]His mother was watching him fixedly, and again her face gathered

[120-147]admiration. After a moment’s silence she said, in a low, persuasive

[120-148]tone,

[120-149]“Sit down again,” and he obeyed, placing himself beside her. She laid

[120-150]her hand on his shoulder and went on,

[120-151]“You rebuke me. Well—I am the loser. And you are angry because I

[120-152]banish you. What could you do for me but weary your own patience? Your

[120-153]mother is a shattered woman. My sense of life is little more than a

[120-154]sense of what was—except when the pain is present. You reproach me

[120-155]that I parted with you. I had joy enough without you then. Now you are

[120-156]come back to me, and I cannot make you a joy. Have you the cursing

[120-157]spirit of the Jew in you? Are you not able to forgive me? Shall you be

[120-158]glad to think that I am punished because I was not a Jewish mother to

[120-159]you?”

[120-160]“How can you ask me that?” said Deronda, remonstrantly. “Have I not

[120-161]besought you that I might now at least be a son to you? My grief is

[120-162]that you have declared me helpless to comfort you. I would give up much

[120-163]that is dear for the sake of soothing your anguish.”

[120-164]“You shall give up nothing,” said his mother, with the hurry of

[120-165]agitation. “You shall be happy. You shall let me think of you as happy.

[120-166]I shall have done you no harm. You have no reason to curse me. You

[120-167]shall feel for me as they feel for the dead whom they say prayers

[120-168]for—you shall long that I may be freed from all suffering—from all

[120-169]punishment. And I shall see you instead of always seeing your

[120-170]grandfather. Will any harm come to me because I broke his trust in the

[120-171]daylight after he was gone into darkness? I cannot tell:—if you think

[120-172]_Kaddish_ will help me—say it, say it. You will come between me and

[120-173]the dead. When I am in your mind, you will look as you do now—always

[120-174]as if you were a tender son—always—as if I had been a tender mother.”

[120-175]She seemed resolved that her agitation should not conquer her, but he

[120-176]felt her hand trembling on his shoulder. Deep, deep [[sympathy|compassion]] hemmed

[120-177]in all words. With a face of beseeching he put his arm around her and

[120-178]pressed her head tenderly under his. They sat so for some moments. Then

[120-179]she lifted her head again and rose from her seat with a great sigh, as

[120-180]if in that breath she were dismissing a weight of thoughts. Deronda,

[120-181]standing in front of her, felt that the parting was near. But one of

[120-182]her swift alternations had come upon his mother.
