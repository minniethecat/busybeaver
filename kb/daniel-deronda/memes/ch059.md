[059-001]“Oh, poor creature! How very unpleasant for him!” said [[Gwendolen-Harleth|Gwendolen]],

[059-002]speaking from the lip, and not meaning any sarcasm. “I wonder if it has

[059-003]left off raining!” she added, rising and going to look out of the

[059-004]window.

[059-005]Happily it did not rain the next day, and Gwendolen rode to [[diplow-hall|Diplow]] on

[059-006]Criterion as she had done on that former day when she returned with her

[059-007]mother in the carriage. She always felt the more daring for being in

[059-008]her riding-dress; besides having the agreeable belief that she looked

[059-009]as well as possible in it—a sustaining consciousness in any meeting

[059-010]which seems formidable. Her anger toward [[Daniel-Deronda|Deronda]] had changed into a

[059-011]superstitious dread—due, perhaps, to the coercion he had exercised

[059-012]over her thought—lest the first interference of his in her life might

[059-013]foreshadow some future influence. It is of such stuff that

[059-014]superstitions are commonly made: an intense feeling about ourselves

[059-015]which makes the evening star shine at us with a threat, and the

[059-016]blessing of a beggar encourage us. And superstitions carry consequences

[059-017]which often verify their hope or their foreboding.

[059-018]The time before luncheon was taken up for Gwendolen by going over the

[059-019]rooms with Mrs. Torrington and [[Mrs-Davilow|Mrs. Davilow]]; and she thought it likely

[059-020]that if she saw Deronda, there would hardly be need for more than a bow

[059-021]between them. She meant to notice him as little as possible.

[059-022]And after all she found herself under an inward compulsion too strong

[059-023]for her pride. From the first moment of their being in the room

[059-024]together, she seemed to herself to be doing nothing but notice him;

[059-025]everything else was automatic [[art|performance]] of an habitual part.

[059-026]When he took his place at lunch, [[Henleigh-Grandcourt|Grandcourt]] had said, “Deronda, Miss

[059-027]Harleth tells me you were not introduced to her at [[Leubronn]]?”

[059-028]“Miss Harleth hardly remembers me, I imagine,” said Deronda, looking at

[059-029]her quite simply, as they bowed. “She was intensely occupied when I saw

[059-030]her.”

[059-031]Now, did he suppose that she had not suspected him of being the person

[059-032]who redeemed her necklace?

[059-033]“On the contrary. I remember you very well,” said Gwendolen, feeling

[059-034]rather nervous, but governing herself and looking at him in return with

[059-035]new examination. “You did not approve of my playing at [[gambling|roulette]].”

[059-036]“How did you come to that conclusion?” said Deronda, gravely.

[059-037]“Oh, you cast an evil eye on my play,” said Gwendolen, with a turn of

[059-038]her head and a smile. “I began to lose as soon as you came to look on.

[059-039]I had always been winning till then.”

[059-040]“Roulette in such a kennel as Leubronn is a horrid bore,” said

[059-041]Grandcourt.

[059-042]“_I_ found it a bore when I began to lose,” said Gwendolen. Her face

[059-043]was turned toward Grandcourt as she smiled and spoke, but she gave a

[059-044]sidelong glance at Deronda, and saw his eyes fixed on her with a look

[059-045]so gravely penetrating that it had a keener edge for her than his

[059-046]ironical smile at her losses—a keener edge than [[Herr-Klesmer|Klesmer]]’s judgment.

[059-047]She wheeled her neck round as if she wanted to listen to what was being

[059-048]said by the rest, while she was only thinking of Deronda. His face had

[059-049]that disturbing kind of form and expression which threatens to affect

[059-050]opinion—as if one’s standard was somehow wrong. (Who has not seen men

[059-051]with faces of this corrective power till they frustrated it by speech

[059-052]or action?) His voice, heard now for the first time, was to

[059-053]Grandcourt’s toneless drawl, which had been in her ears every day, as

[059-054]the deep notes of a violoncello to the broken discourse of poultry and

[059-055]other lazy gentry in the afternoon sunshine. Grandcourt, she inwardly

[059-056]conjectured, was perhaps right in saying that Deronda thought too much

[059-057]of himself:—a favorite way of explaining a superiority that

[059-058]humiliates. However the talk turned on the rinderpest and Jamaica, and

[059-059]no more was said about roulette. Grandcourt held that the Jamaica negro

[059-060]was a beastly sort of baptist Caliban; Deronda said he had always felt

[059-061]a little with Caliban, who naturally had his own point of view and

[059-062]could sing a good song; Mrs. Davilow observed that her father had an

[059-063]estate in Barbadoes, but that she herself had never been in the West

[059-064]Indies; Mrs. Torrington was sure she should never sleep in her bed if

[059-065]she lived among blacks; her husband corrected her by saying that the

[059-066]blacks would be manageable enough if it were not for the half-breeds;

[059-067]and Deronda remarked that the whites had to thank themselves for the

[059-068]half-breeds.

[059-069]While this polite pea-shooting was going on, Gwendolen trifled with her

[059-070]jelly, and looked at every speaker in turn that she might feel at ease

[059-071]in looking at Deronda.

[059-072]“I wonder what he thinks of me, really? He must have felt interested in

[059-073]me, else he would not have sent me my necklace. I wonder what he thinks

[059-074]of my marriage? What notions has he to make him so grave about things?

[059-075]Why is he come to Diplow?”

[059-076]These questions ran in her mind as the voice of an uneasy longing to be

[059-077]judged by Deronda with unmixed admiration—a longing which had had its

[059-078]seed in her first resentment at his critical glance. Why did she care

[059-079]so much about the opinion of this man who was “nothing of any

[059-080]consequence”? She had no time to find the reason—she was too much

[059-081]engaged in caring. In the drawing-room, when something had called

[059-082]Grandcourt away, she went quite unpremeditatedly up to Deronda, who was

[059-083]standing at a table apart, turning over some prints, and said to him,

[059-084]“Shall you hunt to-morrow, Mr. Deronda?”

[059-085]“Yes, I believe so.”

[059-086]“You don’t object to hunting, then?”

[059-087]“I find excuses for it. It is a sin I am inclined to—when I can’t get

[059-088]boating or cricketing.”

[059-089]“Do you object to my hunting?” said Gwendolen, with a saucy movement of

[059-090]the chin.

[059-091]“I have no right to object to anything you choose to do.”

[059-092]“You thought you had a right to object to my gambling,” persisted

[059-093]Gwendolen.

[059-094]“I was sorry for it. I am not aware that I told you of my objection,”

[059-095]said Deronda, with his usual directness of gaze—a large-eyed gravity,

[059-096]innocent of any intention. His eyes had a peculiarity which has drawn

[059-097]many men into trouble; they were of a dark yet mild intensity which

[059-098]seemed to express a special interest in every one on whom he fixed

[059-099]them, and might easily help to bring on him those claims which ardently

[059-100]sympathetic people are often creating in the minds of those who need

[059-101]help. In mendicant fashion we make the goodness of others a reason for

[059-102]exorbitant demands on them. That sort of effect was penetrating

[059-103]Gwendolen.

[059-104]“You hindered me from gambling again,” she answered. But she had no

[059-105]sooner spoken than she blushed over face and neck; and Deronda blushed,

[059-106]too, conscious that in the little affair of the necklace he had taken a

[059-107]questionable freedom.

[059-108]It was impossible to speak further; and she turned away to a window,

[059-109]feeling that she had stupidly said what she had not meant to say, and

[059-110]yet being rather happy that she had plunged into this mutual

[059-111]understanding. Deronda also did not dislike it. Gwendolen seemed more

[059-112]decidedly attractive than before; and certainly there had been changes

[059-113]going on within her since that time at Leubronn: the struggle of mind

[059-114]attending a conscious error had wakened something like a new soul,

[059-115]which had better, but also worse, possibilities than her former poise

[059-116]of crude self-confidence: among the forces she had come to dread was

[059-117]something within her that troubled satisfaction.

[059-118]That evening Mrs. Davilow said, “Was it really so, or only a joke of

[059-119]yours, about Mr. Deronda’s spoiling your play, Gwen?”

[059-120]Her curiosity had been excited, and she could venture to ask a question

[059-121]that did not concern Mr. Grandcourt.

[059-122]“Oh, it merely happened that he was looking on when I began to lose,”

[059-123]said Gwendolen, carelessly. “I noticed him.”

[059-124]“I don’t wonder at that: he is a striking young man. He puts me in mind

[059-125]of Italian paintings. One would guess, without being told, that there

[059-126]was foreign blood in his veins.”

[059-127]“Is there?” said Gwendolen.

[059-128]“Mrs. Torrington says so. I asked particularly who he was, and she told

[059-129]me that his mother was some foreigner of high rank.”

[059-130]“His mother?” said Gwendolen, rather sharply. “Then who was his father?”

[059-131]“Well—every one says he is the son of [[Sir-Hugo-Mallinger|Sir Hugo Mallinger]], who brought

[059-132]him up; though he passes for a ward. She says, if Sir Hugo Mallinger

[059-133]could have done as he liked with his estates, he would have left them

[059-134]to this Mr. Deronda, since he has no legitimate son.”

[059-135]Gwendolen was silent; but her mother observed so marked an effect in

[059-136]her face that she was angry with herself for having repeated Mrs.

[059-137]Torrington’s gossip. It seemed, on reflection, unsuited to the ear of

[059-138]her daughter, for whom Mrs. Davilow disliked what is called knowledge

[059-139]of the world; and indeed she wished that she herself had not had any of

[059-140]it thrust upon her.

[059-141]An image which had immediately arisen in Gwendolen’s mind was that of

[059-142]the unknown mother—no doubt a dark-eyed woman—probably sad. Hardly

[059-143]any face could be less like Deronda’s than that represented as Sir

[059-144]Hugo’s in a crayon portrait at Diplow. A dark-eyed woman, no longer

[059-145]young, had become “stuff o’ the conscience” to Gwendolen.

[059-146]That night when she had got into her little bed, and only a dim light

[059-147]was burning, she said,

[059-148]“Mamma, have men generally children before they are married?”

[059-149]“No, dear, no,” said Mrs. Davilow. “Why do you ask such a question?”

[059-150](But she began to think that she saw the why.)

[059-151]“If it were so, I ought to know,” said Gwendolen, with some indignation.

[059-152]“You are thinking of what I said about Mr. Deronda and Sir Hugo

[059-153]Mallinger. That is a very unusual case, dear.”

[059-154]“Does Lady Mallinger know?”

[059-155]“She knows enough to satisfy her. That is quite clear, because Mr.

[059-156]Deronda has lived with them.”

[059-157]“And people think no worse of him?”

[059-158]“Well, of course he is under some disadvantage: it is not as if he were

[059-159]Lady Mallinger’s son. He does not inherit the property, and he is not

[059-160]of any consequence in the world. But people are not obliged to know

[059-161]anything about his birth; you see, he is very well received.”

[059-162]“I wonder whether he knows about it; and whether he is angry with his

[059-163]father?”

[059-164]“My dear child, why should you think of that?”

[059-165]“Why?” said Gwendolen, impetuously, sitting up in her bed. “Haven’t

[059-166]children reason to be angry with their parents? How can they help their

[059-167]parents marrying or not marrying?”

[059-168]But a consciousness rushed upon her, which made her fall back again on

[059-169]her pillow. It was not only what she would have felt months

[059-170]before—that she might seem to be reproaching her mother for that

[059-171]second marriage of hers; what she chiefly felt now was that she had

[059-172]been led on to a condemnation which seemed to make her own marriage a

[059-173]forbidden thing.

[059-174]There was no further talk, and till sleep came over her Gwendolen lay

[059-175]struggling with the reasons against that marriage—reasons which

[059-176]pressed upon her newly now that they were unexpectedly mirrored in the

[059-177]story of a man whose slight relations with her had, by some hidden

[059-178]affinity, bitten themselves into the most permanent layers of feeling.

[059-179]It was characteristic that, with all her debating, she was never

[059-180]troubled by the question whether the indefensibleness of her marriage

[059-181]did not include the fact that she had accepted Grandcourt solely as a

[059-182]man whom it was convenient for her to marry, not in the least as one to

[059-183]whom she would be binding herself in duty. Gwendolen’s ideas were

[059-184]pitiably crude; but many grand difficulties of life are apt to force

[059-185]themselves on us in our crudity. And to judge wisely, I suppose we must

[059-186]know how things appear to the unwise; that kind of appearance making

[059-187]the larger part of the world’s history.
