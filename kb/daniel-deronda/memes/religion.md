# Religion

Religion in *[[Daniel-Deronda|Daniel Deronda]]* is unique in Victorian fiction: not a matter of doctrine or clerical satire, but of inherited identity and living tradition. George Eliot transforms the novel's religious dimension from Protestant moralism into Jewish particularity — a radical move that redefines what the Victorian novel can do.

## Overview

Eliot's approach to religion reflects her own intellectual journey from Evangelical Christianity through skepticism to a humanistic reverence for tradition. In the novel, religion appears primarily through [[judaism|Judaism]] — not as a set of beliefs but as "a national religion" (005-113), a culture, a history, and a [[fate|destiny]]. The Christian [[reputation|character]]s, by contrast, treat religion as social form (the Arrowpoints' conventional Anglicanism) or as irrelevant ([[Henleigh-Grandcourt|Grandcourt]], [[Gwendolen-Harleth|Gwendolen]]). This asymmetry is deliberate: Eliot argues that only a living religious tradition, embodied in daily practice and inherited [[duty|obligation]], can provide the moral foundation that abstract principle cannot.

## Role in the Novel

Religion [[ambition|drive]]s the Jewish plot — the novel's most distinctive and controversial element. [[Mirah-Lapidoth|Mirah]]'s observance of "her religion" (038-102), [[Mordecai]]'s prophetic vision, and [[Daniel-Deronda|Deronda]]'s discovery of his Jewish [[inheritance|heritage]] all insist that religion is not a private preference but a collective inheritance. The Meyrick family's gentle curiosity about [[Mirah-Lapidoth|Mirah]]'s [[judaism|Judaism]] and the Cohens' lived religious culture provide the novel's most detailed portrait of religion as daily life.

The English [[reputation|character]]s inhabit a post-religious world. [[Gwendolen-Harleth|Gwendolen]] has no religious education beyond the [[reputation|name]] (011-143); [[Henleigh-Grandcourt|Grandcourt]] treats the church as a venue for weddings. Only [[Catherine-Arrowpoint|Catherine Arrowpoint]], who chooses [[Herr-Klesmer|Klesmer]] over her parents' religious objections, approaches religion with the seriousness that the Jewish [[reputation|character]]s embody — and even she is choosing against religion in the [[reputation|name]] of art.

## Expression Through Characters

### Mirah Lapidoth
[[Mirah-Lapidoth|Mirah]] practices her religion as naturally as she breathes. Her observance is "too tender a subject" (065-051) for casual discussion; it is her inheritance and her identity. Her religion gives her the moral clarity that [[Gwendolen-Harleth|Gwendolen]], adrift in a secularized culture, profoundly lacks.

### Mordecai
[[Mordecai]] is the novel's religious visionary — a figure drawn from the Hebrew prophets rather than from the Anglican clergy. His religion is national, historical, and demanding: it requires not belief alone but action, the restoration of a people to their land.

### Daniel Deronda
[[Daniel-Deronda|Deronda]]'s discovery of his Jewish identity is the novel's central religious event. Raised without religion, he comes to it through inheritance rather than conversion — the religion he "finds" has been his all along. Eliot presents this as the deepest form of religious experience: not choice but recognition.

## Key Passages

> She was a Jewess and observed her religion. (038-102)

> I looked at a national religion by daylight, and saw it in its true relation to human need. (005-113)

> The gentle mind's religion; the liberal, universal sympathy. (012-013)

## Historical Context

Eliot began studying Hebrew in the 1860s and was deeply influenced by the Jewish historian Heinrich Graetz. Her portrayal of [[judaism|Judaism]] — as a living national religion rather than a superseded precursor to Christianity — was unprecedented in English fiction and provoked significant controversy. The novel appeared just as the Eastern Question and Disraeli's Jewish [[inheritance|heritage]] were making Jewish identity a topic of public debate. Eliot's attempt to make [[judaism|Judaism]] central to a major English novel was both an artistic gamble and a moral statement.
