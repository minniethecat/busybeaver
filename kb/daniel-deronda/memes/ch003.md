[003-001]Instead of going to bed she made as brilliant a light as she could and

[003-002]began to pack, working diligently, though all the while visited by the

[003-003]scenes that might take place on the coming day—now by the tiresome

[003-004]explanations and farewells, and the whirling journey toward a changed

[003-005]home, now by the alternative of staying just another day and standing

[003-006]again at the [[gambling|roulette]]-table. But always in this latter scene there was

[003-007]the presence of that [[Daniel-Deronda|Deronda]], watching her with exasperating irony,

[003-008]and—the two keen experiences were inevitably revived

[003-009]together—beholding her again forsaken by luck. This importunate image

[003-010]certainly helped to sway her resolve on the side of immediate

[003-011]departure, and to urge her packing to the point which would make a

[003-012]change of mind inconvenient. It had struck twelve when she came into

[003-013]her room, and by the time she was assuring herself that she had left

[003-014]out only what was necessary, the faint dawn was stealing through the

[003-015]white blinds and dulling her candles. What was the use of going to bed?

[003-016]Her cold bath was refreshment enough, and she saw that a slight trace

[003-017]of fatigue about the eyes only made her look the more interesting.

[003-018]Before six o’clock she was completely equipped in her gray traveling

[003-019]dress even to her felt hat, for she meant to walk out as soon as she

[003-020]could count on seeing other ladies on their way to the springs. And

[003-021]happening to be seated sideways before the long strip of mirror between

[003-022]her two windows she turned to look at herself, leaning her elbow on the

[003-023]back of the chair in an attitude that might have been chosen for her

[003-024]portrait. It is possible to have a strong self-love without any

[003-025]self-satisfaction, rather with a self-discontent which is the more

[003-026]intense because one’s own little core of egoistic sensibility is a

[003-027]supreme care; but [[Gwendolen-Harleth|Gwendolen]] knew nothing of such inward strife. She had

[003-028]a _naïve_ delight in her fortunate self, which any but the harshest

[003-029]saintliness will have some indulgence for in a girl who had every day

[003-030]seen a pleasant reflection of that self in her friends’ flattery as

[003-031]well as in the looking-glass. And even in this beginning of troubles,

[003-032]while for lack of anything else to do she sat gazing at her image in

[003-033]the growing light, her face gathered a complacency gradual as the

[003-034]cheerfulness of the morning. Her beautiful lips curled into a more and

[003-035]more decided smile, till at last she took off her hat, leaned forward

[003-036]and kissed the cold glass which had looked so warm. How could she

[003-037]believe in sorrow? If it attacked her, she felt the force to crush it,

[003-038]to defy it, or run away from it, as she had done already. Anything

[003-039]seemed more possible than that she could go on bearing miseries, great

[003-040]or small.

[003-041]Madame von Langen never went out before breakfast, so that Gwendolen

[003-042]could safely end her early walk by taking her way homeward through the

[003-043]Obere Strasse in which was the needed shop, sure to be open after

[003-044]seven. At that hour any observers whom she minded would be either on

[003-045]their walks in the region of the springs, or would be still in their

[003-046]bedrooms; but certainly there was one grand hotel, the _[[czarina-hotel|Czarina]]_ from

[003-047]which eyes might follow her up to Mr. Wiener’s door. This was a chance

[003-048]to be risked: might she not be going in to buy something which had

[003-049]struck her fancy? This implicit falsehood passed through her mind as

[003-050]she remembered that the _Czarina_ was Deronda’s hotel; but she was then

[003-051]already far up the Obere Strasse, and she walked on with her usual

[003-052]floating movement, every line in her figure and drapery falling in

[003-053]gentle curves attractive to all eyes except those which discerned in

[003-054]them too close a resemblance to the serpent, and objected to the

[003-055]revival of serpent-worship. She looked neither to the right hand nor to

[003-056]the left, and transacted her business in the shop with a coolness which

[003-057]gave little Mr. Wiener nothing to remark except her proud grace of

[003-058]manner, and the superior size and quality of the three central

[003-059]turquoises in the necklace she offered him. They had belonged to a

[003-060]chain once her father’s: but she had never known her father; and the

[003-061]necklace was in all respects the ornament she could most conveniently

[003-062]part with. Who supposes that it is an impossible contradiction to be

[003-063]superstitious and rationalizing at the same time? Roulette encourages a

[003-064]romantic superstition as to the chances of the game, and the most

[003-065]prosaic rationalism as to human sentiments which stand in the way of

[003-066]raising needful money. Gwendolen’s dominant regret was that after all

[003-067]she had only nine louis to add to the four in her purse: these Jew

[003-068]dealers were so unscrupulous in taking advantage of Christians

[003-069]unfortunate at play! But she was the Langens’ guest in their hired

[003-070]apartment, and had nothing to pay there: thirteen louis would do more

[003-071]than take her home; even if she determined on risking three, the

[003-072]remaining ten would more than suffice, since she meant to travel right

[003-073]on, day and night. As she turned homeward, nay, entered and seated

[003-074]herself in the _salon_ to await her friends and breakfast, she still

[003-075]wavered as to her immediate departure, or rather she had concluded to

[003-076]tell the Langens simply that she had had a letter from her mamma

[003-077]desiring her return, and to leave it still undecided when she should

[003-078]start. It was already the usual breakfast-time, and hearing some one

[003-079]enter as she was leaning back rather tired and hungry with her eyes

[003-080]shut, she rose expecting to see one or other of the Langens—the words

[003-081]which might determine her lingering at least another day, ready-formed

[003-082]to pass her lips. But it was the servant bringing in a small packet for

[003-083]Miss Harleth, which had at that moment been left at the door. Gwendolen

[003-084]took it in her hand and immediately hurried into her own room. She

[003-085]looked paler and more agitated than when she had first read her mamma’s

[003-086]letter. Something—she never quite knew what—revealed to her before

[003-087]she opened the packet that it contained the necklace she had just

[003-088]parted with. Underneath the paper it was wrapped in a cambric

[003-089]handkerchief, and within this was a scrap of torn-off note-paper, on

[003-090]which was written with a pencil, in clear but rapid handwriting—“_A

[003-091]stranger who has found Miss Harleth’s necklace returns it to her with

[003-092]the hope that she will not again risk the loss of it._”

[003-093]Gwendolen reddened with the vexation of [[pride|wounded pride]]. A large corner

[003-094]of the handkerchief seemed to have been recklessly torn off to get rid

[003-095]of a mark; but she at once believed in the first image of “the

[003-096]stranger” that presented itself to her mind. It was Deronda; he must

[003-097]have seen her go into the shop; he must have gone in immediately after

[003-098]and repurchased the necklace. He had taken an unpardonable [[freedom|liberty]], and

[003-099]had dared to place her in a thoroughly hateful position. What could she

[003-100]do?—Not, assuredly, act on her conviction that it was he who had sent

[003-101]her the necklace and straightway send it back to him: that would be to

[003-102]face the possibility that she had been mistaken; nay, even if the

[003-103]“stranger” were he and no other, it would be something too gross for

[003-104]her to let him know that she had divined this, and to meet him again

[003-105]with that recognition in their minds. He knew very well that he was

[003-106]entangling her in helpless [[shame|humiliation]]: it was another way of smiling

[003-107]at her ironically, and taking the air of a supercilious mentor.

[003-108]Gwendolen felt the bitter tears of mortification rising and rolling

[003-109]down her cheeks. No one had ever before dared to treat her with irony

[003-110]and contempt. One thing was clear: she must carry out her resolution to

[003-111]quit this place at once; it was impossible for her to reappear in the

[003-112]public _salon_, still less stand at the gaming-table with the risk of

[003-113]seeing Deronda. Now came an importunate knock at the door: breakfast

[003-114]was ready. Gwendolen with a passionate movement thrust necklace,

[003-115]cambric, scrap of paper, and all into her _nécessaire_, pressed her

[003-116]handkerchief against her face, and after pausing a minute or two to

[003-117]summon back her proud self-control, went to join her friends. Such

[003-118]signs of tears and fatigue as were left seemed accordant enough with

[003-119]the account she at once gave of her having sat up to do her packing,

[003-120]instead of waiting for help from her friend’s maid. There was much

[003-121]protestation, as she had expected, against her traveling alone, but she

[003-122]persisted in refusing any arrangements for companionship. She would be

[003-123]put into the ladies’ compartment and go right on. She could rest

[003-124]exceedingly well in the train, and was afraid of nothing.

[003-125]In this way it happened that Gwendolen never reappeared at the

[003-126]roulette-table, but that Thursday evening left [[Leubronn]] for Brussels,

[003-127]and on Saturday morning arrived at [[Offendene]], the home to which she and

[003-128]her family were soon to say a last good-bye.

[003-129]CHAPTER III.

[003-130]“Let no flower of the spring pass by us; let us crown ourselves with

[003-131]rosebuds before they be withered.”—BOOK OF WISDOM.

[003-132]Pity that Offendene was not the home of Miss Harleth’s childhood, or

[003-133]endeared to her by family memories! A human life, I think, should be

[003-134]well rooted in some spot of a native land, where it may get the love of

[003-135]tender kinship for the face of earth, for the labors men go forth to,

[003-136]for the sounds and accents that haunt it, for whatever will give that

[003-137]early home a familiar unmistakable difference amid the future widening

[003-138]of knowledge: a spot where the definiteness of early memories may be

[003-139]inwrought with affection, and—kindly acquaintance with all neighbors,

[003-140]even to the dogs and donkeys, may spread not by sentimental effort and

[003-141]reflection, but as a sweet habit of the blood. At five years old,

[003-142]mortals are not prepared to be citizens of the world, to be stimulated

[003-143]by abstract nouns, to soar above preference into impartiality; and that

[003-144]prejudice in favor of milk with which we blindly begin, is a type of

[003-145]the way body and soul must get nourished at least for a time. The best

[003-146]introduction to astronomy is to think of the nightly heavens as a

[003-147]little lot of stars belonging to one’s own homestead.

[003-148]But this blessed persistence in which affection can take root had been

[003-149]wanting in Gwendolen’s life. It was only a year before her recall from

[003-150]Leubronn that Offendene had been chosen as her mamma’s home, simply for

[003-151]its nearness to [[pennicote-rectory|Pennicote Rectory]], and that [[Mrs-Davilow|Mrs. Davilow]], Gwendolen,

[003-152]and her four half-sisters (the governess and the maid following in

[003-153]another vehicle) had been driven along the avenue for the first time,

[003-154]on a late October afternoon when the rooks were crawing loudly above

[003-155]them, and the yellow elm-leaves were whirling.

[003-156]The season suited the aspect of the old oblong red-brick house, rather

[003-157]too anxiously ornamented with stone at every line, not excepting the

[003-158]double row of narrow windows and the large square portico. The stone

[003-159]encouraged a greenish lichen, the brick a powdery gray, so that though

[003-160]the building was rigidly rectangular there was no harshness in the

[003-161]physiognomy which it turned to the three avenues cut east, west and

[003-162]south in the hundred yards’ breadth of old plantation encircling the

[003-163]immediate grounds. One would have liked the house to have been lifted

[003-164]on a knoll, so as to look beyond its own little domain to the long

[003-165]thatched roofs of the distant villages, the church towers, the

[003-166]scattered homesteads, the gradual rise of surging woods, and the green

[003-167]breadths of undulating park which made the beautiful face of the earth

[003-168]in that part of [[wessex|Wessex]]. But though standing thus behind a screen amid

[003-169]flat pastures, it had on one side a glimpse of the wider world in the

[003-170]lofty curves of the chalk downs, grand steadfast forms played over by

[003-171]the changing days.
