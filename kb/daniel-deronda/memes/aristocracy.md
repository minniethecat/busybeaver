# aristocracy

**Aristocracy** in [[Daniel-Deronda|Daniel Deronda]] appears in two distinct registers: the traditional English landed gentry represented by characters such as Lord and Lady Brackenshaw, and Mordecai's radical redefinition of the [[Judaism|Jews]] as "the aristocracy of every land." The novel both participates in and subtly critiques the social world of the hereditary upper class.

## The English aristocracy

The novel depicts the English aristocracy through its country-house settings and social rituals. The Mallinger family at Diplow Hall and the Brackenshaws represent the established gentry. Sir Hugo Mallinger observes the practical value of the aristocracy in a democratic society:

> …as showing the value of an aristocracy in a free country like England; the blacksmith in the hamlet of Diplow felt that a good time had come round… ([016-148])

The archery contest at Brackenshaw Castle ([073-002]) exemplifies the aristocratic social calendar, where rank and [[class]] distinctions are both reinforced and momentarily suspended. The country-house gatherings, with their elaborate rituals of precedence and display, constitute the novel's most vivid portrait of landed society at play.

## Jews as aristocracy

Mordecai offers a striking counter-definition of aristocracy. Speaking to the working-class Cohen family, he declares:

> …the Jews are among the aristocracy of every land—if a literature is called rich in the possession of a few classic tragedies, what shall we say to a National Tragedy lasting for fifteen hundred years… ([093-108])

This claim inverts the conventional social hierarchy: true nobility is measured not by land ownership but by historical continuity and moral vocation. Mordecai's appeal to literary comparison — the "classic tragedies" against the "National Tragedy" — elevates Jewish suffering from mere misfortune to a form of collective distinction.

The irony is sharpened by the immediate setting: "Ezra Cohen was not clad in the other form of aristocracy" ([093-115]), a pawnbroker's shop in a poor quarter of London. Mordecai's vision separates the idea of aristocracy from its material trappings, relocating it in racial and spiritual inheritance.

## Social critique

Through the juxtaposition of English gentry and Jewish "aristocracy," Eliot implicitly questions whether birth and breeding are adequate foundations for social superiority. The novel's [[sympathy]] extends to characters across the class spectrum, from the highest-born to the most marginalized. By placing Mordecai's radical redefinition of aristocracy in the mouth of a consumptive scholar living in a pawnbroker's attic, the novel suggests that true distinction lies not in the accidents of birth but in the cultivation of moral vision.
