# Herr Klesmer

Herr Klesmer is the novel's embodiment of the artist as moral force — a German-Jewish composer whose absolute devotion to his art makes him the most honest judge of character in the novel. Eliot describes him as "a felicitous combination of the German, the Sclave, and the Semite" (008-063), a composite identity that marks him as irreducibly foreign and, in the novel's moral scheme, places him outside the corruptions of English social convention.

## Role in the Novel

Klesmer functions as a kind of artistic conscience — the one character in the novel whose judgments are never compromised by politeness or self-interest. He first appears at the Arrowpoints' musical gathering, where his "mane" of hair and intense expression announce him as a creature of a different order from the English gentry. "Woman was dear to him, but music was dearer" (009-007) — this is not mere aestheticism but a statement of moral priority.

His devastating verdict on Gwendolen's musical ambitions is the novel's first honest assessment of her character: she could at best become "a moderate musical amateur, nothing more" (009-018). Gwendolen has been told all her life that she is exceptional; Klesmer is the first person who refuses to flatter her. "You have not been well taught. You produce your notes badly" (009-009) — his honesty is brutal because it is true and it is true because his standards are absolute.

His courtship and marriage to Catherine Arrowpoint enrage her parents, who see him as a "needy musician" and a "foreigner, a Jew" (017-143). But the novel treats their union as the triumph of genuine vocation over class prejudice — a miniature of the cosmopolitan ideal that Deronda's Jewish plot ultimately endorses.

## Character Traits

### Personality

Klesmer is "excitable," "impetuous," and incapable of flattery. He insists that "a great musician is no more a mere musician than a great statesman is a mere politician" (043-089), and lives by this principle. His harshness is the rigour of genuine artistic standards; he is not cruel but truthful, and the novel treats truthfulness — even when painful — as a form of respect.

His artistic philosophy is articulated most fully in a speech that amounts to the novel's own aesthetic manifesto: art is not an accomplishment or a social ornament but a vocation demanding total commitment. "Genius at first is little more than a great capacity for receiving discipline" (009-011) — this is Eliot's own view, and Klesmer is its spokesman.

### Appearance

Klesmer is physically striking — "a felicitous combination of the German, the Sclave, and the Semite" (008-063) — with a "mane" of hair and an intensity of expression that marks him as an artist. His foreignness is essential to his role as an outsider whose judgments are untainted by English social conventions.

## Character Arc

Klesmer does not change — he is, like Grandcourt, a "finished" character, but in the opposite moral direction. His function is to be a fixed point of artistic integrity against which other characters' pretensions are measured. His marriage to Catherine, however, represents a plot development: it shows that the artist who refuses to compromise may still find love and recognition, and that the English class system's resistance to such unions can be overcome.

## Thematic Significance

Klesmer embodies the novel's argument that art is a vocation, not an accomplishment. His foreignness and Jewishness mark him as an outsider, but the novel treats his outsider status as a source of moral clarity — he sees English society from the outside and judges it accurately. He also represents the possibility of cross-cultural union: his marriage to Catherine is a model of the cosmopolitan relationship the novel values, a domestic parallel to Deronda's embrace of Jewish identity.

## Critical Reception

> Klesmer has been read as a self-portrait of sorts — Eliot's own commitment to artistic seriousness channelled through a male, Jewish, foreign character. His role as truth-teller connects him to a long tradition of artist-figures in fiction who see through society's pretensions. Some critics have noted that his Jewishness, while asserted, is treated more as cultural marker than lived identity. His famous pronouncement on the seriousness of art — "Genius at first is little more than a great capacity for receiving discipline" — has been quoted as Eliot's own artistic credo.
