[113-001]Many nights were watched through by him in gazing from the open window

[113-002]of his room on the double, faintly pierced darkness of the sea and the

[113-003]heavens; often in struggling under the oppressive skepticism which

[113-004]represented his particular lot, with all the importance he was allowing

[113-005][[Mordecai]] to give it, as of no more lasting effect than a dream—a set

[113-006]of changes which made passion to him, but beyond his consciousness were

[113-007]no more than an imperceptible difference of mass and shadow; sometimes

[113-008]with a reaction of emotive force which gave even to sustained

[113-009]disappointment, even to the fulfilled demand of sacrifice, the nature

[113-010]of a satisfied energy, and spread over his young future, whatever it

[113-011]might be, the attraction of devoted service; sometimes with a sweet

[113-012]irresistible hopefulness that the very best of human possibilities

[113-013]might befall him—the blending of a complete personal love in one

[113-014]current with a larger duty; and sometimes again in a mood of rebellion

[113-015](what human creature escapes it?) against things in general because

[113-016]they are thus and not otherwise, a mood in which [[Gwendolen-Harleth|Gwendolen]] and her

[113-017]equivocal fate moved as busy images of what was amiss in the world

[113-018]along with the concealments which he had felt as a hardship in his own

[113-019]life, and which were acting in him now under the form of an afflicting

[113-020]doubtfulness about the mother who had announced herself coldly and

[113-021]still kept away.

[113-022]But at last she was come. One morning in his third week of waiting

[113-023]there was a new kind of knock at the door. A servant in Chasseurs

[113-024]livery entered and delivered in French the verbal message that, the

[113-025][[Leonora-Halm-Eberstein|Princess Halm-Eberstein]] had arrived, that she was going to rest during

[113-026]the day, but would be obliged if Monsieur would dine early, so as to be

[113-027]at [[freedom|liberty]] at seven, when she would be able to receive him.

[113-028]CHAPTER LI.

[113-029]She held the spindle as she sat,

[113-030]Errina with the thick-coiled mat

[113-031]Of raven hair and deepest agate eyes,

[113-032]Gazing with a sad surprise

[113-033]At surging visions of her destiny—

[113-034]To spin the byssus drearily

[113-035]In insect-labor, while the throng

[113-036]Of gods and men wrought deeds that poets wrought in song.

[113-037]When [[Daniel-Deronda|Deronda]] presented himself at the door of his mother’s apartment in

[113-038]the _Italia_ he felt some revival of his boyhood with its premature

[113-039]agitations. The two servants in the antechamber looked at him markedly,

[113-040]a little surprised that the doctor their lady had come to consult was

[113-041]this striking young gentleman whose appearance gave even the severe

[113-042]lines of an evening dress the credit of adornment. But Deronda could

[113-043]notice nothing until, the second door being opened, he found himself in

[113-044]the presence of a figure which at the other end of the large room stood

[113-045]awaiting his approach.

[113-046]She was covered, except as to her face and part of her arms, with black

[113-047]lace hanging loosely from the summit of her whitening hair to the long

[113-048]train stretching from her tall figure. Her arms, naked to the elbow,

[113-049]except for some rich bracelets, were folded before her, and the fine

[113-050]poise of her head made it look handsomer than it really was. But

[113-051]Deronda felt no interval of observation before he was close in front of

[113-052]her, holding the hand she had put out and then raising it to his lips.

[113-053]She still kept her hand in his and looked at him examiningly; while his

[113-054]chief consciousness was that her eyes were piercing and her face so

[113-055]mobile that the next moment she might look like a different person. For

[113-056]even while she was examining him there was a play of the brow and

[113-057]nostril which made a tacit language. Deronda dared no movement, not

[113-058]able to conceive what sort of manifestation her feeling demanded; but

[113-059]he felt himself changing color like a girl, and yet wondering at his

[113-060]own lack of emotion; he had lived through so many ideal meetings with

[113-061]his mother, and they had seemed more real than this! He could not even

[113-062]conjecture in what language she would speak to him. He imagined it

[113-063]would not be English. Suddenly, she let fall his hand, and placed both

[113-064]hers on his shoulders, while her face gave out a flash of admiration in

[113-065]which every worn line disappeared and seemed to leave a restored youth.

[113-066]“You are a beautiful creature!” she said, in a low melodious voice,

[113-067]with syllables which had what might be called a foreign but agreeable

[113-068]outline. “I knew you would be.” Then she kissed him on each cheek, and

[113-069]he returned the kisses. But it was something like a greeting between

[113-070]royalties.

[113-071]She paused a moment while the lines were coming back into her face, and

[113-072]then said in a colder tone, “I am your mother. But you can have no love

[113-073]for me.”

[113-074]“I have thought of you more than of any other being in the world,” said

[113-075]Deronda, his voice trembling nervously.

[113-076]“I am not like what you thought I was,” said the mother decisively,

[113-077]withdrawing her hands from his shoulders, and folding her arms as

[113-078]before, looking at him as if she invited him to observe her. He had

[113-079]often pictured her face in his imagination as one which had a likeness

[113-080]to his own: he saw some of the likeness now, but amidst more striking

[113-081]differences. She was a remarkable looking being. What was it that gave

[113-082]her son a painful sense of aloofness?—Her worn beauty had a

[113-083]strangeness in it as if she were not quite a human mother, but a

[113-084]Melusina, who had ties with some world which is independent of ours.

[113-085]“I used to think that you might be suffering,” said Deronda, anxious

[113-086]above all not to wound her. “I used to wish that I could be a comfort

[113-087]to you.”

[113-088]“I _am_ suffering. But with a suffering that you can’t comfort,” said

[113-089]the Princess, in a harder voice than before, moving to a sofa where

[113-090]cushions had been carefully arranged for her. “Sit down.” She pointed

[113-091]to a seat near her; and then discerning some distress in Deronda’s

[113-092]face, she added, more gently, “I am not suffering at this moment. I am

[113-093]at ease now. I am able to talk.”

[113-094]Deronda seated himself and waited for her to speak again. It seemed as

[113-095]if he were in the presence of a mysterious Fate rather than of the

[113-096]longed-for mother. He was beginning to watch her with wonder, from the

[113-097]spiritual distance to which she had thrown him.

[113-098]“No,” she began: “I did not send for you to comfort me. I could not

[113-099]know beforehand—I don’t know now—what you will feel toward me. I have

[113-100]not the foolish notion that you can love me merely because I am your

[113-101]mother, when you have never seen or heard of me in all your life. But I

[113-102]thought I chose something better for you than being with me. I did not

[113-103]think I deprived you of anything worth having.”

[113-104]“You cannot wish me to believe that your affection would not have been

[113-105]worth having,” said Deronda, finding that she paused as if she expected

[113-106]him to make some answer.

[113-107]“I don’t mean to speak ill of myself,” said the princess, with proud

[113-108]impetuosity, “But I had not much affection to give you. I did not want

[113-109]affection. I had been stifled with it. I wanted to live out the life

[113-110]that was in me, and not to be hampered with other lives. You wonder

[113-111]what I was. I was no princess then.” She rose with a sudden movement,

[113-112]and stood as she had done before. Deronda immediately rose too; he felt

[113-113]breathless.

[113-114]“No princess in this tame life that I live in now. I was a great

[113-115]singer, and I acted as well as I sang. All the rest were poor beside

[113-116]me. Men followed me from one country to another. I was living a myriad

[113-117]lives in one. I did not want a child.”

[113-118]There was a passionate self-defence in her tone. She had cast all

[113-119]precedent out of her mind. Precedent had no excuse for her and she

[113-120]could only seek a justification in the intensest words she could find

[113-121]for her experience. She seemed to fling out the last words against some

[113-122]possible reproach in the mind of her son, who had to stand and hear

[113-123]them—clutching his coat-collar as if he were keeping himself above

[113-124]water by it, and feeling his blood in the sort of commotion that might

[113-125]have been excited if he had seen her going through some strange rite of

[113-126]a religion which gave a sacredness to crime. What else had she to tell

[113-127]him? She went on with the same intensity and a sort of pale

[113-128]illumination in her face.

[113-129]“I did not want to marry. I was forced into marrying your

[113-130]father—forced, I mean, by my father’s wishes and commands; and

[113-131]besides, it was my best way of getting some freedom. I could rule my

[113-132]husband, but not my father. I had a right to be free. I had a right to

[113-133]seek my freedom from a bondage that I hated.”

[113-134]She seated herself again, while there was that subtle movement in her

[113-135]eyes and closed lips which is like the suppressed continuation of

[113-136]speech. Deronda continued standing, and after a moment or two she

[113-137]looked up at him with a less defiant pleading as she said,

[113-138]“And the bondage I hated for myself I wanted to keep you from. What

[113-139]better could the most loving mother have done? I relieved you from the

[113-140]bondage of having been born a Jew.”

[113-141]“Then I _am_ a Jew?” Deronda burst out with a deep-voiced energy that

[113-142]made his mother shrink a little backward against her cushions. “My

[113-143]father was a Jew, and you are a Jewess?”

[113-144]“Yes, your father was my cousin,” said the mother, watching him with a

[113-145]change in her look, as if she saw something that she might have to be

[113-146]afraid of.

[113-147]“I am glad of it,” said Deronda, impetuously, in the veiled voice of

[113-148]passion. He could not have imagined beforehand how he would have come

[113-149]to say that which he had never hitherto admitted. He could not have

[113-150]dreamed that it would be in impulsive opposition to his mother. He was

[113-151]shaken by a mixed anger which no reflection could come soon enough to

[113-152]check, against this mother who it seemed had borne him unwillingly, had

[113-153]willingly made herself a stranger to him, and—perhaps—was now making

[113-154]herself known unwillingly. This last suspicion seemed to flash some

[113-155]explanation over her speech.

[113-156]But the mother was equally shaken by an anger differently mixed, and

[113-157]her frame was less equal to any repression. The shaking with her was

[113-158]visibly physical, and her eyes looked the larger for her pallid

[113-159]excitement as she said violently,

[113-160]“Why do you say you are glad? You are an English gentleman. I secured

[113-161]you that.”

[113-162]“You did not know what you secured me. How could you choose my

[113-163][[inheritance|birthright]] for me?” said Deronda, throwing himself sideways into his

[113-164]chair again, almost unconsciously, and leaning his arm over the back,

[113-165]while he looked away from his mother.

[113-166]He was fired with an intolerance that seemed foreign to him. But he was

[113-167]now trying hard to master himself and keep silence. A horror had swept

[113-168]in upon his anger lest he should say something too hard in this moment

[113-169]which made an epoch never to be recalled. There was a pause before his

[113-170]mother spoke again, and when she spoke her voice had become more firmly

[113-171]resistant in its finely varied tones:

[113-172]“I chose for you what I would have chosen for myself. How could I know

[113-173]that you would have the spirit of my father in you? How could I know

[113-174]that you would love what I hated?—if you really love to be a Jew.” The

[113-175]last words had such bitterness in them that any one overhearing might

[113-176]have supposed some hatred had arisen between the mother and son.
