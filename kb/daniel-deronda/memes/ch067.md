[067-001]“In what way are you not a good Jewess?” said [[Daniel-Deronda|Deronda]].

[067-002]“I am ignorant, and we never observed the laws, but lived among

[067-003]Christians just as they did. But I have heard my father laugh at the

[067-004]strictness of the Jews about their food and all customs, and their not

[067-005]liking Christians. I think my mother was strict; but she could never

[067-006]want me not to like those who are better to me than any of my own

[067-007]people I have ever known. I think I could obey in other things that she

[067-008]wished but not in that. It is so much easier to me to share in love

[067-009]than in hatred. I remember a play I read in German—since I have been

[067-010]here it has come into my mind—where the heroine says something like

[067-011]that.”

[067-012]“_Antigone_,” said Deronda.

[067-013]“Ah, you know it. But I do not believe that my mother would wish me not

[067-014]to love my best friends. She would be grateful to them.” Here [[Mirah-Lapidoth|Mirah]] had

[067-015]turned to Mrs. Meyrick, and with a sudden lighting up of her whole

[067-016]countenance, she said, “Oh, if we ever do meet and know each other as

[067-017]we are now, so that I could tell what would comfort her—I should be so

[067-018]full of blessedness my soul would know no want but to love her!”

[067-019]“God bless you, child!” said Mrs. Meyrick, the words escaping

[067-020]involuntarily from her motherly heart. But to relieve the strain of

[067-021]feeling she looked at Deronda and said, “It is curious that Mirah, who

[067-022]remembers her mother so well it is as if she saw her, cannot recall her

[067-023]brother the least bit—except the feeling of having been carried by him

[067-024]when she was tired, and of his being near her when she was in her

[067-025]mother’s lap. It must be that he was rarely at home. He was already

[067-026]grown up. It is a pity her brother should be quite a stranger to her.”

[067-027]“He is good; I feel sure [[Mordecai|Ezra]] is good,” said Mirah, eagerly. “He loved

[067-028]my mother—he would take care of her. I remember more of him than that.

[067-029]I remember my mother’s voice once calling, ‘Ezra!’ and then his

[067-030]answering from a distance ‘Mother!’”—Mirah had changed her voice a

[067-031]little in each of these words and had given them a loving

[067-032]intonation—“and then he came close to us. I feel sure he is good. I

[067-033]have always taken comfort from that.”

[067-034]It was impossible to answer this either with agreement or doubt. Mrs.

[067-035]Meyrick and Deronda exchanged a quick glance: about this brother she

[067-036]felt as painfully dubious as he did. But Mirah went on, absorbed in her

[067-037]memories,

[067-038]“Is it not wonderful how I remember the voices better than anything

[067-039]else? I think they must go deeper into us than other things. I have

[067-040]often fancied heaven might be made of voices.”

[067-041]“Like your [[music|singing]]—yes,” said Mab, who had hitherto kept a modest

[067-042]silence, and now spoke bashfully, as was her wont in the presence of

[067-043]Prince Camaralzaman—“Ma, do ask Mirah to sing. Mr. Deronda has not

[067-044]heard her.”

[067-045]“Would it be disagreeable to you to sing now?” said Deronda, with a

[067-046]more deferential gentleness than he had ever been conscious of before.

[067-047]“Oh, I shall like it,” said Mirah. “My voice has come back a little

[067-048]with rest.”

[067-049]Perhaps her ease of manner was due to something more than the

[067-050]simplicity of her nature. The circumstances of her life made her think

[067-051]of everything she did as work demanded from her, in which affectation

[067-052]had nothing to do; and she had begun her work before self-consciousness

[067-053]was born.

[067-054]She immediately rose and went to the piano—a somewhat worn instrument

[067-055]that seemed to get the better of its infirmities under the firm touch

[067-056]of her small fingers as she preluded. Deronda placed himself where he

[067-057]could see her while she sang; and she took everything as quietly as if

[067-058]she had been a child going to breakfast.

[067-059]Imagine her—it is always good to imagine a human creature in whom

[067-060]bodily loveliness seems as properly one with the entire being as the

[067-061]bodily loveliness of those wondrous transparent orbs of life that we

[067-062]find in the sea—imagine her with her dark hair brushed from her

[067-063]temples, but yet showing certain tiny rings there which had cunningly

[067-064]found their own way back, the mass of it hanging behind just to the

[067-065]nape of the little neck in curly fibres, such as renew themselves at

[067-066]their own will after being bathed into straightness like that of

[067-067]water-grasses. Then see the perfect cameo her profile makes, cut in a

[067-068]duskish shell, where by some happy fortune there pierced a gem-like

[067-069]darkness for the eye and eyebrow; the delicate nostrils defined enough

[067-070]to be ready for sensitive movements, the finished ear, the firm curves

[067-071]of the chin and neck, entering into the expression of a refinement

[067-072]which was not feebleness.

[067-073]She sang Beethoven’s “Per pietà non dirmi addio” with a subdued but

[067-074]searching pathos which had that essential of perfect singing, the

[067-075]making one oblivious of art or manner, and only possessing one with the

[067-076]song. It was the sort of voice that gives the impression of being meant

[067-077]like a bird’s wooing for an audience near and beloved. Deronda began by

[067-078]looking at her, but felt himself presently covering his eyes with his

[067-079]hand, wanting to seclude the melody in darkness; then he refrained from

[067-080]what might seem oddity, and was ready to meet the look of mute appeal

[067-081]which she turned toward him at the end.

[067-082]“I think I never enjoyed a song more than that,” he said, gratefully.

[067-083]“You like my singing? I am so glad,” she said, with a smile of delight.

[067-084]“It has been a great pain to me, because it failed in what it was

[067-085]wanted for. But now we think I can use it to get my bread. I have

[067-086]really been taught well. And now I have two pupils, that Miss Meyrick

[067-087]found for me. They pay me nearly two crowns for their two lessons.”

[067-088]“I think I know some ladies who would find you many pupils after

[067-089]Christmas,” said Deronda. “You would not mind singing before any one

[067-090]who wished to hear you?”

[067-091]“Oh no, I want to do something to get money. I could teach reading and

[067-092]speaking, Mrs. Meyrick thinks. But if no one would learn of me, that is

[067-093]difficult.” Mirah smiled with a touch of merriment he had not seen in

[067-094]her before. “I dare say I should find her poor—I mean my mother. I

[067-095]should want to get money for her. And I can not always live on charity;

[067-096]though”—here she turned so as to take all three of her companions in

[067-097]one glance—“it is the sweetest charity in all the world.”

[067-098]“I should think you can get rich,” said Deronda, smiling. “Great ladies

[067-099]will perhaps like you to teach their daughters. We shall see. But now

[067-100]do sing again to us.”

[067-101]She went on willingly, singing with ready memory various things by

[067-102]Gordigiani and [[schuberts-music|Schubert]]; then, when she had left the piano, Mab said,

[067-103]entreatingly, “Oh, Mirah, if you would not mind singing the little

[067-104]hymn.”

[067-105]“It is too childish,” said Mirah. “It is like lisping.”

[067-106]“What is the hymn?” said Deronda.

[067-107]“It is the Hebrew hymn she remembers her mother singing over her when

[067-108]she lay in her cot,” said Mrs. Meyrick.

[067-109]“I should like very much to hear it,” said Deronda, “if you think I am

[067-110]worthy to hear what is so sacred.”

[067-111]“I will sing it if you like,” said Mirah, “but I don’t sing real

[067-112]words—only here and there a syllable like hers—the rest is lisping.

[067-113]Do you know Hebrew? because if you do, my singing will seem childish

[067-114]nonsense.”

[067-115]Deronda shook his head. “It will be quite good Hebrew to me.”

[067-116]Mirah crossed her little feet and hands in her easiest attitude, and

[067-117]then lifted up her head at an angle which seemed to be directed to some

[067-118]invisible face bent over her, while she sang a little hymn of quaint

[067-119]melancholy intervals, with syllables that really seemed childish

[067-120]lisping to her audience; the voice in which she gave it forth had

[067-121]gathered even a sweeter, more cooing tenderness than was heard in her

[067-122]other songs.

[067-123]“If I were ever to know the real words, I should still go on in my old

[067-124]way with them,” said Mirah, when she had repeated the hymn several

[067-125]times.

[067-126]“Why not?” said Deronda. “The lisped syllables are very full of

[067-127]meaning.”

[067-128]“Yes, indeed,” said Mrs. Meyrick. “A mother hears something of a lisp

[067-129]in her children’s talk to the very last. Their words are not just what

[067-130]everybody else says, though they may be spelled the same. If I were to

[067-131]live till my [[Hans-Meyrick|Hans]] got old, I should still see the boy in him. A

[067-132]mother’s love, I often say, is like a tree that has got all the wood in

[067-133]it, from the very first it made.”

[067-134]“Is not that the way with friendship, too?” said Deronda, smiling. “We

[067-135]must not let the mothers be too arrogant.”

[067-136]The little woman shook her head over her darning.

[067-137]“It is easier to find an old mother than an old friend. Friendships

[067-138]begin with liking or gratitude—roots that can be pulled up. Mother’s

[067-139]love begins deeper down.”

[067-140]“Like what you were saying about the influence of voices,” said

[067-141]Deronda, looking at Mirah. “I don’t think your hymn would have had more

[067-142]expression for me if I had known the words. I went to the [[the-synagogue|synagogue]] at

[067-143]Frankfort before I came home, and the service impressed me just as much

[067-144]as if I had followed the words—perhaps more.”

[067-145]“Oh, was it great to you? Did it go to your heart?” said Mirah,

[067-146]eagerly. “I thought none but our people would feel that. I thought it

[067-147]was all shut away like a river in a deep valley, where only heaven

[067-148]saw—I mean—” she hesitated, feeling that she could not disentangle

[067-149]her thought from its imagery.

[067-150]“I understand,” said Deronda. “But there is not really such a

[067-151]separation—deeper down, as Mrs. Meyrick says. Our religion is chiefly

[067-152]a Hebrew religion; and since Jews are men, their religious feelings

[067-153]must have much in common with those of other men—just as their poetry,

[067-154]though in one sense peculiar, has a great deal in common with the

[067-155]poetry of other nations. Still it is to be expected that a Jew would

[067-156]feel the forms of his people’s religion more than one of another

[067-157]race—and yet”—here Deronda hesitated in his turn—“that is perhaps

[067-158]not always so.”

[067-159]“Ah no,” said Mirah, sadly. “I have seen that. I have seen them mock.

[067-160]Is it not like mocking your parents?—like rejoicing in your parents’

[067-161]shame?”

[067-162]“Some minds naturally rebel against whatever they were brought up in,

[067-163]and like the opposite; they see the faults in what is nearest to them,”

[067-164]said Deronda apologetically.

[067-165]“But you are not like that,” said Mirah, looking at him with

[067-166]unconscious fixedness.

[067-167]“No, I think not,” said Deronda; “but you know I was not brought up as

[067-168]a Jew.”

[067-169]“Ah, I am always forgetting,” said Mirah, with a look of disappointed

[067-170]recollection, and slightly blushing.

[067-171]Deronda also felt rather embarrassed, and there was an awkward pause,

[067-172]which he put an end to by saying playfully,

[067-173]“Whichever way we take it, we have to tolerate each other; for if we

[067-174]all went in opposition to our teaching, we must end in difference, just

[067-175]the same.”

[067-176]“To be sure. We should go on forever in zig-zags,” said Mrs. Meyrick.

[067-177]“I think it is very weak-minded to make your creed up by the rule of

[067-178]the contrary. Still one may honor one’s parents, without following

[067-179]their notions exactly, any more than the exact cut of their clothing.

[067-180]My father was a Scotch Calvinist and my mother was a French Calvinist;

[067-181]I am neither quite Scotch, nor quite French, nor two Calvinists rolled

[067-182]into one, yet I honor my parents’ memory.”
