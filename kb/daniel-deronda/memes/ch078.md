[078-001]But the coercion is often stronger on the one who takes the reverence.

[078-002]Those who trust us educate us. And perhaps in that ideal consecration

[078-003]of [[Gwendolen-Harleth|Gwendolen]]’s, some education was being prepared for [[Daniel-Deronda|Deronda]].

[078-004]CHAPTER XXXVI.

[078-005]“Rien ne pèse tant qu’un secret

[078-006]Le porter loin est difficile aux dames:

[078-007]Et je sçais mesme sur ce fait

[078-008]Bon nombre d’hommes qui sont femmes.”

[078-009]—LA FONTAINE.

[078-010]Meanwhile Deronda had been fastened and led off by Mr. Vandernoodt, who

[078-011]wished for a brisker walk, a cigar, and a little gossip. Since we

[078-012]cannot tell a man his own secrets, the restraint of being in his

[078-013]company often breeds a desire to pair off in conversation with some

[078-014]more ignorant person, and Mr. Vandernoodt presently said,

[078-015]“What a washed-out piece of cambric [[Henleigh-Grandcourt|Grandcourt]] is! But if he is a

[078-016]favorite of yours, I withdraw the remark.”

[078-017]“Not the least in the world,” said Deronda.

[078-018]“I thought not. One wonders how he came to have a great passion again;

[078-019]and he must have had—to marry in this way. Though [[Lush]], his old chum,

[078-020]hints that he married this girl out of obstinacy. By George! it was a

[078-021]very accountable obstinacy. A man might make up his mind to marry her

[078-022]without the stimulus of contradiction. But he must have made himself a

[078-023]pretty large drain of money, eh?”

[078-024]“I know nothing of his affairs.”

[078-025]“What! not of the other establishment he keeps up?”

[078-026]“[[diplow-hall|Diplow]]? Of course. He took that of [[Sir-Hugo-Mallinger|Sir Hugo]]. But merely for the year.”

[078-027]“No, no; not Diplow: [[gadsmere|Gadsmere]]. Sir Hugo knows, I’ll answer for it.”

[078-028]Deronda said nothing. He really began to feel some curiosity, but he

[078-029]foresaw that he should hear what Mr. Vandernoodt had to tell, without

[078-030]the condescension of asking.

[078-031]“Lush would not altogether own to it, of course. He’s a confident and

[078-032]go-between of Grandcourt’s. But I have it on the best authority. The

[078-033]fact is, there’s another lady with four children at Gadsmere. She has

[078-034]had the upper hand of him these ten years and more, and by what I can

[078-035]understand has it still—left her husband for him, and used to travel

[078-036]with him everywhere. Her husband’s dead now; I found a fellow who was

[078-037]in the same regiment with him, and knew this [[Lydia-Glasher|Mrs. Glasher]] before she

[078-038]took wing. A fiery dark-eyed woman—a noted beauty at that time—he

[078-039]thought she was dead. They say she has Grandcourt under her thumb

[078-040]still, and it’s a wonder he didn’t marry her, for there’s a very fine

[078-041]boy, and I understand Grandcourt can do absolutely as he pleases with

[078-042]the estates. Lush told me as much as that.”

[078-043]“What right had he to marry this girl?” said Deronda, with disgust.

[078-044]Mr. Vandernoodt, adjusting the end of his cigar, shrugged his shoulders

[078-045]and put out his lips.

[078-046]“_She_ can know nothing of it,” said Deronda, emphatically. But that

[078-047]positive statement was immediately followed by an inward query—“Could

[078-048]she have known anything of it?”

[078-049]“It’s rather a piquant picture,” said Mr. Vandernoodt—“Grandcourt

[078-050]between two fiery women. For depend upon it this light-haired one has

[078-051]plenty of devil in her. I formed that opinion of her at [[Leubronn]]. It’s

[078-052]a sort of Medea and Creüsa business. Fancy the two meeting! Grandcourt

[078-053]is a new kind of Jason: I wonder what sort of a part he’ll make of it.

[078-054]It’s a dog’s part at best. I think I hear Ristori now, saying, ‘Jasone!

[078-055]Jasone!’ These fine women generally get hold of a stick.”

[078-056]“Grandcourt can bite, I fancy,” said Deronda. “He is no stick.”

[078-057]“No, no; I meant Jason. I can’t quite make out Grandcourt. But he’s a

[078-058]keen fellow enough—uncommonly well built too. And if he comes into all

[078-059]this property, the estates will bear dividing. This girl, whose friends

[078-060]had come to beggary, I understand, may think herself lucky to get him.

[078-061]I don’t want to be hard on a man because he gets involved in an affair

[078-062]of that sort. But he might make himself more agreeable. I was telling

[078-063]him a capital story last night, and he got up and walked away in the

[078-064]middle. I felt inclined to kick him. Do you suppose that is inattention

[078-065]or insolence, now?”

[078-066]“Oh, a mixture. He generally observes the forms: but he doesn’t listen

[078-067]much,” said Deronda. Then, after a moment’s pause, he went on, “I

[078-068]should think there must be some exaggeration or inaccuracy in what you

[078-069]have heard about this lady at Gadsmere.”

[078-070]“Not a bit, depend upon it; it has all lain snug of late years. People

[078-071]have forgotten all about it. But there the nest is, and the birds are

[078-072]in it. And I know Grandcourt goes there. I have good evidence that he

[078-073]goes there. However, that’s nobody’s business but his own. The affair

[078-074]has sunk below the surface.”

[078-075]“I wonder you could have learned so much about it,” said Deronda,

[078-076]rather drily.

[078-077]“Oh, there are plenty of people who knew all about it; but such stories

[078-078]get packed away like old letters. They interest me. I like to know the

[078-079]manners of my time—contemporary gossip, not antediluvian. These

[078-080]Dryasdust fellows get a reputation by raking up some small scandal

[078-081]about Semiramis or Nitocris, and then we have a thousand and one poems

[078-082]written upon it by all the warblers big and little. But I don’t care a

[078-083]straw about the _faux pas_ of the mummies. You do, though. You are one

[078-084]of the historical men—more interested in a lady when she’s got a rag

[078-085]face and skeleton toes peeping out. Does that flatter your imagination?”

[078-086]“Well, if she had any woes in her love, one has the satisfaction of

[078-087]knowing that she’s well out of them.”

[078-088]“Ah, you are thinking of the Medea, I see.”

[078-089]Deronda then chose to point to some giant oaks worth looking at in

[078-090]their bareness. He also felt an interest in this piece of contemporary

[078-091]gossip, but he was satisfied that Mr. Vandernoodt had no more to tell

[078-092]about it.

[078-093]Since the early days when he tried to construct the hidden story of his

[078-094]own birth, his mind had perhaps never been so active in weaving

[078-095]probabilities about any private affair as it had now begun to be about

[078-096]Gwendolen’s marriage. This unavowed relation of Grandcourt’s—could she

[078-097]have gained some knowledge of it, which caused her to shrink from the

[078-098]match—a shrinking finally overcome by the urgence of poverty? He could

[078-099]recall almost every word she had said to him, and in certain of these

[078-100]words he seemed to discern that she was conscious of having done some

[078-101]wrong—inflicted some injury. His own acute experience made him alive

[078-102]to the form of injury which might affect the unavowed children and

[078-103]their mother. Was Mrs. Grandcourt, under all her determined show of

[078-104]satisfaction, gnawed by a double, a treble-headed grief—self-reproach,

[078-105]disappointment, jealousy? He dwelt especially on all the slight signs

[078-106]of self-reproach: he was inclined to judge her tenderly, to excuse, to

[078-107]pity. He thought he had found a key now by which to interpret her more

[078-108]clearly: what magnifying of her misery might not a young creature get

[078-109]into who had wedded her fresh hopes to old secrets! He thought he saw

[078-110]clearly enough now why Sir Hugo had never dropped any hint of this

[078-111]affair to him; and immediately the image of this Mrs. Glasher became

[078-112]painfully associated with his own hidden birth. Gwendolen knowing of

[078-113]that woman and her children, marrying Grandcourt, and showing herself

[078-114]contented, would have been among the most repulsive of beings to him;

[078-115]but Gwendolen tasting the bitterness of [[conscience|remorse]] for having contributed

[078-116]to their injury was brought very near to his [[sympathy|fellow-feeling]]. If it were

[078-117]so, she had got to a common plane of understanding with him on some

[078-118]difficulties of life which a woman is rarely able to judge of with any

[078-119]justice or generosity; for, according to precedent, Gwendolen’s view of

[078-120]her position might easily have been no other than that her husband’s

[078-121]marriage with her was his entrance on the path of virtue, while Mrs.

[078-122]Glasher represented his forsaken sin. And Deronda had naturally some

[078-123]resentment on behalf of the Hagars and Ishmaels.

[078-124]Undeniably Deronda’s growing solicitude about Gwendolen depended

[078-125]chiefly on her peculiar manner toward him; and I suppose neither man

[078-126]nor woman would be the better for an utter insensibility to such

[078-127]appeals. One sign that his interest in her had changed its footing was

[078-128]that he dismissed any caution against her being a coquette setting

[078-129]snares to involve him in a vulgar flirtation, and determined that he

[078-130]would not again evade any opportunity of talking to her. He had shaken

[078-131]off Mr. Vandernoodt, and got into a solitary corner in the twilight;

[078-132]but half an hour was long enough to think of those possibilities in

[078-133]Gwendolen’s position and state of mind; and on forming the

[078-134]determination not to avoid her, he remembered that she was likely to be

[078-135]at tea with the other ladies in the drawing-room. The conjecture was

[078-136]true; for Gwendolen, after resolving not to go down again for the next

[078-137]four hours, began to feel, at the end of one, that in shutting herself

[078-138]up she missed all chances of seeing and hearing, and that her visit

[078-139]would only last two days more. She adjusted herself, put on her little

[078-140]air of self-possession, and going down, made herself resolutely

[078-141]agreeable. Only ladies were assembled, and Lady Pentreath was amusing

[078-142]them with a description of a drawing-room under the Regency, and the

[078-143]figure that was cut by ladies and gentlemen in 1819, the year she was

[078-144]presented—when Deronda entered.

[078-145]“Shall I be acceptable?” he said. “Perhaps I had better go back and

[078-146]look for the others. I suppose they are in the billiard-room.”

[078-147]“No, no; stay where you are,” said Lady Pentreath. “They were all

[078-148]getting tired of me; let us hear what _you_ have to say.”

[078-149]“That is rather an embarrassing appeal,” said Deronda, drawing up a

[078-150]chair near Lady Mallinger’s elbow at the tea-table. “I think I had

[078-151]better take the opportunity of mentioning our songstress,” he added,

[078-152]looking at Lady Mallinger—“unless you have done so.”

[078-153]“Oh, the little Jewess!” said Lady Mallinger. “No, I have not mentioned

[078-154]her. It never entered my head that any one here wanted [[music|singing]] lessons.”

[078-155]“All ladies know some one else who wants singing lessons,” said

[078-156]Deronda. “I have happened to find an exquisite singer,”—here he turned

[078-157]to Lady Pentreath. “She is living with some ladies who are friends of

[078-158]mine—the mother and sisters of a man who was my chum at Cambridge. She

[078-159]was on the stage at Vienna; but she wants to leave that life, and

[078-160]maintain herself by teaching.”

[078-161]“There are swarms of those people, aren’t there?” said the old lady.

[078-162]“Are her lessons to be very cheap or very expensive? Those are the two

[078-163]baits I know of.”

[078-164]“There is another bait for those who hear her,” said Deronda. “Her

[078-165]singing is something quite exceptional, I think. She has had such

[078-166]first-rate teaching—or rather first-rate instinct with her

[078-167]teaching—that you might imagine her singing all came by nature.”

[078-168]“Why did she leave the stage, then?” said Lady Pentreath. “I’m too old

[078-169]to believe in first-rate people giving up first-rate chances.”

[078-170]“Her voice was too weak. It is a delicious voice for a room. You who

[078-171]put up with my singing of [[schuberts-music|Schubert]] would be enchanted with hers,” said

[078-172]Deronda, looking at Mrs. Raymond. “And I imagine she would not object

[078-173]to sing at private parties or concerts. Her voice is quite equal to

[078-174]that.”

[078-175]“I am to have her in my drawing-room when we go up to town,” said Lady

[078-176]Mallinger. “You shall hear her then. I have not heard her myself yet;

[078-177]but I trust Daniel’s recommendation. I mean my girls to have lessons of

[078-178]her.”

[078-179]“Is it a charitable affair?” said Lady Pentreath. “I can’t bear

[078-180]charitable [[art|music]].”

[078-181]Lady Mallinger, who was rather helpless in conversation, and felt

[078-182]herself under an engagement not to tell anything of [[Mirah-Lapidoth|Mirah]]’s story, had

[078-183]an embarrassed smile on her face, and glanced at Deronda.
