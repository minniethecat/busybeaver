[053-001]“That could make no difference to a man in his position,” said

[053-002][[Gwendolen-Harleth|Gwendolen]], rather contemptuously,

[053-003]“It would to some men,” said [[Mrs-Davilow|Mrs. Davilow]]. “They would not like to take

[053-004]a wife from a family in a state of beggary almost, as we are. Here we

[053-005]are at [[Offendene]] with a great shell over us, as usual. But just imagine

[053-006]his finding us at Sawyer’s Cottage. Most men are afraid of being bored

[053-007]or taxed by a wife’s family. If [[Henleigh-Grandcourt|Mr. Grandcourt]] did know, I think it a

[053-008]strong proof of his attachment to you.”

[053-009]Mrs. Davilow spoke with unusual emphasis: it was the first time she had

[053-010]ventured to say anything about Grandcourt which would necessarily seem

[053-011]intended as an argument in favor of him, her habitual impression being

[053-012]that such arguments would certainly be useless and might be worse. The

[053-013]effect of her words now was stronger than she could imagine: they

[053-014]raised a new set of possibilities in Gwendolen’s mind—a vision of what

[053-015]Grandcourt might do for her mother if she, Gwendolen, did—what she was

[053-016]not going to do. She was so moved by a new rush of ideas that, like one

[053-017]conscious of being urgently called away, she felt that the immediate

[053-018]task must be hastened: the letter must be written, else it might be

[053-019]endlessly deferred. After all, she acted in a hurry, as she had wished

[053-020]to do. To act in a hurry was to have a reason for keeping away from an

[053-021]absolute decision, and to leave open as many issues as possible.

[053-022]She wrote: “Miss Harleth presents her compliments to Mr. Grandcourt.

[053-023]She will be at home after two o’clock to-morrow.”

[053-024]Before addressing the note she said, “Pray ring the bell, mamma, if

[053-025]there is any one to answer it.” She really did not know who did the

[053-026]work of the house.

[053-027]It was not till after the letter had been taken away and Gwendolen had

[053-028]risen again, stretching out one arm and then resting it on her head,

[053-029]with a low moan which had a sound of relief in it, that Mrs. Davilow

[053-030]ventured to ask,

[053-031]“What did you say, Gwen?”

[053-032]“I said that I should be at home,” answered Gwendolen, rather loftily.

[053-033]Then after a pause, “You must not expect, because Mr. Grandcourt is

[053-034]coming, that anything is going to happen, mamma.”

[053-035]“I don’t allow myself to expect anything, dear. I desire you to follow

[053-036]your own feeling. You have never told me what that was.”

[053-037]“What is the use of telling?” said Gwendolen, hearing a reproach in

[053-038]that true statement. “When I have anything pleasant to tell, you may be

[053-039]sure I will tell you.”

[053-040]“But Mr. Grandcourt will consider that you have already accepted him,

[053-041]in allowing him to come. His note tells you plainly enough that he is

[053-042]coming to make you an offer.”

[053-043]“Very well; and I wish to have the pleasure of refusing him.”

[053-044]Mrs. Davilow looked up in wonderment, but Gwendolen implied her wish

[053-045]not to be questioned further by saying,

[053-046]“Put down that detestable needlework, and let us walk in the avenue. I

[053-047]am stifled.”

[053-048]CHAPTER XXVII.

[053-049]Desire has trimmed the sails, and Circumstance

[053-050]Brings but the breeze to fill them.

[053-051]While Grandcourt on his beautiful black Yarico, the groom behind him on

[053-052]Criterion, was taking the pleasant ride from [[diplow-hall|Diplow]] to Offendene,

[053-053]Gwendolen was seated before the mirror while her mother gathered up the

[053-054]lengthy mass of light-brown hair which she had been carefully brushing.

[053-055]“Only gather it up easily and make a coil, mamma,” said Gwendolen.

[053-056]“Let me bring you some earrings, Gwen,” said Mrs. Davilow, when the

[053-057]hair was adjusted, and they were both looking at the reflection in the

[053-058]glass. It was impossible for them not to notice that the eyes looked

[053-059]brighter than they had done of late, that there seemed to be a shadow

[053-060]lifted from the face, leaving all the lines once more in their placid

[053-061]youthfulness. The mother drew some inference that made her voice rather

[053-062]cheerful. “You do want your earrings?”

[053-063]“No, mamma; I shall not wear any ornaments, and I shall put on my black

[053-064]silk. Black is the only wear when one is going to refuse an offer,”

[053-065]said Gwendolen, with one of her old smiles at her mother, while she

[053-066]rose to throw off her dressing-gown.

[053-067]“Suppose the offer is not made after all,” said Mrs. Davilow, not

[053-068]without a sly intention.

[053-069]“Then that will be because I refuse it beforehand,” said Gwendolen. “It

[053-070]comes to the same thing.”

[053-071]There was a proud little toss of the head as she said this; and when

[053-072]she walked down-stairs in her long black robes, there was just that

[053-073]firm poise of head and elasticity of form which had lately been

[053-074]missing, as in a parched plant. Her mother thought, “She is quite

[053-075]herself again. It must be pleasure in his coming. Can her mind be

[053-076]really made up against him?”

[053-077]Gwendolen would have been rather angry if that thought had been

[053-078]uttered; perhaps all the more because through the last twenty hours,

[053-079]with a brief interruption of sleep, she had been so occupied with

[053-080]perpetually alternating images and arguments for and against the

[053-081]possibility of her marrying Grandcourt, that the conclusion which she

[053-082]had determined on beforehand ceased to have any hold on her

[053-083]consciousness: the alternate dip of counterbalancing thoughts begotten

[053-084]of counterbalancing desires had brought her into a state in which no

[053-085]conclusion could look fixed to her. She would have expressed her

[053-086]resolve as before; but it was a form out of which the blood had been

[053-087]sucked—no more a part of quivering life than the “God’s will be done”

[053-088]of one who is eagerly watching chances. She did not mean to accept

[053-089]Grandcourt; from the first moment of receiving his letter she had meant

[053-090]to refuse him; still, that could not but prompt her to look the

[053-091]unwelcome reasons full in the face until she had a little less awe of

[053-092]them, could not hinder her imagination from filling out her knowledge

[053-093]in various ways, some of which seemed to change the aspect of what she

[053-094]knew. By dint of looking at a dubious object with a constructive

[053-095]imagination, one can give it twenty different shapes. Her indistinct

[053-096]grounds of hesitation before the interview at the Whispering Stones, at

[053-097]present counted for nothing; they were all merged in the final

[053-098]repulsion. If it had not been for that day in Cardell Chase, she said

[053-099]to herself now, there would have been no obstacle to her marrying

[053-100]Grandcourt. On that day and after it, she had not reasoned and

[053-101]balanced; she had acted with a force of impulse against which all

[053-102]questioning was no more than a voice against a torrent. The impulse had

[053-103]come—not only from her maidenly pride and jealousy, not only from the

[053-104]shock of another woman’s calamity thrust close on her vision, but—from

[053-105]her dread of wrong-doing, which was vague, it was true, and aloof from

[053-106]the daily details of her life, but not the less strong. Whatever was

[053-107]accepted as consistent with being a lady she had no scruple about; but

[053-108]from the dim region of what was called disgraceful, wrong, guilty, she

[053-109]shrunk with mingled pride and terror; and even apart from shame, her

[053-110]feeling would have made her place any deliberate injury of another in

[053-111]the region of guilt.

[053-112]But now—did she know exactly what was the state of the case with

[053-113]regard to [[Lydia-Glasher|Mrs. Glasher]] and her children? She had given a sort of

[053-114]promise—had said, “I will not interfere with your wishes.” But would

[053-115]another woman who married Grandcourt be in fact the decisive obstacle

[053-116]to her wishes, or be doing her and her boy any real injury? Might it

[053-117]not be just as well, nay better, that Grandcourt should marry? For what

[053-118]could not a woman do when she was married, if she knew how to assert

[053-119]herself? Here all was constructive imagination. Gwendolen had about as

[053-120]accurate a conception of marriage—that is to say, of the mutual

[053-121]influences, demands, duties of man and woman in the state of

[053-122]matrimony—as she had of magnetic currents and the law of storms.

[053-123]“Mamma managed badly,” was her way of summing up what she had seen of

[053-124]her mother’s experience: she herself would manage quite differently.

[053-125]And the trials of matrimony were the last theme into which Mrs. Davilow

[053-126]could choose to enter fully with this daughter.

[053-127]“I wonder what mamma and my uncle would say if they knew about Mrs.

[053-128]Glasher!” thought Gwendolen in her inward debating; not that she could

[053-129]imagine herself telling them, even if she had not felt bound to

[053-130]silence. “I wonder what anybody would say; or what they would say to

[053-131]Mr. Grandcourt’s marrying some one else and having other children!” To

[053-132]consider what “anybody” would say, was to be released from the

[053-133]difficulty of judging where everything was obscure to her when feeling

[053-134]had ceased to be decisive. She had only to collect her memories, which

[053-135]proved to her that “anybody” regarded the illegitimate children as more

[053-136]rightfully to be looked shy on and deprived of social advantages than

[053-137]illegitimate fathers. The verdict of “anybody” seemed to be that she

[053-138]had no reason to concern herself greatly on behalf of Mrs. Glasher and

[053-139]her children.

[053-140]But there was another way in which they had caused her concern. What

[053-141]others might think could not do away with a feeling which in the first

[053-142]instance would hardly be too strongly described as indignation and

[053-143]loathing that she should have been expected to unite herself with an

[053-144]outworn life, full of backward secrets which must have been more keenly

[053-145]felt than any association with _her_. True, the question of love on her

[053-146]own part had occupied her scarcely at all in relation to Grandcourt.

[053-147]The desirability of marriage for her had always seemed due to other

[053-148]feeling than love; and to be enamored was the part of the man, on whom

[053-149]the advances depended. Gwendolen had found no objection to Grandcourt’s

[053-150]way of being enamored before she had had that glimpse of his past,

[053-151]which she resented as if it had been a deliberate offense against her.

[053-152]His advances to _her_ were deliberate, and she felt a retrospective

[053-153]disgust for them. Perhaps other men’s lives were of the same kind—full

[053-154]of secrets which made the ignorant suppositions of the women they

[053-155]wanted to marry a farce at which they were laughing in their sleeves.

[053-156]These feelings of disgust and indignation had sunk deep; and though

[053-157]other troublous experience in the last weeks had dulled them from

[053-158]passion into remembrance, it was chiefly their reverberating activity

[053-159]which kept her firm to the understanding with herself, that she was not

[053-160]going to accept Grandcourt. She had never meant to form a new

[053-161]determination; she had only been considering what might be thought or

[053-162]said. If anything could have induced her to change, it would have been

[053-163]the prospect of making all things easy for “poor mamma:” that, she

[053-164]admitted, was a temptation. But no! she was going to refuse him.

[053-165]Meanwhile, the thought that he was coming to be refused was

[053-166]inspiriting: she had the white reins in her hands again; there was a

[053-167]new current in her frame, reviving her from the beaten-down

[053-168]consciousness in which she had been left by the interview with [[Herr-Klesmer|Klesmer]].

[053-169]She was not now going to crave an opinion of her capabilities; she was

[053-170]going to exercise her power.

[053-171]Was this what made her heart palpitate annoyingly when she heard the

[053-172]horse’s footsteps on the gravel?—when Miss Merry, who opened the door

[053-173]to Grandcourt, came to tell her that he was in the drawing-room? The

[053-174]hours of preparation and the triumph of the situation were apparently

[053-175]of no use: she might as well have seen Grandcourt coming suddenly on

[053-176]her in the midst of her despondency. While walking into the

[053-177]drawing-room, she had to concentrate all her energy in that

[053-178]self-control, which made her appear gravely gracious—as she gave her

[053-179]hand to him, and answered his hope that she was quite well in a voice

[053-180]as low and languid as his own. A moment afterward, when they were both

[053-181]of them seated on two of the wreath-painted chairs—Gwendolen upright

[053-182]with downcast eyelids, Grandcourt about two yards distant, leaning one

[053-183]arm over the back of his chair and looking at her, while he held his

[053-184]hat in his left hand—any one seeing them as a picture would have

[053-185]concluded that they were in some stage of love-making suspense. And

[053-186]certainly the love-making had begun: she already felt herself being

[053-187]wooed by this silent man seated at an agreeable distance, with the

[053-188]subtlest atmosphere of attar of roses and an attention bent wholly on

[053-189]her. And he also considered himself to be wooing: he was not a man to

[053-190]suppose that his presence carried no consequences; and he was exactly

[053-191]the man to feel the utmost piquancy in a girl whom he had not found

[053-192]quite calculable.
