[124-001]“What decision have you come to?” he said, presently looking at her.

[124-002]“What orders shall I give?”

[124-003]“Oh, let us go,” said [[Gwendolen-Harleth|Gwendolen]]. The walls had begun to be an

[124-004]imprisonment, and while there was breath in this man he would have the

[124-005]mastery over her. His words had the power of thumb-screws and the cold

[124-006]touch of the rock. To resist was to act like a stupid animal unable to

[124-007]measure results.

[124-008]So the boat was ordered. She even went down to the quay again with him

[124-009]to see it before midday. [[Henleigh-Grandcourt|Grandcourt]] had recovered perfect quietude of

[124-010]temper, and had a scornful satisfaction in the attention given by the

[124-011]nautical groups to the _milord_, owner of the handsome yacht which had

[124-012]just put in for repairs, and who being an Englishman was naturally so

[124-013]at home on the sea that he could manage a sail with the same ease that

[124-014]he could manage a horse. The sort of exultation he had discerned in

[124-015]Gwendolen this morning she now thought that she discerned in him; and

[124-016]it was true that he had set his mind on this boating, and carried out

[124-017]his purpose as something that people might not expect him to do, with

[124-018]the gratified impulse of a strong will which had nothing better to

[124-019]exert itself upon. He had remarkable physical courage, and was proud of

[124-020]it—or rather he had a great contempt for the coarser, bulkier men who

[124-021]generally had less. Moreover, he was ruling that Gwendolen should go

[124-022]with him.

[124-023]And when they came down again at five o’clock, equipped for their

[124-024]boating, the scene was as good as a theatrical representation for all

[124-025]beholders. This handsome, fair-skinned English couple, manifesting the

[124-026]usual eccentricity of their nation, both of them proud, pale, and calm,

[124-027]without a smile on their faces, moving like creatures who were

[124-028]fulfilling a supernatural destiny—it was a thing to go out and see, a

[124-029]thing to paint. The husband’s chest, back, and arms, showed very well

[124-030]in his close-fitting dress, and the wife was declared to be a statue.

[124-031]Some suggestions were proffered concerning a possible change in the

[124-032]breeze, and the necessary care in putting about, but Grandcourt’s

[124-033]manner made the speakers understand that they were too officious, and

[124-034]that he knew better than they.

[124-035]Gwendolen, keeping her impassable air, as they moved away from the

[124-036]strand, felt her imagination obstinately at work. She was not afraid of

[124-037]any outward dangers—she was afraid of her own wishes which were taking

[124-038]shapes possible and impossible, like a cloud of demon-faces. She was

[124-039]afraid of her own hatred, which under the cold iron touch that had

[124-040]compelled her to-day had gathered a fierce intensity. As she sat

[124-041]guiding the tiller under her husband’s eyes, doing just what he told

[124-042]her, the strife within her seemed like her own effort to escape from

[124-043]herself. She clung to the thought of [[Daniel-Deronda|Deronda]]: she persuaded herself

[124-044]that he would not go away while she was there—he knew that she needed

[124-045]help. The sense that he was there would save her from acting out the

[124-046]evil within. And yet quick, quick, came images, plans of evil that

[124-047]would come again and seize her in the night, like furies preparing the

[124-048]deed that they would straightway avenge.

[124-049]They were taken out of the port and carried eastward by a gentle

[124-050]breeze. Some clouds tempered the sunlight, and the hour was always

[124-051]deepening toward the supreme beauty of evening. Sails larger and

[124-052]smaller changed their aspect like sensitive things, and made a cheerful

[124-053]companionship, alternately near and far. The grand city shone more

[124-054]vaguely, the mountains looked out above it, and there was stillness as

[124-055]in an island sanctuary. Yet suddenly Gwendolen let her hands fall, and

[124-056]said in a scarcely audible tone, “God help me!”

[124-057]“What is the matter?” said Grandcourt, not distinguishing the words.

[124-058]“Oh, nothing,” said Gwendolen, rousing herself from her momentary

[124-059]forgetfulness and resuming the ropes.

[124-060]“Don’t you find this pleasant?” said Grandcourt.

[124-061]“Very.”

[124-062]“You admit now we couldn’t have done anything better?”

[124-063]“No—I see nothing better. I think we shall go on always, like the

[124-064]Flying Dutchman,” said Gwendolen wildly.

[124-065]Grandcourt gave her one of his narrow examining glances, and then said,

[124-066]“If you like, we can go to Spezia in the morning, and let them take us

[124-067]up there.”

[124-068]“No; I shall like nothing better than this.”

[124-069]“Very well: we’ll do the same to-morrow. But we must be turning in

[124-070]soon. I shall put about.”

[124-071]CHAPTER LV.

[124-072]“Ritorna a tua scienza

[124-073]Che vuol, quanto la cosa e più perfetta

[124-074]Più senta il bene, e cosi la doglienza.”

[124-075]—DANTE.

[124-076]When Deronda met Gwendolen and Grandcourt on the staircase, his mind

[124-077]was seriously preoccupied. He had just been summoned to the second

[124-078]interview with his mother.

[124-079]In two hours after his parting from her he knew that the Princess

[124-080]Halm-Eberstein had left the hotel, and so far as the purpose of his

[124-081]journey to [[genoa|Genoa]] was concerned, he might himself have set off on his

[124-082]way to [[mainz|Mainz]], to deliver the letter from Joseph Kalonymos, and get

[124-083]possession of the family chest. But mixed mental conditions, which did

[124-084]not resolve themselves into definite reasons, hindered him from

[124-085]departure. Long after [[derondas-departure-for-the-east|the farewell]] he was kept passive by a weight of

[124-086]retrospective feeling. He lived again, with the new keenness of emotive

[124-087]memory, through the exciting scenes which seemed past only in the sense

[124-088]of preparation for their actual presence in his soul. He allowed

[124-089]himself in his solitude to sob, with perhaps more than a woman’s

[124-090]acuteness of [[sympathy|compassion]], over that woman’s life so near to his, and yet

[124-091]so remote. He beheld the world changed for him by the certitude of ties

[124-092]that altered the poise of hopes and fears, and gave him a new sense of

[124-093]fellowship, as if under cover of the night he had joined the wrong band

[124-094]of wanderers, and found with the rise of morning that the tents of his

[124-095]kindred were grouped far off. He had a quivering imaginative sense of

[124-096]close relation to the grandfather who had been animated by strong

[124-097]impulses and beloved thoughts, which were now perhaps being roused from

[124-098]their slumber within himself. And through all this passionate

[124-099]meditation [[Mordecai]] and [[Mirah-Lapidoth|Mirah]] were always present, as beings who

[124-100]clasped hands with him in sympathetic silence.

[124-101]Of such quick, responsive fibre was Deronda made, under that mantle of

[124-102]self-controlled reserve into which early experience had thrown so much

[124-103]of his young strength.

[124-104]When the persistent ringing of a bell as a signal reminded him of the

[124-105]hour he thought of looking into _Bradshaw_, and making the brief

[124-106]necessary preparations for starting by the next train—thought of it,

[124-107]but made no movement in consequence. Wishes went to Mainz and what he

[124-108]was to get possession of there—to [[london|London]] and the beings there who made

[124-109]the strongest attachments of his life; but there were other wishes that

[124-110]clung in these moments to Genoa, and they kept him where he was by that

[124-111]force which urges us to linger over an interview that carries a

[124-112]presentiment of final farewell or of overshadowing sorrow. Deronda did

[124-113]not formally say, “I will stay over to-night, because it is Friday, and

[124-114]I should like to go to the evening service at the [[the-synagogue|synagogue]] where they

[124-115]must all have gone; and besides, I may see the Grandcourts again.” But

[124-116]simply, instead of packing and ringing for his bill, he sat doing

[124-117]nothing at all, while his mind went to the synagogue and saw faces

[124-118]there probably little different from those of his grandfather’s time,

[124-119]and heard the Spanish-Hebrew liturgy which had lasted through the

[124-120]seasons of wandering generations like a plant with wandering seed, that

[124-121]gives the far-off lands a kinship to the exile’s home—while, also, his

[124-122]mind went toward Gwendolen, with anxious remembrance of what had been,

[124-123]and with a half-admitted impression that it would be hardness in him

[124-124]willingly to go away at once without making some effort, in spite of

[124-125]Grandcourt’s probable dislike, to manifest the continuance of his

[124-126]sympathy with her since their abrupt parting.

[124-127]In this state of mind he deferred departure, ate his dinner without

[124-128]sense of flavor, rose from it quickly to find the synagogue, and in

[124-129]passing the porter asked if Mr. and Mrs. Grandcourt were still in the

[124-130]hotel, and what was the number of their apartment. The porter gave him

[124-131]the number, but added that they were gone out boating. That information

[124-132]had somehow power enough over Deronda to divide his thoughts with the

[124-133]memories wakened among the sparse _talithim_ and keen dark faces of

[124-134]worshippers whose way of taking awful prayers and invocations with the

[124-135]easy familiarity which might be called Hebrew dyed Italian, made him

[124-136]reflect that his grandfather, according to the Princess’s hints of his

[124-137]character, must have been almost as exceptional a Jew as Mordecai. But

[124-138]were not men of ardent zeal and far-reaching hope everywhere

[124-139]exceptional? the men who had the visions which, as Mordecai said, were

[124-140]the creators and feeders of the world—moulding and feeding the more

[124-141]passive life which without them would dwindle and shrivel into the

[124-142]narrow tenacity of insects, unshaken by thoughts beyond the reach of

[124-143]their antennae. Something of a mournful impatience perhaps added itself

[124-144]to the solicitude about Gwendolen (a solicitude that had room to grow

[124-145]in his present release from immediate cares) as an incitement to hasten

[124-146]from the synagogue and choose to take his evening walk toward the quay,

[124-147]always a favorite haunt with him, and just now attractive with the

[124-148]possibility that he might be in time to see the Grandcourts come in

[124-149]from their boating. In this case, he resolved that he would advance to

[124-150]greet them deliberately, and ignore any grounds that the husband might

[124-151]have for wishing him elsewhere.

[124-152]The sun had set behind a bank of cloud, and only a faint yellow light

[124-153]was giving its farewell kisses to the waves, which were agitated by an

[124-154]active breeze. Deronda, sauntering slowly within sight of what took

[124-155]place on the strand, observed the groups there concentrating their

[124-156]attention on a sailing-boat which was advancing swiftly landward, being

[124-157]rowed by two men. Amidst the clamorous talk in various languages,

[124-158]Deronda held it the surer means of getting information not to ask

[124-159]questions, but to elbow his way to the foreground and be an

[124-160]unobstructed witness of what was occurring. Telescopes were being used,

[124-161]and loud statements made that the boat held somebody who had been

[124-162]drowned. One said it was the _milord_ who had gone out in a sailing

[124-163]boat; another maintained that the prostrate figure he discerned was

[124-164]_miladi_; a Frenchman who had no glass would rather say that it was

[124-165]_milord_ who had probably taken his wife out to drown her, according to

[124-166]the national practice—a remark which an English skipper immediately

[124-167]commented on in our native idiom (as nonsense which—had undergone a

[124-168]mining operation), and further dismissed by the decision that the

[124-169]reclining figure was a woman. For Deronda, terribly excited by

[124-170]fluctuating fears, the strokes of the oars as he watched them were

[124-171]divided by swift visions of events, possible and impossible, which

[124-172]might have brought about this issue, or this broken-off fragment of an

[124-173]issue, with a worse half undisclosed—if this woman apparently snatched

[124-174]from the waters were really Mrs. Grandcourt.

[124-175]But soon there was no longer any doubt: the boat was being pulled to

[124-176]land, and he saw Gwendolen half raising herself on her hands, by her

[124-177]own effort, under her heavy covering of tarpaulin and pea-jackets—pale

[124-178]as one of the sheeted dead, shivering, with wet hair streaming, a wild

[124-179]amazed consciousness in her eyes, as if she had waked up in a world

[124-180]where some judgment was impending, and the beings she saw around were

[124-181]coming to seize her. The first rower who jumped to land was also wet

[124-182]through, and ran off; the sailors, close about the boat, hindered

[124-183]Deronda from advancing, and he could only look on while Gwendolen gave

[124-184]scared glances, and seemed to shrink with terror as she was carefully,

[124-185]tenderly helped out, and led on by the strong arms of those rough,

[124-186]bronzed men, her wet clothes clinging about her limbs, and adding to

[124-187]the impediment of her weakness. Suddenly her wandering eyes fell on

[124-188]Deronda, standing before her, and immediately, as if she had been

[124-189]expecting him and looking for him, she tried to stretch out her arms,

[124-190]which were held back by her supporters, saying, in a muffled voice,
