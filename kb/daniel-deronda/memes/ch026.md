[026-001]“I mean this in kindness, my dear.” His tone had softened.

[026-002]“I am aware of that, uncle,” said [[Gwendolen-Harleth|Gwendolen]], rising and shaking her

[026-003]head back, as if to rouse herself out of painful passivity. “I am not

[026-004]foolish. I know that I must be married some time—before it is too

[026-005]late. And I don’t see how I could do better than marry [[Henleigh-Grandcourt|Mr. Grandcourt]].

[026-006]I mean to accept him, if possible.” She felt as if she were reinforcing

[026-007]herself by speaking with this decisiveness to her uncle.

[026-008]But the rector was a little startled by so bare a version of his own

[026-009]meaning from those young lips. He wished that in her mind his advice

[026-010]should be taken in an infusion of sentiments proper to a girl, and such

[026-011]as are presupposed in the advice of a clergyman, although he may not

[026-012]consider them always appropriate to be put forward. He wished his niece

[026-013]parks, carriages, a title—everything that would make this world a

[026-014]pleasant abode; but he wished her not to be cynical—to be, on the

[026-015]contrary, religiously dutiful, and have warm domestic affections.

[026-016]“My dear Gwendolen,” he said, rising also, and speaking with benignant

[026-017]gravity, “I trust that you will find in marriage a new fountain of duty

[026-018]and affection. Marriage is the only true and satisfactory sphere of a

[026-019]woman, and if your marriage with Mr. Grandcourt should be happily

[026-020]decided upon, you will have, probably, an increasing power, both of

[026-021]rank and wealth, which may be used for the benefit of others. These

[026-022]considerations are something higher than romance! You are fitted by

[026-023]natural gifts for a position which, considering your birth and early

[026-024]prospects, could hardly be looked forward to as in the ordinary course

[026-025]of things; and I trust that you will grace it, not only by those

[026-026]personal gifts, but by a good and consistent life.”

[026-027]“I hope mamma will be the happier,” said Gwendolen, in a more cheerful

[026-028]way, lifting her hands backward to her neck and moving toward the door.

[026-029]She wanted to waive those higher considerations.

[026-030][[Rex-Gascoigne|Mr. Gascoigne]] felt that he had come to a satisfactory understanding

[026-031]with his niece, and had furthered her happy settlement in life by

[026-032]furthering her engagement to Grandcourt. Meanwhile there was another

[026-033]person to whom the contemplation of that issue had been a motive for

[026-034]some activity, and who believed that he, too, on this particular day

[026-035]had done something toward bringing about a favorable decision in _his_

[026-036]sense—which happened to be the reverse of the rector’s.

[026-037][[Lush|Mr. Lush]]’s absence from [[diplow-hall|Diplow]] during Gwendolen’s visit had been due,

[026-038]not to any fear on his part of meeting that supercilious young lady, or

[026-039]of being abashed by her frank dislike, but to an engagement from which

[026-040]he expected important consequences. He was gone, in fact, to the

[026-041]Wanchester station to meet a lady, accompanied by a maid and two

[026-042]children, whom he put into a fly, and afterward followed to the hotel

[026-043]of the Golden Keys, in that town. An impressive woman, whom many would

[026-044]turn to look at again in passing; her figure was slim and sufficiently

[026-045]tall, her face rather emaciated, so that its sculpturesque beauty was

[026-046]the more pronounced, her crisp hair perfectly black, and her large,

[026-047]anxious eyes what we call black. Her dress was soberly correct, her

[026-048]age, perhaps, physically more advanced than the number of years would

[026-049]imply, but hardly less than seven-and-thirty. An uneasy-looking woman:

[026-050]her glance seemed to presuppose that the people and things were going

[026-051]to be unfavorable to her, while she was, nevertheless, ready to meet

[026-052]them with resolution. The children were lovely—a dark-haired girl of

[026-053]six or more, a fairer boy of five. When Lush incautiously expressed

[026-054]some surprise at her having brought the children, she said, with a

[026-055]sharp-toned intonation,

[026-056]“Did you suppose I should come wandering about here by myself? Why

[026-057]should I not bring all four if I liked?”

[026-058]“Oh, certainly,” said Lush, with his usual fluent _nonchalance_.

[026-059]He stayed an hour or so in conference with her, and rode back to Diplow

[026-060]in a state of mind that was at once hopeful and busily anxious as to

[026-061]the execution of the little plan on which his hopefulness was based.

[026-062]Grandcourt’s marriage to Gwendolen Harleth would not, he believed, be

[026-063]much of a good to either of them, and it would plainly be fraught with

[026-064]disagreeables to himself. But now he felt confident enough to say

[026-065]inwardly, “I will take, nay, I will lay odds that the marriage will

[026-066]never happen.”

[026-067]CHAPTER XIV.

[026-068]I will not clothe myself in wreck—wear gems

[026-069]Sawed from cramped finger-bones of women drowned;

[026-070]Feel chilly vaporous hands of ireful ghosts

[026-071]Clutching my necklace: trick my maiden breast

[026-072]With orphans’ [[inheritance|heritage]]. Let your dead love

[026-073]Marry its dead.

[026-074]Gwendolen looked lovely and vigorous as a tall, newly-opened lily the

[026-075]next morning: there was a reaction of young energy in her, and

[026-076]yesterday’s self-distrust seemed no more than the transient shiver on

[026-077]the surface of a full stream. The roving archery match in Cardell Chase

[026-078]was a delightful prospect for the sport’s sake: she felt herself

[026-079]beforehand moving about like a wood-nymph under the beeches (in

[026-080]appreciative company), and the imagined scene lent a charm to further

[026-081]advances on the part of Grandcourt—not an impassioned lyrical Daphnis

[026-082]for the wood-nymph, certainly: but so much the better. To-day Gwendolen

[026-083]foresaw him making slow conversational approaches to a declaration, and

[026-084]foresaw herself awaiting and encouraging it according to the rational

[026-085]conclusion which she had expressed to her uncle.

[026-086]When she came down to breakfast (after every one had left the table

[026-087]except [[Mrs-Davilow|Mrs. Davilow]]) there were letters on her plate. One of them she

[026-088]read with a gathering smile, and then handed it to her mamma, who, on

[026-089]returning it, smiled also, finding new cheerfulness in the good spirits

[026-090]her daughter had shown ever since waking, and said,

[026-091]“You don’t feel inclined to go a thousand miles away?”

[026-092]“Not exactly so far.”

[026-093]“It was a sad omission not to have written again before this. Can’t you

[026-094]write now—before we set out this morning?”

[026-095]“It is not so pressing. To-morrow will do. You see they leave town

[026-096]to-day. I must write to [[dover|Dover]]. They will be there till Monday.”

[026-097]“Shall I write for you, dear—if it teases you?”

[026-098]Gwendolen did not speak immediately, but after sipping her coffee,

[026-099]answered brusquely, “Oh no, let it be; I will write to-morrow.” Then,

[026-100]feeling a touch of compunction, she looked up and said with playful

[026-101]tenderness, “Dear, old, beautiful mamma!”

[026-102]“Old, child, truly.”

[026-103]“Please don’t, mamma! I meant old for darling. You are hardly

[026-104]twenty-five years older than I am. When you talk in that way my life

[026-105]shrivels up before me.”

[026-106]“One can have a great deal of happiness in twenty-five years, my dear.”

[026-107]“I must lose no time in beginning,” said Gwendolen, merrily. “The

[026-108]sooner I get my palaces and coaches the better.”

[026-109]“And a good husband who adores you, Gwen,” said Mrs. Davilow,

[026-110]encouragingly.

[026-111]Gwendolen put out her lips saucily and said nothing.

[026-112]It was a slight drawback on her pleasure in starting that the rector

[026-113]was detained by magistrate’s business, and would probably not be able

[026-114]to get to Cardell Chase at all that day. She cared little that Mrs.

[026-115]Gascoigne and Anna chose not to go without him, but her uncle’s

[026-116]presence would have seemed to make it a matter of course that the

[026-117]decision taken would be acted on. For decision in itself began to be

[026-118]formidable. Having come close to accepting Grandcourt, Gwendolen felt

[026-119]this lot of unhoped-for fullness rounding itself too definitely. When

[026-120]we take to wishing a great deal for ourselves, whatever we get soon

[026-121]turns into mere limitation and exclusion. Still there was the

[026-122]reassuring thought that marriage would be the gate into a larger

[026-123]freedom.

[026-124]The place of meeting was a grassy spot called Green Arbor, where a bit

[026-125]of hanging wood made a sheltering amphitheatre. It was here that the

[026-126]coachful of servants with provisions had to prepare the picnic meal;

[026-127]and the warden of the Chase was to guide the roving archers so as to

[026-128]keep them within the due distance from this centre, and hinder them

[026-129]from wandering beyond the limit which had been fixed on—a curve that

[026-130]might be drawn through certain well-known points, such as the double

[026-131]Oak, the Whispering Stones, and the High Cross. The plan was to take

[026-132]only a preliminary stroll before luncheon, keeping the main roving

[026-133]expedition for the more exquisite lights of the afternoon. The muster

[026-134]was rapid enough to save every one from dull moments of waiting, and

[026-135]when the groups began to scatter themselves through the light and

[026-136]shadow made here by closely neighboring beeches and there by rarer

[026-137]oaks, one may suppose that a painter would have been glad to look on.

[026-138]This roving archery was far prettier than the stationary game, but

[026-139]success in shooting at variable marks were less favored by practice,

[026-140]and the hits were distributed among the volunteer archers otherwise

[026-141]than they would have been in target-shooting. From this cause, perhaps,

[026-142]as well as from the twofold distraction of being preoccupied and

[026-143]wishing not to betray her preoccupation, Gwendolen did not greatly

[026-144]distinguish herself in these first experiments, unless it were by the

[026-145]lively grace with which she took her comparative failure. She was in

[026-146]white and green as on the day of the former meeting, when it made an

[026-147]epoch for her that she was introduced to Grandcourt; he was continually

[026-148]by her side now, yet it would have been hard to tell from mere looks

[026-149]and manners that their relation to each other had at all changed since

[026-150]their first conversation. Still there were other grounds that made most

[026-151]persons conclude them to be, if not engaged already, on the eve of

[026-152]being so. And she believed this herself. As they were all returning

[026-153]toward Green Arbor in divergent groups, not thinking at all of taking

[026-154]aim but merely chattering, words passed which seemed really the

[026-155]beginning of that end—the beginning of her acceptance. Grandcourt

[026-156]said, “Do you know how long it is since I first saw you in this dress?”

[026-157]“The archery meeting was on the 25th, and this is the 13th,” said

[026-158]Gwendolen, laughingly. “I am not good at calculating, but I will

[026-159]venture to say that it must be nearly three weeks.”

[026-160]A little pause, and then he said, “That is a great loss of time.”

[026-161]“That your knowing me has caused you? Pray don’t be uncomplimentary; I

[026-162]don’t like it.”

[026-163]Pause again. “It is because of the gain that I feel the loss.”

[026-164]Here Gwendolen herself left a pause. She was thinking, “He is really

[026-165]very ingenious. He never speaks stupidly.” Her silence was so unusual

[026-166]that it seemed the strongest of favorable answers, and he continued:

[026-167]“The gain of knowing you makes me feel the time I lose in uncertainty.

[026-168]Do _you_ like uncertainty?”

[026-169]“I think I do, rather,” said Gwendolen, suddenly beaming on him with a

[026-170]playful smile. “There is more in it.”

[026-171]Grandcourt met her laughing eyes with a slow, steady look right into

[026-172]them, which seemed like vision in the abstract, and then said, “Do you

[026-173]mean more torment for me?”

[026-174]There was something so strange to Gwendolen in this moment that she was

[026-175]quite shaken out of her usual self-consciousness. Blushing and turning

[026-176]away her eyes, she said, “No, that would make me sorry.”

[026-177]Grandcourt would have followed up this answer, which the change in her

[026-178]manner made apparently decisive of her favorable intention; but he was

[026-179]not in any way overcome so as to be unaware that they were now, within

[026-180]sight of everybody, descending the space into Green Arbor, and

[026-181]descending it at an ill-chosen point where it began to be

[026-182]inconveniently steep. This was a reason for offering his hand in the

[026-183]literal sense to help her; she took it, and they came down in silence,

[026-184]much observed by those already on the level—among others by Mrs.

[026-185]Arrowpoint, who happened to be standing with Mrs. Davilow. That lady

[026-186]had now made up her mind that Grandcourt’s merits were not such as

[026-187]would have induced [[Catherine-Arrowpoint|Catherine]] to accept him, Catherine having so high a

[026-188]standard as to have refused Lord Slogan. Hence she looked at the tenant

[026-189]of Diplow with dispassionate eyes.
