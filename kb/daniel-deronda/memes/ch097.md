[097-001]“As plain as a pike-staff,” said Pash, with an ironical laugh. “You

[097-002]pluck it up by the roots, strip off the leaves and bark, shave off the

[097-003]knots, and smooth it at top and bottom; put it where you will, it will

[097-004]do no harm, it will never sprout. You may make a handle of it, or you

[097-005]may throw it on the bonfire of scoured rubbish. I don’t see why our

[097-006]rubbish is to be held sacred any more than the rubbish of Brahmanism or

[097-007]Buddhism.”

[097-008]“No,” said [[Mordecai]], “no, Pash, because you have lost the heart of the

[097-009]Jew. Community was felt before it was called good. I praise no

[097-010]superstition, I praise the living fountains of enlarging belief. What

[097-011]is growth, completion, development? You began with that question, I

[097-012]apply it to the history of our people. I say that the effect of our

[097-013]separateness will not be completed and have its highest transformation

[097-014]unless our race takes on again the character of a nationality. That is

[097-015]the fulfillment of the religious trust that moulded them into a people,

[097-016]whose life has made half the inspiration of the world. What is it to me

[097-017]that the ten tribes are lost untraceably, or that multitudes of the

[097-018]children of Judah have mixed themselves with the Gentile populations as

[097-019]a river with rivers? Behold our people still! Their skirts spread afar;

[097-020]they are torn and soiled and trodden on; but there is a jeweled

[097-021]breastplate. Let the wealthy men, the monarchs of commerce, the learned

[097-022]in all knowledge, the skilful in all arts, the speakers, the political

[097-023]counselors, who carry in their veins the Hebrew blood which has

[097-024]maintained its vigor in all climates, and the pliancy of the Hebrew

[097-025]genius for which difficulty means new device—let them say, ‘we will

[097-026]lift up a standard, we will unite in a labor hard but glorious like

[097-027]that of Moses and Ezra, a labor which shall be a worthy fruit of the

[097-028]long anguish whereby our fathers maintained their separateness,

[097-029]refusing the ease of falsehood.’ They have wealth enough to redeem the

[097-030]soil from debauched and paupered conquerors; they have the skill of the

[097-031]statesman to devise, the tongue of the orator to persuade. And is there

[097-032]no prophet or poet among us to make the ears of Christian Europe tingle

[097-033]with shame at the hideous obloquy of Christian strife which the Turk

[097-034]gazes at as at the fighting of beasts to which he has lent an arena?

[097-035]There is store of wisdom among us to found a new Jewish polity, grand,

[097-036]simple, just, like the old—a republic where there is equality of

[097-037]protection, an equality which shone like a star on the forehead of our

[097-038]ancient community, and gave it more than the brightness of Western

[097-039]freedom amid the despotisms of the East. Then our race shall have an

[097-040]organic centre, a heart and brain to watch and guide and execute; the

[097-041]outraged Jew shall have a defense in the court of nations, as the

[097-042]outraged Englishmen of America. And the world will gain as Israel

[097-043]gains. For there will be a community in the van of the East which

[097-044]carries the culture and the sympathies of every great nation in its

[097-045]bosom: there will be a land set for a halting-place of enmities, a

[097-046]neutral ground for the East as Belgium is for the West. Difficulties? I

[097-047]know there are difficulties. But let the spirit of sublime achievement

[097-048]move in the great among our people, and the work will begin.”

[097-049]“Ay, we may safely admit that, Mordecai,” said Pash. “When there are

[097-050]great men on ’Change, and high-flying professors converted to your

[097-051]doctrine, difficulties will vanish like smoke.”

[097-052][[Daniel-Deronda|Deronda]], inclined by nature to take the side of those on whom the

[097-053]arrows of scorn were falling, could not help replying to Pash’s

[097-054]outfling, and said,

[097-055]“If we look back to the history of efforts which have made great

[097-056]changes, it is astonishing how many of them seemed hopeless to those

[097-057]who looked on in the beginning.

[097-058]“Take what we have all heard and seen something of—the effort after

[097-059]the unity of Italy, which we are sure soon to see accomplished to the

[097-060]very last boundary. Look into Mazzini’s account of his first yearning,

[097-061]when he was a boy, after a restored greatness and a new freedom to

[097-062]Italy, and of his first efforts as a young man to rouse the same

[097-063]feelings in other young men, and get them to work toward a united

[097-064]nationality. Almost everything seemed against him; his countrymen were

[097-065]ignorant or indifferent, governments hostile, Europe incredulous. Of

[097-066]course the scorners often seemed wise. Yet you see the prophecy lay

[097-067]with him. As long as there is a remnant of national consciousness, I

[097-068]suppose nobody will deny that there may be a new stirring of memories

[097-069]and hopes which may inspire arduous action.”

[097-070]“Amen,” said Mordecai, to whom Deronda’s words were a cordial. “What is

[097-071]needed is the leaven—what is needed is the seed of fire. The [[inheritance|heritage]]

[097-072]of Israel is beating in the pulses of millions; it lives in their veins

[097-073]as a power without understanding, like the morning exultation of herds;

[097-074]it is the inborn half of memory, moving as in a dream among writings on

[097-075]the walls, which it sees dimly but cannot divide into speech. Let the

[097-076]torch of visible community be lit! Let the reason of Israel disclose

[097-077]itself in a great outward deed, and let there be another great

[097-078]migration, another choosing of Israel to be a nationality whose members

[097-079]may still stretch to the ends of the earth, even as the sons of England

[097-080]and Germany, whom enterprise carries afar, but who still have a

[097-081]national hearth and a tribunal of national opinion. Will any say ‘It

[097-082]cannot be’? Baruch Spinoza had not a faithful Jewish heart, though he

[097-083]had sucked the life of his intellect at the breasts of Jewish

[097-084]tradition. He laid bare his father’s nakedness and said, ‘They who

[097-085]scorn him have the higher wisdom.’ Yet Baruch Spinoza confessed, he saw

[097-086]not why Israel should not again be a chosen nation. Who says that the

[097-087]history and literature of our race are dead? Are they not as living as

[097-088]the history and literature of Greece and Rome, which have inspired

[097-089]revolutions, enkindled the thought of Europe, and made the unrighteous

[097-090]powers tremble? These were an inheritance dug from the tomb. Ours is an

[097-091]inheritance that has never ceased to quiver in millions of human

[097-092]frames.”

[097-093]Mordecai had stretched his arms upward, and his long thin hands

[097-094]quivered in the air for a moment after he had ceased to speak. Gideon

[097-095]was certainly a little moved, for though there was no long pause before

[097-096]he made a remark in objection, his tone was more mild and deprecatory

[097-097]than before; Pash, meanwhile, pressing his lips together, rubbing his

[097-098]black head with both his hands and wrinkling his brow horizontally,

[097-099]with the expression of one who differs from every speaker, but does not

[097-100]think it worth while to say so. There is a sort of human paste that

[097-101]when it comes near the fire of enthusiasm is only baked into harder

[097-102]shape.

[097-103]“It may seem well enough on one side to make so much of our memories

[097-104]and inheritance as you do, Mordecai,” said Gideon; “but there’s another

[097-105]side. It isn’t all gratitude and harmless glory. Our people have

[097-106]inherited a good deal of hatred. There’s a pretty lot of curses still

[097-107]flying about, and stiff settled rancor inherited from the times of

[097-108]persecution. How will you justify keeping one sort of memory and

[097-109]throwing away the other? There are ugly debts standing on both sides.”

[097-110]“I justify the choice as all other choice is justified,” said Mordecai.

[097-111]“I cherish nothing for the Jewish nation, I seek nothing for them, but

[097-112]the good which promises good to all the nations. The spirit of our

[097-113]religious life, which is one with our national life, is not hatred of

[097-114]aught but wrong. The Master has said, an offence against man is worse

[097-115]than an offence against God. But what wonder if there is hatred in the

[097-116]breasts of Jews, who are children of the ignorant and oppressed—what

[097-117]wonder, since there is hatred in the breasts of Christians? Our

[097-118]national life was a growing light. Let the central fire be kindled

[097-119]again, and the light will reach afar. The degraded and scorned of our

[097-120]race will learn to think of their sacred land, not as a place for

[097-121]saintly beggary to await death in loathsome idleness, but as a republic

[097-122]where the Jewish spirit manifests itself in a new order founded on the

[097-123]old, purified and enriched by the experience our greatest sons have

[097-124]gathered from the life of the ages. How long is it?—only two centuries

[097-125]since a vessel carried over the ocean the beginning of the great North

[097-126]American nation. The people grew like meeting waters—they were various

[097-127]in habit and sect—there came a time, a century ago, when they needed a

[097-128]polity, and there were heroes of peace among them. What had they to

[097-129]form a polity with but memories of Europe, corrected by the vision of a

[097-130]better? Let our wise and wealthy show themselves heroes. They have the

[097-131]memories of the East and West, and they have the full vision of a

[097-132]better. A new Persia with a purified religion magnified itself in art

[097-133]and wisdom. So will a new Judea, poised between East and West—a

[097-134]covenant of reconciliation. Will any say, the prophetic vision of your

[097-135]race has been hopelessly mixed with folly and bigotry: the angel of

[097-136]progress has no message for [[religion|Judaism]]—it is a half-buried city for the

[097-137]paid workers to lay open—the waters are rushing by it as a forsaken

[097-138]field? I say that the strongest principle of growth lies in human

[097-139]choice. The sons of Judah have to choose that God may again choose

[097-140]them. The Messianic time is the time when Israel shall will the

[097-141]planting of the national ensign. The Nile overflowed and rushed onward:

[097-142]the Egyptian could not choose the overflow, but he chose to work and

[097-143]make channels for the fructifying waters, and Egypt became the land of

[097-144]corn. Shall man, whose soul is set in the royalty of discernment and

[097-145]resolve, deny his rank and say, I am an onlooker, ask no choice or

[097-146]purpose of me? That is the blasphemy of this time. The divine principle

[097-147]of our race is action, choice, resolved memory. Let us contradict the

[097-148]blasphemy, and help to will our own better future and the better future

[097-149]of the world—not renounce our higher gift and say, ‘Let us be as if we

[097-150]were not among the populations;’ but choose our full heritage, claim

[097-151]the brotherhood of our nation, and carry into it a new brotherhood with

[097-152]the nations of the Gentiles. The vision is there; it will be fulfilled.”

[097-153]With the last sentence, which was no more than a loud whisper, Mordecai

[097-154]let his chin sink on his breast and his eyelids fall. No one spoke. It

[097-155]was not the first time that he had insisted on the same ideas, but he

[097-156]was seen to-night in a new phase. The quiet tenacity of his ordinary

[097-157]self differed as much from his present exaltation of mood as a man in

[097-158]private talk, giving reasons for a revolution of which no sign is

[097-159]discernable, differs from one who feels himself an agent in a

[097-160]revolution begun. The dawn of fulfillment brought to his hope by

[097-161]Deronda’s presence had wrought Mordecai’s conception into a state of

[097-162]impassioned conviction, and he had found strength in his excitement to

[097-163]pour forth the unlocked floods of emotive argument, with a sense of

[097-164]haste as at a crisis which must be seized. But now there had come with

[097-165]the quiescence of fatigue a sort of thankful wonder that he had

[097-166]spoken—a contemplation of his life as a journey which had come at last

[097-167]to this bourne. After a great excitement, the ebbing strength of

[097-168]impulse is apt to leave us in this aloofness from our active self. And

[097-169]in the moments after Mordecai had sunk his head, his mind was wandering

[097-170]along the paths of his youth, and all the hopes which had ended in

[097-171]bringing him hither.
