[063-001]He was in a state of disgust and embitterment quite new in the history

[063-002]of their relation to each other. It was undeniable that this woman,

[063-003]whose life he had allowed to send such deep suckers into his, had a

[063-004]terrible power of annoyance in her; and the rash hurry of his

[063-005]proceedings had left her opportunities open. His pride saw very ugly

[063-006]possibilities threatening it, and he stood for several minutes in

[063-007]silence reviewing the situation—considering how he could act upon her.

[063-008]Unlike himself she was of a direct nature, with certain simple

[063-009]strongly-colored tendencies, and there was one often-experienced effect

[063-010]which he thought he could count upon now. As [[Sir-Hugo-Mallinger|Sir Hugo]] had said of him,

[063-011][[Henleigh-Grandcourt|Grandcourt]] knew how to play his cards upon occasion.

[063-012]He did not speak again, but looked at his watch, rang the bell, and

[063-013]ordered the vehicle to be brought round immediately. Then he removed

[063-014]farther from her, walked as if in expectation of a summons, and

[063-015]remained silent without turning his eyes upon her.

[063-016]She was suffering the horrible conflict of self-reproach and tenacity.

[063-017]She saw beforehand Grandcourt leaving her without even looking at her

[063-018]again—herself left behind in lonely uncertainty—hearing nothing from

[063-019]him—not knowing whether she had done her children harm—feeling that

[063-020]she had perhaps made him hate her;—all the wretchedness of a creature

[063-021]who had defeated her own motives. And yet she could not bear to give up

[063-022]a purpose which was a sweet morsel to her vindictiveness. If she had

[063-023]not been a mother she would willingly have sacrificed herself to her

[063-024]revenge—to what she felt to be the justice of hindering another from

[063-025]getting happiness by willingly giving her over to misery. The two

[063-026]dominant passions were at struggle. She must satisfy them both.

[063-027]“Don’t let us part in anger, Henleigh,” she began, without changing her

[063-028]voice or attitude: “it is a very little thing I ask. If I were refusing

[063-029]to give anything up that you call yours it would be different: that

[063-030]would be a reason for treating me as if you hated me. But I ask such a

[063-031]little thing. If you will tell me where you are going on the

[063-032]wedding-day I will take care that the diamonds shall be delivered to

[063-033]her without scandal. Without scandal,” she repeated entreatingly.

[063-034]“Such preposterous whims make a woman odious,” said Grandcourt, not

[063-035]giving way in look or movement. “What is the use of talking to mad

[063-036]people?”

[063-037]“Yes, I am foolish—loneliness has made me foolish—indulge me.” Sobs

[063-038]rose as she spoke. “If you will indulge me in this one folly I will be

[063-039]very meek—I will never trouble you.” She burst into hysterical crying,

[063-040]and said again almost with a scream—“I will be very meek after that.”

[063-041]There was a strange mixture of acting and reality in this passion. She

[063-042]kept hold of her purpose as a child might tighten its hand over a small

[063-043]stolen thing, crying and denying all the while. Even Grandcourt was

[063-044]wrought upon by surprise: this capricious wish, this childish violence,

[063-045]was as unlike Lydia’s bearing as it was incongruous with her person.

[063-046]Both had always had a stamp of dignity on them. Yet she seemed more

[063-047]manageable in this state than in her former attitude of defiance. He

[063-048]came close up to her again, and said, in his low imperious tone, “Be

[063-049]quiet, and hear what I tell you, I will never forgive you if you

[063-050]present yourself again and make a scene.”

[063-051]She pressed her handkerchief against her face, and when she could speak

[063-052]firmly said, in the muffled voice that follows sobbing, “I will not—if

[063-053]you will let me have my way—I promise you not to thrust myself forward

[063-054]again. I have never broken my word to you—how many have you broken to

[063-055]me? When you gave me the diamonds to wear you were not thinking of

[063-056]having another wife. And I now give them up—I don’t reproach you—I

[063-057]only ask you to let me give them up in my own way. Have I not borne it

[063-058]well? Everything is to be taken away from me, and when I ask for a

[063-059]straw, a chip—you deny it me.” She had spoken rapidly, but after a

[063-060]little pause she said more slowly, her voice freed from its muffled

[063-061]tone: “I will not bear to have it denied me.”

[063-062]Grandcourt had a baffling sense that he had to deal with something like

[063-063]madness; he could only govern by giving way. The servant came to say

[063-064]the fly was ready. When the door was shut again Grandcourt said

[063-065]sullenly, “We are going to [[ryelands|Ryelands]] then.”

[063-066]“They shall be delivered to her there,” said Lydia, with decision.

[063-067]“Very well, I am going.” He felt no inclination even to take her hand:

[063-068]she had annoyed him too sorely. But now that she had gained her point,

[063-069]she was prepared to humble herself that she might propitiate him.

[063-070]“Forgive me; I will never vex you again,” she said, with beseeching

[063-071]looks. Her inward voice said distinctly—“It is only I who have to

[063-072]forgive.” Yet she was obliged to ask forgiveness.

[063-073]“You had better keep that promise. You have made me feel uncommonly ill

[063-074]with your folly,” said Grandcourt, apparently choosing this statement

[063-075]as the strongest possible use of language.

[063-076]“Poor thing!” cried Lydia, with a faint smile;—was he aware of the

[063-077]minor fact that he made her feel ill this morning?

[063-078]But with the quick transition natural to her, she was now ready to coax

[063-079]him if he would let her, that they might part in some degree

[063-080]reconciled. She ventured to lay her hand on his shoulder, and he did

[063-081]not move away from her: she had so far succeeded in alarming him, that

[063-082]he was not sorry for these proofs of returned subjection.

[063-083]“Light a cigar,” she said, soothingly, taking the case from his

[063-084]breast-pocket and opening it.

[063-085]Amidst such caressing signs of mutual fear they parted. The effect that

[063-086]clung and gnawed within Grandcourt was a sense of imperfect mastery.

[063-087]CHAPTER XXXI.

[063-088]“A wild dedication of yourselves

[063-089]To unpath’d waters, undreamed shores.”

[063-090]—SHAKESPEARE.

[063-091]On the day when [[Gwendolen-Harleth|Gwendolen Harleth]] was married and became Mrs.

[063-092]Grandcourt, the morning was clear and bright, and while the sun was low

[063-093]a slight frost crisped the leaves. The bridal party was worth seeing,

[063-094]and half [[pennicote-rectory|Pennicote]] turned out to see it, lining the pathway up to the

[063-095]church. An old friend of the rector’s performed the marriage ceremony,

[063-096]the rector himself acting as father, to the great advantage of the

[063-097]procession. Only two faces, it was remarked, showed signs of

[063-098]sadness—[[Mrs-Davilow|Mrs. Davilow]]’s and Anna’s. The mother’s delicate eyelids were

[063-099]pink, as if she had been crying half the night; and no one was

[063-100]surprised that, splendid as the match was, she should feel the parting

[063-101]from a daughter who was the flower of her children and of her own life.

[063-102]It was less understood why Anna should be troubled when she was being

[063-103]so well set off by the bridesmaid’s dress. Every one else seemed to

[063-104]reflect the brilliancy of the occasion—the bride most of all. Of her

[063-105]it was agreed that as to figure and carriage she was worthy to be a

[063-106]“lady o’ title”: as to face, perhaps it might be thought that a title

[063-107]required something more rosy; but the bridegroom himself not being

[063-108]fresh-colored—being indeed, as the miller’s wife observed, very much

[063-109]of her own husband’s complexion—the match was the more complete.

[063-110]Anyhow he must be very fond of her; and it was to be hoped that he

[063-111]would never cast it up to her that she had been going out to service as

[063-112]a governess, and her mother to live at Sawyer’s Cottage—vicissitudes

[063-113]which had been much spoken of in the village. The miller’s daughter of

[063-114]fourteen could not believe that high gentry behaved badly to their

[063-115]wives, but her mother instructed her—“Oh, child, men’s men: gentle or

[063-116]simple, they’re much of a muchness. I’ve heard my mother say Squire

[063-117]Pelton used to take his dogs and a long whip into his wife’s room, and

[063-118]flog ’em there to frighten her; and my mother was lady’s-maid there at

[063-119]the very time.”

[063-120]“That’s unlucky talk for a wedding, Mrs. Girdle,” said the tailor. “A

[063-121]quarrel may end wi’ the whip, but it begins wi’ the tongue, and it’s

[063-122]the women have got the most o’ that.”

[063-123]“The Lord gave it ’em to use, I suppose,” said Mrs. Girdle. “_He_ never

[063-124]meant you to have it all your own way.”

[063-125]“By what I can make out from the gentleman as attends to the grooming

[063-126]at [[Offendene]],” said the tailor, “this Mr. Grandcourt has wonderful

[063-127]little tongue. Everything must be done dummy-like without his ordering.”

[063-128]“Then he’s the more whip, I doubt,” said Mrs. Girdle. “_She’s_ got

[063-129]tongue enough, I warrant her. See, there they come out together!”

[063-130]“What wonderful long corners she’s got to her eyes!” said the tailor.

[063-131]“She makes you feel comical when she looks at you.”

[063-132]Gwendolen, in fact, never showed more elasticity in her bearing, more

[063-133]lustre in her long brown glance: she had the brilliancy of strong

[063-134]excitement, which will sometimes come even from pain. It was not pain,

[063-135]however, that she was feeling: she had wrought herself up to much the

[063-136]same condition as that in which she stood at the gambling-table when

[063-137][[Daniel-Deronda|Deronda]] was looking at her, and she began to lose. There was an

[063-138]enjoyment in it: whatever uneasiness a growing conscience had created

[063-139]was disregarded as an ailment might have been, amidst the gratification

[063-140]of that ambitious [[pride|vanity]] and desire for luxury within her which it

[063-141]would take a great deal of slow poisoning to kill. This morning she

[063-142]could not have said truly that she repented her acceptance of

[063-143]Grandcourt, or that any fears in hazy perspective could hinder the

[063-144]glowing effect of the immediate scene in which she was the central

[063-145]object. That she was doing something wrong—that a punishment might be

[063-146]hanging over her—that the woman to whom she had given a promise and

[063-147]broken it, was thinking of her in bitterness and misery with a just

[063-148]reproach—that Deronda with his way of looking into things very likely

[063-149]despised her for marrying Grandcourt, as he had despised her for

[063-150]gambling—above all, that the cord which united her with this lover and

[063-151]which she had heretofore held by the hand, was now being flung over her

[063-152]neck,—all this yeasty mingling of dimly understood facts with vague

[063-153]but deep impressions, and with images half real, half fantastic, had

[063-154]been disturbing her during the weeks of her engagement. Was that

[063-155]agitating experience nullified this morning? No: it was surmounted and

[063-156]thrust down with a sort of exulting defiance as she felt herself

[063-157]standing at the game of life with many eyes upon her, daring everything

[063-158]to win much—or if to lose, still with _éclat_ and a sense of

[063-159]importance. But this morning a losing destiny for herself did not press

[063-160]upon her as a fear: she thought that she was entering on a fuller power

[063-161]of managing circumstances—with all the official strength of marriage,

[063-162]which some women made so poor a use of. That intoxication of youthful

[063-163]egoism out of which she had been shaken by trouble, [[shame|humiliation]], and a

[063-164]new sense of culpability, had returned upon her under a newly-fed

[063-165]strength of the old fumes. She did not in the least present the ideal

[063-166]of the tearful, tremulous bride. Poor Gwendolen, whom some had judged

[063-167]much too forward and instructed in the world’s ways!—with her erect

[063-168]head and elastic footstep she was walking among illusions; and yet,

[063-169]too, there was an under-consciousness of her that she was a little

[063-170]intoxicated.

[063-171]“Thank God you bear it so well, my darling!” said Mrs. Davilow, when

[063-172]she had helped Gwendolen to doff her bridal white and put on her

[063-173]traveling dress. All the trembling had been done by the poor mother,

[063-174]and her agitation urged Gwendolen doubly to take the morning as if it

[063-175]were a triumph.
