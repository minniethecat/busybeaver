# Adelaide Rebekah

Adelaide Rebekah is one of the younger children in the [[Cohen family|Cohen]] household, living in the East End of London, where [[Mordecai]] lodges and [[Mirah-Lapidoth|Mirah]] briefly stays. Though a minor character, her vivid presence contributes to the affectionate realism with which Eliot renders the domestic life of the Cohens.

## Introduction and Appearance

Adelaide Rebekah is formally introduced when Mrs. Cohen presents her children, and her appearance is described with detailed warmth:

> "Adelaide Rebekah is her name," said her mother, introducing the child [071-035].

> Adelaide Rebekah (her miniature crinoline and … [071-046]

The detail of the "miniature crinoline" captures both the family's modest aspiration to respectability and the narrator's affectionate eye for children's self-presentation.

## Speech and Character

Adelaide Rebekah speaks with the distinctive accent of the East End Jewish community, her dialogue rendered phonetically by Eliot to convey the family's linguistic world:

> lav'm Shabbes fyock on," said Adelaide Rebekah [071-037].

This brief utterance — referring to the Sabbath observance — grounds her in the rhythms of Jewish domestic practice that structure the Cohen household.

## Role in the Cohen Household

Adelaide Rebekah participates in the family's domestic rituals and is present at key moments of family gathering. She stands alongside the other family members during communal moments [071-183], and is doted upon by her grandmother:

> Adelaide Rebekah seated on the grandmother's k… [104-072]

Her emotional responses reveal the close bonds within the Cohen family. When her brother [[Jacob Cohen|Jacob]] is distressed, she responds with instinctive sympathy:

> Adelaide Rebekah always cried when her brother was in trouble [104-109].

This detail also appears in a lighter register when her brother deliberately frightens her:

> terrify Adelaide Rebekah in like manner, and succeeded… [086-185]

## At the Wedding-Feast

Adelaide Rebekah appears in the novel's culminating domestic scene — the wedding-feast of [[Mirah-Lapidoth|Mirah]] and [[Daniel-Deronda]] — dressed for the occasion alongside the rest of the Cohen family:

> while Adelaide Rebekah, in a new … [147-176]

Her presence at this gathering bookends her role as a consistent, warmly observed fixture of the Cohen domestic world.
