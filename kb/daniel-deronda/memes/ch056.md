[056-001]“Why?”

[056-002]“He would rather make me put it on than ask me to let him do it. Aha!

[056-003]he is very proud. But so am I. We shall match each other. I should hate

[056-004]a man who went down on his knees, and came fawning on me. He really is

[056-005]not disgusting.”

[056-006]“That is very moderate praise, Gwen.”

[056-007]“No, it is not, for a man,” said [[Gwendolen-Harleth|Gwendolen]] gaily. “But now I must get

[056-008]up and dress. Will you come and do my hair, mamma, dear,” she went on,

[056-009]drawing down her mamma’s face to caress it with her own cheeks, “and

[056-010]not be so naughty any more as to talk of living in poverty? You must

[056-011]bear to be made comfortable, even if you don’t like it. And Mr.

[056-012][[Henleigh-Grandcourt|Grandcourt]] behaves perfectly, now, does he not?”

[056-013]“Certainly he does,” said [[Mrs-Davilow|Mrs. Davilow]], encouraged, and persuaded that

[056-014]after all Gwendolen was fond of her betrothed. She herself thought him

[056-015]a man whose attentions were likely to tell on a girl’s feeling. Suitors

[056-016]must often be judged as words are, by the standing and the figure they

[056-017]make in polite society: it is difficult to know much else of them. And

[056-018]all the mother’s anxiety turned not on Grandcourt’s character, but on

[056-019]Gwendolen’s mood in accepting him.

[056-020]The mood was necessarily passing through a new phase this morning. Even

[056-021]in the hour of making her toilet, she had drawn on all the knowledge

[056-022]she had for grounds to justify her marriage. And what she most dwelt on

[056-023]was the determination, that when she was Grandcourt’s wife, she would

[056-024]urge him to the most liberal conduct toward [[Lydia-Glasher|Mrs. Glasher]]’s children.

[056-025]“Of what use would it be to her that I should not marry him? He could

[056-026]have married her if he liked; but he did _not_ like. Perhaps she is to

[056-027]blame for that. There must be a great deal about her that I know

[056-028]nothing of. And he must have been good to her in many ways, else she

[056-029]would not have wanted to marry him.”

[056-030]But that last argument at once began to appear doubtful. Mrs. Glasher

[056-031]naturally wished to exclude other children who would stand between

[056-032]Grandcourt and her own: and Gwendolen’s comprehension of this feeling

[056-033]prompted another way of reconciling claims.

[056-034]“Perhaps we shall have no children. I hope we shall not. And he might

[056-035]leave the estate to the pretty little boy. My uncle said that Mr.

[056-036]Grandcourt could do as he liked with the estates. Only when [[Sir-Hugo-Mallinger|Sir Hugo]]

[056-037]Mallinger dies there will be enough for two.”

[056-038]This made Mrs. Glasher appear quite unreasonable in demanding that her

[056-039]boy should be sole heir; and the double property was a security that

[056-040]Grandcourt’s marriage would do her no wrong, when the wife was

[056-041]Gwendolen Harleth with all her proud resolution not to be fairly

[056-042]accused. This maiden had been accustomed to think herself blameless;

[056-043]other persons only were faulty.

[056-044]It was striking, that in the hold which this argument of her doing no

[056-045]wrong to Mrs. Glasher had taken on her mind, her repugnance to the idea

[056-046]of Grandcourt’s past had sunk into a subordinate feeling. The terror

[056-047]she had felt in the night-watches at overstepping the border of

[056-048]wickedness by doing what she had at first felt to be wrong, had dulled

[056-049]any emotions about his conduct. She was thinking of him, whatever he

[056-050]might be, as a man over whom she was going to have indefinite power;

[056-051]and her loving him having never been a question with her, any

[056-052]agreeableness he had was so much gain. Poor Gwendolen had no awe of

[056-053]unmanageable forces in the state of matrimony, but regarded it as

[056-054]altogether a matter of management, in which she would know how to act.

[056-055]In relation to Grandcourt’s past she encouraged new doubts whether he

[056-056]were likely to have differed much from other men; and she devised

[056-057]little schemes for learning what was expected of men in general.

[056-058]But whatever else might be true in the world, her hair was dressed

[056-059]suitably for riding, and she went down in her riding-habit, to avoid

[056-060]delay before getting on horseback. She wanted to have her blood stirred

[056-061]once more with the intoxication of youth, and to recover the daring

[056-062]with which she had been used to think of her course in life. Already a

[056-063]load was lifted off her; for in daylight and activity it was less

[056-064]oppressive to have doubts about her choice, than to feel that she had

[056-065]no choice but to endure insignificance and servitude.

[056-066]“Go back and make yourself look like a duchess, mamma,” she said,

[056-067]turning suddenly as she was going down-stairs. “Put your point-lace

[056-068]over your head. I must have you look like a duchess. You must not take

[056-069]things humbly.”

[056-070]When Grandcourt raised her left hand gently and looked at the ring, she

[056-071]said gravely, “It was very good of you to think of everything and send

[056-072]me that packet.”

[056-073]“You will tell me if there is anything I forget?” he said, keeping the

[056-074]hand softly within his own. “I will do anything you wish.”

[056-075]“But I am very unreasonable in my wishes,” said Gwendolen, smiling.

[056-076]“Yes, I expect that. Women always are.”

[056-077]“Then I will not be unreasonable,” said Gwendolen, taking away her hand

[056-078]and tossing her head saucily. “I will not be told that I am what women

[056-079]always are.”

[056-080]“I did not say that,” said Grandcourt, looking at her with his usual

[056-081]gravity. “You are what no other woman is.”

[056-082]“And what is that, pray?” said Gwendolen, moving to a distance with a

[056-083]little air of menace.

[056-084]Grandcourt made his pause before he answered. “You are the woman I

[056-085]love.”

[056-086]“Oh, what nice speeches!” said Gwendolen, laughing. The sense of that

[056-087]love which he must once have given to another woman under strange

[056-088]circumstances was getting familiar.

[056-089]“Give me a nice speech in return. Say when we are to be married.”

[056-090]“Not yet. Not till we have had a gallop over the downs. I am so thirsty

[056-091]for that, I can think of nothing else. I wish the hunting had begun.

[056-092]Sunday the twentieth, twenty-seventh, Monday, Tuesday.” Gwendolen was

[056-093]counting on her fingers with the prettiest nod while she looked at

[056-094]Grandcourt, and at last swept one palm over the other while she said

[056-095]triumphantly, “It will begin in ten days!”

[056-096]“Let us be married in ten days, then,” said Grandcourt, “and we shall

[056-097]not be bored about the stables.”

[056-098]“What do women always say in answer to that?” said Gwendolen,

[056-099]mischievously.

[056-100]“They agree to it,” said the lover, rather off his guard.

[056-101]“Then I will not!” said Gwendolen, taking up her gauntlets and putting

[056-102]them on, while she kept her eyes on him with gathering fun in them.

[056-103]The scene was pleasant on both sides. A cruder lover would have lost

[056-104]the view of her pretty ways and attitudes, and spoiled all by stupid

[056-105]attempts at caresses, utterly destructive of drama. Grandcourt

[056-106]preferred the drama; and Gwendolen, left at ease, found her spirits

[056-107]rising continually as she played at reigning. Perhaps if [[Herr-Klesmer|Klesmer]] had

[056-108]seen more of her in this unconscious kind of acting, instead of when

[056-109]she was trying to be theatrical, he might have rated her chance higher.

[056-110]When they had had a glorious gallop, however, she was in a state of

[056-111]exhilaration that disposed her to think well of hastening the marriage

[056-112]which would make her life all of apiece with this splendid kind of

[056-113]enjoyment. She would not debate any more about an act to which she had

[056-114]committed herself; and she consented to fix the wedding on that day

[056-115]three weeks, notwithstanding the difficulty of fulfilling the customary

[056-116]laws of the _trousseau_.

[056-117][[Lush]], of course, was made aware of the engagement by abundant signs,

[056-118]without being formally told. But he expected some communication as a

[056-119]consequence of it, and after a few days he became rather impatient

[056-120]under Grandcourt’s silence, feeling sure that the change would affect

[056-121]his personal prospects, and wishing to know exactly how. His tactics no

[056-122]longer included any opposition—which he did not love for its own sake.

[056-123]He might easily cause Grandcourt a great deal of annoyance, but it

[056-124]would be to his own injury, and to create annoyance was not a motive

[056-125]with him. Miss Gwendolen he would certainly not have been sorry to

[056-126]frustrate a little, but—after all there was no knowing what would

[056-127]come. It was nothing new that Grandcourt should show a perverse

[056-128]wilfulness; yet in his freak about this girl he struck Lush rather

[056-129]newly as something like a man who was _fey_—led on by an ominous

[056-130]fatality; and that one born to his fortune should make a worse business

[056-131]of his life than was necessary, seemed really pitiable. Having

[056-132]protested against the marriage, Lush had a second-sight for its evil

[056-133]consequences. Grandcourt had been taking the pains to write letters and

[056-134]give orders himself instead of employing Lush, and appeared to be

[056-135]ignoring his usefulness, even choosing, against the habit of years, to

[056-136]breakfast alone in his dressing-room. But a _tête-à-tête_ was not to be

[056-137]avoided in a house empty of guests; and Lush hastened to use an

[056-138]opportunity of saying—it was one day after dinner, for there were

[056-139]difficulties in Grandcourt’s dining at [[Offendene]],

[056-140]“And when is the marriage to take place?”

[056-141]Grandcourt, who drank little wine, had left the table and was lounging,

[056-142]while he smoked, in an easy chair near the hearth, where a fire of oak

[056-143]boughs was gaping to its glowing depths, and edging them with a

[056-144]delicate tint of ashes delightful to behold. The chair of red-brown

[056-145]velvet brocade was a becoming background for his pale-tinted, well-cut

[056-146]features and exquisite long hands. Omitting the cigar, you might have

[056-147]imagined him a portrait by Moroni, who would have rendered wonderfully

[056-148]the impenetrable gaze and air of distinction; and a portrait by that

[056-149]great master would have been quite as lively a companion as Grandcourt

[056-150]was disposed to be. But he answered without unusual delay.

[056-151]“On the tenth.”

[056-152]“I suppose you intend to remain here.”

[056-153]“We shall go to [[ryelands|Ryelands]] for a little while; but we shall return here

[056-154]for the sake of the hunting.”

[056-155]After this word there was the languid inarticulate sound frequent with

[056-156]Grandcourt when he meant to continue speaking, and Lush waited for

[056-157]something more. Nothing came, and he was going to put another question,

[056-158]when the inarticulate sound began again and introduced the mildly

[056-159]uttered suggestion,

[056-160]“You had better make some new arrangement for yourself.”

[056-161]“What! I am to cut and run?” said Lush, prepared to be good-tempered on

[056-162]the occasion.

[056-163]“Something of that kind.”

[056-164]“The bride objects to me. I hope she will make up to you for the want

[056-165]of my services.”

[056-166]“I can’t help your being so damnably disagreeable to women,” said

[056-167]Grandcourt, in soothing apology.

[056-168]“To one woman, if you please.”

[056-169]“It makes no difference since she is the one in question.”

[056-170]“I suppose I am not to be turned adrift after fifteen years without

[056-171]some provision.”

[056-172]“You must have saved something out of me.”

[056-173]“Deuced little. I have often saved something for you.”

[056-174]“You can have three hundred a year. But you must live in town and be

[056-175]ready to look after things when I want you. I shall be rather hard up.”

[056-176]“If you are not going to be at Ryelands this winter, I might run down

[056-177]there and let you know how Swinton goes on.”

[056-178]“If you like. I don’t care a toss where you are, so that you keep out

[056-179]of sight.”

[056-180]“Much obliged,” said Lush, able to take the affair more easily than he

[056-181]had expected. He was supported by the secret belief that he should

[056-182]by-and-by be wanted as much as ever.

[056-183]“Perhaps you will not object to packing up as soon as possible,” said

[056-184]Grandcourt. “The Torringtons are coming, and Miss Harleth will be

[056-185]riding over here.”

[056-186]“With all my heart. Can’t I be of use in going to [[gadsmere|Gadsmere]]?”
