# Mrs. Davilow

Mrs. Davilow is the novel's most poignant study of maternal love without maternal authority — a woman who adores her daughter but cannot guide her, whose very softness has helped create the hardness in Gwendolen that will prove her undoing.

## Role in the Novel

Mrs. Davilow is present from the novel's early chapters as Gwendolen's widowed mother, economically dependent and emotionally subordinate. The family's financial ruin — revealed in the letter Gwendolen receives at Leubronn — is Mrs. Davilow's catastrophe too, but she bears it with the passive endurance that is her characteristic response to trouble. "That idea in Mrs. Davilow's mind" (017-151) about Grandcourt as a husband for Gwendolen captures her ambivalent role: she hopes for her daughter's security but lacks the moral clarity to warn her away from a dangerous match.

## Character Traits

### Personality
Mrs. Davilow is "gentle," "anxious," and "constitutionally incapable of asserting herself." Her love for her daughters is genuine but the love of a woman who has never learned to exercise authority — even maternal authority. She is, the novel suggests, what the Victorian marriage market produces when it values female docility above all else: a woman of perfect goodness and perfect ineffectuality.

### Appearance
Mrs. Davilow is "faded" — her physical softness reflecting her psychological softness. She is what Gwendolen might become if her will were broken: a gentle, diminished version of the formidable girl she once was.

## Relationships

- **Gwendolen Harleth**: Her eldest daughter, whom she loves without understanding. Mrs. Davilow sees Gwendolen's beauty and spirit but cannot see the moral vacuum beneath them. Her failure as a mother is not cruelty but insufficiency — she has never given Gwendolen the moral education the girl needed. (017-092)
- **Her other daughters**: Mrs. Davilow's younger children exist at the margins of the novel but testify to the economic precariousness of her position — a widow with children to support, dependent on relatives' goodwill.

## Character Arc

Mrs. Davilow does not change. Her function is to be what she has always been: a loving, helpless witness to her daughter's suffering. Her inability to protect Gwendolen — from poverty, from Grandcourt, from herself — is the novel's indictment not of Mrs. Davilow personally but of a social system that equips women for dependence rather than agency.

## Thematic Significance

Mrs. Davilow embodies the novel's critique of the Victorian ideal of womanhood. She is the perfect lady — gentle, self-effacing, devoted to her family — and she is entirely incapable of protecting herself or her children. Through her, Eliot argues that feminine virtue without feminine strength is not virtue at all but a form of vulnerability that exposes women to exploitation and their children to moral neglect.

## Key Scenes

- The letter revealing the family's financial ruin (002-137)
- "That idea in Mrs. Davilow's mind" — the hope of Grandcourt as a son-in-law (017-151)
- Her silent presence during Gwendolen's marriage — the witness who cannot act
- The aftermath of Grandcourt's death — mother and daughter, bound by love and mutual incomprehension

## Critical Reception

> Mrs. Davilow has been read as Eliot's critique of the "angel in the house" ideal — the Victorian cult of domestic femininity that produced women of perfect gentleness and perfect helplessness. Some feminist critics have seen her as a cautionary figure: what happens to women who are never taught to exercise their wills. Others read her more sympathetically as a realistic portrait of the constraints within which most Victorian women lived.
