# Deronda Visits the Synagogue

While traveling in Germany, Daniel Deronda feels drawn to enter a synagogue in Frankfort — a seemingly casual act that proves deeply significant when his Jewish heritage is later revealed.

## Context

Deronda has been raised as the ward of Sir Hugo Mallinger, unaware of his parentage. Throughout his youth, he has felt a vague sympathy toward Jews and an inexplicable curiosity about Jewish culture. Mirah tells him of her childhood: "my mother used to take me to the synagogue, and I remember standing by her knee" (038-094). This image stays with Deronda, strengthening his nascent interest in Jewish life.

## The Event

During his travels on the Continent, Deronda finds himself in Frankfort. Walking through the old Jewish quarter, he sees a synagogue and, "on an impulse," enters. The narrator notes that "he began to look for the outsides of synagogues, and the title of books about the Jews" (065-099). Inside, Deronda observes the service with a mixture of curiosity and an unaccountable sense of recognition. Though he does not understand Hebrew, the chanting and the congregation's devotion move him. He does not yet know that his own mother was Jewish and that the synagogue is, in a sense, his birthright.

## Participants

- **Daniel Deronda**: The protagonist, drawn by an unconscious pull toward Jewish worship, not yet knowing his own heritage (065-099)

## Consequences

The synagogue visit deepens Deronda's interest in Judaism. Combined with his encounters with Mordecai and Mirah, it prepares him emotionally for the revelation of his Jewish identity. The scene functions as dramatic irony: Deronda experiences Jewish worship as an outsider, but the reader (and the narrative) know this is a homecoming he cannot yet name. When Deronda later learns of his Jewish birth, the synagogue visit is retrospectively illuminated as an act of unconscious recognition.

## Thematic Significance

The synagogue scene develops the novel's exploration of inherited identity versus chosen identity. Deronda's attraction to the synagogue suggests that identity may operate below the level of conscious knowledge — that "blood" or heritage may exert a pull the mind does not recognize. Yet the novel is careful not to be reductive: Deronda's eventual embrace of Judaism is a conscious ethical choice, not merely the fulfillment of biology. The synagogue visit represents the mysterious interplay of nature and nurture that the novel probes throughout.

## Key Passages
