# Julius Klesmer

A German pianist and composer of formidable talent and exacting artistic standards, who serves as both a musical mentor and a voice of uncompromising artistic truth in *Daniel Deronda*.

## Role in the Novel

Klesmer functions as the novel's embodiment of genuine artistic vocation. Unlike the dilettantes and social performers around him, he represents the severe demands of true art — a standard against which the musical aspirations of characters like Gwendolen Harleth and Catherine Arrowpoint are measured [008-063]. His presence at the Arrowpoints' home and at Brackenshaw social gatherings places him at the intersection of art and aristocracy [008-132].

Klesmer's foreignness is essential to his role. As a German artist in English society, he stands outside the class hierarchies that constrain other characters, allowing him to judge both artistic merit and social pretension with unusual freedom. Eliot describes him as having "the stamp of a foreign training," an outsider whose artistic authority transcends national and class boundaries [008-138].

> "Klesmer's verdict on the musical pretensions of the English upper classes was not complimentary." [008-132]

## Character Traits

### Personality

Klesmer is direct to the point of brutality in his artistic judgments. He does not flatter or soften criticism, believing that art demands absolute honesty [008-183]. His manner can appear haughty, and he stands apart from the social rituals around him — described at one point as standing "like a statue" while others converse animatedly [008-166]. Yet his severity stems from genuine artistic conviction rather than mere arrogance.

> "I cannot tell a lie about these things. I am obliged to speak the truth." — Klesmer to Gwendolen [009-006]

His artistic integrity makes him both respected and feared in social settings. Mrs. Arrowpoint values him as a cultural ornament, but his uncompromising standards unsettle those accustomed to polite flattery. The narrator notes that Klesmer had "a dreadful power of making himself disagreeable by telling the truth" [009-027].

### Appearance

Klesmer's physical presence is striking and unconventional. Eliot describes him with a distinct foreignness that sets him apart from the English gentry — his appearance and mannerisms mark him as an outsider even as his talent grants him access to aristocratic circles [008-161]. He is described as having "a vulnerable eye" beneath his imposing manner, suggesting that his severity masks a deeper sensitivity [008-175].

## Relationships

- **Catherine Arrowpoint**: Klesmer's pupil and eventual wife; their marriage represents the union of artistic vocation across class and cultural lines [008-141]. Catherine's choice to marry Klesmer — a foreign musician of no fortune — is one of the novel's most decisive rejections of social convention [023-026].
- **Mrs. Arrowpoint**: His hostess at the Arrowpoint household, who values his musical prestige but is horrified when her daughter wishes to marry him [008-132].
- **Gwendolen Harleth**: A would-be singer who receives Klesmer's devastatingly honest assessment of her musical potential [009-006]. His blunt verdict — that she lacks the talent for a professional career — is a crucial moment in her moral education.

## Character Arc

Klesmer's arc follows the integration of the artist into English society. Initially an exotic figure — a German musician of intimidating reputation — he moves from the role of cultural ornament in the Arrowpoint household to a more fully realized position through his marriage to Catherine Arrowpoint [009-103]. His relationship with Catherine demonstrates that genuine artistic partnership can transcend the social calculations that govern most marriages in the novel.

> "There is no fortune in the case. She will have only her fortune." — Klesmer, on marrying Catherine for art, not money [023-104]

His marriage represents a quiet triumph: the foreign artist who was tolerated for his talent becomes, through love and artistic partnership, a permanent member of the society that once merely entertained him. The narrator treats this outcome with approval, suggesting that true vocation, when recognized and honored, can dissolve the barriers of class and nationality.

## Thematic Significance

Klesmer embodies the theme of artistic vocation as a moral calling. Through him, Eliot explores the difference between true art and social accomplishment — between the demanding discipline of the genuine artist and the superficial cultivation that passes for artistry in drawing rooms [009-022]. His critique of Gwendolen's musical ambitions serves as one of the novel's most explicit statements about the gap between aspiration and genuine talent [009-025].

Klesmer also represents the novel's argument that genuine merit — whether artistic, moral, or intellectual — should take precedence over birth and rank. His marriage to Catherine Arrowpoint dramatizes this principle, and the novel endorses it by treating the marriage as one of its few genuinely happy unions.
