# Milton's Paradise Lost

John Milton's *Paradise Lost* (1667) — the great English epic of Satan's rebellion and Adam and Eve's expulsion from Eden — is the poem that hovers behind *Daniel Deronda* as the supreme English treatment of moral choice, temptation, and the relationship between human freedom and providential design.

## Overview

Milton's epic, composed after the poet had gone blind and suffered political defeat with the Restoration of Charles II, retells the Genesis story of the Fall with a scope and moral complexity that made it the central work of English Protestant literary culture. For Victorian readers, *Paradise Lost* was the poem that justified God's ways to man — and that gave Satan, the arch-tempter, some of the most compelling poetry in the language. Its treatment of temptation as a psychological drama, its exploration of the relationship between knowledge and sin, and its vision of Providence working through human error all resonate through Eliot's novel.

## Role in the Novel

Milton's epic provides a theological and poetic template for Eliot's treatment of moral choice. Gwendolen Harleth's trajectory — temptation, fall, suffering, and the possibility of redemption — follows a Miltonic pattern, though Eliot's vision of redemption is secular and psychological rather than theological. The novel's treatment of gambling as a species of temptation echoes Milton's treatment of Satan's rebellion as the original gamble against divine order.

Mordecai's prophetic discourse also draws on Milton's fusion of poetry and prophecy. The blind poet who saw into the divine order is a figure for the consumptive scholar who envisions Jewish national revival — both are visionaries whose physical weakness paradoxically enables spiritual sight.

## Connection to Characters

### Gwendolen Harleth

Gwendolen's trajectory follows a Miltonic arc: tempted by the promise of worldly power, she falls through her own choice, and her suffering becomes the instrument of her moral education. Like Milton's Eve, Gwendolen discovers that the knowledge gained through transgression cannot be un-known (030-102).

### Mordecai

Mordecai, the blind visionary, claims Milton's lineage: the poet who saw beyond sight. His fusion of prophecy and poetry echoes Milton's achievement in *Paradise Lost* — a work that is simultaneously epic, theological treatise, and personal testament.

## Thematic Significance

Milton represents the possibility of religious poetry in a secular age — the model for Eliot's own ambition to write fiction that does the work once reserved for epic and scripture. *Paradise Lost* demonstrates that art can treat the largest questions of human existence without sacrificing psychological complexity.
