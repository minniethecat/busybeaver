# Schubert's Music

Franz Schubert's music — the songs and chamber works that Mirah Lapidoth performs with a sincerity that distinguishes her from the social performers around her — represents authentic artistic expression in a novel that treats most "accomplishment" as disguised ambition.

## Overview

Franz Schubert (1797–1828), the Viennese composer whose brief life produced over six hundred songs, symphonies, chamber works, and operas, was by the Victorian period recognized as one of the supreme melodists of European music. His *Lieder* — settings of German poetry for voice and piano — were central to the nineteenth-century domestic music culture that Eliot knew well, and they appear in *Daniel Deronda* as the repertoire through which genuine musical feeling is expressed.

## Role in the Novel

Schubert's music appears through Mirah Lapidoth's performances. Unlike Gwendolen, whose singing is a vehicle for social display, Mirah sings Schubert with an emotional authenticity that moves her listeners. The contrast is deliberate: Schubert represents music as art rather than accomplishment, as expression rather than exhibition. Klesmer, himself a German musician of serious artistic purpose, recognizes in Mirah's Schubert the genuine musicality that Gwendolen's drawing-room performances lack.

## Connection to Characters

### Mirah Lapidoth

Mirah's performance of Schubert demonstrates the authentic artistic feeling that distinguishes her from the novel's social performers. Her Schubert is not entertainment but expression — music as the language of genuine emotion (PN citations linking Mirah to musical performance).

### Herr Klesmer

Klesmer, as the arbiter of genuine musical value, recognizes Schubert's art as the standard against which all other musical claims in the novel are measured. His appreciation of Mirah's singing is, implicitly, an appreciation of her Schubert (PN citations linking Klesmer to musical standards).

## Thematic Significance

Schubert's music embodies the novel's ideal of artistic authenticity. Where Gwendolen's accomplishments serve the marriage market, Mirah's music serves expression. The distinction between social performance and genuine art — crystallized in the difference between Gwendolen at the piano and Mirah singing Schubert — is the novel's criterion for all forms of cultural value.
