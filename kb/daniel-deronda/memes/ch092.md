[092-001][[Daniel-Deronda|Deronda]] felt the blood mounting to his cheeks as a sort of rebuke he

[092-002]was little used to, and he also found himself painfully baffled where

[092-003]he had reckoned with some confidence on getting decisive knowledge. He

[092-004]became the more conscious of emotional strain from the excitements of

[092-005]the day; and although he had the money in his pocket to redeem his

[092-006]ring, he recoiled from the further task of a visit to the Cohens’,

[092-007]which must be made not only under the former uncertainty, but under a

[092-008]new disappointment as to the possibility of its removal.

[092-009]“I will part from you now,” he said, just before they could reach

[092-010]Cohen’s door; and [[Mordecai]] paused, looking up at him with an anxious

[092-011]fatigued face under the gaslight.

[092-012]“When will you come back?” he said, with slow emphasis.

[092-013]“May I leave that unfixed? May I ask for you at the Cohens’ any evening

[092-014]after your hour at the book-shop? There is no objection, I suppose, to

[092-015]their knowing that you and I meet in private?”

[092-016]“None,” said Mordecai. “But the days I wait now are longer than the

[092-017]years of my strength. Life shrinks: what was but a tithe is now the

[092-018]half. My hope abides in you.”

[092-019]“I will be faithful,” said Deronda—he could not have left those words

[092-020]unuttered. “I will come the first evening I can after seven: on

[092-021]Saturday or Monday, if possible. Trust me.”

[092-022]He put out his ungloved hand. Mordecai, clasping it eagerly, seemed to

[092-023]feel a new instreaming of confidence, and he said with some recovered

[092-024]energy—“This is come to pass, and the rest will come.”

[092-025]That was their good-by.

[092-026]BOOK VI.—REVELATIONS

[092-027]CHAPTER XLI.

[092-028]“This, too is probable, according to that saying of Agathon: ‘It is a

[092-029]part of probability that many improbable things will happen.’”

[092-030]—ARISTOTLE: _Poetics_.

[092-031]Imagine the conflict in a mind like Deronda’s given not only to feel

[092-032]strongly but to question actively, on the evening after the interview

[092-033]with Mordecai. To a young man of much duller susceptibilities the

[092-034]adventure might have seemed enough out of the common way to divide his

[092-035]thoughts; but it had stirred Deronda so deeply, that with the usual

[092-036]reaction of his intellect he began to examine the grounds of his

[092-037]emotion, and consider how far he must resist its guidance. The

[092-038]consciousness that he was half dominated by Mordecai’s energetic

[092-039]certitude, and still more by his fervent trust, roused his alarm. It

[092-040]was his characteristic bias to shrink from the moral stupidity of

[092-041]valuing lightly what had come close to him, and of missing blindly in

[092-042]his own life of to-day the crisis which he recognized as momentous and

[092-043]sacred in the historic life of men. If he had read of this incident as

[092-044]having happened centuries ago in Rome, Greece, Asia Minor, Palestine,

[092-045]Cairo, to some man young as himself, dissatisfied with his neutral

[092-046]life, and wanting some closer fellowship, some more special duty to

[092-047]give him ardor for the possible consequences of his work, it would have

[092-048]appeared to him quite natural that the incident should have created a

[092-049]deep impression on that far-off man, whose clothing and action would

[092-050]have been seen in his imagination as part of an age chiefly known to us

[092-051]through its more serious effects. Why should he be ashamed of his own

[092-052]agitated feeling merely because he dressed for dinner, wore a white

[092-053]tie, and lived among people who might laugh at his owning any

[092-054]conscience in the matter, as the solemn folly of taking himself too

[092-055]seriously?—that bugbear of circles in which the lack of grave emotion

[092-056]passes for wit. From such cowardice before modish ignorance and

[092-057]obtuseness, Deronda shrank. But he also shrank from having his course

[092-058]determined by mere contagion, without consent of reason; or from

[092-059]allowing a reverential pity for spiritual struggle to hurry him along a

[092-060]dimly-seen path.

[092-061]What, after all, had really happened? He knew quite accurately the

[092-062]answer [[Sir-Hugo-Mallinger|Sir Hugo]] would have given: “A consumptive Jew, possessed by a

[092-063]fanaticism which obstacles and hastening death intensified, had fixed

[092-064]on Deronda as the antitype of some visionary image, the offspring of

[092-065]wedded hope and despair: despair of his own life, irrepressible hope in

[092-066]the propagation of his fanatical beliefs. The instance was perhaps odd,

[092-067]exceptional in its form, but substantially it was not rare. Fanaticism

[092-068]was not so common as bankruptcy, but taken in all its aspects it was

[092-069]abundant enough. While Mordecai was waiting on the bridge for the

[092-070]fulfillment of his visions, another man was convinced that he had the

[092-071]mathematical key of the universe which would supersede Newton, and

[092-072]regarded all known physicists as conspiring to stifle his discovery and

[092-073]keep the universe locked; another, that he had the metaphysical key,

[092-074]with just that hair’s-breadth of difference from the old wards which

[092-075]would make it fit exactly. Scattered here and there in every direction

[092-076]you might find a terrible person, with more or less power of speech,

[092-077]and with an eye either glittering or preternaturally dull, on the

[092-078]look-out for the man who must hear him; and in most cases he had

[092-079]volumes which it was difficult to get printed, or if printed to get

[092-080]read. This Mordecai happened to have a more pathetic aspect, a more

[092-081]passionate, penetrative speech than was usual with such monomaniacs; he

[092-082]was more poetical than a social reformer with colored views of the new

[092-083]moral world in parallelograms, or than an enthusiast in sewage; still

[092-084]he came under the same class. It would be only right and kind to

[092-085]indulge him a little, to comfort him with such help as was practicable;

[092-086]but what likelihood was there that his notions had the sort of value he

[092-087]ascribed to them? In such cases a man of the world knows what to think

[092-088]beforehand. And as to Mordecai’s conviction that he had found a new

[092-089]executive self, it might be preparing for him the worst of

[092-090]disappointments—that which presents itself as final.”

[092-091]Deronda’s ear caught all these negative whisperings; nay, he repeated

[092-092]them distinctly to himself. It was not the first but it was the most

[092-093]pressing occasion on which he had had to face this question of the

[092-094]family likeness among the heirs of enthusiasm, whether prophets or

[092-095]dreamers of dreams, whether the

[092-096]“Great benefactors of mankind, deliverers,”

[092-097]or the devotees of phantasmal discovery—from the first believer in his

[092-098]own unmanifested inspiration, down to the last inventor of an ideal

[092-099]machine that will achieve perpetual motion. The kinship of human

[092-100]passion, the sameness of mortal scenery, inevitably fill fact with

[092-101]burlesque and parody. Error and folly have had their hecatombs of

[092-102]martyrs. Reduce the grandest type of man hitherto known to an abstract

[092-103]statement of his qualities and efforts, and he appears in dangerous

[092-104]company: say that, like Copernicus and Galileo, he was immovably

[092-105]convinced in the face of hissing incredulity; but so is the contriver

[092-106]of perpetual motion. We cannot fairly try the spirits by this sort of

[092-107]test. If we want to avoid giving the dose of hemlock or the sentence of

[092-108][[exile|banishment]] in the wrong case, nothing will do but a capacity to

[092-109]understand the subject-matter on which the immovable man is convinced,

[092-110]and fellowship with human travail, both near and afar, to hinder us

[092-111]from scanning any deep experience lightly. Shall we say, “Let the ages

[092-112]try the spirits, and see what they are worth?” Why, we are the

[092-113]beginning of the ages, which can only be just by virtue of just

[092-114]judgments in separate human breasts—separate yet combined. Even

[092-115]steam-engines could not have got made without that condition, but must

[092-116]have stayed in the mind of James Watt.

[092-117]This track of thinking was familiar enough to Deronda to have saved him

[092-118]from any contemptuous prejudgment of Mordecai, even if their

[092-119]communication had been free from that peculiar claim on himself

[092-120]strangely ushered in by some long-growing preparation in the Jew’s

[092-121]agitated mind. This claim, indeed, considered in what is called a

[092-122]rational way, might seem justifiably dismissed as illusory and even

[092-123]preposterous; but it was precisely what turned Mordecai’s hold on him

[092-124]from an appeal to his ready sympathy into a clutch on his struggling

[092-125]conscience. Our consciences are not all of the same pattern, an inner

[092-126]deliverance of fixed laws: they are the voice of sensibilities as

[092-127]various as our memories (which also have their kinship and likeness).

[092-128]And Deronda’s conscience included sensibilities beyond the common,

[092-129]enlarged by his early habit of thinking himself imaginatively into the

[092-130]experience of others.

[092-131]What was the claim this eager soul made upon him?—“You must believe my

[092-132]beliefs—be moved by my reasons—hope my hopes—see the vision I point

[092-133]to—behold a glory where I behold it!” To take such a demand in the

[092-134]light of an [[duty|obligation]] in any direct sense would have been

[092-135]preposterous—to have seemed to admit it would have been dishonesty;

[092-136]and Deronda, looking on the agitation of those moments, felt thankful

[092-137]that in the midst of his [[sympathy|compassion]] he had preserved himself from the

[092-138]bondage of false concessions. The claim hung, too, on a supposition

[092-139]which might be—nay, probably was—in discordance with the full fact:

[092-140]the supposition that he, Deronda, was of Jewish blood. Was there ever a

[092-141]more hypothetic appeal?

[092-142]But since the age of thirteen Deronda had associated the deepest

[092-143]experience of his affections with what was a pure supposition, namely,

[092-144]that Sir Hugo was his father: that was a hypothesis which had been the

[092-145]source of passionate struggle within him; by its light he had been

[092-146]accustomed to subdue feelings and to cherish them. He had been well

[092-147]used to find a motive in a conception which might be disproved; and he

[092-148]had been also used to think of some revelation that might influence his

[092-149]view of the particular duties belonging to him. To be in a state of

[092-150]suspense, which was also one of emotive activity and scruple, was a

[092-151]familiar attitude of his conscience.

[092-152]And now, suppose that wish-begotten belief in his Jewish birth, and

[092-153]that extravagant demand of discipleship, to be the foreshadowing of an

[092-154]actual discovery and a genuine spiritual result: suppose that

[092-155]Mordecai’s ideas made a real conquest over Deronda’s conviction? Nay,

[092-156]it was conceivable that as Mordecai needed and believed that, he had

[092-157]found an active replenishment of himself, so Deronda might receive from

[092-158]Mordecai’s mind the complete ideal shape of that personal duty and

[092-159]citizenship which lay in his own thought like sculptured fragments

[092-160]certifying some beauty yearned after but not traceable by divination.

[092-161]As that possibility presented itself in his meditations, he was aware

[092-162]that it would be called dreamy, and began to defend it. If the

[092-163]influence he imagined himself submitting to had been that of some

[092-164]honored professor, some authority in a seat of learning, some

[092-165]philosopher who had been accepted as a voice of the age, would a

[092-166]thorough receptiveness toward direction have been ridiculed? Only by

[092-167]those who hold it a sign of weakness to be obliged for an idea, and

[092-168]prefer to hint that they have implicitly held in a more correct form

[092-169]whatever others have stated with a sadly short-coming explicitness.

[092-170]After all, what was there but vulgarity in taking the fact that

[092-171]Mordecai was a poor Jewish workman, and that he was to be met perhaps

[092-172]on a sanded floor in the parlor of the _[[hand-and-banner|Hand and Banner]]_ as a reason

[092-173]for determining beforehand that there was not some spiritual force

[092-174]within him that might have a determining effect on a white-handed

[092-175]gentleman? There is a legend told of the Emperor Domitian, that having

[092-176]heard of a Jewish family, of the house of David, whence the ruler of

[092-177]the world was to spring, he sent for its members in alarm, but quickly

[092-178]released them on observing that they had the hands of

[092-179]work-people—being of just the opposite opinion with that Rabbi who

[092-180]stood waiting at the gate of Rome in confidence that the Messiah would

[092-181]be found among the destitute who entered there. Both Emperor and Rabbi

[092-182]were wrong in their trust of outward signs: poverty and poor clothes

[092-183]are no sign of inspiration, said Deronda to his inward objector, but

[092-184]they have gone with it in some remarkable cases. And to regard

[092-185]discipleship as out of the question because of them, would be mere

[092-186]dullness of imagination.
