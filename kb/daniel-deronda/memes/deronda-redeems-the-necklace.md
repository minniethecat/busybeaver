# Deronda Redeems the Necklace

After rescuing Mirah Lapidoth from drowning, Daniel Deronda learns that she had been forced by poverty to pawn a treasured necklace. He traces it to the pawnbroker, redeems it, and arranges for its anonymous return through Mrs. Meyrick.

## Context

After rescuing Mirah from the Thames, Deronda takes her to the Meyrick household in Chelsea. Learning of Mirah's circumstances — she fled her father's attempt to sell her into a theatrical career and arrived in London destitute — Deronda feels responsible for her welfare. He discovers that Mirah "had pawned" a necklace "against her inclination" and that "some one else had redeemed" it (028-101, 028-102). This act of anonymous kindness becomes the first in a series of Deronda's benevolent interventions.

## The Event

Deronda traces the pawned necklace through London's Jewish quarter. The search leads him to a pawnbroker's shop of the kind "where the lead is given to jewelry, lace and all equivocal objects" (069-038). The shop window announces "Watches and Jewelry exchanged and repaired" (069-041). Through methodical inquiry, Deronda locates the necklace, redeems it with his own money, and brings it to Mrs. Meyrick with instructions that Mirah should not know the source of the redemption. The act is deliberately anonymous — Deronda wishes to help without creating a sense of obligation.

## Participants

- **Daniel Deronda**: The anonymous benefactor who traces and redeems the necklace, acting from a sense of moral duty (028-101)
- **Mirah Lapidoth**: The beneficiary, unaware that Deronda has redeemed her treasured possession
- **Mrs. Meyrick**: The intermediary who returns the necklace to Mirah without revealing Deronda's role

## Consequences

The redemption of the necklace deepens Deronda's commitment to Mirah's welfare. His anonymous approach — helping without claiming gratitude — establishes a pattern of moral agency that defines his character throughout the novel. For Mirah, the mysterious return of her necklace becomes a sign that providence has not abandoned her, reinforcing her faith. The incident also leads Deronda further into the Jewish quarter of London, initiating the series of encounters that will ultimately reveal his own heritage.

## Thematic Significance

The necklace redemption encapsulates the novel's ethics of sympathetic action. Deronda acts not from romantic interest but from what the narrator calls his "ready-ness to devote" himself to others (033-030). The anonymity of the act distinguishes it from the performative charity that the novel critiques. The necklace itself — a small, personal object — symbolizes the intimate connection between material circumstance and human dignity.

## Key Passages

> Mirah had pawned "against her inclination, a necklace which she had pawned and some one else had redeemed." (028-101, 028-102)

> The pawnbroker's shop was "that kind of pawnbroker's where the lead is given to jewelry, lace and all equivocal objects" (069-038)
