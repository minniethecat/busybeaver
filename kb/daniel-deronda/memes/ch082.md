[082-001][[Daniel-Deronda|Deronda]] paused, but [[Gwendolen-Harleth|Gwendolen]], looking startled and thrilled as by an

[082-002]electric shock, said nothing, and he went on more insistently,

[082-003]“I take what you said of [[art|music]] for a small example—it answers for all

[082-004]larger things—you will not cultivate it for the sake of a private joy

[082-005]in it. What sort of earth or heaven would hold any spiritual wealth in

[082-006]it for souls pauperized by inaction? If one firmament has no stimulus

[082-007]for our attention and awe, I don’t see how four would have it. We

[082-008]should stamp every possible world with the flatness of our own

[082-009]inanity—which is necessarily impious, without [[religion|faith]] or fellowship. The

[082-010]refuge you are needing from personal trouble is the higher, the

[082-011]religious life, which holds an enthusiasm for something more than our

[082-012]own appetites and vanities. The few may find themselves in it simply by

[082-013]an elevation of feeling; but for us who have to struggle for our

[082-014]wisdom, the higher life must be a region in which the affections are

[082-015]clad with knowledge.”

[082-016]The half-indignant remonstrance that vibrated in Deronda’s voice came,

[082-017]as often happens, from the habit of inward argument with himself rather

[082-018]than from severity toward Gwendolen: but it had a more beneficial

[082-019]effect on her than any soothings. Nothing is feebler than the indolent

[082-020]rebellion of complaint; and to be roused into self-judgment is

[082-021]comparative activity. For the moment she felt like a shaken

[082-022]child—shaken out of its wailing into awe, and she said humbly,

[082-023]“I will try. I will think.”

[082-024]They both stood silent for a minute, as if some third presence had

[082-025]arrested them,—for Deronda, too, was under that sense of pressure

[082-026]which is apt to come when our own winged words seem to be hovering

[082-027]around us,—till Gwendolen began again,

[082-028]“You said affection was the best thing, and I have hardly any—none

[082-029]about me. If I could, I would have mamma; but that is impossible.

[082-030]Things have changed to me so—in such a short time. What I used not to

[082-031]like I long for now. I think I am almost getting fond of the old things

[082-032]now they are gone.” Her lip trembled.

[082-033]“Take the present suffering as a painful letting in of light,” said

[082-034]Deronda, more gently. “You are conscious of more beyond the round of

[082-035]your own inclinations—you know more of the way in which your life

[082-036]presses on others, and their life on yours. I don’t think you could

[082-037]have escaped the painful process in some form or other.”

[082-038]“But it is a very cruel form,” said Gwendolen, beating her foot on the

[082-039]ground with returning agitation. “I am frightened at everything. I am

[082-040]frightened at myself. When my blood is fired I can do daring

[082-041]things—take any leap; but that makes me frightened at myself.” She was

[082-042]looking at nothing outside her; but her eyes were directed toward the

[082-043]window, away from Deronda, who, with quick comprehension said,

[082-044]“Turn your fear into a safeguard. Keep your dread fixed on the idea of

[082-045]increasing that [[conscience|remorse]] which is so bitter to you. Fixed meditation may

[082-046]do a great deal toward defining our longing or dread. We are not always

[082-047]in a state of strong emotion, and when we are calm we can use our

[082-048]memories and gradually change the bias of our fear, as we do our

[082-049]tastes. Take your fear as a safeguard. It is like quickness of hearing.

[082-050]It may make consequences passionately present to you. Try to take hold

[082-051]of your sensibility, and use it as if it were a faculty, like vision.”

[082-052]Deronda uttered each sentence more urgently; he felt as if he were

[082-053]seizing a faint chance of rescuing her from some indefinite danger.

[082-054]“Yes, I know; I understand what you mean,” said Gwendolen in her loud

[082-055]whisper, not turning her eyes, but lifting up her small gloved hand and

[082-056]waving it in deprecation of the notion that it was easy to obey that

[082-057]advice. “But if feelings rose—there are some feelings—hatred and

[082-058]anger—how can I be good when they keep rising? And if there came a

[082-059]moment when I felt stifled and could bear it no longer——” She broke

[082-060]off, and with agitated lips looked at Deronda, but the expression on

[082-061]his face pierced her with an entirely new feeling. He was under the

[082-062]baffling difficulty of discerning that what he had been urging on her

[082-063]was thrown into the pallid distance of mere thought before the outburst

[082-064]of her habitual emotion. It was as if he saw her drowning while his

[082-065]limbs were bound. The pained [[sympathy|compassion]] which was spread over his

[082-066]features as he watched her, affected her with a compunction unlike any

[082-067]she had felt before, and in a changed and imploring tone she said,

[082-068]“I am grieving you. I am ungrateful. You _can_ help me. I will think of

[082-069]everything. I will try. Tell me—it will not be a pain to you that I

[082-070]have dared to speak of my trouble to you? You began it, you know, when

[082-071]you rebuked me.” There was a melancholy smile on her lips as she said

[082-072]that, but she added more entreatingly, “It will not be a pain to you?”

[082-073]“Not if it does anything to save you from an evil to come,” said

[082-074]Deronda, with strong emphasis; “otherwise, it will be a lasting pain.”

[082-075]“No—no—it shall not be. It may be—it shall be better with me because

[082-076]I have known you.” She turned immediately, and quitted the room.

[082-077]When she was on the first landing of the staircase, [[Sir-Hugo-Mallinger|Sir Hugo]] passed

[082-078]across the hall on his way to the library, and saw her. [[Henleigh-Grandcourt|Grandcourt]] was

[082-079]not with him.

[082-080]Deronda, when the baronet entered, was standing in his ordinary

[082-081]attitude, grasping his coat-collar, with his back to the table, and

[082-082]with that indefinable expression by which we judge that a man is still

[082-083]in the shadow of a scene which he has just gone through. He moved,

[082-084]however, and began to arrange the letters.

[082-085]“Has Mrs. Grandcourt been in here?” said Sir Hugo.

[082-086]“Yes, she has.”

[082-087]“Where are the others?”

[082-088]“I believe she left them somewhere in the grounds.”

[082-089]After a moment’s silence, in which Sir Hugo looked at a letter without

[082-090]reading it, he said “I hope you are not playing with fire, Dan—you

[082-091]understand me?”

[082-092]“I believe I do, sir,” said Deronda, after a slight hesitation, which

[082-093]had some repressed anger in it. “But there is nothing answering to your

[082-094]metaphor—no fire, and therefore no chance of scorching.”

[082-095]Sir Hugo looked searchingly at him, and then said, “So much the better.

[082-096]For, between ourselves, I fancy there may be some hidden gunpowder in

[082-097]that establishment.”

[082-098]CHAPTER XXXVII.

[082-099]_Aspern._ Pardon, my lord—I speak for Sigismund.

[082-100]_Fronsberg._ For him? Oh, ay—for him I always hold

[082-101]A pardon safe in bank, sure he will draw

[082-102]Sooner or later on me. What his need?

[082-103]Mad project broken? fine mechanic wings

[082-104]That would not fly? durance, assault on watch,

[082-105]Bill for Epernay, not a crust to eat?

[082-106]_Aspern._ Oh, none of these, my lord; he has escaped

[082-107]From Circe’s herd, and seeks to win the love

[082-108]Of your fair ward Cecilia: but would win

[082-109]First your consent. You frown.

[082-110]_Fronsberg._ Distinguish words.

[082-111]I said I held a pardon, not consent.

[082-112]In spite of Deronda’s reasons for wishing to be in town again—reasons

[082-113]in which his anxiety for [[Mirah-Lapidoth|Mirah]] was blent with curiosity to know more of

[082-114]the enigmatic [[Mordecai]]—he did not manage to go up before Sir Hugo, who

[082-115]preceded his family that he might be ready for the opening of

[082-116]Parliament on the sixth of February. Deronda took up his quarters in

[082-117]Park Lane, aware that his chambers were sufficiently tenanted by [[Hans-Meyrick|Hans]]

[082-118]Meyrick. This was what he expected; but he found other things not

[082-119]altogether according to his expectations.

[082-120]Most of us remember Retzsch’s drawing of destiny in the shape of

[082-121]Mephistopheles playing at chess with man for his soul, a game in which

[082-122]we may imagine the clever adversary making a feint of unintended moves

[082-123]so as to set the beguiled mortal on carrying his defensive pieces away

[082-124]from the true point of attack. The fiend makes preparation his favorite

[082-125]object of mockery, that he may fatally persuade us against our taking

[082-126]out waterproofs when he is well aware the sky is going to clear,

[082-127]foreseeing that the imbecile will turn this delusion into a prejudice

[082-128]against waterproofs instead of giving a closer study to the

[082-129]weather-signs. It is a peculiar test of a man’s mettle when, after he

[082-130]has painfully adjusted himself to what seems a wise provision, he finds

[082-131]all his mental precaution a little beside the mark, and his excellent

[082-132]intentions no better than miscalculated dovetails, accurately cut from

[082-133]a wrong starting-point. His magnanimity has got itself ready to meet

[082-134]misbehavior, and finds quite a different call upon it. Something of

[082-135]this kind happened to Deronda.

[082-136]His first impression was one of pure pleasure and amusement at finding

[082-137]his sitting-room transformed into an _atelier_ strewed with

[082-138]miscellaneous drawings and with the contents of two chests from Rome,

[082-139]the lower half of the windows darkened with baize, and the blonde Hans

[082-140]in his weird youth as the presiding genius of the littered place—his

[082-141]hair longer than of old, his face more whimsically creased, and his

[082-142]high voice as usual getting higher under the excitement of rapid talk.

[082-143]The friendship of the two had been kept up warmly since the memorable

[082-144]Cambridge time, not only by correspondence but by little episodes of

[082-145]companionship abroad and in England, and the original relation of

[082-146]confidence on one side and indulgence on the other had been developed

[082-147]in practice, as is wont to be the case where such spiritual borrowing

[082-148]and lending has been well begun.

[082-149]“I knew you would like to see my casts and antiquities,” said Hans,

[082-150]after the first hearty greetings and inquiries, “so I didn’t scruple to

[082-151]unlade my chests here. But I’ve found two rooms at Chelsea not many

[082-152]hundred yards from my mother and sisters, and I shall soon be ready to

[082-153]hang out there—when they’ve scraped the walls and put in some new

[082-154]lights. That’s all I’m waiting for. But you see I don’t wait to begin

[082-155]work: you can’t conceive what a great fellow I’m going to be. The seed

[082-156]of immortality has sprouted within me.”

[082-157]“Only a fungoid growth, I dare say—a growing disease in the lungs,”

[082-158]said Deronda, accustomed to treat Hans in brotherly fashion. He was

[082-159]walking toward some drawings propped on the ledge of his bookcases;

[082-160]five rapidly-sketched heads—different aspects of the same face. He

[082-161]stood at a convenient distance from them, without making any remark.

[082-162]Hans, too, was silent for a minute, took up his palette and began

[082-163]touching the picture on his easel.

[082-164]“What do you think of them?” he said at last.

[082-165]“The full face looks too massive; otherwise the likenesses are good,”

[082-166]said Deronda, more coldly than was usual with him.

[082-167]“No, it is not too massive,” said Hans, decisively. “I have noted that.

[082-168]There is always a little surprise when one passes from the profile to

[082-169]the full face. But I shall enlarge her scale for Berenice. I am making

[082-170]a Berenice series—look at the sketches along there—and now I think of

[082-171]it, you are just the model I want for the Agrippa.” Hans, still with

[082-172]pencil and palette in hand, had moved to Deronda’s side while he said

[082-173]this, but he added hastily, as if conscious of a mistake, “No, no, I

[082-174]forgot; you don’t like sitting for your portrait, confound you!

[082-175]However, I’ve picked up a capital Titus. There are to be five in the

[082-176]series. The first is Berenice clasping the knees of Gessius Florus and

[082-177]beseeching him to spare her people; I’ve got that on the easel. Then,

[082-178]this, where she is standing on the Xystus with Agrippa, entreating the

[082-179]people not to injure themselves by resistance.”

[082-180]“Agrippa’s legs will never do,” said Deronda.

[082-181]“The legs are good realistically,” said Hans, his face creasing drolly;

[082-182]“public men are often shaky about the legs—’ Their legs, the emblem of

[082-183]their various thought,’ as somebody says in the _Rehearsal._”
