[096-001][[Mordecai]] threw himself back in his chair, and there was a moment’s

[096-002]silence. Not one member of the club shared his point of view or his

[096-003]emotion; but his whole personality and speech had on them the effect of

[096-004]a dramatic representation which had some pathos in it, though no

[096-005]practical consequences; and usually he was at once indulged and

[096-006]contradicted. [[Daniel-Deronda|Deronda]]’s mind went back upon what must have been the

[096-007]tragic pressure of outward conditions hindering this man, whose force

[096-008]he felt to be telling on himself, from making any world for his thought

[096-009]in the minds of others—like a poet among people of a strange speech,

[096-010]who may have a poetry of their own, but have no ear for his cadence, no

[096-011]answering thrill to his discovery of the latent virtues in his mother

[096-012]tongue.

[096-013]The cool Buchan was the first to speak, and hint the loss of time. “I

[096-014]submit,” said he, “that ye’re traveling away from the questions I put

[096-015]concerning progress.”

[096-016]“Say they’re levanting, Buchan,” said Miller, who liked his joke, and

[096-017]would not have objected to be called Voltairian. “Never mind. Let us

[096-018]have a Jewish night; we’ve not had one for a long while. Let us take

[096-019]the discussion on Jewish ground. I suppose we’ve no prejudice here;

[096-020]we’re all philosophers; and we like our friends Mordecai, Pash, and

[096-021]Gideon, as well as if they were no more kin to Abraham than the rest of

[096-022]us. We’re all related through Adam, until further showing to the

[096-023]contrary, and if you look into history we’ve all got some discreditable

[096-024]forefathers. So I mean no offence when I say I don’t think any great

[096-025]things of the part the Jewish people have played in the world. What

[096-026]then? I think they were iniquitously dealt by in past times. And I

[096-027]suppose we don’t want any men to be maltreated, white, black, brown, or

[096-028]yellow—I know I’ve just given my half-crown to the contrary. And that

[096-029]reminds me, I’ve a curious old German book—I can’t read it myself, but

[096-030]a friend of mine was reading out of it to me the other day—about the

[096-031]prejudices against the Jews, and the stories used to be told against

[096-032]’em, and what do you think one was? Why, that they’re punished with a

[096-033]bad odor in their bodies; and _that_, says the author, date 1715 (I’ve

[096-034]just been pricing and marking the book this very morning)—that is

[096-035]true, for the ancients spoke of it. But then, he says, the other things

[096-036]are fables, such as that the odor goes away all at once when they’re

[096-037]baptized, and that every one of the ten tribes, mind you, all the ten

[096-038]being concerned in the crucifixion, has got a particular punishment

[096-039]over and above the smell:—Asher, I remember, has the right arm a

[096-040]handbreadth shorter than the left, and Naphthali has pig’s ears and a

[096-041]smell of live pork. What do you think of that? There’s been a good deal

[096-042]of fun made of rabbinical fables, but in point of fables my opinion is,

[096-043]that all over the world it’s six of one and half-a-dozen of the other.

[096-044]However, as I said before, I hold with the philosophers of the last

[096-045]century that the Jews have played no great part as a people, though

[096-046]Pash will have it they’re clever enough to beat all the rest of the

[096-047]world. But if so, I ask, why haven’t they done it?”

[096-048]“For the same reason that the cleverest men in the country don’t get

[096-049]themselves or their ideas into Parliament,” said the ready Pash;

[096-050]“because the blockheads are too many for ’em.”

[096-051]“That is a vain question,” said Mordecai, “whether our people would

[096-052]beat the rest of the world. Each nation has its own work, and is a

[096-053]member of the world, enriched by the work of each. But it is true, as

[096-054]Jehuda-ha-Levi first said, that Israel is the heart of mankind, if we

[096-055]mean by heart the core of affection which binds a race and its families

[096-056]in dutiful love, and the reverence for the human body which lifts the

[096-057]needs of our animal life into religion, and the tenderness which is

[096-058]merciful to the poor and weak and to the dumb creature that wears the

[096-059]yoke for us.”

[096-060]“They’re not behind any nation in arrogance,” said Lily; “and if they

[096-061]have got in the rear, it has not been because they were over-modest.”

[096-062]“Oh, every nation brags in its turn,” said Miller.

[096-063]“Yes,” said Pash, “and some of them in the Hebrew text.”

[096-064]“Well, whatever the Jews contributed at one time, they are a

[096-065]stand-still people,” said Lily. “They are the type of obstinate

[096-066]adherence to the superannuated. They may show good abilities when they

[096-067]take up liberal ideas, but as a race they have no development in them.”

[096-068]“That is false!” said Mordecai, leaning forward again with his former

[096-069]eagerness. “Let their history be known and examined; let the seed be

[096-070]sifted, let its beginning be traced to the weed of the wilderness—the

[096-071]more glorious will be the energy that transformed it. Where else is

[096-072]there a nation of whom it may be as truly said that their religion and

[096-073]law and moral life mingled as the stream of blood in the heart and made

[096-074]one growth—where else a people who kept and enlarged their spiritual

[096-075]store at the very time when they are hated with a hatred as fierce as

[096-076]the forest fires that chase the wild beast from his covert? There is a

[096-077]fable of the Roman, that swimming to save his life he held the roll of

[096-078]his writings between his teeth and saved them from the waters. But how

[096-079]much more than that is true of our race? They struggled to keep their

[096-080]place among the nations like heroes—yea, when the hand was hacked off,

[096-081]they clung with their teeth; but when the plow and the harrow had

[096-082]passed over the last visible signs of their national covenant, and the

[096-083]fruitfulness of their land was stifled with the blood of the sowers and

[096-084]planters, they said, ‘The spirit is alive, let us make it a lasting

[096-085]habitation—lasting because movable—so that it may be carried from

[096-086]generation to generation, and our sons unborn may be rich in the things

[096-087]that have been, and possess a hope built on an unchangeable

[096-088]foundation.’ They said it and they wrought it, though often breathing

[096-089]with scant life, as in a coffin, or as lying wounded amid a heap of

[096-090]slain. Hooted and scared like the unknown dog, the Hebrew made himself

[096-091]envied for his wealth and wisdom, and was bled of them to fill the bath

[096-092]of Gentile luxury; he absorbed knowledge, he diffused it; his dispersed

[096-093]race was a new Phoenicia working the mines of Greece and carrying their

[096-094]products to the world. The native spirit of our tradition was not to

[096-095]stand still, but to use records as a seed and draw out the compressed

[096-096]virtues of law and prophecy; and while the Gentile, who had said, ‘What

[096-097]is yours is ours, and no longer yours,’ was reading the letter of our

[096-098]law as a dark inscription, or was turning its parchments into

[096-099]shoe-soles for an army rabid with lust and cruelty, our Masters were

[096-100]still enlarging and illuminating with fresh-fed interpretation. But the

[096-101]dispersion was wide, the yoke of oppression was a spiked torture as

[096-102]well as a load; the exile was forced afar among brutish people, where

[096-103]the consciousness of his race was no clearer to him than the light of

[096-104]the sun to our fathers in the Roman persecution, who had their

[096-105]hiding-place in a cave, and knew not that it was day save by the dimmer

[096-106]burning of their candles. What wonder that multitudes of our people are

[096-107]ignorant, narrow, superstitious? What wonder?”

[096-108]Here Mordecai, whose seat was next the fire-place, rose and leaned his

[096-109]arm on the little shelf; his excitement had risen, though his voice,

[096-110]which had begun with unusual strength, was getting hoarser.

[096-111]“What wonder? The night is unto them, that they have no vision; in

[096-112]their darkness they are unable to divine; the sun is gone down over the

[096-113]prophets, and the day is dark above them; their observances are as

[096-114]nameless relics. But which among the chief of the Gentile nations has

[096-115]not an ignorant multitude? They scorn our people’s ignorant observance;

[096-116]but the most accursed ignorance is that which has no observance—sunk

[096-117]to the cunning greed of the fox, to which all law is no more than a

[096-118]trap or the cry of the worrying hound. There is a degradation deep down

[096-119]below the memory that has withered into superstition. In the multitudes

[096-120]of the ignorant on three continents who observe our rites and make the

[096-121]confession of the divine Unity, the soul of [[religion|Judaism]] is not dead. Revive

[096-122]the organic centre: let the unity of Israel which has made the growth

[096-123]and form of its religion be an outward reality. Looking toward a land

[096-124]and a polity, our dispersed people in all the ends of the earth may

[096-125]share the dignity of a national life which has a voice among the

[096-126]peoples of the East and the West—which will plant the wisdom and skill

[096-127]of our race so that it may be, as of old, a medium of transmission and

[096-128]understanding. Let that come to pass, and the living warmth will spread

[096-129]to the weak extremities of Israel, and superstition will vanish, not in

[096-130]the lawlessness of the renegade, but in the illumination of great facts

[096-131]which widen feeling, and make all knowledge alive as the young

[096-132]offspring of beloved memories.”

[096-133]Mordecai’s voice had sunk, but with the hectic brilliancy of his gaze

[096-134]it was not the less impressive. His extraordinary excitement was

[096-135]certainly due to Deronda’s presence: it was to Deronda that he was

[096-136]speaking, and the moment had a testamentary solemnity for him which

[096-137]rallied all his powers. Yet the presence of those other familiar men

[096-138]promoted expression, for they embodied the indifference which gave a

[096-139]resistant energy to his speech. Not that he looked at Deronda: he

[096-140]seemed to see nothing immediately around him, and if any one had

[096-141]grasped him he would probably not have known it. Again the former words

[096-142]came back to Deronda’s mind,—“You must hope my hopes—see the vision I

[096-143]point to—behold a glory where I behold it.” They came now with

[096-144]gathered pathos. Before him stood, as a living, suffering reality, what

[096-145]hitherto he had only seen as an effort of imagination, which, in its

[096-146]comparative faintness, yet carried a suspicion, of being exaggerated: a

[096-147]man steeped in poverty and obscurity, weakened by disease, consciously

[096-148]within the shadow of advancing death, but living an intense life in an

[096-149]invisible past and future, careless of his personal lot, except for its

[096-150]possible making some obstruction to a conceived good which he would

[096-151]never share except as a brief inward vision—a day afar off, whose sun

[096-152]would never warm him, but into which he threw his soul’s desire, with a

[096-153]passion often wanting to the personal motives of healthy youth. It was

[096-154]something more than a grandiose transfiguration of the parental love

[096-155]that toils, renounces, endures, resists the suicidal promptings of

[096-156]despair—all because of the little ones, whose future becomes present

[096-157]to the yearning gaze of anxiety.

[096-158]All eyes were fixed on Mordecai as he sat down again, and none with

[096-159]unkindness; but it happened that the one who felt the most kindly was

[096-160]the most prompted to speak in opposition. This was the genial and

[096-161]rational Gideon, who also was not without a sense that he was

[096-162]addressing the guest of the evening. He said,

[096-163]“You have your own way of looking at things, Mordecai, and as you say,

[096-164]your own way seems to you rational. I know you don’t hold with the

[096-165]restoration of Judea by miracle, and so on; but you are as well aware

[096-166]as I am that the subject has been mixed with a heap of nonsense both by

[096-167]Jews and Christians. And as to the connection of our race with

[096-168]Palestine, it has been perverted by superstition till it’s as

[096-169]demoralizing as the old poor-law. The raff and scum go there to be

[096-170]maintained like able-bodied paupers, and to be taken special care of by

[096-171]the angel Gabriel when they die. It’s no use fighting against facts. We

[096-172]must look where they point; that’s what I call rationality. The most

[096-173]learned and liberal men among us who are attached to our religion are

[096-174]for clearing our liturgy of all such notions as a literal fulfillment

[096-175]of the prophecies about restoration, and so on. Prune it of a few

[096-176]useless rites and literal interpretations of that sort, and our

[096-177]religion is the simplest of all religions, and makes no barrier, but a

[096-178]union, between us and the rest of the world.”
