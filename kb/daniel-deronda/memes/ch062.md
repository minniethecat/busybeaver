[062-001]“I expected to see you—it was so long since I had heard from you. I

[062-002]suppose the weeks seem longer at [[gadsmere|Gadsmere]] than they do at [[diplow-hall|Diplow]],” said

[062-003][[Lydia-Glasher|Mrs. Glasher]]. She had a quick, incisive way of speaking that seemed to

[062-004]go with her features, as the tone and _timbre_ of a violin go with its

[062-005]form.

[062-006]“Yes,” drawled [[Henleigh-Grandcourt|Grandcourt]]. “But you found the money paid into the bank.”

[062-007]“Oh, yes,” said Mrs. Glasher, curtly, tingling with impatience. Always

[062-008]before—at least she fancied so—Grandcourt had taken more notice of

[062-009]her and the children than he did to-day.

[062-010]“Yes,” he resumed, playing with his whisker, and at first not looking

[062-011]at her, “the time has gone on at rather a rattling pace with me;

[062-012]generally it is slow enough. But there has been a good deal happening,

[062-013]as you know”—here he turned his eyes upon her.

[062-014]“What do I know?” said she, sharply.

[062-015]He left a pause before he said, without change of manner, “That I was

[062-016]thinking of marrying. You saw [[Gwendolen-Harleth|Miss Harleth]]?”

[062-017]“_She_ told you that?”

[062-018]The pale cheeks looked even paler, perhaps from the fierce brightness

[062-019]in the eyes above them.

[062-020]“No. [[Lush]] told me,” was the slow answer. It was as if the thumb-screw

[062-021]and the iron boot were being placed by creeping hands within sight of

[062-022]the expectant victim.

[062-023]“Good God! say at once that you are going to marry her,” she burst out,

[062-024]passionately, her knees shaking and her hands tightly clasped.

[062-025]“Of course, this kind of thing must happen some time or other, Lydia,”

[062-026]said he; really, now the thumb-screw was on, not wishing to make the

[062-027]pain worse.

[062-028]“You didn’t always see the necessity.”

[062-029]“Perhaps not. I see it now.”

[062-030]In those few undertoned words of Grandcourt’s she felt as absolute a

[062-031]resistance as if her thin fingers had been pushing at a fast shut iron

[062-032]door. She knew her helplessness, and shrank from testing it by any

[062-033]appeal—shrank from crying in a dead ear and clinging to dead knees,

[062-034]only to see the immovable face and feel the rigid limbs. She did not

[062-035]weep nor speak; she was too hard pressed by the sudden certainty which

[062-036]had as much of chill sickness in it as of thought and emotion. The

[062-037]defeated clutch of struggling hope gave her in these first moments a

[062-038]horrible sensation. At last she rose, with a spasmodic effort, and,

[062-039]unconscious of every thing but her wretchedness, pressed her forehead

[062-040]against the hard, cold glass of the window. The children, playing on

[062-041]the gravel, took this as a sign that she wanted them, and, running

[062-042]forward, stood in front of her with their sweet faces upturned

[062-043]expectantly. This roused her: she shook her head at them, waved them

[062-044]off, and overcome with this painful exertion, sank back in the nearest

[062-045]chair.

[062-046]Grandcourt had risen too. He was doubly annoyed—at the scene itself,

[062-047]and at the sense that no imperiousness of his could save him from it;

[062-048]but the task had to be gone through, and there was the administrative

[062-049]necessity of arranging things so that there should be as little

[062-050]annoyance as possible in the future. He was leaning against the corner

[062-051]of the fire-place. She looked up at him and said, bitterly,

[062-052]“All this is of no consequence to you. I and the children are

[062-053]importunate creatures. You wish to get away again and be with Miss

[062-054]Harleth.”

[062-055]“Don’t make the affair more disagreeable than it need be. Lydia. It is

[062-056]of no use to harp on things that can’t be altered. Of course, its

[062-057]deucedly disagreeable to me to see you making yourself miserable. I’ve

[062-058]taken this journey to tell you what you must make up your mind to—you

[062-059]and the children will be provided for as usual—and there’s an end of

[062-060]it.”

[062-061]Silence. She dared not answer. This woman with the intense, eager look

[062-062]had had the iron of the mother’s anguish in her soul, and it had made

[062-063]her sometimes capable of a repression harder than shrieking and

[062-064]struggle. But underneath the silence there was an outlash of hatred and

[062-065]vindictiveness: she wished that the marriage might make two others

[062-066]wretched, besides herself. Presently he went on,

[062-067]“It will be better for you. You may go on living here. But I think of

[062-068]by-and-by settling a good sum on you and the children, and you can live

[062-069]where you like. There will be nothing for you to complain of then.

[062-070]Whatever happens, you will feel secure. Nothing could be done

[062-071]beforehand. Every thing has gone on in a hurry.”

[062-072]Grandcourt ceased his slow delivery of sentences. He did not expect her

[062-073]to thank him, but he considered that she might reasonably be contented;

[062-074]if it were possible for Lydia to be contented. She showed no change,

[062-075]and after a minute he said,

[062-076]“You have never had any reason to fear that I should be illiberal. I

[062-077]don’t care a curse about the money.”

[062-078]“If you did care about it, I suppose you would not give it us,” said

[062-079]Lydia. The sarcasm was irrepressible.

[062-080]“That’s a devilishly unfair thing to say,” Grandcourt replied, in a

[062-081]lower tone; “and I advise you not to say that sort of thing again.”

[062-082]“Should you punish me by leaving the children in beggary?” In spite of

[062-083]herself, the one outlet of venom had brought the other.

[062-084]“There is no question about leaving the children in beggary,” said

[062-085]Grandcourt, still in his low voice. “I advise you not to say things

[062-086]that you will repent of.”

[062-087]“I am used to repenting,” said she, bitterly. “Perhaps you will repent.

[062-088]You have already repented of loving me.”

[062-089]“All this will only make it uncommonly difficult for us to meet again.

[062-090]What friend have you besides me?”

[062-091]“Quite true.”

[062-092]The words came like a low moan. At the same moment there flashed

[062-093]through her the wish that after promising himself a better happiness

[062-094]than that he had had with her, he might feel a misery and loneliness

[062-095]which would [[ambition|drive]] him back to her to find some memory of a time when he

[062-096]was young, glad, and hopeful. But no! he would go scathless; it was she

[062-097]that had to suffer.

[062-098]With this the scorching words were ended. Grandcourt had meant to stay

[062-099]till evening; he wished to curtail his visit, but there was no suitable

[062-100]train earlier than the one he had arranged to go by, and he had still

[062-101]to speak to Lydia on the second object of his visit, which like a

[062-102]second surgical operation seemed to require an interval. The hours had

[062-103]to go by; there was eating to be done; the children came in—all this

[062-104]mechanism of life had to be gone through with the dreary sense of

[062-105]constraint which is often felt in domestic quarrels of a commoner kind.

[062-106]To Lydia it was some slight relief for her stifled fury to have the

[062-107]children present: she felt a savage glory in their loveliness, as if it

[062-108]would taunt Grandcourt with his indifference to her and them—a secret

[062-109]darting of venom which was strongly imaginative. He acquitted himself

[062-110]with all the advantage of a man whose grace of bearing has long been

[062-111]moulded on an experience of boredom—nursed the little Antonia, who sat

[062-112]with her hands crossed and eyes upturned to his bald head, which struck

[062-113]her as worthy of observation—and propitiated Henleigh by promising him

[062-114]a beautiful saddle and bridle. It was only the two eldest girls who had

[062-115]known him as a continual presence; and the intervening years had

[062-116]overlaid their infantine memories with a bashfulness which Grandcourt’s

[062-117]bearing was not likely to dissipate. He and Lydia occasionally, in the

[062-118]presence of the servants, made a conventional remark; otherwise they

[062-119]never spoke; and the stagnant thought in Grandcourt’s mind all the

[062-120]while was of his own infatuation in having given her those diamonds,

[062-121]which obliged him to incur the nuisance of speaking about them. He had

[062-122]an ingrained care for what he held to belong to his caste, and about

[062-123]property he liked to be lordly; also he had a consciousness of

[062-124]indignity to himself in having to ask for anything in the world. But

[062-125]however he might assert his [[freedom|independence]] of Mrs. Glasher’s past, he had

[062-126]made a past for himself which was a stronger yoke than any he could

[062-127]impose. He must ask for the diamonds which he had promised to Gwendolen.

[062-128]At last they were alone again, with the candles above them, face to

[062-129]face with each other. Grandcourt looked at his watch, and then said, in

[062-130]an apparently indifferent drawl, “There is one thing I had to mention,

[062-131]Lydia. My diamonds—you have them.”

[062-132]“Yes, I have them,” she answered promptly, rising and standing with her

[062-133]arms thrust down and her fingers threaded, while Grandcourt sat still.

[062-134]She had expected the topic, and made her resolve about it. But she

[062-135]meant to carry out her resolve, if possible, without exasperating him.

[062-136]During the hours of silence she had longed to recall the words which

[062-137]had only widened the breach between them.

[062-138]“They are in this house, I suppose?”

[062-139]“No; not in this house.”

[062-140]“I thought you said you kept them by you.”

[062-141]“When I said so it was true. They are in the bank at Dudley.”

[062-142]“Get them away, will you? I must make an arrangement for your

[062-143]delivering them to some one.”

[062-144]“Make no arrangement. They shall be delivered to the person you

[062-145]intended them for. _I_ will make the arrangement.”

[062-146]“What do you mean?”

[062-147]“What I say. I have always told you that I would give them up to your

[062-148]wife. I shall keep my word. She is not your wife yet.”

[062-149]“This is foolery,” said Grandcourt, with undertoned disgust. It was too

[062-150]irritating that this indulgence of Lydia had given her a sort of

[062-151]mastery over him in spite of dependent condition.

[062-152]She did not speak. He also rose now, but stood leaning against the

[062-153]mantle-piece with his side-face toward her.

[062-154]“The diamonds must be delivered to me before my marriage,” he began

[062-155]again.

[062-156]“What is your wedding-day?”

[062-157]“The tenth. There is no time to be lost.”

[062-158]“And where do you go after the marriage?”

[062-159]He did not reply except by looking more sullen. Presently he said, “You

[062-160]must appoint a day before then, to get them from the bank and meet

[062-161]me—or somebody else I will commission;—it’s a great nuisance. Mention

[062-162]a day.”

[062-163]“No; I shall not do that. They shall be delivered to her safely. I

[062-164]shall keep my word.”

[062-165]“Do you mean to say,” said Grandcourt, just audibly, turning to face

[062-166]her, “that you will not do as I tell you?”

[062-167]“Yes, I mean that,” was the answer that leaped out, while her eyes

[062-168]flashed close to him. The poor creature was immediately conscious that

[062-169]if her words had any effect on her own lot, the effect must be

[062-170]mischievous, and might nullify all the remaining advantage of her long

[062-171]patience. But the word had been spoken.

[062-172]He was in a position the most irritating to him. He could not shake her

[062-173]nor touch her hostilely; and if he could, the process would not bring

[062-174]his mother’s diamonds. He shrank from the only sort of threat that

[062-175]would frighten her—if she believed it. And in general, there was

[062-176]nothing he hated more than to be forced into anything like violence

[062-177]even in words: his will must impose itself without trouble. After

[062-178]looking at her for a moment, he turned his side-face toward her again,

[062-179]leaning as before, and said,

[062-180]“Infernal idiots that women are!”

[062-181]“Why will you not tell me where you are going after the marriage? I

[062-182]could be at the wedding if I liked, and learn in that way,” said Lydia,

[062-183]not shrinking from the one suicidal form of threat within her power.

[062-184]“Of course, if you like, you can play the mad woman,” said Grandcourt,

[062-185]with _sotto voce_ scorn. “It is not to be supposed that you will wait

[062-186]to think what good will come of it—or what you owe to me.”
