# Renunciation

Renunciation in *[[Daniel-Deronda|Daniel Deronda]]* is the quiet discipline of setting aside one's own demands — an ethical practice that Eliot presents not as passive weakness but as the active ground of moral maturity.

## Overview

Renunciation threads through both plot lines of the novel: in the English story, [[Gwendolen-Harleth|Gwendolen Harleth]] confronts it as the painful price of marrying for [[wealth|money]], and in the Jewish story, [[Mirah-Lapidoth|Mirah Lapidoth]] embodies it as the daily texture of a life devoted to others. For Eliot, renunciation is not the melodramatic self-sacrifice of Victorian sensation fiction — it is a subtler, more demanding moral posture: "the renunciation of demands for self, and from taking the ordinary good of existence, and especially kindness, even from a dog, as a gift above expectation" (145-038–039). This quiet receptivity is the novel's alternative to [[Gwendolen-Harleth|Gwendolen]]'s egoism and [[Henleigh-Grandcourt|Grandcourt]]'s tyranny of will.

## Role in the Novel

Renunciation functions as a moral counterweight to the novel's exploration of egoism, ambition, and the will to dominate. Where [[Henleigh-Grandcourt|Grandcourt]] exerts his will by refusing to renounce anything, and where [[Gwendolen-Harleth|Gwendolen]] suffers because she cannot bear to give up her desires, [[Mirah-Lapidoth|Mirah]] and [[Daniel-Deronda|Deronda]] model a different orientation: renunciation as the precondition of genuine freedom. The novel's moral arc bends toward this insight — that "the hardship of an enforced renunciation" (114-115) can, when embraced rather than resisted, become the foundation of a life lived for others.

## Expression Through Characters

### Mirah Lapidoth

[[Mirah-Lapidoth|Mirah]] is the novel's fullest embodiment of renunciation. Her "quiet renunciation" (117-164) is not the grim self-denial of the ascetic but the natural expression of a [[reputation|character]] who has learned, through suffering, to expect little and to treasure what comes. [[Daniel-Deronda|Deronda]] perceives "amidst all [[Mirah-Lapidoth|Mirah]]'s quiet renunciation, the evident thirst of soul with which she received the tribute of equality" (117-164) — a paradox that defines her: she renounces demands on others precisely because she values human connection so highly.

### Gwendolen Harleth

[[Gwendolen-Harleth|Gwendolen]]'s encounter with renunciation is far more painful. When she agrees to marry [[Henleigh-Grandcourt|Grandcourt]], she takes it as "a tacit renunciation" (061-069) of her own freedom — and later, after the marriage's collapse, she confronts "the hardship of an enforced renunciation" (114-115). For [[Gwendolen-Harleth|Gwendolen]], renunciation is not chosen but imposed, and her moral growth consists in learning to accept it. Her mother's "renunciation were yet not strong enough to hinder her" (055-169) — the older generation's failure to model renunciation is part of what leaves [[Gwendolen-Harleth|Gwendolen]] morally unarmed.

### Daniel Deronda

[[Daniel-Deronda|Deronda]]'s renunciation is structural rather than dramatic: he gives up his English identity and inheritance to embrace his Jewish vocation. His renunciation of "demands for self" (145-038) is the culmination of the novel's moral education — the point at which self-surrender becomes not loss but fulfillment.

## Key Passages

> She was experiencing some of that peaceful melancholy which comes from the renunciation of demands for self, and from taking the ordinary good of existence, and especially kindness, even from a dog, as a gift above expectation. (145-038–039)

> Amidst all Mirah's quiet renunciation, the evident thirst of soul with which she received the tribute of equality implied a corresponding pain. (117-164)

> I can imagine the hardship of an enforced renunciation. (114-115)

> Her mother's renunciation were yet not strong enough to hinder her. (055-169)

## Historical Context

The Victorian cult of self-sacrifice — particularly for women — made renunciation a central theme of nineteenth-century fiction. From Florence Nightingale's *Cassandra* (1852) to Coventry Patmore's *The Angel in the House* (1854), the era celebrated (and enforced) feminine self-abnegation. Eliot, who had herself renounced conventional respectability to live with George Henry Lewes, brings an insider's ambivalence to the theme. The novel distinguishes between neurotic self-suppression and genuinely ethical renunciation — the former a pathology, the latter a liberation. This distinction reflects Eliot's immersion in Feuerbachian humanism, which reconceived Christian self-denial as a this-worldly ethics of care for others.
