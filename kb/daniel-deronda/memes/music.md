# Music

Music in *[[Daniel-Deronda|Daniel Deronda]]* functions both as a concrete social practice and as a metaphor for the soul's capacity to respond to what is noble.

## Overview

Eliot uses [[art|music]] as a ladder of moral intelligence in the novel. At the bottom is [[Gwendolen-Harleth|Gwendolen]]'s amateur singing — pretty, [[pride|self-regard]]ing, a social or[[reputation|name]]nt. At the top is [[Herr-Klesmer|Herr Klesmer]]'s ideal of the artist whose entire being serves the art. Between them sits [[Mirah-Lapidoth|Mirah]]'s genuine gift, [[Catherine-Arrowpoint|Catherine Arrowpoint]]'s disciplined accomplishment, and [[Daniel-Deronda|Deronda]]'s acute [[art|music]]al sensitivity — a responsiveness that signals his deeper moral receptivity. "A noble [[art|music]]" (021-131) becomes, for Eliot, almost a synonym for the life worth living.

## Role in the Novel

Music structures several of the novel's major relationship arcs. [[Daniel-Deronda|Deronda]] first encounters [[Mirah-Lapidoth|Mirah]] through her singing; [[Herr-Klesmer|Klesmer]]'s judgment of [[Gwendolen-Harleth|Gwendolen]]'s [[art|music]]al limitations (009-018) is the novel's first honest assessment of her [[reputation|character]]; [[Catherine-Arrowpoint|Catherine Arrowpoint]]'s choice of [[Herr-Klesmer|Klesmer]] over her family dramatises the conflict between artistic vocation and social expectation. The recurring phrase "a noble [[art|music]]" positions [[art|music]]al responsiveness as a moral capacity — those who hear it ([[Daniel-Deronda|Deronda]], [[Mirah-Lapidoth|Mirah]]) are capable of a depth those who use [[art|music]] as mere display ([[Gwendolen-Harleth|Gwendolen]] early on) cannot access.

## Expression Through Characters

### Herr Klesmer
[[Herr-Klesmer|Klesmer]] is the novel's [[art|music]]al conscience. His devastating verdict on [[Gwendolen-Harleth|Gwendolen]] — that at best she could become "a moderate [[art|music]]al amateur, nothing more" — is both a professional judgment and a moral one. He insists that a true artist "is no more a mere [[art|music]]ian than a great statesman is a mere politician" (043-089). His marriage to [[Catherine-Arrowpoint|Catherine Arrowpoint]] represents the triumph of artistic integrity over class prejudice.

### Mirah Lapidoth
[[Mirah-Lapidoth|Mirah]]'s singing is the antithesis of [[Gwendolen-Harleth|Gwendolen]]'s — it comes from genuine feeling, not display. Her voice first arrests [[Daniel-Deronda|Deronda]] by the river, and her professional ambition as a singer gives her an [[freedom|independence]] rare among the novel's women. Music is, for [[Mirah-Lapidoth|Mirah]], both livelihood and spiritual expression, linked to her Jewish [[inheritance|heritage]] through the memory of her mother's singing.

## Key Passages

> There is a sort of mania among the meanings of a noble music. (021-131)

> A great musician in the fullest sense of the word ought to have not only the manual skill but also the inward vision. (043-101)

> Her singing gave pleasure to ordinary hearers. But it was that music which they call the singing of tone rather than of feeling. (008-158)

> Woman was dear to him, but music was dearer. (009-007)

> Music was a signal for her to step down from her pedestal of indifference. (011-012)

## Historical Context

Victorian attitudes toward [[art|music]] were in transition. The cult of the amateur performer — the young lady at the piano as a matrimonial asset — was giving way to a new respect for professional musicianship, influenced by Continental models. The Royal Academy of Music had been founded in 1822, and by the 1860s serious [[art|music]] criticism was a recognised branch of journalism. Eliot herself was an accomplished amateur pianist. The novel reflects contemporary debates about whether [[art|music]] was an accomplishment or a vocation, and whether a woman could legitimately pursue it as a profession — questions [[Mirah-Lapidoth|Mirah]] and [[Catherine-Arrowpoint|Catherine Arrowpoint]] answer in different ways.
