# Duty

Duty in *[[Daniel-Deronda|Daniel Deronda]]* is the moral force that separates self-interest from ethical action. George Eliot presents duty not as a simple social code but as a complex, often painful commitment — the recognition that we are "bound" to others in ways that constrain freedom but constitute moral identity.

## Overview

The word "duty" appears with remarkable frequency throughout the novel, serving as a touchstone in moments of moral crisis. For [[Gwendolen-Harleth|Gwendolen]], duty is an external imposition to be resisted; for [[Daniel-Deronda|Deronda]], it is an inner vocation to be discovered. Eliot carefully distinguishes between false duty — the social convention that "required her to marry a man who was very fond of her" (042-125) — and true duty, which arises from genuine moral perception. The novel's philosophical depth lies in its insistence that duty is not drudgery but the path to authentic selfhood: "Where there is the duty of service there must be the duty of accepting it" (047-018).

## Role in the Novel

Duty is the novel's primary moral vocabulary. When [[Mrs-Davilow|Gwendolen's mother]] appeals to her "duty" to marry [[Henleigh-Grandcourt|Grandcourt]] for the family's financial salvation (025-160), the word is used as coercion — a weapon of economic necessity. [[Gwendolen-Harleth|Gwendolen]]'s resistance — "Your parent's desire makes no duty for you" (044-072) — is both admirable and self-serving, revealing the ambiguity that makes Eliot's treatment of duty so psychologically acute.

[[Daniel-Deronda|Deronda]] embodies duty as positive vocation. His "duty of caring for" [[Mirah-Lapidoth|Mirah]] (066-127), his duty to reveal his Jewish identity to the Cohens (069-059), and his sense of duty to [[mordecais-vision|Mordecai's vision]] all represent duty as self-discovery rather than self-denial. The novel's ethical climax — [[Daniel-Deronda|Deronda]]'s commitment to the Jewish national cause — is presented as the fulfillment of duty, not its burden.

The critical exchange between [[Gwendolen-Harleth|Gwendolen]] and her mother (Book II, Chapter 6) is the novel's most explicit philosophical treatment of duty. [[Mrs-Davilow|Mrs. Davilow]] invokes "duty" as obedience to social norms; [[Gwendolen-Harleth|Gwendolen]] counters with the Kantian claim that "higher duty forbids" (044-080) — a duty to oneself, to one's own nature — which Eliot treats with sympathy but also with the recognition that it can become a screen for [[egoism|selfishness]].

## Expression Through Characters

### Gwendolen Harleth

[[Gwendolen-Harleth|Gwendolen]]'s relationship to duty defines her moral education. Initially, she treats duty as an infringement on her freedom: "I have lost all sense of duty, then?" (044-058) she asks her mother, with sarcasm. Her arc traces a movement from resisting duty to accepting it — not the false duty of marriage to [[Henleigh-Grandcourt|Grandcourt]] but the genuine duty of moral responsibility that she arrives at through suffering. Her final state — "I will try; I will be better" — is the acceptance of duty without external compulsion.

### Daniel Deronda

[[Daniel-Deronda|Deronda]]'s life is shaped by duty before he knows its source. His care for [[Mirah-Lapidoth|Mirah]], his commitment to [[Mordecai]], his search for his origins — all are [[ambition|drive]]n by "duty" that he feels as an intuition before he understands its rationale. Eliot presents him as the ideal moral agent: one for whom duty and desire converge. His acceptance of his Jewish inheritance is the culmination of a life lived in "strict fulfillment of duty" (071-062).

### Mrs. Davilow

[[Mrs-Davilow|Mrs. Davilow]] represents duty as social conformity — the duty of a mother to secure her daughters' futures, the duty of a wife to endure a disappointing marriage. Her "duty" speeches to [[Gwendolen-Harleth|Gwendolen]] reveal the moral poverty of conventional obligation: duty as fear, duty as sub[[vocation|mission]]. Yet Eliot does not mock her; she presents [[Mrs-Davilow|Mrs. Davilow]]'s position as genuinely constrained, showing how duty can be both necessary and oppressive.

## Key Passages

> Where there is the duty of service there must be the duty of accepting it. (047-018)

> Where duty and inclination clash, she must follow duty. (044-067)

> I can easily take the sacred word duty as a name for what I desire. (044-070)

> Higher duty forbids. (044-080)

> You have lost all sense of duty, then? You have forgotten that you are my daughter. (044-058)

## Historical Context

Eliot wrote *[[Daniel-Deronda|Daniel Deronda]]* in the aftermath of the great Victorian debates about duty and moral philosophy. John Stuart Mill's *Utilitarianism* (1863) had challenged duty-based ethics with consequentialism; the Evangelical revival had made "duty" a household word. Eliot, who translated Feuerbach and was deeply influenced by Spinoza, occupies a distinctive position: duty, in her novel, is neither utilitarian calculation nor divine command, but the natural expression of human sympathy — the feeling that we are "bound" to others because their suffering is real to us.

The novel's philosophical sophistication about duty — its refusal to reduce it to either social convention or abstract principle — places it among the most serious ethical novels of the nineteenth century.
