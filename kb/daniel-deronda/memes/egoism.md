# Egoism

Egoism in *[[Daniel-Deronda|Daniel Deronda]]* is not ordinary selfishness but a technical moral-psychological concept: the contraction of the self that makes sympathy — Eliot's supreme ethical value — impossible. Eliot uses the term with philosophical precision, drawing on her immersion in German thought (Feuerbach, Spinoza) to diagnose [[reputation|character]]s whose moral视野 has shrunk to their own desires. [[Henleigh-Grandcourt|Grandcourt]]'s "fastidious egoism" [PN=050-038] and [[Gwendolen-Harleth|Gwendolen]]'s dawning recognition of her own egoism [PN=063-163] form a linked study in moral narrowness and its consequences.

## Overview

Eliot's ethical philosophy, developed through her translation of Feuerbach and her reading of Spinoza, centres on the expansion of sympathy beyond the self. Egoism is the opposite movement — the narrowing of concern until nothing exists but one's own desires and aversions. The novel presents egoism not as a sin to be punished but as a psychological condition that carries its own consequences: isolation, sterility, and the inability to love.

Eliot distinguishes between different forms of egoism. [[Henleigh-Grandcourt|Grandcourt]]'s is "fastidious" — refined, indifferent, absolute [PN=050-038]. [[Gwendolen-Harleth|Gwendolen]]'s is the egoism of youthful [[pride|vanity]], "out of which she had been shaped" [PN=063-163] by circumstance and beauty. The novel's moral arc traces [[Gwendolen-Harleth|Gwendolen]]'s painful emergence from this egoism through suffering and confession, while [[Henleigh-Grandcourt|Grandcourt]] — who never questions his own centrality — remains trapped in his until death.

## Role in the Novel

Egoism is the primary moral pathology the novel diagnoses. The English gentry world is a hothouse of egoism: [[reputation|character]]s vie for attention, calculate advantage, and treat others as instruments of their own satisfaction. [[Henleigh-Grandcourt|Grandcourt]] represents egoism in its purest form — "a fastidious egoism, in which all direct desires" [PN=050-038] are gratified while all external claims are dismissed with languid contempt. His treatment of [[Lydia-Glasher|Lydia Glasher]], [[Gwendolen-Harleth|Gwendolen]], and even his dogs (creatures valued only for their loyalty to him) demonstrates the essential cruelty of the egoistic posture.

Against egoism, Eliot sets sympathy — the imaginative entry into another's experience. [[Daniel-Deronda|Deronda]] embodies this counter-principle. Where [[Henleigh-Grandcourt|Grandcourt]] asks of every situation "what does it offer me?", [[Daniel-Deronda|Deronda]] asks "what is it like to be you?" The novel's Jewish plot extends the argument: national egoism (English insularity, anti-Semitic prejudice) is as damaging as personal egoism, and [[mordecais-vision|Mordecai's vision]] of a restored Jewish nation is framed as an escape from the egoism of exile into collective purpose.

## Expression Through Characters

### Henleigh Grandcourt

[[Henleigh-Grandcourt|Grandcourt]] is the novel's supreme egoist, rendered with clinical precision. His egoism is "fastidious" [PN=050-038] — not crude or passionate, but refined and absolute. He desires nothing intensely except his own ease and mastery; his cruelty to [[Gwendolen-Harleth|Gwendolen]] consists not in what he does but in the absolute indifference with which he treats her as an extension of his will. His "exorbitant egoism" [PN=057-018] is the trunk from which all his actions branch. Eliot's diagnosis is that such egoism is self-defeating: [[Henleigh-Grandcourt|Grandcourt]] achieves everything he wants and has nothing worth having. His death — a drowning that may or may not have been preventable — is the novel's judgment on a life that touched others only to use them.

### Gwendolen Harleth

[[Gwendolen-Harleth|Gwendolen]]'s egoism is developmental rather than constitutional. She begins the novel as a creature of "egoism, had fullness of nature enough" [PN=009-033] — the qualification is crucial. Unlike [[Henleigh-Grandcourt|Grandcourt]], [[Gwendolen-Harleth|Gwendolen]] has the capacity for something beyond [[pride|self-regard]]; her egoism is the product of beauty, indulgence, and the narrow education permitted to Victorian gentlewomen. Her moral journey is the painful process of being freed from "the egoism out of which she had been shaped" [PN=063-163]. Her confessions to [[Daniel-Deronda|Deronda]] are exercises in self-transcendence — moments when she sees herself from outside and is horrified by what she sees. The novel's final image of her — widowed, solitary, but morally awake — suggests that egoism can be overcome through suffering honestly borne.

## Historical Context

Eliot's use of "egoism" as a moral-psychological term reflects her deep engagement with German philosophy. In her translation of Feuerbach's *The Essence of [[religion|Christianity]]* (1854), she rendered *Egoismus* as a technical term for the self-referential orientation that religion properly transforms into love of others. Spinoza's *Ethics* — which Eliot read in the original Latin — similarly treats self-preservation (*conatus*) as the basic [[ambition|drive]] that must be educated into broader identification with others.

The Victorian moral vocabulary preferred "selfishness" to "egoism," but Eliot's choice of the more philosophical term signals her ambition. This is not Sunday-school moralising about sharing; it is a psychological analysis of how the self becomes a prison. The term appears in her letters and essays as well, always with this technical weight. In *[[Daniel-Deronda|Daniel Deronda]]*, it becomes a diagnostic tool for measuring [[reputation|character]]s' moral health.
