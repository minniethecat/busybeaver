[121-001]“Is she beautiful?” she said, abruptly.

[121-002]“Who?” said [[Daniel-Deronda|Deronda]], changing color.

[121-003]“The woman you love.”

[121-004]It was not a moment for deliberate explanation. He was obliged to say,

[121-005]“Yes.”

[121-006]“Not ambitious?”

[121-007]“No, I think not.”

[121-008]“Not one who must have a path of her own?”

[121-009]“I think her nature is not given to make great claims.”

[121-010]“She is not like that?” said the Princess, taking from her wallet a

[121-011]miniature with jewels around it, and holding it before her son. It was

[121-012]her own in all the fire of youth, and as Deronda looked at it with

[121-013]admiring sadness, she said, “Had I not a rightful claim to be something

[121-014]more than a mere daughter and mother? The voice and the genius matched

[121-015]the face. Whatever else was wrong, acknowledge that I had a right to be

[121-016]an artist, though my father’s will was against it. My nature gave me a

[121-017]charter.”

[121-018]“I do acknowledge that,” said Deronda, looking from the miniature to

[121-019]her face, which even in its worn pallor had an expression of living

[121-020]force beyond anything that the pencil could show.

[121-021]“Will you take the portrait?” said the Princess, more gently. “If she

[121-022]is a kind woman, teach her to think of me kindly.”

[121-023]“I shall be grateful for the portrait,” said Deronda, “but—I ought to

[121-024]say, I have no assurance that she whom I love will have any love for

[121-025]me. I have kept silence.”

[121-026]“Who and what is she?” said the mother. The question seemed a command.

[121-027]“She was brought up as a singer for the stage,” said Deronda, with

[121-028]inward reluctance. “Her father took her away early from her mother, and

[121-029]her life has been unhappy. She is very young—only twenty. Her father

[121-030]wished to bring her up in disregard—even in dislike of her Jewish

[121-031]origin, but she has clung with all her affection to the memory of her

[121-032]mother and the fellowship of her people.”

[121-033]“Ah, like you. She is attached to the [[religion|Judaism]] she knows nothing of,”

[121-034]said the Princess, peremptorily. “That is poetry—fit to last through

[121-035]an [[opera-as-art|opera]] night. Is she fond of her artist’s life—is her [[music|singing]] worth

[121-036]anything?”

[121-037]“Her singing is exquisite. But her voice is not suited to the stage. I

[121-038]think that the artist’s life has been made repugnant to her.”

[121-039]“Why, she is made for you then. [[Sir-Hugo-Mallinger|Sir Hugo]] said you were bitterly against

[121-040]being a singer, and I can see that you would never have let yourself be

[121-041]merged in a wife, as your father was.”

[121-042]“I repeat,” said Deronda, emphatically—“I repeat that I have no

[121-043]assurance of her love for me, of the possibility that we can ever be

[121-044]united. Other things—painful issues may lie before me. I have always

[121-045]felt that I should prepare myself to renounce, not cherish that

[121-046]prospect. But I suppose I might feel so of happiness in general.

[121-047]Whether it may come or not, one should try and prepare one’s self to do

[121-048]without it.”

[121-049]“Do you feel in that way?” said his mother, laying her hands on his

[121-050]shoulders, and perusing his face, while she spoke in a low meditative

[121-051]tone, pausing between her sentences. “Poor boy!——I wonder how it

[121-052]would have been if I had kept you with me——whether you would have

[121-053]turned your heart to the old things against mine——and we should have

[121-054]quarreled——your grandfather would have been in you——and you would

[121-055]have hampered my life with your young growth from the old root.”

[121-056]“I think my affection might have lasted through all our quarreling,”

[121-057]said Deronda, saddened more and more, “and that would not have

[121-058]hampered—surely it would have enriched your life.”

[121-059]“Not then, not then——I did not want it then——I might have been glad

[121-060]of it now,” said the mother, with a bitter melancholy, “if I could have

[121-061]been glad of anything.”

[121-062]“But you love your other children, and they love you?” said Deronda,

[121-063]anxiously.

[121-064]“Oh, yes,” she answered, as to a question about a matter of course,

[121-065]while she folded her arms again. “But,”——she added in a deeper

[121-066]tone,——“I am not a loving woman. That is the truth. It is a talent to

[121-067]love—I lack it. Others have loved me—and I have acted their love. I

[121-068]know very well what love makes of men and women—it is subjection. It

[121-069]takes another for a larger self, enclosing this one,”—she pointed to

[121-070]her own bosom. “I was never willingly subject to any man. Men have been

[121-071]subject to me.”

[121-072]“Perhaps the man who was subject was the happier of the two,” said

[121-073]Deronda—not with a smile, but with a grave, sad sense of his mother’s

[121-074]privation.

[121-075]“Perhaps—but I _was_ happy—for a few years I was happy. If I had not

[121-076]been afraid of defeat and failure, I might have gone on. I

[121-077]miscalculated. What then? It is all over. Another life! Men talk of

[121-078]‘another life,’ as if it only began on the other side of the grave. I

[121-079]have long entered on another life.” With the last words she raised her

[121-080]arms till they were bare to the elbow, her brow was contracted in one

[121-081]deep fold, her eyes were closed, her voice was smothered: in her dusky

[121-082]flame-colored garment, she looked like a dreamed visitant from some

[121-083]region of departed mortals.

[121-084]Deronda’s feeling was wrought to a pitch of acuteness in which he was

[121-085]no longer quite master of himself. He gave an audible sob. His mother

[121-086]opened her eyes, and letting her hands again rest on his shoulders,

[121-087]said,

[121-088]“Good-bye, my son, good-bye. We shall hear no more of each other. Kiss

[121-089]me.”

[121-090]He clasped his arms round her neck, and they kissed each other.

[121-091]Deronda did not know how he got out of the room. He felt an older man.

[121-092]All his boyish yearnings and anxieties about his mother had vanished.

[121-093]He had gone through a tragic experience which must forever solemnize

[121-094]his life and deepen the significance of the acts by which he bound

[121-095]himself to others.

[121-096]CHAPTER LIV.

[121-097]“The unwilling brain

[121-098]Feigns often what it would not; and we trust

[121-099]Imagination with such phantasies

[121-100]As the tongue dares not fashion into words;

[121-101]Which have no words, their horror makes them dim

[121-102]To the mind’s eye.”

[121-103]—SHELLEY.

[121-104]Madonna Pia, whose husband, feeling himself injured by her, took her to

[121-105]his castle amid the swampy flats of the Maremma and got rid of her

[121-106]there, makes a pathetic figure in [[dantes-divine-comedy|Dante]]’s Purgatory, among the sinners

[121-107]who repented at the last and desire to be remembered compassionately by

[121-108]their fellow-countrymen. We know little about the grounds of mutual

[121-109]discontent between the Siennese couple, but we may infer with some

[121-110]confidence that the husband had never been a very delightful companion,

[121-111]and that on the flats of the Maremma his disagreeable manners had a

[121-112]background which threw them out remarkably; whence in his desire to

[121-113]punish his wife to the upmost, the nature of things was so far against

[121-114]him that in relieving himself of her he could not avoid making the

[121-115]relief mutual. And thus, without any hardness to the poor Tuscan lady,

[121-116]who had her deliverance long ago, one may feel warranted in thinking of

[121-117]her with a less sympathetic interest than of the better known [[Gwendolen-Harleth|Gwendolen]]

[121-118]who, instead of being delivered from her errors on earth and cleansed

[121-119]from their effect in purgatory, is at the very height of her

[121-120]entanglement in those fatal meshes which are woven within more closely

[121-121]than without, and often make the inward torture disproportionate to

[121-122]what is discernable as outward cause.

[121-123]In taking his wife with him on a yachting expedition, [[Henleigh-Grandcourt|Grandcourt]] had no

[121-124]intention to get rid of her; on the contrary, he wanted to feel more

[121-125]securely that she was his to do as he liked with, and to make her feel

[121-126]it also. Moreover, he was himself very fond of yachting: its dreamy

[121-127]do-nothing absolutism, unmolested by social demands, suited his

[121-128]disposition, and he did not in the least regard it as an equivalent for

[121-129]the dreariness of the Maremma. He had his reasons for carrying

[121-130]Gwendolen out of reach, but they were not reasons that can seem black

[121-131]in the mere statement. He suspected a growing spirit of opposition in

[121-132]her, and his feeling about the sentimental inclination she betrayed for

[121-133]Deronda was what in another man he would have called jealousy. In

[121-134]himself it seemed merely a resolution to put an end to such foolery as

[121-135]must have been going on in that prearranged visit of Deronda’s which he

[121-136]had divined and interrupted.

[121-137]And Grandcourt might have pleaded that he was perfectly justified in

[121-138]taking care that his wife should fulfill the obligations she had

[121-139]accepted. Her marriage was a contract where all the ostensible

[121-140]advantages were on her side, and it was only of those advantages that

[121-141]her husband should use his power to hinder her from any injurious self

[121-142]committal or unsuitable behavior. He knew quite well that she had not

[121-143]married him—had not overcome her repugnance to certain facts—out of

[121-144]love to him personally; he had won her by the rank and luxuries he had

[121-145]to give her, and these she had got: he had fulfilled his side of the

[121-146]contract.

[121-147]And Gwendolen, we know, was thoroughly aware of the situation. She

[121-148]could not excuse herself by saying that there had been a tacit part of

[121-149]the contract on her side—namely, that she meant to rule and have her

[121-150]own way. With all her early indulgence in the disposition to dominate,

[121-151]she was not one of the narrow-brained women who through life regard all

[121-152]their own selfish demands as rights, and every claim upon themselves as

[121-153]an injury. She had a root of conscience in her, and the process of

[121-154]purgatory had begun for her on the green earth: she knew that she had

[121-155]been wrong.

[121-156]But now enter into the soul of this young creature as she found

[121-157]herself, with the blue Mediterranean dividing her from the world, on

[121-158]the tiny plank-island of a yacht, the domain of the husband to whom she

[121-159]felt that she had sold herself, and had been paid the strict

[121-160]price—nay, paid more than she had dared to ask in the handsome

[121-161]maintenance of her mother:—the husband to whom she had sold her

[121-162]truthfulness and sense of justice, so that he held them throttled into

[121-163]silence, collared and dragged behind him to witness what he would,

[121-164]without remonstrance.

[121-165]What had she to complain of? The yacht was of the prettiest; the cabin

[121-166]fitted up to perfection, smelling of cedar, soft-cushioned, hung with

[121-167]silk, expanded with mirrors; the crew such as suited an elegant toy,

[121-168]one of them having even ringlets, as well as a bronze complexion and

[121-169]fine teeth; and [[Lush|Mr. Lush]] was not there, for he had taken his way back

[121-170]to England as soon as he had seen all and everything on board.

[121-171]Moreover, Gwendolen herself liked the sea: it did not make her ill; and

[121-172]to observe the rigging of the vessel and forecast the necessary

[121-173]adjustments was a sort of amusement that might have gratified her

[121-174]activity and enjoyment of imaginary rule; the weather was fine, and

[121-175]they were coasting southward, where even the rain-furrowed,

[121-176]heat-cracked clay becomes gem-like with purple shadows, and where one

[121-177]may float between blue and blue in an open-eyed dream that the world

[121-178]has done with sorrow.

[121-179]But what can still that hunger of the heart which sickens the eye for

[121-180]beauty, and makes sweet-scented ease an oppression? What sort of Moslem

[121-181]paradise would quiet the terrible fury of moral repulsion and cowed

[121-182]resistance which, like an eating pain intensifying into torture,

[121-183]concentrates the mind in that poisonous misery? While Gwendolen,

[121-184]throned on her cushions at evening, and beholding the glory of sea and

[121-185]sky softening as if with boundless love around her, was hoping that

[121-186]Grandcourt in his march up and down was not going to pause near her,

[121-187]not going to look at her or speak to her, some woman, under a smoky

[121-188]sky, obliged to consider the price of eggs in arranging her dinner, was

[121-189]listening for the [[art|music]] of a footstep that would remove all risk from

[121-190]her foretaste of joy; some couple, bending cheek by cheek, over a bit

[121-191]of work done by the one and delighted in by the other, were reckoning

[121-192]the earnings that would make them rich enough for a holiday among the

[121-193]furze and heather.
