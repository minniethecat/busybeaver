# The Hand and Banner

The Hand and Banner is the workingmen's club where Mordecai holds court — a modest room above a London street where Jewish artisans and intellectuals gather to debate, and where Deronda first witnesses the prophetic energy of Mordecai's vision.

## Description

The club is a deliberately unglamorous space: "the parlor of the *Hand and Banner*" (092-172) located on an upper floor, accessible by stairway rather than grand entrance. Its name evokes the guild traditions of medieval craftsmen, connecting the Jewish artisans who gather there to a long history of skilled labor and mutual aid. The room's modesty — a "parlor" rather than a hall — emphasizes the community's marginal economic position while also suggesting the intimacy of shared conviction. Unlike the aristocratic clubs of London's West End, the Hand and Banner is a place of intellectual rather than social capital, where ideas matter more than connections.

## Narrative Function

The Hand and Banner serves as the forum where Mordecai's prophetic vision meets the skeptical scrutiny of his peers. It is here that Deronda first encounters the intellectual vitality of London's Jewish community — not as a museum piece or a charity case but as a living tradition of argument, faith, and political aspiration. The club's working-class character is essential to Eliot's democratic vision: the future of Judaism, she suggests, will be built not in drawing rooms but in "parlors" like this one, by artisans who debate philosophy after their day's labor.

## Associated Characters

- **Mordecai**: The club's intellectual center; his prophetic vision is tested and refined through debates at the Hand and Banner (PN 091-141)
- **Daniel Deronda**: Witnesses Mordecai at the club, an experience that deepens his commitment to the Jewish cause; the Hand and Banner is where theory becomes community (PN 092-172)
- **The Cohens and fellow artisans**: The club's membership represents the democratic base of Jewish intellectual life

## Symbolic Significance

The Hand and Banner symbolizes the novel's vision of intellectual democracy. Its name — combining the hand of labor with the banner of belief — captures the synthesis of work and vision that Eliot presents as the foundation of genuine cultural revival. The club's location above a London street, accessible only by stairway, suggests the ascent from mundane life to prophetic vision — a movement that mirrors Deronda's own trajectory from English gentleman to Jewish leader.

## Key Scenes

- Mordecai's debates at the Hand and Banner, where his vision encounters the testing ground of community (PN 091-141)
- Deronda's visit to the club, his first direct encounter with the intellectual vitality of Jewish London (PN 092-172)
- The club's "parlor" as a space of democratic argument — a counterpoint to the aristocratic drawing rooms of the English plot
