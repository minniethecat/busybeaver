[055-001][[Gwendolen-Harleth|Gwendolen]] laughed. All that seemed kind and natural enough:

[055-002][[Henleigh-Grandcourt|Grandcourt]]’s fastidiousness enhanced the kindness. And when they

[055-003]reached the door, his way of opening it for her was the perfection of

[055-004]easy homage. Really, she thought, he was likely to be the least

[055-005]disagreeable of husbands.

[055-006][[Mrs-Davilow|Mrs. Davilow]] was waiting anxiously in her bedroom when Gwendolen

[055-007]entered, stepped toward her quickly, and kissing her on both cheeks

[055-008]said in a low tone, “Come down, mamma, and see Mr. Grandcourt. I am

[055-009]engaged to him.”

[055-010]“My darling child,” said Mrs. Davilow, with a surprise that was rather

[055-011]solemn than glad.

[055-012]“Yes,” said Gwendolen, in the same tone, and with a quickness which

[055-013]implied that it was needless to ask questions. “Everything is settled.

[055-014]You are not going to Sawyer’s Cottage, I am not going to be inspected

[055-015]by Mrs. Mompert, and everything is to be as I like. So come down with

[055-016]me immediately.”

[055-017]BOOK IV.—GWENDOLEN GETS HER CHOICE.

[055-018]CHAPTER XXVIII.

[055-019]“Il est plus aisé de connoître l’homme en général que de connoître un

[055-020]homme en particulier.”—LA ROCHEFOUCAULD.

[055-021]An hour after Grandcourt had left, the important news of Gwendolen’s

[055-022]engagement was known at the rectory, and Mr. and Mrs. Gascoigne, with

[055-023]Anna, spent the evening at [[Offendene]].

[055-024]“My dear, let me congratulate you on having created a strong

[055-025]attachment,” said the rector. “You look serious, and I don’t wonder at

[055-026]it: a life-long union is a solemn thing. But from the way Mr. Grandcourt

[055-027]has acted and spoken I think we may already see some good arising out

[055-028]of our adversity. It has given you an opportunity of observing your

[055-029]future husband’s delicate liberality.”

[055-030][[Rex-Gascoigne|Mr. Gascoigne]] referred to Grandcourt’s mode of implying that he would

[055-031]provide for Mrs. Davilow—a part of the love-making which Gwendolen had

[055-032]remembered to cite to her mother with perfect accuracy.

[055-033]“But I have no doubt that Mr. Grandcourt would have behaved quite as

[055-034]handsomely if you had not gone away to Germany, Gwendolen, and had been

[055-035]engaged to him, as you no doubt might have been, more than a month

[055-036]ago,” said Mrs. Gascoigne, feeling that she had to discharge a duty on

[055-037]this occasion. “But now there is no more room for caprice; indeed, I

[055-038]trust you have no inclination to any. A woman has a great debt of

[055-039]gratitude to a man who perseveres in making her such an offer. But no

[055-040]doubt you feel properly.”

[055-041]“I am not at all sure that I do, aunt,” said Gwendolen, with saucy

[055-042]gravity. “I don’t know everything it is proper to feel on being

[055-043]engaged.”

[055-044]The rector patted her shoulder and smiled as at a bit of innocent

[055-045]naughtiness, and his wife took his behavior as an indication that she

[055-046]was not to be displeased. As for Anna, she kissed Gwendolen and said,

[055-047]“I do hope you will be happy,” but then sank into the background and

[055-048]tried to keep the tears back too. In the late days she had been

[055-049]imagining a little romance about Rex—how if he still longed for

[055-050]Gwendolen her heart might be softened by trouble into love, so that

[055-051]they could by-and-by be married. And the romance had turned to a prayer

[055-052]that she, Anna, might be able to rejoice like a good sister, and only

[055-053]think of being useful in working for Gwendolen, as long as Rex was not

[055-054]rich. But now she wanted grace to rejoice in something else. Miss Merry

[055-055]and the four girls, Alice with the high shoulders, Bertha and Fanny the

[055-056]whisperers, and Isabel the listener, were all present on this family

[055-057]occasion, when everything seemed appropriately turning to the honor and

[055-058]glory of Gwendolen, and real life was as interesting as “Sir Charles

[055-059]Grandison.” The evening passed chiefly in decisive remarks from the

[055-060]rector, in answer to conjectures from the two elder ladies. According

[055-061]to him, the case was not one in which he could think it his duty to

[055-062]mention settlements: everything must, and doubtless would safely be

[055-063]left to Mr. Grandcourt.

[055-064]“I should like to know exactly what sort of places [[ryelands|Ryelands]] and

[055-065][[gadsmere|Gadsmere]] are,” said Mrs. Davilow.

[055-066]“Gadsmere, I believe, is a secondary place,” said Mr. Gascoigne; “But

[055-067]Ryelands I know to be one of our finest seats. The park is extensive

[055-068]and the woods of a very valuable order. The house was built by Inigo

[055-069]Jones, and the ceilings are painted in the Italian style. The estate is

[055-070]said to be worth twelve thousand a year, and there are two livings, one

[055-071]a rectory, in the gift of the Grandcourts. There may be some burdens on

[055-072]the land. Still, Mr. Grandcourt was an only child.”

[055-073]“It would be most remarkable,” said Mrs. Gascoigne, “if he were to

[055-074]become Lord Stannery in addition to everything else. Only think: there

[055-075]is the Grandcourt estate, the Mallinger estate, _and_ the baronetcy,

[055-076]_and_ the peerage,”—she was marking off the items on her fingers, and

[055-077]paused on the fourth while she added, “but they say there will be no

[055-078]land coming to him with the peerage.” It seemed a pity there was

[055-079]nothing for the fifth finger.

[055-080]“The peerage,” said the rector, judiciously, “must be regarded as a

[055-081]remote chance. There are two cousins between the present peer and Mr.

[055-082]Grandcourt. It is certainly a serious reflection how death and other

[055-083]causes do sometimes concentrate inheritances on one man. But an excess

[055-084]of that kind is to be deprecated. To be Sir Mallinger Grandcourt

[055-085]Mallinger—I suppose that will be his style—with corresponding

[055-086]properties, is a valuable talent enough for any man to have committed

[055-087]to him. Let us hope it will be well used.”

[055-088]“And what a position for the wife, Gwendolen!” said Mrs. Gascoigne; “a

[055-089]great [[duty|responsibility]] indeed. But you must lose no time in writing to

[055-090]Mrs. Mompert, Henry. It is a good thing that you have an engagement of

[055-091]marriage to offer as an excuse, else she might feel offended. She is

[055-092]rather a high woman.”

[055-093]“I am rid of that horror,” thought Gwendolen, to whom the name of

[055-094]Mompert had become a sort of Mumbo-jumbo. She was very silent through

[055-095]the evening, and that night could hardly sleep at all in her little

[055-096]white bed. It was a rarity in her strong youth to be wakeful: and

[055-097]perhaps a still greater rarity for her to be careful that her mother

[055-098]should not know of her restlessness. But her state of mind was

[055-099]altogether new: she who had been used to feel sure of herself, and

[055-100]ready to manage others, had just taken a decisive step which she had

[055-101]beforehand thought that she would not take—nay, perhaps, was bound not

[055-102]to take. She could not go backward now; she liked a great deal of what

[055-103]lay before her; and there was nothing for her to like if she went back.

[055-104]But her resolution was dogged by the shadow of that previous resolve

[055-105]which had at first come as the undoubting movement of her whole being.

[055-106]While she lay on her pillow with wide-open eyes, “looking on darkness

[055-107]which the blind do see,” she was appalled by the idea that she was

[055-108]going to do what she had once started away from with repugnance. It was

[055-109]new to her that a question of right or wrong in her conduct should

[055-110]rouse her terror; she had known no compunction that atoning caresses

[055-111]and presents could not lay to rest. But here had come a moment when

[055-112]something like a new consciousness was awaked. She seemed on the edge

[055-113]of adopting deliberately, as a notion for all the rest of her life,

[055-114]what she had rashly said in her bitterness, when her discovery had

[055-115]driven her away to [[Leubronn]]:—that it did not signify what she did; she

[055-116]had only to amuse herself as best she could. That lawlessness, that

[055-117]casting away of all care for justification, suddenly frightened her: it

[055-118]came to her with the shadowy array of possible calamity behind

[055-119]it—calamity which had ceased to be a mere name for her; and all the

[055-120]infiltrated influences of disregarded religious teaching, as well as

[055-121]the deeper impressions of something awful and inexorable enveloping

[055-122]her, seemed to concentrate themselves in the vague conception of

[055-123]avenging power. The brilliant position she had longed for, the imagined

[055-124]freedom she would create for herself in marriage, the deliverance from

[055-125]the dull insignificance of her girlhood—all immediately before her;

[055-126]and yet they had come to her hunger like food with the taint of

[055-127]sacrilege upon it, which she must snatch with terror. In the darkness

[055-128]and loneliness of her little bed, her more resistant self could not act

[055-129]against the first onslaught of dread after her irrevocable decision.

[055-130]That unhappy-faced woman and her children—Grandcourt and his relations

[055-131]with her—kept repeating themselves in her imagination like the

[055-132]clinging memory of a [[shame|disgrace]], and gradually obliterated all other

[055-133]thought, leaving only the consciousness that she had taken those scenes

[055-134]into her life. Her long wakefulness seemed a delirium; a faint, faint

[055-135]light penetrated beside the window-curtain; the chillness increased.

[055-136]She could bear it no longer, and cried “Mamma!”

[055-137]“Yes, dear,” said Mrs. Davilow, immediately, in a wakeful voice.

[055-138]“Let me come to you.”

[055-139]She soon went to sleep on her mother’s shoulder, and slept on till

[055-140]late, when, dreaming of a lit-up ball-room, she opened her eyes on her

[055-141]mother standing by the bedside with a small packet in her hand.

[055-142]“I am sorry to wake you, darling, but I thought it better to give you

[055-143]this at once. The groom has brought Criterion; he has come on another

[055-144]horse, and says he is to stay here.”

[055-145]Gwendolen sat up in bed and opened the packet. It was a delicate

[055-146]enameled casket, and inside was a splendid diamond ring with a letter

[055-147]which contained a folded bit of colored paper and these words:

[055-148]Pray wear this ring when I come at twelve in sign of our betrothal. I

[055-149]enclose a check drawn in the name of Mr. Gascoigne, for immediate

[055-150]expenses. Of course Mrs. Davilow will remain at Offendene, at least

[055-151]for some time. I hope, when I come, you will have granted me an early

[055-152]day, when you may begin to command me at a shorter distance.—Yours

[055-153]devotedly,

[055-154]H. M. GRANDCOURT.

[055-155]The check was for five hundred pounds, and Gwendolen turned it toward

[055-156]her mother, with the letter.

[055-157]“How very kind and delicate!” said Mrs. Davilow, with much feeling.

[055-158]“But I really should like better not to be dependent on a son-in-law. I

[055-159]and the girls could get along very well.”

[055-160]“Mamma, if you say that again, I will not marry him,” said Gwendolen,

[055-161]angrily.

[055-162]“My dear child, I trust you are not going to marry only for my sake,”

[055-163]said Mrs. Davilow, deprecatingly.

[055-164]Gwendolen tossed her head on the pillow away from her mother, and let

[055-165]the ring lie. She was irritated at this attempt to take away a motive.

[055-166]Perhaps the deeper cause of her irritation was the consciousness that

[055-167]she was not going to marry solely for her mamma’s sake—that she was

[055-168]drawn toward the marriage in ways against which stronger reasons than

[055-169]her mother’s renunciation were yet not strong enough to hinder her. She

[055-170]had waked up to the signs that she was irrevocably engaged, and all the

[055-171]ugly visions, the alarms, the arguments of the night, must be met by

[055-172]daylight, in which probably they would show themselves weak. “What I

[055-173]long for is your happiness, dear,” continued Mrs. Davilow, pleadingly.

[055-174]“I will not say anything to vex you. Will you not put on the ring?”

[055-175]For a few moments Gwendolen did not answer, but her thoughts were

[055-176]active. At last she raised herself with a determination to do as she

[055-177]would do if she had started on horseback, and go on with spirit,

[055-178]whatever ideas might be running in her head.

[055-179]“I thought the lover always put on the betrothal ring himself,” she

[055-180]said laughingly, slipping the ring on her finger, and looking at it

[055-181]with a charming movement of her head. “I know why he has sent it,” she

[055-182]added, nodding at her mamma.
