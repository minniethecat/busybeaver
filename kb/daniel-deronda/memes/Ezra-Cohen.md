# Ezra Cohen

Ezra Cohen is a Jewish pawnbroker and shopkeeper in London, the proprietor of the establishment over which the name "Ezra Cohen" is "lettered above shop windows" [069-049]. He provides lodgings to [[Mordecai|Mordecai Cohen]] and his family, embodying the materially-grounded, commercially-minded side of Anglo-Jewish life that contrasts sharply with Mordecai's spiritual intensity.

## Role in the Novel

Ezra Cohen serves as an important counterpoint in the novel's exploration of Jewish identity. Where Mordecai represents visionary, prophetic Judaism — the "imagination that pierces or exalts the solid actual world" — Ezra Cohen represents the unpoetic reality of Jewish commercial life in Victorian London. The tension between these two modes of Jewish existence is central to the novel's treatment of identity.

Deronda's first approach to the Cohen household is through "this Ezra Cohen," and his initial reaction is disappointment: "this Ezra Cohen to be the most unpoetic Jew he had ever met" [070-187]. The narrative traces Deronda's gradual recognition that spiritual vision and commercial reality are not mutually exclusive, and that the "unpoetic" Cohen household contains within it the seeds of the national revival Mordecai prophesies.

### The "Unpoetic Jew" and Deronda's Education

Deronda's disappointment on first meeting Ezra Cohen is a critical moment in the novel's moral architecture. Deronda, raised as an English gentleman, approaches the Cohens with expectations shaped by romantic preconceptions — he expects the Jewish world to announce itself in terms of visible distinction, of poetry and prophecy. Ezra Cohen's ordinariness — his shop, his commercial concerns, his very name shared by "a hundred Ezra Cohens lettered above shop windows" [069-050] — constitutes a rebuke to this expectation.

Eliot structures this encounter as an education in perception. Deronda must learn to see that the unpoetic surface of the Cohen household does not negate but sustains the spiritual life within it. Mordecai's visions depend on Ezra Cohen's roof; prophecy requires pawnbroking. This recognition — that the material and the spiritual are not opposed but intertwined — is foundational to Deronda's emerging understanding of Jewish national life.

## Character Traits

### Personality

Ezra Cohen is pragmatic, commercially-minded, and grounded in the material realities of making a living. He is "not clad in the sublime" [093-115] — Eliot's pointed characterization of a man whose concerns are mundane rather than transcendent. Yet the novel does not condemn him for this; his practicality sustains the household in which Mordecai's visions take root.

His commercial identity is not presented as a moral failing. Eliot's treatment of Ezra Cohen reflects her broader commitment to representing the dignity of ordinary economic life — a theme that runs throughout her fiction, from Adam Bede's carpentry to Caleb Garth's business. Trade, in this view, is not antithetical to moral seriousness but can be its necessary foundation.

### Appearance

The shop window bearing the name "Ezra Cohen" is the character's first introduction to the reader [069-049], establishing him through his commercial identity. Deronda notes that "there might be a hundred Ezra Cohens lettered above shop windows" [069-050] — the name is common, unremarkable, its very ordinariness a rebuke to romantic expectations.

### The Pawnbroker as Social Type

Ezra Cohen belongs to a recognizable Victorian social type: the Jewish pawnbroker, a figure familiar to Eliot's contemporary readers from both life and literature. Eliot's treatment of this type is notable for its resistance to caricature. Where a lesser novelist might have made Ezra Cohen a figure of satire or sentiment, Eliot renders him with sociological precision and moral seriousness — a man whose limitations are real but whose dignity is intact.

## Relationships

- **[[Mordecai]]**: Ezra Cohen provides shelter for Mordecai, who has "taken up his abode under Ezra Cohen's roof" [086-063]. Their relationship embodies the tension between material and spiritual Judaism. That Mordecai — the visionary, the prophet, the man consumed by the idea of national revival — depends on his commercially-minded kinsman for shelter is the novel's quiet insistence that vision requires infrastructure.
- **[[Jacob-Cohen]]**: Ezra's son, a young boy whose presence humanizes the household. Through Jacob, the reader sees Ezra Cohen not only as a pawnbroker but as a father, his commercial identity softened by domestic affection.
- **[[Adelaide-Rebekah]]**: Ezra's daughter, who appears in domestic scenes that ground the Cohen household in familial warmth.
- **[[Daniel-Deronda]]**: Deronda's "meditated second visit to Ezra Cohen's" [069-120] is a crucial step in his journey toward Jewish identity. Ezra serves as Deronda's reluctant guide into the everyday reality of Anglo-Jewish life — a reality that initially disappoints but ultimately educates.
- **Mrs. Cohen**: Ezra's wife, who manages the domestic sphere of the household and contributes to the portrait of the Cohens as a functioning family unit.

## Character Arc

Ezra Cohen does not undergo transformation; rather, Deronda's perception of him transforms. What initially appears unpoetic and disappointing gradually reveals itself as the necessary material foundation for Mordecai's spiritual vision — the "other form of aristocracy" that Deronda learns to recognize [093-115].

This arc of perception is the novel's arc. Deronda begins by dividing the world into the poetic and the prosaic, the visionary and the commercial. His education — through Mordecai, through Mirah, through his discovery of his own Jewish identity — teaches him that these categories are false. Ezra Cohen, the "most unpoetic Jew," is not the opposite of Mordecai's prophetic Judaism but its condition of possibility.

## Thematic Significance

Through Ezra Cohen, Eliot explores the relationship between material and spiritual life, and between commercial and visionary modes of Jewish identity. The novel insists that the sublime does not exist apart from the mundane but emerges from within it — Mordecai's prophecy is housed under Ezra Cohen's roof, and the national revival Deronda will serve requires not only visionaries but pawnbrokers, shopkeepers, and the ordinary fabric of communal life.

### Eliot's Sociology of Jewish Life

Ezra Cohen also serves Eliot's larger project of representing the diversity of Anglo-Jewish experience. The Cohens — pawnbrokers, shopkeepers, tradesmen — occupy a different social niche from the cultivated Mirah, the scholarly Mordecai, or the assimilated Deronda. By including them, Eliot insists on the heterogeneity of Jewish life, refusing the homogenizing gaze that would reduce "the Jew" to a single type.

This sociological realism serves the novel's moral vision. If the national revival Deronda will serve is to be genuine, it must encompass Ezra Cohen as well as Mordecai — the pawnbroker as well as the prophet. The novel's democratic imagination refuses to separate the sublime from the ordinary, insisting instead on their mutual dependence.
