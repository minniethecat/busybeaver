[099-001][[Daniel-Deronda|Deronda]] to-night was stirred with the feeling that the brief remnant

[099-002]of this fervid life had become his charge. He had been peculiarly

[099-003]wrought on by what he had seen at the club of the friendly indifference

[099-004]which [[Mordecai]] must have gone on encountering. His own experience of

[099-005]the small room that ardor can make for itself in ordinary minds had had

[099-006]the effect of increasing his reserve; and while tolerance was the

[099-007]easiest attitude to him, there was another bent in him also capable of

[099-008]becoming a weakness—the dislike to appear exceptional or to risk an

[099-009]ineffective insistence on his own opinion. But such caution appeared

[099-010]contemptible to him just now, when he, for the first time, saw in a

[099-011]complete picture and felt as a reality the lives that burn themselves

[099-012]out in solitary enthusiasm: martyrs of obscure circumstance, exiled in

[099-013]the rarity of their own minds, whose deliverances in other ears are no

[099-014]more than a long passionate soliloquy—unless perhaps at last, when

[099-015]they are nearing the invisible shores, signs of recognition and

[099-016]fulfilment may penetrate the cloud of loneliness; or perhaps it may be

[099-017]with them as with the dying Copernicus made to touch the first printed

[099-018]copy of his book when the sense of touch was gone, seeing it only as a

[099-019]dim object through the deepening dusk.

[099-020]Deronda had been brought near to one of those spiritual exiles, and it

[099-021]was in his nature to feel the relation as a strong chain, nay, to feel

[099-022]his imagination moving without repugnance in the direction of

[099-023]Mordecai’s desires. With all his latent objection to schemes only

[099-024]definite in their generality and nebulous in detail—in the poise of

[099-025]his sentiments he felt at one with this man who had made a visionary

[099-026]selection of him: the lines of what may be called their emotional

[099-027]theory touched. He had not the Jewish consciousness, but he had a

[099-028]yearning, grown the stronger for the denial which had been his

[099-029]grievance, after the [[duty|obligation]] of avowed filial and social ties. His

[099-030]feeling was ready for difficult obedience. In this way it came that he

[099-031]set about his new task ungrudgingly; and again he thought of Mrs.

[099-032]Meyrick as his chief helper. To her first he must make known the

[099-033]discovery of [[Mirah-Lapidoth|Mirah]]’s brother, and with her he must consult on all

[099-034]preliminaries of bringing the mutually lost together. Happily the best

[099-035]quarter for a consumptive patient did not lie too far off the small

[099-036]house at Chelsea, and the first office Deronda had to perform for this

[099-037]Hebrew prophet who claimed him as a spiritual inheritor, was to get him

[099-038]a healthy lodging. Such is the irony of earthly mixtures, that the

[099-039]heroes have not always had carpets and teacups of their own; and, seen

[099-040]through the open window by the mackerel-vender, may have been invited

[099-041]with some hopefulness to pay three hundred per cent, in the form of

[099-042]fourpence. However, Deronda’s mind was busy with a prospective

[099-043]arrangement for giving a furnished lodging some faint likeness to a

[099-044]refined home by dismantling his own chambers of his best old books in

[099-045]vellum, his easiest chair, and the bas-reliefs of [[miltons-paradise-lost|Milton]] and [[dantes-divine-comedy|Dante]].

[099-046]But was not Mirah to be there? What furniture can give such finish to a

[099-047]room as a tender woman’s face?—and is there any harmony of tints that

[099-048]has such stirrings of delight as the sweet modulation of her voice?

[099-049]Here is one good, at least, thought Deronda, that comes to Mordecai

[099-050]from his having fixed his imagination on me. He has recovered a perfect

[099-051]sister, whose affection is waiting for him.

[099-052]CHAPTER XLIV.

[099-053]Fairy folk a-listening

[099-054]Hear the seed sprout in the spring.

[099-055]And for [[art|music]] to their dance

[099-056]Hear the hedgerows wake from trance,

[099-057]Sap that trembles into buds

[099-058]Sending little rhythmic floods

[099-059]Of fairy sound in fairy ears.

[099-060]Thus all beauty that appears

[099-061]Has birth as sound to finer sense

[099-062]And lighter-clad intelligence.

[099-063]And [[Gwendolen-Harleth|Gwendolen]]? She was thinking of Deronda much more than he was

[099-064]thinking of her—often wondering what were his ideas “about things,”

[099-065]and how his life was occupied. But a lap-dog would be necessarily at a

[099-066]loss in framing to itself the motives and adventures of doghood at

[099-067]large; and it was as far from Gwendolen’s conception that Deronda’s

[099-068]life could be determined by the historical destiny of the Jews, as that

[099-069]he could rise into the air on a brazen horse, and so vanish from her

[099-070]horizon in the form of a twinkling star.

[099-071]With all the sense of inferiority that had been forced upon her, it was

[099-072]inevitable that she should imagine a larger place for herself in his

[099-073]thoughts than she actually possessed. They must be rather old and wise

[099-074]persons who are not apt to see their own anxiety or elation about

[099-075]themselves reflected in other minds; and Gwendolen, with her youth and

[099-076]inward solitude, may be excused for dwelling on signs of special

[099-077]interest in her shown by the one person who had impressed her with the

[099-078]feeling of submission, and for mistaking the color and proportion of

[099-079]those signs in the mind of Deronda.

[099-080]Meanwhile, what would he tell her that she ought to do? “He said, I

[099-081]must get more interest in others, and more knowledge, and that I must

[099-082]care about the best things—but how am I to begin?” She wondered what

[099-083]books he would tell her to take up to her own room, and recalled the

[099-084]famous writers that she had either not looked into or had found the

[099-085]most unreadable, with a half-smiling wish that she could mischievously

[099-086]ask Deronda if they were not the books called “medicine for the mind.”

[099-087]Then she repented of her sauciness, and when she was safe from

[099-088]observation carried up a miscellaneous selection—Descartes, Bacon,

[099-089]Locke, Butler, Burke, Guizot—knowing, as a clever young lady of

[099-090]education, that these authors were ornaments of mankind, feeling sure

[099-091]that Deronda had read them, and hoping that by dipping into them all in

[099-092]succession, with her rapid understanding she might get a point of view

[099-093]nearer to his level.

[099-094]But it was astonishing how little time she found for these vast mental

[099-095]excursions. Constantly she had to be on the scene as Mrs. [[Henleigh-Grandcourt|Grandcourt]],

[099-096]and to feel herself watched in that part by the exacting eyes of a

[099-097]husband who had found a motive to exercise his tenacity—that of making

[099-098]his marriage answer all the ends he chose, and with the more

[099-099]completeness the more he discerned any opposing will in her. And she

[099-100]herself, whatever rebellion might be going on within her, could not

[099-101]have made up her mind to failure in her representation. No feeling had

[099-102]yet reconciled her for a moment to any act, word, or look that would be

[099-103]a confession to the world: and what she most dreaded in herself was any

[099-104]violent impulse that would make an involuntary confession: it was the

[099-105]will to be silent in every other direction that had thrown the more

[099-106]impetuosity into her confidences toward Deronda, to whom her thought

[099-107]continually turned as a help against herself. Her riding, her hunting,

[099-108]her visiting and receiving of visits, were all performed in a spirit of

[099-109]achievement which served instead of zest and young gladness, so that

[099-110]all around [[diplow-hall|Diplow]], in those weeks of the new year, Mrs. Grandcourt was

[099-111]regarded as wearing her honors with triumph.

[099-112]“She disguises it under an air of taking everything as a matter of

[099-113]course,” said Mrs. Arrowpoint. “A stranger might suppose that she had

[099-114]condescended rather than risen. I always noticed that doubleness in

[099-115]her.”

[099-116]To her mother most of all Gwendolen was bent on acting complete

[099-117]satisfaction, and poor [[Mrs-Davilow|Mrs. Davilow]] was so far deceived that she took

[099-118]the unexpected distance at which she was kept, in spite of what she

[099-119]felt to be Grandcourt’s handsome behavior in providing for her, as a

[099-120]comparative indifference in her daughter, now that marriage had created

[099-121]new interests. To be fetched to lunch and then to dinner along with the

[099-122]Gascoignes, to be driven back soon after breakfast the next morning,

[099-123]and to have brief calls from Gwendolen in which her husband waited for

[099-124]her outside either on horseback or sitting in the carriage, was all the

[099-125]intercourse allowed to her mother.

[099-126]The truth was, that the second time Gwendolen proposed to invite her

[099-127]mother with Mr. and Mrs. Gascoigne, Grandcourt had at first been

[099-128]silent, and then drawled, “We can’t be having _those people_ always.

[099-129]Gascoigne talks too much. Country clergy are always bores—with their

[099-130]confounded fuss about everything.”

[099-131]That speech was full of foreboding for Gwendolen. To have her mother

[099-132]classed under “those people” was enough to confirm the previous dread

[099-133]of bringing her too near. Still, she could not give the true

[099-134]reasons—she could not say to her mother, “Mr. Grandcourt wants to

[099-135]recognize you as little as possible; and besides it is better you

[099-136]should not see much of my married life, else you might find out that I

[099-137]am miserable.” So she waived as lightly as she could every allusion to

[099-138]the subject; and when Mrs. Davilow again hinted the possibility of her

[099-139]having a house close to [[ryelands|Ryelands]], Gwendolen said, “It would not be so

[099-140]nice for you as being near the rectory here, mamma. We shall perhaps be

[099-141]very little at Ryelands. You would miss my aunt and uncle.”

[099-142]And all the while this contemptuous veto of her husband’s on any

[099-143]intimacy with her family, making her proudly shrink from giving them

[099-144]the aspect of troublesome pensioners, was rousing more inward

[099-145]inclination toward them. She had never felt so kindly toward her uncle,

[099-146]so much disposed to look back on his cheerful, complacent activity and

[099-147]spirit of kind management, even when mistaken, as more of a comfort

[099-148]than the neutral loftiness which was every day chilling her. And here

[099-149]perhaps she was unconsciously finding some of that mental enlargement

[099-150]which it was hard to get from her occasional dashes into difficult

[099-151]authors, who instead of blending themselves with her daily agitations

[099-152]required her to dismiss them.

[099-153]It was a delightful surprise one day when Mr. and Mrs. Gascoigne were

[099-154]at [[Offendene]] to see Gwendolen ride up without her husband—with the

[099-155]groom only. All, including the four girls and Miss Merry, seated in the

[099-156]dining-room at lunch, could see the welcome approach; and even the

[099-157]elder ones were not without something of Isabel’s romantic sense that

[099-158]the beautiful sister on the splendid chestnut, which held its head as

[099-159]if proud to bear her, was a sort of Harriet Byron or Miss Wardour

[099-160]reappearing out of her “happiness ever after.”

[099-161]Her uncle went to the door to give her his hand, and she sprang from

[099-162]her horse with an air of alacrity which might well encourage that

[099-163]notion of guaranteed happiness; for Gwendolen was particularly bent

[099-164]to-day on setting her mother’s heart at rest, and her unusual sense of

[099-165]freedom in being able to make this visit alone enabled her to bear up

[099-166]under the pressure of painful facts which were urging themselves anew.

[099-167]The seven family kisses were not so tiresome as they used to be.

[099-168]“Mr. Grandcourt is gone out, so I determined to fill up the time by

[099-169]coming to you, mamma,” said Gwendolen, as she laid down her hat and

[099-170]seated herself next to her mother; and then looking at her with a

[099-171]playfully monitory air, “That is a punishment to you for not wearing

[099-172]better lace on your head. You didn’t think I should come and detect

[099-173]you—you dreadfully careless-about-yourself mamma!” She gave a

[099-174]caressing touch to the dear head.

[099-175]“Scold me, dear,” said Mrs. Davilow, her delicate worn face flushing

[099-176]with delight. “But I wish there was something you could eat after your

[099-177]ride—instead of these scraps. Let Jocosa make you a cup of chocolate

[099-178]in your old way. You used to like that.”

[099-179]Miss Merry immediately rose and went out, though Gwendolen said, “Oh,

[099-180]no, a piece of bread, or one of those hard biscuits. I can’t think

[099-181]about eating. I am come to say good-bye.”
