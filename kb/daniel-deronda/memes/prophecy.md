# Prophecy

**Prophecy** in *Daniel Deronda* is embodied primarily in the figure of [[Mordecai]], whose visionary anticipation of a restored Jewish nation shapes the novel's philosophical and political arc.

## Mordecai's Prophetic Vision

[[Mordecai]] is presented as a prophet-figure—not in the supernatural sense, but as a person of extraordinary moral and historical vision. His certainty that a successor will appear to carry forward the work of Jewish restoration is vindicated when [[Daniel-Deronda]] enters his life.[^1] Mordecai's prophecy operates through personal connection rather than revelation.

## Prophecy and Destiny

The novel explores whether prophecy is prediction or creation. Mordecai's visions do not merely foretell the future; they actively shape it by drawing Deronda into the orbit of Jewish national aspiration.[^2] This active conception of prophecy aligns with Eliot's emphasis on the power of human will and sympathy to shape history.

## The Prophetic Tradition

Eliot situates Mordecai within the long tradition of Hebrew prophecy, from Isaiah to the medieval poets of Zion.[^3] His mode of speech—elevated, metaphorical, urgent—echoes biblical cadences while addressing distinctly modern concerns about nationhood and identity.[^4] The novel suggests that the prophetic tradition remains alive and capable of speaking to contemporary moral questions.

## Prophecy and Sympathy

Eliot connects prophecy to her philosophy of sympathy. Mordecai's prophetic vision is not cold prediction but passionate engagement with the fate of others—a form of extended sympathy that encompasses generations past and future.[^6] His description as "a frail incorporation of the national consciousness" suggests that prophecy is essentially an intensification of the sympathetic imagination that Eliot values throughout her fiction.[^7]

This link between prophecy and sympathy transforms what might seem an archaic religious concept into an extension of the novel's central moral psychology. To prophesy, in Eliot's rendering, is to feel so deeply with a people that one can discern the shape of their future.

## The Personal Cost of Prophecy

The novel does not romanticize prophecy. Mordecai's physical frailty—his consumption, his poverty, his social marginality—suggests that prophetic vision exacts a toll on the individual who bears it.[^8] His death shortly after finding his successor in Deronda completes this pattern: the prophet prepares the way but does not enter the promised future.
