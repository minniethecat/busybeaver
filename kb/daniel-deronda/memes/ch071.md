[071-001]“I have a fine diamond ring to offer as security—not with me at this

[071-002]moment, unfortunately, for I am not in the habit of wearing it. But I

[071-003]will come again this evening and bring it with me. Fifty pounds at once

[071-004]would be a convenience to me.”

[071-005]“Well, you know, this evening is the Sabbath, young gentleman,” said

[071-006]Cohen, “and I go to the _Shool_. The shop will be closed. But

[071-007]accommodation is a work of charity; if you can’t get here before, and

[071-008]are any ways pressed—why, I’ll look at your diamond. You’re perhaps

[071-009]from the West End—a longish [[ambition|drive]]?”

[071-010]“Yes; and your Sabbath begins early at this season. I could be here by

[071-011]five—will that do?” [[Daniel-Deronda|Deronda]] had not been without hope that by asking

[071-012]to come on a Friday evening he might get a better opportunity of

[071-013]observing points in the family character, and might even be able to put

[071-014]some decisive question.

[071-015]Cohen assented; but here the marvelous Jacob, whose _physique_

[071-016]supported a precocity that would have shattered a Gentile of his years,

[071-017]showed that he had been listening with much comprehension by saying,

[071-018]“You are coming again. Have you got any more knives at home?”

[071-019]“I think I have one,” said Deronda, smiling down at him.

[071-020]“Has it two blades and a hook—and a white handle like that?” said

[071-021]Jacob, pointing to the waistcoat-pocket.

[071-022]“I dare say it has.”

[071-023]“Do you like a cork-screw?” said Jacob, exhibiting that article in his

[071-024]own knife again, and looking up with serious inquiry.

[071-025]“Yes,” said Deronda, experimentally.

[071-026]“Bring your knife, then, and we’ll shwop,” said Jacob, returning the

[071-027]knife to his pocket, and stamping about with the sense that he had

[071-028]concluded a good transaction.

[071-029]The grandmother had now recovered her usual manners, and the whole

[071-030]family watched Deronda radiantly when he caressingly lifted the little

[071-031]girl, to whom he had not hitherto given attention, and seating her on

[071-032]the counter, asked for her name also. She looked at him in silence, and

[071-033]put her fingers to her gold earrings, which he did not seem to have

[071-034]noticed.

[071-035]“Adelaide Rebekah is her name,” said her mother, proudly. “Speak to the

[071-036]gentleman, lovey.”

[071-037]“Shlav’m Shabbes fyock on,” said Adelaide Rebekah.

[071-038]“Her Sabbath frock, she means,” said the father, in explanation.

[071-039]“She’ll have her Sabbath frock on this evening.”

[071-040]“And will you let me see you in it, Adelaide?” said Deronda, with that

[071-041]gentle intonation which came very easily to him.

[071-042]“Say yes, lovey—yes, if you please, sir,” said her mother, enchanted

[071-043]with this handsome young gentleman, who appreciated remarkable children.

[071-044]“And will you give me a kiss this evening?” said Deronda with a hand on

[071-045]each of her little brown shoulders.

[071-046]Adelaide Rebekah (her miniature crinoline and monumental features

[071-047]corresponded with the combination of her names) immediately put up her

[071-048]lips to pay the kiss in advance; whereupon her father rising in still

[071-049]more glowing satisfaction with the general meritoriousness of his

[071-050]circumstances, and with the stranger who was an admiring witness, said

[071-051]cordially,

[071-052]“You see there’s somebody will be disappointed if you don’t come this

[071-053]evening, sir. You won’t mind sitting down in our family place and

[071-054]waiting a bit for me, if I’m not in when you come, sir? I’ll stretch a

[071-055]point to accommodate a gent of your sort. Bring the diamond, and I’ll

[071-056]see what I can do for you.”

[071-057]Deronda thus left the most favorable impression behind him, as a

[071-058]preparation for more easy intercourse. But for his own part those

[071-059]amenities had been carried on under the heaviest spirits. If these were

[071-060]really [[Mirah-Lapidoth|Mirah]]’s relatives, he could not imagine that even her fervid

[071-061]filial piety could give the reunion with them any sweetness beyond such

[071-062]as could be found in the strict fulfillment of a painful duty. What did

[071-063]this vaunting brother need? And with the most favorable supposition

[071-064]about the hypothetic mother, Deronda shrank from the image of a first

[071-065]meeting between her and Mirah, and still more from the idea of Mirah’s

[071-066]domestication with this family. He took refuge in disbelief. To find an

[071-067][[Mordecai|Ezra]] Cohen when the name was running in your head was no more

[071-068]extraordinary than to find a Josiah Smith under like circumstances; and

[071-069]as to the coincidence about the daughter, it would probably turn out to

[071-070]be a difference. If, however, further knowledge confirmed the more

[071-071]undesirable conclusion, what would be wise expediency?—to try and

[071-072]determine the best consequences by [[secrecy|concealment]], or to brave other

[071-073]consequences for the sake of that openness which is the sweet fresh air

[071-074]of our moral life.

[071-075]CHAPTER XXXIV.

[071-076]“Er ist geheissen

[071-077]Israel. Ihn hat verwandelt

[071-078]Hexenspruch in einen Hund.

[071-079]* * * * *

[071-080]Aber jeden Freitag Abend,

[071-081]In der Dämm’rungstunde, plötzlich

[071-082]Weicht der Zauber, und der Hund

[071-083]Wird aufs Neu’ ein menschlich Wesen.”

[071-084]—HEINE: _Prinzessin Sabbath_.

[071-085]When Deronda arrived at five o’clock, the shop was closed and the door

[071-086]was opened for him by the Christian servant. When she showed him into

[071-087]the room behind the shop he was surprised at the prettiness of the

[071-088]scene. The house was old, and rather extensive at the back: probably

[071-089]the large room he now entered was gloomy by daylight, but now it was

[071-090]agreeably lit by a fine old brass lamp with seven oil-lights hanging

[071-091]above the snow-white cloth spread on the central table. The ceiling and

[071-092]walls were smoky, and all the surroundings were dark enough to throw

[071-093]into relief the human figures, which had a Venetian glow of coloring.

[071-094]The grandmother was arrayed in yellowish brown with a large gold chain

[071-095]in lieu of the necklace, and by this light her yellow face with its

[071-096]darkly-marked eyebrows and framing roll of gray hair looked as handsome

[071-097]as was necessary for picturesque effect. Young Mrs. Cohen was clad in

[071-098]red and black, with a string of large artificial pearls wound round and

[071-099]round her neck: the baby lay asleep in the cradle under a scarlet

[071-100]counterpane; Adelaide Rebekah was in braided amber, and Jacob Alexander

[071-101]was in black velveteen with scarlet stockings. As the four pairs of

[071-102]black eyes all glistened a welcome at Deronda, he was almost ashamed of

[071-103]the supercilious dislike these happy-looking creatures had raised in

[071-104]him by daylight. Nothing could be more cordial than the greeting he

[071-105]received, and both mother and grandmother seemed to gather more dignity

[071-106]from being seen on the private hearth, showing hospitality. He looked

[071-107]round with some wonder at the old furniture: the oaken bureau and high

[071-108]side-table must surely be mere matters of chance and economy, and not

[071-109]due to the family taste. A large dish of blue and yellow ware was set

[071-110]up on the side-table, and flanking it were two old silver vessels; in

[071-111]front of them a large volume in darkened vellum with a deep-ribbed

[071-112]back. In the corner at the farther end was an open door into an inner

[071-113]room, where there was also a light.

[071-114]Deronda took in these details by parenthetic glances while he met

[071-115]Jacob’s pressing solicitude about the knife. He had taken the pains to

[071-116]buy one with the requisites of the hook and white handle, and produced

[071-117]it on demand, saying,

[071-118]“Is that the sort of thing you want, Jacob?”

[071-119]It was subjected to a severe scrutiny, the hook and blades were opened,

[071-120]and the article of barter with the cork-screw was drawn forth for

[071-121]comparison.

[071-122]“Why do you like a hook better than a cork-screw?” said Deronda.

[071-123]“’Caush I can get hold of things with a hook. A cork-screw won’t go into

[071-124]anything but corks. But it’s better for you, you can draw corks.”

[071-125]“You agree to change, then?” said Deronda, observing that the

[071-126]grandmother was listening with delight.

[071-127]“What else have you got in your pockets?” said Jacob, with deliberative

[071-128]seriousness.

[071-129]“Hush, hush, Jacob, love,” said the grandmother. And Deronda, mindful

[071-130]of discipline, answered,

[071-131]“I think I must not tell you that. Our business was with the knives.”

[071-132]Jacob looked up into his face scanningly for a moment or two, and

[071-133]apparently arriving at his conclusions, said gravely,

[071-134]“I’ll shwop,” handing the cork-screw knife to Deronda, who pocketed it

[071-135]with corresponding gravity.

[071-136]Immediately the small son of Shem ran off into the next room, whence

[071-137]his voice was heard in rapid chat; and then ran back again—when,

[071-138]seeing his father enter, he seized a little velveteen hat which lay on

[071-139]a chair and put it on to approach him. Cohen kept on his own hat, and

[071-140]took no notice of the visitor, but stood still while the two children

[071-141]went up to him and clasped his knees: then he laid his hands on each in

[071-142]turn and uttered his Hebrew benediction; whereupon the wife, who had

[071-143]lately taken baby from the cradle, brought it up to her husband and

[071-144]held it under his outstretched hands, to be blessed in its sleep. For

[071-145]the moment, Deronda thought that this pawnbroker, proud of his

[071-146]vocation, was not utterly prosaic.

[071-147]“Well, sir, you found your welcome in my family, I think,” said Cohen,

[071-148]putting down his hat and becoming his former self. “And you’ve been

[071-149]punctual. Nothing like a little stress here,” he added, tapping his

[071-150]side pocket as he sat down. “It’s good for us all in our turn. I’ve

[071-151]felt it when I’ve had to make up payments. I began to fit every sort of

[071-152]box. It’s bracing to the mind. Now then! let us see, let us see.”

[071-153]“That is the ring I spoke of,” said Deronda, taking it from his finger.

[071-154]“I believe it cost a hundred pounds. It will be a sufficient pledge to

[071-155]you for fifty, I think. I shall probably redeem it in a month or so.”

[071-156]Cohen’s glistening eyes seemed to get a little nearer together as he

[071-157]met the ingenuous look of this crude young gentleman, who apparently

[071-158]supposed that redemption was a satisfaction to pawnbrokers. He took the

[071-159]ring, examined and returned it, saying with indifference, “Good, good.

[071-160]We’ll talk of it after our meal. Perhaps you’ll join us, if you’ve no

[071-161]objection. Me and my wife’ll feel honored, and so will mother; won’t

[071-162]you, mother?”

[071-163]The invitation was doubly echoed, and Deronda gladly accepted it. All

[071-164]now turned and stood round the table. No dish was at present seen

[071-165]except one covered with a napkin; and Mrs. Cohen had placed a china

[071-166]bowl near her husband that he might wash his hands in it. But after

[071-167]putting on his hat again, he paused, and called in a loud voice,

[071-168]“Mordecai!”

[071-169]Can this be part of the religious ceremony? thought Deronda, not

[071-170]knowing what might be expected of the ancient hero. But he heard a

[071-171]“Yes” from the next room, which made him look toward the open door; and

[071-172]there, to his astonishment, he saw the figure of the enigmatic Jew whom

[071-173]he had this morning met with in the book-shop. Their eyes met, and

[071-174]Mordecai looked as much surprised as Deronda—neither in his surprise

[071-175]making any sign of recognition. But when Mordecai was seating himself

[071-176]at the end of the table, he just bent his head to the guest in a cold

[071-177]and distant manner, as if the disappointment of the morning remained a

[071-178]disagreeable association with this new acquaintance.

[071-179]Cohen now washed his hands, pronouncing Hebrew words the while:

[071-180]afterward, he took off the napkin covering the dish and disclosed the

[071-181]two long flat loaves besprinkled with seed—the memorial of the manna

[071-182]that fed the wandering forefathers—and breaking off small pieces gave

[071-183]one to each of the family, including Adelaide Rebekah, who stood on the

[071-184]chair with her whole length exhibited in her amber-colored garment, her

[071-185]little Jewish nose lengthened by compression of the lip in the effort

[071-186]to make a suitable appearance. Cohen then uttered another Hebrew

[071-187]blessing, and after that, the male heads were uncovered, all seated

[071-188]themselves, and the meal went on without any peculiarity that

[071-189]interested Deronda. He was not very conscious of what dishes he ate

[071-190]from; being preoccupied with a desire to turn the conversation in a way

[071-191]that would enable him to ask some leading question; and also thinking

[071-192]of Mordecai, between whom and himself there was an exchange of

[071-193]fascinated, half-furtive glances. Mordecai had no handsome Sabbath

[071-194]garment, but instead of the threadbare rusty black coat of the morning

[071-195]he wore one of light drab, which looked as if it had once been a

[071-196]handsome loose paletot now shrunk with washing; and this change of

[071-197]clothing gave a still stronger accentuation to his dark-haired, eager

[071-198]face which might have belonged to the prophet Ezekiel—also probably

[071-199]not modish in the eyes of contemporaries. It was noticeable that the

[071-200]thin tails of the fried fish were given to Mordecai; and in general the

[071-201]sort of share assigned to a poor relation—no doubt a “survival” of

[071-202]prehistoric practice, not yet generally admitted to be superstitious.
