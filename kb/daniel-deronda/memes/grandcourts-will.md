# The Reading of Grandcourt's Will

Henleigh Grandcourt's will, revealed after his drowning, punishes Gwendolen with the precision of lifelong malice: she inherits nothing, her marriage has purchased her no security, and the estates she reigned over as Grandcourt's wife pass to others — the dead man's final assertion of control.

## Context

Grandcourt's death by drowning has appeared to liberate Gwendolen from his cruelty, but the will reveals that his control extended beyond the grave. Grandcourt, who treated marriage as acquisition and his wife as property, has arranged his affairs to ensure that Gwendolen cannot profit from her widowhood. The context is one of comprehensive dispossession: Gwendolen, who married for security and status, now has neither.

## The Event

The will is read in the aftermath of Grandcourt's death. Its terms are devastating: Gwendolen receives virtually nothing. The estates — Ryelands, Diplow, Gadsmere — pass to other heirs. The fortune she expected to secure through marriage is denied her. Grandcourt has engineered his final revenge: the woman who married him for material advantage will receive no material benefit from her ordeal. The will is thus the completion of Lydia Glasher's curse — Gwendolen has taken what was not hers, and she is left with nothing but the guilt of having wished for what has now occurred.

## Participants

- **Gwendolen Harleth**: The disinherited widow; the will completes her moral education by stripping away the material rewards she married for, leaving only the moral knowledge she has gained through suffering

## Consequences

The will forces Gwendolen to confront the full consequences of her marriage. She is not a wealthy widow but a dependent, returned to the modest circumstances she sought to escape. Yet this dispossession is also, paradoxically, a form of liberation: released from the material benefits of her marriage, Gwendolen can begin to construct a life based on values other than acquisition.

## Thematic Significance

The will scene is the novel's most concentrated critique of the marriage market. Grandcourt's will demonstrates that marriage as a property transaction leaves the wife with no property — her position is entirely dependent on her husband's pleasure, and his displeasure can reach beyond the grave. The scene thus exposes the fiction of security that the marriage market promises: the woman who marries for money may end with neither love nor money.
