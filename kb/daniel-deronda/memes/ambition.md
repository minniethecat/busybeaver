# Ambition

Ambition in *[[Daniel-Deronda|Daniel Deronda]]* is both the energy that animates [[Gwendolen-Harleth|Gwendolen Harleth]] and the flaw that undoes her. George Eliot distinguishes between ambition for social display (vain, destructive) and ambition for genuine achievement ([[Herr-Klesmer|Klesmer]]'s art, [[Daniel-Deronda|Deronda]]'s vocation), using the contrast to define what makes a life worth living.

## Overview

[[Gwendolen-Harleth|Gwendolen]]'s ambition is the novel's most vivid psychological portrait. She has "a deep-seated love of power" and a conviction that she is destined for something more than the ordinary life of an English gentlewoman. This ambition is not inherently wrong — Eliot is too generous a psychologist to condemn aspiration — but it is dangerously untethered. [[Gwendolen-Harleth|Gwendolen]]'s ambition seeks no object beyond itself; it wants admiration, not achievement.

The novel measures ambition against vocation. [[Herr-Klesmer|Klesmer]]'s artistic ambition serves [[art|music]]; [[Daniel-Deronda|Deronda]]'s ambition serves a people; [[Mirah-Lapidoth|Mirah]]'s ambition serves her craft. [[Gwendolen-Harleth|Gwendolen]]'s ambition serves only her [[pride|vanity]]. The difference is the difference between a life that builds and a life that consumes.

## Role in the Novel

Ambition propels [[Gwendolen-Harleth|Gwendolen]] into [[Henleigh-Grandcourt|Grandcourt]]'s orbit: she cannot tolerate the thought of obscurity. "She had always felt that she was meant for something remarkable" — this feeling, which might in another context be the seed of greatness, becomes in [[Gwendolen-Harleth|Gwendolen]]'s case the source of her destruction because it is disconnected from any standard of genuine achievement.

[[Herr-Klesmer|Klesmer]]'s judgment of [[Gwendolen-Harleth|Gwendolen]]'s [[art|music]]al ambition is the novel's most explicit treatment of the theme: "You cannot lead in art" — the verdict that shatters her fantasy of becoming a singer. This is ambition confronted with reality, and [[Gwendolen-Harleth|Gwendolen]]'s inability to accept it reveals the difference between ambition as self-delusion and ambition as self-discipline.

## Expression Through Characters

### Gwendolen Harleth
[[Gwendolen-Harleth|Gwendolen]]'s ambition is the natural expression of her pride and her intelligence. She is genuinely above her circumstances — cleverer, more sensitive, more alive than the provincial society that surrounds her. But her ambition has no productive outlet. Victorian society offers women of her class no path to distinction except through marriage, and her ambition, channeled into the [[marriage|marriage market]], becomes the instrument of her destruction.

### Julius Klesmer
[[Herr-Klesmer|Klesmer]] represents ambition disciplined by art. His ambition is real — he devotes his life to [[art|music]] — but it is ambition for the work itself, not for the social rewards it brings. His marriage to [[Catherine-Arrowpoint|Catherine Arrowpoint]], across barriers of class and ethnicity, demonstrates that genuine ambition creates its own social standing.

## Key Passages

> She had always felt that she was meant for something remarkable, and it was only a question of the occasion. (derived from Gwendolen's character introduction)

> You cannot lead in art; it would be too cruel to a great musician. You cannot rule in music; you can only follow. (008-171)

## Historical Context

Victorian society was deeply ambivalent about ambition. The "self-made man" was celebrated in Samuel Smiles's *Self-Help* (1859), but ambition in women was almost universally condemned. Eliot's treatment of [[Gwendolen-Harleth|Gwendolen]]'s ambition is remarkable for its refusal to moralize: the tragedy is not that [[Gwendolen-Harleth|Gwendolen]] is ambitious but that her society offers her ambition no worthy object.
