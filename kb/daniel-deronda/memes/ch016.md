[016-001]“Yes, to get my bread there. I should like to build a hut, and work

[016-002]hard at clearing, and have everything wild about me, and a great wide

[016-003]quiet.”

[016-004]“And not take me with you?” said Anna, the big tears coming fast.

[016-005]“How could I?”

[016-006]“I should like it better than anything; and settlers go with their

[016-007]families. I would sooner go there than stay here in England. I could

[016-008]make the fires, and mend the clothes, and cook the food; and I could

[016-009]learn how to make the bread before we went. It would be nicer than

[016-010]anything—like playing at life over again, as we used to do when we

[016-011]made our tent with the drugget, and had our little plates and dishes.”

[016-012]“Father and mother would not let you go.”

[016-013]“Yes, I think they would, when I explained everything. It would save

[016-014]money; and papa would have more to bring up the boys with.”

[016-015]There was further talk of the same practical kind at intervals, and it

[016-016]ended in [[Rex-Gascoigne|Rex]]’s being obliged to consent that Anna should go with him

[016-017]when he spoke to his father on the subject.

[016-018]Of course it was when the rector was alone in his study. Their mother

[016-019]would become reconciled to whatever he decided on, but mentioned to her

[016-020]first, the question would have distressed her.

[016-021]“Well, my children!” said Mr. Gascoigne, cheerfully, as they entered.

[016-022]It was a comfort to see Rex about again.

[016-023]“May we sit down with you a little, papa?” said Anna. “Rex has

[016-024]something to say.”

[016-025]“With all my heart.”

[016-026]It was a noticeable group that these three creatures made, each of them

[016-027]with a face of the same structural type—the straight brow, the nose

[016-028]suddenly straightened from an intention of being aquiline, the short

[016-029]upper lip, the short but strong and well-hung chin: there was even the

[016-030]same tone of complexion and set of the eye. The gray-haired father was

[016-031]at once massive and keen-looking; there was a perpendicular line in his

[016-032]brow which when he spoke with any force of interest deepened; and the

[016-033]habit of ruling gave him an air of reserved authoritativeness. Rex

[016-034]would have seemed a vision of his father’s youth, if it had been

[016-035]possible to imagine Mr. Gascoigne without distinct plans and without

[016-036]command, smitten with a heart sorrow, and having no more notion of

[016-037][[secrecy|concealment]] than a sick animal; and Anna was a tiny copy of Rex, with

[016-038]hair drawn back and knotted, her face following his in its changes of

[016-039]expression, as if they had one soul between them.

[016-040]“You know all about what has upset me, father,” Rex began, and Mr.

[016-041]Gascoigne nodded.

[016-042]“I am quite done up for life in this part of the world. I am sure it

[016-043]will be no use my going back to Oxford. I couldn’t do any reading. I

[016-044]should fail, and cause you expense for nothing. I want to have your

[016-045]consent to take another course, sir.”

[016-046]Mr. Gascoigne nodded more slowly, the perpendicular line on his brow

[016-047]deepened, and Anna’s trembling increased.

[016-048]“If you would allow me a small outfit, I should like to go to the

[016-049]colonies and work on the land there.” Rex thought the vagueness of the

[016-050]phrase prudential; “the colonies” necessarily embracing more

[016-051]advantages, and being less capable of being rebutted on a single ground

[016-052]than any particular settlement.

[016-053]“Oh, and with me, papa,” said Anna, not bearing to be left out from the

[016-054]proposal even temporarily. “Rex would want some one to take care of

[016-055]him, you know—some one to keep house. And we shall never, either of

[016-056]us, be married. And I should cost nothing, and I should be so happy. I

[016-057]know it would be hard to leave you and mamma; but there are all the

[016-058]others to bring up, and we two should be no trouble to you any more.”

[016-059]Anna had risen from her seat, and used the feminine argument of going

[016-060]closer to her papa as she spoke. He did not smile, but he drew her on

[016-061]his knee and held her there, as if to put her gently out of the

[016-062]question while he spoke to Rex.

[016-063]“You will admit that my experience gives me some power of judging for

[016-064]you, and that I can probably guide you in practical matters better than

[016-065]you can guide yourself?”

[016-066]Rex was obliged to say, “Yes, sir.”

[016-067]“And perhaps you will admit—though I don’t wish to press that

[016-068]point—that you are bound in duty to consider my judgment and wishes?”

[016-069]“I have never yet placed myself in opposition to you, sir.” Rex in his

[016-070]secret soul could not feel that he was bound not to go to the colonies,

[016-071]but to go to Oxford again—which was the point in question.

[016-072]“But you will do so if you persist in setting your mind toward a rash

[016-073]and foolish procedure, and deafening yourself to considerations which

[016-074]my experience of life assures me of. You think, I suppose, that you

[016-075]have had a shock which has changed all your inclinations, stupefied

[016-076]your brains, unfitted you for anything but manual labor, and given you

[016-077]a dislike to society? Is that what you believe?”

[016-078]“Something like that. I shall never be up to the sort of work I must do

[016-079]to live in this part of the world. I have not the spirit for it. I

[016-080]shall never be the same again. And without any disrespect to you,

[016-081]father, I think a young fellow should be allowed to choose his way of

[016-082]life, if he does nobody any harm. There are plenty to stay at home, and

[016-083]those who like might be allowed to go where there are empty places.”

[016-084]“But suppose I am convinced on good evidence—as I am—that this state

[016-085]of mind of yours is transient, and that if you went off as you propose,

[016-086]you would by-and-by repent, and feel that you had let yourself slip

[016-087]back from the point you have been gaining by your education till now?

[016-088]Have you not strength of mind enough to see that you had better act on

[016-089]my assurance for a time, and test it? In my opinion, so far from

[016-090]agreeing with you that you should be free to turn yourself into a

[016-091]colonist and work in your shirt-sleeves with spade and hatchet—in my

[016-092]opinion you have no right whatever to expatriate yourself until you

[016-093]have honestly endeavored to turn to account the education you have

[016-094]received here. I say nothing of the grief to your mother and me.”

[016-095]“I’m very sorry; but what can I do? I can’t study—that’s certain,”

[016-096]said Rex.

[016-097]“Not just now, perhaps. You will have to miss a term. I have made

[016-098]arrangements for you—how you are to spend the next two months. But I

[016-099]confess I am disappointed in you, Rex. I thought you had more sense

[016-100]than to take up such ideas—to suppose that because you have fallen

[016-101]into a very common trouble, such as most men have to go through, you

[016-102]are loosened from all bonds of duty—just as if your brain had softened

[016-103]and you were no longer a responsible being.”

[016-104]What could Rex say? Inwardly he was in a state of rebellion, but he had

[016-105]no arguments to meet his father’s; and while he was feeling, in spite

[016-106]of any thing that might be said, that he should like to go off to “the

[016-107]colonies” to-morrow, it lay in a deep fold of his consciousness that he

[016-108]ought to feel—if he had been a better fellow he would have felt—more

[016-109]about his old ties. This is the sort of [[religion|faith]] we live by in our soul

[016-110]sicknesses.

[016-111]Rex got up from his seat, as if he held the conference to be at an end.

[016-112]“You assent to my arrangement, then?” said Mr. Gascoigne, with that

[016-113]distinct resolution of tone which seems to hold one in a vise.

[016-114]There was a little pause before Rex answered, “I’ll try what I can do,

[016-115]sir. I can’t promise.” His thought was, that trying would be of no use.

[016-116]Her father kept Anna, holding her fast, though she wanted to follow

[016-117]Rex. “Oh, papa,” she said, the tears coming with her words when the

[016-118]door had closed; “it is very hard for him. Doesn’t he look ill?”

[016-119]“Yes, but he will soon be better; it will all blow over. And now, Anna,

[016-120]be as quiet as a mouse about it all. Never let it be mentioned when he

[016-121]is gone.”

[016-122]“No, papa. But I would not be like [[Gwendolen-Harleth|Gwendolen]] for any thing—to have

[016-123]people fall in love with me so. It is very dreadful.”

[016-124]Anna dared not say that she was disappointed at not being allowed to go

[016-125]to the colonies with Rex; but that was her secret feeling, and she

[016-126]often afterward went inwardly over the whole affair, saying to herself,

[016-127]“I should have done with going out, and gloves, and crinoline, and

[016-128]having to talk when I am taken to dinner—and all that!”

[016-129]I like to mark the time, and connect the course of individual lives

[016-130]with the historic stream, for all classes of thinkers. This was the

[016-131]period when the broadening of gauge in crinolines seemed to demand an

[016-132]agitation for the general enlargement of churches, ball-rooms, and

[016-133]vehicles. But Anna Gascoigne’s figure would only allow the size of

[016-134]skirt manufactured for young ladies of fourteen.

[016-135]CHAPTER IX.

[016-136]I’ll tell thee, Berthold, what men’s hopes are like:

[016-137]A silly child that, quivering with joy,

[016-138]Would cast its little mimic fishing-line

[016-139]Baited with loadstone for a bowl of toys

[016-140]In the salt ocean.

[016-141]Eight months after the arrival of the family at [[Offendene]], that is to

[016-142]say in the end of the following June, a rumor was spread in the

[016-143]neighborhood which to many persons was matter of exciting interest. It

[016-144]had no reference to the results of the American war, but it was one

[016-145]which touched all classes within a certain circuit round Wanchester:

[016-146]the corn-factors, the brewers, the horse-dealers, and saddlers, all

[016-147]held it a laudable thing, and one which was to be rejoiced in on

[016-148]abstract grounds, as showing the value of an aristocracy in a free

[016-149]country like England; the blacksmith in the hamlet of [[diplow-hall|Diplow]] felt that

[016-150]a good time had come round; the wives of laboring men hoped their

[016-151]nimble boys of ten or twelve would be taken into employ by the

[016-152]gentlemen in livery; and the farmers about Diplow admitted, with a

[016-153]tincture of bitterness and reserve, that a man might now again perhaps

[016-154]have an easier market or exchange for a rick of old hay or a wagon-load

[016-155]of straw. If such were the hopes of low persons not in society, it may

[016-156]be easily inferred that their betters had better reasons for

[016-157]satisfaction, probably connected with the pleasures of life rather than

[016-158]its business. Marriage, however, must be considered as coming under

[016-159]both heads; and just as when a visit of majesty is announced, the dream

[016-160]of knighthood or a baronetcy is to be found under various municipal

[016-161]nightcaps, so the news in question raised a floating indeterminate

[016-162]vision of marriage in several well-bred imaginations.

[016-163]The news was that Diplow Hall, [[Sir-Hugo-Mallinger|Sir Hugo Mallinger]]’s place, which had

[016-164]for a couple of years turned its white window-shutters in a painfully

[016-165]wall-eyed manner on its fine elms and beeches, its lilied pool and

[016-166]grassy acres specked with deer, was being prepared for a tenant, and

[016-167]was for the rest of the summer and through the hunting season to be

[016-168]inhabited in a fitting style both as to house and stable. But not by

[016-169]Sir Hugo himself: by his nephew, Mr. Mallinger [[Henleigh-Grandcourt|Grandcourt]], who was

[016-170]presumptive heir to the baronetcy, his uncle’s marriage having produced

[016-171]nothing but girls. Nor was this the only contingency with which fortune

[016-172]flattered young Grandcourt, as he was pleasantly called; for while the

[016-173]chance of the baronetcy came through his father, his mother had given a

[016-174]baronial streak to his blood, so that if certain intervening persons

[016-175]slightly painted in the middle distance died, he would become a baron

[016-176]and peer of this realm.
