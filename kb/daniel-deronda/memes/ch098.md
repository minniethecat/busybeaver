[098-001]Every one felt that the talk was ended, and the tone of phlegmatic

[098-002]discussion made unseasonable by [[Mordecai]]’s high-pitched solemnity. It

[098-003]was as if they had come together to hear the blowing of the _shophar_,

[098-004]and had nothing to do now but to disperse. The movement was unusually

[098-005]general, and in less than ten minutes the room was empty of all except

[098-006]Mordecai and [[Daniel-Deronda|Deronda]]. “Good-nights” had been given to Mordecai, but it

[098-007]was evident he had not heard them, for he remained rapt and motionless.

[098-008]Deronda would not disturb this needful rest, but waited for a

[098-009]spontaneous movement.

[098-010]CHAPTER XLIII.

[098-011]“My spirit is too weak; mortality

[098-012]Weighs heavily on me like unwilling sleep,

[098-013]And each imagined pinnacle and steep

[098-014]Of godlike hardship tells me I must die

[098-015]Like a sick eagle looking at the sky.”

[098-016]—KEATS.

[098-017]After a few minutes the unwonted stillness had penetrated Mordecai’s

[098-018]consciousness, and he looked up at Deronda, not in the least with

[098-019]bewilderment and surprise, but with a gaze full of reposing

[098-020]satisfaction. Deronda rose and placed his chair nearer, where there

[098-021]could be no imagined need for raising the voice. Mordecai felt the

[098-022]action as a patient feels the gentleness that eases his pillow. He

[098-023]began to speak in a low tone, as if he were only thinking articulately,

[098-024]not trying to reach an audience.

[098-025]“In the doctrine of the Cabbala, souls are born again and again in new

[098-026]bodies till they are perfected and purified, and a soul liberated from

[098-027]a worn-out body may join the fellow-soul that needs it, that they may

[098-028]be perfected together, and their earthly work accomplished. Then they

[098-029]will depart from the mortal region, and leave place for new souls to be

[098-030]born out of the store in the eternal bosom. It is the lingering

[098-031]imperfection of the souls already born into the mortal region that

[098-032]hinders the birth of new souls and the preparation of the Messianic

[098-033]time:—thus the mind has given shape to what is hidden, as the shadow

[098-034]of what is known, and has spoken truth, though it were only in parable.

[098-035]When my long-wandering soul is liberated from this weary body, it will

[098-036]join yours, and its work will be perfected.”

[098-037]Mordecai’s pause seemed an appeal which Deronda’s feeling would not let

[098-038]him leave unanswered. He tried to make it truthful; but for Mordecai’s

[098-039]ear it was inevitably filled with unspoken meaning. He only said,

[098-040]“Everything I can in conscience do to make your life effective I will

[098-041]do.”

[098-042]“I know it,” said Mordecai, in a tone of quiet certainty which

[098-043]dispenses with further assurance. “I heard it. You see it all—you are

[098-044]by my side on the mount of vision, and behold the paths of fulfillment

[098-045]which others deny.”

[098-046]He was silent a moment or two, and then went on meditatively,

[098-047]“You will take up my life where it was broken. I feel myself back in

[098-048]that day when my life was broken. The bright morning sun was on the

[098-049]quay—it was at Trieste—the garments of men from all nations shone

[098-050]like jewels—the boats were pushing off—the Greek vessel that would

[098-051]land us at Beyrout was to start in an hour. I was going with a merchant

[098-052]as his clerk and companion. I said, I shall behold the lands and people

[098-053]of the East, and I shall speak with a fuller vision. I breathed then as

[098-054]you do, without labor; I had the light step and the endurance of youth,

[098-055]I could fast, I could sleep on the hard ground. I had wedded poverty,

[098-056]and I loved my bride—for poverty to me was freedom. My heart exulted

[098-057]as if it had been the heart of Moses ben Maimon, strong with the

[098-058]strength of three score years, and knowing the work that was to fill

[098-059]them. It was the first time I had been south; the soul within me felt

[098-060]its former sun; and standing on the quay, where the ground I stood on

[098-061]seemed to send forth light, and the shadows had an azure glory as of

[098-062]spirits become visible, I felt myself in the flood of a glorious life,

[098-063]wherein my own small year-counted existence seemed to melt, so that I

[098-064]knew it not; and a great sob arose within me as at the rush of waters

[098-065]that were too strong a bliss. So I stood there awaiting my companion;

[098-066]and I saw him not till he said: ‘Ezra, I have been to the post and

[098-067]there is your letter.’”

[098-068]“Ezra!” exclaimed Deronda, unable to contain himself.

[098-069]“Ezra,” repeated Mordecai, affirmatively, engrossed in memory. “I was

[098-070]expecting a letter; for I wrote continually to my mother. And that

[098-071]sound of my name was like the touch of a wand that recalled me to the

[098-072]body wherefrom I had been released as it were to mingle with the ocean

[098-073]of human existence, free from the pressure of individual bondage. I

[098-074]opened the letter; and the name came again as a cry that would have

[098-075]disturbed me in the bosom of heaven, and made me yearn to reach where

[098-076]that sorrow was—‘Ezra, my son!’”

[098-077]Mordecai paused again, his imagination arrested by the grasp of that

[098-078]long-passed moment. Deronda’s mind was almost breathlessly suspended on

[098-079]what was coming. A strange possibility had suddenly presented itself.

[098-080]Mordecai’s eyes were cast down in abstracted contemplation, and in a

[098-081]few moments he went on,

[098-082]“She was a mother of whom it might have come—yea, might have come to

[098-083]be said, ‘Her children arise up and call her blessed.’ In her I

[098-084]understood the meaning of that Master who, perceiving the footsteps of

[098-085]his mother, rose up and said, ‘The Majesty of the Eternal cometh near!’

[098-086]And that letter was her cry from the depths of anguish and

[098-087]desolation—the cry of a mother robbed of her little ones. I was her

[098-088]eldest. Death had taken four babes one after the other. Then came,

[098-089]late, my little sister, who was, more than all the rest, the desire of

[098-090]my mother’s eyes; and the letter was a piercing cry to me—‘Ezra, my

[098-091]son, I am robbed of her. He has taken her away and left [[shame|disgrace]]

[098-092]behind. They will never come again.’”—Here Mordecai lifted his eyes

[098-093]suddenly, laid his hand on Deronda’s arm, and said, “Mine was the lot

[098-094]of Israel. For the sin of the father my soul must go into exile. For

[098-095]the sin of the father the work was broken, and the day of fulfilment

[098-096]delayed. She who bore me was desolate, disgraced, destitute. I turned

[098-097]back. On the instant I turned—her spirit and the spirit of her

[098-098]fathers, who had worthy Jewish hearts, moved within me, and drew me.

[098-099]God, in whom dwells the universe, was within me as the strength of

[098-100]obedience. I turned and traveled with hardship—to save the scant money

[098-101]which she would need. I left the sunshine, and traveled into freezing

[098-102]cold. In the last stage I spent a night in exposure to cold and snow.

[098-103]And that was the beginning of this slow death.”

[098-104]Mordecai let his eyes wander again and removed his hand. Deronda

[098-105]resolutely repressed the questions which urged themselves within him.

[098-106]While Mordecai was in this state of emotion, no other confidence must

[098-107]be sought than what came spontaneously: nay, he himself felt a kindred

[098-108]emotion which made him dread his own speech as too momentous.

[098-109]“But I worked. We were destitute—every thing had been seized. And she

[098-110]was ill: the clutch of anguish was too strong for her, and wrought with

[098-111]some lurking disease. At times she could not stand for the beating of

[098-112]her heart, and the images in her brain became as chambers of terror,

[098-113]where she beheld my sister reared in evil. In the dead of night I heard

[098-114]her crying for her child. Then I rose, and we stretched forth our arms

[098-115]together and prayed. We poured forth our souls in desire that [[Mirah-Lapidoth|Mirah]]

[098-116]might be delivered from evil.”

[098-117]“Mirah?” Deronda repeated, wishing to assure, himself that his ears had

[098-118]not been deceived by a forecasting imagination. “Did you say Mirah?”

[098-119]“That was my little sister’s name. After we had prayed for her, my

[098-120]mother would rest awhile. It lasted hardly four years, and in the

[098-121]minute before she died, we were praying the same prayer—I aloud, she

[098-122]silently. Her soul went out upon its wings.”

[098-123]“Have you never since heard of your sister?” said Deronda, as quietly

[098-124]as he could.

[098-125]“Never. Never have I heard whether she was delivered according to our

[098-126]prayer. I know not, I know not. Who shall say where the pathways lie?

[098-127]The poisonous will of the wicked is strong. It poisoned my life—it is

[098-128]slowly stifling this breath. Death delivered my mother, and I felt it a

[098-129]blessedness that I was alone in the winters of suffering. But what are

[098-130]the winters now?—they are far off”—here Mordecai again rested his

[098-131]hand on Deronda’s arm, and looked at him with that joy of the hectic

[098-132]patient which pierces us to sadness—“there is nothing to wail in the

[098-133]withering of my body. The work will be the better done. Once I said the

[098-134]work of this beginning was mine, I am born to do it. Well, I shall do

[098-135]it. I shall live in you. I shall live in you.”

[098-136]His grasp had become convulsive in its force, and Deronda, agitated as

[098-137]he had never been before—the certainty that this was Mirah’s brother

[098-138]suffusing his own strange relation to Mordecai with a new solemnity and

[098-139]tenderness—felt his strong young heart beating faster and his lips

[098-140]paling. He shrank from speech. He feared, in Mordecai’s present state

[098-141]of exaltation (already an alarming strain on his feeble frame), to

[098-142]utter a word of revelation about Mirah. He feared to make an answer

[098-143]below that high pitch of expectation which resembled a flash from a

[098-144]dying fire, making watchers fear to see it die the faster. His dominant

[098-145]impulse was to do as he had once done before: he laid his firm, gentle

[098-146]hand on the hand that grasped him. Mordecai’s, as if it had a soul of

[098-147]its own—for he was not distinctly willing to do what he did—relaxed

[098-148]its grasp, and turned upward under Deronda’s. As the two palms met and

[098-149]pressed each other Mordecai recovered some sense of his surroundings,

[098-150]and said,

[098-151]“Let us go now. I cannot talk any longer.”

[098-152]And in fact they parted at Cohen’s door without having spoken to each

[098-153]other again—merely with another pressure of the hands.

[098-154]Deronda felt a weight on him which was half joy, half anxiety. The joy

[098-155]of finding in Mirah’s brother a nature even more than worthy of that

[098-156]relation to her, had the weight of solemnity and sadness; the reunion

[098-157]of brother and sister was in reality the first stage of a supreme

[098-158]parting—like that farewell kiss which resembles greeting, that last

[098-159]glance of love which becomes the sharpest pang of sorrow. Then there

[098-160]was the weight of anxiety about the revelation of the fact on both

[098-161]sides, and the arrangements it would be desirable to make beforehand. I

[098-162]suppose we should all have felt as Deronda did, without sinking into

[098-163]snobbishness or the notion that the primal duties of life demand a

[098-164]morning and an evening suit, that it was an admissible desire to free

[098-165]Mirah’s first meeting with her brother from all jarring outward

[098-166]conditions. His own sense of deliverance from the dreaded relationship

[098-167]of the other Cohens, notwithstanding their good nature, made him

[098-168]resolve if possible to keep them in the background for Mirah, until her

[098-169]acquaintance with them would be an unmarred rendering of gratitude for

[098-170]any kindness they had shown to her brother. On all accounts he wished

[098-171]to give Mordecai surroundings not only more suited to his frail bodily

[098-172]condition, but less of a hindrance to easy intercourse, even apart from

[098-173]the decisive prospect of Mirah’s taking up her abode with her brother,

[098-174]and tending him through the precious remnant of his life. In the heroic

[098-175]drama, great recognitions are not encumbered with these details; and

[098-176]certainly Deronda had as reverential an interest in Mordecai and Mirah

[098-177]as he could have had in the offspring of Agamemnon; but he was caring

[098-178]for destinies still moving in the dim streets of our earthly life, not

[098-179]yet lifted among the constellations, and his task presented itself to

[098-180]him as difficult and delicate, especially in persuading Mordecai to

[098-181]change his abode and habits. Concerning Mirah’s feeling and resolve he

[098-182]had no doubt: there would be a complete union of sentiment toward the

[098-183]departed mother, and Mirah would understand her brother’s greatness.

[098-184]Yes, greatness: that was the word which Deronda now deliberately chose

[098-185]to signify the impression that Mordecai had made on him. He said to

[098-186]himself, perhaps rather defiantly toward the more negative spirit

[098-187]within him, that this man, however erratic some of his interpretations

[098-188]might be—this consumptive Jewish workman in threadbare clothing,

[098-189]lodged by charity, delivering himself to hearers who took his thoughts

[098-190]without attaching more consequences to them than the Flemings to the

[098-191]ethereal chimes ringing above their market-places—had the chief

[098-192]elements of greatness; a mind consciously, energetically moving with

[098-193]the larger march of human destinies, but not the less full of

[098-194]conscience and tender heart for the footsteps that tread near and need

[098-195]a leaning-place; capable of conceiving and choosing a life’s task with

[098-196]far-off issues, yet capable of the unapplauded heroism which turns off

[098-197]the road of achievement at the call of the nearer duty whose effect

[098-198]lies within the beatings of the hearts that are close to us, as the

[098-199]hunger of the unfledged bird to the breast of its parent.
