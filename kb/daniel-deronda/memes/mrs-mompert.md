## Overview

Mrs. Mompert is the wife of Bishop Mompert, who interviews [[Gwendolen-Harleth]] for a governess position at their household. She embodies the respectable but restrictive expectations placed upon young women seeking employment in the mid-Victorian era, serving as a counterpoint to Gwendolen's aspirations for freedom and distinction.

## Role in the narrative

Mrs. Mompert appears primarily through correspondence and reported speech rather than direct dramatic scenes. Her influence, however, looms large over [[Gwendolen-Harleth]]'s limited options after the financial ruin of her family. The Momperts' household represents the "safe" but stifling path of governess employment — a fate Gwendolen dreads.

> "I should be better off than at Bishop Mompert's." [048-077]

The Mompert household is presented as a respectable establishment where Gwendolen would be under constant supervision — a prospect she finds deeply repugnant after her experience of relative freedom and admiration.

## The governess interview

The prospect of employment with the Momperts precipitates a crisis for Gwendolen. Mrs. Mompert wishes to interview her before confirming the position, and this interview becomes a focal point of Gwendolen's anxiety about her future.

> "When is Mrs. Mompert likely to send for me?" [048-141]

Gwendolen's question to her mother reveals her dread of the impending encounter, while also showing her characteristic desire to control the timing of events that affect her.

## Constant supervision

Mrs. Mompert's household is characterized by the expectation of constant vigilance over the governess's conduct. The position offers security but at the cost of personal freedom — a trade-off that Gwendolen finds intolerable.

> "There was always to be Mrs. Mompert's supervision; always something or other to be done that she had no wish to do." [049-016]

The narrative emphasizes how Mrs. Mompert's oversight would constrain every moment of Gwendolen's day, leaving no room for the self-determination she craves.

## As symbolic figure

In Gwendolen's imagination, Mrs. Mompert takes on almost mythological proportions as the embodiment of her feared future. The name itself becomes a kind of bogeyman representing respectable drudgery.

> "Mrs. Mompert had become a sort of Mumbo-jumbo." [055-094]

This transformation of a real person into a symbolic threat illustrates the psychological dimension of Gwendolen's resistance to the governess role — it is not Mrs. Mompert personally she fears, but what she represents.

## Release and aftermath

Gwendolen's eventual escape from the Momperts' employ — through her marriage to [[Grandcourt]] — is presented with bitter irony. The freedom from Mrs. Mompert's supervision comes at the cost of an even more oppressive bond.

> "Relieved by her own release from the Momperts." [054-128]

The contrast between the two forms of subjection — professional employment versus marital obligation — is one of the novel's central feminist themes.
