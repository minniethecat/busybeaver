[051-001]With this imperfect preparation of a war policy, [[Lush]] was awaiting

[051-002][[Henleigh-Grandcourt|Grandcourt]]’s arrival, doing little more than wondering how the campaign

[051-003]would begin. The first day Grandcourt was much occupied with the

[051-004]stables, and amongst other things he ordered a groom to put a

[051-005]side-saddle on Criterion and let him review the horse’s paces. This

[051-006]marked indication of purpose set Lush on considering over again whether

[051-007]he should incur the ticklish consequences of speaking first, while he

[051-008]was still sure that no compromising step had been taken; and he rose

[051-009]the next morning almost resolved that if Grandcourt seemed in as good a

[051-010]humor as yesterday and entered at all into talk, he would let drop the

[051-011]interesting facts about [[Gwendolen-Harleth|Gwendolen]] and her family, just to see how they

[051-012]would work, and to get some guidance. But Grandcourt did not enter into

[051-013]talk, and in answer to a question even about his own convenience, no

[051-014]fish could have maintained a more unwinking silence. After he had read

[051-015]his letters he gave various orders to be executed or transmitted by

[051-016]Lush, and then thrust his shoulder toward that useful person, who

[051-017]accordingly rose to leave the room. But before he was out of the door

[051-018]Grandcourt turned his head slightly and gave a broken, languid “Oh.”

[051-019]“What is it?” said Lush, who, it must have been observed, did not take

[051-020]his dusty puddings with a respectful air.

[051-021]“Shut the door, will you? I can’t speak into the corridor.”

[051-022]Lush closed the door, came forward, and chose to sit down.

[051-023]After a little pause Grandcourt said, “Is Miss Harleth at [[Offendene]]?”

[051-024]He was quite certain that Lush had made it his business to inquire

[051-025]about her, and he had some pleasure in thinking that Lush did not want

[051-026]_him_ to inquire.

[051-027]“Well, I hardly know,” said Lush, carelessly. “The family’s utterly

[051-028]done up. They and the Gascoignes too have lost all their money. It’s

[051-029]owing to some rascally banking business. The poor mother hasn’t a

[051-030]_sou_, it seems. She and the girls have to huddle themselves into a

[051-031]little cottage like a laborer’s.”

[051-032]“Don’t lie to me, if you please,” said Grandcourt, in his lowest

[051-033]audible tone. “It’s not amusing, and it answers no other purpose.”

[051-034]“What do you mean?” said Lush, more nettled than was common with

[051-035]him—the prospect before him being more than commonly disturbing.

[051-036]“Just tell me the truth, will you?”

[051-037]“It’s no invention of mine. I have heard the story from

[051-038]several—Bazley, Brackenshaw’s man, for one. He is getting a new tenant

[051-039]for Offendene.”

[051-040]“I don’t mean that. Is Miss Harleth there, or is she not?” said

[051-041]Grandcourt, in his former tone.

[051-042]“Upon my soul, I can’t tell,” said Lush, rather sulkily. “She may have

[051-043]left yesterday. I heard she had taken a situation as governess; she may

[051-044]be gone to it for what I know. But if you wanted to see her no doubt

[051-045]the mother would send for her back.” This sneer slipped off his tongue

[051-046]without strict intention.

[051-047]“Send Hutchins to inquire whether she will be there to-morrow.” Lush did

[051-048]not move. Like many persons who have thought over beforehand what they

[051-049]shall say in given cases, he was impelled by an unexpected irritation

[051-050]to say some of those prearranged things before the cases were given.

[051-051]Grandcourt, in fact, was likely to get into a scrape so tremendous that

[051-052]it was impossible to let him take the first step toward it without

[051-053]remonstrance. Lush retained enough caution to use a tone of rational

[051-054]friendliness, still he felt his own value to his patron, and was

[051-055]prepared to be daring.

[051-056]“It would be as well for you to remember, Grandcourt, that you are

[051-057]coming under closer fire now. There can be none of the ordinary

[051-058]flirting done, which may mean everything or nothing. You must make up

[051-059]your mind whether you wish to be accepted; and more than that, how you

[051-060]would like being refused. Either one or the other. You can’t be

[051-061]philandering after her again for six weeks.”

[051-062]Grandcourt said nothing, but pressed the newspaper down on his knees

[051-063]and began to light another cigar. Lush took this as a sign that he was

[051-064]willing to listen, and was the more bent on using the opportunity; he

[051-065]wanted, if possible, to find out which would be the more potent cause

[051-066]of hesitation—probable acceptance or probable refusal.

[051-067]“Everything has a more serious look now than it had before. There is

[051-068]her family to be provided for. You could not let your wife’s mother

[051-069]live in beggary. It will be a confoundedly hampering affair. Marriage

[051-070]will pin you down in a way you haven’t been used to; and in point of

[051-071]money you have not too much elbow-room. And after all, what will you

[051-072]get by it? You are master over your estates, present or future, as far

[051-073]as choosing your heir goes; it’s a pity to go on encumbering them for a

[051-074]mere whim, which you may repent of in a twelvemonth. I should be sorry

[051-075]to see you making a mess of your life in that way. If there were

[051-076]anything solid to be gained by the marriage, that would be a different

[051-077]affair.”

[051-078]Lush’s tone had gradually become more and more unctuous in its

[051-079]friendliness of remonstrance, and he was almost in danger of forgetting

[051-080]that he was merely gambling in argument. When he left off, Grandcourt

[051-081]took his cigar out of his mouth, and looking steadily at the moist end

[051-082]while he adjusted the leaf with his delicate finger-tips, said,

[051-083]“I knew before that you had an objection to my marrying Miss Harleth.”

[051-084]Here he made a little pause before he continued. “But I never

[051-085]considered that a reason against it.”

[051-086]“I never supposed you did,” answered Lush, not unctuously but dryly.

[051-087]“It was not _that_ I urged as a reason. I should have thought it might

[051-088]have been a reason against it, after all your experience, that you

[051-089]would be acting like the hero of a ballad, and making yourself

[051-090]absurd—and all for what? You know you couldn’t make up your mind

[051-091]before. It’s impossible you can care much about her. And as for the

[051-092]tricks she is likely to play, you may judge of that from what you heard

[051-093]at [[Leubronn]]. However, what I wished to point out to you was, that there

[051-094]can be no shilly-shally now.”

[051-095]“Perfectly,” said Grandcourt, looking round at Lush and fixing him with

[051-096]narrow eyes; “I don’t intend that there should be. I dare say it’s

[051-097]disagreeable to you. But if you suppose I care a damn for that you are

[051-098]most stupendously mistaken.”

[051-099]“Oh, well,” said Lush, rising with his hands in his pockets, and

[051-100]feeling some latent venom still within him, “if you have made up your

[051-101]mind!—only there’s another aspect of the affair. I have been speaking

[051-102]on the supposition that it was absolutely certain she would accept you,

[051-103]and that destitution would have no choice. But I am not so sure that

[051-104]the young lady is to be counted on. She is kittle cattle to shoe, I

[051-105]think. And she had her reasons for running away before.” Lush had moved

[051-106]a step or two till he stood nearly in front of Grandcourt, though at

[051-107]some distance from him. He did not feel himself much restrained by

[051-108]consequences, being aware that the only strong hold he had on his

[051-109]present position was his serviceableness; and even after a quarrel the

[051-110]want of him was likely sooner or later to recur. He foresaw that

[051-111]Gwendolen would cause him to be ousted for a time, and his temper at

[051-112]this moment urged him to risk a quarrel.

[051-113]“She had her reasons,” he repeated more significantly.

[051-114]“I had come to that conclusion before,” said Grandcourt, with

[051-115]contemptuous irony.

[051-116]“Yes, but I hardly think you know what her reasons were.”

[051-117]“You do, apparently,” said Grandcourt, not betraying by so much as an

[051-118]eyelash that he cared for the reasons.

[051-119]“Yes, and you had better know too, that you may judge of the influence

[051-120]you have over her if she swallows her reasons and accepts you. For my

[051-121]own part I would take odds against it. She saw Lydia in Cardell Chase

[051-122]and heard the whole story.”

[051-123]Grandcourt made no immediate answer, and only went on smoking. He was

[051-124]so long before he spoke that Lush moved about and looked out of the

[051-125]windows, unwilling to go away without seeing some effect of his daring

[051-126]move. He had expected that Grandcourt would tax him with having

[051-127]contrived the affair, since [[Lydia-Glasher|Mrs. Glasher]] was then living at [[gadsmere|Gadsmere]], a

[051-128]hundred miles off, and he was prepared to admit the fact: what he cared

[051-129]about was that Grandcourt should be staggered by the sense that his

[051-130]intended advances must be made to a girl who had that knowledge in her

[051-131]mind and had been scared by it. At length Grandcourt, seeing Lush turn

[051-132]toward him, looked at him again and said, contemptuously, “What

[051-133]follows?”

[051-134]Here certainly was a “mate” in answer to Lush’s “check”; and though his

[051-135]exasperation with Grandcourt was perhaps stronger than it had ever been

[051-136]before, it would have been idiocy to act as if any further move could

[051-137]be useful. He gave a slight shrug with one shoulder, and was going to

[051-138]walk away, when Grandcourt, turning on his seat toward the table, said,

[051-139]as quietly as if nothing had occurred, “Oblige me by pushing that pen

[051-140]and paper here, will you?”

[051-141]No thunderous, bullying superior could have exercised the imperious

[051-142]spell that Grandcourt did. Why, instead of being obeyed, he had never

[051-143]been told to go to a warmer place, was perhaps a mystery to those who

[051-144]found themselves obeying him. The pen and paper were pushed to him, and

[051-145]as he took them he said, “Just wait for this letter.”

[051-146]He scrawled with ease, and the brief note was quickly addressed. “Let

[051-147]Hutchins go with it at once, will you?” said Grandcourt, pushing the

[051-148]letter away from him.

[051-149]As Lush had expected, it was addressed to Miss Harleth, Offendene. When

[051-150]his irritation had cooled down he was glad there had been no explosive

[051-151]quarrel; but he felt sure that there was a notch made against him, and

[051-152]that somehow or other he was intended to pay. It was also clear to him

[051-153]that the immediate effect of his revelation had been to harden

[051-154]Grandcourt’s previous determination. But as to the particular movements

[051-155]that made this process in his baffling mind, Lush could only toss up

[051-156]his chin in despair of a theory.

[051-157]CHAPTER XXVI.

[051-158]He brings white asses laden with the freight

[051-159]Of Tyrian vessels, purple, gold and balm,

[051-160]To bribe my will: I’ll bid them chase him forth,

[051-161]Nor let him breathe the taint of his surmise

[051-162]On my secure resolve.

[051-163]Ay, ’tis secure:

[051-164]And therefore let him come to spread his freight.

[051-165]For firmness hath its appetite and craves

[051-166]The stronger lure, more strongly to resist;

[051-167]Would know the touch of gold to fling it off;

[051-168]Scent wine to feel its lip the soberer;

[051-169]Behold soft byssus, ivory, and plumes

[051-170]To say, “They’re fair, but I will none of them,”

[051-171]And flout Enticement in the very face.

[051-172][[Rex-Gascoigne|Mr. Gascoigne]] one day came to Offendene with what he felt to be the

[051-173]satisfactory news that Mrs. Mompert had fixed Tuesday in the following

[051-174]week for her interview with Gwendolen at Wanchester. He said nothing of

[051-175]his having incidentally heard that Mr. Grandcourt had returned to

[051-176][[diplow-hall|Diplow]]; knowing no more than she did that Leubronn had been the goal of

[051-177]her admirer’s journeying, and feeling that it would be unkind uselessly

[051-178]to revive the memory of a brilliant prospect under the present

[051-179]reverses. In his secret soul he thought of his niece’s unintelligible

[051-180]caprice with regret, but he vindicated her to himself by considering

[051-181]that Grandcourt had been the first to behave oddly, in suddenly walking

[051-182]away when there had the best opportunity for crowning his marked

[051-183]attentions. The rector’s practical judgment told him that his chief

[051-184]duty to his niece now was to encourage her resolutely to face the

[051-185]change in her lot, since there was no manifest promise of any event

[051-186]that would avert it.
