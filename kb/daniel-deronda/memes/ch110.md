[110-001]And always among the images that drove her back to submission was

[110-002][[Daniel-Deronda|Deronda]]. The idea of herself separated from her husband, gave Deronda a

[110-003]changed, perturbing, painful place in her consciousness: instinctively

[110-004]she felt that the separation would be from him too, and in the

[110-005]prospective vision of herself as a solitary, dubiously-regarded woman,

[110-006]she felt some tingling bashfulness at the remembrance of her behavior

[110-007]towards him. The association of Deronda with a dubious position for

[110-008]herself was intolerable. And what would he say if he knew everything?

[110-009]Probably that she ought to bear what she had brought on herself, unless

[110-010]she were sure that she could make herself a better woman by taking any

[110-011]other course. And what sort of woman was she to be—solitary, sickened

[110-012]of life, looked at with a suspicious kind of pity?—even if she could

[110-013]dream of success in getting that dreary freedom. [[Gwendolen-Harleth|Mrs. Grandcourt]] “run

[110-014]away” would be a more pitiable creature than Gwendolen Harleth

[110-015]condemned to teach the bishop’s daughters, and to be inspected by Mrs.

[110-016]Mompert.

[110-017]One characteristic trait in her conduct is worth mentioning. She would

[110-018]not look a second time at the paper [[Lush]] had given her; and before

[110-019]ringing for her maid she locked it up in a traveling-desk which was at

[110-020]hand, proudly resolved against curiosity about what was allotted to

[110-021]herself in connection with [[gadsmere|Gadsmere]]—feeling herself branded in the

[110-022]minds of her husband and his confidant with the meanness that would

[110-023]accept marriage and wealth on any conditions, however dishonorable and

[110-024]humiliating.

[110-025]Day after day the same pattern of thinking was repeated. There came

[110-026]nothing to change the situation—no new elements in the sketch—only a

[110-027]recurrence which engraved it. The May weeks went on into June, and

[110-028]still Mrs. [[Henleigh-Grandcourt|Grandcourt]] was outwardly in the same place, presenting

[110-029]herself as she was expected to do in the accustomed scenes, with the

[110-030]accustomed grace, beauty, and costume; from church at one end of the

[110-031]week, through all the scale of desirable receptions, to [[opera-as-art|opera]] at the

[110-032]other. Church was not markedly distinguished in her mind from the other

[110-033]forms of self-presentation, for marriage had included no instruction

[110-034]that enabled her to connect liturgy and sermon with any larger order of

[110-035]the world than that of unexplained and perhaps inexplicable social

[110-036]fashions. While a laudable zeal was laboring to carry the light of

[110-037]spiritual law up the alleys where law is chiefly known as the

[110-038]policeman, the brilliant Mrs. Grandcourt, condescending a little to a

[110-039]fashionable rector and conscious of a feminine advantage over a learned

[110-040]dean, was, so far as pastoral care and religious fellowship were

[110-041]concerned, in as complete a solitude as a man in a lighthouse.

[110-042]Can we wonder at the practical submission which hid her constructive

[110-043]rebellion? The combination is common enough, as we know from the number

[110-044]of persons who make us aware of it in their own case by a clamorous

[110-045]unwearied statement of the reasons against their submitting to a

[110-046]situation which, on inquiry, we discover to be the least disagreeable

[110-047]within their reach. Poor Gwendolen had both too much and too little

[110-048]mental power and dignity to make herself exceptional. No wonder that

[110-049]Deronda now marked some hardening in a look and manner which were

[110-050]schooled daily to the suppression of feeling.

[110-051]For example. One morning, riding in Rotten Row with Grandcourt by her

[110-052]side, she saw standing against the railing at the turn, just facing

[110-053]them, a dark-eyed lady with a little girl and a blonde boy, whom she at

[110-054]once recognized as the beings in all the world the most painful for her

[110-055]to behold. She and Grandcourt had just slackened their pace to a walk;

[110-056]he being on the outer side was the nearer to the unwelcome vision, and

[110-057]Gwendolen had not presence of mind to do anything but glance away from

[110-058]the dark eyes that met hers piercingly toward Grandcourt, who wheeled

[110-059]past the group with an unmoved face, giving no sign of recognition.

[110-060]Immediately she felt a rising rage against him mingling with her shame

[110-061]for herself, and the words, “You might at least have raised your hat to

[110-062]her,” flew impetuously to her lips—but did not pass them. If as her

[110-063]husband, in her company, he chose to ignore these creatures whom she

[110-064]herself had excluded from the place she was filling, how could she be

[110-065]the person to reproach him? She was dumb.

[110-066]It was not chance, but her own design, that had brought [[Lydia-Glasher|Mrs. Glasher]]

[110-067]there with her boy. She had come to town under the pretext of making

[110-068]purchases—really wanting educational apparatus for her children, and

[110-069]had had interviews with Lush in which she had not refused to soothe her

[110-070]uneasy mind by representing the probabilities as all on the side of her

[110-071]ultimate triumph. Let her keep quiet, and she might live to see the

[110-072]marriage dissolve itself in one way or other—Lush hinted at several

[110-073]ways—leaving the succession assured to her boy. She had had an

[110-074]interview with Grandcourt, too, who had as usual told her to behave

[110-075]like a reasonable woman, and threatened punishment if she were

[110-076]troublesome; but had, also as usual, vindicated himself from any wish

[110-077]to be stingy, the money he was receiving from [[Sir-Hugo-Mallinger|Sir Hugo]] on account of

[110-078][[diplow-hall|Diplow]] encouraging him to be lavish. Lydia, feeding on the

[110-079]probabilities in her favor, devoured her helpless wrath along with that

[110-080]pleasanter nourishment; but she could not let her discretion go

[110-081]entirely without the reward of making a Medusa-apparition before

[110-082]Gwendolen, vindictiveness and jealousy finding relief in an outlet of

[110-083]venom, though it were as futile as that of a viper already flung on the

[110-084]other side of the hedge. Hence, each day, after finding out from Lush

[110-085]the likely time for Gwendolen to be riding, she had watched at that

[110-086]post, daring Grandcourt so far. Why should she not take little Henleigh

[110-087]into the Park?

[110-088]The Medusa-apparition was made effective beyond Lydia’s conception by

[110-089]the shock it gave Gwendolen actually to see Grandcourt ignoring this

[110-090]woman who had once been the nearest in the world to him, along with the

[110-091]children she had borne him. And all the while the dark shadow thus cast

[110-092]on the lot of a woman destitute of acknowledged social dignity, spread

[110-093]itself over her visions of a future that might be her own, and made

[110-094]part of her dread on her own behalf. She shrank all the more from any

[110-095]lonely action. What possible release could there be for her from this

[110-096]hated vantage ground, which yet she dared not quit, any more than if

[110-097]fire had been raining outside it? What release, but death? Not her own

[110-098]death. Gwendolen was not a woman who could easily think of her own

[110-099]death as a near reality, or front for herself the dark entrance on the

[110-100]untried and invisible. It seemed more possible that Grandcourt should

[110-101]die:—and yet not likely. The power of tyranny in him seemed a power of

[110-102]living in the presence of any wish that he should die. The thought that

[110-103]his death was the only possible deliverance for her was one with the

[110-104]thought that deliverance would never come—the double deliverance from

[110-105]the injury with which other beings might reproach her and from the yoke

[110-106]she had brought on her own neck. No! she foresaw him always living, and

[110-107]her own life dominated by him; the “always” of her young experience not

[110-108]stretching beyond the few immediate years that seemed immeasurably long

[110-109]with her passionate weariness. The thought of his dying would not

[110-110]subsist: it turned as with a dream-change into the terror that she

[110-111]should die with his throttling fingers on her neck avenging that

[110-112]thought. Fantasies moved within her like ghosts, making no break in her

[110-113]more acknowledged consciousness and finding no obstruction in it: dark

[110-114]rays doing their work invisibly in the broad light.

[110-115]Only an evening or two after that encounter in the Park, there was a

[110-116]grand concert at [[Herr-Klesmer|Klesmer]]’s, who was living rather magnificently now in

[110-117]one of the large houses in Grosvenor Place, a patron and prince among

[110-118]musical professors. Gwendolen had looked forward to this occasion as

[110-119]one on which she was sure to meet Deronda, and she had been meditating

[110-120]how to put a question to him which, without containing a word that she

[110-121]would feel a dislike to utter, would yet be explicit enough for him to

[110-122]understand it. The struggle of opposite feelings would not let her

[110-123]abide by her instinct that the very idea of Deronda’s relation to her

[110-124]was a discouragement to any desperate step towards freedom. The next

[110-125]wave of emotion was a longing for some word of his to enforce a

[110-126]resolve. The fact that her opportunities of conversation with him had

[110-127]always to be snatched in the doubtful privacy of large parties, caused

[110-128]her to live through them many times beforehand, imagining how they

[110-129]would take place and what she would say. The irritation was

[110-130]proportionate when no opportunity came; and this evening at Klesmer’s

[110-131]she included Deronda in her anger, because he looked as calm as

[110-132]possible at a distance from her, while she was in danger of betraying

[110-133]her impatience to every one who spoke to her. She found her only safety

[110-134]in a chill haughtiness which made Mr. Vandernoodt remark that Mrs.

[110-135]Grandcourt was becoming a perfect match for her husband. When at last

[110-136]the chances of the evening brought Deronda near her, Sir Hugo and Mrs.

[110-137]Raymond were close by and could hear every word she said. No matter:

[110-138]her husband was not near, and her irritation passed without check into

[110-139]a fit of daring which restored the security of her self-possession.

[110-140]Deronda was there at last, and she would compel him to do what she

[110-141]pleased. Already and without effort rather queenly in her air as she

[110-142]stood in her white lace and green leaves she threw a royal

[110-143]permissiveness into her way of saying, “I wish you would come and see

[110-144]me to-morrow between five and six, Mr. Deronda.”

[110-145]There could be but one answer at that moment: “Certainly,” with a tone

[110-146]of obedience.

[110-147]Afterward it occurred to Deronda that he would write a note to excuse

[110-148]himself. He had always avoided making a call at Grandcourt’s. He could

[110-149]not persuade himself to any step that might hurt her, and whether his

[110-150]excuse were taken for indifference or for the affectation of

[110-151]indifference it would be equally wounding. He kept his promise.

[110-152]Gwendolen had declined to ride out on the plea of not feeling well

[110-153]enough having left her refusal to the last moment when the horses were

[110-154]soon to be at the door—not without alarm lest her husband should say

[110-155]that he too would stay at home. Become almost superstitious about his

[110-156]power of suspicious divination, she had a glancing forethought of what

[110-157]she would do in that case—namely, have herself denied as not well. But

[110-158]Grandcourt accepted her excuse without remark, and rode off.

[110-159]Nevertheless when Gwendolen found herself alone, and had sent down the

[110-160]order that only Mr. Deronda was to be admitted, she began to be alarmed

[110-161]at what she had done, and to feel a growing agitation in the thought

[110-162]that he would soon appear, and she should soon be obliged to speak: not

[110-163]of trivialities, as if she had no serious motive in asking him to come:

[110-164]and yet what she had been for hours determining to say began to seem

[110-165]impossible. For the first time the impulse of appeal to him was being

[110-166]checked by timidity, and now that it was too late she was shaken by the

[110-167]possibility that he might think her invitation unbecoming. If so, she

[110-168]would have sunk in his esteem. But immediately she resisted this

[110-169]intolerable fear as an infection from her husband’s way of thinking.

[110-170]That _he_ would say she was making a fool of herself was rather a

[110-171]reason why such a judgment would be remote from Deronda’s mind. But

[110-172]that she could not rid herself from this sudden invasion of womanly

[110-173]reticence was manifest in a kind of action which had never occurred to

[110-174]her before. In her struggle between agitation and the effort to

[110-175]suppress it, she was walking up and down the length of the two

[110-176]drawing-rooms, where at one end a long mirror reflected her in her

[110-177]black dress, chosen in the early morning with a half-admitted reference

[110-178]to this hour. But above this black dress her head on its white pillar

[110-179]of a neck showed to advantage. Some consciousness of this made her turn

[110-180]hastily and hurry to the boudoir, where again there was a glass, but

[110-181]also, tossed over a chair, a large piece of black lace which she

[110-182]snatched and tied over her crown of hair so as completely to conceal

[110-183]her neck, and leave only her face looking out from the black frame. In

[110-184]this manifest contempt of appearance, she thought it possible to be

[110-185]freer from nervousness, but the black lace did not take away the

[110-186]uneasiness from her eyes and lips.
