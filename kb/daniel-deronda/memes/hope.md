# Hope

Hope in *Daniel Deronda* is the expectation of a desired future — ranging from deluded personal aspiration to world-historical vision, and serving as Eliot's narrative diagnostic for measuring each character's moral imagination.

## Overview

George Eliot uses hope as a moral barometer throughout *Daniel Deronda*: what a character hopes for reveals the quality of their inner life, and whether hope proves realistic or delusory shapes their fate. The novel establishes a sharp distinction between hopes grounded in self-knowledge and moral clarity — which tend to be fulfilled — and hopes built on vanity, self-deception, or mere social ambition, which prove hollow.

Hope operates at multiple scales in the narrative. It appears in trivial social exchanges ("I hope you will not be falling in love with Gwendolen" [006-159]), in the anxious calculations of Victorian mothers ("a conflict of hopes and fears about her daughter's lot" [023-169]), and ultimately in Mordecai's prophetic vision of Jewish national restoration — a hope so expansive it transforms the novel's generic boundaries from domestic fiction toward something approaching epic.

## Role in the Novel

Hope structures the novel's moral architecture. The narrator offers a pointed reflection on the nature of hope through an old saying: "What men's hopes are like: they are made of straw" — suggesting that human hopes are as fragile and combustible as the material they are likened to [016-155]. If such fragility characterizes "the hopes of low persons not in society," the novel asks whether the hopes of its privileged characters are any more substantial.

The novel's treatment of hope is deeply gendered. Female characters — Mrs. Davilow, Gwendolen, Mirah — experience hope primarily as something contingent on male action: marriage, rescue, protection. Mrs. Davilow can "only hope with trembling" for her daughter's future, her hope taking the form of anxious passivity rather than active agency [023-171]. Gwendolen's early hopes for wealth and admiration are revealed as structurally dependent on male patronage.

By contrast, Mordecai's hope for Jewish nationhood represents a form of hope that is active, collective, and historically consequential. It is the only hope in the novel that looks beyond individual fulfillment toward the destiny of a people. His vision transforms Deronda from a man uncertain of his purpose into one with a world-historical mission.

## Expression Through Characters

### Gwendolen Harleth

Gwendolen's hopes evolve across the novel from superficial to profound. Early in the narrative, her hopes are social and material: advantageous marriage, admiration, the exercise of personal power. She expresses a fantasy of traveling "the East like Lady Hester Stanhope" — a hope for exotic self-determination that her subsequent marriage to Grandcourt cruelly forecloses [012-178].

After marriage, Gwendolen's hopes narrow to survival and then to moral redemption. Her growing dependence on Deronda reflects a hope that has been purified by suffering — no longer for admiration or wealth but for guidance, forgiveness, and the possibility of becoming a better person.

### Mordecai Cohen

Mordecai embodies hope at its most visionary. His messianic expectation of Jewish national restoration is not a private wish but a cosmic conviction. He seeks not personal fulfillment but a successor — someone to carry forward the hope he has nurtured through years of obscurity and illness.

His hope transforms Deronda, giving the younger man's life a direction and purpose it had previously lacked. The novel presents Mordecai's hope as the narrative's moral center — the standard against which all other hopes are measured and found wanting.

### Mrs. Davilow

Mrs. Davilow's hope represents the anxious, dependent hope of Victorian womanhood. Her "conflict of hopes and fears about her daughter's lot" captures the structural position of women whose welfare depends entirely on male decisions [023-169]. She can hope, but she cannot act — a limitation the novel registers with sympathy but also with clear-eyed critique.

### Rex Gascoigne

Rex's hope takes the form of romantic idealism. The "hopelessness of his love" for Gwendolen is recognized by everyone except Rex himself — a pattern of hopeful self-deception that the novel treats as a universal aspect of first love [014-134].

## Key Passages

> "What men's hopes are like: they are made of straw" — narrator, quoting an old saying on the fragility of human hopes [016-155]

> "A conflict of hopes and fears about her daughter's lot" — on Mrs. Davilow's anxious maternal hope [023-169]

> "She could only hope with trembling" — narrator, on the passive hope of Victorian womanhood [023-171]

## Historical Context

The Victorian era was marked by competing ideologies of hope. Evangelical Christianity emphasized hope for personal salvation; liberal reformism promoted hope for social progress; and the emerging Zionist movement — to which *Daniel Deronda* made a significant literary contribution — articulated hope for Jewish national restoration. Eliot's novel engages with all three registers while subjecting each to novelistic scrutiny.

The phrase "the East like Lady Hester Stanhope" that Gwendolen invokes refers to an actual historical figure — Lady Hester Lucy Stanhope (1776–1839), a British aristocrat who traveled to the Middle East and lived among the Druze — representing a model of unconventional female agency that Gwendolen admires but cannot emulate [012-178].
