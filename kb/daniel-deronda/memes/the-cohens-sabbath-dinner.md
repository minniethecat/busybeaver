# The Cohens' Sabbath Dinner

Daniel Deronda accepts Ezra Cohen's invitation to Sabbath dinner, gaining intimate exposure to Jewish domestic life and encountering Mordecai, whose spiritual intensity begins to reshape Deronda's understanding of Jewish identity.

## Context

Deronda has been searching for Mirah's family in London's Jewish quarter. His inquiries lead him to Ezra Cohen's pawnshop in a narrow Holborn street, where he first encounters the shop with "Watches and Jewelry exchanged and repaired" in its window (069-041). Deronda engages with the Cohen family — Ezra, his wife, their children Jacob and Adelaide, and the elderly grandmother — while discreetly investigating whether they might be related to Mirah. Ezra Cohen, proud of his growing prosperity, invites Deronda to the family's Sabbath dinner.

## The Event

Deronda arrives at the Cohen household at five o'clock on Friday evening. "The shop was closed and the door was opened by Mrs. Cohen," who has prepared the Sabbath meal (071-085). The grandmother sits by the fire, with "a small black line from the Guardian Office in lieu of the necklace" adorning her (071-095). The children are dressed in their "Sabbath frock" — as little Adelaide explains, "She'll have her Sabbath frock on this evening" (071-039).

The dinner table is set with traditional Sabbath foods. Ezra Cohen presides with patriarchal pride, while Deronda navigates the unfamiliar customs with characteristic courtesy. The meal becomes a window into Anglo-Jewish life: the Cohens' blend of religious observance and commercial ambition, their family warmth, and their complex position in English society.

The evening's most significant moment occurs when Deronda interacts with Mordecai — Ezra Cohen's consumptive lodger, who regards Deronda with intense, prophetic recognition. Mordecai's presence transforms an ordinary family dinner into a charged spiritual encounter.

## Participants

- **Daniel Deronda**: Guest at the Sabbath table, navigating Jewish customs with sympathetic curiosity (071-011)
- **Ezra Cohen**: The pawnbroker patriarch, proud of his prosperity and his family (070-176)
- **Mrs. Cohen**: The warm, welcoming matriarch who presides over the Sabbath preparations (071-095)
- **Jacob Cohen**: The spirited young son who trades his corkscrew knife for Deronda's penknife (071-125)
- **Adelaide Cohen**: The young daughter, eager to show off her Sabbath dress (071-040)
- **Mordecai**: Ezra Cohen's lodger, whose spiritual intensity marks Deronda as his destined disciple (071-174)

## Consequences

The Sabbath dinner establishes Deronda's personal connection to the Cohens, giving him ongoing access to Mordecai. Through Mordecai's impassioned speeches about Jewish nationhood and destiny, Deronda begins to consider Judaism not merely as a religion but as a living national project. The warmth of the Cohen household also provides Deronda with a model of Jewish family life that contrasts with stereotypes he has encountered. The children's affection for him — Jacob's trade, Adelaide's Sabbath frock — humanizes the Jewish world he is entering.

## Thematic Significance

The Sabbath dinner scene is the novel's most detailed depiction of Anglo-Jewish domestic life. Eliot uses it to demystify Jewish practice for her largely Christian readership, presenting the Sabbath not as alien ritual but as sanctified family time. The Cohens embody the novel's interest in "ordinary" Jewish life — commercial, affectionate, religious without fanaticism. Against this backdrop, Mordecai's visionary intensity stands out, representing the spiritual vocation that Deronda will eventually embrace. The scene also develops the theme of sympathy as knowledge: Deronda's willingness to sit at the Cohen table and learn their customs models the ethical engagement the novel advocates.

## Key Passages

> "Well, you know, this evening is the Sabbath, young gentleman," said Ezra Cohen (071-005)

> "She'll have her Sabbath frock on this evening" — Adelaide Cohen, of herself (071-039)

> Mordecai looked at Deronda with an expression that "interested Deronda" more than anything else in the room (071-189)
