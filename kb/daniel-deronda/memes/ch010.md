[010-001]“Do I look as well as Rachel, mamma?” said [[Gwendolen-Harleth|Gwendolen]], one day when she

[010-002]had been showing herself in her Greek dress to Anna, and going through

[010-003]scraps of scenes with much tragic intention.

[010-004]“You have better arms than Rachel,” said [[Mrs-Davilow|Mrs. Davilow]], “your arms would

[010-005]do for anything, Gwen. But your voice is not so tragic as hers; it is

[010-006]not so deep.”

[010-007]“I can make it deeper, if I like,” said Gwendolen, provisionally; then

[010-008]she added, with decision, “I think a higher voice is more tragic: it is

[010-009]more feminine; and the more feminine a woman is, the more tragic it

[010-010]seems when she does desperate actions.”

[010-011]“There may be something in that,” said Mrs. Davilow, languidly. “But I

[010-012]don’t know what good there is in making one’s blood creep. And if there

[010-013]is anything horrible to be done, I should like it to be left to the

[010-014]men.”

[010-015]“Oh, mamma, you are so dreadfully prosaic! As if all the great poetic

[010-016]criminals were not women! I think the men are poor cautious creatures.”

[010-017]“Well, dear, and you—who are afraid to be alone in the night—I don’t

[010-018]think you would be very bold in crime, thank God.”

[010-019]“I am not talking about reality, mamma,” said Gwendolen, impatiently.

[010-020]Then her mamma being called out of the room, she turned quickly to her

[010-021]cousin, as if taking an opportunity, and said, “Anna, do ask my uncle

[010-022]to let us get up some charades at the rectory. Mr. Middleton and Warham

[010-023]could act with us—just for practice. Mamma says it will not do to have

[010-024]Mr. Middleton consulting and rehearsing here. He is a stick, but we

[010-025]could give him suitable parts. Do ask, or else I will.”

[010-026]“Oh, not till [[Rex-Gascoigne|Rex]] comes. He is so clever, and such a dear old thing,

[010-027]and he will act Napoleon looking over the sea. He looks just like

[010-028]Napoleon. Rex can do anything.”

[010-029]“I don’t in the least believe in your Rex, Anna,” said Gwendolen,

[010-030]laughing at her. “He will turn out to be like those wretched blue and

[010-031]yellow water-colors of his which you hang up in your bedroom and

[010-032]worship.”

[010-033]“Very well, you will see,” said Anna. “It is not that I know what is

[010-034]clever, but he has got a scholarship already, and papa says he will get

[010-035]a fellowship, and nobody is better at games. He is cleverer than Mr.

[010-036]Middleton, and everybody but you call Mr. Middleton clever.”

[010-037]“So he may be in a dark-lantern sort of way. But he _is_ a stick. If he

[010-038]had to say, ‘Perdition catch my soul, but I do love her,’ he would say

[010-039]it in just the same tone as, ‘Here endeth the second lesson.’”

[010-040]“Oh, Gwendolen!” said Anna, shocked at these promiscuous allusions.

[010-041]“And it is very unkind of you to speak so of him, for he admires you

[010-042]very much. I heard Warham say one day to mamma, ‘Middleton is regularly

[010-043]spooney upon Gwendolen.’ She was very angry with him; but I know what

[010-044]it means. It is what they say at college for being in love.”

[010-045]“How can I help it?” said Gwendolen, rather contemptuously. “Perdition

[010-046]catch my soul if I love _him_.”

[010-047]“No, of course; papa, I think, would not wish it. And he is to go away

[010-048]soon. But it makes me sorry when you ridicule him.”

[010-049]“What shall you do to me when I ridicule Rex?” said Gwendolen, wickedly.

[010-050]“Now, Gwendolen, dear, you _will not_?” said Anna, her eyes filling

[010-051]with tears. “I could not bear it. But there really is nothing in him to

[010-052]ridicule. Only you may find out things. For no one ever thought of

[010-053]laughing at Mr. Middleton before you. Every one said he was

[010-054]nice-looking, and his manners perfect. I am sure I have always been

[010-055]frightened at him because of his learning and his square-cut coat, and

[010-056]his being a nephew of the bishop’s, and all that. But you will not

[010-057]ridicule Rex—promise me.” Anna ended with a beseeching look which

[010-058]touched Gwendolen.

[010-059]“You are a dear little coz,” she said, just touching the tip of Anna’s

[010-060]chin with her thumb and forefinger. “I don’t ever want to do anything

[010-061]that will vex you. Especially if Rex is to make everything come

[010-062]off—charades and everything.”

[010-063]And when at last Rex was there, the animation he brought into the life

[010-064]of [[Offendene]] and the rectory, and his ready partnership in Gwendolen’s

[010-065]plans, left her no inclination for any ridicule that was not of an open

[010-066]and flattering kind, such as he himself enjoyed. He was a fine

[010-067]open-hearted youth, with a handsome face strongly resembling his

[010-068]father’s and Anna’s, but softer in expression than the one, and larger

[010-069]in scale than the other: a bright, healthy, loving nature, enjoying

[010-070]ordinary innocent things so much that vice had no temptation for him,

[010-071]and what he knew of it lay too entirely in the outer courts and

[010-072]little-visited chambers of his mind for him to think of it with great

[010-073]repulsion. Vicious habits were with him “what some fellows

[010-074]did”—“stupid stuff” which he liked to keep aloof from. He returned

[010-075]Anna’s affection as fully as could be expected of a brother whose

[010-076]pleasures apart from her were more than the sum total of hers; and he

[010-077]had never known a stronger love.

[010-078]The cousins were continually together at the one house or the

[010-079]other—chiefly at Offendene, where there was more freedom, or rather

[010-080]where there was a more complete sway for Gwendolen; and whatever she

[010-081]wished became a ruling purpose for Rex. The charades came off according

[010-082]to her plans; and also some other little scenes not contemplated by her

[010-083]in which her acting was more impromptu. It was at Offendene that the

[010-084]charades and _tableaux_ were rehearsed and presented, Mrs. Davilow

[010-085]seeing no objection even to Mr. Middleton’s being invited to share in

[010-086]them, now that Rex too was there—especially as his services were

[010-087]indispensable: Warham, who was studying for India with a Wanchester

[010-088]“coach,” having no time to spare, and being generally dismal under a

[010-089]cram of everything except the answers needed at the forthcoming

[010-090]examination, which might disclose the welfare of our Indian Empire to

[010-091]be somehow connected with a quotable knowledge of Browne’s Pastorals.

[010-092]Mr. Middleton was persuaded to play various grave parts, Gwendolen

[010-093]having flattered him on his enviable immobility of countenance; and at

[010-094]first a little pained and jealous at her comradeship with Rex, he

[010-095]presently drew encouragement from the thought that this sort of

[010-096]cousinly familiarity excluded any serious passion. Indeed, he

[010-097]occasionally felt that her more formal treatment of himself was such a

[010-098]sign of favor as to warrant his making advances before he left

[010-099][[pennicote-rectory|Pennicote]], though he had intended to keep his feelings in reserve until

[010-100]his position should be more assured. Miss Gwendolen, quite aware that

[010-101]she was adored by this unexceptionable young clergyman with pale

[010-102]whiskers and square-cut collar, felt nothing more on the subject than

[010-103]that she had no objection to being adored: she turned her eyes on him

[010-104]with calm mercilessness and caused him many mildly agitating hopes by

[010-105]seeming always to avoid dramatic contact with him—for all meanings, we

[010-106]know, depend on the key of interpretation.

[010-107]Some persons might have thought beforehand that a young man of Anglican

[010-108]leanings, having a sense of sacredness much exercised on small things

[010-109]as well as great, rarely laughing save from politeness, and in general

[010-110]regarding the mention of spades by their naked names as rather coarse,

[010-111]would not have seen a fitting bride for himself in a girl who was

[010-112]daring in ridicule, and showed none of the special grace required in

[010-113]the clergyman’s wife; or, that a young man informed by theological

[010-114]reading would have reflected that he was not likely to meet the taste

[010-115]of a lively, restless young lady like Miss Harleth. But are we always

[010-116]obliged to explain why the facts are not what some persons thought

[010-117]beforehand? The apology lies on their side, who had that erroneous way

[010-118]of thinking.

[010-119]As for Rex, who would possibly have been sorry for poor Middleton if he

[010-120]had been aware of the excellent curate’s inward conflict, he was too

[010-121]completely absorbed in a first passion to have observation for any

[010-122]person or thing. He did not observe Gwendolen; he only felt what she

[010-123]said or did, and the back of his head seemed to be a good organ of

[010-124]information as to whether she was in the room or out. Before the end of

[010-125]the first fortnight he was so deeply in love that it was impossible for

[010-126]him to think of his life except as bound up with Gwendolen’s. He could

[010-127]see no obstacles, poor boy; his own love seemed a guarantee of hers,

[010-128]since it was one with the unperturbed delight in her image, so that he

[010-129]could no more dream of her giving him pain than an Egyptian could dream

[010-130]of snow. She sang and played to him whenever he liked, was always glad

[010-131]of his companionship in riding, though his borrowed steeds were often

[010-132]comic, was ready to join in any fun of his, and showed a right

[010-133]appreciation of Anna. No mark of sympathy seemed absent. That because

[010-134]Gwendolen was the most perfect creature in the world she was to make a

[010-135]grand match, had not occurred to him. He had no conceit—at least not

[010-136]more than goes to make up the necessary gum and consistence of a

[010-137]substantial personality: it was only that in the young bliss of loving

[010-138]he took Gwendolen’s perfection as part of that good which had seemed

[010-139]one with life to him, being the outcome of a happy, well-embodied

[010-140]nature.

[010-141]One incident which happened in the course of their dramatic attempts

[010-142]impressed Rex as a sign of her unusual sensibility. It showed an aspect

[010-143]of her nature which could not have been preconceived by any one who,

[010-144]like him, had only seen her habitual fearlessness in active exercises

[010-145]and her high spirits in society.

[010-146]After a good deal of rehearsing it was resolved that a select party

[010-147]should be invited to Offendene to witness the performances which went

[010-148]with so much satisfaction to the actors. Anna had caused a pleasant

[010-149]surprise; nothing could be neater than the way in which she played her

[010-150]little parts; one would even have suspected her of hiding much sly

[010-151]observation under her simplicity. And Mr. Middleton answered very well

[010-152]by not trying to be comic. The main source of doubt and retardation had

[010-153]been Gwendolen’s desire to appear in her Greek dress. No word for a

[010-154]charade would occur to her either waking or dreaming that suited her

[010-155]purpose of getting a statuesque pose in this favorite costume. To

[010-156]choose a motive from Racine was of no use, since Rex and the others

[010-157]could not declaim French verse, and improvised speeches would turn the

[010-158]scene into burlesque. Besides, Mr. Gascoigne prohibited the acting of

[010-159]scenes from plays: he usually protested against the notion that an

[010-160]amusement which was fitting for every one else was unfitting for a

[010-161]clergyman; but he would not in this matter overstep the line of decorum

[010-162]as drawn in that part of [[wessex|Wessex]], which did not exclude his sanction of

[010-163]the young people’s acting charades in his sister-in-law’s house—a very

[010-164]different affair from private theatricals in the full sense of the word.

[010-165]Everybody of course was concerned to satisfy this wish of Gwendolen’s,

[010-166]and Rex proposed that they should wind up with a tableau in which the

[010-167]effect of her majesty would not be marred by any one’s speech. This

[010-168]pleased her thoroughly, and the only question was the choice of the

[010-169]tableau.

[010-170]“Something pleasant, children, I beseech you,” said Mrs. Davilow; “I

[010-171]can’t have any Greek wickedness.”

[010-172]“It is no worse than Christian wickedness, mamma,” said Gwendolen,

[010-173]whose mention of Rachelesque heroines had called forth that remark.

[010-174]“And less scandalous,” said Rex. “Besides, one thinks of it as all gone

[010-175]by and done with. What do you say to Briseis being led away? I would be

[010-176]Achilles, and you would be looking round at me—after the print we have

[010-177]at the rectory.”
