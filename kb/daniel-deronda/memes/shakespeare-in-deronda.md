# Shakespeare in Daniel Deronda

Shakespeare's presence in *Daniel Deronda* is subtle but pervasive — the supreme English poet-dramatist provides the model of theatrical self-display that Gwendolen Harleth embodies and the novel critiques, making Shakespeare the unacknowledged legislator of the novel's treatment of performance and identity.

## Overview

Shakespeare (1564–1616) was, by the Victorian period, the central figure of English cultural identity — the poet whose works were quoted, performed, and studied as both entertainment and moral education. His plays provided the vocabulary for English self-understanding, and his characters — Hamlet, Lady Macbeth, Cleopatra — were the archetypes through which English readers understood ambition, guilt, and theatrical self-display.

## Role in the Novel

Shakespeare appears in *Daniel Deronda* primarily through Gwendolen Harleth's theatricality. Gwendolen, who performs her identity rather than inhabiting it, is a Shakespearean character manqué — a Cleopatra without an Antony, a Lady Macbeth without a murder. Eliot's critique of Gwendolen's performative selfhood draws implicitly on Shakespeare's exploration of the relationship between acting and being.

Shakespeare also provides a model for the novel's own dramatic method. Eliot's use of tableau scenes — the roulette-table, the archery club, the confession — owes much to Shakespearean dramaturgy, in which character is revealed through staged moments of pressure and revelation.

## Connection to Characters

### Gwendolen Harleth

Gwendolen's self-presentation is fundamentally theatrical — she acts the part of the confident beauty, the accomplished young lady, the triumphant bride. Shakespeare's tragic heroines — women destroyed by their own ambition — provide the template for Gwendolen's trajectory, and her moral education involves learning to stop performing and start being (PN citations linking Gwendolen to theatricality).

### Herr Klesmer

Klesmer, as the genuine artist, represents the alternative to Shakespearean theatricality — not performance as disguise but performance as revelation. His seriousness challenges Gwendolen's superficial relationship to art, including the theatrical tradition Shakespeare represents (PN citations linking Klesmer to artistic authenticity).

## Thematic Significance

Shakespeare embodies the novel's treatment of the difference between theatrical performance and authentic selfhood. Gwendolen's tragedy is that she has learned to act herself but not to know herself — a Shakespearean predicament that Eliot treats with novelistic rather than dramatic resources.
