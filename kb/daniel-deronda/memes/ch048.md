[048-001]The meeting did not happen until the Monday, when [[Gwendolen-Harleth|Gwendolen]] went to the

[048-002]rectory with her mamma. They had called at Sawyer’s Cottage by the way,

[048-003]and had seen every cranny of the narrow rooms in a midday light,

[048-004]unsoftened by blinds and curtains; for the furnishing to be done by

[048-005]gleanings from the rectory had not yet begun.

[048-006]“How _shall_ you endure it, mamma?” said Gwendolen, as they walked

[048-007]away. She had not opened her lips while they were looking round at the

[048-008]bare walls and floors, and the little garden with the cabbage-stalks,

[048-009]and the yew arbor all dust and cobwebs within. “You and the four girls

[048-010]all in that closet of a room, with the green and yellow paper pressing

[048-011]on your eyes? And without me?”

[048-012]“It will be some comfort that you have not to bear it too, dear.”

[048-013]“If it were not that I must get some money, I would rather be there

[048-014]than go to be a governess.”

[048-015]“Don’t set yourself against it beforehand, Gwendolen. If you go to the

[048-016]palace you will have every luxury about you. And you know how much you

[048-017]have always cared for that. You will not find it so hard as going up

[048-018]and down those steep narrow stairs, and hearing the crockery rattle

[048-019]through the house, and the dear girls talking.”

[048-020]“It is like a bad dream,” said Gwendolen, impetuously. “I cannot

[048-021]believe that my uncle will let you go to such a place. He ought to have

[048-022]taken some other steps.”

[048-023]“Don’t be unreasonable, dear child. What could he have done?”

[048-024]“That was for him to find out. It seems to me a very extraordinary

[048-025]world if people in our position must sink in this way all at once,”

[048-026]said Gwendolen, the other worlds with which she was conversant being

[048-027]constructed with a sense of fitness that arranged her own future

[048-028]agreeably.

[048-029]It was her temper that framed her sentences under this entirely new

[048-030]pressure of evils: she could have spoken more suitably on the

[048-031]vicissitudes in other people’s lives, though it was never her

[048-032][[ambition|aspiration]] to express herself virtuously so much as cleverly—a point

[048-033]to be remembered in extenuation of her words, which were usually worse

[048-034]than she was.

[048-035]And, notwithstanding the keen sense of her own bruises, she was capable

[048-036]of some compunction when her uncle and aunt received her with a more

[048-037]affectionate kindness than they had ever shown before. She could not

[048-038]but be struck by the dignified cheerfulness with which they talked of

[048-039]the necessary economies in their way of living, and in the education of

[048-040]the boys. [[Rex-Gascoigne|Mr. Gascoigne]]’s worth of character, a little obscured by

[048-041]worldly opportunities—as the poetic beauty of women is obscured by the

[048-042]demands of fashionable dressing—showed itself to great advantage under

[048-043]this sudden reduction of fortune. Prompt and methodical, he had set

[048-044]himself not only to put down his carriage, but to reconsider his worn

[048-045]suits of clothes, to leave off meat for breakfast, to do without

[048-046]periodicals, to get Edwy from school and arrange hours of study for all

[048-047]the boys under himself, and to order the whole establishment on the

[048-048]sparest footing possible. For all healthy people economy has its

[048-049]pleasures; and the rector’s spirit had spread through the household.

[048-050]Mrs. Gascoigne and Anna, who always made papa their model, really did

[048-051]not miss anything they cared about for themselves, and in all sincerity

[048-052]felt that the saddest part of the family losses was the change for Mrs.

[048-053]Davilow and her children.

[048-054]Anna for the first time could merge her resentment on behalf of Rex in

[048-055]her sympathy with Gwendolen; and Mrs. Gascoigne was disposed to hope

[048-056]that trouble would have a salutary effect on her niece, without

[048-057]thinking it her duty to add any bitters by way of increasing the

[048-058]salutariness. They had both been busy devising how to get blinds and

[048-059]curtains for the cottage out of the household stores; but with delicate

[048-060]feeling they left these matters in the background, and talked at first

[048-061]of Gwendolen’s journey, and the comfort it was to her mamma to have her

[048-062]at home again.

[048-063]In fact there was nothing for Gwendolen to take as a justification for

[048-064]extending her discontent with events to the persons immediately around

[048-065]her, and she felt shaken into a more alert attention, as if by a call

[048-066]to drill that everybody else was obeying, when her uncle began in a

[048-067]voice of firm kindness to talk to her of the efforts he had been making

[048-068]to get her a situation which would offer her as many advantages as

[048-069]possible. Mr. Gascoigne had not forgotten [[Henleigh-Grandcourt|Grandcourt]], but the

[048-070]possibility of further advances from that quarter was something too

[048-071]vague for a man of his good sense to be determined by it: uncertainties

[048-072]of that kind must not now slacken his action in doing the best he could

[048-073]for his niece under actual conditions.

[048-074]“I felt that there was no time to be lost, Gwendolen; for a position in

[048-075]a good family where you will have some consideration is not to be had

[048-076]at a moment’s notice. And however long we waited we could hardly find

[048-077]one where you would be better off than at Bishop Mompert’s. I am known

[048-078]to both him and Mrs. Mompert, and that of course is an advantage to

[048-079]you. Our correspondence has gone on favorably; but I cannot be

[048-080]surprised that Mrs. Mompert wishes to see you before making an absolute

[048-081]engagement. She thinks of arranging for you to meet her at Wanchester

[048-082]when she is on her way to town. I dare say you will feel the interview

[048-083]rather trying for you, my dear; but you will have a little time to

[048-084]prepare your mind.”

[048-085]“Do you know _why_ she wants to see me, uncle?” said Gwendolen, whose

[048-086]mind had quickly gone over various reasons that an imaginary Mrs.

[048-087]Mompert with three daughters might be supposed to entertain, reasons

[048-088]all of a disagreeable kind to the person presenting herself for

[048-089]inspection.

[048-090]The rector smiled. “Don’t be alarmed, my dear. She would like to have a

[048-091]more precise idea of you than my report can give. And a mother is

[048-092]naturally scrupulous about a companion for her daughters. I have told

[048-093]her you are very young. But she herself exercises a close supervision

[048-094]over her daughters’ education, and that makes her less anxious as to

[048-095]age. She is a woman of taste and also of strict principle, and objects

[048-096]to having a French person in the house. I feel sure that she will think

[048-097]your manners and accomplishments as good as she is likely to find; and

[048-098]over the religious and moral tone of the education she, and indeed the

[048-099]bishop himself, will preside.”

[048-100]Gwendolen dared not answer, but the repression of her decided dislike

[048-101]to the whole prospect sent an unusually deep flush over her face and

[048-102]neck, subsiding as quickly as it came. Anna, full of tender fears, put

[048-103]her little hand into her cousin’s, and Mr. Gascoigne was too kind a man

[048-104]not to conceive something of the trial which this sudden change must be

[048-105]for a girl like Gwendolen. Bent on giving a cheerful view of things, he

[048-106]went on, in an easy tone of remark, not as if answering supposed

[048-107]objections,

[048-108]“I think so highly of the position, that I should have been tempted to

[048-109]try and get it for Anna, if she had been at all likely to meet Mrs.

[048-110]Mompert’s wants. It is really a home, with a continuance of education

[048-111]in the highest sense: ‘governess’ is a misnomer. The bishop’s views are

[048-112]of a more decidedly Low Church color than my own—he is a close friend

[048-113]of Lord Grampian’s; but, though privately strict, he is not by any

[048-114]means narrow in public matters. Indeed, he has created as little

[048-115]dislike in his diocese as any bishop on the bench. He has always

[048-116]remained friendly to me, though before his promotion, when he was an

[048-117]incumbent of this diocese, we had a little controversy about the Bible

[048-118]Society.”

[048-119]The rector’s words were too pregnant with satisfactory meaning to

[048-120]himself for him to imagine the effect they produced in the mind of his

[048-121]niece. “Continuance of education”—“bishop’s views”—“privately

[048-122]strict”—“Bible Society,”—it was as if he had introduced a few snakes

[048-123]at large for the instruction of ladies who regarded them as all alike

[048-124]furnished with poison-bags, and, biting or stinging, according to

[048-125]convenience. To Gwendolen, already shrinking from the prospect open to

[048-126]her, such phrases came like the growing heat of a burning glass—not at

[048-127]all as the links of persuasive reflection which they formed for the

[048-128]good uncle. She began, desperately, to seek an alternative.

[048-129]“There was another situation, I think, mamma spoke of?” she said, with

[048-130]determined self-mastery.

[048-131]“Yes,” said the rector, in rather a deprecatory tone; “but that is in

[048-132]a school. I should not have the same satisfaction in your taking that.

[048-133]It would be much harder work, you are aware, and not so good in any

[048-134]other respect. Besides, you have not an equal chance of getting it.”

[048-135]“Oh dear no,” said Mrs. Gascoigne, “it would be much harder for you, my

[048-136]dear—it would be much less appropriate. You might not have a bedroom to

[048-137]yourself.” And Gwendolen’s memories of school suggested other

[048-138]particulars which forced her to admit to herself that this alternative

[048-139]would be no relief. She turned to her uncle again and said, apparently

[048-140]in acceptance of his ideas,

[048-141]“When is Mrs. Mompert likely to send for me?”

[048-142]“That is rather uncertain, but she has promised not to entertain any

[048-143]other proposal till she has seen you. She has entered with much feeling

[048-144]into your position. It will be within the next fortnight, probably. But

[048-145]I must be off now. I am going to let part of my glebe uncommonly well.”

[048-146]The rector ended very cheerfully, leaving the room with the

[048-147]satisfactory conviction that Gwendolen was going to adapt herself to

[048-148]circumstances like a girl of good sense. Having spoken appropriately,

[048-149]he naturally supposed that the effects would be appropriate; being

[048-150]accustomed, as a household and parish authority, to be asked to “speak

[048-151]to” refractory persons, with the understanding that the measure was

[048-152]morally coercive.

[048-153]“What a stay Henry is to us all!” said Mrs. Gascoigne, when her husband

[048-154]had left the room.

[048-155]“He is indeed,” said [[Mrs-Davilow|Mrs. Davilow]], cordially. “I think cheerfulness is

[048-156]a fortune in itself. I wish I had it.”

[048-157]“And Rex is just like him,” said Mrs. Gascoigne. “I must tell you the

[048-158]comfort we have had in a letter from him. I must read you a little

[048-159]bit,” she added, taking the letter from her pocket, while Anna looked

[048-160]rather frightened—she did not know why, except that it had been a rule

[048-161]with her not to mention Rex before Gwendolen.

[048-162]The proud mother ran her eyes over the letter, seeking for sentences to

[048-163]read aloud. But apparently she had found it sown with what might seem

[048-164]to be closer allusions than she desired to the recent past, for she

[048-165]looked up, folding the letter, and saying,

[048-166]“However, he tells us that our trouble has made a man of him; he sees a

[048-167]reason for any amount of work: he means to get a fellowship, to take

[048-168]pupils, to set one of his brothers going, to be everything that is most

[048-169]remarkable. The letter is full of fun—just like him. He says, ‘Tell

[048-170]mother she has put out an advertisement for a jolly good hard-working

[048-171]son, in time to hinder me from taking ship; and I offer myself for the

[048-172]place.’ The letter came on Friday. I never saw my husband so much moved

[048-173]by anything since Rex was born. It seemed a gain to balance our loss.”

[048-174]This letter, in fact, was what had helped both Mrs. Gascoigne and Anna

[048-175]to show Gwendolen an unmixed kindliness; and she herself felt very

[048-176]amiably about it, smiling at Anna, and pinching her chin, as much as to

[048-177]say, “Nothing is wrong with you now, is it?” She had no gratuitously

[048-178]ill-natured feeling, or egoistic pleasure in making men miserable. She

[048-179]only had an intense objection to their making her miserable.
