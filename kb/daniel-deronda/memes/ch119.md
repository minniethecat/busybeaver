[119-001]It was when Anna Gascoigne, visiting the Meyricks; was led to speak of

[119-002]her cousinship with [[Gwendolen-Harleth|Gwendolen]]. The visit had been arranged that Anna

[119-003]might see [[Mirah-Lapidoth|Mirah]]; the three girls were at home with their mother, and

[119-004]there was naturally a flux of talk among six feminine creatures, free

[119-005]from the presence of a distorting male standard. Anna Gascoigne felt

[119-006]herself much at home with the Meyrick girls, who knew what it was to

[119-007]have a brother, and to be generally regarded as of minor importance in

[119-008]the world; and she had told [[Rex-Gascoigne|Rex]] that she thought the University very

[119-009]nice, because brothers made friends there whose families were not rich

[119-010]and grand, and yet (like the University) were very nice. The Meyricks

[119-011]seemed to her almost alarmingly clever, and she consulted them much on

[119-012]the best mode of teaching Lotta, confiding to them that she herself was

[119-013]the least clever of her family. Mirah had lately come in, and there was

[119-014]a complete bouquet of young faces around the tea-table—Hafiz, seated a

[119-015]little aloft with large eyes on the alert, regarding the whole scene as

[119-016]an apparatus for supplying his allowance of milk.

[119-017]“Think of our surprise, Mirah,” said Kate. “We were speaking of Mr.

[119-018][[Daniel-Deronda|Deronda]] and the Mallingers, and it turns out that Miss Gascoigne knows

[119-019]them.”

[119-020]“I only knew about them,” said Anna, a little flushed with excitement,

[119-021]what she had heard and now saw of the lovely Jewess being an almost

[119-022]startling novelty to her. “I have not even seen them. But some months

[119-023]ago, my cousin married [[Sir-Hugo-Mallinger|Sir Hugo Mallinger]]’s nephew, [[Henleigh-Grandcourt|Mr. Grandcourt]], who

[119-024]lived in Sir Hugo’s place at [[diplow-hall|Diplow]], near us.”

[119-025]“There!” exclaimed Mab, clasping her hands. “Something must come of

[119-026]that. Mrs. Grandcourt, the Vandyke duchess, is your cousin?”

[119-027]“Oh, yes; I was her bridesmaid,” said Anna. “Her mamma and mine are

[119-028]sisters. My aunt was much richer before last year, but then she and

[119-029]mamma lost all their fortune. Papa is a clergyman, you know, so it

[119-030]makes very little difference to us, except that we keep no carriage,

[119-031]and have no dinner parties—and I like it better. But it was very sad

[119-032]for poor Aunt Davilow, for she could not live with us, because she has

[119-033]four daughters besides Gwendolen; but then, when she married Mr.

[119-034]Grandcourt, it did not signify so much, because of his being so rich.”

[119-035]“Oh, this finding out relationships is delightful!” said Mab. “It is

[119-036]like a Chinese puzzle that one has to fit together. I feel sure

[119-037]something wonderful may be made of it, but I can’t tell what.”

[119-038]“Dear me, Mab,” said Amy, “relationships must branch out. The only

[119-039]difference is, that we happen to know some of the people concerned.

[119-040]Such things are going on every day.”

[119-041]“And pray, Amy, why do you insist on the number nine being so

[119-042]wonderful?” said Mab. “I am sure that is happening every day. Never

[119-043]mind, Miss Gascoigne; please go on. And Mr. Deronda?—have you never

[119-044]seen Mr. Deronda? You _must_ bring him in.”

[119-045]“No, I have not seen him,” said Anna; “but he was at Diplow before my

[119-046]cousin was married, and I have heard my aunt speaking of him to papa.

[119-047]She said what you have been saying about him—only not so much: I mean,

[119-048]about Mr. Deronda living with Sir Hugo Mallinger, and being so nice,

[119-049]she thought. We talk a great deal about every one who comes near

[119-050][[pennicote-rectory|Pennicote]], because it is so seldom there is any one new. But I

[119-051]remember, when I asked Gwendolen what she thought of Mr. Deronda, she

[119-052]said, ‘Don’t mention it, Anna: but I think his hair is dark.’ That was

[119-053]her droll way of answering: she was always so lively. It is really

[119-054]rather wonderful that I should come to hear so much about him, all

[119-055]through Mr. [[Hans-Meyrick|Hans]] knowing Rex, and then my having the pleasure of

[119-056]knowing you,” Anna ended, looking at Mrs. Meyrick with a shy grace.

[119-057]“The pleasure is on our side too; but the wonder would have been, if

[119-058]you had come to this house without hearing of Mr. Deronda—wouldn’t it,

[119-059]Mirah?” said Mrs. Meyrick.

[119-060]Mirah smiled acquiescently, but had nothing to say. A confused

[119-061]discontent took possession of her at the mingling of names and images

[119-062]to which she had been listening.

[119-063]“My son calls Mrs. Grandcourt the Vandyke duchess,” continued Mrs.

[119-064]Meyrick, turning again to Anna; “he thinks her so striking and

[119-065]picturesque.”

[119-066]“Yes,” said Anna. “Gwendolen was always so beautiful—people fell

[119-067]dreadfully in love with her. I thought it a pity, because it made them

[119-068]unhappy.”

[119-069]“And how do you like Mr. Grandcourt, the happy lover?” said Mrs.

[119-070]Meyrick, who, in her way, was as much interested as Mab in the hints

[119-071]she had been hearing of vicissitude in the life of a widow with

[119-072]daughters.

[119-073]“Papa approved of Gwendolen’s accepting him, and my aunt says he is

[119-074]very generous,” said Anna, beginning with a virtuous intention of

[119-075]repressing her own sentiments; but then, unable to resist a rare

[119-076]occasion for speaking them freely, she went on—“else I should have

[119-077]thought he was not very nice—rather proud, and not at all lively, like

[119-078]Gwendolen. I should have thought some one younger and more lively would

[119-079]have suited her better. But, perhaps, having a brother who seems to us

[119-080]better than any one makes us think worse of others.”

[119-081]“Wait till you see Mr. Deronda,” said Mab, nodding significantly.

[119-082]“Nobody’s brother will do after him.”

[119-083]“Our brothers _must_ do for people’s husbands,” said Kate, curtly,

[119-084]“because they will not get Mr. Deronda. No woman will do for him to

[119-085]marry.”

[119-086]“No woman ought to want him to marry him,” said Mab, with indignation.

[119-087]“_I_ never should. Fancy finding out that he had a tailor’s bill, and

[119-088]used boot-hooks, like Hans. Who ever thought of his marrying?”

[119-089]“I have,” said Kate. “When I drew a wedding for a frontispiece to

[119-090]‘Hearts and Diamonds,’ I made a sort of likeness to him for the

[119-091]bridegroom, and I went about looking for a grand woman who would do for

[119-092]his countess, but I saw none that would not be poor creatures by the

[119-093]side of him.”

[119-094]“You should have seen this Mrs. Grandcourt then,” said Mrs. Meyrick.

[119-095]“Hans says that she and Mr. Deronda set each other off when they are

[119-096]side by side. She is tall and fair. But you know her, Mirah—you can

[119-097]always say something descriptive. What do _you_ think of Mrs.

[119-098]Grandcourt?”

[119-099]“I think she is the _Princess of Eboli_ in _Don Carlos_,” said Mirah,

[119-100]with a quick intensity. She was pursuing an association in her own mind

[119-101]not intelligible to her hearers—an association with a certain actress

[119-102]as well as the part she represented.

[119-103]“Your comparison is a riddle for me, my dear,” said Mrs. Meyrick,

[119-104]smiling.

[119-105]“You said that Mrs. Grandcourt was tall and fair,” continued Mirah,

[119-106]slightly paler. “That is quite true.”

[119-107]Mrs. Meyrick’s quick eye and ear detected something unusual, but

[119-108]immediately explained it to herself. Fine ladies had often wounded

[119-109]Mirah by caprices of manner and intention.

[119-110]“Mrs. Grandcourt had thought of having lessons of Mirah,” she said

[119-111]turning to Anna. “But many have talked of having lessons, and then have

[119-112]found no time. Fashionable ladies have too much work to do.”

[119-113]And the chat went on without further insistence on the _Princess of

[119-114]Eboli_. That comparison escaped Mirah’s lips under the urgency of a

[119-115]pang unlike anything she had felt before. The conversation from the

[119-116]beginning had revived unpleasant impressions, and Mrs. Meyrick’s

[119-117]suggestion of Gwendolen’s figure by the side of Deronda’s had the

[119-118]stinging effect of a voice outside her, confirming her secret

[119-119]conviction that this tall and fair woman had some hold on his lot. For

[119-120]a long while afterward she felt as if she had had a jarring shock

[119-121]through her frame.

[119-122]In the evening, putting her cheek against her brother’s shoulder as she

[119-123]was sitting by him, while he sat propped up in bed under a new

[119-124]difficulty of breathing, she said,

[119-125]“[[Mordecai|Ezra]], does it ever hurt your love for Mr. Deronda that so much of his

[119-126]life was all hidden away from you—that he is amongst persons and cares

[119-127]about persons who are all so unlike us—I mean unlike you?”

[119-128]“No, assuredly no,” said Mordecai. “Rather it is a precious thought to

[119-129]me that he has a preparation which I lacked, and is an accomplished

[119-130]Egyptian.” Then, recollecting that his words had reference which his

[119-131]sister must not yet understand, he added, “I have the more to give him,

[119-132]since his treasure differs from mine. That is a blessedness in

[119-133]friendship.”

[119-134]Mirah mused a little.

[119-135]“Still,” she said, “it would be a trial to your love for him if that

[119-136]other part of his life were like a crowd in which he had got entangled,

[119-137]so that he was carried away from you—I mean in his thoughts, and not

[119-138]merely carried out of sight as he is now—and not merely for a little

[119-139]while, but continually. How should you bear that! Our religion commands

[119-140]us to bear. But how should you bear it?”

[119-141]“Not well, my sister—not well; but it will never happen,” said

[119-142]Mordecai, looking at her with a tender smile. He thought that her heart

[119-143]needed comfort on his account.

[119-144]Mirah said no more. She mused over the difference between her own state

[119-145]of mind and her brother’s, and felt her comparative pettiness. Why

[119-146]could she not be completely satisfied with what satisfied his larger

[119-147]judgment? She gave herself no fuller reason than a painful sense of

[119-148]unfitness—in what? Airy possibilities to which she could give no

[119-149]outline, but to which one name and one figure gave the wandering

[119-150]persistency of a blot in her vision. Here lay the vaguer source of the

[119-151]hidden sadness rendered noticeable to Hans by some diminution of that

[119-152]sweet ease, that ready joyousness of response in her speech and smile,

[119-153]which had come with the new sense of freedom and safety, and had made

[119-154]her presence like the freshly-opened daisies and clear bird-notes after

[119-155]the rain. She herself regarded her uneasiness as a sort of ingratitude

[119-156]and dullness of sensibility toward the great things that had been given

[119-157]her in her new life; and whenever she threw more energy than usual into

[119-158]her [[music|singing]], it was the energy of indignation against the shallowness

[119-159]of her own content. In that mood she once said, “Shall I tell you what

[119-160]is the difference between you and me, Ezra? You are a spring in the

[119-161]drought, and I am an acorn-cup; the waters of heaven fill me, but the

[119-162]least little shake leaves me empty.”

[119-163]“Why, what has shaken thee?” said Mordecai. He fell into this antique

[119-164]form of speech habitually in talking to his sister and to the Cohen

[119-165]children.

[119-166]“Thoughts,” said Mirah; “thoughts that come like the breeze and shake

[119-167]me—bad people, wrong things, misery—and how they might touch our

[119-168]life.”

[119-169]“We must take our portion, Mirah. It is there. On whose shoulder would

[119-170]we lay it, that we might be free?”

[119-171]The one voluntary sign she made of her inward care was this distant

[119-172]allusion.

[119-177]Before Deronda was summoned to a second interview with his mother, a

[119-178]day had passed in which she had only sent him a message to say that she

[119-179]was not yet well enough to receive him again; but on the third morning

[119-180]he had a note saying, “I leave to-day. Come and see me at once.”

[119-181]He was shown into the same room as before; but it was much darkened

[119-182]with blinds and curtains. The Princess was not there, but she presently

[119-183]entered, dressed in a loose wrap of some soft silk, in color a dusky

[119-184]orange, her head again with black lace floating about it, her arms

[119-185]showing themselves bare from under her wide sleeves. Her face seemed

[119-186]even more impressive in the sombre light, the eyes larger, the lines

[119-187]more vigorous. You might have imagined her a sorceress who would

[119-188]stretch forth her wonderful hand and arm to mix youth-potions for

[119-189]others, but scorned to mix them for herself, having had enough of youth.
