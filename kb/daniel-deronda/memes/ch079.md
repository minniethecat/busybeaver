[079-001]“It is a charity to those who want to have a good model of feminine

[079-002][[music|singing]],” said [[Daniel-Deronda|Deronda]]. “I think everybody who has ears would benefit

[079-003]by a little improvement on the ordinary style. If you heard Miss

[079-004][[Lapidoth]]”—here he looked at [[Gwendolen-Harleth|Gwendolen]]—“perhaps you would revoke your

[079-005]resolution to give up singing.”

[079-006]“I should rather think my resolution would be confirmed,” said

[079-007]Gwendolen. “I don’t feel able to follow your advice of enjoying my own

[079-008]middlingness.”

[079-009]“For my part,” said Deronda, “people who do anything finely always

[079-010]inspirit me to try. I don’t mean that they make me believe I can do it

[079-011]as well. But they make the thing, whatever it may be, seem worthy to be

[079-012]done. I can bear to think my own [[art|music]] not good for much, but the world

[079-013]would be more dismal if I thought music itself not good for much.

[079-014]Excellence encourages one about life generally; it shows the spiritual

[079-015]wealth of the world.”

[079-016]“But then, if we can’t imitate it, it only makes our own life seem the

[079-017]tamer,” said Gwendolen, in a mood to resent encouragement founded on

[079-018]her own insignificance.

[079-019]“That depends on the point of view, I think,” said Deronda. “We should

[079-020]have a poor life of it if we were reduced for all our pleasure to our

[079-021]own performances. A little private imitation of what is good is a sort

[079-022]of private devotion to it, and most of us ought to practice art only in

[079-023]the light of private study—preparation to understand and enjoy what

[079-024]the few can do for us. I think [[Mirah-Lapidoth|Miss Lapidoth]] is one of the few.”

[079-025]“She must be a very happy person, don’t you think?” said Gwendolen,

[079-026]with a touch of sarcasm, and a turn of her neck toward Mrs. Raymond.

[079-027]“I don’t know,” answered the independent lady; “I must hear more of her

[079-028]before I say that.”

[079-029]“It may have been a bitter disappointment to her that her voice failed

[079-030]her for the stage,” said Juliet Fenn, sympathetically.

[079-031]“I suppose she’s past her best, though,” said the deep voice of Lady

[079-032]Pentreath.

[079-033]“On the contrary, she has not reached it,” said Deronda. “She is barely

[079-034]twenty.”

[079-035]“And very pretty,” interposed Lady Mallinger, with an amiable wish to

[079-036]help Deronda. “And she has very good manners. I’m sorry she’s a bigoted

[079-037]Jewess; I should not like it for anything else, but it doesn’t matter

[079-038]in singing.”

[079-039]“Well, since her voice is too weak for her to scream much, I’ll tell

[079-040]Lady Clementina to set her on my nine granddaughters,” said Lady

[079-041]Pentreath; “and I hope she’ll convince eight of them that they have not

[079-042]voice enough to sing anywhere but at church. My notion is, that many of

[079-043]our girls nowadays want lessons not to sing.”

[079-044]“I have had my lessons in that,” said Gwendolen, looking at Deronda.

[079-045]“You see Lady Pentreath is on my side.”

[079-046]While she was speaking, [[Sir-Hugo-Mallinger|Sir Hugo]] entered with some of the other

[079-047]gentlemen, including [[Henleigh-Grandcourt|Grandcourt]], and standing against the group at the

[079-048]low tea-table said,

[079-049]“What imposition is Deronda putting on you, ladies—slipping in among

[079-050]you by himself?”

[079-051]“Wanting to pass off an obscurity on us as better than any celebrity,”

[079-052]said Lady Pentreath—“a pretty singing Jewess who is to astonish these

[079-053]young people. You and I, who heard Catalani in her prime, are not so

[079-054]easily astonished.”

[079-055]Sir Hugo listened with his good-humored smile as he took a cup of tea

[079-056]from his wife, and then said, “Well, you know, a Liberal is bound to

[079-057]think that there have been singers since Catalani’s time.”

[079-058]“Ah, you are younger than I am. I dare say you are one of the men who

[079-059]ran after [[Leonora-Halm-Eberstein|Alcharisi]]. But she married off and left you all in the lurch.”

[079-060]“Yes, yes; it’s rather too bad when these great singers marry

[079-061]themselves into silence before they have a crack in their voices. And

[079-062]the husband is a public robber. I remember Leroux saying, ‘A man might

[079-063]as well take down a fine peal of church bells and carry them off to the

[079-064]steppes,’ said Sir Hugo, setting down his cup and turning away, while

[079-065]Deronda, who had moved from his place to make room for others, and felt

[079-066]that he was not in request, sat down a little apart. Presently he

[079-067]became aware that, in the general dispersion of the group, Gwendolen

[079-068]had extricated herself from the attentions of Mr. Vandernoodt and had

[079-069]walked to the piano, where she stood apparently examining the music

[079-070]which lay on the desk. Will any one be surprised at Deronda’s

[079-071]concluding that she wished him to join her? Perhaps she wanted to make

[079-072]amends for the unpleasant tone of resistance with which she had met his

[079-073]recommendation of Mirah, for he had noticed that her first impulse

[079-074]often was to say what she afterward wished to retract. He went to her

[079-075]side and said,

[079-076]“Are you relenting about the music and looking for something to play or

[079-077]sing?”

[079-078]“I am not looking for anything, but I _am_ relenting,” said Gwendolen,

[079-079]speaking in a submissive tone.

[079-080]“May I know the reason?”

[079-081]“I should like to hear Miss Lapidoth and have lessons from her, since

[079-082]you admire her so much—that is, of course, when we go to town. I mean

[079-083]lessons in rejoicing at her excellence and my own deficiency,” said

[079-084]Gwendolen, turning on him a sweet, open smile.

[079-085]“I shall be really glad for you to see and hear her,” said Deronda,

[079-086]returning the smile in kind.

[079-087]“Is she as perfect in every thing else as in her music?”

[079-088]“I can’t vouch for that exactly. I have not seen enough of her. But I

[079-089]have seen nothing in her that I could wish to be different. She has had

[079-090]an unhappy life. Her troubles began in early childhood, and she has

[079-091]grown up among very painful surroundings. But I think you will say that

[079-092]no advantages could have given her more grace and truer refinement.”

[079-093]“I wonder what sort of trouble hers were?”

[079-094]“I have not any very precise knowledge. But I know that she was on the

[079-095]brink of drowning herself in despair.”

[079-096]“And what hindered her?” said Gwendolen, quickly, looking at Deronda.

[079-097]“Some ray or other came—which made her feel that she ought to

[079-098]live—that it was good to live,” he answered, quietly. “She is full of

[079-099]piety, and seems capable of submitting to anything when it takes the

[079-100]form of duty.”

[079-101]“Those people are not to be pitied,” said Gwendolen, impatiently. “I

[079-102]have no sympathy with women who are always doing right. I don’t believe

[079-103]in their great sufferings.” Her fingers moved quickly among the edges

[079-104]of the music.

[079-105]“It is true,” said Deronda, “that the consciousness of having done

[079-106]wrong is something deeper, more bitter. I suppose we faulty creatures

[079-107]can never feel so much for the irreproachable as for those who are

[079-108]bruised in the struggle with their own faults. It is a very ancient

[079-109]story, that of the lost sheep—but it comes up afresh every day.”

[079-110]“That is a way of speaking—it is not acted upon, it is not real,” said

[079-111]Gwendolen, bitterly. “You admire Miss Lapidoth because you think her

[079-112]blameless, perfect. And you know you would despise a woman who had done

[079-113]something you thought very wrong.”

[079-114]“That would depend entirely upon her own view of what she had done,”

[079-115]said Deronda.

[079-116]“You would be satisfied if she were very wretched, I suppose,” said

[079-117]Gwendolen, impetuously.

[079-118]“No, not satisfied—full of sorrow for her. It was not a mere way of

[079-119]speaking. I did not mean to say that the finer nature is not more

[079-120]adorable; I meant that those who would be comparatively uninteresting

[079-121]beforehand may become worthier of sympathy when they do something that

[079-122]awakens in them a keen [[conscience|remorse]]. Lives are enlarged in different ways. I

[079-123]dare say some would never get their eyes opened if it were not for a

[079-124]violent shock from the consequences of their own actions. And when they

[079-125]are suffering in that way one must care for them more than for the

[079-126]comfortably self-satisfied.” Deronda forgot everything but his vision

[079-127]of what Gwendolen’s experience had probably been, and urged by

[079-128][[sympathy|compassion]] let his eyes and voice express as much interest as they

[079-129]would.

[079-130]Gwendolen had slipped on to the music-stool, and looked up at him with

[079-131]pain in her long eyes, like a wounded animal asking for help.

[079-132]“Are you persuading Mrs. Grandcourt to play to us, Dan?” said Sir Hugo,

[079-133]coming up and putting his hand on Deronda’s shoulder with a gentle,

[079-134]admonitory pinch.

[079-135]“I cannot persuade myself,” said Gwendolen, rising.

[079-136]Others had followed Sir Hugo’s lead, and there was an end of any

[079-137]liability to confidences for that day. But the next was New Year’s Eve;

[079-138]and a grand dance, to which the chief tenants were invited, was to be

[079-139]held in the picture-gallery above the cloister—the sort of

[079-140]entertainment in which numbers and general movement may create privacy.

[079-141]When Gwendolen was dressing, she longed, in remembrance of [[Leubronn]], to

[079-142]put on the old turquoise necklace for her sole ornament; but she dared

[079-143]not offend her husband by appearing in that shabby way on an occasion

[079-144]when he would demand her utmost splendor. Determined to wear the

[079-145]memorial necklace somehow, she wound it thrice round her wrist and made

[079-146]a bracelet of it—having gone to her room to put it on just before the

[079-147]time of entering the ball-room.

[079-148]It was always a beautiful scene, this dance on New Year’s Eve, which

[079-149]had been kept up by the family tradition as nearly in the old fashion

[079-150]as inexorable change would allow. Red carpet was laid down for the

[079-151]occasion: hot-house plants and evergreens were arranged in bowers at

[079-152]the extremities and in every recess of the gallery; and the old

[079-153]portraits stretching back through generations, even to the

[079-154]pre-portraying period, made a piquant line of spectators. Some

[079-155]neighboring gentry, major and minor, were invited; and it was certainly

[079-156]an occasion when a prospective master and mistress of Abbott’s and

[079-157]King’s Topping might see their future glory in an agreeable light, as a

[079-158]picturesque provincial supremacy with a rent-roll personified by the

[079-159]most prosperous-looking tenants. Sir Hugo expected Grandcourt to feel

[079-160]flattered by being asked to the Abbey at a time which included this

[079-161]festival in honor of the family estate; but he also hoped that his own

[079-162]hale appearance might impress his successor with the probable length of

[079-163]time that would elapse before the succession came, and with the wisdom

[079-164]of preferring a good actual sum to a minor property that must be waited

[079-165]for. All present, down to the least important farmer’s daughter, knew

[079-166]that they were to see “young Grandcourt,” Sir Hugo’s nephew, the

[079-167]presumptive heir and future baronet, now visiting the Abbey with his

[079-168]bride after an absence of many years; any coolness between uncle and

[079-169]nephew having, it is understood, given way to a friendly warmth. The

[079-170]bride opening the ball with Sir Hugo was necessarily the cynosure of

[079-171]all eyes; and less than a year before, if some magic mirror could have

[079-172]shown Gwendolen her actual position, she would have imagined herself

[079-173]moving in it with a glow of triumphant pleasure, conscious that she

[079-174]held in her hands a life full of favorable chances which her cleverness

[079-175]and spirit would enable her to make the best of. And now she was

[079-176]wondering that she could get so little joy out of the exaltation to

[079-177]which she had been suddenly lifted, away from the distasteful petty

[079-178]empire of her girlhood with its irksome lack of distinction and

[079-179]superfluity of sisters. She would have been glad to be even

[079-180]unreasonably elated, and to forget everything but the flattery of the

[079-181]moment; but she was like one courting sleep, in whom thoughts insist

[079-182]like willful tormentors.

[079-183]Wondering in this way at her own dullness, and all the while longing

[079-184]for an excitement that would deaden importunate aches, she was passing

[079-185]through files of admiring beholders in the country-dance with which it

[079-186]was traditional to open the ball, and was being generally regarded by

[079-187]her own sex as an enviable woman. It was remarked that she carried

[079-188]herself with a wonderful air, considering that she had been nobody in

[079-189]particular, and without a farthing to her fortune. If she had been a

[079-190]duke’s daughter, or one of the royal princesses, she could not have

[079-191]taken the honors of the evening more as a matter of course. Poor

[079-192]Gwendolen! It would by-and-by become a sort of skill in which she was

[079-193]automatically practiced to bear this last great gambling loss with an

[079-194]air of perfect self-possession.
