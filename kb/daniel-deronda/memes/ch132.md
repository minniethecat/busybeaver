[132-001]It happened to [[Daniel-Deronda|Deronda]] at that moment, as it has often happened to

[132-002]others, that the need for speech made an epoch in resolve. His respect

[132-003]for the questioner would not let him decline to answer, and by the

[132-004]necessity to answer he found out the truth for himself.

[132-005]“Ah, you argue and you look forward—you are Daniel Charisi’s

[132-006]grandson,” said Kalonymos, adding a benediction in Hebrew.

[132-007]With that they parted; and almost as soon as Deronda was in [[london|London]], the

[132-008]aged man was again on shipboard, greeting the friendly stars without

[132-009]any eager curiosity.

[132-010]CHAPTER LXI.

[132-011]“Within the gentle heart Love shelters him,

[132-012]As birds within the green shade of the grove.

[132-013]Before the gentle heart, in Nature’s scheme,

[132-014]Love was not, nor the gentle heart ere Love.”

[132-015]—GUIDO GUINICELLI (_Rossetti’s Translation_).

[132-016]There was another house besides the white house at [[pennicote-rectory|Pennicote]], another

[132-017]breast besides [[Rex-Gascoigne|Rex Gascoigne]]’s, in which the news of [[Henleigh-Grandcourt|Grandcourt]]’s death

[132-018]caused both strong agitation and the effort to repress it.

[132-019]It was [[Hans-Meyrick|Hans Meyrick]]’s habit to send or bring in the _Times_ for his

[132-020]mother’s reading. She was a great reader of news, from the

[132-021]widest-reaching politics to the list of marriages; the latter, she

[132-022]said, giving her the pleasant sense of finishing the fashionable novels

[132-023]without having read them, and seeing the heroes and heroines happy

[132-024]without knowing what poor creatures they were. On a Wednesday, there

[132-025]were reasons why Hans always chose to bring the paper, and to do so

[132-026]about the time that [[Mirah-Lapidoth|Mirah]] had nearly ended giving Mab her weekly

[132-027]lesson, avowing that he came then because he wanted to hear Mirah sing.

[132-028]But on the particular Wednesday now in question, after entering the

[132-029]house as quietly as usual with his latch-key, he appeared in the

[132-030]parlor, shaking the _Times_ aloft with a crackling noise, in

[132-031]remorseless interruption of Mab’s attempt to render _Lascia ch’io

[132-032]pianga_ with a remote imitation of her teacher. Piano and song ceased

[132-033]immediately; Mirah, who had been playing the accompaniment,

[132-034]involuntarily started up and turned round, the crackling sound, after

[132-035]the occasional trick of sounds, having seemed to her something

[132-036]thunderous; and Mab said,

[132-037]“O-o-o, Hans! why do you bring a more horrible noise than my [[music|singing]]?”

[132-038]“What on earth is the wonderful news?” said Mrs. Meyrick, who was the

[132-039]only other person in the room. “Anything about Italy—anything about

[132-040]the Austrians giving up Venice?”

[132-041]“Nothing about Italy, but something from Italy,” said Hans, with a

[132-042]peculiarity in his tone and manner which set his mother interpreting.

[132-043]Imagine how some of us feel and behave when an event, not disagreeable

[132-044]seems to be confirming and carrying out our private constructions. We

[132-045]say, “What do you think?” in a pregnant tone to some innocent person

[132-046]who has not embarked his wisdom in the same boat with ours, and finds

[132-047]our information flat.

[132-048]“Nothing bad?” said Mrs. Meyrick anxiously, thinking immediately of

[132-049]Deronda; and Mirah’s heart had been already clutched by the same

[132-050]thought.

[132-051]“Not bad for anybody we care much about,” said Hans, quickly; “rather

[132-052]uncommonly lucky, I think. I never knew anybody die conveniently

[132-053]before. Considering what a dear gazelle I am, I am constantly wondering

[132-054]to find myself alive.”

[132-055]“Oh me, Hans!” said Mab, impatiently, “if you must talk of yourself,

[132-056]let it be behind your own back. What _is_ it that has happened?”

[132-057]“Duke Alfonso is drowned, and the Duchess is alive, that’s all,” said

[132-058]Hans, putting the paper before Mrs. Meyrick, with his finger against a

[132-059]paragraph. “But more than all is—Deronda was at [[genoa|Genoa]] in the same

[132-060]hotel with them, and he saw her brought in by the fishermen who had got

[132-061]her out of the water time enough to save her from any harm. It seems

[132-062]they saw her jump in after her husband, which was a less judicious

[132-063]action than I should have expected of the Duchess. However Deronda is a

[132-064]lucky fellow in being there to take care of her.”

[132-065]Mirah had sunk on the [[art|music]] stool again, with her eyelids down and her

[132-066]hands tightly clasped; and Mrs. Meyrick, giving up the paper to Mab,

[132-067]said,

[132-068]“Poor thing! she must have been fond of her husband to jump in after

[132-069]him.”

[132-070]“It was an inadvertence—a little absence of mind,” said Hans, creasing

[132-071]his face roguishly, and throwing himself into a chair not far from

[132-072]Mirah. “Who can be fond of a jealous baritone, with freezing glances,

[132-073]always singing asides?—that was the husband’s _rôle_, depend upon it.

[132-074]Nothing can be neater than his getting drowned. The Duchess is at

[132-075][[freedom|liberty]] now to marry a man with a fine head of hair, and glances that

[132-076]will melt instead of freezing her. And I shall be invited to the

[132-077]wedding.”

[132-078]Here Mirah started from her sitting posture, and fixing her eyes on

[132-079]Hans, with an angry gleam in them, she said, in a deeply-shaken voice

[132-080]of indignation,

[132-081]“Mr. Hans, you ought not to speak in that way. Mr. Deronda would not

[132-082]like you to speak so. Why will you say he is lucky—why will you use

[132-083]words of that sort about life and death—when what is life to one is

[132-084]death to another? How do you know it would be lucky if he loved Mrs.

[132-085]Grandcourt? It might be a great evil to him. She would take him away

[132-086]from my brother—I know she would. Mr. Deronda would not call that

[132-087]lucky to pierce my brother’s heart.”

[132-088]All three were struck with the sudden transformation. Mirah’s face,

[132-089]with a look of anger that might have suited Ithuriel, pale, even to the

[132-090]lips that were usually so rich of tint, was not far from poor Hans, who

[132-091]sat transfixed, blushing under it as if he had been a girl, while he

[132-092]said, nervously,

[132-093]“I am a fool and a brute, and I withdraw every word. I’ll go and hang

[132-094]myself like Judas—if it’s allowable to mention him.” Even in Hans’s

[132-095]sorrowful moments, his improvised words had inevitably some drollery.

[132-096]But Mirah’s anger was not appeased: how could it be? She had burst into

[132-097]indignant speech as creatures in intense pain bite and make their teeth

[132-098]meet even through their own flesh, by way of making their agony

[132-099]bearable. She said no more, but, seating herself at the piano, pressed

[132-100]the sheet of music before her, as if she thought of beginning to play

[132-101]again.

[132-102]It was Mab who spoke, while Mrs. Meyrick’s face seemed to reflect some

[132-103]of Hans’ discomfort.

[132-104]“Mirah is quite right to scold you, Hans. You are always taking Mr.

[132-105]Deronda’s name in vain. And it is horrible, joking in that way about

[132-106]his marrying [[Gwendolen-Harleth|Mrs. Grandcourt]]. Men’s minds must be very black, I think,”

[132-107]ended Mab, with much scorn.

[132-108]“Quite true, my dear,” said Hans, in a low tone, rising and turning on

[132-109]his heel to walk toward the back window.

[132-110]“We had better go on, Mab; you have not given your full time to the

[132-111]lesson,” said Mirah, in a higher tone than usual. “Will you sing this

[132-112]again, or shall I sing it to you?”

[132-113]“Oh, please sing it to me,” said Mab, rejoiced to take no more notice

[132-114]of what had happened.

[132-115]And Mirah immediately sang _Lascia ch’io pianga_, giving forth its

[132-116]melodious sobs and cries with new fullness and energy. Hans paused in

[132-117]his walk and leaned against the mantel-piece, keeping his eyes

[132-118]carefully away from his mother’s. When Mirah had sung her last note and

[132-119]touched the last chord, she rose and said, “I must go home now. [[Mordecai|Ezra]]

[132-120]expects me.”

[132-121]She gave her hand silently to Mrs. Meyrick and hung back a little, not

[132-122]daring to look at her, instead of kissing her, as usual. But the little

[132-123]mother drew Mirah’s face down to hers, and said, soothingly, “God bless

[132-124]you, my dear.” Mirah felt that she had committed an offense against

[132-125]Mrs. Meyrick by angrily rebuking Hans, and mixed with the rest of her

[132-126]suffering was the sense that she had shown something like a proud

[132-127]ingratitude, an unbecoming assertion of superiority. And her friend had

[132-128]divined this compunction.

[132-129]Meanwhile Hans had seized his wide-awake, and was ready to open the

[132-130]door.

[132-131]“Now, Hans,” said Mab, with what was really a sister’s tenderness

[132-132]cunningly disguised, “you are not going to walk home with Mirah. I am

[132-133]sure she would rather not. You are so dreadfully disagreeable to-day.”

[132-134]“I shall go to take care of her, if she does not forbid me,” said Hans,

[132-135]opening the door.

[132-136]Mirah said nothing, and when he had opened the outer door for her and

[132-137]closed it behind him, he walked by her side unforbidden. She had not

[132-138]the courage to begin speaking to him again—conscious that she had

[132-139]perhaps been unbecomingly severe in her words to him, yet finding only

[132-140]severer words behind them in her heart. Besides, she was pressed upon

[132-141]by a crowd of thoughts thrusting themselves forward as interpreters of

[132-142]that consciousness which still remained unaltered to herself.

[132-143]Hans, on his side, had a mind equally busy. Mirah’s anger had waked in

[132-144]him a new perception, and with it the unpleasant sense that he was a

[132-145]dolt not to have had it before. Suppose Mirah’s heart were entirely

[132-146]preoccupied with Deronda in another character than that of her own and

[132-147]her brother’s benefactor; the supposition was attended in Hans’s mind

[132-148]with anxieties which, to do him justice, were not altogether selfish.

[132-149]He had a strong persuasion, which only direct evidence to the contrary

[132-150]could have dissipated, and that was that there was a serious attachment

[132-151]between Deronda and Mrs. Grandcourt; he had pieced together many

[132-152]fragments of observation, and gradually gathered knowledge, completed

[132-153]by what his sisters had heard from Anna Gascoigne, which convinced him

[132-154]not only that Mrs. Grandcourt had a passion for Deronda, but also,

[132-155]notwithstanding his friend’s austere self-repression, that Deronda’s

[132-156]susceptibility about her was the sign of concealed love. Some men,

[132-157]having such a conviction, would have avoided allusions that could have

[132-158]roused that susceptibility; but Hans’s talk naturally fluttered toward

[132-159]mischief, and he was given to a form of experiment on live animals

[132-160]which consisted in irritating his friends playfully. His experiments

[132-161]had ended in satisfying him that what he thought likely was true.

[132-162]On the other hand, any susceptibility Deronda had manifested about a

[132-163]lover’s attentions being shown to Mirah, Hans took to be sufficiently

[132-164]accounted for by the alleged reason, namely, her dependent position;

[132-165]for he credited his friend with all possible unselfish anxiety for

[132-166]those whom he could rescue and protect. And Deronda’s insistence that

[132-167]Mirah would never marry one who was not a Jew necessarily seemed to

[132-168]exclude himself, since Hans shared the ordinary opinion, which he knew

[132-169]nothing to disturb, that Deronda was the son of [[Sir-Hugo-Mallinger|Sir Hugo Mallinger]].

[132-170]Thus he felt himself in clearness about the state of Deronda’s

[132-171]affections; but now, the events which really struck him as concurring

[132-172]toward the desirable union with Mrs. Grandcourt, had called forth a

[132-173]flash of revelation from Mirah—a betrayal of her passionate feeling on

[132-174]this subject which had made him melancholy on her account as well as

[132-175]his own—yet on the whole less melancholy than if he had imagined

[132-176]Deronda’s hopes fixed on her. It is not sublime, but it is common, for

[132-177]a man to see the beloved object unhappy because his rival loves

[132-178]another, with more fortitude and a milder jealousy than if he saw her

[132-179]entirely happy in his rival. At least it was so with the mercurial

[132-180]Hans, who fluctuated between the contradictory states of feeling,

[132-181]wounded because Mirah was wounded, and of being almost obliged to

[132-182]Deronda for loving somebody else. It was impossible for him to give

[132-183]Mirah any direct sign of the way in which he had understood her anger,

[132-184]yet he longed that his speechless companionship should be eloquent in a

[132-185]tender, penitent sympathy which is an admissible form of wooing a

[132-186]bruised heart.

[132-187]Thus the two went side by side in a companionship that yet seemed an

[132-188]agitated communication, like that of two chords whose quick vibrations

[132-189]lie outside our hearing. But when they reached the door of Mirah’s

[132-190]home, and Hans said “Good-bye,” putting out his hand with an appealing

[132-191]look of penitence, she met the look with melancholy gentleness, and

[132-192]said, “Will you not come in and see my brother?”
