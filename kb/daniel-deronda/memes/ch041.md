[041-001]Here was the theme on which her discontent rung its sad changes during

[041-002]her slow [[ambition|drive]] in the uneasy barouche, with one great trunk squeezing

[041-003]the meek driver, and the other fastened with a rope on the seat in

[041-004]front of her. Her ruling vision all the way from [[Leubronn]] had been that

[041-005]the family would go abroad again; for of course there must be some

[041-006]little income left—her mamma did not mean that they would have

[041-007]literally nothing. To go to a dull place abroad and live poorly, was

[041-008]the dismal future that threatened her: she had seen plenty of poor

[041-009]English people abroad and imagined herself plunged in the despised

[041-010]dullness of their ill-plenished lives, with Alice, Bertha, Fanny and

[041-011]Isabel all growing up in tediousness around her, while she advanced

[041-012]toward thirty and her mamma got more and more melancholy. But she did

[041-013]not mean to submit, and let misfortune do what it would with her: she

[041-014]had not yet quite believed in the misfortune; but weariness and disgust

[041-015]with this wretched arrival had begun to affect her like an

[041-016]uncomfortable waking, worse than the uneasy dreams which had gone

[041-017]before. The self-delight with which she had kissed her image in the

[041-018]glass had faded before the sense of futility in being anything

[041-019]whatever—charming, clever, resolute—what was the good of it all?

[041-020]Events might turn out anyhow, and men were hateful. Yes, men were

[041-021]hateful. But in these last hours, a certain change had come over their

[041-022]meaning. It is one thing to hate stolen goods, and another thing to

[041-023]hate them the more because their being stolen hinders us from making

[041-024]use of them. [[Gwendolen-Harleth|Gwendolen]] had begun to be angry with [[Henleigh-Grandcourt|Grandcourt]] for being

[041-025]what had hindered her from marrying him, angry with him as the cause of

[041-026]her present dreary lot.

[041-027]But the slow drive was nearly at an end, and the lumbering vehicle

[041-028]coming up the avenue was within sight of the windows. A figure

[041-029]appearing under the portico brought a rush of new and less selfish

[041-030]feeling in Gwendolen, and when springing from the carriage she saw the

[041-031]dear beautiful face with fresh lines of sadness in it, she threw her

[041-032]arms round her mother’s neck, and for the moment felt all sorrows only

[041-033]in relation to her mother’s feeling about them.

[041-034]Behind, of course, were the sad faces of the four superfluous girls,

[041-035]each, poor thing—like those other many thousand sisters of us

[041-036]all—having her peculiar world which was of no importance to any one

[041-037]else, but all of them feeling Gwendolen’s presence to be somehow a

[041-038]relenting of misfortune: where Gwendolen was, something interesting

[041-039]would happen; even her hurried submission to their kisses, and “Now go

[041-040]away, girls,” carried the sort of comfort which all weakness finds in

[041-041]decision and authoritativeness. Good Miss Merry, whose air of meek

[041-042]depression, hitherto held unaccountable in a governess affectionately

[041-043]attached to the family, was now at the general level of circumstances,

[041-044]did not expect any greeting, but busied herself with the trunks and the

[041-045]coachman’s pay; while [[Mrs-Davilow|Mrs. Davilow]] and Gwendolen hastened up-stairs and

[041-046]shut themselves in the black and yellow bedroom.

[041-047]“Never mind, mamma dear,” said Gwendolen, tenderly pressing her

[041-048]handkerchief against the tears that were rolling down Mrs. Davilow’s

[041-049]cheeks. “Never mind. I don’t mind. I will do something. I will be

[041-050]something. Things will come right. It seemed worse because I was away.

[041-051]Come now! you must be glad because I am here.”

[041-052]Gwendolen felt every word of that speech. A rush of compassionate

[041-053]tenderness stirred all her capability of generous resolution; and the

[041-054]self-confident projects which had vaguely glanced before her during her

[041-055]journey sprang instantaneously into new definiteness. Suddenly she

[041-056]seemed to perceive how she could be “something.” It was one of her best

[041-057]moments, and the fond mother, forgetting everything below that tide

[041-058]mark, looked at her with a sort of adoration. She said,

[041-059]“Bless you, my good, good darling! I can be happy, if you can!”

[041-060]But later in the day there was an ebb; the old slippery rocks, the old

[041-061]weedy places reappeared. Naturally, there was a shrinking of courage as

[041-062]misfortune ceased to be a mere announcement, and began to disclose

[041-063]itself as a grievous tyrannical inmate. At first—that ugly drive at an

[041-064]end—it was still [[Offendene]] that Gwendolen had come home to, and all

[041-065]surroundings of immediate consequence to her were still there to secure

[041-066]her personal ease; the roomy stillness of the large solid house while

[041-067]she rested; all the luxuries of her toilet cared for without trouble to

[041-068]her; and a little tray with her favorite food brought to her in

[041-069]private. For she had said, “Keep them all away from us to-day, mamma.

[041-070]Let you and me be alone together.”

[041-071]When Gwendolen came down into the drawing-room, fresh as a newly-dipped

[041-072]swan, and sat leaning against the cushions of the settee beside her

[041-073]mamma, their misfortune had not yet turned its face and breath upon

[041-074]her. She felt prepared to hear everything, and began in a tone of

[041-075]deliberate intention,

[041-076]“What have you thought of doing, exactly, mamma?”

[041-077]“Oh, my dear, the next thing to be done is to move away from this

[041-078]house. Mr. Haynes most fortunately is as glad to have it now as he

[041-079]would have been when we took it. Lord Brackenshaw’s agent is to arrange

[041-080]everything with him to the best advantage for us: Bazley, you know; not

[041-081]at all an ill-natured man.”

[041-082]“I cannot help thinking that Lord Brackenshaw would let you stay here

[041-083]rent-free, mamma,” said Gwendolen, whose talents had not been applied

[041-084]to business so much as to discernment of the admiration excited by her

[041-085]charms.

[041-086]“My dear child, Lord Brackenshaw is in Scotland, and knows nothing

[041-087]about us. Neither your uncle nor I would choose to apply to him.

[041-088]Besides, what could we do in this house without servants, and without

[041-089]money to warm it? The sooner we are out the better. We have nothing to

[041-090]carry but our clothes, you know?”

[041-091]“I suppose you mean to go abroad, then?” said Gwendolen. After all,

[041-092]this is what she had familiarized her mind with.

[041-093]“Oh, no, dear, no. How could we travel? You never did learn anything

[041-094]about income and expenses,” said Mrs. Davilow, trying to smile, and

[041-095]putting her hand on Gwendolen’s as she added, mournfully, “that makes

[041-096]it so much harder for you, my pet.”

[041-097]“But where are we to go?” said Gwendolen, with a trace of sharpness in

[041-098]her tone. She felt a new current of fear passing through her.

[041-099]“It is all decided. A little furniture is to be got in from the

[041-100]rectory—all that can be spared.” Mrs. Davilow hesitated. She dreaded

[041-101]the reality for herself less than the shock she must give to Gwendolen,

[041-102]who looked at her with tense expectancy, but was silent.

[041-103]“It is Sawyer’s Cottage we are to go to.”

[041-104]At first, Gwendolen remained silent, paling with anger—justifiable

[041-105]anger, in her opinion. Then she said with haughtiness,

[041-106]“That is impossible. Something else than that ought to have been

[041-107]thought of. My uncle ought not to allow that. I will not submit to it.”

[041-108]“My sweet child, what else could have been thought of? Your uncle, I am

[041-109]sure, is as kind as he can be: but he is suffering himself; he has his

[041-110]family to bring up. And do you quite understand? You must remember—we

[041-111]have nothing. We shall have absolutely nothing except what he and my

[041-112]sister give us. They have been as wise and active as possible, and we

[041-113]must try to earn something. I and the girls are going to work a

[041-114]table-cloth border for the Ladies’ Charity at Winchester, and a

[041-115]communion cloth that the parishioners are to present to [[pennicote-rectory|Pennicote]]

[041-116]Church.”

[041-117]Mrs. Davilow went into these details timidly: but how else was she to

[041-118]bring the fact of their position home to this poor child who, alas!

[041-119]must submit at present, whatever might be in the background for her?

[041-120]and she herself had a superstition that there must be something better

[041-121]in the background.

[041-122]“But surely somewhere else than Sawyer’s Cottage might have been

[041-123]found,” Gwendolen persisted—taken hold of (as if in a nightmare) by

[041-124]the image of this house where an exciseman had lived.

[041-125]“No, indeed, dear. You know houses are scarce, and we may be thankful

[041-126]to get anything so private. It is not so very bad. There are two little

[041-127]parlors and four bedrooms. You shall sit alone whenever you like.”

[041-128]The ebb of sympathetic care for her mamma had gone so low just now,

[041-129]that Gwendolen took no notice of these deprecatory words.

[041-130]“I cannot conceive that all your property is gone at once, mamma. How

[041-131]can you be sure in so short a time? It is not a week since you wrote to

[041-132]me.”

[041-133]“The first news came much earlier, dear. But I would not spoil your

[041-134]pleasure till it was quite necessary.”

[041-135]“Oh, how vexatious!” said Gwendolen, coloring with fresh anger. “If I

[041-136]had known, I could have brought home the money I had won: and for want

[041-137]of knowing, I stayed and lost it. I had nearly two hundred pounds, and

[041-138]it would have done for us to live on a little while, till I could carry

[041-139]out some plan.” She paused an instant and then added more impetuously,

[041-140]“Everything has gone against me. People have come near me only to

[041-141]blight me.”

[041-142]Among the “people” she was including [[Daniel-Deronda|Deronda]]. If he had not interfered

[041-143]in her life she would have gone to the [[gambling|gaming]]-table again with a few

[041-144]napoleons, and might have won back her losses.

[041-145]“We must resign ourselves to the will of Providence, my child,” said

[041-146]poor Mrs. Davilow, startled by this revelation of the gambling, but not

[041-147]daring to say more. She felt sure that “people” meant Grandcourt, about

[041-148]whom her lips were sealed. And Gwendolen answered immediately,

[041-149]“But I don’t resign myself. I shall do what I can against it. What is

[041-150]the good of calling the people’s wickedness Providence? You said in

[041-151]your letter it was Mr. Lassman’s fault we had lost our money. Has he

[041-152]run away with it all?”

[041-153]“No, dear, you don’t understand. There were great speculations: he

[041-154]meant to gain. It was all about mines and things of that sort. He

[041-155]risked too much.”

[041-156]“I don’t call that Providence: it was his improvidence with our money,

[041-157]and he ought to be punished. Can’t we go to law and recover our

[041-158]fortune? My uncle ought to take measures, and not sit down by such

[041-159]wrongs. We ought to go to law.”

[041-160]“My dear child, law can never bring back money lost in that way. Your

[041-161]uncle says it is milk spilled upon the ground. Besides, one must have a

[041-162]fortune to get any law: there is no law for people who are ruined. And

[041-163]our money has only gone along with other people’s. We are not the

[041-164]only sufferers: others have to resign themselves besides us.”

[041-165]“But I don’t resign myself to live at Sawyer’s Cottage and see you

[041-166]working for sixpences and shillings because of that. I shall not do it.

[041-167]I shall do what is more befitting our rank and education.”

[041-168]“I am sure your uncle and all of us will approve of that, dear, and

[041-169]admire you the more for it,” said Mrs. Davilow, glad of an unexpected

[041-170]opening for speaking on a difficult subject. “I didn’t mean that you

[041-171]should resign yourself to worse when anything better offered itself.

[041-172]Both your uncle and aunt have felt that your abilities and education

[041-173]were a fortune for you, and they have already heard of something within

[041-174]your reach.”

[041-175]“What is that, mamma?” some of Gwendolen’s anger gave way to interest,

[041-176]and she was not without romantic conjectures.

[041-177]“There are two situations that offer themselves. One is in a bishop’s

[041-178]family, where there are three daughters, and the other is in quite a

[041-179]high class of school; and in both, your French, and [[art|music]], and

[041-180]dancing—and then your manners and habits as a lady, are exactly what

[041-181]is wanted. Each is a hundred a year—and—just for the present,”—Mrs.

[041-182]Davilow had become frightened and hesitating,—“to save you from the

[041-183]petty, common way of living that we must go to—you would perhaps

[041-184]accept one of the two.”
