# Mab Meyrick

Mab Meyrick is the younger of the two Meyrick daughters, a quick-witted and warm-hearted girl whose impulsive exclamations punctuate the domestic scenes of the Chelsea household. With Hans's light hair and complexion, Mab is a spirited presence — quick to scold her brother, quick to embrace Mirah, and incapable of hiding what she feels.

## Role in the Novel

Mab's primary role is as a member of the Meyrick household chorus — the family that shelters Mirah Lapidoth after Deronda rescues her. Within that domestic world, Mab functions as emotional barometer and comic relief: her "Oh—oh—oh!" groans and impulsive outbursts externalize the feelings that more restrained characters suppress. "Whenever you are in that mood, Mab, down goes your work" (036-041) — Amy's gentle reproach captures Mab's mercurial temperament. She is also the first of the Meyrick daughters to declare her affection for Mirah: "I am sure you are good," burst out Mab (036-111), her impulsiveness here serving as moral intuition.

## Character Traits

### Personality

Mab is impulsive, affectionate, and constitutionally incapable of dissembling. She groans, bursts out, clasps her hands, and flirts away her thimble — her body is as expressive as her speech. "Poor Mab! don't be hard on her," said Mrs. Meyrick (036-047), suggesting a temperament that needs occasional defending. Her "Oh—oh—oh!" refrain (036-044, 036-052, 065-072) is a leitmotif of exasperated feeling — at her own restlessness, at the world's injustices, at her inability to help those she loves.

### Appearance

Mab has "Hans's light hair and complexion" (036-011), marking her as her brother's sister in looks as well as temperament. In black dress with dark hair (037-089), she presents a striking visual contrast to Mirah's darker, more exotic beauty. Her appearance at the Meyrick household dinner table — embroidering satin with her sisters (035-137) — places her firmly in the domestic sphere.

## Relationships

- **Hans Meyrick**: Mab's brother, toward whom she is protective but also sharp-tongued. "Oh, for shame, Hans! — to speak in that way" (105-094) — her scolding of Hans's romantic presumption captures their sibling dynamic: she loves him but sees his foolishness clearly.
- **Mirah Lapidoth**: Mab becomes Mirah's fierce protector and friend. "See what a nice little mother you are!" cried Mab (036-129), attempting to comfort Mirah by praising her care for her younger siblings. Mab's immediate, instinctive warmth toward Mirah is the novel's argument that goodness can be spontaneous rather than deliberated (036-129).
- **Mrs. Meyrick**: Mab's mother, toward whom she is sometimes grumbling and always affectionate. "Mamma, may I get you some tea?" cried Mab, her impulsiveness equally ready for service as for complaint (037-099).
- **Amy Meyrick**: Mab's older sister and embroidery companion. Their banter — Mab flirting away her thimble, Amy gently reproving — establishes the sisters as a comic double act within the Meyrick household (036-035, 036-041).

## Character Arc

Mab does not undergo dramatic transformation. Her arc is one of deepening emotional investment: from initial curiosity about Mirah to fierce protectiveness, from childish restlessness ("I wish I were not such a high, healthy, obtrusive thing!" 065-072) to the beginning of adult empathy. She is the character who feels most intensely what the reader is meant to feel — outrage at Mirah's suffering, exasperation at Hans's folly, warmth toward the family that shelters the vulnerable.

## Thematic Significance

Mab embodies the novel's argument for spontaneous goodness. Unlike Deronda, whose moral actions emerge from long reflection, Mab acts on instinct — and her instincts are almost always right. Her impulsiveness is both her limitation and her virtue: she cannot think deeply, but she can feel truly. In a novel preoccupied with moral deliberation, Mab represents the possibility that goodness can be temperament rather than decision. Her comic energy — the groans, the clasped hands, the flying thimbles — also serves Eliot's larger design: Mab leavens the novel's intensity without trivialising it, proving that domestic warmth is a genuine value, not merely a backdrop to grander themes.

## Key Scenes

- Embroidering with Amy while their mother reads aloud — the comic overture to Mirah's arrival (036-011, 036-029, 036-035)
- "I am sure you are good" — Mab's impulsive declaration of faith in Mirah (036-111)
- "See what a nice little mother you are!" — comforting Mirah through praise (036-129)
- "Oh—oh—oh! I wish I were not such a high, healthy, obtrusive thing!" — Mab at her most self-aware and endearing (065-072)
- Scolding Hans for his presumptuous speech about Mirah — "Oh, for shame, Hans!" (105-094)
