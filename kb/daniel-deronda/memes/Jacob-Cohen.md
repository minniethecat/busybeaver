# Jacob Cohen

Young Jacob Cohen is the six-year-old son of [[ezra-cohen|Ezra Cohen]], a child of irrepressible vitality whose presence in the Cohen household provides moments of warmth and comic relief amid the novel's weightier themes of identity and prophetic vision.

## Role in the Novel

Jacob appears as the "marvelous Jacob" (071-015), a child whose energy and charm animate the domestic scenes of the Cohen household. His role in the novel is both specific and symbolic: he is a particular child — proud of his full name "Jacob Alexander Cohen" (070-111), persistent in his desire for a knife, restless during Hebrew lessons — and also a figure of generational continuity, the next link in the chain of Jewish transmission that Mordecai prophesies and Deronda ultimately embraces.

Unlike the novel's adult characters, Jacob is not burdened by questions of identity. He simply is what he is: a Jewish child growing up in a loving, bustling, commercially active household. His presence serves as a counterweight to the novel's more melodramatic moments, grounding the narrative in the ordinary textures of family life.

## A Child in the Cohen Household

Jacob first appears in the novel when [[Daniel-Deronda|Deronda]] visits Ezra Cohen's shop. His full name is announced with a child's pride: "Jacob Alexander Cohen" (070-111). At six years old, he already possesses an entrepreneurial spirit, wheedling a knife from Deronda through a combination of persistence and charm. His grandfather — the elder Jacob Cohen — is a dealer in knives (070-115), and the child's fascination with blades connects him to the family trade:

> "Have _you_ got a knife?" says Jacob, coming closer. His small voice carried the urgency of a serious collector (070-123).

The knife becomes a running motif through Jacob's early scenes. Deronda exchanges a knife for a corkscrew in a moment of mutual satisfaction (070-128), and Jacob's pride in his acquisition is absolute: "Mine's the best" (070-135), he declares, returning Deronda's own knife with the confidence of a connoisseur. His "pressing solicitude about the knife" (071-115) continues into subsequent chapters, and he interrogates Deronda with deliberative gravity: "What else have you got in your pockets?" (071-127).

### A Bridge Between Worlds

Jacob serves as an emotional bridge between the novel's disparate worlds. His presence in the Cohen shop — a space that initially disappoints Deronda with its unpoetic ordinariness — humanizes the commercial setting, revealing the warmth and vitality of Jewish domestic life beneath the surface of trade. The child who announces his full name with pride and bargains for a knife with serious intent is both a recognizably universal child and a specific product of his environment.

## Character Traits

### Personality

Jacob is defined by irrepressible energy and a precocious commercial instinct. At six years old, he already negotiates with the confidence of a seasoned trader, extracting a knife from Deronda through a combination of charm and persistence (070-123). His pride in his full name — "Jacob Alexander Cohen" (070-111) — suggests an early sense of self-possession nurtured by a family that values him.

The narrator describes him as "the marvelous Jacob" (071-015), a phrase that captures both the family's affectionate regard and the child's genuine magnetism. His physical energy is inexhaustible: he leaps from his chair with sweet-cake in hand (072-036), slips away into play at unpredictable moments (086-107), and impresses the stranger with his grandfather's trade connection to knives (070-115). Yet beneath the restless surface, Jacob is capable of genuine tenderness — placing a corner of cake into Mordecai's mouth "as an invitation to bite" (072-041), pressing his face against the old man's breast in a moment of unguarded affection (086-181).

His curiosity is pragmatic and material. He wants to know "What makes a sharp knife, father?" (072-032), a question that links his childish interests to the family trade. When his grandmother gently chides him — "Hush, hush, Jacob, love" (071-129) — it is the soft reproach of a household that finds his intensity more amusing than troubling.

### Appearance

Jacob's physical description is woven into the novel's domestic tableaux. On the Sabbath evening, he stands on the counterpane alongside his sister Adelaide Rebekah, "in braided amber" (071-100) — a detail that places him within the ritualized beauty of the family gathering. His "small patriarchal voice" (086-177) marks him as simultaneously a child and an heir to an ancient tradition, a duality captured in the narrator's reference to him as "this modern Jacob at the age of six" (117-025).

## Relationship with Mordecai

Jacob's most touching role is as companion to [[Mordecai]], the consumptive scholar who lodges in the Cohen household. The narrator describes the sight of Mordecai with the child on his knee:

> Jacob Cohen. It is worth while to catch our prophet's expression when he has that remarkable type of young Israel on his knee, and pours forth some Semitic inspiration with a sublime look of melancholy patience and devoutness (117-003).

Mordecai gives Jacob his first lessons (086-072), and the child becomes his most attentive — if unpredictable — pupil. The Hebrew instruction unfolds as a kind of game: Mordecai speaks the words, and "none readier than Jacob at this fascinating game of imitating" (086-096). The child's engagement is sensory rather than intellectual — he responds to the rhythm, the strangeness, the intensity of his teacher's voice rather than the content of what is spoken.

### The Terror of Prophecy

The pedagogical relationship takes a darker turn when Mordecai's prophetic passion overwhelms the child. In a scene of remarkable intensity, Mordecai speaks an invocation with such force that Jacob, who had been playing nearby, falls silent:

> "Child! child!" he called out with a strange cry that startled Jacob to his feet (086-156).

Mordecai's words carry "such a burden of obscure threat — it was as if the thin grappling fingers, shook Jacob's little frame into awe" (086-170, 086-173). The child, overwhelmed, "resolved itself into tears" (086-177). In the aftermath, Jacob performs a gesture of remarkable tenderness: he "lifted up his small patriarchal voice" and pressed his face against Mordecai's breast (086-177, 086-181). The moment encapsulates the doubleness of their relationship — the child both terrified by and drawn to the prophetic intensity he cannot understand.

### Generational Transmission

The relationship between Mordecai and Jacob dramatizes one of the novel's deepest themes: the transmission of Jewish identity across generations. Mordecai, who will not live to see the national revival he prophesies, pours his vision into the child on his knee. Jacob, too young to understand the content of what he receives, nonetheless absorbs it — the Hebrew syllables, the rhythm of prophecy, the presence of a teacher's devotion. The scene prefigures Deronda's eventual role as Mordecai's intellectual heir while also suggesting that transmission happens at many levels, including the pre-verbal, the somatic, the habitual.

The narrator's phrase — "that remarkable type of young Israel" (117-003) — captures the doubleness of Jacob's representation. He is both an individual child and a type: the next generation of Jews who will inherit and transform what their elders have preserved.

## Relationship with Deronda

Deronda's interactions with Jacob reveal his own character. He treats the child with genuine attentiveness, exchanging a knife for a corkscrew in a moment of mutual satisfaction (070-128). Jacob looked up into his face "scanningly for a moment or two" (071-132), a child's instinctive assessment of a stranger's trustworthiness — and found it sufficient. These small kindnesses mark Deronda's growing comfort within the Jewish community he is only beginning to understand as his own.

The knife exchange is a small but significant narrative detail. Deronda, an English gentleman, enters the Cohen shop as an outsider — a visitor to a world he does not yet recognize as his own. His willingness to engage with Jacob on the child's own terms, to take seriously the urgent matter of the knife, signals a capacity for genuine encounter that distinguishes him from characters who would see only the shop, the trade, the stereotype.

## A Portrait of Jewish Domesticity

Through Jacob, Eliot normalizes Jewish family life for her Victorian readers. The Cohen household — with its bustling commerce, doting grandmother, and irrepressible child — counters anti-Semitic stereotypes by presenting Jewish domesticity as warm, ordinary, and recognizably human. The family gathers around the Sabbath table, father and mother "laughed aloud with delight" (070-137) at their son's antics, and the child's restless energy is universal: "most commonly a sudden impulse sent Jacob leaping away into some antic" (086-107).

### Eliot's Strategy of Normalization

The presence of children in Victorian fiction often serves to domesticate characters or settings that might otherwise appear strange or threatening to readers. Eliot employs this strategy with particular care in the Jewish scenes of *Daniel Deronda*. Jacob's irrepressible childishness — his bargaining, his pride in his name, his restlessness during Mordecai's lessons — renders the Cohen household familiar in terms that no reader of any background could fail to recognize. The strategy is both literary and political: by making the Cohens recognizable as a family, Eliot challenges the dehumanizing representations of Jews that circulated in Victorian culture.

The detail that Jacob puts on his hat alongside Mordecai and his father to go to the synagogue (072-083) is presented without commentary — a Jewish custom rendered as ordinary as a child putting on shoes. This is Eliot's method at its most effective: normalization through unremarked detail rather than explicit argument.
