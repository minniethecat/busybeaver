[087-001]But [[Mordecai]] suffered a check which lasted long, from the consciousness

[087-002]of a misapplied agitation; sane as well as excitable, he judged

[087-003]severely his moments of aberration into futile eagerness, and felt

[087-004]discredited with himself. All the more his mind was strained toward the

[087-005]discernment of that friend to come, with whom he would have a calm

[087-006]certainty of fellowship and understanding.

[087-007]It was just then that, in his usual midday guardianship of the old

[087-008]book-shop, he was struck by the appearance of [[Daniel-Deronda|Deronda]], and it is

[087-009]perhaps comprehensible now why Mordecai’s glance took on a sudden eager

[087-010]interest as he looked at the new-comer: he saw a face and frame which

[087-011]seemed to him to realize the long-conceived type. But the disclaimer of

[087-012]Jewish birth was for the moment a backward thrust of double severity,

[087-013]the particular disappointment tending to shake his confidence in the

[087-014]more indefinite expectation. Nevertheless, when he found Deronda seated

[087-015]at the Cohens’ table, the disclaimer was for the moment nullified: the

[087-016]first impression returned with added force, seeming to be guaranteed by

[087-017]this second meeting under circumstance more peculiar than the former;

[087-018]and in asking Deronda if he knew Hebrew, Mordecai was so possessed by

[087-019]the new inrush of belief, that he had forgotten the absence of any

[087-020]other condition to the fulfillment of his hopes. But the answering “No”

[087-021]struck them all down again, and the frustration was more painful than

[087-022]before. After turning his back on the visitor that Sabbath evening,

[087-023]Mordecai went through days of a deep discouragement, like that of men

[087-024]on a doomed ship, who having strained their eyes after a sail, and

[087-025]beheld it with rejoicing, behold it never advance, and say, “Our sick

[087-026]eyes make it.” But the long-contemplated figure had come as an

[087-027]emotional sequence of Mordecai’s firmest theoretic convictions; it had

[087-028]been wrought from the imagery of his most passionate life; and it

[087-029]inevitably reappeared—reappeared in a more specific self-asserting

[087-030]form than ever. Deronda had that sort of resemblance to the

[087-031]preconceived type which a finely individual bust or portrait has to the

[087-032]more generalized copy left in our minds after a long interval: we renew

[087-033]our memory with delight, but we hardly know with how much correction.

[087-034]And now, his face met Mordecai’s inward gaze as it had always belonged

[087-035]to the awaited friend, raying out, moreover, some of that influence

[087-036]which belongs to breathing flesh; till by-and-by it seemed that

[087-037]discouragement had turned into a new obstinacy of resistance, and the

[087-038]ever-recurrent vision had the force of an outward call to disregard

[087-039]counter-evidence, and keep expectation awake. It was Deronda now who

[087-040]was seen in the often painful night-watches, when we are all liable to

[087-041]be held with the clutch of a single thought—whose figure, never with

[087-042]its back turned, was seen in moments of soothed reverie or soothed

[087-043]dozing, painted on that golden sky which was the doubly blessed symbol

[087-044]of advancing day and of approaching rest.

[087-045]Mordecai knew that the nameless stranger was to come and redeem his

[087-046]ring; and, in spite of contrary chances, the wish to see him again was

[087-047]growing into a belief that he should see him. In the January weeks, he

[087-048]felt an increasing agitation of that subdued hidden quality which

[087-049]hinders nervous people from any steady occupation on the eve of an

[087-050]anticipated change. He could not go on with his printing of Hebrew on

[087-051]little Jacob’s mind; or with his attendance at a weekly club, which was

[087-052]another effort of the same forlorn hope: something else was coming. The

[087-053]one thing he longed for was to get as far as the river, which he could

[087-054]do but seldom and with difficulty. He yearned with a poet’s yearning

[087-055]for the wide sky, the far-reaching vista of bridges, the tender and

[087-056]fluctuating lights on the water which seems to breathe with a life that

[087-057]can shiver and mourn, be comforted and rejoice.

[087-058]CHAPTER XXXIX.

[087-059]“Vor den Wissenden sich stellen,

[087-060]Sicher ist’s in allen Fällen!

[087-061]Wenn du lange dich gequälet,

[087-062]Weiß er gleich, wo dir es fehlet.

[087-063]Auch auf Beifall darfst du hoffen;

[087-064]Denn er weiß, wo du’s getroffen.”

[087-065]—GOETHE: _West-östlicher Divan_.

[087-066]Momentous things happened to Deronda the very evening of that visit to

[087-067]the small house at Chelsea, when there was the discussion about [[Mirah-Lapidoth|Mirah]]’s

[087-068]public name. But for the family group there, what appeared to be the

[087-069]chief sequence connected with it occurred two days afterward. [[About]]

[087-070]four o’clock wheels paused before the door, and there came one of those

[087-071]knocks with an accompanying ring which serve to magnify the sense of

[087-072]social existence in a region where the most enlivening signals are

[087-073]usually those of the muffin-man. All the girls were at home, and the

[087-074]two rooms were thrown together to make space for Kate’s drawing, as

[087-075]well as a great length of embroidery which had taken the place of the

[087-076]satin cushions—a sort of _pièce de résistance_ in the courses of

[087-077]needlework, taken up by any clever fingers that happened to be at

[087-078][[freedom|liberty]]. It stretched across the front room picturesquely enough, Mrs.

[087-079]Meyrick bending over it on one corner, Mab in the middle, and Amy at

[087-080]the other end. Mirah, whose performances in point of sewing were on the

[087-081]make-shift level of the tailor-bird’s, her education in that branch

[087-082]having been much neglected, was acting as reader to the party, seated

[087-083]on a camp-stool; in which position she also served Kate as model for a

[087-084]title-page vignette, symbolizing a fair public absorbed in the

[087-085]successive volumes of the family tea-table. She was giving forth with

[087-086]charming distinctness the delightful Essay of Elia, “The Praise of

[087-087]Chimney-Sweeps,” and all were smiling over the “innocent blackness,”

[087-088]when the imposing knock and ring called their thoughts to loftier

[087-089]spheres, and they looked up in wonderment.

[087-090]“Dear me!” said Mrs. Meyrick; “can it be Lady Mallinger? Is there a

[087-091]grand carriage, Amy?”

[087-092]“No—only a hansom cab. It must be a gentleman.”

[087-093]“The Prime Minister, I should think,” said Kate dryly. “[[Hans-Meyrick|Hans]] says the

[087-094]greatest man in [[london|London]] may get into a hansom cab.”

[087-095]“Oh, oh, oh!” cried Mab. “Suppose it should be Lord Russell!”

[087-096]The five bright faces were all looking amused when the old maid-servant

[087-097]bringing in a card distractedly left the parlor-door open, and there

[087-098]was seen bowing toward Mrs. Meyrick a figure quite unlike that of the

[087-099]respected Premier—tall and physically impressive even in his kid and

[087-100]kerseymere, with massive face, flamboyant hair, and gold spectacles: in

[087-101]fact, as Mrs. Meyrick saw from the card, _[[Herr-Klesmer|Julius Klesmer]]_.

[087-102]Even embarrassment could hardly have made the “little mother” awkward,

[087-103]but quick in her perceptions she was at once aware of the situation,

[087-104]and felt well satisfied that the great personage had come to Mirah

[087-105]instead of requiring her to come to him; taking it as a sign of active

[087-106]interest. But when he entered, the rooms shrank into closets, the

[087-107]cottage piano, Mab thought, seemed a ridiculous toy, and the entire

[087-108]family existence as petty and private as an establishment of mice in

[087-109]the Tuileries. Klesmer’s personality, especially his way of glancing

[087-110]round him, immediately suggested vast areas and a multitudinous

[087-111]audience, and probably they made the usual scenery of his

[087-112]consciousness, for we all of us carry on our thinking in some habitual

[087-113]locus where there is a presence of other souls, and those who take in a

[087-114]larger sweep than their neighbors are apt to seem mightily vain and

[087-115]affected. Klesmer was vain, but not more so than many contemporaries of

[087-116]heavy aspect, whose [[pride|vanity]] leaps out and startles one like a spear out

[087-117]of a walking-stick; as to his carriage and gestures, these were as

[087-118]natural to him as the length of his fingers; and the rankest

[087-119]affectation he could have shown would have been to look diffident and

[087-120]demure. While his grandiose air was making Mab feel herself a

[087-121]ridiculous toy to match the cottage piano, he was taking in the details

[087-122]around him with a keen and thoroughly kind sensibility. He remembered a

[087-123]home no longer than this on the outskirts of Bohemia; and in the

[087-124]figurative Bohemia too he had had large acquaintance with the variety

[087-125]and romance which belong to small incomes. He addressed Mrs. Meyrick

[087-126]with the utmost deference.

[087-127]“I hope I have not taken too great a freedom. Being in the

[087-128]neighborhood, I ventured to save time by calling. Our friend, Mr.

[087-129]Deronda, mentioned to me an understanding that I was to have the honor

[087-130]of becoming acquainted with a young lady here—Miss [[Lapidoth]].”

[087-131]Klesmer had really discerned Mirah in the first moment of entering,

[087-132]but, with subtle politeness, he looked round bowingly at the three

[087-133]sisters as if he were uncertain which was the young lady in question.

[087-134]“Those are my daughters: this is Miss Lapidoth,” said Mrs. Meyrick,

[087-135]waving her hand toward Mirah.

[087-136]“Ah,” said Klesmer, in a tone of gratified expectation, turning a

[087-137]radiant smile and deep bow to Mirah, who, instead of being in the least

[087-138]taken by surprise, had a calm pleasure in her face. She liked the look

[087-139]of Klesmer, feeling sure that he would scold her, like a great musician

[087-140]and a kind man.

[087-141]“You will not object to beginning our acquaintance by [[music|singing]] to me,”

[087-142]he added, aware that they would all be relieved by getting rid of

[087-143]preliminaries.

[087-144]“I shall be very glad. It is good of you to be willing to listen to

[087-145]me,” said Mirah, moving to the piano. “Shall I accompany myself?”

[087-146]“By all means,” said Klesmer, seating himself, at Mrs. Meyrick’s

[087-147]invitation, where he could have a good view of the singer. The acute

[087-148]little mother would not have acknowledged the weakness, but she really

[087-149]said to herself, “He will like her singing better if he sees her.”

[087-150]All the feminine hearts except Mirah’s were beating fast with anxiety,

[087-151]thinking Klesmer terrific as he sat with his listening frown on, and

[087-152]only daring to look at him furtively. If he did say anything severe it

[087-153]would be so hard for them all. They could only comfort themselves with

[087-154]thinking that Prince Camaralzaman, who had heard the finest things,

[087-155]preferred Mirah’s singing to any other: also she appeared to be doing

[087-156]her very best, as if she were more instead of less at ease than usual.

[087-157]The song she had chosen was a fine setting of some words selected from

[087-158]Leopardi’s grand Ode to Italy:,

[087-159]“_O patria mia, vedo le mura c gli archi

[087-160]E le colonne e i simula-cri e l’erme

[087-161]Torridegli avi nostri_”,

[087-162]This was recitative: then followed,

[087-163]“_Ma la gloria—non vedo_”,

[087-164]a mournful melody, a rhythmic plaint. After this came a climax of

[087-165]devout triumph—passing from the subdued adoration of a happy Andante

[087-166]in the words,

[087-167]“_Beatissimi voi.

[087-168]Che offriste il petto alle nemiche lance

[087-169]Per amor di costei che al sol vi diede_”,

[087-170]to the joyous outburst of an exultant Allegro in,

[087-171]“_Oh viva, oh viva:

[087-172]Beatissimi voi

[087-173]Mentre nel monde si favelli o scriva._”

[087-174]When she had ended, Klesmer said after a moment,

[087-175]“That is old Leo’s [[art|music]].”

[087-176]“Yes, he was my last master—at Vienna: so fierce and so good,” said

[087-177]Mirah, with a melancholy smile. “He prophesied that my voice would not

[087-178]do for the stage. And he was right.”

[087-179]“_Con_tinue, if you please,” said Klesmer, putting out his lips and

[087-180]shaking his long fingers, while he went on with a smothered

[087-181]articulation quite unintelligible to the audience.

[087-182]The three girls detested him unanimously for not saying one word of

[087-183]praise. Mrs. Meyrick was a little alarmed.

[087-184]Mirah, simply bent on doing what Klesmer desired, and imagining that he

[087-185]would now like to hear her sing some German, went through Prince

[087-186]Radzivill’s music to Gretchen’s songs in the _Faust_, one after the

[087-187]other without any interrogatory pause. When she had finished he rose

[087-188]and walked to the extremity of the small space at command, then walked

[087-189]back to the piano, where Mirah had risen from her seat and stood

[087-190]looking toward him with her little hands crossed before her, meekly

[087-191]awaiting judgment; then with a sudden unknitting of his brow and with

[087-192]beaming eyes, he stretched out his hand and said abruptly, “Let us

[087-193]shake hands: you are a musician.”
