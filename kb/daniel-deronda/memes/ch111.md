[111-001]She was standing in the middle of the room when [[Daniel-Deronda|Deronda]] was announced,

[111-002]and as he approached her she perceived that he too for some reason was

[111-003]not his usual self. She could not have defined the change except by

[111-004]saying that he looked less happy than usual, and appeared to be under

[111-005]some effort in speaking to her. And yet the speaking was the slightest

[111-006]possible. They both said, “How do you do?” quite curtly; and [[Gwendolen-Harleth|Gwendolen]],

[111-007]instead of sitting down, moved to a little distance, resting her arms

[111-008]slightly on the tall back of a chair, while Deronda stood where he

[111-009]was,—both feeling it difficult to say any more, though the

[111-010]preoccupation in his mind could hardly have been more remote than it

[111-011]was from Gwendolen’s conception. She naturally saw in his embarrassment

[111-012]some reflection of her own. Forced to speak, she found all her training

[111-013]in [[secrecy|concealment]] and self-command of no use to her and began with timid

[111-014]awkwardness,

[111-015]“You will wonder why I begged you to come. I wanted to ask you

[111-016]something. You said I was ignorant. That is true. And what can I do but

[111-017]ask you?”

[111-018]And at this moment she was feeling it utterly impossible to put the

[111-019]questions she had intended. Something new in her nervous manner roused

[111-020]Deronda’s anxiety lest there might be a new crisis. He said with the

[111-021]sadness of affection in his voice,

[111-022]“My only regret is, that I can be of so little use to you.” The words

[111-023]and the tone touched a new spring in her, and she went on with more

[111-024]sense of freedom, yet still not saying anything she had designed to

[111-025]say, and beginning to hurry, that she might somehow arrive at the right

[111-026]words.

[111-027]“I wanted to tell you that I have always been thinking of your advice,

[111-028]but is it any use?—I can’t make myself different, because things about

[111-029]me raise bad feelings—and I must go on—I can alter nothing—it is no

[111-030]use.”

[111-031]She paused an instant, with the consciousness that she was not finding

[111-032]the right words, but began again hurriedly, “But if I go on I shall get

[111-033]worse. I want not to get worse. I should like to be what you wish.

[111-034]There are people who are good and enjoy great things—I know there are.

[111-035]I am a contemptible creature. I feel as if I should get wicked with

[111-036]hating people. I have tried to think that I would go away from

[111-037]everybody. But I can’t. There are so many things to hinder me. You

[111-038]think, perhaps, that I don’t mind. But I do mind. I am afraid of

[111-039]everything. I am afraid of getting wicked. Tell me what I can do.”

[111-040]She had forgotten everything but that image of her helpless misery

[111-041]which she was trying to make present to Deronda in broken allusive

[111-042]speech—wishing to convey but not express all her need. Her eyes were

[111-043]tearless, and had a look of smarting in their dilated brilliancy; there

[111-044]was a subdued sob in her voice which was more and more veiled, till it

[111-045]was hardly above a whisper. She was hurting herself with the jewels

[111-046]that glittered on her tightly-clasped fingers pressed against her heart.

[111-047]The feeling Deronda endured in these moments he afterward called

[111-048]horrible. Words seemed to have no more rescue in them than if he had

[111-049]been beholding a vessel in peril of wreck—the poor ship with its

[111-050]many-lived anguish beaten by the inescapable storm. How could he grasp

[111-051]the long-growing process of this young creature’s wretchedness?—how

[111-052]arrest and change it with a sentence? He was afraid of his own voice.

[111-053]The words that rushed into his mind seemed in their feebleness nothing

[111-054]better than despair made audible, or than that insensibility to

[111-055]another’s hardship which applies precept to soothe pain. He felt

[111-056]himself holding a crowd of words imprisoned within his lips, as if the

[111-057]letting them escape would be a violation of awe before the mysteries of

[111-058]our human lot. The thought that urged itself foremost was—“Confess

[111-059]everything to your husband; have nothing concealed:”—the words carried

[111-060]in his mind a vision of reasons which would have needed much fuller

[111-061]expressions for Gwendolen to apprehend them, but before he had begun

[111-062]those brief sentences, the door opened and the husband entered.

[111-063][[Henleigh-Grandcourt|Grandcourt]] had deliberately gone out and turned back to satisfy a

[111-064]suspicion. What he saw was Gwendolen’s face of anguish framed black

[111-065]like a nun’s, and Deronda standing three yards from her with a look of

[111-066]sorrow such as he might have bent on the last struggle of life in a

[111-067]beloved object. Without any show of surprise Grandcourt nodded to

[111-068]Deronda, gave a second look at Gwendolen, passed on, and seated himself

[111-069]easily at a little distance crossing his legs, taking out his

[111-070]handkerchief and trifling with it elegantly.

[111-071]Gwendolen had shrunk and changed her attitude on seeing him, but she

[111-072]did not turn or move from her place. It was not a moment in which she

[111-073]could feign anything, or manifest any strong revulsion of feeling: the

[111-074]passionate movement of her last speech was still too strong within her.

[111-075]What she felt beside was a dull despairing sense that her interview

[111-076]with Deronda was at an end: a curtain had fallen. But he, naturally,

[111-077]was urged into self-possession and effort by susceptibility to what

[111-078]might follow for her from being seen by her husband in this betrayal of

[111-079]agitation; and feeling that any pretense of ease in prolonging his

[111-080]visit would only exaggerate Grandcourt’s possible conjectures of

[111-081]duplicity, he merely said,

[111-082]“I will not stay longer now. Good-bye.”

[111-083]He put out his hand, and she let him press her poor little chill

[111-084]fingers; but she said no good-bye.

[111-085]When he had left the room, Gwendolen threw herself into a seat, with an

[111-086]expectation as dull as her despair—the expectation that she was going

[111-087]to be punished. But Grandcourt took no notice: he was satisfied to have

[111-088]let her know that she had not deceived him, and to keep a silence which

[111-089]was formidable with omniscience. He went out that evening, and her plea

[111-090]of feeling ill was accepted without even a sneer.

[111-091]The next morning at breakfast he said, “I am going yachting to the

[111-092]Mediterranean.”

[111-093]“When?” said Gwendolen, with a leap of heart which had hope in it.

[111-094]“The day after to-morrow. The yacht is at Marseilles. [[Lush]] is gone to

[111-095]get everything ready.”

[111-096]“Shall I have mamma to stay with me, then?” said Gwendolen, the new

[111-097]sudden possibility of peace and affection filling her mind like a burst

[111-098]of morning light.

[111-099]“No; you will go with me.”

[111-103]Triumphed above resentment. ’Tis the mark

[111-104]Of regal natures, with the wider life.

[111-111]Some human property.

[111-112]Deronda, in parting from Gwendolen, had abstained from saying, “I shall

[111-113]not see you again for a long while: I am going away,” lest Grandcourt

[111-114]should understand him to imply that the fact was of importance to her.

[111-115]He was actually going away under circumstances so momentous to himself

[111-116]that when he set out to fulfill his promise of calling on her, he was

[111-117]already under the shadow of a solemn emotion which revived the deepest

[111-118]experience of his life.

[111-119][[Sir-Hugo-Mallinger|Sir Hugo]] had sent for him to his chambers with the note—“Come

[111-120]immediately. Something has happened:” a preparation that caused him

[111-121]some relief when, on entering the baronet’s study, he was received with

[111-122]grave affection instead of the distress which he had apprehended.

[111-123]“It is nothing to grieve you, sir?” said Deronda, in a tone rather of

[111-124]restored confidence than question, as he took the hand held out to him.

[111-125]There was an unusual meaning in Sir Hugo’s look, and a subdued emotion

[111-126]in his voice, as he said,

[111-127]“No, Dan, no. Sit down. I have something to say.”

[111-128]Deronda obeyed, not without presentiment. It was extremely rare for Sir

[111-129]Hugo to show so much serious feeling.

[111-130]“Not to grieve me, my boy, no. At least, if there is nothing in it that

[111-131]will grieve you too much. But I hardly expected that this—just

[111-132]this—would ever happen. There have been reasons why I have never

[111-133]prepared you for it. There have been reasons why I have never told you

[111-134]anything about your parentage. But I have striven in every way not to

[111-135]make that an injury to you.”

[111-136]Sir Hugo paused, but Deronda could not speak. He could not say, “I have

[111-137]never felt it an injury.” Even if that had been true, he could not have

[111-138]trusted his voice to say anything. Far more than any one but himself

[111-139]could know of was hanging on this moment when the secrecy was to be

[111-140]broken. Sir Hugo had never seen the grand face he delighted in so

[111-141]pale—the lips pressed together with such a look of pain. He went on

[111-142]with a more anxious tenderness, as if he had a new fear of wounding.

[111-143]“I have acted in obedience to your mother’s wishes. The secrecy was her

[111-144]wish. But now she desires to remove it. She desires to see you. I will

[111-145]put this letter into your hands, which you can look at by-and-by. It

[111-146]will merely tell you what she wishes you to do, and where you will find

[111-147]her.”

[111-148]Sir Hugo held out a letter written on foreign paper, which Deronda

[111-149]thrust into his breast-pocket, with a sense of relief that he was not

[111-150]called on to read anything immediately. The emotion on Daniel’s face

[111-151]had gained on the baronet, and was visibly shaking his composure. Sir

[111-152]Hugo found it difficult to say more. And Deronda’s whole soul was

[111-153]possessed by a question which was the hardest in the world to utter.

[111-154]Yet he could not bear to delay it. This was a sacramental moment. If he

[111-155]let it pass, he could not recover the influences under which it was

[111-156]possible to utter the words and meet the answer. For some moments his

[111-157]eyes were cast down, and it seemed to both as if thoughts were in the

[111-158]air between them. But at last Deronda looked at Sir Hugo, and said,

[111-159]with a tremulous reverence in his voice—dreading to convey indirectly

[111-160]the reproach that affection had for years been stifling,

[111-161]“Is my father also living?”

[111-162]The answer came immediately in a low emphatic tone—“No.”

[111-163]In the mingled emotions which followed that answer it was impossible to

[111-164]distinguish joy from pain.

[111-165]Some new light had fallen on the past for Sir Hugo too in this

[111-166]interview. After a silence in which Deronda felt like one whose creed

[111-167]is gone before he has religiously embraced another, the baronet said,

[111-168]in a tone of confession,

[111-169]“Perhaps I was wrong, Dan, to undertake what I did. And perhaps I liked

[111-170]it a little too well—having you all to myself. But if you have had any

[111-171]pain which I might have helped, I ask you to forgive me.”

[111-172]“The forgiveness has long been there,” said Deronda “The chief pain has

[111-173]always been on account of some one else—whom I never knew—whom I am

[111-174]now to know. It has not hindered me from feeling an affection for you

[111-175]which has made a large part of all the life I remember.”

[111-176]It seemed one impulse that made the two men clasp each other’s hand for

[111-177]a moment.
