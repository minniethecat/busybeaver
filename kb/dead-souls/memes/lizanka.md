# Lizanka (Madame Manilov)

## Character Profile

Lizanka — known formally as Madame Manilov — is the **wife of the sentimental [[landownership|landowner]] [[manilov|Manilov]]** and mistress of the [[manilovka|Manilovka estate]]. Her given name is revealed only once, when her husband calls her "dearest Lizanka" as [[chichikov|Chichikov]] prepares to depart （002-135）. She has been married to [[manilov|Manilov]] for more than eight years （002-015）.

Physically, she is of "not wholly unpleasing exterior" and receives [[chichikov|Chichikov]] dressed "in a well-fitting, high-necked morning dress of pale-coloured silk" （002-027）. She speaks with a slight lisp and has "small white hands" （002-027） — the picture of a gentlewoman, but one whose gentility is entirely ornamental.

Her education is the **boarding-school curriculum** that Gogol satirizes at length: "the three principal subjects which constitute the basis of human virtue" — the French language ("indispensable to the happiness of married life"), piano-playing ("a thing wherewith to beguile a husband's leisure moments"), and "that particular department of housewifery which is comprised in the knitting of purses and other 'surprises'" （002-015）. This education has left her incapable of managing a household: the storeroom is never checked, the housekeeper steals, servants are slovenly and drunken, and the furniture remains half-finished and covered in grease — yet neither she nor her husband "seemed to entertain the least suspicion" of the disorder around them （002-015）.

The [[manilov|Manilov]]s' marriage is a parody of domestic bliss. They exchange bites of apple and bonbons with the formula "Open your mouth, dearest — and let me pop into it this titbit," and for birthdays offer each other "surprise presents" in the shape of glass receptacles for tooth-powder. Their kisses are "so prolonged and languishing that during its continuance you could have smoked a small cigar" （002-015）. Gogol drily notes that "a household requires other pursuits to be engaged in than lengthy embracings and the preparing of cunning 'surprises.'"

## Role in Dead Souls

Lizanka appears only in Chapter 2, during [[chichikov|Chichikov]]'s visit to [[manilovka|Manilovka]], but she represents a distinct feminine type within Gogol's gallery. She is the **boarding-school product** — a woman whose education has equipped her for drawing-room pleasantries but left her entirely disconnected from any practical or moral substance. Her conversation with [[chichikov|Chichikov]] consists of asking his opinion of the town's [[officialdom|officials]], prompting her husband with "And what of his wife?" and receiving [[chichikov|Chichikov]]'s mechanically flattering responses （002-042–002-044）.

Gogol explicitly contrasts her with the Widow Korobochka in Chapter 3: between Lizanka and Korobochka there is a "gulf" — one represents the drawing-room lady who "spent most of her time in yawning behind books" while the other, however crude, at least engages directly with the material world （003-169）. Through this contrast, Gogol suggests that the supposedly refined boarding-school education produces women no more useful — and perhaps less honest — than the uneducated Korobochka.

## Relationships

- **[[manilov|Manilov]]** — Her husband of over eight years; their relationship is a parody of marital affection, consisting entirely of sweet nothings, surprise presents, and languishing kisses while the household decays around them （002-015）
- **Themistocleus** and **Alkid** — Her two young sons, named with classical pretension. Themistocleus (the elder, about eight) is being groomed for a diplomatic career; Alkid is the younger （002-069–002-070）
- **[[chichikov|Chichikov]]** — The visitor whom she receives with conventional politeness, asking formulaic questions about the town and its [[officialdom|officials]] （002-031–002-044）

## Quotations

> "Is Paul Ivanovitch leaving us so soon, dearest Lizanka?" — Manilov, using her given name （002-135）

> "She had been gently brought up, and gentle nurture, as we all know, is to be acquired only in boarding schools, and boarding schools, as we know, hold the three principal subjects which constitute the basis of human virtue to be the French language..., piano-playing..., and that particular department of housewifery which is comprised in the knitting of purses and other 'surprises.'" （002-015）
