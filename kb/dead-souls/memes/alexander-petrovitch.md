# Alexander Petrovitch

## Character Profile

Alexander Petrovitch is the principal of the educational establishment
where the young [[tientietnikov|Tientietnikov]] spends his formative years. He never
appears in the novel's present tense — he is a figure of memory,
invoked retrospectively in Part II as [[tientietnikov|Tientietnikov]] reflects on his
lost youth [011-070].

Gogol draws him as a portrait of the **ideal [[russian-empire|Russia]]n educator**. "The
idol of his pupils, and the admiration of his assistants," Alexander
Petrovitch is "gifted with an extraordinary measure of good sense"
[011-070]. He knows "the peculiarities of the [[russian-empire|Russia]]n of his day" and
possesses an uncanny ability to reach boys through trust rather than
punishment. A boy who has committed a prank will "voluntarily approach
his preceptor and make to him free confession" — and leave with his
head "held higher, not lower, than before" [011-071].

His educational philosophy is radical for its time. He rejects lectures
on good behaviour in favour of a single imperative: "I want to see
intelligence, and nothing else. The boy who devotes his attention to
becoming clever will never play the fool, for under such circumstances,
folly disappears of itself" [011-071]. Pupils who cannot meet his
standards are not retained; those of exceptional talent enter a senior
class where Alexander Petrovitch personally trains them in the "superior
frame of mind" — the art of bearing ridicule, keeping one's temper,
repressing oneself, and retaining tranquillity of soul [011-073].

## Role in Dead Souls

Alexander Petrovitch serves as the novel's **vanished ideal against
which all subsequent mediocrity is measured**. His death — which occurs
just as [[tientietnikov|Tientietnikov]] reaches the coveted senior class — is the pivot
on which the young man's life turns. The new principal, a man of narrow
pedagogical orthodoxy, dismantles everything Alexander Petrovitch built,
and [[tientietnikov|Tientietnikov]]'s education collapses into listlessness and
disillusionment [011-080].

The narrator explicitly attributes the integrity of Alexander
Petrovitch's pupils to his methods: "no ex-pupil of Alexander
Petrovitch ever wavered from the right road, but, familiar with life
and with men, armed with the weapons of prudence, exerted a powerful
influence upon wrongdoers" [011-077]. The passage is an elegy for a
kind of education [[russian-empire|Russia]] never institutionalised — one that forms
character rather than imparting facts.
