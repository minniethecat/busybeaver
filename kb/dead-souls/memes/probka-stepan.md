# Probka Stepan

## Character Profile

Probka Stepan is a deceased [[serfdom|serf]] carpenter whose name appears among the [[dead-souls|dead soul]]s Sobakevitch is eager to sell. Though dead, Probka receives a vivid imaginary biography — first from Sobakevitch, who praises even a dead man's craftsmanship when there is a sale to be made, and then from [[chichikov|Chichikov]], who contemplates the name on the [[serfdom|serf]] list and reconstructs Probka's life and death.

Sobakevitch describes him as a giant: "What a strong fellow he was! He had served in the Guards, and the Lord only knows what they had given for him, seeing that he was over three arshins in height" [005-085]. When [[chichikov|Chichikov]] tries to note that Probka is dead, Sobakevitch talks over the objection — his eloquence "borne on the torrent of its own verbiage" [005-086].

Later, examining the list of purchased [[dead-souls|souls]], [[chichikov|Chichikov]] pauses at Probka's entry: "Probka Stepan, carpenter, very sober." He imagines the man tramping the country with an axe in his belt and boots on his shoulder, living on bread and dried fish, sewing paper roubles into his breeches [007-028].

Then [[chichikov|Chichikov]] pictures Probka's death: mounting a scaffold around a church cupola for good wages, climbing to the cross above, missing his footing, and falling headlong — with only Uncle Michai to scratch his neck and mutter, "Ah, Vania, for once you have been too clever!" [007-028].

## Role in Dead Souls

Probka Stepan is a vividly realized [[dead-souls|dead soul]] — a ghost given flesh by the very men who profit from his death. Sobakevitch's sales pitch transforms a dead carpenter into the finest workman in [[russian-empire|Russia]], while [[chichikov|Chichikov]]'s private meditation conjures an entire life from a single line on a register.

Between them, they demonstrate that the "[[dead-souls|dead soul]]s" are not abstractions but real men who lived, labored, and died. Probka's imagined fall from the church cupola is a poignantly imagined moment, as the narrator suddenly sees the human cost behind the bureaucratic transaction.

Sobakevitch later admits, when pressed by the President of the Court, that he sold Probka Stepan — along with [[milushkin|Milushkin the bricklayer]] and [[maksim-teliatnikov|Teliatnikov]] the bootmaker — simply out of "a mere whim," hanging his head as though already repenting [007-089].
