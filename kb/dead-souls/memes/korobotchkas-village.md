# Korobotchka's Village

## Description

[[korobotchka|Korobotchka]]'s village is reached by [[chichikov|Chichikov]] and [[selifan|Selifan]] by chance on a dark,
rainy night, after having lost their way from [[manilovka|Manilovka]]. Guided by the barking
of dogs, their britchka collides with a garden wall before they are admitted by
an elderly serving woman [003-023]. The darkness prevents detailed observation,
but a light gleams in a window, and the britchka comes to rest before "a
moderate-sized mansion" where rain beats "sonorously down upon the wooden roof"
[003-023].

The interior parlour reveals the modesty of the household: walls hung with old
striped curtains, pictures of birds, and "small, antique mirrors" set in dark
frames carved like scrolls of foliage, behind which are tucked letters, old
packs of cards, or stockings [003-023]. A clock with a flowered dial hangs on
the wall.

By daylight, the village reveals its character. From the window [[chichikov|Chichikov]] sees
"a poulterer's premises": a narrow yard "full of poultry and other domestic
creatures" — game fowls, barn-door fowls, a strutting cock, and a sow with her
family rooting among litter and eating a young pullet and melon rinds [003-060].
Beyond the yard, a kitchen garden contains "cabbages, onions, potatoes,
beetroots, and other household vegetables," with a few fruit trees covered in
netting against magpies and sparrows, and scarecrows on long poles — one
wearing "a cast-off cap of the hostess's" [003-060].

Beyond the garden stand a number of peasants' huts. Though "scattered, instead
of being arranged in regular rows," these huts are evidently well maintained:
"all rotten planks in their roofing had been replaced with new ones, and none of
their doors were askew," while tiltsheds show the presence of spare wagons,
some almost new [003-060]. This prompts [[chichikov|Chichikov]] to note: "This lady owns by
no means a poor village."

## Role in Dead Souls

[[korobotchka|Korobotchka]]'s village is the second stop on [[chichikov|Chichikov]]'s circuit of [[landownership|landowner]]
[[landownership|estate]]s in Part I, following his visit to [[manilovka|Manilovka]]. The [[landownership|estate]] perfectly
mirrors its owner: modest, frugal, well-maintained, and utterly provincial. Its
orderly cultivation reflects the widow's careful penny-pin[[table-of-ranks|chin]]g habits — she
keeps hoards of money in striped purses while lamenting crop failures — and its
modest scale mirrors her limited intellectual horizon.

The [[landownership|estate]] serves as the setting for [[chichikov|Chichikov]]'s most laborious negotiation. The
widow is suspicious of his offer to buy [[dead-souls|dead soul]]s, and her resistance forces
[[chichikov|Chichikov]] to discard his polished manners and resort to crude persuasion. The
transaction here takes far more effort than at [[manilovka|Manilovka]], demonstrating the
spectrum of [[landownership|landowner]] types Gogol paints across the five [[landownership|estate]]s.

The village reappears in the novel's denouement when the widow herself travels
to the [[town-of-n|town of N.]] to inquire about the value of [[dead-souls|dead soul]]s — a visit that
triggers the unmasking of [[purchase-of-dead-souls|Chichikov's scheme]] [009-016 – 009-022].

## Appearances

- **Chapter 3**: [[chichikov|Chichikov]]'s arrival on a rainy night, the morning view of the
  poultry-yard and garden, the description of the well-maintained peasant huts
  [003-023 – 003-060]. The negotiation over [[dead-souls|dead soul]]s occupies the remainder
  of the chapter.
- **Chapter 9**: [[korobotchka|Madame Korobotchka]] arrives in town to ask about the going price
  of [[dead-souls|dead soul]]s, spreading the [[provincial-scandal|rumor]] that unravels [[chichikov|Chichikov]]'s plans
  [009-016 – 009-022].
