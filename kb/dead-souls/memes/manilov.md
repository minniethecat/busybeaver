# Manilov

## Character Profile

Manilov is the first [[landownership|landowner]] [[chichikov|Chichikov]] visits and in many ways the most elusive. He is a man of "sweet and sentimental" disposition, perpetually smiling, who greets everyone with exaggerated warmth. Gogol describes him as belonging to the category of "so-so" people — neither one thing nor another, pleasant but empty （002-001）. His most characteristic gesture is the unfinished: a bridge he dreams of building, a book he has been reading for two years with a bookmark permanently at page 14, chairs that remain un-upholstered because the work was never completed.

[[manilovka|Manilov's estate]] reflects his character. The manor house stands exposed on a hill, the garden is overgrown, the steward runs everything (and steals), and Manilov himself sits in his study dreaming of improbable improvements while smoking a long pipe. His relationship with his wife is one of elaborate mutual flattery; they exchange bonbons and call each other by pet names.

## Role in Dead Souls

Manilov is the **first test** of [[purchase-of-dead-souls|Chichikov's scheme]] — and it passes easily. When [[chichikov|Chichikov]] proposes buying his [[dead-souls|dead soul]]s, Manilov is so bewildered that he offers them for nothing, even agreeing to pay the transfer costs himself. His inability to grasp that [[chichikov|Chichikov]] is proposing something illegal (or at least deeply strange) reveals the vacuum where his judgment should be.

Manilov represents a particular failure mode of the [[russian-empire|Russia]]n gentry: the substitution of sentiment for action, of manners for morality. He performs gentility flawlessly — the bows, the smiles, the polished phrases — but there is nothing beneath the performance. He is, as Gogol writes, a man "without enthusiasm" — not in the sense of lacking passion, but in the sense of lacking any principle or conviction that could resist another's will.
