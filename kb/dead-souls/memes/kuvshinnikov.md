# Lieutenant Kuvshinnikov

## Character Profile

Lieutenant Kuvshinnikov is known to the reader entirely through
[[nozdrev|Nozdrev]]'s enthusiastic narration — he never appears directly, but
[[nozdrev|Nozdrev]] speaks of him with such vivid admiration that he becomes one
of the novel's most memorable unseen characters.

[[nozdrev|Nozdrev]] describes him as a man of irresistible charm and audacity.
"He is as delightful as you could wish," [[nozdrev|Nozdrev]] declares, recounting
how Kuvshinnikov would breakfast with him in a restaurant and then
proceed to flirt with every woman in the establishment [004-031].
His particular talent is dancing: [[nozdrev|Nozdrev]] boasts of how Kuvshinnikov
once performed the _galop_ with such verve that "even the waiters
stopped to watch" [004-042].

The lieutenant's most famous exploit involves a waitress "who sang like
a canary" — Kuvshinnikov, "happening to be sitting with her," so
thoroughly charmed her that the story became [[nozdrev|Nozdrev]]'s favorite
anecdote [004-050]. Through these secondhand stories, Kuvshinnikov
emerges as the archetype of the devil-may-care officer: all dash,
no substance, irresistible to women and admired by men like [[nozdrev|Nozdrev]]
who mistake bravado for character.

## Role in Dead Souls

Kuvshinnikov serves as [[nozdrev|Nozdrev]]'s **imagined alter ego** — the man
[[nozdrev|Nozdrev]] wishes he were, or believes himself to be. [[nozdrev|Nozdrev]]'s constant
invocation of Kuvshinnikov tells us more about [[nozdrev|Nozdrev]] than about
Kuvshinnikov: the blustering [[landownership|landowner]] needs a more glamorous
companion to validate his own self-image as a man of the world.

His purely narrative existence (we never meet him; we only hear about
him) also illustrates Gogol's technique of creating vivid minor
characters through reported speech alone.
