# Public Prosecutor

## Character Profile

The Public Prosecutor is a senior legal [[officialdom|official]] in the [[town-of-n|provincial town]]
of N., distinguished by his remarkable eyebrows — "beetling brows" over
perpetually winking, blinking eyes [001-009]. Gogol sketches him as a
figure of vague importance rather than active competence: he has
"little to do" [007-076] and is defined more by his physical
peculiarities than by any legal acumen.

He is a fixture at the town's dinner parties and social gatherings.
At the [[governors-ball|Governor's ball]], he is led about by the arm, a passive
participant in the swirl of provincial society [008-024]. His blinking
silence makes him a blank screen onto which others project their
assumptions.

This passive quality may explain why, when the [[chichikov|Chichikov]] scandal
breaks, he is among the most profoundly affected — he has never
cultivated the skepticism needed to survive a genuine crisis.

## Role in Dead Souls

The Public Prosecutor serves as the novel's **most extreme example of
the fragility of provincial [[officialdom|official]]dom**. When [[provincial-scandal|rumor]]s spread that
[[chichikov|Chichikov]] may be a forger, a spy, or even Napoleon in disguise, the
Prosecutor — who has never questioned the order of things — is
overwhelmed by cognitive dissonance.

He goes home, sits down, and dies of sheer shock. Gogol describes it
as the result of "thinking and thinking, and then — for no particular
reason — dying" [010-028]. The absurdity underscores the theme: the
town's entire administrative apparatus is so hollow, so dependent on
surface normalcy, that a single disruption proves fatal.

When the Prosecutor dies, the town briefly comes together for his
funeral, only to resume its petty intrigues the next day. [[chichikov|Chichikov]],
passing the procession, reflects that the dead man's only notable
feature was his eyebrows [011-015].
