# Fetinia

## Character Profile

Fetinia is the housekeeper at Korobochka's [[landownership|estate]], appearing in Chapter 3 during [[chichikov|Chichikov]]'s overnight stay. She is introduced when Korobochka orders her to prepare a bed: "Fetinia, bring a quilt and some pillows and sheets" [003-053]. Gogol's narrator offers an ironic aside about her professional competency: "Clearly Fetinia was a past mistress in the art of beating mattresses" [003-060], a domestic compliment delivered with the narrator's characteristic mock-heroic tone — elevating the mundane skill of fluffing bedding to the status of an art form.

Fetinia is efficient and obedient. When Korobochka addresses her — "Do you hear that, Fetinia?" — she responds "Very well, mistress" and spreads the sheets over the bed [003-057] [003-058]. She also takes [[chichikov|Chichikov]]'s garments — "upper and under" — for the night [003-060], a detail that conveys the intimate and unembarrassed domesticity of a provincial [[russian-empire|Russia]]n household where a guest's clothing is handled by the servants without ceremony.

On the morning of [[chichikovs-flight|Chichikov's departure]], Fetinia is dispatched with two further commissions: first to prepare pancakes — "I will go and tell Fetinia to try a few pancakes" [003-141] — and then to arrange the britchka for [[chichikov|Chichikov]]'s journey [003-152]. She thus bookends [[chichikov|Chichikov]]'s entire stay at Korobochka's, from bedding to breakfast to departure.

## Role in Dead Souls

Fetinia exemplifies Gogol's technique of characterising a household through its servants. Korobochka herself is fussy, suspicious, and provincial; Fetinia, by contrast, is competent and silent, executing her mistress's orders with professional dispatch. The contrast between the anxious [[landownership|landowner]] and her capable housekeeper subtly undermines the social hierarchy that the novel ostensibly records — the [[serfdom|serf]] is more practically useful than the owner.

Fetinia's repeated appearances across the chapter also serve a structural function, marking the passage of [[chichikov|Chichikov]]'s brief visit: arrival (bedding), morning (pancakes), departure (britchka). She is a narrative clock, her actions delineating the rhythm of provincial hospitality.
