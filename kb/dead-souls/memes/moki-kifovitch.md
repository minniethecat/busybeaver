# Moki Kifovitch

## Character Profile

Moki Kifovitch (also rendered as Mofi Kifovitch) is the son of the philosophical peasant [[kifa-mokievitch|Kifa Mokievitch]], introduced in a digression in Chapter 11 of [[dead-souls|Dead Souls]]. He is the destructive counterweight to his father's gentle, abstracted nature. Gogol describes him as "what we [[russian-empire|Russia]]ns call a 'hero'" [011-037] — the word used here with heavy irony, as his heroism consists of uncontrolled, violent energy rather than any noble quality.

At twenty years old, Moki Kifovitch possesses a "lusty, twenty-year-old temperament" that is "violently struggling for development" [011-037]. Yet this energy finds no constructive outlet: "that son could tackle nothing without some accident occurring" [011-037]. His typical exploits involve physical violence — "at one moment would he crack some one's fingers in half, and at another would he raise a bump on somebody's nose" [011-037]. The result is universal terror: "both at home and abroad every one and everything — from the serving-maid to the yard-dog — fled on his approach, and even the bed in his bedroom became shattered to splinters" [011-037].

Despite his violence, Gogol insists that Moki Kifovitch has "a kindly soul" [011-037] — the destruction is not born of malice but of an excess of energy with nowhere to go.

## Role in Dead Souls

Moki Kifovitch and his father appear in a self-contained digression that Gogol inserts immediately after revealing [[chichikov|Chichikov]]'s biography. The narrator uses the father-son pair to illustrate two incompatible tendencies in the [[russian-empire|Russia]]n character: the impulse toward abstract, unworldly speculation ([[kifa-mokievitch|Kifa]]) and the impulse toward anarchic, destructive energy (Moki).

The digression's placement is significant. It follows the full revelation of [[chichikov|Chichikov]]'s past and character, and it functions as a comic parable that both explains and evades explanation. When servants and neighbours complain to [[kifa-mokievitch|Kifa]] about his son's behaviour, asking "what are you going to do about your Moki Kifovitch? We get no rest from him, he is so above himself," [[kifa-mokievitch|Kifa]]'s response is pure evasion: "That is only his play, that is only his play" [011-037]. He cannot bring himself to discipline his son, rationalizing that public reproof would reflect badly on himself, that he is too busy with philosophy, and that "Moki Kifovitch is my son, and very dear to my heart" [011-037].

The parable ends without resolution. [[kifa-mokievitch|Kifa Mokievitch]] returns to his beloved question — "Suppose elephants were to take to being hatched from eggs, would not the shell of such eggs be of a thickness proof against cannonballs?" — while Moki Kifovitch continues his "heroic exploits" unchecked [011-037]. The father's speculative detachment and the son's violent chaos form a self-perpetuating system that Gogol presents as darkly, absurdly [[russian-empire|Russia]]n.
