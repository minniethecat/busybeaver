# Chichikov's Flight from Town

**[[chichikov|Chichikov]]'s flight from the [[town-of-n|town of N.]]** is the climactic event of Volume One of [[Dead Souls]]. After the collapse of his social standing — triggered by [[Korobotchka]]'s anxious inquiry about the market price of [[dead-souls|dead soul]]s and [[Nozdrev]]'s public denunciations — [[Chichikov, Paul Ivanovitch|Chichikov]] orders a hasty departure. The flight is rendered by Gogol as a masterpiece of thwarted urgency: [[selifan|Selifan]] procrastinates, the britchka is broken, the horses need shoeing, and every delay compounds the comic tension before the final escape. The event marks both the practical failure of the [[purchase-of-dead-souls|dead souls scheme]] and the structural close of the novel's first volume.

## Summary

The flight has its origins in the rumours that sweep the [[town-of-n|town of N.]] during Chapters 8–10. After [[chichikov|Chichikov]]'s [[purchase-of-dead-souls|dead souls purchase]]s are registered in Chapter 7, his social standing peaks — he is celebrated as a wealthy [[landownership|landowner]] and potential millionaire. But two events unravel everything.

First, [[Korobotchka]], the "cudgel-headed" widow from Chapter 3, arrives in town "three nights following his departure she had been unable to sleep" [008-036], tormented by the fear that she sold her [[dead-souls|dead soul]]s too cheaply. She visits [[father-cyril|the Archpriest]]'s wife to ask "the current price of [[dead-souls|dead soul]]s" [009-016–009-022]. The inquiry, absurd in itself, reaches the ears of the town's ladies, who transform it into increasingly baroque rumours [009-050].

Second, [[Nozdrev]] — who had nearly fought [[chichikov|Chichikov]] in Chapter 4 — publicly confronts him at a ball, loudly asking why he has been "purchasing [[dead-souls|dead soul]]s" [008-*]. Combined with other rumours (that [[chichikov|Chichikov]] is a forger, a kidnapper of [[governors-daughter|the Governor's daughter]], even Napoleon in disguise), the social climate becomes untenable.

The decision to flee crystallises in Chapter 10. [[chichikov|Chichikov]], realising that his position is lost, returns to his inn and orders immediate preparations:

> "Everything went into the receptacle just as it came to hand, since his one object was to obviate any possible delay in the morning's departure. Meanwhile the reluctant Selifan slowly, very slowly, left the room, as slowly descended the staircase (on each separate step of which he left a muddy foot-print), and, finally, halted to scratch his head." [010-035]

The scrat[[table-of-ranks|chin]]g of [[selifan|Selifan]]'s head is a classic Gogolian detail — the narrator pauses to observe that "with the [[russian-empire|Russia]]n populace, such a scrat[[table-of-ranks|chin]]g may mean any one of a hundred things" [010-035], before the action resumes.

The morning of departure (Chapter 11) is a cascade of frustrations. [[chichikov|Chichikov]] oversleeps — "check number one" [011-001]. He discovers that "neither of those two things had been done" — the britchka is not harnessed, nothing is ready — "check number two" [011-001]. [[selifan|Selifan]] appears with a litany of excuses:

> "'Paul Ivanovitch,' he said, 'the horses require shoeing.'
> 'Blockhead!' exclaimed Chichikov. 'Why did you not tell me of that before, you damned fool? Was there not time enough for them to be shod?'
> 'Yes, I suppose there was,' agreed Selifan. 'Also one of the wheels is in want of a new tyre, for the roads are so rough that the old tyre is worn through. Also, the body of the britchka is so rickety that probably it will not last more than a couple of stages.'" [011-001]

[[chichikov|Chichikov]] rages. [[selifan|Selifan]] dodges. The blacksmiths must be summoned; the wheel must be repaired. The delays stretch interminably. The comedy is rooted in the mismatch between [[chichikov|Chichikov]]'s desperate urgency and the inertia of the [[russian-empire|Russia]]n provincial world — horses, servants, roads, and weather all conspire against haste.

At last, the britchka is ready. As [[chichikov|Chichikov]] departs, the narrative shifts into one of Gogol's most famous lyrical digressions — the "troika" passage, in which [[russian-empire|Russia]] itself is imagined as a speeding carriage:

> The narrator invokes the image of the *troika* (three-horse carriage) hurtling across the Russian landscape, asking "Whither art thou speeding, O Russia?" The passage transforms Chichikov's ignominious flight into a meditation on national destiny, fusing satire with the sublime.

The flight ends not with capture or punishment but with [[chichikov|Chichikov]] simply... gone. He escapes without formal consequence, his scheme in ruins, his britchka disappearing into the distance: "But no longer was he the old [[chichikov|Chichikov]]" [015-313]. The novel's first volume closes on this note of ambiguous resolution.

## Significance

[[chichikov|Chichikov]]'s flight is the structural counterweight to his arrival in Chapter 1. The novel begins with a mysterious stranger entering the town; it ends with the same stranger fleeing it. This circular structure — arrival, social ascent, exposure, flight — mirrors Gogol's larger themes: the impermanence of reputation, the fragility of schemes built on legal fictions, and the inexorable power of *poshlost* (banality) to undo ambition.

The flight also demonstrates Gogol's distinctive tonal range. In the space of a single chapter, the narrative moves from farce ([[selifan|Selifan]]'s head-scrat[[table-of-ranks|chin]]g) to rage ([[chichikov|Chichikov]]'s outburst) to the sublime (the troika digression). This tonal instability is characteristic of Gogol's method — the refusal to settle into a single register that makes *[[dead-souls|Dead Souls]]* so resistant to straightforward interpretation.

## Context

**Before**: The [[purchase-of-dead-souls]] scheme was executed across Chapters 2–6 and legally registered in Chapter 7. [[chichikov|Chichikov]]'s social standing reached its zenith at the [[governors-ball|Governor's ball]] (Chapter 8) before collapsing under the weight of rumour (Chapters 9–10).

**After**: [[chichikov|Chichikov]] escapes without formal punishment, but his scheme in the [[town-of-n|town of N.]] is permanently destroyed. The novel's surviving fragment of Volume Two begins with [[chichikov|Chichikov]] elsewhere, attempting a modified version of his scheme. Gogol later burned the manuscript of Volume Two.
