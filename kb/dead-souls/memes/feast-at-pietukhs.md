# The Feast at Pietukh's

## Summary

While travelling through the countryside in Part II in search of new opportunities, [[chichikov|Chichikov]] and his companion [[platonov|Platon]] are detained at the [[landownership|estate]] of [[peter-petrovitch-pietukh|Peter Petrovitch Pietukh]], a [[landownership|landowner]] whose hospitality is as boundless as his appetite [014-031]. [[peter-petrovitch-pietukh|Pietukh]] insists the guests remain for dinner, and the meal that follows is a monument to [[russian-empire|Russia]]n gastronomic excess. Course after course arrives, and [[peter-petrovitch-pietukh|Pietukh]] presses food upon his guests with a series of proverbial justifications: "Without a mate, neither man nor bird can live in this world" (for a second piece), "God loves a trinity" (for a third), and the punning "Na piat opiat" for a fifth [014-074]. [[chichikov|Chichikov]] consumes at least twelve steaks of sturgeon, followed by spit-roasted veal from a calf [[peter-petrovitch-pietukh|Pietukh]] claims to have "cared for like my own son" [014-075]. After the meal, [[peter-petrovitch-pietukh|Pietukh]] falls asleep and "converts himself into a sort of blacksmith's bellows" with snoring of surpassing volume [014-082]. To prevent their departure, [[peter-petrovitch-pietukh|Pietukh]] has the wheels removed from [[chichikov|Chichikov]]'s carriage, forcing the guests to stay for a boating expedition and a supper that proves equally overwhelming [014-104]. That night, through the thin wall, [[chichikov|Chichikov]] is kept awake by [[peter-petrovitch-pietukh|Pietukh]] dictating the next morning's breakfast menu to his cook in voluptuous detail [014-108].

## Participants

- [[chichikov-paul-ivanovitch]] — the guest who endures the feast and dreams of one day owning such an [[landownership|estate]] [014-106]
- [[peter-petrovitch-pietukh|Peter Petrovitch Pietukh]] — the host, a [[landownership|landowner]] of prodigious hospitality and appetite [014-031]
- [[platonov|Platon]] — [[chichikov|Chichikov]]'s travelling companion, who finds the excess tiresome [014-085]
- Aleksasha and [[nikolasha|Nikolasha]] — [[peter-petrovitch-pietukh|Pietukh]]'s sons, who are plied with wine and dream of Moscow theatres [014-106]
- Emelian ("the gawk") and Antoshka ("the thief") — [[peter-petrovitch-pietukh|Pietukh]]'s servants, so nicknamed to spur their activity [014-073]

## Significance

The Feast at Pietukh's is the novel's most concentrated comic treatment of the theme of material excess and [[hospitality-customs|Russian hospitality]] pushed to absurdity. Gogol renders the meal with an almost Rabelaisian gusto, using food as both a vehicle for characterisation and a symbol of a certain mode of [[russian-empire|Russia]]n country existence — one in which eating, sleeping, and planning the next meal constitute the entire rhythm of life. [[peter-petrovitch-pietukh|Pietukh]]'s hospitality is genuinely warm but also coercive: he removes the wheels from his guests' carriage to prevent their departure [014-104], embodying a peculiarly [[russian-empire|Russia]]n fusion of generosity and tyranny. The episode also provides structural contrast to the dead-[[dead-souls|souls]] transactions of Part I: whereas those scenes were driven by [[chichikov|Chichikov]]'s acquisitive cunning, here he is reduced to a passive recipient of someone else's overwhelming will, offering a comic counterpoint to his usual role as the manipulator.

## Context

The feast occurs during [[chichikov|Chichikov]]'s travels in Part II, after his flight from town N. in Part I. [[peter-petrovitch-pietukh|Pietukh]] is one of the new [[landownership|landowners]] [[chichikov|Chichikov]] encounters in this second phase of his journey. The episode follows [[chichikov|Chichikov]]'s meeting with [[platonov|Platon]] and precedes their visit to [[platonov|Platon]]'s brother-in-law, the leading [[landownership|landowner]] of the district [014-119]. As in Part I, [[chichikov|Chichikov]]'s encounters with [[landownership|landowners]] serve as a structural device for Gogol's portrait of [[russian-empire|Russia]]n provincial types, with [[peter-petrovitch-pietukh|Pietukh]] representing the type of the hospitable glutton.
