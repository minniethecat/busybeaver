# Peter Petrovitch Pietukh

## Character Profile

Peter Petrovitch Pietukh — whose surname means "Rooster" in [[russian-empire|Russia]]n — is a Part II [[landownership|landowner]] whose entire existence revolves around food and hospitality. [[chichikov|Chichikov]] arrives at his [[landownership|estate]] by accident, having told [[selifan|Selifan]] to drive to [[koshkarev|Colonel Koshkarev]]'s house, and is met by a host who immediately sweeps aside all apologies: "But it is not a mistake. When you have tried the dinner which I have in store for you, just see whether you think IT a mistake" [014-045].

Pietukh is a man of enormous physical appetites who has elevated eating to a philosophy and a sport. At the dinner table he employs a system of numerically-coded encouragement: if a guest takes one piece, he urges a second saying "God loves a trinity"; if three, he asks "Where do you see a waggon with three wheels?" [014-077]. After [[chichikov|Chichikov]] consumes at least twelve steaks of sturgeon and declares himself full, Pietukh silently heaps an enormous portion of spit-roasted veal upon his plate — veal from a calf "fed two years on milk" and "cared for like my own son" [014-077].

His [[landownership|estate]] is entirely mortgaged, a fact he announces with the same cheerful unconcern with which he serves sturgeon cheeks: "No, not at all. In fact, they tell me that it is a good thing to do, and that every one else is doing it" [014-043]. He has two sons, [[nikolasha|Nikolasha]] and Aleksasha, home from the provincial gymnasium and already dreaming of the confectioners' shops and theatres of St. Petersburg — a preference that [[chichikov|Chichikov]] privately diagnoses as the first step toward ruin [014-038].

## Role in Dead Souls

Pietukh represents a distinct type of [[russian-empire|Russia]]n [[landownership|landowner]]: not the miser ([[plushkin|Plushkin]]), the brawler ([[nozdrev|Nozdrev]]), or the sentimentalist ([[manilov|Manilov]]), but the compulsive host whose generosity is a form of self-destruction. "With a scale of living like this, he would be ruined in three years," [[chichikov|Chichikov]] reflects — yet notes that "hospitality can dissipate a fortune in three months as easily as it can in three years" [014-105].

His most revealing act is to secretly remove the wheels from [[chichikov|Chichikov]]'s koliaska to prevent his guest from leaving — a gesture that captures the coercive quality of his hospitality. Guests are not invited to stay; they are imprisoned by kindness and force-fed into submission [014-105]. The following morning, [[chichikov|Chichikov]] overhears Pietukh through the thin wall, ordering an early breakfast of such elaborate proportions — sturgeon cheeks, calves' brains, pasty in four divisions — that it "would have excited the appetite of a corpse" [014-112].

Yet Gogol does not present Pietukh as purely comic. The boat trip he organizes for his captive guests, with twelve rowers singing across the river at dusk, produces one of Part II's few moments of genuine beauty, and even [[chichikov|Chichikov]] "felt acutely conscious of the fact that he was a [[russian-empire|Russia]]n" [014-105]. Pietukh's world, for all its folly, contains a vitality that the more calculating characters lack.

## Appearances

- **Part II, Chapter 3**: Pietukh intercepts [[chichikov|Chichikov]], who has been mistakenly driven to his [[landownership|estate]] instead of [[koshkarev|Koshkarev]]'s [014-045]. He hosts an enormous dinner featuring sturgeon and veal [014-077], removes the wheels from [[chichikov|Chichikov]]'s carriage to force an overnight stay, and organizes a boat trip on the river [014-105]. Late at night [[chichikov|Chichikov]] overhears him ordering an even more elaborate breakfast [014-112].
