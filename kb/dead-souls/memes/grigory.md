# Grigory

## Character Profile

Grigory is the **butler** at [[tientietnikov|Andrei Ivanovitch Tientietnikov]]'s country [[landownership|estate]], described as "a [[serfdom|serf]] named Grigory (who purported to act as butler)" — the qualifying phrase suggesting a certain pretension or self-appointment to the role （012-008）. He appears in the novel's Part II, where he serves as part of the domestic backdrop to [[tientietnikov|Tientietnikov]]'s life of idleness.

Grigory's most distinctive trait is his **quarrelsome relationship with the housekeeper**, [[perfilievna|Perfilievna]]. Their daily altercations form a regular spectacle in the courtyard, observed by [[tientietnikov|Tientietnikov]] from his window as he drinks his morning tea. In one exchange, Grigory calls [[perfilievna|Perfilievna]] "a nuisance" and a "good-for-nothing," telling her to "hold your stupid tongue" （012-009）. She retorts that he is "as big a thief" as the bailiff, and warns that the master can hear everything from his window （012-012）. These petty squabbles reveal the slack discipline and low-level disorder that characterizes [[tientietnikov|Tientietnikov]]'s neglected household — a domestic chaos that mirrors the master's own spiritual stagnation.

Grigory later becomes **friends with [[chichikov|Chichikov]]'s valet [[petrushka|Petrushka]]**, though the relationship begins competitively. "At first the pair showed a tendency to outbrag one another," with [[petrushka|Petrushka]] boasting of his travels and Grigory countering with references to St. Petersburg — a city [[petrushka|Petrushka]] had never visited — and claiming knowledge of a town "not to be found on any map in existence" and thirty thousand versts distant, a claim that would "completely flabbergast" [[petrushka|Petrushka]] （012-065）. Eventually, however, "the pair ended by swearing eternal friendship with one another, and making a practice of resorting to the village tavern in company" （012-065）.

## Role in Dead Souls

Grigory is a **minor domestic figure** who serves primarily as local color. His bickering with [[perfilievna|Perfilievna]] illustrates the atmosphere of aimless discord on [[tientietnikov|Tientietnikov]]'s [[landownership|estate]] — a place where even the servants have nothing better to do than trade insults across the courtyard. The fact that the master merely watches these daily scenes without intervening underscores [[tientietnikov|Tientietnikov]]'s complete withdrawal from the management of his own household.

Grigory's friendship with [[petrushka|Petrushka]] serves a structural purpose: it shows how [[chichikov|Chichikov]]'s retinue integrates into [[tientietnikov|Tientietnikov]]'s domestic world, paralleling [[chichikov|Chichikov]]'s own smooth accommodation to his host's habits. The competitive bragging between the two servants is a comic, lower-class echo of the social posturing that Gogol satirizes throughout the novel.
