# Vishnepokromov, Varvar Nikolaievitch

## Character Profile

Varvar Nikolaievitch Vishnepokromov is a minor figure in Part II of [[dead-souls|Dead Souls]], appearing only through the recollections and conversations of other characters. He is a member of the provincial gentry who "called to indulge in a free-and-easy symposium on politics, philosophy, literature, and finance" at [[tientietnikov|Tientietnikov's]] [[landownership|estate]] [012-041]. His full name — Varvar Nikolaievitch — marks him as a member of the educated gentry class, the sort of man who participates in the intellectual discussions that [[tientietnikov|Tientietnikov]] eventually finds "superficial" and abandons.

Though Vishnepokromov himself never appears directly in the novel's action, his name surfaces in a revealing dispute between [[koshkarev|Colonel Koshkarev]] and another character in Chapter 13. The argument concerns whether Vishnepokromov's word can be trusted: "What happened was that you took Vishnepokromov's word — the word of a man who..." [013-033]. The sentence breaks off suggestively, implying that relying on Vishnepokromov's judgment leads to trouble. A "heated dispute on the subject of Vishnepokromov" [013-034] erupts, indicating that his unreliability is a matter of some notoriety among his acquaintances.

## Role in Dead Souls

Vishnepokromov embodies a type that Gogol satirizes throughout [[dead-souls|Dead Souls]]: the gentleman of opinions whose talk is plentiful but whose substance is thin. He belongs to the circle of "students of pronounced, but immature, opinions who culled the bulk of their wisdom from contemporary newspapers and pamphlets" [012-041] — the very company that [[tientietnikov|Tientietnikov]] comes to regard as tedious. His function in the narrative is twofold: he contributes to the portrait of provincial intellectual life as shallow and derivative, and his disputed reliability in Chapter 13 provides a minor narrative friction that illuminates the trustworthiness (or lack thereof) among the gentry.

His name — Vishnepokromov — is characteristically Gogolian, likely a compound suggesting something concealed or covered over, though the precise etymology is less important than the comic effect of the long, elaborate [[russian-empire|Russia]]n name attached to a man of evidently little substance.
