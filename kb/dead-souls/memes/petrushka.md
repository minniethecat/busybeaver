# Petrushka

## Character Profile

Petrushka is [[chichikov|Chichikov]]'s valet — a man of about thirty with prominent
nose and lips, whose most memorable trait is the peculiar, lived-in
smell that follows him everywhere [001-002]. Gogol describes him as
silent and sullen, performing his duties with a kind of mechanical
indifference.

He has two ruling passions: reading and sleeping. His reading, however,
is entirely indiscriminate — he reads not for content but for the mere
act of processing letters into words, finding equal satisfaction in a
prayer book, a chemistry text, or a romance novel [011-030].

Petrushka's personal odor becomes a minor comic motif throughout the
novel. Even in the most transient lodgings, his presence transforms the
atmosphere — the rooms "became permeated with Petrushka's own peculiar
smell" [012-058]. This detail serves as Gogol's earthy reminder that
behind every gentleman's scheme stands a human being with all the
attendant physical realities.

## Role in Dead Souls

Petrushka represents **the invisible labor** of servitude — always
present, rarely noticed, essential to the functioning of [[chichikov|Chichikov]]'s
world. He lays out the clothes, polishes the boots, plugs his nostrils
with clothespins before attending to his master (a habit [[chichikov|Chichikov]]
adopted from him), and follows orders without question.

His indiscriminate reading is Gogol's satire of **unthinking
consumption**: Petrushka "liked not what he read, but the reading
itself — the fact that out of letters a word was eternally forming"
[011-030]. In this he parallels the provincial society of the town of
N., which consumes [[provincial-scandal|rumor]] and [[provincial-scandal|gossip]] with the same undiscriminating
appetite.

Gogol seems to ask: is the gentry's conversation at the [[governor|Governor]]'s
ball really any more meaningful than Petrushka's letter-by-letter
absorption?
