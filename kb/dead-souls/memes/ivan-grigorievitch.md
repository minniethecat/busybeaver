# Ivan Grigorievitch

## Character Profile

Ivan Grigorievitch is the President of the Civil Court in the [[town-of-n|town of N.]],
the senior judge before whom all property transactions must be
registered. He is introduced indirectly: when [[chichikov|Chichikov]] encounters
resistance from the clerk [[ivan-antonovitch|Ivan Antonovitch]], he invokes the President's
name as an "intimate friend" to expedite the process [007-024].

The President proves genuinely affable. He receives [[chichikov|Chichikov]] warmly,
kisses him on both cheeks, and treats the whole business of registering
dubious [[serfdom|serf]] purchases as a social occasion rather than a legal
proceeding. When [[chichikov|Chichikov]] presents [[plushkin|Plushkin]]'s letter of attorney, the
President reads it with visible pleasure — less because he cares about
its legal validity than because the name of a fellow [[landownership|landowner]] provides
an excuse for conversation [007-031].

His physical manner is expansive. He embraces [[sobakevich|Sobakevich]] as an old
comrade, and [[sobakevich|Sobakevich]], for his part, speaks to the President with
an ease that suggests years of mutual accommodation. The President
exclaims at [[sobakevich|Sobakevich]]'s apparent good health, prompting the [[landownership|landowner]]
to deliver one of his characteristic grumbles about the inevitability
of having to "pay for it" sooner or later [007-032].

## Role in Dead Souls

Ivan Grigorievitch represents the **genial face of systemic [[bribery|corruption]]**.
Unlike his subordinate [[ivan-antonovitch|Ivan Antonovitch]], whose [[bribery|bribe-taking]] is wordless
and mechanical, the President processes [[chichikov|Chichikov]]'s transactions with
every appearance of lawful good cheer. He asks no awkward questions
about why [[chichikov|Chichikov]] is buying [[dead-souls|dead serfs]], nor why the sellers are so
eager to part with them. The very speed with which he moves the deeds
through registration — "we need only to wet the bargain" — is itself
a form of complicity [007-095].

The climactic scene in his chambers culminates in a champagne toast.
The President proposes [[chichikov|Chichikov]]'s health, the assembled [[officialdom|officials]]
cheer, and the fraudulent transaction is sealed not with a stamp but
with a bottle. Gogol's irony is precise: the law's highest local
representative consecrates the central fraud of the novel, and does so
with genuine warmth.
