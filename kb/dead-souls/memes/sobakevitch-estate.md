# Sobakevitch's Estate

## Description

Sobakevitch's [[landownership|estate]] announces itself through mass and durability. The country
house, of "very fair size," stands flanked by birch and pine forests in two
shades of green — a wooden edifice with dark-grey walls and a red-gabled roof,
built in the manner of dwellings constructed for military settlers and German
colonists [005-011].

A peculiar detail immediately strikes the visitor: the architect and the
proprietor have quarrelled. The architect, a pedant, desired symmetry; the
proprietor wanted comfort. Consequently, windows have been dispensed with on one
entire side of the mansion, replaced by a single small aperture intended to
light a dark lumber-room. The pediment, too, has been knocked off-centre — one
of its four original columns has been removed by the proprietor [005-011].
Durability, not beauty, has governed every decision.

The courtyard is enclosed by a strong, very high wooden fence. Stables,
coach-house, and kitchen premises are constructed of beams "warranted to last
for centuries." Even the peasants' wooden huts are remarkably solid: not a clay
wall or a carved pattern anywhere. The draw-well is fashioned of oakwood usually
reserved for mills or ships. "In short," Gogol writes, "wherever [[chichikov|Chichikov]]'s eye
turned he saw nothing that was not free from shoddy make and well and skilfully
arranged" [005-011].

The interior continues the theme. The drawing-room walls display portraits of
Greek generals with solid thighs and prodigious moustaches — Movrogordato,
Kanaris — alongside Bagration and the Greek heroine Bobelina, whose legs
"looked larger than do the whole bodies of the drawing-room dandies of the
present day" [005-012]. A blackbird in a cage by the window resembles
Sobakevitch himself. The furniture matches: a hazelwood bureau with a bulging
body on four grotesque legs — "the perfect image of a bear" — tables and
chairs of the same ponderous, unrestful order. Every article in the room seems
to say, "I, too, am a Sobakevitch" or "I am exactly like Sobakevitch" [005-013].

## Role in Dead Souls

Sobakevitch's [[landownership|estate]] is the fourth stop on [[chichikov|Chichikov]]'s circuit, and it
represents the polar opposite of [[manilovka|Manilovka]]. Where [[manilovka|Manilov's estate]] is
sentimental and incomplete, Sobakevitch's is brutally practical and finished
to the last beam. The [[landownership|estate]]'s obsessive solidity mirrors its owner's character:
a man who speaks ill of every [[officialdom|official]] in town yet bargains with cold,
calculating precision over the price of [[dead-souls|dead soul]]s [005-014 – 005-030].

The [[dead-souls|dead soul]]s negotiation at Sobakevitch's [[landownership|estate]] is the novel's most
businesslike transaction. Unlike the dreamy incomprehension of [[manilov|Manilov]], the
superstitious anxiety of Korobochka, or the violent chaos of [[nozdrev|Nozdrev]],
Sobakevitch treats the sale of deceased [[serfdom|serfs]] as a straightforward commercial
opportunity — naming a price of one hundred roubles per soul, haggling down to
two and a half roubles, and even slipping a female [[serfdom|serf]] (Elizaveta Vorobei) into
the list [005-025]. The [[landownership|estate]]'s architecture — a pediment knocked off-centre,
a window removed for comfort — prefigures this moral asymmetry: everything is
structurally sound, nothing is quite right.
