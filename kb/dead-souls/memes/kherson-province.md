# Kherson Province

**Kherson Province** is a recurring narrative device in Gogol's *Dead Souls* — the fictional destination [[Chichikov, Paul Ivanovitch|Chichikov]] declares for his purchased serfs after the [[civil-court-registration|registration]] at the [[civil-court|Civil Court]]. It functions as a satirical marker of the protagonist's self-delusion and the town's capacity for collective fantasy.

## Role in the Novel

When asked where he intends to settle the [[dead-souls|serfs]] he has acquired, [[Chichikov, Paul Ivanovitch|Chichikov]] answers simply: "To the province of Kherson" [007-095]. The [[president-of-council|President of the Local Council]] responds enthusiastically, expatiating "on the fertility of the Kherson pastures" [007-095], and the assembled officials toast "the health of the new landowner of Kherson" [007-100].

The joke gains momentum as [[Chichikov, Paul Ivanovitch|Chichikov]] himself begins to believe in the fantasy. Inflated by the wine and flattery at the registration banquet, he finds himself "imagining himself really and truly to be a landowner of Kherson" and babbling about "villages of his in Kherson" and crop yields — even to the point of making advances to the [[governors-daughter|Governor's daughter]] [007-110].

## Satirical Function

The Kherson joke is revived in Chapter 8 when [[nozdrev|Nozdrev]] mockingly hails [[Chichikov, Paul Ivanovitch|Chichikov]] at the [[governors-ball|Governor's Ball]]:

> "Ah, my fine landowner of Kherson! Have you been doing much trade in departed souls lately?" [008-025]

This public exposure sets the scandal in motion. In Chapter 11, [[Chichikov, Paul Ivanovitch|Chichikov]]'s backstory reveals the full scope of the scheme: he plans to acquire land "in the provinces of Taurida and Kherson almost for nothing," transfer the purchased serfs there, and have the settlement "called Chichikovoe — better still Pavlovskoe" — after his own patronymic [011-277].
