# Ulinka

## Character Profile

Ulinka is the daughter of [[general-betristchev|General Betristchev]], introduced in Part II of the novel as a young woman of striking vitality and contradictory temperament. Gogol's narrator describes her with unusual warmth: "full of life as life itself" [012-045]. Her upbringing is singular — she lost her mother in early childhood and was raised by an English governess who "knew not a single word of [[russian-empire|Russia]]n," while her father, though "excessively fond of her, treated her always as a toy" [012-045]. The result is a character who is at once "wholly wayward and spoilt" and possessed of a fierce moral instinct.

The narrator notes a telling detail about her anger: "that rage gathered only when she had heard of injustice or harsh treatment, and never because she desired to argue on her own behalf" [012-045]. This distinguishes her from the merely capricious — her temper arises from ethical indignation rather than personal pique, making her one of the few genuinely sympathetic female characters in the novel.

When [[chichikov|Chichikov]] is introduced to her, she immediately demonstrates sharp judgment. Hearing her father repeat [[vishnepokromov|Vishnepokromov]]'s slander of [[tientietnikov|Tientietnikov]], she retorts: "But who ever supposed him to be a fool? What happened was that you took [[vishnepokromov|Vishnepokromov]]'s word — the word of a man who is himself both a fool and a good-for-nothing" [013-032]. Her quickness to defend the absent [[tientietnikov|Tientietnikov]] hints at an underlying attachment.

## Role in Dead Souls

Ulinka appears in Part II as a potential romantic interest — first for [[tientietnikov|Tientietnikov]], the indolent young [[landownership|landowner]], and later as a possible match dangled before [[chichikov|Chichikov]] by the General. In a novel otherwise populated by grotesques, sentimentalists, and frauds, Ulinka represents a fleeting ideal of youthful integrity. Her "strange" upbringing has paradoxically produced someone less conventional, more direct, and more alive than the society around her.

Within the fragmentary architecture of Part II — which Gogol left incomplete and later burned — Ulinka's narrative arc remains a sketch. She is introduced, described, and placed in proximity to two male protagonists, but her story never fully unfolds. As such she embodies the larger incompleteness of Part II itself: a character of evident promise whose development was never realised.
