# Captain Kopeikin

## Character Profile

Captain Kopeikin is a **disabled war veteran** of the 1812 campaign against Napoleon. He is described as "a headstrong, lively blade who, whether on duty or under arrest, made things lively for everybody" （010-007）. After losing an arm and a leg at either Krasni or Leipzig, he found himself destitute — in those days no provision was made for wounded soldiers, and he could not work with his left arm alone （010-007）.

Physically, Kopeikin is defined by his war injuries: a missing arm (his right sleeve was pinned to his uniform) and a wooden leg （010-007）. His appearance marks him unmistakably as a veteran. Despite — or perhaps because of — his disabilities, he possesses immense determination. When the St. Petersburg [[officialdom|bureaucracy]] stalls his pension claim, he does not quietly accept his fate but instead raises a "turmoil which sent the staff flying" and gives the secretaries and clerks "a real good hammering" （010-007）.

## Role in Dead Souls

Kopeikin appears only through the **[[postmaster|Postmaster]]'s tale**, not as a character in the novel's primary action. In Chapter 10, when the town [[officialdom|officials]] are frantically trying to determine [[chichikov|Chichikov]]'s identity, the [[postmaster|Postmaster]] suddenly announces: "He is none other than Captain Kopeikin" （010-004）. Asked who Kopeikin is, the [[postmaster|Postmaster]] launches into an extended digression — the "Tale of Captain Kopeikin" — which is one of the most famous set-pieces in [[dead-souls|Dead Souls]].

The tale serves three narrative functions. First, it is a **satirical critique of bureaucratic indifference**: Kopeikin, having "shed his blood" and "sacrificed his life" for his country, is met with endless delays, referrals to commissions, and ultimately told to "make his own resources" （010-007）. Second, it is a **folk-hero legend**: after being expelled from St. Petersburg, Kopeikin vanishes, and "not two months later there appeared in the forests of Riazan a band of robbers; and of that band the chieftain was none other than—" — at which point the [[postmaster|Postmaster]] is interrupted （010-007）. The implication that the wronged veteran became a brigand chief is left hanging. Third, the tale functions as **comic misdirection**: the Head of Police immediately punctures the [[postmaster|Postmaster]]'s theory by pointing out that Kopeikin had lost an arm and a leg, "whereas [[chichikov|Chichikov]]—" has all his limbs intact （010-008）. The [[postmaster|Postmaster]] "clapped his hand to his forehead, and publicly called himself a fool" （010-009）.

Through Kopeikin, Gogol exposes the **gap between patriotic rhetoric and the state's treatment of those who sacrificed for it**. The veteran who "risked his life for his country" is reduced to begging, then to crime — a trajectory the [[officialdom|bureaucracy]] itself creates through its callousness.

Though Kopeikin himself never appears in the novel's main plot, his story occupies a substantial portion of Chapter 10 — one of the longest digressions in the book — and is referenced by name at the critical moment when the town's speculation about [[chichikov|Chichikov]] reaches its peak.

## Critical Reception

The "Tale of Captain Kopeikin" is one of the most analyzed episodes in [[dead-souls|Dead Souls]]. Gogol himself fought extensively with the censors over this passage: the original version explicitly named the brigand chief as Kopeikin, while the censored version left the conclusion implicit. The tale has been read as a microcosm of the novel's larger critique of [[russian-empire|Russia]]n [[officialdom|official]]dom — the dehumanizing ma[[table-of-ranks|chin]]ery that processes human need into bureaucratic procedure.
