# Plushkin's Estate

**[[plushkin|Plushkin]]'s [[landownership|estate]]** is the country property of [[Plushkin]], the fifth and final [[landownership|landowner]] visited by [[Chichikov, Paul Ivanovitch|Chichikov]] in [[Dead Souls]]. It is the most vividly described of all the [[landownership|estate]]s in Gogol's novel — a landscape of such comprehensive decay that it becomes a moral allegory in wood, stone, and dust. Where [[Manilovka]] reflects its owner's vague sentimentality and [[Sobakevitch]]'s [[landownership|estate]] his bear-like solidity, [[plushkin|Plushkin]]'s property is the spatial expression of avarice consuming itself: storehouses crammed with produce rot unused, [[serfdom|serfs]] live in collapsing huts, and the manor house itself resembles a mausoleum of hoarded junk.

## Description

[[chichikov|Chichikov]]'s approach to the [[landownership|estate]] is heralded by a "violent jolt" as the britchka strikes "wooden pavements of a kind compared with which the cobblestones of the town had been as nothing" [006-001]. The planks "kept rising and falling" like piano keys, "and unguarded passage over them entailed either a bump on the back of the neck or a bruise on the forehead or a bite on the tip of one's tongue" [006-001]. This is the physical correlative of [[plushkin|Plushkin]]'s neglect: even the road fights the visitor.

The village reveals the full scale of the decay:

> "The beams of the huts had grown dark with age, many of their roofs were riddled with holes, others had but a tile of the roof remaining, and yet others were reduced to the rib-like framework of the same. It would seem as though the inhabitants themselves had removed the laths and traverses, on the very natural plea that the huts were no protection against the rain." [006-001]

The manor house itself is first mistaken by [[chichikov|Chichikov]] for something else entirely. Entering through "a large, dark hall which reeked like a tomb," he passes into a parlour whose condition "struck him almost with amazement":

> "It would seem that the floor was never washed, and that the room was used as a receptacle for every conceivable kind of furniture. On a table stood a ragged chair, with, beside it, a clock minus a pendulum and covered all over with cobwebs. Against a wall leant a cupboard, full of old silver, glassware, and china." [006-012]

The inventory of the room is one of Gogol's great set-pieces — a catalogue of ruin that accumulates to surreal effect:

> "On a writing table, inlaid with mother-of-pearl which, in places, had broken away and left behind it a number of yellow grooves (stuffed with putty), lay a pile of finely written manuscript, an overturned marble press (turning green), an ancient book in a leather cover with red edges, a lemon dried and shrunken to the dimensions of a hazelnut, the broken arm of a chair, a tumbler containing the dregs of some liquid and three flies (the whole covered over with a sheet of notepaper), a pile of rags, two ink-encrusted pens, and a yellow toothpick with which the master of the house had picked his teeth (apparently) at least before the coming of the French to Moscow." [006-012]

The walls display a similarly grotesque collection: a long engraving of a battle scene "lacking a glass," a "huge, grimy oil painting representative of some flowers and fruit, half a water melon, a boar's head, and the pendent form of a dead wild duck" [006-012]. From the ceiling hangs a chandelier in a holland covering "so dusty as closely to resemble a huge cocoon enclosing a caterpillar" [006-012].

The crescendo of the description is the pile of articles in one corner:

> "Yet what the pile consisted of it would have been difficult to say, seeing that the dust on the same was so thick that any hand which touched it would have at once resembled a glove. Prominently protruding from the pile was the shaft of a wooden spade and the antiquated sole of a shoe. Never would one have supposed that a living creature had tenanted the room, were it not that the presence of such a creature was betrayed by the spectacle of an old nightcap resting on the table." [006-012]

## Role in Dead Souls

The [[landownership|estate]] is the stage for [[chichikov|Chichikov]]'s most profitable transaction. [[plushkin|Plushkin]] owns not only a large number of recently deceased [[serfdom|serfs]] but also a list of runaway [[serfdom|serfs]] — seventy-eight [[dead-souls|souls]] sold at thirty kopecks each [006-081, 006-083]. The total exceeds two hundred [[dead-souls|souls]], making this visit the high-water mark of [[purchase-of-dead-souls|Chichikov's scheme]] [006-089].

But the [[landownership|estate]]'s deeper function is **allegorical**. The contradiction at its heart — "storehouses... that were crammed with homespun cloth and linen, tanned and undressed sheepskins, dried fish, and every conceivable species of produce" [006-012] alongside absolute squalor — is the contradiction of avarice itself. [[plushkin|Plushkin]] hoards, and hoarding destroys. The produce rots; the [[serfdom|serfs]] die or flee; the buildings collapse; the owner himself becomes indistinguishable from a beggar. The [[landownership|estate]] is a memento mori rendered in domestic architecture.

The moment of the deal completed, [[plushkin|Plushkin]] leads [[chichikov|Chichikov]] out, and immediately orders the gates closed and makes his rounds to check the watchmen [006-095]. The [[landownership|estate]] seals itself back into its decay, [[chichikov|Chichikov]] bowling away in "high spirit" at his windfall [006-095].
