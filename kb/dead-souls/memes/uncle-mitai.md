# Uncle Mitai

## Character Profile

Uncle Mitai is a peasant who appears in a brief comic episode in Chapter 5, when [[chichikov|Chichikov]]'s carriage becomes stuck. He is one of several local peasants who gather to help free the immobilized horses. Gogol describes him as "the lean, long, and red-bearded Uncle Mitai" who "mounted the shaft horse; in which position he looked like a village steeple or the winder which is used to raise water from wells" [005-007]. The simile — comparing a man to a village steeple — emphasizes his gangling height and the absurdity of his position astride the horse.

Despite the peasants' enthusiastic efforts, Uncle Mitai proves ineffectual. The horses remain stuck. The peasants cry out: "Hold on, hold on! Do you, Uncle Mitai, mount the trace horse, while [[uncle-minai|Uncle Minai]] mounts the shaft horse" [005-007]. Mitai shifts positions, but nothing improves. Eventually the coachman "sent the two Uncles about their business — and not before it was time, seeing that the horses were steaming" [005-007]. The comic anticlimax — much effort producing no result — is typical of Gogol's treatment of peasant scenes, where collective energy is squandered on misdirected labor.

## Role in Dead Souls

Together with his companion **[[uncle-minai|Uncle Minai]]** — "a peasant with a pair of broad shoulders, a beard as black as charcoal, and a belly like the huge samovar" [005-007] — Uncle Mitai forms one half of a comic duo. The two Uncles, one lean and red-bearded, the other broad and black-bearded, embody a folk-comedy contrast. Their futile efforts with the horses, urged on by a chorus of shouting peasants, form a self-contained miniature of misdirected energy — a theme that resonates with the novel's larger satire of [[russian-empire|Russia]]n inefficiency.
