# Imperial Treasury

## Description

The Imperial Treasury (Государственное казначейство) was the central financial institution of the [[russian-empire|Russian Empire]], responsible for collecting taxes, disbursing government funds, administering state contracts, and — crucially for Gogol's novel — processing mortgages and loans secured against landed property [011-032]. Every [[landownership|landowner]] in the Empire who wished to borrow money against their [[landownership|estate]] or [[serfdom|serfs]] dealt with the Treasury or its provincial agents. The Treasury was the endpoint of the vast bureaucratic ma[[table-of-ranks|chin]]ery that converted human beings into taxable units and land into collateral.

In the early nineteenth century, the Treasury was also a major source of credit for the gentry. Mortgages to the Treasury were a relatively recent innovation — "in those days mortgage to the Treasury was an innovation looked upon with distrust" [011-032] — but they rapidly became a pillar of the landowning economy. The Treasury's role as both tax collector and creditor gave it a dual, sometimes contradictory, relationship with the gentry: it was simultaneously the institution that demanded tax payments and the institution that lent money against the same property.

## Role in Dead Souls

The Treasury is the invisible but omnipresent institution behind [[purchase-of-dead-souls|Chichikov's scheme]]. His plan — to purchase [[dead-souls|dead soul]]s and mortgage them as living property — is designed to defraud the Treasury specifically: to borrow money from the state against assets that do not exist [002-125]. The Treasury is referenced throughout the novel as the ultimate arbiter of financial legitimacy and the ultimate victim of bureaucratic fraud.

[[purchase-of-dead-souls|Chichikov's scheme]] requires the Treasury to remain unaware that the [[dead-souls|souls]] pledged as collateral are dead. The irony Gogol constructs is that the Treasury — the most powerful financial institution in the Empire — is helpless against a scheme that exploits the gap between [[revision-census|census]] records and reality. The [[revision-census|revision]] lists ([[revision-census|census]]) dictate who is alive for tax purposes, and the Treasury, bound by its own paperwork, cannot distinguish between a living [[serfdom|serf]] and one who exists only on paper.

The Treasury appears in the moral calculus of the novel as well. When Sobakevitch condemns [[chichikov|Chichikov]]'s bargaining, he frames it as theft from the state: "you would have robbed the Treasury, since a kulak always remains a kulak" [005-122]. [[plushkin|Plushkin]] similarly refuses to pay "money either to me or to the Treasury" [006-038], treating the Treasury as an adversary to be dodged. The Treasury represents not just a financial institution but a moral claim — the state's legitimate demand on its citizens — that the novel's characters consistently evade.
