# Koshkarev's Estate

## Description

[[koshkarev|Koshkarev]]'s [[landownership|estate]] presents one of the most bizarre spectacles in [[dead-souls|Dead Souls]].
Half an hour's drive from [[kostanzhoglo|Kostanzhoglo]]'s model farm, the village attached to
the manor is found "in a state of utter confusion," with "in every direction
building and repairing operations" in progress, and the alleys "choked with
heaps of lime, bricks, and beams of wood" [014-009].

The most striking feature is the transformation of the peasant huts. Some have
been "arranged to resemble offices" and bear gilt-lettered inscriptions:
"Depot for Agricultural Implements," "Chief Office of Accounts," "Estate Works
Committee," and "Normal School for the Education of Colonists" [014-009]. The
[[landownership|estate]] has effectively been converted into a miniature replica of the Tsarist
[[imperial-civil-service|civil service]], complete with its own [[officialdom|bureaucracy]].

Approa[[table-of-ranks|chin]]g the house through a large vegetable enclosure, one emerges into "a
square near a wooden church," beyond which the roofs of the manorial homestead
are visible [014-020]. The proprietor himself — [[koshkarev|Colonel Koshkarev]] — is found
seated "behind a desk," holding a pen between his teeth, a posture of
permanent bureaucratic readiness [014-009].

The Colonel's conversion of the [[landownership|estate]] into a ministry is absolute: the
[[landownership|estate]] hosts a "Committee for the Reception of Reports and Returns," a
"Department of Estate Affairs," an "Estate Works Committee," an
"Administrator," a "Secretary," and "Commissioners" who guide visitors through
the labyrinth of offices — some of which have their shutters up and doors
locked because the Director has been transferred to yet another newly-formed
committee [014-169].

## Role in Dead Souls

[[koshkarev|Koshkarev]]'s [[landownership|estate]] appears in Part II Chapter 4 as a satiric pinnacle of
Gogol's critique of [[imperial-civil-service|Russian bureaucracy]]. Where the Part I [[landownership|estate]]s each embody
a personal vice or folly, [[koshkarev|Koshkarev]]'s [[landownership|estate]] embodies institutional madness —
the metastasis of paperwork, committees, and [[officialdom|official]] procedure into every
corner of life, even agriculture.

[[chichikov|Chichikov]] visits seeking to acquire [[dead-souls|dead soul]]s, but his simple request is
immediately swallowed by the Colonel's bureaucratic ma[[table-of-ranks|chin]]ery. [[koshkarev|Koshkarev]]
insists that every request must be put "into writing" and processed through a
chain of offices: the "Office for the Reception of Reports and Returns" →
"Estate Works Committee" → "Administrator" → "Secretary" [014-030]. When
[[chichikov|Chichikov]] protests that this will take too long, the Colonel merely suggests
refining the wording of the Statement of Plea.

The [[landownership|estate]] also provides the novel's most explicit statement of utopian
agrarian lunacy: [[koshkarev|Koshkarev]] dreams of inducing his peasants to wear German
costume, to read Franklin on electricity and Virgil's Georgics while walking
behind the plough, and to have every miller's daughter play the piano — as in
Germany, "where he had resided for fourteen years" [014-009]. [[kostanzhoglo|Kostanzhoglo]]'s
verdict — "[[koshkarev|Koshkarev]] is a most reassuring phenomenon. We need him, that we
may see, reflected in him, the follies of our many clever men" [014-207] —
captures the [[landownership|estate]]'s thematic role as mirror of systemic folly.
