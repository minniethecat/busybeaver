# Uncle Minai

## Character Profile

Uncle Minai is a peasant who appears in a single comic scene in Chapter 5 of [[dead-souls|Dead Souls]]. After [[chichikov|Chichikov]]'s carriage becomes entangled with another vehicle on the road, a group of peasants is summoned to disentangle the horses. Uncle Minai is one of two muzhiks — the other being [[uncle-mitai|Uncle Mitai]] — who attempt, with much enthusiasm and little skill, to resolve the situation.

Gogol provides a vivid, caricatured physical description of Uncle Minai: "a peasant with a pair of broad shoulders, a beard as black as charcoal, and a belly like the huge samovar in which sbiten is brewed for all attending a local market" [005-007]. His sheer bulk is the defining feature — when he "hastened to seat himself upon the shaft horse," the animal "almost sank to the ground beneath his weight" [005-007].

The scene is pure Gogolian comedy. After [[uncle-mitai|Uncle Mitai]] mounts the shaft horse "like a village steeple" and proves useless, Uncle Minai is summoned as reinforcement. The peasants, observing the two uncles now mounted on the horses, confidently cry "NOW they will go all right! Lay it on hot, lay it on hot!" [005-007]. But the affair "in no way progressed" — both uncles end up mounting the same horse, while a third peasant takes the trace horse, creating a scene of such confusion that the coachman finally dismisses the lot of them: "he sent the two Uncles about their business — and not before it was time, seeing that the horses were steaming" [005-007].

## Role in Dead Souls

Uncle Minai belongs to a recurring Gogolian type: the well-meaning but incompetent peasant whose interventions only make things worse. Together with [[uncle-mitai|Uncle Mitai]], he forms a comic diptych — the long, lean, red-bearded [[uncle-mitai|Mitai]] and the broad, black-bearded, samovar-bellied Minai. Their failure to disentangle the horses is a miniature set piece of [[russian-empire|Russia]]n inefficiency, enacted in a roadside ditch while [[chichikov|Chichikov]] watches from his carriage.

The scene also provides a rare glimpse of [[chichikov|Chichikov]] as spectator. Throughout the horse-swit[[table-of-ranks|chin]]g comedy, [[chichikov|Chichikov]] "had been gazing at the young unknown with great attention" [005-007] — the governor's daughter, seated in the other carriage. The peasants' bumbling serves as comic foreground to a more consequential moment: [[chichikov|Chichikov]]'s first sight of the young woman who will later contribute to his downfall.
