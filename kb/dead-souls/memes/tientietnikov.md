# Tientietnikov

## Character Profile

Andrei Ivanovitch Tientietnikov is a young [[landownership|landowner]] of about
thirty-three who opens Part II of *[[dead-souls|Dead Souls]]*. Gogol introduces him
with a question: "what manner of man is Tientietnikov, and what are his
attributes and his qualities?" [012-006]. The answer is a portrait of
arrested potential — a man of intelligence, education, and good
intentions who has retreated from the world into a life of lethargic
indolence.

Tientietnikov's story begins in the [[imperial-civil-service|civil service]] in St. Petersburg,
where his idealism collides with the reality of bureaucratic life. His
superior — a man of stone-like impassivity — rebuffs Tientietnikov's
earnest efforts, and the young man, crushed, resigns his post and
returns to his [[landownership|estate]] [012-026]. There he attempts reforms: abolishing
the corvée, reducing the muzhiks' obligations. But when the peasants
fail to respond with gratitude, he withdraws further, spending his days
in a dressing-gown, rarely leaving his room [012-017].

Gogol uses Tientietnikov to illustrate the **paralysis of the
well-meaning [[russian-empire|Russia]]n [[landownership|landowner]]** — a man whose intelligence and
principles are not matched by the will or cunning to put them into
practice. He represents the first stage of [[chichikov|Chichikov]]'s Part II journey:
the [[landownership|landowner]] who has already failed, and who [[chichikov|Chichikov]]'s visit may
either redeem or exploit.

## Role in Dead Souls

Tientietnikov serves as the **entry point into Part II's thematic
landscape**. Where Part I's [[landownership|landowners]] are characterized by various
forms of moral deformity (sentimentality, suspicion, bluster,
materialism, miserliness), Tientietnikov is characterized by
paralysis. He is not a bad man — he is a man who has stopped.

[[chichikov|Chichikov]] arrives at Tientietnikov's [[landownership|estate]], Tientietnikovka, and
immediately sets about ingratiating himself — helping to reconcile
Tientietnikov with [[general-betristchev|General Betristchev]], whose daughter [[ulinka|Ulinka]]
Tientietnikov once loved. This romantic subplot, unfinished in the
surviving text, suggests Gogol's intent to contrast the [[dead-souls|dead soul]]s
of Part I with the possibility of spiritual resurrection.
