# Purchase of Dead Souls

The **purchase of [[dead-souls|dead soul]]s** (*pokupka myortvykh dush*) is the central plot mechanism of Nikolai Gogol's [[Dead Souls]]. Over the course of five chapters (2–6), the protagonist [[Chichikov, Paul Ivanovitch|Paul Ivanovitch Chichikov]] travels through the [[russian-empire|Russia]]n countryside visiting [[landownership|landowners]], from each of whom he proposes to buy "[[dead-souls|dead soul]]s" — [[serfdom|serfs]] who have died since the last [[revision-census|tax census]] (the *[[revision-census|revision]]*) but who remain on the [[officialdom|official]] registers, for whom their owners must continue paying taxes until the next [[revision-census|census]]. [[chichikov|Chichikov]]'s plan is to acquire these legally-alive-but-actually-[[dead-souls|dead serfs]], mortgage them to a government bank as though they were living collateral, and use the proceeds to purchase a real [[landownership|estate]] of his own. The scheme is a brilliant satirical device: it is simultaneously absurd and, within the legal framework of [[russian-empire|Imperial Russia]], perfectly logical.

## Summary

The scheme unfolds across five distinct encounters, each revealing a different facet of the [[russian-empire|Russia]]n landowning class. Before the visits begin, Gogol plants the seed of the idea in a digression: an observer speculates that "[[chichikov|Chichikov]] may buy [[dead-souls|dead soul]]s, or railway shares, or he may borrow money from a bank on the '[[dead-souls|dead soul]]s' no less than on the living ones" [P03-008], suggesting the scheme's fundamental legal plausibility.

**[[manilov|Manilov]] (Chapter 2)**. At [[Manilovka]], [[chichikov|Chichikov]] broaches the proposal with elaborate indirection. [[Manilov]], a sentimental and idle [[landownership|landowner]], is bewildered — he drops his pipe in astonishment — but, flattered by [[chichikov|Chichikov]]'s manner and eager to be agreeable, he gives his [[dead-souls|dead soul]]s away for free [002-045, 002-131]. The narrator notes that [[manilov|Manilov]] "looked upon the [[dead-souls|dead soul]]s as so much worthless rubbish" [002-131]. This sets the baseline: the [[dead-souls|souls]] are worthless to those who do not grasp their legal utility.

**[[korobotchka|Korobotchka]] (Chapter 3)**. The widow [[Korobotchka]] presents the opposite challenge. She cannot comprehend *why* anyone would want [[dead-souls|dead soul]]s, and her incomprehension manifests as suspicion: she fears being cheated on price. [[chichikov|Chichikov]] is driven to exasperation — he privately calls her "cudgel-headed" — but eventually secures eighteen [[dead-souls|souls]] by exploiting her fear of [[officialdom|official]]dom, posing as a government contractor [003-099–003-117].

**[[nozdrev|Nozdrev]] (Chapter 4)**. The visit to [[Nozdrev]] nearly derails the entire enterprise. The boisterous, dishonest [[landownership|landowner]] refuses to sell [[dead-souls|dead soul]]s outright and instead insists on gambling for them. When [[chichikov|Chichikov]] refuses, [[nozdrev|Nozdrev]] accuses him of cheating at cards and nearly starts a fight. The encounter ends with [[chichikov|Chichikov]] fleeing under threat of violence [004-215]. No [[dead-souls|souls]] are acquired, but the visit foreshadows [[nozdrev|Nozdrev]]'s later role in exposing the scheme.

**Sobakevitch (Chapter 5)**. [[Sobakevitch]] immediately grasps the scheme's financial logic. Unlike the others, he neither sentimentalises nor suspects — he *negotiates*. He praises his [[dead-souls|dead serfs]] as though they were still alive (extolling the craftsmanship of a deceased carriage-maker named Mikheiev), bargains aggressively, and secures a price far above what [[chichikov|Chichikov]] intended to pay [005-*]. He also warns [[chichikov|Chichikov]] about [[Plushkin]], the next [[landownership|landowner]] on the list.

**[[plushkin|Plushkin]] (Chapter 6)**. The final visit yields [[chichikov|Chichikov]]'s largest acquisition. [[Plushkin]], a pathological miser with over a thousand [[serfdom|serfs]], has allowed so many to die or flee that he sells over two hundred [[dead-souls|souls]] — including seventy-eight runaways — at thirty kopecks each [006-081, 006-083]. The transaction is grotesquely comic: [[plushkin|Plushkin]] is overjoyed to be relieved of the tax burden and celebrates by offering [[chichikov|Chichikov]] a dusty decanter of ancient liquor [006-048].

**Aftermath (Chapter 7)**. [[chichikov|Chichikov]] returns to the [[town-of-n|town of N.]] and presents the purchase deeds at [[civil-court|the government offices]]. The President of the Council, an old acquaintance of [[plushkin|Plushkin]]'s, recognises the letter of introduction and facilitates the registration [007-001–007-017]. For a brief moment, Chichikov's scheme appears to have succeeded completely. He is treated as a wealthy [[landownership|landowner]], and his social standing in the town reaches its zenith.

## Significance

The purchase of [[dead-souls|dead soul]]s is more than a plot device — it is Gogol's master metaphor. In a society where human beings are legally classified as property, the distinction between "living" and "dead" [[dead-souls|souls]] becomes perilously thin. The [[landownership|landowners]] [[chichikov|Chichikov]] visits are, in various ways, spiritually dead: [[Manilov]] through idleness, [[Korobotchka]] through narrow-mindedness, [[Nozdrev]] through dissipation, [[Sobakevitch]] through materialism, and [[Plushkin]] through avarice. [[chichikov|Chichikov]], who deals in [[dead-souls|dead soul]]s, is himself a kind of [[dead-souls|dead soul]] — Gogol withholds his backstory until the final chapter, rendering him a hollow vehicle of acquisition.

The legal mechanism that makes the scheme possible — the *[[revision-census|revision]]* ([[revision-census|census]]) system, under which [[serfdom|serfs]] were counted and taxed at long intervals — was a real feature of [[russian-empire|Imperial Russia]]n administration. Gogol's satire thus operates at the intersection of absurd fiction and documentary realism: the scheme is outlandish, but the [[officialdom|bureaucracy]] that enables it is entirely authentic.

## Context

**Before**: [[chichikov|Chichikov]] arrives in the [[town-of-n|town of N.]] as a mysterious stranger of modest means [001-001]. He cultivates the acquaintance of local [[officialdom|officials]] and [[landownership|landowners]], attending the [[governor|Governor]]'s soirée and making favourable impressions. His true purpose — the purchase of [[dead-souls|dead soul]]s — is not revealed until Chapter 2.

**After**: The scheme collapses in Chapters 8–10 when [[Korobotchka]], anxious that she sold too cheaply, travels to town to check the market price [008-036]. Her inquiry reaches [[Nozdrev]], who publicly denounces [[chichikov|Chichikov]] at a ball [008-*]. Rumours multiply: [[chichikov|Chichikov]] is a forger, a kidnapper, Napoleon in disguise. His social standing evaporates, and he flees the town in Chapter 11.
