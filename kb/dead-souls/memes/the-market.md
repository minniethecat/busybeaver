# The Market Place

## Description

The market place in [[dead-souls|Dead Souls]] is the province's commercial bloodstream — a space
where the economic transactions that the novel anatomizes are rendered in their most
concrete and unadorned form. Gogol locates the market in the opening pages of the
novel: [[chichikov|Chichikov]]'s arrival in the [[town-of-n|town of N.]] is marked by the sight of "local
tradesmen" who, on market days, "make it their regular practice" to gather and hawk
their wares [001-003].

The market is not a single unified square but a network of stalls, carts, and
temporary encampments. Goods are sold from the back of wagons, and prices are
negotiated in the open air. Sobakevitch, boasting of his [[dead-souls|dead serfs]]' craftsmanship,
invokes the market as the ultimate test of value: "if you were to search the whole
market, you would never find a better" workman than the coach-builder Mikheiev
[004-042]. The market thus functions as the novel's implicit standard of quality —
its rough-and-ready judgment is more honest than any [[officialdom|official]] appraisal.

The physical texture of the market is coarse and sensory: the "local fish market"
supplies the monstrous sturgeon that [[nozdrev|Nozdrev]] consumes at [[the-tavern|the inn]] [007-115]; goods
are described as having been "kicking about the market-place four days or more,"
their freshness negotiated by haggling rather than hygiene [005-044]. Gogol
deliberately strips the market of picturesque charm — it smells of fish, sweat, and
leather, and its aesthetics are those of survival, not display.

## Role in Dead Souls

The market is the backdrop against which Gogol's critique of [[russian-empire|Russia]]n economic
morality unfolds. The buying and selling of [[dead-souls|dead soul]]s — the novel's central
transaction — is a grotesque echo of legitimate commerce. [[purchase-of-dead-souls|Chichikov's scheme]] works
precisely because the boundary between the market's honest trade and his fraudulent
traffic is so easily blurred.

When a character proposes to sell a [[serfdom|serf]] "at the market-place," as though the dead
[[dead-souls|souls]] were ordinary commodities, the absurdity reaches its peak [011-012]. The
market, in Gogol's rendering, is less a place than a principle — the principle that
everything, even human [[dead-souls|souls]], can be priced. The "wizened old lady who went to
market in person and dried her stockings" is both a figure of plucky self-reliance
and a symptom of an economy in which every transaction has been reduced to the
barest calculus of profit [011-025].

By the novel's late chapters, the market has become a symbol of the transactional
logic that governs all provincial relations: peasants "take their own wares to
market," gentlemen buy at "a cheap rate in the local markets," and the circulation
of goods and the circulation of [[dead-souls|souls]] become, in Gogol's satiric vision,
indistinguishable [014-211][015-271].

## Appearances

- **Chapter 1**: The mention of tradesmen on market days during the opening
  description of the [[town-of-n|town of N.]] [001-003].
- **Chapter 5**: Sobakevitch's valuation of his [[dead-souls|dead serfs]] by market standards —
  the coach-builder Mikheiev "you would never find a better" [004-042]. The goods
  "kicking about the market-place" for days [005-044].
- **Chapter 7**: The fish market that supplies [[the-tavern|the inn]]'s kitchen [007-115]. The
  rhetorical question about carting merchandise to market [007-005].
- **Chapter 11 (Part I)**: The proposal to sell someone "at the market-place" and
  the wizened old lady who goes to market herself [011-012][011-025].
- **Chapter 14–15 (Part II)**: Peasants taking wares to market and cheap market
  purchases [014-211][015-271].
