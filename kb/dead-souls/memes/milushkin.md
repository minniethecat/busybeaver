# Milushkin

## Character Profile

Milushkin is a dead [[serfdom|serf]] bricklayer formerly belonging to the [[landownership|landowner]] [[sobakevich|Sobakevich]]. He never appears as a living character in the novel but is conjured through the boasting of his former master and the documentary record of the [[dead-souls|dead soul]]s transaction. [[sobakevich|Sobakevich]] praises him as a craftsman who "could build a stove in any house you liked" [005-087], placing him among the roster of skilled artisans — [[michiev|Michiev]] the wheelwright, [[maksim-teliatnikov|Teliatnikov]] the bootmaker, [[probka-stepan|Probka Stepan]] the carpenter — whose deaths [[sobakevich|Sobakevich]] conceals in order to sell their names at an inflated price [007-089].

Milushkin represents the anonymous labour that undergirds the [[russian-empire|Russia]]n [[landownership|estate]] economy. His skill is vouched for by [[sobakevich|Sobakevich]]'s salesmanship, yet the reader never learns anything of his person beyond his trade. In this he epitomises the condition of the [[serfdom|serf]] in Gogol's world: his labour is valuable, his existence as a human being is invisible.

## Role in Dead Souls

Milushkin belongs to the gallery of [[dead-souls|dead serfs]] whose names [[chichikov|Chichikov]] purchases and whose imagined biographies — supplied partly by [[sobakevich|Sobakevich]]'s hyperbole, partly by [[chichikov|Chichikov]]'s own reverie at the close of Chapter 7 — form a shadow population running parallel to the living characters of the novel [007-089]. His inclusion in the transaction underscores the central irony of the [[purchase-of-dead-souls|dead souls scheme]]: the [[serfdom|serfs]] are valuable precisely as names on a [[revision-census|census]] register, their craftsmanship and human qualities reduced to bargaining counters in a financial fraud.

Milushkin's trade as a bricklayer — specifically, as a builder of stoves — carries symbolic weight within the novel's domestic landscape. The [[russian-empire|Russia]]n stove (*pech*') is the hearth of the household, the centre of warmth and sustenance; that a dead [[serfdom|serf]] was once capable of constructing such a vital object heightens the novel's elegiac undertone regarding wasted human potential.
