# The Prison

## Description

The prison in [[dead-souls|Dead Souls]] appears first as a figure of speech — a threat, a
comparison — before materializing as a physical space in Part II. Its earliest
invocation is figurative: [[plushkin|Plushkin]]'s [[serfdom|serfs]], it is said, "live worse" than
"convicts in prison," their master's miserliness reducing them to a condition of
deprivation that makes the penal system look almost indulgent by contrast [005-049].
The comparison is characteristically Gogolian: it operates by inversion, suggesting
not that the prison is comfortable but that [[plushkin|Plushkin]]'s [[landownership|estate]] is a different kind of
prison altogether.

The threat of imprisonment punctuates the novel. When a character is warned that
[[officialdom|officials]] will "lay hands upon him and off with him to prison," the phrase is
delivered with the casualness of a proverb — arrest is so woven into the fabric of
provincial life that it requires no elaboration [007-006]. Similarly, references
to an "island prison" invoke the spectre of exile and isolation, the ultimate
sanction of the [[russian-empire|Russian state]] [010-010].

In Part II, the prison becomes a concrete setting. The dramatic arrest of a central
character fills the late chapters: "certainty he will be haled to prison on the
morrow, ten years or more" [015-076]. The physical prison is rendered through a
series of rapid, almost cinematic details — the removal order, the physical struggle
with the guards, the prisoner's cry that "They are taking me away to prison and
death!" [015-214]. The "thickness of the prison walls" and the "dull echoes" within
them create a sudden claustrophobia in a novel that has otherwise moved freely
across the open road [015-221].

The prison's [[officialdom|bureaucracy]] is as formidable as its walls. The prisoner's complaint —
that he has been "cast into prison, and treated like a dog" without process or
trial — voices a critique that extends beyond the novel's fictional world to the
arbitrary justice of [[russian-empire|Nicholas I]]'s [[russian-empire|Russia]] [015-233]. Yet Gogol, with his refusal of
simple moral positions, also shows the prison's permeability: [[bribery|bribes]] pass through
its walls, documents are altered, and the prisoner finds it "difficult to credit"
his eventual rescue [015-262][015-270].

## Role in Dead Souls

The prison functions as the novel's ultimate negative space — the place where the
provincial world's improvisations and schemes hit the hard wall of state power. If
[[chichikov|Chichikov]]'s dead-[[dead-souls|souls]] scheme represents the fluidity of [[russian-empire|Russia]]n [[bribery|corruption]] (where
everything is negotiable), the prison represents its limit (where negotiation
encounters force).

Gogol uses the prison to sharpen the novel's moral stakes. The casual references
to imprisonment in Part I — the offhand threats, the proverbial comparisons — are
retrospectively darkened by Part II's incarceration scenes, as though the novel has
been moving toward the prison from its opening pages without the reader quite
realizing it. The open road, the novel's master symbol, leads eventually to a locked
door.
