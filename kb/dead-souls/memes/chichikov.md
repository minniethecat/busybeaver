# Chichikov, Paul Ivanovitch

## Character Profile

Paul Ivanovitch Chichikov is the **anti-hero and protagonist** of [[dead-souls|Dead Souls]] — a middle-aged Collegiate Councillor of modest origins who arrives in the [[town-of-n|provincial town]] of N. with a mysterious scheme. He is neither young nor old, neither handsome nor ugly, neither fat nor thin: a man carefully designed to give no offense and attract no particular attention （001-003）. This deliberate ordinariness is his greatest asset.

Chichikov's appearance mirrors his social strategy. He dresses well but not ostentatiously. His manners are impeccable — he bows at exactly the right angle for each rank, flatters with precision, and adapts his conversation to each interlocutor. To the sentimental [[manilov|Manilov]], he is sentimental; to the brusque [[sobakevich|Sobakevich]], he is direct; to the suspicious Korobochka, he is patient.

His biography (revealed in Chapter 11) explains his character. Born to a minor noble family of dwindling means, Chichikov was taught by his father that the path to success lies in pleasing superiors and saving money: "You will do everything and get on in the world with a kopeck" （011-004）. He internalized this lesson completely, mastering the art of bureaucratic survival — first as a schoolboy currying favor with his teacher, then as a customs [[officialdom|official]] running a smuggling ring, and finally as the architect of the [[purchase-of-dead-souls|dead souls scheme]].

## Role in Dead Souls

Chichikov serves three functions in the novel:

1. **Picaresque traveler**: Structurally, Chichikov is the thread that connects the novel's episodes. His visits to the five [[landownership|landowners]] provide the narrative framework, and his adaptability allows Gogol to reveal each character through their reaction to the same bizarre proposal.

2. **Satirical mirror**: Chichikov reflects back to each [[landownership|landowner]] an idealized version of themselves, and their responses to this mirroring expose their true natures. His chameleon quality is a satirical device as much as a character trait.

3. **Moral void**: Unlike a traditional hero, Chichikov has no inner life beyond calculation. His scheme is audacious but ultimately hollow — he is accumulating paper wealth, ghost [[serfdom|serfs]], a phantom [[landownership|estate]]. He is, as the critic notes, a man who could equally well "buy [[dead-souls|dead soul]]s, or railway shares, or go in for church building" （P03-008） — the object of speculation is irrelevant; the speculative impulse is all.

Gogol planned a three-volume work in which Chichikov would undergo moral regeneration, modeled on Dante's Divine Comedy. Only Part I and fragments of Part II survive. In the extant text, Chichikov remains unredeemed — a man who learns nothing and forgets nothing, whose last act is to flee town before his scheme is fully exposed.

## As Seen by Others

Chichikov is defined as much by how others perceive him as by who he is. Each character in the novel sees a different Chichikov, and their reactions reveal more about themselves than about him:

| Observer | Perception | What It Reveals |
|----------|-----------|----------------|
| [[manilov|Manilov]] | A cultivated gentleman, "most charming" company | Manilov's desperate need for a kindred spirit in provincial isolation |
| Korobochka | A suspicious buyer who might be cheating her | Korobochka's fear-driven, transactional worldview |
| [[nozdrev|Nozdrev]] | A fellow rogue — or a target for "exchange" | Nozdrev's inability to see anyone except as a plaything |
| [[sobakevich|Sobakevich]] | A shrewd dealer who knows market value | Sobakevich's cold, commercial assessment of all human relations |
| Pliushkin | A generous benefactor — or a fool to exploit | Pliushkin's all-consuming suspicion confused by rare generosity |
| Town ladies | The "millionaire" — suddenly the most eligible bachelor | Provincial society's hunger for novelty and status |
| Town [[officialdom|officials]] | A government inspector, a Napoleon in disguise, a counterfeiter | The bureaucracy's paranoid imagination in the face of the inexplicable |

This kaleidoscopic perception is Gogol's central satirical device: Chichikov is a blank screen onto which every character projects their own obsessions. His "ordinariness" is not a failure of characterization but a deliberate narrative strategy — the void at the center of a society that has lost all substance （011-028）.

## Relationships

Chichikov's relationships are entirely instrumental. He has no friends, no family (his father died early), and no lasting loyalties. Each person he meets is evaluated for their usefulness to his scheme:

- **[[manilov|Manilov]]** — Flattered into giving away [[dead-souls|dead soul]]s for free
- **Korobochka** — Reluctantly sells after Chichikov loses patience
- **[[nozdrev|Nozdrev]]** — Attempts to cheat Chichikov; Chichikov barely escapes
- **[[sobakevich|Sobakevich]]** — Drives a hard bargain; mutual respect as fellow businessmen
- **Pliushkin** — Chichikov exploits his greed by offering to pay the tax on [[dead-souls|dead soul]]s
