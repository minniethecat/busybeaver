# Sobakevich

## Character Profile

Michael Semenovitch Sobakevich is the fourth [[landownership|landowner]] [[chichikov|Chichikov]] visits and the most formidable. Gogol describes him as resembling "a medium-sized bear" — his frock coat is bear-colored, his gait is bear-like, and even his furniture seems to have been built to his own sturdy, ungainly proportions [005-001]. Unlike the other [[landownership|landowners]], Sobakevich has no pretensions: he is exactly what he appears to be — coarse, shrewd, and unshakeably self-confident.

His [[landownership|estate]] is the physical expression of his character. Every building is solid, practical, and unadorned — built, as Gogol notes, "without any regard for symmetry." Sobakevich eats enormously, speaks bluntly, and calls every [[officialdom|official]] in town a rascal or a fool. He is the only [[landownership|landowner]] who sees through [[purchase-of-dead-souls|Chichikov's scheme]] instantly — and who sells his [[dead-souls|dead soul]]s at a premium, praising each [[serfdom|serf]]'s craftsmanship as a horse-trader might praise a thoroughbred.

## Role in Dead Souls

Sobakevich serves as [[chichikov|Chichikov]]'s **most equal adversary**. Where [[manilov|Manilov]] is bewildered and [[korobotchka|Korobochka]] is suspicious, Sobakevich immediately understands that [[chichikov|Chichikov]] wants something and determines to extract the maximum price. He invents elaborate biographies for his [[dead-souls|dead serfs]] — [[michiev|Mikheev]] the carriage-builder, [[milushkin|Telyatnikov]] the bricklayer — inflating their value through sheer rhetorical force.

Sobakevich represents the **commercialization of everything** under [[serfdom|serfdom]]. If [[manilov|Manilov]] is too sentimental to value his [[dead-souls|dead souls]] and [[korobotchka|Korobochka]] too ignorant, Sobakevich values them too well — as commodities whose market price, he insists, depends entirely on their quality. His materialism is total and untroubled by any moral consideration.

## The Bargain

Sobakevich's bargaining with [[chichikov|Chichikov]] over the [[dead-souls|dead souls]] is one of the novel's most brilliantly comic scenes [005-031]. He immediately names a price of one hundred roubles per soul — an exorbitant sum — and proceeds to praise each dead [[serfdom|serf]] as though they were still alive and working. The carriage-builder [[michiev|Mikheev]], the bricklayer [[milushkin|Milushkin]], the bootmaker [[maksim-teliatnikov|Maksim Teliatnikov]] — all are extolled as master craftsmen whose skills would command high wages anywhere [005-032].

When [[chichikov|Chichikov]] protests, Sobakevich declares: "You are trying to buy souls, and I am trying to sell them. You want to save your pocket, and I want to fill mine" [005-038]. The deal is finally struck at two and a half roubles per soul — a price still well above what [[chichikov|Chichikov]] paid [[manilov|Manilov]] or [[korobotchka|Korobochka]].

## Character as Satire

In Gogol's gallery of landowners, Sobakevich embodies the principle of **unadorned materialism**. He is free of the sentimentality of [[manilov|Manilov]], the anxious suspicion of [[korobotchka|Korobochka]], the reckless dishonesty of [[nozdrev|Nozdrev]], and the pathological hoarding of [[plushkin|Plushkin]]. His virtues — physical strength, practical intelligence, blunt honesty — are real, but they serve no higher purpose than self-enrichment. Gogol presents him as the logical endpoint of a society that measures everything by its market value.
