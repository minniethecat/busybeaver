# Plushkin

**Stepan Plushkin** is a provincial [[russian-empire|Russia]]n [[landownership|landowner]] who appears in Chapter 5 and Chapter 6 of [[Dead Souls]]. He is the fifth and final [[landownership|landowner]] from whom [[Chichikov, Paul Ivanovitch|Chichikov]] purchases "[[dead-souls|dead soul]]s" — deceased [[serfdom|serfs]] still counted on the tax [[revision-census|revision]] rolls. Plushkin represents the most extreme degeneration of the [[gentry|Russian landowning gentry]]: a pathological miser who, despite owning over a thousand [[serfdom|serfs]] and storehouses crammed with produce, dresses in rags and lives in squalor. His character is one of Gogol's most vivid studies of avarice and the spiritual decay it brings.

## Character Profile

Plushkin is introduced indirectly through [[Sobakevitch]], who describes him as "such a miser as never you could imagine" whose convicts "live better than he does" [005-047]. When [[Chichikov, Paul Ivanovitch|Chichikov]] finally meets him in Chapter 6, he initially mistakes Plushkin for a female housekeeper — the old man is so ragged and dirty as to be "almost indistinguishable from a man" [006-012].

His physical appearance is grotesque. His [[table-of-ranks|chin]] protrudes so far forward that he must wipe it with a handkerchief when speaking, and his "small eyes were not yet grown dull, but twinkled under their overhanging brows like the eyes of mice" [006-012]. His clothes defy description:

> "In no way could it have been guessed of what his coat was made, for both its sleeves and its skirts were so ragged and filthy as to defy description, while instead of two posterior tails, there dangled four of those appendages, with, projecting from them, a torn newspaper. Also, around his neck there was wrapped something which might have been a stocking, a garter, or a stomacher, but was certainly not a tie." [006-012]

The irony is devastating: this beggar-like figure is no mendicant but a [[landownership|landowner]] "possessed of fully a thousand [[serfdom|serfs]], the superior of all his neighbours in wealth of flour and grain, and the owner of storehouses... that were crammed with homespun cloth and linen, tanned and undressed sheepskins, dried fish, and every conceivable species of produce" [006-012].

His living quarters mirror his soul. [[chichikov|Chichikov]] enters a parlour where "the floor was never washed," filled with broken furniture, a clock "minus a pendulum and covered all over with cobwebs," a dried lemon "shrunken to the dimensions of a hazelnut," a tumbler containing "the dregs of some liquid and three flies," and a chandelier in a covering "so dusty as closely to resemble a huge cocoon enclosing a caterpillar" [006-012]. Gogol's narrator remarks that "such a phenomenon is rare in [[russian-empire|Russia]], where the tendency is rather to prodigality than to parsimony" [006-012].

## Role in Dead Souls

Plushkin represents the final stage in Gogol's gallery of [[landownership|landowner]] types — the complete surrender to avarice that destroys all human connection. Unlike [[Manilov]] (sentimental idleness), [[Korobotchka]] (suspicious narrow-mindedness), [[Nozdrev]] (boisterous dishonesty), or [[Sobakevitch]] (shrewd materialism), Plushkin embodies the annihilation of the soul through obsessive accumulation.

He is also [[chichikov|Chichikov]]'s most profitable transaction. Plushkin owns not only a large number of recently deceased [[serfdom|serfs]] but also a list of runaway [[serfdom|serfs]] — seventy-eight in total — whom he sells at thirty kopecks each [006-081, 006-083]. [[chichikov|Chichikov]] acquires over two hundred [[dead-souls|souls]] from Plushkin alone, making the visit the high point of his scheme [006-089].

A crucial moment occurs when [[chichikov|Chichikov]] offers to bear the costs of the sale himself. Plushkin experiences a brief, almost-human reaction:

> "Over Plushkin's wooden features there had gleamed a ray of warmth — a ray which expressed, if not feeling, at all events feeling's pale reflection. Just such a phenomenon may be witnessed when, for a brief moment, a drowning man makes a last re-appearance on the surface of a river... But the hope is short-lived, and the hands disappear. Even so did Plushkin's face, after its momentary manifestation of feeling, become meaner and more insensible than ever." [006-058]

This moment — the drowning man simile — is one of the most famous passages in the novel, suggesting that even in the most degraded soul, a residue of humanity lingers.

## Relationships

- **[[Sobakevitch]]** — Neighbouring [[landownership|landowner]] who warns [[chichikov|Chichikov]] about Plushkin's miserliness. Calls him a "skinflint" who "feeds his people so badly" [005-055, 005-154].
- **[[Chichikov, Paul Ivanovitch|Chichikov]]** — Plushkin is deeply suspicious at first but overcome with joy when [[chichikov|Chichikov]] offers to buy his [[dead-souls|dead serfs]]. Calls [[chichikov|Chichikov]] "my dear sir! My good benefactor!" [006-036].

- **President of the Council** — An old schoolfellow of Plushkin's, to whom [[chichikov|Chichikov]] delivers a letter of introduction [006-095, 007-*].

## Critical Reception

Plushkin is widely regarded as one of the supreme creations of [[russian-empire|Russia]]n literary realism. The character's name has entered the [[russian-empire|Russia]]n language as a common noun (*pliushkin*) meaning a miser or hoarder. Vladimir Nabokov, in his lectures on [[russian-empire|Russia]]n literature, ranked the Plushkin chapter among Gogol's greatest achievements, particularly the extended metaphor of the decaying [[landownership|estate]] as an index of spiritual decay.

The "drowning man" simile ([006-058]) is frequently cited as an example of Gogol's technique of the "lyrical digression" — a moment where the narrator steps back from satire to offer a note of genuine pathos, only to return, more sharply, to satire.
