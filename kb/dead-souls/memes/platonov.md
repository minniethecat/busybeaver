# Platonov, Platon Mikhalitch

## Character Profile

Platon Mikhalitch Platonov is a **young [[landownership|landowner]]** introduced in Part II of [[dead-souls|Dead Souls]]. He is strikingly handsome: "a tall, handsome man, with extremely red hair" （014-052）, possessed of "good looks, his upright, picturesque figure, his appearance of fresh, unwasted youthfulness, and the boyish purity, innocence, and clarity of his features" （014-064）. Yet his beauty is a surface without depth: "Neither passion nor care nor aught of the nature of agitation or anxiety of mind had ventured to touch his unsullied face, or to lay a single wrinkle thereon. Yet the touch of life which those emotions might have imparted was wanting. The face was, as it were, dreaming" （014-064）.

This absence of inner life is Platonov's defining trait. Despite his wealth — he and his brother own "ten thousand desiatins of land, and over a thousand [[dead-souls|souls]]" （014-068） — he is afflicted with a **profound and inescapable ennui**. When [[chichikov|Chichikov]] expresses bewilderment that such a man could find life wearisome, Platonov replies: "Believe me when I say that, for the sake of a diversion, I should be glad of ANY sort of an anxiety. Would that some enemy would conceive a grudge against me! But no one does so. Everything remains eternally dull" （014-066）. He would welcome a threat, a crisis, even an enemy — anything to puncture the suffocating emptiness of his existence.

Platonov is a **study in unearned privilege**. His [[landownership|estate]] is managed entirely by his capable brother; he has no occupation, no ambition, and no responsibilities. During the boating excursion on the river, while others are moved by the peasants' singing, "only Platon reflected: 'What is there so splendid in these melancholy songs? They do but increase one's depression of spirits'" （014-105）. He is impervious to the very experiences that might shake him from his lethargy.

## Role in Dead Souls

Platonov serves as a **foil to [[chichikov|Chichikov]]** and a representative of a particular [[russian-empire|Russia]]n type. Where [[chichikov|Chichikov]] is all restless energy and scheming, Platonov is all stasis and indifference. Gogol uses him to explore the **pathology of the superfluous man** — a figure who would become central to [[russian-empire|Russia]]n literature (Oblomov, the Underground Man) but who appears here in an early form. Platonov has everything — youth, health, beauty, wealth — and none of it matters, because he has nothing to want and nothing to fear.

[[chichikov|Chichikov]], always alert to advantage, sees Platonov as a potential traveling companion who could share expenses. He proposes travel as a cure for ennui, and Platonov — desperate for any change — agrees immediately: "'Then here is my hand on it. Let us be off at once.' Platon seemed suddenly to have come to life again" （014-103）. This momentary animation is the closest Platonov comes to genuine enthusiasm.

In subsequent chapters, Platonov accompanies [[chichikov|Chichikov]] to visit [[khlobuev|Khlobuev]] and other [[landownership|landowners]], serving as a silent witness to [[purchase-of-dead-souls|Chichikov's scheme]]s. His presence allows Gogol to contrast [[chichikov|Chichikov]]'s restless acquisitiveness with the aimless drift of the gentry who already possess everything [[chichikov|Chichikov]] is striving to acquire.

## Relationships

- **Konstantin ([[kostanzhoglo|Kostanzhoglo]])** — Platonov's brother-in-law, an energetic and capable [[landownership|estate]] manager who handles all practical affairs, leaving Platonov with nothing to do （014-068）
- **Platonov's brother (Vasili)** — Joint owner of the [[landownership|estate]]; described as resembling Platonov "as one pea resembles another," though Vasili is more robust and active
- **[[chichikov|Chichikov]]** — The traveler who persuades Platonov to join him on the road, seeing in him a convenient companion to share expenses （014-096–014-103）
- **[[peter-petrovitch-pietukh|Pietukh]]** — The aggressively hospitable host whose excessive [[hospitality-customs|feasting]] provides a comic contrast to Platonov's listlessness （014-048–014-113）
