# Manilovka

**Manilovka** is the country [[landownership|estate]] of [[Manilov]], the first [[landownership|landowner]] visited by [[Chichikov, Paul Ivanovitch|Chichikov]] in [[Dead Souls]]. Situated on an isolated rise and open to every wind, the [[landownership|estate]] embodies its owner's character: it aspires to elegance and refinement but achieves only shabby pretension. Gogol's description of Manilovka is among the novel's most sustained exercises in architectural and landscape satire, where every detail — from the "Temple of Solitary Thought" to the green-coated pond — reflects [[manilov|Manilov]]'s sentimental indolence.

## Description

Manilovka is reached via a confusing exchange with a peasant who distinguishes "Manilovka" from a nonexistent "ZAmanilovka" [002-001]. The approach reveals the [[landownership|estate]]'s defining contradiction: a "two-storied stone mansion" that attempts grandeur but sits on "an isolated rise and was open to every wind that blew" [002-011]. Gogol immediately undercuts any claim to distinction:

> "Not many people would have admired the situation of Manilov's abode, for it stood on an isolated rise and was open to every wind that blew. On the slope of the rise lay closely-mown turf, while, disposed here and there, after the English fashion, were flower-beds containing clumps of lilac and yellow acacia." [002-011]

The [[landownership|estate]]'s centrepiece is the arbour — an absurd monument to [[manilov|Manilov]]'s pretensions:

> "Also, there were a few insignificant groups of slender-leaved, pointed-tipped birch trees, with, under two of the latter, an arbour having a shabby green cupola, some blue-painted wooden supports, and the inscription 'This is the Temple of Solitary Thought.'" [002-011]

The name is pure [[manilov|Manilov]]: grandiose, vaguely intellectual, and utterly divorced from the shabby reality. Lower down the slope lies "a green-coated pond — green-coated ponds constitute a frequent spectacle in the gardens of [[russian-empire|Russia]]n [[landownership|landowners]]" [002-011] — the parenthetical aside is characteristic Gogol, simultaneously describing and satirising.

The village itself consists of "mouldy, log-built huts" stret[[table-of-ranks|chin]]g to over two hundred, with "nowhere... a single leaf of vegetation or a single stick of timber" beyond the logs themselves [002-011]. Two peasant women wade in the pond, dragging a ragged fishing-net and quarrelling. In the distance, a "faint, dusky blur of pinewood" [002-011] completes the scene.

Gogol ties the landscape to the sky itself: "even the weather was in keeping with the surroundings, since the day was neither clear nor dull, but of the grey tint which may be noted in uniforms of garrison soldiers which have seen long service" [002-011]. This is the Manilovka aesthetic: nothing definite, nothing committed, everything suspended in a haze of good intentions.

## Role in Dead Souls

Manilovka serves as the opening station in [[chichikov|Chichikov]]'s circuit of [[landownership|landowner]] visits. It is here that [[chichikov|Chichikov]] first broaches his scheme to purchase [[dead-souls|dead soul]]s — the "[[dead-souls|revision souls]]" (*revizskiye dushi*) who have died since the last [[revision-census|census]] but remain on the tax rolls [002-045]. [[manilov|Manilov]], after a moment of bewildered pipe-dropping, gives his [[dead-souls|dead soul]]s away for free, establishing a baseline against which all subsequent transactions are measured.

The [[landownership|estate]]'s function in the novel is fundamentally **indexical**: Manilovka *is* [[manilov|Manilov]]. The neglected garden mirrors his neglected affairs; the "Temple of Solitary Thought" mirrors his fondness for vague philosophising; the indeterminate grey weather mirrors his indeterminate character. Gogol's narrator notes that [[manilov|Manilov]] "never rode into the country" to inspect his lands [002-015], and the [[landownership|estate]]'s half-finished condition — neither cultivated nor wild, neither grand nor humble — is the spatial expression of his idleness.
