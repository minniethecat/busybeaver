# Alexandra Ivanovna Khanasarov

## Character Profile

Alexandra Ivanovna Khanasarov (also referred to as Madame Khanasarova or Madam Khanasarov) is the aunt of the impoverished [[landownership|landowner]] [[khlobuev|Khlobuev]] and a person of considerable fortune in Part II of [[dead-souls|Dead Souls]]. She resides in the county town [015-073], removed from the rural [[landownership|estate]]s that dominate the novel's geography.

[[khlobuev|Khlobuev]] describes her character to [[chichikov|Chichikov]] and [[platonov|Platon]] in unmistakable terms: "My aunt is of a very stubborn disposition — a perfect stone of a woman" [015-073]. Her stubbornness is not merely a personality trait — it carries direct financial consequences for her nephew. She is surrounded by a circle of favourites and hangers-on who compete for her favour, including, as [[khlobuev|Khlobuev]] notes, "a fellow who is aiming for a [[governor|Governor]]ship, and to that end has managed to insinuate himself into the circle of her kinsfolk" [015-073].

Despite her wealth, she is not depicted as actively cruel or malicious. Her household generates its own troubles, as [[chichikov|Chichikov]] later learns: "troubles came to me from Madame Khanasarova" [015-163], a character remarks, suggesting that her domestic affairs involve legal or administrative entanglements. This is consistent with her role as a woman of property in provincial [[russian-empire|Russia]] — her wealth attracts petitioners, litigants, and fortune-seekers in equal measure.

## Role in Dead Souls

Alexandra Ivanovna Khanasarov serves a structural function in Part II of the novel: she represents the concentration of wealth and property in the hands of a stubborn, elderly [[landownership|landowner]] who is unmoved by familial obligation. Her refusal to assist [[khlobuev|Khlobuev]] is one of the narrative mechanisms that keeps him in his downward spiral. When [[chichikov|Chichikov]] and [[platonov|Platon]] suggest that [[khlobuev|Khlobuev]] apply to his wealthy aunt for relief, [[khlobuev|Khlobuev]] answers flatly that "nothing is to be looked for from that quarter" [015-073], citing her stubbornness and the competing claimants already encircling her.

The character also illustrates a broader theme of Part II: the dysfunction of inheritance and property transfer in provincial [[russian-empire|Russia]]. Her immense [[landownership|estate]] — described after her death as "the immense property which Madam Khanasarov had left behind her" [015-191] — becomes the object of a feeding frenzy. Upon her death, every sort of claimant descends: "chancery courts and pettifogging solicitors appeared, and every variety of individual flocked" [015-191] to claim a share. This posthumous scramble serves as a darkly comic set piece on the rapaciousness of provincial [[officialdom|official]]dom.

Alexandra Ivanovna's stubbornness during life and the chaos after her death together form a pointed commentary on the absence of orderly succession in the world Gogol depicts — a world where property rarely passes smoothly from one generation to the next.
