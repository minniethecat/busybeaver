# Abakum Thirov

Abakum Thirov is a deceased [[serfdom|serf]] whose name appears on the list of [[purchase-of-dead-souls|dead souls purchase]]d by [[Chichikov, Paul Ivanovitch|Chichikov]]. Though known only from two paragraphs, he occasions one of the novel's most evocative passages — [[chichikov|Chichikov]]'s sudden imaginative leap from a name on a page to a vision of life on the Volga River.

## Character Profile

Nothing is known of Thirov's actual life. His name appears in the [[serfdom|serf]] registry that [[chichikov|Chichikov]] reviews after completing his purchases at the Civil Court. Unlike other [[serfdom|serfs]] on the list, for whom [[sobakevich|Sobakevich]] has provided paternal and maternal details, Thirov is presented without biographical annotation — only his name survives.

What makes Thirov significant is the meditation his name provokes. [[chichikov|Chichikov]] pauses and addresses the dead [[serfdom|serf]] directly: "'Abakum Thirov,' what of YOU, brother? Where, and in what capacity, are YOU disporting yourself? Have you gone to the Volga country, and become bitten with the life of freedom, and joined the fishermen of the river?" [007-007].

The narrator then breaks in to observe [[chichikov|Chichikov]] "relapsed into silent meditation" and asks: "Of what was he thinking as he sat there? Was he thinking of the fortunes of Abakum Thirov, or was he meditating as meditates every [[russian-empire|Russia]]n when his thoughts once turn to the joys of an emancipated existence?" [007-008].

## Role in Dead Souls

Thirov's role is less that of a character than of a trigger for the novel's thematic concerns. The movement from a dry bureaucratic list to [[chichikov|Chichikov]]'s vision of Volga fishermen represents in miniature one of Gogol's central techniques — the sudden expansion from the trivial to the sublime, from accounting to poetry.

Unlike the [[serfdom|serf]]-craftsmen ([[michiev]], [[maksim-teliatnikov]], [[milushkin]]) whose skills [[sobakevich|Sobakevich]] enumerates in detail, Thirov is pure possibility. His name releases an image of freedom — the open river, the fishermen's life — that stands in sharp contrast to the enclosed world of the Civil Court and the grubby business of buying [[dead-souls|dead soul]]s.

The narrator's final question — was [[chichikov|Chichikov]] thinking of Thirov specifically, or of "the joys of an emancipated existence" as any [[russian-empire|Russia]]n would? — draws the reader into complicity with the meditation. Thirov's particular identity dissolves into a universal [[russian-empire|Russia]]n yearning for freedom, making him one of the most symbolically charged minor figures in the novel.
