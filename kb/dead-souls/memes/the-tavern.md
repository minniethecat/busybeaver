# The Tavern

## Description

"The little wooden tavern, with its narrow but hospitable curtained doorway,"
stands on the highroad between the [[landownership|estate]]s of Korobochka's village and
[[nozdrevs-estate|Nozdrev's property]] [004-002]. It is the archetypal [[russian-empire|Russia]]n roadside
establishment — one of those "[[russian-empire|Russia]]n taverns" whose oilcloth-covered cushions
"resemble nothing so much as bricks" [001-004].

The tavern serves as a [[post-house|way-station]] for travellers changing horses, taking
refreshment, and encountering chance acquaintances. When [[chichikovs-arrival|Chichikov arrives]], he
orders "a sucking-pig with horse-radish and sour cream" — a meal that
occupies him thoroughly while the britchka's horses are being changed [004-002].

It is at this tavern, while [[chichikov|Chichikov]] is dining, that [[nozdrev|Nozdrev]]'s light britchka
draws up to the door [004-005]. [[nozdrev|Nozdrev]], returning from a fair where he has
"lost every farthing," bursts in with [[porphyri|Porphyri]] and his brother-in-law
[[mizhuev|Mizhuev]], recognizing [[chichikov|Chichikov]] with characteristic ebullience [004-005]. The
encounter at the tavern initiates the entire [[nozdrev|Nozdrev]] episode, leading [[chichikov|Chichikov]]
to [[nozdrev|Nozdrev]]'s [[landownership|estate]] and the disastrous chess game over [[dead-souls|dead soul]]s.

Throughout the novel, the tavern recurs as a symbol of the road's unstable
continuity — a place of temporary refuge where the traveller pauses between
episodes, never fully at rest. Gogol's narrator notes that "everyone you meet is
a wayfarer like yourself" [007-004], and the tavern, as the road's indoor
mirror, embodies this communal transience.

## Role in Dead Souls

The tavern functions as the novel's threshold space — a liminal zone between
the ordered (if decaying) world of the [[landownership|estate]]s and the chaotic freedom of the
road. It is here, not at an [[landownership|estate]], that [[chichikov|Chichikov]]'s most volatile encounter
begins.

[[nozdrev|Nozdrev]]'s appearance at the tavern is perfectly characteristic: he arrives
unannounced, immediately embraces [[chichikov|Chichikov]] as a friend, and within moments is
pressing him to come to his [[landownership|estate]]. The tavern setting — informal, public,
detached from domestic propriety — enables [[nozdrev|Nozdrev]]'s unchecked sociability and
[[chichikov|Chichikov]]'s reluctant acquiescence.

The tavern also appears in the novel's wider symbolic economy. It is mentioned
as a corrupting influence on [[serfdom|serfs]], who "resort to the village tavern" against
their masters' wishes [012-005]. [[nozdrev|Nozdrev]] himself blames "tavern-keepers" for
selling liquor that poisons the peasantry [014-010]. This makes the tavern a
doubled space — a convenience for the travelling gentry, a temptation for the
stationary peasant.

## Appearances

- **Chapter 1** [001-004]: Passing mention — the tavern's brick-hard cushions
  as a metonym for [[russian-empire|Russia]]n roadside life.
- **Chapter 4** [004-001–004-010]: The pivotal encounter. [[chichikov|Chichikov]] dines at
  the tavern; [[nozdrev|Nozdrev]] arrives from the fair and persuades him to visit
  [[nozdrev|Nozdrev]]'s [[landownership|estate]].
- **Chapter 6** [006-001]: Passing mention — the tavern and the highroad as
  alternative objects of desire for [[serfdom|serfs]] contemplating escape.
- **Chapter 7** [007-004–007-005]: Meditations on the road and the tavern as
  the traveller's inevitable destination.
