# The Garden

## Description

Gardens appear throughout [[dead-souls|Dead Souls]] as mirrors of their owners' [[dead-souls|souls]]. The
municipal gardens of the [[town-of-n|town of N.]], introduced in the opening pages, set the tone:
they contain "only a few wretched trees, propped with stakes" — an attempt at
cultivation so meagre that it barely qualifies as a garden at all [001-005]. This
early image of [[officialdom|official]] horticulture, starved and stunted, becomes a visual metaphor
for the provincial world Gogol is about to anatomize.

On the [[landownership|estate]]s, gardens range from the useful to the neglected. [[manilovka|Manilovka]]'s garden
is described in passing — a "frequent spectacle in the gardens of [[russian-empire|Russia]]n
[[landownership|landowners]]," its shrubbery clipped into the shapes of sentimental fashion [002-011].
Korobochka's garden is strictly practical: "a kitchen garden containing cabbages,
onions, potatoes, beetroots, and other household vegetables," the planting driven by
subsistence rather than aesthetics [003-060]. At [[plushkin|Plushkin]]'s [[landownership|estate]], the garden has
decayed into a Gothic wilderness — overgrown, tangled, and suggestive of the
[[landownership|landowner]]'s own moral collapse.

The gardens of Part II are more ambitious. The "narrow garden in front" of one
house, with its "tall wooden fence," [009-001] and the "Englishified gardens and
parterres and other conceits" that appear later signal the incursion of European
taste into the [[russian-empire|Russia]]n countryside [014-133]. Gogol treats these with
characteristic irony — the English garden is a foreign import, at odds with the
[[russian-empire|Russia]]n soil it attempts to reshape. Yet a "small fruit garden containing various
bushes" also appears, modest and productive, suggesting an alternative model of
cultivation that is neither pretentious nor derelict [011-025].

## Role in Dead Souls

The garden is Gogol's most flexible spatial symbol. It can signify order (the
kitchen garden), aspiration (the English parterre), neglect ([[plushkin|Plushkin]]'s overgrown
[[landownership|estate]]), or [[officialdom|official]] inadequacy (the town's propped saplings). As such, it functions
as an index of character: you can read a [[landownership|landowner]]'s moral condition from the state
of his garden as reliably as from the state of his [[serfdom|serfs]].

The garden also embodies Gogol's ambivalence about the civilizing project. The
impulse to shape nature — to plant, prune, and arrange — is, in one sense, the
impulse of culture itself. But in [[dead-souls|Dead Souls]], cultivation always teeters on the edge
of its opposite: the over-manicured shrubbery of [[manilovka|Manilovka]] is as spiritually vacant
as [[plushkin|Plushkin]]'s wilderness. The garden, like the [[russian-empire|Russia]]n soul Gogol is forever trying
to locate, resists stable interpretation.

The association of gardens with feminine domesticity — the "flower garden" that
appears alongside the kitchen garden, the "star-lit night beyond the bedroom" seen
through a glass door opening onto the garden — introduces a rare note of lyricism
into the otherwise satiric texture of the novel [014-244][014-249].

## Appearances

- **Chapter 1**: The municipal gardens of the [[town-of-n|town of N.]] — "only a few wretched
  trees" [001-005].
- **Chapter 2**: The shrubbery at [[manilovka|Manilovka]], typical of [[russian-empire|Russia]]n [[landownership|landowners]]' garden
  fashion [002-011].
- **Chapter 3**: Korobochka's kitchen garden — cabbages, onions, potatoes, and a
  wall collision with a britchka shaft [003-025][003-060]. The sparrows driven
  away from the garden [003-122].
- **Chapter 4**: The "bouquet of a rose garden" used as a sensory comparison at
  [[nozdrev|Nozdrev]]'s [[landownership|estate]] [004-021].
- **Chapter 9 (Part II)**: The "tall wooden fence and narrow garden" in front of
  a house [009-001].
- **Chapter 11 (Part II)**: The "small fruit garden containing various bushes"
  [011-025].
- **Chapter 14 (Part II)**: The kitchen garden road, the "Englishified gardens and
  parterres," and the glass door opening onto the garden at night [014-019]
  [014-133][014-244][014-249].
