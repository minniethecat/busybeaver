# Korobotchka

**Nastasya Petrovna Korobotchka** is a provincial [[russian-empire|Russia]]n widow-[[landownership|landowner]] and the second target of [[Chichikov, Paul Ivanovitch|Chichikov]]'s [[purchase-of-dead-souls|dead souls scheme]]. She is introduced in Chapter 3 of [[Dead Souls]] after [[chichikov|Chichikov]] loses his way in a storm while travelling from [[Manilov]]'s [[landownership|estate]] to [[Sobakevitch]]'s. Unlike the other [[landownership|landowners]], Korobotchka is defined by a rigid, suspicious narrow-mindedness — Gogol's narrator calls her "cudgel-headed" (*dubinnogolovaya*), incapable of grasping any idea beyond her immediate domestic economy. Though she eventually sells [[chichikov|Chichikov]] her [[dead-souls|dead soul]]s, her later appearance in town to inquire about the market price becomes the catalyst that unravels his entire scheme.

## Character Profile

Korobotchka is one of the "small" [[landownership|landowners]] — a widow of a Collegiate Secretary who owns about eighty [[dead-souls|souls]] [003-075, 007-001]. Gogol introduces her through the lens of her domestic world: her parlour is "hung with old striped curtains, and ornamented with pictures of birds and small, antique mirrors" behind which are tucked "either a letter or an old pack of cards or a stocking" [003-028].

She belongs to a distinct Gogolian type:

> "She belonged to that class of lady landowners who are for ever lamenting failures of the harvest and their losses thereby; to the class who, drooping their heads despondently, are all the while stuffing money into striped purses, which they keep hoarded in the drawers of cupboards. Into one purse they will stuff rouble pieces, into another half roubles, and into a third tchetvertachki." [003-028]

Her [[landownership|estate]] reflects her personality: neat, productive, and utterly without aesthetic ambition. Behind the house lie "a kitchen garden containing cabbages, onions, potatoes, beetroots, and other household vegetables" [003-053], and scattered about are "apple trees and other fruit trees protected with nets against magpies and sparrows" [003-053]. Every item serves a practical purpose; nothing exists for beauty alone.

Korobotchka's defining trait is a dense, almost impenetrable suspicion. When [[chichikov|Chichikov]] first broaches the idea of purchasing [[dead-souls|dead soul]]s, she reacts not with the sentimental confusion of [[Manilov]] or the shrewd calculation of [[Sobakevitch]], but with a fundamental inability to comprehend the transaction:

> "To tell the truth, never at any time have I sold dead folk. And though I did sell some live ones three years ago to the Protopopov — two girls at a hundred roubles each — they were very grateful for them, and turned out first-class workers. And the Protopopov himself told me that they could both read and write, and could feed the young gentlemen of his house — but I never have sold DEAD folk." [003-112]

This speech reveals everything: her world is bounded by what she can see, count, and verify. Dead [[dead-souls|souls]] are abstractions; live servant girls are assets with measurable utility. She fears being cheated precisely because she cannot measure the value of what [[chichikov|Chichikov]] wants to buy.

## Role in Dead Souls

Korobotchka serves two crucial narrative functions in Gogol's novel.

First, she represents a specific social type — the "cudgel-headed" provincial [[landownership|landowner]] whose mind cannot stretch beyond her immediate experience. When [[chichikov|Chichikov]] grows frustrated and calls her "a cursed old woman" [003-137], Gogol's narrator steps back to observe:

> "Certainly you might begin to think that a man, once he has got an idea into his head, may be just such another as Madam Korobotchka." [003-112]

This is the novel's satirical method in miniature: the [[landownership|landowner]]'s absurdity reflects a universal human limitation.

Second, and more consequentially, Korobotchka is the **inadvertent destroyer** of [[purchase-of-dead-souls|Chichikov's scheme]]. After the transaction, she grows anxious that she may have sold too cheaply. She travels to the [[town-of-n|town of N.]] to discover "the current price of [[dead-souls|dead soul]]s" [008-036, 009-016–009-022]. Her inquiries reach the ears of the town's ladies, who transform the [[provincial-scandal|rumor]] into increasingly fantastic versions — [[chichikov|Chichikov]] is a kidnapper, a forger, even Napoleon in disguise. The [[provincial-scandal|gossip]] culminates in the collapse of [[chichikov|Chichikov]]'s social standing and his flight from the town [009-050, 010-*].

It is a masterful irony: the least intelligent of [[chichikov|Chichikov]]'s targets proves to be his undoing. The "cudgel-headed" widow accomplishes what the shrewd [[Sobakevitch]] and the worldly [[Nozdrev]] could not.

## Relationships

- **[[Chichikov, Paul Ivanovitch|Chichikov]]** — Negotiates the purchase of eighteen [[dead-souls|dead soul]]s. Korobotchka drives him to exasperation with her inability to grasp the transaction, but he eventually succeeds by exploiting her superstition — pretending to be a government contractor [003-137]. [[chichikov|Chichikov]] later refers to her dismissively as a "cudgel-headed" woman.
- **[[Manilov]]** — Contrast: [[manilov|Manilov]] gives his [[dead-souls|dead soul]]s away for free; Korobotchka bargains obsessively.
- **[[Sobakevitch]]** — Fellow [[landownership|landowner]]. Both drive hard bargains, but Sobakevitch does so from shrewd calculation, Korobotchka from fear of being cheated.
- **The Archpriest's wife** — Confidante in town to whom Korobotchka reveals her anxiety about the transaction price [009-016–009-022].
- **The Protopopov** — A previous buyer who purchased two of Korobotchka's live [[serfdom|serf]]-girls at "a hundred roubles each" [003-112].

## Critical Reception

Korobotchka has been analysed as Gogol's study of *poshlost* (banality, spiritual vulgarity) in its feminine, domestic form. Where [[Manilov]] represents the *poshlost* of vague sentiment and [[Plushkin]] the *poshlost* of avarice, Korobotchka embodies the *poshlost* of narrow practicality — a mind so completely absorbed in cabbages and striped purses that it cannot perceive anything beyond.

The [[russian-empire|Russia]]n Formalist critic Viktor Shklovsky noted that Korobotchka's characteristic speech pattern — repeating, hedging, returning obsessively to domestic detail — is a structural device: Gogol uses the "retardation" of her dialogue to build comic tension before the transaction's resolution.

Her later role in the plot (Ch. 8–9) has been praised as an early example of the "butterfly effect" in narrative: a trivial action by a minor character sets in motion the chain of events that destroys the protagonist.
