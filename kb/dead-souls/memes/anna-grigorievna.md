# Anna Grigorievna

## Character Profile

Anna Grigorievna is a member of the provincial society of the [[town-of-n|town of N.]], introduced in Chapter 9 of [[dead-souls|Dead Souls]] as the close friend and confidante of the unnamed "agreeable lady" who first hears the rumour of [[purchase-of-dead-souls|Chichikov's scheme]]. She is never physically described — Gogol presents her entirely through the monologue that the agreeable lady delivers upon arriving at her house.

The agreeable lady's breathless narration establishes Anna Grigorievna as an intimate friend. She rushes to Anna Grigorievna's house with the urgency of a courier: "I must be off to tell my friend, Anna Grigorievna, the news" [009-020]. The monologue that follows is a virtuoso piece of comic narration — the agreeable lady relates her horror at hearing of [[chichikov|Chichikov]]'s purchase of [[dead-souls|dead soul]]s, her physical shock ("I trembled beyond belief"), and her state of near-catatonia as she departed for Anna Grigorievna's house, unable to speak to her coachman.

Anna Grigorievna's own words are not directly quoted in the chapter, but her response to the tale is indicated by her friend's direct address: "Oh, Anna Grigorievna, if you but knew how upset I am!" [009-020]. She is positioned as the ideal listener — a sympathetic ear to whom the most sensational [[provincial-scandal|gossip]] can be delivered without restraint.

## Role in Dead Souls

Anna Grigorievna serves as a narrative device in the [[provincial-scandal|gossip]]-propagation ma[[table-of-ranks|chin]]ery of Chapter 9. Her role is structurally precise: she is the first recipient of the rumour chain that will eventually consume the entire town. The agreeable lady delivers the news to her, and the two of them will then reprocess and broadcast it, transforming [[purchase-of-dead-souls|Chichikov's scheme]] — through a series of elaborations and misunderstandings — into the full-blown scandal that dominates the novel's second half.

The chapter is a study in how provincial society manufactures its own reality through [[provincial-scandal|gossip]]. Anna Grigorievna, as the first confidante outside the original household, represents the moment at which private shock becomes public narrative. Without her presence as a trusted listener, the agreeable lady's monologue would be merely interior — with Anna Grigorievna, the rumour acquires a second mind, a second set of interpretations, and the amplification that will make it unstoppable.
