# Thoma Bolshoy

## Character Profile

Thoma Bolshoy is a household servant on the [[landownership|estate]] of [[peter-petrovitch-pietukh|Peter Petrovitch Pietukh]] in Part II of [[dead-souls|Dead Souls]]. His name follows the traditional [[russian-empire|Russia]]n peasant naming pattern — a given name (Thoma, a variant of Foma/Thomas) with the distinguishing epithet "Bolshoy," meaning "the Big" or "the Elder," which differentiates him from other servants named Thoma on the same [[landownership|estate]].

Gogol introduces Thoma Bolshoy through a series of peremptory commands. During the arrival sequence at [[peter-petrovitch-pietukh|Pietukh]]'s [[landownership|estate]], he is called out by name: "Don't bear so hard on it, Thoma Bolshoy! Go where Thoma Menshov is, and roll the barrel" [014-008]. Later, as visitors approach the gate, the order comes: "Run, you lout of a Thoma Bolshoy, and open the gate for him" [014-019]. The epithet "lout" and the bare imperatives establish Thoma Bolshoy's position at the bottom of the [[landownership|estate]] hierarchy.

His physical description is brief but vivid: "Thereupon the barelegged Thoma Bolshoy, clad in nothing but a shirt" [014-020] runs to open the gate. The image of a barefoot servant in a single garment, dashing to obey yet another command, encapsulates the condition of the [[russian-empire|Russia]]n [[serfdom|serf]] — perpetually available, minimally provided for, and addressed with casual contempt even by the standards of a household that prides itself on hospitality.

## Role in Dead Souls

Thoma Bolshoy is one of many minor peasant and servant characters who populate the background of [[dead-souls|Dead Souls]]. He never speaks; he is only spoken to, and always in the imperative mood. His function is to provide texture to the portrayal of [[peter-petrovitch-pietukh|Pietukh]]'s [[landownership|estate]] — a place of abundant [[hospitality-customs|feasting]] and aggressive hospitality, but one built on the labor of men like Thoma Bolshoy who are ordered about barefoot.

The contrast between Thoma Bolshoy's bare legs and the lavish banquet that [[peter-petrovitch-pietukh|Pietukh]] later serves to his guests is one of the many small ironies through which Gogol exposes the moral economy of [[serfdom|serf]]dom. The [[landownership|estate]]'s hospitality — praised at length by its owner — rests on the invisible labor of servants who are treated as interchangeable ("Go where Thoma Menshov is") and addressed as "lout."
