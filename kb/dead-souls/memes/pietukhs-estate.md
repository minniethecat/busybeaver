# Pietukh's Estate

## Description

[[peter-petrovitch-pietukh|Pietukh]]'s [[landownership|estate]] is reached by boat across a river, an approach that
distinguishes it from the land-bound [[landownership|estate]]s of Part I [014-006]. The house
itself is spacious and lively, dominated by the kitchen and the dining-room
rather than by offices or parlours. The staff — including the "gawk" Emelian
and the "thief" Antoshka — are named with affectionate abuse, "merely to spur
them to greater activity" [014-025]. The host "was no lover of abuse, but,
rather, a kind-hearted man who, like most [[russian-empire|Russia]]ns, could not get on without a
sharp word or two" [014-025].

The [[landownership|estate]]'s defining feature is its table. From the moment guests arrive,
[[peter-petrovitch-pietukh|Pietukh]] issues "behests" to the kitchen with gustatory fervour: fish pasties,
sturgeon, "a four-cornered fish pie" containing the cheeks of a sturgeon, and "a
spare-rib of veal done to a turn" [014-025, 014-027]. When [[chichikov|Chichikov]] and [[platonov|Platon]]
attempt to refuse additional helpings, [[peter-petrovitch-pietukh|Pietukh]] counters each refusal with a
folk proverb: "Without a mate, neither man nor bird can live in this world," and
"What is the good of two? God loves a trinity" [014-026].

[[kostanzhoglo|Kostanzhoglo]] later remarks dismissively that "any fool of a Peter Petrovitch
[[peter-petrovitch-pietukh|Pietukh]] now ranks as a good [[landownership|landowner]]" [014-020] — a verdict that captures
[[peter-petrovitch-pietukh|Pietukh]]'s reputation for abundance without productive substance. The [[landownership|estate]]
consumes rather than generates wealth, its hospitality a form of conspicuous
expenditure.

## Role in Dead Souls

[[peter-petrovitch-pietukh|Pietukh]]'s [[landownership|estate]] represents the gastronomic extreme of [[russian-empire|Russia]]n landowning life.
Where [[manilovka|Manilovka]] dreams in idle speculation and [[plushkin|Plushkin]]'s [[landownership|estate]] starves in
miserly accumulation, Pietukh's household feasts in perpetual consumption. His
hospitality is genuine — he is "a kind-hearted, fussy fellow" — but it is
also compulsive, as though the sheer volume of food could substitute for the
[[landownership|estate]]'s lack of any other productive purpose [014-026].

The visit to [[peter-petrovitch-pietukh|Pietukh]]'s occurs during [[chichikov|Chichikov]]'s Part II tour with [[platonov|Platon]].
[[chichikov|Chichikov]] uses the visit to recruit [[nikolasha|Nikolasha]] and Aleksasha, [[peter-petrovitch-pietukh|Pietukh]]'s two sons
(students home from the Gymnasium), as potential companions in his schemes
[014-007]. The episode thus links the [[landownership|estate]]'s overabundance with the novel's
ongoing theme of idle youth — boys who "dream of Moscow" while their father
dreams of the next sturgeon.

The [[landownership|estate]] contrasts pointedly with [[kostanzhoglo|Kostanzhoglo]]'s model farm. Where
[[kostanzhoglo|Kostanzhoglo]] generates wealth through rational management, [[peter-petrovitch-pietukh|Pietukh]] simply
expends it through hospitality. The juxtaposition reflects Part II's concern
with distinguishing productive economic activity from the mere display of
prosperity.
