# Chichikov's Early Career

## Summary

[[chichikov|Chichikov]]'s early career is narrated in Chapter 11 as a backstory that explains how the hero of the [[purchase-of-dead-souls|dead souls scheme]] came to be. He is born into a family of impoverished gentry and sent to a local school, where his father delivers the advice that will shape his life: "Guard and save every kopeck." At school, young [[pavlushka|Pavlushka]] distinguishes himself not through academic brilliance but through frugality and the art of pleasing his teachers. He receives a diploma inscribed "For Exemplary Diligence and the Perfection of Good Conduct."

His father dies around the time [[chichikov|Chichikov]] finishes school, leaving an inheritance of "four waistcoats completely worn out, two ancient frockcoats, and a small sum of money." [[chichikov|Chichikov]] sells the old house for a thousand roubles and moves to the capital to enter the Civil Service. He secures a minor post at a salary of thirty or forty roubles a year — wretched by any standard — but he determines to overcome all obstacles through "strict attention to business."

His rise comes through a calculated campaign of personal service. He identifies the one figure who can advance him: a graven Chief Clerk, elderly and unapproachable, whose face suggests that "the Devil has walked by night to grind peas." [[chichikov|Chichikov]] begins by tending to trivial needs — cleaning pens, dusting tables, brushing the Chief Clerk's back. When these go unnoticed, he investigates the man's domestic life and discovers a marriageable daughter. He contrives to meet her at church, wins the family's favor, moves into the Chief Clerk's house, calls him "Papenka," and kisses his hand. The scheme succeeds: [[chichikov|Chichikov]] is promoted to Chief Clerk, and a wedding seems imminent — until he quietly detaches himself once the promotion is secured.

- [[chichikov-paul-ivanovitch]] — the ambitious young provincial, learning to navigate [[officialdom|bureaucracy]]

- The Chief Clerk — [[chichikov|Chichikov]]'s first bureaucratic superior, described as "a graven image of elderly insensibility and inertia"
- The Chief Clerk's daughter — the pockmarked young woman [[chichikov|Chichikov]] courts for professional advantage

## Significance

[[chichikov|Chichikov]]'s early career establishes the psychological template for all his later schemes. The father's deathbed advice — "Guard and save every kopeck" — becomes the iron law of his existence, overriding even natural sympathy: when his old schoolmaster falls into destitution, [[chichikov|Chichikov]] contributes a single five-kopeck piece, which his schoolfellows throw back in his face. The Chief Clerk episode reveals his method: identify the gatekeeper, study his needs, make himself indispensable, then move on once the objective is achieved. Gogol presents [[chichikov|Chichikov]] neither as a villain nor a hero but as a product of a system that rewards patient servility followed by calculated exploitation. The pathos of the episode — the schoolmaster weeping "Ah, [[pavlushka|Pavlushka]], how a human being may become changed!" — introduces the novel's persistent question: what happens to a soul that treats every relationship as a transaction?

## Context

The early career narrative occupies the first portion of [[chichikov|Chichikov]]'s Chapter 11 backstory. It precedes the Building Commission episode and the [[chichikovs-dismissal-from-customs|customs scandal]], forming the first act of a three-part career arc. This phase differs from the later ones in its modesty of scale: the stakes are measured in roubles and clerkships, not mansions and millions. Yet the pattern — scrupulous patience followed by audacious exploitation — is identical. The episode also introduces [[chichikov|Chichikov]]'s peculiar relationship to money: not miserly hoarding for its own sake, but accumulation in service of a "vision of life and its amenities — carriages, an elegantly furnished house, and recherche dinners."
