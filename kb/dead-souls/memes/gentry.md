# Gentry

## Explanation

The **gentry** (дворянство, *dvorianstvo*) was the privileged landowning class of [[russian-empire|Imperial Russia]], legally defined by ownership of inhabited [[landownership|estate]]s ([[serfdom|serfs]]) and entitlement to rank in the [[table-of-ranks|Table of Ranks]]. By Gogol's time, the gentry was a diverse class — ranging from wealthy magnates with thousands of [[serfdom|serfs]] to impoverished nobles clinging to a few dozen [[dead-souls|souls]] and a decaying manor house.

Membership in the gentry could be hereditary (through birth) or personal (through attainment of a certain rank in state service). This distinction created tension between old landed families and the new service nobility — men like [[chichikov|Chichikov]]'s father, who rose through the [[officialdom|bureaucracy]] but never owned land （P04-005）. The gentry's privileges included exemption from personal taxation, freedom from corporal punishment, and exclusive rights to own [[serfdom|serfs]] and vote in local noble assemblies.

Yet the economic foundation of the gentry was precarious. Many [[landownership|estate]]s were mortgaged to the state bank, and the gap between the gentry's social pretensions and their financial reality is one of Gogol's recurring themes. Landowners like [[manilov|Manilov]] maintained the appearance of gentility while their [[landownership|estate]]s decayed from neglect.

## Significance in Dead Souls

Every character of substance in [[dead-souls|Dead Souls]] is a member of the gentry — and Gogol uses them to paint a comprehensive portrait of a class in decline:

- **The absentee landlord**: [[manilov|Manilov]] represents the gentry's detachment from the land that sustains them. He dreams of elegant improvements while his steward steals from him （ch02）.
- **The provincial miser**: Korobochka embodies the gentry reduced to petty anxiety — a "[[landownership|landowner]]'s widow" who manages her small [[landownership|estate]] competently but grasps nothing beyond its boundaries （ch03）.
- **The reckless squanderer**: [[nozdrev|Nozdrev]] is the gentry as perpetual adolescent — gambling, fighting, lying, and treating his [[landownership|estate]] as a playground （ch04）.
- **The brutish pragmatist**: [[sobakevich|Sobakevich]] has no illusions and no ideals — he values everything, including his [[dead-souls|dead serfs]], at its market price （ch05）.
- **The pathological hoarder**: Pliushkin is the gentry at its terminal point — so consumed by acquisition that he has become a beggar in a mansion full of rotting wealth （ch06）.

[[chichikov|Chichikov]] himself aspires to join the gentry — not through birth or service, but through the fraudulent acquisition of "[[dead-souls|souls]]."  His scheme is, in a sense, a satire of gentry aspirations: he wants the *appearance* of landed wealth without its substance.

## Examples from the Text

- **The Higher Ranks** （P04-005） — Gogol discusses how facts "pertaining to the higher ranks of society" might seem incongruous in a book about "men who move in the lower circles," establishing the social hierarchy the novel navigates.

- **The Five Landowners** （ch02–ch06） — Each of [[chichikov|Chichikov]]'s hosts represents a different variant of gentry life — none of them admirable, all of them recognizably human.

- **[[chichikov|Chichikov]]'s Father's Lesson** （011-002） — [[chichikov|Chichikov]] is taught to "save a kopeck" and please his superiors — a petit-bourgeois ethic at odds with gentry ideals of honor and generosity.
