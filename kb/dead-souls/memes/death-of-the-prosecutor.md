# Death of the Public Prosecutor

## Summary

The death of the [[prosecutor|Public Prosecutor]] is the darkly comic culmination of the [[chichikov|Chichikov]] scandal in Chapter 10. As [[provincial-scandal|rumor]]s about [[chichikov|Chichikov]]'s true identity spiral out of control — he is variously suspected of being a forger, a kidnapper, a government spy, and even Napoleon in disguise — the town's [[officialdom|officials]] gather in anxious conferences. The frenzy reaches such a pitch that the [[prosecutor|Public Prosecutor]], overwhelmed by the collective hysteria, suffers a fatal stroke.

[[nozdrev|Nozdrev]] delivers the news to [[chichikov|Chichikov]] with characteristic bluntness: "The [[prosecutor|Public Prosecutor]] even died of it, and is to be buried to-morrow." The narrator adds that this was "true in so far as that, on the previous day, the [[officialdom|official]] in question had had a fatal stroke — probably induced by the excitement of the public meeting." The death is at once absurd and consequential: a man has died not from violence or disease but from the sheer pressure of [[provincial-scandal|provincial gossip]] and bureaucratic panic over the arrival of a new [[governor|Governor]]-General.

## Participants

- The [[prosecutor|Public Prosecutor]] — the victim, a minor [[officialdom|official]] whose death is the ultimate testament to the destructive power of [[provincial-scandal|rumor]]
- [[nozdrev]] — the bringer of the news, delivering the announcement with his usual chaotic candor
- [[chichikov-paul-ivanovitch]] — the unwitting cause of the fatal anxiety
- The town's t[[table-of-ranks|chin]]ovniks — the [[officialdom|officials]] whose panic-fueled meetings create the conditions for the prosecutor's stroke

## Significance

The prosecutor's death embodies Gogol's technique of fusing comedy with the grotesque. The idea that [[provincial-scandal|rumor]] and bureaucratic anxiety could literally kill a man is ludicrous — yet within the novel's logic, it follows with terrible precision. The death serves as a moral bookend to the [[purchase-of-dead-souls|dead souls scheme]]: [[chichikov|Chichikov]] has been buying the names of [[dead-souls|dead serfs]] as legal fictions, but now an actual death results from the scheme's collateral damage. The prosecutor, a man so negligible that "nothing at all" could be said of his character, achieves in death the definition he lacked in life — he becomes a symbol of the system's capacity to consume even its most anonymous functionaries. Gogol's narrator later reflects, during [[chichikovs-encounter-with-funeral|the funeral procession]], that any eulogy of the prosecutor would have to fall back on the observation that "you grew a pair of exceptionally thick eyebrows."

## Context

The prosecutor's death occurs at the height of the panic chronicled in Chapters 9–10. The ladies' [[provincial-scandal|gossip]], Korobochka's anxious inquiry about [[dead-souls|dead soul]]s prices, and [[nozdrev|Nozdrev]]'s public accusations have combined to create an atmosphere of near-hysterical speculation. The death directly precedes [[chichikov|Chichikov]]'s belated realization that he must flee the town. The funeral procession that follows — encountered by [[chichikov|Chichikov]] on his way out of town — becomes the occasion for one of the novel's most famous philosophical digressions, narrated in [[chichikovs-encounter-with-funeral]].
