# Table of Ranks

## Explanation

The **Table of Ranks** (Табель о рангах) was a formal system of classification for [[russian-empire|Imperial Russia]]n state service, instituted by Peter the Great in 1722. It divided all civil, military, and court positions into 14 grades (classes), from the 14th (lowest, Collegiate Registrar) to the 1st (highest, Chancellor). The system was designed to open state service to talent rather than birth, though by Gogol's time (the 1840s), it had ossified into a rigid social hierarchy where one's *chin* (rank) defined one's entire social identity （P04-005）.

Every male of any standing in [[russian-empire|Russia]]n society — [[landownership|landowners]], civil servants, military officers — held or aspired to a rank. Address between individuals was governed by rank difference, with elaborate formulas for how inferiors addressed superiors. A man's rank appeared on all [[officialdom|official]] documents and was often the first thing inquired about when meeting someone new （001-004）.

Crucially, the Table of Ranks intersected with [[serfdom|serf]]dom: [[landownership|landowners]] who held no [[officialdom|official]] rank were, in a sense, outside the state hierarchy even as they dominated the local social order. [[chichikov|Chichikov]] himself is a former civil servant who has left the Table of Ranks behind to pursue wealth through land and [[serfdom|serfs]] rather than bureaucratic advancement.

## Significance in Dead Souls

Gogol's characters cannot be understood without the Table of Ranks. It provides the invisible scaffolding of their social world:

1. **Social navigation**: [[chichikov|Chichikov]]'s entire strategy depends on understanding where each [[landownership|landowner]] and [[officialdom|official]] sits in the social hierarchy. He adjusts his tone, his flattery, and his proposals according to the rank and standing of his interlocutor （001-004）.

2. **The [[town-of-n|provincial town]] as microcosm**: The [[officialdom|officials]] of the [[town-of-n|town of N.]] — the [[governor|Governor]], the [[prosecutor|Public Prosecutor]], the [[postmaster|Postmaster]], the President of the Council — form a complete Table of Ranks in miniature. Their interactions are governed by rank protocol even when they are [[provincial-scandal|gossip]]ing, drinking, or playing cards.

3. **Rank vs. Reality**: Gogol frequently contrasts the formal dignity of rank with the moral vacuity of the rank-holder. Officials who hold respectable grades are revealed to be ignorant, corrupt, or absurd — the rank is a costume that conceals nothing.

The Table of Ranks represents the *[[officialdom|official]]* version of [[russian-empire|Russia]]n society — the one recognized by the state. [[purchase-of-dead-souls|Chichikov's scheme]] operates in the gap between this [[officialdom|official]] world and the actual economic world of land and [[serfdom|serfs]], where different rules apply.

## Examples from the Text

- **Social Identity** （001-004） — Upon arriving in town, [[chichikov|Chichikov]] immediately asks about "the more important functionaries" — his first question is about the governor, the vice-governor, and the public prosecutor, mapping the local Table of Ranks before making any moves.

- **Rank and Deference** （P04-005） — Gogol's Preface discusses how a fact "pertaining to the higher ranks of society would seem incongruous in a book which mainly deals with men who move in the lower circles" — showing that the Table of Ranks structured even literary convention.

- **Civil Service Experience** （011-028） — [[chichikov|Chichikov]]'s own biography reveals that he served in various [[officialdom|official]] positions, mastering the bureaucratic skills that the Table of Ranks rewarded — flattery, document management, and strategic [[bribery|corruption]].
