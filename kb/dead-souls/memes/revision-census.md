# Revision Census

The **revision census** (*reviziya*, *revizskiye skazki*) was the periodic tax census conducted in [[russian-empire|Imperial Russia]] from the early eighteenth century until the abolition of [[Serfdom|serfdom]] in 1861. Its purpose was to count the taxable population — primarily [[serfdom|serfs]], who were referred to in administrative language as "[[dead-souls|souls]]" (*dushi*). Because revisions occurred at irregular intervals (often ten to fifteen years apart), a [[serfdom|serf]] who died between revisions remained on the tax rolls, and the [[landownership|landowner]] continued to owe the *soul tax* (*podushnaya podat*) on that deceased person until the next census corrected the register. It is precisely this legal gap that enables [[purchase-of-dead-souls|Chichikov's scheme]] in [[Dead Souls]]: he purchases the names of [[serfdom|serfs]] who have died since the last revision but who remain, on paper, alive and taxable.

## Mechanism

The revision system was introduced by Peter the Great in 1718, replacing the earlier household-based (*podvornaya*) taxation with a per-capita (*podushnaya*) levy on all males of the taxable [[landownership|estate]]s. Landowners were required to submit lists (*revizskiye skazki*) of the [[serfdom|serfs]] on their property. These lists were then compiled into [[officialdom|official]] registers, and taxes were assessed accordingly.

The crucial inefficiency — and Gogol's satirical lever — was the interval between revisions. A [[landownership|landowner]] whose [[serfdom|serf]] died the day after a revision would pay tax on that "soul" for perhaps a decade or more, until the next census [[officialdom|official]]ly deleted the name. The dead [[serfdom|serf]] remained, in the language of the [[officialdom|bureaucracy]], a "revision soul" (*revizskaya [[soul-ownership|dusha]]*) — legally alive despite being physically dead.

The novel's opening digression spells out the scheme's logic with remarkable clarity:

> "Chichikov may buy dead souls, or railway shares, or he may borrow money from a bank on the 'dead souls' no less than on the living ones." [P03-008]

And more precisely:

> "With this money he would buy an estate and some real life serfs, and then he would borrow money from a bank on the 'dead souls' no less than on the living ones." [P03-005]

The scheme's genius — and its indictment of the system — is that it is perfectly legal. [[chichikov|Chichikov]] is not forging documents; he is exploiting a rationally designed administrative mechanism that happens to produce an irrational result.

## Role in Dead Souls

The revision census is the institutional backbone of Gogol's plot. Every step of [[chichikov|Chichikov]]'s circuit depends on the [[landownership|landowner]]'s relationship to the tax burden created by the revision:

- **[[Manilov]]** gives his [[dead-souls|dead soul]]s away because the tax burden means nothing to him — he does not manage his [[landownership|estate]] [002-045].
- **[[Korobotchka]]** cannot understand why anyone would pay for something that costs her money in taxes, and her incomprehension makes her suspicious [003-099–003-108].
- **[[Sobakevitch]]** understands the system perfectly and exploits it: he bargains aggressively while praising [[dead-souls|dead serfs]] as if alive [005-*].
- **[[Plushkin]]** is the revision's ideal victim: he owns over a thousand [[dead-souls|souls]], many of whom have died or fled, and the tax burden on the dead constitutes a real financial drain on his miserly economy [006-028, 006-031]. His joy at the sale is proportional to his relief from taxation.

[[chichikov|Chichikov]] himself explains the scheme's social benefit to [[korobotchka|Korobotchka]] with a veneer of civic virtue:

> "You will be continuing to pay the tax upon dead souls as though they were still alive. And besides costing you money, the transaction will also save you a great deal of trouble." [003-102]

The irony, of course, is that [[chichikov|Chichikov]]'s "helpful" intervention merely transfers the tax fiction to himself — he plans to mortgage the [[dead-souls|dead soul]]s to a bank, converting a tax liability into fraudulent collateral.

The scheme's registration in Chapter 7 takes place at [[civil-court|the government offices]], where the President of the Council and other [[officialdom|officials]] treat the purchase deeds as routine business [007-001–007-017]. The [[officialdom|bureaucracy]] that created the absurdity is the same [[officialdom|bureaucracy]] that legitimises it. No one questions why a man would buy [[dead-souls|dead serfs]]; the paperwork is in order, and that is sufficient.

## Historical Context

The revision system was one of the most consequential administrative innovations of [[russian-empire|Imperial Russia]]. By tying taxation to individual persons rather than households, it created both a more efficient revenue mechanism and a more rigid system of social control. Serfs became legible to the state as countable, taxable units — "[[dead-souls|souls]]" — a linguistic reduction that Gogol exploits to devastating effect.

Between the first revision (1719) and the tenth (1857–1858), the intervals averaged about fifteen years. During Gogol's lifetime the eighth revision (1833–1835) was conducted, meaning the gap between revisions was a familiar and much-resented feature of provincial life. Contemporary readers would have recognised the [[purchase-of-dead-souls|dead souls scheme]] as a satire not of an obscure legal loophole but of a widely understood absurdity in the tax system.
