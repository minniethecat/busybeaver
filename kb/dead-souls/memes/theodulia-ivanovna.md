# Theodulia Ivanovna

## Character Profile

Theodulia Ivanovna is the wife of the [[landownership|landowner]] [[sobakevich|Sobakevich]] and appears during [[Chichikov, Paul Ivanovitch|Chichikov's]] visit in Chapter 5. She is introduced with minimal ceremony: her husband simply states "This is my wife, Theodulia Ivanovna" [005-014], without elaboration or endearment. Her presence in the scene is almost entirely silent and decorative — she speaks only brief, domestic phrases.

Her physical appearance mirrors her husband's robust solidity. When [[chichikov|Chichikov]] first notices her, she is described as "a very tall lady, wearing a cap adorned with ribbons" [005-017], a figure who moves with the same heavy, deliberate quality that characterizes [[sobakevich|Sobakevich]] himself. She seats herself "as straight as a poker" [005-017], a posture that suggests both formality and a certain rigidity of character.

## Role in Dead Souls

Theodulia Ivanovna functions as an extension of her husband's domestic world rather than as an independent character. Her two spoken contributions to the chapter are purely functional: she invites [[chichikov|Chichikov]] to be seated [005-017], and later prompts her husband with the reminder "Come to luncheon, my dear" [005-034]. These interventions bracket the central business of the chapter — [[sobakevich|Sobakevich]]'s shrewd negotiation over [[dead-souls|dead soul]]s — without affecting it in any way.

Gogol uses Theodulia Ivanovna to reinforce the portrait of [[sobakevich|Sobakevich]] as a man whose domestic life is as coarse and unadorned as his business dealings. There is no affection between husband and wife evident in the text; she is simply another fixture of [[sobakevich|Sobakevich]]'s household, like the "thick-set and sturdy" furniture. Her silence during the [[dead-souls|dead soul]]s negotiation underscores the patriarchal character of the provincial gentry household.
