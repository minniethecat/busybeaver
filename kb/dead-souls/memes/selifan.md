# Selifan

## Character Profile

Selifan is [[chichikov|Chichikov]]'s coachman — a short peasant in a large overcoat
who drives the traveling britchka across the [[russian-empire|Russia]]n provinces
[001-002]. Unlike [[petrushka|Petrushka]] the valet, Selifan has a defined skill:
handling horses, which makes him indispensable to [[chichikov|Chichikov]]'s
peripatetic scheme.

He is long-suffering, accepting his master's rebukes without argument,
and treats his horses as conversational companions, addressing them
with a mixture of affection and philosophical complaint [003-002].

Selifan's defining vice is drink. When intoxicated, he loses his way
and overturns the britchka — an episode that nearly ends [[chichikov|Chichikov]]'s
journey prematurely [003-014]. Yet he remains fundamentally loyal and,
in his own rough manner, competent.

Gogol presents him as the representative [[russian-empire|Russia]]n peasant: uneducated
but possessed of a native cunning, resigned to his station yet capable
of moments of surprising insight.

## Role in Dead Souls

Selifan embodies **the servant class** upon whose labor the entire
gentry world depends. While [[chichikov|Chichikov]] negotiates the purchase of dead
[[dead-souls|souls]], Selifan tends the horses, polishes the brass, and waits — often
for hours in the cold — for his master's return.

Without Selifan, the plot cannot move: every chapter of travel is made
possible by his driving.

He also serves as a **comic foil** to the pretensions of the gentry.
When Selifan drunkenly confuses left and right, crashing into another
carriage, Gogol draws a quiet parallel: [[purchase-of-dead-souls|Chichikov's scheme]] is no less
reckless, and its eventual collapse no less inevitable.

Selifan's muttered philosophy to his horses — "you are a respectable
man when you have eaten your fill" [003-024] — contains a peasant
wisdom that the novel's [[landownership|landowners]] conspicuously lack.
