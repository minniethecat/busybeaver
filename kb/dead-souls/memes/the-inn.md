# The Inn

## Description

The inn of the [[town-of-n|provincial town]] of N. is the first location described in the
novel — [[chichikov|Chichikov]]'s britchka draws up to its door on the opening page
[001-001]. The building is unremarkable in architecture, of the sort "well-known
throughout provincial [[russian-empire|Russia]]" — a wooden structure of two storeys, with the
customary ground-floor shops and an upper floor reached by a narrow wooden
staircase [001-004].

[[chichikov|Chichikov]]'s room is furnished with the standard inventory of provincial inns: a
bed, a table, a chest of drawers, and, behind a screen, the "eternal"
accoutrements of [[russian-empire|Russia]]n travel — a samovar, tea-things, and the traveller's
own portmanteau. The wallpaper is faded and the room bears "the peculiar smell
of fresh paint, old leather, and the recent presence of a human being" — smells
that Gogol catalogues with his characteristic attention to sensory detail
[001-003].

Downstairs, the dining room offers the standard provincial bill of fare: cabbage
soup, a pie of "several weeks' standing," brains with peas, sausages with
cabbage, and a roast duck — the dishes that "in every inn of this kind are
served to travellers" [001-004].

The inn also features in Ch. 4, where [[chichikov|Chichikov]] encounters [[nozdrev|Nozdrev]] in its
vicinity. [[nozdrev|Nozdrev]] bursts in with his characteristic energy, fresh from the fair,
and insists on dragging [[chichikov|Chichikov]] to his [[landownership|estate]]. The inn hallway and dining room
become the stage for [[nozdrev|Nozdrev]]'s boisterous performance — the eating of sturgeon,
the ordering of vodka, the display of the puppy, and the first hints of the
chaos to come [004-001].

## Role in Dead Souls

The inn functions as [[chichikov|Chichikov]]'s neutral ground — a temporary home from which
he launches his social campaign across the town and countryside. It is here that
he receives his initial intelligence about local [[landownership|landowners]] ([[manilov|Manilov]],
Sobakevitch, [[nozdrev|Nozdrev]]) from [[the-tavern|the inn]] servant, a "lad of about fourteen" who
provides the [[provincial-scandal|gossip]] that shapes [[chichikov|Chichikov]]'s visiting strategy [001-006].

More broadly, [[the-tavern|the inn]] is Gogol's archetypal [[post-house|way-station]] — the liminal space
between arrival and departure, between the road and the settled world. It
appears repeatedly in [[russian-empire|Russia]]n literature (and in Gogol's own work) as the locus
of transient identity, where travellers shed one self and prepare to assume
another. [[chichikov|Chichikov]]'s occupancy of [[the-tavern|the inn]] for the entirety of his stay in the
[[town-of-n|town of N.]] underscores his fundamental condition: he is always a visitor, never
a resident; always passing through, never belonging.
