# Maksim Teliatnikov

Maksim Teliatnikov was a [[serfdom|serf]] bootmaker belonging to [[sobakevich|Sobakevich]]. He appears at two points in the novel: first in [[sobakevich|Sobakevich]]'s sales pitch, and later as the subject of [[chichikov|Chichikov]]'s sympathetic meditation on the [[dead-souls|dead serfs]].

## Character Profile

[[sobakevich|Sobakevich]] names Teliatnikov among his finest craftsmen while negotiating the sale of [[dead-souls|dead soul]]s. Grouped with the bricklayer [[milushkin|Milushkin]], Teliatnikov is praised with the boast that "anything that he drove his awl into became a pair of boots — and boots for which you would be thankful" [005-087]. [[sobakevich|Sobakevich]] adds a telling qualification: "although he WAS a bit foul of the mouth" [005-087] — a small note of human particularity that survives even in a commercial pitch for a dead man.

In Chapter 7, when [[chichikov|Chichikov]] reviews the [[serfdom|serf]] list after the Civil Court transaction, he pauses at Teliatnikov's name. The narrator imagines the bootmaker's life history: apprenticed to a German master who "fed you and your fellows at a common table," Teliatnikov would have learned his trade and eventually set up independently [007-004]. But the narrator also imagines his downfall, invoking the proverb "As drunk as a shoemaker" to suggest that Teliatnikov, like many of his trade, fell into dissipation [007-004].

The name resurfaces a third time when [[sobakevich|Sobakevich]], defending his sale at the Civil Court, lists Teliatnikov alongside [[michiev|Michiev]], [[probka-stepan|Probka Stepan]], and [[milushkin|Milushkin]] as proof that he has sold only his best [[serfdom|serfs]] [007-089].

## Role in Dead Souls

Teliatnikov serves two complementary functions. Through [[sobakevich|Sobakevich]]'s boasting, he exemplifies the genuine skill of the [[russian-empire|Russia]]n [[serfdom|serf]]-craftsman — the labor that makes the gentry's economic world possible [005-087]. Through [[chichikov|Chichikov]]'s meditative reconstruction, he becomes a case study in the tragic arc that Gogol sees in many peasant lives: talent and training followed by drink and decline [007-004].

The name also contributes to [[sobakevich|Sobakevich]]'s cumulative fraud. By listing multiple craftsmen together, [[sobakevich|Sobakevich]] creates a rhetorical momentum that makes each individual [[serfdom|serf]] harder for the [[officialdom|officials]] to challenge — the sheer volume of names carries its own persuasive force [007-089].
