# Murazov

## Character Profile

Afanasy Vassilyevitch Murazov is a wealthy merchant and tax-farmer
who emerges in Part II as the novel's most significant **moral
counterweight** to [[chichikov|Chichikov]]. Unlike the [[landownership|landowners]] and [[officialdom|officials]]
[[chichikov|Chichikov]] has manipulated, Murazov cannot be charmed, [[bribery|bribe]]d, or
outmaneuvered — he sees through people because he has no illusions
about human nature, having risen from humble origins through decades
of honest commerce.

Murazov is first mentioned as a standard of integrity: "Take Murazov,
the tax-farmer — he, again, must have started from nothing" [014-116].
When he finally appears, he is described entering a room "almost at the
same time" as other characters, suggesting a man whose movements are
purposeful and unhurried [015-151]. He removes his cap respectfully but
speaks directly, without the obsequiousness that marks most interactions
with the gentry.

His wealth is substantial — he is a "Commissioner of Taxes" — but his
authority derives not from rank or land but from **moral seriousness**.
He advises [[khlobuev|Khlobuev]] to give up his [[landownership|estate]] and live modestly, and when
[[chichikov|Chichikov]] is imprisoned, Murazov visits him in gaol — the only person
who does — and offers him a path to redemption through honest labor.

## Role in Dead Souls

Murazov represents Gogol's vision of **righteous commerce** — the
possibility that wealth, properly earned and properly used, can be a
force for moral good rather than [[bribery|corruption]]. In this he is the
antithesis of [[chichikov|Chichikov]], who pursues wealth through deception and legal
loopholes.

His gaol-house visit to [[chichikov|Chichikov]] is the novel's most explicit moral
confrontation. Murazov looks at [[chichikov|Chichikov]] "sorrowfully" and speaks of
the soul rather than the law [015-219]. In the surviving fragments,
this scene is incomplete — but its trajectory is clear: Murazov is the
instrument of [[chichikov|Chichikov]]'s potential spiritual awakening, a possibility
Gogol intended to develop in the lost portions of the manuscript.
