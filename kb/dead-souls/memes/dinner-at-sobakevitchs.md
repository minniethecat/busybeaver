# The Dinner at Sobakevich's

## Summary

The dinner at Sobakevich's [[landownership|estate]] is the centerpiece of [[chichikov|Chichikov]]'s visit in Chapter 5. After their negotiation over [[dead-souls|dead soul]]s — conducted with the same blunt economy that [[sobakevich|Sobakevich]] applies to everything — the host invites his guest to the table. The meal is a demonstration of [[sobakevich|Sobakevich]]'s gastronomic philosophy: food should be [[russian-empire|Russia]]n, substantial, and served in whole portions.

[[sobakevich|Sobakevich]] launches into a tirade against French and German culinary influence, denouncing the practices of noble kitchens where meat "has been kicking about the market-place four days or more." He declares that he "should like to hang" the doctors who prescribe diets and hunger cures, insisting that such delicacies suit "flaccid German systems" but not a [[russian-empire|Russia]]n stomach. His own preferences are categorical: "When I eat pork at a meal, give me the WHOLE pig; when mutton, the WHOLE sheep; when goose, the WHOLE of the bird."

He puts philosophy into practice by taking half a shoulder of mutton onto his plate and devouring it "down to the last morsel of gristle and bone," prompting [[chichikov|Chichikov]]'s silent admiration: "The fellow has a pretty good holding capacity!" Wiping his hands on his napkin, [[sobakevich|Sobakevich]] then contrasts himself with a neighbor named [[plushkin|Plushkin]] — a miser who "owns eight hundred [[dead-souls|souls]], yet dines worse than does my shepherd." This casual mention plants the seed for [[chichikov|Chichikov]]'s next destination.

## Participants

- [[chichikov-paul-ivanovitch]] — the guest, who uses the dinner as an occasion to gather intelligence
- [[sobakevich]] — the host, whose eating habits embody his philosophy of life
- [[theodulia-ivanovna|Madame Sobakevich]] ([[theodulia-ivanovna]]) — present at table, objects to her husband's graphic descriptions

## Significance

The dinner scene is a masterclass in characterization through consumption. [[sobakevich|Sobakevich]]'s approach to food — whole animals, no waste, contempt for culinary artifice — mirrors his approach to [[dead-souls|dead soul]]s trading: direct, substantial, suspicious of refinement. His denunciation of foreign influence is a comic variation on Slavophile rhetoric, but Gogol undercuts the grandiosity with the sheer physicality of the performance. The scene also advances the plot: it is at this dinner that [[chichikov|Chichikov]] first learns of [[plushkin|Plushkin]], the miser whose [[landownership|estate]] contains the most lucrative [[dead-souls|dead soul]]s of all. The transition from mutton to miser — from the most corporeal of pleasures to the most skeletal of existences — is a characteristic Gogolian juxtaposition.

## Context

The dinner follows [[chichikov|Chichikov]]'s successful negotiation for [[sobakevich|Sobakevich]]'s [[dead-souls|dead soul]]s at the price of two and a half roubles per soul. Throughout the meal, [[sobakevich|Sobakevich]]'s conversation ranges from denunciations of the town's [[officialdom|officials]] (all "swindlers" and "scoundrels") to his culinary manifesto. The mention of [[plushkin|Plushkin]], living only five versts away, sets the course for Chapter 6. The dinner thus functions as both a character set-piece and a narrative hinge, closing the [[sobakevich|Sobakevich]] episode while opening the [[plushkin|Plushkin]] one.
