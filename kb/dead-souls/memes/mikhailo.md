# Mikhailo

## Character Profile

Mikhailo is [[tientietnikov|Tientietnikov]]'s valet, appearing in Part II of the novel. He is introduced in a single extended comic vignette depicting the morning routine at [[tientietnikov|Tientietnikov]]'s [[landownership|estate]]. On the morning described, [[tientietnikov|Tientietnikov]] awoke very late and, "raising himself to a sitting posture, rubbed his eyes. And since those eyes were small, the process of rubbing them occupied a very long time, and throughout its continuance there stood waiting by the door his valet, Mikhailo, armed with a towel and basin" [012-008].

The scene continues with Gogol's characteristic exaggeration: "For one hour, for two hours, did poor Mikhailo stand there: then he departed to the kitchen, and returned to find his master still rubbing his eyes as he sat on the bed" [012-008]. This image of the patient servant waiting interminably while his master performs the most trivial of actions is one of the novel's sharpest comic portraits of the idle [[landownership|landowner]]'s household, where a [[serfdom|serf]]'s entire morning can be consumed by waiting for a single gesture.

## Role in Dead Souls

Mikhailo appears only in this brief vignette, but his role is emblematic. He represents the invisible labour of the [[serfdom|serf]] household — the servants who stand, wait, fetch, and carry, their time wholly absorbed by the whims and lethargy of their masters. [[tientietnikov|Tientietnikov]]'s exaggerated indolence (two hours of eye-rubbing, a two-hour tea, then staring out the window) is made visible precisely through Mikhailo's enforced patience. The valet's repeated journeys — standing, departing to the kitchen, returning — trace the circular emptiness of his master's day.
