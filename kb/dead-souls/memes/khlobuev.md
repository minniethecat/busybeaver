# Khlobuev

## Character Profile

Semen Semenovitch Khlobuev is a [[landownership|landowner]] of Part II who embodies the
**tragedy of gentry decline** — a man of taste, intelligence, and good
breeding who has lost everything through a combination of indolence,
extravagance, and the general mismanagement that Gogol diagnoses as the
[[russian-empire|Russia]]n [[landownership|landowner]]'s fatal disease.

When [[chichikov|Chichikov]] meets him, Khlobuev is already ruined. His [[landownership|estate]] is
mortgaged, his income insufficient to cover the interest, and his
response to catastrophe is a kind of refined despair — he greets
[[chichikov|Chichikov]] with desperate warmth ("Indeed glad to meet you! Pray shake
hands!") and speaks of his troubles with an air of helpless candor
[015-004]. He admits: "How hard you cannot imagine it is for a man to
be obliged to beg" [015-010].

Khlobuev is not a villain or a fool — he is a man who simply cannot
cope. When [[kostanzhoglo|Kostanzhoglo]] recommends he give up his [[landownership|estate]] and take a
modest position, Khlobuev's reply is a sigh and a hand-grip: a gesture
of acknowledgment without resolution [015-022]. He is, in Gogol's
taxonomy, the [[landownership|landowner]] who has passed beyond reform into the terminal
stage of decline.

## Role in Dead Souls

Khlobuev represents the **endpoint of the gentry's trajectory** — what
happens when the [[manilov|Manilov]]s and [[tientietnikov|Tientietnikov]]s do not recover. His
presence in Part II serves as a warning: the idleness Gogol satirized
in Part I has real consequences, and they are not comic.

He also provides a narrative function: [[chichikov|Chichikov]] attempts to buy
Khlobuev's [[landownership|estate]] (continuing his pattern of exploiting the vulnerable),
while [[murazov|Murazov]] — the novel's moral center — intervenes to offer Khlobuev
a path to honest living. This triangulation of schemer, victim, and
savior is one of the clearest expressions of Gogol's intended moral
architecture for the novel.
