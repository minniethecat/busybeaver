# Nikolasha

Nikolasha is one of the two sons of [[peter-petrovitch-pietukh|Peter Petrovitch Pietukh]], introduced in Part II when [[chichikov|Chichikov]] visits the [[peter-petrovitch-pietukh|Pietukh]] [[landownership|estate]] [014-037]. Together with his brother Aleksasha, he represents the restless provincial youth drawn toward the glitter of the capital cities.

## Character Profile

Nikolasha is home from the Gymnasium for the holidays when [[chichikov|Chichikov]] arrives [014-037]. His father instructs him to entertain the visitor while Aleksasha follows to assist with the host's duties. [[chichikov|Chichikov]] finds Nikolasha to be "a budding man about town": the boy immediately opens conversation by declaring that no good is to be derived from studying at a provincial institution, and that he and his brother desire to transfer to St. Petersburg, "the provinces not being worth living in" [014-038].

Later, as evening falls over the countryside, Nikolasha and Aleksasha are observed to be oblivious to the beauty of the rural scene around them. Instead of galloping over the fields on frisky cobs, they are engaged in dreaming of Moscow — its confectioners' shops and theatres — inspired by tales from a cadet newly arrived on a visit from the capital [014-106].

## Role in Dead Souls

Nikolasha serves as a minor satirical sketch of the provincial youth who disdain their native surroundings in favour of metropolitan glamour. His preference for St. Petersburg and dreams of Moscow's sweet-shops represent the shallow aspirations that Gogol saw as symptomatic of [[russian-empire|Russia]]'s cultural drift away from the land and its traditions. The contrast between the "glorious picture" of the moonlit countryside — which "no one thought of admiring" — and the boys' city-dreams underscores the theme of alienation from authentic [[russian-empire|Russia]]n life.
