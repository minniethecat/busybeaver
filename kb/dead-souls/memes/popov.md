# Popov

## Character Profile

Popov is a household [[serfdom|serf]] whose name appears in the register of [[dead-souls|dead soul]]s that [[chichikov|Chichikov]] reviews in Chapter 7. Unlike most [[serfdom|serfs]] in the list, who are identified only by trade or surname, Popov provokes an extended imaginative riff from [[chichikov|Chichikov]] — one of the most vivid passages in the novel's meditation on the anonymous dead.

[[chichikov|Chichikov]]'s internal monologue plays out as a half-mocking, half-sympathetic biography: "Probably you are an educated man, good Popov, and go in for polite thieving, as distinguished from the more vulgar cut-throat sort" [007-006]. He then imagines a scene in which a [[captain-kopeikin|Captain]] of Rural Police challenges Popov for lacking a passport. Popov, in [[chichikov|Chichikov]]'s fantasy, replies with "fluent explanation," claiming his passport is at a certain Miestchanin Pimenov's, and when the [[captain-kopeikin|Captain]] asks if he *is* Pimenov, answers "Yes, I am Pimenov" — a bold, spur-of-the-moment lie [007-006].

This passage extends across multiple paragraphs in Gogol's text, transforming a bare [[revision-census|census]] entry into a miniature picaresque narrative. Popov becomes, in [[chichikov|Chichikov]]'s mind, a figure of [[serfdom|serf]] resourcefulness and roguery — the sort of man who navigates the Tsarist [[officialdom|bureaucracy]] through wit rather than documentation.

## Role in Dead Souls

Popov exemplifies Gogol's technique of conjuring life from death. The [[dead-souls|dead soul]]s register is, literally, a list of the deceased; but through [[chichikov|Chichikov]]'s imagination, each name becomes an entry point into a possible biography. Popov's imagined life — educated, thieving, quick-witted under interrogation — is one of the richest of these flights, and it reveals as much about [[chichikov|Chichikov]] (his sympathy with clever rogues, his own history of bureaucratic manoeuvring) as it does about the anonymous [[serfdom|serf]].

The passage also serves a structural function within Chapter 7. After the tense negotiation with [[sobakevich|Sobakevich]] and the bureaucratic hurdles of the purchase deed, [[chichikov|Chichikov]]'s solitary meditation over the list of names provides a moment of unexpected gravity. Popov's imagined vitality contrasts with the fact of his death, deepening the novel's elegiac undercurrent.
