# Bribery

## Explanation

**Bribery** was so pervasive in 19th-century [[russian-empire|Russia]]n [[provincial-government|provincial administration]] that it functioned as a parallel system of taxation. Officials received meager salaries — or sometimes no salary at all — and were expected to supplement their income through "gifts" (взятки) from those who needed their services. A petition, a license, a court ruling, a passport — everything required a bribe, and the amount was often as standardized as any [[officialdom|official]] fee.

Gogol himself had served in [[imperial-civil-service|civil service]] positions and knew the system intimately. The [[russian-empire|Russia]]n word *vzyatka* (взятка) literally means "something taken," and the practice was so normalized that refusing a bribe could seem stranger than accepting one （008-033）. Higher [[officialdom|officials]] took larger bribes; the [[governor|Governor]] might receive thousands of rubles for a favorable decision, while a lowly copying-clerk might be content with a bottle of vodka.

[[chichikov|Chichikov]], as a former customs [[officialdom|official]], is a product and master of this system. His genius lies not in inventing new forms of corruption but in exploiting the *existing* corrupt infrastructure — the clerks who process his [[dead-souls|dead soul]] deeds without question are simply doing business as usual （011-028）.

## Significance in Dead Souls

Bribery in [[dead-souls|Dead Souls]] operates at multiple levels:

1. **Narrative lubricant**: [[purchase-of-dead-souls|Chichikov's scheme]] succeeds because every [[officialdom|official]] he encounters is bribable. The civil court clerks process his deed of transfer without asking awkward questions — not because they are convinced by his story, but because the customary gratuity has been paid （003-138）.

2. **Satirical mirror**: Gogol uses the universality of bribery to reflect the moral condition of provincial [[russian-empire|Russia]]. When the townspeople speculate about [[chichikov|Chichikov]]'s identity, several suggest he is a government inspector investigating corruption — and nearly everyone panics, revealing a guilty conscience that implicates the entire town.

3. **Moral ambiguity**: Unlike some satirists, Gogol does not treat bribery as the sin of a few villains. It is the water in which his characters swim — so ubiquitous that it barely registers as wrongdoing. Officials who take bribes are also depicted as otherwise decent family men, creating an unsettling moral texture that resists easy judgment.

The famous passage in which the narrator reflects that "we all know why bribes are accepted" （008-033） captures Gogol's approach: bribery is not an aberration but the logical outcome of a system where salaries are inadequate and oversight is absent.

## Examples from the Text

- **Why Bribes Are Accepted** （008-033） — The narrator's aphoristic reflection that the reason for bribery is universally understood, implying that the system rather than individual vice is to blame.

- **[[chichikov|Chichikov]]'s Customs Career** （011-028） — [[chichikov|Chichikov]]'s biography reveals his mastery of bureaucratic corruption. As a customs [[officialdom|official]], he ran a smuggling ring in collusion with his colleagues — until he was betrayed and had to start over.

- **The Town's Guilty Conscience** （009-010） — When [[chichikov|Chichikov]] is [[provincial-scandal|rumor]]ed to be a government inspector, the town [[officialdom|officials]] panic — each one knowing exactly which of their own bribes might be exposed.
