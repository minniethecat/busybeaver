# Serfdom

## Explanation

Serfdom was the system of bonded peasant labor that dominated rural [[russian-empire|Russia]] from the 17th century until its abolition in 1861. Under this system, **serfs** (крепостные крестьяне) were legally tied to the land they worked and could be bought, sold, or mortgaged by the landowning [[gentry|nobility]]. The [[russian-empire|Russian state]] conducted periodic **[[revision-census|census]]es** ([[revision-census|revision]]s) to count serfs for taxation purposes — a bureaucratic mechanism that lies at the heart of *[[dead-souls|Dead Souls]]*.

Crucially, serfs who died between [[revision-census|census]]es remained on the tax rolls until the next [[revision-census|revision]]. Landowners continued to pay taxes on these **"[[dead-souls|dead soul]]s"** as if they were still alive. Serfs could also be mortgaged to the state bank, allowing [[landownership|landowners]] to borrow money against their value as collateral. The gap between [[revision-census|census]] registration and actual mortality created the legal fiction that [[chichikov|Chichikov]] exploits throughout the novel （P03-005）.

A [[landownership|landowner]] might own anywhere from a few dozen to several thousand serfs, and their wealth was often measured by the number of "[[dead-souls|souls]]" they possessed. The system created a rigid social hierarchy, with the **gentry** ([[gentry|дворянство]]) depending entirely on serf labor for their income and status （001-004）.

## Significance in Dead Souls

Serfdom is not merely the background setting for Gogol's novel — it is the **engine of the plot**. [[purchase-of-dead-souls|Chichikov's scheme]] to purchase [[dead-souls|dead soul]]s depends entirely on the legal peculiarities of the serf system: that deceased serfs remain on [[revision-census|census]] rolls, that they can be transferred between owners, and that they can be pledged as collateral for bank loans （002-093）.

Gogol uses serfdom to satirize several aspects of [[russian-empire|Russia]]n society:

1. **The moral vacancy of the gentry**: Landowners like [[manilov|Manilov]], Korobochka, and [[sobakevich|Sobakevich]] reveal their character through how they treat their serfs and how they react to [[chichikov|Chichikov]]'s bizarre proposal. [[manilov|Manilov]], ever sentimental, gives his [[dead-souls|dead soul]]s away for free （002-095）; Korobochka fears being cheated on the price; [[sobakevich|Sobakevich]] inflates the value of his [[dead-souls|dead serfs]] by praising their skills as if they were still alive.

2. **The dehumanization of serfs**: Throughout the novel, serfs are discussed as units of property — counted, priced, and transferred like livestock. [[sobakevich|Sobakevich]]'s extended catalogue of his deceased serfs' talents (ch05) is simultaneously a tribute to their craftsmanship and a grotesque reminder that they were bought and sold as objects.

3. **The absurdity of [[imperial-civil-service|Russian bureaucracy]]**: The [[revision-census|census]] system that makes [[purchase-of-dead-souls|Chichikov's scheme]] possible is portrayed as a vast, indifferent ma[[table-of-ranks|chin]]e — more concerned with paperwork than with human reality. The clerks at the civil court process the deed of transfer without question, revealing a system that values documentation over truth （003-138）.

The title *[[dead-souls|Dead Souls]]* itself is a direct reference to serfdom's [[revision-census|census]] fiction: [[dead-souls|souls]] who are legally alive but physically dead. Gogol turns this bureaucratic euphemism into a metaphor for the spiritual emptiness of his characters.

## Examples from the Text

- **The Census System** （P03-005） — The Introduction explains how [[landownership|landowners]] could mortgage "[[dead-souls|dead soul]]s" to the state bank, establishing the novel's central premise.

- **[[chichikov|Chichikov]]'s First Inquiry** （001-004） — Upon arriving in the [[town-of-n|provincial town]], [[chichikov|Chichikov]] immediately asks about local [[landownership|landowners]] and how many serfs they possess, revealing his motives from the outset.

- **[[manilov|Manilov]]'s Generosity** （002-093） — When [[chichikov|Chichikov]] proposes buying [[manilov|Manilov]]'s [[dead-souls|dead soul]]s, [[manilov|Manilov]] is so bewildered that he offers them for nothing — a transaction that would have been impossible under any other economic system.

- **[[sobakevich|Sobakevich]]'s Sales Pitch** （005-018） — [[sobakevich|Sobakevich]] praises the qualities of his [[dead-souls|dead serfs]] as a horse-trader might praise livestock, illustrating the complete commodification of human beings under serfdom.

- **The Transfer Deed** （003-138） — The legal ma[[table-of-ranks|chin]]ery of serfdom is laid bare in the civil court scene, where the clerks process [[chichikov|Chichikov]]'s paperwork with mechanical indifference.
