# Pietukh, Peter Petrovitch

**Peter Petrovitch Pietukh** is a landowner encountered in Part II of Gogol's *Dead Souls* — a man whose entire existence is organised around one principle: the feeding of guests. His name (from Russian *petukh*, "rooster") signals his role as a comic emblem of hospitality and abundance.

## Arrival at Pietukh's

[[Chichikov, Paul Ivanovitch|Chichikov]] and [[platonov|Platonov]] arrive at Pietukh's estate by mistake, having asked [[selifan|Selifan]] to drive to [[koshkarevs-estate|Colonel Koshkarev]]'s house. When [[Chichikov, Paul Ivanovitch|Chichikov]] remonstrates with his coachman, Pietukh interrupts:

> "Your hopes are vain. It is to my house, not to his, that you have come; and I am Peter Petrovitch Pietukh — yes, Peter Petrovitch Pietukh." [013-010]

The landowner immediately dispatches his two sons — Nikolasha and Aleksasha, home from the Gymnasium for the holidays — to entertain the guests, insisting there can be no question of leaving before dinner [013-012].

## The Sturgeon Feast

Pietukh's hospitality culminates in the [[feast-at-pietukhs|giant sturgeon feast]], one of the novel's most memorable comic set-pieces. The centrepiece is an enormous sturgeon so large that "it actually was a question whether all of us could finish it" [013-035]. The entire household and guests consume the fish, and the feast famously ends with everyone falling asleep at the table in a collective stupor of satiety.

## Pietukh as Type

In Gogol's gallery of landowners, Pietukh represents the principle of plenitude — the pre-modern Russian virtue of unstinting hospitality taken to comic excess. Unlike the miserly [[plushkin|Plushkin]] or the calculating [[sobakevich|Sobakevich]], Pietukh's relationship to food and guests is one of joyful, almost metaphysical, abundance. His philosophy is simple: to feed is to be.
