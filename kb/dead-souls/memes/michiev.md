# Michiev

Michiev was a [[serfdom|serf]] wheelwright belonging to the [[landownership|landowner]] [[sobakevich|Sobakevich]]. He appears only through [[sobakevich|Sobakevich]]'s praise and the subsequent revelation that he was already dead when sold to [[chichikov|Chichikov]] as a "[[dead-souls|dead soul]]" — making him a prime example of [[sobakevich|Sobakevich]]'s fraudulent salesmanship.

## Character Profile

Michiev is never seen directly in the novel but is vividly evoked through [[sobakevich|Sobakevich]]'s bragging [005-083]. The [[landownership|landowner]] describes him as a wheelwright without equal: "Never was there such a one to build spring carts!" [005-083]. Unlike Moscow craftsmanship — which [[sobakevich|Sobakevich]] dismisses as "good only for an hour" — Michiev's work was entirely his own, "even down to the varnishing" [005-083].

[[sobakevich|Sobakevich]] emphasizes Michiev's physical strength as much as his skill, declaring that "nowhere else would you find such a fellow for working heavy tools as was Michiev. He had the strength of a horse in his shoulders" [005-091]. To drive the point home, [[sobakevich|Sobakevich]] turns toward the portrait of Bagration hanging on his wall, appealing to the military hero as though for corroboration [005-091].

The [[ivan-grigorievitch|President of the Civil Court]] also knows of Michiev by reputation, recalling that the wheelwright once made him a drozhki [32] and calling him "a splendid craftsman" [007-086].

## Role in Dead Souls

Michiev appears at two key moments in the novel's [[dead-souls|dead soul]]s drama. In Chapter 5, [[sobakevich|Sobakevich]] praises him while trying to inflate the value of the [[dead-souls|dead serfs]] he is selling to [[chichikov|Chichikov]] [005-083–091]. [[chichikov|Chichikov]] tries to interject that Michiev "had long since departed this world," but [[sobakevich|Sobakevich]]'s eloquence overpowers the objection [005-084].

In Chapter 7, the transaction comes under public scrutiny at the Civil Court. [[sobakevich|Sobakevich]] boasts to the assembled [[officialdom|officials]] that he has sold [[chichikov|Chichikov]] "my wheelwright, Michiev" [007-085]. The President is astonished — he knows the craftsman, but also recalls [[sobakevich|Sobakevich]] having said that Michiev was dead [007-086]. [[sobakevich|Sobakevich]]'s response is an audacious lie: "That was his brother. Michiev himself is very much alive, and in even better health than he used to be. Any day he could knock you up a britchka such as you could not procure even in Moscow" [007-087].

[[sobakevich|Sobakevich]] then names Michiev alongside [[probka-stepan|Probka Stepan]], [[milushkin|Milushkin]], and [[maksim-teliatnikov|Teliatnikov]] as proof that he has not just sold one valuable [[serfdom|serf]] but "the whole lot" [007-089] — compounding the fraud even as it is being exposed.

Michiev thus serves a dual function: he represents the genuine skill of the [[russian-empire|Russia]]n peasantry (a recurring Gogolian motif), and he exposes [[sobakevich|Sobakevich]]'s moral shamelessness — a man who will praise a dead [[serfdom|serf]]'s virtues to the heavens while cheating a buyer.
