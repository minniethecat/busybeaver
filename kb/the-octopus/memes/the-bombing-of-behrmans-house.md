## Overview

The Bombing of S. Behrman's House is the culminating act of Presley's transformation from detached poet-observer to participant in the violence of the railroad conflict. Occurring on the same day as Magnus Derrick's political ruin, the bombing represents the endpoint of a radicalization process carefully traced through the novel: Presley's growing sympathy with Caraher's anarchism, his witness of the Mussel Slough massacre, and his horror at Dyke's life sentence.

The attack itself is narrated with journalistic brevity: a bomb thrown through the dining-room window at suppertime, the room wrecked, every window shattered, and — by what Norris presents as near-miraculous chance — S. Behrman left physically untouched. The bombing fails in its immediate aim (Behrman survives) but succeeds as a moral catastrophe for Presley, who later confesses to Behrman's face with the single word: "It was."

## Narrative Context

The bombing occurs late in Book II, after the Mussel Slough massacre has killed Annixter, Harran, Hooven, Osterman, Broderson, and Dabney. The accumulated weight of railroad violence — the rate manipulation that ruined Dyke, the land regrade that dispossessed the ranchers, the bribery that corrupted Magnus, the armed posse that killed the Leaguers — has transformed Presley from an aesthete who "cared for none of these things" [001-119] into a man capable of attempted murder.

Caraher, the anarchist saloonkeeper, is the catalyzing figure. His doctrine — "There's one thing only it does listen to, one thing it is frightened of — the people with dynamite in their hands — six inches of plugged gaspipe. *That* talks" [001-3564] — has been seeded throughout the novel. Presley, who initially found Caraher repellent, has by this point come to see him as a "character," even "invested... with all the dignity of the tragedy" of his wife's death at the hands of Pinkertons [001-4776].

## Detailed Account

### The Radicalization

Presley's path to the bombing is not a sudden conversion but a gradual erosion of his aesthetic detachment. Early in the novel, he is irritated by "these eternal fierce bickerings between the farmers of the San Joaquin and the Pacific and Southwestern Railroad" — they "did not belong to his world" [001-119]. He searches for the "True Romance" of the West, only to find "grain rates and unjust freight tariffs."

The turning points accumulate. He overhears Caraher's sermon to Dyke on the necessity of dynamite. He witnesses Magnus Derrick's moral corrosion through the League's bribery. He sees Dyke — "big, honest, jovial Dyke" — destroyed, turned outlaw, sentenced to life. And then the massacre at the irrigating ditch, where his friends fall one by one.

The key phrase marking Presley's transformation comes from Caraher's repeated refrain:

> "There's one thing only it does listen to, one thing it is frightened of — the people with dynamite in their hands — six inches of plugged gaspipe. *That* talks." [001-3564]

By the time of the bombing, Presley has internalized this logic. Caraher himself later gloats: "Caraher is right. There is only one thing they listen to, and that's dynamite" [001-3908].

### The Attack

Norris narrates the bombing with deliberate understatement, compressing the event into a single paragraph of reportorial prose:

> "Before nightfall of that day, Bonneville was further excited by an astonishing bit of news. S. Behrman lived in a detached house at some distance from the town, surrounded by a grove of live oak and eucalyptus trees. At a little after half-past six, as he was sitting down to his supper, a bomb was thrown through the window of his dining-room, exploding near the doorway leading into the hall. The room was wrecked and nearly every window of the house shattered. By a miracle, S. Behrman, himself, remained untouched." [002-6182]

The narrative choice is significant: Norris does not show us Presley throwing the bomb. The event is reported as news — something that has already happened, observed from a distance. This technique preserves Presley's moral ambiguity. We know he did it (he confesses later), but we are denied the moment of action itself. The effect is to keep the reader in the position of the Bonneville citizen — hearing the news, piecing together the fragments, struggling to reconcile the act with the man.

The details are precise: half-past six, suppertime, the dining-room window, the doorway to the hall. The domestic framing — Behrman "sitting down to his supper" — heightens the violation. This is not a battlefield. It is a home.

The miracle of Behrman's survival is Norris's naturalist irony: the bomb destroys the room but leaves the man. Violence, in Norris's world, is both powerful and futile. It wrecks everything around its target but fails to touch the target itself.

### The Confession

Presley's confession occurs in a remarkable scene late in the novel, on the County Road, where he encounters S. Behrman — now the "Master of the Wheat," overseeing the harvest of Los Muertos. Behrman, with characteristic placidity, raises the subject:

> "I guess it was you, wasn't it, who threw that bomb into my house?"

Presley's reply is a single word:

> "It was." [002-7132]

The economy of the exchange is devastating. Behrman's tone is conversational, almost casual — a man asking about the weather. Presley's answer is unadorned, without explanation or apology. The two men — the railroad agent and the poet — face each other across the irreducible fact of attempted murder. Behrman's response is not anger but a kind of businesslike incredulity:

> "Well, that don't show any common sense, Presley. What could you have gained by killing me?" [002-7136]

The question hangs unanswered. It is the novel's final judgment on political violence: not that it is immoral (though it is), but that it is useless. The Railroad is a "force only, which no man could control and for which no man was responsible" — killing Behrman would not kill the Railroad.

## Significance

The Bombing of S. Behrman's House is the novel's most concentrated dramatization of the moral logic and ultimate futility of political violence. Presley's trajectory — from poet to bomber — traces the argument that systemic injustice manufactures the very violence it then condemns. The railroad creates the conditions (the massacre, the dispossession, Dyke's fate) that make Presley's act thinkable, then survives that act with a shrug.

But Norris does not endorse the bombing. Presley's subsequent moral collapse, his flight to India, his broken health — these are the consequences of having crossed the line from observer to actor. The bomb that fails to kill Behrman succeeds in destroying something in Presley. The novel's final judgment is not that the bombing was wrong (though it was) but that it was inadequate — a gesture against a system that absorbs all gestures.

The fact that S. Behrman himself, the novel's personification of railroad greed, pronounces this judgment — "What could you have gained by killing me?" — is Norris's most sophisticated irony. The enemy understands the futility of violence against him better than the revolutionary does.
