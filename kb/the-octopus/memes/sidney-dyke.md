## Role in the Novel

Sidney Dyke is the young daughter of the railroad engineer [[dyke|Dyke]] and his mother Mrs. [[dyke|Dyke]], who keeps house for him in [[guadalajara|Guadalajara]]. Sidney's mother died some five years before the novel's present, leaving [[dyke|Dyke]] "a little daughter, Sidney, to bring up as best he could" [001-058]. Although Sidney never speaks directly in the novel and appears only as a child in the background of domestic scenes, she functions as a crucial emotional anchor for her father. [[dyke|Dyke]]'s devotion to Sidney — his ambition to provide her with an education, his purchase of little red slippers with dimes hidden in the toes — transforms his eventual ruin from an abstract injustice into a visceral tragedy of a father who can no longer provide for his child. When [[dyke|Dyke]] becomes a fugitive after his desperate train robbery, Sidney's bewildered grief and her removal to [[quien-sabe|Quien Sabe]] with her grandmother mark the collateral damage of [[the-railroad|the Railroad]]'s war on the individual.

## Character Analysis

Sidney is characterized almost entirely through the eyes of her father and the other adults who encounter her. She is "sweet and gentle" according to her grandmother, yet also exhibits an unsettling precocity — Mrs. [[dyke|Dyke]] reports that Sidney "hisses" at the mention of [[the-railroad|the Railroad]], a habit she picked up from other children at school [006-043]. This detail is one of Norris's most effective uses of a minor character to convey theme: even a child, innocent in every other respect, has absorbed the communal hatred of [[the-railroad|the Railroad]]. Sidney's copybooks, which [[dyke|Dyke]] proudly shows to [[presley|Presley]] and [[vanamee|Vanamee]], contain not only the standard moral aphorisms of the period but also the startling political declarations: "My motto — Public Control of Public Franchises" and "The [[railroad-monopoly|P. and S.W.]] is an Enemy of the State" [006-038]. These entries, filtered through a child's understanding, dramatize how thoroughly the railroad conflict has permeated every level of society.

## Relationships

Sidney's most important relationship is with her father, [[dyke]]. His love for "the little tad" is the novel's purest expression of paternal devotion. When [[dyke|Dyke]] tiptoes into his mother's room to watch Sidney sleeping, "her arm under her head, her lips parted," and when he feigns injury during their play, declaring "his ribs were broken" [008-098], Norris establishes a domestic idyll that [[the-railroad|the Railroad]]'s economic warfare will destroy. Mrs. [[dyke|Dyke]], Sidney's grandmother, is her primary caregiver, and the two are effectively a single domestic unit — when they must flee the hop ranch, they go together, and when [[annixter|Annixter]] offers them shelter at [[quien-sabe|Quien Sabe]], they are installed in "the Trees' old house" as a pair [010-276]. [[hilma-tree]] shows particular solicitude toward Sidney, repeatedly asking "Where is Sidney, Mrs. [[dyke|Dyke]]? Is she well? Is she taken care of?" [010-267]. [[annixter]]'s generosity in taking in Mrs. [[dyke|Dyke]] and Sidney after [[dyke|Dyke]] becomes a fugitive is one of the novel's rare acts of uncomplicated kindness.

## Key Scenes

**The Red Slippers**: In [[bonneville|Bonneville]], after being discharged from his job, [[dyke|Dyke]] buys Sidney "a little pair of red slippers" and surreptitiously "slipped a dime into the toe of each with a wink at [[annixter|Annixter]]" [005-211]. The next morning, Sidney discovers the dime and "had the whole house by the ears ever since till she could spend it" — a moment of pure childish joy that contrasts with the grim financial negotiations occurring simultaneously.

**The Copybooks**: [[dyke|Dyke]] insists on showing [[presley|Presley]] and [[vanamee|Vanamee]] Sidney's school copybooks, "monuments of laborious, elaborate neatness" filled with trite moralities alongside strident anti-railroad slogans [006-038]. Mrs. [[dyke|Dyke]]'s serious protest that she corrects Sidney's "hissing" at the mention of [[the-railroad|the Railroad]] reveals how the political conflict has infiltrated childhood.

**The Flight from the Hop Ranch**: After [[mussel-slough-tragedy|the shootout]] at the irrigation ditch, Mrs. [[dyke|Dyke]] and Sidney flee to [[los-muertos|Los Muertos]]. [[presley|Presley]], looking from his window, hears Mrs. [[dyke|Dyke]] sobbing "Oh, my boy, my boy" as the wagon disappears into the night, with Sidney beside her [010-012]. The image of the child and the grandmother alone in the darkness crystallizes the human cost of [[the-railroad|the Railroad]]'s triumph.

**At [[quien-sabe|Quien Sabe]]**: After [[annixter|Annixter]] brings Mrs. [[dyke|Dyke]] and Sidney to live at [[quien-sabe|Quien Sabe]], [[hilma-tree|Hilma]] embraces her husband and praises his kindness [010-277]. The sight of Sidney and her grandmother "in the doorway of the little house" during the wheat field confrontation, "looking out, bewildered, ignorant of what had happened," is one of the last images of them before the novel's tragic climax.

## Thematic Significance

Sidney Dyke embodies the novel's theme of collateral damage — the innocent who suffer from [[the-railroad|the Railroad]]'s predation without ever understanding the forces that destroy their world. Her father's refrain about her education — his "one ambition" [005-278] — represents the American dream of self-improvement through honest labor, which [[the-railroad|the Railroad]]'s monopoly systematically extinguishes. The dimes in the red slippers are a small but potent symbol: a father's love expressed through coin, the very medium that [[the-railroad|the Railroad]] uses to measure and consume everything it touches. Sidney's copybook slogans — "Truth Crushed to Earth Will Rise Again" — acquire an ironic resonance when applied to her own family's fate, as the truth of [[dyke|Dyke]]'s victimization is indeed crushed, and whether it will rise again is the question the novel leaves open.
