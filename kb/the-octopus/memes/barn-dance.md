## Overview

The Barn Dance at Annixter's is one of the most important social events in *The Octopus*, a novel ostensibly about wheat and railroads but saturated with the texture of community life in California's San Joaquin Valley. Buck Annixter, the irascible young owner of the Quien Sabe ranch, hosts the dance to inaugurate his newly constructed great barn — "the biggest barn," as he boasts [001-255] — inviting the entire community of ranchers, their families, and working hands. The event unfolds in three distinct phases: the festive assembly and dancing, the violent intrusion of the disgruntled cowboy Delaney on horseback with a revolver, and the devastating arrival of railroad regrade notices that transform celebration into outrage and lead directly to the formation of the League of ranchers. It is the novel's preeminent "before the fall" tableau — a last display of communal wholeness before the railroad conflict shatters that community irrevocably.

## Narrative Context

The barn dance occurs early in the novel's first book, shortly after the main characters have been established and the railroad's threat to the ranchers' way of life has been introduced. Several narrative threads converge at the dance. The ranchers have been meeting secretly to discuss forming a political coalition to control the Railroad Commission — a scheme championed by Osterman and reluctantly entertained by Magnus Derrick and Annixter [001-1047]. Annixter's personal life is in turmoil: he is a professed woman-hater who finds himself increasingly obsessed with Hilma Tree, the young dairy maid on his ranch [001-2113]. The cowboy Delaney nurses a grudge against Annixter for having fired him after an argument over a line fence and the unauthorized use of the buckskin horse [001-844].

The dance is preceded by a day of frantic preparation. Annixter works himself into exhaustion decorating the barn, mixing punch, and snapping at everyone around him. "There's nothing done that you don't do yourself, unless you stand over these loafers with a club," he fumes. "I'm sick of the whole business — and I've lost my hat; wish to God I'd never dreamed of givin' this rotten fool dance" [001-2101].

The event's aftermath transforms the novel's trajectory. The formation of the League in the harness room, in response to the regrade notices, commits the ranchers to organized resistance against the railroad. This resistance will eventually culminate in violence — the fight at the irrigating ditch and the death of Annixter himself. The barn dance thus serves as the hinge between the ranchers' relatively peaceful pre-conflict existence and the coming catastrophe.

## Detailed Account

### Invitations and Preparation

Annixter first announces his intention to host a dance while visiting Magnus Derrick's ranch. "I came to see about giving a dance in my new barn," he tells Mrs. Derrick, scowling into his hat "as though reading from notes he had concealed there" [001-1623]. Presley intervenes to correct him: "That's nonsense, Buck. You're not giving a public ball. You *must* send out invitations" [001-1627].

The day of the dance, Quien Sabe is in fête and all work is suspended [001-2005]. Annixter throws himself into the preparations with characteristic ferocity. The harness room of the barn is set apart for himself and intimates, with a long table loaded with "boxes of cigars, bottles of whiskey and of beer and the great china bowls for the punch" [001-2097]. He declares "it would be no fault of his if half the number of his men friends were not uproarious before they left" and that his "barn dance would be the talk of all Tulare County for years to come" [001-2097].

The afternoon "wore to evening, to the accompaniment of wearying and clamorous endeavour." At last "the last bolt of cambric was hung in place from the rafters. The last evergreen tree was nailed to the joists of the walls; the last lantern hung, the last nail driven into the musicians' platform" [001-2115]. Annixter, last to leave, emerges in the dusk carrying a saw and a bag of tools, his hammer thrust into a hip pocket, in "execrable temper" [001-2115].

### Arrival of Guests

The first guests arrive shortly after half-past seven: the Bonneville druggist and his womenfolk, followed by "an express wagon loaded down with a swarming family of Spanish-Mexicans, gorgeous in red and yellow colours" [001-2213]. Caraher the saloonkeeper arrives with the delayed lemons and promptly engages Annixter in a wrangle over the punch recipe. Annixter insists on "Champagne straight and a dash of brandy" [001-2217]. A host of minor characters populate the scene: the druggist, awkward and formal, debating "the propriety of putting on his gloves" [001-2219]; the Spanish-Mexican family sitting "rigid on the edges of the hired chairs, silent, constrained" [001-2219]; young Vacca, who "wore a checked coat and white thread gloves and who paced up and down the length of the barn, frowning, very important, whittling a wax candle over the floor to make it slippery for dancing" [001-2219].

Hilma Tree arrives in white, and Annixter is struck by her beauty. "Such arms! How did she manage to keep them hid on ordinary occasions. Big at the shoulder, tapering with delicious modulations to the elbow and wrist, overlaid with a delicate, gleaming lustre" [001-2275].

### The Dancing

The City Band of Bonneville provides the music — Annixter having "managed to offend the leader of the 'Dirigo' Club orchestra, at the very last moment, to such a point that he had refused his services" [001-2221]. The Grand March begins at half-past eight, with "Hilma and Harran Derrick in the lead, Annixter having obstinately refused to engage in either march, set or dance the whole evening" [001-2279]. As the march settles into measured cadence, Annixter draws a long breath: "Good. The thing is under way at last" [001-2283].

Hilma becomes the undisputed belle of the ball. "In a few moments Hilma was surrounded by a group of young men, clamouring for dances. They came from all corners of the barn, leaving the other girls precipitately, almost rudely" [001-2277]. Dancing with Harran Derrick, she is transfigured:

> Her cheeks were bright red, her eyes half-closed, and through her parted lips she drew from time to time a long, tremulous breath of pure delight. The music, the weaving colours, the heat of the air, by now a little oppressive, the monotony of repeated sensation, even the pain of physical fatigue had exalted all her senses. She was in a dreamy lethargy of happiness. It was her "first ball." [001-2349]

Magnus Derrick, meanwhile, holds court near the open doorway of the barn, surrounded by fellow ranchers — Garnett, Keast, Gethings, and Chattern — "discussing the possibility of a shortage in the world's wheat crop for the next year" [001-2353]. The dance floor fills with vivid detail: the druggist dancing "with the solemnity of an automaton, turning about and about, always in the same direction" [001-2349]; the clerk from Bonneville losing his programme card and roaming "wildly about the barn, bewildered, his eyes rolling" [001-2279]; Minna Hooven promenading with Cutter while her mother Mrs. Hooven nearly weeps at a visible drawstring on her daughter's gown [001-2349].

The men's sanctuary is the harness room, where the famous punch — nicknamed "fertiliser" — is dispensed. Annixter and Caraher continue their argument over its composition. Hooven, after his third glass, is "afflicted with a great sadness," retiring to a corner and "wiping the tears from his eyes, refusing to be comforted" [001-2373]. Old Broderson suddenly winks at Annixter with "infinite craftiness" and whispers, "When I was a lad in Ukiah, I was a devil of a fellow with the girls; but Lordy! I wouldn't have it known!" [001-2377]. Vanamee alone refuses to drink, watching the others "calmly, a little contemptuously" [001-2371].

### The Delaney Crisis

The first great disruption comes when Presley and Vanamee arrive with news: the cowboy Delaney, "full of bad whiskey and dago-red," is coming to "shoot up the place" [001-2341]. Delaney has stolen Annixter's buckskin horse, together with a sixty-dollar saddle, after being fired from Quien Sabe. Annixter, unexpectedly, remains calm: "All right. Don't tell anybody else. You might scare the girls off. Get in and drink" [001-2347].

When Delaney arrives, the effect is explosive. He rides the buckskin "at a gallop straight through the doorway and out into the middle of the floor of the barn," the mare rearing and lashing out with both heels on the slippery floor [001-2455]. Delaney has arrayed himself "with painful elaboration" — campaign hat, blue handkerchief, heavy gauntlets, bearskin chaparejos, and a holstered Army Colt's revolver [001-2459].

> In a second of time the dance was a bedlam. The musicians stopped with a discord, and the middle of the crowded floor bared itself instantly. It was like sand blown from off a rock; the throng of guests, carried by an impulse that was not to be resisted, bore back against the sides of the barn, overturning chairs, tripping upon each other, falling down, scrambling to their feet again ... leaving Annixter and Hilma, alone, deserted, their arms about each other, face to face with Delaney. [001-2461]

In the crisis, a revelation occurs. Hilma, turning from Delaney, her hands clasped on Annixter's arm, exclaims: "You, too!" Annixter suddenly understands: "For the briefest lapse of time he and Hilma looked deep into each other's eyes, and from that moment on, Annixter knew that Hilma cared" [001-2471].

Annixter pushes Hilma to safety and faces Delaney alone, his hands in his coat pockets. Delaney taunts him: "Gives a dance, does he, highfalutin' hoe-down in his barn and forgets to invite his old broncho-bustin' friend" [001-2481]. He "fans" his revolver — "spinning it about his index finger by the trigger-guard with incredible swiftness" — and fires, sending "a little splinter of wood" flipping into the air at Annixter's feet [001-2483].

Then Annixter begins firing — through the pocket of his coat. "Both fired without aiming very deliberately. To empty their revolvers and avoid being hit was the desire common to both" [001-2507]. Annixter's next-to-last shot strikes Delaney's pistol hand, shattering it and knocking the revolver from his grip. Delaney turns and runs; the crowd parts before him, and he disappears [001-2511].

The dance calms. "An hour later the dance was progressing as evenly as though nothing in the least extraordinary had occurred" [001-2515]. Annixter is hailed as a hero, adopting a Lancelot-like pose: "Oh, don't say anything about it. I only did what any man would have done in my place" [001-2539].

### Supper

To restore the company's spirits, Annixter announces supper — a "tremendous surprise." The tables are "arranged around three sides of the barn" and loaded with "cold roasts of beef, cold chickens and cold ducks, mountains of sandwiches, pitchers of milk and lemonade, entire cheeses, bowls of olives, plates of oranges and nuts" [001-2541]. The company attacks the food with abandon; "some even beginning with oranges and nuts and ending with beef and chicken" [001-2541]. Paper crackers are pulled, festive tissue caps distributed, and the evening seems restored.

### The Regrade Notices and the League

At the height of the post-supper revelry, a messenger from Bonneville arrives on his bicycle. He delivers a thick yellow envelope marked "Urgent" containing sealed letters for all the major ranchers. Magnus Derrick opens his and reads aloud to the stunned company:

> By regrade of October 1st, the value of the railroad land you occupy, included in your ranch of Los Muertos, has been fixed at $27.00 per acre. The land is now for sale at that price to anyone. [001-2617]

The letters are the same for all: the railroad has raised land prices tenfold from the promised two dollars and a half per acre. Annixter's rate is set at thirty. The response is immediate and ferocious:

> Then suddenly the tempest burst. A dozen men were on their feet in an instant, their teeth set, their fists clenched, their faces purple with rage. Oaths, curses, maledictions exploded like the firing of successive mines. ... It was the human animal hounded to its corner, exploited, harried to its last stand, at bay, ferocious, terrible, turning at last with bared teeth and upraised claws to meet the death grapple. [001-2634]

In the aftermath, the ranchers clear the barn of casual guests, and behind the closed doors of the harness room, the League is formally organized — with Magnus Derrick as President and Osterman as secretary [001-2760]. The dance is over. "Suddenly there was a great silence" [001-2770].

## Character Perspectives

### Annixter

The barn dance is Annixter's finest hour. He begins the day irritable and overwhelmed, snarling at his Chinese cook and lamenting the theft of his buckskin and saddle. But under pressure — first the pressure of hosting, then the pressure of Delaney's gun — he rises magnificently. His calm during the gunfight ("Excitable and turbulent in trifling matters, when actual bodily danger threatened he was of an abnormal quiet" [001-2491]) and his absurdly modest hero act afterward reveal the man's paradoxical nature: bullying and petty in small things, courageous and generous in great ones. Most importantly, Hilma's involuntary cry — "You, too!" — cracks open his carapace of woman-hating and self-protective cynicism, making possible his eventual transformation. The dance is the turning point in his emotional life.

### Hilma Tree

For Hilma, the dance is her "first ball" — a dream of music, color, and social triumph. She is the undisputed belle, surrounded by young men, intoxicated by the sensations of the evening. But her experience pivots on the terror of Delaney's entrance. In that moment of crisis, she involuntarily reveals her feelings for Annixter — a revelation she herself may not have fully understood before speaking the words. After the gunfight, she sits apart "with a serious expression on her face, her thoughts, no doubt, elsewhere" [001-2575].

### Presley

Presley, the poet-observer, floats through the event. He arrives with Vanamee, warns Annixter of Delaney's approach, and is "disappointed" when Annixter does not rise to his dramatic bait [001-2345]. The gunfight sobers him thoroughly. Presley's perspective on the event later feeds into the novel's final retrospective, where the barn dance is recalled alongside "the harness room with its jam of furious men" as one of the indelible scenes of the drama [001-7384].

### Magnus Derrick

The Governor stands aloof through most of the evening, holding court with other ranchers, discussing world wheat markets. He dances only when pressed, and his dignity provides a counterpoint to the increasingly boisterous harness room. But it is Magnus who reads the railroad's regrade notice aloud to the assembly, and it is Magnus who is unanimously chosen President of the newly-formed League. The dance marks his transition from respected elder statesman to active leader of the resistance.

### Osterman

Osterman, the comic schemer, treats the entire evening as a stage. He arrives in flamboyant clothing, insists on calling Annixter "Buck" in inappropriately public settings, and works the harness room like a politician. At supper he eats "all the olives he could lay his hands on, a score of them, fifty of them, a hundred of them" and boasts of having once swallowed a tree toad [001-2547]. Yet Osterman is also the architect of the League strategy, and when the regrade notices arrive, his quip — "That's a pretty good one. Tell us another" [001-2624] — is a mask for calculating fury.

### Delaney

Delaney is the dance's antagonist and catalyst. Fired by Annixter after a series of grievances, he arrives drunk, armed, and on horseback — a figure of frontier violence invading the orderly social world of the ranchers. His failed attempt to humiliate and possibly kill Annixter has the opposite effect: it transforms Annixter into a hero and precipitates Hilma's confession of feeling. Delaney flees into the night, wounded, and disappears from the narrative.

## Thematic Significance

### Community Before the Fall

The barn dance is Norris's most sustained portrait of the ranchers' community in its intact, festive state. Every stratum of San Joaquin Valley society is present: the great landowners (Magnus Derrick), the rising young ranchers (Annixter, Harran), the small holders (Hooven, Broderson), the working hands, the Mexican families, the town merchants. The novel implicitly asks: what could this community have been, had the railroad not destroyed it? The dance answers: vital, warm, interconnected, occasionally absurd, capable of enormous collective joy.

### The Wheat as Organizing Force

The ranchers' discussions throughout the evening — Magnus on crop shortages, the harness room crowd on land values — keep the wheat economy present even at the moment of festivity. The dance occurs on land the railroad claims, in a barn built on contested ground. When the regrade notices arrive, the wheat asserts itself not as cornucopia but as a weapon: the railroad's monopoly pricing on the crop, and the land beneath it, is the very mechanism of exploitation.

### Violence Beneath the Surface

The Delaney episode reveals how thin the membrane of civilization is. A single armed drunkard on horseback can turn a barn full of a hundred guests into "a wild, clamouring pell-mell, blind, deaf, panic-stricken" [001-2461]. The gunfight, brief and almost farcical, prefigures the more lethal violence to come. In the aftermath, the men become bellicose: "Instantly a spirit of aggression, of truculence, swelled up underneath waistcoats and starched shirt bosoms. More than one offender was promptly asked to 'step outside'" [001-2519]. The communal spirit proves fragile.

### The Railroad's Method

S. Behrman's timing — delivering the regrade notices during the dance — is a calculated act of cruelty. As Annixter observes: "Chose his moment well. Hits his hardest when we're all rounded up having a good time" [001-2640]. The railroad acts impersonally, by mail, from a distance — in contrast to the face-to-face violence of Delaney. Both forms of assault, Norris suggests, are aspects of the same system. Delaney rides into the barn on a horse; the railroad's notice arrives on a bicycle. One is primitive and theatrical; the other is bureaucratic and devastating. The second is far more dangerous.

### The Dance as Microcosm

The three-act structure of the dance — festive assembly, violent interruption, political reckoning — mirrors the novel's larger structure. The ranchers' pre-conflict life (Act I), the railroad war (Act II), and the aftermath of destruction and dispersal (Act III) are all prefigured in this single night at Annixter's barn. The barn itself, "the biggest barn," built for sheltering the wealth of the harvest, becomes instead the birthplace of organized resistance.
