## Overview

The jackrabbit hunt is one of the novel's most vivid set-pieces — a massive community event in which the ranchers and townspeople of the San Joaquin Valley gather to drive, corral, and slaughter jackrabbits that threaten the wheat crop. The event functions simultaneously as agricultural necessity (the rabbits devastate young wheat), as social ritual (the entire community participates), and as symbolic theater (the slaughter foreshadows the human violence to come).

Norris renders the hunt with the epic scale he brings to all his major scenes. The gathering of wagons, the formation of the drive line stretching across miles of stubble, the coordinated movement of men and animals — all are described with the precision of military maneuver. The hunt is organized, disciplined, and efficient, demonstrating the community's capacity for collective action when the enemy is unambiguous.

## The Event

The hunt proceeds as a vast encircling movement. Participants form a line across the valley floor and advance, driving the rabbits before them into an ever-tightening corral. The scale is industrial: hundreds of men, thousands of rabbits, a harvest of flesh that mirrors the wheat harvest. When the corral is closed, the slaughter begins — men wielding clubs in a frenzy of destruction that transforms the disciplined hunters into something more primal.

Norris's description of the killing emphasizes its dual nature. On one level, the hunt is justified: the rabbits are a genuine agricultural threat, and their elimination is necessary for the wheat to survive. On another level, the violence itself exerts a terrible fascination. The men who club rabbits with methodical efficiency are the same men who will later face railroad agents at the irrigation ditch. The hunt's violence is both practice and premonition.

## Social Function

The jackrabbit hunt serves as the valley's great social occasion. Ranchers who see each other only at business meetings share the work of the drive. Townspeople from Bonneville and Guadalajara join the country folk. The event transcends the divisions — between large ranchers and small, between town and country, between native-born and immigrant — that otherwise structure valley society.

The communal meal that follows the hunt, with its shared food and drink, reinforces the sense of collective identity. For one day, the valley is not a collection of competing individuals but a single community united by shared labor and shared purpose. This moment of unity is what the railroad's divide-and-conquer strategy will ultimately destroy.

## Thematic Significance

The jackrabbit hunt embodies Norris's naturalist vision in microcosm. The rabbits are not villains — they are organisms pursuing their biological imperative, eating what grows, multiplying according to nature's law. The ranchers are not villains — they are protecting their livelihood, their families, their community. The violence results not from evil on either side but from the collision of incompatible imperatives. The rabbits must eat; the farmers must protect their wheat; one must die.

This dynamic explicitly parallels the larger conflict with the railroad. The railroad, like the rabbits, is not evil in any moral sense — it is a force pursuing its own logic of expansion and profit. The farmers, like themselves in the hunt, are defending their way of life against a threat that cannot be reasoned with. The difference is that the rabbits can be clubbed; the railroad cannot.

The hunt's transformation of individual men into a collective killing machine also anticipates the League's transformation of individual ranchers into a political entity. Both transformations involve the subordination of individual will to collective purpose, and both have violent outcomes. The hunt succeeds; the League fails — but the mechanism is the same.
