## Definition

In *The Octopus*, tenant farming describes the system by which landowners like Magnus Derrick lease portions of their vast ranches to small farmers who work the land in exchange for a share of the crop or a fixed payment. The tenants occupy an intermediate position in the novel's social hierarchy — they are not owners, like the Derricks or Annixter, but neither are they wage laborers. They have some independence and some investment in the land, but their tenure is precarious, dependent on the landowner's goodwill and the crop's success. When Magnus Derrick decides to "farm the whole demn rench hisseluf" [001-013], the tenants face eviction from land they have worked for years.

## Role in The Octopus

Tenant farming provides Norris with his most direct representation of economic class in rural California. Hooven, the German tenant whom everyone calls "Bismarck," is the tenant figure developed in greatest detail. He has worked on Los Muertos for "sieben yahr — seven yahr" [001-013], long enough to think of the land as his home, and his shock when he learns that Magnus may eliminate tenant leases reveals the system's fundamental insecurity: the tenant invests labor and loyalty but has no legal claim.

The tenant system also serves as a microcosm of the novel's larger social critique. Just as the ranchers are at the mercy of the railroad's rate-setting power, the tenants are at the mercy of the ranchers' land-use decisions. The chain of dependency runs from the railroad president in San Francisco down through the large ranchers, the small tenants, and finally the landless laborers. When crisis comes, it is felt at every level, but the tenants — with the fewest resources — are the first to be destroyed.

The proposed elimination of tenants on Los Muertos is one of the novel's early indicators of Magnus Derrick's moral deterioration. Having once been a paternalistic employer who maintained a feudal relationship with his tenants, Magnus bows to economic pressure and considers "farming the whole ranch himself" — a decision that separates him from the men who have worked his land and, symbolically, from the agrarian values he has always professed.

## Key Passages

> "Yoost der men I look for, Mist'r Praicely," cried the other, twisting the reins around the brake. "Yoost one minute, you wait, hey? I wanta talk mit you." [001-007]

Hooven's approach to Presley — desperate, deferential, struggling with his English — captures the tenant's social position in a single gesture. He needs a mediator, someone with "der pull mit der Governor" to speak on his behalf.

> "Say, Caraher, he tole me dis morgen — say, he tole me Mist'r Derrick gowun to farm der whole demn rench hisseluf der next yahr. No more tenants. Say, Caraher, he tole me all der tenants get der sach; Mist'r Derrick gowun to work der whole demn rench hisseluf, hey? Me, I get der sach alzoh, hey?" [001-013]

The panic in Hooven's fragmented speech — the repetition, the incomplete sentences, the desperate seeking of confirmation — embodies the existential terror of the tenant facing displacement. Seven years of labor have not purchased security.

> "By the way," Presley called after him, "Hooven wants to see you. He asked me about this idea of the Governor's of getting along without the tenants this year. Hooven wants to stay to tend the ditch and drive the cattle." [001-045]

Presley's relay of Hooven's request to Harran models the chain of communication the tenant must navigate — Hooven to Presley to Harran to Magnus — each link a potential point of failure.

## Critical Interpretation

Norris's treatment of tenant farming reflects the actual transformation of California agriculture in the late nineteenth century, as the great Spanish and Mexican land grants were subdivided, sold, and consolidated under Anglo-American ownership. The tenant system was a transitional form between the feudal *rancho* economy and the fully capitalized agribusiness that would come to dominate California in the twentieth century. Norris captures a moment when the paternalistic relationship between landowner and tenant was giving way to a purely contractual one — a shift that the tenants experience as betrayal.

Literary critics have noted the parallel between tenant dispossession and the novel's larger theme of displacement. The tenants are pushed off the land by the ranchers; the ranchers are pushed off the land by the railroad; and the railroad itself is ultimately subject to the Wheat, which flows outward to feed the world without regard for any human claim. The tenant, in this reading, is the novel's most direct figure for the human condition under naturalism: utterly at the mercy of forces beyond comprehension or control.
