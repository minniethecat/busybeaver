## Role in the Novel

Cedarquist appears in Book II as a San Francisco capitalist, introduced at the Bohemian Club dinner where [[magnus-derrick|Magnus Derrick]] and his sons are entertained. [[lyman-derrick|Lyman Derrick]] identifies him: "He is one of our representative men... He was the head of the big Atlas Iron Works. They have shut down recently, you know. Not failed exactly, but just ceased to be a paying investment, and Cedarquist closed them out" [002-2946]. This detail — the idle foundry — is the key to his character and his argument.

His function in the novel is primarily thematic: he articulates the economic critique that [[presley|Presley]] feels but cannot formulate. Where [[presley|Presley]] sees the railroad conflict in poetic terms, Cedarquist sees it as a structural problem of public indifference and corporate monopoly. The Atlas Iron Works, standing idle for want of financial backing while San Francisco spends a million dollars on a "gingerbread fête" [002-2966], becomes his emblem of California's misplaced priorities.

His brief but intense conversation with [[magnus-derrick|Magnus Derrick]] establishes an alliance between the farmer and the manufacturer — "both in the same grist between the two millstones of the lethargy of the Public and the aggression of the Trust" [002-2982] — and he directs [[presley|Presley]] toward his epic subject: "Pres, my boy, there is your epic poem ready to hand" [002-2982].

## Character Analysis

Cedarquist is Norris's portrait of the frustrated industrialist — a man of capital and vision who finds his ambitions blocked not by market forces but by public indifference. His speech to the artist [[hartrath|Hartrath]] at the dinner party is the novel's most sustained economic argument: "We don't want fairs. We want active furnaces. We don't want public statues, and fountains, and park extensions and gingerbread fêtes. We want business enterprise" [002-2966].

His critique is not socialist but capitalist: he believes in industry, enterprise, and production. The failure he diagnoses is not of capitalism but of a corrupt variant of it — one in which [[shelgrim|Shelgrim]]'s [[the-octopus-railroad|railroad monopoly]] crushes independent enterprise. "San Francisco! It is not a city — it is a Midway Plaisance" [002-2966] — this indictment of his city's preference for spectacle over substance is Norris's own authorial position made dramatic.

Cedarquist's family life, glimpsed through his wife's social activities and his two daughters' appearance at the opera, suggests a man whose public frustrations contrast with private comfort. His wife's "forever running after fads" [002-3036] — Russian countesses, Japanese poets, [[christian|Christian]] Scientists — creates an ironic counterpoint to his serious economic concerns: even his own household is not immune to the "gingerbread" culture he deplores.

## Relationships

Cedarquist's most significant interaction is with [[magnus-derrick|Magnus Derrick]], where Norris stages a meeting of two fighters — "We are both of us fighters, it seems, Mr. Derrick... Each with his particular enemy" [002-2982]. This brief alliance between farmer and manufacturer suggests a potential political coalition against the [[the-octopus-railroad|railroad monopoly]], though neither man will see it realized.

With [[presley|Presley]], Cedarquist adopts a mentor-like tone — "Hello, Pres, my boy. How is the great, the very great Poem getting on?" [002-2950] — and his injunction that the epic poem is "ready to hand" [002-2982] in the struggle between farmers and the railroad becomes [[presley|Presley]]'s creative mandate.

His exchange with [[hartrath|Hartrath]], the artist promoting the Million-Dollar Fair, draws the novel's sharpest line between productive and unproductive capital: "It is just such men as you, Mr. [[hartrath|Hartrath]], that are the ruin of us" [002-2966].

## Key Scenes

**The Opera House Dinner Party (Book II)** : Cedarquist's introduction and his extended critique of San Francisco culture form the intellectual center of Book II. His description of the idle Atlas plant — "the only activity is the sale of remnant material and scrap steel" [002-2966] — crystallizes the novel's economic argument.

**The Conversation with [[magnus-derrick|Magnus Derrick]]** : After his tirade against [[hartrath|Hartrath]], Cedarquist turns to Magnus with a new idea — a scheme involving the wheat crop and international shipping. "The manufacturer's idea was new to [[magnus-derrick|the Governor]]. He was greatly interested" [002-2988]. This conversation plants the seed for Magnus's fateful speculation.

**Ladies' Day at the Opera** : Cedarquist excuses himself to "look out for his women folk" [002-3014] and later introduces his wife and two daughters to [[presley|Presley]]. The domestic vignette humanizes the public critic.

## Thematic Significance

Cedarquist serves as the novel's voice of economic reason — a figure who can diagnose the structural sickness that [[presley|Presley]] feels but cannot name. His argument that "the two great evils of modern America" are "the lethargy of the Public and the aggression of the Trust" [002-2982] provides the theoretical framework for the novel's narrative of railroad predation.

His presence in Book II also expands the novel's geographical and social scope beyond the San Joaquin Valley. San Francisco, with its opera houses, Bohemian Clubs, "gingerbread fêtes," and idle factories, represents a second front in the railroad's war — the cultural and financial dimension of the same struggle being fought with guns in the irrigation ditch.
