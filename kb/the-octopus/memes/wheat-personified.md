## Definition

The Wheat in *The Octopus* is a personified cosmic force — the true protagonist of Norris's narrative and the subject of the "Epic of the Wheat" trilogy that the novel inaugurates. Norris consistently describes the wheat not as passive vegetation but as an active, almost willful agent: "the great earth, the mother, after its period of reproduction, its pains of labour, delivered of the fruit of its loins, slept the sleep of exhaustion, the infinite repose of the colossus, benignant, eternal, strong, the nourisher of nations, the feeder of an entire world" [001-377].

This personification operates at multiple levels. At the agricultural level, the wheat is crop — planted, tended, harvested, sold. At the biological level, it is life-force — a blind, generative energy that "came up" even when the land was "resting, unploughed, unharrowed, and unsown" [001-469]. At the cosmic level, it is a world-historical agent whose movements determine the fates of nations: "a drought on the prairies of Dakota, a rain on the plains of India, a frost on the Russian steppes, a hot wind on the llanos of the Argentine" — all these distant events, transmitted through the ticker tape in the ranchers' offices, are expressions of the Wheat's global will [001-429].

Norris's personification strategy blurs the line between metaphor and metaphysics. The wheat is not *like* a force of nature; it *is* a force of nature, as indifferent to human suffering as gravity or plate tectonics. Yet Norris repeatedly describes it in maternal, generative language — "the nourisher of nations," "the mother" — that suggests a relationship between the wheat and humanity that is not merely mechanical but organic and even loving. This tension between the wheat's indifference and its nurturing abundance is the novel's central paradox.

## Role in The Octopus

The personified Wheat serves as the counterforce to the railroad monopoly. Where the railroad represents mechanical, institutional, human-made power, the wheat represents organic, biological, natural power. The two forces struggle for control of the San Joaquin Valley, and while the railroad appears to win — the farmers are killed or ruined — the novel's closing vision suggests that the wheat's power is ultimately greater. The wheat will continue to grow, to be harvested, to be shipped, to be consumed, long after the railroad and its agents have been forgotten.

Mrs. Derrick's reaction to the wheat fields captures a crucial dimension of its personification. "To her mind there was something inordinate about it all; something almost unnatural. The direct brutality of ten thousand acres of wheat, nothing but wheat as far as the eye could see, stunned her a little... She did not want to look at so much wheat. There was something vaguely indecent in the sight, this food of the people, this elemental force, this basic energy, weltering here under the sun in all the unconscious nakedness of a sprawling, primordial Titan" [001-469]. Her discomfort registers the wheat's unsettling doubleness — it is both food (nurturing, maternal) and force (brutal, primordial, naked). The "sprawling, primordial Titan" is a being of myth, not agriculture.

Presley is the character most attuned to the wheat's personified presence. His unwritten "Epic of the West" is, in essence, an attempt to give poetic form to what he intuits about the wheat's cosmic significance. "Ha! there it was, his epic, his inspiration, his West, his thundering progression of hexameters" [001-377]. The wheat's vastness, its indifference, its cyclic rhythm — these are the materials of epic, not of the lyric poetry Presley is temperamentally suited to write. His artistic failure is the failure to find a form adequate to the wheat's scale.

## Key Passages

> The whole gigantic sweep of the San Joaquin expanded, Titanic, before the eye of the mind, flagellated with heat, quivering and shimmering under the sun's red eye. At long intervals, a faint breath of wind out of the south passed slowly over the levels of the baked and empty earth, accentuating the silence, marking off the stillness. It seemed to exhale from the land itself, a prolonged sigh as of deep fatigue. It was the season after the harvest, and the great earth, the mother, after its period of reproduction, its pains of labour, delivered of the fruit of its loins, slept the sleep of exhaustion, the infinite repose of the colossus, benignant, eternal, strong, the nourisher of nations, the feeder of an entire world.
>
> — [001-377]

This is the novel's most sustained personification of the wheat-earth complex. The progression from "the great earth" to "the mother" to "the colossus" to "the nourisher of nations" traces an ascending scale of personification, from agricultural metaphor through mythic figure to world-historical agent. The language of labor and delivery — "reproduction," "pains of labour," "delivered of the fruit of its loins" — casts the harvest as birth, the earth as parturient mother.

> The direct brutality of ten thousand acres of wheat, nothing but wheat as far as the eye could see, stunned her a little. [...] There was something vaguely indecent in the sight, this food of the people, this elemental force, this basic energy, weltering here under the sun in all the unconscious nakedness of a sprawling, primordial Titan.
>
> — [001-469]

Mrs. Derrick's perspective offers the novel's most ambivalent treatment of the wheat. The words "brutality," "indecent," "nakedness," and "primordial" register the wheat's unsettling physicality — it is not beautiful, not pastoral, not the gentle agriculture of her Ohio childhood. It is something older and wilder: a Titan, a being from before the domestication of the earth.

> At such moments they no longer felt their individuality. The ranch became merely the part of an enormous whole, a unit in the vast agglomeration of wheat land the whole world round, feeling the effects of causes thousands of miles distant — a drought on the prairies of Dakota, a rain on the plains of India, a frost on the Russian steppes, a hot wind on the llanos of the Argentine.
>
> — [001-429]

This passage reveals the wheat's world-systemic dimension. The ticker tape that connects the ranchers' offices to Chicago and Liverpool is the nervous system of a global wheat-body. The ranchers lose their individuality not because the railroad crushes them (though it does) but because they are subsumed into the wheat's planetary scale of operation. The wheat, not the railroad, is the truly global power.

## Critical Interpretation

The personification of wheat in *The Octopus* has been central to naturalist criticism since the novel's publication. Early reviewers recognized that Norris was attempting something unprecedented: a novel whose protagonist was not a person but a crop. William Dean Howells, reviewing the novel in 1901, praised Norris's "epic" ambition while noting the difficulty of making wheat a sympathetic hero.

Donald Pizer's influential reading treats the wheat as Norris's metaphor for the naturalist universe itself — a force that is simultaneously creative and destructive, nurturing and indifferent. The wheat's cycle of growth and harvest, in Pizer's reading, is Norris's answer to the problem of determinism: if human life is determined by forces beyond individual control, those forces are at least generative, producing abundance even as they produce suffering.

Ecocritical approaches have read the personified wheat as an early example of what Lawrence Buell calls "environmental imagination" — the attempt to represent non-human agency in literary form. Norris's wheat is not a passive backdrop for human action but an active participant in the narrative. The novel's closing image — the wheat continuing to flow toward the starving in India even after the ranchers who grew it are dead — is, in this reading, a genuinely ecological vision: the recognition that human lives are embedded in cycles of production and consumption that transcend individual morality.

Marxist critics have been more skeptical, noting that the wheat's personification obscures the economic relations of production that the novel otherwise documents so carefully. Calling the wheat a "life-force" or a "Titan" mystifies the fact that it is a commodity whose value is realized through labor — the labor of the farmers, the railroad workers, the stevedores who load it onto ships. The wheat's "unconscious nakedness" is, from this perspective, the nakedness of capital itself.
