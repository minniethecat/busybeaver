## Overview

Dyke's story is a complete tragic arc — one of the most carefully constructed narrative threads in *The Octopus*. An honest, hard-working railroad engineer with over a decade of loyal service, Dyke is fired for refusing to accept a wage cut. He invests his entire life savings into a hop ranch, only to see the Railroad double his freight rate from two cents to five cents per pound, destroying his profit and leaving him financially ruined. Driven to desperation, he holds up the Overland express train, shoots and kills a brakeman, and flees into the Sierra foothills. After weeks as a hunted fugitive, he is captured in a violent struggle and sentenced to life in prison. His arc from loyal employee to convicted felon is Norris's most devastating illustration of how the Railroad manufactures the criminals it then destroys.

## Narrative Context

Dyke's story unfolds across the entire span of the novel, from Book I, Chapter I through the Conclusion. It forms one of three parallel narrative lines (alongside the ranchers' League and Vanamee's spiritual quest) but is structurally distinct: it is the purest example of individual tragedy, uncomplicated by collective politics or mystical experience.

The arc divides into four clear phases. In Phase 1 (Book I, Chapter I), Dyke is introduced as a veteran P. & S.W. engineer, described as "a heavy built, well-looking fellow, nearly twice the weight of Presley, with great shoulders and massive, hairy arms, and a tremendous, rumbling voice" [001-141]. He is fired for refusing to accept a wage cut despite having risked his life during a strike, running trains "with a gun in each hand, never knowing when I was going over a mined culvert" [001-165].

In Phase 2 (Book I, Chapters II-V), Dyke pivots to hop farming, investing his savings and borrowing from S. Behrman — the very agent of the Railroad that fired him. Harran warns him: "Well, mortgage the crops, but don't mortgage the homestead" [001-607]. Dyke checks the rate at two cents per pound and proceeds confidently, assuring Presley: "The rates on hops *are fair*. I've got to admit that; I was in to Bonneville a while ago to find out. It's two cents a pound, and Lord love you, that's reasonable enough to suit any man" [001-2087].

In Phase 3 (Book II, Chapters I-II), the spring morning begins idyllically — Dyke wakes early, kisses his sleeping daughter Sidney, slips a dime into her stocking, and drives into Bonneville in high spirits [002-3336, 3344]. Then comes the catastrophe. At the freight office, he discovers the rate has been raised to five cents. S. Behrman himself pronounces the railroad's principle: "All — the — traffic — will — bear" [002-3456]. Dyke is ruined.

In Phase 4 (Book II, Chapters IV-V and Conclusion), Dyke robs the Overland express, kills a brakeman, and becomes a hunted outlaw. After weeks of pursuit, he is captured in the chaparral. The novel's epilogue reveals he is "sentenced for life" [002-6990]. His mother, broken, tells Presley: "I am going to die very soon, Mr. Presley. There is no reason why I should live any longer. My son is in prison for life" [002-7212].

## Detailed Account

### The Discharge

Dyke's firing establishes the Railroad's policy of treating loyalty as worthless. He stood with the company during a violent strike, refusing to join the Brotherhood. His reward is a wage cut equal to "any dirty little wiper in the yard" [001-165]. When he protests, the company gives him his walking papers. Norris makes the injustice stark:

> "And now, when hard times come along, and they are cutting wages, what do they do? Do they make any discrimination in my case? Do they remember the man that stood by them and risked his life in their service? No." [001-165]

Dyke's challenge to Presley — "*Name one*" railroad not controlled by the P. & S.W. — is unanswerable, establishing the monopoly's total reach [001-171].

### The Hop Venture

Dyke's optimism is painful to witness in hindsight. He pours his entire ten years of savings into the hop ranch, building drying-houses and setting out "a bewildering, innumerable multitude of poles, connected with a maze of wire and twine" [001-2063]. He mortgages both crop and homestead to S. Behrman, the man Annixter calls "railroad to his boots" [001-605].

His motivation is his daughter. Everything is for "the little tad," Sidney, whom he plans to send to a seminary in Marysville. "Smartest little tad in Tulare County," he boasts. "Can recite the whole of 'Snow Bound' without ever stopping" [001-1829]. Their domestic life is rendered with extraordinary tenderness — Dyke lifting Sidney on his feet like a circus acrobat, the two of them "children both" [002-3336].

Norris deliberately invests this domestic happiness with full detail, making the destruction that follows unbearable. A particularly acute touch: Sidney, who barely understands the adult world's grievances, nevertheless "hisses at the train" and "spits her little spit on 'em" whenever a P. & S.W. locomotive passes [001-2082].

### The Rate Increase

The catastrophe scene in Ruggles's freight office is one of the novel's most concentrated moments of dramatic irony. Dyke has come to town in a state of near-euphoria, his crop contracted, his profit assured. He even pauses to buy Sidney a "transparent slate," the toy of a father who believes he can afford small luxuries [002-3367].

At the office, the clerk informs him the rate is five cents. Dyke protests that he was told two cents the previous fall. The clerk's reply — "That was last fall" — is the novel's coldest sentence [002-3420]. S. Behrman delivers the ruling philosophy of the Railroad:

> "All — the — traffic — will — bear." [002-3456]

Each word punctuated with a tap of the forefinger. Dyke's calculations, his contracts, his daughter's future — all erased by a single administrative decision. He sees the panorama of his ruin unroll before his mind's eye:

> "Every cent of his earnings was sunk in this hop business of his. More than that, he had borrowed money to carry it on, certain of success — borrowed of S. Behrman, offering his crop and his little home as security. Once he failed to meet his obligations, S. Behrman would foreclose." [002-3480]

### Caraher's Saloon and the Descent

Dyke stops at Caraher's saloon, where the anarchist saloonkeeper works on him with whiskey and revolutionary rhetoric. Caraher's argument is naked instrumentalism: "There's one thing only it does listen to, one thing it is frightened of — the people with dynamite in their hands — six inches of plugged gaspipe. *That* talks" [002-3564].

Presley, passing the open door, sees Dyke's back: "broad, muscled, bowed down, the great shoulders stooping" [002-3580]. The image is of a man broken physically as well as financially. Annixter pronounces the grim verdict: "Exit Dyke, and score another tally for S. Behrman, Shelgrim and Co." [002-3582].

Norris makes a crucial narrative choice here: the actual robbery is not dramatized directly. The reader learns of it through the passengers on the Overland, who experience the holdup as a terrifying disruption of the ordinary. The brakeman identifies his assailant by his grudge against the road [002-4310]. The doctor on board delivers the clinical facts: "He shot the brakeman through the lungs twice" and "got away with about five thousand in gold coin" [002-4304].

The robbery is described as a feat of precision engineering — fitting for a former railroad man. Dyke rides the roof of the express car, jumps onto the coal tender, disarms the engine crew, uncouples the express car himself, dynamites the safe, and escapes on a horse he had tethered in advance at a road crossing [002-4295].

### The Manhunt and Capture

Days of pursuit follow. S. Behrman placards Bonneville with a $500 reward, "dead or alive" [002-4374]. Bloodhounds are brought from San Jose. The entire county mobilizes. Dyke hides in the mountains, emerging starving and desperate. He makes a last dash to Annixter's ranch for a fresh horse — a scene of desperate pathos in which Annixter gives him the buckskin mare:

> "God bless you, Annixter. Where's the little tad? Is she well, Annixter, and the mother?" [002-4930]

Dyke commandeers a locomotive at Guadalajara — a return to his old identity. The locomotive pursuit is one of Norris's great set-pieces, two engines racing on parallel tracks, men leaning from cabs exchanging gunfire:

> "Roaring, clamouring, reeking with the smell of powder and hot oil, spitting death, resistless, huge, furious, an abrupt vision of chaos — the two engines met and passed." [002-5018]

But the moment he abandons the engine, his identity shifts irrevocably:

> "For the last time in his life, he had been the engineer. Now, once more, he became the highwayman, the outlaw against whom all hands were raised, the fugitive skulking in the mountains, listening for the cry of dogs." [002-5024]

The final capture occurs in the chaparral. Four men against one, Dyke fights with "colossal strength," his arms working "like pistons" [002-5088]. At the critical moment, Dyke faces S. Behrman at ten feet and pulls the trigger: "Had the cartridge exploded, death, certain and swift, would have followed, but at this, of all moments, the revolver missed fire" [002-5090]. The misfire is Norris's clearest statement that naturalist forces — chance, the impersonal operations of systems — determine outcomes, not individual will.

### The Aftermath

S. Behrman forecloses on the hop ranch. Annixter takes in Mrs. Dyke and Sidney. The news of Dyke's conviction and life sentence reaches Presley on the County Road. His meditation captures the novel's tragic vision:

> "To this, Dyke had been brought, Dyke, than whom no man had been more honest, more courageous, more jovial. This was the end of him, a prison; this was his final estate, a criminal." [002-6994]

## Character Perspectives

**Presley** serves as Dyke's primary witness. From their first meeting in the cab of the freight engine, Presley sees Dyke as simultaneously sympathetic and representative — his "friend" but also a specimen of railroad injustice. Presley's reaction to the rate increase drives him toward his unwritten "Epic of the Wheat" and, later, toward the failed attempt to assassinate S. Behrman.

**Annixter** offers the novel's most pragmatic perspective. He warns Dyke not to mortgage to S. Behrman, sees the disaster coming, and pronounces Dyke "stuck." Yet Annixter undergoes his own moral awakening, and his decision to shelter Dyke's mother and Sidney — "I've got to think of those that ain't got anywhere to go" [002-4806] — is one of the novel's few redemptive acts.

**S. Behrman** is the antagonist who destroys Dyke without malice. His demeanor throughout — "invariably placid, unruffled, never losing his temper" [001-1991] — makes him more terrifying than a villain who rages. He forecloses on Dyke's homestead, posts the reward for his capture, and personally joins the posse that hunts him down. His presence at the final capture, watching the fight "complacently" [002-5098], is the novel's most chilling image of institutional indifference.

**Caraher** is the catalyst of Dyke's fall. His barroom rhetoric — dynamite, plugged gaspipe, violent revolution — provides the ideological justification for Dyke's turn to crime. But Presley ultimately judges him as "a bad man, a plague spot in the world of the ranchers, poisoning the farmers' bodies with alcohol and their minds with discontent" [002-6996].

**Mrs. Dyke and Sidney** represent the collateral damage that Norris insists upon showing. Mrs. Dyke's grief-stricken monologue — "He was not right in his mind. They hounded him to it" [002-4414] — articulates the novel's determinist argument. Sidney, "daughter of a convict" [002-7214], is weighed down at the "threshold of existence with a load of disgrace."

## Thematic Significance

### The Railroad as Crime Factory

Dyke's arc makes Norris's most explicit argument about the relationship between corporate power and individual criminality. The Railroad manufactures Dyke's crime through a sequence of institutional decisions — the wage cut, the firing, the rate increase — none of which is "evil" in intent. Each is a rational business calculation. The cumulative effect is murder and a life sentence. Presley's final summary is definitive:

> "It had hounded Dyke from his legitimate employment and had made of him a highwayman and criminal." [002-7388]

### The Failure of Individual Agency

Dyke's tragedy is that every decision he makes is rational in isolation and catastrophic in aggregate. He leaves railroading because the wage cut is unjust. He enters hops because the market conditions are favorable. He borrows from S. Behrman because he needs capital. He checks the rate because he is prudent. None of these choices prevents his destruction. Norris's naturalism insists that individuals are swept by forces larger than their own judgment.

### "All the Traffic Will Bear"

S. Behrman's three-word principle is the philosophical core of the novel's critique. It asserts a purely extractive relationship between the Railroad and the people it supposedly serves — a relationship governed not by fairness, custom, or mutual benefit, but by the maximum the market can be forced to pay. The principle applies equally to Dyke's hop rate and to the wheat ranchers' grain tariffs.

### The Cycle of Violence

The novel traces a tight causal chain: corporate exploitation produces individual desperation; desperation produces crime; crime produces state violence (the manhunt, the life sentence). The system that creates the criminal then punishes him for his criminality, never acknowledging its own role. This cycle, Norris suggests, is the Railroad's true social function — not transportation, but the extraction and destruction of human lives.
