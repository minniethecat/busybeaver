## Role in the Novel

Young Vacca appears primarily in the novel's central social scenes, his role defined by service rather than action. At [[annixter|Annixter]]'s barn dance, he is introduced as "young Vacca, son of one of the division superintendents, who wore a checked coat and white thread gloves and who paced up and down the length of the barn, frowning, very important, whittling a wax candle over the floor to make it slippery for dancing" [002-2219]. This image — the young man absorbed in his task, frowning with self-importance — captures both the communal effort behind the dance and the novel's affection for its minor characters.

His dedication to the floor-waxing becomes a running joke: "Young Vacca, now at his fiftieth wax candle, had brought the floor to the slippery surface of glass" [002-2349], and later, "Young Vacca indefatigably continued the rounds of the barn, paring candle after candle, possessed with this single idea of duty" [002-2413]. This comic persistence — pushing dancers aside, refusing to admit the floor was yet sufficiently slippery — humanizes the grand social event.

Beyond the barn dance, Vacca serves as [[annixter|Annixter]]'s reliable hand. When [[annixter|Annixter]] returns from San Francisco with his new wife [[hilma-tree|Hilma]], it is "young Vacca" who meets them with the team [003-4324], and it is Vacca who points out the posse assembling on the night of the tragedy: "It's the posse" [003-4330].

## Character Analysis

Vacca represents the ordinary competence and good nature of the valley's young men — figures who are not protagonists but whose presence fills out the novel's social world. His single-minded attention to waxing the dance floor suggests a character who finds satisfaction in practical tasks done well, a quality Norris presents without irony.

His role as witness — to the barn dance's gaiety, to the posse's grim departure — positions him at the boundary between the community's celebratory life and its violent transformation. He is not a driver of events but a register of them, the reliable young man who sees what happens and remembers.

The detail of his "checked coat and white thread gloves" [002-2219] at the dance suggests aspiration — the young man dressing up for the social occasion of the season, conscious of his appearance, "frowning, very important." Norris's treatment of Vacca is affectionate, granting dignity to minor effort.

## Relationships

Vacca's relationship with [[annixter|Annixter]] is that of trusted subordinate. [[annixter|Annixter]] telephones the ranch "to have young Vacca bring the buckskin" [003-3576] when he needs transport — the casual delegation suggesting established reliability. After [[annixter|Annixter]]'s marriage, Vacca drives the newlyweds home from [[bonneville|Bonneville]].

With the broader community, Vacca is a figure of cheerful utility. His floor-waxing at the barn dance is performed for everyone's benefit, and his presence at the social event's edge — paring candles, pushing through crowds — places him as both participant and facilitator.

## Key Scenes

**The Barn Dance Floor (Book I, Chapter VI)** : Vacca's comic dedication to waxing the dance floor — "now at his fiftieth wax candle" [002-2349] — provides one of the barn dance sequence's most human details, a minor character absorbed in a task whose importance only he fully appreciates.

**The Return from San Francisco** : Vacca meets [[annixter|Annixter]] and [[hilma-tree|Hilma]] with the team after their wedding trip. [[annixter|Annixter]]'s affectionate pride — "we'll have Vacca meet us with the team" [003-4020] — suggests the young man's place in the ranch's domestic life.

**The Posse's Departure** : As [[annixter|Annixter]] and [[hilma-tree|Hilma]] drive through [[bonneville|Bonneville]] on the night of the tragedy, Vacca points out the assembling posse [003-4328 through 003-4330]. His recognition of what is happening — "It's the posse" — marks the moment when the community's ordinary nighttime becomes extraordinary.

## Thematic Significance

Vacca's value to the novel is atmospheric — he is part of the texture of community life that the railroad's violence will tear apart. His comic floor-waxing at the barn dance occurs at the novel's high point of communal celebration; his later appearance as driver and witness occurs as the tragedy gathers. Norris uses such minor figures to establish the normality that violence ruptures.

His family name — Vacca — appears in the novel's social landscape through his mother Adela, a division superintendent's wife remembered for losing her garter at a social gathering [002-2093]. The Vaccas, like the [[hooven|Hooven]]s, the [[cutter|Cutter]]s, and the Derricks, belong to the valley's fabric — and the railroad's predation threatens them all.
