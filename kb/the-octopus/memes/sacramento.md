## Overview

Sacramento is the political capital of California and the seat of state government in Frank Norris's *The Octopus*. Unlike San Francisco, which represents the Railroad's corporate and market power, Sacramento embodies the political and legislative arena where the struggle between the wheat ranchers and the railroad is fought — and where the ranchers are consistently outmatched. It is a city of committees and caucuses, of lobbyists and legislators, of political ambition and institutional corruption. For Magnus Derrick, it is the site of his greatest political defeat (his failed gubernatorial campaign) and his deepest moral compromise (the bribery of convention delegates). For his son Lyman, it is the destination of an all-consuming ambition — "Lyman, with shut teeth, had resolved that some day he would sit in the gubernatorial chair in Sacramento" (627-628).

## Role in the Narrative

Sacramento functions in the novel as the **arena of political power** — the place where laws governing railroad rates and land rights are made, and where those laws are shown to be instruments of monopoly rather than justice.

First, it is the seat of the **Railroad Commission**, the ostensibly independent regulatory body that is in fact controlled by the railroad itself. Lyman Derrick's election to the Commission — engineered through the railroad's support — reveals the depth of corporate capture of state government. When Annixter enumerates the railroad's power, Sacramento is central: "He keeps a million-dollar lobby at Sacramento every minute of the time the legislature is in session" (971-975). The Commission that should regulate the railroad is, as Annixter bitterly notes, "in his pocket."

Second, Sacramento is the site of **Magnus Derrick's political history and fall**. His gubernatorial campaign — the high point of his political career — was centered there. Lyman was "born at Sacramento during the turmoil and excitement of Derrick's campaign for governor" (491-495). But decades later, Magnus returns to Sacramento politics not as a candidate but as a briber, purchasing Democratic convention delegates to secure Lyman's nomination to the Railroad Commission (4616-4620, 4688-4692). This moral fall — from principled statesman to corrupt bargainer — is the novel's central tragic arc, and Sacramento is its stage.

Third, it is the destination of **Lyman Derrick's ambition**. Unlike his father, who saw politics as the application of principles, Lyman "saw but the office, his own personal aggrandisement." His goal, repeatedly emphasized, is "to sit in the gubernatorial chair in Sacramento" (627-635). The revelation that Lyman was "pledged to the railroad two years ago" — that his election to the Commission was not a rancher victory but a railroad stratagem — makes Sacramento a city of betrayal as well as ambition (4702-4708).

## Description in the Text

Norris does not describe Sacramento's physical appearance in detail; the city exists in the novel primarily as a **site of political action** rather than a sensory landscape. Its character is conveyed through the transactions that occur there:

> S. Behrman had been much in evidence in and about the San Francisco court rooms and the lobby of the legislature in Sacramento. (525-528)

Annixter's single extended physical encounter with Sacramento is grimly comic: having nearly worked himself to death preparing for his bar examinations, "he all but died in a Sacramento boardinghouse, obstinately refusing to have anything to do with doctors, whom he vituperated as a rabble of quacks, dosing himself with a patent medicine and stuffing himself almost to bursting with liver pills and dried prunes" (211-218). The boardinghouse — anonymous, harsh, indifferent — is the only domestic interior Norris associates with the capital.

The bribery scandal brings Sacramento momentarily into stark focus at the novel's climax. Genslinger's newspaper exposé — "the story of how the Sacramento convention was bought by Magnus Derrick" (6108-6112) — reduces the city to a single damning fact: it is where Magnus's honor was sold.

## Symbolic Meaning

Sacramento represents the **political dimension of naturalist determinism** in Norris's world. If San Francisco is where the market controls the ranchers, Sacramento is where the law does — or rather, where the law is revealed to be an extension of the market. The million-dollar lobby, the bought delegates, the captured commission: these are not aberrations but the normal functioning of a system in which political power is simply another commodity.

The Demise of Magnus Derrick is the defeat of a certain idea of politics — the old-school statesmanship of principles and orations — by the new reality of "committees, caucuses, compromises and expedients" (627-635). Magnus's bribery is not merely a personal moral failure; it is his reluctant, desperate adaptation to a political environment that has been structurally corrupted by the railroad's money. He enters the Sacramento fight believing he can play the game, but Genslinger's devastating revelation — "Lyman was pledged to the railroad two years ago" — shows that the game was rigged before he ever sat down at the table.

> "When will you people realise that you can't buck against the Railroad? Why, Magnus, it's like me going out in a paper boat and shooting peas at a battleship." (4702-4708)

Sacramento is the name of that battleship.
