## Role in the Novel

S. Behrman is the human face of the [[the-octopus-railroad|railroad monopoly]] in Frank Norris's *The Octopus*. As the banker of [[bonneville|Bonneville]], he controls the financial infrastructure through which the [[the-railroad|Pacific and Southwestern Railroad]] exercises its power over the wheat ranchers. His name appears everywhere — on signs reading "S. Behrman, Real Estate, Mortgages, Main Street, [[bonneville|Bonneville]]" and "S. Behrman Has Something To Say To You" [001-006] — establishing him as an omnipresent force before he ever speaks a line of dialogue.

Behrman is the novel's most purely malignant character. Unlike [[shelgrim|Shelgrim]], the railroad president who operates at a philosophical remove, Behrman works at ground level, personally supervising "everything from the consignment of a shipment of wheat to the management of a damage suit" [002-058]. "There was no denying the fact that for [[osterman|Osterman]], [[broderson|Broderson]], [[annixter|Annixter]] and Derrick, S. Behrman was the railroad" [002-058].

His function is to translate corporate power into personal menace — he forecloses mortgages, manipulates freight rates, and smiles while doing it. He is [[railroad-monopoly|the Octopus]] made flesh.

## Character Analysis

Norris draws S. Behrman as a study in grotesque physicality. He is "corpulent" with a "thick, throaty voice" [002-056], constantly wiping the back of his neck with his handkerchief [002-062]. His body — soft, sweaty, expansive — contrasts with the lean, erect figures of the ranchers, particularly [[magnus-derrick|Magnus Derrick]], who stands "head and shoulders above the other, tall, thin, erect" [002-060].

This physical contrast carries moral weight. The ranchers work the land with their bodies; Behrman's corpulence suggests a man who feeds on others' labor without producing anything himself. His insinuating manner — "blandly" remonstrating [002-064], spreading out his hands and smiling [002-073] — masks the violence of his economic power. He never raises his voice because he never needs to.

Behrman's most revealing trait is his absolute identification with the railroad's interests. He does not merely work for the corporation; he has internalized its logic completely. When challenged on freight rates, he calmly explains that "the lowest rates that the legislature can establish must be such as will secure us a fair interest on our investment" [002-069] — as if the railroad's profit were a law of nature rather than a political choice.

## Relationships

Behrman's relationship with [[magnus-derrick|Magnus Derrick]] defines the novel's central conflict. Their roadside confrontation in Book I, Chapter II establishes the power dynamics: Magnus refuses Behrman's extended hand [002-060], a symbolic rejection of the collaborative relationship Behrman pretends to offer. Behrman responds not with anger but with wounded innocence — "Now, you know better than that, [[harran-derrick|Harran]]" [002-064] — a performance of victimhood that masks his complete control of the situation.

With the other ranchers, Behrman adopts the same approach: ostensible friendliness backed by unanswerable economic force. He knows every rancher's finances, every mortgage, every vulnerability. His power is informational as much as financial — he was "in and about the San Francisco court rooms and the lobby of the legislature in Sacramento" during the grain-rate case [002-058], working [[bonneville-government|the political machine]]ry while the farmers worked their fields.

Behrman's connection to [[shelgrim|Shelgrim]], the railroad president, reveals the chain of command. Behrman is the local executor of policies crafted in San Francisco; his ruthlessness is authorized and enabled by the corporate hierarchy above him.

## Key Scenes

**First Impression (Book I, Chapter I)**: Before Behrman appears in person, his name saturates the landscape. The freshly painted signs — "S. Behrman, Real Estate, Mortgages" and "S. Behrman Has Something To Say To You" [001-006] — announce his dominion over [[bonneville|Bonneville]]'s economic life. He is already everywhere before he is anywhere.

**The Roadside Confrontation (Book I, Chapter II)**: Behrman intercepts Magnus and [[harran-derrick|Harran]] on the road, ostensibly to offer condolences on the lost rate case. "I saw in the city papers yesterday that our case had gone against you" [002-062] — the pronoun "our" is characteristic, a pretense of shared interest. When Magnus refuses his hand, Behrman maintains his bland smile. The confrontation crystallizes the novel's central opposition: the rancher's dignity against the banker's corrupt cordiality.

**The Rate Debate (Book I, Chapter II)**: [[harran-derrick|Harran]] and Behrman clash over freight rates in an exchange that lays bare the railroad's logic. Behrman invokes "operating expenses," "fixed charges," and "a fair interest on our investment" [002-069] — the language of corporate inevitability. For the ranchers, these are abstractions; for Behrman, they are articles of faith.

**The Final Confrontation (Book II)**: Behrman's role in the novel's climactic events — the bribery exposure, the land seizures, [[mussel-slough-tragedy|the shootout]] — completes his arc from economic predator to agent of destruction. His final fate, buried alive in a wheat bin, delivers the novel's most direct poetic justice: the man who lived by consuming others' wheat is consumed by it in turn.

## Thematic Significance

S. Behrman personifies the [[the-octopus-railroad|railroad monopoly]] in its most intimate form. He is not an abstract corporation but a man who knows each rancher by name, who has eaten at their tables and foreclosed on their mortgages. This intimacy makes his predation more devastating, not less — betrayal requires proximity.

Behrman also embodies Norris's critique of the new economic order. He is a "middleman" in the fullest sense: neither producer nor consumer, he extracts value from every transaction without contributing to it. His prosperity is purely parasitic, measured in the mortgages he holds and the rates he controls. The wheat the ranchers grow and the railroad transports both pass through Behrman's ledgers, and he takes his cut from every bushel.

His death by wheat — suffocated in the very commodity he exploited — is Norris's most explicit act of naturalist retribution. The Wheat, personified throughout the novel as a life-force, becomes an instrument of justice. Behrman, who thought he controlled the wheat by controlling its transportation, learns that the force he sought to dominate will not be dominated.
