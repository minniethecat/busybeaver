## Definition

In Frank Norris's novel *The Octopus*, the "Octopus" is the metaphor for the Pacific and Southwestern Railroad (P. and S.W.), a monopolistic corporation whose tentacles reach into every aspect of life in the San Joaquin Valley. The railroad controls shipping rates, land ownership, and political influence, leaving wheat farmers helpless against its power. The metaphor captures the naturalist theme of vast, impersonal forces crushing individual human will.

## Role in The Octopus

The railroad functions as the primary antagonist of the novel. It is not a single villain but a system — a corporate entity that operates through many agents: S. Behrman (the local banker and railroad agent), the Railroad Commission (which sets rates in the railroad's favor), and the legal system that upholds the railroad's land claims. The conflict between the wheat farmers and the railroad drives the entire plot, culminating in the violent confrontation known as the Mussel Slough tragedy.

The railroad's power manifests in three ways:
- **Economic control**: Setting freight rates that make wheat farming unprofitable [001-043]
- **Land ownership**: Claiming ownership of the land the farmers have worked for years, based on original land grants
- **Political corruption**: Manipulating the Railroad Commission and local officials [001-043]

## Key Passages

> "He holds, Ulsteen does, that 'grain rates as low as the new figure would amount to confiscation of property, and that, on such a basis, the railroad could not be operated at a legitimate profit.'" [001-043]

This passage reveals the railroad's economic logic — the assertion that any reduction in freight rates constitutes "confiscation," treating the railroad's profit as a property right above the farmers' survival.

> "He had set himself the task of giving true, absolutely true, poetical expression to the life of the ranch, and yet, again and again, he brought up against the railroad, that stubborn iron barrier against which his romance shattered itself to froth and disintegrated, flying spume." [001-046]

Presley's artistic struggle mirrors the thematic conflict of the novel — the railroad as an immovable force that destroys the romantic ideal of Western agrarian life. The "stubborn iron barrier" is both literal (the tracks) and metaphorical (the economic system).

## Thematic Significance

The Octopus metaphor embodies Norris's naturalist philosophy. The railroad is not evil in a moral sense — it is a force of nature, like the wheat itself. Norris presents both the wheat and the railroad as vast, impersonal powers between which individual humans are crushed. This deterministic view is the core of literary naturalism: characters are shaped by forces beyond their control — economic, biological, environmental.

The novel's title itself declares this theme. The octopus, with its many tentacles reaching everywhere, represents:
- The railroad's physical network spreading across the valley
- Its financial control over every aspect of farmers' lives
- The impossibility of escape from its influence
