## Overview

The Ploughmen's Barbecue is a communal feast held at Los Muertos during the ploughing and sowing season. Presley, the poet-observer, witnesses the event and recognizes in it something ancient and elemental — a ritual of sustenance that connects the San Joaquin ranchers to the Homeric feasts of antiquity. The scene functions as Norris's most concentrated celebration of agrarian vitality before the novel's descent into tragedy.

The feast is described in Book I, Chapter I, establishing the rhythms of ranch life at Los Muertos before the railroad conflict intensifies. It also provides one of Presley's key moments of aesthetic perception: his realization that the "epic" he has been searching for is not in some distant past or exotic locale, but in the daily life of the wheat lands.

## Narrative Context

The Ploughmen's Barbecue occurs early in the novel, when Los Muertos is still an image of wholeness and abundance. The wheat has not yet been threatened by the railroad's rate manipulation. Magnus Derrick is still the "Governor," presiding over his domain with Jovian authority. The feast embodies a world that the novel will spend its remaining chapters dismantling.

Presley's observation of the feast is critical to his artistic development. He has been searching for the subject of his great poem — the "Song of the West" — and in the ploughmen's crude, vigorous feasting, he glimpses it. The scene enacts his central aesthetic problem: how to find romance and epic dignity in the material, sordid reality of the San Joaquin. His conclusion — that the feast is "Homeric" — is the resolution he will carry into his poetic project.

## Detailed Account

### The Setting

The feast takes place at the Los Muertos ranch house, in a long dining hall lit by kerosene lamps. The ploughmen — the army of laborers who work Magnus Derrick's vast wheat operation — gather at a long table covered with oilcloth. Norris establishes the scene with sensory precision:

> "The table was taken as if by assault; the clatter of iron knives upon the tin plates was as the reverberation of hail upon a metal roof." [001-1225]

The simile — hail on metal — conveys both the violence and the naturalness of the eating. These are men who work with bodies, and they eat with bodies. Nothing is dainty or refined about the scene.

### The Feast

Norris catalogs the consumption with almost ethnographic attention:

> "The ploughmen rinsed their throats with great draughts of wine, and, their elbows wide, their foreheads flushed, resumed the attack upon the beef and bread, eating as though they would never have enough. All up and down the long table, where the kerosene lamps reflected themselves deep in the oilcloth cover, one heard the incessant sounds of mastication, and saw the uninterrupted movement of great jaws." [001-1225]

The language is deliberately excessive: "assault," "attack," "great draughts," "incessant sounds," "uninterrupted movement." Norris is pushing against the conventions of polite dining to insist on the physical reality of eating — the jaw as machine, the stomach as engine. These men are engaged in the primal act of converting the land's produce into human energy.

> "At every moment one or another of the men demanded a fresh portion of beef, another pint of wine, another half-loaf of bread. For upwards of an hour the gang ate. It was no longer a supper. It was a veritable barbecue, a crude and primitive feasting, barbaric, homeric." [001-1225]

The escalation is deliberate: from "supper" to "barbecue" to "barbaric" to "Homeric." Each term reaches further back into human history. Norris is claiming that this scene on a California wheat ranch in the 1880s is continuous with the banquets of the *Iliad* — that the American West is not a departure from epic civilization but its natural heir.

### Presley's Perception

Presley's response to the scene is central to his character arc and to Norris's thematic project. He is "delighted with it all":

> "It was Homeric, this feasting, this vast consuming of meat and bread and wine, followed now by games of strength. An epic simplicity and directness, an honest Anglo-Saxon mirth and innocence, commended it. Crude it was; coarse it was, but no taint of viciousness was here." [001-5352]

The word "Homeric" recurs — it is the key to Norris's aesthetic of the West. The epic is not found in knights and dragons but in the "vast consuming of meat and bread and wine." The "games of strength" that follow the feast — wrestling, feats of endurance — complete the parallel to the Homeric athletic contests.

Presley's conclusion is a declaration of faith in the people he observes:

> "These people were good people, kindly, benignant even, always readier to give than to receive, always more willing to help than to be helped. They were good stock. Of such was the backbone of the nation — sturdy Americans everyone of them. Where else in the world round were such strong, honest men, such strong, beautiful women?" [001-5352]

The rhetoric is nationalist ("backbone of the nation," "sturdy Americans") and gendered ("strong honest men... strong beautiful women"), reflecting Norris's investment in a vision of American exceptionalism grounded in agrarian virtue. But the perception is also genuinely democratic — Presley finds epic dignity not in kings or generals but in ploughmen and ranch hands.

## Significance

The Ploughmen's Barbecue is Norris's clearest statement of the agrarian ideal that the railroad will destroy. The feast is the novel's image of wholeness: land producing wheat, wheat feeding men, men working the land in a closed cycle of production and consumption. The Railroad, by contrast, severs this cycle — it inserts itself between the land and the men, extracting value that should nourish the community.

The feast also establishes Presley's aesthetic education. His recognition that the Homeric exists in the San Joaquin is the breakthrough that enables his poem "The Toilers." Yet the poem will prove inadequate to the reality of the railroad's violence — the epic feast cannot contain the tragedy that follows. The Ploughmen's Barbecue thus stands as both the novel's high point of agrarian celebration and the measure of what is lost.
