## Purpose and Formation

The League is formed by the San Joaquin Valley wheat ranchers as a collective response to the Pacific and Southwestern Railroad's economic stranglehold on the region. Facing rising freight rates and declining wheat prices — "grain rates are increasing, while, on the other hand, the price of wheat is sagging lower and lower all the time" [003-109] — the ranchers recognize that individual action is futile. The railroad "did little business in that part of the country that S. Behrman did not supervise, from the consignment of a shipment of wheat to the management of a damage suit" [002-058]. Only collective political action offers any hope of countering this monopoly power.

The League's formation occurs at a pivotal dinner at Magnus Derrick's Los Muertos ranch house, where the leading ranchers — Derrick, Annixter, Osterman, and Broderson — debate strategy. The choice of Magnus Derrick as president is unanimous: "for President there can be but one name mentioned, one man to whom we all must look as leader — Magnus Derrick" [006-356]. Derrick's stature, integrity, and political experience (he was once a candidate for governor) make him the natural choice.

## Strategy and Tactics

The League pursues a two-pronged strategy. Economically, the ranchers build a giant cooperative storage silo — an innovation proposed by Annixter — that allows them to hold wheat off the market rather than sell immediately at railroad-dictated prices [002-010]. This reduces the railroad's leverage over individual farmers by pooling their market power.

Politically, the League aims to elect sympathetic candidates to the Railroad Commission, which sets freight rates. This strategy involves the ranchers in the machinery of party politics — a world alien to their agricultural lives. Osterman, the most politically sophisticated of the ranchers, orchestrates the plan to buy convention delegates and secure the Commission for the farmers.

It is this political strategy that proves the League's undoing. Magnus Derrick, who initially "formally and definitely refused his adherence to the scheme" [005-017], is eventually drawn into authorizing the bribery. When Genslinger, the railroad-owned newspaper editor, exposes the bribery, the League's moral authority collapses. The very man chosen for his integrity has been forced to compromise it.

## Members

The League's core membership reflects the San Joaquin ranching community:

- **Magnus Derrick** (President): Owner of Los Muertos, the largest ranch. His political past and moral authority make him the League's natural leader, but his fall destroys the organization's legitimacy.
- **Annixter** (Member): Owner of Quien Sabe, the youngest and most innovative rancher. Initially skeptical, he becomes a committed League advocate.
- **Osterman** (Member): The League's political strategist. A rancher who understands the machinery of politics better than his colleagues, he is "quick to see that if he could involve Magnus and Annixter in an argument, Annixter, for the mere love of contention, would oppose the Governor and, without knowing it, would commit himself to his — Osterman's — scheme" [003-092].
- **Broderson** (Member): An older rancher whose experience adds weight to the League's deliberations.
- **Harran Derrick** (Member): Magnus's son, whose "eyes flashed on the instant" [002-056] at the mention of railroad injustice. He inherits his father's commitment to the land.

Other ranchers — Garnett from the Ruby rancho, Keast, Gethings of the San Pablo, Chattern of the Bonanza — attend League meetings and support its efforts, forming the broader constituency Magnus represents [006-177].

## Failure and Legacy

The League fails comprehensively. The political strategy collapses with the exposure of bribery. The economic strategy of cooperative storage cannot overcome the railroad's control of transportation infrastructure. The attempted physical resistance at the irrigation ditch ends in death. By the novel's end, the League no longer exists as an effective organization.

Norris presents the League's failure not as evidence of the farmers' incompetence but as proof of the railroad's total power. The League does everything right — it organizes, strategizes, pools resources, engages the political process — and it still loses. "The wheat has got to be moved" [003-067], as one rancher observes: the railroad controls the means of movement, and that control cannot be negotiated away.

The League's tragedy is the tragedy of collective action in the face of monopoly power. The farmers' individual virtues — Magnus's integrity, Annixter's cleverness, Osterman's political skill — are irrelevant when the system itself is rigged. The League represents the novel's most sustained argument about the impossibility of reform within the existing economic order.

## Historical Context

Norris's League is modeled on actual farmer organizations of the late nineteenth century — the Grange, the Farmers' Alliance, and eventually the Populist Party — that emerged in response to railroad monopolies and declining agricultural prices. These organizations won some regulatory victories (including the Interstate Commerce Act of 1887, partly inspired by the Mussel Slough gunfight) but ultimately could not reverse the consolidation of corporate power over agricultural markets.
