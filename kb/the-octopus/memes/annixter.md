## Role in the Novel

Annixter is the novel's most dynamic character — a young rancher whose personal growth mirrors the farmers' collective struggle. Owner of the [[quien-sabe|Quien Sabe ranch]], some four thousand acres of rich clay and heavy loams [001-092], he is introduced as a prickly, argumentative figure lying in a hammock "reading David Copperfield, and gorging himself with dried prunes" [001-090]. This image — literature and gluttony, refinement and coarseness — captures the contradiction at Annixter's core.

A college graduate and only son of a land speculator who died the year before [001-094], Annixter has inherited wealth and land but no social grace. He "pretended to be a woman-hater, for no other reason than that he was a very bull-calf of awkwardness in feminine surroundings" [001-098]. His letters are typewritten to hide his illegible handwriting; his conversation is argumentative and blunt. He is "quite capable of calling even Magnus a fool to his face" [002-125].

Yet Annixter is also the most innovative of the ranchers. It is his idea — quickly adopted by [[magnus-derrick|Magnus Derrick]] and the others — to build a giant storage silo that allows farmers to hold wheat off the market rather than sell at railroad-dictated prices [002-010]. His shrewdness is recognized even by those he antagonizes: "very clever, that young Annixter" [001-087].

His transformation is catalyzed by [[hilma-tree|Hilma Tree]], the dairyman's daughter who keeps his house. His love for her cracks his defensive shell, and his death in [[mussel-slough-tragedy|the shootout]] — after he has finally opened himself to another person — is the novel's most heartbreaking moment.

## Character Analysis

Annixter functions as Norris's study in American masculinity under pressure. His initial persona — aggressive, competitive, dismissive of women and sentiment — is a recognizable type: the self-made westerner who equates vulnerability with weakness. His reading of Dickens while stuffing himself with dried prunes suggests appetites — intellectual and physical — that his public persona denies.

His engagement with the railroad conflict reveals unexpected depths. Where [[magnus-derrick|Magnus Derrick]] acts from principle (and falls from compromised principle), Annixter acts from a combination of self-interest and growing solidarity. He is initially skeptical of the League — "you give [[magnus-derrick|Magnus Derrick]] my compliments and tell him he's a fool" [001-104] — but becomes one of its fiercest advocates. His conversion is not ideological but experiential: he learns that the railroad threatens everyone, including himself.

The love story with [[hilma-tree|Hilma Tree]] is Norris's most sustained treatment of romantic love. Annixter's awkwardness, his inability to express feeling, his typewritten letters — all these defensive mechanisms collapse when he recognizes what [[hilma-tree|Hilma]] means to him. Their scenes together are among the novel's most tender, and [[hilma-tree|Hilma]]'s pregnancy at the novel's end represents the life-force continuing beyond the tragedy.

## Relationships

Annixter's friendships are marked by friction and loyalty. With [[presley|Presley]], he maintains an improbable friendship — "in all his circle of acquaintances, [[presley|Presley]] was the one man with whom Annixter had never quarrelled" [001-097] — based on Annixter's respect for literature and [[presley|Presley]]'s easygoing nature. With [[magnus-derrick|Magnus Derrick]], Annixter vacillates between respect and challenge; he recognizes [[magnus-derrick|the Governor]]'s authority but refuses to defer to it.

His relationship with [[s-behrman|S. Behrman]] and [[delaney|Delaney]] — the railroad's local representatives — is one of open hostility. When [[delaney|Delaney]] complains about Annixter's fence line, Annixter erupts: "I'll mend my fence, and then, again, maybe I won't mend my fence" [002-138]. These confrontations establish the personal dimension of the larger economic conflict.

With [[hilma-tree|Hilma Tree]], Annixter discovers a capacity for tenderness he did not know he possessed. She is the daughter of the family that runs his dairy; her daily presence in his house erodes his defenses. Their courtship is awkward, tentative, and finally genuine — the emotional heart of a novel otherwise dominated by economic and natural forces.

## Key Scenes

**The Porch Introduction (Book I, Chapter I)**: Annixter is introduced lying in his hammock, eating prunes and reading Dickens — an image that establishes his contradictions [001-090 through 001-098].

**The Fence Confrontation (Book I, Chapter II)**: When [[s-behrman|S. Behrman]] and [[delaney|Delaney]] complain about his fence, Annixter erupts in fury, "his face flamed scarlet to the roots of his stiff yellow hair" [002-134]. The scene establishes his temperament and the personal nature of the railroad conflict.

**The Barn Dance (Book I, Chapter VI)**: Annixter hosts the community dance in his new barn — the social event of the season. The scene brings together all the novel's characters and marks the high point of the agricultural community before the railroad's assault.

**The Harvest Scene**: Annixter watches [[magnus-derrick|Magnus Derrick]]'s grain drills in motion, "like an array of chariots" [005-079], a moment of agricultural triumph that foreshadows destruction.

## Thematic Significance

Annixter's arc embodies Norris's naturalist argument about character and circumstance. He is not determined by his environment — he changes, grows, learns to love — but his growth cannot save him from forces beyond his control. His death is not punishment for flaws but evidence that in the naturalist universe, virtue offers no protection.

His love for [[hilma-tree|Hilma]] introduces the novel's reproductive theme — the idea that life continues through generation even when individuals perish. [[hilma-tree|Hilma]]'s pregnancy at the novel's end connects Annixter's death to the wheat's eternal cycle of death and renewal.
