## Character Role

Mrs. Dyke is the mother of Sidney Dyke, an elderly widow who lives with her son in Guadalajara. She is a quiet domestic presence, devoted to her son and granddaughter, and represents the familial stability that Dyke risks losing through his conflict with the railroad.

## Physical Appearance and Manner

Mrs. Dyke is described as "an old lady with a very" gentle demeanor [006-033]. She is characterized by her protective, maternal concern for her son and her indulgent fondness for her granddaughter. When her granddaughter plays the piano, Mrs. Dyke watches with affection, "with indulgent remonstrance" [006-040], and when someone criticizes the girl's playing, she "protested seriously" in defense [006-042].

## Domestic Life

Mrs. Dyke maintains the household in Guadalajara while Dyke works. She performs traditional domestic duties — "Mrs. Dyke pouring his coffee" at breakfast [008-100] — and provides the stable home life that grounds Dyke's character. Her presence humanizes Dyke, revealing the personal stakes behind his struggle against the railroad: he is not merely an aggrieved worker but a son responsible for his aging mother.

## Role During the Crisis

During the eviction crisis, Mrs. Dyke remains at the Guadalajara house, a fixed point of normalcy amid chaos. When Presley and Magnus Derrick visit, "Mrs. Dyke told Presley and Magnus" the latest news [010-005], serving as a conduit of information. Presley feels compelled to reassure her: "I think it is all right, Mrs. Dyke" [010-009]. Her presence in the narrative underscores the human cost of the railroad's actions — elderly women, children, and families are the collateral damage of corporate greed.

## Relationship with Sidney Dyke

Mrs. Dyke's relationship with her son is one of mutual devotion. Dyke is described as loving "his mother devotedly" [008-096]. He tiptoes into her room in the morning so as not to disturb her [008-099], and their breakfasts together are moments of quiet intimacy. This close mother-son bond makes Dyke's eventual destruction by the railroad system all the more tragic — his transformation from devoted son to desperate fugitive is measured against the domestic peace represented by Mrs. Dyke.
