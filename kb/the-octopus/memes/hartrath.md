## Role in the Novel

Hartrath appears in a single extended scene at the [[cedarquist|Cedarquist]] dinner party in San Francisco, where Magnus and [[harran-derrick|Harran Derrick]] have come to discuss railroad politics. His function in the novel is satirical: he represents the bohemian culture-vulture who attaches himself to men of power and money, ostensibly for artistic purposes but in reality for patronage and self-promotion. Hartrath's Million-Dollar Fair scheme — a plan to bring Eastern capitalists to California through a grandiose art exhibition — serves as Norris's vehicle for mocking the collision of aesthetic pretension with raw commercial ambition, a collision that mirrors the broader theme of culture being crushed by corporate power.

## Character Analysis

Norris introduces Hartrath with a withering physical description that immediately marks him as an object of ridicule: "a certain middle-aged man, flamboyantly dressed, who wore his hair long, who was afflicted with sore eyes, and the collar of whose velvet coat was sprinkled with dandruff" [007-073]. [[lyman-derrick|Lyman Derrick]] characterizes him as "a man absolutely devoid of even the commonest decency" who somehow gained entry to the dinner party through mysterious means [007-073]. Yet [[lyman-derrick|Lyman]]'s public behavior toward Hartrath contradicts his private contempt — "when this Hartrath came across to say 'How do you do' to [[lyman-derrick|Lyman]], [[lyman-derrick|Lyman]] was as eager in his cordiality as his warmest friend could have expected" [007-074]. This hypocrisy earns [[harran-derrick|Harran]]'s scornful question: "Why the devil are you so chummy with him, then?" [007-075]. The gap between [[lyman-derrick|Lyman]]'s private disdain and public warmth toward Hartrath reveals [[lyman-derrick|Lyman]]'s own political calculus: he believes Hartrath "had a 'pull' at the City Hall" and could assist with [[lyman-derrick|Lyman]]'s political ambitions [007-084].

## Relationships

Hartrath's primary on-page interaction is with [[lyman-derrick]], whom he approaches at the dinner party "uninvited" — "As a pretext, he engaged [[lyman-derrick|Lyman]] in conversation" about the Million-Dollar Fair [007-084]. The relationship is purely transactional: Hartrath sees [[lyman-derrick|Lyman]] as a conduit to municipal influence, while [[lyman-derrick|Lyman]] tolerates Hartrath because he believes the artist has connections at City Hall. [[cedarquist]], the host, continues the conversation about the Fair after Hartrath departs, expanding on the scheme's absurdity: "When you get your Eastern capitalists out here with your Million-Dollar Fair, you don't propose, do you, to let them see a Mission Playhouse?" [007-089]. [[harran-derrick]] observes the interaction with undisguised contempt, serving as the reader's proxy for judging both Hartrath's pretension and [[lyman-derrick|Lyman]]'s complicity. [[magnus-derrick]], present at the same dinner, is the silent contrast — the old-guard rancher whose integrity stands against the transactional social world Hartrath embodies.

## Key Scenes

**The Introduction**: [[lyman-derrick|Lyman Derrick]] points Hartrath out to Magnus, delivering the devastating description that defines the character. Even before Hartrath speaks, Norris has painted him as physically repellent and morally dubious — the dandruff on the velvet collar being an especially cruel detail of naturalist observation [007-073].

**The Approach**: Hartrath joins the group "uninvited" and engages [[lyman-derrick|Lyman]] in conversation about the Million-Dollar Fair. The pretext is art; the subtext is political patronage. [[lyman-derrick|Lyman]]'s unctuous friendliness toward a man he privately despises is a study in political insincerity [007-084].

**[[cedarquist|Cedarquist]]'s Expansion**: After Hartrath departs, [[cedarquist|Cedarquist]] develops the Fair idea into a broader satire of California boosterism, imagining Eastern capitalists being shown a Mission Playhouse rather than the industrial reality of the state. The conversation moves from Hartrath's personal absurdity to the larger cultural absurdity he represents [007-089].

## Thematic Significance

Hartrath is a minor figure in the novel's gallery of social types, but his brief appearance carries significant thematic weight. He embodies the intersection of art and commerce that Norris, a naturalist writer himself, viewed with deep suspicion. The artist who grovels before men of power for patronage is a degraded figure, and Hartrath's physical repulsiveness — the sore eyes, the dandruff — externalizes his spiritual corruption. His Million-Dollar Fair, a scheme that dresses commercial ambition in cultural costume, parallels [[the-railroad|the Railroad]]'s own practice of wrapping monopoly in the language of progress and efficiency. In the novel's moral economy, Hartrath is what happens when art abandons truth for money — a warning, perhaps, to [[presley|Presley]] the poet and to Norris himself.
