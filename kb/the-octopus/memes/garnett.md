## Role in the Novel

Garnett of the Ruby Rancho appears as one of the circle of established wheat ranchers who form the backbone of [[magnus-derrick|Magnus Derrick]]'s League. He is consistently present alongside [[gethings]] of the San Pablo at every major gathering — the barn dance, League meetings, the jack[[jackrabbit-hunt|rabbit drive]], and the final confrontation at the irrigating ditch. Like [[gethings|Gethings]], Garnett functions as part of the collective "rancher" body in the novel, his individuality subordinated to his role as a representative of the agrarian interest. His most prominent moment comes at the irrigating ditch, where he is one of the three elder ranchers chosen to parley unarmed with the marshal's posse [0001-5616].

## Character Analysis

Norris introduces Garnett among the "elderly men, for the most part, bearded, slow of speech, deliberate, dressed in broadcloth" who arrive at [[annixter|Annixter]]'s barn dance [0001-2265]. He is consistently paired with [[gethings|Gethings]], the two appearing together so frequently that they function almost as a composite figure representing the stolid, respectable rancher class. Garnett's moments of individual speech are few but pointed. When the League's armed confrontation seems to be fizzling with only a handful of men assembled, it is Garnett who bitterly exclaims, "And the six hundred Leaguers who were going to rise when this happened!" giving voice to the disappointment and dashed expectations of the organized resistance [0001-5426]. Later, as the posse approaches, he asks the practical question "Is the team going towards [[guadalajara|Guadalajara]]?" [0001-5476], maintaining tactical awareness even as the situation deteriorates.

## Relationships

Garnett is inseparable in the narrative from [[gethings]] — the two "preoccupied, bearded men, smoking black cigars" are jointly present at the League Executive Committee meetings [0001-4452]. His relationship with [[magnus-derrick]] is one of deference and solidarity; he stands in Magnus's listening circle at the barn dance and follows him unarmed into the confrontation with [[the-railroad|the Railroad]]'s posse. He receives one of [[the-railroad|the Railroad]]'s rate-increase letters at the barn dance alongside the other ranchers, and participates in the Executive Committee's deliberations at [[los-muertos|Los Muertos]]. His association with the Ruby Rancho, like [[gethings|Gethings]] with San Pablo, defines his identity through land ownership — his very name is incomplete without its territorial suffix.

## Key Scenes

**The Barn Dance and the Rate Letters**: Garnett arrives at [[annixter|Annixter]]'s [[quien-sabe|Quien Sabe]] barn with the other ranchers, dressed in broadcloth and moving slowly through the festivities. He stands in [[magnus-derrick|Magnus Derrick]]'s listening circle discussing the wheat market. When [[the-railroad|the Railroad]]'s letters arrive, he receives and reads his copy — "Only the figures and the proper names varied" — and the news of the rate increase abruptly ends the dance [0001-2628].

**League Executive Committee**: At the [[los-muertos|Los Muertos]] ranch house, Garnett sits on the Executive Committee alongside Magnus, [[annixter|Annixter]], [[osterman|Osterman]], [[broderson|Broderson]], [[harran-derrick|Harran]], [[gethings|Gethings]], and [[dabney|Dabney]]. He is present as [[lyman-derrick|Lyman]] presents the new rate schedule and joins in the questioning of [[the-railroad|the Railroad]]'s good faith [0001-4452].

**The Irrigating Ditch Confrontation**: When Magnus resolves to meet the marshal unarmed, "he beckoned Garnett and [[gethings|Gethings]] to follow, and the three ranchers, who, with the exception of [[broderson|Broderson]], were the oldest men present, advanced, without arms, to meet the marshal" [0001-5616]. The posse gradually surrounds them, and the attempted negotiation dissolves into the chaos that precipitates the fatal shootout.

**The Jackrabbit Drive**: Garnett and [[gethings|Gethings]] ride together, "ignoring the drive and the spirit of the occasion," absorbed in their conversation about wheat prices — a small but telling detail that illustrates how completely the economic struggle dominates these men's consciousness [0001-5260].

## Thematic Significance

Garnett, like [[gethings|Gethings]], embodies the institutional legitimacy of the ranchers' cause. His selection as an unarmed negotiator — "the oldest men present" — marks him as a figure of patriarchal authority in the agrarian community. The fact that he and [[gethings|Gethings]] are so frequently described in paired, almost interchangeable terms suggests Norris's view of the ranchers as a class or type rather than as sharply differentiated individuals. Their collective fate — subsumed into the larger catastrophe of [[the-railroad|the Railroad]]'s victory — enacts the naturalist theme of individual human will being crushed by vast, impersonal economic forces. Garnett's bitter remark about the "six hundred Leaguers" who failed to materialize crystallizes one of the novel's darkest insights: that solidarity is easier proclaimed than achieved, and that the ranchers' individualism, which is their strength as pioneers, is also their fatal weakness as a political movement.
