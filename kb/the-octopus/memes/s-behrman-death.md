## Overview

S. Behrman's death in the hold of the grain ship *Swanhilda* is arguably the most memorable and symbolically charged death in Frank Norris's *The Octopus*. After a novel spent as the Pacific and Southwestern Railroad's chief agent in the San Joaquin Valley — foreclosing on farmers, manipulating freight rates, and amassing wealth from other men's labor — Behrman dies in a manner that Norris designs as the ultimate naturalist retribution: he falls into the hold of a ship being loaded with bulk wheat at Port Costa and is buried alive by the relentless cascade of grain.

The death is not a murder, not an execution, not even an accident caused by another person. Behrman dies alone, his cries drowned by the "prolonged roar, persistent, steady, inevitable" of the wheat pouring from the elevator chute [015-198]. The force that kills him is the same force he spent his career attempting to dominate — the Wheat itself, which Norris imagines throughout the novel as an elemental life-force indifferent to human ambition. The master of the railroad's local machinery is destroyed by the very commodity whose transportation he controlled.

## Narrative Context

Behrman's death occurs near the novel's close, in Book II, Chapter IX, after the railroad has triumphed on every front. The Mussel Slough tragedy has left Annixter and Harran Derrick dead. Magnus Derrick is publicly disgraced. Dyke has been driven to banditry. The League is shattered. Behrman, meanwhile, has ascended to ownership of Los Muertos itself, having foreclosed on Magnus Derrick's ranch.

The death scene is preceded by a passage in which Behrman inspects the grain elevator he has built at Port Costa — "the largest structure discernible, and upon its red roof, in enormous white letters, was his own name" [015-188]. He has come to watch his wheat being loaded onto the *Swanhilda*, a barque bound for Liverpool, savoring "the concrete evidence of his success" [015-183]. The scene is one of apparent triumph: Behrman surveying the machinery of commerce he has mastered, his name writ large upon the landscape, the harvest of Los Muertos flowing through his elevator to feed the world.

The irony that follows is devastating. The man who believed he controlled the wheat discovers that the wheat cannot be controlled. His death sets up the novel's famous Conclusion, in which Presley boards the very same *Swanhilda* — now carrying the wheat that killed Behrman — bound for India, and meditates on the cosmic indifference of the Wheat that "remained untouched, unassailable, undefiled" [016-014].

## Detailed Account

Behrman arrives at Port Costa to inspect the loading of Los Muertos wheat onto the *Swanhilda*. He has sold a full ship's cargo to an Indian Famine Relief Committee and arranged for the grain to be handled in bulk — poured loose into the hold rather than bagged, a cost-saving innovation [015-188]. The elevator chute connects directly to the ship's hatch, and through it rushes "a veritable cataract of wheat" [015-194].

Norris describes the wheat with language that strips away human agency:

> "No men were in sight. The place was deserted. No human agency seemed to be back of the movement of the wheat. Rather, the grain seemed impelled with a force of its own, a resistless, huge force, eager, vivid, impatient for the sea." [015-195]

Behrman, standing at the hatch, puts his hand into the rushing tide of grain. "The contact rasped the flesh of his fingers and like an undertow drew his hand after it in its impetuous dash" [015-196] — a tactile foreshadowing of what is to come. Curious about the increased volume of the grain flow, he turns to look up at the elevator. His foot catches in a coil of rope. He falls headfirst into the hold [015-198].

What follows is an extended, excruciating death sequence. Behrman sinks ankle-deep into the wheat. He looks for a companion ladder, an exit — there is none. He shouts. The roar of the grain drowns his voice. He stumbles toward the hatch and is driven back by the sting of hurtling grains "like a storm of small shot" [015-204]. A wave of wheat immobilizes him to the thighs. He retreats, advances, is driven back again.

Then begins what Norris calls "that terrible dance of death" [015-213]:

> "The man dodging, doubling, squirming, hunted from one corner to another, the wheat slowly, inexorably flowing, rising, spreading to every angle, to every nook and cranny. ... To stand still meant to sink; to lie or sit meant to be buried the quicker; and all this in the dark, all this in an air that could scarcely be breathed, all this while he fought an enemy that could not be gripped, toiling in a sea that could not be stayed." [015-213]

The language is that of a hunt — but the hunter has become the hunted. Behrman, who tracked ranchers' mortgages and foreclosed with precision, is now tracked by an enemy that "could not be gripped."

The death itself is rendered with grisly particularity [015-215]. Behrman collapses. The wheat fills his pockets, climbs his sleeves and trouser legs, covers his "great, protuberant stomach." It runs "in rivulets into the distended, gasping mouth." It covers his face. A hand — "fat, with short fingers and swollen veins" — reaches up once, clutches, falls limp, and is covered. "In the hold of the *Swanhilda* there was no movement but the widening ripples that spread flowing from the ever-breaking, ever-reforming cone; no sound, but the rushing of the Wheat that continued to plunge incessantly from the iron chute in a prolonged roar, persistent, steady, inevitable."

No one witnesses Behrman's death. No one discovers his body. The *Swanhilda* sails for India two days later with Presley aboard as a passenger [016-001]. The wheat that killed Behrman will feed the starving in Asia — a fact that transforms his death from mere retribution into a complex naturalist statement about the indifference of cosmic forces to individual morality.

## Thematic Significance: Naturalist Irony

Behrman's death is the novel's most concentrated enactment of naturalist philosophy. Four dimensions of its irony deserve attention.

**First, the agent of exploitation is consumed by the object of exploitation.** Throughout *The Octopus*, Behrman treats wheat as a resource to be controlled. He sets freight rates on it, stores it in his elevator, sells it to buyers in Liverpool. The wheat is a passive commodity in his calculus. His death reveals that the wheat is not passive at all. Norris has prepared this reversal from the novel's opening, where the wheat is described in terms of elemental force — "fecundating the living soil; implanting deep in the dark womb of the Earth the germ of life, the sustenance of a whole world" [005-079]. Behrman's error is the error of all the novel's exploiters: he mistakes the wheat's apparent passivity for actual submission. The wheat obeys no master. It feeds nations; it can also kill a man.

**Second, the manner of death perfectly inverts Behrman's life.** Behrman's body is "large" and "fat" with a "great stomach" [002-058] — the body of a man who has consumed without producing. In death, it is the wheat that consumes him: it fills his pockets, climbs his sleeves, enters his mouth. The consumer is consumed. The silence of the death — no witness, no outcry heard, no body found — inverts the public sphere in which Behrman operated. He was a man of signs and announcements ("S. Behrman Has Something To Say To You" [001-006]); his death leaves no sign at all.

**Third, the death occurs at the moment of apparent triumph.** Behrman has just acquired Los Muertos. The railroad has won. His name is on the largest building at Port Costa. He has sold a full cargo to the famine relief committee. He has arrived to "enjoy it to its fullest extent, wished to miss no feature of the disposal of the crop" [015-183]. The death that intervenes is not the climax of a defeat but the interruption of a victory — a reminder that natural forces do not wait for convenient dramatic moments.

**Fourth, the death has no moral agent behind it.** No rancher kills Behrman. No Dyke, no Annixter, no Presley administers justice. The wheat kills him — and the wheat is beyond morality. This is the deepest naturalist irony: the universe does not punish Behrman for his evil; it simply operates according to laws that are indifferent to his existence. His death is not poetic justice in any moral sense but the mechanical outcome of physical forces. He fell, and wheat poured, and he suffocated. The universe records no satisfaction.

The conclusion of the novel deepens the irony further. Behrman's corpse is never mentioned again. The *Swanhilda*, still carrying the wheat from Behrman's elevator, sails for India. Presley, aboard the same ship, meditates not on Behrman's death but on the larger truth it illustrates: "Falseness dies; injustice and oppression in the end of everything fade and vanish away. ... *But the Wheat remained*" [016-014]. Behrman is forgotten. The wheat endures.

## Symbolism of the Grain Ship

The *Swanhilda* is not an arbitrary setting. The name — Germanic, suggesting "swan" and "battle" — carries overtones of transformation and finality. Ships in *The Octopus* represent the global economic system into which the San Joaquin wheat is drawn: Liverpool, Calcutta, the markets that set prices the ranchers cannot influence. Behrman's death inside a ship is thus doubly symbolic. He dies within the machinery of international commerce he helped build, and his body becomes part of the commodity that commerce distributes.

Norris's description of the loading process emphasizes its impersonality. The chute connects the elevator to the hold; no human hand touches the grain. "No men were in sight. The place was deserted" [015-195]. This is industrial capitalism in its pure form: a self-operating system that moves matter without human intervention. Behrman, who helped design this system, is destroyed by it — not through sabotage or revolt but through its normal, unimpeded operation.

The ship also provides the novel with physical closure. The *Swanhilda* carries Behrman's wheat (and his corpse) out of California, across the Pacific, toward Asia. The wheat disperses into the world; Behrman vanishes into it. This is Norris's final statement about the relationship between individual lives and cosmic forces. Men die; the Wheat moves on.
