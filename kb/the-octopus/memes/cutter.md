## Role in the Novel

Cutter serves as the division superintendent on Rancho Four of [[los-muertos|Los Muertos]], one of [[magnus-derrick|Magnus Derrick]]'s subordinate managers. His role in the novel is that of a working rancher — he is involved in the daily agricultural operations, particularly the seed preparation and planting that occupy the early chapters. Unlike the major ranchers who appear in political and social scenes, Cutter is defined primarily through his labor and his participation in communal events. He appears at [[annixter|Annixter]]'s barn dance bringing his wife and her two girl cousins, and his recurring subplot involves a running dispute with [[hooven]] over the ownership of a certain steer, a petty conflict that provides comic relief against the novel's larger tragedies.

## Character Analysis

Cutter is introduced through the practical medium of a telephone call — [[harran-derrick|Harran Derrick]] contacts him to arrange the transport of seed wheat for bluestone treatment, giving him detailed instructions about the route, [[hooven|Hooven]], the irrigating ditch, and the expected arrival of new equipment [0001-433]. This introduction firmly establishes him as a man of practical agricultural labor, not of politics or ideas. At the barn dance, Cutter's domestic side emerges: he arrives with "his wife and her two girl cousins" who "had come fifteen miles by the trail" on horseback, with Mrs. Cutter loudly complaining that she was "nearly dead" and her "back nearly broken in two" [0001-2233]. His quarrel with [[hooven|Hooven]] about the steer is a recurring comic motif — at the barn dance, [[hooven|Hooven]] becomes "afflicted with a great sadness" and "asserted that he was 'obbressed'; Cutter had taken his steer," retiring to a corner to weep [0001-2373]. During the gunfight reminiscences, Mrs. Cutter contributes her own violent story of seeing "a claim jumped in Placer County in 1851, when three men were shot" [0001-2517].

## Relationships

Cutter's primary on-page relationship is his running feud with [[hooven]] over the steer, which erupts comically at the barn dance: "Twice it became necessary to separate [[hooven|Hooven]] and Cutter, renewing their quarrel" [0001-2519]. He works under [[harran-derrick]]'s direction, receiving orders by telephone and reporting on crop conditions — [[harran-derrick|Harran]] notes that "Cutter reports that there are spots on Four where we will get forty-two or three" sacks to the acre [0001-4754]. He has a domestic relationship with his wife, Mrs. Cutter, who is portrayed as a voluble complainer. During the League's armed confrontation, Cutter serves a reconnaissance role: "Cutter is watching them from the Long Trestle. They returned to [[guadalajara|Guadalajara]]" [0001-5438]. His relationship with [[magnus-derrick]] is that of employer to employee, part of the feudal hierarchy of the great wheat ranches.

## Key Scenes

**The Seed Transport**: [[harran-derrick|Harran Derrick]] telephones Cutter with detailed instructions for transporting seed wheat, demonstrating the chain of command on [[los-muertos|Los Muertos]] and the practical rhythms of ranch labor [0001-433].

**Arrival at the Barn Dance**: Cutter arrives at [[annixter|Annixter]]'s [[quien-sabe|Quien Sabe]] barn with his wife and her two girl cousins, all on horseback. Mrs. Cutter's loud complaints about the long ride provide a moment of domestic comedy. Cutter immediately enters into "an excited conversation as to the ownership of a certain steer" with [[hooven|Hooven]] [0001-2233].

**The Harness Room**: At the barn dance after-party in the harness room, Cutter participates in the drinking, the gunfight storytelling, and the general masculine revelry. Mrs. Cutter tells her story of the Placer County claim-jumping, and the recurring quarrel with [[hooven|Hooven]] twice requires intervention [0001-2545].

**Reconnaissance at the Long Trestle**: During the League's armed mobilization, Cutter serves as a scout, positioned at the Long Trestle to observe the posse's movements and report back to the assembled Leaguers [0001-5438].

## Thematic Significance

Cutter represents the working stratum of the ranching world — the foremen and superintendents who manage the land but do not own it. His dispute with [[hooven|Hooven]] over the steer is a miniature of the property conflicts that pervade the novel, played out as comedy rather than tragedy. The steer, like the land itself, is an object of contested ownership between men who, in the larger scheme, are both victims of [[the-railroad|the Railroad]]'s monopoly. Cutter's domestic life — his complaining wife, her girl cousins in their "dresses of dotted Swiss over blue sateen" — provides a glimpse of the ordinary human textures that [[the-railroad|the Railroad]]'s economic warfare will ultimately disrupt or destroy. In the final confrontation, his role as scout on the Long Trestle places him at the periphery of the violence, a witness rather than a casualty, but the fate of his livelihood on [[los-muertos|Los Muertos]] is implicitly tied to the larger defeat of the ranchers.
