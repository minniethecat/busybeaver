## Role in the Novel

Vanamee occupies a unique position in *The Octopus* — neither farmer nor railroad man, neither fighter nor victim in the conventional sense, he is the novel's mystic consciousness. "He lived his life in the unknown, one could not tell where — in the desert, in the mountains, throughout all the vast and vague Southwest, solitary, strange. Three, four, five years passed. The shepherd would be almost forgotten" [001-130]. His periodic returns to the San Joaquin punctuate the narrative with a reminder that forces beyond economics — love, grief, spiritual longing — shape human destiny as powerfully as freight rates and land courts.

Unlike the ranchers whose struggle is material and political, Vanamee's conflict is metaphysical. He has been broken not by the railroad but by a crime of sexual violence that destroyed the woman he loved and shattered his capacity for ordinary life. His wanderings in the desert are a form of spiritual quest, an attempt to heal a wound that eighteen years have not closed. In Norris's naturalist framework, Vanamee represents the possibility that — even in a deterministic universe — meaning can be found through mystical connection to the land itself.

## Character Analysis

"College bred though he was, the life pleased him. He was, as he desired, close to nature, living the full measure of life, a worker among workers, taking enjoyment in simple pleasures, healthy in mind and body" [001-132]. Vanamee's education separates him from the other ranch workers, yet he deliberately chooses manual labor and solitude. His intelligence is paired with an almost abnormal sensitivity — "Living close to nature, a poet by instinct, where [[presley|Presley]] was but a poet by training, there developed in him a great sensitiveness to beauty and an almost abnormal capacity for great happiness and great sorrow; he felt things intensely, deeply. He never forgot" [001-131].

His tragedy is the defining element of his character. The rape of [[angele-varian|Angéle Varian]] by an unknown assailant — "the Other" — and her subsequent death in childbirth shattered Vanamee's life at its foundations. "The long, dull ache, the poignant grief had now become a part of him" [001-137]. Unlike the farmers who seek justice through courts and political action, Vanamee sought justice through violence — "with knife and revolver ready, ranged the countryside like a wolf" [001-135] — but the perpetrator was never found. This failure of retribution forced Vanamee into a different relationship with suffering, one that ultimately leads him toward mystical acceptance rather than revenge.

Physically, Vanamee is marked by timelessness. "In all those years he did not seem to have grown older by a single day" [001-130]. [[presley|Presley]] notes that his face bears "an unspeakable sadness, a deathless grief, the permanent imprint of a tragedy long past, but yet a living issue" [001-130]. He is a figure suspended in time, his development arrested at the moment of his loss.

## Relationships

Vanamee's social world is deliberately minimal. "In all that countryside he had but three friends, [[presley|Presley]], [[magnus-derrick|Magnus Derrick]], and the priest at the Mission of San Juan de [[guadalajara|Guadalajara]], [[father-sarria|Father Sarria]]" [001-130]. These three relationships define his connection to the valley's life:

**[[presley|Presley]]** is Vanamee's intellectual peer and closest friend. They share a poetic temperament and an outsider's perspective on the ranchers' struggles. Vanamee challenges [[presley|Presley]]'s artistic ambition directly: "But why write? Why not *live* in it? Steep oneself in the heat of the desert, the glory of the sunset, the blue haze of the mesa and the canyon" [001-150]. This question — whether to represent experience or to inhabit it fully — is one of the novel's central philosophical tensions.

**[[father-sarria|Father Sarria]]** provides Vanamee's connection to the Mission of San Juan, the site of his love affair with Angéle and her burial. [[father-sarria|Sarria]]'s recognition of Vanamee at the Mission door — "his face went all alight with pleasure and astonishment" [001-156] — signals the shepherd's enduring place in the community, however intermittent his presence.

**[[angele-varian|Angéle Varian]]**, though dead eighteen years before the novel's present action, remains Vanamee's defining relationship. "It was one of those legendary passions that sometimes occur, idyllic, untouched by civilisation, spontaneous as the growth of trees, natural as dew-fall, strong as the firm-seated mountains" [001-131]. His love for her has become a kind of religion, sanctified by loss and preserved in memory.

## Key Scenes

**The Reunion with [[presley|Presley]] (Book I, Chapter I)** : After six years' absence, Vanamee reappears on the County Road, recognized first by his voice. [[presley|Presley]]'s recollection of their shared history allows Norris to narrate Vanamee's entire backstory — his love for Angéle, her rape, her death, and his subsequent wanderings through the Southwest [001-130 through 001-150]. The scene establishes Vanamee as the novel's keeper of memory and grief.

**The Night at the Mission**: Vanamee spends the night at the Mission of San Juan, near Angéle's grave in the Mission garden. The garden, with its "moss-encrusted fountain" and ancient pear trees [001-133], is the site of both his greatest happiness and his greatest loss — a sacred space where past and present converge.

**The Desert Wanderings**: Between his appearances in the San Joaquin, Vanamee vanishes into the desert for years at a time. "He had melted off into the surface-shimmer of the desert, into the mirage; he sank below the horizons; he was swallowed up in the waste of sand and sage" [001-130]. These disappearances, recounted to [[presley|Presley]], emphasize his liminal status — neither fully present nor fully absent from the world of the novel.

**The Spiritual Vision**: In the novel's later sections, Vanamee experiences a mystical communion with the spirit of Angéle in the Mission garden, achieving a transcendence that the material struggles of the ranchers never reach. This vision represents Norris's attempt to find meaning beyond the deterministic forces that crush the novel's other characters.

## Thematic Significance

Vanamee embodies the spiritual dimension that complements the novel's economic and political conflicts. Where the railroad represents mechanistic force and the wheat represents biological force, Vanamee represents the force of consciousness itself — the human capacity to transform suffering into meaning. His life asks whether redemptive love can survive in a world governed by corporate power and natural law.

His relationship to the Western landscape is fundamentally different from the farmers'. For [[magnus-derrick|Magnus Derrick]] and [[annixter|Annixter]], the land is capital — soil to be worked, wheat to be sold. For Vanamee, the desert is a spiritual medium, a place of purification and vision. The names of the places he traverses — "Navajo, Quijotoa, Uintah, Sonora, Laredo, Uncompahgre" [001-138] — become, for [[presley|Presley]], "so many symbols," a "West that passed, unrolling there before the eye of the mind" [001-138].

In Norris's naturalist universe, Vanamee offers a fragile counter-argument to determinism. If the railroad can crush farmers and manipulate markets, and if the wheat itself is an indifferent life-force, Vanamee's mystical experience suggests that the human spirit can nevertheless achieve moments of transcendence — not by defeating the forces that oppose it, but by finding a relationship to them beyond material struggle.
