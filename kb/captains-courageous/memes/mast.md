# Mast

The vertical spars that carry the sails on the fishing schooner [[were-here|We're Here]]. The vessel has two masts: a mainmast aft and a shorter stump-foremast forward, characteristic of the Gloucester fishing schooner design of the late 19th century.

## The Two Masts

The We're Here's foremast is a stump-foremast — shorter than a full-rigged foremast. This design feature of fishing schooners makes it easier to work the vessel with a small crew, as there is less sail to handle. The mainmast stands taller and carries the riding-sail used when the schooner is at anchor.

> The angular riding-sail on the mainmast, played easily at anchor. (001-058)

Harvey Cheyne, unfamiliar with sailing vessels, struggles to identify the parts of the ship. His early confusion about basic nautical terms like "mast" highlights the gap between his wealthy upbringing and the working world of the fishermen.

## Learning the Rig

Long Jack takes on the task of teaching Harvey the schooner's gear, using physical methods that leave no room for misunderstanding. There is not much gear on a seventy-ton schooner with a stump-foremast, but Long Jack has a gift for instruction.

When he wishes to draw Harvey's attention to the peak-halyards, he digs his knuckles into the back of the boy's neck and keeps him at gaze. He emphasizes the difference between fore and aft by rubbing Harvey's nose along the boom, and the lead of each rope is fixed in Harvey's mind by the end of the rope itself.

> For an hour Long Jack walked his prey up and down, teaching "things at the sea that ivry man must know, blind, dhrunk, or asleep." (003-097)

Dan, meanwhile, pretends to be busy by the stump-foremast during Harvey's early humiliations, though he chuckles at the spectacle.

> Harvey heard a chuckle from Dan, who was pretending to be busy by the stump-foremast. (001-073)

## The Riding-Sail and Fly

The mainmast carries a triangular riding-sail — a small sail hoisted not for propulsion but to keep the schooner's bow pointed into the wind at anchor, steadying the vessel in the Atlantic swell. At the mainmast-head flies a little telltale, a wind indicator that Captain Disko Troop monitors constantly.

> Disko Troop was smoking by the roof of the cabin — one eye on the craft around, and the other on the little fly at the mainmast-head. (003-002)

The shadow of the masts and rigging, with the never-furled riding-sail, rolls to and fro on the heaving deck in the moonlight, the pile of fish by the stern shining like a dump of fluid silver.

> The shadow of the masts and rigging, with the never-furled riding-sail, rolled to and fro on the heaving deck in the moonlight. (002-145)

## Rigging and Gear

The mainmast is supported by stays — ropes running from the mast to the hull — and carries a fly (wind indicator) at its head to help the crew judge wind direction. The shadow of the masts and rigging falls across the deck as the schooner rolls at sea.

> The shadow of the masts and rigging, with the never-quiet figure of the look-out, fell across the deck. (002-145)

## Masts in Peril

The ocean exacts its toll on the fishing fleet. In heavy weather, a schooner's stump-foremast can be snapped clean across and drift past the bows — a grim reminder of the dangers the fishermen face.

> Presently a schooner's stump-foremast, snapped clean across, drifted past the bows. (009-080)
