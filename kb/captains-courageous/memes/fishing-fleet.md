# Fishing Fleet

The Grand Banks fishing fleet is more than a collection of vessels — it is a floating, self-governing society with its own codes, rivalries, and bonds of mutual aid. When Harvey Cheyne falls from his transatlantic liner, he lands not merely in a dory but into the midst of this working community, which becomes the classroom for his transformation.

## Composition

The fleet comprised schooners from Gloucester, the Maritime Provinces, France, and Portugal, each with its own customs and reputation. The *We're Here*, Disko Troop's schooner, was one among dozens that gathered seasonally on the Banks.

## Social Order

Within the fleet, reputation was everything. "He's a mighty jest man; all the fleet says so" [002-027] was the highest praise. The fleet communicated through a system of signals and rituals — a dory's position, a schooner's bell, a particular sail trim could all convey meaning: "you can see a dory, an' all the fleet knows what it means" [003-051].

## The Morning Spectacle

Harvey's first glimpse of the full fleet at dawn, after days of fog, was an unforgettable sight. The sun was just clear of the horizon as his low red light struck into the riding-sails of three fleets of anchored schooners — north, west, and south.

Nearly a hundred vessels of every make and build bobbed together, with a square-rigged Frenchman far away, all bowing and courtesying one to the other. From every boat, dories dropped away "like bees from a crowded hive" [008-001], and the clamour of voices carried for miles.

## Dangers and Solidarity

Life among the fleet was perilous. Fog, gales, and the treacherous Virgin Rock threatened every vessel. Yet the fleet also provided the only safety net:

> "we're all among the fishing-fleet now" [001-030]

The foghorn of the liner "whistling to warn the fishing-fleet" [001-001] opens the novel — a signal that, even in the vast North Atlantic, the fleet formed a neighborhood that required warning and protection.

## The Fleet's Economy

Beneath the camaraderie, competition was fierce. Schooners raced to fill their holds, and a skipper's reputation — and his crew's pay — depended on the size of the catch.

When the fish struck, every vessel scrambled: "Look at them boats that hev edged up sence mornin'. They're all among the fish" [003-071]. A full hold meant a quick return to Gloucester, top prices, and bragging rights across the fleet.
