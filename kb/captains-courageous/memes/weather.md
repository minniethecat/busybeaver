# Weather

Weather is the dominant force in the lives of the Grand Banks fishermen. Fog, gales, rising winds, and brief spells of calm dictate when the dories can fish, how the schooner sails, and whether the men live or die. Captain Disko Troop's constant attention to weather reflects the experience of a lifetime on the Banks.

## Fog on the Banks

Fog is the defining weather condition of the Grand Banks, where the cold Labrador Current meets the warm Gulf Stream. In thick fog, dories can lose sight of their mother schooner, and men can drift for days. The schooner sounds its bell and conch horn to guide stray dories back.

> It was thick weather outside, with a rising wind, and the elder men stretched themselves and looked at the sky. (003-173)

## Gales and Rising Wind

Gales on the Banks can appear suddenly and with deadly force. The fishermen read the wind by instinct — the feel of it on their faces, the behavior of the schooner at anchor. A living gale can keep the fleet hove-to for days, and men who fall overboard in such conditions are rarely recovered.

> Disko Troop thought of recent weather, and gales, currents, food-supplies, and other domestic arrangements. (003-004)

## Harvey and Weather

Harvey arrives on the We're Here with a pampered boy's understanding of weather. He describes the conditions during his steamer crossing — conditions that made him mildly seasick — as "a gale." Disko Troop's response is a single, devastating whistle, a wordless correction that tells Harvey real weather on the Banks operates on an entirely different scale from the mild rolling of a passenger liner.

> Harvey described the conditions as "a gale." Disko's response was a single whistle. (001-053, 001-054)

## Fair Weather

Fair weather brings a different rhythm to the schooner. On clear days, the long blue seas fill with sails and dories, the horizon glints with schooner topsails, and the crew works at ease. Dan watches the sea glisten in broad sun-struck patches while the fog looms over the distant fleet.

> The long blue seas were full of sails and dories. Disko Troop was smoking by the roof of the cabin — one eye on the craft around, and the other on the little fly at the mainmast-head. (003-002)

## Weather and the Fishermen's Senses

The men read weather through multiple senses. They feel the wind on their faces, watch the behavior of the schooner at anchor, and listen to the slap and chafe of the hull. In thick weather, the older men stretch their watches, and each in turn checks the wheel, sees to the anchor, and glances at the dim anchor-light.

> The nosing bows slapped and scuffed with the seas; the foc'sle stovepipe hissed and sputtered as the spray caught it. (003-173)

The veteran Bank fisherman trusts his body as much as any barometer. A shift in the wind's feel, a change in the sea's rhythm — these are the signs that keep a crew alive through the long weeks on the Western water.
