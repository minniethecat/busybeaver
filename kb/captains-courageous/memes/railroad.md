# Railroad

**Railroads** are the foundation of the Cheyne family's immense wealth in _Captains Courageous_. Mr. Cheyne "owns half a dozen railroads, half the lumber on the Pacific slope" (001-007), and his railroad empire represents everything Harvey is severed from when he falls overboard — and everything he must reconcile with when he returns.

## The Cheyne Railroad Empire

Harvey Cheyne Sr. built his fortune in Western railroads. He "owns 'baout every railroad on Long Island, they say" (002-011), and his influence extends across the continent. The scope of his holdings is staggering — "sixty-three million dollars' worth of variously manipulated railroad interests" depend on his decisions (009-041). When Harvey first comes aboard the _We're Here_, Dan identifies him immediately by his father's business: "Railroads, his father, aind't it?" (001-006).

## Railroads vs. the Sea

The novel draws a deliberate contrast between the railroad world and the fishing world. Mr. Cheyne's life has been spent mastering railroads — he "discovered the building of three railroads and the deliberate wreck of a fourth" (010-052). Yet when he comes to Gloucester to retrieve his son, he finds himself in a world where railroad power means nothing. Disko Troop declares bluntly: "I've no use for railroads. No more hez Dan. Salt water's good enough for the Troops" (009-164). Dan's father asks Harvey in confusion: "I thought you wuz a kinder railroad king" (009-166), unable to reconcile the hardworking boy he knows with the world of railroad tycoons.

## The Journey Between Worlds

The railroad itself is the physical link between Harvey's two worlds. The "Limited" train service reflects the class divisions of Gilded Age America — "Chicago and Boston are cheek by jowl, and some railroads encourage the delusion" (009-061). When Harvey travels by train with his father at the novel's end, the railroad that built his family's fortune now carries him between the fishing ports and the world of business.
