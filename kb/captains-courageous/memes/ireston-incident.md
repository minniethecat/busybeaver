# The Ireston Incident

The wrongful tarring and feathering of Captain Benjamin Ireson of Marblehead — a maritime injustice recounted by Disko Troop in Chapter IV, and revisited at the Gloucester memorial exercises in Chapter X — stands as one of the most powerful moral lessons in *Captains Courageous*: a warning against hasty judgments passed on a partial hearing of the facts.

## Background

The incident occurred before the War of 1812, when Captain Ben Ireson was skipper of the schooner *Betty*, returning home from the Grand Banks fishery. Off Cape Cod Light, the *Betty* encountered the *Active* of Portland, leaking badly in a terrible gale [004-035].

## The Abandonment and Its Aftermath

Ireson judged the sea too dangerous to risk a rescue and laid the matter before his crew, proposing they stand by the *Active* until conditions improved. His men refused — to stand by or to go — and instead "jest up stays'l an' quit," taking Ireson with them against his will. The next day, when the sea had calmed, a Truro boat took off some of the *Active*'s survivors [004-035].

When the *Betty* reached Marblehead, the *Active*'s crew told their own version of events, and public fury turned on Ireson. His own men, frightened by the town's anger, "went back on Ireson, an' swore he was respons'ble for the hull act" [004-035].

## The Tarring and Feathering

A mob of men and boys — not the women of Marblehead, Disko insists — seized Ireson, tarred and feathered him, and hauled him through the streets in an old dory:

> "they carted him araound town in an old dory till the bottom fell aout, and Ireson he told 'em they'd be sorry for it some day" [004-035]

## The Whittier Poem

Years later, the poet John Greenleaf Whittier wrote "Skipper Ireson's Ride," which picked up the false version of the story and "tarred and feathered Ben Ireson all over onct more after he was dead" [004-035]. Disko Troop, whose father had known Ireson personally, considered Whittier's poem "one o' the worst kind" of mistaken judgments — "becaze it's catchin' to the ear" [004-032].

## The Gloucester Memorial (Chapter X)

The Ireston incident resurfaces at the Gloucester memorial exercises in Chapter X. Learning that a Philadelphia actress intends to perform "Skipper Ireson's Ride," Harvey Cheyne — having been educated by Disko — intervenes, spending half a day "explaining to an amused actress with a royal reputation on two seaboards the inwardness of the mistake she contemplated" [010-078]. The actress yields, and instead delivers a poem about a Brixham fishing-port and a fleet beating in against storm by night [010-105].

Afterward, Disko acknowledges Harvey's role: "You've fixed it about old man Ireson, hain't ye, Harve? Ye know why naow" [010-104].
