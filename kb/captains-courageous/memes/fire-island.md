# Fire Island

Fire Island is a barrier island off the southern coast of Long Island, New York. In *Captains Courageous*, it appears in the oral folklore of the fishing fleet — both as a navigational reference point and as the setting for one of Long Jack's ghost stories about Captain Kidd's buried treasure.

## Navigational Landmark

Fire Island serves as a practical reference point in the fishermen's spatial vocabulary. When urging Long Jack to tell a story without delay, a crewman says impatiently: "We're not drawin' twenty-five fut off Fire Island in a fog. There's no trick to ut" [003-034]. The remark implies that navigating close to Fire Island in poor visibility would require careful attention — a dig at Long Jack for drawing out his tale unnecessarily. The casual reference suggests that Fire Island was a familiar landmark to the Gloucester fishermen, part of the mental map of the Atlantic coast they carried in their heads.

## Pirate Treasure and Ghosts

Long Jack's repertoire of supernatural tales includes "hidden treasure on Fire Island guarded by the spirits of Kidd's men" [005-034]. This is one of a series of ghost stories — along with the "Yo-hoes" on Monomoy Beach and the sand-walkers and dune-haunters of Cape Cod — that Long Jack tells to hold his shipmates silent during off-watch hours.

The legend of Captain Kidd's treasure on Fire Island has deep roots in Atlantic folklore. William Kidd, the notorious privateer-turned-pirate, was said to have buried treasure at various locations along the American coast before his capture and execution in 1701. Fire Island, with its remote beaches and shifting dunes, was a natural candidate for such legends. The "spirits of Kidd's men" that guard the treasure reflect the folk belief that pirate gold was protected by supernatural forces — a belief that appealed powerfully to the imagination of nineteenth-century sailors.

## Folklore and the Sea

Kipling's inclusion of the Fire Island legend places the novel within the rich tradition of Atlantic maritime folklore. The fishermen's world is populated not only by cod and schooners but by ghosts, omens, and buried treasure — a supernatural dimension that reflects the isolation and danger of life at sea. Long Jack's stories serve a practical purpose aboard the *We're Here*, providing entertainment during long watches and reinforcing the bonds of the crew through shared tales of wonder and terror.
