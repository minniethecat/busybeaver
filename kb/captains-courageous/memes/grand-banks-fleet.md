# Grand Banks Fishing Fleet

The collection of [[schooner|schooners]] — primarily from [[gloucester|Gloucester]], but also from Nova Scotia, Newfoundland, and France — that work the [[grand-banks|Grand Banks]] each season. Though each schooner is an independent enterprise, the fleet functions as a community: sharing information about fishing grounds, assisting vessels in distress, and maintaining the unwritten code of conduct that governs life on the Banks.

The fleet is the novel's largest social unit — larger than a single crew, larger than Gloucester, stretching across the entire western North Atlantic. It is bound not by law but by custom, not by contract but by the shared understanding of men who face the same sea and the same death.

## 概述

In the 1890s, hundreds of fishing schooners from ports around the North Atlantic converged on the Grand Banks each spring. The Gloucester fleet was the largest and most famous, known for its fast, seaworthy vessels and its skilled captains. The fleet had no formal organization — no admiral, no flag, no charter — but it operated by a powerful set of customs and expectations.

A schooner that failed to assist another in distress would be ostracized. A captain who lied about his catch would lose the trust of his peers. These unwritten rules — what the fishermen call "Bank law" — are enforced by reputation alone, and reputation on the Banks is everything.

The Banks themselves form the fleet's theater of operations: a triangle of ocean roughly two hundred and fifty miles on each side, cloaked in fog, vexed by gales, harried by drifting ice, and scored by the tracks of reckless liners — yet dotted everywhere with the sails of the fishing-fleet (005-008). Into this dangerous arena, the schooners pour each spring, a floating city of working men from every Atlantic port.

## 在书中的角色

### The Social Context

The fleet is the social context for the *We're Here*'s voyage. [[disko-troop|Disko Troop]] navigates not only the sea but also the complex social geography of the Banks — which schooners to trust, which grounds to share, how to signal for help and how to offer it. His authority as captain is reinforced by his standing in this larger community: a man known and respected across the fleet.

> Each schooner in sight seemed to be pulling her dories towards her by invisible strings, and the little black figures in the tiny boats pulled like clockwork toys.
> — (002-061)

Dan explains that his father's ability to find fish is recognized throughout the fleet, and other schooners will shadow his movements hoping to benefit from his knowledge:

> "Dad he knows the cod, an' the Fleet they know Dad knows. 'See 'em comm' up one by one, lookin' fer nothin' in particular, o' course, but scrowgin' on us all the time?"
> — (003-003)

The fleet's collective intelligence shapes every tactical decision. Disko accepts the presence of inquisitive schooners on the horizon as a compliment to his powers, but yearns to slip away and work alone (003-004). His standing within the fleet is his most carefully guarded asset:

> Disko's skill and honour were involved in the march he had stolen on the rest of the Fleet, and he had his reputation as a master artist who knew the Banks blindfold.
> — (003-125)

### The Unwritten Code

The fleet's collective judgment is a constant, unseen presence. When [[penn|Penn's]] kelleg is recognized by every dory on the Banks, the mockery he would face is a powerful social sanction — the fleet enforces its norms through ridicule and respect, not through formal discipline:

> "Big stone instid of an anchor. You kin see a kelleg ridin' in the bows fur's you can see a dory, an' all the fleet knows what it means. They'd guy him dreadful."
> — (003-051)

When the *We're Here*'s dory is spotted with a problem, other schooners recognize it by its paint scheme and offer help without being asked.

This floating community is [[harvey-cheyne|Harvey Cheyne's]] true school. His education is not merely in [[cod-fishing|fishing]] and seamanship but in the code of the fleet — a code that values competence over wealth, effort over station, and mutual obligation over individual advantage.

### The Gathering of the Fleet

The fleet is never more vividly realized than when it assembles at the Virgin — the great shoal where the cod swarm by the million. At such moments, the scale of this floating society becomes visible:

> The sun was just clear of the horizon they had not seen for nearly a week, and his low red light struck into the riding-sails of three fleets of anchored schooners — one to the north, one to the westward, and one to the south. There must have been nearly a hundred of them, of every possible make and build.
> — (008-001)

The fleet becomes a town at anchor, with dories dropping away "like bees from a crowded hive," and the air alive with voices and the rattle of gear (008-002). The assembly at the Virgin is the novel's great set-piece for the fleet as a social organism, where gossip, rivalry, and solidarity all play out at once.

> Men had met one another before, dory-fishing in the fog, and there is no place for gossip like the Bank fleet.
> — (008-008)

### Hierarchy and Seamanship

The fleet is an arena of constant judgment. Seamanship is assessed in silence; incompetence is punished with public ridicule. The cardinal rule of the Banks is competence:

> The Bank fleet pass good seamanship in silence; but a bungler is jeered all along the line.
> — (008-005)

Reputations are formed — and sometimes caricatured — in the collective conversations between dories. Nick Brady is known as "the meanest man on the Banks" (003-073). A Truro man convicted of using a "scrowger" (a tackle with too many hooks) is remembered and shamed years later (008-023). Every schooner and nearly every man gets his turn before the fleet's invisible bench of judges.

> Don't go visitin' raound the Fleet. If anyone asks you what I'm cal'culatin' to do, speak the truth — fer ye don't know.
> — (003-009)

### Protection and Mutual Obligation

The fleet's unwritten law of mutual aid is tested in storms. When a gale catches the schooners at the Virgin, the code holds: the *We're Here* takes aboard seven extra men from wrecked dories, including a Swede, a Chatham skipper, and a boy from Maine (008-042). The fleet sorts itself out after the storm, and all eat with better appetites when boat after boat reports full crews aboard (008-043).

When Uncle Abishai's schooner goes down, Manuel voices the instinct of the fleet:

> "Ver' good thing, I think, to tell the Fleet when we see. Eh, wha-at?"
> — (004-101)

The darker side of the fleet's mutual obligation is the counting of the dead. The annual Gloucester memorial ceremony, where the year's list of losses is read aloud, reveals the price the fleet pays for its harvest — one hundred and seventeen names in a single year (010-098). The widows brace themselves "like people going to be shot in cold blood" as each schooner lost and each dory overturned is read into the record (010-119).

### Rivalry and Solidarity

Competition between schooners — for fish, for bragging rights, for the honor of being first off the Banks — runs through the novel. The races home, with bets of tobacco and the right to fly the Bank flag first, are the fleet's seasonal rituals:

> The We're Here was racing neck and neck for her last few loads against the Parry Norman; and so close was the struggle that the Fleet took side and betted tobacco.
> — (008-100)

When the *We're Here* finishes loading and hoists her flag as "the first boat off the Banks," Disko steers her through the anchored schooners in a triumphant procession, while Dan's accordion and Tom Platt's fiddle ring out the song that can only be sung when all the salt is wet (008-100). The last letters pitched aboard, the shouted messages to wives and owners, and the "musical ride through the Fleet" mark the season's end (008-101).

Yet the rivalry sits within a deeper solidarity. Dan explains that Disko avoids the mixed gatherings of the fleet's multinational character:

> He objected to the mixed gatherings of a fleet of all nations. The bulk of them were mainly Gloucester boats, with a scattering from Provincetown, Harwich, Chatham, and some of the Maine ports, but the crews drew from goodness knows where.
> — (005-038)

The fleet is cosmopolitan — "every dialect from Labrador to Long Island, with Portuguese, Neapolitan, Lingua Franca, French, and Gaelic" rattles through the dory-fishery (008-009). This diversity, combined with risk and greed, creates the volatile social chemistry that gives the fleet its character.

### Harvey's Education in Fleet Life

Harvey's journey from outsider to "full-blooded Banker" (010-077) is measured by his acceptance into the fleet's community. The crew's careful management of his reputation — telling other schooners he was "picked up struck adrift on the Banks" rather than revealing his wealth (009-107) — protects him within the fleet's egalitarian code.

The fleet is introduced to Harvey before he ever reaches the *We're Here*, when the liner's engineer notes they are sailing through the fishing grounds:

> "We'll be on the Grand Bank tonight; but in a general way o' speakin', we're all among the fishing-fleet now. We've shaved three dories an' near scalped the boom off a Frenchman since noon."
> — (001-030)

And the fleet is the first thing the reader sees — the novel opens with the liner "whistling to warn the fishing-fleet" in the North Atlantic fog (001-001). From that opening image to Harvey's final declaration as a "Banker," the fleet frames the entire arc of the story.
