# Bow

The **bow** is the forward section of a ship, the part that first meets the sea. In _Captains Courageous_, the bows of the fishing schooners are constantly described as they cut through the Atlantic swells on the Grand Banks, shaping the experience of life at sea.

## Structure and Equipment

The bow of a Banks fishing schooner like the _We're Here_ carries essential working gear. "In her bows lay a tiny anchor, two jugs of fresh water, and some seventy fathoms of small line" — the equipment needed for the dory fishing operations (003-014). The bow also houses the "foc'sle gangway," the entrance to the crew's living quarters forward (004-072).

The bowsprit is a key feature described throughout the book. Some vessels have distinctive bowsprits — one schooner's "bowsprit cocked up like an old-fashioned frigate's" (004-082), while the _Carrie Pitman_ has "her bowsprit steeved that way" (003-073). A broken or damaged bowsprit was a serious injury; the account of one sailor "over the heel of the bowsprit" who "broken his back" underscores the dangers of working forward (008-044).

## The Bow in Heavy Seas

Kipling repeatedly describes the bow's action in rough water. The "flaring bows cut and squelch" through the waves (004-001), and the "solid bows hatcheted into the troughs" with powerful force (004-072). Harvey hears "the nosing bows slapped and scuffed" against the sea (003-173), a constant soundtrack of life aboard. The bow also generates a distinctive sound: "slapping and chatter at the bows now, varied by a solid thud and a little spout of spray" (003-087).

## The Bow as Viewpoint

The bow serves as a vantage point for watching the sea and weather. Harvey observes "the fog as it smoked over the bows" (003-119), and watches as "a rain-squall or two strayed hissing and wailing over the bows" (004-070). The bow is where the crew peers out into the fog for signs of other vessels or landmarks.

## The Jib-Boom

The jib-boom extends forward from the bow, carrying the headsails. The _We're Here_ is described "under jib and riding-sail" (006-064), and the jib-boom surges "ten feet beyond" as the schooner rides the waves (003-119). The jib-boom is a prominent feature — the cook is seen "riding the jib-boom and shouting Gaelic to a friend" (008-008), and in port the "jib-boom solemnly poked at the low stars" (008-103).

## Bow-to-Stern Action

The full length of the vessel is captured in the phrase "from bow to stern," which appears in the description of a shipboard fight that "raged from bow to stern till Penn came up and separated the men" (005-001). This expression conveys the compact scale of a fishing schooner where every part of the deck is within reach of daily activity.
