# Fishermen

The fishermen of the Grand Banks are the working heart of Kipling's novel — a community of skilled laborers who harvest cod from the North Atlantic under conditions of constant danger and physical hardship. They are characterized not by individual fame but by their collective identity: "We're fishermen," as Disko Troop declares, "an' we've shipped together for six years." This identity, shared across differences of nationality, language, and temperament, is the moral center of *Captains Courageous*. — (002-029)

## The Community of the Fleet

The Banks fishing fleet draws men from Gloucester, Newfoundland, Nova Scotia, Portugal, and beyond. Aboard a single schooner like the *We're Here*, the crew may include a former farmer like Uncle Salters, a displaced genius like Penn, a Portuguese Catholic like Manuel, and a Black cook whose dignity matches anyone aboard. What binds them is not nationality or class but the shared work of fishing — the long hours in dories, the gutting and salting of cod, the rhythm of the Banks seasons. — (004-018)

The fishermen's world operates by its own rules. Bank law — the unwritten code governing gear, territory, and conduct — is understood by all who work the grounds, regardless of what flag their vessel flies. The community extends across vessels: fishermen recognize one another by the cut of a dory's sail or the handling of a schooner in a blow, and they gather for talk and competition when the fleet returns to Gloucester. — (009-100)

## Work and Culture

A fisherman's life is governed by the seasons and the fish. The spring caplin run brings the cod close to shore; summer sends the fleet to the Virgin and Eastern Shoals; autumn brings the race back to Gloucester with the season's final catch. Between the hours of fishing, the men gather in the schooner's cabin for meals, songs, and stories. The oral culture of the Banks — tales of wrecks, remarkable catches, lost ships, and near escapes — is as much a part of the fisherman's world as the trawl line and the gurry bucket.

The discussion among fishermen runs in "a steady, uninterrupted current," moving from the price of salt cod to the handling of a particular schooner, from the memory of a drowned crewman to the merits of a new type of dory. This talk is both entertainment and education: young fishermen like Harvey Cheyne learn the lore of the Banks by listening. — (004-018)

## Fishermen in Port

When the fleet returns to Gloucester, the fishermen's identity shifts but does not dissolve. They crowd the wharves and streets of the port, recognizable in their oilskins and their rolling gait. At the Memorial Day ceremony, the fishermen press forward to see the procession, their presence a reminder that the town's prosperity — and its monuments — are built on their labor. — (010-081), — (010-119)
