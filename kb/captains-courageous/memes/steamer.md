# Steamer

The steamer — specifically the transatlantic passenger liner — serves as the novel's opening setting and the vehicle of Harvey Cheyne's fall from privilege into the world of the Grand Banks fishing fleet. In the late 19th-century North Atlantic depicted by Kipling, steamers represent speed, wealth, and the industrialized world, contrasting sharply with the sail-powered fishing schooners that operate under wind and muscle.

## The Passenger Liner

The novel opens aboard a large passenger steamer crossing the North Atlantic. The vessel carries wealthy travelers between America and Europe, featuring a smoking-room, saloon decks, staterooms, and stewards — the full apparatus of first-class ocean travel circa 1890. It is from this steamer that Harvey Cheyne, "the biggest nuisance aboard," falls overboard into the fog, an accident that transforms his life. — (001-001), — (001-002)

The steamer's crew and passengers represent the world Harvey is about to leave behind: the German man in the frieze overcoat, the drawling New Yorker, the Philadelphian, and the stewards who serve breakfast in the saloon. Their conversation about Harvey — his railroads-and-lumber father, his indulgent mother, his two hundred dollars a month pocket money — establishes the boy's background before he is plunged into the very different world of the fishing fleet. — (001-002), — (001-005)

## Steamer vs. Schooner

The contrast between steamers and sailing vessels runs throughout the novel. Fishermen on schooners like the *We're Here* view steamers with a mixture of wariness and contempt. A steamer can run down a fishing boat without stopping, and the crew knows that the big liners have neither the agility nor the inclination to avoid small craft on the Banks. — (007-004)

Disko Troop recalls the ram-steamer *Arctic* that "breaks the ice between Montreal and Quebec," one of many tales of steam-powered vessels that the crew trades during quiet watches. These stories form part of the oral culture of the Banks fishermen, who measure their own experience against the industrial world that is steadily encroaching on their traditional way of life. — (005-036)

## Practical Role

Steamers serve a practical function for the fishing fleet: they provide a way to send letters home. When a homeward-bound steamer passes close enough, the fishermen pass letters across, maintaining the only link between the months-long fishing voyage and the families waiting in Gloucester. Disko notes the departure and return of steamers as markers of the season's progress. — (005-034)
