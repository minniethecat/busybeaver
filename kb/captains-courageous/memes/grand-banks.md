# Grand Banks

A vast submerged plateau stretching for hundreds of miles southeast of Newfoundland, where the mingling of the cold Labrador Current and the warm Gulf Stream creates one of the most productive marine ecosystems on Earth.

For the Gloucester fishing fleet — and for the crew of the *We're Here* — the Grand Banks are both workplace and world, a dangerous but bountiful expanse that defines their lives and livelihoods.

## Geography

The Grand Banks extend from approximately 42 degrees to 47 degrees north latitude and 49 degrees to 53 degrees west longitude, covering an area larger than Newfoundland itself. The sea floor rises from abyssal depths to as shallow as 15 fathoms, creating conditions for the immense cod populations that drew fishermen for centuries.

The Banks are notorious for their weather — particularly the dense fog that forms where warm air meets cold water, a phenomenon that plays a decisive role in the novel's opening. The very first sentence establishes the elemental presence of the Banks:

> The weather door of the smoking-room had been left open to the North Atlantic fog, as the big liner rolled and lifted, whistling to warn the fishing-fleet.
> — (001-001)

The navigation of the Banks requires both art and science. Disko navigates using the old green-crusted quadrant, an Eldridge chart, and the deep-sea lead as his "spare eye" (005-006). His true navigation is instinctive: when placing anchor he "thought as a cod," drawing on decades of experience with the sea floor (005-007). The deep-sea lead is his most vital instrument, and Dan explains its purpose clearly:

> "'Tain't soundin's dad wants. It's samples. Grease her up good, Harve." Harvey would tallow the cup at the end, and carefully bring the sand, shell, sludge, or whatever it might be, to Disko, who fingered and smelt it and gave judgment.
> — (005-007)

## In the Novel

The Grand Banks are the novel's primary setting. Every significant event takes place on the Banks: Harvey's rescue, his apprenticeship, his halibut fight, the caplin run, the race back to Gloucester.

The novel begins with the luxury liner carrying Harvey Cheyne crossing the Grand Bank on the first night of the story. As the ship's engineer matter-of-factly reports: "We'll be on the Grand Bank tonight; but in a general way o' speakin', we're all among the fishing-fleet now" (001-030). Harvey is pitched overboard into these same waters, and Manuel rescues him in a dory, blowing a conch-shell through the fog to guide the schooner to them (001-044).

Disko Troop knows the Banks intimately. He explains to Harvey that there will be no returning to shore: "we've jest come on to the Banks, workin' fer our bread" (001-085). He navigates by instinct and experience rather than by instruments alone (005-007). Disko's reputation as a master artist who knows the Banks blindfold is his deepest pride; when sounding the lead in fog, he can call the depth — "Sixty, mebbe — ef I'm any judge" — and be right (003-125).

Kipling describes the Grand Bank as Disko's chessboard in the novel's most iconic passage about the setting:

> But Disko's board was the Grand Bank — a triangle two hundred and fifty miles on each side — a waste of wallowing sea, cloaked with dank fog, vexed with gales, harried with drifting ice, scored by the tracks of the reckless liners, and dotted with the sails of the fishing-fleet.
> — (005-008)

The Banks contain hidden dangers beyond the weather. The Whale-deep is a barren hole where the bottom drops away to unfathomable depths, briefly terrorizing Harvey when his anchor finds no ground. The whole length of the roding runs out with nothing to catch, and Harvey grows mortally afraid, for that his last touch with earth is lost (005-009). There are also summer icebergs: Harvey's first introduction to "the dread summer berg of the Banks" leaves him cowering in the bottom of the boat while Manuel laughs (005-009).

> "Whale-hole," said Manuel, hauling in. "That is good joke on Disko. Come!"
> — (005-009)

The Banks are also a place of transformation. Harvey, who arrives as a spoiled millionaire's son trying to impress with "cold dollars and cents" (001-068), becomes a fisherman. Manuel, his rescuer, puts it simply: "How shall I leave you dreeft, dreeft all around the Banks? Now you are a fisherman" (002-080). When Harvey's father receives the telegram, it reads: "Picked up by fishing schooner We're Here having fallen off boat great times on Banks fishing all well" (009-007). Disko later introduces Harvey to the shore crowd with understatement: "We picked him up struck adrift on the Banks" (009-107).

The peak moment of the novel's action takes place on the Banks at the caplin run at the Virgin, where three fleets of anchored schooners gather in a floating town. Harvey witnesses "the greatest thing on the Banks" when the caplin school drives the cod into a frenzy and the sea froths with fish (008-028). Dan tells him:

> "Once ivry fifteen minutes, to the tick. Harve, you've seen the greatest thing on the Banks; an' but for Long Jack you'd seen some dead men too."
> — (008-028)

The Virgin itself is a submerged rock that rises to within twenty feet of the surface, a landmark that Disko shows Harvey on the chart when he teaches him "from berth to berth over the whole string of banks — Le Have, Western, Banquereau, St. Pierre, Green, and Grand" (005-029).

The departure from the Banks is bittersweet. After their final dash for fish, "racing neck and neck for her last few loads" (008-100), the *We're Here* leaves behind the waters that have defined the entire voyage:

> They left the cold gray of the Bank sea, saw the lumber-ships making for Quebec by the Straits of St. Lawrence, with the Jersey salt-brigs from Spain and Sicily.
> — (008-105)

Even in the calmer passages of the novel, the Banks' beauty is rendered vividly:

> The low sun made the water all purple and pinkish, with golden lights on the barrels of the long swells, and blue and green mackerel shades in the hollows. Each schooner in sight seemed to be pulling her dories towards her by invisible strings, and the little black figures in the tiny boats pulled like clockwork toys.
> — (002-061)

## Key Characters

- [[Disko Troop]]: knows the Banks intimately after a lifetime of fishing; navigates "as a blindfolded chess-player moves on the unseen board" (005-007). He "thought as a cod" when placing anchor (005-007), and his reputation as a master artist who knows the Banks blindfold is his deepest pride (003-125).
- [[Harvey Cheyne]]: transformed by his months on the Banks. First encounters the fishing fleet as the liner crosses the Grand Bank at night (001-030). Learns to handle a dory in fog and takes a halibut bigger than Dan has seen all season (003-040). By voyage's end, he has earned his pay as a full crew member.
- [[Dan Troop]]: raised on the Banks since childhood; knows every shoal and berth by name. Warns Harvey that if there is bad luck loose on the Banks, it will find Uncle Salters (002-068). He understands his father's silent reading of the fleet movements (003-003).

## Maritime and Fishing Features

The Grand Banks fishery in the 1890s was dominated by schooner-based dory fishing. Disko knows the cod intimately — he literally studies the fish, "pitting his knowledge and experience on the Banks against the roving cod in his own sea" (003-004). Dan explains his father's method: "Dad he knows the cod, an' the Fleet they know Dad knows" (003-003).

The *We're Here* runs "along the east edge of the Grand Bank in thirty to forty fathom water, and fishing steadily" (006-048). The meeting of the Labrador Current and Gulf Stream creates nutrient-rich conditions that support the entire food chain, from plankton to caplin to cod to the fishermen who pursue them. The squid is one of the best cod baits on the Banks, though uncertain in its moods — a midnight squid catch becomes a frantic, ink-spraying scene aboard the schooner, and the crew finish "as black as sweeps" with a pile of fresh squid on deck (006-049).

Disko navigates using traditional tools — the old green-crusted quadrant, an Eldridge chart, and the deep-sea lead (005-006). He teaches Harvey the meaning of the chart and leads him "from berth to berth over the whole string of banks — Le Have, Western, Banquereau, St. Pierre, Green, and Grand" (005-029). The fleet on the Banks was a floating community. Schooners from Gloucester, Provincetown, Harwich, and Chatham gathered on the fishing grounds, trading gossip, tobacco, and news (008-001). An unwritten code of seamanship governed the Banks — the same code that compelled Disko to rescue Harvey despite the cost to his fishing voyage.

Bank law strictly forbids more than one hook on one line when the dories are on the Virgin or the Eastern Shoals, a regulation born of the crowded fishing conditions (008-016). The constant dangers of the Banks are never forgotten. One foggy dawn, Harvey rings the bell desperately as a liner's siren shrieks nearby, knowing exactly "what that meant" for a fishing dory in the path of a steamer (007-004).

Even ashore, the Banks cast a long shadow. At the Gloucester memorial service, the orator reminds the crowd that "neither Georges nor the Banks were cow-pastures" (010-098). The sea exacts its yearly price in lives for the harvest it yields: a hundred and seventeen men are named as lost from Gloucester alone that year (010-098). The fishermen themselves, Disko foremost among them, resist this characterization: "I jest despise the beggin' pieces in it. It don't give folk a fair notion of us" (010-099).
