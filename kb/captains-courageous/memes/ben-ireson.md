# Ben Ireson

Ben Ireson was the skipper of the fishing schooner _Betty_, a real historical figure from Marblehead, Massachusetts, whose alleged abandonment of a stricken vessel became the subject of John Greenleaf Whittier's poem "Skipper Ireson's Ride." In _Captains Courageous_, Ireson's story is retold — and his reputation passionately defended — by [[tom-platt|Tom Platt]], who uses it to teach [[harvey-cheyne|Harvey Cheyne]] a lesson about "hasty jedgments."

## 身份与背景

Ireson was a Marblehead fishing captain who sailed in the early nineteenth century, "before the war of 1812" (004-037). According to the historical record — and Whittier's poem — his schooner _Betty_ encountered a sinking vessel from the Grand Banks fleet and, depending on which account one believes, either shamefully abandoned the crew to drown or was unjustly accused of doing so.

The incident became a New England byword for maritime cowardice. Women of Marblehead, in the poem's most vivid image, tarred and feathered Ireson and dragged him through the streets in a cart — a punishment usually reserved for Tories and traitors.

Tom Platt, however, tells a different story.

## 外貌与性格

Kipling gives no physical description of Ireson — the man died decades before the novel's events. What we receive instead is a moral portrait refracted through Tom Platt's fierce sense of justice. Ireson, in Platt's telling, was "no sech kind o' man as Whittier makes aout" (004-037). He was a competent, honorable captain whose reputation was destroyed by a "lyin' tale" — and whose posthumous defamation by a famous poet is, to Platt, a continuing injustice.

## 主要经历

### The Incident

The facts, as Tom Platt relates them, are that Ireson's crew — not Ireson himself — made the decision not to render aid. The skipper was overruled by his own men, who feared for their safety in heavy seas. "The facts come aout later, same's they usually do, too late to be any ways useful to an honest man" (004-037).

### Whittier's Poem

Whittier, writing decades after the event, picked up "the slack eend of a lyin' tale" (004-037) and crafted "Skipper Ireson's Ride" — a poem so vivid and damning that it fixed Ireson's guilt in the popular imagination. " 'Twas the only tune Whittier ever slipped up, an' 'tweren't fair," Platt insists. The poem tarred and feathered Ireson "all over onct more after he was dead."

### The Memorial Day Scene

Ireson's ghost haunts the Memorial Day ceremony in Chapter 10. Disko Troop, having heard that a "Philadelphia actress-woman" might recite "Skipper Ireson's Ride" at the Gloucester memorial, "mistrusted" the choice — not out of literary judgment but because "justice was justice" (010-078). The poem that would have been recited was not Ireson's ride after all, but a different maritime piece about Brixham — a choice that, one imagines, Platt would have approved.

## 人物关系

- [[tom-platt|Tom Platt]]: Ireson's posthumous defender, whose father "knew him well, before an' after that business" (004-037)
- [[harvey-cheyne|Harvey Cheyne]]: the young listener who receives Platt's cautionary tale about "hasty jedgments"
- [[disko-troop|Disko Troop]]: shares Platt's skepticism about the Whittier version

## 作用与意义

Ben Ireson serves a dual function in _Captains Courageous_. As a historical figure embedded in New England maritime folklore, he authenticates the novel's fishing-world setting — grounding Kipling's fiction in the real legends and controversies of the Grand Banks community. As a moral exemplum, his story teaches Harvey (and the reader) that received narratives are not always true, that reputations are fragile, and that "jestice is jestice at all times" (004-037).

The Ireson episode also positions Tom Platt as more than a comic Navy veteran. His passionate defense of a dead skipper he never met reveals a deep commitment to truth and fairness that is characteristic of the fishing community Kipling admired.
