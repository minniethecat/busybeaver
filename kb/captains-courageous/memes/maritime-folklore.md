# Maritime Folklore

Maritime folklore, as it appears among the Gloucester fishing fleet, encompasses the superstitions, oral traditions, and shared beliefs that shape the culture of the men who work the Grand Banks. These beliefs range from the fatalistic attribution of bad luck to certain vessels, to the folk explanations for the dangers and mysteries of the sea.

## Superstition and Bad Luck

The fishermen of the fleet hold strong beliefs about luck and its vessels. The *Carrie Pitman* is branded "her own Jonah" — a reference to the Biblical Jonah who brought misfortune to his ship — and one crewman remarks that someone "ought to be on the Carrie Pitman" as a kind of punishment [004-067]. This folk belief in unlucky ships is taken seriously enough that the *Carrie Pitman*'s reputation follows her across the Banks, with men joking that "the Carrie Pitman's anchor doesn't hold her for a week" [008-023].

## Jonahs and Fate

The concept of a "Jonah" — a person or vessel that brings bad luck — runs deep in the fishermen's worldview. The belief traces back to the Old Testament story of Jonah, who was thrown overboard to calm a storm. In the fleet, to be called a Jonah is a serious matter, and the label sticks to ships and men alike. The fishermen interpret mishaps at sea through this folkloric framework, attributing accidents, poor catches, and lost gear to the influence of unlucky persons or boats.

## Catholic Superstitions

The religious dimension of maritime folklore is visible in the cultural clash between the Portuguese Catholic fishermen and the Protestant Yankees. Disko Troop, representing the pragmatic New England tradition, dismissively contrasts "progress and Catholic superstitions" [008-097], recognizing that the Portuguese fishermen's folk Catholicism — with its saints, prayers, and rituals for protection at sea — differs fundamentally from his own worldview. Yet both traditions coexist aboard the *We're Here*, bound together by the shared work and shared dangers of fishing.
