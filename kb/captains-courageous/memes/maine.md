# Maine

Maine, the northeasternmost state in New England, appears in *Captains Courageous* primarily through the voices of its fishermen and the Massachusetts crew's good-natured ribbing of their northern neighbors. The state's extensive coastline and maritime traditions make it an integral part of the fishing world that Kipling depicts, even though the narrative never touches its shores directly.

## Regional Rivalry

The tension between Massachusetts and Maine fishermen surfaces repeatedly in the novel. In one telling episode, Long Jack tells "ghastly stories of the 'Yo-hoes' on Monomoy Beach" [005-034], tales that belong to the shared folklore of the New England coast, from Maine to Cape Cod. The maritime supernatural tradition spans state boundaries, uniting fishing communities in a common culture of sea-ghosts and omens.

The rivalry sharpens at a memorial service in Gloucester, where Salters pointedly defends Massachusetts superiority: "If Dan here couldn't do better'n that with one hand before breakfast, he ought to be switched" [010-121], but then concedes with characteristic gruffness that a contribution is "considerable litt'ery — fer Maine" [010-121]. A voice from the crowd calls out identifying a fallen fisherman as from "Augusty, Maine" [010-127], a reminder that Maine men sailed and died alongside their Massachusetts counterparts.

## "Ain't Maine Progressive?"

In a revealing exchange, the crew discusses a knife auctioned from a deceased French fisherman. When Harvey asks why the Eastport, Maine man present did not bid, the response is dismissive: "Maine? Pshaw! They don't know enough, or they hain't got money enough, to paint their haouses in Maine. I've seen 'em" [008-056]. The comment, while reflecting Massachusetts chauvinism, captures a perception of Maine as the poorer, rougher cousin of the Bay State — a region where maritime life was harder and rewards fewer.

## Maritime Maine

In the 1890s, Maine's economy was deeply tied to the sea. Ports like Portland, Bath, and Eastport sent fishermen to the Grand Banks and built the schooners that carried them. The state's rocky coastline, dotted with islands and harbors, produced generations of seafarers. Though Kipling's narrative centers on Gloucester, the Maine fishing community was a constant presence on the Banks, and its men appear in the novel's casualty lists as a reminder of the shared dangers of the fishery.
