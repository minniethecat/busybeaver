# Jacob Boiler

Jacob Boiler was a Moravian preacher whose memory surfaces among the crew of the *We're Here*, particularly through Uncle Salters and the simple-minded Penn. Though never appearing in the story directly, Boiler serves as a touchstone for memory, faith, trauma, and the fishing community's shared history.

## In the Story

The name Jacob Boiler recurs in the crew's conversations, often linked to memories of Johnstown and the catastrophic flood there. Uncle Salters recalls him as a Moravian preacher with a powerful presence:

> "He was a Moravian preacher once. Jacob Boiler wuz his name, Dad told me, an' he lived right in the middle of the Johnstown flood." [003-062]

The fishermen's attitude toward Boiler is ambivalent—they respect his faith but also joke about his influence on Penn's mental state. When Salters's father encountered Boiler, he recognized something extraordinary:

> "Dad knowed him and his folks. They wouldn't hunt the Banks fer Jacob Boiler. Dad was agreeable, fer Uncle Salters was——" [003-066]

When Penn begins preaching to the crew, Uncle Salters makes the connection immediately:

> "It's Jacob Boiler, an'—he's remembered Johnstown!" cried Uncle Salters. [007-020]

The verb "Jacob Boilerin'" enters the crew's vocabulary as a teasing term for Penn's preaching episodes. Long Jack observes the phenomenon with amusement:

> "Did ye see his face when Salters said Penn had stayed Jacob Boilerin'?" [007-057–059]

The crew's response to Penn's Boiler-like preaching mixes genuine wonder with irreverent humor. The fishermen are not church-going men, but Boiler's memory commands a certain respect:

> "All Gloucester men, wasn't they?" said Tom Platt, referring to the men who remembered Boiler. [007-016]

## Significance

Jacob Boiler represents the enduring presence of faith and memory in the fishing community. Though a figure from the past, his influence shapes how the crew understands and responds to Penn's religious outbursts.

The men's joking use of "Jacob Boilerin'" shows how even sacred topics become part of the everyday banter aboard the schooner—a defense mechanism against the raw power of Penn's unfiltered memories of flood and faith.

The connection between Boiler and the Johnstown Flood ties the novel's maritime world to one of the great American disasters of the 19th century, grounding the fishermen's experiences in a broader historical landscape.
