# New York

The city of [[harvey-cheyne|Harvey Cheyne's]] former life — a world of "Adirondacks, Lakewood, Hot Springs, New York, and round again" (001-007), a circuit of luxury that the drowned boy leaves behind when he falls from the liner into the [[grand-banks|Grand Banks]].

[[new-york|New York]] represents everything Harvey must unlearn: the contempt of the wealthy for manual labor, the assumption that money can command the sea, the belief that a man's worth is measured by his father's fortune. It is the point of departure and the standard of comparison against which the *We're Here* and her crew are measured and found wanting — in Harvey's eyes at first, and then in the reader's.

## 地理位置

New York City lies at the mouth of the Hudson River, the principal port of the eastern United States and the financial capital of the Gilded Age. In the 1890s, it was the terminus of the transatlantic liner routes — Harvey was bound for Europe when he fell overboard from a New York-to-Liverpool steamer (001-004).

The city is connected to [[gloucester|Gloucester]] and [[boston|Boston]] by rail: the "New York Limited" (009-024), the New York Central and Hudson River Railroad (009-061), and a web of connections that Harvey Cheyne Sr. will exploit in his desperate race.

## 在书中的作用

### Harvey's Old World

Harvey's demand to be returned to New York immediately after his rescue exposes the gulf between his world and the fishermen's. "I want him to take me to New York right away. It'll pay him" (001-055); "The sooner you take me back to New York the better it'll pay you" (001-066). The repetition is telling: Harvey's only tool is his father's money, and he cannot conceive of a situation where money does not work.

The fishermen's response — "We don't use Noo York any" (001-074) — is the first crack in Harvey's worldview. New York is not the center of the universe. On the Banks, it is not even a destination.

### The Society of Wealth

New York society appears in the novel through Harvey's family: the "struggling New York practice" his father abandoned for the West (009-010), the "New York paper" with its farming reports (006-014), the "New York club" burgee flying from a pleasure yacht (010-038). These fragments paint a picture of a city defined by status, commerce, and the hierarchies of wealth — the world Harvey must escape to become himself.

> Harvey Cheyne's world had been bounded by Adirondacks, Lakewood, Hot Springs, New York, and round again.
> — (001-007, adapted)

### The Contrast

Kipling sets New York against the Banks as thesis and antithesis. In New York, Harvey was "a son of one of the three or four men who have built up the New York, New Haven, and Hartford" — a boy whose every need was anticipated. On the *We're Here*, he is a second boy at ten and a half dollars a month. The distance between these two identities is the novel's true geography, and New York is the coordinate of everything Harvey leaves behind.

## 航海/渔业特征

New York was a major commercial port but not a fishing port in the novel's world. The distinction is meaningful: New York represents commerce and society; [[gloucester|Gloucester]] represents honest labor. The fishing schooners of Gloucester and the passenger liners of New York occupy different oceans — literally and metaphorically.

The New York Yacht Club burgee (010-038) appears in the final chapter, a final reminder of the world of wealth and leisure that Harvey has left behind. By now, it reads not as aspiration but as foreign territory.
