# Anchor

An anchor on a Grand Banks fishing schooner is more than a piece of iron — it is the one thing that stands between the vessel and drift onto a lee shore or collision with another schooner in the night. The *We're Here* carries a small iron anchor at her bow, supplemented by kellegs — large stones used as improvised anchors on the rocky Bank bottom (003-051). Knowing when and where to anchor, and reading the bottom to ensure the anchor holds, is part of the skipper's craft that Disko Troop has mastered over seven seasons (002-164).

## 定义

A schooner's anchor is a forged iron device with broad flukes designed to dig into the sea floor and hold against wind and current. On the Grand Banks, anchoring is complicated by the uneven bottom: in some places the anchor finds no purchase and drags uselessly across smooth rock, while in others it catches immediately and must be broken free with force. The "kelleg" — a large, flat stone wrapped in rope — serves the same purpose as an iron anchor and is used by dories as well as schooners (003-051). An "anchor-light" is kept burning at night to signal the vessel's position to the rest of the fleet (002-173).

## 具体细节

### The Kelleg

A kelleg is a flat stone used as an anchor, common among Grand Banks fishermen who cannot afford or do not need forged iron anchors for every purpose. Dories carry small kellegs to hold position over fishing ground. The kelleg is tied to a "roding" — a light anchor line — and dropped over the side. Penn shows Harvey how "a Spanish windlass" can be used to tighten a kelleg roding (003-054). When the kelleg's roding runs out and the stone finds nothing, the anchor is said to "find nothing" — a frustrating sign of poor holding ground (005-009).

### Anchoring Routine

At the end of a day's fishing, or when fog threatens to scatter the fleet, the schooner drops anchor. The crew heaves the anchor over the bow with a splash, paying out the rode until the flukes bite into the bottom (003-136). The skipper then checks that the anchor holds — walking forward to see that the vessel is not dragging (003-173). Weighing anchor — hauling it back aboard — is heavy work done at the windlass, the crew heaving in unison as the chain clanks over the bow rollers (003-077).

### The Anchor in the Fishery

Anchoring is a strategic act on the Banks. A schooner that anchors in the wrong place may miss the fish entirely; one that anchors too close to another vessel risks fouling gear. The fishermen know the bottom as intimately as farmers know their fields, and they anchor in places that have produced well in past seasons (005-032). A schooner will sometimes tow its anchor underfoot when making a short move, rather than weighing it fully and then re-anchoring, to save time and effort (005-042).
