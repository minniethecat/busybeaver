# Memorial Service for the Drowned

A gathering of the Gloucester fishing community to remember those who did not return from the sea. The memorial service is a somber counterpoint to the novel's themes of adventure and transformation — a reminder that the Grand Banks take as well as give, and that every fisherman who sails knows he may not come back.

## 背景

The Grand Banks fishery was one of the most dangerous occupations of the nineteenth century. Dories could be lost in fog, swamped by storms, or capsized by a running fish. Men fell overboard, were swept away by rogue waves, or simply vanished — their dories found empty, their fate unknown. At the end of each season, the community gathered to mourn the lost and to reaffirm the bonds that held the living together (010-070).

## 经过

The orator speaks of "the price that must be paid for the yearly harvest" and reads the names — one hundred and seventeen lost (010-098). The service is described with Kipling's characteristic restraint — no melodrama, no sermonizing. Families weep. The surviving fishermen stand in their shore clothes, uncomfortable on land, remembering their lost shipmates. For Harvey, who has come to know and respect the men of the fishery, the service is a sobering lesson in the true cost of the salt cod on his plate.

## 结果与影响

The memorial service reinforces the novel's moral seriousness. The Banks are not merely a setting for adventure but a place where men die. The community's ritual of remembrance transforms individual loss into collective meaning.
