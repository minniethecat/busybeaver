# Riding Sail

A triangular sail hoisted on the mainmast of a Grand Banks fishing schooner while she lies at anchor. Unlike the working sails used for propulsion, the riding sail serves a single purpose: to keep the schooner's bow pointed into the wind and minimize the uncomfortable, gear-damaging roll of the vessel in the open Atlantic swell (001-058).

## 定义

When a schooner anchors on the Grand Banks — often for days at a time while her dories fish the surrounding waters — she is exposed to wind and swell without the stabilizing effect of forward motion. The riding sail, set on the after side of the mainmast in a fore-and-aft orientation, acts like a weathervane: the wind pressure on the sail keeps the bow headed into the seas, preventing the vessel from "sailing around" her anchor and rolling heavily beam-on to the waves (001-058).

The riding sail is typically smaller than the schooner's working sails and is made of heavier canvas to withstand continuous exposure. It is set when the schooner arrives at the fishing ground and struck only when she gets underway again.

## 具体细节

### Harvey's First Morning

When Harvey Cheyne wakes on his first morning aboard the *We're Here*, he climbs on deck to find "the schooner, with a triangular riding-sail on the mainmast, played easily at anchor" (001-058). The image is one of quiet competence — the riding sail doing its silent work while the dories are out fishing and the deck is nearly deserted. To Harvey, who has known only steam liners and private yachts, the riding sail is an unfamiliar detail in an unfamiliar world.

### The Anchored Fleet

On a calm day, the Grand Banks fleet at anchor is a beautiful sight: dozens of schooners, each with her riding sail set, dotted across the horizon. "Each schooner in sight seemed to be pulling her dories towards her by invisible strings" — the riding sails steady, the little black dories rowing back at sunset (002-061). The riding sail is the visual signature of a schooner at work — a sign that she is on station and fishing.
