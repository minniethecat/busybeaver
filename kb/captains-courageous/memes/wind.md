# Wind

The wind is the fundamental motive power on the Grand Banks. Every vessel's movements — from the drift of a dory on a still sea to a schooner running before a gale — are governed by its strength and direction.

In *Captains Courageous*, wind is a constant character: it drops the Bank fog onto the fleet without warning, it pipes up in the night to drive schooners through the dark, and it fails altogether when fish are needed most (003-079).

## 定义

On a sailing fishing schooner, wind is both tool and adversary. Too little wind and the vessel cannot reach good fishing ground; too much and the dories cannot safely work, the gear strains, and the crew must reef sail and ride it out.

The ideal wind for hand-line fishing on the Banks is a moderate breeze — enough to keep the schooner drifting slowly across the ground, but not so much that the dories must be recalled (004-019). A "rising wind" signals danger and forces the captain to decide between safety and the catch (003-173).

## 具体细节

### Wind and Fog on the Banks

The Bank fog is intimately tied to the wind. When the wind drops, the fog closes in without warning — "between fish and fish," as Dan says — blanketing the fleet in white and leaving each schooner isolated and blind (003-077). A shift in the wind can lift the fog as suddenly as it descended, revealing the entire fleet scattered across the sea. The wind and fog together form the defining weather pattern of the Grand Banks, and a skipper who misreads their interplay risks collision or drifting off the fishing ground (005-008).

### The Sailor's Wind-Sense

The Banks fishermen read the wind with a precision born of long experience. Disko Troop can feel a coming wind shift before it arrives, sniffing the air and watching the colour of the sky (003-079). This wind-sense is not superstition but accumulated observation — the difference between making harbour safely and being caught on a lee shore. Harvey Cheyne learns this sensitivity himself over the course of the season: by mid-summer he can "steer in anything short of half a gale from the feel of the wind on his face, humouring the *We're Here* just when she needed it" (005-030).

### Wind and Ship-Handling

A schooner's handling is a constant negotiation with the wind. When the *We're Here* "looked up into the wind and dived off into blank, white fog," Troop had committed the vessel to a course known only to his wind-sense (003-078). Sailing "on the wind with the staysail set" was a point of pride for Harvey after his weeks of training (005-010). In heavy weather, the schooner would be put "head to wind" to ride out the blow, the safest attitude when the sea grew violent (004-087).

### Wind in the Fishermen's Songs

The crew's work songs — "It's windy weather" and "For it's blowy weather" — are chanteys timed to the rhythm of hauling lines and heaving at the windlass, the wind itself woven into the musical fabric of shipboard life (004-018, 004-019). The chantey's refrain "When the winds begin to blow, pipe all hands to heave at the lead" marks the moment when wind shifts from working ally to danger that demands all hands on deck (004-019).

### The Rising Gale as Narrative Turning Point

Wind esc alation marks the novel's dramatic peaks. When the wind rises and shifts, it transforms the familiar Banks into a hostile landscape: "a rain-squall or two strayed aimlessly over the hopeless waste, ran down 'wind and back again, and melted away" (004-070). The gale that strikes during the memorial service for Otto — swept overboard in a gale off Le Have — underscores the mortal stakes that wind represents for fishing crews (008-116). In the story of Abishai's schooner, a "livin' gale" becomes the test of seamanship, the measure by which a vessel and her crew prove their worth (004-089).

## 书中涉及场景

- **The rising wind** (Chapter 3): The elder men stretch out while the wind rises outside, a moment of tense rest before the weather worsens (003-173).
- **Wind and fog interplay** (Chapter 3): Troop announces "there's wind behind this fog" and the *We're Here* dives into blank whiteness, trusting the skipper's wind-sense (003-078).
- **Light wind shifts** (Chapter 2): Penn creeps on deck as the riding-sail slats in the shifts of a light wind, the clock striking ten in the cabin (002-177).
- **Harvey masters the helm** (Chapter 5): Harvey proves he can steer in any wind short of half a gale, reading the wind on his face without instruments (005-030).
- **The gale and the memorial** (Chapter 8): A storm forces wet strangers aboard for shelter while the crew lowers the flag for Otto, lost in a gale (008-042, 008-116).
- **The squall** (Chapter 4): The *We're Here* surfs through a storm while the wind "hooted through her rigging," the schooner flung up gray hills of sea (004-070).
