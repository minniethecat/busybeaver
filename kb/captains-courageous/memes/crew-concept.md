# Crew

The crew of the *We're Here* exemplifies the multicultural, hierarchical world of the Grand Banks fishery. Under Disko Troop's command, Portuguese, Irish, Nova Scotian, and Yankee fishermen worked side by side, their differences subordinated to the demands of the work.

## Composition and Hierarchy

A typical Banks schooner crew included:

| Role | *We're Here* | Responsibilities |
|------|-------------|------------------|
| Skipper | [[disko-troop]] | Absolute command, navigation, business |
| Second hand | [[tom-platt]] | Senior deckhand, relief command |
| Dorymen | [[manuel]], [[long-jack]], [[uncle-salters]] | Fishing from dories |
| Cook | The cook | Galley, provisions, morale |
| Boys | [[dan-troop]], [[harvey-cheyne]] | Learning the trade, assisting |

## The Code of the Crew

"We're fishermen, an' we've shipped together for a cruise" [002-029] — this simple declaration by Disko encapsulates the crew's bond. A man's worth was measured by his work: his ability to split cod, handle a dory in heavy seas, and stand his watch.

## Watches and Rhythm

Life aboard followed the iron rhythm of the watch system. In calm weather, the two boys shared a special duty: "Boys clean up after dressin' down an' first watch in ca'am weather is boy's watch on the *We're Here*" [002-169]. After the men turned in, Dan and Harvey swabbed the pens and kept lookout — a responsibility that marked their transition from passengers to working members of the crew.

Discipline was neither gentle nor cruel, but practical. Tom Platt, "experienced in discipline and etiquette" [006-018], knew exactly when strictness was required and when to let things pass. The goal was not obedience for its own sake, but a crew that could function without hesitation when a gale struck or a man went overboard.

## Multicultural Character

The crew was a microcosm of the North Atlantic fishery. Portuguese, Irish ("Galway men"), Nova Scotians, and Yankees worked, ate, and sang together in the cramped fo'c'sle. Their diverse backgrounds surfaced in songs — Manuel's Portuguese *machete* tunes, Long Jack's Irish ballads, the cook's Gaelic chant — creating the unique cultural texture of the Banks fishery.
