# Sea Shanties

Sea shanties, or sailors' work songs, are a living tradition among the fishermen of the Grand Banks fleet. Aboard the Gloucester schooners, singing serves both as entertainment during long watches and as a rhythm for the coordinated labor of hauling lines and dressing down the catch.

## Singing Aboard the We're Here

Music is a constant presence aboard the *We're Here*. Tom Platt, the former man-o'-war sailor, is known to "tune up" while at his work — Dan Troop tells Harvey to listen for him from across the water as the dories string out along the fishing grounds [002-036]. The crew passes time during the monotonous work of dressing down with song, and Harvey himself, still groggy from his near-drowning, hears the schooner's rigging "singing its own tune" as the wind passes through every square inch of the vessel [004-001].

## The Fishermen's Repertoire

The songs are passed down through oral tradition. One shanty, mocking the unreliable anchor of the *Carrie Pitman*, becomes a fleet-wide joke: the men aboard another vessel are heard singing "The Carrie Pitman's anchor doesn't hold her for a week" [008-023]. Tom Platt, drawing on his Navy background aboard the old *Ohio*, contributes a different musical tradition to the mix. In the still of night, a crewman can be heard "singing something that sounded like a hymn" across the star-powdered black water [008-043].

## Cultural Significance

The shanties are more than entertainment — they bind the crew together across differences of nationality and background. Portuguese Manuel, Nova Scotian Long Jack, Yankee Disko Troop, and the others share in the common language of song while working side by side. The tradition is unselfconscious and practical: a shanty marks the rhythm of work, fills the silence between fish, and keeps the men awake on long watches.
