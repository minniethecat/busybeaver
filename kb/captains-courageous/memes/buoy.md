# Buoy

A **buoy** is a floating marker used at sea to indicate a position underwater. In _Captains Courageous_, the Banks fishermen rely on buoys to mark their trawl lines and anchor cables — without them, gear set on the ocean floor would be nearly impossible to find again, especially in the frequent fog of the Grand Banks.

## Trawl Buoys

When the dories set out their trawl lines, they attach small painted buoys to mark the ends. These "small, painted trawl-buoys" are carried aboard each dory and "hove overboard" when the trawl is set (003-160). Each buoy has a distinctive paint pattern so that each dory crew can identify its own gear among the many lines set by the fleet.

The buoy carries a small flag to make it more visible. Tom Platt points out the "tiny black buoy-flag on the shoulder of a mile-wide swell" (004-073), showing how small and hard to spot these markers are in the vast ocean. The crew must keep "within sight of their own trawl-buoys" to avoid losing their gear (004-101), and losing sight of a buoy in the fog is a constant anxiety. Dan, watching a buoy, says to "watch piece an' see how aour trawl-buoy lays" — the angle and movement of the buoy tells an experienced fisherman how the tide and current are affecting the trawl (004-072).

## Buoying the Cable

Buoys serve another purpose when the schooner needs to mark its anchor position. When the _We're Here_ needs to move quickly, Disko sends "Dan and Manuel out to buoy the _We're Here's_ cable" so the schooner can slip her anchor and return to the same position later (006-050). After the Virgin fleet memorial service, the crew "buoyed the cable — all around the Virgin" to mark the anchor's location (008-008).

## Mooring Buoys

When returning to port, the schooner picks up a mooring buoy. The _We're Here_ "slowed to pick up her mooring-buoy" at Gloucester (010-039), a routine procedure at the end of a fishing trip. The mooring buoy is a permanent fixture in the harbor, marking the schooner's designated berth.

## Buoys in Navigation

Beyond marking fishing gear, buoys appear in the broader seascape. On the approach to Gloucester, "each spar and buoy on the water" is picked out by the light "in blinding phosphorescence" (008-110), a vivid image of the nighttime harbor.
