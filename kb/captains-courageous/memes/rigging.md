# Rigging

The rigging of a Grand Banks fishing schooner is its working nervous system — a web of standing rigging (the fixed lines that support the masts) and running rigging (the movable lines that raise, lower, and trim the sails). On the *We're Here*, the rigging is both workplace and pathway, the vertical geography through which the crew moves as they reef sails, set the riding-sail, and keep the vessel sailing through all weather.

## Standing Rigging

The standing rigging consists of the shrouds and stays — heavy wire or hemp ropes that run from the masts to the sides and ends of the hull, holding the masts upright against the tremendous forces exerted by the wind on the sails. The fore-rigging supports the forward mast; the main rigging supports the mainmast, the vessel's tallest and most heavily loaded spar. These lines hum and sing in a gale, the sound of the wind "hooting through her rigging" as familiar to the Banks fisherman as the cry of gulls. — (004-070)

Climbing the rigging is a test of nerve and skill. Harvey watches with envy as the experienced crew "rather climbed than ran up the main rigging," moving with the practiced ease of men who have spent their lives aloft. For a boy raised on hotel elevators and steamer decks, the vertical world of the rigging is as foreign as the surface of the moon — and as essential to master. — (004-073)

## The Shadow of the Masts

The rigging is not just functional equipment but an atmospheric presence. "The shadow of the masts and rigging, with the never-furled riding-sail," falls across the deck day and night, a constant reminder of the schooner's identity as a sailing vessel. At anchor on the Banks, the rigging's silhouette against the sky or the fog is one of the novel's recurring visual images, grounding the narrative in the physical reality of the ship. — (002-145)

When the rigging drips with condensation — "clammy drops" falling from every line — it signals the pervasive damp of the Banks fog, which soaks men and gear alike. The drips are a metronome of misery for the green hand, marking the slow passage of a wet watch on deck. — (003-087)

## The Basket in the Rigging

A detail that captures the schooner's domestic side: a basket of provisions is sometimes "run up in the fore-rigging" to keep food out of reach of the ship's cat or to protect it from spray. This small domestic detail — groceries hoisted aloft among the shrouds and halyards — illustrates how the rigging serves not only the ship but the daily life aboard it. — (003-044)
