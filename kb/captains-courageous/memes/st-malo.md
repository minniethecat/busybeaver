# St. Malo

St. Malo is the Breton port city from which many French Grand Banks fishing vessels sailed. In *Captains Courageous*, the French schooners encountered on the Banks hail from St. Malo, and the city's name recurs in the banter and negotiations between the Gloucester and French fishermen.

## The Miquelon Boats

When a French vessel approaches the *We're Here*, the identification is immediate and precise. Uncle Salters declares: "Frenchman. Miquelon boat from St. Malo" [005-046]. Tom Platt follows up with his own rough French, calling out: "Hi! *Backez vous — backez vous! Standez awayez*, you butt-ended mucho-bono! Where you from — St. Malo, eh?" [005-048]. The French crew respond with characteristic warmth, shouting "Ah, ha! *Mucho bono! Oui! oui! Clos Poulet — St. Malo! St. Pierre et Miquelon!*" [005-048].

This exchange captures St. Malo's identity as the mother port of the French North Atlantic fishery. The Miquelon boats were crewed by men from St. Malo and the surrounding Breton coast, who sailed each spring to the Grand Banks via the French islands of St. Pierre and Miquelon.

## Men Without Friends

The human cost of the fishery is underscored when a French deckhand from St. Malo dies at sea after a wave breaks his back over the bowsprit. His kit is auctioned off to the fleet because "he had no friends at St. Malo or Miquelon" [008-046]. The absence of anyone to claim his belongings speaks to the anonymity and isolation of the transatlantic fishery, where men from distant ports labored and sometimes died far from home.

The incident provokes mixed reactions. A Gloucesterman declares: "when it comes to a dead St. Malo deckhand scarin' a couple o' pore boys stiff fer the sake of a thirty-cent knife, why, then, the cook can take hold fer all o' me. I mistrust furriners, livin' or dead" [008-055], illustrating the cultural divides that separated the French and American fishing communities.

## Maritime Heritage

St. Malo was, in the 1890s, one of France's premier fishing ports, with a centuries-old tradition of sending vessels to the Grand Banks. The Breton and Norman fishermen who sailed from St. Malo were among the earliest European exploiters of the Newfoundland fishery, dating back to the sixteenth century. Kipling's inclusion of the St. Malo fleet reflects the genuinely international character of the Banks, where French, Portuguese, Canadian, and American vessels worked side by side.
