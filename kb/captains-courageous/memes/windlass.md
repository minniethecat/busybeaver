# Windlass

Every morning on the *We're Here* begins with the windlass. The creak of its barrel, the grunt of men heaving on the handspikes, the rattle of the anchor chain through the hawsehole — these sounds mark the start of the fishing day before breakfast, before daylight, before anything else (003-077).

The windlass is the ship's muscle. It lifts the anchor that holds the [[schooner]] to the [[grand-banks|Grand Banks]] sea floor, and in doing so it demands coordinated effort from the [[crew]] — a physical reminder that on a fishing vessel, no one works alone.

## 定义

A windlass is a horizontal winch mounted on the foredeck of a vessel. It consists of a barrel or drum around which the anchor cable is wound, rotated by handspikes — wooden bars inserted into sockets — and controlled by braking mechanisms that regulate the rate of descent (003-098).

The entire tackle forms an integrated system: windlass, chain cables, hemp cables, chain-locker underneath the deck, and hawsehole through which the chain passes down to the anchor below. As much a part of life aboard a Banks fishing schooner as the galley stove, the windlass and its associated gear occupy the forward section of the vessel, just behind the bow (003-098).

The "Spanish windlass" is a distinct device — an improvised lever-and-rope tightener that [[penn|Penn]] proudly demonstrates to Harvey, sharing the mechanical principle but adapted for portable use (003-054).

## 具体细节

### Morning Ritual

The windlass defines the ship's morning. [[uncle-salters|Uncle Salters]] slips the windlass brakes into their sockets, and the men heave on the handspikes "with a grunt and a will" (003-077). The distinctive sound — creak, grunt, squeal — is one of the first auditory markers of the shipboard day, signaling that the crew has begun their labor before the galley fire is lit.

The work requires timing and coordination. Several men work the handspikes simultaneously, the anchor chain feeding through the hawsehole onto the deck forward of the windlass. One man out of rhythm throws the whole operation off; the windlass teaches teamwork whether the crew likes it or not.

### Physical Labor and Hierarchy

Operating the windlass is demanding physical labor. Uncle Salters is responsible for engaging the brakes — a role befitting his experience — while the younger crew members heave on the handspikes. [[disko-troop|Disko Troop]] later complains that an injury makes the crew "short-handed" for windlass work, underscoring how essential the mechanism is to daily operations (007-046).

This is not delicate work. It is back-straining, arm-burning labor performed in the cold North Atlantic dawn, and it sorts men by strength and willingness. The windlass is a democratic but unforgiving master: it does not care who you were ashore, only whether you can heave.

### Harvey's Changing Relationship

The windlass tracks Harvey's transformation. In his early, miserable days aboard the schooner, the windlass is part of his suffering: "The windlass creaked, and the miserable procession took up its labor once more" (002-177). He is an outsider to its rhythm, a boy who has never heaved on anything.

Later, Harvey leans against the windlass to watch for passing vessels — it has become a familiar vantage point, a place to rest between tasks (005-012). The windlass that once tormented him is now just another part of the ship, as ordinary as the galley stove or the deck planks. The change is small but telling: Harvey has stopped being a passenger and become part of the ship's working life.
