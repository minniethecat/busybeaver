# Madeira

Madeira is a Portuguese archipelago located off the northwest coast of Africa, whose emigrant sons formed a significant portion of the Grand Banks fishing fleet's labor force. In *Captains Courageous*, Manuel — the gentle, devout Portuguese crewman of the *We're Here* — is a Madeira man, and his memories of the island provide the novel with some of its most vivid and tender imagery.

## Manuel's Homeland

Manuel declares his identity with pride and precision: "I am a Madeira man. I am not a Porto Pico boy" [004-092]. This distinction matters to him — Madeira, with its Portuguese culture and Catholic traditions, is separate in his mind from Puerto Rico and the Spanish Caribbean. His faith is woven into his identity: "I always give candles — two, three more when I come to Gloucester. The good Virgin she never forgets me, Manuel" [004-092].

The most beautiful passage associated with Madeira comes during the crew's off-watch storytelling. Manuel's talk was "slow and gentle — all about pretty girls in Madeira washing clothes in the dry beds of streams, by moonlight, under waving bananas; legends of saints, and tales of queer dances or fights away in the cold Newfoundland baiting-ports" [005-037]. This juxtaposition of warm Madeiran nights with the harsh Newfoundland fishery captures the emotional geography of the immigrant fisherman, whose heart remains in a sunlit homeland while his body labors in cold northern waters.

## The Portuguese in the Fishery

Portuguese fishermen from Madeira and the Azores were a vital part of the New England fishing fleet in the late nineteenth century. They were known for their seamanship, their Catholic devotion, and their willingness to work the dangerous dory fishery. Manuel embodies these qualities — he is gentle in manner but tough at sea, devout without being sanctimonious, and fiercely loyal to his shipmates.

The memorial tablet in the Gloucester church includes: "Pedro, supposed Madeira, single" [010-131], listed among the lost fishermen of the *Florrie Anderson*. The uncertainty of "supposed Madeira" reflects the anonymity in which many Portuguese fishermen lived and died — men whose origins were often known only approximately by their shipmates, but whose labor and lives sustained the fishery.
