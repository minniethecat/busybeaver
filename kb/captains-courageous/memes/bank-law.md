# Bank Law

Bank law is the unwritten code of conduct observed by fishing vessels and dorymen on the Grand Banks of Newfoundland. Though not codified by any government, these regulations carry the force of long tradition and are enforced by the mutual expectations of the fishing fleet. They govern equipment use, territorial rights, and the conduct of individual fishermen to ensure both fairness and safety in a competitive and dangerous profession.

## Definition and Origins

Bank law developed organically among the Gloucester fishing fleet and the broader community of North Atlantic cod fishermen. It is not legislated by statute but derives its authority from generations of practice on the Banks, where vessels from New England, Newfoundland, Nova Scotia, and Europe converge during the fishing season. The law's primary concerns are the conservation of fish stocks through gear restrictions and the prevention of conflict between dories working in close proximity. — (008-016)

## The One-Hook Rule

The most frequently cited provision of Bank law is the prohibition on multiple hooks per line when fishing the inshore grounds:

> Bank law strictly forbids more than one hook on one line when the dories are on the Virgin or the Eastern Shoals.
> — (008-016)

This rule serves a practical purpose: on the rich fishing grounds of the Virgin and Eastern Shoals, dories often work so close together that their lines risk tangling. A single hook reduces the chance of snarled gear between neighboring boats. When the cod are biting steadily — swimming below in visible droves — fishermen who respect the one-hook limit can work side by side without conflict, even when hailing from different vessels and different nations.

The provision is limited to specific fishing grounds (the Virgin and the Eastern Shoals); not all areas of the Banks carry the same restriction, reflecting the localized nature of Bank law's application.

## Enforcement and Violations

Bank law has no formal enforcement mechanism — no fisheries officers patrol the Grand Banks. Instead, compliance depends on the shared culture of the fishing fleet. Violations bring social consequences rather than legal penalties: a fisherman who ignores the one-hook rule risks snarling his neighbor's gear and provoking conflict. The text notes that even on ground where the rule applies, men sometimes break it — "so close lay the boats that even single hooks snarled" — and arguments between fishermen of different nationalities ("a gentle, hairy Newfoundlander on one side and a howling Portuguese on the other") are part of the Banks experience. — (008-016)
