# Dory Roding

The thin, strong anchor line coiled in the bow of every fishing dory, typically seventy fathoms (420 feet) of brown rope just thick enough to hold a small anchor against the pull of wind and current on the Grand Banks. Without dory-roding, a fisherman cannot anchor — and without an anchor, he cannot fish productively (003-014).

## 定义

Dory-roding is purpose-made line for the Grand Banks dory fishery: thinner and lighter than the schooner's own anchor cable, designed to be handled by one man in a small boat. Each dory carries approximately seventy fathoms coiled in the bow, attached to a small anchor weighing perhaps fifteen to twenty pounds (003-014). The length allows the dory to anchor in water up to sixty fathoms deep with enough scope to hold — essential on the Banks, where the best cod ground is often in deeper water.

The roding must be kept clean and tangle-free. A kinked or fouled roding means the anchor cannot be set or retrieved properly, and a dory that cannot anchor is at the mercy of wind and current. Dan teaches Harvey to keep the roding "straight up an' down" for a clean set (003-036).

## 具体细节

### Equipment Standard

A properly equipped dory carries its roding in the bow, alongside two jugs of fresh water, a tin dinner-horn, a maul, a short gaff, and a wooden killing stick (003-014). The roding is as essential as any of these — a dory without roding is incomplete, and a fisherman who neglects his roding is considered careless.

### Penn's Kelleg

Penn, the gentle damaged man, cannot manage a proper anchor and roding. Instead, he uses a large stone — a "kelleg" — tied to his line, which the entire fleet recognizes from afar (003-036). The kelleg is a mark of shame: a visible signal that Penn cannot handle the basic equipment of his trade. Dan explains that Penn "couldn't stand that no more'n a dog with a dipper to his tail" — the mockery of the fleet would be unbearable (003-036).

### Setting the Anchor

Setting a dory anchor on the Banks requires skill. Dan shows Harvey the technique: bending low, twitching the roding once or twice to feel the anchor bite into the bottom, and knowing by the tension when it holds (003-055). A man who jerks the line or over-hauls risks fouling the anchor or tearing it loose from the bottom. The phrase "keep your rodin' straight up an' down" encodes the essential technique — the anchor must descend vertically, not drag across the bottom at an angle that prevents a clean set (003-051).

### The Whale-Hole: When Roding Fails

Not all bottom on the Banks is within reach. One day Harvey is out with Manuel on what should be forty-fathom ground, but "the whole length of the roding ran out, and still the anchor found nothing." Harvey grows "mortally afraid, for that his last touch with earth was lost" (005-009). Manuel identifies the spot as a "whale-hole" — a deep depression in the Bank floor, well known to experienced fishermen, where no anchor can reach the bottom. The moment marks a boundary of human knowledge: the Banks are mapped not just in latitude and longitude but in the reach of a dory's roding.

### Roding Etiquette: Crime and Punishment

The roding carries a code of conduct on the Banks. To cut another fisherman's roding is "crime unspeakable" — worse than tangling lines, for it sets a dory adrift and strips it of the ability to work (008-017). During the whale-hunt toward the end of the season, "three or four times that day" some unknown hand cuts rodings, leaving boats drifting and their crews enraged. The confusion of "dory-rodings below water" becomes "worse than any tangle of fishing-lines" (008-017).

Long Jack enforces the unwritten law with direct action: when a reckless boat endangers others for bravado, he rows up behind them and "quietly cut their roding" — a public shaming that says the violator has forfeited the right to anchor (008-024).

- **Penn's kelleg** (Chapter 3): Penn's stone anchor — a kelleg — is visible from far off, and every dory on the Banks knows what it means (003-036).

- **The whale-hole** (Chapter 5): Harvey's roding runs out to its full seventy fathoms without finding bottom, leaving him terrified that his last connection to earth is lost (005-009).

- **Roding wars** (Chapter 8): During the whale-hunt, rodings are fouled, cut, and tangled — "crime unspeakable" committed three or four times in a single day (008-017, 008-024).
