# Nova Scotia

Nova Scotia, the peninsular Canadian province jutting into the North Atlantic, supplies many of the fishermen who work alongside the Gloucestermen on the Grand Banks in _Captains Courageous_. Its people appear throughout the novel — in the crew's banter, at the Memorial Day ceremony, and in the ethnic patchwork of the fishing fleet.

## 地理位置

Nova Scotia forms the southeastern extremity of Canada's Maritime Provinces, connected to New Brunswick by the narrow Isthmus of Chignecto and surrounded on three sides by the Atlantic Ocean. Its coastline — deeply fretted with bays, inlets, and harbors — faces the Grand Banks to the southeast and the Gulf of St. Lawrence to the north. [[cape-breton|Cape Breton Island]], forming the province's northeastern lobe, is a distinct region within Nova Scotia, known for its Scottish Gaelic heritage and rugged highlands.

In the 1890s, Nova Scotia's economy was dominated by fishing, shipbuilding, and coastal trade. Its ports — [[halifax|Halifax]], Lunenburg, Yarmouth — were among the busiest in British North America, and its schooners were famous throughout the Atlantic world.

## 在书中的作用

### The Nova Scotian Fishermen

Nova Scotians form a distinct group within the Grand Banks fleet. They are "clear-eyed" (010-081), hardy men from the Maritime Provinces who work alongside the "blue-jowled Portuguese," French, Italians, Swedes, and Danes that make up the cosmopolitan crews of the fishing schooners. The ethnic rivalry between Gloucestermen and Nova Scotians is a fact of fleet life: as one character notes, "To call a Gloucester man a Nova Scotian is not well received" (006-059).

This rivalry is rooted in competition for the same fishing grounds, cultural differences between American and British North American seamen, and the Gloucesterman's fierce pride in his home port. Yet the two groups work together daily, share the same dangers, and stand together at the Memorial Day ceremony honoring those lost at sea.

### Memorial Day

At the Gloucester Memorial Day service in Chapter 10, Nova Scotians are prominent among the crowd — "clear-eyed Nova Scotians, and men of the Maritime Provinces" (010-081) who have come to honor their dead. The presence of Canadian fishermen at this quintessentially Gloucester ceremony underscores the shared culture of the North Atlantic fishery, transcending national boundaries.

The women of the fishing communities — "everywhere women in black, who saluted one another with gloomy pride" (010-081) — include Nova Scotian widows and mothers, their grief indistinguishable from that of the Gloucester women beside them.

## 航海/渔业特征

Nova Scotia's fishery in the 1890s was among the richest in the world. Its banks — including the Grand Banks, Banquereau, and Sable Island Bank — teemed with cod, haddock, and halibut. Nova Scotian schooners, like their Gloucester counterparts, were built for speed and seaworthiness, racing to the grounds and back to market with their perishable cargoes.

The province's fishing culture shared much with New England's: the same dependence on wind and tide, the same staggering loss of life, the same fierce independence of the men who chose this work. The differences were those of accent, nationality, and the particular pride of port — differences that mattered a great deal to the fishermen themselves but vanish in Kipling's larger portrait of a shared maritime civilization.
