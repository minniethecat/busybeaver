# Boston

The capital of Massachusetts and the economic heart of the New England fishing industry. [[Boston]] is both a distant shore for the fishermen of the [[grand-banks|Grand Banks]] and the destination that draws [[harvey-cheyne|Harvey Cheyne's]] father in a transcontinental race against time.

The city's name echoes through the novel as a landmark, a point of reference, a market for salt cod, and ultimately the reunion point where a father who has conquered railroads and shipping meets a son remade by the sea.

## 地理位置

Boston lies on Massachusetts Bay, at the mouth of the Charles River. In the 1890s, it was the principal deep-water port of New England, the terminus of multiple railroad lines, and the center of the North Atlantic cod trade. The "Boston Light" (006-036) marked the entrance to the harbor for returning vessels — fourteen days' sailing from the Grand Banks under favorable conditions.

South Boston was home to a community of Galway immigrants who made up part of the fishing fleet's labor force: "a Galway man inhabitin' South Boston, where they all live mostly" (002-064). The "Union Depot in Boston" (002-011) served as the railroad nexus connecting the city to Albany, Chicago, and the West — the route Harvey Cheyne Sr. would take in reverse.

## 在书中的作用

### Harvey's Destination (Chapters 1–3)

From the moment Harvey is rescued, Boston — or more precisely, a return to civilization via Boston — is his single-minded desire. He repeatedly demands to be taken to "New York" first (001-055, 001-066), but the fishermen make clear that Boston is the nearest port: "We don't use Noo York any. Ner Boston. We may see Eastern Point about September" (001-074).

Harvey's struggle to accept that Boston — and his father — are weeks away is the first lesson in the fisherman's reality: the schooner will not return until her hold is full, and no amount of money or threats will change that.

### The Cod Market

Boston's commission houses are the economic endpoint of the Grand Banks fishery. "Around the c' mission houses to Boston lookin' fer the Lord to make a good trip up" (006-024) — this is where salt cod is sold, prices are set, and the fortune of a season is determined. The schooner's entire voyage is measured by what her hold will fetch in the Boston market.

### The Father's Race (Chapter 9)

In the novel's most breathless sequence, Harvey Cheyne Sr. races from San Diego to Boston by private railcar. "Private car — straight through — Boston. Fix the connections" (009-019). The sequence of telegraphs, railroad junctions, and the desperate push through Chicago — "Boston and Albany, Barnes, same instant" (009-030) — makes Boston the finish line. Every mile of rail laid by Cheyne's empire now serves to carry him to a son he thought was dead.

> "But it's Boston, Father. Tell them to hurry."

## 航海/渔业特征

Boston in the 1890s was the commercial center of New England's cod fishery, though [[gloucester|Gloucester]] was the primary fishing port. The distinction mattered: Gloucester men sailed from Gloucester and sold in Boston. The "Sou' Boston clam-digger" (002-099) was a figure of mild contempt among deep-sea fishermen — a man who worked the mudflats rather than the Banks.

The Boston-to-Gloucester connection was by rail, and in the novel's final chapters, the Cheyne family's private car would have reached Boston first, then continued to Gloucester for the final reunion.
