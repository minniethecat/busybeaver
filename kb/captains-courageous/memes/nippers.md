# Nippers

Thick woolen rings slipped over the palms and thumbs, the fisherman's only protection against a running cod-line. When a halibut or large cod strikes the baited hook and runs, the line "flashes through the hand, stinging even through the nippers" — without them, a fisherman's hands would be shredded to the bone within the first hour of work (003-036).

## 定义

Nippers are simple but essential pieces of personal fishing gear: rings or mitts of thick wool, worn over the palms, that allow a fisherman to grip and control a hand-line without being cut or burned by the friction of the line running through his hands. Cod-lines are heavy and rough, and a running fish can pull line at tremendous speed — the nippers provide just enough insulation to prevent injury while still allowing the sensitivity needed to feel a bite (003-036).

Every dory fisherman owns his own nippers and maintains them carefully. Wet wool loses its protective qualities, so nippers are dried between uses and replaced when they wear thin. A fisherman without nippers is effectively unable to work — the alternative is raw, bleeding hands that cannot grip a line.

## 具体细节

### Harvey's First Experience

When Harvey hooks his halibut — his first real fish — the line "flashed through his hand, stinging even through the nippers" (003-036). The sensation is new and frightening: the wool, meant to protect, can only do so much against a fish that takes charge of both boy and dory for twenty minutes. His knuckles end up "raw and bleeding where they had been banged against the gunwale"; his face is "purple-blue between excitement and exertion" and he is "half-blinded from staring at the circling sunlit ripples about the swiftly moving line" (003-040).

### The Cost of Being Green

Harvey's hands tell the story of his transformation in the first days aboard. Within hours of his first work on deck, his "hands were beginning to blister" — the soft palms of a boy who has never done manual labor meeting the reality of wet rope and rough gear (003-015). The nippers, worn over those blisters, become both protection and torment: they prevent the line from cutting to the bone but cannot stop the pain of raw skin pressing against wool under strain.

### Dory Work vs. Deck Work

The physical demands differ sharply between dory and schooner. In a dory, "the weight of a cod is waterborne till the last minute, and you are, so to speak, abreast of him." But on the schooner's deck, the few feet of freeboard create "so much extra dead-hauling, and stooping over the bulwarks cramps the stomach" (003-141). The nippers serve in both environments, but the strain on the hands is markedly different: the dory fisherman's hands work close to the water in a rhythmic dance; the deck hand hauls dead weight from below, the line sawing against wool with each pull.

### The Measure of a Man's Hands

A fisherman's hands carry his reputation. At season's end, when Harvey meets his parents in Gloucester, he spreads out his hands and ruefully rubs the palms together: "They're all soft again" (010-013). The calluses that marked him as a working fisherman are already fading after days ashore. The nippers, put away in his sea-chest, are the artifacts of a physical transformation that was visible on his skin — and the fading of those marks signals his return to the world of soft hands and private railroad cars.
