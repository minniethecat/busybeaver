# Fishing Life

Fishing life aboard a Gloucester schooner on the Grand Banks is a world apart from the comforts of shore. The daily routine is governed by the rhythms of the sea, the movements of the cod, and the relentless demands of the fishing gear. For the eight men and boys of the *We're Here*, life is a cycle of baiting, hauling, dressing down, eating, and sleeping — each element stripped to its essentials.

## The Daily Routine

A fisherman's day begins before dawn and ends after dark. The men rise at four in the dim morning, pulling on crackling oilskins in the chill of the forecastle [002-041]. The first task is to bait the trawl lines — hundreds of hooks that must be carefully prepared before the dories are launched. Once the dories are away, the men fish through the day, returning to the schooner to dress down the catch. The work continues until the last fish is cleaned and salted, often late into the night.

## Communal Living

Life aboard the *We're Here* is intimate and crowded. The forecastle is "packed as full of smells as a bale is of cotton" — a mixture of oilskins, fried fish, burnt grease, paint, pepper, and stale tobacco, all "hooped together by one encircling smell of ship and salt water" [002-002]. The men sleep in narrow bunks with no sheets, on "dingy ticking full of lumps and nubbles" [002-002]. Eight men share this small space for months at sea, developing the rough camaraderie and mutual tolerance that survival on the Banks demands.

## The Work

The physical demands of fishing life are unrelenting. From the moment the anchor is raised until the last cod is split and salted, every man works at the limit of his strength. The dories — small, open boats launched from the schooner — put the fishermen directly on the water, exposed to fog, gales, and the ever-present danger of being run down by steamers. Aboard the schooner, the work of dressing down is a rhythmic, mechanical process: Manuel's body moves "like a statue" while his long arms grab fish without ceasing [003-003], and even the weakest crew member, Little Penn, "toiled valiantly" though it was easy to see his frailty [003-003].

## The Reward

For all its hardship, fishing life offers its own satisfactions. The independence of the fisherman — beholden to no employer, sharing in the vessel's catch — is a point of fierce pride. Disko Troop's refusal of charity, his insistence on self-sufficiency, reflects the ethos of the Gloucester fleet. The men take satisfaction in their skill, their knowledge of the sea, and the simple fact of survival in a calling that claims lives every season.
