# Mr. Cheyne

Harvey Cheyne Sr. is a millionaire railroad magnate whose cross-continent dash to rescue his son provides the dramatic climax of _Captains Courageous_. Though present only in Chapters 9 and 10, his character arc — from detached businessman to grateful father — reflects the novel's themes of class, redemption, and the transformative power of the sea.

## 身份与背景

Mr. Cheyne is a self-made railroad tycoon who built his fortune through relentless ambition. He owns a vast business empire spanning freight lines, tea-ships, and mining interests across the American West [009-001]. His wealth allows him to command private rail cars and telegraph networks at will. When he learns his son Harvey — presumed drowned — is alive on a fishing schooner, Cheyne marshals his entire corporate apparatus to race from San Diego to Gloucester [009-026].

## 外貌与性格

Cheyne is described through his actions rather than physical detail — a man of decisive command who "smiled grimly at the consternation of his enemies" when he abruptly diverted business priorities [009-040]. He speaks with the authority of a man accustomed to obedience: "Fix the connections," he shouts down staircases [009-019]. Yet beneath the imperious surface, the text reveals a father capable of profound emotional reckoning.

## 主要经历

### The Race to Gloucester

Upon receiving the telegram that Harvey is alive, Cheyne immediately abandons a critical business negotiation in San Diego and mobilizes his private rail car, the [[Constance Cheyne|Constance]], for a transcontinental journey [009-024, 009-026]. He cares nothing for the commercial disruption: "Harvey Cheyne's in a hurry — a hurry — a hurry" [009-042].

> "Harvey Cheyne is in a hurry, a hurry, a hurry" — the telegraph operators' dispatch that cleared the rails ahead of the Constance private car [009-042]

### Arrival at Gloucester

Cheyne arrives in Gloucester for the Memorial Day fishermen's memorial, where he is reunited with Harvey. His conversation with Disko Troop reveals his transformation — from suspicion of the fishermen to genuine respect [009-131]. The emotional weight of the reunion is expressed in Cheyne's actions rather than words: he offers Dan Troop a position in his fleet without hesitation [009-190].

> "I don't mind ownin' to you, Mr. Cheyne, as I mistrusted the boy to begin with," Disko admits, and Cheyne responds not with indignation but with understanding [009-131]

### The Gloucester Memorial Day

Cheyne witnesses the fishermen's memorial ceremony on his fiftieth birthday. The experience moves him deeply: "the old city did herself credit," he reflects [010-085]. He recognizes the value of the community that saved his son, pledging ongoing support for the fishermen's widows and orphans.

## 作用与意义

Mr. Cheyne serves as the novel's bridge between two worlds — the Gilded Age capitalism of the railroad barons and the traditional maritime community of Gloucester. His journey from San Diego to Gloucester is not merely geographical but spiritual: a man who measures everything in dollars learns that his son's life was saved by values his wealth cannot purchase. Kipling uses Cheyne to critique the excesses of American capitalism while affirming that redemption is possible through human connection.
