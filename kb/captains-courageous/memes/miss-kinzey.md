# Miss Kinzey

Miss Kinzey is the stenographer in [[mr-cheyne|Harvey Cheyne Sr.'s]] office who plays a key supporting role during the frantic Chapter 9 sequence, when Cheyne marshals his business empire to race across America and reclaim his son [[harvey-cheyne|Harvey]] from [[gloucester|Gloucester]].

## 身份与背景

Miss Kinzey is "from Milwaukee, somewhat direct of speech" (009-018). She works as a stenographer — or "typewriter," in the parlance of the 1890s — in Cheyne's San Diego office, managing the flood of telegraph messages that pour in during the crisis. Her role is clerical but essential: she is the human interface between Cheyne's dictation and the continent-spanning network of railroad and telegraph that he commands.

Her name is spelled inconsistently in the text — "Kinzey" in most passages but "Kinsey" at 009-040 — reflecting the casual attitude toward such details in an era when secretaries were often invisible to the men they served. But Kipling gives her enough presence to register as a distinct character.

## 外貌与性格

Kipling sketches Miss Kinzey economically: she has "white fingers" that "called up the Continent of America" on the telegraph keys (009-021). She "nodded between clicks" (009-023) — efficient, composed, keeping pace with Cheyne's rapid-fire dictation. When the magnitude of the operation dawns on her, she offers a look of "admiration" (009-031), and later she is found "laughing over the keys" (009-037) at the absurdity of the situation.

Her directness — noted as a Milwaukee trait — surfaces when she suggests, in response to an impossible demand: "Chauncey Depew himself couldn't hitch his car to her" (009-033). She is no mere automaton; she thinks, judges, and on occasion gently pushes back.

## 主要经历

### The Cross-Country Dash

Miss Kinzey is present throughout the Chapter 9 command-center sequence in Cheyne's office. She clicks out telegrams to railroad agents, ship captains, and business associates across the continent, her fingers translating Cheyne's will into electrical impulses that clear tracks and summon vessels.

When the secretary brings word that Harvey is alive in Gloucester, it is Miss Kinzey who types the reply — and who, significantly, adds the sentiment and "the memorable quotation, 'Let us have peace'" (009-041) to the official message. This small interpolation suggests a personality not entirely submerged by her clerical function.

She is also the recipient of the doctor's wry observation, "About the only medical statement in novels with any truth to it is that joy don't kill, Miss Kinzey" (009-017) — a remark that places her within the human circle of the crisis, not outside it.

### Traveling East

Cheyne decides that "you and Miss Kinsey had better come along" (009-040) on the cross-country journey. Though the narrative focus shifts to Cheyne and his son after the reunion, Miss Kinzey's inclusion in the traveling party confirms her status as a trusted member of Cheyne's inner staff.

## 人物关系

- [[mr-cheyne|Mr. Cheyne]]: her employer, whose dictation she transforms into telegrams that move railroads
- [[harvey-cheyne|Harvey Cheyne]]: the boy whose rescue she helps coordinate from thousands of miles away
- [[constance-cheyne|Constance Cheyne]]: Mrs. Cheyne, whose care and transport Miss Kinzey likely helps arrange

## 作用与意义

Miss Kinzey embodies the largely invisible clerical workforce that made Gilded Age capitalism possible. Behind every railroad magnate stood a roomful of stenographers and telegraph operators — mostly women — whose skilled labor turned dictation into action. Kipling gives her just enough dimensionality to acknowledge that reality without sentimentalizing it.

She also serves a narrative function: her reactions — admiration, laughter, direct commentary — guide the reader's response to the extraordinary events unfolding in Cheyne's office. When Miss Kinzey is impressed, the reader understands the scale of what is happening.
