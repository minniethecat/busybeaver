# Kelleg

A stone used in place of a proper iron anchor, recognizable across the water by its bulk riding in the bow of a dory. On the Grand Banks, a kelleg is a mark of shame — a visible confession that the fisherman cannot afford proper gear or, worse, cannot manage it. Penn's kelleg on the *We're Here* makes him the target of the entire fleet's silent judgment (003-036).

## 定义

A kelleg (also spelled "killick" or "kellick") is a stone wrapped with rope or withes to serve as an improvised anchor. In the resource-scarce world of small-boat fishing, the kelleg is a practical solution to a missing or lost anchor — but it is a poor one. A stone does not bite into the sea floor like an iron anchor; it drags with every shift of wind and current, requiring constant adjustment. A dory anchored with a kelleg drifts off station, tangles other boats' lines, and marks its owner as someone who cannot be trusted with proper equipment (003-036).

The term appears in the novel in the context of Penn, the gentle, damaged man who cannot manage the basic skills of a dory fisherman. His kelleg is visible from far off — "you kin see a kelleg ridin' in the bows fur's you can see a dory" — and every fisherman on the Banks knows what it signifies (003-036).

## 具体细节

### Penn's Shame

Penn is a former preacher whose mind was broken by the loss of his family in a flood. He is kind, gentle, and utterly incapable of the physical and mental demands of Grand Banks fishing. His use of a kelleg is not laziness but incapacity — he genuinely cannot manage a proper anchor and roding. Yet the fleet's judgment is merciless: "They'd guy him dreadful. Penn couldn't stand that no more'n a dog with a dipper to his tail" (003-036).

Dan's explanation to Harvey reveals the social code of the Banks: competence is everything, and inability is not excused by good intentions. Penn's kelleg is a constant, visible reminder of his failure to measure up — and the crew of the *We're Here*, led by Disko Troop, protects Penn from the worst of the mockery by keeping him close and covering for his shortcomings.

### The Social Code

The kelleg embodies the Banks' unwritten law: a fisherman is judged by his gear and his ability to use it. Good gear, well maintained, signals competence and reliability. Makeshift gear signals poverty, carelessness, or incompetence — any of which can endanger not just the individual fisherman but the entire fleet. A dory that drags anchor can drift into another boat's lines, foul the gear, or drift away entirely in the fog.
