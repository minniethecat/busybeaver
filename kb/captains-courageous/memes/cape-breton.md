# Cape Breton

Cape Breton Island, at the northeastern extremity of [[nova-scotia|Nova Scotia]], is a rugged maritime region whose fishermen appear among the crews of the Grand Banks fleet in _Captains Courageous_. The island is referenced in crew banter, tall tales, and the novel's geography of Atlantic fishing communities.

## 地理位置

Cape Breton forms the northeastern lobe of Nova Scotia, separated from the mainland by the narrow Strait of Canso. Its rocky coastline, deeply indented by bays and inlets, faces the Gulf of St. Lawrence to the north and the open Atlantic to the east. In the 1890s, it was a region of small fishing villages, subsistence farms, and isolated communities where "the farmers speak homemade Scotch" (002-135) — a legacy of Highland clearances that drove Scottish Gaels to Cape Breton's shores generations earlier.

The island's position makes it a natural waypoint for vessels sailing between the Grand Banks and the St. Lawrence, and its harbors shelter fishing schooners in the fierce North Atlantic winters.

## 在书中的作用

### The Cape Breton Fisherman

Cape Breton enters the narrative through its people. Dan Troop describes a crewman who "comes from the innards of Cape Breton" (002-135), characterizing the island's inhabitants as rustic and Gaelic-speaking — "all huffy-chuffy" in the fishermen's dismissive phrase. The remark reflects the complex ethnic hierarchy of the Banks fleet, where Nova Scotians, Cape Bretoners, Portuguese, and "Americans" from Gloucester each occupied distinct social niches.

### Winter Mail and Dog Trains

In Chapter 5, as the *We're Here's* crew swaps stories during a quiet watch, one sailor tells "of mail-carrying in the winter up Cape Breton way, of the dog-train that goes to Coudray, and of the ram-steamer Arctic, that breaks the ice between the mainland and Prince Edward Island" (005-036). This passage sketches Cape Breton as a place of extreme winters, where dog teams replace boats when the ice closes in — a world as distant from Harvey Cheyne's California as any on earth.

### The Cook and Porter

In Chapter 10, a dispute between "one Cape Breton and two Alabama negroes" (010-001) aboard Cheyne's train requires the millionaire's personal intervention. The Cape Bretoner, "the cook and porter," represents the island's diaspora — men who left the fisheries for service work on the mainland but retained their distinctive regional identity and prickly pride.

## 航海/渔业特征

Cape Breton's waters lie at the northern margin of the great Atlantic fishing grounds. While not a primary fishing port on the scale of [[gloucester|Gloucester]], the island supplied crewmen — particularly of Scottish descent — to the Banks fleet. Its communities practiced inshore fishing for cod, mackerel, and herring, supplemented by small-scale farming. The island's isolation and harsh winters produced seamen known for endurance and a distinctive Gaelic-influenced maritime culture.
