# Virgin Rock

A submerged rock formation on the Grand Banks, known to every Gloucester fisherman as "the Virgin" or "the Old Virgin." Rising to within twenty feet of the surface, the Virgin is at once a navigational reference point, the turning-point of the fishing cruise, a gathering place for the Bank fleet, and — when the swell runs — one of the most dangerous breakers on the Banks.

On Disko Troop's Eldridge chart, the Virgin appears as one tiny dot amid a nest of curiously named shoals (005-041). Yet this dot marks the destination toward which the *We're Here* steers for weeks: the place where, with good luck, she would wet the balance of her salt and complete her voyage (005-041). In the featureless expanse of the open Atlantic, where depth soundings and dead reckoning are the only guides, a known rock like the Virgin is a crucial landmark — a fixed point by which a captain can verify his position (008-004).

## 地理位置

The Virgin Rock is located on the Grand Banks, entirely submerged beneath the greenish Atlantic. Its "cap" — the summit of the rock — rises to within twenty feet of the surface (008-018). In clear water, a fisherman in a dory can look down upon "the very weed of that lonely rock" and watch the cod marching solemnly over its leathery kelp (008-018).

The area surrounding the Virgin is entirely rocky bottom, and any carelessness with ground-tackle means chafed cables and danger from drifting (008-008). For this reason, the fleet always buoys its cables carefully when anchored on the Virgin grounds — a precaution that Disko Troop and every prudent skipper observes without fail (008-008).

The Virgin is not an isolated feature. Kipling places it in the context of the broader geography: the *We're Here* encounters the Virgin after circling slowly from the east northerly towards the west, approaching the shoals where the caplin run and the cod gather in legions (007-001, 008-018).

## 在书中的作用

The Virgin plays multiple roles in the novel, each revealing a different facet of life on the Banks.

**As a turning-point.** For the *We're Here*, reaching the Virgin marks the final phase of the voyage. Harvey understands from the Eldridge chart that the Virgin and the surrounding shoals are "the turning-point of the cruise," where the schooner will wet the balance of her salt and begin the homeward leg (005-041). Dan tells Harvey that the crew needs to have him "dressed in shape fer the Old Virgin" — meaning they must transform him from a soft-handed millionaire's son into a working fisherman before they reach the critical fishing grounds (005-040).

**As a gathering place for the fleet.** At the Virgin, the scattered schooners of the Gloucester fleet converge. Disko points across the sea and says, "There's about a thousand men here; an' yonder's the Virgin," indicating a vacant space of greenish sea where no dories float — the rock itself, too dangerous to fish directly above (008-004). The fleet anchors as precisely as racing yachts, and "the Bank fleet pass good seamanship in silence; but a bungler is jeered all along the line" (008-005).

**As a source of danger.** When the swell underruns the sea, the Virgin becomes a deadly breaker. The dories draw apart to save their sides, and someone always calls out that the Virgin is about to break (008-024). The break itself is a terrifying spectacle:

> The Virgin flung up a couple of acres of foaming water, white, furious, and ghastly over the shoal sea.
> — (008-026)

Tom Platt tells Harvey he has just witnessed "the greatest thing on the Banks" (008-028). The Virgin breaks on a regular rhythm — "once ivry fifteen minutes, to the tick" — and throughout the night it "roared hoarsely" while the fleet rode out the storm (008-028, 008-042).

**As a test of recklessness.** The Virgin reveals character. A reckless Galway man, defying all warnings, rows his dory directly over the rock to prove his nerve (008-024). He hovers within a foot or two of the hidden rock, spinning on his anchor in the ugly, sucking water, while the other boats look on in uneasy silence. Only Long Jack's swift action — quietly cutting the foolhardy man's roding — saves two lives when the Virgin breaks moments later (008-024, 008-026).

**As a backdrop for fleet camaraderie.** When a Baltimore bark blunders into the fleet, the dories indulge in merciless banter, shouting absurd sailing directions and mocking her skipper. They would have warned her had she been in real peril — "but, seeing her well clear of the Virgin, they made the most of their chances" (008-041). The Virgin thus serves as the unspoken boundary between jest and genuine danger.

## 航海/渔业特征

The Virgin grounds present a unique combination of bounty and peril. The rocky bottom provides ideal habitat for cod — they gather there "in legions, marching solemnly over the leathery kelp" (008-018). When the caplin arrive, the fishing above the Virgin's cap becomes spectacular: "Harvey could see the glimmering cod below, swimming slowly in droves, biting as steadily as they swam" (008-016).

Navigation to the Virgin requires exceptional skill. Harvey, looking at the chart, cannot fathom how Disko — even with his old green-crusted quadrant, the hog-yoke, and the deep-sea lead — can find a dot so tiny in the vast Atlantic (005-041). He learns later that Disko is "entirely equal to that and any other business and could even help others" (005-041).

The rocky bottom demands constant vigilance. Dan explains that "all around the Virgin is rocky bottom, and carelessness means chafed ground-tackle and danger from drifting" (008-008). The fleet observes strict discipline: anchoring, buoying cables, and keeping safe distance from the rock itself.

The Virgin's breaking pattern follows a predictable rhythm. In moderate swell, it breaks approximately every half hour; when the swell piles up, the interval shortens to every fifteen minutes "to the tick" (008-027, 008-028). The spectacle is both beautiful and terrifying — Dan bobs "like a young seal at home" while watching it, and the experienced fishermen regard the break as "the greatest thing on the Banks" (008-027, 008-028).

> She'll break about once every ha'af hour now, 'les the swell piles up good.
> — Dan Troop, (008-027)

> Once ivry fifteen minutes, to the tick. Harve, you've seen the greatest thing on the Banks; an' but for Long Jack you'd seen some dead men too.
> — Tom Platt, (008-028)
