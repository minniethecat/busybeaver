# Liverpool

Liverpool, Nova Scotia, is a port town on the Atlantic coast of Canada whose name surfaces in *Captains Courageous* through the memorial records of fishermen lost at sea and in the sea shanties sung aboard the *We're Here*. Unlike Gloucester or Boston, Liverpool is referenced only in passing, but its presence in the narrative underscores the international scope of the Grand Banks fishing community.

## In Sea Shanties

The name Liverpool appears in the shanties that Tom Platt and the crew sing during their off-watch hours. The famous packet ship *Dreadnought*, celebrated in Platt's repertoire, sailed "every mile of the way between Liverpool and New York" [004-038]. While this Liverpool refers to the English port city, the name resonates with the Nova Scotian Liverpool's own maritime heritage — both cities were nodes in the great North Atlantic shipping network that connected the fishing grounds to the wider world.

## The Memorial Tablet

Liverpool, Nova Scotia, appears directly in the Gloucester church's memorial tablet, which records the names of fishermen lost at sea. Among the entries is: "Same schooner, Charlie Ritchie, Liverpool, Nova Scotia, 33, single" [010-131]. Charlie Ritchie's death, recorded alongside those of Gloucester's own sons, testifies to the shared dangers of the fishery and the bonds that transcended national boundaries. The inclusion of a Nova Scotian's name on the Gloucester memorial reflects the close ties between the fishing communities of Massachusetts and the Canadian Maritimes.

## Maritime Nova Scotia

Liverpool, Nova Scotia, was founded in 1759 by New England Planters from Massachusetts, and its maritime culture remained deeply connected to the New England fishery through the nineteenth century. By Kipling's time, Liverpool was an established fishing and shipbuilding port, its vessels working the same Banks as the Gloucester fleet. The town's Privateer heritage — it was known as the "Port of the Privateers" during the War of 1812 — gave it a reputation for hardy and independent seafarers.
