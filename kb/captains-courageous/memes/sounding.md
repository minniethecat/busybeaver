# Sounding

Sounding is the practice of measuring the depth of water beneath a vessel by lowering a weighted line — the lead line — over the side until it strikes bottom. On the Grand Banks, where the seafloor rises from abyssal depths to shallow plateaus, the art of the sounding is both a navigational necessity and a fishing technique: cod gather on specific banks at specific depths, and knowing the depth tells the fisherman where the fish are.

## The Technique

A sounding is taken by lowering a lead weight attached to a marked line. When the weight touches bottom, the depth is read from the markings on the line. On the Banks schooners, this is typically done from a dory, where the fisherman can feel the subtle changes in bottom composition — sand, gravel, shell, rock — by the way the lead strikes and by examining the material that adheres to a hollow in the base of the weight filled with tallow.

The schooner's chantey captures the cultural importance of the act: "When she's off soundings you shortly will know" — the line between being "on soundings" (in known, navigable waters) and "off soundings" (in deep ocean beyond the reach of the lead line) is the line between safety and the unknown. — (004-031)

## Harvey Learns to Sound

Tom Platt teaches Harvey to "fly the blue pigeon" — the fisherman's phrase for taking a sounding — and it nearly ends in disaster: Harvey "nearly slew Penn with it" on his first attempt (005-006). His strength and stomach are not equal to the task: he can only manage "continuous sounding in any sort of a sea" in calm weather, with a seven-pound lead on shoal water. But Disko uses him freely for the light work, because even a green hand's soundings are useful data on the Banks.

The phrase "fly the blue pigeon" reflects the fisherman's habit of giving irreverent names to the tools of his trade — the lead weight, painted blue to show against the water, becomes a "pigeon" when cast over the side and retrieved.

## Sounding and the Bank Floor

Depth measurements on the Grand Banks follow a precise vocabulary. Dan calls "Thirty fathom" when he baits his hook, and the cod-lines are set accordingly (003-026). The schooner positions itself over known ground by cross-referencing soundings with the chart: when Tom Platt sings out "Fifty" and Disko replies "I mistrust we're right over the nick o' Green Bank on old Sixty-Fifty," the two men are triangulating their position from memory of the bottom alone (003-129).

The bottom itself has names as vivid as any surface geography. "Strawberry-bottom" is Dan's term for a particularly desirable patch of ground, the name suggesting its red color or its sweetness as fishing (003-035). "Sandy bottom" is less reliable — anchors drag and must be reset (003-049). And the deep depressions called "whale-holes" lie beyond even a dory's seventy fathoms of roding, where "still the anchor found nothing" and the fisherman loses his last touch with earth (005-009).

## The Lead-Line Story

The lead-line carries its own folklore. In the crew's after-supper storytelling, the tale of Counahan's voyage becomes a comic meditation on the reliability of soundings. Counahan and his crew, lost and drunk, sound and get sixty fathom. When they sound again and find the same depth despite believing they have drifted, Counahan declares: "Either the lead-line's tuk to stretchin' or else the Bank's sunk" (006-026, 006-027). The joke turns on a fisherman's absolute trust in his soundings — if the lead says sixty fathom, then the Bank itself must have moved before the lead could be wrong.

## Beyond the Banks

Continuous sounding in any kind of sea is difficult work. The motion of the dory makes it hard to keep the line vertical, and a skilled fisherman must compensate for the boat's rise and fall with each wave. Harvey, new to the dory, finds that his stomach is "not equal to continuous sounding in any sort of a sea," his seasickness compromising his ability to perform what the experienced men do without thinking. — (005-006)

The sounding is also a communal signal. Manuel's conch horn, when blown, sounds "thin and faint beside them," the sound carrying across the water between the scattered dories. The conch — like the sounding lead — is both a tool and a language, allowing the fleet's widely dispersed fishermen to maintain contact across the miles of empty sea. — (005-009)
