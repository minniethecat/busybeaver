# Topsail

The topsail is a square or fore-and-aft sail set above the main course on the schooner [[were-here|We're Here]]. It is used in moderate winds to gain extra speed and is one of the larger sails requiring coordinated effort from the crew to set and furl.

## Setting and Handling

The topsail requires careful handling. When the wind is light, the topsail flutters indecisively — a sign that the schooner needs more breeze or that the sail should be taken in. By noon, when the wind freshens, the topsail is set to help the schooner run back to port with a full load of fish.

> The mainsail was looped up, and her topsail flapped undecidedly in what little wind was stirring. (008-032)

> By noon the mainsail was down and the main- and topsail were up, and dories came alongside. (008-100)

## Heavy Work

The big topsail is put over by hand — a heavy job even for the experienced Banks fishermen. Dan, though young, has to wait on the big topsail alongside the men. The work of raising and trimming a sail this size is part of the daily rhythm of a fishing schooner making its passage.

> Dan had to wait on the big topsail, which was put over by hand. (008-103)

## The Topsail as Signal

Among the fishing fleet, a schooner backing her topsail sends a signal. The maneuver may mean she is picking up dories, hailing another vessel, or taking position among the fleet. The topsail's position is visible for miles across the open water of the Banks, making it a means of communication in an era before wireless telegraphy.

> The larger boat backed her topsail with shrieks and shoutings. (005-043)

When a schooner approaches another at sea, the crew watches the topsail for the telltale signs of maneuvering. The angle of the topsail communicates intent — whether the vessel is yielding right of way, heaving to, or preparing to come alongside.

## Weather and the Topsail

The topsail is the first sail to flutter in light airs and the last to come down before a blow. Its behavior tells the helmsman everything about the wind. On a clear day, when the anchor comes up and the schooner points homeward, setting the topsail is the signal that the voyage is ending — the schooner is making for Gloucester with her hold full of salted cod.
