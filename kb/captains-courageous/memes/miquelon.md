# Miquelon

Miquelon, the larger island of the French overseas territory of Saint Pierre and Miquelon, lies off the southern coast of Newfoundland. In the world of *Captains Courageous*, French fishing vessels from Miquelon are a common sight on the Grand Banks, and their encounters with the Gloucester fleet provide moments of both commerce and friction.

## French Fishing Presence

The Miquelon fleet is part of the broader French North Atlantic fishery, operating out of St. Malo and other Breton ports. When a French vessel approaches the *We're Here*, Uncle Salters immediately identifies her origin: "Frenchman. Miquelon boat from St. Malo" [005-046]. His recognition reflects the deep familiarity — and occasional rivalry — between the Gloucestermen and the French fleet.

The French crew hail the *We're Here* with characteristic warmth, calling out "Ah, ha! *Mucho bono! Oui! oui! Clos Poulet — St. Malo! St. Pierre et Miquelon!* Bord! Bord!" [005-048]. This multilingual greeting combines French, Spanish, and Portuguese elements, reflecting the polyglot character of the Grand Banks fishery.

## Tensions and Trade

Relations between American and French fishermen carry an edge of historical tension. Disko Troop warns Uncle Salters: "I don't want no more stone-ballast hove at us 'long o' your callin' Miquelon boats 'footy cochins,' same's you did off Le Have" [005-052], recalling a past incident where Salters's insults provoked a hostile response from a French crew.

Despite such tensions, the Miquelon boats serve a practical function in the maritime economy. The schooner *Abishai* is described as having "run in to bait, Miquelon way" [004-092], indicating that the French islands served as a baiting station for vessels working the Banks.

## Later Appearances

A Miquelon skipper reappears later in the narrative at a gathering in Gloucester, complaining about the price of champagne: "In Miquelon champagne's eighteen dollars a case" [010-091]. This offhand remark reinforces the remoteness of the French islands, where imported luxuries commanded steep premiums.

## Maritime Significance

Saint Pierre and Miquelon have been a French possession since 1763, representing the last fragment of France's once-vast North American empire. In the 1890s, their economy was dominated by the cod fishery, and their vessels were a regular presence on the Grand Banks, interacting — sometimes amicably, sometimes contentiously — with the predominantly American and Canadian fleet.
