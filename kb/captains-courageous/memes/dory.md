# Dory

The small wooden boat that carries every fisherman on the [[grand-banks|Grand Banks]] to his daily labor, and the vessel where [[harvey-cheyne|Harvey Cheyne]] first learns what his hands are for.

Stacked nested on the schooner's deck at night, launched at dawn, and rowed back loaded with cod at dusk, the dory is the irreducible unit of the Grand Banks fishery — one or two men in a flat-bottomed shell, alone on the Atlantic, accountable for every fish they bring back.

## 定义

The dory is a lightweight, flat-bottomed wooden boat designed to be stacked nested on the deck of a fishing schooner and launched over the side for daily fishing operations (001-043). Its flat bottom gives it stability when hauling heavy loads, but makes it lively and prone to swaying in rough seas (001-044).

Each dory carries its own complete set of gear: anchor, water jugs, dory-roding (thin anchor line), cod-lines with heavy leads and double hooks coiled on square reels, a tin dinner-horn for signaling, a maul, a short gaff, and a wooden stick for killing fish (003-010).

The dory defines the working rhythm of the Grand Banks: the schooner anchors at a productive ground, and at dawn the dories launch — each with one or two men — scattering across the water to fish independently (001-058).

At dusk, every schooner pulls her dories back "like toys on invisible strings, the little black figures in the tiny boats pulling like clockwork toys" (002-065). The day's catch — and the crew's wages — depend on what each dory brings home.

The dory is also a social marker. A fisherman's competence is visible in how he handles his dory: where he anchors, how clean he keeps her, whether he uses proper gear or a makeshift [[kelleg]] that the whole fleet recognizes and mocks (003-036). The boat is an extension of the man.

## 具体细节

### Construction and Equipment

A typical fishing dory is painted and labeled with a name — the little red dory *Hattie S.* carried by the *We're Here* (003-010). She is kept specklessly clean, a point of professional pride. In her bows lie a tiny anchor, two jugs of water, and approximately seventy fathoms of thin brown dory-roding (003-014).

A tin dinner-horn rests in cleats under the rower's right hand, beside an ugly-looking maul, a short gaff, and a shorter wooden stick for dispatching fish. The cod-lines, with very heavy leads and double hooks, are neatly coiled on square reels and secured by the gunwale (003-014).

The inventory matters because it is complete — the dory must be self-sufficient. A man alone in a dory in the fog cannot return to the schooner for a forgotten item. Everything needed for the day's work, and for survival, must be aboard.

### Daily Operations

The dory's daily cycle is the heartbeat of the schooner's working life. Each morning the boats are launched and rowed to the fishing ground. When a dory returns loaded, the fisherman uses a short-handled fork to throw the catch into the deck pen (002-073).

A hook-and-tackle system — slipped through a loop of rope at the dory's bow and hooked to the stern-becket — hauls the boat up alongside the schooner (002-074). Harvey is astonished to find how easily the dory rises on the tackle, the mechanical advantage doing what his soft hands could not manage alone (002-075).

After the catch is unloaded comes the maintenance ritual: every dory must be swabbed clean of slime, and the removable footboards slid out of their grooves, swabbed, and laid down flat (002-083).

Dan's instruction is terse and absolute: "Never let a footboard jam. Ye may want her bad some day" (002-083). The warning is not rhetorical. A man's dory is his only link to the mother schooner; a jammed footboard could mean being swept overboard with no footing.

### Fishing from a Dory

Fishing from a dory is hand-lining — no rod, no reel, just strong arms and leather [[nippers]] to grip the line at sixty fathoms. The fisherman baits heavy leads with double cod-hooks and heaves them over the side (003-036).

The dory drifts easily with wind and current; anchoring is not worth the trouble until the fisherman is sure of good ground, because "you don't want to be hauling up an anchor and moving every few minutes" (003-036).

The work is physically punishing. Harvey's knuckles are raw and bleeding from banging against the gunwale, his face purple-blue between excitement and exertion. He drips with sweat fighting a halibut that takes charge of both boys and the dory for twenty minutes (003-036).

The dory, so light and manageable in calm water, becomes a hostile platform when a large fish fights back — every lurch and sway threatens to throw the fisherman overboard.

## 书中涉及场景

The dory appears throughout the narrative as the setting for Harvey's most important moments of growth:

- **Rescue and disorientation** (Chapter 1): [[Manuel]] rescues Harvey from the sea and places him in a dory, telling him "You are with me in the dory" (001-043). The disoriented boy experiences his first taste of working-class reality: the sway of the flat-bottomed boat, the fog, a world without servants or cabins (001-044).

- **The evening return** (Chapter 2): As the sun sets, the schooners pull their dories home — "the little black figures in the tiny boats pulled like clockwork toys" (002-065). Manuel returns with 231 fish; Long Jack and Uncle Salters follow with their own loads (002-073, 002-113). The dory returns as a measure of the day's work.

- **Harvey's first day of real work** (Chapter 3): Dan takes Harvey out in the little red dory *Hattie S.* for his first real fishing lesson. The chapter opens with the boat's inventory carefully itemized, because learning to fish begins with knowing your dory (003-010, 003-014). By its end, Harvey has fought a halibut, bled on the gunwale, and felt the first stirrings of competence (003-036).

- **Penn's incapacity** (Chapter 3): The gentle, damaged [[penn|Penn]] cannot manage his dory properly; he uses a large stone instead of an anchor — a kelleg visible from far off, which the whole fleet would mock (003-036). His dory marks him as broken, in a world where a man is judged by how he handles his boat.

## 叙事意义

The dory is more than a boat in *Captains Courageous* — it is the physical space where the novel's central transformation occurs. Harvey Cheyne arrives on the Grand Banks as a boy who has never done anything useful, who has been carried through life by railroad porters and steamship stewards. The dory is the first place where no one carries him. He must row, bait, haul, and bleed on his own.

Kipling uses the dory to dramatize a contrast between worlds. On the steamship, Harvey was a first-class passenger insulated from labor. In the dory, there is no insulation: the gunwale is hard, the cod-line cuts, the halibut fights back. The boat is small enough that every mistake is visible, and the fleet is large enough that every judgment matters.

When Harvey finally earns the crew's grudging respect, he earns it in a dory — the same boat where Manuel first pulled him from the sea. The circle closes: the boy who was rescued becomes a fisherman who can rescue himself.

## 相关人物

- [[Harvey Cheyne]]: learns to fish from a dory under Dan's instruction; his initiation into manual labor — and his moral education — begins in the little red dory *Hattie S.*
- [[Dan Troop]]: experienced young fisherman who teaches Harvey how to bait lines, haul fish, and maintain a dory; his patient instruction anchors Harvey's first weeks aboard
- [[Manuel]]: the Portuguese fisherman who first places Harvey in a dory after his rescue; his brilliant smile and effortless competence set the standard Harvey must reach
- [[Long Jack]]: returns to the schooner with his loaded dory alongside the others; his howling impatience with Uncle Salters' slow counting is a comic counterpoint to the dory's serious work
- [[Uncle Salters]]: a less skilled oarsman whose dory is described as bringing bad luck; his laborious one-by-one fish count tests the crew's patience
- [[Penn]]: struggles with anchoring his dory, using a kelleg instead of a proper anchor — a visible mark of his difference in a world where dory-handling is the measure of a man
