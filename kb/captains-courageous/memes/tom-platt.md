# Tom Platt

A man "scarred from head to foot" — the legacy of naval service on the USS *Ohio* (002-093). Tom Platt is the oldest and most deliberate of the *We're Here*'s adult crew, a living bridge between the old Navy of wooden ships and the hard-driving world of the Grand Banks fishery. His calm professionalism, salty anecdotes, and unhurried competence make him the crew's anchor and Harvey Cheyne's unwitting instructor in the code of the Banks.

## 身份与背景

Tom Platt is a veteran of the old United States Navy, having served aboard the USS *Ohio* — a veteran of the War of 1812 era — as a man-o'-war's man. In the decades since, he has fished the Grand Banks with Disko Troop's crew, bringing to the *We're Here* the discipline and precision of naval seamanship (002-064). He is past middle age, his body bearing the marks of a life at sea: "scarred from head to foot" (002-093). His age and experience command respect — even Disko Troop, the captain, never questions Tom Platt's authority on matters of stowage and ship handling.

## 外貌与性格

Platt's appearance announces his past before he speaks a word: a face and body covered with scars testifying to decades of naval service (002-093). He moves with the deliberation of a man who has learned that haste at sea costs lives — "last and most deliberate of the crew" at every meal (002-129). He is calm under pressure, speaks little, and when he does speak it carries weight. His humor is dry: when Harvey gapes at the damp foc'sle, Platt replies with the fatalism of a man who has seen worse, "It won't be any dryer our side the da-ay" (003-081).

## 主要经历

### The Ohio Days

Before the Grand Banks, there was the old Navy. Tom Platt served on the USS *Ohio*, a ship-of-the-line that embodied the age of sail. The "double game" — as Disko calls it — is that Platt will happily tell anyone about his naval service "all about the old Ohio" (002-068), yet his stories are never mere boasting. They are the living history of a navy that shaped the nation's maritime character. When he speaks of the *Ohio*, the crew "loosed the jaws" in deference (002-162), for the man who faced cannon-fire is not to be interrupted.

### Fish Stowage: Professionalism at Work

Platt's expertise extends beyond seamanship to the economics of fishing. He counts the fish with "a practised eye" — "One, two, four — nine" — assessing the catch with the precision of a quartermaster (002-116). When Uncle Salters, the farmer-turned-fisherman, beats him at counting — "Forty-one," said Tom Platt. "Beat by a farmer, Salters" (002-119) — Platt takes it in stride, his dignity intact. His domain is the hold: with Disko, he moves among the salt-bins, ensuring the fish are properly dressed down and stowed (002-145). His maxim — "Good stowin's good saltin'" (002-164) — is as close to a philosophy as the Banks allow.

### Teaching Harvey

Platt's role in Harvey Cheyne's transformation is subtle but steady. He watches the boy "critically" from the start (002-093), a naval veteran sizing up a green recruit. When the anchor is weighed, it is Platt who asks Harvey whether he has "ever seen an anchor weighed before" (003-081), the question half-teasing, half-genuine. His presence is one of example — Harvey absorbs the lesson that a man's work speaks for him, that scars are earned and respected, and that the old Navy's discipline translates directly to life on the Banks.

## 作用与意义

Tom Platt embodies the novel's theme of the passing of the old order. His naval service on the *Ohio* connects the *We're Here* to a fading world of wooden ships and iron men, a world Kipling clearly admires. Platt's scars are not disfigurements — they are credentials, the visible proof of a life of service. In the novel's moral economy, Platt represents the ideal of professional competence: a man who knows his trade absolutely, who speaks only when he has something to say, and whose authority derives entirely from experience and character.
