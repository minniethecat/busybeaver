# Fog

It begins in fog and it ends in fog. [[harvey-cheyne|Harvey Cheyne]] falls from a liner into a fogbank and is pulled from the sea by a fisherman blowing a [[conch]]-shell through the mist. The fog that nearly kills him becomes, over the course of the novel, the element in which he learns to work, navigate, and belong.

On the [[grand-banks|Grand Banks]], fog is not an event — it is a condition of existence. It drops without warning "between fish and fish" (003-077). It muffles sound, obscures the [[schooner]], and makes the already dangerous work of hand-lining from a [[dory]] even more perilous. And it teaches fishermen a kind of perception that has nothing to do with sight.

## 定义

Grand Banks fog forms when warm, moist air from the Gulf Stream passes over the cold waters of the Labrador Current, condensing into a dense, low-lying cloud that can persist for days (001-001). The collision of warm and cold — the meeting of two great ocean currents — produces one of the thickest, most persistent fogs on earth.

For fishermen working from small dories, fog is both hazard and fact of life. It obscures the mother schooner, muffles sound, and isolates each dory in a private world of grey (001-044). A man alone in the fog with a hand-line has only his ears to tell him where he is — and ears, on the Banks, can lie.

## 具体细节

### Harvey's Fall

The novel opens with Harvey Cheyne on the deck of a transatlantic liner. "The sea lifted, whistling to warn the fishing-fleet. The fog was thick" (001-001). Disoriented by the fog and sick from a cigar, Harvey leans too far over the rail and falls.

The fog swallows him. The liner steams on, his cries unheard — the same fog that obscures the sea also swallows sound. A wave swings out of the mist and carries him away (001-034). Only [[Manuel]], hearing something through the grey, rows toward the sound and pulls Harvey into his dory (001-043). The fog that nearly kills Harvey is also the fog behind which his rescuer appears: it is simultaneously threat and deliverance.

### The Fog as Constant Presence

The Bank fog comes and goes unpredictably. It drops without warning — "between fish and fish," the crew find themselves wrapped in it while dressing down the catch (003-077). Sometimes it lifts to reveal a horizon full of sails and dories, the whole fleet visible at once; sometimes it stays for days.

This unpredictability shapes the psychology of the Banks. Fishermen learn to work in conditions where the visible world can vanish at any moment. They learn to trust sound over sight, memory over landmarks, and the captain's instinct over charts.

### Sound and Signaling

In the fog, sound becomes the primary sense. The conch-shell horn is each dory's lifeline — a blast of noise that cuts through the mist to warn other boats and signal the mother schooner. Manuel, standing in his dory, blows through a big conch-shell with extraordinary energy: "swaying with the sway of the flat-bottomed dory, and send a grinding, thuttering shriek through the fog" (001-044).

Fishermen learn to read the fog's acoustics — how sound bends and fades in dense air, how a horn from the north sounds different from one to the south, how silence can mean a dory in trouble. It is a kind of hearing that city people never develop: a spatial sense built entirely from echoes.

### Navigation Without Sight

The fog is the reason [[disko-troop|Disko Troop]] is so valued as a captain. He knows the Banks so intimately that he can find his position even when landmarks are invisible (002-175). When he tells Harvey about the thick fog he navigated as a young man, he is teaching a lesson: experience on the Banks means learning to see with something other than your eyes.

## 书中涉及场景

- **The opening** (Chapter 1): Harvey stands on the liner's deck, the fog thick around him, the sea lifting to warn the fishing fleet — and moments later he is in the water, swallowed by the grey (001-001, 001-034).

- **Manuel's horn** (Chapter 1): After the rescue, Manuel blows his conch-shell through the fog, "swaying with the sway of the flat-bottomed dory," until the *We're Here* materializes alongside (001-044).

- **Disko's story** (Chapter 2): Disko tells Harvey about a thick fog he navigated as a young fisherman, the story doubling as an initiation into the knowledge that makes a captain (002-175).

- **Dressing down in fog** (Chapter 3): The fog drops on the crew "between fish and fish" — a sudden, casual reminder that the Banks can blind you at any moment (003-077).

## 叙事意义

Fog is the novel's governing metaphor. Harvey arrives in fog — disoriented, blind, helpless — and must learn to function in a world where nothing is clear. The fog represents the opacity of working-class life to a boy who has never worked: at first he cannot see anything, cannot understand the codes and rhythms of the Banks. Gradually he learns.

The fog also equalizes. On a clear day, a schooner's sails are visible for miles, and every fisherman can orient himself by the mother ship. In fog, every dory is alone. The captain's knowledge, the fisherman's ear, the boy's nerve — these are what matter, not wealth or name.

When Disko tells Harvey about navigating through thick fog as a young man, he is offering the novel's thesis: you learn by doing, in conditions that would terrify anyone sensible. The fog is not an obstacle to be avoided but a teacher to be endured.
