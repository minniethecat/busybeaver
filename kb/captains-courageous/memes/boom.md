# Boom

The **boom** is a horizontal spar that runs along the foot of a fore-and-aft sail, used to extend and control the sail's shape. In _Captains Courageous_, the booms of the fishing schooners are constantly in motion, creaking and swinging as the vessels work the Grand Banks.

## The Foreboom

The foreboom, attached to the foresail, is the most frequently mentioned boom aboard the _We're Here_. When the foresail is raised, "the foreboom creaked as the _We're Here_ looked up to the wind" (003-078). The foreboom occupies a prominent position on deck — the crew works "aft of the foreboom and booby of the main-hatch" (003-098). Dan identifies the foreboom to Harvey as a key reference point when teaching him the rigging: the lines run "along a few feet of the boom, and the lead of each rope was clear" (003-097).

## Handling the Boom

Working with the boom requires both skill and caution. When teaching Harvey about reefing, Dan explains: "Then I'd haul the reef-pennant. I'd hook the tackle on to the reef-pennant, and then haul down the cringle till it was down on the boom. Then I'd tie her up the way I wanted" (003-112). This describes the process of shortening sail by pulling its foot down to the boom — a routine but physically demanding task on a working schooner.

When the jib is dropped, it comes "down in a lump on the boom" (003-121) — the boom catching and cradling the heavy canvas. The lead line that measures depth sings "a deep droning boom" as it pays out, a wordplay that links the spar's name to the sound of the sea (003-121).

## The Booms at Sea

Booms are dangerous in heavy weather. When a sea sweeps the deck, a sailor must "cling with arms, legs, and teeth to rope, boom, or mast" (008-042). The "jaws of the booms whined against the masts" as the schooner rolls (008-104), a sound that speaks to the constant strain on rigging and spars.

Other vessels have their own distinctive boom arrangements. Some schooners carry "her foreboom guyed out over the side" (004-082), and the great nineteenth-century ships described by Disko carry booms of extraordinary length — "hundred-and-ninety-foot booms" that dwarf the modest spars of a Banks fisherman (005-034).

## The Boom in Port

The boom is also a hazard in harbor. The dories are "near scalped the boom off a Frenchman" in a near-collision (001-030), and Manuel tells Harvey about a sailor who went "over the heel of the bowsprit" during a boom-related accident (008-044). Working around booms requires constant awareness — a swinging boom can knock a man overboard or crush him against the bulwarks.
