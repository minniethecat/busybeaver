# Sail

A **sail** is a surface, typically made of canvas, used to catch the wind and propel a vessel through the water. In _Captains Courageous_, sails are central to the daily life and work of the fishing fleet on the Grand Banks.

## Role in the Fishing Fleet

The Grand Banks are filled with the sails of fishing schooners. As Harvey observes after his first night aboard the _We're Here_, "the long blue seas were full of sails and dories" spread across the horizon (003-002). Each schooner in the fleet carries multiple sails, including the mainsail, foresail, jib, and the distinctive riding-sail used when the vessel is at anchor.

## Types of Sails

The schooners of the Banks carry several types of sails. The **mainsail** is the principal sail, bent to the mainmast (002-051). The **foresail** is carried on the foremast, and Dan Troop points out the _Carrie Pitman_ from West Chatham, noting she "has a patch in her foresail an' a new jib" (003-003). The **riding-sail** is a small triangular sail set on the mainmast while the schooner rides at anchor, helping to keep the vessel steady (001-058).

## Sailing a Fishing Dory

The fishermen's dories are not sailed but rowed. When Harvey asks "Where's the sail and mast?" upon entering a dory for the first time, Dan chuckles and explains, "Ye don't sail fishin'-dories much. Ye pull" (003-015)(003-016). This highlights the physical labor involved in dory fishing — the small boats are propelled entirely by oar.

## The Riding-Sail

The riding-sail is a recurring detail throughout the novel. It is described as "never-furled" on the _We're Here_ (002-145). At night, it "slatted a little in the shifts of the light airs" (002-177). When Troop steadies the schooner, "the riding-sail bellied" as she responds to the helm (003-077). Other vessels, including the French schooner, also employ "jib and riding-sail" (006-064).

## Sailing as a Measure of Speed

The crew use sailing as a reference for speed and distance. Disko Troop remarks of a passing liner that she has been visible "since noon, an' that's close sailin'" (001-030), using the phrase to acknowledge the liner has made good time across the Banks.
