# Courage

**Courage** is the defining virtue of _Captains Courageous_. Kipling's title, drawn from the ballad of "Captains Courageous, whom death could not daunt," announces the theme. Throughout the novel, courage takes the form not of grand heroism but of steady competence in the face of the sea's constant dangers.

## Courage as Competence

The fishermen of the _We're Here_ do not speak of bravery — they demonstrate it through action. When Harvey Cheyne first arrives, his notion of courage is the boastful self-regard of a boy who has never been tested. In a moment of self-reflection, Harvey "enlarged, as though he were talking to himself, on his very great courage and resource at all times" — the irony is that he is only beginning to learn what real courage means (010-053).

## The Title's Meaning

The novel's title comes from the traditional ballad, appearing in the book's front matter as "Captains Courageous" (011-002). The captains of the title are the fishing schooner masters — men like Disko Troop — whose courage lies in their judgment, their seamanship, and their willingness to shoulder responsibility for crew and vessel in all conditions.

## Resourcefulness Under Pressure

Kipling links courage with resourcefulness throughout the book. The true test of a captain is not how he handles fair weather but how he responds when things go wrong — in fog, in gales, when gear breaks or men are lost. The fishermen's courage is practical: it is the ability to think clearly and act decisively when failure means death.

## The Railroad Connection

The world Harvey leaves behind values a different kind of boldness. His father, Mr. Cheyne, "enlarged, as though he were talking to himself, on his very great courage and resource at all times" when he recounts building railroads and wrecking competitors (010-053). The parallel is deliberate: father and son both must learn that the courage of boardroom maneuvering is not the same as the courage demanded by the sea.
