# Harvey Falls Overboard

The event that sets the entire novel in motion. On a foggy night in the North Atlantic, [[harvey-cheyne|Harvey Cheyne]] — seasick, dizzy, and alone on the liner's deck — leans too far over the rail and plunges into the [[grand-banks|Grand Banks]]. The liner steams on, his cries unheard.

Minutes later, the Portuguese fisherman [[Manuel]], hearing something in the fog, rows toward the sound and pulls the drowning boy into his [[dory]] (001-001, 001-034, 001-043). It is the most consequential accident in Kipling's fiction: a fall that is simultaneously a death and a birth, destroying one identity and creating another.

## 背景

Harvey Cheyne, fifteen years old and the spoiled only son of a [[mr-cheyne|California railroad millionaire]], is crossing the Atlantic with his mother aboard a luxury liner. He has been given a cigar by a fellow passenger and, unused to tobacco, becomes violently ill. Staggering onto the deck for air, he finds the ship enveloped in thick fog — "the sea lifted, whistling to warn the fishing-fleet" — and in his disorientation, he leans too far over the rail (001-001).

The fog is at once literal weather and moral symbol. Harvey is lost in it before he falls — lost in a world of money without purpose, adrift between "Adirondacks, Lakewood, Hot Springs, New York, and round again" (001-007). The fall through fog is the most honest thing that has ever happened to him.

> The weather door of the smoking-room had been left open to the North Atlantic fog, as the big liner rolled and lifted.
> — (001-001)

## 经过

### The Fall

Harvey falls into the freezing North Atlantic. The liner, a massive steam-powered vessel, cannot hear his cries and steams on without noticing his absence. He is alone in the dark, cold water, certain he will drown.

In one of the novel's most poetic passages, a "white-faced, waving mother-wave" sweeps him up — Kipling's personification of the sea as both destroyer and deliverer (001-034). The wave is "mother" because it will deliver Harvey to his new birth; it is "white-faced" because the sea is indifferent and deadly.

> A white-faced, waving mother-wave swung out of the fog, tucked Harvey under one arm, and pulled him away from the ship.
> — (001-034)

### The Rescue

Manuel, the Portuguese fisherman, has been working his [[dory]] near the schooner *We're Here*. He hears something in the fog — a sound, a splash, a cry — and rows toward it. He pulls Harvey into the little boat, [[conch]]-shell in his belt, and speaks the words that begin Harvey's new life.

> "You are with me in the dory — Manuel my name, and I come from schooner *We're Here* of Gloucester. I find you on the water, an' I bring you aboard."
> — (001-043)

The phrasing is significant. Manuel does not say "I saved you." He says "I find you" — as if Harvey were a stray cod, a piece of driftwood, something the sea had carried to him. The casualness is not cruelty; it is the fisherman's habit of treating the extraordinary as ordinary.

## 结果与影响

The fall is a literal descent that becomes a symbolic rebirth. Harvey loses everything — his wealth, his identity, his soft life — and is plunged into a world where none of it matters. When he wakes the next morning, he finds himself in a berth aboard the [[schooner]] *We're Here*, with [[dan-troop|Dan Troop]] laughing at his expensive but impractical shoes (001-058).

The rescue is not a return to safety but an entry into a harder, more honest world. By the novel's end, the fall will be revealed as the best thing that ever happened to Harvey Cheyne — the accident that made him into a man.

### The Education Begins

From the moment of rescue, Harvey's education proceeds through a series of shocks:
- His demand to be taken to [[new-york|New York]] immediately is met with laughter (001-055, 001-066)
- He discovers that his father's name means nothing on the Banks (001-070)
- He is put to work at ten and a half dollars a month (001-103)
- He learns that a boy who does not work does not eat

Each of these discoveries traces back to the single moment of the fall.
