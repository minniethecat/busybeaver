# The Frenchman's Auction

The Frenchman's auction is one of the most haunting episodes in *Captains Courageous* — a maritime ritual in which the personal effects of a dead sailor are sold to the highest bidder among the fishing fleet. The event illuminates the harsh realities of life on the Grand Banks, where death is common and practicality overrides sentiment.

## The Death

A French deckhand from St. Malo is killed when "a wave had doubled the poor fellow over the heel of the bowsprit and broken his back" [008-046]. The accident occurs in full view of the fleet, and the news "spread like a flash." Unlike the customary practice of simply burying a dead man's kit with him or returning it to his family, the French captain decides to hold an auction.

## The Auction

"Contrary to general custom, the Frenchman held an auction of the dead man's kit — he had no friends at St. Malo or Miquelon" [008-046]. The absence of anyone to claim the belongings is a poignant detail: the dead sailor is utterly alone in the world, with no one in his home port to receive his few possessions.

Tom Platt visits the French vessel because "the dead man was his brother as a Freemason" [008-046], demonstrating the bonds of fraternal organizations that transcended nationality and language on the Banks. The auction proceeds briskly, with fishermen from various schooners bidding on boots, knives, and other practical items.

## The Knife

The most contested item is the dead man's knife. Dan wins it with his bid, and when questioned about why the French crew did not bid themselves, he explains: "On account o' their blame Cath'lic superstitions. They don't fancy takin' iron from off a dead man, so to speak" [008-055]. The Catholic French fishermen's reluctance to take iron from a corpse contrasts with the practical Yankee attitude — Dan is untroubled by superstition.

The knife's provenance becomes a topic of speculation. An Eastport man reports that "the knife had been used — so the French captain told him — used up on the French coast last year" [008-056], adding a layer of history to the dead sailor's blade. A Gloucesterman later declares his distrust: "when it comes to a dead St. Malo deckhand scarin' a couple o' pore boys stiff fer the sake of a thirty-cent knife, why, then, the cook can take hold fer all o' me. I mistrust furriners, livin' or dead" [008-055].

## Significance

The auction encapsulates the matter-of-fact fatalism of the Grand Banks fishery. Death is common enough that a dead man's boots can be sold alongside his knife without undue sentiment. Yet the episode also reveals the human connections — the Freemason bond, the whispered histories of blades — that knit the multinational fleet together.
