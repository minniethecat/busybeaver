# Trawl Line

A mile of line, hundreds of hooks, and hours of baiting in the dusk.

When the hand-lining is slow and the pens are light, [[disko-troop|Disko Troop]] gives the order: "Guess we'll run out aour trawl tonight" (003-140). The crew groans — trawl fishing is "harder on the back" — but they go to the tubs, and the evening passes in the rhythmic work of threading [[caplin]] onto hooks. On the [[grand-banks|Grand Banks]], a trawl line is both a tool and a choice: more fish, faster, but at a cost that some captains will not pay.

## 定义

In the Grand Banks dory fishery, a trawl line (also called a "tub trawl" or "longline") consists of a heavy ground-line to which shorter lines called "snoods" or "gangings" are attached every few feet, each ending in a baited hook (003-149, 003-153). The entire rig is carefully coiled in a wooden tub — the line must pay out smoothly without tangling as the [[dory]] rows, or the whole set is ruined.

Setting a trawl is a two-man operation: one man rows the dory along the chosen ground while the other pays out the ground-line, the baited hooks dropping to the bottom at regular intervals (003-140). Buoys and anchor lines mark each end. After several hours — often overnight — the dory returns to haul the trawl hand-over-hand, coiling the line back into its tub and removing the catch from each hook.

The contrast with hand-lining is stark. A hand-line fisherman works one line, two hooks, in direct contact with the fish. A trawl fisherman deploys a machine: hundreds of hooks working while the crew sleeps. The difference matters not just for the back but for the soul of the fishery.

## 具体细节

### Baiting Up

The most labor-intensive part of trawl fishing is baiting the hooks. Each hook — and there may be hundreds in a single trawl — must be baited by hand with caplin, squid, or pieces of [[cod-fishing|cod]]. The crew sits on deck in the evening, the tubs of coiled trawl-line before them, threading bait onto hooks hour after hour as the light fades (003-149).

[[dan-troop|Dan]] tells Harvey he "helped bait up trawl ashore 'fore I could well walk" (003-155). It is work that starts in childhood and continues for a lifetime — a skill passed down through generations, learned before a boy can even go to sea. The dusk gathers over the tubs full of trawl-line lashed to windward of the main hatch (003-153), and the crew works in the quiet that comes before a big set.

### Harder on the Back

"Harder on the back, but it'll fill the pens," says Disko (003-140). The phrase captures the trade-off at the heart of trawl fishing. Hauling a mile of ground-line hand-over-hand, with the weight of dozens of fish pulling against you, is exhausting in a way that hand-lining is not. The hand-line fisherman feels every fish individually; the trawl fisherman fights the cumulative weight of the whole set.

But when the pens are light and the cod are scattered, the trawl is the answer. Disko deploys it strategically — not every day, but when conditions demand it. He is a pragmatist, not an ideologue.

### The Trawler Controversy

A "trawler" is a schooner that specializes in trawl-line fishing rather than hand-lining. Disko's opinion is blunt: trawlers are "progressive, but fish-injuring" (003-157). The trawl catches everything — undersized fish, non-target species, fish that would grow if left alone. It can deplete a ground faster than hand-lining, reducing the catch for everyone.

This is not mere traditionalism. Disko has been offered work on a trawler and refused it. His refusal is economic as well as ethical: a depleted ground hurts every fisherman eventually. The tension between efficiency and conservation that runs through the novel finds its clearest expression in the trawl line — a technology that works too well for its own good.

## 书中涉及场景

- **The decision** (Chapter 3): Disko decides the hand-lining is slow and orders the crew to bait up the trawl tubs for a night set. The crew's reaction — part resignation, part determination — captures the ambivalence fishermen feel toward the gear (003-140).

- **Baiting in the dusk** (Chapter 3): The crew sits on deck at dusk, the tubs before them, methodically baiting hook after hook as the light fades and the sea darkens around them (003-149, 003-153).

- **Dan's childhood** (Chapter 3): Dan mentions helping bait trawl ashore before he could walk — a glimpse of the fishing childhood that produced the competent young man Harvey is learning from (003-155).

- **Disko's verdict** (Chapter 3): Disko expresses his measured contempt for trawlers, drawing a line between the *We're Here*'s way of fishing and the newer, more aggressive methods gaining ground on the Banks (003-157).

## 叙事意义

The trawl line represents a moral choice disguised as a fishing method. The *We're Here* is a hand-lining schooner by tradition and principle — not because the crew is lazy or backward, but because they believe in a certain relationship with the sea. You take what you earn, one fish at a time, and you leave enough for the next season.

The trawl challenges that. It is more efficient, more productive, more "progressive." But it takes more than it should, and Disko — who has spent his life on the Banks — will not be part of it. His refusal matters because it is not ideological posturing but hard-won wisdom: a man who has seen grounds depleted knows what efficiency costs.

Harvey, listening, absorbs the lesson without comment. He is learning that work on the Banks is not just about catching fish but about how you catch them — that even a boy's first job carries a moral dimension.
