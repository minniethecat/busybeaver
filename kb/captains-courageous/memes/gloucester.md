# Gloucester

The Massachusetts fishing port that serves as home to the *We're Here* and her crew. In the 1890s, Gloucester was the epicenter of the American North Atlantic fishery — a tough, prosperous, and famously independent city whose schooners ranged from the Grand Banks to the Labrador coast. It is where the novel's journey ends, and where Harvey Cheyne's transformation is ultimately revealed to his parents (001-043).

## 地理位置

Gloucester occupies the eastern tip of Cape Ann, approximately thirty miles northeast of Boston. Its deep harbor opens directly onto the Gulf of Maine and the North Atlantic, making it an ideal base for the Grand Banks fishing fleet. In the late nineteenth century, Gloucester was home to hundreds of fishing schooners and thousands of fishermen, many of them immigrants from Portugal, Nova Scotia, and Ireland.

On the approach from sea, the harbor reveals itself in dramatic flashes — the low circle of hills round Gloucester Harbor, Ten Pound Island, the fish-sheds, with the broken line of house-roofs, and each spar and buoy on the water, all caught in blinding photographs of lightning as a schooner comes home through a summer storm (008-110).

## 在书中的作用

Gloucester is both starting point and destination. Manuel announces his origin with pride — "I come from schooner *We're Here* of Gloucester" — and the name carries weight on the Banks (001-043). Disko Troop reinforces this identity when he introduces himself to the shipwrecked Harvey:

> "Not knowin' what your notions o' fun may be, I can't rightly say, young feller. But if I was you, I wouldn't call the boat which, under Providence, was the means o' savin' ye, names. In the first place, it's blame irreligious. In the second, it's annoyin' to my feelin's — an' I'm Disko Troop o' the *We're Here* o' Gloucester, which you don't seem rightly to know."
> — (001-065)

The schooner returns to Gloucester at novel's end, and it is there that Harvey's parents arrive to find a son they barely recognize: sunburned, callused, competent. Harvey's telegram home captures the entire arc: "Picked up by fishing schooner *We're Here* having fallen off boat great times on Banks fishing all well waiting Gloucester Mass care Disko Troop for money or orders" (009-007).

After the return, Cheyne — a self-made railroad magnate — prowls the crooked street which was half wharf and half ship's store, asking questions with cheerful, unslaked Western curiosity, till all the waterfront wanted to know "what in thunder that man was after, anyhow" (010-002). He walks the Mutual Insurance rooms, endures the charity appeals of every Fisherman's Widow and Orphan Aid Society within the city limits, and hands them all over to Mrs. Cheyne (010-002).

## 航海/渔业特征

Gloucester's harbor was the primary market for salt cod on the eastern seaboard. The city's fishing fleet was famous for its speed and seaworthiness — Gloucester schooners like the *We're Here* were built for the demanding conditions of the North Atlantic and designed to carry maximum cargo back to market.

The Gloucester fleet was predominantly composed of the port's own boats, with a scattering from Provincetown, Harwich, Chatham, and some of the Maine ports, though the crews drew from far and wide (005-038). The fleet operated like a mob of sheep, huddled round some unrecognized leader, and risk bred recklessness — especially when greed was added to the crowded conditions on the Banks (005-038).

Gloucester fishermen held strong rivalries, particularly with Nova Scotian boats. The insult "Shrimp! Gloucester shrimp! Git aout, you Novy!" was typical of the Banks banter (006-058), and to call a Gloucester man a Nova Scotian was "not well received" (006-059).

The city also marked the terminus of the fishing calendar. At season's end, the last letters pitched on deck wrapped round pieces of coal, and the Gloucester men shouted messages to their wives and womenfolks and owners as their schooner made its final run through the Fleet (008-101). In slack times, a fisherman might find himself "rowin' summer boarders araound East Gloucester" to make ends meet (007-017).

## 商业与码头生活

The wharf-front economy of Gloucester was built on salt cod. Cheyne, arriving after Harvey's rescue, was overwhelmed with figures in proof — statistics of boats, gear, wharf-frontage, capital invested, salting, packing, factories, insurance, wages, repairs, and profits (010-002). Men said that four out of every five fish-balls served at New England's Sunday breakfast came from Gloucester (010-002).

The waterfront was its own self-contained world, where all the world knew all the world's business (010-078). Dealings with fish buyers like Wouverman were tough: Disko, sure that the *We're Here* was at least a week ahead of any other Gloucester boat, held out until the buyer paid four twenty-five a quintal — premium prices for the first catch of the season (008-119).

Gloucester boats had strong opinions about their rivals' gear. Dan explained his preference for dory trawling over deck trawling with feeling:

> "There's three hundred fathom to each tub. More'n enough to lay out tonight. Ouch! 'Slipped up there, I did." He stuck his finger in his mouth. "I tell you, Harve, there ain't money in Gloucester 'u'd hire me to ship on a reg'lar trawler. It may be progressive, but, barrin' that, it's the putterin'est, slimjammest business top of earth."
> — (003-157)

## 纪念与逝者

Gloucester's fishing industry came at a heavy human cost. After a storm, only a couple of Portuguese and an old man from Gloucester were drowned — but many were cut or bruised, and two schooners had parted their tackle and been blown to the southward (008-043).

The annual toll was starkly acknowledged. At the Gloucester Memorial Day exercises, the orator spoke of the price that must be paid for the yearly harvest, naming the lost dead — one hundred and seventeen of them (010-098).

> "We lose one hundred a year from Gloucester only, Mr. Cheyne — one hundred boys an' men; and I've come so's to hate the sea as if 'twuz alive an' listenin'. God never made it fer humans to anchor on."
> — (009-186)

This woman, the widow of a drowned Gloucester fisherman, voiced a grief shared by countless families along the waterfront. The city's charities — the Fisherman's Widow and Orphan Aid Societies — were a constant presence, and Cheyne found himself unable to walk the wharves without secretaries of every such institution descending upon him, each man anxious to beat the other institution's record (010-002).

## 宗教与民间信仰

For fishermen like Manuel, returning to Gloucester was an occasion for religious observance. He promised to give candles for his good luck:

> "That is because we make you fisherman, these days. If I was you, when I come to Gloucester I would give two, three big candles for my good luck."
> — (004-008)

Manuel, a Madeira man and Catholic, elaborated: "I always give candles — two, three more when I come to Gloucester. The good Virgin she never forgets me, Manuel" (004-012). This mixture of Catholic piety and seafaring superstition was woven into the fabric of Gloucester's immigrant fishing community.

## 竞争与冒险

Not all who sailed from Gloucester returned with goodwill. The deranged skipper of the *Jennie Cushman*, Uncle Abishai, cursed the Gloucester fleet after being denied help:

> "Jounce ye, an' strip ye an' trip ye! A livin' gale — a livin' gale. Yab! Cast up fer your last trip, all you Gloucester haddocks. You won't see Gloucester no more, no more!"
> — (004-089)

Gloucester's progressive-minded fishermen watched for new vessel designs. Dan admired the *Elector o' Gloucester*, a Burgess-modelled haddocker: "She's a daisy, ef she is a toothpick" (004-121). But Disko Troop, like many old-timers, remained "sot agin 'em on account o' their pitchin' an' joltin'," preferring traditional craft despite the "heaps o' money" in the new designs (004-121).
