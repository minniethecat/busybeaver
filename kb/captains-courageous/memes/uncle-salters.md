# Uncle Salters

Disko Troop's own brother, a farmer by trade who has taken to the sea — with mixed results. Uncle Salters rows "tender" (cautiously, clumsily) and counts his fish one by one with maddening slowness, to the vocal despair of Long Jack. He represents the landsman's uneasy adaptation to the fishermen's world (002-068, 002-113).

## 身份与背景

Salters is Disko Troop's brother, but unlike Disko he came to fishing late in life after years of farming. The transition has not been entirely successful: he lacks the fluid instinct of a born fisherman, and his methods — slow, careful, laborious — contrast sharply with the swift efficiency of men like Manuel and Long Jack (002-068). Dan describes him memorably: "There ain't water enough 'tween here an' Hatt'rus to wash the furrer-mold off'n his boots. He's jest everlastin' farmer" (003-066). Before coming to sea, Salters ran a farm up Exeter way with Penn; he sold it to a Boston man wanting to build a summer house, and got a heap for it (003-066). He took quarter-share in the We're Here and has been fishing off and on for thirty years — when he wasn't busy inventing patent manures (003-066).

## 外貌与性格

Salters is described by the narrator as "a fat and tubby little man" (002-108). He rows with exaggerated care — Dan warns Harvey that "ef there's any bad luck loose on the Banks she'll fetch up agin Uncle Salters, sure" — a half-joking but persistent belief that Salters is a Jonah, a bringer of ill fortune (002-068, 004-067). Dan calls him "the Jonerest Jonah in the Fleet regardin' his own special luck" (004-067), and the crew's superstition about him runs deep. Despite this, he is treated with the tolerance due to family and a fellow crew member. Salters was "not an amiable person" and "esteemed it his business to keep the boys in order" (005-028).

## 主要经历

### 数鱼闹剧 (Chapter 2)

Uncle Salters' most memorable scene is his agonizingly slow counting of his catch, marking notches on his dory's gunwale one by one while Long Jack howls "Mother av delight! He's forkin' them wan by wan!" (002-113). The comedy escalates as Salters gets confused: "Hold on, I'm a bit mixed in my caount" (002-117), leading Tom Platt to declare "Forty-one. Beat by a farmer, Salters. An' you sech a sailor, too!" (002-119).

### 剖鱼大师 (Chapter 2)

Paradoxically, despite his clumsiness at sea, Salters is a master at the dressing-down table. Dan declares "Uncle Salters is the best splitter in the fleet. Watch him mind his book!" (002-151). The narrative follows the rhythm of the gutting line — the "caraaah" of Uncle Salters's knife scooping away backbones, the "Knife oh!" shouted when the work is done (002-153, 002-155).

### Jonah 的名声 (Chapter 4)

Salters's reputation as a Jonah is tested when, standing his night watch, he spots a flicker on the horizon (004-071). It turns out to be Abishai's boat, a notorious vessel "full o' gin an' Judique men" (004-083). Disko groans "Salters, your blame luck holds tighter'n a screw in a keg-head" (004-077), and Tom Platt calls him "The head-king of all Jonahs" (004-080). Salters protests with a farmer's bluntness: "I despise superstition, anyway" (004-104).

### 农夫的执念 (Chapters 5-6)

Salters's agricultural obsession follows him everywhere. When the We're Here encounters a cattle-boat, "the smell of the pens woke all the farmer in him" and he earnestly inquires about cattle feed — suggesting oil-cake broken small and sprinkled (006-004). This leads to a prolonged quarrel with Disko about not keeping "things sep'rate" (006-010, 006-011). His talk below deck is "mainly agricultural" — "his mission in life was to prove the value of green manures, and specially of clover, against every form of phosphate whatsoever" (005-035).

## 与 Penn 的关系

Beneath Salters's gruff exterior lies a surprising tenderness toward Penn, the former Moravian preacher whose mind broke after the Johnstown Flood. When Penn's church tried to reclaim him, Salters "jest let 'em hev it both sides o' the bow, 's if he was a Baptist" and refused to give him up (003-066). Though the two bicker constantly — Salters snarling at Penn's every checker move (003-144) — he defends Penn fiercely: "'Tweren't none o' his fault. Penn's deef" (003-147). On Sundays, Penn would suggest preaching and "Uncle Salters nearly jumped down his throat at the mere notion, reminding him that he was not a preacher," fearing the memories it might trigger (005-025).
