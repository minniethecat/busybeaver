# Harvey Cheyne

The fifteen-year-old son of a California railroad magnate, washed overboard from a luxury transatlantic liner into the North Atlantic fog and rescued by the Portuguese fisherman Manuel of the Gloucester schooner *We're Here*. Harvey Cheyne begins the novel as an insufferable, entitled boy who has never worked a day in his life — and ends it as a competent young fisherman who has earned the respect of hardened Grand Banks sailors through honest labor (001-034, 001-043).

## 身份与背景

Harvey Cheyne is the only son of a self-made California millionaire who built his fortune in railroads. Raised in luxury, Harvey has attended expensive private schools, traveled on the best liners, and never known anything but privilege. His mother dotes on him; his father is distant and disappointed in the boy Harvey has become — arrogant, weak, and without any useful skills (001-001).

At fifteen, Harvey is crossing the Atlantic with his mother when he falls overboard in the fog. His rescue by Grand Banks fishermen is an accident that becomes a transformation: forced to work as a "second boy" on the *We're Here* at ten and a half dollars a month, Harvey has no choice but to learn the trade or be useless (001-103).

## 外貌与性格

Harvey arrives on the *We're Here* in fine clothes utterly unsuited to life at sea — warped bicycle shoes, delicate fabrics, and an attitude to match. He is physically soft, unused to any kind of labor, and his hands blister and bleed within hours of starting work (003-036). The crew initially regards him as a joke — a "millionary" who has never done an honest day's work.

Beneath the arrogance, however, Harvey has his father's determination. Once he accepts that his wealth and name mean nothing on the Banks, he applies himself to learning the fishing trade with surprising grit. Dan Troop, the captain's son, becomes his reluctant teacher and eventual friend. By the voyage's end, Harvey's hands are callused, his back is strong, and his character has been fundamentally reshaped.

## 主要经历

### The Fall (Chapter 1)

Harvey is on the deck of a transatlantic liner when he becomes sick from a cigar and the heavy seas. Leaning over the rail in the thick fog, he falls overboard. The liner steams on; his cries are unheard. A "white-faced, waving mother-wave" sweeps him away — and then Manuel, hearing something in the fog, rows toward the sound and pulls him into a dory (001-034, 001-043).

### Learning the Trade (Chapters 1–3)

Brought aboard the *We're Here*, Harvey attempts to assert his wealth and status — and is met with baffled indifference. Disko Troop offers him work as a second boy at ten and a half dollars a month, "all found" (001-103). Harvey's first days are a shock: the smell of fish and gurry, the cold and wet, the physical exhaustion of swabbing dories and hauling lines. Under Dan's impatient instruction, he learns to bait hooks, heave leads, and gut fish (003-036).

### The Halibut (Chapter 3)

Harvey's first real catch — a halibut that fights him for twenty minutes — marks his turning point. When he finally lands the big flat fish, he surveys it with "unspeakable pride" (003-042). He has never earned anything before; now, for the first time, he has something that is his by right of labor.

### The Return (Chapters 8–10)

By the time the *We're Here* returns to Gloucester, Harvey is no longer the boy who fell overboard. He has learned to work, to take orders, to be part of a crew. When his parents arrive — his father having traced him through the transatlantic telegraph — they find a son they barely recognize: sunburned, callused, competent, and confident in a way that wealth never made him.

## 作用与意义

Harvey Cheyne is the vehicle for Kipling's exploration of character formation through labor. The novel argues — explicitly and through Harvey's arc — that physical work, discipline, and membership in a community of skilled laborers can transform a person in ways that wealth and education cannot. Harvey's journey from entitled child to competent young man is the book's central moral argument.
