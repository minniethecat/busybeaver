# Crew

The crew of a Grand Banks fishing schooner is a small, mixed company of men from widely different backgrounds, thrown together for the season and welded into a working unit by the demands of the fishery. Aboard the *We're Here*, the crew numbers eight souls — skipper, cook, and six hands — each with a defined role and a distinct personality (003-074). The crew is a floating democracy in some matters and an absolute hierarchy in others: Disko Troop's word is law on questions of seamanship, but the men josh and argue as equals when the work is done (004-077).

## 定义

A fishing schooner's crew is both a labour unit and a temporary society. The men are hired for the season — typically March through October — and paid in shares of the catch rather than wages. This share system gives every man a direct stake in the fishing: a poor season means lean earnings for all, while a good haul benefits the youngest hand as much as the skipper (001-093). The crew aboard the *We're Here* is unusually stable: Disko Troop has sailed with most of his men for multiple seasons, and the resulting trust allows a rhythm of work that new crews cannot match (005-038).

## 具体细节

### The Crew's Composition

The *We're Here* carries eight men: Disko Troop (skipper), his son Dan (cabin boy and general hand), the cook (unnamed, a Black man from the Caribbean), Uncle Salters (a farmer who fishes to clear his head), Penn (a gentle, damaged man who once served in the Navy), Long Jack (an Irishman of enormous strength and humour), Manuel (a Portuguese from the Western Islands), and Tom Platt (an ex-Navy man who served on the *Ohio*). Harvey Cheyne becomes the ninth, working without pay for the ten dollars and fifty cents Disko charges his father (003-098).

### Skipper and Crew

The relationship between skipper and crew on the Banks is both authoritarian and intimate. Disko Troop commands absolutely when the schooner is working — his judgment on weather, ground, and gear is never questioned. But the same man eats at the same table as his hands, sleeps in the same cabin, and listens to their complaints and stories. An "angry skipper makes an unhappy crew," and the best skippers know when to speak and when to let the men blow off steam (006-011).

### Crew Solidarity

The season forges intense bonds among the crew. When the *Carrie Pitman* signals distress in the fog, the crew of the *We're Here* respond instantly, lending men and gear to help (004-106). The men jeer at one another constantly — Long Jack torments Uncle Salters, the crew mocks Tom Platt's Navy stories — but the teasing is a sign of intimacy, not hostility. A good crew, like Disko's, works as one body, each man anticipating the others' movements on the cluttered deck.
