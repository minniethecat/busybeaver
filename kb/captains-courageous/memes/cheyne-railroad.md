# Cheyne Railroad

The railroad interests controlled by [[mr-cheyne|Harvey Cheyne Sr.]] form the industrial backbone of his fortune. Built through "the building of three railroads and the deliberate wreck of a fourth" (010-052), the Cheyne railroad empire spans the American West, connecting [[san-diego|San Diego]], [[los-angeles|Los Angeles]], and the transcontinental lines that Cheyne commands when racing to reclaim his son.

## 概述

The railroad operations are not a single company but a web of interconnected lines — "half a dozen railroads" (001-007) — assembled through decades of construction, acquisition, and shareholder warfare. Cheyne's career exemplifies the Gilded Age railroad baron: he "crawled round, through, or under mountains and ravines, dragging a string and hoop-iron railroad after him" (010-053), subduing geography and competitors alike.

His holdings extend beyond rails. The empire includes "half the lumber on the Pacific slope" (001-007) — timber that fed the railroad's insatiable appetite for ties, trestles, and rolling stock — and shipping interests that complete the transportation chain from railhead to ocean port.

The scale is vast. When a "war of rates among four Western railroads" breaks out (009-003), Cheyne is at the center, and "representatives of sixty-three million dollars' worth of variously manipulated railroad interests" (009-041) feel the ripples of his decisions.

## 在书中的角色

### The World of Capital

The railroad represents the distant world of high finance that [[harvey-cheyne|Harvey Cheyne Jr.]] is born into — a world of telegraph messages, boardroom battles, and private cars. On the [[were-here|We're Here]], this world is alien and irrelevant. When Harvey boasts of his father's railroads, the fishermen are unmoved: a man is measured by his work, not his father's holdings (001-070).

### The Instrument of Rescue

In Chapter 9, the railroad becomes the means of a father's desperate race across the continent. Cheyne commands his private car — the "Constance" — from Santa Fe at Sixteenth Street through to Buffalo on the N.Y. Limited, connecting to Albany via the New York Central (009-028). Every mile of track represents infrastructure he helped build.

The telegraph operators' dispatch — "Harvey Cheyne is in a hurry, a hurry, a hurry" (009-042) — clears the rails ahead of him. The railroad, normally an instrument of commerce, becomes an instrument of love.

### Cheyne's Legacy

In Chapter 10, Cheyne tells the story of his career to an audience of fishermen. He "described how he had bested his enemies, or forgiven them" (010-053) and the epic labor of "dragging a string and hoop-iron railroad" across the continent. The narrative is framed not as boast but as confession — a man reckoning with what he has built and what it has cost him.
