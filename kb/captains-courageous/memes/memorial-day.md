## Overview

Memorial Day is celebrated in [[gloucester|Gloucester]] at the end of the narrative, marking the culmination of [[harvey-cheyne|Harvey Cheyne]]'s transformation from a spoiled rich boy into a responsible young man. The Cheyne family stays over to attend the Gloucester Memorial Day observance before departing. [010-069]

## The Decision to Stay

Harvey insists on staying for the celebration:

> "We *can't* go without seeing Disko off," said Harvey; "and Monday's Memorial Day. Let's stay over that, anyway." [010-069]

His father, [[mr-cheyne|Mr. Cheyne]], a railroad magnate from the West, is unfamiliar with the tradition but agrees: "We'll stay over for Memorial Day, and get off in the afternoon." [010-074] The event serves as a symbolic conclusion to Harvey's journey — after months aboard the *[[were-here|We're Here]]* and his reunion with his parents, Memorial Day represents his final connection to Gloucester before returning to his former life.

## The Ceremony

Harvey explains the nature of the observance to his father:

> "Well, as far as I can make out, *this* business is a sort of song-and-dance act, whacked up for the summer boarders. Disko don't think much of it, he says, because they take up a collection for the widows and orphans. Disko's independent. Haven't you noticed that?" [010-071]

[[disko-troop|Disko Troop]], ever the independent fisherman, dismisses the summer ceremony as a performance for tourists. Harvey, now identifying as "a Banker — full-blooded Banker," distinguishes between the summer convention and the spring observance: [010-073]

> "The summer convention is. They read out the names of the fellows drowned or gone astray since last time, and they make speeches, and recite, and all. Then, Disko says, the secretaries of the Aid Societies go into the back yard and fight over the catch. The real show, he says, is in the spring. The ministers all take a hand then, and there aren't any summer boarders around." [010-073]

This insider knowledge — the differentiation between the tourist-facing summer event and the authentic spring memorial — demonstrates how completely Harvey has been absorbed into the fishing community's worldview.

## Harvey's Composition

During the Memorial Day observance, Harvey delivers a composition he has written about the Gloucester fishing fleet and its traditions. The piece so impresses the audience that "a farsighted Boston reporter slid away for a full copy of the epic and an interview with the author." [010-112] [010-113]

The composition demonstrates Harvey's deep appreciation for the fishermen's way of life and his integration into their world. [[uncle-salters|Salters]] begrudgingly acknowledges its quality: "that 'u'd cost ye a dollar to hear at any theatre — maybe two." [010-110] The moment marks Harvey's coming of age and his earned place within the community.

## Significance

The Memorial Day episode functions as a narrative bookend: the story begins with Harvey lost at sea and presumed dead on a foggy Atlantic crossing, and ends with a community celebration of remembrance in the very port that restored him to his family. The ceremony's dual nature — a somber reading of the names of "fellows drowned or gone astray" alongside speeches and recitations — echoes the novel's broader themes of loss, resilience, and the dignity of labor at sea.
