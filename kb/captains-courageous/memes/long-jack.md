# Long Jack

A tall, seasoned fisherman whose "melodious bellow" carries across the water as his dory returns at sunset (002-065). Long Jack is part of the adult crew's collective judgment of Harvey Cheyne — skeptical at first, gradually won over by the boy's genuine effort. A grizzly-chinned Galway man with a long lip and a gift of expression, he serves as both taskmaster and storyteller, peacemaker and chorus, over the course of the We're Here's season on the Grand Banks (002-088).

## 身份与背景

Long Jack is a veteran of the Grand Banks fishery, one of the core adult crew members who have fished with Disko Troop for years. His nickname derives from his height, and his reputation is that of a solid, reliable fisherman who does his work without fuss (002-113). A Galway man by origin, he speaks with a rich Irish brogue that Kipling renders phonetically, and his experience ranges from market-boat work off the South Boston coast to the Newfoundland Banks (004-014).

## 外貌与性格

Long Jack's most distinctive trait is his voice — a "melodious bellow" that can be heard across the water (002-065). He is voluble and expressive, given to complaints about Uncle Salters' slow counting, but his complaints are the grumbling of a professional among professionals. He watches Harvey "critically," assessing whether the boy will measure up (002-093). He is also described as "grizzly-chinned" and "long-lipped," a tall Galway man who moves with economy and precision at the splitting-table, catching cod livers with a scoop of his mittened hand while the knives flash around him (002-088, 002-147).

## 主要经历

Long Jack serves multiple roles across the season. In the early chapters he is one of the seasoned fishermen whose collective judgment measures Harvey's worth (002-093). By Chapter 3, he takes active charge of Harvey's nautical education, volunteering to "larn him" when Dan points out the boy's idleness (003-095). For a full hour he walks Harvey up and down the deck, teaching him the name and purpose of every rope — using the rope-ends themselves as mnemonic instruments, rubbing Harvey's nose along the boom to distinguish fore from aft (003-097). He tells Harvey: "'Tis dollars an' cents I'm puttin' into your pocket, ye skinny little supercargo, so that fwhin ye've filled out ye can ship from Boston to Cuba an' tell thim Long Jack larned you" (003-113).

By the midpoint of the voyage, Long Jack has warmed to Harvey's progress. He watches the boy at the windlass and declares, "'Tis beautiful to see how he takes to ut," though he suspects Harvey is half play-acting the bold mariner (005-012). On stormy days when work is impossible, Long Jack reveals another side: he is the crew's master of the supernatural, keeping them silent with stories of Yo-hoes on Monomoy Beach, sand-walkers and dune-haunters, hidden treasure on Fire Island, and the dead crew who row alongside at midnight in a Maine harbor (005-032).

Long Jack also functions as the ship's peacemaker. When Disko and Salters quarrel over whether a cattle-boat qualifies as a proper vessel, Long Jack steps in to calm the waters, arguing that no other packet on the Banks could have held so "compenjus" a conversation with a tramp steamer about navigation (006-016). He preserves harmony among the crew with a wink and a well-timed word (006-047).

At the Virgin Rock, Long Jack's experience proves vital. When a reckless Galway man rows over the rock itself and the Virgin breaks with its "deep sob and gathering roar," it is Long Jack's warning that keeps Harvey and Dan from being among the dead (008-028).

Upon the crew's return to Gloucester, Long Jack is among the first to confront the truth of Harvey's wealth. Climbing out covered with salt and fish-skin, he asks incredulously, "Howly Smoke! D'ye belave his tale av the kid an' the little four-horse rig was thrue?" (009-119). When Mrs. Cheyne thanks the crew for saving her son, Long Jack says simply, "Faith, that pays me a hunder time" (009-139). And when Mrs. Cheyne begins to weep in Chapter 10, moved by the crew's rough kindness, Long Jack murmurs with genuine pity: "She shouldn't ha' come. She shouldn't ha' come" (010-129).
