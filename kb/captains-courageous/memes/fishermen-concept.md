# Fishermen

The fishermen of the Grand Banks were neither romantic figures nor simple laborers. They were skilled professionals operating in one of the world's most dangerous industries, bound by a code that valued competence above all else and judged a man by what he could do with his hands in a pitching dory.

## The Brotherhood

"We're fishermen, an' we've shipped together for a cruise" [002-029] — these words, spoken by Disko Troop to his new crew, capture the voluntary but binding nature of the fishing life. A fisherman's reputation followed him across the Banks; a man known as "a mighty jest man" commanded respect from every schooner in the fleet.

## The Share System

Fishermen were not paid wages but shares of the voyage's profit — a system that made every man a stakeholder in the catch. A full share went to experienced dorymen; younger hands like Dan and Harvey earned fractions.

When Uncle Salters, despite thirty years of fishing, took "quarter-share" [003-066] because his mind was more on "inventin' patent manures" than on cod, it was a mark of the fleet's meritocratic judgment. Dan whispered his awe at a rival's success: "I wouldn't have missed the show fer a half-share in a halibutter" [006-021]. A man's winter depended on the captain's skill in finding fish.

## Faith and Superstition

The fishermen's world was steeped in Catholic faith and folk belief. Manuel declared: "She is very good to fishermen all the time" [004-010], speaking of the Virgin of the Church on the Hill in Gloucester. Portuguese fishermen wore medals and made vows; the "Old Virgin" was both a religious figure and a practical concern — the dangerous reef that bore her name.

## Hazards

The life of a Banks fisherman was among the deadliest occupations of the nineteenth century. Men were lost to gales, fog, the Virgin Rock, and the simple mishaps of heavy work on a pitching deck. The annual Gloucester memorial service — "Memorial Service for the Drowned" — honored those who never returned.
