# Eastport

Eastport, Maine, is the easternmost city in the United States, situated on Moose Island in Passamaquoddy Bay. In *Captains Courageous*, Eastport fishermen appear as participants in the broader maritime community of the North Atlantic, representing the Down East wing of the New England fishing fleet.

## At the Frenchman's Auction

An Eastport man is present at the auction of the dead French deckhand's belongings, held at sea after the St. Malo sailor is killed by a wave. When speculation arises about the French captain's motives for auctioning the kit, the narrative notes that the Eastport man "bought his boots" [008-055] from the sale. His presence at this grisly maritime marketplace underscores the interconnectedness of the fishing fleet — a Maine fisherman buying the boots of a dead Breton sailor on the Grand Banks.

The Eastport man's report about the knife is cited as authoritative: "The Eastport man he told me that the knife had been used — so the French captain told him — used up on the French coast last year" [008-056]. His testimony is invoked in the running argument about whether Maine men are as "progressive" as their Massachusetts counterparts.

## At the Memorial Service

Eastport men appear again at the Gloucester memorial service for lost fishermen. When a poet from Down East rises to read his commemorative verses, an Eastport man in the crowd provides local endorsement: "No keepin' him under. He's a poet, an' he's baound to say his piece. 'Comes from daown aour way, too" [010-121]. After the reading, the same Eastport man vouches for the poet's accuracy: "Naow, I call that sensible. I've bin over that graound with his writin', jest as he read it, in my two hands, and I can testify that he's got it all in" [010-127].

## Maritime Eastport

In the 1890s, Eastport was a significant fishing and sardine-canning center. Its location at the mouth of the Bay of Fundy gave its fishermen access to rich fishing grounds, and its vessels regularly worked the Grand Banks alongside the Gloucester fleet. The Eastport men in Kipling's narrative are portrayed as plain-spoken, practical, and loyal — men who stand by their own and command respect even from the Massachusetts crews who habitually disparage Maine.
