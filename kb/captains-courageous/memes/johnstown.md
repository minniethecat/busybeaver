## Overview

Johnstown, Pennsylvania, is referenced multiple times throughout the narrative in connection with [[jacob-boiler|Jacob Boiler]], a crewman aboard the *[[were-here|We're Here]]* who survived the catastrophic Johnstown Flood of 1889. The town serves as a symbol of tragedy and loss within the novel, contrasting with the resilience and healing found at sea. [003-062]

## The Johnstown Flood

The disaster of May 31, 1889, in which the South Fork Dam failed and unleashed a wall of water upon the industrial city below, is described in stark terms by the crew:

> "He stayed over jest one night in Johnstown. You've heered talk o' Johnstown, ain't ye?... That was the hotel he stayed at. His wife an' kids was to the hotel when Johnstown was wiped out. 'Dam bust an' flood come, an' houses bumped into each other an' folks drowned in the streets.'" [003-062] [003-064]

Jacob Boiler lost his wife and children in the flood, and the memory haunts him throughout the voyage. When certain triggers — a bumping boat, a moment of stress — call up the trauma, Boiler relives the horror. [003-066] [[dan-troop|Dan Troop]] explains to Harvey: "He's struck adrift — sort of a loony, but a good clean loony. He's never done no harm." [003-061]

## Aboard the We're Here

The crew of the *We're Here* learns to handle Boiler's episodes with care and compassion. [[uncle-salters|Salters]] recognizes the triggers: "We'd hev him rememberin' Johnstown next." [005-025] Despite his rough exterior, Salters is protective of Boiler — an unexpected tenderness that reveals the depth of the fishing community's bonds:

> "We want to keep him to anchor till we get back. Dad won't have him worked up." [003-065]

[[disko-troop|Disko Troop]] notes Boiler's haunted eyes: "he's remembered Johnstown! I never seed such eyes in any man's head." [007-020] The crew's collective caretaking of Boiler reflects the novel's broader theme of mutual responsibility within the fishing community — a stark contrast to the competitive, individualistic world of [[mr-cheyne|Cheyne]]'s railroads and boardrooms.

[[harvey-cheyne|Harvey]], newly arrived and still adjusting to shipboard life, is deeply affected by Boiler's story. The caution "You mustn't talk to him about Johnstown" becomes one of the unspoken rules of life aboard the schooner. [009-078]

## Narrative Function

Johnstown serves as a symbol of tragedy and irreversible loss within the novel. While Harvey Cheyne undergoes a positive transformation aboard the *We're Here*, Jacob Boiler carries the scars of one of America's worst industrial disasters — scars that no amount of sea air or camaraderie can heal. The repeated references to Johnstown across chapters 3, 5, 7, and 9 create a persistent undercurrent of grief beneath the novel's adventure narrative, reminding the reader that some wounds are permanent.
