## Overview

Theodore Blunt is the fictional identity under which [[tommy-beresford|Tommy Beresford]] operates the [[international-detective-agency|International Detective Agency]]. The name appears on the agency's brass plate at [[118-haleham-street|118 Haleham Street]]: "[[international-detective-agency|International Detective Agency]]. Theodore Blunt, Manager. Private Inquiries" (001-084). Throughout the novel, Tommy adopts this persona to interview clients, investigate cases, and maintain the cover operation established by [[mr-carter|Mr. Carter]].

The agency's slogan — "[[blunts-brilliant-detectives|Blunt's Brilliant Detectives]]" — becomes well known, and the name Theodore Blunt acquires a reputation for investigative skill that draws clients from across London society.

## Background

The real Theodore Blunt was a genuine private investigator whose name and agency Tommy and [[tuppence-beresford|Tuppence]] were asked to take over by [[mr-carter|Mr. Carter]] of the [[british-secret-service|Secret Service]]. [[mr-carter|Mr. Carter]] explains that Blunt had become involved in foreign business and was detained: "Mr. Blunt has been rather indiscreet" (001-088). The government, needing a cover for intelligence work monitoring Russian communications, arranged for the Beresfords to assume the Blunt identity.

Tommy's impersonation is imperfect — several clients note that he does not match the description of the original Blunt. A visitor in Chapter 4 directly asks: "And you are, perhaps, Mr. Blunt? Mr. Theodore Blunt?" (004-028), having expected someone different. Nevertheless, Tommy maintains the persona effectively enough to run the agency and solve cases.

## Role at the Agency

As Theodore Blunt, Tommy interviews all clients who come to the agency. The desk setup at [[118-haleham-street|118 Haleham Street]] creates the theatrical impression of a busy detective: "behind the desk sat the pseudo Mr. Blunt trying to look as though he had not slept in it for a week" (002-002). [[albert|Albert]], the office boy, plays along with the pretense, telling visitors that "Mr. Blunt is so terribly busy. He's engaged on the phone with the Home Secretary" (002-047).

The dual function of the Blunt identity is crucial to the novel's plot: publicly, Theodore Blunt solves ordinary detective cases brought by clients; privately, he monitors letters with Russian stamps looking for the number sixteen written under the stamp. Tommy describes himself plainly as "I am Theodore Blunt, the head of the firm" (016-013).

## Notable Cases

As Theodore Blunt, Tommy investigates numerous cases across all seventeen chapters:

- **[[sunningdale-mystery|The Sunningdale Mystery]]** (Chapter 2) — Interviewing a young woman whose fiancé has been accused of murder on a golf course
- **[[finessing-the-king|Finessing the King]]** (Chapter 3) — Assisting [[kingston-bruce|Colonel Kingston Bruce]] in the search for a missing diamond pendant, during which Blunt receives "carte blanche" to "search the house from attic to cellar" (003-119)
- **[[clergymans-daughter|The Clergyman's Daughter]]** (Chapter 4) — Investigating strange occurrences at The Larches, where the client says "I must place myself in your hands" (004-051)
- **[[ambassadors-boots|The Ambassador's Boots]]** (Chapter 16) — Consulting with the United States Ambassador, who "seemed amused" by Blunt's questioning (016-030)

## Notable Cases Under the Blunt Identity

Several of the agency's most prominent cases are officially handled under the Blunt name:

- **[[affair-of-the-pink-pearl|The Affair of the Pink Pearl]]**: Tommy, as Blunt, investigates the theft of a valuable pink pearl at [[the-laurels|The Laurels]], the home of the Kingston Bruce family. Tuppence assists under the alias Miss Robinson.
- **[[finessing-the-king|Finessing the King]]**: The Blunt agency receives a cryptic newspaper advertisement — a coded message that draws Tommy and Tuppence into a murder investigation involving a secret society.
- **[[case-of-the-missing-lady|The Case of the Missing Lady]]**: Under the Blunt identity, Tommy takes on the disappearance of a woman, while Tuppence conducts undercover inquiries as Miss Robinson.
- **[[the-crackler|The Crackler]]**: A case involving counterfeit currency, where the Blunt persona proves useful in gaining the trust of concerned clients from high society.
- **[[ch17-the-man-who-was-no-16|The Man Who Was No. 16]]**: The case that connects directly to the agency's intelligence mission — tracking a Russian agent — where the Blunt cover operates in tandem with Tommy's secret work for the [[british-secret-service|Secret Service]].

## Appearance and Manner

The public Theodore Blunt is a creation of Tommy's theatrical instincts. Clients encounter a serious, professional detective who listens intently and asks probing questions. The office is furnished to project an air of busy competence, with [[albert|Albert]]'s stage-managed interruptions reinforcing the impression of a sought-after investigator.

Behind the performance, Tommy uses the Blunt role to indulge his fondness for detective fiction tropes, frequently adopting the mannerisms of famous fictional sleuths depending on the case at hand.

## Relationship to Tommy Beresford

Theodore Blunt is both Tommy's alias and his creative outlet. While [[tommy-beresford|Tommy Beresford]] is the comfortable, settled [[british-secret-service|Secret Service]] employee — "prosperous and sleek and comfortable" (001-020) — Theodore Blunt is the sharp, theatrical detective who brings excitement back into the Beresfords' lives. The relationship between the man and the persona is one of the novel's central tensions, particularly when the real Theodore Blunt threatens to return in the final chapter.

## Appearances

The name Theodore Blunt appears throughout all seventeen chapters of the novel — on the office doorplate, in client introductions, in newspaper advertisements for [[blunts-brilliant-detectives|Blunt's Brilliant Detectives]], and in Tommy's self-introductions to everyone who walks through the agency's door.
