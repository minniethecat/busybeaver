## Overview

**Lady Merivale** is the wife of [[sir-arthur-merivale|Sir Arthur Merivale]] and a central figure in the investigation of the [[affair-of-the-pink-pearl|Affair of the Pink Pearl]] in Chapter 6, "[[gentleman-dressed-in-newspaper|The Gentleman Dressed in Newspaper]]." Her possession of a valuable pink pearl and her independent fortune make her a person of interest in the case.

## The Pink Pearl Affair

Lady Merivale was identified as the owner of the pink pearl that is at the heart of the case:

> "We managed to identify the lady as Lady Merivale. We communicated with Sir Arthur—" (006-013)

The pearl represented considerable value, and her position gave her leverage:

> "If Lady Merivale liked to turn nasty, she could cause a considerable amount of money trouble." (006-020)

## Personal Fortune

Lady Merivale possessed substantial independent wealth:

> "Lady Merivale had a large fortune of her own." (006-102)

This financial independence is noted as a significant factor, as it meant the pearl's loss did not cripple her financially, though its sentimental or status value remained considerable.

## The Disputed Conversation

A point of contention in the investigation involves whether a suspect had spoken to Lady Merivale at a social event:

> "He declared he had never spoken to Lady Merivale at the show. Says somebody should have seen them together." (006-046)

## Character Traits

Lady Merivale is portrayed as a woman of means and social standing, moving in circles where valuable jewellery is both worn and scrutinised. Her involvement in the case is indirect — she is the victim of the theft rather than an active participant in its investigation — yet her fortune and status shape the stakes of the case.
