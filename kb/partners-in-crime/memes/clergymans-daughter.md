## Overview

"The Clergyman's Daughter" combines supernatural mystery with classic treasure-hunting. [[monica-deane|Monica Deane]], the impoverished daughter of a clergyman, consults the Agency about inexplicable events at her inherited property, the Red House. Pictures fall, furniture rearranges itself, and crockery flies across rooms — classic poltergeist phenomena that conceal a more material motive.

## The Client

Tuppence, who was herself a clergyman's daughter, feels an immediate kinship with [[monica-deane|Monica Deane]] (014-001). Monica has inherited the Red House from a wealthy aunt but, after paying legacies, has no money left — only the house. Mysterious buyers have made increasingly tempting offers for the property, and simultaneously, poltergeist-like disturbances have begun (014-004).

## The Investigation

Tommy and Tuppence visit the Red House at Stourton in the Marsh. They observe the phenomena firsthand and suspect human agency rather than the supernatural. The local doctor, Dr. O'Neill, has coined the "poltergeist" explanation, but Tuppence notes the pattern: each disturbance is designed to frighten the inhabitants into selling (014-008).

The repeated purchase offers — always from the same quarter — suggest that someone believes the Red House contains something more valuable than its bricks and mortar. Tuppence, drawing on her childhood knowledge of clerical households, suspects hidden valuables.

## The Resolution

The investigators discover that Monica's late aunt had concealed her wealth — possibly jewellery or bonds — within the house. The poltergeist was a hoax perpetrated by those who knew of the hidden treasure and hoped to buy the property cheaply before the Deanes discovered it. The revelation transforms Monica's fortunes and removes the obstacle to her marriage (014-012).

## Significance

This is Tuppence's case from start to finish. Her identification with the clergyman's daughter is personal, not professional, and it drives her investigation. The case demonstrates that not all Agency work involves crime — sometimes it is about righting a wrong that the law cannot touch.
