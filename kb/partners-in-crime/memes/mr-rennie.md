## Overview

Mr. Rennie is a key figure in Chapter 3 of *[[partners-in-crime-novel|Partners in Crime]]*, "[[finessing-the-king|Finessing the King]]." A guest at the [[kingston-bruce]] household on the evening a valuable pearl was stolen, Rennie quickly becomes a person of interest in the investigation conducted by [[tommy-beresford]] and [[tuppence-beresford]]. His evasive answers and nervous demeanor mark him as a man with something to hide.

## Background

Mr. Rennie dined with the Kingston Bruce family on the evening of the theft, then "looked in after dinner for a coffee" (003-080). Little is revealed about his background or profession, but his presence at the house during the critical window places him squarely within the pool of suspects. [[kingston-bruce|Colonel Kingston Bruce]] identified him as someone who could have had access to the room where the pearl was kept.

When Tommy asked who Rennie was, the implication was clear: Rennie was not a close family friend but an acquaintance whose presence that evening merited scrutiny (003-081). [[kingston-bruce|Colonel Kingston Bruce]], despite his initial reluctance, acknowledged that Rennie was among those who "might have done it" (003-083).

## Involvement in the Pearl Case

Tuppence took the lead in questioning Mr. Rennie, confronting him directly about his movements on the night of the theft. When she sought him out privately, he responded with undisguised hostility:

> "Who the devil are you?" said Mr. Rennie rudely. (003-180)

Tuppence's direct approach caught him off guard. She introduced herself succinctly — and noticed "Mr. Rennie's uncontrollable start" (003-181), a physical reaction that suggested he recognized her name or feared the implications of her visit.

During their exchange, Rennie became increasingly uneasy. Tuppence assured him that recovering the pearl was the priority and that "no one in this house is anxious to call in the police" (003-184). This reassurance had a marked effect:

> Mr. Rennie suddenly became confidential. (003-190)

He shifted from hostility to cooperation, though his sudden change in demeanor was itself suspicious. When asked why he suspected [[kingston-bruce]] of involvement, he demurred (003-192), suggesting either genuine uncertainty or a reluctance to incriminate others.

## Personality

Rennie is characterized by his volatility — swinging from rudeness to sudden confiding, from bluster to cooperation. His "uncontrollable start" at Tuppence's introduction hints at a guilty conscience or at least awareness that he was under suspicion. Yet his willingness to eventually talk suggests he may have been more frightened than criminal.

> "Clearly," said Mr. Rennie, "you don't want to ask me to dine." (003-200)

## Key Relationships

- **[[tuppence-beresford]]** — Rennie's primary interrogator. Tuppence's blend of directness and reassurance proved effective in breaking through his initial hostility.
- **[[kingston-bruce]]** — [[kingston-bruce|Colonel Kingston Bruce]], Rennie's host on the evening of the theft and one of those who viewed him with suspicion.
- **[[kingston-bruce]]** — Beatrice Kingston Bruce, whom Rennie was reluctant to implicate despite apparent grounds for suspicion (003-192).
- **[[tommy-beresford]]** — Though Tommy was less directly involved in questioning Rennie, he monitored the situation and drew conclusions from Rennie's behavior.

## Appearances

Mr. Rennie appears exclusively in Chapter 3, "[[finessing-the-king|Finessing the King]]." He is present during the dinner party, departs after coffee, and is later questioned privately by Tuppence at his own residence. His final appearance in the chapter comes when he returns to the Kingston Bruce house late in the evening, his door having "opened once more to admit Mr. Rennie" (003-216), a reappearance that proves significant to the resolution of the case.
