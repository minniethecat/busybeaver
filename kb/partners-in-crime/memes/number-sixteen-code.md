## Overview

The Number Sixteen Code is the intelligence mechanism at the heart of the detective agency's secret purpose. When Tommy and Tuppence take over the [[international-detective-agency|International Detective Agency]], [[mr-carter|Mr. Carter]] instructs them to watch for blue letters with Russian stamps. When the stamp is moistened, the number 16 appears written underneath — a coded signal indicating that the letter contains intelligence communications.

## Operational Protocol

[[mr-carter|Mr. Carter]]'s instructions are precise. The Beresfords are to "moisten the stamp and you'll find the number 16 written underneath. Make a copy of these letters and send the originals on to me" (001-048). Additionally, "if anyone comes to the office and makes a reference to the number 16, inform me immediately" (001-048).

The code thus operates on two levels: written communications (the hidden stamp marking) and verbal recognition (visitors who know the signal phrase). This dual-channel system allows the intelligence network to function through the legitimate cover of the detective agency.

## Plot Function

The number sixteen serves as a recurring motif that connects the episodic cases to the overarching intelligence plot. Throughout the novel, references to the number appear at critical junctures, reminding the reader that beneath the detective work lies a deeper layer of international intrigue.

The climax of the novel explicitly references the code in its title: "[[man-who-was-no-16|The Man Who Was No. 16]]" (Chapter 17). This final case brings the intelligence thread to its resolution, revealing the identity of the mastermind behind the spy network.

## Significance

The Number Sixteen Code exemplifies how Christie integrates espionage elements into the detective story format. The code transforms the detective agency from a simple commercial enterprise into a front for national security work, raising the stakes of each investigation and providing narrative coherence across the otherwise episodic structure.
