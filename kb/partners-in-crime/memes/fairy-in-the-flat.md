## Overview

"A Fairy in the Flat" marks the beginning of the Beresfords' professional detective careers. After six years of comfortable but dull married life, [[tuppence-beresford|Tuppence Beresford]] yearns for adventure. Her wish is granted when [[mr-carter|Mr. Carter]], their former chief from the [[british-secret-service|Secret Service]], visits with a proposal: take over a defunct detective agency as a cover for counter-espionage work.

## Background

By 1922, Tommy and Tuppence have been married for six years. Tommy works a desk job in the [[british-secret-service|Secret Service]] while Tuppence manages their flat. One afternoon, Tuppence complains of boredom, famously declaring "I wish something would happen" (001-004). Tommy attempts to distract her with a photograph that, under magnification, appears to show a fairy-like shape on the fender — a whimsical detail that gives the chapter its title (001-012).

## The Proposal

[[mr-carter|Mr. Carter]] arrives and outlines his plan. The [[international-detective-agency|International Detective Agency]], run by one [[theodore-blunt|Theodore Blunt]], has been shut down after its proprietor was detained by [[scotland-yard|Scotland Yard]]. The agency at [[118-haleham-street|118 Haleham Street]] is available, and [[mr-carter|Mr. Carter]] proposes that Tommy take over as "[[theodore-blunt|Mr. Blunt]]" for a six-month trial (001-020).

The real purpose is counter-intelligence: the Beresfords are to watch for blue letters with Russian stamps from a "ham merchant" seeking his missing wife. These letters contain coded messages; the number 16 written under the stamp is the authentication signal (001-025). Anyone mentioning the number 16 at the office is to be reported immediately.

## Outcome

Tommy and Tuppence accept the assignment. Tommy takes leave from his office job on grounds of ill health, and the couple prepare to assume their new identities as [[theodore-blunt|Theodore Blunt]] and his confidential secretary. The stage is set for the cases that follow.

## Significance

This chapter establishes the framework for the entire novel. The detective agency serves as both a genuine business (Tuppence is eager to try real detective work) and a front for intelligence operations. The dual nature of the agency — part legitimate business, part [[british-secret-service|Secret Service]] outpost — underpins every subsequent case.
