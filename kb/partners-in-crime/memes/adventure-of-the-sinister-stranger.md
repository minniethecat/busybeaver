## Overview

"The Adventure of the Sinister Stranger" is the first direct engagement with the espionage plot that underlies the Agency's existence. When the long-awaited blue letter with a Russian stamp arrives, Tommy and Tuppence find themselves targeted by a sophisticated foreign intelligence ring — and Tommy must use his wits to summon help while held captive.

## The Blue Letter Arrives

On a quiet afternoon, the Agency receives the coded letter [[mr-carter|Mr. Carter]] had predicted. It purports to be from one Gregor Feodorsky, a ham merchant anxious for news of his refugee wife, but is written in careful stilted English — a clear code (004-002). Tommy verifies the authentication: the number 16 written under the stamp (004-004).

Before they can forward the letter, a burly stranger appears in the doorway — a man of "commanding presence, squarely built, with a very round head and a powerful jaw" (004-006). He introduces himself as [[inspector-dymchurch|Inspector Dymchurch]] from [[scotland-yard|Scotland Yard]], investigating the coded communication.

## The Kidnapping

[[inspector-dymchurch|Dymchurch]], soon revealed as an impostor, takes Tommy captive at gunpoint while Tuppence is sent away with the letter. Tommy is bound and gagged in the Agency office. The captors — [[inspector-dymchurch|Dymchurch]] and his brutal accomplice Coggins — demand that Tommy write to Tuppence, commanding her to bring the letter (004-010).

Under duress, Tommy writes the note — but signs it "Francis," a name only he and Tuppence would recognize from the inscription "Francis from Tuppence" on his cigarette case. The wrong signature is the danger signal (004-014).

## The Rescue

Tuppence, recognizing the false name, alerts [[inspector-marriot|Inspector Marriot]] — the genuine article — and sets a trap. She arrives at the office with an empty envelope while Marriot and two armed men enter through the connecting door. [[inspector-dymchurch|Dymchurch]], Coggins, and their accomplice Vassily are arrested at gunpoint (004-020).

Tommy, freed from his bonds, greets Tuppence with "Well done, brother Francis" — a private joke that [[inspector-dymchurch|Dymchurch]], unfamiliar with the classics, cannot comprehend (004-022).

## Significance

This case demonstrates the real danger behind the Agency's cover operation. It also establishes the Beresfords' method: Tuppence's quick thinking and Tommy's sangfroid under pressure combine to outmaneuver professional adversaries. The "Francis" gambit would not work without the couple's intimate knowledge of each other.
