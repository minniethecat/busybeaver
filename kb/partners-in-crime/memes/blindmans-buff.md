## Overview

"Blindman's Buff" is the climactic confrontation with the foreign espionage ring that has been targeting the Agency. After [[mr-carter|Mr. Carter]] warns that the enemy knows Tommy is not the real [[theodore-blunt|Theodore Blunt]], Tommy and Tuppence play-act as the blind detective Thornley Colton — a rehearsal that becomes chillingly prophetic when Tommy is abducted and forced to play a literal game of Blindman's Buff with death.

## The Warning

[[mr-carter|Mr. Carter]] telephones with an urgent warning: the espionage ring has discovered Tommy's imposture as [[theodore-blunt|Theodore Blunt]]. [[mr-carter|The Chief]] urges Tuppence to go home for her safety, but she refuses — she likes excitement, and business has been slack lately (008-001). Tommy, ever the enthusiast, proposes detective exercises: "Reproductions of the Great Masters." He dons a dark green eyeshade to play Thornley Colton, the blind problemist (008-003).

## The Abduction

While Tuppence is out for lunch with a contact, the espionage ring strikes. Tommy is abducted at gunpoint from the Agency office and taken to a house in an alley. His captor, known only as "the Duke," has prepared an ingenious trap: a metal floor embedded with electrified knobs. If Tommy steps on one, he dies. The game: cross the room blindfolded. "Blindman's Buff with death" (008-008).

## The Escape

Tommy, facing seemingly certain death, recalls his earlier rehearsal. The eyeshade is a fake — he can see perfectly well. But he doesn't trust the Duke to play fair, so he deploys two secret weapons: a sword-stick instead of a hollow cane, and a strip of magnesium wire that creates a blinding flash when ignited (008-012).

The flash temporarily blinds the Duke, and Tommy holds him at sword-point. In a rage, the Duke charges forward — and steps on one of his own electrified knobs, dying instantly (008-013). Tommy navigates the electrified floor with care, exits the house, and phones Tuppence from the nearest telephone box.

## Aftermath

[[albert|Albert]], who had been following in a taxi, alerted the police. Tuppence, meanwhile, had been tailing the other conspirators. The case ends with Tommy vowing to write a large cheque to St. Dunstan's, the charity for blinded veterans — a gesture of gratitude for his sight (008-014).

## Significance

This chapter is the most tense and dangerous in the entire collection. Tommy's survival depends not on physical strength but on preparation (the sword-stick), improvisation (magnesium wire), and psychological manipulation. The fake-blindfold deception, rehearsed earlier as a parlour game, becomes the key to his survival.
