## Overview

Lawrence St. Vincent is a client of the [[international-detective-agency]] who appears in Chapter 2, the [[sunningdale-mystery]]. A well-connected young man and heir to a considerable fortune, he seeks [[tommy-beresford]] and [[tuppence-beresford]]'s help after his fiancée vanishes under mysterious circumstances at [[sunningdale|Sunningdale]].

## Background

St. Vincent is introduced as "the nephew and heir of the Earl of Cheriton" (002-116), placing him firmly in the upper echelons of society. He is young, earnest, and deeply concerned about his missing fiancée. Tuppence, adopting her professional persona, questioned him with soft precision about the disappearance (002-088).

His fiancée was described as "young — and — and — awfully good looking" (002-089), and St. Vincent displayed "lively concern" (002-092) when discussing what might have happened to her. His distress was genuine, though Tuppence noted a certain "cheerfulness which depressed Mr. St. Vincent horribly" (002-093) in her own demeanor — she was already forming theories about the disappearance.

## The Sunningdale Case

The disappearance occurred at [[sunningdale|Sunningdale]], the same golfing locale that later features in [[captain-sessle|Captain Sessle]]'s murder case. St. Vincent's fiancée vanished after a round of golf, leaving behind no clear explanation. The circumstances were suspicious, and St. Vincent feared foul play.

Tuppence's investigation revealed that the disappearance was not what it seemed. The fiancée had not been abducted or harmed — she had been deliberately separated from St. Vincent as part of a scheme involving his inheritance. The resolution satisfied St. Vincent, though the experience left him shaken.

## Key Relationships

- **The Earl of Cheriton** — St. Vincent's uncle, whose fortune he stood to inherit. This connection made St. Vincent a target for financial schemers.
- **Janet St. Vincent** — A relative mentioned in connection with Montgomery Jones's case (013-004), suggesting the St. Vincent family moves in interconnected social circles.

## Personality

St. Vincent is described as somewhat naive and easily flustered — he "turned a brick red" (002-100) when questioned pointedly. His genuine affection for his fiancée is evident, as is his helplessness in the face of the mystery. He represents the type of wealthy, well-meaning client the agency frequently serves.

## Appearances

Lawrence St. Vincent appears in Chapter 2, the [[sunningdale-mystery]], and is referenced in passing in later chapters as part of the Beresfords' growing network of satisfied clients.
