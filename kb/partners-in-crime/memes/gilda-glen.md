## Overview

Gilda Glen was one of the most celebrated actresses on the British stage during the 1920s. Universally recognised for her beauty, she had starred in major theatrical successes including *The Secret of the Heart* and *Pillars of Fire*, commanding an unparalleled hold on the British public. Behind her glamorous public persona, however, lay a carefully concealed lower-middle-class origin — and a web of romantic entanglements that would ultimately lead to her murder (009-012).

## Background

Despite her lavish public image, Gilda Glen came from humble beginnings. She was raised in a respectable but unremarkable household of the lower-middle class — a fact she guarded fiercely throughout her career. [[tommy-beresford|Tommy Beresford]], upon discovering her true origins, marvelled at the contrast: "So it was from this lower middle class respectability that Gilda Glen had sprung. How well she had guarded her secret!" (009-210) Her beauty had been a fixture of the London stage for many years; Tommy himself recalled being taken to see her perform as a small boy (009-210).

## Personality

Gilda Glen was characterised by an aloof, almost disdainful manner in social settings — she took "not the faintest notice" of those she deemed beneath her attention (009-019). Contemporaries noted a stark incongruity between her angelic appearance and her rumoured intellectual shallowness: she was widely reported to be the most beautiful woman in England, and equally widely rumoured to be the stupidest (009-012). Beneath the surface glamour, however, she appears to have been emotionally entangled in several intense relationships, suggesting a more complex private life than her public image allowed.

## Role in "The Crackler"

Gilda Glen is the central figure — and ultimately the victim — in the case known as "[[the-crackler]]" (Chapter 10). She is introduced to [[tommy-beresford]] and [[tuppence-beresford]] by a mutual acquaintance, Mr. Estcourt, who takes evident pride in being seen in her company (009-013).

Shortly after this introduction, she is found murdered. The investigation reveals a tangled web of romantic connections: the pacifist poet [[james-reilly]] had worshipped her and threatened violence if she married another man (009-067); she was also connected to a man named Leconbury; and, critically, she had a husband whose existence she had concealed. This husband, a police constable, confronted her outside her lodgings on the night of her death — and, as Tommy ultimately deduces, killed her in a fit of rage (009-169).
