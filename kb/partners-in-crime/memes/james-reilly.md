## Overview

James Reilly is a red-haired man who becomes a key figure in one of the cases investigated by the [[international-detective-agency|International Detective Agency]]. A poet by profession, Reilly is described as having written verses that "you may have heard" (009-063). He becomes entangled in a forgery investigation when his unexpected departure from a locked house draws the suspicion of [[tommy-beresford|Tommy Beresford]].

## Background

Reilly presents himself as a poet and a gentleman, though his literary reputation appears modest. When he introduces himself, he offers his name with the expectation of recognition: "James Reilly. You may have heard it. I wrote those——" (009-063). His red hair is a distinguishing physical characteristic that Tommy later uses as a key observation point in solving the case.

## Role in the Investigation

The case reaches its climax when Reilly is observed leaving a house that had been under surveillance. "Towards the front door opened and James Reilly came rushing down the steps" (009-107). Crucially, he had been the only person inside the house at the time and "no one but James Reilly had left it" (009-165).

Reilly is subsequently apprehended and implicated in the forgery scheme. [[inspector-marriot|Inspector Marriot]], who has known Reilly for years, expresses genuine shock at the revelation: "I never would have believed it of James Reilly. He's always——" (009-183).

## The Resolution

Tommy's observation of a critical detail proves decisive. He asks pointedly: "That was James Reilly. Didn't you notice what a high——" (009-207), drawing attention to a physical characteristic that exposes the deception. The case reveals that Reilly was not acting alone and that his poetic credentials served as a cover for more criminal activities.

## Character Analysis

James Reilly exemplifies a recurring pattern in the Beresfords' cases: the suspect who appears cultured and respectable but conceals criminal intentions. His identity as a poet — a man of letters and sensibility — makes the revelation of his guilt all the more striking, and [[inspector-marriot|Inspector Marriot]]'s personal disappointment underscores the theme of betrayed trust that runs through several of the agency's investigations.
