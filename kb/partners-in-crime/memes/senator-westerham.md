## Overview

Senator Westerham is an American political figure whose name surfaces during the investigation of a case involving a switched traveling bag. He is mentioned by a client who "ran up against Senator Westerham" (016-020) and inadvertently became involved in a chain of events that leads to the [[international-detective-agency|International Detective Agency]].

## The Ambassador's Boots Case

The case begins when a bag belonging to [[mr-wilmott|the American Ambassador]] is switched with another. Senator Westerham's name enters the investigation through a casual encounter: a client reports that "it chanced, I ran up against Senator Westerham, and I happened to mention the matter" (016-020). The senator's involvement appears incidental, but the mention of his name raises suspicion — "why use Senator Westerham's name? It's a crazy business" (016-022).

## The Valet Connection

A significant figure in the case is a man described as the senator's valet. The suspect is observed with a traveling bag that "belonged to Senator Westerham" (016-066), and witnesses identify him as "Senator Westerham's valet" (016-068). This man "left [[mr-wilmott|Mr. Wilmott's]]" (016-068) under suspicious circumstances, becoming a person of interest in the switched-bag investigation.

## The Resolution

[[tommy-beresford|Tommy's]] investigation reveals that the valet's identity is a fabrication, and the real intrigue involves other parties entirely. Senator Westerham himself emerges as an unwitting bystander whose reputation was borrowed by the actual perpetrators. The senator's involvement proves largely tangential — his name and valet are used by others to create confusion around the bag switch.

## Significance

Senator Westerham represents a category of character in the Beresfords' cases: the prominent person whose name is invoked to lend credibility to a deception. Similar to how Tommy himself operates under an assumed identity via the [[international-detective-agency]], the perpetrators in this case exploit the senator's public standing. The episode illustrates the novel's recurring interest in doubles, impersonation, and the gap between public reputation and private reality.
