## Overview

One of the most distinctive features of *[[partners-in-crime-novel|Partners in Crime]]* is its treatment of role-playing not as comic digression but as a legitimate investigative methodology. Tommy Beresford's [[detective-method-acting|detective method acting]] and Tuppence's tactical self-presentations are not merely entertaining — they constitute a coherent theory of detection in which the adoption of alternative identities becomes a tool for uncovering truth. This essay examines how role-playing functions as method across the novel's cases.

## The Theory Behind the Performance

The premise of Tommy's method acting is deceptively simple: by adopting the identity of a fictional detective, he gains access to that detective's cognitive framework. A Holmes-inspired Tommy sees different things than a Thorndyke-inspired Tommy because he is looking for different things. This is not mysticism but a practical application of the insight that observation is theory-laden — what you see depends on what you expect to see. By deliberately shifting his expectations through persona adoption, Tommy expands the range of what he can notice.

## Tuppence's Complementary Approach

Where Tommy's role-playing is systematic and book-derived, [[tuppence-beresford|Tuppence]]'s is situational and intuitive. She does not consult a library of detective fiction before a case; instead, she reads the social dynamics of the situation and presents whatever version of herself is most useful — sympathetic listener, skeptical interrogator, harmless young woman. Her role-playing is closer to what sociologists call "impression management" — the everyday performance of self that everyone engages in, heightened and made deliberate for investigative purposes.

## The Agency as Theatre

The [[international-detective-agency|International Detective Agency]] itself is a theatrical production. The office at [[118-haleham-street|118 Haleham Street]] is a set, complete with "artistically labeled files" (002-003) that contain nothing. Tommy is playing the role of [[theodore-blunt|Theodore Blunt]], agency manager. The clients who walk through the door are, knowingly or not, participating in a drama in which the Beresfords are simultaneously actors and directors. This theatrical framing does not undermine the seriousness of the work — it enables it. The performance creates the conditions under which detection can occur.

## From Performance to Truth

The novel's most striking claim is that performance leads to truth. Tommy's adoption of fictional personas does not distance him from reality; it brings him closer to it. The detective who tries to be "just himself" is limited by the blind spots of a single perspective. The detective who deliberately adopts multiple perspectives sees more. This is a sophisticated epistemological claim disguised as a comic device: that objectivity is not achieved by removing the self but by multiplying it.

## Christie's Innovation

In making role-playing central to her detectives' methodology, Christie was doing something genuinely innovative within the genre. Previous detectives had used disguise tactically (Holmes's personas, for example), but none had made the systematic adoption of different detective *philosophies* the core of their method. The technique also solves a structural problem: how to write a series of linked short stories without the sameness that afflicts many series detectives. By varying Tommy's persona, Christie varies the narrative mode of each story, giving the collection a variety that is unusual in the genre.
