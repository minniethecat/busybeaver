## Overview

118 Haleham Street is the address of the [[international-detective-agency|International Detective Agency]], the Bloomsbury office that serves as the operational headquarters for Tommy and Tuppence's detective work. Located on the second floor of a "somewhat dilapidated building in Bloomsbury" (002-002), the office becomes the central stage for the novel's many investigations.

## Layout

The office is divided into three rooms. The small outer office is manned by [[albert|Albert]], the office boy, who delays prospective clients with "various artistic devices" while Tommy and Tuppence observe through peepholes (008-001). Two doors lead from the outer office: one marked "Clerks" and the other marked "Private."

Behind the Private door is Tommy's office: a small comfortable room furnished with "an immense businesslike desk, a lot of artistically labeled files, all empty, and some solid leather-seated chairs" (002-003). A telephone stands at Tommy's elbow for rehearsed theatrical effects.

The Clerks room is Tuppence's domain, containing a typewriter, "the necessary tables and chairs of an inferior type to those in the room of the great Chief, and a gas ring for making tea" (002-005).

## The Peephole System

The office features "cunningly contrived peepholes" through which Tommy and Tuppence can observe visitors in the outer office (002-009). This system allows them to assess clients before committing to an interview and is part of the theatrical apparatus they maintain to project professional competence.

## Operational Function

At 118 Haleham Street, the Beresfords receive clients, conduct interviews, stage telephone conversations with fictional [[scotland-yard|Scotland Yard]] contacts, and coordinate their investigations. The office is also the site of critical confrontations — including the takedown of a spy ring when [[inspector-marriot|Inspector Marriot]] and armed men enter through Tuppence's office door (007-016).

## Significance

The office is more than a setting; it is a stage where Tommy and Tuppence perform their detective roles. The empty files, the typing sounds, and the peephole system are all props in an elaborate theatrical production designed to convince clients of the agency's professionalism while masking its intelligence-gathering purpose.
