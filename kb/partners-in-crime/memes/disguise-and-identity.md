## Overview

Disguise and identity run through every case in the collection, from the Agency's founding premise (Tommy impersonating [[theodore-blunt|Theodore Blunt]]) to the final unmasking of No. 16 (disguised as [[mrs-van-snyder|Mrs. Van Snyder]]). The stories explore the permeability of identity — how easily it can be assumed, how fragile it proves, and how truth eventually surfaces.

## The Agency as Disguise

The [[international-detective-agency|International Detective Agency]] itself is a disguise — a front for [[british-secret-service|Secret Service]] counter-intelligence work. Tommy's adoption of the name [[theodore-blunt|Theodore Blunt]] is the foundational impersonation from which all others derive. The "artistically labelled files, all empty" are the props of this performance (002-003). Tuppence takes the role of "[[miss-robinson|Miss Robinson]], confidential secretary" with equal theatrical commitment.

## Disguises in Practice

Tommy dons a Roman Catholic priest's collar at [[adlington-hall|Adlington Hall]] (009-001). He plays the blind detective Thornley Colton with a fake eyeshade (008-003). Tuppence poses as a beauty salon customer to infiltrate Cyclamen Ltd. (016-015). Each disguise is a tool that opens doors closed to the Beresfords' authentic selves.

## The Enemy's Disguises

The antagonists are equally adept at deception. The espionage ring uses false identities. [[sir-arthur-merivale|Sir Arthur Merivale]] hides behind a newspaper costume at the masked ball. The superspy No. 16 is "a past master in the art of disguise," capable of passing as multiple nationalities (017-001). The final confrontation literalizes the theme: Tommy tears off No. 16's wig to reveal the enemy beneath.

## Identity and Marriage

Beneath the disguises lies the question of authentic identity. Who are Tommy and Tuppence when they are not playing roles? The answer, revealed in the final pages, is that they are first and foremost each other's partners. The disguises fall away, and what remains is "Tommy and Tuppence" — a compound identity stronger than any impersonation.

## Significance

The theme of disguise reflects the post-War context in which the stories were written. The Great War had disrupted identities across Europe — borders redrawn, empires fallen, populations displaced. In a world where anyone could be anyone, the ability to see through disguises became a survival skill. The Beresfords' detective work is, at its deepest level, a search for authentic identity in an age of masks.
