## Overview

**Mrs. Cortlandt Van Snyder**, commonly referred to as **Mrs. Van Snyder**, is a wealthy American widow from Detroit who appears in Chapter 17, "[[man-who-was-no-16|The Man Who Was No. 16]]." She is a guest at the same hotel where [[tommy-beresford|Tommy Beresford]], impersonating [[theodore-blunt|Theodore Blunt]], investigates the final case of the novel. Her suite becomes a focal point of the investigation.

## Identity and Background

Mrs. Van Snyder is introduced as "Mrs. Cortlandt Van Snyder of Detroit" (017-106), establishing her as an American socialite. She is depicted as a lady of means, travelling abroad. Her name appears repeatedly throughout the climactic sequences of the final chapter, where she becomes entangled in the mystery surrounding Room No. 16.

## Role in the "Man Who Was No. 16" Case

### The Hotel Suite

Mrs. Van Snyder's hotel suite plays a pivotal role in the investigation. [[tommy-beresford|Tommy Beresford]], in his guise as the famed detective [[theodore-blunt|Theodore Blunt]], must enter her rooms as part of his investigation into the disappearance of a previous occupant of Room No. 16:

> "And now, what significant fact meets our eyes as we enter the suite of Mrs. Van Snyder." (017-184)

The narrative suggests that her suite is positioned in a way that connects to the central mystery, with Tommy and his team entering it during the final confrontation.

### Reaction to the Investigation

Mrs. Van Snyder is portrayed as a woman unaccustomed to interruption. She protests the investigators' intrusion into her room:

> Mrs. Van Snyder's shrill protests filled the air as Tommy "disregarded" them and proceeded with his search. (017-145)

Her reactions range from astonishment to indignation. When the investigation reaches her bedroom:

> "They walked straight into Mrs. Van Snyder's bedroom. The lady was still protesting." (017-199)

### The Climactic Confrontation

In the final moments of the chapter, Mrs. Van Snyder is forcibly removed from her hiding place:

> "Protesting shrilly, Mrs. Van Snyder was hauled from her place of concealment." (017-204)

> "With an unexpected gesture, he seized Mrs. Van Snyder by her elaborately dressed hair." (017-208)

At one point she exclaims, "You've gone plumb crazy," (017-201) revealing her American vernacular and her shock at the unfolding events.

### Victim or Accomplice?

[[tommy-beresford|Tommy Beresford]] initially questions whether Mrs. Van Snyder is an accomplice in the criminal scheme:

> "I wonder now. Is this Mrs. Van Snyder an accomplice, or is she—" (017-108)

The narrative later positions her as a "pitied victim," suggesting she was manipulated rather than complicit:

> "Mrs. Van Snyder was to be a pitied victim." (017-203)

## Character Traits

Mrs. Van Snyder is characterised by her loquacity — she is noted as possessing a "tongue" that could not be "deprived of its use" (017-123) — and her tendency toward dramatic protest when confronted. Her American identity is emphasised through her speech patterns and her association with Detroit high society.
