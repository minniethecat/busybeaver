## Overview

**Inspector French** is a detective referenced by [[tuppence-beresford|Tuppence Beresford]] during the investigation of "[[unbreakable-alibi|The Unbreakable Alibi]]" in Chapter 13. He is invoked as a model of methodical police work, his name serving as shorthand for the kind of thorough, plodding investigation that uncovers minute flaws in criminal schemes.

## References in the Text

### Tuppence's Invocation

When discussing the case with [[tommy-beresford|Tommy Beresford]], Tuppence cites Inspector French by name:

> "Inspector French," said Tuppence. (013-069)

She repeats the reference with emphasis, as if his methods are well known to both her and Tommy:

> "Inspector French of course," said Tuppence. "He would have—" (013-071)

### Benchmark of Detection

Inspector French is held up as the standard against which detective work is measured. The narrative notes that a particular flaw in the alibi was:

> "The ingenious sort of flaw that Inspector French would have detected." (013-234)

The restaurant where key events unfold is compared to him:

> "The Restaurant was exactly like Inspector French." (013-235)

## Significance

### Detective Archetype

Inspector French represents the methodical, detail-oriented police detective who catches criminals through careful observation of inconsistencies. By invoking his name, Tuppence signals that the solution to the "Unbreakable Alibi" case lies in the kind of painstaking scrutiny of minor details that Inspector French exemplifies.

### Narrative Function

Within the structure of the [[partners-in-crime-novel|Partners in Crime]] stories, where Tommy and Tuppence impersonate various famous fictional detectives of the era, the reference to Inspector French situates the Beresfords' investigation within the broader tradition of detective fiction. His name serves as a touchstone for competent, systematic police work against which their own more theatrical methods can be measured.
