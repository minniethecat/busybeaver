## Overview

Mr. Montgomery Jones is a client of the [[international-detective-agency]] who appears in Chapter 13, "[[unbreakable-alibi|The Unbreakable Alibi]]." A well-meaning but intellectually limited gentleman, he hires the agency to verify the whereabouts of his love interest, [[una-drake]], who claims to have been in two different cities on the same day. [[tommy-beresford]] and [[tuppence-beresford]] handle his case with professional efficiency.

## Background

Montgomery Jones is described as "not one of the world's best detectives" (013-003) — an understated way of noting his lack of mental acuity. He is financially comfortable and able to afford private detective services, but struggles to articulate his problem clearly.

When he first arrives at the agency, Jones sits through Tommy's theatrical Blunt persona with visible uncertainty, looking "uncertainly at the third person" (013-019) and struggling to explain his situation. Tuppence observes that "if Mr. Montgomery Jones' troubles were ever to be extracted from him, it would be by feminine tact" (013-037).

## The Case

Jones's predicament involves a young woman named [[una-drake|Una Drake]]. He is infatuated with her and troubled by a confusing circumstance: Una claims to have been in two places simultaneously — an apparent impossibility that Jones finds deeply unsettling. He brings Una's photograph to the agency (013-063) and asks the Beresfords to investigate.

> "It was an absolute shock to me," said Mr. Montgomery Jones, "that a girl could, well — know so little of the world's geography as to make such a claim." (013-039)

Jones is not capable of conducting the investigation himself: "I shouldn't be any good at disguising myself" (013-057), he admits. The agency's professional handling of the matter impresses him, and he departs in relief after handing over the case.

The resolution of the case reveals that Una's "unbreakable alibi" was a playful test she devised to gauge Jones's devotion — her sister had gone to the other location, not Una herself. The Beresfords suggest Jones will be "in such a state of" delight upon learning the truth (013-237).

## Personality

Montgomery Jones is earnest, sincere, and entirely lacking in guile. His simplicity makes him an easy client for the Beresfords, who handle his case with professional efficiency while privately amused by his helplessness. He is generous, paying a substantial fee for the agency's services (013-226).

## Appearances

Jones appears exclusively in Chapter 13, "[[unbreakable-alibi|The Unbreakable Alibi]]," where his romantic predicament provides the Beresfords with their case for the chapter.
