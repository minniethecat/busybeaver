## Overview

"The Red House" is the continuation and resolution of the case introduced in "[[clergymans-daughter|The Clergyman's Daughter]]." Tommy and Tuppence travel to Stourton in the Marsh to investigate the poltergeist disturbances at [[monica-deane|Monica Deane]]'s inherited property. What appears to be supernatural turns out to be a treasure hunt — and the Beresfords arrive on Christmas Eve.

## The Investigation

At the village inn, Tommy and Tuppence review the facts. Tommy, in his usual manner, proposes suspecting the most unlikely person — the invalid mother — before settling on a more logical theory: the late aunt must have hidden money in the house (015-001). A visit to the local bank manager confirms that the previous owner, an eccentric elderly lady, withdrew her entire fortune in gold and bearer bonds shortly before her death (015-005).

The prime suspects are Crockett, the faithful old servant who knew her mistress's peculiarities, and Dr. O'Neill, the "gentlemanly nephew" with gold teeth who has been urging the sale. They know the treasure exists but not its location — hence the poltergeist hoax to frighten Monica into selling (015-008).

## The Search

Tommy and Tuppence search the Red House systematically. The hiding place proves to be not inside the house at all but in the garden — buried in sealed tins beneath the potato patch. After digging up two tins of actual potatoes, the third yields the prize: a velvet bag containing two hundred gold sovereigns, twenty thousand pounds in banknotes, and a magnificent pearl necklace worth another five thousand pounds (015-018).

## Resolution

The fortune transforms Monica's circumstances entirely. She can now marry her sweetheart Gerald, a talented but impoverished engineer, and live "happily ever afterwards." The discovery is made on Christmas Eve, and Tommy and Tuppence celebrate at midnight with the satisfaction of having changed a life (015-022).

## Aftermath

Tuppence reveals how she knew Monica was a clergyman's daughter: a Mr. Deane had been her father's curate, and she remembered a little girl called Monica. The case is one of the Agency's most gratifying — a purely benevolent investigation with no criminal prosecution, only a young woman's future secured.

## Significance

The Red House case represents the apogee of Tuppence's method: intuition, empathy, and persistence over systematic deduction. Her personal connection to Monica — both daughters of clergymen — drives the investigation. The buried-treasure resolution, while less dramatic than a murder, is arguably more satisfying: the Agency has created happiness rather than merely restored order.
