## Overview

Sunningdale is a district in Berkshire, best known for its golf course. In the [[partners-in-crime-novel|Partners in Crime]] stories, it lends its name to one of the Agency's most intricate murder investigations: the [[sunningdale-mystery|Sunningdale Mystery]], in which [[lady-merivale|Lady Merivale]] is murdered under circumstances that appear to implicate [[captain-hale|Captain Hale]].

## Physical Description

Sunningdale is situated in the stockbroker belt of Berkshire, a region of affluent commuter villages and well-tended leisure facilities. Its defining feature is the golf course — a landscaped expanse that serves as the social hub for the district's residents. The area's reputation for respectability and quiet wealth makes the violent crime at its heart all the more shocking to the community. The contrast between Sunningdale's manicured exterior and the brutal reality of murder is central to the case's tension.

## The Crime

A woman dies under suspicious circumstances at Sunningdale. The physical evidence and witness testimony uniformly point to [[captain-hale|Captain Hale]] — known to friends as "Bingo" — as the killer. [[inspector-marriot|Inspector Marriot]] brings the case to the Agency because his professional instincts tell him the evidence is too neat (011-005).

## The Investigation

Marriot provides photographs and evidence for the Beresfords' review. Tuppence becomes convinced the case has been deliberately constructed to frame Hale. Her investigation identifies [[sir-arthur-merivale|Sir Arthur Merivale]], the victim's husband, as the true killer. The motive is financial: [[lady-merivale|Lady Merivale]]'s fortune passed entirely to Sir Arthur upon her death (011-025).

## Appearances

Sunningdale appears in the narrative primarily through the lens of the investigation — Marriot's reports, the photographs he brings to the Agency, and the reconstruction of events that Tuppence undertakes. The location itself is not visited directly by the Beresfords; instead, its geography and social context are mediated through the evidence they review, making Sunningdale a crime scene understood entirely at one remove.

## Significance

Sunningdale represents both a specific crime scene and a type of case: the investigation where surface evidence is perfectly consistent but fundamentally misleading. The name "Sunningdale" — suggesting sunlight and clarity — is ironically at odds with the obscurity of the truth. The case establishes Tuppence's method of questioning evidence that appears too perfect.
