## Overview

"The Unbreakable Alibi" is a purely deductive case with no criminal element — a romantic puzzle brought to the Agency by the amiable but inarticulate [[montgomery-jones|Montgomery Jones]]. His fiancée, [[una-drake|Una Drake]], has made a bet that she can prove she was simultaneously in two places a hundred miles apart. If Montgomery cannot solve the riddle, she will refuse to marry him.

## The Client

[[montgomery-jones|Montgomery Jones]], a tall young man with an "amiable and ingenuous countenance," arrives at the Agency with a peculiar problem (013-003). His fiancée [[una-drake|Una Drake]] claims she can prove she was in London and Torquay at the same time on a specific evening. She has witnesses in both locations who will swear to seeing her.

## The Investigation

Tommy and Tuppence interview witnesses at a restaurant in London and a hotel in Torquay, spending a considerable sum on half-crowns and ten-shilling notes to jog memories. The evidence seems genuinely impossible: [[una-drake|Una Drake]] was seen dining in London at the very hour she was observed at a hotel in Torquay (013-008).

## The Solution

Tuppence, inspired by Tommy's earlier ramblings about "mothers-in-law, twins, and bottles of beer," cables Australia and receives the answer: Una has a twin sister, Vera, who arrived in England the previous Monday (013-016). The "unbreakable alibi" was a prank — Una in London, Vera in Torquay — designed to test Montgomery's devotion and resourcefulness.

## Aftermath

Montgomery Jones, overjoyed, pays the Agency's bill without a murmur. Tuppence reflects that a wife's respect for her husband's abilities should be the foundation of married life — a sentiment Tommy notes with approval. The case earns [[blunts-brilliant-detectives|Blunt's Brilliant Detectives]] a substantial fee and the satisfaction of having outwitted a particularly elegant puzzle.

## Significance

This is the only case in the collection solved entirely by Tuppence with no physical danger. It showcases her approach: social intuition, willingness to spend money on information, and the flash of lateral thinking (twins!) that eludes more methodical minds. The solution's simplicity — once stated — is its hallmark.
