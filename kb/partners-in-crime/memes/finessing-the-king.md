## Overview

"Finessing the King" begins with a curious personal advertisement in the *Daily Leader*: "I should go three hearts. Twelve tricks. Ace of Spades. Necessary to finesse the King." The bridge-themed message draws Tommy and Tuppence to a masked costume ball at the Three Arts Club, where they witness a murder and become entangled in a deadly love triangle.

## The Advertisement

Tommy notices a peculiar detail in the *Daily Leader*: the newspaper's masthead contains tiny white dots that change position each day — a secret journalistic identifier (005-006). More intriguing is a personal advertisement couched in bridge terminology. Tuppence, an enthusiastic bridge player, decodes it as a rendezvous: the Three Arts Club at a specific table, wearing Heart costumes (005-010).

## The Masked Ball

At the ball, Tommy dresses as the Ace of Spades while Tuppence goes as the Queen of Hearts. In the booth next to theirs, they hear a woman dressed as the Queen of Hearts with a man dressed as the King. A cry, followed by a man's laughter, prompts Tuppence to investigate (005-022).

She finds the woman stabbed with a jewelled dagger. Before dying, the victim whispers: "Bingo did it." The woman is identified as [[lady-merivale|Lady Merivale]], and "Bingo" is [[captain-hale|Captain Hale]], a friend of her husband [[sir-arthur-merivale|Sir Arthur Merivale]] (005-025).

## Key Figures

- **[[tommy-beresford|Tommy Beresford]]** (as [[theodore-blunt|Theodore Blunt]]): Investigates the coded message, attending the masked ball in disguise to identify the sender.
- **[[tuppence-beresford|Tuppence Beresford]]**: Assists Tommy with the interpretation of the advertisement and the social dimensions of the case.
- **[[kingston-bruce|Colonel Kingston Bruce]]**: A key figure connected to the case — his military background and social standing are relevant to the events that unfold at the masked ball.
- **The Dying Man**: The sender of the coded advertisement, whose cryptic message sets the investigation in motion.

## The Investigation

[[inspector-marriot|Inspector Marriot]] arrests [[captain-hale|Captain Hale]] on the strength of the dying declaration. However, [[sir-arthur-merivale|Sir Arthur Merivale]] is incredulous — Hale was his closest friend. Tuppence suspects the case is too neat and begins her own investigation, identifying Sir Arthur as the true killer through a second coded advertisement (005-028).

## The Confrontation

In a dramatic scene at the Agency office, Tuppence confronts [[sir-arthur-merivale|Sir Arthur Merivale]]. Trapped by an arrangement that includes Marriot in the adjoining room, Sir Arthur chooses suicide over arrest, vaulting through the window to his death (005-030). The motive: [[lady-merivale|Lady Merivale]]'s fortune passed entirely to her husband; had she run away with Hale, Sir Arthur would have lost everything.

## Significance

The case's bridge metaphor — "finessing the King" — proves tragically apt. Sir Arthur attempts to force a winning play by framing Hale, but Tuppence's counter-finesse exposes him. The chapter title's double meaning (finesse as both a bridge term and a social skill) encapsulates Tuppence's method: social intelligence applied to crime.
