## Overview

Mrs. Honeycott is a key witness in Chapter 9, "[[case-of-the-missing-lady|The Case of the Missing Lady]]." She runs the boarding house where the murder of [[gilda-glen|Gilda Glen]] takes place, and her testimony provides [[tommy-beresford]] with crucial information about the crime.

## Background

Mrs. Honeycott operates a boarding house that serves as the setting for one of the agency's murder investigations. She is described as a sharp, observant woman who "gave him a sharp glance" (009-122) when first meeting Tommy. Her manner is direct and she is not easily intimidated.

## The Gilda Glen Murder

When Tommy arrives at Mrs. Honeycott's establishment, she immediately takes control of the interview: "Sit down. To begin with, you'll excuse my asking — are you connected with the police?" (009-126). She provides a detailed account of the victim's background: [[gilda-glen|Gilda Glen]] was "a girl of seventeen" who had been involved with "some common fellow" (009-137).

Mrs. Honeycott led Tommy upstairs to the scene of the crime, where the body was discovered. Her reaction upon learning of the murder was immediate: "Is she dead?" (009-157). Tommy confirmed: "She's dead. Murdered. The question is — who did it?" (009-158).

> Mrs. Honeycott came to a full stop, and drew a deep breath. "I don't hold with goings-on in my house." (009-127)

## Investigation

Mrs. Honeycott's position in the household made her a natural suspect. She and the maid Ellen "had been the only two people in the house at the time of the murder" (009-169). Tommy listened to "all Mrs. Honeycott's hysterical outbursts" (009-170) while forming his own conclusions.

Crucially, Mrs. Honeycott placed herself in the kitchen at the time of the murder, providing an alibi that required verification. She recalled that her sister "paused before the curtains" (009-205) on the day of the crime, a detail that eventually helped reconstruct the timeline.

## Personality

Mrs. Honeycott is garrulous, opinionated, and protective of her establishment's reputation. Her "hysterical outbursts" (009-170) reveal a woman more concerned with propriety than grief, though her detailed observations prove valuable to the investigation.

## Appearances

Mrs. Honeycott appears in Chapter 9, "[[case-of-the-missing-lady|The Case of the Missing Lady]]," where her boarding house becomes the scene of a murder investigation that tests Tommy's detective skills.
