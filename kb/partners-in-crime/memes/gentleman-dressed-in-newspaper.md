## Overview

"The Gentleman Dressed in Newspaper" is the continuation and resolution of the murder case opened in "[[finessing-the-king|Finessing the King]]." The chapter takes its title from the newspaper-clad costume of the killer at the masked ball. After a sleepless night, Tuppence is called upon to repeat her testimony before [[sir-arthur-merivale|Sir Arthur Merivale]] — and the confrontation produces a startling confession and a fatal conclusion.

## The Morning After

Tuppence, haunted by the image of [[lady-merivale|Lady Merivale]]'s dying face, sleeps fitfully (006-001). At eleven o'clock, [[inspector-marriot|Inspector Marriot]] arrives at [[beresford-flat|the flat]] with [[sir-arthur-merivale|Sir Arthur Merivale]], who refuses to believe his friend [[captain-hale|Captain Hale]] — known to friends as "Bingo" — could have murdered his wife (006-005).

Tuppence gently but firmly confirms the dying woman's words: "Bingo did it" (006-008). Sir Arthur is devastated. Marriot presses his theory that Hale was [[lady-merivale|Lady Merivale]]'s lover — a motive Sir Arthur refuses to credit (006-012).

## The Trap

The scene shifts to the Agency office at Haleham Street. Tuppence has arranged a second meeting, and this time reveals the truth: she and Tommy are not merely witnesses but professional detectives of the [[international-detective-agency|International Detective Agency]] (006-018).

Confronted with the revelation that the Agency office has been a trap — Marriot is concealed in the adjoining room — Sir Arthur's composure breaks. He admits his guilt with a chilling calm: "But you won't get me this time, I'm afraid, Inspector. I prefer to take my own way out" (006-020).

## The Death

Sir Arthur vaults through the office window to his death. Marriot later confirms the motive: [[lady-merivale|Lady Merivale]]'s substantial fortune passed entirely to her husband upon her death. Had she run away with Hale, Sir Arthur would have seen none of it. The case resolves with a grim bridge metaphor: "he finessed the wrong way round" (006-023).

## Aftermath

Marriot, ever practical, advises Tommy to give Tuppence brandy for the shock. Tuppence's observation — that detectives, like tradesmen, know what is real and what is not — proves prescient. The case cements the Beresfords' reputation with [[scotland-yard|Scotland Yard]] and marks the first time Tuppence's intuition directly solves a murder.
