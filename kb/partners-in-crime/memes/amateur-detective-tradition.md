## Overview

The Amateur Detective Tradition is the body of fictional detectives that Tommy and Tuppence draw upon as models and inspiration. Tommy's cupboard of classic detective fiction — containing works by Conan Doyle, Edgar Wallace, and others — provides the Beresfords with a repertoire of methods, disguises, and investigative personas that they adopt throughout their cases.

## The Literary Models

Tommy explicitly identifies and imitates famous literary detectives. He plays Thornley Colton, the blind problemist, in "[[blindmans-buff|Blindman's Buff]]" (008-003). He affects Sherlock Holmes's mannerisms in "[[finessing-the-king|Finessing the King]]," bringing his fingertips together "in the most approved Sherlock Holmes fashion" (005-006). He aspires to the methods of [[inspector-french|Inspector French]], Reggie Fortune, Roger Sheringham, and the Brothers Okewood — though Tuppence often notes that Tommy talks nearly as much as Sheringham but "not nearly so well" (014-001).

## Role-Play as Method

The Beresfords' adoption of fictional detective personas is not mere pastiche. It is a deliberate method: by inhabiting a role, they access modes of thinking and observation that their natural personalities might not reach. Tuppence as "[[miss-robinson|Miss Robinson]], confidential secretary" can ask questions that [[tuppence-beresford|Tuppence Beresford]] could not. Tommy as "[[theodore-blunt|Theodore Blunt]]" commands authority that [[tommy-beresford|Tommy Beresford]], former intelligence officer, would not possess.

## Subversion and Irony

The stories consistently undercut the grandeur of the fictional models. Thornley Colton's blindness is fake — Tommy can see perfectly well. Sherlock Holmes's deductions are sometimes just luck. The amateur detective tradition provides the vocabulary, but the Beresfords speak it with a pronounced ironic accent. They are fans playing at being professionals — and, in the process, becoming professionals in spite of themselves.

## Significance

Christie uses the amateur detective tradition as both homage and gentle satire. The [[partners-in-crime-novel|Partners in Crime]] stories were written in the 1920s, the golden age of detective fiction, and they acknowledge their debt to the genre while maintaining a playful distance. The Beresfords' self-awareness about their role-playing makes them more modern than the models they imitate.
