## Overview

Dennis Radclyffe is the nephew of [[lady-radclyffe|Lady Radclyffe]]'s late husband and a resident of the house where a mysterious poisoning occurs. Described as "the other inmate of the house" (012-050), he becomes the immediate suspect when [[lady-radclyffe|Lady Radclyffe]] falls victim to poisoned food at the [[house-of-lurking-death]]. His position as heir to the estate provides a compelling motive: "He gains by her death" (012-075).

## Background

Dennis Radclyffe lives at the same residence as [[lady-radclyffe|Lady Radclyffe]] and is one of the small household affected by the poisoning incident. The cook's testimony establishes a critical timeline: "Cook is positive Dennis Radclyffe hadn't returned when tea was served" (012-137), making his whereabouts at the time of the poisoning a central question in the investigation.

## The Poisoning Case

When [[lady-radclyffe|Lady Radclyffe]] is poisoned, suspicion immediately falls on Dennis. The case against him appears strong: he stands to inherit, and his movements on the day of the poisoning cannot be fully accounted for. The theory advanced is that "Dennis Radclyffe put the poison in the sandwiches" (012-189) while [[lady-radclyffe|Lady Radclyffe]] was out having tea — an alibi that initially seems to clear him but proves more complicated upon examination.

## Health and Illness

During the investigation, Dennis himself becomes seriously ill. [[tommy-beresford|Tommy Beresford]] receives a report that "Dennis Radclyffe is seriously ill also" (012-096), adding a layer of complexity to the case. The question becomes whether Dennis is a perpetrator who has been caught in his own trap, or another victim of the same poisoner.

## Resolution

The investigation ultimately reveals that Dennis Radclyffe was deliberately framed. The real perpetrator "chose a day when Dennis Radclyffe was out for tea" (012-248) to administer the poison, carefully constructing a timeline that would cast suspicion on the nephew while establishing their own apparent innocence. The case is resolved when Tommy, working with [[tuppence-beresford|Tuppence]], identifies the true culprit through careful analysis of opportunity and motive.

## Character Significance

Dennis Radclyffe represents the wrongly accused suspect — a common figure in detective fiction whom the Beresfords must exonerate. His case illustrates how inheritance and family relationships in English country houses create natural suspicion dynamics that the real criminal exploits. The resolution reinforces the novel's theme that apparent motives can be deliberately misleading, a pattern also seen in the case of [[lois-hargreaves]].
