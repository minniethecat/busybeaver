## Overview

Doris Evans is a key figure in Chapter 11, "[[man-who-was-no-16|The Man Who Was No. 16]]." A young woman arrested and charged with the murder of [[captain-sessle]], she becomes the focus of [[tommy-beresford]]'s investigation when he suspects she has been framed by the real killer.

## Background

Doris Evans was an ordinary young woman whose life was upended when she was "arrested and charged with the murder of [[captain-sessle|Captain Anthony Sessle]]" (011-039). She had been seen near the scene of the crime and was identified by witnesses, leading to her arrest.

## The Case Against Her

According to the prosecution's case, a week after Sessle's murder, Doris Evans was identified as the woman who had "asked the way to [[captain-sessle|Captain Sessle]]'s house" (011-041) on the day of the murder. She was recognized by a cinema attendant who identified her from a photograph.

Doris claimed she had been summoned to meet Sessle. She told investigators that a man approached her at a cinema, claiming to be Sessle, and arranged the meeting (011-110). However, Tommy's investigation revealed a crucial fact: "It wasn't Sessle who met Doris Evans at a Cinema" (011-110) — an impostor had used Sessle's identity to lure her into a trap and frame her for the murder.

> "That is the story that Doris Evans tells — and from which she has never wavered." (011-049)

## Vindication

Tommy planned a confrontation: "We'll confront young [[mr-hollaby|Hollaby]] and Doris Evans and see if she recognizes him" (011-121). The real murderer was exposed as someone who knew Sessle intimately and had impersonated him to cover a financial crime. Doris was innocent — a pawn in a sophisticated murder plot designed to silence [[captain-sessle|Captain Sessle]] before he could expose embezzlement (011-053).

## Personality

Doris Evans's story reveals resilience under extreme pressure. She maintained her innocence consistently, never wavering from her account even as she faced trial for murder. Her experience illustrates the vulnerability of ordinary people caught in criminal schemes beyond their understanding.

## Appearances

Doris Evans appears in Chapter 11, "[[man-who-was-no-16|The Man Who Was No. 16]]," where her wrongful accusation and eventual vindication form the emotional core of the case.
