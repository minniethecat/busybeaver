## Overview

The Sunningdale Mystery is one of the most complex cases handled by the [[international-detective-agency|International Detective Agency]]. A woman is murdered at [[sunningdale|Sunningdale]], and all evidence points to [[captain-hale|Captain Hale]] as the killer. However, [[inspector-marriot|Inspector Marriot]]'s professional instincts tell him the case is not as clear-cut as it appears, and he turns to Tuppence and Tommy for their unconventional perspective.

## Background

The case reaches the Agency through Inspector Marriot, who has been investigating the murder of a woman found dead at Sunningdale. The physical evidence — fingerprints, witness statements, motive — all converge on a single suspect: Captain Anthony "Bingo" Hale. Yet Marriot is troubled by the very perfection of the case. His experience tells him that real crimes are never this neat, and he suspects the evidence has been arranged to point in one direction. Unable to pursue this intuition through official channels, he brings the case files to the Beresfords, hoping their amateur perspective will see what the professionals have missed.

## The Crime

A woman dies under suspicious circumstances at [[sunningdale|Sunningdale]]. The physical evidence and witness testimony all implicate [[captain-hale|Captain Hale]], the primary suspect. Despite the overwhelming case against Hale, [[inspector-marriot|Inspector Marriot]] remains unconvinced, recognizing that professional detectives must "know the real thing when they see it — and they know when it isn't the real thing" (009-010).

## The Investigation

Marriot repeatedly visits the Beresfords, hoping they will recall some overlooked detail or uncover a different angle. He provides photographs and evidence for their review. Tuppence becomes increasingly convinced that the case has been deliberately constructed to frame Hale. She notices a detail that cannot be coincidence — a pattern of coincidences that, taken together, point not to Hale's guilt but to someone else's careful design. Her investigation follows the trail from the crime scene to the financial motives that underpin the murder.

## Resolution

Tuppence identifies [[sir-arthur-merivale|Sir Arthur Merivale]] as the true killer through a deduction that the official investigation had missed. The motive is financial: [[lady-merivale|Lady Merivale]]'s fortune passed entirely to Sir Arthur upon her death (011-025). The resolution involves a confrontation at the Agency office where the real murderer is exposed, and [[inspector-marriot|Inspector Marriot]] arrives to make the arrest, confirming Tuppence's theory.

## Key Characters

- **[[inspector-marriot|Inspector Marriot]]** — The Scotland Yard detective who brings the case to the Agency, trusting the Beresfords' instincts over the weight of evidence
- **[[captain-hale|Captain Anthony "Bingo" Hale]]** — The wrongfully accused suspect, whose acquaintance with the victim makes him the obvious target for a frame-up
- **[[lady-merivale|Lady Merivale]]** — The murder victim, whose substantial independent fortune provides the motive
- **[[sir-arthur-merivale|Sir Arthur Merivale]]** — Lady Merivale's husband, whose financial interest in her death makes him the real killer
- **[[tuppence-beresford|Tuppence Beresford]]** — The investigator who sees through the constructed evidence

## Significance

The Sunningdale Mystery demonstrates Tuppence's growth as a detective. She moves beyond merely assisting Tommy to taking the investigative lead, trusting her instincts over surface-level evidence. The case also illustrates the productive tension between official police methods and the amateur detective's intuitive approach. Marriot's willingness to seek outside help — risking professional embarrassment — underscores the value the Agency brings to cases that are too neatly packaged for conventional investigation.
