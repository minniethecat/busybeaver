### I

[012-001] “What﻿—” began Tuppence, and then stopped.

[012-002] She had just entered the private office of [[theodore-blunt|Mr. Blunt]] from the adjoining one marked “Clerks,” and was surprised to behold her lord and master with his eye riveted to the private peep hole into the outer office.

[012-003] “Ssh,” said Tommy, warningly. “Didn’t you hear the buzzer? It’s a girl﻿—rather a nice girl﻿—in fact she looks to me a frightfully nice girl. [[albert|Albert]] is telling her all that tosh about my being engaged with [[scotland-yard|Scotland Yard]].”

[012-004] “Let *me* see,” demanded Tuppence.

[012-005] Somewhat unwillingly, Tommy moved aside. Tuppence in her turn glued her eye to the peep hole.

[012-006] “She’s not bad,” admitted Tuppence. “And her clothes are simply the latest shout.”

[012-007] “She’s perfectly lovely,” said Tommy. “She’s like those girls Mason writes about﻿—you know, frightfully sympathetic, and beautiful, and distinctly intelligent without being too saucy. I think, yes﻿—I certainly think﻿—I shall be the great Hanaud this morning.”

[012-008] “Hm,” said Tuppence. “If there is one detective out of all the others whom you are most unlike﻿—I should say it was Hanaud. Can you do the lightning changes of personality? Can you be the great comedian, the little gutter boy, the serious and sympathetic friend﻿—all in five minutes?”

[012-009] “I know this,” said Tommy, rapping sharply on the desk, “I am the Captain of the Ship﻿—and don’t you forget it, Tuppence. I’m going to have her in.”

[012-010] He pressed the buzzer on his desk. Albert appeared ushering in the client.

[012-011] The girl stopped in the doorway as though undecided. Tommy came forward.

[012-012] “Come in, Mademoiselle,” he said kindly, “and seat yourself here.”

[012-013] Tuppence choked audibly, and Tommy turned upon her with a swift change of manner. His tone was menacing.

[012-014] “You spoke, [[miss-robinson|Miss Robinson]]? Ah! no, I thought not.”

[012-015] He turned back to the girl.

[012-016] “We will not be serious or formal,” he said. “You will just tell me all about it, and then we will discuss the best way to help you.”

[012-017] “You are very kind,” said the girl. “Excuse me, but are you a foreigner?”

[012-018] A fresh choke from Tuppence. Tommy glared in her direction out of the corner of his eye.

[012-019] “Not exactly,” he said with difficulty. “But of late years I have worked a good deal abroad. My methods are the methods of the Sûreté.”

[012-020] “Oh!” The girl seemed impressed.

[012-021] She was, as Tommy had indicated, a very charming girl. Young and slim, with a trace of golden hair peeping out from under her little brown felt hat, and big serious eyes.

[012-022] That she was nervous could be plainly seen. Her little hands were twisting themselves together, and she kept clasping and unclasping the catch of her lacquer red handbag.

[012-023] “First of all, [[tommy-beresford|Mr. Blunt]], I must tell you that my name is [[lois-hargreaves|Lois Hargreaves]]. I live in a great rambling old fashioned house called Thurnly Grange. It is in the heart of the country. There is the village of Thurnly near by, but it is very small and insignificant. There is plenty of hunting in winter, and we get tennis in summer, and I have never felt lonely there. Indeed I much prefer country to town life.

[012-024] “I tell you this so that you may realise that in a country village like ours, everything that happens is of supreme importance. [[About]] a week ago, I got a box of chocolates sent through the post. There was nothing inside to indicate who they came from. Now I myself am not particularly fond of chocolates, but the others in the house are, and the box was passed around. As a result, everyone who had eaten any chocolates was taken ill. We sent for the doctor, and after various inquiries as to what other things had been eaten, he took the remains of the chocolates away with him, and had them analysed. Mr. Blunt, those chocolates contained arsenic! Not enough to kill anyone, but enough to make anyone quite ill.”

[012-025] “Extraordinary,” commented Tommy.

[012-026] “[[dr-burton|Dr. Burton]] was very excited over the matter. It seems that this was the third occurrence of the kind in the neighborhood. In each case a big house was selected, and the inmates were taken ill after eating the mysterious chocolates. It looked as though some local person of weak intellect was playing a particularly fiendish practical joke.”

[012-027] “Quite so, Miss Hargreaves.”

[012-028] “Dr. Burton put it down to Socialist agitation﻿—rather absurdly, I thought. But there are one or two malcontents in Thurnly village, and it seemed possible that they might have had something to do with it. Dr. Burton was very keen that I should put the whole thing in the hands of the police.”

[012-029] “A very natural suggestion,” said Tommy. “But you have not done so, I gather, Miss Hargreaves?”

[012-030] “No,” admitted the girl. “I hate the fuss and the publicity that would ensue﻿—and you see, I know our local Inspector. I can never imagine him finding out anything! I have often seen your advertisements, and I told Dr. Burton that it would be much better to call in a private detective.”

[012-031] “I see.”

[012-032] “You say a great deal about discretion in your advertisement. I take that to mean﻿—that﻿—that﻿—well, that you would not make anything public without my consent?”

[012-033] Tommy looked at her curiously, but it was Tuppence who spoke.

[012-034] “I think,” she said quietly, “that it would be as well if Miss Hargreaves told us *everything*.”

[012-035] She laid especial stress upon the last word, and Lois Hargreaves flushed nervously.

[012-036] “Yes,” said Tommy quickly. “Miss Robinson is right. You must tell us everything.”

[012-037] “You will not﻿—” she hesitated.

[012-038] “Everything you say is understood to be strictly in confidence.”

[012-039] “Thank you. I know that I ought to have been quite frank with you. I have a reason for not going to the police. Mr. Blunt, that box of chocolates was sent by someone in our house!”

[012-040] “How do you know that, Mademoiselle?”

[012-041] “It’s very simple. I’ve got a habit of drawing a little silly thing﻿—three fish intertwined﻿—whenever I have a pencil in my hand. I’d just been marking something in the newspaper, and without thinking, I began to draw my silly little fish on the label of the parcel before cutting the string and opening it. I thought no more about the matter, but when I was examining the piece of brown paper in which the chocolates had been sent, I caught sight of the corner of the original label﻿—most of which had been torn off. My silly little drawing was on it.”

[012-042] Tommy drew his chair forward.

[012-043] “That is very serious. It creates, as you say, a very strong presumption that the sender of the chocolates is a member of your household. But you will forgive me if I say that I still do not see why that fact should render you indisposed to call in the police?”

[012-044] Lois Hargreaves looked him squarely in the face.

[012-045] “I will tell you, Mr. Blunt. I may want the whole thing hushed up.”

[012-046] Tommy retired gracefully from the position.

[012-047] “In that case,” he murmured, “we know where we are. I see, Miss Hargreaves, that you are not disposed to tell me who it is you suspect?”

[012-048] “I suspect no one﻿—but there are possibilities.”

[012-049] “Quite so. Now will you describe the household to me in detail?”

[012-052] “Just three weeks ago.”

[012-053] “Ah!” said Tommy. “Now will you give me fuller particulars of the members of your household at this minute?”

[012-054] “Servants﻿—or﻿—others?”

[012-055] “Both.”

[012-056] “The servants, as I say, have been with us some time. There is old Mrs. Holloway, the cook, and her niece Rose, the kitchenmaid. [012-057] Tommy thought for a moment.

[012-058] “That all seems fairly clear and straightforward, Miss Hargreaves,” he said after a minute or two. “I take it that you have no special reason for attaching suspicion more to one person than another? You are only afraid it might prove to be﻿—well﻿—not a servant, shall we say?”

[012-059] “That’s it exactly, Mr. Blunt. I have honestly no idea who used that piece of brown paper. The handwriting was printed.”

[012-060] “There seems only one thing to be done,” said Tommy. “I must be on the spot.”

[012-061] The girl looked at him inquiringly.

[012-062] Tommy went on after a moment’s thought.

[012-063] “I suggest that you prepare the way for the arrival of﻿—say, Mr. and Miss Van Dusen﻿—American friends of yours. Will you be able to do that quite naturally?”

[012-064] “Oh! yes. There will be no difficulty at all. When will you come down﻿—tomorrow﻿—or the day after?”

[012-065] “Tomorrow, if you please. There is no time to waste.”

[012-066] “That is settled, then.”

[012-067] The girl rose, and held out her hand.

[012-068] “One thing, Miss Hargreaves, not a word, mind, to anyone﻿—anyone at all, that we are not what we seem.”

[012-069] “What do you think of it, Tuppence?” he asked, when he returned from showing the visitor out.

[012-070] “I don’t like it,” said Tuppence decidedly. “Especially I don’t like the chocolates having so little arsenic in them.”

[012-071] “What *do* you mean?”

[012-072] “Don’t you see? All those chocolates being sent round the neighborhood were a blind. To establish the idea of a local maniac. [012-073] “That was a stroke of luck. You’re right. You think it’s a deliberate plot against the girl herself?”

[012-074] “I’m afraid so. I remember reading about old Lady Radclyffe’s will. That girl has come into a terrific lot of money.”

[012-075] “Yes, and she came of age and made a will three weeks ago. It looks bad﻿—for Dennis Radclyffe. He gains by her death.”

[012-076] Tuppence nodded.

[012-077] “The worst of it is﻿—that she thinks so too! That’s why she won’t have the police called in. Already she suspects him. And she must be more than half in love with him to act as she has done.”

[012-078] “In that case,” said Tommy thoughtfully, “why the devil doesn’t he marry her? Much simpler and safer.”

[012-079] Tuppence stared at him.

[012-080] “You’ve said a mouthful,” she observed. “Oh! boy. I’m getting ready to be Miss Van Dusen, you observe.”

[012-081] “Why rush to crime, where there is a lawful means near at hand?”

[012-082] Tuppence reflected for a minute or two.

[012-083] “I’ve got it,” she announced. “Clearly he must have married a barmaid whilst at Oxford. Origin of the quarrel with his aunt. That explains everything.”

[012-084] “Then why not send poisoned sweets to the barmaid?” suggested Tommy. “Much more practical. I wish you wouldn’t jump to these wild conclusions, Tuppence.”

[012-085] “They’re deductions,” said Tuppence, with a good deal of dignity. “This is your first corrida, my friend, but when you have been twenty minutes in the arena﻿—”

[012-086] Tommy flung the office cushion at her.
