## Overview

Sir Arthur Merivale is the antagonist of Chapter 6, the [[affair-of-the-pink-pearl]]. Initially presenting himself as a wronged husband seeking justice for the theft of his wife's valuable pearl, he is ultimately exposed by [[tuppence-beresford]] as the true culprit — an embezzler who framed his friend [[captain-hale]] to cover his financial crimes.

## Background

Sir Arthur is the husband of Lady Laura Merivale and moves in wealthy social circles. He was introduced to Tuppence by [[inspector-marriot]]: "This is Sir Arthur Merivale" (006-008). On the surface, he appears to be a concerned husband, outraged that his wife's pink pearl has been stolen and eager to see the thief brought to justice.

## The Pink Pearl Scheme

Sir Arthur orchestrated an elaborate scheme to steal his wife's pearl. He suspected his wife of having an affair with [[captain-hale|Captain Bingo Hale]] (006-018), and saw an opportunity to solve two problems at once: eliminate a rival and cover his own embezzlement. The pearl was insured, and Sir Arthur planned to collect the insurance money while sending Hale to prison.

He attended a fancy dress ball where the pearl was stolen, and immediately pointed suspicion at Hale, claiming the Captain had been paying too much attention to Lady Laura (006-014). He insisted he knew Hale was guilty: "I know [[captain-hale|Bingo Hale]]" (006-028).

> "You won't find it," said Sir Arthur. "I know Bingo Hale." (006-028)

However, Tuppence saw through the scheme. She noted that a newspaper costume used in the theft was designed so that Sir Arthur could change identities in "a minute" (006-034) without anyone noticing. She concluded from the start that "Sir Arthur was a bad lot, and that [[captain-hale|Captain Hale]] was all right" (006-104).

## Exposure

When Tuppence presented her findings, Sir Arthur was initially defiant. He challenged her investigation, but when she produced the crucial evidence — the missing pearl found embedded in a newspaper used at the crime scene — Sir Arthur's composure cracked. He read the damning note aloud (006-089), and "a little smile of amusement came to Sir Arthur's lips" (006-095) — the smile of a man caught but still appreciating the cleverness of his opponent.

## Personality

Sir Arthur is superficially charming and polished, but underneath lies a calculating mind. He "sprang angrily to his feet" (006-022) when challenged, revealing a quick temper. His willingness to destroy an innocent friend to save himself marks him as one of the more morally bankrupt characters the Beresfords encounter.

## Appearances

Sir Arthur Merivale appears in Chapter 6, the [[affair-of-the-pink-pearl]], where he transitions from apparent victim to exposed villain through Tuppence's investigation.
