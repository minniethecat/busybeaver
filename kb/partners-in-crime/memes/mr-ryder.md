## Overview

Mr. Hank P. Ryder is a central figure in Chapter 10 of *[[partners-in-crime-novel|Partners in Crime]]*, "[[the-crackler|The Crackler]]." A wealthy gentleman from Alabama, Ryder becomes entangled in a sophisticated counterfeiting operation passing forged currency through London. [[tommy-beresford]], investigating on behalf of [[scotland-yard|Scotland Yard]], encounters Ryder in circumstances that reveal the American's dual role as both a victim of and participant in the scheme.

## Background

Mr. Ryder hailed from Alabama and described himself as a gentleman of means (010-095). He claimed to have come to England in pursuit of a "wonderful woman" — a lady he had followed across the Atlantic (010-097). Whether this romantic story was genuine or part of his cover remains ambiguous throughout the narrative.

Ryder was a stout, genial man with a pronounced American accent and a fondness for drink. When Tommy first encountered him in the street, Ryder was visibly intoxicated, weeping over a hatstand and lamenting that he had "no home to go to" (010-125). His inebriated state, however, concealed a sharper mind than it first appeared.

> "Sho you have," said Mr. Ryder. "Thatsh odd. Thatsh remarkable, that is." (010-122)

## Role in the Counterfeiting Case

Ryder's involvement in the "Crackler" case began when he presented himself as a potential client with knowledge of forged currency. He told Tommy he was engaged in a "treasurehunt" — "Swell thing to do" (010-127) — which served as a thin disguise for his true objective: locating the source of the counterfeit notes.

When Tommy and Ryder eventually confronted the criminals, Ryder's demeanor shifted dramatically. The bumbling drunk gave way to a harder, more capable man. He issued directions with authority as they approached the criminals' hideout (010-143), and when cornered by [[scotland-yard|Scotland Yard]], [[inspector-marriot|Inspector Marriot]] identified him with evident surprise:

> "Why it's Mr. Hank Ryder. This is a surprise." (010-165)

The confrontation revealed that Ryder was no ordinary victim. He knew the counterfeiting operation intimately — he was, in fact, the mastermind behind it. When Marriot moved to arrest him, Ryder smiled genially and warned, "I shouldn't if I were you" (010-171), revealing his true colors as the "Crackler" himself.

## Personality

Ryder's defining trait is his duality. Publicly, he projects the image of a wealthy, slightly foolish American abroad — sentimental about women, prone to drink, and genially indiscreet. This persona disarms his interlocutors and conceals his criminal acumen. Privately, he is shrewd, calculating, and ruthless. His transition from tearful drunk to confident criminal mastermind is one of the chapter's most striking reversals.

> "Mighty clever, didn't you?" said Ryder. (010-173)

## Key Relationships

- **[[tommy-beresford]]** — Ryder initially engages Tommy as a confidant, feeding him fragments of the counterfeiting story while steering him away from the truth. Tommy ultimately sees through the deception.
- **[[inspector-marriot]]** — The [[scotland-yard|Scotland Yard]] inspector who recognizes Ryder and moves to arrest him. Marriot's familiarity with Ryder's reputation suggests a prior history or notoriety.
- **[[marguerite-laidlaw|Marguerite Laidlaw]]** — The "wonderful woman" Ryder claimed to have followed from America (010-097). Whether she was real or fabricated as part of his cover story is left ambiguous.

## Appearances

Mr. Ryder appears exclusively in Chapter 10, "[[the-crackler|The Crackler]]," where his character arc progresses from apparent victim to exposed criminal. His role drives the chapter's central mystery: identifying the source of forged banknotes circulating in London, and the confrontation that follows when the true identity of the "Crackler" is revealed to be Ryder himself.
