## Overview

**Mrs. Laidlaw** is the wife of [[major-laidlaw|Major Laidlaw]] and a figure of interest in Chapter 10, "[[the-crackler|The Crackler]]." Her social activities — particularly her "train of admirers" and her habit of taking people in her car — provide critical leads in the investigation of a counterfeit currency operation.

## Social Circle

Mrs. Laidlaw maintained a notable social presence, surrounded by a circle of male acquaintances:

> "Amongst Mrs. Laidlaw's train of admirers was a simple—" (010-095)

This "train of admirers" is not merely a social detail; it becomes relevant to the case when one of her companions is linked to the counterfeit scheme.

## Financial Transactions

Mrs. Laidlaw was involved in passing money to associates of the scheme:

> "Why, they were the ones Mrs. Laidlaw paid over to me, I reckon." (010-107)

This admission suggests that Mrs. Laidlaw served as a conduit for payments — perhaps knowingly, perhaps unwittingly — within the broader counterfeit operation that [[tommy-beresford|Tommy Beresford]] is investigating.

## The Car Trip

A car journey with Mrs. Laidlaw becomes a point of investigative focus:

> "What's that you say? Mrs. Laidlaw took you in a car?" (010-132)

> "Is this where you came with Mrs. Laidlaw tonight?" (010-141)

These questions, posed during the investigation, suggest that Mrs. Laidlaw's movements — ferrying individuals in her automobile — were instrumental to the logistics of the criminal scheme. The car trip provides a tangible connection between her social world and the underworld of counterfeit currency.

## Relationship to the Case

Mrs. Laidlaw occupies an ambiguous position in the narrative: her wealth and social standing place her above suspicion in conventional police work, yet her actions — the payments, the car trips, the train of admirers — tie her inextricably to the criminal enterprise. Whether she is a knowing accomplice or an unwitting tool is a question the investigation must answer.
