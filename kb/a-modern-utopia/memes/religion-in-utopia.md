# Religion in Utopia

Religion in Wells's modern utopia is neither abolished nor established. It is understood as a natural human instinct — as inseparable from life as thirst or anger — that must be cultivated with the same disciplined discrimination applied to all other desires. The Utopian State takes no official position on doctrine but, through the samurai order, models an approach to religion that repudiates sentimentality while honouring the impulse itself.

## In A Modern Utopia

The leading principle of the Utopian religion is "the repudiation of the doctrine of original sin; the Utopians hold that man, on the whole, is good. That is their cardinal belief" (Chapter 9, Section 3). From this optimistic anthropology flows a distinctive religious posture: religion is not a remedy for a fallen nature but a refinement of an essentially noble one. Man "has pride and conscience, they hold, that you may refine by training as you refine his eye and ear."

Wells's most striking move is to treat religion as analogous to thirst. "They accept Religion as they accept Thirst, as something inseparably in the mysterious rhythms of life." And just as thirst can be perverted into intemperance in drinking, "so too the nobler complex of desires that constitutes religion may be turned to evil by the dull, the base, and the careless." The danger is not religion itself but "slovenly indulgence in religious inclinations, a failure to think hard and discriminate as fairly as possible in religious matters."

The samurai — the governing voluntary order — are subject to a distinctive religious discipline. Utopia "will have its temples and its priests, just as it will have its actresses and wine, but the samurai will be forbidden the religion of dramatically lit altars, organ music, and incense, as distinctly as they are forbidden the love of painted women, or the consolations of brandy." This is not atheism but a demand for intellectual clarity: the samurai must approach religion without the emotional props that risk substituting aesthetic experience for genuine understanding.

"To all the things that are less than religion and that seek to comprehend it, to cosmogonies and philosophies, to creeds and formulae, to catechisms and easy explanations, the attitude of the samurai" is one of disciplined reserve. Whatever "common creed or formula they have must be of the simplest sort" — a minimal framework that leaves room for the individual's own engagement with ultimate questions.

## Significance

This concept sits at the intersection of several core utopian themes. It exemplifies the principle of ordered liberty: the State does not dictate belief, but the samurai voluntarily adopt a rule that requires them to approach religion as thoughtfully as they approach every other domain of life. It also reflects the broader utopian confidence that human nature, properly disciplined, does not need to be coerced into virtue. Religion is a real and valuable human impulse; the task is not to eradicate it but to prevent its corruption.
