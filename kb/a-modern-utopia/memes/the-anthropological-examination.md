The Anthropological Examination is the episode in which the narrator and botanist are examined by the Research Professor of Anthropology at the University of London. The examination is part of the identification process initiated when two strangers with no records in the Utopian system appeared at the Hospenthal inn and registered with fabricated thumbmark signatures. It dramatises the Utopian response to the anomalous — not through force or exclusion, but through scientific curiosity and methodical investigation.

## Background

Upon their first arrival at the Utopian inn at Hospenthal, the narrator and botanist had been asked to register in a guest book with name, number, and thumbmark. The botanist, displaying unexpected readiness, fabricated a complex alphanumeric identity — "A.M.a.1607.2.ab+" — and the narrator followed with an equally imposing invention. They thought themselves "very clever." But the thumbmark signatures have no match in the Utopian central registry, creating what the Utopian authorities perceive as a genuine anomaly: two persons who appear to exist, yet have no records in a system where every human being is registered from birth.

The case is escalated from the local inn to the central registry in [[Paris]], and from there to the anthropological authorities in [[London]]. The examination is conducted at the [[University of Utopia]], where the Research Professor of Anthropology studies the two strangers as specimens of an unknown population — a situation rich with irony, since the narrator and botanist, as Earth-men, regard themselves as the normative observers of Utopia, not as objects of Utopian observation.

## Description

The examination takes place in a setting of scientific precision. The Professor — described with characteristic Wellsian attention to professional type — examines the travellers' physical characteristics, their clothing (which reveals non-Utopian manufacture), their linguistic peculiarities, and most importantly, the absence of any thumbmark records. The narrator, accustomed to being the observer, finds himself uncomfortably repositioned as the observed.

The scene is not described in extended dramatic detail in the text; it is referenced as a process that unfolds over time, with the travellers waiting in Lucerne while the "perplexing problem" they have set the authorities works its way through the Utopian administrative system. The examination itself serves as a narrative device to explain how the travellers are eventually identified as "visitors from another planet" — an explanation that the Utopian scientific establishment, with its commitment to empirical method, is uniquely equipped to entertain.

## Significance

The Anthropological Examination inverts the colonial gaze that structures many earlier Utopian narratives. Instead of the European traveller observing and judging a foreign society, the Utopians scientifically examine the Earth-men as curiosities. This reversal is consistent with the book's broader argument that Utopia represents a more advanced stage of human development — one from which Earth appears as a less evolved, less rational version of the same species.

The episode also demonstrates the Utopian commitment to rational administration. Faced with an anomaly that in most societies would provoke fear or hostility, the Utopian system responds with methodical investigation. The identification case becomes a demonstration of how a scientifically organised world handles the unknown — not through exclusion, but through understanding.
