The Train Journey to London is the narrator and botanist's first experience of Utopian long-distance rail travel. Departing from Lucerne, the journey introduces the corridor train — a mobile hotel-library-club that embodies the Utopian integration of technological capability with human comfort. The journey culminates in the narrator's second awakening, this time in London, marking the narrative's shift from Alpine Switzerland to the urban heart of the World State.

## Background

After their extended sojourn in Lucerne, during which they worked at wood-carving while awaiting identification resolution, the narrator and botanist prepare to travel to [[London]]. The Utopian transport system is a world-state network of "corridor trains" that serve as flying hotels, eliminating the fatigue and discomfort of earthly rail travel.

## Description

At the Lucerne station, the travellers encounter a club-like departure lounge with labeled doorways and phonograph announcements. The narrator asks, "Where is the train for London?" and a uniformed Utopian replies, "This is the train for London."

The train is twice the width of earthly trains, with "the simple device of abandoning any but a few windows" to create wall space for books. The middle section is "a comfortable library with abundant armchairs and couches, each with its green-shaded light, and soft carpets upon the soundproof floor." Further on there is a news-room with a "noiseless but busy tape" printing messages from wayside wires, rooms for conversation and smoking, a billiard room, and a dining car. Behind these are bedrooms, bathrooms, and a hairdresser's.

The narrator falls asleep during the journey, and wakes to find the train already in London — the Utopians having discovered "that it is not necessary to bundle out passengers from a train in the small hours, simply because they have arrived." The train serves as "a peculiar kind of hotel corridor that flies about the earth while one sleeps." No sea crossing interrupts the journey; a Channel tunnel connects the continent to England.

## Significance

The train journey is more than a travel scene — it is a demonstration of Utopian values in physical form. The train's design eliminates the class distinctions of earthly rail travel (no first/second/third class), replaces the tedium of staring out windows with access to books and conversation, and turns transit time into comfortable living time. The Channel tunnel and international rail network embody the World State's unification of geography. The second awakening — this time in London — parallels the first awakening at the inn, marking the narrative's expanding scope from the Alpine periphery to the metropolitan centre of Utopian civilisation.
