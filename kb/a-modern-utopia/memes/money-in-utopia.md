# Money in Utopia

Money in the Modern Utopia occupies a paradoxical position. Wells rejects the notion of a moneyless state — his Utopians "certainly they will need to have money. They will have money" — yet he refuses to treat money as an end in itself. The Utopian monetary system is designed to be a transparent measure of value, stripped of the speculative and accumulative functions that, in Wells's view, distort terrestrial economies. It is neither capitalist worship of gold nor socialist abolition of currency, but a pragmatic monetary framework subordinated to human welfare.

## In A Modern Utopia

The Utopian coinage is introduced to the reader through a narrative device: the botanist picks up a fallen coin on the Gotthard road, and the two travellers examine it together. The coin is a **Lion**, made of gold, "distinctly a beautiful coin, admirably made, with its value in fine, clear letters circling the obverse side." It is equal to "twaindy" bronze Crosses — the unfamiliar word immediately suggesting a duodecimal counting system, "that most Utopian of all things" (Chapter 1).

The choice of a duodecimal base reflects Wells's conviction that decimalisation is an arbitrary convention rather than a rational necessity. Twelve, being divisible by two, three, four, and six, is more practical for daily transactions than ten. This small detail exemplifies the Utopian method: take nothing for granted, redesign from first principles.

Wells explicitly distances his system from both the Platonic moneyless republic and the "iron coinage of austere appearance" prescribed in the Laws. He acknowledges that the Twentieth Century Utopia "has still not passed beyond money and the use of coins," and that it operates "with many unsettled problems of currency." This epistemic modesty — admitting that even Utopia has not solved every monetary question — is characteristic of Wells's kinetic approach.

The most distinctive feature of the Utopian system is the relationship between gold coinage and **energy notes**. Wells proposes that the energy unit — a measure of available physical work — should serve as the "standard of value to the entire Utopian coinage." Every gold Lion and every credit note represents a claim on real energy, tethering the monetary system to physical reality rather than to the fluctuations of precious metal markets. This energy standard is Wells's attempt to prevent money from becoming a self-referential domain of speculation divorced from genuine economic activity.

Wells is also attentive to the dangers of treating currency regulation as a source of public revenue — an "immeasurable error" that the Utopians will have avoided. The State administers the monetary system but does not profit from it, a principle consistent with the broader Utopian rejection of institutional incentives that reward exploitation.

## Significance

The monetary provisions illustrate Wells's characteristic method: accept the inescapable functions of a complex society — here, the need for a medium of exchange — but redesign the institutional framework so that those functions serve human purposes rather than dominating them. Money in Utopia is a tool, not a master. The energy standard, the duodecimal coinage, and the separation of currency from state revenue together constitute a monetary system that is rational, transparent, and subordinate to the broader Utopian commitment to human welfare.
