# Travel and Transport in Utopia

In the Modern Utopia, travel and transport exemplify the World State's capacity to transform daily life through technological rationalisation. The Utopian railway system — featuring corridor trains twice the width of their terrestrial counterparts, travelling at two hundred miles an hour — serves as both practical infrastructure and a symbol of the civilised ease that the World State makes possible.

## In A Modern Utopia

Wells provides his most vivid description of Utopian transport in Section 28, where the narrator and the botanist board a train for London. The experience is designed to eliminate the friction and discomfort associated with terrestrial travel. At the station, passengers find "pleasant rooms with seats and books," exchange outdoor shoes for slippers, and are summoned by a courteous phonograph announcement rather than a jostling crowd.

The train itself is described as resembling "a good club." The middle section houses "a comfortable library with abundant armchairs and couches, each with its green-shaded light, and soft carpets upon the soundproof floor." Beyond the library there is a news-room with telegraphic dispatches, rooms for conversation and smoking, a billiard room, and a dining car. Sleeping compartments, bathrooms, and a hairdresser occupy the rear. Significantly, the train has only a few high-set windows: at two hundred miles an hour, Wells observes, "there is nothing but fatigue in looking out of a window."

Upon arrival in London, the passengers are not hurried off. "A Utopian train is just a peculiar kind of hotel corridor that flies about the earth while one sleeps." Passengers may remain aboard after arrival, unhurried, reflecting the Utopian principle that human convenience takes precedence over operational efficiency.

The transport system presupposes the elimination of national boundaries. A Channel tunnel connects England to the continent without sea crossing, and the "flying track" enables speeds that collapse the geography of the planet into manageable distances. The entire system is integrated with the Utopian hotel network, creating a seamless continuum of mobility and hospitality.

## Significance

Utopian transport is not merely a technological showcase — it embodies several core Utopian principles. First, it demonstrates the civilising effect of rational design applied to everyday experience; the contrast between the frantic, uncomfortable railway journeys of Edwardian England and the calm, club-like Utopian train makes an implicit argument for planned infrastructure. Second, the integration of libraries, news-rooms, and comfortable social spaces into the transport system reflects the Utopian commitment to the life of the mind — travel time is not wasted but enriched. Third, the dissolution of national borders that makes this system possible is a direct consequence of the World State, illustrating how political transformation enables material improvement.

The train-as-hotel concept — "a peculiar kind of hotel corridor that flies about the earth while one sleeps" — captures the Utopian ambition to dissolve the traditional boundaries between categories (transport/lodging, travel/home) in favour of continuous, frictionless experience.
