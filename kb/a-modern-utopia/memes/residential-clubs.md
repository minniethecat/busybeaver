# Residential Clubs

The residential club is the dominant form of housing organisation in the Modern Utopia. Wells describes a society where the private household has been largely replaced: "The autonomy of the household has been reduced far below terrestrial conditions by hotels and clubs, and all sorts of co-operative expedients. People who do not live in hotels seem usually to live in clubs." The club is not merely a dwelling but a social institution, organising daily life around shared amenities and congenial association. (Chapter 24, "Life in the Modern Utopia")

## Structure and Amenities

A typical Utopian club is built around a quadrangle, modelled on the Oxford college. The lower story contains "kitchens and other offices, dining-room, writing-room, smoking and assembly rooms, a barber's shop, and a library." A colonnade with seats runs around the quadrangle, and at its centre "a bronze figure, a sleeping child, reposes above a little basin and fountain, in which water lilies are growing." The upper stories provide bedrooms and private apartments. (Chapter 24)

Beyond the individual club building, the club quadrangle integrates collective amenities for family life: "A common garden, an infant school, play rooms, and a playing garden for children, are universal features of the club quadrangles." This arrangement embeds childcare and early education within the residential community itself. (Chapter 24)

## Membership and Living Arrangements

"The fairly prosperous Utopian belongs, in most cases, to one or two residential clubs of congenial men and women." Clubs are selective communities organised around shared temperament and interests. Members may choose between furnished bedrooms and "more or less elaborate suites of apartments"—and "if a man prefers it one of these latter can be taken and furnished according to his personal taste." Private luxuries include "a pleasant boudoir, a private library and study, a private garden plot." (Chapter 24)

The club system extends the principle of collective living while preserving individual autonomy. It represents a middle ground between the fully communal hotel and the isolated private house, offering "devices to secure" privacy within a shared framework.

## Social Significance

The residential clubs are integral to the Utopian social fabric. They replace the class-stratified housing of earthly cities with communities organised by affinity rather than wealth. The narrator's double explains that clubs house "congenial men and women"—the organising principle is temperamental compatibility, not economic status. This reflects the broader Utopian commitment to classification by personal quality rather than material circumstance.

The club system also supports the Utopian rejection of the isolated nuclear household as the primary social unit. By embedding child-rearing, dining, and leisure in shared spaces, clubs foster the social cohesion that underpins the World State's voluntary order.
