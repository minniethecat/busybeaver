# Energy as Currency

One of the most distinctive features of the Utopian economic system is the replacement of gold-based money with an energy standard of value. In the Modern Utopia, all economic transactions are ultimately denominated in physical energy units — a reform that Wells presents as a necessary clarification of economic thought, eliminating the distortions introduced by fluctuating money values and national currencies.

## In A Modern Utopia

Wells introduces the energy currency in Section 10, where the narrator encounters a newspaper article discussing a proposed monetary reform. The central government calculates the total energy periodically available in each locality and expresses tax assessments and intergovernmental accounts in these physical units. Local Utopian authorities issue "energy notes" — currency notes redeemable for specified quantities of energy at generating stations — against the security of their actual energy surplus. The maximum issuance is capped by the amount of energy produced and consumed in that locality during the previous year.

The system is self-correcting across regions. In a world "without boundaries, with a population largely migratory and emancipated from locality," the price of energy notes from different regions would "constantly tend to be uniform, because employment would constantly shift into the areas where energy was cheap." On a designated day of economic equilibrium, a fixed ratio is declared between the old gold coinage and the energy notes; thereafter, gold ceases to be legal tender beyond defined limits and is gradually withdrawn from circulation.

Wells acknowledges the difficulty of his exposition — the Utopian economists' "unfamiliar, perplexing, and dream-like phraseology" resists easy translation into terrestrial terms. Yet he insists that the Utopians have grasped "a rightness that earthly economists have failed to grasp." The key insight is that earthly economists have been "obsessed with international trade" and "unable to disentangle themselves from patriotisms and politics." The World State removes these obstacles, allowing economic thought to address production and distribution directly, as "a problem in the conversion of energy."

## Significance

The energy currency reform embodies several interlocking Utopian commitments. It grounds economic value in physical reality — actual kilowatt-hours — rather than in the abstractions of gold supply or national monetary policy. It enables frictionless trade across a borderless world by eliminating exchange rate fluctuations and currency speculation. It makes economic inequality transparent: energy flows are measurable and auditable in a way that financial instruments are not.

The reform also has a philosophical dimension. By expressing the economy in energy terms, Utopian economics aligns with the physical sciences, treating human productive activity as a branch of thermodynamics. This unification of economic and scientific thinking is characteristic of the Utopian worldview, which seeks to eliminate the conceptual fragmentation that Wells saw as a weakness of terrestrial intellectual life.

The energy currency is one element of the broader Utopian economic settlement, which also includes the abolition of private property in land and natural resources, the regulation of labour through the Samurai order, and the provision of a social minimum. Together, these reforms aim to liberate human productive capacity from the distortions of rent-seeking and speculation.
