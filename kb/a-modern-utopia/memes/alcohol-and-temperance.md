# Alcohol and Temperance

The regulation of alcohol in the modern utopia serves as Wells's chosen case study for the broader question of how the State should govern personal morals. Following Plato's method — who used wine as his test case in the "Laws" — Wells argues that the principles governing intoxication reveal the proper boundary between individual liberty and public order in a modern, migratory society.

## In A Modern Utopia

"As Plato demonstrated long ago, the principles of the relation of State control to personal morals may be best discussed in the case of intoxication, the most isolated and least complicated of all this group of problems." But while Plato's treatment — a question of "who may or may not have the use of wine" — suited a small city-state where "everybody was the effectual inspector of everybody," it is "entirely beside the mark under modern conditions" (Chapter 9, Section 3).

The modern utopia operates with "an extraordinarily higher standard of individual privacy and an amplitude and quantity of migration inconceivable to the Academic imagination." Wells accepts Plato's principle — that the use of wine should be "among the distinctive privileges of maturity" — while finding that this still leaves "all that a modern would think of as the Drink Question untouched."

The utopian approach rests on four pillars. First, the maintenance of "public order and decency." Second, "the reduction of inducements to form this bad and wasteful habit to their lowest possible minimum." Third, "the complete protection of the immature" — making "the temptation of the young a grave offence." Fourth, and most distinctively modern, a sophisticated understanding of institutional incentives: the Utopians "will have avoided the immeasurable error of making their control of the drink traffic a source of public revenue."

On enforcement, Wells is careful about institutional design. The Utopians "will not put into the hands of a common policeman powers direct and indirect that would be dangerous to the public in the hands of a judge." Privacy is respected: "Privacies they will not invade, but they will certainly restrict the public consumption of intoxicants to specified licensed places and the sale of them to unmistakable adults."

The licensing system reflects the migratory character of utopian society. "In so migratory a population as the Modern Utopian, the licensing of inns and bars would be under the same control as the railways and high roads. Inns exist for the stranger and not for the locality, and we shall meet with nothing there to correspond with our terrestrial absurdity of Local Option."

Public drunkenness — "as distinguished from the mere elation that follows a generous but controlled use of wine" — is "an offence against public decency, and will be dealt with in some very drastic manner. It will, of course, be an aggravation of, and not an excuse for, crime."

## Significance

The alcohol question is methodologically significant as Wells's worked example of how to think about state regulation of personal conduct. Every element — the respect for privacy, the attention to institutional incentives, the distinction between private use and public disorder, the refusal to treat revenue as a motive for regulation — reflects principles that apply across the entire domain of personal morals in the utopian framework.
