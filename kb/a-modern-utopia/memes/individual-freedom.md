# Individual Freedom

Individual freedom is the defining value that separates the modern utopian vision from classical utopias. While earlier thinkers treated liberty as secondary to virtue and happiness, the modern view — driven by "a deepening insistence upon individuality and upon the significance of its uniqueness" — sees freedom as inseparable from a worthwhile life (Chapter 2, Section 6).

## In A Modern Utopia

Wells argues that the idea of individual liberty has grown in importance with every development of modern thought. To the classical Utopists — Plato, More, Campanella — "freedom was relatively trivial.

Clearly they considered virtue and happiness as entirely separable from liberty, and as being altogether more important things." But the modern view "steadily intensifies the value of freedom, until at last we begin to see liberty as the very substance of life, that indeed it is life, and that only the dead things, the choiceless things, live in absolute obedience to law" (Chapter 2, Section 6).

To have "free play for one's individuality is, in the modern view, the subjective triumph of existence." This is not mere hedonism or licence. Rather, it is the conviction that a human being flourishes only when able to exercise genuine choice — that liberty is not a luxury added to life but its essential condition.

Yet Wells is no absolutist about freedom. He recognises that "since man is a social creature, the play of will must fall short of absolute freedom.

Perfect human liberty is possible only to a despot who is absolutely and universally obeyed." For everyone else, "all other liberty is a compromise between our own freedom of will and the wills of those with whom we come in contact" (Chapter 2, Section 6).

In an organised state, each person "limits others by his rights, and is limited by the rights of others, and by considerations affecting the welfare of the community as a whole."

This leads to a crucial insight: "Individual liberty in a community is not, as mathematicians would say, always of the same sign." A general prohibition may increase the sum of liberty — consider how much freedom we gain by collectively agreeing not to kill one another. Conversely, a general permission may diminish it.

This is, Wells contends, "the essential fallacy of the cult called Individualism." A socialism is not necessarily a slavery, and there is no freedom under anarchy.

## Significance

This concept forms the philosophical foundation of the entire Utopian State. The Samurai order, the economic system, the arrangements for marriage and work — all are designed to maximise personal freedom while maintaining the collective conditions that make freedom possible.

Wells's great innovation in utopian thought was to make liberty, rather than order or virtue, the central organising principle. His modern utopia is not a perfected stasis but a dynamic framework for the free play of personality: a "kinetic" utopia in which change, movement, and individual choice are not threats to the ideal but its very essence.
