# Marriage in Utopia

Marriage in the Utopian [[World State]] represents one of Wells's most radical departures from Edwardian social convention. Rejecting both the indissoluble Christian marriage of his own era and the free-love experiments of some Utopian traditions, Wells proposes a **contractual model** centred on the welfare of children and the economic independence of women. The Utopian marriage is a limited-term agreement between equal parties, with the State intervening only to protect offspring and prevent exploitation. As the narrator notes in the sections devoted to this topic, the marriage question is "the most complicated and difficult" in the whole Utopian scheme — precisely because it touches on the most intimate aspects of human life.

## The Contractual Model

Under the Utopian system, marriage is reconceived as a **voluntary contract** rather than a sacred bond. Either party may terminate the marriage at will, with the sole condition that the State is released "from any liability for the support of her illegitimate offspring." This provision is critical to Wells's design: it ensures that sexual freedom does not become a subsidy for male irresponsibility. The father remains financially liable for his children regardless of the marriage's status.

A childless marriage is set to "expire at the end of three or four or five years" unless both parties renew it — a provision that acknowledges the different stakes of marriages with and without children. Wells's reasoning is pragmatic: without children, the State has no compelling interest in maintaining the union, and the parties should be free to reassess their commitment periodically.

| Provision | Rationale |
|-----------|-----------|
| Either party may terminate at will | Prevents entrapment in unhappy unions |
| Father liable for offspring regardless | Protects children and the State from abandonment |
| Childless marriages expire after 3–5 years | Periodic renewal ensures ongoing consent |
| State provides minimum wage to mothers | Economic independence enables free choice |
| Moral intervention only after legal duties met | Separates legal obligation from moral judgement |

## Economic Independence and Motherhood

The Utopian marriage system rests on the economic foundation of the broader Utopian economy. Women receive a **minimum wage** from the State during motherhood, ensuring that the decision to marry — or to leave a marriage — is not constrained by economic desperation. This provision, Wells argues, "practically abolishes the mercenary element in marriage" by removing the financial dependency that trapped Edwardian women in unhappy unions.

Wells devotes considerable attention to the question of motherhood, treating it as a **social service** rather than a private burden. The State supports mothers not out of sentiment but because healthy, well-raised children are a public good. This logic extends to the regulation of marriage itself: the State intervenes only to the extent necessary to protect children and ensure that private choices do not create public costs.

## Limits of State Intervention

Wells is careful to delineate where State authority ends. Beyond ensuring child welfare and enforcing the marriage contract's terms, the Utopian State "lapses into purely moral intervention" — meaning it may disapprove but cannot coerce. This boundary is essential to the Utopian balance between [[Individual Freedom]] and collective welfare. Marriage, in Wells's vision, is too intimate for perpetual state supervision, yet too consequential to be left entirely unregulated.

The tension between freedom and regulation in the marriage system mirrors the broader Utopian compromise. Just as the [[Samurai Order]] governs through voluntary discipline rather than force, the marriage system guides through incentives and clear rules rather than moral exhortation. This approach reflects Wells's conviction that durable social institutions must work with human nature rather than against it.

## Significance

The marriage provisions exemplify Wells's method throughout *A Modern Utopia*: take a familiar institution, strip away its metaphysical claims (sacredness, indissolubility), identify its functional core (child welfare, mutual consent), and rebuild it on rational-utilitarian grounds. The result is neither traditional nor revolutionary in the conventional sense — it is **pragmatic utopianism**, an attempt to design institutions that serve human needs without pretending to transform human nature.
