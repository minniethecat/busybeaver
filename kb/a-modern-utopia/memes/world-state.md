# World State

The World State is the overarching political entity that governs the Modern Utopia. It is a single, unified government for the entire planet — "world-state, therefore, it must be," Wells writes, establishing the scale of his utopian ambition (Chapter 1, Section 5).

The World State provides the institutional framework within which individual freedom, economic activity, and social life are ordered.

## Structure and Authority

The World State is the "sole landowner of the earth."
Private property in land is abolished; all land is held by the State, which leases it to individuals and enterprises.

This arrangement, Wells argues, eliminates unearned wealth from land speculation and ensures that the value created by the community returns to the community rather than to private landlords (Chapter 3, Section 13).

The State controls the major arteries of transport and commerce — the high-roads, the railways, the canals — and provides a uniform legal and administrative framework across the entire planet.
Local administrations exist under the supervision of the central government, handling matters of local concern while the World State manages affairs of global scope.

## Governance and the Samurai

The real governing power of the World State resides with the Samurai order.
The Samurai are "the real body of the State," and all executive committees and responsible positions are drawn exclusively from their ranks.
As Wells puts it: "all the executive committees, and so forth, that constitute the working government of Utopia must be Samurai" (Chapter 9, Section 28).

This arrangement ensures that governance is exercised by those who have voluntarily adopted the Rule — an ascetic discipline that serves as a check on the abuse of power. The Samurai are not elected; they enter the order by qualification and commit to its discipline.

The World State is thus neither a democracy nor an autocracy but a meritocratic order governed by those who have proven their fitness through the adoption of a demanding way of life.

## Functions

The World State performs the functions that any modern government must: it maintains order, administers justice, provides public goods, and manages relations with other populations (though in the Utopian vision, the entire world is under one government).

Beyond these traditional functions, the World State also undertakes the active management of the economy — not through central planning of production, but through ownership of land, control of the monetary system, and the setting of framework conditions within which free exchange operates.

The State also has responsibility for population quality. Wells argues that the State must concern itself with the biological health of the population, preventing the reproduction of those with severe hereditary diseases and encouraging the flourishing of healthy stock.

This aspect of the World State's function reflects the eugenic thinking common in the Edwardian period and is one of the more controversial elements of Wells's vision.

## Significance

The World State represents Wells's attempt to reconcile individual freedom with collective organisation at a planetary scale. It is neither the minimal state of classical liberalism nor the total state of authoritarian socialism, but something distinct: a framework state that owns the land, enforces just rules, and entrusts governance to a disciplined order of volunteers.

The World State is the institutional expression of Wells's conviction that freedom and order are not opposites but complements — that genuine individual liberty requires, and is made possible by, collective organisation.
