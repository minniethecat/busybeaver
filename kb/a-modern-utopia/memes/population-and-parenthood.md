# Population and Parenthood

Population control is one of the foundational principles of the Modern Utopia. Wells argues explicitly that without the determination and ability to limit population increase as well as to stimulate it when necessary, no Utopia is possible — a point he credits to Malthus as having been "demonstrated for all time." The Utopian approach to population is neither pro-natalist nor anti-natalist in the abstract; it is a rational, state-managed equilibrium between demographic growth and economic capacity.

## In A Modern Utopia

Wells addresses population regulation most directly in the context of labour economics. He identifies two potential causes of labour surplus: an increase in population without a corresponding expansion of enterprise, or a diminution of employment due to labour-saving machinery and organisational efficiency. The first cause, he insists, "may be anticipated by wise marriage laws." The full discussion of these laws is deferred, but Wells is unambiguous: "Utopia will control the increase of its population."

The second cause — technological unemployment — is treated differently. Wells sees a surplus of efficient labour at the minimum wage not as a crisis but as the ideal condition for stimulating new enterprises in "a State saturated with science and prolific in invention." Crucially, this surplus is of labour without an absolute increase of population; it does not press on the food supply, and the World State serves as the reserve employer, absorbing excess labour through reduced working days or permanent public works.

The World State's responsibilities include securing "healthy births and a healthy and vigorous new generation" and maintaining public health. The state's role extends to the very beginning of life: Wells envisions a system in which births are not left to chance or private decision alone but are actively secured and managed by the state as a matter of public interest. The chapter "Women in a Modern Utopia" explores the implications for motherhood, including state subsidies for mothers — a recognition that reproduction is a social contribution deserving economic support.

Wells also confronts the darker side of population management. He acknowledges the need for "social surgery" when dealing with transmissible diseases and hereditary defects, and states plainly that Utopia will "kill all deformed and monstrous and evilly diseased births" — a position that reflects the eugenic thinking common in Edwardian progressive thought. However, he frames this not as cruelty but as a precondition for eliminating the mass suffering produced by unregulated reproduction in a world of scarcity.

Wells insists that with "sane marriage and birth laws there is no reason to suppose such calls upon the resources and initiative of the world more than temporary and exceptional occasions." The population policy is thus a stabilising mechanism, not a permanent austerity — it creates the conditions under which abundance becomes sustainable.

## Significance

Population regulation is the economic and demographic foundation upon which all other Utopian institutions rest. Without it, the minimum wage, the labour exchanges, the migratory freedom, and the standard of life guarantees would all collapse under the pressure of unchecked demographic growth. Wells understood that a world of finite resources and open borders — his Utopia has no immigration controls — requires a mechanism for matching human numbers to economic capacity. The marriage and birth laws are thus not peripheral social policy but a constitutive element of the World State itself.
