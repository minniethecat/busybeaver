# The Samurai

The Samurai are the "voluntary nobility" of the Modern Utopia — an order of men and women distinguished by a distinctive costume and bearing, who constitute the essential governing class of the Utopian State.

They are not a hereditary aristocracy but a body open to "every physically and mentally healthy adult in the Utopian State who will observe its prescribed austere rule of living" (Chapter 9, Section 28).

## In A Modern Utopia

Wells presents the Samurai as the "real body of the State" — the class "in whose hands as a class all the real power resides." All the "executive committees, and so forth" that constitute the working government of Utopia are drawn exclusively from them.

Yet the order is "not hereditary — we know just enough of biology and the uncertainties of inheritance to know how silly that would be" (Chapter 9, Section 28).

Entry into the Samurai is based on personal qualification and willingness to adopt the Rule, a disciplined way of life. Candidates must pass examinations and demonstrate both physical and mental fitness. The order began as "a private aggressive cult" that won its way to dominance through demonstrated competence rather than force or inheritance.

## History and Formation

The organisation of the Samurai was "a quite deliberate invention," arising "in the course of social and political troubles and complications, analogous to those of our own time on earth." It represented, Wells explains, the last of a number of political and religious experiments dating back to the first dawn of philosophical state-craft in Greece.

It was the Samurai's "widely sustained activities that had shaped and established the World State in Utopia" (Chapter 9, Section 30).

Crucially, the Samurai order emerged from a recognition that "self-seeking is no more the whole of human life than the satisfaction of hunger." Every sane person, Wells insists, "consists of possibilities beyond the unavoidable needs,

is capable of disinterested feeling." The order was designed to channel this "impersonal energy" into constructive public service rather than letting it dissipate in "religious and political misunderstandings and conflicts" or "unsatisfying amusements and unproductive occupations."

## The Rule

Members of the Samurai adopt an ascetic discipline — the Rule — which governs their conduct, health, and moral life. The precise details of the Rule are only sketched by Wells, but its spirit is one of self-mastery. Samurai must maintain bodily health, cultivate disinterested judgement, and subordinate personal gain to the common good.

The asceticism is not punitive but instrumental: it is the price of the trust placed in them as governors, and it serves to ensure that power is not abused.

## Comparison with Plato's Guardians

Wells explicitly invites comparison with "that strange class of guardians which constitutes the essential substance of Plato's Republic." Like Plato's guardians, the Samurai are a select governing class defined by discipline and education rather than wealth or birth.

But Wells departs from Plato in crucial respects: the Samurai are volunteers, not conscripts; their membership is open to women as well as men; and they operate within a framework that values individual freedom rather than subordinating the individual entirely to the state.

## Significance

The Samurai represent Wells's solution to the fundamental problem of governance: how to ensure that power is exercised competently and disinterestedly without resorting to hereditary privilege or the accidents of popular election.

By making the governing class voluntary and open, subject to a rule of life that demands genuine sacrifice, Wells hopes to attract those with a genuine vocation for public service while deterring those who seek power for its own sake.

The Samurai are, in effect, the institutional embodiment of the belief that "life may pass beyond to an illimitable world of emotions and effort" — that human beings are capable of more than self-seeking.
