# Science and Progress

In the Modern Utopia, the state is conceived as inherently progressive rather than static — a fundamental departure from most historical political thought. Science and technological progress are not left to private initiative or market forces but are actively cultivated by the World State as essential public goods. The Utopian commitment to progress transforms the relationship between knowledge, governance, and daily life.

## In A Modern Utopia

Wells addresses the progressive character of the Utopian state in Section 11, where he writes that "the State is to be progressive, it is no longer to be static, and this alters the general condition of the Utopian problem profoundly." This principle has concrete institutional implications. The state is empowered to "subsidise research, and reward such commercially unprofitable undertakings as benefit the community as a whole." Scientific inquiry and technological innovation are treated as public investments, not speculative ventures.

The Utopian approach to progress extends beyond formal research institutions. Wells envisions a society where the scientific habit of mind permeates culture. The Samurai — the voluntary ruling order — are selected partly on intellectual merit and trained in critical thinking, creating a governing class equipped to evaluate evidence and adapt policy to new knowledge. Education is universal and oriented toward developing the capacity for independent thought, ensuring that the population can participate in and benefit from scientific advancement.

Wells also recognises the institutional mechanisms that enable progress. In Section 11, he observes that "after the invention of money, there is no invention has so facilitated freedom and progress as the invention of the limited liability company to do this work of trial and adventure." The Utopian World State inherits and refines the best institutional innovations of terrestrial civilisation, adapting them to serve collective rather than merely private ends.

The energy-based economic system (see [[Energy as Currency]]) exemplifies the Utopian fusion of science and governance: economic value is measured in physical units, making the economy transparent to scientific analysis. The elimination of national boundaries enables the free circulation of researchers, ideas, and technologies. The comprehensive transport and communication infrastructure reduces the friction that on Earth impedes the diffusion of knowledge.

## Significance

The progressive character of the Utopian state reflects Wells's conviction that modernity had made static political philosophies obsolete. In a world of accelerating technological change, a state that merely preserves order is insufficient; it must actively shape and direct change for the common good. The subsidisation of "commercially unprofitable" research — basic science, public health measures, environmental management — addresses a market failure that Wells recognised as a weakness of laissez-faire capitalism.

The Utopian model also anticipates later developments in science policy: publicly funded research, the concept of science as a public good, and the integration of scientific advice into governance. Wells's insistence that the state itself must be progressive — not merely tolerate progress — remains a distinctive contribution to political thought.
