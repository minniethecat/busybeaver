# The Four Classes

The Four Classes of Utopia — the **Poietic**, the **Kinetic**, the **Dull**, and the **Base** — constitute one of the most distinctive and controversial features of Wells's Utopian social architecture. Unlike the class systems of earthly societies, which are based on wealth, birth, or occupation, the Utopian classification is based on **temperament** — the innate disposition of the individual mind. The Poietic and Kinetic classes are "supposed to constitute the living tissue of the State," while the Dull and the Base "are the fulcra and resistances, the bone and cover of its body." This classification is not merely descriptive; it is the organising principle for education, occupation, and political participation in the [[World State]].

## The Four Temperaments

Wells defines each class by its characteristic mental disposition:

| Class | Temperament | Social Role | Governance Role |
|-------|------------|-------------|-----------------|
| **Poietic** | Creative imagination; ranges "beyond the known and accepted" | Artists, inventors, scientists, original thinkers | Eligible for [[Samurai Order]] |
| **Kinetic** | Active, executive; capable of applying ideas | Administrators, managers, skilled professionals | Eligible for Samurai Order |
| **Dull** | Limited imagination; "a certain bias" toward convention | Routine workers, followers of custom | Governed, not governing |
| **Base** | Anti-social impulses; resistant to collective life | Marginal economic roles | Excluded from civic participation |

The Poietic class "embraces a wide range of types, but they agree in possessing imaginations that range beyond the known and accepted, and that involve the desire to" create or discover. The scientific mind, when not constrained by convention, is a Poietic mind; the artist, the inventor, and the philosophical innovator all belong to this category. The Kinetic class comprises those whose imagination is sufficient to grasp and apply ideas, but not to originate them — the "active" types who make the State function.

The Dull and the Base are distinguished by their relationship to social norms. The Dull are unimaginative but conforming; they follow rules and perform routine work reliably. The Base are those whose impulses run counter to social cooperation — a category that Wells treats with characteristic ambiguity, acknowledging that "people of great though irregular poietic" capacity may sometimes be misclassified here.

## Fluidity and Ambiguity

Wells is careful to insist that the classification is not rigid. "In actual experience these qualities mingle and vary in every possible way," he writes. "It is not a classification for" individuals in their full complexity, but a practical tool for a State that "dealing as it does only with non-individualised affairs, is not only justified in" using broad categories but obliged to do so. The classes are **statistical tendencies**, not fixed identities — a point that distinguishes Wells's system from the hereditary castes of earlier Utopias or the racial hierarchies of his Edwardian contemporaries.

This fluidity is essential to the Utopian compromise between order and [[Individual Freedom]]. The State classifies for administrative purposes but does not imprison individuals within their classification. A person's temperamental type may shift over a lifetime, and the educational system is designed to identify and cultivate Poietic and Kinetic potential wherever it appears.

## Role in the Samurai Order

The classification is most consequential in its relationship to the [[Samurai Order]]. Only the Poietic and Kinetic types are eligible for Samurai membership, and the narrator notes "the understanding of the importance of poietic activities in the majority of the samurai, in whose hands as a class all the real" power of the State resides. This creates a **meritocratic aristocracy** — one based on demonstrated capacity rather than birth, but an aristocracy nonetheless.

The exclusion of the Dull and Base from governance raises some of the most difficult questions in Wells's Utopian scheme. Is temperament truly innate? Can a classification system avoid hardening into a caste system? Wells acknowledges these tensions without fully resolving them, and the Four Classes remain one of the most debated elements of his Utopian design.
