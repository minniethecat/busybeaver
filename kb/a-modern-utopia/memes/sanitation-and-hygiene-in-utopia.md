# Sanitation and Hygiene in Utopia

Sanitation and hygiene in the modern utopia represent the systematic application of biological knowledge to public life. Wells envisions a world in which the eradication of infectious disease, the design of clean living spaces, and the normalisation of personal hygiene are not matters of individual virtue but of state competence — a baseline of civilised existence maintained by intelligent infrastructure.

## In A Modern Utopia

The sanitary vision begins with the physical elimination of disease vectors. "Utopian houses, streets and drains will be planned and built to make rats, mice, and such-like house parasites impossible." The free movement of familiar animals must be suppressed during a systematic campaign to "destroy for ever a great number of contagious and infectious diseases" — a measure that will involve, "for a time at any rate, a stringent suppression" of the free movement of cats, dogs, and horses, whose presence provides "living fastnesses to which such diseases as plague, influenza, catarrhs and the like, can retreat to sally forth again" (Chapter 9, Section 5). "The filth made by horses and the other brutes of the highway" will "vanish from the face of the earth."

This is not merely a public health campaign but an architectural principle. The modern State "will insist upon every citizen being properly housed, well nourished, and in good health, reasonably clean and clothed healthily, and upon that insistence its labour laws will be founded." Any house that does not meet "its rising standard of healthiness and convenience, the Utopian State will incontinently pull down." Any house "unduly crowded or dirty, it must in some effectual manner, directly or indirectly, confiscate and clear and clean." And any citizen "indecently dressed, or ragged and dirty, or publicly unhealthy, or sleeping abroad homeless" must come under the State's care.

The private sphere reflects the same values. Wells's description of the Utopian hotel room is a hymn to hygienic design: "The room has no corners to gather dirt, wall meets floor with a gentle curve." A recess dressing-room is "equipped with a bath and all that is necessary to one's toilette," with water warmed electrically. "A cake of soap drops out of a store machine on the turn of a handle, and when you have done with it, you drop that and your soiled towels... into a little box, through the bottom of which they drop at once, and sail down a smooth shaft." A notice informs the guest that "the price is doubled if you do not leave the toilette as you found it" — hygiene as a matter of considerate behaviour enforced by a modest economic incentive.

The cumulative effect is a world of "simple cleanliness and balance," where "you have only to see the free carriage, the unaffected graciousness of even the common people, to understand how fine and complete the arrangements of this world must be." Cleanliness is not an elite privilege but a universal condition.

## Significance

Sanitation and hygiene exemplify the utopian principle that certain baseline conditions of decent life are not optional. The State does not merely permit cleanliness; it guarantees it through infrastructure, building standards, and public health measures. This is the material foundation of utopian dignity — the conviction that no one should have to live in filth, and that systematic intervention to prevent disease is among the most legitimate functions of organised society.
