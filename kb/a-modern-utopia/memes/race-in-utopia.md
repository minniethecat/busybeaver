# Race in Utopia

Race in the Modern Utopia is a complex and at times troubling topic. Wells dedicates an entire chapter to it — Chapter 10, "Race in Utopia" — and his treatment reflects both the progressive sociological imagination and the racial prejudices of the Edwardian period. The chapter oscillates between a cosmopolitan vision of human unity and a frank endorsement of eugenic policies that modern readers find deeply problematic (Chapter 10).

## Sociology of Belonging

Wells opens the chapter not with race directly but with a general theory of social aggregation. He observes a fundamental tension in human nature:

> The soul of man is in a perpetual vacillation between two conflicting impulses: the desire to assert his individual differences, the desire for distinction, and his terror of isolation. He wants to stand out, but not too far out, and, on the contrary, he wants to merge himself with a group, with some larger body, but not altogether. (Chapter 10, Section 1)

This sociological framework — that human identity is formed in the tension between distinction and belonging — provides the analytical lens through which Wells examines racial identity. Race, in his view, is one of several possible "aggregations" to which people may refer themselves, alongside nation, class, religion, and ultimately humanity itself. The question for the Utopian statesman is which aggregations to encourage and which to dissolve.

## The World State and Racial Homogenisation

The [[World State]], by its nature as a single planetary government, exerts a homogenising pressure on racial and cultural differences. Wells envisions this as largely positive — a dissolution of the tribal solidarities that generate conflict. Global communication, universal education, and the free movement of people across the planet all work against the persistence of sharp racial boundaries.

Wells argues that in the Modern Utopia, racial prejudices would naturally diminish as people come to see themselves as citizens of a single world. The chapter describes a process of "racial amalgamation" that would, over generations, blur the physical distinctions that nineteenth-century race science had invested with so much significance. This vision belongs to a broader progressive tradition that saw racial mixing as a path to human unity, but it also risks erasing cultural diversity in the name of a universalism that implicitly takes European norms as its model.

## Eugenics and Selective Breeding

The most controversial aspect of the chapter is Wells's endorsement of eugenic policies. He discusses the "breeding schemes of Plato and Campanella" and argues that the Modern Utopia, unlike earlier Utopias that "ignored reproductive competition," must confront the question of selective breeding directly. The chapter proposes restrictions on the reproduction of those deemed unfit — the chronically ill, the habitually criminal, those with hereditary defects.

This eugenic discourse is interwoven with racial discourse in ways that are deeply uncomfortable for modern readers. Wells expresses anxiety about demographic trends and speculates about the global distribution of population, sometimes in language that reveals the racial hierarchies of his era. At the same time, he resists the harder forms of scientific racism that were gaining currency in the early twentieth century, insisting that racial differences are superficial compared with the common humanity of all peoples.

## The Global Population Question

A significant portion of Chapter 10 is devoted to global population regulation. Wells proposes that the World State would control population growth, preventing what he saw as the "flooding of the world with low-grade and inefficient peoples." The language reflects the Malthusian and imperial anxieties of the Edwardian period, and it sits uneasily alongside the book's more cosmopolitan and egalitarian impulses.

The chapter's population proposals are part of a larger Utopian concern with rational planning — the idea that the State should manage not only economic production and distribution but also the quantity and quality of the human population itself. This ambition, shared by many progressive thinkers of the period, would later be discredited by its association with coercive eugenics programmes in the twentieth century.

## Critical Assessment

Modern readers approach Chapter 10 with ambivalence. On one hand, Wells's cosmopolitanism — his vision of a world where racial distinctions lose their political significance — remains an inspiring ideal. The sociological framework that opens the chapter, with its emphasis on the constructed nature of group identity, anticipates later social-constructionist theories of race. On the other hand, his eugenic proposals and his casual deployment of racial stereotypes are indefensible by contemporary standards. The chapter is best understood as a transitional document: it moves away from the cruder forms of nineteenth-century racial science toward a more universalist humanism, but it does not complete the journey. Its value for the modern reader lies as much in what it reveals about the limits of Edwardian progressivism as in its utopian prescriptions.
