# Architecture in Utopia

Architecture in the modern utopia is a visible expression of the broader utopian break with inherited forms. Wells imagines buildings freed from the "hampering traditions of Greek temple building, and of Roman and Italian palaces" — a new architecture of simplicity, grace, and functional honesty that uses modern materials to achieve effects impossible in stone or brick. It is one of the most vividly rendered dimensions of the utopian world.

## In A Modern Utopia

The dominant residential form is the quadrangle. Wells describes a typical building in utopian Lucerne: designed by an architect "happily free from the hampering traditions of Greek temple building, and of Roman and Italian palaces; it is simple, unaffected, gracious." The material is "some artificial stone with the dull surface and something of the tint of yellow ivory," with colour slightly irregular, and "a partial confession of girders and pillars" breaking the front with lines of greenish grey (Chapter 9, Section 5).

The quadrangle type permits movement through the city without exposure to the open air: "one may go from end to end of the town along corridors and covered colonnades without emerging by a gateway into the open roads at all." Small shops occupy the colonnades, while larger stores are "usually housed in buildings specially adapted to their needs." The residential edifices are "far finer and more substantial" than terrestrial equivalents.

Looking toward the great urban centres, Wells's vision becomes futurist. The buildings of utopian London will be "the realisation of such dreams" as those glimpsed in "the imaginary architectural landscape of the Pompeian walls" and in Dürer's work — "structures, lighter and bolder than stone or brick can yield." Great arches and domes of glass will span the wider spaces of the town, "the slender beauty of the perfect metal-work far overhead... softened to a fairy-like unsubstantiality." These avenues of architecture will be "emancipated from the last memories of the squat temple boxes of the Greek, the buxom curvatures of Rome; the Goth in us will have taken to steel and countless new materials as kindly as once he took to stone."

Throughout, Wells draws a contrast with terrestrial architecture. The London Tower Bridge is dismissed as "an inconvenient and gawky muddle of ironwork and Flemish architecture." The Greek temple, for all its perfection within its limits, is "a barn with a face." True utopian architecture is not about ornament applied to structure but about the honest and beautiful expression of new constructive possibilities.

The architecture also reflects social arrangements. The autonomy of the household has been reduced by hotels and clubs; "devices to secure roof gardens, loggias, verandahs, and such-like open-air privacies" give variety to the larger apartment buildings. Cooking corners exist but are vestigial, since "the ordinary Utopian would no more think of a special private kitchen for his dinners than he would think of a private flour mill or dairy farm."

## Significance

Architecture in the modern utopia is not merely decorative. It embodies the utopian rejection of inherited forms, the embrace of technological possibility, and the reorganisation of domestic life around collective rather than private provision. It is the built environment of the kinetic utopia — a world in which even the buildings participate in the general movement toward greater freedom and beauty.
