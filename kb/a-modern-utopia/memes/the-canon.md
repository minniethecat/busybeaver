# The Canon

The Canon is the institutional body of texts that forms the intellectual foundation of the Samurai order. It is not a static document but a living corpus, maintained by a dedicated Revision Committee: "the revising and bracing of our Canon is work for poietic as well as kinetic men." The Canon pervades Utopian intellectual life—"very much of it is read and learnt in the schools"—and serves as the fourth intellectual qualification for samurai candidacy. (Chapter 30, "The Samurai")

## Structure: The Double Canon

The Canon is organised into two tiers. "We have now a double Canon, a very fine First Canon, and a Second Canon of work by living men and work of inferior quality." The First Canon contains enduring works—both those inherited from Earth and those produced within Utopia. The Second Canon accommodates contemporary contributions, allowing the corpus to evolve while preserving a stable core. A "satisfactory knowledge of both of these is the fourth intellectual qualification for the samurai." (Chapter 30)

The absorption of earthly literature into the First Canon is illustrated by the inclusion of Henley's "Invictus": "We put that in the Canon almost as soon as he died." This demonstrates the Canon's mechanism for recognising and preserving works of enduring value from any source. (Chapter 30)

## Function and Pervasiveness

The Canon's primary function is to maintain intellectual uniformity among the samurai: "It must keep a sort of uniformity in your tone of thought." By ensuring that all samurai share a common body of reading and reference, the Canon serves as a unifying force across the far-flung World State. The narrator observes that "the Canon pervades our whole world"—it is not merely an elite concern but filters into the general educational system. (Chapter 30)

The Canon also governs conduct. Its provisions extend to marriage regulations, requiring samurai to navigate the tension between personal attachment and duty to the order: "A man under the Rule who loves a woman who does not follow it, must either leave the samurai to marry her, or induce her to accept what..." (Chapter 32)

## The Canon Revision Committee

The maintenance and revision of the Canon is entrusted to a committee composed of both poietic (creative) and kinetic (executive) samurai. This body evaluates new works for inclusion in the Second Canon and periodically revises the First Canon. The triennial council—"the triennial council that revises and alters the Rule"—operates alongside the Canon committee as part of the broader institutional machinery of samurai governance. (Chapter 33)
