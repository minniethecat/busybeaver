# War and Peace in Utopia

War occupies a paradoxical position in Wells's Modern Utopia. It is simultaneously the great evil that the World State has abolished and the historical mechanism through which the Samurai Order first "won their way to the rule of the world." Wells does not imagine a Utopia innocent of violence — he imagines one that has passed through it and emerged on the other side.

## In A Modern Utopia

Wells's discussion of war begins from a geographical premise. Earlier utopias — Plato's Republic, More's Utopia, Bacon's New Atlantis — could afford to be walled gardens because they imagined polities small enough for a mountain valley or an island to provide "sufficient isolation." But "no less than a planet will serve the purpose of a modern Utopia." A world state cannot hide behind walls; it must confront the problem of external relations directly.

The solution is characteristically structural rather than moralistic. War is eliminated not because Utopians are more virtuous but because the conditions that produce war — competing nation-states, economic rivalry, "the stupid illiteracy of the mass of men and the conceit and intellectual indolence of rulers" — no longer exist. Wells is explicit: "Modern war, modern international hostility is, I believe, possible only through the stupid illiteracy of the mass of men and the conceit and intellectual indolence of rulers and those who feed the public mind." Once the world is organised under a single administrative system, the structural basis for international war simply dissolves.

But Wells does not pretend that this transition was peaceful. The Samurai Order, he reveals, "began as a private aggressive cult" and "won their way to the rule of the world" through struggle. The narrator's interlocutor acknowledges "fighting" when asked about the Samurai's rise. The history of Utopia, then, is not a fairy tale of peaceful persuasion but a narrative in which organised moral purpose gradually displaced the old competitive order through a combination of cultural authority and — it is implied — force.

Once established, however, the World State's achievement is unambiguous: "there has been no war, no grinding poverty, not half the disease, and an enormous increase of the order, beauty, and resources of life since the samurai... won their way to the rule of the world." The elimination of war is presented not as a separate accomplishment but as part of a package of civilisational improvements that include public health, economic security, and aesthetic order.

Wells also addresses what might be called the liberal objection to world government — the fear that a unified state would extinguish diversity and produce a "grey" uniformity. His answer is that "in spite of all the pageant of modern war, synthesis is in the trend of the world." The choice is not between diversity and unity but between the violent synthesis imposed by war and the peaceful synthesis achieved by deliberate political organisation. A world state is not the enemy of cultural richness; it is the condition under which cultural richness can flourish without periodically destroying itself.

The Utopian approach to defense is pragmatic rather than pacifist. The Republic of Plato "stood armed ready for defensive war," and Wells's World State presumably maintains a capacity for defense against whatever lies beyond its planetary borders — or against the possibility of internal breakdown. The Samurai themselves, disciplined and organised, constitute a kind of moral defense force, though their primary function is governance rather than military action.

## Significance

The treatment of war in A Modern Utopia reflects Wells's broader philosophical commitment to synthesis over conflict as the organising principle of civilisation. War is not abolished by moral exhortation; it is rendered obsolete by changing the structure of human society. This is consistent with Wells's scepticism about purely moral solutions to social problems — what he elsewhere calls the "scepticism of the Instrument of Thought." Just as a perfectly logical language would imprison thought rather than liberate it, a purely pacifist utopia founded on moral sentiment alone would be fragile and, ultimately, unsustainable. The World State's peace is durable precisely because it rests on structural change, not on the improbable virtue of millions of individuals.
