# Utopian Schools

The educational institutions of the Modern Utopia form a universal, compulsory ladder from early childhood through college at age eighteen. Schools are woven into the fabric of everyday life—"a common garden, an infant school, play rooms, and a playing garden for children, are universal features of the club quadrangles"—and serve as the primary pathway for intellectual development and samurai qualification. (Chapter 24, "Life in the Modern Utopia")

## Structure

The educational system progresses through several stages. Infant schools and play rooms are integrated into residential club quadrangles, providing early childhood care and socialisation. This is followed by a universal lower school system, and finally a college or "upper school" that all students attend until the age of eighteen. Wells describes the system as universally accessible: "All your population?" "With that exception." "Free?" "Of course."

At eighteen, students sit a college leaving examination. "There are several different college courses, but one or other must be followed and a satisfactory examination passed at the end—perhaps ten per cent. fail." Those who fail may re-sit the examination at any time in later life: "anyone who has failed to pass the college leaving examination may at any time in later life sit for it again—and again and again." (Chapter 30, "The Samurai")

The Canon—the body of Samurai texts—pervades the educational system: "very much of it is read and learnt in the schools." This ensures a common intellectual foundation across the Utopian population. (Chapter 30)

## Role in Samurai Qualification

College completion is a prerequisite for samurai candidacy: "the Rule requires that the candidate for the samurai must have passed." The examination serves not merely as an intellectual filter but as a test of character: "Passing an examination is a proof of a certain steadiness of purpose, a certain self-control and submission." After the intellectual qualification comes the physical: "the man must be in sound health, free from certain foul, avoidable, and demoralising diseases, and in good training. We reject men who are fat, or thin and flabby."

The samurai themselves staff the educational hierarchy: "all our head teachers and disciplinary heads of colleges, our judges, barristers, employers of labour beyond a certain limit, practising medical men, legislators, must be samurai." (Chapter 30)

## Disciplinary Schools

For those who fail to meet social expectations between eighteen and twenty-five, the Utopia provides separate institutions: "There will be disciplinary schools and colleges for the young, fair and happy places, but with less confidence and more restraint than the schools and colleges of the ordinary world." These are located "in remote and solitary regions," fenced in and isolated from the general population. The disciplinary function is explicit: "remote from all temptation, the defective citizen will be schooled. There will be no masking of the lesson." (Chapter 17, "Failure in a Modern Utopia")
