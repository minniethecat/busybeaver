## Definition

[[george-macdonald|MacDonald]] distinguishes between two kinds of fear: the [[awe|holy fear]] that is reverent awe before [[god|God]], and the anxious fear that is the soul's dis[[faith|trust]] of divine goodness. The speaker confesses: "I was disturbed — with fear, in sooth, not awe" (001-027), drawing a sharp line between fear-as-panic and fear-as-[[awe|reverence]]. The goal of the spiritual life is not the elimination of fear but its transformation — from the fear that hides to the fear that draws near. [[christ|Christ]] himself provides the model: "He feared lest, in the [[pain|suffering]] waste" (003-029), showing that even the lord can fear for others without fearing for himself.

## Key Passages

The speaker's self-diagnosis in the opening chapter establishes the distinction: "I was disturbed — with fear, in sooth, not awe" (001-027). The disturbance is not the holy trembling of [[awe|reverence]] but the anxious agitation of dis[[faith|trust]]. This negative fear is associated with spiritual paralysis: "Daring all truth, nor fearing anything; / Mighty in love" (002-022) — the ideal state is fearlessness born of [[love]], not fearlessness born of insensibility.

The positive fear — awe — is implied by its absence in the speaker's confession. True fear of God would be compatible with love, as the speaker's address to "dear [[christ|Christ]]" includes the plea: "remember then thy fear" (003-024), suggesting [[christ|Christ]]'s own [[awe|holy fear]] is a model for the believer. The cosmic dimension appears in the description of "lord of lords" who "feared lest" (003-029), where fear becomes compassionate concern rather than personal anxiety.

The speaker describes being "Distract with fears of what may next ensue" (007-013), where fear multiplies into anxious anticipation of the future — the opposite of [[faith|trust]] in divine providence. Against this, the assertion "Thy child should never fear though hell should gape" (007-014) makes fear incompatible with filial [[trust|trust in God]]. The child of [[god|God]], secure in the father's love, has no [[earth|ground]]s for fear regardless of external threat.

The speaker's inventory of his own condition includes "love and weakness, laziness and fear" (011-007), placing fear among the moral impediments to be overcome. Yet [[george-macdonald|MacDonald]] does not simply condemn fear: "hope, fear, [[infinite|boundless]] desire" (001-010) places fear alongside hope as a [[god|God]]-directed passion — [[wake|awake]]ned by [[god|God]], potentially oriented toward [[god|God]], needing only the right object.

## Related Concepts

[[holy|Holy]] fear connects to [[awe]] and [[worship]] as reverent response to the divine. Anxious fear links to [[darkness]], [[doubt]], and [[sin]]. Fear's transformation through love connects to [[love]] as the expelling power (1 John 4:18) and [[grace]] as divine reassurance. The contrast between fear and [[faith|trust]] ties to [[faith]], while fear's place in the speaker's moral inventory links to [[will]] and [[obedience]].
