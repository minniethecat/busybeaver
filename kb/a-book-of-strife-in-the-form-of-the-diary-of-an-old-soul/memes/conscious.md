In [[george-macdonald|George MacDonald]]'s "A Book of [[strife|Strife]] in the Form of The Diary of an Old [[soul|Soul]]", consciousness is not merely psychological self-awareness but the soul's capacity to be aware of [[god|God]] — and, in the same act, aware of itself as known by [[god|God]]. The speaker's consciousness is the field upon which divine light falls: [[god|God]]'s rays reach "across the farthest brink of consciousness" (004-023). Yet consciousness can also be a gulf, a darkness "all astir" (007-001) when [[god|God]] seems absent. The poem traces consciousness from sleep to wakefulness, from the dim mirror of human imagination to the full [[morning|dawn]] of eternal awareness.

## Consciousness Illumined by God

The speaker describes consciousness as a receptive surface upon which divine [[mind|thought]] plays. [[god|God]] "passest through all the channels of my mind," and the "sun of [[mind|thought]]" sends rays across consciousness (004-023). Consciousness is not self-generated light but the capacity to receive light from beyond itself. When [[god|God]] is present, consciousness is luminous; when [[god|God]] seems absent, consciousness turns to blackness. The speaker never claims to illuminate himself — consciousness is always lit from without.

> The sun of thought, across the farthest brink / Of consciousness thou sendest me thy rays. (004-023)

## Consciousness as Darkness

When the speaker feels cut off from [[god|God]], consciousness becomes not illumination but torment: "My consciousness the blackness all astir" (007-001). A whirlwind sweeps through the soul's tent, and "poor Fancy's doves are swept away." Consciousness without [[god|God]]'s presence is homeless: "No pilgrim I, a homeless wanderer — For how canst Thou be in the darkness deep, Who d[[water|well]]est only in the living day?" The dark consciousness is not the absence of consciousness but consciousness emptied of its only proper content.

> I sit alone, a sorrow half asleep, / My consciousness the blackness all astir. (007-001)

## Consciousness Beyond the Self

[[george-macdonald|MacDonald]] distinguishes between the "clasp of consciousness" and [[god|God]] himself. The speaker declares, "Not on the clasp of consciousness — on thee / My life depends" (008-006). To cling to one's own awareness is to mistake the mirror for the thing mirrored. True life is anchored not in the act of being conscious but in the one of whom consciousness is conscious. The speaker can "all forget, so thou remember, [[christ|Lord]]" — consciousness can be surrendered because [[god|God]]'s remembering is more reliable than human awareness.

> Not on the clasp of consciousness — on thee / My life depends; and I can well afford / All to forget, so thou remember, Lord. (008-006)

## The Fear of False Consciousness

The speaker entertains a fearful possibility: what if, at the final encounter, [[god|God]]'s face does not match "poor imagination's mirror"? (008-024). The result would be a "conscious blight" — consciousness turned to poison. This is the risk of constructing [[god|God]] in one's own image: the consciousness that seemed so reliable a guide may prove to have been a distorting glass. The "conscious blight" is the soul's horror at dis[[garment|covering]] it has been conscious of a projection, not a person.

> Beggared of hope, my heart a conscious blight, / Amazed and lost — death's bitterness come and not passed? (008-024)

## Eternal Consciousness

The poem's resolution places consciousness not in the present moment but in the eschaton: "Not I until my consciousness eternal wakes" (012-028). True [[self|selfhood]] is deferred until consciousness is fully [[wake|awake]]ned in the presence of the soul's maker. Until that "morning breaks," life is "delayed." Consciousness, in this vision, is not a possession but a promise — something the speaker will receive when he meets [[god|God]] "absolutely," soul to making [[soul|Soul]].

> I am not I until that morning breaks, / Not I until my consciousness eternal wakes. (012-028)
