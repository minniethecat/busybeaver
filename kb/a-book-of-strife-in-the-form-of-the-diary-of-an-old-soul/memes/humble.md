In [[george-macdonald|George MacDonald]]'s "A Book of [[strife|Strife]] in the Form of The Diary of an Old [[soul|Soul]]", humility is not self-hatred or the pretence of worthlessness but the soul's true orientation toward [[god|God]]. The speaker opens the year asking for a "humbler heart" (001-001), and the entire diary traces the slow education of humility through pain, obe[[death|die]]nce, and divine feeding. Humility is the condition in which the child's soul receives the "high Parent-love" (004-005); it is the posture that makes obe[[death|die]]nce "bold" (012-011). Without humility, the soul cannot see [[god|God]] — with it, the soul looks "straight through" the window of creation.

## The Humbler Heart

The poem begins with a confession: the speaker once acted with "youthful might," but now, "old," he asks for a "humbler heart, if not the brain of youth" (001-001). Humility here is not a reduction of ambition but a reorientation of it. The speaker does not ask to do less but to do "with better [[vision|sight]]." The path back to his "best" — to "young eyes and heart and brain" — runs "by the path of pain." Humility is not the opposite of vigour but the condition of its [[peace|rest]]oration.

> Grant me, now old, to do — with better sight, / And humbler heart, if not the brain of youth. (001-001)

## Humility as the Child's Posture

[[george-macdonald|MacDonald]] images humility through the figure of a child awaiting a parent's gift. The "hues of [[dream|dreamland]]" are but "hint-[[shadow|shadows]]" of [[beauty|splendour]]s that "the high Parent-love will yet unroll / Before his child's [[obedience|obedient]], humble soul" (004-005). Humility is not grovelling but looking up — the expectant posture of a child who knows that what is coming is better than anything already possessed. The parent's love requires the child's humble receptivity; without it, the gift cannot be given.

> Before his child's obedient, humble soul. (004-005)

## The Humble Sheep

Humility takes pastoral form when the speaker prays, "Help me this day to be thy humble [[lamb|sheep]], / Eating thy grass, and following, thou before" (004-019). The humble [[lamb|sheep]] does not lead, does not argue, does not demand better pasture — it eats what is given and follows where the shepherd goes. The prayer is immediately preceded by a self-rebuke for "making his gift of vision [[emptiness|void]] and null" through hypocritical talk. Humility, in this image, is the [[silence|quiet]] alternative to religious performance — not writing about the door but entering it.

> Help me this day to be thy humble sheep, / Eating thy grass, and following, thou before. (004-019)

## Humble-Bold Obedience

The poem's most concentrated statement about humility joins it paradoxically to boldness: "By love made and obe[[death|die]]nce humble-bold, / I shall straight through its window [[god|God]] behold" (012-011). Humility and boldness are not opposites but complements. The soul that has been made by love and shaped by obe[[death|die]]nce acquires a boldness that is entirely humble — it dares to approach [[god|God]] not because it is worthy but because it has been loved. The "humble-bold" soul looks "straight through" the window of a flower and sees [[god|God]]; no elaborate theology is needed when humility has cleared the glass.

> By love made and obedience humble-bold, / I shall straight through its window God behold. (012-011)

## Feeding the Humble

[[divine|Divine]] power operates by "feeding the faint divine in humble men" (005-031). [[god|God]]'s work is not to exalt the proud but to nourish what is already divine but fainting in humble souls. The "heart-power" that goes out from [[god|God]] through all realms works toward the "holy, satisfying hour, / When all shall love, and all be loved again." Humility is the condition in which this feeding can occur; pride has already eaten its fill and is not hungry.

> Feeding the faint divine in humble men. (005-031)
