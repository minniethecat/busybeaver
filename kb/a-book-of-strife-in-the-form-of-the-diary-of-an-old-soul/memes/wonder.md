Wonder in [[george-macdonald|George MacDonald]]'s "A Book of [[strife|Strife]] in the Form of The Diary of an Old [[soul|Soul]]" is the soul's natural response to the nearness of [[god|God]]. It arises not from the spectacular but from the intimate — the realisation that the maker of men stoops to the [[soul]]'s weakness. Wonder is closely allied with [[joy]] and [[gladness]]: the speaker marvels at how close he must come to [[god|God]] before his debt of [[human|humanity]] can be paid, and the marvel itself is joy. When wonder fails, it is because the [[mind]] has made "poor claims" upon a perfect [[christ|Lord]].

## Wonder at Divine Closeness

The speaker takes comfort even from his own badness, for it measures the [[distance]] [[god|God]] must travel to reach him: "It is for lack of thee that I am bad" (004-027). The logical conclusion is staggeringly intimate — the worse the soul, the closer [[god|God]] must draw near to heal it. This proximity provokes wonder: "How close to thee!" The soul's [[joy|gladness]] is not self-generated but the reflex of discovered nearness.

> "How close to thee!" — no wonder, soul, thou art glad! / Oneness with him is the eternal gladness. (004-027)

## The Wonder of Sleep

The speaker anticipates the mystery of sleep as a portal to divine encounter: "Now, ere I sleep, I wonder what I shall [[dream]]" (004-006). [[sleep|Sleep]] is not mere un[[conscious|consciousness]] but a state in which "some sense of being, utter new, may come." The dark hours teem with shapes and scents "of other sort than those that haunt the day," hinting at realities the waking mind cannot grasp. Wonder here is not [[mind|intellect]]ual curiosity but reverent expectation — the soul waits in the dark for gifts the day cannot give.

> Now, ere I sleep, I wonder what I shall dream. / Some sense of being, utter new, may come / Into my soul while I am blind and dumb. (004-006)

## Wonder Lost Through Small Gods

Wonder fails when the [[god]] one approaches is too small. The speaker reflects: "I do not wonder men can ill believe / Who make poor claims upon thee, perfect [[christ|Lord]]" (008-016). A diminished god provokes no [[fear|awe]] — only a god worthy of the name can draw forth wonder. The speaker [[faith|trust]]s most when he would receive most, for the [[infinite]] [[god|God]] is the only one who can answer infinite need. Wonder is thus a function of theology: a small god breeds small wor[[vessel|ship]]; the perfect [[christ|Lord]] breeds astonished faith.

> I do not wonder men can ill believe / Who make poor claims upon thee, perfect Lord; / Then most I trust when most I would receive. (008-016)

## The Wonder of God's Ways

The poem culminates in the exclamation: "Thy ways are wonderful, maker of men!" (011-027). This wonder is not abstract but personal — it arises from the gift of a [[child]] who grows and is claimed by another. The ways of [[god|God]] are wonderful precisely because they exceed human calculation. The maker of men does not consult human expectation; his ways evoke wonder because they come from beyond the horizon of human comprehension.
