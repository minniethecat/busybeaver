In [[george-macdonald|George MacDonald]]'s "A Book of [[strife|Strife]] in the Form of The Diary of an Old [[soul|Soul]]", the tent is the central image for the mortal body — a temporary, fragile dwelling that the soul inhabits during its [[earth|earthly]] pilgrimage. Drawing on the Pauline metaphor of the body as tent (2 Corinthians 5:1–4), [[george-macdonald|MacDonald]] develops the image with characteristic vividness: the tent "flutters" in the wind, strange creatures move within its folds, and yet it is also the place where [[god|God]] may come "to sit beside" the heart. The tent is at once vulnerability and possibility — the soul's exposure to the elements is also its openness to divine visitation.

## The Fluttering Tent

The poem's July section opens with a cry of distress: "Alas, my tent! see through it a whirlwind sweep" (007-001). The tent is not a solid house but a fabric structure that shakes in every storm. [[george-macdonald|MacDonald]] intensifies the image: "somewhere in my fluttering tent, Strange creatures, half tame" (007-002) — the body is inhabited not only by the soul but by impulses, fears, and desires that are not fully under the will's control. The "fluttering" captures both the body's [[death|mortality]] and the soul's unsettled condition within it.

> Alas, my tent! see through it a whirlwind sweep. Somewhere in my fluttering tent, strange creatures, half tame. (007-001, 007-002)

## A Tent for God

Despite its fragility, the tent has a sacred purpose. The speaker prays: "And make a tent for [[god|God]] to come and sit beside" (012-013). The body, for all its fluttering, can become a tabernacle — a meeting-place between the human and the divine. This transforms the meaning of the tent's weakness: the very permeability that lets in the whirlwind also lets in [[god|God]]. What makes the tent vulnerable to chaos also makes it available to grace.

> And make a tent for God to come and sit beside. (012-013)

## Morning's Delight in the Tent

The tent is not only a site of distress but of joy. In one of the poem's most radiant images, the speaker prays: "fill my tent with laughing morn's [[joy|delight]]" (007-003). The tent that shakes in the whirlwind can also be filled with [[morning|dawn]]'s [[joy|gladness]]. This is the resurrected body anticipated — the same tent, but transformed from a place of [[strife|struggle]] into a place of light. The "laughing morn" suggests [[rise|resurrection]], the morning of a new creation breaking into the soul's temporary dwelling.

> Fill my tent with laughing morn's delight. (007-003)

## The Heavenly Tent

At the poem's close, the tent imagery shifts from the mortal body to the eternal dwelling: "with thy father in some [[heaven|heavenly]] tent" (012-030). The tent remains a tent — not a palace or a fortress — even in heaven. This is significant: [[george-macdonald|MacDonald]] does not imagine the afterlife as the exchange of a flimsy tent for a stone mansion but as the transfiguration of the tent itself. The [[heaven|heavenly]] tent is still a dwelling of pilgrimage, but now the pilgrimage is complete and the Father shares the tent with the child.

> With thy father in some heavenly tent. (012-030)

## Beauty's Tent

The tent image extends beyond the body to creation itself. [[george-macdonald|MacDonald]] speaks of the world "o'ercanopied with [[beauty|Beauty]]'s tent" (006-029), a [[garment|covering]] of divine [[beauty|loveliness]] spread over all things. Through this canopy "flies many a golden-winged bird" — moments of transcendence that pierce the fabric of ordinary perception. The world itself is a tent, temporary and permeable, through which divine beauty constantly breaks.

> O'ercanopied with Beauty's tent, through which flies many a golden-winged bird. (006-029)
