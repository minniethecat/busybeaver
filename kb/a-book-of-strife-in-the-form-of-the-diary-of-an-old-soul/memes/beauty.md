Beauty in [[george-macdonald|George MacDonald]]'s "A Book of [[strife|Strife]] in the Form of The Diary of an Old [[soul|Soul]]" (1880) is a theological category before it is an aesthetic one. [[george-macdonald|MacDonald]] treats beauty as [[god|God]]'s [[wind|breath]] made visible — not an optional decoration upon creation but the very character of divine making. The diary traces beauty from its source in [[god|God]]'s creative act through its manifestation in the natural world to its role as a garment that reveals rather than hides.

## Old Strength nor Yet Fresh Beauty

The diary's March opening presents beauty as perpetually renewed:

> Old strength nor yet fresh beauty shall they lack. (003-001)

"Old [[power|strength]]" and "fresh beauty" are paired — beauty is not the fragile opposite of [[power|strength]] but its companion. What is strong in [[george-macdonald|MacDonald]]'s vision is also beautiful; what is beautiful retains its [[power|strength]]. The line promises that the soul, like the natural world it inhabits, shall lack neither: endurance and loveliness are twin gifts.

## Thy Breath Wakes Beauty

May reveals the source of all beauty:

> Thy breath wakes beauty, love, and bliss, and prayer,
> And fills the hollow of the waiting air. (005-006)

Beauty is "waked" by [[god|God]]'s [[wind|breath]] — it is not self-generated but called forth. The four gifts that [[god|God]]'s [[wind|breath]] "wakes" — beauty, love, [[joy|bliss]], and prayer — form a chain: beauty leads to love, love to [[joy|bliss]], [[joy|bliss]] to prayer. Beauty is the first motion outward from [[god|God]], the initial ripple of [[presence|divine presence]] in the created order.

## The Evening Dress of Beauty

The same May meditation develops the image with striking tenderness:

> Put on the evening dress of beauty,
> Thy sunset-flush, of gold and purple blent. (005-017)

Beauty is a "dress" that the world "puts on" at [[god|God]]'s command. The "sunset-flush, of gold and purple blent" is not the world's own possession but a garment given by [[god|God]]. The metaphor is intimate — dressing is an act of care — and it implies that the world's beauty is a gift of love, not a property of matter. [[god|God]] clothes the world as one clothes a beloved.

## Fair Shore We See, Fair Ocean

March returns to beauty as a shore that promises more beyond itself:

> Fair shore we see, fair ocean; but behind
> Lie infinite reaches of the eternal mind. (003-009)

The "fair shore" and "fair ocean" are visible, tangible beauties. But they are not the terminus — they are the [[door|threshold]]. "Behind" them lie "infinite reaches of the eternal mind." Beauty is a window, not a wall. The visible beautiful world does not obscure [[god|God]] but discloses [[god|God]], if the soul has eyes to see past the gift to the Giver.
