Awe in [[george-macdonald|George MacDonald]]'s "A Book of [[strife|Strife]] in the Form of The Diary of an Old [[soul|Soul]]" is the proper response to the sacred — a reverential [[wonder]] that stands opposite to mere fear. The speaker draws the distinction sharply: when [[death]] knocks at his door, he confesses "I was disturbed — with fear, in sooth, not [[fear|awe]]" (001-027), and is [[shame|ashamed]] of the failure. True [[fear|awe]] appears elsewhere in the poem as an "arctic [[fear|awe]]" (004-007) — a strange, cold-bright joy that accompanies encounter with the dead. Awe belongs to the soul that recognises the holy; fear belongs to the soul that cannot find [[God]] in the house.

## Fear, Not Awe

The speaker's most direct treatment of [[fear|awe]] comes through its absence. When [[death|Death]] knocks at his "thin door," the speaker looks out and sees "the shape uncouth, I had not seen before" (001-027). His response is fear — "in sooth, not [[fear|awe]]" — and he is immediately [[shame|ashamed]]. The distinction matters: fear is the natural response to a threat; [[fear|awe]] is the spiritual response to mystery. The speaker's shame arises because he knows he ought to have met [[death|Death]] with [[fear|awe]] — with the recognition that even [[death|Death]] belongs to [[God]] — but instead he reacts as one who cannot find [[god|God]] anywhere: "Alas! I could not find thee in the house."

> Yestereve, Death came, and knocked at my thin door. / I from my window looked: the thing I saw, / The shape uncouth, I had not seen before. / I was disturbed — with fear, in sooth, not awe; / Whereof ashamed, I instantly did rouse / My will to seek thee — only to fear the more: / Alas! I could not find thee in the house. (001-027)

## The Arctic Awe

The word "[[fear|awe]]" appears most vividly in a dream-vision where the speaker meets a woman who has [[death|die]]d: "A strange auroral [[joy|bliss]], an arctic [[fear|awe]], / A new, outworldish joy awoke intense, / To think I talked with one that verily was dead" (004-007). Here [[fear|awe]] is not cold fear but cold [[joy]] — "arctic" suggesting both the chill of the supernatural and the brightness of the aurora. The speaker "scarce believed for [[joy|gladness]]" what he was told. This is [[fear|awe]] in its proper register: the thrill of encountering what lies beyond the ordinary circle of [[life]], not as threat but as revelation.

> Late, in a dream, an unknown lady I saw / Stand on a tomb; down she to me stepped thence. / "They tell me," quoth I, "thou art one of the dead!" / And scarce believed for gladness the yea she said; / A strange auroral bliss, an arctic awe, / A new, outworldish joy awoke intense, / To think I talked with one that verily was dead. (004-007)

## Awe and the Knowledge of God

Underlying both passages is the intimate link between [[fear|awe]] and the [[conscious|awareness]] of [[god|God]]'s [[presence]]. When the speaker cannot find [[god|God]], [[fear|awe]] collapses into fear. When he stands before the supernatural — even the supernatural wrapped in death — and recognises it as within [[god|God]]'s order, [[fear|awe]] becomes [[joy]]. Awe is the affective signature of a [[soul]] that sees through the veil of the ordinary to the divine reality beyond, while fear is the signature of a soul that has lost [[vision|sight]] of [[god|God]] and sees only the shape at the door.
