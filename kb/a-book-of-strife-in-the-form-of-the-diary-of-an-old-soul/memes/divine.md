The divine in [[george-macdonald|George MacDonald]]'s "A Book of [[strife|Strife]] in the Form of The Diary of an Old [[soul|Soul]]" (1880) is not an abstract attribute of a distant [[god|God]] but a living presence that seeks to ind[[water|well]] and transform the human soul. [[george-macdonald|MacDonald]] uses "divine" to describe both [[god|God]]'s essential nature and the quality of life that humans are called to participate in — the goal of the spiritual life is to become "all divine" through union with [[god|God]]'s will.

## Definition in MacDonald's Thought

For [[george-macdonald|MacDonald]], the divine is characterized primarily by love. The opening stanzas of January establish this identification immediately:

> O Love divine, rise up in me and move me (004-009)

[[love|Love]] is not merely something [[god|God]] *has*; it is what [[god|God]] *is*. The adjective "divine" attaches to love as its essential qualifier — there is no love worth the name that is not divine in origin. The speaker's plea is for this divine love to become the motive force within him, displacing his own lesser desires.

The divine nature is also associated with light and life. The speaker describes himself drinking "the [[sun|divine sun]]beams" (002-024), an image of passive receptivity — the soul does not generate divine light but absorbs it like a sponge absorbs water. This receptivity is the precondition for transformation.

## The Soul's Divinization

[[george-macdonald|MacDonald]]'s most radical claim about the divine is that humans are called to share in it:

> Unworthy is my life till all divine, / Till thou see in me only what is thine. (001-005)

The speaker does not ask to be *near* the divine or to *admire* the divine — he asks to *become* divine. This is not self-deification but theosis: the creature so fully surrendered to the Creator that only the Creator's life remains visible. [[george-macdonald|MacDonald]] echoes this in a later stanza:

> Brothers! sisters holy! hearts divine! / Then I shall be all yours, and you all mine. (001-006)

The community of the redeemed is described as "[[heart|hearts]] divine" — not metaphorically but actually, through participation in the divine nature.

The metaphor of gardening captures this gradual transformation:

> The heart hast planted, gardener divine, / A scion of the tree of life (001-021)

[[god|God]] is the "gardener divine" who plants something of [[god|God]]'s own life ("a scion of the tree of life") into the [[human|human heart]]. The divine life grows slowly, organically, from within.

## Divine Power in Human Weakness

[[george-macdonald|MacDonald]] frequently pairs [[power|divine power]] with [[weakness|human weakness]], seeing them not as opposites but as complements:

> That perfects strength divine in weakness' fire (009-007)

Divine [[power|strength]] is not demonstrated by overcoming weakness but by being perfected *within* it. The "fire" of weakness — the burning experience of limitation and failure — becomes the very medium in which divine [[power|strength]] is made complete.

> Feeding the faint divine in humble men (005-031)

The "faint divine" is the small spark of [[god|God]]'s life present even in "humble men." [[god|God]]'s work is not to replace the human with the divine but to feed and grow the divine that is already there, however faintly.
