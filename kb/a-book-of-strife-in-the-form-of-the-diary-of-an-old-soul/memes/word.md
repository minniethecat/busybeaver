## Definition

[[george-macdonald|MacDonald]]'s treatment of "word" spans from the divine Word (Logos) to the speaker's faltering human language. The speaker is acutely conscious of his words' inadequacy: "What though my words glance sideways from the thing" (005-001). Yet words are not abandoned — they are the raw material that divine [[grace]] may transform into [[song]]. This theology of language is incarnational: as the Word became flesh, so human words may become [[clay|vessel]]s of divine truth despite their clumsiness. The speaker's metapoetic [[conscious|awareness]] — writing poems about the inadequacy of his poems — is itself an act of [[faith]].

## Key Passages

The speaker opens the fifth chapter with a confession of linguistic failure: "What though my words glance sideways from the thing" (005-001). Words are arrows that consistently miss their target; they "glance sideways" — a metaphor suggesting both deflection and indirectness. The speaker describes "the necessity / Of stubborn words, that shift sluggish about" (002-028), where language itself resists the poet's intent.

Yet the divine Word stands in contrast to human faltering. When [[christ|Christ]] speaks: "The word he then did speak, fits now a" (005-018), suggesting the eternal fittingness of divine speech — what [[god|God]] speaks remains true across time. The speaker's prayer invokes this creative power: "Thy words may fan to a [[fire|flame]] the low prayer" (003-013), where divine words are not mere information but transformative fire.

The speaker's ultimate hope is linguistic redemption: "stumbling words will some day turn to song" (010-030). Words are not discarded but transfigured — the same syllables, raised to [[worship]]. Meanwhile, the speaker thanks [[god|God]] for language itself: "I thank thee for the word" (003-019), acknowledging even imperfect speech as gift.

[[george-macdonald|MacDonald]] explores the relation[[vessel|ship]] between words and reality: "Thou far! — that word the holy truth doth blur" (007-019). The word "far" applied to [[god|God]] is a distortion — not because language is inherently false but because spatial metaphors misrepresent [[presence|divine presence]]. The speaker desires ordered speech: "my soul talk to thee in ordered words" (003-020), where "ordered" implies both coherence and submission to divine order.

A single loving word carries disproportionate power: "A loving word may set some door ajar" (005-025), where the smallest unit of language, animated by [[love]], opens otherwise closed possibilities.

## Related Concepts

Word is the raw material that grace transforms into [[song]] and the vehicle of [[prayer]]. The divine Word connects to [[christ]] as Logos and [[truth]] as [[god|God]]'s self-expression. The [[strife|struggle]] with language links to [[humility]], while the hope for words to become song ties to [[hope]] and [[worship]]. The image of words as fire-kindling connects to [[fire]] and [[spirit]].
