## Definition

[[george-macdonald|MacDonald]] addresses [[god|God]] and [[christ|Christ]] as *Master* throughout the poems, but the title carries none of the harshness of mere dominion. The Master is the craftsman shaping a house (the soul), the teacher whose mind is worth exploring, and the lord whose authority is exercised through love. "Law is our schoolmaster. Our master, [[christ|Christ]], / Lived under all our laws, yet always prayed" (009-021) — [[lord|the Master]] submits to the very law he fulfills, transforming mastery from above into solidarity from within. The Master's work is patient, often invisible to the one being shaped: "He builds me now — and if I cannot see / At any time what he is doing with me, / 'Tis that he makes the house for me too grand" (007-015).

## Key Passages

> "How better then couldst thou, O master, come, / Than from thy home across into my home, / Straight o'er the marches, all my thorns among?" (004-024)

The Master does not summon from a distance but crosses the border into the disciple's own thorny territory.

> "How should the work the master goes about / Fit the vague sketch my compasses have planned? / I am his house — for him to go in and out. / He builds me now — and if I cannot see / At any time what he is doing with me, / 'Tis that he makes the house for me too grand." (007-015)

The Master's architectural plan exceeds the disciple's "vague sketch"; the house under construction is too grand for the inhabitant yet to comprehend.

> "In my dead moments, master, stir the prayers. / Till they arise, I cannot think thee true." (007-024)

Even the impulse to pray must be stirred by [[lord|the Master]]; the speaker's faith depends on divine initiative.

> "Thy mind, my master, I will dare explore; / What we are told, that we are meant to know. / Into thy soul I search yet more and more, / Led by the lamp of my desire and woe." (003-030)

The Master's mind is not off-limits but is given to be explored; desire and [[pain|suffering]] are the lamps that light the search.

> "Law is our schoolmaster. Our master, Christ, / Lived under all our laws, yet always prayed — / So walked the water when the storm was highest." (009-021)

[[christ|Christ]] [[lord|the Master]] subjects himself to the law that is the schoolmaster, transforming it from external constraint to lived obe[[death|die]]nce.

> "The prince of this world came, and nothing found / In thee, O master; but, ah, woe is me! / He cannot pass me, on other business bound, / But, spying in me things familiar, he / Casts over me the shadow of his flight." (009-028)

The Master is impervious to the "prince of this world"; the disciple is not — the contrast drives the speaker's [[grief|lament]].

## Related Concepts

Mastery in [[george-macdonald|MacDonald]]'s lexicon is the loving authority that shapes [[soul]], [[will]], and [[obedience]]. The Master is inseparable from [[god]] and [[christ]], and his work is the substance of [[prayer]]. The Master–disciple relation stands opposite to the false autonomy of the [[self]]. The Master's pedagogy is explored further in [[trust]] and culminates in [[love]].
