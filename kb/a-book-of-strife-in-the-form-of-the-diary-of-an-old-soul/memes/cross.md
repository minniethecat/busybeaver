The cross in [[george-macdonald|George MacDonald]]'s "A Book of [[strife|Strife]] in the Form of The Diary of an Old [[soul|Soul]]" is not merely the historical instrument of [[christ|Christ]]'s death but the living pattern of the soul's transformation. The speaker's aspiration — "to hang with nails upon thy cross" (005-006) — is startling in its directness: he seeks not to admire the cross from a distance but to be fastened to it. The cross here is the shape that [[love]] takes in a world that resists it, and to be a disciple is to be conformed to that shape.

## The Cross as Participation

The speaker's [[prayer|petition]] is radical: "Give me to share thy travail of thy soul, / That I may know thy [[grief|sorrow]] and thy joy" — followed by the even more direct "To hang with nails upon thy cross" (005-006). [[george-macdonald|MacDonald]] does not spiritualise the cross into a metaphor for general hard[[vessel|ship]]. The nails are specific, the hanging is bodily, and the identification with [[christ|Christ]] is total. This is not [[imitation]] at a distance but a request for incorporation — to be placed inside [[christ|Christ]]'s own experience of self-[[gift|giving]].

> To hang with nails upon thy cross (005-006)

## The Hammer and the Nails

The speaker ac[[wisdom|knowledge]]s the cost with unflinching clarity: "Let not our heart be troubled at the clang / Of hammer and nails, nor [[fear|dread]] the spear's keen fang" (005-006). The "clang" is auditory — [[george-macdonald|MacDonald]] makes the reader hear the sound of construction, the hammer driving iron through flesh into wood. The spear's "keen fang" images [[pain|suffering]] as a predator's bite. Yet the [[prayer|petition]] is not for a[[emptiness|void]]ance but for untroubled endurance: "let not our heart be troubled." The heart will register the pain, but it need not be mastered by fear.

> Let not our heart be troubled at the clang / Of hammer and nails (005-006)

## The Cross-Shaped Life

For [[george-macdonald|MacDonald]], the cross is not only the moment of atonement but the template for all [[christ|Christ]]ian [[discipleship]]. The speaker's desire to be nailed to [[christ|Christ]]'s cross is a desire to have his life take the shape of self-[[gift|giving]] love. This is the "little [[christ|Christ]]" formation — "Take me and make a little [[christ|Christ]] of me" (005-019) follows immediately in the text. The cross is where [[self|the self]] is unmade as a centre of its own [[will]] and remade as a centre of divine love flowing outward. The nails are what hold [[self|the self]] in place while this transformation occurs.
