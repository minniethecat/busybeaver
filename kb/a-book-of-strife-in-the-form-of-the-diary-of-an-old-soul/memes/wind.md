# Wind

The **wind** in [[A Book of Strife in the Form of The Diary of an Old Soul]] is the primary poetic image for the [[holy|Holy]] [[spirit]] — invisible in itself, known only by its effects, and utterly beyond human control. [[george-macdonald|George MacDonald]] draws on the Johannine discourse on the [[spirit|Spirit]] (John 3:8) to develop a rich image of divine freedom and transformative power.

## Faith Opens to God's Wind

[[george-macdonald|MacDonald]] presents the wind as already blowing — the question is whether the soul has opened itself to receive it:

> "Faith opens all the windows to God's wind." (008-005)

[[faith]] is not the effort to produce spiritual experience but the act of opening what has been closed. The wind is [[god]]'s initiative; the human response is to stop resisting and let it enter. This image of opened windows recurs throughout the poem cycle as a figure of spiritual receptivity.

## The Spirit-Wind as Throughfare

The poet prays to become a passage for the divine wind rather than an obstacle to it:

> "Make me a throughfare to thy great spirit-wind; / Then shall I rest within thy restful mind." (012-009)

Becoming a "throughfare" is [[george-macdonald|MacDonald]]'s distinctive image of sanctification — [[self|the self]] is not annihilated but rendered transparent to divine action. [[rest]] is found not in passivity but in perfect conveyance of the [[spirit|Spirit]]'s movement.

## Wind and Cosmic Music

In one of the poem's most [[song|music]]al passages, [[george-macdonald|MacDonald]] imagines the wind transforming the world into instrumentation for [[worship|praise]]:

> "The brooks be bells; the winds, in caverns dumb, / Wake fife and oboe from the earth's deep tomb." (012-012)

The winds that pass through "caverns dumb" produce [[song|music]] where there was silence. This is the [[spirit|Spirit]]'s work: making the mute creation sing. The image of the "earth's deep tomb" yielding [[song|music]] suggests [[resurrection]], with wind as the breath that revives what was dead.

## The Wind That Strips

The divine wind is not always gentle. [[george-macdonald|MacDonald]] invokes it as a purgative force that strips away pretension:

> "In the great wind that from thy gulf doth blow / Let my rag-rights go." (002-017)

The "rag-rights" — the tattered claims to autonomy and merit — must be surrendered to the wind of [[god]]'s righteousness. What the wind takes is only what the soul is better without.
