Spirit in [[george-macdonald|George MacDonald]]'s "A Book of [[strife|Strife]] in the Form of The Diary of an Old [[soul|Soul]]" (1880) operates on two planes simultaneously: the divine Spirit ([[god|God]]'s presence active within the soul) and the human spirit (the [[breath|God-breathed]] faculty by which the soul reaches toward its source). [[george-macdonald|MacDonald]] treats these not as separate entities but as a continuum — the human spirit is the organ [[god|God]]'s Spirit uses to transfigure the whole person.

## Definition in MacDonald's Thought

The Spirit is first and foremost an animating fire — not a distant influence but an interior heat:

> Were there but some deep, holy spell, whereby / Always I should remember thee—some mode / Of feeling the pure heat-throb momently / Of the spirit-fire still uttering this I! (001-008)

The "spirit-fire" is continuously "uttering this I" — generating the speaker's very [[self|selfhood]]. [[george-macdonald|MacDonald]] here roots personal identity in the divine Spirit's ongoing creative act. The self is not a stable substance but a moment-by-moment utterance of the Spirit.

The Spirit also functions as a cleansing wind:

> Fair freshness of the God-breathed spirit air, / Pass through my soul, and make it strong to love (005-012)

The "spirit air" is [[breath|God-breathed]] — a deliberate echo of Genesis 2:7, where [[god|God]] [[wind|breath]]es life into Adam. [[george-macdonald|MacDonald]]'s prayer for the Spirit to "pass through my soul" treats spiritual life as ventilation: the soul must be opened to a wind not its own.

## Key Passages

> Lord, in thy spirit's hurricane, I pray, / Strip my soul naked—dress it then thy way. / Change for me all my rags to cloth of gold. (002-019)

The Spirit comes as "hurricane" — violent, stripping, unsettling. [[george-macdonald|MacDonald]] does not ask for gentle transformation but for radical exposure: "strip my soul naked." Only after the old [[garment|garments]] are torn away can the Spirit "dress it then thy way" in "cloth of gold."

> Then shall thy life my heart all open find, / A thoroughfare to thy great spirit-wind; / Then shall I rest within thy holy vast (012-009)

The heart becomes a "thoroughfare" — not a destination but a passage. The Spirit-wind blows *through* the soul, not into it as a container. This image upends the conventional metaphor of the heart as a [[clay|vessel]] to be filled; for [[george-macdonald|MacDonald]], spiritual health is permeability, not accumulation.

## Spiritual Need and Spiritual Greed

[[george-macdonald|MacDonald]] draws a sharp distinction between receiving from the Spirit and hoarding spiritual experience:

> In holy things may be unholy greed. / Thou giv'st a glimpse of many a lovely thing, / Not to be stored for use in any mind, / But only for the present spiritual need. (008-007)

Spiritual [[wisdom|insight]]s are given for "the present spiritual need" — they are rations, not provisions. To "store" them is to transform them into possessions ("the mammon-moth, the [[pride|having-pride]]"), corrupting what was meant to be received and released. [[george-macdonald|MacDonald]]'s spirituality is radically present-tense: the Spirit gives what is needed *now*, and attempts to bank grace for the future constitute a form of greed.

## The Spirit and the Will

The Spirit's work is not independent of human willing but partnered with it:

> Sometimes, perhaps, the spiritual blood runs slow, / And soft along the veins of will doth flow, / Seeking God's arteries from which it came. (007-022)

The "spiritual blood" flows "along the veins of *will*" — the will is the circulatory system of the Spirit. When the spiritual blood runs slow, it is not because the Spirit has withdrawn but because the will's [[clay|vessel]]s have constricted. [[george-macdonald|MacDonald]] locates spiritual vitality at the intersection of divine initiative and human volition: the Spirit supplies the blood, but the will provides the channels.

> But now the Spirit and I are one in this— / My hunger now is after righteousness; / My spirit hopes in God to set me free / From the low self loathed of the higher me. (006-020)

The union of divine Spirit and human spirit produces a shared desire: "My hunger now is after righteousness." The Spirit does not override the human will but aligns it — the "higher me" loathes the "low self" and hopes for liberation, and this very loathing is evidence of the Spirit's presence.
