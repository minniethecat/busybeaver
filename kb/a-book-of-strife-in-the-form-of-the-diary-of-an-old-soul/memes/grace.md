Grace in [[george-macdonald|George MacDonald]]'s "A Book of [[strife|Strife]] in the Form of The Diary of an Old [[soul|Soul]]" (1880) is not merely [[god|God]]'s unmerited favor in the forensic sense — the pardon of sin — but the active, in[[tent|dwelling]] power of [[god|God]] that transforms the soul from within. [[george-macdonald|MacDonald]]'s grace is relentless, creative, and ultimately intolerant of anything in the creature that falls short of the Creator's likeness.

## Creative Grace

[[george-macdonald|MacDonald]] opens October with a striking description of grace as creative potency:

> Lord it throughout the large creation hence / With the potency of creative grace (008-025)

Grace is "creative" — it does not merely [[peace|rest]]ore a damaged soul to its previous state but makes something new. The verb "lord it" is an imperative: the speaker asks [[god|God]] to exercise dominion *through* grace, using it as the instrument of divine rule. Grace is not [[god|God]]'s leniency but [[god|God]]'s active power sweeping through "the large creation."

## Grace as Refinement

The refining function of grace is central to [[george-macdonald|MacDonald]]'s theology. In a passage that borders on startling, he writes:

> Oh joy! no flaw in me thy grace will brook, / But still refine (010-009)

The exclamation "Oh joy!" is genuine — the speaker is glad that grace will not tolerate his flaws. This is not grace as comfortable acceptance but grace as relentless improvement. Grace "will brook" no flaw: it will not put up with, will not overlook, will not excuse.

This connects to the earlier request:

> Thy grace shall be enough the grief to quell (010-002)

Grace does not remove the refining fire but makes it bearable. The "[[sorrow|grief]]" of being refined is real — transformation hurts — but grace "quells" that [[sorrow|grief]], bringing it under control so that the soul can endure the burning.

## Grace and the Mirror-Heart

One of [[george-macdonald|MacDonald]]'s most distinctive images links grace to reflection:

> Oh make my mirror-heart thy dwelling-place, / And be flooded with life's vital grace (010-007)

The heart is a mirror meant to reflect [[god|God]]. Grace floods the "mirror-heart" — the image is of a tide rushing in, filling every crevice until it can reflect nothing but [[god|God]]. This is not a gentle sprinkling but a flood — grace is overwhelming, invasive, total.

## Grace and Human Failure

[[george-macdonald|MacDonald]] is honest about the human tendency to lose [[vision|sight]] of grace:

> Because my eyes dropped from thy presence and my grace (005-014)

The speaker confesses that he has dropped his eyes — looking away from [[god|God]], and therefore away from grace. The possessive "my grace" is significant: grace belongs to the speaker not as a possession but as a gift continually given and continually received. To look away from [[god|God]] is to lose [[conscious|awareness]] of grace, even though grace itself does not withdraw.
