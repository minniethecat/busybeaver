The garment in [[george-macdonald|George MacDonald]]'s "A Book of [[strife|Strife]] in the Form of The Diary of an Old [[soul|Soul]]" operates across a dual register: it is both the sheltering fold into which the [[soul]] nestles for protection, and the covering that must be stripped away for the soul to stand naked before [[god|God]]. This tension — garment as refuge and garment as impediment — runs through the entire sequence, binding the [[worship|devotion]]al life to a rhythm of being clothed and unclothed by divine hands. The metaphor draws on biblical sources (the hem of [[christ|Christ]]'s garment, the prodigal's robe, the Pauline [[word|language]] of putting off the old self) but fuses them into a distinctively [[george-macdonald|MacDonald]]ian theology of intimacy through exposure.

## The Shelter of the Fold

The garment first appears as encompassing protection — a divine wing under which the [[peace|rest]]less soul finds [[peace|rest]] from its own interrogating mind:

> Might I but scatter interfering things — / Questions and doubts, distrusts and anxious pride, / And in thy garment, as under gathering wings, / Nestle obedient to thy loving side, / Easy it were to love thee. (004-010)

The garment here is synonymous with proximity. To be "in thy garment" is to be so close to [[god|God]] that [[mind|intellect]]ual disturbance — "questions and doubts, dis[[faith|trust]]s and anxious [[pride]]" — falls away. [[love|Love]] becomes "easy" not through effort but through location: nestled, covered, sheltered. The garment provides what the scattered mind cannot — a bounded space where [[love]] needs no justification.

This sheltering function reaches its starkest expression in July:

> Outside the shelter of his garment's fold, / All is a waste, a terror-haunted wold. — / Lord, keep me. 'Tis thy child that cries. Behold. (007-025)

The garment's "fold" marks the boundary between cosmos and chaos. Outside: a "terror-haunted wold," a landscape of existential exposure. Inside: the child's cry is heard. The garment is not theological abstraction but the felt difference between being held and being abandoned.

## Grasping the Hem

Between full shelter and full stripping lies a middle posture — the desperate grasp of the hem:

> Help me, my Father, in whatever dismay, / Whatever terror in whatever shape, / To hold the faster by thy garment's hem; / When my heart sinks, oh, lift it up, I pray. (007-014)

The hem is the minimum contact that still constitutes connection. The speaker does not ask to be enfolded — he asks only to hold on. The [[heart]] that "sinks" is [[george-macdonald|MacDonald]]'s recurring image for spiritual depression; the garment's hem is the last point of tactile faith before the descent into despair. To "hold the faster" is an act of will against the gravitational pull of fear.

## Stripped Naked for Love

The most theologically daring stanza in the garment sequence reverses the metaphor entirely: [[god|God]] does not clothe but *unclothes*:

> God takes the inmost garments off his child, / To have him in his arms, naked and undefiled. (008-011)

The context is the stripping of the [[soul]] in preparation for [[death]] — the body grows "poorer, feebler" as "undressing for its last sweet bed," and the soul too must shed its accumulated layers. "Authority, and power, and memory" are garments that must come off before divine intimacy is possible. The telos is not humiliation but embrace: nakedness is the condition of being held. The paradox is that the garment which once sheltered must now be removed for truer shelter — the Father's arms.

The same stripping appears in March as a burning away:

> O Life, burn at this feeble shell of me, / Till I the sore singed garment off shall push, / Flap out my Psyche wings, and to thee rush. (003-010)

The garment here is the "feeble shell" of [[self|the self]] — the [[body]], the ego, the entire creaturely apparatus that both enables and limits. To "push off" the singed garment is to emerge winged. The burning is not destruction but metamorphosis: what is stripped is not the true self but its constricting casing.

## The New Raiment

The garment sequence closes November with a [[prayer|petition]] for re-clothing — not a return to the old covering but investiture with something new:

> From thy rich treasures, householder divine, / Bring forth fair garments, old and new, I pray, / And like thy brother dress me, in the old home-bred way. (011-013)

The speaker, having been stripped, now asks to be dressed — but dressed "like thy brother," in the likeness of [[christ|Christ]]. The garments are "old and new": old because they belong to the "home-bred way" of the Father's house, new because the speaker has never worn them before. The stripping was not an end but a preparation for this investiture.
