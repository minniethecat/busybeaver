Wrath in [[george-macdonald|George MacDonald]]'s "A Book of [[strife|Strife]] in the Form of The Diary of an Old [[soul|Soul]]" (1880) undergoes careful discrimination. [[george-macdonald|MacDonald]] distinguishes between human wrath — which is almost always self-serving — and a purified anger that can be divine. The diary traces the transformation of wrath from a sin to be suppressed into an energy that, when refined, aligns with [[god|God]]'s own opposition to evil.

## Keep Me from Wrath, Let It Seem Ever So Right

The diary's most personal handling of wrath appears as a prayer in February:

> Keep me from wrath, let it seem ever so right:
> My wrath will grow a wall between my face and God's. (002-018)

[[george-macdonald|MacDonald]] does not claim that wrath is never "right" — it may "seem ever so right." The danger is not that wrath is always morally wrong but that it constructs "a wall" between the soul and [[god|God]]. Even justified wrath separates; it fills the field of vision with the offence rather than with [[god|God]]. The prayer is not "make me right about my wrath" but "keep me from wrath" altogether.

## Spiteful Human Wrath

In June, [[george-macdonald|MacDonald]] contrasts two kinds of anger:

> Love's anger grand grows spiteful human wrath,
> Hunting men out of conscience' sheltering path. (006-022)

"[[love|Love]]'s anger grand" is the divine starting point — an anger rooted in love, expansive and generous even in opposition. But it "grows" — degrades — into "spiteful human wrath," which hunts rather than corrects. [[human|Human]] wrath is predatory; it drives its objects from "conscience' sheltering path" rather than drawing them back.

## Swoln Up with Wrath

May's diary reveals wrath as a s[[water|well]]ing that desires vengeance:

> Swoln up with wrath, desireth vengeance fine.
> Haste, Lord, to help, when human judgment fails. (005-011)

Wrath "s[[water|well]]s" — it inflates [[self|the self]]. The desire for "vengeance fine" is the demand for precise, exquisite retribution. [[george-macdonald|MacDonald]]'s remedy is not self-control but divine intervention: "Haste, [[christ|Lord]], to help, when human judgment fails." [[human|Human]] wrath is a failure of judgment, and only [[god|God]] can judge rightly.

## Make My Anger Pure

The diary prays for a transfigured anger:

> Oh, make my anger pure — let no worst wrong
> Rouse in me any fury that is not thine. (006-025)

The goal is not the elimination of anger but its purification. "Pure" anger is anger that belongs to [[god|God]] — "thine" — rather than to [[self|the self]]. The same energy that fuels wrath can fuel righteousness, if its object is wrong done to others rather than wrong done to oneself. Purified anger no longer walls off [[god|God]] but participates in [[god|God]]'s own opposition to evil.
