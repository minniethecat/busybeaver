Emptiness in [[george-macdonald|George MacDonald]]'s "A Book of [[strife|Strife]] in the Form of The Diary of an Old [[soul|Soul]]" takes two forms: the swollen emptiness of [[self]]-regard that the speaker begs [[god|God]] to press from his heart, and the desolate emptiness of a land that yields nothing to the eye — a condition given by [[god|God]] to some souls as a [[gift]], not a curse. In both cases, emptiness is not the void of nihilism but the hollowing-out that makes room for the divine. The speaker's "s[[water|well]]ing emptiness" (001-031) is the inflation of words without substance; the "land of emptiness and dearth" (006-017) is the landscape of [[unrest]] that drives the soul beyond what it can see.

## The Swelling Emptiness

After a season of public [[word|speech]] — "talking to the people" — the speaker finds his heart puffed up by the "recoil of my words' airy ripple" (001-031). This is the emptiness of performance, of words that return to the speaker as echo rather than reaching [[god|God]]. The "s[[water|well]]ing" is the paradox of emptiness: the more hollow the heart, the more inflated the [[self]]. The only remedy is prostration — "I cast myself before thee prone" — and the cool hands of [[god|God]] pressing the emptiness out.

> O Lord, I have been talking to the people; / Thought's wheels have round me whirled a fiery zone, / And the recoil of my words' airy ripple / My heart unheedful has puffed up and blown. / Therefore I cast myself before thee prone: / Lay cool hands on my burning brain, and press / From my weak heart the swelling emptiness. (001-031)

## The Land of Emptiness and Dearth

The second form of emptiness is not self-inflicted but divinely given: "To some thou giv'st a deep un[[peace|rest]] — a scorn / Of all they are or see upon the earth; / A gaze, at dusky night and clearing morn, / As on a land of emptiness and dearth" (006-017). This is the [[gift]] of dissatisfaction — a holy discontent that makes the visible world appear barren so that the soul will seek what lies beyond it. The "land of emptiness" is not punishment but pedagogy: [[god|God]] empties the landscape to redirect the gaze. [[george-macdonald|MacDonald]] lists this emptiness alongside "a bitter [[grief|sorrow]]" and "a frozen heart" — severe mercies, "oh, worse than anything," yet given by the same hand that gives [[rest]].

> To some thou giv'st a deep unrest — a scorn / Of all they are or see upon the earth; / A gaze, at dusky night and clearing morn, / As on a land of emptiness and dearth; / To some a bitter sorrow; to some the sting / Of love misprized — of sick abandoning; / To some a frozen heart, oh, worse than anything! (006-017)

## Emptiness as Preparation

Across both passages, emptiness in [[george-macdonald|MacDonald]]'s [[mind|thought]] is the condition that precedes filling. The s[[water|well]]ing emptiness of [[pride]] is pressed out so that the heart can receive; the desolate emptiness of the landscape is given so that the soul will seek the one landscape that does not fail. Emptiness is not the enemy of [[faith]] but its [[door|threshold]] — the space [[god|God]] clears for [[god|God]]'s own [[presence]].
