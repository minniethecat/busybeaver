Treasure in [[george-macdonald|George MacDonald]]'s "A Book of [[strife|Strife]] in the Form of The Diary of an Old [[soul|Soul]]" is always located in the divine — hidden in the person of [[Christ]], buried in a field worth every possession, stored in the household of the Father. The speaker draws on biblical imagery of treasure to map the geography of [[faith]]: the "unknown treasures of the truth" lie "in the Son of man, folded in love and ruth" (003-009); the "treasure-field" is worth selling everything to buy (002-019); and the "rich treasures" of the "householder divine" furnish [[garment|garments]] for the ragged [[soul]] (011-013). Treasure is never material in [[george-macdonald|MacDonald]]'s usage; it is always what the soul gains when it relinquishes what the world values.

## The Unknown Treasures of the Truth

The speaker asks, "Where should the unknown treasures of the truth / Lie, but there whence the truth comes out the most — / In the Son of man, folded in love and ruth?" (003-009). Treasure here is identified with [[truth]] itself — not propositions to be mastered but riches hidden in a person. The image of being "folded in love and ruth" suggests a treasure that is held, protected, and given only to those who come close. The "fair shore" and "fair ocean" are visible from a distance, but "behind / Lie infinite reaches bathing many a coast" — treasure beyond the horizon, infinitely extending.

> Where should the unknown treasures of the truth / Lie, but there whence the truth comes out the most — / In the Son of man, folded in love and ruth? / Fair shore we see, fair ocean; but behind / Lie infinite reaches bathing many a coast — / The human thought of the eternal mind, / Pulsed by a living tide, blown by a living wind. (003-009)

## The Treasure-Field Worth Everything

In February the speaker prays for a radical exchange: "Who would not poverty for riches yield? / A hovel sell to buy a treasure-field?" (002-019). The question is rhetorical — the transaction is obvious to anyone who sees clearly. But the treasure-field is not found by accumulation; it is bought by divestment. The speaker asks [[god|God]] to "Strip my soul naked — dress it then thy way. / Change for me all my rags to cloth of gold." The treasure is not added to what the soul already has; it replaces what the soul [[mind|thought]] was wealth.

> Lord, in thy spirit's hurricane, I pray, / Strip my soul naked — dress it then thy way. / Change for me all my rags to cloth of gold. / Who would not poverty for riches yield? / A hovel sell to buy a treasure-field? / Who would a mess of porridge careful hold / Against the universe's birthright old? (002-019)

## The Householder's Riches

In November the speaker turns to [[Christ]] as "elder brother" and begs: "From thy rich treasures, householder divine, / Bring forth fair [[garment|garments]], old and new, I pray, / And like thy brother dress me, in the old home-bred way" (011-013). Treasure here is not gold but [[garment|clothing]] — the [[garment|garments]] of be[[desire|longing]], the dress of the household. The "old and new" [[garment|garments]] suggest a treasury that spans the whole history of [[grace]], ancient and fresh at once. The treasure of the divine householder is not locked away but brought forth to clothe the "defiled and feeble" brother who stands "hanging down his hands."

> Son of the Father, elder brother mine, / See thy poor brother's plight; See how he stands / Defiled and feeble, hanging down his hands! / Make me clean, brother, with thy burning shine; / From thy rich treasures, householder divine, / Bring forth fair garments, old and new, I pray, / And like thy brother dress me, in the old home-bred way. (011-013)
