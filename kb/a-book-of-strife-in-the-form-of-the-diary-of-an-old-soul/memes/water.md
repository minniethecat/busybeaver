Water flows through [[george-macdonald|George MacDonald]]'s "A Book of [[strife|Strife]] in the Form of The Diary of an Old [[soul|Soul]]" as a figure for the divine element in which the [[soul]] lives and moves. As fish [[wind|breath]]e only in water and birds fly only in air, the soul [[wind|breath]]es only "in thy infinite soul" (001-005). But water also appears as the troubled surface of the [[self]] — a mirror dimmed by stirred soil — and as the pool the weary pilgrim rediscovers after the long dry journey through the [[desert]] of self-reliance.

## The Soul's Native Element

[[george-macdonald|MacDonald]]'s most concentrated water metaphor identifies water with the divine life itself. The speaker declares that his soul [[wind|breath]]es "only in thy infinite soul" just as fishes [[wind|breath]]e only in water (001-005). This is not a metaphor of occasional refreshment but of total dependence — the soul has no life apart from the element in which it swims. To leave the water is to [[death|die]]; to remain in it is to live, think, love, and be.

> Thy fishes breathe but where thy waters roll; / Thy birds fly but within thy airy sea; / My soul breathes only in thy infinite soul. (001-005)

## The Thirsty Traveler

The human condition is figured as a journey through a waterless land. The speaker, like a traveler who "stops, and drinks, and turns, and goes / Into a land where never water flows" (008-003), repeatedly leaves the source of life. The dry and thirsty day stretches on until the traveler, driven by [[thirst]], turns and finds again "the bubbling pool." The image of Sychar's margin evokes John 4 — [[christ|Christ]] at the well, offering living water to the Samaritan woman.

> Like one that stops, and drinks, and turns, and goes / Into a land where never water flows, / There travels on, the dry and thirsty day. (008-003)

## Troubled Water, Troubled Sight

Water serves also as a figure for the soul's capacity to reflect [[god|God]] — and the ease with which that reflection is disturbed. "The troubled water, dim with upstirred soil, / Makes not the image which it yet can spoil" (003-020). The soul's own agitation clouds its vision of the divine. The image of [[god|God]] is present but obscured, not by [[god|God]]'s absence but by the soul's turbulence. The remedy is not self-calming but divine approach: "Come nearer, [[christ|Lord]], and smooth the wrinkled wave."

> The troubled water, dim with upstirred soil, / Makes not the image which it yet can spoil. (003-020)

## Through Fire and Water

Water also appears as an element the soul must pass through on its journey home: "Hither if I have come through earth and air, / Through fire and water — I am not of them" (012-004). The soul passes through water but is not constituted by it — it belongs to another order. Water here is tribulation, the passage through which purifies but does not define. The soul's origin and destiny lie beyond all elements.
