# Child

The **child** is a central image in [[A Book of Strife in the Form of The Diary of an Old Soul]], representing both the believer's dependent relation[[vessel|ship]] with [[god]] and the spiritual ideal of humble [[faith|trust]]. [[george-macdonald|George MacDonald]] uses the parent-child bond as the master metaphor for divine-human intimacy, drawing on the New Testament theme of adoption while infusing it with the psychological depth of domestic experience.

## The Weary Child Returning

[[george-macdonald|MacDonald]] portrays the soul as a child who grows weary of independence and longs for the security of the Father's presence. This [[weary|weariness]] is not failure but the beginning of wisdom:

> "The child that, weary of his mother's pace, / Returns to her, and will not brook to go." (008-023)

The image of the child "weary of his mother's pace" captures the exhaustion of trying to keep up with a goodness one cannot match by effort alone.

## Naked Before the Father

The poet uses the child image to express utter vulnerability before [[god|God]]. The soul must be stripped of all pretense:

> "Thou pull'st the inmost garments off his child, / To have him in his arms, naked and wild." (008-011)

This stripping is not punishment but preparation for embrace. [[george-macdonald|MacDonald]] insists that the [[father]]'s intention is always intimate communion — the [[garment|garments]] are removed precisely so the child can be held.

## The Child Running Home

[[george-macdonald|MacDonald]] envisions the soul's approach to [[god|God]] with the unselfconscious boldness of a child:

> "The child throws wide the door, back to the feast, / Rushing, white-robed, to his place and rest." (012-015)

The "white-robed" child recalls baptismal imagery and the prodigal's [[peace|rest]]oration. There is no hesitation, no calculation of worthiness — only the instinctive certainty of be[[desire|longing]] that characterizes the healthiest parent-child relation[[vessel|ship]]s.

## Children of God

The communal dimension of childhood appears when [[george-macdonald|MacDonald]] addresses believers collectively:

> "Thou know'st our angels all, God's children sweet, / And of each two wilt make one perfect child." (012-021)

This passage envisions the eschatological unity of [[love]]d ones in a shared divine childhood, where individual identity is not erased but fulfilled in communion.
