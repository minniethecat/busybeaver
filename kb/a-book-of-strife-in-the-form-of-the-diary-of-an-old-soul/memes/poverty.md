[[spirit|Spirit]]ual poverty in [[george-macdonald|George MacDonald]]'s "A Book of [[strife|Strife]] in the Form of The Diary of an Old [[soul|Soul]]" is not mere lack but the soul's right posture before [[god|God]]. The speaker names his own "life-poverty" (002-025) not as a complaint but as a confession that opens the way to exchange — rags for cloth of gold, hovel for treasure-field. Poverty here is the honest estimate: all that I have, I have not earned. To know this is to be positioned for [[grace]]. To deny it is to remain rich in nothing.

## The Exchange

The speaker presses the logic of exchange with startling directness: "Who would not poverty for [[treasure|riches]] yield? / A hovel sell to buy a treasure-field?" (002-019). The rhetorical questions answer themselves — no sane person would refuse. Yet the spiritual application is precisely what the soul resists: to admit poverty is to admit need, and to admit need is to surrender the illusion of self-sufficiency. The hovel is [[self|the self]]'s cramped shelter; the treasure-field is the [[kingdom]] into which only the poor in spirit enter.

> Change for me all my rags to cloth of gold. / Who would not poverty for riches yield? / A hovel sell to buy a treasure-field? (002-019)

## The Confession

The speaker does not posture as spiritually [[treasure|wealth]]y. He ac[[wisdom|knowledge]]s a condition he cannot remedy: "Sad our poverty doth bow / Before the [[treasure|riches]] of thy making might" (003-002). The poverty is "sad" — it carries affective weight — but it bows, and bowing is the beginning of [[worship]]. The contrast is absolute: human poverty against divine making-might. [[george-macdonald|MacDonald]] does not soften the asymmetry; he makes it the [[earth|ground]] of relation. The poor soul stands (or kneels) before the rich [[god|God]], and this standing-before is the form that faith takes.

> Sad our poverty doth bow / Before the riches of thy making might (003-002)

## The Oppression

Poverty is not only a theological position but an experienced condition: "[[sin|Sin]]ks moveless, with life-poverty opp[[peace|rest]]" (002-025). The "[[mind|thought]]-spirit" here is "weariful and wan" — exhausted, drained, unable to sustain its own motion. The word "opp[[peace|rest]]" carries the weight of an external force; this is not laziness but depletion. [[george-macdonald|MacDonald]]'s realism refuses to spiritualise this away. The remedy is not self-effort but the divine attention: "Watch then, O [[christ|Lord]], thy feebly glimmering coal." The coal is nearly out, but the [[christ|Lord]]'s watching is itself a keeping.

> Sinks moveless, with life-poverty opprest:— / Watch then, O Lord, thy feebly glimmering coal. (002-025)

## The World's Scorn

The world misreads spiritual poverty entirely: "[[god|God]]-fools, [[god|God]]-drunkards these do call the men / Who think the poverty of their all not fit" (005-018). To pour out what little one has at [[god|God]]'s feet looks like folly to those who measure by accumulation. But [[george-macdonald|MacDonald]] inverts the judgment: the "poverty of their all" is "not fit" for anything except total offering. The world's "[[god|God]]-drunkards" are the truly sober, and the world's wise are those who have never tasted the wine of divine [[love]].

> God-fools, God-drunkards these do call the men / Who think the poverty of their all not fit, / Borne humbly by their art, their voice, their pen, / Save for its allness, at thy feet to fling (005-018)
