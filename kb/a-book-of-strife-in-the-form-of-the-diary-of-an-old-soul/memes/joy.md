Joy in [[george-macdonald|George MacDonald]]'s "A Book of [[strife|Strife]] in the Form of The Diary of an Old [[soul|Soul]]" (1880) is not an emotion to be pursued but a condition that arises unbidden when the soul is properly oriented toward [[god|God]]. [[george-macdonald|MacDonald]] treats joy as a byproduct rather than a goal — it is what happens when love, hope, and obe[[death|die]]nce converge in the presence of the divine. Attempts to seize joy directly, he warns, invariably fail.

## Definition in MacDonald's Thought

[[george-macdonald|MacDonald]]'s most pointed statement on joy's nature appears in July:

> Not thine, my Lord, the darkness all is mine— / Save that, as mine, my darkness too is thine: / All things are thine to save or to destroy— / Destroy my darkness, rise my perfect joy (007-003)

Joy is not something [[god|God]] *gives*; it is what *rises* when [[god|God]] destroys the darkness. The verb is intransitive: "rise my perfect joy" — as though joy were the [[morning|dawn]] that naturally follows the night's destruction. [[george-macdonald|MacDonald]] refuses to separate joy from its theological context: joy is the felt experience of [[god|God]]'s victorious presence.

The February section makes the same point from the other direction:

> I cannot see, my God, a reason why / From morn to night I go not gladsome free; / For, if thou art what my soul thinketh thee, / There is no burden but should lightly lie, / No duty but a joy at heart must be (002-023)

If [[god|God]] is who the soul believes him to be, joy should be the default state — "no duty but a joy at heart must be." The conditional "if thou art what my soul thinketh thee" is crucial: the absence of joy is not a failure of effort but a failure of *perception*. When [[god|God]] is truly seen, gladness follows as inevitably as light follows the sun.

## Key Passages

> Destroy my darkness, rise my perfect joy; / Love primal, the live coal of every night, / Flame out, scare the ill things with radiant fright, / And fill my tent with laughing morn's delight. (007-003)

The final line shifts from the speaker's joy to cosmic joy — "laughing morn's delight." [[george-macdonald|MacDonald]] locates individual joy within a larger frame: the [[morning|dawn]] laughs, the tent is filled, and the speaker participates in a gladness that precedes and exceeds him.

> Twilight of the transfiguration-joy, / Gleam-faced, pure-eyed, strong-willed, high-hearted boy! (012-030)

This elegiac stanza addresses a child who [[death|die]]d young but lived in such alignment with [[god|God]] that his life was a "transfiguration-joy." The phrase is remarkable: joy does not merely accompany transfiguration; joy *is* the transfiguration, the visible [[glory|radiance]] of a life lived in [[god|God]].

> The thing that was well dreamed of bliss and joy— / Wilt thou not breathe thy life into the toy? (004-002)

Joy begins as a "dream" and a "toy" — insubstantial, childish. It becomes real only when [[god|God]] "[[wind|breath]]es life" into it. [[george-macdonald|MacDonald]]'s metaphor implies that human [[desire|longing]] for joy is a sketch; divine action is the coloring-in.

## The Danger of Joy-Seeking

[[george-macdonald|MacDonald]] is alert to the spiritual d[[wrath|anger]] of seeking joy for its own sake:

> Discovery's pride, joy's bliss, take aback my sail, / And sweep me from thy presence and my grace, / Because my eyes dropped from the master's face. (005-014)

Even joy itself can become an idol. The speaker describes the experience of reading his own poetry and being swept away by "joy's bliss" — only to realize that this very rapture has carried him "from thy presence." The phrase "my eyes dropped from the master's face" is the diagnostic: joy is safe only when it is a side-effect of looking at [[god|God]]. The moment joy becomes the object of attention, it becomes a distraction.

## Unspeakable Joy

The most profound joy, for [[george-macdonald|MacDonald]], exceeds [[word|language]]:

> Hints come to me from the realm unknown; / Airs drift across the twilight border land, / Odoured with life; and as from some far strand / Sea-murmured, whispers to my heart are blown / That fill me with a joy I cannot speak (005-029)

Joy at its deepest is pre-verbal — "a joy I cannot speak" — arriving as "hints," "airs," "whispers." [[george-macdonald|MacDonald]] locates the most authentic joy at the edge of [[conscious|consciousness]], where the soul brushes against "the realm unknown" and receives impressions too subtle for [[word|language]]. This is not mysticism that devalues the ordinary but a recognition that joy's deepest forms resist articulation.
