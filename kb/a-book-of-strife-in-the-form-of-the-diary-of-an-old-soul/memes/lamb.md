The lamb in [[george-macdonald|George MacDonald]]'s "A Book of [[strife|Strife]] in the Form of The Diary of an Old [[soul|Soul]]" carries a double register. On one hand, it is the speaker's honest self-description — "a stupid, self-willed, greedy lamb" (009-017) who nonetheless longs to belong to the [[Shepherd]]. On the other, it is the [[Christ]] who "answe[[peace|rest]] the lamb" (005-026), the Lamb of [[god|God]] whose self-[[gift|giving]] is the pattern and the power of all true [[prayer]]. The distance between the two lambs — the wayward and the slain — is the distance that [[grace]] traverses.

## The Self-Willed Lamb

The speaker's confession is blunt and unsparing: he is "a stupid, self-willed, greedy lamb" (009-017). The adjectives pile up without mitigation, each naming a distinct failure of the sheep-nature. Stupidity is not [[mind|intellect]]ual deficiency but spiritual obtuseness — the inability to recognise the [[Shepherd]]'s voice. [[self|Self]]-will is the lamb's insistence on its own direction. Greed is the lamb that grabs at pasture rather than receiving what [[shepherd|the Shepherd]] provides. Yet this confession is not despair; it is spoken to [[shepherd|the Shepherd]], and the very act of naming the fault is an act of [[trust]] that [[shepherd|the Shepherd]] can transform it.

> I, a stupid, self-willed, greedy lamb, / Would be thy honest and obedient sheep. (009-017)

## The Lamb Who Answers

The speaker addresses [[Christ]] as the one who answers prayer: "thou answe[[peace|rest]] the lamb" (005-026). The lamb here is both the praying soul and the echo of the Lamb who was slain. That [[christ|Christ]] answers the lamb means that the one who gave himself for the sheep continues to attend to their cry. The answering is not a distant decree but [[shepherd|the Shepherd]]'s immediate response to the bleat of his own. [[prayer|Prayer]] is not the soul's performance but the lamb's cry, and the answer is guaranteed not by the quality of the cry but by the character of the one who hears.

> Thou answerest the lamb. (005-026)

## From Lamb to Sheep

The speaker's desire — "would be thy honest and [[obedience|obedient]] sheep" (009-017) — marks the movement from lamb to sheep, from waywardness to be[[desire|longing]]. A lamb is young, foolish, dependent; a sheep is mature, integrated into the flock, responsive to the [[Shepherd]]'s voice. The speaker does not claim to have made this transition, only to desire it. The honesty he seeks is the opposite of his current self-will; the obe[[death|die]]nce is the opposite of his greed. The transformation is not self-wrought — it is [[shepherd|the Shepherd]]'s work on the lamb.
