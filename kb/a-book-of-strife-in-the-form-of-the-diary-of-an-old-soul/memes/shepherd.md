The Shepherd in [[george-macdonald|George MacDonald]]'s "A Book of [[strife|Strife]] in the Form of The Diary of an Old [[soul|Soul]]" is the divine seeker whose movement toward the lost defines the logic of salvation. Drawing on the parable of the lost [[lamb|sheep]], [[george-macdonald|MacDonald]] presents the Shepherd not as a distant watcher but as the one who leaves safety to pursue the straying soul. The speaker identifies himself as the [[lamb]] — "stupid, self-willed, greedy" — and the Shepherd as the one who comes running when [[lamb|the lamb]] bleats.

## The Seeking Shepherd

The core image is drawn from the Gospels: "Not when the shepherd leaves the ninety and nine" (009-014). [[george-macdonald|MacDonald]] invokes the parable not to retell it but to inhabit it — the speaker is the one [[lamb|sheep]], the one stray, the one for whom the Shepherd abandons the flock. The ninety-nine are safe on "the hunted pasture"; the Shepherd's attention is on the one that is not. This is the particularity of divine [[love]] — it does not deal in aggre[[door|gate]]s but in individual souls, each one worth the full cost of pursuit.

> Not when the shepherd leaves the ninety and nine (009-014)

## The Folded Lamb

The speaker's response to the Shepherd's approach is immediate and bodily: "The shepherd, lo! — I run — fall folded in his arms" (009-016). The word "lo!" registers surprise — the Shepherd has come, and the coming is always a fresh astonishment. The lamb runs, but the running ends in falling, and the falling ends in being folded. "Folded" is the perfect word: it suggests enclosure, protection, warmth, and the cessation of [[strife|struggle]]. The lamb does not fold itself; the Shepherd's arms do the folding. The initiative is entirely divine.

> The shepherd, lo! — I run — fall folded in his arms (009-016)

## The Shepherd and the Lamb

[[george-macdonald|MacDonald]]'s Shepherd-lamb pairing runs through the entire work. The speaker is the "stupid, self-willed, greedy lamb" who desires to become "honest and [[obedience|obedient]]" (009-017). The Shepherd is the one who "answe[[peace|rest]] [[lamb|the lamb]]" (005-026). The relation is asymmetrical at every point: the Shepherd seeks, [[lamb|the lamb]] strays; the Shepherd folds, [[lamb|the lamb]] falls; the Shepherd answers, [[lamb|the lamb]] cries. Yet this asymmetry is not tyranny — it is the shape of [[grace]], in which all the power is on one side and all the need on the other, and that is precisely how love works.
