Worship in [[george-macdonald|George MacDonald]]'s "A Book of [[strife|Strife]] in the Form of The Diary of an Old [[soul|Soul]]" (1880) is expanded beyond the sanctuary. [[george-macdonald|MacDonald]] traces wor[[vessel|ship]] across three widening circles: the veneration of [[christ|Christ]] himself, the service of every human being as image-bearer, and the posture of silent adoration that sits at [[lord|the Master]]'s feet. Worship is not what the soul does on occasion but what the soul is in orientation.

## Whole-Hearted Worship of the Man

The diary begins — strikingly — with wor[[vessel|ship]] directed toward a human being:

> Whole-hearted is my worship of the man
> From whom my earthly history began. (001-004)

The opening dedication locates wor[[vessel|ship]] not in a temple but in gratitude toward a specific person — likely [[george-macdonald|MacDonald]]'s father or spiritual mentor. "Whole-hearted" is the key term: wor[[vessel|ship]] that is partial is not wor[[vessel|ship]]. The "man from whom my [[earth|earthly]] history began" receives wor[[vessel|ship]] not as a rival to [[god|God]] but as a conduit of [[god|God]]'s creative work.

## Sitting Worshipping at Thy Feet

In March, wor[[vessel|ship]] takes the posture of Mary at [[christ|Jesus]]' feet:

> I must sit worshipping — that, in my core,
> Thy word might grow, and I should speak no more. (003-013)

Worship is sitting — not standing, not performing, not speaking. It is a posture of receptive silence: "I should speak no more." The purpose is inward transformation — "in my core, thy word might grow." Worship is not the soul's offering to [[god|God]] but [[god|God]]'s word taking root in the soul. The silence of wor[[vessel|ship]] is the condition of that growth.

## My Heart Would Worship All My Fellow Men

May's diary gives wor[[vessel|ship]] its most radical expansion:

> O God of man, my heart would worship all
> My fellow men, the flashes of thy light. (005-024)

Worship extends horizontally to "all my fellow men." They are "flashes of thy light" — not autonomous beings but refractions of the divine. To wor[[vessel|ship]] [[god|God]] is, in [[george-macdonald|MacDonald]]'s vision, to recognise and revere [[god|God]]'s image in every human face. This is not idolatry but its opposite: the refusal to separate love of [[god|God]] from love of neighbour.

## Worship a God of Wood

September provides the warning that anchors the vision:

> Who trusts a law, might worship a god of wood;
> Half his soul shrivels to a printed code. (009-020)

To wor[[vessel|ship]] a "law" — an abstraction, a system — is indistinguishable from wor[[vessel|ship]]ping "a god of wood." Both are idols. The "printed code" — whether legal, theological, or moral — "shrivels" the soul when it replaces the living [[god|God]]. [[george-macdonald|MacDonald]]'s expansive vision of wor[[vessel|ship]] is the antidote: wor[[vessel|ship]] of the living [[god|God]] overflows into love of living persons, while wor[[vessel|ship]] of abstractions deadens both.
