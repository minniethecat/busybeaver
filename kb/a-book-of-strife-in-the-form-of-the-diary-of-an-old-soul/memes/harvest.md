Harvest in [[george-macdonald|George MacDonald]]'s "A Book of [[strife|Strife]] in the Form of The Diary of an Old [[soul|Soul]]" is a figure for the yield of a life lived in [[trust]] — and for what remains when that yield fails. The speaker confronts his own withering harvest as a test of faith: what he once relied on for sustenance collapses, yet "the dark still is [[god|God]]" (001-015). The golden harvest drawn across the land in September (009-019) is likewise re-framed — not as the reliable bounty of natural seasons but as the gift of a divine [[will]] whose law is "conscious harmony," not mechanical rotation. Harvest is less about reaping than about the posture of dependence that withering and abundance alike demand.

## The Withering Harvest

The speaker faces a season of loss in which "My harvest withers. Health, my means to live — / All things seem rushing straight into the dark" (001-015). This is material and spiritual collapse together — health, livelihood, and the sense of forward momentum all failing at once. Yet the response is not despair but defiance of despair: "But the dark still is [[god|God]]." The withering of harvest becomes the condition for a deeper confession of [[faith]], a refusal to buy back the lost crop at any cost: "I would not give / The smallest silver-piece to turn the rush / Backward or sideways."

> My harvest withers. Health, my means to live — / All things seem rushing straight into the dark. / But the dark still is God. I would not give / The smallest silver-piece to turn the rush / Backward or sideways. Am I not a spark / Of him who is the light? — Fair hope doth flush / My east. — Divine success — Oh, hush and hark! (001-015)

## The Golden Harvest of Faith

By September the harvest imagery returns, now not as withering but as the golden goal toward which the speaker strains — yet the terms have changed. The speaker insists that his "faith and hope in [[god|God]] alone shall stand, / The life of law — not [[faith|trust]] the rain and sun / To draw the golden harvest o'er the land" (009-019). The natural order of rain and sun, the reliable round of seasons, is set aside as an object of [[faith|trust]]. The harvest comes not from the cycle but from the will behind it — "Law is the body of will, of conscious harmony." Agricultural certainty is replaced by [[trust]] in the person who governs the seasons.

> Yet more and more of me thou dost demand; / My faith and hope in God alone shall stand, / The life of law — not trust the rain and sun / To draw the golden harvest o'er the land. / I must not say — "This too will pass and die," / "The wind will change," "Round will the seasons run." / Law is the body of will, of conscious harmony. (009-019)

## Harvest as Spiritual Posture

Across both passages, harvest in [[george-macdonald|MacDonald]]'s [[mind|thought]] is not primarily an agricultural event but a spiritual posture. Whether the harvest withers or ripens golden, the speaker's task is the same: to refuse the temptation to manage the outcome — to "turn the rush / Backward or sideways" (001-015) or to comfort himself with seasonal platitudes (009-019) — and instead to fix his gaze on the [[god|God]] who is present equally in [[darkness]] and in light. The harvest is not the point; the Harvester is.
