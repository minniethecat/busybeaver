In [[george-macdonald|George MacDonald]]'s "A Book of [[strife|Strife]] in the Form of The Diary of an Old [[soul|Soul]]", the category of gift governs the speaker's [[wisdom|understanding]] of everything he receives from [[god|God]]. [[vision|Vision]], prayer, being itself — all are gifts. Yet the gift carries a d[[wrath|anger]]: the soul may "see the gift, see not the giving hand" (005-013) and so "from the living root the apple dead" wring. The proper response to a divine gift is neither grasping nor refusal but reception that turns the gaze from the gift to the Giver. The speaker's deepest inability — "I cannot give myself to thee aright" (008-015) — reveals that [[self|the self]] itself must first be received as gift before it can be given back.

## The Gift and the Giver

[[george-macdonald|MacDonald]] draws a sharp distinction between the gift and its source. When the speaker fixes a "greedy gaze" on the gift, even the "priceless pearl" becomes "poison that doth burn and cling" (005-013). The problem is not the gift — it is good — but the gaze that stops at the gift and refuses to travel to the giver. To "see the gift, see not the giving hand" is to "from the living root the apple dead" wring. The gift detached from the giver [[death|die]]s; the gift held in relation to the giver nourishes.

> They see the gift, see not the giving hand — / From the living root the apple dead I wring. (005-013)

## The Gift of Vision Made Void

The speaker confesses that he can "make his gift of vision [[emptiness|void]] and null" (004-019) through hypocrisy — talking and writing about [[god|God]] without entering the door of obe[[death|die]]nce. The gift is real but revocable in its effect; it can be nullified by the recipient's failure to live in accordance with what the gift discloses. The speaker who rebukes his neighbour while remaining outside the door "wrongs [[christ|Christ]] tenfold more" than the neighbour he criticises. The gift of [[dream|spiritual vision]] carries with it an [[duty|obligation]] to act upon what is seen.

> Making his gift of vision void and null. (004-019)

## The Uttermost of Gift

The speaker confronts his inability to give himself fully to [[god|God]]: "I cannot give myself to thee aright — / With the [[victory|triumph]]ant uttermost of gift" (008-015). The self that would give itself away discovers that it does not fully possess what it would offer. The gift of self back to [[god|God]] requires that [[self|the self]] first be "full of light." The human capacity for gift-giving is incomplete; the speaker must be "inspired, possessed, compelled" before he can give aright. The "[[victory|triumph]]ant uttermost of gift" is an eschatological hope, not a present achievement.

> I cannot give myself to thee aright — / With the triumphant uttermost of gift. (008-015)

## The Gift Claimed as Birthright

The speaker claims his "birthright ineffable" precisely as a gift: "Less than the gift to claim, would be the giver to scorn" (006-010). To refuse to claim what [[god|God]] has given is not [[humble|humility]] but insult — it treats the Giver as if the gift were worthless. The proper response to a divine gift is bold reception. The speaker's claiming is not arrogant because it is done in obe[[death|die]]nce to the Giver's own command: "Thou lay'st it on me, son, to claim thee, sire."

> Less than the gift to claim, would be the giver to scorn. (006-010)

## Death's Assault on the Gift

[[death|Death]] "haunts our souls with dissolution's strife" and "from [[god|God]]'s pu[[peace|rest]] gift / Wipes off the bloom" (009-022). [[death|Death]] is the enemy of gift — it tarnishes what [[god|God]] gives, making "every [[wind|breath]] / A throe, not action." The soul under death's shadow cannot receive the gift as it was meant to be received; the bloom, the freshness, the living quality of the gift is worn away by the [[conscious|consciousness]] of [[death|mortality]]. The only redemption from this "haunting death" is "[[life|Life]] everywhere, perfect, and always life" — the gift [[peace|rest]]ored to its original lustre in the presence of the Giver.

> From God's purest gift / Wipes off the bloom. (009-022)
