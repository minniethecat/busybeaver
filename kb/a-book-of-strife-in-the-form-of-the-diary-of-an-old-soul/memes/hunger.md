Hunger in [[george-macdonald|George MacDonald]]'s "A Book of [[strife|Strife]] in the Form of The Diary of an Old [[soul|Soul]]" (1880) is not a sign of deprivation to be reme[[death|die]]d but a divinely implanted drive toward righteousness. [[george-macdonald|MacDonald]] treats spiritual hunger as a gift — the soul's native orientation toward [[god|God]], given to prevent contentment with anything less. Hunger is the engine of spiritual growth.

## Hunger as Divine Gift

For [[george-macdonald|MacDonald]], hunger is not a defect but a feature of the spiritual constitution. It is the [[peace|rest]]lessness that prevents the soul from settling for lesser goods:

> My soul should rise in eager, hungering leaps; / Through thorny thickets struggle to the goal (004-011)

The "hungering leaps" are not desperate grasping but eager motion — the soul straining forward. The image is active, even athletic. Hunger here is vitality, not lack.

[[george-macdonald|MacDonald]] makes the point explicitly in June, declaring hunger a given condition that drives the soul [[god|God]]ward:

> Not given an infinite unrest, / A hunger — not at first after known good (006-019)

The hunger precedes [[wisdom|knowledge]] of its object. The soul hungers before it knows what it hungers for — an essential [[george-macdonald|MacDonald]]ian [[wisdom|insight]]: the appetite is implanted before the [[bread|food]] is recognised. This ordering ensures that the soul's motion toward [[god|God]] is driven by need, not [[mind|intellect]]ual assent.

## Hunger and Righteousness

The trajectory of hunger in the *Diary* moves from undirected [[peace|rest]]lessness toward specific moral content:

> My hunger now is after righteousness; / My spirit hopes the God of righteousness (006-020)

The hunger that began as "infinite un[[peace|rest]]" has found its proper object. Hunger after righteousness is the mature form of spiritual appetite — no longer a vague divine discontent but a directed desire for moral conformity to [[god|God]]'s character. [[george-macdonald|MacDonald]]'s speaker declares solidarity with [[god|God]] in this: "it and I are one in this."

## Hunger as Eschatological Posture

Hunger also orients the speaker toward the future. It is the felt gap between present condition and promised fulfilment:

> What hours æonian yet betwixt / This hunger in me, and the Father's heart (003-011)

The "æonian hours" stretch between present hunger and future satisfaction. [[george-macdonald|MacDonald]] does not imagine hunger disappearing in this life; it is the creature's permanent posture toward the Creator. The goal is not the abolition of hunger but its continual reorientation toward its true object.

## Wise Hunger

[[george-macdonald|MacDonald]] distinguishes between raw appetite and "wise hunger" — hunger trained and shaped by relation[[vessel|ship]] with [[god|God]]:

> In the inward parts thou dost desire — / Wise hunger, not a fitness fine of speech (005-001)

"Wise hunger" contrasts with rhetorical polish ("fitness fine of [[word|speech]]"). The hunger [[god|God]] desires is interior, not performative. It is the honest ache of the soul, not the [[water|well]]-turned phrase. [[george-macdonald|MacDonald]]'s preference for substance over style is itself a theological claim: [[god|God]] responds to need, not eloquence.
