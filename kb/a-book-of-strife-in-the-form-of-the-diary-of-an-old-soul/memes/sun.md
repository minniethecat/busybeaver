## Definition

In *A Book of [[strife|Strife]]*, the sun serves as [[george-macdonald|MacDonald]]'s most direct natural metaphor for [[god|God]] — the source of light, warmth, life, and vision. Unlike the mediated light of [[fire]] or the refracted illumination of [[light]], the sun represents direct, unmediated [[presence|divine presence]]: "Thou art my south, my summer sun" (001-022). The sun's physical properties — its distance yet felt warmth, its invisibility yet all-illuminating power, its daily [[rise|rising]] after [[night]] — make it a complete metaphorical vehicle for the transcendent-yet-immanent [[god|God]] of [[george-macdonald|MacDonald]]'s theology.

## Key Passages

The speaker's most intimate identification of [[god|God]] with the sun appears in the opening chapter: "Thou art my south, my summer sun" (001-022), where "south" and "summer" double the warmth imagery, making [[god|God]] the orientation point from which all spiritual warmth flows. This is not distant deity but personal sun — "my" sun.

[[george-macdonald|MacDonald]] extends the metaphor to [[mind|intellect]]ual illumination: "The sun of [[mind|thought]], across the farthest mind" (004-023), where the sun becomes the source of [[wisdom|understanding]] itself, traversing mental channels. The speaker's aspiration toward [[god|God]] takes solar form: "To thee, the sun, up[[fire|flame]]s thy kindled fire" (006-010), where the human soul is a fire that leaps upward toward its solar source.

The sun's omnipresence is asserted through a rhetorical question: "Is the sun far from any smallest flower" (007-019). Despite cosmic distance, the sun reaches every living thing — a reassurance that [[god|God]]'s attention extends to the humblest soul. The futility of limiting one's search is captured in: "To dig for a sun within a garden-fence" (007-031), critiquing those who seek divine light within artificially bounded domains.

The sun's directness appears in: "Lo! as the sun shoots straight from out his tent" (008-001), an image of unstoppable, purposeful [[glory|radiance]]. Yet the sun also participates in natural cycles of decline: "Sunset faints after sunset into the night" (003-002), reminding that even divine light may seem to withdraw, though its return is certain.

## Related Concepts

The sun is the source of [[light]] and stands opposite to [[night]] and [[darkness]]. Its illuminating function connects to [[truth]] and [[wisdom]], while its life-[[gift|giving]] warmth links to [[life]] and [[grace]]. The speaker's upward aspiration toward the sun parallels the movement toward [[heaven]]. Sunset's transition into darkness ties the sun to themes of [[death]] and renewal. The sun's fire aligns it with the refining [[fire]] of divine love and judgment.
