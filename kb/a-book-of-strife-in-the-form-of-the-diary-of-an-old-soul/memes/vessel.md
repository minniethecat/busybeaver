Vessel in [[george-macdonald|George MacDonald]]'s "A Book of [[strife|Strife]] in the Form of The Diary of an Old [[soul|Soul]]" works across two registers: the nautical [[clay|vessel]] captained by [[God]] rather than the [[self]], and the clay vessel shaped by the divine [[clay|potter]] from yielding material. In both images, the human being is not the agent but the thing borne or the thing made — and this is presented not as diminishment but as liberation. "With thee on board, each sailor is a king / Nor I mere captain of my [[clay|vessel]] then, / But heir of earth and heaven, eternal child" (002-022). The [[clay|vessel]] that is surrendered to its true captain becomes more, not less: heir, child, servant of all.

## The Vessel Captained by God

The speaker imagines the soul as a ship at sea and insists that with [[god|God]] aboard, the hierarchy of command is inverted: the sailor becomes king, the captain becomes child. "Nor I mere captain of my [[clay|vessel]] then, / But heir of earth and heaven, eternal child" (002-022). To hand over the wheel is not to lose authority but to gain an inheritance — the whole of earth and heaven. The "mere captain" is a diminished figure; the "eternal child" who has yielded command is "daring all truth, nor fearing anything; / Mighty in love, the servant of all men." The [[clay|vessel]] image thus holds together surrender and empowerment in a single figure.

> With thee on board, each sailor is a king / Nor I mere captain of my vessel then, / But heir of earth and heaven, eternal child; / Daring all truth, nor fearing anything; / Mighty in love, the servant of all men; / Resenting nothing, taking rage and blare / Into the Godlike silence of a loving care. (002-022)

## The Vessel of Yielding Clay

In October the [[clay|vessel]] metaphor shifts from ship to pot: "'Tis that I am not good — that is enough; / I pry no farther — that is not the way. / Here, O my [[clay|potter]], is thy making stuff!" (010-011). The speaker offers himself as raw material — clay filled with "chips in me, the stones, the straws, the sand" — and asks the [[clay|potter]] to "Cast them out with fine separating hand, / And make a [[clay|vessel]] of thy yielding clay." The [[clay|vessel]] is not self-fashioned; it is made by the [[clay|potter]] from clay that yields. The impurities — stones, straws, sand — are not hidden but named and offered for removal. The [[clay|vessel]] that results is wholly the [[clay|potter]]'s work, and its quality depends entirely on the clay's willingness to yield.

> 'Tis that I am not good — that is enough; / I pry no farther — that is not the way. / Here, O my potter, is thy making stuff! / Set thy wheel going; let it whir and play. / The chips in me, the stones, the straws, the sand, / Cast them out with fine separating hand, / And make a vessel of thy yielding clay. (010-011)

## Vessel as Surrender

Across both passages, the [[clay|vessel]] in [[george-macdonald|MacDonald]]'s [[mind|thought]] is the image of the [[soul]] that has ceased to be its own master. Whether as ship handed over to the divine captain or as clay yielded to the divine [[clay|potter]], the [[clay|vessel]] is defined by what it receives and who shapes it, not by what it does. The [[clay|vessel]] that insists on captaining itself remains "mere captain"; the [[clay|vessel]] that yields becomes heir, child, and servant — identities far larger than [[self|the self]] could achieve on its own. The [[humility|humble]] clay does not make itself; it is made, and in being made, it becomes what it could never become by its own effort.
