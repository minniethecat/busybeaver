In [[george-macdonald|George MacDonald]]'s "A Book of [[strife|Strife]] in the Form of The Diary of an Old [[soul|Soul]]", the clay-potter metaphor from Jeremiah and Isaiah is taken up and extended into a sustained image of the soul's formation. The speaker is "thy making stuff" (010-011) placed on [[god|God]]'s wheel — but the clay is not passive. It is full of "chips, stones, straws, sand" that must be cast out. The wheel runs "slow" (002-020), the pitcher is fl[[fear|awe]]d (002-011), and the speaker's prayer itself becomes a "clay-sparrow" that only [[god|God]] can make to fly (011-014). The clay image binds together human recalcitrance, [[patience|divine patience]], and the hope that what is now inert and earthbound will one day take wing.

## The Potter's Wheel

The speaker places himself on [[god|God]]'s wheel without reservation: "Here, O my potter, is thy making stuff! / Set thy wheel going; let it whir and play" (010-011). The tone is one of willing surrender — but surrender to a process that includes the painful removal of impurities: "The chips in me, the stones, the straws, the sand, / Cast them out with fine separating hand." The potter's work is not only shaping but sorting, separating what can be formed from what must be discarded. The end is a "vessel of thy yielding clay" — the clay yields, and in yielding becomes something it could never have made itself.

> Here, O my potter, is thy making stuff! / Set thy wheel going; let it whir and play. (010-011)

## The Flawed Pitcher

The speaker ac[[wisdom|knowledge]]s his vessel is fl[[fear|awe]]d — his soul is haunted "with folly — through the clay / Of this my pitcher" (002-011). Yet he does not ask for a new pitcher but for [[god|God]] to "see the lamp's dim flake" through the very clay that obscures it. The fl[[fear|awe]]d vessel is not discarded; it is the only vessel the speaker has, and [[god|God]] is asked to look through its flaws to the light within. The same stanza contains the cry, "hear the blow that would the pitcher break" — the speaker knows his fragility and asks [[god|God]] to temper the blow that would shatter him entirely.

> Through the clay / Of this my pitcher, see the lamp's dim flake. (002-011)

## The Slow Wheel

The speaker chafes at the pace of his own formation. "That with the finest clay my wheel runs slow, / Nor lets the lovely thing the shapely grow" (002-020). The finest clay — the most promising material — is the slowest to shape. The speaker frets that he "cannot more than me is given," that memory fails, that nightly poems vanish "ere morn like cistus-petals." The slow wheel is a trial of patience; the speaker must yield not only his substance but his timetable.

> That with the finest clay my wheel runs slow, / Nor lets the lovely thing the shapely grow. (002-020)

## The Clay-Sparrow

[[george-macdonald|MacDonald]] draws on a legendary tale — "[[love|Love]]-fact or loving fable" — in which [[child|children]] made sparrows of clay and [[god|God]] made them live: "Thou mad'st them birds, with wings to flutter and fold" (011-014). The speaker's own prayer is a "clay-sparrow" that "seems turned to a stone, / And from my heart will neither fly nor run" (011-015). The prayer cannot animate itself; it is inert, earthbound, a lump shaped like a bird but without life. The speaker's plea — "Take, [[christ|Lord]], my prayer in thy hand, and make it pray" — is the clay's confession that only the Potter can give it [[wind|breath]].

> How, when the children had made sparrows of clay, / Thou mad'st them birds, with wings to flutter and fold. (011-014)

## Crushed Back to Clay

The speaker knows that without divine [[power|strength]] he would be "crushed back to clay" (002-010). Clay is not only the material of formation but the condition of disintegration — what the human being returns to when formation fails. "It must be, [[god|God]], thou hast a [[power|strength]] to give / To him that fain would do what thou dost say." The prayer for [[power|strength]] is [[earth|ground]]ed in the Potter's character: if [[god|God]] commands, he must also empower. "Else how shall any soul repentant live, / Old [[sorrow|grief]]s and new fears hurrying on dismay?"

> Thou wouldst not have thy man crushed back to clay; / It must be, God, thou hast a strength to give. (002-010)
