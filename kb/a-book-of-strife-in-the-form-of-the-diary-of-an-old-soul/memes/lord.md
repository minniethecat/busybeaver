The Lord is the primary divine person addressed throughout [[george-macdonald|George MacDonald]]'s "A Book of [[strife|Strife]] in the Form of The Diary of an Old [[soul|Soul]]" (1880). The title "[[christ|Lord]]" appears over 90 times across the 366 daily stanzas, making the speaker's relation[[vessel|ship]] to this divine person the work's central dramatic relation[[vessel|ship]]. In [[george-macdonald|MacDonald]]'s poetic theology, the [[christ|Lord]] is simultaneously transcendent creator and intimate companion — the one to whom the speaker confesses his darkest [[strife|struggle]]s and his deepest [[desire|longing]]s. The Lord of the *Diary* is the [[god|God]] who works through "common things — low souls, weak [[heart|hearts]]" (007-004), using love rather than power as his primary instrument.

## Divine Identity

[[george-macdonald|MacDonald]]'s [[christ|Lord]] is the [[god|God]] of [[christ|Christ]]ian theology, but the *Diary* presents him through a distinctively personal, experiential lens. The speaker addresses the [[christ|Lord]] directly — every stanza is, implicitly or explicitly, a prayer. Unlike systematic theology, the *Diary* does not enumerate divine attributes; instead, the [[christ|Lord]]'s character emerges through the speaker's interactions: the [[christ|Lord]] is patient with failure (010-001), works through gradual transformation rather than sudden [[perfect|perfection]], and desires the speaker's willing love rather than coerced obe[[death|die]]nce.

The Lord is addressed by multiple names throughout the corpus, each emphasizing a different relational dimension:

| Name | Emphasis | Example PN |
|------|----------|------------|
| [[christ|Lord]] | Sovereign authority, worthiness, mastery | 006-021, 007-003, 010-001 |
| [[god|God]] | Ultimate reality, the [[earth|ground]] of being | 010-004 |
| Father | Intimate parent, familial tenderness | 007-014, 010-014 |
| [[master|Master]] | Teacher, guide, workman | 007-004 |

These names are not interchangeable but complementary. The Lord who commands is also the [[father|Father]] who comforts; the [[god|God]] who transcends is also the Master who works patiently with fl[[fear|awe]]d material.

## Role in the Diary

The Lord's role in the *Diary* unfolds across several dimensions:

**As Confessor.** The speaker treats the [[christ|Lord]] as one to whom anything may be said. In a striking admission: "Remember, [[christ|Lord]], thou hast not made me good. / Or if thou didst, it was so long ago / I have forgotten — and never understood" (010-001). The boldness of this opening — virtually accusing [[god|God]] — is characteristic of [[george-macdonald|MacDonald]]'s intimacy. The Lord can handle the speaker's honesty because the relation[[vessel|ship]] is [[earth|ground]]ed in [[love]], not fear.

**As Physician.** The Lord works to heal the speaker's soul, but the treatment is slow and often painful: "Thou workest perfectly. And if it seem / Some things are not so [[water|well]], 'tis but because / They are too loving-deep, too lofty-wise, / For me, poor child" (010-005). The Lord's apparent absence or harshness is reinterpreted as love too deep for the speaker to grasp.

**As [[light|Light]].** "Thou art the [[christ|Lord]] of life, the secret thing. / Thou wilt give endless more than I could find" (007-030). The Lord is the source not only of existence but of meaning — the one who illuminates what would otherwise remain dark.

**As the One Who Waits.** "I will what thou will'st — only keep me sure / That thou art willing" (012-002). The speaker's deepest anxiety is not punishment but divine indifference. The Lord's willingness to respond, to be present, is the [[earth|ground]] of all hope.

## Key Relationships

The Lord stands at the center of the *Diary*'s relational web:

- **The Lord and the [[heart]]**: The Lord works upon the heart from within, sending "healing virtue" into [[heart|hearts]] (006-001). The Lord does not override the heart but transforms it by in[[tent|dwelling]].
- **The Lord and [[love]]**: [[love|Love]] is the [[christ|Lord]]'s essential nature and primary instrument. "[[life|Life]]-[[gift|giving]] love" (006-022) is the [[christ|Lord]]'s alternative to force.
- **The Lord and [[father|the Father]]**: The Lord *is* the Father, but "[[christ|Lord]]" emphasizes sovereignty and "Father" emphasizes intimacy. The speaker's addresses shift between the two depending on the emotional register — "[[christ|Lord]]" in [[fear|awe]] and [[prayer|petition]], "Father" in [[faith|trust]] and tenderness.
- **The Lord and [[christ|Christ]]**: [[christ|Christ]] is the mediator — the one through whom the [[christ|Lord]]'s love reaches the speaker. "Make me a fellow worker with thee, [[christ|Christ]]" (006-005) places [[christ|Christ]] as the pattern of the life the [[christ|Lord]] desires.

## The Lord and Human Transformation

[[george-macdonald|MacDonald]]'s most distinctive theological contribution is his portrait of how the [[christ|Lord]] transforms. Unlike Calvinist predestination, the [[christ|Lord]] does not impose salvation; unlike Pelagian self-help, the speaker cannot transform himself. Instead, the [[christ|Lord]] works through continual, small interventions — "small successes, disappointments small; / By nature, weather, failure, or sore fall" (007-011) — gradually bending the will toward love.

The speaker's posture throughout is one of active receiving: "[[christ|Lord]], let my soul o'erburdened then feel thee / Thrilling through all its brain's stupidity" (007-025). The Lord thrills through the soul — an electrical metaphor for [[presence|divine presence]] — but only if the speaker consents to feel it.
