Duty in [[george-macdonald|George MacDonald]]'s "A Book of [[strife|Strife]] in the Form of The Diary of an Old [[soul|Soul]]" (1880) undergoes a radical transformation. What begins as external obligation — the weight of what one "ought" to do — is progressively internalised until duty becomes indistinguishable from joy. [[george-macdonald|MacDonald]]'s treatment of duty traces an arc from reluctant performance to glad surrender.

## Duty as Joy

The diary's most compressed statement on duty appears in February:

> No duty but a joy at heart must be:
> Light as a feather every load should lie. (002-023)

Duty and joy are not opposed but ultimately identical. The "must" is theological: if duty is what [[god|God]] requires, and [[god|God]] is love, then duty properly performed cannot be a burden. The transformation is not in the duty but in the heart that performs it — a heart aligned with [[god|God]] finds its obligations "light as a feather."

## Duty as Heart's Debt

[[george-macdonald|MacDonald]] personifies the heart as owing a debt that only glad obe[[death|die]]nce can pay:

> To pay the heart her duty and her debt,
> Showing the face of the beloved as yet. (006-006)

The heart owes "duty and her debt" — duty is the heart's obligation to its Maker, a debt incurred by the very gift of existence. The purpose of this payment is revelation: paying duty "shows the face of the beloved." Duty is not an end in itself but a means of [[vision|seeing God]] more clearly.

## The Weariness of Duty

The diary does not pretend that duty is always effortless. In August, the speaker confesses:

> Lest I lose my thoughts, from duty shrink.
> It is but avarice in a thinner dress. (008-009)

The temptation to "shrink" from duty is real — the speaker feels it and names it. But he immediately diagnoses the motive: a[[emptiness|void]]ance of duty is "avarice in a thinner dress," a subtler form of selfishness. The desire to escape obligation is not freedom-seeking but self-seeking disguised.

## Released from Evening Duty

One of the diary's most human moments occurs in May:

> Thou hast carried me through this evening's duty;
> I am released, weary, and glad of rest. (005-017)

The speaker has completed an "evening's duty" — perhaps the writing of the day's stanza itself — and feels "released." The honesty of "weary, and glad of [[peace|rest]]" refuses to spiritualise fatigue. Duty has been done, and the doing of it has cost something. Release is sweet precisely because duty was real.

## Absolute Duty

The diary's final word on duty gestures toward its [[perfect|perfection]]:

> For duty absolute how be fitter than
> The man that doth it for the love of it? (005-022)

"Absolute duty" — obligation without reservation — finds its fittest performer in the one who does it "for the love of it." The love of duty transforms duty from external command into internal desire. The "fittest" servant is not the most disciplined but the most loving — the one for whom obe[[death|die]]nce and [[joy|delight]] have become one.
