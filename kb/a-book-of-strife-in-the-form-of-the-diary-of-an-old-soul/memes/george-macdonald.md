# George MacDonald

## Biography

George MacDonald (10 December 1824 – 18 September 1905) was a
Scottish author, poet, and [[christ|Christ]]ian minister. Born in Huntly,
Aberdeenshire, he stu[[death|die]]d at the University of Aberdeen and trained
for the Congregational ministry at Highbury College, London.

His unorthodox theology — particularly his [[faith|belief]] in universal
reconciliation — led to his resignation from the ministry, after
which he turned to writing full-time.

A pioneering figure in modern fantasy literature, MacDonald authored
"Phantastes" (1858), "The Princess and the Goblin" (1872), and
"Lilith" (1895). He was a mentor to Lewis Carroll and deeply
influenced C.S. Lewis, J.R.R. Tolkien, G.K. Chesterton, and many
others. C.S. Lewis called him "my master" and wrote that MacDonald's
works had "baptized his imagination."

## Connection to the Work

"A Book of [[strife|Strife]] in the Form of The Diary of an Old [[soul|Soul]]" (1880)
is one of MacDonald's most personal works — a collection of 366
seven-line [[worship|devotion]]al poems, one for each day of the year.

Published when MacDonald was 55, the poems reflect a mature faith
w[[peace|rest]]ling with doubt, aging, [[weary|weariness]], and the mystery of divine
love. The voice is that of "an old soul" — introspective, honest
about failure, yet persistently turning to [[god|God]] in [[faith|trust]] and
obe[[death|die]]nce.

The title captures the work's central tension: life as strife, yet
strife that purifies rather than destroys. The diary form gives the
poems an immediacy and intimacy rare in Victorian [[worship|devotion]]al
literature.
