Creation in [[george-macdonald|George MacDonald]]'s "A Book of [[strife|Strife]] in the Form of The Diary of an Old [[soul|Soul]]" (1880) is not a completed past event but a present and ongoing divine activity in which the individual soul participates. [[george-macdonald|MacDonald]] treats creation as the continuous act by which [[god|God]] brings being out of non-being — and the soul is both product and, in a qualified sense, participant in that act.

## Creation as Ongoing Act

[[george-macdonald|MacDonald]] conceives creation not as a finished work but as a present reality in which the soul is caught up:

> I ride the crest of the creation-wave, / Helpless no more, no more to need defence (012-017)

The "creation-wave" image is dynamic, not static. The soul does not observe creation from the shore; it rides the wave. The posture is active participation in something larger than itself. "Helpless no more" registers a transition from passivity to agency — the creature cooperating with the Creator's ongoing work.

The renewal of creation through the individual is explicit:

> And all creation rise in me created new. (012-009)

The soul is not merely within creation; creation rises *in* the soul. The microcosm reflects and participates in the macrocosm. What [[god|God]] does in the individual is a recapitulation of the original creative act — "created new."

## Creation's Problem

[[george-macdonald|MacDonald]] identifies a "problem" at the heart of creation — the reconciliation of unity and plurality:

> Which yet two shall be, / Is thy creation's problem, deep, and true, / To make of those two one, yea one of kind (012-026)

The "two" are [[god|God]] and the soul, or perhaps the soul and its fellows. Creation's "problem" is the achievement of unity without the obliteration of distinction. The goal is "one of kind" — not absorption into undifferentiated identity but harmony of nature.

## Creation's Crown

[[human|Human]]ity, and more specifically the human soul in its perfected state, is creation's purpose and culmination:

> But walkest crowned, creation's heart and bloom. (006-005)

The soul that walks with [[god|God]] is "creation's heart and bloom" — the centre and the flowering. [[george-macdonald|MacDonald]]'s theological anthropology places the individual soul at the summit of the created order, not through merit but through divine intention. Creation exists for the sake of the creature's union with the Creator.

## Creation and Time

[[george-macdonald|MacDonald]]'s creation theology embraces the temporal process as the medium of divine work:

> And time, and thousandfold creation's play. (012-025)

Time is not the enemy of creation but its field. "Creation's play" suggests freedom, joy, and variety — not mechanical necessity. The "thousandfold" multiplication evokes a creation teeming with diversity and vitality.

## The Soul's Ascent

Creation is also portrayed as a hill up which souls are sent:

> Thou hast been sending up creation's hill, / To lift thy souls aloft (011-021)

The image is of [[rise|ascent]] — creation as a gra[[death|die]]nt, not a flat plain. [[soul|Soul]]s are not placed upon the hill but sent *up* it. The movement is progressive, [[growth|development]]al, directed toward elevation. Creation is the instrument and arena of sanctification.
