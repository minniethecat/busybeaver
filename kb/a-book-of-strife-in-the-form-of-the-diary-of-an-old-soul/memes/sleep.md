## Definition

Sleep in [[george-macdonald|MacDonald]]'s poetry carries a double theological valence. Negatively, it signifies spiritual torpor — the soul's drift from alertness to [[god|God]], a state from which one must be wakened: "[[wake|Wake]], thou that sleepest; rise up from the dead" (011-005). Positively, sleep is sacred [[peace|rest]] in [[presence|divine presence]]: "In thee I [[peace|rest]]; in sleep thou dost me fold" (008-006). This duality mirrors the broader pattern of [[george-macdonald|MacDonald]]'s [[night]] imagery, where darkness is both threat and shelter. Sleep's meaning depends entirely on whether the sleeper [[peace|rest]]s in self-enclosure or in [[god|God]].

## Key Passages

The negative sleep is a condition approaching [[death]]: "I sit alone, a [[grief|sorrow]] half asleep, / My [[conscious|consciousness]] the blackness" (007-001). Here sleep is not [[peace|rest]] but diminished existence — the soul neither fully alive nor fully dead, suspended in a grey medium of reduced [[conscious|awareness]]. The speaker can be "sunk, and lost, and dead in sleep" (007-018), where sleep becomes indistinguishable from spiritual death.

Yet sleep in [[god|God]] is the opposite: "In thee I [[peace|rest]]; in sleep thou dost me fold" (008-006). The image shifts from death to embrace — [[god|God]] folds the sleeper as a parent folds a child in blankets. This positive sleep generates visions: "in my sleep, what true, what perfect [[dream|dreams]]!" (002-024), where sleep becomes the medium of divine [[dream]]-revelation rather than un[[conscious|consciousness]].

The speaker's [[conscious|awareness]] of sleep's d[[wrath|anger]] generates the prayer for [[wake]]fulness: "[[christ|Lord]], wake me oftener, lest I sin" (002-015). Sleep is the default state; waking requires divine intervention. The martial call — "[[wake|Wake]], thou that sleepest; rise up from the dead" (011-005) — echoes Ephesians 5:14, casting sleep as a condition requiring [[rise|resurrection]].

[[george-macdonald|MacDonald]] contrasts divine sleeplessness with human somnolence: the prayer ascends "to him who slumbers not nor sleeps" (004-011). [[god|God]] is the one who never sleeps; human spiritual health depends on proximity to this unsleeping center. Even in positive sleep, the speaker wonders: "Now, ere I sleep, I wonder what I shall dream" (004-006), maintaining a liminal [[conscious|awareness]] that sleep's gifts come from beyond [[self|the self]].

## Related Concepts

Sleep is the passive counterpart to active [[wake]]fulness and the night-condition paralleling [[night]]. Positive sleep connects to [[dream]] as revelation, [[peace]] as divine [[peace|rest]], and [[death]] as temporary surrender. Negative sleep links to [[sin]] as spiritual lethargy and [[darkness]] as obscured vision. The call to wake from sleep connects to [[grace]] as divine rousing and [[strife]] as the [[strife|struggle]] against spiritual torpor.
