# Shadow

**Shadow** in [[A Book of Strife in the Form of The Diary of an Old Soul]] is [[george-macdonald|MacDonald]]'s central metaphor for the mediated, partial character of human [[wisdom|knowledge]] of [[god|God]]. All [[earth|earthly]] experience is shadow — not illusion but intimation, the penumbral edge of a reality too bright for direct [[vision|sight]]. The shadow points beyond itself to the [[light]] that casts it.

## All Things Are Shadows

[[george-macdonald|MacDonald]] states the principle with metaphysical clarity:

> "All things are shadows of the shining true." [007-021]

This is not a denial of the world's reality but an affirmation of its dependent, derivative character. Everything visible participates in and gestures toward the invisible reality that is its source. The sun, sea, and earth are themselves shadows — real in their own order but not ultimate.

## Sitting in the Shadow

The proper human posture toward divine reality is one of receptive [[calm|stillness]]:

> "Sitting in thy shadow, look out on the shine." [002-007]

To sit in [[god|God]]'s shadow is to accept the mediated character of present [[wisdom|knowledge]] while looking toward the fuller light. It is a posture of [[humble|humility]] — the soul does not claim direct vision but accepts the shade as a mercy, knowing that unmediated light would blind.

## Shadow and Overshadowing

[[george-macdonald|MacDonald]] uses the [[word|language]] of overshadowing to describe both threat and comfort:

> "Shall rise and gather, and overshadow me." [007-017]

The overshadowing can be a gathering darkness that threatens, but the poet's response is unexpected: "I shall have comfort in the very [[fear|dread]]." The divine shadow, even when it feels ominous, carries within it the presence of the one who casts it.

## Shadow as Hint of Splendour

[[earth|Earth]]ly goods are not the reality itself but hints — shadows that suggest a greater substance:

> "Are but hint-shadows of full many a splendour." [004-005]

[[george-macdonald|MacDonald]] does not despise the shadows. They are true in their measure, and their very insufficiency is a kindness, preparing the soul for what it cannot yet bear.

## We Are a Shadow and a Shining

The poet includes [[human|humanity]] itself in the shadow-[[word|language]]:

> "We are a shadow and a shining, we!" [009-001]

The human self participates in both orders — it is shadow in its [[earth|earthly]] condition and shining in its divine origin and destiny. This dual nature is the [[earth|ground]] of both [[humble|humility]] and hope.
