## Definition

[[george-macdonald|MacDonald]] employs "dream" across a spectrum from divine revelation to self-deception. At its highest, dream is a mode of spiritual perception that transcends waking [[conscious|consciousness]]: "I have had dreams of absolute [[joy|delight]], / Beyond all waking [[joy|bliss]]" (004-003). At its lowest, dream signifies illusion and separation from [[god|God]]: "I lie in a dream / Of separation" (012-007). This duality makes dream one of [[george-macdonald|MacDonald]]'s most complex metaphors — it can be the vehicle of [[truth]] or the opposite of truth, depending on whether the dreamer is oriented toward the divine or trapped in self-enclosure.

## Key Passages

The fourth chapter opens with a sustained meditation on dream as revelation. The speaker asks [[god|God]]: "wilt thou not one day turn to dreams? / Some dreams wilt thou not one day turn to truths?" (004-002), suggesting that dreams are not falsehoods to be discarded but potential truths awaiting divine actualization. He recalls "dreams of absolute [[joy|delight]], / Beyond all waking [[joy|bliss]]" (004-003), positioning dream-[[conscious|consciousness]] as capable of exceeding ordinary perception. The aesthetic quality of this state is evoked in "The hues of dreamland, strange and sweet and tender" (004-005).

Yet dream also represents the soul's distance from [[god|God]]. The speaker confesses: "I lie in a dream / Of separation, where there comes no sign" (012-007). Here dream is not heightened perception but diminished [[conscious|awareness]] — a state of spiritual numbness where divine signals go unregistered. This negative valence appears again when the speaker imagines waking to find that [[earth|earthly]] life was a dream: "rise, and all shall be a dream" (007-002).

[[george-macdonald|MacDonald]] draws a sharp ontological distinction: "They are to truth but as dream-deeds to acts" (007-028). Dream-deeds lack the substance of real acts; they are [[shadow|shadows]] of moral reality. The speaker prays to be delivered "Out of my dream to him who [[sleep|slumber]]s not nor sleeps" (004-011), identifying dream with the soul's somnolence and [[god|God]] as the ever-wakeful reality beyond it.

The eschatological dimension appears in the promise that divine love will "laugh all the dreams to holy scorn" (008-028), where dreams — both the sweet and the troubled — will be exposed as insubstantial compared to the weight of eternal [[life]].

## Related Concepts

Dream's revelatory potential connects to [[vision]] and [[hope]], while its illusory aspect links to [[darkness]] and [[sin]]. The transition out of dream into divine reality is the work of spiritual [[wake]]fulness. Dream's nocturnal setting ties it to [[night]] and [[sleep]], while the content of true dreams relates to [[heaven]] and [[truth]]. The speaker's [[desire|longing]] for dreams to be transformed into reality echoes the broader pattern of divine transformation seen in [[grace]] and [[love]].
