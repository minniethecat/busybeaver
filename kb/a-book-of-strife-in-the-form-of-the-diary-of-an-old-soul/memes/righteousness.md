Righteousness in [[george-macdonald|George MacDonald]]'s "A Book of [[strife|Strife]] in the Form of The Diary of an Old [[soul|Soul]]" is not a status the soul attains but a hunger it feeds. The speaker's "hunger now is after righteousness" (006-020), and this hunger is itself the sign of [[God]]'s work — the appetite for rightness that no [[wrath]] or self-effort can produce. Righteousness is lived in the "day-life" of obe[[death|die]]nce (005-016), not claimed as a settled possession. The speaker warns that wrath — divine or human — "will never work thy righteousness" (002-018), dismantling the old economy of fear in favour of a righteousness received as [[gift]].

## Wrath Cannot Work Righteousness

The speaker's stark dictum — "My wrath will never work thy righteousness" (002-018) — severs the link between divine punishment and human rectitude. Righteousness cannot be frightened into the soul; fear produces compliance, not conversion. The speaker hears this as [[God]]'s own word, a promise that [[god|God]] does not use wrath as an instrument of reformation. What wrath cannot do, love must — and the [[peace|rest]] of the poem is an exploration of how love accomplishes what wrath cannot.

> My wrath will never work thy righteousness. / Up, up the hill, to the white summit draw. (002-018)

## The Hunger for Rightness

The speaker does not claim righteousness but hungers for it: "My spirit hopes in [[god|God]] to set it free" (006-020). The hunger itself is the evidence of the [[Spirit]]'s presence — the soul that desires rightness has already been touched by the one who is rightness. [[george-macdonald|MacDonald]] treats the appetite for righteousness as more significant than any achieved righteousness, because appetite keeps the soul in the posture of reception. The one who hungers "after righteousness" is blessed not because he will become self-righteous but because he will be filled by the only source of rightness.

> My hunger now is after righteousness; / My spirit hopes in God to set it free. (006-020)

## The Day-Life of Obedience

Righteousness is not an abstraction but the texture of a day: "He whose day-life is [[obedience|obedient]] righteousness" (005-016). The compound "day-life" insists that righteousness happens in hours and minutes, not in doctrines or professions. After failure, this person rises again — not because his righteousness was strong but because his [[obedience]] is rekindled by the one who does not fail. The "poor success" of past effort is ac[[wisdom|knowledge]]d and then released; righteousness is always the next hour's task, never the last hour's trophy.

> He whose day-life is obedient righteousness, / Who, after failure, or a poor success... (005-016)
