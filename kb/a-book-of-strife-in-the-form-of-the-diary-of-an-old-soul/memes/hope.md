Hope in [[george-macdonald|George MacDonald]]'s "A Book of [[strife|Strife]] in the Form of The Diary of an Old [[soul|Soul]]" (1880) is not a vague wish for better circumstances but a theological virtue [[earth|ground]]ed in the character of [[god|God]]. For [[george-macdonald|MacDonald]], hope is the soul's orientation toward a future already secured by divine love — it is confidence in [[god|God]]'s ongoing work of transformation, a confidence that persists even when the speaker can see no evidence of progress.

## Definition in MacDonald's Thought

[[george-macdonald|MacDonald]] presents hope as inseparable from faith. In one of the diary's most programmatic stanzas, he makes the connection explicit:

> If to myself—"God sometimes interferes"— / I said, my faith at once would be struck blind. / I see him all in all, the lifing mind, / Or nowhere in the vacant miles and years. (001-009)

If [[god|God]] is not continuously present and active, faith collapses — and with it, hope. The alternative to hope is not despair but a mechanistic universe of "vacant miles and years." Hope is the natural posture of a soul that believes [[god|God]] is "all in all."

Hope is also tied to willing. In December, the speaker pushes hope to its logical extreme:

> Willing thy will beyond all bounds of hope. (012-002)

To will [[god|God]]'s will "beyond all bounds of hope" means to [[faith|trust]] [[god|God]]'s purposes even when they exceed what the speaker can imagine desiring. Hope's horizon is not human imagination but divine goodness.

## Key Passages

> A love he is that watches and that hears, / Or but a mist fumed up from minds of men, / Whose fear and hope reach out beyond their ken. (001-009)

[[george-macdonald|MacDonald]] reduces the alternatives to two: either [[god|God]] is a personal, attentive love, or [[god|God]] is a projection of human [[desire|longing]]. The dismissal of the second option is implicit — the very fact that fear and hope "reach out beyond their ken" points toward a real object.

> I hope entirely. On, Lord, with thy labour grand. (004-013)

This single line captures [[george-macdonald|MacDonald]]'s theology of hope in miniature. The speaker does not partially hope or conditionally hope — he hopes "entirely." And the [[earth|ground]] of that hope is not his own effort but [[god|God]]'s "labour grand" — the work of transformation that [[god|God]] is already doing within him.

> Farewell, my dove!—come back, hope-laden, through the rain. (008-010)

The dove, sent out like Noah's, returns "hope-laden" — carrying evidence that the flood has receded. The rain through which it flies is the present difficulty, but the dove's cargo is the certainty of dry land.

## Hope in Darkness

[[george-macdonald|MacDonald]] does not pretend that hope comes easily. The diary repeatedly ac[[wisdom|knowledge]]s the experience of hope's absence:

> When I no more can stir my soul to move, / And life is but the ashes of a fire; / When I can but remember that my heart / Once used to live and love, long and aspire (001-010)

The speaker describes spiritual exhaustion — "the ashes of a fire" — in which he can only remember having once hoped. Yet even here, the prayer is for [[god|God]] to "be thou the first" and "in me wake hope, fear, [[infinite|boundless]] desire." Hope is not self-generated; it is [[wake|awake]]ned by [[god|God]].

> My harvest withers. Health, my means to live— / All things seem rushing straight into the dark. / But the dark still is God. (001-015)

The most radical statement of hope in the diary: "the dark still is [[god|God]]." Even when every circumstance rushes toward darkness, the darkness itself belongs to [[god|God]]. Hope does not deny the darkness; it [[faith|trust]]s that [[god|God]] is within it.

## Speechless Hopes

[[george-macdonald|MacDonald]] describes a form of hope that precedes articulation:

> Know it creating in me, feel the throng / Of speechless hopes out-throbbing like a tide (008-026)

These "[[word|speech]]less hopes" are pre-conceptual — they "out-throb" like a physical tide before they can be named. Hope, in [[george-macdonald|MacDonald]]'s psychology, is not first a set of propositions about the future but a movement of the heart that rises before the mind can catch up. The image connects hope to the body, to the pulse, to the involuntary — it is something that happens *to* the speaker, not something he produces.
