## Definition

In [[george-macdonald|MacDonald]]'s [[worship|devotion]]al poetry, *home* is a theological metaphor that fuses origin and destination. [[god|God]] is the soul's "natal home" (003-003) — the place where it was born and the place to which it must return. Home is not a location but a relation[[vessel|ship]]: "Be thou my home, my fire, my chamber blest" (002-012). The spiritual life is a homeward journey, and every human [[desire|longing]] is, at its root, the pull of the true home. [[george-macdonald|MacDonald]] draws on the parable of the prodigal son: the wanderer is driven home by hunger and thirst (007-011), and the journey is less a climb than a being-carried — "it shall toss thee homeward to thy leas" (001-013). The "perfect home" is where [[christ|Christ]] d[[water|well]]s as "Ideal brother" (012-021), and the star of perfect love is "the home of [[desire|longing]] heart and brain" (012-031).

## Key Passages

> "Be thou my home, my fire, my chamber blest, / My garden ground." (002-012)

Home is not a shelter apart from [[god|God]]; [[god|God]] himself is the home — the fire, the blessed chamber, the garden where the soul d[[water|well]]s.

> "When we are in our home, our natal home, / When joy shall be the all-in-all of life." (003-003)

The "natal home" is the soul's birthplace and destiny, and its arrival is marked by joy becoming the totality of experience.

> "Not pilgrim I, a homeless wanderer — / For how canst thou be near and I be far?" (007-001)

The speaker refuses the identity of "homeless wanderer" because proximity to [[god|God]] means already being home; distance is a fiction if [[god|God]] is near.

> "The hunger-quest, / Drive home the wanderer to the father's breast." (007-011)

[[hunger|Hunger]] and thirst are not punishments but homing devices — they drive the wanderer back to the Father.

> "Thou hast left the perfect home, / Ideal brother, to seek thy brothers here." (012-021)

[[christ|Christ]] left the "perfect home" to seek lost [[brother|sibling]]s; the incarnation is a home-leaving for the sake of the homeless.

> "Him he has made our brother: homeward, thus, / To find our kin we go." (012-027)

[[christ|Christ]] as brother makes the homeward journey a family reunion — we go to find our kin.

## Related Concepts

Home is the destination of every spiritual [[desire|longing]] described in concepts such as [[desire]], [[hunger]], and [[thirst]]. It is where the [[soul]] finds [[peace|rest]] from the [[peace|rest]]lessness of [[self]]. The homeward journey is guided by [[love]] and sustained by [[trust]]; the Father ([[father]]) awaits at the journey's end. The condition of being homeless — explored in [[weary]] and [[strife]] — is the starting point from which the soul turns homeward.
