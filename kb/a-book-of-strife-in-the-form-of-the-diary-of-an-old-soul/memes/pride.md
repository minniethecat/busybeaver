Pride in [[george-macdonald|George MacDonald]]'s "A Book of [[strife|Strife]] in the Form of The Diary of an Old [[soul|Soul]]" takes three distinct forms, none of them simple arrogance. There is "anxious pride" (004-010) — the [[peace|rest]]less self-concern that cannot [[peace|rest]] in [[God]]'s care. There is "having-pride" (008-007) — the spiritual possessiveness that turns [[gift]] into property until it breeds corruption. And there is "discovery's pride" (005-014) — the [[mind|intellect]]ual thrill of new [[wisdom|insight]] that mistakes the excitement of finding for the substance of [[faith]]. Together they form a taxonomy of the subtle ways the [[soul]] refuses to receive as gift what it would prefer to hold as achievement.

## Anxious Pride

"Anxious pride" is the compound name the speaker gives to the tangle of "doubts, dis[[faith|trust]]s" that keep the soul [[peace|rest]]less (004-010). Pride and anxiety are not opposites but partners: the pride that insists on managing its own safety produces the anxiety of one who cannot [[faith|trust]] another's care. The speaker prays to be covered in [[god|God]]'s "garment" — to be wrapped in a care that supplants the need for self-protection. Anxious pride is the refusal to be held.

> My doubts, distrusts and anxious pride, / And in thy garment, as under tent or hide. (004-010)

## The Having-Pride

The most distinctive form of pride in the Diary is "the having-pride" — the corruption that afflicts spiritual [[gift]] when it is possessed rather than received. The speaker finds it "momently": "The holiest bread, if hoarded, soon will breed / The mammon-moth, the having-pride" (008-007). To "have" a spiritual gift is already to have lost it, because the gift-nature of grace means it cannot be stored. The having-pride turns the living [[bread]] of heaven into a treasury of moths. The answer is to receive each moment's gift afresh and let it go.

> The holiest bread, if hoarded, soon will breed / The mammon-moth, the having-pride, I find. (008-007)

## Discovery's Pride

Even spiritual [[wisdom|insight]] can be [[bread|food]] for pride. "Discovery's pride, joy's [[joy|bliss]], take aback my soul" (005-014) — the speaker names the subtle self-congratulation that follows new [[wisdom|understanding]]. To discover a truth about [[God]] is to be tempted to take credit for the discovery. The thrill of [[wisdom|insight]] — "joy's [[joy|bliss]]" — is real but d[[wrath|anger]]ous when it shifts attention from the truth discovered to the discoverer. The "pulses" of [[self|the self]] quicken with pride precisely when they should [[silence|quiet]] with [[humility]].

> Discovery's pride, joy's bliss, take aback my soul / And its pulses quell. (005-014)
