Unrest in [[george-macdonald|George MacDonald]]'s "A Book of [[strife|Strife]] in the Form of The Diary of an Old [[soul|Soul]]" is not a symptom of spiritual illness but a divine gift. The speaker names it as something [[god|God]] gives: "To some thou giv'st a deep un[[peace|rest]]" (006-017) and "thou hast given an infinite un[[peace|rest]]" (006-019). This un[[peace|rest]] is the engine of spiritual motion — a holy dissatisfaction that refuses to let the soul settle for anything less than [[god|God]]. It is the opposite of complacency, the divine strategy for souls that would otherwise stop short of their true [[rest]].

## The Gift of Discontent

The speaker is explicit: un[[peace|rest]] comes from [[god|God]], and it comes as a gift, not a punishment. "To some thou giv'st a deep un[[peace|rest]] — a scorn / Of all they are or seem to be" (006-017). The un[[peace|rest]] is paired with scorn — not self-loathing but a clear-eyed rejection of the present self as final. This scorn is the energy of [[repentance]] without the morbidity. It says: what I am is not what I shall be, and the gap between them is [[god|God]]'s work-site.

> To some thou giv'st a deep unrest — a scorn / Of all they are or seem to be (006-017)

## The Infinite Hunger

Unrest is infinite because its object is infinite: "thou hast given an infinite un[[peace|rest]], / A hunger — not at first after known good" (006-019). [[george-macdonald|MacDonald]] distinguishes this hunger from ordinary desire for known satisfactions. It is a hunger that does not yet know what it hungers for — a [[peace|rest]]lessness whose object is hidden from the one who suffers it. This unknowing is the point: if the soul knew what it sought, it might try to manufacture it. The infinite un[[peace|rest]] drives beyond every finite satisfaction toward the one who alone is infinite.

> thou hast given an infinite unrest, / A hunger — not at first after known good (006-019)

## The Shaping File

Unrest works on the soul like a tool: "Till comes again the rasp of un[[peace|rest]]'s file" (008-002). The metaphor is artisanal — a file that shapes by abrasion, removing what is rough, bringing the surface to smoothness. The word "again" signals that this is a repeated work, not a one-time crisis. Each return of un[[peace|rest]]'s rasp is another pass of the divine craftsman's tool. The soul being shaped is in [[liberty]] — "From [[freedom|liberty]] is distanced" — the file works in the space between what the soul is and what it will be.

> Till comes again the rasp of unrest's file (008-002)

## The Soaking

Unrest is pervasive, not episodic: "Soaks them with un[[peace|rest]]; makes our every [[wind|breath]] / A throe of his creation" (009-022). To be soaked is to be saturated — no part of [[self|the self]] escapes. Every [[wind|breath]] becomes a throe, a labour-pang of the new creation. [[george-macdonald|MacDonald]]'s vision is uncomfortable: the [[love]] of [[god|God]] manifests as a thoroughgoing agitation that leaves no settled corner of the soul untouched. Yet the soaking is not drowning; it is baptism.

> Soaks them with unrest; makes our every breath / A throe of his creation (009-022)
