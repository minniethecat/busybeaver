Power in [[george-macdonald|George MacDonald]]'s "A Book of [[strife|Strife]] in the Form of The Diary of an Old [[soul|Soul]]" (1880) is understood through a theology of radical dependence. The speaker confesses that he possesses no inherent power, yet discovers that authentic power flows from [[god|God]] into the receptive soul. [[george-macdonald|MacDonald]]'s treatment of power inverts conventional hierarchies: power is not in self-assertion but in surrender, not in holding on but in letting go.

## Human Powerlessness

The diary opens its February section with a stark confession of incapacity:

> I to myself have neither power nor worth,
> Patience nor love, nor anything right good. (002-001)

This is not rhetorical modesty but a theological premise. The speaker claims nothing — no power, no worth, no virtue — as his own possession. The next stanza names the corrosive effect of the one power humans do wield:

> The worst power of an evil mood is this —
> It makes the bastard self seem in the right. (002-002)

The "worst power" is not external but internal: the capacity of a disordered mood to distort moral perception, making the false self seem justified. This is the only power [[george-macdonald|MacDonald]] grants to unaided [[human|human nature]] — the power of self-deception.

## Letting Go as Power

The movement from powerlessness to power takes an unexpected form — not acquisition but release:

> Give me the power to let my rag-rights go
> In the great wind that from thy gulf doth blow. (002-017)

The speaker prays not for more power but for the power to relinquish what he mistakenly holds as rights. The "rag-rights" — tattered claims to autonomy — must be surrendered to the "great wind" of [[god|God]]'s spirit. Power, paradoxically, is the capacity to let go.

## Creative Power Belongs to God Alone

[[george-macdonald|MacDonald]] makes a categorical distinction between human and divine agency:

> We have no making power;
> Then give us making will, adored be thy name. (003-023)

[[human|Human]]s cannot create — "we have no making power." What they can receive is "making will," the desire to be made and remade by the only true Maker. The shift from "power" to "will" is central: human agency is not productive but receptive.

> Thy power doth reach me in so many ways. (004-023)

The speaker ac[[wisdom|knowledge]]s that divine power reaches into his life through multiple channels — through circumstance, through relation[[vessel|ship]], through the inner movements of conscience and desire. The variety of [[god|God]]'s approaches underscores the completeness of dependence: there is no domain of life untouched by divine power.

## Power Disguised as Estrangement

In one of [[george-macdonald|MacDonald]]'s most subtle treatments, power can manifest under an appearance of absence:

> I think I will not cry to thee for aid,
> But, shut within my chamber, think and write;
> Yet most I learn I from the estranged power,
> Through doubt, through faith, through longing, and through fright. (005-015)

[[god|God]] appears here as "an estranged power" — not absent but felt as distant. Yet even this estrangement is pedagogical: the speaker learns "most" from it. The apparent withdrawal of divine power is itself an exercise of that power, teaching the soul through doubt and [[desire|longing]].

## Heart-Power from God

The diary's final treatment of power locates it in the heart's outgoing capacity:

> From thee goes out heart-power,
> Working the holy, satisfying hour. (005-031)

"[[heart|Heart]]-power" flows from [[god|God]] into the believer, enabling the work of [[holy|holiness]]. The power that matters is not the power to perform but the power to love — a power that originates in [[god|God]] and operates through the responsive [[human|human heart]].
