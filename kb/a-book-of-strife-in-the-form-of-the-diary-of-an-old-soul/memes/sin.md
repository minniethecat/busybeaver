Sin in [[george-macdonald|George MacDonald]]'s "A Book of [[strife|Strife]] in the Form of The Diary of an Old [[soul|Soul]]" (1880) is understood not as a legal infraction against divine law but as a relational rupture — the soul's turning away from [[god|God]] toward its own will. [[george-macdonald|MacDonald]] treats sin as forgetfulness of the [[presence|divine presence]], a failure of attention and love rather than a stain demanding punishment. His theology is characteristically anti-forensic: sin is disease, [[god|God]] is physician, and redemption is healing.

## Definition in MacDonald's Thought

For [[george-macdonald|MacDonald]], sin is fundamentally a matter of the will choosing self over [[god|God]]. The opening stanza of April establishes this tension:

> LORD, I do choose the higher than my will. / I would be handled by thy nursing arms / After thy will, not my infant alarms. (004-001)

The speaker consciously elects [[god|God]]'s will over his own, framing sin as the default state — the "infant alarms" of [[self|the self]] — that must be actively overridden. Sin here is not a particular act but an orientation of the will.

This relational [[wisdom|understanding]] reaches its most striking formulation in August, where the speaker imagines a state of spiritual maturity so complete that sin becomes impossible:

> Shall I not grow at last so true within / As to forget thee and yet never sin? (008-021)

The paradox is deliberate: to "forget thee" would normally be the definition of sin, yet [[george-macdonald|MacDonald]] envisions a state where the soul is so permeated by the [[presence|divine presence]] that conscious [[conscious|awareness]] is no longer required. Sin is not the absence of correct [[mind|thought]]s but the absence of the in[[tent|dwelling]] [[god|God]].

## Key Passages

> Shall I not walk the loud world's busy way, / Yet in thy palace-porch sit all the day? / Not conscious think of thee, yet never from thee stray? (008-021)

This stanza envisions a transformed will — active in the world yet rooted in [[god|God]], so that outward busy-ness no longer implies inward separation. The "palace-porch" metaphor suggests proximity without strain.

> Thy beasts are sinless, and do live before thee; / Thy child is sinful, and must run to thee. / Thy angels sin not and in peace adore thee; / But I must will, or never more be free. (012-019)

Here [[george-macdonald|MacDonald]] draws a deliberate contrast between the sinlessness of animals and angels — who lack moral will — and the human condition. [[human|Human]] sin is inseparable from human freedom: "I must will, or never more be free." Sin is the shadow-side of volitional personhood.

> Therefore I make provision, ere I begin / To do the thing thou givest me to do, / Praying,—Lord, wake me oftener, lest I sin. (002-015)

The speaker's preemptive prayer against sin treats it as a constant vulnerability — not to specific temptations but to spiritual drowsiness. The remedy is not self-discipline but divine [[wake|awakening]].

## Sin and the Sinking Heart

A recurring image in the *Diary* associates sin with *sinking* — the heart descending, losing buoyancy, weighed down:

> From heart to heart it sinks, it steals, it flows, / From these that know thee still infecting those. (006-001)

Here "sinks" carries a double meaning: love sinks into [[heart|hearts]] (positive), but the verb itself belongs to a semantic field of descent that elsewhere marks spiritual d[[wrath|anger]]. The heart that "sinks" in [[george-macdonald|MacDonald]] is the heart that has lost its upward orientation.

> Will my soul sink, and shall I stand aghast, / Beggared of hope, my heart a conscious blight? (008-024)

The fear of sinking at the final revelation — of being unable to bear the divine vision — is [[george-macdonald|MacDonald]]'s version of the fear of judgment. But he answers his own fear immediately: love "will sink into me like the father's kiss" (008-025). The same verb that describes sin's effect describes grace's remedy.

## The Corruption of Love Without God

[[george-macdonald|MacDonald]] traces all sin to one root: love separated from its divine source. The June section provides a catalogue of perversions:

> Life-giving love rots to devouring fire; / Justice corrupts to despicable revenge; / Motherhood chokes in the dam's jealous mire (006-022)

Each virtue, divorced from divine love, becomes its opposite vice. This diagnostic framework means [[george-macdonald|MacDonald]] never treats sin as an independent force — it is always parasitic, a distortion of something originally good. The implication for redemption is clear: sin is not destroyed but *re-oriented*, brought back into alignment with the love from which it fell.
