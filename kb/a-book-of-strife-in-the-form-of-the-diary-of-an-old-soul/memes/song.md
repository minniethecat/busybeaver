## Definition

Song in [[george-macdonald|MacDonald]]'s [[worship|devotion]]al poetics is the telos of [[word|language]] — words raised to the pitch of [[worship]]. The speaker envisions a future in which "stumbling words will some day turn to song" (010-030), positioning song not as a separate genre but as [[word|language]] perfected. Song bridges the individual and the cosmic: the speaker desires to join "harmony with every spheric song" (001-020), where personal [[worship|praise]] merges into a universe-wide chorus. [[george-macdonald|MacDonald]]'s own poetic form — rhymed, metrical, sequential — embo[[death|die]]s this movement from [[word|speech]] toward song.

## Key Passages

The opening of the sequence announces song's centrality: "THE song birds that come to me night and morn, / Fly oft away and vanish if I sleep" (003-001). Song birds are visitors, not possessions — inspiration comes and goes, requiring the soul's [[wake]]fulness to receive them. The speaker's ambition is to become song himself: "I am thy book, thy song — thy child would be" (010-031), where "song" is the highest identity before [[god|God]], surpassing even "book."

The cosmic dimension appears in the vision of universal harmony: "harmony with every spheric song, / And conscious power" (001-020). Individual song participates in a vast music that includes the spheres themselves — a Pythagorean-[[christ|Christ]]ian fusion where cosmic order is musical. The speaker imagines drawing others "nigher, / With dance and song to cast their best upon thy fire" (012-010), where song is not performance but offering.

Song's source is divine [[love]]: "The soul of all the songs the saints do sing!" (004-029). Saints' songs are not original compositions but participations in a single soul-song whose origin is [[god|God]]'s love. The speaker's patience is oriented toward this future music: "can be patient for all coming songs, / But let me sing my one monotonous" (005-009) — the present duty is to sing one's own note faithfully, however simple, [[trust|trusting]] the polyphony will come.

The silence that precedes song is also sacred: "is still, no flutter and no song, / Is safe" (007-023). Stillness is safety because false song — premature, self-generated [[worship|praise]] — is distortion. True song emerges from the [[silence|quiet]] soul that waits for divine impulse.

## Related Concepts

Song is the perfected form of [[word]] and the active expression of [[worship]]. Its cosmic harmony connects to [[heaven]] and [[glory]], while its origin in love links to [[love]] and [[grace]]. The patience required for true song ties to [[patience]], and the silence that precedes it connects to [[silence]] and [[peace]]. Song as offering relates to [[fire]] and the sacrificial dimension of [[prayer]].
