## Definition

In *A Book of [[strife|Strife]]*, [[george-macdonald|MacDonald]] treats the body with ambivalent [[awe|reverence]]. On one hand, the body is the "very body of [[god|God]]" in its capacity to house divine being (002-007); the soul knows [[god|God]] "as the body knows its soul" (011-018).

On the other hand, the body is subject to decay: "Well may this body poorer, feebler grow!" (008-011), and its death is "very tolerable" when the will is pure (003-026).

The body is not a prison to be escaped but a [[clay|vessel]] that wears thin as the soul matures. [[george-macdonald|MacDonald]] refuses both gnostic contempt for the body and materialist reduction to it.

## Key Passages

> "Its being's heart, the very body of God." (002-007)

The body participates in the being of [[god|God]]. This startling phrase gives physical form sacred dignity without idolizing it.

> "Well may this body poorer, feebler grow! / It is undressing for its last sweet bed." (008-011)

Bodily decline is reframed as preparation — an undressing for [[peace|rest]]. The body's weakening is a natural shedding.

> "Then shall this body's death be very tolerable." (003-026)

When the will is aligned with [[god|God]]'s pure will, even death loses its sting. Mortality becomes a matter-of-fact passage.

> "The body more than raiment — then than health / Itself." (001-016)

[[george-macdonald|MacDonald]] echoes [[christ|Jesus]]'s teaching and extends it: the body is more than health itself, valuing the physical while relativizing [[water|well]]being.

> "Or knew thee as the body knows its soul." (011-018)

The intimate [[wisdom|knowledge]] between body and soul becomes the model for knowing [[god|God]] — intimate, immediate, inseparable.

## Related Concepts

The body connects to [[soul]] as its intimate companion, to [[death]] as the moment of undressing, to [[life]] as the animating presence that gives the body meaning, to [[pain]] as the body's [[master|teacher]], and to [[earth]] as the body's origin and destination. It also relates to [[spirit]] as the invisible reality the body serves.
