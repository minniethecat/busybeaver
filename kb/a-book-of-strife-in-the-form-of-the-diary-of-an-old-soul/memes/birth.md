In [[george-macdonald|George MacDonald]]'s "A Book of [[strife|Strife]] in the Form of The Diary of an Old [[soul|Soul]]", birth is the master metaphor for spiritual transformation. The speaker distinguishes between the first birth — natural human existence — and the "second birth" (002-006) by which the soul gains eyes to see [[god|God]] in everything. This second birth is not instantaneous; it arrives "by faint degrees" and travels "through centuries" (007-010). The speaker claims his "birthright ineffable" (006-010) not as an entitlement but as a filial duty: the child must claim the Father because the Father has made him to do so. Birth culminates in the "new-day-birth" of eternal morning, when the soul [[wake|awake]]ns panting to clasp the Father's feet.

## Eyes Opened by the Second Birth

[[george-macdonald|MacDonald]] contrasts two kinds of [[vision|sight]]. "Wise men, whose eyes are but for surfaces" cannot find [[god|God]] anywhere, but "men with eyes opened by the second birth" discern [[god|God]] as "soul of everything on earth" (002-006). The second birth is not an addition of new information but a transformation of the organ of perception. The born-again eye sees through the "husk of the unseen" to the reality within. "Eyes made for glory" — the eyes of the twice-born — "soon discover thee."

> Men with eyes opened by the second birth, / To whom the seen, husk of the unseen is, / Descry thee soul of everything on earth. (002-006)

## The Slow Travail of Birth

[[divine|Divine]] creation works "by faint degrees, / By [[shadow|shade]] and shadow from unseen beginning" (007-010). The second birth is not a sudden event but a long gestation. [[god|God]] wills his will in "unfathomable seas," and from those depths a child "at length arrives at never ending birth." The phrase "never ending birth" is crucial: spiritual birth is not a punctiliar event that concludes but an ongoing arrival. The soul is always being born into [[god|God]], each new degree of union a new delivery.

> A child at length arrives at never ending birth. (007-010)

## Claiming the Birthright

The speaker does not passively await birth but actively claims it: "I will / Claim of thee my birthright ineffable" (006-010). This claiming is paradoxically both a right and a duty — "Thou lay'st it on me, son, to claim thee, sire." To claim one's birthright is to honour the [[gift|giver]]; to refrain from claiming is to scorn the gift. [[human|Human]] presumption is not in reaching for [[god|God]] but in refusing to reach. "No man presumes in that to which he was born."

> Claim of thee my birthright ineffable. / Thou lay'st it on me, son, to claim thee, sire. (006-010)

## Birth Through Maternal Pain

The speaker draws an analogy to human birth: "As to our mothers came help in our birth — / Not lost in lifing us, but saved and blest" (003-027). The mother does not lose herself in [[gift|giving]] life but is "saved and blest." So too the soul that bears itself through the pangs of spiritual birth "shall nothing lose, but [[death|die]] and be at [[peace|rest]] / In life eternal." The pain of spiritual birth is real but not destructive; it is the labour that delivers into eternal life. The one who is "[[god|God]]-born truly" loves perfectly and "has whate'er he will."

> As to our mothers came help in our birth — / Not lost in lifing us, but saved and blest. (003-027)

## The Clouded Morning of Birth

The speaker wakes from sleep [[desire|longing]] for a "sudden glorious glee" — for "heaven and earth / In all the [[beauty|splendour]] of a new-day-birth" (010-006). But a cloud hangs between him and [[god|God]]. The "new-day-birth" is the hoped-for [[morning|dawn]] in which the soul springs panting to the Father's feet, but the present experience is of a clouded morning. The speaker's question — "Why hangs a cloud betwixt my lord and me?" — is the cry of one already born but not yet fully delivered, caught between the first birth and the second.

> Why burst not gracious on me heaven and earth / In all the splendour of a new-day-birth? (010-006)
