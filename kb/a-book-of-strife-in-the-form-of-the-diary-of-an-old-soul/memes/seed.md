The seed in [[george-macdonald|George MacDonald]]'s "A Book of [[strife|Strife]] in the Form of The Diary of an Old [[soul|Soul]]" is the primary metaphor for spiritual potential — the divine [[will]] implanted in the [[soul]] that must undergo burial, darkness, and silent growth before it can bear. Unlike the more active images of striving or ascending, the seed metaphor emphasizes passivity, hiddenness, and [[faith|trust]] in a process the seed itself cannot understand. [[george-macdonald|MacDonald]] draws the image from the Gospel of John ("unless a grain of wheat falls into the earth and [[death|die]]s") and fuses it with his own theology of divine in[[tent|dwelling]]: the seed is [[god|God]]'s will, the soul is the pod, and growth is the work of [[god|God]] alone.

## Faith as Growth, Not Mere Sowing

The opening of the seed sequence in April distinguishes static [[faith|trust]] from living [[faith]]:

> To trust is gain and growth, not mere sown seed! / Faith heaves the world round to the heavenly dawn, / In whose great light the soul doth spell and read / Itself high-born, its being derived and drawn / From the eternal self-existent fire. (004-021)

[[trust|Trust]] is emphatically *not* "mere sown seed" — not a one-time deposit that lies inert in the [[earth|ground]]. It is "gain and growth," a dynamic principle that "heaves the world round" toward [[morning|dawn]]. The seed that grows is faith itself, and its growth is what enables the soul to recognize its own "high-born" origin. The "eternal self-existent fire" from which the soul is "derived and drawn" is [[george-macdonald|MacDonald]]'s name for the divine source — the seed knows its origin only when it has grown enough to see the light.

## The Seed in Darkness

The most psychologically acute stanza in the seed sequence imagines the experience of being buried alive:

> If thou hadst closed my life in seed and husk, / And cast me into soft, warm, damp, dark mould, / All unaware of light come through the dusk, / I yet should feel the split of each shelly fold, / Should feel the growing of my prisoned heart, / And dully dream of being slow unrolled, / And in some other vagueness taking part. (005-007)

The seed does not know it is a seed. It feels only "the split of each shelly fold" — the cracking of its own casing — and "the growing of my prisoned heart." The darkness is total ("all unaware of light") yet not绝望: there is a "dull dream" of unfolding, a vague sense of participation in something larger. This is [[george-macdonald|MacDonald]]'s [[darkness|dark night]] — not the absence of [[god|God]] but the presence of [[god|God]] as pressure from within, splitting the husk. The [[heart]] is "prisoned" but growing; the prison is also the womb.

## The Seed and the Pod

The December stanza brings the metaphor to its most compressed form, identifying [[god|God]]'s will as the seed and the soul as its container:

> I will be quiet as lark upon the sod; / God's will, the seed, shall rest in me the pod. (012-007)

The couplet is deceptively simple. The soul's work is not to *grow* but to *[[peace|rest]]* — "[[silence|quiet]] as lark upon the sod." The seed does the growing; the pod does the holding. This is a theology of surrender so complete that even the effort to grow is relinquished. The [[will]] of [[god|God]] is the active agent; the human soul is the "pod" — protective, receptive, still. The lark on the sod is an image of poised [[calm|stillness]]: earthbound but not earth-be[[desire|longing]], ready for flight when the seed's work is done.

The same image appears obliquely in the final stanza, where the separateness of seeds becomes a metaphor for the radical individuality of souls:

> As touch not in the sack two grains of seed, / Touch no two hearts in great worlds populous. (012-027)

Each seed, each [[heart]], is isolated in its own growth. The "great worlds populous" cannot bridge the fundamental solitude of spiritual germination. Only "outside the making [[god|God]]" — outside the creative in[[tent|dwelling]] — can souls truly meet. The seed metaphor thus carries a social implication: real communion requires the completion of each soul's individual growth, not its bypass.
