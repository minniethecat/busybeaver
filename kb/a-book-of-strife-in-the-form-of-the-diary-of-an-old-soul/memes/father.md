The Father is the intimate, paternal dimension of the divine in [[george-macdonald|George MacDonald]]'s "A Book of [[strife|Strife]] in the Form of The Diary of an Old [[soul|Soul]]" (1880). Appearing over 50 times across the 366 stanzas, "Father" represents the warmest register of address to [[god|God]]. Where "[[lord|Lord]]" conveys sovereignty and transcendence, "Father" conveys familial closeness and unconditional care. Together, the two names form a complementary portrait: the [[god|God]] who commands is also the parent who holds. [[george-macdonald|MacDonald]]'s theology is, at its deepest level, a theology of fatherhood — the conviction that the ultimate reality is not power but parenthood, and that the cosmic process is best understood as a father raising [[child|children]].

## The Father-Child Relationship

The central metaphor of the *Diary* is not king-subject or judge-defendant but father-child. This choice is theologically loaded. A father does not hire servants; he raises sons and daughters. A father's discipline is pedagogical, not punitive. A father desires the child's free love, not coerced obe[[death|die]]nce. Every [[prayer|petition]] in the *Diary* is inflected by this familial framing.

The speaker's self-[[wisdom|understanding]] as a child is most pronounced in moments of [[strife|struggle]]: "Help me, my Father, in whatever dismay, / Whatever terror in whatever shape, / To hold the faster by thy garment's hem; / When my heart sinks, oh, lift it up to thee!" (007-014). The image of clinging to a garment's hem is deliberately childlike — a small child clutching a parent's [[garment|clothing]] for security. [[george-macdonald|MacDonald]] does not counsel stoic self-sufficiency; he models the vulnerability of dependence.

## The Father's Plan

The Father works according to a "plan" (006-001), but it is not the inscrutable decree of Calvinist predestination. [[george-macdonald|MacDonald]]'s Father-plan is organic and relational — it works through the transmission of [[love]] from one [[heart]] to another:

> From thine, as then, the healing virtue goes / Into our hearts--that is the Father's plan. / From heart to heart it sinks, it steals, it flows, / From these hearts on, into the wide world's van (006-001)

The Father does not save individuals in isolation. Salvation is contagious — a chain reaction of [[heart|hearts]] receiving and transmitting. The Father's plan is for all [[heart|hearts]] to become "hearted throughout with thee" (006-003), living by the same life that animates [[god|God]].

## The Father and the Son

The Father's relation[[vessel|ship]] with [[christ|Christ the Son]] is implicit throughout the *Diary*. When the speaker prays to become a "fellow worker" with [[christ|Christ]] (006-005), the filial relation[[vessel|ship]] extends outward: [[christ|Christ]] is the pattern of what a child of the Father looks like. The speaker's goal is to become what [[christ|Christ]] already is — a perfect child of the Father, whose will and the Father's will are one.

[[george-macdonald|MacDonald]]'s famous line — "Thou art my father's child — come to my heart" (006-027) — voices the Father's invitation. Each human being is a "father's child," bearing the divine image. The spiritual life is the progressive realization of this filial identity, the return of the child to the Father's heart.

## The Father's Responses

The speaker's experience of the Father oscillates between felt presence and felt absence. The Father is sometimes near, sometimes distant — but the speaker's [[faith|trust]] is that even apparent distance serves the Father's purpose:

| Experience | PN | Interpretation |
|------------|-----|----------------|
| Nearness | 010-018 | "Father of me, thou art my [[joy|bliss]] secure" |
| Distance | 010-014 | "It troubles me I am not better" — distance as spur to growth |
| [[trust|Trust]] amid doubt | 010-022 | "Against my fears, my doubts, my ignorance, / I [[faith|trust]] in thee, O father of my [[christ|Lord]]!" |
| Longing | 010-025 | "If with my father I did wander free" — memory of intimacy as hope |

The Father's most characteristic action is not dramatic intervention but steady presence. The speaker's "[[joy|bliss]] secure" (010-018) [[peace|rest]]s not on answered [[prayer|petition]]s but on the Father's unchanging identity: "Make of me, maker, whatsoe'er thou wilt" (010-018). This is the [[faith|trust]] of a child who does not understand the parent's decisions but [[faith|trust]]s the parent's character.

## Theological Significance

[[george-macdonald|MacDonald]]'s emphasis on divine fatherhood places him in a tradition stretching from the New Testament's "Abba" through the me[[death|die]]val mystics' bridal and filial imagery to the Victorian era's recovery of the tenderness of [[god|God]]. His contribution is the integration of fatherhood with the problem of [[pain|suffering]]. The Father is not absent from [[pain|suffering]]; [[pain|suffering]] is the Father's method:

> Thou workest perfectly. And if it seem / Some things are not so well, 'tis but because / They are too loving-deep, too lofty-wise, / For me, poor child, to understand (010-005)

The speaker cannot understand the Father's ways because the Father is "too loving-deep" — love is the explanation hidden beneath apparent harshness. This is not a philosophical theodicy but a relational [[faith|trust]]: the child does not understand, but clings to the garment's hem anyway.
