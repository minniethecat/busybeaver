# Presence

**Presence** in [[A Book of Strife in the Form of The Diary of an Old Soul]] is the felt nearness of [[god|God]] — the experiential [[earth|ground]] of the soul's life and the object of its deepest [[desire|longing]]. [[george-macdonald|George MacDonald]] treats divine presence not as a theological abstraction but as the actual, living reality in which the soul finds its [[home|true home]].

## Presence as Life's Sustaining Element

For [[george-macdonald|MacDonald]], presence is not occasional but constitutive. The soul lives by presence as a flower lives by [[sun|sunlight]]:

> "That lives by his dear presence every hour." [007-019]

This is the poet's core conviction: that the soul's existence is sustained moment by moment through [[god|God]]'s nearness. The question "what of the night?" arises precisely because presence is so fundamental that its felt absence is disorienting:

> "'Dear presence every hour!' — what of the night." [007-020]

## Turning from Presence

[[george-macdonald|MacDonald]] ac[[wisdom|knowledge]]s the soul's bewildering tendency to turn away from the very presence that sustains it:

> "Turns from thy presence for a foolish while." [008-002]

This turning is described as foolish, not wicked — a momentary distraction rather than a definitive rejection. The poet's honesty about this fickleness makes his [[desire|longing]] for presence all the more credible.

## Presence as Actual, Not Merely Symbolic

[[george-macdonald|MacDonald]] insists on the actuality of divine presence, going beyond sign or symbol:

> "Not with us — not by sign, / But presence, actual as the wounds that bled." [003-023]

This remarkable image — presence as actual as [[christ|Christ]]'s wounds — anchors the spiritual experience in the concrete reality of the incarnation. Presence is not a metaphor but a fact, as tangible as the body of the risen [[christ|Lord]].

## Presence Transforming Pain

The poet envisions divine presence as transforming the experience of [[pain|suffering]] from within:

> "His presence shall be better in our pain, / Than joy itself without him." [003-028]

This revaluation of pain is central to [[george-macdonald|MacDonald]]'s spirituality: the presence of [[god|God]] changes the quality of every experience, so that [[pain|suffering]] with presence is preferable to pleasure without it.
