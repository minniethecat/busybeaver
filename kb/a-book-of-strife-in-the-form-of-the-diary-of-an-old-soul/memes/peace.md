Peace in [[george-macdonald|George MacDonald]]'s "A Book of [[strife|Strife]] in the Form of The Diary of an Old [[soul|Soul]]" (1880) is not the cessation of difficulty but a quality of soul that coexists with unresolved tension. [[george-macdonald|MacDonald]] consistently ties peace to [[presence|divine presence]] rather than changed circumstances, making it one of the diary's central paradoxes: peace is "strangely wrought" from within strife.

## Peace Born of Strife

The diary's most concentrated statement on peace appears in May:

> Out of strange strife thy peace is strangely wrought. (005-026)

The re[[prayer|petition]] of "strange" signals that the peace [[george-macdonald|MacDonald]] describes defies expectation. It is not peace *after* strife — a resolution that follows conflict — but peace that emerges *within* strife, inseparable from it. The adjective "strangely" is doubled to mark the paradox: this peace is counterintuitive, irreducible to psychological explanation.

[[george-macdonald|MacDonald]] elaborates this paradox in later months:

> Thy angels sin not and in peace adore thee;
> But I must will, or thou must do the more. (012-019)

Angels possess peace by nature — they "sin not" and therefore adore without [[strife|struggle]]. The human condition is different: peace comes only through the exercise of will, through active surrender. The alternative — "or thou must do the more" — ac[[wisdom|knowledge]]s that when human will falters, divine action compensates.

## Peace Not as Laziness

[[george-macdonald|MacDonald]] is careful to distinguish authentic peace from its counterfeits:

> He seeks thee for a little lazy peace,
> Then, like a man all weary of his life,
> Would turn and lie at ease upon the road. (008-002)

The speaker critiques those who seek [[god|God]] only for "a little lazy peace" — the desire to be comfortable rather than transformed. True peace is not the cessation of effort but the [[harvest|fruit]] of sustained engagement. The image of lying "at ease upon the road" suggests spiritual sloth disguised as rest.

## The Pace of Peace

The diary's treatment of peace includes a temporal dimension — peace arrives not on the soul's schedule but gradually:

> So slow to the peace of heart-reluctant man. (007-008)

The "heart-reluctant" — the soul that resists surrender — finds peace slow in coming. The slowness is not divine reluctance but human resistance. Peace is offered; it is received slowly because the receiving requires the letting-go that the reluctant heart refuses.

## Gospel Peace as Armour

In November, peace takes on an active, protective character:

> Who with the gospel of thy peace are girt,
> The belt from which all weapons hang. (011-010)

Peace here is martial imagery — a "belt" from which weapons hang. The "gospel of thy peace" is not a retreat from battle but the equipment for it. Those who are "girt" with peace are armed for [[strife|spiritual conflict]], not removed from it.

## Peace as Rest in God

The diary's final vision of peace is rest — not rest from work but rest within [[god|God]]:

> Then, whelmed in peace, rest like a man outworn,
> That lies in the great depth of the sky. (010-015)

The speaker envisions being "whelmed" — overwhelmed, engulfed — in peace. The rest is that of a weary labourer, not an idle lounger. It is earned rest, received as gift, experienced as immersion in a depth beyond [[self|the self]].
