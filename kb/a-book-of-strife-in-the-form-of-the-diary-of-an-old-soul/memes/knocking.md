The image of knocking in [[george-macdonald|George MacDonald]]'s "A Book of [[strife|Strife]] in the Form of The Diary of an Old [[soul|Soul]]" is drawn from Revelation 3:20 — "Behold, I stand at the door and knock" — but [[george-macdonald|MacDonald]] complicates the trope in a characteristically unsettling way. The knocker is [[christ|Christ]], the house is the soul, and the door is closed. The speaker sees himself both as the child knocking at an empty house and as the one within whose door may or may not receive a knock. The reversal is the point: who is outside, and who is inside? The answer is not as simple as the biblical text alone would suggest.

## The Child at the Door

The opening stanza of the work presents a dis[[silence|quiet]]ing image: "I see a child before an empty house, / Knocking and knocking at the closed door" (001-007). The child wakes "dull echoes" — there is no answer from within. The house is empty; no one is home. The child would knock forever, but a "mother angel" intervenes, crossing the empty floor and opening "to the obstinate praying thing." The obstinacy belongs to the child — this is prayer as refusal to stop, and it is blessed not for its quality but for its persistence.

> I see a child before an empty house, / Knocking and knocking at the closed door (001-007)

## The Mother Angel

The figure who opens the door is not the one knocked for but a "mother angel" who "soft-walking, crosses straight the empty floor" (001-007). The angel is maternal, silent, direct — she does not hesitate or negotiate. She "opens to the obstinate praying thing," and the phrase "praying thing" reduces the child to its single defining act: it prays, and that is enough. [[george-macdonald|MacDonald]]'s [[theology]] here is generous: the answer to prayer may come through unexpected intermediaries, and the empty house may be filled by divine proxies.

## The Reversed Question

Later in the work, the speaker asks: "For how believe thee somewhere in blank space, / If through the darkness come no knocking to our door?" (011-003). The roles are reversed — now the speaker is inside the house, waiting for a knock from outside. [[faith|Faith]] depends on divine initiative; if [[god|God]] does not knock, how shall the soul believe? The darkness is "blank space" — featureless, silent, offering no purchase for [[faith|trust]]. The knock, when it comes, is the sole evidence that there is someone beyond the dark.

> For how believe thee somewhere in blank space, / If through the darkness come no knocking to our door? (011-003)

## The Dialectic of the Door

Across these two passages, [[george-macdonald|MacDonald]] constructs a theology of the door that refuses simple resolution. The soul both knocks and is knocked for; the house is both empty and occupied; the initiative is both human obstinacy and divine approach. The door is the membrane between two freedoms — the soul's freedom to remain closed and [[god|God]]'s freedom to knock. That the door opens at all is grace; that it requires knocking is the condition of [[love]], which will not force entry.
