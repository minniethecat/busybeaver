Sorrow in [[george-macdonald|George MacDonald]]'s "A Book of [[strife|Strife]] in the Form of The Diary of an Old [[soul|Soul]]" (1880) is never simply a problem to be solved or a pain to be escaped. It is, rather, a mode of spiritual deepening — an experience that, rightly received, draws the soul closer to [[god|God]] rather than driving it away. [[george-macdonald|MacDonald]] treats [[grief|sorrow]] as a form of service, even a form of love, and refuses to oppose it simplistically to joy.

## Sorrow as Divine Service

The most striking claim [[george-macdonald|MacDonald]] makes about [[grief|sorrow]] is that it can become an act of wor[[vessel|ship]]:

> Or sorrow by love's service robed and crowned (011-028)

Sorrow is not cancelled by love; it is "robed and crowned" by it — dressed in dignity and elevated to honor through its participation in love's work. The image transforms [[grief|sorrow]] from a passive [[pain|suffering]] into an active vocation. To [[grief|sorrow]] *in love* — to grieve what love grieves — is to participate in [[god|God]]'s own heart.

This dignifying of [[grief|sorrow]] appears again when [[george-macdonald|MacDonald]] describes the kinds of experience that shape the human soul from the beginning:

> Of love and sorrow that were ever born (008-028)

[[love|Love]] and [[grief|sorrow]] are paired as twins — "ever born" together, a[[rise|rising]] from the same source. Every love carries within it the seed of [[grief|sorrow]], because to love is to be vulnerable to loss. [[george-macdonald|MacDonald]] does not see this as a flaw in creation but as its deep structure.

## The Shared Nature of Sorrow

[[george-macdonald|MacDonald]] resists the temptation to rank [[grief|sorrow]]s or isolate the sufferer:

> To some a bitter sorrow; to some the sting / Of love misspent (006-017)

Sorrow comes in different forms — "bitter [[grief|sorrow]]" for some, "the sting of love misspent" for others — but the experience is universal. No one escapes [[grief|sorrow]], and the particular form matters less than the response. By listing varieties of [[grief|sorrow]] alongside varieties of spiritual experience, [[george-macdonald|MacDonald]] normalizes grief as part of the human condition rather than an aberration from it.

## Sorrow and the Presence of God

Sorrow does not indicate [[god|God]]'s absence. On the contrary, [[george-macdonald|MacDonald]] suggests that the [[conscious|awareness]] of [[god|God]]'s presence transforms [[grief|sorrow]]'s meaning:

> Should I be sorrowful, father, having thee? (010-025)

The question is addressed to [[god|God]] as "father" and is genuinely open — can one who has [[god|God]] truly be [[grief|sorrow]]ful? The answer is not a simple no. [[george-macdonald|MacDonald]] ac[[wisdom|knowledge]]s that [[grief|sorrow]] persists even in the presence of [[god|God]], but that presence changes [[grief|sorrow]]'s character. Sorrow without [[god|God]] is despair; [[grief|sorrow]] with [[god|God]] is — potentially — a form of intimacy.

The speaker describes a state of suspended animation in which [[grief|sorrow]] itself seems half-conscious:

> I sit alone, a sorrow half asleep (007-001)

Sorrow is "half asleep" — not fully active, not fully gone. It is a state of waiting, of diminished [[conscious|consciousness]], in which the speaker is "alone" but not abandoned. The half-sleep of [[grief|sorrow]] is a liminal space between desolation and [[peace|rest]]oration.
