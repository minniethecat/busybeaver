# Strife

**Strife** in [[A Book of Strife in the Form of The Diary of an Old Soul]] names the spiritual struggle at the heart of the [[worship|devotion]]al life. More than the book's title suggests, strife is the condition of the soul caught between its own resistance and [[god|God]]'s transforming work — not a mark of failure but the very texture of sanctification.

## The Weariness of Strife

[[george-macdonald|MacDonald]] begins with honest acknowledgment of exhaustion:

> "For I am weary of weariness and strife." [012-001]

The poet's [[weary|weariness]] is not merely physical but spiritual — the fatigue of a soul that has long contended. This opening confession sets the tone: strife is not glamorised but named as a burden from which the soul seeks release.

## Strife as the Context of Growth

Despite its pain, strife is not meaningless. [[george-macdonald|MacDonald]] envisions it as the arena in which the soul is formed:

> "God, give me strength my evil to deride, / And conquer in the strife." [003-026]

The prayer is not for escape from strife but for [[power|strength]] within it. Strife becomes the occasion for the exercise of virtues that would otherwise remain latent.

## The Strife of Contradiction

The poet ac[[wisdom|knowledge]]s that strife includes [[mind|intellect]]ual as [[water|well]] as moral struggle:

> "I must breed contradiction, strife, and doubt." [011-025]

[[doubt|Doubt]]s and contradictions are not external to the spiritual life but arise from within it. [[george-macdonald|MacDonald]] does not pretend that faith eliminates [[mind|intellect]]ual tension; rather, faith persists through it.

## Peace Born of Strife

In one of [[george-macdonald|MacDonald]]'s most characteristic paradoxes, peace emerges not after strife but from within it:

> "Out of strange strife thy peace is strangely wrought." [005-026]

The peace that comes is "strange" — unexpected, counter-intuitive, irreducible to formula. It is not the peace of resolution but the peace of presence in the midst of unresolved tension.

## The Unyielding Soul

The poet's ultimate posture is not escape but endurance:

> "Stunned, fallen — awake, arise, and fight again." [012-031]

This is the final word on strife: not victory through [[victory|conquest]] but fidelity through persistence. The soul that will not yield the strife is the soul that remains engaged with [[god|God]].
