In [[george-macdonald|George MacDonald]]'s "A Book of [[strife|Strife]] in the Form of The Diary of an Old [[soul|Soul]]", growth is the soul's organic movement toward [[God]], not a project of self-cultivation but the natural [[harvest|fruit]] of [[faith|trust]]. Growth is both gain — "To [[faith|trust]] is gain and growth, not mere sown [[seed]]" (004-021) — and ache: the speaker feels the "aching growth at my dull-s[[water|well]]ing [[heart]]" (005-009). The poem holds these two truths together: growth brings joy, but growth also hurts. The soul s[[water|well]]s toward [[god|God]] as a seed s[[water|well]]s toward the sun, pressed from within by a life it did not originate and cannot control.

## Trust as the Engine of Growth

Growth is not achieved by effort but received through [[faith|trust]]. The speaker declares that [[faith|trust]] "is gain and growth, not mere sown seed" (004-021). [[faith|Faith]] does not merely plant a seed that may grow later — faith is itself the growing. [[trust|Trust]] heaves the world round to the [[heaven|heavenly]] [[morning|dawn]], and in that light the soul discovers its own high birth. Growth is the soul waking to what it already is in [[god|God]], not the soul becoming something new by its own [[power]].

> To trust is gain and growth, not mere sown seed! / Faith heaves the world round to the heavenly dawn, / In whose great light the soul doth spell and read / Itself high-born. (004-021)

## The Ache of Growth

Growth is not painless. The speaker longs to "spur and start / The aching growth at my dull-s[[water|well]]ing heart" (005-009). The verb is telling: growth aches because it stretches the soul beyond its present capacity. The heart s[[water|well]]s "dull" — growth is felt as a pressure, a fullness that is not yet [[joy]]. The speaker cannot hasten it, however much he longs to. He must wait "patient for all coming songs" while [[song|singing]] his "one monotonous song" — the slow, repetitive work of waiting for growth [[god|God]] alone can give.

> To quicker life I fain would spur and start / The aching growth at my dull-swelling heart. (005-009)

## The Swiftness of Growth

If growth can ache with slowness, it can also startle with speed. The poem's elegy for a young [[child]] describes a growth so swift it seems to belong to another order of time: "So swift thy growth, so true thy goalward bent, / Thou, child and sage inextricably blent" (012-030). The child's life "clear forth of heaven was sent, / Ere it broke out into a [[smile]], and went." Growth here is not slow accumulation but sudden flowering — the full realisation of a life that was always already complete in [[god|God]]. The child and sage are one because true growth is not the addition of years but the unfolding of what [[god|God]] has planted.

> So swift thy growth, so true thy goalward bent, / Thou, child and sage inextricably blent. (012-030)

## Growth Corrupted

Growth can be twisted. When spiritual hunger goes awry, "[[hunger|Hunger]] for growth turns fluctuating change" (006-022). The desire to grow, divorced from [[faith|trust]], becomes mere [[peace|rest]]lessness — a hunger that consumes rather than builds. True growth is steady and goalward; false growth is "fluctuating change," motion without direction. The difference lies in whether the soul grows toward [[god|God]] or merely away from its present self.
