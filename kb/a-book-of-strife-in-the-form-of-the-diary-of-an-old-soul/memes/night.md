## Definition

In [[george-macdonald|MacDonald]]'s *A Book of [[strife|Strife]] in the Form of The Diary of an Old [[soul|Soul]]*, "night" operates as a dual metaphor: the darkness of [[doubt|spiritual doubt]] and alienation from [[god|God]], and paradoxically, the [[peace|rest]]orative evening in which the soul [[peace|rest]]s before divine morning. Unlike the purely negative darkness of [[sin]], night in [[george-macdonald|MacDonald]] carries the promise of [[morning|dawn]] — it is a transitional state rather than a permanent condition. The speaker repeatedly finds himself in night, yet the movement of the poems is always toward morning (006-009).

## Key Passages

The speaker describes night as a veiling of divine orientation: "Until the hot night veils the farther way, / Then tired and thirsty day" (008-003). Here night obscures the pilgrim's path, intensifying [[weary|weariness]] and thirst. Yet night also brings cooling comfort — "And on my heart at night a fresh leaf cooling lay" (001-014) — suggesting natural rhythms of spiritual [[peace|rest]] within the [[strife|struggle]].

[[george-macdonald|MacDonald]]'s most sharply defined contrast appears in: "Thy night with morning, and thy clouds with lightning" (006-002). Night is not outside [[god|God]]'s domain; it is "Thy night," be[[desire|longing]] to the divine economy of transformation. The speaker's aspiration is explicitly framed as emergence: "to step forward out of the night — / To be — to share in the out[[gift|giving]] light" (006-009), where night equals non-participation in divine being.

The introspective dimension surfaces when the speaker lies [[wake|awake]]: "I lay last night and knew not why I was sad" (012-006). Night becomes the setting for unarticulated spiritual distress, the "pathless night" where the soul "wanders in a land of dreary sound" (007-012). Yet even here, the "live coal of every night" may "[[fire|flame]] out" (007-003), suggesting night contains within itself the seed of illumination.

The natural cycle of evening and morning frames the [[worship|devotion]]al rhythm: "evening fall before the [[peace|rest]]ing night" (012-003), while "the morn trampling the dull night" (002-008) affirms that night's dominance is temporary. Night "gives it cannot keep" (002-020) — its gifts are transient, its [[grief|sorrow]]s passing.

## Related Concepts

Night stands in polar relation[[vessel|ship]] with [[light]], which [[george-macdonald|MacDonald]] identifies with [[presence|divine presence]] and truth. The sequence night→morning maps onto the spiritual progression from [[darkness]] to illumination, from [[death]]-like separation to resurrected [[life]]. Night's introspective quality links it to [[sorrow]] and [[grief]], while its [[peace|rest]]orative aspect connects to [[peace]] and [[sleep]]. The speaker's cry to be wakened from night ties this concept to spiritual [[wake]]fulness. Night's temporary character reflects [[george-macdonald|MacDonald]]'s broader theology of divine transformation found in [[hope]] and [[grace]].
