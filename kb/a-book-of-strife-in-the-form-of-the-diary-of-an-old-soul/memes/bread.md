Bread in [[george-macdonald|George MacDonald]]'s "A Book of [[strife|Strife]] in the Form of The Diary of an Old [[soul|Soul]]" is the daily gift of divine [[life]] — sustenance that comes not from human labour but directly from the hand of [[God]]. The speaker insists that his bread "comes not from any human tilth" (001-017); it is the "in[[tent|dwelling]] [[wine]]" that flows into every clod where [[god|God]]'s foot has pressed. Yet bread carries a warning: the holiest bread, if hoarded, breeds the "mammon-moth, the [[pride|having-pride]]" (008-007). [[spirit|Spirit]]ual nourishment must be received fresh each moment or it corrupts into spiritual possessiveness.

## Bread Not From Human Tilth

The speaker's radical claim is that his very sustenance has no human source. "My bread comes not from any human tilth" (001-017) — a statement that refuses to locate the origin of life in creaturely provision. [[god|God]] himself is "my life, my health, my bank, my barn." This is not a denial of ordinary means but an insistence that behind every means stands the unmediated gift of the maker. The bread the speaker eats is always more than bread — it is the body of [[God]] broken into the world.

> My bread comes not from any human tilth; / No wings will grow upon my changeless wealth; / Wrong cannot touch it, violence or deceit. (001-017)

## The Indwelling Bread

The speaker greets his daily bread with keen-eyed recognition: in it he finds "its being's heart, the very body of [[God]]" (002-007). Bread is not merely instrumental — fuel for the spiritual life — but sacramental. The [[presence|divine presence]] ind[[water|well]]s the ordinary loaf. To eat is to commune; to break bread is to recognise the one who breaks it. The [[Emmaus]] echo is deliberate: the disciples knew him in the breaking of bread, and the speaker greets the same presence in his daily meal.

> And in my daily bread, keen-eyed I greet / Its being's heart, the very body of God. (002-007)

## The Danger of Hoarded Bread

[[spirit|Spirit]]ual sustenance cannot be stored. "The holiest bread, if hoarded, soon will breed / The mammon-moth, the [[pride|having-pride]]" (008-007). What [[god|God]] gives for the present moment cannot be accumulated without corruption. The warning applies to spiritual experience itself — yesterday's [[grace]] cannot feed today's [[soul]]. The [[pride|having-pride]] is the subtle temptation to turn gift into possession, moment into treasury. [[god|God]] gives bread "only for the present spiritual need" (008-007), and the soul that tries to live on stored bread will find only moths.

> The holiest bread, if hoarded, soon will breed / The mammon-moth, the having-pride, I find. (008-007)

## The Hair's-Breadth of Separation

The speaker cries from the dark to be kept with [[god|God]], acknowledging that "a mere hair's-breadth" of separation from the vine is "eternal, infinite [[death]]" (008-004). The branch that separates from the stem loses the sap — the bread of life. The hair's-breadth is the distance between receiving bread as gift and seizing bread as possession. To be kept "with thee" is to remain in the posture of daily, hourly reception.
