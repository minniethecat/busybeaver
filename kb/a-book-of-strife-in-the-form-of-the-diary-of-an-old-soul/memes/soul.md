# Soul

The **soul** is the central subject of [[A Book of Strife in the Form of The Diary of an Old Soul]] — the "old soul" of the title itself. [[george-macdonald|George MacDonald]] traces the soul's journey from estrangement through purification to union with [[god]], presenting the soul as the arena in which the drama of divine love unfolds.

## The Old Soul

The title of the work identifies the speaker as "an old soul" — not merely aged but worn by the long [[strife|struggle]] toward [[god|God]]. [[george-macdonald|MacDonald]] opens with an invocation that names the path:

> "Lead back thy old soul, by the path of pain." [001-003]

The soul is "old" in its [[weary|weariness]], its accumulated experience of striving and falling short. Yet this oldness is not hopeless; it is the condition that makes the soul ready for [[god|God]]'s leading.

## The Soul's Poverty and Potential

[[george-macdonald|MacDonald]] is candid about the soul's impoverished state apart from [[god|God]]:

> "My soul is a poor land, plenteous in dearth." [002-001]

The paradox "plenteous in [[emptiness|dearth]]" captures the soul's condition — it has an abundance of need. Yet this poverty is the preface to enrichment, not a final verdict.

## The Soul's Restlessness

The soul that has known peace with [[god|God]] cannot find [[peace|rest]] elsewhere:

> "My soul that was at rest now resteth not." [001-005]

This [[peace|rest]]lessness is not a curse but a sign — the soul's memory of its [[home|true home]] prevents it from settling for anything less.

## The Soul Transformed

The goal of the soul's journey is not escape from itself but transformation into a [[clay|vessel]] fit for divine life:

> "Open my soul-gates to thy living flood." [012-003]

The image of "soul-[[door|gate]]s" opening to a "living flood" suggests both receptivity and magnitude — the soul is not diminished but expanded to receive the fullness of [[god|God]].

## The Soul's Humble Obedience

Before the soul can be exalted, it must learn [[humble|humility]]:

> "Before his child's obedient, humble soul." [004-005]

The soul's true greatness lies not in its achievements but in its posture of childlike obe[[death|die]]nce before the Father.
