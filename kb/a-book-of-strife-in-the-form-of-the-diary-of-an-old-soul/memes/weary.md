## Definition

[[george-macdonald|MacDonald]] treats *weariness* not as a moral failure but as an honest condition of the spiritual life. The speaker repeatedly confesses, "I am a little weary of my life," and the admission is never met with rebuke. Weariness can be a temptation to despair, but it can also be the exhaustion that drives the soul back to its source: "Then to thy knee [[trust|trusting]] I turn, and lean." [[george-macdonald|MacDonald]] distinguishes between weariness of the false self (which is salutary) and weariness of the divine life (which requires renewal). The weary state, honestly named, becomes a [[door|threshold]] of [[faith|trust]].

## Key Passages

> "I AM a little weary of my life — / Not thy life, blessed Father! Or the blood / Too slowly laves the coral shores of thought, / Or I am weary of weariness and strife." (012-001)

The opening of December's sequence: the speaker clarifies it is his own life that wearies him, not the life [[god|God]] gives.

> "More life I need ere I myself can be. / Sometimes, when the eternal tide ebbs low, / A moment weary of my life I grow — / Weary of my existence' self, I mean." (008-017)

Weariness of "my existence' self" is distinguished from weariness of life itself — the false self exhausts; the true self is sustained.

> "This weariness of mine, may it not come / From something that doth need no setting right? / Shall fruit be blamed if it hang wearily / A day before it perfected drop plumb?" (012-003)

Weariness may simply be the weight of ripeness — a natural stage before fulfilment, not a fault.

> "Cold, weary, joyless, waste of air and sun — / Let me not glow when earth-born vapours roll / Across the infinite zenith of my soul." (001-022)

The speaker asks [[god|God]] to guard his inner weather against the gloom of weariness.

> "When I am very weary with hard thought, / And yet the question burns and is not quenched, / My heart grows cool when to remembrance wrought / That thou who know'st the light-born answer sought / Know'st too the dark where the doubt lies entrenched." (006-014)

Intellectual weariness finds relief not in answers but in [[faith|trust]] that [[god|God]] knows both the question and the darkness surrounding it.

## Related Concepts

Weariness is the shadow-side of [[strife]] — the exhaustion that follows [[strife|struggle]]. It stands in contrast to [[rest]] (a term [[george-macdonald|MacDonald]] does not directly name but implies through [[faith|trust]]) and drives the soul toward [[trust]] and [[obedience]]. The weariness of the [[self]] is distinguished from the vitality of [[life]] in [[god|God]]. Its remedy is not escape but turning toward [[god]], as expressed in [[prayer]].
