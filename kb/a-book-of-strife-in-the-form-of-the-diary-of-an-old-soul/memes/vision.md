In [[george-macdonald|George MacDonald]]'s "A Book of [[strife|Strife]] in the Form of The Diary of an Old [[soul|Soul]]", vision is the soul's capacity to perceive divine reality — not with physical eyes but with the whole person oriented toward [[god|God]]. Vision is both gift and task: [[god|God]] grants the capacity to see, but the soul must not make that "gift of vision [[emptiness|void]] and null" (004-019) through disobe[[death|die]]nce. [[george-macdonald|MacDonald]] treats spiritual sight as something that can be sharpened or dulled, amplified or lost, depending on whether the will aligns with the light that makes seeing possible.

## The Gift of Vision

Vision originates in [[god|God]] and is a gift of divine love. The speaker prays not for new eyes but for the grace to use the vision already given: "Help me this day to behold thee everywhere" (004-029). Vision is not esoteric or reserved for mystics; it is the ordinary capacity of the soul to recognise [[god|God]]'s presence in all things. The gift is universal — what is rare is the soul's willingness to exercise it. When the speaker confesses making [[god|God]]'s "gift of vision [[emptiness|void]] and null" (004-019), the fault lies not in weak eyes but in a diverted will.

> Help me this day to behold thee everywhere. The vision rises of a speechless thing. (004-029)

## Vision and Obedience

The sharpest warning about vision in the poem is that it can be lost through disobe[[death|die]]nce. To make the "gift of vision [[emptiness|void]] and null" (004-019) is to receive the capacity for spiritual sight and then, through neglect or rebellion, render it useless. Vision is tied to the ethical life: the soul that refuses to act on what it sees will eventually cease to see. The clear-eyed perception of [[god|God]] depends on the will's alignment — "doing the truth" precedes "seeing the truth" in [[george-macdonald|MacDonald]]'s spiritual logic.

> Making his gift of vision void and null. (004-019)

## The Beatific Vision

The ultimate object of spiritual sight is [[god|God]] himself. In one of the poem's most expansive passages, the speaker imagines the soul "in the vision mighty grown, Its father and its feast" (010-004), suggesting that the beatific vision is not a passive beholding but a transformative encounter in which the soul grows into the likeness of what it sees. Vision of [[god|God]] is the soul's true nourishment — "its father and its feast" — indicating that sight of the divine is both origin and sustenance.

> My soul then, in the vision mighty grown, Its father and its feast. (010-004)

## Sparks of Blissful Vision

Even in the midst of spiritual [[strife|struggle]], the speaker receives moments of illuminating sight: "spark of [[joy|bliss]]ful vision on my pathway rough" (006-008). These glimpses are not the full beatific vision but foretastes — momentary illuminations that sustain the soul on its difficult journey. The image of a "spark" suggests both brevity and intensity: a flash of divine light that, though transient, is enough to reorient the pilgrim and confirm that the path, however rough, leads toward the light.

> Spark of blissful vision on my pathway rough. (006-008)

## Vision as the Meeting of Wills

The deepest [[wisdom|insight]] [[george-macdonald|MacDonald]] offers about vision is that it is not a separate faculty but the form that union with [[god|God]] takes at the level of knowing. Between the soul and [[god|God]] "there's no division, Except the meeting of thy will and mine" (004-025). Vision is this meeting — the point where [[god|God]]'s self-disclosure and the soul's receptive attention converge. It is not that [[god|God]] is distant and vision bridges the gap; rather, there is no gap, and vision is simply the soul's [[wake|awake]]ned [[conscious|awareness]] of the presence that has always been there.

> 'Twixt thee and me there's no division, Except the meeting of thy will and mine. (004-025)
