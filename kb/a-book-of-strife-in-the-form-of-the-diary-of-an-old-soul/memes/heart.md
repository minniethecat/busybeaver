The heart is the master metaphor of spiritual anatomy in [[george-macdonald|George MacDonald]]'s "A Book of [[strife|Strife]] in the Form of The Diary of an Old [[soul|Soul]]" (1880). With over 125 appearances across the 366 stanzas, "heart" serves as the interior landscape where the drama of salvation unfolds. In [[george-macdonald|MacDonald]]'s poetic theology, the heart is not the seat of emotion in opposition to reason, but the unified center of the person — the place where [[love]] is received, where transformation occurs, and where the [[lord|Lord]] meets the soul. As the speaker declares in the opening of June: "From heart to heart it sinks, it steals, it flows" (006-001).

## Biblical and Theological Background

[[george-macdonald|MacDonald]]'s use of "heart" draws deeply on the Hebrew concept of *leb* (לב) and the Greek *kardia* (καρδία), both denoting the inner person in its totality — [[mind|intellect]], will, and affections united. This stands in marked contrast to the modern tendency to oppose "head" and "heart." For [[george-macdonald|MacDonald]], following the biblical tradition, the heart *thinks*: "My heart grows cool when to remembrance wrought / That thou who knowest all things knowest this" (006-014). The heart is capable of [[wisdom|knowledge]], memory, and judgment — it is the whole self turned toward or away from [[god|God]].

The heart's primacy also reflects [[george-macdonald|MacDonald]]'s theological anthropology. [[human|Human]] beings are not [[mind|intellect]]s housed in bo[[death|die]]s; they are hearts — desiring, willing centers — that think. The heart's desires determine the direction of [[mind|thought]], not the reverse.

## The Heart as Receiver

The heart's first and most fundamental role is receptivity. [[divine|Divine]] [[love]] enters the human person through the heart, working from within like medicine:

> From thine, as then, the healing virtue goes / Into our hearts--that is the Father's plan. / From heart to heart it sinks, it steals, it flows, / From these hearts on, into the wide world's van (006-001)

This passage establishes a chain of transmission: the [[father|Father]] → individual hearts → the wider world. The heart is a conduit, not a terminus. [[love|Love]] received must flow onward. [[george-macdonald|MacDonald]] envisions a contagion of health spreading from heart to heart.

The heart's receptivity, however, can be blocked. The speaker repeatedly ac[[wisdom|knowledge]]s the heart's resistance — its "indocile" (007-011) quality, its tendency to grow "cool" (006-014), its capacity to be "sinking" (007-014). The spiritual life is a continual re-opening of the heart to divine influence.

## The Heart's Afflictions

[[george-macdonald|MacDonald]] is strikingly honest about the heart's difficulties. The diary form permits him to catalogue the heart's troubles with clinical precision:

> To some sore pain, to others a sinking heart; / To some a weariness worse than any smart; / To some a haunting, fearing, borrowing lie (006-016)

The heart suffers — this is a given. But [[pain|suffering]] is not meaningless; it is pedagogical. The "sinking heart" and the "[[weary|weariness]]" are themselves divine helps, opening the heart to receive what it would otherwise resist. [[pain|Pain]] is the heart's [[master|teacher]].

[[george-macdonald|MacDonald]] does not counsel stoic suppression of the heart's troubles. Instead, he models continual [[prayer|petition]]: "Help me, my Father, in whatever dismay, / Whatever terror in whatever shape, / To hold the faster by thy garment's hem; / When my heart sinks, oh, lift it up to thee!" (007-014). The sinking heart is not a failure of faith but an occasion for deeper dependence.

## The Heart and the Will

The heart in [[george-macdonald|MacDonald]] is inseparably linked to the will. The speaker prays:

> Make me a fellow worker with thee, Christ; / Nought else befits a God-born energy; / Of all that's lovely, only lives the highest, / Lifting the rest that it shall rise with it (006-005)

The heart is "[[god|God]]-born energy" — not passive but active. It lifts what it loves. [[george-macdonald|MacDonald]]'s repeated insistence that the will must be conformed to [[god|God]]'s will ([[obedience]]) is, at root, a matter of the heart. One cannot will rightly with a misdirected heart.

## The Heart Transformed

The arc of the diary traces the heart's gradual purification. The heart begins resistant, "indocile" (007-011), but through the repeated application of divine love — through "small successes, disappointments small" (007-011) — it is slowly remade. The end toward which the heart tends is union: a heart so conformed to [[god|God]]'s heart that the two beat as one.
