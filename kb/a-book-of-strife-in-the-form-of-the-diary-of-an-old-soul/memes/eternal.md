# Eternal

The **eternal** in [[A Book of Strife in the Form of The Diary of an Old Soul]] is not mere endless duration but a different quality of existence altogether — the life of [[god]] into which the [[soul]] is progressively drawn. [[george-macdonald|George MacDonald]] follows the classical theological distinction between time as the mode of creaturely existence and eternity as the mode of divine being, while [[gift|giving]] it experiential urgency.

## The Eternal Morning

[[george-macdonald|MacDonald]] images eternity as [[morning|dawn]] — not a departure from the familiar world but its transfiguration into something more real:

> "And, open to the eternal morning's might, / Each human face will shine my window for thy [[light]]." (004-012)

The "eternal morning" does not replace the human face but causes it to "shine." Eternity illuminates rather than abolishes the temporal. Each person becomes a "window" through which divine light enters — a striking image of the communion of saints as the architecture of eternal vision.

## The Eternal Tide

The poet experiences eternity not as a steady state but as a rhythm of presence and withdrawal:

> "Sometimes, when the eternal tide ebbs low, / A moment weary of what I can be." (008-017)

The "eternal tide" metaphor captures the experiential reality of spiritual life: the soul does not d[[water|well]] continuously in the sense of [[god]]'s presence. The ebb is as much a part of eternity's pedagogy as the flow, teaching the soul to [[trust]] what it cannot always feel.

## Heir of Earth and Heaven

[[george-macdonald|MacDonald]] boldly claims the [[child]] of [[god|God]] as inheritor of both realms:

> "But heir of earth and heaven, eternal child; / Daring all [[truth]], nor fearing to be wrong." (002-022)

The "eternal child" is not naive but daring — the confidence of inheritance enables [[mind|intellect]]ual courage. Because the child belongs to eternity, she can risk error in the pursuit of truth, knowing that her identity is secured by relation[[vessel|ship]] rather than correctness.

## Consciousness Eternal Wakes

The poet looks toward a final [[wake|awakening]] into eternal [[conscious|consciousness]]:

> "Not I until my consciousness eternal wakes." (012-028)

This is [[george-macdonald|MacDonald]]'s version of the [[rise|resurrection]] hope: the true self has not yet fully arrived. Present [[conscious|consciousness]] is a foretaste, a preliminary sketch. The "eternal" waking is the moment when the soul comes into full [[knowledge]] of itself in [[god|God]].
