Freedom in [[george-macdonald|George MacDonald]]'s "A Book of [[strife|Strife]] in the Form of The Diary of an Old [[soul|Soul]]" is not the power to choose arbitrarily but the capacity to become what one was designed to be. [[george-macdonald|MacDonald]] inverts the modern equation of freedom with autonomy: true freedom is likeness to [[god|God]], and the soul that insists on its own way is not free but enslaved. This theology emerges across three concentrated stanzas that trace an arc from divine constraint through human aspiration to the ultimate impossibility of imagining freedom apart from [[love|divine love]].

## Divine Freedom Hampered by Human Will

[[george-macdonald|MacDonald]] opens with a startling claim: [[god|God]]'s freedom is constrained by human unfreedom. The divine working is "hampered" not by any external power but by the very creatures designed for liberty:

> Thou art hampered in thy natural working then / When beings designed on freedom's holy plan / Will not be free: with thy poor, foolish men, / Thou therefore hast to work just like a man. (007-005)

The "holy plan" of freedom is the original design; human refusal to be free forces [[god|God]] into a slow, incarnational mode of working — "just like a man." The stanza does not resolve into despair but into a promise: [[god|God]] tangles himself in human need until he has "to freedom fashioned them indeed," at which point divine action becomes "grandly move, and [[god|God]]like speed." Freedom is not a starting-point but a destination — something [[god|God]] must *fashion* in the soul through long entanglement.

## The Upward Traction

The second freedom stanza, from May, describes an experience of being pulled upward by something beyond [[self|the self]]:

> Ever above my coldness and my doubt / Rises up something, reaching forth a hand: / This thing I know, but cannot understand. / Is it the God in me that rises out / Beyond my self, trailing it up with him, / Towards the spirit-home, the freedom-land, / Beyond my conscious ken, my near horizon's brim? (005-023)

The experience is paradoxical: something within the speaker ("the [[god|God]] in me") is simultaneously beyond the speaker ("rises out beyond my self"). This in[[tent|dwelling]] transcendence exerts an upward traction — "trailing it up with him" — toward a "freedom-land" that lies past the horizon of [[conscious|consciousness]]. Freedom is not chosen but *drawn toward*; the speaker is passive before this [[rise|rising]], not its initiator. The [[will]] participates by consent, not by origination.

## The Unimaginable Freedom

The final passage from May is [[george-macdonald|MacDonald]]'s most compressed statement on freedom. It is not something [[self|the self]] can picture because it requires a self the speaker does not yet possess:

> For, that great freedom how should such as I / Be able to imagine in such a self? / Less hopeless far the miser man might try / To image the delight of friend-shared pelf. / Freedom is to be like thee, face and heart; / To know it, Lord, I must be as thou art, / I cannot breed the imagination high. (005-028)

"Freedom is to be like thee, face and heart" — this is the theological centre of the entire sequence. Freedom is not the absence of constraint but the presence of divine likeness. The [[soul]] cannot imagine freedom because imagination itself is constrained by [[self|the self]] that does the imagining. The miser cannot picture the joy of sharing; the unfree self cannot picture the condition of liberty. The conclusion is epistemological [[humble|humility]]: "I cannot breed the imagination high." Freedom must be received, not conceived.
