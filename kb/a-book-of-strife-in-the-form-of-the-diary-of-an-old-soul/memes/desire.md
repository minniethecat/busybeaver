Desire in [[george-macdonald|George MacDonald]]'s "A Book of [[strife|Strife]] in the Form of The Diary of an Old [[soul|Soul]]" (1880) is the soul's native motion toward [[god|God]] — not a temptation to be suppressed but the gravitational pull of the divine upon the [[human|human heart]]. Unlike traditions that treat desire as a symptom of fallenness, [[george-macdonald|MacDonald]] presents it as the fundamental orientation of the creature toward the Creator, a gift that keeps the soul from spiritual inertia.

## Desire as Awakening

The poem opens with desire portrayed as one of the primordial forces that rouse the soul from [[sleep|slumber]]:

> Make me awake hope, fear, boundless desire. (001-010)

Desire is listed alongside hope and fear — it belongs to the triad of [[wake|awakening]] affects. The modifier "[[infinite|boundless]]" is deliberate: desire that is bounded sets its own limit and cannot carry the soul to [[god|God]]. Only [[infinite|boundless]] desire can correspond to a [[infinite|boundless]] [[god|God]].

## Desire and Divine Purpose

[[george-macdonald|MacDonald]] traces desire to its divine origin in the act of creation. [[god|God]]'s own desire is the pattern for human desire:

> Led by the lamp of my desire and woe. / If thee, my Lord, I may, I desire more. (003-030)

The parallelism is striking: desire and woe are paired as the two lamps by which the soul finds its way. Desire illuminates; it does not blind. And its proper direction is intensification — "I desire more" — not satisfaction that extinguishes the appetite but fulfilment that deepens it.

In October, [[george-macdonald|MacDonald]] makes the divine origin of desire explicit:

> More than thou didst think when thy desire designed me. (010-013)

[[god|God]]'s desiring precedes and produces the human desirer. [[creation|Creation]] is an act of divine desire, and human desire is its echo. The creature who desires is the image of the Creator who desired him into being.

## Desire Purified

Desire in its raw form is not yet holy — it must be trained and oriented. [[george-macdonald|MacDonald]] ac[[wisdom|knowledge]]s the ambiguity:

> Rousing in me this vague desire of good. / Thou art more to me than I could, / Even if I knew creation's ends divine (008-014)

The desire of good is "vague" — unfocused, pre-conceptual. It knows its direction but not its object. The speaker's confession "thou art more to me than I could" ac[[wisdom|knowledge]]s that the [[god|God]] who [[wake|awake]]ns desire exceeds the desirer's capacity to comprehend. Desire always reaches beyond [[wisdom|understanding]].

## Desire Without Return

The pu[[peace|rest]] desire, for [[george-macdonald|MacDonald]], is that which seeks no recompense:

> The primal love, that no return desires. (011-008)

This is desire perfected — a love so complete that it gives without needing reciprocation. It is the character of divine love, and the telos toward which human desire is being shaped. [[george-macdonald|MacDonald]] does not expect his speaker to have arrived here; he holds it as the standard against which present desire is measured.
