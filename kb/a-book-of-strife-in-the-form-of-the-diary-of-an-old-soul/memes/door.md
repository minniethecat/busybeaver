In [[george-macdonald|George MacDonald]]'s "A Book of [[strife|Strife]] in the Form of The Diary of an Old [[soul|Soul]]", the door is a recurring image for the boundary between the human soul and [[god|God]] — a threshold that the speaker alternately keeps shut and throws open. The door appears in three primary modes: the closed door of human resistance, the door that [[god|God]] knocks upon, and the door of death through which the soul passes into full [[presence|divine presence]]. Each mode belongs to a single spiritual dynamic: the door is never locked from [[god|God]]'s side; it is always the human will that determines whether it stands open or shut.

## The Closed Door

The speaker repeatedly confesses that he has kept the door closed against [[god|God]], not through outright refusal but through sluggishness, distraction, and self-absorption. In the opening days of the year, he hears [[god|God]] "knocking at the closed door" (001-007), waking "dull echoes" but receiving no answer. The closed door is not a mark of [[god|God]]'s absence but of the soul's inattention: the speaker looks from his window at the visitor but does not rise to open (001-027). This image of the unopened door carries the weight of spiritual negligence — the soul knows [[god|God]] is near and yet remains passive.

> He came, and knocked at my thin door. I from my window looked: the door remained unopened. (001-027)

## The Door Set Wide

The resolution to the closed door is not gradual opening but a decisive, childlike fling: "The child throws wide the door, back to the wall, I run to thee" (012-015). The door thrown "back to the wall" is an image of complete welcome — no partial opening, no cautious peering out, but total exposure and invitation. The speaker prays explicitly for this posture: "Set my door open wide, back to the wall" (009-018). To set the door wide is to abandon the illusion of spiritual self-sufficiency and to receive [[god|God]] without reservation.

> The child throws wide the door, back to the wall, I run to thee. (012-015)

## The Door of Death

[[death|Death]] itself is figured as a door — not a wall or an ending but a passage. When the speaker contemplates his [[death|mortality]], he [[faith|trust]]s that [[god|God]] will not send a proxy but will himself come: "When [[death|Death]] comes in earnest to my door, Thou wouldst thyself go" (001-028). The door of death is the final threshold, and the speaker's confidence is that the one who knocks at the soul's door throughout life will personally escort the soul through death's door. The two doors — the heart's door in life and death's door at the end — are governed by the same [[presence|divine presence]].

> When Death comes in earnest to my door, Thou wouldst thyself go, where thou hast gone before. (001-028)

## The Door of Deeds

[[george-macdonald|MacDonald]] draws a sharp distinction between feeling and acting. Doors are not opened by sentiment but by obe[[death|die]]nce: "Thy doors are deeds; the handles are their doing" (005-016). The door to [[god|God]] is not a matter of emotional warmth or religious sensation — these can "run out of doors" or "stand apart" (005-017). Instead, access to the divine life is through concrete action, through the will's choice to [[obedience|obey]] even when feeling fails.

> Thy doors are deeds; the handles are their doing. (005-016)

## Doors Opened by Love

A single loving word has power to open doors that seemed permanently shut: "A loving word may set some door ajar / Where seemed no door" (005-025). This is the communal dimension of the door image — the speaker is not alone in his threshold-keeping. Others' love can create openings where the soul saw only a blank wall. The image reverses the expected direction: doors are not only for [[god|God]]'s entrance but for love's circulation among human souls.

> A loving word may set some door ajar where seemed no door. (005-025)
