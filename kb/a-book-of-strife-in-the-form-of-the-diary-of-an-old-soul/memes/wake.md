## Definition

"Wake" in [[george-macdonald|MacDonald]]'s [[worship|devotion]]al vocabulary is the verb of spiritual [[rise|resurrection]] — the soul's repeated [[rise|rising]] from the [[sleep]] of self-absorption, sin, and forgetfulness of [[god|God]]. Unlike a single conversion event, waking is iterative: "Must wake his soul unnumbered times a day" (005-021). This concept is the active counterpart to the static condition of being spiritually alive. Where [[life]] describes the state of union with [[god|God]], waking describes the motion of entering that state, again and again, from the gravitational pull of spiritual lethargy.

## Key Passages

The speaker opens the sequence with the confession: "Sometimes I wake, and, lo! I have forgot" (001-003). The waking is momentary and fragile — clarity of divine [[conscious|awareness]] slips away. Yet waking brings integration of [[self|the self]]: "When I awake, my daughter and my son, / Grow to my side" (001-004), suggesting that spiritual alertness [[peace|rest]]ores right relation[[vessel|ship]] not only with [[god|God]] but with others.

[[george-macdonald|MacDonald]]'s most theologically concentrated statement on waking comes in the fifth chapter: "Must wake his soul unnumbered times a day" (005-021). This is not moral [[perfect|perfection]]ism but spiritual realism — the soul constantly drifts toward un[[conscious|consciousness]] of [[god|God]] and must be continually roused. The speaker prays for divine intervention in this process: "[[christ|Lord]], wake me oftener, lest I sin" (002-015), acknowledging that he cannot sustain wakefulness by his own effort.

The eschatological dimension appears in: "I shall awake, / And know my [[god|God]], at one with him" (005-010) and "until my [[conscious|consciousness]] eternal wakes" (012-028). Here waking culminates in permanent divine [[conscious|consciousness]] beyond the cycle of sleep and rousing. The speaker's present state is contrasted: "I am waked enough to feel a woe, / To rise and sink" (011-005) — partial waking that registers pain but cannot sustain elevation.

The martial metaphor surfaces in the final chapter: "Stunned, fallen-awake, arise, and fight again" (012-031). Waking is not peaceful emergence but combat readiness — the soul, knocked down, regains [[conscious|consciousness]] only to re-enter the [[strife]].

## Related Concepts

Waking is the active verb paired with the static noun [[life]] and opposed to [[sleep]] and the illusory state of [[dream]]. The need for repeated waking connects to [[sin]] as a recurring gravitational pull, and to [[grace]] as the divine rousing power. The speaker's prayer for waking links to [[prayer]] itself as a cry for alertness. Waking's destination — full divine [[conscious|consciousness]] — connects to [[heaven]], [[light]], and [[truth]]. The [[strife|struggle]] to remain awake ties this concept to [[strife]] and [[will]].
