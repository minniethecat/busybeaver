In [[george-macdonald|George MacDonald]]'s "A Book of [[strife|Strife]] in the Form of The Diary of an Old [[soul|Soul]]", [[peace|rest]] is not laziness or the mere absence of effort but the soul's anchorage in [[god|God]]. The speaker oscillates between [[peace|rest]] and [[unrest]], dis[[garment|covering]] that [[peace|rest]] is lost the moment he drifts from [[presence|divine presence]] and regained only in the clasp of [[trust]]. True [[peace|rest]] is not opposed to work — it is the [[earth|ground]] of all [[harvest|fruit]]ful action, the "sweetest [[peace|rest]]" that comes when work and wor[[vessel|ship]] are one.

## Rest in God

The speaker locates [[peace|rest]] not in circumstances but in the person of [[god|God]]: "In thee I [[peace|rest]]; in sleep thou dost me fold" (008-006). This [[peace|rest]] is ontological rather than circumstantial — it depends on whose presence the soul inhabits, not on what the soul experiences. The same stanza declares that the speaker's very life depends not "on the clasp of [[conscious|consciousness]] — on thee." Rest is the natural state of a soul oriented toward its maker.

> In thee I rest; in sleep thou dost me fold; / In thee I labour; still in thee, grow old; / And dying, shall I not in thee, my Life, be bold? (008-006)

## The Loss of Rest

Rest is fragile because it depends on relational proximity. The speaker wakes to find himself adrift: "My soul that was at [[peace|rest]] now [[peace|rest]]eth not, / For I am with myself and not with thee" (001-003). The loss of [[peace|rest]] is the birth of self-[[conscious|consciousness]] without [[god|God]]-[[conscious|consciousness]]. [[self|Self]]-absorption is the enemy of [[peace|rest]] — when the soul gazes inward rather than upward, peace evaporates.

> Sometimes I wake, and, lo! I have forgot, / And drifted out upon an ebbing sea! / My soul that was at rest now resteth not. (001-003)

## Unrest as Divine Surgery

Restlessness is not always an enemy. The speaker ac[[wisdom|knowledge]]s that [[god|God]] gives "a deep un[[peace|rest]] — a scorn / Of all they are or seem" (006-017) to some souls. This holy un[[peace|rest]] is the [[gift]] of divine dissatisfaction, a spur that drives the soul away from complacency toward its [[home|true home]]. The "rasp of un[[peace|rest]]'s file" (008-002) is painful but purgative — it shapes the soul for a deeper [[peace|rest]] than it could otherwise know.

> Till comes again the rasp of unrest's file, / From liberty is distant many a mile. (008-002)

## Rest as the End of Strife

The poem's movement is toward a [[peace|rest]] that is not passivity but final harmony: "work at one with sweetest [[peace|rest]]" (012-011). In the eschatological vision, the division between labour and repose collapses because all action flows from and returns to [[god|God]]. The soul no longer needs to alternate between striving and collapsing — it [[peace|rest]]s even as it moves, because its movement is [[god|God]]'s movement within it.
