Prayer in [[george-macdonald|George MacDonald]]'s "A Book of [[strife|Strife]] in the Form of The Diary of an Old [[soul|Soul]]" (1880) is not a discrete religious practice but the very medium of the poem itself. Every stanza is addressed to [[god|God]] — the entire *Diary* is a sustained act of prayer. [[george-macdonald|MacDonald]] treats prayer as the soul's native [[word|language]], the form of [[word|speech]] appropriate to a creature who lives in constant [[conscious|awareness]] of the Creator's presence.

## Prayer as the Poem's Form

The *Diary* is, from first line to last, prayer. [[george-macdonald|MacDonald]] opens not with an invocation of the muse but with direct address:

> Keep me beyond all prayers: / For more than all my prayers, yea, / Thou art Lord. (003-008)

This is a striking paradox — a prayer to be kept "beyond" prayer. The speaker asks not for prayer to be answered but to be transcended, to arrive at a relation[[vessel|ship]] so immediate that petition becomes obsolete. [[god|God]] is "more than all my prayers" — the reality exceeds the request.

## Prayer as Inadequate

[[george-macdonald|MacDonald]] is acutely aware of prayer's limitations. [[human|Human]] [[word|speech]] before [[god|God]] is always partial, always failing:

> A something deepest prayer will not express: / Lord, with thy heart, not mine, my heart impress. (011-008)

Prayer reaches its limit at the "something deepest" that [[word|language]] cannot carry. The speaker's response is not to abandon prayer but to redirect it — asking [[god|God]] to work directly on the heart rather than through the medium of words. This is prayer confessing its own insufficiency and asking to be superseded.

The image of the "prayer-bird" recurs across the poem, figuring prayer as a fragile creature that can fall, grow cold, or fail to fly:

> Yet some poor half-fledged prayer-bird from the nest / May fall, and its young life with flutterings die. (001-014)

> My prayer-bird was cold — would not away. (011-014)

The prayer-bird is "half-fledged" — immature, not yet capable of sustained flight. It "would not away" — the will to pray can fail even when the need is greatest. [[george-macdonald|MacDonald]]'s honesty about prayer's difficulty is theologically significant: he refuses to pretend that prayer comes easily or naturally to the fallen soul.

## Prayer and Divine Initiative

Crucially, [[george-macdonald|MacDonald]] reverses the assumed causal direction of prayer. The impulse to pray comes from [[god|God]], not [[self|the self]]:

> Thou who inspirest prayer, then bend'st thine ear; / It, sent by thee, comes back to thee (008-015)

Prayer is a circuit: inspired by [[god|God]], sent upward by the soul, received by [[god|God]]. The human contribution is real but secondary. [[god|God]] is both origin and destination of the prayer. This means prayer is not an achievement but a participation — the soul joining a conversation already in progress.

## Prayer as the Last Earthly Act

In the poem's eschatological imagination, prayer is the final thing the speaker will do before union with [[god|God]]:

> Last earthly prayer with which to thee I cling — / That thou wouldst take me out into thy air. (001-018)

Prayer is the act of "clinging" — not a dignified posture but desperate attachment. The speaker does not present himself as a confident petitioner but as a dependent child. The content of this last prayer is simple: to be taken into [[god|God]]'s "air," to [[wind|breath]]e the atmosphere of [[presence|divine presence]].

## Prayer as Self-Disclosure

[[george-macdonald|MacDonald]] recognises that prayer reveals the one who prays more than it moves the one to whom it is addressed:

> My prayers, my God, flow from what I am (005-026)

Prayer is self-expression before it is petition. What flows in prayer is the substance of [[self|the self]] — its condition, its orientation, its loves. This is why [[george-macdonald|MacDonald]] can treat the whole *Diary* as prayer: every stanza reveals the speaker's soul, and self-revelation before [[god|God]] is the essence of prayer.
