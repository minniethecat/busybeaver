# World

The **world** in [[A Book of Strife in the Form of The Diary of an Old Soul]] carries a deliberate double meaning: it is [[god]]'s creation and therefore good, yet it is also the sphere of resistance to [[god|God]] — "the world" in the Johannine sense. [[george-macdonald|George MacDonald]] holds these two meanings in tension, refusing both world-hatred and world-wor[[vessel|ship]].

## Not of This World

[[george-macdonald|MacDonald]] ac[[wisdom|knowledge]]s the soul's fundamental alienation from the world as a closed system of meaning:

> "Not of this world, this world my life doth hem; / What is this world? My [[soul]] is not of it." (012-004)

The re[[prayer|petition]] of "this world" underscores the distance between the soul's true nature and its present environment. Yet the question "What is this world?" is genuine inquiry, not rhetorical dismissal — [[george-macdonald|MacDonald]] wants to understand the world's proper place in the divine order.

## The Loud World's Busy Way

The poet does not advocate physical withdrawal from worldly life. The challenge is to live *in* the world while remaining oriented toward [[god|God]]:

> "Shall I not walk the loud world's busy way, / Yet in thy palace stay?" (008-021)

This is the central paradox of [[george-macdonald|MacDonald]]'s spirituality: simultaneous presence in two orders of reality. The "loud world's busy way" is not to be fled but transformed into the very corridor of the divine palace.

## A World for Praise

In a startling turn, [[george-macdonald|MacDonald]] prays for a world — not to possess but to offer back in wor[[vessel|ship]]:

> "Give me a world, to part for praise and sunder." (012-012)

The world, properly understood, is material for doxology. The verb "part" and "sunder" suggest the analytical work of theology and poetry itself — breaking the world into its elements so each may be offered back to its maker in [[praise]].

## The World-Wise World

The poet warns of the worldly wisdom that scoffs at faith, personifying it as a voice of discouragement:

> "The world-wise world that laughs thee so to scorn." (005-030)

The doubling "world-wise world" is sharply ironic — the world's wisdom is merely worldliness reflecting upon itself. Against this closed circuit, [[george-macdonald|MacDonald]] sets the [[hope]] of "the undiscovered bourne," a destination beyond the world's horizon of comprehension.
