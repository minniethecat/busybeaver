Glory in [[george-macdonald|George MacDonald]]'s "A Book of [[strife|Strife]] in the Form of The Diary of an Old [[soul|Soul]]" (1880) is the visible radiance of divine [[perfect|perfection]] — simultaneously attractive and overwhelming. [[george-macdonald|MacDonald]] treats glory not as a static attribute of [[god|God]] but as a dynamic reality that both reveals [[god|God]] to the soul and hides [[god|God]] behind excess of light.

## The Eyes Made for Glory

[[george-macdonald|MacDonald]] [[earth|ground]]s the human capacity to perceive glory in the original design of the soul:

> Eyes made for glory soon discover thee. (002-006)

The human eye — and by extension, the human soul — was created with glory as its intended object. The capacity to see glory is not acquired but given; it is part of the soul's native equipment. The word "soon" suggests that glory is not deeply hidden — the properly oriented soul discovers [[god|God]] quickly, because it was made for that very discovery.

The preceding stanza confirms this anthropology:

> Who sees a glory, towards it will go. (006-012)

Perception of glory generates movement. To see glory is to be drawn toward it — not by external compulsion but by an inner correspondence between the glory perceived and the soul's own design. Glory is magnetic, pulling the soul toward its source.

## Glory Hidden and Revealed

Macdonald explores the paradoxical double function of glory — it reveals and conceals simultaneously:

> Ere, glory-hid, the other face I spied. (008-026)

Glory hides as much as it reveals. The "other face" — a fuller vision of [[god|God]] — is glimpsed only when the overwhelming brightness momentarily recedes. Glory protects the finite observer from what cannot yet be borne.

The next stanza develops this further:

> The mighty perfectness
> With glory blurs the dim-reflected less. (008-027)

[[divine|Divine]] [[perfect|perfection]] is so luminous that it "blurs" the lesser reflections of itself in creation. Glory makes the secondary, the derivative, the "dim-reflected" appear indistinct — not by diminishing them but by outshining them. The soul that gazes on glory loses the sharp outlines of lesser things.

## Sitting in Glory

The diary's most intimate image of glory places the speaker within it:

> Then only in thy glory I seem to sit,
> When my heart doth at thy footstool rest. (008-016)

The speaker does not observe glory from outside but "sits" within it — yet only when his heart [[peace|rest]]s at [[god|God]]'s footstool in [[humble|humility]]. Glory is not a spectacle to be viewed but a condition to be inhabited, and the condition of entry is self-abasement. The footstool posture is the throne posture: [[humble|humility]] is the doorway to glory.

## Shadow-Blent Glory

In October, [[george-macdonald|MacDonald]] ac[[wisdom|knowledge]]s that glory is seen imperfectly in this life:

> All vaguely spy a glory shadow-blent. (010-017)

The universal reach of glory — "all" see it — is qualified by its mixed appearance: it is "shadow-blent," interwoven with darkness. The soul sees glory not in pure radiance but in chiaroscuro, light tangled with shadow. This is the condition of mortal perception: glory is real and visible, but never unmixed.
