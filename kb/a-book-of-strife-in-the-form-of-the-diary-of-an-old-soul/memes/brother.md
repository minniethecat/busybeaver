## Definition

In [[george-macdonald|MacDonald]]'s poetry, "brother" operates at three interconnected levels: [[christ|Christ]] as the "elder brother" (006-020) who leads [[human|humanity]] into divine son[[vessel|ship]], the human [[duty|obligation]] of brotherly forgiveness and kin[[vessel|ship]], and the eschatological vision of all souls united as "each sister and each brother" (012-028). Unlike the abstract theological concept of universal [[love]], brotherhood is the lived, embo[[death|die]]d form of divine kin[[vessel|ship]] — it is love with a face, forgiveness with a name. The speaker's address to "brothers! sisters holy!" (001-006) establishes the [[worship|devotion]]al community as a family bound not by blood but by shared orientation toward [[god|God]].

## Key Passages

The [[christ|Christ]]ological foundation of brotherhood appears in: "Great elder brother of my second birth" (006-020). [[christ|Christ]] is not merely savior or lord but elder sibling — the firstborn who opens the way for younger siblings to follow. This familial intimacy is reinforced by the direct plea: "Son of the Father, elder brother mine, / See thy poor brother's" (011-013), where the speaker claims brotherhood with [[christ|Christ]] himself.

The ethical imperative of brotherhood is forgiveness: "What am I brother for, but to forgive!" (006-026). Brotherhood is not sentiment but action — the purpose of being a brother is to extend the forgiveness one has received. The speaker's posture of [[humble|humility]] before others takes physical form: "cast myself down, and kiss thy brother-feet" (012-021), a gesture of servant-love toward the [[presence|divine presence]] in the other.

[[george-macdonald|MacDonald]] [[earth|ground]]s brotherhood in shared origin: "Him he has made our brother: [[home|homeward]], thus, / To find our" (012-027). Brotherhood is not a human achievement but a divine creation — [[god|God]] has made us siblings. The eschatological gathering envisions universal reunion: "each sister and each brother" (012-028), where all divisions dissolve in shared divine parentage.

The communal address "Therefore, my brothers, therefore, sisters dear" (003-012) places the entire poem sequence within a familial frame — the speaker writes not to an abstract au[[death|die]]nce but to spiritual siblings. The inner orientation of brotherhood includes "keep heart-open-house to brother men" (004-012), where the heart is a [[tent|dwelling]] kept open for others.

## Related Concepts

Brotherhood is the relational expression of [[love]] and the horizontal dimension of the vertical relation[[vessel|ship]] with the divine [[father]]. [[christ|Christ]] as elder brother connects to [[christ]] as mediator and [[grace]] as the gift of adoption. The forgiveness imperative links to [[sin]] and [[mercy]], while the eschatological sibling reunion connects to [[heaven]] and [[hope]]. The "heart-open-house" image ties brotherhood to [[heart]] as the center of spiritual life.
