# Dickty

A slang term used in 1920s [[harlem|Harlem]] to describe Black people who were perceived as high-class, snobbish, or putting on airs — often associated with lighter skin, wealth, education, or association with white society.

## Definition

"Dickty" (also spelled "dikty") was a period-specific [[harlem|Harlem]] slang term that could function as both adjective and noun. It described Black individuals who were seen as elevated above the working-class masses — whether through lighter complexion, economic success, refined manners, or social connections to white Manhattan. The term carried an ambivalent charge: it marked status distinction within the Black community while often implying that such distinction came at the cost of solidarity. (003-047)

## Cultural Context

### Harlem Renaissance Context

The term "dickty" reflects the complex internal class and color stratification of 1920s [[harlem|Harlem]]. While the [[harlem|Harlem]] Renaissance celebrated Black culture, the community itself was stratified along lines of skin tone, education, occupation, and proximity to whiteness. Terms like "dickty," "high yaller," "blue-vein," and "[[ofay]]" formed a rich vocabulary for navigating these distinctions.

The "dickty" label was part of what scholars later termed the "color complex" — a system of social valuation within Black communities that privileged lighter skin, a legacy of slavery and colonialism that the [[harlem|Harlem]] Renaissance both challenged and, at times, reproduced.

### In the Novel

McKay uses "dickty" at key moments to mark class and status tensions. When the narrator describes the crowd at a swell party, he notes "so many dickty shines in sich swell motorcars" (003-047), using the term to characterize [[harlem|Harlem]]'s aspiring elite. 

Gertie Kendall is identified as knowing "the dickty niggers" (006-051), marking her as someone who moves in higher social circles. [[jake-brown|Jake]]'s internal monologue in Chapter XIX sums up a whole social scene: "A dickty gang sure as [[harlem|Harlem]] is black" (019-037) — his outsider's observation of [[harlem|Harlem]]'s class structure.

In Chapter XIII, the narrator's social taxonomy places "them of the dickty [[buffet-flat|buffet flats]]" (013-098) as a distinct category, showing how the term mapped onto physical spaces in [[harlem|Harlem]]'s nightlife geography.

## Key Passages

> Nevah befoh I seed so many dickty shines in sich swell motorcars. (003-047)

The exclamation captures the aspirational quality of "dickty" — the amazement at seeing well-dressed Black people in expensive cars speaks to both the reality of [[harlem|Harlem]]'s class spectrum and the novelty of visible Black affluence.

> Jake mentally noted: "A dickty gang sure as Harlem is black." (019-037)

[[jake-brown|Jake]]'s observation positions "dickty" as an inevitable feature of [[harlem|Harlem]]'s social landscape — where there is a Black community, there will be class stratification — but frames it with wry acceptance rather than resentment.

## Variations & Related Terms

- **High yaller** — A term specifically for very light-skinned Black people, often overlapping with "dickty" in social connotation
- **[[ofay|Ofay]]** — [[harlem|Harlem]] slang for white people; the "dickty" were sometimes criticized for imitating [[ofay]] manners
- **Shine** — A slang term for a Black person, used neutrally or with affection depending on context; "dickty [[shine]]" combined the two terms (003-047)
- **Blue-vein** — Referring to Black people so light-skinned their veins were visible, associated with elite social circles; [[madame-suarez|Madame Suarez]] claimed to "move like a queen among the blue-veins" (008-007)

## Historical Significance

The term "dickty" is documented in period sources including Zora Neale Hurston's glossary of [[harlem|Harlem]] slang and Cab Calloway's *Hepster's Dictionary*. It belongs to a larger lexicon of intraracial color and class terminology that scholars of the [[harlem|Harlem]] Renaissance have analyzed as both a symptom of internalized racism and a complex system of community self-definition. While the specific term faded from use after the 1940s, the social dynamics it named — class and color stratification within Black communities — remain subjects of scholarly and cultural discussion.
