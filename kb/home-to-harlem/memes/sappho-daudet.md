# Sappho (Daudet)

Alphonse Daudet's novel *Sappho: Parisian Manners* (1884) — a French naturalist work about a young provincial's destructive affair with a courtesan — appears in *[[Home to Harlem]]* as the book [[ray|Ray]] reads on the railroad, his choice of European literature dramatizing the educational and cultural distance between his intellectual consciousness and [[jake-brown|Jake]]'s vernacular world.

## Overview

Daudet (1840–1897) was a major figure in French naturalism, known for his detailed social observation, sympathy for marginal characters, and accessible prose style. *Sappho* (originally *Sapho: Mœurs parisiennes*) is one of his most enduring works — a novel about Jean Gaussin, a young man from Provence who falls in love with Fanny Legrand, an older courtesan known as "Sappho," and is gradually destroyed by the relationship's asymmetries of power, money, and desire.

The novel belongs to the 19th-century tradition of the "courtesan novel" — a genre that includes works by Balzac, Zola, and Flaubert — in which the figure of the kept woman serves as a vehicle for exploring modernity, commodification, and the fragility of masculine identity. Daudet's treatment is notable for its ambivalence toward Fanny/Sappho, who is neither villain nor victim but a complex figure navigating the limited options available to women of her class.

## Appearances

The novel appears in a single but structurally significant scene. [[ray|Ray]], working as a Pullman waiter on the railroad, is reading on his bunk when [[jake-brown|Jake]] enters:

> "What's that you're reading?" Jake asks.

> Ray describes the novel: "It's about a sporting woman who was beautiful like a picture." (010-019)

[[ray|Ray]]'s summary — translating Daudet's French naturalism into [[jake-brown|Jake]]'s vernacular — is an act of cultural bridge-building that simultaneously reveals the gap it attempts to cross. "Sporting woman" renders *courtisane* into [[harlem|Harlem]]'s idiom; "beautiful like a picture" preserves the aesthetic romance of the original while stripping it of Daudet's irony. The translation is both generous and reductive — [[ray|Ray]] making French literature accessible to [[jake-brown|Jake]] while necessarily simplifying it.

## Significance

[[ray|Ray]]'s choice of Daudet is significant in several registers:

**European vs. African American Literature**: [[ray|Ray]] reads both European works (Daudet, Dostoevsky's *[[Crime and Punishment]]*) and African American works ([[the-new-negro|The New Negro]]). His bilingual, bicultural reading list maps the divided consciousness of the educated Black intellectual in the 1920s — fluent in European high culture but increasingly committed to Black cultural production.

**The Body/Mind Binary**: [[ray|Ray]]'s absorption in Daudet — a novel about sexual obsession — while [[jake-brown|Jake]] lives the life of sexual experience outside the text, inverts the novel's body/mind binary. [[ray|Ray]] reads about desire; [[jake-brown|Jake]] experiences it. The scene suggests that the intellectual is not above the body but displaced from it.

**Class & Education**: The scene dramatizes the educational stratification within Black communities. [[ray|Ray]] can read French novels; [[jake-brown|Jake]] cannot. This difference is not treated as superiority — McKay, with his working-class commitments, refuses to valorize [[ray|Ray]]'s learning over [[jake-brown|Jake]]'s experience — but it is a real difference that structures their friendship.

## Creator

Alphonse Daudet (1840–1897) was a French novelist, playwright, and short story writer associated with the naturalist movement. His most famous works include *Lettres de mon moulin* (1869), *Tartarin de Tarascon* (1872), and *Sappho* (1884). Daudet was admired for his warmth and humor — qualities that distinguished him from the more austere naturalism of Zola — and for his sympathetic treatment of characters on society's margins.
