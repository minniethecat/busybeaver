**South Ferry** is a terminal at the southern tip of Manhattan, appearing in [[claude-mckay|Claude McKay]]'s *[[home-to-harlem|Home to Harlem]]* as the point of arrival from which [[jake-brown|Jake Brown]] begins his journey back to [[harlem|Harlem]].

## Location

### In the Novel's World

South Ferry is where [[jake-brown|Jake]] disembarks after being paid off from his ship. "Jake was paid off. He changed a pound note he had brought with him. He had fifty-nine dollars. From South Ferry he took an express subway train for Harlem." (002-002) The location functions as a threshold — the liminal space between Jake's life at sea and his return to [[harlem|Harlem]]'s streets. He arrives with the remnants of his London wages and immediately re-enters the Harlem world of drinking, eating, and social display.

## Significance to the Narrative

South Ferry appears only briefly but marks a key structural moment in the novel: the transition from Jake's cosmopolitan maritime world to the specific, racially-coded geography of [[harlem|Harlem]]. The express subway ride from South Ferry to [[harlem|Harlem]] traces the physical distance between white downtown Manhattan and Black uptown, a geographic segregation that defined New York in the 1920s.

## Historical Context

South Ferry has been a major transportation hub since the nineteenth century, serving ferries to Staten Island and, in the early twentieth century, connecting to the newly-built IRT subway line. For sailors and dockworkers like Jake, it was a common point of entry to Manhattan.
