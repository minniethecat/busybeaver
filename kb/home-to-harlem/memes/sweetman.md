A pointed slang term in [[claude-mckay|Claude McKay]]'s *Home to [[harlem|Harlem]]*: "sweetman" denotes a man who lives off a woman's earnings — a kept man, a pimp, or a gigolo. The term circulates as an accusation, a boast, and a disavowal, marking a fault line in the novel's negotiations of Black masculinity and economic survival.

## Definition

In the novel's vocabulary, a sweetman is a man who does not work for wages but is instead supported financially by a woman or women. The term carries moral ambiguity: on one hand, it is an insult implying parasitism and a failure of masculine self-sufficiency; on the other, it is a recognized social role that some men adopt strategically and others are accused of defensively. [[jake-brown|Jake]] explicitly disclaims the identity: "I've never been a sweetman yet. Never lived off no women" (004-104). The "sweetman disinclination" (013-092) that [[jake-brown|Jake]] professes marks his investment in a particular ideal of masculine independence.

## Cultural Context

### Harlem Renaissance Context

The figure of the kept man or "sweetback" had a long history in African American vernacular culture, immortalized in the folk ballad "The Sweetback" and later in the 1971 film *Sweet Sweetback's Baadasssss Song*. In the economic precarity of 1920s [[harlem|Harlem]], where Black men faced discrimination in employment and women often had more reliable access to domestic service jobs, the sweetman role was both a real economic strategy and a charged symbol in debates about Black gender relations. The term is related to but distinct from "pimp" — a sweetman may or may not be involved in sex work; the defining feature is financial dependence on a woman.

### In the Novel

McKay uses "sweetman" sparingly but pointedly, always in moments when characters are negotiating masculine status. [[jake-brown|Jake]]'s denial of sweetman identity comes when [[zeddy-plummer|Zeddy]] is teasing him about women. [[billy-biasse|Billy Biasse]] uses it as a kind of backhanded compliment: [[ray|Ray]] was "a pretty good skate for a sweetman" (019-023). The term maps a specific anxiety in McKay's [[harlem|Harlem]]: the tension between the masculine ideal of the self-sufficient worker and the economic reality that made such independence precarious.

## Variations and Related Terms

- **Sweetman vs. Pimp** — A pimp manages sex workers for profit; a sweetman simply lives off a woman's earnings, which may or may not come from sex work. In the novel, the term is closer to "kept man" than to "pimp."
- **Sweetman vs. Skirt-man** — In the novel's slang, a "skirt-man" is a womanizer, a man who chases women sexually. [[zeddy-plummer|Zeddy]] is called a "skirt-man" (007-086) as distinct from a "cocky sweetman" — the former is about sexual pursuit, the latter about economic dependence.
- **"Sweetback"** — The more common term in African American vernacular for the same figure; McKay's use of "sweetman" may be a variant or reflects period usage.

## Key Passages

> "I've never been a sweetman yet," Jake defended himself. "Never lived off no women. I work for my living. I ain't no sweetman." (004-104)

> Zeddy assured the boys that he was a cocky sweetman and no skirt-man. Plenty of men coveted his reputation. (007-086)

> "Ef I had the sweetman disinclination I wouldn't have to worry about no job," Jake said. (013-092)

> "He was a pretty good skate for a sweetman," Billy Biasse said of Ray. (019-023)

## Historical Significance

The sweetman as a social type reflected real economic conditions in early twentieth-century Black urban communities. Employment discrimination meant that Black women often had more stable access to domestic service and laundry work than Black men had to industrial and maritime jobs. This created conditions where some households were supported primarily by women's earnings, generating anxieties about dependent Black masculinity that the "sweetman" label crystallized.

The term belongs to a rich vocabulary of gender-role slang in the period, alongside related terms like "sweetback," "mack," and "pimp." Its use in McKay's novel reflects the broader [[harlem|Harlem]] Renaissance interest in vernacular speech as literary material and in the social types of the Black working class as subjects worthy of serious representation.
