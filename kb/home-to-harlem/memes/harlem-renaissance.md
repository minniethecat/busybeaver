# Harlem Renaissance

The Harlem Renaissance was the extraordinary efflorescence of [[africa|Africa]]n American literary, musical, artistic, and intellectual production that centered in [[Harlem]] during the 1920s and early 1930s. Sometimes called the New Negro Movement — after Alain Locke's landmark 1925 anthology *[[The New Negro]]* — the Renaissance transformed [[harlem|Harlem]] into a global symbol of Black modernity and produced a body of work that redefined American culture. [[claude-mckay|Claude McKay]] was among its most important and most controversial figures, and *[[Home to Harlem]]* (1928) was both a product of the Renaissance and a challenge to its internal orthodoxies.

## Definition

The Harlem Renaissance denotes both a historical period (roughly 1918–1935) and a cultural program: the assertion that [[africa|Africa]]n American art, literature, and music could embody racial pride, demonstrate intellectual sophistication, and demand full citizenship in American cultural life. The movement encompassed literature (McKay, [[langston-hughes|Langston Hughes]], Zora Neale Hurston, Jean Toomer, Countee Cullen, Nella Larsen), music ([[jazz]], [[blues]], spirituals), visual art (Aaron Douglas, Augusta Savage), theater, political journalism, and an unprecedented network of institutions — journals like *The Crisis* (NAACP) and *Opportunity* (Urban League), publishing houses, patronage networks, and [[Harlem]]'s own venues and social spaces.

## Cultural Context

### The New Negro

The term "New Negro" crystallized the Renaissance's self-conception: a break with the plantation past, an assertion of urban sophistication, education, and political militancy. Alain Locke's anthology *[[The New Negro]]* (1925) gathered essays, fiction, and poetry from the movement's leading figures, including McKay's poem "The White House." The "New Negro" was self-consciously modern — urban, literate, [[race-and-color|race]]-proud, and unwilling to accept the subservient roles prescribed by Jim Crow America.

Yet the Renaissance was not monolithic. A persistent tension divided those who favored "respectable" representations of Black life — middle-class, educated, morally upright — from those, like McKay, who insisted on depicting the full complexity of Black experience, including its working-class, sexual, and morally ambiguous dimensions. *[[home-to-harlem|Home to Harlem]]*'s frank treatment of sexuality and its focus on [[longshoreman|longshoremen]], gamblers, and sex workers made it a flashpoint in this debate.

### In the Novel

*[[home-to-harlem|Home to Harlem]]* does not directly name the "Harlem Renaissance" — the term itself was a later coinage — but it inhabits the movement's world at every level. The novel's [[Harlem]] is the [[harlem|Harlem]] of the Renaissance: a neighborhood dense with music, vernacular artistry, and social ambition. "[[the-congo|The Congo]] was [[africa|Africa]]n in spirit and [[race-and-color|color]]" (004-006) — a [[cabaret-culture|cabaret]] that attracted "sailors, the black from the South, the brown from the islands, and even some from [[africa|Africa]] itself," embodying the pan-[[africa|Africa]]n consciousness that was a Renaissance hallmark.

[[ray|Ray]] reads "Negro newspapers" [011-024, 014-022], the periodicals that constituted the Renaissance's intellectual infrastructure. [[jake-brown|Jake]], though uninterested in formal culture, navigates a social world — [[lenox-avenue|Lenox Avenue]] [[cabaret-culture|cabarets]], [[buffet-flat|buffet flats]], [[speakeasy|speakeasies]] — that was the Renaissance's material base. The music that defines the novel — "snake-wriggling" [[jazz]] (008-013), "black-framed [[blues]]" (005-057) — was the Renaissance's most consequential cultural export.

The novel's controversial reception — W.E.B. [[w-e-b-du-bois|Du Bois]] wrote that it made him feel "distinctly like taking a bath" — is itself evidence of the Renaissance's internal debates. McKay chose to represent the [[Harlem]] of working-class [[pleasure-seeking|pleasure]] rather than middle-class aspiration, and the resulting novel is both a Renaissance artifact and a critique of Renaissance respectability.

## Key Passages

The novel does not contain a single passage that explicitly defines the Harlem Renaissance. Rather, the Renaissance is the ambient cultural condition of the world McKay describes. Key passages that illuminate the movement's concerns include:

> Lenox Avenue was leading all the Negro cabarets a cruel dance. (004-004)

[[lenox-avenue|Lenox Avenue]] as agent — the street itself driving the cultural economy — captures the Renaissance's sense of [[harlem|Harlem]] as a dynamic, self-propelling cultural en[[gin-whisky|gin]]e.

> Every free day I have in New York I spend at the library downtown. I read there and I write. (010-091)

[[ray|Ray]]'s self-description distills the Renaissance intellectual ethos: the autodidact, the emb[[race-and-color|race]] of literacy as liberation, the commitment to reading and writing as political acts.

> Different, naturally, from New York, which molds all peoples into one metropolitan type. (013-022)

This reflection on [[new-york-city|New York]]'s assimilative power — and its limits: "cannot rob Negroes of their native [[race-and-color|color]] and lineaments" (013-022) — engages the Renaissance's central question of what happens to Black identity in the modern city.

## Historical Significance

The Harlem Renaissance transformed American culture permanently. It established that [[africa|Africa]]n American art was not a regional curiosity but an integral part of American modernism. It produced literary works (*Their Eyes Were Watching God*, *Cane*, *[[home-to-harlem|Home to Harlem]]*, Hughes's poetry) that remain central to the American canon. It created cultural institutions — journals, patronage networks, artistic communities — that sustained Black cultural production through the lean years of the Depression and beyond. Politically, the Renaissance's assertion of Black cultural dignity laid groundwork for the Civil Rights Movement's later claims to full citizenship.

McKay's specific contribution — his insistence on working-class authenticity, his refusal of respectability, his attention to the body and to [[pleasure-seeking|pleasure]] — influenced subsequent generations of Black writers from Richard Wright to James Baldwin, who cited McKay as a precursor.
