# Strivers' Row

Strivers' Row is the colloquial name for a pair of facing blocks of handsome Renaissance Revival and Georgian-style townhouses on West 138th and West 139th Streets between Seventh Avenue (now Adam Clayton Powell Jr. Boulevard) and Eighth Avenue (now Frederick Douglass Boulevard) in [[Harlem]]. Designed by prominent architects in the 1890s for white residents, the houses became available to African American tenants and buyers in the 1910s–1920s, and by the time of [[claude-mckay|Claude McKay]]'s *[[Home to Harlem]]* (1928), Strivers' Row had become the neighborhood's premier address for its professional class — doctors, lawyers, undertakers, entertainers — and the symbolic center of Black middle-class aspiration.

> _Note:_ The name "Strivers' Row" does not appear in the corpus of *Home to Harlem*. This entry provides historical and contextual information about a landmark that was central to the social geography of the [[Harlem]] the novel depicts — the world of "dicty" respectability that [[jake-brown|Jake]] and his working-class associates navigate around and in tension with.

## Location

### Geography

Strivers' Row occupies the south side of West 138th Street and the north side of West 139th Street, between Seventh Avenue and Eighth Avenue, in the heart of Harlem's most desirable residential zone. The 138th Street side features red-brick and brownstone townhouses designed by James Brown Lord in the Georgian style; the 139th Street side features yellow-brick and limestone townhouses designed by Stanford White (of McKim, Mead & White) in the Italian Renaissance Revival style. Both rows include rear alleys with service entrances and private stables — luxury features unusual in Manhattan townhouse construction.

### In Harlem's Social Geography

Strivers' Row sits in the western section of central Harlem, an area that by the 1920s had become associated with the neighborhood's Black elite and aspiring middle class. The east-west axis that structured Harlem's class geography placed Strivers' Row in the "respectable" zone — toward the western, quieter side — as opposed to [[lenox-avenue|Lenox Avenue]]'s commercial and nightlife corridor to the east.

## Significance to the Novel's World

While Strivers' Row is not explicitly named in *Home to Harlem*, its presence looms over the novel's class geography as the architectural counterpart to the world of "dicty" (respectable, propertied) Black Harlem — a world the novel acknowledges but does not center. The distance between Strivers' Row townhouses and [[jake-brown|Jake]]'s boarding houses, between the professional class and the longshoreman, between the parlor and the [[buffet-flat]], maps the internal class spectrum of 1920s Black Harlem that McKay's novel both registers and resists.

McKay's deliberate focus on working-class life — the docks, the cabarets, the buffet flats — is in part a choice _against_ the Harlem of Strivers' Row, and this choice is political. The novel was criticized by W.E.B. Du Bois and other middle-class gatekeepers precisely because it refused to foreground the respectable Black Harlem that Strivers' Row symbolized.

## Historical Context

### Origins

The houses of Strivers' Row were built between 1891 and 1893 by developer David H. King Jr., who commissioned three of New York's most prestigious architectural firms for the project. Originally intended for affluent white families, the blocks struggled to attract buyers after the Panic of 1893. As Harlem transitioned from a white to a Black neighborhood during the 1910s–1920s — a process driven by the [[Great Migration]] and the aggressive real estate speculation of Black entrepreneur Philip Payton — the townhouses gradually became available to African American tenants and owners.

### The Name

The name "Strivers' Row" emerged from Harlem's own vernacular — a term that acknowledged both the ambition of its Black residents and the aspirations their presence represented. To "strive" was to pursue upward mobility, respectability, and the American dream on terms that acknowledged racial obstacles. The name carried an edge of irony among some observers — W.E.B. Du Bois's concept of the "talented tenth" was both embodied and gently mocked by the term — but it stuck because it captured something real about the social meaning of these particular blocks.

### Notable Residents

By the 1920s and 1930s, Strivers' Row was home to figures including Eubie Blake (composer of *Shuffle Along*), Fletcher Henderson (bandleader), Harry Wills (heavyweight boxer), and Vertner Tandy (one of the founders of Alpha Phi Alpha fraternity and the first Black architect registered in New York State). This concentration of talent and achievement made Strivers' Row a microcosm of the [[Harlem Renaissance]]'s professional class.

## Appearances

The name "Strivers' Row" does not appear in *Home to Harlem*'s passages. However, the social stratum it represents is registered throughout the novel — in the distinction between "dicty" and "sporting" Harlem, in the economic dynamics of [[rose-congo|Rose]] supporting [[jake-brown|Jake]] from her cabaret earnings, in Ray's self-consciousness as an educated man working as a Pullman cook, and in the novel's sustained attention to the material conditions (housing, wages, clothing) that determine social status.

The closest the novel comes to registering this stratum is in references to the "dicty" — the respectable, churchgoing, often lighter-skinned Black middle class whom Jake's world exists alongside but rarely penetrates. Strivers' Row is where the dicty lived; the [[lenox-avenue|Lenox Avenue]] cabarets and [[buffet-flat|buffet flats]] are where Jake lived.
