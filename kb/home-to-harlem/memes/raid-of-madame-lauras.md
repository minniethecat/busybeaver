# Raid of Madame Laura's Buffet Flat

A white policeman stages a mock raid on [[madame-laura|Madame Laura]]'s [[buffet-flat|buffet flat]] in [[philadelphia|Philadelphia]] — an event that terrifies newcomers, leaves old-timers unruffled, and resolves into champagne and a toast, exposing the negotiated relationship between [[harlem|Harlem]] nightlife and the law under [[prohibition|Prohibition]].

## Overview

> "Raided!" A voice screamed. Standing in the rear door, a policeman, white, in full uniform, smilingly contemplated the spectacle. There was a wild scramble for hats and wraps. The old-timers giggled, shrugged, and kept their seats. Madame Laura pushed aside the policeman.
> — (013-058)

The raid is the dramatic climax of the [[buffet-flat|buffet flat]] party in Chapter XIII — the evening at [[madame-laura|Madame Laura]]'s house on [[fifteenth-street|Fifteenth Street]] in [[philadelphia|Philadelphia]] to which [[jake-brown|Jake]] has brought [[ray|Ray]]. What appears at first a police raid — a white officer at the rear door, the scream of warning, the panic of unfamiliar guests — is revealed within moments as a routine performance. [[madame-laura|Madame Laura]] pushes past the policeman, scolds him for frightening her guests, and seats him at a table with champagne. The raid is not a rupture of the evening but a set piece within it: the law arrives, is domesticated, and departs, and the party's economy of protection is made visible.

## Context

### Historical Background

During [[prohibition|Prohibition]] 1920–1933, Black nightlife establishments in northern cities operated in a legal gray zone. Police raids on [[speakeasy|speakeasies]], [[buffet-flat|buffet flats]], and [[cabaret-culture|cabarets]] were frequent — but so were informal protection arrangements. A white policeman on the beat might receive payments, favors, or simply a drink and a seat at the table in exchange for overlooking violations. These arrangements were asymmetrical: the officer held legal authority, but the proprietor — often a Black woman — held local knowledge, social capital, and the power to make the officer's job easier or harder. The raid at [[madame-laura|Madame Laura]]'s dramatizes this negotiation with documentary precision.

[[claude-mckay|McKay]]'s portrayal of the policeman as "smilingly" contemplating the scene (013-058) captures the ambiguous affect of this arrangement — part amusement, part condescension, part genuine familiarity.

### In the Novel

The raid occurs deep into the [[buffet-flat|buffet flat]] party that occupies most of Chapter XIII — the richest extended scene of Black nightlife in the novel. [[jake-brown|Jake]] has brought [[ray|Ray]] to the house on [[fifteenth-street|Fifteenth Street]] as a kind of education: [[ray|Ray]] is reluctant, squeamish about "filth" (013-014), and [[jake-brown|Jake]] insists that "Them's different ovah there" (013-015). The evening unfolds as a series of escalating intensities — music, flirtation, champagne, the startling appearance of [[madame-laura|Madame Laura]]'s eleven-year-old son, couples making love at the tables, the pianist sinking into a "dim, faraway, ancestral source of music" (013-056) — and then the raid, which punctures the atmosphere without destroying it.

The raid also serves as a structural hinge: [[ray|Ray]], who has already slipped away, misses it entirely — a fact that isolates him further from the world [[jake-brown|Jake]] inhabits. When [[jake-brown|Jake]] asks the next morning, "Did the policeman scare you, too, last night?" and [[ray|Ray]] answers, "What policeman?" (013-078)(013-079), the exchange crystallizes the distance between the two friends.

## Key Participants

- [[madame-laura|Madame Laura]] — host of the [[buffet-flat|buffet flat]], who handles the policeman with the authority of someone who has performed this ritual many times before
- [[jake-brown|Jake]] — guest who stays through the raid; afterward dismisses the fire-alarm-[[fashion-and-style|style]] scream as excessive: "Geewizard! Tell 'em next time not to ring the fire alarm so loud" (013-061)
- [[ray|Ray]] — the intellectual outsider who has already left the party before the raid, missing it entirely
- Pat — the white policeman, on a first-name basis with [[madame-laura|Madame Laura]]; drinks champagne, accepts the toast, and leaves "Time for duty" (013-074)
- The happy-faced black woman — [[madame-laura|Madame Laura]]'s co-manager, who is romantically involved with the policeman: "He's loving *her* — but he's protecting *me*" (013-076)

## Details and Sequence

### The Party Before the Raid

The evening at [[fifteenth-street|Fifteenth Street]] is the novel's most sustained depiction of a [[buffet-flat|buffet flat]] in operation. The interior resembles "a comfortable boardinghouse where everybody was cheerful and nice coquettish girls in [[race-and-color|color]]ful frocks were doing the waiting" (013-022). A pianist plays [[blues]]; couples flirt; men play cards; an attractive black woman serves sandwiches, [[gin-whisky|gin]], and bottled beer. When [[madame-laura|Madame Laura]] produces champagne for [[jake-brown|Jake]], the cork hits the ceiling (013-027).

The atmosphere intensifies through McKay's careful modulation. [[madame-laura|Madame Laura]]'s young son appears at her side — "a boy of dull-gold complexion," about eleven, "Clever kid, too. He loves books" (013-032) — and then drifts toward the piano. [[ray|Ray]] is disgusted by the child's presence, though he cannot explain his aversion (013-034). He retreats from the table, leaving [[madame-laura|Madame Laura]] with her arm around [[jake-brown|Jake]]'s neck.

McKay marks the shift from sociability to sensuality with an economical sentence: "[[madame-laura|Madame Laura]] had set the pace. There were four other couples making love" (013-036). The stags continue their card game; "the happy-faced black woman was doing the mana[[gin-whisky|gin]]g, as [[madame-laura|Madame Laura]] was otherwise engaged" (013-037). The pianist be[[gin-whisky|gin]]s "ban[[gin-whisky|gin]]g another [[blues]]."

### Ray's Departure

[[ray|Ray]] feels "alone and a little sorry for himself" (013-038). He envies [[jake-brown|Jake]]'s ability to fall "naturally into its rhythm" (013-038). A girl approaches him — "Why is you so all by you'self, baby?" (013-044) — but he parries with the offer of a drink instead. He buys glasses of [[virginia|Virginia]] Dare wine for two brown girls (013-049)–(013-054), but the encounter goes nowhere. By the time the pianist has descended into his "dim, faraway, ancestral source of music" (013-056) and the room has dissolved into a "phallic celebration" (013-056), [[ray|Ray]] has already slipped away and walked back to [[the-railroad|the railroad]] quarters on Market Street.

### The Raid

The word arrives as a scream: "Raided!" (013-058). A white policeman in full uniform stands in the rear door, "smilingly contemplated the spectacle" (013-058). The response divides along lines of experience:

> There was a wild scramble for hats and wraps. The old-timers giggled, shrugged, and kept their seats.
> — (013-058)

[[madame-laura|Madame Laura]] pushes the policeman aside and ad[[fashion-and-style|dress]]es the room: "Keep you' pants on, all of you and carry on with you' fun. What's matter? Scared of a uniform?" (013-059). She turns to the officer: "Pat — what you want to throw a scare in the company for? Come on here with you" (013-059).

The policeman, "twirling his baton," marches to a table and sits down with [[madame-laura|Madame Laura]] (013-060). [[jake-brown|Jake]] sits too: "Geewizard! Tell 'em next time not to ring the fire alarm so loud" (013-061). [[madame-laura|Madame Laura]] twists the policeman's ear: "You said it, honey-stick. There are no cops in [[philadelphia|Philly]] going to mess with this girl. Ain't it the truth, Pat?" (013-062).

The happy-faced black woman produces another bottle of champagne and seats herself "familiarly beside the policeman" (013-063). The glasses foam: "Four brown hands and one white. Chink!" (013-067). [[madame-laura|Madame Laura]] toasts Pat with a couplet that declares her mixed heritage and the alliance it underwrites:

> "Flixy, flaxy, fleasy,
> Make it good and easy,
> Flix for start and flax for snappy,
> Niggers and Irish will always be happy."
> — (013-070)–(013-073)

The policeman drinks, rises: "Gotta go now. Time for duty" (013-074). After he leaves, [[jake-brown|Jake]] asks: "You treat him nice. Is it for love or protection?" (013-075). [[madame-laura|Madame Laura]]'s answer is the scene's analytical key:

> "He's loving *her*" — Madame Laura indicated the now coy lady who helped her manage — "but he's protecting *me*. It's a long time since I ain't got no loving inclination for any skin but chocolate. Get me?"
> — (013-076)

The protection economy is triangulated: the white policeman loves the black co-manager, and through that romantic tie, he protects the Black woman who runs the business. Desire, law, and commerce are braided together.

### The Morning After

When [[jake-brown|Jake]] returns to the quarters, [[ray|Ray]] is already asleep. The next morning, walking to [[the-railroad|the railroad]] yards, [[jake-brown|Jake]] asks if the policeman scared him. [[ray|Ray]]'s blank response — "What policeman?" (013-079) — reveals that he left before the raid, before the party reached its climax. [[jake-brown|Jake]] assumes [[ray|Ray]] fled in fear; [[ray|Ray]]'s real reason — a girl's "nasty perfume" (013-083) — is both trivial and telling: it is the fastidiousness of the intellectual, the man who cannot surrender to the rhythm.

The conversation continues in a saloon over [[gin-whisky|whisky]] and eggnog. [[ray|Ray]] presses the question that has bothered him all night: "But [[madame-laura|Madame Laura]] isn't young" (013-090). [[jake-brown|Jake]]'s response is unhesitating: "I'd back her against some of the youngest. She's a wonder, chappie. Her blood's like good liquor" (013-091). He shows off the necktie she gave him — "a lovely slate-[[race-and-color|color]]ed necktie sprinkled with red dots" (013-091).

The discussion opens into [[jake-brown|Jake]]'s fullest statement on the taxonomy of nightlife: his catalog of "sweet marchants" ran[[gin-whisky|gin]]g from "slap-up private-apartmant ones" to "them of the [[dickty]] [[buffet-flat|buffet flats]]; then the low-down [[speakeasy]] customers; the [[cabaret-culture|cabaret]] babies, the family-entrance clients, and the street fliers" (013-098). The raid — and the world it revealed — has prompted [[jake-brown|Jake]]'s most elaborate sociological speech.

## Aftermath and Significance

The raid of [[madame-laura|Madame Laura]]'s [[buffet-flat|buffet flat]] is, in one sense, a non-event: nobody is arrested, nobody flees permanently, the champagne flows, and the policeman leaves on friendly terms. But its very uneventfulness is the point. McKay uses the raid to demonstrate the *normality* of the protection economy — the way Black women entrepreneurs like [[madame-laura|Madame Laura]] converted the threat of policing into a manageable transaction, a ritual that could be laughed off with a toast.

The scene crystallizes several of the novel's thematic concerns:

**Race and the law.** The policeman is white; the proprietor and guests are Black. The power differential is absolute in theory — but in practice, [[madame-laura|Madame Laura]] pushes past the officer, scolds him, twists his ear, and sends him on his way. The raid reveals that formal legal authority and actual social power do not always align.

**Gender and entrepreneurship.** [[madame-laura|Madame Laura]] is one of a series of "Madames" in the novel — [[madame-suarez|Madame Suarez]], [[madame-mulberry|Madame Mulberry]] — each of whom operates a space of [[pleasure-seeking|pleasure]] and profit outside formal legality. The raid scene shows the specific skills this role demanded: negotiation, performance, the management of men's egos (both the policeman's and the customers'), and the conversion of sexual economy into protection.

**The [[jake-brown|Jake]]-[[ray|Ray]] dyad.** [[ray|Ray]]'s absence from the raid is a structural device. He misses the evening's climax — and with it, the knowledge of how this world actually works. [[jake-brown|Jake]] explains it to him the next morning, but the explanation is secondhand. The raid thus reinforces the distance between the two friends: [[jake-brown|Jake]] is inside the world of the [[buffet-flat|buffet flat]], navigating its rituals; [[ray|Ray]] has already walked away, his fastidiousness keeping him outside.

**The old-timers and the newcomers.** McKay's detail that "the old-timers giggled, shrugged, and kept their seats" while others scrambled (013-058) encodes a social distinction: those who know the codes of the nightlife world and those who do not. The raid is a test of belon[[gin-whisky|gin]]g, and [[ray|Ray]], who has already failed it by leaving, is placed definitively among the outsiders.
