**Shine** appears in Claude McKay's *Home to Harlem* as a slang term, most notably in the compound phrase "dickty shines." In the linguistic landscape of 1920s Harlem, "shine" carried multiple meanings — as a noun, it could refer to a Black person (sometimes derogatory), while in the compound "dickty shines" it described a specific social type: well-dressed, status-conscious, and often pretentious Black Harlemites.

## "Dickty Shines" in the Novel

The phrase appears in descriptions of Harlem's social types:

> "…dickty shines…" (003-047)

> "…dickty shines…" (007-107)

> "…shine…" (020-019)

"Dickty" (also spelled "dickty" or "dicty") was Harlem slang for high-class, uppity, or pretentious — someone who put on airs. Combined with "shine," the phrase described a recognizable social figure: the striver who dressed sharp, affected sophistication, and looked down on ordinary working people.

## Class and Performance

The "dickty shine" embodies the novel's interest in class stratification within Harlem's Black community. These figures — often waiters, railroad porters, or small business owners with disposable income — invested heavily in appearance as a marker of status. Their "shine" was both literal (polished shoes, pressed suits) and metaphorical (social polish, aspiration).

## McKay's Ambivalence

McKay's treatment of "dickty shines" reflects his broader ambivalence about class in Harlem. While the novel celebrates the vitality and style of working-class life, it also registers the pretensions and anxieties of those striving to rise above it. The "dickty shine" is both admired for his style and mocked for his pretension.
