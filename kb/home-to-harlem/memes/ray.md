# Ray

An educated Haitian immigrant who works as a railroad [[waiter]] alongside [[jake-brown|Jake]] in *Home to [[harlem|Harlem]]*. Ray serves as the novel's intellectual conscience — a figure who reads Dostoevsky and discusses race politics while [[jake-brown|Jake]] dances and drinks — and whose alienation from both white America and Black [[harlem|Harlem]]'s nightlife embodies the diasporic intellectual's dilemma.

## Role in the Novel

Ray enters the novel in Chapter XI, when he shares a railroad bunk with [[jake-brown|Jake]]. His educated speech and bookish habits immediately distinguish him from [[jake-brown|Jake]]'s working-class world: "Ray bought some weekly Negro newspapers and a copy of *[[the-new-negro|The New Negro]]*" (011-024). He functions as [[jake-brown|Jake]]'s foil — where [[jake-brown|Jake]] experiences [[harlem|Harlem]] as sensual abundance, Ray experiences it as intellectual and political problem.

Ray's chapters (XI–XIII, XVIII–XXI) introduce the novel's most explicit political discourse: critiques of American racism, reflections on the Haitian Revolution and Toussaint L'Ouverture, debates about the role of education in Black liberation, and meditations on the relationship between the "Talented Tenth" and the Black masses.

## Characterization

### Appearance

Ray is described as "slim and medium brown" in contrast to [[jake-brown|Jake]]'s "tall, brawny, and black." His physical slightness suggests the life of the mind — he is not a manual laborer by nature, though economic necessity has made him one.

### Personality & Traits

Ray is characterized by his intellectual restlessness. He reads constantly — Dostoevsky's *Crime and Punishment*, Daudet's *[[sappho-daudet|Sappho]]*, Barbusse's *Le Feu*, the *New Negro* anthology — and his mind moves across a global terrain of literature and politics. He is acutely conscious of race as a structural condition, not just a personal experience, and his analysis of American racism is informed by his Haitian background, where Black people constitute the nation rather than a minority.

His alienation is his defining emotional state: he is too educated for the working-class Black world of the railroad, too Black for the white intellectual world he aspires to, and too Haitian for an America that sees only "Negro." At the [[house-party|house party]] in Chapter XIII, when [[syncopation]] pulls bodies toward the dance floor, Ray feels "that he ought to dance" but instead drifts into reverie about "far, far away from music-hall [[syncopation]] and [[jazz]]" (013-056) — a daydream of escape from the very culture that sustains his people.

### Relationships

- [[jake-brown]] — His railroad companion and friend, the embodiment of the physical life Ray can only observe
- [[zeddy-plummer]] — Fellow railroad worker, represents the working-class masculinity Ray cannot emulate

## Key Scenes

**The railroad bunk (Chapter XI):** Ray's introduction — reading newspapers while [[jake-brown|Jake]] sleeps — establishes the intellectual/physical binary that structures their relationship (011-024).

**The [[house-party|house party]] (Chapter XIII):** Ray's alienation from [[harlem|Harlem]]'s bodily culture is dramatized. While others dance to [[blues|the blues]], Ray sits apart, lost in thought about Haiti and literature. "Tickling, enticing [[syncopation]]. Ray felt that he ought to dance" — but he cannot (013-045).

**The farewell (Chapter XVIII):** Ray's decision to leave America — unable to reconcile his intellectual aspirations with the racial constraints of American life — is one of the novel's most politically charged moments.

**The closing scene (Chapter XXI):** Ray's final appearance contemplating the fate of [[james-reese-europe|James Reese Europe]], the [[jazz]] master shot by "a savage buck of his race," crystallizes the novel's tragic vision of Black artistic achievement cut short by Black violence (021-010).

## Themes

Ray embodies the theme of **the diasporic intellectual** — educated, multilingual, cosmopolitan, but unable to find a home in any national or racial community. His Haitian background introduces **pan-Africanist consciousness** — Haiti as the site of successful Black revolution — as an alternative to American racial despair. His relationship with [[jake-brown|Jake]] dramatizes the tension between **intellectual critique and lived experience** that animated [[harlem|Harlem]] Renaissance debates about the role of the Black artist.

## Quotes

> "Raymond, but everybody calls me Ray." (011-023)

The shortening of his name from the formal "Raymond" to the colloquial "Ray" mirrors his social descent — from Haitian student to American railroad [[waiter]] — and his ambivalent accommodation to American Black culture.

> Ray felt that he ought to dance. (013-045)

In four words, McKay captures the intellectual's alienation from bodily pleasure — Ray knows he *ought* to participate in [[harlem|Harlem]]'s collective joy, but knowledge does not produce ability or desire.
