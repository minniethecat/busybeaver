# Madame Mulberry

A named figure in [[harlem|Harlem]]'s nightlife world who appears in two distinct registers: first mentioned offhandedly by [[zeddy-plummer|Zeddy Plummer]] as proof of his social reach, then introduced in full glamour at the novel's culminating [[cabaret-culture|cabaret]] scene. Her biography — "Brown Glory" of the old [[fifty-third-street|Fifty-third Street]] era, wife of show promoter [[dick-mulberry|Dick Mulberry]], now a society fixture — t[[race-and-color|race]]s the [[migration]] of Black elite nightlife from downtown to [[harlem|Harlem]].

## Role in the Novel

Madame Mulberry first enters the novel through [[zeddy-plummer|Zeddy Plummer]]'s boasting. When [[gin-head-susy|Susy]] mocks him, he retorts: "I even danced with her at Madame Mulberry's apartment. You thinks I only hangs out with low-down trash becassin Ise in a place like this, eh? I done met mos'n all our big niggers: [[jack-johnson|Jack Johnson]], [[james-reese-europe|James Reese Europe]], Adah Walker, Buddy, who used to play that theah drum for them Castle Walkers, and Madame Walker" (005-095). In [[zeddy-plummer|Zeddy]]'s taxonomy of status, Madame Mulberry's name ranks alongside the heavyweight champion and the most celebrated Black bandleader of the era — hers is a name that confers legitimacy.

She appears in person in Chapter XXI at a [[fashion-and-style|fashion]]able [[cabaret-culture|cabaret]] — possibly the novel's unnamed "new [[cabaret-culture|cabaret]]" — where "the press, theatrical, and music world were represented" (021-003). McKay's narrator provides her biography:

> Madame Mulberry was there wearing peacock blue with patches of yellow. Madame Mulberry was a famous black beauty in the days when Fifty-third Street was the hub of fashionable Negro life. They called her then, Brown Glory. She was the wife of Dick Mulberry, a promoter of Negro shows. She had no talent for the stage herself, but she knew all the celebrated stage people of her race. She always gossiped reminiscently of Bert Williams, George Walker and Aida Overton Walker, Anita Patti Brown and Cole and Johnson. (021-003)

Later in the same scene, [[maunie-whitewing|Maunie Whitewing]] — a scandalous figure seated beside Madame Mulberry (021-008) — married to a nationally-known Negro artist — "smiled upon [[jake-brown|Jake]] with a bold stare and remarked to Madame Mulberry: '*Quel beau garçon! J'aimerais beaucoup faire l'amour avec lui.*'" Madame Mulberry's response is delivered through a lorgnette: "'Superb!' agreed Madame Mulberry, appreciating [[jake-brown|Jake]] through her lorgnette" (021-015). The lorgnette — an accessory of class pretension — positions Madame Mulberry as a woman who appraises men with the same discriminating eye she once applied to [[fifty-third-street|Fifty-third Street]]'s theatrical talent.

## Characterization

McKay draws Madame Mulberry as a woman of taste, memory, and social capital. She has no stage talent — the narrator is explicit about this — but she has something equally valuable: knowledge of everyone who does. She "gossiped reminiscently" of [[bert-williams|Bert Williams]] and the Walker dynasty; she moves through the new [[cabaret-culture|cabaret]] as a living archive of Black entertainment history.

Her [[fashion-and-style|fashion]] — "peacock blue with patches of yellow" — signals wealth and confidence. The lorgnette through which she appreciates [[jake-brown|Jake]] is both an instrument of class performance and a genuine tool of appraisal. She looks, judges, and approves, all from behind the glass.

## Historical Significance

Madame Mulberry's biography maps a crucial arc in Black cultural geography. [[fifty-third-street|Fifty-third Street]] was the center of [[fashion-and-style|fashion]]able Negro life before [[harlem|Harlem]] — the midtown theater district where Black performers, entrepreneurs, and socialites built the infrastructure that would later migrate uptown. Her marriage to [[dick-mulberry|Dick Mulberry]], "a promoter of Negro shows," places her at the intersection of art and commerce in this world.

Her presence in the novel's final [[cabaret-culture|cabaret]] scene — alongside the sports editor of *[[colored-life|Colored Life]]*, a prominent Negro pianist, and the rumor that "[[bert-williams|Bert Williams]] might drop in after midnight" (021-003) — and Madame Mulberry was certain he would (021-009) — represents the persistence of an older Black elite within the new [[harlem|Harlem]] nightlife. She is a bridge figure: old enough to remember [[fifty-third-street|Fifty-third Street]]'s glory days, present enough to appraise [[jake-brown|Jake]]'s beauty through her lorgnette.
