**Race and Color** in *Home to [[harlem|Harlem]]* are not abstract categories but lived hierarchies — a vocabulary of skin tone that maps social status, desirability, and power within [[harlem|Harlem]]'s Black community. [[claude-mckay|Claude McKay]] systematically catalogs the color spectrum (004-034) and the social meanings attached to each shade, producing one of the most detailed literary treatments of colorism in the Harlem Renaissance canon.

## Definition

In the novel's world, "color" operates as a complex social code. Skin tone is parsed with taxonomic precision: the spectrum runs from "spade or chocolate-to-the-bone" (006-007) through "low-brown" and "high-brown" (006-009) to "yellow" and "almost-white on the brink of change" (006-009). Each designation carries social meaning — privilege, desirability, suspicion — and characters navigate these meanings constantly, whether in romance, employment, or casual social encounter.

Color is distinct from race in the novel's lexicon. "Race" refers to the collective identity of Black people — "a romance of his race" (010-072), "yellow youth of her race" (006-008) — while "color" refers to the internal hierarchy within that collective, the gradations of skin tone that determine one's position relative to other Black people.

## Cultural Context

### Harlem Renaissance Context

Colorism — discrimination based on skin tone within the Black community — was a pervasive feature of early twentieth-century African American life. Lighter-skinned Black people often had access to better employment, education, and social circles, a legacy of slavery's pigmentocracy and the post-emancipation consolidation of a mulatto elite. The Harlem Renaissance was deeply marked by these divisions: venues like the Cotton Club hired only light-skinned performers; "blue vein" societies excluded those whose skin was too dark to show veins; and the "dicty" versus "sporting" divide in [[harlem|Harlem]] mapped class onto color.

McKay's treatment of colorism is remarkable for its honesty. Unlike some Renaissance writers who elided internal color hierarchies for the sake of racial solidarity, McKay documents them in granular detail — the preferences, the resentments, the micro-dramas of skin tone that structured [[harlem|Harlem]] social life.

### In the Novel

The novel's most concentrated treatment of colorism appears in the portrait of [[susy|Susy]] in Chapter 6. "The passion of Susy's life was the yellow youth of her race" (006-008). [[susy|Susy]]'s romantic fixation on light-skinned men — "Black reaching out beyond yellow. Almost-white on the brink of change" (006-009) — is presented as a kind of pathology, an internalized hierarchy that McKay links to the broader "yellow complexity" (006-010) of [[harlem|Harlem]]'s color-conscious social world.

McKay describes her as "a victim to that" — the "that" being the entire system of color preference that governs Black desire: "And like many, many Negroes, she was a victim to that" (006-009). The repetition ("many, many") emphasizes that [[susy|Susy]]'s condition is not individual pathology but collective social fact.

The color hierarchy shapes male competition too. At a buffet flat, "a potato-yellow man and a dull-black were locked" in confrontation (004-040), and "the black man heaved toward the yellow" (004-045). Color difference translates directly into physical aggression — the spectrum is not merely aesthetic but charged with rivalry and resentment.

McKay also documents racial dynamics between Black and white. The narrator records "Negro proprietors would not admit white persons" (008-039) to certain venues, while other establishments "depended on its downtown white trade" (004-003). The racial economy of [[harlem|Harlem]] nightlife was structured around a complex negotiation of white patronage and Black autonomy.

White racism is registered through language: [[jake-brown|Jake]] "preferred white folks' hatred to their friendly contempt" (001-014), recognizing that the Englishman's affectionate "darky" is as dehumanizing as the American's hostile "nigger." The narrator observes that "when he said 'nigger' he meant hatred for Negroes, whereas when he said 'darky' he meant friendly contempt" (001-014) — a linguistic analysis of racial attitudes that distinguishes British from American racism.

The novel's racial vision extends globally. [[ray|Ray]] reflects that "each marine down in Haiti must be conscious of being white" (011-080), linking American racism at home to imperial racism abroad. [[jake-brown|Jake]], in turn, is described as looking "askew at foreign niggers" (010-073) — the novel acknowledges that even within the Black diaspora, national and ethnic prejudices complicate any simple racial solidarity.

These terms operate together as a complete racial vocabulary, mapping every shade and social position within [[harlem|Harlem]]'s color-conscious world.

## Key Passages

> Dandies and pansies, chocolate, chestnut, coffee, ebony, cream, yellow, everybody was teased up to the high point of excitement. (004-034)

McKay's catalog of skin tones — "chocolate, chestnut, coffee, ebony, cream, yellow" — is presented as a neutral taxonomy before the collective "everybody" subsumes individual color into communal excitement. The list performs the novel's commitment to documenting the full spectrum of Black Harlem.

> Black reaching out beyond yellow. Almost-white on the brink of change. And like many, many Negroes, she was a victim to that. (006-009)

The novel's most compressed statement on colorism: desire organized by hue, the asymptote of whiteness, and the pathologizing of color preference as collective "victimhood." The phrase "reaching out beyond yellow" captures the aspirational quality of colorism — the movement toward lightness as movement toward status.

> White against white and black against white and yellow against black and brown. We's all just crazy-dog mad. (004-049)

The fight at the [[cabaret]] catalogs racial antagonisms — white against white, black against white, yellow against black and brown — collapsing class, color, and race into an undifferentiated "crazy-dog mad." The speaker's "we's" implicates the entire community in the violence of racial division.

## Historical Significance

Colorism has deep roots in the history of slavery and colonialism, where lighter-skinned enslaved people (often the children of slaveholders) were given domestic work while darker-skinned people labored in fields. After emancipation, the mulatto elite consolidated social and economic advantages, forming churches, schools, and social clubs that excluded darker-skinned Black people.

The Harlem Renaissance both challenged and reproduced these hierarchies. While the movement celebrated Blackness in all its forms, institutions like the Cotton Club (which hired only light-skinned women) and social phenomena like "the brown paper bag test" (if you were darker than a paper bag, you couldn't join certain organizations) reinforced color hierarchy. McKay's documentation of this system — through [[susy|Susy]]'s "yellow complexity," through the [[cabaret]] fights between "potato-yellow" and "dull-black" men, through the vocabulary of "high-brown" aspiration — is one of the novel's most significant contributions to the social history of Black America.
