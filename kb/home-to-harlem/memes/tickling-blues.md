**"Tickling Blues"** is a [[blues]] song referenced in [[claude-mckay|Claude McKay]]'s 1928 novel *[[home-to-harlem|Home to Harlem]]*. It is one of several contemporary [[blues]] and [[ragtime]] numbers mentioned in the novel that evoke the musical landscape of 1920s [[harlem|Harlem]].

## In the Novel

"Tickling Blues" is requested by characters in [[harlem|Harlem]]'s social gatherings. [[miss-curdy|Miss Curdy]] calls out: "put on that theah 'Tickling Blues' on the victroly" (006-077), referring to a Victrola record player. Characters dance to the song: a scene describes a couple "stepping with [[gin-head-susy|Susy]] to the 'Tickling Blues'" (006-080). At another gathering, a character urgently requests: "Put on that theah 'Tickling Blues' that we's all just crazy about" (007-060).

The song belongs to [[blues|the blues]] tradition that permeates the novel's soundscape, alongside other musical references to [[ragtime]], syncopation, and [[jazz]]. Its insistent rhythm is hinted at in the narrative's description of "tickling, enticing syncopation" (013-045), which may allude to the song's characteristically playful [[blues]] tempo.

## Cultural Context

The repeated requests for "Tickling Blues" across multiple chapters suggest it was a well-known popular recording of the era. The novel uses such musical references to ground its depiction of [[harlem|Harlem]]'s social life in authentic period detail, where the Victrola and its [[blues]] records provided the soundtrack for [[buffet-flat|buffet flat]] parties, [[rent-party|rent parties]], and [[cabaret-culture|cabaret]] evenings.
