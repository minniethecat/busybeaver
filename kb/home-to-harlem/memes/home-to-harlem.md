# Home to Harlem

Landmark 1928 novel by [[claude-mckay]] depicting working-class Black life in [[harlem]] after World War I, the first novel by a Black writer to become a national bestseller.

## Overview

*Home to Harlem* is a novel by [[claude-mckay]], first published in 1928 by Harper & Brothers. It was the first novel by a Black writer to achieve national bestseller status in the United States, and it became one of the defining texts of the [[harlem-renaissance]]. The novel follows the intertwined lives of two Black men — Jake Brown, a working-class longshoreman and Army deserter, and Ray, a Haitian intellectual and aspiring writer — against the backdrop of Harlem's cabarets, buffet flats, and working-class neighborhoods in the years immediately following World War I.

The novel is structured in three parts comprising twenty-one chapters. Part One follows Jake's return to Harlem after deserting from the U.S. Army in Brest and spending two years in London's East End. Part Two shifts to the Pennsylvania Railroad dining-car circuit, where Jake meets Ray and their friendship deepens amid the harsh realities of railroad labor. Part Three returns to Harlem, where Jake reunites with Felice, his lost love from his first night back, and confronts a violent rivalry with his old Army friend Zeddy before deciding to leave for [[chicago]] with Felice.

McKay wrote the novel in a distinctive prose style that blended vernacular dialogue with lyrical descriptive passages, capturing the rhythms of Harlem street life, cabaret music, and working-class speech. The novel was controversial upon publication for its frank depictions of sexuality, nightlife, and the "low-life" of Harlem's Black Belt, drawing criticism from [[w-e-b-du-bois]] and other proponents of "respectable" Negro literature while earning praise from younger writers of the [[harlem-renaissance]].

## Plot Summary

### Part One: Jake's Return to Harlem

The novel opens with Jake Brown stoking coal on a freighter bound for New York, desperate to return to Harlem after two years away. Jake, a tall, brawny black longshoreman from [[brooklyn]], enlisted in 1917 hoping to fight the Germans, but was instead assigned to manual labor in [[brest]], hauling lumber for Army huts. Frustrated and denied the combat he sought, he deserted, made his way to [[london]], and worked as a docker in the East End. The Armistice brought an influx of Black men to London and a subsequent race riot, after which Jake felt an overwhelming homesickness for Harlem.

Upon arriving in Harlem, Jake immediately immerses himself in its nightlife. On his first night, he meets a beautiful brown-skinned sex worker at a cabaret on [[lenox-avenue]]. After spending the night together, she secretly returns his fifty dollars with a note reading "Just a little gift from a baby girl to a honey boy." Jake, elated, spends his days reconnecting with old friends — including Zeddy Plummer, a fellow veteran from Brest — exploring the cabarets, poolrooms, chitterling joints, and buffet flats that constitute the world of working-class Harlem. He searches for the mysterious girl who returned his money but cannot find her.

### Part Two: The Railroad and Ray

Jake takes a job as a third cook on a Pennsylvania Railroad dining car, working the New York-to-Pittsburgh run. In [[pittsburgh]], on Wiley Avenue — the Black workers' district — he meets Raymond, a Haitian waiter who is everything Jake is not: well-read, introspective, and haunted by intellectual ambitions. Ray is a graduate of Howard who has fallen into railroad work after his dreams of becoming a writer collided with racial and economic realities. He reads Dostoevsky, admires the Russian realists, and dreams of creating art from the raw life around him.

Their friendship forms the emotional and thematic core of the novel. Jake introduces Ray to the visceral pleasures of working-class life — cabarets, women, unmediated experience — while Ray gives Jake glimpses of a larger world of ideas, history, and racial consciousness. Ray tells Jake about the Haitian Revolution and the maroons of the Caribbean, stirring in Jake a new awareness of Black collective struggle. Meanwhile, Ray suffers under the brutal conditions of railroad work, the bedbugs, the tyrannical chef, and the psychological toll of being an intellectual trapped in manual labor. After a nervous collapse and a stint in the hospital, Ray decides to leave the railroad and go to sea, seeking escape from America's racial nightmare.

### Part Three: Reunion, Violence, and Departure

Jake returns to Harlem but falls seriously ill — a venereal relapse exacerbated by heavy drinking. His friend [[billy-biasse]] and landlady nurse him through the crisis. After recovering and moving to a better room arranged by Ray, Jake finally finds the girl from his first night back: Felice, a lively, independent young woman who had been working in Palm Beach and living with Zeddy in Jake's absence.

The reunion culminates in a night at a fashionable new cabaret on [[seventh-avenue]], where the Harlem elite — theater people, musicians, journalists, and society figures like [[madame-mulberry]] and [[maunie-whitewing]] — gather amid champagne and jazz. The celebrated bandleader [[james-reese-europe]] makes an appearance. But the night turns violent when Zeddy confronts Jake and Felice, pulling a razor. Jake, armed with a pistol given to him by Billy Biasse, forces Zeddy to back down. Zeddy publicly reveals that Jake deserted from the Army — a revelation that could bring the military police down on Jake at any moment.

Zeddy later returns to apologize, acknowledging he was "crazy-mad" and affirming that Jake was no worse a "slacker" than himself. But the damage is done. Felice persuades Jake that they must leave Harlem to protect him from the authorities. The novel ends with Jake and Felice deciding to flee together to [[chicago]], the next destination in their restless search for freedom and joy.

## Appearances

*Home to Harlem* is the **primary source text** for this wiki. Every other page in this corpus — from character studies of [[jake-brown]] and [[ray]] to analyses of [[harlem-nightlife-economy]] — draws directly from this novel. It was originally published by Harper & Brothers in 1928 and has been reprinted in numerous editions. The novel comprises three parts and twenty-one chapters, totaling approximately 72,000 words. Its narrative voice shifts between Jake's vernacular perspective and a more formal omniscient narration, with Ray's interior monologues providing the novel's most explicitly intellectual content.

## Significance

### Place in the Harlem Renaissance

*Home to Harlem* occupies a uniquely contested position in the [[harlem-renaissance]]. It was an immediate commercial success, going through five printings in its first two months and becoming the first novel by a Black American to reach the bestseller lists. Its success demonstrated that there was a substantial market for fiction about Black life written by Black authors — a finding that publishers of the era had long disputed.

Yet the novel's unvarnished portrait of working-class Harlem provoked fierce criticism from the Black intellectual establishment. [[w-e-b-du-bois]], the era's most influential Black leader, famously wrote in *The Crisis* that the novel made him feel "distinctly like taking a bath." Du Bois argued that McKay had pandered to white readers' prurient interest in Black "low-life" and had failed to present the "respectable" Negro — the educated, middle-class, morally upright Black citizen that Du Bois believed literature should hold up as a model.

This controversy crystallized a fundamental debate within the Harlem Renaissance over artistic freedom versus racial representation. Younger writers, including [[langston-hughes]], defended McKay's right to depict Black life in all its complexity, arguing that art should not be constrained by the politics of respectability. The debate echoed through subsequent decades and remains central to discussions of Black literature and the politics of representation.

### Working-Class Focus

What distinguished *Home to Harlem* from most previous African American novels was its centering of working-class Black life on its own terms. McKay's Harlem is not the "talented tenth" world of educated professionals and artists — though figures like Ray gesture toward that world — but the world of longshoremen, cooks, waiters, sex workers, and hustlers. The novel's settings — buffet flats, basement chitterling joints, poolrooms, cabarets — constitute a cartography of Black working-class pleasure and survival that had rarely been rendered in American fiction.

McKay's treatment of sexuality was equally unprecedented. His characters pursue pleasure openly, without shame or moralizing. Jake's erotic adventures are narrated with gusto rather than disapproval. Felice, a sex worker who is also resourceful, generous, and ultimately loyal, defies the era's stereotypes of the "fallen woman." This frankness was precisely what scandalized Du Bois and thrilled younger readers.

### Literary Innovation

McKay's prose style in *Home to Harlem* was a deliberate experiment in vernacular modernism. He rendered Black working-class speech phonetically — Jake's "mah" for "my," "ovah" for "over," "gwine" for "going" — not as minstrel caricature but as a serious attempt to capture the music and dignity of a living oral culture. The novel alternates between this vernacular register and passages of high lyrical prose, particularly in descriptions of Harlem streets and cabarets. This stylistic duality mirrors the novel's thematic interest in the relationship between the sensual and the intellectual, the "low" and the "high," embodied in the friendship between Jake and Ray.

## Creator: Claude McKay

[[claude-mckay]] (1889–1948) was a Jamaican-born poet, novelist, and key figure of the [[harlem-renaissance]]. Born Festus Claudius McKay in Sunny Ville, Clarendon Parish, Jamaica, he published his first volumes of poetry in Jamaican dialect before emigrating to the United States in 1912 to study at Tuskegee Institute and later Kansas State College. He moved to New York in 1914.

McKay's 1919 sonnet "If We Must Die" became a rallying cry against racial violence during the Red Summer of 1919 and established his reputation as a militant voice for Black resistance. During the 1920s, he lived in London, where he worked for the socialist journal *Workers' Dreadnought*, and later traveled through Europe and North Africa. *Home to Harlem* was written largely during his time in France and drew on his observations of working-class Black communities in New York, London, and Marseilles.

McKay followed *Home to Harlem* with two more novels: *Banjo* (1929), set among the Black waterfront workers of Marseilles, and *Banana Bottom* (1933), a novel of return set in Jamaica. His 1937 autobiography, *A Long Way from Home*, provides extensive context on the composition and reception of *Home to Harlem*. McKay spent his later years in Chicago and New York, converting to Catholicism in 1944. He died in Chicago in 1948.
