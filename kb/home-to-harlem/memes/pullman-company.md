# Pullman Company

The Pullman Palace Car Company (later the Pullman Company) was the manufacturer and operator of luxury [[sleeping-late|sleeping]] cars, [[dining-car|dining car]]s, and parlor cars that dominated American long-distance rail travel from the late nineteenth century through the mid-twentieth century. By the 1920s, Pullman was the largest single employer of Black workers in the United States, and its porters, cooks, and [[waiter|waiters]] — almost exclusively [[africa|Africa]]n American men — formed the backbone of the Black working class's upper economic tier.

## Overview

Founded by George Pullman in 1867, the company built its business on the innovation of the luxury [[sleeping-late|sleeping]] car — a rail car that converted from daytime s[[food-and-dining|eating]] to nighttime berths, served by uniformed porters who attended to passengers' every need. Pullman cars operated under contract with [[the-railroad|railroad]]s across the country, including the [[pennsylvania-railroad|Pennsylvania Railroad]], the [[new-york-city|New York]] Central, and the Santa Fe. The company also built a company town — Pullman, Illinois (now part of [[chicago|Chicago]]) — that became notorious for its paternalistic control of workers' lives.

Pullman's decision to hire Black men as porters, be[[gin-whisky|gin]]ning in the years after the Civil War, was a calculated one. Black labor was cheap, and the servile role of the porter — carrying bags, shining shoes, responding to passengers' whims — mapped onto the racial hierarchy of post-Reconstruction America. But the strategy had an unintended consequence: Pullman employment gave Black men access to stable wages, travel, and a national network of information and contacts that would prove crucial to the [[great-migration|Great Migration]].

## Role in the Novel

Though not named directly in *Home to [[harlem|Harlem]]*, the Pullman Company is the institutional context for the [[dining-car|dining-car]] scenes in which [[jake-brown|Jake]] and [[ray]] work as cook and [[waiter]]. The Pullman [[dining-car|dining car]] was a distinct workplace with its own hierarchy: the white chief steward at the top, Black cooks in the kitchen, Black [[waiter|waiters]] in the [[food-and-dining|dining]] room, and porters attending to [[sleeping-late|sleeping]]-car passengers. The confrontations between [[the-chef|the chef]] and his workers, the tips and tensions that structure the [[food-and-dining|dining]]-car chapters, and [[the-railroad|the railroad]]'s function as a conduit between cities ([[harlem|Harlem]], [[pittsburgh|Pittsburgh]], [[philadelphia|Philadelphia]]) all occur within the Pullman system.

## Historical Context

### Black Employment on Pullman

By the mid-1920s, Pullman employed approximately 12,000 Black workers, making it the single largest private employer of [[africa|Africa]]n Americans in the country. Porters earned roughly $60–$80 per month in wages plus tips — respectable pay in an era when most Black employment was confined to agriculture and domestic service. The job also conferred a degree of status within Black communities: the Pullman porter was a figure of cosmopolitanism, a man who had seen the country, who carried himself with the polish of service to wealthy white travelers.

The work was also grueling. Porters worked 400 hours per month (far exceeding the standard work week), slept in cramped quarters, and were subject to the whims of passengers who often called every Black porter "George" — after George Pullman — erasing individual identity into corporate function. The constant demand for deference, the low base pay supplemented by tips, and the racial hierarchy of [[the-railroad|the railroad]] created conditions that were ripe for labor organizing.

### The Brotherhood of Sleeping Car Porters

In 1925, [[asa-philip-randolph|A. Philip Randolph]] organized the Brotherhood of Sleeping Car Porters (BSCP), the first [[africa|Africa]]n American labor union to win a collective bargaining agreement with a major American corporation. The BSCP's twelve-year fight for recognition — Pullman resisted until 1937 — was a watershed in both labor and civil rights history. [[asa-philip-randolph|Randolph]] would later organize the 1963 March on [[washington|Washington]], and the BSCP's victory demonstrated that Black workers could successfully challenge corporate power through collective action.

While *[[home-to-harlem|Home to Harlem]]* does not ad[[fashion-and-style|dress]] union organizing directly, the novel's attentive depiction of [[food-and-dining|dining]]-car labor conditions — the racial hierarchy, the economic pressures, the solidarity and conflict among Black workers — documents the world that the BSCP sought to transform.
