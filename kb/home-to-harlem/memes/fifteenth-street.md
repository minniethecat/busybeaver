**Fifteenth Street** is a street in [[philadelphia|Philadelphia]] mentioned in [[claude-mckay|Claude McKay]]'s *Home to [[harlem|Harlem]]* as the location of a house where [[jake-brown|Jake]] visits during his travels outside [[harlem|Harlem]]. It appears in chapter 13 as part of the novel's comparative geography of Black urban life.

## Location

### Geography

Fifteenth Street in [[philadelphia|Philadelphia]] runs through the city's central neighborhoods. In the early twentieth century, the area around Fifteenth Street contained a mix of residential and commercial blocks, and parts of it were within [[philadelphia|Philadelphia]]'s historically Black neighborhoods.

### In the Novel's World

The street is introduced when a character suggests "something good right there in Fifteenth Street." (013-007) The phrase is suggestive — "something good" implies pleasure, perhaps illicit — and the street becomes a destination for [[jake-brown|Jake]] and his companions: "They went to a house in Fifteenth Street." (013-016)

## Significance to the Narrative

The house in Fifteenth Street is a social space where men "made a night of it," enjoying themselves in a private domestic setting. The narrator's description — "making a night of themselves, in a house in Fifteenth Street" (013-057) — captures the improvisational pleasure-seeking that defines [[jake-brown|Jake]]'s world outside [[harlem|Harlem]]. Unlike the [[cabaret-culture|cabarets]] and [[buffet-flat|buffet flats]] of [[harlem|Harlem]], the Fifteenth Street house is a temporary stop, part of a larger itinerary of pleasure across mid-Atlantic cities.

[[philadelphia|Philadelphia]], and Fifteenth Street within it, functions as part of the novel's geographic counterpoint: "In [[philadelphia|Philadelphia]] or [[washington|Washington]] he never went to a [[buffet-flat|buffet flat]]." (012-084) The house in Fifteenth Street is not a [[buffet-flat|buffet flat]] — it is something else, a private arrangement rather than the semi-public commerce of [[harlem|Harlem]]'s nightlife. The distinction matters for understanding the spectrum of Black social spaces the novel maps.

## From the Novel

McKay's prose rises to rhapsody over the Fifteenth Street revelers:

> Black lovers of life caught up in their own free native rhythm, threaded to a remote scarce-remembered past, celebrating the midnight hours in themselves, for themselves, of themselves, in a house in Fifteenth Street, Philadelphia.
>
> (013-057)

## Historical Context

[[philadelphia|Philadelphia]]'s Black population grew significantly during the Great Migration, and by the 1910s the city had a well-established Black community centered in the Seventh Ward. Fifteenth Street was near this area, and its mention in the novel reflects the real geography of Black social life in the city. Unlike [[harlem|Harlem]], where the concentration of Black residents created a dense entertainment economy of [[cabaret-culture|cabarets]] and rent parties, [[philadelphia|Philadelphia]]'s Black social life was more dispersed and domestically centered — a difference that McKay's characters register.
