**Tammany Hall** was the dominant Democratic Party political machine in [[new-york-city|New York City]], referenced in [[claude-mckay|Claude McKay]]'s *[[home-to-harlem|Home to Harlem]]* as the political force that protected [[the-baltimore|the Baltimore]] [[cabaret-culture|cabaret]] from police interference.

## In the Novel

Tammany Hall appears as a shadowy but powerful presence in [[harlem|Harlem]]'s nightlife economy. When [[the-baltimore|the Baltimore]] [[cabaret-culture|cabaret]] loses its license, the narrator explains that "the mass of [[harlem|Harlem]] held [[the-baltimore-cabaret|the Baltimore]] in high respect because (it was rumored and believed) it was protected by Tammany Hall." (008-003) This protection — however informal — had shielded the [[cabaret-culture|cabaret]] from the kind of vice raids that [[prohibition|Prohibition]]-era police regularly conducted on unprotected establishments.

The connection to Tammany Hall also speaks to the broader political economy of [[harlem|Harlem]]'s [[cabaret-culture|cabarets]] and [[speakeasy|speakeasies]]. [[the-baltimore|the Baltimore]] "had mortgages and policies in the best of the [[speakeasy]] places of [[black-belt|the Belt]]" (008-003), suggesting a web of financial and political relationships linking the world of Black entertainment to white political power.

## Quotations

The passage explaining [[the-baltimore-cabaret|the Baltimore]]'s protected status in [[harlem|Harlem]]'s nightlife economy:

> The Baltimore was not just an ordinary cabaret. It had mortgages and policies in the best of the speakeasy places of the Belt. And the mass of Harlem held the Baltimore in high respect because (it was rumored and believed) it was protected by Tammany Hall.
>
> (008-003)

## Historical Context

Tammany Hall (formally the Tammany Society) dominated [[new-york-city|New York City]] politics from the late eighteenth century through the early twentieth century. By the 1920s, under leaders like Charles F. Murphy, it maintained power through a network of ward-level organizations that traded services and jobs for votes. Its relationship to vice — tolerating gambling, prostitution, and illegal liquor in exchange for payoffs — was well known, and McKay's reference reflects the real-world understanding that [[harlem|Harlem]]'s nightlife operated under the machine's umbrella.
