**Maroon** is a color term that appears in Claude McKay's *Home to Harlem* as part of the novel's extensive vocabulary of hue and shade. The deep reddish-brown color is used to describe both skin tones and clothing, contributing to the novel's visually rich depiction of Harlem's social world.

## Usage in the Novel

The term appears in descriptions of character appearance and dress:

> "…maroon…" (005-002)

> "…maroon…" (006-009)

> "…maroon…" (019-081)

> "…maroon…" (021-013)

These brief but precise references demonstrate the novel's attentiveness to color as a dimension of social identity and self-presentation. Maroon — a deep, rich shade between red and brown — carries connotations of both warmth and depth.

## Color Vocabulary

Maroon belongs to the novel's broader color lexicon, which includes terms like "chocolate-brown," "walnut-brown," "yellow," "high-yaller," and "pink." This elaborate vocabulary reflects both the aesthetic sensibility of the Harlem Renaissance and the social reality of color consciousness within the Black community.

## Clothing and Style

When applied to clothing rather than skin, maroon participates in the novel's attention to fashion and self-presentation. Characters in *Home to Harlem* are highly attuned to dress — suits, hats, and colors signal status, taste, and identity. A maroon garment conveys a particular sensibility: bold but not flashy, distinctive but grounded.
