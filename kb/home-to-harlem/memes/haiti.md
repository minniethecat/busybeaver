## Location

Haiti is a Caribbean nation occupying the western portion of the island of Hispaniola. In the novel, it is referenced primarily through Ray's personal history and the extended discussion of Haitian history that takes place on the train to Pittsburgh.

## Significance in the Novel

Haiti serves as Ray's homeland and a crucial element of his identity. When asked if he is Negro, Ray responds: "Of course I'm Negro, but I was born in Haiti and the language down there is French" (010-048). This dual identity — Black and Haitian, French-speaking and American — shapes Ray's perspective throughout the novel.

> "Of course I'm Negro," the waiter said, "but I was born in Haiti and the language down there is French."
> — (010-048)

## Historical Context

Ray uses the train journey to Pittsburgh to educate Jake about Haitian history, specifically the Haitian Revolution and its leader Toussaint L'Ouverture. The narrative of Black slaves rising up and defeating their French masters to establish an independent Black republic serves as a powerful counternarrative to American racial oppression.

> Jake sat like a big eager boy and learned many facts about Haiti before the train reached Pittsburgh. He learned that the universal spirit of the French Revolution had reached and lifted up the slaves far away in that remote island; that Black Haiti's independence was more dramatic and picturesque than the United States' independence.
> — (010-051)

Ray tells Jake about Toussaint L'Ouverture, "that black slave and leader of the Haitian slaves," who "fought off the French and kept Haiti independent" (010-052)(010-054). The story carries both pride and tragedy: Ray notes that "the history of Haiti today might have been different" had Toussaint not been betrayed and captured by Napoleon's forces.

## Personal Connection

For Ray, Haiti represents both pride and loss. His father was "an official down there until the United States Marines took over" (010-089). The American occupation of Haiti (1915–1934) stripped his family of status and forced Ray into exile — a personal dimension of the imperial relationship between the United States and the Caribbean.

> When the World War Uncle Sam grabbed Haiti. My father was an official down there until the United States Marines took over.
> — (010-089)

## Themes

- **Black Independence**: Haiti stands as proof of Black self-governance
- **Diaspora Identity**: Ray embodies tension between Caribbean and African American identities
- **Imperialism and Race**: U.S. occupation mirrors domestic racial oppression
- **Historical Memory**: Ray's knowledge contrasts with historical amnesia imposed by American racism

## Appearances

Haiti is discussed primarily in Chapter X (The Railroad) and Chapter XI (Snowstorm in Pittsburgh), where Ray educates Jake about Haitian history. The Haitian waiter reappears in Chapter XI, hosting Jake at Wiley Avenue in Pittsburgh.
