**Seventh Avenue** is a major thoroughfare in [[harlem|Harlem]] that appears throughout [[claude-mckay|Claude McKay]]'s *Home to [[harlem|Harlem]]* as a space of promenading, social display, and neighborhood transformation. It is one of the novel's most visually rendered streets, associated with fashion, leisure, and the changing racial character of [[harlem|Harlem]].

## Location

### In the Novel's World

Seventh Avenue runs through central [[harlem|Harlem]], and [[jake-brown|Jake]]'s habitual route includes a "promenade on Seventh Avenue between 135th and 140th Streets." (002-004) The street is characterized by its "broad pavements" which are "colorful with promenaders." (019-035) The "lovely trees of Seventh Avenue were a vivid flame-green" in springtime (019-002), making it one of the novel's most aesthetically celebrated streetscapes.

## Significance to the Narrative

Seventh Avenue serves as [[harlem|Harlem]]'s social stage — a place to see and be seen. [[jake-brown|Jake]] "lingered along Seventh Avenue" (019-003) and strolls there "in a new nigger-brown suit" (019-033), using the avenue as a space of self-display. Couples "sauntered along Seventh Avenue, far from the rough environment" of the side streets. (020-007)

The avenue is also a marker of [[harlem|Harlem]]'s changing social geography. An older character laments: "Seventh Avenue done gone high-brown" (003-047), suggesting the street's transformation from working-class to aspirational. The "veneer of Seventh Avenue" (004-007) is contrasted with the more authentic, African atmosphere of [[the-congo|the Congo]] [[cabaret-culture|cabaret]] — the avenue represents the respectable, light-skinned, upwardly mobile face of [[harlem|Harlem]].

## Historical Context

Seventh Avenue, now known as Adam Clayton Powell Jr. Boulevard, was [[harlem|Harlem]]'s premier promenade during the 1920s. The "broad pavements" and tree-lined blocks made it a natural gathering and strolling space, where [[harlem|Harlem]] residents displayed their finest clothes on Sunday afternoons. McKay's rendering captures this culture of public self-fashioning while also registering its class and color tensions.

## Appearances

Seventh Avenue appears or is referenced in chapters 2–4 and 19–21. (002-004)–(021-002)

## Quotations

McKay's celebrated panorama of Seventh Avenue as a stage for Harlem's human diversity:

> The broad pavements of Seventh Avenue were colorful with promenaders. Brown babies in white carriages pushed by little black brothers wearing nice sailor suits. All the various and varying pigmentation of the human race were assembled there: dim brown, clear brown, rich brown, chestnut, copper, yellow, near-white, mahogany, and gleaming anthracite. Charming brown matrons, proud yellow matrons, dark nursemaids pulled a zigzag course by their restive little charges.
>
> (019-035)
