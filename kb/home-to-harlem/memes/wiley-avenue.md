# Wiley Avenue

Wiley Avenue is the main commercial thoroughfare of the Black neighborhood in [[pittsburgh|Pittsburgh]]'s Hill District, appearing in Chapter XI ("Snowstorm in Pittsburgh") of [[claude-mckay|Claude McKay]]'s *[[Home to Harlem]]*. It serves as the Pittsburgh counterpart to [[lenox-avenue|Lenox Avenue]] — another Black urban main street — but one defined by industrial grime rather than [[harlem|Harlem]]'s vivid nightlife.

## Location

### Geography

Wiley Avenue (historically Wylie Avenue) runs through the Hill District of [[pittsburgh|Pittsburgh]], the neighborhood that served as the cultural and residential center of Black Pittsburgh during the Great Migration. The Hill District, located just east of downtown Pittsburgh, was home to a growing Black population drawn by jobs in the steel mills and on the railroads — including the Pennsylvania Railroad, for which [[jake-brown|Jake]] works as a cook.

### In the Novel's World

McKay presents Wiley Avenue as a study in industrial monotony, in sharp contrast to the sensory richness of [[harlem|Harlem]]:

> He stepped off the bridge into a saloon. From there along a dull-gray street of grocery and fruit shops and piddling South-European children. Then he was on Wiley Avenue, the long, gray, uphill street.
> — (011-002)

The avenue's social life is shaped by industrial labor: "Brawny bronze men in coal-blackened and oil-spotted blue overalls shadowed the doorways of saloons, poolrooms, and little basement restaurants. The street was animated with dark figures going up, going down. Houses and men, women, and squinting cats and slinking dogs, everything seemed touched with soot and steel dust" (011-003). Where [[lenox-avenue|Lenox Avenue]] is defined by cabarets, fashion, and display, Wiley Avenue is defined by the evidence of work — coal, oil, soot, steel dust.

## Significance to the Narrative

Wiley Avenue represents the other Black urban experience — not the cultural capital of [[harlem|Harlem]] but the industrial labor of the Great Migration's northern destinations. [[jake-brown|Jake]]'s arrival on Wiley Avenue initiates a chapter that functions as a [[harlem|Harlem]]-in-miniature: he finds a "bouncing little chestnut-brown" girl in a basement eating-joint (011-004), meets the Haitian waiter at "the big Wiley Avenue poolroom" (011-011), and navigates the limited pleasures available to Black railroad men on layover.

The big Wiley Avenue poolroom is described as "quite different from the poolrooms in Harlem" — it is "a sort of social center for the railroad men and the more intelligent black workmen of the quarter," selling tobacco, stationery, and "pretty chocolate dolls and pictures of Negroid types" (011-011). The poolroom's hybrid function — part social club, part store, part post office — reflects the more constrained Black institutional landscape of industrial Pittsburgh compared to the specialized nightlife economy of [[harlem|Harlem]].

The railroad men's hatred of the Pittsburgh run is inseparable from their hatred of Wiley Avenue itself: "The railroad men hated the Pittsburgh run. They hated the town, they hated Wiley Avenue and their wretched free quarters that were in it" (011-014). The avenue becomes a metonym for the entire experience of Black industrial labor — the compulsory layover in a city that offers none of [[harlem|Harlem]]'s warmth or pleasure.

## Historical Context

Wylie Avenue was historically the main street of Pittsburgh's Hill District, which by the 1920s was one of the most significant Black urban communities in the North. The Hill District was a major destination for Black migrants from the South, drawn by employment in the steel industry and on railroads like the Pennsylvania Railroad. The avenue's poolrooms, saloons, and basement restaurants that McKay describes were real features of the neighborhood's commercial life, documented in photographs and oral histories of the period.

The Pittsburgh Courier, one of the most widely circulated Black newspapers in America (011-024), had its offices in the Hill District. The presence of the Courier — alongside the poolroom as social center — suggests a community with its own information networks and cultural institutions, even if the novel's characters experience it only as a place of transit and resentment.

## Appearances

Wiley Avenue appears in Chapter XI (Snowstorm in Pittsburgh). Key passages: [[jake-brown|Jake]]'s arrival on "the long, gray, uphill street" (011-002); the street's social geography of saloons, poolrooms, and basement restaurants (011-003); the Haitian waiter at the big Wiley Avenue poolroom (011-011); and the railroad men's hatred of Wiley Avenue (011-014).
