**Virginia Dare** is a brand of golden wine frequently mentioned in [[claude-mckay|Claude McKay]]'s 1928 novel *[[home-to-harlem|Home to Harlem]]*. It appears as a popular beverage in the [[cabaret-culture|cabarets]], [[buffet-flat|buffet flats]], and social gatherings of 1920s [[harlem|Harlem]].

## In the Novel

Virginia Dare wine is a recurring detail in the novel's social scenes. At a [[harlem|Harlem]] gathering, "a newly found brown had a glass of Virginia Dare before her" (007-087). In a [[pittsburgh|Pittsburgh]] scene, a character orders the drink by name: "'Virginia Dare'" (013-049), and "the woman brought two glasses of Virginia Dare and Ray ordered a third." (013-054)

The novel's most evocative description of the wine appears in the Sheba Palace scene, where McKay sketches a panorama of Harlem nightlife:

> Little white-filled glasses, little yellow-filled glasses, general guzzling of gin and whisky. Little saucy brown lips, rouged maroon, sucking up iced crème de menthe through straws, and many were sipping the golden Virginia Dare, in those days the favorite wine of the Belt.
>
> (019-081)

The wine's "golden" color is noted as part of its appeal, and its status as the "favorite wine of the Belt" — the novel's central cabaret district — underscores its cultural centrality. Virginia Dare appears across multiple chapters (VII, XIII, XIX), spanning both [[harlem|Harlem]] and [[pittsburgh|Pittsburgh]] settings, marking it as a shared touchstone of Black urban social life in the novel's world.

## Historical Note

Virginia Dare wine was a real commercial product of the early 20th century, produced by the Garrett & Company of North Carolina and named after the first English child born in the Americas (1587). Its inclusion in McKay's novel reflects the brand's genuine popularity in African American communities during the [[prohibition|Prohibition]] era, when wine was often sold as a sacramental or medicinal product. The brand's name — invoking an icon of early American colonial history — adds an ironic layer to its popularity in 1920s [[harlem|Harlem]].
