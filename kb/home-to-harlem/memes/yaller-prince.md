# Yaller Prince

A named figure in [[harlem|Harlem]]'s [[cabaret-culture|cabaret]] world whose nickname — "Yaller Prince" — condenses the novel's color politics into two words. "Yaller" (light-skinned) plus "Prince" (elevated status) equals a person whose social position derives from his complexion as much as his character.

## Role in the Novel

Yaller Prince appears as one of the named denizens of [[harlem|Harlem]]'s [[cabaret-culture|cabaret]] scene. He is referenced specifically as "a [[high-yaller]] entertainer" at the [[the-congo|Congo]] — the nightclub that serves as the novel's primary cabaret setting. [[zeddy-plummer|Zeddy Plummer]], describing the Congo's attractions to [[jake-brown|Jake]], singles him out: "Theah's a high-yaller entertainer there that I'se got a crush on, but she ain't nevah gived me a encouraging eye" (005-016). Notably, Zeddy's description uses feminine pronouns — "she" — suggesting Yaller Prince may be a female entertainer, or that McKay's treatment of gender around performance at the Congo is deliberately fluid.

[[jake-brown|Jake]]'s dismissive response — "I ain't much for the high-yallers after having been so much fed-up on the ofays" (005-022) — establishes both his preference for darker-skinned women and the color hierarchy that structures desire in the novel.

## Characterization

Like many of McKay's minor named characters, Yaller Prince exists primarily through his nickname — a [[harlem|Harlem]] street name that functions as both identification and social evaluation. The name itself tells the reader everything the novel thinks necessary: this is a light-skinned Black person whose desirability or status is encoded in skin color, and who occupies an elevated position within [[harlem|Harlem]]'s social hierarchy as a result.

The term "high-yaller" in the novel carries specific social meaning. [[miss-curdy|Miss Curdy]] is explicit about her distrust: "I know them from long experience. They never want to work. They're a lazy and shiftless lot. Want to be kept like women" (008-023). This valorization of dark-skinned Blackness over light-skinned — a recurring McKay theme — positions Yaller Prince within the novel's complex negotiation of intraracial color politics.

## Themes

Yaller Prince embodies the theme of **colorism** — the intraracial hierarchy based on skin tone that structured social relations in 1920s [[harlem|Harlem]]. His very name is a document of the period's pigmentocracy, where "yaller" was both a descriptor and a value judgment. The high-yaller entertainer occupies a privileged position in the Congo's social world — visible, desired, the object of [[zeddy-plummer|Zeddy]]'s admitted "crush" — but is also subject to the novel's critique of color-based status, voiced through [[miss-curdy|Miss Curdy]]'s dismissals and [[jake-brown|Jake]]'s preference for darker women.

The term "yaller" pervades the novel, appearing nearly fifty times. It marks the boundary between light and dark within Black [[harlem|Harlem]] and carries a volatile mix of desire, resentment, aspiration, and critique. [[gin-head-susy|Susy]] lies to [[miss-curdy|Miss Curdy]] about preferring "a black man" to "a yaller" (008-027), then immediately takes another "yaller" sweetie — revealing that stated preferences and actual behavior diverge under the pressure of desire.
