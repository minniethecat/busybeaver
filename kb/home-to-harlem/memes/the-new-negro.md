# The New Negro

Alain Locke's *The New Negro: An Interpretation* (1925) is the defining anthology of the [[Harlem Renaissance]] — a collection of fiction, poetry, essays, and visual art that announced the arrival of a self-confident, urbane, modern Black cultural consciousness. In *[[Home to Harlem]]*, [[ray|Ray]] purchases a copy on the railroad, his ownership of the anthology marking his connection to the intellectual currents of the era in contrast to [[jake-brown|Jake]]'s experiential, unlettered engagement with [[Harlem]] culture.

## Overview

Published by Albert and Charles Boni, *The New Negro* gathered work by over thirty Black writers and artists, including Langston Hughes, Zora Neale Hurston, Countee Cullen, Jean Toomer, [[claude-mckay|Claude McKay]], and Aaron Douglas (whose illustrations defined the volume's visual identity). Locke's introductory essay, "The New Negro," provided the era with its name and its framing narrative: the "Old Negro" — rural, Southern, submissive — was being replaced by the "New Negro" — urban, Northern, assertive, culturally sophisticated.

The anthology was both a literary event and a political intervention. By collecting the best of contemporary Black writing between hard covers, Locke made an implicit argument about the maturity of African American letters — that Black culture had arrived, that it deserved (and could command) serious critical attention, that it was not folkloric curiosity but modern art.

## Appearances

[[ray|Ray]] buys *The New Negro* alongside "weekly Negro news magazines" at a railroad stop:

> Ray bought some weekly Negro news magazines and a copy of Alain Locke's "The New Negro." (011-024)

The pairing is significant. The "weekly Negro news magazines" connect [[ray|Ray]] to the Black public sphere — the network of newspapers (the *[[chicago|Chicago]] Defender*, the *[[pittsburgh|Pittsburgh]] Courier*, the *Amsterdam News*) that constituted what Benedict Anderson would later call the "Black counter-public." *The New Negro* connects him to the elite literary expression of that same public — the high-cultural face of the same community whose vernacular life [[jake-brown|Jake]] inhabits directly.

[[ray|Ray]]'s purchase of the anthology while working as a Pullman waiter — a job that required him to perform servility for white passengers — creates a sharp irony. He is both a member of the "New Negro" intellectual vanguard and a railway servant, reading about Black cultural modernity while working in one of the most racially stratified occupations in America. The contradiction is not resolved; it is the condition of [[ray|Ray]]'s existence.

## Significance

**[[ray|Ray]]'s Divided Consciousness**: [[ray|Ray]] reads *The New Negro* alongside [[sappho-daudet|Sappho (Daudet)]] — European naturalism and African American modernism in the same satchel. This dual reading list maps the divided consciousness of the 1920s Black intellectual: educated in European traditions, increasingly committed to Black cultural production, at home in neither.

**The Anthology's Absence from [[jake-brown|Jake]]'s World**: [[jake-brown|Jake]] never sees *The New Negro*, never knows it exists. The anthology circulates in a [[harlem|Harlem]] — the [[harlem|Harlem]] of salons, publishing houses, and university-educated intellectuals — that barely intersects with [[jake-brown|Jake]]'s [[harlem|Harlem]] of [[cabaret-culture|cabarets]], docks, and [[buffet-flat|buffet flats]]. McKay's novel, by focusing entirely on [[jake-brown|Jake]]'s [[harlem|Harlem]] and relegating the Renaissance's official culture to a single offhand reference, makes an implicit argument about which [[harlem|Harlem]] matters more.

**McKay's Ambivalent Relationship**: [[claude-mckay|Claude McKay]] contributed to Locke's anthology (his poem "The White House" appeared in it), but he was profoundly ambivalent about the Renaissance's middle-class leadership and its preference for "respectable" representations of Black life. *Home to [[harlem|Harlem]]* — with its frank sexuality, working-class protagonists, and vernacular language — was precisely the kind of book that embarrassed Locke and Du Bois. [[ray|Ray]]'s ownership of the anthology thus functions as an ironic self-reference: McKay placing his own work in the hands of a character who represents a path (intellectual commitment to Black culture) that McKay respected but did not entirely share.

## Creator

Alain Locke (1885–1954) was a philosopher, educator, and the intellectual architect of the [[Harlem Renaissance]]. A Harvard graduate (and the first Black Rhodes Scholar), Locke taught at Howard University and used his position to advocate for African American art as a vehicle for racial advancement. His philosophy of cultural pluralism — the idea that American democracy required the full expression of its constituent ethnic cultures — provided the intellectual framework for the Renaissance. His editorial shaping of *The New Negro* was his most consequential act, establishing the canon and the terms of debate for an entire generation of Black artists and writers.
