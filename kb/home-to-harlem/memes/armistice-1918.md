# Armistice of 1918

The Armistice of November 11, 1918 — the ceasefire that ended combat operations in [[world-war-i|World War I]] — serves as the historical hinge of *[[Home to Harlem]]*'s opening chapters, finding [[jake-brown|Jake Brown]] in [[london|London]] and catalyzing the chain of desertion, [[migration]], and desire that launches the novel's narrative.

## Overview

The Armistice ended four years of industrialized warfare that had killed approximately 20 million people, redrawn the map of Europe, and mobilized colonial subjects — including nearly 400,000 [[africa|Africa]]n American soldiers — into the conflict. For Black Americans, the war had been freighted with expectations: service overseas was understood by many as a claim to full citizenship at home, and the postwar racial violence of 1919 (the "Red Summer") would cruelly betray those expectations.

[[jake-brown|Jake]]'s war experience complicates this narrative. He enlisted expecting to fight but was assigned to labor — "He had enlisted to fight. For was he not a Negro, and wasn't his chance of fighting at that time good?" (001-011). Instead, [[jake-brown|Jake]] spent the war "taking care of some mules" (001-019), a detail that registers the U.S. military's systematic channeling of Black soldiers into service roles rather than combat.

## Context

### Jake's Wartime Arc

McKay's treatment of [[jake-brown|Jake]]'s war experience is deliberately compressed — a flashback within the opening chapter — but its implications structure the entire novel:

| Phase | Location | [[jake-brown|Jake]]'s Status | Significance |
|-------|---------|--------------|-------------|
| Enlistment | [[virginia|Virginia]] | Eager volunteer | Expectation: combat as path to citizenship |
| Service | [[france|France]] | Mule handler | Reality: Black soldiers consigned to labor |
| Armistice | [[london|London]] | Celebrating | The war's end as liberation — "On New Year's Eve, 1919, [[jake-brown|Jake]] went to a monster dance" (001-025) |
| Desertion | [[london|London]] | AWOL | Rejection of military authority; be[[gin-whisky|gin]]ning of [[jake-brown|Jake]]'s autonomous [[migration]] |
| Return | Freighter → [[harlem|Harlem]] | Stowaway/cook | The opening scene: "Take me home to [[harlem|Harlem]], Mister Ship!" (001-008) |

### The Armistice as Narrative Device

McKay uses the Armistice as a precise temporal anchor that does several things simultaneously. It establishes [[jake-brown|Jake]]'s generation as the war generation — young Black men whose coming of age coincided with global conflict. It creates a clear before/after structure: pre-Armistice [[jake-brown|Jake]] was a soldier (nominally) in a white army; post-Armistice [[jake-brown|Jake]] is a deserter charting his own course. And it situates the novel's [[harlem|Harlem]] present (the mid-1920s) against the war's immediate aftermath, suggesting that the [[pleasure-seeking]] intensity of the novel's social world is partly a response to having survived mass death.

> The Armistice found him there. On New Year's Eve the big black fellows from America and the West Indies gave a party. (001-025)

"The Armistice found him" — the passive construction is telling. [[jake-brown|Jake]] does not seek the war's end; it discovers him, already in [[london|London]], already drifting away from military discipline. The verb "found" transforms a world-historical event into a personal milestone.

## Key Details

[[jake-brown|Jake]]'s [[london|London]] after the Armistice is a world of interracial working-class sociability. "On New Year's Eve, 1919, [[jake-brown|Jake]] went to a monster dance with some of his pals at a big club in Drury Lane" (001-025) — the Black American and [[west-india|West Indian]] soldiers party alongside white working-class [[london|London]]ers, a brief moment of cross-racial proletarian solidarity that contrasts sharply with the segregated America [[jake-brown|Jake]] returns to.

The Armistice also sets up [[jake-brown|Jake]]'s romantic consciousness. It is in [[london|London]], after the war's end, that [[jake-brown|Jake]] "saw a big battle staged between the white and [[race-and-color|color]]ed boxers" (001-026) — the sublimation of war into sport, combat into entertainment, a pattern that will repeat throughout the novel's treatment of violence.

## Aftermath

The Armistice's most consequential narrative effect is [[jake-brown|Jake]]'s decision to desert and return to [[harlem|Harlem]]. The war gave [[jake-brown|Jake]] a reason to leave [[virginia|Virginia]]; the peace gave him a reason not to return. His desertion is less a political act than an existential one — he simply chooses [[harlem|Harlem]] over the army, [[pleasure-seeking|pleasure]] over discipline, Black space over white authority. The novel's refusal to moralize this choice ([[jake-brown|Jake]] is never punished for desertion) is characteristic of McKay's unsentimental treatment of his protagonist's autonomy.
