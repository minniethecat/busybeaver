# Speakeasy

An illegal drinking establishment operating during the [[prohibition|Prohibition]] era (1920–1933), when the manufacture, sale, and transportation of [[gin-whisky|alcohol]] were banned in the United States. In 1920s [[harlem|Harlem]], the speakeasy economy underpinned virtually all nightlife — [[cabaret-culture|cabarets]], [[buffet-flat|buffet flats]], and private clubs all operated as speakeasies.

## Definition

The term "speakeasy" (also "speak-easy" or "blind pig") referred to any illicit venue where patrons could purchase and consume [[gin-whisky|alcohol]]ic beverages during [[prohibition|Prohibition]]. Speakeasies ranged from rough basement joints to luxurious clubs with elaborate décor, entertainment, and selective admission. The name itself came from the practice of speaking quietly ("easy") about such establishments to avoid detection by law enforcement. (008-003)

## Cultural Context

### Harlem Renaissance Context

[[prohibition|Prohibition]] created the economic conditions for [[harlem|Harlem]]'s nightlife boom. Because [[gin-whisky|alcohol]] was illegal nationwide, the speakeasy economy leveled certain racial hierarchies — Black entrepreneurs could compete in the illicit liquor trade on more equal terms, and white customers had to come to [[harlem|Harlem]] to drink, creating cross-racial economic and social dynamics that would have been unimaginable in legal commerce.

However, [[prohibition|Prohibition]] enforcement was racially unequal. White-owned speakeasies in midtown Manhattan often operated with police protection purchased through Tammany Hall connections, while Black venues in [[harlem|Harlem]] were more vulnerable to raids — as dramatized in [[the-baltimore-cabaret|the Baltimore]]'s closure.

### In the Novel

McKay's [[harlem|Harlem]] is a world where [[gin-whisky|alcohol]] flows freely despite [[prohibition|Prohibition]] — or perhaps because of it. The "speakeasy places of [[harlem|the Belt]]" are where [[the-baltimore-cabaret|the Baltimore]] "had mortgages and policies" (008-003), showing how the [[cabaret-culture|cabaret]] economy was interwoven with the illegal liquor trade.

[[madame-suarez-buffet-flat|Madame Suarez's]] venue is described as a "speakeasy" (008-009), though its [[buffet-flat|buffet flat]] form places it in a more exclusive category. The narrator's Chapter XIII taxonomy of [[harlem|Harlem]] nightlife explicitly distinguishes "the low-down speakeasy customers" (013-098) from other social types, showing the speakeasy as the entry-level tier of the nightlife economy.

Gin — "cases o' [[gin-whisky|gin]]" (006-029) — appears as the default liquor of working-class [[harlem|Harlem]] speakeasies, while champagne flows at [[madame-suarez-buffet-flat|Madame Suarez's]] more elevated establishment (008-012). The drink itself marks class distinctions within the speakeasy world.

## Key Passages

> It had mortgages and policies in the best of the speakeasy places of the Belt. (008-003)

This line reveals the economic integration of [[harlem|Harlem]] nightlife: [[the-baltimore-cabaret|the Baltimore]] is not just a [[cabaret-culture|cabaret]] but a financial entity with investments across the speakeasy network. [[prohibition|Prohibition]] made liquor a commodity that could be leveraged, insured, and used as collateral.

> The low-down speakeasy customers; the cabaret babies; the stylish Lost Generation'ers from downtown. (013-098)

McKay's narrator categorizes speakeasy patrons as a distinct social type — distinct from [[cabaret-culture|cabaret]]-goers and the downtown white crowd — suggesting a hierarchy within the underground drinking world itself.

## Historical Significance

The speakeasy era transformed American social life. [[prohibition|Prohibition]], intended to eliminate [[gin-whisky|alcohol]] consumption, instead drove drinking underground and made it glamorous. Speakeasies in [[harlem|Harlem]] were particularly significant because they created spaces where racial boundaries were fluid in ways impossible in legal public accommodations. Women drank in public alongside men for the first time in American history, and [[jazz]] — the music of speakeasies — became the soundtrack of a generation. When [[prohibition|Prohibition]] ended in 1933, the speakeasy economy collapsed, but its cultural legacy — the nightclub, the cocktail, the [[jazz]] club — survived and became permanent features of American urban life.
