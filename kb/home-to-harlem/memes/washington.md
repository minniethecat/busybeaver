**Washington** (Washington, D.C.) appears in [[claude-mckay|Claude McKay]]'s *Home to [[harlem|Harlem]]* primarily through the recollections and judgments of characters who have lived there. It represents the world of respectable Black society — Congressmen, educated Negroes, Howard University — set against [[harlem|Harlem]]'s raw, pleasure-seeking working class.

## Location

### Geography

Washington, D.C. is the capital of the United States, located on the Potomac River between Maryland and Virginia. By the early twentieth century, it had a substantial Black middle class centered around Howard University and the U Street corridor.

### In the Novel's World

Washington is invoked as a place where "respectable colored people lived" — a city of "Congressmen" and the "Negro university." (006-051)(007-155)(010-085) It is not a place the novel visits directly; rather, it exists in the dialogue and memory of characters who measure [[harlem|Harlem]] against its standards. "There was no [[harlem|Harlem]] full a niggers," one character says of Washington, framing the city as the antithesis of [[harlem|Harlem]]'s disorderly vitality. (007-155)

## Significance to the Narrative

Washington functions as an implied foil to [[harlem|Harlem]]'s nightlife economy. Characters invoke the capital to establish social credibility — or to debunk it. The "Negro university at Washington" (Howard) is mentioned dismissively: a character "haven't even finished there." (010-085) This registers the distance between educated Black Washington and the uncredentialed world of [[jake-brown|Jake]] and his circle.

The capital also appears in recollections of domestic labor. "One morning in Washington the iceman forgot to supply ice," begins a story told by one of the women, connecting Washington to the routines of domestic service. (012-078)(012-080) In [[philadelphia|Philadelphia]] or Washington, the narrator notes, [[jake-brown|Jake]] "never went to a [[buffet-flat|buffet flat]]," suggesting the cities' different social codes. (012-084)

## A City of Contrast

Washington is paired with other cities in the novel's comparative geography of Black life. "In [[philadelphia|Philadelphia]] or Washington he never went to a [[buffet-flat|buffet flat]]." (012-084) The city appears in a list with Charleston, Richmond, and "even Washington, DC" — the "even" marking it as the most respectable of the group, the furthest from [[harlem|Harlem]]'s sensuality. (013-022) When [[jake-brown|Jake]] reflects on his travels, "Baltimore, [[pittsburgh|Pittsburgh]] and Washington" appear together: "More than enough of the bar-room and beginning to go elsewhere." (018-002) The implication is that Washington, too, was part of a circuit eventually exhausted.

## From the Novel

Miss Curdy, a veteran of sporting life, brushes aside social pretension with the authority of someone who has seen it all:

> I lived in Washington and had Congressmen for my friends — foop! Why you can get in with the top-crust crowd at any swell ball in Harlem. All you need is clothes and the coin.
>
> (006-051)

## Historical Context

By the 1910s, Washington, D.C. had the largest urban Black population in the United States. Howard University, founded in 1867, was the nation's premier Black institution of higher learning. The city's Black elite — including congressmen, lawyers, and educators — formed what W.E.B. Du Bois called the "Talented Tenth." This social world, with its church socials, fraternal orders, and institutional respectability, is the Washington that McKay's characters invoke and implicitly reject in favor of [[harlem|Harlem]]'s [[jazz]] clubs and [[cabaret-culture|cabarets]].
