**Claude McKay** (1889–1948) was a [[jamaica|Jamaica]]n-American poet, novelist, and essayist, recognized as one of the central figures of the [[harlem-renaissance|Harlem Renaissance]]. His novel *Home to [[harlem|Harlem]]*, published in 1928 by Harper & Brothers, was the first novel by a Black American to become a bestseller.

## Role in the Novel

McKay is the author of *Home to [[harlem|Harlem]]*, credited on the volume's title page. (P01-001) His name appears in the colophon identifying him as the author (023-004) and within the novel narrative (021-151). The novel represents McKay's literary exploration of [[harlem|Harlem]]'s working-class life, drawing on his own experiences living in the neighborhood after arriving from [[jamaica|Jamaica]].

## Biography

Claude McKay was born Festus Claudius McKay in Sunny Ville, [[jamaica|Jamaica]], in 1889. He published his first poetry collections in [[jamaica|Jamaica]] before moving to the United States to study at Tuskegee Institute and Kansas State College. He settled in [[harlem|Harlem]] in 1914 and became a central voice of the New Negro movement, publishing militant sonnets including "If We Must Die" (1919). His works include the poetry collections *[[harlem|Harlem]] Shadows* (1922) and the novels *Home to [[harlem|Harlem]]* (1928), *Banjo* (1929), and *Banana Bottom* (1933).

## Historical/Cultural Context

McKay's *Home to [[harlem|Harlem]]* was both celebrated and criticized upon publication. It won the Harmon Gold Award for Literature but drew criticism from some Black intellectuals, including [[w-e-b-du-bois|W. E. B. Du Bois]], who objected to its frank portrayal of [[harlem|Harlem]] nightlife and working-class sexuality. The novel's unvarnished depiction of [[rent-party|rent parties]], [[cabaret-culture|cabarets]], poolrooms, and street life reflected McKay's commitment to representing the full spectrum of Black urban experience rather than only its respectable middle class.

McKay's literary [[fashion-and-style|style]] in *Home to [[harlem|Harlem]]* employs dialect, [[jazz]] rhythms, and a documentary-like attention to the sensory texture of [[harlem|Harlem]] life. His portrayal of characters like [[jake-brown|Jake Brown]], [[zeddy-plummer|Zeddy Plummer]], and [[gin-head-susy|Susy]] established a model for urban Black fiction that influenced later writers.

## Themes

McKay's work in *Home to [[harlem|Harlem]]* engages with [[migration]] — both the [[great-migration|Great Migration]] of [[africa|Africa]]n Americans from the South and the Caribbean diaspora to [[new-york-city|New York]] — as well as sexuality, class divisions within Black communities, and the tension between artistic expression and political respectability. His use of vernacular speech and his sympathetic portrayal of working-class characters were part of a broader project to expand the range of Black literary representation.
