**[[pullman-company|Pullman]] porters** were [[africa|Africa]]n American [[the-railroad|railroad]] workers employed by the [[pullman-company|Pullman Company]] to serve passengers in [[sleeping-late|sleeping]] cars. In [[claude-mckay|Claude McKay]]'s *[[home-to-harlem|Home to Harlem]]*, [[pullman-company|Pullman]] porters appear as members of [[harlem|Harlem]]'s diverse working-class community, representing one of the key occupational groups that shaped Black urban life.

## Definition

[[pullman-company|Pullman]] porters were attendants on [[pullman-company|Pullman]] [[sleeping-late|sleeping]] cars, responsible for passenger service, luggage, and berth preparation. The [[pullman-company|Pullman Company]], founded by George [[pullman-company|Pullman]], became one of the largest employers of [[africa|Africa]]n Americans in the United States. Porters worked long hours for low wages but the job conferred a degree of middle-class respectability and connected them to broader networks of Black life across the country.

## Cultural Context

### In the Novel

McKay includes [[pullman-company|Pullman]] porters among the varied occupational groups that populate [[harlem|Harlem]]'s streets. In a panoramic description of [[lenox-avenue|Lenox Avenue]] at dusk, porters are listed alongside other workers: "Longshoremen in overalls with hooks, [[pullman-company|Pullman]] porters holding their bags, [[waiter|waiters]], elevator boys." (011-008) The image captures the diversity of [[harlem|Harlem]]'s laboring class and the porter's distinct professional identity — signaled by the bags they carry.

Earlier in the novel, two [[pullman-company|Pullman]] porters enter a [[food-and-dining|dining]]-car during the [[waiter|waiters]]' meal on the [[the-railroad|train]] from [[philadelphia|Philadelphia]], provoking the second cook's joke: "Here is the chambermaids." (013-051) The brief scene illustrates the porter's ambiguous status — uniformed and respectable, yet subject to the same racial mockery as other Black workers.

The [[pullman-company|Pullman]] porter was also integral to the [[great-migration|Great Migration]], as porters distributed Black newspapers like the *[[chicago|Chicago]] Defender* throughout the South, spreading information about opportunities in northern cities.
