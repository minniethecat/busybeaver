**Connor's Cabaret** is one of the Harlem nightlife venues name-checked in Claude McKay's *Home to Harlem*. Alongside Barron's, Leroy's, and Goldgraben's, Connor's forms part of the novel's detailed topography of cabarets that defined Harlem's entertainment district during the Prohibition era.

## In the Novel

Connor's appears early in the novel's survey of Harlem nightlife, listed among the cabarets that continued to thrive:

> "All the old cabarets were going still. Connor's w…" (004-002)

> "Connor's…" (004-002)

The adjective "old" applied to Connor's suggests it was an established institution — one that had survived the changes sweeping through Harlem's entertainment scene. The novel presents Connor's as part of a competitive landscape where cabarets vied for the patronage of Harlem residents and downtown visitors alike.

## Nightlife Competition

The novel depicts a dynamic cabaret economy where venues compete for customers:

> "…was leading all the Negro cabarets a cruel dance." (004-004)

Connor's operates within this competitive environment, where a cabaret's success depended on its music, its liquor, and its ability to attract a fashionable crowd.
