A **[[boarding-house|boarding house]]** is a residential building where individual rooms are rented to tenants, typically with shared bathrooms, kitchens, and common areas. In 1920s [[harlem|Harlem]], boarding houses were a dominant form of working-class housing, accommodating the neighborhood's growing population of laborers, [[the-railroad|railroad]] workers, domestic servants, and recent [[migration|migrants]] from the [[south|South]].

## Definition

A boarding house in the world of *Home to [[harlem|Harlem]]* is distinct from a private apartment or family home. Tenants, known as [[the-lodger|lodgers]], rent individual furnished rooms, usually on a weekly or monthly basis. Cooking facilities, if available, are shared, and the boarding house keeper — often a woman — may also provide meals for additional payment.

The boarding house serves a population that cannot afford or does not need a private [[apartment]]: single men and women, temporary workers, and recent arrivals to [[harlem|Harlem]] who have not yet established permanent residences. In the novel, the boarding house appears as a site of both community and transience — a space where lives intersect briefly before diver[[gin-whisky|gin]]g.

## In the Novel

Boarding houses appear at multiple points in *Home to [[harlem|Harlem]]*, functioning as essential infrastructure for the novel's mobile, casually employed characters. One character is identified as "the house man at the boarding house where she cooked" (006-026), indicating the gendered division of labor within these establishments: women as cooks and keepers, men as house men or resident workers.

The boarding house [[mrs-grimes|Mrs. Grimes]] operates is a more specific type: what the novel frames as a [[buffet-flat]], but which functionally operates as a boarding house with additional social dimensions. As one character reports, "It looked so much like a comfortable boarding house where everybody was cheerful" (013-022), blurring the line between residential boarding and the [[buffet-flat|buffet flat]]'s party culture.

The lodger experience is rendered with vivid intimacy. Characters are "gentlemen lodgers" (017-015) in the keeper's eyes, and the relationship between keeper and lodger carries implicit boundaries: "I ain't going to dip mah nose too deep in a lodger's affairs" (017-023) marks the careful distance maintained even in close quarters. [[the-lodger|Lodgers]] include working men such as "elevator runners and another lodger, a porter who worked on Ellis Island" (017-063), reflecting the occupational range of boarding house tenants.

The boarding house also serves as a theater for communal observation. When a dramatic event occurs — a street fight, a police raid — "those lodgers whose windows gave on the street" (020-048) become a collective audience, and "the women lodgers cackled gayly over the novel" (020-049) events unfolding below.

## Historical Context

Boarding houses proliferated in [[harlem|Harlem]] during the [[great-migration|Great Migration]], as the neighborhood's population swelled beyond the capacity of its existing housing stock. Landlords converted single-family brownstones into rooming houses to maximize rental income, and the boarding house became a standard feature of [[harlem|Harlem]]'s residential landscape.

For Black tenants, boarding houses offered particular advantages: they typically required no long-term lease, no references from previous white landlords, and no large deposits — barriers that excluded many Black renters from conventional apartment buildings. The boarding house was thus both a necessity produced by discrimination in the formal housing market and a flexible institution adapted to the economic precarity of working-class Black life.

The boarding house keeper — usually a Black woman — occupied an ambiguous social position: entrepreneurial and independent, yet dependent on the unpredictable incomes of her transient tenants. Her work blurred the lines between domestic labor, property management, and social work, and she was often a de facto community anchor within the boarding house's microcosm of [[harlem|Harlem]] society.
