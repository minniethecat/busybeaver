# World War I

World War I (1914–1918) is the historical event that frames the opening of *[[Home to Harlem]]*, shaping [[jake-brown|Jake Brown]]'s trajectory from eager enlistee to disillusioned deserter and setting the novel's postwar [[harlem|Harlem]] in motion.

## Overview

When the United States declared war on Germany in 1917, [[jake-brown|Jake]] was working as a [[longshoreman]] in Virginia. "When America declared war upon Germany in 1917 he was a longshoreman" (001-009). Like many African American men, he enlisted with expectations of combat and the citizenship rights that military service promised — but was instead assigned to a labor battalion handling mules.

The war mobilized approximately 400,000 African American soldiers, the vast majority of whom were channeled into service and labor roles rather than combat. [[jake-brown|Jake]]'s experience — "He had enlisted to fight" (001-011) only to find himself tending animals — registers this systematic exclusion.

## Context

### The War in the Novel

McKay uses the war sparingly but precisely. It appears as memory and backstory rather than direct action: [[jake-brown|Jake]]'s wartime experience is compressed into the opening chapter's flashback. The war's presence is felt more through its aftermath — the draft dodgers still being hunted in [[harlem|Harlem]], the freighter stokers crossing the Atlantic, the "colored and white soldiers" whose fights in France (006-063) foreshadow racial tensions at home.

### Racial Dimensions

> During the World War Uncle Sam grabbed Haiti. (010-089)

This brief reference — spoken by a [[waiter]] discussing global politics — connects the war to American imperialism in the Caribbean. For Black characters in the novel, the war is inseparable from questions of race, citizenship, and colonial power.

## Aftermath

The war's end in November 1918 finds [[jake-brown|Jake]] in [[london|London]], where the Armistice celebration becomes the occasion for his break with military authority. The novel's [[harlem|Harlem]] of the mid-1920s — with its cabarets, its migrants from the South and the Caribbean, its tense pleasures — is partly the world the war made: a Black metropolis shaped by the Great Migration that the war accelerated.
