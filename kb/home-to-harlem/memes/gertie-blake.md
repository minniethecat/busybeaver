**Gertie Blake** is a minor character in [[claude-mckay|Claude McKay]]'s *Home to [[harlem|Harlem]]*, appearing in chapter 9 as part of [[rose-congo|Rose]]'s social circle. Her brief presence illuminates the social dynamics and gender tensions within [[rose-congo|Rose]]'s world.

## Role in the Novel

Gertie Blake appears in three brief references during chapter 9, which focuses on [[rose-congo|Rose]] and her relationships with men. Her role is to color the social backdrop of [[rose-congo|Rose]]'s room and to provide a narrative pivot point involving mistaken identity and jealousy.

## Key Scenes

Gertie Blake is first mentioned when someone asks [[rose-congo|Rose]] about a visitor: "Yep. It was only Gertie Blake." (009-004) The dismissive "only" suggests Gertie is a regular, unremarkable presence in [[rose-congo|Rose]]'s social world. However, the visitor had in fact not been Gertie Blake — "It had been a man, a stranger." (009-005) This mistaken identity is significant to the chapter's exploration of [[rose-congo|Rose]]'s romantic entanglements.

Later, Gertie Blake is present when [[rose-congo|Rose]] is "telling something that made them laugh," with "laughter in the room." (009-019) She functions as part of the social audience in [[rose-congo|Rose]]'s world, contributing to the lively, gossipy atmosphere of the boarding house.

## Themes

Gertie Blake's minor role illustrates the dense social networks of [[harlem|Harlem]]'s boarding-house culture, where visitors, neighbors, and friends circulate through private rooms carrying news and gossip. Her mistaken identity as [[rose-congo|Rose]]'s visitor also touches on the chapter's concern with secrecy, jealousy, and sexual rivalry — when a male visitor is disguised as a female friend, the everyday becomes fraught with suspicion.
