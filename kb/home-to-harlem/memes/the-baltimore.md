## Overview

The Baltimore is a fictional Harlem cabaret that serves as one of the most significant settings in Claude McKay's *Home to Harlem*. It is the venue where Jake first encounters the "little lost brown" -- the cabaret singer Rose -- and the site of a dramatic police raid that exemplifies the precariousness of nightlife under Prohibition.

> The blazing lights of the Baltimore were put out and the entrance was barred. (008-002)

## Jake and the Baltimore

Jake is drawn repeatedly to the Baltimore, where the atmosphere of music, dancing, and sensuality offers an escape from the hardships of working-class life. The cabaret becomes central to his emotional world, particularly through his relationship with Rose, the singer he meets there.

> Jake went down to the Baltimore. No sign of his honey girl and friend. (004-008)

His attachment to the cabaret and to Rose defines a significant arc in the narrative. Eventually, however, his feelings sour as the relationship turns fraught.

> Then he grew to hate that cabaret and stopped going there. (005-002)

## The Raid of the Baltimore

Chapter 8 of the novel is dedicated entirely to a dramatic police raid on the Baltimore, recounted under the chapter heading "The Raid of the Baltimore." The raid represents the collision between Harlem's underground nightlife economy and the enforcement of Prohibition laws.

> Sensational had happened in the Baltimore. The police had not, on a certain night, made a raid. (008-003)

Jake happens to be present during the raid, and the event exposes the corruption and contradictions of the speakeasy system, where the boss of the Baltimore is ultimately indicted as an accessory.

> The indictment of the boss of the Baltimore as accessory to the speakeasy crime. (008-038)

## Patronage and Atmosphere

The Baltimore attracts a diverse clientele, including wealthy white patrons drawn to Harlem's nightlife. The cabaret is portrayed as a space where money flows freely and where racial and class boundaries are both transgressed and reinforced.

> Money seemed worthless to the people who filled the Baltimore. Sometimes they were accompanied by white patrons. (008-033)

The cabaret singer at the Baltimore, a "shiny coffee-colored" woman, embodies the intersection of entertainment, sexuality, and labor that characterizes the cabaret scene, and her relationship with Jake drives much of the novel's personal drama.

> The cabaret singer, a shiny coffee-colored woman. (004-012)
