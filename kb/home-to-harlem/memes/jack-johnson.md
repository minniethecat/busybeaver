# Jack Johnson

John Arthur "Jack" Johnson (1878–1946) was the first Black heavyweight boxing champion of the world, holding the title from 1908 to 1915. In [[claude-mckay|Claude McKay]]'s *Home to [[harlem|Harlem]]*, Johnson is never a speaking character but appears four times across two chapters as a cultural touchstone — his name invoked by working-class Black characters as the ultimate measure of Black celebrity, physical power, and the complicated relationship between [[race-and-color|race]], fame, and respectability.

## Role in the Novel

Jack Johnson's name surfaces twice in Chapter VI ([[gin-head-susy|Susy]]'s [[speakeasy]]) and once in Chapter XVIII ([[the-railroad|the railroad]] flat), each time functioning as a different kind of symbolic shorthand. He is referenced alongside other real historical figures — [[james-reese-europe|James Reese Europe]], [[bert-williams|Bert Williams]], and Madam C.J. Walker — placing him within a pantheon of Black achievers that the novel's characters use to map status, aspiration, and community boundaries.

In Chapter VI, a light-brown "[[high-yaller|yaller]]" lad at [[gin-head-susy|Susy]]'s claims to have met "mos'n all our big niggers: Jack Johnson, [[james-reese-europe|James Reese Europe]], Adah Walker, Buddy... and Madame Walker" (006-049), invoking Johnson's name first in a list intended to establish his own social credibility. The self-aggrandizing claim — immediately dismissed by his interlocutor — uses Johnson as the apex of Black masculine fame, the name you drop to prove you have arrived.

Later in the same scene, [[gin-head-susy|Susy]] ejects a chocolate-skinned youth who has violently head-butted another man, calling him "Mis'er Jack Johnson" with dripping sarcasm (006-072). Here Johnson's name becomes a byword for explosive fighting — a man whose physical response to conflict is so ferocious it evokes the heavyweight champion himself. The nickname is not a compliment; it is [[gin-head-susy|Susy]]'s way of saying *you're too dangerous to have in my establishment*.

The novel's most telling reference comes in Chapter XVIII, when [[ray|Ray]] — the educated Haitian [[waiter]] — tells [[jake-brown|Jake]], "If I was famous as Jack Johnson and rich as Madame Walker I'd prefer to have you as my friend than — President Wilson" (018-049). [[ray|Ray]] pairs Johnson with Madam C.J. Walker as the twin poles of Black achievement: Johnson the benchmark of *fame*, Walker the benchmark of *wealth*. That [[ray|Ray]] ranks [[jake-brown|Jake]]'s friendship above even the presidency — provided he had Johnson's renown — speaks to the extraordinary symbolic weight Johnson's name carried in the Black ima[[gin-whisky|gin]]ation.

## Historical/Cultural Context

Jack Johnson was arguably the most famous — and most controversial — Black man in America during the years *[[home-to-harlem|Home to Harlem]]* is set. He won the world heavyweight title on December 26, 1908, by def[[food-and-dining|eating]] Tommy Burns in Sydney, Australia, in a fight stopped by police after fourteen rounds. His subsequent victory over former undefeated champion Jim Jeffries — the so-called "Great White Hope" — on July 4, 1910, in Reno, Nevada, sparked [[race-and-color|race]] riots across the United States as white mobs attacked Black communities celebrating the victory.

Johnson's flamboyant life[[fashion-and-style|style]] made him a lightning rod. He married white women (Etta Duryea, who died by suicide in 1912, and Lucille Cameron), drove expensive cars, wore tailored [[fashion-and-style|suits]], and openly defied the racial codes of Jim Crow America. In 1913, he was convicted under the Mann Act — a federal law ostensibly targeting the transportation of women across state lines for "immoral purposes" — in what was widely understood as a politically motivated prosecution. He fled the country and fought abroad until 1915, when he lost the title to Jess Willard in Havana, Cuba.

The "upstage [[race-and-color|race]] gang that wouldn't touch Jack Johnson with a ten-foot pole" (006-051), mentioned by [[miss-curdy|Miss Curdy]] in the novel, refers to the class divide within Black America over Johnson. The Black middle class and "respectable" leadership — figures associated with Booker T. [[washington|Washington]]'s philosophy of accommodation and the NAACP's legal strategy — often distanced themselves from Johnson, whose open relationships with white women and defiant demeanor threatened to provoke white backlash against the entire [[race-and-color|race]]. McKay's characters, situated in [[harlem|Harlem]]'s working-class [[the-sporting-world|sporting world]], occupy a different social stratum — one that might admire Johnson's defiance but also recognizes the costs of his notoriety.

## Key References

All four PN references to Jack Johnson appear in two chapters, each deploying his name for a distinct rhetorical purpose:

**Chapter VI — [[gin-head-susy|Susy]]'s [[speakeasy|Speakeasy]] (three references):** The [[speakeasy]] is the novel's densest concentration of Johnson references. The [[high-yaller|yaller]] lad's boast (006-049) uses Johnson as social currency; [[miss-curdy|Miss Curdy]]'s retort about the "[[race-and-color|race]] gang" (006-051) reveals the respectability politics that divided Black opinion of the champion; and [[gin-head-susy|Susy]]'s sarcastic "Mis'er Jack Johnson" (006-072) transforms his name into a synonym for brute force.

**Chapter XVIII — [[billy-biasse|Billy Biasse]]'s [[railroad-flat|Railroad Flat]] (one reference):** [[ray|Ray]]'s declaration to [[jake-brown|Jake]] (018-049) is the novel's most intimate Johnson reference — a moment of male friendship in which the champion's fame becomes a measure of the worth [[ray|Ray]] places on [[jake-brown|Jake]]'s companionship.

> "I done met mos'n all our big niggers: Jack Johnson, James Reese Europe, Adah Walker, Buddy, who used to play that theah drum for them Castle Walkers, and Madame Walker."
> — the yaller lad at Susy's, establishing his credentials (006-049)

> "If I was famous as Jack Johnson and rich as Madame Walker I'd prefer to have you as my friend than—President Wilson."
> — [[ray|Ray]] to [[jake-brown|Jake]] (018-049)

## Themes

**Black Fame and Its Burdens:** Johnson's name in the novel embodies the paradox of Black celebrity in early twentieth-century America. He is simultaneously the pride of the [[race-and-color|race]] and a figure whose notoriety makes "respectable" Black people keep their distance. McKay's characters, navigating their own relationships to ambition and survival, use Johnson as a yardstick — the height of what a Black man can achieve, and a cautionary tale about the price of visibility.

**Masculine Prowess and Physicality:** Johnson's boxing career made him the literal embodiment of Black masculine power in an era when claims of white physical superiority underwrote the entire racial order. When [[gin-head-susy|Susy]] calls a brawling chocolate youth "Mis'er Jack Johnson" (006-072), she taps into this association — Johnson as raw, dangerous, body-centered masculinity — but also signals its limits: such power gets you thrown out of respectable establishments.

**Class and Respectability Politics:** The novel distinguishes between the "big niggers" of popular acclaim (Johnson, Europe, Walker) and the "upstage [[race-and-color|race]] gang" of the Black elite who kept their distance. Johnson occupies both categories at once — he is the most famous, but also the most disreputable. McKay's working-class characters acknowledge this tension without fully resolving it, reflecting the real ambivalence Johnson inspired within Black communities.
