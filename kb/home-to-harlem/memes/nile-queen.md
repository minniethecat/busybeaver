**Nile Queen** is a restaurant on [[seventh-avenue|Seventh Avenue]] in [[harlem|Harlem]], appearing in [[claude-mckay|Claude McKay]]'s *[[home-to-harlem|Home to Harlem]]* as an upscale dining establishment where [[jake-brown|Jake]] and [[felice|Felice]] celebrate.

## Overview

The Nile Queen is presented as a step above the working-class eateries like [[aunt-hattie-cookshop|Aunt Hattie's]]. When [[jake-brown|Jake]] wants to dine at Aunt Hattie's, [[felice|Felice]] "preferred a 'niftier' place. So they dined at the Nile Queen restaurant on Seventh Avenue." (018-020) The restaurant's location on [[seventh-avenue|Seventh Avenue]] — Harlem's premier promenade — and its Egyptian-themed name (part of a broader fashion for exoticism in Harlem Renaissance naming) signal its aspirational character.

## Role in the Narrative

The Nile Queen appears during [[jake-brown|Jake]] and [[felice|Felice]]'s celebratory Saturday outing. After dining at the restaurant, they "subwayed down to Broadway" and bought tickets "for the nigger heaven of a theater, whence they watched high-class people make luxurious love on the screen." (018-020) The Nile Queen thus marks the beginning of an evening of cross-class cultural consumption — from Harlem restaurant to Broadway cinema — where Jake and Felice experience both pleasure and racial exclusion.

## Quotations

Felice chooses the Nile Queen over Aunt Hattie's — marking her preference for Harlem's aspirational spaces:

> He wanted to dine at Aunt Hattie's, but Felice preferred a "niftier" place. So they dined at the Nile Queen restaurant on Seventh Avenue. After dinner they subwayed down to Broadway. They bought tickets for the nigger heaven of a theater, whence they watched high-class people make luxurious love on the screen.
>
