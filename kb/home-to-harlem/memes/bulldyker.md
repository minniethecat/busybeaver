# Bulldyker

A period slang term used in 1920s [[harlem|Harlem]] for a masculine-presenting lesbian or gender-nonconforming woman. The term appears in a single exchange in *Home to [[harlem|Harlem]]* that condenses the novel's — and the era's — complex attitudes toward gender, sexuality, and community judgment.

## Definition

"Bulldyker" (or "bulldagger") was early 20th-century [[africa|Africa]]n American slang for a masculine-presenting lesbian. The term's etymology is uncertain — theories include derivation from "bull" (suggesting masculinity or aggression) plus "dyke" — but its usage in [[harlem|Harlem]] Renaissance-era sources is well documented. The term could be used neutrally, descriptively, or pejoratively depending on context. (010-030)

## Cultural Context

### Harlem Renaissance Context

Queer sexuality was a significant but often coded presence in [[harlem|Harlem]] Renaissance culture. While writers like Bruce Nugent, Countee Cullen, and [[langston-hughes|Langston Hughes]] navigated same-sex desire through indirection and allusion, working-class Black communities had their own vocabulary for gender and sexual variance. Terms like "bulldyker," "fairy," and "sissy" mapped a landscape of gender-nonconforming identities that existed in tension with both white homophobia and Black respectability politics.

The [[harlem|Harlem]] Renaissance's [[blues]] women — Bessie Smith, Ma Rainey, Ethel Waters — sometimes sang explicitly about same-sex desire ("Prove It On Me Blues," "Sissy Blues"), providing a public, if contested, space for queer expression within Black popular culture.

### In the Novel

The term "bulldyker" appears in a brief but layered exchange in Chapter X. When [[ray|Ray]] uses the word "Lesbian" — a clinical, "lovely word" — [[jake-brown|Jake]] translates it into [[harlem|Harlem]] vernacular: "Tha's what we calls bulldyker in [[harlem|Harlem]]. Them's all ugly womens" (010-030). The exchange reveals multiple attitudes in collision:

[[ray|Ray]]'s educated vocabulary ("Lesbian") versus [[jake-brown|Jake]]'s street slang ("bulldyker") — a class and education divide within the Black community. [[jake-brown|Jake]]'s dismissive "them's all ugly womens" expresses the casual homophobia of working-class masculinity, but the [[waiter]] immediately challenges him: "Not *all*. And that's a damned ugly name. [[harlem|Harlem]] is too savage about some things" (010-031).

The [[waiter]]'s critique — italicizing *all*, sneering at the word "bulldyker," naming [[harlem|Harlem]]'s "savagery" — introduces a counter-voice within the novel. For a brief moment, McKay allows a critique of [[harlem|Harlem]]'s gender policing to emerge, even as [[jake-brown|Jake]]'s grinning response ("But tha's what they is, ain't it?") reasserts the normative position.

## Key Passages

> "Tha's what we calls bulldyker in Harlem," drawled Jake. "Them's all ugly womens." (010-030)

[[jake-brown|Jake]]'s "drawled" delivery and the collective "we" position "bulldyker" as communal knowledge — this is what [[harlem|Harlem]] calls such women. The blanket judgment "all ugly womens" reveals the aesthetic dimension of gender policing: masculine-presenting women violate a standard of female beauty that [[jake-brown|Jake]] treats as self-evident.

> "Not *all*. And that's a damned ugly name," the waiter said. "Harlem is too savage about some things. *Bulldyker*," the waiter stressed with a sneer. (010-031)

The [[waiter]]'s rejoinder is remarkable for 1928: a Black male character pushing back against another's homophobia, defending the dignity of masculine-presenting women, and naming [[harlem|Harlem]]'s collective judgment as "savage." The sneer at the word "bulldyker" distances the [[waiter]] from the slang, associating him with [[ray|Ray]]'s more cosmopolitan perspective.

## Historical Significance

The term "bulldyker" is documented in [[harlem|Harlem]] Renaissance-era sources including [[blues]] lyrics, oral histories, and the period's Black press. It belongs to a lexicon of gender-variant identities that existed within Black communities long before the modern LGBTQ+ rights movement — a lexicon that both named and stigmatized gender nonconformity. Recent scholarship in Black queer studies has recovered figures like Gladys Bentley, a [[harlem|Harlem]] performer who married another woman in a public ceremony, as evidence of visible queer presence in 1920s [[harlem|Harlem]] despite social hostility. McKay's brief, ambivalent treatment of the term — giving voice to both the homophobic [[jake-brown|Jake]] and the critical [[waiter]] — captures the era's contested discourse around gender and sexuality.
