# The Railroad

The railroad in [[claude-mckay|Claude McKay]]'s *Home to [[harlem|Harlem]]* is both literal and symbolic: the [[pennsylvania-railroad|Pennsylvania Railroad]] dining car where [[jake-brown|Jake]] works as a third cook, and the broader rail infrastructure connecting the cities — [[harlem|Harlem]], [[pittsburgh|Pittsburgh]], [[philadelphia|Philadelphia]], [[chicago|Chicago]] — that form the novel's [[migration|Great Migration]] geography. Chapters X through XII take the railroad as their exclusive setting, transforming the dining car into a pressure cooker of race, class, and masculinity.

## Definition

In the novel's world, "the railroad" refers specifically to the [[pennsylvania-railroad|Pennsylvania Railroad]]'s [[dining-car]] service between New York and [[pittsburgh|Pittsburgh]]. It is a segregated labor hierarchy — white stewards, Black chefs and waiters and cooks — within "the richest railroad in the world" (011-088). For [[jake-brown|Jake]], the railroad is a job; for [[ray|Ray]], it is a classroom; for the chef, it is a kingdom; for the "timid" Georgia boy, it is a gauntlet.

## Cultural Context

### Harlem Renaissance Context

The Pullman Company and major railroads were among the largest employers of Black men in the early 20th century. The Brotherhood of Sleeping Car Porters, founded in 1925 by A. Philip Randolph, was the first Black-led labor union to win a collective bargaining agreement with a major American corporation. Railroad work was simultaneously exploitative and prestigious: it offered steady wages and travel, but within a strict racial hierarchy that required constant deference to white passengers and superiors.

McKay's railroad chapters are among the earliest literary treatments of this labor world from the inside — not the porters' experience (as in the later Pullman porter literature) but the dining-car kitchen, a space even more brutal and intimate than the passenger cars.

### In the Novel

[[jake-brown|Jake]] "had taken the job on the railroad just to break the hold that [[harlem|Harlem]] had on him" (010-013). The railroad offers escape from [[harlem|Harlem]]'s exhausting pleasures — sex, drinking, nightlife — and opens a different social world. But the escape is into another kind of intensity.

The dining car is a "coffin-shaped kitchen" (010-003), a space of suffocation and death. The chef — "a great black bundle of concentrated energy" (012-004) — rules it absolutely. He "bellied his way up to the top" from the lowest rank, terrorizing his subordinates as he was once terrorized: "Keep 'em up a tree all the time" (010-011). The hierarchy is rigid: chef, second cook, third cook ([[jake-brown|Jake]]), fourth cook, waiters, pantry man.

Violence is structural. "Difficulties on the dining-car were worsened by a feud between waiters and cooks" (012-014). "Nothing can be worse on a dining-car than trouble between the chef and the waiters" (012-015). The chef "hit the fourth [[waiter]]" and "used to clump the timid boy from Georgia right across the head" (012-016)(012-019). The iron rule: "For on the dining-car, he who strikes the first blow loses his job" (012-021). Violence is omnipresent but must be absorbed, never returned.

The crew's quarters in Pittsburgh are "the quarters that the richest railroad in the world had provided for its men" (011-088) — a bitterly ironic phrase, as the quarters are filthy, cramped, and degrading. The chef "sprawled like the carcass of a dead elephant" (011-026) in his bunk; men gamble, fight, and snore through the layover hours.

[[ray|Ray]] enters this world as a waiter. His intellectualism — he reads [[alphonse-daudet|Alphonse Daudet]], discusses the [[french-revolution|French Revolution]], speaks French — is incomprehensible to the chef, who calls it "the professor and his nonsense" (010-095). But [[jake-brown|Jake]] is drawn to [[ray|Ray]]'s difference. Their friendship, forged in the dining-car crucible, is the novel's central relationship: the working-class sensualist and the displaced intellectual meeting across the kitchen counter.

The railroad connects cities. The "huge black animal" of the train crosses "the heart of the vast gray Pennsylvania country" (010-002). [[pittsburgh|Pittsburgh]] — "the rottenest layover in the whole railroad field" (011-038) — is a landscape of "steelworkers in overalls and railroad men" (011-045). The train's movement structures the novel's geography of linked Black communities across the industrial North.

## Variations & Related Terms

- **Dining car** — The "coffin-shaped" kitchen workspace, distinguished from passenger cars and Pullman sleepers
- **The chef** — The absolute ruler of the dining car: "the most hated chef in the service" (012-002)
- **Layover** — The period between runs in Pittsburgh, spent in filthy crew quarters: "the rottenest layover in the whole railroad field" (011-038)
- **Pullman porters** — Black service workers on passenger cars who "came into the dining-car in the middle of the waiters' dinner" (012-062), sharing the racial economy of railroad labor
- **The run** — The New York-Pittsburgh route, hated by the crew: "The railroad men hated the Pittsburgh run" (011-014)

## Key Passages

> Over the heart of the vast gray Pennsylvania country the huge black animal of the train went roaring. (010-002)

McKay opens Chapter X with the train figured as a "black animal" — powerful, dark, and barely contained — crossing the gray expanse of industrial [[pennsylvania|Pennsylvania]]. The image fuses machine and beast, the railroad as a living force.

> Nothing can be worse on a dining-car than trouble between the chef and the waiters. (012-015)

The narrator's generalization — delivered as universal wisdom — captures the dining car as a pressure cooker. The chef-waiter feud is the novel's most concentrated study of Black-on-Black hierarchy within white corporate power.

> He had taken the job on the railroad just to break the hold that Harlem had on him. (010-013)

[[jake-brown|Jake]]'s railroad job is an escape from one form of intensity ([[harlem|Harlem]]'s nightlife) into another (the dining-car kitchen). The railroad offers discipline, wages, and a different masculinity — the working man rather than the pleasure-seeker.

> The quarters that the richest railroad in the world had provided for its men. (011-088)

McKay's irony is at its sharpest here. The Pennsylvania Railroad's wealth — it was, historically, among the world's largest corporations — contrasts with the squalor of the Black workers' quarters. The sentence is a political indictment tucked into narrative description.

## Historical Significance

The Pennsylvania Railroad was the largest railroad in the United States by revenue and traffic in the early 20th century. Its dining-car service employed thousands of Black men in a strict hierarchy: white stewards at the top, Black chefs commanding the kitchen, and Black waiters and cooks below them. McKay's account of the "feud" system — in which chefs and waiters were structurally pitted against each other — documents a labor-management strategy of racial and occupational division that kept wages low and unions weak. The 1920s saw the rise of the Brotherhood of Sleeping Car Porters, but dining-car workers were slower to organize. McKay's railroad chapters are thus a rare literary document of pre-union Black railroad labor.
