# France

France in [[claude-mckay|Claude McKay]]'s *Home to [[harlem|Harlem]]* is not a geographic destination so much as a racial idea: the country where — as [[jake-brown|Jake]] declares — "a black man's got a chance" (001-021). It appears primarily as memory, invoked in flashback and conversation by characters who served there during the First World War, and it functions as a counter-argument to American segregation throughout the novel.

## Location

### Geography

France is a Western European nation that was a major theater of the First World War (1914–1918). In the novel's geography, France is encountered through its port cities: [[brest|Brest]], on the Brittany coast, where [[jake-brown|Jake]] was stationed and from which he "sailed for Brest with a happy chocolate company" (001-010); and [[le-havre|Le Havre]], on the Normandy coast, a transit point between [[brest|Brest]], [[london|London]], and New York. These ports formed the French nodes of the Black Atlantic circuit McKay's characters traverse.

### In the Novel's World

France exists in the novel primarily in three modes: as wartime experience (the [[brest|Brest]] of Jake's army service), as racial ideology (the idea of France as non-racist), and as a contrast to America (the country where Black men could "go to a place and ask for a drink" without incident). The physical France of the novel is largely a France of ports and military camps — not Parisian sophistication but the rough, cosmopolitan waterfront of soldiers and sailors.

## Significance to the Narrative

France serves as the novel's racial foil to America. [[jake-brown|Jake]]'s assertion — "France is the only country I've struck where a black man's got a chance" (001-021) — is delivered early and becomes a refrain. The statement embeds its own qualification ("I've struck," not "there is") and its own irony: France is better than America, but the standard is low.

The French colonial context is present but ambivalent. [[ray|Ray]], the Haitian intellectual, is a product of French colonial education — "French is my native language" (010-046) — and his knowledge of French literature and the [[french-revolution|French Revolution]] distinguishes him from American-born Black workers. Yet Haiti's history under French colonialism — "taken to France, and sent by Napoleon to die in the Alps" (010-052), referring to Toussaint Louverture — marks French racial liberalism as deeply compromised. [[ray|Ray]] holds both ideas simultaneously: French culture as personal heritage, French colonialism as historical crime.

The port of [[brest|Brest]] is the novel's most vivid French location. It was the debarkation point for American troops, and McKay's characters remember it as a place of racial mixing and relative freedom. "The French sailors that couldn't get their fill of the colored boys" (003-030) — gendered and sexualized encounters that the novel presents without judgment. [[brest|Brest]] appears in nostalgic conversation: "You showed the goose-step around Brest. Have a shot on me" (003-016), an invitation to drink in memory of shared wartime experience.

[[le-havre|Le Havre]] functions as a transit point: "Lit out for Havre. He liquored himself up and he went off" (001-012). The port cities — [[brest|Brest]], [[le-havre|Le Havre]], and across the Channel, [[london|London]] — form a geography of Black male mobility that the novel maps against the fixed geography of American segregation.

French fashion appears in [[harlem|Harlem]]'s style consciousness. "'Everywhere. ... French style'" (003-009) is how a woman describes the ubiquity of French influence. [[rose-congo|Rose]] remembers "the popular French styles. Broad-pleated, long-waisted" (019-034). France enters [[harlem|Harlem]] not only as political ideal but as aesthetic aspiration.

The novel also acknowledges France's limits. One character notes: "I beat it away from Brest because they wouldn't give us black men the same privileges as the white soldiers" (021-089). The freedom France offered was real but partial — better than America, not yet equal.

## Historical Context

During World War I, approximately 200,000 Black American soldiers served in France, primarily in segregated labor and service units. The experience of serving in a country without Jim Crow laws — where Black men could enter cafes, ride trains, and interact socially with white French civilians — was transformative for many and contributed to the postwar militancy of the "New Negro" movement. French colonial soldiers from Africa and the Caribbean also served in large numbers, creating a multiracial military society that contrasted with the American Army's strict segregation.

However, the French racial liberalism of the 1910s had its own limits. French colonialism in Africa, the Caribbean, and Indochina was brutal; the "French color line" was real if differently configured than the American one. McKay, as a colonial subject himself (born in British-ruled [[jamaica|Jamaica]]), was attuned to these complexities. Through [[ray|Ray]], he embeds an awareness of French colonialism within the celebration of French racial attitudes.
