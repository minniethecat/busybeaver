# The Great Migration

The Great Migration was the mass relocation of approximately six million [[africa|Africa]]n Americans from the rural South to the urban North, Midwest, and West between roughly 1910 and 1970 — one of the largest internal population movements in modern history. In [[claude-mckay|Claude McKay]]'s *[[Home to Harlem]]*, the Great Migration is not a theme that characters discuss abstractly; it is the biographical fact that underlies nearly every character's presence in [[Harlem]], shaping their ori[[gin-whisky|gin]]s, their aspirations, their estrangements, and their social worlds.

## Definition

The Great Migration was driven by the convergence of "push" factors in the South — racial violence, economic exploitation under sharecropping, disenfranchisement, the boll weevil's devastation of cotton agriculture — and "pull" factors in the North: industrial jobs created by [[world-war-i|World War I]]'s labor shortages, the promise of higher wages, the relative absence of Jim Crow laws, and the cultural magnetism of Black urban communities like [[harlem|Harlem]], [[chicago|Chicago]]'s South Side, and Detroit's Paradise Valley. Between 1910 and 1930 alone, the Black population of [[new-york-city|New York City]] more than tripled, and [[harlem|Harlem]] became the largest single Black urban concentration in the United States.

| Push Factor | Southern Reality | Pull Factor | Northern Promise |
|-------------|-----------------|-------------|-----------------|
| Racial violence | Lynching, Klan terror | Physical safety | Rule of law, police protection (in theory) |
| Sharecropping | Debt peonage, cotton collapse | Industrial wages | Factory, dock, and [[the-railroad|railroad]] jobs |
| Disenfranchisement | Poll taxes, literacy tests | Political voice | Voting rights, political organization |
| Educational exclusion | Underfunded segregated schools | Educational access | Public schools, libraries, night classes |
| Cultural isolation | Rural dispersal | Community density | [[harlem|Harlem]], South Side [[chicago|Chicago]], Black institutions |

## Cultural Context

### In the Novel

Every character in *[[home-to-harlem|Home to Harlem]]* carries a [[migration]] story — whether stated, implied, or embodied. The novel's opening page announces this: [[jake-brown|Jake]] has just returned from two years abroad, but his ori[[gin-whisky|gin]]ation point is [[virginia|Virginia]], from which he migrated to [[harlem|Harlem]] before the war. The novel's geography — [[virginia|Virginia]], [[south-carolina|South Carolina]], [[harlem|Harlem]], [[pittsburgh|Pittsburgh]], [[chicago|Chicago]] — is a Great Migration map compressed into a single narrative.

> Girls coming from the South to try their future in New York always reached the Congo first. (004-006)

This single sentence captures the [[migration]]'s gendered dimension and its social logic: the [[the-congo|Congo]] [[cabaret-culture|cabaret]] functions as a reception center, a labor market, and a social sorting mechanism for young Southern Black women arriving in [[harlem|Harlem]]. The direction of movement — South to [[new-york-city|New York]] — is taken for granted as a fact of life.

[[gin-head-susy|Gin-head Susy]], whose story is told in Chapter VI, embodies the [[migration]]'s tragic arc:

> Susy came from South Carolina. A yellow youngster who had lost her mother early and been raised by a grandmother who told her stories of the glory of her race. (006-008)

The specificity of "[[south-carolina|South Carolina]]" — not a generic "the South" but a particular state — connects [[gin-head-susy|Susy]] to a real [[migration]] corridor (the Atlantic Coast route from the Carolinas to [[new-york-city|New York]]). Her subsequent decline — "her drab childhood in a [[south-carolina|South Carolina]] town" (006-020) giving way to exploitation in [[harlem|Harlem]] — shows [[migration]] as a mixed promise, offering not only opportunity but vulnerability.

> Her drab childhood in a South Carolina town. Her early marriage to a man much older than herself who had treated her cruelly. (006-020)

[[migration|Migration]] here is not simply "escape"; it carries old traumas into new settings. [[gin-head-susy|Susy]]'s story complicates any simple narrative of Northern redemption.

### Jake as Migration Archetype

[[jake-brown|Jake]]'s trajectory — rural [[virginia|Virginia]] to wartime service, to [[london|London]] longshore work, to [[harlem|Harlem]], to [[the-railroad|railroad]] labor between [[new-york-city|New York]] and [[pittsburgh|Pittsburgh]], and finally (with [[felice|Felice]]) to [[chicago|Chicago]] — maps the Great Migration's geographic range. His restlessness ("I'm searching for something new" (001-017)) is the migrant's permanent condition: the belief, justified or not, that the next city offers what the current one lacks. [[jake-brown|Jake]]'s body registers [[migration]]'s toll — "rough, calloused, hard and broad palms" from longshore work — and its [[pleasure-seeking|pleasure]]s: the "steel-gray English suit" worn on his first night back in [[harlem|Harlem]] (002-006), purchased with wages earned abroad.

## Key Passages

> Girls coming from the South to try their future in New York always reached the Congo first. (004-006)

The novel's most economical statement on the Great Migration: Southern arrival, Northern destination, and the [[cabaret-culture|cabaret]] as threshold institution. "Always" makes the observation sociological, not merely narrative.

> Susy came from South Carolina. A yellow youngster who had lost her mother early. (006-008)

McKay's compression — ori[[gin-whisky|gin]], appearance, orphanhood — in a single sentence. [[gin-head-susy|Susy]]'s "yellow" complexion (light skin) is part of her story: her grandmother "told her stories of the glory of her [[race-and-color|race]]" (006-008), suggesting a family history of [[race-and-color|color]]-consciousness that [[migration]] will both exploit and complicate.

> Jake was tall, brawny, and black. (001-009)

Not a [[migration]] passage in content, but in narrative function: [[jake-brown|Jake]]'s body is presented as a fact before his history is narrated. [[migration|Migration]] is written on the body — "brawny" from labor — before it is told in flashback. The Great Migration was a movement not just of people but of laboring bodies, and McKay's description registers this.

## Historical Significance

The Great Migration transformed American society irreversibly. It created majority-Black urban centers in the North whose political power — once organized — would drive the Civil Rights Movement's Northern wing. It produced new forms of [[africa|Africa]]n American culture: the [[blues]] and [[jazz]] that migrated north with Southern musicians, the urban vernacular speech that McKay reproduces in dialogue, the denser social networks and institutions that made [[Harlem]] a Black capital. It also generated new forms of racial conflict — housing segregation, job competition, white flight — whose legacies persist in American cities.

*[[home-to-harlem|Home to Harlem]]*, published in 1928, captures the Migration at its peak. The [[harlem|Harlem]] McKay describes — teeming with [[virginia|Virginia]]ns and South Carolinians, [[longshoreman|longshoremen]] and [[the-railroad|railroad]] cooks, idealists and hustlers — is a community still being assembled, still negotiating what it means to be Southern-born people living Northern lives.
