# Longshoreman

A dock worker employed in loading and unloading cargo ships. In *Home to [[harlem|Harlem]]*, longshoring defines [[jake-brown|Jake Brown]]'s working-class identity and provides the physical and economic foundation from which he navigates [[harlem|Harlem]]'s nightlife, romance, and social hierarchy.

## Definition

A longshoreman (also stevedore or docker) is a manual laborer who works on the docks, loading and unloading ship cargo. In early 20th-century America, longshoring was one of the few industrial occupations open to Black workers, though it was often casual, dangerous, and subject to racial discrimination in hiring. The work required immense physical strength and endurance, qualities that McKay associates with [[jake-brown|Jake]]'s bodily presence throughout the novel. (001-009)

## Cultural Context

### Harlem Renaissance Context

Longshoring represents the working-class foundation of McKay's literary vision. Unlike many [[harlem|Harlem]] Renaissance writers who focused on the "Talented Tenth" — educated, middle-class Black professionals — McKay centered his fiction on working-class Black characters: longshoremen, cooks, railroad workers, and entertainers. This was a deliberate artistic and political choice, asserting the dignity and complexity of lives that mainstream literature ignored.

Longshoring also connects the novel to global labor movements. The Industrial Workers of the World (I.W.W.) had organized dock workers across racial lines, and McKay's own political radicalism — he was associated with socialist and communist circles — informs his portrayal of interracial working-class solidarity in the novel's dock and shipboard scenes.

### In the Novel

[[jake-brown|Jake]]'s identity as a longshoreman is established early: when the U.S. entered WWI in 1917, "he was a longshoreman. He was working on a [[brooklyn|Brooklyn]] pier" (001-009). This fact grounds [[jake-brown|Jake]] in a specific class position — he is not an artist or intellectual like [[ray|Ray]], but a manual laborer whose body is his primary asset.

The longshoreman identity gives [[jake-brown|Jake]] access to cross-racial working-class spaces. He is "the first longshoreman, colored or white, to tread that magnificent red carpet" of [[madame-suarez-buffet-flat|Madame Suarez's]] [[buffet-flat|buffet flat]] (008-006), a crossing of class boundaries that astonishes the venue's elite clientele. [[jake-brown|Jake]]'s physical charm transcends the class barrier.

The longshoreman broker [[nije-gridley|Nije Gridley]] (005-005) represents the informal economy within the docks — a Black intermediary who connects workers to jobs, taking a cut. Longshoremen also form a social network: [[jake-brown|Jake]] finds a fellow longshoreman at [[billy-biasse|Billy Biasse]]'s joint who can take him to a [[buffet-flat|buffet flat]] (005-034), and a gang of longshoremen appears at the novel's closing party (019-041).

## Key Passages

> He was the first longshoreman, colored or white, to tread that magnificent red carpet. (008-006)

This line crystallizes the class crossing that defines [[jake-brown|Jake]]'s social mobility. His body — "so naturally beautiful" — grants him entry to spaces his occupation would normally exclude him from.

> He was a longshoreman. He was working on a Brooklyn pier. (001-009)

The flat, declarative simplicity of this biographical fact contrasts with the sensuous complexity of [[harlem|Harlem]] nightlife that will follow. Longshoring is the baseline — honest, physical, economically necessary — against which the novel's pleasures and dangers are measured.

## Historical Significance

Longshore work was central to Black economic life in early 20th-century port cities. The docks were sites of both racial exclusion and interracial labor organizing — the I.W.W.'s Marine Transport Workers union actively recruited Black dock workers, and the [[philadelphia|Philadelphia]] and New York waterfronts saw significant Black union participation by the 1920s. The occupation's physical demands and casual hiring practices created a rough meritocracy in which strength and reliability could partially offset racial discrimination, though Black longshoremen remained concentrated in the lowest-paid, most dangerous jobs.
