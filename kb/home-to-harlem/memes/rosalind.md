**Rosalind** (born Rosalind Whicher) is a supporting character in [[claude-mckay|Claude McKay]]'s *Home to [[harlem|Harlem]]*, appearing in chapter 17 through the retrospective narrative of the narrator, a [[waiter]] at [[uncle-doc|Uncle Doc]]'s [[saloon]]. Her relationship with [[jerco|Jerco]] and her subsequent illness and death form one of the novel's most emotionally resonant storylines.

## Role in the Novel

Rosalind appears in chapter 17, which is narrated by a waiter at [[uncle-doc|Uncle Doc]]'s saloon who recounts the story of [[jerco|Jerco]] and Rosalind — a couple living in [[ma-lawton|Ma Lawton]]'s boarding house. The retrospective narrative traces their relationship from the narrator's arrival at the boarding house through Rosalind's illness and death, serving as the novel's most sustained treatment of romantic love, caregiving, and loss within [[harlem|Harlem]]'s working class.

## Characterization

### Appearance

Rosalind has a fur coat that becomes a significant plot element — [[jerco|Jerco]] refuses to pawn it even when they desperately need money for medicine, saying "She'll need it soons she gets up." (017-045) She also has silk shirts she gave [[jerco|Jerco]] as presents. (017-050)

### Personality and Traits

Rosalind is characterized as "an awful good woman" (017-055) — kind, generous, and deserving of [[jerco|Jerco]]'s devotion. When sick with grippe, she maintains her dignity, smiling even as she acknowledges the possibility of death: "S'posin' I don't get up again." (017-033) She refuses a religious prayer, which "shocked [[ma-lawton|Ma Lawton]], who believed in God's word." (017-036) She is stubborn — when told to pawn her coat for medicine, she refuses: "You kain pawn my coat. Old man Greenbaum will give you something on that gold watch." (017-031)

### Relationships

- [[jerco|Jerco]] — devoted partner; "I kain't live without Rosalind" (017-052)
- [[ma-lawton|Ma Lawton]] — landlady who cares for her during illness
- [[uncle-doc|Uncle Doc]] — the narrator works at his saloon

## Key Scenes

Rosalind's illness and death form chapter 17's emotional core. She falls sick with grippe (017-028), and the boarding house community rallies around her — [[ma-lawton|Ma Lawton]] cares for her, the narrator visits, and [[jerco|Jerco]] is desperate. The doctor "stopped Rosalind from drinking beer," but [[jerco|Jerco]] "slipped it in to her when [[ma-lawton|Ma Lawton]] was not around." (017-037)

As her condition worsens, Rosalind refuses a prayer and faces death with quiet dignity: "S'posin' I don't get up again, Rosalind smiled. But her countenance changed when she saw [[jerco|Jerco]] crying." (017-033) She dies that evening — "[[ma-lawton|Ma Lawton]] came in the saloon about nine o'clock that evening and said that Rosalind was dead." (017-057)

After her death, [[jerco|Jerco]] is inconsolable: "I'll nevah find another one like Rosalind, nevah!" (017-059)

## Themes

Rosalind's story represents the fragility of working-class domestic life in McKay's [[harlem|Harlem]], where illness could unravel the most devoted partnerships. Her relationship with [[jerco|Jerco]] is presented as one of the novel's few genuinely loving, non-transactional bonds between a man and a woman, making her death all the more affecting. The fur coat — a symbol of her dignity and [[jerco|Jerco]]'s love — becomes a narrative motif that embodies their bond beyond death.
