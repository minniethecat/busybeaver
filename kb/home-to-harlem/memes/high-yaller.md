# High-Yaller

"High-yaller" (also "high yellow," "yaller") is a color-complex slang term that appears throughout *[[home-to-harlem|Home to Harlem]]*, designating light-skinned Black people — particularly women — and indexing the hierarchies of colorism that structured desire, status, and identity in 1920s Harlem.

## Usage in Home to Harlem

The term appears over twenty times in the novel, always in dialogue or free indirect discourse, and carries a range of valences from desire to disdain. At [[the-congo|the Congo]] [[cabaret]], light-skinned patrons are rare: "High yallers were scarce there. Except for such sweetmen that lived off the low-down dark trade" (004-006). The Congo's identity as a space for the darker-skinned Black working class is partly defined by the absence of the "high-yaller" type associated with more commercially oriented venues.

The term's ambivalence is captured in Jake's friend's remark: "I ain't much for the high-yallers after having been so much fed up with the ofays" (004-057) — a statement that links light skin to whiteness and, by extension, to the racial power structure. Yet the attraction persists despite the suspicion:

> "Theah's a high-yaller entertainer there that I'se got mah eye on, and I'm just going to be nosing around some. Coax her out this evening" (004-056)

## The Color Hierarchy

"High-yaller" participates in a color hierarchy that structured Black social life in the early twentieth century. At the top were light-skinned Black people whose proximity to whiteness conferred social advantages — access to certain jobs, social clubs, and marriage markets. This hierarchy was policed from both directions: darker-skinned Black people often resented the privileges of the "high-yaller" class, while the "high-yaller" class itself policed its boundaries against darker-skinned aspirants.

The term also carried gendered meanings. Women described as "high-yaller" were often figured as objects of heightened desire — a desirability that the novel consistently links to their approximation of whiteness. The "high-yaller queen" is a recurrent type: desirable, but also dangerous, unreliable, and associated with the economic precarity of the sporting life. Susy warns about "yaller men," saying "I know them from long experience" (006-021), and another character's "latest 'yaller' sweetie had stolen her suitcase" (006-026).

## Colorism as Social Critique

McKay's use of "high-yaller" throughout the novel functions not as an endorsement of colorism but as a documentary exposure of it. By placing the term in the mouths of his characters — in their boasts, insults, desires, and disappointments — McKay reveals colorism as a lived reality of Black social life, a structure of feeling that shaped intimate relationships and community dynamics as powerfully as class or gender.

The novel's treatment of skin color is complex rather than polemical. Jake, who is described as brown-skinned, moves through a social world where skin color is constantly read, commented on, and invested with meaning. The term "yaller" can be a compliment, an insult, or a warning, depending on context — and it is precisely this contextual flexibility that makes it such a revealing index of Harlem's social life.

## In Harlem Context

The "high-yaller" figure occupied a distinct position in the [[social-hierarchy-in-harlem|social hierarchy of Harlem]], above the darker-skinned working class but below the [[ofay|white world]]. This intermediate position made the "high-yaller" a figure of both aspiration and resentment — someone who could pass, or nearly pass, for white, and whose social mobility was thus both envied and suspected. The "high-yaller queen" who married a man "with a grand automobile" (006-036) represented a particular form of social advancement that combined color privilege with the economics of the sporting life.
