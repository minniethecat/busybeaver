**Prince Street** is a street in [[harlem|Harlem]] mentioned in [[claude-mckay|Claude McKay]]'s *Home to [[harlem|Harlem]]* in connection with [[gin-head-susy|Susy]]'s apartment and the rougher, more dangerous side of the neighborhood. It appears as a landmark of working-class [[harlem|Harlem]] life, a place where violence and poverty are part of the everyday texture.

## Location

### Geography

Prince Street runs through [[harlem|Harlem]], intersecting with [[myrtle-avenue|Myrtle Avenue]] near [[gin-head-susy|Susy]]'s apartment. The specific block is not identified in the novel, but it is part of the dense grid of streets that define [[harlem|Harlem]]'s working-class residential geography.

### In the Novel's World

[[gin-head-susy|Susy]]'s "apartment in [[myrtle-avenue|Myrtle Avenue]]" is described as being "near Prince Street," anchoring it in [[harlem|Harlem]]'s street geography. (006-006) The proximity is significant — [[myrtle-avenue|Myrtle Avenue]] is a major residential artery, and Prince Street marks the edge of a rougher zone. [[jake-brown|Jake]] and his circle move between the respectable and the dangerous parts of [[harlem|Harlem]]; Prince Street sits on the boundary.

## Significance to the Narrative

Prince Street appears as a shorthand for [[harlem|Harlem]]'s violent undercurrent. One character warns of "shooting-up and cut-up in Prince Street," invoking the street as a place where knife and gun violence are part of daily life. (007-158) The line trails off — "and —" — leaving the implication unspoken, as if everyone in [[harlem|Harlem]] already knows what happens on Prince Street.

The street also contrasts with [[harlem|Harlem]]'s [[fifth-avenue|Fifth Avenue]], the more aspirational thoroughfare. The narrator associates Prince Street with "a bellboy or railroad waiter" as opposed to the higher-status residents of "[[fifth-avenue|Fifth Avenue]]" (006-010), marking it as a street of working-class service workers rather than professionals. This geographic coding of class within [[harlem|Harlem]] is a recurring feature of the novel's urban geography.

## Historical Context

Prince Street in the 1910s–1920s was part of the rapidly densifying [[harlem|Harlem]] neighborhood that had recently transitioned from largely white to predominantly Black as a result of the Great Migration. Streets like Prince Street housed the porters, waiters, and domestic workers who formed the backbone of [[harlem|Harlem]]'s service economy. The street-level violence alluded to in the novel reflects the real conditions of overcrowding, poverty, and inadequate policing that characterized working-class [[harlem|Harlem]] in this period.
