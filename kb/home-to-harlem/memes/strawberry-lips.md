# Strawberry Lips

A vivid figure in *Home to [[harlem|Harlem]]* — "burnt-cork black" with "salmon-pink and planky" lips — who is introduced as [[zeddy-plummer|Zeddy Plummer]]'s "new pal" and becomes [[jake-brown|Jake]]'s companion through a series of nightlife excursions. His nickname, drawn from the "peculiar stage-red color of his mouth," marks him as the novel's most explicit representation of the minstrel type — a figure McKay uses to explore the relationship between Black identity and racist caricature.

## Role in the Novel

Strawberry Lips first appears when [[zeddy-plummer|Zeddy]] brings him along for a night in Brooklyn. [[jake-brown|Jake]], [[zeddy-plummer|Zeddy]], and Strawberry Lips travel to [[myrtle-avenue|Myrtle Avenue]] for an evening of gin, food, and sociability at [[gin-head-susy|Susy]] and [[miss-curdy|Miss Curdy]]'s apartment (006-090).

He is the novel's resident raconteur of [[harlem|Harlem]] nightlife history (006-087). When the group walks down [[myrtle-avenue|Myrtle Avenue]], Strawberry Lips delivers an extended monologue about the rise and fall of Elijah Bowers's [[cabaret]] — "some big guy" with "a grand automobile and a [[high-yaller]] wife" who killed a man in his cabaret and was financially ruined by legal fees: "The lawyers got him off. But they cleaned him out dry. Done broke him, that case did. And today he's plumb down and out" (006-087).

At the Myrtle Avenue gathering, he becomes [[miss-curdy|Miss Curdy]]'s object of flirtation: "Miss Curdy was sitting against the mantelpiece, charming Strawberry Lips. Marvellous lips. Salmon-pink and planky. She had hoisted herself upon his knees, her arm around his thick neck" (006-106). He later dances with her to the phonograph (006-113).

A week after the Myrtle Avenue gin-fest, Strawberry Lips reappears for [[gin-head-susy|Susy]]'s magnificent free-love feast — a Sunday dinner of knockout gin cocktails and food that demonstrates [[gin-head-susy|Susy]]'s skills as a cook: "She belonged to the ancient aristocracy of black cooks" (006-127). At this feast, [[gin-head-susy|Susy]] distributes chicken pieces — Jake gets a leg, Zeddy a wing, and "Strawberry Lips received a bit of breast" (006-127).

## Characterization

McKay's narrator introduces Strawberry Lips with an anthropological distance unusual even for this novel: "Strawberry Lips was typically the stage Negro. He was proof that a generalization has some foundation in truth" (006-084). The narrator then elaborates: "You might live your life in many black belts and arrive at the conclusion that there is no such thing as a typical Negro — no minstrel coon off the stage, no Thomas Nelson Page's nigger, no Octavus Roy Cohen's porter, no lineal descendant of Uncle Tom. Then one day your theory may be upset through meeting with a type by far more perfect than any created counterpart" (006-084).

This framing is deliberately provocative — McKay simultaneously invokes and destabilizes the minstrel stereotype. Strawberry Lips *is* the stage Negro, but he is also a fully realized presence in the novel's social world: he drinks, eats, flirts, dances, tells stories, and is desired by [[miss-curdy|Miss Curdy]] for his spectacular lips. The stereotype is named and then lived through, complicating any simple reading.

## Key Passages

> Susy had concocted a pitcherful of knockout gin cocktails. And such food! Susy could cook. Perhaps it was her splendid style that made her sink all her wages in gin and sweetmen. For she belonged to the ancient aristocracy of black cooks. (006-127)

## Themes

Strawberry Lips embodies the novel's complex engagement with **racial typology and minstrelsy**. McKay's narrator presents him as a walking contradiction: the "typical stage Negro" who is nonetheless an individual — with a nickname derived from his body, a voice that narrates Harlem history, and a social presence that attracts [[miss-curdy|Miss Curdy]]'s frank desire. The salmon-pink lips that give him his name are also the feature that makes him a minstrel type; McKay refuses to resolve the tension between stereotype and personhood.

He also functions as the novel's **oral historian** of [[harlem|Harlem]] nightlife. His monologue about Elijah Bowers's cabaret (006-087) preserves a vanished world — the pre-war Black entertainment district on [[myrtle-avenue|Myrtle Avenue]] — that predates the [[harlem|Harlem]] of the novel's present. Through Strawberry Lips, McKay records the ephemeral history of Black institutions that left no written archive.
