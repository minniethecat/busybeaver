**[[virginia|Virginia]]** is a Southern state referenced in [[claude-mckay|Claude McKay]]'s *Home to [[harlem|Harlem]]* in two distinct registers: as a geographical place of origin in a character's remembered past, and as the namesake of "Virginia Dare" wine, a popular alcoholic drink in [[harlem|Harlem]]'s [[prohibition|Prohibition]]-era nightlife.

## Location

### Geography

[[virginia|Virginia]] is a state in the American South, bordering the Atlantic Ocean, Maryland, West Virginia, Kentucky, Tennessee, and North Carolina. During the Great Migration era (roughly 1910–1930), it was a significant source of Black out-migration to Northern cities including New York, Philadelphia, and Pittsburgh.

### In the Novel's World

[[virginia|Virginia]] appears in the novel's world primarily as a remembered landscape — the "down home" of personal history — rather than as a present-tense destination. It is mentioned once directly and implicitly invoked through the ubiquity of "Virginia Dare" wine in [[harlem|Harlem]]'s speakeasies and cabarets. The state also stands as representative of the broader American South from which many of [[harlem|Harlem]]'s Black residents had migrated: "Girls coming from the South to try their future in New York" (004-006) captures the collective movement of which Virginia was a part.

## Significance to the Narrative

The sole direct reference to [[virginia|Virginia]] as a place occurs in a passage reflecting on adolescent experience: "without disgust, down home in [[virginia|Virginia]], Of his adolescent breaking-through" (019-005). The phrase "down home" carries the double valence of nostalgia and distance — [[virginia|Virginia]] is a place left behind, associated with youthful awakening but not with present belonging. The character's memory of [[virginia|Virginia]] is filtered through the lens of adult life in the North, a common psychological dynamic of the Great Migration generation.

The far more frequent invocation of "Virginia" in the novel is through **Virginia Dare** wine, described as "the favorite drink of all the little browns" (019-081). It appears throughout the nightlife scenes: a "brown had a glass of Virginia Dare before her" (007-087), women order "Virginia Dare" at tables (013-049), and "a woman brought two glasses of Virginia Dare" (013-054). This sweet, golden wine carries the name of the state into Harlem's speakeasies — a commercial echo of the Southern geography so many migrants had left behind. The drink's name links Southern origin (Virginia) with Northern consumption (Harlem nightlife), a material trace of the migration's cultural imprint.

## Historical Context

During the Great Migration (1910–1930), Virginia was one of the significant source states for Black migration to Northern cities. Norfolk, Richmond, and rural Tidewater communities sent thousands northward along the Atlantic seaboard railroad routes. The Pennsylvania Railroad, on which [[jake-brown|Jake]] works as a [[dining-car|dining-car]] cook, was one of the primary transportation corridors for this migration.

Virginia Dare wine itself was a real product — a sweet, fortified wine named after the first English child born in the Americas (Roanoke, 1587). The branding evoked colonial nostalgia but the product found its market among working-class Black consumers in Harlem, becoming a fixture of the Prohibition-era nightlife McKay documents. The drink's ubiquity in the novel — appearing in multiple chapters — suggests its cultural significance as an affordable, accessible alcoholic beverage in the speakeasy economy.

## Appearances

[[virginia|Virginia]] the state is referenced directly in chapter 19 (019-005). Virginia Dare wine appears in chapters 7, 13, and 19 (007-087)(013-049)(013-054)(019-081). The broader Southern migration context informing Virginia's role is established in chapters 4, 6, and 7 (004-006)(006-008)(007-020).
