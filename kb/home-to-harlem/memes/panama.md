**[[panama|Panama]]** is a Central American country and the location of the Panama Canal, referenced in [[claude-mckay|Claude McKay]]'s *Home to [[harlem|Harlem]]* as a node in the Black Atlantic geography of labor, migration, and racial identity. It appears in the [[dining-car|dining-car]] conversations between [[jake-brown|Jake]] and [[ray|Ray]] and in a later passage about a character's detention there.

## Location

### Geography

[[panama|Panama]] is a narrow isthmus connecting Central and South America, bordered by Costa Rica to the west and Colombia to the east. The Panama Canal, completed by the United States in 1914, cuts through the country and links the Atlantic and Pacific Oceans. It is "in the Caribbean — near the Panama Canal" (010-050) that [[ray|Ray]] locates a key part of his personal geography.

### In the Novel's World

[[panama|Panama]] appears in the novel's world as a transit point in the Black diaspora — a place where laborers from the Caribbean passed through or were detained, part of the vast infrastructure of American empire that relied on Black and brown bodies. It is mentioned not as a destination or home but as a waypoint, a site of passage and labor.

## Significance to the Narrative

The first mention of [[panama|Panama]] occurs during [[ray|Ray]]'s conversation with [[jake-brown|Jake]] on the [[dining-car|dining-car]]. [[ray|Ray]] explains that Haiti is "in the Caribbean — near the Panama Canal" (010-050), situating his homeland within a recognizable American imperial geography. The Panama Canal, opened just fourteen years before the novel's publication, was a symbol of American engineering and expansion — and of the Black labor that built and maintained it.

[[jake-brown|Jake]]'s response reveals his astonishment at learning there exists a Black nation so close to the Canal: "his race, just down there by [[panama|Panama]]. How strange!" (010-072). The phrase "his race" reflects [[jake-brown|Jake]]'s expanding consciousness — he is beginning to recognize a transnational Black community extending beyond the United States. That a Black republic exists "just down there" from an American imperial project collapses the distance between Black sovereignty and American domination.

Later in the novel, a passage references a character "after keeping him in [[panama|Panama]] and bringing him to New York" (020-043), suggesting [[panama|Panama]] as a site of detention or indentured labor — a limbo between origin and destination in the migrant's journey. This brief reference gestures toward the thousands of Caribbean workers who passed through Panama during and after the Canal's construction, many under conditions of semi-forced labor.

## Historical Context

The Panama Canal (completed 1914) was built by a multinational workforce that included tens of thousands of Afro-Caribbean laborers, primarily from Barbados, Jamaica, and other British West Indian colonies. These workers were subjected to a racialized wage system — "gold" (paid to white American workers) versus "silver" (paid to non-white workers) — that institutionalized discrimination on the Canal Zone.

After the Canal's completion, many West Indian workers migrated onward to the United States, particularly to [[harlem|Harlem]] and other Black urban centers, forming part of the broader post-WWI Caribbean diaspora that McKay himself belonged to. [[panama|Panama]] thus represents a node in the triangular geography of Black migration: Caribbean origin, Panamanian transit, and Harlem destination.

McKay's inclusion of [[panama|Panama]] — however brief — registers the centrality of the Canal to early 20th-century Black consciousness. The Canal was not just an engineering marvel but a racialized labor system that shaped the movement of Black bodies across the hemisphere.
