**Brownskin** is a color term used throughout Claude McKay's *Home to Harlem* to describe brown-complexioned Black women and men. The term functions as both a physical descriptor and a social category within the novel's elaborate taxonomy of skin color, which ranges from "chocolate-brown" and "walnut-brown" to lighter shades of "yellow" and "high-yaller."

## Color as Desire

The novel's opening pages establish brownskin as an object of desire and nostalgia. Jake, returning from Europe, expresses his longing for Harlem's women in explicitly color-coded terms:

> "I'm crazy to see again the brown-skin chippies 'long Lenox Ave…" (001-008)

This passage links brownskin not only to beauty but to place — the "brown-skin chippies" are inseparable from Lenox Avenue, part of the geography of desire that draws Jake home.

## The Color Spectrum

The novel deploys a precise vocabulary of color that positions brownskin within a spectrum:

> "…their chocolate-brown and walnut-brown girls…" (001-029)

> "A little brown girl aimed the arrow of her e…" (002-006)

> "…brown gals waiting for the brown bo…" (001-033)

These variants — chocolate-brown, walnut-brown, brown — establish a finely graded color consciousness that permeates the novel's social world.

## Brown Men, Brown Women

Brownskin applies to both men and women, though it carries different social meanings:

> "Black and brown men sat at two tables in one…" (002-029)

This passage suggests that even within Harlem's Black community, distinctions between "black" and "brown" men shaped social groupings and interactions.

## Social Significance

The novel's attention to color — and brownskin as a specific category within it — reflects the complex color politics of early twentieth-century Black America. Lighter skin could confer social advantages, but it also generated tensions and resentments. McKay's nuanced treatment of brownskin characters avoids simple moralizing, instead presenting color as one of many dimensions of identity in Harlem's dense social world.
