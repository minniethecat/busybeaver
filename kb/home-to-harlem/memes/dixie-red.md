**Dixie Red** is a minor character in [[claude-mckay|Claude McKay]]'s *Home to [[harlem|Harlem]]*, the owner of a poolroom that functions as a key social space for the novel's working-class male characters. Though Dixie Red himself never appears directly in a scene, his establishment is referenced across multiple chapters as a regular haunt.

## Role in the Novel

Dixie Red's poolroom serves as one of [[harlem|Harlem]]'s informal social institutions alongside [[uncle-doc|Uncle Doc]]'s [[saloon]] and [[jake-brown|Jake]]'s apartment. It is mentioned in the same breath as these establishments as part of the fabric of neighborhood life: "[[uncle-doc|Uncle Doc]]'s saloon and Dixie Red's poolroom." (005-004) The poolroom appears across chapters 5, 7, 9, 16, and 19, marking it as one of the novel's most frequently revisited locations and a consistent backdrop to the male characters' social routines.

## Characterization

### Personality and Traits

Dixie Red is characterized indirectly through the reputation and clientele of his establishment. His poolroom attracts "all the shark players" (016-042), suggesting he runs a serious [[gambling]] venue rather than a casual social club. The poolroom is a site of masculine contest, where fights break out — [[zeddy-plummer|Zeddy]] recalls having "had to fight a fellow in Dixie Red's poolroom, for calling him a liver-fed." (007-071)

### Relationships

- [[billy-biasse|Billy Biasse]] — regular patron; seen leaving the poolroom with [[jake-brown|Jake]] (019-007)
- [[jake-brown|Jake Brown]] — regular patron; "strolled down to Dixie Red's poolroom and played awhile" (009-023)
- [[uncle-doc|Uncle Doc]] — fellow neighborhood establishment owner; saloon is paired with the poolroom in the novel's geography

## Key Scenes

Dixie Red's poolroom first appears in chapter 5 as part of the [[harlem|Harlem]] landscape that [[jake-brown|Jake]] navigates. (005-004) It is the setting for a fight in chapter 7 when [[zeddy-plummer|Zeddy]] is called "a liver-fed" and must defend himself. (007-071) In chapter 9, [[jake-brown|Jake]] visits casually: "He strolled down to Dixie Red's poolroom and played awhile." (009-023) By chapter 16, the poolroom's reputation is established as a place for serious gambling — "All the shark players of Dixie Red's poolroom were proud of losing money." (016-042) In the novel's closing chapter, [[jake-brown|Jake]] and [[billy-biasse|Billy Biasse]] are seen "leaving Dixie Red's poolroom together." (019-007)

## Themes

Dixie Red's poolroom represents the informal economy and social infrastructure of working-class [[harlem|Harlem]]. Alongside [[uncle-doc|Uncle Doc]]'s saloon, it forms part of a network of black-owned leisure establishments that anchor the neighborhood's male social life. The poolroom is a space of masculine competition — both at the table and between patrons — where reputation, money, and pride are wagered and contested.
