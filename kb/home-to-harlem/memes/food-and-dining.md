# Food and Dining

Food and dining in [[claude-mckay|Claude McKay]]'s *Home to [[harlem|Harlem]]* operates at multiple levels: as a marker of [[harlem|Harlem]]'s working-class culinary culture, as the economic foundation of the [[the-railroad|railroad]] dining-car world where [[jake-brown|Jake]] earns his living, and as a sensory vehicle for McKay's celebration of Black [[pleasure-seeking|pleasure]] and community.

## Definition

In the novel's world, "food" encompasses both the Southern cooking traditions carried north by [[migration|Great Migration]] [[migration|migrants]] and the institutional cuisine of the [[the-railroad|Pennsylvania Railroad]] [[dining-car|dining car]]. Food marks class boundaries — from [[harlem|Harlem]]'s "fried chicken" feeds to [[the-chef|the dining-car chef]]'s tyranny over "the company's foodstuffs" — and serves as a medium of care, community, and exploitation. (012-005)

## Cultural Context

### Harlem Renaissance Context

Food culture in 1920s [[harlem|Harlem]] was shaped by the [[great-migration|Great Migration]], which brought Southern Black culinary traditions — fried chicken, candied sweet potatoes, corn pone — into the urban North. These foods carried regional identity, family memory, and community ritual. At the same time, the [[prohibition|Prohibition]]-era [[cabaret-culture|cabaret]] and [[buffet-flat|buffet flat]] economy made drinking and eating intertwined social acts. The [[dining-car|dining car]] itself was a site of Black labor organization, where [[the-railroad|railroad]] corporations employed Black men as cooks, [[waiter|waiters]], and chefs within a rigid racial hierarchy. (004-005)

### In the Novel

[[jake-brown|Jake]]'s identity is shaped by food labor. He works as a "third cook" on the [[the-railroad|Pennsylvania Railroad]] [[dining-car|dining car]], where "the short, stout, hard-and-horny chef was terrible as a rhinoceros" (010-003). The dining-car kitchen is a "coffin-shaped" space (010-003) — simultaneously the source of [[jake-brown|Jake]]'s income and a site of brutal hierarchy. The chef tyrannizes his staff: "Don't rush me, nigger," [[the-chef|the chef]] bellowed at the [[waiter|waiters]] (010-008).

Yet McKay also presents food as profound communal [[pleasure-seeking|pleasure]]. At [[aunt-hattie|Aunt Hattie]]'s table: "Fricassee chicken and rice. Green peas. Stewed apples" (003-027). [[gin-head-susy|Susy]]'s cooking wins [[jake-brown|Jake]]'s loyalty: "She cooked delicious food — home-cooked food they called it" (003-028). The [[waiter|waiters]]' own meal is lavish: "First a great platter of fried chicken" (012-057).

The chef — "a great black bundle of concentrated energy" (012-004) — is a complex figure. He "always had his food ready on time, feeding the help better than any chef they had ever known" (012-004), yet he steals "choice brands of the company's foodstuffs" (012-005). The [[dining-car|dining car]]'s economy of theft and favor — "Other dining-car men had devised a system of making gifts of the company's goods to them" (012-005) — reveals food as a currency of power among Black [[the-railroad|railroad]] workers.

[[ray|Ray]], the Haitian intellectual [[waiter]], disrupts the kitchen's racial logic by refusing chicken-eating stereotypes: during the chicken feast, [[ray|Ray]]'s "stomach turned. He left the food and went outside" (012-058). When [[jake-brown|Jake]] insists "the food is all right," (012-059), the exchange marks the distance between [[jake-brown|Jake]]'s un-self-conscious appetite and [[ray|Ray]]'s racial self-consciousness.

Alcohol accompanies food throughout. [[gin-whisky|Gin]] — "good old red Kentucky" (004-082) — flows with meals at [[buffet-flat|buffet flats]] and [[cabaret-culture|cabarets]]. The phrase "he was not hungry for food" (011-009) signals moments when deeper hungers — for sex, freedom, recognition — displace the bodily appetite.

## Variations & Related Terms

- **Home cooking** — "home-cooked food they called it" (003-028): [[gin-head-susy|Susy]]'s cuisine, representing domestic care and Southern tradition
- **Fried chicken** — The recurring dish of celebration and community: "The fried chicken, with sweet potatoes, was excellent" (014-068); also a racial stereotype [[ray|Ray]] refuses
- **Corn pone** — Marked as rural [[alabama|Alabama]] food: "the idea of eating chicken gave him a spasm. Of the odds and ends of corn pone" (012-007)
- **Chicken-loving nigger** — [[ray|Ray]]'s phrase (reported by [[the-chef|the chef]]): "All this heah talk about chicken-loving niggers" (012-008), a racial stereotype that [[the-chef|the chef]] himself perpetuates
- **Gin / Kentucky** — "Gimme [[gin-whisky|gin]] every time or good old red Kentucky" (004-082): the preferred [[gin-whisky|alcohol]]s of [[harlem|Harlem]]'s working class

## Key Passages

> She cooked delicious food — home-cooked food they called it, well made and well served. (003-028)

[[gin-head-susy|Susy]]'s cooking wins [[jake-brown|Jake]]'s affection and loyalty. The repetition of "home-cooked" — deployed by the community, not just the narrator — underscores the migrant's nostalgia for Southern domesticity recreated in [[harlem|Harlem]].

> First a great platter of fried chicken. The chef had piled it up high. Then came corn on the cob and sweet potatoes and peas and string beans and fresh biscuits. (012-057)

The [[waiter|dining-car waiter]]s' meal is excessive and communal. The chef's generosity with food — however political — creates moments of shared [[pleasure-seeking|pleasure]] within the exploitative [[the-railroad|railroad]] system.

> "All this heah talk about chicken-loving niggers," he growled contemptuously. (012-008)

The chef's racial self-contempt, expressed through food stereotype, reveals the internalized racism that pervades dining-car hierarchy. [[ray|Ray]] refuses to eat, rejecting the stereotype by abstaining entirely.

## Historical Significance

The [[pennsylvania-railroad|Pennsylvania Railroad]] [[dining-car|dining car]] was a major employer of Black men in the 1910s–1920s, part of the broader [[pullman-company|Pullman]] porter and [[the-railroad|railroad]] service economy that created a Black labor aristocracy within segregation. Fried chicken — central to the novel's food imagery — has a complex history in [[africa|Africa]]n American culture: a dish of celebration and community that was also weaponized as racist caricature (most notoriously in the film *The Birth of a Nation*). McKay's treatment of chicken-eating navigates this dual legacy, presenting it as both authentic [[pleasure-seeking|pleasure]] and stigmatized identity.
