# French Revolution

The French Revolution (1789–1799) appears in [[claude-mckay|Claude McKay]]'s *[[Home to Harlem]]* (1928) not as a historical event but as a political idea — specifically, as the ideological source of "Liberté, Egalité, Fraternité," the universalist principles that, the novel argues, reached across the Atlantic to inspire the [[haitian-revolution|Haitian Revolution]] and "lift up" enslaved Black people to freedom. It is invoked by the [[haiti|Haitian]] [[waiter]] who educates [[jake-brown|Jake]] during a [[the-railroad|train]] journey, and it functions in the novel as a demonstration that revolutionary ideas can transcend [[race-and-color|race]] and geography.

## In the Novel

The French Revolution enters the novel in Chapter X, during the extended conversation between [[jake-brown|Jake]] and the Haitian [[waiter]] on the [[pennsylvania-railroad|Pennsylvania Railroad]] [[dining-car|dining car]]. The [[waiter]], a student from [[haiti|Haiti]] who speaks [[france|French]] as his native language, is telling [[jake-brown|Jake]] the history of his country, and he t[[race-and-color|race]]s Haiti's independence directly to the French Revolution:

> "He learned that the universal spirit of the French Revolution had reached and lifted up the slaves far away in that remote island; that Black Haiti's independence was more dramatic and picturesque than the United States' independence and that it was a strange, almost unimaginable eruption of the beautiful ideas of the 'Liberté, Egalité, Fraternité' of Mankind, that shook the foundations of that romantic era." (010-051)

The passage makes several linked claims: that the Revolution's ideas were "universal" — not bounded by [[race-and-color|race]] or nation; that they "reached and lifted up the slaves" — traveled across the Atlantic and produced material liberation; and that the [[haitian-revolution|Haitian Revolution]] was more "dramatic and picturesque" than the American one — a claim that re-centers Black agency within the revolutionary tradition.

The [[waiter]] goes on to describe [[toussaint-louverture|Toussaint L'Ouverture]]'s leadership and Napoleon's betrayal, and the French Revolution appears as both ori[[gin-whisky|gin]] and irony: the same [[france|France]] that proclaimed universal liberty sent an army to crush Haiti's freedom. The Revolution's ideals are real, but their application is contested — and Black people had to fight to claim them.

## The Haitian Connection

The novel's treatment of the French Revolution is inseparable from its treatment of the [[haitian-revolution|Haitian Revolution]] (1791–1804). The [[waiter]] presents the sequence as causal: the French Revolution created the ideological conditions that made the [[haitian-revolution|Haitian Revolution]] possible. The slaves of Saint-Domingue heard the language of "Liberté, Egalité, Fraternité" and understood it to include themselves — a radical extension of the Revolution's logic that [[france|French]] slaveowners violently resisted.

This is presented as a lesson in how ideas work: they spread, they are claimed, they exceed their ori[[gin-whisky|gin]]ators' intentions. The [[france|French]] revolutionaries may not have ima[[gin-whisky|gin]]ed their slogans applying to Black slaves in the Caribbean, but the slaves ima[[gin-whisky|gin]]ed it, and their ima[[gin-whisky|gin]]ation — backed by arms — made it real.

For [[jake-brown|Jake]], an American Black man who served in [[france|France]] during [[world-war-i|World War I]], this history is transformative: "[[jake-brown|Jake]] felt like one passing through a dream, vivid in rich, varied [[race-and-color|color]]s. It was revelation beautiful in his mind" (010-072). The French Revolution reaches [[jake-brown|Jake]] through Haiti — through the Black translation of European ideals into Black freedom.

## Ideological Significance

The novel presents the French Revolution's ideals — liberty, equality, fraternity — as a counter-tradition to American democracy. The United States declared independence in 1776 and maintained chattel slavery for another 89 years. [[france|France]] declared liberty in 1789, abolished slavery in its colonies in 1794 (Napoleon restored it in 1802), and the Haitian revolutionaries abolished it permanently by force of arms in 1804. In this timeline, the novel suggests, the [[france|French]] revolutionary tradition has a stronger claim to universalism than the American one — not because [[france|France]] was less racist, but because its revolution's ideas were claimed and realized by Black people.

This argument is consistent with McKay's broader political sympathies. A socialist and anti-colonial thinker, McKay was drawn to revolutionary universalism — to the idea that liberation is indivisible across [[race-and-color|race]] and nation. The French Revolution, in the novel's rendering, is proof that such universalism is possible: that ideas can cross oceans, that slogans belong to whoever claims them, that Black people are not outside the revolutionary tradition but at its most dramatic realization.

## Historical Context

The actual French Revolution (1789–1799) overthrew the Bourbon monarchy, established a republic, executed Louis XVI, and produced the Declaration of the Rights of Man and of the Citizen (1789). Its most radical phase, the Jacobin period under Robespierre, abolished slavery in [[france|French]] colonies in 1794 — making [[france|France]] the first European power to do so. Napoleon reversed this abolition in 1802, and slavery was not permanently abolished in [[france|French]] territories until 1848.

The [[haitian-revolution|Haitian Revolution]] (1791–1804), led by [[toussaint-louverture|Toussaint L'Ouverture]] and later Jean-Jacques Dessalines, was directly shaped by these events. The enslaved population of Saint-Domingue drew on [[france|French]] revolutionary rhetoric, [[africa|Africa]]n military traditions, and the material fact of their own majority to defeat [[france|French]], Spanish, and British forces and establish the world's first Black republic.

For McKay, writing in 1928 — in the midst of the [[harlem-renaissance|Harlem Renaissance]] and the "New Negro" movement, with the U.S. occupation of Haiti (1915–1934) still ongoing — this history was not remote. It was a usable past, a demonstration that Black liberation was neither impossible nor dependent on white benevolence.
