**Vaudeville** was the dominant form of variety entertainment in the United States from the 1880s through the 1920s, and forms an important cultural backdrop to [[claude-mckay|Claude McKay]]'s *[[home-to-harlem|Home to Harlem]]*.

## In the Novel

Though the word "vaudeville" does not appear directly in the novel's text, the world of Black vaudeville is present throughout *Home to Harlem*. Characters "gossiped reminiscently" about vaudeville pioneers — "[[bert-williams|Bert Williams]], [[george-walker|George Walker]] and [[aida-overton-walker|Aida Overton Walker]], [[anita-patti-brown|Anita Patti Brown]] and [[cole-and-johnson|Cole and Johnson]]." (021-007) These performers, who rose to fame on the vaudeville stage, represent an earlier generation of Black entertainers whose achievements were revered by the novel's Harlem characters. Bert Williams in particular was the first Black star to break vaudeville's color line, becoming one of the most popular comedians in America while working within the constraints of the minstrel tradition.
