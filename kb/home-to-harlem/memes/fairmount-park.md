**Fairmount Park** is a large urban park in [[philadelphia|Philadelphia]], Pennsylvania, that appears in [[claude-mckay|Claude McKay]]'s *[[home-to-harlem|Home to Harlem]]* as a setting for social encounters between characters in Philadelphia.

## Location

### In the Novel's World

Fairmount Park serves as a leisure and meeting space for Philadelphia's Black residents. [[ray|Ray]]'s acquaintance reports seeing [[madame-mulberry|Madame Mulberry]] there: "I saw Madame Laura in Fairmount Park and I told her you were sick." (015-025) Madame Laura herself later recounts "I was riding in Fairmount Park one afternoon, just taking the air as usual" (016-004) when she encountered a friend who informed her of [[ray|Ray]]'s illness. The park functions as a place of informal social circulation — a space where news and gossip travel.

## Significance to the Narrative

Fairmount Park appears during the Philadelphia section of the novel (chapters 13–16), when [[ray|Ray]] is convalescing and the narrative shifts temporarily away from [[harlem|Harlem]]. The park's appearances are brief but they establish Philadelphia as having its own social geography, paralleling [[harlem|Harlem]]'s promenading culture on Seventh Avenue.

## Historical Context

Fairmount Park, established in 1855, is one of the largest urban park systems in the United States, spanning over 2,000 acres along the Schuylkill River. During the early twentieth century it was a popular destination for Philadelphia residents of all backgrounds.

## Appearances

Fairmount Park appears or is referenced in chapters 15 and 16. (015-025, 016-004)
