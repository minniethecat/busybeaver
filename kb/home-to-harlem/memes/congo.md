**[[the-congo|The Congo]]** is a [[cabaret-culture|cabaret]] and nightclub in [[harlem|Harlem]] that appears throughout [[claude-mckay|Claude McKay]]'s *Home to [[harlem|Harlem]]*. Along with [[the-baltimore-cabaret|the Baltimore]], it is one of the novel's two primary nightlife venues, and is specifically associated with the entertainer [[rose-congo|Congo Rose]], who takes her stage name from the establishment.

## Location

### In the Novel's World

[[the-congo|The Congo]] is a [[harlem|Harlem]] [[cabaret-culture|cabaret]] at the heart of the neighborhood's working-class social scene. [[zeddy-plummer|Zeddy Plummer]] describes it as "the best pick-me-up" in [[harlem|Harlem]] (004-054), and characters gravitate there habitually: after an evening out, "there they gravitated to [[the-congo|the Congo]]." (007-085) The venue is described as "thick, dark-colorful, and fascinating" (004-059) — a space dense with sensory and racial intensity.

## Significance to the Narrative

[[the-congo|The Congo]] is first introduced in chapter 4 as the venue where [[rose-congo|Congo Rose]] performs as "[[rose-congo|the Congo entertainer]], singing at the tables." (004-078) It is here that [[jake-brown|Jake]] first sees [[rose-congo|Rose]], who "left [[the-congo|the Congo]] with both hands entwined in [[jake-brown|Jake]]'s." (004-086) The [[cabaret-culture|cabaret]] functions as a space of romantic encounter, entertainment, and social networking for [[harlem|Harlem]]'s working class.

McKay positions [[the-congo|the Congo]] as distinctively African in its atmosphere — "[[the-congo|The Congo]] was African" (004-006) — and suggests that "anything in New York always reached [[the-congo|the Congo]] first." (004-006) This description racializes the space as primal and culturally authentic, in contrast to more assimilated [[harlem|Harlem]] venues.

## Appearances

[[the-congo|The Congo]] appears or is referenced in chapters 4, 7–10, 12, 14, 16, and 19. (004-001)–(019-079) It is a consistent backdrop to the novel's nightlife sequences and serves as a gathering point for characters including [[jake-brown|Jake]], [[zeddy-plummer|Zeddy]], [[billy-biasse|Billy Biasse]], [[rose-congo|Rose]], and other regulars.

## Historical Context

[[the-congo|The Congo]] represents a type of [[harlem|Harlem]] [[cabaret-culture|cabaret]] — distinct from the more formal "dancing academies" and "ballrooms" — where working-class patrons could drink, dance, and socialize. Its African-themed name reflects the era's vogue for primitivist exoticism in [[harlem|Harlem]] entertainment venues. The [[cabaret-culture|cabaret]]'s function as both workplace (for performers like [[rose-congo|Rose]]) and leisure space (for patrons like [[jake-brown|Jake]] and [[zeddy-plummer|Zeddy]]) illustrates the intertwined economies of entertainment and community in [[prohibition|Prohibition]]-era [[harlem|Harlem]].
