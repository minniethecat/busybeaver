# 135th Street

A key east-west corridor in [[harlem|Harlem]], 135th Street appears in *Home to [[harlem|Harlem]]* as a street of thresholds — it calls to [[jake-brown|Jake]] as he arrives home from the war, anchors the nightlife district of [[seventh-avenue|Seventh Avenue]], and becomes the final setting of his romance with [[felice|Felice]].

## Location

### In the Novel

135th Street is invoked at the novel's opening as one of the three Harlem streets that summon [[jake-brown|Jake]] home: "It was two years since he had left Harlem. Fifth Avenue, Lenox Avenue, and 135th Street, with their chocolate-brown and walnut-brown girls, were calling him" (001-011). The street is named not for its buildings but for its women — a characteristic McKay move that maps urban geography through desire.

After arriving in [[harlem|Harlem]], [[jake-brown|Jake]]'s first promenade establishes the street's role as a boundary marker in his mental map: "He went for a promenade on Seventh Avenue between 135th and 140th Streets. He thrilled to Harlem. His blood was hot. His eyes were alert as he sniffed the street like a hound. Seventh Avenue was nice, a little too nice that night" (001-016). The stretch between 135th and 140th defines the zone of his initial reconnaissance — the heart of Black [[harlem|Harlem]]'s commercial and nightlife district.

The street returns at the novel's climax, when [[jake-brown|Jake]] waits for [[felice|Felice]] at "the corner of Seventh Avenue and 135th Street" (020-068). This corner becomes the fulcrum of the novel's romantic resolution: [[felice|Felice]] leaves the man she was "merely makeshifting with" and hurries "as much as she could along 135th Street, bumping a cumbersome portmanteau on the pavement and holding up one unruly lemonbright silk stocking with her left hand" (020-097). [[jake-brown|Jake]] takes her bag; they buy chicken, ice-cream, and cigarettes; and the novel's final journey — toward Chicago and an uncertain future — begins on this street corner.

### Historical Geography

The real 135th Street in the 1920s was a major crosstown artery running through the center of Black [[harlem|Harlem]]. The intersection of 135th Street and Seventh Avenue was a particularly dense node of nightlife, commerce, and street-level social activity. The street also intersected with [[lenox-avenue|Lenox Avenue]], forming the grid that defined Harlem's nightlife corridor.

## Significance

135th Street functions in the novel as a **spatial anchor** for [[jake-brown|Jake]]'s relationship to [[harlem|Harlem]]. It is named at the novel's beginning as one of the streets that calls him home, and it returns at the novel's end as the place where he waits for [[felice|Felice]] and begins a new journey. This structural symmetry — the street as point of arrival and point of departure — gives 135th Street a symbolic weight beyond its geographic function.

The street also encodes **class and color distinctions** within the novel's urban geography. [[jake-brown|Jake]]'s early confusion about a street address — "Street and streets! 132nd, 33rd, 34th. It wasn't 135th and it wasn't beyond theah" (003-008) — reveals his disorientation in the grid of numbers that maps social status within Black [[harlem|Harlem]]. Knowing the right street is knowing where you belong.
