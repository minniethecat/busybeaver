# Barrelhouse

A rough-and-ready drinking establishment catering to working-class Black patrons in the 1920s — cheaper and more informal than a [[saloon|saloon]], less exclusive than a [[buffet-flat|buffet flat]], and positioned at the bottom rung of [[harlem|Harlem]]'s nightlife hierarchy. The [[barrelhouse|barrelhouse]] was an all-night refuge for longshoremen, steelworkers, and railroad men seeking [[gin-whisky|whiskey]] and companionship after hours.

## Definition

In McKay's *Home to [[harlem|Harlem]]*, a "barrelhouse" is an informal, low-end drinking establishment. The term suggests a rough-hewn, unadorned space — the name itself evokes wooden barrels, the raw containers of illicit [[gin-whisky|liquor]]. Unlike the [[cabaret-culture|cabaret]] with its live music and dancing, or the [[buffet-flat|buffet flat]] with its curated clientele and elegant appointments, the [[barrelhouse|barrelhouse]] was a bare-bones operation: a place to buy drink and perhaps find company, nothing more. (007-160)(011-045)

## Cultural Context

### Harlem Renaissance Context

The [[barrelhouse|barrelhouse]] occupied the lowest tier of [[harlem|Harlem]]'s stratified nightlife economy. The narrator's famous taxonomy in Chapter XIII distinguishes "them of the [[dickty|dickty]] [[buffet-flat|buffet flats]]; then the low-down [[speakeasy]] customers; the [[cabaret-culture|cabaret]] babies" (013-098). While the [[barrelhouse|barrelhouse]] is not explicitly named in this taxonomy, its description as "all-night" and associated with [[gin-whisky|raw whiskey]] places it firmly among the "low-down" establishments — those venues so unadorned they barely qualified as [[speakeasy|speakeasies]].

The [[barrelhouse|barrelhouse]]'s all-night operation was significant. Most [[saloon|saloons]] and [[speakeasy|speakeasies]] kept conventional hours; an establishment open "all-night" (007-160) served the nocturnal schedules of [[longshoreman|longshoremen]] coming off late shifts, railroad workers on irregular layovers, and the general population of [[harlem|Harlem]]'s after-midnight street life. The [[barrelhouse|barrelhouse]] was a utility, not a destination — a place of necessity rather than pleasure.

### In the Novel

The [[barrelhouse|barrelhouse]] appears twice in the novel, each time in a different city and serving a different function.

In a [[harlem|Harlem]] context, after losing his "little brown," [[jake-brown|Jake]] "went straight to an all-night [[barrelhouse|barrelhouse]] and bought a half a pint of whiskey" (007-160). The verb "straight" — no detour, no hesitation — signals the [[barrelhouse|barrelhouse]]'s functional role: it is where one goes for immediate, uncomplicated relief. There is no social ritual, no performance, no courtship — just a transaction. The "half a pint" suggests solitary drinking, the [[barrelhouse|barrelhouse]] as medicine for disappointment.

In [[pittsburgh|Pittsburgh]], the [[barrelhouse|barrelhouse]] takes on a more communal character. [[jake-brown|Jake]] and a companion find that a "little place was something of a [[barrelhouse|barrelhouse]] [[speakeasy]], crowded with black steelworkers" (011-045). Here the [[barrelhouse|barrelhouse]] is a workers' refuge — a space of racial and class solidarity where industrial laborers gather after their shifts. The "little place" description and the fusion of terms ("barrelhouse speakeasy") suggest a venue that eludes easy categorization: too rough to be a proper [[speakeasy]], too open to be a private [[buffet-flat|buffet flat]].

The [[barrelhouse|barrelhouse]]'s association with heavy industry — steelworkers in [[pittsburgh|Pittsburgh]], [[longshoreman|longshoremen]] in [[harlem|Harlem]] — places it at the intersection of manual labor and nightlife. It is the drinking space of men whose bodies are worn down by physical work, who drink not for entertainment but for escape or relief.

McKay also provides context for the all-night culture that sustains the [[barrelhouse|barrelhouse]]: Jake and his friends "would like to go to a little open-all-night place" (011-043), and indeed they find one in the [[barrelhouse|barrelhouse]] speakeasy. The desire for a space that never closes reflects the irregular temporality of working-class life — the [[barrelhouse|barrelhouse]] is open when everything else is shut.

## Key Passages

> He went straight to an all-night barrelhouse and bought a half a pint of whiskey. (007-160)

This brief, stark passage distills the [[barrelhouse|barrelhouse]]'s essence: direct, transactional, nocturnal. [[jake-brown|Jake]] does not go to a cabaret or seek out company — he goes to the most basic venue available, buys drink, and the sentence ends. The [[barrelhouse|barrelhouse]] is the terminus of a disappointed night.

> The little place was something of a barrelhouse speakeasy, crowded with black steelworkers. (011-045)

The qualifying phrase "something of a" indicates the hybrid, informal nature of the [[barrelhouse|barrelhouse]]. It is not quite a [[barrelhouse|barrelhouse]], not quite a [[speakeasy]] — but occupies the overlap between both categories. The presence of steelworkers identifies the clientele as industrial labor, reinforcing the [[barrelhouse|barrelhouse]]'s class positioning at the working-class bottom of the nightlife hierarchy.

## Variations & Related Terms

- **[[speakeasy|Speakeasy]]** — The broader category of illicit drinking venue; the "low-down speakeasy" (013-098) is the closest relative to the barrelhouse in McKay's taxonomy, but even a low-down speakeasy implies some pretense of respectability
- **[[buffet-flat|Buffet Flat]]** — The private, curated alternative operating at the opposite end of the nightlife spectrum; where the buffet flat is exclusive, the barrelhouse is open to any working man with cash
- **[[saloon|Saloon]]** — A more established, name-brand drinking establishment (Uncle Doc's, for instance); saloons have regulars and reputations, while the barrelhouse is more transient and impersonal
- **[[dickty|Dickty]]** — The class marker that separates "dickty buffet flats" from "low-down" barrelhouse-type venues
- **[[cabaret-culture|Cabaret]]** — Public entertainment venues with music and dancing; the barrelhouse offers no entertainment beyond the bottle

## Historical Significance

The term "barrelhouse" has roots in the late 19th-century South, where it originally referred to cheap, rough drinking establishments attached to barrel-making operations or located near wharves where barrels of goods were loaded and unloaded. By the 1920s, it had migrated north with the Great Migration, acquiring in Black usage an association with working-class authenticity.

The barrelhouse was also the namesake of an early, rough style of piano blues — "barrelhouse piano" — characterized by heavy, percussive left-hand patterns and played in the cheap drinking joints that gave the style its name. This musical connection places the barrelhouse at the root of the [[blues|blues]] and early [[jazz|jazz]] traditions, even though McKay's novel does not explicitly describe music in the barrelhouse scenes.

The barrelhouse represents the economic floor of [[prohibition|Prohibition]]-era drinking culture — the point below which no "establishment" could sink and still function. Its persistence in the novel and in the historical record testifies to the demand for alcohol across all class levels of Black [[harlem|Harlem]], and to the entrepreneurial response that created venues for every pocketbook.
