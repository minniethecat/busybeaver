**[[pennsylvania|Pennsylvania]]** is a U.S. state in the Mid-Atlantic and Northeastern regions, referenced in [[claude-mckay|Claude McKay]]'s *Home to [[harlem|Harlem]]* as part of the railroad geography that structures the novel's second half. The state appears in a single descriptive passage, but its presence situates the novel's characters within a broader American geography of industrial labor, migration, and movement.

## Location

### Geography

[[pennsylvania|Pennsylvania]] lies between the Atlantic coast and the Midwest, bordered by [[New York]] to the north and east, [[new-jersey|New Jersey]] to the east, [[maryland|Maryland]] to the south, [[west-virginia|West Virginia]] to the southwest, [[ohio|Ohio]] to the west, and Lake Erie to the northwest. Its largest city, [[philadelphia|Philadelphia]], and its steel-producing western anchor, [[pittsburgh|Pittsburgh]], were both significant destinations during the [[great-migration|Great Migration]], drawing Black workers from the [[south|South]] to industrial employment.

[[pennsylvania|Pennsylvania]] is a critical link in the railroad networks connecting the Northeast to the Midwest: the [[pennsylvania-railroad|Pennsylvania Railroad]], one of the largest corporations of the era, operated thousands of miles of track through the state, employing large numbers of Black workers as Pullman porters, waiters, and dining-car cooks.

### In the Novel

The novel's only direct reference to [[pennsylvania|Pennsylvania]] occurs during a train journey: the narrative describes movement "over the heart of the vast gray Pennsylvania country" (010-002), with "the huge black animal" of the locomotive traversing the landscape. This passage appears when [[jake-brown|Jake]] is working as a cook on the railroad, part of the novel's shift from [[harlem|Harlem]]'s cabaret geography to the geography of rail labor that dominates the middle chapters.

## Quotations

McKay's description of the train crossing the Pennsylvania landscape:

> Over the heart of the vast gray Pennsylvania country the huge black animal snorted and roared, with sounding rods and couplings, pulling a long chain of dull-brown boxes packed with people and things, trailing on the blue-cold air its white masses of breath.
>
> (010-002)

## Significance

[[pennsylvania|Pennsylvania]] functions in the novel primarily as railroad geography — the terrain crossed between [[harlem|Harlem]] and the industrial Midwest. It is not a destination but a landscape traversed, part of the "vast gray" American interior that [[jake-brown|Jake]], [[ray]], and other railroad workers experience as occupational scenery rather than place.

This treatment is consistent with McKay's broader geographic imagination in the novel. Unlike [[harlem|Harlem]], which is rendered with intimate sensory detail, or [[pittsburgh|Pittsburgh]], which is named as a specific destination, [[pennsylvania|Pennsylvania]] fades into the "vast gray" — a blur of countryside through a train window. The state's appearance is structural rather than experiential: it is the space between more significant places, the geographic evidence that [[jake-brown|Jake]]'s railroad employment takes him beyond [[harlem|Harlem]]'s familiar streets into the broader American continent.

## Historical Context

[[pennsylvania|Pennsylvania]] was a major destination — and transit corridor — for Black migration during the [[great-migration|Great Migration]]. [[pittsburgh|Pittsburgh]]'s steel mills and [[philadelphia|Philadelphia]]'s diversified industrial base drew tens of thousands of Black workers from the [[south|South]] between 1910 and 1930. The [[pennsylvania-railroad|Pennsylvania Railroad]] itself was a major employer of Black labor, particularly in dining car and porter services, making the state's rail infrastructure directly relevant to [[jake-brown|Jake]]'s occupation as a railroad cook.

McKay himself knew this geography. After arriving in the United States from [[jamaica|Jamaica]], he worked briefly as a Pullman porter and later as a railroad waiter, experiencing firsthand the "vast gray" American landscape from the windows of dining cars and Pullman sleepers. The Pennsylvania countryside [[jake-brown|Jake]] passes through is thus a geography McKay knew not as a tourist or traveler, but as a worker.
