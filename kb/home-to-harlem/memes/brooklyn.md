**Brooklyn** is a New York City borough that appears in [[claude-mckay|Claude McKay]]'s *Home to [[harlem|Harlem]]* as a counterpoint to [[harlem|Harlem]] — a quieter, less vibrant space that characters sometimes visit but never prefer. It is associated with domestic life, failed nightlife ventures, and [[gin-head-susy|Susy]]'s brief assertion of independence.

## Location

### Geography

Brooklyn lies across the East River from Manhattan, accessible by the subway. Characters catch "the subway train for Brooklyn" (007-161), establishing it as a distinct but reachable part of the city.

### In the Novel's World

Brooklyn is rendered as a quieter alternative to [[harlem|Harlem]]'s intensity. [[gin-head-susy|Susy]] describes it defensively: "It's good and quiet ovah in Brooklyn" (007-156) — to which the response is dismissive: "Brooklyn ain't no better than [[harlem|Harlem]]." (007-158) A "new place that was opened in Brooklyn was freezing to death" (004-003), suggesting that Brooklyn's nightlife cannot compete with [[harlem|Harlem]]'s energy. A "grass-widow" living in Brooklyn works "as a cook downtown" (006-006), linking the borough to domestic labor and respectability.

## Significance to the Narrative

Brooklyn serves as a narrative foil to [[harlem|Harlem]]. When [[gin-head-susy|Susy]] threatens to retreat to Brooklyn after a fight, [[zeddy-plummer|Zeddy]]'s mockery — "Brooklyn ain't no better than [[harlem|Harlem]]" — reveals the hierarchy of neighborhoods in the characters' world. (007-158) [[jake-brown|Jake]] is skeptical of Brooklyn's appeal, challenging [[zeddy-plummer|Zeddy]]: "You got to show me that Brooklyn's got any life to it." (006-028) The failed Brooklyn [[cabaret-culture|cabaret]] — "freezing to death" — contrasts with the packed, vibrant venues of [[the-baltimore-cabaret|the Baltimore]] and [[the-congo|the Congo]] in [[harlem|Harlem]]. (004-003)

## From the Novel

A blunt verdict on Brooklyn's nightlife is delivered in passing:

> A fine new place that was opened in Brooklyn was freezing to death. Brooklyn never could support anything.
>
> (004-003)

## Historical Context

In the 1920s, Brooklyn was home to a smaller Black population than [[harlem|Harlem]], which had become the cultural capital of Black America. [[harlem|Harlem]]'s density of nightlife venues, entertainment, and community institutions made it the gravitational center of New York's Black social world, and other boroughs were perceived as quieter, less exciting spaces by comparison.

## Appearances

Brooklyn appears or is referenced in chapters 1, 4, 6–7, and 21. (001-009)–(021-008)
