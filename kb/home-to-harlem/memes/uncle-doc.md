Uncle Doc is the proprietor of a [[saloon]] on [[lenox-avenue|Lenox Avenue]] in [[claude-mckay|Claude McKay]]'s *Home to [[harlem|Harlem]]*. His establishment serves as a recurring social hub where [[jake-brown|Jake Brown]], [[zeddy-plummer|Zeddy Plummer]], and other [[longshoreman|longshoremen]] gather to drink, socialize, and conduct their business.

## Role in the Novel

Uncle Doc's saloon functions as one of the novel's key social spaces — a working-class counterpart to the [[cabaret-culture|cabarets]] and [[buffet-flat|buffet flats]]. It is where [[jake-brown|Jake]] leaves his suitcase upon arriving in [[harlem|Harlem]], where he and [[zeddy-plummer|Zeddy]] meet to plan their days, and where the rhythms of [[longshoreman]] life are mediated through drinking and conversation. Uncle Doc himself is a quiet but steady presence, pouring drinks and providing a stable anchor for the neighborhood's male social world.

## Characterization

### Personality and Traits

Uncle Doc is characterized by his reliability and taciturn presence. He serves [[jake-brown|Jake]] a glass of Scotch on his first visit and remembers to remind [[jake-brown|Jake]] that his suitcase is still there. (001-030) He pours Bourbon for [[zeddy-plummer|Zeddy]] without needing to be asked twice. (002-010) His saloon is described matter-of-factly as a gathering place — not glamorous, but essential to the social fabric of the neighborhood.

### Appearance

The text provides little physical description of Uncle Doc, focusing instead on the function of his establishment within the community.

## Key Scenes

[[jake-brown|Jake]] enters Uncle Doc's saloon in chapter 1, fresh from his arrival in [[harlem|Harlem]]. He calls for a glass of Scotch: "Gimme the siphon, Doc. I'm off the straight stuff." (001-030) Later, Uncle Doc reminds [[jake-brown|Jake]] that his suitcase is still there — a small act of caretaking. (002-020)

In chapter 2, [[zeddy-plummer|Zeddy]] arrives and Uncle Doc pours him Bourbon. The saloon fills with more customers, and [[jake-brown|Jake]] and [[zeddy-plummer|Zeddy]] "eased round to the short side of the bar" to make room. (002-010) The saloon becomes a recurring reference point: [[jake-brown|Jake]] divides his days "between [[rose-congo|Rose]] and Uncle Doc's saloon and [[dixie-red|Dixie Red]]'s [[poolroom]]." (003-010)

## Themes

Uncle Doc's saloon embodies the theme of working-class male community in [[harlem|Harlem]]. Unlike the [[cabaret-culture|cabarets]] with their [[jazz]], dancing, and sexual commerce, the saloon is a space of everyday ritual — drinking, talking, and maintaining the loose ties that bind a transient workforce. Uncle Doc himself represents the small-business ownership that offered one path to economic stability for Black [[harlem|Harlem]]ites.
