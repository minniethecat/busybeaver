# Pennsylvania Railroad

The [[the-railroad|railroad]] company that employed thousands of Black workers — including [[jake-brown|Jake]] as a [[food-and-dining|dining]]-car cook and [[ray]] as a [[waiter]] — during the [[great-migration|Great Migration]] era. The Pennsylvania Railroad (PRR) was the largest [[the-railroad|railroad]] in the United States by revenue and traffic, its network connecting [[new-york-city|New York]], [[philadelphia|Philadelphia]], [[pittsburgh|Pittsburgh]], [[chicago|Chicago]], and [[washington|Washington]] — precisely the cities that constitute the novel's Black urban geography.

## Overview

The Pennsylvania Railroad was chartered in 1846 and grew to dominate rail transport in the northeastern United States. By the 1920s, the "Pennsy" operated over 10,000 miles of track and employed more than 250,000 workers. Its flagship [[the-railroad|train]]s — including the Broadway Limited ([[new-york-city|New York]]–[[chicago|Chicago]]) and the [[pennsylvania|Pennsylvania]] Limited — were symbols of American industrial might.

For Black workers during the [[great-migration|Great Migration]], [[the-railroad|the railroad]] represented one of the few paths to stable, union-wage employment outside the Jim Crow South's agricultural economy. The [[pullman-company|Pullman Company]], which operated [[sleeping-late|sleeping]] and [[dining-car|dining car]]s under contract with [[the-railroad|railroad]]s including the PRR, was the largest single employer of Black workers in the United States during this period. [[pullman-company|Pullman]] porters, cooks, and [[waiter|waiters]] — almost exclusively Black men — formed the backbone of the Brotherhood of Sleeping Car Porters, the first [[africa|Africa]]n American labor union to win a collective bargaining agreement.

## Role in the Novel

[[jake-brown|Jake]]'s stint as a [[the-railroad|railroad]] cook on the Pennsylvania Railroad provides the setting for his meeting with [[ray]] and for the geographic mobility that maps the Black urban network of the 1920s. The [[food-and-dining|dining]]-car — the novel's recurring workplace setting — is a microcosm of Black labor relations: the white chief, the Black cooks and [[waiter|waiters]], the hierarchy of the kitchen, the tips and temptations and fights that structure the working day.

The [[the-railroad|railroad]] also enables the novel's geographic range. [[jake-brown|Jake]]'s route — [[harlem|Harlem]] to [[pittsburgh|Pittsburgh]] to [[philadelphia|Philadelphia]] and back — t[[race-and-color|race]]s the PRR's main line, the same tracks that carried hundreds of thousands of Black [[migration|migrants]] north during the [[great-migration|Great Migration]]. The [[food-and-dining|dining]]-car is thus both a workplace and a vehicle of history: the men who serve meals on the Pennsy are part of the same [[migration]] stream as the passengers they serve.

## Historical Context

### Black Employment on the Rails

By the 1920s, the [[pullman-company|Pullman Company]] employed approximately 12,000 Black workers — primarily as porters, but also as cooks, [[waiter|waiters]], and attendants. These jobs were among the best-paying available to Black men without college education, offering wages of $60–$80 per month plus tips. However, the work was demanding, the hours long, and the racial hierarchy of [[the-railroad|the railroad]] rigidly enforced: supervisory positions (conductors, en[[gin-whisky|gin]]eers, chief stewards) were reserved for white workers.

The Pennsylvania Railroad employed Black workers not only through [[pullman-company|Pullman]] but also directly in maintenance-of-way crews, station services, and [[food-and-dining|dining]]-car operations. The PRR's main line through [[philadelphia|Philadelphia]] — the city with the largest Black population north of the Mason-Dixon Line before the [[great-migration|Great Migration]] — made it a natural conduit for Black labor mobility.

### The Brotherhood of Sleeping Car Porters

Organized in 1925 by [[asa-philip-randolph|A. Philip Randolph]], the Brotherhood of Sleeping Car Porters (BSCP) fought for better wages, shorter hours, and dignity on the job. The union's struggle — which would not achieve a contract with [[pullman-company|Pullman]] until 1937 — was a cornerstone of the civil rights movement's labor wing. While the novel does not explicitly ad[[fashion-and-style|dress]] union organizing, the [[food-and-dining|dining]]-car scenes (particularly the confrontations between [[the-chef|the chef]] and his Black workers) dramatize the racialized labor relations that the BSCP fought to transform.

## Key References

The Pennsylvania Railroad is not named directly in the novel's narrative, but its geography structures the world the characters inhabit. The [[food-and-dining|dining]]-car kitchen hierarchy — the "white chef" barking orders, the "[[high-yaller|yaller]]" cook, the [[waiter|waiters]] navigating between kitchen and passengers — embodies the racial stratification of [[the-railroad|railroad]] labor. [[jake-brown|Jake]]'s money ("he had plenty of money from the [[pennsylvania|Pennsylvania]] job") funds his [[harlem|Harlem]] nightlife, making [[the-railroad|the railroad]] a silent underwriter of the novel's [[pleasure-seeking|pleasure]]s.
