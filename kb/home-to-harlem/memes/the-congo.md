# The Congo

An all-Black cabaret in *Home to Harlem*, the Congo is described as "a real throbbing little Africa in New York" — a dance hall exclusively for the Black working class of Harlem, where no white patrons are admitted and where the blues live "fresh and green as grass" (004-006).

## Overview

The Congo was "an amusement place entirely for the unwashed of the Black Belt. Or, if they were washed, smells lingered telling the nature of their occupation. Pot-wrestlers, third cooks, W.C. attendants, scrub maids, dishwashers, stevedores" (004-006). Unlike the Baltimore, which courted white downtown trade, the Congo's proprietor "did not cater to the fast trade" — his market was the Black working class. "High yallers were scarce there. Except for such sweetmen that lived off the low-down dark trade" (004-006). For young Black newcomers arriving from the South, the Congo was the first stop: "Girls coming from the South to try their future in New York always reached the Congo first" (004-006).

## Atmosphere

"The Congo was thick, dark-colorful, and fascinating. Drum and saxophone were fighting out the wonderful drag 'blues' that was the favorite of all the low-down dance halls. In all the better places it was banned. Rumor said it was a police ban. It was an old tune, so far as popular tunes go. But at the Congo it lived fresh and green as grass. Everybody there was giggling and wriggling to it" (006-002).

McKay contrasts the Congo's raw authenticity with the polished, commercialized venues of Harlem: "When you were fed up with the veneer of Seventh Avenue, and Goldgraben's Afro-Oriental garishness, you would go to the Congo and turn rioting loose in all the tenacious odors of service and the warm indigenous smells of Harlem, fooping or jig-jagging the night away. You would if you were a black kid hunting for joy in New York" (004-009).

## Role

The Congo functions as the novel's primary setting for working-class Black social life. It is where [[jake-brown|Jake]] spends his nights drinking and dancing, where [[rose-congo|Rose]] works as an entertainer, and where [[zeddy-plummer|Zeddy]] flaunts his independence from [[gin-head-susy|Susy]]. The Congo's all-Black admission policy — "No white persons were admitted there" (004-006) — makes it the novel's model of autonomous Black cultural space, in deliberate contrast to integrated venues like [[the-baltimore-cabaret|the Baltimore]] and [[madame-suarez-buffet-flat|Madame Suarez's]].

## Social Function

The Congo serves as a social leveler within the Black community. Its clientele ranges from "stevedores" to "sweetmen," but all are Black — the color line is drawn outward, not inward. This creates a space where the rigid class distinctions of Harlem ([[dickty]], [[ofay]], [[high-yaller]]) temporarily dissolve. [[zeddy-plummer|Zeddy]] uses the Congo to perform his swaggering independence after leaving Susy, dressing in an "elegant nigger-brown sports suit and patent-leather shoes with cream-light spats" to show the boys "that he was a cocky sweetman and no skirt-man" (017-009).

## Key Scenes

### Jake Meets Rose

The Congo is where [[jake-brown|Jake]] first encounters [[rose-congo|Rose]], the cabaret's star entertainer. [[zeddy-plummer|Zeddy]], "happy for the moment," gives her fifty cents for a song, but "Zeddy saw that it was all for Jake" (006-058). When the cabaret closes, "the milk cans were sounding on the pavements and a few pale stars were still visible in the sky when Rose left the Congo with both hands entwined in Jake's arm" (006-064) — the beginning of their tumultuous relationship.

### The West Indian Girl and "Bumbole"

One night at the Congo, a "fighting West Indian girl" cries out "I'll slap you bumbole" during an altercation. Jake adopts the word as his expletive of choice, replacing "Bull" and "blimey." When his friends ask what it means, "he grinned and said, 'Ask the monks'" (019-108). "Bumbole" becomes Jake's signature expression, a linguistic souvenir of the Congo's untamed energy.

### Rose's Breakdown

After Jake leaves Rose, she suffers a public collapse at the Congo: "One night she stahted to sing 'You broke mah haht and went away' and she jest bust out crying theah in the cabaret and couldn't sing no moh. She hauled harself whimpering out there, and she laid off o' the Congo foh moh than a week" (014-058). Rose's breakdown on the Congo stage — the same stage where she once commanded tips and admirers — marks the emotional cost of the "sporting life" for its women.
