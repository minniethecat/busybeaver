## Overview

The Sheba Palace is an immense dance hall in Harlem that serves the working-class Black community — longshoremen, kitchen-workers, laundresses, and "W.C. tenders" — providing a democratic space for music, dancing, and socializing. In the novel's social geography, it stands in contrast to venues like the Baltimore Cabaret, which cater to a higher class of Black servitors.

> Sheba Palace was an immense hall that was entirely monopolized for the amusements of the common people. Longshoremen, kitchen-workers, laundresses, and W.C. tenders — all gravitated to the Sheba Palace, while the upper class of servitors — bellboys, butlers, some railroad workers and waiters — went to the Baltimore and the Congo.
> — (019-080)

## Atmosphere

The Sheba Palace is described with its "garish gold" painted walls and "screaming green" tables and chairs — a deliberately loud, unsubtle aesthetic that matches the raw energy of its working-class patrons. Unlike the more refined cabarets, the Sheba Palace is characterized by rough, direct, unpretentious pleasure.

> The walls of Sheba Palace were painted with garish gold, and tables and chairs were screaming green. There was an elevated platform for the jazz band.
> — (019-081)

## Role in the Novel

Jake and his companions choose the Sheba Palace as an alternative to the Congo, which Jake finds less appealing on a particular night. "All the same, I don't feel like the Congo tonight," Jake says, "Le's go to Sheba Palace and jazz around a little" (019-079).

The Sheba Palace is the setting for Jake's first encounter with Felice in Chapter XX, where their romantic relationship begins against the backdrop of the working-class dance hall. Felice notices Jake at the Sheba Palace, and the venue becomes central to their courtship.

## Social Function

The Sheba Palace represents the democratic, working-class wing of Harlem's nightlife. While venues like the Baltimore Cabaret enforce a de facto class hierarchy, the Sheba Palace welcomes the laborers who form the backbone of Harlem's economy. This class stratification of nightlife is a significant theme in McKay's depiction of Harlem.

## Key Scenes

### Jake and Felice's Meeting (Ch. XX)

Jake first encounters Felice at the Sheba Palace. Their mutual attraction develops across several visits to the dance hall. The venue provides the backdrop for one of the novel's central romantic relationships.

### Social Contrast

The novel explicitly contrasts the Sheba Palace with the Baltimore and the Congo, mapping class divisions within the Black community onto the geography of Harlem's nightlife. The Sheba Palace is where "common people" go — a point of pride as much as a class marker.
