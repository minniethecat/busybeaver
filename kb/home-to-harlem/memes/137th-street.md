**137th Street** is a street in [[harlem|Harlem]] that appears in [[claude-mckay|Claude McKay]]'s *[[home-to-harlem|Home to Harlem]]* as the location of a buffet flat — a private apartment serving as an unlicensed drinking and socializing venue.

## Location

### In the Novel's World

Early in the novel, [[jake-brown|Jake]] is taken by a female companion to "a buffet flat on 137th Street." (002-017) The proprietress, a "matronly mulatto woman," opens the door cautiously with the chain still on, recognizing the girl and admitting them. The flat's windows are "heavily and carefully shaded" — a necessary precaution for an establishment serving Prohibition-era liquor. Inside, "there was beer and wine, and there was plenty of hard liquor." (002-017)

## Significance to the Narrative

The 137th Street buffet flat is Jake's introduction to Harlem's informal nightlife economy. There, "black and brown men sat at two tables in one room, playing poker," while in the other room "a phonograph was grinding out a 'blues,' and some couples were dancing, thick as maggots in a vat of sweet liquor." (002-017) The scene captures the dense, sensual atmosphere of Harlem's private social spaces — where gambling, music, drinking, and sex intermingled outside the reach of legal regulation.

## From the Novel

Jake's initiation into Harlem's private nightlife begins with a cautious door:

> They went to a buffet flat on 137th Street. The proprietress opened the door without removing the chain and peeked out. She was a matronly mulatto woman. She recognized the girl, who had put herself in front of Jake, and she slid back the chain and said, "Come right in."
>
> (002-017)

## Historical Context

West 137th Street, located in central Harlem, was part of the residential district that became predominantly African American during the Great Migration of the 1910s–1920s. Buffet flats — private apartments converted into informal nightspots — were a characteristic feature of Harlem's social landscape during Prohibition, offering alternatives to the more expensive and conspicuous cabarets.
