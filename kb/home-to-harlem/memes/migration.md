# Migration

Migration — the movement of Black people from the American South, the Caribbean, and wartime Europe into [[harlem|Harlem]] — is the demographic engine of [[claude-mckay|Claude McKay]]'s novel. Every major character is a migrant: [[jake-brown|Jake]] from [[virginia|Virginia]] via [[france|France]] and [[london|London]]; [[zeddy-plummer|Zeddy]] from the South via wartime service; [[ray|Ray]] from [[haiti|Haiti]]; [[gin-head-susy|Susy]] from [[south-carolina|South Carolina]]; the anonymous "girls coming from the South to try their future in New York" (004-006). [[harlem|Harlem]] itself is a city of arrivals.

## Definition

In the novel's world, "migration" refers to two intersecting movements: the internal Great Migration of Black Southerners to Northern cities (approximately 1916–1970), and the transnational Black Atlantic migration of Caribbeans, Africans, and returning soldiers through port cities like [[brest|Brest]], [[le-havre|Le Havre]], and [[london|London]]. McKay's characters are shaped by both vectors, and the novel's geography — [[harlem|Harlem]] as destination, the South as memory, Europe as liberatory interlude — is a migrant geography. (001-024)(004-006)

## Cultural Context

### Harlem Renaissance Context

The Great Migration was arguably the single most consequential demographic event in early 20th-century Black American history. Between 1916 and 1930, approximately 1.6 million Black Southerners moved North, transforming [[harlem|Harlem]] from a white neighborhood into the cultural capital of Black America. The [[harlem|Harlem]] Renaissance was itself a product of this migration: the concentration of Black population, capital, and creativity in one neighborhood made a cultural movement possible.

McKay himself was a migrant: born in [[jamaica|Jamaica]], he lived in the United States, [[england|England]], [[france|France]], and [[morocco|Morocco]] before returning to America. His novel renders migration not as abstract demographic trend but as lived experience — the suitcase, the port city, the rented room, the memory of "down home."

### In the Novel

The novel opens with a migration narrative: [[jake-brown|Jake]] aboard a freighter "sailing for [[brest|Brest]]" during the war (001-010), then deserting in [[london|London]], then returning to [[harlem|Harlem]]. His journey traces a Black Atlantic circuit: [[virginia|Virginia]] to [[france|France]] to [[london|London]] to [[harlem|Harlem]]. Each city offers a different racial regime: [[france|France]] is "the only country I've struck" where "a black man's got a chance" (001-021); [[london|London]] provides work "on the docks" (001-024); [[harlem|Harlem]] promises pleasure and community.

Southern origins shape character and conflict. [[gin-head-susy|Susy]] "came from [[south-carolina|South Carolina]]" (006-008); her "drab childhood in a South Carolina town" (006-020) forms the backstory that explains her fierce independence. The chef "came from the [[alabama|Alabama]] country" (012-007), his rural Southern upbringing producing his contempt for the "chicken-loving" stereotype he simultaneously embodies. The "coffee-skinned Georgia village boy" (012-016) on the dining car is "timid like a country bumpkin" — his rural origins making him vulnerable in the brutal railroad hierarchy.

The novel documents [[harlem|Harlem]]'s migrant economy. "Girls coming from the South to try their future in New York" (004-006) feed the [[cabaret-culture|cabaret]] and [[buffet-flat|buffet flat]] economy. Railroad work — [[jake-brown|Jake]]'s occupation — was a quintessential migrant job: the [[the-railroad|Pennsylvania Railroad]] connected the very cities ([[philadelphia|Philadelphia]], [[pittsburgh|Pittsburgh]], [[chicago|Chicago]]) that were Great Migration destinations.

The Caribbean migration is equally present. [[ray|Ray]] is Haitian, educated in the French colonial system: "French is my native language" (010-046). His outsider status on the dining car — he is both Black and not "American Negro" — produces the cultural commentary that is the novel's intellectual core. A "buxom Negress from [[jamaica|Jamaica]]" (020-042) appears in a quarrel, a tiny cameo that gestures toward the broader West Indian presence in [[harlem|Harlem]].

Migration also means loss. [[jake-brown|Jake]]'s "nine years sence I quite home" (014-029) marks nearly a decade of displacement. Memory of the South — "the backyard of bleached clothes on the line, the large lilac bush" (014-061) — is the migrant's permanent undertow.

## Variations & Related Terms

- **"Come North"** — The euphemism for Great Migration: implied in "girls coming from the South to try their future in New York" (004-006)
- **The South** — Both geographic origin and cultural identity: Southern food, Southern accents, Southern "home" remembered in [[harlem|Harlem]] rooms
- **Foreign-born / Islanders** — Caribbean migrants like [[ray|Ray]] who occupy a distinct social position from American-born Black Southerners
- **The Black Atlantic** — The circuit of port cities ([[brest|Brest]], [[le-havre|Le Havre]], [[london|London]], New York) through which Black sailors and soldiers moved

## Key Passages

> "France is the only country I've struck where a black man's got a chance," said Jake. (001-021)

[[jake-brown|Jake]]'s endorsement of [[france|France]] — delivered early in the novel, aboard a ship — establishes the comparative racial geography that structures the entire narrative. Migration means comparing racial regimes and choosing destinations accordingly.

> Girls coming from the South to try their future in New York. (004-006)

McKay's narrator folds the female migrant experience into a single compressed clause. These women are agents — "coming," "trying" — not passive objects of demographic forces.

> Nine years sence I quite home. (014-029)

[[jake-brown|Jake]]'s offhand remark to Agatha reveals a decade of displacement. The dialect ("sence," "quite") carries the Southern speech the migrant never loses.

> He crossed over to London. He found plenty of work there on the docks. (001-024)

[[jake-brown|Jake]]'s wartime migration from [[france|France]] to [[england|England]] is narrated with the casualness of a man accustomed to movement. The docks — the migrant's universal point of entry into urban labor — connect [[london|London]] to the port-city geography the novel maps.

## Historical Significance

The Great Migration transformed American cities, reshaped American music and literature, and laid the foundation for the Civil Rights Movement. McKay's novel is one of the earliest literary treatments of this migration from the perspective of the Black working class. Unlike the sociological studies that would follow, *Home to [[harlem|Harlem]]* renders migration as felt experience — the suitcase, the rented room, the memory of "down home" — and as a story of pleasure and loss rather than statistics.
