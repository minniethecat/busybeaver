**Ma Lawton** is a supporting character in [[claude-mckay|Claude McKay]]'s *Home to [[harlem|Harlem]]*, appearing in chapter 17 as the landlady of a boarding house where the narrator, [[rosalind|Rosalind]], and [[jerco|Jerco]] all reside. She functions as a maternal caretaker, job broker, and moral arbiter within her household.

## Role in the Novel

Ma Lawton runs a boarding house that serves as the central setting of chapter 17's retrospective narrative, told by a [[waiter]] at [[uncle-doc|Uncle Doc]]'s [[saloon]]. She is deeply involved in the lives of her tenants — helping them find work, mana[[gin-whisky|gin]]g domestic conflicts, and caring for them in illness. Her presence anchors the boarding house community and provides the chapter's moral and emotional grounding.

## Characterization

### Personality and Traits

Ma Lawton is practical, energetic, and maternal. When the narrator falls behind on rent, she is worried but works with him rather than evicting — she "asked me to give it up" when the snoring of other boarders kept him awake, and "moved into the little room that I had occupied." (017-004)(017-005) She acts as a job broker, getting the narrator work at the little fruit store the day after he arrives. (017-008) She is religious, believing "in God's word," and is shocked when [[rosalind|Rosalind]] refuses a prayer during her illness. (017-036)

### Relationships

- [[rosalind|Rosalind]] — tenant; Ma Lawton nurses her through her final illness
- [[jerco|Jerco]] — tenant; Ma Lawton has observed his devotion to [[rosalind|Rosalind]]
- [[uncle-doc|Uncle Doc]] — the narrator (her tenant) works at his saloon

## Key Scenes

Ma Lawton is introduced when the narrator falls behind on rent — she is "worried" but accommodates him, moving rooms around the boarding house. (017-002)(017-004) She introduces him to [[jerco|Jerco]] and [[rosalind|Rosalind]] (017-007) and helps him find work. (017-008)

When the elevator boys give Ma Lawton "notice to quit" because of "the goings-on in [[rosalind|Rosalind]]'s room," she is caught between tenants. (017-015) But her deepest involvement comes during [[rosalind|Rosalind]]'s illness — she tends to [[rosalind|Rosalind]], adjusts her pillow, and insists she needs a doctor. (017-028)(017-029)(017-033) She is the one who brings news of [[rosalind|Rosalind]]'s death to the saloon: "Ma Lawton came in the saloon about nine o'clock that evening and said that [[rosalind|Rosalind]] was dead." (017-057)

After the death, "Ma Lawton slept in the kitchen and put [[jerco|Jerco]] and me together" (017-058) — a practical act of care. She is "an early riser" who makes coffee in the morning. (017-063)

## Themes

Ma Lawton represents the informal care economy of [[harlem|Harlem]]'s working class — women whose domestic labor ([[food-and-dining|cooking]], nursing, mediating, job-finding) sustained the neighborhood's social fabric. She embodies both the pragmatism and the moral gravity of the older generation, offering stability in a precarious world. Her rolling-pin — which the narrator will later use to "break through" a window — marks her domain as a space of both nurture and necessity. (017-065)
