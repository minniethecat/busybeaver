# Cabaret

The cabaret is the central institution of [[harlem|Harlem]] nightlife in [[claude-mckay|Claude McKay]]'s *Home to [[harlem|Harlem]]* — a commercial entertainment venue combining music, dancing, drinking, and social mixing. Cabarets function as the novel's primary stage for social interaction across lines of class, color, and race, and their fate mirrors the broader political economy of Prohibition-era [[harlem|Harlem]].

## Definition

In the novel's world, a cabaret is a licensed or semi-legal nightclub offering live musical performance (typically [[jazz]] or [[blues]]), a dance floor, and alcohol service in defiance of [[prohibition|Prohibition]]. Cabarets ranged from elegant, white-patronized establishments like **Goldgraben's** (004-004) to the rough [[harlem|Harlem]]-working-class houses like **Connor's** (004-002). Unlike private [[buffet-flat|buffet flats]], cabarets were public commercial spaces — open to any paying customer, and therefore sites of both social possibility and interracial tension.

## Cultural Context

### Harlem Renaissance Context

Cabarets were the physical infrastructure of the [[harlem-renaissance|Harlem Renaissance]] as a popular movement. While the Renaissance's literary wing wrote manifestos and novels, its nightlife wing — singers, dancers, jazz musicians, and the cabaret owners who employed them — produced the cultural energy that drew white New York uptown. The cabaret was where [[langston-hughes|Langston Hughes]] heard the blues that shaped his poetry, where white patrons encountered Black artistry on Black terms, and where the racial boundaries of American society were nightly transgressed and reinforced.

The novel explicitly tracks the shifting fortunes of [[harlem|Harlem]]'s cabarets: "All the old cabarets were going still. Connor's was losing ground" (004-002), while "Goldgraben's on Lenox Avenue was leading all the Negro cabarets a cruel dance" (004-004). This competition between venues — driven by changing tastes, white patronage patterns, and the economics of Prohibition — constitutes a micro-history of Harlem's cultural economy.

### In the Novel

[[jake-brown|Jake]]'s emotional relationship to cabarets evolves over the course of the novel. He first encounters [[rose-congo|Rose]] in a cabaret, and "in one night, had revealed a fine different world to him" (006-051) through that setting. But after losing her, "he grew to hate that cabaret and stopped going there" (005-002), illustrating how the cabaret's meaning is inseparable from the emotional and erotic life of its patrons.

The cabaret is also a space of violence and conflict. When the cabaret singer is struck, the "cabaret was closed for the rest of the night" (004-047) — the violence of interpersonal conflict reverberating through the institutional space. The singer herself is both performer and commodity: "Sitting at a table, the cabaret singer was soothing her eye" (004-045) after the altercation, her body marked by the violence of the space she entertains.

The novel's cabarets serve as the backdrop for its most charged scenes: Is dancing in the cabaret (007-101); the cabaret singer's performance (004-012); the brawl that closes the venue (004-039); and the endless circulation of patrons — "color, face, passing through cabarets, saloons, speakeasies, and restaurants" (005-003).

## Historical Significance

The cabaret as an institution was central to the cultural economy of the [[harlem-renaissance|Harlem Renaissance]]. Real venues like the Cotton Club, Connie's Inn, and Small's Paradise were the proving grounds for Black musical talent that would eventually transform American popular culture. However, McKay's cabarets are notably not the Cotton Club — they are the smaller, more rough-and-tumble venues of the working-class Black Belt, and their depiction reflects McKay's commitment to representing Harlem life as Black people experienced it, not as white tourists consumed it.

The cabaret's decline in the novel — from public venues to private flats — mirrors the actual trajectory of Prohibition-era nightlife, where police raids and political pressures drove social life into more intimate, less visible spaces.
