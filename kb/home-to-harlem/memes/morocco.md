**Morocco** is a North African nation on the Mediterranean and Atlantic coasts, which during the 1920s setting of *Home to [[harlem|Harlem]]* was under French and Spanish colonial rule. While Morocco does not appear directly in the novel's action, it belongs to the broader geography of the African diaspora and colonial world that shapes the consciousness of [[ray|Ray]] and other internationally-minded characters.

## Location

### Geography

Morocco occupies the northwestern corner of Africa, separated from Europe by the narrow Strait of Gibraltar. It borders Algeria to the east, the Mediterranean Sea to the north, the Atlantic Ocean to the west, and the disputed territory of Western Sahara to the south. The country's major cities include Casablanca, Marrakech, Fez, Rabat (the capital), and Tangier — the latter an international zone from 1923 onward, famous for its cosmopolitan and often lawless character.

### In the Novel's World

Morocco belongs to the colonial world-system that [[ray|Ray]] contemplates during his intellectual journey. [[ray|Ray]]'s reading of European literature and his engagement with questions of race and empire place him in conversation with a global geography that includes North Africa. The French presence in Morocco — formalized by the Treaty of Fez in 1912 — made the country part of the same colonial network that shaped the experience of Black intellectuals across the diaspora.

Morocco, and particularly Tangier, was a destination for African American expatriates and artists in the decades following the novel's publication, but even in the early 1920s, North Africa figured in the Black imagination as a site of cultural authenticity and connection to an African past that stood in contrast to the degradations of [[jim-crow|Jim Crow]] America.

## Historical Context

The 1920s were a period of continued European colonial domination in Morocco. France and Spain had partitioned the country in 1912, and the Rif War (1921--1926), in which Berber rebels under Abd el-Krim fought Spanish and later French forces, was a major anticolonial conflict that resonated with other independence movements worldwide. The war attracted attention among politically conscious African Americans who saw parallels between colonial subjugation in Africa and racial oppression in the United States.

Morocco's position at the crossroads of Africa, Europe, and the Arab world made it a symbolically rich location. For Black intellectuals of the Harlem Renaissance period, North Africa sometimes occupied an ambiguous place — not sub-Saharan Africa, the imagined homeland of the [[back-to-africa-movement|Back-to-Africa movement]], but also not Europe, the seat of colonial power. It was a liminal space, neither here nor there, and this very ambiguity made it a site of fascination.

## Significance to the Narrative

Morocco functions in the novel's imaginative geography rather than as an explicit setting. It is part of the world that exists beyond [[harlem|Harlem]] — a world that [[ray|Ray]] accesses through books and that [[jake-brown|Jake]] accesses through travel and the cosmopolitan community of seafarers and railroad workers. The novel's international consciousness, which stretches from [[haiti|Haiti]] to [[london|London]] to [[pittsburgh|Pittsburgh]], implicitly includes the colonial territories of North Africa as part of the wider landscape of the African diaspora.
