# Grant

James Grant is a minor character in *Home to [[harlem|Harlem]]* — a student [[ray|Ray]] introduces to [[jake-brown|Jake]] in Chapter XVI. He appears as a foil to both the earthy [[jake-brown|Jake]] and the bookish [[ray|Ray]]: more cynical and disaffected than either, Grant voices some of the novel's darkest judgments about racial conditions in America.

## Role in the Novel

Grant functions as an intellectual provocateur in Chapter XVI, where [[ray|Ray]] brings him along to meet [[jake-brown|Jake]]. He debates the moral condition of Negro life with a sneering intensity that unsettles even [[jake-brown|Jake]], who usually navigates such conversations with equanimity. In Chapter XVIII, Grant and [[ray|Ray]] find jobs together on a freighter heading across the Atlantic (018-002), and [[jake-brown|Jake]] throws them both a farewell feed at [[billy-biasse|Billy Biasse]]'s [[the-railroad|railroad]] flat (018-013).

## Characterization

### Personality and Traits

Grant is introduced as "James Grant," a student (016-050). He speaks with a biting, sardonic edge: when [[jake-brown|Jake]] praises a mutual acquaintance as "a real good-hearted scout," Grant immediately retorts with "Good-hearted! A man's heart is co—" [016-065–066], cutting off mid-sentence in a way that suggests both disdain and frustration. He sneers at those who "live the life of carrion" and "fatten on rotting human flesh" (016-073), condemning the moral compromises he sees in Black urban life.

Yet Grant is not merely a cynic. When pressed, he defends his own integrity: "I am not rotten, and I couldn't bring myself" (016-082) — suggesting his harsh judgments arise from a wounded idealism rather than mere contempt. He is "slightly embarrassed" (016-068) when challenged, revealing a self-consciousness beneath the hard exterior.

### Relationships

- [[ray]] — Friend and fellow student; they ship out together on a freighter. Grant represents a more embittered version of the intellectual type that [[ray|Ray]] embodies more sympathetically.
- [[jake-brown]] — A respectful acquaintance. [[jake-brown|Jake]] extends hospitality to Grant, inviting him to the farewell feed (018-013), but their worldviews clash sharply in conversation.

## Key Scenes

**Chapter XVI — The Debate:** [[ray|Ray]] introduces Grant to [[jake-brown|Jake]], and the three men engage in a charged discussion about [[race-and-color|race]], morality, and the conditions of Negro life. Grant's sarcasm — "Can't a Negro have fine feelings?" (016-076) — challenges the assumption that racial oppression has stripped Black people of moral sensibility. His question is itself a rebuke to those who would sentimentalize or degrade Black character.

**Chapter XVIII — The Freighter:** Grant and [[ray|Ray]] "had found jobs on a freighter that was going down across the" Atlantic (018-002). The two students choose the hard life of the sea over remaining in [[harlem|Harlem]] — a flight from the intellectual and moral stagnation they both perceive there.

**The [[farewell-feed|Farewell Feed]]:** [[jake-brown|Jake]] "invited [[ray|Ray]] and Grant to a farewell feed, for which [[billy-biasse|Billy Biasse]]" provided the space (018-013). The gathering marks Grant's exit from the novel and underscores [[jake-brown|Jake]]'s capacity to bridge the gap between working-class and educated Black men.

## Themes

Grant embodies the theme of intellectual **disaffection** among educated Black men of the 1920s. Unlike [[ray|Ray]], who is troubled but still receptive to experience, Grant has hardened into a posture of cynical detachment. His sneering about "carrion" (016-073) registers the novel's concern with how racism corrodes the spirit — and how even resistance can curdle into bitterness.

He also represents the **student-exile** figure: the educated Negro who cannot find a place in America and so takes to the sea, joining the novel's broader pattern of [[migration]] and rootlessness that connects [[harlem|Harlem]] to the ports of Europe.
