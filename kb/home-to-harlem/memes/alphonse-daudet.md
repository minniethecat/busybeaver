# Alphonse Daudet

Alphonse Daudet (1840–1897) was a [[france|French]] novelist, playwright, and short story writer associated with the Naturalist movement. In [[claude-mckay|Claude McKay]]'s *[[Home to Harlem]]* (1928), Daudet appears through his 1884 novel *Sapho*, which the Haitian [[waiter]] reads aboard the [[pennsylvania-railroad|Pennsylvania Railroad]] [[dining-car|dining car]] — a book that becomes the catalyst for one of the novel's most significant conversations about literature, [[race-and-color|race]], gender, sexuality, and the politics of naming.

## In the Novel

Daudet enters *[[home-to-harlem|Home to Harlem]]* through his book. In Chapter X, [[jake-brown|Jake]] — working as a third cook on [[the-railroad|the railroad]] — approaches a solitary [[waiter]] who is reading at a small table. [[jake-brown|Jake]] asks what the book is, and the exchange becomes the gateway to an extended conversation about [[sappho|Sappho]], [[bulldyker|lesbian]] identity, [[france|French]] language, Haitian history, and Black liberation.

The [[waiter]] introduces the book:

> "It's a story," he told Jake, "by a French writer named Alphonse Daudet. It's about a sporting woman who was beautiful like a rose and had the soul of a wandering cat. Her lovers called her Sapho. I like the story, but I hate the use of Sapho for its title." (010-019)

The [[waiter]]'s ambivalence — he likes the story but "hates" the title — is the hinge on which the scene turns. For the [[waiter]], "[[sappho-daudet|Sappho]] was a real person. A wonderful woman, a great Greek poet" (010-021), and Daudet's use of her name for a "sporting woman" (a courtesan or kept woman) is a kind of theft. This objection launches the [[waiter]] into telling [[jake-brown|Jake]] the story of [[sappho|Sappho]] — her poetry, her loves, her leap from the Leucadian cliff — and from there into the meaning of "Sapphic and Lesbian" (010-027), into [[jake-brown|Jake]]'s vernacular gloss ("Tha's what we calls [[bulldyker]] in [[harlem|Harlem]]," 010-030), and into a critique of [[harlem|Harlem]]'s sexual vocabulary ("That's a damned ugly name," 010-031).

## Daudet's *Sapho* (1884)

Daudet's novel *Sapho: moeurs parisiennes* (*[[sappho-daudet|Sappho]]: Parisian Manners*) was published in 1884 and was one of his most popular works. It tells the story of Jean Gaussin, a young provincial man who falls in love with Fanny Legrand, an older woman who had been a model and courtesan. Fanny's former lovers called her "Sapho" — a reference to the ancient Greek poet that, in the novel's world, has been reduced to a nickname for a woman with a sexual past.

The scene the [[waiter]] is reading when [[jake-brown|Jake]] approaches — "the scene between Fanny and Jean when the lover discovers the letters of his mistress's former woman friend and exclaims: 'Ah Oui ... Sapho ... toute la lyre ...'" (010-018) — is the moment of crisis. Jean finds evidence of Fanny's past relationship with another woman and recoils. The novel treats Fanny's bisexuality as part of her corrupted worldliness, and the name "Sapho" as a mark of sexual notoriety rather than poetic genius.

## The Critique

The Haitian [[waiter]]'s objection — "I like the story, but I hate the use of Sapho for its title" — is a sophisticated piece of literary criticism delivered in a single sentence. It distinguishes between Daudet's craft (the story is good) and his cultural politics (the title is offensive). For the [[waiter]], [[sappho-daudet|Sappho]] is a sacred figure — a great poet, a real woman, a symbol of female creativity and love — and Daudet's reduction of her name to a label for a courtesan is an act of erasure.

McKay uses this objection to stage a layered argument about representation. Daudet, a white [[france|French]]man, took a Greek woman poet's name and attached it to a fictional courtesan. The Haitian [[waiter]], a Black student from a colonized nation, objects — and in objecting, he teaches [[jake-brown|Jake]] the real story. The colonized subject reclaims [[sappho-daudet|Sappho]] from the [[france|French]] canon, and in doing so asserts his own literary and historical authority. His knowledge of Daudet, of [[sappho-daudet|Sappho]], of Wordsworth, of Haitian history — all of this is displayed on a [[dining-car|railroad dining car]], to a [[longshoreman]], as part of the intellectual self-possession that the novel consistently attributes to its Black characters.

## Historical Daudet

Alphonse Daudet was born in Nimes, in the south of [[france|France]], in 1840. He moved to Paris as a young man and established himself as a writer of provincial tales (*Lettres de mon moulin*, 1869), naturalist novels (*Fromont jeune et Risler aine*, 1874; *Le Nabab*, 1877), and the picaresque *Tartarin de Tarascon* (1872). He was associated with the circle of Gustave Flaubert, Emile Zola, and the Goncourt brothers, though he was less doctrinaire in his naturalism than Zola.

*Sapho* (1884) was a commercial success and contributed to the late-19th-century [[france|French]] fascination with the figure of the courtesan as a symbol of modern Paris. The novel was adapted into an opera by Jules Massenet (1897) and several films. Daudet died in 1897, having suffered for years from syphilis — a disease that also appears, obliquely, as a theme in *Sapho*.

## Significance

The Daudet episode is a masterclass in how McKay embeds literary criticism within fiction. Daudet's book, an actual 19th-century novel, is placed in the hands of a Black character who reads it critically — who likes the story but rejects the politics of naming. The scene demonstrates Black literary competence (the [[waiter]] reads [[france|French]] fluently, discusses literary merit, knows classical history) while at the same time asserting Black critical authority (the [[waiter]] does not defer to the [[france|French]] canon; he judges it).

For [[jake-brown|Jake]], who cannot read, the book is opaque — "Bumbole! This heah language is most different from how they talk it" (010-038) — but the conversation it triggers is transformative. Through Daudet, [[jake-brown|Jake]] gains access to [[sappho-daudet|Sappho]], to [[france|French]], to Haiti, to the history of Black revolution. A [[france|French]] novel on a [[dining-car|railroad dining car]] becomes, in McKay's hands, a vehicle for Black consciousness-raising.
