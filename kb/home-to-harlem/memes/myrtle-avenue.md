**Myrtle Avenue** is a street in [[harlem|Harlem]] that serves as the residential setting for [[gin-head-susy|Susy's]] apartment in [[claude-mckay|Claude McKay]]'s *Home to [[harlem|Harlem]]*. It is the locus of several key social scenes, including [[gin-head-susy|Susy]]'s [[gin-whisky|gin]] party and free-love feast.

## Location

### Geography

Myrtle Avenue runs through the heart of working-class [[harlem|Harlem]]. [[gin-head-susy|Susy]]'s apartment is situated on Myrtle Avenue "near [[prince-street|Prince Street]]" (006-006), anchoring the street in the neighborhood's densely populated residential blocks.

### In the Novel's World

Myrtle Avenue is primarily experienced through [[gin-head-susy|Susy]]'s apartment — a space of rent parties, [[gin-whisky|gin]] drinking, and domestic conflict. The street itself is rendered through sensory details: the "Myrtle Avenue Elevated train passed with a strange high clanking noise." (006-087) [[jake-brown|Jake]] murmurs appreciatively: "Myrtle Avenue. Pretty name." (006-088) The elevated train, the window overlooking the street (007-032), and the staggering return of drunken men at night (007-161) all create a vivid sense of place.

## Significance to the Narrative

Myrtle Avenue is the site of two major social events in the novel. First, the "Myrtle Avenue [[gin-whisky|gin]]-fest" (007-002) — a party at [[gin-head-susy|Susy]]'s apartment featuring drinking and the gramophone. Second, "the magnificent free-love feast" (007-020) — a Sunday meal [[gin-head-susy|Susy]] prepares for [[jake-brown|Jake]], [[zeddy-plummer|Zeddy]], and [[miss-curdy|Miss Curdy]]. These events showcase the informal economy of working-class hospitality and the gender dynamics of domestic labor and leisure.

The street is also the setting for [[zeddy-plummer|Zeddy]]'s comings and goings — he "returned to Myrtle Avenue" late at night (007-078) and "staggered down Myrtle Avenue well primed with the powerful [[prohibition|Prohibition]] liquor." (007-161) [[zeddy-plummer|Zeddy]] and [[gin-head-susy|Susy]]'s domestic conflicts unfold here, with [[gin-head-susy|Susy]] leaning "against the window overlooking Myrtle Avenue" during an argument. (007-032)

## Historical Context

Myrtle Avenue and [[prince-street|Prince Street]] represent the residential geography of working-class [[harlem|Harlem]] in the 1920s — blocks of tenement apartments where migration from the South and the Caribbean created dense, socially vibrant communities. The elevated train passing overhead was a defining feature of New York street life in the era.

## Appearances

Myrtle Avenue appears or is referenced in chapters 6, 7, and 16. (006-001)–(016-044)

## Quotations

>
> (006-087)
