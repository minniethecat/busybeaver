**Mile End Road** is a street in the [[east-end|East End]] of [[london|London]], mentioned in [[claude-mckay|Claude McKay]]'s 1928 novel *[[home-to-harlem|Home to Harlem]]*. It is where the protagonist [[jake-brown|Jake Brown]] spent New Year's Eve 1919 with his community of fellow dockworkers.

## In the Novel

The novel's opening chapter establishes [[jake-brown|Jake]]'s life in [[london|London]] before his return to [[harlem|Harlem]]. McKay situates Jake's social world on this specific street:

> The Armistice found him there. On New Year's Eve, 1919, Jake went to a monster dance with his woman, and his docker friends and their women, in the Mile End Road.
>
> (001-025)

This single sentence does considerable work: it marks the temporal setting (post-Armistice, the winter of 1919–1920), locates Jake within a community of Black colonial workers ("his docker friends and their women"), and captures a moment of collective celebration — a "monster dance" — before the racial violence of the summer of 1919 and Jake's subsequent "fever of lonesomeness" that drives him back across the Atlantic. The Mile End Road scene is a snapshot of the diasporic working-class sociability that the novel both celebrates and mourns.

## Historical Context

Mile End Road is a historic thoroughfare in London's East End, running through the districts of Stepney and Mile End. In the early 20th century, it was at the heart of a working-class and increasingly diverse immigrant neighborhood. The area's proximity to the [[west-india-docks|West India Docks]] made it a natural residential zone for colonial seamen and dockworkers like Jake and his friends, forming part of the real-world geography that underpins McKay's fictional [[london|London]].
