**Fifty-third Street** (West 53rd Street) is a street in Midtown Manhattan that appears in [[claude-mckay|Claude McKay]]'s *[[home-to-harlem|Home to Harlem]]* as a remembered center of Black cultural and social life in an earlier era, before [[harlem|Harlem]] became the dominant hub.

## Location

### In the Novel's World

Fifty-third Street is invoked as a historical benchmark of Black achievement and style. It is referenced as the site of Marshall's, a hotel and cabaret that competed with Elijah Bowers's establishment on Myrtle Avenue: "His cabaret was running neck and neck with Marshall's in Fifty-third Street." (006-036) The street also features in the nostalgic recollections of [[madame-mulberry|Madame Mulberry]], who "was a famous black beauty in the days when Fifty-third Street was the hub of fashionable Negro life." (021-007)

## Significance to the Narrative

Fifty-third Street functions in the novel as a marker of historical time — it represents the pre-Harlem era of Black New York, the world before the Great Migration shifted the center of gravity uptown. The references to Fifty-third Street, particularly through the reminiscences of older characters like [[madame-mulberry|Madame Mulberry]], situate [[harlem|Harlem]]'s 1920s ascendancy within a longer trajectory of Black cultural geography in New York City.

## Quotations

Madame Mulberry's era recalled — when Fifty-third Street was the center of fashionable Black life:

> Madame Mulberry was a famous black beauty in the days when Fifty-third Street was the hub of fashionable Negro life. They called her then, Brown Glory. She was the wife of Dick Mulberry, a promoter of Negro shows.
>
> (021-007)

## Historical Context

West 53rd Street in Midtown Manhattan was indeed a center of African American cultural and musical life in the early twentieth century, particularly through venues like Marshall's Hotel. Before [[harlem|Harlem]] emerged as the "Negro capital" in the 1910s–1920s, the area known as the Tenderloin and San Juan Hill (roughly West 50s to West 60s) was home to a significant Black community and a thriving entertainment district. The migration of Black institutions and residents uptown marked a major shift in the city's social geography.
