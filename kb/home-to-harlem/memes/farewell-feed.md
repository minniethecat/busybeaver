# A Farewell Feed

[[jake-brown|Jake]] throws a farewell dinner for [[ray|Ray]] and Grant at [[aunt-hattie|Aunt Hattie]]'s cookshop, paid for by [[billy-biasse|Billy Biasse]] — a send-off that mingles fried chicken, Scotch whisky, and naked declarations of friendship with the sorrow of departure and the irreconcilable differences between two ways of being Black in America.

## Overview

> Ray and Grant had found jobs on a freighter that was going down across the Pacific to Australia and from there to Europe.
> — (018-002)

[[ray|Ray]] has reached his breaking point on the railroad. He has had "enough to vomit up of Philadelphia and Baltimore, [[pittsburgh|Pittsburgh]] and [[washington|Washington]]. More than enough of the bar-to-bar camaraderie of railroad life" (018-002). A freighter job — mess boy on a ship bound for Australia — offers escape. Jake, knowing the brutality of sea life, tries to dissuade him. But the farewell feed is not an argument; it is a ritual of friendship, a "last night" in [[harlem|Harlem]] that ends in [[uncle-doc|Uncle Doc]]'s, "sweet jazzing," and the knowledge that tomorrow the sea will take Ray away.

## Context

### Historical Background

Freighter travel was one of the few means of global mobility available to working-class Black men in the 1920s. Mess boys, cooks, and stokers on cargo ships could see the ports of the world — albeit in conditions of grueling labor and racial hierarchy. McKay himself had worked as a mess boy on a freighter, and the novel's maritime episodes draw on autobiographical experience. The farewell dinner at a neighborhood cookshop — paid for by a friend, held in a space of Black hospitality — was a recognizable ritual in Harlem's working-class social world.

### In the Novel

The farewell feed (Chapter 18) is the penultimate chapter before the spring-in-Harlem reverie and Jake's final reunion with [[felice]] in Chicago. It is the last extended scene between Jake and Ray — the novel's central friendship — and it distills their philosophical differences into moments of extraordinary tenderness. Ray's departure closes the body/mind dialectic that has driven the novel: the intellectual leaves, the sensualist stays.

## Key Participants

- [[jake-brown|Jake]] — hosts the farewell; wishes he were "edjucated" so he could "understand things better" (018-050)
- [[ray|Ray]] — departing intellectual, running from "the contented animal that was a Harlem nigger strutting his stuff" (018-003)
- Grant — Ray's fellow seaman, named but not characterized
- [[billy-biasse|Billy Biasse]] — pays for the feed; "a better pal for Jake than [[zeddy-plummer|Zeddy]]" (018-013)
- [[aunt-hattie|Aunt Hattie]] — host at her cookshop, fusses over Jake with a "senile-fond look" (018-017)

## Details and Sequence

### The Decision to Leave

The chapter opens with Ray's internal monologue. The railroad has become unbearable — "he would bang right off his head" if he stayed (018-004). His relationship with [[agatha|Agatha]] threatens to yoke him into domesticity: "Soon he would become one of the contented hogs in the pigpen of Harlem, getting ready to litter little black piggies" (018-003). He is terrified of becoming "a Harlem nigger strutting his stuff" — the phrase haunts him "like jets of saliva, spewing from the lips of his work pals" (018-003).

Yet Harlem also holds him: "Oh, the rich bloodred color of it! The warm accent of its composite voice, the fruitiness of its laughter, the trailing rhythm of its '[[blues]]' and the improvised surprises of its [[jazz]]" (018-008). He has known "happiness, too, in Harlem, joy that glowed gloriously upon him like the high-noon sunlight of his tropic island home" (018-008).

Jake has tried to warn him about sea life: "A seaman's life is no good, chappie, and it's easier to jump off a train in the field than offn a ship gwine across the pond" (018-009). "Stoking and A.B.S. is cleaner work than messing with raw meat and garbage" (018-011). But Ray is committed.

> Life burned in Ray perhaps more intensely than in Jake. Ray felt more and his range was wider and he could not be satisfied with the easy, simple things that sufficed for Jake.
> — (018-005)

### The Feed at Aunt Hattie's

The feed is held at [[aunt-hattie|Aunt Hattie]]'s cookshop — "the best place for good eats in Harlem" in Jake's estimation (018-016). Billy Biasse pays for everything. A bottle of Scotch whisky and a bottle of gin sit on the table (018-016).

Aunt Hattie fusses over Jake: "Why, it's good and long sence you ain't been in reg'lar to see me, chile" (018-018). When she hears he's been sick, her response is immediate: "Poah chile, and I nevah knowed so I could come off'ring you a li'l chicken broth. You jest come heah and eats any time you wanta, whether youse got money or not" (018-022). She shuffles to the kitchen to "pick the nicest piece of fried chicken for Jake" (018-023).

Billy toasts: "Well, here's to us, fellahs... Let's hope that hard luck nevah turn our glasses down or shet the door of a [[saloon]] in our face" (018-031). Jake amends the toast darkly: "You had better said, 'Le's hope that this heah Gawd's own don't shut the pub in our face.' Prohibition is right under our tail" (018-033).

### The Heart of the Matter: Friendship and Education

The dinner's emotional climax comes when Jake, after Ray mentions Goethe, grips Ray's shoulder and confesses: "Chappie, I wish I was edjucated mahself" (018-045). When Ray asks why, Jake's answer is naked: "Becaz I likes you" (018-047).

> Like a black Pan out of the woods Jake looked into Ray's eyes with frank savage affection.
> — (018-047)

Billy exclaims: "Lawdy in heaben! A li'l foreign booze gwine turn you all soft?" (018-048). But Jake is earnest. If he were educated, "I could understand things better and be proper-speaking like you is... And I mighta helped mah li'l sister to get edjucated, too... and she would be nice-speaking like you' sweet brown, good enough foh you to hitch up with. Then we could all settle down and make money like edjucated people do, instead a you gwine off to throw you' self away on some lousy dinghy and me chasing around all the time lak a hungry dawg" (018-050).

Ray's response is the novel's most explicit statement of his existential dilemma: "I wonder sometimes if I could get rid of it and go and lose myself in some savage culture in the jungles of Africa. I am a misfit... a misfit with my little education and constant dreaming, when I should be getting the nightmare habit to hog in a whole lot of dough like everybody else in this country" (018-054).

Jake's reply is practical: "If I had your edjucation I wouldn't be slinging no hash on the white man's chu-chu" (018-055). Ray's counter is philosophical: "The more I learn the less I understand and love life. All the learning in this world can't answer this little question, Why are we living?" (018-056). Billy supplies the folk answer: "Why, becaz Gawd wants us to, chappie" (018-057).

### The Night Continues

Jake insists: "Come on le's all go to Uncle Doc's, and finish the night with a li'l sweet jazzing. This is you last night, chappie. Make the most of it, foh there ain't no jazzing like Harlem jazzing over the other side" (018-058). They drink "many ceremonious rounds" at [[uncle-doc|Uncle Doc]]'s and then go on to Leroy's Cabaret (018-059).

> The next afternoon the freighter left with Ray signed on as a mess boy.
> — (018-060)

## Aftermath and Significance

The farewell feed is the novel's emotional farewell to the Jake-Ray dyad that has structured its philosophical architecture. Jake's confession — that he wishes he were educated "becaz I likes you" — is the most emotionally naked moment in the novel, and it inverts the expected hierarchy: the uneducated sensualist expresses the purest valuation of education, not as a tool of advancement, but as a bridge to friendship.

Ray's departure does not resolve the novel's central tension between body and mind, instinct and intellect. It disperses it — Ray to the Pacific, Jake to remain in Harlem and [[chicago|Chicago]], each taking his way of being Black into a different world. The chapter's closing line — "The next afternoon the freighter left with Ray signed on as a mess boy" — is deliberately flat, a documentary notation that refuses sentimentality even as the farewell has been saturated with it.

The feed also showcases the informal economy of Black social life in Harlem: Billy pays, Aunt Hattie cooks, Jake hosts, Ray departs. No institution organizes this farewell — it is entirely generated by the networks of friendship, credit, and cooking that sustain the novel's working-class world.
