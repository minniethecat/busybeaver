# Bert Williams

Egbert Austin "Bert" Williams (1874–1922) was one of the most famous Black entertainers of the early 20th century — a vaudeville star, Ziegfeld Follies headliner, and pioneering recording artist. In *Home to [[harlem|Harlem]]*, he is invoked as a marker of the "sporting days" — a previous era of Black entertainment excellence.

## Role in the Novel

Bert Williams appears only as a name in dialogue, but his invocation carries significant weight. When Gertie Kendall says "In my sporting days I knew Bert Williams and all that upstage [[race-and-color|race]] gang that wouldn't touch [[jack-johnson|Jack Johnson]]" (006-051), the reference to Williams situates the novel's characters within a real historical continuum of Black celebrity. Williams represents the pinnacle of pre-Renaissance Black entertainment — a figure who achieved mainstream success while performing in blackface, a contradiction that the novel's characters implicitly acknowledge through their ambivalent mention.

Williams is also recalled in Chapter XXI, where [[harlem|Harlem]]'s social elite "gossiped reminiscently of Bert Williams, [[george-walker|George Walker]] and [[aida-overton-walker|Aida Overton Walker]]" (021-007), and a rumor circulated that "Bert Williams might drop in after midnight" (021-009) — his name still commanding mystique in [[harlem|Harlem]] nightlife decades after his prime.

## Quotations

[[miss-curdy|Miss Curdy]]'s recollection of the "sporting days" and the elite Black entertainment circle that included Bert Williams:

> In my sporting days I knew Bert Williams and Walker and Adah Overton and Editor Tukslack and all that upstage race gang that wouldn't touch Jack Johnson with a ten-foot pole.
>
> (006-051)

## Historical/Cultural Context

Bert Williams was the first Black performer to headline on Broadway and the first to record with a major label. His signature song "Nobody" (1905) was one of the best-selling recordings of the era. However, his career was built on [[minstrelsy|minstrel]]-show conventions — performing in burnt-cork blackface for predominantly white audiences — making him a complex figure in [[africa|Africa]]n American cultural history. The "upstage [[race-and-color|race]] gang" that "wouldn't touch [[jack-johnson|Jack Johnson]]" (the Black heavyweight champion reviled by white America) suggests the class and respectability politics that divided Black entertainers of Williams's generation.
