**West India** refers to the West India [[uncle-doc|Doc]]k district of [[london|London]], a working-class port neighborhood on the Isle of Dogs that appears throughout [[claude-mckay|Claude McKay]]'s *Home to [[harlem|Harlem]]* as part of the novel's transatlantic geography. It is a place of labor, migration, and cultural encounter for the Black seamen who circulate between the Caribbean, Europe, and America.

## Location

### Geography

The West India Docks are located on the Isle of Dogs in East [[london|London]], built in the early nineteenth century as part of the Port of [[london|London]]'s dock system. By the time of the novel's setting (the 1910s), the docks were a major point of arrival and departure for ships trading with the Caribbean.

### In the Novel's World

[[jake-brown|Jake]] "liked the West India Docks" and "found plenty of work there." (001-024) The docks are part of a circuit that includes [[brest|Brest]], Le Havre, and [[london|London]]: "[[brest|Brest]]. Le Havre. [[london|London]]. The West India Docks. And back home to [[harlem|Harlem]]." (003-034) This itinerary defines the world of the Black maritime worker, for whom the West India Docks function as both a workplace and a way-station in a larger transatlantic network.

## Significance to the Narrative

The West India Docks anchor [[jake-brown|Jake]]'s working identity. Unlike the [[cabaret-culture|cabaret]] world of [[harlem|Harlem]], the docks represent honest labor — a place where a Black man could earn a wage without performing for white pleasure. [[jake-brown|Jake]] "liked" the docks (001-024), and their appearance in his mental map (003-034) suggests they hold a steady place in his consciousness, in contrast to the fugitive pleasures of [[the-baltimore-cabaret|the Baltimore]] and [[the-congo|the Congo]].

The dock district also appears as a site of cosmopolitan encounter. In the [[london|East End]], [[jake-brown|Jake]] finds a multiracial working-class community that stands in contrast to the segregated spaces of the United States. (019-005) The docks function narratively as a space of relative freedom and economic agency for Black men who moved between the imperial ports.

## West Indians in the Novel

The docks are connected to a larger West Indian presence in the novel. Several West Indian characters appear in [[harlem|Harlem]]'s social spaces — notably the "West Indian girl" who fights with another woman (007-142)(007-145), the "young West Indian behind [[jake-brown|Jake]]" (007-151), and the "red-brown West Indian" who volunteers to sing (019-051). The novel's social world is populated by Caribbean migrants who moved through the same dock-and-ship network that [[jake-brown|Jake]] navigates.

The term "monkey-chasers" — a derogatory label applied to West Indians — registers the ethnic tensions within [[harlem|Harlem]]'s Black community: "West Indians were monkey-chasers. But no..." (010-073) The unfinished sentence is revealing, gesturing toward solidarity that is not quite spoken.

## From the Novel

A West Indian girl's curse becomes Jake's favorite word, tracing how Caribbean speech enters Harlem's vernacular:

> "Bumbole" was now a popular expletive for Jake. ... Ever since the night at the Congo when he heard the fighting West Indian girl cry, "I'll slap you bumbole," he had always used the word.
>
> (020-042)

## Historical Context

The West India Docks were built between 1800 and 1802, funded largely by sugar and rum merchants whose wealth depended on Caribbean slave plantations. By the early twentieth century, the docks were a hub for the British merchant marine, drawing sailors and dockers from across the empire — including a significant Black workforce from the Caribbean and West Africa. This labor network was central to the formation of Black communities in British port cities and to the circuits of cultural exchange that produced twentieth-century Black Atlantic culture.
