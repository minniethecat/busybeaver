# Social Hierarchy in Home to Harlem

[[claude-mckay|Claude McKay]]'s *[[home-to-harlem|Home to Harlem]]* deploys a precise vocabulary of social types — each term a miniature sociology — to map the internal hierarchies of 1920s Black [[harlem|Harlem]]. These labels are not merely slang; they constitute a taxonomy of class, [[race-and-color|color]], gender performance, and sexual identity that reveals the community's internal fault lines as sharply as any external analysis.

## The Class Axis

### Dickty

The [[dickty]] represent [[harlem|Harlem]]'s Black middle class — "the upstage [[race-and-color|race]] gang," as [[miss-curdy|Miss Curdy]] calls them, who "wouldn't touch [[jack-johnson|Jack Johnson]] with a ten-foot pole" (007-040). They are defined by aspiration, respectability, and distance from the "low-down" life. The [[dickty]] host "swell balls," cultivate connections with influential whites, and maintain a careful separation from the working-class world of [[cabaret-culture|cabarets]] and [[buffet-flat|buffet flats]]. McKay's portrait is ambivalent: the [[dickty]]'s respectability politics are mocked, but their economic achievement is noted.

### Longshoreman

The [[longshoreman]] is the novel's emblematic working-class Black man — a dockworker whose labor is essential to the economy but whose union excludes him. [[jake-brown|Jake]] was a [[longshoreman]] before the war, "a little boss and a very good friend of his big boss, who was Irish" (001-072). The [[longshoreman]]'s position at the bottom of the formal labor market — "they gived the [[race-and-color|color]]ed mens the worser piers and holds the bes'n a' them foh the Irishmen" (005-827) — drives many into the informal nightlife economy.

### Sweetman

The [[sweetman]] is a man kept by a woman — a figure who inverts the expected gender economy. [[zeddy-plummer|Zeddy]] aspires to sweetman status, leaving [[gin-head-susy|Susy]] to demonstrate "that he was a cocky sweetman and no skirt-man" (017-009). The [[yaller-prince|Yaller Prince]] is the type's apotheosis: "Three girls, they said, were engaged in the business of keeping him princely — one chocolate-to-the-bone, one teasing-brown, and one yellow" (016-042). The sweetman is both admired (for getting something for nothing) and resented (for exploiting women's labor).

## The Color Axis

### Ofay

"[[ofay]]" is the novel's term for white people — borrowed from Black vernacular, carrying connotations of the outsider, the oppressor, the customer. The word appears when [[race-and-color|race]] relations are most charged: [[jake-brown|Jake]] exclaims "[[ofay|Ofay]]'s mixing in!" when he first enters [[madame-suarez-buffet-flat|Madame Suarez's]] integrated [[buffet-flat|buffet flat]] (017-011). "[[ofay|Ofay]]" marks the boundary between Black autonomous space and white intrusion.

### High-Yaller

The "[[high-yaller]]" — a light-skinned Black person — occupies an ambivalent position in the [[race-and-color|color]] hierarchy. "High [[high-yaller|yaller]]s were scarce" at [[the-congo|the Congo]] (004-006), the all-Black working-class [[cabaret-culture|cabaret]]. The term encodes both the privilege of lighter skin within the [[race-and-color|color]] hierarchy and the suspicion that "[[high-yaller|yaller]]" people are closer to — and may prefer — the white world. [[gin-head-susy|Susy]]'s "yellow complex" — her obsessive preference for light-skinned men — dramatizes the destructive power of [[race-and-color|colorism]] within the Black community.

## The Gender and Sexuality Axis

### Dandy

The [[dandy]] represents a particular mode of Black masculine self-presentation — flamboyant, [[fashion-and-style|fashion]]-conscious, performative. The "moving-picture [[fashion-and-style|dandy]]" who struts [[lenox-avenue|Lenox Avenue]] in straightened hair and a nigger-brown suit is a figure of aspiration and ridicule. McKay does not moralize about the [[fashion-and-style|dandy]] but records his place in the social ecology: a man who performs success through [[fashion-and-style|dress]] whether or not he has achieved it economically.

### Bulldyker

The [[bulldyker]] is McKay's term for a masculine-presenting [[bulldyker|lesbian]] — one of the novel's most remarkable inclusions for 1928. McKay treats the [[bulldyker]] not as a moral scandal but as a social type among others, positioned within the "[[the-sporting-world|sporting life]]" alongside gamblers, entertainers, and sex workers. The naturalization of queer identity within [[harlem|Harlem]]'s social taxonomy is itself a political statement — an assertion that Black social life in [[harlem|Harlem]] was more sexually diverse and less judgmental than the white mainstream assumed.

## The Taxonomy as Sociology

What makes McKay's social taxonomy powerful is its insistence on complexity. These labels are not merely descriptive — they are weapons, identities, aspirations. Characters use "[[dickty]]" to mock social climbers, "[[ofay]]" to mark racial boundaries, "[[high-yaller]]" to police [[race-and-color|color]] lines within the [[race-and-color|race]]. The taxonomy maps power: who has it, who wants it, who is excluded from it. In a novel that refuses explicit political commentary, the social labels do the analytical work — showing, rather than telling, how [[harlem|Harlem]] was stratified.
