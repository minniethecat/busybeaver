Miss Curdy is a supporting character in [[claude-mckay|Claude McKay]]'s *Home to [[harlem|Harlem]]*, appearing primarily in chapters 6 and 7 as a friend and drinking companion to [[gin-head-susy|Susy]]. Known by her first name Lavinia, she is characterized by her sharp social commentary, drinking habits, and efforts to present herself as a woman of refinement.

## Role in the Novel

Miss Curdy functions as part of [[gin-head-susy|Susy]]'s social circle, participating in the rent parties and nightlife of working-class [[harlem|Harlem]]. She appears alongside [[gin-head-susy|Susy]] and [[strawberry-lips|Strawberry Lips]] during a free Sunday outing, and is present at several sociable gatherings. Her dialogue reveals class tensions within [[harlem|Harlem]] society, as she frequently contrasts her present circumstances with her claimed past in "real society." She also serves as a foil to [[jake-brown|Jake Brown]], whom she attempts to charm at a sociable, only to be rebuffed and disgusted by his rejection. (006-054)(006-066)

## Characterization

### Appearance

Miss Curdy is described as less attractive than [[gin-head-susy|Susy]], with McKay noting that "That was not so difficult, as Miss Curdy was less attractive than [[gin-head-susy|Susy]]." (006-019) She wears French heels when going out socially. (007-127)

### Personality and Traits

Miss Curdy prides herself on her social knowledge and past refinement. She advises [[gin-head-susy|Susy]] on men and money, urging her to save rather than "spend so much coin on [[gin-whisky|gin]]." (006-024) She is sharp-tongued and quick to assert her opinions, at one point bridling: "I don't know about that. There's many nice women." (006-057) She is also a heavy drinker — described as "guzzling hard" alongside [[gin-head-susy|Susy]], "growing uglier" as the evening wears on. (006-053)

### Relationships

- [[gin-head-susy|Susy]] — close friend and drinking companion; Miss Curdy advises and scolds her
- [[strawberry-lips|Strawberry Lips]] — companion at social gatherings
- [[jake-brown|Jake Brown]] — attempts to charm him and is rejected; leers at him and disgusts him (006-066)

## Key Scenes

At a sociable in chapter 6, Miss Curdy observes the crowd and tunes "her ears to the conversation" around her. (006-045)(006-051) When [[jake-brown|Jake]] throws up the dice, she "seized her chance to get him" but is rejected. (006-054)(006-066)

During [[gin-head-susy|Susy]]'s free Sunday outing (chapter 7), Miss Curdy joins [[gin-head-susy|Susy]] and [[strawberry-lips|Strawberry Lips]] for drinking and eating. She declines more chicken, mumbling "No more chicken for me, [[gin-head-susy|Susy]], but I will have another drink." (007-024) At a [[cabaret-culture|cabaret]] later, Miss Curdy is among [[gin-head-susy|Susy]]'s party when she spots [[zeddy-plummer|Zeddy]] across the room — it is Miss Curdy who sees him first, digging into [[gin-head-susy|Susy]] to point him out. (007-130)

In a rare moment of reflection, Miss Curdy sighs about Sweetie May's success, saying she was "blessed with sweet flute and fiddle," and laments that things are "not like they used to in real society." (007-064)(007-066)

## Themes

Miss Curdy embodies tensions of class aspiration and social performance within [[harlem|Harlem]]'s working-class milieu. Her claims of having been "high up in society" in San Francisco (007-155) stand in tension with her present circumstances, highlighting the gap between self-perception and social reality that McKay explores throughout the novel.
