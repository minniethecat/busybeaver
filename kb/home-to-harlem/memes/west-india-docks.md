**West India Docks** are a historic dock complex in [[london|London]] that appear in [[claude-mckay|Claude McKay]]'s *[[home-to-harlem|Home to Harlem]]* as the workplace of [[jake-brown|Jake Brown]] during his time in England.

## Location

### In the Novel's World

The West India Docks are part of [[jake-brown|Jake]]'s London geography. After deserting from the U.S. Army in Brest and crossing to [[london|London]], "he found plenty of work there as a docker. He liked the West India Docks. He liked [[limehouse|Limehouse]]." (001-024) The docks represent honest physical labor and a period of contentment before Jake's return to [[harlem|Harlem]].

## Significance to the Narrative

The West India Docks serve as a symbolic waypoint in Jake's journey. They are listed among the ports of his travels — "Brest. Le Havre. London. The West India Docks. And back home to Harlem." (003-034) — mapping his geographic and emotional arc from wartime Europe back to Black America. The docks embody the international working-class identity Jake briefly inhabits before re-entering the specific racial and social landscape of [[harlem|Harlem]].

## Historical Context

The West India Docks were opened in 1802 on the Isle of Dogs in East London, built to handle trade with the West Indies. By the early twentieth century they were a hub of London's dockland economy, employing thousands of laborers including many from Britain's colonies and beyond. They were closed in 1980 and later redeveloped as part of Canary Wharf.

## Quotations

Jake's contentment at the West India Docks during his London period:

> Jake crossed over to London. He found plenty of work there as a docker. He liked the West India Docks. He liked Limehouse. In the pubs men gave him their friendly paws and called him "darky." He liked how they called him "darky." He made friends. He found a woman. He was happy in the East End.
>
> (001-024)
