# Jake's Desertion

[[jake-brown|Jake Brown]]'s desertion from the United States Army after the Armistice of November 1918 is the act that sets *[[Home to Harlem]]* in motion — a refusal of military authority that sends him from [[brest|Brest]], France, across the Atlantic and back to [[harlem|Harlem]].

## Overview

After enlisting to fight in World War I and being assigned to a labor battalion instead, [[jake-brown|Jake]] spent the war handling mules. The Armistice found him in [[london|London]], and rather than return to his unit, he chose to desert. His desertion is presented not as cowardice but as a kind of existential autonomy — a Black man choosing his own direction over the white army's command.

## Context

### The Decision to Leave

> But I beat it away from Brest because they wouldn't give us no chance. (021-089)

[[jake-brown|Jake]]'s explanation of his desertion is characteristically direct. He "beat it away from Brest" — the verb phrase suggesting both urgency and self-determination. The reason he gives is not fear of combat but the army's refusal to treat Black soldiers equally: "they wouldn't give us no chance."

### Desertion as Liberation

[[jake-brown|Jake]] is not alone in his rejection of military authority. The novel's [[harlem|Harlem]] contains other men who evaded service: "Gov'mant still smoking out deserters and draft dodgers" (003-035). Desertion and draft evasion form a counterpoint to the official narrative of patriotic service — a quiet, collective refusal.

## Aftermath

[[jake-brown|Jake]]'s desertion has no legal consequences in the novel. McKay never shows him arrested or punished, a narrative choice that aligns with the novel's refusal to judge its protagonist. The desertion becomes simply the prelude to his journey home: "Take me home to Harlem, Mister Ship!" (001-008).
