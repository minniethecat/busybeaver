**Asa Philip Randolph** (1889--1979) was the most significant [[africa|Africa]]n American labor leader of the twentieth century and a central figure in the struggle for civil rights. Though he does not appear directly in *Home to [[harlem|Harlem]]*, his organizing work among [[pullman-company|Pullman]] porters and his leadership of the March on [[washington|Washington]] Movement are essential context for understanding the [[labor|labor]] world that [[claude-mckay|Claude McKay]] depicts — particularly the lives of [[the-railroad|railroad]] workers, [[longshoreman|longshoremen]], and [[food-and-dining|dining]]-car cooks who populate the novel.

## Historical/Cultural Context

### Labor Organizing and the Brotherhood

Randolph founded the Brotherhood of Sleeping Car Porters (BSCP) in 1925, the same era in which *Home to [[harlem|Harlem]]* is set. The [[pullman-company|Pullman Company]] was one of the largest employers of [[africa|Africa]]n Americans, and its [[sleeping-late|sleeping]] car porters worked grueling hours for low wages under humiliating conditions. Randolph's twelve-year campaign to win recognition for the BSCP — finally achieved in 1937 with the first collective bargaining agreement between a Black union and a major American corporation — transformed Black labor politics.

The [[the-railroad|railroad]] world that Randolph organized was the same one that [[jake-brown|Jake Brown]] and [[ray|Ray]] inhabit. When [[jake-brown|Jake]] works as a cook on the [[pennsylvania-railroad|Pennsylvania]] [[dining-car|dining car]]s — "the [[waiter|waiters]] in the [[food-and-dining|dining]]-car were all white, but few of them were native-born Americans" (012-017) — he occupies precisely the occupational tier that Randolph's organizing sought to empower. The [[pullman-company|Pullman]] porters, [[waiter|dining-car waiter]]s, and kitchen staff that [[claude-mckay|McKay]] renders with ethnographic precision were the constituency Randolph would soon mobilize.

### March on Washington Movement

In 1941, Randolph threatened to lead 100,000 [[africa|Africa]]n Americans in a march on [[washington|Washington]] to protest discrimination in defense industries. President Franklin D. Roosevelt responded by issuing Executive Order 8802, banning discrimination in defense contracting — the first major federal civil rights action since Reconstruction. Randolph later served as a key organizer of the 1963 March on [[washington|Washington]] for Jobs and Freedom, where Martin Luther King Jr. delivered his "I Have a Dream" speech.

### Significance to the Harlem Renaissance

Randolph's magazine *The Messenger*, which he co-edited with Chandler Owen starting in 1917, was an important venue for radical Black thought during the [[harlem-renaissance|Harlem Renaissance]] period. Unlike the literary and cultural emphasis of publications such as *The Crisis* (edited by [[w-e-b-du-bois|W. E. B. Du Bois]]), *The Messenger* combined socialist politics with cultural commentary, advocating for both economic justice and racial equality. It published early work by Black writers and provided a forum for debate about the direction of the New Negro movement.

## Role in the Novel's World

Although Randolph is not named in *Home to [[harlem|Harlem]]*, the labor consciousness that pervades the novel — from [[jake-brown|Jake]]'s [[jakes-desertion|desertion from the army]] after being assigned to menial labor (003-004), to the [[longshoreman|longshoremen]]'s strike in [[harlem|Harlem]], to [[ray|Ray]]'s intellectual engagement with racial politics — reflects the labor radicalism that Randolph embodied. The novel's working-class [[harlem|Harlem]] is precisely the world from which the BSCP drew its membership.
