A **railroad flat** is a type of tenement apartment in which rooms are arranged in a straight line, one behind the other like railroad cars, with no hallway. In [[claude-mckay|Claude McKay]]'s *[[home-to-harlem|Home to Harlem]]*, the railroad flat serves as a key setting for informal social and economic life in [[harlem|Harlem]].

## Definition

Railroad flats were a common housing type in New York City tenements, consisting of three or four rooms connected in sequence. The front room faced the street while the back rooms, often lacking windows, opened directly into each other. This layout maximized density but provided little privacy.

## In the Novel

[[billy-biasse|Billy Biasse]]'s railroad flat serves as "the longshoremen gaming rendezvous" (005-023) — a site of informal [[gambling]] where working men gather. [[jake-brown|Jake]] prefers Billy Biasse's railroad flat to more upscale gambling venues: "He preferred Billy Biasse's, where he could go whenever he liked with hook and overalls." (005-023) The railroad flat thus represents the democratic, working-class spaces of [[harlem|Harlem]] — in contrast to the "buffet flat in 140th Street" frequented by railroad porters and club waiters "who gambled for big stakes" and where "he had to dress up as if he were going to a [[cabaret]]." (005-023)

The flat's informality and accessibility make it a node of male working-class sociability, where labor, leisure, and risk intersect.

## From the Novel

Jake's preference for the unpretentious railroad flat over fancier [[gambling]] venues maps the class distinctions within Harlem's nightlife:

> He preferred Billy Biasse's, where he could go whenever he liked with hook and overalls. Also, he was not a big-stake gambler.
>
> (005-023)
