**Aunt Hattie's Cookshop** is a basement restaurant and chitterling joint on 132nd Street in [[harlem|Harlem]], featured in [[claude-mckay|Claude McKay]]'s *[[home-to-harlem|Home to Harlem]]* as a beloved gathering place for the neighborhood's working-class Black residents. Run by the cheery-faced Aunt Hattie, it is renowned for its home-cooked Southern food and its role as an informal social hub.

## Overview

Aunt Hattie's is a "little basement joint, smoke-colored" (003-028) located on 132nd Street, operating as both a restaurant and a social nexus for Harlem's working class. [[jake-brown|Jake Brown]] is introduced to it by [[strawberry-lips|Strawberry Lips]] during his first days back in Harlem, and it becomes one of his regular haunts. The establishment serves traditional Southern fare — fried chicken, rice, green peas, stewed corn, coconut pie — prepared by Aunt Hattie herself.

> From the poolroom they went to Aunt Hattie's chitterling joint in 132nd Street, where they fed. Fricassee chicken and rice. Green peas. Stewed corn. (003-027)

## Atmosphere and Character

Aunt Hattie's is renowned among "the lowly of Harlem's Black Belt" (003-028) for its authentic home cooking — "home-cooked food they called it." (003-028) The proprietress, Aunt Hattie, is "weather-beaten dark-brown, cheery-faced, with two rusty-red front teeth sticking together conspicuously out of her twisted, spread-away mouth." (003-028) Despite her age, she retains a lively sensuality; on first meeting [[jake-brown|Jake]], "a sensual light gleamed in her aged smoke-red eyes" (003-029) as she brushes against him while serving.

## Role in the Narrative

Aunt Hattie's serves multiple functions in the novel. It is where [[jake-brown|Jake]] first eats upon returning to [[harlem|Harlem]] (003-027), where [[strawberry-lips|Strawberry Lips]] brings him to socialize, and where [[jake-brown|Jake]] later hosts a celebratory feed for his friends (018-016). The cookshop also functions as a site of informal social networking — characters meet, eat, and make arrangements there.

> The feed was spread at Aunt Hattie's cookshop. Jake maintained that Aunt Hattie's was the best place for good eats in Harlem. (018-016)

## Social Function

Aunt Hattie's represents the informal, working-class food economy of [[harlem|Harlem]] — distinct from the [[cabaret-culture|cabarets]] and [[speakeasy|speakeasies]] that dominate the novel's nightlife settings. It is a daytime gathering place where the neighborhood's ordinary residents — laborers, poolroom regulars, women from the South — can eat affordably and socialize. The cookshop anchors the community life of the side streets, away from the glamour of Seventh Avenue and the cabarets.

## Key Scenes

> Aunt Hattie's was renowned among the lowly of Harlem's Black Belt. It was a little basement joint, smoke-colored. And Aunt Hattie was weather-beaten dark-brown, cheery-faced, with two rusty-red front teeth sticking together conspicuously out of her twisted, spread-away mouth. She cooked delicious food — home-cooked food they called it. None of the boys loafing round that section of Fifth Avenue would dream of going to any other place for their "poke chops." (003-028)
