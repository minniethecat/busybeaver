The elevated train, commonly called the "El," was a defining feature of New York City's transit landscape in the early twentieth century — iron and steel railways mounted on trestles above city streets, carrying passengers through the boroughs with a characteristic roar. By the 1920s setting of *Home to [[harlem|Harlem]]*, elevated lines coexisted with the newer [[subway]] system, and both were essential to the mobility of working-class New Yorkers, including the characters of [[claude-mckay|Claude McKay]]'s novel.

## Definition

Elevated railways were urban transit lines built on raised structures, typically of iron or steel, above street level. New York's first El opened in 1868, and by 1880 the city had a network of elevated lines serving Manhattan. Brooklyn developed its own elevated system as well. By the 1920s, many Manhattan elevated lines had been or were being replaced by subways (which were quieter and did not darken streets), but Brooklyn's elevated lines — including the Myrtle Avenue Line, the Fifth Avenue Line, and the Third Avenue Line — remained in operation.

The El was more than transit: it was an environmental presence. The trains were loud, producing a distinctive clatter and roar that filled the streets below. Sunlight was blocked beneath the trestles, creating a permanent twilight zone that became associated with commerce, vice, and working-class life. The Els created different kinds of urban space — above (the platform worlds of stations), below (the shadowed streets), and inside (the cars themselves, where the city's diverse population mixed).

## Cultural Context

### In the Novel

[[claude-mckay|McKay]] makes the elevated train a vivid part of his urban soundscape. The most significant mention occurs during a scene in Brooklyn: "The Myrtle Avenue Elevated train passed with a high raucous rushing noise, drowning the quietude of the sleepy Black Belt neighborhood." (006-087) This sentence does considerable work: it identifies a specific line (Myrtle Avenue), conveys the sensory experience of the El's passage (high, raucous, rushing), and contrasts it with the neighborhood's character — the "sleepy" quality of the Black Belt disrupted by industrial transit.

Throughout the novel, characters rely on the transit system to move between [[harlem|Harlem]], Brooklyn, and downtown Manhattan. [[jake-brown|Jake]] takes "an express subway train for Harlem" (002-002) at the novel's opening, establishing mobility as a theme from the first pages. The working-class characters of *Home to [[harlem|Harlem]]* are constantly in motion, and the combination of elevated and underground trains makes their city accessible.

The novel's broader transit geography includes repeated subway journeys: "Strawberry Lips had left the subway train at Borough Hall" (006-037), "They caught the subway train for Harlem" (007-085), and "[Jake] caught the subway train for Brooklyn" (007-161). These movements trace the novel's map of Black New York across boroughs, connected by the transit system.

## Key Passages

> The Myrtle Avenue Elevated train passed with a high raucous rushing noise, drowning the quietude of the sleepy Black Belt neighborhood. (006-087)

This passage captures the El as an aural and physical intrusion into neighborhood life. The word "high" suggests both the elevated position and the pitch of the noise; "raucous" conveys a disorderly, almost violent sound; "rushing" emphasizes speed and force. The contrast between industrial transit and the "sleepy" neighborhood enacts a recurring modernist tension between machine and human scale.

## Historical Significance

Elevated trains were more than transportation — they were a class marker. The streets beneath the Els, darkened and noisy, were cheaper precincts of the city, associated with working-class housing, shops, and entertainments. The removal of Manhattan's elevated lines in the 1930s and 1940s was part of a larger urban transformation that pushed working-class life to the margins and restructured the city's racial geography.

For Black New Yorkers in the 1920s, the transit system — elevated and underground — was one of the few public spaces where racial mixing occurred routinely, though not without tension. The El car, like the subway car, was a microcosm of the city, bringing together passengers of different races, classes, and destinations in enforced proximity.
