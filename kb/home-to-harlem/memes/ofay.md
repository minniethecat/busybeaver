# Ofay

A period-specific [[harlem|Harlem]] slang term meaning "white person" (or "white people" collectively), used throughout *Home to [[harlem|Harlem]]* to mark the omnipresent racial boundary that structured every aspect of life in 1920s [[new-york-city|New York]].

## Definition

"Ofay" (also spelled "fay") was a slang term used in Black communities during the early 20th century to refer to white people. Its etymology is uncertain — theories include Pig Latin for "foe," West [[africa|Africa]]n language roots, and carnival slang — but its function in McKay's novel is clear: it is the linguistic marker of racial difference, used by Black characters to name, assess, and sometimes mock the white world that surrounds and cons[[the-railroad|train]]s them. (003-045)

## Cultural Context

### Harlem Renaissance Context

Slang terms for racial categories formed a rich lexicon in 1920s [[harlem|Harlem]], reflecting the community's need to name and navigate the realities of American racial hierarchy. "Ofay" was one of several terms — alongside "[[dickty]]," "shine," "[[high-yaller|high yaller]]," and others — that mapped the racial landscape from a distinctly Black perspective. Using "ofay" rather than "white" asserted a Black linguistic autonomy, an insider vocabulary that white people might not understand.

The term also featured in the broader discourse of the [[harlem|Harlem]] Renaissance about racial authenticity and the relationship between Black artists and white patrons. White financial support for [[harlem-renaissance|the Renaissance]] created uncomfortable dependencies that writers like McKay explored through characters' relationships with "ofays."

### In the Novel

McKay's characters use "ofay" with a range of emotional registers. [[jake-brown|Jake]] uses it matter-of-factly: "Same old [[new-york-city|New York]]. But the ofay faces am different from those ovah across the pond" (003-045), comparing white Americans unfavorably to white Europeans he encountered during the war.

The term can carry resentment: characters speak of "vomiting out their guts to the ofays about one another" (003-037), criticizing Black people who perform their conflicts for white audiences. [[rose-congo|Rose]] dismisses Scotch [[gin-whisky|whisky]] as "an ofay drink" (004-080), using the term to mark cultural territory — what belongs to Black people versus what belongs to white people.

At [[madame-suarez-buffet-flat|Madame Suarez's]] [[buffet-flat|buffet flat]], [[jake-brown|Jake]] reflects: "But ofay or ofay not, this here is the real stuff" (008-009), acknowled[[gin-whisky|gin]]g the presence of white patrons while affirming the essential Blackness of the space and its culture. The phrase "ofay or ofay not" suggests a pragmatism — the racial boundary exists but doesn't define every experience.

The [[cabaret-culture|cabaret]] economy's dependence on white trade creates tension: "Connor's had plunged deeper for the ofay trade. Goldgraben was grabbing all the ofay money" (004-004). The term "ofay trade" frames white patronage as a commercial resource, something to be captured and exploited — but also something that could corrupt or dilute Black cultural spaces.

## Key Passages

> Same old New York. But the ofay faces am different from those ovah across the pond. (003-045)

[[jake-brown|Jake]]'s comparison of white Americans to white Europeans suggests that whiteness itself is culturally variable — the "ofay" of [[london|London's East End]] is different from the "ofay" of [[new-york-city|New York]], and the former, in [[jake-brown|Jake]]'s experience, was less hostile.

> They's so doggone used to being fed-up on the ofays. (004-057)

[[jake-brown|Jake]]'s observation about [[cabaret-culture|cabaret]] workers who have grown accustomed to serving white patrons captures the economic dependency that underlay [[harlem|Harlem]] nightlife. "Fed-up" conveys both saturation and resentment.

> But ofay or ofay not, this here is the real stuff. (008-009)

This phrase encapsulates a key theme of the novel: the presence of white people in Black spaces does not automatically diminish those spaces' authenticity. The "real stuff" of Black culture persists regardless of who is watching.

## Variations & Related Terms

- **[[dickty|Dickty]]** — A class marker within the Black community, distinct from the racial boundary "ofay" marks between Black and white
- **[[shine|Shine]]** — Affectionate or neutral slang for a Black person; the "ofay"/"shine" pair defined the racial binary in [[harlem|Harlem]] slang
- **Cracker** — A more hostile term for white people, used occasionally in the novel
- **Pigmentocracy** — The scholarly term for the [[race-and-color|color]]-based social hierarchy that "ofay" vocabulary navigated

## Historical Significance

The term "ofay" belongs to a long tradition of [[africa|Africa]]n American linguistic creativity in naming racial categories — a tradition that includes "buckra" (from West [[africa|Africa]]n languages), "cracker," "honky," and others. These terms served multiple functions: they provided in-group solidarity through shared vocabulary, they allowed Black people to discuss white people without being understood by them, and they asserted a Black-centered perspective on the racial order. While "ofay" faded from common usage after mid-century, its presence in [[harlem|Harlem]] Renaissance literature preserves a record of how Black Americans linguistically negotiated the [[race-and-color|color]] line.
