**England** appears in *Home to Harlem* as a key node in the transatlantic geography that shapes the novel's characters and their migrations. Referenced primarily through the lens of London and its East End, England represents both wartime service and post-war labor opportunities for Black men from the British Empire and the United States.

## England in the Black Transatlantic Circuit

The novel positions England within a broader circuit of ports and cities through which Black laborers moved:

> "…station in Brest. Le Havre. London. The West India Docks. And ba…" (003-034)

> "West India Dock districts of London." (019-005)

These references connect England — specifically London — to the French ports of Brest and Le Havre, establishing a maritime geography familiar to Jake and his fellow seamen and soldiers.

## Wartime Context

During World War I, England became a destination and way station for Black soldiers and laborers from across the British Empire. The novel's opening chapters, set in London's East End, depict the multiracial community that formed around the docks and working-class neighborhoods.

> "…away from him, going back to London, to Brest, Le Havre, wanderin…" (005-003)

This passage captures the restless mobility of characters who move between England and the Continent, part of a displaced generation shaped by war.

## Colonial and Racial Dimensions

England in the novel carries complex associations — it is both a site of relative freedom (compared to the American South) and a reminder of colonial hierarchy. Characters reference England in the context of fights between Black and white men:

> "Fights between black men and white…" (003-030)

The presence of Black men in England — many from Britain's Caribbean colonies — creates racial tensions that mirror, in different form, those of Harlem.
