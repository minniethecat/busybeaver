Agatha is a minor character in [[claude-mckay|Claude McKay]]'s *Home to [[harlem|Harlem]]*, appearing in chapters 14 and 18 as a romantic interest of [[ray|Ray]]. She is depicted as a "rich-brown girl" who visits [[ray|Ray]] in his room and later acts wistfully as he prepares to leave [[harlem|Harlem]].

## Role in the Novel

Agatha serves as a connection to [[ray|Ray]]'s emotional life in [[harlem|Harlem]]. She has "heard much of [[ray|Ray]]'s best self" (014-020) and visits him at his room, where their encounter is brief but charged with unspoken feeling. In chapter 18, she appears again, "acting wistfully" as [[ray|Ray]] contemplates his departure. (018-003)

## Characterization

### Appearance

Agatha is described as "a rich-brown girl, with slim legs" who wears a "plain nigger-brown coat" that, when unbuttoned, reveals "a fresh white stiff-starched blouse." (014-021)(014-030) Her attire suggests modesty and simplicity.

### Personality and Traits

Agatha's behavior suggests quiet lon[[gin-whisky|gin]]g and res[[the-railroad|train]]t. She raps on [[ray|Ray]]'s door to visit him (014-022), stays only twenty minutes (014-037), and later "acts wistfully" as [[ray|Ray]] prepares to leave — knowing, as he does, that their connection will not last. ### Relationships

- [[ray|Ray]] — romantic interest; she visits him and shares a brief, emotionally charged connection

## Key Scenes

Agatha visits [[ray|Ray]] at his room. She finds him "with a pail of beer beside him" when she raps on the door. (014-022) The narrator observes her appearance in detail — "The plain nigger-brown coat Agatha wore, unbuttoned, showed a fresh white stiff-starched blouse." (014-030) Her visit lasts exactly twenty minutes. (014-037)

Later, in chapter 18, Agatha appears again, "acting wistfully" as [[ray|Ray]] contemplates leaving [[harlem|Harlem]]. (018-003)

## Themes

Agatha represents the transient relationships of [[harlem|Harlem]] life, where connections form quickly but rarely last. Her wistfulness reflects the broader theme of impermanence and the difficulty of sustaining intimate bonds in a world shaped by [[migration]], economic precarity, and racial exclusion.
