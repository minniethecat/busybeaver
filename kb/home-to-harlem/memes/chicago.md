**Chicago** is an Illinois city that appears at the close of [[claude-mckay|Claude McKay]]'s *Home to [[harlem|Harlem]]* chapter 21 as a destination of hope and renewal. It represents the next frontier for [[harlem|Harlem]]'s characters, a city of mythic scale and possibility.

## Location

### In the Novel's World

Chicago is imagined rather than visited. It is invoked as a place "to git lost in" (021-096) — a city of vastness and anonymity. The characters discuss it with excitement: "Why, le's go to Chicago, then. I hear it's a mahvelous place." (021-098) Unlike the East Coast cities that [[jake-brown|Jake]] has exhausted — [[pittsburgh|Pittsburgh]], [[philadelphia|Philadelphia]], Baltimore — Chicago represents the promise of the new.

## Significance to the Narrative

Chicago appears only in the novel's final pages, but it carries symbolic weight. The *Chicago Defender*, one of the most influential Black newspapers of the era, is mentioned earlier alongside the *[[pittsburgh|Pittsburgh]] Courier* and *Baltimore American*. (011-024) In chapter 21, a character declares: "Anyhow, I'm bound foh Chicago. I done made up mah mind to go there." (021-136) A parting gift — a "black necklace" — is guarded with the words: "I couldn't go to Chicago without it." (021-141)

Chicago thus functions as the novel's symbolic horizon — a place beyond the known circuit of East Coast cities, associated with the *Defender*'s call to migration, with the promise of reinvention, and with the restless energy that drives McKay's characters ever onward.

## From the Novel

Jake's determination to reach Chicago is unshakable, even when his plans unravel:

> Anyhow, I'm bound foh Chicago. I done made up mah mind to go all becausing a her, and I ain'ta gwineta change it whether she throws me down or not.
>
> (021-136)

## Historical Context

Chicago was the premier destination of the Great Migration — between 1910 and 1930, the city's Black population grew from about 44,000 to over 230,000. The *Chicago Defender*, founded by Robert S. Abbott in 1905, actively encouraged Southern Black readers to migrate north. McKay's positioning of Chicago at the novel's end reflects its real-world status as the ultimate destination for Black migrants seeking a new life.
