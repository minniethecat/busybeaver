# W. E. B. Du Bois

William Edward Burghardt Du Bois (1868–1963) was an American sociologist, historian, and civil rights activist. As editor of *The Crisis*, the magazine of the NAACP, and a central architect of the [[harlem-renaissance|Harlem Renaissance]], Du Bois was the period's most influential arbiter of what Black art and literature should be.

## Role in the Harlem Renaissance

Du Bois believed that art and literature should serve the political project of racial uplift. In his 1926 essay "Criteria of Negro Art," he argued that "all Art is propaganda and ever must be," and that Black artists had a moral obligation to present dignified, aspirational images of Black life that would counter white supremacist stereotypes. This position put him at odds with younger writers who insisted on the freedom to depict Black life in all its complexity — including its "low-life" dimensions.

## Criticism of Home to Harlem

When [[claude-mckay|Claude McKay]]'s *[[home-to-harlem|Home to Harlem]]* appeared in 1928, Du Bois reviewed it harshly in *The Crisis*. He wrote that the novel "for the most part nauseates me, and after the dirtier parts of its filth I feel distinctly like taking a bath." Du Bois objected specifically to McKay's unvarnished depiction of [[harlem|Harlem]]'s working-class nightlife — the drinking, gambling, casual sex, and violence that the novel renders without moral judgment.

This review crystallized a fundamental debate within the [[harlem-renaissance|Harlem Renaissance]] over artistic freedom versus racial representation. Younger writers, most prominently [[langston-hughes|Langston Hughes]], defended McKay's right to depict Black life honestly and without the cons[[the-railroad|train]]ts of respectability politics. The debate echoed through subsequent decades and remains central to discussions of Black literature and the politics of representation.

## Intellectual Legacy

Du Bois's larger intellectual project — articulated in *The Souls of Black Folk* (1903) and developed across a lifetime of scholarship and activism — shaped the framework within which the [[harlem-renaissance|Harlem Renaissance]] unfolded. His concept of "double consciousness," the sense of "always looking at one's self through the eyes of others," provided a foundational vocabulary for understanding Black identity in America. His insistence on the "Talented Tenth" as a leadership class for Black progress influenced [[harlem-renaissance|the Renaissance]]'s emphasis on artistic and intellectual achievement as vehicles for racial advancement.

Tensions between Du Bois's program of uplift and the more radical, bohemian energies of [[harlem-renaissance|the Renaissance]] remained generative throughout the period. Writers like McKay, Hughes, and [[zoraneale-hurston|Zora Neale Hurston]] stretched the boundaries that Du Bois sought to define, producing work that was richer for the friction.
