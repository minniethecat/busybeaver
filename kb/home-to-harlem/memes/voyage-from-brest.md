# Jake's Voyage from Brest to Harlem

The journey from [[brest|Brest]], [[france|France]], to [[harlem|Harlem]] — by way of [[le-havre|Le Havre]] and [[london|London]] — is the geographical arc that opens *[[Home to Harlem]]*, mapping a Black Atlantic circuit of ports, freighters, and migrant desire.

## Overview

When [[jake-brown|Jake]] deserts the army in [[brest|Brest]] after the [[armistice-1918|Armistice]], he does not simply go home. He travels: [[brest|Brest]] → [[le-havre|Le Havre]] → [[london|London]] → a freighter across the Atlantic → [[harlem|Harlem]]. The journey is both literal [[migration]] and symbolic passage — from the white army's discipline to Black [[harlem|Harlem]]'s [[pleasure-seeking|pleasure]]s, from Europe's war to America's racial order.

## The Route

[[jake-brown|Jake]]'s path follows a well-worn circuit of Black maritime labor. "In the winter he sailed for [[brest|Brest]] with a happy chocolate company" (001-010) — the journey out was collective and "happy." The return is solitary and improvised. [[zeddy|Zeddy]], a fellow veteran, recalls "the everlasting days of [[brest|Brest]]" (003-030) as a shared memory of waiting, of labor, of the port city's liminal space between army life and civilian freedom.

The route — "[[brest|Brest]]. [[le-havre|Le Havre]]. [[london|London]]. The [[west-india|West India]]" (003-034) — names a geography of Black working-class mobility. These are not tourist destinations but labor ports, the nodes of a maritime economy that carried coal, cargo, and [[race-and-color|color]]ed stokers across the Atlantic.

## Significance

The voyage from [[brest|Brest]] establishes the novel's spatial ima[[gin-whisky|gin]]ation. [[harlem|Harlem]] is not an insular neighborhood but a destination reached through global movement. [[jake-brown|Jake]] arrives not as a provincial migrant from the American South but as a veteran of European ports, a man who has seen [[london|London]] and chosen [[harlem|Harlem]].

> Take me home to Harlem, Mister Ship! (001-008)

This cry — spoken to the freighter carrying him across the Atlantic — is the novel's first articulation of desire. "Home" is not [[virginia|Virginia]], where [[jake-brown|Jake]] enlisted, but [[harlem|Harlem]], the Black metropolis he has not yet seen.
