A recurring figure in [[claude-mckay|Claude McKay]]'s *Home to [[harlem|Harlem]]*: the dandy is the sharply dressed, style-conscious Black man whose clothing signals status, aspiration, and a distinctive mode of masculine self-presentation. The dandy appears in the novel's street scenes, poolrooms, and train stations, his elegant appearance contrasting with the drabness of working-class labor.

## Definition

In the novel, "dandy" describes a man whose dress exceeds the functional — he is "smart as a moving-picture dandy" (006-012), "extravagantly-dressed" (007-051). The term carries both admiration and irony. To be a dandy is to claim visibility and status through appearance, rejecting the invisibility that racism imposed on Black men. But the dandy's claim to status through clothing can also be read as a critique of materialism, a performance that substitutes surface for substance.

## Cultural Context

### Harlem Renaissance Context

The Black dandy has a long history in African American culture, dating back to the eighteenth and nineteenth centuries when enslaved and free Black men used clothing to contest their assigned social position. In the 1920s, the dandy flourished in [[harlem|Harlem]]'s streets, where the "strutting" Black man in a well-cut suit and polished shoes was a visible assertion of presence and pride. The dandy was also a stock figure in [[harlem|Harlem]] Renaissance literature, appearing in the work of Zora Neale Hurston, Wallace Thurman, and others as a symbol of both racial aspiration and the seductions of consumer culture.

### In the Novel

McKay's use of "dandy" is economical but significant. The term marks class and style distinctions among Black men: the poolroom youngster who dresses sharp to leave [[gin-head-susy|Susy]] (006-012), the "extravagantly-dressed dark dandy" observed on the street (007-051), the generic wish to be "strutting fine and dandy" (019-088). The dandy is not a character type that McKay develops at length but a visual and social category that his characters recognize and evaluate — part of the novel's documentary attention to the varieties of Black masculine self-presentation.

## Variations and Related Terms

- **Dandy vs. [[sweetman|Sweetman]]** — Both are social types concerned with appearance, but the sweetman is defined by his economic relationship to women (kept by them), while the dandy is defined by his aesthetic self-presentation (dressing sharp). A man could be both.
- **Dandy vs. [[dickty|Dickty]]** — "[[dickty|Dickty]]" in the novel denotes someone who puts on airs of class superiority; a dandy may or may not be [[dickty]], and a [[dickty]] person may or may not be a dandy. [[dickty|Dickty]] is about attitude; dandy is about appearance.
- **"Strutting"** — The characteristic gait associated with the dandy, connoting confidence and display (019-088).

## Key Passages

> Looking smart as a moving-picture dandy, he would leave Susy — that was his ambition. (006-012)

> An extravagantly-dressed dark dandy passed by and Zeddy remarked, "He was living swell before the war, but he ain't got nothing now." (007-051)

> Keep him strutting fine and dandy. (019-088)

## Historical Significance

The Black dandy tradition in the United States has been the subject of significant scholarship, from Monica Miller's *Slaves to Fashion: Black Dandyism and the Styling of Black Diasporic Identity* (2009) to studies of the "New Negro" aesthetic. The dandy represented a form of resistance to the dehumanization of racism: by dressing elegantly, Black men asserted a selfhood that white society denied. At the same time, the dandy was critiqued within Black communities for prioritizing surface over substance — a tension visible in McKay's ambivalent deployment of the term.

In the context of the Great Migration, the dandy was also a figure of urban modernity. Unlike the rural Southern field hand, the [[harlem|Harlem]] dandy participated in a consumer economy of suits, shoes, and accessories. His appearance declared that he belonged to the city, to the twentieth century, and to himself.
