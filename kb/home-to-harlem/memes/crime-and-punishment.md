# Crime and Punishment

*Crime and Punishment* is a novel by Fyodor Dostoevsky, first published in 1866. It appears in *[[home-to-harlem|Home to Harlem]]* as the book that [[ray|Ray]] — one of the waiters at the dining-car job — is reading.

## In the Novel

Ray is introduced as a literate, contemplative presence among the workingmen of the dining car. While his coworkers gamble at poker, Ray reads Dostoevsky:

> "Ray was reading Dostoevsky's Crime and Punishment. The fourth waiter was working the blackjack game with three of the men and a waiter from the next car" (012-018)

The detail of Ray reading Dostoevsky positions him as intellectually curious — a working-class Black man engaging with Russian literature in the midst of the dining car's gambling culture. This is one of several references to Russian literature in the novel, with Ray's friend later invoking the Russian tradition more broadly:

> "You know the English are big islanders, but the Russians are the biggest continentals. The greatest writers are Russian. It's a big new land. And their writers are like giants in the new. Gogol, Dostoevsky, Tolstoy, Chekhov, Turgenev" (015-041)

## The Novel's Significance

Published in serial form in *The Russian Messenger* (1866), *Crime and Punishment* follows Rodion Raskolnikov, a destitute former student in St. Petersburg who murders a pawnbroker, believing himself morally entitled to transgress conventional law for a higher purpose. The novel traces his psychological disintegration and eventual redemption through suffering and confession.

Dostoevsky's exploration of guilt, morality, and the divided self resonates with *Home to Harlem*'s own concerns — though McKay's novel operates in a very different register, both works examine what happens to a person after transgression, and both locate redemption in human connection rather than abstract principle.

## Themes

- **Guilt and Conscience**: Raskolnikov's psychological torment after the murder demonstrates Dostoevsky's belief that conscience operates independently of rational justification.
- **Poverty and Degradation**: The St. Petersburg of the novel, like McKay's Harlem, is a world shaped by economic desperation, where survival pressures test moral boundaries.
- **Redemption Through Suffering**: Dostoevsky's Christian existentialism holds that suffering, accepted rather than resisted, is the path to spiritual renewal.
