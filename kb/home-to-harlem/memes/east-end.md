The **East End** of [[london|London]] is a historically working-class district that serves as a key setting in the opening chapter of [[claude-mckay|Claude McKay]]'s 1928 novel *[[home-to-harlem|Home to Harlem]]*. It is where the protagonist [[jake-brown|Jake Brown]] lived and worked before his return to America.

## In the Novel

[[jake-brown|Jake]], the novel's protagonist, has been living in [[london-east-end|London's East End]], where he worked as a [[longshoreman]] and stoker. The opening chapter describes his experience with warmth and simplicity: "He liked the West India Docks. He liked Limehouse. In the pubs men gave him their friendly paws and called him 'darky.' He liked how they called him 'darky.' He made friends. He found a woman. He was happy in the East End." (001-024) On New Year's Eve, 1919, Jake celebrated at "a monster dance with his woman, and his docker friends and their women, in the Mile End Road." (001-025)

The post-war transformation of the East End is captured in a passage that also foreshadows Jake's departure:

> The Armistice had brought many more black men to the East End of London. Hundreds of them. Some of them found work. Some did not. Many were getting a little pension from the government. The price of sex went up in the East End, and the dignity of it also. And that summer Jake saw a big battle staged between the colored and white men of London's East End. Fisticuffs, razor and knife and gun play. For three days his woman would not let him out-of-doors. And when it was all over he was seized with the awful fever of lonesomeness. He felt all alone in the world. He wanted to run away from the kindheartedness of his lady of the East End.
>
> (001-026)

This passage — with its racial tensions, community bonds, and Jake's ultimate "fever of lonesomeness" — establishes the East End as a place of both belonging and limitation. The contrast between the grimy, working-class [[london|London]] district and the vibrant Black community of [[harlem|Harlem]] drives the novel's central theme: the magnetic pull of home.

## Historical Context

During and after [[world-war-i|World War I]], [[london-east-end|London's East End]] saw an influx of Black colonial workers, particularly seamen and dockworkers from the Caribbean and West [[africa|Africa]]. This migration created small but significant Black communities in areas like Stepney and Canning Town, forming the real-world backdrop to [[jake-brown|Jake]]'s fictional experience. The 1919 race riots that swept through British port cities — including London's East End — are echoed in McKay's description of "a big battle staged between the colored and white men."
