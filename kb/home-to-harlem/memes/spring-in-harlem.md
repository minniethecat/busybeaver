# Spring in Harlem

A single spring day in [[harlem|Harlem]] — from dandelion-dotted lots in the morning Bronx to the "black shouting chorus" of Sheba Palace at night — tracing [[jake-brown|Jake]]'s movement through a transformed city where nature, desire, violence, music, and unexpected reunion converge.

## Overview

> The lovely trees of Seventh Avenue were a vivid flame-green. Children, lightly clad, skipped on the pavement. Light open coats prevailed and the smooth bare throats of brown girls were a token as charming as the first pussy-willows. Far and high over all, the sky was a grand blue benediction, and beneath it the wonderful air of New York tasted like fine dry champagne.
> — (019-002)

Chapter XIX opens with one of the novel's most lyrical passages — the sensory explosion of spring in [[harlem|Harlem]]. The chapter that follows moves through the full spectrum of [[harlem|Harlem]] life in a single day: [[jake-brown|Jake]]'s solitary reverie among dandelions, a brutal street fight, an evening of music and drinking at [[aunt-hattie|Aunt Hattie]]'s, the raucous dancing at Sheba Palace, and an unexpected reunion with his "little brown" — the woman he has been searching for since the novel's opening.

## Context

### Historical Background

[[harlem|Harlem]] in the 1920s was a densely packed neighborhood whose population swelled dramatically during the [[great-migration|Great Migration]]. Spring, after the brutal [[new-york-city|New York]] winter, was a season of public display — promenading on [[seventh-avenue|Seventh Avenue]] in new clothes, open windows letting music spill into streets, and the resumption of outdoor social life. McKay's rendering of spring captures both the genuine beauty of the tree-lined avenues and the social theater of "[[black-belt|the Belt]]" in its finest season.

### In the Novel

"Spring in Harlem" (Chapter 19) is the novel's turning point from winter hardship toward resolution. [[jake-brown|Jake]] has been wandering — through [[the-railroad|railroad]] jobs, through [[pittsburgh|Pittsburgh]] and [[philadelphia|Philadelphia]] — and this day in [[harlem|Harlem]] brings him full circle. The chapter moves from solitary lyricism ([[jake-brown|Jake]] alone among the dandelions) through social violence (the [[yaller-prince|Yaller Prince]] fight) to communal [[pleasure-seeking|pleasure]] ([[aunt-hattie|Aunt Hattie]]'s, Sheba Palace) and finally to romantic reunion — the "little brown" he has been seeking since Chapter 1 appears at Sheba Palace.

## Key Participants

- [[jake-brown|Jake]] — protagonist, moves through the day with "lyrical feelings" (019-032); reunites with his lost love (019-105)
- [[billy-biasse|Billy Biasse]] — gambling-house operator and friend, witnesses the [[yaller-prince|Yaller Prince]] fight with [[jake-brown|Jake]]; "I always travel with mah gun ready" (019-027)
- [[yaller-prince|Yaller Prince]] — sweetback beaten in the street by Obadiah (019-009)(019-017)
- [[aunt-hattie|Aunt Hattie]] — host of the evening's gathering of [[longshoreman|longshoremen]] (019-041)
- [[felice]] — [[jake-brown|Jake]]'s "little brown," reunited at Sheba Palace (019-103)–(019-116)

## Details and Sequence

### Morning: Dandelions and Desire

[[jake-brown|Jake]] loiters along [[seventh-avenue|Seventh Avenue]] in the spring morning. The "lovely trees" are "a vivid flame-green" (019-002). He crosses to [[lenox-avenue|Lenox]], wanders into the Bronx, and sits on "a mound thick-covered with dandelions. They glittered in the sun away down to the rear of a rusty-gray shack. They filled all the green spaces" (019-004).

> Oh, the common little things were glorious there under the sun in the tender spring grass. Oh, sweet to be alive in that sun beneath that sky!
> — (019-004)

The dandelion field triggers a cascade of erotic memory: "his curious pure nectarine be[[gin-whisky|gin]]nings" in [[virginia|Virginia]], "rude contacts and swift satisfactions in Norfolk, Baltimore, and other coast ports" (019-005). He thinks of [[the-baltimore-cabaret|the Baltimore]] girl: "Only that cute heartbreaking brown of [[the-baltimore-cabaret|the Baltimore]]... A day like this sure feels like her. Didn't even get her name" (019-006).

### Afternoon: Street Violence

[[jake-brown|Jake]] and [[billy-biasse|Billy Biasse]], leaving a poolroom, encounter a street fight at [[fifth-avenue|Fifth Avenue]] and 133rd Street — a "yellow youth, a chocolate youth, and a brown girl" sta[[gin-whisky|gin]]g "a rough play" (019-007). The girl, [[yaller-prince|Yaller Prince]]'s former lover, chants over and over: "Bleed his mouf!" (019-014). Obadiah downs [[yaller-prince|Yaller Prince]] with a bottle: "blood bubbled from his nose and his mouth" (019-017).

[[billy-biasse|Billy]] observes: "He's plumb crazy gwine around without a gun when he's a-playing that theah game, with all these cut-thwoat niggers in [[harlem|Harlem]] ready to carve up one another foh a li'l insisnificant humpy" (019-025). [[jake-brown|Jake]] responds with his characteristic transatlantic perspective: "It's the same ole life everywhere. In white man's town or nigger town. Same bloody-sweet life across the pond" (019-026).

[[billy-biasse|Billy]] offers [[jake-brown|Jake]] a gun — "Youse a punk customer, then, I tell you" — and [[jake-brown|Jake]] reluctantly accepts: "Guess I'll take the li'l dawg offn [[billy-biasse|Billy]], all right" (019-031)(019-032).

### Evening: Music and Feasting at Aunt Hattie's

The spring afternoon gives way to twilight: "Twilight was enveloping [[black-belt|the Belt]], mer[[gin-whisky|gin]]g its life into a soft blue-black symphony" (019-040). [[jake-brown|Jake]] goes to [[aunt-hattie|Aunt Hattie]]'s where he finds [[billy-biasse|Billy]] and a gang of [[longshoreman|longshoremen]] "swapping love stories and sin[[gin-whisky|gin]]g" (019-041). They sing "Back home in Dixie is a brown gal there" (019-043)–(019-050). A [[west-india|West Indian]] volunteer sings a Port-of-Spain lament — "Ring the bell again, / But the sharks won't puke him up" (019-053)(019-056) — "curious, like so many Negro songs of its kind, for the strange strengthening of its wistful melody by a happy rhythm that was suitable for dancing" (019-068).

### Night: Sheba Palace and Reunion

[[jake-brown|Jake]] declares: "I sure don't feel lak spending a lonesome night this heah mahvelous night" (019-070). He and [[billy-biasse|Billy]] head to Sheba Palace, "an immense hall that was entirely monopolized for the amusements of the common workaday Negroes of [[black-belt|the Belt]]" (019-080). The hall is "garish gold" with "screaming green" tables (019-081). Couples guzzle [[gin-whisky|gin]], sip crème de menthe, and lounge on green benches loving "in every way but" (019-081).

The band plays a new song: "Brown gal crying on the corner, / Yaller gal done stole her candy" (019-085)(019-086). The yellow in the music "must have stood out in their ima[[gin-whisky|gin]]ation like a challenge, conveying a sense of that primitive, ancient, eternal, inexplicable antagonism in the [[race-and-color|color]] taboo of sex and society" (019-093).

> "Tell me, pa-pa, Ise you' ma-ma." The black players grinned and swayed and let the music go with all their might.
> — (019-093)

[[jake-brown|Jake]] is dancing with a "tall, shapely quadroon girl" when he glances up at the balcony and sees "the little brown that he had entirely given over as lost" (019-103). He drops his partner mid-slide, bounds up the stairs: "Hello, sweetness! What youse doing here?" (019-105). The girl jumps up, flustered — she is with someone else, "a bad actor" — and hurries [[jake-brown|Jake]] out of Sheba Palace before her companion returns from the lavatory (019-109).

Outside, under the spring night, she takes [[jake-brown|Jake]]'s arm and declares: "There ain't no [[high-yaller|yaller]] gal gwine get mah honey daddy thisanight" (019-114). [[jake-brown|Jake]] answers: "Aw no, sweetness. I was dog[[gin-whisky|gin]]g it with one and jest drops her flat when I seen you" (019-115).

> "And there ain't no nigger in the wul' I wouldn't ditch foh you, daddy. O Lawdy! How Ise been crazy longing to meet you again."
> — (019-116)

## Aftermath and Significance

The spring day unites all the novel's registers — natural beauty, urban violence, communal music, and erotic pursuit — into a single diurnal arc. [[jake-brown|Jake]]'s movement through [[harlem|Harlem]] t[[race-and-color|race]]s the geography of Black working-class [[pleasure-seeking|pleasure]]: from the natural respite of the dandelion field to the commercial spectacle of Sheba Palace.

The reunion with [[felice]] (the "little brown") is the novel's emotional climax in miniature. [[jake-brown|Jake]] has searched for her since Chapter 1, and their meeting at Sheba Palace — impulsive, passionate, threatening violence — is spring's promise fulfilled: desire renewed, the city alive, the search rewarded. That she has to drag him out past her "bad actor" companion only sharpens the sense that spring's beauty in [[harlem|Harlem]] is always shadowed by the violence [[jake-brown|Jake]] and [[billy-biasse|Billy]] witnessed earlier in the day.

The chapter is also the novel's most concentrated treatment of [[race-and-color|color]] consciousness within Black social life. The Sheba Palace song ("Yaller gal can't make you fall") and the "handful of yellow dancers" who are "even more abandoned to the spirit of the song" (019-093) dramatize the internal [[race-and-color|color]] politics that the novel refuses to simplify.
