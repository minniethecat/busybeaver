# Ragtime

A syncopated musical [[fashion-and-style|style]], primarily for piano, that emerged in the 1890s and dominated American popular music through the 1910s. By the 1920s setting of *Home to [[harlem|Harlem]]*, ragtime coexisted with [[blues]] and [[jazz]] as part of [[harlem|Harlem]]'s dense musical landscape, representing an earlier layer of Black musical innovation.

## Definition

Ragtime is characterized by a syncopated (or "ragged") melody played against a steady, march-like bass line. Its classic form was the piano rag — compositions by Scott Joplin, James Scott, and Joseph Lamb that elevated popular music to formal sophistication. By the 1920s, ragtime had evolved into stride piano and merged with [[blues]] elements to help form early [[jazz]], but pure ragtime pieces were still performed alongside newer [[fashion-and-style|style]]s. (002-035)

## Cultural Context

### Harlem Renaissance Context

Ragtime was the first [[africa|Africa]]n American musical form to achieve national and international popularity, paving the way for [[blues|the blues]] and [[jazz]] crazes that followed. While some [[harlem|Harlem]] Renaissance intellectuals viewed ragtime as "low" culture, its commercial success demonstrated that Black music could dominate the American marketplace — a fact that fueled Renaissance-era debates about cultural authenticity versus commercialization.

Ragtime's association with the pre-war era gave it a nostalgic quality in 1920s [[harlem|Harlem]]. It was the music of an earlier generation, but still played alongside the newer [[blues]] and [[jazz]], cr[[food-and-dining|eating]] a layered musical environment where different eras of Black musical innovation coexisted.

### In the Novel

McKay mentions ragtime twice, each time in conjunction with [[blues|the blues]]. The narrator describes how "all night long, ragtime and '[[blues]]' playing somewhere, sin[[gin-whisky|gin]]g somewhere, dancing somewhere" (002-035), pairing the two forms as the inseparable soundtrack of [[harlem|Harlem]] nightlife.

The novel's most extended musical passage features "the greatest ragtime song of the day" (008-013), performed at [[madame-suarez-buffet-flat|Madame Suarez's]] by a girl "curiously made up in mauve." McKay notes that "Broadway was wild about it and [[harlem|Harlem]] was crazy. All America [[jazz]]ed to it, and it was already world-famous" — capturing how Black music traveled from [[harlem|Harlem]] outward to conquer global popular culture.

## Key Passages

> And all night long, ragtime and "blues" playing somewhere, singing somewhere, dancing somewhere, oh, the singing and the dancing of the Black Belt! (002-035)

The pairing of "ragtime and '[[blues]]'" as the diptych of [[harlem|Harlem]] music reflects the actual historical overlap of these forms in the early 1920s. The exclamatory "oh" and the exclamation mark convey the narrator's immersion in — and celebration of — this musical abundance.

> At the piano a girl curiously made up in mauve was rendering the greatest ragtime song of the day. Broadway was wild about it and Harlem was crazy. All America jazzed to it, and it was already world-famous. (008-013)

McKay's use of "[[jazz]]ed" as a verb for how America received a ragtime song blurs the boundary between the two genres — historically accurate, since the transition from ragtime to [[jazz]] was gradual and the terms were sometimes used interchangeably in the 1920s.

## Historical Significance

Ragtime was a revolutionary musical form that fundamentally changed American popular music. Scott Joplin's "Maple Leaf Rag" (1899) sold over a million copies of sheet music, demonstrating the commercial potential of Black music and establishing the syncopated rhythm as a defining feature of American popular song. Ragtime's piano-based solo performance tradition fed directly into [[harlem|Harlem]] stride piano (James P. Johnson, Fats Waller, Willie "The Lion" Smith), which in turn fed into big band [[jazz]]. The form's international popularity — it was played in "Paris and Cairo, Shanghai, Honolulu, and Java" — anticipated [[jazz]]'s global conquest and established the pattern of [[africa|Africa]]n American music becoming America's most successful cultural export.
