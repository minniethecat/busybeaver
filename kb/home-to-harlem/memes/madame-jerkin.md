# Madame Jerkin

Madame Jerkin is a figure invoked but never directly encountered in [[claude-mckay|Claude McKay]]'s *[[Home to Harlem]]* (1928). She is named once — in an exclamation of alarm and admiration — as the proprietress of "the niftiest buffet flat in Harlem" (005-051). Like a rumor of the best party in town, she exists in the novel as reputation rather than presence: the gold standard of Harlem's informal nightlife, the benchmark against which other buffet flats are measured.

## Role in the Novel

Madame Jerkin appears only through reported speech, in a passage where news of a police raid circulates through the social network of Harlem's nightlife. The speaker delivers the news with a mixture of outrage and connoisseurship:

> "And last week they done raided Madame Jerkin's, the niftiest buffet flat in Harlem. O Lawdy!" (005-051)

The exclamation — "O Lawdy!" — registers both dismay at the raid and admiration for the establishment. The speaker is someone who knows the hierarchy of Harlem's buffet flats and can rank them with authority. Madame Jerkin's, in this ranking, is at the top: "the niftiest."

The fact that she is named and ranked suggests a recognized figure — someone whose operation is known in the community, whose quality is a matter of consensus. The raid on her establishment is news precisely because it is the best: if the police are hitting Madame Jerkin's, nowhere is safe.

## Characterization

McKay characterizes Madame Jerkin with remarkable economy — a single sentence, a single citation. And yet the characterization is full. She is a "Madame" — part of the sorority of women who operated [[buffet-flat|buffet flats]] in [[harlem|Harlem]] and whose honorific titles marked them as proprietors, hostesses, and entrepreneurs rather than simply landladies. She is associated with the superlative — "niftiest" — which a speaker who knows the scene delivers as established fact. And she is named in the context of a raid, which places her inside the legal precarity that all buffet flat operators navigated.

Unlike [[madame-laura|Madame Laura]], who appears onstage and hosts a fully rendered party scene, or [[madame-suarez|Madame Suarez]], whose buffet flat is described in some detail, Madame Jerkin remains offstage — a legend, a reputation, the proprietor of a place the reader never enters. This absence is itself a form of characterization: the best buffet flat is always the one you haven't been to yet.

## The Buffet Flat Economy

Madame Jerkin's establishment belongs to the ecosystem of [[harlem|Harlem]] buffet flats — private apartments converted into informal social clubs where patrons could drink, dance, gamble, and find sexual company during [[prohibition|Prohibition]]. These operations existed in a legal gray zone, subject to periodic police raids, and were typically run by women who converted domestic space into commercial enterprise.

The hierarchy implied by "niftiest" suggests a stratified market. At the lower end were rough joints — a few bottles of gin and a phonograph. At the upper end were establishments like Madame Jerkin's: presumably well-appointed, well-stocked, well-managed, and well-connected enough to operate with some confidence until the law intervened. The [[buffet-flat|buffet flat]] economy was one of the few entrepreneurial avenues available to Black women in 1920s Harlem, and the "Madames" who ran the best flats — Jerkin, [[madame-suarez|Suarez]], [[madame-laura|Laura]], [[madame-mulberry|Mulberry]] — were effective businesswomen operating in a hostile regulatory environment.

## Key Passage

> "And last week they done raided Madame Jerkin's, the niftiest buffet flat in Harlem. O Lawdy!" (005-051)

## Themes

Madame Jerkin represents the **reputation economy** of Harlem nightlife. She is known by quality, not by description; her name circulates as a standard. The exclamation that names her is an act of connoisseurship — the speaker demonstrates knowledge of the scene by knowing who is best — and the news of the raid is gossip that binds the community of patrons and operators together.

She also embodies the **precarity of Black women's entrepreneurship** under [[prohibition|Prohibition]]. However "nifty" her establishment, a police raid could shut it down. The "Madames" of Harlem operated businesses that were illegal by statute but licit by community consensus, and their survival depended on skill, reputation, and the careful management of relationships with police, patrons, and neighbors.
