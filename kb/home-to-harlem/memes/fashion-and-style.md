# Fashion and Style

Fashion and style in [[claude-mckay|Claude McKay]]'s *Home to [[harlem|Harlem]]* are never superficial. Dress is self-making: a visible declaration of status, taste, and aspiration in a world where Black people are constantly judged by their appearance. McKay's characters invest heavily in clothing — suits, silk shirts, patent-leather shoes — as both personal [[pleasure-seeking|pleasure]] and social strategy.

## Definition

In the novel's world, "fashion" encompasses both the material culture of clothing (fabrics, [[race-and-color|color]]s, accessories) and the performative practice of "style" — the way a man carries himself in a suit, the way a woman's dress signals her desirability. The key aesthetic is the "nigger-brown" palette: a rich, dark brown suit [[race-and-color|color]] that McKay names repeatedly across the novel, claiming a racialized term for sartorial elegance. (007-086)(013-036)(019-033)

## Cultural Context

### Harlem Renaissance Context

The 1920s [[harlem|Harlem]] "[[the-sporting-world|sporting world]]" was famously style-conscious. [[dandy|Dandy]]ism — the practice of meticulous, often extravagant male dress — had roots in both European aesthetic tradition and [[africa|Africa]]n American vernacular culture. For Black men, a fine suit was more than vanity: it countered racist assumptions of inferiority, asserted economic competence, and attracted women. The "New Negro" of [[harlem-renaissance|the Renaissance]] was as often defined by tailored clothes as by literary output.

### In the Novel

[[jake-brown|Jake]]'s suits are central to his identity. When he first appears in [[harlem|Harlem]], he is "wearing a steel-gray English suit. It fitted him loosely and well" (002-006). The suit's English ori[[gin-whisky|gin]] — acquired in [[london|London]], where [[jake-brown|Jake]] worked during the war — marks his cosmopolitan experience and distinguishes him from the Southern [[migration|migrants]] in lesser dress.

The novel's most distinctive fashion motif is the "nigger-brown" suit. McKay uses the phrase eight times: [[zeddy-plummer|Zeddy]] wears "an elegant nigger-brown sports suit and patent-leather shoes" (007-086); a dandy at a [[buffet-flat|buffet flat]] is "well dressed in nigger-brown" wearing "an expensive diamond" (013-036); [[jake-brown|Jake]] himself appears "along [[seventh-avenue|Seventh Avenue]] in a new nigger-brown suit" (019-033). The repetition forms a pattern: nigger-brown is the uniform of the aspiring Black man, reclaimed from slur into style.

Women's fashion is equally emphasized. [[rose-congo|Rose]] is introduced through her "fine champagne-[[race-and-color|color]]ed stockings" (002-006). Silk — "two fancy-[[race-and-color|color]]ed silk shirts, silk handkerchiefs" (007-163), "three expensive striped silk shirts" (017-050), "one unruly lemonbright silk stocking" (020-055) — recurs as a marker of luxury and sensuality. The fabrics McKay names are never neutral: silk, fur, patent leather all signal expenditure, aspiration, and the [[pleasure-seeking|pleasure]] of the body.

Fashion is also a class marker. [[ray|Ray]], the Haitian intellectual, notices style precisely: he observes the "extravagantly-dressed dark dandy" (007-051) and the "well dressed in nigger-brown" man (013-036) with the ethnographic eye of someone analyzing a culture he is both inside and outside of. The dandy represents a form of Black masculinity that competes with [[ray|Ray]]'s intellectual self-presentation.

Clothing serves erotic and social functions simultaneously. [[rose-congo|Rose]] "fondled his suit in admiration" when she first meets [[jake-brown|Jake]] (004-084); [[agatha|Agatha]]'s "plain nigger-brown coat" is "unbuttoned" (014-030), its plainness and openness together signaling availability; the "bright-hued clothes" (006-007) of [[harlem|Harlem]] women blaze against the gray city.

## Variations & Related Terms

- **Nigger-brown** — The novel's signature aesthetic: a rich brown suit [[race-and-color|color]], worn by [[jake-brown|Jake]], [[zeddy-plummer|Zeddy]], and anonymous dandies (007-086)(013-036)(019-033)
- **Steel-gray English suit** — [[jake-brown|Jake]]'s cosmopolitan suit, acquired abroad: "wearing a steel-gray English suit. It fitted him loosely and well" (002-006)
- **Silk** — Luxury fabric associated with both male dandies and female desirability: "silk shirts, silk handkerchiefs" (007-163)
- **Patent-leather shoes** — Marker of the dandy: "patent-leather shoes with big shiny buckles" (007-086)
- **[[dandy|Dandy]] / flashily-dressed** — The masculine performance of sartorial ambition: "men, clean-shaven, flashily-dressed" (008-012)
- **Sporting world aesthetic** — The broader style culture of [[harlem|Harlem]]'s gamblers, [[the-railroad|railroad]] men, and nightlife figures

## Key Passages

> He was wearing a steel-gray English suit. It fitted him loosely and well. (002-006)

[[jake-brown|Jake]]'s first appearance in [[harlem|Harlem]] emphasizes his clothing before any other characteristic. The suit — English, well-fitting — establishes him as worldly, attractive, and distinct from the mass of returning soldiers.

> He wore an elegant nigger-brown sports suit and patent-leather shoes with big shiny buckles. (007-086)

[[zeddy-plummer|Zeddy]] fully costumed, the "nigger-brown" suit and patent-leather shoes forming a complete look. The terms "elegant" and "sports" together capture the dandy ethic: elegance through conspicuous leisure-wear.

> Jake loved women's pretty clothes. The plain nigger-brown coat Agatha wore, unbuttoned, showed her beautiful coffee-colored breasts. (014-030)

Fashion mediates desire: the plainness of [[agatha|Agatha]]'s coat contrasts with the sensuality of what it reveals. [[jake-brown|Jake]]'s appreciation of women's clothing is both aesthetic and erotic.

## Historical Significance

Black dandyism has a long history dating to the 19th century, when free Black men used dress to assert citizenship and dignity against slavery's dehumanization. The tradition intensified during the [[harlem|Harlem]] Renaissance, when the "[[the-sporting-world|sporting world]]" — gamblers, entertainers, [[the-railroad|railroad]] men — developed a flamboyant sartorial code that influenced broader American fashion. Zoot suits of the 1930s–40s are a direct descendant of the nigger-brown aesthetic McKay documents. The novel's eight repetitions of "nigger-brown" constitute a deliberate aesthetic intervention: McKay takes a racial slur and makes it the name of a [[race-and-color|color]] of elegance.
