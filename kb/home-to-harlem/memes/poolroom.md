A key social and recreational institution in [[claude-mckay|Claude McKay]]'s *Home to [[harlem|Harlem]]*, the poolroom serves as a gathering place for Black working-class men, a venue for gambling and camaraderie, and a marker of [[harlem|Harlem]]'s street-level social geography.

## Definition

In the novel's world, the poolroom is a commercial establishment centered on billiards but functioning primarily as a male social club, gambling den, and informal community hub. It is distinct from the saloon (which centers on drinking), the [[cabaret-culture|cabaret]] (which centers on music and dancing), and the [[speakeasy]] (which centers on illicit [[gin-whisky|alcohol]] during [[prohibition|Prohibition]]). The poolroom offers billiards and card games, attracting [[longshoreman|longshoremen]], railroad men, waiters, and other working-class Black men (003-027)(005-004).

## Cultural Context

### Harlem Renaissance Context

Poolrooms were ubiquitous in 1920s [[harlem|Harlem]], lining [[lenox-avenue|Lenox Avenue]] and [[fifth-avenue|Fifth Avenue]] alongside saloons, [[cabaret-culture|cabarets]], and basement restaurants. They occupied a specific niche in the neighborhood's social ecology: less respectable than a church or lodge hall, but more structured than street-corner loitering. For men who worked irregular jobs on the docks and railroads, the poolroom provided a consistent social anchor (009-023)(011-003).

### In the Novel

McKay uses the poolroom as a recurring setting that defines character relationships and social status. [[dixie-red|Dixie Red]]'s poolroom is the novel's primary poolroom, appearing across multiple chapters as a key node in [[jake-brown|Jake]]'s [[harlem|Harlem]] geography. The poolroom sits between [[rose-congo|Rose]]'s place and [[uncle-doc|Uncle Doc]]'s saloon in the triangle of [[jake-brown|Jake]]'s social world: "between [[rose-congo|Rose]] and [[uncle-doc|Uncle Doc]]'s saloon and [[dixie-red|Dixie Red]]'s poolroom" (007-071). Characters negotiate masculinity, reputation, and community belonging within its walls.

## Variations and Related Terms

- **Poolroom vs. [[saloon|Saloon]]** — The saloon centers on drinking and sometimes food ([[uncle-doc|Uncle Doc]]'s saloon serves free lunch); the poolroom centers on games (billiards, poker, craps).
- **Poolroom vs. Gambling House** — [[billy-biasse|Billy Biasse]]'s establishment is described as a "gambling house" (018-014), a more explicitly illicit operation; the poolroom includes gambling but is not defined solely by it.
- **Poolroom vs. Cabaret** — The [[cabaret-culture|cabaret]] (like [[the-baltimore-cabaret|the Baltimore]]) is about music, dancing, and mixed-gender socializing; the poolroom is predominantly male.

## Key Passages

> At Dixie Red's poolroom that evening there were some of the longshoremen who had fought in the recent strike. (005-022)

## Historical Significance

The poolroom in Black [[harlem|Harlem]] was part of a broader American urban working-class culture. Pool halls had been popular since the late nineteenth century, but in segregated Black neighborhoods they took on additional significance as one of the few commercial leisure spaces where Black men could gather without white scrutiny. The poolroom economy — gambling, informal credit, reputation-based status — operated parallel to and sometimes against the values of respectability promoted by the Black middle class and the church.
