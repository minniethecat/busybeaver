**[[the-baltimore-cabaret|The Baltimore]]** is a [[cabaret-culture|cabaret]] and nightclub in [[harlem|Harlem]] that appears throughout [[claude-mckay|Claude McKay]]'s *Home to [[harlem|Harlem]]*. It is one of the novel's most frequently mentioned locations, functioning as a key site of nightlife, romance, racial performance, and police violence.

## Location

### In the Novel's World

[[the-baltimore-cabaret|The Baltimore]] is a [[harlem|Harlem]] [[cabaret-culture|cabaret]] — a [[speakeasy]]-style establishment where patrons drink, dance, and socialize. It is managed by a "boss" who is ultimately indicted as "accessory to the [[speakeasy]] law." (008-038) The [[cabaret-culture|cabaret]] is "packed with happy, grinning darkies" on a typical night (021-120), and its "blazing lights" form a beacon of [[harlem|Harlem]] nightlife until they are "put out" during the raid. (008-001)(008-002)

## Significance to the Narrative

[[the-baltimore-cabaret|The Baltimore]] is where [[jake-brown|Jake]] first encounters the "little lost maroon-brown" woman who becomes his romantic obsession. (005-002) He "went frequently to [[the-baltimore-cabaret|the Baltimore]]" looking for her, and the [[cabaret-culture|cabaret]] becomes entangled with his longing. (005-002) The "little brown of [[the-baltimore-cabaret|the Baltimore]]" is described as a recurring preoccupation, and her absence haunts him: "No sign of his honey girl anywhere." (004-008)

The [[cabaret-culture|cabaret]] is also the site of chapter 8's central event — the raid by police — described in the chapter titled "The [[raid-of-the-baltimore|Raid of the Baltimore]]." (008-001) The raid occurs when "plainclothesmen" and officers shut down the establishment, though "the police had not, on a certain recent morning, made a haul of importance." (008-003)

## The Raid of the Baltimore

Chapter 8 depicts the police raid in detail. Plainclothesmen had been spending money "like water at [[the-baltimore-cabaret|the Baltimore]]" to gain entry and build a case. (008-033) The "wary boss of [[the-baltimore-cabaret|the Baltimore]]" was convinced they were "fine fellows" (008-034) — a fatal error of judgment that leads to his indictment. The raid extinguishes the [[cabaret-culture|cabaret]]'s lights and renders the venue "padlocked." (021-116) Yet by the novel's end, the padlock is gone and "[[the-baltimore-cabaret|the Baltimore]] was packed" once more. (021-120)

## From the Novel

A character's longing for the Baltimore captures the cabaret's hold on Harlem's nightlife:

> Feet in mah hands! Take me back to the Baltimore tonight. I ain't gwine to know no peace till I lay these here hands on mah tantalizing brown again.
>
> (003-050)

## Historical Context

[[the-baltimore-cabaret|The Baltimore]] represents the [[prohibition|Prohibition]]-era [[cabaret-culture|cabaret culture]] of [[harlem|Harlem]], where [[speakeasy|speakeasies]] operated under constant threat of police raids. These venues were central to [[harlem|Harlem]]'s social and economic life, providing entertainment and community for the neighborhood's working class while existing in a legal gray zone that exposed both proprietors and patrons to police violence and legal jeopardy.

## Appearances

[[the-baltimore-cabaret|The Baltimore]] appears or is referenced in chapters 3–5, 8–9, 13–14, and 21, making it one of the novel's most frequently recurring locations. (003-050)(004-008)(005-002)(008-001)–(008-038)(009-008)(013-012)(014-021)(014-042)(021-115)–(021-130)
