# Waiter

The waiter is a recurring occupational role in [[claude-mckay|Claude McKay]]'s *Home to [[harlem|Harlem]]* — most importantly embodied by protagonist [[jake-brown|Jake]], who works as a [[dining-car]] waiter on the [[pennsylvania-railroad|Pennsylvania Railroad]] during the novel's extended [[the-railroad|railroad]] sequence (Chapters X–XII). The occupation serves both as a realistic depiction of Black working-class employment in the 1920s and as the narrative vehicle for the novel's most significant collective labor action: the treeing of [[the-chef|the chef]].

## Definition

In the novel's world, a waiter is a Black man employed in [[food-and-dining|food]] service — either on [[dining-car|railroad dining car]]s, in restaurants, or in [[harlem|Harlem]]'s [[cabaret|cabarets]] and clubs. The occupation carried specific class connotations: waiters occupied a middling position in [[harlem|Harlem]]'s social hierarchy, earning steady wages superior to day laborers but never approaching the wealth of the [[harlem|Harlem]] sporting class of gamblers and club owners.

Waiters in the novel are "tight-faced, anxious-eyed" (005-035), laboring under white supervision and sometimes under tyrannical Black bosses like [[the-chef|the chef]], who "glared at the waiters" with "his tough, aproned front" (010-003).

## Cultural Context

### Labor in 1920s Harlem

The [[pullman-company|Pullman Company]] had pioneered the practice of hiring Black men as [[the-railroad|railroad]] porters, waiters, and cooks — deliberately excluding white workers from these service roles while paying Black employees significantly less. By the 1920s, [[the-railroad|railroad]] work was one of the few stable sources of employment available to Black men, and the dining-car waiter occupied a recognizable place in Black working-class life. The novel's [[the-railroad|railroad]] chapters draw on [[claude-mckay|McKay]]'s own experience working as a dining-car waiter on the [[pennsylvania-railroad|Pennsylvania Railroad]].

The existence of "club waiters who gambled for big stakes" (005-031) reflects the economic aspirations within the occupation — some waiters accumulated significant cash through tips and side earnings, enabling them to participate in [[harlem|Harlem]]'s [[gambling-culture|gambling culture]].

### In the Novel

[[jake-brown|Jake]]'s tenure as a waiter on the [[pennsylvania-railroad|Pennsylvania Railroad]] occupies Chapters X through XII and forms the novel's central labor narrative. He joins a crew of waiters who serve passengers while navigating the tyranny of [[the-chef|the chef]], "the most hated chef in the [[dining-car|dining-car service]]" (012-002). The waiters' collective action — "treeing" [[the-chef|the chef]] — represents the novel's most fully realized depiction of working-class solidarity.

The waiters operate in a world governed by tips and tips-giving: "He repaid the waiter and said: 'Youse a good sport'" (010-016). Economic transactions between waiters and customers establish brief solidarities and resentments alike. One waiter is described reading the social scene "between" customers (010-018), demonstrating the interpersonal intelligence required of the role.

## Historical Significance

The occupation of dining-car waiter intersected with the labor organizing drives that culminated in the 1925 founding of the Brotherhood of Sleeping Car Porters under [[asa-philip-randolph|A. Philip Randolph]]. While McKay wrote *[[home-to-harlem|Home to Harlem]]* contemporaneously with these organizing efforts, the novel avoids depicting formal union activity, instead portraying workplace solidarity as spontaneous and collective — a form of resistance rooted in shared experience rather than institutional politics.

The waiter's position as a Black worker within white-owned industries — the [[pennsylvania-railroad|Pennsylvania Railroad]], the club, the [[cabaret-culture|cabaret]] — made it a site of intersecting racial and class tensions. The dining-car waiter was simultaneously an economic necessity for white travelers and a target of their contempt, a dynamic that McKay captures through [[the-chef|the chef]]'s intermediary position as a Black boss who brutalizes Black workers on behalf of a white corporate structure.
