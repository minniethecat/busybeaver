# Treeing of the Chef

The growing revolt of the [[dining-car]] waiters against their abusive chef, culminating in a cunning act of sabotage — the "treeing" — that gets the chef demoted and restores dignity to the crew. A dramatic enactment of worker solidarity among Black railroad men in Jim Crow America.

## Overview

> "The chef is up a tree, all right," said Ray to the pantryman.
> "And he'll break his black hide getting down," the pantryman replied, bitterly.
> — (012-096)(012-097)

The chef of the dining-car is the most hated chef in the railroad service — a "great black bundle of consciously suppressed desires" who rules his kitchen through intimidation, insults, and petty tyranny (012-002)(012-004). The waiters and undercooks endure his abuse until the pantryman (first [[waiter]]), nursing a deep personal grudge over being called "bastard-begotten dime-snatcher" (012-035), orchestrates the chef's downfall. The climax comes when the pantryman, aided by the chef's momentary distraction with a yellow girl in the railroad yards, hides the basket of eggs — causing the chef to lose his composure before the steward and ultimately his position.

## Context

### Historical Background

Dining-car service on American railroads was one of the few skilled occupations open to Black men during the early twentieth century. Chefs, waiters, and cooks occupied a rigid hierarchy — the chef held near-absolute authority over the kitchen, second only to the white steward (012-004). The Pullman Company and other railroad employers cultivated this internal stratification, knowing that a divided Black workforce was easier to control.

### In the Novel

The "treeing" occurs during [[jake-brown|Jake]]'s stint as a third cook on the railroad (Chapters 10–12), part of his restless movement through the Black working-class economy after returning from Europe. It is here that Jake meets [[ray|Ray]], the Haitian intellectual working as a waiter, whose presence catalyzes both Jake's thinking and the waiters' collective resistance (012-010). The chapter is the novel's most sustained treatment of labor conflict and workplace solidarity among Black men.

## Key Participants

- [[jake-brown|Jake]] — third cook, silent ally of the waiters (012-009); "I ain't standing in with him in no low-down stuff" (012-059)
- [[ray|Ray]] — waiter, provides strategic counsel and moral witness; coins the phrase "treeing of the chef" (012-096)
- the chef — unnamed tyrant of the kitchen, a "perfect brutal machine" (012-037), eventually demoted (012-110)
- the pantryman (first waiter) — "illegitimate" son of a white man, nursing a cancer of resentment; architect of the chef's downfall (012-035)(012-087)
- the fourth waiter — timid Georgia boy nicknamed "the Mule"; his abuse by the chef sparks the open rebellion (012-016)(012-019)
- the steward (Sah Farrel) — Irish steward caught between kitchen and pantry (012-047)
- the second cook — sycophant waiting to take the chef's job (012-009)

## Details and Sequence

The conflict builds over multiple trips. The chef's defining cruelty is calling the fourth waiter "Mule" — a nickname the timid Georgia boy loathes (012-016). Encouraged by [[ray|Ray]], the fourth waiter finally stands his ground in a confrontation over ice, calling the chef a "dirty rhinoceros" (012-025). [[ray|Ray]] explains: "Why, chef, don't you know? That's the ugliest animal in all Africa" (012-027).

The chef escalates. He threatens to make the waiters "eat mah spittle" and boasts he has already done so (012-050). The pantryman alerts the steward, but the chef deflects the accusation (012-053). A tense standoff persists: "The coldness between the kitchen and the pantry continued, unpleasantly nasty, like the wearing of wet clothes, after the fall of a heavy shower, when the sun is shining again" (012-076).

> The coldness between the kitchen and the pantry continued, unpleasantly nasty, like the wearing of wet clothes, after the fall of a heavy shower, when the sun is shining again.
> — (012-076)

The chef tries to placate the pantryman with choice tidbits, but the pantryman refuses — "he started in hating back with profound African hate" (012-077). The pantryman bides his time, feigning cordiality while waiting for the "big moment" (012-081).

The moment arrives when both the second and fourth cooks fail to report for duty. The chef and pantryman fetch provisions together. Back at the yards, a yellow girl waves at the chef through the window and he steps outside to flirt with her. "In that moment the pantryman made a lightning-bolt move; and shut down the little glass door between the pantry and the kitchen" (012-087). The basket of eggs — personally carried by the chef — vanishes.

When the train is underway and the first dinner call comes, the chef discovers the eggs are gone. Pandemonium erupts. The steward is "radish-red" with fury (012-088). The chef, transformed from "rhinoceros" to "meek black lamb," can only moan: "O Lawd! and I ain't been outa the kitchen sence. Ain't no mortal hand could tuk them. Some evil hand" (012-091). His flirtation with the yellow girl is "completely wiped off his memory" (012-093).

The crisis peaks when the chef shies a ham bone at the pantryman, smashing a bottle of milk (012-101). When the steward intervenes, the chef threatens to "jump off" the train — a suicidal bluff that seals his fate (012-105)(012-107).

## Aftermath and Significance

> "Hit those fellahs in the pocketbook is the only way," the pantryman overheard the steward talking to one of his colleagues.
> — (012-111)

The chef is replaced on the next trip and demoted to second cook on another car (012-110). The steward's logic is pragmatic, not moral — threatening to jump off the train when short-staffed was the unforgivable offense, not the prior abuse of the crew.

Jake, suspecting the pantryman's role, presses him: "But it was sure peculiar, though, how them aiggs just fly outa that kitchen lak that way" (012-112). The pantryman deflects with a laugh: "Maybe they all hatched and growed wings when ole black bull was playing with that sweet yaller piece" (012-113). He refuses to confess outright: "What you' right hand does don't tell it to the left, says I" (012-115).

The "treeing" closes with Jake's hope for a new era on the car — "now that wese good and rid of him, I hope all we niggers will pull together like civilization folks" (012-118) — and the pantryman's ambiguous last word: "There ain't another down-home nigger like him in this white man's service. He was riding too high and fly, brother. I knew he would tumble and bust something nasty" (012-119).

The event dramatizes a central tension in the novel's treatment of labor: Black workers in a white-controlled industry must fight internal tyrants even as they remain subordinate to white stewards and railroad companies. The solidarity is real — Jake stays on the job because he likes Ray (012-010) — but it is achieved through sabotage, not collective bargaining. The chef, for all his cruelty, was himself a product of the system, a man who "did his duty as only a martinet can" (012-004) and who would rather "give them his money than a piece of the company's raw meat" (012-085).
