The **razor** in Claude McKay's *Home to Harlem* is more than a weapon — it is a recurring symbol of masculine violence, honor culture, and the precariousness of Black life in the novel's world. From the East End of London to the streets of Harlem, razors appear in fights between men, between rivals for women, and as instruments of retribution.

## London: The Razor's First Appearance

The razor is introduced early, in the novel's London opening, as part of the violent landscape of the East End:

> "…London's East End. Fisticuffs, razor and knife and gun play. For t…" (001-026)

This passage establishes the razor alongside fists, knives, and guns as part of the repertoire of masculine violence that crosses national boundaries — from London to Harlem, men settle disputes with blades.

## The Razor as Threat

In Harlem, razors appear in moments of confrontation and danger:

> "The razor flew spinning upward and fell…" (005-043)

This vivid image — a razor spinning through the air — captures the sudden, kinetic violence that can erupt in the novel's social spaces. The razor is a weapon of proximity, requiring close quarters and personal engagement.

## Razor Violence and Reputation

Characters in the novel carry reputations shaped by razor violence:

> "…been mixed up in more than one razor cut-up. He did have a nasty l…" (017-019)

A "razor cut-up" confers a certain notoriety — a mark of dangerousness that affects how other characters perceive and interact with the bearer.

## Razors and Women

Razors also appear in the context of relationships and jealousy:

> "…to each other's flesh and men razored and gunned at each other ov…" (020-041)

This passage links razor violence to sexual competition — men razoring and gunning "at each other over" women, the blade becoming an extension of masculine possessiveness and rage.

## Cultural Context

The straight razor as a weapon has a complex history in African American culture. In the early twentieth century, when gun ownership was often restricted for Black men, the razor — a common personal item — became an accessible weapon. Its prominence in the novel reflects both material reality and its symbolic resonance as an instrument of last-resort violence in a society that offered Black men limited legal recourse for disputes.
