**Madame Walker** is a character in [[claude-mckay|Claude McKay]]'s 1928 novel *[[home-to-harlem|Home to Harlem]]*. She is a [[harlem|Harlem]] salon proprietor known for her wealth and her distinctive absinthe-[[race-and-color|color]]ed establishment.

## In the Novel

Madame Walker operates a salon in [[harlem|Harlem]] with notable décor described as "plastered and glossy under Madame Walker's absinthe-[[race-and-color|color]]ed salon" (007-088). Her name appears in connection with [[harlem|Harlem]]'s social world: a character boasts of having danced with someone "at [[madame-mulberry|Madame Mulberry]]'s apartment" (006-049), situating Walker within the network of female entrepreneurs who ran [[harlem|Harlem]]'s [[buffet-flat|buffet flats]], [[cabaret-culture|cabarets]], and social gathering spaces.

Her wealth is proverbial among the novel's characters. One character declares, "if I was as rich as [[jack-johnson|Jack Johnson]] and rich as Madame Walker I'd prefer to have you" (018-049), placing her affluence alongside that of the famous boxer [[jack-johnson|Jack Johnson]]. This comparison marks Madame Walker as a figure of notable economic success within the Black community of 1920s [[harlem|Harlem]].

## Cultural Significance

Madame Walker represents the class of Black women entrepreneurs who built businesses and social institutions in [[harlem|Harlem]] during [[harlem-renaissance|the Renaissance]] era. Alongside figures like [[madame-laura|Madame Laura]] and [[madame-mulberry|Madame Mulberry]], she embodies the economic agency and social influence of women within [[harlem|Harlem]]'s nightlife and entertainment economy.
