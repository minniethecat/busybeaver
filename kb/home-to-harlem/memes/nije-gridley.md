# Nije Gridley

The [[longshoreman]] broker in *Home to [[harlem|Harlem]]* — a tall-tale-telling intermediary who connects dock workers to jobs and takes a cut of their wages. Nije Gridley represents the informal labor economy that sustained Black workers excluded from formal employment structures.

## Role in the Novel

Nije Gridley is introduced as "the [[longshoreman]] broker" from whom [[jake-brown|Jake]] "had borrowed" money (005-005). His role as broker — neither employer nor worker but an intermediary who controls access to work — makes him a figure of ambiguous power in the [[longshoreman|longshoremen]]'s world. He is described as "a tall, lanky, light-brown man with a long, thin, humorous face," whose stories and jokes lubricate the business transactions he facilitates.

## Key Scenes

**The borrowing (Chapter V):** [[jake-brown|Jake]]'s financial relationship with Nije is established — he has borrowed from him and the debt creates obligation. Nije, in turn, connects [[jake-brown|Jake]] to the [[buffet-flat|buffet flat]] on 140th Street through another [[longshoreman]].

## Selected Passages

> Nije Gridley was a tall, thin, shiny black man. His long eyelashes gave his sharp eyes a sleepy appearance, but he was always wide awake. Before Jake was shipped to France, Nije had a rooming-house in Harlem's Fifth Avenue, worked a little at long-shoring himself, and lent money on the checks of the hard-gambling boys. Now he had three rooming-houses, one of which, free of mortgage, he owned. His lean belly bore a heavy gold chain and he strutted Fifth and Lenox in a ministerial crow-black suit.
>
> (005-005)

> Ordinarily, Nije lent money at the rate of a dollar on four and two on eight per week. He complained bitterly of losses. Twenty-five dollars loaned on a check which, presented, brought only a day's pay. There were tough fellows that played him that game sometimes. They went and never returned to borrow again. But Nije's interest covered up such gaps.
