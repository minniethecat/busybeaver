**[[langston-hughes|Langston Hughes]]** (1901--1967) was an American poet, novelist, playwright, and columnist, widely regarded as one of the most important writers of the [[harlem-renaissance|Harlem Renaissance]]. His work, characterized by its emb[[race-and-color|race]] of Black vernacular speech, [[blues]] and [[jazz]] rhythms, and the daily experience of working-class Black life, made him a defining voice of 1920s [[harlem|Harlem]] and a literary contemporary of [[claude-mckay|Claude McKay]], the author of *Home to [[harlem|Harlem]]* (1928).

## Literary Career and the Harlem Renaissance

Hughes first gained national attention with his poem "The Negro Speaks of Rivers," published in *The Crisis* in 1921 while he was still a teenager. He arrived in [[harlem|Harlem]] later that year to attend Columbia University, but soon left, finding the institution unwelcoming. He worked a series of manual jobs — busboy, launderer, steward on a ship bound for [[africa|Africa]] and Europe — experiences that shaped his lifelong identification with Black working people.

By the mid-1920s, Hughes had become a central figure in the [[harlem-renaissance|Harlem Renaissance]], alongside writers such as [[claude-mckay|Claude McKay]], Zora Neale Hurston, Countee Cullen, and [[jean-toomer|Jean Toomer]]. His 1926 essay "The Negro Artist and the Racial Mountain," published in *The Nation*, articulated a manifesto for Black artistic independence: the Black artist must be free to draw on "the low-down folks" — [[jazz]], [[blues]], spirituals, vernacular speech — without shame or apology to white or middle-class Black audiences.

Hughes's first poetry collection, *The Weary Blues* (1926), was published two years before [[claude-mckay|McKay]]'s *Home to [[harlem|Harlem]]*, and the two writers shared an investment in representing Black life from below — the [[cabaret-culture|cabarets]], [[rent-party|rent parties]], street corners, and [[buffet-flat|buffet flats]] that elite [[harlem|Harlem]]ites preferred to ignore. Where [[claude-mckay|McKay]] rendered this world through the novel's episodic, picaresque form, Hughes rendered it through [[blues]]-inflected lyric poetry.

## Connection to *Home to Harlem*

Although [[langston-hughes|Hughes]] does not appear as a character in *Home to [[harlem|Harlem]]*, his literary presence forms part of the intellectual context within which the novel was written and received. Both [[claude-mckay|McKay]] and Hughes were key figures in what scholars have called the "New Negro" movement: a cultural efflorescence that sought to redefine Black identity through art, away from the accommodationist politics of Booker T. [[washington|Washington]] and toward a more assertive, self-defined modernism.

The novel's protagonist [[jake-brown|Jake Brown]] — a [[longshoreman]] and sensual wanderer who "lived the life" without abstracting it — embodies precisely the "low-down folks" that Hughes championed as the proper subject of Black art. McKay's novel, like Hughes's poetry of the same period, insisted that [[harlem|Harlem]]'s [[cabaret-culture|cabarets]], [[buffet-flat|buffet flats]], and street corners were not sites of shame but of cultural vitality worthy of serious literature.

Both writers faced criticism from Black middle-class arbiters of respectability. W.E.B. [[w-e-b-du-bois|Du Bois]] famously wrote that *Home to [[harlem|Harlem]]* made him feel "distinctly like taking a bath," and Hughes's [[blues]] poetry was similarly attacked for its frankness about sexuality and working-class life. This shared experience of censure from within the [[race-and-color|race]] placed [[claude-mckay|McKay]] and Hughes in a common literary position: defenders of an aesthetic that privileged authenticity over propriety.

## Legacy

Hughes's influence extended far beyond the 1920s. His Simple stories, published in the *[[chicago|Chicago]] Defender* from 1943 onward, continued his practice of channeling Black vernacular voice into literary form. He mentored younger generations of Black writers and maintained a prolific output of poetry, fiction, drama, and journalism until his death in 1967.

Within the specific literary history of *Home to [[harlem|Harlem]]*, Hughes represents the broader movement that received, debated, and canonized [[claude-mckay|McKay]]'s novel. Both writers are now regarded as foundational figures in [[africa|Africa]]n American literature, and their shared investment in the cultural richness of ordinary Black life remains a cornerstone of the [[harlem-renaissance|Harlem Renaissance]]'s enduring legacy.
