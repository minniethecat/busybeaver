**Monkey-chaser** is a derogatory slang term used in Claude McKay's *Home to Harlem* to refer to West Indian immigrants. The term captures the intra-racial tensions between African Americans and Caribbean immigrants in 1920s Harlem, where cultural differences and economic competition created friction within the Black community.

## Usage in the Novel

The term appears in moments of conflict and commentary, deployed by African American characters against West Indians:

> "And I've seen the monkey-chasers order it when they want to p…" (004-080)

> "The monkey-chasers am scrapping," Zeddy comment… (007-149)

These passages show the term used both casually and in situations of actual conflict — "the monkey-chasers am scrapping" suggests that fights between West Indians were a recognizable social phenomenon.

## Jake's Evolving Perspective

The novel's treatment of the term evolves through Jake's consciousness. Early in the novel, he shares the prejudices of his African American peers. But his perspective shifts as he encounters West Indians more directly:

> "…West Indians were monkey-chasers. But now he felt like a boy …" (010-073)

This passage marks a turn — Jake's initial prejudice gives way to a more complex understanding. The term "monkey-chaser" becomes, through the course of the novel, a marker of the xenophobia that Jake gradually outgrows.

## Intra-Racial Tensions

The term reflects real historical tensions between African Americans and West Indian immigrants in early twentieth-century Harlem. Caribbean immigrants, often better educated and more familiar with British colonial institutions, sometimes achieved economic success that generated resentment. At the same time, they faced their own forms of discrimination and struggled to find acceptance within Harlem's established Black community.

## Claude McKay's Perspective

As a Jamaican-born writer, Claude McKay was intimately familiar with these dynamics. His depiction of the term "monkey-chaser" is neither simple condemnation nor endorsement — it is a realistic portrayal of the linguistic landscape of intra-racial conflict, rendered with the ethnographic precision that characterizes the novel.
