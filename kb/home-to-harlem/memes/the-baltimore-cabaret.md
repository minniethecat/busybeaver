# The Baltimore

The most important cabaret in *Home to Harlem* — a Black-owned nightclub on [[lenox-avenue|Lenox Avenue]] whose "blazing lights" anchor Harlem's public nightlife, and whose closure by police action (the "Raid of the Baltimore") is the novel's central public event, reshaping the entire geography of Black social life.

## Overview

The Baltimore was not "just an ordinary cabaret. It had mortgages and policies in the best of the speakeasy places of the Belt. And the mass of Harlem held the Baltimore in high respect because (it was rumored and believed) it was protected by Tammany Hall" (008-003). Functioning as both entertainment venue and financial institution, the Baltimore represented the pinnacle of Black entrepreneurial achievement within the system of Prohibition-era political patronage.

## Atmosphere

The cabaret's "blazing lights" (008-001) made it a landmark on Lenox Avenue. It attracted a mixed clientele — from Harlem's working class to white downtown pleasure-seekers — and served as a meeting ground where racial and class lines temporarily blurred. [[jake-brown|Jake]] first encounters his mysterious "little brown of the Baltimore" here, a woman who "in one night, had revealed a fine different world to him" (006-051). The Baltimore's atmosphere combined glamour with danger: it was where [[rose-congo|Rose]]'s fellow entertainer "got beat up" (005-051), and where white undercover agents later insinuated themselves.

## The Raid

The raid of the Baltimore is the novel's central public event. Notably, it was not a violent police sweep: "Nothing sensational had happened in the Baltimore. The police had not, on a certain night, swept into it and closed it up because of indecent doings. No. It was an indirect raid. Oh, and that made the gossip toothier!" (008-003).

The mechanism was a legal-maneuver closure: three young white undercover men — "clean-shaven, flashily-dressed" — spent two weeks at the Baltimore, spending "money like water," sometimes "accompanied by white girls who swilled enormous quantities of champagne and outshrieked the little ginned-up Negresses and mulattresses of the cabaret" (008-015). They posed as "good fellows, regular guys, looking for a good time only in the Black Belt. They were wearied of the pleasures of the big white world, wanted something new — the primitive joy of Harlem" (008-015).

After gaining the trust of "the wary boss of the Baltimore," the trio was introduced to [[madame-suarez-buffet-flat|Madame Suarez's buffet flat]]. The night they visited, the flat was raided. "The high point of the case was the indictment of the boss of the Baltimore as accessory to the speakeasy crime. The boss was not convicted, but the Baltimore was ordered to be padlocked" (008-018). McKay's political commentary is pointed: "A black member of Tammany had no chances against the Moral Arm of the city" (008-018).

## Social Function

The Baltimore's closure fundamentally restructured Harlem's nightlife. With the premier public cabaret padlocked, social life shifted to private spaces — [[buffet-flat|buffet flats]], [[speakeasy|speakeasies]], and apartment gatherings. This privatization of entertainment had consequences for the novel's characters: [[jake-brown|Jake]], who "had stopped haunting the Baltimore" after losing his brown-skinned lover, found himself drawn into the more intimate, more dangerous world of [[madame-suarez-buffet-flat|Madame Suarez's]] through the connections he made at the Baltimore's periphery.

The raid also illustrates the racial politics of Prohibition enforcement. The Baltimore's white clientele and Tammany protection had made it seem untouchable; its closure demonstrated that Black institutions, no matter how politically connected, were always vulnerable to the "Moral Arm" of a white-run city.

## Key Scenes

### Jake's Lost Brown

The Baltimore is the setting for Jake's most romantic memory: his encounter with a "maroon-brown" woman who "in one night, had revealed a fine different world to him" (006-051). Jake returns repeatedly to the Baltimore searching for her, but never sees her again. "Then he grew to hate that cabaret and stopped going there" (005-053). Throughout the novel, the "little lost brown of the Baltimore" haunts Jake's imagination: "He thought of the surging of desire in his boy's body… Only that cute heartbreaking brown of the Baltimore. A day like this sure feels like her. Didn't even get her name" (020-103). At the novel's end, Jake suggests to [[felice|Felice]]: "Le's go to the Baltimore and finish the night" — only to be reminded, "Why, the Baltimore is padlocked!" (022-071).

### The Undercover Operation

The three white detectives' infiltration of the Baltimore and subsequent introduction to Madame Suarez's buffet flat constitutes the novel's most detailed depiction of how the "Moral Arm" of Prohibition selectively targeted Black establishments. Their performance of interracial camaraderie — buying champagne, dancing with "colored beauties," jazz piano — was a calculated seduction that the "wary boss," a man who "loved life and various forms of fun and had no morals about them" (008-015), could not resist.
