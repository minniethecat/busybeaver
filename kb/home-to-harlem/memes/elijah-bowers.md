# Elijah Bowers

A figure from [[harlem|Harlem]]'s pre-war nightlife whose story is preserved only through [[strawberry-lips|Strawberry Lips]]'s oral history — a monologue that makes him the novel's emblem of the rise and fall that defined Black entertainment entrepreneurship. Elijah Bowers never appears in the novel's present tense; he exists entirely as a story told on a walk down [[myrtle-avenue|Myrtle Avenue]].

## Role in the Novel

Elijah Bowers enters the novel through [[strawberry-lips|Strawberry Lips]]'s extended monologue (006-087). Walking down [[myrtle-avenue|Myrtle Avenue]] with [[jake-brown|Jake]] and [[zeddy-plummer|Zeddy]], [[strawberry-lips|Strawberry Lips]] recalls the avenue's glory days:

> "Myrtle Avenue used to be a be-be itching of a place, when Doc Giles had his gambling house on there and Elijah Bowers was running his cabaret. H'm. But Bowers was some big guy. He knew swell white folks in politics, and had a grand automobile and a high-yaller wife that hadn't no need of painting to pass. His cabaret was running neck and neck with Marshall's in Fifty-third Street. Then one night he killed a man in his cabaret, and that finished him. The lawyers got him off. But they cleaned him out dry. Done broke him, that case did. And today he's plumb down and out." (006-087)

The monologue encapsulates the arc of a Black entertainment entrepreneur in the era before [[harlem|Harlem]] became the center of Black nightlife. Bowers's [[cabaret-culture|cabaret]] on [[myrtle-avenue|Myrtle Avenue]] in [[brooklyn|Brooklyn]] — not [[harlem|Harlem]] — competed with Marshall's in [[fifty-third-street|Fifty-third Street]], the midtown district that was the ori[[gin-whisky|gin]]al hub of Black entertainment before the northward [[migration]] of the 1920s.

## Characterization

Bowers is characterized entirely through [[strawberry-lips|Strawberry Lips]]'s narration, which makes him a figure of oral history rather than direct observation. The key details are carefully chosen: his political connections ("knew swell white folks in politics"), his wealth signifiers ("a grand automobile"), his [[race-and-color|color]]-conscious marriage ("a [[high-yaller]] wife that hadn't no need of painting to pass"), and his downfall — a single act of violence ("killed a man in his [[cabaret-culture|cabaret]]") that the legal system exploited to strip him of everything.

The phrase "the lawyers got him off. But they cleaned him out dry" captures a recurring theme in McKay's depiction of Black entrepreneurship: the legal system is not an instrument of justice but a mechanism of extraction. Bowers escapes prison but is financially destroyed — "plumb down and out" — the law functioning as a tax on Black success rather than a protection of it.

## Historical Significance

Elijah Bowers represents the **pre-history of [[harlem|Harlem]] nightlife** — the era when the center of Black entertainment was not yet [[harlem|Harlem]] but downtown Manhattan and [[brooklyn|Brooklyn]]. His [[cabaret-culture|cabaret]] on [[myrtle-avenue|Myrtle Avenue]] competed with Marshall's in [[fifty-third-street|Fifty-third Street]]; both predate the northward shift that would make [[harlem|Harlem]] the capital of Black nightlife by the mid-1920s.

His story also illustrates the **precarity of Black business** in the period. Bowers achieved wealth, status, and political connections — and lost everything through a single violent incident that the legal system monetized. The pattern — Black entrepreneur rises, violence occurs, legal system extracts wealth — would have been recognizable to McKay's readers as a structural feature of Black economic life, not an individual tragedy.
