The **Haitian Revolution** (1791–1804) was the successful slave uprising that established Haiti as the first Black republic in the Western Hemisphere, referenced in [[claude-mckay|Claude McKay]]'s *[[home-to-harlem|Home to Harlem]]* through the figure of [[toussaint-louverture|Toussaint Louverture]].

## In the Novel

The revolution enters the novel through a conversation in which a Haitian [[waiter]] on the [[pennsylvania-railroad|Pennsylvania Railroad]] tells [[jake-brown|Jake Brown]] about "[[toussaint-louverture|Toussaint Louverture]], the black slave and leader of the Haitian slaves." (010-052) Jake learns that:

> For the first time he heard the name Toussaint L'Ouverture, the black slave and leader of the Haitian slaves. Heard how he fought and conquered the slave-owners and then protected them; decreed laws for Haiti that held more of human wisdom and nobility than the Code Napoleon; defended his baby revolution against the Spanish and the English vultures; defeated Napoleon's punitive expedition; and how tragically he was captured by a civilized trick, taken to France, and sent by Napoleon to die brokenhearted in a cold dungeon.
>
> "A black man! A black man! Oh, I wish I'd been a soldier under sich a man!" Jake said, simply.
>
> (010-052)(010-053)

The Haitian waiter, who is Haitian-born, also tells Jake about Dessalines, who "carried on the fight begun by Toussaint L'Ouverture and kept Haiti independent" (010-054). Ray later reflects on Toussaint's greatness, quoting Wordsworth's sonnet and declaring: "The world doesn't know how great Toussaint L'Ouverture really was. He was not merely great. He was lofty. He was good." (010-055)

A Haitian waiter also appears at [[wiley-avenue|Wiley Avenue]], (011-011) suggesting the presence of Afro-Caribbean [[migration|immigrants]] in [[harlem|Harlem]]'s working-class community — a population whose migration was shaped by the revolution's legacy.
