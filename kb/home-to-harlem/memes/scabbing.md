# Scabbing

The act of working during a labor strike, crossing a picket line to take the job of a striking worker. In *Home to [[harlem|Harlem]]*, scabbing is the moral crucible through which [[jake-brown|Jake]]'s working-class identity is tested — and affirmed.

## Definition

"Scabbing" (or "blackleg[[gin-whisky|gin]]g" in British usage) refers to workers who accept employment at a struck workplace, undermining the strike by providing the employer with replacement labor. In early 20th-century America, scabbing was a deeply charged moral issue within working-class communities — scabs were seen as traitors to their class, and the epithet carried lasting social consequences. The term appears in the novel during a flashback to [[jake-brown|Jake]]'s time in [[london|London]], where he was the only Black man in a work gang during a dock strike. (005-013)

## Cultural Context

### Harlem Renaissance Context

Labor organizing was a significant undercurrent of the [[harlem|Harlem]] Renaissance, though often overshadowed by its cultural and artistic dimensions. Black workers faced dual exclusion — from white-dominated unions that often barred Black membership, and from jobs reserved for white workers. This created a complex relationship between Black workers and the labor movement: unions could be vehicles for working-class solidarity across racial lines (as the I.W.W. advocated) or instruments of white job protectionism (as many AFL craft unions practiced).

Scabbing occupied an especially fraught position for Black workers, who were sometimes recruited as strikebreakers precisely because unions had excluded them — a dynamic that pitted economic survival against class solidarity.

### In the Novel

McKay dramatizes the scabbing dilemma in Chapter V, when [[jake-brown|Jake]] recalls his time in [[london|London]] during a dock strike. A fellow worker asks why [[jake-brown|Jake]] is "scabbing," and [[jake-brown|Jake]]'s response is immediate and proud: "I been heah all mah lifetime, but I ain't nevah yet scabbed it on any man" (005-014). He was the "only black man in mah gang" (005-014), but his racial isolation did not justify crossing the picket line.

When the other man argues that the striking dockers "wouldn't even allow a black man in their union," [[jake-brown|Jake]]'s reply — "Nope, I won't scab, but I ain't a joiner kind of fellah" (005-017) — reveals a nuanced position: he refuses to betray striking workers even when those workers' unions discriminate against him. He is not pro-union (he's "not a joiner") but he is anti-scab — an ethical distinction that separates class solidarity from institutional membership.

[[jake-brown|Jake]]'s moral clarity on scabbing contrasts with the other man's rationalization: he took the job not knowing "it was a scab job" (005-024) but stayed on. When he finally declares "I won't scab on nobody, not even the orneriest gang" (005-025), [[jake-brown|Jake]]'s influence has shifted his position — a small victory for working-class ethics over economic expediency.

## Key Passages

> "I been heah all mah lifetime, but I ain't nevah yet scabbed it on any man. I done worked at jobs where theah was strikes, but I alluz left. Nevah scabbed it on any man." (005-014)

[[jake-brown|Jake]]'s triple repetition of "nevah scabbed it on any man" is an oath of class honor, more fundamental to his identity than [[race-and-color|race]] or nationality. The phrase "on any man" frames scabbing as a betrayal of other workers personally, not just an abstract political position.

> "Nope, I won't scab, but I ain't a joiner kind of fellah." (005-017)

This line crystallizes [[jake-brown|Jake]]'s working-class individualism: he won't betray his class, but he won't join its institutions either. It's a position that reflects McKay's own ambivalent relationship with organized labor and left-wing politics.

## Historical Significance

The scabbing dilemma McKay dramatizes reflects real historical tensions. Black workers were indeed used as strikebreakers in numerous early 20th-century labor conflicts — from the 1919 steel strike to the [[chicago|Chicago]] meatpacking strikes — often because white unions had systematically excluded them. This created a tragic dynamic: white racism in the labor movement produced a pool of Black workers who could be weaponized against unions, which then reinforced white workers' racism. The Industrial Workers of the World (I.W.W.) was the major exception, actively organizing across racial lines on the docks and elsewhere. [[jake-brown|Jake]]'s anti-scab ethics without union membership mirrors the I.W.W.'s ethos of class solidarity independent of institutional affiliation.
