# The Blues

The blues is the foundational African American musical genre characterized by twelve-bar chord progressions, flattened "blue" notes, and lyrical themes of sorrow and resilience. In [[claude-mckay|Claude McKay]]'s *[[Home to Harlem]]*, the blues permeates every social space — from [[cabaret-culture|cabarets]] to [[buffet-flat|buffet flats]] to [[house-party|house parties]] — functioning as both entertainment and the collective emotional expression of working-class Black life.

## Definition

The blues emerged from African American folk music traditions in the late 19th-century Deep South, crystallizing into a standardized twelve-bar form by the early 1900s. Its defining musical features include "blue notes" (microtonal flattening of the third, fifth, and seventh scale degrees), AAB lyrical structure, and thematic preoccupations with hardship, love, travel, and resilience.

By the 1920s, the blues had migrated north with the Great Migration, becoming a cornerstone of [[Harlem]]'s musical landscape alongside [[ragtime]] and [[jazz]].

## Cultural Context

### Harlem Renaissance Context

The blues occupied an ambivalent position in [[Harlem Renaissance]] cultural politics. Some Renaissance leaders viewed the blues as "low" folk culture — authentic but unrefined, in need of elevation by classically trained Black composers. Others, including Langston Hughes and Zora Neale Hurston, embraced the blues as the truest expression of Black American experience, arguing that its formal sophistication made it high art in its own right.

McKay, true to his working-class literary commitments, treats the blues with unapologetic respect. The blues is never condescended to or aestheticized in this novel — it is simply the music that Black people make and love, indelible to daily life.

### In the Novel

The blues enters the novel in Chapter II: a phonograph "grinding out a 'blues'" at a [[buffet-flat|buffet flat]] creates the atmosphere for dancing couples "thick as maggots in a vat of sweet liquor" (002-029). This early appearance establishes the blues as the baseline music of [[harlem|Harlem]] nightlife — lower-status than the elegant [[jazz]] of [[madame-suarez-buffet-flat|Madame Suarez's]], but more democratic and physically immediate.

The blues recurs at key moments of working-class leisure. At a [[house-party|house party]] in Chapter XIII, "a slim yellow youth was playing a 'blues'" (013-022), the pianist "began banging another blues" (013-037), and bodies "rocked, rocked to the 'blues'" (013-043). The triple repetition of the word mimics the music's hypnotic quality within McKay's prose.

> Oh, "blues," "blues," "blues." Black-framed blues. (005-057)

The hyphenated compound "black-framed" — unique to this passage — makes explicit the racial identity of the blues while suggesting music as a frame around Black experience, containing and expressing it. The triple repetition ("blues, blues, blues") reproduces the blues's AAB lyrical structure within the novel's prose.

The novel also connects blues geographically beyond [[harlem|Harlem]]: a "drag 'blues'" is described as "the favorite of all the low-down darky dives of [[philadelphia|Philadelphia]]" (004-059), linking [[harlem|Harlem]]'s musical taste to a broader Black urban geography and suggesting a shared working-class musical culture across the Great Migration's destinations.

## Key Passages

> A phonograph was grinding out a "blues," and some couples were dancing, thick as maggots in a vat of sweet liquor, and as wriggling. (002-029)

The "grinding" phonograph suggests mechanical reproduction of music once live and immediate — the blues commodified through 1920s recording technology. Yet the dancing bodies resist mechanization: "thick as maggots" is deliberately grotesque and sensuous, conveying the density of human contact that blues enables.

> Oh, "blues," "blues," "blues." Black-framed blues. (005-057)

The novel's most compressed statement on blues and racial identity — a character cries out the word until it becomes pure sound, then McKay frames it in black.

## Historical Significance

The blues is one of the most consequential musical forms of the 20th century. From rural Southern origins through urbanization in [[chicago|Chicago]] and [[Harlem]], the blues provided the musical vocabulary for [[jazz]], rhythm and blues, rock and roll, and hip-hop. The "classic blues" singers of the 1920s — Bessie Smith, Ma Rainey, Ethel Waters — brought the form to national audiences, making Black women the first major recording stars of any race in American popular music. The blues lyrics McKay's characters sing and hear belong to this tradition of Black female vocal artistry.
