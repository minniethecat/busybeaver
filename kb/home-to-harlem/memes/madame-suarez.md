Madame Suarez is the proprietor of a [[harlem|Harlem]] [[cabaret-culture|cabaret]] and [[buffet-flat|buffet flat]] that appears in chapter 8 of [[claude-mckay|Claude McKay]]'s *Home to [[harlem|Harlem]]*. She caters to an upscale colored clientele and is fiercely protective of her establishment's exclusivity, though her business ultimately falls victim to a police raid.

## Role in the Novel

Madame Suarez runs one of [[harlem|Harlem]]'s "sporty" establishments — a [[cabaret-culture|cabaret]] with a "magnificent red carpet" that attracts "chocolate-browns and yellow-creams." (008-006)(008-010) She represents the entrepreneurial side of [[harlem|Harlem]] nightlife, a businesswoman who understands her market and enforces its racial and class boundaries. Her establishment is raided by police, and she is both fined and sentenced more harshly than her clients. (008-036)

## Characterization

### Personality and Traits

Madame Suarez has a sharp tongue and a clear sense of her establishment's social hierarchy. She frequently disparages poorer patrons: "Gimme their room rent and let 'em go. This heah place is for nice colored people. It ain't no dump for cheap-no-'count niggers." (008-007) Despite her class snobbery, she makes "special efforts to please her patrons," understanding the art of hospitality. (008-012)

### Appearance

The text does not provide a detailed physical description of Madame Suarez, but her establishment is described as having a "magnificent red carpet," suggesting she invests in the appearance of her venue. (008-006)

## Key Scenes

[[jake-brown|Jake]] first visits [[madame-suarez-buffet-flat|Madame Suarez's]] establishment in chapter 8, noting the "chocolate-browns and yellow-creams" who frequent it. (008-010) The atmosphere is described as "fairly bacchic" in the early morning hours, with [[jazz]] and dancing. (008-013)

The establishment's fortunes turn when uniformed police are summoned. "Madame Suarez and her clients were ordered to appear in court the following morning." (008-035) The outcome is severe: "They were all fined. But Madame Suarez, besides being fined, was sentenced to the workhouse for thirty days." (008-036)

## Themes

Madame Suarez embodies the intersection of race, class, and entrepreneurship in [[harlem|Harlem]]'s nightlife economy. Her class prejudice — catering to a "better" clientele while disparaging poorer patrons — reflects the internal stratification of the Black community that McKay documents throughout the novel. Her harsher punishment compared to her clients underscores the vulnerability of Black women entrepreneurs to the legal system.
