The **cakewalk** is a historic [[africa|Africa]]n American dance form that ori[[gin-whisky|gin]]ated on Southern plantations in the nineteenth century and later became a popular stage performance, briefly referenced in [[claude-mckay|Claude McKay]]'s *[[home-to-harlem|Home to Harlem]]*.

## Definition

The cakewalk was ori[[gin-whisky|gin]]ally a dance performed by enslaved [[africa|Africa]]n Americans parodying the formal European-[[fashion-and-style|style]] dancing of white plantation owners. Couples competed with high-stepping, strutting movements, often for the prize of a cake — hence the name. By the late nineteenth century, the cakewalk had been appropriated by [[minstrelsy|minstrel shows]] and vaudeville, becoming one of the first [[africa|Africa]]n American dance forms to achieve widespread popularity among white audiences.

## In the Novel

McKay references the cakewalk in a single, telling gesture. When [[jake-brown|Jake]] is introducing his friend [[ray|Ray]] to [[madame-laura|Madame Laura]] in [[philadelphia|Philadelphia]], he grins while "prancing a little, as if he were going to do the old cakewalk." (013-020) The brief moment evokes the dance's status as a cultural memory — something "old" that belongs to an earlier generation of Black performance. [[jake-brown|Jake]]'s instinctive physical reference to the cakewalk, even in a casual social moment, suggests the dance's deep embedding in [[africa|Africa]]n American bodily culture.
