***The Chicago Defender*** was the most influential African American newspaper of the early twentieth century, referenced in [[claude-mckay|Claude McKay]]'s *[[home-to-harlem|Home to Harlem]]* as part of [[ray|Ray]]'s reading in Pittsburgh.

## In the Novel

While in Pittsburgh, [[ray|Ray]] buys several weekly Negro newspapers including *The Chicago Defender*, *The Pittsburgh Courier*, *The Baltimore American*, and *The Negro World*. The narrator notes that in Pittsburgh "he found a big assortment of all the Negro publications that he never could find in Harlem." (011-021) The brief reference situates the Defender within the broader ecosystem of the Black press, which played a crucial role in shaping African American political consciousness during the 1920s.

## Historical Context

Founded by Robert S. Abbott in 1905, *The Chicago Defender* became the most widely circulated Black newspaper in the United States. It was instrumental in promoting the [[great-migration|Great Migration]], encouraging Southern Black readers to move North for better opportunities. The paper's "red summer" coverage of the 1919 race riots and its advocacy for anti-lynching legislation made it a powerful voice for racial justice. By the 1920s, it had a national readership, distributed through Pullman porters and other networks.
