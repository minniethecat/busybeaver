# Longshoremen's Strike

A labor strike on the [[brooklyn|Brooklyn]] docks that becomes a moral crucible for [[jake-brown|Jake Brown]], forcing him to choose between economic survival and racial solidarity — and producing one of the novel's most explicit confrontations over the meaning of work, [[race-and-color|race]], and dignity in white America.

## Overview

After returning from [[world-war-i|World War I]], [[jake-brown|Jake Brown]] seeks work on the [[brooklyn|Brooklyn]] piers. He arrives to find a strike in progress — but no pickets, because union leaders have colluded with police to suppress them. Offered a job as a strikebreaker by [[zeddy-plummer|Zeddy Plummer]], [[jake-brown|Jake]] refuses, declaring he has never "[[scabbing|scab]]bed it on any man" (005-818). The episode crystallizes the racial double bind of Black labor: excluded from white unions, yet morally unwilling to undermine worker solidarity.

## Context

### Labor and Race in Early 20th-Century New York

Longshore work was one of the few industrial jobs open to Black men in [[new-york-city|New York]], but the union locals — dominated by Irish Americans — systematically assigned Black workers to the worst piers and lowest-paid holds (005-827). When strikes occurred, Black workers faced an impossible choice: honor the picket line of a union that discriminated against them, or [[scabbing|scab]] and earn a day's wage. The strike in the novel reflects the real-world dynamics of the 1919–1920 postwar labor unrest, when a wave of strikes swept American ports and the [[race-and-color|color]] line fractured worker solidarity.

### In the Novel

The strike scene occurs early in [[jake-brown|Jake]]'s postwar life (Chapter 5), immediately establishing his moral code. Before the war, [[jake-brown|Jake]] had been a "little boss" of a longshore gang with an Irish supervisor who was "a very good friend" (001-072). The strike tests whether wartime experience — in [[france|France]], and in [[london-east-end|London's East End]] — has changed his relationship to white authority and Black labor.

## Details and Sequence

[[jake-brown|Jake]], short on money after returning from Europe, asks [[zeddy-plummer|Zeddy]] about work. [[zeddy-plummer|Zeddy]] brings him to the [[brooklyn|Brooklyn]] piers where men are loading ships — but the regular [[longshoreman|longshoremen]] are on strike. A worker tells [[jake-brown|Jake]] "there was a strike on and he was [[scabbing]]," explaining that union leaders "had connived with the police to beat up and jail the pickets" (005-815). There are no pickets visible.

[[jake-brown|Jake]] immediately withdraws. "I've done worked through a tur'ble assortaments o' jobs in mah lifetime, but I ain't nevah yet [[scabbing|scab]]bed it on any man," he tells the striker (005-818). He recounts his labor history: a good union man in [[philadelphia|Philadelphia]], but in [[new-york-city|New York]] "they gived the [[race-and-color|color]]ed mens the worser piers and holds the bes'n a' them foh the Irishmen" (005-827).

That evening, [[jake-brown|Jake]] confronts [[zeddy-plummer|Zeddy]] for not warning him it was a [[scabbing|scab]] job. "I won't [[scabbing|scab]] on nobody, not even the orneriest crackers," [[jake-brown|Jake]] says (005-851). [[zeddy-plummer|Zeddy]] erupts: "Decent mah black moon! I'll [[scabbing|scab]] through hell to make mah living. Scab job or open shop or union am all the same jobs to me" (005-860). His argument is materialist and racial: white unions exclude Black workers, so Black workers owe them no loyalty. "Ain't white mens done [[scabbing|scab]]bed niggers outa all the jobs they useter hold down heah in this city?" (005-860).

[[jake-brown|Jake]] holds his ground: "With all a that [[scabbing]] is a low-down deal" (005-863). [[zeddy-plummer|Zeddy]] delivers the novel's most bitter credo: "I loves life and I got to live and I'll [[scabbing|scab]] through hell to live" (005-866).

## Aftermath and Significance

The strike episode establishes the novel's moral geography of labor. [[jake-brown|Jake]]'s refusal to [[scabbing|scab]] — at real economic cost — distinguishes him from [[zeddy-plummer|Zeddy]]'s survival-at-any-cost pragmatism. Yet the novel does not resolve the tension: [[jake-brown|Jake]]'s principle leaves him penniless by Saturday, forcing him to borrow from [[nije-gridley|Nije Gridley]], the [[longshoreman]] moneylender whose usury (005-794) exploits the very workers [[jake-brown|Jake]] refuses to betray. The strike is thus not a triumph of solidarity but an exposure of the structural trap facing Black labor: principle cannot pay the rent, and the informal credit economy ([[nije-gridley|Nije]], [[billy-biasse|Billy Biasse]]'s gambling flat) fills the gap that white unions and employers leave.

The episode also foreshadows the novel's broader treatment of solidarity. [[jake-brown|Jake]]'s wartime experiences — fighting for a country that treats him as a second-class citizen, witnessing interracial solidarity in [[france|French]] ports — inform his refusal to cross the picket line. The [[longshoreman|longshoremen]]'s strike is the domestic echo of the racial battles [[jake-brown|Jake]] witnessed in [[brest|Brest]] and [[london-east-end|London's East End]].
