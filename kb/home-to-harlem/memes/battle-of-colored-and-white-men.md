# Battle of Colored and White Men

A violent racial confrontation in [[london-east-end|London's East End]] in the summer after the [[armistice-1918|Armistice]] — fisticuffs, razors, knives, and gunplay — that marks a turning point in [[jake-brown|Jake Brown]]'s emotional journey, triggering the "awful fever of lonesomeness" that drives him back toward America.

## Overview

Following the November 1918 [[armistice-1918|Armistice]], hundreds of Black soldiers remained in [[london|London]], some working, some living on small government pensions. The influx of Black men into the [[london-east-end|East End]] drove up both "the price of sex" and "the dignity of it" (001-123). Tensions between white and Black men — economic, sexual, racial — erupted in a multi-day battle that [[jake-brown|Jake]] observed from the enforced safety of his woman's rooms. The event crystallizes the racial violence latent in the interracial social order of wartime and postwar [[london|London]], and directly precipitates [[jake-brown|Jake]]'s decision to return to [[harlem|Harlem]].

## Context

### Black Soldiers in Post-Armistice London

The [[armistice-1918|Armistice]] of November 1918 did not mean immediate demobilization. Thousands of colonial and American soldiers — including Black troops from the U.S. — stayed in Europe for months. [[london-east-end|London's East End]], already a working-class immigrant district, absorbed a significant Black male population. The racial and sexual economy shifted: Black men with military pay competed with local white men for work, housing, and women. [[claude-mckay|Claude McKay]] himself lived in [[london|London]] during 1919–1920 and witnessed the racial tensions he fictionalizes through [[jake-brown|Jake]].

### In the Novel

The battle is narrated retrospectively in Chapter 1, as part of [[jake-brown|Jake]]'s memory of his time in [[london|London]]. It is one of the first events the reader learns about [[jake-brown|Jake]]'s past, establishing the novel's thematic concern with [[race-and-color|race]], violence, and male competition for women. The battle is also echoed later when characters recall "the bal-musette fights between [[race-and-color|color]]ed and white soldiers in [[france|France]]" (006-1142) and the fatal fights in [[brest|Brest]] (001-364), linking [[london|London]] to a broader pattern of postwar interracial violence across Europe.

## Key Participants

- [[jake-brown]] — American [[longshoreman]] turned soldier, kept indoors by his English woman for three days during the battle (001-123)
- [[london]] — the [[london-east-end|East End]] setting, where Black soldiers gathered after the [[armistice-1918|Armistice]]
- [[brest]] — [[france|French]] port city where similar interracial fights occurred between Black and white soldiers (001-364)

## Details and Sequence

"The [[armistice-1918|Armistice]] had brought many more black men to [[london-east-end|the East End of London]]. Hundreds of them" (001-123). The sudden demographic shift created friction: "Some of them found work. Some did not. Many were getting a little pension from the government." The narrator notes the sexual-economic dimension directly: "The price of sex went up in the [[london-east-end|East End]], and the dignity of it also."

The battle itself was large and lethal: "[[jake-brown|Jake]] saw a big battle staged between the [[race-and-color|color]]ed and white men of [[london-east-end|London's East End]]. Fisticuffs, [[razor]] and knife and gun play" (001-123). The mention of firearms — "gun play" — distinguishes this from an ordinary street brawl; this was an organized racial confrontation with serious weaponry.

[[jake-brown|Jake]]'s woman "would not let him out-of-doors" for three days — a protective confinement that saved his life but left him isolated. When the battle ended, [[jake-brown|Jake]] was seized by "the awful fever of lonesomeness. He felt all alone in the world. He wanted to run away from the kindheartedness of his lady of the [[london-east-end|East End]]" (001-123). The paradox is sharp: the woman who protected him now represents a world he needs to flee.

## Aftermath and Significance

The battle's immediate aftermath is psychological rather than political. [[jake-brown|Jake]] does not analyze the racial dynamics or seek justice; he feels lonesomeness — an existential isolation that no individual kindness can heal. His response is physical flight: deserting his ship in [[new-york-city|New York]], he returns to [[harlem|Harlem]].

The novel embeds this [[london|London]] battle within a pattern of racial violence that spans the Atlantic. [[jake-brown|Jake]] recalls "the bal-musette fights between [[race-and-color|color]]ed and white soldiers in [[france|France]]. Blacks, browns, yellows, whites" (006-1142), and [[zeddy-plummer|Zeddy]] recalls "the fatal fights between black men and white in the *maisons closes*" of [[brest|Brest]] (001-364). The [[london|London]] battle is not a singular event but one node in a transatlantic archipelago of racial violence — all connected to the movement of Black soldiers, the competition for women, and the fragility of interracial peace in the aftermath of a war fought, ostensibly, for democracy.

The battle also serves a narrative function: it is the catalyst that sends [[jake-brown|Jake]] home. Without the lonesomeness induced by the battle, [[jake-brown|Jake]] might have stayed in [[london|London]], and the novel's [[harlem|Harlem]] setting would never materialize. The racial violence of Europe is thus the negative force that propels the protagonist toward the Black world of [[harlem|Harlem]] — not as a refuge from racism, but as a place where [[race-and-color|racial identity]] is not the solitary burden it is in a white-majority society.
