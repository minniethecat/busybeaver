# Felice

The mysterious woman [[jake-brown|Jake]] meets at [[the-baltimore-cabaret|the Baltimore]] [[cabaret-culture|cabaret]] at the novel's opening and loses — his "little lost brown" — whose absence drives his wanderings through [[harlem|Harlem]] nightlife and whose reappearance in [[chicago|Chicago]] closes the novel on a note of romantic possibility.

## Role in the Novel

Felice is the novel's absent center for most of its length. [[jake-brown|Jake]] meets her once, loses her, and searches for her through [[cabaret-culture|cabarets]], [[buffet-flat|buffet flats]], and railroad journeys. She exists primarily as a memory and a quest — a structuring absence that organizes [[jake-brown|Jake]]'s otherwise episodic adventures. When she finally appears in Chapter XX in [[chicago|Chicago]], she is not idealized: "They calls me Felice" (020-012) is a practical self-introduction, and their reunion is tender but grounded.

## Characterization

### Appearance

Felice is described in terms of color and desirability: she is "brown" — the novel's preferred descriptor for attractive Black women — and specifically "the little lost brown of [[the-baltimore-cabaret|the Baltimore]]." The possessive "of [[the-baltimore-cabaret|the Baltimore]]" ties her identity to the [[cabaret-culture|cabaret]] where [[jake-brown|Jake]] first saw her, as if she is part of the venue's atmosphere rather than a fully independent person. Her reappearance in [[chicago|Chicago]] allows her to emerge as a fuller character.

### Personality & Traits

Felice is practical more than romantic. She has moved from [[harlem|Harlem]] to [[chicago|Chicago]], found work, and built a life — she does not spend the novel pining for [[jake-brown|Jake]] as [[jake-brown|Jake]] pines for her. Her autonomy and self-possession, revealed in the [[chicago|Chicago]] chapters, rebalance the novel's gender dynamics: she is not a prize to be won but a person making her own decisions.

## Key Scenes

**The reunion (Chapter XX):** [[jake-brown|Jake]] finally finds Felice in [[chicago|Chicago]]. Her simple self-introduction — "They calls me Felice" (020-012) — and [[jake-brown|Jake]]'s recognition — "But I didn't fohget the color" — dramatize the bodily memory that has sustained his quest across hundreds of miles and dozens of chapters.

## Quotes

> "They calls me Felice." (020-012)

The demotic "they calls me" rather than "my name is" positions Felice within the novel's vernacular register. She names herself through other people's naming of her — a reminder that identity in McKay's [[harlem|Harlem]] is always socially constituted.

> "But I didn't fohget the color." (020-013)

[[jake-brown|Jake]]'s memory of Felice is a memory of skin — color as the most intimate and inescapable form of knowledge.
