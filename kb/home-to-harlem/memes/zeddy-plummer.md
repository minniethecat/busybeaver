# Zeddy Plummer

Zeddy Plummer is [[jake-brown|Jake Brown]]'s childhood friend from [[virginia|Virginia]], a stocky [[longshoreman]] and volatile fixture of [[Harlem]]'s [[the-sporting-world|sporting world]]. Zeddy is [[jake-brown|Jake]]'s shadow — a figure who shares [[jake-brown|Jake]]'s class background, occupation, and appetites but lacks his effortless magnetism, making him simultaneously companion, foil, and rival. Where [[jake-brown|Jake]] moves through [[harlem|Harlem]] with natural g[[race-and-color|race]], Zeddy operates through hustle, loyalty, and a wounding awareness that he will always finish second.

## Role in the Novel

Zeddy's sudden reappearance in Chapter III — "Zeddy Plummer! What grave is you arised from?" [[jake-brown|Jake]] exclaims at the sight of him on a [[harlem|Harlem]] street (003-006) — reactivates [[jake-brown|Jake]]'s [[virginia|Virginia]] past in the [[harlem|Harlem]] present. Their emb[[race-and-color|race]], complete with Zeddy pouring out "pure Bourbon" from a flask and declaring his joy, is one of the novel's warmest moments of male friendship.

But the warmth is short-lived. Zeddy's simmering jealousy over [[rose-congo|Rose]] — he had been with her before [[jake-brown|Jake]], and his claim to her is both sexual and territorial — introduces the novel's theme of male competition as the shadow-side of male solidarity. "That gal got beat up in [[the-baltimore-cabaret|the Baltimore]]. She done sings me into a tangle" (004-053) — Zeddy's warning to [[jake-brown|Jake]] about [[rose-congo|Rose]] is half-protective, half-possessive, and fully unreliable.

## Characterization

### Appearance

Zeddy "reached a little above [[jake-brown|Jake]]'s shoulders" (003-013) — shorter than the tall, brawny protagonist, a physical marker of his secondary status. He is "stocky" and has "smooth black skin," and his [[fashion-and-style|dress]] — like [[jake-brown|Jake]]'s — follows the sporting-life aesthetic of [[harlem|Harlem]]'s working-class dandies: [[fashion-and-style|fashion]]able [[fashion-and-style|suits]] worn as armor and advertisement.

> Zeddy reached a little above Jake's shoulders. He was stocky, thick-set and his smooth black skin gave him the appearance of an elegant little bull. (003-013)

The "elegant little bull" — McKay's compressed characterization of Zeddy's physical presence: powerful, handsome, compact, vaguely comic in his intensity.

### Personality

Zeddy's defining quality is his volatility — a combination of expansive generosity and sharp resentment. When he first sees [[jake-brown|Jake]], he pours Bourbon and exclaims "If you was a woman, I'd say you looked good enough to eat — you look so good to me, I could kiss you" (003-007). The homoerotic charge of this moment — a man telling another man he looks good enough to eat — is characteristic of McKay's depiction of male intimacy as a force that exceeds the boundaries of "friendship."

But Zeddy's warmth curdles when [[jake-brown|Jake]] displaces him. His jealousy is not simply romantic; it is existential. [[jake-brown|Jake]] takes what Zeddy wants ([[rose-congo|Rose]]) and what Zeddy is (central in his social world), and Zeddy's resentment is the resentment of the second-place finisher who cannot understand why he keeps losing.

### Relationships

- **[[jake-brown|Jake Brown]]** — Childhood friend, fellow [[longshoreman]], and rival. Their friendship survives rivalry but is permanently altered by it.
- **[[rose-congo|Rose]]** — The [[cabaret-culture|cabaret]] singer over whom Zeddy and [[jake-brown|Jake]] compete. Zeddy's claim to her is both possessive and protective.
- **[[nije-gridley|Nije Gridley]]** — The [[longshoreman]] broker who connects both men to dock work — a figure representing the informal labor economy.

## Themes

**Male Friendship & Competition**: Zeddy embodies the novel's central insight about male friendship — that solidarity and rivalry are not opposites but twins. He and [[jake-brown|Jake]] share everything (work, space, women, past) and this sharing is the source of both their bond and their conflict.

**The Second Self**: Zeddy is what [[jake-brown|Jake]] would be without [[jake-brown|Jake]]'s beauty and ease — a working-class Black man whose survival depends on hustle rather than magnetism. His presence in the novel allows McKay to show that [[jake-brown|Jake]]'s exceptionalism is a gift, not an achievement.

**[[migration|Migration]] & Memory**: Zeddy carries [[virginia|Virginia]] into [[harlem|Harlem]]. His presence reactivates [[jake-brown|Jake]]'s rural past, and their friendship is a living archive of a world left behind — a reminder that the [[great-migration|Great Migration]]'s urban communities were stitched together from rural relationships.

## Quotes

> "Good Gawd, boy! Wheah you come from? If you was a woman, I'd say you looked good enough to eat — you look so good to me, I could kiss you." (003-007)

The novel's most charged moment of male intimacy — affection, attraction, and aggression compressed into a single exclamation.

> "That gal got beat up in the Baltimore. She done sings me into a tangle." (004-053)

Zeddy's warning about [[rose-congo|Rose]] — half concern for [[jake-brown|Jake]], half territorial claim — captures his inability to separate love from possession.
