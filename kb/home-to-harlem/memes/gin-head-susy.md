# Gin-head Susy

A Brooklyn domestic worker whose Saturday-night gin parties — and her tragic preference for "yellow" men — make her one of *Home to Harlem*'s most fully realized minor characters. Susy embodies the intersection of colorism, gender exploitation, and the desperate search for intimacy in 1920s Black working-class life.

## Characterization

Susy is "of the complexion known among Negroes as spade or chocolate-to-the-bone. Her eyes shone like big white stars. Her chest was majestic and the general effect like a mountain. And that mountain was overgrand because Susy never wore any other but extremely French-heeled shoes" (007-006). She works as a cook in downtown New York and lives in a "little apartment in Myrtle Avenue near Prince Street" in Brooklyn (007-003).

Her defining trait is what McKay calls her "yellow complex": "The burning passion of Susy's life was the yellow youth of her race." A yellow-skinned man married her at fifteen in South Carolina and left her before she was eighteen. "Since then she had lived with a yellow complex at the core of her heart" (007-008). McKay frames this personal obsession within the larger racial history: "Civilization had brought strikingly exotic types into Susy's race. And like many, many Negroes, she was a victim to that… Ancient black life rooted upon its base with all its fascinating new layers of brown, low-brown, high-brown, nut-brown, lemon, [[maroon]], olive, mauve, gold" (007-009).

## The Gin Parties

Susy's parties are "all-male" and "as yellow as she could make them" — "A lemon-colored or paper-brown [[poolroom]] youngster from Harlem's Fifth Avenue or from Prince Street. A bellboy or railroad [[waiter]] or porter. Sometimes a chocolate who was a quick, nondiscriminating lover and not remote of attitude like the pampered high-browns. But chocolates were always a rarity among Susy's front-roomful of gin-lovers" (007-010).

The economics are exploitative: "Her guests consumed her gin and listened to the phonograph, exchanged rakish stories, and when they felt fruit-ripe to dropping, left her place in pursuit of pleasures elsewhere" (007-010). Susy repeatedly takes in yellow men — "Something all a piece of dirty rags and stench picked up in the street. Cleansed, clothed, and booted it" — only to have them leave "so soon as he got his curly hair straightened" and started "strutting the pavement of Lenox Avenue, feeling smart as a moving-picture [[dandy]]" (007-012).

## Color and Exploitation

Susy is exploited not only by her guests but by the ridicule of the men's peer group: "Apart from Susy's repellent person, no youthful [[sweetman]] attempting to love her could hold out under the ridicule of his pals. Over their games of pool and craps the boys had their cracks at Susy" (007-014). The nickname "Gin-head" itself is an insult conferred by the very men who drink her gin.

Under the influence of her friend [[miss-curdy|Lavinia Curdy]], Susy briefly tries to renounce yellow men. Miss Curdy advises: "I know them from long experience… They never want to work. They're a lazy and shiftless lot. Want to be kept like women" (007-021). But when Susy's latest yellow lover steals "her suitcase and best dresses and pawned even her gas range," her resolution crumbles. "The sociables went on and the same types came to drink the Saturday evenings away" (007-023).

## The Party Scene (Chapter VII)

The novel's fullest rendering of Susy's world occurs in Chapter VII, when a "yellow-skinned youth" invites [[zeddy-plummer|Zeddy]] to Susy's. The party features five young men, a phonograph discharging "brassy [[jazz]] notes," and Susy herself "jerking herself from one side to the other" while "hugging a bottle" on her French heels (007-034). [[miss-curdy|Miss Curdy]] and [[jake-brown|Jake]] join the gathering. The room becomes "a close, live, intense place" with desultory dancing, dice, blackjack, and poker — "A jungle atmosphere pervaded the room, and, like shameless wild animals hungry for raw meat, the females savagely searched the eyes of the males" (007-046).

The party erupts into violence when a chocolate-skinned man, offended, punches a light-brown boy who had been Susy's real object of desire. Susy herself "flung him out in the corridor and slammed the door" (007-056), then immediately puts on the "Tickling Blues" on the phonograph and resumes dancing with Zeddy — a demonstration of the emotional resilience (or callousness) required to survive in her world.

## Zeddy and Susy

After [[zeddy-plummer|Zeddy]] leaves [[gin-head-susy|Susy]] — or rather, after he has been "fed up" with her — he returns to her. By Chapter XII, Zeddy has moved his possessions to Susy's apartment: "Transfer mah suitcase and all mah pohsitions to Susy… Susy ain't nothing to look at like you' fair-brown queen, but she's tur'bly sweet loving… She treats me right. Gimme all I wants to drink and brings home the goodest poke chops and fried chicken" (012-015). Their relationship is transactional — Zeddy gets domestic comfort and gin; Susy gets a man in her apartment. It is, within the novel's terms, one of the more stable arrangements.

## Historical Context

Susy's "yellow complex" reflects the real color hierarchy within early-20th-century Black America — a legacy of slavery's pigmentocracy reinforced by the social preference for lighter skin. Her gin parties document the centrality of bootleg liquor to working-class Black social life during Prohibition. And her serial exploitation by the men she supports mirrors the broader gendered economy McKay documents throughout the novel, where women's wages (from domestic work, entertainment, or sex work) subsidize men's leisure.
