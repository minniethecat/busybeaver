**London** in *Home to [[harlem|Harlem]]* is not the imperial capital of Whitehall and Buckingham Palace but the East End docklands — Limehouse, the [[west-india|West India Docks]], the working-class neighborhoods where Black seamen found work, community, and a precarious freedom. The novel opens with [[jake-brown|Jake]] in London, establishing it as the point of departure for his return to [[harlem|Harlem]].

## Location

### Geography

London's East End encompasses the docklands along the Thames: Limehouse, Poplar, and the [[west-india|West India Docks]] on the Isle of Dogs. By the early twentieth century, this area was home to a multiracial working-class population that included African, Caribbean, Chinese, and South Asian sailors and laborers.

### In the Novel's World

[[jake-brown|Jake]] "crossed over to London" and "found plenty of work there." (001-024) The opening chapter places him in London's working world — "I works in London and I nevah was a [[scabbing|blackleg]]." (005-014) The East End is a place of harsh labor but also of masculine pride: [[jake-brown|Jake]]'s insistence that he "was no [[scabbing|blackleg]]" (strikebreaker) anchors his working-class identity in the London docks.

## Significance to the Narrative

London frames the novel. The first chapter places [[jake-brown|Jake]] in the East End, and the opening dialogue — "Ever been to London?" — establishes the city as a yardstick of masculine experience. (001-020) London is part of a circuit: "[[brest|Brest]]. Le Havre. London. The [[west-india|West India Docks]]. And back home to [[harlem|Harlem]]." (003-034) The journey from London to [[harlem|Harlem]] is the novel's originating movement.

The London sections also establish the novel's theme of wartime labor and desertion. The narrator recalls "hundreds of" Black men "to the East End of London" during the war (001-026), and [[jake-brown|Jake]]'s departure from London is not a straightforward homecoming but an escape: he deserts the merchant marine, drawn by the pull of [[harlem|Harlem]].

## The East End World

London's East End recurs in memory and conversation throughout the novel. Characters speak of "[[brest|Brest]] at Havre and in London" (008-009), and the "[[west-india|West India]] [[uncle-doc|Doc]]k districts of London" figure in the novel's concluding panoramas. (019-005) One character complains about "womens ovah theah in London," weaving the city into the novel's sexual geography. (019-026) In the novel's final pages, the narrator returns to "[[brest|Brest]], London, and his America" as the defining triad of [[jake-brown|Jake]]'s consciousness. (021-071)

## From the Novel

A postwar race riot and its aftermath drive Jake back across the Atlantic:

> Jake saw a big battle staged between the colored and white men of London's East End. Fisticuffs, razor and knife and gun play. For three days his woman would not let him out-of-doors. And when it was all over he was seized with the awful fever of lonesomeness.
>
> (001-026)

## Historical Context

During World War I, London's East End docks were a crucial hub for the British merchant marine, drawing labor from across the empire. Black seamen from the Caribbean and West Africa formed communities around Cable Street and Limehouse, often intermarrying with local white women. These communities faced periodic racial violence — notably the 1919 race riots that swept British port cities — but also offered a degree of economic opportunity unavailable in the segregated United States. McKay himself lived in London in 1919–1920, working for the socialist newspaper *Workers' Dreadnought*, and his knowledge of the East End is grounded in direct experience.
