**Sah Farrel** is a minor character in [[claude-mckay|Claude McKay]]'s 1928 novel *[[home-to-harlem|Home to Harlem]]*, appearing in Chapter XII. His name is a dialect rendering of "Sir Farrel" or "Mr. Farrell," spoken by other characters in the novel's characteristic [[harlem|Harlem]] vernacular.

## In the Novel

Sah Farrel appears exclusively in Chapter XII, which is set in the kitchen and pantry of a dining car on the Pennsylvania Railroad. He is addressed with deferential language by the African American chef and waiters who work under him. The chapter opens with a dispute among the kitchen staff, and the pantryman declares: "Ain't any of us waiters gwine to stay on heah Mis'r Farrel, with a chef like this." (012-051)

The chef, in defending himself against accusations from the waiters, repeatedly appeals to Sah Farrel as the authority figure:

> "Nothing at all, Sah Farrel. I done pull a good bull on them fellars, tha's all. Cause theyse all trying to get mah goat. L'em quit fooling with the kitchen, Sah Farrel. I does mah wuk and I don't want no fooling fwom them nigger waiters, nohow."
>
> (012-053)

Later, when an argument about missing eggs escalates, the chef pleads with Farrel again: "Cut them aiggs off o' the bill, Sah Farrel. O Lawd! Ise sartain sure I brought them aiggs on the car mahself, and now I don't know where they is." (012-089) The chef's distress culminates in a half-roared, half-moaned ultimatum: "Sah Farrel, leave me alone, I say, or I'll jump off right now and let you run you' kitchen you'self." (012-105)

The repeated honorific "Sah" (dialectal "Sir") and the fact that the entire kitchen staff directs their grievances and defenses to him suggests Farrel occupies a supervisory position — likely the steward or manager of the dining car. The limited corpus coverage places him among the novel's many walk-on characters who populate McKay's vivid portrait of 1920s Black working life, in this case the world of railroad dining cars that employed many African Americans during the period.
