**Philadelphia** is a [[pennsylvania-railroad|Pennsy]]lvania city that appears in [[claude-mckay|Claude McKay]]'s *Home to [[harlem|Harlem]]* as a recurring stop on [[jake-brown|Jake]]'s travels — a port and railroad city where he finds adventure, gossip, and romantic possibilities. It is one of several East Coast cities that form [[jake-brown|Jake]]'s geographic circuit outside [[harlem|Harlem]].

## Location

### In the Novel's World

Philadelphia is rendered through specific streets and landmarks: a "house in [[fifteenth-street|Fifteenth Street]]" (013-057) and "the great Philadelphia yards" with "five hundred tracks." (013-099) The railroad infrastructure — particularly the massive rail yards — dominates the city's portrayal, reflecting its role as a major transportation hub.

## Significance to the Narrative

Philadelphia appears as a site of nightlife and intrigue. "One night in Philadelphia [[jake-brown|Jake]] breezed into the waiters' club" (013-002), suggesting a social world parallel to [[harlem|Harlem]]'s. The city is also a source of gossip: "the places he had nosed out in Baltimore and Philadelphia." (014-060) A romantic interest is described as "laying over in Philadelphia" (016-004), and love letters arrive from there: "Philadelphia is lonesome without you." (016-006)

However, by the novel's closing, Philadelphia is listed among the cities the protagonist has had "enough to vomit up of" (018-002), alongside Baltimore, [[pittsburgh|Pittsburgh]], and [[washington|Washington]] — suggesting the weariness of the constant traveler with the repeated landscapes of industrial America.

## From the Novel

Jake's restless arrival in Philadelphia is rendered in a single narrative stroke:

> One night in Philadelphia Jake breezed into the waiters' quarters in Market Street, looking for Ray. It was late. Ray was in bed. Jake pulled him up.
>
> (013-002)

## Historical Context

Philadelphia was a major destination during the Great Migration, with its Black population growing significantly in the 1910s–1920s. The city's rail yards and port facilities employed many Black workers, and its Black social world — like those of Baltimore, [[washington|Washington]], and New York — supported a network of clubs, entertainments, and community institutions that travelers like [[jake-brown|Jake]] could access.

## Appearances

Philadelphia appears or is referenced in chapters 12–14, 16, and 18. (012-084)–(018-002)
