# Jake Brown

Jake Brown is the protagonist of [[Claude McKay]]'s novel *[[Home to Harlem]]* (1928) — a tall, dark-skinned Black [[longshoreman]] and U.S. Army deserter whose physical, instinctual navigation of [[Harlem]]'s nightlife and labor markets drives the novel's episodic structure.

As the narrative's center of gravity, Jake filters every significant location ([[Harlem]], [[london|London]], [[Pittsburgh]], [[Chicago]]), social world ([[cabaret-culture|cabaret]], dock, [[buffet-flat]], [[the-railroad|railroad]]), and relationship through his bodily consciousness — making him McKay's vehicle for exploring working-class Black masculinity in all its sensuality and violence.

## Role in the Novel

Jake opens the novel as a deserter aboard a freighter returning to [[harlem|Harlem]] after two years abroad — first as a soldier in [[france|France]], then as a [[longshoreman]] in [[london|London]]. His defining desire is to find "them little brown legs" of a woman he spent one night with at [[the-baltimore-cabaret|the Baltimore]] [[cabaret-culture|cabaret]] before shipping out: "Take me home to [[harlem|Harlem]], Mister Ship! Take me home to [[harlem|Harlem]]!" (001-008). This quest — for the "little lost brown of [[the-baltimore-cabaret|the Baltimore]]" — provides the novel's loose narrative thread, though Jake's attention frequently wanders to other women ([[rose-congo|Rose]], [[rose-congo|Congo Rose]], [[gin-head-susy|Gin-head Susy]]), other [[pleasure-seeking|pleasure]]s, and other jobs.

Jake is deliberately not an intellectual. His foil [[ray]] — a Haitian writer and [[pullman-company|Pullman]] [[waiter]] he meets on [[the-railroad|the railroad]] — embodies the life of the mind that Jake instinctively distrusts. Where [[ray|Ray]] reads [[sappho-daudet|Sappho and Daudet]] and wrestles with racial ideology, Jake "lived the life" (012-015) without abstracting it. This binary — body versus mind, intuition versus analysis — is the novel's central philosophical tension, and Jake is its embodied thesis.

## Characterization

### Appearance

McKay establishes Jake's physical presence in the novel's opening pages with compressed intensity: "Jake was tall, brawny, and black" (001-009). The triple adjective — height, musculature, skin tone — defines Jake's body as his primary social asset and identity marker. His dark skin is significant in a novel deeply preoccupied with the [[race-and-color|color]] hierarchy within Black communities; Jake is unambiguously dark, and his attractiveness to women across the [[race-and-color|color]] spectrum implicitly challenges the period's valorization of lighter skin.

His body bears the marks of his trade: "rough, calloused, hard and broad palms" from longshore work. Yet he is also a figure of working-class [[fashion-and-style|style]] — "wearing a steel-gray English suit" (002-006) on his first night back in [[harlem|Harlem]] — a gesture of "[[the-sporting-world|sporting life]]" [[fashion-and-style|dandy]]ism that signals both self-respect and participation in [[harlem|Harlem]]'s aesthetic economy of [[pleasure-seeking|pleasure]].

> Jake was tall, brawny, and black. (001-009)

The novel's definitive physical description — in three words, establishing body, [[race-and-color|race]], and physical power as Jake's essential coordinates.

### Personality

Jake's defining trait is his natural ease — a confidence that allows him to cross social boundaries without pretension or anxiety. "That was his natural way, wherever he went, whatever new people he met. It had helped him over many a bad crossing" (008-009). He is generous with money when he has it, sexually magnetic, and capable of both romantic tenderness and casual cruelty.

His moral code is working-class and individualist: he refuses to [[scabbing|scab]] during a dock strike despite being excluded from the union, asserting "I ain't a joiner kind of fellah" (005-017). He has class solidarity without institutional loyalty — a distinctly unaffiliated ethics that reflects McKay's own ambivalence toward organized labor and political movements.

> "Nope, I won't scab, but I ain't a joiner kind of fellah." (005-017)

Jake's moral self-definition in five words of dialogue — class solidarity without institutional commitment.

### Relationships

- **[[zeddy-plummer|Zeddy Plummer]]** — Childhood friend from [[virginia|Virginia]] and fellow [[longshoreman]]; their friendship turns to rivalry over women (especially [[rose-congo|Rose]]) and social standing, dramatizing the fragility of male solidarity under sexual competition.
- **[[rose-congo|Rose]]** — [[cabaret|Cabaret]] singer at [[the-congo|the Congo]] with whom Jake lives during the novel's middle chapters; their domestic arrangement (she supports him financially) reverses gender expectations and exposes Jake's casual entitlement.
- **[[felice|Felice]]** — The "little lost brown of [[the-baltimore-cabaret|the Baltimore]]" who Jake pursues across the novel; their reunion in [[chicago|Chicago]] (Chapter XX) provides the novel's romantic resolution and a gesture toward domestic futurity.
- **[[ray|Ray]]** — The Haitian intellectual Jake meets working as a [[pullman-company|Pullman]] cook; their cross-[[race-and-color|race]], cross-class friendship is the novel's most sustained relationship and its central philosophical dialogue.
- **[[billy-biasse|Billy Biasse]]** — Fellow [[longshoreman]] and host of a [[lenox-avenue|Lenox Avenue]] [[gambling]] joint; represents the informal economy of [[harlem|Harlem]]'s working-class social world.

## Themes

**Working-Class Black Masculinity**: Jake embodies an unapologetically physical mode of being — sexual, violent, tender, economically precarious but socially central. McKay neither idealizes nor condemns; he presents Jake's contradictions without resolution. Jake can be generous ("he gave the singer fifty cents" (002-009)) and casually cruel (his treatment of [[rose-congo|Rose]]), and the novel refuses to reconcile these.

**The Body/Mind Binary**: Jake's relationship with [[ray|Ray]] dramatizes the novel's central philosophical tension. Jake "lived the life" — his knowledge is somatic, intuitive — while [[ray|Ray]] reads, thinks, and abstracts. Neither position is presented as superior; their friendship is built on mutual respect across difference.

**[[migration|Migration]] Geography**: Jake's arc — rural [[virginia|Virginia]] → wartime [[france|France]] → working-class [[london|London]] → [[harlem|Harlem]] → [[chicago|Chicago]] — maps the [[great-migration|Great Migration]]'s urban geography. His restlessness ("I'm searching for something new" (001-017)) is both personal and structural, reflecting the economic imperatives and psychic dislocations of [[great-migration|Black migration]] in the early twentieth century.

**Colorism & Desire**: Jake's dark skin and his attractiveness to women across the [[race-and-color|color]] spectrum implicitly challenge the period's pigmentocracy. The novel registers but does not resolve the [[race-and-color|color]] tensions within Black social life, and Jake's body is a site where these tensions are both enacted and complicated.

Jake's ethos in five words — restlessness as identity, the migrant's perpetual dissatisfaction with stasis.
