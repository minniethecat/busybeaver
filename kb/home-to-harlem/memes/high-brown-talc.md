# High-Brown Talc

A cosmetic product mentioned in *Home to [[harlem|Harlem]]* that condenses the intersection of beauty culture, consumer capitalism, and colorism in 1920s Black life. High-Brown Talc powder appears in a single detail at Madame Laura's buffet flat, where it heightens the complexions of the women being entertained.

## Definition

High-Brown Talc was a face powder marketed to African American women in the early twentieth century. Like other "skin-lightening" or "complexion-enhancing" products of the era — Overton's High-Brown Face Powder, Black and White Powder, Nadinola — it occupied an ambiguous position between cosmetic enhancement and color hierarchy. The name itself encodes the valuation: "High-Brown" (lighter) is the desirable end of the spectrum; the product promises to move the user toward it.

## Cultural Context

### In the Novel

McKay's single mention of High-Brown Talc occurs in Chapter XIII at Madame Laura's buffet flat, where "a big-built, very black man was amusing himself with two attractive girls, one brown-skinned and the other yellow. The girls' complexion was heightened by High-Brown Talc powder and rouge" (013-031).

The detail is doubly revealing. First, it documents the specific commodity culture of 1920s [[harlem|Harlem]] beauty practices — these are not generic cosmetics but a named brand that McKay's readers would recognize. Second, the verb "heightened" captures the ambiguity of the product: is it simply enhancing natural complexion, or is it lightening it? The girls are already "brown-skinned" and "yellow"; the powder intensifies rather than transforms. But the name "High-Brown" — promising elevation through lightness — embeds the color hierarchy within the product itself.

### Consumer Culture Context

The Black cosmetics industry flourished during the [[harlem-renaissance|Harlem Renaissance]], led by entrepreneurs like Madame C.J. Walker and Annie Turnbo Malone. These products operated in a contested cultural space. On one hand, Black-owned cosmetics companies represented economic empowerment and self-definition — beauty products made by and for Black women, in an era when mainstream cosmetics ignored or denigrated Black consumers. On the other hand, many products explicitly or implicitly promised to lighten skin, reinforcing the very color hierarchy that Black intellectuals and activists were working to dismantle.

McKay's mention of High-Brown Talc registers this contradiction without resolving it. The powder is a fact of the scene, a material detail that situates the women in a consumer economy — and its name quietly testifies to the color politics that economy encoded.
