# Prohibition

The Eighteenth Amendment to the U.S. Constitution (ratified 1919, effective 1920) and the Volstead Act that enforced it, banning the manufacture, sale, and transportation of intoxicating liquors. In *Home to [[harlem|Harlem]]*, Prohibition is the legal-economic condition that makes every [[cabaret-culture|cabaret]], [[buffet-flat|buffet flat]], and [[speakeasy]] an illicit enterprise and every drink an act of defiance.

## Definition

Prohibition was the result of decades of temperance activism, ratified as a wartime measure and implemented through the Volstead Act. It outlawed the production, importation, transportation, and sale of [[gin-whisky|alcohol]]ic beverages throughout the United States from 1920 until its repeal by the Twenty-first Amendment in 1933. In practice, Prohibition created a vast illegal economy of bootlegging, [[speakeasy|speakeasies]], and organized crime while doing little to reduce actual [[gin-whisky|alcohol]] consumption. (021-011)

## Cultural Context

### Harlem Renaissance Context

Prohibition had paradoxical effects on Black [[harlem|Harlem]]. On one hand, it criminalized the nightlife economy that was one of [[harlem|Harlem]]'s few engines of Black economic independence. Police raids on Black-owned [[cabaret-culture|cabarets]] — like [[the-baltimore-cabaret|the Baltimore]] in the novel — were a constant threat, and enforcement was racially discriminatory: white-owned venues with Tammany Hall protection operated freely while Black venues were more vulnerable.

On the other hand, Prohibition drove white "slummers" uptown to [[harlem|Harlem]] in unprecedented numbers, creating an interracial nightlife economy that, however exploitative, generated significant revenue within the Black community. The [[speakeasy]] economy also created entrepreneurial opportunities for Black women — figures like McKay's fictional [[madame-suarez|Madame Suarez]] — who could run illegal drinking establishments from their apartments.

### In the Novel

Prohibition is the invisible infrastructure of the novel's entire nightlife world. Every drink consumed — from the "beer and wine" and "plenty of [[gin-whisky|hard liquor]]" at the [[buffet-flat|buffet flat]] (002-028) to the champagne bought by flashy white men at [[madame-suarez-buffet-flat|Madame Suarez's]] (008-012) — is an illegal commodity. The constant threat of police raids (dramatized in [[the-baltimore-cabaret|the Baltimore]]'s closure) creates the tension that underlies every nightlife scene.

[[jake-brown|Jake]]'s toast at [[aunt-hattie|Aunt Hattie]]'s dinner table makes the connection explicit: "'Le's hope that this heah Gawd's own don't shut the pub in our face.' Prohibition is right under our tail" (018-033). The phrase "right under our tail" captures Prohibition as a constant, pursuing presence — something that follows Black [[harlem|Harlem]]ites through every pleasure-seeking act.

The novel's closing scene, set as "Prohibition was on the threshold of the country and drinking was becoming a luxury" (021-011), frames the entire narrative within the Prohibition era. Drinking has become "a luxury" — the illegal status of [[gin-whisky|alcohol]] has transformed it from an everyday commodity into a marker of sophistication and defiance.

## Key Passages

> "You had better said, 'Le's hope that this heah Gawd's own don't shut the pub in our face,'" replied Jake. "Prohibition is right under our tail." (018-033)

[[jake-brown|Jake]]'s correction of [[billy-biasse|Billy Biasse]]'s more innocent toast reveals his awareness of the legal reality that shadows their drinking. [[billy-biasse|Billy]] hopes for luck; [[jake-brown|Jake]] names the structural condition — Prohibition — that makes luck necessary. The bodily metaphor ("right under our tail") conveys the intimacy of state surveillance.

> Prohibition was on the threshold of the country and drinking was becoming a luxury, but all the joy-pacers of the Belt who adore the novel and the fashionable and had a dollar to burn had come together in a body to fill the new cabaret. (021-011)

McKay's narrator historicizes the scene — Prohibition is arriving, transforming drinking from a working-class custom into an act of conspicuous consumption. Those with "a dollar to burn" embrace the transformation, turning the new legal constraint into a marker of status.

## Historical Significance

Prohibition reshaped American society in ways that far outlasted its thirteen-year legal span. It catalyzed the rise of organized crime, transformed women's relationship to public drinking ([[speakeasy|speakeasies]] were among the first public spaces where women drank alongside men), and created the cultural infrastructure — nightclubs, cocktail culture, [[jazz]] venues — that defined American urban entertainment for the rest of the 20th century. For Black communities, Prohibition's repeal in 1933 was a mixed blessing: it eliminated the illegal economy that had provided Black entrepreneurs with rare business opportunities, while returning the liquor trade to white-controlled corporate distribution networks. The [[speakeasy]] era left a lasting imprint on African American cultural memory as a time when, despite its dangers and exploitations, Black nightlife entrepreneurs built something autonomous and world-changing.
