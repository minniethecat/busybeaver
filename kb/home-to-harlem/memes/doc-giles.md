# Doc Giles

A figure from [[brooklyn|Brooklyn]]'s pre-war Black nightlife, mentioned in a single breath by [[strawberry-lips|Strawberry Lips]] as the proprietor of a gambling house on [[myrtle-avenue|Myrtle Avenue]]. Like [[elijah-bowers|Elijah Bowers]], Doc Giles exists in the novel only as a name preserved by oral memory — a t[[race-and-color|race]] of the entertainment infrastructure that flourished before [[harlem|Harlem]] became the center of Black nightlife.

## Role in the Novel

Doc Giles appears solely in [[strawberry-lips|Strawberry Lips]]'s monologue on [[myrtle-avenue|Myrtle Avenue]], when he recalls the street's vanished glory: "[[myrtle-avenue|Myrtle Avenue]] used to be a be-be itching of a place, when Doc Giles had his gambling house on there and [[elijah-bowers|Elijah Bowers]] was running his [[cabaret-culture|cabaret]]" (006-087).

The pairing of Giles and [[elijah-bowers|Bowers]] — gambling house and [[cabaret-culture|cabaret]], side by side on the same street — sketches a complete entertainment ecosystem. The gambling house and the [[cabaret-culture|cabaret]] were complementary institutions in Black nightlife: one centered on games and money ([[poolroom|poolrooms]], card tables, the [[numbers-game|numbers game]]), the other on music, drinking, and performance. Together, they made [[myrtle-avenue|Myrtle Avenue]] "a be-be itching of a place" — a phrase that in [[strawberry-lips|Strawberry Lips]]'s vernacular conveys both excitement and danger.

## Characterization

Doc Giles is characterized by his title ("[[uncle-doc|Doc]]"), his trade (gambling), and his location ([[myrtle-avenue|Myrtle Avenue]]). The honorific "[[uncle-doc|Doc]]" — like "Madame" for women entrepreneurs — suggests a figure of some standing in the community, whether earned through professional [[the-railroad|train]]ing or adopted as a marker of respect. The gambling house he ran would have been one node in the network of informal Black economic institutions that sustained working-class communities outside the formal (and racially restricted) banking and credit systems.

His name is preserved because [[strawberry-lips|Strawberry Lips]] remembers him — the novel's oral historian, whose monologue functions as an archive of institutions that left no written record. Doc Giles exists because someone told a story about him.

## Key Passages

> "Myrtle Avenue used to be a be-be itching of a place," said Strawberry Lips, "when Doc Giles had his gambling house on there and Elijah Bowers was running his cabaret." (006-087)

## Historical Significance

Doc Giles and [[elijah-bowers|Elijah Bowers]] together represent the **pre-[[harlem|Harlem]] geography of Black nightlife** — a world centered on [[myrtle-avenue|Myrtle Avenue]] in [[brooklyn|Brooklyn]] and [[fifty-third-street|Fifty-third Street]] in Manhattan, before the great northward [[migration]] of Black entertainment to [[harlem|Harlem]] in the 1920s. Their presence in [[strawberry-lips|Strawberry Lips]]'s oral history preserves a map of Black [[pleasure-seeking|pleasure]] and entrepreneurship that predates the [[harlem-renaissance|Harlem Renaissance]].

The gambling house as an institution was central to Black working-class economic life. In an era when Black access to banks, loans, and formal credit was severely restricted, gambling — particularly the [[numbers-game|numbers game]] — functioned as an alternative financial system, circulating money through the community and occasionally producing the kind of windfall that could fund a business or a [[migration]].
