Jerco, whose full name is Jeremiah Jones, is a tragic figure in [[claude-mckay|Claude McKay]]'s *Home to [[harlem|Harlem]]*, appearing in chapter 17. His story, narrated by a first-person observer, follows his relationship with [[rosalind|Rosalind]] and ends in his suicide after her death.

## Role in the Novel

Jerco appears in a self-contained episode narrated in the first person by a [[saloon]] observer. He and his partner [[rosalind|Rosalind]] (also called Rosaline) frequent [[ma-lawton|Ma Lawton]]'s establishment, where their troubled relationship unfolds. Jerco's story serves as a counterpoint to the novel's main narrative, illustrating the precariousness of Black life and love in [[harlem|Harlem]], where poverty, illness, and despair can culminate in tragedy.

## Characterization

### Appearance

Jerco is described indirectly through his actions and the narrator's observations. He is noted as being unable to pay for [[food-and-dining|food]] and drink, pawning his watch chain and other possessions to get by. (017-034) After [[rosalind|Rosalind]]'s death, he is described kneeling by an open wardrobe with "doglike" eyes regarding his possessions. (017-048)

### Personality and Traits

Jerco is devoted to [[rosalind|Rosalind]] despite their hardships. He brings her ice-cream and slips her beer even when [[ma-lawton|Ma Lawton]] tries to prevent it for health reasons. (017-019)(017-037) He is also whining and pitiable — when told they might have to make out without money, "Jerco whined. He was sitting there with a face like a whipped dog." (017-030) His deep attachment to [[rosalind|Rosalind]] ultimately leads to his suicide after her death. (017-066)

### Relationships

- [[rosalind|Rosalind]] (Rosaline) — Jerco's partner, who falls ill and dies; Jerco is devoted to her
- [[ma-lawton|Ma Lawton]] — proprietor of the saloon where Jerco and [[rosalind|Rosalind]] spend time; she tolerates them but is "fidgety" about their presence (017-013)
- The narrator — a first-person observer who works at the saloon and witnesses the tragedy

## Key Scenes

Jerco and [[rosalind|Rosalind]] first appear at [[ma-lawton|Ma Lawton]]'s saloon, where "Strange enough, too, Jerco and [[rosalind|Rosalind]] took a liking to each other." (017-009) One afternoon Jerco comes in with money and the narrator changes clothes upstairs. (017-012)

As [[rosalind|Rosalind]] grows ill, tensions rise. [[ma-lawton|Ma Lawton]] tries to prevent [[rosalind|Rosalind]] from drinking beer, "But Jerco slipped it in to her when [[ma-lawton|Ma Lawton]]'s back was turned." (017-037) Jerco pawns his watch chain and other valuables to keep them afloat. (017-034)

The tragedy culminates when the narrator finds Jerco alone in the back room after [[rosalind|Rosalind]]'s death: "I saw Jerco kneeling down by the open wardrobe... Jerco regarded the lot with doglike eyes." (017-047) Later, Jerco cuts his throat. (017-066)

## Themes

Jerco's story embodies themes of love and desperation under poverty, the fragility of Black life in [[harlem|Harlem]], and the toll of economic precarity on intimate relationships. His suicide after [[rosalind|Rosalind]]'s death represents the ultimate cost of a society that provided no safety net for its most vulnerable residents.
