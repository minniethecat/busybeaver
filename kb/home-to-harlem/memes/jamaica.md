**Jamaica** is referenced in Claude McKay's *Home to Harlem* as one of the Caribbean sources of the West Indian immigrant community in Harlem. As a British colony at the time of the novel's setting, Jamaica was home to many of the Black seamen and laborers who migrated to both London and New York, forming part of the novel's transnational Black diaspora.

## West Indian Presence in Harlem

The novel depicts tensions and solidarities between African Americans and West Indian immigrants, with Jamaicans forming a significant part of the latter group. A character is described in terms of her Jamaican origin:

> "…buxom Negress from Jamaica." (020-042)

This brief reference situates Jamaica within the novel's broader mapping of Black diasporic identity in Harlem, where immigrants from the British Caribbean navigated distinct social positions.

## Claude McKay's Jamaican Roots

Claude McKay himself was born in Jamaica in 1889 and emigrated to the United States in 1912. His Jamaican origins informed his perspective on Harlem's immigrant communities, and his depiction of West Indian characters reflects both insider knowledge and the complexities of intra-racial dynamics between native-born African Americans and Caribbean immigrants.

## Jamaica and the Transatlantic Circuit

Like other Caribbean locations referenced in the novel, Jamaica is part of the maritime geography that connects the novel's settings — from the West India Docks in London to the piers of Harlem. Jamaican seamen, laborers, and immigrants populate the novel's working-class world, contributing to the cosmopolitan character of Harlem's Black community.
