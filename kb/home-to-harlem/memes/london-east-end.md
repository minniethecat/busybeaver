**London's East End** is the docklands district along the Thames — encompassing [[west-india|West India Docks]], Limehouse, and Poplar — where [[jake-brown|Jake]] works, carouses, and lives among a multiracial working-class community before deserting the merchant marine to return to [[harlem|Harlem]]. The East End is the novel's first setting: the opening dialogue — "Ever been to London?" — launches [[jake-brown|Jake]]'s journey homeward from East End soil.

## Location

### Geography

London's East End encompasses the historically working-class districts east of the City of London: Limehouse, Poplar, Stepney, and the Isle of Dogs with the [[west-india|West India Docks]]. By the early twentieth century, this area was home to a multiracial population of sailors, dockworkers, and laborers drawn from across the British Empire — including significant communities of Black Caribbean and West African seamen who had settled around Cable Street and the docks.

### In the Novel's World

For [[jake-brown|Jake]], the East End is a place of work, masculine community, and relative racial freedom. "He was happy in the East End" (001-024) — the simple declarative captures the district's importance as a space where Jake could earn a living and enjoy himself without the constant pressure of American-style segregation. "Jake crossed over to London. He found plenty of work there" (001-024) — the East End docks were an economic engine that absorbed Black labor, part of a broader transatlantic working circuit that connected [[brest|Brest]], [[le-havre|Le Havre]], and [[harlem|Harlem]].

## Significance to the Narrative

The East End frames the novel's opening. The first chapter establishes [[jake-brown|Jake]]'s East End experience as the immediate backdrop against which his return to [[harlem|Harlem]] is measured. His assertion that "I works in London and I nevah was a [[scabbing|blackleg]]" (005-014) — spoken later in [[harlem|Harlem]] — carries his East End working-class identity across the Atlantic, anchoring his masculine self-respect in the London docks.

The East End also introduces the novel's theme of racial encounter in wartime. The narrator describes how WWI "brought many more black men to the East End of London. Hundreds of them" (001-026), framing the district as a gathering point for the Black diaspora during the war. The "big battle staged between the colored and white men of London's East End" (001-026) — the 1919 race riots that swept British port cities — marks the East End as a site of both multiracial community and racial violence, a duality that recurs throughout the novel's geography.

The East End remains a reference point throughout the novel. Characters speak casually of "[[brest|Brest]] at Havre and in London" (008-009), and the "[[west-india|West India]] Dock districts of London" (019-005) appear in the novel's panoramic surveys of Black maritime geography. In the novel's closing pages, the narrator returns to "[[brest|Brest]], London, and his America" (021-071) as the defining triad of [[jake-brown|Jake]]'s consciousness — London, and specifically the East End, is one-third of his geographic identity.

## The East End World

The East End's distinctive social world was shaped by its docks. "Fights between black men and white" broke out in the pubs and streets (003-030), but so did friendships and romances. [[jake-brown|Jake]]'s fondness for the East End was remembered vividly as he was departing: he recalled "Brest at Havre and in London" (008-009) — the three ports forming a single memory of masculine adventure. The East End was the English-speaking pole of this circuit, the place where Jake's labor identity was forged before he carried it home to [[harlem|Harlem]].

## Historical Context

During World War I, London's East End docks were a crucial hub for the British merchant marine, drawing labor from across the empire. Black seamen from the Caribbean and West Africa formed communities around Cable Street and Limehouse, often intermarrying with local white women. These communities faced periodic racial violence — notably the 1919 race riots that swept British port cities including London, Liverpool, Cardiff, and Glasgow — but also offered a degree of economic opportunity unavailable in the segregated United States.

McKay himself lived in London in 1919–1920, working for the socialist newspaper *Workers' Dreadnought* and moving through the East End's radical political circles. His portrait of the East End is grounded in direct observation: the docks, the multiracial working class, the tension between solidarity and racial violence that characterized the district in the war's aftermath.
