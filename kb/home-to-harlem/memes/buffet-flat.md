# Buffet Flat

A type of informal private club operating out of residential apartments in 1920s [[harlem|Harlem]], where patrons could drink, gamble, dance, and socialize during [[prohibition|Prohibition]]. More intimate than public [[cabaret-culture|cabarets]], buffet flats were central to [[harlem|Harlem]]'s underground nightlife economy.

## Definition

A "buffet flat" was an apartment converted into an illicit entertainment venue during [[prohibition|Prohibition]]-era [[harlem|Harlem]]. Unlike public [[cabaret-culture|cabarets]], buffet flats were private spaces — often run by women — that offered a combination of liquor, [[gambling]], music, and sometimes sex work in a domestic setting. The term was distinctive to [[harlem|Harlem]]'s Black [[the-sporting-world|sporting world]], marking a space where the [[race-and-color|color]] line operated differently than in white-owned establishments. (002-028)

## Cultural Context

### Harlem Renaissance Context

During the 1920s, [[harlem|Harlem]]'s nightlife was a central expression of Black cultural vitality. While [[cabaret-culture|cabarets]] like [[the-baltimore-cabaret|the Baltimore]] and [[the-congo|the Congo]] were public-facing institutions, buffet flats represented a more exclusive, intimate tier of the nightlife economy. They operated under the radar of [[prohibition|Prohibition]] enforcement and allowed Black proprietors — particularly women — to build independent businesses. (008-004)

The buffet flat sat at the intersection of several [[harlem|Harlem]] Renaissance themes: the underground economy created by [[prohibition|Prohibition]], the agency of Black women entrepreneurs, the mixing of classes and [[race-and-color|race]]s in private spaces, and the creation of Black-controlled social institutions. (008-011)

### In the Novel

McKay uses buffet flats as key settings throughout *Home to [[harlem|Harlem]]*. They appear at multiple levels of [[harlem|Harlem]] society. The most prominent is **Madame Adeline Suarez's** buffet flat, which [[jake-brown|Jake]] accesses through his connection with [[rose-congo|Rose]] — a mark of his rising social status (008-006). [[madame-suarez|Madame Suarez]] "catered to sporty [[race-and-color|color]]ed persons of consequence only and certain groups of downtown whites" (008-007), positioning her flat at the top of the buffet-flat hierarchy.

In Chapter V, [[jake-brown|Jake]] and [[zeddy-plummer|Zeddy]] visit a different kind of buffet flat on 140th Street, where "there was bigger game than sex" — the copper-hued proprietress runs a [[gambling]] operation (005-035). This flat serves a working-class clientele, contrasting with [[madame-suarez-buffet-flat|Madame Suarez's]] elegant establishment.

The narrator's taxonomy of [[harlem|Harlem]] nightlife in Chapter XIII distinguishes "them of the [[dickty]] buffet flats" from "the low-down [[speakeasy]] customers" and "the [[cabaret-culture|cabaret]] babies" (013-098), placing buffet flats in an intermediate social position — above cheap [[speakeasy|speakeasies]] but below or alongside the most exclusive venues.

## Key Passages

> They went to a buffet flat on 137th Street. The proprietress opened the door without removing the chain and peeked out. She was a matronly mulatto woman. She recognized the girl, who had put herself in front of Jake, and she slid back the chain and said, "Come right in." (002-028)

This passage from Chapter II illustrates buffet flat security: chained doors, screened entry, known patrons only — all necessary for [[prohibition|Prohibition]]-era survival.

> Here Jake brushed against big men of the colored sporting world and their white friends. That strange un-American world where colored meets and mingles freely and naturally with white in amusement basements, buffet flats, poker establishments. (008-011)

McKay's narrator frames the buffet flat as part of a "strange un-American world" — a space where racial mixing occurred naturally, in defiance of American segregation norms.

## Variations & Related Terms

- **[[speakeasy|Speakeasy]]** — A broader term for any illicit drinking establishment; buffet flats were a subset, operating out of residential apartments (008-003)
- **[[cabaret|Cabaret]]** — Public entertainment venues like [[the-baltimore-cabaret|the Baltimore]] and [[the-congo|the Congo]]; larger, more visible, and more vulnerable to police raids than private buffet flats (004-002)
- **[[dickty|Dickty]]** — An adjective applied to high-class buffet flats ("[[dickty]] buffet flats"), indicating their association with [[harlem|Harlem]]'s social elite (013-098)
- **"Nifty"** — Slang for "elegant" or "stylish," used to describe private rendezvous apartments associated with buffet flat culture (008-004)

## Historical Significance

Buffet flats were a real phenomenon of [[prohibition|Prohibition]]-era [[harlem|Harlem]], documented in historical accounts of the period. They represented an economic niche for Black women entrepreneurs at a time when legal business opportunities were severely restricted by both racism and sexism. The buffet flat allowed women like the fictional [[madame-suarez|Madame Suarez]] to achieve a measure of economic independence and social power within the cons[[the-railroad|train]]ts of the era.
