# Muscatel

A sweet, fortified wine that appears in *Home to [[harlem|Harlem]]* as the drink of choice at [[buffet-flat|buffet flats]] and gatherings — a specific detail that anchors the novel's nightlife scenes in the material culture of 1920s [[harlem|Harlem]]. Alongside [[gin-whisky|gin]], Muscatel was one of the accessible [[gin-whisky|alcohol]]s that defined [[prohibition|Prohibition]]-era drinking in Black working-class communities.

## Definition

Muscatel is a sweet fortified wine made from Muscat grapes, typically from California, with an [[gin-whisky|alcohol]] content around 15–20%. During [[prohibition|Prohibition]], wines like Muscatel were produced legally for "sacramental" or "medicinal" purposes and found their way into the illicit market. Compared to [[gin-whisky|whiskey]] and [[gin-whisky|gin]], wine was easier to obtain and less likely to be dangerously adulterated — though bootleg Muscatel varied widely in quality and safety.

## Cultural Context

### In the Novel

McKay places a bottle of Muscatel on the table at the gathering in [[madame-laura|Madame Laura]]'s [[buffet-flat|buffet flat]] in Chapter XIII: "A bottle of Muscatel stood on the table. The man was well [[fashion-and-style|dress]]ed in nigger-brown and he wore an expensive diamond ring on his little finger" (013-031). The wine is a class marker — not the rough [[gin-whisky|gin]] of the working-class saloon, not the champagne of the elite [[cabaret-culture|cabaret]], but a middle-tier drink appropriate to the [[buffet-flat|buffet flat]]'s ambiguous social status.

The detail is characteristic of McKay's documentary method: specific commodities (Muscatel, nigger-brown [[fashion-and-style|suits]], [[high-brown-talc|High-Brown Talc]] powder) locate the novel's characters in a precise economic stratum. Muscatel costs more than bathtub [[gin-whisky|gin]] but less than imported [[france|French]] wine; it signals enough disposable income for a bottle but not enough for a champagne bucket.

### Prohibition Context

Under [[prohibition|Prohibition]], the legal exceptions for sacramental and medicinal [[gin-whisky|alcohol]] created an enormous gray market. Wineries that had produced communion wine for Catholic and Jewish congregations suddenly found demand exploding; "sacramental" Muscatel flowed into [[speakeasy|speakeasies]] and [[buffet-flat|buffet flats]] across the country. In [[harlem|Harlem]], where enforcement was laxer than in white neighborhoods, wine and liquor were relatively easy to obtain — a fact that McKay's detailed inventories of drinking scenes documents without explicit commentary.
