# Madison Avenue

Madison Avenue is a major north-south thoroughfare in Manhattan, appearing in [[claude-mckay|Claude McKay]]'s *[[Home to Harlem]]* in a single yet significant passage in the novel's final chapter, where [[jake-brown|Jake]] and [[felice|Felice]] walk its length before turning east toward the Block Beautiful.

## Location

### Geography

Madison Avenue runs from 23rd Street northward through Manhattan, passing through Midtown and the Upper East Side before terminating at the Madison Avenue Bridge at 138th Street. In the context of 1920s [[harlem|Harlem]], Madison Avenue marks the eastern boundary of the neighborhood's more affluent residential blocks — east of [[fifth-avenue|Fifth Avenue]], one block closer to the Harlem River, in a zone where the "black invasion" was pressing against white "Respectability."

### In the Novel's World

Madison Avenue appears only in the novel's final chapter, Chapter XX, as [[jake-brown|Jake]] and [[felice|Felice]] walk together through the streets of [[harlem|Harlem]] after their reunion at the Sheba Palace:

> They had walked down Madison Avenue, turned on 130th Street, passing the solid gray-grim mass of the whites' Presbyterian church, and were under the timidly whispering trees of the decorously silent and distinguished Block Beautiful.
> — (020-003)

This brief traversal situates Madison Avenue on the racial frontier of 1920s [[harlem|Harlem]] — a boundary zone where Black and white residential territories met. The narrator notes that "the whites had not evacuated that block yet. The black invasion was threatening it from 131st Street, from Fifth Avenue, even from behind in 129th Street." Madison Avenue, in this passage, is part of the route that takes the Black couple from the entertainment zone of the Sheba Palace (further west) toward the sedate, white-occupied Block Beautiful on 130th Street — a journey that maps the racial geography of a neighborhood in demographic transition.

## Significance to the Narrative

The single mention of Madison Avenue is brief, but it carries narrative weight as part of the novel's closing movement. [[jake-brown|Jake]]'s reunion with [[felice|Felice]] — the "little lost brown of the Baltimore" he has been searching for since Chapter II — is the novel's emotional climax, and their walk together through [[harlem|Harlem]]'s streets is a kind of geographic consummation. Madison Avenue, in this sequence, is not merely a street name but a coordinate in the novel's mapping of Black urban belonging — another piece of the [[harlem|Harlem]] that Jake has claimed as "mine" (001-032).

The passage also registers the racial tension of the moment. The "solid gray-grim mass of the whites' Presbyterian church" on 130th Street is described as frowning "like a fortress against the invasion" — the Black couple walking down Madison Avenue toward the Block Beautiful represents, in the novel's racial geography, a claim on urban space that white residents experience as threat.

## Historical Context

Madison Avenue in the 1920s was primarily associated with advertising (the "Madison Avenue" of the ad industry had already emerged by the 1910s) and with the wealthy Upper East Side. Its northern reaches, above 110th Street, fell within the zone of [[harlem|Harlem]]'s expansion as Black residents moved northward during the Great Migration. The racial transition of blocks around Madison Avenue in the 120s and 130s — which McKay dramatizes through the image of "desperate, frightened, blanch-faced" white "Respectability" holding on — was a historical reality of 1920s Manhattan, as restrictive covenants broke down and Black residents pressed into formerly all-white blocks.

## Appearances

Madison Avenue appears once, in Chapter XX: [[jake-brown|Jake]] and [[felice|Felice]] walk "down Madison Avenue, turned on 130th Street" (020-003).
