**Fifth Avenue** is a major street in [[harlem|Harlem]] that appears throughout [[claude-mckay|Claude McKay]]'s *Home to [[harlem|Harlem]]* as both a residential and commercial corridor. Together with [[lenox-avenue|Lenox Avenue]] and [[135th-street|135th Street]], it forms part of the novel's central geographic grid.

## Location

### In the Novel's World

Fifth Avenue in [[harlem|Harlem]] is a mixed-use thoroughfare. [[jake-brown|Jake]] walks "rolling along Fifth Avenue" and crosses "over to [[lenox-avenue|Lenox Avenue]]" (003-003), establishing it as part of his habitual route through the neighborhood. The street is lined with rooming houses — one character "had a rooming-house in [[harlem|Harlem]]'s Fifth Avenue" (005-005) — as well as shops where "she was waited upon" (016-021), and restaurants including "Burrell's on Fifth Avenue" serving "corned beef and cabbage." (017-007) A florist's window on Fifth Avenue provides a vivid image of beauty: "Rouge on brown, a warm, insinuating advertisement." (013-055)

## Significance to the Narrative

Fifth Avenue appears as a spatial marker of [[harlem|Harlem]]'s geography, often paired with [[lenox-avenue|Lenox Avenue]]: "Fifth Avenue, [[lenox-avenue|Lenox Avenue]], and [[135th-street|135th Street]]" — the triad [[jake-brown|Jake]] has been away from for five years. (001-029) After the raid on [[the-baltimore-cabaret|the Baltimore]], "Fifth Avenue and [[lenox-avenue|Lenox]] talked about nothing else." (008-002) The street serves as a gathering point for the neighborhood — a crowd is found "at the angle of Fifth Avenue and 133rd Street." (019-007)

The street also represents upward aspiration: "Those who can afford it have got the Fifth Avenue fever." (016-005) This reference to the more famous Fifth Avenue of midtown Manhattan suggests the class ambitions that [[harlem|Harlem]]'s Fifth Avenue both invokes and falls short of.

## From the Novel

Harlem's streets call Jake home after years away:

> It was two years since he had left Harlem. Fifth Avenue, Lenox Avenue, and 135th Street, with their chocolate-brown and walnut-brown girls, were calling him.
>
> (001-029)

## Historical Context

Fifth Avenue in [[harlem|Harlem]] was a central corridor of the neighborhood's commercial and residential life during the 1920s. Unlike the Fifth Avenue of midtown Manhattan — associated with wealth and luxury — [[harlem|Harlem]]'s Fifth Avenue was a working-class street of rooming houses, small businesses, and community gathering spaces, reflecting the economic realities of the Great Migration era.

## Appearances

Fifth Avenue appears or is referenced in chapters 1, 3, 5, 6, 8, 13–14, 16–17, and 19–20. (001-029)–(020-003)
