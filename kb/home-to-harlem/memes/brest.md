# Brest

The French port city that haunts [[jake-brown|Jake]]'s wartime memory — a place of "rainy, sloppy, Gawd-forsaken" labor and segregated military service, but also a site of racial possibility that contrasts with American segregation. Brest is where [[jake-brown|Jake]] "beat it away" from the U.S. Army, and the city recurs throughout the novel as a touchstone in his mental geography of ports, women, and freedom.

## Location

### In the Novel

Brest appears in the novel's opening pages as the point of departure for [[jake-brown|Jake]]'s journey home: "In the winter he sailed for Brest with a happy chocolate company" (001-002). But his hopes of combat are dashed: "His company was held at Brest. Jake toted lumber — boards, planks, posts, rafters — for the hundreds of huts that were built around the walls of Brest and along the coast between Brest and Saint-Pierre, to house the United States soldiers" (001-002). The Black soldiers are not permitted to fight; they are laborers, "despised hod-carriers" in Jake's bitter recollection.

The city returns in the extended flashback of Chapter III, where [[jake-brown|Jake]] and [[zeddy-plummer|Zeddy]] reminisce about their shared experience. [[zeddy-plummer|Zeddy]]'s opening line captures the tone: "What part of the earth done belch you out? Nevah heared no God's tidings a you sence we missed you from Brest" (003-011).

Their recollections map Brest as a complete sensory geography: "the everlasting unloading and unloading of ships and the toting of lumber," the YMCA overlooking the harbor "where colored soldiers were not wanted," "the central Rue de Siam and the point near the Prefecture of Marine, from which you could look down on the red lights of the Quartier Réservé" (003-021). The city contains violence: "The fatal fights between black men and white in the *maisons closes*," encounters between French *apaches* and white Americans, French sailors baffled by "the Yankee idea of amour and men." And it contains loss: "the cemetery, just beyond the old medieval gate of the town, where he left his second-best buddy" (003-021).

[[jake-brown|Jake]]'s route home traces through Brest as the origin point: "Jake told Zeddy how he walked out of it straight to the station in Brest. Le Havre. London. The West India Docks. And back home to Harlem" (003-025). Brest is where the transatlantic journey begins — both the outward journey toward war and the return journey toward [[harlem|Harlem]].

Later, the city joins [[le-havre|Le Havre]] and [[london|London]] in [[jake-brown|Jake]]'s mental catalog of ports that taught him survival: "That was his natural way, wherever he went, whatever new people he met. It had helped him over many a bad crossing at Brest at Havre and in London" (008-007).

In the novel's final chapter, [[jake-brown|Jake]] offers his definitive accounting of Brest: "I didn't run away because I was scared a them Germans. But I beat it away from Brest because they wouldn't give us a chance at them, but kept us in that rainy, sloppy, Gawd-forsaken burg working like wops. They didn't seem to want us niggers foh no soldiers. We was jest a bunch a despised hod-carriers" (021-109).

### Historical Geography

The real Brest was a major American debarkation port during World War I. The U.S. Navy established a base there, and thousands of American troops — including the segregated Black labor battalions of the Services of Supply — passed through the city. The "huts" [[jake-brown|Jake]] describes building were part of the vast infrastructure of the American Expeditionary Forces, and the racial segregation he records — Black soldiers confined to labor roles, excluded from the YMCA, denied the chance to fight — was standard U.S. Army policy.

## Significance

Brest functions in the novel on three levels. **Biographically**, it is the site of [[jake-brown|Jake]]'s disillusionment with the American military — the place where he learns that Black patriotism will not be rewarded with the dignity of combat. **Geographically**, it is the first node in his transatlantic circuit of port cities (Brest → Le Havre → London → Harlem), a circuit he will continue to trace in memory throughout the novel. **Politically**, the contrast between French and American racial attitudes — the *maisons closes* where "black men and white" fought over women, the French sailors who "couldn't get the Yankee idea of amour" — registers the possibility of a world organized differently than Jim Crow America.

The recurrence of Brest in the novel's dialogue — it is mentioned in Chapters I, III, VIII, and XXI — establishes it as a keyword in [[jake-brown|Jake]]'s identity, a shorthand for his war experience and his refusal of a subordinated role.
