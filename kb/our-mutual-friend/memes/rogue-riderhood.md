# Rogue Riderhood

## Overview

Rogue Riderhood is the novel's purest predator — a man who lives by extracting value from the [[the-river-thames|river]]'s flotsam, human and material alike. Introduced as the partner-turned-accuser of [[gaffer-hexam|Gaffer Hexam]], Riderhood attempts to claim the reward for [[john-harmon-presumed-drowning|the Harmon murder]] by denouncing his former mate. When that scheme fails, he simply finds another: [[crime|blackmail]], lock-keeping, corpse-robbing, informing. Riderhood survives by attaching himself to whatever the [[the-river-thames|river]] brings — including, in the novel's most symmetrical [[death-and-resurrection|death]], the water itself.

Riderhood is Dickens's portrait of the man who has been stripped of every [[morality|moral]] claim and who has, as a result, become perfectly adapted to the predatory economy of the [[the-river-thames|Thames]]. His "honesty" — the word he applies to himself with savage irony (012-117) — is the honesty of [[covent-garden|the market]]: he will sell anything, including his own reputation, if the price is right. In a novel that asks repeatedly whether character can survive money, Riderhood is the answer: character was never there to begin with.

## Role in the Novel

Riderhood is the novel's connective tissue between the [[the-river-thames|river]]-world and the legal-world. His false accusation of [[gaffer-hexam|Gaffer Hexam]] sets the [[crime|murder]] investigation in motion; his [[education|knowledge]] of the Harmon [[death-and-resurrection|drowning]] keeps the [[money|inheritance]] plot alive; his presence at Plashwater Weir Mill Lock in the novel's final movement connects him — fatally — to [[bradley-headstone|Bradley Headstone]].

His function is to demonstrate the [[morality|moral]] economy of the [[the-river-thames|Thames]]: the [[the-river-thames|river]] takes and the [[the-river-thames|river]] gives, and men like Riderhood and Hexam live in the space between. His [[death-and-resurrection|death]] — dragged into [[brentford-lock|the lock]] by Headstone, whose hands lock around his throat even as both drown — is the novel's most violent enactment of the [[the-river-thames|river]]'s logic: what you take from the water, the water takes back.

## Key Relationships

- **[[gaffer-hexam]]** — His former partner in [[the-river-thames|river]]-scavenging, whom he denounces for [[john-harmon-presumed-drowning|the Harmon murder]] to claim the reward. T[[inheritance|heir]] relationship — defined by betrayal, mutual [[education|knowledge]], and the [[the-river-thames|river]] they both work — is the novel's portrait of friendship in the criminal under[[social-class|class]].
- **[[bradley-headstone]]** — His final antagonist. T[[inheritance|heir]] fatal struggle in Plashwater Weir Mill Lock — Headstone pulling Riderhood into the water, both [[death-and-resurrection|drowning]] in each other's grip — is the novel's most symmetrical [[death-and-resurrection|death]]: the respectable schoolmaster and the ac[[education|knowledge]]d rogue, indistinguishable in [[death-and-resurrection|death]].
- **[[pleasant-riderhood]]** — His daughter, whose name is the novel's cruelest joke and whose loyalty to her undeserving father is one of the novel's most painful demonstrations of filial love tested beyond reason.

## Thematic Significance

Riderhood is the novel's embodiment of the [[the-river-thames|river]]'s darkest logic: that survival depends on predation, that loyalty is a liability, and that what the water gives, the water eventually claims. His [[death-and-resurrection|death]] — locked in Headstone's [[death-and-resurrection|drowning]] embrace — is the novel's final statement about the [[morality|moral]] equivalence of respectable and criminal violence, both dragged down together into the [[the-river-thames|Thames]] that has measured everyone's worth from the novel's first page.
