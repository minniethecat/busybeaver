# Gaffer Hexam's Death

## Overview

Gaffer Hexam's death — found drowned in the Thames, possibly murdered — is the event that releases both his children from his world. The river-scavenger who made his living retrieving the dead becomes, in the novel's characteristic symmetry, one of the river's dead himself. His removal from the narrative shifts the novel's attention from paternal authority to filial independence: Lizzie and Charley must now navigate the world without the father who defined their place in it.

The circumstances of Hexam's death are deliberately ambiguous. Was he murdered by Rogue Riderhood, who had motive and opportunity? Did he drown accidentally, claimed by the river he worked all his life? The novel does not resolve the question, and this irresolution is characteristic: the river keeps its secrets. What matters is not how Hexam died but what his death releases — Lizzie's moral independence, Charley's social ambition, and Riderhood's subsequent schemes.

## Narrative Context

Hexam's death occurs early in Book I, shortly after the retrieval of the body presumed to be John Harmon. Riderhood, who knows more about the Harmon drowning than he admits, accuses Hexam of murder — a false accusation designed to deflect suspicion. Hexam, unable to clear his name, is found dead shortly after. Riderhood's involvement is never proven, but his subsequent use of the knowledge he claims to possess suggests guilt.

The death severs both children from their riverside origins. Lizzie, freed from her father's authority, must make her own way — which she does, with characteristic dignity, finding refuge at the Fellowship Porters and eventually at Plashwater Mill. Charley, freed from his father's shame, accelerates his climb toward respectability — a climb that will cost him his moral bearings.

## Key Participants

- **[[gaffer-hexam]]** — The victim. His death by drowning is the novel's symmetrical judgment on a man who made his living from the river's dead (001-001).
- **[[lizzie-hexam]]** — The daughter who is freed to become herself. Her grief for her father is genuine; her liberation from his world is real. Both feelings coexist.
- **[[charley-hexam]]** — The son who uses his father's death as an escape. His shame at his origins, previously suppressed, becomes active: he will leave the riverside behind at any moral cost.
- **[[rogue-riderhood]]** — The probable murderer. His accusation of Hexam and his subsequent schemes are linked by the knowledge he alone possesses about the river's dead.

## Scene Description

Hexam's death is reported rather than shown — a narrative choice that preserves the river's mystery. The body is found; the community speculates; the truth is never established. Lizzie's grief is rendered with Dickens's characteristic attention to the intersection of feeling and circumstance: she mourns a father who was hard but not cruel, distant but not unloving.

## Thematic Significance

Hexam's death completes the novel's opening argument about the river as moral force. The man who retrieved the river's dead becomes one of them — not through poetic justice (he was not a villain) but through the river's indifference to individual character. The Thames claims the innocent and the guilty alike, and Hexam — neither wholly one nor the other — is claimed in his turn.
