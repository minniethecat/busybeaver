# Mrs. Wilfer

## Overview

Mrs. Wilfer is one of Dickens's great comic creations — a woman whose self-estimation bears no relation to her circumstances, whose dignity is entirely self-conferred, and whose majestic bearing transforms every domestic occasion into a scene of ceremonial gravity. "Mrs. Wilfer was, of course, a tall woman and an angular woman" (004-012), Dickens tells us, and everything about her — her posture, her rhetoric, her silences — is designed to remind the world that she was not always Mrs. Wilfer of Holloway but was once a young woman with prospects, and that her marriage to "R. W." constitutes a descent from which she has never fully recovered.

Her comedy derives from the gap between her rhetorical register and her actual circumstances. She speaks of "the feelings of a Mother" and "the dignity of a Matron" while presiding over a household of shabby-genteel poverty, a husband who is cheerfully indifferent to dignity, and daughters who refuse to be awed. Her great set-piece — the silent reproach — is the novel's most economical portrait of domestic power exercised through absence rather than presence.

## Role in the Novel

Mrs. Wilfer provides the novel's most sustained domestic comedy while simultaneously embodying its critique of social pretension. She is [[the-veneering-circle|the Veneerings]]' mirror in the lower-middle [[social-class|class]]: where [[the-veneering-circle|the Veneerings]] perform [[money|wealth]] they do not have, Mrs. Wilfer performs dignity she cannot enforce. Both are comic; both are, in t[[inheritance|heir]] different registers, tragic — people whose lives are consumed by the performance of a self they cannot inhabit.

Her relationship with [[bella-wilfer|Bella]] is the novel's most complex treatment of mother-daughter dynamics. She disapproves of everything [[bella-wilfer|Bella]] does — her mercenary declarations, her acceptance of [[the-boffin-household|the Boffins]]' patronage, her marriage to a "secretary" — and her disapproval is the background against which [[bella-wilfer|Bella]]'s [[morality|moral]] education unfolds. Mrs. Wilfer is, finally, the woman [[bella-wilfer|Bella]] must not become: a performer of dignity whose performance has consumed the self it was meant to protect.

## Key Relationships

- **[[r-wilfer]]** — Her husband, "[[r-wilfer|the Cherub]]," whom she treats with majestic condescension. T[[inheritance|heir]] marriage — she performing dignity, he cheerfully ignoring it — is the novel's most sustained comic portrait of marital accommodation. She calls him "R. W." as a form of distance; he calls her nothing at all, having long since given up.
- **[[bella-wilfer]]** — Her elder daughter, whose "mercenary" declarations horrify her and whose eventual marriage to a "mere secretary" confirms her maternal disappointment. [[bella-wilfer|Bella]]'s escape from her mother's orbit is one of the novel's quiet triumphs.
- **[[lavinia-wilfer]]** — Her younger daughter, who has inherited her forcefulness without her dignity and who treats her mother with the same exasperation her mother deploys against everyone else.

## Thematic Significance

Mrs. Wilfer is the novel's argument that dignity without substance is the most exhausting of performances — and that the performer is always the performance's first victim. Her majestic bearing, her rhetorical grandeur, her silent reproaches: all are attempts to construct a self that her circumstances cannot sustain. In a novel where nearly every character performs, Mrs. Wilfer is the one whose performance is most transparently a defence against insignificance.
