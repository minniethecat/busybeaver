# Inheritance

## Overview

Inheritance is the mechanism that sets *Our Mutual Friend* in motion and the metaphor through which it examines the relationship between past and present, dead and living. Old Harmon's will — with its marriage condition, its dust-mound bequest, its alternate heirs — is the legal document that generates the novel's entire plot. But Dickens extends the concept of inheritance beyond property law to encompass the broader question of what the living owe to the dead and what the dead can demand of the living.

The novel treats inheritance as a moral problem rather than a legal convenience. The Harmon will attempts to control lives from beyond the grave — dictating whom John Harmon must marry, what the Boffins receive, how the fortune is to be distributed. The novel's resolution, in which living characters freely arrange what the dead hand of the will had coercively prescribed, argues that genuine inheritance is not the passive receipt of property but the active assumption of responsibility. The Boffins inherit not just dust-mounds but a moral obligation; Bella inherits not just money but the lesson that love is worth more than wealth.

## Expression in the Novel

### The Harmon Will

The will is the novel's originating document and its central symbol of how the dead can tyrannise the living. Old Harmon, "a tremendous old rascal who made his money by Dust" (002-051), attempts to control his son's marriage and his servant's fate from the grave. The will's conditions — marriage to Bella Wilfer, the dust-mounds to Boffin as alternate heir — treat human beings as property to be allocated. The novel's plot is driven by the gap between the will's coercive logic and the characters' moral choices.

### Boffin's Inheritance

Boffin's inheritance of the dust-mounds transforms him from servant to "Golden Dustman" overnight. But the inheritance is also a moral test: can the Boffins hold wealth without being corrupted by it? The novel's answer — that they can, but only through deliberate moral effort — transforms the inheritance plot from a financial transaction into a moral drama. Boffin's feigned miserliness is, among other things, a lesson in what inheritance should mean: not the passive receipt of wealth but the active cultivation of character.

### Bella's Moral Inheritance

Bella Wilfer's inheritance is moral rather than financial. Initially positioned as the object of the Harmon will's marriage condition — a woman "left to him in a will, like a dozen of spoons" (004-050) — she eventually inherits something more valuable than money: the capacity to value love over wealth. Her moral education, orchestrated by the Boffins and completed by her love for Rokesmith, represents inheritance transformed from legal compulsion into freely chosen growth.

## Victorian Context

Inheritance law was a central preoccupation of Victorian fiction because it was a central fact of Victorian life. Primogeniture concentrated family wealth in eldest sons; entails preserved estates across generations; the Court of Chancery's handling of wills and trusts was notoriously costly and slow (Dickens had satirised it in *Bleak House*). The Married Women's Property Acts of 1870 and 1882 would eventually address some of the inequities Dickens dramatises — particularly the treatment of women as property to be transferred through marriage and inheritance. *Our Mutual Friend* registers these legal realities while transforming them into a broader meditation on what the living owe the dead and what inheritance, rightly understood, should mean.
