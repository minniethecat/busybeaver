Miss Abbey Potterson is the sovereign of the Six Jolly Fellowship Porters, a [[the-river-thames|river]]side tavern that serves as the [[morality|moral]] and social centre of the waterside community. She rules "on her own authority" (006-006), a phrase that captures both her independence and the respect she commands. No license from husband, father, or institution defines her power — it is entirely personal, earned through decades of fair dealing and unbending principle.

Her most characteristic gesture is the "emphatic forefinger" with which she delivers judgment, most memorably when she banishes [[rogue-riderhood|Rogue Riderhood]] from her premises: "Get me a little of the smell of the likes of you, and you may never want for that taste again" (006-007). The sentence is both a ban and a [[morality|moral]] verdict; Miss Abbey's tavern is clean in every sense, and she will not have it sullied.

## Role in the Novel

Miss Abbey Potterson functions as the [[morality|moral]] anchor of the [[the-river-thames|river]] world, a counterweight to its darkness and violence. Where the [[the-river-thames|river]] itself is associated with [[death-and-resurrection|death]], predation, and the dredging of corpses, the Six Jolly Fellowship Porters is a place of warmth, order, and community. Miss Abbey maintains this boundary through sheer force of character.

She also serves as a protector figure for the novel's vulnerable characters. When [[lizzie-hexam|Lizzie Hexam]] is in distress, it is to Miss Abbey's establishment that she is brought, and Miss Abbey's presence that guarantees her safety (013-001). In a novel where women are frequently victimised by men, Miss Abbey's autonomy and authority offer an alternative model of female power.

## Key Relationships

- **[[rogue-riderhood|Rogue Riderhood]]**: The defining adversarial relationship. Miss Abbey bans [[rogue-riderhood|Riderhood]] from the Six Jolly Fellowship Porters for his [[morality|moral]] failings, a judgment that [[rogue-riderhood|Riderhood]] — for all his [[crime|villainy]] — never successfully challenges. Her authority over him is absolute within her domain.
- **[[lizzie-hexam|Lizzie Hexam]]**: Miss Abbey serves as a neighbourly protector. When [[lizzie-hexam|Lizzie]] is in crisis, Miss Abbey's establishment provides refuge.
- **[[eugene-wrayburn|Eugene Wrayburn]]**: Miss Abbey is one of the sources through whom [[eugene-wrayburn|Eugene]] learns about the [[the-river-thames|river]] community and [[lizzie-hexam|Lizzie]]'s circumstances. T[[inheritance|heir]] interactions highlight the [[social-class|class]] distance between the waterside world and [[eugene-wrayburn|Eugene]]'s gentlemanly milieu.

## Thematic Significance

Miss Abbey Potterson represents a form of [[morality|moral]] authority that is entirely independent of [[social-class|class]], gender, or institutional position. She is not a magistrate, not a clergyman, not a philanthropist — she is a publican. Yet her [[morality|moral]] judgments carry more weight in the waterside community than any formal authority's. Dickens uses her to suggest that genuine righteousness is a function of character, not station.

Her establishment — the Six Jolly Fellowship Porters — is one of the novel's few genuinely wholesome spaces, a refuge from the [[morality|moral]] fungibility that characterises so many of the novel's other social settings. In a world of Veneering surfaces, the Six Jolly Fellowship Porters is what it appears to be, because Miss Abbey Potterson would permit nothing else.
