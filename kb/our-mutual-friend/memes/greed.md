# Greed

## Overview

Greed in *Our Mutual Friend* is not the crude miserliness of a Scrooge but something more insidious and pervasive: the belief that wealth exists to be extracted rather than earned, that the world is divided into those who dig and those who are dug from. Dickens presents greed as a logic rather than merely a vice — a way of seeing the world that transforms all human relations into transactions and all value into spoils.

The novel gives greed its most vivid embodiment in Silas Wegg, the one-legged ballad-seller who insinuates himself into the Boffin household with the explicit aim of extracting whatever he can. His "poking and prodding into dust-mounds" (017-008) becomes the novel's master image of predatory greed — the conviction that hidden treasure awaits the sufficiently unscrupulous. But Wegg is only the most visible instance. The Lammles marry for money and find each other penniless. Rogue Riderhood attempts to blackmail Bradley Headstone. Even the respectable Veneerings live entirely on credit, extracting social status from borrowed wealth.

What distinguishes Dickens's treatment of greed is his insistence that it corrupts not only the greedy but everyone they touch. Wegg's scheming poisons the atmosphere of the Bower. The Lammles' mercenary marriage produces only mutual contempt. Greed in this novel is not a private sin but a social poison, spreading outward from the greedy heart to infect every relationship it touches.

## Expression in the Novel

### Silas Wegg: The Logic of Extraction

Wegg is introduced as a street vendor whose stock-in-trade is half-penny ballads and dubious antiquarian knowledge. His wooden leg — the visible sign of his incompleteness — becomes a metaphor for the fundamental asymmetry of greed: he always wants more than he has. When Mr. Boffin hires him to read Gibbon's *Decline and Fall*, Wegg immediately begins calculating: "here might be money to be got beyond present calculation" (005-114).

Wegg's greed operates through proximity. By inserting himself into the Bower, he gains access to the dust-mounds and becomes obsessed with what might be "found in the dust" (024-057). His partnership with Mr. Venus, the taxidermist, transforms greed into a collaborative enterprise — two men pooling their respective expertise (bone-articulation and mound-prodding) in pursuit of hidden treasure. The image of Wegg on his stomach, wooden leg in the air, peering under bedsteads with an iron rod, is the novel's definitive portrait of greed as degrading physical comedy.

### The Lammles: Greed as Marriage

Sophronia and Alfred Lammle marry for money, each believing the other wealthy, and discover on their wedding day that they have been mutually deceived. Their response is not repentance but redoubled predation: they become partners in the "shark" trade, hunting wealthy young people (Georgiana Podsnap, Fascination Fledgeby) as marriage prey. The Lammles embody greed's self-perpetuating logic — once you have entered the world of calculation and advantage, there is no exit, only an escalation of the hunt.

### Rogue Riderhood and the Cash Value of Life

Jesse "Rogue" Riderhood represents greed at its most elemental: the attempt to convert human life directly into cash. He offers to testify against Gaffer Hexam for a reward; he later attempts to blackmail Bradley Headstone, sensing a vulnerability he can monetise. Riderhood's greed has no long-term strategy — he is a pure opportunist, converting whatever comes his way (information, vulnerability, a drowning man) into immediate profit.

### Boffin's Feigned Greed

The novel's most complex treatment of greed is Mr. Boffin's apparent transformation into a miser. His tightening fist, his cruelty toward Rokesmith, his parade of avarice before Bella — all are revealed to be a deliberate performance designed to teach Bella what money can do to a person. This is greed as pedagogy: Boffin acts the miser so that Bella can recognise and reject the role. But the performance is so convincing that it raises an uncomfortable question: if Boffin can play the miser so well, is the miser in him after all?

## Victorian Context

*Our Mutual Friend* appeared in the midst of what historians now call the "Age of Capital" — the mid-Victorian boom years when fortunes were made (and lost) with unprecedented speed. The 1860s saw the rise of the limited liability company, speculative investment manias, and a public discourse that increasingly celebrated wealth as the measure of human worth. Dickens, who had watched his own father imprisoned for debt at the Marshalsea, understood the psychology of financial desperation from experience. The novel's insistence that greed is a social poison rather than a private vice places it squarely in the tradition of Victorian social criticism that connected individual morality to systemic conditions.

The dust-mounds themselves reflect a genuine economic phenomenon: the "dust-contracting" trade produced real fortunes from London's refuse, and the men who controlled the dust-yards were among the city's wealthiest. Dickens's choice to make the Harmon fortune derive from literal waste is his most pointed commentary on the sources of Victorian wealth — the suggestion that no fortune, examined closely, has clean hands.
