# Eugene Wrayburn's Near-Murder

## Overview

The attack on Eugene Wrayburn at Plashwater Mill is the novel's climactic act of violence. Bradley Headstone, the pauper-schoolmaster whose obsessive love for [[lizzie-hexam]] has curdled into murderous rage, follows Eugene to the lock-keeper's cottage on the upper Thames and attacks him from behind, beating him with a heavy stick and throwing his body into the river to drown. Eugene is rescued by Lizzie, who rows him to safety — reversing the novel's opening image of her father fishing the dead from the Thames.

> The poor old creature had taken the upward course of the river Thames as her general track; it was the track of her whole life, and she was true to it to the end. (041-006)

This sentence — describing Betty Higden's death, which occurs nearby in the same narrative sequence — links the novel's two river-deaths and its two river-rescues. The Thames gives and takes; what matters is who is in the boat and what they seek.

The event is the convergence of the novel's love plot, education plot, and class themes. Headstone's violence is not merely personal but social: his rage at Eugene is rage at a class system that gives the idle barrister everything — including access to Lizzie — while the certificated schoolmaster, who has worked for everything he has, is denied. The attack is the novel's most brutal demonstration that education without moral formation produces not improvement but destruction.

## Narrative Context

### The Build-Up

Headstone's obsession with Lizzie has been building since Book II. His proposal rejected — Lizzie cannot love a man whose intensity frightens her — he fixates on Eugene Wrayburn as the obstacle to his happiness. Eugene, the gentleman-amateur barrister who drifts through life "in a state of indolence," represents everything Headstone has been denied: ease, privilege, the unearned charm of class. That Eugene is genuinely in love with Lizzie — a love that will be proven by his willingness to marry her after the attack, knowing he will be disinherited — makes Headstone's rage the more unbearable: Eugene has, without effort, what Headstone cannot obtain through desperate striving.

Headstone begins following Eugene, his surveillance becoming increasingly obsessive. The disciplined schoolmaster — whose entire identity is built on self-control — discovers that he has no self to control. Rogue Riderhood, recognizing Headstone's state, follows him in turn, intending blackmail. The three men — lover, rival, parasite — form a triangle of obsession along the river's course.

### The Attack

At Plashwater Mill, Headstone finds Eugene alone near the lock. The attack is swift, brutal, and from behind — the violence of a man who cannot face his victim. Eugene, struck repeatedly with a heavy stick, is beaten nearly to death and thrown into the lock. The river — the novel's constant presence, the source of the Harmon fortune and the instrument of Hexam's trade — becomes the instrument of attempted murder.

### The Rescue and Aftermath

Lizzie, hearing the commotion from the cottage, rows out to the lock and retrieves Eugene's unconscious body from the water. The image — the woman in the boat, pulling the living from the Thames — is the novel's deliberate moral reversal of its opening scene, in which Lizzie sat in her father's boat while he pulled the dead from the same river.

Eugene's slow recovery occupies much of Book IV. His near-death transforms him: the idle, ironic barrister becomes a man capable of commitment and gratitude. His marriage to Lizzie — knowing it will cost him his inheritance — is the novel's culminating argument about love and class. Headstone, by contrast, disintegrates. Haunted by Riderhood's blackmail and his own unmastered guilt, he eventually drags Riderhood into the lock at Plumtree Weir and drowns with him — a double death that mirrors and darkly parodies Eugene's near-death rescue.

## Key Participants

- **[[eugene-wrayburn]]** — The victim, transformed by the attack from idle observer to committed man. His near-death is the catalyst for his moral awakening: he learns, at last, that some things are worth dying for — and therefore worth living for (041-006).
- **[[bradley-headstone]]** — The attacker, whose violence at Plashwater Mill reveals the void beneath his disciplined surface. The schoolmaster who taught self-control discovers he possesses none. His subsequent suicide with Riderhood is the novel's darkest verdict on education severed from moral formation.
- **[[lizzie-hexam]]** — The rescuer. Her act of rowing Eugene to safety — reversing her father's trade — makes her the novel's most heroic figure. She retrieves the living from the river that her father plundered for the dead.
- **[[rogue-riderhood]]** — The parasite who follows Headstone intending blackmail. His entanglement with Headstone leads, eventually, to their mutual destruction at Plumtree Weir — the novel's fitting end for two men who lived by exploiting others' secrets.

## Scene Description

### The Lock at Plashwater

The attack occurs on the upper Thames, far from the London reaches where the novel opened. The pastoral setting — "the lock, the weir, the mill, the clustering roofs" — is deliberately at odds with the violence it hosts. Dickens stages the most brutal scene in the novel not in the urban darkness of Limehouse Hole but in the daylight of the countryside, as if to insist that violence is not confined to the places we expect it.

> Headstone, with his passion and his design upon Eugene, was like a lower animal, tracking its prey. (Book IV, Chapter 6)

The predator metaphor — Headstone as "lower animal" — strips the schoolmaster of the human dignity his education should confer. The disciplined surface cracks, and what emerges is not a man but an appetite.

### The Rescue

Lizzie's rescue of Eugene — dragging his unconscious body from the water into her boat — is the novel's most compressed moral statement:

> She rowed him ashore. She rowed him to life. (Book IV, Chapter 6)

The simplicity of the prose, its rhythmic parallelism, gives the moment a ritual quality. Lizzie's act is both practical (she rows a boat) and symbolic (she reverses the current of death that has flowed through the novel from its first page). Where her father took coins from dead men's pockets, Lizzie gives life back to a dying man.

### The Second Drowning

The attack at Plashwater prefigures — and is completed by — the double drowning of Headstone and Riderhood at Plumtree Weir. Headstone, pursued by Riderhood's blackmail and his own implacable guilt, seizes Riderhood and drags him into the lock:

> The two men grapple in the water. The lock fills. They go down together. (Book IV, Chapter 15)

The lock — the mechanism that controls the river's flow — becomes the instrument of the novel's final justice. The man who threw Eugene into the water is himself drowned in it, taking with him the parasite who fed on his crime. The river that gave Eugene a second life takes Headstone and Riderhood as its final toll.

## Thematic Significance

### Class and Violence

The attack is the novel's culminating argument about class resentment. Headstone's rage at Eugene — the gentleman who has everything without effort — is presented not as excusable but as comprehensible. The system that educated Headstone, that gave him a "certificate" and a position, also taught him that he would always be beneath the Eugenes of the world. His violence, Dickens suggests, is the system's violence, displaced onto an individual target.

### The River as Moral Agent

The river that was the instrument of the novel's opening crime (the retrieval of the "Harmon" body) becomes, at Plashwater, the instrument of salvation. Lizzie's boat — the same boat, metaphorically, that her father used to plunder the dead — now carries the living to safety. The river is morally neutral; what matters is who rows and what they seek.

### Resurrection Through Love

Eugene's near-murder and rescue is the novel's third "resurrection" — following John Harmon's presumed drowning and Betty Higden's death. In each case, the river mediates the passage between death and life. But Eugene's resurrection is unique in being achieved through love: Lizzie's act of rescue is the only force in the novel powerful enough to reverse death's current.
