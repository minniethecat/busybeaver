# Brentford Lock

## Overview

Brentford Lock — a Thames lock west of London — is one of the novel's most liminal spaces. It is neither London nor country, neither the fashionable upper Thames (Hampton, Richmond) nor the working riverside (Limehouse). As a lock, it is a place of transition: boats pass through, water levels change, the river is temporarily controlled before being released. This transitional quality makes it the perfect setting for the novel's scenes of passage and transformation.

Brentford appears most significantly in connection with Rogue Riderhood's schemes. It is here that he attempts to claim a body from the river — falsely asserting it as that of a relative to extract payment. The lock, with its controlled flow and regulated passage, becomes the scene of Riderhood's attempted manipulation of the river's economy of death. But the river cannot be controlled: what happens at Brentford Lock demonstrates that the Thames is not a mechanism to be exploited but a force that answers only to itself.

The lock's geographical marginality — too far west for Limehouse, too far east for the pastoral reaches — makes it a space where the novel's usual social categories are suspended. It is a place of transit, not settlement, and the characters who appear there are, like the boats that pass through the lock, in motion between identities and fates.

## In the Novel

Brentford Lock appears primarily in Book I, when Riderhood attempts to claim a body discovered in the river. The scene establishes the river's economy of death — the trade in corpses, the monetary value assigned to the drowned — that the novel's opening chapters introduce. Riderhood's attempt to profit from the dead, at Brentford's transitional space, is his first act of predation in the novel and sets the pattern for his subsequent schemes.

The lock also serves as a geographical marker of the river's extent. The Thames in *Our Mutual Friend* stretches from Limehouse (east) to Brentford (west) to the upper reaches at Plashwater Mill. This geography maps the novel's class spectrum: the working poor downstream, the respectable middle classes in the middle reaches, and the pastoral retreats (and their violence) upstream.
