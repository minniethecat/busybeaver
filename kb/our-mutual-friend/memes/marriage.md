# Marriage

## Overview

Every marriage in *Our Mutual Friend* is shadowed by money. The novel treats the Victorian marriage plot not as the natural resolution of romantic love but as a financial transaction that reveals the moral character of those who enter into it — or refuse to. From the Harmon will's marriage condition to the Lammles' mercenary pact, from Bella Wilfer's mercenary start to Eugene Wrayburn's cross-class union with Lizzie Hexam, Dickens systematically exposes how the institution of marriage in Victorian England served as a mechanism for transferring and consolidating wealth rather than celebrating love.

The novel's treatment of marriage is notably unsentimental. The only genuinely happy marriages — the Boffins, and the eventual unions of Bella/Harmon and Eugene/Lizzie — are achieved only after the characters have been tested by money and found capable of valuing love above it. The novel's unhappy marriages, by contrast, are those in which money was the primary consideration: the Lammles, who married for wealth and found only mutual contempt; the Veneerings, whose union is as hollow as their furniture. In making marriage a moral litmus test, Dickens transforms the Victorian novel's most conventional plot device into its sharpest social criticism.

## Expression in the Novel

### The Harmon Will: Marriage by Coercion

The novel's central plot mechanism is a will that conditions inheritance on marriage. Old Harmon's demand that his son marry Bella Wilfer — a woman John Harmon has never met — reduces marriage to a commercial transaction. The will treats Bella as property to be transferred and John as an economic agent whose personal desires are irrelevant. By making this the engine of his plot, Dickens asks whether any marriage contracted under financial compulsion can be morally legitimate.

### The Lammles: Marriage as Mutual Deception

Alfred and Sophronia Lammle represent the novel's definitive statement on mercenary marriage. Each married the other believing their partner wealthy; each discovered on the wedding day that they had been deceived. Their response — "Then you married me on false pretences?" (010-061) — is both accusation and confession. The Lammles become partners in predation, hunting wealthy young people for the marriage market with the cold efficiency of professionals. Their marriage is a contract without love, a partnership without trust, and Dickens's harshest verdict on what happens when money replaces affection as the basis of union.

### Bella and Harmon: The Marriage That Earns Itself

Bella Wilfer's journey from "I love money" to genuine love for John Rokesmith is the novel's demonstration that a good marriage must be earned through moral growth, not inherited through legal compulsion. Her rejection of Rokesmith's proposal when she believes him a poor secretary — followed by her acceptance when she realises she loves him regardless — completes her moral education. When Harmon's true identity is revealed, the money becomes a reward for love rather than a substitute for it.

### Eugene and Lizzie: Cross-Class Union

Eugene Wrayburn's marriage to Lizzie Hexam is the novel's most radical marriage plot. A gentleman marries a waterman's daughter — a union that in Victorian class terms is nearly unthinkable. The marriage is only made possible by Eugene's near-death and Lizzie's devotion during his recovery, and even then it carries the cost of social exile. Dickens presents this cross-class marriage not as a sentimental fantasy but as a hard-won achievement that requires both partners to transcend the expectations of their respective classes.

## Victorian Context

Marriage law was a live political issue in the 1860s. The Matrimonial Causes Act of 1857 had established civil divorce (though on starkly unequal terms for men and women), and debates about women's property rights — culminating in the Married Women's Property Act of 1870 — were reshaping the legal landscape in which Dickens wrote. Under coverture, a married woman's legal existence was subsumed into her husband's; her property became his. *Our Mutual Friend* registers this reality in its treatment of marriage as a property transaction, but its deeper argument is that legal reform alone cannot purify an institution built on financial calculation. The novel's insistence that genuine marriage requires moral transformation — not merely legal protection — places it at the radical edge of Victorian thinking about the institution.
