# Identity and Disguise

## Overview

Identity in *Our Mutual Friend* is never stable, never given, never simply what it appears to be. The novel's central plot mechanism — John Harmon's disguise as John Rokesmith — is only the most literal instance of a pattern that extends through every character and social relationship. Characters are constantly performing identities, concealing identities, discovering identities, and losing identities. The Veneerings perform wealth they do not possess; Bradley Headstone performs respectability that masks murderous rage; Boffin performs miserliness that conceals generosity; Bella Wilfer performs mercenary calculation that hides a capacity for love.

Dickens's treatment of identity is distinctively modern. Identity in this novel is not an essence to be discovered but a performance to be managed — and moral worth is determined not by the identity one inhabits but by the relationship between one's performed self and one's true self. The characters who are morally admirable (the Boffins, Lizzie Hexam) are those for whom performance and reality are aligned; the characters who are morally corrupt (the Veneerings, Headstone) are those whose performances conceal destructive truths.

## Expression in the Novel

### John Harmon/Rokesmith: The Deliberate Disguise

Harmon's assumption of the Rokesmith identity is the novel's most extended treatment of disguise. Unlike the conventional hidden-identity plot, Harmon's disguise is voluntary and morally purposive — he becomes Rokesmith not to escape danger but to observe, unseen, how those around him behave when they believe the heir is dead. He is presumed dead, "come by his death under highly suspicious circumstances" (003-170), and this presumed death is the condition of his hidden life. His disguise is a moral experiment, and its success depends on the gap between what people show to a wealthy heir and what they show to a humble secretary.

### Boffin's Performed Miserliness

Boffin's apparent decline into miserliness — his tightened fist, his cruelty toward Rokesmith, his parade of avarice — is the novel's most complex treatment of performed identity. When the performance is revealed, it retroactively transforms everything the reader has witnessed: Boffin was playing a role to teach Bella what money can do to a person. The performance was a moral act, and its success depended on its convincingness — which raises the uncomfortable question of whether Boffin's performance drew on something real within him.

### Social Identity as Performance

The Veneerings, the Podsnaps, the Lammles — all perform social identities that are at odds with reality. The Veneerings perform wealth on credit; the Podsnaps perform respectability as moral anaesthetic; the Lammles perform happy marriage as joint predation. In each case, the performed identity is a surface that conceals either emptiness (Veneerings), complacency (Podsnaps), or active malevolence (Lammles). The novel's social critique consists in exposing these performances and revealing what lies beneath.

## Victorian Context

The Victorian period was an age of intense anxiety about identity and its authentication. The expansion of cities made anonymity possible; the rise of the credit economy made trust in strangers' identities essential; the sensation novel (of which *Our Mutual Friend* is in part an example) exploited fears about hidden identities and concealed criminality. At the same time, the Victorian cult of "character" — the belief that moral worth should be visible in a person's face and bearing — created a cultural imperative to make inner virtue outwardly legible. Dickens's novel both participates in this cultural discourse and subverts it: identity in *Our Mutual Friend* is never reliably legible from surfaces, and the ability to read beneath surfaces — to discern the true self beneath the performed one — is the novel's highest moral skill.
