# Mr. Venus

## Overview

Mr. Venus, the "articulator of human bones," runs the most memorably grotesque establishment in *Our Mutual Friend*: a shop in Clerkenwell filled with "human warious" — preserved specimens, articulated skeletons, stuffed birds, and a miscellany of preserved creatures that blur the boundary between the living and the dead. His professional specialty — assembling human skeletons from disparate bones — is the novel's most concentrated metaphor for its central concerns: what can be assembled from fragments, what value resides in discarded things, whether the parts make a whole.

Venus enters the novel as [[silas-wegg|Silas Wegg]]'s reluctant partner in the dust-plot. Wegg has a wooden leg that Venus wants (to complete a skeleton), and Venus has information Wegg needs. T[[inheritance|heir]] grotesque partnership — the ballad-monger and the bone-grinder, negotiating over body parts in a shop of preserved specimens — is Dickens at his most darkly comic. Venus's eventual betrayal of Wegg (he warns Boffin of the [[crime|blackmail]] scheme) reveals him as a man with a [[morality|conscience]], however eccentrically housed.

## Role in the Novel

Venus serves two functions. Comically, his shop is the novel's most sustained piece of grotesque invention — a space where the boundary between body and object, life and [[death-and-resurrection|death]], value and waste is systematically dissolved. Philosophically, Venus's profession embodies the novel's central metaphor of assembly: the Harmon [[money|fortune]], like a skeleton, must be assembled from scattered parts (the [[dust|dust-heaps]], the [[inheritance|wills]], the [[death-and-resurrection|presumed-dead]] [[inheritance|heir]]).

His partnership with Wegg — sealed by the negotiation over Wegg's wooden leg — drives the counter-conspiracy plot. But Venus's [[morality|conscience]] eventually prevails: he warns Boffin, ensuring that [[silas-weggs-counter-conspiracy|Wegg's blackmail]] scheme collapses. His arc suggests that even the most grotesque character may possess a [[morality|moral]] core — that a bone-grinder may be more trustworthy than a "literary man."

## Thematic Significance

Venus embodies the novel's argument about assembly and value. His profession — making wholes from parts — is the novel's central metaphor for its own narrative method: assembling scattered plots, identities, and [[morality|moral]] fragments into a coherent whole. His shop, where bodies become objects and objects become specimens, is the novel's laboratory for examining what makes a thing valuable — and whether value inheres in the parts or in t[[inheritance|heir]] arrangement.
