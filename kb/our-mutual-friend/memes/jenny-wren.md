# Jenny Wren

## Overview

Jenny Wren — whose "real name" is Fanny Cleaver, though she prefers the name she has chosen for herself — is one of Dickens's most singular creations: a crippled child who supports her alcoholic father by making dolls' dresses, and who has developed, as a survival mechanism, a second sight that transforms the sordid London streets into a visionary landscape of flowers, birds, and "children coming to be dressed." Her refrain — "Come up and be dead!" — addressed to her drunkard father, is both comic and genuinely unsettling, the voice of a child who has become, by necessity, the adult in her household.

Jenny's physical limitation — her twisted back and stunted growth — is the condition of her extraordinary imaginative power. Because she cannot participate in the adult world, she sees through it: her sharp tongue cuts through the pretensions of every character she encounters, from Fledgeby's cowardice to [[mr-riah|Riah]]'s gentle sorrow. She is the novel's most penetrating social critic precisely because she is the character with the least social power.

## Role in the Novel

Jenny serves as the novel's [[morality|moral]] [[morality|conscience]] and its most radical voice. Her friendship with [[lizzie-hexam|Lizzie Hexam]] provides the novel's warmest portrait of female solidarity across suffering; her verbal dismantling of Fledgeby (who has been brutally beaten) is the novel's most satisfying comic reversal. Her "Come up and be dead!" — repeated throughout her scenes — is the novel's most condensed statement of its argument about the inversion of adult and child, parent and caregiver.

Her relationship with her father inverts the expected dependency: she works while he drinks, she scolds while he cowers, she imagines while he sinks. This inversion is both Dickens's most radical treatment of parent-child relations and his most intimate portrait of the working child's burden.

- **[[lizzie-hexam]]** — Her closest friend. Jenny's love for [[lizzie-hexam|Lizzie]] is fierce, protective, and entirely unsentimental.
- **[[jenny-wrens-father]]** — Her drunkard father, whom she supports with her dolls' dresses and scolds with her refrain "Come up and be dead!" T[[inheritance|heir]] relationship — the child as parent, the father as burden — is the novel's most radical critique of Victorian family structures.
- **[[r-fledgeby]]** — The cowardly moneylender whose beating and humiliation Jenny witnesses and celebrates. Her verbal lashing of Fledgeby in his sickbed is one of Dickens's most satisfying scenes of comeuppance.

## Thematic Significance

Jenny Wren is the novel's argument that the truest vision often comes from the most unlikely source — the crippled child who sees more clearly than the able-bodied adults around her. Her "second sight" — the ability to imagine London's children as dolls awaiting beautiful dresses — is at once a symptom of trauma and a strategy of survival, a way of making the unbearable bearable through imaginative transformation. She is, in the novel's [[morality|moral]] economy, the figure who most completely embodies the union of suffering and wisdom.
