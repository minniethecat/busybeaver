# The Six Jolly Fellowship Porters

## Overview

The Six Jolly Fellowship Porters is the novel's counter-establishment — the alternative to the Veneering dinner table (006-001). Whereas the Veneerings' gatherings are hollow rituals of social performance, the Fellowship Porters is a place where character is tested and revealed. Its name is its argument: six jolly fellowship porters, bound by honest labour and mutual regard, create the kind of community that fashionable society, for all its wealth and ceremony, cannot produce.

Dickens gives the tavern one of the novel's most loving descriptions. Its "dropsical appearance" — bulging, swollen, leaning out over the river — is a physical manifestation of its abundance: it has been so thoroughly filled with life, so long saturated with the pleasures and sorrows of its patrons, that it can no longer contain itself. The "six Jolly Fellowship Porters" painted on the signboard have become six blurred shapes, their jollity weathered into ambiguity. This is not neglect but character: the tavern wears its history on its walls (006-001–006-006).

The establishment is ruled by Abbey Potterson, a figure of almost priestly authority whose nickname — "the Abbess" — invests the tavern with the dignity of a religious institution. Her rule is absolute and benign: she presides over the Fellowship Porters as a moral guardian, keeping order among the riverside workers and protecting the vulnerable — particularly Lizzie Hexam — from exploitation (006-004).

## In the Novel

The Fellowship Porters serves as the novel's riverside headquarters. It is the natural gathering place for the working characters of the Thames: watermen, lightermen, stevedores, and the families who depend on them. Here the river's dramas — Gaffer Hexam's death, Riderhood's schemes, Lizzie's flight — are discussed, judged, and assimilated into the community's shared understanding.

The tavern also functions as a sanctuary. When Lizzie Hexam needs protection from Bradley Headstone's obsessive pursuit, it is to the Fellowship Porters she turns. Abbey Potterson's authority creates a space where the river's most vulnerable can find refuge from its predators. The contrast is pointed: the law (the Temple, Mortimer Lightwood's profession) offers Lizzie no protection; the Fellowship Porters, with no legal authority whatsoever, provides what the law cannot (003-136).

The tavern's riverside location — its back rooms opening directly onto the water — makes it a liminal space between land and river, respectability and danger, the known and the unknown. Characters who enter the Fellowship Porters enter a space where the usual social categories are suspended: here, a waterman and a barrister might share a table, and the only authority that matters is the Abbess's moral sense (006-005).

## Associated Characters

- **[[lizzie-hexam]]** — The tavern is her natural social centre. She is known and respected here; Abbey Potterson is her protector. When the world of education and law threatens her, the Fellowship Porters is her sanctuary (003-136).
- **[[gaffer-hexam]]** — Before his death, the tavern was his place — where his reputation as a river-scavenger was known and his daughter's position accepted (003-167).
- **[[rogue-riderhood]]** — He haunts the edges of the Fellowship Porters, part of its world but never wholly welcome. His relationship to the tavern reflects his relationship to the riverside community: tolerated but distrusted (Book III).
- **Abbey Potterson** — The "abbess" of this secular convent. Her moral authority, exercised with a combination of warmth and steel, makes the Fellowship Porters what it is. She is one of the novel's most fully realized working-class characters (006-004).
- **[[eugene-wrayburn]]** — His visits to the Fellowship Porters, pursuing his interest in Lizzie, bring the novel's class worlds into direct collision. The tavern is the space where his West End indolence meets the riverside's stark realities.
