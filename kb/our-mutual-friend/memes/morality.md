# Morality

## Overview

*Our Mutual Friend* proposes a distinctively secular morality — one grounded not in religious doctrine but in the capacity to see clearly and love genuinely. The novel's moral system operates through testing and revelation: characters are placed in situations (usually involving money) that expose their true natures, and their moral worth is determined by how they respond. There are no deathbed conversions, no providential interventions, no appeals to divine judgment. Morality is demonstrated through action, not profession, and the highest virtues are discernment (seeing through surfaces to realities) and love (valuing persons over things).

The Boffins are the novel's moral centre not because they are pious or principled in any doctrinal sense but because they are genuine — incapable of the performance that defines Veneering respectability. Bella Wilfer's moral education, the novel's most sustained treatment of moral growth, consists in learning to distinguish the genuine from the counterfeit — in Rokesmith, in Boffin, and in herself. The novel's moral argument is that virtue is not a fixed quality but an achievement, something that must be actively cultivated through the exercise of discernment and the choice to love.

## Expression in the Novel

### Money as Moral Test

The novel consistently uses money to reveal character. Boffin's inheritance tests whether wealth corrupts; Bella's poverty tests whether she values love over money; Wegg's greed tests whether proximity to wealth breeds predation. As Mr. Boffin reflects on his new fortune, "I have been in the house when death was in it, and I have been in the house when money was in it" (015-166) — death and money as the twin revelations of human nature. Money in this novel does not create character — it exposes it. The characters who pass the test (the Boffins, Bella, Harmon/Rokesmith) are those who can hold wealth without being possessed by it. Those who fail (Wegg, the Lammles, Riderhood) are consumed by their desire for what they do not have.

### Discernment as Virtue

The novel's central moral skill is the ability to see beneath surfaces. Harmon/Rokesmith must discern Bella's true character beneath her mercenary performance. Bella must discern Rokesmith's worth beneath his humble position. The reader must discern Boffin's true generosity beneath his feigned miserliness. Morality in this novel is fundamentally epistemological: to be good is to see clearly.

### Love as Redemption

Love is the novel's only redemptive force. Eugene Wrayburn is saved — morally as well as physically — by Lizzie's love. Bella is educated out of her mercenary self by her growing love for Rokesmith and the Boffins' love for her. The Boffins' marriage, weathered and genuine, is the novel's model of love as steady mutual care. Against these, the Lammles' union demonstrates that without love, marriage is mutual predation.

## Victorian Context

The Victorian period is often characterised as an age of moral certainty — of firm religious conviction and clear social codes. But by the 1860s, the challenges of Darwinism, higher criticism of the Bible, and industrial capitalism had generated what historians now call the "Victorian crisis of faith." *Our Mutual Friend* registers this crisis by constructing a moral framework that operates independently of religious doctrine. Its virtues — discernment, love, genuineness — are secular and democratic, available to the uneducated Boffins as readily as to the educated professional classes. This is Dickens's most radical moral vision: a world in which goodness requires no theological foundation, only the courage to see clearly and the capacity to love.
