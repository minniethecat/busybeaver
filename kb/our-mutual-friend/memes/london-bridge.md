London Bridge is the historic crossing of the [[river-thames|River Thames]], connecting the City of London to the Surrey shore at Southwark. It appears in the opening sentence of *Our Mutual Friend* and serves as a recurring geographical reference point throughout the novel.

## Opening Image

The novel begins with the image of a boat on the Thames, between two bridges — Southwark Bridge (iron) and London Bridge (stone). This juxtaposition of old and new bridge technology establishes the novel's concern with tradition and modernity:

> "a boat of dirty and disreputable appearance, with two figures in it, floated on the Thames, between Southwark bridge which is of iron, and London Bridge which is of stone" (001-001)

London Bridge, the older of the two, is described as "of stone" — a detail that underscores its antiquity and solidity compared with the newer iron Southwark Bridge.

## Passage and Transition

London Bridge marks an important threshold in the novel's geography. Characters cross it at significant moments — [[eugene-wrayburn|Eugene Wrayburn]] and [[mortimer-lightwood|Mortimer Lightwood]] "crossed the Thames by way of London Bridge" (035-028) during their investigation of the Harmon case. The bridge serves as a literal and figurative crossing point, connecting the City with the poorer districts of Southwark and the riverside communities beyond.

## The Bridge in Dickens's London

In the 1860s, the medieval London Bridge — the "stone" bridge Dickens references — had been replaced in 1831 by John Rennie's granite bridge. Dickens's description of London Bridge as "of stone" evokes the older structure, lending a sense of historical continuity to the novel's modern setting. The bridge was the primary crossing of the Thames in central London, lined with shops and houses until the 1760s, and remained the symbolic heart of London's river traffic throughout the Victorian period.
