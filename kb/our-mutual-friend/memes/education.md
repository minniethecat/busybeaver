# Education

## Overview

Education in *Our Mutual Friend* is not the benign engine of self-improvement familiar from Victorian ideology but a morally ambiguous force that can elevate or corrupt depending on the character of those it touches. Dickens presents three distinct educational trajectories — Charley Hexam's climb from poverty to respectability, Bradley Headstone's disciplined acquisition of professional credentials, and Lizzie Hexam's self-directed moral learning — and uses them to ask whether formal education makes people better or merely more competent at concealing their vices.

The novel's treatment of education is notably sceptical for a writer who had campaigned for Ragged Schools and popular literacy. Charley Hexam, the novel's primary "scholarship boy," uses his education to distance himself from his sister Lizzie and his dead father, equating learning with social elevation and social elevation with the right to despise those left behind. Bradley Headstone, the "highly certificated" schoolmaster, is the novel's most terrifying character — a man whose education has given him the surface of respectability while leaving untouched the volcanic passions beneath. Against these, Lizzie Hexam, who teaches herself to read by her brother's discarded books, represents education as moral growth rather than social climbing.

## Expression in the Novel

### Charley Hexam: Education as Escape

Charley Hexam's entire identity is built on education. A pupil-teacher who has climbed from his father's riverside shack to a respectable schoolroom, Charley regards learning as a ladder out of poverty — and he is willing to kick away anyone who might pull him back down. His rejection of his sister Lizzie, who sacrificed to pay for his schooling, is the novel's sharpest indictment of education divorced from moral obligation: "I want to raise myself in the scale of society by my own exertions" (023-081). The "by my own" is the key — education has taught Charley to see his achievements as purely individual, erasing the debts he owes to others.

### Bradley Headstone: The Educated Monster

Headstone is Dickens's most disturbing portrait of the educated man. A "highly certificated" schoolmaster who has risen through "the sweat of his brow" (012-073), he is the product of a system that values credentials over character. His education has equipped him with social respectability and professional standing but has done nothing to temper the furious jealousy and possessiveness that drive him to attempted murder. Headstone is the novel's proof that education without moral formation produces not enlightenment but a more dangerous form of savagery — the kind that wears a collar and carries a certificate.

### Lizzie Hexam: Moral Education

Lizzie, who learns to read from Charley's discarded books, represents education as moral growth rather than social mobility. Her learning is private, self-directed, and driven by a desire to understand rather than to rise. Unlike Charley, for whom education is a weapon against his origins, Lizzie's learning deepens her connection to others — she reads to her brother, she reads the Bible to Betty Higden, she reads the world with a moral intelligence that no certificate can confer.

### The School as Institution

Dickens's depiction of the schoolroom — "a gabbling crowd of little things" undergoing "educational tricks" (044-002) — is notably grim. Education in its institutional form appears as rote performance, a mechanical process that produces neither knowledge nor virtue. The novel's contrast between institutional schooling (Headstone's classroom) and self-directed moral learning (Lizzie's reading) is stark and deliberate.

## Victorian Context

The 1860s saw the expansion of popular education in England, driven by the Revised Code of 1862 (which introduced payment-by-results for schools) and the growing recognition that an industrial economy required a literate workforce. The pupil-teacher system that produced Charley Hexam and Bradley Headstone was a real pathway out of poverty — bright working-class children could apprentice as teachers and eventually qualify for professional positions. Dickens, who had served on the council of the Ragged School Union, was deeply invested in education but deeply sceptical of its institutional forms. *Our Mutual Friend* reflects this ambivalence: education is necessary but insufficient, and in the wrong hands it becomes not a ladder but a scaffolding for pride, resentment, and violence.
