Mr. Dolls is perhaps the most purely pathetic figure in *Our Mutual Friend*. Known only by the contemptuous nickname his daughter [[jenny-wren|Jenny Wren]] has given him — she who is herself known only by a nickname — he is an alcoholic whose existence is reduced to the pursuit of his next drink. His daughter, physically disabled but fiercely independent, supports him through her dolls' dressmaking while treating him with a mixture of exasperation and dutiful care that inverts the normal parent–child relationship.

[[eugene-wrayburn|Eugene Wrayburn]]'s interactions with Mr. Dolls provide some of the novel's darkest comedy. [[eugene-wrayburn|Eugene]] "winds up" Mr. Dolls with doses of rum — "Threepenn'orth rum," said Mr. Dolls (043-069) — treating the man as a kind of mechanical curiosity, a broken clockwork that can be temporarily restored to motion. The comedy is uneasy: Mr. Dolls's degradation is so complete that it verges on the tragic.

## Role in the Novel

Mr. Dolls functions primarily as a foil to his daughter. Where Jenny is sharp, industrious, and [[morality|moral]]ly clear-sighted, her father is blurred, helpless, and [[morality|moral]]ly void. The inversion of t[[inheritance|heir]] relationship — Jenny as the responsible "adult," Mr. Dolls as the dependent "child" — is one of Dickens's most economical demonstrations of how poverty and addiction can dissolve the structures that are supposed to sustain family life.

## Thematic Significance

Mr. Dolls represents the extreme endpoint of the novel's concern with what money — or its absence — does to human beings. He is what happens when poverty strips away every dignity, leaving only the animal need for oblivion. His namelessness — "Mr. Dolls" is not a name but a label, and a contemptuous one at that — signals his almost complete erasure as a person.
