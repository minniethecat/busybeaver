# Bella Wilfer

## Overview

Bella Wilfer is the [[morality|moral]] heart of *Our Mutual Friend* and the vehicle for Dickens's most sustained treatment of [[morality|moral]] education. Introduced as the "marriageable young woman" (002-072) named in [[old-harmon|Old Harmon]]'s will, Bella begins the novel resentful of her poverty and honestly mercenary: "I love money, and want money — want it dreadfully. I hate to be [[poverty|poor]], and we are degradingly [[poverty|poor]], offensively [[poverty|poor]], miserably [[poverty|poor]], beastly [[poverty|poor]]" (004-050). This speech, shocking in its nakedness, establishes Bella as a character who refuses the sentimental conventions of the Victorian heroine — she is not virtuous in her poverty; she is angry about it.

Bella's journey from this starting point to a woman who can reject [[money|wealth]] for love is the novel's central [[morality|moral]] argument. Her education is not abstract but experiential: she watches, and participates in, [[the-boffin-household|the Boffins]]' apparent [[morality|moral]] decline; she is courted by the humble secretary [[john-harmon|John Rokesmith]]; she learns, through trial and error, to distinguish genuine worth from its counterfeits. When she finally accepts Rokesmith's proposal — choosing a [[poverty|poor]] secretary over the [[money|wealth]]y suitors she once imagined — she has earned the right to receive the Harmon [[money|fortune]] as a gift rather than a condition.

## Role in the Novel

Bella is the novel's [[morality|moral]] protagonist — the character whose psychological journey most fully embodies Dickens's argument about money, love, and human worth. Structurally, she serves as the hinge between the novel's two worlds: born into the shabby-genteel poverty of the Wilfer household, she enters [[the-boffin-household|the Boffins]]' world of new [[money|wealth]], where she is educated through observation and experience. Her eventual union with Harmon/Rokesmith resolves the novel's [[money|inheritance]] plot on terms of love rather than legal compulsion.

Bella is also the novel's most psychologically vivid character. Her moods — petulance, tenderness, greed, generosity — are rendered with a complexity unusual even for Dickens. She is capable of extraordinary self-awareness ("It was ridiculous enough to know I shouldn't like him — how could I like him, left to him in a will, like a dozen of spoons," 004-050) and extraordinary self-[[appearances-and-reality|deception]]. Her growth is credible precisely because she begins so far from [[morality|virtue]].

## Key Relationships

- **[[john-harmon]]** — The man who [[justice-and-law|courts]] her as Rokesmith and reveals himself as Harmon only after she has chosen love over money. T[[inheritance|heir]] relationship is the novel's demonstration that marriage must be freely chosen, not legally compelled.
- **[[nicodemus-boffin]]** — The Golden [[dust|Dust]]man whose feigned miserliness teaches Bella what money can do to a person. Her horrified response to "miser-Boffin" is the culmination of her [[morality|moral]] education.
- **[[r-wilfer]]** — Her father, the cheerful clerk whose shabby gentility Bella both loves and resents. T[[inheritance|heir]] affectionate relationship ("the cherub") provides the novel's warmest portrait of parent-child love.

## Thematic Significance

Bella represents Dickens's argument that [[morality|moral]] growth is achieved, not innate. She begins mercenary and becomes generous; she begins valuing money and learns to value love. Her transformation is credible because it is gradual and because Dickens does not sentimentalise her starting point — her poverty is genuinely degrading, her resentment genuinely earned. The novel's radical implication is that [[morality|virtue]] is not a fixed quality of character but an achievement that requires the right conditions ([[the-boffin-household|the Boffins]]' love, Rokesmith's patience) and the right choices.
