# Lizzie Hexam

## Overview

Lizzie Hexam is the [[morality|moral]] lodestar of *Our Mutual Friend* — the character against whom all others are measured and found wanting. Daughter of [[gaffer-hexam|Gaffer Hexam]], the [[the-river-thames|river]]-scavenger who dredges corpses from the [[the-river-thames|Thames]], Lizzie is introduced in the novel's opening scene rowing her father's boat through the London dark (001-001). She is defined by loyalty — to her father, whose trade she abhors but whom she will not abandon; to her brother [[charley-hexam|Charley]], whose education she funds with her labour; to [[eugene-wrayburn|Eugene Wrayburn]], the gentleman whose love for her transgresses every boundary of Victorian [[social-class|class]].

Lizzie's [[morality|moral]] authority derives not from formal education (she teaches herself to read from [[charley-hexam|Charley]]'s discarded books) but from an innate capacity to see clearly and love genuinely. In a novel where every character performs, Lizzie is incapable of performance — she is what she appears to be. Her flight from [[eugene-wrayburn|Eugene]]'s advances, her care for him after [[eugene-wrayburn-near-murder|Headstone's attack]], and her eventual marriage to him represent the novel's most radical assertion: that [[morality|moral]] worth has no relation to social [[social-class|class]], and that the truest [[morality|virtue]] may be found in the character with the least social standing.

## Role in the Novel

Lizzie functions as the novel's [[morality|moral]] touchstone — the character whose judgment the reader learns to trust. Her refusal of [[eugene-wrayburn|Eugene]]'s initial advances (which she correctly identifies as predatory despite his gentlemanly surface) demonstrates a [[morality|moral]] intelligence that formal education cannot confer. Her devotion to her undeserving brother [[charley-hexam|Charley]], who repays her sacrifice with contempt, exposes the [[morality|moral]] bankruptcy of his aspirational climb. Her care for the dying [[betty-higden|Betty Higden]] shows compassion in action.

Her relationship with [[eugene-wrayburn|Eugene]] is the novel's most challenging plot: a cross-[[social-class|class]] love that requires [[eugene-wrayburn|Eugene]]'s near-[[death-and-resurrection|death]] and [[morality|moral]] transformation to become legitimate. Lizzie's eventual marriage to him — as Mrs. Eugene Wrayburn — is not a conventional happy ending but a hard-won achievement that requires both characters to transcend the expectations of t[[inheritance|heir]] respective [[social-class|class]]es.

## Key Relationships

- **[[gaffer-hexam]]** — Her father, whose trade she detests but whom she refuses to abandon. T[[inheritance|heir]] relationship — fierce, conflicted, bound by the [[the-river-thames|river]] — is the novel's most powerful portrait of filial loyalty.
- **[[charley-hexam]]** — Her brother, whose education she funds and who repays her with contempt. [[charley-hexam|Charley]]'s rejection of Lizzie is the novel's sharpest indictment of education divorced from [[morality|moral]] obligation.
- **[[eugene-wrayburn]]** — The gentleman whose love for Lizzie transgresses every [[social-class|class]] boundary. T[[inheritance|heir]] relationship — from his initial predatory interest to his near-[[death-and-resurrection|death]] and her devoted care to t[[inheritance|heir]] eventual marriage — is the novel's most radical love plot.

## Thematic Significance

Lizzie embodies the novel's democratic [[morality|moral]] vision: that worth is a function of character, not [[social-class|class]]; that the truest [[morality|virtue]] may be found in the [[poverty|poor]]est dwelling; that love, not money, is the only reliable measure of human value. Her self-taught literacy, her [[morality|moral]] intelligence, and her capacity for forgiveness make her Dickens's most complete argument against a [[social-class|society]] that equates social standing with human worth.
