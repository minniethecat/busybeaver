# Bella Wilfer's Moral Transformation

## Overview

Bella Wilfer's moral transformation is the novel's most carefully orchestrated piece of moral education. Raised in the shabby-genteel Wilfer household, where money is perpetually short and perpetually discussed, Bella has learned to value wealth above all else. Her initial response to the Harmon inheritance — she is the "widow" of a man she never met, entitled to nothing but resentful of everything — is the novel's diagnosis of a society that teaches young women to value money over love.

The transformation is engineered by Nicodemus Boffin. Recognizing that Bella has been taught greed but is not naturally greedy, he stages an elaborate performance: pretending to become a miser, he demonstrates to Bella what the love of money does to a person. Watching the "Golden Dustman" transform into a grasping, suspicious hoarder — and watching John Rokesmith's quiet decency throughout — Bella learns the lesson Boffin intends. She chooses Rokesmith over the fortune she once coveted, and the novel celebrates her choice as a genuine moral achievement.

## Narrative Context

Bella enters the novel as a problematic figure: she is beautiful, intelligent, and honest about her mercenary nature. Her declaration that she "hates poverty" and intends to marry for money is not hypocrisy but candor — and this honesty is what makes her redeemable. Boffin sees this. His "miser" performance — dismissing Rokesmith, growing suspicious and grasping — is a mirror held up to Bella's own stated values. Watching Boffin's transformation horrifies her because she recognizes in it the person she might become.

Her choice of Rokesmith — who has nothing but his salary — is her moral climax. She renounces the wealth she once pursued, choosing love over money. The revelation that Rokesmith is actually John Harmon, the heir to the fortune she renounced, is the novel's reward for her moral growth: she gets the wealth she stopped wanting.

## Key Participants

- **[[bella-wilfer]]** — The transformand. Her journey from "I hate poverty" to "I choose you over money" is the novel's most complete moral arc.
- **[[nicodemus-boffin]]** — The moral educator. His "miser" performance is the novel's most elaborate act of generosity — pretending to be corrupted by money to save Bella from that corruption.
- **[[john-harmon]]** — As Rokesmith, he is the penniless man Bella learns to love. His concealment of his identity makes Bella's choice genuine: she chooses him believing he has nothing.

## Scene Description

Boffin's miser performance unfolds gradually across Book III. He dismisses Rokesmith, grows suspicious of those around him, and begins to speak of his wealth with the grasping anxiety of a true miser. Bella watches, horrified:

> "Mr. Boffin, being grown immensely rich, was being corrupted by his wealth." (Book III)

The climax comes when Bella confronts Boffin about his treatment of Rokesmith — defending the penniless secretary against the wealthy master. In that moment, she proves that her moral education is complete: she has learned to value character over money, and her defense of Rokesmith is her defense of her own transformed self.

## Thematic Significance

Bella's transformation is the novel's argument that moral education is possible — that a person taught to value the wrong things can learn to value the right ones. But it is also the novel's most artificial plot: Bella is transformed not by life but by a staged performance. The novel acknowledges, through this artifice, that genuine moral education may require the kind of elaborate intervention that only fiction can provide.
