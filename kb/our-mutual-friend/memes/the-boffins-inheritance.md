# The Boffins' Inheritance

## Overview

The Boffins' inheritance of the Harmon fortune is the novel's great social experiment. Nicodemus and Henrietta Boffin — Old Harmon's loyal servants — become, through the presumed death of John Harmon, the wealthiest dust-contractors in London. The event transforms them from domestics who served the miserly Old Harmon into the "Golden Dustman" and his wife, proprietors of Harmony Jail (soon renamed Boffin's Bower) and custodians of the great dust-heaps.

The inheritance is the novel's test case for its central question: what does wealth do to people? The Boffins' answer — generosity, loyalty, and genuine affection — stands in pointed contrast to every other wealthy character's response. They are the novel's proof that money need not corrupt, that sudden wealth can express rather than destroy character.

## Narrative Context

The inheritance is triggered by John Harmon's presumed drowning. Old Harmon's will, bypassing his son's claim (invalid without the Bella Wilfer condition), leaves everything to the Boffins. Mortimer Lightwood administers the transfer. The Boffins move from their modest situation to Harmony Jail, bringing with them their working-class sensibilities and unshakeable decency.

The consequences ripple through every plot. Silas Wegg sees the dust-heaps as his opportunity. Bella Wilfer, as the "widow" of the presumed-dead Harmon, becomes the Boffins' ward. John Harmon himself — alive, disguised as Rokesmith — becomes the Boffins' secretary, observing from within the household that should have been his.

## Key Participants

- **[[nicodemus-boffin]]** — The inheritor. His transformation from servant to "Golden Dustman" is the novel's most complete study of how wealth expresses character rather than changing it.
- **[[john-harmon]]** — The rightful heir, presumed dead. His decision to work as the Boffins' secretary places him in the ambiguous position of serving those who hold his fortune.
- **[[mortimer-lightwood]]** — The solicitor who administers the transfer. His legal machinery converts a death into a fortune.
- **[[silas-wegg]]** — The ballad-monger who sees the Boffins' inheritance as his opportunity. Every scheme he hatches targets their wealth.
- **[[bella-wilfer]]** — The "widow" who becomes the Boffins' ward. Her moral education begins in the Boffin household.

## Scene Description

The Boffins receive the news through Lightwood — the quintessentially legal moment when a document transforms a servant into a master. Their response is characteristic: not greed but bewilderment, not ambition but a determination to "do right" by the fortune they never expected.

The move to Harmony Jail — the miser's house surrounded by dust-heaps — is the physical enactment of the inheritance. The Boffins' transformation of the house from prison to bower, and their maintenance of their working-class decency despite their wealth, is the novel's argument that money is neutral: it amplifies what is already there.

## Thematic Significance

The Boffins' inheritance is the novel's central social argument: that wealth does not determine character, but reveals it. The same fortune that made Old Harmon a miser makes the Boffins generous. The novel's most radical claim is that the Boffins — uneducated, working-class, wholly without the Veneerings' "polish" — are morally superior to every wealthy character in the book. Their inheritance is not a corruption but a vindication.
