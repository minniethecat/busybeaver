Old Harmon — John Harmon Sr. — is the man whose [[death-and-resurrection|death]] and [[inheritance|testament]]ary dispositions generate the entire machinery of the novel's plot, yet he never appears alive in its pages. A [[dust|dust-contractor]] who made his [[money|fortune]] from London's refuse — the [[dust|dust-heaps]] that loom over the novel as symbols of [[money|wealth]] extracted from waste — he is the archetypal Dickensian miser-father, ruling his household with the same tyrannical parsimony that built his [[money|fortune]].

"Old Harmon's" (005-117): the place-name itself — the dust-heap referred to simply by its owner's name — signals the degree to which Old Harmon's [[identity-and-disguise|identity]] is fused with his accumulation. He is his property. The hoarse-voiced informant who directs [[charley-hexam|Charley Hexam]] to the site speaks the name as though it explains everything: the heap, the [[money|fortune]], the [[inheritance|legacy]], the loneliness. "On accounts of Old Harmon living solitary there" (005-125): the solitude of the miser, living among his heaps, is the defining biographical fact available to the public.

## Role in the Novel

Old Harmon is the absent centre of the novel's [[money|inheritance]] plot. His will — which conditions his son [[john-harmon|John Harmon]]'s [[money|inheritance]] upon marriage to [[bella-wilfer|Bella Wilfer]], a young woman Harmon has never met, and names [[nicodemus-boffin|Nicodemus Boffin]] as secondary [[inheritance|heir]] — is the mechanism that sets every major character in motion. The will is Old Harmon's revenge from beyond the grave: a final exercise of control over the son he raised with "the most grinding discipline" and whose affections he never secured.

The [[dust|dust-heaps]] themselves are Old Harmon's afterlife. They are monuments to his industry and his miserliness, and t[[inheritance|heir]] eventual dispersal — carted away, sold, scattered — parallels the dissolution of his [[money|fortune]] through the novel's machinery of [[money|inheritance]], fraud, and redistribution.

## Key Relationships

**[[john-harmon|John Harmon]]**: Old Harmon's relationship with his son is the novel's founding trauma. He raised John harshly, disinherited him emotionally, and then attempted to control him from beyond the grave through the marriage condition in his will. The son's return, presumed [[death-and-resurrection|drowning]], and assumption of the Rokesmith [[identity-and-disguise|identity]] are all consequences of Old Harmon's paternal failure.

**[[nicodemus-boffin|Nicodemus Boffin]]**: Boffin is Old Harmon's old servant and foreman, promoted by the will to [[inheritance|heir]]-in-waiting. The relationship between the illiterate golden-hearted dustman and the miserly [[dust|dust-contractor]] is the novel's most pointed contrast between two kinds of [[money|wealth]].

## Thematic Significance

Old Harmon represents the novel's central question: what is [[money|wealth]] for? His [[money|fortune]], extracted from London's waste, is the novel's great symbol of value transmuted from filth. But his failure to transmit that value into anything humanly worthwhile — his son's unhappiness, his employee's bafflement, his [[money|fortune]]'s corruption of everyone who touches it — is the novel's indictment of accumulation without purpose.

The [[dust|dust-heaps]] that survive him are the physical manifestation of this theme. They are valuable but repellent, solid but ultimately dispersible, a [[money|fortune]] built on what [[social-class|society]] throws away. "Cut away to Old Harmon's" (005-123): the phrase suggests not a home but a destination — the heap as the man, the man as the heap.
