# The Veneering Household

## Overview

The Veneering household is the novel's great architectural joke. Its inhabitants, Mr. and Mrs. Veneering, are "bran-new people in a bran-new house in a bran-new quarter of London" — everything about them, from their furniture to their friends, has been acquired for the purpose of appearing to have always possessed it. The house is a stage set where the performance of wealth and status is conducted with deadening regularity, and where the only genuine thing is the mutual indifference of the performers.

Dickens's satire of the Veneering household operates at the level of the furniture. The table is the household's true protagonist: it is around the table that the "friends" are assembled, that the campaign for Veneering's election to Parliament is launched, and that the novel's most sustained critique of social performance takes place. The Veneerings' possessions — "everything about the Veneerings was spick and span new" — are not expressions of taste or personality but instruments of social ambition. The house is not a home; it is a mechanism for manufacturing respectability.

The household's particular horror is its interchangeability. The Veneerings are not uniquely awful — they are awfully generic. Any other bran-new couple in any other bran-new house could host the same dinner parties, recruit the same friends, and achieve the same emptiness. The house, like its inhabitants, has no history, no character, and no claim on anyone's affection.

## In the Novel

The Veneering household appears throughout the novel as the foil to Boffin's Bower. Where the Bower transforms from jail to garden through genuine affection, the Veneering house remains what it always was: a display case. The contrast structures the novel's social argument: wealth that expresses love (the Boffins) creates community; wealth that performs status (the Veneerings) creates only the appearance of it.

The household's most significant narrative function is its role in the Veneering election plot. The dinner table becomes a campaign headquarters, where political influence is traded over champagne and the distinction between friend and patron, principle and convenience, dissolves into the sauce. Twemlow, the novel's "innocent," sits at this table trying to understand what obligates him to support Veneering's candidacy — and the answer, which the novel makes clear, is nothing except the social fiction that dining together creates obligation (Book II).

The Veneering household's collapse — as inevitable as the Boffins' flourishing — is the novel's verdict on a society that values appearance over substance. When the Veneerings' financial situation deteriorates, their friends disappear with the efficiency of a mechanism designed for exactly this eventuality. The house, stripped of its social function, reveals itself as what it always was: empty rooms.

## Associated Characters

- **Twemlow** — The novel's "innocent at the dinner table," whose confusion about social obligation is the moral barometer of the Veneering circle. His anguished question — "What do I owe these people?" — is the question the novel asks about all social performance.
- **[[mortimer-lightwood]]** — A Veneering "friend" whose detachment allows him to observe the circle's absurdities without being contaminated by them. He attends the dinners but refuses to be defined by them.
- **[[eugene-wrayburn]]** — Another reluctant diner, whose indolent presence at the Veneering table is a form of passive resistance to its claims.
- **[[nicodemus-boffin]]** — The contrast figure: he hosts no dinner parties, performs no social rituals, and creates genuine community through generosity rather than performance.
