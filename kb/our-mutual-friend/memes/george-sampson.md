George Sampson is a minor figure in *Our Mutual Friend*, significant primarily for what he represents in [[bella-wilfer|Bella Wilfer]]'s romantic history. He is the suitor [[bella-wilfer|Bella]] rejected before the novel begins — "George Sampson was very fond of me, and admired me," she recalls (004-046) — and his inadequacy serves as a benchmark against which her eventual choice of [[john-harmon|John Rokesmith]] is measured.

[[bella-wilfer|Bella]]'s sister Lavinia taunts her about Sampson with characteristic sharpness: "You caught George Sampson with it, [[bella-wilfer|Bella]], bad as your mother" (004-102). The accusation is that [[bella-wilfer|Bella]], like her mercenary mother, uses her beauty to attract admirers. [[bella-wilfer|Bella]]'s rejection of Sampson — and her irritation at being reminded of him — marks the beginning of her [[morality|moral]] trajectory: she has already rejected the merely adequate in favour of something she cannot yet name.

His later appearances at the Boffin household — where "Mr. George Sampson's legs" (009-079) are noted among the visitors — reduce him to a purely physical, almost comic presence. He is a man defined by his rejection, a ghost of a romantic possibility that was never seriously entertained.

## Role in the Novel

George Sampson exists to populate the novel's romantic background. His function is to demonstrate that [[bella-wilfer|Bella]] had options before [[john-harmon|John Rokesmith]] — and that those options, though socially acceptable, were inadequate to her emerging character. He is the "before" in a before-and-after portrait of [[bella-wilfer|Bella]]'s [[morality|moral]] development.
