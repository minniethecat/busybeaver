Mr. Riah is one of the most significant characters in *Our Mutual Friend* for reasons that extend beyond the novel's plot. Created by Dickens in partial response to criticism of the Fagin stereotype in *Oliver Twist*, Riah is a deliberate counter-portrait: a Jewish moneylender who is gentle, honourable, and [[morality|moral]]ly superior to the Christian "gentleman" who exploits him.

Riah is the public face of Pubsey and Co., the moneylending business actually owned by [[mr-fledgeby|Fascination Fledgeby]]. While Riah absorbs the social contempt directed at the visible Jew, Fledgeby — the real predator — enjoys the protected status of a Christian gentleman. This arrangement allows Dickens to dramatise the structural nature of anti-Semitism: the stereotype of the Jewish moneylender persists not because it is true but because the real exploiters hide behind Jewish fronts.

His friendship with [[jenny-wren|Jenny Wren]] is one of the novel's tenderest relationships. She, with characteristic directness, calls him her "fairy godmother" — a recognition of his protective, nurturing role that transcends gender and, implicitly, religious categories. His care for [[lizzie-hexam|Lizzie Hexam]] similarly demonstrates a generosity that the novel's Christian characters frequently fail to match.

## Role in the Novel

Riah functions as a [[morality|moral]] corrective on multiple levels. Within the plot, he serves as the counterweight to Fledgeby's [[crime|villainy]] — the honourable face of a dishonourable enterprise. Within the novel's [[morality|moral]] architecture, he demonstrates that [[morality|virtue]] is not distributed by religion or race. And within Dickens's own career, he represents an author's attempt to atone for a harmful stereotype.

## Key Relationships

### Fascination Fledgeby

The Fledgeby–Riah relationship is the novel's most pointed critique of the intersection between capitalism and prejudice. Fledgeby's casual exploitation of Riah — "They say, 'We know, Mr. Riah, we know'" (022-118) — demonstrates how anti-Semitic assumptions enable the real predators to operate invisibly.

### Jenny Wren

Riah's friendship with [[jenny-wren|the dolls' dressmaker]] is built on mutual recognition of each other's fundamental decency. Jenny, who is merciless toward Fledgeby, treats Riah with genuine affection. T[[inheritance|heir]] rooftop garden meetings provide some of the novel's quietest and most hopeful moments.

## Thematic Significance

Riah is crucial to understanding Dickens's evolving treatment of Jewish characters. The author had been publicly criticised for Fagin, and Riah — along with his speech in defence of his people — represents a deliberate attempt to create a Jewish character of unambiguously positive [[morality|moral]] stature. Whether the portrait succeeds in transcending sentimentality is debated; that it represents a serious [[morality|moral]] effort is undeniable.
