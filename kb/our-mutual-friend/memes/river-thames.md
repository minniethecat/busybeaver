The River Thames is the major waterway flowing through London and the novel's opening setting. In *Our Mutual Friend*, the Thames functions as both a physical geography and a potent symbol — of death and recovery, of the city's commercial life, and of the boundary between different strata of Victorian society.

## Opening Scene

The novel opens on the Thames, with [[gaffer-hexam|Gaffer Hexam]] and his daughter [[lizzie-hexam|Lizzie Hexam]] rowing a small boat between [[london-bridge|London Bridge]] and Southwark Bridge. Dickens paints the river as a liminal space — neither land nor water, where the living and the dead intersect.

> "a boat of dirty and disreputable appearance, with two figures in it, floated on the Thames, between Southwark bridge which is of iron, and London Bridge which is of stone, as an autumn evening was closing in" (001-001)

## Death and the River

Gaffer Hexam makes his living by recovering dead bodies from the Thames — a grim but lucrative trade. The river delivers corpses to him, including the body presumed to be [[john-harmon|John Harmon]] (003-169, 003-170). This trade establishes the Thames as an underworld economy, where death becomes a commodity.

> "his father's calling, in having rescued from the Thames so many dead bodies" (003-169)

## The Thames as Setting

The river serves as a backdrop for key moments in the novel. The [[boffins-bower|Boffin residence]] sits near the Thames at [[hammersmith|Hammersmith]], described as "on the brink of the Thames, with a lawn, and a boathouse" (012-001). Characters cross the Thames repeatedly — via [[london-bridge|London Bridge]], [[westminster-bridge|Westminster Bridge]], or by boat — connecting the Surrey and Middlesex shores of London.

## Symbolism

| Aspect | Significance in the Novel |
|--------|--------------------------|
| Death and Recovery | The river is a source of corpses, linking water with mortality and Gaffer Hexam's livelihood |
| Commerce | The "Thames lightermen" (035-083) and riverside trade represent London's commercial arteries |
| Social Boundary | Crossing the Thames marks transitions between different social worlds — from the City to the West End, from poverty to wealth |
| The River's Course | The "upward course of the river Thames" (041-006) traces the geography of characters' lives, from the docklands to the genteel suburbs |

## The Thames in Dickens's London

In the 1860s, the Thames was both London's main commercial artery and an open sewer. Dickens's portrayal captures this duality — the river is a source of livelihood for watermen like Gaffer Hexam, a route for pleasure excursions to [[greenwich|Greenwich]] (054-031), and a repository of the city's dead. The "water enough in all the Thames" (051-088) becomes a darkly ironic reference to suicide by drowning.
