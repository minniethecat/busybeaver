# Crime

## Overview

*Our Mutual Friend* opens with an act that hovers between salvage and theft — Gaffer Hexam dredging a corpse from the Thames and pocketing the coins in its pockets — and it never resolves the question this opening poses: where is the boundary between crime and commerce, between predation and profession, between murder and the ordinary violence of a society organised by money?

Dickens presents crime not as a distinct category of human behaviour but as a spectrum along which every character is positioned. At one end are acts of unambiguous violence: Bradley Headstone's attempted murder of Eugene Wrayburn, Rogue Riderhood's blackmail. At the other are the socially sanctioned predations of the Veneerings and Lammles, whose operations are legal but morally indistinguishable from theft. In between lie Gaffer Hexam's corpse-robbing (crime by legal definition, profession by his own), Silas Wegg's scheming (theft by any name), and the entire credit economy that sustains "Society" while destroying the poor.

The novel's most radical implication is that crime is not a deviation from the social order but its logical extension. When money is the measure of all things, the difference between a dust-contractor and a corpse-robber, between a marriage-broker and a blackmailer, between a creditor and an extortionist — these differences are matters of degree, not kind.

## Expression in the Novel

### Gaffer Hexam and the Ambiguity of Scavenging

Gaffer's trade — dredging bodies from the river and taking what they carry — is the novel's founding ambiguity. He insists it is honest work: "Has a dead man any use for money? ... What world does money belong to? This world" (001-042). By his logic, taking from the dead is not theft because the dead cannot own. The law disagrees, but the novel refuses to resolve the question, leaving Gaffer's trade in the grey zone between profession and predation where so much of Victorian commerce operated.

### Bradley Headstone: The Respectable Criminal

Headstone is the novel's most disturbing criminal because he is so thoroughly respectable. A schoolmaster with certificates, a man who has earned his position through "the sweat of his brow" (012-073), he is driven to attempted murder not by poverty or desperation but by sexual jealousy and class resentment. His crime is not a fall from respectability but its natural expression — the violence that respectability contains and channels rather than eliminates.

### Rogue Riderhood: Crime as Profession

Riderhood is the novel's professional criminal, but his professionalism is indistinguishable from the professionalism of any other character. He offers testimony for reward, trades information for cash, and attempts blackmail — all with the same calculating self-interest that drives the Lammles' marriage schemes or Wegg's mound-prodding. Riderhood's death, locked in the drowning embrace of Headstone, is the novel's final image of crime: two predators destroying each other in the element that has sustained them both.

### The Veneerings and Lammles: Legal Predation

The Veneerings live entirely on credit and collapse into bankruptcy; the Lammles hunt wealthy young people for the marriage market. Both are perfectly legal. Neither is morally distinguishable from the criminals the law condemns. Dickens's point is structural: a society that measures everything by money cannot distinguish between the crime that enriches and the commerce that impoverishes, because both operate by the same logic.

## Victorian Context

The 1860s saw intense public fascination with crime, fed by the expansion of the popular press, the rise of detective fiction, and high-profile murder trials. The "sensation novel" — of which *Our Mutual Friend* is in part an example — exploited the gap between respectable surfaces and criminal realities. But Dickens's treatment of crime is more radical than the sensation genre's: rather than exposing hidden criminality beneath respectability, he suggests that respectability and criminality are points on a single spectrum. The dust-contractor and the corpse-robber, the marriage-broker and the blackmailer, operate by the same logic — the conversion of human relations into cash. In a commercial society, the novel implies, crime is not an aberration but an intensification of the norm.
