# Smithfield Market

## Overview

Smithfield — the great London livestock market — is the city's most unapologetically material space. Here, animals are bought and sold, slaughtered and butchered, the living converted into commodities with brutal efficiency. It is the ground-level economy that the Veneering dinner table — with its polished silver and arranged flowers — pretends does not exist. The meat that appears, transformed and garnished, on fashionable plates originates in the blood and noise of Smithfield.

Dickens knew Smithfield well and had campaigned against its cruelties in his journalism. In *Our Mutual Friend*, the market appears primarily through its proximity to other locations — Church Street, where Jenny Wren lives and works, is near Smithfield — and through the novel's broader critique of the city's economy. The market's function is to remind the reader that every act of consumption has a material origin, and that the city's refinement is built on a foundation of the slaughter and exploitation it prefers not to see.

Smithfield is not a place where the novel's principal scenes occur; it is, rather, an atmospheric presence — a reminder of what lies just beyond the edges of the narrative. Like the dust-heaps (the residue of consumption) and the river (the recipient of the city's dead), Smithfield (the source of consumption) is part of the novel's material infrastructure: the unmentionable origins of the polite world's plenitude.

## In the Novel

Smithfield's most significant narrative function is as a geographical marker. Its proximity to Church Street places Jenny Wren's creative workshop in suggestive relationship to the city's most brutal commerce. The dolls' dressmaker, who transforms waste into beauty, works within earshot of the slaughter that turns animals into meat. The juxtaposition is not accidental: both Jenny and the butcher are engaged in transforming raw material into product, but Jenny's transformation creates value without violence.

The market also serves as a counterpoint to the Veneering table. The Veneerings' dinner parties — where food appears as if by magic, without origin or cost — are the willful antithesis of Smithfield. The market is what the Veneerings must not see, must not know, must not acknowledge. And their determination not to see it is precisely what Dickens condemns.
