# The Riverside Community

## Overview

The informal community of watermen, lightermen, and their families centered on the Six Jolly Fellowship Porters — the novel's most fully realized working-class collective, governed by Abbey Potterson's moral authority.

The organization functions as both a social unit and a narrative mechanism in *Our Mutual Friend*. Through its members' interactions, Dickens explores the novel's central themes: the relationship between wealth and worth, the fluidity of identity, the moral emptiness of social performance, or the dignity of honest community. The organization's structure — whether a family bound by blood, a household assembled by circumstance, or a community forged by shared labour — determines the kind of relationships it produces and the moral quality of those relationships.

## Structure and Function

The organization operates within the novel's broader social geography. Its position — geographical, economic, and moral — defines the possibilities available to its members. Some organizations in the novel enable genuine human connection; others prevent it. Some foster growth and transformation; others enforce stasis and performance. The structure of the organization is not neutral: it shapes the character of everyone within it.

## In the Novel

The organization appears throughout the novel as both setting and subject. Its functioning — the way decisions are made, authority exercised, loyalty tested, and betrayal enacted — is the novel's laboratory for examining how human relationships are structured by social forms. The organization's fate — whether it endures, transforms, or collapses — is one measure of the novel's moral judgment.

## Thematic Significance

The organization embodies the novel's argument about the relationship between social structure and moral character. Institutions do not determine character — the same family produces a saint (Lizzie) and a moral failure (Charley) — but they shape the conditions under which character is formed and tested. The novel's most radical claim is that the best organizations are not the wealthiest or most powerful but those that enable genuine human connection: the Boffin household over the Veneering circle, the Fellowship Porters over the Podsnap drawing room, the informal community of the riverside over the formal institutions of law and education.
