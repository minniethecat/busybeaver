# Silas Wegg

## Overview

Silas Wegg is the novel's comic villain — a one-legged ballad-monger who keeps a street stall selling "fruit, sweetstuff, and baked taturs" near Cavendish Square, and who becomes the instrument of the dust-plot counter-conspiracy. Hired by [[nicodemus-boffin|Nicodemus Boffin]] to read to him from *The Decline and Fall of the Roman Empire*, Wegg uses his position to search for a second Harmon will that would disinherit Boffin entirely. When he finds it (in a dust-heap, the novel's master symbol), he attempts [[crime|blackmail]] — only to discover that Boffin has been playing a deeper game all along.

Wegg is Dickens's satirical portrait of the self-educated man who mistakes literacy for [[morality|moral]] superiority. His catchphrase — "I'm a literary man — with a wooden leg" — is the novel's most concise statement of its running joke about the gap between [[education|learning]] and wisdom. His wooden leg, bought from [[mr-venus|Mr. Venus]] [[mr-venus|the articulator]], is both comic prop and [[morality|moral]] symbol: a prosthetic [[identity-and-disguise|identity]] as suspect as his claims to [[education|learning]].

## Role in the Novel

Wegg drives the novel's dust-plot counter-conspiracy. His discovery of the second will in the dust-heap, his alliance with [[mr-venus|Mr. Venus]], and his attempted [[crime|blackmail]] of Boffin constitute the novel's most sustained treatment of greed's self-defeating logic. Time and again, Wegg reaches for the Harmon [[money|fortune]] only to find it slip through his fingers — the dust-heap rewards no one.

His relationship with Boffin is the novel's most sustained comic set-piece: [[nicodemus-boffin|the Golden Dustman]] paying the one-legged ballad-monger to read Gibbon, neither understanding the text, both performing a ceremony of [[education|self-improvement]] that exposes the hollowness of "improving" culture. When Wegg turns on Boffin, his betrayal is both villainous and pitiful — he is, finally, a man who mistook proximity to [[money|wealth]] for entitlement to it.

## Key Relationships

- **[[nicodemus-boffin]]** — The Golden [[dust|Dust]]man who hires Wegg as reader and becomes the target of his [[crime|blackmail]] scheme. T[[inheritance|heir]] relationship is the novel's most elaborate satire of the "improving" instinct — two uneducated men performing culture for each other, neither benefiting, both exposed.
- **[[mr-venus]]** — The articulator of bones who holds Wegg's wooden leg in hock and becomes his reluctant co-conspirator. T[[inheritance|heir]] partnership — the "literary man" and [[mr-venus|the bone-grinder]] — is Dickens at his most grotesquely comic (005-088).
- **[[mrs-boffin]]** — The target of Wegg's contempt ("a high-flyer at Fashion") and the object of his would-be [[morality|moral]] superiority. His dismissal of her exposes his own pretension.

## Thematic Significance

Wegg represents the novel's argument that proximity to culture — to books, to [[money|wealth]], to gentility — does not confer [[morality|moral]] worth. He owns books but cannot read them properly; he handles the [[dust|dust-heaps]] but cannot extract value from them; he discovers the will but cannot profit from it. His wooden leg is the perfect emblem of his [[morality|moral]] condition: something that looks like a leg from a distance but cannot walk. In a novel where the [[the-river-thames|river]] dredges up real gold ([[lizzie-hexam|Lizzie]]'s love, [[eugene-wrayburn|Eugene]]'s transformation), Wegg's dust-heap yields only fool's gold.
