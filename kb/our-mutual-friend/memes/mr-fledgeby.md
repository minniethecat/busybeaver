"Fascination" Fledgeby — so called with heavy irony — is one of the most thoroughly unpleasant characters in *Our Mutual Friend*. A young man of independent means who operates as a clandestine moneylender behind the respectable front of the elderly Jew [[mr-riah|Mr. Riah]] and the firm of Pubsey and Co., Fledgeby combines [[greed|avarice]], cowardice, and social pretension in a figure that represents everything Dickens despised about unregulated finance capital.

Fledgeby's method is to conceal his ownership of the moneylending business, using [[mr-riah|Riah]] as the public face while he collects the profits and evades both social stigma and legal responsibility. This arrangement — the respectable gentleman who profits from usury while keeping his hands visibly clean — is Dickens's indictment of a financial system that separates [[morality|moral]] accountability from economic gain. When [[jenny-wren|Jenny Wren]] confronts him, calling him a "grasping, clutching, mercenary wretch," she speaks for the novel's [[morality|moral]] judgment on his type.

## Role in the Novel

Fledgeby functions as the novel's embodiment of financial predation dressed in gentleman's clothing. Unlike the overtly criminal figures of [[limehouse|the riverside]] — [[rogue-riderhood|Rogue Riderhood]] or the corpse-robbers — Fledgeby operates entirely within [[mincing-lane|the law]], protected by the structures of [[social-class|respectability]] that his [[money|wealth]] purchases. His [[crime|villainy]] is bureaucratic rather than violent, which makes it, in Dickens's treatment, all the more insidious.

His two main plot functions are intertwined. First, he is the silent partner of Pubsey and Co., extracting ruinous interest from borrowers while [[mr-riah|Riah]] absorbs the public hatred that should rightfully be directed at him. Second, he is the would-be suitor to [[georgiana-podsnap|Georgiana Podsnap]], enlisted by the [[alfred-lammle|Lammles]] in a scheme to secure her dowry. In this second role, Fledgeby's cowardice and social ineptitude are on full display: "Young Fledgeby was none of these" (021-070) — neither handsome nor charming nor even competently villainous. He is thwarted not by heroic intervention but by [[mrs-lammle|Mrs. Lammle]]'s belated [[morality|conscience]] and [[georgiana-podsnap|Georgiana]]'s flight.

## Key Relationships

### Mr. Riah

Fledgeby's exploitation of [[mr-riah|Riah]] is one of the novel's sharpest critiques of anti-Semitic stereotyping. [[mr-riah|Riah]], the gentle and honourable Jew, is the public face of Fledgeby's predatory lending; [[social-class|society]] directs its contempt at the visible Jew while the real predator — the Christian gentleman — goes unremarked. Fledgeby treats [[mr-riah|Riah]] with casual contempt, knowing that t[[inheritance|heir]] arrangement depends on [[mr-riah|Riah]]'s vulnerability to anti-Jewish prejudice.

### The Lammles

Fledgeby's dealings with Alfred and [[mrs-lammle|Sophronia Lammle]] reveal his social aspirations. He pursues [[georgiana-podsnap|Georgiana Podsnap]] not from affection but from the calculation that a [[mr-podsnap|Podsnap]] dowry would elevate his standing. The Lammles, for t[[inheritance|heir]] part, regard him as a useful tool — "young Fledgeby goes to that [[alfred-lammle|Alfred Lammle]] and says..." (021-056) — and despise him privately.

### Jenny Wren

Fledgeby's encounters with [[jenny-wren|the dolls' dressmaker]] provide some of the novel's most satisfying comic [[justice-and-law|justice]]. Jenny, whose sharp tongue and physical disability give her a unique immunity to social intimidation, sees through Fledgeby instantly and rebukes him with a directness that no other character dares employ. T[[inheritance|heir]] scenes together function as a kind of [[morality|moral]] court in which Fledgeby is tried and found contemptible.

## Thematic Significance

Fledgeby represents the dissociation of [[morality|moral]] responsibility from economic power that Dickens saw as characteristic of modern capitalism. He never dirties his hands; [[mr-riah|Riah]] faces the angry borrowers while Fledgeby collects the profits. This arrangement mirrors the larger structure of the novel's financial world, in which the Harmon dust [[money|fortune]] — extracted from literal filth — is sanitised by its passage through [[money|inheritance]] and investment.

His nickname, "Fascination," is a bitter joke. There is nothing fascinating about Fledgeby; he is petty, cowardly, and dull. Dickens withholds from him even the dark glamour of a genuine villain like Rigaud in *Little Dorrit*. Fledgeby's particular horror is his banality: he is not a monster but a type, and a common one at that.

The Pubsey and Co. arrangement also allows Dickens to advance one of his most pointed arguments about religious prejudice. By making the Jewish [[mr-riah|Riah]] the [[morality|moral]] superior of the Christian Fledgeby, Dickens — who had been criticised for the Fagin stereotype in *Oliver Twist* — offers a deliberate corrective. The villain of the piece is not the Jew but the Christian gentleman who exploits him.
