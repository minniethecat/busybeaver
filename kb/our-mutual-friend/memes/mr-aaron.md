Mr. Aaron is a minor figure in the novel's moneylending subplot, appearing primarily as an associate of [[mr-riah|Mr. Riah]] in the financial operations controlled by [[mr-fledgeby|Mr. Fledgeby]]. Where [[mr-riah|Riah]] is developed into a figure of considerable [[morality|moral]] complexity, Aaron remains sketch-like — defined more by his function in the plot than by psychological depth. He appears in two key scenes, both of which illuminate the power dynamics of the moneylending world.

The most revealing encounter involving Aaron is his confrontation with [[eugene-wrayburn|Eugene Wrayburn]], who treats him with a mixture of gentlemanly contempt and tactical politeness. "Good evening, Mr. Aaron," says [[eugene-wrayburn|Eugene]], "politely" (032-162), but the politeness is a weapon — the condescension of a gentleman toward a man whose trade places him outside the boundaries of respectable [[social-class|society]].

## Role in the Novel

Aaron serves as a structural device in the novel's exploration of moneylending and its social stigma. His Jewish [[identity-and-disguise|identity]] links him to the broader theme of antisemitic attitudes in Victorian [[social-class|society]], though Dickens's treatment of Aaron is notably less developed than his treatment of [[mr-riah|Riah]], whose [[morality|moral]] dignity is established at length.

In the scene with [[eugene-wrayburn|Eugene Wrayburn]] (032-160–032-175), Aaron functions as a foil: [[eugene-wrayburn|Eugene]]'s gentlemanly bearing exposes the power asymmetry between those who inherit social status and those who must earn t[[inheritance|heir]] living through trade, however disreputable. The later scene with [[mr-fledgeby|Fledgeby]] (043-040–043-048) complicates this picture, as Fledgeby's mockery of Aaron's name and [[identity-and-disguise|identity]] reveals the antisemitic contempt that even disreputable Christians feel entitled to express.

## Key Relationships

- **[[mr-riah|Mr. Riah]]**: Aaron's fellow agent in Fledgeby's operations. The contrast between the two is instructive: where [[mr-riah|Riah]] is developed as a figure of Old Testament dignity, Aaron remains a sketch, suggesting the limits of Dickens's interest in characters at this level of the moneylending hierarchy.
- **[[eugene-wrayburn|Eugene Wrayburn]]**: T[[inheritance|heir]] confrontation (032-160–032-175) dramatises the social gulf between gentleman and tradesman. [[eugene-wrayburn|Eugene]]'s politeness is a form of power.
- **[[mr-fledgeby|Mr. Fledgeby]]**: Fledgeby's attitude toward Aaron — veering between utilitarian dependence and antisemitic contempt — reveals the [[morality|moral]] squalor of the moneylending world from the top down.

## Thematic Significance

Aaron occupies an ambiguous position in the novel's treatment of Jewish [[identity-and-disguise|identity]]. Published in 1864–65, *Our Mutual Friend* reflects the period's complex and often uncomfortable attitudes toward Jewish characters. [[mr-riah|Riah]], created partly in response to criticism of *Oliver Twist*'s Fagin, represents Dickens's attempt at a dignified Jewish portrait. Aaron, by contrast, remains in [[mr-riah|Riah]]'s shadow — a figure whose Jewishness is noted but whose interior life is never explored.

This asymmetry is itself thematically significant: it suggests that even within a single novel, the representation of marginalised [[identity-and-disguise|identity]] can be uneven, shaped as much by narrative convenience as by authorial principle.
