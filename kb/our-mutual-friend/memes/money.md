# Money

## Overview

Money is the engine that drives every plot mechanism and nearly every character motivation in *Our Mutual Friend*. The novel opens with Gaffer Hexam dredging a corpse from the Thames and pocketing the coins in its pockets (001-010), and closes with the final disposition of the Harmon fortune. Between these poles, Dickens constructs a comprehensive anatomy of how money shapes human relations in Victorian England — through inheritance, marriage, work, crime, charity, and social aspiration.

> "Has a dead man any use for money? … What world does money belong to? This world." (001-042)

Gaffer's brutal amoralism — delivered as he pockets money from a drowned man — establishes the novel's insistent question: what claim does the living have on the dead's possessions, and what does the act of claiming do to the claimant?

The novel's central plot springs from the Harmon will, which ties the vast dust-fortune to a marriage condition: John Harmon must marry Bella Wilfer, a woman he has never met, or lose all. This device allows Dickens to examine money not as an abstract force but as something that enters into the most intimate human bonds — courtship, marriage, parent-child relations — and distorts them.

> "I love money, and want money — want it dreadfully. I hate to be poor, and we are degradingly poor, offensively poor, miserably poor, beastly poor." (004-050)

Bella's famous outburst — shocking in its nakedness, deeply sympathetic in its honesty — is the novel's most concentrated statement of money's psychological power. Her speech refuses the sentimental convention that virtue and poverty are natural companions. Money, Bella recognizes, is not merely useful; it is the medium through which dignity itself is transacted in a commercial society.

## Expression in the Novel

### Money as Plot Mechanism

The Harmon will is Dickens's most elaborate inheritance plot. Old Harmon, "a tremendous old rascal who made his money by Dust" (002-051), leaves his fortune to his son on condition of marrying Bella Wilfer — simultaneously, and perversely, leaving the dust-mounds to his faithful servant Nicodemus Boffin if the son fails to comply. When John Harmon is presumed murdered, Boffin inherits and becomes the "Golden Dustman," setting in motion the novel's central moral test: can the Boffins, thrust from servant poverty into immense wealth, remain uncorrupted?

The inheritance plot is mirrored and parodied at every social level. The Lammles marry for money and find each other penniless. The Veneerings build social status on credit and surface. Silas Wegg schemes to extract hidden treasure from the dust-mounds. Even the river-scavengers operate a cash economy of the dead. Money is the connective tissue of the entire narrative.

### Bella Wilfer's Moral Education

Bella's journey from mercenary girl to morally serious woman is the novel's most sustained treatment of money's effect on character. Her early rants are deliberately uncomfortable reading. Dickens refuses to make her sentimentally virtuous. Instead, he shows her gradually learning, through her relationship with John Rokesmith (the disguised Harmon) and the Boffins, that wealth without love is meaningless and that character is tested, not created, by money. Her education is completed not by renouncing money but by learning to value it rightly — as a means to human flourishing rather than an end in itself.

### The Boffins and the Test of Wealth

The Boffins — Noddy and his wife — are Dickens's experiment in whether money inevitably corrupts. Servants who inherit a fortune, they navigate a world they never expected to enter:

> "Live like our means, without extravagance, and be happy." (009-014)

Mrs. Boffin's modest philosophy of wealth — neither hoarding nor squandering, neither ashamed of riches nor defined by them — is the novel's moral baseline. Mr. Boffin's apparent moral decline (his tightened fist, his miser-parade before Bella) is eventually revealed as a deliberate moral test: Boffin plays the miser to teach Bella what money can do to a person. The lesson — that money amplifies character rather than creating it — is Dickens's final word on the subject.

### The Veneering World

At the opposite social extreme, the [[the-veneering-circle]] represents money without substance — new wealth applied as surface veneer over emptiness. Their dinner parties, their "bran-new" possessions, their friends-of-convenience, all suggest that money in the absence of genuine human connection produces only hollow performance. The Veneerings' inevitable financial collapse is the novel's verdict on a society built on credit and appearance rather than authentic value.

### Money, Work, and Dignity

A crucial counter-current in the novel concerns earned versus unearned money. John Harmon, though the rightful heir, chooses to work as a secretary and earn his living before claiming his inheritance. Bella Wilfer must learn to value the modest domestic economy of Rokesmith's salary before she is worthy of the Harmon fortune. And [[betty-higden]]'s fierce insistence on earning her own bread — even to the point of death on the road rather than the workhouse — makes the novel's most radical claim: that independence, not wealth, is the true measure of human dignity.

## Related Characters

- **[[bella-wilfer]]** — The novel's moral centre on money. Her journey from "I love money" to valuing love over wealth is Dickens's argument that money is a test of character, not its determinant.
- **[[nicodemus-boffin]]** — The "Golden Dustman" who inherits the Harmon fortune. His apparent transformation into a miser and subsequent revelation demonstrate that wealth amplifies rather than changes character.
- **[[john-harmon]]** — The presumed-dead heir who returns disguised as John Rokesmith to observe how money shapes those around him before revealing himself.
- **[[silas-wegg]]** — The embodiment of predatory greed, who sees the Boffins' fortune as something to be extracted through scheming and blackmail.
- **[[betty-higden]]** — The counter-example: a woman whose fierce independence refuses money that would compromise her dignity.
- **[[alfred-lammle]]** — A character whose entire identity is a speculative financial instrument, floated on the expectation of wealth that does not exist.

## Victorian Context

The mid-Victorian period saw unprecedented growth in personal wealth alongside devastating poverty. The novel was published in 1864–65, when debates about inheritance law, women's property rights (the Married Women's Property Act would not pass until 1870), and the moral legitimacy of fortunes made in trade and industry were live political issues. Dickens's choice to make the Harmon fortune derive from dust — from literal waste — was a pointed commentary on the sources of Victorian prosperity, questioning whether any large fortune could be traced to clean origins.

The novel also engages with the period's anxiety about speculation and paper wealth. The 1860s saw a series of financial scandals — most notably the Overend, Gurney crash of 1866 — and Dickens's portrayal of the Veneering world, with its credit-funded sociability and its inevitable collapse, reads as both satire and prophecy.
