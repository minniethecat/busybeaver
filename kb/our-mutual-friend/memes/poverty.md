# Poverty

## Overview

Poverty in *Our Mutual Friend* is not merely a background condition but an active force that shapes character, drives plot, and provides the novel's most searing social criticism. Dickens presents poverty across a spectrum: from the desperate river-scavenging of the Hexams to the shabby gentility of the Wilfers, from Betty Higden's terror of the workhouse to the hidden poverty that lurks beneath the Veneerings' credit-fuelled surface. At every level, the novel insists that poverty is not a moral failure but a structural condition — and that the true moral failure belongs to a society that treats the poor as criminals.

The novel's treatment of poverty is notably unsentimental. Bella Wilfer is poor and hates it — her "I love money" speech is not the confession of a villain but the honest cry of someone degraded by poverty's daily humiliations. Betty Higden's death on the road, fleeing the workhouse, is the novel's most powerful indictment of institutional "charity." And the Veneerings, who are poor beneath their surface prosperity (living entirely on credit), demonstrate that poverty is not always visible — that the boundary between solvency and destitution is thinner than respectable society admits.

## Expression in the Novel

### Bella Wilfer: The Psychology of Poverty

Bella's famous outburst — "I hate to be poor, and we are degradingly poor, offensively poor, miserably poor, beastly poor" (004-050) — is the novel's most honest account of what poverty does to the soul. Dickens refuses to make her virtuous in her poverty; instead, he shows how poverty breeds resentment, envy, and the desperate calculation that makes her initially mercenary. Bella's moral education is not about learning to love poverty but about learning that love is worth more than money — a lesson she can only learn once the Boffins' wealth has removed her from poverty's immediate pressures.

### Betty Higden: The Workhouse and Death

Betty Higden, the aged poor woman who supports herself by minding children and selling small wares, is the novel's most eloquent witness against the 1834 Poor Law. Her determination to die on the road rather than enter the workhouse — "Kill me sooner than take me there" — is not paranoia but rational assessment. The workhouse, which separated husbands from wives and parents from children, was genuinely feared by the Victorian poor as a fate worse than death. Betty's death in the open air, free, is the novel's judgment on a system that made death preferable to institutional "care."

### The Veneerings: Credit and Hidden Poverty

The Veneerings appear wealthy — "bran-new people in a bran-new house" — but their prosperity is entirely borrowed. They live on credit, their possessions owned by others, their social position as hollow as their furniture. Their eventual collapse into bankruptcy reveals the hidden poverty at the heart of commercial society: the Veneerings are poor people performing wealth, and their performance is sustained only by a credit system that values appearance over substance.

## Victorian Context

The 1834 Poor Law Amendment Act, which established the workhouse system, was one of the most controversial pieces of Victorian social legislation. It was designed to deter the "undeserving" poor from seeking relief by making conditions in the workhouse deliberately harsh — separating families, imposing hard labour, and providing only minimal sustenance. Dickens had been campaigning against the workhouse since *Oliver Twist* (1837–39). In *Our Mutual Friend*, his critique is sharper and more despairing: Betty Higden's death represents his final major statement on a system that made the poor prefer death to assistance. The novel appeared during a period of intense debate about poverty, philanthropy, and the state's responsibility toward the destitute, and its argument — that poverty is structural, not moral, and that institutional "charity" can be more cruel than indifference — remains radical.
