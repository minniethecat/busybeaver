# Silas Wegg's Counter-Conspiracy

## Overview

Silas Wegg's counter-conspiracy — his scheme to blackmail Nicodemus Boffin by searching the dust-heaps for a hidden will that would overturn the Harmon inheritance — is the novel's great comic subplot. The ballad-monger with the wooden leg, convinced that the dust-mounds conceal treasure, enlists Mr. Venus as his reluctant partner and begins a campaign of nocturnal digging and daytime insinuation that is as absurd as it is doomed.

The conspiracy is Dickens's satire of the plotter's imagination. Wegg — illiterate in everything except self-interest — reads the dust-heaps as a text that confirms his greed. His conviction that treasure is hidden within them is not based on evidence but on desire: he wants the mounds to contain wealth, so he believes they do. His conspiracy is the novel's funniest demonstration that greed is not merely immoral but stupid — that the desire for unearned wealth systematically distorts perception.

## Narrative Context

The conspiracy originates in Wegg's stall near Harmony Jail. Observing the Boffins' inheritance, he becomes convinced that the dust-heaps contain hidden valuables — perhaps even a later will that would invalidate the Boffins' claim. He enlists Venus, promising a share of the treasure, and begins his midnight excavations.

The scheme unravels through Venus's conscience. The taxidermist, more decent than Wegg and more observant than either of them, warns Boffin of the blackmail plot. Wegg is exposed, humiliated, and dismissed — the dust-heaps yielding nothing except the lesson that greed reads treasure into every heap of rubbish.

## Key Participants

- **[[silas-wegg]]** — The conspirator whose greed reads treasure into dust. His wooden leg — the body part he tried to sell to Venus — is the novel's most literal statement of its money theme (007-007).
- **[[mr-venus]]** — The reluctant partner whose conscience ultimately betrays Wegg. His decency, maintained despite his grotesque profession, makes him the conspiracy's moral pivot.
- **[[nicodemus-boffin]]** — The target. His handling of Wegg's exposure — firm but not vindictive — confirms his character. The conspiracy cannot touch him because he has nothing to hide.

## Scene Description

Wegg's midnight digging — alone among the dust-heaps, searching for treasure that does not exist — is the novel's great comic image of greed. The mounds, sifted and sold long ago, contain nothing but Wegg's determination to find something. His conversations with Venus, conducted in the taxidermist's shop among preserved specimens, add grotesque comedy to the conspiracy's inevitable failure.

## Thematic Significance

The counter-conspiracy is the novel's argument that greed is self-defeating. Wegg's scheme fails not because Boffin is clever but because greed is stupid: it reads evidence backward, from desire to fact rather than from fact to judgment. The dust-heaps, which really did contain the Harmon fortune, have been emptied — but Wegg, seeing them, still sees treasure. His failure is the failure of all conspiratorial thinking: reality will not conform to greed's demands.
