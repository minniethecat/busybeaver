# John Harmon / John Rokesmith

## Overview

John Harmon is the protagonist of *Our Mutual Friend* and the figure around whom the novel's central [[money|inheritance]] plot revolves. Son of "a tremendous old rascal who made his money by [[dust|Dust]]" (002-051), Harmon returns to England from abroad to claim his [[money|inheritance]] under the terms of his father's will — only to be attacked, drugged, thrown into the [[the-river-thames|Thames]], and presumed dead. He survives by exchanging clothes with his would-be [[crime|murder]]er, assumes the [[identity-and-disguise|identity]] of John Rokesmith, and enters the Boffin household as a humble secretary to observe, unseen, how [[bella-wilfer|Bella Wilfer]] (the woman the will requires him to marry) and [[the-boffin-household|the Boffins]] behave when they believe the [[inheritance|heir]] is dead.

Harmon/Rokesmith is Dickens's most sophisticated treatment of the [[identity-and-disguise|disguise]]d-[[identity-and-disguise|identity]] plot. Unlike conventional hidden [[inheritance|heir]]s, Harmon's [[identity-and-disguise|disguise]] is voluntary and [[morality|moral]]ly purposive — he is not hiding from danger but conducting a [[morality|moral]] experiment. His return to his true [[identity-and-disguise|identity]] at the novel's end is not merely a plot resolution but the completion of a [[morality|moral]] education: he has learned, through observation and self-effacement, what kind of man he wants to be and what kind of wife he wants to have.

## Role in the Novel

Harmon functions simultaneously as protagonist, [[morality|moral]] observer, and structural device. As protagonist, his journey from passive [[inheritance|heir]] to active [[morality|moral]] agent drives the novel's central plot. As observer, his Rokesmith persona allows him — and the reader — to see characters as they are, not as they present themselves to a [[money|wealth]]y man. As structural device, his presumed [[death-and-resurrection|death]] generates the [[money|inheritance]] crisis that sets every other plot in motion: [[the-boffin-household|the Boffins]]' elevation, [[bella-wilfer|Bella]]'s [[morality|moral]] education, Wegg's [[greed|scheming]], the Lammles' predation.

His relationship with [[bella-wilfer|Bella]] is the novel's most sustained treatment of love, education, and the question of whether character can be tested by money. His [[identity-and-disguise|disguise]] allows him to win her love as a [[poverty|poor]] secretary rather than claim her as a rich [[inheritance|heir]] — transforming the will's coercive marriage condition into a freely chosen union.

## Key Relationships

- **[[bella-wilfer]]** — His eventual wife. Harmon observes her in [[identity-and-disguise|disguise]] as Rokesmith, watching her grow from a mercenary girl ("I love money, and want money — want it dreadfully," 004-050) into a woman who values love over [[money|wealth]]. His proposal as Rokesmith tests whether she can love a [[poverty|poor]] man; his revelation as Harmon rewards her for passing the test.
- **[[nicodemus-boffin]]** — The Golden [[dust|Dust]]man who inherits the Harmon [[money|fortune]] when John is presumed dead, then employs "Rokesmith" as secretary. Boffin's apparent [[morality|moral]] decline into miserliness is later revealed as a performance designed to teach [[bella-wilfer|Bella]] — a performance Harmon/Rokesmith must endure without revealing his [[identity-and-disguise|identity]].
- **[[old-harmon]]** — His father, "a tremendous old rascal who made his money by [[dust|Dust]]" (002-051), whose will with its marriage condition is the novel's originating legal document. T[[inheritance|heir]] relationship, defined entirely by the will's coercive logic, represents the dead hand of [[money|inheritance]] that Harmon must escape.

## Thematic Significance

Harmon embodies the novel's central argument about [[identity-and-disguise|identity]] and [[morality|moral]] worth. By voluntarily surrendering his [[identity-and-disguise|identity]], [[money|wealth]], and social position to become Rokesmith, he enacts the novel's thesis that true worth is revealed, not created, by money. His [[identity-and-disguise|disguise]] is a [[morality|moral]] experiment that tests whether love can exist independent of [[money|wealth]] and whether character can be discerned beneath social performance.

His triple [[identity-and-disguise|identity]] (Harmon, Handford, Rokesmith) also represents the novel's broader concern with the instability of social [[identity-and-disguise|identity]]. In a world where everyone performs — [[the-veneering-circle|the Veneerings]] perform [[money|wealth]], Headstone performs [[social-class|respectability]], Boffin performs miserliness — Harmon's deliberate performance of humility becomes the vehicle for discovering genuine human connection.

## Critical Reception

Critics have long noted that John Harmon is among Dickens's least vivid protagonists — deliberately so. Unlike David Copperfield or Pip, Harmon's personality is submerged in his [[identity-and-disguise|disguise]], his inner life largely inaccessible to the reader. This reticence has been read as a structural necessity (the plot requires him to observe rather than act) and as a thematic statement ([[identity-and-disguise|identity]] in the modern world is performance, and the self beneath the performance may be unknowable even to itself). Recent criticism has focused on Harmon/Rokesmith as a figure for the novelist himself — the hidden observer who arranges the [[morality|moral]] education of others before revealing his hand.
