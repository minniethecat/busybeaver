# Appearances and Reality

## Overview

The tension between surface and substance structures every dimension of *Our Mutual Friend*. The novel's plot is built on hidden identities (John Harmon disguised as John Rokesmith), its social world on performed respectability (the Veneerings, the Podsnaps), its physical landscape on concealed value (the dust-mounds, the river-bottom), and its moral universe on the gap between what people appear to be and what they are.

Dickens signals this theme from the novel's first page, where we encounter Gaffer Hexam and his daughter Lizzie in "a boat of dirty and disreputable appearance" (001-001). The boat is a surface that tells nothing of what lies beneath — not the dead body being towed, not the money in the corpse's pockets, not the fierce paternal loyalty and moral complexity of the people in it. Every subsequent chapter extends the pattern: things are never what they seem, and the act of seeing below the surface is the novel's central moral imperative.

The Veneerings are Dickens's most explicit embodiment of the theme — their very name announces that they are surface without substance. "Everything about the Veneerings was spick and span new. All their furniture was new, all their friends were new, all their servants were new, their plate was new, their carriage was new, their harness was new" (002-002). The repetition of "new" becomes a kind of indictment: what is only surface has no history, no depth, no reality. The "veneer" that gives them their name also predicts their fate — surfaces crack, and what lies beneath is nothing.

## Expression in the Novel

### Disguised Identity

The novel's central disguise — John Harmon's transformation into John Rokesmith — is not a Gothic contrivance but a moral experiment. Harmon/Rokesmith enters the Boffin household to observe, unseen, how Bella Wilfer and the Boffins behave when they believe the heir is dead. His disguise is the novel's most literal enactment of the surface/depth theme: he is one person on the surface (the humble secretary), another in reality (the rightful heir), and the gap between them is the space in which the novel's moral drama unfolds.

Other disguises proliferate. Bradley Headstone appears the respectable schoolmaster but conceals murderous rage. Eugene Wrayburn appears the idle gentleman but reveals surprising moral seriousness. The Lammles appear the happily married couple but are joined only in mutual predation. Even the Boffins' parlour with its floral carpet giving way to "sand and sawdust" (005-135) maps the tension between ornamental surface and dirty reality.

### Social Performance

Dickens presents Victorian society as a theatre in which everyone performs a role. Mr. Podsnap performs respectability so completely that he has become his performance — "as a so eminently respectable man, Mr. Podsnap was sensible of its being required of him to take Providence under his protection" (011-004). The Veneerings' dinner parties are staged events with guests as props, conversation as script, and the entire production designed to create an impression of substance that does not exist.

The novel's radical implication is that all class status is performance — that the difference between a Boffin (genuine but unpolished) and a Veneering (polished but hollow) is not a matter of degree but of kind. The Boffins may lack polish but they possess reality; the Veneerings have polish and nothing else.

### The Dust-Mounds

The dust-mounds themselves are the novel's great physical metaphor for the appearance/reality tension. To the casual observer they are "tall dark mounds" (005-132), shapeless refuse heaps. But to those who understand dust — to Old Harmon, to Boffin ("I'm a pretty fair scholar in dust," 015-087) — they are repositories of hidden value: bones for fertiliser, metal for resale, cinders for brick-making. The mounds literalise the novel's central claim: value is always hidden beneath an unprepossessing surface, and discernment is a moral skill.

### The River

The Thames functions as the dust-mounds' liquid counterpart. Its murky surface conceals everything that London has discarded — corpses, coins, secrets. Gaffer Hexam reads the river's surface as Boffin reads the dust-mounds, discerning value in what appears valueless. The river's opacity is the novel's visual metaphor for the unknowability of truth: you can see the surface, but the depths remain hidden until you dredge.

## Victorian Context

Victorian culture was intensely preoccupied with the relationship between appearance and reality — a concern that manifested in everything from the vogue for physiognomy (reading character from facial features) to debates about religious doubt (how can one know the invisible divine?), to the rise of detective fiction. *Our Mutual Friend*, published in 1864–65, appeared alongside the sensation novels of Wilkie Collins and Mary Elizabeth Braddon, which similarly exploited the gap between respectable surfaces and criminal realities.

Dickens's contribution to this cultural conversation is distinctively moral: the ability to see beneath surfaces is not merely a practical skill but a moral one. Characters who cannot read surfaces — Podsnap, the Veneerings, the Lammles — are not merely deceived but morally deficient. The novel proposes that discernment is the cardinal virtue of modern life, and that a society that values polish over substance is not merely shallow but corrupt.
