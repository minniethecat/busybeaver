# Mr. Venus's Shop

## Overview

Mr. Venus's shop — the taxidermist's establishment in Clerkenwell — is the novel's strangest and most philosophically concentrated interior space. Filled with "human warious" — preserved specimens, articulated skeletons, stuffed birds in glass cases, pickled preparations, and the famous "French gentleman" skeleton that lacks only a pair of legs to be complete — the shop is a museum of the boundary between life and death, nature and artifice, value and waste.

The shop's presiding genius is Mr. Venus himself, the "articulator of human bones," whose professional specialty — assembling complete skeletons from disparate bones — is the novel's most direct metaphor for its own narrative method. The Harmon fortune must be assembled from scattered parts (dust-heaps, wills, presumed-dead heirs); the novel's multiple plots must be articulated into a single skeleton; the city's social fragments must be assembled into a recognizable body. Venus's shop is the novel's workshop — the place where parts become wholes.

The shop also functions as a grotesque mirror of the dust-heaps. Both are collections of discarded matter that may contain hidden value: the dust-heaps conceal fortunes in cinders and rags; Venus's shop conceals beauty in preserved specimens. The difference is Venus's craft: he knows how to assemble what the dust-heaps merely accumulate. His art is the novel's art — taking the fragments of a broken world and making them into something that looks, if not alive, then at least complete.

## In the Novel

Venus's shop enters the novel through Silas Wegg, who visits to negotiate the sale of his amputated leg. Wegg's wooden leg is precisely what Venus needs to complete the "French gentleman" skeleton, and the bargaining between the ballad-monger and the bone-grinder — conducted among glass cases of preserved specimens — is Dickens at his most macabre and comic. The shop makes visible what the dust-heaps keep hidden: the human body as commodity, the relationship between parts and value, the unsettling proximity of the preserved and the dead (007-007).

The shop also serves as Wegg's confidant-space. His schemes — to discover hidden treasure in the dust-heaps, to blackmail Boffin — are hatched and discussed in Venus's shop, amid the mute audience of stuffed animals and articulated bones. The shop's collection of dead matter that mimics life is the perfect setting for Wegg's dead-end plots (Book II).

Venus's proposal to Pleasant Riderhood — delivered in the shop, amid the specimens — is one of the novel's oddest romantic scenes. His declaration of love, offered to a woman who deals in unredeemed pledges, in a room filled with unredeemed bodies, is Dickens's recognition that romance, like everything else in *Our Mutual Friend*, is a form of assembly: bringing together fragments (two lonely people, each marked by circumstance) to make something whole.

## Associated Characters

- **[[mr-venus]]** — The shop is his world. He moves among his specimens with the authority of a curator and the anxiety of a man who knows he is one failed transaction away from ruin.
- **[[silas-wegg]]** — The shop is where Wegg's venality is most visible. His willingness to sell his own leg — to treat his body as a commodity — is the novel's most literal statement of its money theme.
- **[[pleasant-riderhood]]** — The object of Venus's improbable courtship. Her appearance in the shop, receiving his proposal among the specimens, creates the novel's most memorably odd romantic tableau.
- **[[nicodemus-boffin]]** — Though he never enters the shop, the Boffin presence haunts it: every scheme hatched here is directed against him, and Venus's ultimate choice to warn Boffin rather than join Wegg is the shop's moral climax.
