# Hexam Riverside Dwelling

## Overview

The Hexam riverside dwelling is the novel's poorest domestic interior — a space where the boundary between home and workplace, family and trade, living and dead is continuously blurred. Gaffer Hexam's trade — fishing corpses from the Thames — is conducted from this dwelling; the river's dead are brought here before being handed over to the authorities. The house is thus neither purely domestic (it is a workplace) nor purely professional (it is where Lizzie keeps the fire going and Charley learns to read). It is, like the river that defines it, a space of dual nature.

The dwelling's poverty is not merely economic but moral. Lizzie and Charley are raised in a house whose primary economic activity is corpse retrieval. The dead are as present in the Hexam household as the living — more present, perhaps, since Gaffer's relationship with his children is largely instrumental, while his relationship with the river's dead is professional and attentive. Lizzie's moral clarity and Charley's later rejection of his origins are both rooted in this house: one child absorbed its lessons about human dignity, the other fled from them.

The house's riverside location — "a hole" near Limehouse, overlooking the water — makes it a space of constant exposure. The Thames is always present: its sounds, its smells, its tides. There is no separation between the domestic and the elemental. This exposure is what the novel values about the Hexam household: unlike the sealed interiors of Podsnap's house or the Veneering drawing room, the Hexam dwelling cannot pretend the river doesn't exist. Its poverty is its honesty.

## In the Novel

The Hexam dwelling appears primarily in Book I, establishing the conditions from which Lizzie and Charley emerge. The scenes set here — Gaffer returning with his night's catch, Lizzie keeping the fire burning, the boy (later identified as Charley) reading by the poor light — are some of the novel's most powerful domestic tableaux. They establish the novel's moral baseline: here, in the poorest house on the river, character is formed, tested, and revealed.

The dwelling's most significant narrative function is as the origin from which both Lizzie and Charley depart — in opposite moral directions. Lizzie carries the house's lessons about human dignity into her subsequent life; Charley rejects everything the house represents. The dwelling is the novel's argument that origin does not determine character — the same house produces a saintly sister and a morally bankrupt brother — but that character is revealed by how one relates to one's origin.
