# Social Class

## Overview

*Our Mutual Friend* presents one of Dickens's most systematic and pessimistic analyses of social class. Unlike earlier novels where honest poverty is eventually rewarded and benevolent wealth redeems, the class structure here appears almost immutable — a vast mechanism for sorting human beings by money, appearance, and connection, in which moral worth and social position bear no necessary relation.

The novel maps Victorian society from the river-bottom to the dinner table: from Gaffer Hexam, who scavenges corpses for coins, through the shabby-genteel Wilfers, the newly wealthy Boffins, the established mercantile Podsnaps, to the hollow aristocracy represented by the Veneerings and Lady Tippins. At every level, Dickens shows class not as a ladder to be climbed but as a performance to be maintained — and the cost of that performance is always moral.

The novel's radical democratic impulse emerges most clearly in its treatment of the "low" characters: Betty Higden, who dies rather than enter the workhouse; Jenny Wren, the dolls' dressmaker whose crippled body houses the novel's sharpest intelligence; Sloppy, the workhouse orphan whose innate decency surpasses any gentleman's. Against them, the Veneerings, Podsnaps, and Lammles are visibly hollow — their class status is a veneer over moral vacancy.

## Expression in the Novel

### The Veneerings: Class as Surface

Mr. and Mrs. Veneering are "bran-new people in a bran-new house in a bran-new quarter of London" (002-001). Their name is Dickens's explicit thesis: their class position is a veneer, a thin surface polished to a high shine but with nothing beneath. Their dinner parties bring together a cross-section of society — the aristocrat (Lady Tippins), the professional class (Mortimer Lightwood, Eugene Wrayburn), the established bourgeoisie (the Podsnaps) — but only to demonstrate that all are performing, none are genuine. "Everything about the Veneerings was spick and span new" (002-002), and what is new can be repossessed.

### The Podsnaps: Respectability as Religion

Mr. Podsnap represents the novel's sharpest satire of middle-class complacency. "As a so eminently respectable man, Mr. Podsnap was sensible of its being required of him to take Providence under his protection" (011-004). Podsnap's respectability is a fortress: he has one fixed idea — "the question about everything was, would it bring a blush into the cheek of the young person?" — and he deploys it to exclude everything uncomfortable, foreign, or morally demanding from his consciousness. His famous dismissal of the starving poor — that they are simply not English, because "Providence" would not allow English people to starve — is the novel's most devastating critique of class-based moral blindness.

### The Boffins: New Money and Old Prejudice

The Boffins' elevation from servants to wealthy property-owners exposes the arbitrariness of class. Mr. Boffin, the "Golden Dustman," is simultaneously a figure of fun (the street children chant "Down with the dust, Boffin!" (009-034)) and the novel's moral compass. His wealth never changes his essential character — or rather, it reveals it. The novel asks whether class mobility is possible without moral corruption, and the Boffins' answer is a qualified yes: possible, but only through deliberate moral effort and genuine love.

### The Poor: The Workhouse and the River

Betty Higden's terror of the workhouse — "Kill me sooner than take me there" — is one of Dickens's most powerful indictments of the 1834 Poor Law. Her death on the road, fleeing institutional "charity," makes visible what the Podsnaps refuse to see: that Victorian class structure was maintained by literal violence against the poor. Gaffer Hexam and his daughter Lizzie, living by the river's edge and scavenging from the dead, represent a class so marginal it barely registers on the social map — yet their moral seriousness far exceeds that of their social betters.

## Victorian Context

The 1860s saw Victorian class structures under pressure from multiple directions: the expansion of the franchise (Reform Act 1867 was imminent), the growth of a wealthy industrial and commercial middle class, the emergence of organised labour, and ongoing debate over the 1834 Poor Law and the workhouse system. Dickens had been campaigning against the workhouse since *Oliver Twist* (1837–39); in *Our Mutual Friend*, the critique is sharper and more despairing. Betty Higden's death — and the novel's explicit argument that the poor fear the workhouse more than death itself — represents Dickens's final major statement on institutional "charity."
