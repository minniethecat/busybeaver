# The River Thames

## Overview

The Thames in *Our Mutual Friend* is not a picturesque backdrop but an active, symbolically charged presence — the novel's liquid unconscious, where everything society discards (corpses, coins, secrets, identities) sinks below the surface to be dredged up by those who know how to read the currents. The river opens the novel ("a boat of dirty and disreputable appearance" floating between Southwark Bridge and London Bridge, 001-001) and provides its final image of violent resolution (Headstone and Riderhood drowning locked together). Between these poles, it functions as the dust-mounds' mirror and opposite: wet where dust is dry, mobile where dust is static, concealment by depth where dust conceals by volume.

Dickens knew the Thames intimately — its tides, its traffic, its capacity to receive everything London produced and hide it beneath an opaque surface. The river in this novel is not nature but infrastructure: a working waterway plied by scavengers, lightermen, and steamer pilots, its beauty ("the kindling lights of London Bridge," 001-009) inseparable from its function as the city's drainage system and unofficial graveyard.

## Expression in the Novel

### The River as Livelihood

For Gaffer Hexam and his daughter Lizzie, the river is both home and workplace — the source of everything they have and the medium of everything they fear. Gaffer's boat is his office, his dredging equipment his tools of trade. The river provides corpses, and corpses provide coins. This is the novel's most radical destabilisation of economic categories: the river transforms death into income, blurring the line between salvage and theft, profession and predation. Lizzie's relationship to the river is more complex — she fears it (it killed her mother, it threatens to claim her father) but cannot escape it. Her eventual departure from the riverside marks her entry into a different social world.

### The River as Concealment

The river's opacity is its defining characteristic. "Allied to the bottom of the river rather than the surface, by reason of the slime and ooze" (001-003) — this early description establishes the Thames as a medium of hiddenness. What goes into the river disappears from view: John Harmon's supposed body, the evidence of Headstone's attack, the secrets that Riderhood attempts to monetise. The river is the novel's metaphor for the unknowability of truth — you can see the surface, but the depths are always opaque.

### The River and Death

Every major death in the novel is associated with water. The opening corpse, dredged by Gaffer; the false identification of John Harmon's body; the climactic double drowning of Headstone and Riderhood. The river receives the dead and occasionally returns them — not resurrected but revealed, their secrets extracted along with the coins from their pockets. This association of water with mortality gives the novel's Thames a mythic dimension: it is the Styx as much as the Thames, a boundary between the living and the dead that certain characters (Gaffer, Riderhood) learn to navigate.

### The River vs. the Dust-Mounds

The novel's two great symbolic landscapes — the Thames and the dust-mounds — are structurally parallel but morally distinct. Both conceal value in waste (coins in corpses, treasure in refuse). Both reward those who know how to read them (Gaffer reading currents, Boffin pricing mounds). But the river is associated with active violence (drowning, murder), while the dust-mounds are associated with passive accumulation (the slow piling-up of urban waste). The river kills; the mounds simply are. Between them, they map the novel's moral geography.

## Victorian Context

The Thames in the 1860s was simultaneously London's greatest economic asset and its most notorious public health crisis. The "Great Stink" of 1858 — when the river's pollution became so severe that Parliament considered relocating — had prompted the construction of Bazalgette's embankment and sewer system, still underway when Dickens wrote *Our Mutual Friend*. The river was a working waterway, a sewer, a transportation artery, and a site of frequent drowning deaths. Dickens, who had walked the riverside at night observing the scavengers and watermen, knew its dangers and its romance. The novel's Thames is both document and symbol — a realistic depiction of a Victorian working river and a mythic landscape of death, concealment, and ambiguous value.
