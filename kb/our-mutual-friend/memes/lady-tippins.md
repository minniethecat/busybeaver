Lady Tippins is one of Dickens's most macabre comic creations. "Reflects charming old Lady Tippins on Veneering's right; with an immense obtuse drab oblong face, like a face in a tablespoon" (002-025) — the image is both precise and merciless. She is ancient, artificial, and voraciously flirtatious, a figure whose grotesque vitality serves as a satirical indictment of the [[social-class|society]] that continues to indulge her.

At [[the-veneering-circle|the Veneering dinner table]], Lady Tippins functions as a kind of predatory social force. Her attentions to the younger men — particularly [[mortimer-lightwood|Mortimer Lightwood]] — are simultaneously ridiculous and menacing, a parody of romantic interest that has outlived any biological basis. "Lady Tippins has made a series of experiments" (002-026) on the assembled company, testing for romantic intrigue like a scientist probing specimens.

## Role in the Novel

Lady Tippins is a purely satirical character: she has no plot function beyond her presence at [[the-veneering-household|the Veneering table]] and her commentary on the romantic lives of the younger characters. Her role is to embody the grotesquerie of a social world that sustains figures of no genuine value. She is, in effect, [[the-veneering-household|the Veneering circle]]'s id — all appetite, no substance.

## Thematic Significance

If [[mr-podsnap|Mr. Podsnap]] represents the intellectual bankruptcy of the English bourgeoisie, Lady Tippins represents its social and erotic exhaustion. She is what remains when a [[social-class|society]] has consumed everything of value and is left with nothing but the performance of consumption. Her flirtatiousness — grotesque given her age — mirrors [[the-veneering-circle|the Veneerings]]' own social performance: a pantomime of vitality that masks complete spiritual [[death-and-resurrection|death]].

Dickens's treatment of Lady Tippins walks a fine line between comedy and cruelty. Her age, her ugliness, and her delusions are all subjects of relentless mockery. Yet the mockery is directed less at Lady Tippins herself than at the [[social-class|society]] that has made her possible — a world in which a woman of no evident merit can sustain herself on nothing but title and tenacity.
