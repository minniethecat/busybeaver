# Death and Resurrection

## Overview

Death saturates *Our Mutual Friend* from its opening scene, in which Gaffer Hexam dredges a corpse from the Thames, to its climax, in which the villains Riderhood and Headstone drown locked in each other's arms. But the novel is equally concerned with resurrection — not in any religious sense, but as a secular pattern of symbolic death and moral rebirth that structures the fates of its major characters.

John Harmon's "death" is the central instance: presumed drowned after his return to England, he resurfaces as John Rokesmith to observe, unseen, how those who would inherit his fortune behave in his absence. His resurrection at the novel's end is not merely a plot twist but the resolution of a moral experiment. Eugene Wrayburn nearly dies at Bradley Headstone's hands and is "resurrected" through Lizzie Hexam's devotion into a man capable of genuine love and moral seriousness. Bella Wilfer undergoes a moral death (her mercenary self) and resurrection (her loving, principled self). Even the Boffins' apparent moral decline is a kind of staged death, with their true characters resurrected in the novel's final revelations.

Dickens's use of the death-and-resurrection pattern owes something to the Victorian novel's broader fascination with inheritance plots and lost heirs, but *Our Mutual Friend* pushes the pattern beyond convention into a sustained meditation on what it means to die to one's former self and be reborn.

## Expression in the Novel

### John Harmon/Rokesmith: The Central Resurrection

Harmon's return from presumed death is the novel's master plot. The man who steps off the ship in London, is attacked, drugged, and thrown into the Thames — only to be rescued, exchange clothes with his would-be murderer (who then drowns in his place), and re-enter his own story as a stranger — is both a literal survivor and a symbolic figure. His "death" frees him from the constraints of his identity as heir and allows him to become John Rokesmith, the observing secretary. His eventual self-revelation is a resurrection in the fullest sense: not merely a return to life but a return to a life transformed by what he has witnessed in the interval.

### Eugene Wrayburn: Death as Moral Catalyst

Eugene's near-fatal beating by Bradley Headstone and his slow recovery under Lizzie's care constitute the novel's most explicit death-and-resurrection arc. Eugene begins as an amoral idler, a gentleman who drifts through life without commitment or purpose. His brush with death strips away his irony and reveals a capacity for love and moral seriousness that his surface persona concealed. When he marries Lizzie from what may be his deathbed, and subsequently recovers, the marriage represents his resurrection into authentic life — a life he could not have accessed without first being brought to the brink of death.

### Moral Resurrection

The pattern extends beyond physical death. Bella Wilfer "dies" to her mercenary self and is "resurrected" through the Boffins' moral tutelage into someone who values love over money. The Boffins themselves, having seemingly succumbed to the corrupting influence of wealth, are "resurrected" when their apparent moral decline is revealed as performance. Even Rogue Riderhood, the most irredeemable character, experiences a kind of false resurrection — dragged from the river only to return to his predatory ways, proving that physical survival without moral transformation is mere continuation, not rebirth.

## Victorian Context

The Victorian period saw a dramatic shift in attitudes toward death, driven by urbanisation, the rise of the cemetery movement, changes in mourning ritual, and — most significantly for Dickens — the drowning deaths that were a constant feature of life along the Thames. *Our Mutual Friend* registers this cultural preoccupation while transforming it into a secular resurrection narrative. The novel's treatment of death is notably irreligious: there are no deathbed conversions, no consolatory visions of heaven. Resurrection is achieved through human love, not divine intervention. This places the novel within the broader Victorian movement toward secular frameworks for understanding mortality, even as it exploits the narrative power of death-and-return that Christianity had bequeathed to Western literature.
