# Nicodemus Boffin

## Overview

Nicodemus "Noddy" Boffin is the [[morality|moral]] centre of *Our Mutual Friend* — the former servant who inherits the Harmon dust [[money|fortune]] and becomes the "Golden [[dust|Dust]]man," a figure of both comedy and profound [[morality|moral]] seriousness. Introduced as a simple, good-natured man "with a face as broad and red as a harvest moon," Boffin's journey through [[money|wealth]] tests whether character can survive prosperity. His apparent decline into miserliness — his "tightening fist," his cruelty toward Rokesmith, his parade of [[greed|avarice]] — is the novel's most complex [[morality|moral]] performance: a deliberate act designed to teach [[bella-wilfer|Bella Wilfer]] what money can do to a person.

When the performance is revealed, Boffin emerges as the novel's true [[morality|moral]] hero — a man who used his own reputation and [[bella-wilfer|Bella]]'s affection to teach a lesson no sermon could convey. His marriage to [[mrs-boffin|Mrs. Boffin]], weathered and genuine, is the novel's model of love: steady, mutual, and immune to the corruptions of [[money|wealth]]. In a novel populated by performers, Boffin is the one character whose performance serves a [[morality|moral]] purpose.

## Role in the Novel

Boffin inherits the Harmon dust [[money|fortune]] when John Harmon is presumed dead, transforming overnight from servant to [[money|wealth]]y man. His elevation exposes him to the novel's full range of predators — [[silas-wegg|Silas Wegg]], the Lammles, the social pressure to "live like our means" — and his navigation of these threats constitutes the novel's extended meditation on whether [[money|wealth]] necessarily corrupts.

His employment of [[john-harmon|John Rokesmith]] as secretary and his relationship with [[bella-wilfer|Bella Wilfer]] form the novel's [[morality|moral]] pedagogy. Boffin teaches [[bella-wilfer|Bella]] not through precept but through experience, staging his own [[morality|moral]] decline so that she can recognise and reject the miser's logic. His declaration — "I'm a pretty fair scholar in dust. I can price the mounds to a fraction" (015-087) — establishes the novel's central irony: the uneducated former servant understands the true value of things better than [[the-veneering-circle|the Veneerings]] and [[mr-podsnap|Podsnap]]s of "Society."

## Key Relationships

- **[[bella-wilfer]]** — The young woman he and [[mrs-boffin|Mrs. Boffin]] take into t[[inheritance|heir]] home, whose [[morality|moral]] education he orchestrates through his feigned miserliness. T[[inheritance|heir]] relationship is the novel's warmest portrait of non-biological parenthood.
- **[[mrs-boffin]]** — His wife, partner in both poverty and [[money|wealth]]. T[[inheritance|heir]] marriage — childless, weathered, affectionate — is the novel's model of love uncorrupted by money.
- **[[john-harmon]]** — The [[inheritance|heir]] whose [[identity-and-disguise|identity]] Boffin knows (or suspects) but whose secret he protects. T[[inheritance|heir]] relationship as employer and "secretary" is the novel's sustained treatment of mutual [[morality|moral]] testing.

## Thematic Significance

Boffin embodies the novel's argument that [[money|wealth]] reveals rather than creates character. His performance as miser demonstrates that greed is a role anyone can play — and that the refusal to play it genuinely requires [[morality|moral]] effort. His famous line — "There's some things that I never found among the dust" (008-046) — ac[[education|knowledge]]s that dust-[[money|wealth]] has [[morality|moral]] limits, and that some things (love, integrity, [[the-boffin-household|the Boffins]]' marriage) cannot be extracted from refuse.
