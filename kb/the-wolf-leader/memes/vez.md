## Overview

Vez is the feudal lordship and castle that serves as the seat of power for the [[baron-of-vez|Seigneur Jean]], [[baron-of-vez|Baron of Vez]], the nobleman whose harsh punishment of [[thibault|Thibault]] initiates the novel's central revenge plot [P02-013, 001-001]. The castle and its surrounding [[class|estate]] dominate the landscape of the opening chapters, establishing the rural hierarchy of 18th-century France against which the supernatural events unfold.

## The Castle and Estate

The Seigneur's dwelling is described as a "lordly dwelling" [001-004], reflective of the power and wealth concentrated in the hands of the pro[[wine|vin]]cial nobility. In 1780—just years before the French Revolution would sweep away this feudal order—the castle of Vez "was looked upon with different" eyes [001-004], a suggestion from Dumas that the social world he depicts was already on the brink of transformation.

The castle serves as the administrative and military center of the Baron's domain. His [[hunt|wolf-hunt]]ing expeditions originate from Vez, and the huntsmen who populate the novel's early chapters—[[engoulevent|Engoulevent]], [[marcotte|Marcotte]], [[mocquet|Mocquet]]—are all in the Baron's employ at Vez. The castle's hounds, horses, and forest rights make it the organizing principle of the rural economy and social order.

## The Lordship

The Lordship of Vez extends across multiple villages, including [[oigny|Oigny]] [001-048] and the surrounding [[forest|woods]] and hamlets. The Baron's jurisdictional [[power|authority]] over [[thibault|Thibault]]'s poaching case [[devil|demon]]strates the reach of seigneurial [[law|justice]] in the *ancien régime*: a nobleman can have a commoner publicly flogged for killing game on what he considers his land.

The phrase "the [[baron-of-vez|Lord of Vez]]" recurs throughout the novel [P02-013, 001-022, 002-028, 002-060], identifying the Baron not by his personal name ([[baron-of-vez|Seigneur Jean]]) but by his territorial title. This metonymic identification of person with place underscores the feudal logic that the novel both depicts and critiques.

## Historical Context

Vez is a real location in the Aisne department of northern France. Dumas situates his fictional narrative in this actual geography, lending verisimilitude to the supernatural tale. The castle's historical existence and the real power relations it encodes provide the material grounding for the novel's fantastic elements.
