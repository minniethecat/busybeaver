## Overview

Haramont is a real village in the Aisne department of northern France, situated "between Haramont and [[longpre|Longpré]]" [P02-017] near the forests of [[villers-cotterets|Villers-Cotterêts]]. It occupies a distinctive position in the novel's architecture: it belongs simultaneously to the autobiographical framing narrative (the preface, where the narrator describes his mother's world) and to the fictional geography of "The Wolf-Leader" proper.

## Autobiographical Context

In the preface (P02), Haramont is intimately connected with the narrator's mother, Marie-Louise-Élisabeth Labouret Dumas. "Mother Durand, of Haramont" [P02-039] is the woman whose tales of witchcraft and wolf-[[soul|spirit]]s inspire the young Alexandre Dumas. The mother's journeys "to Haramont" [P02-155] for medicinal remedies frame the village as a place of folk knowledge and oral [[custom|tradition]]—the wellspring from which the novel's supernatural elements emerge.

[[mocquet|Mocquet]], the narrator's gamekeeper, fears the witch Mother Durand's power: he believes she has "nightmared" him, and his fear persists despite the narrator's rational skepticism [P02-051, P02-083–85]. This tension between Enlightenment rationality and folk [[fear|superstition]], centered on Haramont, prefigures the novel's core dynamic.

## As Fictional Landmark

Within the novel's chapters, Haramont serves as a geographic reference point. The "little wood of Haramont" [001-029] is where the [[hunt|hunting]] party pursues its quarry. The Magloire household is located "halfway between Haramont and Bonneuil" [010-024], and the road "leading to [[longpre|Longpré]] and Haramont" [011-001] marks the direction of village traffic. When the wolf-pack [[fear|terror]]izes the countryside, "the people of Corcy and Vertfeuille" are safe only while [[thibault|Thibault]]'s attention is "busy near Haramont or [[longpre|Longpré]]" [021-016].

## Significance

Haramont bridges Dumas's personal history and his fictional creation. It is the real place where the author heard the stories that became his novel, and it is also the fictionalized place where those stories' logic operates. This dual identity makes Haramont a key to understanding the novel's relationship to its source material.
