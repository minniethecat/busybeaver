## Overview

Vauparfond is the ancestral [[class|estate]] of [[raoul-de-vauparfond|Raoul de Vauparfond]], a young baron whose romantic involvement with [[countess|the Countess]] of [[mont-gobert|Mont-Gobert]] leads to his violent death and the supernatural inhabitation of his body by [[thibault|Thibault]]. The [[class|estate]] serves as Raoul's home base, the presumed destination after his [[escape|flight]], and ultimately the site of his burial in the family vault [020-046].

## Social Position

Vauparfond occupies a position in the local hierarchy parallel to but distinct from [[mont-gobert|Mont-Gobert]] and [[vez|Vez]]. References to the [[class|estate]] cluster around Raoul's identity: he is "the [[raoul-de-vauparfond|Lord of Vauparfond]]" [015-003, 015-020], and his valet François Levasseur introduces him by this title. The [[class|estate]]'s name is invoked in competitive comparisons—"They say the same thing about me at [[mont-gobert|Mont-Gobert]] as they do at Vauparfond" [015-049]—reflecting the rivalry and social jockeying among the local nobility.

## As Destination and Tomb

After the [[comte-de-mont-gobert|Count of Mont-Gobert]] discovers the affair and attacks Raoul, [[countess|the Countess]] urges him to flee: "Do not stop till you get to Vauparfond" [017-063]. The [[class|estate]] is conceptualized as a place of safety, beyond the reach of the enraged Count. But this safety proves illusory: [[thibault|Thibault]]'s inhabitation of Raoul's body means that the man who arrives at Vauparfond is not the Baron who left.

The narrative's closing image of Vauparfond is funereal: Raoul's body "was being borne from thence to Vauparfond, where he was to be laid in the family vault" [020-046]. The [[class|estate]], which represents aristocratic lineage and inheritance, becomes a site of death rather than continuity.

## The Curé of Vauparfond

An additional detail links the [[class|estate]] to the novel's broader [[soul|spirit]]ual themes: the priest "had at one time been Curé of Vauparfond" [018-005], and during his tenure had been "engaged in theological discussions" with the locals. This brief mention roots Vauparfond in a community with religious and intellectual life beyond the love-triangle plot.
