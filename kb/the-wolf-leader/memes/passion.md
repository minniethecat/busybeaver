# Passion

## Definition

In *The Wolf-Leader*, passion is not one emotion among others but the intensifying force that transforms ordinary emotions into consuming drives. It is what turns a taste for hunting into an "inordinate passion," what makes anger into "towering passion," what gives energy a "passionate" charge. Passion is the qualitative difference between merely feeling something and being possessed by it.

The novel uses passion to characterize both Thibault and those around him. Thibault's passions — for the chase, for revenge, for social advancement — are what the wolf exploits. But passion is not inherently evil: it is the raw material of character, capable of producing both heroic energy and destructive obsession.

## Role in the Novel

### The Passion for the Chase

The Baron's and Thibault's shared "passion for the chase" (PN=001-006, PN=021-011) is the novel's primary example of passion as a double-edged force. The Baron's passion for hunting is the privilege of nobility — "he had the right to gratify the inordinate passion he had for the chase" (PN=001-006). Thibault's poaching is the same passion, criminalized by class. The passion for hunting unites noble and peasant in a shared drive, even as the law divides them.

The passion for the chase is also what brings Thibault into the forest at the fateful moment — the passion is the proximate cause of his encounter with the black wolf. Passion leads to the devil-pact not through moral weakness but through the sheer intensity of wanting.

### Passion as Performance

"Worthy of Polichinello tearing a passion to tatters in a puppet-show" (PN=005-040) — the narrator compares the Baron's rage to a theatrical performance. The simile suggests that passion, when displayed, is always partly artificial: it is something one performs as much as feels. The Baron's "towering passion" (PN=005-041) makes others "mighty uneasy" — passion in the powerful is a threat to those beneath them.

### Passionate Energy

Thibault and his antagonist confront each other "with equally passionate energy" (PN=021-017). In the novel's climactic confrontations, passion equalizes — the werewolf and the huntsman share the same quality of intensity. Passionate energy is what remains when all social distinctions are stripped away.

### Passion and Compassion

The novel plays on the etymological connection between "passion" and "compassion." The father listens to Mocquet "compassionately" (P02-034); the stranger "had had compassion on this handsome young man" (PN=018-004). Compassion — literally "suffering with" — is passion tempered by empathy. Thibault's tragedy is that his passions never mature into compassion.

### Thibault's Passionate Speech

"And so," cried Thibault "passionately, rising and stamping his foot" (PN=023-025) — even at the novel's end, Thibault expresses himself with passion. The passion that drove his fall never wholly leaves him. It is the constant in his character: the intensity that makes him susceptible to the wolf's temptations is also what makes him capable of final, redemptive action.

## Key Examples

- **PN=001-006**: "To gratify the inordinate passion he had for the chase" — passion as a force that demands satisfaction, institutionalized in aristocratic privilege.
- **PN=005-040**: "Tearing a passion to tatters in a puppet-show" — passion as performance, the artificial display of intense emotion.
- **PN=005-041**: "The towering passion into which it had thrown my lord" — the Baron's anger as overwhelming and threatening force.
- **PN=021-011**: "His passion for the chase, excited by the presence of the wolf" — passion as the hunter's drive, shared across class lines.
- **PN=021-017**: "With equally passionate energy" — passion as the equalizer in the final confrontation between man and beast.
- **PN=023-025**: Thibault speaks "passionately, rising and stamping his foot" — passionate expression as character constant, from first to last.
