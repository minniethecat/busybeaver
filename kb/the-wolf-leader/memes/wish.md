# Wish

## Definition

In *The Wolf-Leader*, the wish (*le souhait*) is both the mechanism of the diabolical pact and the measure of [[thibault|Thibault]]'s moral deterioration. Each wish spoken aloud in the presence of the [[devil|black wolf]] is granted — immediately and literally — and each fulfillment costs [[thibault|Thibault]] one strand of his hair. The wish is a transaction: worldly desire satisfied in exchange for spiritual substance. The novel's genius is to make the mechanism perfectly fair in its arithmetic and perfectly devastating in its results.

## Role in the Novel

The wish system is revealed gradually. [[thibault|Thibault]] stumbles upon it when, in a moment of frustrated desire, he voices a wish and the [[devil|black wolf]] fulfills it. The wolf never explains the mechanism; [[thibault|Thibault]] deduces the rules through experience — and through suffering the consequences. The procedural opacity is itself the trap: [[thibault|Thibault]] makes wishes before understanding what they cost.

The wishes follow an escalating pattern. Early wishes are for material gains: the stag redirected from the Baron, money to settle debts, social advantage. But Dumas carefully shows that [[thibault|Thibault]]'s desires are never purely material: "These two ideas, moreover, of vengeance and gluttony, were so jumbled together in Thibault's mind that he could not distinguish one from the other" (003-056). Every wish is alloyed with resentment, and resentment is what makes the wishes multiply.

As [[thibault|Thibault]]'s resentment grows, his wishes become increasingly malevolent — wishing harm upon enemies, wishing for power over others, wishing for revenge. Each wish is granted with scrupulous exactness by the [[devil|black wolf]] (P02-246). The irony is structural: a wish granted too perfectly becomes indistinguishable from a curse.

The cost of wishing is physical and visible. Each granted wish darkens or removes a strand of [[thibault|Thibault]]'s hair. [[thibault|Thibault]] tries to drown awareness of this cost, "trying to drown his thoughts, but his voice filled him with fear, for there was a new and curious sound in it" (005-031) — the voice itself changes as the self is eroded.

The tragedy of the wish mechanism is that [[thibault|Thibault]] never wishes for the right things. He could wish for wisdom, for [[forgiveness]], for [[repentance|redemption]] — but his character drives him exclusively toward wishes of acquisition and revenge. The [[devil|black wolf]] does not corrupt [[thibault|Thibault]]; it merely provides the mechanism through which [[thibault|Thibault]] corrupts himself. "His first wish had caused the death of poor Marcotte, and had not even procured him a haunch of the buck that he had been so ambitious to obtain" (014-035) — the retrospective accounting reveals both the cost and the futility. Each wish delivers less than it promised.

[[thibault|Thibault]]'s most revealing wish is the one he makes aloud to himself: "Would the devil, I might just for once have my turn at being one of you great lords, might just for twenty-four hours take your place" (015-084). This wish, which will be granted with lethal precision, encapsulates the wish mechanism's tragic logic: the desire is understandable, the means are diabolical, and the consequences are catastrophic.

## Key Examples

The impurity of desire — every wish is a compound of legitimate want and illegitimate resentment, and the mechanism makes no distinction between them.

The somatic cost of wishing — [[thibault|Thibault]] hears the change in himself before he can name it.

The wish's ironic ledger: a man dead, and the venison never arrived. The transaction is always worse than it appeared.

> "Would the devil, I might just for once have my turn at being one of you great lords." (015-084)

The wish that defines [[thibault|Thibault]]'s tragedy — the desire to transcend his [[class|station]], spoken aloud and granted with fatal precision.

## Historical and Folkloric Background

The wish-granting devil is a well-established folkloric motif, appearing in tales from the medieval *Gesta Romanorum* to the Brothers Grimm. The classic three-wish story — where wishes are granted literally but disastrously — shares DNA with Dumas's treatment. Dumas innovates by making the number of wishes open-ended (limited only by the number of hairs on [[thibault|Thibault]]'s head) and by making each wish's cost cumulative rather than isolated. The hair as token draws on widespread folk beliefs about hair containing a person's vital essence — from Samson's strength to French folk magic's use of hair in binding spells.

## Author's Treatment

Dumas's wish mechanism is notable for its restraint as a narrative device. Unlike fairy-tale wishes that produce spectacular transformations, [[thibault|Thibault]]'s wishes produce plausible — if darkly ironic — outcomes. A diverted stag leads to a drowned pricker. A wished-for dinner leads to a jealous husband's rage. This realism grounds the supernatural element in psychological credibility: [[thibault|Thibault]]'s destruction feels earned rather than arbitrary, the consequence of character rather than of magic. The wish is the novel's most elegant invention — a device that is simultaneously folkloric, psychological, and moral.
