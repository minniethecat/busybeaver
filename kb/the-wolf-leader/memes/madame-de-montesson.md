# Madame de Montesson

## Character Overview

Madame de Montesson is the morganatic wife of the [[duke-of-orleans|Duke of Orleans]]. Her marriage to the Duke in 1773 was a major social event that drew the aristocracy to [[villers-cotterets|Villers-Cotterêts]] (001-007). She accompanies the Duke on his hunting visits to the forest, and her presence amplifies the social magnetism of the ducal court, drawing the provincial nobility into its orbit.

## Role in the Story

Madame de Montesson's role is ambient rather than active — she is part of the aristocratic machinery that shapes the novel's social world. Her marriage to the Duke of Orleans marks a key moment in the novel's historical backdrop:

> "This had taken place in 1773, at the time of the marriage of his Royal Highness with Madame de Montesson" (001-007).

Her arrival at [[villers-cotterets|Villers-Cotterêts]] alongside the Duke disrupts the normal rhythms of the forest:

> "As ill luck would have it, the Duke of Orleans and Madame de Montesson came to spend a few days at Villers-Cotterêts" (009-016).

The Duke, "for he had just married Madame de Montesson" (011-041), now visited the region only rarely, making her presence at the hunt a notable event that concentrates aristocratic activity.

[[thibault|Thibault]] learns through [[francois|François]] that the Duke is "hunting with his Highness the Duke of Orleans and Madame de Montesson" (015-026), confirming her continued presence at the season's events.

## Key Relationships

- **[[duke-of-orleans|The Duke of Orleans]]** — Her husband (morganatic marriage, 1773); her presence accompanies his during the hunting season
- **[[baron-of-vez|The Baron of Vez]]** — The Duke's chief wolf-hunter, whose activities are heightened during the ducal visits in which she participates
- **[[villers-cotterets|Villers-Cotterêts]]** — The forest estate where she stays with the Duke during the hunting season

## Historical Context

Charlotte-Jeanne Béraud de La Haye de Riou (1738–1806) was a French noblewoman and writer. Her marriage to Louis Philippe I, Duke of Orleans, in 1773 was morganatic — meaning she did not share her husband's rank and titles, and any children would not inherit. This required royal approval and was a subject of considerable social interest.

The Marquise was known for her literary salon and her own writings. Dumas's reference to her in the context of the Villers-Cotterêts hunting season is historically plausible: the Orleans family maintained a residence in the region and the Duke's hunting parties were genuine social events of the pre-Revolutionary period.
