## Context

The use of dreams as supernatural portents has deep roots in both folklore and Gothic fiction. Dumas, writing in the 1850s when interest in mesmerism, somnambulism, and altered states of consciousness was widespread in French literary circles, integrates dream-visions into his werewolf narrative with unusual sophistication. The dreams are not mere plot devices; they are expressions of a psychological realism that coexists with the supernatural — characters perceive in sleep what they cannot or will not perceive while awake.

[[thibault|Thibault]]'s first mention in the novel establishes his ambitious nature through his dreams: "[[thibault|Thibault]] had certainly had dreams of being something other than a peasant" (001-039). This early [[custom|usage]] establishes the dream as a marker of [[ambition|aspiration]] — but the novel will progressively darken this trope, turning dreams from wish-fulfillment to nightmare.

## Event Summary

Dreams in *The Wolf-Leader* function as a second narrative register — a parallel plane in which the truth of [[thibault|Thibault]]'s condition is visible before it manifests in waking life. From the novel's earliest chapters to its final night, dreams serve as warnings ignored, as windows into supernatural reality, and as measures of [[thibault|Thibault]]'s progressive estrangement from ordinary consciousness. [[agnette|Agnelette]] dreams of [[thibault|Thibault]]'s danger; [[thibault|Thibault]] dreams of his abandoned humanity; the novel itself unfolds like a dream from which the dreamer cannot wake.

## Detailed Account

The earliest reference establishes the dream as a positive, if naive, [[violence|force]] in [[thibault|Thibault]]'s character: he "had certainly had dreams of being something other than a peasant" (001-039). This ambition-dream, which any reader of Balzac or Stendhal would recognize, is fundamentally secular — the dream of social mobility.

After [[the-devils-pact|the pact]] with the [[devil|black wolf]], the first morning brings [[thibault|Thibault]]'s first moment of doubt: "The whole thing must have been a dream, but, looking down, he saw the deer that the [[devil|black wolf]] had brought" (005-029). The supernatural has breached the boundary between dream and reality — the evidence of last night's impossibility is lying on his floor.

[[agnette|Agnelette]]'s premonitory dream marks a crucial turning point. She dreams of [[thibault|Thibault]], prays for him, and seeks him out — and her dream has been correct about his danger. But [[thibault|Thibault]], flattered and distracted, fails to register the warning. The novel punishes this failure: every subsequent dream brings worse news.

By the final chapters, dream and reality are inextricable. [[thibault|Thibault]]'s "agitated sleep full of terrible dreams" (019-001) is no escape from his waking horror but its continuation. The church clock of [[oigny|Oigny]] tolls through his nightmares, and the hours pass "one after the other, like the dreams of the night which gradually fade away" (020-038).

[[thibault|Thibault]]'s dying moment returns to the ambition-dream of his youth, now inverted: "dreaming of the time when he trod the earth as a human being" (021-019). The dream of rising above his class has been replaced by the dream of simply being human — a wish that the wolf, ha[[wine|vin]]g taken all his hair, can no longer grant.

## Key Participants

- **[[Thibault]]** — The primary dreamer, whose dreams chart his moral and supernatural transformation. His early dreams are of [[ambition|social ambition]] (001-039); his later dreams, as the werewolf transformation accelerates, become increasingly nightmarish — "agitated sleep full of terrible dreams" (019-001).
- **[[agnette]]** — Her dreams connect her intuitively to [[thibault|Thibault]]'s condition. She dreams of him "much of last night" and prays for his safety (006-009), percei[[wine|vin]]g through her dreams what others cannot see in daylight. Her dreams are always of concern and care, never of ambition or fear for herself.

## Consequences

[[agnette|Agnelette]]'s dreams introduce a pattern of premonitory perception that runs throughout the novel. Her dream of [[thibault|Thibault]] is followed by prayer — she acts on her dream-perception, attempting through [[soul|spirit]]ual means to avert what she has foreseen. [[thibault|Thibault]]'s response — "And what made you dream of me and pray for me, my pretty child?" (006-010) — is touched but ultimately dismissive. He treats her dream as a romantic gesture rather than a genuine premonition.

[[thibault|Thibault]]'s own dreams deteriorate as the novel progresses. The early confidence of his ambition-dreams gives way to the "horrible dream" from which he struggles awake "as a man fighting against some horrible dream, and gave a piercing cry of anguish" (022-016). By the novel's climax, the boundary between dream and reality has collapsed entirely: events in waking life "seemed to him like a dream" (019-004).

The novel's final "dream" is [[thibault|Thibault]]'s dying vision of the forest he once trod as a man, "dreaming of the time when he trod the earth as a human being" (021-019). The dream has become memory; the man has become the monster; and the waking world has become, for [[thibault|Thibault]], the true nightmare.

## Narrative Significance

The dream motif serves Dumas's larger project of Gothic democratization. In the typical Gothic novel, dreams and premonitions belong to aristocrats in castles; here, they belong to a sabot-maker in a forest hut. The supernatural is not imported into the peasant world from outside — it grows there, as native to the soil as the trees.

The dream structure also provides the novel's deepest psychological insight. [[thibault|Thibault]] is not transformed against his will; he is transformed through [[wish|desire]]s that announce themselves first in dreams. The dreams are the unconscious truth of his character, and the wolf merely gives those truths permission to become acts. Dumas's werewolf is not a victim of possession but a man whose dreams have been answered — and the answer is [[soul|damnation]].
