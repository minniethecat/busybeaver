# Instinct

**Instinct** in _The Wolf-Leader_ is the bridge concept that connects human and animal nature, operating in both realms and revealing their uncomfortable proximity. The novel uses instinct to explore what remains of the self when reason, conscience, and will are stripped away — and what arrives in their place.

## Instinctive Fear

The novel's first deployment of instinct is tied to the supernatural. "As the idea, however, of being under the protection of the spirit of darkness filled him with an instinctive fear, he tried to pray" [004-006]. Instinct here is the body's pre-rational response to spiritual danger — Thibault does not _decide_ to fear the devil; his body fears before his mind can form the thought.

The adjective "instinctive" is carefully chosen. Fear of darkness, of the uncanny, of the predator — these operate below the threshold of reasoned belief. Thibault may tell himself that the wolf's offers are merely advantageous, but his instinct knows otherwise. The failure to heed this instinctive warning is his first fully culpable act.

## The Flight Instinct

When Thibault flees from Madame Polet's farm, his direction is determined by instinct. "Thibault, fleeing from before Madame Polet's threats and her farm servants' weapons, turned instinctively towards the forest, thinking to take shelter within it" [009-001]. The forest, which has been the site of his humiliation (the flogging), his temptation (the wolf's appearance), and his crimes (the wishes), is also the direction his body chooses when threatened.

The repetition of "instinctively" in successive paragraphs deepens the pattern. "Turning instinctively to the left, saw a fourth wolf flanking him on that side" [009-002]. Thibault is being herded — his instinctive turns are being anticipated and enclosed by the wolves. Instinct, which should guide a creature to safety, is here guiding Thibault deeper into the wolf-pack's formation.

## Inherited Instinct

The body-swap with Raoul de Vauparfond provides the novel's most remarkable treatment of instinct: the idea that physical skills can be inherited through the body. ""While inheriting Raoul's body, he also inherited his physical qualities, for the horse, being an intelligent beast, and perfectly conscious of the momentary weakness of its rider, began to behave in a manner which would have done honour to the best groom in the stables of the Duke of Orleans"" [016-009].

Later, when forced to fence, "Thibault, who was something of an adept at quarterstaff, had no idea of fencing; what was his surprise therefore, when he found, that he knew by instinct how to handle his weapon" [017-068]. The instinct here is not his own — it belongs to Raoul's body, whose muscles have been trained through years of aristocratic fencing practice. Thibault the sabot-maker cannot fence; Raoul's body can, and instinct is the medium through which bodily memory transfers between identities.

This notion of inherited instinct complicates the novel's human/animal division. If instinct can cross between two human bodies, it can also cross between human and wolf — and the werewolf transformation is precisely this crossing, made literal and irreversible.

## The Instinct of Self-Preservation

The novel's final treatment of instinct returns to its most primitive form: the drive to survive. "But still the instinct of self-preservation did not fail him; and still his strength was undiminished" [024-003]. Even as Thibault's humanity, his relationships, his very physical form are stripped away, the instinct of self-preservation persists.

Yet the novel immediately qualifies this persistence. "But the grief of the man overcame this instinct of the wild beast at bay; a shudder passed through the body hidden beneath its wolf skin; tears fell from the fierce bloodred eyes" [024-010]. Grief — an emotion the wolf should not be capable of — overcomes instinct. The human within the beast temporarily reasserts itself not through reason or will but through sorrow, the one emotion the novel has consistently associated with Thibault's surviving moral sense.

## Instinct and the Werewolf

The novel's philosophical treatment of instinct maps precisely onto the werewolf transformation. The wolf is pure instinct — hunting, fleeing, surviving — unmediated by reflection. The human is instinct overlaid with conscience, reason, and moral choice. Thibault's tragedy is not that he becomes a wolf but that he discovers how much wolf was already in him. The pact does not implant instinct where none existed; it strips away the overlays that kept instinct in check, revealing the beast that was always there.
