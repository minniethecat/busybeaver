# Wine

## Definition

In *The Wolf-Leader*, wine (*le vin*) is not merely a beverage — it is a social institution, a class signifier, and a moral reagent. What a character drinks, how much, and with whom locate that character precisely within the novel's social order. The Baron drinks vintage Burgundy; the peasants drink *pignolet*, a thin local wine; [[thibault|Thibault]] drinks both, and his passage from one register to the other maps his social ascent and moral descent.

Wine in the novel belongs to a long [[custom|tradition]] in French literature — from Rabelais to Balzac — in which food and drink are never merely sustenance but always social text. Dumas, a legendary host and gourmand whose dinner parties were Parisian institutions, writes about wine with the [[power|authority]] of lived experience. The novel's wine scenes are not decorative; they are diagnostic, revealing character and class with each poured glass.

## Role in the Novel

Wine enters the novel early as an index of [[thibault|Thibault]]'s deprivation and [[wish|desire]]. His resentment of the Baron crystallizes around consumption: "their fresh meats and their old wines at every meal, while I have nothing but black bread and water" (002-049). The opposition — fresh meats and old wines against black bread and water — establishes the gustatory chasm between noble and peasant, and [[thibault|Thibault]]'s ambition is, in its first articulation, a hunger for the Baron's table.

The wedding feast at [[croyolles|Croyolles]] becomes the novel's central wine set-piece. [[thibault|Thibault]] drinks three bottles: "The fumes of the heady wine he had been drinking had begun to cloud his brain" (011-051). The progression is precisely measured — from sociability to excess to violence — and wine is the accelerant at each stage. The three bottles mark the dissolution of [[thibault|Thibault]]'s self-control and the emergence of the wolf within:

> "It is not because you drank three bottles of wine, but because you let yourself be carried away by your wicked passions" (011-066).

Wine also functions as medicine in the novel's folk economy. The frame narrative establishes this [[custom|usage]]: "I d[[class|rank]] a large bowl of hot wine before going to bed" (P02-058). Hot wine — wine heated with spices — was the peasant pharmacopeia, the remedy for chills, fatigue, and the nameless complaints of rural life. This therapeutic wine belongs to the world of the body, not the world of status; it is wine as work rather than wine as display.

The Baron's wine cellar represents the opposite register — wine as privilege, wine as exclusion, wine as the stored surplus of feudal extraction. When [[thibault|Thibault]] accesses the Baron's wines through supernatural means, he commits a gustatory crime that parallels his poaching of the Baron's game. Both transgressions are violations of the seigneurial monopoly, and both announce that the old order's boundaries are permeable to those who have made alternative arrangements.

Wine as folk medicine — the peasant pharmacopeia in the frame narrative:

> "I drank a large bowl of hot wine before going to bed" (P02-058).

The wedding feast — wine as social accelerant and moral solvent:

> "The fumes of the heady wine he had been drinking had begun to cloud his brain" (011-051).

The moral diagnosis — wine as occasion rather than cause:

> "It is not because you drank three bottles of wine, but because you let yourself be carried away by your wicked passions" (011-066).

Wine as hospitality — the code of the table that structures rural sociability:

> "We will drink a bottle of old wine together, if Perrine will get it for us" (010-017).

## Historical and Folkloric Background

Wine in eighteenth-century France was universal but stratified. The nobility d[[class|rank]] aged wines from Burgundy and Bordeaux; the [[peasant|peasantry]] d[[class|rank]] *pignolet*, *vin de pays*, or cider, often produced locally and consumed young. Wine was also a caloric staple, providing a significant portion of the peasant diet's energy. The *cabaret* (tavern) was the peasant's social center, and wine was its currency.

The medical use of wine — as a warming agent, a soporific, a digestive — was standard in both folk and learned medicine. *Vin chaud* (hot wine with spices) appears in medical treatises and folk remedies alike, recommended for everything from ague to melancholy. The novel's frame narrative, with its bowl of hot wine before bed, accurately reflects this therapeutic culture.

## Author's Treatment

Dumas's treatment of wine is morally subtle. Wine is not condemned — Dumas was far too fond of it himself for that — but it is revealed as a substance that exposes rather than creates. [[thibault|Thibault]] drunk is [[thibault|Thibault]] undefended, and what emerges from beneath the defenses is the ambition, the resentment, the violence that sobriety kept in check. Wine does not corrupt; it reveals corruption already present.

The novel's wine economy is also a class economy. Access to good wine tracks access to power, and [[thibault|Thibault]]'s gustatory ascent — from water to *pignolet* to the Baron's cellar — is a synecdoche for his social ascent. That this ascent ends in [[hell|damnation]] does not invalidate the [[wish|desire]]; it only [[devil|demon]]strates that in the *ancien régime*, even the [[wish|desire]] for good wine could lead a man to the devil.
