# Mocquet

## Character Overview

Mocquet is the [[baron-of-vez|Baron of Vez]]'s gamekeeper — a keeper of both the forest's game and its stories. It is Mocquet who first identifies the [[devil|black wolf]] as a supernatural entity, providing the folkloric framework through which the reader (and the other characters) understand [[thibault|Thibault]]'s transformation.

## Role in the Story

Mocquet serves as the novel's chorus — the voice of rural folk wisdom that recognizes supernatural evil when it appears. When the [[devil|black wolf]] evades all attempts to kill it, Mocquet explains why: "Nor can any bullet pierce the skin of the devil, or kill those who have made a [[devil-pact|compact]] with him" (P02-330). He identifies the [[devil|black wolf]] explicitly as the devil (P02-336), establishing the theological stakes of [[thibault|Thibault]]'s pact before [[thibault|Thibault]] himself understands them.

Mocquet's storytelling frames the entire narrative. The novel is presented as a tale told by Mocquet to the author, and his voice — earthy, superstitious, and morally certain — provides the narrative's folkloric authenticity. He is the link between the supernatural events of the story and the oral [[custom|tradition]] that preserves them.

## Key Relationships

- **The [[black-wolf|Black Wolf]]** — Mocquet is the character who most clearly understands what the [[devil|black wolf]] is, identifying it as the devil himself (P02-336).
- **[[Baron-of-Vez]]** — Mocquet's employer, who values his gamekeeper's knowledge of the forest and its creatures.
- **[[Thibault]]** — Mocquet knows the story of [[thibault|Thibault]] from thirty years past and tells it as a cautionary tale.

## Character Analysis

Mocquet embodies the oral storytelling [[custom|tradition]] that Dumas uses to frame *The Wolf-Leader*. His tales are not merely exposition; they are the mechanism by which folk knowledge is transmitted and preserved. When Mocquet says the [[devil|black wolf]] is the devil, he is not speculating — he is reporting the accumulated wisdom of generations of forest-dwellers.

His role as storyteller also serves a metafictional function. Mocquet is Dumas's surrogate within the text — the tale-teller whose art transforms local legend into lasting narrative. The frame story (the author listening to Mocquet's tales) establishes the novel as a written record of oral [[custom|tradition]], lending the supernatural elements an ambiguity that sophisticated readers can interpret either literally or allegorically.
