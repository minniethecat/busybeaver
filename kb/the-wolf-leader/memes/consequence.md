# Consequence

Consequence (*conséquence*) is the result or effect of an action — the outcome that follows, with varying degrees of necessity, from a prior cause. In *The Wolf-Leader*, consequence is the novel's implicit moral principle: every wish produces effects that exceed the wisher's intention, and these effects inevitably return to their source.

## The Chain of Consequences

Each of [[thibault|Thibault]]'s wishes sets off a chain of consequences that he did not foresee and cannot control. His wish to strike the [[baron-of-vez|Baron]] results in the blow landing on [[agnette|Agnelette]] — the first demonstration that supernatural power does not exempt its user from the law of unintended consequences. Subsequent wishes bring wealth that isolates, power that corrupts, and transformations that cannot be reversed.

The novel's structure is a study in consequence: each chapter follows from the choices made in the one before, and the accumulation of effects builds toward a conclusion that feels both surprising and inevitable.

## Consequence and Responsibility

Thibault's tragedy is partly his refusal to accept responsibility for the consequences of his actions. He blames the [[devil|devil]], the [[baron-of-vez|Baron]], his circumstances — everyone but himself. The novel quietly insists that consequences do not depend on acceptance: they arrive whether or not the agent acknowledges his role in producing them.

This is the novel's most durable moral insight: you are responsible not only for what you intend but for what your actions cause. The devil may provide the mechanism, but Thibault provides the will, and the consequences belong to him.

## Consequence as Narrative Engine

At the level of craft, consequence is what drives the novel forward. Unlike episodic narratives in which each adventure is self-contained, *The Wolf-Leader* is a tightly causal story in which each event is the consequence of previous events. The final hunt is the consequence of the first wish; the werewolf transformation is the consequence of the progressive surrender to predation; Thibault's death is the consequence of a life lived without regard for the chain of effects that every action initiates.
