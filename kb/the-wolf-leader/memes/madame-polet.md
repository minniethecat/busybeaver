# Madame Polet

## Character Overview

Madame Polet is the widowed owner of the Mill at [[croyolles|Croyolles]], described by Dumas as "the rich and beautiful Madame Polet" (006-005). She is a woman of independent means — a mill owner in her own right — and represents the rural bourgeoisie that occupies the social space between the [[peasant|peasantry]] and the [[class|nobility]] in the novel's world. Her mill is a significant local enterprise, employing [[landry|Landry]] as head boy and other workers.

She is a figure of romantic interest: her beauty, wealth, and availability make her the most desirable match in the countryside, and both [[landry|Landry]] and [[thibault|Thibault]] pursue her affections in Chapters 6 through 8.

## Role in the Story

[[thibault|Thibault]], emboldened by his [[the-devils-pact|pact with the black wolf]], resolves to court Madame Polet as the first test of his newfound power:

> "Thibault was determined to try his fortune with Madame Polet before the day was over" (006-007).

He arrives at the mill to find his cousin [[landry|Landry]] already in her service — and already in love with her. [[landry|Landry]] confesses that he had declared his feelings and was harshly rebuffed:

> "Madame Polet flew into a great rage; called me an insolent beggar, and threatened to turn me out of doors the very next week" (007-045).

[[thibault|Thibault]], comparing his own "five feet six and well-set figure" favorably against [[landry|Landry]]'s "timid, boyish looks," concludes that his cousin's failure augurs his own success (007-064). Over dinner, Madame Polet's behavior toward [[thibault|Thibault]] shifts from distant politeness to flirtatious attention — "sidelong glances and smiles" — until an interruption from the authorities, seeking a conscript named Landry, breaks the scene (007-075).

When [[thibault|Thibault]], transformed by his wishes into a more handsome and commanding figure, renews his pursuit, Madame Polet responds with unambiguous interest:

> "And have I turned you away?" asked Madame Polet, with an expression which it was impossible to misinterpret (008-009).

## Key Relationships

- **[[thibault|Thibault]]** — Her ambitious suitor, who sees her as a means of social advancement. His pursuit of her is motivated less by affection than by the desire to possess what his station would normally deny him (006-007).
- **[[landry|Landry]]** — Her employee and first suitor, whose declaration of love she rejected with fury, though her later behavior suggests her affections may be more complex than her initial reaction implied (007-045).
- **[[croyolles|The Mill at Croyolles]]** — Madame Polet's property and domain, the setting where her interactions with both suitors unfold.

## Quotations

Dumas introduces her as a figure of social consequence:

> "Formerly, it would never have occurred to Thibault to aspire to anyone in the position of the rich and beautiful Madame Polet, for such was the name of the owner of the mill" (006-005).

Her reaction to [[landry|Landry]]'s overture reveals her temper:

> "Then Madame Polet flew into a great rage; called me an insolent beggar, and threatened to turn me out of doors the very next week" (007-045).

Her ambiguous response to [[thibault|Thibault]]'s advances:

> "And have I not turned you away?" asked Madame Polet, with an expression which it was impossible to misinterpret (008-009).
