## Definition

In *The Wolf-Leader*, "fortune" encompasses the pre-modern understanding of luck and fate — a quasi-personal force that distributes worldly goods and misfortunes without reference to moral desert. The concept bridges folk belief and social observation: fortune is what one "makes" through travel and trade, yet also what one is born into. It stands apart from Christian providence (God's will) and from the devil's interventions (the pact), occupying the middle ground of secular happenstance that the villagers use to explain life's inequalities.

## Role in the Novel

Fortune shapes Thibault's social position from the outset. He is described as "more envious of the good fortune of his neighbour than was altogether conducive to his peace of mind" [001-051], establishing fortune as the comparative currency of village life. His three years of travel fail to improve his station: "he did not make his fortune during that time" [001-044], and the narrative presents this failure as a turning point — the thwarted ambition that opens the door to supernatural alternatives.

The concept also operates as a foil to the devil's pact. Where the wolf offers supernatural shortcuts to wealth and power, fortune represents the legitimate, slow path that Thibault lacks the patience and character to pursue. The tension between fortune (earned through work and time) and the pact (granted instantly but at terrible cost) structures the novel's moral economy.

## Key Examples

> "he was, perhaps, rather more envious of the good fortune of his neighbour than was altogether conducive to his peace of mind" [001-051]

This early characterization establishes fortune-envy as Thibault's defining flaw — more fundamental than his specific grievances against the Baron.

> "He did not make his fortune during that time, but he learned a great many things" [001-044]

The narrator's tone is almost gentle here, but the failure to make one's fortune is the failure that sets everything else in motion — the economic frustration that the wolf exploits.

## Fortune in Action

The novel introduces fortune-envy as Thibault's defining trait: he harboured toward neighbours "more favoured by fortune than himself" a grain of envy that was "not altogether conducive to the welfare of his soul" [001-037] — the narrator's moralizing aside yokes worldly fortune to spiritual peril from the very first chapter.

The novel's minor episodes reinforce fortune's role as the default explanation for life's vicissitudes. When the Baron's menials react to Thibault's impending flogging, they are said to have "thought it good luck to have a culprit on whom they could avenge themselves for the mishaps of the day" [003-004] — fortune here is zero-sum, one man's suffering becoming another's relief. Later, having renounced Agnelette in favour of ambition, "Thibault was determined to try his fortune with Madame Polet before the day was over" [006-007], the verb "try" capturing fortune's inherent uncertainty: one can only attempt it, never command it.
