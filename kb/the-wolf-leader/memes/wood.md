# Wood

## Definition

In *The Wolf-Leader*, wood (*le bois*) is more than landscape — it is the material substance of [[thibault|Thibault]]'s identity. He is introduced not as a peasant in the abstract but specifically as a *sabot-maker* — a craftsman who transforms raw wood into wooden shoes. The novel's opening establishes this material identity with precision: "stood the hut of [[thibault|Thibault]], the sabot-maker" (001-032). The hut is made of wood, the trade is worked in wood, and the man is inseparable from the material he shapes.

Wood in the novel operates on two registers: as raw material from the forest (connecting to [[forest|Forest]] and [[nature|Nature]]) and as crafted object (connecting to labor, skill, and class). The second register is the more socially significant. A sabot-maker occupies a specific niche in the rural economy — below the blacksmith, above the day laborer — and his relationship to wood is both technical [[power|mastery]] and social constraint. He can shape wood but cannot transcend the [[class|station]] that wood-working defines.

## Role in the Novel

[[thibault|Thibault]]'s trade is established in the novel's opening paragraphs and recurs throughout as a marker of his social position:

> "He had made a hundred pairs of wooden shoes and had sold these to Bedeau" (001-049).

The hundred pairs represent honest labor — the life [[thibault|Thibault]] could have led, the identity he might have accepted. The novel repeatedly returns to the sabot-maker's bench as a site of normality against which the supernatural plot measures its deviations. When [[thibault|Thibault]] pauses to shape a wooden shoe, the reader is reminded of what he is abandoning:

> "He had begun, however, to shape his wooden shoe, when, some distance off, from the direction of Oigny, the church clock struck nine" (005-032).

The sabot-maker's craft also carries class signification. The proverb about wooden shoes — "'Some folks are like ill-made sabots — smooth outside, but rough within'" (012-045) — [[devil|demon]]strates how wood-working permeates folk speech. Wood is what peasants work, wear, and inhabit; it is the material sign of their condition.

The wolf, significantly, does not approach a generic peasant but a *sabot-maker* specifically. The devil's interest in a wood-worker recalls folk [[custom|tradition]]s of craftsmen who bargain with supernatural powers for skill — the Faust legend transposed to a rural French key. [[thibault|Thibault]]'s wish is not merely to escape poverty but to escape the wooden shoes that mark him as what he is.

## Key Examples

Establishing identity through craft — the first description of [[thibault|Thibault]]:

> "Stood the hut of Thibault, the sabot-maker" (001-032).

Honest labor quantified — the scale of the life being abandoned:

> "He had made a hundred pairs of wooden shoes and had sold these to Bedeau" (001-049).

The candle carved as a miniature sabot — craft as identity even in domestic objects:

> "The handle had been carved a little wooden shoe, which had served as Thibault's candidate for the *chef-d'oeuvre*" (003-001).

Wood in folk speech — the material basis of peasant wisdom:

> "'Some folks are like ill-made sabots — smooth outside, but rough within'" (012-045).

The transformation that ambition effects — from craftsman to something unrecognizable:

> "Thibault no longer appeared like a wooden shoemaker's apprentice, but like some great lord" (010-002).

## Historical and Folkloric Background

The sabot-maker (*sabotier*) was a recognized rural trade in *ancien régime* France, occupying a position between agricultural laborer and skilled artisan. Sabots were the universal footwear of the [[peasant|peasantry]] — cheap, durable, and made from locally available wood (typically beech or alder). The trade required apprenticeship and skill but conferred little social prestige; a sabot-maker remained firmly within the Third Estate.

In French folklore, the sabot carries ambiguous symbolism. The *sabot de Noël* (Christmas shoe) receives gifts; the *sabot* left by the hearth is a vessel for luck or mischief. The proverb quoted in the novel — distinguishing surface from substance — belongs to a rich [[custom|tradition]] of sabot-related folk speech. Dumas, who knew rural France intimately from his travels and his father's origins, deploys this material culture with ethnographic precision.

## Author's Treatment

Dumas uses wood-working as a measure of [[thibault|Thibault]]'s moral trajectory. When [[thibault|Thibault]] shapes a sabot, he is engaged in honest, productive labor — creating value from raw material, participating in the social economy, performing the identity his world has assigned him. When he abandons the bench for the wolf's wishes, he abandons creation for consumption, craft for shortcut.

The novel's treatment of wood is unsentimental. Sabot-making is not romanticized; it is presented as hard, repetitive work whose only reward is subsistence. But it is *real* — a genuine relationship between human skill and natural material — in contrast to the wolf's wishes, which produce effects without labor and consequences without foreknowledge. [[thibault|Thibault]]'s tragedy is partly the tragedy of craft abandoned: the skill that defined him, set aside for powers that destroy him.
