# Sleep

## Definition

Sleep in *The Wolf-Leader* is more than a biological necessity — it is a symbolic threshold. The novel's frame narrative is organized around sleep and its disturbances: Mocquet cannot sleep because he is nightmared; the tale is told to restore his ability to "sleep in peace." Sleep is the state in which the rational self is suspended, leaving the sleeper vulnerable to supernatural visitation — nightmare, dream, and the memories that constitute the novel's own narrative method.

The novel distinguishes between peaceful sleep and troubled sleep. Peaceful sleep is what Mocquet desires, what Mother Durand is promised, and what Thibault forfeits. Troubled sleep — interrupted, nightmare-haunted, impossible — is the condition of those who have encountered the supernatural.

## Role in the Novel

### Sleep as Frame Narrative Engine

The entire novel is told to cure Mocquet's inability to sleep. "Mother Durand will be able to sleep in peace, which I should certainly not be able to do, if I knew you had been murdering a mole" (P02-137) — the father's dry humor connects the murder of the witch's goat to the practical goal of restorative sleep. The frame returns repeatedly to the question of sleep: "Are you in a hurry to go to sleep, Mocquet?" (P02-219), and "two hours before I could get to sleep" (P02-226). The storytelling session is measured in sleepless hours.

### Sleep and Death

The novel draws a persistent analogy between sleep and death. The wolf-leader, encountering the silent castle, asks whether its occupant is in "the sleep of this world or of eternity" (PN=020-034). The ambiguity between sleep and death echoes the broader thematic concern with what lies beyond waking life. Sleep is the little death that prefigures the final one.

### The Sleeping Dogs Proverb

"Best let sleeping dogs lie" (PN=007-063) — a folk proverb that appears at a crucial narrative juncture. The wisdom of the proverb is precisely what Thibault violates: he rouses what should have been left to sleep, and the consequences are catastrophic. The proverb encodes the novel's implicit warning about disturbing the natural order.

### Sleep Interrupted

Sleep is repeatedly interrupted in the novel. The forest is "roused out of its first sleep" by the hunt (PN=008-047). Thibault is kept from sleeping by his consuming thoughts (PN=009-006), then "fell fast asleep" from exhaustion (PN=009-008). Sleep and its interruption mark the rhythm of the narrative — rest is never secure, and wakefulness brings new dangers.

### The Sleeplessness of Guilt

The frame narrator recalls "How many sleepless nights" (P02-226) — a confession that the tale itself disturbs sleep. The story of the wolf-leader, once heard, becomes its own kind of nightmare. The transmission of the tale creates the very condition it purports to cure.

## Key Examples

- **P02-137**: "Mother Durand will be able to sleep in peace" — the father's ironic promise, linking the tale's resolution to the restoration of sleep.
- **P02-190**: "And where will the poor child sleep?" — practical concern in the frame, grounding the supernatural tale in domestic reality.
- **P02-219**: "Are you in a hurry to go to sleep, Mocquet?" — the storyteller's question that keeps the tale and the night going.
- **P02-226**: "Two hours before I could get to sleep. How many sleepless nights" — the tale's effect on its listener, the contagion of sleeplessness.
- **PN=007-063**: "Best let sleeping dogs lie" — folk wisdom as moral warning, the proverb Thibault fatally ignores.
- **PN=008-047**: The forest "roused out of its first sleep" — the natural world personified as a sleeper disturbed by human violence.
- **PN=020-034**: "The sleep of this world or of eternity?" — sleep as metaphor for death, the ultimate ambiguity.
