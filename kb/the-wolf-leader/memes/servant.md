## Definition

The servant in *The Wolf-Leader* occupies a distinct and structurally significant position within the novel's feudal social order. Unlike the peasant (tied to the land) or the noble (tied to birth), the servant exists in a zone of proximity to power without possessing it — close enough to observe the workings of authority, distant enough to be its instrument rather than its beneficiary. The novel's servant characters — from farm-hands to liveried domestics to the Bailiff (who styles himself a "humble servant") — illuminate the gradations of status within the ancien régime and the moral complexities of service.

## Role in the Novel

Servants function as both actors and witnesses in the novel's social drama. The farm servants who refuse to attack Thibault (008-048) demonstrate the limits of delegated authority: they are bound to obey, but their obedience has moral thresholds. The liveried servants at the Baron's castle embody the performance of hierarchy — their grey coats marking them as instruments of their master's will, their "well-bred" intuition (015-031) a form of social knowledge acquired through proximity to power.

Thibault himself occupies an ambiguous position relative to the servant class. As a sabot-maker, he is technically a free artisan, not a domestic servant. Yet his social position — dependent on noble patronage, subject to noble whim, powerless before the law — mirrors that of the servant. His transformation into the wolf-leader can be read as a grotesque inversion of service: instead of serving a human master, he commands a pack of wolves; instead of being beaten by his betters, he devours them.

The novel's treatment of servants also reveals the intimate violence of the feudal household. The "grey-coat" (015-017) — a liveried servant in a grey overall — is both a person and a function, his identity subsumed into the livery he wears. When "all the servants had fled" (020-062) from the excommunicated castle, their departure marks the total collapse of the social order they sustained.

## Key Examples

- **"Tell your servants to let the poor man go"** (003-013): Agnelette appeals to the Seigneur Jean to command his servants to release Thibault. The servants are extensions of the master's will — they act, but their actions are attributed to him.

- **The farm servants' refusal** (008-048): "'Knock him down!' But the farm servants shook their heads." A moment of quiet moral resistance: the servants decline to enact violence on command, asserting a limit to what service demands.

- **The liveried servant** (015-017): "A grey-coat is a liveried servant, who puts on a grey overall to do his work." The definition of the servant by his uniform, his identity absorbed into his function.

- **The well-bred servant's intuition** (015-031): "With that ready intuition of the well-bred servant, understood at once, and went to open the gate." The servant's social intelligence — a skill cultivated through proximity to power — deployed in service of the master's romantic intrigue.

- **The servants' sympathy** (015-035): "Great sympathy on the part of the servants with their master's happiness." The emotional entanglement of servants in their master's fortunes, suggesting both genuine affection and the dependency that makes the master's wellbeing the servant's own.

- **"I cannot treat her quite like a servant"** (017-025): The ambiguous status of the confidante who knows all the family secrets — neither equal nor fully subordinate, inhabiting the servant role's intimate paradox.

- **The servants flee** (020-062–063): "All the servants had fled, saying that the excommunicated body of their master was enough to bring down the walls of the Castle on their heads." Supernatural fear dissolves the servant-master bond; self-preservation overrides feudal obligation.
