## The Clock-Vision: Eternity as Hallucination

The novel's most striking meditation on eternity occurs in Chapter 18,
during Thibault's fever after the body-swap with Raoul de Vauparfond.
Lying in the priest's house, he experiences a visionary transformation of time itself:

"The spirit of Thibault could hear the priest's clock striking the hours...
the clock seemed to have grown to gigantic proportions and the face of it was the blue vault of heaven,
and the numbers on it were flames; and the clock was called eternity,
and the monstrous pendulum, as it swung backwards and forwards called out in turn at every beat: 'Never! Forever!'" (018-021).

This is the novel's most explicit philosophical passage. Eternity is rendered as a clock — the instrument of measured time — yet the measurement is of the unmeasurable.
The pendulum's alternating cry — Never, Forever — captures the Faustian bargain's essential horror:
a finite transaction (wishes) with infinite consequence (damnation).

## Eternity in Death

The body of Raoul de Vauparfond, when Thibault sees it laid out, bears "the still, unearthly beauty which is born of eternity" (019-008).
Death here is not merely absence of life but presence of eternity — a state more profound than sleep, marked by the "immovable calm" that signals "King Death" (019-008).

This eternal beauty on Raoul's face contrasts with Thibault's deteriorating humanity.
Raoul, dead, achieves a perfection that Thibault, alive, is losing — the eternal dignity of one who died naturally as opposed to the eternal degradation of one who bargained with the devil.

## The Unending Ring and Unanswered Question

The eternity motif crystallises in a single image: "the ring which seemed riveted to his finger for all eternity" (021-040).
The devil's ring — the pact's physical token — binds across time without end.

Finally, eternity haunts the unresolved question that wakes Thibault from his fever:
"Was the Countess sleeping the sleep of this world or of eternity?" (020-034).
The line between sleep and death, between temporary and permanent,
is the line Thibault has blurred through his Faustian bargain.
