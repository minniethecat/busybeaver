## Definition

Hope is the emotional state of anticipating and desiring a positive future outcome. In *The Wolf-Leader*, hope operates as a psychological motivator that propels Thibault forward after each setback, functioning as both a genuine human emotion and a trap laid by the wolf's supernatural intervention. Unlike ambition — which is oriented toward social status — hope is the underlying emotional fuel that makes ambition feel achievable.

## Role in the Novel

Hope is the emotional arc driver of Thibault's journey. Before the wolf-pact, Thibault "had often thought about her, but without hope" [006-005] — his peasant station precluded any aspiration toward the wealthy widow. The pact changes this calculus entirely: "seeing that he was under the protection of the wolf, and that he had been endowed with a supernatural power... it seemed to him an easy matter to get rid of all his rivals" [006-005].

Hope is repeatedly kindled and extinguished throughout the narrative. When Madame Polet shows him attention at dinner, Thibault "took heart again, filled with hope" [007-072]. After Landry's removal, he "still cherished the most sanguine hopes" [008-029]. The Baron's favorable comment "reviving Thibault's hopes, restored him once more to good humour" [012-044]. Each time hope rises, it is subsequently crushed — culminating in the moment Thibault leaves the Baron's house "where he had left behind so many fond hopes, now lost forever" [013-128].

In the novel's climax, hope transmutes into the bare instinct of survival: as the werewolf flees the hunt, "he knew his ground and hoped to evade the dogs" [024-003]. Even this last desperate hope proves insufficient against the relentless pursuit.

## Key Examples

- **Hope without the pact [006-005]**: Before supernatural intervention, Thibault's social reality precluded hope entirely. The wolf's power is what converts impossible aspiration into seemingly achievable goal.
- **Hope restored at Croyolles [007-072]**: The widow's "attention and politeness" rekindles Thibault's confidence, demonstrating hope's fragility and dependence on external validation.
- **Fond hopes lost forever [013-128]**: The definitive shattering of Thibault's romantic and social hopes as he slams the Baron's door, marking the irreversible turn toward vengeance.

## Author's Treatment

Dumas structures hope as a cyclical rhythm — rise and fall, rise and fall — that mirrors the werewolf transformation motif. Each wish grants Thibault temporary power, each hope rises on that power, and each hope collapses under the wish's unintended consequences. Hope is thus inseparable from the devil-pact mechanism: the wolf does not create hope, but exploits Thibault's pre-existing capacity for it, weaponizing hope as the bait that keeps Thibault wishing.
