# Night

Night (*la nuit*) is the period between sunset and sunrise — the time of darkness, sleep, and, in the folk imagination, of supernatural activity. In *The Wolf-Leader*, night is the temporal domain in which the novel's central transformations occur. It is when wolves hunt, when the [[devil|devil]] appears, and when [[thibault|Thibault]]'s wished-for powers manifest.

## The Prologue's Nocturnal Frame

The novel opens at night. The framing narrator describes how he came to hear the story of Thibault during a night-time gathering, establishing night as the proper time for the telling of dark tales. The prologue's atmosphere — "the wind moaning through the chimney, the dogs howling at the unseen" [P02-051] — prepares the reader for a world in which night is not merely the absence of day but the presence of something else.

## Night and the Hunt

The wolf hunt that opens the main narrative begins at dawn, but wolves are creatures of the night. The hunters speak of wolves as "nocturnal brigands" [001-004], and the entire enterprise of the hunt is an attempt to impose human, diurnal order upon a threat that belongs to darkness. The hunt's failure — the wolves escape — suggests the limits of daylight authority.

## Night as Transformation Time

Thibault's wishes are granted at night. The black wolf appears after sunset, and each wish takes effect in the darkness. This temporal pattern aligns with folkloric tradition — the devil's hour is midnight, and supernatural bargains are sealed in darkness — but Dumas gives it psychological weight. Night is when social supervision lapses, when the village sleeps, and when a man's hidden desires can surface without witnesses.

## The Night of the Final Hunt

The novel's climactic hunt occurs at night, inverting the opening's dawn chase. Now it is Thibault who is hunted, transformed into a wolf, fleeing through darkness that is no longer his ally. "The night was so black that the sky seemed to have been swallowed by the earth" — night has become Thibault's prison rather than his refuge.
