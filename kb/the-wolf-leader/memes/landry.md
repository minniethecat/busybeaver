## Character Overview

Landry is a peasant carter and [[thibault|Thibault]]'s cousin, introduced in Chapter 7 when [[thibault|Thibault]] encounters him in the forest of [[villers-cotterets|Villers-Cotterêts]]. He is described as the "head boy at the school at [[villers-cotterets|Villers-Cotterets]]" [007-005] in his youth, and works as a transporter of goods through the forest — "unloading his asses" [007-069] after a day's labor. His lovelorn confession to [[thibault|Thibault]] becomes the catalyst that sends [[thibault|Thibault]] toward the fateful encounter with the [[devil|black wolf]], making him an unwitting but crucial trigger of the novel's supernatural plot.

## Role in the Story

Landry's narrative function is to set the main plot in motion. When [[thibault|Thibault]] meets him in the forest, Landry is sunk in despair — "generally as bright" [007-011] but now "down in the dumps" [007-008]. Pressed by [[thibault|Thibault]], Landry confesses that he is in love with the widow Polet's daughter but lacks the means and courage to pursue her advantageously.

His tale of romantic frustration resonates with [[thibault|Thibault]]'s own resentments. Landry explains that a rival, [[mocquet|the gamekeeper]]'s son, is courting the same woman with better prospects. "In the beginning, I was foolish enough to hope," Landry says [007-043], but now he sees no path forward. [[thibault|Thibault]], hearing this, feels a mixture of [[mercy|pity]] and superiority — the narrator notes that [[thibault|Thibault]] "understood women better than his cousin Landry" [007-048].

After listening, [[thibault|Thibault]] sends Landry home and stays alone in the forest, brooding on the in[[law|justice]] that a peasant's love is always thwarted by social [[class|station]]. It is in this state of bitter reflection that [[thibault|Thibault]] first encounters the [[devil|black wolf]] and makes his fateful pact.

## Key Relationships

- **[[thibault|Thibault]]** — Landry's cousin and confidant. Their relationship shows the class solidarity of the [[peasant|peasantry]] but also [[thibault|Thibault]]'s growing sense of superiority — he pities Landry while belie[[wine|vin]]g himself more clever and deser[[wine|vin]]g.

- **The Widow Polet** — The mother of the woman Landry loves. Landry fears her disapproval and has "not even dared speak" [007-061] of his intentions openly.

- **Madame Polet's Daughter** — The unnamed object of Landry's [[love|affection]]. She receives "long glances and smiles" from Landry [007-075] during meals at her mother's house.

## Character Analysis

Landry represents the ordinary peasant whose modest [[wish|desire]]s are thwarted by the rigid [[class|social hierarchy]] of pre-Revolutionary France. Unlike [[thibault|Thibault]], who will resort to supernatural means to transcend his [[class|station]], Landry simply despairs — his reaction is passive suffering, the typical response of those conditioned to accept their place.

His confession scene serves as the novel's thesis statement on social in[[law|justice]]. Landry's suffering is not extraordinary; it is the normal condition of [[peasant|the peasant class]]. [[thibault|Thibault]]'s subsequent pact with the wolf represents a fantasy of breaking that condition, but the novel's tragic arc suggests that such escape comes at a terrible price.
