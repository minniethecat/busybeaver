## Physical Grace

Grace first enters the novel through descriptions of bodily movement and form.
Agnelette's youth is rendered in terms of grace that combines physical beauty with innocence:

"All the flexibility and grace of her youthful figure were brought into relief as the girl lifted her pretty rounded arms" (003-109).

Thibault watches her walk away across the moor, her silhouette against the dark sky — a moment of pure aesthetic appreciation that stands in contrast to his later predatory impulses.

Later, Thibault himself is described through the lens of grace as he helps unload the mill cart:
"He put forth all his strength, and displayed to the full his athletic grace" (007-072).
His physical prowess becomes a tool of seduction, grace twisted toward self-interest.

## Social Grace

The widow Polet embodies social grace — the art of reception, the subtle management of attention and favour.
At her dinner table, "she was all attention and politeness" to Thibault (007-072),
managing the jealousy between Thibault and Landry with the skill of a practiced hostess.

Yet social grace is also a mask. The widow's graceful attentions conceal calculation;
her silent signals to Landry ("a whole world of loving promises in this little pantomime" (007-072))
show grace operating as a language of power.

## Grace as Mercy

The word "grace" carries its theological weight in the novel's opening crisis.
When Thibault is to be flogged, the plea rings out:
"Grace for that scoundrel in return for a kiss" (002-051).
Grace here is unearned pardon, the suspension of just punishment — a concept Dumas plays against the devil-pact that grants wishes without mercy.

The structural opposition of grace and curse structures the novel's moral world.
The devil's gifts carry no grace; they are contracts, bargains with exacted cost.
Agnelette alone embodies uncalculated, grace-like love — the love Thibault throws away.
