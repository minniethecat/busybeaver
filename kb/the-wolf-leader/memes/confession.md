## Definition

Confession in *The Wolf-Leader* is the act of admitting wrongdoing or revealing hidden truth, operating in two registers: the sacramental confession of Catholic practice, and the secular confession of character and motive. The novel deploys confession as a narrative mechanism that forces characters — particularly Thibault — to speak aloud what they would prefer to conceal, creating moments of dramatic reckoning when silent guilt becomes voiced acknowledgement.

## Role in the Novel

Confession appears both as social pressure and as religious ritual. Early in the novel, Agnelette urges Thibault to "confess that you deserved those blows" [003-061] — an informal confession that performs social reconciliation. The demand treats confession not as sacramental obligation but as interpersonal settlement: admit fault and restore peace.

The concept deepens as the novel progresses toward its climax. When the wounded Thibault-Raoul lies dying in the priest's house, the priest attempts to administer sacramental absolution — the formal confession that prepares a soul for death. But Thibault violently refuses: "I want no absolution! I am damned! damned! damned!" [018-020]. This refusal of confession is the novel's most profound statement about the limits of forgiveness — Thibault judges himself beyond even the Church's power to absolve.

The aftermath of confession-denied shapes the novel's final chapters. Thibault carries the unconfessed weight of his sins, and in a late moment of self-doubt, "he began to doubt whether he might not find himself deceived" [024-004] — the unconfessed life breeds a corrosive uncertainty about all moral truth.

## Key Examples

> "Come now, frankly, friend, confess that you deserved those blows." [003-061]

Agnelette's gentle demand for confession reframes Thibault's suffering as deserved, turning confession from punishment into a path toward restored relationship.

> "I want no absolution! I am damned! damned! damned!" [018-020]

Thibault's threefold refusal of confession before death is the novel's theological climax — a man who has traded his soul for wishes now refuses the only mechanism the Church offers for its recovery.

> "He began to doubt whether he might not find himself deceived" [024-004]

The consequence of unconfessed sin is a permanent erosion of certainty — Thibault can no longer trust his own judgments because he has never submitted them to confession's clarifying act of speech.
