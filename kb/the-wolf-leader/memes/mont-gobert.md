## Overview

Mont-Gobert is the country [[class|estate]] of the Comte and Comtesse de Mont-Gobert, situated in the same region as [[vez|Vez]] and [[villers-cotterets|Villers-Cotterêts]] [010-010]. It serves as the primary setting for the love-triangle subplot of Chapters 15–20, where [[countess|the Countess]] entertains her lover [[raoul-de-vauparfond|Raoul de Vauparfond]] under the nose of her husband. The [[class|estate]] becomes a stage for aristocratic intrigue, jealousy, and violence.

## Location and Context

Mont-Gobert is listed among the neighboring [[class|estate]]s to [[villers-cotterets|Villers-Cotterêts]], alongside "Crespy" (Crépy) [010-010]. It forms part of the dense network of noble houses in the Aisne-Oise region that Dumas populates with his fictional characters. The geographic proximity of Mont-Gobert to [[vauparfond|Vauparfond]] and [[vez|Vez]] creates a tight social web where the affairs and rivalries of the local nobility are inescapably entangled.

## The Estate as a Social Stage

The château at Mont-Gobert is where [[countess|the Countess]] sends her secret letters to Raoul [016-011, 016-023]. It is the site of their clandestine meetings—and the place where [[thibault|Thibault]], now inhabiting Raoul's body, enters [[countess|the Countess]]'s chambers [017-013]. The [[class|estate]]'s rooms and corridors become the theater for the novel's exploration of identity, deception, and the permeable boundary between self and other.

When the Count discovers his wife's [[fidelity|infidelity]], Mont-Gobert transforms from a place of romance into a place of violence. The Count hamstrings Raoul's horse on the [[class|estate]] grounds [017-064], and the confrontation that follows is remembered in the physical fabric of the château.

## Social Significance

Like [[vez|Vez]], Mont-Gobert represents the world of the pro[[wine|vin]]cial aristocracy that Dumas's narrative both depicts and implicitly critiques. The [[class|estate]]'s name is invoked by characters as a marker of social standing—"They say the same thing about me at Mont-Gobert as they do at [[vauparfond|Vauparfond]]" [015-049]—illustrating how place-names function as metonymic identifiers of noble identity and reputation.
