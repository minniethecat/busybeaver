# Nightmare

## Definition

In *The Wolf-Leader*, the nightmare (*cauchemar* in French folklore) is a supernatural visitation — a malevolent entity or witch that torments the sleeper by sitting upon their chest. It is both a folk belief of rural 18th-century France and the narrative device that triggers the entire novel: the story of the wolf-leader is told to cure Mocquet of his nightmare.

The nightmare in the novel is not a psychological dream but an external, personified supernatural agent. Mocquet believes he is "nightmared" by Mother Durand, an old woman whom he suspects of witchcraft. The French verb *cauchemarder* gives Dumas the linguistic material for the coinage "nightmared" — a double verb, "both active and passive," as the narrator notes.

## Role in the Novel

### The Frame Narrative Device

The nightmare is the reason the wolf-leader story exists at all. Mocquet, the gamekeeper, arrives at the narrator's father's house at an early hour with the utmost gravity and announces: "The matter is that I am nightmared" (P02-029). When the father learns that Mocquet has been nightmared for a whole week (P02-035), he asks for the story behind it — and Mocquet begins to recount the tale of Thibault the sabot-maker and his pact with the black wolf.

The father's motivation is practical: he fears Mocquet will take violent revenge on Mother Durand, the woman he believes is bewitching him. The father pretends to take the superstition seriously to gain Mocquet's confidence, creating the narrative situation in which the tale unfolds over successive nights.

### Nightmare as Social Danger

The narrator's father — General Dumas — is not ignorant of "the superstitions of the people" and knows that "belief in spells was still widespread among the peasantry in the country districts." He has "heard of terrible acts of revenge carried out by the victims on some man or woman who they thought had bewitched them" (P02-051). Mocquet's menacing grip on his gun persuades the father to intervene through storytelling rather than reason.

There is a darker edge to the folk belief: "no one can cure you of your nightmare" (P02-053) — a statement that frames the tale itself as the only possible remedy. The folk cure for nightmare is not medicine or exorcism but narrative: the telling of a story that holds the listener's attention until the nightmare's power is broken.

### Nightmare and the Wolf-Leader Legend

The nightmare functions as the transmission mechanism for the wolf-leader legend. Mocquet believes Mother Durand has been nightmaring him as revenge for his witnessing her diabolical dance "at midnight on the heath of Gondreville" (P02-045). The nightmare is thus linked to the same nocturnal, supernatural world that produces werewolves and devil-pacts — the world of midnight encounters and infernal bargains that Thibault later enters.

The nightmare frame also echoes thematically with Thibault's own story (PN=019-001): when the wolf-leader believes the events at the castle "was a continuation of his nightmare," the word collapses the distance between the frame narrative and the embedded tale.

## Key Examples

- **P02-029**: Mocquet announces to the narrator's father, "the matter is that I am nightmared" — the declaration that initiates the entire novel.
- **P02-034**: "And how long have you been nightmared?" — the father's compassionate inquiry that draws out the story.
- **P02-044–045**: "And why should she come and nightmare you?" — Mocquet explains it is Mother Durand's revenge for his witnessing her midnight Witches' Sabbath.
- **P02-051**: The father's internal reasoning — he does not believe in the supernatural but knows the danger of peasant superstition and the real violence it can provoke.
- **P02-053**: "No one can cure you of your nightmare" — the father's strategic claim that sets up the storytelling cure.
- **P02-135–144**: Years pass; the narrator, now over fifteen, remembers Mocquet's nightmare and the tale that cured it.
- **PN=019-001**: The wolf-leader's first waking thought — that the events are a "continuation of his nightmare" — echoing the frame.
