## Definition

In *The Wolf-Leader*, family is the fundamental social unit that anchors characters in a network of obligation, identity, and belonging. The novel depicts family across class lines — from the narrator's own household at Les Fossés to the peasant families of the forest villages to the aristocratic lineages of the Baron and Count. Family names carry weight: when Thibault sees "Thibault and Satan, the family names of the two contracting parties" (005-030) inscribed on the devil's ring, the juxtaposition of a human family name with a diabolical one marks the pact as a perversion of kinship itself.

## Role in the Novel

The novel's framing narrative establishes family as the lens through which story is transmitted. The narrator recounts his own family — father, mother, the household at Les Fossés — as the context in which Mocquet's tale of Thibault was received. Family is thus both the subject of storytelling and its condition of possibility: tales pass from generation to generation through kinship lines.

Within the main narrative, family defines characters' material circumstances and social horizons. Agnelette supports her mother with goat-keeping — "my mother and I have three goats to feed" (003-040) — her family obligation precluding the leisure of courtship. The wooden drinking-cup that Engoulevent hands Thibault is "a family possession" (005-059), its value derived not from material worth but from lineage. These details construct a world where family is the primary unit of economic survival and emotional identity.

Thibault's own family background — his father's social transgression, his father's death leaving "very little ready money" (001-042) — establishes the inherited burden that shapes his ambition. His father "had committed a fault, a serious one at all times, but more especially in those days of absolutism, when a man was not able to rise above his station" (001-038). The sins of the father constrain the son; Thibault's pact with the wolf is, in part, an attempt to escape family inheritance through supernatural means.

The novel's resolution reaffirms family's primacy: the household, the marriage bond, and the obligations of kinship survive the supernatural disruption. What the devil's pact threatens is not merely individual souls but the fabric of family relations that holds the social world together.

## Key Examples

> "His father had committed a fault, a serious one at all times, but more especially in those days of absolutism." (001-038)

Thibault's family inheritance is a social wound — the father's fault becomes the son's limitation, establishing the intergenerational dimension of the novel's class critique.

> "I am obliged to do it, my Lord, for my mother and I have three goats to feed." (003-040)

Agnelette's family obligation is stated as simple fact — family is the non-negotiable framework within which all other choices are made.

> "Thibault and Satan, the family names of the two contracting parties." (005-030)

The devil's contract is presented in the language of family — a marriage certificate of damnation — perverting the novel's most sacred social institution.

> "a family possession, of which Thibault was very proud" (005-059)

The wooden drinking-cup embodies family as material continuity — objects that carry lineage forward across generations.
