# Envy

**Envy** — the pain of seeing another possess what one lacks — is the seed emotion from which Thibault's entire tragic trajectory grows. The novel identifies envy as a "little grain" planted in his character before the plot begins, a pre-existing moral vulnerability that the wolf's temptations exploit and amplify rather than create.

## The Seed of Envy

The novel's psychological diagnosis of Thibault opens with envy. "This depression of spirits arose from a little grain of envy, which, in spite of himself, perhaps unconsciously to himself, he harboured towards all such of his neighbours as had been more favoured by fortune than himself" [001-037]. The language is precise: envy is a _grain_ — small, granular, easily overlooked — and it operates _unconsciously_, beneath Thibault's own self-awareness.

This formulation makes envy structurally prior to ambition. Thibault does not first desire social advancement and then envy those who have it; he first envies, and the envy generates the ambition. The emotional wound precedes the aspirational project. This ordering is theologically significant — envy is one of the seven deadly sins, and the novel positions it as the root sin from which Thibault's other transgressions branch.

## Envy and the Aristocratic Gaze

Thibault's envy is trained specifically on the aristocracy he has observed at court. "He recalled with envy the young lords whom he had seen at the court of Madame de Maintenon, for, with their powdered wigs to cover it, the colour of their hair, whatever it might be, could be no bar to their success" [007-003]. The detail of the powdered wigs is revealing: what Thibault envies is not merely wealth or power but the _concealment_ that aristocratic costume provides — the ability to hide one's natural defects behind artificial adornment.

His red hair becomes in his own mind the visible sign of his exclusion from this world of concealment and performance. Envy in this sense is not just about having less but about being seen as less — the social visibility of inferiority.

## Envy Inverted

The novel's treatment of envy contains a symmetrical reversal. If Thibault begins by envying the aristocracy, his pact eventually allows him to become the object of envy himself. "If this dear and charming little woman were to become his, he would be envied in turn by all the earls and barons in the countryside" [003-094]. The dream of being envied — of reversing the social direction of the envious gaze — is part of the fantasy that sustains his pursuit of Agnelette.

Later, inhabiting Raoul de Vauparfond's body, he "wished the other drinkers to envy him his three different wines, drunk out of three different shaped glasses" [015-001]. The satisfaction of being envied is depicted as petty and performative — the three glasses are chosen for display value, not taste. Having achieved what he envied, Thibault immediately turns to cultivating envy in others, replicating the social dynamic that once wounded him.

## The Universal Envy

The novel briefly generalizes envy beyond Thibault's personal psychology into a cosmic principle. "Man envies the tree, the butterfly envies man" [014-006] — an aphoristic formulation that makes envy a law of nature rather than a personal failing. Every being looks upward in the hierarchy and envies what is above it.

This momentary universalization is striking because it simultaneously excuses and condemns Thibault. If envy is universal, his is not uniquely blameworthy. But if envy is universal, it is also inescapable — and the novel's tragic arc suggests that to be governed by envy is to be trapped in a chain of dissatisfaction from which even the devil's wishes offer no release.

## Distinction from Jealousy

The novel carefully distinguishes envy from jealousy, though the two emotions are often conflated. Jealousy in _The Wolf-Leader_ is romantic possessiveness — Thibault's jealousy of the Baron's relationship with Agnelette, the fear of losing a beloved to a rival. Envy, by contrast, is the simpler and more fundamental pain of lacking what another has — status, wealth, hair color, social invisibility. Jealousy requires a triangular relationship (self, beloved, rival); envy requires only two (self, envied other). The novel positions envy as the deeper and more dangerous of the two because it can be directed at anyone and anything, while jealousy is at least bounded by its specific object.
