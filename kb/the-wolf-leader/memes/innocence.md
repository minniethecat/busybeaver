## Agnelette's Innocence

Agnelette is the novel's embodiment of innocence.
From her first appearance — the young woman carrying a burden on her head across the moor,
silhouetted against the sky (003-109) — she is described through the language of purity and simplicity.

Her innocence is not naivety but a moral condition. She loves without calculation,
forgives without hesitation, and remains the fixed point of goodness against which
all of Thibault's moral drift is measured.

When Thibault later looks back, the narrative voice makes explicit what was lost:
"You disdained Agnelette's innocence, and went after Madame Poulet" (023-014).
Innocence here is the path not taken — the honest, laborious life Thibault could have had
if he had not been consumed by envy and ambition.

## The Countess's Claim

In Chapter 17, the Countess of Mont-Gobert defends herself against suspicion with a claim of innocence:

"How can you expect me, conscious as I am of my innocence, to accuse myself?" (017-021).

Her assertion operates differently from Agnelette's — it is innocence claimed in self-defense
rather than innocence evidenced by a life. The Countess's innocence is juridical,
a plea within a social framework of accusation and judgment.

The juxtaposition of these two modes of innocence — Agnelette's moral purity
and the Countess's asserted blamelessness — complicates the novel's moral world.
Innocence can be a state of being or a legal position; the novel explores both
without fully resolving their relationship.

## Progressive Loss and Final Echo

Thibault's arc is a study in the progressive loss of innocence.
The devil-pact does not corrupt him all at once; each wish, each self-serving choice,
each rationalisation erodes another layer.

Yet even at the novel's darkest point, innocence returns as memory and measure:
Agnelette appears before Thibault's inner vision "with the same sweetness and innocence as before" (021-047).
Her unchanged innocence — she remains what she always was — judges him by contrast.
He has fallen; she has not. Innocence survives where he cannot reach it.
