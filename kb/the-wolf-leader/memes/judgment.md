# Judgment

Judgment (*jugement*) is the act of forming an opinion or conclusion — especially a moral or legal determination of guilt, merit, or value. In *The Wolf-Leader*, judgment operates on multiple levels: the legal judgment of the [[baron-of-vez|Baron]], the social judgment of the [[community|village]], and the self-judgment that [[thibault|Thibault]] cannot escape.

## The Baron's Judgment

The novel opens with an act of judgment: the Baron sentences Thibault to a flogging for a minor transgression. This judgment is legal in form but arbitrary in substance — the Baron's authority is absolute within his domain, and there is no appeal. The flogging is the novel's primal scene of injustice, and it sets in motion Thibault's desire for [[revenge|revenge]].

The Baron's judgment is class-based: the same act, committed by a noble, would not be punished. The novel is explicit about this asymmetry — judgment in the feudal system serves power, not justice.

## The Community's Judgment

The village exercises a different kind of judgment — informal, collective, and enforced through reputation rather than law. As Thibault changes, the community judges him: his new wealth is suspect, his arrogance is noted, his absence from communal life is interpreted as contempt. This social judgment is not unjust — Thibault *is* becoming dangerous — but it accelerates his alienation. The community that judges him also excludes him.

## Self-Judgment

The novel's most devastating judgment is Thibault's own. In his final moments, hunted through the [[forest|forest]], he understands — too late — what he has done and what he has become. This self-judgment is the tragic recognition (*anagnorisis*) that classical tragedy requires: the protagonist sees his fault and acknowledges it, but understanding comes when it can no longer change anything.
