# Marcotte

## Character Overview

Marcotte is the [[baron-of-vez|Baron of Vez]]'s pricker — the huntsman responsible for managing the hounds and administering corporal punishment. Though a minor character in terms of page-time, he plays a crucial thematic role: he is the human face of institutional violence, the man whose hand delivers the [[violence|blow]]s that the system prescribes.

## Role in the Story

Marcotte appears most prominently in the flogging scene. When the Baron sentences [[thibault|Thibault]] to thirty-six lashes, it is Marcotte who strips him, ties him to a tree, and administers [[the-flogging|the punishment]] (003-003). Dumas notes that Marcotte's strokes are unusually heavy, and that he "religiously counted his strokes" — a detail that captures the terrifying combination of bureaucratic precision and personal [[violence|brutality]] (003-031).

When the Baron orders [[the-flogging|the punishment]] stopped after [[agnette|Agnelette]]'s intervention, Marcotte allows one final [[violence|blow]] to fall anyway. "It is possible that he also thought it would be as well to give it the weight of two ordinary [[violence|blow]]s, so as to make up good measure and give a thirteenth in" (003-031). This extra [[violence|blow]], administered after the order to stop, reveals the gap between [[power|authority]]'s command and the en[[violence|force]]r's pleasure in en[[violence|force]]ment.

Marcotte's role is small but symbolically weighty. He represents the class of men who make unjust systems work — the functionaries, the en[[violence|force]]rs, the ones who do not make the rules but apply them with an enthusiasm the rule-makers may lack.

## Key Relationships

- **[[Baron-of-Vez]]** — Marcotte's master. The Baron prescribes punishment; Marcotte administers it with a zeal that exceeds his instructions.
- **[[Thibault]]** — The victim of Marcotte's strokes. [[thibault|Thibault]]'s hatred is directed primarily at the Baron, but Marcotte's [[violence|blow]]s are the physical cause of his pain and [[shame|humiliation]].

## Character Analysis

Marcotte is a study in the banality of institutional violence. He is not portrayed as personally evil — he is doing his job, following orders, counting strokes. But Dumas's portrait is devastating precisely because of its ordinariness. Marcotte is the kind of man who makes tyranny possible: obedient, thorough, and untroubled by the suffering he inflicts.

The detail about the extra [[violence|blow]] — the thirteenth stroke after the order to stop — is the key to Marcotte's characterization. It is not enough for him to do his duty; he must add a little extra, a little gift to himself, a small exercise of power within the larger exercise of power. This is how systems of violence reproduce themselves: not through the grand designs of barons but through the small cruelties of prickers.
