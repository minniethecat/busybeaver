# Castle

A castle (*château*) is a fortified noble residence that serves as both military stronghold and administrative center of a seigneurial domain. In *The Wolf-Leader*, castles function as the architectural embodiment of the feudal order — visible markers of the hierarchy that [[thibault|Thibault]] both resents and covets.

## In the Novel

The narrative is structured around three castles, each marking a different relationship to power. The **castle of Vez**, seat of the [[baron-of-vez|Baron of Vez]], is the center of seigneurial authority. The hunting party departs from its court "in full hunting array" [001-009], and the Baron's men return there "after their fruitless day's hunting" [002-049]. The castle is where justice is dispensed — Thibault receives his flogging sentence within its jurisdiction.

The **castle of Oigny**, seat of the Count of Mont-Gobert, represents an alternative locus of noble power. When Thibault seeks redress, the Baron "rode at full speed to the castle of Oigny" [005-059] to request intervention. This secondary castle introduces competition between lords, complicating the feudal geography.

The **castle of Villers-Cotterets**, the [[duke-of-orleans|Duke of Orléans]]'s residence, represents the highest tier of the feudal hierarchy. The Duke "had almost abandoned his castle at Villers-Cotterets" [001-007], preferring the royal court — a detail that signals the centrifugal pull of Versailles and the declining relevance of provincial seats.

## The Baron's Castle as Social Theater

The castle of Vez is the stage upon which the novel's opening displays the feudal order in motion. The hunting procession filing out of the castle court [001-009] is a choreographed performance of rank: the Baron at its head, huntsmen in order of precedence, hounds and grooms following. This visual hierarchy is the world that Thibault, the sabot-maker, observes from outside.

When Thibault later enters the castle — first as a punished subordinate, then as a supernatural predator — the spatial transgression mirrors his social ascent. "The silence and solitude of the castle filled him with fear" [020-022] captures the uncanny quality of his intrusion: the peasant in the lord's seat, made possible only by his devil-granted powers.

## Wizard's Castle and Fairy Palace

At the height of his powers, Thibault imagines his dwelling transformed: "been carried to some wizard's castle, to some fairy's palace?" [017-004]. This invocation of the enchanted castle — a staple of medieval romance — reveals Thibault's conception of power as fundamentally architectural. To possess power is to inhabit a castle; to be a lord is to command stone walls.
