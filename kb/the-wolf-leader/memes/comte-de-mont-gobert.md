## Overview

The Comte de Mont-Gobert is a pro[[wine|vin]]cial nobleman and the husband of [[countess|the Countess]] Jane (née de [[mont-gobert|Mont-Gobert]]). He is defined primarily by his role in a violent love triangle: his wife is conducting an affair with [[raoul-de-vauparfond|Raoul de Vauparfond]], and the Count's jealous, vengeful actions drive much of the plot in Chapters 15 through 20.

## The Love Triangle

The Count's marriage to [[countess|the Countess]] is a source of public knowledge and private misery. The Countess openly maintains her liaison with [[raoul-de-vauparfond|Raoul de Vauparfond]] [015-020], and the narrative presents the Count as a cuckolded husband whose pride demands violent redress. The fear of what "would happen if the Comte de Mont-Gobert were to surprise his rival" is a constant source of tension [015-081].

## Violent Confrontation

The Count does not merely discover the affair—he acts. When he learns of Raoul's rendezvous with [[countess|the Countess]], he hamstrings Raoul's horse to prevent escape [017-064]. The mutilation is both strategic and symbolic: the Count disables his rival's means of [[escape|flight]], forcing a confrontation.

The encounter that follows is brutal. [[thibault|Thibault]], inhabiting Raoul's body, recognizes the Count [017-066]. The Count himself is described as ha[[wine|vin]]g a "mutilated leg" [018-019], suggesting that the violent exchange was not one-sided. Yet the Count survives and persists as a menacing presence—"the Comte de Mont-Gobert had really taken place" in the events that culminate in Raoul's death and [[thibault|Thibault]]'s continued inhabitation [019-004].

## Head Keeper Lestocq

The Count maintains his own [[hunt|hunting]] establishment: [[lestocq|Lestocq]] serves as his Head Keeper [020-004], paralleling the [[baron-of-vez|Baron of Vez]]'s retinue and indicating the Count's equivalent social standing among the local nobility.

## Character Assessment

The Count is one of the novel's figures of aristocratic violence—a man whose wounded pride translates directly into physical [[violence|brutality]]. Unlike the [[baron-of-vez|Baron of Vez]], whose cruelty is institutional and procedural, the Count's is personal and impassioned. He is the jealous husband of farce transformed into the vengeful rival of tragedy.
