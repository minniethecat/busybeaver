## Definition

In *The Wolf-Leader*, "voice" operates on multiple registers. It is the narrative mechanism of the frame story — "what memory tells him in her soft, low voice" (P02-010). It is the supernatural instrument of temptation, as the black wolf speaks with an "accent of menace in his voice" (P02-051) and a "full and sonorous voice" (004-022). And it is an index of Thibault's transformation: after the pact, "his voice filled him with fear, for there was a new and curious sound in it, even to his own ears" (005-031). Voice is thus simultaneously narrative device, supernatural force, and psychological marker.

## Role in the Novel

The frame narrative establishes voice as the novel's originating medium. The entire story is framed as memory speaking aloud — "he loves to repeat aloud what memory tells him in her soft, low voice" (P02-010). This framing positions all subsequent events as a vocal transmission, a tale told, a voice remembered and re-voiced.

The wolf's voice is the novel's primary supernatural instrument. When the wolf first speaks, Thibault is stunned by "hearing a voice exactly like his own coming forth from a beast's mouth" (004-022). The uncanny doubling — his own voice from the wolf — prefigures the identity dissolution to come. The wolf deploys "a sneer in his voice" (004-057), modulating tone to manipulate and mock.

Thibault's own voice becomes alien to him after the pact. Attempting to distract himself by singing, "his voice filled him with fear, for there was a new and curious sound in it" (005-031). This vocal estrangement mirrors the deeper transformation of his nature — the werewolf's howl replacing the human song.

Throughout the novel, human voices are a constant presence — the Baron's voice heard "above all the other voices... like the blast of a tempest" (002-067), Agnelette's supplicating "voice and gesture of even more earnest supplication" (003-012), the villagers' "sound of voices" (024-005) pursuing the wolf — creating a polyphonic soundscape against which Thibault's isolation is measured.

## Key Examples

- **Memory's voice (P02-010)**: The founding narrative gesture — memory speaking softly, the old man repeating aloud — establishes voice as the medium of storytelling itself.
- **The wolf's uncanny voice (004-022)**: Thibault's own voice emanating from a beast's mouth, the first and most disorienting sign of the pact's consequences.
- **The changed voice (005-031)**: Thibault's self-estrangement crystallized in the moment he no longer recognizes his own singing voice.

## Author's Treatment

Dumas uses voice as a unifying motif across the novel's structural layers. The frame narrative's "soft, low voice" of memory is feminine, intimate, and retrospective; the wolf's voice is deep, menacing, and immediate; Thibault's transformed voice is strange, alien, and prospective — it sounds like what he is becoming. Together they create a vocal architecture that mirrors the novel's moral architecture: memory judges, the wolf tempts, and Thibault loses even the sound of his own humanity.
