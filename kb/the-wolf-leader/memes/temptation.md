# Temptation

## Definition

In *The Wolf-Leader*, temptation (*la tentation*) is the mechanism through which the wolf transforms [[thibault|Thibault]]'s ordinary [[wish|desire]]s into instruments of [[hell|damnation]]. It is not a single event — one offer, one acceptance — but a graduated process in which each concession makes the next one easier, each wish broadens the horizon of the next, and each satisfaction breeds a hungrier [[wish|desire]]. The wolf does not tempt [[thibault|Thibault]] with exotic evils; it tempts him with things he already wants — the Baron's buck, [[agnette|Agnelette]]'s heart, the widow's mill — and shows him that wanting them is not enough. Wanting must become taking, and taking must become destroying.

Dumas presents temptation as a narrative of escalation. The temptations that surround [[thibault|Thibault]] grow in scope and severity as the novel progresses, from the venial (coveting a neighbor's meal) to the [[death|mort]]al (murder and [[hell|damnation]]). The process is cumulative: each temptation is both a consequence of the previous surrender and a preparation for the next.

## Role in the Novel

The novel's most concentrated description of temptation appears in Chapter 9, as [[thibault|Thibault]] walks home through the forest accompanied by his pack of wolves. The passage is a set-piece of moral psychology:

> "Surrounded by the temptations which now assailed him, it was only what was to be expected that Thibault who had already gone so far in the direction of evil, should break away from what little good was still left in him, losing even the very remembrance of having once led an honest life. What were the few paltry crowns that the Landlord of the Boule-d'Or gave him in payment for the game which his good friends the wolves procured for him? Saved up for months, even for years, they would still be insufficient to satisfy a single one of the humblest of the desires which kept tormenting his brain. It would be scarcely safe to say that Thibault, who had first wished for a haunch of the Baron's buck, then for Agnelette's heart, and then for the widow Polet's mill, would now be satisfied even with the Castle at Oigny or Longpont, to such extravagant issues had his ambition been excited" (009-019).

The passage traces the novel's moral arc in miniature. Three stages of temptation are enumerated: the haunch of buck (material [[wish|desire]]), [[agnette|Agnelette]]'s heart (romantic [[wish|desire]]), the widow's mill ([[ambition|social ambition]]). Each is larger than the last, and none has satisfied. The wolf's strategy is revealed: not to tempt with the outrageous, but to escalate from the ordinary — to take [[wish|desire]]s that any peasant might feel and inflate them until they outgrow the world that contains them. The castle at [[oigny|Oigny]] or [[longpont|Longpont]] is the symbolic terminus: a [[wish|desire]] so large that even a castle cannot contain it.

The temptations are described as surrounding [[thibault|Thibault]] — "surrounded by temptations" — suggesting that by Chapter 9 he lives in a transformed moral landscape where temptation is not occasional but environmental. He cannot walk home without being "assailed." This is the wolf's achievement: to make evil the default, the normal, the air [[thibault|Thibault]] breathes.

The phrase "tormenting his brain" is crucial. Temptation is not pleasurable; it is torment. The [[wish|desire]]s the wolf cultivates are not satisfactions but [[sorrow|affliction]]s — needs that deepen rather than diminish with each fulfillment. This is the [[devil|demon]]ic inversion of ordinary [[wish|desire]]: [[wish|desire]], in the wolf's economy, is not for the object but for the wanting itself, an engine that runs on its own exhaust.

The escalation pattern echoes the theological [[custom|tradition]] of the *gradus ad peccatum* (steps to sin): suggestion, delight, consent, habit, despair. [[thibault|Thibault]] moves through each stage: the wolf suggests (the first wish), [[thibault|Thibault]] delights (the buck arrives), he consents (he wishes again), habit forms (the wishes become routine), and despair follows (he cannot stop).

## Historical and Folkloric Background

Temptation (*tentatio*) in Catholic moral theology was understood as a three-stage process: *suggestio* (the initial proposal of evil, which could come from the devil, the world, or the flesh), *delectatio* (the pleasurable attraction to the suggestion), and *consensus* (the will's consent, which alone constituted sin). The crucial distinction was between temptation and sin: temptation itself was not sinful; consent to temptation was. This distinction gave the devil a precise role — he could suggest and delight, but he could not compel consent. The will remained free.

In French folk tales of diabolical pacts, temptation typically followed a pattern of progressive entrapment. The devil's first offers were small — a coin, a favor, a meal — and the victim's acceptance of the small gift created an obligation that led to larger demands. This folk pattern underlies Dumas's escalation structure: the haunch of buck leads to [[agnette|Agnelette]]'s heart, which leads to the widow's mill, which leads to the castle.

In werewolf folklore specifically, temptation was often linked to the seven-year cycle. The werewolf's condition could be broken by faithful love, but the temptation to use the wolf's power for personal gain was nearly irresistible. [[thibault|Thibault]]'s arc recapitulates this pattern: each wish uses the wolf's power, and each use deepens the wolf's hold.

## Author's Treatment

Dumas's treatment of temptation is distinguished by its psychological realism. The wolf does not appear in a puff of sulfur [[sacrifice|offering]] [[devil-pact|Faustian bargain]]s; it appears as a voice in the forest, a presence that feels almost natural, [[sacrifice|offering]] what [[thibault|Thibault]] already wants. This is the novel's most unsettling insight: the most effective temptations do not come from outside, as alien invasions of the soul, but from within, as amplifications of [[wish|desire]]s that were already there.

Dumas also refuses the easy resolution of the temptation narrative. In most folk tales, the tempted protagonist eventually recognizes the devil and resists. [[thibault|Thibault]] never does — or rather, he recognizes what is happening too late, when his will has been so eroded by habit that recognition and resistance are no longer the same thing. The novel's tragedy is not that [[thibault|Thibault]] is tempted but that he discovers, too late, that he has lost the capacity to refuse.

The escalation structure — from buck to heart to mill to castle — is Dumas's own contribution to the literature of temptation. It transforms temptation from a single dramatic moment into a narrative principle, the engine that drives the plot forward. Temptation is not an interruption of the story; it is the story.
