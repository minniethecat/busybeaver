# Folklore

## Definition

In *The Wolf-Leader*, folklore (*les croyances populaires*) provides the conceptual framework through which characters — particularly peasants — understand the supernatural events that unfold. Werewolf legends, witch beliefs, and folk remedies are not presented as quaint background but as living systems of knowledge that compete with, and sometimes prove more accurate than, official religion and rational skepticism. Dumas, writing in the tradition of the Romantic rediscovery of folk culture, treats these beliefs with ethnographic seriousness even as he exploits them for narrative effect.

The folkloric elements cluster around three poles: the werewolf (*loup-garou*) tradition, which provides the novel's central supernatural mechanism; witch lore, embodied in figures like [[mother-durand|Mother Durand]]; and the folk Catholic practices that blend Christian ritual with pre-Christian magic. These three strands intertwine throughout the novel, creating a world where the supernatural is not an intrusion from outside but a latent possibility always present in the rural landscape.

## Role in the Novel

Folklore enters the novel most explicitly through the preface, where the narrator recounts stories told by the gamekeeper Mocquet. The tale of [[mother-durand|Mother Durand]], the "old witch" of Haramont who nightmares people in their sleep, establishes the folkloric register: "By Mother Durand, of Haramont, who, as you will have heard, is an old witch" (P02-039). Mocquet's belief in her powers is presented not as simple-mindedness but as a coherent worldview with its own logic.

The werewolf tradition that structures the novel draws on deep folkloric roots. The *loup-garou* of French folklore was not merely a man who turned into a wolf but a specific type of cursed individual, often associated with witchcraft and devil-pacts. Dumas's innovation is to graft this folk tradition onto a psychological and social narrative: [[thibault|Thibault]] becomes a werewolf not through a witch's curse but through his own [[ambition|ambition]] and [[revenge|vengeance]], channeled through the [[devil-pact|devil's pact]].

The novel also registers the tension between folk belief and elite skepticism. The [[baron-of-vez|Baron of Vez]] and his aristocratic circle dismiss werewolf talk as peasant superstition — and they are proven catastrophically wrong. Dumas's sympathy, as so often, lies with the folk: their beliefs may be crude, but they recognize truths that Enlightenment rationalism cannot see.

## Key Examples

Mocquet's folk diagnosis of the nightmare:

> "By Mother Durand, of Haramont, who, as you will have heard, is an old witch" (P02-039).

The villagers' immediate recourse to folk categories when confronted with the uncanny:

> "He is a wizard, a sorcerer! a werewolf!" (008-047).

The trap for the witch — folk practice as practical response:

> "Do you mean to tell me you have set a trap to catch Mother Durand?" (P02-083).
