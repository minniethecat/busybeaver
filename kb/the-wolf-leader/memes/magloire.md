## Character Overview

Népomucène Magloire is the Bailiff (magistrate) of [[vez|Vez]], a man of comfortable pro[[wine|vin]]cial wealth who married his fourth wife, the young [[madame-suzanne|Madame Suzanne]], late in life. He is the son of Chrysostom Magloire, a church ornament maker who "placed his son Népomucène" [011-038] in the legal profession. By fifty-five, Magloire had "found himself so rounded in belly and in purse" [011-040] that he retired from active legal practice to enjoy his fortune.

Magloire is a figure of the pro[[wine|vin]]cial bourgeoisie under the Ancien Régime — wealthy enough to entertain the local nobility, but ultimately subordinate to their power. His dinner party in Chapters 10–13 becomes the stage for [[thibault|Thibault]]'s social [[shame|humiliation]] and the [[baron-of-vez|Baron of Vez]]'s [[temptation|seduction]] of Madame Magloire, with Magloire himself largely oblivious to the intrigue unfolding in his own house.

## Role in the Story

Magloire's history is told in flashback during the dinner sequence. His father Chrysostom accumulated sa[[wine|vin]]gs of "eleven to twelve thousand" livres [011-043], gi[[wine|vin]]g Népomucène "a thousand livres a year" income [011-043]. He married four times, each marriage consolidating his social and financial position.

The dinner at Magloire's house is the novel's pivotal domestic set-piece. As host, Magloire is expansive and hospitable, "emptied" of his wine and displaying "habitual good nature" [011-050]. He discusses his wife with "no sign of jealousy" [011-045], proud rather than suspicious — an ironic blindness given what transpires in his own house that night.

When [[thibault|Thibault]]'s clandestine presence in [[madame-suzanne|Madame Suzanne]]'s room is revealed, Magloire's [[power|authority]] as both husband and magistrate is invoked. The text notes that "Monsieur Magloire sat as magistrate" [010-024], and he demands an account of the night's events. Yet his power proves limited: he cannot punish the Baron, and his investigation devolves into a domestic confrontation with his wife.

## Key Relationships

- **[[madame-suzanne|Madame Suzanne Magloire]]** — His fourth wife, significantly younger. Their marriage is a late-life arrangement: he provides wealth and status, while she provides the prestige of a young, attractive wife. His pride in her ("with no sign of jealousy" [011-045]) is laced with self-deception.

- **Chrysostom Magloire** — His father, a church ornament maker whose frugality built the family fortune. Magloire inherited both his father's money and his [[ambition|social ambition]].

- **The [[baron-of-vez|Baron of Vez]]** — The local nobleman who is Magloire's social superior. Magloire hosts him as an honored guest, unaware that the Baron uses the occasion to pursue Madame Magloire.

- **[[thibault|Thibault]]** — An invited guest at the dinner who becomes Magloire's rival for [[madame-suzanne|Madame Suzanne]]'s attention. [[thibault|Thibault]]'s hidden presence in the house represents a violation of Magloire's domestic order.

## Character Analysis

Magloire represents the pro[[wine|vin]]cial bourgeoisie at a particular historical moment: wealthy enough to live comfortably but lacking the inherited [[power|authority]] of the aristocracy. His father's profession as a "church ornament maker" [011-037] marks him as new money, and his legal position as bailiff gives him local [[power|authority]] that is nevertheless subservient to noble privilege.

The dinner party functions as a microcosm of Ancien Régime social relations. Magloire, the host, believes he controls the space. In reality, the Baron navigates it with noble impunity, and even the peasant [[thibault|Thibault]] (empowered by his pact) disrupts it. Magloire's [[power|authority]] is theatrical — he performs the role of magistrate and paterfamilias — but the events of the night expose its limits.
