## Character Overview

Madame Suzanne Magloire is the young wife of [[magloire|Népomucène Magloire]], the Bailiff of [[vez|Vez]]. She is a woman of the pro[[wine|vin]]cial bourgeoisie, married to a man more than twice her age, and becomes the object of romantic fascination for multiple male characters — first [[thibault|Thibault]], then the [[baron-of-vez|Baron of Vez]]. The narrator describes her as "in every possible respect" a desirable woman [012-003], and her presence forms the social center of several key domestic scenes.

She is the daughter-in-law of [[village-church|the church]] ornament maker Chrysostom Magloire, and the fourth wife of Népomucène, who married her late in life after accumulating his fortune [011-044]. Despite her marriage to a man of fifty-five, she retains a coquettish liveliness that attracts masculine attention and drives much of the novel's domestic intrigue.

## Role in the Story

Madame Suzanne first appears in the extended dinner-party sequence (Chapters 10–13) at the Bailiff's house, where [[thibault|Thibault]] is an invited guest. [[thibault|Thibault]], already nursing ambitions awakened by his pact with the [[devil|black wolf]], becomes enchanted with her — the narrator notes that [[thibault|Thibault]] had "ended by belie[[wine|vin]]g that he was madly in love with Madame Magloire" [012-058].

The dinner becomes the vehicle for [[thibault|Thibault]]'s social [[shame|humiliation]] and the [[baron-of-vez|Baron of Vez]]'s triumph. While [[thibault|Thibault]] hides in Madame Suzanne's room — ha[[wine|vin]]g been placed there by the Baron's scheming — the Baron himself enters. Madame Suzanne, belie[[wine|vin]]g her nocturnal visitor to be the Baron, "held out her hand to the intruder" [012-067] and her voice is heard saying "Oh! take care, my lord" [012-068]. When the Baron eventually departs and [[thibault|Thibault]] confronts her, Madame Suzanne immediately shifts her allegiance, laughing and telling [[thibault|Thibault]] "That is so, my lord" [012-089] when he claims superiority over the Baron.

After the dinner, Madame Suzanne attempts mediation. When Magloire demands an account of the night, she defends herself vigorously. Ultimately she "had thrown herself at his feet" [013-119] pleading, showing the precarious position of a wife caught between her husband's [[power|authority]] and the designs of powerful noblemen.

## Key Relationships

- **[[magloire|Népomucène Magloire]] (Bailiff)** — Her husband, more than twice her age. Their marriage is transactional: he provides security and status, while she has given him the social capital of a young, attractive wife [011-044]. She manages her husband with a mixture of deference and manipulation.

- **[[thibault|Thibault]]** — [[thibault|Thibault]] becomes infatuated with her, "madly in love" [012-058], but his feelings are a mix of genuine attraction and status-[[jealousy|envy]]. During the dinner, he hides in her room and witnesses her intimacy with the Baron, a turning point in his bitterness toward the aristocracy.

- **The [[baron-of-vez|Baron of Vez]]** — The Baron pursues Madame Suzanne during the dinner at her husband's house, making advances that she appears to welcome. His treatment of her — using her as a conquest while her husband is in the same house — exemplifies the corrupt power dynamics of the Ancien Régime.

## Character Analysis

Madame Suzanne embodies the constrained position of a bourgeois woman in pre-Revolutionary rural France. Married to a man of her father's generation for financial security, she navigates the competing attentions of men with a survival instinct that mixes coquetry with pragmatism. When caught between [[thibault|Thibault]] and the Baron, she adapts instantly — "laughing" [012-089] at [[thibault|Thibault]]'s claims, then later "thrown herself at his feet" [013-119] when her position is threatened.

The narrator treats her with a degree of satirical distance. She is described as ha[[wine|vin]]g "swallowed a whole glass of some cordial" [012-041] and making faces "at this low proverb" [012-046], revealing a woman conscious of social performance. Yet the text also acknowledges her genuine vulnerability: a young wife in a house where powerful men scheme and her husband's [[power|authority]] is barely nominal.
