## Definition

Peace in *The Wolf-Leader* operates on multiple registers: the physical peace of undisturbed sleep, the social peace of a harmonious community, the spiritual peace of a clear conscience, and the existential peace of a life free from supernatural predation. The novel traces Thibault's progressive loss of peace across all these dimensions, from the initial disruption caused by the flogging to the final impossibility of peace after the devil's pact. Peace functions as the baseline condition — the state of nature before the fall — against which all subsequent suffering is measured.

## Role in the Novel

Peace serves as the novel's primary thematic counterpoint to revenge, violence, and supernatural horror. It is what Thibault possesses at the outset (however tenuously), what he progressively loses through each wish, and what he can never fully regain. The frame narrative's tone of elegiac retrospection — the older narrator recalling the story — is itself a meditation on lost peace.

The novel presents peace as fundamentally incompatible with the devil's pact. Each wish granted by the black wolf extracts peace as its price: peace of mind, peace of body, peace of community. By the novel's end, only death and repentance offer the possibility of peace restored, though even this is framed as a posthumous hope rather than a lived reality.

Dumas uses peace as a structural counterweight: moments of apparent calm (the pastoral beauty of the forest, Agnelette's simple faith) are repeatedly shattered by violence, creating the novel's characteristic Gothic rhythm of tension and release.

## Key Examples

- **The grandfather's wish for peace** (P02-137): "Your grand will be able to sleep in peace, which I should certainly not do if I thought you were going to be a poacher." Peace as the reward for a moral life, contrasted with the restless fate of the transgressor. The frame narrator's grandfather equates peace with righteous conduct.

- **Peace suddenly swallowed up** (008-032): "All his anticipations of future happiness and peace thus suddenly swallowed up!" Thibault's realization that his second wish has destroyed the domestic peace he hoped to secure. The abrupt loss of peace is the signature of the wolf's ironic wish-granting.

- **Let him go in peace** (013-126): "I beg you, my Lord," said the Bailiff, "to let him go in peace; and deign to remember, that, as my witness, he is under the protection of the law." Peace as a legal and social condition — the protected status that law supposedly guarantees but which the novel repeatedly shows to be fragile.

- **A peaceful day interrupted** (020-009): Thibault "had determined to spend his day there in peace. Towards midday, he heard the sound of a horse's hoofs." The intrusion of the social world (embodied by the mounted rider) into Thibault's attempted retreat into natural peace.

- **The wolf-leader's inner turmoil** (021-017): "The wolf-leader was by no means at peace in himself." The explicit statement of Thibault's lost inner peace, the inevitable consequence of accumulated sins and wishes.

- **Comparison of past and present** (023-003): "The comparison between that peaceful past and this terrible present." Peace is defined negatively — by its absence — throughout the novel's climactic chapters. The peaceful past becomes a distant, irretrievable memory.
