# Enemy

## Definition

The enemy (*ennemi*) is a relational category that defines self against other, human against monster. In *The Wolf-Leader*, the concept of enmity structures the social world: the wolf is the "public enemy, a murderer beyond the pale" (prologue), wolves are humanity's "mortal and implacable enemy" (001-005), and [[thibault|Thibault]]'s transformation culminates in his own recognition that he has become "the enemy of all created things" (014-003). Enemy is not merely an adversary but a designation that marks the boundary of the moral community.

## Role in the Novel

The novel opens with the wolf framed as the archetypal enemy: "a public enemy, a murderer beyond the pale of humanity" (P02-149). This framing establishes the hunt as a collective moral enterprise. [[thibault|Thibault]]'s specific enemy — the [[baron-of-vez|Lord of Vez]] — is described as "his chief enemy" (021-006) and "his mortal enemy" (022-014). But as [[thibault|Thibault]] transforms, his enmity expands: he recognizes "I am not then the enemy of all created things" (014-003) as a hope he can no longer sustain. The wolves are recognized as "our mortal and implacable enemy" (001-005) by the hunters, yet [[thibault|Thibault]] becomes exactly that — the thing he once hunted. He drinks "to the enemy of mankind" (015-069), sealing his identification with what he once opposed.

## Key Examples

> "The wolf, being a public enemy, a murderer beyond the pale of humanity" (P02-149) — the prologue frames wolf-as-enemy as social fact.

> "Their mortal and implacable enemy" (001-005) — the collective designation of the wolf by the hunting community.

> "Flung it at his enemy's head" (011-074) — combat with a tangible foe.

> "I am not then the enemy of all created things" (014-003) — [[thibault|Thibault]]'s desperate hope, already false.

> "Thibault had drunk to the enemy of mankind" (015-069) — the toast that confirms his allegiance.

> "There was lodged his chief enemy" (021-006) — the [[baron-of-vez|Baron of Vez]] as primary antagonist.

## Historical and Folklore Background

The concept of the "public enemy" (*hostis publicus*) has Roman legal origins: the *hostis* was originally a foreigner, then an enemy of the state, and the declaration of *hostis publicus* stripped a citizen of legal protection, making them an outlaw in the most literal sense — outside the law, killable by anyone. The wolf in European folklore occupies precisely this legal position: the *lupus* is the archetypal outlaw, the creature that can be killed without ceremony or consequence.

The medieval *chasse aux loups* (wolf hunt) was not merely pest control but a communal ritual of boundary enforcement. To hunt the wolf was to reassert the boundary between the human community and the wild, between those who belong within the pale and those who are beyond it. Dumas's novel begins precisely with this ritual — the prologue's great wolf hunt — and [[thibault|Thibault]]'s tragedy is that he crosses the boundary the hunt is meant to enforce. The hunter becomes the hunted; the man becomes the "public enemy" he once pursued.

## Author's Treatment

Dumas's central insight is that enmity is not a fixed property but a sliding designation. The categories of "enemy" and "neighbor" are permeable: [[thibault|Thibault]] slides from one to the other, and the novel measures his descent by the expanding circle of his enmity — from the [[baron-of-vez|Baron]] (personal enemy) to all humans (species enemy) to "all created things" (cosmic enemy).

The structural irony is that [[thibault|Thibault]]'s original grievance is just: the [[baron-of-vez|Baron]] wronged him. But the devil's pact turns justified enmity into generalized enmity; the man who had one legitimate enemy becomes the enemy of everything. Dumas suggests that the logic of revenge, pursued without limit, transforms the avenger into the thing he hunts. The enemy designation, once applied, tends to expand — and the final person it devours is the one who first applied it.
