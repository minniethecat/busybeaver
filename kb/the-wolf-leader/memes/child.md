## Definition

In *The Wolf-Leader*, the child appears as both a literal presence — Agnelette, the young characters of the village — and a symbolic category representing innocence, vulnerability, and the fragility of human life against supernatural forces. The novel draws on the sentimental tradition's idealization of childhood while also grounding its child characters in the harsh realities of rural poverty. "Child" serves as a measure of moral worth: those who protect children demonstrate virtue; those who threaten them reveal their corruption.

## Role in the Novel

Agnelette is introduced emphatically as a child: "hardly sixteen years of age" (003-010), described as "a lovely child" with the innocence of youth. This framing is crucial — her youth makes Thibault's pursuit both romantic and ethically charged, and it makes her ultimate fate all the more tragic. The Baron addresses her with paternal condescension: "that's an unlucky name, my child" (003-035), reinforcing the power imbalance between nobility and peasantry through the language of childhood.

Mocquet's childhood narratives establish the oral tradition that shapes the novel's supernatural framework. He received his knowledge as a child: "had given it me when I was a child" (P02-147). The transmission of wolf-lore from generation to generation roots the supernatural in childhood experience. The narrator also frames Thibault's agency through the image of childhood: "as you are behind this child, he would never even have had the courage" (P02-203), using "child" as the baseline of vulnerability against which courage is measured.

The most pointed use of "child" occurs in Agnelette's final moments, when Thibault asks her to "come and pray for me, my pretty child" (006-010), his voice "as unco[uth]." The endearment "my child" becomes a marker of their irreparable distance — she is no longer a child by the novel's end, and he is no longer a man.

### The Baron's "My Child"

The Baron's language toward Agnelette systematically uses "child" as a mask for predation. "You take a great interest in that rascal over there, my pretty child" (003-014) — the possessive "my" claims ownership; "pretty" reduces her to an aesthetic object; "child" asserts the paternal authority that licenses his attention. When he asks "And you come alone like this into the forest, my child? that's brave for a lambkin" (003-039), the address is structurally a compliment (brave) wrapped in a diminutive (lambkin) that reasserts vulnerability. The Baron's "my child" is never protective; it is always appropriative.

The pattern extends across the novel's class hierarchy. Thibault, too, eventually adopts the Baron's language. His "Well then, Agnelette, we will speak again about this, dear child" (003-099) borrows the paternal register without the Baron's power to make it stick. When Thibault returns in the Baron's form and says "Yes, my child, it is I" (016-028), the address now backed by aristocratic appearance, the novel reaches its bitterest irony: the man who once resented the Baron's condescension now reproduces it.

### Self-Sacrifice and the Child

The novel's moral center is located in the duty adults owe to children. Thibault's moment of greatest moral clarity comes when he recognizes this: "I am an upright man, and it is my duty to sacrifice my personal pleasure to the welfare of the dear child" (006-004). The recognition that Agnelette's welfare outweighs his desire is the novel's ethical high-water mark — and its tragedy is that Thibault cannot sustain it. The "dear child" whose welfare he briefly prioritizes becomes, by the novel's end, the victim of his inability to maintain that sacrifice.

Agnelette herself embodies a counter-model. Her "childish eagerness" (006-038) when asking to see the ring is the novel's most undamaged expression of joy. Childhood here is not vulnerability but vitality — the capacity for wonder that the adult world systematically extinguishes. That this eagerness is attached to the ring (the symbol of her bond with Thibault) makes it doubly tragic: the joy is real, and it is doomed.

### The Death of the Child

The novel's treatment of childhood culminates in Agnelette's death. After Thibault's final assault, "her condition became so alarming that before the night was over the priest was sent for; she had evidently now but a few hours longer to live and suffer" (022-018). The "child" who opened the novel — the beautiful sixteen-year-old dancer — dies because the forces she was too innocent to recognize finally consumed her. The novel does not sentimentalize this death; it records it as the inevitable terminus of a world in which children are currency, predators wear paternal masks, and even love cannot protect what it most values.

The narrator's observation about Thibault's suppressed wish — "God, who was reserving the two children for other trials, did not allow this fatal wish to formulate itself" (014-023) — reveals the theological framework: childhood is providentially protected, but only up to a point. The trials for which the "two children" (Agnelette and her bridegroom) are reserved are not protection but suffering deferred. The child is, in the novel's final accounting, the one who cannot escape.

## Key Examples

> "She was indeed a lovely child; hardly sixteen years of age" (003-010)

Agnelette's introduction establishes the child-motif that will shadow her throughout the narrative — her youth makes every subsequent event register as a potential violation of innocence.

> "that's an unlucky name, my child" (003-035)

The Baron's paternal address to Agnelette reveals how "child" functions as a social marker — a term that masks power beneath affection in the ancien régime's class structure.

> "You take a great interest in that rascal over there, my pretty child." (003-014)

The Baron's triple address — possessive, aesthetic, paternal — encapsulates the feudal predation that the language of "child" enables.

> "I am an upright man, and it is my duty to sacrifice my personal pleasure to the welfare of the dear child." (006-004)

Thibault's moral high-water mark — the recognition of duty to the vulnerable — which the novel's arc will systematically betray.

> "And now let me see the ring," she said with childish eagerness (006-038)

The novel's most undamaged expression of joy, attached to the symbol of the bond that will destroy her.

> "she had evidently now but a few hours longer to live and suffer" (022-018)

The death of the child — not sentimentalized but recorded as the terminus of a world that consumes what it pretends to cherish.
