# Melancholy

## Definition

Melancholy (*mélancolie*) is a pervasive sadness — not the acute grief of loss nor the sharp pang of sorrow, but an ambient, constitutional disposition toward darkness. In *The Wolf-Leader*, melancholy is established as [[thibault|Thibault]]'s fundamental character trait before the supernatural plot begins, and it becomes the emotional register through which the forest, the wolves, and ultimately the transformed [[thibault|Thibault]] himself are perceived.

## Role in the Novel

[[thibault|Thibault]] is introduced as "by nature melancholy and sad of heart" (001-037), with this "depression" described as inherent to his constitution despite his physical robustness. The melancholy of his character is amplified by the nocturnal soundscape of the forest: the "melancholy sighs and moanings" of the wind through the forest ways (009-002) and "the other owls uttered their melancholy cries in concert" (014-001). The melancholy howling of the wolves becomes a form of recognition — they perceive [[thibault|Thibault]] as one of their own: "the wolves continued their melancholy howling: 'Yes, my friends,' said Thibault" (021-004), and "a melancholy howl made answer to his words" (019-005).

## Key Examples

> "By nature melancholy and sad of heart" (001-037) — the narrator establishes [[thibault|Thibault]]'s fundamental disposition.

> "The wind passed through the forest ways with melancholy sighs and moanings" (009-002) — the natural world echoes the emotional tone.

> "The other owls uttered their melancholy cries in concert" (014-001) — nocturnal creatures collectively voice the melancholy atmosphere.

> "The wolves continued their melancholy howling: 'Yes, my friends,' said Thibault" (021-004) — melancholy as kinship signal between [[thibault|Thibault]] and the wolves.

> "A melancholy howl made answer to his words" (019-005) — the wolves respond to [[thibault|Thibault]] in a shared language of melancholy.

## Historical and Folklore Background

Melancholy has a long intellectual history predating its Romantic associations. In the Galenic humoral theory that dominated European medicine through the early modern period, melancholy was one of the four temperaments, caused by an excess of black bile and associated with the element of earth, the season of autumn, and the planet Saturn. The melancholic temperament was understood as introspective, creative, and prone to both genius and despair — a duality captured in the Renaissance figure of the "melancholy scholar" and later in Dürer's *Melencolia I*.

In the French literary tradition, melancholy passes through Chateaubriand's *René* and the *mal du siècle* — the nameless malaise of the post-Revolutionary generation — before reaching Dumas. By the 1850s, melancholy had become a standard literary affect, but Dumas gives it structural rather than merely decorative function: [[thibault|Thibault]]'s melancholy is not an ornament but a vulnerability, the predisposition that makes him susceptible to the [[black-wolf|devil]]'s approach.

## Author's Treatment

Dumas makes melancholy the precondition of supernatural vulnerability. [[thibault|Thibault]] is not chosen by the [[black-wolf|devil]] at random; his constitutional melancholy — established in the novel's first description of him — is what makes him permeable to diabolical influence. The melancholy man is already half in darkness; the devil need only complete what disposition has begun.

This is a significant departure from the Faust tradition, where the scholar's intellectual pride drives the pact. In Dumas's version, it is not ambition but sadness that opens the door. The melancholic is the one who already feels the world's wrongness; the devil's offer of wishes appeals not to his greed but to his grievance. Dumas thus rewrites the Faustian bargain as a tragedy of temperament — [[thibault|Thibault]] does not sell his soul for knowledge or power but for redress, and it is his melancholy that convinces him redress is owed.
