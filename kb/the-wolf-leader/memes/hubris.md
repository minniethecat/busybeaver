# Hubris

Hubris (*hybris*, *orgueil démesuré*) is excessive pride or self-confidence that leads a protagonist to overstep the bounds of human limitation — the classic tragic flaw of Greek drama, adapted by Dumas to the idiom of the nineteenth-century novel. In *The Wolf-Leader*, hubris is the engine of Thibault's tragedy.

## From Grievance to Hubris

Thibault does not begin as a hubristic figure. His initial grievances against the [[baron-of-vez|Baron]] are legitimate — the flogging is unjust, the feudal system is oppressive, his poverty is real. Hubris enters not with the grievance but with his response to power. Once the [[devil|devil]] grants him the ability to wish, Thibault's legitimate desire for justice transforms into an illegitimate desire for supremacy. He does not want to be equal to the Baron; he wants to replace him.

This arc — from justified complaint to destructive overreach — is the classical pattern of hubris. The flaw is not in wanting better conditions; it is in the escalation from "better" to "more" to "everything."

## Hubris and the Wishes

Each of Thibault's wishes marks a stage in the growth of hubris. The first wishes are defensive — to strike back at an aggressor, to escape a dangerous situation. But as power becomes familiar, his wishes become aggressive: to dominate, to possess, to destroy. The wish mechanism externalizes hubris — each wish is a concrete enactment of the belief that one's desires should govern reality.

The [[devil|devil]] understands the psychology of hubris perfectly. He never tells Thibault what to wish for; he merely provides the mechanism and waits for hubris to do its work. The devil's genius is to make damnation a product of the protagonist's own character.

## Hubris and Recognition

The tragic arc of hubris culminates in *anagnorisis* — the moment of recognition when the protagonist sees what he has become. Thibault's final flight through the [[forest|forest]], now a [[wolf|wolf]] hunted by those he once knew, contains the elements of tragic recognition. He understands, too late, that each choice was his own, that the devil was merely an enabler, that his destruction was self-authored.
