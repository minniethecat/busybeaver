## Appearances in the Novel

Horror appears throughout *The Wolf-Leader* as one of its defining affective registers.
The word marks key moments of the supernatural and the uncanny.

Thibault's first visceral encounter with horror occurs in the wolf's hut:
"Thibault drew back in horror. The place where the wolf had been lying was empty" (004-054).
The sudden disappearance of the wolf — the impossible vanishing from a sealed room — inaugurates Thibault's experience of a reality that defies natural law.

Later, his self-image is disrupted by horror at his own transformation.
After making his hasty wishes, Thibault looks in the mirror:
"He uttered a cry, half of astonishment, half of horror" (009-006).
The red lock of hair has grown — outward sign of his pact with the devil and his progressive loss of humanity.

## Horror at the Self

The novel's deepest horror is moral rather than supernatural.
By Chapter 21, Thibault no longer recoils at external monstrosity but at what he himself has become:

"The terrible deeds in which he was an active agent... did not fill him exactly with horror, for he thought them justifiable" (021-018).

This is the horror of self-deception — the protagonist who rationalises atrocity.
Dumas then deepens the register:

"The image of Agnelette would rise before him... and more than that, he felt he loved her... filled him with a horror of himself" (021-024).

Here horror becomes moral self-awareness: the man who still loves the woman he wronged,
and who judges himself by the standard of that love.

## Gothic Context

Horror in *The Wolf-Leader* draws on the Gothic tradition of the late eighteenth century.
Like Lewis's *The Monk* (1796), it blends supernatural terror with moral corruption.
Unlike pure Gothic, Dumas anchors the horror in a provincial, realistic setting — the forest, the mill, the marriage feast — making the intrusion of the demonic more jarring.

The werewolf body is itself a site of horror: the body that is no longer one's own,
the human form becoming animal, the red hair spreading like fire across the scalp.
This body horror prefigures later traditions from Stevenson's *Dr Jekyll and Mr Hyde* to twentieth-century werewolf cinema.
