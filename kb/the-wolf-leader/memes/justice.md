## Definition

Justice in *The Wolf-Leader* operates on two incompatible planes. Feudal justice — the legal system of the ancien régime — is embodied by the [[baron-of-vez|Baron of Vez]], who dispenses punishment according to class prerogative rather than moral desert. Moral justice — the ethical intuition that wrongdoing should meet proportionate consequence — is what the feudal system fails to deliver and what [[thibault|Thibault]] seeks through his pact with the wolf.

The gap between these two justices is the novel's central social critique. The flogging scene establishes that legal punishment and moral rightness have no necessary connection in the world of the novel. Thibault is legally guilty of poaching but morally the victim of a system that criminalizes survival.

## Role in the Novel

The flogging incident (Chapter 3) is the novel's founding injustice. Thibault receives "a dozen for poaching and two dozen for perjuring himself, God's portion must be the largest" [003-003]. The Baron's arithmetic — thirty-six strokes allocated with mock-pious precision — is a parody of justice, where punishment is measured but not merited. The beating is legal; it is also cruel, disproportionate, and class-based.

Thibault's subsequent revenge plot is his attempt to restore moral justice through extralegal means. Each wish granted by the wolf is, in Thibault's mind, a correction: the Baron humiliated, the system that oppressed him punished. But the revenge escalates beyond proportionality, and Thibault becomes an agent of injustice in turn — the punisher who exceeds the crime.

The novel thus traces a cycle: feudal injustice begets private vengeance, which begets further injustice. Justice, in Dumas's treatment, is not restored but transformed — from structural oppression to personal retribution to indiscriminate destruction.

## Key Examples

The flogging's legal framing is explicit: poaching "is a serious crime" and perjury "is a great sin" [003-002]. The Baron conflates legal and religious categories, positioning himself as enforcer of both. This conflation is the essence of feudal justice — the lord's will is law, and law is indistinguishable from the lord's will.

Agnelette's intervention — "In the name of the God of mercy, my Lord, have pity on that man!" [003-009] — introduces mercy as a counter-principle to justice. Her plea invokes a higher moral authority than feudal law, and the Baron's response (granting mercy in exchange for a kiss) demonstrates that feudal "mercy" is merely another exercise of arbitrary power.

## Author's Treatment

Dumas embeds his critique of ancien régime justice in narrative form. The flogging scene is structured as spectacle — the menials "rejoicing" at having a victim — which implicates the reader as spectator. By making the punishment vivid and the law abstract, Dumas forces the reader to feel the injustice before analyzing it. The novel's position is clear: feudal justice is theater, and the audience is complicit.
