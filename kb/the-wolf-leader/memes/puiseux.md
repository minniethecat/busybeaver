## Location Overview

Puiseux is a small village situated approximately three miles from [[oigny|Oigny]] and less than half a mile from [[mont-gobert|Mont-Gobert]], within the forest region surrounding [[villers-cotterets|Villers-Cotterêts]]. It is the site of the Curé's house — a priest's residence — where the wounded [[baron-of-vez|Baron Raoul de Vauparfond]] is carried after the hunting accident that fatally injures him, and where [[thibault|Thibault]]'s soul, inhabiting the Baron's body, finally departs.

## Description in the Text

Puiseux appears in the narrative primarily as a waypoint in the Baron's final journey. The wounded man cannot endure the full distance to [[villers-cotterets|Villers-Cotterêts]]:

> "By the time they reached Puiseux, the wounded man felt that he could no longer bear the movement, and begged them to put him down in the first peasant's hut they came to" (018-004).

The Curé's house becomes the Baron's deathbed:

> "Only half-an-hour before he had been still in the body of the Baron Raoul, as he lay dying in the house of the Curé of Puiseux" (019-004).

The village bells mark the Baron's passage to his ancestral burial ground:

> "The Countess had remained in bed part of the day, listening to the village bells of Puiseux, which were tolling as the Baron's body was being borne from thence to Vauparfond" (020-046).

## Key Events

1. **The Baron's Arrival** (Chapter 18): Peasants carry the mortally wounded [[baron-of-vez|Baron Raoul]] toward [[villers-cotterets|Villers-Cotterêts]], but his pain is so severe they can go no farther than Puiseux. He is laid in the Curé's house, where he will die (018-004).

2. **[[thibault|Thibault]]'s Departure** (Chapter 19): [[thibault|Thibault]], having been expelled from the Baron's body at the stroke of nine-thirty, realizes he must confirm the Baron's death. He races from [[oigny|Oigny]] to Puiseux — "covering the ground between Oigny and Puiseux in less than a quarter of an hour" — in the form of the [[black-wolf|black wolf]] with his pack (019-006).

3. **The Funeral Procession** (Chapter 20): The church bells of Puiseux toll as the Baron's body is borne from the Curé's house to [[vauparfond|Vauparfond]] for burial in the family grave. The [[comte-de-mont-gobert|Comtesse de Mont-Gobert]] listens from her chamber (020-046).

4. **The Final Hunt** (Chapter 24): During the climactic pursuit of the werewolf, [[thibault|Thibault]] "first ran back to Puiseux, then skirted past Viviers" in a desperate flight through the forest system (024-003).

## Significance

Puiseux is a threshold space — neither the Baron's home at [[vauparfond|Vauparfond]] nor his destination at [[villers-cotterets|Villers-Cotterêts]], but the place where his journey ends and [[thibault|Thibault]]'s brief inhabitation of nobility concludes. The Curé's house, a place of spiritual authority, becomes the site where [[thibault|Thibault]]'s supernatural bargain reaches its limit: the body he has commandeered expires exactly when the [[devil|devil]]'s terms dictate.

The tolling of Puiseux's bells bridges two worlds — the village's communal acknowledgment of death and the private grief of the [[comte-de-mont-gobert|Comtesse]], who hears them from her chamber. In a novel where sound often signals the supernatural (howling wolves, the wind), the church bells of Puiseux represent the human and the sacred asserting themselves against the demonic.
