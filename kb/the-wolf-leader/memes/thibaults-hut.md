# Thibault's Hut

## Overview

[[thibault|Thibault]]'s hut stands among the Osier-beds of the forest, isolated from both village and castle (001-032). It is at once a workplace (where he makes his [[wood|wooden shoes]]), a dwelling, and — most significantly — the threshold where the natural and supernatural worlds intersect. It is here that the [[devil|black wolf]] enters and the diabolical pact is sealed.

## Role in the Novel

The hut is introduced as [[thibault|Thibault]]'s home and workshop, a modest structure that reflects his social position. Its isolation is both practical (osiers for [[wood|sabot-making]] grow nearby) and symbolic: [[thibault|Thibault]] lives apart from community, and his physical isolation mirrors his moral isolation.

The pivotal scene of the novel takes place within its walls. The [[devil|black wolf]] enters through the door — the boundary between domestic safety and supernatural danger breached without resistance (004-017). The pact is sealed here with a handshake, transforming the hut from a home into a site of [[soul|damnation]].

By the novel's end, the hut has been abandoned. [[thibault|Thibault]] no longer makes shoes or lives as a man; the dwelling that once defined his social identity stands empty, a monument to what he chose to leave behind.

## Significance

The hut is the novel's most important threshold space — the boundary between the human world of work and community and the supernatural world of wishes and wolves. Its violation by the [[devil|black wolf]] is the novel's original sin, and its abandonment marks [[thibault|Thibault]]'s final severance from his human identity.
