# France

## Geography and Setting

Dumas sets *The Wolf-Leader* in the rural provinces of late 18th-century France, a landscape of forests, villages, and feudal estates. The novel's geography is anchored in the region around [[villers-cotterets|Villers-Cotterêts]] and [[vez|Vez]], with the dark forest of [[oigny|Oigny]] serving as the central wilderness where [[thibault|Thibault]] encounters the supernatural. The wider country is referenced as the backdrop of the characters' movements — the "tour of France" that young journeymen like Thibault undertake as part of their trade apprenticeship:

> "it was then called the tour of France" (001-043).

## Social Structure

The France of the novel is characterized by the rigid hierarchies of the *ancien régime*. At the top stand the [[nobility|nobles]] — figures like the [[comte-de-mont-gobert|Count of Mont-Gobert]] and the [[duke-of-orleans|Duke of Orleans]] — whose privileges include hunting rights over vast territories. Below them, the rural peasantry, represented by characters like [[agnette|Agnelette]] and her guardian, live in simple dependence on the land and the [[seigneur|seigneurial]] system. Thibault, as a sabot-maker, occupies the ambiguous space of the rural artisan — neither peasant nor bourgeois, yet aspiring to rise above his station.

The novel uses this social structure to explore the tensions between [[class|class]] privilege and individual [[ambition|ambition]]. The feudal right of hunting, central to the plot, exemplifies how the law protected noble prerogatives:

> "the one known to be surest of all means of gaining a reputation was to be in the company who made the tour of France" (002-053).

## The Tour of France

The "tour de France" was a traditional journey undertaken by journeymen artisans (compagnons) to perfect their craft by working in different regions. Thibault's completion of this tour marks his transition to full craftsman status:

> "completion of his tour round France, and there was nothing for which he was more anxious than to establish himself as a master sabot-maker" (001-051).

His trade service, performed partly to remain near [[agnette|Agnelette]], reflects how the tour system shaped personal and professional destinies:

> "service while making the tour of France, as thereby he was able to remain in the neighbourhood of Agnelette" (003-001).

## The Wider World

Beyond the village and forest, France represents civilization, law, and escape. When [[thibault|Thibault]]'s crimes close in, the possibility of flight beyond France's borders is considered:

> "leave the country, leave France, Europe; there are large countries beyond the seas" (021-059).

The [[countess|Countess of Mont-Gobert]]'s table features the wines of France as markers of refinement:

> "did everything she could to procure a reputation for the various wines of France" (011-046).

Even the learned institutions of Paris — "the Collège de France" (010-009) — are invoked as arbiters of knowledge, contrasting with the folkloric wisdom of the rural world.
