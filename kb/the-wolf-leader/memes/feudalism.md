# Feudalism

Feudalism (*féodalité*) is the political and economic system based on land tenure, personal loyalty, and hierarchical obligation that structured European society from the medieval period through the *ancien régime*. In *The Wolf-Leader*, set in late eighteenth-century France, feudalism is the invisible architecture that determines every character's social position.

## The Feudal Hierarchy

The novel presents a clear feudal hierarchy. At the apex stands the [[duke-of-orleans|Duke of Orléans]], the king's brother, whose castle at [[villers-cotterets|Villers-Cotterêts]] represents royal authority at one remove. Below him are the local seigneurs: the [[baron-of-vez|Baron of Vez]] and the Count of Mont-Gobert, each holding land and exercising jurisdiction within their domains. Below the seigneurs are their retainers — huntsmen, stewards, servants — who owe service in exchange for protection and maintenance. At the base are the peasants and artisans, including [[thibault|Thibault]], who owe dues, labor, and deference to those above them.

## Feudal Hunting Rights

The novel foregrounds one particular aspect of feudalism: hunting rights. The right to hunt is a seigneurial privilege; peasants are forbidden to take game, even when wolves have reduced the deer population and the peasants themselves are hungry. The Baron's wolf hunt, which opens the novel, is an assertion of this privilege — the hunt is not merely pest control but a display of seigneurial authority.

Thibault's crime — poaching, or the suspicion of poaching — strikes at the heart of the feudal order. By taking game that belongs by right to the seigneur, the poacher challenges the entire system of privilege. The severity of Thibault's punishment (the flogging) reflects the seriousness with which the system defends its prerogatives.

## Feudalism in Decline

Dumas sets the novel in a period when the feudal system is beginning to fray. The Duke of Orléans prefers the court at Versailles to his provincial castle [001-007]; the local seigneurs exercise authority that is increasingly ceremonial rather than functional; the peasants, while still deferential, are aware of the growing gap between feudal theory and social reality. The novel, written in 1857, is retrospective — Dumas and his readers know that the Revolution is coming, and the feudal world depicted in the novel is already, historically, doomed.
