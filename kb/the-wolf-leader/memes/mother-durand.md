# Mother Durand

## Character Overview

Mother Durand (*la mère Durand*) is an elderly peasant woman from the village of [[haramont|Haramont]] who appears only in the novel's preface — yet her presence haunts the folkloric atmosphere that Dumas establishes for the entire narrative. She is introduced through the testimony of Mocquet, the gamekeeper, who attributes his nightmare to her witchcraft: "By Mother Durand, of Haramont, who, as you will have heard, is an old witch" (P02-039). She never speaks, never appears directly, and exercises her influence entirely through the beliefs others hold about her.

## Role in the Story

Mother Durand's narrative function is to establish the folkloric register of the novel before the main story begins. The preface, narrated by Dumas himself (or a version of himself), recounts his father's encounter with the gamekeeper Mocquet, who claims to have been "nightmared" by Mother Durand. Mocquet's belief is presented with ethnographic neutrality: Dumas does not endorse the witchcraft accusation, but neither does he dismiss it as mere superstition.

The episode serves as a miniature of the novel's larger method. A man experiences something uncanny; folk belief provides the only available explanation; the rational listener is skeptical but cannot definitively refute the folk account. When Mocquet sets "a trap to catch Mother Durand" (P02-083), the gesture is simultaneously absurd (how do you trap a witch?) and practical (a physical trap, a concrete response to a supernatural threat). This ambiguous register — the folk and the rational, the absurd and the practical coexisting — is the tonal key to the entire novel.

The postscript to Mother Durand's story is telling. After Mocquet is removed from the district, "Mother Durand will be able to sleep in peace" (P02-137) — the implication being that the witch needed protection from her accuser, not the other way around. Dumas's irony is gentle but unmistakable: folk belief persecutes the powerless, and the witch is more often victim than predator.

## Character Analysis

Mother Durand is less a character than a narrative function — she is the absent center of a belief system that the novel takes seriously without endorsing. She belongs to the same folkloric universe as the werewolf: a world where the boundary between natural and supernatural is porous, where old women can curse their neighbors, and where the forest at night contains more than trees. Her presence in the preface primes the reader for the supernatural events of the main narrative by establishing that in this world, folk belief is a legitimate way of knowing.

She also represents a French literary archetype: the village witch whose power is a function of social marginality. Like the *sorcière* of Michelet, Mother Durand's alleged witchcraft is inseparable from her poverty, her age, and her gender. She is dangerous because she is powerless, and she is believed to be powerful because she is otherwise invisible.
