# Wedding

## Definition

The wedding in *The Wolf-Leader* is the public ceremony that transforms a marriage from private intention into social fact. Distinct from [[marriage]] as an institution, the wedding is the event — the feast, the dress, the gathering, the exchange of vows. It is the moment when private love becomes community property.

The novel presents the wedding as a site of both joy and pain. Agnelette's wedding to the gardener is the novel's central instance of "the road not taken" — Thibault encounters the wedding party as an outsider, watching happiness he has forfeited. The wedding at Magloire's house is a major set-piece, bringing the village community together and serving as backdrop for critical narrative developments.

## Role in the Novel

### Agnelette's Wedding as the Lost Future

The novel establishes the wedding as Thibault's forfeited future early on. His first earnings "should be devoted to buying a wedding dress for his betrothed" (PN=004-010). The wedding dress is a material symbol of a future that Thibault already plans but will never reach. The dress becomes the measure of what the devil-pact costs him: not just his soul but a specific, concrete happiness — Agnelette in her wedding dress.

Agnelette herself asks "When shall we have the wedding?" (PN=003-105), and the question hangs over the entire novel. The answer turns out to be "never" — or rather, she will have a wedding, but not with Thibault. The wedding that happens without him is his deepest wound.

### The Village Wedding as Social Theater

"When some village wedding passed by the castle" (PN=014-012) — the wedding functions as a mobile social event, processing through the landscape. The wedding party is visible and audible — music, laughter, a crowd in celebration. Thibault encounters it not as participant but as observer, the werewolf watching from the margins of human joy.

"He saw himself as Agnelette and the happy wedding party went by" (PN=018-019) — Thibault's self-perception in the moment of seeing the wedding is split. He sees himself seeing; he is spectator to his own exclusion. The wedding makes visible what he has lost.

### The Wedding Feast at Magloire's

The wedding at Magloire's house is one of the novel's major set-pieces. Extended family and the village community gather for feasting and celebration. It is at this wedding that key plot developments unfold — the intersection of Thibault's revenge arc with the community's ritual life.

The wedding feast also showcases the material culture of rural celebration: the food, the wine, the social dynamics of a peasant wedding in 18th-century France. It is the novel's most detailed depiction of communal festivity.

### Wedding Dress and Material Hope

The wedding dress functions as a token of hope throughout the early chapters. "Give it me to keep till our wedding day, and on that day I will give it back to you" (PN=006-033) — Agnelette's request turns the dress into a pledge, a material bond between the lovers. The dress is both promise and test: it will be returned on the wedding day, if the wedding day ever comes. It never does.

### The Wedding That Never Was

The novel's tragedy is crystallized in the wedding that Thibault never has. Every reference to weddings in the later chapters is a reminder of what was lost. The wedding party that passes the castle, the wedding feast he cannot join, the wedding dress that will never be returned — these are the ghost-outlines of a life Thibault chose to renounce.

## Key Examples

- **PN=003-105**: "And when shall we have the wedding, Agnelette?" — the question that establishes the wedding as the horizon of Thibault and Agnelette's shared future.
- **PN=004-010**: Thibault's plan "to buy a wedding dress for his betrothed" — the wedding dress as symbol of material hope and future planning.
- **PN=006-033**: "Give it me to keep till our wedding day, and on that day I will give it back to you" — the dress as pledge and test of fidelity.
- **PN=014-012**: "A village wedding party passed by" — Thibault's exclusion from the community's celebration, the werewolf as outsider to human joy.
- **PN=018-019**: "He saw himself as Agnelette and the happy wedding party went by" — the splitting of self-perception at the moment of loss.
