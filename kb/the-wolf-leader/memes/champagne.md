# Champagne

## Definition

In *The Wolf-Leader*, "champagne" carries a double referent: the sparkling wine from the Aÿ region of [[france|France]], and a servant named Champagne who works in the [[comte-de-mont-gobert|Comtesse de Mont-Gobert]]'s household. The wine serves as a marker of festivity and social distinction at the [[the-wedding|wedding feast]]; the servant appears in Chapters 15 and 16 as a messenger and confidant. These two meanings — luxury commodity and liveried attendant — are linked by the social structure of the *ancien régime*, where a servant might be named for the luxury good associated with the household he serves.

## Role in the Novel

### As Wine: The Wedding Feast

Champagne appears at the wedding of [[magloire|Monsieur Magloire]]'s daughter, where it functions as the culminating symbol of festivity and excess. The vintage is specified — "prime vintage of Aï" — and its effect on the guests is transformative:

> "The rosy champagne — prime vintage of Aï, and the last bottle emptied — had brought Népomucène Magloire's habitual good nature to the level of tender affection" (011-050).

[[magloire|Monsieur Magloire]] makes a particular request of the bride, asking that she add "to the flavour and bouquet of the champagne by pouring it out herself for her guest" — a ceremonial act that elevates the wine from mere refreshment to a gesture of hospitality and honor (011-005). The pouring of champagne by the bride's own hand marks the social significance of the occasion and the guest being honored.

Champagne thus operates as a class marker. It is not the everyday wine of peasants but a luxury reserved for weddings, celebrations, and the entertainment of important guests. Its presence at the [[magloire|Magloire]] table signals both the family's prosperity and the exceptional nature of the occasion.

### As Character: The Servant

The servant Champagne is employed by the [[comte-de-mont-gobert|Comtesse de Mont-Gobert]] and serves as a go-between in the affair between the Comtesse and [[baron-of-vez|Raoul de Vauparfond]]:

> "Champagne, who is in the Comtesse de Mont-Gobert's service" (015-019).

He is tasked with carrying letters between the lovers and appears at an inn where [[francois|François]], the Baron's huntsman, awaits him:

> "Your master, the Lord of Vauparfond, is in love with the Comtesse de Mont-Gobert, and you are now awaiting a letter which Champagne is to bring from the lady" (015-020).

Champagne is portrayed as a "well-bred servant" with "ready intuition" (015-031). He shares gossip and news with [[francois|François]] over wine, revealing the informal intelligence network that servants maintained across noble households — a system of information exchange that both sustains and complicates the romantic intrigues of their employers.

## Key Examples

The ceremonial pouring of champagne at the wedding:

> "Her affability did not go so far as to lead her to comply with the request of Monsieur Magloire, who begged her to add to the flavour and bouquet of the champagne by pouring it out herself for her guest" (011-005).

The transformative power of the wine:

> "The rosy champagne — prime vintage of Aï, and the last bottle emptied — had brought Népomucène Magloire's habitual good nature to the level of tender affection" (011-050).

Champagne the servant as messenger:

> "Your master, the Lord of Vauparfond, is in love with the Comtesse de Mont-Gobert, and you are now awaiting a letter which Champagne is to bring from the lady" (015-020).

The servant's professional intuition:

> "Champagne, with all the ready intuition of the well-bred servant, understood at once, and went upstairs" (015-031).

## Author's Treatment

Dumas's use of champagne — both wine and character — reflects the novel's attention to the material culture of rural France under the *ancien régime*. The wine is not merely mentioned but specified: it is from Aÿ, it is rosy, it is the last bottle. These details ground the wedding feast in a particular time, place, and social stratum. The "last bottle emptied" carries a faint [[melancholy|melancholic]] note — the feast's peak is also its end.

The servant Champagne exemplifies the novel's treatment of the servant class: named but not developed, present but not central. He is a functionary of the plot, moving letters and information between noble households, and his name — like that of other servants — ties his identity to the household he serves rather than to any individual self.
