## The Doctor's Diagnosis

Delirium enters the novel through medical terminology.
After his first encounter with the speaking wolf, Thibault rationalises his experience:

"This no doubt is what the good doctor spoke about as delirium" (018-018).

The word is a defence mechanism — naming the supernatural as illness.
If the wolf's speech, the impossible vanishing, the growing red hair can all be dismissed as delirium,
then Thibault's pact with the devil can remain a hallucination rather than a reality.

But the novel refuses this medical reduction. The doctor's category is invoked precisely
at the moment when the supernatural is most undeniable.
Delirium becomes not an explanation but a marker of the boundary — the threshold state where
rational categories fail and the demonic becomes possible.

## Delirium and the Deathbed

In Chapter 22, delirium returns in a more traditional form — the deathbed fever:

"A paroxysm of despair which bordered on delirium, and which at last became almost continuous" (022-014).

The dying woman's "breath shorter, although her delirium was as violent as ever" (022-015)
places delirium at the passage between life and death.
Here it is not a diagnostic category but a spiritual condition — the soul's agitation
as it approaches judgment.

## Liminality and Transformation

The novel deploys delirium as part of its larger pattern of liminal states.
Fever, hallucination, dream, and vision all occupy the space between human and animal,
between waking life and death, between natural law and supernatural intervention.

Thibault's clock-vision — the hallucinatory eternity of Chapter 18 — is itself a form of delirium,
one that yields philosophical insight even as it registers physiological breakdown.
The novel suggests that delirium is not merely error or illness
but a mode of perception that reveals truths invisible to healthy reason.

In this, Dumas draws on a Romantic conception of altered consciousness:
the fevered mind sees what the well mind cannot — the eternal pendulum,
the wolf within — even if it cannot bear what it sees.
