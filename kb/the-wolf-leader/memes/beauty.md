## Definition

In *The Wolf-Leader*, beauty operates as a counter-force to the novel's predominant darkness. Dumas lavishes descriptive attention on the "beautiful valley which runs between Berval and Longpré" (001-002), on Agnelette's "large, beautiful eyes, all wet with tears" (003-008), on the "most beautiful parts of the woods" (001-048). Beauty in the novel is not decorative but structural — it establishes a normative world of light, order, and proportion against which the wolf's darkness and Thibault's disfigurement are measured as violations.

## Role in the Novel

The novel's opening pages establish beauty as the default register of the natural world. The landscape is beautiful; the forest is beautiful; even the old tower, "by reason of its isolated position," appears "doubly high and formidable" yet part of a beautiful valley. This aesthetic grounding is strategic: the horror of the werewolf narrative depends on a prior establishment of beauty that can be corrupted.

Human beauty functions as social currency. Agnelette's beauty is her primary asset in a world where peasant women have few others — "they called you the beautiful dancer, and stood round in a circle to watch you" (003-074). The Baron notices her because of "the beautiful suppliant" and "her large, beautiful eyes" (003-008, 003-011). Beauty opens doors that class keeps closed — but it also makes its bearer a target.

The novel's most striking use of beauty comes in the supernatural register. The mysterious lady of Erneville's "wonderful beauty dazzled me like a ray of May sunshine" (011-007). When Thibault is temporarily transformed into the Baron's form, he rushes home "intending, like Narcissus, to enjoy his own beauty in peace" (016-020) — beauty become self-regarding, another trap. The novel ultimately suggests that beauty, like the devil's gifts, is dangerous when sought for its own sake and redemptive only when appreciated as part of a larger moral order.

### Beauty as Predation Marker

The Baron's language to Agnelette reveals a darker function of beauty in the feudal world. His repeated address — "my pretty child" (003-014), "pretty girl" (003-025), "my pretty one" (003-033), "my beauty" (003-037) — layers possessiveness over aesthetic appreciation. The possessive pronoun "my" recurs across all his addresses, transforming beauty from a quality Agnelette possesses into something the Baron claims. His warning that her name makes her "a prey for the wolf, my beauty" (003-037) is a double entendre: the literal wolf of the forest and the figurative wolf of aristocratic predation are the same threat.

When the Baron asks "And you are not afraid, young and pretty as you are?" (003-043), the question itself enacts the threat it pretends to observe. Beauty in the feudal hierarchy is not protective but provocative — it attracts danger precisely because it is desirable. The Baron's "protection" — "there is a way, my pretty one, whereby you may spare yourself all these fears and tremblings" (003-048) — is the classic predator's offer: safety in exchange for submission.

### Thibault's Vanity

Thibault's relationship to beauty is different from the Baron's but equally compromised. The narrator establishes early that Thibault considers himself "handsomer, stronger, and cleverer than many of the nobles" (001-046), a self-assessment that fuels his resentment. His physical vanity — he "could dance a jig beautifully" and was proud of his appearance (001-046) — makes his eventual disfigurement by the wolf's pact all the more significant. The man who prided himself on being handsome loses his face; the dancer loses his form.

This vanity extends to his perception of Agnelette. When the Baron kisses her, Thibault's jealousy manifests as mockery: "the Baron's kiss has turned your head, has it, my pretty Agnelette?" (003-062). But his jealousy is not primarily about Agnelette — it is about his own status relative to the Baron. Agnelette's beauty is valuable to Thibault primarily as a measure of his own worth; when the Baron claims a share of her attention, Thibault experiences it as theft. His love of beauty is inseparable from his love of self.

### The Blush

Agnelette's beauty reaches its fullest expression not in display but in concealment: "The girl blushed and lowered her eyes, and the blush and the shy embarrassment only made her look more charming still" (003-079). The blush is beauty's paradox — it is the attempt to hide that most reveals. Unlike the Baron's predatory gaze or Thibault's self-regarding vanity, Agnelette's beauty is involuntary, a function of her innocence rather than her will. The novel suggests, through the blush, that true beauty cannot be performed — it is what happens when performance fails.

## Key Examples

> "If you follow the beautiful valley which runs between Berval and Longpré, you will see, on your left hand, an old tower." (001-002)

The novel's first descriptive passage establishes beauty as the baseline — the world is beautiful before it is terrifying, which is precisely what makes the terror matter.

> "lifting her large, beautiful eyes, all wet with tears, to the Baron" (003-008)

Agnelette's beauty is introduced in the context of supplication — beauty as plea, as moral claim on the powerful.

> "Because it makes you a prey for the wolf, my beauty." (003-037)

The Baron's double-edged warning collapses the distinction between the literal wolf and the aristocratic predator — beauty attracts both.

> "The girl blushed and lowered her eyes, and the blush and the shy embarrassment only made her look more charming still." (003-079)

The blush as beauty's paradox — the self that tries to hide reveals itself most fully.

> "its wonderful beauty dazzled me like a ray of May sunshine" (011-007)

Supernatural beauty is described in the language of natural beauty, blurring the boundary between what is divinely radiant and what is demonically seductive.

> "intending, like Narcissus, to enjoy his own beauty in peace" (016-020)

Thibault's transformation into the Baron's form culminates in self-regard — beauty turned inward, the classical sign of damnation.
