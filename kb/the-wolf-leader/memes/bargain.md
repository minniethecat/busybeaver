# Bargain

## Definition

A bargain (*marché*) is a negotiated exchange in which two parties agree upon terms of reciprocal obligation. In *The Wolf-Leader*, the logic of bargaining permeates every level of social interaction: the peasant haggles over a kiss, the nobleman bargains for sexual favors, and the [[thibault|sabot-maker]] bargains with the [[devil|black wolf]] for wishes. Bargaining is the novel's dominant mode of social and supernatural transaction, distinct from the [[devil-pact|devil's pact]] itself — the pact is the contract; bargaining is the process of establishing its terms.

## Role in the Novel

[[thibault|Thibault]]'s introduction establishes him as a "bit of a carpenter into the bargain" (001-049), framing his character from the outset as one defined by supplementary exchange. The novel's key bargaining scenes escalate in stakes: from the [[baron-of-vez|Lord of Vez]] bargaining with [[agnette|Agnelette]] for a kiss — "Come, a bargain, pretty girl!" (003-025) — to the peasants bargaining for [[baron-of-vez|the Baron]]'s life, "his precious life was being bargained for in that miserly way" (005-054). [[thibault|Thibault]] himself feels "relief at getting out of the bargain so cheaply" (004-031) when the [[baron-of-vez|Baron]] accepts his retribution on [[thibault|Thibault]] alone.

## Key Examples

The bargaining logic escalates from flirtation to damnation:

> "Come, a bargain, pretty girl!" (003-025) — the [[baron-of-vez|Lord of Vez]] bargains for [[agnette|Agnelette]]'s kiss.

> The Lord of Vez had bargained for one kiss, but he took two (003-030).

> "His precious life was being bargained for in that miserly way" (005-054).

> "When I concluded that unhappy bargain with you, I did not understand that it was to be a compact of this kind" (023-023) — [[thibault|Thibault]] protests the terms of his [[devil-pact|devil's pact]].

## Historical and Folklore Background

The bargain as social form pervades French *Ancien Régime* culture. In the feudal economy, the *marché* governed not only commercial exchange but also social relations: marriage contracts, labor agreements, and the complex web of seigneurial obligations were all understood through the logic of the bargain. The *coutume* (customary law) that governed rural life was itself a kind of historical bargain between lords and peasants, specifying mutual obligations in granular detail.

In folklore, the bargain with supernatural entities — fairies, demons, the devil — is a pan-European motif classified as ATU 756B ("The Devil's Contract") in the Aarne-Thompson-Uther index. The key feature of the folkloric bargain is its asymmetry: the supernatural party always extracts more than the human bargainer anticipates. Dumas draws on this tradition while innovating in the mechanism of incremental payment (hair by hair, wish by wish).

## Author's Treatment

Dumas structures *The Wolf-Leader* as a chain of escalating bargains, each one worse than the last. This is not merely decorative — it is the novel's structural principle. The flirtatious bargain between the [[baron-of-vez|Lord]] and [[agnette|Agnelette]] echoes and foreshadows the diabolical bargain between [[thibault|Thibault]] and the [[black-wolf|black wolf]]. Both involve an exchange of consent for favor; both are asymmetrical; both end with one party taking more than was offered.

What distinguishes Dumas's treatment is his insistence on the continuity between social and supernatural bargains. The [[devil-pact|devil's pact]] is not a rupture with the normal social order — it is its logical extension. The feudal lord who bargains for a peasant girl's kiss and the devil who bargains for a sabot-maker's soul operate on the same principle: the powerful set the terms, and the weak pay more than they intended. Dumas's critique of the *Ancien Régime* is embedded in this structural homology between feudal and diabolical bargaining.
