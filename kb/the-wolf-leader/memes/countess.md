## Character Overview

The Countess of Mont-Gobert is a noblewoman whose illicit affair with the transformed [[thibault|Thibault]] (now the [[baron-of-vez|Baron of Vez]]) forms the tragic climax of the novel's middle section. Married to the [[comte-de-mont-gobert|Comte de Mont-Gobert]], she conducts her liaison with the Baron through a series of secret meetings facilitated by her maid [[lisette|Lisette]]. The narrator describes her as "a beautiful creature" [015-024], and her chambers in the château are described in terms of opulent luxury: "the magnificence" of her room, with every surface adorned [017-001].

Her story arc moves from romantic passion to jealous [[jealousy|suspicion]] to tragic resolution. When she discovers the Baron's [[fidelity|infidelity]] — his continued entanglement with Madame Magloire and others — she does not simply end the affair. Instead, she orchestrates a fatal confrontation that destroys both her husband and herself.

## Role in the Story

The Countess first enters the narrative indirectly, through the letters exchanged with the Baron. [[thibault|Thibault]], in his new identity, intercepts "the [[comte-de-mont-gobert|Comte de Mont-Gobert]] in answer to the Countess's letter" [016-011], and learns of her interest in him. She "held out a hand" [017-012] at their first meeting, and [[thibault|Thibault]] — who had previously never touched "anything so like satin" [017-013] — is immediately enthralled.

The affair escalates rapidly. The Countess "seated herself on the settee" [017-013] and demands to know why the Baron stayed away "since you were last here" [017-014]. Their conversation is charged with erotic tension and mutual manipulation. When [[thibault|Thibault]] asks what would happen "supposing we were surprised" [017-019], the Countess's response reveals her awareness of the danger and her willingness to risk it.

The tragic turn comes when the Count arrives unexpectedly. The Countess "exclaimed" [017-054] in alarm, and the lovers scramble to hide evidence of the Baron's presence. In the ensuing chaos, the Count is killed — stabbed with "the dagger given him by the Countess" [020-043], which she had given to the Baron as a token of her [[love|affection]].

Her final act is suicide. She tells [[lisette|Lisette]] "I am going to take a sleeping-draught" [020-056], but the draught is poison. She says "It will be enough, [[lisette|Lisette]]; for after I have taken it, I shall sleep a long sleep" [020-058]. When [[thibault|Thibault]] arrives at her room that night, she is already dead: "The Countess lay with her arms stretched out" [020-037].

## Key Relationships

- **[[thibault|Thibault]] (the [[baron-of-vez|Baron of Vez]])** — Her lover. Their relationship is passionate but founded on mutual deception: she does not know his true identity as a transformed peasant, and he does not reveal his supernatural pact. She gives him a dagger as a love-token, which he uses to kill her husband.

- **The [[comte-de-mont-gobert|Comte de Mont-Gobert]]** — Her husband, whom she deceives and whose death she indirectly causes. The narrative does not show their marriage in detail, but the Countess's willingness to risk everything for the Baron suggests a deeply unhappy union.

- **[[lisette|Lisette]]** — Her loyal waiting-maid, who serves as confidante and facilitator. The Countess trusts [[lisette|Lisette]] with knowledge of her affair and ultimately with the execution of her suicide plan.

## Character Analysis

The Countess embodies the Gothic archetype of the passionate, doomed noblewoman. Her tragedy operates on multiple levels: personal (a loveless marriage dri[[wine|vin]]g her to adultery), social (the moral hypocrisy of a class that demands female chastity while permitting male license), and supernatural (she is an unwitting participant in the consequences of [[thibault|Thibault]]'s [[devil|demon]]ic pact).

The dagger she gives the Baron functions as a loaded symbol. What she intends as a romantic gift becomes a murder weapon. Her suicide by poison mirrors classic literary suicides by aristocratic women — a death that preserves a certain dignity while escaping the shame of exposure. Her final words to [[lisette|Lisette]], describing her poison as "a sleeping-draught" [020-056], encapsulate her mix of self-deception and clear-eyed resolution.
