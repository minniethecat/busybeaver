# Power

## Definition

Power in *The Wolf-Leader* operates in two registers: the visible power of the *ancien régime* [[class|social hierarchy]] (the Baron, the Duke of Orleans, the Church) and the invisible power of the supernatural (the [[devil|black wolf]], the wish mechanism, the curse). [[thibault|Thibault]]'s tragedy is his failure to understand that the second kind of power is not an escape from the first but a deeper entanglement in a more absolute hierarchy.

## Role in the Novel

Social power is established from the novel's opening scene. The [[baron-of-vez|Baron of Vez]] commands huntsmen, hounds, and the right of corporal punishment over peasants. This power is arbitrary but not random — it follows the logic of [[class|rank]] and precedent, and even the Baron's cruelty operates within understood limits. The flogging is brutal but not lawless.

The [[devil|black wolf]] offers [[thibault|Thibault]] an alternative power: the ability to bypass [[class|social hierarchy]] through supernatural means. Each wish is an exercise of power that circumvents the normal channels of achievement. But this power is illusory — [[thibault|Thibault]] is not the source of the power but merely its conduit, and every exercise of "his" power transfers more of himself to the wolf.

The novel's power structure is ultimately feudal: everyone serves someone. The Baron serves the Duke; the peasants serve the Baron; [[thibault|Thibault]] serves the [[devil|black wolf]], who serves no one but demands everything. The illusion of independence is the devil's oldest trick, and [[thibault|Thibault]] falls for it completely.

## Key Examples

The Baron's power is absolute within its domain:

> As Grand Master of His Highness's Wolf Hounds, the Baron embodies seigneurial authority over men and beasts alike.

The [[devil|black wolf]]'s power is absolute but contractual — every exercise of power has a cost:

> Each wish granted with "scrupulous exactness" — the precision of diabolical accounting.

## Author's Treatment

Dumas, who lived through the rise and fall of Napoleon, the Restoration, and the Second Empire, understood power as inherently unstable. The *ancien régime* that [[thibault|Thibault]] resents would collapse within a generation of the novel's events. But Dumas does not present [[thibault|Thibault]] as a proto-revolutionary; he presents him as someone who wants to join the oppressors rather than end oppression. His [[wish|desire]] for power is not revolutionary but envious — he wants what the Baron has, not [[law|justice]] for his class.
