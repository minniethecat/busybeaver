# Possession

**Possession** operates in _The Wolf-Leader_ along two intersecting axes: the supernatural (demonic possession of the soul) and the material (ownership of property, bodies, and labor). The novel's genius lies in making these two forms of possession mutually constitutive — the wolf's possession of Thibault is enabled by Thibault's desire for material possession, and his subsequent material gains are themselves the outward sign of inward spiritual dispossession.

## Demonic Possession as Progressive Occupation

The wolf's possession of Thibault is not instantaneous but progressive. The opening authorial note establishes the paradigm: "it is not the author who decides on the subject, but the subject which takes possession of the author" [001-036]. This framing — the subject as an invading force that "takes possession" of the writer — mirrors the wolf's occupation of Thibault. Both are cases of agency transferred, will displaced, identity progressively hollowed out by an occupying presence.

The possession operates through the mechanism of exchanged tokens. The wolf gives Thibault a gold ring in exchange for his silver one: "You have a silver ring, I have a gold one; let us exchange; the barter will be to your advantage, as you see" [005-021]. The ring is the material token of possession — wearing it, Thibault carries the sign of his dispossession on his own body.

## Material Possession as the Engine of the Plot

Every major plot movement is driven by Thibault's desire for material possession. He wants the deer — "in proportion as the difficulty increased, the greater became Thibault's desire to get possession of the deer" [002-056] — not for sustenance but for the status its possession confers. He wants the widow's mill, Agnelette's beauty, the Baron's hunting rights, the Countess's body. Each desired possession represents a rung on a ladder he cannot climb without supernatural aid.

The novel's feudal setting makes possession inherently political. The Baron possesses the forest and its game; Thibault's poaching is a crime against possession itself. The wolf's offer — unlimited possession — is attractive precisely because it promises to overturn the feudal order of who possesses what.

## Possession and the Body

The body-swap with Raoul de Vauparfond literalizes the concept of possession. Thibault "appeared to be in perfect possession of his senses" [014-011] while inhabiting another man's body — the phrase is ironic, for he possesses Raoul's body while his own body lies elsewhere, and his "perfect possession" of his senses is the most profound dispossession of all.

The novel also stages the inverse: possession as the loss of self. When a "violent feeling of anger towards the Bailiff's wife took possession of Thibault" [012-093], the passive construction is telling — Thibault does not _feel_ anger; anger _takes possession of_ him. The wolf's occupation has made him a vessel for emotions that are not fully his own. Earlier, "a momentary stupefaction which took possession of the farm people" [008-047] shows the same grammar of possession-as-passivity operating on the collective level.

## The Family Possession

Against the logic of supernatural and feudal possession, the novel briefly gestures at an alternative: the family possession held in common and passed down through generations. Engoulevent gives Thibault water "in a wooden drinking-cup, a family possession, of which Thibault was very proud" [005-059]. This modest object — a wooden cup, not a gold ring — represents possession without predation, ownership grounded in lineage rather than conquest. It is the novel's single image of possession that is not corrupt or corrupting, and it is notable that this cup appears only once, before the pact, as a relic of the life Thibault is about to lose.
