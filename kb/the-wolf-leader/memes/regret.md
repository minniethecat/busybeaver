# Regret

## Definition

Regret in *The Wolf-Leader* is the backward-looking moral emotion — the pain of recognizing that one has made the wrong choice. Unlike grief or sorrow, which respond to loss, regret is specific to agency: it arises from the knowledge that one could have acted differently. It is the emotional signature of moral responsibility in the novel.

Thibault's regret is not repentance — at least not until the very end. The novel carefully distinguishes between regret (a secular, psychological response to bad outcomes) and repentance (a theological turning-away from sin). Thibault feels regret long before he feels repentance, and the gap between the two constitutes his moral tragedy.

## Role in the Novel

### Regret as Early Warning

The first instance of regret in the novel is not Thibault's but the Lord of Vez's: he is "regretting the black wolf" (PN=001-029) — an ambiguous regret that may be for the lost hunt or for the harm already set in motion. This early regret signals the wolf's significance before Thibault's own arc begins.

### Thibault's Missing Regret

After Thibault's first wish — the destruction of the Baron's leg — he experiences "little regret at the departure of lord, dogs, and huntsmen" (PN=006-007). His satisfaction at revenge outweighs any moral unease. The absence of regret at this crucial juncture marks Thibault's moral descent: the first wish works, and satisfaction silences conscience.

### Regret and Anger

Thibault's emotional responses are often unstable mixtures. When Agnelette departs, her tears fall "rather from anger than from regret" (PN=014-022). The proximity of anger and regret in the novel suggests that regret is what anger becomes when the heat of the moment cools — and that Thibault's dominant emotion (anger) prevents him from fully experiencing regret until it is too late.

### Regret Without Repentance

At the emotional climax of his transformation, Thibault "felt neither repentance nor regret" (PN=021-003). The double negation is crucial: he has reached a state beyond both. What "dominated all other feelings" is the thirst for destruction. The absence of regret at this point is the novel's most chilling measurement of Thibault's fall — he has become incapable of the one emotion that could have saved him.

### Regret as Retrospective

In the novel's final pages, the retrospective voice asks: "vain aspirations and endless regrets?" (PN=023-008). Here regret is no longer Thibault's experience but the frame narrator's — the story itself is an exercise in regret, a looking-back at what could have been. The frame narrative turns regret from a private emotion into a shared one: the reader is invited to regret on Thibault's behalf.

## Key Examples

- **PN=001-029**: The Lord of Vez, "regretting the black wolf" — the novel's first instance of regret, establishing it as a response to the supernatural.
- **PN=006-007**: Thibault "experienced little regret" at the hunt's departure — the absence of regret as the first sign of moral corruption.
- **PN=008-039**: "Qualities that you admire and regret in Landry" — regret as appreciation of lost good, applied to a secondary character.
- **PN=011-041**: The Duke "consented, not without regret, but with less regret than he had feared" — regret as social calculation in aristocratic life, contrasted with Thibault's consuming passions.
- **PN=014-022**: Agnelette's tears, "rather from anger than from regret" — the emotional proximity and distinction between anger and regret.
- **PN=021-003**: Thibault "felt neither repentance nor regret" — the nadir of his moral arc, beyond even the pain of wrong choice.
- **PN=023-008**: "Vain aspirations and endless regrets" — the retrospective framing that turns Thibault's story into an object of collective regret.
