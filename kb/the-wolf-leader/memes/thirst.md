# Thirst

## Definition

Thirst (*soif*) begins as an ordinary bodily need but transforms, through the werewolf's curse, into something monstrous. In *The Wolf-Leader*, thirst progresses from the mundane ([[thibault|Thibault]]'s preference for drinking water over wasting time at taverns) through the convivial (wine at the wedding feast, "provoking thirst") to the diabolical — the werewolf's "thirst for blood" (021-007). Thirst is the craving that escalation charts: from water to wine to blood, from human refreshment to monstrous appetite.

## Role in the Novel

[[thibault|Thibault]]'s relationship with thirst is established early: "He would rather not drink, although he was often thirsty, than have spent that time which might have been given to his work at the tavern" (001-011). This disciplined, productive thirst is the baseline against which his later degradation is measured. At the wedding feast, wine becomes the "chief quality is to provoke thirst" (011-026), linking social drinking to escalating desire. The transformation's climax is marked by thirst transformed into bloodlust: the wolves "stimulating their thirst for blood" (021-007), and [[thibault|Thibault]] who "ate raw flesh and quenched his thirst in blood" (021-009). The human who once moderated his thirst now quenches it in the life of others.

## Key Examples

> "He would rather not drink, although he was often thirsty" (001-011) — the baseline: controlled, moderate thirst.

> "Wine, whose chief quality is to provoke thirst" (011-026) — the convivial escalation at the wedding feast.

> "Such was their hunger and thirst, that half the carp disappeared" (011-030) — communal consumption, the last human feast.

> "Stimulating their thirst for blood" (021-007) — thirst transformed into predatory bloodlust.

> "Ate raw flesh and quenched his thirst in blood. And the people went forth to hunt the werewolf" (021-009) — the final stage: thirst satisfied in blood, triggering the community's hunt.
