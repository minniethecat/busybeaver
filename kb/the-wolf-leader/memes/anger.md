## Definition

Anger, in the context of *The Wolf-Leader*, is the visceral emotional response to perceived injustice, humiliation, and powerlessness. It is the primary affective driver that propels Thibault from victim to avenger. Unlike hatred (a sustained, rationalized hostility) or revenge (the action taken in response), anger is the immediate, physiological reaction — "the raw material of the revenge plot" — that the black wolf exploits and channels toward supernatural ends.

## Role in the Novel

Anger functions as the psychological gateway through which the devil's temptation enters. Thibault's anger at the public flogging, at the class system that permits it, and at his own powerlessness creates the emotional vulnerability that the wolf exploits. The novel presents anger as both a natural response to injustice and a dangerous spiritual state — one that, when nurtured, opens the soul to darker forces.

The feudal context amplifies anger's destructive potential. When the powerful (the Baron, the widow Polet) express anger, it becomes a weapon against the weak. When the weak (Thibault) express anger, it becomes the fuel for supernatural retribution. The asymmetry of anger — who is permitted to express it and how — is itself a marker of class hierarchy.

### The Physiology of Anger

Dumas renders anger in bodily terms throughout the novel, making it a physical event rather than merely a psychological one. The Baron's "angry look" at Thibault (002-028) precedes words — the aristocratic gaze itself is an act of aggression. Thibault's "enraged" state after his failed boar-spear throw (002-053) is described as a blow to his professional pride. The progression from hurt pride to rage traces a physiological arc: the body tenses, the face flushes, and the will to violence awakens.

Anger in the novel is also a contagious emotion. When Marcotte, the huntsman, finally breaks his habitual patience at the Baron's insult to his dogs (002-076), his outburst reveals how anger accumulates under feudal hierarchy — silent endurance gives way to explosive defiance only when a man's deepest pride is touched. The word "trash" applied to his beloved hounds crosses a threshold that personal insults could not.

### Anger Transformed

The novel's most significant anger narrative is not its expression but its transformation. Thibault's "wild fits of rage" (005-040) are described as "fits only worthy of Polichinello" — a puppet-show passion, exaggerated and absurd. But "his anger was in an instant converted into something worse" (005-042) when supernatural forces intervene. This conversion — from human anger to demonic instrument — is the novel's central psychological mechanism. Anger becomes the doorway through which the wolf enters: not the sin itself, but the openness to sin that unexamined rage creates.

## Key Examples

- **The Lord of Vez's angry look** (002-028): "The Lord of Vez cast an angry look at Thibault." The Baron's anger is pre-verbal, a facial expression that carries the weight of feudal authority — a single look can condemn.

- **The Lord of Vez's rage** (002-060): "The Lord of Vez was in a state of rage that we will not even endeavour to describe." The Baron's anger at the poaching is depicted as aristocratic excess, a privilege of power that stands in stark contrast to Thibault's suppressed fury.

- **Marcotte's outburst** (002-076): When the Baron calls his dogs "trash," Marcotte draws himself "up to his full height" and answers vehemently — anger as the breaking point of feudal deference, a rare moment when professional pride overrides hierarchical submission.

- **Thibault's anger subsides** (003-070): "Thibault's anger subsided at once before the soft words of the young girl." Agnelette's intervention temporarily quells his rage, showing anger as a manageable emotion when met with compassion — a possibility the novel ultimately forecloses.

- **Thibault's wild fits** (005-040–042): "Giving way to one of his wild fits of rage, fits only worthy of Polichinelle... his anger was in an instant converted into something worse." The transformation of anger into something malevolent marks the wolf's intervention, showing anger as the emotional raw material the supernatural feeds upon.

- **Landry's angry astonishment** (007-022): "You laugh? My trouble makes you laugh?" Landry's anger at Thibault's callous response to his distress shows anger as the natural response to being mocked in one's suffering.

- **Madame Polet's fury** (007-045): "Madame Polet flew into a great rage; called me an insolent beggar." The widow's class-privileged anger at Thibault's presumption mirrors the structural dynamic: anger from above is righteous indignation; anger from below is insolence.

- **The widow's explosive fury** (008-045): "What! cried the widow, whose fury was the greater for having been kept waiting." Anger compounded by wounded pride and social station.

- **Thibault flees from anger** (009-001): Thibault, fleeing Madame Polet's threats, "turned instinctively towards the forest." Anger from the powerful literally drives the powerless into the wilderness — the geographic displacement mirrors the spiritual displacement to come.

- **Anger toward the Bailiff's wife** (012-093): "A violent feeling of anger towards the Bailiff's wife took possession of Thibault." The passive construction — anger "took possession" of him — prefigures the supernatural possession that follows, blurring the line between Thibault's own emotions and the forces that control him.

- **The wolf's rage** (011-074): "Rising in fury to his feet again, with the shock of a wild boar..." The lycanthropic rage that follows the transformation, where anger becomes literally animalistic.
