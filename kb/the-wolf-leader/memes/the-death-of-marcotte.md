## Context

Marcotte is the Baron of Vez's pricker — the huntsman responsible for tracking and locating game before the hunt. Introduced in the novel's opening pages, he is a figure of professional competence and feudal loyalty. When he reports trouble locating a wolf that has "employed himself so well all night crossing his track and doubling," the Baron threatens to have him "tied to the nearest tree" [001-015], establishing both Marcotte's subordinate status and the Baron's tyrannical temperament.

## Event Summary

During a hunt, [[thibault|Thibault]] makes his first wish under the [[the-devils-pact|devil's pact]]: that the stag the hunters are chasing should cross the river rather than stay on the near bank where the Baron can kill it. The wish is granted — the stag swims across. But in pursuing it, the pricker [[marcotte|Marcotte]] is swept away by the current and drowns.

This is the first death directly caused by [[thibault|Thibault]]'s wishes — a death he did not intend but which follows inexorably from his interference with natural events. The wish was harmless in itself (redirect a stag), but its secondary effects proved fatal to an innocent man.

## Detailed Account

The hunt proceeds normally until [[thibault|Thibault]], watching from concealment, sees the stag headed toward the Baron's position. Motivated by petty malice — the Baron had him flogged — [[thibault|Thibault]] voices the wish aloud. The black wolf (the devil's emissary) acknowledges it, and the stag immediately veers toward the river.

Marcotte, following the hunt-craft that is his profession, pursues the stag into the water. The current proves stronger than anticipated. The experienced huntsman, who has tracked game through every season and terrain, is pulled under. His body is later recovered downstream.

The news of Marcotte's drowning triggers the Baron's apoplectic fit — a seizure of rage and shock at losing his trusted pricker. The Baron's reaction compounds the tragedy: the feudal lord's fury at losing a useful servant outweighs any grief for the man himself.

## Key Participants

- **[[thibault|Thibault]]** — The unwitting cause. His wish, motivated by [[revenge]] against the Baron, sets in motion consequences he never anticipated. This establishes the pattern of all subsequent wishes: desire achieved at a hidden cost.
- **Marcotte** — The victim. A skilled professional huntsman, loyal to his feudal lord, whose death is the collateral damage of supernatural interference in natural events.
- **[[baron-of-vez|The Baron of Vez]]** — The indirect target of [[thibault|Thibault]]'s wish, who loses his pricker to the river. His apoplectic reaction reveals the transactional nature of feudal relationships — Marcotte is mourned as lost property, not as a human being.

## Consequences

Marcotte's death is the first link in a chain of unintended fatalities. Each subsequent wish produces consequences [[thibault|Thibault]] cannot foresee, but Marcotte's drowning is uniquely important because it happens without [[thibault|Thibault]] understanding the mechanism. He wished for the stag to cross the river — how could that kill a man? The answer (the stag's path drew Marcotte into dangerous water) teaches [[thibault|Thibault]] nothing, because he never fully connects cause and effect.

The death also reveals the disproportion between [[thibault|Thibault]]'s grievances and his weapons. A flogging — however humiliating — does not justify a man's death. But [[thibault|Thibault]]'s supernatural power operates without moral calibration; it delivers what is wished for, not what is deserved.

## Narrative Significance

Marcotte's drowning establishes the novel's moral physics. Every wish has consequences. No wish is safe. The mechanism is not punitive — the devil does not *add* suffering to the wishes — but structural: [[thibault|Thibault]]'s interference with the natural order inevitably injures innocent bystanders because the natural order is a web of interdependence. Redirect one thread, and threads elsewhere snap.

This death also marks the point of no return. After Marcotte, [[thibault|Thibault]] has blood on his hands whether he acknowledges it or not. His subsequent wishes become morally different in kind — they are no longer experiments with unknown consequences but repetitions of a pattern whose deadly logic has already been demonstrated.
