## Definition

In *The Wolf-Leader*, truth operates as a moral axis around which character and narrative consequence rotate. Characters are measured by their relationship to truth: Agnelette's "truthfulness was in such contrast to his falsehood" (003-070) establishes the novel's core moral calibration. Truth is not merely factual accuracy — though it is also that, as when huntsmen deduce from physical evidence "what is a truth, what is the fact" (002-082) — but a quality of soul that determines which characters are aligned with the novel's moral order and which are estranged from it.

## Role in the Novel

The novel constructs a spectrum of truth-relationships. At one extreme stands Agnelette, whose instinctive honesty is her defining virtue — "Why not tell me the truth, Monsieur Thibault?" (006-017) is both a question and a moral challenge. Her truthfulness is not strategic but constitutive: she cannot be other than she appears. At the opposite extreme stands Thibault after the pact, whose entire existence becomes a lie — he pretends to be a simple sabot-maker while wielding supernatural power, pretends to love Agnelette while pursuing other desires, pretends to be human while becoming something else.

Between these poles, other characters navigate truth pragmatically. The huntsmen deduce truth from physical evidence — "as sure as it is now daylight" (002-082) — applying an empirical standard that the supernatural events of the novel systematically undermine. The Baron speaks truth when it serves his interests and dissembles when it does not, a privilege of power that Thibault's wishes attempt to replicate but can never match, because the Baron's lies are social while Thibault's are ontological.

The novel's resolution insists on truth's ultimate victory: Thibault's final confession is an act of truth-telling that breaks the pact's hold. "It is not a lie, it is the truth" (007-027) — the simple declarative sentence carries the weight of the entire moral architecture.

## Key Examples

> "Thibault's anger subsided at once before the straightforward attitude of the girl, whose truthfulness was in such contrast to his falsehood." (003-070)

The novel's moral centre stated directly — Agnelette's truthfulness as the standard against which Thibault's progressive corruption is measured.

> "What is a truth, what is the fact, is that there is some witchcraft behind all this." (002-082)

The empirical standard of truth — deduction from evidence — confronts its limit in the supernatural. Truth as fact collides with truth as mystery.

> "Why not tell me the truth, Monsieur Thibault?" (006-017)

Agnelette's question is the novel's moral challenge in miniature. Thibault cannot answer it without undoing himself.
