## Context

The wedding is the culmination of events set in motion by [[thibault|Thibault]]'s own actions. Ha[[wine|vin]]g abandoned [[agnette|Agnelette]] after gaining his diabolical powers, [[thibault|Thibault]] had given her a hollow promise — that he would find her someone to help care for her blind grandmother — never intending to fulfill it [014-018]. The Lady of the [[castle-of-vez|Castle of Vez]], acting as a patroness to the young couple, provides [[agnette|Agnelette]] with her wedding finery: a wreath of orange flowers, tulle veil, and muslin dress [014-017].

The wedding takes place in an atmosphere of incongruous spring-like brightness in the heart of winter — a pale ray of sunlight, a singing bird, and a flowering snowdrop all seem to mock [[thibault|Thibault]]'s inner darkness [014-009].

## Event Summary

[[agnette|Agnelette]]'s wedding to [[engoulevent|Engoulevent]], [[engoulevent|the second huntsman]] of the [[baron-of-vez|Lord of Vez]], takes place as a bright village celebration in late winter [014-012]. The wedding procession passes through the forest of [[villers-cotterets|Villers-Cotterets]], where [[thibault|Thibault]] — hiding in the hollow of an ancient oak — watches in torment [014-013]. [[agnette|Agnelette]] appears radiant and happy, ha[[wine|vin]]g found in [[engoulevent|Engoulevent]] someone who will help her support her blind grandmother [014-018]. For [[thibault|Thibault]], the sight of his former betrothed marrying another man ignites a devastating jealousy that marks a turning point in his moral descent [014-019].

## Detailed Account

The wedding procession approaches through the long lane of [[ham|Ham]], headed by fiddlers playing violins and hautboy. The participants include keepers in the livery of the [[baron-of-vez|Lord of Vez]], [[engoulevent|Engoulevent]] escorting [[agnette|Agnelette]]'s blind grandmother decked in ribbons, and the majordomo of the [[castle-of-vez|Castle of Vez]] gi[[wine|vin]]g his arm to the bride [014-013].

[[thibault|Thibault]] watches from the hollow oak, his flaming hair and fiery eyes concealed. The procession passes within twenty paces of him [014-019]. The music fades; the forest returns to silence. Alone, [[thibault|Thibault]]'s despair erupts — "He bit his fists, he knocked his head against the sides of the tree, and finally began to cry and sob" [014-022]. But these are tears of anger, not of [[sorrow|grief]]; "some of his tears fell visibly adown his face, so it seemed that others fell on his heart within like drops of gall" [014-022].

## Key Participants

- **[[agnette|Agnelette]]** — The bride, "as bright and happy as the bird that was singing," radiating innocent joy [014-016]. Her happiness stems not from passionate love for [[engoulevent|Engoulevent]] but from the security of ha[[wine|vin]]g found someone who will care for her blind grandmother [014-018].
- **[[engoulevent|Engoulevent]]** — The bridegroom, second huntsman to the [[baron-of-vez|Lord of Vez]], who had previously spied on [[thibault|Thibault]] hiding in a tree and discovered his boar-spear [014-013].
- **[[thibault|Thibault]]** — Hidden observer, crouching in the hollow of an ancient oak tree. His reaction transforms from shock to possessive rage to consuming jealousy [014-019].
- **The [[baron-of-vez|Lord of Vez]]** — Represented by his majordomo at the wedding, acting as patron [014-013].

## Consequences

The wedding triggers a catastrophic chain reaction in [[thibault|Thibault]]'s psychology. He con[[wine|vin]]ces himself retroactively that he has never ceased to love [[agnette|Agnelette]] and that [[engoulevent|Engoulevent]] has stolen what rightfully belonged to him [014-021]. This self-deception gives birth to a jealousy "which gnaws at the vitals like the sharpest serpent's tooth, and corrodes the blood like the most destructive poison" [014-019].

More broadly, the wedding crystallizes [[thibault|Thibault]]'s grievance against the social order. He broods at length on the in[[law|justice]] of inequality: "Why should some be born in bondage and others be born to power?" [014-027]. Yet his own experience with diabolical power proves equally hollow — "Each time he had held a good hand, each time he had felt sure of the game, it was the devil after all who had won" [014-031].

## Narrative Significance

The wedding chapter serves as the emotional fulcrum of the novel's middle section. It transforms [[thibault|Thibault]] from an ambitious but bumbling social climber into a man consumed by jealous rage. The scene's structure — the bright, communal celebration observed from darkness by a solitary, excluded figure — embodies the novel's central theme of isolation through evil. [[thibault|Thibault]]'s wolves and owls, his "flock with flaming eyes," have replaced human community [014-001].
