# Master

## Definition

A master (*maître*, *seigneur*) is one who commands — the holder of authority over persons, animals, or circumstances. In *The Wolf-Leader*, the concept of mastery structures every social relationship: the feudal lord is master over his huntsmen; the huntsman is master over his dogs; the [[devil|black wolf]] becomes master over [[thibault|Thibault]]; and bodily needs like sleep and hunger compete for "mastery" over the human will. Master is the position from which commands issue — and the position [[thibault|Thibault]] progressively loses as his transformation advances.

## Role in the Novel

The novel's social world is organized by mastery. The [[baron-of-vez|Lord of Vez]] is "the master" to his huntsmen: servants act "at his master's command" (002-066), dogs recognize their "master approach" (009-010), and the servant class diverts "their master's ill-humour" (002-110). [[thibault|Thibault]]'s original master was "a very kindhearted man" (001-047), establishing the norm of benevolent mastery he later loses. The social catastrophe of the novel is registered in the servants' panic over "their master's health" (006-001), their desperate remedies to "bring their master back to life" (005-044), and the relief when "our dear master is saved!" (005-046). Against this feudal mastery stands the diabolical mastery of the [[black-wolf|devil]] and the biological mastery of "midnight fatigue" that "gained the mastery over me" (P02-226) — forces that make even the feudal lord subject to higher powers.

## Key Examples

> "Midnight fatigue gained the mastery over me" (P02-226) — the prologue's acknowledgment that even human will yields to bodily need.

> "Knew by experience that his master was a very kindhearted man" (001-047) — the lost norm of benevolent feudal mastery.

> "Marcotte, at his master's command, dismounted to see what it was" (002-066) — the servant's reflexive obedience.

> "Delighted to divert their master's ill-humour from himself" (002-110) — servant psychology under feudal mastery.

> "Their affection for their master was fatal to the poor pricker" (005-042) — loyalty to the master drives the plot's central injustice.

> "Oh! my friends! now our dear master is saved!" (005-046) — the emotional economy of feudal dependency.

> "The dogs on seeing their master approach" (009-010) — mastery extending across species, the hunt's hierarchy.
