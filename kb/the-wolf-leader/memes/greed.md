## Definition

Greed in *The Wolf-Leader* is the raw, consuming hunger that cannot be satisfied — the psychological mechanism by which each granted wish breeds not contentment but a larger appetite. Distinct from [[ambition]] (social aspiration toward status) and [[desire]] (the general category of wanting), greed is specifically the *escalation* of wanting: the dynamic whereby satisfaction recedes faster than fulfillment approaches.

The wolf exploits greed as its primary tool. Thibault's first wish is for food — a basic need. Each subsequent wish expands in scope and moral cost. The pattern reveals greed's logic: the more one gets, the more one needs, because greed feeds on its own satisfaction.

## Role in the Novel

Greed structures Thibault's wish-trajectory. His initial wishes are modest — the buck that would feed him, the revenge on the Baron that would restore his dignity. But each granted wish inflames rather than extinguishes his appetite. The wolf understands this mechanism [008-016]; it offers wishes knowing that satisfaction is temporary and that each fulfilled desire creates a larger void.

The novel traces this escalation through Thibault's social climbing. He begins wanting food, then a woman, then wealth, then power over others, then the capacity to kill at will. At each stage, the previous wish's fulfillment becomes the baseline for the next demand. By the final chapters, Thibault's greed has become cosmic in scope — he wants nothing less than dominion over life and death.

This escalation serves the novel's moral argument: greed is not a fixed quantity but a dynamic process. It is less like a hunger that can be fed and more like a fire that grows by consuming.

## Key Examples

Thibault's internal monologue after receiving the buck reveals greed's rationalization mechanism: "The devil who helps one is certainly better worth than the angel who forsakes one" [004-009]. Having received one supernatural gift, he immediately calculates how to profit further — selling the buck to the nuns, buying a wedding dress, securing Agnelette. The buck is not enough; it is merely the first installment.

The progression from need to greed is marked by Thibault's changing attitude toward his wishes. Early wishes are defensive (escape punishment, secure food); later wishes are acquisitive (take the widow from Landry, claim power over the Baron). The threshold between need and greed is crossed invisibly, and the novel's tragedy is that Thibault never recognizes the crossing.

## Author's Treatment

Dumas treats greed as a structural principle rather than a character trait. It is not that Thibault *is* greedy; it is that the wish-granting mechanism *produces* greed in anyone who uses it. The wolf-pact is thus a parable about the psychology of unlimited desire — a critique that extends beyond the supernatural frame to comment on human nature under any system that promises infinite satisfaction.
