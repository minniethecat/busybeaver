# Marriage

## Definition

In *The Wolf-Leader*, marriage (*le mariage*) functions as the social institution that most clearly marks the boundary between legitimate and illegitimate [[wish|desire]]. [[thibault|Thibault]]'s [[escape|pursuit]] of women — [[agnette|Agnelette]], the widow of [[croyolles|Croyolles]], [[countess|the Countess]] — repeatedly encounters the obstacle of marriage, whether existing, proposed, or strategically avoided. Marriage in the novel is never merely a romantic arrangement; it is always an economic and social transaction, one that [[thibault|Thibault]], as a propertyless peasant, is structurally disadvantaged from entering on favorable terms.

Dumas uses marriage as a diagnostic tool: every character's attitude toward marriage reveals their moral character. [[agnette|Agnelette]] values the marriage [[oath|vow]] above material advantage; the Bailiff treats marriage as serial consumption; [[thibault|Thibault]] views it as a vehicle for social [[ambition|climbing]]. The institution itself is neutral — it is what characters bring to it that determines its moral weight.

## Role in the Novel

Marriage appears at three critical narrative junctures, each revealing a different aspect of the institution's social function. The novel opens with a reference to royal marriage — the remarriage of His Royal Highness (001-007) — establishing immediately that marriage, at the highest social level, is a matter of state. This frames all subsequent marriages in the novel as transactions within a hierarchy.

[[thibault|Thibault]]'s own attitude toward marriage is f[[class|rank]]ly instrumental. When he considers pursuing the widow of [[croyolles|Croyolles]], the possibility of marriage is weighed against its cost:

> "This would not hardly upset his plans of marriage" (006-049).

The negative construction is revealing. Marriage is a "plan," not a [[wish|desire]] — a strategic calculation, not an emotional commitment. The widow's wealth makes marriage worth considering; her person makes it tolerable. This is the logic of the social climber, and [[thibault|Thibault]] applies it with the same cold pragmatism he brings to poaching.

The [[magloire|Bailiff Magloire]]'s marital history provides the novel's most explicit commentary on marriage as institution:

> "Magloire had no fear in entering on a fifth marriage" (011-045).

The ordinal — "fifth" — does the work. Marriage, for the Bailiff, is not a sacrament but a renewable resource. He consumes wives as he consumes anything else, and the law permits it. Dumas's tone is coolly observational: this is how the world works, and the world's workings are not pretty.

The Keeper's marriage, by contrast, provides the novel's most positive image of the institution:

> "He who, in consideration of his marriage, had been raised to the rank of Keeper" (021-011).

Here marriage is portrayed as an avenue of [[ambition|advancement]] — but notably, it is [[ambition|advancement]] through recognition, not predation. The Keeper earned his position through the social legitimacy that marriage conferred, not through the supernatural shortcuts that [[thibault|Thibault]] pursues. The contrast is structural: legitimate institutions reward patience and service; the wolf rewards appetite and transgression.

The novel's most poignant reference to marriage comes in [[agnette|Agnelette]]'s words:

> "She desired no dowry beyond my marriage vow" (021-066).

This is the novel's moral standard for marriage — a bond valued for itself, not for its material advantages. [[agnette|Agnelette]]'s position is not naivety but a different kind of wisdom: she understands that a marriage valued for its dowry is a marriage already poisoned. [[thibault|Thibault]], who can only value things by what they yield, cannot comprehend this — and his incomprehension is his tragedy.

## Historical and Folkloric Background

Marriage in the *ancien régime* was emphatically not a private arrangement between individuals. It was a contract that transferred property, consolidated alliances, and reproduced [[class|social hierarchy]]. Among the [[peasant|peasantry]], marriage required the consent of the seigneur in many jurisdictions; the *droit de cuissage* (or *droit du seigneur*), though its historical reality is debated, persisted in folklore as a symbol of aristocratic sexual privilege over peasant brides.

The Church's regulation of marriage — its status as a sacrament, its indissolubility — coexisted uneasily with these economic realities. The tension between marriage as [[soul|spirit]]ual bond and marriage as property transaction runs through French literature from Molière to Balzac. Dumas, writing in the mid-nineteenth century, inherits this [[custom|tradition]] and sharpens it: his characters navigate marriage with full awareness of its dual nature, and their choices reveal their moral orientation.

## Author's Treatment

Dumas treats marriage with characteristic anti-sentimentality. There are no weddings in *The Wolf-Leader* — no celebrations, no festivities, no romantic consummations. Marriage appears only as prospect, threat, or memory. This absence is deliberate: in a novel structured around appetite and predation, the stability that marriage represents has no place.

The novel's most striking absence is the marriage that does not happen — between [[thibault|Thibault]] and [[agnette|Agnelette]]. [[thibault|Thibault]]'s [[escape|pursuit]] of other women, his supernatural ambitions, and his moral deterioration foreclose the possibility of a marriage that might have redeemed him. The marriage that never was becomes the novel's silent indictment: [[thibault|Thibault]] chose power over love, and the choice was final.

Dumas's treatment of the Bailiff's serial marriages is equally pointed. The comedic tone — "he had no fear in entering on a fifth marriage" — masks a structural critique: a [[law|legal system]] that permits the powerful to consume spouses with impunity is a system in need of reform. The Bailiff's marriages are legal; [[thibault|Thibault]]'s [[wish|desire]]s are not. But the novel quietly suggests that the difference is one of degree, not kind.
