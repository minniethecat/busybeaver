## Definition

In *The Wolf-Leader*, the term "beast" oscillates between its literal meaning — wild animals of the forest — and its symbolic weight as the embodiment of primal, untamed nature. The novel deploys "beast" across a spectrum: from the hunted wolves that terrorize the countryside, to the "poor beasts" of burden, to the philosophical question of what distinguishes the human from the animal. The beast represents both external threat and internal potential — the creature that can be hunted and killed, and the instinct that can take residence within a man.

## Role in the Novel

The beast appears first through the villagers' fear of the great black wolf. Mocquet, the gamekeeper, defines the quarry with folk precision: the wolf is a "wily beast" — "cunning, treacherous, and deceitful" (P02-077). This characterization, repeated across multiple passages (P02-068–079), establishes the wolf not merely as animal but as adversary possessing almost human intelligence.

When Thibault encounters the wolf that speaks and bargains, the boundary between man and beast dissolves. The narrator's description of a trapped female wolf "with all the strength of which a beast of her kind is capable" resisting capture (004-002) foreshadows Thibault's own futile resistance against his transformation. The most philosophically significant passage comes when Thibault protests his innocence through the lens of animal harm: "I, kill a poor beast! and, my God, with what? Where would be the blood?" (002-113). This ironic self-defense — denying he could kill a beast — rings hollow as he gradually becomes one.

The beast motif reaches its culmination in the novel's central inversion: the hunter becomes the hunted, the man becomes the beast. What began as a wolf in the forest ends as a wolf in the soul.

### The Hunter's Relationship to the Beast

The opening pages establish the Baron de Vez as the forest's predator-in-chief, but the relationship is complex. The Baron's ferocity is described with a telling qualification: "his bark was worse than his bite" (001-005). This canine metaphor applied to the human hunter immediately blurs the species line — the Baron is himself a kind of beast, territorial and fierce. The hunt is not a simple contest of man against animal but a ritualized expression of power where the participants begin to resemble one another.

Thibault's pursuit of the wounded buck enacts this blurring in miniature. When he corners the animal, he sees "the trembling animal, quivering in every limb, standing a few paces in front of him, gazing at him with intelligent and terrified eyes" (002-001). The buck's "intelligent" gaze establishes a moral equivalence — the animal is not merely quarry but a fellow creature capable of fear and awareness. Thibault's hesitation before the kill is the novel's first suggestion that the boundary between hunter and hunted is permeable.

### The Vanishing Beast

The supernatural aspect of "beast" emerges most powerfully through disappearance. When the hunted animals vanish without trace — the buck that "must have flown into the clouds or disappeared in the bowels of the earth" (002-080) — the novel signals that the forest operates by rules beyond natural hunting logic. The Baron's frustrated exclamation that "in the forest of Villers-Cotterets the deer burrow like foxes, and men perch on trees like crows" (002-091) inverts the natural order: deer behave like foxes, and men (Thibault) like birds. The beast that cannot be caught becomes the beast that catches — the wolf, nature's apex predator, waiting to turn the hunter into prey.

### The Beast Within

The novel's deepest treatment of "beast" is internal. When Thibault curses the deer — "In the name of God or of the Devil, I want you and I will have you, cursed animal!" (002-058) — the curse itself reveals his transformation. Invoking both God and the Devil in the same breath, he has already begun to blur the sacred and profane. The "cursed animal" he pursues is, in the novel's symbolic logic, himself. Each failed kill brings him closer to the beast he hunts, until the distinction collapses entirely.

## Key Examples

> "cunning, treacherous, and deceitful, in short, a wily beast." (P02-077)

Mocquet's definition of the wily beast serves double duty — describing the wolf's nature while inadvertently characterizing the qualities Thibault himself will develop through his pact.

> "I, kill a poor beast! and, my God, with what?" (002-113)

Thibault's rhetorical question marks the dramatic irony at the novel's core — the man who protests he cannot kill an animal will become one.

> "the trembling animal, quivering in every limb... gazing at him with intelligent and terrified eyes" (002-001)

The buck's gaze establishes moral equivalence between hunter and hunted, foreshadowing the inversion to come.

> "the damned animal must have flown into the clouds or disappeared in the bowels of the earth" (002-080)

The vanishing beast signals the supernatural logic that governs the forest — the hunt becomes a metaphysical contest, not merely a physical one.

> "In the name of God or of the Devil, I want you and I will have you, cursed animal!" (002-058)

Thibault's curse reveals his internal transformation — the hunter who invokes both sacred and profane has already lost the moral clarity that distinguishes man from beast.
