# Peasant

## Definition

In *The Wolf-Leader*, the peasant (*le paysan*) is not simply a demographic category but the moral and social ground against which all action is measured. The peasantry of the *ancien régime* — the roughly ninety percent of the French population who worked the land and owned little of it — provides the novel its characters, its conflicts, and its most searing critique. [[thibault|Thibault]] is a peasant; [[agnette|Agnelette]] is a peasant; their neighbors, their friends, their antagonists among the lower classes are all peasants. The Baron is not.

The novel's treatment of the peasantry is ambivalent. Peasants are depicted with the sympathy of Dumas's own republican convictions, yet they are not sentimentalized. They can be generous ([[agnette|Agnelette]]) or grasping ([[thibault|Thibault]]), courageous or cowardly, shrewd or superstitious. The point is that they are fully human — and the social order that treats them as less than human is therefore monstrous.

## Role in the Novel

The peasant condition is established in the novel's first substantive chapter. The narrator describes the forest's forbidding aspect, noting that the peasants passing by in summer "hastened their steps" (001-003) — a detail that establishes both the forest's menace and the peasant's habitual fear.

The class divide is rendered in precise economic terms. [[thibault|Thibault]]'s [[wood|sabot-making]] brings him a precarious li[[wine|vin]]g. His journeyman travels have given him [[ambition|aspiration]]s that his [[class|station]] cannot satisfy — he "had certainly had [[dream|dreams]] of being something other than a peasant" (001-039). This gap between [[ambition|aspiration]] and [[class|station]] is the crack through which the [[devil|black wolf]] enters.

The [[hunt|hunting]] laws — which reserve game for the nobility and punish poaching with brutal severity — are the novel's most concrete illustration of class oppression. When [[thibault|Thibault]] protests that game should "belong to everybody, to the peasant just as much as to the great man" (003-066), he is voicing a revolutionary sentiment that was already stirring in the France of the 1780s. His punishment — a public flogging — [[devil|demon]]strates the violence that upholds the class order.

The novel's most subversive observation is that the peasant's [[wish|desire]] for social [[ambition|advancement]] is treated as a moral failing, while the noble's assumption of superiority requires no justification at all. [[thibault|Thibault]] is condemned for wanting what the Baron takes for granted.

## Key Examples

The narrator's anthropological observation of peasant [[faith|belief]]s:

> "The belief in werewolves was still widespread among the peasantry in the country districts" (P02-051).

[[thibault|Thibault]]'s critique of class privilege, grounded in natural law:

> "Game should belong to everybody, to the peasant just as much as to the great man" (003-066).

The peasant's habitual deference to [[power|authority]], even when [[power|authority]] is absurd:

> "Fiddlers; then followed a few peasants, and among them some figures that Thibault recognized" (014-013).

The Count's contemptuous dismissal, revealing the chasm between classes:

> "What pigs these peasants are! I wonder how they can live in such hovels!" (016-006).

The final hunt reverses the social order: [[thibault|Thibault]], now a werewolf, is "hunted down by the lowest peasants, whom formerly I despised" (023-009). His reversal is complete — he who sought to rise above his class is brought down by it.

## Historical and Folklore Background

The French peasantry of the 1780s lived under the *seigneurial system*, paying rents, tithes, and feudal dues to the nobility and clergy. The [[hunt|hunting]] privileges of the nobility — the *droit de [[hunt|chasse]]* — were among the most resented because they reserved a critical food source for the leisure class. [[poaching|Poaching]] was both a practical necessity for many peasants and a form of quiet resistance.

Dumas, the son of a Revolutionary general (Thomas-Alexandre Dumas) and the grandson of a Haitian slave, was acutely aware of class hierarchies and their in[[law|justice]]s. His novels repeatedly stage conflicts between individual merit and inherited privilege. *The Wolf-Leader* (1857) was written during the Second Empire, when many of the Revolution's egalitarian gains had been reversed — making its critique of aristocratic privilege both historically framed and politically current.

## Author's Treatment

Dumas's treatment of the peasantry in *The Wolf-Leader* is notable for its refusal of two competing stereotypes: the virtuous peasant of pastoral convention and the brutish peasant of aristocratic satire. [[thibault|Thibault]] is intelligent but not wise; [[agnette|Agnelette]] is good but not simple; the villagers are credulous but not stupid.

The novel's formal innovation is to make a peasant the protagonist of a Gothic horror story — a genre typically populated by nobles and castles. By locating the supernatural in a sabot-maker's hut rather than a baron's dungeon, Dumas democratizes the Gothic. The horror is not that a nobleman is damned (a [[black-wolf|familiar]] trope) but that a peasant, with his modest ambitions and legitimate grievances, can be seduced into evil one wish at a time.

The werewolf transformation is, in this reading, a metaphor for what the class system does to those it humiliates: it turns them into monsters. But Dumas is too honest a writer to let the metaphor become an excuse. [[thibault|Thibault]] is offered alternatives — love, work, the ordinary consolations of peasant life — and chooses otherwise. Class is context, not destiny.
