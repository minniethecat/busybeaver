# Village

A village (*village*) is a rural settlement — smaller than a town, organized around agriculture and artisanal trades, and bound by ties of kinship, custom, and feudal obligation. In *The Wolf-Leader*, the village represents the world Thibault inhabits before his transformation: a world of material scarcity, communal surveillance, and fixed social place.

## Thibault's Village World

Thibault is a sabot-maker — a rural artisan whose trade places him above the landless peasant but far below the seigneur. His village is defined by its relationship to the forest: the villagers depend on forest resources (wood for sabot-making, game for food) yet live in fear of the wolves that the forest harbors. The Baron's hunt, which opens the novel, is framed as a defense of the villages against the wolf menace [001-003].

The village is also the site of Thibault's humiliations. It is in the village that his poverty is visible, that his low status is enacted through the deference he must show to the Baron and his huntsmen, and that his desire for [[agnette|Agnelette]] — a village girl herself — becomes entangled with his ambition to rise above his station.

## The Village as Moral Community

Dumas presents the village as a moral community with its own norms and judgments. The village disapproves of Thibault's transformation — not because the villagers understand its supernatural origin, but because his new wealth and arrogance violate the communal expectation of humility. The village operates through gossip, observation, and collective memory: nothing escapes its notice, and Thibault's changes do not go unremarked.

This moral function of the village contrasts with the castle's formal justice. Where the [[baron-of-vez|Baron]] dispenses punishment by legal authority, the village dispenses judgment by social exclusion. Thibault loses his place in both systems.

## The Village Church

The village church serves as the spiritual center of the community. It is where baptisms, weddings, and funerals mark the life cycle, and where the [[curate|curate]] exercises moral authority that sometimes rivals the seigneur's. The church bell that tolls across the valley is the acoustic boundary of village identity — those who hear it belong; those beyond its range are strangers.

## Key Passages

Significant village scenes appear throughout the novel:

- The widow Polet's cottage as a site of [[grief]] and [[poverty]]
- The village wedding of [[agnette|Agnelette]] to [[engoulevent|Engoulevent]]
