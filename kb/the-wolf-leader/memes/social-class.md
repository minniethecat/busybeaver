# Social Class

Social class (*classe sociale*, *condition*) is the division of society into hierarchical strata based on birth, wealth, occupation, and privilege. In *The Wolf-Leader*, class is the primary determinant of character and plot: it dictates who may hunt and who may not, who commands and who obeys, who is believed and who is punished.

## The Three Estates in the Novel

Dumas's novel, though set in the late eighteenth century, depicts a society still organized on the model of the three estates. The **First Estate** (clergy) is represented by the [[curate|curate]], whose authority is spiritual but whose social position is modest. The **Second Estate** (nobility) is represented by the [[baron-of-vez|Baron of Vez]], the Count of Mont-Gobert, and — at the apex — the [[duke-of-orleans|Duke of Orléans]]. The **Third Estate** (everyone else) encompasses [[thibault|Thibault]] the artisan, the peasants of the village, and the servants and retainers who staff the noble households.

The novel is attentive to gradations within the Third Estate. Thibault, as a skilled sabot-maker, occupies a higher position than a landless laborer — but this distinction matters little when he is confronted with noble authority. The flogging reduces him to a generic body available for punishment, erasing the distinctions of skill and respectability that define his identity within the village.

## Class and Mobility

Thibault's story is, among other things, a study in the impossibility of class mobility under the *ancien régime*. His supernatural powers allow him to acquire wealth and influence, but they cannot change his birth. The nobles who benefit from his wishes do not accept him as an equal; the villagers who witness his rise resent rather than admire him. Class, the novel suggests, is not merely economic but social and psychological — it inheres in bearing, speech, and the network of relationships that no amount of money can purchase.

The [[devil|devil]]'s bargain is thus a cruel joke: it gives Thibault the material trappings of higher status while denying him the social recognition that would make that status real.

## Class and Justice

The novel's treatment of justice is explicitly class-based. Thibault is flogged for a minor infraction, while the Baron's transgressions — which include arbitrary violence — go unpunished. The feudal system does not pretend to equality before the law; it is built on the principle that different orders of society are subject to different standards. Thibault's grievance is not merely personal but structural: the system is designed to protect the powerful and discipline the weak.
