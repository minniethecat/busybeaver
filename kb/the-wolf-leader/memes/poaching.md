# Poaching

## Definition

Poaching (*le braconnage*) — the illegal taking of game from lands owned by the nobility — was among the most fiercely prosecuted crimes of the *ancien régime*. In *The Wolf-Leader*, poaching is the catalyst that sets the entire plot in motion: [[thibault|Thibault]]'s attempt to kill a deer be[[wish|longing]] to the Duke of Orleans leads to his public flogging, which in turn fuels the resentment that makes him receptive to the [[devil|black wolf]]'s diabolical offer.

## Role in the Novel

Poaching is [[thibault|Thibault]]'s original sin — not merely a legal transgression but a marker of his social position and his refusal to accept it. The game belongs to the nobility by law and custom; [[thibault|Thibault]]'s claim that "the game belongs to everybody, to the peasant just as much as to the great lords" is both a statement of natural [[law|justice]] and a declaration of social war.

The Baron's response to [[thibault|Thibault]]'s poaching is swift and brutal: thirty-six lashes, apportioned between [[oath|perjury]] and poaching as "God's portion must be the largest" (P02-146). The punishment is public, humiliating, and carefully calibrated to rein[[violence|force]] the social order that poaching threatens.

[[thibault|Thibault]]'s defense — that he killed neither "buck, nor doe, neither goat nor kidling" — is technically true but legally irrelevant. What matters is not the act but the intention: the boar-spear in his hand, the deer tracks he was following, the social presumption of a peasant who dares hunt like a nobleman.

The poaching incident establishes the novel's central social dynamic: the collision between [[thibault|Thibault]]'s limitless ambition and the rigid hierarchies of the *ancien régime*. The [[devil|black wolf]]'s wish-granting offers [[thibault|Thibault]] what the [[law|legal system]] denies him — a way to circumvent the social order. But the circumvention is itself a deeper entanglement in cosmic rather than merely [[class|social hierarchy]].

## Key Examples

[[thibault|Thibault]]'s rationalization of poaching reveals his social philosophy:

> "Does not the game belong to everybody, to the peasant just as much as to the great lords?"

The Baron's sentencing formula intertwines crime and sin:

> "You have been poaching, which is a serious crime; you have perjured yourself, which is a great sin" (P02-146).

A poacher's self-description:

> He "spent the remainder of my time with poaching" (P02-147).

## Historical and Folkloric Background

Poaching was endemic in 18th-century France, where game laws reserved [[hunt|hunting]] rights to the nobility and imposed draconian penalties on commoners. The *Capitainerie des Chasses* en[[violence|force]]d these laws with paramilitary [[violence|force]], and the sight of peasants hanged for poaching was not uncommon. The in[[law|justice]] of these laws — where a deer's life could cost a man's — was a grievance that fed into revolutionary sentiment.

Dumas, writing in 1857 with the French Revolution in li[[wine|vin]]g memory, treats poaching not merely as crime but as symptom. [[thibault|Thibault]]'s poaching is both illegal and, in the novel's moral economy, understandable — which makes his subsequent descent into supernatural evil all the more tragic. He begins with a just grievance and ends as a monster.

## Author's Treatment

Dumas uses poaching as a narrative hinge between social realism and supernatural horror. The opening chapters are grounded in the material realities of rural poverty and aristocratic privilege; poaching is the bridge from this world to the world of the [[devil|black wolf]]. By making [[thibault|Thibault]]'s first crime comprehensible and even sympathetic, Dumas complicates the reader's moral response to his [[soul|damnation]].
