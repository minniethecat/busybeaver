## Context

[[agnette|Agnelette]] has died of consumption, her health broken by the chain of misfortunes set in motion by [[thibault|Thibault]]'s actions. The funeral takes place during the climactic final hunt, when the [[baron-of-vez|Baron of Vez]]'s full pack of hounds is pursuing [[thibault|Thibault]] in his wolf form through the forest [024-003]. By a desperate instinct for survival, [[thibault|Thibault]] has taken refuge in the cemetery — only to find himself an unwilling witness to the burial of the woman whose death he has caused.

The funeral is conducted according to the local custom: "when a young girl, or young married woman, dies in the fullness of her youth and beauty, to carry her to the graveyard in an open coffin, with only a pall over her, so that her friends may bid her a last farewell, her relations give her a last kiss" [024-008].

## Event Summary

From his hiding place beneath a bramble bush in the cemetery at [[preciamont|Préciamont]], [[thibault|Thibault]] — now in his werewolf form, hunted by the [[baron-of-vez|Baron of Vez]]'s hounds — watches the burial of [[agnette|Agnelette]] [024-006]. Her open coffin, borne on a bier covered with a white pall and scattered with green branches and flowers, is laid beside a newly-dug grave [024-007]. As the blind grandmother gives the dead girl a last kiss and the hammer drives nails into the coffin, [[thibault|Thibault]] experiences for the first time a [[sorrow|grief]] free of self-interest: "a genuine and overpowering [[grief|sorrow]], the more poignant since for the first time for many long months he forgot to think of himself, and thought only of the dead woman, now lost to him forever" [024-008].

## Detailed Account

The funeral procession is small and humble: a child with holy water, a man with a silver cross, a priest chanting psalms, four peasants carrying the bier, and a few villagers from [[preciamont|Préciamont]] [024-006]. The open coffin reveals [[agnette|Agnelette]] beneath a wreath of forget-me-nots and daisies — flowers of remembrance and innocence [024-008].

After the coffin is nailed shut and lowered into the ground, the sound of the falling earth becomes for [[thibault|Thibault]] an almost physical [[violence|assault]]. His cry to God — "take my life, I give it gladly" — is overheard by a peasant, and becomes part of the local [[custom|tradition]] surrounding the event [024-016].

The aftermath is recorded in folklore: "every year on the anniversary of [[agnette|Agnelette]]'s death, up to the time when the Monasteries were all abolished at the Revolution, a monk from the Abbey of the Premonstratensians at [[bourg-fontaine|Bourg-Fontaine]], which stands half a league from [[preciamont|Préciamont]], was seen to come and pray beside her grave" [024-017].

## Key Participants

- **[[agnette|Agnelette]]** — The deceased, "as she lay there so pale in death, wrapped in an ineffable calm, appeared more beautiful than when in life, beneath her wreath of forget-me-nots and daisies" [024-008].
- **[[thibault|Thibault]]** — Hidden witness in wolf form. His reaction marks the novel's moral turning point: tears fall from his "fierce bloodred eyes" and he cries out to God [024-010].
- **The blind grandmother** — Led to the coffin for a final kiss, her blindness sparing her the sight of her granddaughter's dead face while intensifying the pathos of the scene [024-008].
- **The priest** — Chanting the psalms for the dead, preceded by a child in a white alb carrying holy water and a man bearing a silver cross [024-006].

## Consequences

The funeral produces the most significant moral transformation in the novel. As the earth and stones fall onto the coffin with "a dull thud," [[thibault|Thibault]] experiences a vertigo of empathy: "The hard stones he thought must be bruising [[agnette|Agnelette]]'s tender flesh, so fresh and sweet but a few days ago" [024-009]. He makes a movement to rush out and snatch the body away, belie[[wine|vin]]g that "dead, must surely belong to him, since, li[[wine|vin]]g, it had belonged to another" [024-009].

But then something unprecedented occurs: "the [[sorrow|grief]] of the man overcame this instinct of the wild beast at bay" [024-010]. [[thibault|Thibault]] — for the first time since acquiring his diabolical powers — prays: "O God! take my life, I give it gladly, if only by my death I may give back life to her whom I have killed!" [024-010].

This prayer of [[sacrifice|self-sacrifice]] becomes the theological resolution of the novel. The priest later declares "that [[thibault|Thibault]], by reason of his [[sacrifice]] and repentance, had been saved" [024-016].

## Narrative Significance

The funeral is the novel's moral and theological climax. Until this moment, [[thibault|Thibault]]'s [[grief|sorrow]] has always been [[pride|self-regard]]ing — [[sorrow|grief]] at his own losses, rage at his own [[shame|humiliation]]s. At [[agnette|Agnelette]]'s graveside, he experiences [[sorrow|grief]] for another for the first time: "he forgot to think of himself" [024-008].

This moment of genuine empathy — the werewolf weeping at a human grave — is what makes [[repentance|salvation]] possible. Dumas constructs a theology in which [[sacrifice]] and repentance, not mere [[faith|belief]], constitute the path to [[repentance|redemption]]. The funeral is the crucible in which [[thibault|Thibault]]'s soul is finally melted and recast.
