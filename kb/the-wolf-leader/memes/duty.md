## Definition

Duty in *The Wolf-Leader* is the web of obligations — social, moral, religious, and feudal — that binds characters to particular courses of action regardless of personal inclination. It is the counterforce to desire, the social mechanism that constrains individual will within the ancien régime's hierarchical order. Unlike the oath (a voluntary, often supernatural pledge) or the law (an external, codified system), duty is internalized obligation — the voice of social expectation that characters hear as conscience.

Duty in the novel is asymmetrical: the duties owed by the weak to the strong are explicit and enforceable; the duties owed by the strong to the weak are discretionary and often honoured only in the breach. This asymmetry makes duty a vehicle for the novel's social critique.

## Role in the Novel

Duty functions as a moral test throughout the narrative. Characters who honour their duties (Agnelette's conjugal duty, the priest's pastoral duty) are aligned with the novel's moral centre. Characters who invoke duty cynically or selectively (the Seigneur's "duty to inform") reveal duty's capacity to serve as a mask for self-interest.

Thibault's tragedy is partly a failure of duty. His duty to Agnelette, to his craft, to his community — all are progressively sacrificed to the pursuit of revenge and power. The wolf's temptation works by reframing the abandonment of duty as an assertion of freedom: why should Thibault honour obligations to a society that has never honoured its obligations to him?

The novel's resolution restores duty as a redemptive possibility. The priest's duty to prepare the dying, Agnelette's duty to her husband even in death, and Thibault's final acceptance of his duty to sacrifice himself — these acts of obligation fulfilled provide the moral closure that the revenge plot cannot.

## Key Examples

- **"It is my duty to inform you"** (001-026): The Seigneur Jean's formulaic invocation of duty as preface to delivering unwelcome news. Duty here is the language of feudal protocol — a social script that dignifies the exercise of power while masking its arbitrariness.

- **"It is my duty to sacrifice my personal pleasure"** (006-004): Thibault's internal resolution to place duty above desire. The moment marks his moral awareness of duty's claim, even as the subsequent plot traces his progressive failure to honour it.

- **Conjugal duty** (012-053): "Some pious sense of conjugal duty had taken her there." The widow's visit to her husband's grave, framed as duty rather than grief. Conjugal duty — the obligations of marriage — persists beyond death, binding the living to the dead.

- **The priest's pastoral duty** (018-013): "'You think then that it is my duty to prepare the poor young man for death?'" The priest's question about his obligation to a dying man. Pastoral duty is presented as a genuine moral burden, not a mere professional formality — the question mark indicates the weight of the decision.

- **Performing a duty vs. committing treason** (022-013–014): The Thibault-wolf's hunting of the Baron's game is framed as "doing his duty" in one context but "committing an act of treason" in another. Duty is contested terrain — the same action can be duty or treason depending on who authorizes it and for whom it is performed.

## Historical Background

In the ancien régime, duty (*devoir*) was the foundational concept of social order. The three estates — clergy, nobility, and commoners — were defined by reciprocal duties: the clergy prayed, the nobility fought, the commoners laboured. This organic model of society, however, masked profound asymmetries of power. The duties of the peasant were enforced by law and violence; the duties of the noble were largely matters of honour, enforced only by reputation.

Dumas, writing in the post-revolutionary nineteenth century, brings a retrospective critical eye to feudal duty. The novel's treatment of duty reflects both nostalgia for a lost order of reciprocal obligation and a clear-eyed recognition that this order was always more ideal than reality.
