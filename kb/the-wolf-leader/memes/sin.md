## Definition

In *The Wolf-Leader*, sin operates as both a theological framework and a narrative gauge of moral transgression. Characters invoke sin to name wrongdoing, measure its gravity, and assert the necessity of expiation. The concept bridges the novel's Catholic cultural backdrop — where "our good Curé himself would say there was no sin in that" [003-028] — with the older folkloric logic of the devil's pact, where sin is not merely broken rule but an ontological debt that must be paid in blood or suffering.

## Role in the Novel

The language of sin pervades the novel's moral discourse. When Agnelette offers to save a man's life with a kiss, she immediately frames the act in theological terms, appealing to the Curé's authority to certify its sinlessness [003-028]. This reflex reveals a world where every significant act is weighed on the scale of sin and expiation.

The concept gains darker resonance as Thibault's bargains accumulate. The devil-pact itself is the archetypal sin — a willing exchange of soul for power. Yet the novel complicates simple moral categories: when Madame Magloire speaks of owing Thibault "some expiation" [011-005], the language of sin and reparation enters the domestic sphere, blurring the line between theological transgression and social debt.

In the climactic confrontation, the phrase "it would be a sin indeed to bring trouble upon you" [013-120] is spoken with ironic understatement — the speaker has already committed far graver sins. This patterning of sin-language across both trivial and profound contexts creates a moral texture in which the reader, like Thibault, must discern which debts can be repaid and which are irredeemable.

## Key Examples

> "I am sure that our good Curé himself would say there was no sin in that." [003-028]

Agnelette's instinctive appeal to clerical authority to sanction a morally ambiguous act reveals the embedded Catholic framework through which even the novel's most innocent character navigates ethical questions.

> "she owed him some expiation" [011-005]

The vocabulary of sin and atonement infiltrates social relations — Madame Magloire's hospitality toward Thibault is couched in the language of moral debt, showing how thoroughly the concept of sin shapes even secular interactions.

> "it would be a sin indeed to bring trouble upon you" [013-120]

Spoken in a context of mortal peril, the word "sin" here functions as a social pledge as much as a theological category — sin is what one does against a friend as well as against God.
