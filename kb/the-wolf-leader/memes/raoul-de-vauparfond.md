## Overview

Raoul de Vauparfond is a young nobleman, the Lord (Baron) of [[vauparfond|Vauparfond]], who appears in the later chapters of the narrative. He is the lover of [[countess|the Countess]] of [[mont-gobert|Mont-Gobert]], and becomes entangled in [[thibault|Thibault]]'s dark transformation when [[thibault|Thibault]]—through supernatural means—inhabits Raoul's body after the latter is fatally injured in a duel [016-007]. This body-inhabitation forms one of the novel's most fantastical episodes (Chapters 15–20).

## Character and Position

Raoul is presented as a gallant young aristocrat. His valet, François Levasseur, describes him with admiration [015-003]. Others remark on his good fortune in love: "My Lord Raoul is a lucky fellow!" [015-022]. He maintains a rivalry with the husband of his mistress—the [[comte-de-mont-gobert|Comte de Mont-Gobert]]—and the narrative treats this love triangle as a source of dramatic tension [015-080–81].

## Affair with the Countess of Mont-Gobert

Raoul's romantic involvement with [[countess|the Countess]] of [[mont-gobert|Mont-Gobert]] drives much of the action in Chapters 15–17. The Countess sends him a letter summoning him to a rendezvous [016-016, 016-023]. He is described as her "dear Raoul" [016-016], and their intimacy is well-known: [[countess|the Countess]] "called him simply 'Raoul'" [016-063]. When pressed to kill the Count, Raoul hesitates—"Are you mad, Raoul? No, no, you must fly"—indicating some moral scruple beneath his romantic recklessness [017-062].

## Duel and Inhabitation by Thibault

The rivalry between Raoul and the Count reaches a crisis point. Raoul is involved in a violent confrontation; his body is found injured after a duel [016-001, 016-007]. [[thibault|Thibault]], who has been granted the power to project his [[soul|spirit]], recalls his wish and enters the Baron's vacant body: "Then [[thibault|Thibault]] recalled the wish... Come now, Monsieur de [[vauparfond|Vauparfond]], do not be so fastidious; carry me along with you" [016-007–08]. From this point onward, [[thibault|Thibault]] walks in Raoul's body, inheriting his physical form while retaining his own consciousness [016-010].

This episode endures for several chapters. The Countess herself notices nothing amiss, belie[[wine|vin]]g she is entertaining her lover Raoul [016-063, 017-013]. The deception unravels only gradually.

## Death

Raoul's body is ultimately carried back to [[vauparfond|Vauparfond]] for burial: "as being borne from thence to [[vauparfond|Vauparfond]], where he was to be laid in the family vault" [020-046]. His death leaves [[countess|the Countess]] grie[[wine|vin]]g—[[thibault|Thibault]] observes the dead body "was that of Raoul de Vauparfond. On his face lay the still, unmo[[wine|vin]]g features of the Baron" [019-008].

## Historical Context

The Curé of [[vauparfond|Vauparfond]], a former priest who had once served the parish, appears briefly in the narrative [018-005], grounding the fictional [[vauparfond|Vauparfond estate]] in the novel's quasi-historical setting of 18th-century rural France.
