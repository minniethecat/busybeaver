# Poverty

Poverty (*pauvreté*) is the condition of lacking sufficient material resources — the daily reality of most inhabitants of Thibault's village. In *The Wolf-Leader*, poverty is not a background detail but the engine of the plot: Thibault's material deprivation drives his susceptibility to the [[devil|devil]]'s offer.

## Poverty and the Devil's Bargain

Thibault is poor. He is a sabot-maker — a skilled trade, but one that provides only subsistence. The novel is precise about the economics: he owns little, saves nothing, and depends on the forest for raw materials that are themselves contested by the seigneurial hunting rights. When the [[devil|devil]] offers him the power to wish, Thibault's first thought is of escape from poverty.

Dumas does not romanticize poverty. The widow Polet's household — bereft of its sole breadwinner when [[landry|Landry]] is conscripted — illustrates poverty's material consequences: hunger, inadequate shelter, the inability to maintain one's place in the community. Poverty in the novel is not ennobling; it is grinding, and it makes people vulnerable.

## The Widow Polet

The widow Polet is the novel's most fully realized portrait of poverty. After her son's conscription, she faces destitution — no income, no protector, no social standing. Her [[grief|cottage]] becomes a site of both material and emotional desolation. Thibault's initial sympathy for her is one of his last genuinely moral impulses before the [[devil|devil]]'s influence corrupts his character.

## Poverty and the Feudal Order

The novel presents poverty as structural, not individual. The peasants are poor because the seigneurial system extracts their surplus — in dues, in labor, in the game they are forbidden to hunt. The [[baron-of-vez|Baron]]'s wealth is the other side of the village's poverty. This is not an abstract critique but a concrete depiction of how the *ancien régime* economy functioned.
