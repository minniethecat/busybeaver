# Pride

## Definition

Pride (*l'orgueil*) in the theological [[custom|tradition]] is the deadliest of the seven deadly sins — the sin of Lucifer, the refusal to accept one's place in the cosmic order. In *The Wolf-Leader*, pride is [[thibault|Thibault]]'s defining character flaw: the conviction that he deserves more than his [[class|station]], that the Baron's punishment is not [[law|justice]] but insult, that the world owes him satisfaction.

## Role in the Novel

[[thibault|Thibault]]'s pride manifests before the supernatural plot begins. He is a sabot-maker who considers himself above [[wood|sabot-making]]; a peasant who believes himself the equal of barons. His travels as a *compagnon* have given him a taste of a wider world, but instead of contentment they have bred resentment — he has seen what others have and believes himself entitled to the same.

The Baron's flogging wounds [[thibault|Thibault]]'s pride more than his body. Physical pain he could endure; it is [[the-flogging|the public humiliation]] — being stripped, tied to a tree, and beaten before the eyes of a peasant girl — that kindles his hatred. His pride transforms a just punishment (he did poach the deer) into an unbearable grievance.

The [[devil|black wolf]] exploits [[thibault|Thibault]]'s pride with surgical precision. Each wish is framed as what [[thibault|Thibault]] "deserves" — the recognition, the wealth, the power that his pride tells him is his due. The devil does not corrupt [[thibault|Thibault]] through fear or temptation but through the validation of his most dangerous self-assessment.

The novel's resolution hinges on pride's incurability. [[thibault|Thibault]] could repent at any moment; the [[devil|black wolf]] never prevents him. But his pride will not permit him to admit that he was wrong, that his grievances were exaggerated, that his hatred was unjustified. Pride becomes the lock on his [[soul|damnation]].

## Key Examples

[[thibault|Thibault]]'s sense of injured merit:

> He had "spent hours at a time watching the beautiful and aristocratic dames" — always measuring, always comparing, always finding himself short-changed.

## Author's Treatment

Dumas treats pride with the ambivalence of a self-made man. The son of a general who rose from the [[class|rank]]s, Dumas understood the value of ambition and the dignity of refusing one's assigned [[class|station]]. [[thibault|Thibault]]'s pride is not wrong because he is a peasant who wants more — it is wrong because his wanting is inseparable from his hatred of others who have what he lacks. The distinction is subtle but crucial: ambition can be noble, but pride that feeds on resentment is poison.
