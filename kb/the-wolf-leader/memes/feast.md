# Feast

A feast (*festin*, *banquet*) is a communal meal marked by abundance, ritual, and social display. In *The Wolf-Leader*, feasting scenes function as barometers of social belonging. Who sits at the table, who is served, and who watches from outside are questions that map precisely onto the novel's hierarchy of power.

## The Wedding Feast

The most significant feast in the novel is the wedding banquet for [[agnette|Agnelette]] and [[engoulevent|Engoulevent]]. This feast is a ritual of communal inclusion from which [[thibault|Thibault]] is excluded. His exclusion is both literal — he is not invited — and symbolic: the feast celebrates a union that represents everything Thibault has lost. Agnelette, whom he desired, marries another man; the community, which he has increasingly alienated, gathers to celebrate without him.

Dumas uses the feast to crystallize Thibault's social death. The sounds of music and laughter that reach him from the banquet hall are the sounds of a world that has closed its doors against him. His revenge against the wedding party, when it comes, is directed not at individuals but at the principle of communal joy from which he has been exiled.

## The Baron's Table

The [[baron-of-vez|Baron]]'s table represents the apex of the feasting hierarchy. At his castle, huntsmen and guests are fed according to rank — the Baron at the head, favored retainers near him, lesser servants at the far end. Thibault, as a village artisan, would never be seated at this table. The Baron's feast is an enactment of the feudal order in edible form.

## Feasting and Famine

The novel contrasts feast with scarcity. The peasants' ordinary diet — bread, cheese, a little wine — is set against descriptions of the Baron's laden table. This contrast is not merely descriptive but polemical: the wealth that sustains the feast is extracted from those who can barely feed themselves. When Thibault, through his devil-granted powers, acquires the means to feast, his consumption is joyless — a private indulgence rather than a communal ritual.
