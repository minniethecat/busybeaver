# Pity

**Pity** in _The Wolf-Leader_ functions as the emotional boundary between human and beast, between redeemable sinner and damned soul. The novel stages scenes of pity — its plea, its granting, its refusal — as moral tests, and Thibault's progressive loss of access to pity marks the arc of his damnation more reliably than any external action.

## The Plea for Pity

The novel's most concentrated scene of pity occurs at the flogging, when Agnelette intercedes on Thibault's behalf. ""In the name of the God of mercy, my Lord, have pity on that man!"" she cries [003-009]. When the Baron does not relent, she repeats the plea in different language: ""Have pity, in the name of Heaven, my Lord! Tell your servants to let the poor man go, his cries pierce my heart."" [003-013].

The shift from "God of mercy" to "Heaven" marks a rhetorical escalation — Agnelette appeals first to divine authority, then to the more immediate image of the heavenly realm, then to the bodily effect of Thibault's suffering on herself ("his cries pierce my heart"). Pity here is structured as a chain of transmission: the sufferer's pain travels outward, pierces the witness's heart, and demands to be relayed to the person with the power to act. The Baron's refusal to receive this transmitted pain is the first clear sign of his own moral deficiency.

## Pity as Social Performance

Not all invocations of pity in the novel are genuine. When Thibault asks, ""Your mistress of the mill is hard-hearted then, is she? and takes no pity on your despair, poor fellow?"" his words are described as "though seemingly expressive of great sympathy" — the qualifier "seemingly" exposing his performance [007-042]. Thibault uses the language of pity instrumentally, to position himself as a sympathetic confidant to Landry while actually seeking to displace him in the widow's affections.

This instrumentalization of pity is a marker of moral decay. The language of compassion, when separated from genuine fellow-feeling, becomes another tool of predation — the wolf learning to dress its hunger in sheep's clothing.

## The Absence of Pity

As the novel progresses, pity's absence becomes more conspicuous than its presence. When Thibault, transformed into a wolf, hears the howling of his pack, the sound is explicitly described as lacking pity: "the wolves continued their melancholy howling" [021-004]. The melancholy is aesthetic — a quality of the sound — not moral. The wolves feel no pity for Thibault, and by this point in the narrative, neither does the reader.

The final plea for pity comes from Thibault himself, now fully transformed and desperate: ""Have pity on me, come with me; let us go somewhere together, where we shall no longer hear these howlings"" [021-063]. The echo of Agnelette's earlier plea — the same words, "have pity on me" — marks the full circle of Thibault's decline. He who was once the object of pity's plea is now the one begging for it; he who once instrumentalized pity now discovers its true value only when it is withheld.

## Pity and the Divine

The novel's pity-language is consistently theological. Agnelette's plea invokes "the God of mercy"; the frame narrator offers to "have pity on your youth" [P02-243]. Pity in the novel's moral universe is not merely a human emotion but a divine attribute that humans are called to imitate. To refuse pity is to refuse the imitation of God; to be beyond the reach of pity is to be beyond the reach of salvation.

Thibault's tragedy is not simply that he becomes pitiless — the wolf-nature ensures that — but that he loses his own capacity to _receive_ pity. His final howl is answered by wolves, not by Agnelette, because he has placed himself in a realm where pity has no jurisdiction.
