# Pisseleu

## Location Overview

Pisseleu is a hamlet situated on the route between [[oigny|Oigny]] and [[croyolles|Croyolles]] in the forest region of [[villers-cotterets|Villers-Cotterêts]]. Though a minor waypoint in the novel's geography, it is threaded through [[thibault|Thibault]]'s movements in the early chapters, marking his path through the rural byways he knows intimately as a shoemaker and poacher.

## Description in the Text

Pisseleu is introduced as a geographical reference point when describing the route to the mill at Croyolles:

> "The shortest way to Croyolles was round by the rear of Oigny and Pisseleu. Now Thibault knew every inch of the country" (006-008).

The hamlet sits on a footpath that provides a more direct route across the fields:

> "He took the footpath which runs direct to Pisseleu across the fields" (007-005).

On an autumn night, Thibault reaches Pisseleu under cover of darkness:

> "It was an autumn night, dark and stormy... had reached the rear of Pisseleu" (009-002).

## Key Events

1. **The Journey to Croyolles** (Chapter 6): [[thibault|Thibault]], planning to visit the mill at [[croyolles|Croyolles]], considers his route. The shortest way passes by way of Pisseleu, a path he knows intimately from his extensive knowledge of the forest's geography (006-008).

2. **The Night Journey** (Chapter 7): [[thibault|Thibault]] takes the footpath that runs "direct to Pisseleu across the fields" (007-005), using his familiarity with these back routes — knowledge characteristic of a poacher who must move unseen.

3. **An Autumn Night** (Chapter 9): Under cover of darkness and storm, [[thibault|Thibault]] reaches the rear of Pisseleu, using the hamlet as a staging point in his nocturnal travels (009-002).

## Significance

Pisseleu is one of many small place-names that Dumas uses to construct the novel's verisimilar geography. As a hamlet on a back route — not the main road — it belongs to the poacher's map of the region rather than the aristocrat's. [[thibault|Thibault]]'s knowledge of routes through Pisseleu is part of what makes him an effective poacher: he knows the shortcuts, the field-paths, and the approaches that avoid detection. The hamlet thus contributes to the novel's detailed portrait of a rural world where every byway has a name and every short-cut is known to those who live by night.

## Real-World Counterpart

"Pisseleu" is a historical place-name in northern France. There is a Pisseleu in the Oise department (near Marseille-en-Beauvaisis) and a Pisseleu hamlet near Oigny in the Aisne. Given the novel's setting in the Aisne region around Villers-Cotterêts, the latter is more likely Dumas's referent, though precise identification is uncertain. The name's unusual character — literally suggesting a place where one urinates (*pisse* + *leu*, old French for "wolf" or "place") — may also carry a certain ironic folk resonance in a novel about werewolves.
