## Definition

In *The Wolf-Leader*, the wound operates on multiple levels: as literal physical injury, as metaphor for psychological damage, and as narrative marker of escalating violence. Every wound in the novel carries a double significance — the visible damage to flesh and the invisible wound to pride, honor, or the soul. The wound is the novel's mechanism for making internal states physically legible: Thibault's wounded self-esteem drives the plot as surely as the knife-wounds that punctuate the action.

## Role in the Novel

The wound first appears in the logic of the hunt. When Thibault denies killing an animal, his reasoning is forensic: "if I had wounded him, blood would have flowed" (002-113). This early articulation of wound-as-evidence establishes a standard that the supernatural will progressively undermine — the wolf's wounds do not bleed in the expected way, and Thibault's own wounds become ambiguous markers of his dual nature.

The most consequential wound in the novel is the one Thibault inflicts on the Baron: "the same knife with which he had wounded the Baron" (020-008) recurs as an object of narrative memory. This wound sets in motion the revenge cycle that structures the novel's second half. The surgical detail of the wound's examination — "the knife had passed under the shoulder" (018-005), the doctor's assessment of its seriousness (018-006), the admission that immediate treatment "might perhaps" have helped (018-008) — invests the wound with medical gravity that mirrors its moral weight.

The wound also serves a social function: "their wounded self-esteem should have its revenge" (005-041). Here the wound is purely psychological but no less real — the nobles' wounded pride after the flogging incident demands satisfaction, demonstrating how the language of physical injury extends to encompass social humiliation in the ancien régime's honor culture.

In the novel's climax, the Count's wounds become a public spectacle: "themselves in a body on their wounded comrade" (020-087). The collective response to the wounded body — the gathering, the examination, the declaration that "the Count's life" is in danger (020-046) — transforms the individual wound into a communal event demanding collective justice.

## Key Examples

> "if I had wounded him, blood would have flowed" (002-113)

The huntsman's forensic logic establishes the wound as the novel's primary sign of truth — a logic the supernatural pact will systematically destabilize as Thibault's body becomes increasingly illegible.

> "their wounded self-esteem should have its revenge" (005-041)

The metaphorical wound reveals the novel's psychological depth — the wound to pride is as motivating as the wound to flesh, and in the ancien régime, perhaps more dangerous.
