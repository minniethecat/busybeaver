# Fidelity
## Definition

In *The Wolf-Leader*, [[faith|fidelity]] (*la fidélité*) is the bond that holds human relationships together — in marriage, in courtship, in feudal service — and infidelity is the [[violence|force]] that dissolves them. The novel presents [[faith|fidelity]] not as an abstract virtue but as a web of concrete obligations: [[thibault|Thibault]]'s promise to [[agnette|Agnelette]], [[agnette|Agnelette]]'s subsequent marriage to [[engoulevent|Engoulevent]], the [[comte-de-mont-gobert|Countess of Mont-Gobert]]'s adultery with [[raoul-de-vauparfond|Raoul de Vauparfond]], and the feudal loyalty of the wolf-pack that serves [[thibault|Thibault]] as faithfully as the King's bodyguard. Each thread of [[faith|fidelity]], when broken, produces consequences that radiate outward through the narrative.

Dumas structures [[faith|fidelity]] as a moral spectrum rather than a binary: at one end stands [[agnette|Agnelette]]'s simple, unwavering [[faith|faithfulness]]; at the other, [[thibault|Thibault]]'s cascade of betrayals. Between them, characters like [[countess|the Countess]] and her faithful servitors complicate the picture — [[faith|fidelity]] and infidelity can coexist in the same person, toward different objects.

## Historical and Folkloric Background

Fidelity in the *ancien régime* was woven into multiple overlapping codes: the feudal oath of loyalty from vassal to lord, the marriage [[oath|vow]] before God, the promise of betrothal before witnesses. Each carried distinct legal and social consequences. Betrothal (*fiançailles*) was a binding contract in customary law — [[thibault|Thibault]]'s promise to [[agnette|Agnelette]] under the birch trees was not merely romantic; it carried legal weight, which is why his abandonment of it constitutes a genuine breach.

In Catholic moral theology, infidelity was classified as a sin against [[law|justice]] — the betrayal of a freely undertaken obligation. Marital infidelity was also a sin against the sacrament of marriage, and in folk [[faith|belief]], adulterers were thought to be especially vulnerable to diabolical influence. The unfaithful partner had already broken one sacred bond; breaking others became easier.

In werewolf folklore, [[faith|fidelity]] carries additional resonance: the werewolf is himself a figure of doubleness, unfaithful to his own humanity, divided between human and beast. [[thibault|Thibault]]'s infidelity to [[agnette|Agnelette]] and his transformation into a wolf run parallel — both are ruptures of essential bonds, one social, one ontological.

## Role in the Novel

Fidelity first appears as a promise made freely and regretted quickly. [[thibault|Thibault]], ha[[wine|vin]]g won [[agnette|Agnelette]]'s [[love|affection]], lets his ambition override his commitment. When he sees her with [[engoulevent|Engoulevent]], his response reveals the self-ser[[wine|vin]]g character of his understanding of [[faith|fidelity]]:

> "He persuaded himself that Agnelette was engaged to him by oath, that Engoulevent was carrying off what belonged to him, and he almost leaped from his hiding place to rush after her and reproach her with her infidelity. Agnelette, now no longer his, at once appeared to his eyes as endowed with all the virtues and good qualities" (014-021).

The passage exposes [[thibault|Thibault]]'s moral narcissism: he demands [[faith|fidelity]] from [[agnette|Agnelette]] while actively betraying his own promise to her. His accusation of "infidelity" is a projection — he sees in her the fault he is himself committing. And the irony is that [[agnette|Agnelette]]'s apparent infidelity only increases her value in his eyes: "now no longer his, at once appeared to his eyes as endowed with all the virtues." Desire, for [[thibault|Thibault]], is structured by loss, not by presence.

The theme returns at the novel's climax, when the wolf-voice interrogates [[thibault|Thibault]]'s crimes:

> "And do they say nothing of the infidelity which has led Thibault to commit such crimes?" (021-027).

Here infidelity is elevated from a personal failing to the root cause of [[thibault|Thibault]]'s entire criminal trajectory. The question implies a causal chain: infidelity is not merely one sin among many but the originating breach from which other sins follow. [[thibault|Thibault]]'s infidelity to [[agnette|Agnelette]] leads to jealousy and rage; those passions, magnified by the wolf's power, lead to murder and [[death|destruction]].

Against these violations, the novel sets examples of [[faith|fidelity]]. Madame Magloire is described as a "kind and faithful wife" (013-061). The [[countess|Countess of Mont-Gobert]], though herself unfaithful to her husband, keeps faith with her lover — "true and faithful unto death" (020-037). Her bodyguards are "his finest and most faithful servitors" (021-014), loyal to the end even as they are cut down. And [[agnette|Agnelette]], confronted with death, declares: "You may kill me, but I shall remain faithful to my husband" (021-080). This is the novel's moral counterweight: [[faith|fidelity]] perseveres even when the cost is life itself.

## Key Examples

[[thibault|Thibault]]'s projection — accusing [[agnette|Agnelette]] of the infidelity he is himself committing, and desiring her more for being lost:

> "He persuaded himself that Agnelette was engaged to him by oath, that Engoulevent was carrying off what belonged to him, and he almost leaped from his hiding place to rush after her and reproach her with her infidelity" (014-021).

In[[faith|fidelity]] named as the originating sin from which [[thibault|Thibault]]'s crimes flow:

> "And do they say nothing of the infidelity which has led Thibault to commit such crimes?" (021-027).

Fidelity unto death — [[agnette|Agnelette]]'s refusal to betray her husband even at the cost of her life:

> "You may kill me, but I shall remain faithful to my husband" (021-080).

The [[countess|Countess of Mont-Gobert]], true to her lover even as she is unfaithful to her husband:

> "True and faithful unto death, for the Countess was just then expiring" (020-037).

Madame Magloire as the model of domestic [[faith|fidelity]]:

> "Kind and faithful wife as she is, Madame Magloire" (013-061).

## Author's Treatment

Dumas constructs [[faith|fidelity]] as a structural principle rather than a sermonizing theme. He does not preach against infidelity; he [[devil|demon]]strates its consequences through narrative causality. Every act of infidelity in the novel — [[thibault|Thibault]]'s toward [[agnette|Agnelette]], [[countess|the Countess]]'s toward her husband, [[thibault|Thibault]]'s toward his own humanity — sets in motion chains of events that destroy the unfaithful and those around them.

The novel's most subtle treatment of [[faith|fidelity]] involves its asymmetry. The Countess is unfaithful to her husband but faithful to her lover — [[faith|fidelity]] is directional, not total. [[thibault|Thibault]] demands [[faith|fidelity]] from others while practicing none himself — [[faith|fidelity]], in the hands of the powerful, becomes a tool of control rather than a mutual bond. [[agnette|Agnelette]] alone is faithful in every direction: to her promise, to her husband, to herself. Her [[faith|fidelity]] is the novel's moral standard, and its cost — her life — is the novel's darkest commentary on what it means to keep faith in a world governed by wolf-logic.

The werewolf transformation itself can be read as the ultimate infidelity: [[thibault|Thibault]] becomes unfaithful to his own species, his own form, his own soul. The wolf is the infidelity made flesh.
