## Event Summary

In the forest near [[villers-cotterets|Villers-Cotterets]], [[thibault|Thibault]] — a young sabot-maker newly emboldened by survi[[wine|vin]]g his encounter with the [[devil|black wolf]] — proposes marriage to [[agnette|Agnelette]], the orphaned goatherd who lives with her blind grandmother [003-105]. She accepts, and [[thibault|Thibault]] promises to buy her a wedding dress. The betrothal is sealed with a ring — actually the devil's enchanted ring which [[thibault|Thibault]] attempts, and fails, to slip onto [[agnette|Agnelette]]'s finger [006-033]. This promise, made in sincerity by [[agnette|Agnelette]] but treated as disposable by [[thibault|Thibault]], becomes the moral anchor-point against which his later betrayals are measured.

## Context

The betrothal occurs early in the novel, before [[thibault|Thibault]] has fully succumbed to the temptations of the [[devil|black wolf]]'s diabolical power. At this stage, [[thibault|Thibault]] is still a struggling artisan: "the clever workman, able to earn as much as six francs a day" [014-042]. His feelings for [[agnette|Agnelette]] are genuine but shallow — he appreciates her beauty and innocence but has no intention of committing to a life of humble domesticity.

[[agnette|Agnelette]]'s situation makes the betrothal particularly significant. As an orphan supporting a blind grandmother, marriage represents not merely romantic fulfillment but economic survival [014-018]. The promise [[thibault|Thibault]] makes is thus freighted with a weight of practical consequence that he callously ignores.

## Key Participants

- **[[thibault|Thibault]]** — The proposer. His offer is made in a moment of post-danger euphoria, after the flogging incident and his first meeting with the [[devil|black wolf]]. He asks: "And when shall we have the wedding, [[agnette|Agnelette]]?" [003-105] with apparent earnestness but hidden insincerity.
- **[[agnette|Agnelette]]** — The betrothed. Beautiful, innocent, and trusting, she accepts [[thibault|Thibault]]'s proposal with joy. Her love for him is genuine: "she was the one whom [[agnette|Agnelette]] had first loved" [014-042]. She safeguards the betrothal ring, asking to keep it until their wedding day [006-033].

## Consequences

The betrothal's most profound consequence is its violation. [[thibault|Thibault]] abandons [[agnette|Agnelette]] almost immediately as ambition and diabolical power corrupt his [[wish|desire]]s. The ring — the devil's enchanted ring that [[thibault|Thibault]] could not successfully place on her finger — becomes a symbol of the betrothal's doomed nature: a pact with evil cannot sanctify a human bond.

When [[agnette|Agnelette]] later marries [[engoulevent|Engoulevent]], the betrayal of the betrothal becomes the emotional core of [[thibault|Thibault]]'s jealousy. He con[[wine|vin]]ces himself retroactively "that [[agnette|Agnelette]] was engaged to him by oath, that [[engoulevent|Engoulevent]] was carrying off what belonged to him" [014-021]. This self-deception transforms a promise he himself broke into a grievance he uses to justify his own descent.

## Detailed Account

The betrothal unfolds across two chapters. In Ch03, beneath the oak trees of the forest, [[thibault|Thibault]] makes his proposal. [[agnette|Agnelette]], her "beautiful eyes, all wet with tears," accepts with innocent joy [003-008]. The scene embodies pastoral simplicity: a young couple, the dappled forest light, the promise of a shared future.

In Ch06, the betrothal ring episode reveals the supernatural intrusion already corrupting the relationship. [[thibault|Thibault]] attempts to place the devil's enchanted ring on [[agnette|Agnelette]]'s finger, but somehow the ring will not go on [006-033]. [[agnette|Agnelette]], unaware of the ring's true nature, offers to keep it safely: "give it me to keep till our wedding day, and on that day I will give it back to you" [006-033]. The ring that cannot be worn becomes the promise that cannot be kept.

## Narrative Significance

The betrothal serves as the novel's "road not taken" — the domestic happiness [[thibault|Thibault]] might have had if he had resisted the [[devil|black wolf]]'s temptations. Dumas underscores this in the retrospective passage where [[thibault|Thibault]] imagines the life he has thrown away: "what happiness might have been his, as the clever workman, able to earn as much as six francs a day, with [[agnette|Agnelette]] for his charming little housekeeper!" [014-042].

The betrothal also establishes the pattern that defines [[thibault|Thibault]]'s tragedy: he makes promises he never intends to keep, and each broken promise becomes a grievance he uses to justify further evil.
