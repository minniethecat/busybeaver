# Ambition

## Definition

In *The Wolf-Leader*, ambition (*l'ambition*) is the psychological [[violence|force]] that makes [[thibault|Thibault]] vulnerable to the wolf's temptation. Unlike simple greed, which [[wish|desire]]s objects, ambition [[wish|desire]]s [[class|station]] — a different place in the social order, a different identity, a different self. [[thibault|Thibault]] does not merely want the Baron's wealth; he wants to *be* the Baron, to occupy the position from which wealth, power, and women flow effortlessly. This is ambition in its purest form: the [[wish|desire]] not for what another has but for what another is.

Dumas treats ambition with notable ambivalence. It is, on the one hand, the most sympathetic of [[thibault|Thibault]]'s traits — the [[wish|desire]] to rise is, after all, the [[wish|desire]] that drove Dumas's own father from slavery to generalship. On the other hand, ambition in a rigidly hierarchical society has no legitimate channels. The peasant who would rise must either wait for a revolution that has not yet come or strike a bargain with darker powers. [[thibault|Thibault]]'s ambition is not inherently corrupt; it becomes corrupt because the world he inhabits offers no uncorrupted path to its fulfillment.

## Role in the Novel

Ambition is established early as a defining trait. The novel's narrator observes:

> "It was by no means Thibault's ambition to wear a short vest and sail a boat on the ponds of Bourg-Fontaine" (001-040).

The negative construction is revealing. Ambition is defined by what it is not — by what [[thibault|Thibault]] refuses to settle for. He will not be a mere boatman, a mere peasant, a mere anything. The ambition itself is not excessive; what is excessive is the gap between ambition and the social reality that forbids its fulfillment.

The tension between ambition and contented domesticity structures [[thibault|Thibault]]'s relationship with [[agnette|Agnelette]]. She offers a life of honest labor and mutual [[love|affection]] — the life his [[class|station]] prescribes. He is tempted:

> "There should be no thought, no ambition on his part beyond the wish to share his life with Agnelette" (003-109).

The subjunctive mood — "should be" — registers the distance between what [[thibault|Thibault]] knows he ought to want and what he actually wants. Ambition cannot be reasoned away; it is a condition of his character, as ineradicable as his melancholy.

Ambition's escalation tracks the novel's plot with precision. What begins as a vague dissatisfaction with peasant life becomes, under the wolf's influence, a program of systematic advancement:

> "To what extravagant issues had his ambition been excited by those dainty dishes and fine wines" (009-019).

The word "excited" is carefully chosen. Ambition is not a rational calculation but an aroused appetite, and appetites, once aroused, demand satiation. The wolf understands this; [[thibault|Thibault]] does not. Each wish gratifies ambition while enlarging it, creating a spiral from which there is no exit except exhaustion or collapse.

The novel's most poignant treatment of ambition comes through contrast. The Keeper, describing his own life, articulates ambition's healthy form:

> "He had no ambition beyond that of gaining his daily bread and winning my love" (021-066).

This is ambition bounded by necessity and [[love|affection]] — ambition that knows its limits and finds satisfaction within them. It is precisely the kind of ambition [[thibault|Thibault]] cannot sustain, and the novel treats this incapacity not as villainy but as tragedy.

## Key Examples

Ambition as refusal — what [[thibault|Thibault]] will not become:

> "It was by no means Thibault's ambition to wear a short vest and sail a boat on the ponds of Bourg-Fontaine" (001-040).

Ambition as disturbed dream — the impossibility of contentment:

> "He was ambitious; but his dreams of ambition were disturbed at moments by the remembrance of Agnelette" (003-093).

The fantasy of [[sacrifice|renunciation]] — what [[thibault|Thibault]] wishes he could want:

> "There should be no thought, no ambition on his part beyond the wish to share his life with Agnelette" (003-109).

Ambition's escalation under the wolf's feeding:

> "To what extravagant issues had his ambition been excited by those dainty dishes and fine wines" (009-019).

The novel's normative standard — healthy ambition bounded by love:

> "He had no ambition beyond that of gaining his daily bread and winning my love" (021-066).

## Historical and Folkloric Background

Ambition in the *ancien régime* was a structurally problematic [[wish|desire]]. The three [[class|estate]]s — clergy, nobility, and commoners — were di[[wine|vin]]ely ordained, and movement between them was, in theory, impossible. In practice, social mobility occurred through the Church, the military, venal office, and marriage — but always within limits and always at the pleasure of superiors. Ambition that exceeded these channels was not merely impractical but impious: it challenged the order God had established.

The French Revolution, which occurred three decades before Dumas's birth, had overturned this order in the name of *carrière ouverte aux talents* — careers open to talent. Dumas, the son of a mixed-race general who rose through merit during the Revolutionary wars, was a direct beneficiary of this new dispensation. His treatment of [[thibault|Thibault]]'s ambition reflects this history: ambition is not condemned as such but lamented for arri[[wine|vin]]g too early, in a world not yet ready to receive it.

## Author's Treatment

Dumas's treatment of ambition is among the most psychologically nuanced aspects of the novel. He refuses both the conservative position (ambition as sin) and the revolutionary position (ambition as virtue). Instead, he presents ambition as a neutral [[violence|force]] whose moral valence depends entirely on context. In a just society, ambition might be a motor of progress; in an unjust one, it is a pipeline to [[hell|damnation]].

The wolf's genius is to recognize this. It does not offer [[thibault|Thibault]] wealth or pleasure — it offers advancement. Each wish is a rung on a ladder that leads nowhere, because the ladder is the wolf's, and the wolf collects its fee at the top. [[thibault|Thibault]]'s ambition is real, legitimate, and exploited — and the exploitation is what makes the novel a tragedy rather than a morality play.
