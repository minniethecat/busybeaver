## Context

The duel occurs as the catastrophic endpoint of [[thibault|Thibault]]'s wish to become Baron Raoul for twenty-four hours. Before making the wish, [[thibault|Thibault]] had mockingly predicted the danger: "I should laugh, Raoul, if the [[comte-de-mont-gobert|Comte de Mont-Gobert]] were to take you by surprise; you would not get off so easily as if he were the [[magloire|Bailiff Magloire]]; there would be swords drawn, and [[violence|blow]]s given and received" [018-003]. The [[vision|prophecy]] proves accurate.

[[thibault|Thibault]]'s inhabitation of Raoul's body renders his [[devil|demon]]ic powers dormant — "his [[devil|demon]]iacal power had been and would continue in abeyance" [018-001] — lea[[wine|vin]]g him as vulnerable as any [[death|mort]]al man.

## Event Summary

During the twenty-four hours in which [[thibault|Thibault]] inhabits the body of [[raoul-de-vauparfond|Baron Raoul de Vauparfond]] through a [[devil|demon]]ic wish, he is discovered by the [[comte-de-mont-gobert|Comte de Mont-Gobert]] in a compromising situation with [[countess|the Countess]] [018-001]. A desperate duel ensues, ending with [[thibault|Thibault]]-Raoul recei[[wine|vin]]g a near-fatal knife wound through the right lung. The Count's head keeper [[lestocq|Lestocq]] carries his wounded master inside, while [[thibault|Thibault]] is left to die "like a dog" in the breach of the castle wall [018-001]. This wound initiates the death-and-resurrection sequence that dominates the novel's later chapters.

## Detailed Account

The duel takes place in the breach of the castle wall. [[thibault|Thibault]]-Raoul regains consciousness on the cold ground with no memory of what happened. "The ground near him was red with blood, but the Count was no longer there" [018-001].

The aftermath is a study in isolation. "[[thibault|Thibault]] they had left there, to die like a dog, as far as they cared" [018-001]. His attempts to call for help are thwarted — "the blood filled his mouth and nearly choked him" [018-004]. Eventually, peasants carrying goods to market spot his hat raised on a knife point and construct a makeshift handbarrow from branches to carry him [018-004].

At the priest's house, the doctor's grim prognosis sets the stage: "If his wound runs the ordinary course, he will probably not last out the day" [018-010]. The priest, a former tutor to the real Raoul, attempts to give the dying man absolution, but [[thibault|Thibault]] pushes him away: "I want no absolution! I am damned! damned! damned!" [018-020].

## Key Participants

- **[[thibault|Thibault]] (as Baron Raoul)** — The wounded duelist. Though in Raoul's body, [[thibault|Thibault]]'s consciousness remains fully alert, trapped inside the dying flesh [018-016]. He experiences the wound as an imprisonment: "his [[soul|spirit]] was as if imprisoned in this dying body, and, against his will, was [[violence|force]]d to submit to lying thus within its cell" [018-016].
- **[[comte-de-mont-gobert|Comte de Mont-Gobert]]** — The aggrieved husband. His wounding of [[thibault|Thibault]]-Raoul results in his own severe injury; later seen dragging himself on one knee with a mutilated leg in [[thibault|Thibault]]'s fever [[dream|visions]] [018-019].
- **[[lestocq|Lestocq]]** — The Count's head keeper who delivers the near-fatal knife thrust and carries his master to safety, abandoning [[thibault|Thibault]]-Raoul [018-001].
- **The [[countess|Countess of Mont-Gobert]]** — Her presence with the Baron triggers the confrontation that leads to the duel.

## Consequences

The duel's immediate consequence is [[thibault|Thibault]]-Raoul's near-fatal wound — a knife wound passing under the shoulder-blade, through the right lung, and out between the second and third ribs [018-005]. Carried by [[mercy|compassion]]ate peasants to the priest's house at Puiseux, [[thibault|Thibault]] endures a day of fever and delirium during which his entire life passes before him in hallucinatory review [018-019].

The wound proves the instrument of a profound narrative irony. The doctor pronounces the wound fatal, and indeed Raoul's body dies at precisely half-past nine — one second after the twenty-four hours of the wish expire [018-023]. [[thibault|Thibault]]'s consciousness returns to his own body, but the experience of inhabiting a dying man and confronting his victims in fever [[dream|visions]] becomes a catalyst for his eventual moral reckoning.

## Narrative Significance

The duel represents the physical manife[[class|station]] of [[thibault|Thibault]]'s [[soul|spirit]]ual condition. Ha[[wine|vin]]g wished his way into another man's life, he must now suffer another man's death. The duel's consequences — the wound, the fever, the delirium, the confession — strip away all pretense and [[violence|force]] [[thibault|Thibault]] to confront the sum of his sins. The clock that marks the hours becomes "the blue vault of heaven" with numbers of flame, and its pendulum intones: "Never! Forever!" [018-021].
