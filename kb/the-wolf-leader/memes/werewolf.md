# Werewolf

## Definition

In European folklore, a werewolf (French: *loup-garou*) is a human being who, through a curse, pact with the devil, or inherited condition, transforms into a wolf — typically at night or during the full moon. In the world of *The Wolf-Leader*, the werewolf is not merely a monstrous predator but the endpoint of a moral and supernatural degeneration: a human who, through a series of wicked wishes granted by a diabolical [[black-wolf|familiar]], gradually loses his humanity and becomes a wolf in both body and nature.

## Role in the Novel

The werewolf concept is the novel's central supernatural mechanism and its ultimate moral allegory. [[thibault|Thibault]], the sabot-maker, does not become a werewolf through a single dramatic event but through a progressive transformation driven by his own malevolent [[wish|desire]]s (003-050). Each wish he makes to the [[devil|black wolf]] — his diabolical [[black-wolf|familiar]] — transfers a strand of his hair to the beast, and with each transfer, [[thibault|Thibault]]'s human nature erodes further (005-019).

The villagers' fear of werewolves is established early as a cultural backdrop. [[agnette|Agnelette]] confesses that tales of werewolves terrify her when alone in the forest (003-046), and the [[baron-of-vez|Baron of Vez]] reassures her that no werewolf has ever crossed his moat (003-050). These early references establish the werewolf as a known folkloric threat before [[thibault|Thibault]]'s transformation begins.

By Chapter 8, the accusation becomes explicit: a crowd denounces [[thibault|Thibault]] as "a wizard, a sorcerer! a werewolf!" (008-047), and the fearful question "what can we do against a werewolf?" (008-049) underscores the helplessness of ordinary people against supernatural evil.

The transformation culminates in the novel's final chapters. The werewolf's skin becomes the literal object of the hunt, as the Baron's hounds pursue [[thibault|Thibault]] by scent (023-076), leading to a desperate struggle between the werewolf and the dogs (024-003). The climax sees the hounds tearing at what they believe to be the werewolf's retreat (024-004), and the final revelation that the skin they have been [[hunt|hunting]] belongs to [[thibault|Thibault]] himself (024-014).

## Key Examples

[[agnette|Agnelette]]'s early confession reveals the pervasive fear of werewolves among the rural population:

> "Well, my Lord, I hear so many tales, during the winter evenings, about werewolves, that when I find myself all alone among the trees, and can hear no sound but the west wind, and the branches creaking as it blows through them, I feel a kind of shiver run through me, and my hair seems to stand on end" (003-046).

The villagers' accusation of [[thibault|Thibault]] marks the moment when supernatural [[jealousy|suspicion]] becomes public denunciation:

> "he is a wizard, a sorcerer! a werewolf!" — applying to Thibault with the full weight of communal dread (008-047).

The final hunt scene crystallizes the werewolf's tragic position — hunted by both human society and its own former nature:

> The Baron's hounds "at once on to the scent of the werewolf, and began to give cry" (023-076), and "the struggle between the werewolf and the dogs" begins "in very earnest" (024-003).

## Historical and Folkloric Background

The werewolf legend was widespread in early modern France, particularly in rural regions like the novel's setting in the Île-de-France. The *loup-garou* trials of the 16th and 17th centuries — most famously the case of Gilles Garnier (1573) and the trial of the "Werewolf of Châlons" (1599) — established the werewolf in French legal and folkloric consciousness. These cases often involved accusations of pact with the devil, echoing the novel's central bargain between [[thibault|Thibault]] and the [[devil|black wolf]].

Dumas, writing in 1857, draws on this rich [[custom|tradition]] while adapting it for Romantic-era Gothic fiction. The werewolf serves as both a supernatural thriller element and a moral metaphor: [[thibaults-transformation|the loss of humanity]] is gradual, earned through repeated acts of malevolent will rather than a single involuntary curse.

## Dumas's Treatment

Dumas innovates on the [[custom|tradition]]al werewolf myth in several key respects. First, the transformation is incremental and psychological rather than instantaneous and purely physical — [[thibault|Thibault]] loses his humanity hair by hair, wish by wish. Second, the werewolf's [[black-wolf|familiar]] (the [[devil|black wolf]]) is explicitly identified with the devil (P02-336), making the werewolf state a consequence of diabolical contract rather than involuntary fate. Third, Dumas frames the narrative as a moral tale told by a country storyteller (P02-013), embedding the supernatural events within a frame of oral [[custom|tradition]] that allows readers to take the werewolf story as either literal or allegorical.
