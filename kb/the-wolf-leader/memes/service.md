# Service

**Service** — the performance of feudal obligation through labor, obedience, and bodily submission — is the social glue of the world of _The Wolf-Leader_. Every character occupies a position in a chain of service relationships that extends from the King's soldiers down to the sabot-maker's apprentice. Service is both the means of survival and the mark of subordination, both duty and degradation.

## The Grammar of Service

The novel's characters are defined by whom they serve. Landry is "head boy at the mill, in the service of the owner whom Thibault was on his way to visit" [007-005]. The keepers wear the "livery" [014-013] that marks them as men "in the service of the lord of Vez." Champagne is identified by his service relation: "Champagne, who is in the Comtesse de Mont-Gobert's service" [015-019]. Service is not what these characters do but what they _are_ — it is ontological, not merely occupational.

Thibault's own position is established through negation: "his comrades who had worn the double livery of king and country, had left the service as they entered it, mere soldiers of the ranks, having failed during five or six years of slavery to obtain promotion" [001-039]. He has escaped military service but not the servitude of poverty. The "double livery of king and country" makes explicit that service is always also a form of marking — the livery identifies the servant as property of the served.

## Service as Degradation

Service in the novel is consistently associated with humiliation. Landry's position at the mill is described as one he might wish to escape: "it would have been better for you to have been dragged under by the mill wheel the first day you entered into the service of Madame Polet, than to have been taken on by her as chief hand" [007-038]. Service to the widow elevates Landry materially but at a cost that the text presents as potentially fatal.

The sergeant's mocking of Landry crystallizes the contempt that adheres to service: "so we prefer the service of beauty to the King's service? That is easy to understand" [008-022]. The irony positions King's service as the default obligation and any other service as a dereliction — yet the King's service is itself a form of slavery, the "five or six years of slavery" [001-039] that Thibault escaped.

## Service and Reciprocity

The novel explores the question of what service entitles the servant to expect in return. Thibault asks the wolf: "are you willing to make me some return for the service I have done you?" [004-027]. The question is the novel's central economic problem: does service create obligation in the served, or is it merely extracted by the powerful without reciprocal claim?

Magloire's experience provides an answer. "He, therefore, sent for Magloire, and asked him how much he had been able to save while in his service" [011-042]. The master inquires about savings — a recognition of the servant's economic personhood — but the question is preparatory to a transaction, not an acknowledgement of debt. Service creates value for the master but not claim for the servant.

The most explicit statement of the servant's position comes from a minor character: "the only idiot here is the man who gives insults in return for the services of which he has made use" [011-070]. Service, this suggests, _ought_ to create obligations of gratitude, but the novel systematically shows that it does not.

## The Escape from Service

Thibault's pact with the wolf is fundamentally an attempt to escape the service economy. Instead of serving the Baron, he will become the Baron's equal or superior. Instead of laboring for subsistence, he will command supernatural abundance. But the irony of the pact is that it merely exchanges one form of service for another — service to the social order becomes service to the wolf. "I suppose it will have to be a case of give and take, eh?" [005-013] — the wolf's language of exchange conceals a totalizing claim. Thibault escapes feudal service only to enter into diabolical service, and the latter is the more absolute bondage.

## Distinct from Duty

Service in the novel is consistently distinguished from the abstract moral category of duty. Duty is the internalized obligation — what one owes regardless of enforcement. Service is the external, enforced performance — what one does because the alternative is punishment. When the widow Polet speaks of "conjugal duty" [012-053], she invokes the moral realm. When characters speak of being "in the service of" a lord, they invoke the social. The novel's pessimism lies in its suggestion that in the feudal world, service has entirely replaced duty — there are no moral obligations, only enforced ones.
