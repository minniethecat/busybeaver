# Villers-Cotterêts

## Location Overview

Villers-Cotterêts is the principal town of the region in which the novel's action takes place, situated at the edge of the vast forest that bears its name. It serves as the commercial, administrative, and ecclesiastical center for the scattered hamlets and forest dwellings that [[thibault|Thibault]] and the other characters inhabit. The [[baron-of-vez|Baron of Vez]] maintains a castle there, though he prefers his residence at [[vez|Vez]] (001-007).

The town functions as a gravitational center in the novel's geography: characters travel to it to trade goods, to attend church, to transact legal business, and to participate in social life. [[thibault|Thibault]]'s hut lies several miles distant, deep in the forest, but Villers-Cotterêts is the destination for his commercial errands and, later, the scene of some of his most audacious supernatural displays.

## Description in the Text

Dumas's narrator introduces the town through the lens of local memory and folklore, framing it as a place where the extraordinary breaks through the fabric of ordinary life. The opening frame narrative establishes Villers-Cotterêts as the location where the story of [[thibault|Thibault]] is still told — a story passed down through generations of townspeople:

> "In my dear home country of Villers-Cotterets, there are more sabot-makers than in any other part of France" (001-035).

The town's commercial character is woven into the narrative through its shops, its market, and its central role in the regional economy. [[thibault|Thibault]]'s [[wood|sabot-making]] is viable precisely because Villers-Cotterêts provides a market:

> "The shoemaker put up his workshop in the woods, less than a mile from Oigny and about three times that distance from Villers-Cotterets" (001-048).

The town also houses the school run by the Abbé Fortier, where [[thibault|Thibault]] received his education — an education that sets him apart from other peasants:

> "Thibault had been to school under the Abbé Fortier, at Villers-Cotterets, and had learnt to read, write, and keep accounts" (001-038).

## Key Events

Villers-Cotterêts is the setting for several significant narrative moments:

- **The doctor's visit**: The injured servant is treated "by the doctor in repute at Villers-Cotterets" (P02-059), establishing the town as the regional center of expertise.
- **[[thibault|Thibault]]'s transformation walk**: In one of the novel's most striking scenes, [[thibault|Thibault]] traverses "the whole town of Villers-Cotterets with a dozen wolves walking alongside him" (012-009) — his supernatural power on public display.
- **Game sales**: [[thibault|Thibault]] uses the town to convert his poached game into money, as when he "started for Villers-Cotterets" where "he sold a part of this game" (012-021).
- **Social ascent**: [[thibault|Thibault]]'s purchases at the general shop kept by "Monsieur Perrin... at Villers-Cotterets" enable him to present himself as a man of means, paying "a sufficient sum to prevent the affair going further" (001-049).

## Significance

Villers-Cotterêts represents civilization in the novel's symbolic geography — the structured, social world from which [[thibault|Thibault]] is progressively exiled. At the novel's start, [[thibault|Thibault]] moves between the forest and the town, a liminal figure who belongs fully to neither. As his transformation accelerates, his visits to the town become more audacious and less natural — the man walking with wolves is no longer a participant in civil society but a threat to it.

The real-world Villers-Cotterêts carries additional resonance: it was Alexandre Dumas's birthplace (1802), and the Abbey of Villers-Cotterêts, founded by Francis I, was where the Ordonnance de Villers-Cotterêts (1539) established French as the official language of the kingdom. Dumas's choice to set his werewolf tale in the forest of his birthplace is a deliberate literary homecoming.

## Real-World Counterpart

The modern Villers-Cotterêts (Aisne department, Hauts-de-France region) is a commune of approximately 10,000 inhabitants. The Forêt de Retz, which Dumas calls the [[forest|Forest]] of Villers-Cotterêts, still surrounds the town and covers some 13,000 hectares. The Château de Villers-Cotterêts (also known as Château François Ier or Château de Noüe) was extensively renovated in the 2020s to become the Cité internationale de la langue française.

The town's historical importance in French legal history — as the site where Francis I mandated the use of French in official documents — adds an ironic layer to Dumas's use of the location for a novel written in richly vernacular French prose.
