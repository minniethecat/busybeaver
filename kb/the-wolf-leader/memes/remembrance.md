# Remembrance

**Remembrance** — the willed, emotional act of recalling the past — functions throughout _The Wolf-Leader_ as a measure of Thibault's moral decline. The further he falls under the wolf's influence, the more painful and yet more necessary the act of remembrance becomes, pitting nostalgic recollection of his pre-pact innocence against the inescapable memory of his crimes.

## Remembrance of the Unholy Invocation

Thibault's first act of remembrance after his pact is the recollection of his own blasphemy. "Thibault had a perfect remembrance of the unholy invocation he had addressed to Satan," and although his prayer had been miraculously answered, he still could not bring himself to believe that it was really the devil who had intervened on his behalf [004-005]. This remembrance is precise and undeniable — he remembers exactly what he said — yet he struggles to accept its consequences. The clarity of remembrance here functions as moral evidence against self-deception; he cannot claim ignorance when his memory is so exact.

The same passage links remembrance to the failure of prayer: "when he wished to raise his hand to make the sign of the Cross, he found it impossible to do so" [004-006]. Remembrance of the invocation blocks access to the religious ritual that might undo it. Memory here is not merely passive recollection but an active barrier to redemption.

## Remembrance as Moral Anchor

The Widow Polet's resistance to Thibault's advances is grounded in remembrance of her dead husband. "The remembrance of him will prevent me ever wishing to look at another man," she tells Thibault frankly, "and I know that I must resign myself to remaining a widow all my life" [008-036]. Her remembrance is a moral choice — the deliberate cultivation of fidelity through memory — and it stands in sharp contrast to Thibault's inability to remember his own better nature.

Where Thibault's remembrance leads to despair, the widow's leads to a resigned but dignified constancy. She uses the past to structure her present, while Thibault's past overwhelms his present.

## The Failure of Remembrance

As Thibault sinks deeper into his wolf-nature, his capacity for redemptive remembrance weakens. "In vain he tried to remember the carols he had been in the habit of singing in the happier days when the beech and the birch shaped themselves so quickly beneath his fingers; his throat refused to utter a sound" [009-007]. The craftsman's songs of his pre-pact life are beyond recall — not because he has forgotten them, but because his transformed self cannot give them voice.

This failed remembrance is the novel's clearest image of lost identity. The sabot-maker who sang at his workbench is no longer the same person who now crouches in the forest. Remembrance offers the knowledge of what was lost but no path back to it.

## Remembrance and Temptation

Remembrance also serves as a trigger for renewed temptation. When Thibault "remembered that only a few paces from where he stood, there was a spring as transparent as crystal, which fed the pond near Baisemont" [006-049], the memory leads him not to nostalgia but to new danger — the spring is near the widow's mill, and the memory of the place awakens his desire for her.

Similarly, his remembrance of "the heavy expenses entailed on each and all by New Year's Day" [010-001] leads him to "exact double rations from his usual purveyor's," compounding his moral debts. Memory of practical obligation becomes a rationalization for greed.

## Distinction from Memory

Remembrance in _The Wolf-Leader_ is consistently distinguished from the mere cognitive faculty of memory. Where memory is passive — the storage of information — remembrance is active, willed, and emotionally charged. The widow _chooses_ to remember her husband; Thibault _tries_ to remember his songs but fails. Remembrance is a moral act, not a neurological one. Its success or failure is determined by the rememberer's spiritual condition, not by the passage of time.

This aligns with the novel's broader moral framework: the soul that has submitted to darkness loses not only its future but its past.
