## Overview

Miss Oscar is [[george-lomax|George Lomax]]'s secretary at the [[foreign-office|Foreign Office]]. She never appears in person but is frequently referenced as the invisible force that keeps Lomax's political machinery running. She manages his engagement diary, writes his correspondence, and handles his telephone communications — an indispensable administrative presence without whom Lomax would be, in [[bill-eversleigh|Bill Eversleigh]]'s view, utterly lost.

## Character Profile

Miss Oscar is defined entirely through her professional competence. She is the gatekeeper of Lomax's schedule, the author of his letters, and the filter through which his communications pass. [[bill-eversleigh|Bill Eversleigh]], Lomax's subordinate, regards her with a mixture of admiration and dependency — "he wished that Miss Oscar were here" whenever the workload becomes overwhelming [012-026].

> "Miss Oscar is dealing with most of them. Mr. Isaacstein wants to know if you can dine with him at the Savoy tomorrow." [004-007]

## Key Actions

Miss Oscar's role is entirely facilitative. When [[herman-isaacstein|Herman Isaacstein]] requests a dinner meeting with Lomax, it is Miss Oscar who relays the invitation [004-007]. Lomax's reflexive response — "Tell Miss Oscar to look in my engagement-book. If I'm not engaged, she can ring up and accept" [004-008] — reveals the extent of his reliance on her.

She writes "all his letters for him, and remembered who they were to and what they were about — a great mountain of a woman with a deep bass voice" [012-026]. This brief physical description — the only physical detail provided about her — paints her as a formidable figure, more solid and reliable than the politicians she serves.

When [[bill-eversleigh|Bill Eversleigh]] is temporarily assigned to handle Lomax's affairs at [[chimneys|Chimneys]], he finds himself out of his depth precisely because Miss Oscar is not there to manage things [017-003]. Her absence during the [[chimneys|Chimneys]] crisis underscores how much of Lomax's effectiveness depends on his staff.

## Relationships

- **[[george-lomax|George Lomax]]** — Miss Oscar's employer, utterly dependent on her administrative competence [004-007], [004-008]
- **[[bill-eversleigh|Bill Eversleigh]]** — Lomax's subordinate who feels the loss of Miss Oscar's support most acutely [012-026], [017-003]

## Significance

Miss Oscar is a minor character who exemplifies Agatha Christie's skill at creating memorable figures with the briefest of strokes. Described only as "a great mountain of a woman with a deep bass voice," she nevertheless conveys the solidity and competence of the professional civil servant class — the invisible infrastructure upon which government actually runs. Her absence from [[chimneys|Chimneys]] is a recurring note of comic frustration, a reminder that even the most ambitious political schemes depend on someone to answer the telephone and write the letters.
