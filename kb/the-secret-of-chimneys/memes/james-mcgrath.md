## Overview

James "Jimmy" McGrath is a Canadian adventurer who has spent time in Africa. He is an old friend of [[anthony-cade|Anthony Cade]] and the catalyst for the novel's central plot. Before departing for Africa, he entrusts Anthony with two critical items: [[count-stylptitch-memoirs|the manuscript]] of [[count-stylptitch|Count Stylptitch]]'s memoirs and a packet of letters from a deceased British agent. His off-stage role as the keeper of these documents drives the political intrigue that unfolds at [[chimneys|Chimneys]].

## Character Background

McGrath is a Canadian by nationality and a journalist by profession, with influence in journalistic circles [014-008]. He spent considerable time in the interior of Africa, where he first met [[anthony-cade|Anthony Cade]] under dramatic circumstances — tied up and about to be cooked by a cannibal tribe before [[anthony-cade|Cade]] arrived to rescue him [001-018]. This shared experience forged a strong bond between the two men.

## Role in the Plot

McGrath appears only in the opening chapter, but his actions propel the entire narrative. He visits [[anthony-cade|Anthony Cade]] in Bulawayo with a proposition: deliver [[count-stylptitch-memoirs|the memoirs]] of the late [[count-stylptitch|Count Stylptitch]] to a [[london|London]] publisher for a thousand pounds, and return a packet of compromising letters to their sender [001-004]. McGrath had come into possession of these documents after rescuing a dying man in Africa — a British agent who had been carrying the letters [001-020]. The memoirs, however, came to him by a different route: four years earlier in Paris, McGrath had intervened in a street assault and rescued an elderly gentleman who turned out to be [[count-stylptitch|Count Stylptitch]] himself [001-092]. After Stylptitch's death, the manuscript was forwarded to McGrath as a token of gratitude [001-096].

> "I was walking along one night in rather a lonely part, when I saw half a dozen French toughs beating up a respectable-looking old gentleman. I hate a one-sided show, so I promptly butted in and proceeded to beat up the toughs." [001-092]

McGrath's decision to have Anthony impersonate him (traveling under the name "James McGrath") creates a case of mistaken identity that leads to Anthony being pursued by various interested parties [001-039]. [[george-lomax|George Lomax]] and the [[foreign-office|British Foreign Office]], believing Anthony to be the real McGrath, attempt to recruit him for their [[herzoslovakia|Herzoslovakia]]n political scheme [007-028].

> "Passage booked in your name, I suppose, so I'd better travel as James McGrath. We've outgrown the passport business, haven't we?" [001-039]

## Personality and Traits

McGrath is portrayed as a practical, easy-going man with a taste for whiskey. He is described as having a "slow grin" and a straightforward manner [001-050]. Unlike Anthony, who is drawn into the political machinations, McGrath prefers to stay out of matters that don't directly interest him [001-048]. His willingness to part with the documents for a modest fee suggests a lack of personal ambition, in contrast to the politically motivated characters. His one consuming passion is the pursuit of gold — a fixation that explains why he would rather prospect in the African interior than deliver the memoirs himself [001-035].

> Mcgrath poured out his own drink, tossed it off with a practised hand and mixed a second one. [001-021]

> "I'm after gold, Anthony—far up in the interior." [001-035]

## Relationship to Other Characters

- **[[anthony-cade|Anthony Cade]]**: McGrath's closest friend, whom he trusts completely with the delivery of the sensitive documents [001-008]
- **[[count-stylptitch|Count Stylptitch]]**: McGrath never meets the Count, but becomes the custodian of his memoirs through a chain of events in Africa
- **[[george-lomax|George Lomax]]**: Never meets McGrath directly, but Lomax's interest in "Mr. McGrath" as a journalist with Dominion influence drives much of the early plot [005-022]

## Significance

James McGrath serves as the novel's catalyst figure. Though absent for most of the story, the documents he entrusts to [[anthony-cade|Anthony Cade]] are the MacGuffin that draws all the major characters — British politicians, [[herzoslovakia|Herzoslovakia]]n royalists, and international criminals — into conflict at [[chimneys|Chimneys]]. His role exemplifies Agatha Christie's technique of using an off-stage character to generate the central mystery.
