## Overview

The Rose Garden at [[chimneys|Chimneys]] is a formal garden featuring "a sundial which was on a raised hillock in the exact centre" [023-113]. It has a little gate at either end and is the site where [[anthony-cade|Anthony Cade]], in one of the novel's most satisfying moments of deduction, realizes the true meaning of [[count-stylptitch|Count Stylptitch]]'s cryptic clue.

## Description

The Rose Garden is accessed from the house by a side door, approached "by a circuitous route" [023-113]. Its defining features are its roses and its central sundial. The garden's enclosure — with gates at both ends — makes it a space for private encounters, as demonstrated when Anthony unexpectedly meets another character there.

The garden's significance lies not in its physical grandeur but in its symbolic value: the rose, as Anthony realizes, is the key to Stylptitch's cipher.

## Role in the Novel

The Rose Garden is the setting for the novel's central moment of revelation. After the frustrating search of the secret passage, Anthony is walking alone when the solution strikes him: "Of course. The Rose Garden! That's it! The Rose Garden" [023-112]. He realizes that "that little device of [[count-stylptitch|Count Stylptitch]]'s stood for what it had originally stood for — a Rose" [029-010] and that the jewel is hidden not in a secret passage but in the garden itself.

When Anthony arrives at the Rose Garden, he "stopped dead and stared at another occupant of the Rose Garden who seemed equally surprised to see him" [023-114] — a dramatic encounter that brings the hunt for [[koh-i-noor-diamond|the K]]oh-i-Noor to its climax.

The Rose Garden also serves as a place of private contemplation earlier in the novel — [[hiram-fish|Hiram Fish]] is observed "pacing up and down the Rose Garden yesterday afternoon, smoking one of those unpleasant cigars of his" [025-015], behaviour that takes on new significance in retrospect.
