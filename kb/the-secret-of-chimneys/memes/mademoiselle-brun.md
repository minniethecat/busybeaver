## Overview

Mademoiselle Brun is introduced as the French governess employed by the [[lord-caterham|Caterham]] family to look after [[lord-caterham|Lord Caterham]]'s younger daughters, Dulcie and Daisy. Hired approximately two months before the events of the novel, she came to the household while the family was in Scotland [011-066]. Beneath this unassuming exterior, however, she conceals one of the most remarkable criminal identities in the novel: she is in fact Angèle Mory, a former Parisian actress who rose to become [[queen-varaga|Queen Varaga of Herzoslovakia]], and who faked her own death during the revolution that deposed the monarchy [028-032].

## Character Background

Mademoiselle Brun is described as "a small, middle-aged woman with a sallow face, pepper and salt hair, and a budding moustache" [016-003] — a deliberately nondescript appearance that makes her virtually invisible among the household staff. According to the household, she has been employed by the Comtesse de Breteuil in Brittany for the previous seven to ten years and comes with excellent references [022-008].

In reality, she is Angèle Mory, a clever actress who performed at the Folies Bergères in Paris. The Sûreté suspected her of association with the master criminal [[king-victor|King Victor]], operating under the alias Captain O'Neill. When the young [[nicholas-iv|King Nicholas IV]] of [[herzoslovakia|Herzoslovakia]] visited Paris, the revolutionary [[red-hand|Comrades of the Red Hand]] approached her to help decoy the king — but Angèle Mory was far more ambitious than her employers. She captivated the king, who fell in love with her, and she successfully maneuvered herself into becoming [[queen-varaga|Queen Varaga of Herzoslovakia]], passing herself off as an offshoot of the Romanov dynasty [028-015].

## Role in the Plot

As governess at [[chimneys|Chimneys]], Mademoiselle Brun occupies the second room from the end on the left — the very room where [[anthony-cade|Anthony Cade]] saw a light burning on the night of Prince Michael's murder [011-066]. She consistently avoids contact with the house guests who might recognize her, retreating with a migraine on the day [[baron-lolopretjzyl|Baron Lolopretjzyl]] visits, and keeping to the schoolroom [015-011].

Her true purpose at [[chimneys|Chimneys]] is to recover the Koh-i-Noor diamond, which she had hidden there seven years earlier during her visit as [[queen-varaga|Queen Varaga]]. On the night of the murder, she is searching the library when she unexpectedly encounters Prince Michael, who recognizes her. Facing exposure, she shoots him [028-003]. She then returns the stolen letters to [[virginia-revel|Virginia Revel]]'s room to confuse the investigation and plants the revolver in [[herman-isaacstein|Herman Isaacstein]]'s suitcase [028-006].

> "I suspected her from the first," explained Anthony. "There was a light in her room on the night of the murder. Afterwards, I wavered. I made inquiries about her in Brittany, and came back satisfied that she was what she represented herself to be. I was a fool. Because the Comtesse de Breteuil had employed a Mademoiselle Brun and spoke highly of her, it never occurred to me that the real Mademoiselle Brun might have been kidnapped on her way to her new post, and that it might be a substitute taking her place." [028-003]

## The Imposture

The key to Mademoiselle Brun's deception is a substitution. The real Mademoiselle Brun — a genuine governess with years of service at the Château de Breteuil — was intercepted and replaced by her impostor en route to her new position with the [[lord-caterham|Caterham]] family. [[anthony-cade|Anthony Cade]]'s inquiries in Dinard confirmed the existence and good reputation of a Mademoiselle Brun, which initially cleared her of suspicion [028-003]. The elaborate backstory — seven years of service with a respected aristocratic family — was borrowed wholesale from the real governess's life.

## The Final Confrontation

Mademoiselle Brun's true identity is exposed during a climactic scene in the library at [[chimneys|Chimneys]]. While she is frantically searching the bookshelves for the hidden diamond, [[boris-anchoukoff|Boris Anchoukoff]] — Prince Michael's devoted valet — attacks her. In the ensuing struggle, the pistol she carries discharges, and she is killed [027-026]. Her death reveals the full extent of her duplicity.

> They drew nearer. On the floor, the pistol still clasped in her hand, and an expression of deadly malignity on her face, lay — Mademoiselle Brun. [027-029]

[[baron-lolopretjzyl|Baron Lolopretjzyl]], who had known the [[herzoslovakia|Herzoslovakia]]n court, confirms her identity after Anthony urges him to look past the makeup and remember that she was once an actress [028-022].

## Relationship to Other Characters

- **[[king-victor|King Victor]]**: Her long-time confederate and lover, with whom she corresponded in code throughout her time as Queen of [[herzoslovakia|Herzoslovakia]]. Together they planned the theft of the Koh-i-Noor [028-015].
- **[[prince-michael-obolovitch|Prince Michael Obolovitch]]**: The man who recognized her at [[chimneys|Chimneys]], forcing her to murder him to protect her identity [028-003].
- **[[nicholas-iv|Nicholas IV]] of [[herzoslovakia|Herzoslovakia]]**: Her husband, the young king she captivated and married, later killed alongside her (ostensibly) in the revolution [028-013].
- **[[virginia-revel|Virginia Revel]]**: The only person at [[chimneys|Chimneys]] who might recognize her face, since [[virginia-revel|Virginia]] had known her as [[queen-varaga|Queen Varaga]] in [[herzoslovakia|Herzoslovakia]]. A deliberate plot involving a dead body was staged to prevent [[virginia-revel|Virginia]] from coming to [[chimneys|Chimneys]] [028-004].
- **[[boris-anchoukoff|Boris Anchoukoff]]**: Prince Michael's valet, who ultimately kills her in an act of vengeance [027-025].
- **The [[red-hand|Comrades of the Red Hand]]**: The revolutionary organization that originally recruited her but later became her enemy after she betrayed them [028-004].
