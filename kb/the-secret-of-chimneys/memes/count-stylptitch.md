# Count Stylptitch

Count Stylptitch was a legendary [[herzoslovakia|Herzoslovakia]]n statesman, diplomat, and master of Balkan intrigue whose posthumously published memoirs became the catalyst for the conspiracy that unfolded at [[chimneys|Chimneys]]. Known as "the Grand Old Man of the Balkans," he was widely regarded as one of the most accomplished political operators of his era.

## Reputation

[[anthony-cade|Anthony Cade]] described Stylptitch in emphatic terms:

> Many people who have never heard of Herzoslovakia would brighten at the mention of Count Stylptitch. The Grand Old Man of the Balkans. The Greatest Statesman of Modern Times. The biggest villain unhung. The point of view all depends on which newspaper you take in. [001-106]

His influence on Near Eastern politics was all-encompassing: "Every move and counter move in the Near East for the last twenty years has had Count Stylptitch at the bottom of it. He's been a dictator and a patriot and a statesman — and nobody knows exactly what he has been, except that he's been a perfect king of intrigue" [001-106].

## Life in Exile

After the assassination of [[nicholas-iv|King Nicholas IV]] of [[herzoslovakia|Herzoslovakia]], Stylptitch "went to live in Paris after the assassination of [[nicholas-iv|King Nicholas]]. They wanted him to come back and be president later, but he wasn't taking any. He remained sound to his monarchical principles, though he was reported to have his finger in all the backstairs pies that went on in the Balkans. Very deep, the late Count Stylptitch" [001-116]. He maintained a house near the Bois de Boulogne.

## The Memoirs

Stylptitch's memoirs became a central plot device. He arranged for his memoirs to be delivered to [[james-mcgrath|James McGrath]] — a man who had once rescued him from Apaches in Paris — with instructions to deliver [[count-stylptitch-memoirs|the manuscript]] to a [[london|London]] publisher by October 13, in exchange for a thousand pounds. The memoirs were routed from Paris to South Africa and back to England, an eccentric but deliberate arrangement by the Count.

The memoirs contained information of immense political sensitivity. [[lord-caterham|Lord Caterham]] noted they might expose "tyranny and misbehaviour of the Obolovitch family generally" [003-041], threatening the planned restoration of Prince Michael. The [[foreign-office|British Foreign Office]] was desperate to suppress them. As [[george-lomax|George Lomax]] explained, [[count-stylptitch-memoirs|the memoirs]] could "upset the apple cart" and raise awkward questions in Parliament about "why replace the present broad-minded and democratic form of Government by an obsolete tyranny" [003-041].

Stylptitch's motives for publishing the memoirs were a matter of speculation. [[anthony-cade|Anthony Cade]] questioned whether they were an act of posthumous malice:

> You know a lot of trouble has been caused by memoirs. Indiscreet revelations, that sort of thing. People who have been closed as an oyster all their lives seem positively to relish causing trouble when they themselves shall be comfortably dead. It gives them a kind of malicious glee. Jimmy, what sort of a man was Count Stylptitch? You met him and talked to him, and you're a pretty good judge of raw human nature. Could you imagine him being a vindictive old devil? [002-025]

The convergence of Stylptitch's death, his memoirs, and Balkan geopolitics struck Cade as no coincidence:

> The whole thing seems rather suggestive, doesn't it? The death of Stylptitch, the memoirs, and the rumours in the papers — all vague but interesting. And there's a further rumour to the effect that they've found oil in Herzoslovakia. I've a feeling in my bones, James, that people are getting ready to be interested in that unimportant little country. [002-054]

## Knowledge of the Koh-i-Noor

Stylptitch possessed crucial knowledge about the theft of the famous Koh-i-Noor diamond, which had disappeared from [[chimneys|Chimneys]] seven years earlier. He was present at [[chimneys|Chimneys]] when the jewel vanished, and understood "not all, but something" [003-052] of what occurred. He was at one time "at loggerheads" with the Foreign Office "over the Turkish question," raising the possibility that he had "in sheer malice" recorded the full story in his memoirs [003-052]. When inebriated during his rescue in Paris, he told [[james-mcgrath|McGrath]] "he knew where the Koh-i-Noor was" [019-061] and that "the gang in question were [[king-victor|King Victor]]'s men" [019-061]. This intelligence proved critical to the eventual recovery of the jewel.

## The Rose Clue

Stylptitch left a final puzzle in his memoirs: a reference to a "Flower dinner" where guests wore flower badges. He himself wore "the exact duplicate of that curious device we found in the cavity in the secret passage. It represented a rose" [027-048]. This clue, combined with the cipher message referencing Richmond, led [[anthony-cade|Anthony Cade]] to deduce the jewel's hiding place in the Rose Garden at [[chimneys|Chimneys]] [029-010].
