# Theft of the Koh-i-Noor

The theft (or disappearance) of [[koh-i-noor-diamond|the K]]oh-i-Noor diamond from [[chimneys]] is the novel's central hidden mystery — a scandal from the past that was carefully suppressed by the [[foreign-office]], but whose secrets threaten to erupt into the present through [[count-stylptitch]]'s memoirs. The incident occurred during the tenure of the eighth Marquis of [[lord-caterham|Caterham]], and its exposure threatens to destabilize the [[herzoslovakia]] negotiations and ruin political careers.

## The Historical Incident

The disappearance occurred "while they were at [[chimneys|Chimneys]]" during the time of the eighth Marquis — [[lord-caterham]]'s illustrious brother, who was then Secretary of State for Foreign Affairs. According to [[george-lomax]], the event "almost ruined his career" (003-PN). The precise circumstances remain undisclosed to the reader for much of the novel, but the incident was serious enough that "no hint of the loss could be allowed to leak out" (003-PN).

## The Cover-Up

The British government went to extraordinary lengths to suppress knowledge of the theft. Lomax describes the approach taken as one of "the utmost discretion" (003-PN). The implication is that [[koh-i-noor-diamond|the K]]oh-i-Noor — one of the most famous diamonds in the world — was at [[chimneys]] for reasons never fully explained, and its disappearance would have caused a scandal of international proportions.

As [[lord-caterham]] observes with characteristic relish: "Everyone would say — why was it hushed up?" And Lomax's own prediction — "Of course they would" — confirms that the official response would face intense public scrutiny if ever revealed (003-PN).

## Count Stylptitch's Knowledge

[[count-stylptitch]], the "Grand Old Man of the [[balkans|Balkans]]" and former prime minister of [[herzoslovakia]], was present at [[chimneys]] at the time of the disappearance. As Lomax explains: "He knew something. Not all, but something. We were at loggerheads with him once or twice over the Turkish question. Suppose that in sheer malice he has set the whole thing down for the world to read" (003-PN).

This knowledge — contained in [[count-stylptitch-memoirs|the memoirs]] that [[james-mcgrath]] brings from Africa — is what makes [[count-stylptitch-memoirs|the manuscript]] so dangerous. Its publication could expose not only the theft itself but the government's decision to conceal it.

## Connection to the Present Plot

The theft of [[koh-i-noor-diamond|the K]]oh-i-Noor connects to the novel's present-day events in several ways:

1. **The Memoirs**: [[count-stylptitch]]'s manuscript, delivered by [[anthony-cade]], contains revelations about the theft that could derail the [[herzoslovakia]] restoration
2. **The Murder**: [[prince-michael-obolovitch]]'s murder at [[chimneys]] may be connected to [[koh-i-noor-diamond|the diamond]] — revolutionary factions, jewel thieves, and political operatives all have motives
3. **[[king-victor|King Victor]]**: The international [[king-victor|jewel thief]] [[king-victor]] has an interest in [[koh-i-noor-diamond|the diamond]] and may have been involved in its original disappearance or its present whereabouts
4. **The Coded Message**: The manuscript itself contains hidden information, possibly including the location of the missing gem

## The Diamond Itself

The Koh-i-Noor (Persian for "Mountain of Light") is one of the largest cut diamonds in the world, historically part of the British Crown Jewels. Its presence at [[chimneys]] rather than in the Tower of [[london|London]] is itself a mystery — as [[anthony-cade]] quips: "We all know that. They keep it in the Tower, don't they? Behind thick plate glass and iron bars, with a lot of gentlemen in fancy dress standing round to see you don't pinch anything" (002-PN).

## Significance

The theft of [[koh-i-noor-diamond|the K]]oh-i-Noor represents the novel's "buried past" — a crime whose suppression embodies the tension between public appearance and hidden reality that drives the entire plot. Like the [[count-stylptitch-memoirs|coded manuscript]], the hidden passages of [[chimneys]], and the assumed identities of several characters, [[koh-i-noor-diamond|the diamond]] symbolizes the gap between official narrative and underlying truth.
