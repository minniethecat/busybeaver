## Overview

King Nicholas IV of [[herzoslovakia|Herzoslovakia]] was the last reigning monarch of the Balkan kingdom before its transformation into a republic. Referred to in reverent tones by the [[herzoslovakia|Herzoslovakia]]n royalists as "His Most Gracious Majesty King Nicholas IV of blessed memory" [005-041], he was assassinated in a popular uprising approximately seven years before the novel's events. His death — and the circumstances surrounding his controversial marriage — set in motion the political conspiracy that drives the plot.

## Character Profile

### Background

Nicholas IV was the ruler of [[herzoslovakia|Herzoslovakia]], one of the Balkan States, with its capital at Ekarest [001-100]. He belonged to the Obolovitch dynasty, which royalists sought to restore after his death [002-040]. His premiership included [[count-stylptitch|Count Stylptitch]], who went into exile in Paris after the King's assassination [001-116].

### Marriage to Angèle Mory

Nicholas IV's defining and ultimately fatal decision was his marriage to [[mademoiselle-brun|Angèle Mory]], a Parisian actress of humble origins. According to [[superintendent-battle|Superintendent Battle]]'s account, the young king was captivated by Mory during a state visit to Paris, where she had been hired by the revolutionary [[red-hand|Comrades of the Red Hand]] to "infatuate the young king" [019-0[[TOC|31]]]. However, Mory proved more ambitious than her employers anticipated — she "conceived the idea of being — not a king's mistress, but a queen" [019-032].

> "She was introduced into Herzoslovakia as the Countess Varaga Popoleffsky, an offshoot of the Romanovs, and became eventually Queen Varaga of Herzoslovakia. Not bad for a little Parisian actress!" [019-032]

[[anthony-cade|Anthony Cade]] offers a more irreverent summary: "She was some little guttersnipe of a music hall artiste in Paris — not even suitable for a morganatic alliance. But Nicholas had a frightful crush on her, and she was all out for being a queen" [001-118].

## Key Actions

Nicholas IV's reign ended in bloodshed. He "squared his ministers" to approve the marriage to [[queen-varaga|Varaga]], had the ceremony performed "in the cathedral at Ekarest with a couple of unwilling Archbishops" [001-118], and had [[queen-varaga|Varaga]] crowned as Queen. However, he "forgot to reckon with the populace" — the aristocratic and reactionary [[herzoslovakia|Herzoslovakia]]ns resented their low-born queen [001-118].

The result was "mutterings and discontent, and the usual ruthless suppressions, and the final uprising which stormed the palace, murdered the king and queen, and proclaimed a republic" [001-118]. The bodies of Nicholas and [[queen-varaga|Varaga]] were "horribly mutilated and hardly recognizable," attesting to "the fury of the populace against the lowborn foreign queen" [019-032].

Seven years before the novel's action, Nicholas IV and [[queen-varaga|Queen Varaga]] visited England as guests of the late Marquis of [[lord-caterham|Caterham]], then Secretary of State for Foreign Affairs [019-037]. During this visit, it is believed that [[queen-varaga|Varaga]] — using her skills as a professional thief — stole the Koh-i-Noor diamond, with a substitute created by [[king-victor|King Victor]] [019-037].

## Relationships

- **[[queen-varaga|Queen Varaga]] ([[mademoiselle-brun|Angèle Mory]])** — his wife, whose low birth and criminal past led to his downfall [001-118], [019-0[[TOC|31]]]
- **[[count-stylptitch|Count Stylptitch]]** — his prime minister, who went into exile after the assassination [001-116]
- **[[prince-michael-obolovitch|Prince Michael Obolovitch]]** — his heir and the pretender to the [[herzoslovakia|Herzoslovakia]]n throne [005-041]
- **The Obolovitch Dynasty** — the royal house Nicholas represented, which partisans still seek to restore [002-040]

## Significance

Nicholas IV never appears directly in the novel — he is dead before the story begins — but his fate casts a long shadow over every character. His ill-advised marriage to [[mademoiselle-brun|Angèle Mory]]/[[queen-varaga|Varaga]] connects the [[herzoslovakia|Herzoslovakia]]n political crisis directly to the jewel theft at [[chimneys|Chimneys]]. The dead king is the absent centre around which the novel's competing factions — royalists, revolutionaries, British diplomats, and international criminals — all orbit.
