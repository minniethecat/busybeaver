## Overview

"Count Stanislaus" is the alias used by [[prince-michael-obolovitch|Prince Michael Obolovitch]] of [[herzoslovakia|Herzoslovakia]] during his stay at [[chimneys|Chimneys]]. The incognito is intended to conceal his true identity and the political purpose of his visit — to negotiate an oil concession loan with the financier [[herman-isaacstein|Herman Isaacstein]], with the backing of the British Government [011-048]. The name is revealed to be a fiction on the morning after the murder, when [[george-lomax|George Lomax]] is forced to disclose the victim's true identity to [[superintendent-battle|Superintendent Battle]] [012-012].

## The Alias

The name "Count Stanislaus" is chosen as a thin cover for the Prince's presence at the weekend party. [[lord-caterham|Lord Caterham]], when asked by the doctor to identify the dead man, hesitates before offering the name, suggesting his discomfort with the deception [010-023]. The alias is known to the household staff, who address the guest accordingly, and to the innkeeper at the Jolly Cricketers, where the name was used to identify the foreign gentleman traveling with [[lord-caterham|Lord Caterham]] [012-016].

The ruse is maintained even after the murder, with [[george-lomax|George Lomax]] insisting on its importance to the investigation. Only when pressed directly by [[superintendent-battle|Superintendent Battle]] does Lomax reveal: "The gentleman who was staying here incognito as Count Stanislaus was His Highness [[prince-michael-obolovitch|Prince Michael of Herzoslovakia]]" [012-018].

## Role in the Plot

The alias "Count Stanislaus" serves a dual narrative function. On the level of plot, it represents the British Government's attempt to conduct secret diplomacy — arranging a loan to a would-be monarch under the cover of a country-house shooting party. On the level of mystery, the false identity complicates the initial investigation, as the police must first determine who the victim really was before they can identify a motive for his murder.

> "And the name of the murdered man was Count Stanislaus?" queried the doctor. [010-024]

The revelation of the alias is a turning point in the investigation. Once [[superintendent-battle|Battle]] knows the victim's true identity as Prince Michael, the political dimensions of the crime become clear — the [[herzoslovakia|Herzoslovakia]]n succession, the oil concessions, and the involvement of revolutionary groups all come into focus [012-018].

## Significance

The alias "Count Stanislaus" exemplifies the novel's preoccupation with concealed identities and imposture. It stands at the center of a web of false identities that includes [[mademoiselle-brun|Mademoiselle Brun]] (actually [[queen-varaga|Queen Varaga]] / [[mademoiselle-brun|Angèle Mory]]), the false [[monsieur-lemoine|Lemoine]] ([[king-victor|King Victor]] in disguise), and [[anthony-cade|Anthony Cade]] (traveling under [[james-mcgrath|James McGrath]]'s name). The name itself, with its vaguely Eastern European sound, is deliberately vague — as Anthony later explains to [[hiram-fish|Hiram Fish]], "He was staying here as Count Stanislaus" [015-006].
