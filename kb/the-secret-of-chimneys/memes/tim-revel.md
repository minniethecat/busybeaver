# Tim Revel

Tim Revel (full name Timothy Revel) was the late husband of [[virginia-revel|Virginia Revel]], a diplomat whose career and death shaped Virginia's circumstances at the opening of *The Secret of Chimneys*. Though deceased before the novel begins, his influence on the plot is significant: his diplomatic posting in [[herzoslovakia|Herzoslovakia]] gave Virginia the Balkan expertise that [[george-lomax|George Lomax]] sought to exploit, and his death left her a young, independent widow of means.

## Character and Background

Tim Revel was Irish — "he was Irish, you know, and most attractive, with a genius for expressing himself" (024-026). He had been attached to the British Embassy in [[herzoslovakia|Herzoslovakia]], where he would have had "a fine career — and mostly owing to Virginia" had he lived (024-021). [[lord-caterham|Lord Caterham]], who knew of him through diplomatic circles, noted that Virginia's knowledge of Herzoslovakia came through "her late husband, Tim Revel, who had been attached to the British Embassy there" (003-075).

His death preceded the novel's events by an unspecified period. Virginia was listed in the telephone directory as "The Hon. Mrs. Timothy Revel of 487, Pont Street" (005-085), suggesting both that Tim held an honorific title and that the couple maintained a fashionable London address.

## Marriage to Virginia

The marriage to Virginia appears to have been one of genuine affection, but not necessarily of deep romantic passion. When [[superintendent-battle|Battle]] remarks upon Virginia's apparent lack of grief, [[anthony-cade|Anthony]] defends her: "Tim Revel was bowled over by Virginia — he was Irish, you know, and most attractive, with a genius for expressing himself. The chances are that Virginia was swept off her feet" (024-026). Anthony concludes that "Virginia was never in love with Tim Revel" (024-023), suggesting the marriage was a product of youthful impulse rather than lasting devotion.

Nevertheless, society regarded Virginia's widowed status with a mixture of admiration and suspicion. Some interpreted her lightheartedness as evidence that "Virginia had never really cared for Tim Revel, and that she found it insincere to profess a grief she did not feel" (006-006). Virginia herself embraced the role of "wicked widow" [004-071] with characteristic independence and humour.

## Diplomatic Legacy

Tim Revel's diplomatic career had one lasting consequence: it connected Virginia to [[herzoslovakia|Herzoslovakia]] and the [[balkans|Balkans]] at exactly the moment when British interests there were intensifying. [[george-lomax|George Lomax]]'s selection of Virginia as hostess for the [[chimneys|Chimneys]] conference rested on this connection — she knew the players and the stakes from her time in Herzoslovakia with Tim.

The posting also established Virginia's financial independence. The [[pont-street|Pont Street]] house, where the body of [[prince-michael-obolovitch|Prince Michael]] is discovered, was the Revels' London residence, and Virginia's widowhood left her in comfortable circumstances that allowed her to refuse George Lomax's summonses with impunity.

## Significance

Though never seen onstage, Tim Revel is a structural necessity in the novel's architecture. His death:
- Positions Virginia as an independent widow, free to become [[anthony-cade|Anthony Cade]]'s love interest
- Provides Virginia's Balkan expertise, justifying her role in the diplomatic plot
- Establishes her social standing (Hon. Mrs.) and financial independence
- Creates the [[pont-street|Pont Street]] household where the novel's central crime occurs

His absence is as functional as any character's presence — Christie uses the dead husband to give Virginia the freedom of action that a married woman of her era would not have possessed.
