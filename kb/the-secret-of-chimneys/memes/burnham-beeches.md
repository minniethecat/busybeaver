## Role in the Novel

Burnham Beeches is the location where Anthony Cade conceals a critical piece of evidence—a pistol wrapped in brown paper, hidden in the branches of a giant tree [2093-2093]. This act of concealment demonstrates Anthony's resourcefulness and his instinct to protect himself by dispersing evidence rather than destroying it.

## Description

The area is described as a wooded region on the route between Chimneys and London. Anthony makes a deliberate detour to reach it after an important errand, choosing "a giant of the forest" to climb—a feat described as "something of a feat, even for Anthony" [2093-2093].

## Plot Significance

The hidden pistol later becomes a point of private satisfaction for Anthony. When he reflects on his changing circumstances and his growing relationship with Virginia Revel, he thinks of the pistol still hidden in the tree at Burnham Beeches and "smiled inwardly" [2999-2999]. The concealment represents Anthony's awareness that he may still need the evidence, even as his social position improves through Virginia's vouching for him.

> He made a detour to the right, returning to London by way of Burnham Beeches. There again he halted the car, and choosing a giant of the forest he deliberately climbed the huge tree. It was something of a feat, even for Anthony. To one of the topmost branches, he affixed a small brown-paper parcel, concealing it in a little niche close to the bole. [2093-2093]

> He thought of the pistol in the tree at Burnham Beeches, and smiled inwardly. [2999-2999]
