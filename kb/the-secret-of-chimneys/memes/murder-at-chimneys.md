# Murder at Chimneys

The murder that takes place at the [[chimneys|Chimneys]] country house, serving as the criminal event that draws [[scotland-yard|Scotland Yard]] into the [[herzoslovakia|Herzoslovakia]]n conspiracy and sets the detective investigation in motion alongside the political intrigue. The victim is [[prince-michael-obolovitch|Prince Michael Obolovitch]] of Herzoslovakia, shot dead in the Council Chamber on the night of his arrival at the estate.

## Summary

During a house party at [[chimneys|Chimneys]] — gathered under the pretense of diplomatic discussions about the [[herzoslovakia|Herzoslovakia]]n succession — a body is discovered in the Council Chamber on Friday morning. The victim, staying incognito as "Count Stanislaus," is revealed to be [[prince-michael-obolovitch|Prince Michael Obolovitch]], a claimant to the Herzoslovakian throne who had come to negotiate oil concessions with the financier [[herman-isaacstein|Herman Isaacstein]], backed by the [[foreign-office|British Foreign Office]] through [[george-lomax|George Lomax]].

The discovery triggers an immediate call to [[scotland-yard|Scotland Yard]], and [[superintendent-battle|Superintendent Battle]] arrives to lead the investigation. Working alongside the house guests — including [[anthony-cade|Anthony Cade]], who had heard the fatal shot while trespassing on the estate, and [[lady-eileen-bundle-brent|Lady Eileen "Bundle" Brent]], the daughter of the house — Battle must untangle a web of competing interests: royal claimants, revolutionary agents, international financiers, and the secret of [[chimneys|Chimneys]] itself.

The investigation reveals that the murder is inextricably linked to the earlier death of Giuseppe Manelli, an Italian blackmailer shot dead in London at the home of [[virginia-revel|Virginia Revel]]. Anthony Cade had disposed of that body to protect Virginia, an act that places him under immediate suspicion when the Chimneys murder is discovered. [016-035]

## The Crime

### Discovery of the Body

The body was discovered at approximately 7:45 a.m. on Friday morning by a housemaid polishing the floor of the Council Chamber. The room had been dark when she entered; she had crossed to the windows, drawn the curtains, and begun polishing before she came upon the corpse. By that time, half the parquet floor had already been polished, destroying any interior footprints that might have been left. [014-079]

Lord Caterham was summoned from his bed. Inspector Badgworthy of the Market Basing police arrived with Dr. Cartwright, and the body was examined in situ. George Lomax, staying at nearby Wyvern Abbey, was telephoned by Bundle and arrived in a state of considerable agitation, immediately dispatching wires to summon Superintendent Battle of Scotland Yard. [014-131]

> Near the left-hand wall, about halfway between the door and the window, a man was lying on his back, his arms flung wide. Dr. Cartwright went over and knelt down by the body. The inspector strode across to the windows, and examined them in turn. The centre one was closed, but not fastened. On the steps outside were footprints leading up to the window, and a second set going away again. [014-076]

### Time of Death

The victim's wristwatch provided the critical temporal evidence: it had been smashed by the fall when the body dropped, its hands frozen at a quarter to twelve. This precise time stamp was corroborated by Anthony Cade, who later testified that he had been on the terrace at approximately 11:45 p.m. and had heard a single shot from within the house. [016-011]

> This morning a housemaid discovered the body at approximately 7:45 a.m. Dr. Cartwright examined the dead man and found that death was the result of a bullet fired from a revolver. No revolver was found, and no one in the house seems to have heard the shot. On the other hand the dead man's wristwatch was smashed by the fall, and marks the crime as having been committed at exactly a quarter to twelve. [015-040]

Superintendent Battle, once he arrived and took charge of the case from Inspector Badgworthy, confirmed these essential facts: the victim had been shot through the heart in the Council Chamber, the bullet had not been recovered, and the murder weapon was missing. The autopsy would later be required to determine the make of the revolver. [016-107]

### The Open Window

The most puzzling element of the crime scene was the state of the French windows. Three long windows opened from the Council Chamber onto the terrace. When Anthony Cade had tried them all immediately after hearing the shot, every one had been fastened from the inside. Yet by the following morning, the centre window stood unlatched. This discrepancy was the first indication to Superintendent Battle that the crime might not be the simple outside job that the footprints suggested. [016-095]

> "When the crime was discovered this morning, the middle one was open — not latched, that is to say."
>
> "Whew!" said Anthony, sinking down on the window seat, and taking out his cigarette case. "That's rather a blow. That opens up quite a different aspect of the case. It leaves us two alternatives. Either he was killed by someone in the house, and that someone unlatched the window after I had gone to make it look like an outside job — incidentally with me as Little Willie — or else, not to mince matters, I'm lying." [016-095]

Battle, as he later confided, had entertained suspicions of an inside job from the very start. The trail of footprints, the convenient suspicious stranger at the village inn, and the boots that matched the marks too perfectly — all of it was, in his assessment, "a bit too flaring." [016-101]

## The Victim

Prince Michael Obolovitch of Herzoslovakia had arrived at Chimneys on Thursday afternoon, travelling down from London with Lord Caterham. He was accompanied by his valet, Boris Anchoukoff, a loyal Herzoslovakian retainer; his equerry, Captain Andrassy, had remained in town. The Prince registered under the name "Count Stanislaus" — an incognito whose flimsiness was immediately apparent to everyone in the house. [015-038]

The purpose of his visit was not social but financial. Through the good offices of George Lomax and the British Foreign Office, Prince Michael was to meet with Herman Isaacstein, the international financier, to arrange a substantial loan. In return, the Prince pledged to grant oil concessions in Herzoslovakia to Isaacstein's companies upon ascending the throne. The British Government supported Prince Michael's claim because of his pronounced pro-British sympathies. [015-031]

The Prince had not appeared at dinner on Thursday evening. Claiming fatigue from the journey, he had dined in his own apartments and retired early. He met none of the other house guests — a circumstance that narrowed the field of those who could have known his true identity, yet paradoxically widened the circle of those who might have had reason to wish him dead. [015-038]

## Key Evidence

### Physical Evidence

The crime scene yielded several pieces of physical evidence:

- **Footprints**: Two sets of footprints outside the Council Chamber windows — one approaching, one departing. These matched the boots of Anthony Cade, who had walked across the park from the road. The boots had been collected from the Jolly Cricketers inn by Constable Johnson for comparison. [015-040]
- **The Red Hand Note**: A half-sheet of notepaper bearing the emblem of the Comrades of the Red Hand — a revolutionary Herzoslovakian organization — was found underneath the body. Anthony Cade had encountered this group before in London, when they had attempted to recruit him to their cause. [016-115]
- **Smashed Wristwatch**: The victim's watch, broken in the fall, fixed the time of death precisely at 11:45 p.m. [015-040]
- **Absent Revolver**: The murder weapon was not found at the scene or anywhere in the vicinity. Its disappearance was one of the case's persistent mysteries until the final resolution. [015-040]

### Circumstantial Complexities

The investigation was complicated by several overlapping factors. Anthony Cade had been present on the estate at the exact time of the murder. He had come to Chimneys not as an invited guest but as an investigator following up a torn scrap of paper, recovered from the dead blackmailer Giuseppe in London, which read "Chimneys 11:45 Thursday." [016-052] This scrap linked the London murder to the Chimneys murder, suggesting a coordinated conspiracy.

Furthermore, Anthony's disposal of Giuseppe's body — driving it out of London and depositing it by a roadside near Staines — meant he could not fully disclose his movements without incriminating himself in a separate crime. [013-084] He instead presented a carefully edited version of events to Superintendent Battle, suppressing the body disposal while truthfully recounting his presence on the terrace when the shot was fired. [016-052]

The presence of the Red Hand emblem at both crime scenes further muddied the waters. Giuseppe had carried Red Hand material in London; the same emblem appeared beneath Prince Michael's body. Whether this indicated genuine revolutionary involvement or a frame-up by someone exploiting the Red Hand's reputation was a question that occupied Battle throughout the case. [016-114]

## Investigation

### Superintendent Battle Takes Charge

Superintendent Battle of Scotland Yard arrived at Chimneys within hours of the discovery, summoned personally by George Lomax, who insisted on "the utmost discretion" given the sensitive political dimensions of the case. [015-016] Battle, a squarely built middle-aged man with a face "singularly devoid of expression," brought to the investigation his characteristic patience and methodical approach. [015-017]

Battle's first act was to establish the facts: the identity of the dead man, the circumstances of his presence at Chimneys, the composition of the house party, and the movements of everyone on the night of the murder. He was struck at once by the anomaly of Anthony Cade's behaviour — a man who, if guilty, should have fled, but instead presented himself voluntarily at the house to offer his account. [015-076]

### Anthony Cade's Testimony

Anthony Cade arrived at Chimneys from the Jolly Cricketers, the village inn where he had spent the night, and asked to speak to Lord Caterham. Ushered into the library where Battle, Colonel Melrose (the Chief Constable), George Lomax, and Lord Caterham were conferring, he volunteered that he had been on the estate at 11:45 the previous night and had heard the shot. [016-011]

His story, as told to the assembled group, was a calculated blend of truth and omission. He recounted his journey from Bulawayo, his commission from Jimmy McGrath to deliver Count Stylptitch's memoirs, his encounters with rival Herzoslovakian factions in London, and the burglary attempt at the Blitz Hotel. He produced the torn scrap of paper — "Chimneys 11:45 Thursday" — explaining that it had prompted his nocturnal visit to the estate. He described hearing the shot, trying the windows, finding them fastened, and concluding the sound must have been a poacher. [016-052]

What he did not reveal was his disposal of Giuseppe's body the previous evening — a suppression that would hang over the investigation until the final revelations forced everything into the open.

### Identification of the Victim

Battle took Anthony to view the body, which had been moved to a small drawing-room. The superintendent whisked away the sheet, watching for Anthony's reaction. Anthony's start of recognition was immediate: he had seen this man before, but not as Prince Michael Obolovitch. The man had come to his hotel room days earlier, purporting to be a representative of the publishers Balderson and Hodgkins and calling himself "Mr. Holmes" — an alias for Prince Michael, who had been attempting to retrieve Count Stylptitch's memoirs before they could be published. [016-129]

This revelation connected the London events — the manuscript, the blackmailer Giuseppe, the burglary attempt, the rival political deputations — directly to the dead man at Chimneys. The murder was not an isolated crime but the culmination of a conspiracy that had been unfolding since Anthony Cade first set foot in England. [016-130]

## Motives and Suspects

The investigation uncovered a dense thicket of competing motives. Prince Michael's death could have served the interests of:

- **Rival claimants** to the Herzoslovakian throne, who would benefit from the elimination of a British-backed candidate.
- **The Comrades of the Red Hand**, the revolutionary organization opposed to any monarchical restoration and committed to establishing a republic in Herzoslovakia.
- **Financial rivals**, including an American syndicate that had also sought oil concessions from Prince Michael and been rebuffed. [015-032]
- **Possessors of the Koh-i-Noor diamond**, the legendary gem stolen from the Tower of London years earlier, which was rumoured to be connected to the Herzoslovakian royal family and hidden somewhere within Chimneys.
- **Holders of Count Stylptitch's coded memoirs**, which contained dangerous political secrets that Prince Michael had been desperate to suppress.

The house party itself provided a limited circle of suspects: Lord Caterham (the reluctant host), Bundle Brent (his daughter), George Lomax (the Foreign Office mandarin), Herman Isaacstein (the financier), Hiram Fish (an American bibliophile with a letter of introduction from Lucius Gott), Bill Eversleigh (Lomax's young assistant), and Virginia Revel (a charming widow and cousin to George Lomax). The servants, including the Prince's own valet Boris Anchoukoff, also fell under Battle's scrutiny. [015-046]

## Significance

The murder at Chimneys serves as the central pivot of the novel, transforming what began as a political intrigue into a criminal investigation. It forces the hidden agendas of every character into the light and provides the mechanism through which the deeper secret of Chimneys — the hiding place of the Koh-i-Noor diamond and the true identity of the conspirator known as King Victor — is ultimately exposed.

The crime also marks a turning point in the relationship between Anthony Cade and Superintendent Battle. Initially positioned as suspect and investigator, they evolve into wary collaborators, with Battle recognizing in Cade an ally whose unorthodox methods complement official procedure. As Battle himself admits, the key to solving the case lies not in the physical evidence — the footprints, the open window, the Red Hand emblem — but in understanding the true identity of the man who called himself Prince Michael, and the secret that the real King Victor had been hiding in plain sight. [016-101]
